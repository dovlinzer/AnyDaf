# Bava Batra 153 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shchiv Mera Retraction

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דִּקְנוֹ מִינֵּיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא, אָמַר: מַאי אֶיעְבֵּיד לָךְ, דְּלָא אַקְנֵית כִּדְמַקְנוּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person on his deathbed who wrote a deed of transfer granting his property to another, and **it was acquired from him** by means of an act of acquisition. He subsequently recovered and wanted to retract the gift, and **he came before Rav Huna.** Rav Huna **said** to him: **What can I do for you?** You cannot retract the gift, **as you did not transfer** the gift **in the manner that people** on their deathbed **transfer** ownership of gifts, and you enhanced the legal power of the recipient by performing an act of acquisition.

We pick up at the top of 153, three lines from the top, at the words *hu d'kanu minei* — the conclusion of a lengthy discussion concerning a *shchiv mera* who gives away all of his property but also performs a *kinyan*. Rav holds that this combination means he cannot be *chozer* — he cannot retract the gift. Shmuel, by contrast, questions whether the gift is effective even if he dies.

### Rashbam & Tosafot

The Rashbam explains that the preceding discussion concerned a case of a *stam* *kinyan* — a straightforward act of acquisition — and argues that this does not fit well with the evidence presented earlier, which seemed to indicate that whenever a *kinyan* is performed, everyone agrees the gift takes effect, and that if he recovers (*amad*) the gift is revoked (*chozer*). Tosafot therefore explains that the case involves a *kinyan* that was specifically written into the *shtar* itself, which is what the *gemara* appears to indicate. This accounts for the range of opinions — from the view that the gift is fully effective and the donor cannot retract, to the view that it does not take effect at all.

### Rav Huna Case

The *gemara* now concludes this discussion with a practical illustration. The case is one where *kanu minei* — they performed this *kinyan* together with the *shtar* in the especially strong manner described — and presumably the donor subsequently recovered and sought to retract the gift. He came before Rav Huna, who said: *mai avad lach* — what can I do for you? You did not execute a standard *matnas shchiv mera*; you did it in this exceptionally strong form, and you have locked yourself in. You are not able to be *chozer*. This is essentially a practical application of what was established in the preceding analysis.

## Bechayim U-Vemavet

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא מַתַּנְתָּא דַּהֲוָה כָּתוּב בָּהּ ״בַּחַיִּים וּבַמָּוֶת״ – רַב אָמַר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת בָּרִיא.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: There was a deed pertaining to **a certain gift** of a person on his deathbed, **in which it was written** that the gift is given both **in life and in death.** In such a case, **Rav says: It is like** the **gift of a person on his deathbed,** and if he recovers he can retract it. **And Shmuel says: It is like** the **gift of a healthy person,** and he cannot retract it.

The *gemara* now introduces a new case: a gift document that was written with the language *be-hayim u-ve-mavet* — "in life and in death." The question is whether the presence of the word *mavet* — death — transforms this into a *matnas shchiv mera*, with all of the attendant implications: that if the donor recovers (*amad*) the gift is revoked, that it can potentially be nullified, and so on. Or does the phrase as a whole indicate a straightforward, unconditional gift?

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת שְׁכִיב מְרַע – מִדִּכְתִיב בָּהּ ״בַּמָּוֶת״, אַחַר מִיתָה קָאָמַר לֵיהּ; וְהַאי דִּכְתִיב ״בַּחַיִּים״ – סִימָן בְּעָלְמָא, דְּחָיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav says: It is like** the **gift of a person on his deathbed.** This can be inferred **from** the fact **that it is written in** the deed that the gift is given **in death.** This means that the giver **is saying to him** that the gift should take effect **after** his **death, and that which is written** in the deed, that the gift is given **in life,** is **merely** an auspicious **omen,** expressing hope **that** the giver **will live.**

Rav says: *harei hi ke-matnas shchiv mera* — it has the character of a *matnas shchiv mera*. The reasoning is that because the document includes the word *be-mavet*, it is clear that the donor means to designate this as taking effect at or around death — that is, he is *metzaveh machmat mita*, issuing instructions by reason of impending death, and the gift carries all the conditions of a *matnas shchiv mera*. As for why he wrote *be-hayim* — "in life" — Rav explains that this is merely a conventional flourish, a *siman be-alma de-hayyei*, a way of signaling that he is still alive, to ward off an *ayin hara*, as it were. His true intent is that the gift should take effect after his death.

### Shmuel Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר הֲרֵי הִיא כְּמַתְּנַת בָּרִיא – מִדִּכְתִיב בָּהּ ״בַּחַיִּים״, מֵחַיִּים קָאָמַר; וְהַאי דִּכְתַב ״וּבַמָּוֶת״ – כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״מֵעַתָּה וְעַד עוֹלָם״. אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: It is like** the **gift of a healthy person.** This can be inferred **from** the fact **that it is written in** the deed that the gift is given **in life.** This means that the giver **is saying** that the gift takes effect **during** his **life,** i.e., immediately. **And that which he wrote,** that the gift is given **in death, is like one who says: From now and for evermore,** i.e., that the gift is not retractable. The Sages **of Neharde'a say: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav.**

Shmuel disagrees: *harei hi ke-matnas bari* — it is equivalent to the gift of a healthy person. The word *be-hayim* signals that he is giving it *me-hayim* — from the time he is still living, meaning it takes effect immediately and unconditionally. As for the phrase *u-ve-mavet*, Shmuel reads this as equivalent to saying *me-ata ve-ad olam* — from now and henceforth. He simply means: you should have it now, and it should remain yours even after I die. The phrase is therefore an expression of perpetuity, not an indication of *shchiv mera* intent.

### Rava Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְאִי כְּתִיב בַּהּ ״מֵחַיִּים״ – קְנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And if it was written in** the deed that the gift is given **during life** and in death, the recipient **acquires** the gift and it cannot be retracted, as this term indicates that the gift takes effect while the giver still lives.

Rava introduces a qualification to Rav's position: *ve-i katav bei me-hayim kana* — even Rav would concede that if the document says *me-hayim* rather than *be-hayim*, the recipient acquires the gift outright. The difference in preposition is meaningful: *me-hayim* — "from life," from the time of living — indicates that the donor intends the gift to take immediate effect, and it would therefore be treated as a *matnas bari*.

### Ruling & Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: לֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּהָא אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא ״מֵחַיִּים״ – מוֹדֵי רַב; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar:** Isn't that **obvious, as** the Sages **of Neharde'a say** that **the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav?** Ameimar replied: **Lest you say** that with regard to the term: **During life, Rav concedes** that the giver intends for the gift to take effect immediately, Ameimar **teaches us** that since the giver also mentioned death, he intended the gift as the gift of a person on his deathbed and he can retract it.

Ameimar states as a *meimra*: *lo hilchata kevateih de-Rava* — we do not rule in accordance with Rava's limitation. This is then clarified by Rav Ashi, who explains that the *gemara*'s purpose is to forestall the very reasoning Rava employed. One might have thought that once we rule like Rav, Rav himself would concede if the phrase used is *me-hayim*. The *gemara* teaches us that this is not the case: once the ruling follows Rav, it makes no difference whether the document says *be-hayim* or *me-hayim* — as long as it includes *u-ve-mavet*, it is treated as a *matnas shchiv mera*. Strikingly, it is the *Nehardaei* — the sages of Nehardea, the very academy associated with Shmuel — who declare that the *halacha* follows Rav.

## Rav Nachman Case

### Case Presented

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן לִנְהַרְדְּעָא. שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא לְשׁוּם טַמְיָא, אָמַר: הָכָא אַתְרָא דִשְׁמוּאֵל – הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** person who wrote in the deed that the gift is given in life and in death. When he recovered he wanted to retract the gift. He **came before Rav Naḥman in Neharde'a.** Rav Naḥman **sent him to** appear **before Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba in Shum Tamya.** Rav Naḥman **said** in explanation of his actions: **Here,** Neharde'a, **is the place of Shmuel.** Consequently, Shmuel's rulings should be followed, and therefore **how can we act in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav?**

The *gemara* now turns to a practical application of the foregoing. A case came before Rav Nachman in Nehardea involving a gift document written with the language *bechayim u-ve-mavet*.

### Rav Nachman's Respect

Although the *halacha* follows Rav — and the Nehardaei themselves had declared as much — Rav Nachman declined to rule on the case himself. He sent it to Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba in Shum Tamaya. His reasoning: *hacha asrei de-Shmuel, heichi navid ke-vasei de-Rav* — this is the city of Shmuel; how can I render a ruling here against the tradition of Shmuel's academy? The comparison evoked is the verse from Esther, *hagam lichbosh es hamalkah imi babayis* — even if the *halacha* follows Rav, to issue that ruling within Shmuel's own city would be an act of disrespect to the legacy of the place. Rav Nachman therefore deferred to another jurisdiction, where the ruling in accordance with Rav could properly be delivered.

## Rava & Widow

### Rava Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, עֲבַד לַהּ רָבָא כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ. הֲוָה קָא טָרְדָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain** woman who wrote in the deed that the gift is given during life and in death. She **came before Rava. Rava acted with regard to her** case **in accordance with his halakhic** ruling, and he ruled that she cannot retract the gift. She did not accept the ruling, and **she** constantly **troubled him,** saying that he had not judged her case properly.

A separate case came before Rava himself. *Avad lei Rava ke-shma'atei* — Rava ruled in accordance with his own stated position, that if the document uses the language *me-hayim*, it is treated as a *matnas bari*, a gift of a healthy person. In this particular instance, it appears the donor had subsequently recovered and then died, and because Rava ruled that the gift was a valid and irrevocable *matnas bari*, the property passed to the outside recipient. The widow and heirs were left without recourse, since had it been ruled a *matnas shchiv mera*, the gift would have been reversed and the estate would have remained with the heirs, leaving the widow with support. Rava's ruling thus left her destitute.

### Deception Attempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְרַב פָּפָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חָנָן – סָפְרֵיהּ: זִיל כְּתוֹב לַהּ, וּכְתוֹב בָּהּ: ״שׂוֹכֵר עֲלֵיהֶן אוֹ מַטְעָן״. אָמְרָה: לִיטְבַּע אַרְבֵּיהּ! אַטְעוֹיֵי קָא מַטְעֵית לִי! אַמְשִׁינְהוּ לְמָנֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בְּמַיָּא, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא אִיפְּרַק מִטִּיבְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to Rav Pappa, his scribe, son of Rav Ḥanan: Go, write for her** a ruling in her favor, **and write in** the ruling the phrase: He may **hire** replacements **at their** expense, **or deceive them** to get them to return to work. This is a phrase from the mishna (*Bava Metzia* 75b) that discusses the ruling in the case of one who hired laborers to perform a task that cannot be delayed, and they quit. Rava intended this phrase to indicate to the court that the ruling was merely a ruse in order to persuade the woman to leave. The woman understood the ruse. **She said: May his ship sink; you are deceiving me. Rava had his clothes immersed in water** so that the curse should be fulfilled in this alternative manner, **but even so he was not saved from** the **sinking** of his ship.

She was not prepared to accept this quietly. *Hava ka-tarda lei* — she continued to press him, arguing that the gift was in fact a *matnas shchiv mera* and that the heirs were entitled to the property. Rava, apparently in an attempt to quiet her, summoned Rav Pappa the son of Rav Chanan, who served as the court's *sofer* — the official scribe — and instructed him: *zil kesov la* — go write her a document indicating that the property belongs to the heirs. But then, anticipating the problem that she might use such a document to collect, he added a further instruction: write at the bottom of it *shocheira aleiha o mata'an* — a reference to the *mishna* in Bava Metzia that permits a degree of strategic maneuvering when a worker reneges mid-task, in order to get the job completed at the worker's expense. By embedding this cryptic reference, the document would serve as a signal to any court that read it: this document was written merely to placate her, and is not a genuine *psak halacha*. The impropriety here is considerable — not only is this an act of deception toward the woman, but it would mean that the court was issuing a document whose stated content was false, with a secret message embedded to undermine it.

### Curse & Punishment

She was not deceived. She recognized the ruse and was furious. *Litba arbei* — let your boat sink, she cursed him. *Atuya ka-matfi li?* — Are you trying to deceive me? Rava took the curse seriously. The Rashbam notes the principle: *al tehi klalas hedyot kala be-einecha* — do not regard lightly the curse of an ordinary person. He therefore immersed his garments in water — a symbolic act intended to constitute a minor fulfillment of the curse, so that the more severe punishment would be averted. Nevertheless, *ve-afilu hacha lo ifrik mi-tiv'a* — even this did not save him, and he was not spared from the punishment; his boat apparently did sink. The *gemara* seems to be signaling its alignment with the widow: the *halacha* was on her side, and beyond that, the attempt to deceive her was simply wrong.

## Burden of Proof

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא כָּתַב בָּהּ שְׁכִיב מְרַע, הוּא אוֹמֵר ״שְׁכִיב מְרַע הָיִיתִי״, וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים ״בָּרִיא הָיִיתָ״ – צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁשְּׁכִיב מְרַע הָיָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵרוֹ – עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **did not write in** the deed that he was **on his deathbed,** and he then recovered and wished to retract the gift, and **he says: I was on my deathbed,** and since I recovered, I can retract the gift, **but** the recipients **say: You were healthy,** and the gift cannot be retracted, the giver **must bring proof that he was on his deathbed** in order to retract the gift. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: The burden of proof rests upon the claimant,** and since the property is in the possession of the giver, the recipients must bring proof that they have the right to receive it.

Having laid out the principles of *matnas shchiv mera* — with all the distinctions concerning *kinyan*, *amad*, and related factors — the *gemara* now proceeds to a *mishna* that addresses an equally fundamental question: who bears the burden of proof when the circumstances of the gift are in dispute? The *mishna* case involves a gift document that does not explicitly state that the donor was a *shchiv mera*. This matters greatly because the standard language of a *matnas shchiv mera* document would be written in the *shtar* itself, and its presence or absence determines the applicable legal framework — above all, the rule of *amad chozer*.

### The Dispute

The donor says: *shchiv mera hayiti* — I was a *shchiv mera* when I gave this gift; it was a *matana be-kula*, a gift of all my property, and now that I have recovered, the gift should be reversed. The recipients counter: *bari hayita* — you were healthy when you gave it; the document contains nothing indicating you were a *shchiv mera*. The competing considerations are real. On one hand, the donor is *muchzak* in the property — he is the recognized owner — and one might therefore reason that the burden falls on those who would take from him. On the other hand, the recipients hold a *shtar*, and a *shtar* carries the force of testimony. The *shtar* makes no mention of *shchiv mera* status; if the donor wishes to claim special circumstances, it would seem that he bears the burden of establishing them.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir holds: *tzarich lehavi raaya she-shchiv mera haya* — the donor must bring proof that he was a *shchiv mera*. The *shtar* as it stands is a plain gift document, and he cannot override it with an unsubstantiated claim of special circumstances.

### Chachamim

The *chachamim* hold: *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-raaya* — the burden falls on those seeking to extract from his possession. Since he is the *muchzak*, the recipients bear the burden of proving that he was healthy when he gave it. This position is initially difficult to understand. They possess a *shtar*; he is healthy now; the document makes no mention of illness. Why should they be required to prove anything? This puzzle will be addressed directly by the *gemara*.

## Hashta Principle

### Rabba's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָהוּא מַתַּנְתָּא דַּהֲוָה כְּתִב בַּהּ: ״כַּד הֲוָה קְצִיר וּרְמֵי בְּעַרְסֵיהּ״; וְלָא כְּתַב בָּהּ: ״וּמִגּוֹ מַרְעֵיהּ אִיפְּטַר לְבֵית עוֹלָמֵיהּ״. אֲמַר רַבָּה: הֲרֵי מֵת, וַהֲרֵי קִבְרוֹ מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: There was a deed pertaining to **a certain gift** of a person on his deathbed, **in which it was written** that the gift was bestowed **when** the giver **was sick and lying in his bed, but** the continuation of the standard formula: **And from his sickness he departed to his** eternal **home, was not written in it.** The giver's heirs claimed that although he was ill when he wrote the deed, he later recovered, and his gift is not valid.
>
> **Rabba said: He is dead, and his grave proves** that **he** died. It may therefore be assumed that he did not recover from his sickness, and his gift remains valid.

The *gemara* presents a case that is related to but distinct from the *mishna*'s scenario. Here, the document *does* record that the gift was given in the context of illness — the standard language *kad hava katzir u-rami be-arseih*, "when he was sick and lying in his bed," is present. But the document does not include the subsequent notation that *u-migo mara'eih iptar le-beit olamo* — that he departed from his illness to the world to come, meaning that he died directly from that illness without having recovered in between. Normally, witnesses would wait until after the donor's death and then record that fact as well, establishing both that the gift was given while he was a *shchiv mera* and that he died from that illness — demonstrating that the gift was effective, since he never recovered in the interim. Here, that notation is absent. So we know the gift was given in the context of illness, but we do not know whether he subsequently recovered and then died (which would have revoked the gift automatically, as Tosafot notes — *amad* revokes the *matnas shchiv mera* without requiring a specific act of revocation, at least when the full estate was given), or whether he died directly from his illness (in which case the gift stands). Rabba rules: *harei met ve-harei kivro mochiach alav* — he is dead, and his grave testifies on his behalf. Since he was sick and is now dead, we do not assume that some intervening event — a recovery — occurred between the illness and the death. We treat it as a *chazaka de-hashta*: given the current state of affairs, we reconstruct the past in its most straightforward configuration.

### Abaye Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַשְׁתָּא, וּמָה סְפִינָה – שֶׁרוּבָּן לֵאָבֵד, נוֹתְנִין עֲלֵיהֶן חוּמְרֵי חַיִּים וְחוּמְרֵי מֵתִים; חוֹלִין – שֶׁרוֹב חוֹלִין לְחַיִּים, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: And now, if** in the case of **a ship** that sank, **where** the fate of **most of** the passengers of sunken ships is **to perish,** the **stringencies of** the **living and** the **stringencies of** the **dead are applied to them** due to the uncertainty as to whether they are alive or dead, in the case of **sick people, where** the fate of **most sick people** is **to** return to **life, all the more so** is it **not** clear that one should assume that he recovered from the illness and his gift is invalid?

Abaye challenges Rabba's reasoning by invoking a well-known principle concerning a lost ship. When a ship is lost at sea, *she-ruban le-abed* — the majority of such cases end in the passengers' deaths. And yet the *halacha* is that we impose on the passengers *chomrei chayim ve-chomrei metim* — the stringencies of both the living and the dead. A *kohen*'s wife, for instance, must be concerned that her husband may have drowned and she cannot eat *teruma*, yet a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael* cannot presume her husband dead and return to her father's household to eat *teruma* either. Even though statistically the outcome is most likely death, we do not presume death until it is confirmed. Now, Abaye continues: *she-rov cholim le-chayim* — when it comes to someone who is sick and lying in bed in the manner of a *shchiv mera*, the majority of such people actually recover. So if we cannot presume death even in the case of a ship, where the majority perish, *lo kol she-ken* — certainly in the case of illness, where the majority recover, we should not presume that he died directly from his illness without recovering in between.

### Rabba Reply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָבְרוּ עָלָיו יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, אֲבָל עָבְרוּ עָלָיו יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְכָאן וּלְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba replied: She is dead, and his grave testifies on his behalf. The assumption that he died, based on the grave as testimony, is a reconstruction from the endpoint. Rava extends this into a more general principle concerning the "rainy season" limitation.

Rabba's reply is pointed: the ship case and this case are not comparable. In the ship case, we begin from a state of confirmed life and are being asked to presume death — that we do not do. Here, we *know* he is dead. The grave is before us. The only question is whether he recovered in the interim. And on that question, we look at the current state of affairs — *hashta* — and reason that since he was sick and is now dead, nothing unusual intervened. That reconstruction is the application of *chazaka de-hashta*.

### Rabba Clarifies

This becomes the axis of a debate between Abaye and Rava. Rava aligns with Rabba's approach — given that we know the endpoint (death), we reason backward from the current status to assume the most direct path. It is this position that Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua will link, in the next section, to the broader framework of Rabbi Natan's principle.

## Three Positions

### Rav Huna Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא שְׁמַעְתָּא דְּרַבָּה – כְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא: מִי מוֹצִיא מִיַּד מִי? הוּא מוֹצִיא מִידֵיהֶן בְּלֹא רְאָיָה, וְהֵן אֵין מוֹצִיאִין מִיָּדוֹ אֶלָּא בִּרְאָיָה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אִם בָּרִיא הוּא – עָלָיו לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁהָיָה שְׁכִיב מְרַע. אִם שְׁכִיב מְרַע הוּא – עֲלֵיהֶן לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁבָּרִיא הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: In accordance with whose** opinion **is that *halakha* of Rabba?** It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Natan, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a case where one who gave his property to others claims that since he was on his deathbed at the time he can retract the gifts, and the recipients claim that he was healthy and cannot retract it, **who removes** the property **from whose possession?** The giver **can remove** it **from** the recipients' **possession without proof,** as the property was previously established to be in his possession, **but** the recipients **can remove** it **from** the giver's **possession only with proof.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Ya'akov.** **Rabbi Natan says:** The presumption is that the current situation reflects the situation at the time the gift was bestowed. Therefore, **if he is** currently **healthy,** the obligation **is upon him to bring proof that he was on his deathbed** when he gave his property to others. **If he is** currently **on his deathbed,** the obligation **is upon** the recipients **to bring proof that he was healthy** then.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua asks: *keman azla ha shemata de-Rabba* — whose position does Rabba's ruling align with? He answers: *ke-Rabbi Natan* — it follows Rabbi Natan. A *braita* is cited that lays out the identical dispute we see in the *mishna*, but in a more developed form, making explicit the range of opinions.

### Rabbi Yaakov

Rabbi Yaakov holds that the donor takes the property out of the recipients' hands without bringing any proof — *hu motzi mi-yadeihen belo raaya* — and they cannot recover it from him except by bringing proof — *ve-hen motzi-in mi-yado ela be-raaya*. In other words, he is the *muchzak*, and they must prove their claim. This position is striking: they hold a *shtar*; moreover, the donor is currently healthy — which would seem to strengthen, not weaken, the case that he gave it as a healthy person. Yet Rabbi Yaakov says none of that is sufficient. He is the *muchzak* in the property, and that presumption of ownership trumps even the *shtar* in their hands, as long as the *shtar*'s circumstances are contested. This position corresponds to that of the *chachamim* in the *mishna*.

### Rabbi Natan

Rabbi Natan takes a different approach entirely. *Im bari hu alav lehavi raaya she-shchiv mera* — if the donor is currently healthy, then the burden falls on him to prove he was a *shchiv mera* when he gave the gift, since his current healthy status creates the presumption that he was healthy when the gift was made. *Im shchiv mera hu aleinu lehavi raaya she-bari haya* — if he is currently ill and bedridden, then the burden falls on the recipients to prove he was healthy at the time of the gift. The determinative factor for Rabbi Natan is neither the *shtar* nor the *muchzak* presumption — it is *hashta*, the current status. Whatever his condition is now, we assume that is the condition he was in when he gave the gift. This position corresponds to that of Rabbi Meir in the *mishna*: since the donor is presumably healthy at the time he brings his claim (otherwise he could not be litigating), we presume he was healthy when he wrote the *shtar*, and he therefore bears the burden of proving he was a *shchiv mera*.

This yields three distinct positions. Rabbi Yaakov holds that the donor is always *muchzak* and the recipients always bear the burden, even with a *shtar* in hand. Rabbi Meir or Rabbi Natan holds that current status — *hashta* — is determinative: if he is now healthy, he bears the burden. And a third position, which simply goes by the *shtar* at face value, would say that the document as written governs and no further proof is required in either direction. It is Rava's application of *hashta* — reasoning from the current status backward — that aligns him with Rabbi Natan.

## Tuma Analogy

### Field Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: וּלְטוּמְאָה – כַּמַּחְלוֹקֶת. דִּתְנַן: בִּקְעָה בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה – רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לַשַּׁבָּת, וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לַטּוּמְאָה. בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְכָאן וּלְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: And with regard to** a case of uncertain **ritual impurity,** the *halakha* depends **on** the same **dispute.** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 6:7): The *halakha* is that a case where it is uncertain if something or someone became impure in the public domain, the item or person is deemed pure. With regard to **an expanse of fields, in the summer,** when many people pass through the fields, it **is** considered **the private domain with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat, but** it is considered **the public domain with regard to** the *halakhot* of **ritual impurity,** and if one is uncertain as to whether he was rendered impure there, he is deemed pure. **In the rainy season,** when not many people pass through the fields, an expanse of fields is considered **the private domain** both **with regard to this,** Shabbat, **and with regard to that,** ritual impurity. Therefore, if one is uncertain whether he was rendered impure there, he is deemed impure. If one does not know whether the day he entered the expanse of fields was considered part of the summer or the rainy season, and he is uncertain whether he was rendered impure there, Rabbi Ya'akov maintains that he retains the status of purity that he held before entering the fields. According to Rabbi Natan, it is presumed that he entered the fields during the same season in which he came to ask whether he was rendered impure.

Rabbi Elazar introduces a parallel from the laws of *tuma* that illuminates the *hashta* principle in a different legal domain. The *mishna* he cites concerns a valley used for growing grain. *Bika bi-mot ha-chama reshut ha-yachid le-Shabbat ve-reshut ha-rabim le-tuma* — in the dry summer season, the valley has the status of a *reshut ha-yachid* for the purposes of *Shabbat*, but a *reshut ha-rabim* for the purposes of *tuma*. The two categories are entirely distinct: *reshut ha-yachid* for *Shabbat* is defined by its physical enclosure — three or four walls — while *reshut ha-yachid* for *tuma* is defined by the principle derived from *Sotah*: a private, secluded space. The *gemara* here may be referring either to a field that is literally enclosed by a fence or, in another version, to a *karmelit*, a space that is neither a full *reshut ha-yachid* nor a *reshut ha-rabim* for *Shabbat* purposes. Either way, the point is that in the summer, people regularly cross through the field on their way to other destinations — not in a manner that constitutes a major thoroughfare, but enough to render it non-secluded. Consequently, while it may be enclosed (and therefore a *reshut ha-yachid* for *Shabbat*), it lacks the seclusion required for the *tuma* definition of *reshut ha-yachid*, and it is therefore a *reshut ha-rabim* for *tuma* purposes. This matters because *safek tuma* in a *reshut ha-yachid* renders one *tamei*, while *safek tuma* in a *reshut ha-rabim* leaves one *tahor*. *Bi-mot ha-geshamim reshut ha-yachid le-chan u-le-chan* — in the rainy season, however, the field is a *reshut ha-yachid* both for *Shabbat* and for *tuma*. As Tosafot explains, this is not merely because wet weather deters travelers; it is because the rainy season is when the grain is growing, and no one will walk through a field of growing grain and risk damaging the crops. The field is therefore genuinely secluded in the rainy season, qualifying as a *reshut ha-yachid* for *tuma* as well.

The relevance to *hashta*: suppose someone comes to a *posek* and says he was in a field and is uncertain whether he encountered a source of *tuma* — but he cannot remember whether it was the rainy season or the dry season when this occurred. Rabbi Elazar's implicit suggestion is that one might apply *hashta* here as well — if you are asking in the rainy season, assume it occurred in the rainy season; if in the dry season, assume the dry season. This would determine whether it is a *safek* in a *reshut ha-yachid* (and you are *tamei*) or in a *reshut ha-rabim* (and you are *tahor*).

### Rava on Seasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא עָבְרוּ עָלָיו יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, אֲבָל עָבְרוּ עָלָיו יְמוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים – רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְכָאן וּלְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** disagrees with Rabbi Elazar and **says: They taught** the ruling of the mishna **only** with regard to a case **where the rainy season has not** yet **passed over** the fields after the possibility arose that the fields contained impurity. **But** if **the rainy season has** already **passed over** the fields, it is considered **the private domain** both **with regard to this,** Shabbat, **and with regard to that,** ritual impurity. Even though the one who passed through the fields came to ask about his status in the summer, when the fields should be considered the public domain, he is nevertheless deemed impure even according to Rabbi Natan, and there is no parallel dispute with regard to uncertain ritual impurity.

This is precisely the limitation that Rava introduces. *Lo shanu ela she-lo averu alav yemot ha-geshamim* — this rule applies only if a rainy season has not yet passed since the event in question. If the question is being asked within the same season as the uncertain event — or close enough that no rainy season has intervened — then applying the current season's status to the past makes a degree of sense: you cannot remember whether it was early or late in this season, so you go by what season it currently is. But *aval averu alav yemot ha-geshamim* — if a full rainy season has passed since the event occurred, then regardless of what season it currently is, the field is treated as a *reshut ha-yachid* for *tuma* purposes. Why? Because if the event happened before the most recent rainy season, then in reconstructing the timeline, you necessarily pass through a rainy season on the way back to when it occurred. That rainy season is the most recent relevant status before the event, and since rainy season means *reshut ha-yachid*, you treat it as a *safek* in a *reshut ha-yachid* and rule *tamei*. Applying the current dry season's status to an event that, by definition, preceded a rainy season would be logically untenable.

This is a brilliant clarification of what *hashta* means and what it does not mean. It is not simply that we look at the calendar right now and impose that on an arbitrarily distant past. *Hashta* means: when the temporal gap is small enough that the current status genuinely reflects the most proximate condition before the event, we use it. Once an intervening change of status is established — once a rainy season is confirmed to have occurred between then and now — the principle of *hashta* no longer overrides the logic of reconstruction.

## Proof Standards

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאָיָה – בְּמַאי? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: רְאָיָה בְּעֵדִים. רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי: רְאָיָה בְּקִיּוּם הַשְּׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to the **proof** that the recipients must bring, **in what** manner is it brought? **Rav Huna says:** The **proof** is presented **by** bringing **witnesses** who testify that the giver was healthy. **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna say:** The **proof** is presented **by** the **ratification of the deed,** i.e., the recipients are required only to ascertain that the signatures of the witnesses on the deed are authentic in order to prove that it is not forged.

The *gemara* now turns to a precise question arising from the *chachamim*'s ruling in the *mishna*: what exactly constitutes the *raaya* — the proof — that the recipients must bring? Rav Huna says: *raaya be-edim* — they must bring proof in the form of independent witnesses, beyond what the *shtar* itself provides. The *shtar* alone is insufficient to carry their burden; they need additional testimonial evidence establishing that the donor was healthy at the time of the gift.

### Rav Chisda

Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna disagree: *raaya be-kiyum ha-shtar* — the proof required is simply the authentication of the *shtar* itself. They need not produce additional *edim* attesting to the donor's health. All that is required is that the *shtar* be properly authenticated — *mkuyam* — and once its signatures are established and it stands on its own footing, it suffices. The debate between Rav Huna on one side and Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna on the other thus maps onto a deeper question: precisely what burden does a *shtar* carry in a contested situation, and when does authentication resolve that burden?

### Peh She'asar

This in turn illuminates the underlying debate between Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* in the *mishna*. Rav Huna's reading is that Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* are disputing the same point as Rabbi Natan and Rabbi Yaakov: the question is whose *hashta* — whose current status — determines the burden. Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna, however, read the *mishna* differently. Their view is that the dispute in the *mishna* concerns *modeh be-shtar she-ketavo tzarich le-kaymo* — whether one who acknowledges the authenticity of a *shtar* but contests its validity on other grounds is required to independently authenticate it. Rabbi Meir holds: *eino tzarich le-kaymo* — since the donor is not claiming the *shtar* is a forgery, only that the circumstances render it inapplicable, there is no need for them to do a *kiyum*. The document as acknowledged governs, and he bears the burden of proving his *shchiv mera* claim. The *chachamim*, however, hold: *tzarich le-kaymo* — since the donor is simultaneously acknowledging the *shtar* and undermining its operative force by claiming he was a *shchiv mera*, this is akin to the principle of *peh she'asar hu peh she-hitir* — the mouth that imposes a restriction is the same mouth that permits. Because the *shtar*'s legal weight is entangled with the donor's ambivalent acknowledgement, the recipients cannot rely on that acknowledgement alone; they must produce an independent *kiyum ha-shtar* to give it independent force. The classic analogy is the case of a debtor who, when presented with an unauthenticated *shtar*, says: "Yes, it is a real document, but I already paid." The question there is the same: does his acknowledgement that the *shtar* is genuine suffice to give it force, or does the fact that he simultaneously undermines it require independent authentication?

Once Rav Chisda's framing is accepted, the *chachamim*'s position becomes fully coherent. They are not saying that a *shtar* plus a healthy donor is somehow insufficient to overcome the *muchzak* presumption — they are saying that the recipients must perform a *kiyum ha-shtar* to establish the document on independent grounds, after which it will indeed prevail.

## Found Document

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא כָּתַב בָּהּ שְׁכִיב מְרַע, הוּא אוֹמֵר ״שְׁכִיב מְרַע הָיִיתִי״, וְהֵן אוֹמְרִים ״בָּרִיא הָיִיתָ״ – צָרִיךְ לְהָבִיא רְאָיָה שֶׁשְּׁכִיב מְרַע הָיָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵרוֹ – עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **did not write in** the deed that he was **on his deathbed,** and he then recovered and wished to retract the gift, and **he says: I was on my deathbed,** and since I recovered, I can retract the gift, **but** the recipients **say: You were healthy,** and the gift cannot be retracted, the giver **must bring proof that he was on his deathbed** in order to retract the gift. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: The burden of proof rests upon the claimant,** and since the property is in the possession of the giver, the recipients must bring proof that they have the right to receive it.

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishna*: *mi she-meit ve-nimtza'as dyatiki keshura lo al yereicho* — someone dies and a document, a *dyatiki* — a will or gift document — is found tied to his thigh. *Harei zu eina klum* — it is entirely worthless. The *mishna* goes on to explain that if the donor had previously handed over the document to a third party (*zicha ba le-acher*) to take possession on behalf of the recipient, then the gift is valid whether the document was inside the house or outside. The *gemara* notes that the word *dyatiki* is an acronym for *da tehei le-mikam u-li-hyot* — "this shall stand and be" — which was the formula a dying person would use in declaring his final disposition of property.

### Gemara Question

The *gemara* asks: why should the document found on his thigh be invalid? *Ve-lima dilma be-ma'amad shloshtan amra* — perhaps he transferred the gift in the presence of three people. A verbal gift made in the presence of three witnesses is a valid and recognized mode of transfer even without a physical document. The mere fact that the document was discovered on his body does not rule out the possibility that he had already accomplished the transfer through this other method.

### Found Implication

The *gemara* answers: the *mishna* says *found*. The word *nimtza'as* — found — carries a specific implication. When a document is *found*, it means that no one knew about it until it was discovered. And if no one knew about it, then there was no prior occasion on which it was handed over in the presence of three people. Had a *ma'amad shloshtan* occurred, people would have been present and would have known. The very fact of discovery — the surprise, the absence of prior knowledge — excludes the possibility of any prior effective transfer. The document is therefore worthless precisely because it was found.
