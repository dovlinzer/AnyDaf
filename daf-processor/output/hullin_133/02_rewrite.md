# Hullin 133 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exemption Framework

### Shochet & Ownership

The *mishnah* at the top of 132b opens with the ruling *hashochet l'Kohen ul'oved kochavim patur min hamatanot*: one who slaughters for a *kohen* or a non-Jew is exempt from the *matanot*. The *mishnah*'s phrasing, which focuses on the *shochet* rather than the owner, raises an immediate structural question: why frame the law around the act of slaughtering rather than around the ownership of the animal? After all, it is the ownership — or, more precisely, for whom the animal is being slaughtered — that determines whether an obligation exists.

The *gemara* navigates a subtle distinction here. The determination of *chiyuv* or *p'tur* turns on who owns the animal, or at least for whom the *shechitah* is being performed. Yet the person with whom the *kohen* deals to collect his gifts is the *shochet*, not necessarily the owner. One could well imagine a case where a *yisrael* owns a cow and hires a *shochet* to slaughter it: the obligation to give the gifts falls on the *shochet*, even though the *shochet* does not own the cow. Conversely, if the cow belongs to a *kohen* and the *shochet* is a *yisrael*, the *shochet* is exempt. The ownership of the animal — or the identity of the party for whom the slaughter is performed — governs the question of obligation, while the *shochet* is the relevant legal actor for purposes of fulfilling or defaulting on that obligation.

One might ask whether the question is truly about ownership at the moment of *shechitah* or about a more functional notion of "for whom" the animal is being slaughtered. The *gemara* never fully resolves this distinction, but working assumption throughout is that ownership at the moment of *shechitah* is the operative criterion.

### Rava's Exposition

The *gemara* asks pointedly: why does the *mishnah* say "one who slaughters *for* a *kohen*" rather than simply "a *kohen* and a non-Jew are exempt"? *Rava* answers: *zos omeres hadini im hatabach* — this teaches us that the legal relationship is with the butcher, the *shochet*. The *mishnah*'s formulation is deliberate. Had it stated that the *kohen* is exempt, one might have concluded that you pursue the owner of the cow. By framing it as "one who slaughters for a *kohen*," the *mishnah* locates the obligation squarely with the *shochet*.

*Rava* further derives this from a *derasha*. The verse states *me'eis ha'am* — "from the people" — excluding *kohanim*: a *kohen* need not give these gifts to a fellow *kohen*. What determines the obligation is for whom the animal belongs, not who performs the slaughter. As the verse adds, *me'izovchei hazevach* — "from those who slaughter the slaughtering" — this language encompasses even a *kohen* who serves as a butcher. The irony is precise: the determination of obligation depends on the ownership of the animal, but the person obligated is always the *shochet*.

## Exemption Framework (II)

### Partnership Strategy

This framework naturally gives rise to a strategy. If ownership by a *kohen* exempts the *shochet* from giving *matanot*, what if a *kohen* becomes a business partner in the butchery operation? Insofar as the *kohen* holds a share of the animals being slaughtered, those animals are — at least partially — being slaughtered for a *kohen*, and the *shochet* might claim exemption on that basis.

## Rav Tavla's Plan

### Partnership Proposal

The *gemara* presents a concrete case. *Rav Tavla* had a guest — a *kohen* — who was suffering financial hardship (*dachik lei milsa*). *Rav Tavla* advised him: *zil ishtatef bahadi tavchei Yisrael* — go and enter into a partnership with the Jewish butchers. The logic is that since you will be a partner with them, and they will thereby be slaughtering partly for a *kohen*, they will be exempt from giving the *matanot* (*d'migo d'miptari mimatnasa mishtatfi bahadach*). In exchange, the *kohen* receives a percentage of the business's profits — a more reliable income stream than occasionally collecting *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva* and waiting in line behind other *kohanim*.

The implicit assumption is that these butchers own the animals they slaughter — they purchase livestock, slaughter it, and sell the meat — so that at the moment of *shechitah* the animal is theirs, and the partnership with the *kohen* means part of it is being slaughtered on behalf of a *kohen*-owner. Even though the meat is immediately destined for sale to the general public, the ownership at the moment of *shechitah* appears to be what matters.

### Nachman's Rejection

*Rav Nachman* rejected the arrangement. When the butcher protested — *v'ha Rav Tavla patran*, "but *Rav Tavla* exempted us!" — *Rav Nachman* replied sharply: *zil afik*, go and give the gifts, *v'ei lo mafkina lach Rav Tavla m'oneich* — if you do not, I will remove *Rav Tavla* from your ear. The dismissiveness of the phrase is telling. *Rav Nachman* was not persuaded by the authority of *Rav Tavla*'s ruling and enforced the giving of *matanot* regardless.

*Rav Tavla* then came before *Rav Nachman* to ask why he had done this. *Rav Nachman* explained by citing a tradition brought by *Rav Acha bar Chanina* from the south, in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*.

### South Elders' Rule

*Ziknei darom* — the elders of the south — ruled as follows: *Kohen tabach* — if a *kohen* is himself a butcher, meaning he owns the animals and is slaughtering them for sale, he is technically exempt from giving *matanot*, since at the moment of *shechitah* the animals are his. Nevertheless, *shtayim v'shalosh shabbatos patur min hamatnos* — for two or three weeks this exemption holds. *Mikan v'eilach chayav b'matnos* — from that point onward, he becomes obligated.

The reasoning is implicit but important. Technically, as long as the *kohen* owns the meat at the moment of *shechitah*, even with the intention of immediately reselling it to the public, he is exempt. The moment of slaughter is what matters, not the ultimate destination of the meat. However, once it becomes established that the *kohen* is operating as a butcher for the broader community — and that is what his business consists of — it is no longer appropriate or legitimate to claim the exemption. There is no biblical basis for the two-to-three week grace period, as *Tosafot* notes; it must be a rabbinic ruling. But the principle it reflects is that the exemption cannot serve as a vehicle for permanently avoiding the institution of *matanot*.

## System Protection

### Tosafot's Concern

*Tosafot* explicitly articulates the broader concern underlying *Rav Nachman*'s ruling. The worry is not merely about the individual *kohen* who happens to be a butcher, but about the systemic abuse of the exemption. As *Tosafot* states: *shelo yargilu tibchei Yisrael leshatef kohanim imahem lehipater min hamatnot* — we do not want Jewish butchers to make it a practice to partner with *kohanim* as a mechanism for evading *matanot* altogether. For the first two or three weeks, one cannot be certain whether the arrangement is a deliberate evasion or a genuine situation. After that point, the intent is clear, and the exemption is disallowed.

*Rav Nachman* added a further refinement to the storefront test. He explained: *hani mili d'lo kavia michtata* — this leniency applies only where the person does not have an established storefront. *Aval heicha d'ka-hacha kavia michtata* — but where there is a visible, fixed business operation, allowing the partnership exemption would obviously subvert the entire institution of *matanot*, since every butcher would immediately obtain a *kohen* as a nominal partner. The storefront is the dispositive marker: when the commercial operation is unmistakably running as a community-serving business, no partnership exemption is available.

### Chisda's Ban

*Rav Chisda* declared: *hai kahana d'lo mafrish matnata lei havei beshamta le-Elohei Yisrael* — a *kohen* who does not separate off the *matanot* is under a *ban* in the name of God. The phrase *beshamta le-Elohei Yisrael* is striking. The concern here, as clarified by the *Rif* and the *Rosh* cited in the commentaries, is the case of a *kohen* acting as a butcher where the animals are not owned by a *kohen* — and yet, because the *shochet* himself is a *kohen*, he declines to give the gifts. Against this, *Rav Chisda* imposes the *shamta*.

*Rava bar Rav Shila* then reports that the butchers of *Hutza* have been under *Rav Chisda*'s ban for twenty-two years.

### Compliance Not Punishment

One might read this statement as a critique of the ban's ineffectiveness, but the *gemara* draws a different conclusion. The question becomes: *le-mai hilchata* — what is the legal significance of this observation?

The suggestion that we should now lift the ban since it has clearly not worked is rejected by a *baraita*. The *baraita* distinguishes between punishment for a *lo ta'aseh* — a negative commandment — where one receives lashes and the matter is concluded, and a *mitzvas aseh* — a positive commandment. For a positive commandment, the principle is that we apply continuing pressure, *makin oto*, not as punishment for the past transgression but to compel future compliance. The ban on the butchers of *Hutza* is therefore not a punitive measure that has run its course. It is an instrument of compulsion, and the fact that it has persisted for twenty-two years without yielding compliance is not a reason to rescind it.

The *gemara* identifies a secondary legal implication of the twenty-two-year duration: *d'kansinan lehu belo asraisa* — we may now fine them without prior warning. They have had twenty-two years of warning. If it is now decided to shift tactics and begin imposing fines, no fresh warning is required. The *gemara* cites examples: *Rava* fined a butcher an entire thigh (*atma*) for refusing to give *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva*, awarding a larger cut to the *kohanim* as the penalty. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* went further and confiscated a *glima* — a cloak — underscoring that the fine need not be taken from the meat itself but can come from whatever is effective.

## Gift Protocol

### Proper Eating

*Rav Chisda* states: *matnot kehuna einan ne'akhalot ela tzali* — the gifts of the *kehunah* should be eaten only roasted, *v'ein ne'echelet ela bechardal* — and only with mustard. This is particularly evocative with respect to the tongue, which was included among the gifts along with the rest of the jaw area, and which would naturally be served roasted with mustard. The *gemara* asks for the basis of this requirement and cites the verse *lemoshcha* — meaning *ligdula*, for greatness. *Kederech shehamelachim ochlim* — the *kohen* should eat the way kings eat. The giving of these gifts is not a charity transaction. It is an act of honor, recognizing the *kohen*'s status as the religious representative of the people, performing the *avodah* on their behalf. The manner of eating should reflect that.

This raises an apparent tension with the *gemara* in *Zevachim*, where the rule is that *kohanim* may prepare *kodshim* however they like — roasted, boiled, or cooked. *Tosafot* addresses this directly. The ruling there (*resha'in hakohanim leshanot ba'achila lochelan tzloyim shluchin umevushalin*) grants discretion to the *kohen* to eat as he prefers. *Tosafot* explains: if roasting is genuinely how he most enjoys his food, he fulfills the intent by eating it roasted. But if all preparations are equally satisfying to him — if he has no strong preference — then *yochal tzli shehu gedula yoter*: he should eat it roasted, since that is the socially recognized mode of refined eating. The requirement is not an absolute override of personal preference but a default toward the manner of eating that signals honor and status to the broader community.

### Knowledge Requirement

*Rav Chisda* adds: *kol kohen she'eino baki bahen ein notnin lo matanah* — a *kohen* who is not expert in the twenty-four *matnot kehunah* does not receive a gift. *Rashi* interprets this in light of the preceding teaching: *heich din matanah heich ne'echalim bigdula* — "expert in them" means knowing how to eat them properly, understanding the nature and significance of the gifts and receiving them in the manner of honor. The *gemara* thus presents a coherent picture in which giving and receiving are both governed by an ethos of respect: the *shochet* is the one obligated to give, but the *kohen* too must understand the context in which he is receiving. The institution is not a mechanical transfer of meat but a social practice laden with religious meaning.

### Avodah Requirement

The *gemara*, however, immediately rejects this as the operative ruling. *V'lav milta hi* — that is not the *halacha*. In its place, a *baraita* is cited. *Rabbi Shimon* teaches: *kol kohen she'eino modeh ba'avodah ein lo chelek bakehunah* — any *kohen* who does not accept the *avodah*, who disclaims responsibility for or denies the legitimacy of the Temple service, has no portion in the *kehunah* and receives none of the gifts. The verse is cited: *hamakriv et dam hashelamim ve'et hachelev mivnei Aharon lo tihyeh shok hayamin lemana* — the one who offers the blood and the fat receives the thigh as his portion. From this the *gemara* derives that participation in and acceptance of the *avodah* is a prerequisite for receiving any priestly portion.

### Fifteen Services

The *baraita* then asks how we know this principle extends to all the *avodot* beyond the specific one mentioned in the verse. The answer is derived from the phrase *mivnei Aharon* — the service entrusted to the children of *Aharon*. Any *kohen* who does not accept his responsibility for the full range of that service has no portion. The *baraita* enumerates fifteen *avodot*: the *yetzikot* and *belilot* (the pouring and mixing of oil on *menachot*), the *ptitot* (breaking *menachot* into pieces), the *melichot* (salting the *korbanot*), *tnufot* and *hagashot* (waving and presenting to the altar), *kmitzot* (taking the handful), *ketoret* (burning incense or the handful on the altar), *melikot* (the *melika* for a bird), *mitzui hadam* (squeezing the blood of a bird), *kabalot* (catching the blood of an animal), *haza'ot* (throwing the blood), *hashka'at sotah* (administering the *sotah* waters), *arifat eglah* (breaking the neck of the calf in the *eglah arufah* ceremony), *taharat metzora* (the purification of the *metzora*), and *nesiat kapayim* (the lifting of the hands in the priestly blessing — *bein mibifnim bein mibachutz*, whether in the *Beit HaMikdash* or in the *beit hakeneset*).

The *gemara* draws a pointed conclusion from this. The original attractive idea — that you must know *how* to eat the gifts in a dignified manner — was rejected in favor of something more fundamental: you must accept the *chiyuv* of the *avodah* itself. *Ta'ama d'eino modeh bah* — the disqualification is because you reject the obligation. *Hamodeh bah*, af al gav *d'eino baki bahen* — if you accept it, even if you are not an expert in the details of the gifts, you are still entitled to receive them. The point is acceptance of responsibility, not technical mastery.

## Gift Mechanics

### Grabbing vs. Receiving

*Rav Yosef* posed a question to his students: *hai kahana d'chatif matnata* — a *kohen* who grabs the gift — is this an expression of *chivuv mitzvah*, eagerness to perform the *mitzvah*, or is it *mezalzel b'mitzvah*, a denigration of the *mitzvah*? The framing captures a genuine ambiguity. One could argue that running to grab the gift reflects zeal for the religious obligation. But there is an equally plausible reading: grabbing is undignified and undermines the very context of honor that should surround the giving and receiving of *matanot kehunah*.

The students resolved the question from a *pasuk*: *v'natan* — "and he shall give" to the *kohen* — *v'lo she-yitol me-atzmo*, which implies the *kohen* should not take it for himself. The verse specifies an act of giving by the *shochet* and receiving by the *kohen*, not a seizure. This both resolves the technical question — grabbing is impermissible — and reinforces the broader social meaning of the transaction: the giving of the gift is a relational act between two parties, not a unilateral appropriation.

### Abaye's Evolution

*Abaye*, himself a *kohen*, offers an unusually candid account of his own evolving practice. Initially, he would grab the *matanot* — *me-reisha hava chatifna matnasa* — telling himself *amina chivuvei ka-mechaveivna mitzvah*, that he was expressing love for the *mitzvah* by rushing to perform it. Once he heard the *drasha* on *v'natan v'lo she-yitol me-atzmo*, he stopped grabbing. Instead, *meimar amri havu li* — he would verbally request the gifts from the butcher, reasoning that at least he was not physically seizing them and was still expressing his desire to participate in the *mitzvah*.

### Shemuel's Sons

Then *Abaye* heard a *baraita* citing *Rabbi Meir* on the sons of *Shmuel*. The verse says *vayitu acharei habatza* — "and they turned after gain." *Rabbi Meir* explains: *bnei Shmuel chelkom sha'alu b'fihem* — the sons of *Shmuel* requested their portions verbally. The *gemara* operates with the assumption that *Shmuel* was a *levi*, and his sons would accordingly be entitled to their *matnos Levi'ah*. Yet their practice of asking for what was owed them is interpreted as "turning after gain" — pursuing material benefit. Even verbally requesting one's legitimate portion, rather than waiting for it to be offered, is read as a kind of avarice, since it signals that the desire for the material benefit is the dominant motivation. *Meimar nami lo amina* — *Abaye* therefore stopped asking as well. However, *i yahavu li* — if a butcher were to come and offer him the gifts spontaneously, *shaklina* — he would of course accept.

### Yom Kippur Exception

Even this final posture — accepting only when freely offered — was then called into question. *Abaye* heard a *baraita* about the *lechem hapanim* toward the end of the Temple period, when there was insufficient bread to go around among all the *kohanim*. The *tznu'im*, the modest ones, would step back and decline to take; the *gargaranim*, the gluttonous ones, would divide it up among themselves. *Tosafot* frames this in terms of deference to others with greater need: even to simply receive when other *kohanim* are present and may need it more is not the ideal posture. Therefore, *mishkal nami lo shaklina* — *Abaye* also stopped accepting the gifts.

There was, however, one exception: *levar mi-malei yoma d'kippurai* — except for *Erev Yom Kippur*. Why? *Lachazukei nafshai bi-khahuna* — to establish publicly that he is a *kohen*. On *Erev Yom Kippur*, when everyone is slaughtering and eating meat, all the *kohanim* are receiving their portions. If *Abaye* were the only one not receiving, people would assume he is not a *kohen* at all. To avoid that false impression — *v'illu lo hayah notel hayu omerim she'eino Kohen*, as *Tosafot* states — he would take on that occasion. The *gemara* then asks: why not instead do *birkat kohanim* to publicly establish his identity as a *kohen*? The answer is *anseiah leh idna* — his schedule prevented him from regularly attending *shul*. Whether due to illness, as some explain, or because his teaching obligations conflicted with the *minyan* time, as *Rashi* understands it, *Abaye* did not have a reliable opportunity to perform *nesiat kapayim*.

## Teaching & Transfers

### Transfer to Student

*Rav Yosef* rules: *hai kahana d'it leh tzurba merabbanan b'shivvavuteih v'dachika leh milta* — if a *kohen* has a *talmid chacham* in his neighborhood who is suffering financial hardship, *lizkei leh matnasa* — he should transfer his *matanot* to the *talmid chacham*. This is straightforward once the *kohen* has received the gifts; what is novel here is that the transfer can be effected even before the gifts come into the *kohen*'s physical possession.

How? The institution of *makirei kehunah u'leviya* makes this possible. When a *kohen* is an established recipient — when it is well known that a particular family consistently gives to him — his ownership of the forthcoming gifts is considered sufficiently certain that even before *shechitah* he can transfer that entitlement to another. Whether this operates as a form of *mechiras davar shelo ba l'olam* turns on a broader discussion in *Gittin* about whether one can sell something not yet in one's possession. The comparison is drawn to a case where a commodity is reliably available in the market: if I know I can obtain it immediately, there is an argument that a pre-sale is valid. Similarly, where the gifts will predictably be given to this *kohen*, the transfer in advance has legal effect.

### Rava & Safra Story

*Rava* and *Rav Safra* once came to the home of *Mar Yohana*, the son of *Rav Chana bar Adda* (or, in an alternative version, *Rav Chana bar Bizna*), who prepared for them an *egla tilsa* — a prized young calf. There is some dispute as to what *tilsa* means precisely: one interpretation is a third-grown calf, tender and young; another is the third calf born to the cow; a third understands it as three years old, though this last reading is flagged by *Jastrow* as incorrect. Whatever the precise meaning, it was understood as a special and honored dish.

*Mar Yohana*'s attendant was a *kohen*, and it was known that the master of the house customarily gave the *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva* to this attendant. *Rava* said to him: *zachi lan matnasa* — please transfer your gifts to us, *d'beina l'meichal lishna b'chardala* — I would like to eat the tongue in mustard. The attendant complied. *Rava* ate; *Rav Safra* declined to eat from the gift.

That night, the following verse was read to *Rav Safra* in a dream: *maade beged beyom karah*, *chometz al neter*, *v'shar beshirim al lev ra* — a worn cloak on a cold day, vinegar on mineral, singing to someone with a wicked heart. The implication seemed to be a rebuke for refusing to act on a valid teaching.

*Rav Safra* came before *Rav Yosef* and asked whether this was because he had gone against *Rav Yosef*'s ruling that a *kohen* may transfer *matanot* to a *talmid chacham*. *Rav Yosef* clarified: the ruling applies to an independent *kohen* who acts of his own free will — *b'acher, shama*. In the case of the attendant, however, *ba'al korcho masar* — he had no real choice. He was a servant in the household; when an honored guest like *Rava* asks him to transfer his portion, he cannot refuse. Such a transfer under duress is not what *Rav Yosef* had in mind. Moreover, *Rav Yosef* added: even the legitimate case applies only when the *talmid chacham* genuinely lacks means — *l'man d'lo efshar leih*. *Ha efshar leih* — but *Rav Safra* had means, so the transfer should never have been solicited from him in the first place.

The dream, then, was not directed at *Rav Safra*. It was meant for *Rava*, who had compelled the attendant and had sought a portion for a colleague who did not need it. *V'likriun l'Rava* — so why was it read to *Rav Safra* rather than to *Rava*? *Rava nazuf hava* — *Rava* was in a state of divine displeasure; heaven did not wish to engage with him directly. The verse was therefore delivered to *Rav Safra*, the intermediary. This precise dynamic is captured by the Yiddish expression: *der tochter zog ich, di shner mein ich* — I am speaking to my daughter, but I mean my daughter-in-law. Unable to address *Rava* directly, the rebuke reached him through *Rav Safra*.

### Yosef's Clarification

*Rav Yosef*'s clarification thus draws sharp parameters around the legitimate use of the transfer mechanism. It requires: a *kohen* who is an independent agent and gives willingly; a *talmid chacham* who is genuinely in need; and a genuine gift relationship — *makirei kehunah* — that gives legal force to the pre-*shechitah* transfer. Absent these conditions, the mechanism is inapplicable or worse — an abuse.

### Unworthy Students

The *gemara* then asks *Abaye* what the simple *peshat* of the verse that was read to *Rav Safra* is, and *Rav Dimi* answers: *b'shoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun* — it refers to one who teaches a student of bad character. This launches the *gemara* into a sharp discussion about the dangers of teaching Torah to an unworthy student.

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: *kol hashoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun nofel b'Gehinnom* — one who teaches an unworthy student will fall into *Gehinnom*. The danger is not merely that the Torah will be wasted, but that it will be weaponized. Teaching Torah to someone of genuinely bad character gives that person tools to undermine the Torah from within. The verse cited — *kol choshech tamun litzfunav tochlehu esh lo nupach yera sarid b'ohalo* — is interpreted with the phrase *eil sarid ela talmid chacham*. The *Maharsha* objects to reading *sarid* as the unworthy student; one would not call such a person a *talmid chacham*. He therefore reads the verse as referring to the *talmid chacham* doing the teaching: *yera b'ohalo* — it will be evil in his tent if he teaches such a person.

*Rav Zeira* in the name of *Rav* adds: *kol hashoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun k'zorek even l'markulis* — it is like throwing a stone at the *markulis*, the idol. The metaphor is explained by the *Maharsha*: throwing a stone at the *markulis* appears to be a hostile or denigrating act, but in fact it constitutes a form of worship of that idol. Similarly, teaching the unworthy student looks like the dissemination of Torah, but in reality it provides ammunition to those who will use it destructively. The verse cited: *k'tzror even b'margemah kein nosein l'ksil kavod* — like binding a stone in a sling is giving honor to the fool.

The *gemara* then resolves this with the general principle of *kol ha'osek b'Torah shelo lishmah* — that learning Torah not for its own sake can eventually lead to learning *lishmah*. *Tosafot* elsewhere distinguishes between two categories: one who comes to learning with fundamentally redeemable character, in whom the process of learning will itself effect transformation, and one who comes with deliberate malicious intent to use Torah destructively. It is the second category — clearly of bad character and bent on abuse — toward which these severe warnings are directed. The gray areas remain, and the tradition maintains the tension between an open approach that hopes learning will elevate and a more guarded approach that recognizes the dangers of arming those who will cause harm.

## Ownership Shares

### Marking Rule

The *mishnah* states: *hamishtatef imahem tzarich lirshom* — one who enters into a business partnership with a *kohen* or non-Jew must mark the animal. Since the partnership arrangement exempts the *shochet* from giving *matanot*, without a visible marker people observing the butcher not giving the gifts would be puzzled or misled. The mark communicates the reason for the exemption.

### Marking Distinctions

The *gemara* introduces a tension from a *baraita*: *hamishtatef im kohen tzarich lirshom* — partnership with a *kohen* requires marking, but *hamishtatef im ovdei kochavim u'psulei mukdashin* — partnership with a non-Jew, or animals that were once *hekdesh* and have been redeemed (*psulei mukdashin*), does not require marking. Why should there be a difference?

After several attempts to explain the distinction by appeal to various observable circumstances — whether the non-Jew is sitting visibly in the shop, whether he is counting the money, whether he is simply present at the counter — the *gemara* settles on a more pointed explanation. When the non-Jew is sitting at the counter, the *gemara* initially suggests he might be mistaken for a customer. But could people not equally mistake the *kohen* for a customer? The answer turns on trust: *ein heimuna b'oved kochavim*. *Rashi* is careful to soften this: *ein Yisrael regilim leha'amin bahem* — Jews are not accustomed to trusting non-Jews. It is not an evaluative claim about non-Jewish character, but a descriptive sociological observation: in that world, Jews did not typically entrust their business finances to non-Jews. Therefore, if a non-Jew is seen managing the money box, people immediately understand he must be a genuine partner, and no mark is needed. By contrast, when a *kohen* sits at the money box, people might assume the butcher simply trusts him — not that he is a legal partner — hence the need for a mark.

A second resolution is also offered: *stam oved kochavim mefap-peit* — the non-Jew partner will simply talk too much and be too visibly directive. If a non-Jew is sitting in the shop giving instructions about which piece to sell and at what price, it will be unmistakably clear to everyone that he is a business partner. No marking is required because the partnership is self-evident.

For *psulei mukdashin*, the *gemara* ultimately explains that the exemption-without-marking applies specifically to those animals — such as a *bechor* with a *mum* or an animal *ma'aser* — that must by law be sold from the home rather than in the marketplace, and weighed by the unit rather than by the pound. That distinctive mode of sale makes the animal's prior *hekdesh* status apparent to anyone who sees it. Where *psulei mukdashin* are sold in the ordinary way, in the marketplace and by weight (*yikru bahen nishchatin b'itliz v'nishkalin b'litra*), no one would know the animal's history, and a mark would be required. The resolution — *targema Rav Adda bar Ahava kammai d'Rav Pappa b'ozen hen nimkarin betoch habayis* — limits the "no marking needed" rule precisely to those cases where the manner of sale already broadcasts the animal's status.

### Huna's Rule

*Rav Huna* rules: *shutfut berosh patur min halechi* — if the *kohen* is a partner in the head, you are exempt from the cheek area. *Shutfut beyad patur min hazroa* — if he is a partner in the arm, you are exempt from the *zero'a*. *Shutfut bivnei meayim patur min hakeivah* — if he is a partner in the intestines, you are exempt from the *keivah*. The operative principle is that the *kohen*'s ownership must extend to the actual body part from which the gift is taken. Partial ownership of the rest of the animal does not suffice; the partnership must encompass the specific gift portion itself.

### Chiya's Rule

*Chiya bar Rav* stakes out a more expansive position: *afilu shutfut be'achat mehen patur mikulan* — even if the *kohen* is a partner in only one of the three gifts, he is exempt from all of them. On this view, the three gifts — *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* — form a single halachic unit. Partial ownership in any one of them generates a blanket exemption from all three.

### Specific Exemption

The *gemara* challenges *Chiya bar Rav* from a *baraita*. The *baraita* distinguishes explicitly: *harosh sheli v'chulah shelcha* — if the *kohen* retains the head, even *afilu echad mimeah berosh* — even one percent of the head — *patur min halechi* — you are exempt from the jaw portion — *v'chayav bekulan* — but obligated in the remaining two gifts. Similarly, ownership only of the hand exempts only from the *zero'a* but not the others; ownership only of the intestines exempts only from the *keivah* but not the others. *Tiyuvta d'Chiya bar Rav tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation. The *halacha* is that the exemption is specific to the gift for which the *kohen* holds a portion.

*Rav Chisda* explains what led *Chiya bar Rav* to his erroneous position: *ha matnitin atisya l'Chiya bar Rav* — it was the following *baraita* that caused him to err. That *baraita* enumerates the twenty-four *matnot kehunah*, and in its enumeration counts *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* as a single unit. *Chiya bar Rav* inferred from this that the three gifts are treated as one halachic entity. But the inference is mistaken. The same *baraita* counts the gift from the *todah* and the gift from the ram of a *nazir* as one unit (*muram mitodah v'eil nazir*), even though those are quite different: the *todah* involves the *chazeh* and *shok* with loaves of bread, while the *nazir* involves the *zero'a*. They are plainly distinct gifts. The reason they are counted as one is simply that they are similar in kind — both are *kodshim kalim* in which the *kohen* receives a part — *damyan lehadadi*, and so they are grouped together for the purposes of counting. The same is true of *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah*: they are similar to one another and grouped together for counting purposes, but that grouping carries no halachic implication that they form a unified entity for purposes of the exemption.

## 24 Matanot List

### 10 in Temple

The *baraita* enumerates the twenty-four *matnot kehunah* in three geographical categories. The first group, *eser bamikdash* — ten gifts given in the *Beit HaMikdash* and eaten there — comprises: the meat of the *chatat*; the meat of the *chatat ha'of*, the bird *chatat*; the *asham*, including both *asham vadai* (for a definite sin) and *asham taluy* (for a doubtful sin); *zivchei shalmei tzibur*, the communal *shlamim* brought on *Shavuot*; *log shemen shel metzora*, the remaining oil from the *metzora*'s purification; *shtei halechem*, the two loaves of *Shavuot*; *lechem hapanim*, the twelve loaves on the *shulchan*; *she'ar haminchot*, the remainder of individual *menachot*; and *minchat ha'omer*, which is the communal *mincha* and is listed separately for that reason.

### 4 in Jerusalem

The second group, *arba b'Yerushalayim* — four gifts eaten throughout all of *Yerushalayim* — consists of: *habechorah*, the firstborn animal; *habikkurim*, the first fruits (which are brought to the *Beit HaMikdash* but may be eaten throughout *Yerushalayim*); *muram mitodah u'me'eil nazir*, the priestly portions from the *todah* offering and the ram of the *nazir*, which are *kodshim kalim* and may be eaten anywhere in *Yerushalayim*; and *orot kodshim*, the hides of sacrificial animals, including the hide of the *olah*, which are given to the *kohanim* and may be used throughout *Yerushalayim*.

### 10 Throughout Israel

The third group, *eser bigvulin* — ten gifts distributed throughout all of *Eretz Yisrael* — includes: *terumah*; *terumat ma'aser* (the *terumah* separated by the *levi* from his *ma'aser*); *challah*; *reishit hagez*, the first shearings; *matanot* — meaning *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah*, counted here as one; *pidyon haben*, the redemption of the firstborn son; *pidyon peter chamor*, the redemption of the firstborn donkey; *sdei achuzah u'sdei charamim*, certain dedicated fields that revert to the *kohanim* at *yovel* or at other applicable times; and *gezel hager*, the restitution for theft from a convert who dies without heirs.

### Counting Convention

The fact that *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* are listed as a single entry in this enumeration, despite being three distinct gifts, was what misled *Chiya bar Rav* into treating them as a single halachic unit. The *gemara* makes clear that this grouping is a counting convenience, not a substantive legal merger. The comparison to *muram mitodah v'eil nazir* — which are counted as one despite being structurally distinct — establishes that the counting convention reflects similarity of category rather than halachic identity. *Kivan d'damyan lehadadi chashiv lehu kechada* — since they resemble one another, they are counted as one; but for the purpose of determining the exemption through partnership, each gift stands independently. There is also an aesthetic dimension to the enumeration: the ten-four-ten structure is symmetrical and easy to remember, and achieving that symmetry required counting the three *matanot* as one unit. The grouping serves the purposes of enumeration and memorization, not legal derivation.
