# Hullin 133 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Exemption Framework

### Shochet & Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט לַכֹּהֵן וְלַגּוֹי, פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת. וְלִיתְנֵי: כֹּהֵן וְגוֹי פְּטוּרִין מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת? אָמַר רָבָא: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַדִּין עִם הַטַּבָּח.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one who slaughters** the animal of a priest **for the priest or** the animal of a gentile **for the gentile is exempt from** the obligation to give **the gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw. The Gemara suggests: **And let** the *tanna* simply **teach** that **a priest and a gentile are exempt from** the obligation to give **the gifts. Rava says** in explanation: **That is to say,** i.e., the wording of the mishna indicates, that **the demand** of a priest who seeks to claim gifts of the priesthood is **with the butcher,** not with the owner of the animal. Even if the butcher is himself a priest, if he slaughters an animal on behalf of an Israelite he is obligated to give the gifts.

The *mishnah* at the top of 132b opens with the ruling *hashochet l'Kohen ul'oved kochavim patur min hamatanot*: one who slaughters for a *kohen* or a non-Jew is exempt from the *matanot*. The *mishnah*'s phrasing, which focuses on the *shochet* rather than the owner, raises an immediate structural question: why frame the law around the act of slaughtering rather than around the ownership of the animal? After all, it is the ownership — or, more precisely, for whom the animal is being slaughtered — that determines whether an obligation exists.

The *gemara* navigates a subtle distinction here. The determination of *chiyuv* or *p'tur* turns on who owns the animal, or at least for whom the *shechitah* is being performed. Yet the person with whom the *kohen* deals to collect his gifts is the *shochet*, not necessarily the owner. One could well imagine a case where a *yisrael* owns a cow and hires a *shochet* to slaughter it: the obligation to give the gifts falls on the *shochet*, even though the *shochet* does not own the cow. Conversely, if the cow belongs to a *kohen* and the *shochet* is a *yisrael*, the *shochet* is exempt. The ownership of the animal — or the identity of the party for whom the slaughter is performed — governs the question of obligation, while the *shochet* is the relevant legal actor for purposes of fulfilling or defaulting on that obligation.

One might ask whether the question is truly about ownership at the moment of *shechitah* or about a more functional notion of "for whom" the animal is being slaughtered. The *gemara* never fully resolves this distinction, but working assumption throughout is that ownership at the moment of *shechitah* is the operative criterion.

### Rava's Exposition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: ״מֵאֵת הָעָם״ – וְלֹא מֵאֵת הַכֹּהֲנִים, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מֵאֵת זוֹבְחֵי הַזֶּבַח״ – הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר אֲפִילּוּ טַבָּח כֹּהֵן בַּמַּשְׁמָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Rava** also **interpreted** the verse in such a manner. The verse states: "This shall be the priests' due from the people, from them that perform a slaughter, whether it be ox or sheep, that they shall give to the priest the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw" (Deuteronomy 18:3). The verse specifies that the gifts are taken **"from the people," and not from the priests. When** the verse **states: "From them that perform a slaughter,"** indicating that the gifts are given by anyone who slaughters an animal, **you must say** that this teaches that **even a butcher** who is **a priest is included** in the obligation to give the gifts.

The *gemara* asks pointedly: why does the *mishnah* say "one who slaughters *for* a *kohen*" rather than simply "a *kohen* and a non-Jew are exempt"? *Rava* answers: *zos omeres hadini im hatabach* — this teaches us that the legal relationship is with the butcher, the *shochet*. The *mishnah*'s formulation is deliberate. Had it stated that the *kohen* is exempt, one might have concluded that you pursue the owner of the cow. By framing it as "one who slaughters for a *kohen*," the *mishnah* locates the obligation squarely with the *shochet*.

*Rava* further derives this from a *derasha*. The verse states *me'eis ha'am* — "from the people" — excluding *kohanim*: a *kohen* need not give these gifts to a fellow *kohen*. What determines the obligation is for whom the animal belongs, not who performs the slaughter. As the verse adds, *me'izovchei hazevach* — "from those who slaughter the slaughtering" — this language encompasses even a *kohen* who serves as a butcher. The irony is precise: the determination of obligation depends on the ownership of the animal, but the person obligated is always the *shochet*.

## Exemption Framework…

### Partnership Strategy

This framework naturally gives rise to a strategy. If ownership by a *kohen* exempts the *shochet* from giving *matanot*, what if a *kohen* becomes a business partner in the butchery operation? Insofar as the *kohen* holds a share of the animals being slaughtered, those animals are — at least partially — being slaughtered for a *kohen*, and the *shochet* might claim exemption on that basis.

## Rav Tavla's Plan

### Partnership Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּשְׁפִּיזְכָנֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי טַבְלָא כֹּהֵן הֲוָה, וַהֲוָה דְּחִיק לֵיהּ מִלְּתָא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי טַבְלָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אִישְׁתַּתַּף בַּהֲדֵי טַבָּחֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, דְּמִגּוֹ דְּמִפַּטְרִי מִמַּתְּנָתָא, מִשְׁתַּתְּפִי בַּהֲדָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that the obligation to give the gifts of the priesthood does not apply to an animal jointly owned by an Israelite and a priest. The Gemara relates that the **host [*ushpizikhnei*] of Rabbi Tavla was a priest and he was hard-pressed** for money. **He came before Rabbi Tavla** to ask for advice. Rabbi Tavla **said to him: Go** and **enter into a partnership with those Israelite butchers,** to obtain part ownership of their animals, **as since they** will be **exempt from** the obligation to give the **gifts** on account of this partnership, they will agree to **enter into** a business **partnership with you** free of charge.

The *gemara* presents a concrete case. *Rav Tavla* had a guest — a *kohen* — who was suffering financial hardship (*dachik lei milsa*). *Rav Tavla* advised him: *zil ishtatef bahadi tavchei Yisrael* — go and enter into a partnership with the Jewish butchers. The logic is that since you will be a partner with them, and they will thereby be slaughtering partly for a *kohen*, they will be exempt from giving the *matanot* (*d'migo d'miptari mimatnasa mishtatfi bahadach*). In exchange, the *kohen* receives a percentage of the business's profits — a more reliable income stream than occasionally collecting *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva* and waiting in line behind other *kohanim*.

The implicit assumption is that these butchers own the animals they slaughter — they purchase livestock, slaughter it, and sell the meat — so that at the moment of *shechitah* the animal is theirs, and the partnership with the *kohen* means part of it is being slaughtered on behalf of a *kohen*-owner. Even though the meat is immediately destined for sale to the general public, the ownership at the moment of *shechitah* appears to be what matters.

### Nachman's Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַיְּיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא רַבִּי טַבְלָא פַּטְרַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַפֵּיק, וְאִי לָא – מַפֵּיקְנָא לָךְ רַבִּי טַבְלָא מֵאוּנָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The priest heeded the advice of Rabbi Tavla and entered into a partnership with an Israelite butcher. Nevertheless, **Rav Naḥman obligated** the butcher to give the gifts of the priesthood from the animals he slaughtered. The priest **said to** Rav Naḥman: **But Rabbi Tavla exempted us** from this obligation. Rav Naḥman **said to him: Go remove** the gifts of the priesthood that are in your possession and give them to a priest, **and if** you will **not** do so, **I will remove Rabbi Tavla from your ear [*me'unakh*],** i.e., I will refute his basis for deeming you exempt.

*Rav Nachman* rejected the arrangement. When the butcher protested — *v'ha Rav Tavla patran*, "but *Rav Tavla* exempted us!" — *Rav Nachman* replied sharply: *zil afik*, go and give the gifts, *v'ei lo mafkina lach Rav Tavla m'oneich* — if you do not, I will remove *Rav Tavla* from your ear. The dismissiveness of the phrase is telling. *Rav Nachman* was not persuaded by the authority of *Rav Tavla*'s ruling and enforced the giving of *matanot* regardless.

*Rav Tavla* then came before *Rav Nachman* to ask why he had done this. *Rav Nachman* explained by citing a tradition brought by *Rav Acha bar Chanina* from the south, in the name of *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi*.

### South Elders' Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכִי אֲתָא רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא מִדָּרוֹמָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, זִקְנֵי דָרוֹם אָמְרוּ: כֹּהֵן טַבָּח – שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ שַׁבָּתוֹת פָּטוּר מִן הַמַּתָּנוֹת, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ חַיָּיב בְּמַתָּנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** I ruled in this manner, **as when Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina of the south came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he **said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** and all **the elders of the south said:** With regard to **a priest** who becomes **a butcher,** for the first **two or three weeks** he is **exempt from** the obligation to give **the gifts,** as he has not yet established himself in the community as a butcher. But **from this** point **forward** he is **obligated to** give the **gifts,** as he is now known as a butcher.

*Ziknei darom* — the elders of the south — ruled as follows: *Kohen tabach* — if a *kohen* is himself a butcher, meaning he owns the animals and is slaughtering them for sale, he is technically exempt from giving *matanot*, since at the moment of *shechitah* the animals are his. Nevertheless, *shtayim v'shalosh shabbatos patur min hamatnos* — for two or three weeks this exemption holds. *Mikan v'eilach chayav b'matnos* — from that point onward, he becomes obligated.

The reasoning is implicit but important. Technically, as long as the *kohen* owns the meat at the moment of *shechitah*, even with the intention of immediately reselling it to the public, he is exempt. The moment of slaughter is what matters, not the ultimate destination of the meat. However, once it becomes established that the *kohen* is operating as a butcher for the broader community — and that is what his business consists of — it is no longer appropriate or legitimate to claim the exemption. There is no biblical basis for the two-to-three week grace period, as *Tosafot* notes; it must be a rabbinic ruling. But the principle it reflects is that the exemption cannot serve as a vehicle for permanently avoiding the institution of *matanot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלַעֲבֵיד לֵיהּ מָר מִיהַת כְּרַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּלָא קְבַע מְסַחְתָּא, אֲבָל הָכָא הָא קְבַע מְסַחְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Tavla **said to** Rav Naḥman: **And let the Master at least do for** the priest **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina** and exempt him from giving the gifts for the first three weeks of his partnership. Rav Naḥman **said to him: This statement** of Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina applies only when the priest **did not** immediately **establish a butcher shop.** In such a case, the priest is exempt until he becomes known as a recognized butcher. **But here, he has** already **established a butcher shop** and is therefore obligated to give the gifts without delay.

## System Protection

### Tosafot's Concern

*Tosafot* explicitly articulates the broader concern underlying *Rav Nachman*'s ruling. The worry is not merely about the individual *kohen* who happens to be a butcher, but about the systemic abuse of the exemption. As *Tosafot* states: *shelo yargilu tibchei Yisrael leshatef kohanim imahem lehipater min hamatnot* — we do not want Jewish butchers to make it a practice to partner with *kohanim* as a mechanism for evading *matanot* altogether. For the first two or three weeks, one cannot be certain whether the arrangement is a deliberate evasion or a genuine situation. After that point, the intent is clear, and the exemption is disallowed.

*Rav Nachman* added a further refinement to the storefront test. He explained: *hani mili d'lo kavia michtata* — this leniency applies only where the person does not have an established storefront. *Aval heicha d'ka-hacha kavia michtata* — but where there is a visible, fixed business operation, allowing the partnership exemption would obviously subvert the entire institution of *matanot*, since every butcher would immediately obtain a *kohen* as a nominal partner. The storefront is the dispositive marker: when the commercial operation is unmistakably running as a community-serving business, no partnership exemption is available.

### Chisda's Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי כָּהֲנָא דְּלָא מַפְרֵישׁ מַתְּנָתָא – לֶיהֱוֵי בְּשַׁמְתָּא דֶּאֱלָהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא: הָנֵי טַבָּחֵי דְּהוּצָל קָיְימִי בְּשַׁמְתָּא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא הָא עֶשְׂרִים וְתַרְתֵּי שְׁנִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **a priest who** slaughters an animal and **does not separate gifts** of the priesthood from them for another priest, **let him be** under the **excommunication of the God of Israel. Rabba bar Rav Sheila said: These butchers of** the city of **Huzal** have **remained** under the **excommunication of Rav Ḥisda these** last **twenty-two years,** as they have continuously refused to separate gifts of the priesthood for this period.

*Rav Chisda* declared: *hai kahana d'lo mafrish matnata lei havei beshamta le-Elohei Yisrael* — a *kohen* who does not separate off the *matanot* is under a *ban* in the name of God. The phrase *beshamta le-Elohei Yisrael* is striking. The concern here, as clarified by the *Rif* and the *Rosh* cited in the commentaries, is the case of a *kohen* acting as a butcher where the animals are not owned by a *kohen* — and yet, because the *shochet* himself is a *kohen*, he declines to give the gifts. Against this, *Rav Chisda* imposes the *shamta*.

*Rava bar Rav Shila* then reports that the butchers of *Hutza* have been under *Rav Chisda*'s ban for twenty-two years.

### Compliance Not Punishment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּתוּ לָא מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לְהוּ – וְהָא תַּנְיָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּמִצְוַת לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה, אֲבָל בְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה, כְּגוֹן אוֹמְרִים לוֹ ״עֲשֵׂה סוּכָּה״ וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה, ״לוּלָב״ וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה, ״עֲשֵׂה צִיצִית״ וְאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה – מַכִּין אוֹתוֹ עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא נַפְשׁוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what *halakha*** did Rabba bar Rav Sheila state that the butchers of Huzal have been under excommunication for twenty-two years? **If we say that we do not excommunicate them** for a period **any longer** than twenty-two years, **but isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement,** that one is not excommunicated for committing a transgression, **said?** It is said **with regard to a prohibition,** for which one is liable to receive a relatively severe punishment, e.g., death or *karet*. **But with regard to** one who refuses to perform **a positive mitzva, e.g.,** the court **says to him: Perform** the mitzva of ***sukka*, and he does not do** so, or: Perform the mitzva of taking the ***lulav*, and he does not do** so, or: **Prepare ritual fringes** for your garments, **and he does not do** so, the court **strikes him** an unlimited number of times, even **until his soul departs.** Accordingly, the butchers of Huzal should remain under excommunication indefinitely until they separate the gifts.

One might read this statement as a critique of the ban's ineffectiveness, but the *gemara* draws a different conclusion. The question becomes: *le-mai hilchata* — what is the legal significance of this observation?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּקָנְסִינַן לְהוּ בְּלָא אַתְרַיְיתָא, כִּי הָא דְּרָבָא קָנֵיס אַטְמָא, רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק קָנֵיס גְּלִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabba bar Rav Sheila means **that** because the butchers of Huzal have refused to give the gifts for so many years, **we fine them** even **without forewarning.** There was a case **like this** of a person who refused to give the gifts of the priesthood to a priest, **where Rava fined** him by taking the entire **thigh** of his animal and giving it to a priest. Similarly, **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak fined** an individual who refused to give the gifts of the priesthood to a priest by taking his **cloak** and giving it to a priest.

The suggestion that we should now lift the ban since it has clearly not worked is rejected by a *baraita*. The *baraita* distinguishes between punishment for a *lo ta'aseh* — a negative commandment — where one receives lashes and the matter is concluded, and a *mitzvas aseh* — a positive commandment. For a positive commandment, the principle is that we apply continuing pressure, *makin oto*, not as punishment for the past transgression but to compel future compliance. The ban on the butchers of *Hutza* is therefore not a punitive measure that has run its course. It is an instrument of compulsion, and the fact that it has persisted for twenty-two years without yielding compliance is not a reason to rescind it.

The *gemara* identifies a secondary legal implication of the twenty-two-year duration: *d'kansinan lehu belo asraisa* — we may now fine them without prior warning. They have had twenty-two years of warning. If it is now decided to shift tactics and begin imposing fines, no fresh warning is required. The *gemara* cites examples: *Rava* fined a butcher an entire thigh (*atma*) for refusing to give *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva*, awarding a larger cut to the *kohanim* as the penalty. *Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* went further and confiscated a *glima* — a cloak — underscoring that the fine need not be taken from the meat itself but can come from whatever is effective.

## Gift Protocol

### Proper Eating

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה אֵין נֶאֱכָלוֹת אֶלָּא צָלִי, וְאֵין נֶאֱכָלוֹת אֶלָּא בְּחַרְדָּל. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״לְמׇשְׁחָה״ – לִגְדוּלָּה, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהַמְּלָכִים אוֹכְלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda says: Gifts of the priesthood may be consumed only** when they are **roasted, and they may be consumed only with mustard** seasoning. **What is the reason** for this *halakha*? The **verse states:** "And the Lord spoke to Aaron: And I, behold, I have given you the charge of My gifts; of all the consecrated items of the children of Israel to you have I given them for prominence, and to your sons, as an eternal portion" (Numbers 18:8). The term **"for prominence"** means that the portions were given to the priests as a mark **of greatness.** Accordingly, they should be eaten **in a manner that kings eat,** i.e., roasted and with mustard.

*Rav Chisda* states: *matnot kehuna einan ne'akhalot ela tzali* — the gifts of the *kehunah* should be eaten only roasted, *v'ein ne'echelet ela bechardal* — and only with mustard. This is particularly evocative with respect to the tongue, which was included among the gifts along with the rest of the jaw area, and which would naturally be served roasted with mustard. The *gemara* asks for the basis of this requirement and cites the verse *lemoshcha* — meaning *ligdula*, for greatness. *Kederech shehamelachim ochlim* — the *kohen* should eat the way kings eat. The giving of these gifts is not a charity transaction. It is an act of honor, recognizing the *kohen*'s status as the religious representative of the people, performing the *avodah* on their behalf. The manner of eating should reflect that.

This raises an apparent tension with the *gemara* in *Zevachim*, where the rule is that *kohanim* may prepare *kodshim* however they like — roasted, boiled, or cooked. *Tosafot* addresses this directly. The ruling there (*resha'in hakohanim leshanot ba'achila lochelan tzloyim shluchin umevushalin*) grants discretion to the *kohen* to eat as he prefers. *Tosafot* explains: if roasting is genuinely how he most enjoys his food, he fulfills the intent by eating it roasted. But if all preparations are equally satisfying to him — if he has no strong preference — then *yochal tzli shehu gedula yoter*: he should eat it roasted, since that is the socially recognized mode of refined eating. The requirement is not an absolute override of personal preference but a default toward the manner of eating that signals honor and status to the broader community.

### Knowledge Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה, כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בָּהֶן – אֵין נוֹתְנִין לוֹ מַתָּנָה. וְלָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹדֶה בָּעֲבוֹדָה – אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בַּכְּהוּנָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַמַּקְרִיב אֶת דַּם הַשְּׁלָמִים וְאֶת הַחֵלֶב מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן לוֹ תִהְיֶה שׁוֹק הַיָּמִין לְמָנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda says: One may not give a gift to any priest who is not an expert in** the *halakhot* pertaining to all **twenty-four gifts of the priesthood.** The Gemara notes: **But** this **is not correct, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says: Any priest who does not believe in** the validity of the Temple **service has no portion in** any of the gifts given to the **priesthood, as it is stated: "He among the sons of Aaron, who offers [*hamakriv*] the blood of the peace offerings, and the fat, shall have the right thigh for a portion"** (Leviticus 7:33). The word "*hamakriv*," which literally means: Who brings it close, indicates that only one who believes in the validity of conveying the blood to the altar is entitled to receive the right thigh of the offering, as only one who believes in the rite would perform it.

*Rav Chisda* adds: *kol kohen she'eino baki bahen ein notnin lo matanah* — a *kohen* who is not expert in the twenty-four *matnot kehunah* does not receive a gift. *Rashi* interprets this in light of the preceding teaching: *heich din matanah heich ne'echalim bigdula* — "expert in them" means knowing how to eat them properly, understanding the nature and significance of the gifts and receiving them in the manner of honor. The *gemara* thus presents a coherent picture in which giving and receiving are both governed by an ethos of respect: the *shochet* is the one obligated to give, but the *kohen* too must understand the context in which he is receiving. The institution is not a mechanical transfer of meat but a social practice laden with religious meaning.

### Avodah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זֶה בִּלְבַד, מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה עֲבוֹדוֹת, כְּגוֹן: הַיְּצִיקוֹת, וְהַבְּלִילוֹת, וְהַפְּתִיתוֹת, וְהַמְּלִיחוֹת, תְּנוּפוֹת, וְהַגָּשׁוֹת, [וְהַקְּמִיצוֹת], הַקְטָרוֹת, (וְהַמְּצִיּוֹת), [וְהַמְּלִיקוֹת],
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **I have** derived **only** that a priest does not have a share in the priestly gifts if he does not believe in **this** rite of conveying of the blood **alone. From where** do I derive **to include fifteen** additional sacrificial **rites, such as** the rites of a meal offering, i.e., **the pouring** of the oil **and the mixing** of the oil and the subsequent pouring of the oil; **and the crumbling** of meal offerings prepared in a shallow or deep pan or in an oven, whose handfuls are removed after they are baked and subsequently crumbled; **and the salting** of meal offerings (see Leviticus 2:13); and the **waving** of certain meal offerings; **and the bringing** of certain meal offerings to the southwestern corner of the altar before a handful is removed; **and the removal of the handful;** and **the burning** of offerings on the altar; **and the squeezing** of a bird offering to extract its blood; **and the pinching** of the nape of the neck of a bird offering;

The *gemara*, however, immediately rejects this as the operative ruling. *V'lav milta hi* — that is not the *halacha*. In its place, a *baraita* is cited. *Rabbi Shimon* teaches: *kol kohen she'eino modeh ba'avodah ein lo chelek bakehunah* — any *kohen* who does not accept the *avodah*, who disclaims responsibility for or denies the legitimacy of the Temple service, has no portion in the *kehunah* and receives none of the gifts. The verse is cited: *hamakriv et dam hashelamim ve'et hachelev mivnei Aharon lo tihyeh shok hayamin lemana* — the one who offers the blood and the fat receives the thigh as his portion. From this the *gemara* derives that participation in and acceptance of the *avodah* is a prerequisite for receiving any priestly portion.

### Fifteen Services

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַבָּלוֹת, הַזָּאוֹת, וְהַשְׁקָאַת סוֹטָה, וַעֲרִיפַת עֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְטׇהֳרַת מְצוֹרָע, וּנְשִׂיאוּת כַּפַּיִם – בֵּין מִבִּפְנִים בֵּין מִבַּחוּץ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מִבְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן״ – עֲבוֹדָה הָאֲמוּרָה לִבְנֵי אַהֲרֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **and** the **receiving** of the blood in a vessel; and its **sprinkling** upon the altar, each offering according to its *halakha*; **and** the **giving of water to a woman suspected by her husband of having been unfaithful [*sota*]; and** the ritual of **breaking a heifer's neck** in an untilled valley when a corpse is discovered and the murderer is unknown; **and** the **purification of a leper; and** the **lifting of the hands** for the Priestly Benediction, **whether inside or outside** the Temple; from where are these derived? **The verse states: "Among the sons of Aaron,"** indicating that this is the *halakha* with regard to any sacrificial **rite that is stated to the sons of Aaron.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל כֹּהֵן שֶׁאֵינוֹ מוֹדֶה בָּהּ – אֵין לוֹ חֵלֶק בַּכְּהוּנָּה. טַעְמָא דְּאֵינוֹ מוֹדֶה בָּהּ, הָא מוֹדֶה בָּהּ – אַף עַל גַּב דְּאֵינוֹ בָּקִי בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara explains the proof: The *baraita* teaches that **any priest who does not believe in** the validity of these rites **has no portion in the** gifts of the **priesthood.** It may be inferred that the **reason** the priest has no portion is **that he does not believe in it,** whereas if **he does believe in it, even though he is not an expert in its** *halakhot*, he receives a portion. This contradicts the opinion of Rav Ḥisda.

The *baraita* then asks how we know this principle extends to all the *avodot* beyond the specific one mentioned in the verse. The answer is derived from the phrase *mivnei Aharon* — the service entrusted to the children of *Aharon*. Any *kohen* who does not accept his responsibility for the full range of that service has no portion. The *baraita* enumerates fifteen *avodot*: the *yetzikot* and *belilot* (the pouring and mixing of oil on *menachot*), the *ptitot* (breaking *menachot* into pieces), the *melichot* (salting the *korbanot*), *tnufot* and *hagashot* (waving and presenting to the altar), *kmitzot* (taking the handful), *ketoret* (burning incense or the handful on the altar), *melikot* (the *melika* for a bird), *mitzui hadam* (squeezing the blood of a bird), *kabalot* (catching the blood of an animal), *haza'ot* (throwing the blood), *hashka'at sotah* (administering the *sotah* waters), *arifat eglah* (breaking the neck of the calf in the *eglah arufah* ceremony), *taharat metzora* (the purification of the *metzora*), and *nesiat kapayim* (the lifting of the hands in the priestly blessing — *bein mibifnim bein mibachutz*, whether in the *Beit HaMikdash* or in the *beit hakeneset*).

The *gemara* draws a pointed conclusion from this. The original attractive idea — that you must know *how* to eat the gifts in a dignified manner — was rejected in favor of something more fundamental: you must accept the *chiyuv* of the *avodah* itself. *Ta'ama d'eino modeh bah* — the disqualification is because you reject the obligation. *Hamodeh bah*, af al gav *d'eino baki bahen* — if you accept it, even if you are not an expert in the details of the gifts, you are still entitled to receive them. The point is acceptance of responsibility, not technical mastery.

## Gift Mechanics

### Grabbing vs. Receiving

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: בָּדַק לַן רַב יוֹסֵף, הַאי כָּהֲנָא דְּחָטֵיף מַתְּנָתָא – חַבּוֹבֵי קָא מְחַבֵּב מִצְוָה, אוֹ זַלְזוֹלֵי קָא מְזַלְזֵל בְּמִצְוָה? וּפְשַׁטְנָא לֵיהּ ״וְנָתַן״ – וְלֹא שֶׁיִּטּוֹל מֵעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the manner in which a priest takes gifts of the priesthood from their owners, **Rava said: Rav Yosef examined us,** his students, with the following question: **A priest who seizes gifts** of the priesthood from their owners, is **he demonstrating fondness** for the **mitzva or is he demonstrating contempt for the mitzva? And I resolved** this question **for him** from the verse: "That they shall give to the priest the foreleg, and the jaw, and the maw" (Deuteronomy 18:3). The term **"that they shall give"** indicates that the owner should give the gifts, **and not that** a priest **should take them by himself.** Accordingly, a priest who seizes the gifts from their owners is demonstrating contempt for the mitzva.

*Rav Yosef* posed a question to his students: *hai kahana d'chatif matnata* — a *kohen* who grabs the gift — is this an expression of *chivuv mitzvah*, eagerness to perform the *mitzvah*, or is it *mezalzel b'mitzvah*, a denigration of the *mitzvah*? The framing captures a genuine ambiguity. One could argue that running to grab the gift reflects zeal for the religious obligation. But there is an equally plausible reading: grabbing is undignified and undermines the very context of honor that should surround the giving and receiving of *matanot kehunah*.

The students resolved the question from a *pasuk*: *v'natan* — "and he shall give" to the *kohen* — *v'lo she-yitol me-atzmo*, which implies the *kohen* should not take it for himself. The verse specifies an act of giving by the *shochet* and receiving by the *kohen*, not a seizure. This both resolves the technical question — grabbing is impermissible — and reinforces the broader social meaning of the transaction: the giving of the gift is a relational act between two parties, not a unilateral appropriation.

### Abaye's Evolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵרֵישׁ הֲוָה חָטֵיפְנָא מַתְּנָתָא, אָמֵינָא: חַבּוֹבֵי קָא מְחַבֵּיבְנָא מִצְוָה. כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא ״וְנָתַן״ – וְלֹא שֶׁיִּטּוֹל מֵעַצְמוֹ, מִיחְטָף לָא חָטֵיפְנָא, מֵימָר (אֲמַרִי) [אָמֵינָא]: הַבוּ לִי. וְכֵיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: ״וַיִּטּוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבָּצַע״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵי שְׁמוּאֵל חֶלְקָם שָׁאֲלוּ בְּפִיהֶם, מֵימָר נָמֵי לָא אָמֵינָא, וְאִי יָהֲבוּ לִי – שָׁקֵילְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye,** who was a priest, **said: At first, I would seize gifts** of the priesthood, as **I said** to myself that **I am demonstrating fondness** for the **mitzva** in this manner. **Once I heard this** interpretation: **"That they shall give," and not that he should take by himself, I did not seize** them anymore. Instead, **I would say** to the owners of the gifts: **Give me. And once I heard that which is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the sons of Samuel, who were Levites: **"But turned aside after wealth"** (I Samuel 8:3), and **Rabbi Meir says: The sons of Samuel** sinned when they **asked for their portion,** the first tithe given to the Levite, **with their mouths,** i.e., they demanded that the owners give them the first tithe. Abaye continued: After I heard that, **I also did not say** anything to the owners, **but if they would give me** gifts **I would take** them.

*Abaye*, himself a *kohen*, offers an unusually candid account of his own evolving practice. Initially, he would grab the *matanot* — *me-reisha hava chatifna matnasa* — telling himself *amina chivuvei ka-mechaveivna mitzvah*, that he was expressing love for the *mitzvah* by rushing to perform it. Once he heard the *drasha* on *v'natan v'lo she-yitol me-atzmo*, he stopped grabbing. Instead, *meimar amri havu li* — he would verbally request the gifts from the butcher, reasoning that at least he was not physically seizing them and was still expressing his desire to participate in the *mitzvah*.

### Shemuel's Sons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא דְּתַנְיָא: הַצְּנוּעִים מוֹשְׁכִין אֶת יְדֵיהֶם, וְהַגַּרְגְּרָנִים חוֹלְקִים – מִשְׁקָל נָמֵי לָא שָׁקֵילְנָא, לְבַר מִמַּעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי, לְאַחְזוֹקֵי נַפְשַׁאי בְּכָהֲנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said further: **Once I heard that which is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the distribution of the shewbread among the priests: **The modest ones [*hatzenu'im*] withdraw their hands** and do not take, **and the gluttons divide** all the bread; **I also did not take** gifts even when they were offered to me, **except for** when they were given on **the eve of Yom Kippur,** when there was an abundance of gifts due to the many animals that were slaughtered, as it is a mitzva to eat that day. In that instance, I took the gifts in order **to affirm myself among the priests,** i.e., if I would never take gifts then my status as a priest might be called into question.

Then *Abaye* heard a *baraita* citing *Rabbi Meir* on the sons of *Shmuel*. The verse says *vayitu acharei habatza* — "and they turned after gain." *Rabbi Meir* explains: *bnei Shmuel chelkom sha'alu b'pihem* — the sons of *Shmuel* requested their portions verbally. The *gemara* operates with the assumption that *Shmuel* was a *levi*, and his sons would accordingly be entitled to their *matnos Levi'ah*. Yet their practice of asking for what was owed them is interpreted as "turning after gain" — pursuing material benefit. Even verbally requesting one's legitimate portion, rather than waiting for it to be offered, is read as a kind of avarice, since it signals that the desire for the material benefit is the dominant motivation. *Meimar nami lo amina* — *Abaye* therefore stopped asking as well. However, *i yahavu li* — if a butcher were to come and offer him the gifts spontaneously, *shaklina* — he would of course accept.

### Yom Kippur Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִפְרוֹס יְדֵיהּ! אַנְסֵיהּ לֵיהּ עִידָּנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But let him spread his hands** for the Priestly Benediction throughout the year, as this will make it clear to all that he is in fact a priest. The Gemara responds: **His schedule constrained him,** as he was constantly involved in teaching Torah to his students, so much so that he would miss the time during which the community gathered in the synagogue for the Priestly Benediction.

Even this final posture — accepting only when freely offered — was then called into question. *Abaye* heard a *baraita* about the *lechem hapanim* toward the end of the Temple period, when there was insufficient bread to go around among all the *kohanim*. The *tznu'im*, the modest ones, would step back and decline to take; the *gargaranim*, the gluttonous ones, would divide it up among themselves. *Tosafot* frames this in terms of deference to others with greater need: even to simply receive when other *kohanim* are present and may need it more is not the ideal posture. Therefore, *mishkal nami lo shaklina* — *Abaye* also stopped accepting the gifts.

There was, however, one exception: *levar mi-malei yoma d'kippurai* — except for *Erev Yom Kippur*. Why? *Lachazukei nafshai bi-khahuna* — to establish publicly that he is a *kohen*. On *Erev Yom Kippur*, when everyone is slaughtering and eating meat, all the *kohanim* are receiving their portions. If *Abaye* were the only one not receiving, people would assume he is not a *kohen* at all. To avoid that false impression — *v'illu lo hayah notel hayu omerim she'eino Kohen*, as *Tosafot* states — he would take on that occasion. The *gemara* then asks: why not instead do *birkat kohanim* to publicly establish his identity as a *kohen*? The answer is *anseiah leh idna* — his schedule prevented him from regularly attending *shul*. Whether due to illness, as some explain, or because his teaching obligations conflicted with the *minyan* time, as *Rashi* understands it, *Abaye* did not have a reliable opportunity to perform *nesiat kapayim*.

## Teaching & Transfers

### Transfer to Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי כָּהֲנָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן בְּשִׁבָבוּתֵיהּ, וּדְחִיקָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא – לִיזַכֵּי לֵיהּ מַתְּנָתָא, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אָתֵי לִידֵיהּ, בְּמַכָּרֵי כְּהוּנָּה וּלְוִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** In a case where **a priest who has a Torah scholar [*tzurva merabbanan*]** living **in his neighborhood and** that Torah scholar **is hard-pressed** for money, **let** the priest **grant** his **gifts to him,** i.e., the priest may declare that those gifts he is destined to receive should be given to the poor Torah scholar. **And even though** the gifts **have not** yet **come into his possession,** he may grant them to the Torah scholar **in** a case where there are **associates of the priesthood and the Levites,** i.e., if that specific priest or Levite was well known in his neighborhood and has a standard arrangement with many people that they give him their gifts, as he is certain that he will be given them.

*Rav Yosef* rules: *hai kahana d'it leh tzurba merabbanan b'shivvavuteih v'dachika leh milta* — if a *kohen* has a *talmid chacham* in his neighborhood who is suffering financial hardship, *lizkei leh matnasa* — he should transfer his *matanot* to the *talmid chacham*. This is straightforward once the *kohen* has received the gifts; what is novel here is that the transfer can be effected even before the gifts come into the *kohen*'s physical possession.

How? The institution of *makirei kehunah u'leviya* makes this possible. When a *kohen* is an established recipient — when it is well known that a particular family consistently gives to him — his ownership of the forthcoming gifts is considered sufficiently certain that even before *shechitah* he can transfer that entitlement to another. Whether this operates as a form of *mechiras davar shelo ba l'olam* turns on a broader discussion in *Gittin* about whether one can sell something not yet in one's possession. The comparison is drawn to a case where a commodity is reliably available in the market: if I know I can obtain it immediately, there is an argument that a pre-sale is valid. Similarly, where the gifts will predictably be given to this *kohen*, the transfer in advance has legal effect.

### Rava & Safra Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא וְרַב סָפְרָא אִיקְּלַעוּ לְבֵי מָר יוֹחָנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חָנָא בַּר אַדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְבֵי מָר יוֹחָנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חָנָא בַּר בִּיזְנָא. עָבֵיד לְהוּ עִגְלָא תִּילְתָּא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ: זַכִּי לַן מַתְּנָתָא, דִּבְעֵינָא לְמֵיכַל לִישָּׁנָא בְּחַרְדְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the statement of Rav Yosef, the Gemara relates that **Rava and Rav Safra visited the house of Mar Yoḥana, son of Rav Ḥana bar Adda, and some say** they visited **the house of Mar Yoḥana, son of Rav Ḥana bar Bizna.** Mar Yoḥana **prepared for them a third-born calf. Rava said to** the **attendant** of Mar Yoḥana, who was a priest and would normally receive gifts of the priesthood from Mar Yoḥana: **Grant us the gifts, as I wish to eat tongue with mustard,** and the tongue along with the jaw is one of the gifts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זַכִּי לֵיהּ. רָבָא אֲכַל, וְרַב סָפְרָא לָא אֲכַל. אַקְרְיוּהּ לְרַב סָפְרָא בְּחֶלְמָא: ״מַעֲדֶה בֶּגֶד בְּיוֹם קָרָה חֹמֶץ עַל נָתֶר וְשָׁר בַּשִּׁירִים עַל לֶב רָע״.
>
> **Translation:** The attendant **granted** the gifts **to him; Rava ate** from them **but Rav Safra did not eat.** Following this incident, **they read** the following verse **to Rav Safra in a dream: "As one that takes off a garment in cold weather, and as vinegar upon niter, so is he that sings songs to a heavy heart"** (Proverbs 25:20). The verse can be interpreted allegorically as a chastisement of one who studies Torah but fails to understand it.

*Rava* and *Rav Safra* once came to the home of *Mar Yohana*, the son of *Rav Chana bar Adda* (or, in an alternative version, *Rav Chana bar Bizna*), who prepared for them an *egla tilsa* — a prized young calf. There is some dispute as to what *tilsa* means precisely: one interpretation is a third-grown calf, tender and young; another is the third calf born to the cow; a third understands it as three years old, though this last reading is flagged by *Jastrow* as incorrect. Whatever the precise meaning, it was understood as a special and honored dish.

*Mar Yohana*'s attendant was a *kohen*, and it was known that the master of the house customarily gave the *zero'a lechayayim v'keiva* to this attendant. *Rava* said to him: *zachi lan matnasa* — please transfer your gifts to us, *d'beina l'meichal lishna b'chardala* — I would like to eat the tongue in mustard. The attendant complied. *Rava* ate; *Rav Safra* declined to eat from the gift.

That night, the following verse was read to *Rav Safra* in a dream: *maade beged beyom karah*, *chometz al neter*, *v'shar beshirim al lev ra* — a worn cloak on a cold day, vinegar on mineral, singing to someone with a wicked heart. The implication seemed to be a rebuke for refusing to act on a valid teaching.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּלְמָא מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַרִי אַשְּׁמַעְתָּא דְּמָר אַקְרְיֻין הָכִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי אֲמַרִי אֲנָא – בְּאַחֵר, שַׁמָּעָא – בְּעַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ מְזַכֵּי, וְכִי אֲמַרִי אֲנָא – לְמַאן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ, הָא אֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra **came before Rav Yosef** and **said to him: Perhaps because I transgressed the *halakha* of the Master they read this** verse to me in chastisement. Rav Yosef **said to him:** No, you acted appropriately by refraining from consuming the gifts. **When I said** that a priest may grant the gifts to a Torah scholar, that was only **with regard to** a priest who grants them to **another** person of his own choosing. I did not permit this in the case of **an attendant** who grants the gifts to a dignified guest of the homeowner. The reason is that **he grants** the gifts **against his will,** as he feels pressured by the homeowner to acquiesce. **And** furthermore, **when I said** this *halakha*, it was only **for one who cannot** eat under another circumstance, as he is hard-pressed for money. In **this** incident, it was **possible for** Rava to consume his own meat with mustard, since Rava was not poor.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי טַעְמָא אַקְרְיֻין הָכִי? כְּלַפֵּי רָבָא! וְלַקְרְיוּהּ לְרָבָא? רָבָא נָזוּף הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Safra asked Rav Yosef: **But** if so, **what is the reason** that **they read this** verse to me? Rav Yosef responded: The reading was directed not toward you but **toward Rava,** who ate from the gifts against my ruling. The Gemara objects: **But let them read** this verse **to Rava** himself in a dream. The Gemara responds: **Rava was rebuked** as a result of this incident and therefore was not granted heavenly communication. The verse was therefore proclaimed to Rav Safra instead.

*Rav Safra* came before *Rav Yosef* and asked whether this was because he had gone against *Rav Yosef*'s ruling that a *kohen* may transfer *matanot* to a *talmid chacham*. *Rav Yosef* clarified: the ruling applies to an independent *kohen* who acts of his own free will — *b'acher, shama*. In the case of the attendant, however, *ba'al korcho masar* — he had no real choice. He was a servant in the household; when an honored guest like *Rava* asks him to transfer his portion, he cannot refuse. Such a transfer under duress is not what *Rav Yosef* had in mind. Moreover, *Rav Yosef* added: even the legitimate case applies only when the *talmid chacham* genuinely lacks means — *l'man d'lo efshar leih*. *Ha efshar leih* — but *Rav Safra* had means, so the transfer should never have been solicited from her in the first place.

The dream, then, was not directed at *Rav Safra*. It was meant for *Rava*, who had compelled the attendant and had sought a portion for a colleague who did not need it. *V'likriun l'Rava* — so why was it read to *Rav Safra* rather than to *Rava*? *Rava nazuf hava* — *Rava* was in a state of divine displeasure; heaven did not wish to engage with him directly. The verse was therefore delivered to *Rav Safra*, the intermediary. This precise dynamic is captured by the Yiddish expression: *der tochter zog ich, di shner mein ich* — I am speaking to my daughter, but I mean my daughter-in-law. Unable to address *Rava* directly, the rebuke reached him through *Rav Safra*.

### Yosef's Clarification

*Rav Yosef*'s clarification thus draws sharp parameters around the legitimate use of the transfer mechanism. It requires: a *kohen* who is an independent agent and gives willingly; a *talmid chacham* who is genuinely in need; and a genuine gift relationship — *makirei kehunah* — that gives legal force to the pre-*shechitah* transfer. Absent these conditions, the mechanism is inapplicable or worse — an abuse.

### Unworthy Students

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי: וּפְשָׁטֵיהּ דִּקְרָא בְּמַאי כְּתִיב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּשׁוֹנֶה לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן.
>
> **Translation:** In the preceding incident, the Gemara related that the verse: "As one that takes off a garment in cold weather, and as vinegar upon niter, so is he that sings songs to a heavy heart" (Proverbs 25:20), was proclaimed to Rav Safra in a dream. **Abaye said to Rav Dimi: And with regard to what** matter **is the plain meaning of the verse written?** Rav Dimi **said to him:** It is referring **to** one who **teaches an unworthy student.** In other words, just as one should remove a worn garment that has no use in cold weather, or just as vinegar upon niter ruins the niter, rendering it unusable, so too, there is no use in singing songs, i.e., teaching Torah, to an unworthy student who has a heavy heart, i.e., who does not intend to adhere to the *halakhot* he is taught.

The *gemara* then asks *Abaye* what the simple *peshat* of the verse that was read to *Rav Safra* is, and *Rav Dimi* answers: *b'shoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun* — it refers to one who teaches a student of bad character. This launches the *gemara* into a sharp discussion about the dangers of teaching Torah to an unworthy student.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹנֶה לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן – נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כׇּל חֹשֶׁךְ טָמוּן לִצְפּוּנָיו תְּאׇכְלֵהוּ אֵשׁ לֹא נֻפָּח יֵרַע שָׂרִיד בְּאׇהֳלוֹ״, וְאֵין ״שָׂרִיד״ אֶלָּא תַּלְמִיד חָכָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּבַשְּׂרִידִים אֲשֶׁר ה׳ קֹרֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: Anyone who teaches** Torah **to an unworthy student falls into Gehenna, as it is stated: "All darkness is laid up for his treasures; a fire not blown by man shall consume him; it shall go ill with him that remains [*yera sarid*] in his tent"** (Job 20:26), **and *sarid*** means **nothing other than a Torah scholar, as it is stated: "And among the remnant [*sarid*] those whom the Lord shall call"** (Joel 3:5). The word *yera* shares a root with the word *ra*, evil, and "*yera sarid*" is therefore understood to be referring to an unworthy student.

*Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: *kol hashoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun nofel b'Gehinnom* — one who teaches an unworthy student will fall into *Gehinnom*. The danger is not merely that the Torah will be wasted, but that it will be weaponized. Teaching Torah to someone of genuinely bad character gives that person tools to undermine the Torah from within. The verse cited — *kol choshech tamun litzfunav tochlehu esh lo nupach yera sarid b'ohalo* — is interpreted with the phrase *eil sarid ela talmid chacham*. The *Maharsha* objects to reading *sarid* as the unworthy student; one would not call such a person a *talmid chacham*. He therefore reads the verse as referring to the *talmid chacham* doing the teaching: *yera b'ohalo* — it will be evil in his tent if he teaches such a person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹנֶה לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן – כְּזוֹרֵק אֶבֶן לְמֶרְקוּלִיס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּצְרוֹר אֶבֶן בְּמַרְגֵּמָה כֵּן נוֹתֵן לִכְסִיל כָּבוֹד״, וּכְתִיב: ״לֹא נָאוֶה לִכְסִיל תַּעֲנוּג״.
>
> **Translation:** On a similar note, **Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rav says: Anyone who teaches** Torah **to an unworthy student** is considered **like one who throws a stone to Markulis, as it is stated: "As a small stone in a heap of stones, so is he that gives honor to a fool"** (Proverbs 26:8), **and it is written: "Luxury is not seemly for a fool"** (Proverbs 19:10).

*Rav Zeira* in the name of *Rav* adds: *kol hashoneh l'talmid she'eino hagun k'zorek even l'markulis* — it is like throwing a stone at the *markulis*, the idol. The metaphor is explained by the *Maharsha*: throwing a stone at the *markulis* appears to be a hostile or denigrating act, but in fact it constitutes a form of worship of that idol. Similarly, teaching the unworthy student looks like the dissemination of Torah, but in reality it provides ammunition to those who will use it destructively. The verse cited: *k'tzror even b'margemah kein nosein l'ksil kavod* — like binding a stone in a sling is giving honor to the fool.

The *gemara* then resolves this with the general principle of *kol ha'osek b'Torah shelo lishmah* — that learning Torah not for its own sake can eventually lead to learning *lishmah*. *Tosafot* elsewhere distinguishes between two categories: one who comes to learning with fundamentally redeemable character, in whom the process of learning will itself effect transformation, and one who comes with deliberate malicious intent to use Torah destructively. It is the second category — clearly of bad character and bent on abuse — toward which these severe warnings are directed. The gray areas remain, and the tradition maintains the tension between an open approach that hopes learning will elevate and a more guarded approach that recognizes the dangers of arming those who will cause harm.

## Ownership Shares

### Marking Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף עִמָּהֶן צָרִיךְ לִרְשׁוֹם, וַאֲפִילּוּ עִם הַגּוֹי. וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף עִם כֹּהֵן – צָרִיךְ לִרְשׁוֹם, וְהַמִּשְׁתַּתֵּף עִם הַגּוֹי וּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁים – אֵין צָרִיךְ לִרְשׁוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one who enters into partnership with** a priest or a gentile **must mark** the animal. The Gemara asks: **And** is one obligated to mark the animal **even** if he enters into partnership **with a gentile? And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a *baraita*: **One who enters into partnership with a priest must mark** the animal, **but one who enters into partnership with a gentile and** one who slaughters **disqualified consecrated** animals **does not need to mark** the animal.

The *mishnah* states: *hamishtatef imahem tzarich lirshom* — one who enters into a business partnership with a *kohen* or non-Jew must mark the animal. Since the partnership arrangement exempts the *shochet* from giving *matanot*, without a visible marker people observing the butcher not giving the gifts would be puzzled or misled. The mark communicates the reason for the exemption.

### Marking Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּיָתֵיב גּוֹי אַמְּסַחְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where** the **gentile is sitting in the butcher shop** of the Israelite. In such a situation, it is evident that the gentile is in partnership with the Israelite and the animal need not be marked. By contrast, the mishna is referring to a case where the gentile does not sit in the shop.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְוָתַהּ גַּבֵּי כֹהֵן, דְּיָתֵיב אַמְּסַחְתָּא, אַמַּאי צָרִיךְ לִרְשׁוֹם? דְּאָמְרִי: בִּשְׂרָא קָא זָבֵין. אִי הָכִי, גּוֹי נָמֵי אָמְרִי: בִּשְׂרָא קָא זָבֵין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, then **in the corresponding** situation **involving a priest,** where the *baraita* teaches that one who enters into partnership with a priest must mark the animal, this must also be referring to a case **where** the priest **sits in the butcher shop. Why,** then, **must one mark** the animal? The Gemara responds: The fact that the priest sits in the shop does not indicate that he is in partnership with the Israelite, **as** people will **say:** He is in the shop because **he is purchasing meat.** The Gemara objects: **If so,** then in the case of the **gentile as well, they** will **say** that he is in the shop because **he is purchasing meat.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין אֱמוּנָה בְּגוֹי. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: סְתַם גּוֹי מִפְּעָא פָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It is **not** common for a Jew to place his **trust in a gentile. If you wish, say** instead that in both cases of the *baraita* the priest and the gentile sit in the butcher shop, not by the safe. It is evident that the gentile is in partnership with the Israelite, as **an ordinary gentile** partner **yells** at the seller, saying, for example: Do not sell an item for this price but for a different price. By contrast, a priest, even if he sits in the shop, would not question the practices of the salesman, due to his modesty, but would defer to the seller's expertise. Accordingly, it is not evident that the priest is a partner, and the animal must therefore be marked.

The *gemara* introduces a tension from a *baraita*: *hamishtatef im kohen tzarich lirshom* — partnership with a *kohen* requires marking, but *hamishtatef im ovdei kochavim u'psulei mukdashin* — partnership with a non-Jew, or animals that were once *hekdesh* and have been redeemed (*psulei mukdashin*), does not require marking. Why should there be a difference?

After several attempts to explain the distinction by appeal to various observable circumstances — whether the non-Jew is sitting visibly in the shop, whether he is counting the money, whether he is simply present at the counter — the *gemara* settles on a more pointed explanation. When the non-Jew is sitting at the counter, the *gemara* initially suggests he might be mistaken for a customer. But could people not equally mistake the *kohen* for a customer? The answer turns on trust: *ein heimuna b'oved kochavim*. *Rashi* is careful to soften this: *ein Yisrael regilim leha'amin bahem* — Jews are not accustomed to trusting non-Jews. It is not an evaluative claim about non-Jewish character, but a descriptive sociological observation: in that world, Jews did not typically entrust their business finances to non-Jews. Therefore, if a non-Jew is seen managing the money box, people immediately understand he must be a genuine partner, and no mark is needed. By contrast, when a *kohen* sits at the money box, people might assume the butcher simply trusts him — not that he is a legal partner — hence the need for a mark.

A second resolution is also offered: *stam oved kochavim mefap-peit* — the non-Jew partner will simply talk too much and be too visibly directive. If a non-Jew is sitting in the shop giving instructions about which piece to sell and at what price, it will be unmistakably clear to everyone that he is a business partner. No marking is required because the partnership is self-evident.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין אֵין צָרִיךְ לִרְשׁוֹם. אַלְמָא מוֹכְחָא מִלְּתָא, וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִמְכָּרִים בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁחָטִים בָּאִיטְלִיז, וְנִשְׁקָלִים בְּלִיטְרָא!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **And** the owner of **disqualified consecrated** animals, which were slaughtered after they were redeemed and became non-sacred, is exempt from the obligation to give the gifts, and one **does not need to mark** the animal. The Gemara objects: **Apparently, it is an evident matter** that this is a disqualified consecrated animal, and therefore one need not mark it. **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 31a): All **disqualified consecrated** animals **are sold in the meat market [*be'itliz*], and are slaughtered in the meat market, and are weighed** and sold **by the *litra*,** in the manner of non-sacred meat? Since disqualified consecrated animals are treated in the same manner as ordinary non-sacred animals, how is it evident that this is a disqualified consecrated animal?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמָא רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא, בְּאוֹתָן הַנִּמְכָּרִים בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rav Adda bar Ahava interpreted** the *baraita* **before Rav Pappa** as referring **to those** disqualified consecrated animals **that are sold** from **inside the house,** i.e., a firstborn animal and an animal tithe, whose redemption payments belong to their owners, as their value has no sanctity. Since no gain would accrue to the Temple, the Sages prohibited one from selling these animals in the market, despite the fact one could have obtained a higher sale price there due to the high concentration of customers.

For *psulei mukdashin*, the *gemara* ultimately explains that the exemption-without-marking applies specifically to those animals — such as a *bechor* with a *mum* or an animal *ma'aser* — that must by law be sold from the home rather than in the marketplace, and weighed by the unit rather than by the pound. That distinctive mode of sale makes the animal's prior *hekdesh* status apparent to anyone who sees it. Where *psulei mukdashin* are sold in the ordinary way, in the marketplace and by weight (*yikru bahen nishchatin b'itliz v'nishkalin b'litra*), no one would know the animal's history, and a mark would be required. The resolution — *targema Rav Adda bar Ahava kammai d'Rav Pappa b'ozen hen nimkarin betoch habayis* — limits the "no marking needed" rule precisely to those cases where the manner of sale already broadcasts the animal's status.

### Huna's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שׁוּתָּף בָּרֹאשׁ – פָּטוּר מִן הַלֶּחִי, שׁוּתָּף בַּיָּד – פָּטוּר מִן הַזְּרוֹעַ, שׁוּתָּף בִּבְנֵי מֵעַיִין – פָּטוּר מִן הַקֵּבָה. וְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ שׁוּתָּף בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן – פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to gifts of the priesthood from animals owned in partnership, **Rav Huna says:** If an Israelite is **a partner** with a priest or gentile **in** only **the head** of the animal, he is **exempt** merely **from** the obligation to give the **jaw,** which is part of the head. He remains obligated to give the foreleg and the maw. If he is **a partner** with a priest or gentile **in the leg and hoof** of the animal, he is **exempt** only **from** the obligation to give **the foreleg.** If he is **a partner** with a priest or gentile **in the innards,** he is **exempt** merely **from** the obligation to give **the maw. And Ḥiyya bar Rav says** that **even** if the priest or gentile is **a partner in** only **one of them,** the Israelite is **exempt from all of** the gifts.

*Rav Huna* rules: *shutfut berosh patur min halechi* — if the *kohen* is a partner in the head, you are exempt from the cheek area. *Shutfut beyad patur min hazroa* — if he is a partner in the arm, you are exempt from the *zero'a*. *Shutfut bivnei meayim patur min hakeivah* — if he is a partner in the intestines, you are exempt from the *keivah*. The operative principle is that the *kohen*'s ownership must extend to the actual body part from which the gift is taken. Partial ownership of the rest of the animal does not suffice; the partnership must encompass the specific gift portion itself.

### Chiya's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁלִּי וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלָּךְ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד מִמֵּאָה בָּרֹאשׁ – פָּטוּר. ״הַיָּד שֶׁלִּי וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלָּךְ״, אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד מִמֵּאָה בַּיָּד – פָּטוּר. ״בְּנֵי מֵעַיִין שֶׁלִּי וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלָּךְ״, אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד מִמֵּאָה בָּהֶן – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Ḥiyya bar Rav from a *baraita*: If a priest says to an Israelite butcher: Let us enter into a partnership in which **the head** of the animal **is mine and the entire** remainder **is yours, even** if the priest proposes that only **one-hundredth of the head** should be his, the Israelite is **exempt.** Similarly, if the priest offers: Let us enter into a partnership in which **the leg and hoof** of the animal **are mine and the entire** remainder **is yours, even** if the priest suggests that only **one-hundredth of the leg and hoof** will be his, the Israelite is **exempt.** And likewise, if the priest says: Let us enter into a partnership in which **the innards** of the animal **are mine and the entire** remainder **is yours, even** if the priest offers to take only **one-hundredth of the innards,** the Israelite is **exempt.**

*Chiya bar Rav* stakes out a more expansive position: *afilu shutfut be'achat mehen patur mikulan* — even if the *kohen* is a partner in only one of the three gifts, he is exempt from all of them. On this view, the three gifts — *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* — form a single halachic unit. Partial ownership in any one of them generates a blanket exemption from all three.

### Specific Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו: פָּטוּר מִן הַלֶּחִי וְחַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן, פָּטוּר מִן הַזְּרוֹעַ וְחַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן, פָּטוּר מִן הַקֵּבָה וְחַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן? לָא, פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **What, is it not** correct that the *baraita* is teaching that when the priest owns the head the butcher is **exempt from** giving **the jaw, but obligated in** giving **all** the rest, and likewise when the priest owns the leg and hoof, he is **exempt from** giving **the foreleg but obligated in all** the rest, and when the priest owns the innards, he is **exempt from the maw and obligated in all** the rest? This would contradict the opinion of Ḥiyya bar Rav. The Gemara responds: **No;** in each case the *baraita* means that he is **exempt from all** the gifts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְנֵי פָּטוּר מִכּוּלָּן! וְעוֹד, תַּנְיָא: הָרֹאשׁ שֶׁלִּי וְכוּלָּהּ שֶׁלָּךְ – אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד מִמֵּאָה בָּרֹאשׁ פָּטוּר מִן הַלֶּחִי וְחַיָּיב בְּכוּלָּן. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּחִיָּיא בַּר רַב, תְּיוּבְתָּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **But** if so, **let** the *baraita* **teach** explicitly that he is **exempt from all the** gifts, rather than simply stating that he is exempt. **And furthermore,** it **is taught** in another *baraita* that if a priest says to an Israelite butcher: Let us enter into a partnership in which **the head** of the animal **is mine and the entire** remainder **is yours, even** if the priest suggests that only **one-hundredth of the head** will be his, the butcher is **exempt from** the obligation to give **the jaw but is obligated in** giving **all the** rest. This *baraita* explicitly contradicts the opinion of Ḥiyya bar Rav. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Ḥiyya bar Rav** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The *gemara* challenges *Chiya bar Rav* from a *baraita*. The *baraita* distinguishes explicitly: *harosh sheli v'chulah shelcha* — if the *kohen* retains the head, even *afilu echad mimeah berosh* — even one percent of the head — *patur min halechi* — you are exempt from the jaw portion — *v'chayav bekulan* — but obligated in the remaining two gifts. Similarly, ownership only of the hand exempts only from the *zero'a* but not the others; ownership only of the intestines exempts only from the *keivah* but not the others. *Tiyuvta d'Chiya bar Rav tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation. The *halacha* is that the exemption is specific to the gift for which the *kohen* holds a portion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָא מַתְנִיתָא אַטְעִיתֵיהּ לְחִיָּיא בַּר רַב, דְּתַנְיָא: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע מַתְּנוֹת כְּהוּנָּה הֵן, וְכוּלָּן נִיתְּנוּ לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּבְרִית מֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** in explanation of the opinion of Ḥiyya bar Rav: **This *baraita* misled Ḥiyya bar Rav, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **There are twenty-four gifts of the priesthood, and all of them were given** in the Torah **to Aaron and his sons by a generalization and a detail, and** with **a covenant of salt.** The verse states: "And the Lord spoke to Aaron: And I, behold, I have given you the charge of My gifts; of all the consecrated items of the children of Israel to you have I given them for prominence, and to your sons, as an eternal portion" (Numbers 18:8). After this generalization, the Torah proceeds to list the gifts in detail. Finally, after delineating all the gifts of the priesthood, the verse states: "It is an everlasting covenant of salt" (Numbers 18:19), assuring Aaron that just as a covenant of salt is unbreakable, so too, the gifts of the priesthood are eternal.

*Rav Chisda* explains what led *Chiya bar Rav* to his erroneous position: *ha matnitin atisya l'Chiya bar Rav* — it was the following *baraita* that caused him to err. That *baraita* enumerates the twenty-four *matnot kehunah*, and in its enumeration counts *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* as a single unit. *Chiya bar Rav* inferred from this that the three gifts are treated as one halachic entity. But the inference is mistaken. The same *baraita* counts the gift from the *todah* and the gift from the ram of a *nazir* as one unit (*muram mitodah v'eil nazir*), even though those are quite different: the *todah* involves the *chazeh* and *shok* with loaves of bread, while the *nazir* involves the *zero'a*. They are plainly distinct gifts. The reason they are counted as one is simply that they are similar in kind — both are *kodshim kalim* in which the *kohen* receives a part — *damyan lehadadi*, and so they are grouped together for the purposes of counting. The same is true of *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah*: they are similar to one another and grouped together for counting purposes, but that grouping carries no halachic implication that they form a unified entity for purposes of the exemption.

## 24 Matanot List

### 10 in Temple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַמְקַיְּימָן – כְּאִילּוּ קִיֵּם בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּבְרִית מֶלַח, וְכׇל הָעוֹבֵר עֲלֵיהֶן – כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֵר עַל בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּבְרִית מֶלַח.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Therefore, **anyone who fulfills** the mitzva of giving the gifts of the priesthood is considered **as though he fulfills** all these mitzvot that are derived using the principle of **a generalization and a detail, and** as though he has upheld the **covenant of salt. And anyone who violates** the mitzva of giving the gifts of the priesthood is considered **as though he violates** all of the mitzvot derived by the principle of **a generalization and a detail, and** as though he has not upheld the **covenant of salt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: עֶשֶׂר בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאַרְבַּע בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְעֶשֶׂר בַּגְּבוּלִים. עֶשֶׂר בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ: חַטָּאת, וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, אָשָׁם וַדַּאי, וְאָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וְזִבְחֵי שַׁלְמֵי צִבּוּר, וְלוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע, וּשְׁתֵּי הַלֶּחֶם, וְלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים, וּשְׁיָרֵי מְנָחוֹת, וּמִנְחַת הָעוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the twenty-four gifts: **Ten** are consumed **in the Temple, and four in Jerusalem, and ten in the boundaries** of Eretz Yisrael. The **ten** gifts consumed only **in the Temple** are an animal **sin offering; and a bird sin offering;** and **a definite guilt offering; and a provisional guilt offering; and communal peace offerings,** i.e., the lambs that accompany the two loaves brought on *Shavuot* and which are considered offerings of the most sacred order; **and** the remainder of **the *log* of oil** that accompanies the guilt offering of a recovered **leper; and the two loaves** offering from the new wheat brought on *Shavuot*; **and the shewbread; and the leftovers of meal offerings; and the *omer* meal offering,** i.e., the measure of barley brought as a communal offering on the sixteenth of Nisan.

### 4 in Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַרְבַּע בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם: הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְהַבִּכּוּרִים, וּמוּרָם מִן הַתּוֹדָה וּמֵאֵיל נָזִיר, וְעוֹרוֹת קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** these are the **four** gifts that the priests consume anywhere **in Jerusalem: The** unblemished **firstborn** of kosher animals; **and the first fruits; and the** portions **separated** for the priests **from the thanks offering,** i.e., the breast and thigh and one loaf from each of the four types of loaves brought with a thanks offerings: Cakes, wafers, cakes of fine flour, and leavened bread, **and** the portions separated **from the nazirite's ram,** i.e., the boiled shoulder, a cake, and a wafer, in addition to the breast and thigh of any peace offering; **and** the **hides of sacrificial** animals brought as burnt offerings, sin offerings, and guilt offerings.

### 10 Throughout Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעֶשֶׂר בַּגְּבוּלִים: תְּרוּמָה, וּתְרוּמַת מַעֲשֵׂר, וְחַלָּה, וְרֵאשִׁית הַגֵּז, וּמַתָּנוֹת, וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן, וּפִדְיוֹן פֶּטֶר חֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** these are the **ten** gifts that the priests consume anywhere **in the boundaries** of Eretz Yisrael: ***Teruma*,** i.e., the portion of the produce designated for the priest; **and *teruma* of the tithe; and *ḥalla*; and the first sheared** wool; **and the gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw; **and** the five *sela* given as **redemption of** the firstborn **son; and** a sheep or goat given as **redemption of the firstborn donkey.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה, וּשְׂדֵה חֲרָמִים, וְגֶזֶל הַגֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues the list: **And a** consecrated **ancestral field** that one did not redeem, which was sold to another by the Temple treasurer and is therefore given to the members of the priestly watch serving during the Jubilee Year; **and a dedicated field; and** payment for the **robbery of a convert** who died without heirs. If one takes an oath that he did not rob from a convert, and after the death of the convert he admits to having taken a false oath, he must pay the principal value of the stolen item and an additional one-fifth of the value, all of which is given to the priests.

### Counting Convention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא סָבַר: מִדְּקָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ לְמַתָּנוֹת בַּחֲדָא – חֲדָא נִינְהוּ, וְלָא הִיא! אַטּוּ מוּרָם מִתּוֹדָה וְאֵיל נָזִיר דְּקָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ כַּחֲדָא – מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲדָא נִינְהוּ?! אֶלָּא, כֵּיוָן דְּדָמְיָין לַהֲדָדֵי – חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ כַּחֲדָא, הָכִי נָמֵי: כֵּיוָן דְּדָמְיָין לַהֲדָדֵי – חֲשִׁיב לְהוּ כַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda explains how the *baraita* misled Ḥiyya bar Rav: **He thought** that **from** the fact **that** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **counts the gifts** of the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw **as one** gift, **they are** treated as **one** gift, which means that if one is exempt from one of them he is exempt from all of them. **But it is not so,** as **is that to say** that **the** portions **separated** for the priests **from the thanks offering and** from **the nazirite's ram, which** the *tanna* **counts as one,** are counted this way **because they are one?** Obviously, these are independent gifts. **Rather, since they are similar to one another** the *tanna* **counts them as one. So too,** with regard to the foreleg, the jaw, and the maw, **since they are similar to one another he counts them as one.**

The fact that *zero'a*, *lechayayim*, and *keivah* are listed as a single entry in this enumeration, despite being three distinct gifts, was what misled *Chiya bar Rav* into treating them as a single halachic unit. The *gemara* makes clear that this grouping is a counting convenience, not a substantive legal merger. The comparison to *muram mitodah v'eil nazir* — which are counted as one despite being structurally distinct — establishes that the counting convention reflects similarity of category rather than halachic identity. *Kivan d'damyan lehadadi chashiv lehu kechada* — since they resemble one another, they are counted as one; but for the purpose of determining the exemption through partnership, each gift stands independently. There is also an aesthetic dimension to the enumeration: the ten-four-ten structure is symmetrical and easy to remember, and achieving that symmetry required counting the three *matanot* as one unit. The grouping serves the purposes of enumeration and memorization, not legal derivation.
