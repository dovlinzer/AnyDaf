# Hullin 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oto Ve'et Beno Intro

### Chapter Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בֵּין בָּאָרֶץ בֵּין בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ, בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי הַבַּיִת, בְּחוּלִּין וּבְמוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring applies both in Eretz** Yisrael **and outside of Eretz** Yisrael, both **in the presence,** i.e., the time, **of the Temple and not in the presence of the Temple,** and it applies **with regard to non-sacred** animals **and with regard to sacrificial** animals.

With the opening of this new *perek*, we transition from the demanding material of *treifos*, severed limbs, and fetal edge cases into territory that is, comparatively speaking, more accessible — though no less rich. The subject is *oto ve'et beno*: the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day, as stated in *vayikra* 22:28: *oto ve'et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad*.

It is worth pausing to situate this chapter within the broader structure of *masechet hullin*. The *perek* belongs to a cluster of chapters dealing not strictly with the *kashrus* of the animal itself, but with the range of obligations and prohibitions that surround the act of *shechita* and the consumption of meat. Over the coming chapters the same structural questions will recur across multiple *mitzvot* — *gid hanasheh*, *reishit hagez*, *zero'a lechayayim vekivah* — and in each case the *mishnah* will systematically address: Does the law apply in the Land of Israel or outside it? With the *beit haMikdash* or without it? To *chullin* (non-sanctified animals) or also to *kodshim* (sacrifices)? This chapter on *oto ve'et beno* is the first of that series, and the reader should keep that overarching architectural purpose in mind.

### Mishnah Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? הַשּׁוֹחֵט אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, חוּלִּין בַּחוּץ – שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּשֵׁרִים, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal **itself and its offspring,** both of which are **non-sacred,** and slaughters them **outside** the Temple courtyard, **both of** the animals **are fit** for consumption, **but** for slaughtering **the second** animal, **one incurs [*sofeg*] the forty** lashes for violating the prohibition: "You shall not slaughter it and its offspring both in one day" (Leviticus 22:28).

The *mishnah* opens by establishing the scope of the prohibition. *oto ve'et beno noheg bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — it applies whether one is in the Land of Israel or outside it. This is expected: the prohibition has nothing to do with agricultural conditions or the sanctity of the land, so there is no basis for restricting it geographically. The *mishnah* adds: *bein bifnei habayit bein shelo bifnei habayit* — whether the *beit haMikdash* stands or not. Again, this is intuitive. And finally: *bechullin uvemukdashin* — it applies to both non-sanctified animals and sanctified ones. It is this last point — the extension to *kodshim* — that will drive much of the subsequent discussion.

## Mishna Permutations

### Chullin Bachutz


Before examining the full matrix of cases, several foundational parameters of *oto ve'et beno* must be stated. First, the order of *shechita* is immaterial: whether one slaughters the mother first or the child first, the prohibition is equally triggered. Second, the two *shechitot* need not be performed by the same person; it is always the one who performs the *second* slaughter who transgresses. Third — and critically — the *shechita* itself remains valid. Transgressing the prohibition does not render the meat forbidden.

The *mishnah* then works through the permutations. The simplest case: *chullin bachutz* — both animals are non-sanctified and are slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*. *sheneihem kesherim*: both animals are *kasher*. The meat may be eaten. The second person, however — whoever performs the second *shechita* — *sofeg et ha'arba'im*, absorbs the forty lashes for having violated the prohibition.

## Oto Ve'et Beno Intro…

### Prohibition Parameters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב כָּרֵת, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִים, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם סוֹפְגִים אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** If both animals were **sacrificial** animals slaughtered **outside** the Temple courtyard, **then** for slaughtering **the first** animal, **one is liable** to receive **excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*].** For slaughtering the second animal one is not liable to receive *karet*. The second animal was not fit for sacrifice, since one may not slaughter an animal and its offspring on the same day. **And both** animals **are disqualified** for use as offerings, **and** for the slaughter of **both of them, one incurs forty** lashes apiece: The first being a sacrificial animal slaughtered outside the courtyard and the second being the offspring of an animal slaughtered that day.

Before the *mishnah* can proceed to the more complex permutations, it is necessary to lay out a set of parameters governing *kodshim*. When a *korban* is slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*, the offender violates *shechitat chutz* and is liable to *karet* (and lashes, since virtually every *karet* offense also carries lashes). The sacrifice, moreover, is entirely invalid and may not be eaten. If, on the other hand, a *korban* is slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash* on a day when its mother or offspring has already been slaughtered, the offender does not incur liability for *kodshim bachutz* — because that violation requires that the animal *could* have been brought inside. Since *oto ve'et beno* prohibits its *shechita* that day, it is no longer eligible for the *beit haMikdash* that day, and therefore the prohibition of slaughtering *kodshim* outside does not apply.

A further parameter: *chullin* slaughtered inside the *beit haMikdash* — *chullin ba'azarah* — is prohibited, but carries no lashes, since the prohibition is derived from a positive commandment rather than an explicit negative one. The meat nonetheless cannot be eaten.

These parameters, taken together with the structure of *oto ve'et beno* itself, set up the matrix of cases the *mishnah* will now work through. The organizing question is: given the interaction between the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* and the distinct rules governing *kodshim*, what are the *kashrus* and liability consequences in each scenario?

## Mishna Permutations…

### Kodshim Bachutz


When both animals are *kodshim* and both are slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*: the first person incurs *karet* for *shechitat kodshim bachutz*, and both animals are *pasul* — invalid as sacrifices and forbidden to eat. As for liability, note that the word *sheneihem* appears twice in this *mishnah*: once referring to the animals (both invalid), and once referring to the people. Both people *sofgin et ha'arba'im* — they each receive lashes. The first for the violation of slaughtering *kodshim* outside the *beit haMikdash*; the second for the violation of *oto ve'et beno*.

Why does the second person not also receive lashes — or *karet* — for *kodshim bachutz*? Because by the time the second *shechita* is performed, *oto ve'et beno* has already prohibited slaughtering this animal that day. An animal that may not be slaughtered today is not eligible to be brought as a *korban* today. And since it could not have been brought inside that day, slaughtering it outside does not constitute the offense of *shechitat kodshim bachutz*.

There is a corollary worth noting here. When *kodshim* are slaughtered in the proper place — inside the *beit haMikdash* — the first person's *shechita* is perfectly valid; *harishon kasher upatur*, the animal is *kasher* and he is exempt. The second person performs his *shechita* in the right place, but at the wrong time — the mother was slaughtered that day. The principle of *mechusar zman* applies: just as the *torah* requires an animal to be at least eight days old before it may be offered (*uvayom hashmini vahalah yeratzeh lekorban isheh laShem*), so too any animal whose offering is prohibited on a given day by virtue of *oto ve'et beno* is treated as *mechusar zman* — lacking its proper time — and is therefore invalid as a *korban*. The second person receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno* and the *korban* is *pasul*.

This distinction is significant: by ordinary *chullin*, the violation of *oto ve'et beno* does not affect the *kashrus* of the meat at all. By *kodshim*, the prohibition indirectly invalidates the sacrifice through the principle of *mechusar zman*, which in turn means the meat cannot be eaten.

### Mixed Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּלִּין וְקָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ – הָרִאשׁוֹן כָּשֵׁר, וּפָטוּר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וּפָסוּל. קָדָשִׁים וְחוּלִּין בַּחוּץ – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב כָּרֵת וּפָסוּל, וְהַשֵּׁנִי כָּשֵׁר, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם סוֹפְגִים אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. חוּלִּין וְקָדָשִׁים בִּפְנִים – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִים, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים. קָדָשִׁים וְחוּלִּין בִּפְנִים – הָרִאשׁוֹן כָּשֵׁר וּפָטוּר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** If the first animal was **non-sacred and** the second **a sacrificial** animal, and both were slaughtered **outside** the Temple courtyard, **the first is fit** for consumption **and** one who slaughters it is **exempt** from any punishment. **But** for slaughtering **the second** animal, **one incurs the forty** lashes for slaughtering an animal and its offspring on a single day, **and** the animal is **unfit** for sacrifice. If the first animal was **a sacrificial** animal **and** the second was **non-sacred** and both were slaughtered **outside** the Temple courtyard, for **the first** animal, **one is liable** to receive ***karet*** for slaughtering a sacrificial animal outside the courtyard, **and** the animal is **unfit** for sacrifice. **And the second is fit** for consumption; **and** for the slaughter of **both of them** one **incurs forty** lashes apiece: The first being a sacrificial animal slaughtered outside the courtyard and the second being the offspring of an animal slaughtered that day. If the first animal was **non-sacred and** the second was **a sacrificial** animal and both were slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard, **both of them are unfit** for sacrifice. **And** for slaughtering **the second** animal, **one incurs the forty** lashes. If the first animal was **a sacrificial** animal **and** the second was **non-sacred** and both were slaughtered **inside** the Temple courtyard, **the first is fit** for sacrifice **and** one who slaughters it is **exempt** from any punishment. **And** for slaughtering **the second** animal, **one incurs the forty** lashes, **and** the animal is **unfit** for sacrifice, as it is non-sacred.

The *mishnah* now turns to cases where the two animals are of different types: one *chullin*, one *kodshim*.

*Chullin v'kodshim bachutz*: the first animal is *chullin* slaughtered outside — *harishon kasher upatur*, perfectly fine. The second animal is *kodshim* slaughtered outside. The second person certainly transgresses *oto ve'et beno*. Does he also transgress *kodshim bachutz*? No — since *oto ve'et beno* prohibits this animal's *shechita* today, it is not eligible to be brought in the *beit haMikdash* today, and so the violation of *shechitat kodshim bachutz* does not apply. *vehasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im*, the second person receives one set of lashes. *upasul*: the animal is invalid, not because of *oto ve'et beno* per se, but because it is a *korban* that was not brought in the *beit haMikdash*.

*Kodshim v'chullin bachutz*: now the order is reversed. The first animal is *kodshim* slaughtered outside — *harishon chayav karet*, lashes, and the animal is *pasul*. The second animal is *chullin* slaughtered outside. The second person transgresses *oto ve'et beno*, but the meat is *kasher* — the violation does not invalidate ordinary *chullin* meat. *usheneihem sofgin et ha'arba'im*: both receive lashes, the first for *kodshim bachutz*, the second for *oto ve'et beno*.

*Chullin v'kodshim bifnim*: both slaughtered inside the *beit haMikdash*, beginning with the *chullin*. The *chullin ba'azarah* is *pasul* — it cannot be eaten — but carries no lashes. The second animal is *kodshim* brought in the proper place, but since *oto ve'et beno* applies, it is *mechusar zman* and thus invalid. *vehasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* — only the second person receives lashes, for *oto ve'et beno*. *usheneihem psulim* — both animals are invalid, one due to *chullin ba'azarah*, the other due to *mechusar zman*.

*Kodshim v'chullin bifnim*: the reverse. The first animal is a *korban* brought in the *beit haMikdash* — *harishon kasher upatur*, done properly. The second is *chullin ba'azarah*: cannot be eaten, but no lashes for that violation. *hasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*. *upasul* — the meat cannot be eaten because of *chullin ba'azarah*.

### Same Type Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּלִּין בַּחוּץ וּבִפְנִים – הָרִאשׁוֹן כָּשֵׁר, וּפָטוּר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וּפָסוּל. קָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ וּבִפְנִים – הָרִאשׁוֹן חַיָּיב כָּרֵת, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם סוֹפְגִים אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וּשְׁנֵיהֶם פְּסוּלִים. חוּלִּין בִּפְנִים וּבַחוּץ – הָרִאשׁוֹן פָּסוּל, וּפָטוּר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וְכָשֵׁר. קָדָשִׁים בִּפְנִים וּבַחוּץ – הָרִאשׁוֹן כָּשֵׁר, וּפָטוּר, וְהַשֵּׁנִי סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים, וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** If both animals were **non-sacred,** and one slaughters them, the first **outside** the Temple courtyard **and** the second **inside** the Temple courtyard, **the first is fit** for consumption **and** one who slaughters it is **exempt** from any punishment. **And** for slaughtering **the second** animal, **one incurs the forty** lashes for slaughtering an animal and its offspring on a single day, **and** the animal is **unfit** for sacrifice as it is non-sacred. If both animals were **sacrificial** animals, and one slaughters them, the first **outside** the Temple courtyard **and** the second **inside** the Temple courtyard, for slaughtering **the first** animal **one is liable** to receive ***karet*, and** for slaughtering **both of them one incurs forty** lashes apiece. One set of lashes is given because the first was a sacrificial animal slaughtered outside the courtyard, and the second set of lashes is given because the second animal is the offspring of an animal slaughtered that day. **And both of them are unfit** for sacrifice. If both animals were **non-sacred,** and one slaughters them, the first **inside** the Temple courtyard **and** the second **outside** the Temple courtyard, **the first is unfit** for sacrifice, as it is non-sacred, **and** the one who slaughters it is **exempt. And** for **the second, one incurs the forty** lashes **and** the animal is **fit** for consumption. If both animals were **sacrificial** animals, and one slaughters them, the first **inside** the Temple courtyard **and** the second **outside** the Temple courtyard, **the first is fit** for sacrifice **and** one who slaughters it is **exempt. And** for **the second** animal, **one incurs the forty** lashes, **and** the animal is **unfit** for sacrifice because its requisite time has not yet arrived.

Having exhausted the mixed-type cases, the *mishnah* turns to cases where both animals are of the same type but slaughtered in different locations.

*Chullin bachutz uvifnim*: both animals are *chullin*, one outside and one inside the *beit haMikdash*. The first, *chullin* outside, is *kasher upatur*. The second, *chullin ba'azarah*, cannot be eaten. *hasheni sofeg arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, and *upasul* on account of *chullin ba'azarah*.

*Kodshim bachutz uvifnim*: both animals are *kodshim*, first outside and then inside. The first incurs *karet*. When the second *korban* is brought inside, *oto ve'et beno* renders it *mechusar zman* and thus *pasul*. He receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno* but not *karet*, since it was not in the *beit haMikdash*. *sheneihem sofkin et ha'arba'im* — both receive lashes — and *sheneihem psulim* as invalid *korbanot* that cannot be eaten.

*Chullin bifnim uvachutz*: *chullin* first inside, then outside. *harishon pasul upatur* — invalid as *chullin ba'azarah*, but no lashes. The second is *chullin* outside — the meat is *kasher* despite the transgression. *hasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, *vekasher*.

*Kodshim bifnim uvachutz*: a *korban* inside first — *harishon kasher upatur*, brought properly. The second is *kodshim* outside: *sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, and *upasul* because it is *kodshim* slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*. However, the second person does not incur *karet* — because *oto ve'et beno* prohibited this animal's *shechita* today, and lashes and *karet* are mutually exclusive when both apply to the same act.

The unified principle running through all these cases: the second person always receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno*. Nothing exempts the second person from that. The variables are: can the meat be eaten? What does the first person incur? Is the *korban* valid? Those depend entirely on the type of animal and the location of *shechita*. And specifically with regard to *kodshim*, the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* invalidates the *korban* through the mechanism of *mechusar zman*, thereby indirectly affecting the *kashrus* of the meat.

## Sacrifices & Crossbreeds

### Applies to Sacrifices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מִנַּיִן לְאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב אוֹ עֵז כִּי יִוָּלֵד״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְשׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה אֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ לֹא תִשְׁחֲטוּ בְּיוֹם אֶחָד״ – לִימֵּד עַל אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ שֶׁנּוֹהֵג בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived **that** the prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring** in a single day **applies to sacrificial** animals? It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "When a bull, or a sheep, or a goat, is born**…but from the eighth day and forward it may be accepted for an offering…to the Lord" (Leviticus 22:27), **and it is written** in the **following** verse: **"And whether it be a bull or a sheep, you shall not slaughter it and its offspring both in one day."** The juxtaposition of the verses **teaches with regard to** the prohibition against slaughtering an animal **itself and its offspring that it applies to sacrificial** animals as well.

The *gemara* opens with the question of the scriptural basis for applying *oto ve'et beno* to *kodshim*. The *baraita* asks: *minayin le'oto ve'et beno shenoheig bemukdashin* — from where do we know that the prohibition applies even to sacrifices?

The answer lies in the juxtaposition of verses. The passage in *vayikra* 22 begins: *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled vehaya shivat yamim tachat imo, uvayom hashmini vahalah yeratzeh lekorban isheh laShem* — the requirement to wait eight days before bringing an animal as an offering, a classic rule of *kodshim*. Immediately following, the *torah* states: *veshor o seh oto ve'et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad*. Since the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* appears directly after a passage governing sacrifices, the juxtaposition implies that it applies to *kodshim* as well. Furthermore, this same juxtaposition lends support to the *mishnah*'s ruling that a second *shechita* performed in violation of *oto ve'et beno* renders a *korban mechusar zman* — analogous to the requirement that a *korban* be at least eight days old.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין – אִין, בְּחוּלִּין – לָא! ״שׁוֹר״ הִפְסִיק הָעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** since this prohibition is taught in the context of other *halakhot* of consecrated animals, perhaps **I will say: Yes,** it applies **to sacrificial** animals, but it does **not** apply **to non-sacred** animals. The Gemara explains: The repetitive phrase "and whether it be **a bull** or a sheep" in the second verse, when those types of animals, i.e., bulls and sheep, were already mentioned in the first verse, **interrupted the topic,** clarifying that the second verse is not referring to sacrificial animals.

But perhaps the verse intends the opposite inference: since the preceding passage addressed only *kodshim*, maybe *oto ve'et beno* applies *exclusively* to *kodshim* and not to *chullin*? The *gemara* counters: *shor hifsik ha'inyan* — the repetition of the animals at the start of the prohibition (*veshor o seh*) signals a fresh topic, not a continuation of the prior context. This shows that the prohibition is not limited to sacrifices. But if so, one might reverse the inference: since a new topic has begun, perhaps the prohibition applies *only* to non-sanctified animals and not to *kodshim*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: בְּחוּלִּין – אִין, בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין – לָא! כְּתִיב: ״וְשׁוֹר״ – וָי״ו מוֹסִיף עַל עִנְיָן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** if so, **I will say: Yes,** the prohibition applies **to non-sacred** animals, but it does **not** apply **to sacrificial** animals. The Gemara explains: Since in that verse **it is written: "And whether it be a bull**…you shall not slaughter it and its offspring," the conjunction **"and" adds** the prohibition stated in the second verse **to the first matter,** including sacrificial animals as well.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *ketiv veshor* — the *vav* that introduces the verse connects it to the preceding passage even as it opens a new subject. The *vav* adds (*vav mosif al inyan rishon*): it includes *kodshim* by virtue of the connection to the prior passage, while the repetition of the animal names ensures the prohibition is not *limited* to *kodshim* but extends to *chullin* as well.

### Crossbreed Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה קָדָשִׁים כִּלְאַיִם לָא, אַף אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ כִּלְאַיִם לָא? אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בְּכִלְאַיִם וּבְכוֹי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If** this prohibition also applies to sacrificial animals, perhaps **just as** with regard to **sacrificial** animals, the offspring of **diverse kinds** is **not** included, e.g., the offspring of a ewe and a goat is unfit to be an offering, **so too** with regard to the prohibition of: **A mother and its offspring,** the offspring of **diverse kinds** should **not** be included, so that in the case of the offspring of a ewe and a goat, it would be permitted to slaughter the mother and offspring on the same day. **Why,** then, **is** it **taught** in a *baraita* (see *Tosefta* 5:1): The prohibition of: **A mother and its offspring, applies to** the offspring of **diverse kinds and to a *koy*,** a kosher animal with characteristics of both domesticated and undomesticated animals?

The *gemara* now raises a further inference from the juxtaposition. Since sacrificial law excludes *kilayim* — crossbreeds, such as the hybrid offspring of a sheep and a goat — perhaps *oto ve'et beno* likewise does not apply to crossbreeds? The *torah* says *shor o seh*, an ox or a sheep, and as *rava* teaches elsewhere, *kol makom shene'emar seh eino ela lehotzi et hakilayim* — wherever the *torah* says *seh*, it is precisely to exclude a crossbreed. *rashi* explains that *seh kesavim v'seh izim* — the *torah* specifies a *seh* of sheep and a *seh* of goats, indicating that it must be one or the other, not an intermediate. There are thus two independent reasons to exclude crossbreeds from *oto ve'et beno*.

Yet a *baraita* explicitly teaches: *oto ve'et beno noheg bekilayim uvekoy* — it applies to crossbreeds and even to the *koy*, the creature of indeterminate status (neither definitively *behemah* nor *chayah*). The *gemara* must explain how crossbreeds are included despite the double indication that they should be excluded.

### Verse Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, ״שֶׂה״ כְּתִיב, וְאָמַר רָבָא: זֶה בָּנָה אָב – כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״שֶׂה״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַכִּלְאַיִם. אָמַר קְרָא ״אוֹ״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַכִּלְאַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And additionally,** why should the prohibition of: A mother and its offspring, apply to the offspring of diverse kinds? **"A sheep," is written** in the verse with regard to that prohibition, **and Rava said** concerning the verse: "These are the animals that you may eat: An ox, a *seh* of sheep, and a *seh* of goats" (Deuteronomy 14:4), that **this** verse **establishes a paradigm** for other cases: **Wherever** the word ***seh* is stated** in the Torah, **it** serves **only to exclude** an animal of **diverse kinds.** The Hebrew word *seh* denotes either a sheep or a goat. The offspring of diverse kinds, which is neither a sheep nor a goat, does not qualify as a *seh*. The Gemara answers that with regard to a mother and its offspring, **the verse states:** "Whether it be a bull **or** a sheep" (Leviticus 22:28), and the "or" is superfluous there and serves **to include** the offspring of **diverse kinds.**

The *gemara* proposes that the word *o* — "or" — in the verse *veshor o seh* is extra and therefore available to include crossbreeds. A natural *or* would include even something intermediate between the named categories.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״אוֹ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְחַלֵּק, דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: עַד דְּשָׁחֵיט שׁוֹר וּבְנוֹ שֶׂה וּבְנוֹ לָא מִיחַיַּיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. לְחַלֵּק – מִ״בְּנוֹ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **This** word **"or" is necessary to separate** the prohibitions, **as** it might **enter your mind to say: One is not liable unless he slaughters** both **a bull and its offspring** and **a sheep and its offspring** in a single day. Therefore, the word "or" **teaches us** that one is liable for slaughtering either type of animal with its offspring. The Gemara responds: **Separating** the prohibitions **is derived from** the use of the words **"its offspring"** instead of their offspring.

One might object: the word *o* is not extra — you need it to separate the two animals (*lechaleik*), to establish that one need not slaughter both an ox with its child *and* a sheep with its child in order to be liable. The *gemara* replies: that separating function could have been derived from the word *oto* alone, since *oto ve'et beno* is in the singular — had the verse meant both together, it would have said *otam* or *bnam*. Thus *oto* already tells us we are dealing with one animal at a time, and *o* is still available to include crossbreeds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה וּבְנוֹ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיִּשְׁחוֹט שׁוֹר וָשֶׂה וּבְנוֹ, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ״. מַאי לָאו מֵ״אוֹ״ נָפְקָא לֵיהּ? לָא, מֵ״אוֹתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** the word "or" **is still necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **If it were stated: A bull, and a sheep, and its offspring** you shall not slaughter in one day, **I would say:** One is not liable **unless he slaughters a bull, and a sheep, and the offspring** of one of them in a single day. Therefore, **the verse states: "A bull or a sheep…it and its offspring"** (Leviticus 22:28), to teach that one is liable even for slaughtering either of them and its offspring. **What, is it not from** the word **"or"** that the *baraita* **derives** this *halakha*? The Gemara responds: **No,** it is derived **from** the word **"it,"** and the offspring of diverse kinds are included in the prohibition due to the word "or."

But the *gemara* pushes back: *oto* is itself needed for another purpose. Consider a hypothetical verse: *shor veseh uvno* — an ox and a sheep and its child. Even granting that *bno* (singular) clarifies we need only one child, one might have thought we require all three animals: an ox, a sheep, and one child. The actual verse — *shor o seh oto ve'et beno* — uses *o* to make clear that the prohibition involves either an ox or a sheep (two animals only), not a combination of three.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבָּנַן, דִּמְיַיתַּר לְהוּ ״אוֹתוֹ״, אֶלָּא לַחֲנַנְיָה, דְּלָא מְיַיתַּר לֵיהּ ״אוֹתוֹ״, לְחַלֵּק מְנָא לֵיהּ? לְחַלֵּק לָא צְרִיךְ קְרָא, דְּסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹנָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** cited further in the discussion, **for whom** the word **"it" is superfluous** and can be used for this derivation, leaving the word "or" available to include the offspring of diverse kinds; **but according to** the opinion of **Ḥananya, for whom** the word **"it" is not superfluous, from where does he** derive that one is **to separate** into two prohibitions slaughtering either a bull with its offspring or a sheep with its offspring? The Gemara answers that there is **no need** for **a verse to separate** them into two prohibitions, **as** Ḥananya **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yonatan.**

The *gemara* counters once more: *lo me'oto* — even this separation is derivable without *o*, because the word *oto ve'et beno* already implies one parent and one child. Had the verse said *veshor veseh oto ve'et beno* (without *o*), the singular *oto ve'et beno* at the end would already clarify we mean one animal and one child — you cannot read *oto ve'et beno* (singular) as referring to both an ox and a sheep together; that would have required *otam* or *bnam*. Therefore the *o* remains available to include *kilayim*.

The *gemara* then raises the position of *chananya* (whose view will be elaborated shortly): *chananya* uses the word *oto* for a different purpose — to extend the prohibition to *fathers* as well as mothers. For him, *oto* is not available to perform the *lechaleik* function. Does he then need the word *o* for that?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יְקַלֵּל אֶת אָבִיו וְאֶת אִמּוֹ״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ, אָבִיו שֶׁלֹּא אִמּוֹ וְאִמּוֹ שֶׁלֹּא אָבִיו מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ קִלֵּל״ – אָבִיו קִלֵּל, אִמּוֹ קִלֵּל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: מַשְׁמָע שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּאֶחָד, וּמַשְׁמָע אֶחָד בִּפְנֵי עַצְמוֹ, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרוֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב ״יַחְדָּו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: From the verse: **"A man who curses his father and his mother** shall die" (Leviticus 20:9), **I have** derived **only** that one is liable if he curses both **his father and his mother. From where** do I derive that if one curses **his father but not his mother,** or **his mother but not his father,** he is liable? The continuation of **the verse states: "His father and his mother he has cursed,** his blood is upon him." In the first part of the verse, the word "curses" is in proximity to "his father," and in the last part of the verse, "cursed" is in proximity to "his mother." This teaches that the verse is referring to both a case where **he cursed** only **his father** and a case where **he cursed** only **his mother;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya.** **Rabbi Yonatan says:** There is no need for this derivation, because the phrase "his father and his mother" **indicates** that one is liable if he curses **both of them together, and it** also **indicates** that he is liable if he curses either **one** of them **on their own, unless the verse specifies** that one is liable only if he curses both **together.** An example of a verse where the Torah specifies that the *halakha* applies only to the two elements in conjunction is: "You shall not plow with an ox and a donkey together" (Deuteronomy 22:10). According to Rabbi Yonatan, had the verse stated with regard to a mother and its offspring: A bull and a sheep, and not: A bull or a sheep, one would still be liable for slaughtering each with its own offspring independently. Therefore, the word "or" is superfluous, and is utilized by Ḥananya, who agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yonatan, to include the offspring of diverse kinds in this prohibition.

The answer is: *chananya* holds like *rabbi yonatan*, who maintains that a *vav* in the *torah* can always be read as "or" unless the verse explicitly says *yachdav* ("together," as in *lo tacharosh beshor uvachamor yachdav*). This is in contrast to *rabbi yoshiya*, who requires an additional verse to establish that a *vav* means "or" rather than "and." Since *chananya* reads *vav* as naturally capable of meaning "or," he does not need a separate word to establish *lechaleik* — and the word *o* is therefore available for him as well to include *kilayim*.

## Mother vs. Father

### Rabbis' View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חֲנַנְיָה, וּמַאי רַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בִּנְקֵבוֹת, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹהֵג בִּזְכָרִים. חֲנַנְיָה אוֹמֵר: נוֹהֵג בֵּין בִּזְכָרִים וּבֵין בִּנְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the opinion of **Ḥananya, and what** is the opinion of **the Rabbis** that were mentioned earlier? Their opinions are elucidated **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: Despite the fact that the verse is written in the masculine form, the prohibition against slaughtering **itself and its offspring** in a single day **applies to females,** i.e., to a mother and its offspring, **but** it **does not apply to males,** i.e., a male animal and its offspring. **Ḥananya says: It applies both to males and to females.**

Having established that *chananya* uses the word *oto* to extend the prohibition to fathers, the *gemara* now examines the full debate between the Rabbis and *chananya* regarding whether *oto ve'et beno* applies to the mother alone or to both parents.

A *baraita* states: *oto ve'et beno noheg binkevot ve'eino noheg bizcharim* — it applies to mothers and not to fathers. This is, on its face, surprising: the word *oto* is in the masculine, yet it is the Rabbis who restrict the prohibition to the female. *chananya omer*: *noheg bein bizcharim bein binkevot* — it applies to both fathers and mothers.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל יְהֵא אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ נוֹהֵג בֵּין בִּזְכָרִים וּבֵין בִּנְקֵבוֹת? וְדִין הוּא: חִיֵּיב כָּאן, וְחַיָּיב בְּאֵם עַל הַבָּנִים; מָה כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב בְּאֵם עַל הַבָּנִים – בִּנְקֵבוֹת וְלֹא בִּזְכָרִים, אַף כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב כָּאן – בִּנְקֵבוֹת וְלֹא בִּזְכָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of the Rabbis,** i.e., the first *tanna*? Their reasoning is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that the prohibition against slaughtering **a mother and its offspring would apply both to males and to females.** But could one not derive **this** by **logical inference,** reaching the opposite conclusion: The Torah **rendered one obligated here** not to slaughter an animal and its offspring in a single day, **and** the Torah **rendered one obligated with regard to a mother bird with its chicks** not to seize them together, but to dispatch the mother. **Just as when it rendered one obligated with regard to a mother bird with its chicks,** the obligation applies **to female** birds **but not to males,** as the verse states: "And the mother sitting on the chicks" (Deuteronomy 22:6), **so too, when it rendered one obligated here,** with regard to an animal and its offspring, the obligation should apply **to female** animals, **but not to males.**

The *gemara* asks for the reasoning of the Rabbis. A *baraita* first considers whether the prohibition might have been extended to fathers through a logical argument. One might reason: just as *shiluach haken* — the obligation to send away the mother bird — applies only to the female, so too *oto ve'et beno* should apply only to the female, by analogy. But one might counter that this analogy is problematic: *shiluach haken* applies only in a case of *eino mezuman* — when one chances upon the nest unexpectedly (*ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha*), not when the birds are under one's control. *oto ve'et beno*, by contrast, applies even to *mezuman* — animals that are domesticated and set aside. Since *oto ve'et beno* is more stringent in that respect, perhaps it should also extend to fathers where *shiluach haken* does not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּאֵם עַל הַבָּנִים, שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשָׂה בָּהּ מְזוּמָּן כְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְזוּמָּן, תֹּאמַר בְּאוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, שֶׁעָשָׂה בּוֹ מְזוּמָּן כְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְזוּמָּן?
>
> **Translation:** One may respond: **No, if you say** that this is so **with regard to a mother bird with its chicks, for which** the Torah **did not render prepared** ones equivalent **to unprepared** ones, as the obligation to dispatch the mother bird applies only where one happens to encounter a mother bird with its chicks spontaneously, but not to ones that he keeps in his property, **shall you** also **say** that this is so **with regard to** the prohibition of an animal **itself and its offspring,** for **which** the Torah **rendered prepared** ones equivalent **to unprepared** ones, prohibiting an animal and its offspring even if they are prepared? If so, the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring should apply to both males and females.

The *baraita* answers: the verse says *oto* — in the singular — to teach *echad velo shenayim*: only one of the two parents. Once the verse limits the prohibition to one parent, we may then reason: which parent? By analogy to the case of the mother bird, where the verse (*ha'em rovetzet al habanim*) specifies the mother explicitly, we derive that here too the prohibition applies to the mother and not the father.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אוֹתוֹ״ – אֶחָד וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם. אַחַר שֶׁחִלֵּק הַכָּתוּב, זָכִיתִי לְדִין: חִיֵּיב כָּאן וְחִיֵּיב בְּאֵם עַל הַבָּנִים – מָה כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב בְּאֵם עַל הַבָּנִים – בִּנְקֵבוֹת וְלֹא בִּזְכָרִים, אַף כְּשֶׁחִיֵּיב כָּאן – בִּנְקֵבוֹת וְלֹא בִּזְכָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, **the verse states:** "A bull or a sheep, **it** and its offspring" (Leviticus 22:28). The superfluous word "it" indicates that this applies to only **one** parent, **but not** to **two.** The *baraita* continues: **After the verse separated** the parents, rendering the prohibition applicable to only one of them, **I merited** returning **to the logical inference** mentioned earlier: The Torah **rendered one obligated here** not to slaughter an animal and its offspring in a single day, **and** the Torah **rendered one obligated** to dispatch the mother **with regard to a mother bird with its chicks. Just as when it rendered one obligated with regard to a mother bird with its chicks,** the obligation applies **to females but not to males, so too, when it rendered one obligated here,** the obligation applies **to females but not to males.**

A further dimension of the Rabbis' reasoning: one might attempt a *kal vachomer* to include fathers. The argument would run: *chayav kan v'chayav be'em al habanim* — there is liability here (in *oto ve'et beno*) and there is liability by the mother bird. Just as there it is explicit that the prohibition concerns the mother, so here too the prohibition should be read to concern the mother. The *kal vachomer* approach would have extended the prohibition *beyond* the mother by reasoning from the more lenient case (*shiluach haken*, which applies only by *eino mezuman*) to the more stringent one (*oto ve'et beno*, which applies even by *mezuman*). But the word *oto*, establishing that only one parent is involved, blocks this extension and forces the comparison in the other direction: once we know it is one parent, we identify which by analogy to the established case of the mother bird.

### Biological Closeness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר, ״בְּנוֹ״ – מִי שֶׁבְּנוֹ כָּרוּךְ אַחֲרָיו, יָצָא זָכָר שֶׁאֵין בְּנוֹ כָּרוּךְ אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is your wish to say** that one can refute this, that refutation can be countered by the following derivation: The verse states: "It and **its offspring"** (Leviticus 22:28), indicating that this applies to **that** parent **whose offspring clings to it.** This serves to **exclude** the **male** parent, **whose offspring does not cling to it.**

Should one object that this analogy is insufficient to override the plain masculine language of the verse (*oto zachar mashma*), the *baraita* offers an alternative rationale: *mi she'beno karuch acharav* — the prohibition applies to one whose child literally entwines itself after it, to the one with whom the child has an intense and enveloping bond. *yatza zachar she'ein beno karuch acharav* — the male is excluded because the father-child bond in the animal world does not have this character.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה אִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר? וְכִי תֵּימָא ״אוֹתוֹ״ זָכָר מַשְׁמַע, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״בְּנוֹ״ – מִי שֶׁבְּנוֹ כָּרוּךְ אַחֲרָיו, יָצָא זָכָר שֶׁאֵין בְּנוֹ כָּרוּךְ אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: To **what** possible refutation is the expression: **If it is your wish to say,** referring? The Gemara explains that the possible refutation is: **And if you would say** that the word **"it,"** in the verse **denotes a male,** as it is expressed in the masculine gender in the Hebrew, the response is that the verse also **states "its offspring"** in that verse, indicating that this applies to **that** parent **whose offspring clings to it.** This serves to **exclude** the **male** parent, **whose offspring does not cling to it.**

This rationale speaks to a deeper issue that the *gemara* is treating as foundational here: *chosheshin lezera ha'av* — the question of whether we attribute significance to the paternal line among animals. In the human sphere, halacha traces lineage and identity through the father, giving primacy to the paternal relationship in ways that override or complicate the biological closeness of the mother. But that structure is a product of human social organization — a recognition of fatherhood as a social and legal institution. Animals do not have that social structure. The father's contribution is biological but brief; the relationship between a father and his offspring in the animal world lacks the sustained bond that gives the paternal relationship its weight in human law. Among animals, the relationship of parent and child — the family unit that the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* is meant to protect or recognize — is a reality lived almost entirely through the mother. *beno karuch acharav*: it is the mother whose child is enwrapped with her.

This is why, despite the masculine language of the verse, *chazal* did not find it plausible that the *torah*'s concern here could meaningfully extend to the father in the animal context. The analogy to *shiluach haken* and the phrase *beno karuch acharav* are not merely technical derivations; they articulate a coherent understanding of what the prohibition is about and why its scope, in the animal world, is naturally delimited to the mother.

### Chananya's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלַחֲנַנְיָה, כְּתִיב ״אוֹתוֹ״ דְּמַשְׁמַע זָכָר, וּכְתִיב ״בְּנוֹ״ – מִי שֶׁבְּנוֹ כָּרוּךְ אַחֲרָיו דְּמַשְׁמַע נְקֵבָה, הִלְכָּךְ נוֹהֵג בֵּין בִּזְכָרִים בֵּין בִּנְקֵבוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to** the opinion of **Ḥananya,** the reason for his ruling is that **it is written "it," which indicates a male, and it is written "its offspring,"** teaching that the prohibition applies to **that** parent **whose offspring clings to it, which indicates a female. Therefore,** this prohibition **applies to both males and females.**

*Chananya* reads the verse differently. *ketiv oto d'mashma zachar* — the verse says *oto*, which indicates the masculine, the father. *uchetiv beno* — but it also says *beno*, its child — using the possessive, which connotes an intense parent-child bond, *mi she'beno karuch acharav*, the one whose child entwines itself after it, which ordinarily suggests the mother. *chananya* does not resolve this tension in favor of one reading; instead he reads both signals together. The masculine *oto* includes the father; the relational *beno* includes the mother. *hilkach noheg bein bizcharim bein benekevot* — therefore the prohibition applies to both fathers and mothers.

The practical implications of *chananya*'s view are significant. One might ask: if the prohibition extends to fathers, how can it ever be practically enforced? In most herds, it will simply not be known which male fathered which offspring — and if it cannot be known, the question of whether one has triggered the prohibition by the second *shechita* may be practically unresolvable. That is a second-order question — whether the prohibition applies to fathers at all must be established before one can address what to do when paternity is unknown.
