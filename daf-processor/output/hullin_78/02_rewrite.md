# Hullin 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oto Ve'et Beno Intro

### Chapter Overview

With the opening of this new *perek*, we transition from the demanding material of *treifos*, severed limbs, and fetal edge cases into territory that is, comparatively speaking, more accessible — though no less rich. The subject is *oto ve'et beno*: the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day, as stated in *vayikra* 22:28: *oto ve'et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad*.

It is worth pausing to situate this chapter within the broader structure of *masechet hullin*. The *perek* belongs to a cluster of chapters dealing not strictly with the *kashrus* of the animal itself, but with the range of obligations and prohibitions that surround the act of *shechita* and the consumption of meat. Over the coming chapters the same structural questions will recur across multiple *mitzvot* — *gid hanasheh*, *reishit hagez*, *zero'a lechayayim vekivah* — and in each case the *mishnah* will systematically address: Does the law apply in the Land of Israel or outside it? With the *beit haMikdash* or without it? To *chullin* (non-sanctified animals) or also to *kodshim* (sacrifices)? This chapter on *oto ve'et beno* is the first of that series, and the reader should keep that overarching architectural purpose in mind.

### Mishnah Opening

The *mishnah* opens by establishing the scope of the prohibition. *oto ve'et beno noheg bein ba'aretz bein bechutz la'aretz* — it applies whether one is in the Land of Israel or outside it. This is expected: the prohibition has nothing to do with agricultural conditions or the sanctity of the land, so there is no basis for restricting it geographically. The *mishnah* adds: *bein bifnei habayit bein shelo bifnei habayit* — whether the *beit haMikdash* stands or not. Again, this is intuitive. And finally: *bechullin uvemukdashin* — it applies to both non-sanctified animals and sanctified ones. It is this last point — the extension to *kodshim* — that will drive much of the subsequent discussion.

## Mishna Permutations

### Chullin Bachutz

Before examining the full matrix of cases, several foundational parameters of *oto ve'et beno* must be stated. First, the order of *shechita* is immaterial: whether one slaughters the mother first or the child first, the prohibition is equally triggered. Second, the two *shechitot* need not be performed by the same person; it is always the one who performs the *second* slaughter who transgresses. Third — and critically — the *shechita* itself remains valid. Transgressing the prohibition does not render the meat forbidden.

The *mishnah* then works through the permutations. The simplest case: *chullin bachutz* — both animals are non-sanctified and are slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*. *sheneihem kesherim*: both animals are *kasher*. The meat may be eaten. The second person, however — whoever performs the second *shechita* — *sofeg et ha'arba'im*, absorbs the forty lashes for having violated the prohibition.

## Oto Ve'et Beno Intro…

### Prohibition Parameters

Before the *mishnah* can proceed to the more complex permutations, it is necessary to lay out a set of parameters governing *kodshim*. When a *korban* is slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*, the offender violates *shechitat chutz* and is liable to *karet* (and lashes, since virtually every *karet* offense also carries lashes). The sacrifice, moreover, is entirely invalid and may not be eaten. If, on the other hand, a *korban* is slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash* on a day when its mother or offspring has already been slaughtered, the offender does not incur liability for *kodshim bachutz* — because that violation requires that the animal *could* have been brought inside. Since *oto ve'et beno* prohibits its *shechita* that day, it is no longer eligible for the *beit haMikdash* that day, and therefore the prohibition of slaughtering *kodshim* outside does not apply.

A further parameter: *chullin* slaughtered inside the *beit haMikdash* — *chullin ba'azarah* — is prohibited, but carries no lashes, since the prohibition is derived from a positive commandment rather than an explicit negative one. The meat nonetheless cannot be eaten.

These parameters, taken together with the structure of *oto ve'et beno* itself, set up the matrix of cases the *mishnah* will now work through. The organizing question is: given the interaction between the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* and the distinct rules governing *kodshim*, what are the *kashrus* and liability consequences in each scenario?

## Mishna Permutations…

### Kodshim Bachutz

When both animals are *kodshim* and both are slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*: the first person incurs *karet* for *shechitat kodshim bachutz*, and both animals are *pasul* — invalid as sacrifices and forbidden to eat. As for liability, note that the word *sheneihem* appears twice in this *mishnah*: once referring to the animals (both invalid), and once referring to the people. Both people *sofgin et ha'arba'im* — they each receive lashes. The first for the violation of slaughtering *kodshim* outside the *beit haMikdash*; the second for the violation of *oto ve'et beno*.

Why does the second person not also receive lashes — or *karet* — for *kodshim bachutz*? Because by the time the second *shechita* is performed, *oto ve'et beno* has already prohibited slaughtering this animal that day. An animal that may not be slaughtered today is not eligible to be brought as a *korban* today. And since it could not have been brought inside that day, slaughtering it outside does not constitute the offense of *shechitat kodshim bachutz*.

There is a corollary worth noting here. When *kodshim* are slaughtered in the proper place — inside the *beit haMikdash* — the first person's *shechita* is perfectly valid; *harishon kasher upatur*, the animal is *kasher* and he is exempt. The second person performs his *shechita* in the right place, but at the wrong time — the mother was slaughtered that day. The principle of *mechusar zman* applies: just as the *torah* requires an animal to be at least eight days old before it may be offered (*uvayom hashmini vahalah yeratzeh lekorban isheh laShem*), so too any animal whose offering is prohibited on a given day by virtue of *oto ve'et beno* is treated as *mechusar zman* — lacking its proper time — and is therefore invalid as a *korban*. The second person receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno* and the *korban* is *pasul*.

This distinction is significant: by ordinary *chullin*, the violation of *oto ve'et beno* does not affect the *kashrus* of the meat at all. By *kodshim*, the prohibition indirectly invalidates the sacrifice through the principle of *mechusar zman*, which in turn means the meat cannot be eaten.

### Mixed Groups

The *mishnah* now turns to cases where the two animals are of different types: one *chullin*, one *kodshim*.

*Chullin v'kodshim bachutz*: the first animal is *chullin* slaughtered outside — *harishon kasher upatur*, perfectly fine. The second animal is *kodshim* slaughtered outside. The second person certainly transgresses *oto ve'et beno*. Does he also transgress *kodshim bachutz*? No — since *oto ve'et beno* prohibits this animal's *shechita* today, it is not eligible to be brought in the *beit haMikdash* today, and so the violation of *shechitat kodshim bachutz* does not apply. *vehasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im*, the second person receives one set of lashes. *upasul*: the animal is invalid, not because of *oto ve'et beno* per se, but because it is a *korban* that was not brought in the *beit haMikdash*.

*Kodshim v'chullin bachutz*: now the order is reversed. The first animal is *kodshim* slaughtered outside — *harishon chayav karet*, lashes, and the animal is *pasul*. The second animal is *chullin* slaughtered outside. The second person transgresses *oto ve'et beno*, but the meat is *kasher* — the violation does not invalidate ordinary *chullin* meat. *usheneihem sofgin et ha'arba'im*: both receive lashes, the first for *kodshim bachutz*, the second for *oto ve'et beno*.

*Chullin v'kodshim bifnim*: both slaughtered inside the *beit haMikdash*, beginning with the *chullin*. The *chullin ba'azarah* is *pasul* — it cannot be eaten — but carries no lashes. The second animal is *kodshim* brought in the proper place, but since *oto ve'et beno* applies, it is *mechusar zman* and thus invalid. *vehasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* — only the second person receives lashes, for *oto ve'et beno*. *usheneihem psulim* — both animals are invalid, one due to *chullin ba'azarah*, the other due to *mechusar zman*.

*Kodshim v'chullin bifnim*: the reverse. The first animal is a *korban* brought in the *beit haMikdash* — *harishon kasher upatur*, done properly. The second is *chullin ba'azarah*: cannot be eaten, but no lashes for that violation. *hasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*. *upasul* — the meat cannot be eaten because of *chullin ba'azarah*.

### Same Type Locations

Having exhausted the mixed-type cases, the *mishnah* turns to cases where both animals are of the same type but slaughtered in different locations.

*Chullin bachutz uvifnim*: both animals are *chullin*, one outside and one inside the *beit haMikdash*. The first, *chullin* outside, is *kasher upatur*. The second, *chullin ba'azarah*, cannot be eaten. *hasheni sofeg arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, and *upasul* on account of *chullin ba'azarah*.

*Kodshim bachutz uvifnim*: both animals are *kodshim*, first outside and then inside. The first incurs *karet*. When the second *korban* is brought inside, *oto ve'et beno* renders it *mechusar zman* and thus *pasul*. He receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno* but not *karet*, since it was not in the *beit haMikdash*. *sheneihem sofkin et ha'arba'im* — both receive lashes — and *sheneihem psulim* as invalid *korbanot* that cannot be eaten.

*Chullin bifnim uvachutz*: *chullin* first inside, then outside. *harishon pasul upatur* — invalid as *chullin ba'azarah*, but no lashes. The second is *chullin* outside — the meat is *kasher* despite the transgression. *hasheni sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, *vekasher*.

*Kodshim bifnim uvachutz*: a *korban* inside first — *harishon kasher upatur*, brought properly. The second is *kodshim* outside: *sofeg et ha'arba'im* for *oto ve'et beno*, and *upasul* because it is *kodshim* slaughtered outside the *beit haMikdash*. However, the second person does not incur *karet* — because *oto ve'et beno* prohibited this animal's *shechita* today, and lashes and *karet* are mutually exclusive when both apply to the same act.

The unified principle running through all these cases: the second person always receives lashes for *oto ve'et beno*. Nothing exempts the second person from that. The variables are: can the meat be eaten? What does the first person incur? Is the *korban* valid? Those depend entirely on the type of animal and the location of *shechita*. And specifically with regard to *kodshim*, the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* invalidates the *korban* through the mechanism of *mechusar zman*, thereby indirectly affecting the *kashrus* of the meat.

## Sacrifices & Crossbreeds

### Applies to Sacrifices

The *gemara* opens with the question of the scriptural basis for applying *oto ve'et beno* to *kodshim*. The *baraita* asks: *minayin le'oto ve'et beno shenoheig bemukdashin* — from where do we know that the prohibition applies even to sacrifices?

The answer lies in the juxtaposition of verses. The passage in *vayikra* 22 begins: *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled vehaya shivat yamim tachat imo, uvayom hashmini vahalah yeratzeh lekorban isheh laShem* — the requirement to wait eight days before bringing an animal as an offering, a classic rule of *kodshim*. Immediately following, the *torah* states: *veshor o seh oto ve'et beno lo tishchatu beyom echad*. Since the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* appears directly after a passage governing sacrifices, the juxtaposition implies that it applies to *kodshim* as well. Furthermore, this same juxtaposition lends support to the *mishnah*'s ruling that a second *shechita* performed in violation of *oto ve'et beno* renders a *korban mechusar zman* — analogous to the requirement that a *korban* be at least eight days old.

But perhaps the verse intends the opposite inference: since the preceding passage addressed only *kodshim*, maybe *oto ve'et beno* applies *exclusively* to *kodshim* and not to *chullin*? The *gemara* counters: *shor hifsik ha'inyan* — the repetition of the animals at the start of the prohibition (*veshor o seh*) signals a fresh topic, not a continuation of the prior context. This shows that the prohibition is not limited to sacrifices. But if so, one might reverse the inference: since a new topic has begun, perhaps the prohibition applies *only* to non-sanctified animals and not to *kodshim*? The *gemara* resolves the tension: *ketiv veshor* — the *vav* that introduces the verse connects it to the preceding passage even as it opens a new subject. The *vav* adds (*vav mosif al inyan rishon*): it includes *kodshim* by virtue of the connection to the prior passage, while the repetition of the animal names ensures the prohibition is not *limited* to *kodshim* but extends to *chullin* as well.

### Crossbreed Debate

The *gemara* now raises a further inference from the juxtaposition. Since sacrificial law excludes *kilayim* — crossbreeds, such as the hybrid offspring of a sheep and a goat — perhaps *oto ve'et beno* likewise does not apply to crossbreeds? The *torah* says *shor o seh*, an ox or a sheep, and as *rava* teaches elsewhere, *kol makom shene'emar seh eino ela lehotzi et hakilayim* — wherever the *torah* says *seh*, it is precisely to exclude a crossbreed. *rashi* explains that *seh kesavim v'seh izim* — the *torah* specifies a *seh* of sheep and a *seh* of goats, indicating that it must be one or the other, not an intermediate. There are thus two independent reasons to exclude crossbreeds from *oto ve'et beno*.

Yet a *baraita* explicitly teaches: *oto ve'et beno noheg bekilayim uvekoy* — it applies to crossbreeds and even to the *koy*, the creature of indeterminate status (neither definitively *behemah* nor *chayah*). The *gemara* must explain how crossbreeds are included despite the double indication that they should be excluded.

### Verse Analysis

The *gemara* proposes that the word *o* — "or" — in the verse *veshor o seh* is extra and therefore available to include crossbreeds. A natural *or* would include even something intermediate between the named categories.

One might object: the word *o* is not extra — you need it to separate the two animals (*lechaleik*), to establish that one need not slaughter both an ox with its child *and* a sheep with its child in order to be liable. The *gemara* replies: that separating function could have been derived from the word *oto* alone, since *oto ve'et beno* is in the singular — had the verse meant both together, it would have said *otam* or *bnam*. Thus *oto* already tells us we are dealing with one animal at a time, and *o* is still available to include crossbreeds.

But the *gemara* pushes back: *oto* is itself needed for another purpose. Consider a hypothetical verse: *shor veseh uvno* — an ox and a sheep and its child. Even granting that *bno* (singular) clarifies we need only one child, one might have thought we require all three animals: an ox, a sheep, and one child. The actual verse — *shor o seh oto ve'et beno* — uses *o* to make clear that the prohibition involves either an ox or a sheep (two animals only), not a combination of three.

The *gemara* counters once more: *lo me'oto* — even this separation is derivable without *o*, because the word *oto ve'et beno* already implies one parent and one child. Had the verse said *veshor veseh oto ve'et beno* (without *o*), the singular *oto ve'et beno* at the end would already clarify we mean one animal and one child — you cannot read *oto ve'et beno* (singular) as referring to both an ox and a sheep together; that would have required *otam* or *bnam*. Therefore the *o* remains available to include *kilayim*.

The *gemara* then raises the position of *chananya* (whose view will be elaborated shortly): *chananya* uses the word *oto* for a different purpose — to extend the prohibition to *fathers* as well as mothers. For him, *oto* is not available to perform the *lechaleik* function. Does he then need the word *o* for that? The *gemara* answers: *chananya* holds like *rabbi yonatan*, who maintains that a *vav* in the *torah* can always be read as "or" unless the verse explicitly says *yachdav* ("together," as in *lo tacharosh beshor uvachamor yachdav*). This is in contrast to *rabbi yoshiya*, who requires an additional verse to establish that a *vav* means "or" rather than "and." Since *chananya* reads *vav* as naturally capable of meaning "or," he does not need a separate word to establish *lechaleik* — and the word *o* is therefore available for him as well to include *kilayim*.

## Mother vs. Father

### Rabbis' View

Having established that *chananya* uses the word *oto* to extend the prohibition to fathers, the *gemara* now examines the full debate between the Rabbis and *chananya* regarding whether *oto ve'et beno* applies to the mother alone or to both parents.

A *baraita* states: *oto ve'et beno noheg binkevot ve'eino noheg bizcharim* — it applies to mothers and not to fathers. This is, on its face, surprising: the word *oto* is in the masculine, yet it is the Rabbis who restrict the prohibition to the female. *chananya omer*: *noheg bein bizcharim bein binkevot* — it applies to both fathers and mothers.

The *gemara* asks for the reasoning of the Rabbis. A *baraita* first considers whether the prohibition might have been extended to fathers through a logical argument. One might reason: just as *shiluach haken* — the obligation to send away the mother bird — applies only to the female, so too *oto ve'et beno* should apply only to the female, by analogy. But one might counter that this analogy is problematic: *shiluach haken* applies only in a case of *eino mezuman* — when one chances upon the nest unexpectedly (*ki yikarei kan tzippor lefanecha*), not when the birds are under one's control. *oto ve'et beno*, by contrast, applies even to *mezuman* — animals that are domesticated and set aside. Since *oto ve'et beno* is more stringent in that respect, perhaps it should also extend to fathers where *shiluach haken* does not.

The *baraita* answers: the verse says *oto* — in the singular — to teach *echad velo shenayim*: only one of the two parents. Once the verse limits the prohibition to one parent, we may then reason: which parent? By analogy to the case of the mother bird, where the verse (*ha'em rovetzet al habanim*) specifies the mother explicitly, we derive that here too the prohibition applies to the mother and not the father.

### Kal Vachomer Logic

A further dimension of the Rabbis' reasoning: one might attempt a *kal vachomer* to include fathers. The argument would run: *chayav kan v'chayav be'em al habanim* — there is liability here (in *oto ve'et beno*) and there is liability by the mother bird. Just as there it is explicit that the prohibition concerns the mother, so here too the prohibition should be read to concern the mother. The *kal vachomer* approach would have extended the prohibition *beyond* the mother by reasoning from the more lenient case (*shiluach haken*, which applies only by *eino mezuman*) to the more stringent one (*oto ve'et beno*, which applies even by *mezuman*). But the word *oto*, establishing that only one parent is involved, blocks this extension and forces the comparison in the other direction: once we know it is one parent, we identify which by analogy to the established case of the mother bird.

### Biological Closeness

Should one object that this analogy is insufficient to override the plain masculine language of the verse (*oto zachar mashma*), the *baraita* offers an alternative rationale: *mi she'beno karuch acharav* — the prohibition applies to one whose child literally entwines itself after it, to the one with whom the child has an intense and enveloping bond. *yatza zachar she'ein beno karuch acharav* — the male is excluded because the father-child bond in the animal world does not have this character.

This rationale speaks to a deeper issue that the *gemara* is treating as foundational here: *chosheshin lezera ha'av* — the question of whether we attribute significance to the paternal line among animals. In the human sphere, halacha traces lineage and identity through the father, giving primacy to the paternal relationship in ways that override or complicate the biological closeness of the mother. But that structure is a product of human social organization — a recognition of fatherhood as a social and legal institution. Animals do not have that social structure. The father's contribution is biological but brief; the relationship between a father and his offspring in the animal world lacks the sustained bond that gives the paternal relationship its weight in human law. Among animals, the relationship of parent and child — the family unit that the prohibition of *oto ve'et beno* is meant to protect or recognize — is a reality lived almost entirely through the mother. *beno karuch acharav*: it is the mother whose child is enwrapped with her.

This is why, despite the masculine language of the verse, *chazal* did not find it plausible that the *torah*'s concern here could meaningfully extend to the father in the animal context. The analogy to *shiluach haken* and the phrase *beno karuch acharav* are not merely technical derivations; they articulate a coherent understanding of what the prohibition is about and why its scope, in the animal world, is naturally delimited to the mother.

### Chananya's View

*Chananya* reads the verse differently. *ketiv oto d'mashma zachar* — the verse says *oto*, which indicates the masculine, the father. *uchetiv beno* — but it also says *beno*, its child — using the possessive, which connotes an intense parent-child bond, *mi she'beno karuch acharav*, the one whose child entwines itself after it, which ordinarily suggests the mother. *chananya* does not resolve this tension in favor of one reading; instead he reads both signals together. The masculine *oto* includes the father; the relational *beno* includes the mother. *hilkach noheg bein bizcharim bein benekevot* — therefore the prohibition applies to both fathers and mothers.

The practical implications of *chananya*'s view are significant. One might ask: if the prohibition extends to fathers, how can it ever be practically enforced? In most herds, it will simply not be known which male fathered which offspring — and if it cannot be known, the question of whether one has triggered the prohibition by the second *shechita* may be practically unresolvable. That is a second-order question — whether the prohibition applies to fathers at all must be established before one can address what to do when paternity is unknown.
