# Sanhedrin 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Five Groups/Dry Bones

### Five Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן נִינְהוּ מֵתִים שֶׁהֶחְיָה יְחֶזְקֵאל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And who are** the **dead that Ezekiel revived?**

The *gemara* on 92b asks: *uman nin hu meisim shehechaya Yechezkel* — who are the dead that *Yechezkel* revived in the valley? The *gemara* identifies five distinct groups, and there is reason to suggest that for approximately two thousand years those five categories may not have resonated with the same force as they do in the last hundred years or so — and that is perhaps what has prompted a second look.

### Bnei Ephraim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אֶפְרַיִם שֶׁמָּנוּ לַקֵּץ וְטָעוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְנֵי אֶפְרַיִם שׁוּתָלַח וּבֶרֶד בְּנוֹ וְתַחַת בְּנוֹ וְאֶלְעָדָה בְנוֹ וְתַחַת בְּנוֹ וְזָבָד בְּנוֹ וְשׁוּתֶלַח בְּנוֹ וְעֵזֶר וְאֶלְעָד וַהֲרָגוּם אַנְשֵׁי גַת הַנּוֹלָדִים בָּאָרֶץ וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּתְאַבֵּל אֶפְרַיִם אֲבִיהֶם יָמִים רַבִּים וַיָּבֹאוּ אֶחָיו לְנַחֲמוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: These were the descendants of Ephraim who calculated** the time **of the end** of the enslavement and the redemption from Egypt **and erred** in their calculation. They left before the appointed time and were killed, **as it is stated: "And the sons of Ephraim; Shuthelah, and Bered his son, and Tahath his son, and Eleadah his son, and Tahath his son. And Zabad his son, and Shuthelah his son, and Ezer and Elead whom the men of Gath that were born in the land slew,** because they came down to take their cattle" (I Chronicles 7:20–21), **and it is written: "And Ephraim their father mourned many days, and his brothers came to comfort him"** (I Chronicles 7:22).

The first group is *bnei Ephraim shemanu laketz veta'u* — those who miscalculated when the slavery was to end. We know the Egyptian servitude was decreed for four hundred years, though the precise starting point was disputed. This group hastened the *geulah*, pushed ahead prematurely, and paid dearly for it: *haragu anshei Gat hanoladim ba'aretz* — the Philistines of Gat who were already settled in the land attacked and killed them, because the time was not yet ripe. These are the people who press for faster redemption before redemption is ready.

### Rejected Resurrection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁכָּפְרוּ בִּתְחִיַּית הַמֵּתִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי בֶּן אָדָם הָעֲצָמוֹת הָאֵלֶּה כׇּל בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל הֵמָּה הִנֵּה אֹמְרִים יָבְשׁוּ עַצְמוֹתֵינוּ וְאָבְדָה תִקְוָתֵנוּ נִגְזַרְנוּ לָנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: These** dead that Ezekiel revived were **people who denied the resurrection of the dead, as it is stated: "Then He said to me: Son of man, these bones are the whole house of Israel; behold, they say: Our bones are dried and our hope is lost; we are cut off"** (Ezekiel 37:11). God tells Ezekiel that these were people who had lost hope for resurrection.

Shmuel's opinion is that the dead revived by *Yechezkel* were *bnei adam shekafru bitchiyas hameisim* — people who denied the concept of *techiyas hameisim*, who lost all faith in a future. The *pasuk* cited in connection with this group — *yaveshu atzmoiseinu ve'avda sikvaseinu*, "our bones have dried and our hope is lost" — is, of course, the very verse that the modern State of Israel inverted into *lo avda sikvaseinu*, "we have not lost our hope." The second group, then, consists of those who did lose that hope — people who perhaps assimilated, who were not necessarily lost physically but who lost faith in the Jewish people's collective future. When they died, *Yechezkel*'s prophecy of the dry bones encompasses them as well.

### Dry Practitioners

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן לַחְלוּחִית שֶׁל מִצְוָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הָעֲצָמוֹת הַיְבֵשׁוֹת שִׁמְעוּ דְּבַר ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba says: These** were **people in whom there** was **not** even **the moist** residue **of a mitzva, as it is stated: "The dry bones, hear the word of the Lord"** (Ezekiel 37:4). Even during their lifetime they were comparable to dry bones.

Rabbi *Yirmeya*'s opinion is particularly striking: *elu bnei adam she'ein bahen lachluchis shel mitzvah* — these are people who have no moisture of *mitzvah* in them. Notably, he does not say *she'ein bahen mitzvos* — that they have no *mitzvos* — and that distinction is significant. The *gemara* could have said simply that these are people without *mitzvos*. Instead, by borrowing the imagery of *ha'atzamos hayeveshos* — the dry bones — the text layers an extra degree of intensity onto the dryness. Rabbi *Yirmeya* is pointing to the *dati'im yeveshim*, as one might characterize them: people who observe the letter but not the spirit, who do *mitzvos* on the outside without allowing them to penetrate. One is reminded of Rabbi *Akiva*'s image of water gradually wearing through rock — to what degree do we allow *mitzvos* to truly affect us, or do we perform them while remaining wholly untouched within?

### Temple Desecrators

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: אֵלּוּ בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁחִיפּוּ אֶת הַהֵיכָל כּוּלּוֹ שְׁקָצִים וּרְמָשִׂים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וָאָבוֹא וָאֶרְאֶה וְהִנֵּה כׇל תַּבְנִית רֶמֶשׂ וּבְהֵמָה שֶׁקֶץ וְכׇל גִּלּוּלֵי בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל מְחֻקֶּה עַל הַקִּיר סָבִיב וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וְהֶעֱבִירַנִי עֲלֵיהֶם סָבִיב סָבִיב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says: These are the people who covered the Sanctuary with repugnant creatures and creeping animals, as it is stated: "So I entered and saw and behold, every form of creeping animal and repugnant animal, and all the idols of the house of Israel, etched upon the wall around"** (Ezekiel 8:10), **and it is written there** with regard to the prophecy of the dry bones: **"And he passed me over them around and around"** (Ezekiel 37:2). By means of a verbal analogy between: Around, in one verse and the identical term in the other it is derived that the two verses are referring to the same people.

Rabbi *Yitzchak Nafcha* identifies a fourth group: *bnei adam shechipu et haheichal kulo shkatzim uremasim* — people who plastered or covered the sanctuary entirely with the most abhorrent creatures. The image is provocative and can be understood metaphorically as people who not only abandoned the religious path but actively defiled and smeared it — the *mumar lehachis*, the one who rebels specifically to spite.

### Beautiful Youth/Dura

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵלּוּ מֵתִים שֶׁבְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנְּהַר אֵשֶׁל עַד רַבַּת בִּקְעַת דּוּרָא, שֶׁבְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִגְלָה נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל הָיוּ בָּהֶן בַּחוּרִים שֶׁהָיוּ מְגַנִּים אֶת הַחַמָּה בְּיוֹפְיָין, וְהָיוּ כַּשְׂדִּיּוֹת רוֹאוֹת אוֹתָן וְשׁוֹפְעוֹת זָבוּת. אָמְרוּ לְבַעֲלֵיהֶן, וּבַעֲלֵיהֶן לַמֶּלֶךְ. צִוָּה הַמֶּלֶךְ וַהֲרָגוּם, וַעֲדַיִין הָיוּ שׁוֹפְעוֹת זָבוּת. צִוָּה הַמֶּלֶךְ וּרְמָסוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: These are the dead from the Dura Valley** (see Daniel, chapter 3). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** These corpses filled the area **from the Eshel River to Rabat,** which constitutes **the Dura Valley. As, at the time that Nebuchadnezzar the wicked exiled the Jewish people** to Babylonia, **there were among them youths who overshadowed the sun with their beauty, and the Chaldean women would see them, and** desire them, **and discharge the flow of a *zava*.** These women **told their husbands, and their husbands** told **the king. The king ordered** the youths executed, **and** his servants **executed them. And** their execution did not resolve the problem, as the women **were still discharging the flow of a *zava*.** Even after death, the youths' beauty remained intact. **The king ordered** that their corpses be taken to the valley, **and** his servants **trampled them** so that their form would no longer be visible.

The fifth category, stated by Rabbi *Yochanan*, is entirely different in character. It is worth recalling what we know about Rabbi *Yochanan* himself — that in his youth he was renowned for his exceptional beauty, to the degree that stories are told of *Reish Lakish* encountering him at the river and of Rabbi *Yochanan* sitting at the entrance to the *mikveh* to inspire women through the sight of his beauty. With that context in mind, it is notable that the fifth group he identifies is *bachurim shehayu meganim et hachama beyofyam* — young men so beautiful they outshone the sun itself. These are *metim shebe-Bik'at Dura* — the dead of the *Bik'at Dura*, the valley of Dura in Syria, along what may have been the route of the exile. When *Nevuchadnezzar* drove Israel into exile, there were among them young men of such physical beauty that the Chaldean women who saw them were overcome with desire. They told their husbands, the husbands told the king, the king commanded that these men be killed, and still the women were drawn to them even afterward. The king had them trampled. Those are the fifth group included in *Yechezkel*'s resurrection.

### Modern Context

This fifth category invites reflection across historical distance. For roughly two thousand years — and especially in the era of caricature and persecution leading up to and through the Nazi period — the Jewish people did not walk through the world with an image of themselves as beautiful. And yet in the last seventy to a hundred years, a new image has emerged: the *tzabar*, sun-tanned, strong, and vital, and with it a new kind of Jewish presence in the world. When one considers all five of these groups together — the overly militant who hasten the end, the assimilated who have lost hope, those who perform *mitzvos* without any inner moisture, those who actively desecrate the sacred, and those beautiful young men cut down in the exile — one sees the full scope of *kol Yisrael*. Every one of these categories is included in *Yechezkel*'s vision and in the promise of revival.

## Furnace & Revival

### Six Miracles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהִפִּיל נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר הָרָשָׁע אֶת חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה לְכִבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, אָמַר לוֹ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לִיחֶזְקֵאל: לֵךְ וְהַחְיֵיה מֵתִים בְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא. כֵּיוָן שֶׁהֶחְיָיה אוֹתָן, בָּאוּ עֲצָמוֹת וְטָפְחוּ לוֹ לְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע עַל פָּנָיו. אָמַר: מָה טִיבָן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: חַבְרֵיהֶן שֶׁל אֵלּוּ מְחַיֶּיה מֵתִים בְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא. פָּתַח וְאָמַר: ״אָתוֹהִי כְּמָה רַבְרְבִין וְתִמְהוֹהִי כְּמָה תַקִּיפִין מַלְכוּתֵיהּ מַלְכוּת עָלַם וְשׇׁלְטָנֵהּ עִם דָּר וְדָר וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **At the moment that Nebuchadnezzar the wicked cast Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah into the fiery furnace, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Ezekiel: Go and revive the dead in the Dura Valley. Once** Ezekiel **revived them, the bones came and struck** Nebuchadnezzar, **that wicked** man, **in his face.** Nebuchadnezzar **said: What is the nature of these?** His servants **said to him: The friend of these** three, Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, **is reviving** the **dead in the Dura Valley.** Nebuchadnezzar **began and said: "How great are His signs and how mighty are His wonders; His kingdom is an everlasting kingdom and His dominion is from generation to generation"** (Daniel 3:33).

*Tanu Rabbanan*: *besha'ah shehipil Nevuchadnezzar harasha et Chananyah Mishael va'Azaryah lekivshan ha'esh amar lo HaKadosh Baruch Hu leYechezkel lech vehachayeih metim beVik'at Dura* — at the very same moment that *Nevuchadnezzar* cast *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* into the furnace, the Holy One Blessed Be He said to *Yechezkel*: go and revive the dead in *Bik'at Dura*. The simultaneity is arresting. *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* were evidently the last three people in the entire exile who refused to bow down to *Nevuchadnezzar*'s statue — a golden colossus sixty *amot* in height. The text does not tell us of two hundred thousand others taken to the furnace who happened to survive; it tells us of three. Everyone else had bowed. *Nevuchadnezzar* was about to annihilate the final remnant of Jewish resistance, and at that precise moment — *besha'ah* — God commands *Yechezkel* to revive the dead. It is one of those moments in the *gemara* that reads differently in this century than it may have in 1500.

The *baraita* then enumerates: *shisha nissim na'asu be'oto hayom* — six miracles occurred on that day — *ve'elu hen*: the furnace rose, the furnace was breached, its foundation dissolved (*humak sodo*), the graven image fell on its face, four kingdoms were burned, and *Yechezkel* revived the dead in *Bik'at Dura*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שִׁשָּׁה נִסִּים נַעֲשׂוּ בְּאוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: צָף הַכִּבְשָׁן, וְנִפְרַץ הַכִּבְשָׁן, וְהוּמַק סוֹדוֹ, וְנֶהְפַּךְ צֶלֶם עַל פָּנָיו, וְנִשְׂרְפוּ אַרְבַּע מַלְכִיּוֹת, וְהֶחְיָה יְחֶזְקֵאל אֶת הַמֵּתִים בְּבִקְעַת דּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Six miracles were performed on that day** that Hananiah, Mishael, and Azarya were delivered from the furnace, **and they are: The furnace rose** from where it was sunken to ground level; **and the furnace was breached; and its limestone dissipated; and the graven image** that Nebuchadnezzar established, which he commanded his subjects to worship (see Daniel 3:5–6), **fell on its face; and four** ranks of officials from **monarchies,** who stood around the furnace, **were burned; and Ezekiel revived the dead in the Dura Valley.**

### Furnace Rising

What precisely is the *kivshan* — the furnace into which *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* were thrown? The *kivshan* is a kind of kiln or hot furnace sunk into the ground, with the fire buried beneath. This architectural feature is critical to understanding the sequence of miracles. If the furnace is below ground level, anything cast into it becomes invisible from the outside, and the full scope of a miracle occurring within it would be obscured. Therefore, for the miracle to be witnessed in its entirety, the furnace first had to rise from its sunken position. Then — *nifratz hakivshan* — it was breached or cracked open, making visible what was inside.

### Furnace Lime/Base

The third miracle, *humak sodo*, presents some interpretive difficulty. The conventional reading is that the *kivshan* was constructed from limestone — *sid* — and that this limestone base dissolved or melted. *Rashi* addresses this in the *dibur hamatchil* of *humak sodo* and offers two possibilities. In one formulation he reads the word as *yesodo* — its foundation — indicating that the structural base of the furnace came apart. In another he reads it as *humak sido* — *hasid hakivshan namas merov chamimut* — the limestone of the furnace melted from the intensity of the heat. He then adds: *kach shamati* — this is what I heard — *v'haya nimshechet lirachok mehahavel shel oto hasid nisrefu otan sheyashvu* — and that the heat and vapor emanating from that melting limestone spread outward and consumed those who were standing nearby.

### Officials Burned

This leads directly to the next element of the narrative: four of *Nevuchadnezzar*'s officials were themselves burned. *Nevuchadnezzar* had dispatched eight ranks of officials and governors — the *gemara* works through the list appearing in *Daniel* — yet when *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* emerged from the furnace, only four ranks of officials are described as witnessing it. There is a tradition that the other four were burned in the fire. According to *Rashi*, the *dibur hamatchil* of *humak sodo* makes this explicit: *nisrefo otan she'hishlichu Chananyah Mishael va'Azaryah l'toch kivshan ha'esh* — those who had thrown *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* into the furnace were themselves consumed by it.

## Dignity/Garments

### Royal Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת הַסַּכָּנָה לֹא יְשַׁנֶּה אָדָם אֶת עַצְמוֹ מִן הָרַבָּנוּת (שֶׁלּוֹ), שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בֵּאדַיִן גֻּבְרַיָּא אִלֵּךְ כְּפִתוּ בְּסַרְבָּלֵיהוֹן פַּטְּשֵׁיהוֹן וְכַרְבְּלָתְהוֹן וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov teaches: Even during a period of danger, a person should not deviate from his prominence** and demean himself, **as it is stated: "Then these men were bound in their mantles, their tunics, and their hats,** and their other garments, and they were cast into the blazing fiery furnace" (Daniel 3:21). Even when cast into the furnace, they donned garments befitting their station.

*Tnai devei Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov*: *afilu b'sha'at hasakanah lo yeshaneh adam et atzmo min harabbanut* — even in a moment of mortal danger, a person should not alter his dignified bearing or his station. The *pasuk* that generates this teaching is from *Daniel*: *be'edin gavraya ilech kaftu besarbalehon patishehon vecharbelatehon* — these men were bound in their tunics, cloaks, and hats, fully clothed in their royal garments when they were cast into the fire. They did not strip away their dignity even at the moment of their execution. From this the school of Rabbi *Eliezer ben Yaakov* derives a general principle: even under threat, one does not shed one's *rabbanut*.

### Pirkei Avot

This teaching stands in apparent tension with a principle in *Pirkei Avot* — *snai et harabbanut*, distance yourself from *rabbanut*, from positions of prominence and closeness to the ruling authorities. The two statements seem to pull in opposite directions. The resolution lies in distinguishing between the two situations: *Pirkei Avot* counsels against seeking out or cultivating proximity to power in the first place, while Rabbi *Eliezer ben Yaakov* addresses what one does when already enmeshed in that situation through no choice of one's own. Once you are there, maintain your dignity.

### Historical Dignity

An image that captures this distinction: there is a film about two girls who are close friends in Vienna in 1938 — one from a family that joins the Nazi party, the other Jewish. When the Nazis come and compel the Jews to scrub the streets with toothbrushes, the Jewish grandfather turns to his granddaughter and says: help me choose my finest tie — I want to look especially well today. That is the teaching. One does not seek out the confrontation. But when the confrontation arrives, one does not strip away who one is.

## Tzadikim>Angels

### Greater Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּדוֹלִים צַדִּיקִים יוֹתֵר מִמַּלְאֲכֵי הַשָּׁרֵת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עָנֵה וְאָמַר הָא אֲנָא חָזֵי גֻּבְרִין אַרְבְּעָה שְׁרַיִן מַהְלְכִין בְּגוֹ נוּרָא וַחֲבָל לָא אִיתַי בְּהוֹן וְרֵוֵהּ דִּי רְבִיעָאָה דָּמֵה לְבַר אֱלָהִין״.
>
> **Translation:** **The righteous are greater than the ministering angels, as it is stated: "He answered and said: I see four men unbound, walking in the midst of the fire, and they have no hurt; and the appearance of the fourth is like a son of the gods"** (Daniel 3:25). Nebuchadnezzar saw three righteous people and an angel in the fire of the furnace and noted the presence of the righteous people before noting the presence of the angel.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *gdolim tzadikim yoter mimalachei hasharet* — the *tzadikim* are greater than the ministering angels. One dimension of this is straightforward: an angel has a single function and performs it perfectly, while a *tzaddik* operates across the full complexity of human life and still chooses righteousness. A further question is how to order the different categories of the spiritually elevated relative to one another — we know separately that *bemakom sheba'alei teshuvah omdim tzadikim lo omdim*, that *ba'alei teshuvah* stand in a place where *tzadikim* cannot — but the claim here is that the *tzadikim* of the caliber of *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* stand above even the ministering angels.

### Fourth Figure

The source for this emerges from the verse in *Daniel* itself: *anah ve'amar ha ana chazei gavrin arba sharin mehalechin bego nura, vechaval lo itai behon, verevi'aya domeh l'var elohin* — I see four men walking unharmed in the midst of the fire, and the fourth one resembles a son of God, that is, an angel. Yet when *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* emerge from the furnace, only three come out. The fourth figure — the one who appeared angelic — is gone. One possibility is that this figure entered the fire in order to save them and was consumed in doing so; another reading is that he was an angel who stepped in and did not emerge. The *tzadikim* came out; the angel did not. This is part of what the *gemara* means in asserting that the *tzadikim* are greater.

## Nations Slap Haters

### Nations React

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם בַּר חֲנִילַאי: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁיָּצְאוּ חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה מִכִּבְשַׁן הָאֵשׁ, בָּאוּ כׇּל אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם וְטָפְחוּ לְשׂוֹנְאֵיהֶן שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל עַל פְּנֵיהֶם. אָמְרוּ לָהֶם: יֵשׁ לָכֶם אֱלוֹהַּ כָּזֶה, וְאַתֶּם מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים לַצֶּלֶם?! מִיָּד פָּתְחוּ וְאָמְרוּ: ״לְךָ ה׳ הַצְּדָקָה וְלָנוּ בֹּשֶׁת הַפָּנִים כַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tanḥum bar Ḥanilai says: At the moment that Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah emerged from the fiery furnace, all the nations of the world came and struck the enemies of Israel,** a euphemism for the Jewish people, **in the face** and **said to them: You have a God** with capabilities **like that and you bow to the graven image? Immediately** Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah **began and said: "Lord, righteousness is Yours, but we are shamefaced, as of this day"** (Daniel 9:7).

*Amar Rav Tanchum bar Chanilai*: *besha'ah she'yatzu Chananyah Mishael va'Azaryah mi'kivshan ha'esh, ba'u kol umot ha'olam ve'taphchu l'soneihen shel Yisrael al pneihem* — at the moment that *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* emerged from the furnace, all the nations of the world went and slapped the enemies of Israel across the face. This is of course *sagi nahor* — a euphemism — and the ones being struck are the Jews themselves who had capitulated. The nations turned to them and said: *yesh lachem eloh kaze ve'atem mishtachavim la'tzelem* — you have a God of this magnitude, capable of saving men from a furnace, and you bow down to a graven image?

### Admission of Sin

*Mi'yad patchu ve'amru*: immediately those same people opened their mouths and declared, from *Daniel*: *Lecha Hashem ha'tzedakah u'lanu boshet ha'panim k'hayom ha'zeh* — to You, *Hashem*, belongs righteousness, and to us belongs *boshet ha'panim*, shame of face, as on this day.

### Shir HaMa'alot

This *pasuk* connects to something encountered regularly without necessarily being fully registered. In *Shir HaMa'alot* recited before *bentching* on *Shabbat*, we read: *az yomru va'goyim higdil Hashem la'asot im eleh* — then the nations said, *Hashem* has done great things for these people — followed immediately by *higdil Hashem la'asot imanu* — *Hashem* has done great things for us. The sequence seems counterintuitive: one might expect the Jewish people to proclaim God's greatness first, and then the nations to affirm it. But *beshuv Hashem et shivat Tzion* — in the context of exile — we gradually absorb the way we are perceived by those around us. When the *goyim* themselves declare *higdil Hashem la'asot im eleh*, something shifts, and we find ourselves able to say: *higdil Hashem la'asot imanu*. We sometimes need the external affirmation to recover the internal one.

### Shame Concept

On *boshet ha'panim*: there is something valuable in a sense of shame that keeps one properly oriented — a *yirei boshet*, as various commentaries explore, is someone in whom humility functions as a moral anchor. The risk is when that sense of shame is not internally generated but rather absorbed from an external, hostile gaze — when we begin to see ourselves through the eyes of those who diminish us, and become hunched. The glory of *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* emerging from the fire, and the slap it delivers to those who had abandoned faith, is precisely the restoration of a proper interiority.

## Palm Tree/Tamar

### Palm/Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אָמַרְתִּי אֶעֱלֶה בְתָמָר אֹחֲזָה בְּסַנְסִנָּיו״? ״אָמַרְתִּי אֶעֱלֶה בְתָמָר״ – אֵלּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְעַכְשָׁיו לֹא עָלָה בְּיָדִי אֶלָּא סַנְסַן אֶחָד שֶׁל חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says** on a similar note: **What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "I said: I will climb into the palm tree; I will grasp its boughs"** (Song of Songs 7:9)? **"I said: I will climb into the palm tree"; this is** a reference to **the Jewish people,** who are likened to a palm tree, as they are upright and have one heart directed toward their Father in Heaven. God continues: **And now** that I have tested them by means of the decrees of Nebuchadnezzar, **I have succeeded** in grasping **in My hand only** the **one bough of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah,** as only they were willing to give their lives.

*Amar Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani amar Rabbi Yonatan*: the verse in *Shir HaShirim* reads, *amarti e'eleh ve'tamar ochazah ve'sansinav* — I said I will climb the palm tree and take hold of its branches. Who is the speaker? *HaKadosh Baruch Hu*. And what is the *tamar*? *Amarti e'eleh ve'tamar elu Yisrael* — the palm tree is Israel. The *tamar* is a rich symbol: *tzaddik katamar yifrach*, the *tzaddik* flourishes like a palm; and the *lulav*, the palm branch, contains within it the *lev*, the heart — the *tamar* has one heart, and the Jewish people are meant to be a people of one heart, unified in focus toward *Hashem*.

### One Branch

*Ve'achshav lo alah b'yadi* — and now it has not quite come up in my hand. There is something deeply moving in this formulation. God, who can sustain three men in a furnace, who can command *Yechezkel* to revive the dead, says: it is genuinely difficult to bring the Jewish people fully to where they ought to be. The attempt was made and was not entirely successful — *ela sansan echad shel Chananyah Mishael va'Azaryah*, only a single branch of *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* was achieved.

### Tamar/Judah

An alternative reading that deserves to be noted: *tamar* can also be understood as *Tamar*, the wife of *Yehudah*, about whom there is a tradition that she was willing to be thrown into a furnace of fire in order to preserve the lineage of the Jewish people. The implication of the *kal vachomer* is plain: if she was prepared to make such a sacrifice for the sake of a human being, how much more so should such willingness apply in service of *Hashem*. The connection to *Yehudah* and *Yehudim* gives the metaphor an additional layer of resonance.

## Vision/Red Horse

### Darkness/Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״רָאִיתִי הַלַּיְלָה וְהִנֵּה אִישׁ רֹכֵב עַל סוּס אָדֹם וְהוּא עֹמֵד בֵּין הַהֲדַסִּים אֲשֶׁר בַּמְּצֻלָה [וְגוֹ׳]״? מַאי ״רָאִיתִי הַלַּיְלָה״? בִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַהֲפוֹךְ אֶת כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לְלַיְלָה. ״וְהִנֵּה אִישׁ רֹכֵב״ – אֵין ״אִישׁ״ אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״ה׳ אִישׁ מִלְחָמָה ה׳ שְׁמוֹ״. ״עַל סוּס אָדֹם״ – בִּיקֵּשׁ הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לַהֲפוֹךְ אֶת הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ לְדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "I saw the night, and behold, a man riding upon a red horse, and he stood among the myrtle bushes that were in the depths"** (Zechariah 1:8)? **What** is the meaning of the phrase **"I saw the night"? The Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to transform the entire world into night** and destroy it, as there were no righteous people. **"And behold, a man riding";** the word **"man"** is referring to **no** one **but the Holy One, Blessed be He, as it is stated: "The Lord is a man of war, The Lord is His name"** (Exodus 15:3). **"Upon a red horse"** alludes to the fact that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to transform the whole world into blood.**

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: the verse in *Zechariah* reads — *raiti halaila vehineh ish rocheiv al sus adom vehu omed bein hahadasim asher bametzula* — I saw in the night, and behold a man riding a red horse, standing among the myrtles in a deep place. What is the *laila* — the night? *Bikesh HaKadosh Baruch Hu lehafoch et kol ha'olam kulo le'laila* — the Holy One Blessed Be He sought to turn the entire world to night, because the whole of it had abandoned the path. Only three people remained who had not.

### God/Red Horse

*Vehineh ish rocheiv* — and behold a man riding. The word *ish* is a loaded term throughout *Tanach*. When *Moshe* looks around and sees *ve'ein ish*, when the *mishnah* in *Avot* says *bemakom she'ein anashim heye ata ish* — sometimes *ish* refers to *Eliyahu*, sometimes to an extraordinary human figure. Here, the *gemara* identifies the *ish* as *HaKadosh Baruch Hu* Himself, because it is written in *Shirat Hayam*: *Hashem ish milchama Hashem shemo* — that is His name, He is the *ish*. And the horse is red: *bikesh HaKadosh Baruch Hu lehafoch et ha'olam kulo ledam* — He sought to turn the entire world to blood. The color red, the night, the blood — these are all metaphors for the catastrophic destruction that was imminent when the faith of the Jewish people was on the verge of being annihilated entirely.

### God Cools

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּסְתַּכֵּל בַּחֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה, נִתְקָרְרָה דַּעְתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהוּא עֹמֵד בֵּין הַהֲדַסִּים אֲשֶׁר בַּמְּצֻלָה״. וְאֵין ״הֲדַסִּים״ אֶלָּא צַדִּיקִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי אֹמֵן אֶת הֲדַסָּה״. וְאֵין ״מְצוּלָה״ אֶלָּא בָּבֶל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָאֹמֵר לַצּוּלָה חֳרָבִי וְנַהֲרֹתַיִךְ אוֹבִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Once He looked at Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah He was placated, as it is stated: "And he stood among the myrtle bushes [*hadassim*] that were in the depths [*bametzula*]." And *hadassim*** is referring to **no** one **but the righteous, as it is stated: "And he raised Hadassah,** that is Esther, his uncle's daughter" (Esther 2:7). Hadassah is an appellation for the righteous Esther. **And *metzula*** is referring to **no** place **but Babylonia, as it is stated** with regard to the downfall of Babylonia: **"That says to the deep [*latzula*]: Be dry, and I will dry up your rivers"** (Isaiah 44:27).

*Keivan shenistakel beChananyah Mishael va'Azaryah nitkarera da'ato* — but when He looked upon *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah*, His mind cooled. We have, in the language of the *gemara*, a passionate God — one moved to fury and moved to relent.

### Myrtles/Righteous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד, מְלֵאִים רוֹגֶז נַעֲשִׂים שְׂרוּקִּים, וַאֲדוּמִּים נַעֲשׂוּ לְבָנִים. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סוּסְיָא חִיוָּרָא מְעַלֵּי לְחֶלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately** those messengers, in the form of horses, **filled with anger** and **became gray, and** those who were **red became white. Rav Pappa says: Conclude from it** that seeing **a white horse** in a dream **is a good** portent **for** that **dream,** as it presages peace and quiet.

*Vehu omed bein hahadasim asher bametzula* — He stands among the myrtles in a deep place. Is God literally standing in a forest of myrtle trees and feeling calmed by the scenery? The *gemara* resolves: *ve'ein hadasim ela tzaddikim* — the *hadasim* are none other than the *tzaddikim*. He stands among the righteous — specifically, *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* — and His anger is assuaged.

### Esther/Hadassah

And here *Esther* returns to the discussion, because *hadasim* bring *Hadassah* in their wake. The verse states: *vayehi omen et Hadassah* — and he raised *Hadassah*. *Esther* had two names. The significance of the name *Hadassah* — connecting her to the *hadas*, the myrtle — is that the righteous are called *hadasim*, and she belongs in their company.

### Three Leaves

One commentary notes that a *hadas* leaf grows in clusters of three. What do the three leaves represent? Various groupings of three suggest themselves: *Torah*, *avodah*, *gemilut chasadim*; *Avraham*, *Yitzchak*, *ve'Yaakov*; or simply *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, *va'Azaryah* themselves. The *hadasim* carry this threefold symbolism, and somehow within it the *tzaddikim* are always represented. Whether this also builds on the *lulav* and its component species — having just spoken of the *tamar* — is a further layer worth sitting with, even if not fully resolved.

### White Horse

*Amar Rav Papa*: *shema mina sus yachivra malei lealma* — from here we learn that a white horse is a good sign. If one dreams of a white horse, it is a favorable omen.

## Where They Went

### Three Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, לְהֵיכָא אֲזַלוּ? אָמַר רַב: בְּעַיִן [הָרָע] מֵתוּ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּרוֹק טָבְעוּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: עָלוּ לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְנָשְׂאוּ נָשִׁים וְהוֹלִידוּ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **the Sages,** Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, **where did they go** after their miraculous deliverance, as there is no further mention of them? **Rav says: They died** as the result **of the evil eye,** as everyone was jealous of their deliverance. **And Shmuel says: They drowned in the spittle** of the nations of the world who held the Jewish people in contempt due to their failure to serve God in the appropriate manner. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They ascended to Eretz Yisrael and married women and fathered sons and daughters.**

After *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* walked out of the furnace — where did they go? *Verabanan leichan azlu?* The *gemara* records three opinions among the *Amoraim*, and then notes that these positions already appear among the *Tannaim*, making it a *kitnai* — the dispute is not new.

### Evil Eye Death

*Amar Rav*: *be'ayin hara metu* — they died from the *ayin hara*. After all this, *davka* because of all this — the intensity of attention, the envy, the *kin'ah* — they were struck down. There is a position in *Bava Metzia* that ninety-nine percent of deaths in the world are attributable to the *ayin hara*. What does that say about the nature of human beings and how we look at one another?

### Spat Upon

*Shmuel amar*: *berok tav'u* — they were spat upon in contempt and disdain. The world's reaction to their emergence from the furnace was, for some, not reverence but derision.

### Went to Israel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בְּעַיִן [הָרָע] מֵתוּ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: בְּרוֹק טָבְעוּ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: עָלוּ לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנָשְׂאוּ נָשִׁים וְהוֹלִידוּ בָּנִים וּבָנוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁמַע נָא יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הַכֹּהֵן הַגָּדוֹל אַתָּה וְרֵעֶיךָ הַיֹּשְׁבִים לְפָנֶיךָ כִּי אַנְשֵׁי מוֹפֵת הֵמָּה״. אֵיזוֹ הֵם אֲנָשִׁים שֶׁנַּעֲשָׂה לָהֶם מוֹפֵת? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This amoraic dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*. Rabbi Eliezer says: They died** as the result **of the evil eye. Rabbi Yehoshua says: They drowned in the spittle. And the Rabbis say: They ascended to Eretz Yisrael and married women and fathered sons and daughters, as it is stated: "Hear now, Joshua the High Priest, you and your fellows who sit before you, for they are men of wonder"** (Zechariah 3:8). **Who are the people who had a wonder performed for them** in that generation? **You must say that** it is **Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah.**

Rabbi *Yochanan* says: *alu le'Eretz Yisrael venasu nashim ve'holidu banim uvanot* — they went up to *Eretz Yisrael*, married, and had sons and daughters. This is itself a form of *techiyas hameisim* — the *atzamot yeveshot* revived and took root in the land.

### Tannaitic Sources

The *gemara* notes that this is *kitnai* — these three positions already appear in the earlier generation: Rabbi *Eliezer* holds *be'ayin hara metu*, Rabbi *Yehoshua* holds *berok tav'u*, and the *chachamim* align with Rabbi *Yochanan* — *alu le'Eretz Yisrael venasu nashim ve'holidu banim uvanot*.

### Anshei Mofet

The *chachamim*'s view is supported by a *pasuk* from *Zechariah*: *shema na Yehoshua Kohen Gadol ata vere'echa hayoshevim lefanecha ki anshei mofet hema* — hear, *Yehoshua* the high priest, you and your fellows who sit before you, for they are *anshei mofet* — men of a miracle, men around whom miracles occur. The *gemara* asks: who are these *anshei mofet* who sit before *Yehoshua*? *Eizehem anashim shenasa lahem mofet? Hevei omer zeh Chananya Mishael va'Azarya* — these are *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah*, around whom the great miracle was performed.

## Where Was Daniel?

### Three Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָנִיֵּאל לְהֵיכָן אֲזַל? אֲמַר רַב: לְמִיכְרֵא נַהֲרָא רַבָּא בִּטְבֶרְיָא. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: לְאֵתוֹיֵי בִּיזְרָא דְאַסְפַּסְתָּא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: לְאֵתוֹיֵי חֲזִירֵי דַּאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם. אִינִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: תּוֹדוֹס הָרוֹפֵא אָמַר, אֵין פָּרָה וַחֲזִירָה יוֹצֵא מֵאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם שֶׁאֵין חוֹתְכִין הָאֵם שֶׁלָּהּ בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁלֹּא תֵּלֵד! זוּטְרֵי אַיְיתִי בְּלָא דַּעְתַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where did Daniel go?** He certainly did not bow to the graven image, and he was not cast into the furnace. Apparently, he was elsewhere. **Rav says:** He went **to dig the great river in Tiberias. And Shmuel says:** Daniel went **to bring** choice **alfalfa seed** from a distance, and therefore he was not in Babylonia. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** He went **to bring the** high-quality **pigs of Alexandria of Egypt.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so** that he went to bring the pigs? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Theodosius the doctor says: No cow or sow emerges from Alexandria of Egypt whose womb is not severed so that it will not give birth?** The Gemara answers: They were **small** pigs that **he brought without the knowledge of** the people of Alexandria.

This leads immediately to the obvious question: *veDaniel leichan azal* — where was *Daniel*? Is it conceivable that *Daniel* bowed to the idol while only those three refused? The *gemara* offers three explanations for his absence, each from a different *Amora*, and each carrying its own theological and historical texture.

---

## Daniel in Tverya

### Tverya Aqueduct

*Amar Rav*: *lemichra nahara raba biTverya* — Daniel went to dig the great river aqueduct in *Tverya*, the national water infrastructure, the *movila artzi*, in Israel. He is described as going to establish the hydraulic foundation for the eventual center of Jewish life in the land.

### Tverya Center

What is *Tverya*? One etymology traces the name to *Tiberius*, the Roman ruler. But in Hebrew, *tabur* is the navel, the belly button — and so *Tverya* functions as the *tabur* of *Eretz Yisrael*, the center of the land. Daniel goes to dig water — to bring *Torah*, as it were, to revive the center — and we know from history that *Tverya* did indeed later become a center of Jewish scholarship and life.

---

## Daniel/Seeds

### Alfalfa Seeds

*Ushmuel amar leatoyei bizra deaspasta* — Shmuel says Daniel went to bring seeds, specifically a superior variety of alfalfa that was not yet available in the land. Shmuel's role in *halacha* is that of the preeminent *dayan* in monetary law, the economic authority — and fittingly, his Daniel is an economic advisor to the king who takes advantage of his position to bring vital agricultural resources to *Eretz Yisrael*.

---

## Daniel/Pigs

### Egyptian Pigs

*Amar leatoyei chazirei deAlexandria shel Mitzrayim* — Rabbi *Yochanan* says Daniel went to bring pigs from Alexandria in Egypt. The surprise this generates — *ini?* — is acknowledged. What is the nature of these Egyptian pigs? *V'ha tanya*: there is a *baraita* that states that no cow or pig leaves Alexandria of Egypt unless its womb has been surgically cut, ensuring that the uniquely valuable Egyptian breed cannot be reproduced outside Egypt. The Egyptians maintained a monopoly on their special breeds through this practice.

### Thodos Practice

This practice is associated with *Thodos HaRofe* — *Thodos the doctor* — who is credited with its development or implementation. The animals whose wombs were not cut were the very young ones — those not yet slated for breeding — who would simply be eaten.

### Unauthorized Pigs

Rabbi *Yochanan*'s explanation continues: *zutari aytei belo da'ata d'atu* — Daniel, when he went there, took young ones without the authorization of those who controlled the Egyptian supply. He took them before they could be operated on, because taking adults would have presented a different set of concerns. The rabbis' explanation, then, is that Daniel's mission on behalf of *Nevuchadnezzar* — as royal advisor — took him to Alexandria, and he used the opportunity to secure these animals for the benefit of the community.

## Where Was Daniel? (II)

### God's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה הָיוּ בְּאוֹתָהּ עֵצָה – הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְדָנִיֵּאל, וּנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר. הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֲמַר: נֵיזִיל דָּנִיֵּאל מֵהָכָא, דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ בִּזְכוּתֵיהּ אִיתַּנְצוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Three were** partners **in that plan** to ensure that Daniel would not be in Babylonia when the decree of persecution was in effect: **The Holy One, Blessed be He; Daniel; and Nebuchadnezzar. The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Let Daniel go from here, so that** people **would not say** that Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah **were delivered** from the fiery furnace **due to the virtue of** Daniel, rather than due to their own righteousness.

*Tanu Rabbanan*: there were three parties to the decision that Daniel should not be present in *Bavel* when *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah* were thrown into the furnace. *HaKadosh Baruch Hu amar*: *yeizal Daniel mehacha d'lo limru bizchuto inatzel* — let Daniel leave from here, so that people will not say that the three of them were saved from the furnace through Daniel's merit. It should reflect their own righteousness alone — the miracle should belong to them and not be attributed to the presence of a greater figure.

### Daniel's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדָנִיֵּאל אֲמַר: אֵיזִיל מֵהָכָא, דְּלָא לִיקַיַּים בִּי ״פְּסִילֵי אֱלֹהֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Daniel said** to himself: **I will go** away **from here so** that this verse **will not be fulfilled in my** regard: **"The graven images of their gods shall you burn with fire"** (Deuteronomy 7:25). Daniel was concerned that because Nebuchadnezzar worshipped him like a deity, his legal status was that of an idol, and he would be burned.

*V'Daniel amar*: *eizal mehacha d'lo lekayem bi pesilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* — Daniel said: I should leave from here lest the verse *pesilei eloheihem tisrefun ba'esh* — you shall burn their idols in fire — be fulfilled through me. This requires the next point to be understood.

### King's Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר אֲמַר: יֵזִיל דָּנִיאֵל מֵהָכָא, דְּלָא לֵימְרוּ ״קַלְיֵיהּ לֶאֱלָהֵיהּ בְּנוּרָא״. וּמִנַּיִין דִּסְגִיד לֵיהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״בֵּאדַיִן מַלְכָּא נְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר נְפַל עַל אַנְפּוֹהִי וּלְדָנִיֵּאל סְגִד וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Nebuchadnezzar said: Daniel should go** away **from here so that** the people **will not say:** Nebuchadnezzar **burned his god in fire. And from where** is it derived **that** Nebuchadnezzar **worshipped** Daniel? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "Then the king Nebuchadnezzar fell upon his face and worshipped Daniel** and commanded that they should offer an offering and pleasing aromas to him" (Daniel 2:46).

*V'Nevuchadnezzar amar*: *yeizil Daniel mehacha d'lo limru kali leh l'elohei beneura* — *Nevuchadnezzar* said: Daniel should leave from here so that people will not say that *Nevuchadnezzar* burned his own god in the fire. For *Nevuchadnezzar* effectively worshiped *Daniel*, and to throw someone whom the king treats as a god into a furnace would be to do precisely what the Torah commands regarding idols — burn them in fire. From Daniel's perspective, he did not want to become an idol; from *Nevuchadnezzar*'s perspective, he did not want to be seen as burning his own god.

### King Worshipped

Where do we derive that *Nevuchadnezzar* worshiped *Daniel*? *Mna lan d'sagid leh?* The source is a verse in *Daniel* itself: *be'edayin malka Nevuchadnezzar nefal al anpohi u'l'Daniel sagid* — then King *Nevuchadnezzar* fell on his face and worshiped *Daniel*. That verse establishes the premise for all three parties' reasoning.

## False Prophets

### False Prophets Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אֱלֹהֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֶל אַחְאָב בֶּן קוֹלָיָה וְאֶל צִדְקִיָּהוּ בֶן מַעֲשֵׂיָה הַנִּבְּאִים לָכֶם בִּשְׁמִי לַשָּׁקֶר וְגוֹ׳״. וּכְתִיב: ״וְלֻקַּח מֵהֶם קְלָלָה לְכֹל גָּלוּת יְהוּדָה אֲשֶׁר בְּבָבֶל לֵאמֹר יְשִׂימְךָ ה׳ כְּצִדְקִיָּהוּ וּכְאֶחְאָב אֲשֶׁר קָלָם מֶלֶךְ בָּבֶל בָּאֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the deliverance of Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah, the Gemara cites the verses: **"So says the Lord of hosts, the God of Israel, concerning Ahab, son of Kolaiah, and of Zedekiah, son of Maaseiah, who prophesy to you a lie in My name:** Behold, I will deliver them into the hand of Nebuchadrezzar, king of Babylonia; and he shall slay them before your eyes" (Jeremiah 29:21). **And it is written: "And of them shall be taken a curse by all the captivity of Judea, who are in Babylonia, saying: May the Lord make you like Zedekiah and like Ahab, whom the king of Babylonia toasted in the fire"** (Jeremiah 29:22).

The *gemara* now turns to two other figures who appear in the context of *galut Bavel* and involve fire — but in a very different register. A verse in *Yirmiyahu* is cited: *koh amar Hashem Tzevaot Elokei Yisrael el Achav ben Kolaya v'el Tzidkiya ben Maaseiya haniva'im lachem bishmi lasheker* — thus says *Hashem Tzvaot*, the God of Israel, concerning *Achav ben Kolaya* and *Tzidkiyah ben Maaseiyah*, who are prophesying to you falsely in My name. These are not the famous *Achav* king of Israel or *Tzidkiyahu* king of Judah — they are false prophets active among the Jewish exiles in *Bavel*.

### Curse Comparison

*V'lukach mehem klala l'chol galut Yehuda asher b'Bavel lemor yesimcha Hashem k'Tzidkiya u'ch'Achav asher kalam melech Bavel ba'esh* — and from them shall be taken a curse for all the exile of Judah in *Bavel*, saying: may *Hashem* make you like *Tzidkiyah* and *Achav* whom the king of *Bavel* roasted in the fire. Compare this to the blessing *yesimcha Hashem keEphraim ucheMenashe* — Ephraim and Menashe lived through the exile in Egypt and strengthened the Jewish people. Here we have figures who lived through *galut Bavel* and instead weakened and corrupted the Jewish people, and whose names become a curse rather than a blessing.

### Roasted Seeds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר שְׂרָפָם״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״אֲשֶׁר קָלָם״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁעֲשָׂאָן כִּקְלָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** It **is not stated: Whom** the king of Babylonia **burned, but "whom** the king of Babylonia **toasted." Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: This teaches that he rendered them like toasted wheat,** which is toasted on all sides.

*Asher srafam lo ne'emar ela asher kalam* — the verse does not say they were *burned*, it says they were *klam* — roasted. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai melamed sheasaan kekliyot* — we are taught that they were made like toasted seeds, like grain roasted on a fire. The connection between *klala* — curse — and *kalam* — roasted — is striking even if not formally derived.

### Sexual Sin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״יַעַן אֲשֶׁר עָשׂוּ נְבָלָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל וַיְנַאֲפוּ אֶת נְשֵׁי רֵעֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: **"Because they have committed baseness in Israel, and have committed adultery with the wives of their neighbors"** (Jeremiah 29:23).

Why this particular punishment? *Yaan asher asu nevalah beYisrael vayinafu et neshei rei'ehem* — because they committed an abomination in Israel and committed adultery with the wives of their neighbors. *Mai avud* — what exactly did they do? The *gemara* now tells the story.

### Seduction Plan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי עֲבוּד? אֲזוּל לְגַבֵּי בְּרַתֵּיה דִּנְבוּכַדְנֶצַּר. אַחְאָב אֲמַר לַהּ: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הִשָּׁמֵיעִי אֶל צִדְקִיָּה״. וְצִדְקִיָּה אֲמַר: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ הִשָּׁמֵיעִי אֶל אַחְאָב״. אֲזַלָה וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: אֱלֹהֵיהֶם שֶׁל אֵלּוּ שׂוֹנֵא זִימָּה הוּא. כִּי אָתוּ לְגַבָּךְ, שַׁדְּרִינְהוּ לְגַבַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **What did they do?** The Gemara relates: **They went to the daughter of Nebuchadnezzar. Ahab said to her: So says the Lord: Submit to Zedekiah** and engage in intercourse with him. **And Zedekiah said** to her: **So says the Lord: Submit to Ahab** and engage in intercourse with him. **She went and said to her father** what they said to her. Nebuchadnezzar **said to her: The God of these** people **abhors lewdness,** so this is likely a false prophecy. **When they come to you, send them to me.**

*Achav amar lah koh amar Hashem hashmeyi el Tzidkiyah* — *Achav* said to the daughter of *Nevuchadnezzar*: this is the word of *Hashem* — you should be intimate with *Tzidkiyah*. And *Tzidkiyah amar koh amar Hashem hashmeyi el Achav* — *Tzidkiyah* said to her: this is the word of *Hashem* — you should be intimate with *Achav*. Each one was using false prophecy to push the king's daughter toward the other.

### Daughter Reports

*Azlah ve'amrah leih leavuh* — she went and told her father. *Amar lah*: he said to her — and here the *gemara* notes something remarkable: *eloheihem shel elu soneh zimah hu* — the God of these people abhors this kind of behavior. The Babylonian king, without Torah study, knows that the God of Israel is *soneh zimah*. *Ki atu legabach shadreinhu legabi* — when they come to you, send them to me. She did so: *ki atu legabah shadratehu legabei avuh* — when they came to her, she sent them to her father.

### Prohibition Confirmed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתוֹ לְגַבַּהּ, שַׁדַּרְתַּנְהוּ לְגַבֵּי אֲבוּהָ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מַאן אֲמַר לְכוֹן? אֲמַרוּ: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא. וְהָא חֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה שְׁאֵלְתִּינְהוּ, וַאֲמַרוּ לִי: אָסוּר! אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אֲנַן נָמֵי נְבִיאֵי כְּוָותַיְיהוּ. לְדִידְהוּ לָא אֲמַר לְהוּ, לְדִידַן אֲמַר לַן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא בָּעֵינָא דְּאֶיבְדְּקִינְכוּ, כִּי הֵיכִי דִּבְדַקְתִּינְהוּ לַחֲנַנְיָה מִישָׁאֵל וַעֲזַרְיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When they came to her, she sent them to her father.** Nebuchadnezzar **said to them: Who told you** to do this? **They said:** It was **the Holy One, Blessed be He.** Nebuchadnezzar said: **But haven't I asked Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah** about this **and they said to me: It is prohibited? They said to him: We too are prophets like they are.** God **did not say** this prophecy **to them; He said** it **to us.** Nebuchadnezzar **said to them:** If so, **I wish to put you to the test** to determine if you are righteous, **just as I put Hananiah, Mishael, and Azariah to the test.**

*Amar lah*: he said to them — and here the *gemara* notes something remarkable: *eloheihem shel elu soneh zimah hu* — the God of these people abhors this kind of behavior. The Babylonian king, without Torah study, knows that the God of Israel is *soneh zimah*. *Ki atu legabach shadreinhu legabi* — when they come to you, send them to me. She did so: *ki atu legabah shadratehu legabei avuh* — when they came to her, she sent them to her father.

### King's Test

*Amar lehu anan nami nevi'ei kevathaihu ledidhu lo amar lehu ledidan amar lan* — the false prophets said: we are also prophets just like them. To them He did not say it; to us He did. *Amar lehu*: fine. *Ana beina deivadkinechu* — I will test you just as I tested *Chananyah*, *Mishael*, and *Azaryah*. I will put you to the same proof.

### Joshua Chosen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: אִינּוּ תְּלָתָא הֲווֹ, וַאֲנַן תְּרֵין. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בְּחַרוּ לְכוֹן מַאן דְּבָעֵיתוּ בַּהֲדַיְיכוּ. אֲמַרוּ: יְהוֹשֻׁעַ כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל. סָבְרִי: לֵיתֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ דִּנְפִישׁ זְכוּתֵיהּ וּמַגְּנָא עֲלַן.
>
> **Translation:** Ahab and Zedekiah **said to** Nebuchadnezzar: **They were three and we are** only **two,** and our merit is not great enough to save us from the fire. Nebuchadnezzar **said to them: Choose for yourselves** a third person, **whomever you wish,** to be put to the test **with you. They said** to him: We choose **Joshua the High Priest.** They chose him because **they thought: Let Joshua** the High Priest **come** with us, **since his merit is great and it will protect us.**

*Amru leim inun tlata havu ve'anan terein* — they said: there were three of them; we are only two. Three survived, two cannot be expected to. *Amar lehu bacharu lachon man deito bahadayichu* — the king said: choose yourselves a third to accompany you. *Amru Yehoshua Kohen Gadol* — they chose *Yehoshua* the *Kohen Gadol*. Their reasoning: *savri tfei Yehoshua denafish zechuteih umagen alan* — they calculated that *Yehoshua*'s merit was so great that it would shield them. As the *gemara* will go on to show, this calculation did not succeed.
