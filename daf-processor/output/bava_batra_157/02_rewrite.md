# Bava Batra 157 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shchiv Mera Shabbat

### Rabbi Eliezer Shabbat

The daf begins at the bottom of 156b, picking up the *mishna* that was introduced at the close of the previous session. The *mishna* records the following: *Rabbi Eliezer omer beshabbos ve'al pi kayaman* — Rabbi Eliezer says that on Shabbat the verbal gift of a *shchiv mera* is effective. The apparent tension with the previous *mishna* requires some untangling. In that earlier context, an opinion — seemingly also attributed to Rabbi Eliezer — appeared to deny the entire institution of the *shchiv mera*'s verbal gift. Here, however, the same Rabbi Eliezer draws a distinction: specifically on Shabbat, the verbal gift is valid, *mipnei she'ein yachol lichtov* — because on Shabbat he cannot write, and therefore the Rabbis extended this mechanism to him. Within the broader framework of *lo tithrosh beiteih* and the concern not to leave the dying man's household destitute, the Rabbis had no other option but to allow it. *Aval lo bechol* — but on a weekday, when writing is perfectly available, they did not permit the verbal gift to substitute for a proper written instrument.

### Tosafot Clarification

Tosafot clarifies that the opinion in the earlier *mishna* and the opinion here are not necessarily the same person. One was Rabbi Elazar and the other Rabbi Eliezer — two distinct Tannaim. The Rif, by contrast, appears to read them as a single authority, treating the current *mishna* as a refinement of the position stated earlier. The Yerushalmi, for its part, attributes that earlier opinion to the *chachamim* generally. Regardless of how one resolves the attribution, the operative distinction being drawn here is between Shabbat and the weekday.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Kal Vacho…

Rabbi Yehoshua's response is structured as a *kal vachomer*: *beshabbos amru kal vachomer bechol* — they established the rule for Shabbat, and we may infer that it certainly applies during the week. The reasoning is as follows: Shabbat is a time when no *kinyanim* are normally operative, yet the Rabbis allowed the *shchiv mera*'s verbal gift to function as a form of *kinyan*. If it can work during a period when *kinyanim* are altogether suspended, then certainly it should work during the week, when ordinary *kinyanim* are fully available and this mechanism can be understood as a species of them. Rabbi Yehoshua thus extends the institution across both contexts.

### Zochin LeKatan

The *mishna* then presents a parallel dispute using the same logical structure. *Zochin lekatan ve'ein zochin legadol, divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that one may take possession of property on behalf of a minor, but not on behalf of an adult. The adult is capable of acting for himself; the Rabbis created this dispensation only where there was no other option, namely for a *katan* who cannot perform the *kinyan* independently. Rabbi Eliezer accordingly limits the principle to the case where it was originally taught.

### Zochin Principle

Rabbi Yehoshua counters with the same *kal vachomer* logic: *lekatan amru kal vachomer legadol* — if you can take possession on behalf of a minor who cannot act for himself at all, then certainly you can do so for an adult. After all, the adult's capacity to act is precisely what makes the case easier, not harder, to permit. According to this reading, Rabbi Yehoshua endorses the full principle of *zochin leadam shelo befanav* without restriction.

This is a striking *gemara*. The principle of *zochin leadam shelo befanav* — that one may acquire something on behalf of another in their absence — is treated elsewhere as virtually axiomatic. Similarly, the principle of *divrei shchiv mera kechtuvim uchemesurim damu* — that the words of a *shchiv mera* are treated as if written and delivered — is often invoked as though it were beyond dispute. Yet Rabbi Eliezer rejects both, save in narrow circumstances. One illuminating feature of Rabbi Eliezer's general approach is the *gemara*'s own characterization of him elsewhere: *le'olam lo amar davar shelo shama mipi rabo* — he never stated a teaching he had not received directly from his teacher. He consistently interpreted received traditions in their most conservative scope, neither abstracting nor innovating. This explains his position here: the teaching was given in the case of the *katan*; the teaching was given on Shabbat; he will not expand it beyond those specific contexts.

### Mishna Compilation

The *gemara* then identifies whose formulation our *mishna* follows: *matnitin mani Rabbi Yehuda hi* — our *mishna*, which frames the debate as Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua disagreeing about Shabbat versus weekday and *katan* versus *gadol* in the particular direction we have seen, is Rabbi Yehuda's version. A *braita* then introduces Rabbi Meir's version of the same dispute.

### Rabbi Meir Version

In Rabbi Meir's formulation, the entire structure is a mirror image. *Rabbi Eliezer omer bechol devarav kayamin mipnei sheyachol lichtov aval lo beshabbos* — Rabbi Eliezer says the verbal gift is valid on weekdays, because then it stands in place of a written *kinyan* at a time when *kinyanim* function, but it does not work on Shabbat, which is entirely excluded from the domain of *kinyanim*. Rabbi Eliezer still limits the institution; he simply limits it to the opposite scenario. Rabbi Yehoshua once again holds that it applies in both cases: *bechol amru kal vachomer beshabbos* — they established it for weekdays; certainly it applies on Shabbat when there is no alternative.

### Rabbi Meir Zochin

The parallel dispute about *zochin* is likewise inverted in Rabbi Meir's version. *Zochin legadol ve'ein zochin lekatan divrei Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi Eliezer holds that one may take possession on behalf of an adult, because the adult could in principle appoint a *shaliach* and thus the acquisition can be conceived as an extension of his agency, but not for a minor, who cannot appoint an agent and for whom the very concept is therefore incoherent. Rabbi Yehoshua responds: *begadol amru kal vachomer lekatan* — if it works for an adult, it certainly works for a *katan* who has no other option. The two versions of the dispute thus present a genuine mirror image: Rabbi Eliezer always restricts the institution to one pole, Rabbi Yehoshua always extends it across both, and whether that pole is Shabbat or weekday, *katan* or *gadol*, depends entirely on which tradent — Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Meir — is reporting the debate.

## Collapsed House

### Case Setup

The *gemara* now transitions away from *shchiv mera* into a distinct set of questions about *yerusha* — not the ordering of heirs or the rabbinic will, but rather the factual question of who died when and what consequences flow from the sequence of death. The *mishna* presents the following scenario, using the names the Rashbam supplies: a house collapsed on Reuven and his father Yaakov. The *mishna* notes that the same analysis applies to any *morish* — anyone who would be in a position to bequeath property to Reuven, whether mother, brother, or uncle — but the paradigm case is father and son. Reuven, furthermore, had outstanding debts: *v'haysa alav kesubas isha u'va'al chov* — there was a creditor holding an IOU against him, and there was the claim of his ex-wife's *ketubah*. And crucially, Reuven had no money of his own.

### Competing Interests

The competing interests in this scenario are sharp and precisely defined. Yaakov has property. If Yaakov died first, his property would pass to Reuven; Reuven would then die, and his son Chanoch would inherit whatever Reuven had. The creditor wants exactly this sequence. If Yaakov's property passed through Reuven's hands, even momentarily, the creditor would have a lien on it by virtue of his outstanding claim against Reuven. He could then collect that debt from Chanoch as the heir who received encumbered property.

Chanoch, naturally, wants the opposite. He would prefer that Reuven died first — before inheriting anything from Yaakov — and that Yaakov died second, with the property passing directly from grandfather to grandson. In that scenario, the property arrives in Chanoch's hands via Yaakov, untouched by any of Reuven's liabilities. Either way, Chanoch ends up with the property; the question is which path it took through the family tree, and that path determines whether the creditor's lien attaches to it.

### Mishna Positions

*Yorshei ha'av omrim haben mes rishon* — the heirs of the father, in our simplified case Chanoch, claim that Reuven died first and Yaakov second, meaning the property came to them debt-free. *Uba'al chov omrim ha'av mes rishon* — the creditors claim the reverse, that Yaakov died first, the property passed to Reuven, and they therefore hold a lien on it.

### Beit Shammai Hillel

*Beis Shammai omrim yachloku* — Beit Shammai rules that they split the property. *Uveis Hillel omrim nechasim bechezkasan* — Beit Hillel rules that the property remains in its presumed possession, meaning in the hands of the family, not the creditors. The *gemara* here does not develop the full debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel at length — that is treated in another *gemara* — but the underlying logic of each position is clear. Beit Hillel's position is the most intuitive: regardless of which sequence of death occurred, Chanoch owns the property. The creditor is attempting to extract it on the basis of an alleged lien. *Hamotzi mechaveiro alav hara'ayah* — the burden of proof falls on the one seeking to take from the other. Since Chanoch is unquestionably *muchzak* in the property, the creditor who asserts a lien must prove it.

### Shtar Explanation

Beit Shammai's position, as the *gemara* elsewhere explains, rests on the principle of *shtar ha'omed ligvos kigavu dami* — a debt document that is standing to be collected is treated as though it has already been collected. Under this principle, if the property passed through Reuven's hands and the creditor held a *shtar* at that moment, it is as though the creditor had already taken possession. The situation is therefore not simply that Chanoch is *muchzak* and the creditor asserts an unproven lien — rather, if the property did pass through Reuven, the creditor's possession is itself treated as already established. From the creditor's side, Chanoch would then be the *motzi* attempting to extract from him. Because neither sequence of death can be verified, *yachloku* — they divide.

## Liens Property

### Shtar Liens Rules

The *gemara* now cites a ruling from elsewhere: *hamalveh es chaveiro bishtar goveh minechasim meshubadim* — one who lends to another with a *shtar* may collect from encumbered property, that is, real estate even after it has been sold to a third party. *Al yedei edim*, however — if the loan was witnessed but no *shtar* was written — *goveh minechasim benei chorin*, the creditor may collect only from free property, meaning property still in the direct control of the original debtor, not from any third-party purchaser.

The logic of the *shtar* creating liens is the following. When I borrow money and execute a *shtar* on a given date, that act of borrowing with a document creates, at that moment, liens on all the real property I presently own. Those liens travel with the property: if I subsequently sell it, the buyer acquires it subject to that prior encumbrance, and the creditor may reclaim it to satisfy the debt.

### Shmuel Future Liens

This sets the stage for Shmuel's question. What happens to property I acquire *after* the date of the loan? On January 1, I borrow money and execute a *shtar*. On January 15, I purchase a new plot of land. Does the creditor have liens on that new property? Logically, at the moment the lien was created, that property did not yet exist in my possession, so the lien could not have attached to it. But what if at the time of the loan I explicitly stated — as indeed the *ketubah* formula does, with the phrase *d'kanai u'd'asid ana lemiknei* — that I am hereby creating liens on property I currently own and on property I will come to own in the future? The *ketubah* language even specifies, *afilu glima d'al katfei* — even the cloak on his shoulder — and distinguishes between *nechasim* that carry *achrayus* and those that do not. Does such a declaration actually create enforceable liens on future-acquired property? That is Shmuel's question: *d'ikni d'asid ana lemiknei d'ikni* — future property, followed by an act of acquisition — *mahu*?

### Rabbi Meir Future

The question becomes most pressing in the position of the *Rabbanan*. *Aliba d'Rabbi Meir d'amar adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam* — according to Rabbi Meir, who holds that a person can sell or transfer something he does not yet own, it is fairly clear that he should be able to create liens on future property as well. If you can effect a full transfer of something you do not yet possess, you can certainly encumber it with a *shibud*. The harder question is for the Rabbanan, who hold that one cannot be *makneh davar shelo ba l'olam*.

### Rashbam Reasons

The Rashbam provides two independent reasons why even according to the Rabbanan, future liens might be permissible. The first: *mi shani shibud mi'kinyan* — a *shibud* is qualitatively different from a *kinyan*. A full transfer of ownership in something not yet in one's possession is one thing; a *shibud* is a more abstract right, not a transfer of the thing itself, and perhaps that more abstract quality makes it possible to pre-create such a right before the underlying property comes into one's hands. The second reason, which the Rashbam introduces with *ei nami*: the Rabbis specifically legislated that *shibud* can attach to future property — *d'lo tinal delet bifnei lovin* — so as not to lock the door before borrowers. The policy concern is to facilitate lending. If creditors could never secure future-acquired property, their confidence in lending would be undermined; the Rabbis therefore empowered this mechanism to encourage commerce and the extension of credit.

### Rav Yosef Proof

Rav Yosef attempts to bring a proof from a *sugya* involving a dispute between Admon and the *chachamim*. The case: Michael presents an IOU showing that Dov owes him a sum of money, borrowed on January 1. Dov responds: that *shtar* is clearly a forgery, because on February 1 you sold me a plot of land for precisely that sum. If I truly owed you the money, why did you accept cash for the land instead of simply applying it to the debt? That is Admon's argument — the sale proves the debt cannot be real. The *chachamim* reject this: *zeh haya pikeach shemachar lo es hakarka mipnei sheyachol lemiashhkeno alav* — he was clever to sell him the land. The reasoning: Dov, if simply pressed for the money, would claim poverty. Cash is concealable. But a plot of land cannot be hidden. By selling Dov the land for cash — pocketing the money he knew Dov had — Michael converted Dov's liquid, concealable assets into real property that Michael can now collect against as a lien. The land purchased after the loan now stands as collateral for that prior debt.

Rav Yosef's point is that this demonstrates liens attaching to property acquired after the loan — which is precisely the principle under debate.

### Rava Pushback

Rava pushes back: *minei*? Collecting *from the debtor himself*? That was never in dispute. *Minei afilu miglima d'al katfei* — a creditor can collect from any property the debtor currently possesses, whenever he came into possession of it, even the shirt off his back. The question of liens concerns collection from *third parties* — from a *lokei'ach* who bought the property from the debtor after the loan. If Dov still holds the land, of course Michael can collect it. The issue arises only if Dov had since sold that land to someone else; does Michael's prior lien survive the transfer? The case of *d'ikni kana umachar* — future property acquired and then sold — or *d'ikni kana vehoreish* — future property acquired and then bequeathed — is what remains unresolved.

## Inheritance Liens

### Rav Chana Proof

Rav Chana attempts to bring proof from the collapsed house *mishna* itself. He reconstructs the scenario: *nafal habayis alav v'al aviv... yorshei ha'av omrim haben mes rishon... uba'al chov omrim ha'av mes rishon*. Recall the creditor's desired sequence of events: Yaakov dies first, his property passes to Reuven, and the creditor then uses that property to collect the debt from Chanoch. But this relies on liens attaching to property that Reuven came into possession of only *after* the loan. How else could the creditor lay claim to property that Yaakov owned when Reuven borrowed the money?

### Gemara Question

The *gemara* presses the question: *ve'i salka da'atach de'ikni kana umachar lo mishta'abed* — if you hold that future-acquired property does not become encumbered, then even if Yaakov did die first and the property passed to Reuven, on what basis would the creditor have any claim? The property was Yaakov's when the loan was made; it was not Reuven's property at all.

### Mitzvah Obligation

Rav Nachman Zeira answers: *mitzvah al hayesomim lifro'a chovos avihen* — there is an obligation upon orphans to repay their father's debts. The creditor does not necessarily need liens in the conventional sense. Even without liens on the inherited property, once Chanoch inherits from his father Reuven — even property that came to Reuven from Yaakov — there is an obligation upon Chanoch to use that property to discharge Reuven's debts.

### Two Degrees

Tosafot clarifies that there are two distinct degrees of this obligation, and the distinction is crucial. If a father dies leaving debts but no property — or leaving only *metaltelin* while the creditor holds liens only on *karka* — the children have no legal obligation to pay from their own resources. They have a moral obligation; some people of high scruple will indeed pay off a deceased father's debts from their personal funds, much as some bankrupted businessmen eventually repay creditors beyond any legal requirement. But *beit din* will not enforce it. However, if the children actually inherited property from the father, the obligation to repay the father's debts from that inherited property rises to the level of a legal, judicially enforceable duty. It is a personal obligation upon the heir, not a *shibud* on the property itself — but it is binding and actionable.

The subtle question, of course, is why one would need this *mitzvah* framework at all if the creditor already has proper liens. The *gemara* is operating on the premise that perhaps there are no liens in the usual sense — the obligation is what fills the gap.

### Rav Ashi Challenge

Rav Ashi challenges this construction: *milveh al peh hu* — if there are no liens, you have effectively reduced this claim to the status of an oral loan, a loan recorded only by witnesses and without a *shtar*. And Rav and Shmuel both rule that *milveh al peh eino goveh lo min hayorshim v'lo min halokechim* — an oral loan carries no liens and cannot be collected from heirs or from purchasers. The *gemara* understands this ruling to mean not only that there are no liens but that there is not even an enforceable *mitzvah lifro'a chov avihen* in this case. The *mitzvah* in its legally binding form applies only where actual liens exist; absent liens, the obligation reverts to the purely moral sphere that *beit din* will not enforce. The attempted answer thus collapses: if we posit no liens, the creditor's claim is unenforceable, and the scenario of Rav Chana's proof fails to establish what he intended.

## Shtar Dating

### Ha Mani Defense

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty with a single move: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi d'amar adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam*. Any proof one might adduce — from the collapsed house case, from the *shevach* case, from the postdated *shtar* — that shows liens attaching to property acquired after the loan can simply be attributed to Rabbi Meir's position. This becomes the *gemara*'s standing defense throughout this entire discussion. Whenever evidence accumulates that future liens are operative, the *gemara* deflects it with this attribution. The claim is: the *mishna* of the collapsed house works on the assumption that the *shtar* contained an explicit *d'ikni ana lemiknei* clause, and even so, it reflects only Rabbi Meir, not the *Rabbanan*. One cannot prove from it that future liens work according to everyone.

### Predating Invalid

The *gemara* then cites the rule: *shtarei chov hamukdamim pesulim vehamukharim kesherim* — predated loan documents are invalid; postdated ones are valid. The reason predating is *pasul* is straightforward. Suppose I borrow money on January 15 but write January 1 as the date. On January 1, the true date in the document, I had sold a plot of land to an innocent purchaser. That buyer performed a proper title search, confirmed there were no existing liens, and acquired the property in good faith. Now I am claiming retroactive liens from January 1 — and those fictitious liens reach back and undermine that innocent purchase. The predated *shtar* thus exposes third parties to liens they had no way to know about and no reason to anticipate. The document is therefore entirely disqualified. Tosafot raises whether the *pesul* invalidates the *shtar* entirely — including its use to collect from the debtor himself — or whether it merely strips away its liens while leaving the underlying debt intact. The plain meaning of *pesulim* suggests complete disqualification, though Tosafot entertains the possibility of a more limited reading.

### Postdating Benefit

Postdating, by contrast, is permissible, because the creditor is only harming himself by waiving liens for the interval between the true date and the written date. If I lend money on January 15 but write January 30 as the date, I am allowing the borrower to sell off property freely until January 30 without my liens attaching — a concession at my own expense. That is unobjectionable.

But the *gemara* observes that something more is happening. If the borrower purchases a new plot of land on January 25 — after the true date of the loan but before the written date — and the written date is January 30, then when the *shtar* is presented it appears that the loan was made on the 30th. Under a properly dated document, would the creditor have had liens on property bought on January 25? Only if future liens can be created. Yet by the postdating, the new property now falls squarely within the *shtar*'s apparent coverage — and this is treated as a benefit to the creditor.

### Inference Proof

The *gemara* presses: *ve'i salka da'atach de'ikni kana umachar lo mishta'abed* — if you hold that future-acquired property does not create liens, then why is the postdated *shtar* valid or even useful? Whatever benefit accrues to the creditor through postdating — the appearance of liens on intervening property — is only intelligible if there exists a mechanism by which future liens can be created. *Ha mani Rabbi Meir hi d'amar adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam* — once again the answer: this postdating scenario works only according to Rabbi Meir's position. The defense holds, but the evidentiary weight is accumulating.

## Shevach Karka'ot

### Indeterminate Amounts

The *gemara* introduces a new category: *shevach karkaos* — improvements to land — as an illustration of a class of debts for which the Rabbis did not allow liens to operate against third parties. The general principle is that where the amount of the debt is indeterminate, it is unfair to expose innocent purchasers to unquantifiable liens they cannot foresee. A purchaser can inquire whether there are prior liens and calculate the risk. He cannot calculate a lien whose ultimate value depends on how much improvement the property will receive after he acquires it.

### Insurance Scenario

The concrete scenario: I sell Yisrael a plot of land, and I *insure* it. That is, I undertake an obligation of *achrayus* — if a creditor subsequently seizes the land on the basis of a prior lien, or if it emerges that the land was not mine to sell, I will reimburse Yisrael for his loss. This is the seller's standard guarantee. Yisrael, reasonably, wants more: he wants me to insure not just the value of the land itself but also the *shevach*, the improvements he invests in the property. If he builds and plants and develops, and then the land is seized, he does not want to receive back only what he paid for bare land. I agree to this extended insurance as well.

### Collection Rights

The *halacha* that emerges from this scenario is the following: when a creditor comes and seizes the land from Yisrael — *ke'she'hu goveh goveh es hakeren minechasim meshubadim* — the creditor collects the *keren*, the principal value of the land, from encumbered property, even from land I have since sold to others. But *ve'es hashevach minechasim benei chorin* — for the *shevach*, he can collect only from free property under my direct control. Yisrael's right to reimbursement from me follows the same structure: the principal creates liens, the *shevach* does not. Yisrael can therefore chase me through encumbered property for the principal, but for the improvements he can only collect from what I presently hold.

### Shevach Proof

What interests the *gemara* in this scenario, however, is not primarily where Yisrael can collect. The key question is: how did the creditor — call him Michael — get to take not just the land but the *shevach* as well? Michael's lien predates Yisrael's improvements. At the time the lien was created, the *shevach* did not exist. If Michael is entitled to collect his debt by taking the land together with the improvements that Yisrael made *after* the lien was created, this implies that Michael had liens extending to property or value that came into existence *after* the date of the loan. That is, it implies that future liens are operative. *D'ikni mah de'asid lemiknei* — the lien I created covers not just present property but future-acquired value.

The *gemara* again deflects: *ha mani Rabbi Meir hi d'amar adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam*. Rabbi Meir's position accounts for this. The evidence continues to accumulate, the defense continues to be deployed, and the pattern itself — the sheer volume of cases in which the *gemara* finds it necessary to invoke this defense — signals that the underlying assumption of the entire legal system is that future liens are operative.

### Two Liens Priority

The *gemara* then poses a cascading hypothetical, introduced with *im timtzei lomar* — if you were to rule that future liens do work. The question: *lava lava vachazar vekana mahu* — suppose a debtor creates two separate future-lien agreements with two different creditors, and then subsequently acquires a new piece of property. Who has priority?

### Sequential Liens

The scenario is constructed as follows. On January 1, the debtor tells creditor one: future property I acquire will be encumbered to you. On January 15, he makes the same agreement with creditor two. On January 30, he acquires a plot of land. When that land comes into existence in his possession, both creditors' liens activate simultaneously. On pre-existing property, the principle is clear: *rishon kana* — the first lien in time has priority, because when the second lien was created the property was already partially encumbered. But here, neither lien was attached to anything at the time it was created. Both materialize at the same moment — when the property is acquired on January 30. Does the first creditor's temporal priority carry through? Or do both liens spring into existence simultaneously, giving them equal standing?

### Rav Nachman Debate

Rav Nachman reports that this question was sent to the scholars of Eretz Yisrael and the response that came back was *rishon kana* — the first creditor takes priority, analogous to the ordinary rule of liens. Rav Huna disagrees and rules *yachloku* — they divide equally. Rabba bar Avuha likewise rules *yachloku*.

### Rav Ashi Cycles

Ravina adds a historically notable detail: in Rav Ashi's first *madura* — his first cycle of teaching — he ruled *rishon kana*, first takes all. In his latter *madura*, he ruled *yachloku*, split equally. The term *madura kama* and *madura basra* refers to the two great cycles through which Rav Ashi reviewed all of his learning, a process that the Rashbam documents from responsa of Rav Hai Gaon and the commentary of Rabbeinu Chananel. Rav Ashi lived sixty years, and every year he devoted the two months of the *kala* — Nisan and Tishrei — to *chazarah*, public review of prior learning. After thirty years he had completed one full cycle of review; the second thirty years constituted his second cycle. The first cycle is *madura kama*, the second *madura basra*. This terminology was subsequently adopted by later *Rishonim* and *Acharonim* to describe first and revised editions of their own works, most famously the *Noda Biyehuda*, whose *kama* and *tinyana* are volumes one and two — though those are sequential volumes rather than revised editions of the same material.

### Hilchata Ruling

*Ve'hilchasa yachloku* — the *halacha* is that the two creditors split the liens equally. When future liens materialize simultaneously upon acquisition of the property, neither creditor has temporal priority over the other in the moment that matters, and they divide the encumbrance equally.

### Meisivei Question

The *gemara* now raises an objection (*meisivei*) from the *shevach karkaos* case. We said: Michael, the creditor, comes and seizes the land Yisrael improved, taking both the land and its *shevach*. But if future liens exist and split equally, then Yisrael — who himself has a future-lien claim against me for the *achrayus* on the principal — should have equal standing on the *shevach* alongside Michael. Why does Michael collect all of the *shevach*? Should not Yisrael share it?

### Split Resolution

The *gemara* resolves this: *lo mai goveh nami de'katani chatzi shevach* — that is precisely what the *mishna* means. Michael does not collect all the *shevach*. He collects half the *shevach*. Yisrael retains the other half as payment toward the principal he is owed. Once we acknowledge that both Michael and Yisrael hold future-lien claims that activate simultaneously on the *shevach*, the *hilchata* of *yachloku* applies: they split it equally, and Yisrael's half of the *shevach* is credited toward what I owe him.

One might ask why the *shevach* that Yisrael generated is treated as property that I came into possession of — which is the premise for my lien extending to it. The *gemara* does not fully develop this point, but it operates on that assumption.

The daf closes with the principle, supported by the full weight of the evidence marshaled throughout the discussion, that future liens are indeed operative — presumably for one of the two reasons the Rashbam identifies: either because a *shibud* is sufficiently abstract to be distinguishable from a full *kinyan*, such that one can pre-create it on property not yet in one's possession, or, more likely, because the Rabbis affirmatively legislated this power into existence — *d'lo tinal delet bifnei lovin* — so as not to lock the door before borrowers and thereby impede the extension of credit.
