# Berakhot 12b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Emet Veyatziv & Emunah

### Intro: Morning/Evening

The preceding discussion on this daf concerned the *brachot* that surround the *Shema* — those recited before and after. We examined *Yotzer HaMeorot* and *Ahava Rabbah* before the *Shema*, noting how *Yotzer HaMeorot* belongs distinctively to the morning (paralleled by *Maariv Aravim* in the evening), while *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam* are tied more to the themes of *Shema* itself and *Talmud Torah*. We even discussed the possibility of reciting *Shema* without *Yotzer HaMeorot* if the proper time has not yet arrived — a ruling that underscores that fundamental distinction. We now turn to the *brachot* recited after the *Shema*: specifically, *Emet Veyatziv* and *Emet Ve'emunah*.

### Emet Veyatziv vs Emunah

Rabbah bar Chinena Sava teaches in the name of Rav: whoever did not say *Emet Veyatziv* in the morning and *Emet Ve'emunah* in the evening has not fulfilled his obligation. The point here is not merely that one must recite a *bracha* at the close of *Shema* — that much is already established — but that the specific formulation matters. Just as there is a distinction between *Maariv Aravim* and *Yotzer HaMeorot*, and a subtler but real distinction between *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam*, so too there is a meaningful difference in the closing *bracha*: *Emet Veyatziv* in the morning and *Emet Ve'emunah* in the evening.

### Psalm Source

The textual basis is drawn from Psalms: *lehagid baboker chasdecha ve'emunatecha baleilot* — "to tell of Your kindness in the morning and Your faithfulness in the nights." The morning is the time of *chesed*, of experiencing God's beneficence and gifts; the evening draws forth a different register — a sense of concern, of darkness, of uncertainty about the night ahead, evoking hope and trust rather than celebration of manifest blessing. This parallels the distinction between *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam* precisely: the morning *bracha* emphasizes experienced love and abundance, the evening *bracha* an enduring love sustained through faith even when God's presence is less immediately felt.

### Rashi: Content Themes

Rashi makes an important observation two lines before the relevant passage. He notes that *Emet Veyatziv* is entirely about the *chesed* God performed for our ancestors — taking them out of Egypt, splitting the sea, and bringing them across. By contrast, *Emet Ve'emunah* speaks also of future hope: we await the fulfillment of God's promise and His faithfulness to redeem us from the power of kings. This is not only a matter of past versus future, of praise versus petition, but reflects the experiential morning-evening difference as well — the same dynamic that distinguishes *Ahava Rabbah* from *Ahavat Olam*.

## Bowing & Rising

### Bow at Baruch, Rise at S…

Rabbah bar Chinena Sava teaches another ruling in the name of Rav, this one concerning bodily posture during the *Amidah*: when one bows, one should bow at the word *Baruch*; when one rises, one should rise at *Shem Hashem*. The choreography is precise — bending the knees at *Baruch*, remaining bowed through *Atah*, and straightening as God's name is pronounced.

### Samuel's Reason

Samuel offers the rationale: as it is written, *Hashem zokef kefufim* — God straightens those who are bent. It is therefore the utterance of God's name that causes the worshiper to rise from the bowed position. The name of God is what elevates us.

### Challenge: Name & Humili…

The Gemara raises a challenge from the verse *mipnei shemi nichat* — "because of My name he is humbled." This verse appears to indicate the opposite: that God's name produces a sense of lowliness and humility, not elevation.

### Resolution

The Gemara resolves the tension: the verse says *mipnei shemi* — because of My name, on account of the concept of God's name — not *bishmi*, in the articulation of My name. The contemplation of God's greatness produces humility; but the actual utterance of God's name in prayer is the moment of reaching out and connecting, which is what raises the worshiper upright.

This resolution reflects a dialectic that has run throughout the earlier discussion. Prayer simultaneously requires a profound sense of our own smallness and an act of reaching toward God. We encountered this earlier in the ruling that one should stand in a low place yet with feet together like an angel. Here too: the bowing down expresses our nothingness in the presence of God, while the pronouncing of His name — the act of connection — is precisely what lifts us. Samuel's teaching to Chiya bar Rav, whom he addresses as *bar orayan* — whether "son of a lion" or "son of Torah" — conveys this exact principle: "Let me tell you something beautiful your father said." What follows is the same teaching we have just seen, transmitted in Rav's name.

### Rav Sheshet's Example

The Gemara moves to 12b and describes the practice of Rav Sheshet: when he bowed down, he would bow like a rod — that is, like a staff thrown to the ground, immediately, all at once, without hesitation. And when he rose, he rose like a snake — slowly, gradually, the way a snake rises from the ground, head first. Rashi explains that the bowing should not resemble a *massa*, a burden one is eager to put down; the emphasis must fall on the immediacy of awe before God. The weight is on falling before Him without hesitation, while the rising is a slow, effortful process of regaining one's stature — again capturing that interplay between humility and the dignity of connection.

## HaMelech HaKadosh

### Year-Round Phrasing

Rabbah bar Chinena Sava transmits yet another teaching from Rav, now concerning the *nusach* of *Shemoneh Esrei* rather than its physical performance. Throughout the entire year, when one recites the third *bracha* of *Shemoneh Esrei*, one says *Ha'El HaKadosh*; and in the *bracha* concerning justice in the middle of *Shemoneh Esrei*, one says *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*.

### Ten Days Exception

The exception is the ten days encompassing Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*. It should be noted that technically there are only seven days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, but the expression refers to the ten days as a unit. During this period, one says *HaMelech HaKadosh* instead of *Ha'El HaKadosh*, and *HaMelech HaMishpat* instead of *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*. The theme of God's *malchut* dominates these days; accordingly, not only does *Melech* remain in the formula (as it always was), but *mishpat* is no longer merely something God loves — it becomes the very expression of His identity. Both *melech* and *mishpat* are intensified.

It is worth noting that *HaMelech HaKadosh* on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves was not really in dispute — the question was whether to extend this formulation across the full *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, and whether *HaMelech HaMishpat* follows the same pattern.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Lenient

Rabbi Eliezer takes a more lenient position: even if one said *Ha'El HaKadosh* during the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, one has fulfilled the obligation *bedieved*. His proof is from the verse: *vayigbah Hashem Tzeva'ot bamishpat veha'El haKadosh nikdash bitzedakah* — the glory of God is magnified through judgment, and the holy God is sanctified through righteousness. Rabbi Eliezer reads this verse as referring precisely to the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, when the focus is on God as judge, and yet the verse concludes with *Ha'El HaKadosh*. This suggests that *Ha'El HaKadosh* remains acceptable even during those days, and presumably *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat* as well.

### Rav Yosef vs Rabbah

The Gemara asks what the final ruling is. Rav Yosef holds that one maintains the regular *nusach* — presumably even *lechatchila* one says *Ha'El HaKadosh* and *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*, and the change to *HaMelech HaKadosh* applies only on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves, not across the intervening days. Rabbah disagrees and rules that one says *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat* throughout the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*. The Gemara rules: *ve'hilchata keRabbah* — the *halacha* follows Rabbah, as indeed reflects our accepted *psak*.

### Tosafot: Must Repeat

Tosafot notes that if one failed to say *HaMelech HaKadosh* or *HaMelech HaMishpat* during the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, one must repeat the *Shemoneh Esrei*. Tosafot then extends this principle to the additional insertions of *Zachreinu leChayyim*, *Mi Chamocha*, *Ketov leChayyim*, and *uveSefer Chayyim* — all of which were added to the *nusach* by the *Geonim* — and initially suggests that if one omitted these as well, one must return. However, Tosafot ultimately qualifies this: Rabbeinu Tam (or Rabbeinu Chananel, depending on the reading) writes that since these additions were not explicitly ruled upon in the Talmud, one does not return for them. And that is indeed how we rule: one returns for *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*, but not for the *Zachreinu* formulas and similar additions introduced by later authorities.

## Praying for Others

### Can Pray = Must Pray

We have now seen three teachings transmitted by Rabbah bar Chinena Sava in the name of Rav: the distinction between *Emet Veyatziv* and *Emet Ve'emunah*; the laws of bowing and rising; and the *nusach* of *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*. A fourth teaching follows: whoever is in a position to pray on behalf of a friend and does not do so is called a sinner.

### Samuel's Proof

The proof is from the words of Samuel to Saul: *Gam anochi chalilah li mecheto la'Hashem mechadol lehitpalel ba'adchem* — "Far be it from me to sin against God by ceasing to pray on your behalf." The implication is that withholding prayer when you could offer it constitutes a sin.

The force of this teaching deserves reflection. People typically engage in *tefillah* as a statutory obligation, which does not necessarily express a deep personal conviction about the way God operates in the world. But when someone responds to a specific situation — when they perceive a friend's need and pray because something is actually at stake — that reflects a real, lived belief in the efficacy of prayer. The *chaveiro* dimension adds an additional layer: it is not only about faith, but about care for another person. If you believe that prayer can help, and your friend needs it, then failing to pray constitutes a kind of abandonment. That gives the entire concept of prayer a dimension beyond obligation — it becomes a means of helping others, something we bear responsibility for.

### Rava: Extra Measure

Rava adds a further requirement: if the person who needs your prayers is a *talmid chacham*, it is not sufficient merely to pray — one must make oneself ill on his behalf. The Gemara asks what the source for this is. The verse cited is Saul's complaint that no one was *choleh* for him — nobody was sick with worry on his account, no one was revealing things to him. The Gemara reads this literally: making yourself sick over the person is the standard required. But perhaps that obligation applies only for a king? The Gemara deflects to a different verse: David's statement, *va'ani bechalotam levushi* — in their illness I wore sackcloth. David afflicted himself when others fell ill, and that becomes the model for how intensely one must pray for a *talmid chacham*.

### Shame Leads to Forgivene…

The teaching shifts now from prayer to moral psychology, though the common thread is the interior life of religious obligation. Rabbah bar Chinena Sava teaches in the name of Rav: anyone who commits a sin and is ashamed by it has all his sins forgiven. This connects to an earlier theme — that internalizing a message through genuine feeling is more transformative than external punishment. When a person feels shame, when they are genuinely embarrassed by what they have done, that experience can catalyze a profound inner transformation. The forgiveness of *kol avonotav* is not merely a consequence of suffering; it reflects the hope that shame will produce real change.

### Shame & Atonement

The textual basis is the verse: *lema'an tizkeri uvoshet velo yihyeh lach od pitchon peh mipnei kelimatecha bechappri lach lechol asher asit ne'um Hashem Elokim* — "in order that you remember and be embarrassed, and no longer have any opening because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done, says the Lord God." The Gemara reads this causally: because you bear the shame, God will atone for you.

### Saul's Example

The Gemara asks whether perhaps this applies only to the entire community, *tzibbur*, in the kind of collective national shame the verse describes — not to an individual. A second proof is therefore brought for the individual case, drawn from the exchange between Samuel and Saul when Samuel's spirit was raised.

Saul says to Samuel: *tzar li me'od uPlishtim nilchamim bi ve'Elokim sar me'alai velo anani od gam beyad hanevi'im gam bachalomot* — I am greatly distressed, the Philistines are fighting me, God has turned away from me and no longer answers me through prophets or through dreams. The Gemara notices something significant: Saul lists the channels through which he sought God's guidance — prophets and dreams — but conspicuously omits the *Urim v'Tummim*. Why? Because he had destroyed Nov, the city of the *kohanim*, and there was no one left to consult the *Urim v'Tummim*. His failure to mention it reflects an acute awareness of his guilt — he was too ashamed to raise the subject. His silence, for the Gemara, is itself an expression of genuine remorse.

The proof that Saul was forgiven: Samuel says to him, *umachar atah uvannecha immi* — tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. Rabbi Yochanan interprets this as *immi bimchitzati* — in my portion in the World to Come. Because Saul bore his shame, even in this minimal and largely passive form of not mentioning what he had done, he merited the World to Come and all his sins were forgiven. The bar for what constitutes meaningful shame, in this teaching, is set strikingly low — and perhaps that is precisely the point.

A second proof is brought from the verse *bechir Hashem* — "the chosen of God" — which appears in the context of the Gibeonites' complaint against Saul. The Gemara argues that it is not the Gibeonites themselves using this honorific but rather a heavenly voice that declared Saul to be God's chosen — and the reason is the same: because he felt shame over the destruction of Nov and withheld mention of it.

## Parashat Balak

### Proposal Overview

The Gemara now returns from this extended discussion to the topic of the liturgy surrounding *Kriyas Shema*. Rabbi Abba bar Zutrati cites Rabbi Yehuda bar Zvida: the Sages sought to include *Parshas Balak* as part of *Kriyas Shema*. This is a striking proposal — that a portion of the prophetic narrative concerning Balak and Bilam would become part of the daily recitation of *Shema*.

### Why Balak?

The Gemara first suggests the reason: *Parshas Balak* contains the verse *El motzi'am miMitzrayim* — "God who brought them out of Egypt." The third *parsha* of *Shema* — *Parshas Tzitzis* — is recited largely in order to fulfill the independent *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. One might think that *Parshas Balak* could serve this function equally, containing as it does a reference to the exodus. Indeed, the Gemara implies they would have made this substitution had the passage not been too lengthy — *torach tzibbur*, an imposition on the community.

### Objection

The Gemara presses further: many *parshiyot* contain references to the exodus — the prohibition of *ribis*, the laws of honest weights. If the sole criterion were the mention of *Yetzias Mitzrayim*, why should *Parshas Balak* be singled out? The reference to the exodus alone cannot be the full explanation.

### Rabbi Yosi Answer

Rabbi Yosi bar Avin provides the deeper reason: *Parshas Balak* contains the verse *kara shachav ka'ari uchelavia mi yekimenu* — "he crouched, he lay down like a lion, like a whelp — who will rouse him?" Rashi explains that this verse precisely parallels the central theme of *Kriyas Shema* — the lying down (*shechivah*) and rising up (*kimah*) that frame the daily recitation of *Shema*. Thus *Parshas Balak* would not merely supply a reference to *Yetzias Mitzrayim* but would echo the very thematic structure of *Kriyas Shema* itself, making it an organically fitting inclusion.

### Moshe's Divisions Rule

The Gemara then asks: if the critical verse is *kara shachav ka'ari* along with *El motzi'am miMitzrayim*, why not simply recite those two verses and leave it at that? There would be no *torach tzibbur* — just the essential content. The Gemara answers with a principle: *kol parsha d'paska Moshe Rabbeinu paskinan; d'la paska Moshe Rabbeinu la paskinan* — any *parsha* that Moses divided, we may divide; any *parsha* that Moses did not divide, we may not divide. We cannot extract individual verses from *Parshas Balak* for liturgical use; it must be either included in its entirety or not at all.

It is worth distinguishing this principle from the related rule that one does not divide a *pasuk* — that even as an individual, one should not quote only part of a verse. That rule governs individual speech. The principle here is specifically about the creation of official liturgy: we do not construct liturgy by taking selections from within an undivided *parsha*. That is why *Parshas Balak* was ultimately not included — its length made full inclusion impossible, and partial inclusion is prohibited by tradition.

## Parashat Tzitzit

### Five Themes

If *Parshas Balak* was not chosen, why was *Parshas Tzitzis* selected for *Kriyas Shema*? Rav Yehuda bar Chaviva explains: because *Parshas Tzitzis* contains not merely one or two significant themes but five essential elements that together make it uniquely suited.

### The Five Elements

The five themes are: the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* itself; *Yetzias Mitzrayim*; *ol mitzvot*, the yoke of the commandments; *da'at minim*, guarding against heretical thoughts; and *hirhur aveira*, the guarding against sinful thoughts — including *hirhur avodah zarah*, thoughts of idolatry. The Gemara notes that some reckon these as six rather than five, leading some to emend the *girsa* accordingly.

### Yoke of Mitzvot

The first three are readily derivable from the text. *Ol mitzvot* is based on the verse *ur'item oto uzchartem et kol mitzvot Hashem* — "you shall see it and remember all the commandments of God." *Tzitzit* itself is stated explicitly: *asu lachem tzitzit*. And *Yetzias Mitzrayim* is stated directly: *asher hotzeiti etchem*. These three are straightforward.

### Heresy Theme

The remaining themes require derivation. *Da'at minim* — heresy — comes from the phrase *acharei levavchem*, "do not stray after your hearts." In the conceptual world of *Chazal*, the heart is not only the seat of emotion but of intellect and thought. Straying after the heart therefore means being led astray by the mind into heretical ideas, into the intellectual rejection of God's existence or sovereignty. The Gemara supports this with the verse: *amar naval belibo ein Elohim* — the degenerate says in his heart there is no God. The *leiv* is the organ of heresy; the *tzitzit* keep the eye and mind anchored to truth.

### Sexual Sin Theme

*Hirhur aveira* — sinful thoughts, specifically of a sexual nature — is derived from *acharei eineichem*, "do not stray after your eyes." The support is from Shimshon, who says of the Philistine woman he desires: *otah kach li ki hi yashra be'einai* — "take her for me, for she is right in my eyes." Desire begins with visual perception; the eyes lead the heart into sin. The *tzitzit*, visible and present, redirect that gaze.

### Idolatry Theme

Finally, *hirhur avodah zarah* — thoughts of idolatry — is derived from *asher atem zonim achareihem*, "you will stray after them." Though the word *zonim* sounds like sexual infidelity, the Gemara explains it refers to spiritual infidelity, citing the verse *vayiznu acharei habalim* — "and they went astray after the *Ba'alim*." The relationship between Israel and God is consistently figured in the Torah as a marriage; worship of false gods is therefore described through the metaphor of adultery. The *tzitzit* guard against this ultimate betrayal.

Beyond the intrinsic importance of each of these themes, they resonate with the broader content of *Kriyas Shema* itself. *Ol mitzvot* echoes the commitment to observe the *mitzvot* expressed in the first two *parshiyot*. The warnings against *minut* and *avodah zarah* echo *Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* — the proclamation of God's unity and the rejection of all that contradicts it. The inclusion of *Parshas Tzitzis* thus reinforces and expands the central themes of *Shema* while simultaneously fulfilling the independent *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*.

## Remember Exodus

### Mishnah Statement

This discussion leads directly into the final *Mishnah* of the chapter. The *Mishnah* states: *mazkirin Yetzias Mitzrayim balaylot* — one mentions the exodus from Egypt at night. The context is *Kriyas Shema*: we know that *Shema* is recited both by day and by night, and that *Parshas Tzitzis* fulfills the separate *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. The question the *Mishnah* addresses is whether there is an obligation to remember *Yetzias Mitzrayim* at night as well.

### Rabbi Elazar's Lament

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — who was appointed *nasi* and miraculously appeared to age, such that he could say "I am like a seventy-year-old man" — declares that he never merited understanding how to derive the obligation of mentioning *Yetzias Mitzrayim* at night, until Ben Zoma came and explained it.

### Ben Zoma's Proof

Ben Zoma's proof rests on close reading of the verse *lema'an tizkor et yom tzeischa me'eretz Mitzrayim kol yemei chayyecha* — "so that you remember the day of your going out from Egypt all the days of your life." *Yemei chayyecha* would mean simply "the days of your life," i.e., daytime. But the word *kol* — "all" — extends the obligation to the fullness of each day, which includes the night. Thus the obligation to remember *Yetzias Mitzrayim* applies not only by day but by night as well.

### Sages' Counter

The Sages offer a different reading of the same *kol*. For them, *yemei chayyecha* refers to the days of this world, and *kol yemei chayyecha* — "all the days of your life" — means throughout this world, even into the future. The *kol* does not double the daily obligation into day and night; rather, it extends the temporal scope to ensure the obligation is not nullified even in the messianic era.

## Remember Exodus…

### Messianic Era

The Sages add: *lehavi limos haMashiach* — even in the days of the Messiah, when a greater redemption will have occurred, the memory of *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will not be set aside. The obligation persists across all time.

## Ben Zoma's Challenge

### Challenge Posed

The *Baraita* reports that Ben Zoma posed a direct challenge to the Sages on this very point. Would one really continue to mention *Yetzias Mitzrayim* in the messianic era? He cites the prophet's words: *hinei yamim ba'im ne'um Hashem velo ye'amer od Chai Hashem asher he'elah et Benei Yisrael me'eretz Mitzrayim ki im Chai Hashem asher he'elah ve'asher hevi et zera Beit Yisrael me'eretz tzafonah umikol ha'aratzos asher hidachtim sham* — "Days are coming, says God, when they will no longer say 'by the life of God who brought the Children of Israel out of Egypt,' but rather 'by the life of God who brought up and led the descendants of the House of Israel from the northern land and from all the lands where He had driven them.'" The future redemption will eclipse *Yetzias Mitzrayim* as the primary frame of reference; oaths will be sworn by the greater act of redemption. Ben Zoma therefore asks: will *Yetzias Mitzrayim* even be mentioned?

### Sages' Response

The Sages respond: *lo she'te'aker Yetzias Mitzrayim mimkomah* — this does not mean that *Yetzias Mitzrayim* loses its place entirely. Rather, *shibud malchuyot* — the redemption from all the nations — will become the *ikar*, the primary focus, and *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will become *tafel*, secondary, but still present and still remembered.

### Jacob Example

The Sages support this with the parallel of Jacob's name change: *lo yikarei shimcha od Yaakov ki im Yisrael yihyeh shemecha* — "your name shall no longer be called Jacob, but Israel shall be your name." Yet God Himself later calls him Jacob. This is not a contradiction; it means that the name Yisrael becomes the *ikar* while Yaakov becomes secondary — but both names remain his. So too with *Yetzias Mitzrayim*: it does not disappear; it is simply no longer primary.

### Prophet's Support

Further prophetic support is adduced from the verse: *al tizkeru rishonot veKadmoniyot al titbonanu* — "do not remember the former things and do not contemplate the earlier ones." The Gemara interprets: *rishonot* refers to *shibud malchuyot* — the servitude under the nations; *kadmoniyot* refers to *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. Both will still be remembered, but neither will dominate the consciousness of the redeemed — the new salvation, *hineni oseh chadashah*, will be primary. The school of Rav Yosef identifies this new thing as the war of *Gog u'Magog*.

### Parable of Dangers

A parable is offered: a man was walking and encountered a wolf, from which he was saved. He told everyone about the wolf. Then he encountered a lion and was saved; he told everyone about the lion. Then he encountered a snake and was saved — and he forgot both the wolf and the lion, speaking only of the snake. So too with Israel: *tzaros achronos meshakechos es harishonos* — the later afflictions cause the earlier ones to recede in memory, because the most recent danger seems greatest.

It must be acknowledged that as a parable, this seems to cut somewhat against the Sages' conclusion rather than supporting it — for the man in the story actually *forgot* the earlier dangers, which would seem to support Ben Zoma's view that *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will no longer be mentioned. One reading is that the Gemara's point may be precisely the inverse: because human nature is to focus only on the most recent and most acute experience, the obligation is *davka* to continue remembering the earlier ones. The *mitzvah* exists precisely because the inclination is to forget. The Gemara, however, does not explicitly close this circle, and the tension remains.

## Name Changes

### Avram to Avraham

The Gemara now addresses the question of name changes as such, beginning with Avraham. The verse in *Divrei HaYamim* states: *Avram hu Avraham* — Avram is Avraham. The Gemara explains: initially he was made a father — *av* — to Aram alone, to the people of his region, *Aram Naharayim*. In the end he was made a father to the entire world — *av hamon goyim*, the father of many nations. The letter *heh* added to his name captures this expansion of scope.

### Sarai to Sarah

Similarly, *Sarai hi Sarah* — Sarai is Sarah. Initially she was *sarai* to her own nation alone — the possessive form, "my princess." In the end she became *Sarah* without the possessive, a princess to all the world.

### Bar Kappara Positive

Bar Kappara rules: anyone who calls Avraham by the name Avram transgresses a positive commandment, as the verse states: *v'hayah shimcha Avraham* — "your name shall be Avraham." The declaration of the name change carries the force of a *mitzvat asei*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Negative

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: it is not only a positive commandment but a negative prohibition. The basis is *v'lo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — "your name shall no longer be called Avram." This is not merely a statement of fact but an active prohibition.

### Sarah Question

The Gemara asks: if this is so regarding Avraham, should the same not apply to Sarah — that calling her by the old name Sarai would also be forbidden?

### Distinction

The Gemara draws a distinction. In the case of Avraham's name, God's command was given to Avraham in general terms, applicable to all: *v'hayah shimcha Avraham* and *v'lo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — universal declarations. But regarding Sarah's name, the verse reads: *Sarai ishtecha lo tikra et shemah Sarai ki Sarah shemah* — "Sarai your wife, you shall not call her name Sarai, for Sarah is her name." The address is in the second person singular — *lo tikra*, *you* shall not call her — directed specifically to Avraham, not to the world at large. There is therefore no general prohibition for others to call her Sarai.

### Chronicles Reference

The Gemara anticipates an objection: the verse in *Divrei HaYamim* itself uses the name Avram — *asher bacharta be'Avram* — "who chose Avram." Does this not indicate that calling him Avram is permissible?

The Gemara answers: there the *navi* is recounting the past — he is describing the moment of God's original choice, when the patriarch's name was indeed Avram, and declaring that God chose him then and subsequently gave him the name Avraham. The prophet is narrating what *was*, not referring to the patriarch by his old name as a present designation. The question of precisely when that line is crossed — whether a teacher explaining *Parshas Lech Lecha* may say "when God spoke to Avram" or must say "when God spoke to Avraham" — is left somewhat ambiguous by the Gemara. Most would naturally say Avraham in any case. Whether one would transgress a *lav* or an *asei* by saying Avram in such a context, and whether these are truly biblical prohibitions or function as *asmachta*, remains an open question that the sugya raises without fully resolving.
