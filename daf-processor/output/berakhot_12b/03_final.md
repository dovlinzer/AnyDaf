# Berakhot 12b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Emet Veyatziv & Emunah

### Intro: Morning/Evening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִינָּנָא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כָּל שֶׁלֹּא אָמַר "אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב" שַׁחֲרִית, וֶ"אֱמֶת וֶאֱמוּנָה" עַרְבִית — לֹא יָצָא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְהַגִּיד בַּבֹּקֶר חַסְדֶּךָ וֶאֱמוּנָתְךָ בַּלֵּילוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ḥinnana Sava said in the name of Rav: One who did not recite: True and Firm [*emet veyatziv*]** at the beginning of the blessing of redemption that follows *Shema* **in the morning prayer, and: True and Trustworthy [*emet ve'emuna*] in the evening prayer, he did not fulfill his obligation.** An allusion to the difference in formulation between morning and evening is, **as it is stated: "To declare Your kindness in the morning and Your faith in the nights"** (Psalms 92:3).

The preceding discussion on this daf concerned the *brachot* that surround the *Shema* — those recited before and after. We examined *Yotzer HaMeorot* and *Ahava Rabbah* before the *Shema*, noting how *Yotzer HaMeorot* belongs distinctively to the morning (paralleled by *Maariv Aravim* in the evening), while *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam* are tied more to the themes of *Shema* itself and *Talmud Torah*. We even discussed the possibility of reciting *Shema* without *Yotzer HaMeorot* if the proper time has not yet arrived — a ruling that underscores that fundamental distinction. We now turn to the *brachot* recited after the *Shema*: specifically, *Emet Veyatziv* and *Emet Ve'emunah*.

### Emet Veyatziv vs Emunah

The point here is not merely that one must recite a *bracha* at the close of *Shema* — that much is already established — but that the specific formulation matters. Just as there is a distinction between *Maariv Aravim* and *Yotzer HaMeorot*, and a subtler but real distinction between *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam*, so too there is a meaningful difference in the closing *bracha*: *Emet Veyatziv* in the morning and *Emet Ve'emunah* in the evening.

### Psalm Source

The textual basis is drawn from Psalms: *lehagid baboker chasdecha ve'emunatecha baleilot* — "to tell of Your kindness in the morning and Your faithfulness in the nights." The morning is the time of *chesed*, of experiencing God's beneficence and gifts; the evening draws forth a different register — a sense of concern, of darkness, of uncertainty about the night ahead, evoking hope and trust rather than celebration of manifest blessing. This parallels the distinction between *Ahava Rabbah* and *Ahavat Olam* precisely: the morning *bracha* emphasizes experienced love and abundance, the evening *bracha* an enduring love sustained through faith even when God's presence is less immediately felt.

### Rashi: Content Themes

Rashi makes an important observation two lines before the relevant passage. He notes that *Emet Veyatziv* is entirely about the *chesed* God performed for our ancestors — taking them out of Egypt, splitting the sea, and bringing them across. By contrast, *Emet Ve'emunah* speaks also of future hope: we await the fulfillment of God's promise and His faithfulness to redeem us from the power of kings. This is not only a matter of past versus future, of praise versus petition, but reflects the experiential morning-evening difference as well — the same dynamic that distinguishes *Ahava Rabbah* from *Ahavat Olam*.

## Bowing & Rising

### Bow at Baruch, Rise at S…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִינָּנָא [סָבָא] מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל, כְּשֶׁהוּא כּוֹרֵעַ כּוֹרֵעַ בְּבָרוּךְ, וּכְשֶׁהוּא זוֹקֵף — זוֹקֵף בַּשֵּׁם
>
> **Translation:** **And,** with regard to the formulation of the blessings, **Rabba bar Ḥinnana Sava said in the name of Rav: One who is praying, when he bows** in the appropriate places, **he bows when** he says: **Blessed, and when he** subsequently **stands upright, he stands upright when he says** God's **name.**

Rabbah bar Chinena Sava teaches another ruling in the name of Rav, this one concerning bodily posture during the *Amidah*: when one bows, one should bow at the word *Baruch*; when one rises, one should rise at *Shem Hashem*. The choreography is precise — bending the knees at *Baruch*, remaining bowed through *Atah*, and straightening as God's name is pronounced.

### Samuel's Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַאי טַעְמָא דְרַב — דִּכְתִיב: "ה׳ זוֹקֵף כְּפוּפִים".
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel,** who was Rav's colleague and significantly outlived him, **said: What is Rav's reason** for saying that one should stand upright at the mention of God's name? **As it is written: "The Lord, who raises the bowed"** (Psalms 146:8);

Samuel offers the rationale: as it is written, *Hashem zokef kefufim* — God straightens those who are bent. It is therefore the utterance of God's name that causes the worshiper to rise from the bowed position. The name of God is what elevates us.

### Challenge: Name & Humili…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיבִי: "מִפְּנֵי שְׁמִי נִחַת הוּא".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on what we learned in praise of a priest: **"And he was afraid before My name"** (Malachi 2:5),

The Gemara raises a challenge from the verse *mipnei shemi nichat* — "because of My name he is humbled." This verse appears to indicate the opposite: that God's name produces a sense of lowliness and humility, not elevation.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי כְּתִיב "בִּשְׁמִי"? "מִפְּנֵי שְׁמִי" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Is it written: At My name? Before My name, is written,** meaning that one is humbled and bows prior to the mention of God's name, when he says: Blessed.

The Gemara resolves the tension: the verse says *mipnei shemi* — because of My name, on account of the concept of God's name — not *bishmi*, in the articulation of My name. The contemplation of God's greatness produces humility; but the actual utterance of God's name in prayer is the moment of reaching out and connecting, which is what raises the worshiper upright.

This resolution reflects a dialectic that has run throughout the earlier discussion. Prayer simultaneously requires a profound sense of our own smallness and an act of reaching toward God. We encountered this earlier in the ruling that one should stand in a low place yet with feet together like an angel. Here too: the bowing down expresses our nothingness in the presence of God, while the pronouncing of His name — the act of connection — is precisely what lifts us. Samuel's teaching to Chiya bar Rav, whom he addresses as *bar orayan* — whether "son of a lion" or "son of Torah" — conveys this exact principle: "Let me tell you something beautiful your father said." What follows is the same teaching we have just seen, transmitted in Rav's name.

### Rav Sheshet's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, כִּי כָּרַע — כָּרַע כְּחִיזְרָא. כִּי קָא זָקֵיף — זָקֵיף כְּחִיוְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to bowing, the Gemara relates: **When Rav Sheshet bowed he bowed** all at once, **like a cane,** without delay. **When he stood upright he stood upright like a snake,** lifting himself slowly, demonstrating that the awe of God was upon him in the manner that he bowed and stood upright.

The Gemara moves to 12b and describes the practice of Rav Sheshet: when he bowed down, he would bow like a rod — that is, like a staff thrown to the ground, immediately, all at once, without hesitation. And when he rose, he rose like a snake — slowly, gradually, the way a snake rises from the ground, head first. Rashi explains that the bowing should not resemble a *massa*, a burden one is eager to put down; the emphasis must fall on the immediacy of awe before God. The weight is on falling before Him without hesitation, while the rising is a slow, effortful process of regaining one's stature — again capturing that interplay between humility and the dignity of connection.

## HaMelech HaKadosh

### Year-Round Phrasing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִינָּנָא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּל הַשָּׁנָה כּוּלָּהּ אָדָם מִתְפַּלֵּל "הָאֵל הַקָּדוֹשׁ", "מֶלֶךְ אוֹהֵב צְדָקָה וּמִשְׁפָּט", חוּץ מֵעֲשָׂרָה יָמִים שֶׁבֵּין רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, שֶׁמִּתְפַּלֵּל "הַמֶּלֶךְ הַקָּדוֹשׁ", וְ"הַמֶּלֶךְ הַמִּשְׁפָּט".
>
> **Translation:** **And,** with regard to the formulation of the blessings, **Rabba bar Ḥinnana Sava said in the name of Rav: Throughout the year a person prays** and concludes the third blessing of the *Amida* prayer with: **The holy God,** and concludes the blessing regarding the restoration of justice to Israel with: **King who loves righteousness and justice, with the exception of the ten days between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur,** the Ten Days of Atonement. These days are comprised of Rosh HaShana, Yom Kippur, and the seven days in between, when one emphasizes God's sovereignty, and so **when he prays** he concludes these blessings with: **The holy King and: The King of justice,** i.e., the King who reveals Himself through justice.

Throughout the entire year, when one recites the third *bracha* of *Shemoneh Esrei*, one says *Ha'El HaKadosh*; and in the *bracha* concerning justice in the middle of *Shemoneh Esrei*, one says *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*.

### Ten Days Exception

The exception is the ten days encompassing Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur — the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*. It should be noted that technically there are only seven days between Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur, but the expression refers to the ten days as a unit. During this period, one says *HaMelech HaKadosh* instead of *Ha'El HaKadosh*, and *HaMelech HaMishpat* instead of *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*. The theme of God's *malchut* dominates these days; accordingly, not only does *Melech* remain in the formula (as it always was), but *mishpat* is no longer merely something God loves — it becomes the very expression of His identity. Both *melech* and *mishpat* are intensified.

It is worth noting that *HaMelech HaKadosh* on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves was not really in dispute — the question was whether to extend this formulation across the full *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, and whether *HaMelech HaMishpat* follows the same pattern.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר "הָאֵל הַקָּדוֹשׁ" — יָצָא. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיִּגְבַּה ה׳ צְבָאוֹת בַּמִּשְׁפָּט וְהָאֵל הַקָּדוֹשׁ נִקְדָּשׁ בִּצְדָקָה". אֵימָתַי "וַיִּגְבַּה ה׳ צְבָאוֹת בַּמִּשְׁפָּט" — אֵלּוּ עֲשָׂרָה יָמִים שֶׁמֵּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וְעַד יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, וְקָאָמַר "הָאֵל הַקָּדוֹשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** In contrast, **Rabbi Elazar said** that one need not be exacting, and **even** if **he said: The holy God** during those ten days, he fulfilled his obligation, **as it is stated: "And the Lord of Hosts is exalted through justice, and the holy God is sanctified through righteousness"** (Isaiah 5:16). The Gemara explains: **When** is it appropriate to describe God with terms like: **And the Lord of Hosts is exalted through justice?** It is appropriate when God reveals Himself through justice, **during the ten days between Rosh HaShana and Yom Kippur,** yet the verse **says: The holy God.** This appellation sufficiently underscores God's transcendence, and there is no need to change the standard formula.

Rabbi Eliezer takes a more lenient position: even if one said *Ha'El HaKadosh* during the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, one has fulfilled the obligation *bedieved*. His proof is from the verse: *vayigbah Hashem Tzeva'ot bamishpat veha'El haKadosh nikdash bitzedakah* — the glory of God is magnified through judgment, and the holy God is sanctified through righteousness. Rabbi Eliezer reads this verse as referring precisely to the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, when the focus is on God as judge, and yet the verse concludes with *Ha'El HaKadosh*. This suggests that *Ha'El HaKadosh* remains acceptable even during those days, and presumably *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat* as well.

### Rav Yosef vs Rabbah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הֲוָה עֲלַהּ? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: "הָאֵל הַקָּדוֹשׁ" וּ"מֶלֶךְ אוֹהֵב צְדָקָה וּמִשְׁפָּט". רַבָּה אָמַר: "הַמֶּלֶךְ הַקָּדוֹשׁ" וְ"הַמֶּלֶךְ הַמִּשְׁפָּט". וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the conclusion that was reached **about this** *halakha*? Here, too, opinions differ: **Rav Yosef said** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar: There is no need to change the standard formula: **The holy God and: King Who loves righteousness and justice. Rabba said** in accordance with the opinion of Rav: **The holy King and: The King of justice.** The Gemara concludes: **The *halakha* is** in accordance **with** the opinion of **Rabba.**

The Gemara asks what the final ruling is. Rav Yosef holds that one maintains the regular *nusach* — presumably even *lechatchila* one says *Ha'El HaKadosh* and *Melech Ohev Tzedakah u'Mishpat*, and the change to *HaMelech HaKadosh* applies only on Rosh Hashanah and Yom Kippur themselves, not across the intervening days. Rabbah disagrees and rules that one says *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat* throughout the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*. The Gemara rules: *ve'hilchata keRabbah* — the *halacha* follows Rabbah, as indeed reflects our accepted *psak*.

### Tosafot: Must Repeat

Tosafot notes that if one failed to say *HaMelech HaKadosh* or *HaMelech HaMishpat* during the *Aseret Yemei Teshuvah*, one must repeat the *Shemoneh Esrei*. Tosafot then extends this principle to the additional insertions of *Zachreinu leChayyim*, *Mi Chamocha*, *Ketov leChayyim*, and *uveSefer Chayyim* — all of which were added to the *nusach* by the *Geonim* — and initially suggests that if one omitted these as well, one must return. However, Tosafot ultimately qualifies this: Rabbeinu Tam (or Rabbeinu Chananel, depending on the reading) writes that since these additions were not explicitly ruled upon in the Talmud, one does not return for them. And that is indeed how we rule: one returns for *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*, but not for the *Zachreinu* formulas and similar additions introduced by later authorities.

## Praying for Others

### Can Pray = Must Pray

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִינָּנָא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כֹּל שֶׁאֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל חֲבֵירוֹ וְאֵינוֹ מְבַקֵּשׁ — נִקְרָא "חוֹטֵא". שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "גַּם אָנֹכִי חָלִילָה לִּי מֵחֲטֹא לַה׳ מֵחֲדֹל לְהִתְפַּלֵּל בַּעַדְכֶם".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba bar Ḥinnana Sava said in the name of Rav: Anyone who can ask for mercy on behalf of another, and does not ask is called a sinner, as it is stated** following Samuel's rebuke of the people: **"As for me, far be it from me that I should transgress against the Lord in ceasing to pray for you,** but I will teach you the good and the right way" (I Samuel 12:23).

We have now seen three teachings transmitted by Rabbah bar Chinena Sava in the name of Rav: the distinction between *Emet Veyatziv* and *Emet Ve'emunah*; the laws of bowing and rising; and the *nusach* of *HaMelech HaKadosh* and *HaMelech HaMishpat*. A fourth teaching follows: whoever is in a position to pray on behalf of a friend and does not do so is called a sinner.

### Samuel's Proof

The proof is from the words of Samuel to Saul: *Gam anochi chalilah li mecheto la'Hashem mechadol lehitpalel ba'adchem* — "Far be it from me to sin against God by ceasing to pray on your behalf." The implication is that withholding prayer when you could offer it constitutes a sin.

The force of this teaching deserves reflection. People typically engage in *tefillah* as a statutory obligation, which does not necessarily express a deep personal conviction about the way God operates in the world. But when someone responds to a specific situation — when they perceive a friend's need and pray because something is actually at stake — that reflects a real, lived belief in the efficacy of prayer. The *chaveiro* dimension adds an additional layer: it is not only about faith, but about care for another person. If you believe that prayer can help, and your friend needs it, then failing to pray constitutes a kind of abandonment. That gives the entire concept of prayer a dimension beyond obligation — it becomes a means of helping others, something we bear responsibility for.

### Rava: Extra Measure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִם תַּלְמִיד חָכָם הוּא, צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּחֲלֶה עַצְמוֹ עָלָיו. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב: "וְאֵין חֹלֶה מִכֶּם עָלַי וְאֵין גּוֹלֶה אֶת אָזְנִי" — דִּילְמָא מֶלֶךְ שָׁאנֵי. אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: "וַאֲנִי בַּחֲלוֹתָם לְבוּשִׁי וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: If** the one in need of mercy **is a Torah scholar,** it is insufficient to merely pray on his behalf. Rather, **one must make himself ill** worrying **about him.** The Gemara seeks to clarify the source of this *halakha*. **What is the reason** that one must make oneself ill over a Torah scholar in need of mercy? **If you say** that it is **because** of what Saul said to his men, **as it is written: "And there is none of you that is ill over me or tells unto me"** (I Samuel 22:8), meaning that because Saul was a Torah scholar, it would have been appropriate for people to make themselves ill worrying about him; this is not an absolute proof. **Perhaps a king is different,** and excessive worry is appropriate in that case. Rather, proof that one must make oneself ill over a Torah scholar in need for mercy is **from here:** When David speaks of his enemies, Doeg and Ahitophel, who were Torah scholars, he says: **"But for me, when they were sick, my clothing was sackcloth,** I afflicted my soul with fasting" (Psalms 35:13).

Rava adds a further requirement: if the person who needs your prayers is a *talmid chacham*, it is not sufficient merely to pray — one must make oneself ill on his behalf. The Gemara asks what the source for this is. The verse cited is Saul's complaint that no one was *choleh* for him — nobody was sick with worry on his account, no one was revealing things to him. The Gemara reads this literally: making yourself sick over the person is the standard required. But perhaps that obligation applies only for a king? The Gemara deflects to a different verse: David's statement, *va'ani bechalotam levushi* — in their illness I wore sackcloth. David afflicted himself when others fell ill, and that becomes the model for how intensely one must pray for a *talmid chacham*.

### Shame Leads to Forgivene…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר חִינָּנָא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כָּל הָעוֹשֶׂה דְּבַר עֲבֵירָה וּמִתְבַּיֵּישׁ בּוֹ — מוֹחֲלִין לוֹ עַל כָּל עֲוֹנוֹתָיו. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לְמַעַן תִּזְכְּרִי וָבֹשְׁתְּ וְלֹא יִהְיֶה לָּךְ עוֹד פִּתְחוֹן פֶּה מִפְּנֵי כְּלִמָּתֵךְ בְּכַפְּרִי לָךְ לְכָל אֲשֶׁר עָשִׂית נְאֻם ה׳ אֱלֹהִים".
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba bar Ḥinnana Sava said in the name of Rav: One who commits an act of transgression and is ashamed of it, all of his transgressions are forgiven.** Shame is a sign that one truly despises his transgressions and that shame has the power to atone for his actions (Rabbi Yoshiyahu Pinto), **as it is stated: "In order that you remember, and be embarrassed, and never open your mouth anymore, because of your shame, when I have forgiven you for all that you have done, said the Lord, God"** (Ezekiel 16:63).

The teaching shifts now from prayer to moral psychology, though the common thread is the interior life of religious obligation. Rabbah bar Chinena Sava teaches in the name of Rav: anyone who commits a sin and is ashamed by it has all his sins forgiven. This connects to an earlier theme — that internalizing a message through genuine feeling is more transformative than external punishment. When a person feels shame, when they are genuinely embarrassed by what they have done, that experience can catalyze a profound inner transformation. The forgiveness of *kol avonotav* is not merely a consequence of suffering; it reflects the hope that shame will produce real change.

### Shame & Atonement

The textual basis is the verse: *lema'an tizkeri uvoshet velo yihyeh lach od pitchon peh mipnei kelimatecha bechappri lach lechol asher asit ne'um Hashem Elokim* — "in order that you remember and be embarrassed, and no longer have any opening because of your shame, when I atone for you for all that you have done, says the Lord God." The Gemara reads this causally: because you bear the shame, God will atone for you.

### Saul's Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא צִבּוּר שָׁאנֵי. אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: "וַיֹּאמֶר שְׁמוּאֵל אֶל שָׁאוּל לָמָּה הִרְגַּזְתַּנִי לְהַעֲלוֹת אֹתִי וַיֹּאמֶר שָׁאוּל צַר לִי מְאֹד וּפְלִשְׁתִּים נִלְחָמִים בִּי וַה׳ סָר מֵעָלַי וְלֹא עָנָנִי עוֹד גַּם בְּיַד הַנְּבִיאִים גַּם בַּחֲלֹמוֹת וָאֶקְרָאֶה לְךָ לְהוֹדִיעֵנִי מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה". וְאִילּוּ "אוּרִים וְתֻמִּים" לָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** However this proof is rejected: **Perhaps a community is different,** as a community is forgiven more easily than an individual. **Rather,** proof that an individual ashamed of his actions is forgiven for his transgressions is cited **from here,** when King Saul consulted Samuel by means of a necromancer before his final war with the Philistines: **"And Samuel said to Saul, why have you angered me to bring me up? And Saul said, I am very pained, and the Philistines are waging war against me, and God has removed Himself from me and answers me no more, neither by the hands of the prophets nor by dreams. And I call to you to tell me what to do"** (I Samuel 28:15). Saul says that he consulted prophets and dreams, but **he did not say** that he consulted **the *Urim VeTummim*.**

The Gemara asks whether perhaps this applies only to the entire community, *tzibbur*, in the kind of collective national shame the verse describes — not to an individual. A second proof is therefore brought for the individual case, drawn from the exchange between Samuel and Saul when Samuel's spirit was raised.

Saul says to Samuel: *tzar li me'od uPlishtim nilchamim bi ve'Elokim sar me'alai velo anani od gam beyad hanevi'im gam bachalomot* — I am greatly distressed, the Philistines are fighting me, God has turned away from me and no longer answers me through prophets or through dreams. The Gemara notices something significant: Saul lists the channels through which he sought God's guidance — prophets and dreams — but conspicuously omits the *Urim v'Tummim*. Why? Because he had destroyed Nov, the city of the *kohanim*, and there was no one left to consult the *Urim v'Tummim*. His failure to mention it reflects an acute awareness of his guilt — he was too ashamed to raise the subject. His silence, for the Gemara, is itself an expression of genuine remorse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּקַטְלֵיהּ לְנוֹב עִיר הַכֹּהֲנִים. וּמִנַּיִן דְּאַחִילוּ לֵיהּ מִן שְׁמַיָּא — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר שְׁמוּאֵל אֶל שָׁאוּל (מָחָר) [וּמָחָר] אַתָּה וּבָנֶיךָ עִמִּי", וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן "עִמִּי" — בִּמְחִיצָתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The reason for this is **because he killed** all the residents of **Nov, the city of priests,** and because of this transgression Saul was ashamed to consult the *Urim VeTummim*, which was accomplished by means of a priest. The Gemara concludes: **And from where** is it derived **that Saul was pardoned by** God in **the heavens** for his transgressions? **As it is stated: "And Samuel said to Saul: Tomorrow you and your sons will be with me"** (I Samuel 28:19). **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: With me** does not only mean that they will die, but also means, in a statement that contains an aspect of consolation, that they will be **in my company** among the righteous in heaven,

The proof that Saul was forgiven: Samuel says to him, *umachar atah uvannecha immi* — tomorrow you and your sons will be with me. Rabbi Yochanan interprets this as *immi bimchitzati* — in my portion in the World to Come. Because Saul bore his shame, even in this minimal and largely passive form of not mentioning what he had done, he merited the World to Come and all his sins were forgiven. The bar for what constitutes meaningful shame, in this teaching, is set strikingly low — and perhaps that is precisely the point.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן אָמְרִי, מֵהָכָא: "וְהוֹקַעְנוּם לַה׳ בְּגִבְעַת שָׁאוּל בְּחִיר ה׳". יָצְתָה בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה "בְּחִיר ה׳".
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say** that proof that Saul was pardoned is derived **from here,** from what the Gibeonites said to David: "Let seven men of his sons be given to us **and we will hang them up unto the Lord in the Giva of Saul, the chosen of the Lord"** (II Samuel 21:6). Certainly the Gibeonites, who were furious at Saul, would not refer to him as the chosen of the Lord. Therefore, this phrase must be understood as having been spoken by **a Divine Voice that emerged and said the chosen of the Lord,** because Saul had been pardoned for his transgressions and included among the completely righteous.

A second proof is brought from the verse *bechir Hashem* — "the chosen of God" — which appears in the context of the Gibeonites' complaint against Saul. The Gemara argues that it is not the Gibeonites themselves using this honorific but rather a heavenly voice that declared Saul to be God's chosen — and the reason is the same: because he felt shame over the destruction of Nov and withheld mention of it.

## Parashat Balak

### Proposal Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ בֶּן זוּטַרְתִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר זְבִידָא: בִּקְּשׁוּ לִקְבּוֹעַ פָּרָשַׁת בָּלָק בִּקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וּמִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא קְבָעוּהָ — מִשּׁוּם טוֹרַח צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu ben Zutarti said** that **Rabbi Yehuda bar Zevida said:** The Sages **sought to establish** the blessings of Balaam that appear in **the** Torah **portion of Balak,** as part of **the** twice-daily **recitation of** *Shema*. **And why did they not establish it** there? **Because** extending *Shema* would place an **encumbrance on the congregation,** from which the Sages sought to refrain.

The Gemara now returns from this extended discussion to the topic of the liturgy surrounding *Kriyas Shema*. Rabbi Abba bar Zutrati cites Rabbi Yehuda bar Zvida: the Sages sought to include *Parshas Balak* as part of *Kriyas Shema*. This is a striking proposal — that a portion of the prophetic narrative concerning Balak and Bilam would become part of the daily recitation of *Shema*.

### Why Balak?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב בָּהּ: "אֵל מֹצִיאָם מִמִּצְרַיִם" — לֵימָא פָּרָשַׁת רִבִּית וּפָרָשַׁת מִשְׁקָלוֹת דִּכְתִיב בָּהֶן יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks: **Why** did the Sages seek to add the blessings of Balaam in the first place? **If you say** that they did so **because** the exodus from Egypt is mentioned, **as it is written therein: "God, who brought them forth out of Egypt,** is like the horns of the wild ram" (Numbers 23:22), certainly mention of the Exodus is not unique to this Torah portion. Many other portions mention the exodus as well. **Let us say the portion of usury** (Leviticus 25:35–38) or the **portion of weights** (Leviticus 19:35–37), **as the exodus from Egypt is written therein** as well. In addition, they are brief and would not constitute an encumbrance on the congregation.

The Gemara first suggests the reason: *Parshas Balak* contains the verse *El motzi'am miMitzrayim* — "God who brought them out of Egypt." The third *parsha* of *Shema* — *Parshas Tzitzis* — is recited largely in order to fulfill the independent *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. One might think that *Parshas Balak* could serve this function equally, containing as it does a reference to the exodus. Indeed, the Gemara implies they would have made this substitution had the passage not been too lengthy — *torach tzibbur*, an imposition on the community.

### Objection

The Gemara presses further: many *parshiyot* contain references to the exodus — the prohibition of *ribis*, the laws of honest weights. If the sole criterion were the mention of *Yetzias Mitzrayim*, why should *Parshas Balak* be singled out? The reference to the exodus alone cannot be the full explanation.

### Rabbi Yosi Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין, מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב בָּהּ הַאי קְרָא: "כָּרַע שָׁכַב כַּאֲרִי וּכְלָבִיא מִי יְקִימֶנּוּ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yosei bar Avin said:** The reason the Sages sought to establish the portion of Balak as part of the recitation of *Shema* is **because it is written therein: "He couched, He lay down like a lion and a lioness; who shall rouse Him?** Those who bless You are blessed and those who curse You are cursed" (Numbers 24:9). This is reminiscent of what is said in *Shema*: When you lie down, and when you rise.

Rabbi Yosi bar Avin provides the deeper reason: *Parshas Balak* contains the verse *kara shachav ka'ari uchelavia mi yekimenu* — "he crouched, he lay down like a lion, like a whelp — who will rouse him?" Rashi explains that this verse precisely parallels the central theme of *Kriyas Shema* — the lying down (*shechivah*) and rising up (*kimah*) that frame the daily recitation of *Shema*. Thus *Parshas Balak* would not merely supply a reference to *Yetzias Mitzrayim* but would echo the very thematic structure of *Kriyas Shema* itself, making it an organically fitting inclusion.

### Moshe's Divisions Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵימָא הַאי פְּסוּקָא וְתוּ לָא. גְּמִירִי: כֹּל פָּרָשָׁה דְּפַסְקַהּ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ — פָּסְקִינַן, דְּלָא פַּסְקַהּ מֹשֶׁה רַבֵּינוּ — לָא פָּסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** On this, the Gemara asks: **And** if it is important to include this as part of *Shema* because of this single verse, then **let us say this verse and nothing more.** The Gemara rejects this: It is impossible to do this, as **they learned** through tradition that **any portion that Moses, our teacher, divided, we** too **divide** and read separately. However, a portion **that Moses, our teacher, did not divide, we do not divide** and read separately. And, as stated above, the Sages did not wish to institute the recitation of the entire portion of Balak to avoid placing an encumbrance on the congregation.

The Gemara then asks: if the critical verse is *kara shachav ka'ari* along with *El motzi'am miMitzrayim*, why not simply recite those two verses and leave it at that? There would be no *torach tzibbur* — just the essential content. The Gemara answers with a principle: *kol parsha d'paska Moshe Rabbeinu paskinan; d'la paska Moshe Rabbeinu la paskinan* — any *parsha* that Moses divided, we may divide; any *parsha* that Moses did not divide, we may not divide. We cannot extract individual verses from *Parshas Balak* for liturgical use; it must be either included in its entirety or not at all.

It is worth distinguishing this principle from the related rule that one does not divide a *pasuk* — that even as an individual, one should not quote only part of a verse. That rule governs individual speech. The principle here is specifically about the creation of official liturgy: we do not construct liturgy by taking selections from within an undivided *parsha*. That is why *Parshas Balak* was ultimately not included — its length made full inclusion impossible, and partial inclusion is prohibited by tradition.

## Parashat Tzitzit

### Five Themes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּרָשַׁת צִיצִית מִפְּנֵי מָה קְבָעוּהָ? אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר חֲבִיבָא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים: מִצְוַת צִיצִית, יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם, עוֹל מִצְוֹת, וְדַעַת מִינִים, הִרְהוּר עֲבֵירָה, וְהִרְהוּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Why was the portion of ritual fringes established** as part of the recitation of *Shema* when its content is unrelated to that of the preceding portions? **Rabbi Yehuda bar Ḥaviva said:** The portion of ritual fringes was added **because it includes five elements** including the primary reason for its inclusion, the exodus from Egypt (*Melo HaRo'im*): **The mitzva of ritual fringes,** mention of **the exodus from Egypt,** the acceptance of **the yoke of mitzvot,** admonition against **the opinions of the heretics,** admonition against **thoughts of** the **transgressions** of licentiousness, **and** admonition against **thoughts of idolatry.**

If *Parshas Balak* was not chosen, why was *Parshas Tzitzis* selected for *Kriyas Shema*? Rav Yehuda bar Chaviva explains: because *Parshas Tzitzis* contains not merely one or two significant themes but five essential elements that together make it uniquely suited.

### The Five Elements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא הָנֵי תְּלָת מְפָרְשָׁן. עוֹל מִצְוֹת, דִּכְתִיב: "וּרְאִיתֶם אֹתוֹ וּזְכַרְתֶּם אֶת כָּל מִצְוֹת ה׳". צִיצִית, דִּכְתִיב: "וְעָשׂוּ לָהֶם צִיצִית וְגוֹ׳". יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם, דִּכְתִיב: "אֲשֶׁר הוֹצֵאתִי וְגוֹ׳". אֶלָּא: דַּעַת מִינִים, הִרְהוּר עֲבֵירָה, וְהִרְהוּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted, these three are mentioned explicitly: The yoke of mitzvot** is mentioned in the portion of ritual fringes, **as it is written: "And you shall look upon them and remember all the mitzvot of the Lord** and you shall do them" (Numbers 15:39). **Ritual fringes** are mentioned explicitly, **as it is written: "And they will make for themselves ritual fringes"** (Numbers 15:38). **The exodus from Egypt** is also mentioned explicitly, **as it is written:** "I am the Lord, your God, **who took you out** from the Land of Egypt" (Numbers 15:41). **But where do we** derive the other elements mentioned above: Admonition against **the opinions of the heretics,** admonition against **thoughts of transgressions** of licentiousness, **and** admonition against **thoughts of idolatry?**

The first three are readily derivable from the text. *Ol mitzvot* is based on the verse *ur'item oto uzchartem et kol mitzvot Hashem* — "you shall see it and remember all the commandments of God." *Tzitzit* itself is stated explicitly: *asu lachem tzitzit*. And *Yetzias Mitzrayim* is stated directly: *asher hotzeiti etchem*. These three are straightforward.

### Heresy Theme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: "אַחֲרֵי לְבַבְכֶם" — זוֹ מִינוּת, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "אָמַר נָבָל בְּלִבּוֹ אֵין אֱלֹהִים". "אַחֲרֵי עֵינֵיכֶם" — זֶה הִרְהוּר עֲבֵירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֹּאמֶר שִׁמְשׁוֹן אֶל אָבִיו אוֹתָהּ קַח לִי, כִּי הִיא יָשְׁרָה בְעֵינָי". "אַתֶּם זוֹנִים" — זֶה הִרְהוּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וַיִּזְנוּ אַחֲרֵי הַבְּעָלִים״.
>
> **Translation:** In response, the Gemara cites a *baraita* where these elements were derived from allusions in the verse, "You shall stray neither after your hearts nor after your eyes, after which you would lust" (Numbers 15:39). **As it was taught: "After your hearts" refers to following opinions **of heresy** that may arise in one's heart. The Gemara offers a proof, **as it is stated: "The fool said in his heart: 'There is no God';** they have been corrupt, they have acted abominably; there is none who does good" (Psalms 14:1). The phrase: **"After your eyes,"** in **this** verse refers to following **thoughts of transgressions** of licentiousness, that a person might see and desire, **as it is stated: "And Samson said to his father, 'That one take for me, for she is upright in my eyes'"** (Judges 14:3). The passage: **"You shall stray after"** refers to promiscuity, which in the parlance of the prophets is a metaphor for **idol worship, as it is stated:** "The children of Israel again **went astray after the Be'alim"** (Judges 8:33).

The remaining themes require derivation. *Da'at minim* — heresy — comes from the phrase *acharei levavchem*, "do not stray after your hearts." In the conceptual world of *Chazal*, the heart is not only the seat of emotion but of intellect and thought. Straying after the heart therefore means being led astray by the mind into heretical ideas, into the intellectual rejection of God's existence or sovereignty. The Gemara supports this with the verse: *amar naval belibo ein Elohim* — the degenerate says in his heart there is no God. The *leiv* is the organ of heresy; the *tzitzit* keep the eye and mind anchored to truth.

### Sexual Sin Theme

*Hirhur aveira* — sinful thoughts, specifically of a sexual nature — is derived from *acharei eineichem*, "do not stray after your eyes." The support is from Shimshon, who says of the Philistine woman he desires: *otah kach li ki hi yashra be'einai* — "take her for me, for she is right in my eyes." Desire begins with visual perception; the eyes lead the heart into sin. The *tzitzit*, visible and present, redirect that gaze.

### Idolatry Theme

Finally, *hirhur avodah zarah* — thoughts of idolatry — is derived from *asher atem zonim achareihem*, "you will stray after them." Though the word *zonim* sounds like sexual infidelity, the Gemara explains it refers to spiritual infidelity, citing the verse *vayiznu acharei habalim* — "and they went astray after the *Ba'alim*." The relationship between Israel and God is consistently figured in the Torah as a marriage; worship of false gods is therefore described through the metaphor of adultery. The *tzitzit* guard against this ultimate betrayal.

Beyond the intrinsic importance of each of these themes, they resonate with the broader content of *Kriyas Shema* itself. *Ol mitzvot* echoes the commitment to observe the *mitzvot* expressed in the first two *parshiyot*. The warnings against *minut* and *avodah zarah* echo *Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* — the proclamation of God's unity and the rejection of all that contradicts it. The inclusion of *Parshas Tzitzis* thus reinforces and expands the central themes of *Shema* while simultaneously fulfilling the independent *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*.

## Remember Exodus

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַזְכִּירִין יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם בַּלֵּילוֹת. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: הֲרֵי אֲנִי כְּבֶן שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, וְלֹא זָכִיתִי שֶׁתֵּאָמֵר יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם בַּלֵּילוֹת, עַד שֶׁדְּרָשָׁהּ בֶּן זוֹמָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It is a mitzva by Torah law to mention the exodus from Egypt at night, but some held that this mitzva was, like phylacteries or ritual fringes, fulfilled only during the day and not at night. For this reason it was decided: **The exodus from Egypt is mentioned at night,** adjacent to the recitation of *Shema*. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya said: I am approximately seventy years old, and** although I have long held this opinion, **I was never privileged** to prevail (*Me'iri*) and prove that there is a biblical obligation to fulfill the accepted custom (Ra'avad) and have **the exodus from Egypt mentioned at night, until Ben Zoma interpreted it homiletically** and proved it obligatory.

This discussion leads directly into the final *Mishnah* of the chapter. The *Mishnah* states: *mazkirin Yetzias Mitzrayim balaylot* — one mentions the exodus from Egypt at night. The context is *Kriyas Shema*: we know that *Shema* is recited both by day and by night, and that *Parshas Tzitzis* fulfills the separate *mitzvah* of remembering *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. The question the *Mishnah* addresses is whether there is an obligation to remember *Yetzias Mitzrayim* at night as well.

### Rabbi Elazar's Lament

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah — who was appointed *nasi* and miraculously appeared to age, such that he could say "I am like a seventy-year-old man" — declares that he never merited understanding how to derive the obligation of mentioning *Yetzias Mitzrayim* at night, until Ben Zoma came and explained it.

### Ben Zoma's Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לְמַעַן תִּזְכֹּר אֶת יוֹם צֵאתְךָ מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם כֹּל יְמֵי חַיֶּיךָ״. ״יְמֵי חַיֶּיךָ״ — הַיָּמִים, ״כֹּל יְמֵי חַיֶּיךָ״ — הַלֵּילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Ben Zoma derived it **as it is stated: "That you may remember the day you went out of the land of Egypt all the days of your life"** (Deuteronomy 16:3). **The days of your life,** refers to daytime alone; however, the addition of the word all, as it is stated: **All the days of your life,** comes to add nights as well.

Ben Zoma's proof rests on close reading of the verse *lema'an tizkor et yom tzeischa me'eretz Mitzrayim kol yemei chayyecha* — "so that you remember the day of your going out from Egypt all the days of your life." *Yemei chayyecha* would mean simply "the days of your life," i.e., daytime. But the word *kol* — "all" — extends the obligation to the fullness of each day, which includes the night. Thus the obligation to remember *Yetzias Mitzrayim* applies not only by day but by night as well.

### Sages' Counter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: ״יְמֵי חַיֶּיךָ״ — הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה. ״כֹּל״ — לְהָבִיא לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who posit that there is no biblical obligation to mention the exodus from Egypt at night, explain the word, all, differently and **say: The days of your life,** refers to the days in **this world, all** is added **to include the days of the Messiah.**

The Sages offer a different reading of the same *kol*. For them, *yemei chayyecha* refers to the days of this world, and *kol yemei chayyecha* — "all the days of your life" — means throughout this world, even into the future. The *kol* does not double the daily obligation into day and night; rather, it extends the temporal scope to ensure the obligation is not nullified even in the messianic era.

---

## Remember Exodus…

### Messianic Era

The Sages add: *lehavi limos haMashiach* — even in the days of the Messiah, when a greater redemption will have occurred, the memory of *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will not be set aside. The obligation persists across all time.

## Ben Zoma's Challenge

### Challenge Posed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶם בֶּן זוֹמָא לַחֲכָמִים: וְכִי מַזְכִּירִין יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם לִימוֹת הַמָּשִׁיחַ! וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר ״הִנֵּה יָמִים בָּאִים נְאֻם ה׳ וְלֹא יֹאמְרוּ עוֹד חַי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלָה אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם כִּי אִם חַי ה׳ אֲשֶׁר הֶעֱלָה וַאֲשֶׁר הֵבִיא אֶת זֶרַע בֵּית יִשְׂרָאֵל מֵאֶרֶץ צָפוֹנָה וּמִכֹּל הָאֲרָצוֹת אֲשֶׁר הִדַּחְתִּים שָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The fundamental dispute between Ben Zoma and the Sages appears in the mishna, and the *baraita* cites its continuation. Disputing the position of the Sages that: All the days of your life, refers to both this world and the days of the Messiah, **it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Ben Zoma said to the Sages: And is the exodus from Egypt mentioned in the days of the Messiah? Was it not already said** that Jeremiah prophesied that in the days of the Messiah: **"Behold, days are coming, says the Lord, that they will no longer say: The Lord lives Who brought up the children of Israel out of the Land of Egypt. Rather: As the Lord lives, that brought up and led the seed of the house of Israel up out of the north country and from all the countries where I had driven them"** (Jeremiah 23:7–8).

The *Baraita* reports that Ben Zoma posed a direct challenge to the Sages on this very point. Would one really continue to mention *Yetzias Mitzrayim* in the messianic era? He cites the prophet's words: *hinei yamim ba'im ne'um Hashem velo ye'amer od Chai Hashem asher he'elah et Benei Yisrael me'eretz Mitzrayim ki im Chai Hashem asher he'elah ve'asher hevi et zera Beit Yisrael me'eretz tzafonah umikol ha'aratzos asher hidachtim sham* — "Days are coming, says God, when they will no longer say 'by the life of God who brought the Children of Israel out of Egypt,' but rather 'by the life of God who brought up and led the descendants of the House of Israel from the northern land and from all the lands where He had driven them.'" The future redemption will eclipse *Yetzias Mitzrayim* as the primary frame of reference; oaths will be sworn by the greater act of redemption. Ben Zoma therefore asks: will *Yetzias Mitzrayim* even be mentioned?

### Sages' Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שֶׁתֵּעָקֵר יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם מִמְּקוֹמָהּ, אֶלָּא שֶׁתְּהֵא שִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכֻיוֹת עִיקָּר, וִיצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם טָפֵל לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages rejected this claim **and they said to him** that these verses do **not** mean **that** in the future **the exodus from Egypt will be uprooted from its place** and will be mentioned no more. **Rather,** redemption from **the subjugation of the kingdoms will be primary and the exodus from Egypt will be secondary.**

The Sages respond: *lo she'te'aker Yetzias Mitzrayim mimkomah* — this does not mean that *Yetzias Mitzrayim* loses its place entirely. Rather, *shibud malchuyot* — the redemption from all the nations — will become the *ikar*, the primary focus, and *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will become *tafel*, secondary, but still present and still remembered.

### Jacob Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּיּוֹצֵא בּוֹ אַתָּה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא יִקָּרֵא שִׁמְךָ עוֹד יַעֲקֹב כִּי אִם יִשְׂרָאֵל יִהְיֶה שְׁמֶךָ״,
>
> **Translation:** **On a similar** note, **you say:** The meaning of the expressions: It will not say, and they will no longer mention, are not absolute, as in the verse: **"Your name shall no longer be called Jacob; rather, Israel will be your name"** (Genesis 35:10). There, too, the meaning is

The Sages support this with the parallel of Jacob's name change: *lo yikarei shimcha od Yaakov ki im Yisrael yihyeh shemecha* — "your name shall no longer be called Jacob, but Israel shall be your name." Yet God Himself later calls him Jacob. This is not a contradiction; it means that the name Yisrael becomes the *ikar* while Yaakov becomes secondary — but both names remain his. So too with *Yetzias Mitzrayim*: it does not disappear; it is simply no longer primary.

The text that follows is found in the next daf (Berakhot 13a):

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא שֶׁיֵּעָקֵר "יַעֲקֹב" מִמְּקוֹמוֹ, אֶלָּא "יִשְׂרָאֵל" עִיקָּר וְ"יַעֲקֹב" טָפֵל לוֹ. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אַל תִּזְכְּרוּ רִאשֹׁנוֹת וְקַדְמֹנִיּוֹת אַל תִּתְבֹּנָנוּ״: אַל תִּזְכְּרוּ רִאשֹׁנוֹת״ — זֶה שִׁעְבּוּד מַלְכֻיוֹת, ״וְקַדְמֹנִיּוֹת אַל תִּתְבֹּנָנוּ״ — זוֹ יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **not that** the name **Jacob will be** entirely **uprooted from its place, but that** the name **Israel** will be the **primary** name **to which** the name **Jacob** will be **secondary,** as the Torah continues to refer to him as Jacob after this event. **And it also says** that the ultimate redemption will overshadow the previous redemption in the verse: **"Do not remember the former events, and do not ponder things of old"** (Isaiah 43:18), and the Gemara explains: **"Do not remember the former events," that is the subjugation to the kingdoms, and "do not ponder things of old," that is the exodus from Egypt,** which occurred before the subjugation to the nations.

### Prophet's Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִנְנִי עֹשֶׂה חֲדָשָׁה עַתָּה תִצְמָח״, תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: זוֹ מִלְחֶמֶת גּוֹג וּמָגוֹג. מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה — לְאָדָם שֶׁהָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּפָגַע בּוֹ זְאֵב וְנִיצַּל מִמֶּנּוּ, וְהָיָה מְסַפֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ מַעֲשֵׂה זְאֵב. פָּגַע בּוֹ אֲרִי וְנִיצַּל מִמֶּנּוּ, וְהָיָה מְסַפֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ מַעֲשֵׂה אֲרִי. פָּגַע בּוֹ נָחָשׁ וְנִיצַּל מִמֶּנּוּ, שָׁכַח מַעֲשֵׂה שְׁנֵיהֶם, וְהָיָה מְסַפֵּר וְהוֹלֵךְ מַעֲשֵׂה נָחָשׁ. אַף כָּךְ יִשְׂרָאֵל — צָרוֹת אַחֲרוֹנוֹת מְשַׁכְּחוֹת אֶת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the following verse: **"Behold, I will do new things, now it will spring forth"** (Isaiah 43:19), **Rav Yosef taught** a *baraita*: **This refers to the** future **war of Gog and Magog,** which will cause all earlier events to be forgotten. The Gemara cites **a parable: To what is this comparable? To a person who was walking along the way and a wolf accosted him and he survived it, and he continued to relate the story of the wolf. A lion accosted him and he survived it, and he continued to relate the story of the lion. A snake accosted him and he survived it, he forgot both** the lion and the wolf, **and he continued to relate the story of the snake.** Each encounter was more dangerous and each escape more miraculous than the last, so he would continue to relate the most recent story. **So too** with **Israel; more recent troubles cause the earlier** troubles **to be forgotten.**

Further prophetic support is adduced from the verse: *al tizkeru rishonot veKadmoniyot al titbonanu* — "do not remember the former things and do not contemplate the earlier ones." The Gemara interprets: *rishonot* refers to *shibud malchuyot* — the servitude under the nations; *kadmoniyot* refers to *Yetzias Mitzrayim*. Both will still be remembered, but neither will dominate the consciousness of the redeemed — the new salvation, *hineni oseh chadashah*, will be primary. The school of Rav Yosef identifies this new thing as the war of *Gog u'Magog*.

### Parable of Dangers

A parable is offered: a man was walking and encountered a wolf, from which he was saved. He told everyone about the wolf. Then he encountered a lion and was saved; he told everyone about the lion. Then he encountered a snake and was saved — and he forgot both the wolf and the lion, speaking only of the snake. So too with Israel: *tzaros achronos meshakechos es harishonos* — the later afflictions cause the earlier ones to recede in memory, because the most recent danger seems greatest.

It must be acknowledged that as a parable, this seems to cut somewhat against the Sages' conclusion rather than supporting it — for the man in the story actually *forgot* the earlier dangers, which would seem to support Ben Zoma's view that *Yetzias Mitzrayim* will no longer be mentioned. One reading is that the Gemara's point may be precisely the inverse: because human nature is to focus only on the most recent and most acute experience, the obligation is *davka* to continue remembering the earlier ones. The *mitzvah* exists precisely because the inclination is to forget. The Gemara, however, does not explicitly close this circle, and the tension remains.

## Name Changes

### Avram to Avraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אַבְרָם הוּא אַבְרָהָם״: בַּתְּחִלָּה נַעֲשָׂה אָב לַאֲרָם, וּלְבַסּוֹף נַעֲשָׂה אָב לְכָל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the changing of Jacob's name, the Gemara addresses the changing of the names of Abraham and Sarah. What is the meaning of changing Abram's name to Abraham? As it is stated: **"Abram is Abraham"** (I Chronicles 1:27). The Gemara explains: **Initially he became a father,** a minister, and prominent person, only **to Aram,** so he was called Abram, father [av] of Aram, **and ultimately** with God's blessing **he became the father of the entire world,** so he was called Abraham, father of the masses [*av hamon*], as it is stated: "I have made you the father of a multitude of nations" (Genesis 17:5).

The Gemara now addresses the question of name changes as such, beginning with Avraham. The verse in *Divrei HaYamim* states: *Avram hu Avraham* — Avram is Avraham. The Gemara explains: initially he was made a father — *av* — to Aram alone, to the people of his region, *Aram Naharayim*. In the end he was made a father to the entire world — *av hamon goyim*, the father of many nations. The letter *heh* added to his name captures this expansion of scope.

### Sarai to Sarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״שָׂרַי הִיא שָׂרָה״. בַּתְּחִלָּה נַעֲשֵׂית שָׂרַי לְאוּמָּתָהּ, וּלְבַסּוֹף נַעֲשֵׂית שָׂרָה לְכָל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, what is the meaning of changing Sarai's name to Sarah? The same concept applies to Sarai as to Abram: **Sarai is Sarah."** The Gemara explains: **Initially she was a princess** only **to her nation:** My princess [*Sarai*], **but ultimately she became Sarah,** a general term indicating that she was princess **for the entire world.**

Similarly, *Sarai hi Sarah* — Sarai is Sarah. Initially she was *sarai* to her own nation alone — the possessive form, "my princess." In the end she became *Sarah* without the possessive, a princess to all the world.

### Bar Kappara Positive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי בַּר קַפָּרָא: כָּל הַקּוֹרֵא לְאַבְרָהָם ״אַבְרָם״ — עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה שִׁמְךָ אַבְרָהָם״. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: עוֹבֵר בְּלָאו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא יִקָּרֵא עוֹד [אֶת] שִׁמְךָ אַבְרָם״.
>
> **Translation:** Also, with regard to Abraham's name, **bar Kappara taught: Anyone who calls Abraham Abram transgresses a positive mitzva, as it is stated: "And your name will be Abraham"** (Genesis 17:5). This is a positive mitzva to refer to him as Abraham. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** One who calls Abraham Abram **transgresses a negative mitzva, as it is stated: "And your name shall no longer be called Abram,** and your name will be Abraham, for I have made you the father of a multitude of nations" (Genesis 17:5).

Bar Kappara rules: anyone who calls Avraham by the name Avram transgresses a positive commandment, as the verse states: *v'hayah shimcha Avraham* — "your name shall be Avraham." The declaration of the name change carries the force of a *mitzvat asei*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Negative

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: it is not only a positive commandment but a negative prohibition. The basis is *v'lo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — "your name shall no longer be called Avram." This is not merely a statement of fact but an active prohibition.

### Sarah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הַקּוֹרֵא לְשָׂרָה ״שָׂרַי״ הָכִי נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if we consider these obligatory statements, then **from here** we must infer that **one who calls Sarah Sarai also** transgresses a positive or negative mitzva.

The Gemara asks: if this is so regarding Avraham, should the same not apply to Sarah — that calling her by the old name Sarai would also be forbidden?

### Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא אָמַר לְאַבְרָהָם: ״שָׂרַי אִשְׁתְּךָ לֹא תִקְרָא אֶת שְׁמָהּ שָׂרָי כִּי שָׂרָה שְׁמָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There** in the case of Sarah, it is not a general mitzva, rather **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Abraham** alone: "And God said to Abraham, **your wife Sarai, you shall not call her name Sarai; rather, Sarah is her name"** (Genesis 17:15). In contrast, this is stated regarding Abraham in general terms: "Your name shall no longer be called Abram."

The Gemara draws a distinction. In the case of Avraham's name, God's command was given to Avraham in general terms, applicable to all: *v'hayah shimcha Avraham* and *v'lo yikarei od shimcha Avram* — universal declarations. But regarding Sarah's name, the verse reads: *Sarai ishtecha lo tikra et shemah Sarai ki Sarah shemah* — "Sarai your wife, you shall not call her name Sarai, for Sarah is her name." The address is in the second person singular — *lo tikra*, *you* shall not call her — directed specifically to Avraham, not to the world at large. There is therefore no general prohibition for others to call her Sarai.

### Chronicles Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה הַקּוֹרֵא לְיַעֲקֹב ״יַעֲקֹב״ הָכִי נָמֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** Again, the Gemara asks: **But if that is so, one who calls Jacob Jacob,** about whom it is written: "Your name shall no longer be Jacob, but Israel" (Genesis 32:29), **also** transgresses a mitzva.

The Gemara anticipates an objection: the verse in *Divrei HaYamim* itself uses the name Avram — *asher bacharta be'Avram* — "who chose Avram." Does this not indicate that calling him Avram is permissible?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דַּהֲדַר אַהְדְּרֵיהּ קְרָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֱלֹהִים לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמַרְאוֹת הַלַּיְלָה וַיֹּאמֶר יַעֲקֹב יַעֲקֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different there, as the verse reverts back** and God Himself refers to Jacob as Jacob, **as it is written** before his descent to Egypt: **"And God said to Israel in the visions of the night, and said, Jacob, Jacob,** and he said, 'Here I am'" (Genesis 46:2).

The Gemara answers: there the *navi* is recounting the past — he is describing the moment of God's original choice, when the patriarch's name was indeed Avram, and declaring that God chose him then and subsequently gave him the name Avraham. The prophet is narrating what *was*, not referring to the patriarch by his old name as a present designation. The question of precisely when that line is crossed — whether a teacher explaining *Parshas Lech Lecha* may say "when God spoke to Avram" or must say "when God spoke to Avraham" — is left somewhat ambiguous by the Gemara. Most would naturally say Avraham in any case. Whether one would transgress a *lav* or an *asei* by saying Avram in such a context, and whether these are truly biblical prohibitions or function as *asmachta*, remains an open question that the sugya raises without fully resolving.
