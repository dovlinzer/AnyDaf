# Bava Kamma 93 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Popular Sayings

### Intro to Sayings

The daf we take up begins partway through 92b, where Rava poses a series of questions to Rabba bar Mari: *mina ha-milsa de-amri rabbanan* — where is the source, in Torah or in rabbinic literature, for common popular sayings? The structure of the passage is consistent: Rava cites a folk saying, Rabba bar Mari identifies its biblical root. What follows is a rich collection of popular wisdom, each tethered to a scriptural verse.

### Saddle & Donkey

The first saying: *chavrach karcha chamra, uchva legabach mosh* — if your friend calls you a donkey, go ahead and put the saddle on your own back. The meaning is that rather than fighting every insult or unflattering characterization, one might simply accept it and move on. The source is the verse in which the angel addresses Hagar: *Hagar shifchat Sarai, ay mi-zeh bat ve-ana telechi* — "Hagar, maidservant of Sarah, where do you come from and where are you going?"

### Hagar's Shame

The angel's address to Hagar as *shifchat Sarai* — the maidservant of Sarah — is notable because by that point Hagar had been elevated to the role of concubine of Avraham. She might have objected to being called Sarah's maidservant. Yet she did not fight it. She accepted the description rather than pressing for a more exalted title. That acceptance is the biblical illustration of the popular saying: tolerate the unflattering label rather than make life harder by contesting it.

### Abraham's Servant

The second saying: *milsa gina'a de-is bach, kadem amra* — if there is something unseemly about you, be the first to disclose it rather than waiting for others to discover it. The source is *Vayomer eved Avraham anochi* — "And he said: I am the servant of Avraham." When Avraham's servant traveled to find a wife for Yitzchak, he identified himself immediately as a slave rather than allowing his interlocutors to discover it on their own.

### Duck's Eyes

The third saying: *shafil ve-azil bar avza, ve-einei mitayfe* — the duck walks with its eyes downcast, yet its eyes gaze far into the distance. Even one of lowly station may harbor high aspirations. The verse cited is from the story of Avigail, who while still the wife of Naval said to David: *ve-heitiv Hashem la-adoni... ve-zacharta es amasecha* — may God do good for my master, and remember your maidservant. Avigail, still formally bound in her station, nonetheless looked forward to what might yet come to be. Whether the saying is meant descriptively — that people of humble status naturally have high aspirations — or prescriptively — that they *should* — is left somewhat open.

### Abigail & David

The Avigail verse thus furnishes the biblical grounding for the duck proverb. She was subordinate in her present circumstances, yet her eyes, so to speak, looked far into the distance — toward a future in which she might be remembered by someone far greater.

### Wine & Steward

The next saying: *chamra le-marei, tivuta le-shakya* — the wine belongs to the owner, but the gratitude goes to the waiter, the one who pours. The source is the passage in which Moshe is commanded to place his hand upon Yehoshua: *ve-samachta es yadcha alav, le-maan yishmeu ve-yiru kol adat Bnei Yisrael*. What follows is that Yehoshua was filled with the spirit of wisdom *ki samach Moshe es yadav alav* — because Moshe placed his hands upon him. The verse attributes the result to Moshe's act, even though the divine spirit ultimately comes from God. Moshe was merely the vehicle, the *kli* through which it passed — yet the verse credits him.

### Moses & Joshua

Tosafot offers a different version of this same point. According to Tosafot, God tells Moshe to place his *hand* (singular) upon Yehoshua, yet Moshe places his *hands* (plural). The one who does the pouring can decide how much to give. The application: this is why one should be good to one's waiter. The underlying theological observation is that even the intermediary who merely conveys a gift receives — and perhaps merits — the gratitude for it.

### Honey & Hunger

The next saying: *nefesh seva tavus nofet, ve-nefesh re'eiva kol mar matok* — a satisfied soul refuses honey, and a hungry soul finds anything bitter sweet. This is taken directly from the verse in Mishlei. The observation is more descriptive than prescriptive: it captures the reality of human appetite and perception. Not every saying in this collection carries profound moral instruction; the larger point of the entire series is that all human wisdom, even folk wisdom, finds its root in Torah.

### Bad Tree Parable

The next saying: *metayel ve-azal dikla bisha gabei kina de-sarchi* — a bad tree moves toward a barren field of barren trees. Evil attracts evil; those of bad character seek out others of their kind. This is distinguished from the common proverb that a bad apple spoils the bunch. Here the motion is deliberate — the bad one *goes toward* those of similar character.

Rabba bar Mari responds that this saying is found in not one but multiple strata of tradition: *davar ze kasuv ba-Torah, shanuy ba-Nevi'im, u-meshulash ba-Kesuvim* — it is written in the Torah, repeated in the Prophets, and stated a third time in the Writings. It is also found in a *mishnah* and in a *baraita*.

### Esau & Ishmael

The Torah source is *va-yelech Esav el Yishmael* — Esav went to Yishmael. The bad tree sought the barren tree. The Maharsha offers a particularly elegant reading of the two-part metaphor: a *bad tree* is one that comes from good stock but produces bad fruit, while a *barren tree* is one that comes from bad stock to begin with. Esav descended from Yitzchak and Rivka — good stock — yet he produced bad fruit. He went to Yishmael, whose mother was Hagar, representing bad stock from the outset. The pairing thus reflects not only character but lineage.

### Jiftach Story

The Prophets-source is *va-yitlaketsu el Yiftach anashim reikim* — worthless men gathered around Yiftach. And indeed, Yiftach was himself the son of a promiscuous woman. The Maharsha's reading applies here as well: low company gravitates toward one who shares that origin. The Kesuvim-source is the verse *kol of le-mino yishkon, u-vnei adam le-domeh lo* — every bird dwells with its kind, and people with those similar to them. Notably, this verse is from *Ben Sira*, not from the canonical *Ketuvim*. The Gemara nonetheless introduces it with the formulation *de-chsiv*, as if quoting Scripture, and frames it as *meshulash ba-Kesuvim*. The Gemara is clearly familiar with Ben Sira and does not hesitate to cite it, even while the *mishnah* in Sanhedrin famously cautions against reading *sefarim chitzoniyim*.

### Connection & Purity

The *mishnah*-source: *kol ha-mechubar le-tamei tamei, kol ha-mechubar le-tahor tahor* — anything connected to something impure is impure; anything connected to something pure is pure. The illustration: a metal nail is *mekabel tum'ah*, susceptible to ritual impurity. If one hammers that nail into a wooden implement that lacks a *beit kibbul* — a receptacle — and is therefore not itself *mekabel tum'ah*, the nail loses its susceptibility and becomes *tahor*, because it is now *mechubar le-tahor*. Conversely, a wooden nail hammered into a metal utensil that is *mekabel tum'ah* becomes *tamei* by virtue of its connection. This *mishnah* captures the same structural principle as the proverb, though the point about *lineage* that the Maharsha highlighted does not map onto the *tum'ah* case in the same way.

### Starling Species

The *baraita*-source: Rabbi Eliezer states, *lo le-hinam halakh zarzir etzel orev* — it is not for nothing that the starling goes to the raven. There was a question about whether the *zarzir* is a kosher bird. Rabbi Eliezer holds it is not, reasoning that it flocks with the raven — *mipnei she-hu mino* — because they are of the same species. Bad attracts bad; the bird's companionship is a diagnostic of its own nature.

### Unheeding Friend

The next saying: *kari leih le-havrach ve-la anach, remi guda raba shady beih* — if you called out to your friend and he did not respond, take a large wall and throw it on him. The context is not casual greeting but warning — you tried to warn someone and they ignored you. The response: to hell with him. The verse: *yaan tihara-tikha ve-lo taharta, mi-tumatecha lo tithari od* — since I tried to purify you and you would not become pure, you will no longer become pure. The verse is not exactly the same: God withdraws rather than actively retaliating. Still, the resonance is clear: once warning is refused, the one who warned withdraws all further concern.

### Well Respect

The next saying: *beira de-shatit mineih, la tishdei beih kala* — the well from which you drank, do not throw clods of earth into it. That which benefited you, even if it is now dried up or no longer useful, deserves a measure of respect. Do not go back and denigrate it.

### Edom & Egypt

The biblical source: *lo tetaev Adomi ki achikha hu, ve-lo tetaev Mitzri ki ger hayita be-artzo* — do not despise the Edomite for he is your brother, and do not despise the Egyptian, for you were a sojourner in his land. Even given the subsequent hostility of Edom, who refused passage through his land, and certainly despite the enslavement by Egypt, the Torah demands a measure of *hakarat ha-tov* — gratitude for past benefit. The facts may have changed, but the prior benefit is not erased.

### Shared Burden

The next saying: *i dali padli dalina, ve-i lo lo dalina* — if you carry the burden I will carry it with you, but if you will not, I will not carry it alone. The verse: Barak said to Devorah, *im telkhi imi ve-halakhti, ve-im lo telkhi imi lo elekh* — if you go with me I will go; if you will not go with me I will not go.

### Barak & Deborah

The verse from Shoftim captures precisely this conditionality: willingness to act is contingent on the other party's participation. I will do it if you are with me; I will not do it alone.

### Youth & Age

The next saying: *kad havina zutrei le-gavrei* — when we were young we thought ourselves like real adults, *ve-hashta de-kashishna le-dardekei* — and now that we have grown old we are like children again, because the elderly become infirm and dependent on others. We begin thinking we are adults and end up back as children.

### Divine Guidance

The verse-source comes in two parts. Initially, *ve-Adonai holekh lifneihem yomam be-amud anan le-nahotam ha-derekh, u-ve-layla be-amud esh le-hair lahem* — God led them with a pillar of cloud and a pillar of fire. This formulation suggests empowerment: God shows the way, but the people themselves are moving forward, fully capable. Later, *hineh anochi sholeach malach lefanecha lishmorcha ba-derekh* — I will send my angel to guard you on the way. This second formulation is more protective, more caretaking — more, one might say, infantilizing. The trajectory from pillar of cloud to guardian angel mirrors the trajectory from feeling like adults in youth to needing to be watched over in old age.

### Wealthy Patron

At the top of 93a, the final saying: *batar marei nichsei tzevi mashach* — after the man of wealth, the wood chips follow. Hang around the wealthy and you benefit from being in their orbit. This is contrasted with the inverse principle, *batar anya azal anyuta* — poverty follows the poor.

### Lot & Abraham

The source: *ve-gam le-Lot ha-holech et Avram hayah tzon u-vakar ve-ohalim* — and also Lot, who went with Avram, had flocks and herds and tents. Lot's prosperity was a direct consequence of accompanying Avraham. The wealth accrued by proximity.

## Divine Judgment

### Moser Din

The Gemara now shifts from popular sayings to several related teachings about Avraham and Sarah and connected themes. Rav Chanan states: *ha-moser din al chaveiro, hu ne'enash techila* — one who gives over to Heaven the judgment against another person — who says, in effect, let God come and judge between us — will himself be the first to be punished. When you invoke God's judgment, God's attention turns first to you, and any wrongdoing on your part will be reckoned before anything else.

### Sarah's Curse

The proof is from the verse: *vatomer Sarai el Avram, chamasi alecha, yishpot Hashem beini u-veinecha* — Sarah said to Avraham, my injury is upon you; let God judge between us. The critical clause is the last: she called upon God to adjudicate. And indeed, Sarah died before Avraham: *vayavo Avraham lispod le-Sarah ve-livkota*. The punishment was not immediate, but it came. Once you bring God into the matter, God's reckoning with you will come first.

### Lower Court

An important qualification follows: *ve-hanei milei de-it lei dina be-ar'a* — this principle applies only where the person had other recourse. If one could have brought the matter to a *beit din* and chose instead to invoke divine judgment, then one has made a deliberate choice to bypass available legal process and must bear the consequence. Where there is no available *beit din*, the calculus presumably differs. (The Maharsha notes that there was always the *beit din* of Shem — but that is not without its difficulties.)

### Cry Out First

Rabbi Yitzchak adds a related formulation: *oi lo la-tzo'ek yoter min ha-nitza'ak* — woe to the one who cries out more than the one being cried about. The one who brings a complaint to God should worry more about himself than the one against whom the complaint is directed.

### Both Suffer

A *baraita* teaches similarly: *echad ha-tzo'ek ve-echad ha-nitza'ak be-mashma* — both the one who cries out and the one being cried about are included. The verse supporting this — *ve-charah api ve-haragti etchem be-charev* — speaks of God's anger being kindled not only against the oppressor but in a broader sense: punishment falls on both parties. Moreover, *she-memaharim la-tzo'ek yoter min ha-nitza'ak* — the one who cried out will be punished first. This is a striking Gemara. The intuitive religious response to oppression is to cry out to God; yet the Gemara suggests that the act of crying out itself subjects the crier to prior scrutiny. One distinction worth noting: *tzo'ek* — simply crying out — is not the same as *moser din*, which is the more deliberate act of formally invoking divine judgment. Even *Bnei Yisrael* in Egypt did not pray; *va-yiz'aku*, they simply cried out from their suffering. Yet even that cry, the Gemara suggests, draws divine attention to the crier.

### Tosafot Version

Tosafot cites a different version of this teaching from the *Sifrei*, in the name of Rabbeinu Shmuel: *echad ha-tzo'ek ve-echad she-ein tzo'ek be-mashma* — whether one cries out or whether one does not cry out, God punishes the oppressor. The question then becomes: what is the purpose of crying out at all? The *Sifrei's* resolution is that God punishes the oppressor regardless, but if the victim cries out, God punishes the oppressor faster: *she-memaharim li-pro'a yoter mi-mi she-eino tzo'ek*. This reading is far more consistent with the *peshat* of the *psukim*, which present God as responsive to the cry of the oppressed. Under the *Sifrei's* version, crying out accelerates divine response against the oppressor; it does not expose the crier to prior punishment. This is clearly a different orientation than the Gemara's own reading, which seems to caution against calling God to intervene at all — since doing so will bring God's scrutiny upon you first. As counterintuitive as that is, the Gemara's formulation stands.

### Commoner's Curse

Rabbi Yitzchak continues: *le-olam al tehi klalas hedyot kala be-einecha* — never regard lightly the curse of even a commoner. This stands in some tension with the foregoing: rather than calling God to adjudicate, one might simply curse the wrongdoer — and that curse may prove surprisingly potent. The implication is almost ironic: if you want divine response, a direct curse may be more effective than a formal *moser din*.

### Abimelech's Curse

The demonstration: Avimelech cursed Sarah. The verse is *hinei hu lach ksus einayim* — here is money; it should be a covering of the eyes. The plain meaning is that the money serves as a kind of veil, shielding Sarah from embarrassment. But the Gemara reads it differently: Avimelech, though externally giving something, was in effect saying — since you concealed from me that this was your husband and caused me all this anguish, *yehi ratzon she-yiyu lach bnei kisuyei einayim* — may your children have their eyes covered. It is a curse dressed in the language of a gift.

### Isaac's Blindness

And the curse was fulfilled: *vayehi ki zakein Yitzchak va-tich'hein einav mei-rot* — when Yitzchak grew old his eyes became dim. The curse of the commoner — even of someone who, in the larger narrative, was not entirely blameless — took hold in his descendants.

### Be Oppressed

Rabbi Abbahu teaches: *le-olam yehei adam min ha-nirdafim ve-lo min ha-rodphim* — a person should always be among the pursued and not among the pursuers. It is better to be the one chased than the one doing the chasing.

### Doves & Pigeons

The proof: *she-ein lach nirdaf ba-ofot yoter mi-turim u-vnei yonah* — there is no bird more persistently prey than the turtledove and the pigeon. And those are precisely the ones *hichshiram ha-katuv le-gabei mizbeiach* — that Scripture validated as sacrificial offerings. The predators are not the ones brought to the altar. One could generalize and observe that among kosher animals broadly, the predators are excluded — but the Gemara here focuses specifically on the birds, where the contrast is most vivid.

## Mechila Doctrine

### Blind vs. Tear

With that *aggadata* digression concluded, the Gemara returns to the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* distinguishes two cases. One who says to another, "blind my eye" — even if he adds "on the condition that you will be exempt," the one who blinds him is nonetheless liable. But one who says "tear my garment" — if he adds "on the condition that you will be exempt," the one who tears it is indeed exempt.

### Assi's Question

Rav Assi bar Chama poses the question to Rava: *mai shna reisha u-mai shna seifa* — what is different about the first case and what is different about the second? Why does a waiver of exemption work for a garment but not for bodily injury?

### Forfeit Principle

Rava responds: *reisha lefi she-ein adam mochel al rashei evarim* — a person does not grant *mechila* on the loss of a limb. This statement can be read two ways: either descriptively — a person never truly means it when he purports to waive such injury — or normatively — a person *cannot* meaningfully waive it, because an act of bodily injury is objectively an act of injury regardless of prior consent. The latter reading has significant implications. It is important to note that this does not preclude *mechila* after the injury occurs. Once the harm has been done and a debt of damages has accrued, that is simply a monetary obligation and can be forgiven like any other. The question here is specifically about a *mechila* in advance — a statement that reframes the anticipated act so that it is not treated as harmful at all. That reframing, the Gemara suggests, cannot work for bodily injury the way it can for damage to an object.

### Waive Beforehand

The questioner presses further: if the problem is waiving a debt before it exists, or reframing an act as non-injurious in advance, then how does this work even for lesser injuries like pain alone? A *baraita* is cited: *ha-kakeini u-f'tzeni, al menat liftor* — "hit me, wound me, on the condition that you will be exempt" — and the one who does so is *patur*, exempt. Even though there is no permanent injury, there is still pain — *tza'ar* — and yet the prior consent exempts the actor. Why should that be possible if a person cannot truly reframe injurious acts in advance? The questioner had no answer — *ishteiki* — he was silent.

### Family Defect

When asked whether he had heard any resolution, Rava transmitted the answer of Rav Sheshes: *mishum pgam mishpacha* — because of the family stigma. A permanent injury to a person's body carries with it a lasting blemish felt by the entire family. The family walks around bearing witness to what happened. That objective social reality — the *pgam mishpacha* — cannot be undone by the individual's subjective consent. It is not that the family is suing for their embarrassment; rather, the family's enduring stigma is evidence that the injury is objectively real and permanent, regardless of what the individual declared in advance. Because of that objective reality, the prior *mechila* is ineffective.

### Tosafot Objective

Tosafot sharpens this analysis: *mishum pgam mishpacha u-meshalem ha-kol afilu tza'ar* — once there is *pgam mishpacha*, the tortfeasor must pay everything, including the *tza'ar*. One cannot say: I am *mochel* on the *tza'ar* even if I cannot waive the permanent injury. The *pgam mishpacha* functions as a kind of litmus test: once objective, lasting injury is established, the entire *mechila* is void. Nothing can be reframed. But where the harm is purely transient — affecting only the individual in the moment, leaving no permanent mark — then perhaps the subjective reframing does hold, and a prior *mechila* on *tza'ar* is valid.

The discussion touches on what exactly the *pgam* signifies. It is not embarrassment over an event — that is *boshet*. *Pgam* is a lasting stigma, something that remains. And a brief wound, once healed, carries no such lasting stigma. It is the permanence that makes the injury objectively real in a way that cannot be wished away. One might observe that the family's reaction is evoking a social sensibility — perhaps suggesting that no sensible person could truly want this done to them, and so the prior consent cannot be sincere. But the analysis runs deeper than that: the *pgam mishpacha* establishes that there is an objective injury here that exists independently of the individual's subjective framing.

### Yes Means No

Rabbi Yochanan offers a third explanation: *yesh hen she-hu ke-lav ve-yesh lav she-hu ke-hen* — sometimes a yes means no and sometimes a no means yes. This explanation operates differently from those of Rava and Rav Sheshes. Rather than saying that *mechila* on bodily injury is intrinsically impossible or objectively ineffective, Rabbi Yochanan says that we interpret the consent based on contextual assumptions about what people ordinarily mean.

### Inflection Test

Tosafot elaborates on Rabbi Yochanan's position with considerable care, noting that the straightforward reading of Rashi is *dochak* — forced. Tosafot explains: when the *mishnah* posits the case of *al menat liftor*, the scenario is that someone says "blind me" and the other responds "al menat liftor?" — on the condition that I'll be exempt? — and the first person says "yes." If the "yes" is clearly sarcastic, we take it as a no. If it is clearly sincere, we take it as a yes. The *mishnah* is addressing the ambiguous case — where the inflection does not make it plain. In the case of bodily injury, the default assumption is that even an apparently sincere "yes" should be interpreted as a "no" — because we presume that no person truly means it when consenting to the loss of a limb. Conversely, if someone says "tear my garment, al menat liftor?" and the response is "no" — even that no, if ambiguous, will be interpreted as a yes, because we presume that of course the person meant to exempt the other; why else would he ask him to do it? The rule is not absolute. If the inflection makes the meaning unmistakable, we follow the actual meaning. But where there is doubt, we apply the presumption: in bodily injury cases, resolve toward "no" — no true consent was given; in property cases, resolve toward "yes" — the prior exemption holds.

The *baraita* confirms this structure: *ha-kakeini u-f'tzeni, al menat liftor, ve-amar lo hen* — hit me, wound me, on the condition you'll be exempt, and he says yes — *yesh hen she-hu ke-lav*. *Kera k'susi, al menat liftor, ve-amar lo lav* — tear my garment, on condition you'll be exempt, and he says no — *yesh lav she-hu ke-hen*.

It is worth noting what distinguishes Rabbi Yochanan's position from the earlier two. Rava and Rav Sheshes are saying that whatever one tries to do, the objective reality of bodily injury cannot be reframed. Rabbi Yochanan says: it is not that reframing is always impossible, but that we interpret statements within a general framework of what people normally mean — and that framework tilts differently in the two categories of case.

### Charity Wallet

A difficulty is raised from a *baraita* about a watchman: *lishmor ve-lo le-abed* — one takes an object to watch it, not to destroy it. If given an object to destroy, or to distribute to the poor, the watchman is not obligated to watch it — the implication being that the owner's permission to destroy is substantive and effective without any explicit *al menat liftor*. How does this square with the *mishnah's* requirement of explicit exemption in the case of garments?

### Trustee Liability

Rav Yosef's case bears on this question. A wallet of *tzedaka* funds came to Pumbedita. Rav Yosef entrusted it to a certain person for distribution. The trustee was negligent — *pasha bah* — and thieves came and stole it. Rav Yosef ruled the trustee liable. Abaye objected: does not the *baraita* say that one given an object to distribute to the poor is not obligated to watch it — *lishmor ve-lo le-chalek la-aniyim*? If the money was given over to *tzedaka*, it is no longer the donor's property, and no one can bring the trustee to a *din torah*. Rav Yosef replied: *aniyei de-Pumbedita mikatz kaitzi lehu, u-lishmor hu* — the poor of Pumbedita have fixed allocations. Each pauper is entitled to a defined daily amount, like a welfare distribution. Therefore, each recipient effectively has a specified claim, and can individually sue for the failure to deliver their portion. Since the allocations were fixed, it is treated as if the trustee was holding each person's specific money — and therefore his obligation to watch it remains.

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction between the *baraita* and the *mishnah* by distinguishing the circumstances under which the object came into the person's possession. Where the object arrived in his hands in the capacity of something to be watched — *be-torat shemira* — and the owner later says to go ahead and destroy it, then since he already assumed a watchman's responsibility, the owner must be more explicit in granting exemption and must say *al menat liftor*. But where the object was handed over from the outset with the understanding that it could be destroyed — *be-torat kri'a* — then no such explicit exemption is needed, because no protective responsibility was ever assumed. Tosafot adds a further nuance: even the statement "you can destroy it" may not be fully sincere. It may be said in order to relieve the watchman of anxiety — "don't worry if something happens to it" — without genuinely meaning to authorize destruction and forfeit all compensation. Under that reading, the explicit *al menat liftor* is needed precisely to cut through any such ambiguity.

## Shinui Koneh

### Wood to Vessel

With *Perek Ha-Chovel* concluded, the Gemara moves to the *perakim* dealing with *gezeila*. The opening *mishnah* of the new *perek*: *ha-gozel eitzim ve-asa'an keilim* — one who steals wood and fashions it into a utensil. The principle of *shinui koneh* — that a change in the object confers ownership on the thief — means that the owner cannot simply reclaim the new utensil. Rather, the *gazlan* now owns it and returns only the value of the stolen material as it was at the time of the theft: *meshalem ki-sha'at ha-gezeila*.

### Wool to Garment

The same applies to wool fashioned into garments. In each case, the transformation is significant enough that the object has become something new, and the original owner is compensated in money rather than receiving the transformed object back.

### Pregnant Cow

The *mishnah* then addresses more complex cases. One who steals a pregnant cow and it gives birth, or a wool-bearing sheep and shears it: *meshalem dmei parah ha-omedet le-yaled u-dmei rachel ha-omedet le-gazez* — he pays the value of a cow about to give birth and a sheep about to be shorn. The calf and the wool, acquired through *shinui*, belong to the *gazlan*. He returns the cow and the sheep and pays the differential — the premium in value attributable to the imminent birth and the accumulated wool.

### Full Cycle

If the thief goes through the full cycle — he stole the cow, it became pregnant in his possession and then gave birth; he stole the sheep, it grew wool in his possession and he sheared it — in that case as well, *meshalem ki-sha'at ha-gezeila*. He keeps the calf and the wool. He gives back the animal and pays its value as it was at the time of theft. The *gazlan* therefore profits from the natural productivity of the stolen animal, which seems inequitable, yet this is the consequence of *shinui koneh*.

### Payment Rule

The general rule: *zeh ha-klal, kol ha-gazlanim meshalmin ki-sha'at ha-gezeila* — all thieves pay according to the value at the time of theft. The *mishnah* does not address the inverse case — where the thief stole a shorn sheep that then grew wool, and now wishes to return the animal and recover the increased value. In that scenario there is no *shinui*, so the thief would not be *koneh* and cannot claim the increase; he simply returns the improved animal.

### Earlier Stages

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraita* that speaks of earlier stages of transformation: *gazal eitzim ve-shiphan* — he stole wood and planed it; *avanim ve-sitatan* — stones and chiseled them; *tzemer ve-libnan* — wool and whitened it; *pishtan ve-nika'an* — flax and cleaned it. In these cases as well, *meshalem ki-sha'at ha-gezeilah*. But if the *mishnah* requires the fuller transformation — making actual utensils or garments — why does the *baraita* permit ownership from these earlier, less complete stages of change?

### Reversible Change

Abaye resolves the contradiction by inverting the apparent hierarchy: *tana didan katani shinuy de-rabbanan de-hadra* — our *mishnah* is actually describing a more minor, rabbinic-level *shinui*, one that is reversible — *shinui she-hozer*. The *baraita*, by contrast, describes an irreversible, biblical-level *shinui*. The *mishnah's* case of "wood into utensils" refers to stolen planks — *nesarim* — already smoothed, that the thief simply assembled in a structure without nails, so that they could be dismantled. The *mishnah's* case of "wool into garments" refers to stolen yarn — wool already spun — that was then knitted, and could in principle be unknitted and returned to yarn. These are *shinuyim* that could revert; they confer ownership only rabbinically. The *baraita*, which speaks of planing, chiseling, and whitening, describes physical alterations that are permanent and irreversible — a higher, biblical category of *shinui* — and the *mishnah* does not need to state what is true *a fortiori* from that level.

### Biblical Change

Rav Ashi disagrees with Abaye's re-reading and maintains that even the *mishnah's* cases are biblical-level *shinuyim*. *Tanna didan nami shinuy d'Oraita katani* — our *mishnah* also describes a biblical change. When the *mishnah* says "wood into utensils," it means the same scenario as the *baraita* — planed wood used as a simple pounder or pestle, *bukhni* — which is the same as *shiphan*, planing. When the *mishnah* says "wool into garments," it refers to felt — *namti* — which is produced without the intermediate stages of spinning and weaving and represents an irreversible change. The two sources thus describe the same category.

### Identity Change

Tosafot enters with a critical limitation on the concept of *shinui* as it emerges from both Abaye and Rav Ashi. The mere fact that a physical change cannot be reversed is not sufficient to constitute a *shinui* that confers ownership. The *shinui* must alter the identity of the object — its essential character and name must change. Planing or smoothing, for example, is effective only if the result is something that now has a different identity: *she-na'aseh be-kach bukhni* — it was made thereby into a pestle. It is then a new kind of object, not simply a smoother version of the original.

### Smoothing Test

If one took a rough piece of wood and merely sanded it smooth — *shifuy she-ein mishtaneh shmo be-kach* — without producing an object of a different character, that would not constitute a valid *shinui*. Before: a rough beam. After: a smooth beam. The name has not changed; the identity has not changed. No *shinui* has occurred.

### Palm Tree Planks

Tosafot finds confirmation of this principle in a later Gemara passage: *gazal dikla ve-katleih, lo kani* — if one stole a palm tree and cut it down, no acquisition through *shinui* has occurred. *Dikla ve-avdei guvi, lo kani* — if one made planks from it, still no acquisition — *d'guvi de-dikla mikrei* — because the planks are still called "planks of the palm tree." The name carries over; the identity of the material persists. These cases drive home the point: a *shinui* that confers ownership must be substantial enough to alter the identity and appellation of the object, not merely its surface condition or shape.

### Whitening Wool

Having established the principle of *shinui koneh* through transformation of wood and wool, the Gemara turns to a specific question: *ve-halibun mi havei shinuy* — is whitening wool truly a *shinui*? A contradiction is noted. The *baraita* treats whitening as a *shinui* for purposes of *gezeila*. But in the context of *reishit ha-gez* — the obligation to give a portion of one's first shearings to the *kohen* — a *baraita* teaches that if the wool was whitened before it was given, the owner is still *chayav*; only if it was dyed is he exempt. This implies that whitening is *not* a *shinui* in the context of *reishit ha-gez*.

### Libun Debate

Abaye answers that it is a *tannaic* dispute: *ha Rabbi Shimon, ha Rabbanan* — one *baraita* follows Rabbi Shimon and the other follows the *Chachamim*. The underlying *baraita* in the context of *reishit ha-gez* records this explicitly: if one sheared the wool and then spun it, wove it, or dyed it before giving *reishit ha-gez*, the shearings do not combine with those of other sheep — a *shinui* has intervened. If one merely whitened it: Rabbi Shimon says they do not combine — *ein mitztaref*, treating whitening as a *shinui* — while the *Chachamim* say they do combine — *mitztaref* — treating whitening as insufficient. The debate is thus about whether *libun* constitutes a significant enough change to alter the identity of the wool, and it maps directly onto the contradiction between the two *baraitot*.

### Degrees of Libun

Rava offers an alternative resolution: *ha ve-ha Rabbi Shimon, ve-lo kashya* — both sources can be reconciled within Rabbi Shimon's own view, because there are different degrees of whitening. One type of whitening is where the wool is merely shaken out by hand — *ha de-naftzei naftzei* — a relatively light process. The other is where it is combed out vigorously with a comb — *ha de-sarkeh sarki* — a far more thorough and transformative procedure. The more vigorous process constitutes a *shinui*; the lighter one does not.

### Sulfur Whitening

Rav Chiya bar Avin offers a parallel distinction based on the chemical process used: *ha de-chorei chivra* — one case involves simple washing; *ha de-chorei chivra de-gufrisa* — the other involves whitening with sulfur, a qualitatively more intense and irreversible transformation. Under this reading, sulfur-whitening is a *shinui* and ordinary washing is not. Both Rava and Rav Chiya bar Avin thus agree that the question of *libun* as *shinui* is not a simple binary but admits of gradations — and the *tannaic* sources, rather than contradicting each other, each address a different point on the spectrum.
