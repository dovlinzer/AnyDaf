# Arakhin 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Field at Yovel

### Torah's Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הִגִּיעַ יוֹבֵל וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה, הַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְתוֹכָהּ וְנוֹתְנִין אֶת דָּמֶיהָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: נִכְנָסִין וְלֹא נוֹתְנִין. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נִכְנָסִין וְלֹא נוֹתְנִין, אֶלָּא נִקְרֵאת "שְׂדֵה רְטוּשִׁין" עַד הַיּוֹבֵל הַשֵּׁנִי. הִגִּיעַ הַיּוֹבֵל הַשֵּׁנִי וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה, נִקְרֵאת "רְטוּשֵׁי רְטוּשִׁין" עַד הַיּוֹבֵל הַשְּׁלִישִׁי. לְעוֹלָם אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁיִּגְאָלֶנָּה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one consecrated his ancestral field and the **Jubilee** Year **arrived and it was not redeemed** by the owner or anyone else, **the priests enter into** the field **and give its** redemption **payment** to the Temple treasury; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says: They enter** into the field, **but they do not give** its redemption payment to the Temple treasury. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priests **do not enter** into the field, **and** they also **do not give** its redemption payment to the Temple treasury. **Rather,** the field remains in the possession of the Temple treasury, and **it is called: An abandoned field, until the second Jubilee** Year. If **the second Jubilee arrived and it was** still **not redeemed, it is called: An abandoned** field from among the **abandoned** fields, meaning one that was abandoned twice, **until the third Jubilee.** In any case, **the priests never enter into** a consecrated field during the Jubilee Year **until another** person **redeems it** first.

The *daf* begins at the bottom of 25b, where the *mishnah* addresses a case that the *Torah* itself does not explicitly resolve. The *Torah* discusses a scenario in which someone is *makdish* a *sdeh achuza* — a field of ancestral inheritance — and that field is subsequently redeemed or sold. If the owner redeems it himself, the matter is concluded cleanly. If someone else purchases it from *hekdesh*, then when the *yovel* arrives, the field passes to the *kohanim*. But what happens if *hekdesh* simply sits on the field and nothing occurs — no owner redemption, no sale to a third party? The *Torah* provides no explicit ruling for this case, and it is precisely this lacuna that the *mishnah* addresses.

### R. Yehuda Pays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? גָּמַר קֹדֶשׁ קֹדֶשׁ מִמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּיִת, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּדָמִים — אַף כָּאן בְּדָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion that the priests who enter into an unredeemed consecrated field during the Jubilee Year must give its redemption payment to the Temple treasury? The Gemara answers: **He derives** this *halakha* from a verbal analogy **from** the word **"holy"** stated in reference to an ancestral field, and the word **"holy"** that appears with regard to one who **consecrates a house.** The Gemara explains: **Just as there,** with regard to one who consecrates a house, where the verse states: "And when a man shall consecrate his house to be holy for the Lord" (Leviticus 27:14), it can be redeemed from the Temple treasury only **by** payment of **money,** as the verse concludes: "As the priest shall value it, so shall it stand," **so too here,** where the verse states with regard to the priests entering into an ancestral field: "But the field, when it goes out in the Jubilee, shall be holy for the Lord…his ancestral possession shall be for the priest" (Leviticus 27:21), the priests can enter the field only **by** payment of **money.**

*Higia yovel velo nigal* — the *yovel* arrives and the field has not been redeemed by anyone. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *hakohanim nichnasim letocha venosnim et dameha* — the *kohanim* enter and take possession of the field, but they must pay money for it. The reasoning is as follows: had someone purchased the field from *hekdesh*, that transaction would have transferred the *kedusha* from the field to the money. But here, no such transaction occurred. The field has been sitting in the possession of *hekdesh* the entire time, and so it still carries its *kedusha*. The *kohanim* do receive the field, but since the *kedusha* has never been transferred, they must pay money in order to remove it.

Several details remain unclear according to Rabbi Yehuda. What is the applicable price — the fixed fifty *shekel* per *beis chomer se'orim*, which diminishes as the *yovel* approaches, or the market value? The simple inference from the *Gemara*'s comparison to sanctifying a house, as we will see, suggests market value. It is also not entirely clear whether the *kohanim* have any choice in the matter, or whether they are compelled to take possession and pay. These questions are left open.

### R. Shimon No Pay

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, גָּמַר קֹדֶשׁ קֹדֶשׁ מִכִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת, מָה לְהַלָּן בְּחִנָּם, אַף כָּאן בְּחִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what is the source for the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who maintains that the priests are not required to give the redemption payment to the Temple treasury? The Gemara answers: He **derives** the *halakha* **from** the word **"holy"** used with regard to an ancestral field, and the word **"holy"** used with regard to the communal peace offering of two **lambs** that accompanies the two loaves on ***Shavuot*. Just as there,** with regard to the offering of two lambs, where the verse states: "They shall be holy to the Lord for the priest" (Leviticus 23:20), the lambs are given to the priests **for free,** as is the *halakha* with regard to all offerings to which members of the priesthood are entitled, **so too here,** the consecrated and unredeemed ancestral field is given to the priests **for free.**

Rabbi Shimon agrees that the *kohanim* acquire the field when the *yovel* arrives, but disagrees that they must pay. In his view, *nichnasim velo nosnim* — they enter without payment. Even though the field has remained in *hekdesh* and technically still carries its *kedusha*, that *kedusha* is extinguished by virtue of the transfer to the *kohanim*, without any monetary exchange being required.

### R. Eliezer Waits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: לֹא נִכְנָסִין, וְלֹא נוֹתְנִין, אֶלָּא נִקְרֵאת "שְׂדֵה רְטוּשִׁין" עַד הַיּוֹבֵל הַשֵּׁנִי. הִגִּיעַ הַיּוֹבֵל הַשֵּׁנִי וְלֹא נִגְאֲלָה, נִקְרֵאת "רְטוּשֵׁי רְטוּשִׁין" עַד הַיּוֹבֵל הַשְּׁלִישִׁי. לְעוֹלָם אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁיִּגְאָלֶנָּה אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The priests **do not enter** into the field, **and** they also **do not give** its redemption payment to the Temple treasury. **Rather,** the field remains in the possession of the Temple treasury, and **it is called: An abandoned field, until the second Jubilee** Year. If **the second Jubilee arrived and it was** still **not redeemed, it is called: An abandoned** field from among the **abandoned** fields, meaning one that was abandoned twice, **until the third Jubilee.** In any case, **the priests never enter into** a consecrated field during the Jubilee Year **until another** person **redeems it** first.

Rabbi Eliezer adopts an entirely different position: *lo nichnasim velo nosnim* — the *kohanim* neither enter the field nor pay any money. His fundamental premise is that the *Torah* provides rulings only for two specific scenarios: either the owner redeems the field and retains it, or someone else purchases it from *hekdesh*, in which case when the *yovel* arrives, the field goes to the *kohanim*. If neither of these conditions has been satisfied — if the field simply remained in the possession of *hekdesh* without being purchased by anyone — then the *Torah* does not address the outcome. The field simply stays as *hekdesh*.

### Abandoned Field

So what becomes of this field? The *mishnah* says it is called *sdeh retushin* — an abandoned field — *ad hayovel hasheni*, until the second *yovel*. Rabbi Eliezer's position, in other words, is that the matter defers. The *Torah* provides for two scenarios; if neither has yet occurred, the field awaits a future cycle in which one of them might. Over the course of this second fifty-year period, the owner or a third party can still redeem or purchase the field, after which the normal laws will apply.

If the second *yovel* also passes with no redemption, the field is called *retushei retushin* — the abandoned of the abandoned — and the same logic extends: *ad hayovel hashlishi*, until the third *yovel*, and so on indefinitely. *Le'olam ein hakohanim nichnasim letocha ad sheyigalena acher* — the *kohanim* never acquire the field unless and until someone else redeems it, at which point the *Torah*'s provision regarding the next *yovel* becomes applicable.

A practical question arises: once a subsequent cycle begins, how does one calculate the redemption price? Presumably the cycle resets, so that at fifty years' distance from the next *yovel*, one again pays the full fifty *shekel*, diminishing as the *yovel* approaches.

The discussion also surfaces the question — which the *Gemara* will take up at length — of what happens if the original owner himself redeems the field during the second or a later cycle. Does he retain his status as the ancestral owner and therefore get to keep the field? Or has the passage of the first *yovel* without redemption irrevocably altered his standing, such that he is now treated like any random purchaser, meaning the field would pass to the *kohanim* at the next *yovel*?

## R. Yehuda Analysis (II)

### Sanctified House

The *Gemara* now turns to the underlying reasoning of each opinion. *Mai ta'ama de-Rabbi Yehuda* — what is the basis for Rabbi Yehuda's requirement that the *kohanim* pay? The answer is *gamar kodesh kodesh mi-makdish bayit*: Rabbi Yehuda derives the rule through a *gezeira shava* from the case of one who sanctifies his house. The *Torah* states, *ve-ish ki yakdish et beito kodesh l'Hashem ve-he'ericho ha-kohen bein tov u-vein ra ka'asher ya'arich oto ha-kohen ken yakum* — a man who sanctifies his house must have it valuated by the *kohen*, and the redemption proceeds by paying that assessed value. The *kedusha* is removed through payment. Rabbi Yehuda applies the same principle here: *mah le-halan bi-damim*, just as the *kedusha* of a sanctified house is dissolved through monetary payment, *af kan bi-damim*, so too here the transfer to the *kohanim* requires the payment of money.

The *Gemara* notes that if this analogy to the sanctified house is the operative source, the amount would naturally be market value, since that is how the *kohen*'s valuation of a house works.

### Kodesh Transfer

The broader principle emerging from Rabbi Yehuda's position is that *kedusha* attaches to an item and cannot simply evaporate. It must be displaced through a formal act of valuation and monetary exchange. The field remained *kadosh* throughout its time in *hekdesh*; for the *kohanim* to receive it as a secular holding, that *kedusha* must transfer to the money they pay, which itself then becomes *kadosh*.

## R. Shimon Analysis

### Korbanot Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי [יְהוּדָה] נָמֵי נֵילַיף מִכִּבְשֵׂי עֲצֶרֶת! דָּנִין קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת מִקׇּדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת, וְאֵין דָּנִין קׇדְשֵׁי בֶּדֶק הַבַּיִת מִקׇּדְשֵׁי מִזְבֵּחַ. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן נָמֵי, נֵילַף מִמַּקְדִּישׁ בַּיִת! דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁמַּתָּנָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, מִדָּבָר שֶׁמַּתָּנָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְאֵין דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁמַּתָּנָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, מִדָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַתָּנָה לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And let Rabbi Yehuda also derive** the *halakha* by means of a verbal analogy **from** the two **lambs** brought on *Shavuot*. Why does he disagree with Rabbi Shimon? The Gemara explains: **One derives** the *halakha* with regard to items **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance,** such as an ancestral field, **from** that of other items **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance,** e.g., a consecrated house, **but one does not derive** the *halakha* with regard to items **consecrated** for **Temple maintenance from** items that are **consecrated** for the **altar,** such as the two lambs brought on *Shavuot*. The Gemara objects: **And let Rabbi Shimon also derive** the *halakha* by a verbal analogy **from** one who **consecrates a house.** Why does he disagree with Rabbi Yehuda? The Gemara explains: **One derives** the *halakha* of an **item that** is **a gift to the priests,** such as an ancestral field that is given to the priests during the Jubilee Year, **from** that of another **item that is a gift to the priests,** i.e., the two lambs brought on *Shavuot*, **but one does not derive** the *halakha* of an **item that is a gift to the priests from** that of an **item that is not a gift to the priests,** i.e., a consecrated house.

The *Gemara* asks why Rabbi Yehuda does not instead derive the rule from the *kivsei ha-atzeret* — the lambs of *Shavuot*. These are *korbanot* that are *kodesh*, yet the *kohanim* consume them without any monetary payment. The answer is that Rabbi Yehuda employs the principle *danin kodshei bedek habayit mi-kodshei bedek habayit* — we derive the law of items sanctified for the *Temple Treasury* from other items sanctified for the *Temple Treasury*, not from *kodshei mizbeach*, items consecrated for the altar. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, argues for the comparison to *korbanot*: *mah le-halan be-chinam*, just as the *kohanim* receive those offerings without payment, *af kan be-chinam*, the same applies here.

### Kodesh Consumption

However, there is a significant conceptual asymmetry between the two cases. When *kohanim* eat a *korban*, they consume it while it retains its *kodesh* status — within the *Beit HaMikdash*, subject to the restrictions of *notar*, *tumah*, and so on. It is consumed *as hekdesh*, not after being converted into something secular. By contrast, a field that is acquired by *kohanim* for ongoing agricultural use is no longer being used *as hekdesh* — it becomes a secular holding. One works the field without restriction, employs non-*kohanim*, and ignores the constraints that would attend actual *kedusha*.

This is the conceptual problem with Rabbi Shimon's position: in every other case, either the item was never *kodesh* to begin with, like *pidyon haben* money or *reishit hagez*, or it was consumed under strict *kedusha* conditions, like *terumah* and *korbanot*. Here we are positing something that begins as *kodesh* but transitions into fully secular use — and Rabbi Shimon maintains that no monetary transfer is required to effect that transition.

## R. Yehuda Analysis (II)

### Treasury vs Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר? אָמַר קְרָא: "וְאִם לֹא יִגְאַל אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה לֹא יִגָּאֵל עוֹד, וְאִם מָכַר אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה וְהָיָה הַשָּׂדֶה בְּצֵאתוֹ בַיּוֹבֵל".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbah said: What is the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion? It is because the **verse states:** "And if he will not redeem the field, or if he sold the field to another man, it shall not be redeemed anymore. But the field, when it goes out in the Jubilee, shall be holy for the Lord, as a dedicated field; his ancestral possession shall be for the priest" (Leviticus 27:20–21). According to Rabbah, Rabbi Eliezer maintains that these verses teach two separate *halakhot*, and should be read as follows: **"And if he will not redeem the field…it shall not be redeemed anymore,"** as an ancestral field, and: **"Or if he sold the field…But the field, when it goes out in the Jubilee…**his ancestral possession shall be for the priest." Accordingly, if the field has not yet been sold to another man, it is not transferred to the priests during the Jubilee.

The *Gemara* now presents the full dialectic regarding why each opinion cannot simply borrow the other's proof. The question is posed: why does Rabbi Shimon not derive from the sanctified house — *neilef mi-makdish bayit* — that payment is required? His answer is *danin davar she-matanah la-kohanim mi-davar she-matanah la-kohanim* — we learn from things that are given to the *kohanim* to other things that are given to the *kohanim*. The field sanctified by *achuza* that goes to the *kohanim* at *yovel* should be compared to other *matanot kehunah*, all of which the *kohanim* receive without payment: *terumah*, *korbanot*, *pidyon haben*, *reishit hagez*. In every instance, the *kohanim* receive what is due them without having to pay for it.

The reason Rabbi Shimon rejects the comparison to *makdish bayit* is that a sanctified house goes to the *Temple Treasury*, not to the *kohanim* as their personal *matanah*. Its proceeds fund the operating expenses or structural repairs of the *Beit HaMikdash*. Rabbi Yehuda, in turn, refuses the comparison to *korbanot* because *kodshei bedek habayit* — items consecrated to the Temple Treasury — must be redeemed through money in order to become usable, and one does not derive that rule from *kodshei mizbeach*, altar offerings, where a fundamentally different mechanism is at work.

The crux of the debate, then, is this: Rabbi Yehuda insists on deriving from items that share the *hekdesh le-bedek habayit* structure, where monetary exchange is required to release the *kedusha*. Rabbi Shimon insists on deriving from items that share the *matanah la-kohanim* structure, where the *kohanim* simply receive without payment. Each has a legitimate formal basis; neither can simply be dismissed.

## R. Eliezer Analysis

### Only If Sold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: סַכִּינָא חֲרִיפָא מַפְסְקָא קְרָאֵי? אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כִּדְתַנְיָא: "לֹא יִגָּאֵל" — יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "עוֹד" — לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת, אֲבָל נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Does **a sharp knife cut** through the **verses,** that they may be interpreted by reading the words out of order? **Rather, Abaye said: The reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: Since the verse states: "And if he will not redeem the field, or if he sold the field to another man, **it shall not be redeemed,"** one **might** have thought that this means **it shall not be redeemed** at all by its owner, even **for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field,** which remains in his possession until the Jubilee. Therefore, **the verse states: "Anymore,"** indicating that **it shall not be redeemed** in order for it **to** return to **the way it was,** i.e., to regain the status of an ancestral field, **but it** may be **redeemed for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.**

### Abaye Objects

Abaye is sharply critical of this method. *Sakina charifa mafseqa kra'ei?* — Are you taking a sharp knife and splitting the *pasuk*? You cannot simply lift the opening phrase, skip over the entire middle of the verse, and attach it to the closing phrase. The *pasuk* is a structured unit; you cannot splice it with scissors and tape. The *drasha* of Rabbah is therefore rejected.

Before moving to Abaye's alternative, it is worth pausing over the observation raised by *Tosafot*. *Tosafot* asks why anyone finds Rabbi Eliezer's position surprising in the first place: the *psukim* straightforwardly discuss what happens *if* the field is sold — *ve-im machar* — which naturally implies that if it is not sold, the provision does not apply. Rabbi Eliezer may simply be reading *pshat*. The problem, as *Tosafot* acknowledges, is that the *Gemara* — both Rava and Abaye — insists that the *Torah* must be actively speaking to this scenario, not merely leaving it in silence. There must be a *drasha* that locates Rabbi Eliezer's ruling in the text, not merely an argument from the *Torah*'s omission.

### Lo Yiga'el Drasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בְּיוֹבֵל רִאשׁוֹן — אַמַּאי אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת? שְׂדֵה אֲחוּזָּה נָמֵי הָוְיָא! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי. וּלְמַאן? אִילֵימָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לְכֹהֲנִים נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מֵהָכָא. וְתִסְבְּרָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַאי "עוֹד" מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁיָּצְאָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ כֹּהֵן, וְאָתוּ בְּעָלִים לְמִיפְרְקַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: According to this *baraita*, of **when,** i.e., about which time period, is the verse speaking? **If we say** that it is referring to a redemption occurring **during the first Jubilee** cycle in which the field was consecrated, then **why may it not be redeemed** in order for it to return to the way it was? At that point, **it is even** redeemable as **an ancestral field,** because if the owner redeems it then, it is not removed from his possession during the Jubilee Year. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* interprets the verse as referring to a redemption occurring **during the second Jubilee** cycle. **And according to whose** opinion is this *baraita*? **If we say** that it is **according to** either **Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Shimon,** this cannot be correct, as according to them, the field **leaves** the possession of the Temple treasury and is given **to the priests** during the first Jubilee Year, after which it may no longer be redeemed. **Rather, is it not** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who maintains that the field remains in the possession of the Temple treasury until it is redeemed, even during a subsequent Jubilee cycle? **And** consequently, **conclude from** this *baraita* that **the reason for Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion is **from here,** i.e., from the superfluous term "anymore." The Gemara asks: **And can you understand** the *baraita* this way? If so, then **what do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derive from this** term: **"Anymore"? Rather,** the *baraita* can be understood in accordance with their opinions as well, and **what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing **with** an ancestral **field** whose owner consecrated it and did not redeem it, **which left** the possession of the Temple treasury and was given **to the priests** during the first Jubilee Year; **and** the **priest** who received the field then **consecrated it, and** then the original **owner came to redeem it** from the Temple treasury.

## Lo Yiga'el Od

### Field to Kohanim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לָא תִּיפְרוֹק, שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "עוֹד", לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת, אֲבָל נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: "בִּשְׁנַת הַיּוֹבֵל יָשׁוּב הַשָּׂדֶה לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ מֵאִתּוֹ", יָכוֹל יַחְזוֹר לַגִּזְבָּר שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לַאֲשֶׁר לוֹ אֲחֻזַּת הָאָרֶץ". יֵאָמֵר: "לַאֲשֶׁר לוֹ אֲחֻזַּת הָאָרֶץ", מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ מֵאִתּוֹ"? שָׂדֶה שֶׁיָּצָאת לַכֹּהֲנִים, וּמְכָרָהּ כֹּהֵן, וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ לוֹקֵחַ, וּגְאָלָהּ אַחֵר — יָכוֹל תַּחְזוֹר לַבְּעָלִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ".
>
> **Translation:** It might **enter your mind to say:** Since the owner failed to redeem this field during the first Jubilee cycle, **it shall not be redeemed** by him at all, even **for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Anymore,"** indicating that **it shall not be redeemed** in order for it **to** return to **the way it was,** i.e., an ancestral field, **but it** may be **redeemed for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field,** which remains in his possession only until the Jubilee Year. **And** similarly, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who consecrated a purchased field and redeemed it, since the verse states: **"In the Jubilee Year the field shall return to the one from whom it was bought"** (Leviticus 27:24), one **might** have thought that the field **shall return to the** Temple **treasurer, from whom he bought it** when he redeemed it. Therefore, **the verse states** immediately afterward: **"To the one to whom the possession of the land belongs,"** i.e., it goes to the ancestral owner who initially sold the field to the one who consecrated it. The *baraita* continues: If so, **let** the verse simply **state: "To the one to whom the possession of the land belongs." Why** must **the verse state** first: **"To the one from whom it was bought"?** The *baraita* answers: The verse is referring to an ancestral **field** whose owner consecrated it and did not redeem it, **which left** the possession of the Temple treasury and was given **to the priests** during the Jubilee Year, **and** the **priest** who received the field then **sold it** to another, **and** the **buyer** then **consecrated it, and another** person **redeemed it** from the Temple treasury. One **might** have thought that when the next Jubilee arrives, **it shall return to the original owner.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "In the Jubilee Year the field shall return **to the one** from whom **it was bought,"** indicating that the field is returned to the priest who sold the field.

### Original Owner Rights

The question is: can the original owner, when redeeming a field that was once his but has since become the *kohen*'s ancestral holding, claim any special status? One might argue that the *Torah*'s declaration *lo yigal od* — he shall not redeem it any further — bars him entirely, meaning he cannot touch the field at all. The *baraita* teaches that this would be an overreading. The word *od* narrows the prohibition: he cannot redeem it *ke-moshe hayetah* — in the way he once could, such that it would revert to being his ancestral plot. But he can certainly redeem it like a random purchaser, using it until the next *yovel*. The *kedusha* does not act as a total barrier against him.

### Two Phrases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִךְ "לֹא יִגָּאֵל", וְאִיצְטְרִיכָא לְמִיכְתַּב "לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ". דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "לֹא יִגָּאֵל", דְּלָא קָא הָדְרָא כְּלָל, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּקָא הָדְרָא, תִּיהְדַּר לְמָרַהּ קַמָּא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ". וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ", דְּלָא קָא יָהֲבִי בְּעָלִים דְּמֵי, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּקָא יָהֲבִי דְּמֵי, תֵּיקוּם בִּידַיְיהוּ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "לֹא יִגָּאֵל". וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "לֹא יִגָּאֵל", וְלָא כְּתַב "עוֹד", הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לָא תִּיפְרוֹק כְּלָל, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא "עוֹד", לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת, אֲבָל נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** it **was necessary** for the Torah to write: **"It shall not be redeemed," and** it **was** also **necessary** for it **to write: "To the one** from whom **it was bought,"** despite the fact both phrases apparently teach the same *halakha*, i.e., that after a consecrated ancestral field is left unredeemed and given to the priests during the Jubilee Year, it never returns to the original owner. **Because if the Merciful One had written** only: **"It shall not be redeemed,"** one might have thought that the field does not return to the original owner only in a case where the priest consecrates the field and the original owner redeems it, **as** an ancestral field that is consecrated by its owner and redeemed by another **does not return at all,** but is divided among the priests. **But here,** where the priest sold the field that he received and the field was consecrated by the buyer as a purchased field and subsequently redeemed by another, **as** the field **does return** to its owner in the Jubilee Year, perhaps **it should return to its original owner,** and not the priest. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote: "To the one** from whom **it was bought,"** indicating that it returns to the priest. **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"To the one** from whom **it was bought,"** one might have thought that the field does not return to the original owner during the Jubilee Year only in a case where the priest sold it and the buyer consecrated it, **as the owner did not give** any **payment for** the field to the Temple treasury. **But here,** where the priest consecrated the field and its original owner redeemed it, **as he gives payment for** the field to the Temple treasury, perhaps **it should remain in his hands** as his ancestral field. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote: "It shall not be redeemed."** **And if the Merciful One had written** only: **"It shall not be redeemed," and did not write: "Anymore," I would say** that **it shall not be redeemed at all,** as its original owner failed to redeem it during the first Jubilee cycle. Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "Anymore,"** to indicate that **it shall not be redeemed** in order for it **to** return to **the way it was,** i.e., an ancestral field, **but it** may be **redeemed for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.**

The *Gemara* then asks why both phrases — *lo yiga'el* and *la'asher kanahu* — are necessary, given that they seem to be making the same basic point about the loss of original-owner status. The answer is that each covers a case the other would not.

Had the *Torah* stated only *lo yiga'el* — ruling that the original owner loses his redemptive status when he comes to actively buy it back — one might reason as follows: there, his loss of status makes sense because the field does not revert automatically. He is attempting an affirmative act of purchase, and evaluated purely in present terms, he is no longer the ancestral owner. But in the case of a *sdeh miknah* — where the field *does* revert automatically to the ancestral owner at *yovel* — maybe the automatic character of the reversion invokes the original ancestral order, and the original owner could claim that the *yovel* should restore everything to its primordial state, including his ownership.

Conversely, had the *Torah* stated only *la'asher kanahu* — that when a purchased field reverts at *yovel* it goes to the most recent ancestral holder, not the original one — one might argue: that makes sense because I am claiming something for nothing, without paying; naturally my claim is weak. But when I am actually paying money to redeem the field after Joe sanctified it, perhaps my original status should reassert itself, since I am acting in the way an owner would act by paying for the redemption.

Therefore both phrases are required. *Lo yiga'el* rules out the case where the original owner pays to redeem but seeks to retain ancestral standing. *La'asher kanahu* rules out the case where the reversion is automatic and one might argue that the original order is being restored. And the word *od* is essential in *lo yiga'el*, because without it one might read the prohibition as absolute — totally excluding the original owner from any use of the field, even as a random purchaser. *Od* clarifies that the prohibition is specifically against redeeming it in the manner he once could, with full ancestral retention; he retains the ordinary rights of any person.

### Status Never Returns

The upshot is elegant and firm: once the original owner loses the field to the *kohanim*, he has no special status. The *kohanim* become the new ancestral holders. If the original owner wishes to use the field, he is entitled to do so as any other person would, by paying for the redemption and occupying the field until the next *yovel* — at which point it reverts not to him but to the *kohanim*, who are now the *ba'alei ha-achuza*.

This principle receives further reinforcement from a second *baraita* on the verse *la'asher kanahu me'ito la'asher lo achuzat ha'aretz* — the field reverts to the one from whom it was purchased, to the one who holds the ancestral claim. One might have thought this refers to the *gizbar*, the Temple treasurer, since he is the one from whom the buyer technically purchased it. The verse clarifies: it goes to the one who holds the ancestral claim in the land. Yet if that is all the verse intends, why does it need to say *la'asher kanahu* — the one from whom it was purchased? Because it must address the case of *sadeh sheyatzah la-kohanim u-machrah kohen* — a field that transferred to Joe as *kohen*, which Joe then sold to David, who sanctified it and had it redeemed by yet another party. In that scenario, the original owner might argue that he is the true ancestral holder and the field should return to him. The verse counters: *la'asher kanahu* — it goes to the one most recently constituting the ancestral owner, meaning Joe. The original owner has no claim whatsoever.

## Second Yovel Status

### Second Cycle Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּעָלִים בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי, כְּאַחֵר דָּמוּ אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: "לֹא יִגָּאֵל" — יָכוֹל לֹא תְּהֵא נִגְאֶלֶת, שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "עוֹד" — לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת, אֲבָל נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה. אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא בְּיוֹבֵל רִאשׁוֹן — אַמַּאי אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת? אֲחוּזָּה נָמֵי הָוְיָא! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי. וּלְמַאן? אִי לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — לְכֹהֲנִים נָפְקָא! אֶלָּא לָאו לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בְּעָלִים בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי כְּאַחֵר דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: According to Rabbi Eliezer, if the **owner** consecrated his ancestral field and failed to redeem it before the first Jubilee Year, but he redeemed it **during the second Jubilee** cycle, is he **considered like another** person who redeemed the field, in which case it is given to the priests at the following Jubilee Year, **or not,** i.e., as the one who ultimately redeemed the field first was the owner, does it remain in his possession as it would have if he had redeemed it during the first Jubilee cycle? The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this dilemma from the following *baraita*: Since the verse states: "And if he will not redeem the field, or if he sold the field to another man, **it shall not be redeemed"** (Leviticus 27:20), one **might** have thought that this means **it shall not be redeemed** at all by its owner, even **for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Anymore,"** indicating that **it shall not be redeemed** in order for it **to** return to **the way it was,** i.e., an ancestral field, **but it** may be **redeemed for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.** The Gemara continues: According to this *baraita*, of **when,** i.e., about which time period, is the verse speaking? **If we say** that it is referring to a redemption that occurs **during the first Jubilee** cycle, in which the field was consecrated, **why may it not be redeemed** in order for it to return to the way it was? At that point **it is even** redeemable as **an ancestral** field, since if the owner redeems it then, it is not removed from his possession during the Jubilee Year. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* interprets the verse as referring to a redemption occurring **during the second Jubilee** cycle. **And according to whose** opinion is this *baraita*? **If** we say that it is **according to** either **Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Shimon,** this cannot be correct, as according to them, the field **leaves** the possession of the Temple treasury and is given **to the priests** during the first Jubilee Year, after which it may no longer be redeemed. **Rather, is it not** in accordance with the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer,** who maintains that the field remains in the possession of the Temple treasury until it is redeemed, even during a subsequent Jubilee cycle? **And** consequently, you can **conclude from** this *baraita* that according to Rabbi Eliezer, an **owner** who redeems his ancestral field from the Temple treasury **during the second Jubilee** cycle is **considered like another,** as he may redeem it only for it to be treated like a purchased field.

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרַאּ?! רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הַאי "עוֹד" מַאי דָּרְשִׁי בֵּיהּ? אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁיָּצְאָה לַכֹּהֲנִים, וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ כֹּהֵן, וַאֲתוֹ בְּעָלִים לְמִיפְרְקַהּ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לָא תִּיפְרוֹק, שֶׁתְּהֵא כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "עוֹד" — לִכְמוֹת שֶׁהָיְתָה אֵינָהּ נִגְאֶלֶת, אֲבָל נִגְאֶלֶת שֶׁתְּהֵא לְפָנָיו כִּשְׂדֵה מִקְנָה. וְהָתַנְיָא: "יָשׁוּב הַשָּׂדֶה לַאֲשֶׁר קָנָהוּ מֵאִתּוֹ", יָכוֹל יַחְזוֹר לַגִּזְבָּר שֶׁלְּקָחָהּ הֵימֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לַאֲשֶׁר לוֹ אֲחֻזַּת הָאָרֶץ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And can you understand** the *baraita* this way? If so, **what do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon derive from this** term: **"Anymore"? Rather, what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing **with** an ancestral **field** whose owner consecrated it and did not redeem it, **which left** the possession of the Temple treasury and was given **to the priests** at the first Jubilee Year; **and** the **priest** who received the field subsequently **consecrated it, and** then the original **owner came to redeem it** from the Temple treasury. It may **enter your mind to say:** Since the owner failed to redeem it during the first Jubilee cycle, **it shall not be redeemed** by him at all, even **for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Anymore,"** indicating that **it shall not be redeemed** in order for it **to** return to **the way it was,** i.e., an ancestral field, **but it** may be **redeemed for it to be** treated **for him like a purchased field.** **And** similarly, **it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to one who consecrated a purchased field and redeemed it, since the verse states: "In the Jubilee Year **the field shall return to the one** from whom **it was bought"** (Leviticus 27:24), one **might** have thought that the field **shall return to the** Temple **treasurer, from whom he bought it** when he redeemed it. Therefore, **the verse states: "To the one to whom the possession of the land belongs,"** i.e., the ancestral owner.

But the *Gemara* immediately objects: *vetisbara* — can this reasoning really stand? Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon also need to account for the word *od*. You cannot simply declare the entire *drasha* relevant only to Rabbi Eliezer. The *drasha* of *od* must serve a function within all opinions. Therefore the *baraita* must be reinterpreted, as already argued, as referring to the case of the *kohen* who sanctified a field that came to him at *yovel*, and the original owner seeking to redeem it — a case perfectly applicable under all three opinions.

Accordingly, the *Gemara* concedes that the earlier attempt to resolve the question from the *baraita* fails. A different *baraita* is then cited explicitly: *Rabbi Eliezer omer ga'alah ba'alim be-yovel sheni, yotzei la-kohanim be-yovel* — Rabbi Eliezer says that if the owners redeem it during the second *yovel* cycle, it goes to the *kohanim* at the subsequent *yovel*. This would definitively answer the question: the owners have lost their status; in the second cycle they are treated like any other purchaser.

### Mishnah Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: גְּאָלָהּ בְּעָלִים בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי — יוֹצְאָה לַכֹּהֲנִים בַּיּוֹבֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָאֲנַן לָא תְּנַן הָכִי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁיִּגְאָלֶנָּה אַחֵר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּעָלִים בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי כְּאַחֵר דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Since it has been established that the term "anymore" is necessary according to all opinions, **what** conclusion **was** reached **about** the original dilemma with regard to whether the owner is considered like another person during the second Jubilee cycle? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the following *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If the **owner redeemed it during the second Jubilee** cycle, it **leaves** the possession of the Temple treasury and is given **to the priests during the Jubilee** Year. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: But we did not learn this** interpretation of Rabbi Eliezer's opinion in the mishna. Instead, the mishna states that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The priests** never **enter into** a consecrated field during the Jubilee Year **until another** person **redeems it** first. This indicates that if the owner, rather than any other person, is the one who ultimately redeems the field, it remains in his possession even if he did so during the second Jubilee cycle. Rav Ashi **said to** Ravina: **During the second Jubilee** cycle, the **owner** is **considered like another** person.

### Competing Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: גְּאָלָהּ בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי — אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה לַכֹּהֲנִים בַּיּוֹבֵל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין הַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לְתוֹכָהּ עַד שֶׁיִּגְאָלֶנָּה אַחֵר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מִמַּתְנִיתִין הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּעָלִים בְּיוֹבֵל שֵׁנִי כְּאַחֵר דָּמוּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Some say** a different version of the *baraita* and ensuing discussion: **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If the owner **redeemed it during the second Jubilee** cycle, it **does not leave** the possession of the Temple treasury and is not given **to the priests during the Jubilee** Year. **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** Why must a proof be brought from this *baraita*? **We learn** in the mishna **as well** that **Rabbi Eliezer says: The priests** never **enter into** a consecrated field during the Jubilee Year **until another** person **redeems it** first. Rav Ashi **said to him: If** a proof is learned **from the mishna** alone **I would say** that **during the second Jubilee** cycle the **owner** is **considered like another** person. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that according to Rabbi Eliezer the owner is not considered like another person, and the field returns to him as an ancestral possession.

The *Gemara* closes with an alternative formulation: *ika de-amri* — there are those who say the *baraita* was reported in the opposite version. In this second version, *Rabbi Eliezer omer ga'alah be-yovel sheni eina yotza la-kohanim be-yovel* — if the owners redeem it in the second cycle, it does *not* go to the *kohanim*; the original owner retains his status. Ravina then notes that this version is consistent with our *mishnah*, since the *mishnah*'s use of *acher* implies that the owner is distinct from the stranger, and the owner's redemption produces a different outcome.

Rav Ashi counters that the *mishnah* alone is insufficient to settle the question, since the *mishnah*'s language is ambiguous: *iy matnitin, hava amina ba'alim be-yovel sheni ka'acher damu* — even from the *mishnah* one might argue that in the second cycle the owners are already considered *acher*. The resolution would have required the explicit *baraita*, but since there are two versions of that *baraita* — one saying the field does go to *kohanim*, the other saying it does not — the question remains unresolved.

This is the *Gemara*'s conclusion, and it is a striking one. The fundamental question of whether the original owner retains any ancestral status during a subsequent *yovel* cycle, according to Rabbi Eliezer, is left entirely open — *gemara de-avda kizeh ve-kizeh*. Two versions of the *baraita* exist, pointing in opposite directions, and the *mishnah* alone cannot adjudicate between them.
