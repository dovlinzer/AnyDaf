# Nazir 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Father Making Son Nazir

### Age Limits Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: עַד מָתַי מַדִּיר אֶת בְּנוֹ בְּנָזִיר — עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the question of whether the ruling that a man can impose naziriteship on his son is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai or whether it is part of the boy's education. **Let us say** that this is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it is taught: **Until when can** a father **vow** that **his son** should **be a nazirite?** It is **until** the son **develops two** pubic **hairs;** this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says:** It is **until he reaches the age of vows,** usually approximately a year earlier, when he demonstrates an understanding of the nature of vows. If he vows at that age and comprehends the significance of his utterance, his vow is valid.

The Gemara opens with the suggestion that the Amoraic debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish — concerning whether a father's power to impose *nezirut* on his son derives from a special *halacha* or from the rabbinic concept of *chinuch* — may be rooted in an earlier Tannaitic dispute. The Gemara introduces a *baraita* with the question: *ad matai madzir beno benazir* — until what age can a father make his son a *nazir*?

### Rabbi's Halacha View

Rabbi answers: *ad sheyavi shtei se'arot* — until the son brings two pubic hairs, i.e., until the son reaches full halachic adulthood at thirteen. In Rabbi's view, the father retains the power to impose *nezirut* on his son as long as the son remains a minor in the complete sense. Even if the son may have already acquired a certain degree of independence in the realm of vows, the son is still fundamentally under the father's jurisdiction as long as he has not reached full maturity.

### R. Yose Chinuch View

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda takes a different position: *ad sheyagia le'onat nedarim* — until the son arrives at the age of vow-making. This refers to the year preceding *bar mitzvah*, during which a child who sufficiently understands the meaning and gravity of a vow may make one that is halachically binding. This status is known as *mufla samuch l'ish* — literally, one who can enunciate vows and is close to adulthood. The capacity to make independent vows, according to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, removes the son from his father's sphere of authority in this domain. The underlying rationale is that *chinuch* — the father's obligation to train his son in *mitzvot* — is the conceptual basis for the father's power here. Once the son can himself function as an agent in the area of *nedarim*, the justification for *chinuch* falls away.

### Gemara Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? דְּרַבִּי סָבַר הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּהִגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים, מַדִּיר לֵיהּ וְאָזֵיל עַד דְּמַיְיתֵי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים, סָבַר כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכוֹ בְּמִצְוֹת, וְכֵיוָן דְּנָפֵיק מֵרְשׁוּתֵיהּ — תּוּ לָא מִיחַיַּיב?
>
> **Translation:** **What, is it not** the case that this **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*: As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **it is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **with regard to a nazirite, and** therefore **even though** the son has already **reached the age of vows** and is capable of making his own decision with regard to the vow, this *halakha* states that the father **continues to vow for him until he develops two** pubic **hairs,** the sign of maturity. **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who says** that the father can vow for the son only **until he reaches the age of vows, holds** that he imposes a vow on his son **in order to educate him in mitzvot, and since** the son **has left** the father's **supervision** to the degree that he can utter his own vows, the father **is no longer obligated** to educate him in this area of *halakha*.

The Gemara pauses before concluding that this Tannaitic debate straightforwardly maps onto the Amoraic one. The positions could align, but it is not self-evident that they must. The debate between thirteen and twelve does not automatically imply a debate about *halacha* versus *chinuch*, because each of those two conceptual positions could potentially lead to either age cutoff. The Gemara therefore explores two distinct approaches to reading the Tannaitic dispute.

### Halacha Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא. דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר, וְהָכָא בְּ״מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ״ קָמִיפַּלְגִי. רַבִּי סָבַר: מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ — דְּרַבָּנַן, וְאָתְיָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא דָּחֲיָא דְּרַבָּנַן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ — דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument. The Sages **say** in response: **No;** this is not the correct explanation of that dispute, as one can say **that everyone,** both Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, agrees with Rabbi Yoḥanan that **it is a *halakha* with regard to a nazirite, and here they disagree with regard to** the issue of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority.** In other words, they dispute the case of a child who utters a vow when he is almost mature, on the verge of puberty. It is an accepted *halakha* that the vow of this child is valid if he understands its meaning. The dispute concerns the source of this *halakha*. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that the status of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority** applies **by rabbinic law, and** therefore the *halakha* **by Torah law** that a father can vow on behalf of his son **comes and overrides** the decree that one who is near the age of maturity is considered an independent adult with regard to vows, which is **by rabbinic law. And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that the status of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority** applies **by Torah law.** Consequently, the *halakha* that a father can vow on behalf of his son does not apply to this case.

The first approach posits that *dechulei alma halacha hi benader* — both Rabbi and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda agree that the father's power to impose *nezirut* on his son is a *halacha*, not a rabbinic device of *chinuch*. The point of contention is a different one: *vehacha bemufla samuch le'ish kamiflagi* — they disagree about the legal status of *mufla samuch l'ish*.

Rabbi holds that *mufla samuch l'ish d'rabanan* — the son's independent vow-making capacity in the year before *bar mitzvah* is only a rabbinic institution. Since the father's power derives from a *halacha* — and this Gemara clearly assumes that *halacha* here means *d'oraita* — *ve'ata de'oraita dachi d'rabanan*: a biblical rule overrides a rabbinic one. Therefore, the father retains the power to impose *nezirut* on his son even during the year of *onat nedarim*.

Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that *mufla samuch l'ish de'oraita* — the son's independent capacity for vows during that year is a biblical status. And since *nezirut* is itself a form of vow, even a *halacha*-based power in the father cannot override the son's biblically recognized independence in that very domain.

### Halacha L'Moshe Status

This raises an important question about the nature of *halacha* itself. The Gemara never actually uses the phrase *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* — it says only *halacha*. The assumption that *halacha* means *d'oraita* is operative in this sugya, but deserves scrutiny. There are cases where *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* is used rhetorically or metaphorically rather than literally — a notable example appears in a *mishnah* in Eduyot, which uses the phrase *halacha* to describe what is clearly a rabbinic obligation regarding *terumos u'maasros* in lands outside of Israel. Scholars have traced how the concept evolved across the Tannaitic and Amoraic periods, and the phrase does not invariably signal *d'oraita* status.

A second consideration: there is a view, associated with the Netziv, that the word *halacha* in certain contexts — particularly as Rashi uses it — may mean simply an ancient tradition, without carrying the full force of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai*. These caveats notwithstanding, the Gemara's own logic in this passage clearly treats *halacha* as *d'oraita*. The critical point is that the *halacha* here is not an entirely independent law but rather a detail within the existing *d'oraita* framework of *nezirut*. When a *halacha* supplies a parameter internal to a *d'oraita* category, it is naturally folded into that category's biblical standing. That is presumably why *halacha* here is treated as carrying *d'oraita* weight: it tells us something about how the *d'oraita* laws of *nezirut* operate, not about some independent, externally-derived rule.

### Chinuch Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּדֵי לְחַנְּכוֹ בְּמִצְוֹת, וּמוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ — דְּרַבָּנַן הִיא. רַבִּי סָבַר: אָתֵי חִינּוּךְ דְּרַבָּנַן וְדָחֵי מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ דְּרַבָּנַן. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים, קָסָבַר: לָא אָתֵי חִינּוּךְ דְּרַבָּנַן וְדָחֵי מוּפְלָא הַסָּמוּךְ לְאִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead **that everyone** agrees that a father vows for his son **in order to educate him in mitzvot,** which means it is by rabbinic law, **and** likewise the status of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority is** also **by rabbinic law.** In that case, the *tanna'im* dispute the following: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that the mitzva of **education,** which applies **by rabbinic law, comes and overrides** the status of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority,** which is also **by rabbinic law. And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that a father can vow for his son only **until he reaches the age of vows, holds** that the mitzva of **education by rabbinic law does not come and override** the status of **a minor one year before** he or she **reaches majority.** Consequently, there is no necessary connection between this dispute of *tanna'im* and the argument between Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish as to whether the ruling that a father can impose naziriteship on his son is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai or an educational tool.

The second approach argues in precisely the opposite direction: *dechulei alma k'dei l'chancho b'mitzvot* — everyone agrees the father's power is based on *chinuch*, and the entire institution is rabbinic. Here too, *mufla samuch l'ish* is *d'rabanan*. The question becomes which of two competing rabbinic institutions prevails.

According to Rabbi: *asi chinuch d'rabanan v'dachi mufla samuch l'ish d'rabanan* — the father's rabbinic obligation of *chinuch* overrides the son's rabbinic independence in vow-making. The father's role as trainer retains priority; the son's ability to make his own vows does not terminate the father's *chinuch* jurisdiction in this area. This is striking given that this framework already requires overriding numerous other halachic principles — *chullin b'azarah*, considerations of *shechitah*, and so forth. Adding one more override is consistent with the broader latitude the rabbis extended to the father's *chinuch* role.

According to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, once the rabbis established that a son of twelve has recognized independence in the realm of *nedarim*, that recognition itself removes him from the category of one requiring *chinuch* in this domain. The rabbis' acknowledgment of the son's capacity is not merely an incidental permission to make vows; it is a statement that the son is no longer a subject of the father's *chinuch* in this particular area. Therefore, *lo asi chinuch d'rabanan v'dachi mufla samuch l'ish* — the father's *chinuch* authority cannot override that recognized independence.

## Rabbi Chanina Story

### Chanina Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי כִּי הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא שֶׁהִדִּירוֹ אָבִיו בְּנָזִיר, וֶהֱבִיאוֹ לִפְנֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. וְהָיָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בּוֹדְקוֹ לֵידַע אִם הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, אִם לֹא הֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the dispute concerning the time period for a father's vow on behalf of his son, the Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that the dispute between **these *tanna'im* is like** the dispute between **those** other ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Nidda* 5:9): **An incident** occurred **involving Rabbi Ḥanina** in **which his father vowed** that he should **be a nazirite** when he was a minor, **and they brought him before Rabban Gamliel, and Rabban Gamliel examined him to discern if he had** already **developed two** pubic **hairs,** in which case the vow would not take effect, or **if he had not developed** them, which would mean that the vow was valid.

Having established the theoretical Tannaitic background, the Gemara now transitions to a concrete narrative. A *baraita* records: *ma'aseh b'Rebbi Chanina she'hadiro aviv b'nazir* — there was an incident involving Rabbi Chanina whose father had made him a *nazir*. When the *nezirut* was completed, he was brought before Rabban Gamliel. *V'haya Rabban Gamliel bodek bo leida im hevi shtei se'arot im lav hevi* — Rabban Gamliel examined him to determine whether he had brought two pubic hairs. The question was whether the father had validly imposed *nezirut* on the son, or whether the son had already been an adult at the time — in which case the father's act would have had no effect. Rabbi Yose, for his part, holds that what Rabban Gamliel was checking was not *shtei se'arot* but whether the boy *higia l'onat nedarim* — had reached the age of vow-making — meaning that the decisive question was whether the child was twelve and sufficiently understood the nature of vows.

### Chanina's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֵידַע אִם הִגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים, אִם לָאו. אָמַר לוֹ: רַבִּי, אַל תִּצְטַעֵר לְבוֹדְקֵנִי, אִם קָטָן אֲנִי — אֶהְיֶה בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא, אִם גָּדוֹל אֲנִי — אֶהְיֶה בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי. עָמַד רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּנְשָׁקוֹ עַל רֹאשׁוֹ, אָמַר: מוּבְטָח אֲנִי בָּזֶה שֶׁמּוֹרֶה הֲלָכָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל. אָמְרוּ: לֹא הָיוּ יָמִים מוּעָטִים עַד שֶׁהוֹרָה הוֹרָאָה בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says** that Rabban Gamliel examined him **to discern whether he had reached the age of vows or not,** which he maintains is the decisive factor. The child **said to** Rabban Gamliel: **My teacher, do not go to the trouble of examining me,** since **if I am a minor I shall be** a nazirite **due to** my **father's** vow, and **if I am an adult, I shall be** a nazirite **due to my own** vow. **Rabban Gamliel arose and kissed** the child **on his head. He said: I am certain of this** child **that** he will eventually become **an authority of *halakha* for the Jewish people. They said:** In fact, **it was only a few years** later **that** the child **issued rulings for the Jewish people.**

Before Rabban Gamliel could complete his inquiry, Chanina himself spoke up: *Rabbi, al tista'er livdok kani* — do not trouble yourself to examine me. *Im katan ani, eyei bishvil abba* — if I was a minor when my father imposed *nezirut* on me, then I am a *nazir* by virtue of my father's act. *Im gadol ani, eyei bishvil atzmi* — and if I was an adult, I hereby accept *nezirut* upon myself. Either way, I am a *nazir*. There is no need for the examination because the outcome is the same regardless.

### Gamliel's Praise

Rabban Gamliel was moved by this response. *Amar Rabban Gamliel venashko al rosho* — Rabban Gamliel kissed him on the head and declared: *muvtach ani be-zeh she-moreh halacha be-Yisrael* — I am certain this young man will grow up to render halachic decisions in Israel. The *baraita* records that it was not long before this prediction was fulfilled.

### Middle Adulthood Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים, הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר ״אִם קָטָן אֲנִי אֶהְיֶה בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא״. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת — ״וְאִם גָּדוֹל אֲנִי אֶהְיֶה בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its suggestion: **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that a father can vow on behalf of his son only **until he reaches the age of vows, this is** why the child **said: If I am a minor I shall be** a nazirite **due to** my **father,** as he means: If I have not yet reached the age of vows. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** that a father can vow naziriteship for his son **until he develops two** pubic **hairs,** despite the fact that he is already mature with regard to vows, what is the meaning of: **And if I am an adult, I shall be** a nazirite **due to my own vow?**

The Gemara now presses harder. For Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda — who holds that the cutoff is *onat nedarim* — the case is relatively straightforward. If the boy had reached the age of vow-making, the father could not have imposed *nezirut*; if he had not, the father's imposition stands. Either way, Chanina's statement works: if he was still within the father's authority, the father's *nezirut* holds; if he was independent enough to make vows himself, he can accept *nezirut* on himself at that point. The analysis proceeds fluently.

The Gemara's challenge is directed primarily at Rabbi, who holds the threshold is *shtei se'arot* — full adulthood. There are three possible scenarios for when Chanina became an adult: before the father imposed *nezirut*, after the completion of the thirty-day period, or somewhere in the middle. The first two cases are manageable. If he was already an adult when the father acted, his silent acquiescence — or perhaps his verbal acceptance — makes him a *nazir* on his own terms. If he only became an adult after the thirty days were complete, the father's *nezirut* runs its course uninterrupted.

The problem is the third scenario. It emerges — explicitly in the *Tosefta*, and implicitly from the Gemara's reasoning — that if a son becomes a full adult *in the middle* of the thirty-day period his father imposed, that transition of legal status terminates the father's *nezirut* even without any active protest. The son's transition to independent legal standing simply dissolves the earlier imposition. If so, Chanina's statement — "either way I am a *nazir*" — is not accurate for every scenario. If Chanina became an adult on, say, day ten, the period that preceded his adulthood would be invalidated, and he could not simply count backward from the start and claim he completed a full *nezirut*.

### Adult Status Interrupts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בִּרְשׁוּתֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ קָאֵים! אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר: אֱהֵא בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא, אֱהֵא בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי. אִי אַיְיתִי שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת מֵעִיקָּרָא — קָאֵים בִּנְזִירוּת דִּילֵיהּ, וּלְבַסּוֹף — קָאֵים בִּנְזִירוּת דַּאֲבוּהּ. וְאִי אַיְיתִי בְּמִצְעֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** After all, he still **remains under his father's authority** with regard to naziriteship, as he has yet to develop two pubic hairs. **Rather,** one must explain **that he said** the following: **I shall be** a nazirite **due to** my **father; I shall be** a nazirite **due to my own** vow. In other words, he did not link his statement to the question of whether he was old enough to vow but to the issue of his father's authority with regard to naziriteship, i.e., whether he had developed two hairs. The Gemara asks: According to the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **if he had developed two** pubic **hairs from the outset,** i.e., before his father vowed, he would **stand** bound **by his own naziriteship, and** if he reached physical maturity **at the end,** i.e., after the vow ended, he would **stand** bound **by his father's** vow of **naziriteship. But if he developed** two hairs **in the middle** of his naziriteship term, of **what** use would the son's vow be?

The Gemara probes the asymmetry between *onat nedarim* and full adulthood more carefully. If it is the age of vow-making that matters, the fact that a boy arrives at *onat nedarim* mid-*nezirut* does not terminate the father's imposition. The father was entitled to impose the *nezirut* at the outset; the son's newly acquired capacity to make his own vows does not retroactively disrupt an already-operative process. The son's basic legal status has not fundamentally changed. He is still a minor in the general sense; he has merely acquired a limited, specific independence in the realm of vows.

Full adulthood, however — marked by *shtei se'arot* — is categorically different. Becoming a *bar mitzvah* represents a total transformation of legal status. It is not a narrow expansion of one competency; it is the complete emergence of the person as an independent legal subject. Therefore, according to Rabbi, who holds that the threshold is *shtei se'arot*, adulthood reached in the middle of the thirty-day period would genuinely rupture the father's *nezirut*. This is what makes Chanina's statement potentially problematic under Rabbi's view.

### Dual Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ לְעוֹנַת נְדָרִים. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? אָמְרִי: לְרַבִּי לֵיכָּא תַּקַּנְתָּא עַד דְּיָתֵיב דִּילֵיהּ וְיָתֵיב דַּאֲבוּהִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, who said** that a father can vow on behalf of his son **until he reaches the age of vows.** The reason is that this stage is by rabbinic law, and therefore the fact that the son has reached this stage does not automatically cancel the father's vow. The son would complete his term and bring his offerings. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** that a father can vow for his son only **until he develops two** pubic **hairs, what can be said?** If he grew two hairs during his father's naziriteship it is no longer in effect, as by Torah law he is no longer under his father's authority, so what is the *halakha* in that case? The Sages **say** in response: According **to** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, this child **has no rectification until he sits for his** term of naziriteship **and** also **sits for** the naziriteship **of his father,** i.e., he must be a nazirite for sixty days, to ensure that he completes a full term of thirty days, either for his own naziriteship or for that of his father.

The Gemara's resolution involves a careful rereading of what Chanina actually said. Chanina's statement was not simply binary. It can be understood as an acceptance of *nezirut* from that very moment forward, regardless of when he became an adult. The question becomes whether this simultaneous declaration can cover all scenarios. For the scenario where Chanina became an adult precisely at the end of thirty days, the father's *nezirut* is intact and complete. For the scenario where he became an adult at the very beginning, his own verbal acceptance covered the *nezirut* from the start. The difficult case — adulthood in the middle — requires that Chanina's declaration on that day be treated as an independent acceptance of *nezirut* effective from that moment, with a full thirty days running from that point. By accepting *nezirut* upon himself regardless of circumstances, Chanina ensured that *mimah nafshach* — in any event — he would emerge as a valid *nazir* who completed a full thirty-day period: either his father's from the outset, or his own from whatever point adulthood arrived.

This observation about the difference between what terminates paternal *nezirut* and what merely precluded the father from imposing it in the first place is worth underscoring. Even Rabbi, who holds that the father retains authority until *shtei se'arot*, concedes that once *shtei se'arot* arrive, the father's *nezirut* ends — even mid-process and even without any active objection from the son. It is a structural termination, not a volitional one. The son's ability to object, mentioned in an earlier *mishnah*, operates on a different axis: even as a minor, a son can terminate the father's imposition through protest. That is a separate mechanism. The present case addresses the automatic legal effect of reaching adulthood itself.

## Using Father's Funds

### Mishnah Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִישׁ מְגַלֵּחַ עַל נְזִירוּת אָבִיו, וְאֵין הָאִשָּׁה מְגַלַּחַת עַל נְזִירוּת אָבִיהָ. כֵּיצַד? מִי שֶׁהָיָה אָבִיו נָזִיר, וְהִפְרִישׁ מָעוֹת סְתוּמִים עַל נְזִירוּתוֹ וָמֵת, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲגַלֵּחַ עַל מְעוֹת אַבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A man can shave,** i.e., bring the offerings at the close of his term of naziriteship, **by** using offerings originally designated for **his father's naziriteship, but a woman cannot shave by** means of the offerings for **her father's naziriteship. How so;** how is this *halakha* applied? It applies to **one whose father was a nazirite and separated unallocated money for his naziriteship,** i.e., he did not state which coins were for which of his offerings, **and he died** before buying the animals, **and** the son **said** after his father's death: **I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that I will shave by** means of **the money** that my **father** set aside.

The next *mishnah* introduces a distinct but related topic: the ability of a son to make use of funds his father had set aside for his own *nazir* sacrifices. The rule stated is that a son may use such funds, but a daughter may not: *ha'ish megalei'ach al nezirut aviv ve'ein ha'ishah megalei'achat al nezirut aviha*.

### After Death Case

The *mishnah* presents one configuration for this law: *mi shehaya aviv nazir vehifrish ma'ot stumim al neziruto umet* — a father who was a *nazir* and set aside undesignated funds for his *nezirut* and then died. Under normal circumstances, unspecified funds dedicated for *nezirut* go to an *olah*. But if the son thereafter declares: *hareini nazir al menat she'agalei'ach al me'ot abba* — I become a *nazir* on the condition that I may use my father's set-aside funds — the *mishnah* addresses whether this stipulation is effective. The *chiddush* is that funds which would otherwise be channeled to a communal *olah* can instead be redirected to the son's own *korbanot*, provided the son explicitly stipulates this at the time he accepts *nezirut*.

### Simultaneous Nazirs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ יִפְּלוּ לִנְדָבָה, אֵין זֶה מְגַלֵּחַ עַל נְזִירוּת אָבִיו. אֵיזֶהוּ שֶׁמְּגַלֵּחַ עַל נְזִירוּת אָבִיו? מִי שֶׁהָיָה הוּא וְאָבִיו נְזִירִים, וְהִפְרִישׁ אָבִיו מָעוֹת סְתוּמִים לִנְזִירוּתוֹ וָמֵת — זֶה הוּא שֶׁמְּגַלֵּחַ עַל נְזִירוּת אָבִיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei said:** In that case **these** coins are **allocated for** communal **gift** offerings, and the son may not use them, as **this is not** the case of the *halakha* that a son **can shave by** using **his father's naziriteship.** Rather, **who is** the son **who can shave by** using **his father's naziriteship?** This is referring to a son **and his father who were** both **nazirites** during his father's lifetime, **and his father separated unallocated money for his naziriteship and died; this is the one who may shave by** using **his father's naziriteship.**

A second configuration involves a case where father and son are *nezirim* simultaneously. The father sets aside funds for his own *nezirut* and then dies mid-process. May the son use those funds for his own *korbanot*?

The text of the *mishnah* and an accompanying *baraita* present the opinions of Rabbi Yosi and others, and there is a notable textual difficulty: *Tosafot* cites a *girsa* of the *mishnah* that is the exact reverse of what appears in the standard printed text. In the *girsa* consistent with the *baraita* that the Gemara quotes, Rabbi Yosi holds that the son may use the funds only if he accepted *nezirut* after the father's death, stipulating at that moment that he would use the father's funds. If the son and father were *nezirim* simultaneously, Rabbi Yosi holds *harei elu yiplu li-nedavah* — those funds go to a communal contribution and may not be used by the son. Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda appear to hold the opposite on the simultaneous case — that when son and father are *nezirim* at the same time, the son may use the father's funds.

The intuition behind the simultaneous case is suggestive: when father and son undertake *nezirut* together, it has the character of a shared family enterprise. The funds the father set aside are not entirely his alone in some moral sense; they belong to a joint endeavor. Accordingly, when the father dies, the funds pass naturally to the son who is continuing the same communal *nezirut*. In the after-death case, by contrast, the son's *nezirut* is an entirely independent undertaking — he has no special claim to the father's funds without an explicit stipulation.

### Halacha Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה הִיא בְּנָזִיר. פְּשִׁיטָא! מַאי לְמֵימְרָא: דְּבֵן יוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אָבִיו, בַּת לָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this difference between a man and a woman? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai **with regard to a nazirite.** The Gemara asks: It is **obvious** that this is so, even without this *halakha*. **What** is the purpose **of stating** this? Is Rabbi Yoḥanan coming to say **that a son inherits** from **his father** whereas **a daughter** does **not,** and therefore only a son who inherits from his father can use his animals, but not a daughter? This is obvious, as it is stated in the Torah that a daughter does not inherit from her father if he has a son (see Numbers 27:8).

One might ask what halachic basis underlies any of this. The Gemara poses precisely this question — *mai ta'ama* — and Rabbi Yochanan responds simply: *halacha b'nazir hi*. There is no derivable source, no *pasuk*, no extension of *chinuch*. It is, as Rabbi Yochanan characteristically states in this tractate, simply a received tradition pertaining to *nezirut*. Reish Lakish, who elsewhere offers alternative reasoning, is silent here — presumably because there is no alternative to offer.

### Son-Only Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּלֵית לֵיהּ אֶלָּא בַּת. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: יוֹרְשִׁין גְּמִירִין לָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** this *halakha* is **necessary** in a case **when he has only a daughter,** who does inherit from him. **Lest you say** that **we learned** this *halakha* with regard to **heirs,** i.e., that the *halakha* is that all heirs, including a daughter, can shave by means of their father's offerings,

The Gemara then asks: is it not obvious that only a son and not a daughter is entitled to use the funds, given that a son inherits from his father and a daughter does not? The Gemara answers that this is not at all obvious. Consider a case where the deceased father left only a daughter — *de-leit lei ella barta* — and she stands to inherit all his other property. One might argue that since we learn the son's entitlement from the concept of *yerusha*, and she inherits just as he does, she should also be entitled to use the funds. The Gemara therefore must address this: *mahu de-teima yorshin gamrinan lah* — lest one say we derive her entitlement from the laws of inheritance. To this, Rabbi Yochanan's answer stands — *halacha b'nazir hi*, and the *halacha* specifies sons, not daughters. Even a daughter who would inherit all the father's ordinary property cannot use the dedicated *nazir* funds for her own *korbanot*. The *halacha* carries its own internal definition of who qualifies, independent of the general laws of *yerusha*.

This point illuminates a broader feature of *halacha l'Moshe miSinai* in this context. Not only does the *halacha* establish the basic rule that a son may use the father's funds — it also defines the precise contours of that rule: only sons, not daughters, even in a case where the daughter would be the sole heir. Without that component of the *halacha*, one would be tempted to extend the rule through reasoning from *yerusha*. The *halacha* forecloses that extension.

### Baraita Positions

The Gemara then asks whether the *rabanan* who disagree with Rabbi Yosi dispute his ruling at the first point — the case where the son accepts *nezirut* after the father died — or at the second point, the simultaneous case, or both. The *baraita* quoted in response makes Rabbi Yosi's position explicit: the son may use the funds when he stipulates this after the father's death, but not when they were *nezirim* simultaneously. Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Yehuda are cited in the *baraita* as holding that the simultaneous case also works — but the *baraita* does not make entirely clear whether they dispute the first case as well or only add the second. What does emerge is a spectrum of positions: Rabbi Yosi endorses only the after-death stipulation; others extend the permission to the simultaneous case; and whether any authority holds that the simultaneous case works while the after-death case does not remains to be resolved.

## Multiple Sons Funds

### Rava's Splitting

Rava raises a question that pushes at the limits of the *halacha*: what if a father had two sons, both of whom are *nezirim* and both of whom wish to use the father's dedicated funds? *Mahu* — what is the rule? Is this *halacha* a purely independent tradition within *nezirut* — one that operates without reference to *yerusha* — such that we say *kol dekadim galach galach*, whoever gets there first and uses the funds gets them, first come first served? Or does the *halacha*, despite its independent status, nonetheless draw upon the framework of *yerusha* in certain respects, such that the funds are divided equally between the two sons?

### Yerusha Connection

The question is whether the *halacha* is fully self-contained or whether it rides atop a *yerusha* infrastructure. If it is *yerusha*-governed in its details, then just as two sons would split the father's ordinary estate equally, each would receive an equal share of the *nezirut* funds to apply toward his *korbanot*. If it is purely independent, there is no particular reason why the funds should not simply go to whichever son acts first. The tension is meaningful: we have already seen that this *halacha* does not follow *yerusha* in every respect — daughters are excluded even when they are the only heir. But exclusion of daughters does not tell us how the funds are divided among multiple qualifying sons.

### Bechor Double Share

Rava's second question sharpens this further: what if one of the sons is a *bechor* — a firstborn son — and the other is a *pashut*, an ordinary son? In the normal laws of inheritance, the *bechor* receives a double portion. Does that double portion apply here as well? *Hilkheta gamrinan lah* — do we say this is purely a matter of *halacha* and therefore the *bechor* does not receive a double portion? Or do we say *yerusha hi* — the *yerusha* framework applies, and just as the *bechor* takes two portions of the father's ordinary estate, *hacha nami megale'ach* — so too he receives double the funds toward his *korbanot*?

### Chulin vs Hekdesh

The Gemara introduces a further distinction within the question about the *bechor*. Even if we say the *yerusha* framework applies, perhaps it applies only to *chulin* — ordinary property — and not to *hekdesh* — consecrated property. To understand this, the Rosh offers a more precise reading of the Gemara's language. He points out that the *gemara's* question about *chulin* and *hekdesh* is not a freestanding inquiry; it is a refinement of the *bechor* question specifically. According to the Rosh, the Gemara's formulation should be read as follows: even if we conclude that the *yerusha* framework governs how the funds are divided among the sons — so that each son receives a proportionate share — it might still be true that the *bechor*'s double portion does not apply, because the *bechor*'s entitlement to a double share may be categorically limited to *chulin*.

### Bechor Inapplicable

The basis for this distinction lies in the scriptural language used for *bechor* — *bechol asher yimatze lo* — the firstborn gets a double portion of all that is *found*, meaning all that is available. The term implies accessible, ordinary wealth. *Hekdesh* funds, however, are pre-designated and consecrated; they are not simply *found* in the estate as available resources. Therefore, even if the general *yerusha* framework extends to dividing *nezirut* funds between sons, the specific entitlement of the *bechor* to a double portion may not apply, because *hekdesh* property does not satisfy the criterion of *bechol asher yimatze lo*. The Rosh emphasizes that the *chulin*-*hekdesh* distinction is not a separate question the Gemara entertains alongside the *bechor* question — it is part of the *bechor* question itself, clarifying why the *bechor*'s double portion might not apply even under a *yerusha*-governed model.

### Different Nazir Types

Rava then asks: what if the father and son were different types of *nezirim*? For example, the father was a *nazir olam* — a perpetual *nazir* — while the son is a *nazir stam*, a standard thirty-day *nazir*. (A *nazir olam* does periodically bring *korbanot* — the *nezirut* of a *nazir olam* permits a haircut once every thirty days or annually, accompanied by the appropriate *korbanot* — so the dedicated funds are not without purpose.) The question is whether the *halacha* that permits the son to use the father's funds operates only when both are the same type of *nazir*, or whether it functions even across different categories of *nezirut*.

*Tosafot* notes that one could further ask whether the *halacha* applies even when both are *nazir olam* — perhaps the rule was only ever formulated for standard *nezirut*, not for *nezirut* of the *olam* variety. And even if both *nazir olam* types can access the funds, perhaps that only holds when father and son share the same type, not when one is *olam* and the other *stam*. In any event, if the different types of *nezirut* are both *nezirut tahara* — both involving purity-related sacrifices — there is an argument that the funds can be redirected, since they are fundamentally the same category of *korban*.

### Tamei-Tahor Funds

The final question is posed by Rav Ashi and concerns a more fundamental divergence. What if the father's funds were dedicated not for *nezirut tahara* but for *nezirut* in a state of *tumah* — the father had become *tamei* and set aside funds for the *korbanot* a *tamei* *nazir* must bring, which involve birds rather than the standard animals? Can the son, who is a *nazir tahor*, use those *korbanot tumah* funds for his own *korbanot tahara*? Conversely: *aviu nazir tahor vehu nazir tamei mai* — what if the father was *tahor* and the son is *tamei*?

These are not merely different *types* of *nezirut* — they represent categorically different *korbanot*, with different animals and different ritual contexts. The question is whether the *halacha* that transfers the father's dedicated funds to the son operates even across this kind of disparity. The Gemara leaves both versions of the question unresolved — *teiku*.

With that, the daf concludes: *hadran alach Mi She'amar*.
