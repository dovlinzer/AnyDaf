# Sanhedrin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avodah Zarah Intro

### Overview & Acceptance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: אֶחָד הָעוֹבֵד, וְאֶחָד הַמְזַבֵּחַ, וְאֶחָד הַמְקַטֵּר, וְאֶחָד הַמְנַסֵּךְ, וְאֶחָד הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה, וְאֶחָד הַמְקַבְּלוֹ עָלָיו לֶאֱלוֹהַּ, וְהָאוֹמֵר לוֹ ״אֵלִי אַתָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who worships idols** is executed by stoning. This includes **one who worships** an idol, **and one who slaughters** an animal as an idolatrous offering, **and one who burns** incense as an idolatrous offering, **and one who pours a libation** in idol worship, **and one who bows** to an idol, **and one who** declares that he **accepts** an idol **upon himself as a god, and one who says to** an idol: **You are my god.**

The daf begins on 61b, immediately following the *mishna*, as the Gemara turns to the topic of *avodah zarah* — one of the cardinal offenses punishable by *sekilah*. Before enumerating the classic forms of worship, the Gemara makes a striking preliminary observation: the act of accepting a foreign deity as one's god is itself considered an *avodah*. One might have supposed that this falls only under the prohibition of *lo yihiyeh lecha elohim acherim* — the general negative commandment against acknowledging other gods — but not under the specific category of *avodah*, of worship. Nevertheless, the Gemara treats it as a genuine act of *avodah zarah* in its own right.

The generic term *ovedet* — the classic or paradigmatic form of worship — denotes both what the worshippers of a given deity themselves regard as the central act of veneration, and those forms of worship that are universally recognized as classic, particularly those performed in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Five Forms of Worship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: הָכִי קָאָמַר, אֶחָד הָעוֹבֵד כְּדַרְכָּהּ, וְאֶחָד הַמְזַבֵּחַ, וְאֶחָד הַמְקַטֵּר, וְאֶחָד הַמְנַסֵּךְ, וְאֶחָד הַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה, וַאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya says** that **this** is what the mishna **is saying: One who worships** an idol **in its** typical **manner** of worship is liable to be executed; **and** furthermore, **one who slaughters** an animal as an idolatrous offering, **and one who burns** incense, **and one who pours** a libation, **and the one who bows** to an idol are all liable to be executed, **and** this applies **even** if this manner of worship is **not** the idol's typical **manner** of worship. Even if the idol in question is not typically worshipped in these ways, these specific acts are still considered idol worship because these are the ways in which God is worshipped in the Temple.

The Gemara proceeds to clarify the *mishna*'s phrase *echad ha-oved*, asking what it encompasses. Rabbi Yirmiyah explains: whether one worships *kedarkah* — in the manner specific and natural to that deity — or whether one performs any of four additional acts, even if those acts are not the natural form of worship for that particular god, one is liable. Those four acts are *mezabeiach* (slaughtering), *maktir* (burning on the altar), *menaseiach* (libation), and *mishtachaveh* (prostration), and the liability applies *afilu shelo kedarkah* — even if this is not the deity's usual mode of worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי זוֹרֵק? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: זוֹרֵק הַיְינוּ מְנַסֵּךְ, דִּכְתִיב ״בַּל אַסִּיךְ נִסְכֵּיהֶם מִדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let** the mishna **also count sprinkling** the blood of an offering, which is also one of the sacrificial rites in the Temple. **Abaye says: Sprinkling** the blood of an offering **is** the same as **pouring** a libation, since they are essentially one sacrificial rite, **as it is written: "I will not pour their libations of blood"** (Psalms 16:4), indicating that sprinkling the blood of an offering is also referred to as offering a libation.

The Gemara then notes that the act of throwing blood — *zerikat hadam* — was not separately listed. Abaye explains that this is subsumed under the category of *menaseiach*, since both involve the offering of a liquid substance upon the altar. The verse is adduced: *kol bal asich niskeihem midam* — "let me not pour their libations of blood" — confirming that the throwing of blood is included within the concept of libation. Conceptually, of course, the two are distinct: the libation is the offering of wine, while the throwing of blood is the climactic act of the animal sacrifice. Nevertheless, for purposes of this halachic enumeration, they are grouped together.

### Source & Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״זֹבֵחַ יׇחֳרָם״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר בְּזוֹבֵחַ קֳדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לָאֱלֹהִים״ – בְּזוֹבֵחַ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks with regard to the *halakha* that one is liable for worshipping an idol in these manners even if they are not the way the idol is typically worshipped: **From where are these matters** derived? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "One who sacrifices to the gods shall be utterly destroyed, except to the Lord alone" (Exodus 22:19): **If it were stated: One who sacrifices shall be utterly destroyed, I would have said** that **the verse speaks of one who slaughters a sacrificial** animal **outside** the Temple courtyard, referring to a severe transgression that is stated elsewhere in the Torah. Therefore, **the verse states: "To the gods,"** indicating that **the verse speaks of one who slaughters** an offering **to an idol.**

The Gemara then asks: *minayin milei* — what is the scriptural source for this entire framework? A *baraita* is cited. The verse states *zoveach le'elohim yecharam bilti l'Hashem levado* — "one who slaughters to other gods shall be put under the ban, except only to God alone" (Shemot 22:19). The *baraita* proceeds through a careful derivation.

Had the verse said only *zoveach yecharam* — "one who slaughters shall be put under the ban" — one might have supposed it was referring merely to one who slaughters *kodshim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*. The addition of *le'elohim* clarifies that the verse is speaking of one who slaughters to foreign gods — *avodah zarah*. Thus the base case is established: slaughtering to a foreign deity is prohibited and punishable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּזוֹבֵחַ, מְקַטֵּר וּמְנַסֵּךְ מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בִּלְתִּי לַה׳ לְבַדּוֹ״. רִיקֵּן הָעֲבוֹדוֹת כּוּלָּן לַשֵּׁם הַמְיוּחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: From here **I** know **only** the *halakha* of **one who slaughters** an offering to an idol. **From where** is it derived that one who **burns** incense **or pours** a libation is also liable to receive the death penalty? The *baraita* answers that **the verse states: "Except to the Lord alone";** the verse **emptied out,** i.e., designated, **all the** sacrificial **rites to the ineffable name** of God, indicating that one who performs any of these rites in idol worship is liable.

But the verse goes on to say *bilti l'Hashem levado*. The *baraita* reads this phrase as a sweeping exclusivity clause: all acts of worship have been emptied out — reserved exclusively — for God. This phrase extends the prohibition beyond *zevichah* alone to include *maktir* (burning the fats), *menaseiach* (libation), and — through the inclusive force of the phrase — the throwing of blood as well. Rashi notes that the classic *avodot* performed in the *Beit HaMikdash* are *shechitah*, *kabbalat hadam*, *holachat hadam*, and *zerikat hadam*. One might ask why *kabbalah* and *holachah* are not included in the *baraita*'s enumeration. Rashi explains that no verse elsewhere in *Tanach* identifies *kabbalah* or *holachah* as acts of *avodah zarah*, whereas the other forms — slaughtering, burning, libation, and blood-throwing — do appear in other scriptural contexts as recognized modes of foreign worship. Rashi's formulation is thus an intersection: those acts that are classic to the *Beit HaMikdash* and that also appear in verses as recognized forms of *avodah zarah* are the ones that carry liability. As for *holachah* — even within the *Beit HaMikdash* context, it functions more as a preparatory step than as an act of worship in its own right, and accordingly it carries only a *lav* but not the death penalty.

The extension to *nisuach* through outside verses fits naturally with the broader halachic world of *stam yeinam* and *yayin nesech*, where libation to a foreign deity is a well-established category. Rashi characterizes this set as an intersection of what is classic *avodat pnim* and what appears in verses as classic *avodah zarah* — and once included, these acts carry the full prohibition even when performed for a deity to which this particular form of worship is not customary.

## Paradigm Debate

### Counterfactual Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁיָּצְאָה זְבִיחָה לִידּוֹן בַּעֲבוֹדוֹת פְּנִים, מִנַּיִין לְרַבּוֹת הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **Since slaughter is singled out** from all of the forms of worship to be mentioned in this verse in order **to derive** from it that **with regard to** all the sacrificial **rites** performed **inside** the Temple, one who performs them in idol worship is liable to receive capital punishment, **from where** is it derived that this *halakha* **includes bowing** down to an idol, which is not a sacrificial rite?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ וַיַּעֲבֹד אֱלֹהִים אֲחֵרִים וַיִּשְׁתַּחוּ לָהֶם״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״וְהוֹצֵאתָ אֶת הָאִישׁ הַהוּא וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers that **the verse states: "And has gone and served other gods and bowed to them"** (Deuteronomy 17:3), **and** a verse **near it** states: **"Then you shall bring forth that man** or that woman who has done this evil thing to your gates, the man or the woman, and you shall stone them with stones and they shall die" (Deuteronomy 17:5). It is derived from the juxtaposition of these two verses that one who bows down to an idol is liable to be executed.

Having established the basic framework, the Gemara now poses a sustained counterfactual: why is *zevichah* — slaughtering — the paradigm that defines the general category, with *hishtachavah* treated as a separate special case? Why not reverse the structure and make *hishtachavah* the general category?

The Gemara first explains how *hishtachavah* enters the picture at all. The verse from Devarim — *vayelech vaya'avod elohim acherim vayishtachu lahem* — "he went and worshipped other gods and prostrated to them" — establishes that *hishtachavah* is an act of *avodah zarah*. Unlike slaughtering, burning, or libation, prostration is not a formal act of worship within the *Beit HaMikdash* in the same technical halachic sense, yet it is universally recognized as a form of reverence for the divine. The subsequent verse in that passage — *ve'hotzeisa es ha'ish hahu ... el she'arecha* — specifying execution — tells us that prostrating before a foreign deity incurs the death penalty.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבִיחָה בַּכְּלָל הָיְתָה, וְלָמָּה יָצְאָה? לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ וְלוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה זְבִיחָה מְיוּחֶדֶת שֶׁהִיא עֲבוֹדַת פְּנִים וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהִיא עֲבוֹדַת פְּנִים וְחַיָּיבִין עָלֶיהָ מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains: The **slaughter** of an offering **was included** in the general category of the prohibited forms of idol worship, **and why was it singled out** to be mentioned in this verse? It was singled out **to compare** a matter **to it and say to you: Just as slaughter is notable** in **that it is** a sacrificial **rite** performed **inside** the Temple, **and** those who perform this rite for the purpose of idol worship **are liable** to receive **the death penalty for it, so too, any** form of worship **that is** a sacrificial **rite** performed **inside** the Temple is prohibited when performed for idol worship, **and** transgressors **are liable** to receive **the death penalty for it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאָה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה לִידּוֹן בְּעַצְמָהּ, יָצְאָה זְבִיחָה לִידּוֹן עַל הַכְּלָל כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, the act of **bowing** to an idol **is singled out** to be mentioned in a separate verse **to derive** the death penalty for that action **itself,** despite the fact that it is not a sacrificial rite performed in the Temple, whereas the **slaughter** of an offering **is singled out** to be mentioned in order **to derive** from it the principle **with regard to the entire category,** namely, that generally one is not liable to receive capital punishment for a form of worship that is not performed in the Temple.

The *baraita* then frames the structural principle: *zevichah*, it says, was among the general class of worships, and it was singled out — *yatzas* — to become the paradigm, *liddon al haklal*, to define the set. The singling-out is purposive: just as *zevichah* is an *avodat pnim* — an act of worship within the *Beit HaMikdash* — and carries the death penalty, so too any act that shares those characteristics is included in the set defined by the paradigm. Forms of adoration such as hugging, kissing, or putting on the shoes of an idol are thereby excluded — they are not *avodat pnim* and the verse *zoveach* limits the class to acts with that character.

*Hishtachavah*, by contrast, exits to stand alone — *yatzas hishtachavah liddon b'atzmah* — as a separately enumerated exception. And then there is the third category: *avodat kedarkah*, the specific form of worship particular to that deity, derived from the verse *eicha ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem* — "how do these nations worship their gods?" — which extends liability to the unique worship of each particular idol even when it resembles none of the above.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר מָר: הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר בְּזוֹבֵחַ קֳדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. זוֹבֵחַ קֳדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ כָּרֵת הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds to clarify the *baraita*. **The Master said** in the *baraita*: **I would have said** that **the verse speaks of one who slaughters a sacrificial** animal **outside** the Temple courtyard. The Gemara asks: The punishment for **one who slaughters a sacrificial** animal **outside** the Temple courtyard **is *karet*,** not capital punishment, as it is stated: "Each and every man…who offers a burnt-offering or sacrifice, and does not bring it to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting to sacrifice it to the Lord, even that man shall be cut off from his people" (Leviticus 17:8–9). Therefore, how can the *baraita* suggest that a verse that mentions capital punishment could be referring to this transgression?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כִּי אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ – קְטָלָא, כִּי לָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ – כָּרֵת. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It might **enter your mind to say** that in a case **when** the witnesses **warned** the transgressor not to slaughter the offering outside the Temple he is liable to receive **the death** penalty, but in a case **when they did not warn him** he is liable to receive ***karet*.** Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that one who slaughters an offering outside the Temple is not liable to receive the death penalty even if he was warned.

Now Rabba bar Rav Chanan poses the question to Abaye directly: why not invert the structure? Let *hishtachavah* define the general class — since it is broad and non-specific, encompassing any act of respectful reverence — and let *zevichah* be the exceptional case, singled out to teach something specific about the act of slaughtering alone. If *hishtachavah* were the general class, the class would include every form of adoration, making the set much larger.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי: אֵימָא, יָצְאָה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה לְלַמֵּד עַל הַכְּלָל כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states that the principle concerning the forms of idol worship that are punishable by execution is derived from the verse that mentions slaughtering an animal as an idolatrous offering, whereas bowing to an idol is mentioned in the verse as an exception. **Rava bar Rav Ḥanan said to Abaye: Say** that **bowing is singled out** to be mentioned **to teach, with regard to the entire category,** that any honorable form of worship, when performed for the sake of idol worship, carries the death penalty, not only sacrificial rites performed in the Temple.

### Machshavim Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: זוֹבֵחַ לְמָה לִי? לְגוּפֵיהּ, דִּמְחַשְּׁבִין מֵעֲבוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that if so, **why do I** need **slaughter** to be mentioned in the verse specifically, one can say that mentioning slaughter is necessary **for** the matter of slaughter **itself,** i.e., to teach **that one can have intention from** one sacrificial **rite to** another sacrificial **rite.** In other words, slaughtering an animal with idolatrous intent is punishable by death even if the intention of the transgressor pertains not to the slaughter itself but to another part of the sacrificial process.

The Gemara considers what *zevichah* would then be needed to teach, if *hishtachavah* were already the general paradigm. The answer proposed is the principle of *machshavim me'avodah le'avodah* — the case where one slaughters an animal not as the act of worship itself, but with the intent to use the blood afterward as the act of worship. In other words: one slaughters an animal for the purpose of throwing its blood before a foreign deity, where the *zevichah* is preparatory and the *zerikah* is the intended act of worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט בְּהֵמָה לִזְרוֹק דָּמָהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה וּלְהַקְטִיר חֶלְבָּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: מוּתֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was stated** that with regard to **one who slaughters an animal** in order **to sprinkle its blood in idol worship or to burn its fat in idol worship, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Deriving benefit from the animal is **prohibited,** as it is considered an offering of idol worship, even if its blood was not ultimately sprinkled for idol worship or its forbidden fat burned for that purpose. **And Reish Lakish says:** Deriving benefit from the animal is **permitted.**

This scenario has a direct parallel in the laws of *kodshim*: one who slaughters an animal with intent to perform an act — such as throwing the blood — at the wrong time renders the sacrifice *pigul*. Does the same logic apply to *avodah zarah*? If one slaughters an animal for the purpose of using its blood in *avodah zarah*, is the animal thereby forbidden in benefit as an animal offered to a foreign deity?

Rabbi Yochanan says the animal is *assur* — forbidden — applying the same logic as *kodshim*: since the slaughter was performed with intent directed toward *avodah zarah*, the animal acquires the status of an offering to *avodah zarah*. Reish Lakish, however, says the animal is *mutar* — permitted — because the act of slaughter was not itself an act of worship; no worship was yet performed on the animal directly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֶלָּא לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בָּעֵי קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan concludes his analysis: **According to Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who derives this *halakha* from another source, **this** statement in the *baraita* **works out well.** The verse that mentions the slaughter of an animal in idol worship apparently teaches a principle, as it is superfluous with regard to the *halakha* of slaughter itself, as stated in the *baraita*. **But according to Reish Lakish,** who does not derive from any verse that one can have intention from one rite to another rite, **one needs** this **verse** to derive precisely this *halakha*, that one can have intention from one rite to another rite. Consequently, the verse is not superfluous, and it cannot be assumed that it teaches a principle with regard to the forms of idol worship that are punishable by death.

Tosafot adds a pointed remark: even according to Reish Lakish, who permits the animal, it remains forbidden for consumption — not because of its status as an offering, but because the person performing the slaughter was, in the course of doing so, engaged in *avodah zarah*, rendering his *shechitah* halachically invalid. Tosafot then entertains a further subtlety: perhaps the person only became an *apostate* through the very act of this *shechitah*, meaning he was not yet a *mumar* at the moment he began — *bishchitah zu na'aseh mumar* — in which case perhaps even eating might be permitted, since the *shechitah* was valid at its inception.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: וּלְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא בָּעֵי קְרָא? עַד כָּאן לָא קָא אָסַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אֶלָּא בְּהֵמָה, אֲבָל גַּבְרָא לָא בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! וַאֲתָא קְרָא לְחַיּוֹבֵי גַּבְרָא לִקְטָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: And according to Rabbi Yoḥanan, does one not need a verse** to teach the *halakha* in a case of intention from one rite to another rite? **Rabbi Yoḥanan** initially **only prohibits** deriving benefit from the **animal; but** if not for this verse, **the man** who slaughtered the animal would **not be liable** to receive **the death** penalty. **And** therefore **the verse comes to render the man liable** to receive **the death** penalty. Therefore this verse is not superfluous according to Rabbi Yoḥanan either.

Returning to the structural question: if *hishtachavah* is the general paradigm, then the specific word *zoveach* in the verse needs to teach something — and according to Rabbi Yochanan, it need not teach *machshavim me'avodah le'avodah*, since he derives that principle from *kodshim* by analogy. The word *zoveach* would therefore be extra and without purpose under the counterfactual arrangement. This is exactly what the Gemara needs: if *zoveach* would be superfluous when *hishtachavah* is the paradigm, that proves the arrangement cannot work — and so the original structure, with *zevichah* as the paradigm, is confirmed.

But the Gemara presses: Rav Papa challenges that Rabbi Yochanan also needs the word *zoveach* — not to establish that the animal is forbidden (which he derives from *kodshim*), but to establish that the *person* is liable for the death penalty. The animal's status and the person's liability are separate questions. The verse *zoveach* may be needed to teach that even in this preparatory-slaughter scenario, the person has performed an act of *avodah zarah* and is *chayav misa*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: וּלְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, מִי בָּעֵי קְרָא? עַד כָּאן לָא קָא שָׁרֵי רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אֶלָּא בְּהֵמָה, אֲבָל גַּבְרָא בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמִּשְׁתַּחֲוֶה לְהַר, דְּהַר מוּתָּר וְעוֹבְדוֹ בְּסַיִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, objects to** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan's challenge to the derivation in the *baraita*: **And** even **according to Reish Lakish, does one need a verse** to teach that the one who slaughtered the animal is liable? **Reish Lakish only permits** one to derive benefit from the **animal; but the man is** certainly **liable to** receive **the death** penalty. This is **just as** the *halakha* **is** in the case of **one who bows to a mountain, as** deriving benefit from the **mountain is permitted, but** nevertheless **the one who worshipped it** is liable to be executed **by** the **sword.** Therefore, the verse that states that one who slaughters an offering in idol worship is liable to be executed is superfluous with regard to the issue of intention from one rite to another.

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika then pushes in the opposite direction: perhaps the person's liability is obvious even without a verse — from the case of one who prostrates before a mountain. The mountain itself is not rendered forbidden; a mountain cannot become prohibited, as we learn from the verse *eloheihem al he'harim v'lo he'harim eloheihem* — "their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains do not become their gods." Yet the person who worships the mountain is executed, as the verse makes clear. So the principle that an act can constitute *avodah zarah* even when the object itself is not rendered forbidden is established without need of an additional verse. The status of the object and the liability of the person are categorically independent. In that case, Reish Lakish too might not need the word *zoveach* for this purpose, making it genuinely superfluous.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: לְמַאי דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי, אֵימָא יָצְאָה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה לְלַמֵּד עַל הַכְּלָל כּוּלּוֹ, ״אֵיכָה יַעַבְדוּ״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: According to what Rava bar Rav Ḥanan said to Abaye,** that there is room to **say** that **bowing was singled out** in the verse **to teach, with regard to the entire category,** that any honorable form of worship carries the death penalty, there is a difficulty. With regard to the verse: "Take heed to yourself that you not be ensnared to follow them…saying, **how do** these nations **serve** their gods, so I will do likewise" (Deuteronomy 12:30), which indicates that one is liable for worshipping an idol in the manner that the gentiles worship it, this is stated **to exclude what?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לְמַעוֹטֵי הַפּוֹעֵר עַצְמוֹ לְזוֹבְחִים? מֵהִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה נָפְקָא! מָה הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה דֶּרֶךְ כִּיבּוּד, אַף כֹּל דֶּרֶךְ כִּיבּוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that this verse is stated **to exclude one who defecates before** an idol that is worshipped by **sacrificing** offerings, it is not necessary to derive this *halakha* from this verse, as **it is derived from** the *halakha* of **bowing** to an idol, as follows: **Just as bowing is an honorable** form of worship and is punishable by death, **so too, any honorable** form of worship is punishable by death when performed as idol worship, which excludes an offensive act such as defecating. Therefore, the *halakha* that one is not liable for defecating before an idol can be derived from bowing.

The Gemara then returns to the question of what would have been done with the verse *eicha ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem* had *hishtachavah* been the general paradigm. In the actual structure, this verse serves to include the *avodat kedarkah* — the unique, natural form of worship particular to each deity. But if *hishtachavah* already served as the all-encompassing general class, what would this verse need to exclude?

The Gemara considers: perhaps the verse would exclude acts of worship that are degrading rather than honorable — for example, defecating before an ordinary idol that is normally worshipped through sacrifice. This is analogous to *poreh atzmo le'Peor*, the act of defecating before Ba'al Peor, which is indeed the natural mode of worship for that deity. But what if someone performed that act before an idol that is not Ba'al Peor?

The Gemara responds that this exclusion would be unnecessary — it already flows from the nature of *hishtachavah* itself. *Hishtachavah* as a class encompasses only *derech kavod* — acts performed in a manner of honor. By extension, *af kol derech kavod* — any act performed in a dignified, reverential manner — would be included, and acts of degradation would be automatically excluded. The verse *eicha ya'avdu* would then be redundant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לְמַעוֹטֵי הַפּוֹעֵר עַצְמוֹ לְמַרְקוּלִיס. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וַעֲבוֹדָתוֹ בִּזָּיוֹן הוּא, אַף כֹּל בִּזָּיוֹן? קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse is stated **to exclude one who defecates before** the idol called **Mercury,** which is not its typical manner of worship. It might **enter your mind to say** that **since** the standard manner of **worship** of Mercury, namely, throwing stones at it, **is** in any event an act of **degradation, so too,** one who performs **any** act of **degradation** as worship of Mercury should be liable. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that one is liable only for worshiping Mercury in its standard manner of worship.

The Gemara therefore revises the question: perhaps the verse *eicha ya'avdu* is needed instead to exclude *poreh atzmo le'Markolis*. Markolis is a deity worshipped by the throwing of stones — itself an act that has an element of degradation to it. One might reason that since Markolis too is worshipped through an act that involves a kind of dishonor, perhaps any degrading act performed before any deity would be included. The verse *eicha ya'avdu* comes to exclude this: only acts that constitute *derech kavod* are included in the general category, and an act of degradation before a deity not customarily worshipped that way is not included, even if such acts are done before *other* deities.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מִנַּיִן לַזּוֹבֵחַ בְּהֵמָה לְמַרְקוּלִיס שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא יִזְבְּחוּ עוֹד אֶת זִבְחֵיהֶם לַשְּׂעִירִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **But** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan's interpretation of the verses appears to contradict **that which Rabbi Eliezer says: From where** is it derived **that** one who **sacrifices an animal to Mercury is liable? As it is stated: "And they shall no longer sacrifice their sacrifices to the satyrs"** (Leviticus 17:7).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לִכְדַרְכָּהּ, דִּכְתִיב ״אֵיכָה יַעַבְדוּ״, תְּנֵיהוּ עִנְיָן לְשֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer explains: **If** the verse **is not** needed for the **matter of** one who worships an idol **in its** typical **manner,** i.e., one who sacrifices an animal as an offering to an idol that is worshipped by sacrificing offerings, **as** the *halakha* in this case **is** already **written** in the aforementioned verse: **"How do** these nations **serve** their gods, so I will do likewise," **apply it to** the **matter** of one who worships an idol **not in its** typical **manner,** by sacrificing an offering to an idol that is not typically worshipped by sacrificing offerings. Therefore, one who sacrifices an offering to Mercury is liable, even though that is not its typical form of worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ, מֵהִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** According to Rava bar Rav Ḥanan, by contrast, **it is derived** that one is liable for worshipping an idol in any honorable manner, even **not in its** typical **manner** of worship, **from** the *halakha* of **bowing.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, בְּזוֹבֵחַ לְהַכְעִיס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the verse mentioned by Rabbi Eliezer, the reference is **to one who sacrifices** an offering to Mercury not as a form of worship, but rather **in order to express insolence** toward the Torah prohibition against sacrificing an offering to an idol. It is derived from the verse that this too is a transgression of the prohibition and renders one liable for the death penalty.

At this point, Rabbi Eliezer's teaching is introduced: how do we know that one who slaughters an animal *le'Markolis* — to a deity worshipped through stone-throwing, not through sacrifice — is liable? The verse *ve'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* — "they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices" — which serves as the prohibition, and *zoveach le'elohim yecharam* as the source for the death penalty. The phrase *eicha ya'avdu* establishes the general prohibition, and where it does not naturally apply — i.e., where this is not the natural form of worship for that particular deity — these other verses fill in the liability. The interplay between the general class of *zevichah*, the special case of *hishtachavah*, and the *avodat kedarkah* derived from *eicha ya'avdu* produces the complete and internally consistent structure. This is what would have been lost had *hishtachavah* been made the paradigm.

## Liability Threshold

### Rabba's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הַמְנוּנָא אִירְכַסוּ לֵיהּ תּוֹרֵי. פְּגַע בֵּיהּ רַבָּה, רְמָא לֵיהּ מַתְנְיָתִין אַהֲדָדֵי: תְּנַן ״הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה״ – עוֹבֵד אִין, אוֹמֵר לָא. וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: ״הָאוֹמֵר ׳אֶעֱבוֹד׳, ׳אֵלֵךְ וְאֶעֱבוֹד׳, ׳נֵלֵךְ וְנַעֲבוֹד׳״?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rav Hamnuna lost his oxen** and went to search for them. **Rabba met him** and **raised** a contradiction **between** two ***mishnayot*. We learned** in the mishna that **one who worships idols** is liable. By inference, one who **worships** idols, **yes,** he is liable, but one who merely **says** that he will worship idols is **not** liable. **But didn't we learn** in another mishna (67a): **One who says: I will worship** an idol, or: **I will go and worship** an idol, or: **Let us go and worship** an idol, is liable, just as one who actually worships an idol is liable. Evidently, one is liable for merely stating his intention to engage in idol worship.

A new sugya opens with a classic *tzarich iyun*: Rabba bar Rav Chanan raises an apparent contradiction between two *mishnayot*. The first *mishna* states that one is liable for *avodah zarah* only when one actually performs the worship — *oved in*, one who worships, yes, but one who merely says he intends to worship, no. Yet a second *mishna* later in the tractate states that one who says *e'evod* — "I will worship" — or *elech ve'e'evod* — "I will go and worship" — or *nelech vene'evod* — "let us go and worship" — is already liable, even before performing any act of worship. The two *mishnayot* appear to be in direct tension.

### Acceptance Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי מְקַבְּלוֹ עָלַי אֶלָּא בַּעֲבוֹדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Hamnuna **said to him:** The mishna here is referring **to one who states: I** intend to **accept** this idol **upon myself** as a god **only through worship.** Consequently, he is liable only when he actually worships it. One who intends to accept the idol as a god immediately is liable even before he actually worships it.

Before turning to the proposed resolutions, it is worth noting the background principle invoked by Tosafot. The Gemara elsewhere states that *machshavah tovah hakadosh baruch hu metzarfah lema'aseh* — God counts a good intention as equivalent to the deed when circumstances prevent its fulfillment — but *machshavah ra'ah ein hakadosh baruch hu metzarfah lema'aseh* — He does not count an evil intention as equivalent to the deed. The single exception to this rule is *avodah zarah*. Tosafot accordingly explains the second *mishna* by appeal to the principle that accepting a foreign god as one's god is itself an act of *avodah zarah* — the same principle introduced at the very outset of the Gemara. When a person, in response to incitement, says "I will go and worship," he is not merely expressing a future plan; he is, through that verbal commitment, accepting that deity as his god — and that acceptance is an independent act of *avodah zarah*, independent of any subsequent act of worship.

The first *mishna*, then, must be read as describing a case where the person specifically makes clear that he does not accept the deity as his god until the moment he actually performs the worship — *eini mekabla alai ela ba'avodah*. In that case, no acceptance occurs at the verbal stage, and no liability arises until the worship is performed.

### Rav Yosef's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: תַּנָּאֵי שָׁקְלַתְּ מֵעָלְמָא? תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״בּוֹאוּ וְעִבְדוּנִי״ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** This answer is unnecessary; **have you removed the *tanna'im* from the world? It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says:** I am a god; **come and worship me, Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable** for incitement to idol worship, **and Rabbi Yehuda deems** him **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דִּפְלַחוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – לָא פְּלִיגִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה לְךָ פֶסֶל״. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּדִיבּוּרָא בְּעָלְמָא. רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: דִּיבּוּרָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: דִּיבּוּרָא לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: In a case **where** those to whom the inciter spoke subsequently **worshiped** him, **everyone agrees** that the inciter is liable, **as it is written: "You shall not make yourself a graven image"** (Exodus 20:4), which is interpreted to include a case where one establishes himself as a god. **When they disagree** is in a case where the incident ended **in mere speech. Rabbi Meir holds** that **speech is** a significant **matter,** and therefore the inciter is liable for incitement, **and Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **speech is nothing.** Therefore, the contradiction between the *mishnayot* can be resolved by attributing each mishna to a different *tanna*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא דַּאֲמַרִי, דַּאֲפִילּוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּדִיבּוּרָא נָמֵי חַיּוֹבֵי מְחַיֵּיב. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״אֶעֱבוֹד״, ״אֵלֵךְ וְאֶעֱבוֹד״, ״נֵלֵךְ וְנַעֲבוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef then said** after reconsidering the matter: **What I said is not correct, as even according to Rabbi Yehuda, one is deemed liable for speech as well. As we learned** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Actually,** one **is not liable unless he says: I will worship,** or: **I will go and worship,** or: **Let us go and worship.** Evidently, Rabbi Yehuda holds that one is liable even for speech alone, not only for actual worship.

Rav Yosef offers a different resolution: the two *mishnayot* reflect a disagreement among *Tannaim*, not two aspects of a single consistent system. One *mishna* holds that a verbal commitment already constitutes liability; the other holds it does not. This is a *machlokes* between the *mishnayot*.

Rav Yosef then appends a further argument: the distinction he initially proposed between an individual being incited (*yachid hanisas*) and a group being incited (*rabbim hanisatin*) is the basis for the difference. When an individual is incited and says yes, we assume he will follow through — *lo mimlach veta'ei basrei* — and therefore he is already liable from the moment of his verbal agreement. When masses are incited, the outcome is less certain — *mimlachi velo ta'u basrei* — the group dynamic may produce second thoughts, and it is possible that despite their initial assent the masses will not ultimately follow through. Therefore the masses are not liable until actual worship occurs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַתְנְיָתִין: כָּאן בְּיָחִיד הַנִּיסָּת, כָּאן בְּרַבִּים הַנִּיסָּתִים. יָחִיד – לָא מִימְּלַךְ, וְטָעֵי בָּתְרֵיהּ; רַבִּים – מִימַּלְכִי, וְלָא טָעוּ בָּתְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the contradiction between the ***mishnayot*** can be resolved as follows: **There,** where the mishna states that one is liable for speech alone, the reference is **to an individual** who was **incited; here,** where the mishna indicates that one is not liable for speech alone, it is referring **to** a case of **an incited multitude** of people. The Gemara explains: **An individual** who was incited **does not** typically **change his mind, and he goes astray after** idol worship. Therefore, once he agrees to the suggestion to worship an idol, he has fully accepted the idol upon himself as a god and is liable. By contrast, **a multitude** of people are apt to **change their minds, and** consequently they **do not go astray after** idol worship.

Rav Yosef grounds this distinction in the verse *lo tove lo velo tishma eilav* — the prohibition in Devarim commanding the person being incited not to consent and not to listen to the inciter. The inference is: *ha-avah v'shama — chayav* — if one did consent and did listen, one is already liable. This verse, Tosafot notes, marks a conceptual shift: the liability of the person who says yes in the context of incitement derives not merely from the acceptance of a foreign deity as a god, but from the unique halachic category of responding to a *meisit*. The verse specifically addresses the context of being incited, and within that context, verbal assent triggers liability.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא תֹאבֶה לוֹ וְלֹא תִשְׁמַע אֵלָיו״. הָא אָבָה וְשָׁמַע – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: From where do I say** that the *halakha* that one is liable for merely stating that he will worship an idol is referring to an individual who was incited? **As it is written** with regard to one who incites people to engage in idol worship: **"You shall not approve of him, nor listen to him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9), which indicates that if one **approved** of **and listened** to the inciter, stating his intention to engage in idol worship, he is **liable** even if he does not actually worship an idol.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי שָׁאנֵי בֵּין נִיסָּת דְּרַבִּים לְנִיסָּת יָחִיד? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״כִּי יְסִיתְךָ אָחִיךָ בֶן אִמֶּךָ״ – אֶחָד יָחִיד הַנִּיסָּת וְאֶחָד רַבִּים הַנִּיסָּתִים, וְהוֹצִיא הַכָּתוּב יָחִיד מִכְּלַל רַבִּים וְרַבִּים מִכְּלַל יָחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rav Yosef's opinion: **Is** there **a difference** in *halakha* **between** the cases of **an incited multitude** of people **and an individual** who was **incited? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: **"If your brother, the son of your mother,** or your son, or your daughter, or the wife of your bosom, or your neighbor who is like your own soul, **incites you** secretly, saying: Let us go and serve other gods" (Deuteronomy 13:7), that **both an individual** who was **incited and an incited multitude** of people are included in this *halakha*, **but the verse singles out the individual from the category of the multitude, and** another verse singles out **the multitude from the category of the individual.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָחִיד מִכְּלַל רַבִּים, לְהַחְמִיר עַל גּוּפוֹ וּלְהָקֵל עַל מָמוֹנוֹ. רַבִּים מִכְּלַל יָחִיד, לְהָקֵל עַל גּוּפָם וּלְהַחְמִיר עַל מָמוֹנָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Torah states separately the *halakhot* of an individual who is incited to engage in idol worship and of an entire city that is subverted to engage in idol worship in order to distinguish between the two cases, as follows: **An individual** who was incited is singled out **from the category of** a subverted **multitude** of people in order **to render** the punishment **to** an individual's **body** more **stringent.** The individual is executed by stoning, whereas the residents of an idolatrous city are executed by decapitation. **And** an individual was singled out **to render** the treatment of **the property of** an individual who was incited more **lenient,** as it is not destroyed like that of the residents of an idolatrous city. Additionally, the Torah singles out the subverted **multitude** of people **from the category of an individual** who was incited in order **to render** the punishment given **to the bodies of** the residents of an idolatrous city, decapitation, more **lenient** than that given to an individual who was incited, **and to render** the treatment **of their property** more **stringent,** as the city and the property of its residents are burned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהָא מִילְּתָא הוּא דְּשָׁאנֵי, אֲבָל בְּכֹל מִילֵּי כִּי הֲדָדֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye concluded his objection to Rav Yosef's opinion: It can be inferred from the *baraita* that only **with regard to this matter,** i.e., the *halakhot* mentioned in the *baraita*, **is** the *halakha* of an individual who was incited **different** than that of an incited multitude of people, **but with regard to all** other halakhic **matters they are the same.** Therefore, a distinction cannot be made between them with regard to the *halakha* in the case of a verbal commitment to idol worship.

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef's distinction between the individual and the masses. He cites a *baraita* that discusses the verse *ki yesitcha achicha ben imecha* — "when your brother, your mother's son, incites you" — and concludes that the verse applies equally whether the one being incited is an individual or a group. The *baraita* does distinguish between the two, but only with respect to the severity of punishment and the treatment of property, not with respect to the stage at which liability arises. The individual's punishment is more severe on his person — *sekilah* — but his property is spared. The group's punishment is lighter on their persons — *hereg by the sword*, as in *ir hanidachat* — but their property is destroyed. For all other matters, Abaye argues, individual and group are governed by the same rules. The difference Rav Yosef proposed — that the individual is liable upon verbal assent while the group is not — has no basis in this *baraita*.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כָּאן – בְּנִיסָּת מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ, כָּאן – בְּנִיסָּת מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים. מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ – מִימְּלֵךְ, מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים – גְּרִיר בָּתְרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the contradiction between the *mishnayot* is to be resolved as follows: **Here,** where the mishna indicates that one is liable only for actual worship, the reference is **to** one who is **incited by himself,** i.e., no one incited him to idol worship and he made the decision on his own. Whereas **there,** in the mishna that deems one liable for stating that he will worship an idol, the reference is **to** one who is **incited by others.** The reason for the difference is that one who makes the decision **on his own** is apt to **change his mind,** whereas one who is incited **by others is drawn after them** and is unlikely to change his mind.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תֹאבֶה לוֹ וְלֹא תִשְׁמַע אֵלָיו״. הָא אָבָה וְשָׁמַע – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: From where do I say** that this distinction is correct? **As it is written: "You shall not approve of him, nor listen to him"** (Deuteronomy 13:9), referring to another individual who tried to incite him, and the verse indicates that if one **approved** of **and listened** to the inciter, he is **liable** even for the approval alone.

Rava offers yet another resolution, one that does not depend on the individual-versus-masses distinction. He maintains that both *mishnayot* could be dealing with a case where one is incited by others. The difference lies in the quality of the incitement itself. If the inciter has made an elaborate and persuasive case — *kach ocheles kach shosah kach meitivah kach meriah* — "this is how it eats, this is how it drinks, this is how it rewards its followers, this is how it punishes its enemies" — then when the person says yes, we assume that the persuasion was effective and the commitment is real. In that case, the verbal assent triggers immediate liability. But if the incitement was perfunctory — "let's go worship" — with no elaborate sell job, then the yes is shallow and we do not assume the person will follow through. In that case, liability requires actual worship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּנִיסָּת מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים, הָא דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ ״כָּךְ אוֹכֶלֶת״, ״כָּךְ שׁוֹתָה״, ״כָּךְ מְטִיבָה״, ״כָּךְ מְרֵיעָה״, הָא דְּלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״כָּךְ אוֹכֶלֶת״, ״כָּךְ שׁוֹתָה״ כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** Both **this** mishna **and that** mishna are referring **to one incited by others,** and they should be differentiated as follows: **That** mishna, which deems one liable for merely expressing approval, is referring to a case **where** the inciter described the qualities of the idol and **said to him: It eats like this; it drinks like this; it does good** for its worshippers **like this;** and **it harms** those who do not worship it **like this.** In this case, expressing approval verbally suffices to render one liable, as he was evidently convinced by the description. **This** mishna, which indicates that one is not liable for speech alone, is referring to a case **where** the inciter **did not say to him: It eats like this; it, drinks like this,** it does good for its worshippers like this; and it harms those who do not worship it like this.

Rava derives this from the verse *me'elohei ha'amim asher sevivoseichem ha'kerovim eilecha o ha'rechokim mimecha* — the verse warns against being drawn to the gods of surrounding nations, whether near or far. Rava reads the reference to near and far as indicating the form the incitement takes: the inciter will tell you to ignore the gods you know locally — which you can see are powerless — and will entice you with the allure of foreign gods you have never encountered. The very structure of the verse presupposes a sustained rhetorical effort. This confirms that the context of *lo tove lo velo tishma eilav* is precisely such an elaborate incitement — and therefore the liability that triggers upon saying yes applies specifically in that context.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״מֵאֱלֹהֵי הָעַמִּים אֲשֶׁר סְבִיבֹתֵיכֶם הַקְּרֹבִים אֵלֶיךָ וְגוֹ׳״. מָה לִי קְרוֹבִים וּמָה לִי רְחוֹקִים? הֲכִי קָאָמַר לָךְ: מִטִּיבוּתָן שֶׁל קְרוֹבִים אַתָּה לָמֵד מָה טִיבוּתָן שֶׁל רְחוֹקִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where do I say** that this distinction is correct? **As it is written:** "Let us go and worship other gods…**from the gods of the peoples that surround you, the ones near to you** or the ones far from you" (Deuteronomy 13:7–8). **What** difference is there **to me** whether they are **near, and what** difference is there **to me** whether they are **far?** Why would the distance affect the prohibition? Rather, **this** is what the Torah **is saying to you:** Do not be tempted to listen to the inciter, as **from the nature of** the objects of idol worship that are **near** you, which you recognize to be false, **you can derive what the nature** is **of** the ones that are **far** from you. Therefore, if you are told that there is an idol in a distant land that is real, realize that it is a lie.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ אוֹכֶלֶת, כָּךְ שׁוֹתֶה, כָּךְ מִטִּיבָהּ, כָּךְ מְרֵיעָה? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is from this interpretation that Rava derives his halakhic distinction: **What,** is it **not** referring to a case **where** the inciter described to another the qualities of the idol and **said to him: It eats like this; it drinks like this; it does good** for its worshippers **like this;** and **it harms** those who do not worship it **like this? Conclude from it** that only in such a case is the incited person liable for stating his approval.

### Rav Ashi's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: סֵיפָא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says** that there is a different resolution to the contradiction between the *mishnayot*: **The latter clause** of the mishna (67a), which deems one liable for speech alone, is referring **to an apostate Jew;** since he is already an apostate, his stated commitment to idol worship is certainly final. Consequently, he is liable. By contrast, a regular Jew is not liable for speech alone.

Rav Ashi resolves the contradiction differently. The second *mishna*, in which verbal assent suffices for liability, is referring specifically to a Jew who is already an apostate — already established as *oved avodah zarah*. For such a person, saying yes means yes; there is no reason to assume a subsequent change of heart. But for any other person, even after a full and compelling incitement, verbal assent alone does not constitute liability. Actual worship is required. The first *mishna* describes the ordinary case; the second *mishna* is the exceptional case of the *mumar*.

### Ravina's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לָא זוֹ אַף זוֹ קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** that there is no contradiction; rather, the *tanna* **teaches** the mishna employing the style: **Not** only **this** but **also that.** In other words, the *tanna* first teaches the elementary *halakha* that one who worships an idol is liable, and afterward it teaches the more novel *halakha* that even one who merely says that he will engage in idol worship is immediately liable.

Finally, Ravina resolves the apparent contradiction by rejecting the premise that there is any contradiction at all. He reads the two *mishnayot* as articulating a *lo zu af zu* — "not only this, but also this" — structure of escalating stringency. The first *mishna* teaches that one who actually worships is liable. The second *mishna* goes further: one need not even worship — merely saying one will go and worship already incurs liability. According to Ravina, this applies even to someone acting entirely on their own initiative, without any incitement from others. The two *mishnayot* are additive, each adding a layer of liability, with no contradiction between them.

The range of positions spans a wide spectrum. The easiest resolution conceptually — and the one that Tosafot ultimately privileges — is that verbal commitment in the context of incitement constitutes an act of *kabbalat elohus*, acceptance of a foreign deity, which is itself an enumerated act of *avodah zarah*. When that acceptance has not yet occurred — either because the person explicitly withholds it, or because the incitement was insufficiently persuasive, or because the person is acting alone without an inciter — the verbal statement does not rise to the level of *avodah zarah*, and only actual worship triggers liability.
