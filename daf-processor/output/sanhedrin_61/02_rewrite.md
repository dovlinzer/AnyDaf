# Sanhedrin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Avodah Zarah Intro

### Overview & Acceptance

The daf begins on 61b, immediately following the *mishna*, as the Gemara turns to the topic of *avodah zarah* — one of the cardinal offenses punishable by *sekilah*. Before enumerating the classic forms of worship, the Gemara makes a striking preliminary observation: the act of accepting a foreign deity as one's god is itself considered an *avodah*. One might have supposed that this falls only under the prohibition of *lo yihiyeh lecha elohim acherim* — the general negative commandment against acknowledging other gods — but not under the specific category of *avodah*, of worship. Nevertheless, the Gemara treats it as a genuine act of *avodah zarah* in its own right.

The generic term *ovedet* — the classic or paradigmatic form of worship — denotes both what the worshippers of a given deity themselves regard as the central act of veneration, and those forms of worship that are universally recognized as classic, particularly those performed in the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Five Forms of Worship

The Gemara proceeds to clarify the *mishna*'s phrase *echad ha-oved*, asking what it encompasses. Rabbi Yirmiyah explains: whether one worships *kedarkah* — in the manner specific and natural to that deity — or whether one performs any of four additional acts, even if those acts are not the natural form of worship for that particular god, one is liable. Those four acts are *mezabeiach* (slaughtering), *maktir* (burning on the altar), *menaseiach* (libation), and *mishtachaveh* (prostration), and the liability applies *afilu shelo kedarkah* — even if this is not the deity's usual mode of worship.

The Gemara then notes that the act of throwing blood — *zerikat hadam* — was not separately listed. Abaye explains that this is subsumed under the category of *menaseiach*, since both involve the offering of a liquid substance upon the altar. The verse is adduced: *kol bal asich niskeihem midam* — "let me not pour their libations of blood" — confirming that the throwing of blood is included within the concept of libation. Conceptually, of course, the two are distinct: the libation is the offering of wine, while the throwing of blood is the climactic act of the animal sacrifice. Nevertheless, for purposes of this halachic enumeration, they are grouped together.

### Source & Derivation

The Gemara then asks: *minayin milei* — what is the scriptural source for this entire framework? A *baraita* is cited. The verse states *zoveach le'elohim yecharam bilti l'Hashem levado* — "one who slaughters to other gods shall be put under the ban, except only to God alone" (Shemot 22:19). The *baraita* proceeds through a careful derivation.

Had the verse said only *zoveach yecharam* — "one who slaughters shall be put under the ban" — one might have supposed it was referring merely to one who slaughters *kodshim* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, the prohibition of *shechutei chutz*. The addition of *le'elohim* clarifies that the verse is speaking of one who slaughters to foreign gods — *avodah zarah*. Thus the base case is established: slaughtering to a foreign deity is prohibited and punishable.

But the verse goes on to say *bilti l'Hashem levado*. The *baraita* reads this phrase as a sweeping exclusivity clause: all acts of worship have been emptied out — reserved exclusively — for God. This phrase extends the prohibition beyond *zevichah* alone to include *maktir* (burning the fats), *menaseiach* (libation), and — through the inclusive force of the phrase — the throwing of blood as well. Rashi notes that the classic *avodot* performed in the *Beit HaMikdash* are *shechitah*, *kabbalat hadam*, *holachat hadam*, and *zerikat hadam*. One might ask why *kabbalah* and *holachah* are not included in the *baraita*'s enumeration. Rashi explains that no verse elsewhere in *Tanach* identifies *kabbalah* or *holachah* as acts of *avodah zarah*, whereas the other forms — slaughtering, burning, libation, and blood-throwing — do appear in other scriptural contexts as recognized modes of foreign worship. Rashi's formulation is thus an intersection: those acts that are classic to the *Beit HaMikdash* and that also appear in verses as recognized forms of *avodah zarah* are the ones that carry liability. As for *holachah* — even within the *Beit HaMikdash* context, it functions more as a preparatory step than as an act of worship in its own right, and accordingly it carries only a *lav* but not the death penalty.

The extension to *nisuach* through outside verses fits naturally with the broader halachic world of *stam yeinam* and *yayin nesech*, where libation to a foreign deity is a well-established category. Rashi characterizes this set as an intersection of what is classic *avodat pnim* and what appears in verses as classic *avodah zarah* — and once included, these acts carry the full prohibition even when performed for a deity to which this particular form of worship is not customary.

## Paradigm Debate

### Counterfactual Setup

Having established the basic framework, the Gemara now poses a sustained counterfactual: why is *zevichah* — slaughtering — the paradigm that defines the general category, with *hishtachavah* treated as a separate special case? Why not reverse the structure and make *hishtachavah* the general category?

The Gemara first explains how *hishtachavah* enters the picture at all. The verse from Devarim — *vayelech vaya'avod elohim acherim vayishtachu lahem* — "he went and worshipped other gods and prostrated to them" — establishes that *hishtachavah* is an act of *avodah zarah*. Unlike slaughtering, burning, or libation, prostration is not a formal act of worship within the *Beit HaMikdash* in the same technical halachic sense, yet it is universally recognized as a form of reverence for the divine. The subsequent verse in that passage — *ve'hotzeisa es ha'ish hahu ... el she'arecha* — specifying execution — tells us that prostrating before a foreign deity incurs the death penalty.

The *baraita* then frames the structural principle: *zevichah*, it says, was among the general class of worships, and it was singled out — *yatzas* — to become the paradigm, *liddon al haklal*, to define the set. The singling-out is purposive: just as *zevichah* is an *avodat pnim* — an act of worship within the *Beit HaMikdash* — and carries the death penalty, so too any act that shares those characteristics is included in the set defined by the paradigm. Forms of adoration such as hugging, kissing, or putting on the shoes of an idol are thereby excluded — they are not *avodat pnim* and the verse *zoveach* limits the class to acts with that character.

*Hishtachavah*, by contrast, exits to stand alone — *yatzas hishtachavah liddon b'atzmah* — as a separately enumerated exception. And then there is the third category: *avodat kedarkah*, the specific form of worship particular to that deity, derived from the verse *eicha ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem* — "how do these nations worship their gods?" — which extends liability to the unique worship of each particular idol even when it resembles none of the above.

Now Rabba bar Rav Chanan poses the question to Abaye directly: why not invert the structure? Let *hishtachavah* define the general class — since it is broad and non-specific, encompassing any act of respectful reverence — and let *zevichah* be the exceptional case, singled out to teach something specific about the act of slaughtering alone. If *hishtachavah* were the general class, the class would include every form of adoration, making the set much larger.

### Machshavim Debate

The Gemara considers what *zevichah* would then be needed to teach, if *hishtachavah* were already the general paradigm. The answer proposed is the principle of *machshavim me'avodah le'avodah* — the case where one slaughters an animal not as the act of worship itself, but with the intent to use the blood afterward as the act of worship. In other words: one slaughters an animal for the purpose of throwing its blood before a foreign deity, where the *zevichah* is preparatory and the *zerikah* is the intended act of worship.

This scenario has a direct parallel in the laws of *kodshim*: one who slaughters an animal with intent to perform an act — such as throwing the blood — at the wrong time renders the sacrifice *pigul*. Does the same logic apply to *avodah zarah*? If one slaughters an animal for the purpose of using its blood in *avodah zarah*, is the animal thereby forbidden in benefit as an animal offered to a foreign deity?

Rabbi Yochanan says the animal is *assur* — forbidden — applying the same logic as *kodshim*: since the slaughter was performed with intent directed toward *avodah zarah*, the animal acquires the status of an offering to *avodah zarah*. Reish Lakish, however, says the animal is *mutar* — permitted — because the act of slaughter was not itself an act of worship; no worship was yet performed on the animal directly.

Tosafot adds a pointed remark: even according to Reish Lakish, who permits the animal, it remains forbidden for consumption — not because of its status as an offering, but because the person performing the slaughter was, in the course of doing so, engaged in *avodah zarah*, rendering his *shechitah* halachically invalid. Tosafot then entertains a further subtlety: perhaps the person only became an *apostate* through the very act of this *shechitah*, meaning he was not yet a *mumar* at the moment he began — *bishchitah zu na'aseh mumar* — in which case perhaps even eating might be permitted, since the *shechitah* was valid at its inception.

Returning to the structural question: if *hishtachavah* is the general paradigm, then the specific word *zoveach* in the verse needs to teach something — and according to Rabbi Yochanan, it need not teach *machshavim me'avodah le'avodah*, since he derives that principle from *kodshim* by analogy. The word *zoveach* would therefore be extra and without purpose under the counterfactual arrangement. This is exactly what the Gemara needs: if *zoveach* would be superfluous when *hishtachavah* is the paradigm, that proves the arrangement cannot work — and so the original structure, with *zevichah* as the paradigm, is confirmed.

But the Gemara presses: Rav Papa challenges that Rabbi Yochanan also needs the word *zoveach* — not to establish that the animal is forbidden (which he derives from *kodshim*), but to establish that the *person* is liable for the death penalty. The animal's status and the person's liability are separate questions. The verse *zoveach* may be needed to teach that even in this preparatory-slaughter scenario, the person has performed an act of *avodah zarah* and is *chayav misa*.

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika then pushes in the opposite direction: perhaps the person's liability is obvious even without a verse — from the case of one who prostrates before a mountain. The mountain itself is not rendered forbidden; a mountain cannot become prohibited, as we learn from the verse *eloheihem al he'harim v'lo he'harim eloheihem* — "their gods are on the mountains, but the mountains do not become their gods." Yet the person who worships the mountain is executed, as the verse makes clear. So the principle that an act can constitute *avodah zarah* even when the object itself is not rendered forbidden is established without need of an additional verse. The status of the object and the liability of the person are categorically independent. In that case, Reish Lakish too might not need the word *zoveach* for this purpose, making it genuinely superfluous.

### Mountain & Peor

The Gemara then returns to the question of what would have been done with the verse *eicha ya'avdu hagoyim ha'eileh et eloheihem* had *hishtachavah* been the general paradigm. In the actual structure, this verse serves to include the *avodat kedarkah* — the unique, natural form of worship particular to each deity. But if *hishtachavah* already served as the all-encompassing general class, what would this verse need to exclude?

The Gemara considers: perhaps the verse would exclude acts of worship that are degrading rather than honorable — for example, defecating before an ordinary idol that is normally worshipped through sacrifice. This is analogous to *poreh atzmo le'Peor*, the act of defecating before Ba'al Peor, which is indeed the natural mode of worship for that deity. But what if someone performed that act before an idol that is not Ba'al Peor?

The Gemara responds that this exclusion would be unnecessary — it already flows from the nature of *hishtachavah* itself. *Hishtachavah* as a class encompasses only *derech kavod* — acts performed in a manner of honor. By extension, *af kol derech kavod* — any act performed in a dignified, reverential manner — would be included, and acts of degradation would be automatically excluded. The verse *eicha ya'avdu* would then be redundant.

### Eichah Ye'avdu Phrase

The Gemara therefore revises the question: perhaps the verse *eicha ya'avdu* is needed instead to exclude *poreh atzmo le'Markolis*. Markolis is a deity worshipped by the throwing of stones — itself an act that has an element of degradation to it. One might reason that since Markolis too is worshipped through an act that involves a kind of dishonor, perhaps any degrading act performed before any deity would be included. The verse *eicha ya'avdu* comes to exclude this: only acts that constitute *derech kavod* are included in the general category, and an act of degradation before a deity not customarily worshipped that way is not included, even if such acts are done before *other* deities.

At this point, Rabbi Eliezer's teaching is introduced: how do we know that one who slaughters an animal *le'Markolis* — to a deity worshipped through stone-throwing, not through sacrifice — is liable? The verse *ve'lo yizbechu od et zivcheihem* — "they shall no longer slaughter their sacrifices" — which serves as the prohibition, and *zoveach le'elohim yecharam* as the source for the death penalty. The phrase *eicha ya'avdu* establishes the general prohibition, and where it does not naturally apply — i.e., where this is not the natural form of worship for that particular deity — these other verses fill in the liability. The interplay between the general class of *zevichah*, the special case of *hishtachavah*, and the *avodat kedarkah* derived from *eicha ya'avdu* produces the complete and internally consistent structure. This is what would have been lost had *hishtachavah* been made the paradigm.

## Liability Threshold

### Rabba's Question

A new sugya opens with a classic *tzarich iyun*: Rabba bar Rav Chanan raises an apparent contradiction between two *mishnayot*. The first *mishna* states that one is liable for *avodah zarah* only when one actually performs the worship — *oved in*, one who worships, yes, but one who merely says he intends to worship, no. Yet a second *mishna* later in the tractate states that one who says *e'evod* — "I will worship" — or *elech ve'e'evod* — "I will go and worship" — or *nelech vene'evod* — "let us go and worship" — is already liable, even before performing any act of worship. The two *mishnayot* appear to be in direct tension.

### Acceptance Principle

Before turning to the proposed resolutions, it is worth noting the background principle invoked by Tosafot. The Gemara elsewhere states that *machshavah tovah hakadosh baruch hu metzarfah lema'aseh* — God counts a good intention as equivalent to the deed when circumstances prevent its fulfillment — but *machshavah ra'ah ein hakadosh baruch hu metzarfah lema'aseh* — He does not count an evil intention as equivalent to the deed. The single exception to this rule is *avodah zarah*. Tosafot accordingly explains the second *mishna* by appeal to the principle that accepting a foreign god as one's god is itself an act of *avodah zarah* — the same principle introduced at the very outset of the Gemara. When a person, in response to incitement, says "I will go and worship," he is not merely expressing a future plan; he is, through that verbal commitment, accepting that deity as his god — and that acceptance is an independent act of *avodah zarah*, independent of any subsequent act of worship.

The first *mishna*, then, must be read as describing a case where the person specifically makes clear that he does not accept the deity as his god until the moment he actually performs the worship — *eini mekabla alai ela ba'avodah*. In that case, no acceptance occurs at the verbal stage, and no liability arises until the worship is performed.

### Rav Yosef's Answer

Rav Yosef offers a different resolution: the two *mishnayot* reflect a disagreement among *Tannaim*, not two aspects of a single consistent system. One *mishna* holds that a verbal commitment already constitutes liability; the other holds it does not. This is a *machlokes* between the *mishnayot*.

Rav Yosef then appends a further argument: the distinction he initially proposed between an individual being incited (*yachid hanisas*) and a group being incited (*rabbim hanisatin*) is the basis for the difference. When an individual is incited and says yes, we assume he will follow through — *lo mimlach veta'ei basrei* — and therefore he is already liable from the moment of his verbal agreement. When masses are incited, the outcome is less certain — *mimlachi velo ta'u basrei* — the group dynamic may produce second thoughts, and it is possible that despite their initial assent the masses will not ultimately follow through. Therefore the masses are not liable until actual worship occurs.

Rav Yosef grounds this distinction in the verse *lo tove lo velo tishma eilav* — the prohibition in Devarim commanding the person being incited not to consent and not to listen to the inciter. The inference is: *ha-avah v'shama — chayav* — if one did consent and did listen, one is already liable. This verse, Tosafot notes, marks a conceptual shift: the liability of the person who says yes in the context of incitement derives not merely from the acceptance of a foreign deity as a god, but from the unique halachic category of responding to a *meisit*. The verse specifically addresses the context of being incited, and within that context, verbal assent triggers liability.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges Rav Yosef's distinction between the individual and the masses. He cites a *baraita* that discusses the verse *ki yesitcha achicha ben imecha* — "when your brother, your mother's son, incites you" — and concludes that the verse applies equally whether the one being incited is an individual or a group. The *baraita* does distinguish between the two, but only with respect to the severity of punishment and the treatment of property, not with respect to the stage at which liability arises. The individual's punishment is more severe on his person — *sekilah* — but his property is spared. The group's punishment is lighter on their persons — *hereg by the sword*, as in *ir hanidachat* — but their property is destroyed. For all other matters, Abaye argues, individual and group are governed by the same rules. The difference Rav Yosef proposed — that the individual is liable upon verbal assent while the group is not — has no basis in this *baraita*.

### Rava's Answer

Rava offers yet another resolution, one that does not depend on the individual-versus-masses distinction. He maintains that both *mishnayot* could be dealing with a case where one is incited by others. The difference lies in the quality of the incitement itself. If the inciter has made an elaborate and persuasive case — *kach ocheles kach shosah kach meitivah kach meriah* — "this is how it eats, this is how it drinks, this is how it rewards its followers, this is how it punishes its enemies" — then when the person says yes, we assume that the persuasion was effective and the commitment is real. In that case, the verbal assent triggers immediate liability. But if the incitement was perfunctory — "let's go worship" — with no elaborate sell job, then the yes is shallow and we do not assume the person will follow through. In that case, liability requires actual worship.

Rava derives this from the verse *me'elohei ha'amim asher sevivoseichem ha'kerovim eilecha o ha'rechokim mimecha* — the verse warns against being drawn to the gods of surrounding nations, whether near or far. Rava reads the reference to near and far as indicating the form the incitement takes: the inciter will tell you to ignore the gods you know locally — which you can see are powerless — and will entice you with the allure of foreign gods you have never encountered. The very structure of the verse presupposes a sustained rhetorical effort. This confirms that the context of *lo tove lo velo tishma eilav* is precisely such an elaborate incitement — and therefore the liability that triggers upon saying yes applies specifically in that context.

### Rav Ashi's Answer

Rav Ashi resolves the contradiction differently. The second *mishna*, in which verbal assent suffices for liability, is referring specifically to a Jew who is already an apostate — already established as *oved avodah zarah*. For such a person, saying yes means yes; there is no reason to assume a subsequent change of heart. But for any other person, even after a full and compelling incitement, verbal assent alone does not constitute liability. Actual worship is required. The first *mishna* describes the ordinary case; the second *mishna* is the exceptional case of the *mumar*.

### Ravina's Answer

Finally, Ravina resolves the apparent contradiction by rejecting the premise that there is any contradiction at all. He reads the two *mishnayot* as articulating a *lo zu af zu* — "not only this, but also this" — structure of escalating stringency. The first *mishna* teaches that one who actually worships is liable. The second *mishna* goes further: one need not even worship — merely saying one will go and worship already incurs liability. According to Ravina, this applies even to someone acting entirely on their own initiative, without any incitement from others. The two *mishnayot* are additive, each adding a layer of liability, with no contradiction between them.

The range of positions spans a wide spectrum. The easiest resolution conceptually — and the one that Tosafot ultimately privileges — is that verbal commitment in the context of incitement constitutes an act of *kabbalat elohus*, acceptance of a foreign deity, which is itself an enumerated act of *avodah zarah*. When that acceptance has not yet occurred — either because the person explicitly withholds it, or because the incitement was insufficiently persuasive, or because the person is acting alone without an inciter — the verbal statement does not rise to the level of *avodah zarah*, and only actual worship triggers liability.
