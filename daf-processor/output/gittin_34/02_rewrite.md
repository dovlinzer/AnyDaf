# Gittin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Giddul & Gilluy Da'as

### Giddul's Regret

The daf opens six lines from the top of 34a, within the broader context of the *mishna*'s discussion of a husband's ability to be *mevatel* a *get* before it reaches his wife. Recall that *Rabban Gamliel Hazaken* had already reformed the older practice of nullifying a *get* without the wife's knowledge, as part of *tikkun olam*. The *gemara* now introduces a narrative that will occasion a major *machloket* between Abaye and Rava.

*Giddul bar I'lai shadru lei gita l'devitehu* — Giddul bar I'lai sent a *get* to his wife. The *shliach* arrived and found her sitting and weaving. He said: here is your *get*. She responded: go for now, come back tomorrow. The *shliach* obliged — though he was not technically required to, since a husband may divorce his wife against her will — and returned to Giddul bar I'lai to report what had happened. At that point, Giddul bar I'lai broke into the blessing *Baruch Hatov Vehametiv*, an expression of gratitude — transparently indicating his relief that the *get* had not yet been delivered and his clear regret at having sent it.

### Abaye: No Gilluy

*Abaye amar Baruch Hatov Vehametiv v'lo batil gita* — Abaye holds that despite this unambiguous expression of remorse, the *get* was not nullified. If the *shliach* were to proceed and deliver the *get*, it would still be fully effective. Abaye's formulation speaks of *bitul* of the *get* itself, though Rashi frames the analysis more in terms of *bitul* of the *shlichut*, the agency. Either way, for Abaye, both the *get* and the *shlichut* remain operative.

### Rava: Gilluy Works

*Rava amar Baruch Hatov Vehametiv u'batil gita* — Rava disagrees. Once Giddul bar I'lai's expressed delight makes his intent manifest, the *get* is *batel*. Rashi again emphasizes that it is primarily the *shlichut* that is *batel*, with the implication for the *get* being that if one wished to proceed with a divorce, a new *get* would be required.

### The Debate

*Bemai kamiflagi? Begilui da'ata begita kamiflagi* — the *gemara* identifies the crux: the dispute concerns whether *gilui da'as* — the public revelation of one's intent — is legally operative in the context of a *get*.

There is no ambiguity about Giddul bar I'lai's *da'as*. The question is whether demonstrating desire, without a formal speech act of revocation, carries legal weight. Abaye holds: *gilui da'as begita lav milta hi* — it is of no legal consequence. From a strictly formal legal standpoint, this is compelling: one must execute an actual act of *bitul* of the *get* and revoke the *shlichut* through a proper verbal declaration. The mere revelation of intent, however transparent, leaves everything in place.

Rava counters: *gilui da'as begita milta hi* — once the *shliach* can no longer be said to be acting on the husband's behalf, because the husband's contrary wishes are plain, the *shlichut* is dissolved.

## Rav Sheshet Proof

### Forced Divorce

Rava now offers a proof from a case involving Rav Sheshet. Rav Sheshet compelled a man to divorce his wife against his will — a practice recognized as legitimate when carried out through a *beis din* with proper grounds, even when the husband's compliance is extracted under duress. The man submitted under pressure, saying the requisite words of consent and directing witnesses to proceed with the *get*. Thereafter, however, he turned to the witnesses and declared: *hachi amar lacho Rav Sheshet l'vatulei gita* — so said Rav Sheshet to you, that the *get* should be nullified. And Rav Sheshet then found himself needing to produce a new *get* — implying that the first had somehow been undermined.

### Rava's Inference

Rava's argument is that the man's statement — though patently false, since Rav Sheshet had said no such thing and lacks the authority to nullify someone else's *get* — nonetheless served to manifest the man's lack of genuine desire to divorce. It was a declaration of intent, not a formally valid act of *bitul*. And yet it apparently sufficed to create a problem with the *get*, requiring a second one. This is Rava's evidence that *gilui da'as* is operative.

---

## Abaye's Rebuttal

### Active Bitul

Abaye responds with a complete reinterpretation of the case. *Atu Rav Sheshet mevatel gitta de'inshei havae?* — Rav Sheshet is certainly not in a position to nullify someone else's *get*. An ordinary person might not appreciate this — especially someone facing a *beis din* — but logically the story cannot be what Rava says it is. Abaye's reading is that the man actually did perform an act of *bitul*, a genuine formal revocation of the *get* after he had directed the witnesses to proceed.

### Lashes Defense

The reason the man invoked Rav Sheshet's name was simply a cover story. The *beis din*'s officers were lashing him for attempting to nullify the *get* he had been compelled to give. Under the pain of those lashes, he gave a pretextual excuse — he falsely claimed that Rav Sheshet had authorized the *bitul* — not because the *bitul* consisted of that claim, but because he needed a reason to make the lashing stop. In Abaye's account, the *gilui da'as* was incidental; the *bitul* was an actual formal act. The need for a second *get* is therefore no evidence that *gilui da'as* alone sufficed.

---

## Plugged Ears

### Forced Get

Abaye now marshals his own proof. The case involves Rav Yehuda, who sought to compel his son-in-law — the son-in-law of Rav Yirmiyah Birah — to divorce his wife. Each time Rav Yehuda applied sufficient pressure for the man to say he consented and direct the *sofer* and witnesses to write the *get*, the man would then formally be *mevatel* the *get* as soon as the pressure lifted. This happened repeatedly: *tana ashkaleih u'batlei, hadar tana v'ashkaleih al korchei* — again and again Rav Yehuda forced the declaration, and again and again the man performed *bitul*.

Finally, Rav Yehuda directed the witnesses: *otivu karrei b'oznaichu* — put a gourd in your ears. Write the *get* and ensure you are not in a position to hear him issue any further revocation directed at you, the persons charged with writing and delivering it.

### Formal Act

Abaye's inference is precise and elegant. The instruction to plug the witnesses' ears only makes sense if one holds that *bitul* or revocation of *shlichut* requires a formal speech act directed at those authorized to act. If *gilui da'as* sufficed — if merely demonstrating one's intent were enough — then plugging their ears would be useless. As the *gemara* states: *ve'ha chazu leih d'ka rahi batraihu* — they can plainly see him chasing after them, making every effort to communicate his desire to retract. His intent is manifest even without words. If *gilui da'as* worked, all of that visible, frantic effort would already constitute *bitul*. The only reason the plugged ears accomplish anything is if formal, audible speech is required — precisely Abaye's position.

## Conditional Gets

### Rava's Counter

Rava responds to the plugged-ears proof. He argues that one may reinterpret what the man was shouting as he ran after the witnesses. Perhaps he was not attempting *bitul* at all — perhaps he was calling out: *ashur havu lah* — go and give it to her quickly, already, so that I can be done with this ordeal. Once the witnesses' ears are stopped up, Rava argues, we are free to reinterpret ambiguous behavior. The man is running — perhaps out of urgency to complete the divorce, not to abort it. In Rava's view, *gilui da'as* must genuinely manifest the relevant intent, and under these circumstances that manifestation is contestable.

Rava's position is thus nuanced: he does not say *gilui da'as* is always sufficient regardless of context. He says it suffices when the intent is genuinely and unambiguously revealed — but the circumstances may sometimes allow for alternative readings that prevent *gilui da'as* from being established.

### Ferry Case

Abaye is not yet finished pressing his position. A man gave a *get* to his wife on a *tenai*, a condition: *ee lo ateina ad tlatin yomei leihavei gitta* — if I do not return within thirty days, let it be a *get*. This was perhaps motivated by concern that he might perish on a journey with no witnesses to testify, leaving his wife an *agunah*. The thirty days elapsed; the man actually did return and was attempting to cross the river back to the city. But the ferry was on the opposite bank, and by the time it could have reached him, the thirty-day limit would have passed. He stood at the water's edge, clearly within earshot, and called out: *chazu de'asai chazu de'asai* — look, I have arrived, I have come back!

### Shmuel Ruling

*Lav shemei matya* — Shmuel rules that this does not count as having returned. The condition was not met; the *get* takes effect. This is a striking ruling, because the man's desire that the *get* not take effect could not be more apparent. He is standing at the riverbank, calling out, clearly demonstrating with everything at his disposal that he does not wish to be divorced. And yet the *get* is effective.

Abaye draws his inference: even this transparent *gilui da'as* is insufficient to prevent the *get* from taking effect. Why, then, does it not constitute *bitul* according to Rava?

Rava's response, however, is subtle. *Atu hasam levitulei gitta baei? Hasam lekiyumei tnaia ka baei* — he is not attempting *bitul* of the *get*. He is attempting to argue that he fulfilled his *tenai*, that the condition of the *get* was satisfied on his end. His *gilui da'as* is not focused on the *get* itself; it is focused on the factual question of whether he returned. Moreover — and this is analytically significant — there is no *shlichut* in play here. He gave the *get* thirty days ago with a condition attached. There is no agent whose authority can be revoked. The only question is whether the condition was met, and the only mechanism by which *gilui da'as* could operate would be through an actual *bitul* of the *get* as an object. But his *gilui da'as* is not directed at the *get*; it is directed at the *tenai*. And even if one were to say that Rava holds *gilui da'as* suffices, it would have to be *gilui da'as* of an intent to perform *bitul* — which is not what is happening here.

### Nissuin Condition

Another case: a man gave a *get* to his betrothed — there being *kiddushin* already in place — on the condition that if they did not proceed to *nissuin* within thirty days, the *get* would take effect. When thirty days arrived without *nissuin* having been completed, he claimed to be working on it, trying to make the arrangements. Is that enough to stave off the effectiveness of the *get*?

### Debate Applied

*L'mai neichush lah* — what legal theory could help him? *Ei mishum ones ein ones b'gittin* — if the argument is that he was unable to satisfy the condition through no fault of his own, there is a well-established ruling in a separate *sugya* that a claim of *ones* does not operate to excuse an unmet *tenai* in a *get*. So the condition is simply unfulfilled. As for *gilui da'as* — *plugta d'Abaye v'Rava* — that is precisely the dispute between Abaye and Rava. And yet here too, as the *gemara* notes, following Rava's logic in the ferry case, one could argue that even Rava would concede: there is no *shlichut* to revoke and there is no *gilui da'as* directed at a *bitul* of the *get* itself. His *gilui da'as* is about the *tenai*, not the *get*.

### Month Confusion

A third case: a man similarly gave a *get* on condition that *nissuin* not occur by *Rosh Chodesh Adar*. When *Rosh Chodesh Adar* arrived without *nissuin*, he claimed: I said *Nissan*, not *Adar*. The witnesses clearly remembered he had said *Adar*; he was either lying or misremembering. In any event, it is manifest that he does not want the *get* to take effect.

Again the *gemara* asks: *l'mai neichush lah*? *Ei mishum ones ein ones b'gittin* — his inability or failure to satisfy the condition does not excuse him. *Ei mishum gilui da'as — plugta d'Abaye v'Rava* — and whether his clear manifest desire is legally operative is once again the debate. And as before, even granting Rava's position, the *gilui da'as* here is not a *gilui da'as* of *bitul*; there is neither *shlichut* to revoke nor a properly focused *gilui da'as* of the *get* itself.

## Final Halacha

### Ruled Like Abaye

*V'hilcheta k'Nachman* — the *gemara* now states the *halacha* on several related matters. It rules like Rav Nachman on the earlier questions concerning whether *bitul* before two or three witnesses is required and whether a *bedi'eved* *bitul* performed in violation of *Rabban Gamliel*'s *takanah* is nonetheless effective. And crucially: *v'hilcheta k'Nachmani* — the *halacha* follows Nachmani, which is a name for Abaye.

We rule like Abaye: *gilui da'as begita lav milta hi*. Revealing one's intent is not legally sufficient to effect *bitul* of a *get* or to revoke a *shlichut*. An affirmative speech act of revocation is required. This is one of the exceptional cases in which the *halacha* follows Abaye against Rava, enumerated by the mnemonic *ya'al kagam* — and this is the *gimel* of *ya'al kagam*: *gilui da'as begita*.

### Nachmani Etymology

Why was Abaye called Nachmani? Rashi, in the second line of his commentary here, explains: *nire b'einai d'al shem she-Rabbah bar Nachmani gidel Abaye b'veito v'limdo Torah* — it appears to him that Abaye was named after Rabbah bar Nachmani, who is otherwise referred to simply as *stam* Rabbah, because Rabbah raised the orphaned Abaye in his home and taught him Torah. Others reverse the relationship and suggest that Nachmani was actually Abaye's given name, but because it too closely resembled the name of Rabbah bar Nachmani — or Rabbah's own father — he was given the name Abaye instead. The *Maharsha* notes that the name *Abaye* carries a beautiful allusive quality as a *rashei teivos*: *asher b'cha yerucham yasom* — through You, O God, the orphan finds consolation. Either way, the *halacha* follows Abaye — Nachmani — that *gilui da'as begita* does not suffice.

## Multiple Names

### Rabban Gamliel

The *gemara* transitions to the next *mishna*, still within the thematic context of *tikkun olam* in the world of *gittin*. *Berishona haya mishane shemo v'shma shem iro v'shem ira* — originally, a *get* might be written using a name or a city name that was not the primary, fully established designation: a secondary name, a name recognized in one locale but not another, a nickname, without full transparency about all the names by which a party was known.

*Hitkin Rabban Gamliel Hazaken* — Rabban Gamliel the Elder enacted that the *get* should state: this man and any name that he has, and this woman and any name that she has — *mipnei tikkun olam*. This raises an important question that will recur throughout the *gemara*'s analysis: does the phrase *v'chol shum sheyesh lo* mean that one writes those words formulaically — a standard rubric inserted in all *gittin* — or does it mean that one must actually enumerate and list each additional name explicitly? That question, as the *gemara* makes clear, remains open to significant debate among the *rishonim*.

## Regional Names

### Name Problem

The *gemara* provides the historical context for Rabban Gamliel's enactment. *Shalchu leih bnei medinas hayam l'Rabban Gamliel* — the people of overseas communities sent a question to Rabban Gamliel in *Eretz Yisrael*. The problem was this: *shemo Yosef v'korin lo Yochanan, Yochanan v'korin lo Yosef* — people emigrating from *Eretz Yisrael* were known by one name at home and an entirely different name in their new community. Perhaps they had adopted a local name; perhaps the names were used interchangeably in different social contexts. The question was pointed: *heichi megarshin nishoseihem?* — if such a man wished to send a *get* back to his wife in *Eretz Yisrael*, which name should be used — the name by which he is known where the *get* is written, or the name by which he is known where it will be received?

This question illuminates precisely what the *mishna* meant by its concern about changing or misusing names. The ambiguity was real and consequential.

*Amar Rabban Gamliel v'hitkin* — Rabban Gamliel responded with his enactment: write the name and every other name, so that there is no ambiguity whatsoever. The question of which name takes priority — whether the primary name is determined by the location of writing or the location of delivery — is not resolved explicitly here; the *Tosafot* engage that question at length.

### Rav Ashi's Rule

*Amar Rav Ashi v'hu de-itchazak bitrei shemei* — Rav Ashi qualifies: the requirement applies only when it is *established* that a person goes by two different names. If only one name is known, the obligation to enumerate additional names does not apply.

One might wonder what this qualification adds — after all, one cannot be expected to list names one does not know. The practical significance, as Rashi explains, lies in the *bedi'eved* question. If a man is known by only one name at the time the *get* is written and someone later comes forward to assert that he also goes by another name, the *get* already delivered is nonetheless *kosher bedi'eved*. The *takanah* — and the consequence of *bitul* — applies only when both names were already *established* at the time of writing.

This in turn raises a significant question that will resonate through the rest of the discussion: is the *bitul* a rabbinic consequence that *Chazal* imposed through Rabban Gamliel's enactment — meaning *Chazal* are exercising their authority to invalidate a *get* — or is the invalidity rooted *de-Oraita*, in that a *get* lacking sufficient identification is inherently defective, and Rabban Gamliel's enactment was simply designed to prevent that underlying problem? The answer has significant implications for how broadly the *bitul* applies.

*Amar lei Rabbi Abba le-Rav Ashi* — Rabbi Abba confirmed to Rav Ashi that Rav Mari and Rav Elazar both hold in accordance with his view. And a *braita* is cited that supports Rav Ashi: *tanya kevathei de-Rav Ashi*.

## Two Wives Braita

### Two-Region Case

*Hayu lo shetei nashim* — a man had two wives, one in Judea and one in Galilee. Furthermore, he had two names: one name by which he was known in Judea and another in Galilee. This is the case that makes the *bedi'eved* ruling explicit.

### Both Names Required

*Gireish et ishah she-bi-Yehudah bishmo she-bi-Yehudah ve-et ishah she-ba-Galil bishmo she-ba-Galil — einah megureshet* — if he divorced his Judean wife using only his Judean name, or his Galilean wife using only his Galilean name, the *get* is invalid. The *braita* makes clear that to divorce his Judean wife, he must use his Judean name and include his Galilean name as well — *veshem de-Galil imo* — and vice versa for his Galilean wife. Failure to include both names produces a *batel get bedi'eved*.

This *braita* establishes two important points. First, the *bitul bedi'eved* is real and operative when both names were established and only one was included. Second — and here the *Tosafot* comment at length — the default name, the one written first, appears to be determined by the place where the *get* is being delivered: where the wife lives. Divorcing the Judean wife, one begins with the Judean name; divorcing the Galilean wife, one begins with the Galilean name.

### Third Place

*Yatza le-makom acher vegireish be-achad mei-hem megureshet* — now the *braita* introduces what appears to be a surprising ruling. If the same man leaves both Judea and Galilee and goes to a third place, and from there divorces one of his wives using only one of his names, the *get* is valid. This seems inconsistent. The same man, the same two names — why should his geographic location affect the validity of the *get*?

### Unknown Names

*Ela shema mina ha de-itchazek ha de-lo itchazek* — the *gemara* draws the inference: the distinction must rest entirely on whether the second name is *established* in the place of writing. In Judea, it is known that this man also goes by a Galilean name; in Galilee, his Judean name is known. In either case, writing only one name leaves a known gap. But if he travels to a third place — say, outside the land — that community knows him by only one name. His other name is not *established* there. Therefore, *bedi'eved*, the *get* is *kosher*.

This reasoning powerfully suggests that the *bitul* is rabbinic in nature. If the problem were a genuine *de-Oraita* lack of identification, the man's geographic location would be irrelevant — the ambiguity would exist regardless of whether the community writing the *get* was aware of it. The fact that ignorance of the second name renders the *get* valid implies that the requirement is imposed by *Chazal*, and *Chazal* calibrated the *takanah* to circumstances where the omission is blameworthy. Where one cannot be blamed — because the second name is simply unknown — the *get* stands.

## Majority Name

### Miriam vs Sarah

The *gemara* concludes with a case of a different character: *hahi d'hava karu lah Miriam v'ukmusa Sarah* — a woman was known by two names, Miriam and Sarah, and both names were used in the same locale. This differs from the earlier cases involving different names in different geographic regions; here, the two names coexist in a single community.

*Amar Nahardai: Miriam v'chol shum sheish lah v'lo Sarah v'chol shum sheish lah* — the sages of Nehardea rule that one writes Miriam — the majority name, the name by which she is predominantly known — as the primary name, and not Sarah. The logic is straightforward: the default name should be the one most widely recognized.

This ruling prompts further questions that the *gemara* does not fully resolve. The question of what happens *bedi'eved* if the names are reversed — if Sarah is written first and Miriam second — is left open. And importantly, this case extends Rabban Gamliel's *takanah* beyond the geographic scenario to the situation where a single person goes by multiple names in the same place. One must list all established names even absent any regional ambiguity.

Finally, the underlying structural question persists: does *v'chol shum sheish lah* mean that one writes those words as a formula, or that one actually lists each name by name? This question, raised at the outset of the *gemara*'s discussion, threads through every case and remains a central point of dispute among the *rishonim*.
