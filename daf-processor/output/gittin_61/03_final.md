# Gittin 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eruv & Reputation

### Eruv Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעָרְבִין בְּבַיִת יָשָׁן, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם כָּבוֹד, וְהָא הָהוּא שִׁיפּוּרָא, דַּהֲוָה מֵעִיקָּרָא בֵּי רַב יְהוּדָה, וּלְבַסּוֹף בֵּי רַבָּה, וּלְבַסּוֹף בֵּי רַב יוֹסֵף, וּלְבַסּוֹף בֵּי אַבָּיֵי, וּלְבַסּוֹף בֵּי רָבָא!
>
> **Translation:** A joining of courtyards [*eiruv*] is placed in an old house where it had regularly been placed on account of the ways of peace. The Gemara asks: What is the reason for this? If we say that it is to show respect to the owner of that house, but wasn't it related about a certain charity box, which was fashioned for the benefit of the community and brought honor to the person in whose house it was placed, that initially it was placed in Rav Yehuda's house, and afterward it was moved to Rabba's house, and afterward it was transferred to Rav Yosef's house, and afterward it was moved to Abaye's house, and afterward it was moved to Rava's house. This teaches that there is no issue here of respect, and that such items would ordinarily be moved from place to place.

The daf opens in the middle of Gittin 61b, returning to the halachic portion of the Mishnah and its discussion of *darkei shalom* — the principle of maintaining peaceful relations. The Mishnah states: *me'arvin b'bayit yashan mipnei darkei shalom* — one places the *eruv* in the established house, the house in which it has been kept until now, because of the ways of peace. The Gemara then asks: *mai ta'ama darkei shalom* — what precisely is the *darkei shalom* rationale here? If we suppose it is a matter of *kavod* — of honor, that the person in whose house the *eruv* is stored would feel slighted were it moved — we can challenge this from a case involving a *shippura*.

### Shippura Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם חֲשָׁדָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, say instead that the Sages instituted this enactment to avoid arousing suspicion. Since the *eiruv* had regularly been placed in a particular house, were it to be moved, people might think that the residents of the alleyway suspected that the owner of the house was stealing from them, and therefore they put it somewhere else.

The Gemara introduces the *shippura* that was kept in the house of Rav Yehuda and subsequently moved as the generations changed. Rashi explains that the *shippura* was a shofar blown to warn the public of the approaching Shabbat. Tosafot, citing one of the Geonim, offers an alternative reading: the *shippura* was a shofar-shaped charity box — a vessel with a narrow slot at the top and a wide base — used to collect donations for the students of the yeshiva. The Geonic source indeed reflects the reality of that era, when such collections were essential for the maintenance of the great academies.

### Shippura Succession

The Gemara traces the movement of the *shippura*: first it was kept in the house of Rav Yehuda, then in the house of Rabba, then Rav Yosef, then Abaye, and finally Rava. Rashi explains the succession in terms of the passing of each *rosh yeshiva* of Pumbedita: when one died and the next rose to lead the academy, the *shippura* was transferred to his household. Tosafot raises a geographic difficulty: Rav Yehuda was in Pumbedita while Rava was in Mechuza — these are different cities. Tosafot resolves this by suggesting that the *shippura* followed whoever held the position of primary *rosh mesivta*, wherever he happened to reside, since it was understood communally that the fund traveled with the leading authority and was his to distribute to the students.

### Suspicion Rationale

People are accustomed to seeing the *eruv* kept in a particular house. If it is moved and someone enters that house and does not find it there, they may assume the *eruv* was never made, and suspect the entire courtyard of carrying without an *eruv*. It is not an issue of dishonoring the original host; it is the concern for generating ill repute among the members of the *mavoy*.

Tosafot formulates the suspicion differently. According to Rabbeinu Tam, the concern is that if the *eruv* is removed from one house and placed in another, people will suspect that the original host can no longer be trusted — that he was suspected of stealing from the communal *eruv* chest. That is the reputational harm *darkei shalom* seeks to prevent. Tosafot then asks: if we are worried about such suspicion, how much more so should we be worried about moving the *tzedakah* box, where suspicion of misappropriation would be even more natural? Tosafot answers that everyone understands the charity fund follows the *rosh mesivta*, and so its movement from one leading scholar's home to the next raises no suspicion — it is a known and accepted practice.

## Water Rights Debate

### Well & Canal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹר שֶׁהוּא קָרוֹב לָאַמָּה וְכוּ׳: אִיתְּמַר: בְּנֵי נַהֲרָא; רַב אָמַר: תַּתָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אֲמַר: עִילָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the Sages enacted that the pit that is nearest to the irrigation channel that supplies water to several pits or fields is filled first on account of the ways of peace. It was stated that the *amora'im* disagree about the following issue: When people own fields along a river and they irrigate their fields with water that is redirected from it, who among them enjoys first rights to irrigate his field? Rav said: The owners of the lowermost fields drink the water, i.e., irrigate their fields, first. And Shmuel said: The owners of the uppermost fields drink the water first.

The Gemara next takes up the Mishnah's case of *bor she-haya karov le-amah* — a well that is situated near a water channel — and the question of who has priority to draw from that channel first. The underlying dispute concerns which landowners along a waterway have preferential rights to its water.

### Rav vs Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּדְמֵיזַל – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְלִיגִי. כִּי פְלִיגִי – בְּמִיסְכַּר וְאַשְׁקוֹיֵי; שְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עִילָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא, דְּאָמְרִי: אֲנַן מְקָרְבִינַן טְפֵי. וְרַב אָמַר: תַּתָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא, דְּאָמְרִי: נַהֲרָא כִּפְשָׁטֵיהּ לֵיזִיל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where the water flows on its own, everyone agrees that whoever wishes to irrigate may do so as he wishes. When they disagree, it is with regard to a case where they need to dam the river and irrigate through channels. Shmuel said: The owners of the uppermost fields drink the water first because they can say: We are nearer to the river's headwaters. And Rav said: The owners of the lowermost fields drink the water first because they can say: Let the river go its natural way and after we take what we need, dam it as you please.

The Gemara clarifies that when water flows freely and there is no concern of it running out — or when people are simply drawing from the moving river without damming it — there is no dispute; everyone may use the water freely. The debate is specifically about *be-miskar ve-ashkoyei* — the act of damming the river in order to irrigate one's fields from the pooled water. Who has the right to dam it up first?

Shmuel maintains that those upstream have priority, reasoning *de-amri anan mekarvinan tfei* — they are closer to the water's source and thus have a stronger natural claim. Rav disagrees, ruling in favor of those downstream: *nahara ke-fashtei leizil* — the river must be allowed to run its natural course. The phrase *ke-fashtei*, meaning "as it spreads," connects to the well-known expression *nahara nahara u-fashtei* — each river and its tributaries — used to acknowledge the legitimacy of varying practices. Rav's position is that you cannot stop the river at its upper reach before it has fed into the tributaries along the way; only after the river has done that may those downstream claim the right to dam it. Rashi elaborates: as the river descends it branches into tributaries, and the people upstream argue they must act first because by the time the water has spread into all those branches, little will remain. The people downstream counter that it is unjust to stop the flow before all those branches have been fed.

### Shmuel's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: בּוֹר הַקָּרוֹב לָאַמָּה, מִתְמַלֵּא רִאשׁוֹן – מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם! תַּרְגְּמַהּ שְׁמוּאֵל אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַב: בְּאַמָּה הַמִּתְהַלֶּכֶת עַל פִּי בוֹרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that the Sages enacted that the pit that is nearest to the irrigation channel that supplies water to several pits or fields is filled first on account of the ways of peace. This teaches that the party who is nearest to the water's source enjoys first rights, and it supports Shmuel's opinion and is difficult for Rav. Shmuel interpreted the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Rav: The mishna refers here to an irrigation channel that passes the mouth of the pit, so that the pit fills with water on its own, even without damming.

Having stated their own positions, the Gemara now invokes the Mishnah: *bor ha-karov le-amah mitmalei rishon mipnei darkei shalom* — the well closest to the channel fills first because of *darkei shalom*. This would seem to favor Rav, since proximity to the channel would imply priority for those upstream. But *targumah Shmuel aliba de-Rav* — Shmuel, to his credit, interprets this Mishnah in a way that is consistent with Rav's position even though it appears to contradict his own. The Mishnah, Shmuel explains, is dealing with *amah ha-malechet al pi boro* — a channel that runs directly along the mouth of the well, such that the well fills naturally from the water's flow without any damming at all. Rashi: *she-mitmalei me-eilav ke-derech hillucho* — it fills on its own as the water runs its course. In such a case neither Rav nor Shmuel has anything to say; the water simply overflows into the adjacent well with no act of diversion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: סְכַר מִיסְכָּר, וְאַשְׁקִי בְּהִינְדָּזָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, what is the purpose of stating this? It is obvious. The Gemara answers: Lest you say that the owners of the other fields can say to the owner of the pit: Dam your pit as well so that water not enter it, and irrigate your fields in proportion [*hindeza*], just like the rest of us. The mishna therefore teaches us that the owner of the pit is not required to do this, and consequently his pit is filled first.

The Gemara raises an objection: if that is so, what is the novelty of the ruling? What is the *chiddush*? The Gemara answers: one might have thought the well's owner is obligated to seal or cover his well so as to allow more water to flow downstream to those further along. The Mishnah tells us he is not required to do so. If water naturally flows into his well, he is permitted to allow it. This, however, says nothing about the separate question of whether one may actively dam the river — and it is only in that context that the dispute between Rav and Shmuel applies.

### Might Makes Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָא אִיתְּמַר הִלְכְתָא לָא כְּמָר וְלָא כְּמָר, כֹּל דְּאַלִּים גָּבַר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna bar Taḥalifa said: Now that the *halakha* was stated neither in accordance with the opinion of this Sage, Rav, nor in accordance with the opinion of that Sage, Shmuel, whoever is stronger prevails. Since the *halakha* has not been decided, the court refuses to judge the case and leaves the claimants to settle the matter themselves, in the hope that the rightful party will exert himself and prevail.

Since neither Rav nor Shmuel's position was definitively ruled upon, Rav Huna bar Tachalifa states: *kol d'alim gavar* — whoever is stronger prevails. This is, as the Gemara implicitly acknowledges, the antithesis of *darkei shalom*. Rather than establishing orderly norms for communal resource allocation — perhaps alternating months or some other equitable arrangement — the outcome is effectively "go fight it out." It is both ironic and troubling that the discussion of *darkei shalom* ends at this juncture with a principle that is precisely the opposite of peaceful coexistence.

### Rav Shimi's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לוֹתְבַן מָר בְּעִידָּנָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִית לִי עִידָּנָא לְדִידִי. וְלוֹתְבַן מָר בְּלֵילְיָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִית לִי מַיָּא לְאַשְׁקוֹיֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא מַשְׁקֵינָא לֵיהּ לְמָר מַיָּא בִּימָמָא, וְלוֹתְבַן מָר בְּלֵילְיָא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְחַיֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi came before Abaye and said to him: Master, set a time for me to study with you. Abaye said to him: I have a set time for myself, and I cannot devote it to you. Rav Shimi bar Ashi said to him: Master, set a time for me at night, and we can study then. Abaye said to him: I have to bring water at night with which to irrigate my fields. Rav Shimi bar Ashi said to him: I will irrigate for Master during the day, and then Master can set a time for me at night to study with him. Abaye said to him: Very well; this is an acceptable arrangement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל לְעִילָּאֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: תַּתָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא. אֲזַל לְתַתָּאֵי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: עִילָּאֵי שָׁתוּ מַיָּא בְּרֵישָׁא. אַדְּהָכִי סְכַר מִיסְכָּר וְאַשְׁקִי. כִּי אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבֵי תְרֵי עֲבַדְתְּ לִי! וְלָא טַעְמִינְהוּ אַבָּיֵי לְפֵירֵי דְּהַהִיא שַׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** What did Rav Shimi bar Ashi do? He first went to the owners of the uppermost fields, and said to them: The owners of the lowermost fields drink the water first, in accordance with the opinion of Rav. He then went to the owners of the lowermost fields, and said to them: The owners of the uppermost fields drink the water first, in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel. In the meantime, while the owners of the upper fields and the lower fields were arguing over who has first rights to the water, Rav Shimi bar Ashi dammed the river and irrigated Abaye's fields. When he came before Abaye, the latter said to him: You have acted for me in accordance with two opposing opinions. And Abaye would not even taste the produce of that year because he thought that the water had reached his field in an unlawful manner.

The Gemara then relates the story of Rav Shimi bar Ashi, which illustrates how *kol d'alim gavar* can be exploited. Rav Shimi bar Ashi came before Abaye and asked him to learn with him. Abaye replied that he had only his own study time left and could not spare it for teaching. Rav Shimi then asked about learning at night, and Abaye said he used his nights to water his fields. Rav Shimi offered a deal: he would water Abaye's fields during the day if Abaye would teach him at night. Abaye agreed.

To fulfill his end of the bargain conveniently, Rav Shimi went to the people upstream and told them the ruling was *tata'ei shatu be-reisha* — those downstream have priority. He then went to the people downstream and told them the ruling was *ila'ei shatu be-reisha* — those upstream have priority. Each group, deferring to his halakhic authority, held back from damming the river, whereupon Rav Shimi dammed it first himself and irrigated Abaye's fields.

When Rav Shimi returned to Abaye, Abaye confronted him: *k'bei trei avadt li* — you issued contradictory rulings on my behalf? This attribution to me is entirely inappropriate. As a consequence, Abaye refused to eat the produce of those fields for the entire year. Tosafot, sensing the need to explain how Rav Shimi could have done something so manifestly unjust, suggests he reasoned that since the law was *kol d'alim gavar* — a total free-for-all — any stratagem was equally valid. But Abaye clearly did not accept that justification.

### Beni Becharmach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בְּנֵי בֵּי חַרְמָךְ, דַּאֲזוּל כְּרוֹ בְּרֵישָׁא דְשַׁנְווֹתָא, וְאַהְדְּרוּהּ וְשַׁדְיוּהּ בְּשִׁילְהֵי נַהֲרָא. אֲתוֹ עִילָּאֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: קָא מַתְקֵיל לְנַהֲרִין! אֲמַר לְהוּ: כְּרוֹ בַּהֲדַיְיהוּ טְפֵי פּוּרְתָּא. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: קָא יָבְשִׁי פֵּירִין. אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ סַלִּיקוּ נַפְשַׁיְיכוּ מֵהָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that there were certain residents of a place called Bei Ḥarmakh who went and dug a channel at the head of the Shanvata River in order to divert the water and allow it to circle their fields, and then they returned the water to the river further downstream. Those who owned fields further upstream came before Abaye, and said to him: This damages our river, as the water is not flowing as it once had. Abaye said to them: Dig a little deeper with them, and that should solve the problem. They said to him: If we do that, our pits will become dry. Once Abaye heard this he said to the residents of Bei Ḥarmakh: Go remove yourselves from there, and dam the diversion that you made for the river.

The Gemara closes this section with the case of the *bnei Becharmach* — the people of that locale. They dug a canal diverting the river *Shanvata* so that it would flow around their fields and then return to the river's lower course, thereby ensuring their fields received the lion's share of the water. The people upstream, whose water supply was now cut off, came before Abaye complaining: *ka-matzli lana nahara* — they are destroying our river.

Abaye first suggested that they dig the channel deeper, perhaps allowing the water to flow below and reach them more readily; he acknowledged not fully understanding the mechanics but supposed it might help. They replied that digging deeper would lower the water level relative to their orchards and the fruit would dry out. Abaye then said *lizlu ve-lisalku nafshaikhu meihatam* — go and remove yourselves from there. The question is to whom this was addressed. Rashi reads it as directed at the *bnei Becharmach*, telling them they must remove their illegal diversion. But Tosafot reads it as addressed to the complainants — Abaye was telling them he could not help them, since the rule was *kol d'alim gavar*. This is a deeply unsatisfying conclusion. The entire *sugya* has been framed around *darkei shalom*, yet it ends with an outcome that encourages precisely the kind of deceptive and unjust behavior — and the sense of grievance — that *darkei shalom* was meant to prevent.

## Property & Possession

### Traps & Possession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְצוּדוֹת חַיָּה וְעוֹפוֹת וְדָגִים, יֵשׁ בָּהֶן מִשּׁוּם גָּזֵל, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גָּזֵל גָּמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Taking animals, birds, or fish that were caught in traps belonging to another person is considered robbery on account of the ways of peace. And Rabbi Yosei says that this is full-fledged robbery.

The Gemara continues with further applications of *darkei shalom* in the domain of property: *metzudot chaya ve-ofot ve-dagim yesh bahem mishum gezel mipnei darkei shalom* — if one's animal traps catch something, taking what is in them constitutes a form of theft, but only *mipnei darkei shalom*, since full legal possession has not yet been acquired. Rabbi Yossi goes further, ruling it is actual theft. The Gemara asks how this can be real theft if the owner of the trap has not legally acquired the animal.

### Vessels vs Hooks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּאוּזְלֵי וְאוֹהָרֵי – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּלֶחִי וְקוּקְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to nets [*uzlei*] and woven traps [*oharei*], everyone agrees that since they are receptacles that hold the fish or animal entering them, by right the trapped animals belong to the owner of the trap. When they disagree, it is with regard to a fishhook or other traps [*kokrei*] that merely catch the fish or animal but are not receptacles that hold it. In such cases, there is reason to say that the owner of the trap does not take possession of the trapped animal, and therefore another person who takes it is guilty only of robbery on account of the ways of peace.

The Gemara distinguishes between types of traps. When it comes to *ozlei u-oharei* — nets and wicker baskets used for fishing — everyone agrees there is no debate about *darkei shalom* at all; the catch is actually owned by the trap's owner through *kinyan kli*, since the animal is fully enclosed within a vessel belonging to that person. The debate arises specifically with regard to *lechi ve-kukari* — hooks or spiked barriers against which fish or animals become caught without being contained. In such cases there is no *kinyan kli*, and taking what is caught there is prohibited only *mipnei darkei shalom*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: גָּזֵל גָּמוּר. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גָּזֵל גָּמוּר מִדִּבְרֵיהֶם. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְהוֹצִיאוֹ בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei says: It is full-fledged robbery. Rav Ḥisda says: Rabbi Yosei means that it is full-fledged robbery by rabbinic law but not by Torah law. The Gemara asks: What difference is there between full-fledged robbery by rabbinic law and robbery on account of the ways of peace? The Gemara answers: If it is full-fledged robbery by rabbinic law, the victim of robbery can recover the property from the robber by appealing to judges, i.e., the court can expropriate it from him by force.

If Rabbi Yossi holds it is *gezel gamur* — actual theft — Rav Chisda clarifies that he means *gezel gamur mi-divreikhem*, real theft by rabbinic enactment rather than biblical law, since genuine acquisition cannot occur through a trap of this type. The practical difference between the two positions — *darkei shalom* versus *gezel midrabanan* — is whether a beit din can compel the return of what was taken. If the prohibition is merely one of *darkei shalom*, it is a behavioral norm without enforceable judicial consequence; if it is *gezel midrabanan*, a court may order restitution.

### Olive Tree Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָנִי הַמְנַקֵּף בְּרֹאשׁ הַזַּיִת, מַה שֶּׁתַּחְתָּיו וְכוּ׳: תָּנָא: אִם לִיקֵּט וְנָתַן בַּיָּד – הֲרֵי זֶה גָּזֵל גָּמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that if a poor person gleans olives at the top of an olive tree and olives fall to the ground under the tree, then taking those olives that are beneath it is considered robbery on account of the ways of peace. According to Rabbi Yosei, it is full-fledged robbery. A Sage taught: If the poor person gathered the olives and placed them in his hand before they fell to the ground, this is full-fledged robbery, because the poor person had already acquired legal ownership of the olives when they were in his hand.

Similarly, a poor person perched atop an olive tree knocking down olives has not yet taken possession of them. This is prohibited only *mipnei darkei shalom*. However, the Gemara cites a *baraita*: if he plucked the olives with his hand, that is *gezel gamur* — genuine theft — because he has physically taken possession. Tosafot points out that this seems obvious and asks what the novelty is. Tosafot answers that the case involves someone who picked the olive off the branch with his hand but did not hold it in his palm — he simply pulled it from the tree and let it fall. One might have thought that since the olive never rested in his hand, there was no act of *kinyan*. The *baraita* teaches that even so, the act of *tlisha* — detaching it from the tree — constitutes sufficient acquisition, and it is *gezel gamur* at the biblical level.

### Dates Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲוָה קָאָזֵיל לְהוּצָל, חַזְיֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה שָׁדֵי אוּפְיֵי וְקָא נָתְרָן תַּמְרֵי, אֲזַל קָא מְנַקֵּיט וְאָכֵיל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי מָר דְּבִידַאי שְׁדֵיתִינְהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵאַתְרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אַתָּה. קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ: ״וְצַדִּיק יְסוֹד עוֹלָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that Rav Kahana was once walking to the city of Huzal when he saw a certain man who was throwing sticks at a palm tree and dates were falling to the ground. Rav Kahana went, gathered up some of the dates, and ate them. That man said to Rav Kahana: See, Master, that I threw them down with my hand, i.e., the dates were already in my hand, and therefore they are legally mine. Rav Kahana said to him: You are from the place of Rabbi Yoshiya, who was a great Sage in the city of Huzal. For that reason, you are knowledgeable in *halakha*. Rav Kahana read the verse about Rabbi Yoshiya: "And a righteous man is the foundation of the world" (Proverbs 10:25). Even after his death, Rabbi Yoshiya left a foundation for the world, as his city continued to be a center of Torah study.

The Gemara then relates a story involving Rav Kahana. He was traveling and observed a man throwing palm branches up into a date palm, causing dates to fall. According to Rashi, the man was hurling the branches like javelins to knock down the dates. Rav Kahana went over and ate the dates, reasoning the man had not yet taken possession of them. The man then challenged him: *chazi mar de-vi-yadash dasin hu* — look, I knocked those down with my own hands. Whether the man was claiming *gezel gamur* or merely *darkei shalom* is not fully clear.

Rav Kahana responded: *m'atra de-Rav Yoshiya at* — you must be from the place where Rav Yoshiya teaches. He quoted the verse: *v'tzaddik yesod olam* — the righteous is the foundation of the world — implying that Rav Yoshiya's teachings had properly shaped this man's halakhic sensibility.

Tosafot offers a different reading of the story. In Tosafot's version, the man was not hurling branches to knock down dates but was cutting down the palm branches from atop the tree while the dates fell accidentally. Rav Kahana assumed the man was interested only in the branches and not the dates, so that the falling dates were *hefker* and he could take them. The man then corrected him: I am also shaking the tree in order to bring down the dates, so they are mine, and the *darkei shalom* prohibition applies.

## Non-Jewish Poor

### Braita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְמַחִין בְּיַד עֲנִיֵּי גוֹיִם בְּלֶקֶט בְּשִׁכְחָה וּבְפֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם: תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְפַרְנְסִים עֲנִיֵּי גוֹיִם עִם עֲנִיֵּי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וּמְבַקְּרִין חוֹלֵי גוֹיִם עִם חוֹלֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, וְקוֹבְרִין מֵתֵי גוֹיִם עִם מֵתֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל, מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** One does not protest against poor gentiles who come to take gleanings, forgotten sheaves, and the produce in the corner of the field, which is given to the poor [*pe'a*], although they are meant exclusively for the Jewish poor, on account of the ways of peace. Similarly, the Sages taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 5:4): One sustains poor gentiles along with poor Jews, and one visits sick gentiles along with sick Jews, and one buries dead gentiles along with dead Jews. All this is done on account of the ways of peace, to foster peaceful relations between Jews and gentiles.

The Gemara turns to another dimension of *darkei shalom*: obligations toward non-Jews. The Mishnah states: *ein memachin be-yad aniyei nochrim be-leket shikha u-feah mipnei darkei shalom* — one does not prevent the non-Jewish poor from collecting *leket*, *shikha*, and *peah* because of *darkei shalom*. This is a relatively passive form of *darkei shalom*: one does not proactively give but simply does not obstruct.

The Gemara then quotes a *baraita*: *mefarnesin aniyei nochrim im aniyei Yisrael* — one sustains the poor of the non-Jews together with the poor of Israel; *u-mevakrin cholei nochrim im cholei Yisrael* — one visits the sick of the non-Jews together with the sick of Israel; *ve-kovrin metei nochrim im metei Yisrael* — one buries the dead of the non-Jews together with the dead of Israel; all of this *mipnei darkei shalom*. The word for non-Jews in the *baraita* is *nochrim* rather than *Akum*, signaling that this applies to all non-Jews without distinction.

### Rashi's Reading

A number of questions arise immediately. First, what does the word *im* — "with" or "together" — mean? Does it imply burying non-Jews in the same cemetery as Jews? Rashi rules it does not. Rashi explains that *im* means in the context of the same communal effort: if one is engaged in an effort of burying the dead and there are both Jews and non-Jews involved, one attends to both. Similarly, if one is feeding the poor, one includes the non-Jewish poor in that same effort. The primary obligation is oriented toward *Yisrael*, but one does not exclude the non-Jewish poor encountered in the same circumstances. This reading is careful and perhaps measured — it does not assert an independent primary obligation but rather a policy of inclusion within efforts already directed toward the Jewish community.

The question of what *darkei shalom* means in this context is itself significant. The most self-serving reading would be that we treat non-Jews well so they will treat us well — a kind of *mipnei eiva* logic. But it is worth noting that the *darkei shalom* of earlier portions of the chapter was not self-serving in that narrow sense. When it came to establishing priority for *aliyot* or setting norms for the *eruv*, the point was to build a society and community with less conflict, to establish conventions that allowed people to live together better. One can read the obligation toward the non-Jewish poor in the same light: *darkei shalom* as the cultivation of a broader peaceful social order, not merely an instrumental calculation.

### Rambam View

Maimonides' approach to this passage is striking. Recall that at the beginning of the broader *sugya* on *darkei shalom*, the Gemara considered whether honoring the *kohen* was biblical or only *darkei shalom*, and ultimately stated that the entire Torah is *mipnei darkei shalom* — for it says *dracheha darchei noam ve-chol netivoteha shalom*, its ways are ways of pleasantness and all its paths are peace. Rambam, when codifying the obligation to sustain the non-Jewish poor *mipnei darkei shalom*, invokes precisely this framework: *vaharei ne'emar tov Hashem la-kol ve-rachamav al kol ma'asav* — for it is said that God is good to all and His mercies extend over all His creatures — *ve-ne'emar dracheha darchei noam ve-chol netivoteha shalom*. For Rambam, *mipnei darkei shalom* is not a pragmatic accommodation but an expression of the core moral values of the Torah. Acting in accordance with *darkei shalom* means acting in accordance with God's own attribute of *rachamim* over all creation, and living a life shaped by Torah values of care and concern for others.

### Reciprocity Theme

A further dimension of this passage was noted by Suzanne Stone, who observed that the logic of *darkei shalom* is often presented as unidirectional: we are generous to non-Jews so they will be generous to us. But she proposed reversing the framing entirely: because of all that we receive from living within a broader society — all that we benefit from our participation in a larger human community — what are our reciprocal obligations toward that society? *Darkei shalom*, on this reading, is not primarily self-interested but arises from an awareness of what we owe to those alongside whom we live. The emphasis shifts from instrumental self-protection to genuine social responsibility. This is a considerably more demanding and more morally compelling reading, and it is worth appreciating that when the Gemara presents this principle it may have precisely this richer meaning in view.

### Tosefta Version

The *Tosefta* on this subject — in chapter 3, section 18 — goes considerably further than the Mishnah. It opens with the striking ruling that in a city containing both Jews and non-Jews, the *parnasim* — the communal charity collectors — collect from both Jews and *ovdei kokhavim* because of *darkei shalom*. This is not merely a case of not excluding non-Jews from what one is already doing for Jews; it is a statement of shared communal responsibility. The non-Jews are taxed alongside the Jews for communal welfare purposes. The *Tosefta* then lists the same obligations — feeding the poor, eulogizing and burying the dead, comforting mourners — but notably does not include the phrase *im Yisrael* in each case. The formulations stand independently, without the qualifier that limits the obligation to circumstances in which one is already acting on behalf of Jews. The *Tosefta* thus projects a more expansive and more egalitarian vision of *darkei shalom* than does the Mishnah. It is worth noting that the *Tosefta* was composed in Eretz Yisrael, as was the Mishnah — yet Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi chose in the Mishnah to articulate a considerably more limited version of this principle than the *Tosefta* contains.

## Lending & Suspicion

### Shemitah Lending

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַשְׁאֶלֶת אִשָּׁה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ הַחֲשׁוּדָה עַל הַשְּׁבִיעִית – נָפָה, וּכְבָרָה, וְרֵיחַיִם, וְתַנּוּר. אֲבָל לֹא תָּבוֹר וְלֹא תִּטְחַן עִמָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman may lend utensils to her friend who is suspect with regard to eating produce that grew in the Sabbatical Year after the time that such produce must be removed from the house and may no longer be eaten. The utensils that she may lend her include: A winnow, a sieve, a mill, and an oven. Lending her such utensils is not considered aiding in the commission of a transgression. But she may not select the grain from the chaff or grind wheat with her, i.e., she may not actively assist her in the performance of a sin.

The Gemara returns to further applications of *darkei shalom*, now examining when one is permitted to lend tools or otherwise assist someone suspected of violating religious law. The Mishnah rules: *mash'elet ishah le-chavertah ha-chashudah al ha-shevi'it nafah ve-kevar ve-reichayim ve-tannur* — a woman may lend her neighbor who is suspected of disregarding the laws of *shemitah* a sieve, a colander, a millstone, and an oven, despite the fact that such neighbor may be using them to process *shemitah* produce she should not have. *Aval* — however — she may not actively grind or sift together with her, since that would cross the line into *mesayea yedei oveir aveirah*.

Tosafot brings into the discussion the parallel *sugya* in Avodah Zarah regarding *lifnei iver*. The question is how one can lend tools to someone when one knows, or strongly suspects, they will use them for a prohibited purpose. Tosafot notes that if there were genuine doubt about the intended use, no *darkei shalom* argument would be needed — the lending would be permitted in any case since the item might be used legitimately. Therefore, Tosafot concludes, the Mishnah must be dealing with a case where one is fairly certain the neighbor will use the tools for *shemitah* produce. This raises the difficulty: if so, why is it not *lifnei iver*? Tosafot does not fully resolve this, but one possible answer — developed by Tosafot in other contexts — is that since the borrower could obtain the same items from someone else, the lender is not the direct enabler of the violation. This principle became a major basis in the medieval period for permitting Jews to sell certain items to non-Jews: since the non-Jew could acquire the item from another source, the Jewish seller was not the cause of the prohibition. The Mishnah's ruling, then, permits lending but draws the line at active participation in the processing.

### Am Ha'aretz Wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵשֶׁת חָבֵר מַשְׁאֶלֶת לְאֵשֶׁת עַם הָאָרֶץ – נָפָה וּכְבָרָה; וּבוֹרֶרֶת, וְטוֹחֶנֶת, וּמְרַקֶּדֶת עִמָּהּ; אֲבָל מִשֶּׁתָּטִיל אֶת הַמַּיִם – לֹא תִּגַּע עִמָּהּ, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַחְזִיקִין יְדֵי עוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה. וְכוּלָּן לֹא אָמְרוּ, אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי דַּרְכֵי שָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The wife of a *ḥaver*, one who is devoted to the meticulous observance of mitzvot, especially the *halakhot* of ritual purity, *teruma*, and tithes, may lend the wife of an *am ha'aretz*, one who is not scrupulous in these areas, a winnow and a sieve, and she may even select, grind, and sift with her. But once the wife of the *am ha'aretz* pours water into the flour, thereby rendering it susceptible to ritual impurity, the wife of the *ḥaver* may not touch anything with her, because one may not assist those who commit transgressions. And all of the allowances mentioned in the mishna were stated only on account of the ways of peace.

The Mishnah continues with a further case: the wife of a *chaver* — someone meticulous about *terumos u-ma'asros* and *tumah ve-taharah* — may lend to the wife of an *am ha-aretz* a sieve, a colander, and a millstone, and may actively sort, grind, and sift together with her. Yet once water has been added to the flour — once dough is being prepared — she may not continue to work with her, because *ein machzikin yedei ovrei aveirah* — we do not strengthen the hands of those who transgress. *Machzik* implies more than merely enabling; it means actively encouraging or affirming the transgression by one's participation.

This *halakha* immediately invites a comparison with the previous case. There, you were not permitted to actively grind together with the *shemitah* suspect; here you are. What is the distinction?

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רוֹב עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ מְעַשְּׂרִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks with regard to the *halakhot* taught in the mishna: What is different in the first clause of the mishna that teaches that a woman may not select and grind grain with a woman who is suspected of eating produce of the Sabbatical Year after it is forbidden, and what is different in the latter clause that teaches that it is permitted for the wife of a *ḥaver* to assist the wife of an *am ha'aretz* in her selecting and grinding? Abaye said: Most *amei ha'aretz* tithe their produce, and therefore there is no reason to render it prohibited to assist the wife of an *am ha'aretz* in her work, as she is probably preparing a permitted food. Although there may be concern that the food was not tithed due to the minority of *amei ha'aretz* who do not separate tithes, this concern is ignored on account of the ways of peace.

Abaye answers: *rov ammei ha-aretz me'asrin hen* — the majority of *ammei ha-aretz* do in fact tithe their produce. The concern about *tevel* is therefore addressed by the principle of *rov*: we can assume, probabilistically, that the flour she is working with has been properly tithed. Since the risk of enabling a *terumos u-ma'asros* violation is low enough to be set aside by majority, *darkei shalom* can override it. But once water touches the grain and it becomes dough, it becomes *chayav be-challah*, and if the *am ha-aretz* wife handles it in a state of *tumah*, she renders the *challah* portion *tamei* — and that is a more sensitive concern that cannot be overridden by *darkei shalom*.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָכָא בְּעַם הָאָרֶץ דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְטוּמְאָה וְטׇהֳרָה דְּרַבָּנַן – דְּתַנְיָא: אֵיזֶהוּ עַם הָאָרֶץ? כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל חוּלָּיו בְּטָהֳרָה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֹּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְעַשֵּׂר פֵּירוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: Here the mishna speaks about the *am ha'aretz* as defined by Rabbi Meir and the issue of ritual impurity and purity by rabbinic law. It does not speak about the matter of separating *teruma* and tithes. As it is taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Avoda Zara* 3:10): Who is an *am ha'aretz*? Anyone who does not eat his non-sacred produce in a state of ritual purity; this is the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: An *am ha'aretz* is anyone who does not tithe his produce. Since the mishna is referring to the type of *am ha'aretz* about whom there is an assumption that he tithes his produce but does not eat his non-sacred produce in a state of ritual purity, and in light of the fact that eating non-sacred produce in a state of ritual purity is stipulated by rabbinic law, on account of the ways of peace, the Sages did not prohibit the wife of a *ḥaver* from assisting the wife of an *am ha'aretz*.

Rava offers a different explanation. The *am ha-aretz* of this Mishnah is not primarily a *terumos u-ma'asros* concern at all. Rava cites a *baraita*: Rabbi Meir defines *am ha-aretz* as one who does not eat *chullin be-tahara*, while the Sages define *am ha-aretz* as one who does not tithe his produce. Rava adopts Rabbi Meir's definition for the Mishnah's case: we are dealing with an *am ha-aretz* with respect to *tumah ve-taharah* only, and the problem is entirely about *tumah*. Before water touches the grain it cannot become *tamei*, so there is no violation to be concerned about, and working with her is permitted. Once water is added, the grain becomes susceptible to *tumah*, and if she contaminates it she creates *chulin tamei* — which is merely a rabbinic *chaver* concern — but she also contaminates the *challah* that is now embedded in the dough, and *challah* is a form of *terumah*, whose defilement is a biblical prohibition. At that point the wife of the *chaver* must stop.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: מִשֶּׁתָּטִיל אֶת הַמַּיִם לֹא תִּגַּע עִמָּהּ; מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא, לָאו בְּטוּמְאָה וְטׇהֳרָה עָסְקִינַן! רֵישָׁא וְסֵיפָא בְּטוּמְאָה וְטׇהֳרָה; וְרֵישָׁא – בְּטוּמְאַת חוּלִּין, וְסֵיפָא – בְּטוּמְאַת חַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: But from the fact that the latter clause of the mishna teaches: Once the wife of the *am ha'aretz* pours water into the flour, the wife of the *ḥaver* may not touch anything with her because the water has rendered the dough susceptible to ritual impurity, it may be inferred that in the first clause in the mishna we are not dealing with concern about the *halakhot* of impurity and purity. Rather, the concern pertains to tithes. The Gemara answers: Both in the first clause and in the latter clause the concern relates to impurity and purity. The difference is that in the first clause, even if the grain was already rendered susceptible to impurity, the concern is only about impurity of non-sacred produce. Rendering non-sacred produce impure is not prohibited by Torah law; it is a matter about which *ḥaverim* were meticulous. But in the latter clause, the concern is the impurity of *ḥalla*, the portion that must be separated from the dough and given to a priest. It is at the time that water is added to the flour that the obligation to separate *ḥalla* from the dough takes effect. Due to the *ḥalla* that will be separated from the dough, it is prohibited by Torah law for one to render the dough impure.

The Gemara then challenges Rava's reading from a *baraita* which states that you may not help someone who eats their produce in *tumah*, implying this applies even to ordinary *chulin*. But according to Rava, helping with *chulin be-tumah* should be permitted. Abaye resolves the contradiction: that *baraita* is dealing specifically with a *kohen* who eats *terumah* in a state of *tumah*, which is a biblical violation. That is why one cannot assist him — it is a *tumah de-oraita* concern.

## Entrust & Swap

### Grain Grinding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טוֹחֲנִין וּמַפְקִידִין אֵצֶל אוֹכְלֵי שְׁבִיעִית, וְאֵצֶל אוֹכְלֵי פֵירוֹתֵיהֶן בְּטוּמְאָה; אֲבָל לֹא לְאוֹכְלֵי שְׁבִיעִית, וְלֹא לְאוֹכְלֵי פֵירוֹתֵיהֶן בְּטוּמְאָה!
>
> **Translation:** One may grind one's tithed produce and deposit it with those who eat Sabbatical-Year produce and those who eat their own produce in a state of impurity, because there is no concern that they will exchange this produce with Sabbatical-Year produce or with impure produce, or that they will touch the produce. But one may not grind for those who eat Sabbatical-Year produce or for those who eat their own produce in a state of impurity, so as not to assist them in committing a transgression. This is difficult according to Rava's explanation of the mishna, that the wife of a *ḥaver* may grind non-sacred produce with the wife of an *am ha'aretz*, who eats his own produce in a state of ritual impurity.

The Gemara continues with the question of entrusting grain to those who may not be careful about *tumah* or *shemitah*. A new text is introduced: *tochnin u-mafkidin etzel ochlei shevi'it ve-etzel ochlei peroteihen be-tumah* — one may grind grain and entrust it with those who eat *shemitah* produce or who eat their produce in *tumah*. The rationale for the absence of concern is that we trust people to be careful about other people's property even if they are not careful about their own. However, you may not grind on behalf of someone who eats *shemitah* produce or on behalf of someone who eats in *tumah*, because doing so would be *mesayea yedei ovrei aveirah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָתָם, בְּכֹהֵן הֶחָשׁוּד לֶאֱכוֹל תְּרוּמָה בְּטוּמְאָה עָסְקִינַן – דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ טוּמְאָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said: There, in that *baraita*, we are dealing with a priest who is suspected with regard to partaking of *teruma* in a state of ritual impurity, which involves a prohibition of impurity by Torah law.

This last clause — that you cannot help someone who eats their produce in *tumah* — creates a tension with Rava's position, since Rava permits helping someone with *chulin be-tumah*. The Gemara's earlier resolution, attributed to Abaye, is that the case involves a *kohen* eating *terumah* in *tumah*, which is a biblical violation. The Gemara then asks: if we trust people to handle others' grain carefully — even a *kohen am ha-aretz* who is known to be comfortable with *tumah* — how can we do so if he might render it *tamei* by contact? The Gemara brings another text establishing that one may entrust *terumah* with an ordinary *Yisrael am ha-aretz* but not with a *kohen am ha-aretz*, precisely because the *kohen* feels at ease around *terumah* and is not careful. Rabbi Ila resolves this by positing the case involves a pottery vessel sealed with a tight cover (*kaveiret* with *tzamid patil*), so that even if the *kohen* handles the vessel the *terumah* inside cannot become *tamei*. This resolution is admittedly not *peshat* of the *baraita*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: סְכַר מִיסְכָּר, וְאַשְׁקִי בְּהִינְדָּזָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וּרְמִינְהוּ: הַנּוֹתֵן לַחֲמוֹתוֹ, מְעַשֵּׂר אֵת שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן לָהּ וְאֵת שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֵל הֵימֶנָּא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁחֲשׁוּדָה מַחְלֶפֶת הַמִּתְקַלְקֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: even with a sealed vessel, one should be concerned that the *kohen*'s wife, when she is a *niddah*, might move the vessel, and since *tumas ha-guf* can convey *tumah* to objects through moving them, the sealed vessel would not protect the contents. Rabbi Yirmiyah therefore offers a different resolution: the case where one need not be concerned about the *kohen* touching the produce is specifically a case of grain that has not come in contact with water — *peirot she-lo huchsharu*. Before being soaked, produce cannot become *tamei* regardless of contact, so there is nothing to worry about. This again resolves the apparent contradiction but is concededly not the straightforward reading of the *baraita*. The Gemara then introduces yet another text: *ha-molikh chittin le-tochein Kuti o le-tochein am ha-aretz harei elu be-chezkasan* — if one brings wheat to a Samaritan miller or to an *am ha-aretz* miller, one may assume upon receiving it back that it has not been swapped, and one need not take *terumos u-ma'asros* again or be concerned about *shemitah* produce having been substituted. However, one must be concerned about *tumah* — that the miller may have come into contact with the grain. The Gemara notes this seems to contradict the earlier conclusion that we are not always worried about *tumah* by such individuals. But the Gemara responds: it does not contradict it, since the only cases where we said there is no concern are those where the grain had not come in contact with water — making the matter of *tumah* irrelevant in any case. The Gemara then acknowledges something unusual: this text was not introduced to raise a genuine difficulty; it was introduced to lay the groundwork for the next question, which is about the concern of swapping.

### Mother-in-Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּדְקָתָנֵי טַעְמָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: רוֹצֶה הִיא בְּתַקָּנַת בִּתָּהּ, וּבוֹשָׁה מֵחֲתָנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that there the concern is for the reason that was explicitly taught in that mishna: Rabbi Yehuda said: His mother-in-law wants what is good for her daughter, that she not eat anything that is spoiled, and she is ashamed before her son-in-law to tell him that he had given her something that was spoiled. For this reason, she does not tell him that she exchanged the food.

The *baraita* just cited said we are not concerned that the miller swapped out the grain. Is this really true as a general principle? The Gemara challenges from the Mishnah: *ha-noten la-chamoto* — if one gives flour to one's mother-in-law to prepare baked goods, one must take *terumos u-ma'asros* from the flour given to her, and again from the finished product received back. The reason: we are concerned she swapped out the flour. Specifically, she is suspected of swapping when something goes wrong: *mipnei she-chashudah mechalfet ha-miskalkeil* — she will swap out flour that has spoiled or baked goods that have fallen. The mother-in-law is not trying to defraud; she is trying to do good for her daughter. If her first attempt at baking fails, she will discard it, bake again from her own flour, and return a beautiful cake — but that cake is now made from her own un-tithed flour, so both what was given to her and what was received back must be subject to *ma'aser*. The Gemara accepts this as a special case: the concern for swapping does not apply generally but arises specifically because of the mother-in-law's emotional investment. Rav Yehuda articulates it: *rotzah hee be-takkunat bitah u-voshah mi-chasna* — she wants her daughter's household to be well-served, and she is embarrassed in front of her son-in-law.

### Innkeeper Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְעָלְמָא – לָא חָיְישִׁינַן?! וְהָתְנַן: הַנּוֹתֵן לַפּוּנְדָּקִית, מְעַשֵּׂר אֶת שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן לָהּ וְאֶת שֶׁהוּא נוֹטֵל הֵימֶנָּה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּחְלֶפֶת! הָתָם מוֹרְיָא וְאָמְרָה: בַּר בֵּי רַב לֵיכוֹל חַמִּימָא, וַאֲנָא אֵיכוֹל קָרִירָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And is this to say that in the case of ordinary people we are not concerned that food will be exchanged in a similar situation? But didn't we learn in a mishna (*Demai* 3:5): If one gives food to an innkeeper [*pundakit*] so that she can prepare it for him, he must tithe everything that he gives her and everything that he takes from her, because she exchanges the food received from him with food of her own? This indicates that the concern is not limited to the case of a mother-in-law. The Gemara answers: There, the innkeeper rules for herself that it is permitted for her to do this and says: Why should this student of Torah eat hot food while I will eat cold food? In other words, the innkeeper may justify her behavior to herself and exchange his food for hers.

The Gemara then tests the principle further. If one gives flour to a woman innkeeper to bake bread for a traveler, the same *halakha* applies: one must take *terumos u-ma'asros* both before and after, because the innkeeper is also suspected of swapping. But here the motive is entirely different. Rav Yehuda explains: *bar bei rav lachol chamima, ve-ana achol karira* — she reasons, "Should this yeshiva student get fresh bread while I eat my day-old stale bread?" The innkeeper swaps not out of warmth but out of a sense of resentment at the inequity. In both cases the swap is driven by a subjective emotional response to the transaction, and both are recognized as occasions for concern. But these remain special cases — not the general rule — and as a matter of principle one does not assume that every miller or handler has swapped out the grain entrusted to him.
