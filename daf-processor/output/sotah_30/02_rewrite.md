# Sotah 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sheini/Shelishi/Revii

### Tvul Yom & Mechussar

The discussion on this *daf* picks up from the bottom of 29b, deep in the analysis of the degrees of *tumah* and how they propagate across different categories of food. The foundational structure of the argument is this: we learn that there is a concept of *shlishi bi-terumah* — that *terumah* can become *tamei* at a third degree of remove — from the case of a *tvul yom*, a person who has immersed in a *mikvah* but whose *taharah* is not yet complete until nightfall. Such a person, though carrying only residual *tumah*, is forbidden to eat *terumah* but is permitted to eat *chulin*. The analogy then runs: if a person with residual *tumah* is forbidden to eat *terumah*, then food which is itself *tamei* — a *sheini* — should certainly be capable of rendering *terumah tamei*, producing a *shlishi*.

The second step extends the logic further. A *mechussar kippurim* — one who has immersed and whose *taharah* is complete but who has not yet brought the required atonement offerings — is permitted to eat *terumah* but is forbidden to eat *kodashim*. By the same analogical structure, if a person with this residual status cannot partake of *kodashim*, then food which is itself *tamei* for *terumah* — a *shlishi* — should certainly be capable of rendering *kodashim tamei* at a fourth degree, producing a *revii*. These derivations proceed via *kal va-chomer* or *gezerah shavah*, not as *halachah le-Moshe mi-Sinai*.

### Yochanan's Challenge

Rabbi Yochanan objects to the entire structure of this *drashah* attributed to Rabbi Yose, declaring that he cannot understand it — *ta'am b'Rebbi eini yodea* — because the refutation lies right alongside the argument. He introduces a counterexample: *ochel ha-ba machmat tvul yom* — food that was touched by a *tvul yom*. Such food, namely *terumah* touched by a *tvul yom*, would effectively be a *shlishi*. According to the logic of the derivation, it should then be capable of rendering *kodashim* into a *revii*. Yet we know that this is not the case: such food is *pasul bi-terumah* — it invalidates the *terumah* — but *eino oseh revii ba-kodesh*, it does not go on to produce a *revii* in *kodashim*. The existence of this category — things that are *pasul* for *terumah* and yet do not transmit *tumah* further into *kodashim* — undermines the inference from *mechussar kippurim* to *shlishi bi-terumah* making *kodashim* into a *revii*. If the pattern were consistent, Rabbi Yochanan argues, why should we assume that a *shlishi* of *terumah* — which proceeds through food all the way down — passes the *tumah* into *kodashim* when the analogous case of food derived from a *tvul yom* does not?

### Tvul Yom Food Status

The question is how we know that food touched by a *tvul yom* does not make *kodashim* into a *revii*. The Gemara cites a *Braitah* in which a dispute among *Tannaim* is recorded. Abba Shaul holds a stringency *de-rabbanan*: a *tvul yom*, when it comes to matters of *kodashim*, is treated as a *rishon*, not merely as a *sheini*. The formulation is *metamei shnayim u-fosel echad* — he renders two levels *tamei* and invalidates one further level. In practical terms, for *kodshim* purposes, the *tvul yom* touching food makes the food a *sheini*, which touching further food makes it a *shlishi*, and that *shlishi* touching *kodesh* renders it *pasul* as a *revii*. So according to Abba Shaul, the *tumah* deriving from a *tvul yom* does in fact descend all the way to a *revii* in *kodashim*. Rabbi Meir holds that the *tvul yom* is treated as a *sheini* — *metamei echad u-fosel echad* — making *terumah* into a *shlishi*, which then renders *kodesh* into a *revii*. So according to both Abba Shaul and Rabbi Meir, the *tumah* of the *tvul yom* does proceed through to a *revii* in *kodashim*.

The position that creates a genuine counterexample is that of the *Chachamim*, who say: just as a *tvul yom* invalidates *terumah* food and liquid, so too he invalidates *kodashim* food and liquid — but it stops there. The food that he invalidated as a *shlishi* does not go on to render *kodashim* into a *revii*. If we rule like the *Chachamim*, then food touched by a *tvul yom* is indeed a counterexample to the proposed derivation from *mechussar kippurim*.

### Rav Pappa Defense

Rav Pappa challenges this framing: *be-mai* — upon what basis does Rabbi Yochanan's objection rest? It rests on the assumption that the ruling follows the *Rabbanan*, who say the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* stops at the *shlishi* and does not extend to a *revii* in *kodashim*. But perhaps the *drashah* of Rabbi Yose follows the position of Abba Shaul, who holds that the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* does reach a *revii* in *kodashim*. According to Abba Shaul, even though this represents a rabbinic stringency in its characterization of the *tvul yom* as a *rishon*, the underlying principle is that *tumah* deriving from a *tvul yom* can descend two full levels to render *kodashim* into a *revii*. Under that view, there is no counterexample at all.

### Countermove Analysis

The Gemara responds that if we accept Abba Shaul's position — that the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* does pass all the way through to render *kodashim* into a *revii* — then there is a different problem. If this is consistent rather than a counterexample, then there is no need to derive the rule about *revii* in *kodashim* from the case of *mechussar kippurim* at all. The Gemara proposes an alternative derivation: *u-mah ochel ha-ba machmat tvul yom* — if food that comes from a *tvul yom*, who himself is *mutar be-chulin*, whose *tumah* starts at a relatively weak point, nonetheless generates *tumah* that descends all the way to produce a *revii* in *kodashim*, then certainly *ochel shlishi ha-ba machmat sheni* — food that is a *shlishi* deriving from a *sheini* — where the *sheini* itself, unlike the *tvul yom*, is *assur be-chulin*, should certainly produce a *revii* in *kodashim* by *kal va-chomer*. The Gemara is asking: why did the *drashah* run to the case of *mechussar kippurim* when, if Abba Shaul is correct, there was a ready inference available from the case of food touched by a *tvul yom*?

### Tzad Hashaveh

The Gemara then considers whether one might deflect this proposed inference with a *pircha*: perhaps the *tvul yom* case cannot serve as the basis for the derivation because *mah la-tvul yom she-ken av tumah* — the *tvul yom* could be an *av tumah*, a primary source of *tumah*, which is a stringency that food can never possess. Food can never be an *av tumah*, and so one cannot argue from the *tvul yom*'s *tumah* extending to a *revii* in *kodashim* to the same being true for food that is itself *tamei*. The Gemara notes that this same *pircha* would apply equally to the case of the *mechussar kippurim* — he too is a person, who could potentially be an *av tumah*.

### Person-to-Object Challen…

The challenge of learning from a person to food is a recurring structural problem in this entire *sugya*. The *tvul yom* and the *mechussar kippurim* are both persons, and persons have a dimension of *tumah* — their potential to be an *av tumah* — that food simply does not share. This asymmetry means that any inference from the person's *tumah* behavior to what food does with *tumah* is potentially flawed by this *pircha*. It is worth noting that this objection could have been raised much earlier in the entire deliberation; indeed, it was, and the Gemara dealt with it by invoking a *tzad ha-shaveh* involving *keli cheres*, drawing on the *Sotah* context in which the very problematic of persons being *tamei* generating rules about objects being *tamei* is central to the tractate.

### Summary & Closure

To summarize the core of this difficult passage: the Gemara seeks to establish, through derivation, that a *sheini* can make *terumah* into a *shlishi*, and that a *shlishi* of *terumah* can make *kodashim* into a *revii*. The first is derived by analogy to the *tvul yom* who is forbidden from *terumah* yet permitted with *chulin* — so *chulin* that is itself *tamei* as a *sheini* should make *terumah* into a *shlishi*. The second is derived by analogy to the *mechussar kippurim* who may eat *terumah* yet is forbidden from *kodashim* — so *terumah* that is itself *tamei* as a *shlishi* should render *kodashim* into a *revii*. The Gemara's challenge comes from the case of food touched by a *tvul yom*, which is *pasul* for *terumah* yet — according to the *Chachamim* — does not pass its *tumah* on to *kodashim*. This is a counterexample if we follow the *Chachamim*; but if we follow Abba Shaul and say it is not a counterexample, then why was it not itself used as the basis for the derivation? The Gemara leaves the tension unresolved in a fully satisfying way, and ends by noting that the challenge of learning from a person to food — the *pircha* that persons can be *avos tumah* while food cannot — remains a relevant critique of both potential sources of derivation, even if it was not formally pressed to its ultimate conclusion.

## Shelishi Limits

### Rav Assi Statement

The Gemara now introduces a broad statement from Rav Assi in the name of Rav — with an alternative tradition attributing it to Rabba bar bar Assi in the name of Rav — establishing that the following *Tannaim* all share a single foundational position: Rabbi Meir, Rabbi Yose, Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabbi Elazar — all of them hold *de-ein sheni oseh shlishi be-chulin*, that a *sheini* does not make a *shlishi* in ordinary *chulin*. The category of *shlishi* is limited to *terumah*; ordinary *chulin* does not achieve third-degree *tumah* status. This point is worth pausing on, because the *limudim* of the preceding discussion — the *tvul yom* and *mechussar kippurim* derivations — were in each case explicitly about *terumah*. The *tvul yom* who may not eat *terumah*, the *mechussar kippurim* who may not eat *kodashim* — both derivations operated within the world of *terumah* and *kodashim*, never extending the rule of *shlishi* to *chulin*. Nevertheless, Rashi on the *Mishnah* had noted that Rabbi Akiva's derivation from the *pasuk* of *kol asher yipol me-hem el tocho yitma* — *yitma le-tamei acherim*, that the loaf in the *keli cheres* is *tamei* to make other things into a *shlishi* — contains nothing in its text specifically limiting it to *terumah*. It sounds as though Rabbi Akiva's *pasuk*-based derivation would extend *shlishi* even to *chulin*. The Gemara now reinforces that the consensus of *Tannaim* restricts *shlishi* to *terumah*, and proceeds to demonstrate this from each authority's rulings.

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir is sourced from a *Mishnah*: *kol ha-ta'un bi'at mayim mi-divrei soferim* — anything that requires immersion by rabbinic decree. The paradigmatic case of rabbinic *tumah* requiring immersion is human hands. Hands were assigned a default status of *sheini le-tumah* by rabbinic enactment, because their condition is uncertain — one does not know what they have touched. This is a *tumah* that exists only *de-rabbanan*; *de-oraita* it is impossible for a part of the body to be *tamei* while the rest is not, but rabbinically they assigned this status. The consequence is that one washes hands before eating *terumah* since hands in this *sheini* status cannot handle *terumah* — and this practice eventually extended to washing before eating bread even for non-*kohanim*.

The rule the *Mishnah* articulates is: *kol ha-tamei mi-divrei soferim metamei et ha-kodesh u-fosel et ha-terumah*. Anyone who is *tamei* by rabbinic decree — thus effectively a *sheini* — *metamei et ha-kodesh*, renders *kodesh tamei*, and *fosel et ha-terumah*, invalidates *terumah*. The distinction between *metamei* and *fosel* is crucial: *metamei* means the *tumah* can be transmitted one further level, whereas *fosel* means it invalidates but does not transmit further. So if such a person touches *kodesh*, they make it a *shlishi*, and that *shlishi* can render other *kodesh* into a *revii* — since *kodesh* goes up to *revii*. But if they touch *terumah*, it is merely *pasul* without passing on further. And the continuation: *u-mutar be-chulin u-ve-ma'aser* — this person is permitted to handle *chulin* and *ma'aser*; *chulin* is entirely unaffected. This is explicit: a *sheini* does not produce a *shlishi* in *chulin*. These are the words of Rabbi Meir. The *Chachamim* add *ma'aser* to the list of things that are affected — the debate about *ma'aser* concerns whether *ma'aser* is treated for these purposes like *kodesh* — but both Rabbi Meir and the *Chachamim* agree unambiguously that *sheini* does not make a *shlishi* in *chulin*.

### Rabbi Yose Position

Rabbi Yose's position is sourced from the very derivation discussed earlier in this *sugya* — *ha d'amaran*, the entire preceding analysis. The evidence that Rabbi Yose does not hold *sheini* makes *shlishi* in *chulin* comes not from what he said but from what he did not say. Had Rabbi Yose held that *chulin* could become a *shlishi*, his derivation would have needed to go further: *de-im ita, leisei le-revii bi-terumah va-chamishi be-kodesh*. If *chulin* itself could be a *shlishi*, then *terumah* should have been able to reach a *revii*, and *kodesh* should have been able to reach a *chamishi*, a fifth degree. The logic flows from the same *tvul yom* analogy: a *tvul yom* who is *mutar be-chulin* renders *terumah tamei*, so *chulin tamei* as a *sheini* should render *terumah* into a *shlishi*. But if *chulin* could be a *shlishi*, then by the same logic *chulin tamei* as a *shlishi* should render *terumah* into a *revii*, and then *terumah* as a *revii* should render *kodesh* into a *chamishi*. The fact that Rabbi Yose's derivation stopped at *revii* for *kodashim* demonstrates that he holds *chulin* reaches only to *sheini*, and it is *terumah* that reaches *shlishi* and *kodesh* that reaches *revii*. The hierarchy is clean: the greater the sanctity of the object, the more susceptible it is to progressively attenuated *tumah*. As one of the participants observed, this is structurally analogous to the principle that *tzaddikim ha-kadosh baruch hu medakdek aleihem ke-chut ha-sa'arah* — those who are holier attract even the most residual forces of *tumah*; or conversely, the forces of *tumah* can adhere even to the most attenuated *tumah* precisely when it encounters the most sacred objects.

### Rabbi Yehoshua Position

Rabbi Yehoshua's position emerges from a *Mishnah*: Rabbi Eliezer says that if one eats food that is *rishon* one becomes a *rishon*; if one eats food that is *sheini* one becomes a *sheini*; if one eats food that is *shlishi* one becomes a *shlishi*. The principle is, in effect, that you are what you eat — eating *tamei* food assigns you the same *tumah* status as that food. Tosafot notes that this operates on two levels of rabbinic enactment: the first level holds that if you eat something like a *sheini*, you cannot then eat *terumah*, since people would perceive the *terumah* as going into a stomach that contains *sheini* and being rendered *tamei* there. The second level goes further — after eating it, you are treated as being at that level even for purposes of what you touch.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees and says: one who eats *rishon* or *sheini* becomes a *sheini*; one who eats *shlishi* becomes a *sheini* only for *kodesh* — *sheni be-kodesh ve-ein sheni bi-terumah* — meaning it would affect *kodesh* but not *terumah*. What kind of food that was a *shlishi* could one have eaten? The *Mishnah* specifies: *be-chulin she-naasu al taharas ha-terumah* — *chulin* that was treated with the same care and stringency as *terumah*. This leads to the next subsection.

### Chulin Al Taharas

When a *kohen* has *terumah* in the house continuously and wishes to maintain a single uniform standard of purity, he may treat his *chulin* as if it were *terumah* — handling it with the same care and scrutiny. This practice is called *chulin she-naasu al taharas ha-terumah*. The halachic consequence is that the *chulin* that is handled with this *terumah*-level care acquires — by rabbinic enactment — the same *tumah* susceptibility as *terumah*. If a *sheini* touches standard *chulin*, nothing happens. But if a *sheini* touches *chulin* treated *al taharas ha-terumah*, that *chulin* becomes a *shlishi*. The Gemara draws out the implication: *al taharas ha-terumah in*, yes — only if it was treated with the care of *terumah* does it achieve *shlishi* status — *al taharas ha-kodesh lo*, not if it was merely treated with the care of *kodesh*. Rashi's text simply reads *chulin lo* — that standard *chulin* simply does not become a *shlishi*. The conclusion is the same either way: *alma kasavar ein sheni oseh shlishi be-chulin* — it is clear that Rabbi Yehoshua holds a *sheini* does not make a *shlishi* in standard *chulin*.

### Rabbi Elazar Formula

Rabbi Elazar's position comes from a *Braitah*: *shloshtan shavin* — the following three are equivalent in one respect. Whether one has a *rishon* in the world of *kodesh*, of *chulin*, or of *terumah*, it is *metamei shnayim u-fosel echad be-kodesh* — it transmits *tumah* two levels down and invalidates one more, meaning it reaches all the way to *revii* in *kodesh* and there stops. Then: *metamei echad u-fosel echad bi-terumah* — in the world of *terumah*, the *rishon* goes down two levels, making a *sheini* and then a *shlishi*, and stops. And *u-fosel echad ba-chulin* — in the world of *chulin*, the *rishon* goes down only one level to a *sheini* and stops. This is a beautifully clear formulation: *chulin* ends at *sheini*, *terumah* ends at *shlishi*, and *kodesh* ends at *revii*.

## Shelishi Limits…

### Challah Case

Rabbi Eliezer's position is derived from a *Mishnah* concerning *challah*: *Rabbi Eliezer omer, challah nitelet min ha-tehorah al ha-teme'ah* — *challah* may be taken from *tahor* dough on behalf of *tamei* dough. The background is as follows: if one's dough became *tamei*, the *challah* separated from it becomes *terumah teme'ah*, which the *kohen* cannot eat and can only burn. Rabbi Eliezer rules that if the dough was made *tamei* deliberately — *be-meizid* — one is required to separate *tahor challah* from *tahor* dough on behalf of the *tamei* dough as well, so that the *kohen* receives a *tahor challah*. The practical challenge is how to accomplish this while keeping the doughs connected for purposes of the separation.

---

## Challah Dispute

### Eliezer Leniency

Rabbi Eliezer's solution is to interpose a small piece of *tahor* dough between the *tahor* and *tamei* portions. The quantity of this interposing piece is critical: it must be *pachos mi-kebeitzah*, less than a *kebeitzah* in volume. This is because food that is less than a *kebeitzah* can become *tamei* itself but does not transmit *tumah* further. The piece in the middle will be rendered *tamei* by contact with the *tamei* dough, but since it is less than a *kebeitzah*, it will not pass the *tumah* on to the *tahor* dough on the other side. The three portions — *tamei* dough, small interposing piece, *tahor* dough — are thereby treated as one connected mass, and *challah* can be taken from the *tahor* portion *mi-n ha-mukaf*, as if taken from a connected whole. The *Chachamim* forbid this arrangement.

---

## Challah Dispute…

### Kebeitzah Mechanics

A second *Braitah* reports that Rabbi Eliezer permitted this even with a *kebeitzah*-sized interposing piece, not only with less than a *kebeitzah*. This version of the ruling is more striking, because a *kebeitzah* of food does transmit *tumah*. How then could Rabbi Eliezer permit using a *kebeitzah* of *tahor* dough in the middle without concern that it will become a *shlishi* and transmit *tumah* to the *tahor* dough on the far side? The configuration would be: *tamei* dough — a *rishon* — touching the interposing piece making it a *sheini*, which then contacts the far *tahor* dough making it potentially a *shlishi*.

### Gemara Analysis

The Gemara initially assumes that the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim* is precisely about whether *sheini* makes *shlishi* in *chulin*: *mai lav be-ha kamifligi* — is this not the crux of their disagreement? The *Tamei* dough is a *rishon*, the interposing dough becomes a *sheini*, and the question is whether the far *tahor* dough becomes a *shlishi*. Rabbi Eliezer holds *ein sheini oseh shlishi be-chulin* — no, it stops at *sheini* — so the scheme works. The *Chachamim* hold *sheini oseh shlishi be-chulin* — yes, the *tumah* would reach the far dough — so they forbid it.

An additional assumption underlying this reading is *chulin ha-tevulim le-challah lo ke-challah damu* — dough from which *challah* has not yet been separated is treated as ordinary *chulin*, not as *challah*/*terumah*, even though it contains actualized potential *challah* within it.

### Rav Mari Resolution

Rav Mari son of Rav Kahana resolves the apparent debate differently. Since we have already shown from so many *Tannaim* that all agree *ein sheini oseh shlishi be-chulin*, it cannot be that this is the point of contention between Rabbi Eliezer and the *Chachamim*. Instead, *ve-hacha be-chulin ha-tevulim le-challah kamifligi* — what they actually debate is whether *chulin* from which *challah* has not yet been separated is treated as *challah* (*terumah*) in its *tumah* susceptibility, or as plain *chulin*. The *Chachamim* hold *ke-challah damu* — it is like *challah*, and therefore the interposing dough can become a *shlishi*, making the scheme impermissible. Rabbi Eliezer holds *lo ke-challah damu* — it remains *chulin*, cannot become a *shlishi*, and the scheme is therefore permissible. Alternatively, the Gemara offers a second resolution: *de-chulei alma chulin ha-tevulim le-challah lo ke-challah damu ve-ein sheini oseh shlishi be-chulin* — everyone agrees on both of these points, and the dispute is actually about *ha-mutar ligrom tumah le-chulin she-be-chutz la-aretz* — whether one is permitted to cause *tumah* to spread to *chulin* outside of the Land of Israel. Rabbi Eliezer permits deliberately spreading *tumah* to the interposing *chulin* dough for the constructive purpose of enabling *challah tehorah* to be separated on behalf of everything combined. The *Chachamim* hold this is forbidden: *assur ligrom tumah le-chulin she-be-chutz la-aretz*. The upshot in any case is that virtually every *Tanna* agrees that *shlishi* is limited to *terumah*; the consensus is overwhelming, even if Rashi's observation about Rabbi Akiva's *pasuk*-based derivation raises a lingering question about whether the *Mishnah* itself implies a broader reach.

## Techumin Drasha

### Levitic Cities

The Gemara now transitions to a different *drashah* of Rabbi Akiva: *bo bayom darash Rabbi Akiva*. The *pesukim* about the *arei ha-Leviyim* — the Levitic cities — contain an apparent contradiction. One *pasuk* speaks of a thousand *amah* around the cities and another speaks of two thousand. Rabbi Akiva's resolution is that a thousand *amah* represents one measure — the immediate surrounds, fields or yards — while two thousand *amah* represents the city limit proper.

### Akiva Derivation

The halachic significance of establishing a two-thousand *amah* city limit is its relevance to *Shabbat* boundaries. If Rabbi Akiva is deriving a two-thousand *amah* measure from these *pesukim*, it is presumably because this establishes *techumin* — the *Shabbat* boundary — as a *de-oraita* concept, derived from these very verses.

### Debate Framework

The Gemara frames the underlying debate as follows: *mar savar techumin de-oraita u-mar savar de-rabbanan* — one authority holds that *techumin* is a biblical prohibition and the other holds it is rabbinic. If Rabbi Akiva's *drashah* establishes a two-thousand *amah* city limit for the *Leviyim*, the point of the derivation — what *nafka mina* does it serve — is presumably to establish that the prohibition of leaving the *Shabbat* boundary is *de-oraita*. The other *Tanna* who does not accept this reading of the *pesukim* would instead understand the two thousand *amah* as describing where the *Leviyim*'s fields and vineyards lay, not as establishing a halachic limit for *Shabbat*.

### Tosafot Nuance

Tosafot raises a nuance: the dichotomy need not be absolute. Perhaps even one who reads the *pasuk* as describing the *Leviyim*'s agricultural land could still derive *techumin de-oraita* from a different *pasuk*. Conversely, perhaps Rabbi Akiva could acknowledge that the two-thousand *amah* figure also describes the *Leviyim*'s land without that undermining the *Shabbat* application. However, at minimum, Rabbi Akiva's derivation is most coherent if he is drawing a *de-oraita* norm about *Shabbat* boundaries, since for him the verse's import cannot simply be the agricultural distribution to the *Leviyim*. One might also note that there is another potential application of knowing the city limit of the *arei ha-Leviyim* — namely, establishing when one is considered to be within the city for purposes of *arei miklat*, cities of refuge, which function as Levitic cities. Yet the Gemara specifically frames the debate around *techumin de-oraita* versus *de-rabbanan*, and this is the most naturally compelling reason why Rabbi Akiva would need to derive a precise two-thousand *amah* measure from these *pesukim*.

## Song at Sea

### Song Setup

The next *drashah* of Rabbi Akiva concerns the *Shirah* at the sea — *Az Yashir Moshe*. The *Braitah* introduces the scene: when Israel ascended from the sea, *natnu eineihem lomar shirah* — they set their eyes upon saying *shirah*. The question is: how did they say it? The word *lemor* in the verse — *vayomru lemor Ashira la-Shem* — suggests a responsive mode, but what exactly does this mean?

### Akiva Responsive

Rabbi Akiva's position is that *lemor* refers to responsive singing analogous to the way an adult *makri* reads *Hallel* for a congregation when that congregation is largely unlettered. The adult would chant each section, and the congregation — not knowing the full text — would respond with the opening words of the first verse: *Ashira la-Shem*. So Moshe would say *Ashira la-Shem ki ga'oh ga'ah*, and they would respond *Ashira la-Shem*; Moshe would continue, and they would again respond *Ashira la-Shem* — a recurring *refrain* that constituted their participation. This is like the practice of *Hodu la-Shem ki tov, Hodu la-Shem ki tov* — a responsive verse that allows full participation without requiring knowledge of the entire text. The *lemor* thus means: Moshe said it, and they responded with the opening line.

### Eliezer Repetition

Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds differently. He compares it to the case where a *katan*, a minor, reads *Hallel* for the congregation. Since a minor cannot discharge the obligation of adults, the congregation cannot simply respond *Amen* and be *yotzei*; they must repeat every word themselves. So here too, *lemor* means that for every word Moshe said, all of Israel repeated that word — complete word-for-word repetition, which also explains how they knew the text: they simply repeated after Moshe.

### Chachamim Unison

The *Chachamim* interpret the verse differently. *Vayomru* — they all said it — indicates that everyone recited the entire *Shirah* simultaneously, in unison. *Lemor* means that Moshe opened first — *d'patach Moshe be-reisha* — and then everyone said it together with him. This is analogous to the way *Shema* was recited in the *beit ha-knesset*, where the *sofer* or *sheliach tzibbur* would open and then everyone would join in unison, rather than responding responsively or repeating word by word. But this position raises the obvious question: how did all of Israel know the words if Moshe composed the song on the spot?

### Yose HaGelili Answer

Rabbi Yose HaGelili addresses this question directly in a *drashah*: *b'sha'ah she-alu Yisrael min ha-yam, natnu eineihem lomar shira* — when they came up from the sea, they wanted to say *shira*. How did they all know to say exactly what they said? His answer: *ol mutal al birkei imo* — a toddler sitting on his mother's lap — and *tinok yonek mi-shadei imo* — an infant nursing from its mother's breast. When the *Shechina* revealed itself at *Yam Suf*, the toddler lifted his neck and the infant released the breast from his mouth, and they said: *Zeh Eli ve-anvehu*. The miracle is that even infants were imbued with prophecy and song at that moment. The people did not need to learn the words from Moshe; they were supernaturally inspired to sing.

### Infants Midrash

The *pasuk* cited as the proof text is *mi-pi olelim ve-yonkim yisadta oz* — from the mouths of toddlers and infants You established strength — where *oz* echoes *Ozzi ve-zimrat Yah*, the opening of the *Shirah* itself, suggesting that the *oz*, the strength of song, came even from the youngest. The image is deliberately vivid and even graphic: the infant releases the nipple from his mouth to sing. The Gemara records that this text was once cited at a *brit*, which is perhaps not surprising.

### Meir Enhancement

Rabbi Meir goes further still: *minayin she-afilu ubarim she-be-me'ei imman amru shira* — how do we know that even the fetuses in their mothers' wombs said *shira*? The proof text is *be-makhelot barechu Elohim, Hashem, mi-mekor Yisrael* — in the assemblies bless God, from the *mekor* of Israel — where *mekor* is the rabbinic anatomical term for the womb, the source that nurtures the child. The objection is immediately raised: *va-halo lo chazu* — the fetuses could not see anything. Rabbi Tanchum resolves this: *keres na'asa lahem ke-aspaklarya ha-me'irah* — the womb became for them like a luminous transparent glass, and they could see through it and behold the divine presence. What the infants perceived through sight, the fetuses perceived through a miraculous transparency of the womb itself.

## Job Love vs Fear

### Lo Ambiguity

The *daf* concludes with a discussion of *Iyov* and the question of whether his service of God was *me-ahavah* or *mi-yirah* — from love or from fear. The *Mishnah* had cited the verse *hen yikteleni, lo ayachel* — even if He kills me, I will still hope in Him — as evidence that Iyov served from love. However, as the *Mishnah* noted, *adayin ha-davar shakul* — the matter is still uncertain — because the word *lo* could be written with a *lammed alef* meaning "not" — I will not have faith — or with a *lammed vav* meaning "for Him" — I will maintain my faith in Him. The Gemara asks the obvious question: let us simply look at how the word is written. It is in fact written with a *lammed alef*. Does that not settle it?

### Written Form Challenge

The Gemara challenges the reliability of reading the written form as definitive by bringing another *pasuk*: *be-chol tzaratam lo tzar* — in all their afflictions, He is not afflicted — where *lo* is also written with a *lammed alef*, yet the meaning, given the continuation of the verse (*u-malach panav hoshi'am*, and His angel saved them), must be *lo* with a *vav* — meaning God is afflicted by their suffering. So the written *lammed alef* cannot be read as determinative; it can carry either meaning. The Gemara notes that this is not a standard *keri u-chtiv* discrepancy; rather, a *lammed alef* itself can mean *lo* with a *vav*. This may reflect archaic orthographic ambiguity: just as the feminine pronoun *hi* was sometimes written with a *vav* rather than a *yod* in older Hebrew texts, the *alef* in *lo* may have functioned as a vowel placeholder rather than a semantic indicator of negation.

### Meir Approach

Rabbi Meir resolves the question differently, without relying on the written form of the word at all. He notes that *Iyov* is described as *yere Elokim*, fearing God — and so is *Avraham*, of whom the *pasuk* says *ki ata yadati ki yere Elokim atah* — now I know that you fear God — after the *Akeidah*. Since we know from another *pasuk* that Avraham is called *ohavi* — My beloved, the one who loves Me — the description of Avraham as *yere Elokim* did not preclude but rather included *ahavah*. By *gezeirah shavah* of the common phrase *yere Elokim*, the same is true of Iyov: *yirah* in Iyov's case also emerged from a foundation of *ahavah*.

### Reward Differential

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar articulates the practical difference in reward between those who serve *mi-yirah* and those who serve *me-ahavah*. One who serves from fear — *ze taluy le-elef dor* — the reward extends for a thousand generations. One who serves from love — *le-alpayim dor* — two thousand generations. The Gemara supports this from two *pesukim*. In one, *le-alafim le-ohavai u-le-shomrei mitzvotai* — thousands of generations for those who love Me and keep My commandments — *alafim* (thousands) is juxtaposed to *ohavai* (those who love Me). In the other, *le-ohavav u-le-shomrei mitzvotav le-elef dor* — for those who love Him and keep His commandments, a thousand generations — *elef* (one thousand) is juxtaposed to *shomrei mitzvotav* (those who keep His commandments). The distinction turns on what each word is adjacent to: love is paired with thousands, observance without the same quality of love is paired with one thousand.

### Two Students

The *sugya* concludes with an aggadic coda. Two students sat before Rava. The first said: I was told in my dream — *ma rav tuvcha asher tzafanta li-rei'echa* — how great is Your good that you have stored away for those who fear You. The second said: I was told in my dream — *ve-yismechu vecha kol chosei vach, le-olam yeranenu ve-ya'altzu vach ohavei shemecha* — and all who take shelter in You will rejoice; forever those who love Your name will sing.

### Rava Judgment

Rava said to both of them: *travaychu rabbanan tzadikei gemurei atun* — you are both complete *tzaddikim*. But: *mar me-ahavah u-mar mi-yirah* — one serves from love, and one serves from fear. The first student's dream cited a verse about *rei'echa*, those who fear; the second student's dream cited a verse about *ohavei shemecha*, those who love Your name. Each man's dream reflected his own spiritual mode of service — and Rava affirmed that both paths lead to the status of *tzaddik gamur*, a fully righteous person, even as they are not identical in their nature or their reward.

*Hadran alach ha-Shole'ach.*
