# Shabbat 134 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Metzitzah & Sakana

### Metzitzah Overview

We pick up at the bottom of 143b and continue into 144a, still within the discussion of *brit milah* on *Shabbat*. The subject now turns to an issue that has become a matter of considerable controversy in our own time: *metzitzah*. Eight lines from the bottom of the page, the last word on the line is *motzitzin* — the *Mishna* states that after cutting the skin and peeling away the membrane, one performs *metzitzah*, sucking out the blood from the wound, before proceeding to describe the medical treatment of the wound.

What is particularly striking about the placement of *metzitzah* in the *Mishna* is that it sits precisely at the crossroads between the first section — which describes the acts constituting the *milah* itself — and the second section, which concerns the medical care required in relation to *sakana*. The question is: on which side of that line does the *Gemara* place *metzitzah*?

### Rav Papa on Health

The answer comes from Rav Papa. *Amar Rav Papa: hai umna d'lo mayitz, sakana hu, ve-avrinan lei* — a *mohel* who does not perform *metzitzah* is endangering the infant and is therefore removed from his position. The *hiddush* here has two components. First, if someone is irresponsible about health matters, we remove him from his role. Second, and more fundamentally, *metzitzah* is a health issue, not a *mitzvah* issue.

### Blood Vessels & Melacha

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *pshita* — this is obvious! If *metzitzah* involves *mechalel Shabbat*, since the suction causes a *habura* (wound), then clearly it must involve a risk to life. The *Gemara* responds that one might have thought otherwise: *hai dama nifkad pakid* — perhaps the blood in the wound is merely stored there, not truly within a blood vessel, and therefore not the kind of *habura* that constitutes a biblical violation. After all, if blood were simply contained in a sac beneath the skin rather than flowing through a vessel, opening it would not constitute the *melacha* of *habura*. The *Gemara* therefore clarifies: *hibubi mikhbar* — the blood is indeed connected to the blood vessels, it is real *hilul Shabbat*, and nonetheless it is permitted because of *sakana*.

### Isplanit & Kammon

The *Gemara* adds a further proof by analogy: *vedumiya de-isplanit ve-kammon* — just as the *isplanit* (poultice) and *kammon* (cumin) discussed in the *Mishna* are plainly medical interventions whose omission would constitute a *sakana*, and for which *Shabbat* is set aside, so too *metzitzah* belongs in the same category. If these treatments are understood as *sakana* issues, *metzitzah* falls in the same class.

### Metzitzah Evolution

That is all the *Gemara* has to say on *metzitzah*. The voluminous discussion that has grown up around this practice — the Kabbalistic weight attributed to it, the intense focus on *metzitzah b'peh* specifically — is a post-*Talmudic* development, and much of it does not find its roots in the *Gemara* itself. Indeed, the *Gemara*'s treatment is clear: *metzitzah* is a medical act, categorized alongside the poultice and the cumin.

This context renders the contemporary controversy particularly ironic. The *Gemara* frames *metzitzah* as a health-preserving measure — one for which a *mohel* who neglects it is removed from his position precisely because he is endangering the infant. If that same act has become, under certain practices, a vector for genuine medical danger, then the logic of the *Gemara* itself would counsel reassessment. In the *Gemara*'s own framework, health concerns of this kind are evaluated on the basis of current medical knowledge. One might note further that those who insist with great stringency on *metzitzah b'peh* do not, to any comparable degree, ensure the application of *isplanit* and *kammon* — the other medical interventions listed in the very same *Mishna*. The *Gemara* treats them all as equivalent. *Metzitzah* has become ritualized in a way that cumin and bandages have not, and this asymmetry is difficult to account for from the *Gemara*'s own perspective.

## Post-Milah Care

### Poultice Recipe

Continuing with the *Mishna*'s instruction that one places a bandage (*isplanit*) on the wound, Abaye transmits a teaching from his *em* — his nursemaid, as his mother had died, and throughout the *Gemara* Abaye consistently quotes his nursemaid using this term. Some suggest, perhaps apocryphally, that the name *Abaye* itself is an acronym for *asher becha yerucham yatom* — that through You, God, the orphan finds compassion.

His nursemaid taught: *isplanita de-kulhon kivei* — the poultice that addresses all types of pain and ailment is made from seven types of fat (*shav minei tarba*) and one unit of wax (*kira*), as Rashi explains.

### Rava's Secret

Rava adds his own formula: the poultice consists of wax (*kira*) and *kalva rishana*, which Rashi identifies as a type of tree sap. Rava expounded this in Mechuza, his home city. The effect on the local medical practitioners was immediate: the sons of Minyomi the doctor tore their garments, for Rava had given away their trade secret, threatening their livelihood.

Rava consoled them: he had left them one secret he did not reveal, which he now cited in the name of Shmuel. Shmuel teaches that if a person washes his face and does not dry it properly, the skin will become cracked and fissured. And what is the remedy? *Limshi tuva bemaiya de-silka* — one should wash the face thoroughly with beet water. That was the secret he withheld.

### Cumin Grinding

The *Mishna* now addresses a case where the cumin was not ground from *erev Shabbat*. In such circumstances, it may be ground on *Shabbat* itself — but with a *shinuy*, by chewing it with one's teeth rather than using the normal grinding implement. This is notable, because in principle this infant's care falls under *sakanat nefashot*, and one would expect no *shinuy* to be required. The broader context, however, is relevant: when the *Gemara* discusses post-*milah* care and the care given after childbirth, there is a tendency to introduce *shinuy* requirements even where a general *pikuach nefesh* designation has been applied broadly, since the *sakana* is not always immediately and individually confirmed in each case. Hence, where a *shinuy* does not impair the treatment, there is some pressure to employ it.

### Yom Tov Differences

A *Baraita* is now introduced: *devarim she'ein osim le-milah be-Shabbat osim lah be-Yom Tov* — things one may only do for *brit milah* on *Shabbat* in an indirect or modified manner may be done directly on *Yom Tov*. For example, *shochin lah kamon* — the cumin may be ground directly on *Yom Tov*, since grinding spices for food is a permitted form of *ochel nefesh* on *Yom Tov*, and therefore it may be done here for the infant's care without the workaround of chewing.

Similarly, *vetarfin lah yayin ve-shemen* — on *Yom Tov* one may beat wine and oil together in a vessel. On *Shabbat*, by contrast, one may only apply them separately. What precisely the concern is about beating wine and oil together is not entirely clear from the *Gemara*. It does not appear to be simply the issue of medicinal treatments, since medicinal concerns are waived even for a *choleh she'ein bo sakana*, and the infant's status is considerably more acute than that. The *Tiferes Yisrael*, a commentator on the *Mishna* of roughly 150 to 200 years ago, suggests that the concern may be an appearance issue — that vigorously beating two components together in a dish resembles preparatory cooking, such as beating an egg before frying or mixing batter before baking. In any event, it is a *rabbinic* concern, and on *Yom Tov* it is set aside in the context of food-related preparation.

## Wine & Oil Debate

### Abaye's Question

Abaye poses the following question to Rav Yosef: *mai shna kamun be-Yom Tov d'chazi li-kdeira* — why do we permit grinding cumin on *Yom Tov* for the infant? The answer is that cumin is something that could theoretically go into a pot, so it falls within the category of legitimate *Yom Tov* food preparation. But then, Abaye continues, *yayin ve-shemen chazi nami be-Shabbos le-choleh* — wine and oil are also things one is apparently permitted to mix for a *choleh* on *Shabbat*. Why, then, does the *Mishna* restrict their mixing even in the infant's case?

### Tanna Kamma Dispute

A *Baraita* is cited: *ein torfin yayin ve-shemen le-choleh be-Shabbos* — one may not mix wine and oil for a *choleh* on *Shabbat*. However, Rabban Shimon ben Elazar reports in the name of Rabbi Meir: *af torfin yayin ve-shemen* — one may indeed do so.

### Rabbi Meir's Humility

Rabban Shimon ben Elazar then relates a personal episode. Once, Rabbi Meir suffered a stomach ailment on *Shabbat*, and those present wished to beat wine and oil for him. Rabbi Meir would not allow it. They protested: *devarecha yivatlu be-chayecha* — should your own ruling be set aside in your very lifetime? He replied: *af al pi she-ani omer kach, ve-chaverai omrim kach, miyami lo milani libi la'avor al divrei chaverai* — even though my position is as I have stated, and my colleagues disagree, in all my days I have never brought myself to transgress the view of my colleagues. If they have outvoted me, I will not act against them, even on my own behalf.

The *Gemara* comments: *hu nihu d'machmir anafshei* — this was a personal stringency only. He was not reversing his *halachic* opinion; he still believed the *halacha* should permit mixing for everyone. His personal conduct was simply one of deference to the majority view.

This phenomenon — a rabbi ruling one way for the public while holding himself to a stricter standard — is worth examining carefully. There is genuine value in an individual finding areas of personal *chumra* as an expression of punctiliousness and *dikduk*, of giving form to a heightened sense of concern for potential violation. That, at a private level, has a place. But when a rabbinic figure is functioning in a public capacity — attesting to the permissibility of something, upon which a community relies — acting personally in a more restrictive way sends a very different message. The distinction is sharpest when the area of stringency is precisely the one the rabbi has certified for others. A case where someone maintains a private standard that diverges from their public *psak* for principled *halachic* reasons — such as personally following *Rabbeinu Tam* for *tzeis ha-kochavim* while ruling more leniently for others, or personally being careful about *chadash* while certifying a product for those who do not observe it — is distinct from a case where the implicit message is that the certified standard is not truly adequate. The *eruv* case is particularly complex because some authorities hold that no *eruv* is acceptable regardless of construction standards, since the underlying question concerns whether our public thoroughfares qualify as *reshus ha-rabbim* — a matter of *psak*, not implementation. Rabbi Meir, in any event, was not attesting to something he personally rejected; he was deferring to a majority ruling while maintaining his own position openly.

### Vigor vs. Method

The *Gemara* now asks: if Rabbi Meir's position is that mixing is permitted, and the *stam Mishna* is typically Rabbi Meir, why does our *Mishna* appear to restrict mixing even for the infant on *Shabbat*? The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between degrees of mixing. *Lo ba'ei leicha* — for a normal *choleh*, the mixing need not be vigorous; it is permissible in a more casual form. *Hacha ba'ei leicha* — for the infant's wound, the mixture must be beaten vigorously, and that even Rabbi Meir would not permit on *Shabbat*.

### Authority & Trust

The *Gemara* then asks: why not simply permit the less vigorous mixing for the infant as well? The answer is: *hainu d'katani noten zeh bifnei atzmo ve-zeh bifnei atzmo* — that is precisely what the *Mishna* means when it says to place each component separately. It does not mean in completely separate locations; it means not to beat them vigorously together. So whatever the underlying concern is about beating wine and oil — whether it overlaps with medicinal treatment concerns or touches on something distinct — it is present in the infant's case as well, and the *Mishna*'s instruction to apply them separately is the *halachic* accommodation: wine and oil may both be used, but without vigorous beating. This is, the *Gemara* acknowledges, somewhat surprising, since one would expect a *choleh she'yesh bo sakana* to receive a full waiver of even *rabbinic* restrictions, not a modified version of them. The broader principle operative here seems to be that where alternative methods exist that do not compromise the treatment, those methods are preferred — a principle that stands in some tension with the standard approach to *pikuach nefesh* situations.

## Mustard & Coal

### Filtering Ban

The *Gemara* now introduces a new *Baraita* concerning *Yom Tov*: *ein masnenin et ha-chardal be-misnenes shelo* — one may not filter mustard through its designated filter on *Yom Tov*. Similarly, *ein mematkin oto be-gachalet* — one may not sweeten mustard by pressing a coal against it, since that process would cause the coal to be partially extinguished.

### Egg Filter

Abaye asks Rav Yosef: how is this different from what we have learned in the *Mishna*, that one may pass an egg through a mustard filter? The mustard filter retains a deep yellow residue, and passing an egg through it imparts a more intense yellow color to the egg. If passing an egg through a mustard filter is permitted, why should filtering mustard through the same filter be prohibited?

### Borer Analysis

Rav Yosef responds: *hasam lo meichzi ke-vorer, hacha meichzi ke-vorer* — there, when the egg passes through, it does not resemble *borer* (the forbidden act of selecting), because the entire egg passes through and merely absorbs color; nothing is separated. Here, however, filtering mustard through its filter does resemble *borer* because different components of the mustard are being separated from one another.

One might ask: why does the *Gemara* say it only *resembles* *borer* rather than saying it *is* *borer*? *Tosafot* addresses this directly: *mihu borer lo havei* — it is not technically *borer*, because even the separated residue is edible, and the rule is that *borer* applies when one separates the undesirable from the desirable, not when both components are themselves fit for consumption. Furthermore, the general principle allows liquids to be poured through filters when the liquid itself could have been consumed without filtering. The case of mustard filtering differs from the egg case in that genuine separation of components occurs, making it at least resemble the prohibited act — hence the restriction on *Yom Tov*.

## Mustard & Coal (II)

### Coal & Kibui

Returning to the prohibition on sweetening mustard with a coal: a *Baraita* appears to contradict this, stating that *mematkinin oto be-gachalet* — one may sweeten mustard with a coal. The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *kan be-gachelet shel matekhet, kan be-gachelet shel etz* — the distinction turns on whether the coal is of metal or of wood. The underlying prohibition is *kibui*, extinguishing. Biblically, the *melacha* of extinguishing applies only when the extinguishing is constructive — specifically, when it produces an ember or coal. That result is achievable only with wood; with metal, no coal is produced through extinguishing, and therefore extinguishing a metal ember does not fall within the biblical *melacha* of *kibui* at all. Hence, using a metal ember to sweeten the mustard involves no biblical concern and is permitted on *Yom Tov*; using a wood coal creates a potential biblical violation and is therefore prohibited. This distinction, incidentally, has implications worth noting for broader questions about extinguishing lights and turning off gas flames — where no coal or ember is produced — though in those contexts the discussion proceeds separately and no one concludes that such acts are *muttar*.

### Ochel Nefesh Rules

Abaye then raises a further question to Rav Yosef: even granting that using a wood coal involves a potential biblical act, one is after all doing this for the sake of food on *Yom Tov*. Why should it be different from cooking meat directly on coals (*bishra agumrei*), which is permitted even though placing the meat on burning coals causes them to partially extinguish? That too involves *kibui*, yet it is permitted as *ochel nefesh*.

Rav Yosef responds: *hasam lo efshar, hacha efshar* — there, with meat on coals, there is no alternative method; here, with mustard, there is an alternative, namely using a metal ember rather than a wood coal. This opens onto a larger question to be explored when the *Gemara* treats *Beitza*: when an act of *melacha* is done for food preparation on *Yom Tov*, is one required to choose the method that minimizes *melacha* when an alternative is available? The general principle of *ochel nefesh* permits food-related *melacha* on *Yom Tov*, but whether that permission is absolute or requires minimization when alternatives exist is a more nuanced question, as this passage illustrates.

## Cheese-Making

### Cheese vs. Bread

Abaye continues with a further question to Rav Yosef: *mahu le-gabein* — may one make cheese on *Yom Tov*? Rav Yosef responds: it is prohibited. Abaye presses: *mai shna mi-lisha* — how is this different from kneading dough? Both involve combining components into a new form, and both concern food. Why should kneading bread be permitted on *Yom Tov* while making cheese is not?

### Pre-Yom Tov Option

Rav Yosef gives the same answer as before: *hasam lo efshar, hacha efshar* — there, with bread, no one wishes to eat stale bread, so there is a genuine reason to knead specifically on *Yom Tov* itself, and the act is therefore permitted. Here, with cheese, it could have been made the day before *Yom Tov*, so one should have done so. This represents a distinct principle from the earlier discussion: there, the question was whether one must minimize *melacha* by choosing a less problematic method; here, the question is whether one must avoid the *melacha* entirely by performing the equivalent act before *Yom Tov* when that was feasible.

### Fresh Cheese Value

The *Gemara* anticipates an objection: *ve-amri Nahardai, gvina bar yoma me'alya* — the sages of Nehardea say that fresh, same-day cheese is of superior quality. Does that not provide a reason to make it specifically on *Yom Tov*, just as the freshness of bread justifies kneading then? The *Gemara* responds: *hachi ka'amar* — even granting that fresh cheese is excellent, day-old cheese is at minimum equally good. Unlike bread, where staleness is a genuine loss, cheese does not meaningfully deteriorate overnight. The option of making it before *Yom Tov* remains fully adequate, and therefore the prohibition stands.

These two issues — whether one must minimize *melacha* when an alternative method exists, and whether one must prepare in advance when the preparation could have been done before *Yom Tov* — will resurface when the *Gemara* reaches *Beitza*, where both questions receive fuller treatment.

## Milah Garments

### Seam Placement

The *Mishna* states that one does not make the small protective wrap for the *milah* wound on *Shabbat*, though one may wrap a piece of cloth. Abaye transmits a teaching from his nursemaid: *hai chaluka de-yanuka* — the special cloth garment made for the infant for *brit milah* — should have its stitching turned to the outside. The concern is *dilma midbak garda minei* — a thread from the stitching might adhere to skin near the incision, and when the cloth is removed it could cause serious injury.

### Inner Lining

Abaye's nursemaid refined this further in her own practice. When she assisted families in preparing these garments, she would fashion a sheath — a cover that extended halfway into the cloth — so as to create an inner lining that shielded the wound from the stitching entirely. This inner lining meant that when the garment was removed, no thread could catch on the wound.

### Alternative Garment

If the infant does not have such a specially prepared garment available, Abaye advises: take a worn piece of cloth (*blisa*) that has a hem (*sipsa*). The hem should be positioned at the bottom, away from the incision, while the body of the cloth is extended upward to cover the wound. The presence of the hem helps anchor the cloth in place without allowing the edge to come into direct contact with the incision.

## Neonatal Medicine

### Imperforate Anus

Abaye continues transmitting from his nursemaid. If there is an infant whose anal opening cannot be identified — a condition we would today call imperforate anus — the recommended approach is to rub him with oil and place him facing the sun. Where the skin appears more translucent, one should tear gently with the point of a barley seed. The barley seed's point was understood to be sharp enough to create an opening but not so dangerous as to cause severe injury. The tearing should be done in a cross-pattern, *shesi ve-erev* — horizontal and vertical. However, one should not use a metal instrument for this purpose, as that would cause injurious laceration.

## Neonatal Medicine (II)

### Nursing Issues

Abaye reports further from his nursemaid: if an infant will not suckle — *hai yanuka de-lo mayit* — and his mouth feels cold to the touch, one should take a vessel containing embers and hold it near the infant's mouth. The warmth will stimulate the mouth, and the infant will begin to nurse. The belief was that cold lips inhibited the sucking reflex, and warmth would restore it.

### Breathing Issues

Another teaching: if an infant is not breathing normally — *hai yanuka de-lo minshantei*, as Rashi explains it, an infant whose heartbeat or breath is barely perceptible — one should fan him with a sieve-like implement. If an infant is not breathing well — *de-lo mapi* — one should take the mother's placenta and rub it on the infant, and this will help restore his breathing.

### Low Birth Weight

If the infant is too thin or frail — *hai yanuka de-katin* — one again takes the mother's placenta and rubs it on him, this time *mi-kutna le-uma*, from the narrow part to the wide part, symbolically expressing the hope that the infant will grow and fill out.

### Ruddy Appearance

If the infant appears excessively ruddy — *hai yanuka de-sumak* — and the blood has not yet been fully absorbed into the body — *de-akati lo ivla bei dama* — one must wait, *ad de-ivla bei dama*, until the blood is absorbed, and only then perform the *brit milah*. He is not yet ready, and proceeding would be dangerously premature.

### Jaundice Concern

Conversely, if the infant appears jaundiced — *de-yarok* — the interpretation is that the blood is not yet flowing adequately through his system — *de-akati lo nafal bei dama*. One must likewise wait, *ad de-nafal bei dama*, until the blood is circulating properly before performing the *brit milah*.

## Rabbi Natan Cases

### Ruddy Infant Case

A *Baraita* is cited: Rabbi Natan relates that he once traveled to a coastal island community. A woman came before him whose first two sons had each died following *brit milah*. She brought him her third son. Rabbi Natan saw that the child was excessively red — *she-hu adom*. He instructed her: *himtini lo ad she-yivla bo damo* — wait until the blood is fully absorbed into him. She waited, performed the *brit milah*, and the child survived. They named the child Natan ha-Bavli in his honor.

### Jaundiced Infant Case

On another occasion Rabbi Natan traveled to Cappadocia — identified as a region in what is today southern Turkey. Again, a woman came before him whose first two sons had died following *brit milah*. She brought her third son. Rabbi Natan examined him closely and saw that he was jaundiced — *she-hu yarok*. He looked carefully at the genitals and observed that there was no sign of blood at the site of the incision — *v'lo raiti bo dam brit*. He instructed her to wait until the blood flow improved. She waited, performed the *brit milah*, and the child lived. He too was named Natan ha-Bavli.

This case presents an interesting parallel with the Amoraic teaching of Abaye's nursemaid: the *Baraita* of Rabbi Natan makes essentially the same point, which raises the question of why Abaye needed to quote his nursemaid at all on these matters. In any event, the entire discussion here is conducted from a purely medical perspective. The decision of whether to proceed with *brit milah* is governed by observable physical conditions in the infant — skin color, blood circulation — and nothing else.

### Rashi on Blood

Rashi, however, makes an important addition at this point. In his comment on *l'karchei ha-yam*, the second case, Rashi writes: *tartei le-tivuta* — there are two distinct concerns in the case of the jaundiced infant. The first is the health risk of performing *milah* when the blood is not flowing adequately. The second, which Rashi treats as a parallel concern, is this: if *milah* is performed, *lo nafik mineih dama* — blood will not emerge from the wound. And, Rashi asserts, *hatafat dam brit mitzvah* — the flow of blood at *brit milah* is itself a *mitzvah*, as indicated by the verse *gam at be-dam britecha* — "even with you, through the blood of your covenant." The verse recited at the *brit milah*, *be-damayich chayi*, is similarly adduced.

### Blood Ritualization

It is worth noting what Rashi is doing here, because it marks a meaningful development. In the *Gemara* itself, the entire framework for *milah* and the care surrounding it is clinical and medical. The *Mishna* that opened this discussion described the acts of *milah* — cutting, periah, *metzitzah* — without any particular focus on blood as a ritual element. The concern about the ruddy or jaundiced infant is entirely a health concern. Rashi, writing in the post-*Talmudic* period, introduces the notion that blood flow at *brit milah* carries independent ritual significance as a *mitzvah*. This reflects a broader shift: in the period following the *Gemara*, the role of blood in *brit milah* becomes increasingly foregrounded, appearing in questions about the use of clamps, discussions about *hatafat dam brit* for those already circumcised, and connections to narratives like the story of Tzippora — *chatan damim la-mulot*. All of this represents a trajectory that, while having some roots in pre-*Talmudic* literature, does not find its center of gravity in the *Gemara* itself.

## Bathing Infant

### Bathing Allowed

We now reach the next *Mishna* on 134b: *marchitzin et ha-katan bein lifnei milah bein le-achar ha-milah* — one may bathe the infant whether before or after the *brit milah*. This presumably encompasses heating the water as needed, given the health considerations already discussed. The *Mishna* then adds: *u-mezalfin alav be-yad aval lo be-kli* — one may sprinkle water on the infant by hand but not directly with a vessel. This immediately raises a tension with the opening clause, which seemed to permit bathing without restriction.

### Third Day Exception

Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah adds: *marchitzin et ha-katan be-yom ha-shlishi she-chal lihyot be-Shabbat* — one may bathe the infant on the third day even when it falls on *Shabbat*, for this is a day of particular medical vulnerability. The source is the verse *va-yehi ba-yom ha-shlishi bi-hyotam ko'avim* — on the third day, when they were in acute pain, that was when Shimon and Levi struck the city of Shechem. The third day after circumcision is thus identified as the day of greatest danger.

The *Mishna* also addresses two additional cases: a *safek* infant — which Rashi explains refers not necessarily to uncertainty about when the birth occurred but to an infant born possibly in the eighth month of gestation, whom *Chazal* considered likely not to survive, and for whom one does not violate *Shabbat* — and an *androgynos*, one who has both male and female genitalia, for whom *Shabbat* is not violated due to the halachic uncertainty about the obligation of *milah*. Rabbi Yehuda, however, permits violation of *Shabbat* for an *androgynos*, since the male genitalia are present and the eighth-day obligation is considered definite. The *androgynos* and *safek* cases will be taken up tomorrow; today's discussion focuses on the bathing of the normal infant.

### Sprinkling Only

The *Gemara* notes the apparent internal contradiction: the *Mishna* first says *marchitzin*, implying normal bathing, and then seems to limit this to sprinkling by hand. Rav Yehuda and Rabbah bar Avuha both propose a resolution: the *Mishna* is to be read as a single continuous statement. *Marchitzin* — how does one bathe? *Mezalfin alav be-yad aval lo be-kli* — by sprinkling with the hand, not using a vessel. Under this reading, the *Mishna* never permitted full immersion-style bathing; the only permitted mode at all is hand-sprinkling.

### Rava's Reading

Rava objects: you cannot read the *Mishna* that way, because it says *marchitzin*, which denotes normal bathing, not a mere trickle. Rava proposes an alternative reading. The *Mishna* says: you may bathe the infant normally — *marchitzin et ha-katan bein lifnei ha-milah bein le-achar ha-milah* — that applies to the first day, and it means normal bathing, including heating water, and placing the infant in a tub if necessary. But then: *u-mezalfin alav be-yad aval lo be-kli* — this restriction applies to the third day, when it falls on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah then disputes even that restriction for day three, holding that even on day three one may bathe normally.

Under Rava's reading, the debate in the *Mishna* is entirely about day three: the *Tanna Kamma* permits only sprinkling by hand on day three, while Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah permits full normal bathing. Day one, under this reading, presents no debate: full bathing is permitted by all. Day two is not explicitly addressed; Rashi appears to assume it is treated like day one and carries equivalent risk.

A *Baraita* is then cited that explicitly supports Rava's reading: on day one, bathing the infant — both before and after *milah* — is permitted in the normal manner; on day three when it falls on *Shabbat*, it is limited to sprinkling by hand; Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah holds that even on day three full bathing is permitted. The *Baraita* further specifies that when sprinkling is the permitted mode, one may not use a cup, dish, or other vessel to sprinkle — only the hand itself.

The *Gemara* then addresses why Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah qualifies his citation of the verse about the third day by saying *af al pi she-ein raya la-davar zecher la-davar* — even though there is no absolute proof, there is a hint. The objection is: is this not a strong proof? The verse explicitly identifies the third day as the time of acute pain. The *Gemara* responds: the verse describes adults, and for adults the wound does not heal quickly — so even by day three an adult remains in a state of *sakana*. An infant, by contrast, *salik bisra hai* — the flesh heals rapidly, and by day three the infant's wound has largely recovered. Therefore the verse does not constitute a direct proof for the infant's case; it is only a *zecher*, a hint.

### Rava's Doubt

A case then arose where someone came before Rava and he ruled according to his own position — permitting full bathing on day one, including heating water in the normal way. Subsequently the infant became ill. At this point Rava himself fell ill, and he said: *ana ba-hadi targinah de-savei lama li* — why did I have to go against the interpretation of the elders? He was referring to Rav Yehuda and Rabbah bar Avuha, who had read the *Mishna* more narrowly, permitting only sprinkling. Having ruled against their position, Rava experienced doubt about whether he had overstepped his authority.

His students pointed out that a *Baraita* explicitly supports his reading. Rava acknowledged this but maintained that the textual logic of the *Mishna* itself, read closely, favors the other position. His argument turns on the word *af*: if the *Tanna Kamma* had permitted full bathing on day one and limited day three to sprinkling, then when Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah comes to contest the restriction on day three, he should have said *af marchitzin* — *even* full bathing is permitted on day three, paralleling what the *Tanna Kamma* permitted on day one. The absence of that *af* suggests that the *Tanna Kamma* never introduced full bathing at all — the entire *Mishna* deals only with the question of sprinkling versus full bathing, and the *Tanna Kamma* never departed from sprinkling as the permitted mode. Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah then says: I say *marchitzin*, full bathing, which is an innovation relative to the *Tanna Kamma*'s *zilluf*. Because this word *af* is absent, Rava has doubts about his own reading — though the *Baraita* still formally supports him.

### Rav Dimi Ruling

Rav Dimi, upon arriving from Eretz Yisrael, ruled definitively: *halacha ke-Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah* — the *halacha* follows Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah. Under this ruling, one may bathe the infant normally, and one may do so even on the third day when it falls on *Shabbat*. The entire dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah becomes practically resolved in favor of the more lenient position.

## Bathing Scope

### Scope Question

A further question was posed in Eretz Yisrael: when we say one may bathe the infant — whether according to the *Tanna Kamma* on day one or according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah even on day three — does *rechitza* refer to bathing the entire body (*harchatzat kol gufo*), or only to washing the area of the *milah* wound (*harchatzat milah*)?

### Rabbi Yaakov's View

One of the anonymous rabbinic questioners was answered by Rabbi Yaakov: *mistavra harchatzat kol gufo* — it makes more sense to understand the permission as encompassing the entire body. The reasoning is that if the *Mishna* only meant washing the area of the wound, it would not need to tell us anything novel. The rule that hot water and oil may be applied to a wound on *Shabbat* is already established independently. As Rav states: *ein moni'in chamin ve-shemen me-al gabei maka be-Shabbat* — one does not withhold hot water and oil from a wound on *Shabbat*. If the *Mishna*'s permission were only about the wound area, it would be redundant. The *Mishna* must therefore be adding something — namely, that the entire body may be bathed.

### Rav Yosef Pushes

Rav Yosef objects: the comparison does not hold, because Rav's rule about hot water and oil on wounds applies to water that was preheated before *Shabbat*. That involves only the rabbinic concern about medicinal treatments, which is straightforwardly set aside. But the question here is whether one may heat water *on Shabbat itself* — which is an entirely different level of intervention. If the *Mishna*'s permission extends only to the wound area, then perhaps heating water on *Shabbat* itself is not permitted, and the *Mishna*'s novelty consists in permitting even preheated water applied to the wound despite its medicinal character. The question of whether one may heat the water on *Shabbat* and whether that permission extends to the entire body are thus not answered simply by reference to Rav's rule.

### Rav Dimi Answer

Rav Dimi responds to Rav Yosef: what makes you assume that the dispute between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah concerns water heated on *Shabbat* at all? Perhaps the entire debate concerns only water that was heated before *Shabbat*, and the question is only about how that preheated water may be applied — by hand or by full immersion. If so, the question of heating water on *Shabbat* remains entirely open, and nothing in the *Mishna* authorizes it.

### Sakana Permits

Abaye answers: *mipnei she-sakana hi lo* — the key is the *Mishna*'s own framework. The entire discussion of bathing the infant is framed by the principle that this infant is in a state of *sakana*. The verse cited by Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah — *va-yehi ba-yom ha-shlishi bi-hyotam ko'avim* — makes explicit that we are treating this infant's condition as one of acute danger. Given that the *Mishna* is operating within a *sakana* framework, it necessarily contemplates heating water on *Shabbat* as a permitted act. Preheated water within a *sakana* framework would not require any special *Mishna* at all. The *Mishna*'s novelty lies in permitting the heating itself; and once we establish that heating is permitted for *sakana*, we can then ask whether it is permitted only for the wound area or for the whole body.

A tradition transmitted via Ravin in the name of Rabbi Abbahu — some say in the name of Rabbi Yochanan — resolves this definitively: *halacha ke-Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah*, and the ruling encompasses both water heated on *Shabbat* and water heated before *Shabbat*, and both bathing the entire body and bathing only the wound area. The permission is comprehensive: *mipnei she-sakana hi lo* — because the infant is in a state of *sakana*.

## Hot Water & Oil

### Rav Permits

The *Gemara* now returns to the independent rule cited above: *amar Rav, ein moni'in chamin ve-shemen me-al gabei maka be-Shabbat* — Rav holds that one does not withhold hot water and oil from a wound on *Shabbat*, meaning one may apply them. As *Tosafot* notes, this refers to the two substances mixed together, since mixed hot water and oil would more clearly carry the character of a medicinal treatment; applying them individually might not even be notable enough to require explicit dispensation.

### Squeezing Concern

A *baraita* is adduced that seems to challenge Rav's position: *ein notnin shemen ve-chamin al gabei moch liten al gabei maka be-Shabbat* — one may not apply oil and hot water on a cloth (*moch*) in order to then place it on the wound on *Shabbat*. The *Gemara* considers this a proof for Shmuel's more restrictive position. The *Gemara* responds: *hatam mishum sechita* — that prohibition has nothing to do with medicinal treatment. The concern is *sechita*, wringing: if liquid is placed on a cloth that is then applied to the wound, one will inevitably come to squeeze the cloth, violating the *melacha* of *sechita*. The medicinal treatment issue is not the operative concern there.

### Shmuel Forbids

A second challenge: *ein notnin chamin ve-shemen al gabei moch she-al gabei ha-maka be-Shabbat* — even if the cloth bandage is already in place on the wound, one may not apply oil and hot water to it on *Shabbat*. Here the *Gemara* initially considers whether the *sechita* concern still applies, since the bandage is already attached to the wound and perhaps squeezing is less likely. The *Gemara* concludes that *sechita* remains the concern even in this case. If there is no bandage, however, one may apply the substances directly to the wound, and this is Rav's position.

### Running vs. Applying

A *Baraita* is then cited that supports Shmuel: *ein notnin chamin ve-shemen al gabei maka be-Shabbat* — one may not put hot water and oil directly on the wound on *Shabbat*. However, *aval noten chutz la-maka ve-shotef ve-yored la-maka* — one may apply it alongside the wound and allow it to run down into the wound. This is precisely Shmuel's position: not direct application to the wound itself, but allowing the liquid to flow in from adjacent skin.

## Dry Bandages

### Dry Materials OK

A final *Baraita* is presented: *notnin al gabei ha-maka moch yavesh u-sfog yavesh* — one may place a dry piece of cloth or a dry sponge on a wound on *Shabbat*. The rationale is that these act as protection — preventing the wound from being aggravated by contact with garments or from becoming infected — and the effect is preventative rather than curative. A purely protective intervention of this kind does not constitute a prohibited medicinal treatment.

### Old vs. New

However, *aval lo gemi yavesh* — one may not place a dried piece of reed-like material, *ve-lo ktitin yaveshim* — and not dried-up pieces of cloth. These latter items were understood to have curative properties: they were believed to actively treat the wound, not merely protect it. This creates an apparent contradiction: the *Baraita* first permits a *moch* — a cloth — and then seems to prohibit *ktitin*, which are also pieces of cloth. The *Gemara* resolves this: *lo kashya; ha be-chadti, ha be-atiki* — new cloths have curative properties and are therefore prohibited; old, worn cloths have lost their curative character and serve only a protective function, and are therefore permitted.

### Curative Effect

Abaye draws a conclusion from this: *shema mina, hanei ktitin masu* — one learns from this that cloth pieces placed on a wound can in fact have genuine curative effect. This is not merely a presumption; the *Baraita*'s distinction between new and old cloth presupposes that new cloth actively promotes healing. And since that curative property exists, placing new cloth on a wound falls within the category of prohibited medicinal treatments on *Shabbat* — and the entire discussion of what may and may not be applied to a wound must be understood against that backdrop.
