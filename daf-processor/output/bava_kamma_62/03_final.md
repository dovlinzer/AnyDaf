# Bava Kamma 62 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava's Guardianship Case

### Intro: Tamun Machlokes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְהָלְכָה וְדָלְקָה בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, אֲבָל מַדְלִיק בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁהָיָה בְּתוֹכוֹ. וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לַחֲכָמִים בְּמַשְׁאִיל מָקוֹם לַחֲבֵירוֹ לְהַגְדִּישׁ גָּדִישׁ, וְהִגְדִּישׁ וְהִטְמִין, שֶׁאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי גָדִישׁ בִּלְבָד. לְהַגְדִּישׁ חִטִּין וְהִגְדִּישׁ שְׂעוֹרִין; שְׂעוֹרִין וְהִגְדִּישׁ חִטִּין; חִטִּין וְחִיפָּן בִּשְׂעוֹרִין; שְׂעוֹרִין וְחִיפָּן בְּחִטִּים – שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי שְׂעוֹרִין בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is in a case where one **kindled a fire on his** premises **and** the fire **spread and burned** items **in** premises belonging to **another. But** with regard to one who **kindles a fire on** premises belonging to **another** that destroys a stack of wheat, **all agree** that **he pays** compensation for **everything that was inside** the stack. **And Rabbi Yehuda concedes to the Rabbis that in** a case where one **lends space to another** in his field **to stack grain, and** this person **stacked** grain in the field **and concealed** items inside the stack, if the owner of the field causes the stack to be burned down, **he pays compensation for the stack alone,** since the other person did not have permission to conceal items inside his stack. Similarly, if one received permission **to stack wheat** in the field of another **and he stacked barley;** or conversely, if he received permission to stack **barley and he stacked wheat;** or similarly, if he stacked **wheat and covered** the stack **with barley,** or stacked **barley and covered** the stack **with wheat,** in all these cases, Rabbi Yehuda concedes **that he pays compensation for** the **barley alone,** which is less than the value of wheat.

The daf opens amid a series of cases that work through the finer implications raised by the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* had established a *machlokes* between Rabbi Yehuda and the *chachamim* concerning *tamun* — items concealed within a haystack that is subsequently destroyed by fire. The person who lit the fire is clearly liable for the damage to the haystack itself; the question is whether liability extends to items hidden inside, which the person who set the fire presumably was unaware of and could not reasonably have been expected to anticipate. This dispute opens into a broader discussion of the levels of *shemirah* — guardianship — that a person accepts upon himself, and what consequences flow from those differing levels of accepted responsibility.

### Golden Coin Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַנּוֹתֵן דִּינַר זָהָב לְאִשָּׁה, וְאָמַר לָהּ: "הִזָּהֲרִי בּוֹ, שֶׁל כֶּסֶף הוּא"; הִזִּיקַתּוּ – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת דִּינַר זָהָב, מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמַר לַהּ: מַאי הֲוָה לִיךְ גַּבֵּיהּ דְּאַזַּקְתֵּיהּ. פָּשְׁעָה בּוֹ – מְשַׁלֶּמֶת שֶׁל כֶּסֶף, דְּאָמְרָה לֵיהּ: נְטִירוּתָא דְכַסְפָּא קַבֵּילִי עֲלַי, נְטִירוּתָא דְּדַהֲבָא לָא קַבֵּילִי עֲלַי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** With regard to **one who gives a gold dinar to a woman** for safekeeping **and says to her: Be careful with this** dinar because **it is** made of **silver,** if **she** herself **damaged** the dinar, **she must pay** compensation for the value of **a gold dinar.** This is **because he** can **say to her: What** business **did you have damaging it?** If **she was negligent** and it was lost or stolen, **she pays** as compensation only the value of **a silver** dinar, **since she** can **say to him: I undertook to safeguard only a silver** dinar, **but I did not undertake to safeguard a gold** dinar.

Rava presents a case that illustrates this principle in a guardianship context: a person gave a golden coin to a woman and said to her, "Be careful with it — it is made of silver." In fact, this description was false or deliberately misleading. The coin was gold, worth substantially more than silver — the *gemara* assumes throughout that gold is worth approximately twenty-five times the value of silver, though the shiur notes that exchange rates between silver and gold have shifted considerably since the time of the *gemara*, and whether this was understood as a fixed or variable ratio is itself an interesting question.

### Damage Type Liability

Rava rules that if she damaged the coin directly — for example, she threw it into a fire or struck it with a hammer — she must pay back the full value of a gold *dinar*. The reasoning is straightforward: since she went ahead and actively destroyed the object, she bears full liability for whatever it actually was, regardless of what she was told. The fact that she did not know its true composition does not exempt her from liability for her own destructive act.

However, if the damage occurred through negligence while she was in the role of *shomeres* — that is, she failed in her duty of guardianship in some way that led to damage — she pays back only the value of a silver *dinar*. She can argue: *netirusa d'chaspa kabili alai, netirusa d'dahava lo kabili alai* — I accepted upon myself only the level of *shemirah* appropriate for something made of silver; I never accepted the level of *shemirah* required for something made of gold. Since she believed the item to be silver and acted accordingly throughout her guardianship, her accepted level of liability is calibrated to silver alone.

### Mordechai's Sword Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַתּוּ – בִּדְרָבָא מַתְנִיתוּ לַהּ; אֲנַן – מִמַּתְנִיתָא פְּשִׁיטָא לַן: חִטִּין וְחִיפָּן בִּשְׂעוֹרִין, שְׂעוֹרִין וְחִיפָּן בְּחִטִּין – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶלָּא דְּמֵי שְׂעוֹרִין בִּלְבָד. אַלְמָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ: "נְטִירוּתָא דִשְׂעָרֵי קַבֵּילִי עֲלַי", הָכָא נָמֵי אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: "נְטִירוּתָא דְּדַהֲבָא לָא קַבֵּילִי עֲלַי".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: You teach this** *halakha* as a statement **of Rava,** but for **us** this *halakha* is **obvious from the *baraita*** that taught: If one stacked **wheat and covered** the stack **with barley,** or stacked **barley and covered** the stack **with wheat,** the one responsible for the fire **pays compensation for the barley alone. Evidently,** the one who kindled the fire can **say to** the owner of the stack: **I undertook to safeguard** a stack of **barley** but not a stack of wheat. **Here too,** in the case of a golden dinar presented as a silver dinar, **the** woman **can say to him: I did not undertake to safeguard a gold** dinar.

The Mordechai applies Rava's ruling to a striking practical case rooted in a recognizable historical reality: Jews were frequently in possession of collateral on loans to non-Jews, since they were heavily involved in moneylending and mercantile trade. The case is: Reuven borrowed a sword that Shimon held as collateral (*mashkon*) on a loan he had extended to a non-Jew. While borrowing it, Reuven lost the sword. Later, the non-Jew returns and claims the sword was actually an extraordinarily valuable antique heirloom — worth far more than a standard sword — and Shimon accordingly attempts to extract that inflated value from Reuven.

The Mordechai rules that Reuven cannot be held liable for more than the value of an ordinary sword, since that is what he understood himself to be borrowing and what he accepted *shemirah* over. However, the Mordechai himself adds *tzarich iyun* — he is uncertain whether the case is fully analogous to Rava's.

### Deception Distinction

The Yam Shel Shlomo (the Maharshal) explains the distinction that underlies the Mordechai's hesitation. In Rava's case, the person handing over the coin explicitly stated that it was silver — he actively described the item and thereby defined the scope of *shemirah* the woman would reasonably accept, even if his description was intended to mislead. In the Mordechai's sword case, by contrast, Reuven simply borrowed the sword with no explicit representation about its nature or value. There was no deception, no stated description — he simply took the sword at face value. The absence of any explicit communication about the object's value creates a *machlokes* between the Mordechai and the Yam Shel Shlomo as to whether the same principle applies.

## Baraita Challenge

### Baraita vs. Rava

The *gemara* continues with Rav Mordechai posing a pointed question to Rav Ashi: why must we derive this *halacha* from Rava's case at all? The exact same conclusion, he argues, is demonstrably established by a *baraita* — making the citation of Rava's ruling unnecessary.

### Wheat-Barley Mix

The *baraita* presents the following scenario: a person received permission to deposit a load of wheat in a *chatzer* and heap it into a large pile, but instead placed the wheat first and then covered it with barley on top — or alternatively, placed barley first and then covered it with wheat. In either configuration, the exterior appearance of the heap does not accurately represent what is inside, whether because what lies beneath is more valuable or less.

### Baraita Ruling

The *baraita* rules that if the pile is damaged, the liable party pays only the value of barley (*dmei se'orin bilvad*). This holds in both configurations: if barley was actually burned, the damage is limited to barley even if the outside appeared to be wheat; and if wheat was burned but it was hidden inside, that is *tamun* — concealed — and the liability is accordingly limited to the lower valuation of barley. Either way, the maximum recovery is barley.

### Parallel Conclusion

The *baraita* thus establishes the same principle Rava articulated: the person watching the pile can always claim, *netiruta de-se'orei kabili alai* — I only accepted guardianship over barley-level value. And so too the woman in Rava's case can say she never accepted guardianship over gold. The *baraita* already teaches everything Rava's case teaches, which was Rav Mordechai's point.

## Takanat Nigzal

### Rav's Forgotten Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: שְׁמַעִית מִילְּתָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי הִיא. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וְלָא יָדַע אַבָּא מַאי שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ? לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דִּמְחַיֵּיב עַל נִזְקֵי טָמוּן בָּאֵשׁ – עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנַת נִגְזָל בְּאִשּׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: I heard** a halakhic **matter** in connection **with Rabbi Yehuda's** opinion in the mishna **but I do not know what it is. Shmuel said: And does Abba,** which was Rav's name, really **not know what he heard?** This is the statement that he heard: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, who deems** one **liable for concealed** articles damaged by **a fire,** the Sages **applied the ordinance of a robbery victim to** one whose concealed items are damaged by **his fire.** Just as the Sages instituted an ordinance that a robbery victim can take an oath as to what was stolen from him and the robber must repay him accordingly, similarly, according to Rabbi Yehuda the owner of the stack can take an oath that certain items were inside the stack, and the one responsible for the fire must pay compensation for them.

Rav states: *shamei'a li milta de-Rabbi Yehuda ve-lo yadana mai hi* — I recall having learned a *halacha* connected to Rabbi Yehuda's *shita*, but I cannot remember what it was.

### Reminder of Yehuda

Shmuel, Rav's colleague, addresses him in the third person with the respectful appellation *Abba* — a name by which Rav was also known. Shmuel says: does *Abba* not know what he heard from Rabbi Yehuda? The *halacha* is that Rabbi Yehuda holds one is *chayav* for *nizkei tamun ba'esh* — damages to items concealed within a haystack destroyed by fire.

### Nigzal Oath Mechanism

The additional *halacha* Rav had heard in connection with Rabbi Yehuda's *shita* but forgotten is: *asu takanat nigzal be'isho* — the rabbis also enacted the *takanat nigzal* in connection with fire cases. To understand this, one must appreciate the broader institution found in Masechet Shavuot. Whereas biblical oaths (*shevuot de-oraita*) are almost always *nishba ve-niftar* — you take an oath to exempt yourself from liability — the rabbis instituted a category of oaths that operate in the reverse direction: *nishba ve-notel*, you take an oath in order to collect. These are rabbinic oaths designed for situations where the amount owed is in dispute or uncertain.

The *takanat nigzal* is one such institution. Suppose someone's wallet was stolen. The thief is caught but claims there were only two dollars inside. The victim claims there were two hundred. In such a case, the *nigzal* — the person who was robbed — takes a rabbinic oath swearing to the full amount, and the thief must then pay that amount. Without such a mechanism, a person who has genuinely been robbed could be systematically cheated, since the thief controls the narrative.

### Why Nigzal Needed

Within Rabbi Yehuda's *shita* — which holds one liable even for *tamun ba'esh*, items hidden inside a burned haystack — a *takanat nigzal* becomes necessary for precisely this reason. Once liability for concealed items is established, the question of how much those items were worth becomes unresolvable through ordinary means, since no one can be certain what was inside. The property owner must therefore take a *shevuah* to establish the value, and the party who caused the fire must pay accordingly.

## Moser & Nigzal

### Moser Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי אַמֵּימָר: עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנַת נִגְזָל בְּמָסוֹר, אוֹ לָא? אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא דָּיְינִינַן דִּינָא דִגְרָמֵי – לָא תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּמָסוֹרוֹת נָמֵי לָא דָּיְינִינַן; אֶלָּא כִּי תִּבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר דָּיְינִינַן דִּינָא דִגְרָמֵי – עָשׂוּ תַּקָּנַת נִגְזָל בְּמָסוֹר, דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע וְשָׁקֵיל, אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar asks:** Did **they apply** this **ordinance of a robbery victim in** the case of **an informer** who causes another person's property to be confiscated by the gentile authorities, **or not?** The question is clarified: **According to** the opinion of **the one who says** that **we do not judge** cases of **liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action, **do not ask** the question, **as** according to that opinion **we also do not judge** cases **of** liability for **informers** who cause indirect damage. **Rather, when you ask** this question, **it is in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said** that **we judge** cases of **liability** for damage caused by **indirect** action, and the question is: Did the Sages **apply the ordinance of a robbery victim to** one whose items were taken due to an **informer,** meaning **that** the victim **can take an oath** to support his claim with regard to what was taken **and take** this amount in compensation, **or not?** No conclusion was reached about this, and the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Amimar, who lived considerably later than Rav and Shmuel, raises a new question: does the *takanat nigzal* apply in a *moser* case — that is, a case where someone turned over another person's money to the non-Jewish authorities?

### Moser Definition

A *moser* is one who informs on another Jew to external, non-Jewish authorities, causing that person's property to be seized or their financial affairs to be disrupted. The laws of *mesirah* carry serious *halachic* weight, and the extent to which they apply has been extensively discussed in later *poskim*.

### Modern Authority Discussion

One *posek* — likely the Tzitz Eliezer — formulated the principle that the degree to which the laws of *moser* apply is inversely proportional to how much weight one gives to *dina demalchusa dina*, the principle that the law of the land carries legal validity. If one trusts the civil authorities and regards the surrounding legal system as legitimate and non-discriminatory, there is far less reason to fear that turning someone over to those authorities will result in anything unjust or harmful. The concern about *mesirah* is most acute under discriminatory or antisemitic governments that might manipulate the law against a Jew. In such circumstances, if a Jewish person turns another over to a system that will treat them unfairly, the outcome may be that their money or property is unjustly taken — and that constitutes a kind of *gezeila*.

### Moser as Gezel

The argument for treating *mesirah* as a form of *gezel* runs as follows: if one tells the authorities that a fellow Jew owes back taxes, for instance, and as a result that person's money is extracted by the government — perhaps wrongly or in an inflated amount — the *moser* has effectively caused the theft of that person's money. The victim would then need a *takanat nigzal* to establish how much was lost, just as in the direct theft case. Amimar's question is whether *chazal* actually enacted the *shevuah* mechanism for this situation.

### Garmi & Nigzal

The *gemara* structures the question around the dispute regarding *dinei degarmi*. According to the opinion that holds we do not adjudicate *dinei degarmi* — indirect damage — there is no question at all: *mesirah* is at best a species of *garmi*, and if *garmi* carries no liability, there is nothing to discuss. The question only arises, Amimar states, according to the opinion that holds we do adjudicate *dinei degarmi*.

Even according to that opinion, however, there remains a question. Tosafot Rabbeinu Peretz (citing an argument from the Tosafot on 54a) argues that this *sugya* itself demonstrates that *dinei degarmi* cannot be *mide-oraita* — cannot operate at the same biblical level as direct damage — because if it were, there would be no question about whether *takanat nigzal* applies: it would apply automatically, just as it does in cases of direct damage. The Ramban, by contrast, holds that *dinei degarmi* is *de-oraita* and is equivalent to direct *nezek*; but the Ramban then must account for why a question still arises here, and his answer is that there remains a meaningful distinction between *garmi* damage and direct damage even if both operate at the biblical level.

### Teiku Resolution

The question is left as *teiku* — unresolved. This creates a practical problem: if a *moser* case arises, who pays, and how much? The *Rif* is quoted as holding that since the question is *teiku* in *mammonot*, we rule *le-kula* — leniently — meaning the *moser* can only be compelled to pay the amount actually proven, and no oath-based collection mechanism is available to the victim. A second approach, associated with the view of Sumchos, would have the parties split the disputed amount. The Ri rejects both of these and argues that a *teiku* means the case cannot be resolved at all — not just that we rule *le-kula*, but that we fundamentally lack the tools to adjudicate it. Rabbeinu Tam has yet another answer. The shiur notes that the difficulty with *teiku* in this context is that leniency for one party is stringency for the other, which makes applying a simple *le-kula* rule complicated.

## Money Box Jewels

### Scenario Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דִּבְטַשׁ בְּכַסְפְּתָא דְחַבְרֵיהּ, שַׁדְיַיהּ בְּנַהֲרָא. אֲתָא מָרֵיהּ וְאָמַר: הָכִי וְהָכִי הֲוָה לִי בְּגַוַּהּ. יָתֵיב רַב אָשֵׁי וְקָא מְעַיֵּין בֵּיהּ: כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** An incident was related about **a certain man who kicked a safe belonging to another, sending it** flying **into the river. The owner** of the safe **came** to court **and said: I had such and such inside it. Rav Ashi sat and was investigating** the question: **In a case like this, what** is the *halakha*? Does the court believe the claimant or not?

The *gemara* presents a new case. A man kicked his friend's money box (*kaspesa*) into a river, causing it to be lost. The owner of the box came and claimed he is entitled to recover a specific sum, asserting that the box contained a large amount of valuables.

### Rav Ashi's Doubt

Rav Ashi sat and carefully considered the case, uncertain how to rule. The scenario presumably activates the *takanat nigzal* framework — the owner is making a claim about what was inside the box — but something about the specifics gave Rav Ashi pause.

### House Fire Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לָאו הַיְינוּ מַתְנִיתִין? דִּתְנַן: וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּמַדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה, שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם כׇּל מַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכוֹ; שֶׁכֵּן דֶּרֶךְ בְּנֵי אָדָם לְהַנִּיחַ בַּבָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, and some say** that **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: Is this not** identical to the case of **the mishna, as we learned** in the mishna: **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Yehuda** that if one **sets fire to a building, he pays** compensation **for everything that was** burned **inside it, since it is the** normal **way of people to place** items **in houses?** Similarly, it is the normal way of people to place money in a safe, and the court should rely on the claim of the victim.

Ravina, speaking either to Rav Acha son of Rava or directly to Rav Ashi, suggested a parallel to the *mishnah*: the *chachamim* agree with Rabbi Yehuda that if a person lights a fire and burns down a house, they are liable for everything in the house (*kol she-betocha*). Items inside a house do not count as *tamun* in the same sense as items in a haystack, and so even the *chachamim* concede liability for the house's contents.

### Normal vs. Unexpected

However, the *mishnah* limits this liability to what people normally keep in their houses (*sheken derech benei adam lehaniach bavattim*). One cannot claim beyond that — one cannot say there was an antique of extraordinary value, because that claim would be unverifiable and would effectively be treated like *tamun* — something no one could have been expected to anticipate. But where an object is the kind of thing one would normally expect to find in such a location, liability is clear because the *ta'am* for the *tamun* exemption — namely, the unexpectedness of the item — simply does not apply.

So why was Rav Ashi uncertain at all? Because the owner was not claiming that the box contained money — he was claiming it contained *marganita*, pearls. That is a claim well beyond what one would ordinarily expect in a cash box, and that is why the house fire *mishnah* does not straightforwardly resolve the case.

### Pearls in Box Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי דְּקָא טָעֵין זוּזֵי – הָכָא נָמֵי, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דְּקָא טָעֵין מַרְגָּנִיתָא. מַאי? מִי מַנְּחִי אִינָשֵׁי מַרְגָּנִיתָא בְּכַסְפְּתָא, אוֹ לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: If** the claimant **were to claim** that there were **coins** in the safe, this ruling would apply **here also.** But **with what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the claimant **claims** that there was **a pearl [*marganita*]** in the safe. **What** is the *halakha*? **Do people** typically **place pearls in a safe,** in which case he should be believed, **or not?** The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* frames the unresolved issue: *ee manach inish marganita be-kaspesa o lo* — do people normally store pearls in cash boxes or not? The shiur notes an interesting parallel from Masechet Kelim: the *mishnayot* there distinguish between a *tsror ha-margalit*, a folded leather pouch used for pearls — typically kept sealed for months or years at a time — and a *tsror ma'ot*, a similar pouch used for coins, which is opened and closed repeatedly throughout the day. The functional difference in usage patterns reflects a real distinction in how people relate to pearls versus money. This context sheds light on the question of whether a *kaspesa* — a cash box — is the kind of vessel one would use for pearls. The *gemara* leaves this *teiku*.

## Status & Reliability

### Silver Goblet Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: טָעֵין כָּסָא דְכַסְפָּא בְּבִירָה, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: (חָזֵינָא) [חָזֵינַן], אִי אִינִישׁ אֲמִיד הוּא – דְּאִית לֵיהּ כָּסָא דְכַסְפָּא, אִי נָמֵי אִינִישׁ מְהֵימְנָא הוּא – דְּמַפְקְדִי אִינָשֵׁי גַּבֵּיהּ; מִשְׁתְּבַע וְשָׁקֵיל. וְאִי לָא, לָאו כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar said to Rav Ashi:** If the one whose home was burned by the fire were to **claim** that he had, among other items, **a silver goblet in the building, what** is the *halakha*? Is he believed or not? Rav Ashi **said to him: We view** his situation. **If he is a wealthy person, who** would typically **have a silver goblet, or alternatively, if he is a trustworthy person with whom people entrust** valuable items, he **can take an oath** that this is what he had **and take** compensation according to his claim. **But if** he is **not** such a person, it **is not in his** power to be believed in such a claim.

Rav Yeimar poses a further question to Rav Ashi: what if a person claims there was a *kasa de-khaspa* — a silver goblet — inside his house when it burned down? Should that claim be accepted?

### Evaluation Criteria

Rav Ashi answers by laying out two distinct criteria. First: *hazina im inish amid hu de-it lei kasa de-khaspa* — we examine whether this person is wealthy enough that owning a silver goblet would be a reasonable expectation. Second, even if the person is not personally wealthy: *inish mehemna hu de-mapkidei inishei gabei* — is he perhaps a trustworthy individual with whom others would deposit valuables for *shemirah*? In other words, even a person of modest means might credibly have had a silver goblet in his possession if he was known as a reliable guardian.

### Socioeconomic Factor

If either criterion is met, the claimant may take the *shevuah* and collect — operating within the *nishba ve-notel* framework of rabbinic oaths. If neither criterion applies, there are no adequate grounds to grant the oath: the claim simply lacks sufficient plausibility. This ruling establishes that a person's economic standing and social reputation are directly relevant to whether their claim about a valuable item is legally cognizable.

## Gazlan vs. Chamsan

### Definition Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אַוְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מָה בֵּין גַּזְלָן לְחַמְסָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַמְסָן – יָהֵיב דְּמֵי, גַּזְלָן – לָא יָהֵיב דְּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda, son of Rav Avya, said to Rav Ashi:** Concerning two terms used to describe those who take another's property against his will, **a *gazlan* and a *ḥamsan*, what** is the difference **between them?** Rav Ashi **said to him: A *ḥamsan* gives money** for the article that he takes from its owner, albeit against the owner's will, whereas **a *gazlan* does not give money.**

Rav Abba son of Rav Aviya raises a question to Rav Ashi that appears at first glance to be off-topic, though many *mefarshim* grapple with why it appears here. The question is: *mah bein gazlan le-chamsan* — what is the difference between a *gazlan*, an armed robber, and a *chamsan*, a term we are about to learn refers to one who pressures another person into selling something they did not wish to sell?

### Payment Difference

The answer: a *chamsan* pays for the item, whereas a *gazlan* does not. Both are forcibly taking something from another person, but the crucial distinction is whether money changes hands. A *gazlan* simply takes the item; a *chamsan* coerces the owner into a transaction, paying a price but doing so under duress.

### Rambam-Ra'avad

This leads naturally to the question of what *aveira* a *chamsan* has violated, and the *rishonim* address this at length. The operative prohibition appears to be *lo tachmod*. The Rambam and Ra'avad have what might be called a classic *machlokes* on the nature of this prohibition. The Rambam understands *lo tachmod* — the prohibition against coveting another's property — as a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh*, a prohibition without an active physical component in its violation. Because the violation is essentially psychological — a state of wanting and pressuring, not a discrete physical act — one does not receive *makkot* for it. The Ra'avad objects: if the entire thrust of the prohibition is that one pressured another person into handing over the object — pestering and coercing them until they capitulated — how can that be called a *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh*? Surely the act of coercion is the *ma'aseh*! The Ra'avad therefore explains the absence of *makkot* differently: since the violation is *de-ita be-tashlumin* — one can make monetary restitution — it falls into the category of transgressions for which one does not receive *makkot*, just as a thief pays back but does not receive lashes.

### Sefer HaChinuch

Another dimension is raised by the Sefer HaChinuch in discussing *lo titaveh*, the formulation of the coveting prohibition as it appears in Devarim. The Sefer HaChinuch states that *lo titaveh* applies to non-Jews as well. This raises the obvious question: which of the seven *mitzvot bnei noach* does this fall under? The Sefer HaChinuch answers that it is subsumed under *geneivah* and *gezeila* — theft. His reasoning is that the seven Noahide categories are broad headings, each containing numerous sub-prohibitions; *avodah zarah* and *giluy arayot*, for instance, each encompass many distinct prohibitions within them. *Lo titaveh* is simply a sub-category of the prohibition against theft.

### Forced Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי יָהֵיב דְּמֵי – חַמְסָן קָרֵית לֵיהּ?! וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תְּלוּהּ וְזַבֵּין – זְבִינֵיהּ זְבִינֵי! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאָמַר "רוֹצֶה אֲנִי", הָא דְּלָא אָמַר "רוֹצֶה אֲנִי".
>
> **Translation:** Rav Adda was puzzled by this response and **said to him: If he gives money, do you** still **call him a *ḥamsan*?** Since he pays money for it, he acquires it lawfully, despite the fact that its owner did not sell it voluntarily. **But doesn't Rav Huna say:** If one was **strung up** so that another could coerce him to sell a certain item, **and he sold** it, **his sale is** a valid **sale.** This indicates that a sale under duress is considered a valid sale. Rav Ashi answered: This is **not difficult. This** case, where the sale under duress is legally considered to be a sale, is referring to a case **where he** eventually **says: I want** to sell the item, despite having been forced. By contrast, in **that** case, where the sale is invalid, **he did not say: I want** to sell the item.

A *gemara* challenge arises from a ruling of Rav Huna: *talyuha ve-zavin, zavinei zavina* — if a person was coerced and threatened into selling something, it is nonetheless a valid sale. This seems to undermine the distinction between *gazlan* and *chamsan*, since if someone forced a sale and paid the price, Rav Huna says the sale stands — implying there is no residual liability.

### Consent Distinction

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *ha de-amar rotzeh ani, ha de-lo amar rotzeh ani* — the determining factor is whether the person said *rotzeh ani*, "I consent." If under duress the person ultimately said "I am willing" and received the payment, Rav Huna's rule applies and the transaction stands. But if the person never said *rotzeh ani* — if they were so completely coerced that they never expressed even a formal acquiescence — then the forced taker is classified as a *chamsan*. The shiur notes that the Rambam does not cite this distinction in the Mishneh Torah, which makes his omission worthy of further analysis.

## Sparks & Camel

### Hammer Spark

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ גֵּץ שֶׁיָּצָא מִתַּחַת הַפַּטִּישׁ וְהִזִּיק – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a spark that emerged from under the hammer** of a blacksmith and started a fire, **causing damage,** the blacksmith is **liable** for the damage caused.

The final *mishnah* of Perek Hakoneis presents several cases involving fire and liability across domain boundaries. The first: a spark (*gaits*) flies out from beneath a blacksmith's hammer and causes damage after crossing from *reshut ha-yachid* into *reshut ha-rabbim*. The blacksmith is *chayav* because fire — and by extension a *gaits* that functions as fire — spreads, and that is the nature of *esh*.

### Camel Flax Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גָּמָל שֶׁהָיָה טָעוּן פִּשְׁתָּן, וְעָבַר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְנִכְנְסָה פִּשְׁתָּנוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַחֲנוּת וְדָלְקָה בְּנֵרוֹ שֶׁל חֶנְוָנִי, וְהִדְלִיק אֶת הַבִּירָה – בַּעַל גָּמָל חַיָּיב. הִנִּיחַ חֶנְוָנִי נֵרוֹ מִבַּחוּץ – הַחֶנְוָנִי חַיָּיב. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּנֵר חֲנוּכָּה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a camel that was laden with flax and was passing through the public domain, and its flax extended into a store and** the flax **caught fire from a lamp** in the store **belonging to the storekeeper, and** as a result of the burning flax the camel **set fire to the building** together with all its contents, **the owner of the camel** is **liable.** But if **the storekeeper placed his lamp outside,** thereby causing the flax on the camel to catch fire, and consequently the building was set on fire, **the storekeeper** is **liable. Rabbi Yehuda says: In** a case where the lamp placed outside was **a Hanukkah lamp,** the storekeeper is **exempt,** since it is a mitzva for a Hanukkah lamp to be placed outside.

The second case: a camel was very heavily laden with flax, an extremely flammable material, and was walking through *reshut ha-rabbim*. Because the load was so excessive, the flax pushed through a window into a nearby shop and caught fire on the shopkeeper's candle. That fire then spread and consumed an entire building.

### Camel Liability

The owner of the camel is *chayav*. The reasoning is that the camel should not have been transporting such a flammable material in such a way that it would intrude into another person's *reshut ha-yachid* — a space where the shop owner had no reason to guard against an overloaded camel igniting his lamp. The camel's encroachment into the private domain is what triggers liability.

### Lamp Outside

The *mishnah* presents the reverse case: if the shop owner placed his lamp outside — that is, in *reshut ha-rabbim* — then the shop owner is *chayav*. He placed something dangerous — an open flame — in the public domain, and is therefore liable for any damage that results, even if it cascades into the destruction of an entire building.

### Fuel vs. Flame

An important equivalence principle emerges from these cases, one that appears in several other contexts in *chazal*: bringing fuel to an existing flame and bringing a flame to existing fuel are treated symmetrically in terms of liability. Whether the *baal ha-gamal* brought combustible material to the lamp, or the shop owner brought the flame to a place where flammable material might encounter it, the structure of liability is parallel. The shiur notes that this equivalence appears as well in the laws of Shabbat, and there is a *baraita* in Beitzah that similarly draws on it.

## Chanukah Candle

### Chanukah Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רָבִינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, נֵר חֲנוּכָּה – מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחָהּ בְּתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה, אַמַּאי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה פָּטוּר? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְאַנּוֹחַהּ לְמַעְלָה מִגָּמָל וְרוֹכְבוֹ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: מִצְוָה לְהַנִּיחָהּ בְּתוֹךְ עֲשָׂרָה?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ravina said in the name of Rava: Conclude from Rabbi Yehuda's** statement that **the mitzva is to place the Hanukkah lamp within** a height of **ten** handbreadths from the ground **as, if it enters your mind** to say that the mitzva can also be fulfilled by placing it **above ten handbreadths, why did Rabbi Yehuda say that** in the case of **a Hanukkah lamp** placed outside, the storekeeper is **exempt? Let** the owner of the camel **say to** the storekeeper: **You should have placed** the lamp **above the** height **of a camel and its rider** so that the flax would not catch fire. **Rather,** must one **not conclude from** here that Rabbi Yehuda's opinion is that **the mitzva is to place it within ten handbreadths** of the ground and not higher?

At the close of the *mishnah* — one of only seven places in the entire Mishnah that mentions Chanukah — Rabbi Yehuda rules: if the lamp in the public domain that caused the fire was a *ner Chanukah*, the shopkeeper is *patur*. There is a special license, which the *gemara* will elaborate, to place a Chanukah candle into the public street.

### Ten Tefachim Rule

Ravina states in the name of Rava: *shma mina mi-divrei Rabbi Yehuda ner Chanukah mitzvah lehaniha betoch asarah* — from Rabbi Yehuda's ruling we can derive that it is a *mitzvah* to place the Chanukah candle within ten *tefachim* of the ground. The *gemara* reasons as follows: if one could place the candle above ten *tefachim*, then in the camel case one would be obligated to do precisely that — put it somewhere safely above the height of a camel and its rider. Since Rabbi Yehuda exempts the candle owner even when a camel is damaged, it must be that the candle was placed below ten *tefachim* and there was no option to place it safely above.

### Mitzvah License

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: לָא; לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ אֲפִילּוּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה, מַאי אָמְרַתְּ – אִבְּעִי לָךְ לְאַנּוֹחַהּ לְמַעְלָה מִגָּמָל וְרוֹכְבוֹ? כֵּיוָן דִּבְמִצְוָה קָא עָסֵיק, כּוּלֵּי הַאי לָא אַטְרְחוּהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **No,** there is no proof from here. **Actually, I** could **say to you** that one may place a Hanukkah lamp **even above** a height of **ten** handbreadths, and as for **what you said,** that the owner of the camel can claim: **You should have placed** the lamp **above** the height **of a camel and its rider,** in response to this one can say: **Since** the store owner **is performing a mitzva** by placing his Hanukkah lamp outside, **the Sages did not trouble him to such an extent** to have him place his lamp at a height that would be inconvenient for him to reach.

The *gemara* then offers an alternative reading: perhaps even above ten *tefachim* is acceptable for a Chanukah candle, and the exemption in the camel case simply reflects the fact that *rabbanan* do not impose such logistical burdens on someone engaged in a *mitzvah*. The rabbis wanted to make fulfilling the *mitzvah* accessible and did not require the person to install special elevated holders to keep the candle out of harm's way. On this view, whether the candle is above or below ten *tefachim*, one would be *patur* for damage caused, because the act of placing the candle in the street is itself mandated or at least licensed by rabbinic enactment.

### Legal Domain

The shiur connects this to a *halacha* from Hilchot Shabbat: only the lowest ten *tefachim* of *reshut ha-rabbim* are halachically categorized as *reshut ha-rabbim* proper. Above ten *tefachim* is *makom patur* — an exempted domain entirely. The case of throwing a fig against a wall illustrates this: if the fig comes to rest above ten *tefachim*, it is not considered to have landed in *reshut ha-rabbim* even though it was thrown there. So too, the Chanukah candle must be within the halachically operative portion of *reshut ha-rabbim* — below ten *tefachim* — rather than in the zone above.

### Why Not Higher

Rabbi Yehuda's exemption thus requires placing the candle in a genuinely dangerous position — the very place where a camel and its rider might encounter it — because that is where the *mitzvah* requires it to be. The *gemara* notes that Chanukah candles are among those *mitzvot* for which the rabbis were particularly emphatic in their expectation of fulfillment: one must beg for money (*sho'el al ha-petachim*) if necessary to afford oil for the lamp. The rabbis' strong desire to enable performance of this *mitzvah* is thus reflected in the ruling that one is not held liable for placing it in the public street, even when doing so creates genuine hazard.

### Above Twenty Amah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא, דָּרֵשׁ רַב נָתָן בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם: נֵר חֲנוּכָּה שֶׁהִנִּיחָהּ לְמַעְלָה מֵעֶשְׂרִים אַמָּה – פְּסוּלָה, כְּסוּכָּה וּכְמָבוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** Further on the subject of the height of a Hanukkah lamp, **Rav Kahana says** that **Rav Natan bar Minyumi taught in the name of Rabbi Tanḥum: A Hanukkah lamp that is placed higher than twenty cubits** from the ground is **unfit** to be used for the mitzva, since people do not generally look up to such a height, and the main purpose of the Hanukkah lamp is for it to be seen by others. In this regard, the *halakha* of the height of a Hanukkah lamp is **like** the *halakha* of the height of **a *sukka* and** the *halakha* of the height of a cross beam at the entrance to **an alleyway,** which are unfit for their respective purposes if they are higher than twenty cubits.

Rav Kahana cites a teaching of Rav Natan bar Minyumi in the name of Rabbi Tanchum: a Chanukah candle placed above twenty *amot* is *psulah* — disqualified — comparable to a *sukkah* and to a *mavoi*.

### Twenty Amah Principle

The *sukkah*, of course, is the subject of the opening *daf* of Masechet Sukkah, where a three-way *machlokes amoraim* addresses why a *sukkah* taller than twenty *amot* is disqualified. The *mavoi* — the alleyway entrance with a cross-beam (*korah*) — is governed by the first *mishnah* in Eruvin and similarly must have its *korah* below twenty *amot*.

### Visibility Principle

The opinion most relevant to the Chanukah case is that of Rabba: a *sukkah* taller than twenty *amot* is disqualified because one sitting inside cannot see the *s'chach* from below — the roof is simply too high to be perceived, and the essential character of the *sukkah* is therefore not experienced. Rava's opinion — that a structure twenty *amot* tall must be permanently built and is therefore *dirat keva* rather than *dirat arai*, a permanent dwelling rather than a temporary one — is distinct. The Chanukah case maps most naturally onto the visibility rationale: a candle placed above twenty *amot* cannot be seen by people walking in the street below, and since the purpose of the Chanukah candle is *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle — a candle no one can see does not accomplish its purpose and is therefore disqualified.

The same logic underlies the *mavoi* rule: if the *korah* marking the boundary of the *eruv* is above twenty *amot*, people approaching the end of the *mavoi* will not see it and will unwittingly cross into *reshut ha-rabbim*. The shiur observes that this concern — people not noticing the edge of an *eruv* — was clearly significant to *chazal*, and raises the question of whether modern *eruvin*, which are often not readily visible to the untrained eye, adequately address this requirement.

## Perek Meruba Intro

### Fine Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מְרוּבָּה מִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל מִמִּדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. שֶׁמִּדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בְּדָבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים וּבֵין בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ רוּחַ חַיִּים, וּמִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בְּשׁוֹר וָשֶׂה בִּלְבַד – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי יִגְנֹב אִישׁ שׁוֹר אוֹ שֶׂה וּטְבָחוֹ אוֹ מְכָרוֹ וְגוֹ׳".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The principle of double payment** applies **more broadly than the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment, as the principle of double payment applies both to** the theft of **something that is alive and to** the theft of **something that is not alive, but the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment applies only to** the theft of **an ox or a sheep, as it is stated: "If a man steal an ox or a sheep, and slaughter it or sell it,** he shall pay five oxen for an ox and four sheep for a sheep" (Exodus 21:37).

With the conclusion of Perek Hakoneis — marked by the traditional *hadran* — the *gemara* opens Perek Meruba, which launches the extended treatment of *geneivah* and *gezeila*, theft and robbery. The opening *mishnah* introduces the two categories of monetary fines the Torah imposes in cases of theft committed as a *ganav* — secretly, in the manner of a burglar.

### Kefel More Extensive

The *mishnah* states: *meruba midat tashlumei kefel mi-midat tashlumei arba ve-chamisha* — the scope of *kefel*, the double-payment penalty, is broader than that of *tashlumei arba ve-chamisha*, the four- and five-fold payment. The *mishnah* proceeds to demonstrate the ways in which this is so.

### Application Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הַגּוֹנֵב אַחַר הַגַּנָּב מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל, וְלֹא הַטּוֹבֵחַ וְלֹא הַמּוֹכֵר אַחַר הַגַּנָּב מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Having stated a limitation to the *halakha* of fourfold and fivefold payment, the mishna mentions a further limitation, which applies to all three types of payments. **One who steals** an item **after a thief** has already stolen it, i.e., one who steals a stolen item, **does not pay** the **double payment** to the thief or to the prior owner, **nor does one who slaughters or sells** an ox or a sheep **after a thief** has already stolen it **pay** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** Rather, he pays only the principal, i.e., the value of the item he stole.

*Kefel* applies to theft of any object whatsoever — whether animate or inanimate — while *tashlumei arba ve-chamisha* applies only to an ox (*shor*) and a sheep (*seh*). The explicit biblical verse states: *ki yignov ish shor o seh u-tvacho o mecharo* — if a man steals an ox or a sheep and slaughters or sells it. The four- and five-fold penalty is triggered only by these animals and only when the theft was followed by slaughter or sale; there is no analog for any other stolen item.

### Exceptions Listed

The *mishnah* notes several additional distinctions. A thief who steals from another thief does not pay *kefel*. And if the theft and the subsequent slaughter or sale were carried out by two different individuals — one stole the animal, another sold or slaughtered it — *tashlumei arba ve-chamisha* is not paid by the second person either.

### Source Derivation

Rashi explains the exclusion of a thief stealing from a thief: the verse states *ve-gunav mi-beit ha-ish* — it was stolen from the person's house, not from the thief's house. Stealing from a thief's possession does not carry the same level of liability as stealing from the original owner.

## Toen Ta'anat Ganav

### Applicability Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ "מִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל נוֹהֶגֶת בֵּין בְּגַנָּב בֵּין בְּטוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב, וּמִדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה אֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת אֶלָּא בְּגַנָּב בִּלְבַד" – לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches one particular difference between double payment and fourfold or fivefold payment, **while it does not teach** this difference between them: **The principle of double payment applies both in** the case of **a thief and in** the case of a bailee who falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole the deposit entrusted to him and who takes an oath to that effect. **But the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment applies only in** the case of **a thief,** not in the case of one who falsely claims that a deposit was stolen.

The *gemara* notes an apparent gap in the *mishnah's* enumeration. The *mishnah* establishes that *kefel* is broader than *arba ve-chamisha*, but leaves out one category: *kefel* applies not only to an actual *ganav* but also to a *toen ta'anat ganav* — a guardian who falsely claims the item was stolen. The *mishnah* does not mention this, which prompts the question of whether the list is meant to be exhaustive.

### Toen Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַטּוֹעֵן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. טָבַח וּמָכַר – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The fact that the mishna does not teach the latter distinction **supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, as Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of **one who** falsely **states the claim** that **a thief** stole a **deposit** from him, and it is discovered that he himself is the thief, he **pays double payment.** And if the stolen item is an ox or sheep and **he slaughtered or sold** it, **he pays** the **fourfold or fivefold payment.** According to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, there is no difference between the applications of double payment and fourfold or fivefold payment in this case.

A *toen ta'anat ganav* is a *shomer chinam* — an unpaid guardian — who is watching an object. An unpaid guardian is exempt from liability if the object is stolen; theft is simply not among the risks he accepted. To avail himself of this exemption, he must take *shvuat ha-shomrim*, the biblical guardian's oath, swearing the object was stolen. If witnesses subsequently come and establish that he was lying — that he himself stole it — he is called a *toen ta'anat ganav*. He must return the object and, the *gemara* holds, also pay *kefel*, because falsely claiming theft while having stolen the item oneself places one in a category equivalent to that of a thief.

### Resolution

The *gemara* establishes: *kefel* does apply to a *toen ta'anat ganav*, but *arba ve-chamisha* does not. If the *toen ta'anat ganav* subsequently slaughtered or sold the animal, he pays *kefel* but not four or five times. This is one more respect in which *kefel* is broader — it reaches the *toen ta'anat ganav* in full, while *arba ve-chamisha* does not apply to him at all.

### Tosafot Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי קָתָנֵי: "אֵין בֵּין"?! "מְרוּבָּה" קָתָנֵי – תְּנָא וְשַׁיַּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the inference: **Does** the mishna **teach** that **there is no** difference **between** double payment and fourfold or fivefold payment except for the one mentioned in the mishna? **It teaches** merely that the principle of double payment applies **more broadly** than the principle of fourfold or fivefold payment, and it provides an example of this statement. This does not mean that this is the only difference, and it is possible that the *tanna* **taught** this one case **and omitted** others.

Tosafot raises a pointed question about the word *meruba* — "broader" — in the *mishnah*. The concern is whether *meruba* functions analogously to *ein bein* formulations found elsewhere in the Mishnah, such as in Masechet Megillah, which enumerate *only* the differences between two cases and thereby implicitly exclude any unlisted differences. If *meruba* carries the same exhaustive force, the omission of *toen ta'anat ganav* from the list would imply that this is not actually a way in which *kefel* exceeds *arba ve-chamisha* — a problematic conclusion.

### Ri Me'Orleans

The Ri me'Orleans challenges this reading. He cites the *mishnah* in the first chapter of Megillah — *ein bein nedarim le-nedavot* — and works through multiple examples in which the *mishnah* uses *ein bein* or comparable formulations yet demonstrably leaves items off the list. This shows that rabbinic enumerations, even those introduced with limiting language, are not always exhaustive. *Meruba*, then, simply identifies some of the ways in which *kefel* is broader; it does not purport to be a complete accounting. The distinction between the *mishnah* listing examples for convenience versus making an exhaustive legal declaration is an important one, the shiur notes, with implications well beyond this particular case.

## Klal U'Prat Analysis

### Oath Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמִּדַּת תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל נוֹהֶגֶת כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: "עַל כׇּל דְּבַר פֶּשַׁע" – כָּלַל, "עַל שׁוֹר, עַל חֲמוֹר, עַל שֶׂה וְעַל שַׂלְמָה" – פָּרַט, "עַל כׇּל אֲבֵדָה" – חָזַר וְכָלַל;
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches **that the principle of double payment applies** both to the theft of something that is alive and to the theft of something that is not alive. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse that discusses double payment: "For any matter of trespass, whether it be for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment, or for any manner of lost thing about which one shall say: This is it, the claims of both of them shall come before the judges, the one whom the judges convict shall pay double to his neighbor" (Exodus 22:8). **"For any matter of trespass"** is **a generalization; "whether it be for an ox, for a donkey, for a sheep, for a garment"** is **a detail.** And when the verse states: **"Or for any manner of lost thing," it then generalizes** again.

The *gemara* turns to the biblical basis for the laws of *shomrim* and the applicability of *kefel* and related payments. A *baraita* cites the verse governing oaths for *shomrim* and applies the hermeneutic rule of *klal u-frat u-klal*.

### Hermeneutic Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל – אִי אַתָּה דָן אֶלָּא כְּעֵין הַפְּרָט; מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר הַמִּטַּלְטֵל וְגוּפוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, this verse contains **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** and one of the thirteen rules of exegesis states that in such a case **you may deduce** that the verse is referring **only** to items **similar to the detail.** Therefore, **just as** each of the items mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as an item that is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value, **so too** double payment is practiced with regard to **any item that is movable property and** has **intrinsic monetary** value.

The rule of *klal u-frat u-klal* holds that when this sandwich structure appears, the general law is limited to cases similar in character to the specific examples listed in the *prat*.

### Movable Property

Applying this rule: the *prat* — ox, donkey, sheep, and garment — consists of items that are movable and have intrinsic monetary value (*davar ha-metaltel ve-gufo mamon*). The *klal* is therefore limited to things similarly movable and intrinsically valuable.

### Four Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָצְאוּ קַרְקָעוֹת – שֶׁאֵינָן מִטַּלְטְלִין; יָצְאוּ עֲבָדִים – שֶׁהוּקְּשׁוּ לְקַרְקָעוֹת; יָצְאוּ שְׁטָרוֹת – שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמִּטַּלְטְלִין, אֵין גּוּפָן מָמוֹן; יָצָא הֶקְדֵּשׁ – "רֵעֵהוּ" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Land is excluded, as it is not movable property.** Canaanite **slaves are excluded, as they are compared to land** in many areas of *halakha*. Financial **documents are excluded, as, although they are movable property, they do not** have **intrinsic monetary** value. The value of the material on which the document is written is negligible; documents are valuable only because they serve as proof for monetary claims. Finally, **consecrated** property **is excluded** because **it is written** in the verse that the one found liable shall pay double to **"his neighbor,"** i.e., to another person, rather than to the Temple treasury.

The *baraita* derives four exclusions from this limitation. Land is excluded because it is not movable (*she-einan metaltelin*). Slaves are excluded because they are conceptually equated with land (*hukshu le-karkaot*), a principle that appears in numerous other *sugyot*. Documents (*shtarot*) are excluded because even though they are physically movable, their value is not intrinsic. Hekdesh — consecrated Temple property — is excluded because the verse says *re'ehu*, "his fellow," which by convention excludes *hekdesh*.

### Documents & Garmi

The exclusion of documents raises an important practical point. Tosafot notes that if one burns someone's *shtar*, liability arises only under *dinei degarmi* — indirect damage — because the document itself has no intrinsic value. The shiur extends this question to modern financial instruments: stocks and checks might similarly lack intrinsic value in the *halachic* sense, and tearing up a check would therefore be much closer to *dinei degarmi* than to direct *nezek*. Even paper money might arguably operate more like a *shtar* than like coins with intrinsic metal value. The shiur notes that Rabbi Bleich has addressed the *halachic* status of checks at length.

### Animal Impurity Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי, מָה הַפְּרָט מְפוֹרָשׁ – דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אַף כֹּל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא, אֲבָל עוֹפוֹת לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** the *halakha* of double payment is limited to cases similar to the details mentioned in the verse, it should also be derived that **just as** each of the items mentioned in **the detail** is clearly **defined as an item whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying, so too any item whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying** it is subject to double payment. **But birds,** whose carcasses do not transmit impurity through contact or carrying, should **not** be subject to double payment.

The *gemara* explores whether the *prat* implies an additional restriction: perhaps the law applies only to animals whose carcasses convey *tumah* by touch and carrying (*maga u-masa*) — that is, the standard *neveilah* impurity applicable to most kosher mammals. Under this reading, only such animals would be included in the scope of the *shomrim* laws.

### Mammals vs. Birds

This leads to the question of birds. Birds have a different *halacha* of *neveilah*: *neveilat ha-of* conveys impurity only *be-veis ha-beliyah* — at the moment of swallowing, through the throat — rather than through touch or carrying. There is considerable discussion about the nature of *tum'at neveilat ha-of* — whether it is a distinct kind of *tumah* or a modified form of regular *neveilah tumah* transmitted through an unusual point of contact. If the *prat* limits us to animals whose carcasses are *metamei be-maga u-masa*, birds would be excluded. However, the *baraita* itself lists a garment (*salmah*) among the *prat*, and garments have no *neveilah* at all — there is no such concept as a *neveilah* garment. This undermines the suggestion that the *prat* should be read as implicitly limited to animals with *maga u-masa* impurity.

### Four-Item Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְהָא "שַׂלְמָה״ כְּתִיב! אָמְרִי: אֲנַן בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים קָאָמְרִינַן; אֵימָא בְּבַעֲלֵי חַיִּים, דָּבָר שֶׁנִּבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – אִין, דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין נִבְלָתוֹ מְטַמְּאָ[ה] בְּמַגָּע וּבְמַשָּׂא – לָא,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to the question: **But how can you say this? But isn't "garment" written** in the list of specified items (Exodus 22:8)? It is impossible to speak of the impurity of carcasses with regard to clothing. The Gemara responds: **Say,** in response to this objection: **We are speaking** specifically **of animals,** and this is the question that was asked: Why not **say** that **with regard to animals,** an animal **whose carcass transmits impurity through contact and carrying, yes,** it is subject to double payment, whereas an animal **whose carcass does not transmit impurity through contact and carrying,** such as a bird, **no,** a thief would not pay double payment for stealing it?

The *gemara* rejects the attempt to limit the scope to the three animals alone (setting aside the garment) by observing that each of the four items in the *prat* can function as its own *klal u-frat* — each generates its own interpretive derivation independent of the others. Therefore the animals cannot be collapsed into a single category for purposes of delineating what the *prat* excludes.

### Item-by-Item Logic

The *gemara* then explains why all four items in the *prat* were necessary. Had the Torah mentioned only an ox, one might have thought the law applies only to animals brought as *korbanos* — since oxen are eligible for the altar. Had it mentioned only a donkey, one might have thought the law applies only to animals sanctified through the laws of *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey, the only non-kosher animal that carries a *bechor*-like sanctity and must be redeemed with a sheep given to a *kohen*. To avoid these mistaken restrictions, both ox and donkey had to be mentioned.

### Sheep Includes Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא חַד פְּרָטָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **If so, let the Merciful One write** just **one detail,** i.e., animal, and that would have been enough to teach that animals are subject to double payment only if they transmit impurity through contact and carrying, so birds are excluded. Since the Torah listed several animals, birds are included.

Yet even mentioning ox and donkey together would not have been sufficient, since one might then limit the law to animals with some special sanctity — either *korban* eligibility or *peter chamor* consecration — thereby excluding ordinary kosher animals with no such status. The inclusion of *seh*, sheep, comes to rule out this restriction. And beyond that, the word *seh* specifically comes to include *ofot* — birds — within the scope of these laws, thereby expanding applicability beyond mammals to birds as well. The shiur notes that the *sugya* continues at length through the remainder of the *daf* and into the following day's material, working through all the implications of these *drashot* in detail.
