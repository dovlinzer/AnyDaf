# Shabbat 140 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Filtering Liquids

### Clear vs. Cloudy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר זְעֵירִי: נוֹתֵן אָדָם יַיִן צָלוּל וּמַיִם צְלוּלִין לְתוֹךְ הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בְּשַׁבָּת וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ, אֲבָל עֲכוּרִין — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ze'iri said: A person may place clear wine and clear water into a strainer on Shabbat, and he need not be concerned** over the prohibition of selecting, as the wine is drinkable even without this filtering. **However,** doing so with **murky** liquids, **no,** one may not strain them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: טוֹרֵד אָדָם חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן יֵינָהּ וּשְׁמָרֶיהָ, וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַמְשַׁמֶּרֶת בַּשַּׁבָּת וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ! תַּרְגְּמַהּ זְעֵירִי: בֵּין הַגִּיתּוֹת שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A person may stir a barrel of wine,** mixing **its wine and its sediment, and place it in a strainer on Shabbat, and he need not be concerned.** Apparently, it is permitted to place even murky wine in a strainer. **Ze'iri explained that** *baraita* and resolved the difficulty: **That was taught** with regard to wine **between the presses,** when the wine has yet to ferment and will remain murky even after filtering.

The daf opens by returning to the topic of filtering liquids on *Shabbos*, picking up at the bottom of 139b. An earlier *mishnah* had addressed the prohibition of using a standard filter on *Shabbos*, while the *mishnah* under discussion permits the use of non-classic filters — such as cloth or a wicker basket. Before turning to those cases, however, the *Gemara* revisits a foundational distinction introduced by Rabbi Zeiri.

The relevant passage reads: *amar Zeiri: nosen adam yayin tzalul umayim tzalulim litoch mishmeres be-Shabbos ve-eino choshesh* — one may place clear wine or clear water into a filter on *Shabbos* without concern. The underlying rationale is that people would drink these liquids as they are, and therefore no genuine act of separation is being performed. *Le-achurin lo* — but for cloudy liquids, this is not permitted, since that is not the manner in which people normally drink.

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who states: *toref adam chaviso shel yayin, yeino ushmarav, venosen litoch mishmeres be-Shabbos ve-eino choshesh* — one may even mix the wine together with its lees and then pour the mixture into a filter on *Shabbos* without concern, even though the liquid is at that point visibly cloudy. Rabbi Zeiri resolves the apparent contradiction: *bein hagitot shanu* — this was taught specifically with reference to the period between pressings, when people were accustomed to drinking the wine in that turbid state.

### Classic Filter Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַנְּנִין אֶת הַיַּיִן בְּסוּדָרִין. אָמַר רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂה גּוּמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One may filter wine through cloths** on Shabbat. **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya said:** This is permitted **provided one does not make an indentation** in the cloth, making it into a receptacle, as one must diverge from the usual, weekday manner of performing this activity.

The practical *halacha* that emerges from this discussion is striking. Not only may one filter clear wine or water on *Shabbos*, but one may use a *misnenes* — a classic, purpose-built filter — to do so. The *Rambam* disagrees, but the *Shulchan Aruch* rules that if a liquid is one that the individual and most people would drink in its current state without hesitation, then filtering it with a classic filter does not constitute *borer*. The rule is not confined literally to liquids that appear visibly pristine. It is abstracted to the broader principle: if most people would drink the liquid as it is, a classic filter is permitted. The requirement to use only a non-classic filter — cloth or a wicker basket — applies when most people would prefer not to drink the liquid in its present state. This carries immediate practical relevance: one who needs to filter water on *Shabbos* due to concerns about insects or other contaminants must do so before *Shabbos*.

### Non-Classic Filters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִכְפִיפָה מִצְרִית. אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַגְבִּיהַּ מִקַּרְקָעִיתוֹ שֶׁל כְּלִי טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna: **And** it is permitted to filter wine on Shabbat **through an Egyptian basket. Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said** the following caveat to that statement: It is permitted **provided one does not lift** the basket **a handbreadth from the bottom of the** lower **vessel.** This ensures that one performs the activity in an atypical manner (Rabbeinu Yona).

The *Gemara* continues: *misannen hayayin bisudarin* — one may filter wine through cloths. This applies in the context of a somewhat cloudy liquid, and several conditions govern how such filtering may be performed.

### Indentations & Vessels

Rav Shimi bar Chiya qualifies the use of cloth: *uvildad shelo yaaseh gumma* — one must not create an indentation in the cloth, since doing so gives it the character of a vessel. Rashi offers an additional concern: making such an indentation invites the wine to pool heavily in the cloth, which may then lead one to wring out the cloth — an act of *sechitah* that is clearly prohibited.

Alternatively, one may use a wicker basket as a filter. Here Rav Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav adds a further qualification: *uvildad shelo yagbia mikarkito shel kli tefach* — the base of the wicker basket must not be raised a *tefach* above the bottom of the vessel into which it is placed, because this would constitute forming a tent — a concern of *ohel*. This is somewhat ironic in light of the *Gemara*'s earlier skepticism about whether placing a cover on a vessel truly constitutes *ohel*. Yet here the *tefach* measurement re-ignites that concern, framing the basket as a kind of roof.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הַאי פְּרוֹנְקָא, אַפַּלְגֵיהּ דְכוּבָּא — שְׁרֵי, אַכּוּלֵּיהּ כּוּבָּא — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: It is permitted** to place **this cover** that has holes for filtering **over half a barrel,** but **it is prohibited** to spread it **over the entire barrel,** in order that it not appear as though one is using it as a filter (Rabbeinu Ḥananel).

Rav then states: *hai perumka apalgei de-kubba shari* — placing a piece of cloth over half the mouth of a barrel is permitted, *akkulei kuba assur* — but covering the entire mouth is forbidden. Again, Rashi attributes this to the concern of *ohel*, which remains puzzling given Abaye's earlier skepticism about that framing. Some authorities prefer to explain these cases as *uvda de-chol* — an activity that has too much the appearance of a weekday affair.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא נִיהַדַּק אִינִישׁ צִינְיָיתָא בְּפוּמֵּיהּ דְּכוּזַנְתָּא (דְּחָבִיתָא), מִשּׁוּם דְּמֶיחְזֵי כִּמְשַׁמֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: A person may not attach a bundle of straw to the mouth of a barrel's spigot, because it appears like a strainer.**

Rav Papa adds: one should not press a strainer tightly into the mouth of the vessel used to draw wine from a barrel — *mishum de-meichzei ki-mashmeret* — because it has the appearance of a filter. This would function somewhat like a slotted spoon in reverse: rather than removing solids from liquid, one is drawing off the liquid while leaving the sediment behind. Such an arrangement is problematic precisely because the liquid is something that would not normally be drunk in its current state.

### Slow Pouring Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַב פָּפָּא שָׁאפוּ שִׁיכְרָא מִמָּנָא לְמָנָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּיפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: הָאִיכָּא נִיצוֹצוֹת! נִיצוֹצוֹת לְבֵי רַב פָּפָּא לָא חֲשִׁיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The members **of Rav Pappa's household would carefully pour beer from one vessel to another** so that the dregs from one vessel would not pass to the other. **Rav Aḥa from Difti said to Ravina: But there are the** final **drops,** which remain when one pours the beer into another vessel. When pouring the final drops from the dregs left in the vessel, one is in violation of the prohibition of selecting. He replied: In Rav Pappa's house they would leave the final drops in the first vessel together with the dregs. They would not attempt to separate them, as **the last drops were not significant to the household of Rav Pappa,** because beer was always readily available in his house.

The house of Rav Papa employed a practical workaround: *shafu shichra mimana lemana* — they would pour beer from one vessel to another, allowing the upper liquid to pour off while the sediment remained behind, without using a sieve. The question then arises regarding *nitzotzot* — the final droplets. Rav Acha mi-Difti raises this with Ravina: when the pouring reaches the stage of slow individual drops, one is no longer simply decanting the liquid above the sediment; one is extracting liquid that is genuinely mixed within the sediment. Is that not *borer*? The response is *nitzotzot le-bei Rav lo chashivi* — the house of Rav was not concerned with getting out every last drop. They would stop before reaching that stage, and only the earlier clean pour — where the liquid sitting above the mixture is decanted off — is what they permitted.

A broader clarification of *borer* is in order here. The prohibition applies when one separates *ochel* from *psolet*, that is, wanted from unwanted material within a mixture. It is permitted when: one removes the desired item rather than the unwanted; one does so immediately before eating; and one does not use a *kli* — an implement designed specifically for the act of separation. A slotted spoon, for instance, is problematic precisely because its function is to separate; using it to scoop out the desired solid while the liquid drains through constitutes using a designated separating implement. Scooping with an ordinary spoon while avoiding the liquid is not in the same category. A pour-through method, such as a tea bag or similar arrangement, is treated differently: one is not starting with a mixture and then separating it; rather, one is pouring liquid through a substance that imparts flavor, and the water passes through continuously. That is not considered *borer*.

### Egg & Mustard Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנִין בֵּיצָה בִּמְסַנֶּנֶת. תָּנֵי יַעֲקֹב קָרְחָה: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ אֶלָּא לְגָוֶון.
>
> **Translation:** **And** we further learned in the mishna: **One may place an egg in** a mustard **strainer** on Shabbat in order to separate the yolk from the egg-white. The Sage, **Ya'akov Korḥa, taught** a reason for this: **Because it is only made to** enhance the **color** of the food. That does not negate the egg-white as significant food in the sense that it would be considered waste, and therefore, no actual selection is performed.

A *baraita* teaches: *venosein beitza bimesenes* — one may pass an egg through a filter of mustard on *Shabbos*. The reason given is *lefi she'ein osim osah ella legavern* — one does this only for the purpose of coloring.

This *halacha* presents genuine difficulty. One reading is straightforward: the filter has been previously used with mustard and retains its yellow color; one pours the egg through so that it picks up the color, just as water poured through grape skins picks up flavor and continues through — no act of *borer* occurs because the egg simply passes through and absorbs color along the way. That reading presents no difficulty.

Rashi, however, reads the case differently: the mustard is below the filter, and one pours the egg through the filter in order to send the yolk down into the mustard to color it. On that reading, the question is sharp: one is using a *kli* to extract the yolk from the white — why is that not *borer*? Rashi's answer is that since one is not discarding the white — it remains food — one cannot say that one "wants" the yolk and "does not want" the white in the sense that defines *borer*. The purpose is coloring, not separation per se. Yet this answer is not entirely satisfying, since the general rule is that *borer* does apply even when separating two types of food from one another if one wants one and not the other in that moment. The *halacha* here remains difficult to fully account for.

## Mustard Preparation

### Mustard Kneading Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר. חַרְדָּל שֶׁלָּשׁוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר, אָמַר רַב: מְמַחוֹ בִּכְלִי אֲבָל לֹא בַּיָּד. אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל: (בַּיָּד?!) אַטּוּ כׇּל יוֹמָא בְּיָד מְמַחוּ לֵיהּ?! מַאֲכַל חֲמוֹרִים הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מְמַחוֹ בַּיָּד וְאֵינוֹ מְמַחוֹ בִּכְלִי.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated:** With regard to **mustard that one kneaded on Shabbat eve, on the following day,** Shabbat, **Rav said: One may dissolve it** in wine or water **with a vessel, but not with** his **hand,** as using a vessel diverges from the normal method of preparation. **Shmuel said to him:** Why may he not dissolve it **with** his **hand? Is that to say that he dissolves it with** his **hand every day?** If it is prepared in that manner, **it is donkey food.** Certainly dissolving by hand is not the manner in which mustard is prepared during the week. Therefore, dissolving mustard with a vessel should not be permitted on Shabbat. **Rather, Shmuel said** the opposite: **He may dissolve it with** his **hand** as a divergence from the typical method of preparation, **but he may not dissolve it** in the usual manner, **with a vessel.**

The *Gemara* turns to the preparation of mustard — crushed mustard seed combined with liquid to form a paste — a process that raises concerns of *lash* (kneading). If one kneaded the mustard before *Shabbos*, what may one do with it the following day?

Rav rules: *memacho bi-chli* — one may dissolve it further by adding liquid, using a vessel. *Aval lo be-yad* — but not by hand. Shmuel finds this inversion perplexing: *atu kol yoma memacho lei be-yad* — do people normally stir mustard with their hands? *Ma'achal chamorim hu* — that is what one would do when preparing it for donkeys. Rather, Shmuel holds the opposite: *memacho be-yad, ve-eino memacho bi-chli* — one may dissolve it by hand, which constitutes a *shinuy*, but not with a vessel.

The nature of the underlying concern is itself not entirely clear. Adding liquid to an already-formed paste does not appear to constitute *lash* in its classic sense, since *lash* involves the initial transformation of powder and liquid into dough. Adding liquid to loosen an existing mixture is, as a general matter of *lash*, not prohibited. The concern here may be a form of *uvda de-chol* — an activity that too closely resembles what one would do on a weekday.

### Three Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — אָסוּר. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — מוּתָּר. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** **It is stated** that *amora'im* from Eretz Yisrael also disputed this issue, as **Rabbi Elazar said: Both this and that,** dissolving by hand and with a vessel, **are prohibited;** while **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Both this and that are permitted. Abaye and Rava both said:** The ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the lenient opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

Three distinct positions are recorded. Rabbi Elazar rules that *echad ze ve-echad ze assur* — both methods, hand and vessel, are forbidden. Rabbi Yochanan rules that *echad ze ve-echad ze mutar* — both are permitted. And Shmuel, as above, permits the hand and forbids the vessel.

### Final Halacha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בְּשִׁיטְתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, קָם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּשִׁיטְתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** Subsequently, **Rabbi Yoḥanan stood** and adopted **the opinion** of **Rabbi Elazar** and prohibited both methods of dissolving. **Rabbi Elazar stood** and adopted **the opinion** of **Shmuel,** who prohibited using a vessel but permitted it by hand. Given these stances, **Abaye and Rava both said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who prohibited both methods.

The resolution involves reversals by two of the principals. Abaye and Rava both initially stated that the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Yochanan's lenient position permitting everything. Subsequently, Rabbi Yochanan himself reversed his position and ruled like Rabbi Elazar — everything is forbidden. Then Rabbi Elazar reversed his position, moving not to Rabbi Yochanan's new stringent position but rather to Shmuel's intermediate position. At this later stage, Abaye and Rava both then stated that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan — now that Rabbi Yochanan holds everything is forbidden, we rule accordingly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִימֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ, וְלָא אֲכַל. דְּבֵיתְהוּ דִּזְעִירָא עֲבַדָא לֵיהּ לְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי, וְלָא אֲכַל. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לְרַבָּךְ עֲבַדִי לֵיהּ וַאֲכַל, וְאַתְּ לָא אָכְלַתְּ? אָמַר רָבָא בַּר שְׁבָא: הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא וּבְחַשִׁי לֵיהּ בְּשׁוּפְתָּא דְתוּמָא וַאֲכַל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Abaye's mother,** actually his foster mother, **prepared** mustard **for him, and he did not eat** it. **Ze'eira's wife prepared** mustard **for Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi,** her husband's student, **and he did not eat** it. **She said to him: I made** this **for your rabbi and he ate** it, **and you do not eat** it? **Rava bar Shabba said: I was standing before Ravina, and they stirred** mustard **for him with the inner part of the garlic, and he ate** it.

Several practical illustrations follow. Abaye's mother — his nursemaid, the same figure known from many medicinal traditions — prepared mustard for him on *Shabbos*, and he refused to eat it, consistent with his ruling. Similarly, the wife of Zeiri prepared it for Rav Chiya bar Ashi on *Shabbos* and he refused, whereupon she challenged him: your master's master, my husband Zeiri, eats it — are you more stringent than he? Rava bar Shaba reports that in Ravina's presence, mustard was stirred using an inner portion of a garlic clove, which constitutes a *shinuy* — an altered manner of performing the action.

### Rav Zutra Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּכׇל הָנֵי שְׁמַעְתָּתָא, אֶלָּא כִּי הָא דְּאִתְּמַר: חַרְדָּל שֶׁלָּשׁוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר מְמַחוֹ בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בִּכְלִי וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹכוֹ דְּבַשׁ. וְלֹא יִטְרוֹף, אֶלָּא מְעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra said: The *halakha* is not in accordance with any of these statements. Rather, it is in accordance with this** one, **as it was stated: Mustard that one kneaded on Shabbat eve, on the following day he may dissolve it either by hand or with a vessel, and he may place honey in it. And** when he dissolves it **he may not beat** it forcefully as would a craftsman, **but** he may **mix** it gently.

Rav Zutra cuts through the complexity: *leit hilchata ke-chol hani shema'teta* — we do not rule according to all that preceding discussion. Rather, the operative ruling is as follows: *chardal she-lasho me-erev Shabbat, le-machar memacho bein be-yad bein bi-chli* — if mustard was kneaded the eve of *Shabbos*, the next day one may dissolve it by adding liquid whether by hand or by vessel. *Ve-noten le-tocho devash* — one may also add honey. *Ve-lo yitrof ela me'arev* — but one should not beat it together vigorously; rather, one may stir it gently. The beating is considered a form of *lisha*, though with a *shinuy* even that may sometimes be permitted.

The broader principles of *lash* that emerge from this passage are significant. One may add liquids to a substance if doing so does not transform it into a paste. One may not add liquids if they would create a paste — that constitutes *lash*. If the substance is already a paste, one may add liquid to loosen it further. One may stir gently but not beat vigorously. These principles have practical application to many common food preparations on *Shabbos*.

### Cress & Garlic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁחֲלַיִים שֶׁשְּׁחָקָן מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר נוֹתֵן לְתוֹכָן שֶׁמֶן וָחוֹמֶץ, וּמַמְשִׁיךְ לְתוֹכָן אַמִּיתָא. וְלֹא יִטְרוֹף אֶלָּא מְעָרֵב. שׁוּם שֶׁרִיסְּקוֹ מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת, לְמָחָר נוֹתֵן לְתוֹכוֹ פּוֹל וּגְרִיסִין, וְלֹא יִשְׁחוֹק אֶלָּא מְעָרֵב, וּמַמְשִׁיךְ אֶת אַמִּיתָא לְתוֹכָן. מַאי אַמִּיתָא? נִינְיָיא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ הַאי נִינְיָיא מְעַלְּיָא לְתַחְלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Cress that one ground on Shabbat eve, on the following day he may place oil and vinegar in it, and add *amita* into** the mixture, **and he may not beat** it, **but** he may **mix** it. Similarly, **garlic that one crushed on Shabbat eve, on the following day, he may place beans and grits in it, and he may not pound, but mix, and he may add *amita* into** the mixture. The Gemara asks: **What is *amita*?** The Gemara answers: It is **mint. Abaye said: Learn from this** that adding **mint is beneficial for cress.**

The *Gemara* extends these principles to analogous cases. If one ground *shachlayim* (cress) before *Shabbos*, the next day one may add oil and vinegar, and also draw into it *amita* (mint) — but one may not beat the mixture, only stir it, and only because this was done from the night before. Similarly, if one crushed garlic from *erev Shabbos*, the next day one may add beans and coarse groats, but should not grind them together — only incorporate them with gentle mixing. And one may add *amita* into the garlic as well.

The *Gemara* notes from this context that mint is beneficial for cress. More substantively, the discussion of adding oil and vinegar to the ground cress — while water to flour remains problematic — prompted a well-known practical question posed by Rav Eider to Rav Moshe Feinstein: may one add mayonnaise to tuna fish on *Shabbos*? The argument for permissibility, advanced by Rav Eider, was that unlike the addition of water to flour — which chemically transforms the flour into a fundamentally different substance, namely dough — mayonnaise added to tuna does not transform the tuna chunks; it merely binds them together as a paste without creating a homogeneous new substance. Rav Moshe was not persuaded by this reasoning and held that making tuna salad on *Shabbos* raises a concern of *lash*. Many other *poskim* permit it entirely. The *Gemara*'s discussion here of which liquids may and may not be added to which already-ground substances forms the basis for all of these later discussions.

## Wine & Medicinal Prep

### Wine Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹשִׂין אֵנוֹמֵלִין בְּשַׁבָּת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: עוֹשִׂין אֵנוֹמֵלִין בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין אֲלוּנְטִית. וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא אֵנוֹמֵלִין וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא אֲלוּנְטִית? אֵנוֹמֵלִין יַיִן וּדְבַשׁ וּפִלְפְּלִין. אֲלוּנְטִית יַיִן יָשָׁן וּמַיִם צְלוּלִין וַאֲפַרְסְמוֹן, דְּעָבְדִי לְבֵי מַסּוּתָא לְמֵיקַר.
>
> **Translation:** We also learned in the mishna: **One may prepare *anumlin* on Shabbat. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may prepare *anumlin* on Shabbat. However, one may not prepare *aluntit*.** The *baraita* explains: **And what is *anumlin* and what is *aluntit*? *Anumlin*** is a drink, which is a mixture of **wine, honey, and pepper. *Aluntit*** is a mixture of **aged wine and clear water and balsam, which they prepare** for use after bathing in **a bathhouse to cool down** from the heat of the bathhouse. It is prohibited to prepare it on Shabbat because it is a form of remedy.

The *Gemara* turns to *anomlin*, a type of spiced wine: *osin anomlin b'Shabbos* — one may prepare it on *Shabbos*. A *baraita* clarifies the contrast: *osin anomlin b'Shabbos v'ein osin aluntis* — one may make *anomlin* but not *aluntis*. *Anomlin* is wine with honey and peppers — a straightforward food preparation, and therefore permitted. *Aluntis* is old wine with clear water and *afarsemon* (balsam), prepared *d'avdi l'bei masusa* — for people emerging from a bathhouse, to cool them down. The problem with *aluntis* is not the bathhouse per se but the nature of the preparation: it is made as a medicinal or restorative drink, and preparing medicinal items on *Shabbos* is a rabbinic prohibition.

### Fennel Medicinal Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין שׁוֹרִין אֶת הַחִילְתִּית בְּפוֹשְׁרִין, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַחוֹמֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not soak asafoetida in lukewarm water** to prepare a medicinal drink from it; **however, one may place** it **into vinegar** like a standard spice.

The *mishnah* states: *ein shorin et hachiltis b'poshrin* — one may not submerge *chiltis* (asafoetida, a potent spice with onion-like and garlic-like qualities) in lukewarm water on *Shabbos*. When dissolved in lukewarm water, it is being prepared for medicinal use, and that is the concern. *Aval nosen l'toch hachometz* — but one may add it to vinegar, where it functions as a culinary spice rather than as medicine.

### Vetch & Chaff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין שׁוֹלִין אֶת הַכַּרְשִׁינִין וְלֹא שָׁפִין אוֹתָן, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַכְּבָרָה אוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַכַּלְכָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not soak vetches** in water in order to separate them from their chaff, **nor rub them** by hand so as to remove their chaff. **However, one may place** them **into a sieve or into a basket,** and if the chaff gets removed, so be it.

The *mishnah* continues: *v'ein tulin et hakarshinim* — one may not submerge certain legumes in water in order to separate the chaff or other impurities from them. This is not a medicinal concern but a concern of *borer* — using water as a medium of separation. *V'lo shafin otan* — nor may one rub them by hand to remove the hull. However, *aval notnin l'toch hakivra o l'toch hakalkala* — one may place them in a sieve or basket to carry them; if some material incidentally falls through, that is not one's concern, and the intent is not to sift.

### Straw Sifting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין כּוֹבְרִין אֶת הַתֶּבֶן בִּכְבָרָה, וְלֹא יִתְּנֶנּוּ עַל גַּבֵּי מָקוֹם גָּבוֹהַּ בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיֵּרֵד הַמּוֹץ. אֲבָל נוֹטֵל הוּא בַּכְּבָרָה וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הָאֵיבוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not sift straw in a sieve, and** similarly, **one may not place it on a high place so that that the chaff** blows away in the wind; **however, one may take** the straw **in a sieve and place it into the trough** of an animal, and one need not be concerned if the chaff is removed in the process.

Similarly, *ein kovrin et hateven bakivra* — one may not place straw in a sieve to remove the chaff from it. *V'lo yitnenu al gabi makom gavoah bishvil sheyeired hamotz* — nor may one place it on an elevated surface so that the chaff will settle downward. However, one may use such a sieve-like basket to carry the straw to an animal's trough — *l'toch ha'evus* — using it as a carrying vessel rather than as a sifting implement, even though some material may incidentally fall through the openings. The question of *marit ayin* is raised: does carrying the straw in a sieve-like basket create the appearance of sifting? The *Gemara* responds that onlookers see one carrying it toward the trough, so the carrying function is apparent.

### Borer Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: שָׁרָה, מַאי? תַּרְגְּמָא רַב אַדָּא נַרְשָׁאָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף: שָׁרָה — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **If one** unwittingly **soaked** asafoetida on Shabbat, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rav Adda from** the city of **Naresh interpreted it before Rav Yosef: If one soaked** it, **he is liable** to bring **a sin-offering.**

The *Gemara* asks what the severity of these prohibitions is — are they biblical or rabbinic? The initial answer — *shara chayav chatat* — is that submerging the fennel in warm water renders one liable for a *korban chatat*, indicating a biblical violation. The claim of biblical liability is immediately challenged.

### Fennel Cold Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה שְׁרָה אוּמְצָא בְּמַיָּא הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיחַיַּיב? אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מִדְּרַבָּנַן — שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲשֶׂה כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה בַּחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But if that is so,** if **one soaked a piece of meat in water,** would you **also** say **that he is liable?** No cooking was performed at all. **Rather, Abaye said:** It is prohibited **by rabbinic decree,** so **that one will not conduct** himself on Shabbat **in the manner in which he conducts** himself **during the week.**

Abaye challenges the *chatat* position sharply: if any food preparation involving water is to be deemed a biblical violation, then even soaking raw meat in water would theoretically be biblically prohibited — an absurd conclusion. *Ela amar Abaye m'drabbanan* — rather, the prohibition is entirely rabbinic in nature, based on the principle of *uvda de-chol*: it resembles too closely what one does on a weekday.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵרַבִּי יַנַּאי: מַהוּ לִשְׁרוֹת אֶת הַחִלְתִּית בְּצוֹנֵן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָסוּר. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֵין שׁוֹרִין אֶת הַחִלְתִּית בְּפוֹשְׁרִין — הָא בְּצוֹנֵן מוּתָּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yannai: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to soaking asafoetida in cold water** on Shabbat? Rabbi Yannai **said to him: It is prohibited.** Rabbi Yoḥanan asked: **Didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One may not soak asafoetida in lukewarm** water, indicating that **it is permitted** to soak it **in cold** water?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן מַה בֵּין לִי וְלָךְ. מַתְנִיתִין יְחִידָאָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין שׁוֹרִין אֶת הַחִלְתִּית לֹא בְּחַמִּין וְלֹא בְּצוֹנֵן. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּחַמִּין — אָסוּר, בְּצוֹנֵן — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yannai **said to him: If so, that** is the difference **between my** knowledge **and yours,** as I am able to analyze the *halakha* more profoundly. In this case, the mishna is not a reliable source, as **the mishna** expresses **an individual** opinion. **As it was taught** in the *Tosefta*: **One may neither soak asafoetida in hot** water **nor in cold** water. **Rabbi Yosei says: In hot** water it is **prohibited; in cold** water it is **permitted.** The mishna that does not prohibit cold water is in accordance with the individual opinion of Rabbi Yosei, but the *halakha* is not ruled based on that opinion.

A question is then raised: may one submerge the *chiltis* in cold water? Rabbi Yannai rules: *assur* — it is forbidden. But the *mishnah* mentions only lukewarm water as the locus of the prohibition — does that not imply that cold water is permitted? Rabbi Yannai responds: if that is what the *mishnah* already implies, what insight does asking me add? He then explains that he regards the *mishnah* as a minority opinion — *da'at yachid*. The *baraita* records a broader prohibition: *ein shorin et hachiltit lo b'chamin v'lo b'tzonen* — neither hot nor cold water is permitted. Rabbi Yossi dissents: hot water is forbidden, cold water is permitted. The *mishnah* reflects Rabbi Yossi's position, but Rabbi Yannai holds that we rule with the majority view — cold water is also forbidden.

### Medicine Continuation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? לְיוּקְרָא דְלִיבָּא. רַב אַחָא בַּר יוֹסֵף חָשׁ בְּיוּקְרָא דְלִיבָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָר עוּקְבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁתִי תְּלָתָא תִּיקְלֵי חִילְתִּיתָא בִּתְלָתָא יוֹמֵי. אֲזַל אִישְׁתִּי חַמְשָׁא בְּשַׁבְּתָא וּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא. לְצַפְרָא אֲזַל שְׁאַל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **For what** purpose **is** soaked asafoetida **prepared?** The Gemara answers: As a cure **for heaviness of the heart.** One who feels a pain in his heart drinks asafoetida. The Gemara relates: **Rav Aḥa bar Yosef felt heaviness in** his **heart. He came before Mar Ukva** to ask his advice. Mar Ukva **said to him: Go drink** the weight of **three shekels of asafoetida in three days. He went and drank on Thursday and Shabbat eve. In the morning, he went** and **asked in the study hall** if he could drink it on Shabbat.

The *Gemara* clarifies that the medicinal application of *chiltis* is for *yukra d'liba* — a heaviness of the heart, something like indigestion or heartburn. A case is then recounted: Rav Acha bar Yosef suffered from this condition and came before Mar Ukva, who prescribed that he drink three measures of *chiltis* over three days. He drank it Thursday and Friday. On *Shabbos* morning he came to the *beis midrash* to ask whether he could continue. The initial response — that one may drink as much as one wishes, the *mishnah*'s concern being only the preparation — did not address his question, for he had not asked about drinking but about preparing it on *Shabbos*.

### Cold Water Workaround

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ: לִשְׁתּוֹת — לָא קָמִיבַּעְיָא לִי, כִּי קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי — לִשְׁרוֹת, מַאי? אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: בְּדִידִי הֲוָה עוֹבָדָא, וַאֲתַאי שְׁאֵילְתֵּיהּ לְרַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה וְלָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. אֲתַאי שְׁאֵילְתֵּיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא, וַאֲמַר: הָכִי קָאָמַר רַב, שׁוֹרֶה בְּצוֹנֵן וּמַנִּיחַ בַּחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Yosef **said to them:** With regard **to drinking, I have no dilemma. When I raised a dilemma,** it is with regard **to soaking** asafoetida; **what is** the *halakha*? **Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said to them: There was an incident** that happened **with me, and I came** and **asked** that question **to Rav Adda bar Ahava, and he did not have** an answer for it. **I came** and **asked Rav Huna, and he said** that **this is what Rav is saying: One may soak** asafoetida **in cold water and place** it **in the sun** to warm it so it will be fit to drink.

Rav Chiyya bar Avin reports that he had faced the same question and asked Rav Adda bar Ahava, who did not know the answer. He then asked Rav Huna, who transmitted in the name of Rav: *shoreh b'tzonen umeniach b'chamah* — one may place it in cold water and leave it in the sun to warm. This follows the position of Rabbi Yossi that cold water is permitted, and the sun does the warming indirectly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי? אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָסַר — הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אִישְׁתִּי כְּלָל, אֲבָל הָכָא — כֵּיוָן דְּאִישְׁתִּי חַמְשָׁא וּמַעֲלֵי שַׁבְּתָא, אִי לָא שָׁתֵי בְּשַׁבָּת — מִיסְתַּכַּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Was Rav's ruling **according to** the opinion of **the one who permitted** soaking asafoetida in cold water? The Gemara answers: His ruling could be **even according to** the opinion of **the one who prohibited** doing so; **this** prohibition **applies only when he had not drunk** asafoetida **at all; however, here, since he drank** it **on Thursday and on Shabbat eve, if he does not drink on Shabbat he** would thereby be **endangered.** Therefore, he is permitted even to soak the asafoetida.

The *Gemara* then extends this ruling: even according to the stricter view that prohibits both cold and hot water, in this particular case — since Rav Acha bar Yosef had already begun his course of treatment Thursday and Friday, and failing to take the medicine on the third day would place him in a state of danger — it is permitted regardless. The principle articulated is clear and practically significant: when a person has already begun a course of medical treatment and interrupting it poses a genuine risk, the treatment may be continued on *Shabbos*. This principle — that one does not discontinue a prescribed course of medicine on *Shabbos* — is already rooted here in the *Gemara*.

## Practical Applications

### Linen Rubbing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיסְתְּמִיךְ וְאָזֵיל רַב אַחָא בַּר יוֹסֵף אַכַּתְפֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק בַּר אֲחָתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי מָטֵינַן לְבֵי רַב סָפְרָא עַיְּילִינַּן. כִּי מְטוֹ עַיְּילֵיהּ. בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ: מַהוּ לְכַסְכּוֹסֵי כִּיתָּנִיתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא? לְרַכּוֹכֵי כִּיתָּנִיתָא קָא מִיכַּוֵּין — וְשַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, אוֹ דִילְמָא לְאוֹלוֹדֵי חִיוָּרָא קָמִיכַּוֵּין — וַאֲסִיר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְרַכּוֹכֵי קָא מִיכַּוֵּין וְשַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident involving Rav Aḥa bar Yosef: **Rav Aḥa bar Yosef was walking** and **leaning on the shoulder of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, his sister's son.** Rav Aḥa **said to him: When we arrive at Rav Safra's house, bring me in. When they arrived,** Rav Naḥman **brought him in.** Rav Aḥa then **raised a dilemma before** Rav Safra: **What is** the *halakha* pertaining **to rubbing** and thereby softening **a linen shirt** that is hard after being laundered **on Shabbat?** Does **one intend to soften the shirt, and one may well** do so? **Or perhaps he intends to generate whiteness** in the shirt, **and it is prohibited?** Rav Safra **said to him: He intends to soften, and one may well** do so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי נְפַק אֲתָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי בְּעָא מָר מִינֵּיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ בְּעַי מִינֵּיהּ: מַהוּ לְכַסְכּוֹסֵי כִּיתָּנִיתָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא, וַאֲמַר לִי: שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rav Aḥa **emerged** and **came** from Rav Safra's house, Rav Naḥman **said to him: What dilemma did the Master raise before** Rav Safra? Rav Aḥa **said to him: I raised** the following **dilemma before him: What is** the *halakha* pertaining **to rubbing** and thereby softening **a linen shirt on Shabbat, and he said to me: One may well** do so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְמָר סוּדָרָא? סוּדָרָא לָא קָא מִיבַּעְיָא לִי, דִּבְעַי מֵרַב הוּנָא וּפְשַׁט לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak asked: **And let the Master raise** the same **dilemma** with regard to rubbing a **scarf.** He replied: **I did not raise the dilemma** with regard to a **scarf, as I raised it before Rav Huna. And he resolved it for me** and said that it is permitted (Rif).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפְּשִׁיט לֵיהּ לְמָר מִסּוּדָרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם — מִיחְזֵי כִּי אוֹלוֹדֵי חִיוָּרָא, הָכָא — לָא מִיחְזֵי כְּאוֹלוֹדֵי חִיוָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman asked him: **And let the Master resolve** the dilemma with regard to a shirt **from** Rav Huna's ruling with regard to **a scarf.** Rav Aḥa **said to him:** There is a difference between the cases: **There,** in the case of a shirt, **it appears that one is generating whiteness,** whereas **here,** in the case of a scarf, **it does not appear that one is generating whiteness,** as people are not so particular with the whiteness of a scarf, and one's intention is undoubtedly to soften it.

The *Gemara* records that while being carried on the shoulders of his nephew Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak to visit Rav Safra, Rav Acha bar Yosef asked: *mahu lesachsochei kitna beShabta* — may one rub a linen garment against itself on *Shabbos* in order to soften it? Rav Safra's response distinguishes between two possible intentions. If one's intent is *lesichochei* — merely to soften the stiff fabric — *shappir dami*, it is entirely permitted. If, however, one's intent is *le-aludei chivra* — to whiten the garment — it is forbidden, constituting a form of *libbun*, laundering or whitening.

Rav Safra confirmed that in this instance the intent was to soften, and so it is permitted. Afterward, Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak asked his uncle why he had not also inquired about a *sudra* — a cloth or scarf. Rav Acha replied that he had already asked Rav Huna about a *sudra* and received a ruling — *upashat li le'issura* — that it is forbidden. The question then becomes: why is the garment more lenient than the scarf? The answer given is that rubbing a scarf is more visibly associated with whitening — *mechazi ke'aludei chivra* — whereas rubbing a garment is more obviously understood as an attempt to soften rather than to whiten.

### Rack Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי כִּיתָּנִיתָא מִשְׁלְפָא לְדִידַהּ מִקַּנְיָא — שְׁרֵי, קַנְיָא מִינַּהּ — אֲסִיר. אָמַר רָבָא: וְאִם כְּלִי קִיוָּאֵי הוּא — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** With regard to **this linen shirt,** to remove it from the reed upon which it is hanging **is permitted;** however, to remove **the reed from it is prohibited.** Since the reed is not a vessel, it is set-aside. **Rava said: And if it is** hung on **a weaver's vessel, it is permitted** to remove the vessel as well. Although its primary function is for a prohibited labor, since it is a vessel, it may be moved.

Rav Chisda rules regarding a garment hung on a rack to dry: *meshalfu ledida mikanya shari* — removing the garment from the rack is permitted, *kanya mimena assur* — but removing the rack from underneath the garment is forbidden. The rack, if it is merely a stick or improvised implement rather than a designated vessel, may be *muktzah*, and one may not displace a *muktzah* object. Rava qualifies: if the rack is a proper *kli* — a vessel genuinely made and designated for that purpose, as weavers use — then it is not *muktzah* and may be handled in either direction.

### Bundle Muktzah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי כִּישְׁתָּא דְיַרְקָא, אִי חַזְיָא לְמַאֲכַל בְּהֵמָה — שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלַהּ, וְאִי לָא — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: This bundle of vegetables, if it is suitable for animal food, it is permitted to move it** on Shabbat, **but if not, it is prohibited** to move it.

Rav Chisda rules: *hai kishta di'yarka* — if one has a bundle of herbs, *ee chazya l'meichal behema shari letitulei* — if it is fit for animal consumption it is not *muktzah* and may be handled. If it is not fit even for animals, it is *muktzah* and may not be moved.

### Meat vs. Fish Hooks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: הַאי תַּלְיָא, דְבִשְׂרָא — שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵהּ, דְּכַוְורֵי — אֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said** as follows: In the case of **this hook,** if it is used for hanging **meat, it is permitted to move it,** as it is also suitable for other uses. However, if it is a hook for hanging **fish, it is prohibited** to move it, because it smells bad (Rabbeinu Ḥananel) and is used exclusively for fish.

Rav Chiya bar Ashi in the name of Rav states: *hai taya de'bisra shari letitulei* — a hook used for hanging meat may be handled on *Shabbos*. *De'kivrei assur* — a hook used for fish is *muktzah*. Rashi suggests that the distinction rests on the fact that raw meat might conceivably be eaten, while raw fish would not be, making the fish hook without current purpose. An alternative reading suggests that the smell of the fish hook renders it repulsive, *mius*, and therefore more readily treated as *muktzah*.

## Rav Chisda's Wisdom

### Vegetables & Appetite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּזָבֵין יַרְקָא — לִיזְבֵּין אֲרִיכָא, כִּישָּׁא כִּי כִישָּׁא, וְאוּרְכָּא מִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the bundle of vegetables, the Gemara cites additional advice on similar issues that **Rav Ḥisda said** to poor scholars experiencing difficulty earning a livelihood: **A student of a Torah academy who buys vegetables should buy long ones. A bundle is a bundle,** and they have a standard thickness at a standard price. **However, the** addition of **length** comes **on its own** for free.

Rav Chisda teaches: *bar bei Rav dela nafisha lei rifta lo leichul yarka* — a Torah student who does not have enough bread should not eat vegetables, because *mishum degarir* — vegetables stimulate the appetite and will only leave him hungrier without the means to be satisfied.

### Rav Chisda on Vegetables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲנָא לָא בַּעֲנִיּוּתִי אֲכַלִי יַרְקָא, וְלָא בְּעַתִּירוּתִי אֲכַלִי יַרְקָא. בַּעֲנִיּוּתִי — מִשּׁוּם דְּגָרֵיר. בְּעַתִּירוּתִי — דְּאָמֵינָא: הֵיכָא דְּעָיֵיל יַרְקָא, לֵיעוּל בִּשְׂרָא וְכַוְורֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: I neither ate a vegetable in my** state of **poverty, nor did I eat a vegetable in my** state of **wealth. In my poverty,** I did not eat a vegetable **because it whets** the appetite. **In my wealth,** I did not eat a vegetable **because I said: Where a vegetable enters, let meat and fish enter** instead.

Rav Chisda applies this principle to his own life: *ana lo be'anyusi achli yarka velo be'atirusi achli yarka* — I did not eat vegetables when I was poor, and I do not eat vegetables now that I am wealthy. When poor, because they would increase the appetite without the means to satisfy it. When wealthy, because he reasoned: wherever vegetables would go, meat and fish should go instead — *heika d'azil yarka lei'ol bisra ve'chivrei*. Rather than filling oneself with vegetables, one should use the space for more substantial fare.

### Torah Student Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּלָא נְפִישָׁא לֵיהּ רִיפְתָּא — לָא לִיבְצַע בַּצּוֹעֵי. (וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּלָא נְפִישָׁא לֵיהּ רִיפְתָּא — לָא לִיבְצַע. מַאי טַעְמָא? — דְּלָא עָבֵיד בְּעַיִן יָפָה.) וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אֲנָא מֵעִיקָּרָא לָא הֲוַאי בָּצַעְנָא עַד דִּשְׁדַאי יָדִי בְּכוּלֵּי מָנָא, וְאַשְׁכַּחִי [בֵּיהּ כׇּל צֻרְכִּי].
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy who does not have much bread should not cut it into thin slices;** rather, he should eat what he has in one helping. **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy who does not have much bread should not break** it for guests. **What is the reason? As he will not do so in a generous manner. And Rav Ḥisda said: Originally, I would not break** bread **until I placed my hand in the entire dish** to assure **that I found** that there was enough bread **to meet my needs.**

Rav Chisda further teaches regarding a student who lacks sufficient bread: *lo livtza bitzuei* — he should not break off small pieces one at a time. Rashi understands this as practical advice: do not eat little by little, taking small crumbs, because one will never become satisfied that way. Additionally, such a student should not be the one to break bread for others at the table — *de-lo avi be-ayin yafa* — because, aware of his own scarcity, he will inevitably do so with a stingy eye.

Rav Chisda reflects on his own practice: he would not break bread for others until he had placed his own hand into the bread basket and confirmed that there was enough for himself — *ve-ashkachatei kol tzorchi*. Only then would he break generously for those at his table.

### Bal Tashchit Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הַאי מַאן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ לְמֵיכַל נַהֲמָא דִשְׂעָרֵי וְאָכַל דְּחִיטֵּי קָעָבַר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּשְׁחִית״. וְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הַאי מַאן דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְמִישְׁתֵּי שִׁיכְרָא וְשָׁתֵי חַמְרָא — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּשְׁחִית״. וְלָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא — ״בַּל תַּשְׁחִית״ דְּגוּפָא עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda** also **said: One who is able to eat barley bread and** nevertheless **eats wheat** bread **violates the prohibition against** wanton **destruction.** One who wastes resources is comparable to one who destroys items of value. **And Rav Pappa said: One who is able to drink beer and** nevertheless **drinks wine violates the prohibition against** wanton **destruction.** The Gemara comments: **And this is not** a correct **matter,** as **the prohibition against destruction of one's body takes precedence.** It is preferable for one to care for his body by eating higher quality food than to conserve his money.

Rav Chisda teaches: *hai man de-efshar lei lemeichal nahama de-sa'arei ve-achal de-chittei* — one who can get by on barley bread but chooses to eat wheat bread transgresses *bal tashchit*, wasting resources on unnecessary refinement. This sentiment, sensitive to the realities of poverty, reflects the perspective of someone who had known genuine scarcity.

Rav Pappa makes a parallel statement: one who can manage on beer but drinks wine instead transgresses *bal tashchit*. The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this: *ve-lav milta hi* — that is not actually a concern. *Bal tashchit de-gufa adif* — the more important concern is care of one's body. When one spends somewhat more in order to nourish oneself properly or to experience a measure of physical pleasure and wellbeing, that is not wanton waste — it is appropriate self-care, and *bal tashchit* does not apply. The boundary of *bal tashchit* is wanton destruction or needless extravagance, not every instance of spending more than the minimum.

### Smart Purchasing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּזָבֵין קַנְיָא — לִיזְבֵּין אֲרִיכָא, טוּנָא כִּי טוּנָא, וְאוּרְכָּא מִמֵּילָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda** also **said: A student of a Torah academy who buys reeds should buy long ones,** since **a bundle is a bundle.** Bundles of reeds have a standard thickness, **but the length** comes **on its own** for free.

A series of practical purchasing recommendations follows. When buying vegetables sold by the bundle, purchase the longer ones — *kisha kekisha*, a bundle is a bundle, all at the same price, but the longer one yields more. The same applies to buying firewood: purchase the longest pieces, since price is by the bundle. These were directed at Torah students managing on minimal resources, for whom even marginal economies mattered.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּזָבֵין אוּמְצָא — לִיזְבַּן אוּנְקָא, דְּאִית בֵּיהּ תְּלָתָא מִינֵי בִישְׂרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy who buys meat should buy from the neck [*unka*], as there are three types of meat** there.

Regarding meat: if one purchases meat from the neck area of the animal, *de-it beih tlata minei bisra* — it contains three different types of meat, fatty and lean together, and thus offers the greatest value for the purchase.

### Hygiene & Guests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ מִשְׁחָא — נִימְשֵׁי בְּמַיָּא דַחֲרִיצִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy who has no oil should wash,** i.e., smear himself, **with ditch water,** as the scum that accumulates in it is as useful as oil.

If a Torah student lacks oil for personal grooming, Rav Chisda advises: *nimesh be-maya de-charbitzi* — he should bathe with water from the ditches, which may have some lubricating properties that serve a similar function.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב לָא לִישַׁדַּר מָאנֵיהּ לְאוּשְׁפִּיזֵיהּ לְחַוּוֹרֵיהּ לֵיהּ, דְּלָאו אוֹרַח אַרְעָא, דִּילְמָא חָזֵי בֵּיהּ מִידֵּי וְאָתֵי לְמִגַּנְּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy should not give his clothes to his host to wash for him, as** that is **not proper behavior, for** the host **might see something on it,** such as signs of a seminal emission, **and he will be demeaned** in the eyes of his host.

A student should not give his garments to his host at an inn to launder: *d'lav orech ara* — it is not proper conduct, and *dilma chazi bei midi v'asi l'meganyei* — perhaps the host will observe signs of a seminal emission and the student will be embarrassed. This reflects a broader ethic of maintaining personal dignity in social settings.

### Clothing Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּזָבֵין כִּיתּוֹנִיתָא — לִיזְבַּן מִדִּנְהַר אַבָּא, וְנִיחַוְּורַהּ כֹּל תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין, דִּמְפַטְּיָא לֵיהּ תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, וַאֲנָא עָרְבָא. מַאי ״כִּיתּוֹנִיתָא״? — כִּיתָּא נָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy who buys a linen shirt [*kitonita*] should buy** it **from** those who work by **the river Abba, and should wash it every thirty days, so that it will last him for the twelve months of the year. And I guarantee** that the shirt will remain in good shape. The Gemara comments: **What is** the meaning of ***kitonita*? A fine class [*kita*],** as fine clothing provide one entry into a well-dressed class of people.

Rav Chisda advises the Torah student to purchase linen garments from *Nahar Abba* and to launder them every thirty days. If this regimen is followed, the garment will last all twelve months of the year — *de-mapatzeia lei tresar yarchi shata*. Rav Chisda adds: *va-ana areva* — I will personally guarantee it. The underlying principle is to buy quality and care for it consistently. The word *kitunisa* is interpreted as *kita na'eh* — a beautiful garment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בַּר בֵּי רַב לָא לִיתִּיב אַצִּיפְּתָא חַדְתָּא, דִּמְכַלְּיָא מָאנֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Ḥisda said: A student of a Torah academy should not sit on a new mat, as** its dampness **ruins his garments.**

A further caution: a Torah student should not sit on a new reed mat, as it will damage his clothing.

## Modesty & Marriage

### Physical Modesty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב חִסְדָּא לִבְנָתֵיהּ: תִּיהְוְיָין צְנִיעָן בְּאַפֵּי גַּבְרַיְיכוּ, לָא תֵּיכְלוּן נַהֲמָא בְּאַפֵּי גַבְרַיְיכוּ. לָא תֵּיכְלוּן יַרְקָא בְּלֵילְיָא, לָא תֵּיכְלוּן תַּמְרֵי בְּלֵילְיָא, וְלָא תִּשְׁתּוֹן שִׁיכְרָא בְּלֵילְיָא, וְלָא תִּיפְּנוֹן הֵיכָא דְּמִפְּנוּ גַּבְרַיְיכוּ.
>
> **Translation:** After citing Rav Ḥisda's recommendations to students, the Gemara cites his advice to his daughters. **Rav Ḥisda said to his daughters: Be modest before your husbands; do not eat bread before your husbands,** lest you eat too much and be demeaned in their eyes. Similarly, he advised: **Do not eat vegetables at night,** as vegetables cause bad breath. **Do not eat dates at night and do not drink beer at night,** as these loosen the bowels. **And do not relieve** yourself **in the place where your husbands relieve** themselves, so that they will not be revolted by you.

Rav Chisda turns to address his daughters directly, offering counsel on conduct in marriage. He instructs them to be *tznu'os* — modest — in the presence of their husbands. Specifically: do not eat bread in front of them; do not eat vegetables at night, apparently because of concerns of bad breath; do not eat dates at night; and do not drink beer at night — Rashi explains that these last items function as diuretics and may cause flatulence. Furthermore, do not use the bathroom in the same location as your husbands: even within marriage, certain privacies ought to be maintained, and mutual respect demands that one not be entirely without restraint simply because of intimacy.

### Visitor Responses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי קָא קָארֵי אִינִישׁ אַבָּבָא — לָא תֵּימְרוּן ״מַנּוּ״, אֶלָּא ״מַנִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And when a person calls at the door** seeking to enter, **do not say: Who is it,** in the masculine form, **but** rather: **Who is it,** in the feminine form. Avoid creating the impression that you have dealings with other men.

When someone knocks at the door, do not call out *manu* — who is it? — which is masculine in form, as it creates the impression that one is expecting a male visitor. Rather, say *mani*, the feminine form.

### Pearl Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָקֵיט מַרְגָּנִיתָא בַּחֲדָא יְדֵיהּ וְכוּרָא בַּחֲדָא יְדֵיהּ. מַרְגָּנִיתָא — אַחְוִי לְהוּ, וְכוּרָא — לָא אַחְוִי לְהוּ עַד דְּמִיצְטַעֲרָן, וַהֲדַר אַחְוִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In order to demonstrate the value of modesty to his daughters, Rav Ḥisda **held a pearl in one hand and a clod of earth in the other. The pearl he showed them** immediately, **and the clod of earth, he did not show them until they were upset** due to their curiosity, **and then he showed it to them.** This taught them that a concealed object is more attractive than one on display, even if it is less valuable.

The passage culminates in a famous image. Rav Chisda held a pearl in one hand and something lesser — a kiln piece or similar object — in the other. He showed his daughters the pearl immediately, but concealed the other item until they grew curious and pained by not knowing what it was — *ad d'nistara* — and only then revealed it. The lesson: even an object of lesser value becomes precious and alluring when it is withheld and hidden. Applied to marriage, the teaching is that mystery and reserve — even between husband and wife — sustain desire and value. What is always immediately available loses its capacity to intrigue; what is sometimes withheld, even within intimacy, is treasured more deeply.

### Rashi's Reading

Rashi reads this passage as a metaphor for conduct in the marital bed. He understands *marganisa* as a reference to the breasts, and *kura* as a reference to the genitals, and interprets Rav Chisda's lesson as advice about foreplay — that one should first engage in lesser intimacies before proceeding to full sexual union. On this reading, Rav Chisda was offering his daughters direct counsel about sexual conduct in marriage: that the gradual, measured revelation of intimacy is more powerful and more honoring than immediacy. The passage operates simultaneously on both levels — the literal object lesson about withholding and desire, and, in Rashi's reading, a frank and tender piece of marital advice from a father to his daughters.

## Sieves Appearance

### Sieve Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שׁוֹלִין אֶת הַכַּרְשִׁינִין. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כִי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: אֵין מַשְׁגִּיחִין בַּכְּבָרָה כׇּל עִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **One may not soak vetches** in water in order to separate them from their chaff. However, one may take the straw in a sieve and place it into the trough of an animal. The Gemara comments: **The mishna** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: One may not look at a sieve at all** on Shabbat, lest one come to violate the prohibited labor of selecting.

The *Gemara* closes with a brief note regarding the *mishnah*'s permission to use a sieve-like basket for carrying purposes, provided one does not intend to sift. The *mishnah* is noted to be at variance with the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who teaches in a *baraita*: *ein mashgichin b'chavira kol ikar* — one should not use such a sieve basket at all, under any circumstance, regardless of intent. Because the implement looks like a sifting device, the concern is that one will in fact use it to sift, or that observers will assume one is doing so. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov thus maintains a categorical stringency that admits of no workaround based on intent. The discussion of the following *mishnah* is reserved for the next session.
