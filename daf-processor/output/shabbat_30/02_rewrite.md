# Shabbat 30 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eggshell Lamp Issue

### Dripping Oil Setup

The Gemara on 29b, approximately ten lines down in the wide lines, opens with the phrase *ve'im chibrah hayotzer*. The background is a ruling in the Mishna concerning a vessel — such as an eggshell — suspended over a lamp and dripping oil into it, or a configuration in which the wick of one lamp draws oil from a separate vessel. The concern, according to Rashi, is twofold: one might dip bread into the oil and thereby remove fuel designated for the lamp, and more directly, one would thereby cause the lamp to extinguish prematurely — an act of *gram kibu'i*, indirect extinguishing. Rabbi Yehuda, however, is not concerned about this possibility.

### Connection Requirement

The Mishna continues: *ve'im chibrah hayotzer mitchilah, mutar* — but if the two vessels were joined from the outset by the one who fashioned the lamp, it is permitted. The reasoning is psychological: when the configuration appears as a single unified vessel, one intuitively understands that the oil is part of the lamp and would not presume to dip into it. It is only when the two are visibly separate that the concern arises.

### Yotzer vs. Ke'ein Yotzer

The Gemara introduces a *braita* teaching that even if it was not the original manufacturer who connected the vessels, but rather the owner who joined them using plaster or a similar adhesive, it is likewise permitted. The Gemara then raises an objection: does the Mishna not specify that it must be done by the *yotzer*, the original maker? The Gemara resolves this: *mai yotzer, ke'ein yotzer* — the term does not mean literally the original artisan, but rather that the connection must be made in a manner resembling the work of the original artisan. It must be a permanent, substantial joining, not a makeshift or improvised attachment.

### Beit Nitza Precedent

The Gemara then cites a *braita*. Rabbi Yehuda relates: *paam achat shavtinu ba'aliyah beit nitza belod* — once we spent Shabbat in the upper room, the *aliyah*, of the house of Nitza in Lod. One might note that the *aliyah* — the upper chamber — appears throughout the literature as a designated venue for significant rabbinic gatherings. The *Mishnat Eretz Yisrael* by the Safrais observes that the *aliyah* appears to have functioned as a kind of meeting room for the rabbinic world, and that the word *alinu* sometimes functions almost as a verb denoting a rabbinic convocation. In any event, at that gathering, they brought an eggshell filled with oil, punctured at the bottom, and placed it over the mouth of the lamp so that it would drip — all set up before Shabbat. Rabbi Tarfon and the elders were present and said nothing. Rabbi Yehuda takes this silence as tacit approval: you see, it is permitted.

The Sages respond: *misham ra'ayah?* Can you really bring a proof from that case? *Shani beit nitza dezrizin hein* — the house of Nitza is different, because they are exceptionally scrupulous, and therefore there is no concern that they would err.

### Lo Pelug Principle

This response, while recorded, raises a significant difficulty. Normally the Gemara strongly resists making individual exceptions to a *gezerah* based on how careful a particular person or household happens to be — this runs directly against the principle of *lo pelug*. One might occasionally find the Gemara making exceptions for a recognized class, such as *kohanim* in the *beit hamikdash* or the groups working on the *Korban Pesach*, who are classified generically as *zrizin hein*. There are also contexts where the Gemara says *adam chashuv shani* — a distinguished person is different — and that too is arguably a class-based distinction. Perhaps the house of Nitza was understood as representing a type of class, namely, those who are *zerizin* and for whom the protective concern behind the rabbinic enactment simply does not apply. Nevertheless, the approach of carving out exceptions for the careful individual is in sharp tension with the *lo pelug* framework that governs *gezeirot* generally, and the Gemara's answer here remains somewhat unusual. In any case, this is the response the rabbis offered to Rabbi Yehuda's evidence.

## Dragging Benches Case

### Avin's Bench Example

The Gemara then transitions to a related discussion. Avin of Tzipori dragged a bench in an upper room — again, an *aliyah* — whose floor was made of marble, and he did so in the presence of Rabbi Yitzchak ben Elazar.

The Gemara's reasoning for why such dragging might be problematic is that dragging furniture on a dirt floor risks creating a furrow, which constitutes a prohibited act of plowing on Shabbat. On a marble floor, that concern does not apply. Nevertheless, Rabbi Yitzchak ben Elazar responds sharply.

### Silence Misinterpreted

Rabbi Yitzchak ben Elazar says: *ishtekuki lach kedi de'ishtiku lei chavraya le'Rabbi Yehuda* — if I stay silent with you the way the colleagues stayed silent with Rabbi Yehuda when he set up that lamp arrangement, *nafak minei churba* — something harmful will result, because people will mistakenly conclude that the practice is permitted. His point is that the colleagues' silence in the Beit Nitza episode should not have been taken as approval — they were simply being too polite to correct Rabbi Yehuda on the spot. He, having learned that lesson, refuses to remain silent here lest his silence be similarly misread.

This is a notably different resolution than the one offered above. Rather than invoking the special scrupulousness of a particular household, Rabbi Yitzchak ben Elazar's answer implies that the colleagues were simply wrong not to have spoken up, and that silence is not acquiescence. Even on a marble floor, the practice is prohibited as a *gezerah* — because if dragging on marble is allowed, it may lead to dragging on earthen floors, where a furrow genuinely would result. The decree cannot be applied selectively based on floor type.

### Kenishta Bench Query

The Gemara continues. The *reish kenishta* — the head of the synagogue, or according to Rashi, perhaps more precisely the *shamash* — of Batzrah dragged a bench in the presence of Rabbi Yirmiya Rabba.

### Big vs. Small Objects

Rabbi Yirmiya Rabba said to him: *ke'man hava iyalat lei? ke'Rabbi Shimon?* — like whom were you going, like Rabbi Shimon? This invokes the central and far-reaching debate between Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Yehuda concerning *davar she'eino miskavein* — performing an action that might incidentally produce a prohibited result, even though one does not intend that result. Rabbi Yehuda holds that even an unintended outcome creates liability if the action might cause a violation of Shabbat. Rabbi Shimon holds that so long as the outcome is not certain and not intended, one has not transgressed.

Dragging a bench across a yard is permissible under Rabbi Shimon's view because it will not necessarily make a furrow. Rabbi Yirmiya Rabba's challenge, however, is more pointed: even granting Rabbi Shimon's position, perhaps it applies only to *gdolim* — large, heavy benches that cannot simply be lifted and carried, leaving the person with no alternative but to drag them. For *ketanim* — smaller benches that one could easily pick up — did Rabbi Shimon really extend his leniency?

At first glance one might expect large benches to be the greater problem, since they are more likely to create a furrow. But the Gemara's reasoning runs in the opposite direction, and this is worth examining carefully. One explanation is rabbinic in character: while *davar she'eino miskavein* is technically permitted by Rabbi Shimon, it is not ideally sanctioned, and if one has a ready alternative, one ought to use it. Therefore, for small benches where carrying is feasible, the dragging is discouraged even under Rabbi Shimon. A deeper explanation, however, is that the availability of an alternative affects the degree to which the outcome can be attributed to one's action. When there is no alternative — when a bench is too heavy to carry — the resulting furrow is entirely incidental: the person simply had no choice. But when one could have carried the bench and instead chose to drag it, that choice carries with it a greater degree of responsibility for what results. The act of dragging, when avoidable, reflects a kind of indifference to the prohibited consequence, even if not a positive intention to produce it.

### Rav Yosef's Kashya

Rav Yosef raises a challenge against this framework. He cites the original teaching of Rabbi Shimon directly: *gorer adam mitah kisei v'safsal, u'vilvad shelo yiskaven la'asos charitz* — a person may drag a bed, a chair, and a bench, provided he does not intend to make a furrow. The teaching lists both large objects (*mitah*, a bed) and small ones (*kisei*, a chair), and permits both. This is a challenge to both positions that have been articulated: the view that small objects are the subject of the debate (with Rabbi Shimon requiring an alternative for small ones), and the view that large objects are uncontested (with both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon permitting them). The original source seems to allow both categories without qualification.

### Two Resolution Paths

The Gemara presents two competing resolutions. Ula explains: *mitah dumiya d'kisei* — when the teaching mentioned a bed, it meant a bed comparable in portability to a chair, i.e., a small, manageable bed that could in theory have been carried. Therefore, the debate concerns cases where an alternative exists, but by beds too large to carry, everyone agrees it is permitted. Rabbi Yirmiya Rabba explains from his own standpoint: *kisei dumiya d'mitah* — when the teaching mentioned a chair, it meant a chair as heavy as a bed, i.e., one that cannot easily be carried. Therefore, the debate concerns only cases where there is no alternative, and when one does have a choice, even Rabbi Shimon agrees that dragging is not permitted. The somewhat awkward implication of both readings is that the teaching's use of multiple examples collapses into a single scenario, raising the question of why multiple examples were offered at all.

### Shatnez Parallel Case

Rava then raises a further challenge from a different domain. The Gemara's discussion of *davar she'eino miskavein* is not limited to Shabbat; the question of whether an unintended consequence constitutes a violation applies across other prohibitions as well. Rava cites the case of garment merchants: *mochrei kesus mochrin k'darkan* — they may go about their business in their usual manner, wearing the garments they sell in order to transport them, even if those garments contain *shatnez*, the prohibited mixture of wool and linen. This is permitted *u'vilvad shelo yiskaven bachamah mipnei hachamah u'v'geshamim mipnei hageshamim* — provided they do not intend to derive the usual benefit of clothing, namely warmth from the sun or protection from the rain. The careful ones, *hatznuyin*, are still more stringent: they carry the garments suspended from a pole behind them, avoiding even the appearance of wearing them.

The Gemara notes: *ve'hacha de'efshar leme'avad ketznuyin* — here one has a perfectly available alternative, namely carrying the garments on a pole like the scrupulous ones. Yet even so, Rabbi Shimon's principle permits the wearing. This demonstrates that Rabbi Shimon allows *davar she'eino miskavein* even where another option exists.

### Rabbi Shimon's Scope

This constitutes a decisive refutation of Rabbi Yirmiya Rabba's position: *tyufta de'Rabbi Yirmeya Rabba, tyufta*. We see clearly that when Rabbi Shimon permits *davar she'eino miskavein*, he does so even when one could have avoided the action entirely. As long as the outcome is not intended and not certain, it is permitted — and this is a critically important principle for practical *halacha*.

One should note, however, that the *shatnez* example presents its own analytical difficulty. According to Abaye and Rava's qualification — that even Rabbi Shimon agrees one is liable when the outcome is a *psik reisha*, a certain and unavoidable consequence — the case of wearing garments would seem to fall into that category. Warmth and protection from the elements are essentially guaranteed consequences of wearing clothing. Rashi attempts to resolve this by arguing that when one has no *kavanah* for wearing, the act is not halakhically classified as an act of wearing at all, such that the prohibitions of *shatnez* do not attach. But if that is the resolution, it is a rationale specific to the definition of *levosha* and unique to the laws of *shatnez*, and cannot serve as a straightforward model for the general category of *davar she'eino miskavein*. The example is therefore somewhat difficult to integrate cleanly into the broader debate's parameters.

## Extinguishing Lamps

### Exemption Categories

The Gemara now turns to the next Mishna. *Hamechabeh et haner mipnei she'hu yarei mipnei nochri u'mipnei listim u'mipnei ruach ra'ah, mipnei hacholeh she'yishan, patur* — one who extinguishes a lamp out of fear of gentiles, out of fear of bandits, because of an evil spirit, or for the sake of a sick person who needs to sleep, is exempt. Rashi notes that the fear of gentiles likely refers to the concern that being seen with lights on during certain periods — particularly relevant to Zoroastrians — could bring serious danger. The fear of bandits is somewhat counterintuitive, since ordinarily keeping lights on signals that someone is home and deters attack; presumably in this case the person does not want to be detected. The evil spirit case is also curious, since the Gemara generally assumes that evil spirits are more dangerous in the dark than in the light. But these cases are what the Mishna lists, and as the Gemara will discuss, many of them sound like *pikuach nefesh* — life-threatening situations — in which the action is not merely exempt but actively permitted and even obligatory.

### Liability Cases

In contrast, *ke'chas al haner, ke'chas al hashemen, ke'chas al haptilah, chayav* — one who extinguishes purely to preserve the lamp from singeing, to conserve oil, or to conserve the wick, is *chayav*, fully liable.

### Rabbi Yosi Exception

Rabbi Yosi exempts all these cases — *poter be'kulan* — except for one: if one extinguishes in order to preserve the wick so as to create a singed, partially charred wick for future use, *mipnei she'hu oseh pecham* — because in that act one is actually creating charcoal, or a charred wick. This exception reflects the other major debate between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon: *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* — performing a biblically defined act of labor not for its standard, constructive purpose. When you extinguish a flame, the only recognizable act of creation is the production of charcoal or a singed wick. Extinguishing for any other reason — to preserve oil, to spare the lamp — yields no creative result and is therefore a *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah*.

The *tanna kamma* holds that even in those cases one is *chayav*: that is the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who rules that *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* carries full liability. Rabbi Yosi adopts the position of Rabbi Shimon, who holds that *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* renders one exempt — though not permitted. The one case Rabbi Yosi excepts, extinguishing to create a charred wick, is treated as a *melacha* performed for its genuine constructive purpose, and there even Rabbi Shimon would agree one is *chayav*.

It is worth noting the precision of the term *patur* in this context. Even where Rabbi Shimon exempts, no one has argued that *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* is outright permitted *le'chatchila*. The exemption is from biblical liability only; the act remains rabbinically forbidden. One who extinguishes a lamp to help a child sleep who is afraid of the dark would, according to Rabbi Yosi, be *patur* from a *chatas* — but the act is nonetheless *patur aval assur*.

### Patur vs. Chayav

The Gemara now raises a sharper analytical difficulty. Since the *seifa* of the Mishna — the liability clause — evidently reflects the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* is *chayav*, the *reisha* must be understood against that same backdrop. But then what does the *patur* in the opening cases mean? The Gemara poses the dilemma: if those cases involve a genuine threat to life, the ruling should be *mutar* — permitted and indeed obligatory — not merely *patur*. And if there is no threat to life, then under Rabbi Yehuda one should be *chayav*, not merely *patur*. There is no room for a middle ground of simple exemption under Rabbi Yehuda's framework.

The Gemara resolves this: the opening cases do in fact involve danger to life (*choleh she'yesh bo sakana*), and the ruling there is indeed effectively *mutar* and obligatory. The Mishna uses the term *patur* not as a precise legal category but because it needed to stand in contrast to the *chayav* stated at the end; the language is shaped by the parallel structure of the Mishna, not by precise legal definition. In truth, *patur* in the opening clause means the person is not only exempt but permitted — and obligated — to act.

### Rabbi Yoshiya's Braita

A complication arises from a *braita* in which Rabbi Yoshiya teaches: *im bishvil hacholeh she'yishan lo yechabeh* — if one wishes to extinguish for the sake of a sick person who needs sleep, one should not do so; *ve'im kibah, patur aval assur* — and if one did extinguish, one is exempt but it is forbidden. Here *patur* is used with explicit precision, excluding the possibility that the Mishna's *patur* has the expansive meaning the Gemara just assigned it.

### Life-Threatening Exception

The Gemara resolves the tension: *ha'hi be'choleh she'ein bo sakana, Rabbi Shimon hi* — that *braita* addresses a case where there is no danger to life, and it reflects Rabbi Shimon's position. For Rabbi Shimon, when there is no mortal danger, extinguishing the lamp constitutes a *melacha she'einah tzrichah legufah* — the person is exempt from biblical liability, but the act remains rabbinically forbidden. This is the middle ground that only Rabbi Shimon's framework can generate. The Mishna's *seder* of *patur* followed by *chayav*, by contrast, follows Rabbi Yehuda, for whom the only tenable reading of the *reisha* is that those cases involve genuine *pikuach nefesh*, and *patur* there is therefore not precise — it really means *mutar* and obligatory.

## Solomon's Wisdom Sermon

### Soul-Saving Question

Building on the discussion of extinguishing lamps to save a life, the Gemara introduces an extended aggadic passage in the form of a complete rabbinic sermon. The question presented is: *ma lechabos botzina denura mikamei bisha beshabata?* — what is the ruling about extinguishing a lamp for the sake of a sick person on Shabbat? The Aramaic here is notably different from the standard Talmudic idiom, feeling more vernacular.

Rabbi Tanchum of Noy (*d'min Noy*) does not begin with an answer. Instead, he opens with a challenge addressed dramatically to King Solomon himself.

### Challenge to Solomon

Rabbi Tanchum declares: *ant Shlomo, an chochmasach, an suchlesanusach?* — Shlomo, where is your wisdom, where is your understanding? The term *suchlesanut* is read by Rashi as a form of wisdom or *sechel*, though the Maharsha connects it to the word *sachal*, noting that *Kohelet* itself contains a discussion of *sichlut* — pointing toward the book's own exploration of wisdom and foolishness. In any case, the challenge is pointed: it is not enough that your words contradict the words of your father David — your words contradict one another.

David said: *lo hameitim yehalelu Yah* — the dead do not praise God. Yet Solomon said: *ve'shabeach ani et hameitim she'kevar metu* — I praise the dead who have already died. And then Solomon himself said: *ki la'kelev chai hu tov min ha'aryeh hameit* — a live dog is better than a dead lion. How can these coexist? This is the kind of dramatically engaging sermon style that opens with a provocative contradiction and proceeds to resolve it through sustained exposition — the rhetorical approach characteristic of the *Tanchuma* and *Yelamdenu* midrashim that take their name from this same Rabbi Tanchum.

### Dead vs. Living Theme

The Gemara responds: *lo kashya* — there is no contradiction. When David says the dead do not praise God, the meaning is: *le'olam ya'asok adam baTorah u'va'mitzvot kodem she'yamut* — one should always engage in Torah and *mitzvot* before one dies, for once one is dead, *batel min haTorah u'min hamitzvot* — one is released from Torah and *mitzvot*, and there is no more praise of God that one can produce through action. The living person's engagement in Torah and *mitzvot* is itself a form of divine praise; the dead have lost that capacity. Rabbi Yochanan connects this to the verse *ba'meitim chofshi* — the dead are free — which the Gemara interprets to mean: when one dies, one becomes free from Torah and *mitzvot*. While alive, one is bound by that responsibility — but it is a responsibility that is simultaneously an opportunity.

When Solomon says *ve'shabeach ani et hameitim she'kevar metu*, the Gemara offers multiple readings. The first: when the Jewish people sinned in the desert, Moses stood before God and offered many prayers and supplications for forty days and forty nights — yet was not answered. Only when he invoked *zechor le'Avraham le'Yitzchak u'le'Yisrael avadecha* — remember Abraham, Isaac, and Israel, Your servants — was he answered immediately. Rashi understands this to mean that those who engaged in Torah and *mitzvot* during their lifetimes and then died are worthy of praise — because sometimes the great figures of the past become even more powerful in their death than they were in their lives. This might be understood psychologically: like the artist underappreciated in his own time, those who are present and living are often taken for granted, while those who are gone are remembered with a romanticized reverence that amplifies their actual stature. The *zechus* of the *tzadikim*, their accumulated merit, also seems to operate through their deaths in a way that eludes precise articulation.

A *davar acher* follows. It is the way of the world that when a human ruler issues an edict, one cannot be sure whether it will be obeyed while he lives, let alone after he dies. Moses, however, issued any number of *gezeirot* and *takanot* — the reading of Torah in the synagogue among them — and they endure forever, long after his death. Rashi understands this not as praising the dead over the living in some metaphysical sense, but as praising the earlier generations over the present one. The power of those earlier generations lies in the fact that their institutions and rulings continue to shape Jewish life. This does create a tension with a verse in *Kohelet* itself that cautions against saying the earlier days were better than the present — *al tomar ki hayamim harishonim* — *ki lo me'chochma sha'alta zot* — but Rashi reads the Gemara's point as being specifically about the endurance of those earlier generations' contributions, not a blanket disparagement of the present.

### Past Generations

A third explanation is offered in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav. The verse in *Tehillim* reads: *hodieini Hashem kitzi u'midas yamai mah hi, eida mah chadel ani* — God, inform me of my end and the measure of my days, that I may know when I am to cease. David asked to know his end. God replied: *gezerah hi milfanai she'ein modiin kitzo shel basar va'dam* — it is an edict that the endpoint of a mortal is not revealed. David then asked to know the measure of his days. That too, God said, is not revealed. David asked at least for the day of the week on which he would die.

The Maharsha observes that there is a profound reason for this concealment: the very fact that one does not know the day of one's death ought to make every day feel like it could be one's last — not merely as a stimulus for *teshuvah*, but as a reason to treat every moment as precious. If one knew one's precise end date, the other days of the year would feel weightless. If one knew even the season, three quarters of the year would pass without that urgency. The Maharsha's reading makes non-disclosure not a deprivation but a gift.

### David's Forgiveness

God eventually informed David that he would die on Shabbat. David asked to die on Sunday instead, so that burial arrangements could be made. God replied: *kvar higya malchus Shlomo bincha* — by that point, the reign of your son Solomon will already have begun, and one reign cannot encroach upon another even by a hairbreadth. David then asked to die on Friday, at least. God responded by quoting David's own words back at him: *ki tov yom bachatzerecha me'alef* — one day in Your courts is better than a thousand. God says in return: *tov li yomecha echad she'ata yoshev ve'osek baTorah me'alef olos she'asid Shlomo bincha lehakriv lefanai al gabi hamizbe'ach* — one day of your sitting and learning Torah is worth more to Me than a thousand sacrifices that Solomon your son will one day offer upon the altar. Every single day of life devoted to Torah has a value that outweighs even the grandest acts of divine service one might imagine.

### David's Shabbat Death

From that point on, knowing that his death would come on Shabbat, David took precautions: *kol yoma de'shabta hava yasiv ve'garis kulei yoma* — every Shabbat he would sit and study the entire day, so that the learning would protect him. On the day appointed for his death, the Angel of Death could find no opening, since *lo pasik pumia mi'girsa* — David's mouth did not cease from learning for a single moment. The Angel of Death could not act. Finally, the Angel of Death went out to the garden behind David's house and shook the trees. David went out to see what the noise was. As he stepped onto a stair, the step cracked beneath him, and in that moment of silence his soul departed. The one instant he stopped learning was enough.

### Solomon's Queries

After David's death, Solomon sent to the *beit midrash* with two urgent questions. The first: *aba meit u'mutal bachama* — my father has died and the body is lying outdoors in the sun. What may be done? The corpse is *muktzah*. The second question was equally pressing: *ve'haklavim shel beit aba re'evim* — my father's dogs are hungry. What can be done for them? Tosafot explains the connection between these two questions: Solomon feared that if the dogs were not fed, they might come and consume his father's exposed body. Given that the corpse was lying in the sun and the dogs were hungry, the fear was real — as coroners can attest, such tragedies do occur.

The *beit midrash* answered: as for the dogs, take a *neveilah* — an animal that has died on its own — and chop it up and place it before them. As for your father, *hanach alav kikar o tinok u'taltelo* — place a loaf of bread or an infant upon the body and carry it, because if you are carrying the loaf or the child, the incidental movement of the corpse alongside is not prohibited.

And this is where Solomon's verse comes full circle: *ki la'kelev chai hu tov min ha'aryeh hameit* — the live dog is better than the dead lion. The dogs' need was addressed directly, while the corpse of the lion — the great king David — required a legal workaround just to be moved. The answer about the dogs came before the answer about David, and in halakhic terms the dogs' treatment was simpler and less encumbered.

### Candle Metaphor

Rabbi Tanchum now returns to the original question that opened the sermon: *u'v'inyan she'ila d'sh'ila kadmeichon* — regarding the question that was raised before you. He frames his answer in the language of metaphor rather than formal derivation. *Ner k'ri neshama, ner* — a candle is called a candle, *ve'nishmato shel adam k'ri ner* — and the soul of a person is called a candle. *Mutav she'tichbe ner shel basar va'dam mipnei nero shel Hakadosh Baruch Hu* — it is better that the candle of flesh and blood be extinguished rather than the lamp of the Holy One, blessed be He. Rashi notes that this is not the formal legal derivation of the *halacha* — the source for permitting *pikuach nefesh* on Shabbat is derived from *ve'chai bahem* and the principle that one should live by the commandments rather than die through them. But the metaphor captures the *aggadic* spirit of the legal ruling in a manner deeply resonant with everything that has preceded it in the sermon. The preciousness of every moment of human life, emphasized throughout the entire discussion of David's death and Solomon's wisdom, is precisely what justifies extinguishing one Shabbat candle — a human candle — in order to preserve the divine candle, to allow that person to go on living and serving God.

One might also notice a possible play on words: the original question used the term *botzina denura* — a lamp of fire — and the story's denouement turned on David going out to his *bustana*, his garden. Whether intentional or not, there is a certain resonance between the two terms as the sermon circles back to its opening.

## Ecclesiastes Preservation

### Hiding Ecclesiastes

The Gemara continues with a related discussion. Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat in the name of Rav teaches: *bikshu chachamim lignoz sefer Kohelet* — the Sages sought to suppress, or hide, the book of *Kohelet*, because *devarav sotrin zeh et zeh* — its words contradict one another. The discussion of Solomon's internal contradictions in the sermon above serves as a direct bridge to this topic. If the Sages nearly suppressed *Kohelet* on account of its contradictions, why ultimately did they not do so?

### Torah Framing

The answer: *mipnei she'techilato divrei Torah ve'sofo divrei Torah* — because its opening and its closing are both matters of Torah. The opening verse, *mah yitron la'adam b'chol amalo she'ya'amol tachat hashemesh* — what profit does a person have from all his labor that he labors under the sun? — is read in the school of Rabbi Yannai as implying that under the sun, worldly labor yields no lasting profit, but *ha'kodem hashemesh yesh lo* — before the sun, there is profit. What existed before the sun? According to Rashi, the Torah, which preceded creation. The *amal* of Torah therefore has an enduring value that no other toil possesses.

The closing verse, *sof davar hakol nishma, et ha'Elohim yera ve'et mitzvotav shemor, ki zeh kol ha'adam* — the conclusion of the matter, all having been heard: fear God and keep His commandments, for this is the whole of humanity — is likewise Torah. The phrase *ki zeh kol ha'adam* — this is the whole of humanity — is interpreted by Rav Elazar as meaning that the entire world was created for nothing other than this. Rav Abba bar Kahana reads it as meaning the value of this is equal to the entire world. Shimon ben Azzai — and some say Shimon ben Zoma — reads it as meaning the entire world was created to accompany and serve those who engage in Torah. This last reading echoes an earlier *aggadeta* in tractate *Berachot* — that everything in the world exists to provide for and accompany those who dedicate themselves to Torah study.

### Nevuah vs. Chochma

The Maharsha makes an important structural observation here. Recall that earlier — in the *aliyah* of a certain rabbinic gathering — there was also a need to justify the preservation of the book of *Yechezkel*, which likewise contained passages that seemed to contradict the Torah. Chananya ben Chizkiya ben Goron worked to reconcile those contradictions. But there is a critical difference between the two cases. *Sefer Yechezkel* is *nevuah* — prophecy. A prophetic book's inclusion in the canon is not a matter of discretion; by definition, if it is genuine prophecy, it must be preserved, and the rabbis' task was only to reconcile its apparent contradictions, not to decide whether to include it. *Kohelet*, however, belongs to the genre of *sefer chochma* — wisdom literature. Solomon's *chochma*, however divinely inspired, does not carry the same automatic authority as prophecy. For *Kohelet*, the Sages had first to make a positive determination that it was worth preserving at all, and only then, having decided to preserve it, did they need to address its internal tensions. Tomorrow the Gemara will address the same question regarding *Mishlei* — which likewise was initially under consideration for suppression — and we will see the contradictions that were identified and how they were ultimately resolved.
