# Bava Kamma 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Patur/Chayav Framework

### Opening Framework

The daf begins approximately six lines from the top, with the words *amar mar* — "said the master" — introducing a return to the *baraita* that lists cases in which a person is *patur b'dinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*: exempt from payment in a civil court, yet liable in heaven. The category refers specifically to compensatory payment. A person in such a situation cannot be compelled by a human court to make restitution, yet on a moral and theological plane — and, as we shall see, on something deeper than mere moral censure — he remains genuinely obligated.

### Fence Case

The reason this principle is introduced at this point in the *gemara* is that one of the cases on the *baraita*'s list is the case of someone who breaks down a fence, after which the animal escapes and is lost. The person who broke the fence may be liable for the fence itself, as the *gemara* had already discussed, but he did not directly destroy or injure the animal. He is therefore technically exempt from paying for the animal's loss — yet the consequences of his action are obvious. This is the paradigm case of *patur b'dinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*. It is also directly relevant because the *mishna* under discussion speaks of someone who breaks a fence and the animal escapes and causes damage — for which the *mishna* rules that person *patur*.

## False Witnesses

### Hiring Witnesses

Continuing with the *baraita*'s list, the *gemara* takes up the case of *hasocher edei sheker* — someone who hires false witnesses. The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the person who hired the false witnesses was himself the litigant and, as a result of their false testimony, he won a case he should have lost, then he is holding someone else's money. Were the facts known, a court would compel him to return it. In that situation, he would be *chayav b'dinei adam* — so the premise that he is *patur* in civil court collapses. The category only works, the *gemara* concludes, when he hired the witnesses on behalf of a third party: he arranged for false witnesses to testify on behalf of one litigant against another. The wrongful winner may have taken the money and disappeared, while the person who hired the witnesses has no direct legal liability. He did not steal the money himself. He is a *grama* — an indirect cause — and more than that, the witnesses themselves bore independent moral responsibility for their own decision to testify falsely. There is no doctrine of *shaliach l'dvar aveira* — a messenger's liability does not transfer for the commission of a sin — and so the hirer escapes civil liability entirely.

### Tosafot: Hire vs. Ask

Tosafot raises a pointed question: why does the *baraita* specify that the person *hired* the false witnesses? What if he merely asked them, without payment, to testify falsely? Presumably the same rule would apply — *patur b'dinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*. Tosafot's answer, found at the end of his comment to *ella l'chavrei*, is significant: *v'davka socher* — specifically hiring is required. If one merely asked the witnesses to testify falsely, he would not even have *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*. The reasoning is *d'savar she'lo yishma lo* — there is no basis to assume they would listen to a mere request; nothing compelled them. Tosafot adds that it would be wrong to read the *chiddush* of the case as: even someone who hires witnesses is still *patur b'dinei adam* — that is obvious, since *v'ein shaliach l'dvar aveira*, and the person clearly did not perform the act of *nezek* himself, as the witnesses who became the *edei zomemim* were the direct actors.

### Grama & Responsibility

One might question the underlying *sevara*: if you assumed they would not listen, why did you ask? The answer seems to be not that they literally would not have listened, but that there was no mechanism compelling their compliance — no power differential, no financial incentive of the kind that money creates. It remains an open question whether in some circumstances a non-financial request — by virtue of social pressure, authority, or persuasion — could approximate the moral weight of *socher* and thus create some *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*. The answer presumably depends on the facts, but Tosafot draws the line at a case with no structural compulsion equivalent to hiring.

### Chayav Shamayim

This discussion sharpens a critical conceptual point: *chayav b'dinei shamayim* does not simply mean that one has behaved morally or religiously reprehensibly. If someone, in the course of these events, had also moved *muktzah* on *shabbat*, we would not say he is *chayav b'dinei shamayim* to compensate the injured party for that. The category carries a specific meaning: at some deeper level, this person genuinely owes the other party the money. Heaven views the obligation as a real financial liability — not merely a sin. This is why the *poskim* have discussed whether the category of *chayav b'dinei shamayim* could support the injured party in seizing funds: the court would not force the defendant to pay, but if the injured party seized the money on his own, the court might not compel him to return it — because at some level the defendant really did owe it. The *gemara* does not say this explicitly, but there are positions in that direction. For this reason, merely asking someone to testify falsely does not rise to the level of *chayav b'dinei shamayim*: it does not create the kind of genuine moral-financial obligation that the category requires.

## Omission: No Testimony

### Not Testifying

The fourth case on the *baraita*'s list is *v'yodea edut l'chavero v'eino meid lo* — one who knows testimony that could help another person win a case in court, yet does not testify. This is the most striking case in the list because it is a pure sin of omission. No act was committed; property was not touched; the wrong consists entirely in failing to come forward. One might ask: what about other cases of passive omission? If one sees a car about to run over another person's valuable property and does nothing to stop it, surely one has done something wrong — but would we say there is *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim* to compensate for the loss of that property? The complete passivity involved in not testifying seems no different. Perhaps the distinction lies in the fact that a person may have been specifically summoned to testify and actively denied knowledge — something more than mere inaction. But this will require closer examination.

### Pasuk Analysis

The *gemara* asks: *bemai askinan* — what is the case? If there are two witnesses who are choosing not to testify, is there even a *chiddush*? The *gemara* replies that it is *peshita* — obvious — because a verse explicitly governs: *im lo yagid v'nasa avono* — "if he does not tell, he shall bear the sin." This verse appears to establish liability for failure to testify. The question, however, is how to read it. The classic context of *v'nasa avono* in that passage is the case of *shvuat ha'edut* — someone who is asked to testify, swears falsely that he knows nothing, and must bring a special *korban*. So the *v'nasa avono* there is specifically attached to taking a false oath. Does the verse independently establish an *issur* against not testifying, apart from any oath? Tosafot addresses this directly: *peshita im lo yagid*, he begins, and acknowledges the first view that the *v'nasa avono* applies only *keshe'avar shvuato* — when one has violated an oath, as indicated by the verse's reference to *kol alah*, the voice of an oath. However, Tosafot then cites a second position: *yesh omrim* — some say that the verse teaches that there is a *nesiat avon* simply from the failure to testify, independent of any oath. The oath, on this view, adds the specific consequence of a *korban*, but the wrongfulness of not testifying exists in any case.

### Tosafot Limits

Tosafot goes further and adds a significant limitation. Even if we accept that there is *nesiat avon* for not testifying without an oath, this applies *b'veit din* — specifically when one denies the testimony in court. For if you deny testimony in court, the principle *keivan shehigid shuv eino chozer u'magid* applies — once a witness has stated a position in court, he cannot retract it. By lying in court, you have permanently and irrevocably deprived the other party of that testimony. This is not the same as staying home and doing nothing — it is an active destruction of the other person's claim. By contrast, if you deny outside of court, you can always change your mind and come to testify later; the case may even be reopened. There is therefore no finality to the harm, and thus no *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*, according to Tosafot, unless the denial happens in court.

### Rambam View

The Rambam takes a significantly different position. In *Hilchot Edut*, perek 1, halacha 1, he rules: *ha'ed mitzuvah leha'id b'veit din b'chol edut sheyodea* — a witness is obligated to testify in court with any testimony he knows, whether it will make the other party liable or exempt him — *vehu sheyitba'enu leha'id b'dinei mamonot* — provided, in a civil case, that the litigant has demanded that he come and testify. The Rambam does not require the denial to be in court, nor does he require that the witness actively lied. Simple refusal to testify when demanded is sufficient to constitute a transgression. The verse *vehu ed o ra'ah o yada im lo yagid v'nasa avono* is his source. For the Rambam, then, once the litigant has summoned you and you refuse to come, you have transgressed — regardless of whether you said anything at all.

### One vs. Two

The *gemara* notes that if there are two witnesses refusing to testify, the *chiddush* is clear — their combined testimony would have been decisive, so their failure to testify is unambiguously harmful. The *gemara* therefore says this case is *peshita*. The genuinely novel case is *ela b'chad* — one witness. If only one witness refuses to testify, his testimony alone would not have decided the case, since monetary matters require two witnesses for a ruling. Rashi explains that even a single witness's presence might have tipped the scales — the opposing party might have preferred to settle rather than take a *shevua*, or the dynamic in court might have shifted. So the *chiddush* is that even one witness can be *chayav b'dinei shamayim* despite the fact that his testimony alone would not have compelled a ruling. Whether such a witness is technically in violation of the *pasuk* remains a separate question, but the *gemara* maintains that the moral-financial liability in heaven applies regardless.

## Cases Enumerated

### Fence Case

Returning now to the *baraita*'s enumeration, each case in the list raises the question: why is *chayav b'dinei shamayim* even necessary to state? In most of these situations, the *patur b'dinei adam* is obvious enough — given the rules of *grama*, it is hardly surprising that a civil court cannot compel payment. The *gemara* therefore wants to establish that each case nonetheless reaches the threshold of genuine heavenly liability, and that this is not self-evident in any of them.

The first case revisited is *haporeitz geder bifnei behemat chaveiro* — breaking the fence in front of another's animal. The *gemara* will later explain that the novelty here lies in the fact that one might have thought the fence was already so dilapidated that it was about to collapse on its own, and that breaking it down contributed nothing of substance.

### Fire Obstacle

The second case is placing something in front of a fire — described by the *gemara* as *kofeif kamatah shel chaveiro*, bending another's standing grain toward a fire. The *gemara* had established that the fire reached the grain only because of a *ruach she'einah metzuyah* — an unusual wind that was not reasonably foreseeable. One might therefore have thought that even *chayav b'dinei shamayim* does not apply, since the person could not have anticipated this outcome. The *gemara* dismisses this: you had no business interfering with another person's property at all, and that interference is sufficient to establish heavenly liability even when the probability of harm was low.

### Tamun/Blanket

The third case on the enumerated list is the *tamun* case: someone covers another's grain with a blanket in order to render it *tamun* — hidden — so that the owner of a nearby fire would be exempt from paying for it, since the rule is that one who starts a fire is not liable for hidden objects that are consumed. The *gemara* identifies this as the view of Rav Ashi. One might have thought the person could claim he was simply trying to help the grain owner by protecting the grain. The *gemara* rules that this claim does not exempt one from *chayav b'dinei shamayim*.

### Good Samaritan

Tosafot's discussion of the *tamun* case generates an important and broader principle. Tosafot observes that Heaven knows what a person truly intended — *galui v'yaduah lifnei hamakom l'man itkavein* — and so if someone was in fact trying to help the owner of the fire escape liability, the *chiyyuv b'dinei shamayim* is obvious. The *chiddush*, Tosafot says, is that even if the person genuinely intended to help — *afilu b'mitkavein l'tovah* — he is still *chayav b'dinei shamayim*. The reasoning: *d'iba'i lei l'izdahorei* — he was still obligated to exercise care, because he should have foreseen that this intervention might cause loss.

This raises the broader question of what might be called the Good Samaritan doctrine as applied to property. The Good Samaritan law in civil jurisprudence does not require someone to save a drowning person, but it does protect a rescuer from liability for incidental harm caused in the course of a genuine rescue attempt — the rationale being that we want to encourage intervention. A parallel question arises with property: if one sees another's property about to be destroyed and intervenes to prevent the loss, but in doing so causes additional damage, is there liability? Tosafot's answer is that *chayav b'dinei shamayim* remains even when the intervention was well-intentioned, because the obligation to protect another's property does not come with a waiver of the duty of care. In fact, there is a *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah* that affirmatively obliges one to act in such cases — which means one has a right to intervene, and perhaps even an obligation. But Tosafot maintains that this does not automatically exempt one from *chiyuv* if care was not exercised. The exemption in civil court exists only because the resulting harm was indirect — *grama* — not because the act of helping was itself a defense. If one caused direct damage in the course of trying to protect another's property, the question of whether *adam muad l'olam* would create liability even then becomes a serious one, and the answer likely turns on whether the person acted with reasonable care under the circumstances.

### False Witnesses Redux

The case of *hasocher edei sheker* — hiring false witnesses — appears again in the enumeration. The *gemara* explains the *chiddush* here as follows: one might have thought that even *chayav b'dinei shamayim* does not apply, because *divrei harav v'divrei hatalmid divrei mi shomein* — when God says "do not do this" and I say "do it," obviously the witnesses should have listened to God. Since they had every reason to refuse, one could argue the moral weight falls entirely on them and the hirer bears no responsibility whatsoever, even in heaven. The *gemara* rejects this: the *kamashma lan* is that the hirer does bear *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim* despite this argument.

### No Testimony Redux

The case of *hayodeia edut l'chaveiro v'eino meid lo* — one who knows testimony and withholds it — returns as well. The *chiddush* is stated as follows: one might have argued, particularly in the case of a single witness, that his testimony might not have made a difference anyway. The defendant might have simply sworn falsely rather than concede — *deilma mishtava l'shikra*. So even his failure to testify may have had no impact on the outcome, and there would be no *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*. The *gemara* rules that we do not say this. Yet, as noted earlier, the more fundamental *chiddush* is that pure passivity — doing nothing at all — can create *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*, unless one follows Tosafot in requiring an active in-court denial.

### Parah Adumah

Another case added by the *gemara* is *ha'oseh melacha b'mei chatas u'v'parah chatas* — performing labor with the water drawn for use in the *Parah Adumah* purification ritual, or with the *Parah Adumah* cow itself. Such labor renders these items invalid for use in the ritual. The person is *patur midinei adam* because the damage is *hezek she'eino nikkar* — invisible damage, damage that is not physically apparent. Yet the person is *chayav b'dinei shamayim*, because the practical consequence is severe: an object worth an enormous sum has been rendered nearly worthless. The liability runs either to the individual owner of the cow or to the *Beit Hamikdash*.

### Poison Case

The case of *ha'noten sam mavet lifnei behemat chaveiro* — placing poison before another's animal so that the animal eats it and dies — is then introduced. This case is plainly similar in structure to the other *grama*-type cases on the list. You did not directly kill the animal; you merely placed something before it that it chose to eat. You are nonetheless *chayav b'dinei shamayim*.

### Fire via Intermediary

The *gemara* adds *hashole'ach et ha'beirah b'yad cheresh shoteh v'katan* — sending a fire in the hands of a deaf person, a fool, or a minor. This too is a case familiar from an earlier *mishna* in the previous *perek*, where *masar l'cheresh* — handing over to such an individual — was discussed. The person who sent the fire is a *grama* only, since it was the legally incompetent agent who actually brought the fire to its destination. He is *patur* in civil court but *chayav b'dinei shamayim*.

### Scaring

The *gemara* also lists *hamavis et chaveiro* — frightening someone without physical contact. If someone shouts in another's ear or otherwise terrifies them, causing them to fall or sustain injury, there is no direct physical contact and technically no *nezek* through bodily force. The person is *patur midinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*. This is consistent with the general pattern: all of these are *grama*-type cases in which some intervening act or circumstance separates the person's conduct from the resulting harm.

### Pitcher & Camel

Finally, the *gemara* cites the cases of *nishbar kado b'reshut harabbim v'lo silkah* — one whose pitcher broke in the public domain and who did not remove the shards — and *nafla gemalo v'lo he'emidah* — whose camel fell and who did not get it back on its feet. Rabbi Meir rules these cases *chayav* on the grounds that leaving debris or a fallen animal in the public domain is itself a *peshiya* — an act of negligence. But the *Chachamim* rule *patur midinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*, reasoning that the person did not cause the breakage or the fall — it was an *ones* — and that the subsequent failure to clean up, while certainly irresponsible, does not constitute *peshiya* in the technical legal sense, because the person had already become *mafkir* — had effectively abandoned the object — before the obligation to clear it arose. Nonetheless, one made a mess and someone was hurt by it, and the heavenly standard demands restitution.

The *gemara* then asks: given that there are many cases of *patur b'dinei adam v'chayav b'dinei shamayim*, why list only these? The answer is *hani itzrechicha* — these specific cases required statement because one might have thought that even *chayav b'dinei shamayim* does not apply. In the other examples, while the *patur b'dinei adam* may be surprising to someone unfamiliar with the rules of *nezikin*, the *chayav b'dinei shamayim* is not in question. In each of the enumerated cases, there was a plausible argument that the person bears so little responsibility that even heaven would not hold him to account — and the *gemara* rules that this is not so.

## Chiddush Analysis

### Why Chiddush?

Having enumerated the cases, the *gemara* now turns to the analytical question: in each case, what is the *chiddush* of saying *chayav b'dinei shamayim*? Why might one have thought that heavenly liability too does not apply? The *gemara*'s answer in each instance reveals something about what *chayav b'dinei shamayim* really means. As established earlier, it does not mean simply that a wrongful act was committed — it means that a genuine obligation of restitution exists, recognized at the level of divine judgment. To reach that threshold, the person's connection to the harm must be substantial enough. In each of the listed cases, there is a specific reason to wonder whether that threshold is met, and the *gemara*'s ruling that it is constitutes the *chiddush*.

### Weak Fence

In the case of the broken fence, the *chiddush* according to Rabba is: *keivan d'limister kai* — since the fence was already on the verge of collapse, one might have argued, what did you really do? The animal would have gotten out anyway. There was essentially no fence providing meaningful protection. Perhaps you contributed nothing, and there should be no *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*. The *kamashma lan* is that even a decrepit fence was, at the moment you knocked it down, still performing some function of containment. You had no right to remove that protection, however minimal, and having done so, you bear heavenly liability for the consequences.

### Fire/Wind

In the fire case — placing another's grain in the path of a fire — the *chiddush* is: *mi havei yadeinah d'yistav'ar ruach she'einah metzuyah* — who could have known that this unlikely wind would come and carry the fire to the grain? The probability of harm was so low that one might have argued there is no heavenly liability. The *kamashma lan* is that regardless of probability, you had no business touching another person's property. If the unlikely wind came and turned the low-probability event into an actual loss, you remain the proximate moral cause — you interfered, and through your interference, the damage resulted. Whatever the odds, this is enough for *chayav b'dinei shamayim*.

### Coverage Intent

In the *tamun* case, the *chiddush* as framed by Tosafot is particularly striking. The person who covered the grain might say: *ana kissuyei kissiteih nihalach* — I was just trying to help you, I covered it with a blanket to protect it. And Tosafot asks: what is the *chiddush* there? God knows what the person truly intended. If he was in fact trying to benefit the fire owner at the grain owner's expense, obviously he is *chayav b'dinei shamayim*. The *chiddush*, says Tosafot, is that even if he genuinely intended to help the grain owner — believing that the blanket would slow the flames and give time for rescue — he is still *chayav b'dinei shamayim*. The reason: *d'iba'i lei l'izdahorei* — he was nonetheless obligated to be careful, to think through whether his intervention might cause loss rather than prevent it. Good intentions do not eliminate the duty of care, and the failure to exercise that care, even when acting with genuine benevolent intent, creates *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim*. This is a significant ruling about the nature of responsibility when one acts in another's interest.

### Witnesses Redux

For the case of hiring false witnesses, the *chiddush* is the argument that *divrei harav v'divrei hatalmid divrei mi shomein* — since God commanded against false testimony and I am asking them to violate that command, surely they should listen to God rather than to me. On this logic, the moral weight falls entirely on the witnesses and I have no *chiyuv b'dinei shamayim* at all. The *kamashma lan* is that we do not accept this argument. The act of hiring — providing financial motivation, creating a power dynamic — is enough to make you complicit in the resulting harm at the level of heavenly judgment.

### Passive Liability

For the case of withholding testimony, the *chiddush* is that one might reason: *mi yeimar d'chi havina mistahedna d'havah modeh* — even if I had testified, who says it would have made a difference? Perhaps the opposing party would have sworn falsely regardless — *deilma mishtava l'shikra*. So my failure to testify caused no certain harm, and I should not be *chayav b'dinei shamayim*. The *kamashma lan* is that we do not accept this argument either. As was noted, this remains the most challenging *chiddush* in the list, because the behavior is entirely passive — unless, as Tosafot argues, one defines the case as specifically including an in-court denial, which converts the passivity into an affirmative act of destruction of the other party's evidentiary position.

## Animal Escape

### Night/Bandit

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishna*'s case of an animal that breaks out at night or is let out by bandits, with the *shomer* (guardian) being ruled *patur*. The *gemara* notes that the *mishna*'s reference to nighttime is significant: if the animal breaks out during the day, one might expect the *shomer* to have heard and taken corrective action. At night, however, even if one hears the animal breaking out, Tosafot argues that there is no obligation to leap out of bed at that hour — the duty of *shmira* does not extend to that level of vigilance.

### Rava/Tunnel

Rava introduces a qualification: the *mishna*'s ruling that the *shomer* is *patur* applies specifically *vehu shechatra* — when the animal tunneled out from beneath the wall. If the animal did not tunnel but broke through the wall in some other way, what would the ruling be? The *gemara* asks: if the wall was strong — *koseul bari* — and the animal got out somehow anyway, through a storm or some extraordinary means, then the *shomer* clearly did his job, and there should be no liability regardless of how the animal escaped. And if the wall was weak — *koseul reuah* — then if the animal simply walked through it, the *shomer* was negligent for using a wall inadequate for containment. In that case, why would tunneling exempt him? He did not build a proper enclosure in the first place.

### Techilato/Sofo

This analysis leads to the doctrine of *techilato b'peshiya sofo b'ones* — a case in which a negligent beginning is followed by a conclusion that has the character of *ones*, an unavoidable accident. If one holds that *techilato b'peshiya sofo b'ones* is *patur*, then tunneling — the *ones* element — would exempt the *shomer* even though the weak wall was the *peshiya*. If one holds *techilato b'peshiya sofo b'ones chayav*, then the fact that the animal escaped by tunneling would not help the *shomer*, since his negligence in not providing adequate containment is what made the escape possible.

### Wall Strength

The *gemara* concludes that the *mishna* must be speaking of a strong wall — *koseul bari*. In such a case, however the animal escaped — whether by tunneling or otherwise — the *shomer* did not fail in his duty, and the escape is an *ones*. Rava's statement, it turns out, was not about the case of *patur* but about the *mishna*'s other case: *hini'chah bachama* — leaving the animal tied in the hot sun.

### Negligence Scope

This distinction illuminates the concept of *techilato b'peshiya sofo b'ones* more precisely. The *chiddush* of that doctrine, when applicable, is that it renders one liable despite the fact that the *ones* element was the proximate event. But this is not unlimited. The *gemara* had addressed earlier a case where the initial *peshiya* relates to one category of danger — say, negligence regarding camels falling into a well — while the actual harm occurs through a different category — an ox falls in. In such a case, the *peshiya* does not carry over to the different type of harm. The connection between the *peshiya* and the *ones* must be meaningful: your negligence must have specifically created the conditions that led to the harm. When the *ones* flows directly from the *peshiya* — when your failure to provide adequate protection is precisely what allowed the particular harm to occur — then the law regards the entire sequence as a single *peshiya*.

### Sun Case

Rava's statement is applied to the case of the animal left in the sun. He rules: *afilu chatra* — even if the animal tunneled its way out, the *shomer* is *chayav*. *Lo mibaya heicha dela chatra* — it is unnecessary to say that if it broke through the wall in a normal way, the *shomer* is liable, because in the context of being left in the blazing sun, an animal's attempt to break through the wall in any conventional manner is entirely foreseeable and therefore within the scope of the original *peshiya*. But even if it tunneled — which would normally be regarded as something extraordinary and hence an *ones* — Rava rules *chayav* here too, because the reasoning is: *keivan deshavakta bechama kol tachsisa deit lah lemievad avda venafka* — since you left it in the sun, the animal will use every means at its disposal to escape. Any method of escape, however unusual, is part of the foreseeable consequence of that initial negligent act. The entire range of behaviors that an animal will engage in when left in extreme conditions is encompassed within the original *peshiya*. Nothing the animal does to get out counts as a true *ones* when it was the *shomer*'s own negligence that put it in that situation.

A clarifying point: all of this operates within the framework of *shmira* — the duty of custody. The question is not whether one can trace a chain of direct causation from the *shomer*'s act to the specific damage, as one might in a *mazik b'yadayim* scenario. The question is whether the *shomer* fulfilled his *chiyuv shmira*. Since he shirked that responsibility, and the animal escaped and caused damage as a direct result, the *shomer* is liable — regardless of the particular manner of escape. The manner of escape is relevant only insofar as it bears on whether the *shomer*'s failure specifically created the conditions for that escape.

## Thieves & Ownership

### Thieves Liable

The *gemara* then states: *hotziuha listim, listim chayavim* — if bandits took the animal out, the bandits are liable for damage the animal subsequently causes. The *gemara* asks: *peshita* — is this not obvious? Since they took it, *keivan de'afkuha kaima lah b'irshuteihu l'chol mili* — once they extracted it, it is considered theirs for all purposes, and so they bear liability for damage it causes. The *gemara* clarifies: the novelty applies when the bandits did not physically drag the animal out but instead positioned themselves around it and forced it to move — *dekamu lah b'apah* — without actually laying hands on it.

### Cow/Grain Case

This connects to a broader ruling. *Rabba amar Rav Mattana amar Rav*: if *Reuven* took *Shimon*'s cow and had it eat *Shimon*'s grain, *Reuven* is *chayav*. The *gemara* asks: if you actually placed the animal's head directly into the grain, obviously you are liable. The novelty, it continues, arises in the case where *Reuven* did not place the animal directly in the grain but stood in its way and directed it toward the grain — *dekama lei b'appa* — making it go where he wanted without direct physical contact.

### Abaye Kinyan

Abaye clarifies, addressing Rav Yosef, that the original formulation of the ruling referred not to standing in its way but to hitting the animal with a stick and driving it to eat the grain. Similarly, when the bandits "took" the animal, they did so by driving it with a stick. The *chiddush* here, according to Abaye, is that hitting an animal with a stick and driving it constitutes a sufficient act of *meshicha* — a form of *kinyan* — to transfer practical ownership for purposes of *nizkei mamon*. Even though *Reuven* did not intend to acquire *Shimon*'s cow in any conventional sense and had no plan to keep it, the act of driving it with a stick is enough of an act of possession that the cow becomes *Reuven*'s for the purpose of *shen v'regel* liability. The *chiddush* of *Abaye*'s formulation is therefore that even a temporary, purpose-limited act of possession — not for the purpose of acquisition but for causing damage — creates *baalut* for liability.

### Rav Control

The first and more expansive version of the ruling, attributed to *Rav*, is that standing in the animal's way and directing it — without any act of *kinyan* at all — is sufficient to create liability. This is a considerably larger *chiddush*. Without a *kinyan*, without physically holding or driving the animal, merely positioning oneself so as to control its direction and purpose creates enough of a relationship with the animal that one becomes the *baal* for *nezikin* purposes. The conceptual argument would be that the person has, de facto, taken over the position of the one who is responsible for watching and controlling the animal, and in doing so, has displaced the actual owner from the ability to exercise that responsibility.

### Tosafot Ownership

Tosafot draws out the underlying principle from this discussion. His formulation is: *dekeevan shehotzi mereshut ba'alim shehaya chayavin bishmirasa, v'eino yachol lishomra lefee shenigzela mehem, yesh al hagazlan lishomra* — since you removed the animal from the custody of its owners who were responsible for guarding it, and since by doing so you have made it impossible for them to guard it, you have assumed the responsibility to guard it. And the principle that emerges is: *delinyan nezikin ikri ba'alim kol mi shebeyado lishomra* — for purposes of *nezikin*, the "owner" is whoever is in a position to guard the animal. This is not ownership in the property-law sense; it is ownership defined by custodial capacity and responsibility. One does not need to own the object, does not need a formal *kinyan*, and according to one view does not even need to have performed a *meshicha*-like act. Being in the position of control — having displaced the actual owner from his custodial role — is sufficient.

Tosafot draws a practical *nafka minah* from this: if *Reuven* directed *Shimon*'s animal to eat *Shimon*'s grain in the *reshut harabim*, would *Reuven* be liable? The answer would be no — because even though the animal is treated as *Reuven*'s for *shen v'regel* purposes, *shen v'regel* is *patur* in the *reshut harabim*. The fact that the analysis runs through the lens of *shen v'regel* ownership — rather than, say, *mazik b'yadayim* — has direct practical consequences for when and where liability attaches.

## Guardian/Shepherd

### Mishna Interpretation

The *gemara* now examines the *mishna*'s statement *masra lero'eh, nichnas haro'eh tachtav* — he gave it over to the shepherd, and the shepherd took on his responsibilities. The *gemara* asks: *tachtav d'man* — whose responsibility does the shepherd assume? If the straightforward reading is that the owner handed the animal to a shepherd and the shepherd takes over the owner's responsibility, this is unremarkable: an earlier *mishna* had already established that when an owner gives an animal to a *shomer chinam*, borrower, paid guardian, or renter, those parties step into the owner's shoes. Repeating this rule here would be redundant.

### First Guardian

The *gemara* therefore proposes an alternative reading: the *mishna* is not speaking of the owner giving the animal to a shepherd but of a *shomer* — a guardian — giving it to a shepherd. The *mishna* is therefore telling us that when the *shomer* hands the animal to the shepherd, the shepherd takes over responsibility and the original *shomer* is completely exempt — *shomer kama iptar lei legamrei*. This is a striking reading, and the *gemara* immediately notes that it may stand in tension with a ruling of Rava.

### Rava Liability

Rava had ruled *shomer shemasar l'shomer chayav* — a guardian who hands over the object entrusted to him to another guardian is himself liable. The reasoning is that the original owner entrusted the object specifically to this guardian, and by passing it on, the guardian has violated the terms of the bailment and is therefore negligent. If so, how can our *mishna* say that the first *shomer* walks away clean? The *gemara* raises this as a potential *tiyuvta* to Rava.

### Apprentice Exception

Rava's response is that *masra lero'eh* in the *mishna* does not mean the *shomer* gave it to some outside shepherd. It means the shepherd gave it to his *varzilei* — his apprentice or assistant. *De'orchei dero'eh lemimsar levarzeilei* — it is customary and expected for a shepherd to hand responsibility for an animal to his assistant. This was therefore within the scope of what was implicitly understood when the original *shomer* accepted responsibility. It is not a case of *shomer shemasar l'shomer* in Rava's prohibited sense, because handing it to an apprentice is part of normal shepherding practice and was presumably anticipated.

### Two Explanations

The *gemara* offers two versions of the reasoning. One position (*ika d'amri*) argues that the specification of *ro'eh* in the *mishna* — rather than the generic *acher*, "another person" — is itself the indicator that the case is a shepherd handing to his apprentice, since that particular phrasing implies the natural chain of custody within the shepherding profession. The other position holds that the *mishna* is simply illustrating the common case — a shepherd giving to his assistant — but the same principle would apply if the guardian gave the object to any other reliable custodian, and the case of the *ro'eh* and *varzilei* is chosen because it is the most natural example. The two positions differ on whether the exemption is specific to the shepherd-apprentice relationship as a matter of implicit contractual understanding, or whether it reflects a broader principle about when transferring custody to another is considered within the scope of the original *shomer*'s mandate.

A further question worth noting: when we discuss *shomer shemasar l'shomer*, the classic issue is whether the first *shomer* remains liable to the original owner if the object is damaged or lost under the second *shomer*'s watch. But a distinct question arises in the context of damage the animal itself causes: if the first *shomer*'s negligence in handing it away creates liability to the original owner for loss, does it also create liability to third parties whose property is subsequently damaged by the animal? This remains an open question. The *pshat* of the discussion is clearly focused on the first *shomer*'s responsibility to the owner who entrusted the animal. Whether that negligence also creates a duty running to the rest of the world is not directly addressed.

## Shomer Aveidah

### Lost Item Intro

The *gemara* now introduces a new topic through the term *itmar* — "it was said" — signaling an *amoraic* discussion. The question is: *shomer aveidah* — what is the status of someone watching a lost object? When one finds another person's lost item and is in the process of watching over it until it can be returned, does this constitute an act of *shemira* that carries halachic liability? If so, at what level? Everyone agrees that if the person is genuinely negligent — falling below even the minimum standard of *shmira* — he is liable to compensate. The question is about the intermediate level: if the object is stolen or lost without his negligence, is he exempt as a *shomer chinam* would be, or liable as a *shomer sachar* would be?

### Rabba Chinam

Rabba rules *ke-shomer chinam dami* — the finder of a lost object is like an unpaid guardian. His reasoning is *mai hana'ah mati lei* — what benefit is the person receiving? He is acting entirely out of obligation, performing a *mitzvah* at his own inconvenience with no compensation whatsoever. It is sufficient of a *chiddush* that he has any liability at all; to place upon him the elevated duties of a *shomer sachar* without any financial benefit would be excessive.

### Rav Yosef Sachar

Rav Yosef disagrees and rules *ke-shomer sachar dami* — the finder is like a paid guardian with a higher standard of *shmira*. His reasoning: *b'hahu hana'ah d'lo ba'ei le-meisav lei rifta le-anya* — the benefit the finder receives is the exemption from giving charity. Because of the principle of *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* — one who is engaged in performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from other *mitzvot* — while the person is occupied with the *aveidah*, someone comes to him seeking *tzedakah* or an *hachnasas kallah* contribution, and he can legitimately say: I am otherwise engaged. This exemption from other *mitzvot* constitutes a real benefit, and because he receives this benefit, he takes on the higher level of responsibility.

### Osek Mitzvah

The principle of *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* is thus central to Rav Yosef's position. The finder is not simply doing a good deed; he is in the middle of an active *mitzvah* performance, and this frees him from other obligatory acts during that time.

### Tosafot Limit

Tosafot places an important limitation on this principle. One might think that having an *aveidah* in one's house creates an ongoing blanket exemption from *mitzvot*: I have this lost object at home, so I am *patur* from *tefillin*, *tzitzis*, davening, and *tzedakah* for as long as it sits there. This would be, Tosafot notes, an extraordinarily convenient arrangement. Tosafot rules it out: *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* applies only when one is actively and simultaneously engaged in the *mitzvah* — making phone calls to locate the owner, airing out a rug, attending to the object — in a manner that makes it impossible to simultaneously perform another *mitzvah*. The exemption is limited to moments of genuine engagement where performing two *mitzvot* at once is not feasible. Some *rishonim* go further and hold that *osek be-mitzvah* can exempt even when both *mitzvot* could theoretically be performed simultaneously — for example, one could recite *shema* while driving to redeem captives. On this view, active engagement itself is sufficient for *patur*, even if the simultaneous performance would be physically possible. But Tosafot's position is that the exemption requires the active engagement to be of a kind that precludes simultaneous performance of the other *mitzvah*.

### Closing Reasons

The *gemara* notes a second reason for Rav Yosef's position, cited in an alternate formulation: *keivan derachmana shab'dei be'al korchei, hilkach ke-shomer sachar dami* — since the *Torah* has obligated this person against his will to watch the *aveidah*, it follows that the *Torah* is mandating a high level of *shmira*, and therefore he is like a *shomer sachar*. Rabba does not dispute that the *Torah* mandates the watching; the question is whether a *Torah*-imposed obligation carries with it an elevated level of *shmira*. The counterargument to Rav Yosef's second reasoning is that a *Torah* obligation does not automatically imply the highest standard of care. We know that *Torah* obligations to guard one's well or one's ox carry only a minimum standard of *shmira*. If anything, the fact that the *Torah* is imposing the obligation here rather than a contractual agreement might suggest there is *no* mechanism for elevation to *shomer sachar* status — because that elevated responsibility arises from a bilateral contractual relationship, an implicit agreement that in exchange for payment a higher standard will be maintained. Where there is no such relationship — where a person watches another's property not because of any agreement but purely because the *Torah* says so — there may simply be no mechanism by which the higher level of *shmira* is triggered. This fundamental tension in the nature of *shomer aveidah* will be taken up in the continuation of the *sugya*.
