# Keritot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Gufei M'chulakin Debate

### Marketplace Inquiry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְעוֹד (שָׁאֲלוּ) [שְׁאֵלָן] רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט חֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים בַּחוּץ בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] מַהוּ? חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת, אוֹ אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And furthermore, Rabbi Akiva asked** Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua: With regard to **one who** unwittingly **slaughters five offerings outside** the Temple **during one lapse of awareness, what is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring five sin offerings, one **for each and every** act of slaughter, **or** is he liable to bring **one** sin offering **for all** the acts of slaughter? **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We have not heard** a ruling from our teachers in that specific case.

The *Gemara* opens in the middle of 15b, returning to the *Mishnah*'s account of a discussion *Rabbi Akiva* had in the marketplace, in the company of *Rabban Gamliel* and *Rabbi Yehoshua*. The central question under examination is what defines something as *gufei m'chulakin* — acts sufficiently distinct from one another to generate separate *korbanot*. The concrete question *Rabbi Akiva* had posed was: if one slaughters many animals as *shechutei chutz* (slaughtering sacrificial animals outside the Temple) in one state of forgetfulness, does each animal constitute a distinct act? This is not obviously parallel to the case of relations with different people; animals, one might think, are objects, not subjects. *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s initial response was to draw a comparison to *me'ilah* — specifically, eating from five different *tamchuyin* (pots) from a single animal before the blood had been sprinkled.

### Defining Gufei M'chulakin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? הָכִי קָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ: מָה רְאָיָה לְשׁוֹחֵט מֵאוֹכֵל? מָה לְאוֹכֵל שֶׁכֵּן נֶהֱנֶה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** is **difficult for Rabbi Shimon** with the first version of Rabbi Akiva's question, that he insists that Rabbi Akiva had asked about one who eats *notar* from five offerings? The Gemara answers that **this** is what is **difficult for him:** If Rabbi Akiva asked about one who unwittingly sins by slaughtering, why is the answer derived from an unwitting sin involving eating? After all, **what proof** can be cited **for** a case of **one who slaughters from** a case of **one who eats? What** is notable **about one who eats?** It is notable **in that he derives benefit** from the act of eating, whereas one who slaughters does not derive physical benefit.

Why could one not use the case of eating *me'ilah* from five different pots as a proof for the case of *shechitah*? The *Gemara* raises the objection that these are categorically different acts: *shechitah* is slaughtering, while *me'ilah* involves eating and benefiting. The principle underlying the objection is *ma l'ochel sheken neheneh* — the act of eating involves bodily benefit in a way that *shechitah* does not. Perhaps precisely because eating carries greater personal benefit, *me'ilah* is a stricter category, and therefore we might be more willing to regard the five pots as five distinct violations — while by *shechitah*, even if we're dealing with different animals, the same leniency would not necessarily apply. The point, counterintuitive as it may initially seem, is that the question of whether something is *gufei m'chulakin* is not a uniform determination that can be carried across all halachic categories. The strictness of the category may itself affect how we define distinctness.

### Me'ilah Five Pots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּאוֹכֵל מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד מֵחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מְעִילָה, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר – מָה מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד שֶׁאֵין גּוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם תַּמְחוּיִין מוּחְלָקִין, חֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים דְּגוּפִין מוּחְלָקִין לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I have heard with regard to one who eats** meat **from one offering from five** different **pots, during one lapse of awareness, that** he is **liable** to bring a separate guilt offering **for** the meat prepared in **each and every** pot, **due to misuse** of consecrated property. **And I consider that these matters** may be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** one who eats **from one offering** prepared in five pots, **in** which case the different parts of the offering do **not** come from **differentiated physical entities,** is **liable** to bring a separate guilt offering **for** the food prepared in **each and every** pot **because** he ate from **differentiated pots,** with regard to one who eats *notar* from **five** separate **offerings, which** are **differentiated physical entities,** is it **not all the more so** logical that he is liable to bring a separate sin offering for each and every offering?

*Rabbi Yehoshua* reports a tradition: if one eats from a single *korban* but from five different *tamchuyin* — five different pots — in a single state of forgetfulness, and this occurs before the blood has been sprinkled (making it a case of *me'ilah*), one is *chayav* on each one separately. And he draws a *kal vachomer*: if even eating from one animal but five different pots — which are not *gufei m'chulakin* in the most literal sense, since it is after all the same animal — nevertheless generates five separate *chata'ot*, then eating *notar* from five distinct *zevachim*, which are genuinely different bodies, *kol sheken* should be *gufei m'chulakin* and generate five separate *korbanot*.

### Rabbi Shimon's Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: לֹא כָּךְ שָׁאַל(ו), אֶלָּא בְּאוֹכֵל (מזבח אחד) נוֹתָר מֵחֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד], מַהוּ? חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Shimon said:** It was **not that** question that Rabbi Akiva **asked** them. **Rather,** it was **with regard to one who eats *notar* from five offerings during one lapse of awareness. What is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for all** the offerings from which he ate *notar*, **or** is he **liable** to bring five sin offerings, one **for each and every one** of the offerings from which he ate *notar*? **They said to** Rabbi Akiva: **We have not heard** a ruling from our teachers in that specific case.

*Rabbi Shimon* objects to the framing that had *Rabbi Akiva* asking about *shechitah* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* answering with a case of eating. *Ma ra'ish mishchitah me'ochel* — what comparison is there between slaughter and eating? You cannot answer a question about one type of act with a proof from a fundamentally different category. *Rabbi Shimon* therefore clarifies: the original question was never about *shechitah* at all, but about eating *notar* from five different animals. The answer *Rabbi Yehoshua* gave — a tradition about eating *me'ilah* from five different pots from one animal — is thus a comparison of eating to eating, two acts of the same type, properly parallel.

The *Shita Mekubetzes* notes that the three subsequent lines in the text effectively repeat what was just said, and accordingly brackets them as a scribal duplication. Nonetheless, the structure of the argument remains clear: the question is eating *notar* from five animals, and the answer invokes eating *me'ilah* from five pots of one animal. If five different pots of one animal generates five *korbanot* for *me'ilah*, then five different animals certainly does so for *notar* — *kal vachomer*.

### Akiva's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שָׁמַעְתִּי בְּאוֹכֵל מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד בַּחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] שֶׁחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִשּׁוּם מְעִילָה, וְרוֹאֶה אֲנִי שֶׁהַדְּבָרִים קַל וָחוֹמֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua said: I have heard with regard to one who eats** meat **from one offering from five** different **pots, during one lapse of awareness, that** he is **liable** to bring a separate guilt offering **for** the meat prepared in **each and every** pot, **due to misuse** of consecrated property. **And I consider that these matters** may be derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference.

*Rabbi Akiva* responds: *im halacha nekabel* — if you have a received tradition, we accept it. But if you are arguing by logic, I can distinguish your *me'ilah* case from my *notar* case. *Me'ilah* is a stricter category; perhaps only by *me'ilah* do we regard five different pots as five distinct violations. *Notar* may be more lenient, and even five different animals might constitute only one *chiyuv*. The *Gemara* then asks: did *Rabbi Yehoshua* accept *Rabbi Akiva*'s response and concede that *notar* is more lenient than *me'ilah*, or did he hold his ground?

### Braita Notar/Me'ilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: אָכַל חָמֵשׁ חֲתִיכוֹת נוֹתָר מִזֶּבַח אֶחָד בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] בַּחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין – אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא חַטָּאת אַחַת, וְעַל לֹא הוֹדַע שֶׁלָּהֶן אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא אֶלָּא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי. מֵחֲמִשָּׁה תַּמְחוּיִין בַּחֲמִשָּׁ[ה] הֶעְלֵמוֹת – מֵבִיא חַטָּאת עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, וְעַל לֹא הוֹדַע שֶׁלָּהֶן מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. מֵחֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a proof in this regard, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If one **ate five pieces of *notar* from one offering during one lapse of awareness,** and the pieces were cooked **in five** different **pots, he brings only one sin offering. And in** a situation where it is **not known** to him with certainty whether he unwittingly ate **from them, he brings only one provisional guilt offering,** which is sacrificed by one who is uncertain whether he committed a sin that renders him liable to bring a sin offering. By contrast, if one ate *notar* from a single offering **from five** different **pots during five** separate **lapses of awareness he brings a sin offering for each and every one, and in** a situation where it is **not known** to him with certainty whether he unwittingly ate **from them he brings a provisional guilt offering for each and every one.** If he ate *notar* **from five** separate **offerings during one lapse of awareness** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering for each one, as he ate from differentiated physical entities.

To resolve this question, the *Gemara* cites a *braita*. The *braita* presents a series of cases. If one eats five pieces of *notar* from one *korban* in a single state of forgetfulness, even if those five pieces were prepared in five different pots — *eino meivi ella chatas achas*, one brings only a single *chatas*. The same applies to the doubtful case: one brings only a single *asham talui*. The *braita* thus establishes that for *notar*, five different pots from one animal is treated as a single act.

By contrast, if the same portions were eaten across five separate states of awareness — meaning the person remembered and forgot between each one — *meivi chatas al kol achas v'achas*: one brings a separate *chatas* for each, since the intervening moments of awareness distinguish the acts. This latter point is uncontroversial: remembering in between has always been recognized as separating acts.

Continuing, the *braita* addresses five different *zevachim*: if one eats *notar* from five different sacrifices in a single state of forgetfulness — *chayav al kol achas v'achas* — one is *chayav* on each. This stands against *Rabbi Akiva*'s position, since he held that even five different animals in a case of *notar* generates only one *korban*. *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda* offers the opposing view: even five different *zevachim* in one forgetfulness generates only one *chatas*, and correspondingly only one *asham talui* in the case of doubt. The general rule articulated is: wherever acts are *chalukim* (divided) for *chata'ot*, they are equally divided for *ashamot*, and where they are not, they are not.

The *braita* then turns to *me'ilah*: eating five pieces from five different *zevachim* before the blood is sprinkled — even in a single state of forgetfulness — *chayav al kol achas v'achas mishum me'ilah*: one brings five *korbanot* for *me'ilah*. The *braita* does not, however, complete the parallel by stating that a doubtful *me'ilah* obligation generates an *asham talui*.

### Braita Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ עַל סְפֵיקוֹ, מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי לָא קָתָנֵי. מַנִּי הָא? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי סֵיפָא: ״וְעַל סְפֵיקָן מֵבִיא אָשָׁם תָּלוּי״, דְּהָתְנַן: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְחַיֵּיב עַל סְפֵק מְעִילָה אָשָׁם תָּלוּי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And yet** in the final case in the *baraita*, it **does not teach** that **in** a situation where he is **uncertain** whether he ate from **it he brings a provisional guilt offering. Whose** opinion is **this? If we say** it is the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, let him also teach** in **the last clause: For** a case where he is **uncertain** whether he ate from **them he brings a provisional guilt offering, as we learned** in the mishna (22b) that **Rabbi Akiva deems** one **liable** to bring **a provisional guilt offering for uncertainty** about **misuse** of consecrated property.

The absence of any mention of an *asham talui* for doubtful *me'ilah* becomes the key to attributing this *braita*. The question is: is this *braita* the position of *Rabbi Akiva* or of *Rabbi Yehoshua*?

If it were *Rabbi Akiva*, the *braita* would have stated — consistently with the pattern it employs throughout — that a doubtful *me'ilah* obligation generates an *asham talui*. The *Mishnah* itself establishes that *Rabbi Akiva mechayev al safek me'ilah asham talui*: *Rabbi Akiva* holds one brings an *asham talui* for a doubtful violation of *me'ilah*, even though a definite *me'ilah* does not generate a *chatas* but an *asham me'ilot*. The *braita*'s silence on this point implies the opposite view — that a doubtful *me'ilah* does not generate an *asham talui*. This is not *Rabbi Akiva*'s position. Therefore, *ela lav Rabbi Yehoshua hi* — it must be *Rabbi Yehoshua*.

The significance is clear: *Rabbi Yehoshua* has conceded to *Rabbi Akiva* that *notar* is more lenient than *me'ilah*. Five different pots for *notar* generates only one *korban*, whereas five different pots for *me'ilah* generates five. One can thus read the *braita* as *Rabbi Yehoshua* accepting the distinction *Rabbi Akiva* insisted upon.

### Braita Multiple Voices

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא, וְקָתָנֵי: ״בַּחֲמִשָּׁה הֶעְלֵמוֹת – מֵבִיא חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת״, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא אַדְּרַבָּה, מִסֵּיפָא, דְּקָתָנֵי: מֵחֲמִשָּׁה זְבָחִים אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד] – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָא קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, isn't** the *baraita* in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua,** who disagrees with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva and holds that one is not liable to bring a provisional guilt offering for uncertainty about misuse of consecrated property? **And it teaches** that one who eats *notar* from one offering cooked in five pots **during five** separate **lapses of awareness brings five sin offerings,** which indicates that if he did so during a single lapse of awareness he would be liable to bring only one sin offering, in accordance with the opinion stated by Rabbi Akiva in the mishna. **And** one can **conclude from** here that Rabbi Yehoshua **accepted** the refutation **from** Rabbi Akiva. The Gemara responds: **But on the contrary,** one can infer otherwise **from the latter clause** of the *baraita*, **which teaches:** If he ate **from five** separate **offerings, even during one lapse of awareness,** he is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one.** One can **conclude from** this statement that Rabbi Yehoshua **did not accept** the refutation **from** Rabbi Akiva.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא מַאי אִית [לָךְ] לְמֵימַר, תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּאִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּקַיבְּלַהּ וְאִיכָּא תַּנָּא דְּלָא קַיבְּלַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is there to say** about this *baraita*? It must be said that **it is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, that there is a *tanna*** who maintains **that** Rabbi Yehoshua **accepted** Rabbi Akiva's refutation, **and there is a *tanna*** who maintains **that** Rabbi Yehoshua **did not accept it.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, וְהַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, סָבַר לַהּ כְּוָתֵיהּ בְּהַעֲלָמָה, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בִּמְעִילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** But once the *baraita* is interpreted in accordance with two different *tanna'im*, **you** may **even say** that the earlier clause of the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** and not Rabbi Yehoshua. **And this *tanna*** of the *baraita* **holds in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Akiva **in one** matter **and disagrees with his** opinion **in one** matter: **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Akiva **with regard to a lapse of awareness,** that if one eats from five offerings during a single lapse of awareness he is liable to bring only one sin offering. **But he disagrees with his** opinion **with regard to misuse** of consecrated property, as he does not deem one liable to bring a provisional guilt offering for uncertainty about whether he misused consecrated property.

The *Gemara* immediately qualifies this inference. The *braita*'s *seifa* — specifically the opinion that eating *notar* from five different *zevachim* generates five *korbanot* — is inconsistent with *Rabbi Akiva*'s position, which would say that even five different animals is only one *korban* for *notar*. Since the *braita* contains at least one opinion that says five animals is five *korbanot*, and *Rabbi Akiva* would say one, it follows that the *braita* does not entirely reflect *Rabbi Akiva*'s view — and therefore cannot straightforwardly prove that *Rabbi Yehoshua* accepted from *Rabbi Akiva*.

The *Gemara* resolves this by saying *tannai hi*: there are multiple voices within this *braita*. There is the *stam* of the *braita* — which the *Gemara* associates with one position — and the explicit opinion of *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda*, which represents another. One voice holds that even five different animals is only one *korban* for *notar* (consistent with *Rabbi Akiva*'s acceptance), while the other holds it is five (suggesting *Rabbi Yehoshua* did not fully concede). The *Gemara* then adds: given that we must admit there are multiple tannaitic voices within the *braita* in any case, one may say the *braita* could even reflect *Rabbi Akiva*'s general framework — with the *tanna* agreeing with *Rabbi Akiva* on the question of *he'elam* (that forgetting separates acts), while disagreeing with *Rabbi Akiva* on whether doubt about *me'ilah* generates an *asham talui*. That latter point does not necessarily undermine *Rabbi Akiva*'s authorship of the rest.

What emerges is a complex picture: within the *braita* itself, the boundary between *Rabbi Akiva*'s and *Rabbi Yehoshua*'s positions is not sharply delineated, and the question of whether *Rabbi Yehoshua* ultimately accepted *Rabbi Akiva*'s distinction between *me'ilah* and *notar* remains unresolved. The consensus position, however, is that for *me'ilah* five different pots generates five *korbanot*, while for *notar* and other violations five different pots generates only one, and the remaining question — whether five different animals for *notar* is one or five — is itself a *machloket* embedded in the *braita*.

### Shmuel's Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי חָמֵשׁ מְעִילוֹת? אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּאוֹתָהּ שֶׁשָּׁנִינוּ: חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים בָּעוֹלָה מִצְטָרְפִין: הַבָּשָׂר, וְהֶחָלָב, וְהַיַּיִן, וְהַסֹּלֶת, וְהַשֶּׁמֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one eats five pieces from a single offering, each of which was cooked in a separate pot, he is liable to bring five guilt offerings for misuse of consecrated property. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** which require **five** guilt offerings for **misuse** of consecrated property? **Shmuel says:** The five pieces must be from separate parts of the offering, **like that which we learned** in a mishna (*Me'ila* 15b) with regard to a different topic: **Five items from a burnt offering combine** to form an olive-bulk that renders one liable for sacrificing an offering outside the Temple: **The meat, and the** forbidden **fat, and the wine** from the libations of the burnt offering, **and the fine flour** from the meal offering brought with the libations, **and the oil** for mixing with the meal offering.

Having established that by *me'ilah* at least, five different pots generates five *korbanot*, the *Gemara* turns to define what exactly counts as "five different pots" in the context of *me'ilah*. *Heichi dami me'ilah* — what is the concrete case?

*Shmuel* says: as we taught, there are five things in an *olah* that combine for a *kazayit* toward the prohibition of burning outside the Temple. These are *habasar, hachelev, hayayin, hasoles, vehashemen* — the meat, the fat, the wine, the flour, and the oil of the accompanying *mincha*. *Shmuel*'s position is that the "five different pots" refers to literally five different types of food — meat, fat, wine, flour, oil — not five different preparations of the same substance. On this reading, the *chiddush* is moderate: of course five genuinely different foodstuffs, even if all associated with one *olah*, are considered five different acts.

### Amoraic Opinions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חִזְקִיָּה אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל מֵחֲמִשָּׁה אֵבָרִים. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא בְּאֵבֶר אֶחָד, מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּכָתֵף. רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל בַּחֲמִשָּׁה מִינֵי קְדֵירָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁאָכַל בַּחֲמִשָּׁה טְעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya says:** The obligation to bring five guilt offerings applies in a case **where he ate from five** different **limbs** of one offering. **Reish Lakish says: You** may **even say** that he ate **from one limb,** and **you find** this **in** a case where he ate from **the shoulder** of an offering, which has several different sections. **Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa says:** It applies in a case **where he ate** pieces of meat prepared **as five** different **types of cooked dishes. Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It applies in a case **where he ate** five pieces of meat that had **five** different **flavors,** as they were prepared with different types of seasonings.

The *Amoraim* offer increasingly expansive definitions. *Chizkiya* says the case is eating from five different limbs of the animal — a foreleg, a thigh, the ribs, and so on — literally distinct anatomical sections, all meat, but from genuinely different parts of the animal. *Reish Lakish* goes further: *afilu teima b'eiver echad meshkachat lah b'katef* — even within a single limb, such as the shoulder, one can identify five distinct sections, and eating from those different sections would constitute five separate acts. This is a striking position: even if it is all meat from the same limb, the different sub-sections of the shoulder are treated as sufficiently distinct for *me'ilah*.

*Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha* takes yet another approach: the case is five different pots in the sense of five different preparations — one spiced with onions, another with garlic, and so on, all from the same type of meat but seasoned or prepared differently. *Rabbi Yochanan* defines it as five different tastes — meaning the meat was prepared through five different methods of cooking: roasting, boiling, and so forth. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* differ on which of these latter two opinions corresponds to which definition, reversing the assignment, but the substance of the range is clear.

The spectrum of opinions is illuminating. At the most conservative end, *Shmuel* limits the multiplicity of sin offerings to a case of genuinely different foodstuffs — meat, fat, wine, flour, oil. At the most expansive end, *Reish Lakish* finds sufficient distinction within the same limb of the same animal. In between, *Chizkiya* requires different limbs, while *Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha* and *Rabbi Yochanan* focus on different preparations or tastes. Notably, the more restrictive position (*Shmuel*) would not yield much by way of *notar*, since *notar* in the technical sense applies to the meat and fat but raises questions regarding the accompanying *shemen* and *soles* of the *mincha*. In any case, all of these definitions apply in the domain of *me'ilah*, where we are in any case more stringent; for *notar* and other categories, the standard would not be met even by the most expansive definitions above, and even five different animals would likely generate only one *korban* — except according to the one opinion in the *braita* that disputes this.

## Shabbat Melachot

### Akiva's Shabbat Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: שָׁאַלְתִּי אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה אַחַת בְּהֶעְלֵם (אַחַת) [אֶחָד], מַהוּ? חַיָּיב אַחַת עַל כּוּלָּן, אוֹ חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Akiva said: I asked Rabbi Eliezer with regard to** one who **performs multiple** prohibited **labors on several *Shabbatot*,** and all those labors were **subsumed** as subcategories of **one** primary category of prohibited **labor,** and he performed them **during one lapse of awareness. What is** the *halakha*? Is he **liable** to bring **one** sin offering **for** unwitting performance of **all** these labors **or** is he **liable** to bring a sin offering **for** violation of **each and every one** of the labors?

The *Gemara* transitions to a parallel inquiry that *Rabbi Akiva* directed at *Rabbi Eliezer* — almost certainly not at the same marketplace setting, but reported here because of its structural similarity to the earlier debate. *Rabbi Akiva* asks: if one performs many different *melachos* that are all *me'ein melacha achas* — all of the same category, i.e., multiple *toldos* (subcategory acts) of a single *av melacha* such as *zri'ah* (planting) — over multiple *Shabbatot*, in a single state of forgetfulness, how many *chata'ot* does one bring? This combines two separate questions: are multiple *toldos* of the same *av* considered distinct acts? And does doing the same *melacha* across multiple *Shabbatot* generate separate obligations per *Shabbos*?

### Forgetting Shabbat vs Me…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* גְּמָ׳ מַאי קָבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ? אִי שַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין וְאִי לָא בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, נִיבְעֵי: הָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכָה אַחַת בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What** precise **dilemma** did Rabbi Akiva **raise** before Rabbi Eliezer? **If he raised a dilemma** about **whether** different ***Shabbatot* are comparable to** separate **entities or not,** i.e., if performing one labor on several *Shabbatot* during one lapse of awareness is similar to engaging in intercourse with multiple menstruating women during one lapse of awareness, **let him raise** the **dilemma** as follows: If **one performs one** prohibited **labor on** each of **several *Shabbatot*** during a single lapse of awareness, what is the *halakha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: וַלְדֵי מְלָאכוֹת אִי כְּאָבוֹת דָּמְיָין וְאִי לָא בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ, נִיבְעֵי מִינֵּיהּ: הָעוֹשֶׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה מֵעֵין מְלָאכָה אַחַת בַּשַּׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** perhaps **he raised a dilemma** about **whether** different **subcategories of** a single prohibited **labor are comparable** to different **primary categories,** which would mean one is liable separately for each, **or not.** But if so, **let him ask raise** the **dilemma** in the following manner: In the case of **one who performs multiple** prohibited **labors,** all **subsumed** as subcategories of **one** primary category of **labor, on** a single **Shabbat,** during a single lapse of awareness, what is the *halakha*? Why did Rabbi Akiva specify a case of one who performed multiple prohibited labors on multiple *Shabbatot*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב, תַּרְתֵּי בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ: שַׁבָּתוֹת כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין וְאִי לָא בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ, וַלְדֵי מְלָאכוֹת כִּמְלָאכוֹת דָּמְיָין אִי לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: They say** in **the school of Rav [*bei Rav*]** that Rabbi Akiva **raised two dilemmas before him: He raised a dilemma** about **whether *Shabbatot* are comparable** to separate **entities or whether** they are **not** comparable to separate entities, and he also raised a dilemma about whether different **subcategories of** a single prohibited **labor are comparable** to different primary categories of **labor,** or **whether** they are **not.** Rabbi Eliezer answered that multiple *Shabbatot* are comparable to separate entities, and that one who performs different subcategories of a single category of prohibited labor is like one who performs multiple primary categories of labor.

*Rava* resolves this by citing a statement of the scholars of *Rav*'s academy: *tarti ba'i minei* — he was asking both questions simultaneously. The first: are different *Shabbatot* like different *gufin m'chulakin*, each generating its own *chatas*? The second: are different *toldos* treated like different *avos*, each generating its own *chatas*? Both questions were implicit in the single inquiry.

One should also be aware at this point that the two questions are not entirely symmetric. For *Shabbos* violations, there is a further complication introduced by the category of what one forgot: one who forgot *that it was Shabbos* is in a different position from one who forgot *which melachos are forbidden on Shabbos*. This distinction will bear heavily on the resolution.

### Eliezer's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה נִדָּה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה וְחַטָּאוֹת הַרְבֵּה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת, שַׁבָּת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ תּוֹצָאוֹת הַרְבֵּה וּמִיתוֹת הַרְבֵּה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to** Rabbi Akiva: He is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for** violation of **each and every one** of the labors, and this is derived **from an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** in the case of **a menstruating woman, with regard to whom there are not multiple** actions that **result** in transgression **and** that result in **multiple sin offerings,** but rather only the prohibition against engaging in intercourse with her, and nevertheless **one** is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for each and every one** of his acts of unwitting intercourse; in the case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there are multiple** primary categories and subcategories of labor that **result** in transgression **and** that result in **multiple death** penalties or sin offerings, **is it not right that he will be liable** to bring a sin offering **for** performance of **each and every one** of the prohibited labors?

*Rabbi Eliezer* answers: *chayav al kol achas v'achas mikal vachomer* — one is *chayav* for each separate *Shabbos*, and he derives this from a *kal vachomer* based on *niddah*. The *kal vachomer* proceeds as follows. *Niddah* does not contain multiple subcategories (*toldos*) and does not generate multiple *chata'ot* on the basis of different types of violations. Yet a man who has relations with five different women who are *niddot* in a single state of forgetfulness brings five separate *korbanot* — because different women are *gufei m'chulakin*. *Shabbos*, by contrast, does contain multiple categories: a person who violates different *avos melachos* brings different *chata'ot*, a feature not present in *niddah*. If *niddah*, with its conceptually simpler structure, already generates multiple *chata'ot* when different people are involved, then *Shabbos* — which already has a built-in structure of multiplicity — *kol sheken* should generate multiple *chata'ot* for different *Shabbatot*.

### Objects vs Subjects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: אִם אָמַרְתָּ בַּנִּדָּה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׁתֵּי אַזְהָרוֹת, שֶׁהוּא מוּזְהָר עַל הַנִּדָּה וְהַנִּדָּה מוּזְהֶרֶת עָלָיו, תֹּאמַר בַּשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה אֶחָת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva continues: **I said to** Rabbi Eliezer that the inference is not valid: **If you said** one is liable to bring multiple sin offerings **in the** case of **a menstruating woman, with regard to whom there are two prohibitions, as** the man **is prohibited** from engaging in intercourse **with the menstruating woman and the menstruating woman is prohibited** from engaging in intercourse **with him, would you say** the same **in the** case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there is only one prohibition?**

*Rabbi Akiva* responds with what is, on reflection, a conceptually penetrating objection. He says: *im amarta beniddah sheyesh bah shtei azharos* — by *niddah* there are two prohibitions. The man is forbidden to have relations with a *niddah*, and the *niddah* herself is forbidden to have relations with him. *Tomar beShabbos she'ein bo ela azharah achas* — by *Shabbos* there is only one prohibition. The person is forbidden to perform *melacha* on *Shabbos*, but *Shabbos* cannot itself be prohibited from anything. *Shabbos* is the object being violated, not a participant in the violation.

This is a fundamental conceptual point. The reason different women constitute *gufei m'chulakin* in *niddah* is not merely that they are different objects — like five different pieces of *cheilev* — but that each woman is herself a *subject*, a participant in the prohibited act. Both parties are prohibited. The woman defines the act as fundamentally different because she is an actor, not merely an object. When there are two subjects in a prohibited act, different instances of that act involving different subjects are genuinely different sins. But *Shabbos* is never a subject; it is always the object being violated. Therefore, violating five different *Shabbatot* is not like engaging in prohibited relations with five different women — it is more like eating five different pieces of *cheilev*, which is one sin repeated, not five different sins.

### Minor Women Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִי: הַבָּא עַל הַקְּטַנּוֹת יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא אַזְהָרָה אַחַת, וְחַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאֶחָת!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to me:** The *halakha* of **one who engages in intercourse with minor** menstruating **girls will prove** this refutation is not valid, **as in** that case **there is only one prohibition,** because the minor is exempt from the mitzvot, **and** nevertheless the man is **liable** to bring a separate sin offering **for** intercourse for **each and every one** of the acts of intercourse.

*Rabbi Eliezer* challenges this framework by raising the case of a man who has relations with multiple minor women who are *niddot*. A minor is not halachically obligated; she cannot be said to be a subject in the same sense as an adult. Yet *Rabbi Eliezer* assumes — and this is the premise of his challenge — that even in that case the man is *chayav* on each one separately. If so, it cannot be the woman's status as a subject that explains the multiplicity of obligations: even when the woman is effectively an object, different women generate different *chata'ot*.

### Akiva on Minors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: אִם אָמַרְתָּ בַּקְּטַנּוֹת, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּהֶן עַכְשָׁיו – יֵשׁ בָּהֶן לְאַחַר זְמַן, תֹּאמַר בַּשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ עַכְשָׁיו וְלֹא לְאַחַר זְמַן?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva said: **I said to** Rabbi Eliezer that the cases of Shabbat and minor menstruating girls are not comparable. **If you said in the** case of **minor girls that although it is not** prohibited **for them at present it is** prohibited **for them after** the passage of **time,** when they reach majority, **would you say** the same **in the** case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there are neither** two prohibitions **at present, nor** will there be **after** the passage of **time?**

*Rabbi Akiva* responds: *lo, im amarta betinokos she'af al pi she'ein bah achshav yesh bah le'achar zman* — by minors, even though she is not presently obligated, she will be obligated in the future. The halachic definition of the act of sexual relations is inherently defined by two participants. Even if in a specific case one of the participants is currently exempt — because she is a minor — the fundamental nature of the act is as a two-person act, both parties of whom are subjects under normal circumstances. Therefore, different women are always *gufei m'chulakin* because the act of *niddah* is constitutionally defined by two participating subjects.

### Shabbat as Object

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֹּאמַר בַּשַּׁבָּת, שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ עַכְשָׁיו וְלֹא לְאַחַר זְמַן?
>
> **Translation:** **would you say** the same **in the** case of **Shabbat, with regard to which there are neither** two prohibitions **at present, nor** will there be **after** the passage of **time?**

The contrast with *Shabbos* is then explicit: *tomar beShabbos she'ein bah lo achshav velo le'achar zman* — by *Shabbos*, it is not only that *Shabbos* is not currently a participant; it will never be a participant, at no point in time. *Shabbos* is permanently an object of the violation, never a subject. The act of violating *Shabbos* is therefore fundamentally different in kind from the act of prohibited relations: it is a one-person act against an object, not a two-person act between subjects. And since the object being violated — *Shabbos* — is never a subject, different *Shabbatot* do not constitute *gufei m'chulakin*. Violating this *Shabbos* and violating next *Shabbos* is, conceptually, like eating one piece of *cheilev* and then another: the same sin repeated, not two fundamentally different sins.

### Animal Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לִי: הַבָּא עַל הַבְּהֵמָה יוֹכִיחַ! אָמַרְתִּי לוֹ: הַבְּהֵמָה כַּשַּׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer **said to me:** The *halakha* of **one who copulates with an animal will prove** this refutation is not valid, as there are never two prohibitions in that case, and nevertheless the person is liable to bring a sin offering for each and every act. Rabbi Akiva said: **I said to** Rabbi Eliezer that no proof can be cited from the case of an animal, as in my opinion the case of **the animal is just like** that of **Shabbat;** there is uncertainty with regard to both cases.

*Rabbi Eliezer* presses further: *haba al habeheimah yochiach* — let the case of relations with an animal prove the point. An animal is always an object; it is never a subject under any halachic framework, and it never will be. Yet *Rabbi Eliezer* assumes that relations with five different animals generates five separate *chata'ot* — that different animals are *gufei m'chulakin* even though the animal is purely an object. If so, *Rabbi Akiva*'s distinction between subjects and objects cannot be the decisive factor.

*Rabbi Akiva* responds with a single phrase: *habeheimah keShabbos* — the animal is just like *Shabbos*. The phrase is deliberately ambiguous: is he saying that just as he argued *Shabbos* is only one sin, so too relations with different animals is only one sin? Or is he conceding that just as different animals generate multiple *chata'ot*, so too different *Shabbatot* should? The *pshat* of the *Mishnah* supports the first reading: *Rabbi Akiva* is consistent in his position that when the violated entity is an object — whether *Shabbos* or an animal — the same sin repeated is one sin, not multiple. Just as he argues different *Shabbatot* is one sin, so too relations with different animals he would view as one sin repeated.

## Rava's Analysis

### Days as Separation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁבָּתוֹת, הֵיכִי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ? שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת – פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם הָוְיָין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another question with regard to the mishna: **And** in connection to the performance of the same category of prohibited labor on each of several ***Shabbatot*** during a single lapse of awareness, **what** is the precise case about which Rabbi Akiva **raises** his **dilemma?** If the individual was **unwitting** in that he was unaware that it was **Shabbat but** acted **intentionally** with regard to the prohibited **labors,** i.e., he knew that these labors are prohibited on Shabbat, perhaps it is **obvious to** Rabbi Akiva **that** the **intervening days** constitute **an awareness** of his sin, which serves **to differentiate** between the *Shabbatot* and render the individual liable to bring separate sin offerings for each Shabbat.

*Rava* now clarifies which of the two possible formulations of *Rabbi Akiva*'s question is correct. The first possibility: *Rabbi Akiva*'s question was in a case of *shiggagas Shabbos v'zadon melachos* — one forgot it was *Shabbos* but knew that the *melachos* were forbidden — and the question was whether the passing days between *Shabbatot* constitute a *yedia l'chalek*, an intervening awareness that distinguishes the acts. If so, the *kal vachomer* from *niddah* and the entire debate about whether different *Shabbatot* are *gufin damyan* would be irrelevant; the relevant question would simply be about the passage of time.

*Rava* states: *mistavra* — it makes sense — that in this scenario, *Rabbi Akiva* would readily concede that the days in between are indeed treated as if one had become aware. The six days between one *Shabbos* and the next make it as though one should have remembered, and this intervening quasi-awareness separates the acts. This point, in *Rava*'s analysis, *Rabbi Akiva* takes for granted and does not require a *machloket*.

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מִסְתַּבְּרָא שִׁגְגַת שַׁבָּת וּזְדוֹן מְלָאכוֹת פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּיָמִים שֶׁבֵּינְתַיִם הָוַיִין יְדִיעָה לְחַלֵּק, וּזְדוֹן שַׁבָּת וְשִׁגְגַת מְלָאכוֹת הוּא דְּבָעֵי מִינֵּיהּ – כְּגוּפִין דָּמְיָין, אוֹ לָאו.
>
> **Translation:** **It stands to reason,** based on a proof from a mishna, to say that in a case where he acts **unwittingly** in that he does not know that it is **Shabbat, and intentionally** in that he knows that the **labors** he is performing are prohibited on Shabbat, it is **obvious to** Rabbi Akiva **that** the **intervening days** between the *Shabbatot* **are** considered **awareness** of his sin, which would serve **to differentiate** between one sin and the next. **And it is** in reference to a case where he acts **intentionally** with regard to **Shabbat and unwittingly** with regard to prohibited **labors that** Rabbi Akiva **raises his dilemma,** which is about whether the *Shabbatot* are **comparable** to separate **entities or not.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, כְּלָל גָּדוֹל אָמְרוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** As we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 67b): The Sages **stated a significant principle with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat:** With regard to **anyone who forgets the essence of Shabbat,** i.e., one who is entirely ignorant that labor is prohibited on Shabbat, **and** he **performed multiple** prohibited **labors on multiple *Shabbatot*, he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, as there is only a single, comprehensive lapse of awareness.

As evidence for this framework, *Rava* invokes the well-known general principle from the beginning of the seventh *perek* of *Shabbos*: *kol hashocheach ikar Shabbos* — one who forgets the very concept of *Shabbos*, such as a person who grew up without ever being taught of its existence, and who performs many different *melachos* on many different *Shabbatot* — *eino chayav ela achas*. One brings only a single *chatas*, because the mistake was a single, undifferentiated unawareness. There were not multiple distinct forgettings; the person simply never knew. This case establishes the floor: when there is truly one uninterrupted state of ignorance with no basis for intervening awareness, there is one *korban* regardless of how many *Shabbatot* pass or how many *melachos* are performed.

### Forgetting Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיּוֹדֵעַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת. הַיּוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא שַׁבָּת וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַב מְלָאכָה וּמְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who knows the essence of** the prohibition against labor on **Shabbat, and** he **performed multiple** prohibited **labors on multiple *Shabbatot*** because he was unaware that those days were Shabbat, he is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every Shabbat,** as the intervening days are considered awareness which differentiates between the *Shabbatot*. **One who is aware that** the day **is Shabbat and performed multiple** prohibited **labors,** which he did not know were prohibited, **on multiple *Shabbatot*,** is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each and every primary category of labor** that he performed.

*Hayodeia ikar Shabbos* — by contrast, if one knows the concept of *Shabbos* but forgets which day is *Shabbos* — and performs many *melachos* on many *Shabbatot* — *chayav al kol Shabbos v'Shabbos*: one brings a separate *chatas* for each *Shabbos*. The reason is that in this case the very sin was forgetting which day is *Shabbos*, and the passage of six days between one *Shabbos* and the next is treated as an intervening awareness: one should have recalibrated over the course of a week. Therefore each *Shabbos* is a separate sin, not because different *Shabbatot* are *gufin m'chulakin*, but because the time elapsed serves as *yedia l'chalek*.

*Hayodeia shehu Shabbos* — and if one knew it was *Shabbos* but forgot which *melachos* are forbidden — and performs many *melachos* on many *Shabbatot* — *chayav al kol av melacha umelacha*: one brings a *chatas* for each distinct *av melacha* performed. Here the mistake was not about *Shabbos* itself but about each individual *melacha*; each *melacha* was a separate forgetting, and therefore each generates its own *korban*.

### Why Not Multiplied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ ״חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַב מְלָאכָה שֶׁל כׇּל שַׁבָּת וְשַׁבָּת״ לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba comments: **But** in the latter case, the mishna **does not teach** that he is **liable** to bring a sin offering **for each** act of **a primary category of labor** that he performed **on each and every Shabbat.** He is liable to bring one sin offering for each primary category of labor, despite the fact that he performed the act on multiple *Shabbatot*. This indicates that the different *Shabbatot* are not considered separate entities.

The critical observation is that this last formulation — *chayav al kol av melacha umelacha* — does not say *chayav al kol av melacha umelacha times each Shabbos*. One does not multiply the number of *melachos* by the number of *Shabbatot* and bring a *korban* for each combination. Why not? If one forgot that *zri'ah* was forbidden, and one planted on five successive *Shabbatot*, why should that be treated as five repetitions of one sin rather than five separate sins?

The answer *Rava* draws from the structure of the *Mishnah* is precisely *Rabbi Akiva*'s position: *Shabbos* is not *gufin m'chulakin*. Different *Shabbatot*, unlike different women in *niddah* violations, are not to be treated as distinct objects that define distinct acts. Violating the prohibition of *zri'ah* on five different *Shabbatot* is halachically equivalent to violating it five times on the same *Shabbos* — one sin repeated, not five different sins. The *Shabbos* itself, being an object and never a subject, does not differentiate the act.

In the case where one forgot *which day* was *Shabbos*, by contrast, the days in between create separating awareness — *yedia l'chalek* — which is a different mechanism entirely. It is not that different *Shabbatot* are *gufin m'chulakin*; it is that the temporal gap creates the equivalent of a remembered interval between sins.

### Final Synthesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דִּתְנַן, כְּלָל גָּדוֹל אָמְרוּ בַּשַּׁבָּת: כׇּל הַשּׁוֹכֵחַ עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת, וְעָשָׂה מְלָאכוֹת הַרְבֵּה בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת הַרְבֵּה – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: From where do I say this? As we learned** in a mishna (*Shabbat* 67b): The Sages **stated a significant principle with regard to** the *halakhot* of **Shabbat:** With regard to **anyone who forgets the essence of Shabbat,** i.e., one who is entirely ignorant that labor is prohibited on Shabbat, **and** he **performed multiple** prohibited **labors on multiple *Shabbatot*, he is liable** to bring **only one** sin offering, as there is only a single, comprehensive lapse of awareness.

*Rava* thus frames the entire debate between *Rabbi Akiva* and *Rabbi Eliezer* as follows. The question *Rabbi Akiva* posed was in the case of *zadon Shabbos v'shiggagas melachos* — one who remembered it was *Shabbos* but forgot the *melachos*. In this configuration, the days between *Shabbatot* are irrelevant to creating separating awareness (since the issue was never which day is *Shabbos*), and the only operative question is whether different *Shabbatot* constitute *gufin m'chulakin*. *Rabbi Eliezer* said yes, deriving this from *niddah* via *kal vachomer*. *Rabbi Akiva* said no, distinguishing *niddah* — in which both parties are subjects — from *Shabbos*, which is permanently an object of the violation. *Rabbi Akiva* also did not accept *Rabbi Eliezer*'s parallel claim that multiple *toldos* of the same *av* generate multiple *korbanot*; the end of the *Mishnah* in *Shabbos* establishes this as the accepted position, ruling that one who performs many *melachos* all of the same category brings only one *chatas*.

The *Mishnah* in *Shabbos* thus encodes *Rabbi Akiva*'s position on both questions. Different *toldos* of one *av*: one *korban*. Different *Shabbatot* where the sin was forgetting the *melachos*: not multiplied by the number of *Shabbatot*, only by the number of *melachos* forgotten — because *Shabbos* is not *gufin m'chulakin*. And different *Shabbatot* where the sin was forgetting which day was *Shabbos*: one per *Shabbos*, not because of *gufin m'chulakin*, but because the six intervening days are treated as an interval of restored awareness.
