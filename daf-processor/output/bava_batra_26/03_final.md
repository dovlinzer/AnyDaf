# Bava Batra 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Trees & Cisterns

### Mishna: Distances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הָאִילָן מִן הַבּוֹר עֶשְׂרִים וְחָמֵשׁ אַמָּה, וּבְחָרוּב וּבַשִּׁקְמָה – חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה; בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָה בֵּין מִן הַצַּד. אִם הַבּוֹר קָדְמָה – קוֹצֵץ וְנוֹתֵן דָּמִים, וְאִם אִילָן קָדַם – לֹא יָקוֹץ. סָפֵק זֶה קָדַם וְסָפֵק זֶה קָדַם – לֹא יָקוֹץ. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהַבּוֹר קוֹדֶמֶת לָאִילָן – לֹא יָקוֹץ, שֶׁזֶּה חוֹפֵר בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ וְזֶה נוֹטֵעַ בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One must distance a tree twenty-five cubits from a cistern, and in the case of a carob and of a sycamore tree,** whose roots extend farther, one must distance the tree **fifty cubits. Whether the cistern or tree is located above or to the side** of the other. **If the digging of the cistern preceded** the tree, the owner of the tree **cuts down** the tree **and** the owner of the cistern **pays** him **money. And if the tree preceded** the cistern the owner of the tree need **not cut down** the tree. If it is **uncertain whether this came first or that came first,** the owner of the tree need **not cut down** the tree. **Rabbi Yosei says: Even if the cistern preceded the tree,** the owner of the tree need **not cut down** the tree. This is due to the fact **that this one digs in his own** property, **and that one plants in his own** property.

The daf opens by picking up from the *mishnah* of 25b, which continues the general theme of maintaining distances between neighbors' properties — but with a particular focus that brings us to the essential intersection of Bava Batra and Bava Kamma: when does causing indirect harm to a neighbor rise to the level of making one a *mazik*, and how do the *arba avot nezikin* bear on that question?

The *mishnah* states: *marchikin et ha'ilan min habor esrim vechamesh amah* — one must keep a tree at least 25 *amot* from a cistern, because the roots will spread and damage it. For a carob tree or a sycamore, the distance is 50 *amot*, since those roots spread even further. This rule applies anywhere — not just near a town, where distancing requirements have more to do with zoning and the appearance of the city, but even in one's own field. The concern here is entirely about not causing indirect damage to a neighbor's property.

The *mishnah* adds: *bein milmalah bein min hatsad* — it does not matter whether the configuration is on an incline or on level ground. The question of what *milmalah* (above) means — whether it refers to the tree being higher or the cistern — will be addressed in the *Gemara*.

### Compensation Rules

*Im habor kadam kotzetz venoten damim* — if the cistern was dug first, then the person who subsequently planted the tree was in violation of the distancing rule. In that case, he must cut down the tree, but the owner of the cistern must reimburse him for it. This is, notably, less strict than the rule we encountered in the previous *mishnah* concerning trees planted too close to a town: there, the town need not pay anything. A neighbor has fewer rights than the community.

But the question naturally arises: if the tree-planter was in violation, why must the cistern-owner pay at all? Part of the answer is that even though the planting was improper, the actual damage only occurs after many years — the roots take considerable time to spread and reach the cistern. Tosafot makes this point: the delayed nature of the harm creates a kind of balance between the violation and the compensation owed.

### Proof & Uncertainty

*Im ilan kadam lo yikotz* — if the tree was planted first, then of course the cistern-owner has no right to demand its removal. *Safek zeh kadam vesafek zeh kadam lo yikotz* — if the chronological priority is genuinely uncertain, the tree is not cut down. This follows the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah*: since the cistern-owner cannot prove his right to demand the tree's removal, he cannot compel it.

### Rabbi Yosi's View

*Rabbi Yosi omer: af al pi shehabor kodem la'ilan lo yikotz* — Rabbi Yosi takes a radical position: even if the cistern predates the tree, the tree need not be cut down. His reasoning: *shezeh chofer betoch shelo vezeh note'ah betoch shelo* — each party is acting within his own property, and as the *Gemara* frames it elsewhere, *al hanizak leharichik et atzmo* — it is the responsibility of the injured party to distance himself from the source of harm.

This position goes against the entire thrust of the *perek*. Rabbi Yosi is essentially the outlier, the one who seems to reject all the distancing requirements on principle: you are the master of your domain, and indirect damage that befalls a neighbor is not your concern. If a neighbor does not wish to be harmed indirectly, he must take whatever precautions he sees fit.

One might ask how the opening rule of the *mishnah* — the 25-*amah* requirement — fits with Rabbi Yosi's view. The answer is that Rabbi Yosi simply does not hold of that requirement. And practically speaking, if a tree is already 26 *amot* away, everyone agrees: there is no claim against the tree's owner.

### Incline Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: בֵּין שֶׁהַבּוֹר לְמַטָּה וְאִילָן לְמַעְלָה, בֵּין שֶׁהַבּוֹר לְמַעְלָה וְאִילָן לְמַטָּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא בּוֹר לְמַטָּה וְאִילָן לְמַעְלָה – קָא אָזְלִין שׇׁרָשִׁים מַזְּקִי לַהּ לְבוֹר. אֶלָּא בּוֹר לְמַעְלָה וְאִילָן לְמַטָּה, אַמַּאי? אָמַר רַבִּי חַגָּא בְּשֵׁם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּחְלִידִין אֶת הַקַּרְקַע וּמַלְקִין קַרְקָעִיתָהּ שֶׁל בּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **A Sage taught:** This is the *halakha* **whether the cistern is below and the tree is above, or whether the cistern is above and the tree is below. Granted,** if the **cistern is below and the tree is above,** it will cause damage, as **the roots extend and damage the cistern.** **But** if **the cistern is above and the tree is below, why** should he have to distance the tree, considering that the roots extend downward? **Rabbi Ḥagga says in the name of Rabbi Yosei:** He must distance the tree **because** its roots **form holes in the ground and ruin the floor of the cistern.**

The *Tanna* clarifies: it does not matter whether the tree is higher on the incline and the cistern is lower, or whether the cistern is higher and the tree is lower. The first case — *bor lematah ve'ilan lemalah* — is intuitive: roots grow downward through the ground and penetrate the cistern directly. The second case — *bor lemalah ve'ilan lematah* — requires explanation.

*Amar Rav Chaga beshem Rabi Yosi*: in that case, the roots *machlidin et hakarka* — they loosen and corrode the ground (the word *machlid* relates to *chaluda*, rust, the sense of structural deterioration) — and ultimately weaken the very foundation of the cistern, even without penetrating it directly. The distance requirement therefore applies in both configurations.

## Rabbi Yosi Accepted

### Law Follows Yosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוָאן בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲוָה אָמְרִינַן: מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּגִירֵי דִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rav Ashi said: When we were** studying **in the study hall of Rav Kahana, we would say** that **Rabbi Yosei concedes with regard to one's arrows,** i.e., one must distance his activities from his neighbor if his actions will cause immediate damage to his neighbor, even if he is acting on his own property.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: halacha keRabbi Yosi.* This is a striking ruling. We have spent an entire *perek* building up a framework of distancing rules, and Rabbi Yosi is the outlier who rejects them all on principle — and in the end, we embrace him. The *halacha* follows Rabbi Yosi.

From a strictly technical perspective, Rabbi Yosi's position is actually quite defensible. The second *perek* of Bava Batra is, in a sense, rabbinic policy: from a Bava Kamma standpoint, there is no direct *mazik* in most of these cases. One plants a tree, and years later roots penetrate a neighbor's cistern — there is nothing immediate, nothing that communicates directly from one person to another. The damage is indirect, deferred, *grama* extended over time. Rabbi Yosi stands on the technical rights and rejects the policy overlay. The Rabbis of the *perek* were creating norms necessary for neighbors to coexist — smoke pollution, structural threats, and the like — but Rabbi Yosi insists that without a technical *chiyuv*, one cannot be compelled to act differently within one's own property.

### Direct Actions

*Amar Rav Ashi: ki havina bei Rav Kahana hava amrinan — modi Rabbi Yosi begirei didei.* Now the *Gemara* begins to limit the ruling: Rabbi Yosi himself would agree that where the harm is a direct product of one's own actions — *girei didei*, literally one's own arrows — one is obligated to keep distance.

The paradigm case of *girei didei* is straightforward: if I shoot an arrow into your property, that is clearly a direct act and a clear Bava Kamma liability. But what of a barbecue whose smoke drifts immediately into a neighbor's crops? Or a fire lit on one's property whose effects occur within minutes? These are far more direct than tree roots spreading over years, but still not a literal arrow. The question is how to define "direct."

### What is Direct?

The category of *girei didei* occupies a continuum. On one end sits a literal arrow — one releases it, it travels, it strikes. On the other end sit tree roots that take perhaps five years to spread and damage a cistern. Somewhere in between is smoke, vibrations, sparks, and loosened chaff. How one positions each case on that continuum determines the extent of Rabbi Yosi's concession.

An instructive example from the *Gemara* involves placing a ladder in one's yard: a weasel immediately uses it as a springboard to jump into the neighbor's property and cause damage. The causal chain here is even more attenuated than smoke — the weasel is acting on its own — yet because it happens almost instantaneously upon the placement of the ladder, there is something in the immediacy that brings it closer to the category of *girei didei*, at least for purposes of the neighbor-law obligation, even if not necessarily from a Bava Kamma liability perspective.

The question of time plays a role in defining *grama* more broadly. As a parallel: when one closes an electrical circuit, it triggers a nearly instantaneous chain of physical events — and it becomes difficult to call that *grama* precisely because it all happens within a split second, even though the sequence of causation is technically mediated. So timing matters, even if it is not the only factor.

There is also the question of how broadly *girei didei* must extend before it swallows Rabbi Yosi's entire concession to the *perek*. Does the smell rising from a barn (*refet bakar*) into a neighbor's warehouse count as one's arrows? Does that operate quickly enough? The boundary is not crisp, and the *Rishonim* will need to work through the cases.

## Sesame Case

### Papi Yonah Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּאפִּי יוֹנָאָה עָנִי וְהֶעֱשִׁיר הֲוָה. בְּנָה אַפַּדְנָא. הֲווֹ הָנָךְ עָצוֹרֵי בְּשִׁיבָבוּתֵיהּ, דְּכִי הֲווֹ דָּיְיקִי שׁוּמְשְׁמֵי הֲוָה נָיְידָא אַפַּדְנֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כִּי הֲוָאן בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא הֲוָה אָמְרִינַן: מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּגִירֵי דִילֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that a man called **Pappei Yona'a was poor and became wealthy.** He **built a mansion [*appadna*]** on his land. **There were these** sesame seed **pressers in his neighborhood who** would work, and **when they would press the sesame seeds** their activity **would shake his mansion. He came before Rav Ashi** to complain. Rav Ashi **said to him: When we were** studying **in the study hall of Rav Kahana, we would say** that **Rabbi Yosei concedes with regard to one's arrows.** Here too, because the sesame seed pressers cause immediate damage they must distance themselves.

*Papi Yonah ani vehe'ashir havah* — Papi Yonah had once been poor and later became wealthy. He built himself an *apadna*, a fine mansion, on his property. Neighboring him were people who pressed sesame oil — *atzorei* who would pound sesame. When they did so, *havi naydei apadnei* — the mansion would shake from the vibrations.

Papi Yonah came before Rav Ashi. Rav Ashi invoked the principle he had learned in the yeshiva of Rav Kahana: *modi Rabbi Yosi begirei diley* — even Rabbi Yosi concedes when the harm is the direct product of one's actions. And this, Rav Ashi ruled, is precisely such a case. The vibrations travel directly from the sesame-pressing operation; they shake the ground; they shake the mansion. This is as close to one's own arrows as anything short of a literal projectile.

### Vibration Threshold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? כִּדְנָיֵיד נִכְתְּמָא אַפּוּמֵּיהּ דְּחַצְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how much** must the mansion shake for the owner to have the right to compel the sesame seed pressers to distance themselves? It must shake enough **that the lid [*nakhtema*]** positioned **at the mouth of a jug shakes** if it is placed on a wall.

The *Gemara* then asks: *vekhama* — how much vibration is too much? The measure given is *kadi naydei nakhmata apumei de'khatzva*: if a cover placed on top of a pottery jar would be jostled loose by the tremor, that constitutes a structural threat, and the sesame-pressers must move.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot raises an important question: if we rule that *girei diley* requires distancing even under Rabbi Yosi, what happens when the harmful party was there first? The general rule — stated by Tosafot — is: *af al gav d'samakh beheiter ein tzarikh leharkhik aflu begirei diley* — even in a case of one's own arrows, if you were there first, you need not move. His proof is the case of the barn (*refet bakar*): smells rising directly from the barn into a neighbor's warehouse are treated as a case of *girei diley*, yet the *Baraita* rules that if the barn was established first, it may remain. The same principle applies to the baker's vinegar fumes and the dyer's materials — *ein khilluk bein dira lishar kol hatzrakhin*.

Why, then, were the sesame-pressers required to move? Rabbeinu Tam explains: *delo kevi'a tashmishai kol kakh* — their operation was not sufficiently established or ongoing. They only pressed sesame occasionally, perhaps once a year. Since they lacked the kind of continuous, regularized presence that would constitute "being there first" in a meaningful sense, they could not claim the protection afforded to an established prior occupant. By contrast, if one regularly conducts a smoky barbecue every summer weekend as an ongoing practice, and then a neighbor arrives who finds the smoke objectionable, the established occupant may invoke his priority even in a case of *girei diley*.

Tosafot's analysis here contains several significant *chiddushim*: first, that the barn constitutes *girei diley*; second, that priority in time overrides even *girei diley*; and third, that "being there first" requires an ongoing, regularized presence — not a seasonal or occasional one.

## Flax Beating

### Bar Miryon Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי בַּר מָרִיּוֹן בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִין, כִּי הֲוָה נָפְצִי כִּיתָּנָא – הֲוָה אָזְלָא רַקְתָּא וּמַזְּקָא אִינָשֵׁי. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: כִּי אָמְרִינַן מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּגִירֵי דִּילֵיהּ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי דְּקָא אָזְלָא מִכֹּחוֹ, הָכָא – זִיקָא הוּא דְּקָא מַמְטֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When the** members of the **household of bar Maryon, son of Ravin, would beat** their **flax, the chaff [*rakta*] would fly off and harm people.** Those people **came before Ravina** to complain. Ravina **said to them: When we say** that **Rabbi Yosei concedes with regard to his arrows, this statement** applies only **when** the damaging item **moves by his direct force. Here,** by contrast, **it is the wind that carries** the chaff.

The house of Bar Miryon, son of Ravin — *debei Bar Miryon brei deRavin* — would beat flax: *ki havei nakshei kitna*. The material loosened from the flax — *riksha* — would fly out and strike people nearby, causing injury. On its face, this seems like an unambiguous case of *girei diley*: the beaters put their energy into the flax, the material is propelled by that energy, and it strikes others. It is practically like shooting an arrow.

### Ravina's View

Came before Ravina. *Amar lehu: ki amrina modeh Rabbi Yose begirei diley, hani mili deka azla mikocho* — when we say Rabbi Yosi agrees regarding one's arrows, that is only when the damaging agent travels through the person's own energy. *Hacha zika hu dekamamtila* — here it is the wind that carries the chaff away. The energy of the beaters loosens the material, but it is the wind's force that propels it into people's faces.

This is a remarkably narrow definition of *girei diley*. Under Ravina, almost anything short of direct physical transmission of one's kinetic energy to the damaging object would be excluded. The barbecue: the wind carries the smoke. The roots: they grow through the soil under their own biological process. Even certain cases of vibration might be mediated by the earth rather than direct mechanical force. If *girei diley* is this narrow, Rabbi Yosi's concession becomes very small indeed.

### Winnowing Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: מַאי שְׁנָא מִזּוֹרֶה וְרוּחַ מְסַיַּיעְתּוֹ? אַמְרוּהָ קַמֵּיהּ דְּמָרִימָר, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הַיְינוּ זוֹרֶה וְרוּחַ מְסַיַּיעְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi objects to this:** In **what** way **is** this case **different from** one who **winnows** on Shabbat by throwing the grain into the wind so that the chaff is blown away **and the wind assists him?** That is considered a primary category of labor on Shabbat despite the fact that the act is performed partly with the aid of the wind. The Gemara relates that the Sages **stated** this objection with regard to beating flax **before Mareimar.** Mareimar **said to them: This** case **is** the same as **one who winnows and the wind assists him.** Just as this is considered his direct force for the purposes of the *halakhot* of Shabbat, it is likewise considered his direct force with regard to the *halakhot* of damages.

Mar bar Rav Ashi challenged: *mai shna mizoreh veruach mesayato?* How is this different from the case of *zoreh* — winnowing? When one throws grain into the air and the wind separates the chaff, one is liable for the *melacha* of winnowing on *Shabbat*, even though it is the wind that does the separating. The principle at work — termed *koach acher me'urav bo* in Bava Kamma — is that one who employs natural forces to accomplish a result is treated as if one acted directly. If that principle makes one *chayav* for *Shabbat*, should it not equally make the flax-beaters liable here, since they are effectively using the wind to carry the chaff away?

The force of this argument is obvious: you would not winnow without wind; you depend on the wind; the wind is, in effect, your instrument. Similarly, the flax-beaters may not "want" the wind in the same deliberate way — but the Gemara will address that distinction.

### Spark Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרָבִינָא, מַאי שְׁנָא מִגֵּץ הַיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת הַפַּטִּישׁ וְהִזִּיק – דְּחַיָּיב לְשַׁלֵּם? הָתָם נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלֵיזִל, הָכָא לָא נִיחָא לֵיהּ דְּלֵיזִל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Ravina,** who rejects this comparison and claims that flying chaff is not considered one's arrow, in **what** way **is** this situation **different from** that of **a spark that flies from a hammer and causes damage,** in which case all agree **that** the one wielding the hammer **is liable to pay?** The Gemara answers: **There,** it is **preferable for him that** the spark **go** as far as possible, rather than staying nearby. **Here** it is **not preferable for him,** i.e., it is immaterial to him, **that** the chaff **go** some distance.

*Amru kamei deMermer* — they reported this argument to Mermer, who agreed: it is indeed just like *zoreh veruach mesayato*, and the beaters are liable under *girei diley*. But the *Gemara* then asks: and according to Ravina, who said it is different — how does he respond? The *Gemara* does not press the *Shabbat* comparison further, apparently recognizing that *hilchos Shabbat* operates under different principles than *nezikin*. On *Shabbat*, the concept of *melachet machshevet* — intentional, purposive action — can broaden the scope of liability, because the Torah focuses on achieving an end result rather than narrowly defining the precise physical act. This is why cooking by various methods all constitute the same *melacha*, and why *toldot* are liable alongside *avot* — the focus is on the class of result achieved. But that broadened framework may not translate to the domain of damages.

Instead, the *Gemara* poses a challenge from a genuine *nezikin* case: *mai shna migetz hayotzei mitachas hapatish?* When a blacksmith hammers on an anvil, sparks fly and cause damage — and he is *chayav leshalem*. So how is the flying chaff different from flying sparks? In both cases one's action releases something that the wind then carries to cause harm.

The *Gemara* answers: *hasam nicha lei deleizal* — there, with the sparks, one wants them to fly away. A blacksmith does not want the sparks landing on his own property, his own materials, his own body. He benefits from the sparks flying away, and in that sense he is using the wind's dispersal, much like the winnower. *Hacha lo nicha lei deleizal* — here, the flax-beater has no stake in where the loosened material goes. Its dispersal is entirely incidental to the work. That deliberateness — wanting the force to travel away — is what aligns the spark case with the winnowing case and distinguishes both from the flax.

### Tosafot Resolution

Tosafot raises a further difficulty: even granting that *girei diley* does not apply, should the flax-beaters not be liable under the category of *esh* — fire? In the *esh* category, one creates a damaging force and the wind carries it elsewhere. Isn't that precisely what is happening here?

Tosafot's answer draws an important distinction: *esh* requires that the actor create something that is already, in itself, a damaging force — and the wind merely transports it to another location. Fire is inherently destructive; the wind brings the destruction to a new place. But loosened flax chaff is not inherently destructive. It has no intrinsic damaging power. It is the wind itself that transforms the chaff into something capable of injuring people. When the wind is not merely the carrier of a damaging force but is actually what makes the substance dangerous, the actor is not the author of the damage — the wind is. Therefore no liability for *esh* either.

This resolution also illuminates the *Gemara*'s answer about the spark: the blacksmith wants the sparks — which are inherently destructive — to fly away. He is using the wind to transport a damaging force he created. That is *esh*. The flax-beater creates something harmless, and only the wind's intervention makes it harmful. The beater is therefore exempt.

One larger question emerges from this entire *sugya*: what is the relationship between *girei diley* as a Bava Batra obligation to distance oneself and *girei diley* as a Bava Kamma basis for monetary liability? Is it possible that one could be exempt from the obligation to distance yet still be required to pay damages after the fact — as one participant suggested might be the fuller reading of Rabbi Yosi's position? Tosafot appears to assume this is impossible: if we say there is no obligation to distance, then a fortiori there is no liability to pay. But the intersection of the two tractates here is genuinely complex, and the precise scope of Rabbi Yosi's concession remains a matter of debate among the *Rishonim*. Rabbeinu Tam's ruling follows Mar bar Rav Ashi — a broader definition of *girei diley* — which is important practically, since we rule like Rabbi Yosi: one does not want to give that ruling an excessively narrow reading that would leave neighbors essentially without recourse.

## Trees & Fields

### Four Amot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ לֹא יִטַּע אָדָם אִילָן סָמוּךְ לִשְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – אֶחָד גְּפָנִים וְאֶחָד כׇּל אִילָן. הָיָה גָּדֵר בֵּינָתַיִם – זֶה סוֹמֵךְ לַגָּדֵר מִכָּאן, וְזֶה סוֹמֵךְ לַגָּדֵר מִכָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may not plant a tree near the field of another unless he distances it four cubits from** the field. This is the case **whether** he is planting **grapevines or any** kind of **tree.** If there **was a fence between** them, **this** one **may place,** i.e., plant, his grapevines or trees **close to the fence from here, and that** one **may place,** i.e., plant, his produce **close to the fence from there.**

The next *mishnah*: *lo yita adam ilan samuch lisedeh chaveiro ela im kein hirchik mimenu arba amot* — one may not plant a tree adjacent to a neighbor's field without maintaining a distance of four *amot*. Unlike the cistern case, roots are not the concern here — we are in open fields, and the roots of various plantings are not threatening one another underground. Rashi understands the *sadeh* to include even a vegetable garden, while Tosafot reads it from the *Gemara* as referring more specifically to a field of trees, vines, or other cultivated plantings — not simply one's front yard. *Echad gefanim ve'echad kol ilan* — the rule applies equally to vines and to other trees.

### Wall Exception

The *mishnah* continues: *haya gader beintayim* — if there is a wall between the two properties, then *zeh someich la'gader mikan vezeh someich la'gader mikan* — each party may plant right up to the wall from his side. The wall removes the practical reason for the buffer: there is no risk of inadvertently crossing into the neighbor's property, so the four-*amah* requirement does not apply.

### Root Cutting Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שׇׁרָשִׁים יוֹצְאִים לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מַעֲמִיק שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְעַכֵּב אֶת הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה. הָיָה חוֹפֵר בּוֹר, שִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה – קוֹצֵץ וְיוֹרֵד, וְהָעֵצִים שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If the **roots were spreading into** the field **of another,** the owner of the field **may dig to a depth** of **three handbreadths** even if he severs those roots, **so that** they do **not impede** his **plow.** If he **was digging a cistern** in that spot, or **a ditch, or a cave,** and he came upon the roots of his neighbor's tree, **he may cut downward** normally, **and the wood** from the roots **is his.**

*Hayu shorashim yotzim letoch shel chaveiro* — suppose the roots of a tree have in fact spread into the neighbor's field, and that neighbor wishes to plow. His plow will strike the roots and be impeded or damaged. What is he entitled to do?

The *mishnah* rules: *ma'amik shelosha tefachim kedei shelo ye'akeiv et hamachareshah* — he may dig down three *tefachim* deep and cut away any roots he finds within that depth, so as not to impede the plow. This is roughly nine inches below ground level. The tree's owner had every right to plant where he did; the neighbor has every right to plow. The three-*tefachim* depth marks the zone of plow access. If roots are cut in the process and the tree suffers, that is a risk the tree's owner takes when planting near a boundary.

### Deep Digging

*Haya chofer bor siach ume'ara kotzetz veyored veha'etzim shelo* — if the neighbor is not merely plowing but digging a cistern or deep ditch, he may cut away roots to whatever depth is necessary and keep the wood. The *mishnah* says *ha'etzim shelo* — the wood is his — but the *Gemara* will have a dispute about whose "his" refers to: the digger's, or the tree-owner's. We will return to that question.

### Four Amot Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ – כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדַת הַכֶּרֶם. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֲבָל בְּבָבֶל – שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: לֹא יִטַּע אָדָם אִילָן סָמוּךְ לִשְׂדֵה חֲבֵירוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי אַמּוֹת. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! אֶלָּא לָאו כְּדִשְׁמוּאֵל? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: A *tanna* **taught:** The **four cubits that** the Sages **stated** one must leave between a vineyard and a neighbor's field are **for the work of the vineyard,** so that the owner of the vineyard does not take oxen and a plow into his neighbor's field while working his vineyard. **Shmuel says: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to Eretz Yisrael, but in Babylonia two cubits** are sufficient, as their plows are shorter. **This** opinion **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may not plant a tree near the field of another unless he distances** the tree **two cubits from** the field. **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **Four cubits? Rather, is it not** correct that there is a difference between Eretz Yisrael and Babylonia in this regard, **as** stated **by Shmuel?** The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that it is so.

*Tana: arba she'amru kedei avodat hakerem* — the *Gemara* explains that the four *amot* is the standard working space required to cultivate a vineyard or similar planting, providing maneuvering access without needing to encroach on the neighbor's property.

*Amar Shmuel: lo shanu ela be'Eretz Yisrael aval be'Bavel shtei amot* — Shmuel observes a regional variation: the four-*amah* rule applies in the Land of Israel, but in Babylonia, two *amot* suffice, presumably because the plowing implements used there were narrower. A *Baraita* is cited that states the distance as two *amot*, and the *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction between the *Baraita* and the *mishnah* precisely on this basis — one reflects Eretz Yisrael practice, the other Babylonian practice.

## Birds & Vines

### Rava Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן הֲווֹ לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ דִּיקְלֵי, אַמִּיצְרָא דְּפַרְדֵּיסָא דְּרַב יוֹסֵף. הֲווֹ אָתוּ צִפּוֹרֵי יָתְבִי בְּדִיקְלֵי, וְנָחֲתִי בְּפַרְדֵּיסָא וּמַפְסְדִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל קוֹץ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אַרְחֵיקִי לִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְאִילָנוֹת, אֲבָל לִגְפָנִים בָּעִינַן טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava bar Rav Ḥanan had these palm trees** that stood **adjacent to the boundary of Rav Yosef's vineyard. Birds would come and roost on the palm trees and** would subsequently **descend to the vineyard and damage it.** Rav Yosef **said to** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan: **Go** and **cut down** your palm trees. Rava bar Rav Ḥanan **said to him: But I distanced** them the required amount. Rav Yosef **said to him: This matter,** i.e., this specific distance, applies only **to trees, but a greater** distance **is required for vines.**

*Rava bar Rav Chanan havu lei hanhu diklei amitzra d'pardesa d'Rav Yosef* — Rava bar Rav Chanan had palm trees right along the boundary of Rav Yosef's vineyard. *Hava atei tziporei yasvi b'diklei* — birds would come and roost in the palm trees, then launch themselves directly into the vineyard, causing damage to the grapes.

Rav Yosef said: cut down your trees. The owner responded: *ve'ha archiki li* — I have kept the four *amot* required by the *mishnah*. I have done nothing wrong.

### Vine Distance

Rav Yosef replied: *hani mili le'ilanot* — the four-*amah* rule governs the distance between trees. For a vineyard, for vines, *ba'inan tefei* — more is required.

### Tree-to-Vine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֶחָד גְּפָנִים וְאֶחָד כׇּל אִילָן! אֲמַר לְהוּ: הָנֵי מִילֵּי אִילָן לְאִילָן וּגְפָנִים לִגְפָנִים, אֲבָל אִילָן לִגְפָנִים בָּעִינַן טְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan protested: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that this is the *halakha* **whether** he is planting **grapevines or any** kind of **tree?** Rav Yosef **said to him: This matter** applies only to the distance between one **tree and** another **tree, or** the distance between one **vine and** other **vines. But** with regard to the space between **a tree and vines, one requires a greater distance.**

But does not the *mishnah* itself say *echad gefanim ve'echad kol ilan* — that the same rule applies to both vines and other trees? Rav Yosef's answer: *hani mili ilan le'ilan, u'gefanim le'gefanim* — that equality applies when you are measuring tree-to-tree or vine-to-vine. But *ilan le'gefanim* — a tree adjacent to a vineyard — presents a qualitatively different problem, precisely because of the birds. Palm trees provide a perch and a launching platform; their proximity to vines creates an access pathway that would not exist if the neighbor were simply another orchard. In that configuration, four *amot* is insufficient and more distance is required.

This ruling also raises the question of what happened to Rabbi Yosi. We appear to have moved from one *mishnah* to the next without reckoning with his position. Tosafot grapples with this: once we broaden the category of *girei diley* to include the bird-pathway scenario — which is even more attenuated than vibrations or smoke — the scope of Rabbi Yosi's concession becomes quite expansive. But where exactly the line falls is not stated explicitly, and the *Rishonim* continue to debate it.

### Moral Hesitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא לָא קָיֵיצְנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב: הַאי דִּיקְלָא דְּטָעֵין קַבָּא – אָסוּר לְמִקְצְיֵיהּ. וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לָא שְׁכֵיב שִׁכְחַת בְּרִי, אֶלָּא דְּקַץ תְּאֵנְתָּא בְּלָא זִימְנֵיהּ. מָר – אִי נִיחָא לֵיהּ, לִיקּוֹץ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan **said to him: I** myself **will not cut them down, as Rav said:** With regard to **this palm tree that produces one *kav*** of fruit, it is **prohibited to cut it down,** due to the verse: "You shall not destroy the trees" (Deuteronomy 20:19). **And Rabbi Ḥanina says: My son Shikhḥat died only because he cut down a fig tree before its time.** Rava bar Rav Ḥanan continued: **If the Master is amenable** to do so, he **may cut** them down, but I will not do it.

*Amar lei ana lo katzna* — the tree-owner refused to cut down his trees. Why? *De'amar Rav: hai dikla d'tein kava, asur lemikatzatzei* — Rav rules that any palm tree capable of producing even a *kav* of fruit per year may not be cut down. This invokes the prohibition of *bal tashchit* — the destruction of productive fruit trees — rooted in the *Torah*.

Rav Yosef added a striking personal note: *de'amar Rav Chanina: lo shachiv Shichat beri ella de'katz te'enata belo zimneih* — Rav Chanina said that his son Shichat died young for no other reason than that he cut down a fig tree before its time. The metaphor is vivid: one who cuts down a tree in the prime of its productive life may himself be cut down in the prime of his. The *Gemara* does not frame this as a technical *halachic* prohibition — indeed, the response is *ei nicha lei likotz* — if you wish to cut it down, be my guest. Perhaps technically it is not forbidden in this context. But the moral-religious weight of the act is considerable, and the tree-owner refuses to be the agent of it.

The *Gemara* does not tell us what happened in the end — perhaps the force of the moral argument was enough.

## Rav Pappa Story

### Root Cutting Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא הֲווֹ לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ דִּיקְלֵי אַמִּיצְרָא דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. אֲזַל אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה חָפַר, וְקָא קָאֵיץ שׇׁרָשָׁיו. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנַן: הָיוּ שׇׁרָשִׁים יוֹצְאִים לְתוֹךְ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ – מַעֲמִיק שְׁלֹשָׁה, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יְעַכֵּב הַמַּחֲרֵישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates that **Rav Pappa had these palm trees** that stood **adjacent to the boundary of** the property of **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua. He went** and **found** Rav Huna **digging and cutting his roots.** Rav Pappa **said to him: What is this?** Rav Huna **said to him** that **we learned** in the mishna: If the **roots were spreading into** the field **of another,** the owner of the field **may dig to a depth** of **three handbreadths** even if he severs those roots, **so that** they do **not impede** his **plow.**

*Rav Pappa hava lei hanhu diklei amitzra de'Rav Huna berei de'Rav Yehoshua* — Rav Pappa had palm trees right on the boundary of the field belonging to Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua. Rav Pappa went and found that Rav Huna was digging in his field and cutting away the roots — exactly as the *mishnah* permits: *hayu sharshin yotzin letoch shel chaveiro ma'amik shelosha kedei shelo ya'akev hamachareshah*.

Rav Pappa objected. Rav Huna replied: I am doing exactly what the *mishnah* allows. Rav Pappa said: the *mishnah* only permits going down three *tefachim*; you are digging deeper. Rav Huna replied: I am digging cisterns and ditches — *ana borot shichin ume'arot ka'chaferna* — and the *mishnah* explicitly permits cutting roots to full depth when digging cisterns.

### Public Path Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ כּוּלְּהִי, וְלָא יְכֵילִי לֵיהּ; עַד דַּאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מֶצֶר שֶׁהֶחֱזִיקוּ בּוֹ רַבִּים – אָסוּר לְקַלְקְלוֹ. לְבָתַר דִּנְפַק, אֲמַר: אַמַּאי לָא אֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: כָּאן בְּתוֹךְ שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה, כָּאן חוּץ לְשֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה אַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: I told him all** the proofs I could find, **but I was unable to** convince **him** that I was correct, **until I told him that** which **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **a strip** of land over **which the public has an acquired privilege** of use, one **may not destroy it.** Here too, since I have an acquired privilege of use of this land, you are not permitted to destroy that which I possess. **After** Rav Pappa **left,** Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, **said: Why did I not say to him** that **there,** an acquired privilege of use is effective when it is **within sixteen cubits,** as within that area the roots are considered part of the tree, whereas **here** I cut the roots of the palm trees **beyond sixteen cubits.**

Rav Pappa reported: *amrei lei kulei hai velo yachli lei* — I marshaled every argument I could and was unable to dislodge him, given that the *mishnah* was plainly on his side. Finally, I said to him: consider *ha de'amar Rav Yehuda — meitzar shehechziku bo rabbim assur lekalkelo* — a pathway over which the public has established use may not be damaged or obstructed, even if it crosses private property.

What does that ruling have to do with cutting roots? On Rashi's reading, it is an argument by analogy: just as the public's sustained use of a path through private property gives them enforceable rights in that path, so too the sustained presence of Rav Pappa's roots in Rav Huna's field gives Rav Pappa a form of right in that subsurface space — a right that Rav Huna cannot simply destroy by digging. The roots have been "walking there" just as the public has been walking on the path.

### Rashi & Tosafot

Rashi's analogy is admittedly difficult: the public's right over a pathway is a specific and recognized legal category (*reshut harabbim*), while the spread of roots is something the *mishnah* explicitly permits the field-owner to cut. How does a comparison to the public pathway override a direct *mishnah*? Furthermore, what are the precise parameters of such a "root right" — does it expire? Does it require a minimum time?

Tosafot offers a completely different reading that redirects the entire argument. On Tosafot's account, the path that *Rav Yehuda* speaks of ran directly through Rav Huna's field — the *rabbim* had been using it as a thoroughfare. When Rav Huna decided to dig his cistern, he chose to dig it in that public pathway. Rav Pappa's argument, then, is not about the roots at all; it is that Rav Huna is prohibited from digging in the public pathway. Rav Pappa, having been unable to defend his trees through the *mishnah*, found another grounds entirely to stop the digging. This reading is simpler and more direct, though less immediately connected to the root-cutting question.

### Sixteen Amot

After they left court, Rav Huna reconsidered and said: *amai lo amri lei* — why did I not respond with this: *kan betoch sheish esrei amah kan chutz lesheish esrei amah* — within sixteen *amot* is one rule; beyond sixteen *amot* is another?

On Tosafot's reading, this fits neatly: the standard halachic width of a *reshut harabbim* is sixteen *amot* (as established for *Shabbat*). Even if the public has acquired rights to a pathway, the original property owner retains the right to reclaim portions of it so long as he leaves sixteen *amot* of usable width. If Rav Huna's cistern was being dug in a way that still left sixteen *amot* of path, he was within his rights all along, and Rav Pappa's argument was defeated.

On Rashi's reading, the sixteen *amot* connects to the question of root rights: the *Gemara* going forward will examine whether a tree-owner has a recognized claim to the roots that spread within a certain radius — up to sixteen *amot* — such that the field-owner cannot simply cut them at will within that zone. Beyond sixteen *amot*, different rules may apply. This will be relevant to reconciling the apparent leniency of the *mishnah* with the idea of root-ownership rights. Tosafot engages with this as well and will need to work through how the *mishnah*'s plain permission to cut roots survives if there is a sixteen-*amah* protected zone — a question that carries into the following *Gemara*.

## Roots Ownership

### Hekdesh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה חוֹפֵר בּוֹר, שִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה – קוֹצֵץ וְיוֹרֵד וְהָעֵצִים שֶׁלּוֹ (וְכוּ׳). בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ יַעֲקֹב הַדְיָיבָא מֵרַב חִסְדָּא: עֵצִים שֶׁל מִי?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **he was digging a cistern, a ditch, or a cave, he may cut downward and the wood is his.** The Sage **Ya'akov of Hadeyyav raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda: To whom does the wood belong?** The mishna says that the wood is his, without specifying to which of the two individuals this refers, the owner of the tree or the owner of the land.

The *Gemara* addresses the question of who owns roots that have grown from a private tree into a neighboring field. Rav Yaakov Hadaiva asked Rav Chisda: *shorshei ilan shel mi* — when roots spread into someone else's field, to whom do they belong — the owner of the tree or the owner of the field in which they now grow?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, תְּנֵיתוּהָ: שׇׁרְשֵׁי אִילָן שֶׁל הֶדְיוֹט הַבָּאִים בְּשֶׁל הֶקְדֵּשׁ, לֹא נֶהֱנִין וְלֹא מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: You learned** the answer in a mishna in tractate *Me'ila* (13b). If **roots of a tree** belonging **to an ordinary person [*hedyot*] extend into** a field belonging **to the Temple** treasury, **one may not derive benefit** from them, but if one derived benefit from them he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. That is, even if one does transgress the prohibition and benefit from them, it is not considered misuse and he is not liable to bring an offering.

Rav Chisda replied: we already have a *Baraita* that resolves this. *Shorshei ilan shel hedyot haba'im beshel hekdesh lo nehenin velo mo'alin* — roots of a privately-owned tree that have grown into a field consecrated as *hekdesh*: one may not derive benefit from them (*lo nehenin* — a rabbinic stringency because they are growing in consecrated ground), but if one did derive benefit, there is no violation of *meila* (*lo mo'alin*).

### Meila Inference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא: בָּתַר אִילָן אָזְלִינַן – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לֹא מוֹעֲלִין; אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ: בָּתַר קַרְקַע אָזְלִינַן, אַמַּאי לֹא מוֹעֲלִין?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that **we follow the tree,** and the roots are considered part of it, it is **due to that** reason that **one is not** liable for **misuse,** as the tree is not consecrated. **But if you say we follow the land,** i.e., the roots belong to the land's owner, **why** is he **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property?

The *Gemara* draws the inference: *ei amart bishlama batar ilan azlinan* — if we say we follow the tree, meaning the roots belong to the owner of the tree, then *mishum hachi lo ma'ali* — of course there is no *meila*, because the roots are not actually *hekdesh* property; they belong to a private individual who happens to have his roots in consecrated ground. The Rabbis prohibited deriving benefit as a precautionary measure, but technically they are not sanctified.

*Ela ei amart batar karka azlinan* — but if we say we follow the field, meaning the roots belong to the owner of whatever land they currently occupy, then these roots are growing in *hekdesh* land and are *hekdesh* property — and one who benefits from them should be liable for *meila*. The fact that the *Baraita* says there is no *meila* therefore proves that we follow the tree: the roots belong to the tree's owner, not to the owner of the field in which they happen to grow.

The *Gemara* leaves the matter here for now, noting that the next material will introduce an argument in the opposite direction — and with that, the daf's discussion continues into the following day.
