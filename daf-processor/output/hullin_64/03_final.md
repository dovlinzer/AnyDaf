# Hullin 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eggs from Non-Jews

### Basic Permissibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לוֹקְחִין בֵּיצִים מִן הַגּוֹיִם בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְאֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לֹא מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלוֹת וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם טְרֵפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may buy eggs from the gentiles anywhere, and one** need **not** be **concerned, neither with regard to carcasses,** i.e., that the egg may have been removed from a carcass of a bird and therefore forbidden, **nor with regard to** eggs from ***tereifot*,** because neither of these possibilities is likely.

The daf opens with a *baraita*: *locḥin beitzim min ha'ovdei kokhavim bechol makom* — one may purchase eggs from non-Jews wherever one finds them. The concern of *neveilah* — that the eggs came from birds that died without proper *shechita* — does not apply, nor does the concern of *tereifah* — that the bird had some internal injury rendering it *tereif* and its eggs consequently forbidden — because the overwhelming majority of eggs come from live, healthy birds. These concerns are therefore statistically negligible.

### Merchant's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא דְּעוֹף טָמֵא נִינְהוּ? אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל: בְּאוֹמֵר שֶׁל עוֹף פְּלוֹנִי טָהוֹר. וְלֵימָא שֶׁל עוֹף טָהוֹר? אִי הָכִי, אִית לֵיהּ לְאִישְׁתְּמוֹטֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But perhaps they** are from **a non-kosher bird. Shmuel's father said:** The *baraita* is referring **to** a case where the gentile **says** they are **of such and such bird,** which is known to be **kosher.** The Gemara challenges: **But** if the gentile is deemed credible, **let him say** only that they are **of a kosher bird.** Why does he need to name the species? The Gemara responds: **If so,** if he does not name the species, **he has** the opportunity to **deflect** scrutiny if he is dishonest; but if he names the species, one can bring other eggs of the same species to compare and validate the claim.

The *Gemara* immediately raises a sharper question: *vedilma d'of tamei ninhu* — perhaps these eggs came from a non-kosher bird? Something derived from a non-kosher animal is itself forbidden, and a bird being alive does not address whether it belongs to a forbidden species. The answer, attributed to Avuha deShmuel, the father of Shmuel, is that we are speaking of a case *be'omer shel of ploni tahor* — where the non-Jewish merchant explicitly states that the eggs come from a particular identified kosher species. Rashi clarifies why the specification must name a particular species (*ploni*) rather than merely asserting "these are from a kosher bird." If the merchant only made a general claim, he could always escape scrutiny by saying, "Well, I don't remember exactly which kosher bird, but it was some kosher bird." By naming a specific species, however, his claim becomes falsifiable: one can always take a known sample of that species and compare.

### Testability Standard

This leads naturally into one of the central principles underlying *kashrus* credibility: under what circumstances can one trust the statement of a person — especially a non-Jew, who is outside our halakhic framework, and especially a merchant with a financial stake in the sale? Two distinct mechanisms are sometimes invoked in such cases. The first is *masiach lefi tumo* — the speaker volunteers information innocently, with no vested interest in the halakhic outcome. That is clearly not the situation here, where the merchant is consciously telling the buyer what the eggs are in order to make a sale. The second is *uman lo marei umnutei* — an expert will not say something that undermines his professional reputation. This principle applies somewhat differently: it is less about dishonesty resulting in lost sales and more about an expert whose claim of expertise can be directly tested.

### Avida Legluya

The more precise principle operating here is *milsa d'avida legluya* — a matter that is easily and publicly verifiable. When a claim can be readily tested and disproven, the person making it has a powerful incentive not to lie, because exposure would be swift. By specifying the particular species, the merchant's claim becomes just such a testable assertion — one can always procure a sample of that species and compare the eggs directly. The *Gemara* will later refine how these factors interact, but the implicit logic at this stage is that testability provides sufficient assurance.

## Egg Indicators

### Fish Egg Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִבְדּוֹק בְּסִימָנִין! דְּתַנְיָא: כְּסִימָנֵי בֵיצִים כָּךְ סִימָנֵי דָגִים. סִימָנֵי דָגִים סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? ״סְנַפִּיר וְקַשְׂקֶשֶׂת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: כָּךְ סִימָנֵי עוּבְּרֵי דָּגִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why must one rely on the gentile? **Let him inspect** the eggs **for signs, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Like the signs of** kosher **eggs, so** too are **the signs of fish.** The Gemara interjects: Can it **enter your mind** that the *baraita* is referring to **the signs of fish? The Merciful One states** them explicitly in the Torah: **"Fins and scales"** (Leviticus 11:9). **Rather, say: So** too are **the signs of fish embryos,** i.e., fish eggs found in the fish's innards.

Having established that the merchant's specific claim is required, the *Gemara* asks: why rely on the merchant at all? Why not simply examine the eggs themselves? After all, the *Gemara* introduces a *baraita*: *ke'simanei beitzim kach simanei dagim* — the indicators for eggs are like the indicators for fish. The *Gemara* immediately parses this: surely one cannot look for *snapir* and *kaskeset* — fins and scales — on bird eggs. Rather, the statement means that the indicators used to identify kosher fish eggs (*ovrei dagim*) are parallel to the indicators used for bird eggs. That is, the same type of morphological examination that reveals whether fish eggs come from a kosher species also applies to bird eggs.

### Shape Indicators

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא גַּבֵּי בֵיצִים: אֵלּוּ הֵן סִימָנֵי בֵיצִים – כָּל שֶׁכּוֹדֶרֶת וַעֲגוּלְגּוֹלֶת, רֹאשָׁהּ אֶחָד כַּד וְרֹאשָׁהּ אֶחָד חַד – טְהוֹרָה, שְׁנֵי רָאשֶׁיהָ כַּדִּין אוֹ שְׁנֵי רָאשֶׁיהָ חַדִּין – טְמֵאִין. חֶלְבּוֹן מִבַּחוּץ וְחֶלְמוֹן מִבִּפְנִים – טְהוֹרָה, חֶלְמוֹן מִבַּחוּץ וְחֶלְבּוֹן מִבְּפָנִים – טְמֵאָה, חֶלְמוֹן וְחֶלְבּוֹן מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And it is taught** in a *baraita* **about eggs** that **these are the signs of** bird **eggs: Any** egg **that narrows** at the top **and is rounded,** so that **one of its ends is rounded and** the other **one of its ends is pointed, is kosher.** If **both of its ends are rounded or both of its ends are pointed,** they are **non-kosher.** If the **albumen** is **on the outside and** the **yolk on the inside,** it is **kosher.** If the **yolk** is **on the outside and** the **albumen on the inside,** it is **non-kosher.** If the **yolk and albumen are mixed with each other, it is certainly the egg of a creeping animal.**

The *baraita* then specifies: *kol shekoderet ve'algulgelet* — any egg that is spherical and rounded. Rashi explains that both terms are really conveying a single idea: *algulgelet* alone might suggest roundness in only two dimensions, like a lentil or a wheel of cheese, whereas *koderet* adds depth, indicating that the egg is spherical in three dimensions — not perfectly spherical, as we know, but genuinely ovoid. Then: *rosha echad kad, ve'rosha echad chad* — one end of the egg is like a *kad*, a pitcher, meaning rounded and blunt, and one end is sharp and pointed. If an egg has one rounded end and one pointed end, it is *tehorah* — kosher. If *shnei rasheha kadin* — both ends come to a point — or *shnei rasheha kadin* in the opposite direction, both ends are rounded, *tme'in* — it is from a non-kosher species.

### White/Yolk Test

The *baraita* continues: *chelbon mibachutz ve'chelmon mibifnim* — if the albumin (*chelbon*) is on the outside and the yolk (*chelmon*) is on the inside, the egg is *tehorah*, kosher. A useful mnemonic: *chelbon* sounds like *chalav* — milk — which aids in remembering that it refers to the white. Whether this criterion is independent of the shape indicator or must accompany it is not explicitly resolved at this point in the *Gemara*, but the implication is that both are relevant indicators. It is worth noting that this test of albumen-outside and yolk-inside applies only to bird eggs. Fish eggs, having no such internal structure, are assessed purely on the basis of shape.

### Non-Kosher Indicators

Conversely: *chelmon mibachutz ve'chelbon mibifnim* — if the yolk is on the outside and the white on the inside, the egg is *teme'ah*, non-kosher. Whether this inversion ever actually occurs in nature is left open, but it is stated as a negative indicator. More significantly: *chelmon ve'chelbon me'uravin zeh bezeh* — if the white and yolk are thoroughly intermingled, *beyadu'a she-hi beitzat ha-sheretz* — one may assume with confidence that this is the egg of a *sheretz*, a creeping or crawling creature. The *sheratzim* of the Torah are not merely insects but include small reptiles, rodents, lizards, chameleons, and the like. These creatures' eggs apparently contain no distinct albumin, the entire content being essentially yolk — or, as the ArtScroll footnote suggests, what would normally be the white's constituents are incorporated into the yolk, producing the appearance of intermingling.

### Scrambled Egg Ban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּחֲתוּכוֹת. וְלִיבְדּוֹק בְּחֶלְמוֹן וְחֶלְבּוֹן? בִּטְרוּפוֹת בִּקְעָרָה. וּכְהַאי גַּוְונָא מִי זָבְנִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: אֵין מוֹכְרִין בֵּיצַת טְרֵפָה לְגוֹי, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן טְרוּפָה בִּקְעָרָה; לְפִיכָךְ אֵין לוֹקְחִין מֵהֶם בֵּיצִים טְרוּפוֹת בִּקְעָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** it is **necessary** if the eggs have already been **cut** and one cannot know their original shape. The Gemara challenges: **But let him inspect the yolk and the albumen,** to see which is inside and which is outside. The Gemara responds: The *baraita* deals **with** a case where the eggs are **mixed in a bowl,** and it is impossible to discern this. The Gemara counters: **But in a case like this,** where the eggs are mixed, **may we buy** eggs **from them? Isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **One may sell the egg of a *tereifa*** bird **to a gentile only if it is mixed in a bowl; therefore, one may not buy eggs mixed in a bowl from them,** because they may be from *tereifot*?

If these indicators exist, why must one rely on the merchant's testimony at all? The *Gemara* works through a series of scenarios. If the eggs are chopped and cooked (*dechatuchot*), one cannot assess their shape — but perhaps the white-yolk arrangement can still be seen. If, however, they are *betrufot beke'arah* — scrambled together in a bowl — neither shape nor internal arrangement can be examined. Yet at this point a striking *baraita* intervenes: *ein mochrin beitzat tereifah le-nochri ela im ken trufah beke'arah* — one may not sell the egg of a *tereifah* bird to a non-Jew lest he resell it to a Jew, unless it has been scrambled in a bowl. Only when scrambled will no Jew purchase it, because a Jew encountering scrambled eggs from a non-Jew knows there are no indicators to check. Consequently, *ein locḥin mimenu beitzim trufot beke'arah* — one may not purchase scrambled eggs from a non-Jew, because the very fact that the eggs have been scrambled raises suspicion: perhaps they were scrambled precisely because they were *tereifah* eggs being sold off. So the *Gemara* has arrived at a closed circle: if the eggs are intact and unscrambled, the indicators should suffice — so why need the merchant's claim? And if they are scrambled, one cannot buy them at all.

## Simanim Framework

### Lav De'oraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּאִי לָא תֵּימָא הָכִי, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: שְׁמֹנָה סְפֵיקוֹת הֵן – לִיבְדּוֹק בְּבֵיצִים דִּידְהוּ! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: סִימָנִין לָאו דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Zeira said:** The **signs** of a kosher egg **are not** valid **by Torah law. As, if you do not say so,** one encounters difficulty with **that which Rav Asi says: There are eight** birds whose kosher status is **uncertain.** Why is there uncertainty? **Let one inspect their eggs** for the signs listed in the *baraita* to determine whether they are kosher. **Rather, learn from it** that the **signs are not** valid **by Torah law** and one may not rely on them.

This impasse leads Rabbi Zeira to state the governing principle: *simanim lav de'oraita* — the egg indicators are not sufficient at the biblical level (*de'oraita*) to serve as a basis for determination. It is important to clarify what this means. It does not simply mean that these indicators are not written explicitly in the Torah, because the indicators for identifying kosher birds — the *doresh*, the extra toe, the *korkovan nikshal*, and so forth — are likewise not written in the Torah but were derived by the rabbis, yet they are fully sufficient for *de'oraita* purposes. Rather, *simanim lav de'oraita* here means that the indicators for egg kashrus are not a sufficiently reliable basis on which to make a biblical-level determination, because they are not one hundred percent definitive. They are useful but not conclusive.

The phrase itself is borrowed from a major *sugyia* in the second *perek* of *Bava Metzia*, which discusses the return of lost objects. There the question is whether the identifying characteristics (*simanim*) given by a claimant are a biblically valid basis for returning the object. The debate of *simanim de'oraita o lav de'oraita* in that context provides the vocabulary, but the application here is specific: egg indicators, unlike the full morphological indicators of the birds themselves, cannot by themselves settle questions that have biblical-level stakes.

### Eight Birds Problem

The *Gemara* illustrates this principle with a compelling proof. Rav Assi had stated that there were eight birds of uncertain status — the rabbis did not know definitively whether they were kosher or not, the question turning on whether their gizzard was of the peelable kind. One might suppose that this uncertainty could be resolved elegantly: simply examine the eggs of these birds and see whether the eggs display kosher indicators. If these indicators were fully reliable for *de'oraita* purposes, this would be an obvious solution. The very fact that the *Gemara* never proposes this solution — and indeed that the eight birds remain uncertain — proves that *simanim lav de'oraita*: the egg indicators cannot resolve questions of biblical-level bird kashrus.

### Negative vs Positive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא קָתָנֵי לַהּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁנֵי רָאשֶׁיהָ כַּדִּין, אוֹ שְׁנֵי רָאשֶׁיהָ חַדִּין, אוֹ חֶלְמוֹן מִבַּחוּץ וְחֶלְבּוֹן מִבִּפְנִים – וַדַּאי טְמֵאָה. רֵישֵׁיהּ חַד חַד, רֵישֵׁיהּ חַד כַּד, וְחֶלְבּוֹן מִבַּחוּץ וְחֶלְמוֹן מִבִּפְנִים, וְאָמַר לָךְ שֶׁל עוֹף פְּלוֹנִי וְטָהוֹר הוּא – סְמוֹךְ עֲלֵיהּ. בִּסְתָמָא – לָא תִּסְמוֹךְ עֲלֵיהּ, דְּאִיכָּא דְּעוֹרְבָא דְּדָמֵי לִדְיוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if one may not rely on these signs, **with regard to what *halakha*** is** the *baraita* **teaching** them? The Gemara responds: **This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If **both of its ends are rounded, or both its ends are pointed, or** the **yolk** is **on the outside and** the **albumen** is **on the inside,** it is **certainly non-kosher.** But if **one of its ends is pointed** and **one of its ends is rounded, and** the **albumen** is **on the outside and** the **yolk on the inside, and** the gentile **says to you** that it is **from such and such bird, and** that bird **is kosher, rely on** the signs. But if he offers **no specification** of the type of bird, or if one simply finds eggs with these signs, **do not rely on them, since there are crow's** eggs **that resemble those of a pigeon.**

The *Gemara* then articulates the precise role these indicators do play: *elu hen simanei beitzim* in their negative capacity are absolutely reliable. If an egg has *two* rounded ends, or two pointed ends, or the yolk is on the outside rather than the inside, that egg is *vadai teme'ah* — certainly non-kosher, and no testimony from any merchant can override it. The negative indicators are conclusive. However, the positive indicators — one rounded end, one pointed end, white on the outside and yolk inside — are necessary but not sufficient. They rule out obvious non-kosher eggs, but they do not by themselves establish that the egg is from a kosher species. Positive indicators in combination with a merchant's testable claim (*veamar lach shelo ploni ve'tahor hu*) together permit reliance. Without the merchant's claim, even a perfectly shaped egg with the correct white-yolk arrangement — *bistama*, without specification — one may not rely on the indicators alone.

### Raven-Dove Problem

Why not? Because *de'ika de'urva dedami leyona* — there are raven eggs that resemble dove eggs. The ArtScroll notes that raven eggs and dove eggs actually do share a similar morphology in terms of the rounded-pointed shape, even though they typically differ in color — and since color has not been mentioned as a criterion in this *baraita*, shape alone is insufficient to distinguish them. If even such a similar case of confusable eggs exists, it stands to reason that other non-kosher bird eggs may share the positive indicators of kosher bird eggs. This is precisely why the indicators cannot stand alone. What is required is both: the positive indicators confirming that nothing is obviously wrong, *and* the merchant's specific, falsifiable claim naming the particular species. The indicators on their own do not suffice because some non-kosher eggs look like kosher ones; the merchant's claim alone would not suffice either, since those same misleading similarities exist. It is the convergence of the two — clean indicators plus a testable, specific claim — that generates sufficient reliability to permit purchase.

### Combined Evidence

This analysis raises broader questions about the role of non-Jewish testimony in *halakha* generally. Some authorities read this *Gemara* as establishing that even when a claim is easily testable and thus subject to *milsa d'avida legluya*, it may still require corroborating physical evidence. Others note that the specific requirement for corroboration here may arise from the unusual circumstance that even the corroborating evidence itself is imperfect — because raven eggs can look like dove eggs, the physical evidence alone is compromised, and so neither strand of proof is sufficient by itself. Whether this *Gemara* establishes a general rule that non-Jewish testimony always requires corroboration, or whether it reflects the particular weakness of these specific indicators, remains a matter of halakhic discussion.

## Tosafot Practical Rules

### Majority Principle

How did the *rishonim* actually conduct themselves in a world without formal certification? Tosafot addresses this directly and with great frankness. He notes that the practice in medieval Europe was to purchase eggs from non-Jewish sellers without ever asking them to specify the species — the very step the *Gemara* seemed to require. His justification: *anu somkhim al rov beitzim she-metzuyim beineinu de-shel ofot tehorim hem* — we rely on the fact that the overwhelming majority of eggs found among us come from kosher birds. Just as in discussions of *chalav Yisrael* the question arises whether one need worry when the only available milk is certainly from kosher animals, so too here: where there is no realistic population of non-kosher bird eggs in the marketplace, one may rely on the majority without requiring the merchant's verbal corroboration.

### Mixed Products

Tosafot then extends this analysis to the more complex case of baked goods — *oblias* and *kantias*, various types of pastries and breads that the Jews of medieval Europe regularly purchased from non-Jews, all of which were made with eggs. Here the concern is twofold: the eggs are incorporated into the dough and cannot be individually inspected, and the final product resembles scrambled eggs in that the whites and yolks are no longer distinguishable. Tosafot distinguishes this from the prohibited scrambled egg purchase of the *Gemara*: in that case, the very fact that a non-Jew was selling eggs already mixed and scrambled raised a specific suspicion that they had been scrambled to hide their *tereifah* origin. When a baker incorporates eggs into bread dough, however, no such suspicion attaches — eggs are simply an ingredient in the recipe, and there is nothing suspicious about their appearing in mixed form. *Lo damya* — the two cases are not comparable. One then applies a double reliance: *rov beitzim* that the eggs are from kosher species, and the further likelihood that even among those, the overwhelming majority are not from *tereifah* birds.

### Contextual Leniency

Tosafot also addresses the blood-spot question in this context: *ve-lo chashinan shema haya ba-hem dam* — we do not worry that perhaps there were blood spots in the eggs used for baking, because *rov beitzim ein ba-hem dam* — most eggs simply do not have blood spots. What is striking about Tosafot's entire treatment is that he begins not from abstract legal analysis but from existing practice, and works backward to justify it. He starts from the fact that Jews were buying these eggs and these pastries, and then asks how that practice can be defended. This methodological move — defending common practice through legal reasoning — is transparent in Tosafot and raises the natural question of whether the justifications are analyses of reality or accommodations of it. Tosafot himself seems aware that if the realities were to change — if somehow the majority of eggs in a given locale were no longer from kosher species — his reasoning would not hold. His leniencies are inherently contextual, tied to the specific empirical conditions of the Jewish communities of medieval France and Germany.

## Reptile Egg Status

### Tumah Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: חֶלְבּוֹן וְחֶלְמוֹן מְעוֹרָבִין זֶה בָּזֶה – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהִיא בֵּיצַת הַשֶּׁרֶץ. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: לוֹמַר שֶׁאִם רִיקְּמָה וְנִיקְּבָה בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה – מְטַמֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **The Master said:** If the **albumen and yolk are mixed with each other, it is certainly the egg of a creeping animal** and not of a non-kosher bird. The Gemara asks: **For what *halakha*** is this information necessary? Either way, the egg is forbidden. **Rav Ukva bar Ḥama said:** The *baraita* means **to say that if tissue** of an embryo **developed [*rikema*]** inside it **and it was perforated,** and one touched it, **it transmits ritual impurity** provided **that** the embryo is at least the size **of a lentil-bulk,** because the carcass of a creeping animal of this size is a source of ritual impurity.

The *Gemara* returns to the ruling that intermingled white and yolk indicate an egg of a *sheretz*, and asks: *lema'i hilchata* — why does it matter? Non-kosher is non-kosher; whether the egg comes from a non-kosher bird or from a lizard, one may not eat it. Rav Ukva bar Chama provides the answer: it matters for *tumah*.

### Embryo & Tumah

Specifically: *she-rikma ve-nikva* — if the embryo has already begun to form inside the egg and there is a hole in the shell of the egg, then the developing creature *metameh ke-adashah* — conveys *tumah* even at the size of a lentil bean. The reason a hole is required is that the reptile embryo can only convey *tumah* if it is in principle touchable. If there is no hole in the shell, the embryo is sealed inside and cannot be contacted. If there is a hole, then the shell itself serves as a protective barrier (*dofen*) for the contents, and touching the shell is halakhically equivalent to touching the thing protected by it — but only if the thing inside is itself touchable, which requires that hole. The *tumah* implications differ significantly depending on the source: a developing lizard embryo conveys *tumah* at the size of a lentil bean, whereas a non-kosher bird does not convey *tumah* at such a small measure, and indeed may not convey *tumah* in the same category at all.

### Ravina Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: וְדִלְמָא דְּנָחָשׁ הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina objects to this: But perhaps it is** the egg **of a snake,** whose carcass does not transmit ritual impurity, even though the albumen and yolk of its eggs are mixed together.

Ravina challenges this analysis: *dilema de-nachash hi* — perhaps this is a snake egg, not a lizard egg. The Torah enumerates only eight *sheratzim* that convey *tumah*, and not all reptiles are on that list. A snake, for example, does not appear among the eight, and would therefore not convey *tumah* even if an embryo were developing. Simply knowing that the intermingled egg is of reptilian origin does not identify *which* reptile, and therefore cannot establish *tumah*.

### Rava Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁאִם רִיקְּמָה וְאָכְלָה – לוֹקֶה עָלֶיהָ מִשּׁוּם ״שֶׁרֶץ הַשֹּׁרֵץ עַל הָאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** The *baraita* does not teach about ritual impurity, but rather **that if tissue** of an embryo **developed** inside the egg **and one ate it, he is flogged on its account, due to** the prohibition: "And every **swarming thing that swarms upon the earth** is a detestable thing; it shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 11:41).

Rava responds by reframing the entire question: forget *tumah* entirely. *She-rikma ve-achlah loka lah mishum sheretz ha-shoretz al ha-aretz* — if an embryo has begun to form in this egg and one eats it, one has transgressed the biblical prohibition against eating a *sheretz*, a creeping thing. The relevance of identifying the egg as reptilian is therefore about the nature of the transgression, not about *tumah*. Eating the egg of a non-kosher bird that has not yet developed an embryo violates the general prohibition against eating derivatives of non-kosher animals — a prohibition of somewhat different character. Eating an egg in which a reptile embryo has begun to form is eating an actual *sheretz*, which is a distinct and more severe biblical transgression carrying *malkut* — lashes — if one has received the appropriate prior warning.

### Embryo Significance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא דִּטְמֵאָה? אֲפִילּוּ דִּטְהוֹרָה נָמֵי! דְּתַנְיָא: ״כׇּל הַשֶּׁרֶץ הַשֹּׁרֵץ עַל הָאָרֶץ״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶפְרוֹחִים שֶׁלֹּא נִפְתְּחוּ עֵינֵיהֶם! מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why specifically** mention the egg **of a non-kosher** creeping animal? The same would apply **even** for one who eats the embryo **of a kosher** bird **as well.** In either case, one is flogged, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse: **"Every swarming thing that swarms upon the earth,"** serves **to include chicks** of pigeons **whose eyes have not** yet **opened** in the prohibition against consuming swarming things. The Gemara responds: This prohibition is **by rabbinic** law, **and the verse** cited is **a mere support** for it. One who eats the embryo of a kosher bird is not liable to receive lashes.

Why does the formation of the embryo change matters so dramatically? A fundamental principle has been established throughout these dapim: products derived from non-kosher animals are themselves forbidden — *ha-ba min ha-tamei tamei* — but this derivative prohibition is not the same as the prohibition against eating the non-kosher animal itself. One who drinks camel's milk has not transgressed the prohibition of eating a camel. One who eats the egg of an eagle has not transgressed the prohibition of eating a non-kosher bird; one has transgressed the prohibition of eating a forbidden derivative. The Ramban articulates this distinction clearly. Once an embryo begins to form, however, one is no longer eating a derivative — one is eating the creature itself, an actual *sheretz*. It is only when one eats the thing itself that the most severe level of prohibition applies and *malkut* becomes relevant.

### Kosher Embryos

The *Gemara* presses further: if the transgression depends on an embryo developing, why does that apply only to reptile eggs? A *baraita* is cited that derives from the verse *kol ha-sheretz ha-shoretz al ha-aretz* — creeping things that creep on the ground — an inclusion of *efrochim she-lo niftchu eineihem*, chicks that have not yet opened their eyes. This teaching, attributed to Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, suggests that even a newly hatched chick, before it has opened its eyes, has a status resembling a creeping thing and is forbidden. If so, an embryo developing within a kosher hen's egg would seem to be equally problematic, regardless of whether the egg is from a reptile or a chicken.

The *Gemara* answers: *miderabbanan* — that prohibition on unopened-eyed chicks is only rabbinic. *Ve'kara asmachta be'alma* — the verse is merely a support, an *asmachta*, not the true biblical source of the prohibition. Obviously a chicken at any stage of development is not a *sheretz*, and therefore the concern about its embryo developing is only rabbinic in character. The *de'oraita* prohibition of eating a *sheretz* applies strictly to actual reptiles and their kind, not to embryos of kosher birds, even if at early developmental stages they superficially resemble creeping creatures.

## Blood Spots

### Unfertilized Spots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גִּיעוּלֵי בֵּיצִים מוּתָּרוֹת, בֵּיצִים מוּזָרוֹת – נֶפֶשׁ יָפָה תֹּאכְלֵם, נִמְצָא עָלֶיהָ קוֹרֶט דָּם – זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם וְאוֹכֵל אֶת הַשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Kosher **eggs** that have been **boiled** with non-kosher eggs are **permitted. Unfertilized eggs** are permitted, and one of **strong constitution may eat them,** even if the hen has sat on them for a long period of time. If **a drop [*koret*] of blood is found on it,** one **discards the blood,** which is the first stage of an embryo's development, **and eats the rest.**

The *Gemara* turns to a new *baraita*: *giulei beitzim mutarot* — eggs that have been expelled are permitted. Rashi understands this as referring to kosher eggs cooked in their shells together with non-kosher eggs: since virtually no flavor penetrates through a sealed eggshell during cooking, the kosher eggs remain entirely unaffected by the non-kosher ones present in the same pot. Tosafot, by contrast, reads *giulei beitzim* as referring to eggs spewed out prematurely — a hen that was struck and dropped its egg before its time. One might have thought such an egg is forbidden as *ever min ha-chai*, since it left the body of a live animal under duress rather than naturally. The *baraita* establishes that it is nevertheless permitted.

The next ruling: *beitzim muzarot* — brooded eggs, eggs that were never fertilized and upon which the hen sat for a long period — may be eaten if one is not finicky, as *nefesh yafah tocheilem* delicately puts it. If, however, a blood spot (*koret dam*) is found on such an egg, one discards the blood spot and eats the remainder.

### Kesher Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: וְהוּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא עַל קֶשֶׁר שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya said: And this** applies **when** the blood **is found on its knot,** i.e., the place in the albumen where the embryo begins to develop, since this proves that tissue has not developed elsewhere in the egg.

Rabbi Yirmeyah qualifies: *ve-hu she-nimtza al kesher shelah* — this ruling applies when the blood spot is found on the *kesher*, the cluster point. As Rashi explains, the *kesher* is the area at one end of the egg — either the rounded or pointed end — where the embryo would begin to form if the egg were fertilized. This is the location of concern.

### White vs Yolk Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי דּוֹסְתַּאי אֲבוּהּ דְּרַבִּי אַפְטוֹרִיקִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְצָא עַל חֶלְבּוֹן שֶׁלָּהּ, אֲבָל נִמְצָא עַל חֶלְמוֹן שֶׁלָּהּ – אֲפִילּוּ בֵּיצָה אֲסוּרָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּשְׁדָא תִּכְלָא בְּכוּלַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** And so **taught Dostai, father of Rabbi Aptoriki:** The Sages **taught** that one may eat the rest of the egg **only when** the blood **is found on its albumen,** i.e., the knot; **but** if **it is found on its yolk, even** the rest of **the egg is forbidden. What is the reason** for this? It is **that the decay has spread through all of it.**

Rabbi Dostai, the father of Rabbi Aptoriki, further refines: *lo shanu ela she-nimtza al chelbon shelah* — the ruling that one simply discards the spot and eats the rest applies only when the blood is found on the *white* of the egg. *Aval nimtza al chelmon shelah* — but if the blood spot is found on the *yolk*, then *afilu beitzah asurah* — the entire egg is forbidden. The reason: *d'shada tichla b'chulah* — the spoiling or corruption has spread throughout the egg. The interpretation is that when the blood is on the yolk, it indicates a more advanced stage of embryonic development, such that the entire egg is drawn into and affected by the formation process, rendering the whole egg forbidden rather than just the spot itself.

### Spot Prohibition Source

Tosafot raises this as a genuine question worth examining. Technically, the Torah forbids the blood of living animals. The blood spot on an egg is not blood drawn from a living animal in the ordinary sense — it is associated with the early formation of an embryo. Tosafot offers two possible answers. One is that the prohibition is entirely rabbinic: the rabbis forbade blood spots lest people become comfortable with blood generally and come to consume the biblically forbidden blood of animals. The second possibility Tosafot raises is that the prohibition may be biblical — on the grounds that since a chick will eventually form from this blood, it is considered as if one were consuming blood from an actual forming chick. Tosafot seems more inclined toward the rabbinic explanation but leaves the matter open.

The practical implication is significant: if the prohibition is only rabbinic and arises from the concern about embryo formation, it would follow that in unfertilized eggs — where there is no embryo and no formation process underway — there may be no prohibition at all, or at worst one discards only the spot. Tosafot notes that in his time, since essentially all available eggs were unfertilized (having never encountered a rooster), one who finds a blood spot in such an egg may simply discard the spot and eat the remainder, and one might even argue that technically not even the spot itself is biblically prohibited.

### Abaye Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב גְּבִיהָה מִבֵּי כְתִיל לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אִיפְּכָא תָּנֵי תַּנָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, וְאַבָּיֵי הוּא דְּתָרְצַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Geviha from Bei Ketil said to Rav Ashi: A *tanna* taught the opposite before Abaye,** that one may eat the rest of the egg only when the blood is found on its yolk, **and it is Abaye that corrected it** in **this** manner.

Rav Gaviha from Bei Katil reports to Rav Ashi that a *tanna* once taught the ruling in front of Abaye in the reversed form — with the stringency applying to the white rather than the yolk. Abaye corrected him and reversed the teaching back to its proper formulation: it is the spot on the yolk that renders the whole egg forbidden, while a spot on the white requires only the removal of the spot itself.

## Bat Ha-Ya'anah

### Etymology Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מִנַּיִן לְבֵיצַת טְמֵאָה שֶׁהִיא אֲסוּרָה מִן הַתּוֹרָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶת בַּת הַיַּעֲנָה״, וְכִי בַּת יֵשׁ לָהּ לַיַּעֲנָה? אֶלָּא אֵיזוֹ – זוֹ בֵּיצָה טְמֵאָה. וְדִלְמָא הַיְינוּ שְׁמַיְיהוּ? לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״בַּת עַמִּי לְאַכְזָר כַּיְעֵנִים בַּמִּדְבָּר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ḥizkiyya says: From where** is it derived **that the egg of a non-kosher** bird **is prohibited by Torah** law? **As it is stated: "And the daughter [*bat*] of the *ya'ana*"** (Leviticus 11:16). **But does the *ya'ana* have a daughter** whose forbidden status would be different from that of its mother? Both daughter and mother should be included in the same prohibition. **Rather, which** is this? **This is a non-kosher egg.** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps this is their name,** i.e., the bird is called *bat ya'ana*. The Gemara responds: This **should not enter your mind, as it is written: "The daughter of my people has become cruel, like the *ye'enim* in the wilderness"** (Lamentations 4:3).

From blood spots, the *Gemara* transitions to Chizkiyah's derivation of the prohibition against eating non-kosher bird eggs from the Torah itself. The verse listing forbidden birds includes *ve'et bat ha-ya'anah* — "and the daughter of the *ya'anah*," referring to the ostrich. Chizkiyah's reasoning: *vechi bat yesh lo la-ya'anah*? Does the ostrich have daughters in the sense of live-born offspring? Ostriches reproduce by laying eggs; they do not give birth to live young. The term *bat* — daughter, implying live birth — cannot refer to the offspring of the ostrich in the ordinary sense. Therefore, *zu beitzah teme'ah* — the term *bat ha-ya'anah* must refer to the egg of the ostrich. And since the Torah prohibits *bat ha-ya'anah* and that term denotes the egg, we have a biblical source establishing that eggs of non-kosher birds are themselves forbidden.

### Name vs Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״אֶעֱשֶׂה מִסְפֵּד כַּתַּנִּים וְאֵבֶל כִּבְנוֹת יַעֲנָה״? כְּיַעֲנָה זוֹ שֶׁמִּתְאַבֶּלֶת עַל בָּנֶיהָ. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְשָׁכְנוּ שָׁם בְּנוֹת יַעֲנָה״? כְּיַעֲנָה זוֹ שֶׁשּׁוֹכֶנֶת עִם בָּנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara persists: **And** is *bat ya'ana* **not** its name? **But isn't it written: "I will make a wailing like the jackals, and a mourning like the daughters [*benot*] of the *ya'ana*"** (Micah 1:8)? The Gemara responds: The verse means: I will make mourning **like this *ya'ana* that mourns for its children.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "And *benot ya'ana* shall dwell there"** (Isaiah 13:21)? The Gemara responds: Here too, the verse means: And they shall dwell there **like this *ya'ana* that dwells with its children.**

The *Gemara* challenges this immediately: *vedilma haynu shemah* — perhaps *bat ha-ya'anah* is simply the name of the ostrich, a proper designation for the bird itself, with no derivational significance. The *Gemara* dismisses this: *lo salka d'atach* — that reading does not work. The proof is from a verse in which the same animal is referred to simply as *ye'enim* without the *bat* prefix: *bat ami le'achzar ke-ya'enim ba-midbar* — the daughter of my people has become cruel like *ya'enim* in the wilderness. There the bird is called *ya'enim* alone; the *bat* is evidently an additional and meaningful word, not merely part of the bird's name.

### Scriptural Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: ״תְּכַבְּדֵנִי חַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה תַּנִּים וּבְנוֹת יַעֲנָה״, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בֵּיצָה, בֵּיצָה בַּת מֵימַר שִׁירָה הִיא? אֶלָּא, כְּתִיב ״הַיַּעֲנָה״, וּכְתִיב ״בַּת הַיַּעֲנָה״. וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא, דְּפָסֵק סָפְרָא לִשְׁתֵּי תֵּיבוֹת, וּמִדְּפָסֵיק לְהוּ סָפְרָא בְּתַרְתֵּי תֵּיבוֹת, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּרֵי שְׁמוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "The animals of the field shall honor Me, the jackals and the *benot ya'ana*"** (Isaiah 43:20)? **And if it enters your mind** that the term *bat ya'ana* is referring to **an egg, can an egg sing a song** of praise to God? **Rather,** one must say: **The *ya'ana* is written, and the *bat ya'ana* is** also **written,** and both are acceptable names for this bird. **And** still, the name **here is different** from those of other animals, in **that the scribe splits** it **into two words:** *Bat ya'ana*, even though it is one species. **And from** the fact **that the scribe splits it into two words, conclude from it** that **they are two names,** prohibiting the egg as well.

The *Gemara* responds with a counter-proof: *ve-ha ketiv e'eseh misped ke-tanim ve'evel ki-vnot ya'anah* — "I will make a mourning like jackals and a lamentation like *vnot ya'anah*." Here *vnot ya'anah* appears to be the name of the bird, using *bnot* — daughters — as part of the animal's designation. The *Gemara* answers: this verse means "like a *ya'anah* mourning for her children" — the *bnot* refers to the ostrich's actual offspring, her young, not to the animal itself as a species name. Another verse is then cited: *ve-shakhnu sham vnot ya'anah* — "and *vnot ya'anah* shall dwell there" — suggesting again that *vnot ya'anah* is simply the ostrich's name. The *Gemara*'s response: *ki yanah zo she-shochenet im baneha* — like an ostrich that dwells together with her children. Here too, *bnot* is understood as referring to the ostrich's offspring rather than constituting a species name.

The *Gemara* ends here, mid-analysis, leaving unresolved whether *bat ha-ya'anah* is ultimately the ostrich's name or a reference to the egg — and therefore whether Chizkiyah's derivation of the prohibition on non-kosher bird eggs from this verse stands or falls. That question will be taken up on the following daf.
