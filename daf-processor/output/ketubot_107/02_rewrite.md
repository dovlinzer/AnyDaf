# Ketubot 107 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's Position

The daf begins at the very bottom of 106b, where the Gemara takes up the mishna concerning the *shnei dayanei gezerot* and the case of *Admon*. Among Admon's rulings was the scenario of someone who traveled overseas (*limdinat hayam*) and left his wife without provision. Admon holds that the wife takes an oath at the end of the process — presumably when it comes time to collect her full *ketuba* upon his death, at which point she swears that nothing had been prepaid and that her collection of *mezonot* was conducted properly. The *bnei kohanim gedolim*, by contrast, require an oath at the beginning as well, ensuring that before she draws on the estate she genuinely deserves the money. Admon's concern is the wife's immediate need: should the *shvua* stand in the way of her being provided for? The Gemara does not fully spell out what divides the two positions, but it opens, at the top of 107a, with the dispute between Rav and Shmuel, which frames the entire discussion.

The case is stated plainly: *mi shehalach limdinat hayam* — a husband has gone overseas. Rav rules *poskin mezonot l'eshet ish*: *beit din* assesses the situation and provides an allowance for the wife. As Rashi explains, *beit din yordin lenichsei mi shehalach limdinat hayam uposkin mezonot leishto* — the court descends to the husband's property and liquidates assets as necessary to feed his wife, since he carries a clear *ketuba* obligation to support her.

### Shmuel's Objection

Shmuel disagrees: *ein poskin mezonot l'eshet ish*. One should not do this. Shmuel himself, however, concedes that Rav would agree with him on one point: *bishlosha chadashim harishonim* — in the first three months, even Rav would not yet seize the property. The reason is *lefi she'ein adam maniach beito reikam*: we do not assume a person would be so irresponsible as to leave his household entirely unprovided for. This already signals the logic behind Shmuel's broader position — we simply do not know what arrangements may have been made, and we must give the husband some benefit of the doubt.

The Gemara then clarifies where agreement ends and dispute begins. If the husband has been heard to have died (*sheshamu bo shemet*), nobody disagrees: the wife is supported as a widow from the estate. The dispute concerns only the case where no such report has arrived and the husband is simply absent. Rav rules *poskin*, since he is obligated to support her and is merely not physically present. Shmuel rules *ein poskin*, and two explanations are offered for his position. One: *emma tzerorei atfsa* — perhaps he had already set aside money for her, a bundle of funds deposited on her behalf. The other, offered by Rav Pappa: *chaishinan shema amar lah tzei ma'aseh yadayich bimzonotayich* — perhaps he had told her that in his absence her own labor would substitute for his obligation to feed her, so that she would keep her earnings in lieu of receiving *mezonot* from him.

Rashi and Tosafot both address whether the husband could make such an arrangement unilaterally or whether her agreement was required, and both conclude — though this is not straightforward from the Gemara itself — that the case involves her having agreed to it. The underlying issue goes back to earlier discussions about whose interest is protected by the exchange of *mezonot* for *ma'aseh yadayim*. In any case, Shmuel's point is that we cannot know which of these arrangements was in place, and therefore we cannot move to seize the husband's property on the basis of her claim alone.

One might then ask: why not simply require her to take a *shvua* and resolve the doubt? The general rule of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* places the burden of proof on the one seeking to extract money. Here, no one is explicitly denying her claim; the husband is simply absent. Yet Shmuel apparently feels the doubt is serious enough that a *shvua* would not suffice to override it and justify seizing someone's property. That seems to be at least part of the reason why the debate between Admon and the *bnei kohanim gedolim* about the timing of the *shvua* is relevant, though the Gemara leaves this somewhat open.

The Gemara then asks why there is no dispute in the case where the husband has actually died. Even there, after all, he might have set aside money for her, or they might have made the arrangement about her labor. Rashi addresses the *tzerorei* concern by noting that when she eventually collects her *ketuba* she must take a *shvua* that she has not pre-collected, and that resolves the matter. As for the *ma'aseh yadayim* arrangement — that cannot apply to the heirs; whatever he agreed to during his lifetime does not automatically bind the estate, and any new arrangement would have to be contracted afresh. Tosafot takes a different approach: the presumption that the husband made some arrangement (*ein adam maniach beito reikam*) is only reasonable when he is alive and presumably acting responsibly. Death, however, is not something people arrange for, and therefore we cannot extend the presumption to a man who has actually died. That is why, once he is dead, the doubts Shmuel raises no longer apply, and everyone agrees to support the wife.

### Abaye's Distinction

Abaye points out a practical difference between the two explanations of Shmuel's position. The case to consider is either: an adult wife who does not earn enough to support herself (*gedola v'lo safka*) — perhaps she has no marketable skills, or as Rashi explains, it is a time of famine and food is prohibitively expensive — or: a minor wife who does earn enough to support herself (*ktana v'safka*). These two scenarios pull in opposite directions depending on which of Shmuel's rationales one adopts.

If the concern is *tzerorei* — that the husband set aside funds — that presumption applies only to an adult wife, someone capable of taking possession of money and managing it responsibly. A husband would not deposit a sum of money for a minor wife who cannot be entrusted with it. So if the case involves a minor, the *tzerorei* concern evaporates, and we might well go ahead and provide for her. But if she is an adult who cannot earn her own keep, then *tzerorei* remains a live concern and we would not provide. Conversely, the *ma'aseh yadayim* concern — that he told her to work for herself — is meaningful only when she can actually do so. If she is an adult who cannot earn enough to live on, that arrangement was never a real option, and therefore we would provide for her. But if she is a minor who can earn, we would say perhaps such an arrangement is operative here. These are the *ikei beinayhu*, the practical differences between the two explanations.

## Mishna & Death

### Admon & Priests

The Gemara now turns to the text of the mishna itself. *Mi shehalach limdinat hayam v'ishto tova'at mezonot* — a man goes overseas and his wife demands support. *Chanan omer tishava basof* — Chanan says she takes an oath at the end, not at the outset. The *bnei kohanim gedolim* disagree and say she must swear both at the beginning and at the end.

The Gemara immediately clarifies: *ad kan lo pligi ela l'inyan shevu'ah* — their dispute is only about the oath. But as for actually giving her *mezonot*, all agree that she receives them — *aval mezonei yahvinan lah*. This directly contradicts Shmuel, who holds that we do not provide *mezonot* when the husband is merely absent. The Gemara therefore reports that Shmuel interpreted the mishna — *targama Shmuel b'sheshamu bo shemet* — as referring to a case where the husband has been reported dead, and the woman is being supported as a presumed widow. Of course, this reading is strained: if the man is dead, why does the mishna introduce him as someone who went overseas? It should simply say *mi shemet*. Nevertheless, this is Shmuel's *tirutz*.

### He Set Aside Money

A *braita* is cited that appears to expand on the mishna. The husband goes overseas, the wife demands support, and the *bnei kohanim gedolim* say she must take an oath while Chanan says she need not. Then: *v'im ba v'amar pasakti lah mezonot ne'eman* — if the husband returns and says he had already made arrangements for her support, he is believed. The question is when this claim is being made: if he has returned and made the claim, why did the matter need to go to *beit din* in the first place? It seems the scenario is that *beit din* had already acted — selling off property to feed her — and only then did the husband return and protest that he had already made arrangements.

### Rashi & Tosafot

Rashi explains that the husband is saying he had set aside money for her — *af tafsa tzerori* — a box of funds she could have drawn from. He is believed, and she must return what *beit din* disbursed — *umachzeret mah shenatna lah beis din*. But why is he simply believed if she currently holds the money? Rashi resolves this by saying he must take a *shvua* in support of his claim.

Tosafot's Ri offers a different reading: *ne'eman l'hatrichah shvuah* — he is believed to the extent that she must now swear to him. That is, rather than forcing her to return the money, we impose an oath on her: she swears that he did not in fact make the arrangement he claims. She does not automatically pay back the money, but she must now substantiate her position under oath. These two readings leave the practical mechanics of the case somewhat unclear, but both agree that the original seizure of property — before any such claim was made — was legitimate, and this is precisely the kind of scenario where Shmuel has to intervene and explain things away.

### Rumors Exaggerated

The Gemara adds: *hacha nami b'sheshamu bo shemet* — this too, says Shmuel, is the case where the husband had been reported dead. But then how could he come back? *Im ba achar shemuah* — like Mark Twain, the rumors of his death were greatly exaggerated. We had heard that he died, we treated his wife as a widow and supported her from the estate, and it turned out he was alive all along. According to Shmuel, this actually makes the ending of the *braita* more intelligible: since Shmuel holds that one should never sell off the property of a living husband, the fact that we did so and then he returned to protest makes perfect sense — we shouldn't have done it at all, and now that he's back and saying he had set aside money, we'll believe him.

A second *braita* makes the same point. The husband is overseas, the wife demands support, and the text says: *im ba v'amar tzei ma'aseh yadayich bimzonotayich* — if he comes and says she should have provided for herself from her own labor, he is permitted to say so. Again, Rashi notes that if *beit din* had already acted and sold property — *ma shepasqu pasqu* — that stands. And again, this *braita* explicitly acknowledges the two scenarios Shmuel uses to explain his position: the possibility that money was set aside (*pasakti lah mezonot*) and the possibility that a *ma'aseh yadayim* arrangement was made (*tzei ma'aseh yadayich bimzonotayich*). From Shmuel's perspective, the *braisos* themselves show these are live possibilities, which is precisely why we should not assume they are absent and proceed to seize property. Yet the simple reading of all these *braisos* is that *beit din* does provide for the wife as a matter of course, and deals with any such claims by the husband only if and when he comes forward with them.

## Children Case

### Wife Not Children

A third *braita* is cited and proves to be the most powerful challenge to Shmuel. *Beit din yordin lenichsav vezonin umfarnisin l'ishto* — the court descends to the husband's property and provides for his wife — *aval lo banav uvenosav* — but not for his sons and daughters, and not for anything else. This is a decisive proof against Shmuel's reading that all these cases presume the husband to be dead. If we were presuming the husband dead, then his sons would inherit and his daughters would be supported as *yetomot* from the estate. The very fact that the sons and daughters are excluded demonstrates that we are presupposing the husband is *alive* — we are simply exacting his existing legal obligation to feed his wife. Sons and daughters are excluded because a father has no legal obligation to feed his children beyond a very young age; his wife is a different matter entirely.

### Trustee Appointed

Rav Sheshet responds: *b'mashlish et ishto bidei shalish* — this is the case where the husband had appointed a trustee to manage disbursements to his wife. That trustee has now either left for overseas himself or the funds in his care have been exhausted. So we are not acting in ignorance of what arrangement the husband made — we know precisely what arrangement he made, and we know it has lapsed. That is why we now descend to his property: the previously-known arrangement is gone, and the doubt Shmuel raises about possible alternative arrangements no longer applies.

The Gemara then asks: if so, why not also provide for the sons and daughters? Presumably if the husband was organized enough to appoint a trustee, he cared about his whole household. The Gemara answers: *kesheshlish lezu v'lo hishlish lezu* — he made the arrangement specifically for his wife, not for his sons and daughters. His legal obligation ran to his wife; it is entirely reasonable that he funded his legal obligation and not beyond. The Gemara then asks *mai piska* — what is the logical basis for splitting? Why assume he provided for one and not the other? Tosafot finds this question puzzling, noting that the distinction between legal obligation and voluntary generosity is obvious. Perhaps the Gemara is suggesting that if he cared enough to appoint a trustee at all, he would not have abandoned his family. In any case, this response narrows the *braita* considerably: it now addresses only a specific, unusual scenario.

### One Witness

Rav Pappa offers an alternative explanation of the *braita*. *Kesheshama bo shemet b'ed echad* — we heard that the husband died through the testimony of a single witness. This creative solution draws on the well-known principle from Yevamot: a woman is permitted to remarry on the basis of *ed echad* testifying to her husband's death, because she is trusted to take such a consequential step only when she is genuinely certain. The practical difference between wife and children follows directly. The wife, because of the special dispensation of *ed echad* regarding remarriage, is treated as if she can presume her husband dead. Since even when her husband is alive she would be entitled to support, and since *ed echad* allows her to treat him as dead, we provide *mezonot* for her. Her sons and daughters, however, have no comparable dispensation: *ed echad* does not allow them to treat their father as dead for purposes of inheritance. From their perspective, the father is still alive, and therefore they may not descend to his property. The result is a peculiar situation: from the wife's standpoint her husband is dead, from the children's standpoint their father is alive.

One might ask whether this is entirely coherent — in Yevamot, the reliability of *ed echad* was predicated on the wife's willingness to expose herself to severe consequences if she is wrong. Here she is only seeking *mezonot*, not remarriage; the stakes are lower. This is a fair question, and Tosafot addresses under what circumstances *ed echad* would suffice for various monetary claims related to the husband's presumed death. But the Gemara here uses this mechanism to preserve Shmuel's position: even this powerful *braita*, with its explicit exclusion of sons and daughters, can be explained without conceding that we support a wife whose husband is simply absent and alive.

The Gemara also addresses *mai davar acher* — the other things the *braita* excludes. Rav Chisda says we do not provide *tachshit* — jewelry or, as Rashi notes, perfume — from the estate. Rav Yosef says we do not draw from the estate for *tzedakah* contributions. The one who excludes *tachshit* would certainly exclude *tzedakah*, since that is not even for the wife's benefit. The one who excludes *tzedakah* would still provide *tachshit*, on the reasoning that *lo nicha lei detitgana v'detinval* — the husband would not want his wife going about in a degraded state simply because he is away. Some basic expenditures on her appearance and wellbeing are still covered.

## Sister-in-Law & Divorce

### Yevamah

The Gemara cites another *braita*: the *yevamah* — a sister-in-law awaiting *yibbum* or *chalitzah* — is supported from her deceased husband's estate for the first three months. During this period she cannot yet perform *yibbum* because she may be pregnant. *Mikan ve'eylach* — thereafter, she is supported neither by her dead husband's estate nor by the *yavam*'s. This leaves her in a difficult middle state: the obligation to perform *yibbum* is still pending, but as long as it is pending she is no longer simply a widow drawing on her late husband's estate, and since the marriage to the *yavam* has not occurred, she is not yet his responsibility.

The *braita* continues: *amda badin u-varach* — she brought him to *beit din* to compel him to act, and he fled. In this case, he is responsible to support her. The Gemara asks whether this applies specifically because he ran away — evading his legal responsibilities — or whether it applies more broadly whenever *beit din* has formally called upon him to act and his inaction, whether culpable or not, traps her in this state of *agunah*. In any case, once *beit din* has summoned him and he is not fulfilling his obligation, he must provide for her even in his absence. The Gemara asks: is this not exactly the same as a husband being absent overseas and *beit din* providing for his wife? Shmuel responds: *lemai neichush lah* — what are we really worried about here? The two concerns that Shmuel raises in the main case do not apply here. *Ai mishum tzerorei* — maybe he set aside money? *Lo mikrav daiteih legabah* — he has no closeness to her; they are not yet married, he has only this pending *mitzva* of *yibbum* and moreover he ran away from it, so obviously he would not have prepared a fund for her. *Ai mishum ma'aseh yadeha* — maybe he told her to work for herself? *Lo mishtabda lei* — she is not obligated to give her *ma'aseh yadayim* to him; in this middle state before *yibbum* is performed, her labor does not belong to him, though his obligation to support her exists. Since neither concern applies, we provide for her, but this tells us nothing about the case where those concerns are live.

### Claiming Divorce

A further *braita*: *ha'isha shehalcha hi uva'alah limdinat hayam* — she and her husband went overseas together, and she returned saying *met ba'ali* — my husband died. She is believed. She may either be supported from the estate as an *almana* or collect her *ketuba* in a lump sum. But: *im amra geirashani ba'ali* — if instead she says he divorced her, a different rule applies. *Mitparneset v'holekhet ad kedei kesuvatah* — she is supported incrementally until the total value of her *ketuba* is reached. This is because a woman is believed when she claims her husband died but not when she claims he divorced her. Since we do not fully accept her claim of divorce, we cannot give her the *ketuba* as a lump sum — perhaps she is still married. But *mi'man nafshach* — either way there is a liability: if still married, he owes her *mezonot*; if divorced, she has a *ketuba* claim. So we pay out *mezonot* until it reaches the value of the *ketuba* and then stop. The *chiddush* is that by saying *geirashani* rather than *met*, she has actually harmed herself: had she said nothing, we would have continued supporting her indefinitely as a presumed widow. By introducing the claim of divorce, she capped her own entitlement.

The Gemara raises this *braita* as evidence that we support a woman from the estate even though the husband's status is uncertain — precisely what Rav holds. Shmuel, predictably, replies *hacha nami deshamu bo shemet* — here too, it is a case where the husband had been reported dead.

## Minor Refused

### Mi'un Case

The Gemara cites a further *braita* on the theme of *mi'un*. *Keitzad amru mima'enet ein lah mezonot* — in what case is a girl who refuses (*mima'enet*) her minor marriage left without *mezonot*? It cannot be while she is still living together with the husband under normal marital arrangements, because *ba'alah chayav bimzonoteha* — as long as they are together, the husband is obligated to support her. The case must therefore be: *ella k'gon shehalach limdinat hayam* — the husband has gone overseas. *Lavta v'achalah* — she borrowed money and used it to feed herself. *V'amda u'mi'anah* — and then she performed *mi'un*, retroactively dissolving the marriage as though it had never existed.

The *chiddush* is not merely that from this point forward there is no marriage — that is obvious. The *chiddush* is the retroactive effect: since *mi'un* unwinds the marriage entirely, she is not in a position to collect from her ex-husband the borrowed money she had spent on *mezonot*, because retroactively she was never truly married and he never had that obligation. Importantly, however, the *braita* implies that *had she not done mi'un*, she would have been entitled to recover that borrowed money from the estate. The husband, even while overseas, had an obligation to support her, and her borrowed expenditures during that period would have been collectible. *Ta'ama d'mi'anah* — it is specifically because she did *mi'un* that she loses the claim; *ha lo mi'anah, yehavinan lah* — had she not done *mi'un*, we would have provided.

This is again strong evidence for Rav's position. Shmuel responds: *amar lach Shmuel hacha b'mai askinan* — the case here is a special one. *Ei mishum tzerorei, l'ktana lo masar* — the concern that the husband set aside money does not apply to a minor wife; one does not entrust funds to a minor. *V'ei mishum ma'aseh yadeha, l'ktana lo safka* — and the concern that she would earn her own keep does not apply as a general rule to a minor; minors typically cannot support themselves. So in this case, uniquely, both of Shmuel's concerns are absent, and therefore even Shmuel agrees that she should be supported — but this is a narrow exception that tells us nothing about adult wives.

A question arises about how a minor could have borrowed money in the first place. The Gemara presupposes transactions that minors can engage in, either under the rabbinic category of *pe'utot* — transactions by children of sufficient understanding — or perhaps the borrowing was done by the mother on her behalf.

## Rabbi Stories

### Rav Dimi's Story

After exhausting the textual evidence, the Gemara turns to recorded practice. *Ki ata Rav Dimi* — when Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported: *ma'aseh ba lifnei Rabbi b'Beit She'arim* — a case came before *Rabbi* (Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi) in Beit She'arim, and he *pasak lah mezonot* — ruled to provide *mezonot* for the wife whose husband had gone overseas. *Lifnei Rabbi Yishmael b'Tzippori, v'lo pasak lah mezonot* — a similar case came before Rabbi Yishmael b'Rabbi Yose in Tzippori and he refused to provide.

### Yochanan's Challenge

*Tamah Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan was astonished, and asked: *v'chi mah ra'ah Rabbi Yishmael shelo pasak lah mezonot?* What could Rabbi Yishmael have seen to refuse support to this woman? After all, *halo nechelku Bnei Kohanim Gedolim v'Chanan ela l'inyan shvuah* — the only dispute in our mishna between Chanan and the *bnei kohanim gedolim* concerns the oath. *Aval mezonei yahvinan lah* — everyone agrees that *mezonot* is provided. How could Rabbi Yishmael simply refuse?

Rav Shemen bar Abba replied: *kvar tirgemah Rabbeinu Shmuel b'Bavel* — our master Shmuel already dealt with this mishna in Babylonia. His interpretation — *ksheshamu bo shemet* — explains the mishna as applying only to the case where the husband is presumed dead. Rabbi Yishmael presumably followed Shmuel's position and therefore refused to provide when the husband was merely absent. Rabbi Yochanan responded: *pastrisu bekulei hai?* — you are going to such lengths, twisting the plain meaning of the mishna by that much? Clearly that is not the *pshat*, and to do that to a woman who needs to eat is not a defensible *tirutz*.

### Ravin's Story

*Ki ata Ravin* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported the same story in reverse: the case came before *Rabbi* in Beit She'arim and *he* refused to provide, while the case before Rabbi Yishmael in Tzippori resulted in an order to provide. The same exchange between Rabbi Yochanan and Rav Shemen bar Abba follows verbatim. The two reports from Eretz Yisrael — Rav Dimi's and Ravin's — simply contradict each other on who ruled which way, and the Gemara makes no effort to reconcile them.

### Final Ruling

*V'hilchesa kevasei d'Rav* — the *halakha* follows Rav. We provide *mezonot* for a married woman whose husband is away. The entire sustained effort by Shmuel to explain away every *braita* and every recorded practice ultimately fails to persuade, and the final ruling goes with the straightforward reading of the mishna and all the supporting *braisos*: *poskin mezonot l'eshet ish*.

## Final Rulings

### Mezonot Ruling

The Gemara summarizes three *hilchesos* as the masekhta draws to its close. The first follows directly from the foregoing discussion: *u-paskinan mezonot l'eshet ish* — we rule that *beit din* provides *mezonot* for a wife whose husband has gone overseas.

### Woman's Right

The second ruling: *v'hilchesa kevasei d'Rav Huna* — we rule in accordance with Rav Huna citing Rav, who said: *yechola isha she-tomar l'ba'alah eini nizonet v'eini osa* — a woman may unilaterally declare to her husband that she does not wish to receive *mezonot* from him and will not turn over her *ma'aseh yadayim* to him. The exchange of *mezonot* for labor was established to protect *her* interests, not his. She is therefore entitled to waive *mezonot* and retain her own earnings. This is not a decision reserved to the husband — she may invoke it herself.

### Kunia Vessels

The third ruling: *v'hilchesa kevasei d'Rav Zvid b'kunya* — we rule in accordance with Rav Zvid regarding *kunya*, which are glazed pottery vessels. The topic is included here apparently because the Gemara favors grouping *hilchesos* in threes. Rashi understands the glaze to be a metallic coating; Tosafot understands it as glass glaze.

Rav Zvid rules: *hanei mane d'kunya chivarei v'uchmei sharei* — white and black *kunya* vessels are permitted. *Yerokei, asirei* — green ones are forbidden. Rashi identifies the problematic substance in the green glaze as *alum*, a specific mineral compound (not the modern aluminum) that causes the glaze to absorb and then retain absorbed taste so that it cannot be extracted. The context is most likely wine of non-Jews (*stam yeinam*) stored cold — the question being whether the pottery absorbs enough of the wine's flavor that the taste cannot be expelled through normal *kashering*.

Even permissible white or black vessels must meet an additional condition: *ella d'reish bei kartufi* — there must be no ridges, cracks, or indentations. *Aval it beih kartufei, assur* — if there are such imperfections, the taste enters the crevices and cannot be expelled, rendering the vessel forbidden.

Tosafot, proceeding from the premise that the glaze is glass, adds an important observation. *Aval b'hataxas zechuchis nicha d'afilu konyah shel zechuchis einu ne'asar* — in principle, genuinely glass-glazed vessels would not absorb, *lefi shenisbashel heiteiv v'eino nivla* — because well-processed pure glass is non-porous. The problem with *kunya* is that the glass in question was not fully processed and contained metallic impurities or alloys that caused it to absorb. This sets up a broader question that Tosafot engages implicitly: does the non-porous character of true glass extend to hot liquids as well as cold, and what is the general *halakhic* status of glass vessels? These questions carry through into the wider *halakhic* literature on *kashering* glass, where there is significant debate about whether glass is entirely non-absorbent under all circumstances or only under specific conditions.
