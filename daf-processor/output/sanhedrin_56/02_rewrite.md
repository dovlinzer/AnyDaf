# Sanhedrin 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blasphemy & God's Name

### Intro to Blasphemy

The daf opens at the very end of 55b, transitioning from the category of sexual sins to sins relating to God directly. The Mishnah states: *hamigadef eino chayav ad sheyifareish hashem* — one who blasphemes God is not liable unless he explicitly articulates God's name. The immediate question is what "explicitly" means: does it require pronouncing the four-letter name, *yud-kei-vav-kei*, or does using one of the other divine names — such as *Elohim* — also suffice? The Gemara will address this in full.

### Kinuy in Examination

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha teaches that throughout the proceedings, the witnesses are cross-examined using a *kinuy* — which here, as Rashi explains at length, does not mean an alternative divine name but rather a substitution in which the content of the curse is expressed with reference to someone else entirely. The witnesses would say *yakeh Yose et Yose* — "May Yose smite Yose." Rashi notes that the name *Yose* is four letters, shares the letters *yud* and *vav* with the divine name, and even shares the *gematria* of *Elohim*, making it a transparent but formally safe stand-in. The point is that the witnesses demonstrate what they heard without themselves uttering an actual curse of God.

### Witness Procedure

When the final judgment arrives — *nigmar hadin* — indirect testimony is no longer sufficient. You cannot condemn someone to death on the basis of inference; direct testimony is required. The court therefore clears the room of everyone except the judges and the witnesses, since exposing others unnecessarily to the words of such a curse would be disrespectful. The senior witness is then asked to state explicitly what he heard — *emor mah sheshamata beferush*. When he does so, the judges rise to their feet, tear their garments, and do not sew them up, because hearing a curse of God's name — even when recounted by a witness with no blasphemous intent — obligates *keriah*. The second and third witnesses then each say *af ani kamohu*, "I too heard the same," rather than repeating the words themselves. The procedure thus limits repetition of the curse to the absolute minimum required for a capital verdict.

### Nokev Shem Source

A *braitah* states that liability requires *ad sheyivarech shem beshem* — the blasphemer must curse God *with* God's name, making God both subject and object of the imprecation. The Gemara asks: what is the scriptural source for this requirement? Shmuel derives it from the verse in Parashat Emor: *venokev shem Hashem mot yumat* — "one who *nokev* the name of God shall be put to death" — which then concludes *benokvo shem yumat*, "in his *nokev*-ing of the Name he shall die." The repetition of *shem* — name upon name — establishes the principle of *shem beshem*. The punishment is stoning, as derived from *ragom yirgemu bo kol ha'edah*. The prohibition (*azhara*) is from *elohim lo tekallel*, "do not curse God." As for knowing that *nokev* means to curse, the proof is from Bilaam's rhetorical question: *mah ekov lo kaboh El* — "how shall I curse what God has not cursed?" — where the root *kuf-bet-heh* unambiguously means cursing.

## Nokev Debate

### Pierce vs. Curse

The Gemara does not accept the identification of *nokev* with cursing without first exploring alternatives. The first alternative: *v'eima mivraz hu* — perhaps *nokev* means to pierce or puncture, as in *vayikov chor be-dalto*, "he pierced a hole in his door." On this reading, the offense would consist of physically destroying a written form of God's name. The *azhara* would then come from *lo ta'asun kein la'Hashem Eloheichem* — "you shall not do thus to the Lord your God" — the verse from which we derive the prohibition against erasing divine names, the foundation of all the laws of *sheimos*. Could destroying something bearing God's name be capital?

The Gemara responds: *ba'ina shem beshem ve-leika* — we require the structure of God's name acting upon God's name, and that structure is absent from this reading. The Gemara entertains a creative counter: perhaps one places two pieces of parchment inscribed with God's name on top of each other and pierces through both — that would be God's name piercing God's name. The Gemara rejects this: *hu nokev ve-chozer ve-nokev hu* — that is simply piercing God's name twice in succession; it is not using one instance of God's name as the agent to pierce another.

### Shem B'Shem Logic

The Gemara proposes a second scenario: carve God's name onto the edge of a knife and then use that knife to cut through a parchment bearing God's name. Now God's name is the instrument piercing God's name. The Gemara again rejects it: *hahu churpa de-sakina hu de-ka baza* — it is the sharpness of the knife that does the cutting, not the name engraved on it. The name merely happens to be present on the knife; it is not the operative agent. This stands in contrast to the verbal case of cursing, where one actually invokes God as the subject — calling upon God to act against God. The non-verbal scenarios cannot replicate this structure.

A closely related question presents itself at this point: if the prohibition is formulated verbally, does writing a blasphemous curse count? Normally the question of whether writing is equivalent to speech — *hirhur ke-dibbur* — is disputed. Here, the formulation *be-nokvo shem*, with its emphasis on articulation, suggests intuitively that one would have to actually utter the words; writing alone would likely not suffice. This remains an open question worth investigation.

### Articulation Issue

A third alternative: perhaps *nokev* means simply to articulate, as in *asher nikvu be-shemot* — "those who were mentioned by name" in the census narrative. On this reading, the sin would be pronouncing God's explicit name — *yud-kei-vav-kei* as it is spelled — which would normally only be done by the *kohen* in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *azhara* would come from *et Hashem Elohecha tira* — you shall fear the Lord your God — with certain licensed contexts being exceptions.

The Gemara offers two objections. First, *ba'ina shem beshem ve-leika*: articulating God's name cannot satisfy the *shem beshem* requirement, since there is no way to have God's name function as both subject and object in a verbal act. Second — and this is where the Gemara's legal reasoning becomes particularly striking — *ve-od haveya azharat asei, ve-azharat asei lo shema azhara*: fearing God is a positive commandment, not a prohibition. A positive commandment cannot serve as the required *azhara* for a capital offense. This reflects a fundamental principle: *ein onshin ela im ken mazhirin* — punishment presupposes a prohibition. The legal logic here deserves attention. The Rabbis understood the entire framework of divine punishment as requiring a genuine act of transgression against an explicit prohibition. A positive commandment — something one is obligated to do — describes a failure of omission; the death penalty requires a violation of a *lav*, an active commission of a forbidden act. When the Gemara cannot find a negative prohibition for the "articulation" interpretation, that itself becomes proof that *nokev* cannot mean articulation.

One might note, as raised in this context, that *elohim lo tekallel* — the *azhara* for cursing — is sometimes read as a prohibition against cursing judges. This is a valid reading: *elohim* in Scripture frequently denotes judges. If the verse is used for cursing God, what happens to cursing a judge? The answer involves the principle that a *lav she-nitan le-azharat misat beit din* — a prohibition used to ground a capital offense — cannot simultaneously serve as the *azhara* for another offense that would otherwise yield lashes. So someone who curses a judge would not receive *malkut* through that verse.

### Nokev = Curse

With both alternative readings eliminated, the Gemara turns to the narrative context to confirm that *nokev* means to curse. The verse describing the incident that prompted this entire legislation reads: *vayikov et ha-shem vayikallel* — "he *nokev*-ed the name and he cursed." One might read this as two distinct acts: articulating God's name and separately cursing. The Gemara considers this possibility — *de-dilma ad d'avid tarvaihu*, perhaps both acts together constitute the offense. But the Gemara refutes this from the verdict: the Torah instructs, *hotzei et ha-mekallel* — "take out the one who cursed" — not *hotzei et ha-nokev*, and certainly not *hotzei et ha-nokev ve-ha-mekallel*. The verdict language refers solely to the *mekallel*, the curser. This demonstrates that *nokev* is not a second distinct act but a synonym for the act of cursing — more precisely, *shema mina chada hi*, they are one and the same thing. The *peshat* of the verse supports this: *nokev* here means to curse, and specifically to curse using God's explicit name — the act of articulating God's name in the context of a malediction.

## Explicit vs. Kinuy

### Two Clauses

Having established the meaning of *nokev*, the Gemara turns to the full structure of the verse: *ish ish ki yekallel elohav ve-nasa cheto — ve-nokev shem Hashem mot yumat*. The text presents what appears to be two distinct clauses with two distinct consequences. The first clause — *yekallel elohav*, "he curses his God" — carries the consequence *ve-nasa cheto*, he bears his sin, which the Gemara understands as *karet* without court-administered punishment. The second clause — *ve-nokev shem Hashem* — carries *mot yumat*, death by the court. The straightforward reading is that the first clause refers to cursing with a *kinuy*, an alternative divine name like *Elohim*, without using the four-letter name and without the *shem beshem* structure, while the second clause refers to cursing with God's explicit name in the full *shem beshem* formulation.

The *braitah* articulates the question explicitly: since we already know from *ve-nokev shem Hashem mot yumat* that cursing God with the explicit name incurs death, what is the purpose of *ki yekallel elohav*? The answer initially proposed is that *ki yekallel elohav* comes to include *kinuyim* — alternative divine names — within the scope of the prohibition.

### Rabbi Meir View

Rabbi Meir reads the verse expansively. The *braitah* records his position: *yachol lo yehei chayav ela al shem ha-meyuchad bilvad* — one might think that liability extends only to the explicit name; *minayin le-rabot kol ha-kinuyim? Talmud lomar: ish ki yekallel elohav mikol makom* — the verse *ki yekallel elohav* comes to include all *kinuyim*, in all cases. For Rabbi Meir, both clauses carry the death penalty. The consequence of *ve-nasa cheto* — he bears his sin — does not mean the offense is downgraded to *karet*; it means that when there are no witnesses and no warning, the offender bears his sin through divine punishment rather than court-administered death. But the act of cursing with a *kinuy* is capital in principle. This is a striking position.

### Sages View

The Sages (*chachamim*) read the two clauses as marking a genuine legal distinction: *al shem ha-meyuchad be-mita ve-al ha-kinuyim be-azhara* — using God's explicit name incurs the death penalty, while using a *kinuy* carries only the prohibition and its consequence of *karet* but not court-administered execution. This is the more straightforward reading: the verse itself presents two different formulations with two different legal consequences.

## Non-Jews & Blasphemy

### Ish Ish Non-Jews

The verse opens with the doubled phrase *ish ish* — "any man, any man" — which the *braitah* derives as coming *le-rabot et ha-ovdei kochavim she-muzharim al birkat hashem*: to include non-Jews, who are among those warned against blaspheming God, and who in fact carry this prohibition as one of the seven *mitzvot* of *bnei Noach*. The natural question: but isn't the source for the non-Jewish prohibition against blasphemy already derived from the verse *vayitzav Hashem Elokim al ha-adam*, which will shortly be established as the root of all seven *mitzvot*? Why is *ish ish* needed?

Rav Yitzchak Napcha answers: the *ish ish* is not needed to establish the prohibition for non-Jews as such — that is already known — but rather *le-rabot ha-kinuyim*, to include *kinuyim*, specifically *aliba de-Rabbi Meir*. That is: according to Rabbi Meir's view that cursing with a *kinuy* is a capital offense, *ish ish* extends that ruling to non-Jews. For Rabbi Meir, the placement of non-Jews within this verse means non-Jews are prohibited even when using a *kinuy*. For the Sages, the question of whether non-Jews are held to the same distinction as Jews — explicit name yielding capital punishment, *kinuy* yielding *karet* — will be explored in the view of Rav Manashia.

### Kigar Ka'Ezrach

The verse continues: *kager ka'ezrach be-nokvo shem yumat* — "like the stranger, like the native citizen, in his *nokev*-ing of the Name he shall die." Rabbi Meir uses this phrase differently from the Sages. Since for Rabbi Meir the entire verse already equalizes Jews and non-Jews in the scope of liability — *ish ish* having placed non-Jews fully within the prohibition including for *kinuyim* — the phrase *kager ka'ezrach* does not serve to create a distinction in liability. Instead, it distinguishes the *mode* of execution: *ger ve-ezrach be-skila, aval oved kochavim be-sayif* — Jews are executed by stoning, non-Jews by the sword. The principle that all capital offenses for *bnei Noach* are carried out by the sword is established elsewhere, but this verse clarifies that even though *ish ish* places non-Jews within the category of this offense, they do not thereby assume the Jewish punishment of stoning. The verse's parallel formulation might have suggested *hoa'il ve-israbu israbu* — since they are included, include them entirely — but *kager ka'ezrach* forestalls this by restricting stoning to the *ger* and *ezrach*.

### Napcha Non-Jew View

Rav Manashia argues that the debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages regarding Jews does not apply symmetrically to non-Jews. Even according to the Sages — who hold that a Jew cursing with a *kinuy* incurs only *karet* and not the death penalty — a non-Jew cursing with a *kinuy* is liable to death. The source: *de-amar kra kager ka'ezrach* — the verse *kager ka'ezrach* establishes that the full legal distinction between *kinuy* and explicit name, with *kinuy* carrying only *karet*, applies to the *ger* and the *ezrach*, meaning those subject to the Jewish legal framework. For non-Jews, that distinction does not apply, and even a *kinuy* suffices for liability.

The Gemara then asks: if, according to this view, the distinction between *kinuy* and explicit name holds equally for Jews and non-Jews, what does *kager ka'ezrach* add for Rav Yitzchak Napcha? His answer: *ger ve-ezrach hu de-ba'inan shem beshem, aval oved kochavim lo ba'inan shem beshem* — it is for the Jew that the *shem beshem* structure is required; for the non-Jew, while the explicit name is necessary, the full double structure — God's name as both subject and object of the curse — is not required. This is a precise and somewhat technical distinction: a non-Jew who curses God using God's name is liable even without invoking God as the agent of the curse.

The Gemara further asks why *ish ish* is needed at all in Rav Yitzchak Napcha's scheme, given that the non-Jewish obligation is already derivable and the distinctions are already clear. The answer: *dibra Torah ke-lishon bnei adam* — the Torah speaks in ordinary human language, and the doubled *ish ish* is simply the stylistic form of an inclusive statement rather than a technical derivation. The practical upshot of all the positions can be summarized: for a Jew, *shem beshem* is required, and whether a *kinuy* alone triggers capital punishment or merely *karet* is debated between Rabbi Meir and the Sages. For a non-Jew, stoning is replaced by the sword, *shem beshem* is not required, and according to Rav Manashia even the Sages would agree that a *kinuy* suffices for liability. Whether Rabbi Meir's view about *kinuyim* applies symmetrically to non-Jews, or whether *ish ish* places them in the full scope of Rabbi Meir's position including *shem beshem*, remains a point within the discussion.

## Sheva Mitzvot Intro

### Seven List

Having discussed the prohibition against blasphemy in the context of non-Jews, and having mentioned that it is one of the seven *mitzvot* of *bnei Noach*, the Gemara now opens the extended treatment of those seven commandments. A *braitah* states: *sheva mitzvot nistavu bnei Noach* — the *bnei Noach* were commanded in seven *mitzvot*: *dinim* (a legal system), *birkat Hashem* (prohibition against cursing God), *avodah zarah* (idolatry), *giluy arayot* (sexual immorality), *shefichut damim* (murder), *gezel* (theft), and *eiver min ha-chai* (eating a limb torn from a living animal). A useful mnemonic for the seven: the three major categories — *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayot*, and *shefichut damim* — are the "big three," and the remaining four follow the acronym *alef-bet-gimel-dalet*: *eiver min ha-chai*, *birkat Hashem*, *gezel*, and *dinim*.

### Additional Opinions

Beyond the standard seven, additional opinions expand the list. Rabbi Chananya ben Gamla adds blood from a living animal — *dam* in addition to *eiver min ha-chai*. Rabbi Chidkya adds *sirus* — emasculation or sterilization, applicable to both animals and people. Rabbi Shimon adds *kishuf* — witchcraft. Rabbi Yossi elaborates on Rabbi Shimon's position: everything enumerated in the Torah's passage on witchcraft in Deuteronomy (*lo yimatzei becha ma'avir beno u-vito ba-esh, kosem kesamim, me'onen u-menachesh u-mechashef, ve-chover chaver, ve-sho'el ov ve-yidoni, ve-doresh el ha-metim*) is prohibited to a *ben Noach*. The reasoning is that the Torah states these abominations caused God to drive the nations from the land — and the principle *lo anash ha-katuv ela im ken hizhir* demands that punishment presupposes warning. If God punished the nations for witchcraft, they must have been warned against it through their own seven commandments. Rabbi Eliezer adds *kilayim* — crossbreeding of animals and grafting of trees (though planting mixed seeds is permitted to non-Jews).

The rationale for including *sirus* and *kilayim* in the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* — even without explicit scriptural derivation — is worth considering. The human being was commanded *pru u-revu*, to be fruitful and multiply, from the very earliest chapters of Genesis; *sirus* directly frustrates this imperative. And the Torah's account of creation repeatedly emphasizes *le-minehu* — each creature reproducing according to its kind — which *kilayim* violates. These prohibitions thus connect to the fundamental structure of creation as God designed it: living things are to multiply and to do so true to their nature. As for *kishuf*, it too can be understood as related to *avodah zarah* or alternatively as a violation of the natural order.

### Vayitzav Source

The Gemara asks: *mena hani mili* — what is the scriptural basis for all seven? Rabbi Yochanan derives them from a single verse: *vayitzav Hashem Elokim al ha-adam lemor, mikol etz ha-gan achol tochel* — "and the Lord God commanded the human, saying: of every tree of the garden you may freely eat." This is a remarkable piece of hermeneutics. From a verse that on its plain reading grants permission to eat, the Rabbis extract all seven universal commandments. The method: each word of the verse is made to correspond to one of the seven, as will be detailed in the following section.

## Exegesis Details

### Dinim Courts

*Vayitzav* — "and He commanded" — corresponds to *dinim*, the legal system. A supporting verse: God says of Abraham *ki yedativ lemaan asher yitzaveh et banav ve-et beito ve-shamru derech Hashem la'asot tzedakah u-mishpat* — "I have known him, that he will command his children and his household after him, and they will keep the way of the Lord, doing righteousness and justice." The combination of *vayitzav* with *tzedakah u-mishpat* points to the substantive content of a just legal order. This connection will bear on the later question of whether *dinim* means merely a court system or a full body of civil law.

### Birkat Hashem

*Hashem* — the divine name — corresponds to *birkat Hashem*, the prohibition against cursing God. The supporting verse is the one just analyzed: *ve-nokev shem Hashem mot yumat*.

### Avodah Zarah

*Elokim* — the word for God used in creation — corresponds to *avodah zarah*, idolatry. The supporting verse: *lo yihyeh lecha elohim acherim al panai*.

### Shfichut Damim

*Al ha-adam* — "concerning the human" — corresponds to *shefichut damim*, murder. This is the most explicit of the seven, grounded in the verse spoken directly to Noah: *shofech dam ha-adam ba-adam damo yishapech* — "one who sheds the blood of a human, by a human shall his blood be shed."

### Giluy Arayot

*Lemor* — "saying" — corresponds to *giluy arayot*, forbidden sexual relations. The supporting verse is from the prophets: *lemor, hein yishalach ish et ishto ve-halcha me-ito ve-haysah le-ish acher* — if a man sends away his wife and she goes and becomes another man's, she may not return to the first. This is admittedly a slender textual link; any verse containing *lemor* would serve equally well. The choice of *lemor* for *giluy arayot* is likely deliberate. Of the seven, certain categories of *giluy arayot* — a brother's wife after his death, a son-in-law's wife after divorce — may appear more arbitrary than others, less transparently derivable from natural intuition. The word *lemor*, with its connotation of "because I said so," may reflect that the prohibition in certain of its expressions rests on divine command rather than self-evident moral reasoning. This connects to a broader debate about the nature of the *sheva mitzvot*: Moses Mendelssohn wished to treat them as a Jewish version of natural law, which is why he was troubled by the Rambam's ruling that a *ben Noach* who observes them because they make rational sense — rather than because God commanded them through Moses — does not receive full credit. The Rambam's formulation emphasizes divine command; the Noda Bi-Yehuda was reportedly unable to identify the Rambam's precise source for this ruling.

### Ever Min Hachai

*Achol tochel* — "you may freely eat" — yields *eiver min ha-chai* through a reading offered by Rashi: the doubled form *achol tochel* — something that is already *ochel*, already in the status of food, you may eat — implies that something not yet in the status of food may not be eaten. A living animal, which is still engaged in producing offspring, giving milk, and giving wool, is not yet "food." Tearing a limb from it violates the permission structure of *mikol etz ha-gan achol tochel*. The Gemara is also working to root this commandment in the verse about Adam, even though *eiver min ha-chai* is most famously stated explicitly to Noah in the verse *ach basar be-nafsho damo lo tocheilu*. The drive is to locate all seven within a single verse addressed to the first human being.

### Gezel Theft

*Mikol etz ha-gan* — "of every tree of the garden" — carries the implicit meaning that the trees God designated are available; what is not designated is not available. The explicit grant of permission implies the existence of things for which no permission has been granted, and taking those without authorization constitutes *gezel*.

## Dinim for Jews

### Yitzchak Reversed

Rabbi Yitzchak, who taught when he came from Eretz Yisrael, taught *ipcha* — a reversed attribution. He maintains the same list of seven but reassigns the derivations: *vayitzav* corresponds not to *dinim* but to *avodah zarah*, and *Elokim* corresponds not to *avodah zarah* but to *dinim*.

### Making vs. Worship

The reassignment is straightforward for *Elokim* meaning *dinim* — the verse *ve-nikrav ba'al ha-bayit el ha-Elohim* shows that *Elokim* denotes judges. But how does *vayitzav* connect to *avodah zarah*? This requires explanation, which the Gemara supplies through a dispute.

### Chisda Dispute

Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avdimi disagree over which verse links *vayitzav* to *avodah zarah*. One says the verse is *saru maher min ha-derech asher tzivitim, asu lahem eigel masecha* — "they turned quickly from the path I commanded them and made a golden calf." The word *tzivitim* — "I commanded them" — connects to *vayitzav*, and the subject is idolatry. The other says the verse is *ashuk Efrayim, retzutz mishpat, ki ho'il acharei tzav* — Ephraim has been oppressed and trampled in judgment, because they walked after the command of the false prophet and worshiped the *ba'al*. Here *tzav* again means a command, but the command of a false prophet leading to idolatry.

The practical difference between the two views is whether *akum she-asah avodah zarah ve-lo hishtachavah lah* — a non-Jew who made an idol but did not bow down to it — is liable. The verse *asu lahem eigel masecha* emphasizes the act of making: *mishe'at asiyah mechayev*, from the moment of making there is liability. The verse *ki ho'il acharei tzav* emphasizes the act of following and worshiping: *ad de-azil batra u-falach*, only once they actually go and worship do they incur liability. Normally the assumption is that *avodah zarah* for *bnei Noach* requires an act of worship rather than merely the making of an idol — but if the verse about the *eigel* is the source, even making may be prohibited.

Rava raises a challenge: can anyone truly maintain that a non-Jew is liable for making an idol without worshiping it? A *braitah* states: *be-Akum, devarim she-beit din shel Yisrael memitin aleihem, ben Noach muzhar aleihem* — regarding non-Jews, they are prohibited only for acts for which a Jewish court would execute a Jew. Since a Jewish court would not execute a Jew merely for making an idol, a non-Jew should likewise not be liable for making alone. Rav Pappa responds that the *braitah* comes to exclude *gipuf ve-nishuk* — hugging and kissing an idol as a form of adoration — which is not a mode of worship for which a Jewish court would execute, and is therefore not an act of worship that makes a non-Jew liable either. The case of making an idol is different: *gezel* is the clearest example of an act for which a non-Jew is liable by death but for which a Jewish court does not execute a Jew. So it is not a universal rule that non-Jewish liability tracks Jewish capital liability; it is a rule specific to acts of *avodah zarah*, where the qualifying criterion is whether the act constitutes genuine worship.

### Marah Ten Mitzvot

The discussion of *dinim* raises a broader question: were *bnei Noach* actually commanded in *dinim* at all? A *braitah* states that at Marah — the Israelites' first encampment after leaving Egypt — they received ten *mitzvot*: the seven they had already accepted as *bnei Noach*, plus *dinim*, *Shabbat*, and *kibud av va'em*. The inclusion of *dinim* here as something newly added at Marah implies that it was *not* among the original seven. The source for *dinim* at Marah: *sham sam lo chok u-mishpat* — "there He gave them statute and justice." The source for *Shabbat* and *kibud av va'em*: the phrase *ka'asher tzivcha Hashem Elohecha* in the second Decalogue is read as referring to a prior command, and the only prior command on record is at Marah.

## Jewish Court Requirements

### Nachman Court Rules

The Gemara reconciles the tension between the *braitah* placing *dinim* at Marah and the standard list that includes *dinim* among the seven. Rav Nachman bar Rabbah bar Avuha explains: *lo nitzrecha ela le-edah ve-edim ve-hatra'ah* — what was added at Marah was not the category of *dinim* as such, but its specifically Jewish requirements. For *bnei Noach*, a legal system functions without the full Jewish procedural constraints: one witness suffices, even hearsay may suffice, and no formal warning is required. What was added for Israel at Marah was that capital cases require an *edah* — a full court of twenty-three — two *edim*, formal witnesses, and *hatra'ah*, a warning administered before the act.

The Gemara objects: if only procedural refinements were added, the *braitah* should have said "they added to *dinim*," not "they added *dinim*." This implies a categorical addition, not a procedural one.

### Kenasot Fines

Rava therefore offers an alternative: *lo nitzrecha ela le-dinei kenasot* — what was added was the authority to adjudicate fines, that is, monetary penalties beyond the actual damage incurred. Rashi explains that the *dinim* of *bnei Noach*, as derived from *la'asot tzedakah u-mishpat* by Abraham, encompasses fair compensation — rectifying actual loss. But a *kenas*, a fine that exceeds the damage, goes beyond restitution into a different register of deterrence-based liability. That authority was granted to the Jewish legal system at Marah but was not part of the universal Noahide legal obligation. This interpretation also offers evidence supporting the Ramban's position: the *dinim* of *bnei Noach* appears to include substantive civil law, not merely the organizational infrastructure of courts. The fact that Jews were given expanded jurisdiction over *kenasot* implies that the underlying *dinim* obligation encompasses real legal substance.

The Gemara objects again: the *braitah* says they "added *dinim*," implying a new category, whereas the addition of *kenasot* is an expansion within an existing category.

### Courts Districts

Rav Acha bar Yaakov therefore proposes: *lo nitzrecha ela le-hoshiv batei dinim be-chol pelech u-felech u-ve-chol ir va-ir* — what was specifically added for Jews was the obligation to establish courts in every district and in every city, not merely to have a functional legal system but to ensure its geographic comprehensiveness throughout the land. This sounds categorical enough to justify saying "they added *dinim*." However, a *braitah* then challenges even this: *ke-shem she-nistavu Yisrael le-hoshiv batei dinim be-chol pelech u-felech u-ve-chol ir va-ir, kach nistavu bnei Noach* — just as Israel was commanded to establish courts in every district and city, so too were *bnei Noach*. This obligation was not uniquely added to Israel; it was always part of the universal commandment. Taken together, the course of this discussion makes clear that *dinim* for *bnei Noach* encompasses both the creation of court systems with geographic coverage and a body of substantive civil law — a conclusion that supports the Ramban's understanding over the Rambam's more minimalist reading.

### Menashe List

Rava resolves the impasse: *hai tana de-vei Menashe hu* — the *tana* who says *dinim* was added to Israel at Marah as a new category is from the school of Menashe, who works from a different list of seven altogether. The Tana de-vei Menashe's list: *avodah zarah, giluy arayot, shefichut damim, gezel, eiver min ha-chai, sirus*, and *kilayim* — that is, he removes *dinim* and *birkat Hashem* from the standard seven and substitutes *sirus* and *kilayim*. According to this tradition, *dinim* was never part of the universal Noahide obligation; it is genuinely a commandment added to Israel. This explains why his tradition describes *dinim* as added at Marah without contradiction. We thus emerge with not just variant opinions about what the seven commandments include, but a fundamental dispute over whether *dinim* belongs to the universal moral order or to the specifically Israelite legal covenant.

The discussion leaves us with a rich picture of the *sheva mitzvot bnei Noach* as a subject of live rabbinic debate: their content, their derivation, their procedural requirements, and their relationship to the expanded obligations of the Jewish legal system all remain active questions as the Gemara continues.
