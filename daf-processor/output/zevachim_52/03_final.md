# Zevachim 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rav Papa's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא – שִׁירַיִם אֵין מְעַכְּבִים; וְהָכָא בְּמִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – מָר סָבַר מְעַכֵּב, וּמָר סָבַר לָא מְעַכֵּב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa says: Everyone** agrees that failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood on the base does **not disqualify** the offering. **But here they disagree with regard to** the issue of whether failure to **squeeze** the blood from **a bird sin offering** after sprinkling the blood **disqualifies** the offering or not. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **holds** that it does **disqualify** the offering, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **holds** that it does **not disqualify** the offering.

### Yesterday's Recap

Today's daf is 52, and we pick up in the middle of an ongoing discussion about the *halachot* of the *chatat* and the spilling of its blood at the outer *mizbeach*, including when dealing with an inner *chatat*. Yesterday's discussion centered on a debate between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael who appeared to be making essentially the same argument by way of a *kal vachomer*.

According to Rashi, the *kal vachomer* concerned the placement of blood for outer sacrifices. By an outer *chatat* one does not throw the blood; rather, one applies it to all four corners. But when one applies the blood of an *olah*, a *shlamim*, or other outer sacrifices, this must be done at corners that have a base opposite them — as opposed to the southeast corner, which does not have a base below it. The argument is that one does not even need a *pasuk* to establish this, because a *kal vachomer* suffices: if the remnant blood — which is not itself an act of atonement — must be spilled at the base, then certainly the initial application of blood, which is the act of atonement, must be placed at a corner that has a base beneath it.

The question then arose: given that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael appeared to be saying almost exactly the same thing — that the spilling of the remnant blood is not *mechapeir* — what was the difference between them? The *Gemara* raised the possibility that although Rabbi Yishmael agrees the spilling of the blood of an inner *chatat* does not achieve atonement, he might nonetheless hold that it is *me'akev*, that is, that its omission invalidates the inner *chatat*. Our *Mishnah* in this *perek* stated that the spilling of the blood of an inner *chatat* is not *me'akev*. But the near-identical formulations of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael prompted the *Gemara* to suggest that their debate might hinge on precisely this point — whether the spilling, while not an act of atonement, is nonetheless required for validity.

### Rav Papa's Point

Rav Papa rejects this suggestion. He says that *dechulei alma shirayim ein me'akvin* — even Rabbi Yishmael would agree that the spilling of the remnant blood of an inner *chatat* is not *me'akev*. What then are Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael debating? Rav Papa explains: *ve-hacha be-mitzuy chatat ha-of k-miflagi* — their dispute concerns the *mitzuy chatat ha-of*, the squeezing of the blood of a bird *chatat*.

The procedure is as follows: after performing *melikah* on a bird *chatat*, the blood that flows from the neck is squeezed against the side of the altar and runs down. The question Rav Papa identifies is whether this squeezing is *me'akev*. That is what Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael are debating — not whether the spilling of the remnant blood of an inner *chatat* is *me'akev*, but whether the squeezing of the bird *chatat* blood is *me'akev*.

### Inner vs. Outer

One might ask whether the inner *chatat* is treated differently from the outer with respect to the question of *me'akev* status. The answer is that the possibility of the spilling being *me'akev* would apply only to an inner *chatat*, since by an outer *chatat* even one of the four applications suffices *bedieved*. If even one application is sufficient, clearly the spilling of the blood is not *me'akev*. The question of the spilling being *me'akev* arises only by an inner *chatat*, where every single application is *me'akev*.

## Shirayim Me'akev?

### Extra Word 'Par'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: "וְאֵת כׇּל הַדָּם יִשְׁפֹּךְ" – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר "הַפָּר"? לִימֵּד עַל פַּר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, שֶׁטָּעוּן מַתַּן דָּמִים לַיְסוֹד. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Pappa** that even Rabbi Yishmael agrees that failure to pour the remainder of the blood on the base does not disqualify the offering. The verse states: "And the priest shall sprinkle the blood upon the corners of the altar of sweet incense before the Lord, which is in the Tent of Meeting; **and all the remaining blood** of the bull **he shall pour out** at the base of the altar of burnt offering, which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:7). **What** is the meaning when **the verse states "of the bull"?** This seems superfluous, as the entire passage is referring to the bull. This serves to **teach** the *halakha* **of** another bull, i.e., **the bull of Yom Kippur, which** also **requires placement of blood on the base** of the external altar. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi.

A *baraita* is now cited in support of Rav Papa's position. The verse in the context of the *par kohen mashiach* states: *ve-et kol dam ha-par yishapech* — all the remaining blood of the ox you shall pour out at the base of the altar. The *baraita* asks: why does the verse use the word *ha-par*, "the ox"? The context already makes clear we are dealing with an ox. The *baraita* answers — *limed al par Yom HaKippurim she-taun damim l'yesod*, divrei Rabbi — this extra word teaches that the ox of Yom Kippur, an inner *chatat* whose blood is applied to the *kaporet*, the *parochet*, and the *mizbeach ha-zahav*, also requires that the remnant blood be poured at the base of the outer altar. This is the view of Rabbi.

### Kal Vachomer Args

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: קַל וָחוֹמֶר; וּמָה אִם מִי שֶׁאֵין נִכְנָס דָּמוֹ לִפְנִים חוֹבָה – טָעוּן יְסוֹד, מִי שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמוֹ לִפְנִים חוֹבָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטָּעוּן יְסוֹד?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yishmael said:** It is not necessary for the Torah to write that the blood of the bull of Yom Kippur requires placement of blood on the base of the external altar. This is because it can be derived via **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as if** the bull of the anointed priest, i.e., the sin offering of a High Priest, with regard to **which it is not obligatory to bring its blood inside** the Sanctuary, i.e., it is not an obligatory offering, as he brings it only if he sins, nevertheless **requires** sprinkling blood on the **base** of the altar; with regard to the bull of Yom Kippur, with regard to **which it is obligatory to bring its blood inside** the Sanctuary, i.e., it must be brought every year, **is it not logical that it requires** sprinkling of its blood on the **base** of the altar?

Rabbi Yishmael does not disagree with this conclusion but notes that one could have derived it from a *kal vachomer* without needing the extra word *par*. His *kal vachomer* is as follows: the *par kohen mashiach* — the ox brought when the *kohen gadol* sins — is not a *chova*, not a standing obligation, since one only brings it if the *kohen gadol* actually transgresses. And yet the Torah requires that its blood be poured at the base. The *par* of Yom Kippur, by contrast, is a fixed, recurring *chova*. Is it not all the more so that it should require the blood to be poured at the base?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וּמָה מִי שֶׁאֵין דָּמוֹ נִכְנַס לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים, בֵּין לְחוֹבָה בֵּין לְמִצְוָה – טָעוּן יְסוֹד, מִי שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמוֹ חוֹבָה לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטָּעוּן יְסוֹד?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said: Just as** the bull of a High Priest, with regard to **which its blood does not enter the innermost sanctum, whether as** a fixed **obligation or as a mitzva,** nevertheless **requires** pouring of blood on the **base** of the altar, concerning the bull of Yom Kippur, with regard to **which its blood enters the innermost sanctum** as **an obligation, is it not logical that it requires** pouring of blood on the **base** of the altar?

Rabbi Akiva sharpens the *kal vachomer* further. He argues: *uma mi she-ein damo taun lifnai v'lifnim bein l'chova bein l'mitzva taun yesod* — the *par kohen mashiach* is weighty in two ways relative to other sacrifices, yet its blood does not enter the Holy of Holies for any purpose, neither as a *chova* nor as a *mitzva*, and yet it requires the base. The *par* of Yom Kippur is weightier in two ways: first, it is a standing annual obligation, and second, its blood is brought into the Holy of Holies. How much more so should it require the base?

### Doubling as Meakev?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְעַכְּבֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְכִלָּה מִכַּפֵּר אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ" – שָׁלְמוּ כָּל הַכַּפָּרוֹת כּוּלָּן. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** If so, the verse that states that there is a requirement that the blood of the sin offering of a High Priest is poured on the base of the altar is apparently superfluous. One **might** have thought that the reason the Torah writes it is to teach that failure to pour the blood there **disqualifies** the offering. Therefore, **the verse states** with regard to the service of Yom Kippur, after sprinkling the blood in the Sanctuary: **"And when he has made an end of atoning for the Sanctuary,** and the Tent of Meeting, and the altar, he shall present the live goat" (Leviticus 16:20). This teaches that once he has sprinkled the blood in the innermost sanctum **all the atonements are completed.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

Now the *baraita* raises a question. Since one could derive the requirement for the *par* of Yom Kippur from a *kal vachomer*, the explicit *pasuk* that uses the extra word *par* functions as a kind of doubling. The principle is that when the Torah repeats a requirement that could have been derived independently, this doubling may indicate that the requirement is *me'akev*. Accordingly, one might say: *yachol y'akeivenu* — perhaps the verse is teaching that the spilling of the blood at the base is *me'akev*, at least for Yom Kippur.

### Vichilah Mechapeir

The *baraita* rejects this possibility. The verse states: *v'chilah mikapeir et ha-kodesh* — "and when he has finished atoning for the Holy." Rabbi Yishmael derives from here: once all the *kaparot* — the atoning acts — are completed, one has fulfilled the obligation. What are the *kaparot*? The applications of blood on the *kaporet*, the *parochet*, and the *mizbeach ha-zahav*. The spilling of the blood at the base is not identified as an act of atonement and is not even mentioned by Yom Kippur explicitly. Therefore, when the verse says one is done once the atoning is complete, it means only the blood applications count. The spilling at the base is not *me'akev*. Even though the spilling is indeed required on Yom Kippur, its omission does not invalidate the *korban*. This is *divrei Rabbi Yishmael* — and it directly confirms Rav Papa's position that Rabbi Yishmael agrees the *shirayim* are not *me'akev*.

### Par Kohen Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְפַר כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ מִשְּׂעִיר נָשִׂיא, מֵעַתָּה: וּמָה מִי שֶׁאֵין נִכְנָס דָּמוֹ לִפְנִים, לֹא חוֹבָה וְלָא מִצְוָה – טָעוּן יְסוֹד, מִי שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמוֹ לִפְנִים בֵּין לְחוֹבָה בֵּין לְמִצְוָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁטָּעוּן יְסוֹד?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From now,** i.e., based on this, one can state **an *a fortiori*** inference **to** derive the *halakha* of **the bull of** the **anointed priest from** the *halakha* of **the goat of** the **king. Just as** the goat sin offering of a king, with regard to **which its blood does not enter inside** the Sanctuary, **whether** as a fixed **obligation or** as **a mitzva,** but is sprinkled, like other individual sin offerings, on the external altar, **requires** that the blood be poured on the **base** of the altar (see Leviticus 4:25); with regard to the sin offering of a High Priest, the blood of **which enters inside** the Sanctuary, **whether as** a fixed **obligation or as a mitzva, is it not logical that it should require** pouring on the **base** of the altar?

Having established that the spilling is not *me'akev* for Yom Kippur, the *baraita* proceeds to make a parallel argument for the *par kohen mashiach*. A *kal vachomer* can be constructed from an outer *chatat*: if an ordinary outer *chatat*, whose blood does not enter the inner sanctum for any purpose — neither as a *chova* nor as a *mitzva* — requires the blood to be spilled at the base, then the *par kohen mashiach*, whose blood does enter the inner sanctum, certainly should require the base as well. And since one could have derived this from a *kal vachomer*, the explicit verse stating *ve-et kol dam ha-par yishapech* might be read as a doubling — and one might therefore argue it renders the spilling *me'akev*: *yachol y'akeivenu*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְעַכְּבֶנּוּ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "וְאֶת כׇּל (הַדָּם) [דַּם הַפָּר] יִשְׁפֹּךְ" – נִתְּקוֹ הַכָּתוּב לַעֲשֵׂה, וַעֲשָׂאוֹ שְׁיָרֵי מִצְוָה; לוֹמַר לְךָ: שִׁירַיִם אֵין מְעַכְּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** If so, the verse that states that there is a requirement that the blood of the sin offering of a High Priest is poured on the base of the altar is apparently superfluous. One **might** have thought that the reason the Torah writes it is to teach that failure to pour the blood there **disqualifies** the offering. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And the priest shall sprinkle of the blood upon the corners of the altar of sweet incense before the Lord, which is in the Tent of Meeting, **and all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour** out at the base of the altar of burnt offering, which is at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 4:7). Since the verse inverted the terms in the clause, writing: "And all the remaining blood of the bull he shall pour out," and not: He shall pour out all the remaining blood of the bull, the **verse detaches this positive** mitzva of pouring the remaining blood from the other mitzvot in the verse. **And** the verse thereby **made** this **a non-essential mitzva, to tell you** that failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar does **not disqualify** the offering.

The *baraita* responds by pointing to the style of the verse itself. The verse could have written simply *ve-shafach et ha-dam el yesod ha-mizbeach*, as one more verb in a sequential list — *ve-lakach, ve-hizah, ve-natan*, and so on. Instead, the verse shifts register and speaks of the blood in a more tangential, wrap-up manner: *ve-et kol dam ha-par yishapech*. It is not phrased as another step in the procedure but as a separate, secondary note. This, says the *baraita*, indicates the spilling is not central to the act and is not *me'akev*. The verse *nitka ha-kasuv le-aseh va-asahu shiyarei mitzva* — the Torah has detached this instruction and treated it as a residual *mitzva*, thereby signaling that the *shirayim* do not invalidate.

### Rashi's Grammar

This grammatical reading is developed by Rashi. He observes that the earlier verbs in the passage flow as a direct sequence of procedural steps. When the Torah arrives at the spilling of the blood, it could have maintained that rhythm. Instead, it breaks the pattern, addressing the blood separately in a way that distinguishes this act from the atoning applications. The stylistic disruption is itself interpretively significant: the Torah is indicating that the spilling of the remnant blood is secondary, not integral. This is how one knows that even though the spilling is obligatory, it is not *me'akev* — the manner in which the verse is formulated tells you so.

## Bird Blood Meakev?

### Rabbi Yishmael's Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: מִיצּוּי חַטַּאת הָעוֹף מְעַכֵּב?! וְהָתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: "וְהַנִּשְׁאָר בַּדָּם יִמָּצֵה" – וְהַנִּשְׁאָר יִמָּצֵה, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נִשְׁאָר לֹא יִמָּצֵה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** does **Rabbi Yishmael hold** that failure to **squeeze out** the blood of **a bird sin offering disqualifies** the offering, as Rav Pappa explained? **But the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The verse states: "And he shall sprinkle of the blood of the sin offering upon the side of the altar; **and the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out** at the base of the altar; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:9). This means: **And the remainder shall be squeezed out,** i.e., it needs to be squeezed out only if some blood remains. **And that which does not remain,** i.e., if there is no blood remaining, **he shall not squeeze** it **out.** This indicates that failure to squeeze the blood does not disqualify the offering.

With the *baraita* having confirmed that Rabbi Yishmael holds the *shirayim* of an inner *chatat* are not *me'akev*, the *Gemara* now examines whether Rabbi Yishmael really holds the *mitzuy chatat ha-of* — the squeezing of bird *chatat* blood — is *me'akev*, as Rav Papa suggested. A *baraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael is cited: *ve-ha-nishar ba-dam yimatze* — what remains of the blood you shall squeeze out. And: if there is blood remaining, squeeze it; if there is nothing remaining, do not squeeze it. This implies that the squeezing is done only when it is possible to do so. Since the procedure is sometimes not going to happen — there may simply be no blood left — this implicitly signals that the *mitzuy* is not *me'akev*. How then can one say Rabbi Yishmael holds it is *me'akev*?

### Two Tanaiim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּרֵי תַּנָּאֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** There are **two *tanna'im*, and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yishmael.**

The *Gemara* resolves this by saying *trei tanaei aliba de-Rabbi Yishmael* — there are two *tannaim* within Rabbi Yishmael's school who disagree about whether the *mitzuy* of a bird *chatat* is *me'akev*. The *baraita* just cited represents one view within his school, but another view within that same school holds it is *me'akev*.

## Rami's Tanna

### Osah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא, הַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר: שִׁירַיִם מְעַכְּבִי. דְּתַנְיָא: "הַכֹּהֵן הַמְחַטֵּא אֹתָהּ" – אוֹתָהּ שֶׁנִּיתָּן דָּמָהּ לְמַעְלָה, וְלֹא אוֹתָהּ שֶׁנִּיתָּן דָּמָהּ לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of the remainder of the blood. **Rami bar Ḥama says: This** following ***tanna* holds** that failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood of offerings whose blood is sprinkled inside the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the offering, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"The priest that sacrifices it for sin** shall eat it" (Leviticus 6:19). The verse states the word "it" to teach that **it,** the offering **whose blood was sprinkled** correctly, **above** the red line of the altar, is valid, and the priest may eat the meat. **But** this is **not** so for an offering **whose blood was sprinkled below** the red line, which is disqualified.

Rami bar Chama now identifies a *tanna* who holds that the *shirayim* of an inner *chatat* are *me'akev*. He does so by pointing to a *baraita* that deals with the word *osah* — "it," referring to the *chatat* — found in the verse *ha-kohen ha-mechate osah yochelenah*, the *kohen* who performs the *chatuy* of the *chatat* shall eat it.

The *baraita* derives from *osah* that only a *chatat* performed in the prescribed manner is valid. Specifically: if one applied the blood of an outer *chatat* in the upper half of the altar — at the horns — it is a valid *chatat*; but if one applied the blood in the lower half of the altar, it is invalid. The word *osah* — "it" — excludes a *chatat* whose blood was applied in the wrong zone.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: וְכִי מֵאַיִן בָּאתָה? מִכְּלָל שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְדַם זְבָחֶיךָ יִשָּׁפֵךְ עַל מִזְבַּח וְגוֹ׳" – לָמַדְנוּ לַנִּיתָּנִין בְּמַתַּן אַרְבַּע, שֶׁאִם נְתָנָן בְּמַתָּנָה אַחַת – כִּיפֵּר. יָכוֹל אַף הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה כִּיפֵּר?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **You said** this, **but from where did you come?** In other words, why would one think that such an offering is valid, so that the verse needs to teach that it is not? The *baraita* explains: **From the fact that it is stated:** "And you shall offer your burnt offerings, the flesh and the blood, upon the altar of the Lord your God; **and the blood of your offerings shall be poured out against the altar** of the Lord your God, and you shall eat the flesh" (Deuteronomy 12:27), **we learned** that **with regard to** those offerings whose blood is **placed with four placements** on the corners of the altar, **that if** the priest **placed them with** only **one placement, he has effected atonement.** Therefore, since it is derived that if the priest does not present the blood on the specified corners of the altar, the offering is nevertheless valid, one **might** have thought that blood **that** should have been **placed above** the red line but **that one placed below** the red line **effects atonement as well,** and the offering is valid.

The *baraita* then asks: why would one have thought the lower half could work, such that a *pasuk* was needed to exclude it? The argument would have been as follows. We know that by an outer *chatat*, even one of the four applications suffices *bedieved* — this is derived from the verse *ve-dam zevachecha yishapech al mizbeach yesod ha-olah ve-ha-basar tochal*, which teaches that any valid blood application at the *mizbeach* suffices. Given this latitude, and given that blood applied at the top naturally runs down to the bottom due to gravity — *shekein sofan lemata* — one might have thought that placing the blood at the lower half initially is also valid, since that is where the blood ends up anyway.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הוּא – נֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים לְמַעְלָה, וְנֶאֶמְרוּ דָּמִים לְמַטָּן; מָה דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִים לְמַטָּן שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַעְלָן – לֹא כִּיפֵּר, אַף דָּמִים הָאֲמוּרִים לְמַעְלָן – אִם נָתַן לְמַטָּה לֹא כִּיפֵּר!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** it would seem there **is a logical inference** to counter this logic. **It is stated** that **blood** is to be sprinkled **above** the red line, referring to the blood of an animal sin offering, which is to be sprinkled on the corners on the upper half of the altar, **and it is stated** that **blood** is to be sprinkled **below** the red line, referring to the blood of a bird sin offering, which is to be sprinkled on the lower half of the altar. **Just as** with regard to the **blood** about **which** it **is stated** that it is to be **below** the red line, if it is a case **where one placed** it **above** the red line, it does **not effect atonement,** as the Sages derived from the verse: "And he shall sprinkle of the blood of the sin offering upon the side of the altar, and the remainder of the blood shall be squeezed out at the base of the altar; it is a sin offering" (Leviticus 5:9), **so too,** with regard to the **blood,** about **which** it **is stated** that it is to be **above** the red line, if it is a case **where one placed** it **below** the red line, it does **not effect atonement.**

### Hava Amina Refuted

The *baraita* introduces a counter-argument to this *hava amina*, based on the principle of *shirayim pnimiyim*. The remnant blood of an inner *chatat* eventually ends up at the base of the outer altar. Yet if one were to place the blood of an inner *chatat* directly on the outer altar *lechatchila*, bypassing the inner service entirely, it would obviously be invalid. This shows that the fact that blood eventually reaches a certain destination does not permit one to begin there. Apply the same logic to the outer *chatat*: even though the blood from the horns eventually runs down, one may not begin at the bottom.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בַּתַּחְתּוֹנִים שֶׁנִּיתָּנִין בִּנְתִינָה לְמַעְלָה שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן לְמַעְלָן – לֹא כִּיפֵּר; תֹּאמַר בָּעֶלְיוֹנִים שֶׁנְּתָנָן לְמַטָּה, שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֵהֶן קָרֵב לְמַטָּה?!
>
> **Translation:** This logical inference is rejected: **No, if you said** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the blood of a bird sin offering, which is to be sprinkled **below** the red line **that was placed with a placement above** the red line, that may be **because they will not ultimately** be sprinkled **above.** For this reason it does **not effect atonement. Shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the blood that is to be sprinkled **above** the red line, i.e., the blood of an animal sin offering, but **which one placed below** the red line, that it will not effect atonement? The blood that is to be sprinkled above the red line is different, **as** some **of it is sacrificed below** the red line, when the remainder of the blood is poured on the base of the altar.

The *Gemara* pushes back. The cases are not parallel. When one says the inner altar does not "finish" the blood — *she-ein mizbeach ha-pnimi memarkan* — and the outer altar's horns do "finish" the outer *chatat* blood — *karnav memarkot otan* — this creates an asymmetry. Because the inner *chatat* blood is not finished at the inner altar, one might think more latitude exists for the outer *chatat*, whose blood is fully discharged with one corner application. Therefore, the word *osah* is still necessary to teach that even so, the outer *chatat* blood may not be applied at the lower half.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּמִים (שִׁירַיִים) הַפְּנִימִיִּים יוֹכִיחוּ – שֶׁיֵּשׁ מֵהֶן קָרֵב בַּחוּץ, וְאִם נְתָנָן בַּתְּחִלָּה בַּחוּץ לֹא כִּיפֵּר!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* responds: The **blood** that is placed **inside** the Sanctuary **will prove** it, **as** some **of it is sacrificed outside, but if** the priest **initially placed** the blood on the altar **outside** the Sanctuary it does **not effect atonement.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּדָמִים הַפְּנִימִיִּים – שֶׁאֵין מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי מְמָרְקָן; תֹּאמַר בְּעֶלְיוֹנִים – שֶׁהֲרֵי קְרָנוֹת מְמָרְקוֹת אוֹתָן; אִם נְתָנָן לְמַטָּה – כְּשֵׁרִים!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* rejects this proof: **No, if you said** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the **blood** that is placed **inside** the Sanctuary, concerning **which the inner altar does not complete** the atonement, as they require additional blood placements, **shall you** also **say** that this is the *halakha* **with regard to** the blood offered **above** the red line, i.e., the blood of an animal sin offering, concerning **which** the **corners** of the altar **complete** the atonement? Accordingly, it is possible to say that **if one placed them below** the red line **they are valid.**

### Rava's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, תֵּיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר: מָה שִׁירַיִים הַפְּנִימִיִּים, שֶׁסּוֹפָן חוֹבָה בַּחוּץ – עֲשָׂאָן בַּתְּחִלָּה בַּחוּץ לֹא כִּיפֵּר; הַנִּיתָּנִין לְמַעְלָה, שֶׁאֵין סוֹפָן חוֹבָה לְמַטָּה, וַעֲשָׂאָן בַּתְּחִלָּה לְמַטָּה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא כִּיפֵּר?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rami bar Ḥama: **If so,** that the *tanna* of the *baraita* holds that failure to pour the remainder of the blood of the offerings whose blood is sprinkled inside the Sanctuary disqualifies the offering, you could **derive** the *halakha* that if the priest sprinkled the blood below the red line the offering is disqualified **via an *a fortiori*** inference. **Just as** it is with regard to **the remainder** of the blood of the offerings **of the inner** altar, concerning **which their ultimate** rite, pouring on the base of the altar, is **obligatory on the external** altar, but if the priest **initially performed** the rite of placing the blood **on the external** altar, it does **not effect atonement,** with regard to those offerings whose blood **is placed above** the red line, concerning **which their ultimate** rite, pouring on the base of the altar, is **not obligatory below** the red line of the altar, **and** the priest **initially performed** the rite of placing the blood **below** the red line, **is it not logical that** it does **not effect atonement?** Since the *baraita* does not advance this claim, but derived the *halakha* from a verse, this indicates that pouring the remainder of the blood is not obligatory.

Rava disagrees with this reading of the *baraita*. He argues that if the *baraita* were assuming *shirayim* are *me'akev* by an inner *chatat*, the *kal vachomer* would have run in the opposite direction entirely, and would actually have strengthened the case against the *hava amina* — not weakened it. If *shirayim* of the inner *chatat* are *me'akev* and the outer base is a required part of the process, and yet one may not simply begin with the outer base, then *a fortiori* for an outer *chatat* where the base is not *me'akev*, one certainly should not be permitted to begin there. The comparison, on that reading, would only fortify the prohibition, making the word *osah* unnecessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵין מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי מְמָרְקָן בִּלְבַד, אֶלָּא פָּרוֹכֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **Rather,** when the *baraita* states that the blood of the offerings offered inside the Sanctuary are those concerning which the inner altar does not complete the atonement it means that **the inner altar does not complete** the atonement **alone,** but **rather** requires that blood also be sprinkled inside the Sanctuary on the **Curtain** separating the Sanctuary and Holy of Holies.

Rava therefore offers a different reading. The phrase "the inner altar does not finish them" does not refer to the *shirayim* and does not assume they are *me'akev*. Rather, it refers to the fact that the inner *chatat*'s blood service is not confined to a single location — it requires application both at the inner *mizbeach* and at the *parochet*. Because the inner *chatat* requires blood applications in two distinct locations, the overall process is more demanding and more constrained. This heightened stringency — not anything to do with the *me'akev* status of the *shirayim* — is what makes the inner *chatat* a poor basis for a *hava amina* about the outer. Rava thus concludes that this *baraita* does not in fact demonstrate that any *tanna* holds *shirayim* are *me'akev*.

## Akiva vs. Yehuda

### Akiva's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "וְכִלָּה מִכַּפֵּר" – אִם כִּיפֵּר כִּלָּה, וְאִם לֹא כִּיפֵּר לָא כִּלָּה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נֹאמַר: אִם כִּלָּה כִּיפֵּר, אִם לֹא כִּלָּה לֹא כִּיפֵּר? שֶׁאִם חִיסַּר אַחַת מִכׇּל הַמַּתָּנוֹת – לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states concerning the sacrificial rite performed by the High Priest on Yom Kippur: **"And when he has finished atoning** for the Sanctuary, and the Tent of Meeting, and the altar, he shall present the live goat" (Leviticus 16:20). This verse indicates that **if he performed** the **atonement, he has finished** the service, **but if he did not perform** the **atonement, he has not finished.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda said to him: For what** reason **do we not say: If he finished, he has performed atonement,** but **if he did not finish, he has not performed atonement?** This derivation would indicate **that if one of any of the** blood **placements is lacking** it is as though **he did nothing.**

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* directly addressing the question. The verse *v'chilah mikapeir et ha-kodesh* — "and when he has finished atoning for the Holy" — is the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Akiva reads the verse as follows: *im kipeir chila* — if he has atoned, then he is done. The condition for completion is the performance of the atoning acts. Only those acts that are identified as acts of atonement — the blood applications — are *me'akev*. The *shirayim* spilling, which is not *mechapeir*, is therefore not *me'akev*.

### Yehuda's Position

Rabbi Yehuda challenges this reading. He asks: why not read the verse the other way — *im chila kipeir*, if he is done, then he has atoned? Rather than making atonement the condition for completion, one makes completion the condition for atonement. And he adds: *im chisair achas mi-kol ha-matanot lo asa ve-lo klum* — if one omitted even one of the blood applications, one has accomplished nothing. All applications are *me'akev* by an inner *chatat*.

### Amoraim's Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי; חַד אָמַר: מַשְׁמָעוּת דּוֹרְשִׁין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, וְחַד אָמַר: שִׁירַיִים מְעַכְּבִין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the two opinions: **What** is the difference **between them? Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** engaged in a dispute concerning this. **One says** that **interpretation of the meaning** of the verse **is** the difference **between them,** i.e., there is no halakhic difference between them but only a dispute as to how to interpret the verses. **And one says** that **there is** a difference **between them** with regard to whether failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood at the base of the altar **disqualifies** the offering. According to Rabbi Akiva, it does not disqualify the offering, whereas Rabbi Yehuda maintains that it does disqualify the offering.

The *Gemara* asks: what exactly is the practical difference between their positions? Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi debate this question. One *amora* says: *mashmaus darshein ika beinaihu* — their dispute is purely hermeneutical, a question of which formulation better captures the verse's meaning, with no practical *halachic* difference between them. The other *amora* says: *shirayim me'akvin ika beinaihu* — their dispute is whether the *shirayim* are *me'akev*. According to Rabbi Akiva, only atoning acts are *me'akev*, and since the *shirayim* are not an act of atonement, they are not *me'akev*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, who reads "when you are done you have atoned," completion of everything — possibly including the *shirayim* — is the condition, and therefore the *shirayim* may be *me'akev*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיֵּים דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי הוּא דְּאָמַר: שִׁירַיִים דִּמְעַכְּבִי; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שִׁירַיִים מְעַכְּבִין – מֵבִיא פַּר אֶחָד, וּמַתְחִיל בַּתְּחִלָּה בִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **It may be concluded that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is** the one **who says** that Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda disagree as to whether or not failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood on the altar **disqualifies** the offering. **As Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: According to the statement of the one who says** that failure to pour the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the altar **disqualifies** the offering, if the priest finished placing the blood on the inner altar and the blood was spilled before he poured the remainder on the external altar, he must **bring one bull** and slaughter it, **and begin** the sprinkling of the blood as he did **initially on the inner** altar, so that there will be blood remaining from the sprinkling, and then he pours the remainder of the blood on the external altar. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi discusses the opinion that failure to pour the remainder of the blood on the base of the altar disqualifies the offering, apparently in reference to the *baraita* cited here.

The *Gemara* notes that it appears Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is the one who holds that this is the debate, since he explicitly entertains the practical consequences of the *shirayim* being *me'akev*: *l'divrei ha-omer shirayim me'akvin* — according to the opinion that *shirayim* are *me'akev* — if on Yom Kippur the remnant blood spilled accidentally before being placed at the base, the *kohen gadol* would face a problem. One cannot simply slaughter another animal and call that blood "remnant" — it is original blood, not *shirayim*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi concludes that in such a case, one would be required to bring an entirely new *par* and repeat the inner blood service, so as to generate genuine *shirayim* to spill at the base.

### Final Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לֵית לֵיהּ הָא סְבָרָא?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תַּנָּא רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר שִׁירַיִם מְעַכְּבִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that to say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan does not** agree with **this reasoning? But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **say** (111a): **Rabbi Neḥemya taught** a *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that failure to pour the **remainder** of blood **disqualifies** the offering? Rabbi Yoḥanan also discusses the opinion of a *tanna* who holds that failure to pour the remainder of blood disqualifies the offering, apparently in reference to the *baraita* cited here.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר – וְלָאו לְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי; הָכָא נָמֵי, כְּדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר – וְלָאו לְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** there is no proof that Rabbi Yoḥanan is referring to the dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda. He is stating a *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that it disqualifies the offering, whichever *tanna* that may be, **but** he is **not** referring **to** the dispute between **these *tanna'im*. Here too,** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is stating a *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says** that it disqualifies the offering, whichever *tanna* that may be, **but** he is **not** referring **to** the dispute between **these *tanna'im*.**

The *Gemara* objects: surely Rabbi Yochanan also acknowledges that some *tanna* holds *shirayim* are *me'akev*? Indeed, Rabbi Yochanan himself stated that Rabbi Nechemia — in the context of the law that one is liable for performing *avoda bachutz* with *shirayim* blood — holds in accordance with the opinion that *shirayim* are *me'akev*. If the remnant blood constitutes a required *avoda* within the *Beis HaMikdash*, then using it as an *avoda* outside incurs liability. This makes clear that Rabbi Yochanan too acknowledges such an opinion exists.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent difficulty: *ela k'divrei ha-omer v'lav le-hani tannai* — both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi agree that some *tanna* holds *shirayim* are *me'akev*. What they debate is whether that opinion is specifically the position of Rabbi Yehuda in his dispute with Rabbi Akiva. One *amora* identifies the debate of Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yehuda as hinging precisely on this question; the other says the dispute is merely stylistic, and the *tanna* who holds *shirayim* are *me'akev* is someone else entirely.

The conclusion is that both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi concede the existence of at least one *tanna* who holds — at minimum for Yom Kippur, and possibly for all inner *chataot* — that the *shirayim* are *me'akev*. This is the *chidush* that the *Gemara* has been working toward. Whether that opinion belongs to Rabbi Yehuda specifically remains a point of dispute between the *amoraim*. And so, to summarize the complete picture of the inner *chatat*: all blood applications are *me'akev*, while whether the *shirayim* are *me'akev* is left as an unresolved debate among the *tannaim*, with a majority view — including Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yishmael — holding they are not.

## Regular Chattaot

### Communal Chattaot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חַטּאוֹת הַצִּבּוּר וְהַיָּחִיד – אֵלּוּ הֵן חַטְּאוֹת הַצִּבּוּר? שְׂעִירֵי רָאשֵׁי חֳדָשִׁים וְשֶׁל מוֹעֲדוֹת. שְׁחִיטָתָן בַּצָּפוֹן, וְקִיבּוּל דָּמָן בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת בַּצָּפוֹן, וְדָמָן טָעוּן אַרְבַּע מַתָּנוֹת עַל אַרְבַּע קְרָנוֹת. כֵּיצַד?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: These are the *halakhot* of **the communal and the individual sin offerings. These are the communal sin offerings: Goats of the New Moon and of the Festivals. Their slaughter** is **in the north** of the Temple courtyard, **and** the **collection of their blood in a service vessel** is **in the north, and their blood requires four placements on the four corners of the altar. How** did the priest do so?

The *Mishnah* now transitions from the inner *chatat* to the regular outer *chatat*, addressing both communal and individual varieties. The *Mishnah* begins: *chataot ha-tzibbur ve-ha-yachid* — the outer *chataot* of the community and the individual. The communal outer *chataot* include, as the *Mishnah* specifies, the *se'irei roshei chodashim ve-shel mo'adot* — the goats of *Rosh Chodesh* and of the festivals, which form the *chatat* component of the *musaf* offering brought on each *Rosh Chodesh* and *Yom Tov*.

### Slaughter North

The *Mishnah* states: *shechitatan ba-tzafon* — slaughter is in the north. The *shechita* must take place in the northern section of the *azarah*. And further: *u-kibul damam bi-chli sharet ba-tzafon* — the reception of the blood must take place in a holy vessel, in the north. This means the catching of the blood, the vessel itself, and the person catching must all be in the north. By *shechita*, one notes, the person performing it does not have to stand in the north.

This raises an observation noted by Tosafos: why does the *Mishnah* mention *kli sharet* specifically in connection with receiving the blood, but not in connection with *shechita*? Tosafos explains that this may simply reflect the stylistic need to specify that both the act of catching and the vessel must occur in the north. By *shechita*, since the person and the knife can be in the south — it is sufficient that enough of the blade reaches the animal's neck — there is no reason to specify the knife's status. This leads Tosafos to raise the fascinating question of whether the knife used for *shechting* a *korban* must be a *kli sharet* at all, since *shechita* does not require a *kohen*, need not occur in the *Beis HaMikdash*, and carries no inherent restriction to standing in the north.

### Four Corners

The *Mishnah* continues: *ve-damam te'un arba matanot al arba kranot* — their blood requires four applications on the four corners of the outer altar. Unlike the inner *chataot*, which involve the *parochet*, the *mizbeach ha-zahav*, and the *kaporet*, the outer *chatat* involves the four corners of the outer altar. And as has been established, *bedieved* even one application suffices.

### Ramp & Circle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלָה בַּכֶּבֶשׁ וּפָנָה לַסּוֹבֵב, וּבָא לוֹ לְקֶרֶן דְּרוֹמִית מִזְרָחִית, מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית, צְפוֹנִית מַעֲרָבִית, מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית. שְׁיָרֵי הַדָּם הָיָה שׁוֹפֵךְ עַל יְסוֹד דְּרוֹמִית, וְנֶאֱכָלִין לִפְנִים מִן הַקְּלָעִים לְזִכְרֵי כְהוּנָּה בְּכׇל מַאֲכָל, לְיוֹם וָלַיְלָה עַד חֲצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **He ascended the ramp** of the altar **and turned** right **to the surrounding ledge** and he continued east, **and he came to the southeast corner** and sprinkled the blood of the sin offering there and then to the **northeast** corner and sprinkled the blood there, and then to the **northwest** corner and sprinkled the blood there, and the **southwest** corner, where he performed the fourth sprinkling and descended from the altar. **He would pour the remainder of the blood on the southern base** of the altar. **And** the meat portions of the offering **are eaten within the curtains,** i.e., in the Temple courtyard, **by the males of the priesthood.** And they are eaten prepared **in any** form of **food** preparation, **on** the **day** the offering is sacrificed **and** during the **night** that follows, **until midnight.**

The *Mishnah* then describes the procedure: *oleh ba-kevesh* — the *kohen* ascends the ramp, which is on the south side of the altar. He then *poneh la-sovev* — turns onto the walkway that encircles the altar, known as the *sovev*, which allows him to reach the horns. He proceeds counterclockwise — always moving to his right — beginning at the southeast corner, then northeast, northwest, and finally southwest. This reflects the general principle that *kol pinos she-ata poneh lo yehei ela le-yamina* — whenever you turn, you turn to the right. This is the same principle that governs circling in other ritual contexts.

### South Base & Eating

After completing the four applications, the *kohen* descends. *Shiyarei ha-dam haya shofech le-yesod dromi* — the remnant of the blood was poured at the southern base. This is in contrast to the inner *chatat*, whose *shirayim* were poured at the western base — the base one encounters first when approaching from inside. For the outer *chatat*, having descended the ramp on the south side, the *kohen* is closest to the southern base, and that is where the *shirayim* are spilled.

*Ve-nechlaim bifnim min ha-klaim* — the meat of the *chatat* is eaten inside the curtains, meaning within the *azarah* proper. This formulation evokes the *Mishkan*, where curtains surrounded the courtyard. It excludes the *Ezrat Nashim* and the *Ezrat Yisrael*; the meat must be consumed within the inner courtyard of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

### Eating Rules

The *Mishnah* specifies further: *le-zichrei kehuna* — it may be eaten only by male *kohanim*, even those who are *baalei mumim*. *Be-chol ma'achal* — it may be prepared and eaten in any form; unlike the *Korban Pesach*, it need not be roasted. *Le-yom ve-layla ad chatzot* — it may be eaten for that day and the following night, until midnight. In principle the *halacha* permits eating throughout the entire night, but the Rabbis established a cutoff at midnight to prevent people from being lax and missing the deadline, as the first *Mishnah* in *Brachot* teaches.
