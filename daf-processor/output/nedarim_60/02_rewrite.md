# Nedarim 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Onion Bitul Resolution

### Rabbi Chanina's Onion

We open on daf 60 by returning to a statement introduced at the bottom of 59b. The Gemara cites *guffa* — returning to the matter itself — and quotes: *tzirtam Rav Yanai betzalim shel terumah sheneta'u verabu gidulav al ikaro mutar*. Rabbi Chanina reports in the name of Rav Yanai: if one plants an onion of *terumah* and the subsequent growth exceeds the original amount, it is *mutar* — permissible — because the original *terumah* onion is rendered *batel* by the new growth. This statement has been our primary focus throughout the preceding discussion: the question of whether *bitul* applies to this kind of growth at all, and whether *bitul* can even operate in a direction that converts *heter* into *issur* — the fascinating question raised in the prior session. Here, the Gemara accepts as its working premise that the growth does indeed effect *bitul*.

### Knockout Question

The Gemara now asks what it calls *le-meimra* — "does this suggest" — that *gidulei heter ma'alin et ha-issur*, that growth from permissible material nullifies the original forbidden quantity? And then comes the challenge: *ve-hatnan* — but we have already learned in a Mishna — *gidulei terumah, terumah*: growth from *terumah* is itself *terumah*. If whatever grows from a *terumah* onion is itself considered *terumah*, then the entire discussion of *bitul* seems beside the point. Before asking whether the new growth can nullify the original, one must ask whether the new growth is itself forbidden *terumah* by virtue of having grown from *terumah* in the first place. This is, as the Gemara essentially admits, a knockout question — and it is striking that it is posed only now, at the very end of the *sugya*, rather than at the outset where it would have reframed the entire discussion.

### Gidulei Gidulin

The Gemara's response is *begidulei gidulin amrinan* — Rabbi Chanina's statement was not referring to the second-generation growth, but to the third generation: growth from growth. When the Mishna says *gidulei terumah, terumah*, it means that the second-generation product retains *terumah* status. Rabbi Chanina's ruling about *bitul* concerns only the third generation.

This raises an immediate conceptual difficulty. With a crop like wheat, the notion of generational sequence is clear: one plants a seed, the seed disintegrates, new grain grows — that is the second generation. One then plants kernels of that harvested grain and a third generation of wheat emerges. But with an onion, which is a *davar she-ein zaro kalah* — something whose original seed does not disintegrate — how does one define a third generation? The onion simply continues to expand and produce shoots. One possibility is that a shoot is separated and replanted, akin to the horticultural technique of layering (*havrachah*) known from the laws of *Pe'ah*. The precise agricultural mechanics are not entirely clear from the text, but the conceptual point stands: the Gemara posits that a third generation of onion growth is analyzable.

The Gemara then anticipates an objection: we already know that *gidulei gidulin chullin* — that the third generation is *chulin* and no longer *terumah*. So what is the *chiddush* of Rabbi Chanina's statement? The Gemara answers: *afilu bedavar she-ein zaro kalah* — even in the case of an item whose seed does not disintegrate, such as an onion. By wheat, the ruling that the second generation is *terumah* and the third is not is comprehensible: each new generation involves the complete disappearance of the prior seed and a genuinely new plant. By the onion, one might have thought that since the original material is still physically present — *ein zaro kalah* — all subsequent growth, however remote, retains *terumah* status. The *chiddush* is that we do not reason this way: even for the onion, the third generation is *chulin*, and furthermore, it can effect *bitul* of the original.

### Terumah vs Tevel

The Gemara then introduces a further complication from a Mishna concerning *tevel* — grain from which *terumot u-ma'asrot* have not yet been separated. The Mishna states: *ha-tevel gidulav mutarin* — growth from *tevel* is permissible. So while *terumah* passes its status to the second generation, *tevel* does not. The Mishna qualifies this: *gidulav mutarin bedavar she-zaro kalah* — the second generation of *tevel* is permissible only if it is a crop whose seed disintegrates, such as wheat. *Aval bedavar she-ein zaro kalah, gidulei gidulav asurin* — but if the original seed does not disintegrate, even *tevel*, which is more lenient than *terumah* in not transmitting its status to the second generation, transmits its status through to the third generation.

This creates a seemingly insurmountable difficulty for the entire preceding discussion. If we are dealing with an onion — a *davar she-ein zaro kalah* — then: by *terumah*, the second generation is *terumah*, and by *tevel*, which is more lenient, even the third generation is still *tevel*-status. Under what circumstances could *bitul* ever apply? The new growth should always carry the forbidden status. This truly is, as one might ask, a question that should have appeared at the very beginning of the *sugya* rather than at its conclusion. The Gemara's having deferred it to this point is at once rhetorically effective and analytically puzzling.

### Sugya Conclusion

The Gemara's response reads: *ribuya de-rabu gidulin al ikaro mutar kamashma lan* — the novelty concerns the case where the new growth exceeds the original. This phrase is difficult to parse on its own terms, and it is precisely because of this difficulty that the *Rishonim* propose alternative readings.

### Ran's Resolution

The *Ran* offers a different *girsa* — textual reading — which he signals with *hachi garsinan*, and which provides the clearest resolution. The *Ran* reads the Gemara as follows: *hatam delo rabu gidula al ikaro* — when we say that the third generation of an onion remains forbidden even in a case of *tevel*, that ruling applies only when the new growth has not become a majority over the original. In that scenario, the onion is forbidden not because the new growth itself has acquired *terumah* or *tevel* status, but because the original forbidden piece is still physically present and has not been nullified. The new growth has not outnumbered it.

*Aval hacha gabi bitul shel terumah mishum she-rabu gidulei gidulim al ha-gidulim* — but in our case concerning *bitul* of *terumah*, the third-generation growth has become the majority over what preceded it. Under these circumstances, two conclusions follow: first, the new third-generation growth does not itself carry *terumah* status; second, it constitutes a majority sufficient to effect *bitul* of the original *terumah* onion.

The earlier Mishna's ruling that the onion remains forbidden through the third generation in the *tevel* case is thus reinterpreted: it was never saying that the growth itself acquires *tevel* status. It was simply saying that as long as the original *tevel* onion persists without having been nullified by a majority, the combined mass remains forbidden. Once the growth does constitute a majority — which is precisely the case Rabbi Chanina describes — *bitul* can and does apply. In this way, the *Ran* preserves the entirety of the *sugya*'s analysis by arguing that what grew from an onion is not itself *terumah*, that *bitul* is operative once the growth forms a majority, and that the Mishna's apparently restrictive ruling about the onion concerned only cases where no such majority existed. The conclusion: everything discussed over the preceding two days stands, circumscribed to the context of third-generation growth rather than second-generation growth.

## Temporal Language

### Today & Nightfall

Having concluded the lengthy discussion of the onion with *Hadran Alach Hanodea Min Hayarak*, the Gemara now opens a new *perek* focused on a different dimension of vows: not the object of the prohibition, but its temporal scope. If the preceding *perek* asked what is included in the noun specifying what is forbidden, this *perek* asks what is included in the time specification accompanying the vow.

The Mishna opens with a case: *konam yayin she'ani to'em hayom* — "Wine that I taste is *konam* for me today." The ruling is *eino asur ela ad shetechshach* — it is forbidden only until nightfall. The vow follows the halachic day, which ends at nightfall. Critically, this applies regardless of when during the day the vow was made: whether the person made the vow at noon or a minute before sunset, the prohibition ends when night falls. As a practical matter, this means the duration could be as brief as minutes or as long as nearly an entire calendar day.

### Weeks & Months

The Mishna proceeds to expand these principles across larger time units. *Shabbat zu* — "this Shabbat," meaning this week — renders wine forbidden *bechol ha-Shabbat*, the entire week. And the Shabbat day itself, the Mishna notes, counts *le-she'avar* — it belongs to the prior week rather than inaugurating the next. If one makes such a vow on Sunday, the coming Shabbat is included within "this week." This is treated as relatively obvious by the Gemara, with the *chiddush* being only that one not suppose the term *Shabbat* refers exclusively to the weekdays, excluding the Shabbat day itself.

Similarly, *chodesh zeh* — "this month" — forbids wine for the entire month, with *Rosh Chodesh* belonging to the next month and not included in the prohibition. The Gemara notes that this requires clarification specifically in the case of a full thirty-day month, where the thirtieth day is simultaneously the last day of the current month and the first day of *Rosh Chodesh* of the next. Since people call that day *Rosh Chodesh* — *kari inish kari lei Rosh Yarcha* — it is conceptually associated with the coming month, and the vow does not extend to cover it, even though technically it is the thirtieth day of the month in which the vow was made.

### Years & Rosh Hashana

*Shanah zo* — "this year" — forbids wine for the entire year, with *Rosh Hashana* belonging to the coming year. The Gemara observes that the Mishna uses different phrasing for *Rosh Chodesh* and *Rosh Hashana*: *Rosh Chodesh* is described as *lehaba* — what is coming — while *Rosh Hashana* is described as *le'atid lavo* — the future that is yet to come — suggesting perhaps a sense of greater temporal distance.

The seven-year *shemitah* cycle follows the same logic: *shavua zeh* forbids wine for the entire cycle, with the *shemitah* year itself belonging to the end of the prior cycle rather than beginning a new one.

### Duration Specificity

The Mishna then introduces a crucial distinction. If instead of saying "this day," "this week," "this month," or "this year," one specifies an absolute duration — *yom echad*, one day; *shabbat achat*, one week; *chodesh echad*, one month; *shanah achat*, one year — then the vow operates differently: *asur miyom leyom*, it goes from the moment of the vow forward for a full measured unit of time. If one makes a vow at 1:00 PM on Monday saying *yom echad*, the prohibition extends until 1:00 PM on Tuesday — a full twenty-four hours — rather than ending at nightfall that same day.

One might ask whether such duration-based vows calculate to the precise minute or only to the day. The Mishna does not resolve this fully for longer units such as a week or a month, though the logic of the rule implies a degree of precision: if one is on the third of *Adar* and takes a one-month vow, the vow presumably extends until the third of *Nissan*.

### Until Pesach Debate

The Mishna addresses the phrase *ad haPesach* — "until Pesach." If one uses the formulation *ad sheyagia* — until it arrives — the vow lasts until Pesach begins, and the phrase is read as exclusive: *ad velo ad bichlal*, until but not including. If one says *ad sheyehei* — until it has been — the vow lasts until Pesach concludes.

The Mishna then records a debate concerning the phrase *ad pnei haPesach* — "until the face of Pesach." Rabbi Meir holds *asur ad sheyagia*: the face of Pesach means its beginning, so the vow expires when Pesach arrives. Rabbi Yossi holds *ad sheyetze*: *pnei* refers to the receding face, i.e., the end of Pesach, so the vow continues through the entire festival. The correct *girsa* here, as noted in the margin and as established by *Rashi*, the *Ran*, and the *Rosh*, includes the word *pnei* in the Mishna text.

One might note the practical implication: a vow not to drink wine *ad pnei haPesach* could, depending on which position one follows, create an obligation to seek alternative means of fulfilling the *arba kosos* at the Seder — since *nedarim*, operating on objects rather than actions, can indirectly conflict with the performance of *mitzvot*.

### Gezeirah Logic

The Gemara turns to explore the ruling of Rabbi Yirmiyah that even though a vow said *hayom* — "today" — expires at nightfall, one nonetheless requires *she'eila lechacham*, a formal release from a sage, once night falls. The vow has lapsed; why is *heter chacham* necessary?

Rav Yosef explains: *gezeira hayom mishum yom echad* — it is a rabbinic decree, lest one who has experienced a *hayom* vow expiring at nightfall come to treat a *yom echad* vow as though it too expires at nightfall, when in fact it runs for a full twenty-four hours. If we allow a *hayom* vow to conclude without any formal act, one might reason by analogy that a *yom echad* vow likewise expires at the end of the calendar day.

Abaye challenges this: if so, should we not make the reverse decree as well — that a *yom echad* vow, even after twenty-four hours, requires *hatarat chacham* lest one mistakenly think that *hayom* means twenty-four hours too? Rav responds: *hayom beyom echad michlaf* — "today" could be confused with "one day," but *yom echad behayom lo michlaf* — "one day" cannot be confused with "today." Tosafot makes the underlying logic explicit. If one practices a *hayom* vow that expires at nightfall and then applies that experience to a *yom echad* vow, one would stop drinking too early. But if one practices a *yom echad* vow that runs twenty-four hours and tries to apply that experience backward to a *hayom* situation, the result is simply that one would abstain for longer — which causes no violation. The confusion is asymmetric: leniency in the *hayom* case could lead to improperly terminating a *yom echad* vow early, but leniency in the *yom echad* direction creates no practical error.

The Gemara then introduces a different framework through Ravina citing Ameimar in the name of Rav Yosef: this ruling of Rabbi Yirmiyah accords with the view of Rabbi Natan, who holds that anyone who makes a vow is as one who built a *bamah*, and one who fulfills it is as one who offered a sacrifice upon it. According to the *Ran*, the *gezeira* about *hatarat chacham* even after the vow has lapsed is not purely about preventing logical confusion between *hayom* and *yom echad*. Rather, the rabbinic antipathy toward vows as a category provides the underlying impetus: since vows are discouraged in principle, the rabbis imposed the requirement of *heter chacham* even for the most trivial of vows — the person who says "this item is forbidden to me until tonight" — precisely because such short, minor vows are perhaps the most prone to casual proliferation and need to be subjected to some procedural friction.

### Vows vs Fasts

The *Ran* addresses a practical corollary that flows naturally from this analysis. The custom of *ta'anit yachidim* — a private fast accepted at *mincha* time, observed from dawn until nightfall — follows the same temporal structure as a *hayom* vow: it expires at nightfall. Yet the *Ran* rules that at nightfall one does not require *hatarat chacham* to eat. *Velo gazru mishum yom echad* — no decree was made here lest one confuse it with a *yom echad* vow.

The reason: *hadavar yadua shedin shel ta'anis kach hi* — everyone knows that the law of a *ta'anis* is precisely this: it ends at nightfall. The practice of the *ta'anis* is a recognized institution with well-understood parameters, and no one observing a *ta'anis* will confuse it with a *neder* forbidding food for a full twenty-four-hour period. Moreover, *ein issuro kish'ar nedarim shekhvar kav'u lo chachamim zman* — its prohibition is not structured like ordinary *nedarim*; the sages already established its duration as a fixed matter. Even though a *ta'anis* is technically accepted through verbal declaration, the declaration does not create a *neder* in the sense of rendering all food forbidden as an object; it is an acceptance of a recognized institution that concludes at nightfall by established law. No one will confuse being in a *ta'anis* with having taken a vow that lasts a full day.

## Ambiguous Specifications

### Rosh Chodesh Dating

The Gemara raises a series of *ibayei lehu* — unresolved questions — that extend the Mishna's framework into more difficult territory. Before reaching these, however, the Gemara pauses to draw out the practical *halacha le-ma'aseh* implications of the ruling that *Rosh Chodesh* belongs to the coming month even when it falls on the thirtieth day of the preceding month — specifically, the question of how to date documents.

Tosafot addresses this directly. When writing a *ketubah*, a *shtar*, or a *get* on the first day of a two-day *Rosh Chodesh*, what date does one record? The first day of a two-day *Rosh Chodesh* is technically the thirtieth day of the prior month — so today, for instance, might be the thirtieth of *Shevat* — yet it is universally called *Rosh Chodesh Adar*. Tosafot rules: *kosvin yom ploni shehu Rosh Chodesh* — one writes the day of the week together with *Rosh Chodesh* of the new month. One does not record it as the thirtieth of the prior month, since in common usage — *lashon benei adam* — the day is called *Rosh Chodesh Adar*, not the thirtieth of *Shevat*. Since the Gemara elsewhere demonstrates that the laws of documents follow vernacular language in the same manner as *nedarim*, the same principle applies: one writes Monday, *Rosh Chodesh Adar*.

However, Tosafot adds that by a *get*, where precision is critical and error could determine whether a woman is bound or free, it is preferable to write both: *Rosh Chodesh Adar shehu yom shloshim le-chodesh Shevat* — identifying the day by both its popular name and its technical calendar position. Tosafot then cites Rabbeinu Tam as having followed this practice in the *ketubah* of his own daughter, and notes that this dual identification has become the accepted practice in *ketubot* as well. The distinction Tosafot draws — that the dual formulation is strictly required only for a *get* but recommended for a *ketubah* — is worth noting, since in practice both documents receive the fuller formulation.

### Unresolved 'Day'

The first *ibayei lehu* concerns a person who says *konam yayin she'ani to'em yom* — not *hayom* ("today") and not *yom echad* ("one day"), but simply *yom*, "day," without a demonstrative or numeral. The Mishna specifies outcomes for *hayom* (until nightfall) and for *yom echad* (twenty-four hours), but does not address the bare word *yom*. Which model does it follow?

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the Mishna itself. One might reason: since the Mishna says *hayom* goes until nightfall, surely *yom* without the demonstrative *ha-* would be treated more like *yom echad* — a full day. The Gemara responds by pointing to the *seifa* of the Mishna, which rules that *yom echad* means twenty-four hours. Does not that imply by contrast that anything short of the explicit *yom echad* formulation — including bare *yom* — is treated like *hayom*? The Gemara rejects this reasoning: *ela meha lika le-mishma minah* — one cannot derive the answer from the Mishna, because the Mishna addresses only the two explicit cases and says nothing about what falls between them.

Rav Ashi then attempts to bring a proof from a *Beraita*: *konam yayin she'ani to'em hashana nitabra hashana asur bah u've'iburah* — if one says "this year" and the year is subsequently declared a leap year with an added second *Adar*, the vow extends through the added month as well. Rav Ashi suggests that the *chiddush* of this *Beraita* is not the leap-year extension — since if the year ends at the conclusion of *Elul*, the inclusion of *Adar Sheni* is entirely obvious — but rather that the *Beraita* was formulated using the word *shana* alone, without *ha-*, and yet it treats that as equivalent to *hashana*, "this year." By analogy, *yom* would be equivalent to *hayom*, ending at nightfall.

The Gemara rejects this argument: *le'olam de'amar hashana* — the *Beraita* was in fact saying *hashana*, "this year," all along, and its *chiddush* is precisely the leap-year point. The *hava amina* would have been: since most years are not leap years, one could follow the majority of years and hold that the vow ended with the ordinary *Adar* even in a leap year. The *Beraita* teaches that the vow follows the actual year in which it was taken, not the statistical norm. The word *shana* does not enter the analysis.

Since the question remains unresolved — *teiku* — practical *halacha* defaults to the more stringent position: *yom* is treated as *yom echad*, a full twenty-four hours.

### Yovel Cycle Debate

The Gemara next poses an *ibayei lehu* regarding the *Yovel* — the Jubilee year that concludes a fifty-year cycle. If one says wine is forbidden to him for "this *Yovel*," does the prohibition extend through the fiftieth year itself, or does the fiftieth year belong to the next cycle?

The parallel to the weekly and monthly cases is suggestive. Just as *Shabbat* was said to belong to the prior week rather than inaugurate the next, one might reason that the *Yovel* year, as the culmination of seven *shemitah* cycles, belongs to the period just concluded, and the vow therefore extends through it. But equally, one might argue as follows.

### Rabbi Meir vs Yehuda

The Gemara brings a *Beraita* recording a dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis derived from the verse *vekidashtem et shnat ha-hamishim shana* — "you shall sanctify the fiftieth year." The *derashah* yields: *shnat hamishim ata moneh ve-ee ata moneh shnat hamishim ve-ahat* — you count year fifty as fifty, and you do not count it simultaneously as fifty and as one of the next cycle. The Rabbis derive from here that year fifty is the conclusion of the prior cycle; the following year begins a fresh cycle as year one. The fifty-year unit is a complete and closed unit.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees: *yovel eino oleh le-minyan shavua* — the *Yovel* year does not count toward the seven-year cycle that follows it. Rather, *yovel oleh le-minyan shavua* — the *Yovel* year counts as year one of the next cycle. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the effective unit is forty-nine years, and what one calls the "fiftieth year" is simultaneously the first year of the subsequent cycle. This produces a practically significant consequence: the first cycle after a *Yovel* would have only five years of permitted planting, since the *Yovel* year itself is forbidden for agricultural work, and the immediately following year is *shemitah*, leaving only five intervening years.

The Rabbis challenge Rabbi Yehuda: the Torah says *shesh shanim tizra sadecha* — you shall plant your field for six years — yet according to his system, in the cycle immediately following a *Yovel*, there are only five plantable years. Rabbi Yehuda replies symmetrically: and according to you, the Torah says *ve-tziviti et birchati li-shlosh ha-shanim* — I will command My blessing for the three years — referring to the produce of year six sustaining the people through year seven (the *shemitah*), year eight (until the new harvest), and part of year six itself. But according to his interlocutors' system, at the boundary of a *Yovel* cycle, the produce of year six would need to sustain the people through year seven (*shemitah*), year eight (*Yovel*), and into year nine — four years, not three. The Gemara responds for both sides: in each case, the verse is speaking of the ordinary situation, not the boundary case involving *Yovel*. Each disputant's anomalous case is simply not the referent of that verse.

### Yovel & Vows

The Gemara closes by applying this dispute directly to the vow question: if someone says "this *Yovel* is forbidden to me," the question of whether the *Yovel* year itself is included depends precisely on whether year fifty belongs to the prior cycle (the Rabbis' view, in which case the vow runs through the *Yovel* year) or inaugurates the next (Rabbi Yehuda's view, in which case by the time the *Yovel* arrives, the "this *Yovel*" period has already concluded and wine becomes permissible).

## Hermeneutic Debate

### Ambiguity Principle

Turning to daf 60b, the Gemara addresses the broader interpretive principle underlying the debate between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi about *ad pnei haPesach*. The Gemara frames the dispute not merely as a question about the meaning of the word *pnei* but as a fundamental disagreement about how to interpret inherently ambiguous language in vows.

The Gemara formulates it thus: *Rabbi Meir savar lo ma'ayil inish nafshieh li-sfeika* — Rabbi Meir holds that a person does not wish to place himself in doubtful territory. Since the phrase *pnei haPesach* is ambiguous — it could mean the beginning of Pesach, the middle, or the end — Rabbi Meir assumes the speaker intended only the unambiguous, clear meaning: until Pesach begins, but not into the period of the festival itself, whose inclusion would be uncertain. *Rabbi Yossi savar ma'ayil inish nafshieh li-sfeika* — Rabbi Yossi holds that a person is willing to have his language carry ambiguous implications. The speaker using the word *pnei* may well have intended one of the possible meanings that extends into or through Pesach. Rabbi Yossi therefore rules that the prohibition extends until Pesach concludes.

### Marriage Ambiguity

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty: the positions appear reversed in a different *sugya*. In a case where a man has two wives and from each wife has two daughters — an older daughter and a younger daughter — and he says *kidashti et biti ha-gedolah*, "I betrothed my older daughter," the Gemara in Kiddushin records a dispute about which daughter is meant. The terms *gedolah she-ba-gedolot* (the older daughter of the older wife), *gedolah she-ba-ketanot* (the older daughter of the younger wife), and *ketanah she-ba-gedolot* (the younger daughter of the older wife, who is "older" only in the sense of coming from the first wife) are all plausible referents of the term *gedolah*. The only daughter clearly excluded is *ketanah she-ba-ketanot* — the younger daughter of the younger wife, who cannot by any reading be called "older."

Rabbi Meir there rules *kulan asurot chutz min ha-ketanah she-ba-ketanot* — all daughters except the youngest of the younger wife are in a state of doubtful betrothal. Rabbi Yossi rules *kulan mutarot chutz min ha-gedolah she-ba-gedolot* — only the older daughter of the older wife is presumptively betrothed, since that is the narrowest and most definite meaning of *gedolah*. In Kiddushin, then, it is Rabbi Meir who extends the ambiguous possibilities and Rabbi Yossi who restricts interpretation to the unambiguous core — precisely the reverse of what the Gemara stated above regarding *pnei haPesach*.

### Reversed Positions

The Gemara resolves the contradiction by citing Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa in the name of Rav: *muchlefa ha-shita* — the positions are transposed. In our Mishna — concerning *pnei haPesach* — Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yossi should be understood in reverse of how they were first presented. The version of the debate in Kiddushin, where Rabbi Meir expands to all ambiguous possibilities and Rabbi Yossi restricts to the most definite meaning, reflects their true and consistent positions.

### General Principle

A *Beraita* then resolves the matter by formulating a general principle: *zeh ha-kelal, kol she-zmano kavu'a, ve-amar ad pnei* — whenever something has a fixed, defined time, and one uses the phrase *ad pnei*, Rabbi Meir rules *ad sheyetze* — until it concludes — and Rabbi Yossi rules *ad sheyagia* — until it begins. This *Beraita* is consistent with the Kiddushin ruling and confirms that the *muchlefa* applies to our Mishna: in the final understanding, Rabbi Meir includes the ambiguous duration and Rabbi Yossi restricts interpretation to the clear and definite beginning point. The underlying principle, on which both cases rest, is precisely whether one assumes a speaker intends his ambiguous language to carry its full possible range of meaning or only its most restricted and uncontested sense.
