# Pesachim 5 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Timing of Chametz Removal

### Previous Discussion Cont…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ הַשְׁבָּתַת שְׂאוֹר לַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ, וַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ לַאֲכִילַת מַצָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The time for **the removal of leaven is juxtaposed to** the time when **the eating of leavened bread** is prohibited. When the prohibition against eating leaven goes into effect, the obligation to remove leaven is in effect as well. **And** furthermore, the time of the prohibition against **the eating of leavened bread** is juxtaposed **to** the time for **the eating of *matza*,** as its prohibition takes effect from the time that the mitzva to eat *matza* takes effect.

The discussion on this daf flows directly from the previous day's analysis of *hashbata* and *biur*. The central question concerns the precise timing of *biyur chametz*: when exactly must one eliminate *chametz* before *Pesach*?

### Why Not Night Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁבָּתַת שְׂאוֹר לַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ, דִּכְתִיב: "שִׁבְעַת יָמִים שְׂאֹר לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּבָתֵּיכֶם כִּי כָּל אֹכֵל מַחְמֶצֶת וְנִכְרְתָה".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The removal of leaven** is juxtaposed **to the eating of leavened bread,** as they appear in the same verse, **as it is written: "Seven days leaven shall not be found in your houses, as anyone who eats that which is leavened,** that soul **shall be cut off** from the congregation of Israel" (Exodus 12:19).

The answer emerges from a chain of interconnected obligations. The removal of *se'or* is linked to the prohibition of *chametz*, which is in turn linked to the eating of *matzah*. Since we know that when one begins eating *matzah* one must already have been rid of *chametz*, the elimination must precede that moment. This rules out the night before — but it equally rules out doing it a full twenty-four hours prior, since the relevant *pesukim* indicate the removal occurs during the day.

### Why Not Early Morning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ לַאֲכִילַת מַצָּה, דִּכְתִיב: "כָּל מַחְמֶצֶת לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ בְּכֹל מוֹשְׁבֹתֵיכֶם תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת (וְגוֹ׳)", וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּמַצָּה: "בָּעֶרֶב תֹּאכְלוּ מַצּוֹת".
>
> **Translation:** **And** the prohibition against **the eating of leavened bread** is juxtaposed **to the eating of *matza*,** as both appear in the same verse, **as it is written: "You shall not eat anything that is leavened; in all of your dwellings you shall eat *matzot*, etc."** (Exodus 12:20), **and it is written with regard to *matza*:** "On the first day, on the fourteenth day **in the evening you shall eat *matzot*"** (Exodus 12:18). Since the *halakha* that leaven is prohibited on the first night of Passover is derived from this source, there is no need for an additional derivation.

If the removal must happen during the day, why not first thing in the morning, in keeping with the principle of *zerizin makdimin*? The answer lies in the word *ach*. The particle *ach* functions as a limiting term — *mechaleik* — dividing the day. It indicates that only a portion of the day is the relevant time for this act, not the entirety of it.

### Biyur After Six Hours

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא לְרַבּוֹת לֵיל אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר לְבִיעוּר? "בַּיּוֹם" כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that the verse: "Yet on the first day you shall remove leaven from your houses" comes **to include the night of the fourteenth** in the obligation **to remove** leaven, which would mean that one must remove all leaven from his house on the night of the fourteenth. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: That is not possible, as **"on the day" is written.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא מִצַּפְרָא! "אַךְ" חִלֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to ask: **And say** that leaven must be removed immediately **from the morning** of the fourteenth. The Gemara answers: That is also incorrect, as the verse says, "Yet on the first day"; and the word **yet divides.** The connotation of the word yet is one of restriction. In this context, it teaches that leaven is prohibited not for the entire day, but only for part of it. One is obligated to remove leaven only for the second half of the fourteenth of Nisan, not for the first half of the day.

Drawing together all these elements — the *hekesh* linking *se'or*, *chametz*, and *matzah*, the requirement that removal occur during the day rather than the night before, and the limiting force of *ach* — the *gemara* concludes that *biyur chametz* is performed after six hours of the day have passed.

## Dvar Bei R. Ishmael

### Rishon Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: מָצִינוּ אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר שֶׁנִּקְרָא רִאשׁוֹן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "בָּרִאשׁוֹן בְּאַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם לַחֹדֶשׁ".
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: We found** that the **fourteenth** day **is called: First, as it is stated: "On *harishon*, on the fourteenth day of the month"** (Exodus 12:18).

A different approach to the same question is introduced by a teaching from the school of Rabbi Ishmael. The central question is what *rishon* refers to in the verse *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem*. Does it mean the first day of *Pesach* itself, or does it mean a different day entirely?

### 14th of Nisan = Rishon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: רִאשׁוֹן — דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: "הָרִאשׁוֹן אָדָם תִּוָּלֵד".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** It is evident from the verse itself that it is referring to the removal of leaven on the fourteenth, **as *rishon* means previous.** In this context, first means the day that precedes the others, i.e., the day before the Festival begins, **as the verse stated: "Are you first [*rishon*] man born?** Or were you brought forth before the hills?" (Job 15:7). Based on the parallelism between the two parts of the verse, the word *rishon* here means before; the one preceding all others.

The school of Rabbi Ishmael teaches: *matzinu yud-daled shenikra rishon* — we find that the fourteenth of Nisan is explicitly called *rishon* in a *pasuk*. The verse *barishon b'arba'ah asar yom lachodesh* demonstrates that the term *rishon* can refer to the fourteenth of the month. When the Torah therefore commands *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem*, the *rishon* does not refer to the first day of *Pesach* but rather to the fourteenth of Nisan, the day preceding the *chag*.

### Textual Parallels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, "וּלְקַחְתֶּם לָכֶם בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן", הָכִי נָמֵי רִאשׁוֹן דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is **so,** consider a verse written with regard to *Sukkot*: **"And you shall take for yourselves on the first [*harishon*] day** the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of palm trees, and boughs of thick trees, and willows of the brook" (Leviticus 23:40). **So too,** in this case, does ***harishon* mean** the day **previous** to the Festival? Clearly, one is not obligated to take the four species on the fourteenth of Tishrei, the eve of *Sukkot*.

The broader textual context in Parshat Bo reinforces this reading. Several *pesukim* in close proximity employ the term *rishon*. The verse *ki kol ochel chametz venichreta hanefesh hahi miyom harishon ad yom hashvi'i* uses *harishon* to describe the first day of the *chag*. Immediately following: *ubayom harishon mikra kodesh uvayom hashvi'i mikra kodesh yihyeh lachem*. Then, one *pasuk* further: *barishon b'arba'ah asar yom lachodesh baerev tochlu matzot* — where *barishon* clearly refers to the first month of the year, not the first day of the festival. This last usage is admittedly *pshat*, not the school of Rabbi Ishmael's intended *drash*, but the point stands that *rishon* carries multiple referents across the *pesukim*.

## Rav Nachman's View

### Rishon = Prior

The school of Rabbi Ishmael's interpretation is challenged by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, who offers an independent interpretation. He proposes that *rishon* in biblical usage can inherently mean "prior" or "beforehand" — not merely "first." His proof is a *pasuk* outside the Torah: *harishon adam tivaled* — "were you born prior to the first man?" This usage establishes that *rishon* can indicate temporal priority rather than sequential position. Accordingly, when the Torah commands *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem*, *rishon* means the day prior to the *chag*.

### Sukkot Counterexample

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דִּכְתִיב: "וּשְׂמַחְתֶּם לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים", מָה "שְׁבִיעִי" — שְׁבִיעִי לֶחָג, אַף "רִאשׁוֹן" — רִאשׁוֹן לֶחָג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this contention. **There it is different, as it is written** immediately thereafter: **"And you shall rejoice before the Lord your God seven days"** (Leviticus 23:40). **Just as the seventh** of these seven days is **the seventh day of the Festival,** so **too,** the **first** of these days is the **first** day **of the Festival** itself, not the day before *Sukkot*. However, where it is not explicitly stated, *rishon* means the day before the Festival.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this position. If *rishon* invariably means "prior," what is one to make of the verse *ul'kachtem lachem bayom harishon* — the commandment to take the *lulav* on the first day of *Sukkot*? Would we conclude that one must take the *lulav* the day before *Sukkot* begins? That is plainly not the practice, and no one suggests it. Thus *rishon* cannot categorically mean "prior."

### Seventh = First (Chag)

The *gemara* responds that *Sukkot* presents a different case. The *pasuk* states *u'semachtem lifnei Hashem Elokeichem shivat yamim* — you shall rejoice before Hashem for seven days. Within this framework, the comparison is explicit: *ma shevii shevii lachag*, just as the seventh day of rejoicing plainly means the seventh day of the *chag*, *af rishon rishon lachag* — so too the first day means the first day of the *chag*. This parallel locks the *rishon* in *Sukkot* to the festival itself and prevents it from meaning "the day before."

But then the same logic could be applied to *Pesach*: the verse *shivat yamim matzot tochelu ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem* similarly situates *harishon* within the framework of seven days of eating *matzah*. By the same reasoning, *bayom harishon* in this verse would refer to the first day of *Pesach*, not the day before.

### Mikra Kodesh Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי כְּתִיב: "אַךְ בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן תַּשְׁבִּיתוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים מַצּוֹת תֹּאכֵלוּ"! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא "רִאשׁוֹן", "הָרִאשׁוֹן" לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty. **Here too, it is written** with regard to Passover: **"Yet on the first [*harishon*] day you shall remove** leaven from your houses"; **"for seven days you shall eat *matza*"** (Exodus 12:15). Just as seventh here is referring to the seventh day of the Festival, so too, *rishon* must refer to the first day of the Festival. The Gemara answers: **If so, let** the verse **write *rishon*; why do I** need the addition of the definite article, ***harishon*?**

The difficulty intensifies when one considers *ubayom harishon mikra kodesh uvayom hashvi'i mikra kodesh yihyeh lachem kol melacha lo ye'aseh bahem*. Here *harishon* and *hashvi'i* stand in precise parallel: the first day is a *mikra kodesh* and the seventh day is a *mikra kodesh*. If the seventh day unambiguously refers to a day of the festival, the parallelism makes it extremely difficult to argue that *harishon* means anything other than the actual first day of *Pesach*. The *pesukim* are so symmetrical that the interpretation strains credulity.

### HaRishon Excludes Chol H…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִכְדַאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** Learn from it, as we said:** *Harishon* means the day before the Festival.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי הָתָם נָמֵי, "הָרִאשׁוֹן" לְמָה לִי? וְתוּ, הָתָם דִּכְתִיב: "בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן שַׁבָּתוֹן וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי שַׁבָּתוֹן", אֵימַר "רִאשׁוֹן" דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא מַשְׁמַע?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **If so, there too,** with regard to *Sukkot*, **why do I** need the verse to say ***harishon*? And furthermore, there it is written: "On the first [*harishon*] day a solemn rest and on the eighth day a solemn rest"** (Leviticus 23:39). Here too, **say** that **first means previous,** the day preceding the Festival.

The *gemara* then pivots to a grammatical observation. The text could have written simply *ach bayom rishon* — but instead it writes *ach bayom harishon*, with the definite article *heh*. The extra *heh* is the critical textual element. The question is: *harishon lama li* — why is the definite article needed? The answer is *shema mina lechde'amran* — it signals that *rishon* here does not mean the first day of the *chag*, but rather something prior to it. Meanwhile, the *harishon* in the *Sukkot* context serves a different purpose: *lemiyutei cholo shel moed* — to exclude *Chol HaMoed*. Only the first and seventh days are days on which *melacha* is forbidden, not the intermediate days of the festival.

### Grammar: Vav and He

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּאָמַר קְרָא: "וּבַיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי שַׁבָּתוֹן", מָה "שְׁמִינִי" — שְׁמִינִי דְּחַג, אַף "רִאשׁוֹן" — רִאשׁוֹן דְּחַג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **It is different there, as the verse said: "And on the eighth day a solemn rest,"** from which it can be inferred that **just as** the **eighth** means the **eighth** day **of the Festival,** so **too, *rishon*** is referring to the **first** day **of the Festival.**

This answer generates its own difficulty. The reason one might have thought *Chol HaMoed* was included is because of the verse *uvayom hashmini shabaton* — the *vav* of *uvayom* invokes the principle *vav mosif al inyan rishon*, meaning the conjunctive *vav* adds the eighth day to what came before and might suggest that the intermediate days are included as well. It is therefore necessary to use *harishon* — with the *heh* — to teach that only the first day and the eighth day carry the *issur melacha*, not *Chol HaMoed*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הָרִאשׁוֹן״ לְמָה לִי? לְמַעוֹטֵי חוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד. חוּלּוֹ שֶׁל מוֹעֵד מֵ״רִאשׁוֹן״ וּ״שְׁמִינִי״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara repeats its earlier question: **Why do I** need the verse to say ***harishon*?** The Gemara answers: The definite article comes **to exclude the intermediate days** of the Festival. It is not prohibited to perform labor on these days, as the full-fledged sanctity of the Festival does not apply to them. The Gemara says: The status of **the intermediate days is derived from** the words **first and eighth.** The fact that the verse mentions only the first and the eighth days as Festivals clearly indicates that the days between them are not Festivals.

But the *gemara* then asks: if the only reason we need the *heh* in *harishon* is because of the *vav* in *uvayom hashmini*, why not simply write *bayom shemini* without the *vav*, thereby eliminating the need for the *heh* in *harishon* in the first place? The *gemara* answers that this move is blocked by the verse *ubayom harishon mikra kodesh*, which itself uses *harishon* with the definite article. If *rishon* there means the actual first day of the *chag*, which seems unavoidable given the context, then the attempt to use *harishon* in the *hashbata* verse to mean "the day before" runs into an almost inescapable parallel that argues against it. The structural parallels between the *pesukim* are so close that the argument of *rishon* meaning "prior" has largely reached its limits.

## Three Rishonim

### Rishon for Derash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, הָנֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה "רִאשׁוֹן" מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that **those three** times the word ***rishon*** is mentioned with regard to the Festivals are **necessary for that which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught.**

At this point, the *gemara* takes a step back and acknowledges the difficulty. On its face, the attempt to read *rishon* as "the day before" cannot be sustained against the full weight of the parallel *pesukim* in *Sukkot* and *Pesach*. The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between *peshat* and *drash*: the three instances of *harishon* in the *Sukkot* and *Pesach* contexts — the *issur melacha* on *Pesach*, the *issur melacha* on *Sukkot* together with the *mitzvah* of *lulav* — are each used for their plain meaning in context, referring to the actual first day of the *chag*. The only reason one might have thought otherwise is to serve the *drasha* of *rishon* meaning "prior" — but that *drasha* is not needed for *peshat* purposes in those verses.

Instead, the *gemara* says: *ella hanei shelosha rishon miba'ei lei kedatanya devei Rabbi Yishmael*. These three instances of *rishon* are required for a specific *drasha* taught by the school of Rabbi Ishmael, and it is precisely because they are "used up" for this *drasha* that the *harishon* in the *hashbata* verse can be understood as meaning "prior." The argument is not that *rishon* always means "the day before" everywhere — it is that in the specific context of *hashbata*, once the other *harishon* instances are accounted for, this one remains available to mean something different.

### Merit of Rishonim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: בִּשְׂכַר שְׁלֹשָׁה "רִאשׁוֹן", זָכוּ לִשְׁלֹשָׁה "רִאשׁוֹן": לְהַכְרִית זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל עֵשָׂו, לְבִנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְלִשְׁמוֹ שֶׁל מָשִׁיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **As the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: In reward for the three** times the word ***rishon*** is stated with regard to the Festivals observed by the Jewish people, **they were entitled to three** matters also referred to as ***rishon*: To eradicate the descendants of Esau, to the construction of the Temple, and to the name of Messiah.**

The teaching of the school of Rabbi Ishmael is: *bisechar shelosha rishon zachu leshelosha rishon* — in the merit of three *rishonim*, Israel merited three other *rishonim*. The three rewards are: the destruction of the seed of Esau, the building of the *Beit HaMikdash*, and the name of *Mashiach*.

### Esau's Seed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַכְרִית זַרְעוֹ שֶׁל עֵשָׂו, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיֵּצֵא הָרִאשׁוֹן אַדְמוֹנִי כֻּלּוֹ כְּאַדֶּרֶת שֵׂעָר".
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* provides the sources for his statement. **To eradicate the descendants of Esau, as it is written: "And the first [*harishon*] came forth red, all over like a hairy mantle;** and they called his name Esau" (Genesis 25:25).

The first of the three *rishonim* for which Israel receives reward corresponds to the destruction of the seed of Esau. The *pasuk* cited is *vayeitzei harishon admoni kulo keaderet se'ar* — Esau emerged first, red all over like a hairy garment. The *Maharsha* elaborates: *Pesach* celebrates the *geulah* from Egypt, and just as God redeemed Israel from that subjugation, so too will Israel be redeemed from the subjugation of the seed of Esau. The parallel between past and future redemption is the animating logic here.

The *Maharsha* also develops a homiletic dimension: the *se'or* that one removes is understood to represent the *yetzer hara*, identified with the *koach* of Esau. The ability to overcome the *yetzer hara* — to eliminate the *se'or* — prefigures the ultimate elimination of the evil it represents. The future redemption will bring the eradication of the desire to do evil, corresponding to the removal of the *yetzer hara* entirely.

### Beit HaMikdash

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבִנְיַן בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, דִּכְתִיב: "כִּסֵּא כָבוֹד מָרוֹם מֵרִאשׁוֹן מְקוֹם מִקְדָּשֵׁנוּ".
>
> **Translation:** **And to the construction of the Temple, as it is written:** "The **Throne of Glory, on High from the beginning [*merishon*], the place of our Temple"** (Jeremiah 17:12).

The second *rishon* for which Israel is rewarded corresponds to the building of the *Beit HaMikdash*. The *pasuk* cited is *kissei kavod marom merishon mekom mikdashenu* — a throne of glory, exalted from the very beginning, is the place of our sanctuary. The festival of *Sukkot* and the verse *ki vasuccot hoshavti et b'nei Yisrael behotzi'i otam me'eretz Mitzrayim* also commemorates the redemption from Egypt. Just as God spread the *ananei hakavod* over Israel in the wilderness — which *Sukkot* recalls — so too will God restore the *shechina* through the rebuilding of the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Mashiach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִשְׁמוֹ שֶׁל מָשִׁיחַ, דִּכְתִיב: "רִאשׁוֹן לְצִיּוֹן הִנֵּה הִנָּם".
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Jewish people were also entitled **to the name of Messiah, as it is written: "A harbinger [*rishon*] to Zion** I will give: **Behold, behold them;** and to Jerusalem a messenger of good tidings" (Isaiah 41:27). However, *harishon* stated with regard to Passover is referring to the day before the Festival.

The third *rishon* for which Israel is rewarded corresponds to the name of *Mashiach*. The *pasuk* cited is *rishon leTziyon hinei hinam* — the first one to Zion, here they are. The connection runs through the *mitzvah* of *lulav*, which the verse introduces with *ul'kachtem lachem bayom harishon*, and which is associated with *Sukkot* as *zman simchatenu*. Because of this *rishon* of rejoicing, Israel merits the *rishon* who is *Mashiach*.

The *gemara*'s resolution thus works as follows: the three *harishon* instances elsewhere are legitimately occupied by this *drasha* and do not function as proofs that *rishon* means the first day of the festival in the *hashbata* verse. It must be said, though, that this is not the most straightforwardly convincing of readings — and indeed, several other, more direct answers were already provided earlier and will be developed further.

## Rava's Proof

### Lo Tishchat Al Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: "לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חָמֵץ דַּם זִבְחִי" — לֹא תִּשְׁחַט הַפֶּסַח וַעֲדַיִן חָמֵץ קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *halakha* that leaven is prohibited from midday on the fourteenth of Nisan is derived **from here: "You shall not slaughter the blood of My offering over leavened bread;** neither shall the offering of the feast of the Passover be left to the morning" (Exodus 34:25). This verse means that **you shall not slaughter the Paschal lamb while** your **leavened bread is still intact.** In other words, all leaven must be removed before the time the Paschal lamb may be slaughtered.

Rava offers an independent and more direct source for the prohibition. The verse states *lo tishchat al chametz dam zivchi* — do not slaughter the Pesach offering while *chametz* still exists in your possession. Rava reads this as a temporal command: one cannot perform *shechitat haPesach* so long as one still has *chametz*. Since *shechitat haPesach* takes place from midday of the fourteenth of Nisan onward, it follows that *chametz* must be eliminated before that point. This provides a clear *asmachta* for the timing of *biyur chametz*.

### Individual Shifts Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כָּל חַד וְחַד כִּי שָׁחֵיט! זְמַן שְׁחִיטָה אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **And say** that the verse means that **each and every person** must ensure that he has no leaven in his possession **when he slaughters** his own Paschal lamb, and there is no fixed time for this prohibition. The Gemara answers: **The Merciful One states the time of** the **slaughter** of the Paschal lamb, which begins at the end of the sixth hour. In other words, this verse is referring to a particular point in time, rather than the individual act of slaughtering the Paschal lamb.

The *gemara* raises a difficulty. The verse literally addresses each individual at the moment of *shechita*. But in the *Beit HaMikdash*, the *bnei Yisrael* were divided into three *katim* — three shifts — for the *shechita*. Each individual performs *shechita* at a slightly different time. If the prohibition means that each person cannot have *chametz* at the precise moment he slaughters, then one person's *zman* might fall later than another's, just as one who accepts *Shabbat* early is bound from that moment while others are not yet bound. The person slaughtering in the third shift could theoretically still be eating *chametz* while someone in the first shift has already slaughtered. Why should the prohibition apply uniformly to everyone from the same moment?

### Zman Shechita Begins

The *gemara* resolves this by explaining that *zman shechita amar Rachmana* — the Torah is referring not to each individual's personal moment of slaughter but to the *zman shechita* as a collective institution: the moment when *shechita* begins for people generally. Unlike the individual acceptance of *Shabbat*, which genuinely operates on a personal timeline, the *zman shechita* of the *Pesach* is a fixed communal threshold. From the point when *shechita* becomes possible for anyone, *chametz* must already have been eliminated.

## Tanya Interpretations

### Three Meanings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: "אַךְ בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן תַּשְׁבִּיתוּ שְּׂאֹר מִבָּתֵּיכֶם" — מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם טוֹב עַצְמוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לֹא תִשְׁחַט עַל חָמֵץ דַּם זִבְחִי" — לֹא תִּשְׁחַט אֶת הַפֶּסַח וַעֲדַיִין חָמֵץ קַיָּים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds that some of the aforementioned opinions **were also taught** in a *baraita*: **"Yet on the first day you shall remove leaven from your houses"** (Exodus 12:15). This prohibition is in effect **from the eve of the Festival. Or perhaps** that is **not** the case, but **it** applies **only to the Festival itself. The verse states: "You shall not slaughter the blood of My offering over leavened bread"** (Exodus 34:25), meaning that **you shall not slaughter the Paschal lamb while** your **leavened bread is still intact.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

A *baraita* addresses the same verse — *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem* — and considers three possible readings: does the commandment apply *me'erev Yom Tov*, from the eve of the festival the night before; does it apply *beyom Yom Tov atzmo*, on the festival day itself; or does it refer to some division of the day?

### Cannot Slaughter with Ch…

The *baraita* resolves the question by citing the prohibition of *lo tishchat al chametz*: one cannot slaughter the Pesach offering while *chametz* remains. This demonstrates that *chametz* must be eliminated before the *shechita* of the Pesach, which occurs on the fourteenth from midday — and therefore the commandment to remove *se'or* cannot be referring to the festival day itself.

## Rabbi Akiva's Answer

### Melacha Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: "אַךְ בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן תַּשְׁבִּיתוּ שְּׂאֹר מִבָּתֵּיכֶם", וּכְתִיב: "כָּל מְלָאכָה לֹא (תַעֲשׂוּ)", וּמָצִינוּ לַהַבְעָרָה שֶׁהִיא אַב מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: There is no need** for this proof, **as it says: "Yet on the first day you shall remove leaven from your houses," and it is written:** "And on the first day there shall be to you a sacred convocation, and on the seventh day a sacred convocation; **you shall perform no manner of work** on them" (Exodus 12:16). **And we found that kindling** a fire **is a primary category of** prohibited **labor.** Since the fire in which the leaven is burned is not for the preparation of food, kindling it is not performed for the purpose of the Festival. Therefore, it is prohibited to burn the leaven on the Festival itself. Consequently, one must burn the leaven on the day before the Festival.

Rabbi Akiva offers yet another independent argument. He cites two verses in proximity: *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem* — on the first day eliminate *se'or* from your homes — and the adjacent verse *kol melacha lo ta'asu* — no work shall be done. If *harishon* referred to the first day of *Pesach*, and if eliminating *chametz* requires burning, there would be a direct contradiction: one cannot perform the *melacha* of burning on a *Yom Tov* when *melacha* is forbidden. The only way to resolve the contradiction is to read *harishon* as referring to the day before *Yom Tov* — the fourteenth of Nisan — when *melacha* is still permitted. Thus, Rabbi Akiva derives from the internal tension between the two verses that *biyur chametz* must occur prior to *Yom Tov*.

## Rabbi Yossi's Answer

### Ach Divides Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: "אַךְ בַּיּוֹם הָרִאשׁוֹן תַּשְׁבִּיתוּ שְּׂאֹר מִבָּתֵּיכֶם" — מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב. אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּיוֹם טוֹב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אַךְ״, חִלֵּק. וְאִי בְּיוֹם טוֹב עַצְמוֹ, מִי שְׁרֵי? הָא אִיתַּקַּשׁ הַשְׁבָּתַת שְׂאוֹר לַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ, וַאֲכִילַת חָמֵץ לַאֲכִילַת מַצָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: There is no need** for that proof either, **as it says: "Yet on the first day you shall remove leaven from your houses."** This prohibition applies **from the eve of the Festival. Or perhaps** that is **not** the case, but **it** applies only **to the Festival itself. The verse states: Yet,** which comes **to divide** the day into two parts; the first half, when leaven is permitted, and the second half, when it is prohibited. **And if** this verse is referring **to** the first day of **the Festival itself, is** leaven **permitted** on the actual Festival? As explained above, **the removal of leaven is juxtaposed to the eating of leavened bread, and the eating of leavened bread** is juxtaposed **to the eating of *matza*.** 

Rabbi Yossi returns to the limiting force of *ach*. The word *ach* in *ach bayom harishon tashbitu se'or mibateichem* functions as *chalak* — it divides the day, indicating that only part of the day is meant. And if *harishon* refers to the first day of *Pesach*, *ach* would be meaningless, since one cannot have *chametz* at all during any part of that day. The verse must therefore be speaking of a day during which one is permitted to have *chametz* for part of the time and must eliminate it during another part — which can only be *erev Pesach*.

### Hekesh to Matzah

Rabbi Yossi's argument, when unpacked, relies on the same chain of *heikeshim* established earlier. The prohibition of *hashbatat se'or* is linked by *hekesh* to *achilat chametz*, which is itself linked to *achilat matzah* — *itkash hashbatat se'or la'achilat chametz, vechol chametz la'achilat matzah*. On the first day of *Pesach* one cannot have *chametz* at any point; it is therefore impossible to read *harishon* in the *hashbata* verse as referring to that day. Rabbi Yossi thus arrives at the same conclusion as the earlier discussion, but via the *ach* rather than through the *heikesh* alone.

## Rabbi Akiva's Answer (II)

### Matzah on Erev Pesach

An implicit assumption in Rabbi Akiva's argument requires clarification. One might ask: if *erev Pesach* is a day during which one may still have *chametz* for part of the time, why is it that one does not eat *matzah* on that day? After all, if the *hekesh* ties together *se'or*, *chametz*, and *matzah*, one might think *matzah* eating begins from the same point that *chametz* must cease. The answer is that the practice of not eating *matzah* on *erev Pesach* is rabbinic in origin — the concern being *lete'avon*, that one should approach the *seder* night with appetite and enthusiasm for the *matzah*. Some extend this practice even further, refraining from *matzah* for thirty days before *Pesach*. This practice, however, is distinct from any biblical prohibition.

## Three Lessons from R. Ak…

### Biyur = Sreifah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא תְּלָת. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אֵין בִּיעוּר חָמֵץ אֶלָּא שְׂרֵיפָה, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַבְעָרָה לְחַלֵּק יָצָאת, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: לָא אָמְרִינַן הוֹאִיל וְהוּתְּרָה הַבְעָרָה לְצוֹרֶךְ — הוּתְּרָה נָמֵי שֶׁלֹּא לְצוֹרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Learn from** the statement **of Rabbi Akiva three** *halakhot*. **Learn from it** that **the removal of leavened bread** can be performed **only by** means of **burning.** Rabbi Akiva bases his opinion on the fact that it is prohibited to kindle a fire on the Festival. **And** second, **learn from it** that the prohibition against kindling a fire on Shabbat **was** specifically singled **out** in the Torah **to divide** the various primary categories of labor and to establish liability for performance of each of them. The dissenting opinion is that kindling is singled out to teach that there is no capital punishment for performing that primary category of labor. **And** third, **learn from it** that **we do not say: Since** it is **permitted** to **kindle** a fire **for** the **purpose** of preparing food, it is **also permitted** to light a fire **not for** the **purpose** of preparing food, e.g., to burn leaven.

Rava observes: *shema mina midivrei Rabbi Akiva telat* — three distinct *halachot* can be derived from Rabbi Akiva's position. The first: *ein bi'ur chametz ela sereifah* — the proper method of *biyur chametz* is specifically burning. How does this follow from Rabbi Akiva? Rabbi Akiva's entire argument rests on the premise that eliminating *chametz* constitutes a *melacha*. There are many ways one might conceivably eliminate *chametz* — eating it beforehand, throwing it into the ocean, disposing of it otherwise — none of which involve a *melacha*. If Rabbi Akiva says that performing *biyur* on *Yom Tov* would be problematic because of *melacha*, he must be holding that *biyur* specifically means *sereifah*. Indeed, even according to opinions that eating one's *chametz* beforehand is a valid form of elimination, the prevailing practice is still to preserve a small amount specifically to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *sereifah*. Rabbi Akiva's argument thus implicitly establishes *sereifah* as the required form of *biyur*.

### Sreifah Melacha

The second lesson: *havara l'chalek yatza* — the *melacha* of burning is a distinct and independent *melacha*. This connects to a well-known debate regarding the verse *lo tivaru eish b'chol moshvoteichem b'yom hashabat* — you shall not kindle a fire in any of your dwellings on *Shabbat*. The question raised by this verse is: given that *melacha* is already prohibited on *Shabbat* by the verse *lo ta'aseh kol melacha*, what does the prohibition on kindling fire add? The two positions are: *l'chalek yatza* — it emerges as a distinct *melacha*, meaning it is counted separately among the 39 *melachot*, such that one who performs it receives a distinct punishment independent of other *melachot*; or *lav yatza* — it does not emerge as a *melacha* but rather as a *lav*, a negative prohibition, meaning it is not subject to the same *issur melacha* framework that carries *kareth*. Since Rabbi Akiva grounds his argument in the impossibility of performing *sereifah* on *Yom Tov* due to its being a *melacha*, he must hold *l'chalek yatza* — that *havara* is indeed counted as a full and distinct *melacha*.

### Not Permitted on Yom Tov

The third lesson: *lo amrinan ho'il v'hutchra havara l'tzorech hutchra nami shelo l'tzorech* — one does not apply the principle that since burning is permitted on *Yom Tov* for *ochel nefesh* purposes it is thereby permitted for other purposes as well. On *Yom Tov*, the general permission to cook — to use fire *l'tzorech* — is sometimes extended to other uses of fire that are not strictly for food preparation, on the grounds that once a category of *melacha* is permitted, it is permitted broadly. Rabbi Akiva's position demonstrates that this principle does not apply to burning *chametz*. Eliminating *chametz* provides no tangible personal benefit; it is purely destructive in nature. Since one does not stand to gain from burning the *chametz* in the manner one gains from cooking food, the leniency of *ochel nefesh* does not carry over to this act. Therefore, even if one has a fire burning on *Yom Tov* for legitimate purposes, one may not use it to burn *chametz* on *Yom Tov* — this must be done beforehand.

## Seven Days Chametz Not F…

### Yimatzei vs. Yeira'eh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: "שִׁבְעַת יָמִים שְׂאֹר לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּבָתֵּיכֶם" מַה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? וַהֲלֹא כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: "לֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ שְׂאֹר [וְלֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ חָמֵץ] בְּכׇל גְּבֻלֶךָ"!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **"Seven days leaven shall not be found in your houses"** (Exodus 12:19). To **what** purpose does **the verse state** this prohibition? **Wasn't it already stated:** "And **no leaven shall be seen with you, neither shall there be leavened bread seen with you, in all your borders"** (Exodus 13:7)?

The *gemara* returns to the *pesukim* in Parshat Bo. The *baraita* opens by citing two distinct verses: *shiv'at yamim se'or lo yimatzei bevateichem* — seven days *se'or* shall not be found in your homes — and *lo yeira'eh lecha chametz velo yeira'eh lecha se'or bechol gevulecha* — *chametz* and *se'or* shall not be seen anywhere within your borders. Why do we need both verses? Each teaches something the other does not.

It is worth noting that these verses come from different contexts within the Torah. The first is the instruction given by Hashem to Moshe; the second is Moshe's transmission of those instructions to the people, to which Moshe added additional elements — *tefillin*, the commemoration of the exodus, and other matters. This is why Moshe's account does not simply restate the divine command verbatim. Nevertheless, the *gemara* treats both verses as independently operative sources and asks what each contributes.

### Your Own vs. Others'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ שְׂאֹר" — שֶׁלְּךָ אִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה רוֹאֶה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים וְשֶׁל גָּבוֹהַּ. יָכוֹל יַטְמִין וִיקַבֵּל פִּקְדוֹנוֹת מִן הַגּוֹי — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: **Because it is stated: "And no leaven shall be seen with you,"** which teaches that **your** own leaven **you** may **not see, but you may see** leaven that belongs **to others,** i.e., gentiles, **and** leaven consecrated **to God.** In light of this *halakha*, I **might** have thought that **one** may **conceal** leaven in one's home or **accept deposits** of leaven **from a gentile.** Therefore, **the verse states: "Shall not be found,"** meaning that one may not retain any type of leaven in one's house at all.

The *gemara*'s operative principle emerges from the verse *lo yeira'eh lecha se'or*. The word *lecha* — "to you" — is read as *shelcha*, "yours." The verse therefore teaches: *shelcha ee atta ro'eh aval atta ro'eh shel acheirim veshel gavo'ah* — your own *se'or* cannot be seen, but you may look upon *se'or* belonging to others, whether to other Jews or to *hekdesh*. The prohibition of *bal yeira'eh* applies only to one's own *chametz*.

This distinction between one's own *chametz* and others' *chametz* is fundamental. One is prohibited from possessing or seeing *chametz* that belongs to oneself; the property of others is not one's concern. This is why the verse says *lecha* — *shelcha* specifically cannot be seen.

### Hiding & Deposits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּגוֹי שֶׁלֹּא כִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְאֵין שָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר. גּוֹי שֶׁכִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְשָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּבָתֵּיכֶם".
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* continues: Had only this verse been stated, **I** would **have** derived **nothing other** than this *halakha* **with regard to a gentile whom you did not overcome and who does not dwell with you in the courtyard.** With regard to **a gentile whom you overcame and who dwells with you in the courtyard, from where** do we know that he is also included in this prohibition? **The verse states: "It shall not be found in your houses"** at all, i.e., anywhere in one's possession.

One might think that if visibility is the standard, one could satisfy the prohibition simply by hiding the *chametz* — burying it in a pit or concealing it in some other fashion. Furthermore, one might think one could accept a *pikadon* — a deposit — of *chametz* from a non-Jew and keep it over *Pesach* without violation, since it does not belong to oneself. The *gemara* addresses both scenarios by invoking the other verse: *lo yimatzei* — it shall not be found. This prohibition extends beyond visibility to actual possession. Even if *chametz* is hidden and cannot be seen, *lo yimatzei* prohibits its very presence. And even if *chametz* deposited by a non-Jew is not technically one's own, *lo yimatzei* in your homes prohibits maintaining such possession.

### Houses vs. Borders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא שֶׁבְּבָתֵּיכֶם. בְּבוֹרוֹת בְּשִׁיחִין וּבִמְעָרוֹת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בְּכׇל גְּבֻלֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states that from the verse: "It shall not be found in your houses," **I have** derived **nothing other** than the fact that this prohibition applies to leaven **that is** actually **in your houses. From where** is it derived that this *halakha* applies even to leaven **in pits, ditches, and caves? The verse states: In all your borders,** i.e., anywhere that belongs to you.

The two verses serve complementary and non-redundant functions. The verse *lo yimatzei bevateichem* — in your houses — covers *se'or* that is in the domestic sphere and adds the prohibition of possession even when hidden, and the prohibition of accepting deposits from non-Jews. The verse *lo yeira'eh lecha se'or bechol gevulecha* — in all your borders — extends the prohibition beyond the house to all of one's property: pits, cisterns, caves, and fields. Neither verse alone would cover both categories. *Lo yimatzei bevateichem* would not cover property outside the home; *lo yeira'eh bechol gevulecha* would not prohibit hidden *chametz* or deposits, since one might think that what is not visible is not subject to this verse. Both are therefore necessary.

## Gezeirah Shava Se'or

### Se'or Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַעֲדַיִין אֲנִי אוֹמֵר: בַּבָּתִּים עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם בַּל יֵרָאֶה וּבַל יִמָּצֵא וּבַל יַטְמִין וּבַל יְקַבֵּל פִּקְדוֹנוֹת מִן הַגּוֹי. בַּגְּבוּלִין — שֶׁלְּךָ אִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה רוֹאֶה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים וְשֶׁל גָּבוֹהַּ. מִנַּיִין לִיתֵּן אֶת הָאָמוּר שֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה וְשֶׁל זֶה בָּזֶה?
>
> **Translation:** **And still I** can **say:** If there is leaven **in your houses, one violates** the prohibition that leaven **shall not be seen and** the prohibition that **it shall not be found, as well as** the prohibitions of **you shall not conceal and you shall not receive deposits from a gentile.** Meanwhile, **in your borders,** outside your home, **your** own leaven **you may not see, but you may see** leaven that belongs **to others,** i.e., gentiles, **and** leaven consecrated **to God. From where** is it derived that it is proper **to apply** the prohibition **that was said about this** place **to that** place, **and** the prohibition **that was said about that** place **to this** place?

The *gemara* then establishes a *gezeirah shava* between the two verses. Both employ the word *se'or*: *shiv'at yamim se'or lo yimatzei bevateichem* uses *se'or* in the context of houses, and *lo yeira'eh lecha se'or bechol gevulecha* uses *se'or* in the context of borders. The verbal parallel creates the basis for drawing equivalence between the two domains.

### House Rules to Borders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "שְׂאוֹר" "שְׂאוֹר" לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה. נֶאֱמַר שְׂאוֹר בַּבָּתִּים: "שְׂאֹר לֹא יִמָּצֵא בְּבָתֵּיכֶם", וְנֶאֱמַר שְׂאֹר בַּגְּבוּלִין: "לֹא יֵרָאֶה לְךָ שְׂאֹר". מָה שְׂאוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּבָּתִּים — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם בַּל יֵרָאֶה וּבַל יִמָּצֵא וּבַל יַטְמִין וּבַל יְקַבֵּל פִּקְדוֹנוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם, אַף שְׂאוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּגְּבוּלִין — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם בַּל יֵרָאֶה וּבַל יִמָּצֵא וּבַל יַטְמִין וּבַל יְקַבֵּל פִּקְדוֹנוֹת מִן הַגּוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The *tanna* answers that **the verse states** the term **leaven** with regard to houses and the term **leaven** with regard to borders **as a verbal analogy. It states leaven with regard to houses:** "Seven days **leaven shall not be found in your houses," and it states leaven with regard to borders: "Neither shall there be leaven seen with you." Just as** for the **leaven stated with regard to houses one transgresses** the prohibition that leaven **shall not be seen and** the prohibition that **it shall not be found, and** the prohibitions of **one shall not conceal and one shall not receive deposits from a gentile,** so **too,** for the **leaven stated with regard to borders, one transgresses** the prohibition that leaven **shall not be seen and** the prohibition that **it shall not be found, and** the prohibitions of **one shall not conceal and one shall not receive deposits from a gentile.**

From the *gezeirah shava*, what is said of *se'or* in houses applies to *se'or* in borders. Specifically: just as one transgresses in one's house regarding *bal yeira'eh*, *bal yimatzei*, hidden *chametz* (*tamun*), and deposits from non-Jews (*pikadonot*), so too one transgresses on all these grounds with respect to *chametz* or *se'or* anywhere within one's broader property.

### Border Rules to Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה שְׂאוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּגְּבוּלִין — שֶׁלְּךָ אִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה רוֹאֶה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים וְשֶׁל גָּבוֹהַּ, אַף שְׂאוֹר הָאָמוּר בַּבָּתִּים — שֶׁלְּךָ אִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה רוֹאֶה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים וְשֶׁל גָּבוֹהַּ.
>
> **Translation:** The converse is also true: **Just as** concerning the **leaven stated with regard to borders, your** own leaven **you may not see, but you may see** leaven that belongs **to others,** i.e., gentiles, **and** leaven consecrated **to God,** so **too,** concerning the **leaven stated with regard to houses: Your** own leaven **you may not see, but you may see** leaven that belongs **to others and** leaven consecrated **to God.**

Conversely, what is derived from the borders verse applies to houses as well. The ruling that *shelcha ee atta ro'eh aval atta ro'eh shel acheirim veshel gavo'ah* — one's own *chametz* is prohibited but others' is permitted — is derived from the borders verse. Via the *gezeirah shava*, this principle extends to houses as well. Thus, one does not violate any prohibition by having the *chametz* of others in one's home, provided it genuinely belongs to someone else and not to oneself.

## Non-Jewish Chametz

### Uncontrolled Non-Jews

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּגוֹי שֶׁלֹּא כִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְאֵין שָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר. גּוֹי שֶׁכִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְשָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara addresses several difficult aspects of this *baraita*. **The Master said: I** would **have** derived **nothing other** than this *halakha* **with regard to a gentile whom you did not overcome and who does not dwell with you in the courtyard.** With regard to **a gentile whom you overcame and who dwells with you in the courtyard, from where** is it derived that he is also included in this prohibition? **The verse states: "It shall not be found** in your houses."

The *gemara* turns to the question of chametz belonging to a non-Jew in one's possession. The *baraita* initially frames the permission to have another's *chametz* as applying to a non-Jew who is *shelo k'vashatuhu ve'eino sharui imcha b'chatzer* — not under one's control and not dwelling in one's courtyard. Such a non-Jew is entirely independent of the Jew's household, and his *chametz* is clearly his own.

### Controlled Non-Jews Prob…

But what about a non-Jew who *is* under one's control (*kavush*) or who does live in one's courtyard (*sharui imcha b'chatzer*)? How can a Jew have that individual's *chametz* in the home? One might think that the closer a non-Jew's connection to the Jew's household, the more his property would be considered the Jew's own for purposes of the prohibition. It would seem, on this reasoning, to be a greater *chiddush* to permit *chametz* from a non-Jew who has no connection to the household than to permit it from one who is entirely integrated into it.

### Abaye's Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵיפוֹךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the logic of this proof. **On the contrary;** the prohibition regarding the leaven of a gentile who is subservient to and lives with a Jew is more obvious than the prohibition regarding the leaven of a gentile who is neither. The *tanna* should have started with the leaven belonging to a gentile who is subservient to a Jew. **Abaye said: Reverse** the order of the statement: I might have thought that only leaven owned by a gentile whom you overcame and who dwells with you in the courtyard is prohibited; but leaven owned by a gentile whom you did not overcome and who does not dwell with you in the courtyard is permitted.

Abaye suggests *ipecha* — reversing the formulation. In his view, the greater *chiddush* is the permission regarding the non-Jew who is *not* under one's control and does not live with one. This should be the case requiring explicit license.

### Rava Maintains Reisha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, וְאַרֵישָׁא קָאֵי: שֶׁלְּךָ אִי אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, אֲבָל אַתָּה רוֹאֶה שֶׁל אֲחֵרִים וְשֶׁל גָּבוֹהַּ. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּגוֹי שֶׁלֹּא כִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְאֵין שָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר. גּוֹי שֶׁכִּיבַּשְׁתּוֹ, וְשָׁרוּי עִמְּךָ בֶּחָצֵר, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״לֹא יִמָּצֵא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Actually, do not reverse** the order, as this statement is not in fact a continuation of the previous one **and** instead **it applies to the first clause** of the *baraita*, which deals with the time when leaven is permitted. The entire statement should read as follows: **Your** own leaven **you** may **not see, but you may see** leaven that belongs **to others,** i.e., gentiles, **and** leaven consecrated **to God,** as one is not commanded to remove leaven that belongs to a gentile. **I have** derived **nothing other** than this *halakha* **with regard to a gentile whom you did not overcome and who does not dwell with you in the courtyard,** as the leaven belonging to that gentile is in no way tied to the Jew. With regard to **a gentile whom you overcame and who dwells with you in the courtyard, from where** is it derived that he is also included in this leniency? **The verse states: "It shall not be found."**

Rava disagrees: *lo teipecha*, do not reverse it, and *reisha ka'ei* — the *reisha* of the *baraita* stands as stated. The initial permission regarding the uncontrolled, non-resident non-Jew is the *chiddush* that requires derivation, and the ruling regarding the controlled or resident non-Jew follows from an additional source.

### Shelcha Twice = Ribui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי תַּנָּא מְיהַדַּר אַהֶיתֵּירָא, וְנָסֵיב לֵהּ קְרָא לְאִיסּוּרָא?! מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״לְךָ״ ״לְךָ״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **This *tanna* seeks permission** for seeing the leaven of a gentile, and yet **he cites a verse to** establish **a prohibition.** The Gemara answers that the *tanna* did not cite proof from the phrase: It shall not be found. **Due to** the fact **that it is stated:** "No leaven shall be seen **with you** in all your borders" (Exodus 13:7) and "No leaven shall be seen **with you** in all your borders" (Deuteronomy 16:4) **twice,** one of them is superfluous and may be appended to: It shall not be found, creating the prohibition: It shall not be found with you. Only leaven belonging to a Jew is prohibited.

The textual basis for the permission regarding even the non-Jew who is under one's control is derived from a *ribui*. The word *shelcha* — "yours" — appears twice in the relevant verses: once in Shemot and once in the repetition in Parshat Re'eh (*velo yeira'eh lecha se'or bechol gevulecha shivat yamim*). The doubling of *shelcha* functions as a *ribui*, an inclusion. The *gemara* derives that the repeated *shelcha* comes to expand the scope of the permission: it includes *chametz* belonging even to a non-Jew who is under one's control and living in one's courtyard. The greater *chiddush*, in Rava's reading, is precisely this: even in that situation of apparent integration, the non-Jew's *chametz* remains his own and does not become the Jew's for purposes of *bal yeira'eh* and *bal yimatzei*.
