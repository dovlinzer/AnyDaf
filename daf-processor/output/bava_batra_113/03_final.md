# Bava Batra 113 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Braisas: Husband/Son

### Problem Setup

The Gemara on this daf continues a discussion that has occupied the preceding pages: what is the textual source for the rule that a husband inherits from his wife? The classical *drasha* is the verse *uvenasatem et nachalaso lish'eiro hakarov eilav* — "you shall give his inheritance to his flesh, the one close to him" — with *she'eiro* being read as a reference to his wife. The grammatical difficulty, however, is glaring: the plain meaning of the verse would suggest that the property goes *to* the wife, not *from* her to her husband. This drove the Gemara to seek other textual anchors, and the discussion focused in particular on the verses in Parashat Masei concerning the daughters of Tzelafchad.

The context is this: the *bnos Tzelafchad* were instructed not to marry men from a different tribe, lest the inheritance be transferred from the mother's tribe to another. The Gemara asked whether those verses — *velo sishov nachala* ("do not let the inheritance pass away") — are evidence that a husband inherits from his wife, or whether they might all be referring exclusively to the son inheriting from his mother. After all, when a woman dies, her property does pass to her son, and her son belongs to his father's tribe, not hers. So perhaps the prohibition against inter-tribal marriage is entirely about preventing the inheritance from passing through a son to a different tribe, and nothing in these verses establishes that the husband inherits at all.

### Two Braisas Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וְלֹא תִסֹּב נַחֲלָה מִמַּטֶּה לְמַטֶּה אַחֵר״ – בְּסִיבַּת הַבַּעַל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּסִיבַּת הַבַּעַל; אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא בְּסִיבַּת הַבֵּן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְלֹא תִסֹּב נַחֲלָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמַּטֶּה אֶל מַטֶּה״ – הֲרֵי הֲסִיבַּת הַבֵּן אָמוּר; הָא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים ״וְלֹא תִסֹּב נַחֲלָה מִמַּטֶּה לְמַטֶּה אַחֵר״? בְּסִיבַּת הַבַּעַל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **"So shall no inheritance transfer from one tribe to another tribe"** (Numbers 36:9); **the verse speaks of** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the husband.** The Torah prohibits the woman from marrying a man from a different tribe since her husband will inherit from her, thereby transferring her inheritance away from its original tribe.
>
> **Do you say** that this is **with regard to** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the husband, or is it only with regard to** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the son? When it says: "So shall no inheritance of the children of Israel transfer from tribe to tribe"** (Numbers 36:7), the verse is **speaking with regard to** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the son. How do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"So shall no inheritance transfer from one tribe to another tribe"** (Numbers 36:9)? That **verse speaks of** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the husband.**

The Gemara now presents two *braisos* that appear to address precisely this question. The two *psukim* in question are nearly identical. The first reads: *velo sishov nachala livnei Yisrael mimatteh el matteh* — do not let the inheritance pass from one tribe to another. The second reads: *velo sishov nachala mimatteh el matteh achair* — do not let the inheritance pass from one tribe to a different, or "other," tribe. Each *braisa* takes up these two verses and asks whether a given instance of inheritance passing refers to the son or the husband.

### Rashbam Reading

According to the *Rashbam*, both *braisos* are substantively identical — they present the same argument and reach the same conclusion, linking one verse to the son and the other to the husband. The only difference between them is which verse each *braisa* leads with. The first *braisa* opens with the verse about the son: *tanya besibas haben* — it teaches with respect to the turning that happens through the son. It then asks: perhaps this verse is about the husband? It resolves the question by pointing to the second verse — the one containing the word *achair* — as the verse that speaks about the husband. Once we have a verse for the husband, the first verse must be about the son. The second *braisa* simply reverses the order of presentation: it opens with the *achair* verse and identifies that as referring to the husband, then resolves that the other verse must be about the son.

In the *Rashbam*'s reading, therefore, the Gemara is not recording a dispute between *braisos*. They are structurally parallel, each confirming the same result through the same pair of verses, simply beginning from different ends of the analysis.

### Tosafos Reading

*Tosafos* finds this unsatisfying. If both *braisos* say the same thing and reach the same conclusion, presented in different order alone, there would be no reason for the Gemara to distinguish them as two separate *braisos*. *Tosafos* therefore maintains that the two *braisos* reflect a genuine textual disagreement and proposes a different *girsa*.

According to *Tosafos*, the *achair* verse is agreed upon by all — everyone accepts that this verse speaks about the *baal*, the husband, as we will see below. The dispute between the *braisos* concerns the other verse, *velo sishov nachala livnei Yisrael mimatteh el matteh*. One *braisa* maintains that this second verse speaks about the son — the words *livnei Yisrael* connecting to inheritance passing through children. The other *braisa* in *Tosafos*'s *girsa* reads differently: it says *benachalas habaal hakasuv omer* — the verse is speaking about the inheritance of the husband. According to that *braisa*, both verses refer to the husband, and the *achair* verse then serves to confirm this reading rather than to distinguish the two. So in *Tosafos*'s account, one *braisa* holds that one verse is about the husband and the other about the son, while the second *braisa* holds that both are about the husband.

This reading has the advantage of explaining why the Gemara proceeds the way it does. The Gemara's subsequent analysis focuses entirely on why everyone agrees that the *achair* verse refers to the husband. From the *Rashbam*'s perspective, the Gemara could just as well have asked the symmetrical question about the son — what signals that the other verse speaks about the son? *Tosafos*'s reading renders the focus on the *achair* verse natural: it is the one verse about which there is no disagreement between the *braisos*, and so the Gemara asks what marks it as specifically referring to the husband.

## Markers: Ish/Yidbeku

### Consensus: Acher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהַת, ״מִמַּטֶּה לְמַטֶּה אַחֵר״ – בְּסִיבַּת הַבַּעַל הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר; מַאי מַשְׁמַע? סִימָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אִישׁ״. תַּרְוַיְיהוּ ״אִישׁ״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **In any event,** according to **everyone,** i.e., according to both *baraitot*, the phrase in **the verse "from one tribe to another tribe" speaks of** the **transfer** of the inheritance **by means of the husband.** The Gemara asks: **From where** is this **inferred?** The Gemara supplies **a mnemonic. Rabba bar Rav Sheila said** that the latter part of **the verse states:** "So shall no inheritance transfer from one tribe to another tribe; for the tribes of the children of Israel shall cleave **each one [*ish*]** to its own inheritance," alluding to the transfer by means of the husband, as the word "*ish*" means husband, in the context of: "Elimelech the husband [*ish*] of Naomi" (Ruth 1:3). The Gemara asks: But the word **"*ish*" is written in both of** the verses. Therefore, both verses should be interpreted with regard to the transfer of the inheritance by means of the husband.

The Gemara states plainly: *d'kulei alma miha* — in any event, everyone agrees that the verse *mimatteh lemateh achair* speaks about the passing of inheritance through the husband, *besibas habaal hakasuv medaber*. Having established this consensus, the Gemara then presses the question: *mai mashma*? What in the text of that verse signals that it concerns the husband rather than the son?

### Ish Signal Rejected

The Gemara's first answer is offered in the name of Rabba bar Rav Shila: *amar kra ish* — the verse uses the word *ish*. The full verse reads *ki ish benachalato yidbeku matos Bnei Yisrael*, and the word *ish* is taken as a signal that we are speaking about a husband, on the model of *ish Naomi* — where *ish* denotes a husband. The *Rashbam* cites this usage. Although it is not the plain meaning of *ish* in this context, the term carries a connotation of marital relationship.

The Gemara immediately objects: *tarveihu ish kesiv behu* — the other verse also uses the word *ish*. Looking at the parallel verse, *ki ish benachalas mateh avosav yidbeku Bnei Yisrael*, it too contains *ish*. If the word *ish* marked the *achair* verse as referring to the husband, it would equally mark the other verse the same way. The signal therefore cannot be *ish* alone.

### Veyidbeku Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אָמַר קְרָא: ״יִדְבְּקוּ״. תַּרְוַיְיהוּ ״יִדְבְּקוּ״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** a different explanation. The **verse states: "Shall cleave,"** and this term alludes to marriage, as in the context of: "And he shall cleave to his wife" (Genesis 2:24). The Gemara raises a difficulty: But the term **"shall cleave" is written in both of** the verses. Therefore, both verses should be interpreted with regard to the transfer of the inheritance by means of the husband.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers an alternative: *amar kra yidbeku* — the word *yidbeku* (they shall cleave) signals the husband, evoking the verse *veyidbak be'ishto*, the cleaving of a husband to his wife. This is the marital union described in *Bereishis*; the use of the same root *dalet-beit-kuf* in the inheritance verse links it to the husband.

The Gemara once again objects: *tarvaiyu veyidbeku ketivu* — both verses use *yidbeku*. The root appears in the parallel verse as well, so it cannot serve as the distinguishing marker.

### Acher Signals Other

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״יִדְבְּקוּ מַטּוֹת״. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִמַּטֶּה לְמַטֶּה אַחֵר״ – וּבֵן לָאו אַחֵר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** a different explanation: The **verse states: "The tribes** of the children of Israel **shall cleave,"** and tribes cleave to one another through marriage. **Rav Ashi said** another explanation: The **verse states: "From one tribe to another tribe," and a son is not** considered **"another,"** as he is an extension of his mother.

Rava then offers the resolution: *amar kra veyidbeku matot* — the verse uses the plural *matot* (tribes), not the singular *mateh*. When two tribes are bound together through *deveika*, it signals that the person to whom the inheritance passes belongs to a different tribe entirely — and that is the husband, who cleaves to his wife across tribal lines. The singular *mateh*, by contrast, suggests a connection within one tribal line, pointing toward a son who belongs to the mother's tribe through his father.

The Gemara finally arrives at the resolution of Rav Ashi: *amar kra mimmateh lemateh achair* — the distinguishing word is *achair*, "other." This word appears in the *achair* verse and not in the parallel verse. It signals that the inheritance is passing to someone who is, in some sense, an *other*. A child is biologically connected to the family in a way that cannot be severed — even after death, the child remains the grandchild, the nephew, the heir in the biological chain. A husband, however, is in a different category: the relationship was created by marriage, and marriage can end through death or divorce. However deep the *deveika* of husband and wife, the husband remains, in some structural sense, an *achair* — an other — in a way the son does not. This, says Rav Ashi, is why that verse refers to the husband.

### Matot vs. Mateh

An important observation: *Tosafos* raises a striking point about the earlier attempts involving *ish* and *yidbeku*. The Gemara rejected each because the same word appears in both verses. But surely the *amoraim* who proposed these signals knew that both verses contained the same word? *Tosafos* suggests, almost startlingly, that *pe'amim shelo hayu beki'in bipesukim* — sometimes the Sages were not fully expert in the exact wording of particular verses. He cites a parallel case where one *amora* was asked why the word *tov* does not appear in the second *Dibrot*, and responded that he did not even know whether it appeared in the first *Dibrot* or not.

*Tosafos* himself, however, is uncomfortable with this explanation in the present context. The continuation of the Gemara — in which the objection *tarveihu ish kesiv behu* is raised precisely because both verses contain *ish* — suggests that the Sages knew the texts perfectly well. The *Rashbam* offers a more refined reading: the *amoraim* were not claiming that the word *ish* or *yidbeku* was absent from the other verse. Rather, they were pointing to these words as sufficient grounds to identify the *achair* verse as referring to the husband. The Gemara's response was not "you are wrong about what this word means" but rather "given that the same word appears in both verses, you have not explained why you begin with this verse and allow the other to refer to something else." The final answer — the word *achair* — works because it appears in one verse only, providing an asymmetric signal that anchors one verse to the husband and leaves the other for the son.

## Husband: Ra'uy Exclusion

### Ra'uy vs. Muchzak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר רַבִּי; וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: מִנַּיִן לְבַעַל שֶׁאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **Rabbi Yannai says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says, and** some **determined it** was **in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa: From where** is it derived **that a husband** who inherits from his wife does not **take in** inheritance the property **due** to the deceased **as** he does the property she **possessed?** Instead of the husband inheriting the property that was due to her, that property is inherited by her other relatives, such as her son, or other relatives of her father.

The Gemara introduces a tradition in the name of Rabbi Yannai, citing Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *minayin l'baal she'eino notel b'rauy k'vemuchzak* — from where do we know that a husband does not inherit *rauy* in the same way he inherits *muchzak*? This is a significant and somewhat counterintuitive *halacha* that requires careful unpacking.

*Muchzak* refers to property that a woman already possesses at the moment of her death. *Rauy* refers to property that she was in line to receive — property to which she had an expectation of entitlement — but which had not yet come into her possession when she died. The rule being articulated is that while the husband inherits the former, he does not inherit the latter. This parallels the rule concerning a *bechor*: a firstborn receives a double portion of the estate only from property the father actually possessed, not from *rauy* — property the father was entitled to but had not yet received.

### Grandson/Husband Logic

Consider a concrete case. A woman's father is still alive and owns property. The woman has both a husband and a son. If the sequence of deaths is: father dies first, then woman, then her property passes to her husband in the normal course — her husband inherits her *muchzak*, the property she actually possessed when she died, before it goes to her son. But suppose the sequence is reversed: the woman dies first, then her father dies afterward. At the moment of her death, the father's property had not yet come to her — it was *rauy*, expected but not possessed. When the father subsequently dies, where does his property go?

The ruling is that in this case the property bypasses the husband entirely and descends to the woman's son — or, if the son is also deceased, to any surviving descendant in her line. The husband does not receive it. The logic, as *Tosafos* will explain, is rooted in the nature of the marital bond: once the woman has died, the connection between the husband and her family of origin has been severed. The grandfather has no relationship with his son-in-law. His biological heirs are his grandchildren. The property therefore flows downward through the biological line, not sideways to the former husband.

This reasoning clarifies why *muchzak* and *rauy* are treated differently. When a woman dies with property she already possesses, the inheritance takes effect at the moment of death — and at that moment she is still, legally and halakhically, his wife. The property transfers to him. But property held by her father at the moment of her death cannot transfer to the husband, because after her death there is no remaining legal or familial nexus between the husband and the grandfather's estate.

## Ya'ir & Pinchas Cases

### Ya'ir Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׂגוּב הוֹלִיד אֶת יָאִיר, וַיְהִי לוֹ עֶשְׂרִים וְשָׁלוֹשׁ עָרִים בְּאֶרֶץ הַגִּלְעָד״. מִנַּיִן לְיָאִיר – שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ לִשְׂגוּב? אֶלָּא מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּשָׂא שְׂגוּב אִשָּׁה, וּמֵתָה בְּחַיֵּי מוֹרִישֶׁיהָ; וּמֵתוּ מוֹרִישֶׁיהָ, וִירָשָׁהּ יָאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated: "And Seguv begot Yair, who had twenty three cities in the land of Gilead"** (I Chronicles 2:22). The Gemara asks: **From where** did **Yair have** land **that** his father, **Seguv, did not have? Rather,** this **teaches that Seguv married a woman and she died in the lifetime of her** potential **legators, and her legators** then **died, and Yair,** her son, not Seguv, her husband, **inherited** these inheritances from **her.**

The Gemara brings two prooftexts from Chronicles and the book of Yehoshua to establish that a son does indeed inherit *rauy* — property that was expected to come to his mother but had not yet arrived before she died. The first involves the genealogy of *Chevron* in Divrei HaYamim: *veSeguv holid et Ya'ir vayehi lo esrim veshalosh arim b'eretz haGil'ad* — Seguv fathered Ya'ir, and he had twenty-three cities in the land of Gilad. The puzzle is that Ya'ir had cities that his father Seguv did not have. Earlier in the Gemara's discussion, this was explained by saying that Ya'ir married a woman who brought property with her, and she died, leaving Ya'ir with cities that Seguv never possessed.

But now the Gemara offers a different reading of the same case. The explanation proposed here is that *shenasa Seguv ishah umetah bechayei morisheha* — Seguv married a woman who died while her own father was still alive. She was in line to inherit from her father — the property was *rauy* to her — but she died before that inheritance materialized. Subsequently, her father died, and the property descended not to Seguv the husband, but directly to Ya'ir the son. This is why Ya'ir had cities that Seguv never had: the property bypassed Seguv entirely, going to the grandson as the closest biological heir.

### Pinchas Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאוֹמֵר: ״וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַהֲרֹן מֵת וַיִּקְבְּרוּ וְגוֹ׳״. מִנַּיִן לְפִנְחָס – שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה לוֹ לְאֶלְעָזָר? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנָּשָׂא אֶלְעָזָר אִשָּׁה, וּמֵתָה בְּחַיֵּי מוֹרִישֶׁיהָ; וּמֵתוּ מוֹרִישֶׁיהָ, וִירָשָׁהּ פִּנְחָס.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is stated: "And Elazar, the son of Aaron, died; and they buried** him in the Hill of Pinehas his son" (Joshua 24:33). **From where** did **Pinehas have** land **that** his father, **Elazar, did not have?** Rather, this **teaches that Elazar married a woman and she died in the lifetime of her** potential **legators, and her legators** then **died, and Pinehas** her son, not Elazar her husband, **inherited** the property from **her.**

A parallel case is derived from the book of Yehoshua: *veElazar ben Aharon met vayikberu oto* — Elazar son of Aharon died and they buried him, and a detail in the text implies that Pinchas possessed property that Elazar did not. The same structure applies: *shenasa Elazar ishah* — Elazar married a woman, Pinchas's mother — *umetah bechayei morisheha* — she died while her own father was still alive — *umetu morisheha veyirshah Pinchas* — and when her father subsequently died, that inheritance descended directly to Pinchas rather than to Elazar. Again the husband was bypassed because she died before her father, and once she was dead the connection between Elazar and her father's estate was severed.

### Two Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא, יָאִיר – דַּהֲוָה נְסִיב אִיתְּתָא וּמֵתָה, וְיַרְתַהּ; תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: וְאֶלְעָזָר בֶּן אַהֲרֹן מֵת״. וְכִי תֵּימָא דִּנְפַלָה לֵיהּ בִּשְׂדֵה חֲרָמִים, אָמַר קְרָא: ״בְּנוֹ״ – נַחֲלָה הָרְאוּיָה לוֹ, וִירָשָׁהּ בְּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** is the meaning of: **And it is stated?** Why is it necessary to provide an additional proof beyond the first verse? The Gemara explains. **And if you would say:** In the verse concerning Seguv and Yair, it is **Yair,** not Seguv, **who married a woman and she died and he inherited** from **her,** and he did not inherit from his mother, **the verse states: "And Elazar, the son of Aaron, died;** and they buried him in the Hill of Pinehas his son" (Joshua 24:33), teaching that Pinehas inherited the land of those from whom his mother inherited, and Elazar did not. **And if you would say that** this land **came into** the possession of Pinehas **as a dedicated field,** as he was a priest, and he did not inherit it from his mother, **the verse states: "His son,"** indicating that it was **an inheritance that was fitting for him,** i.e., Elazar, had his wife not predeceased her legators, **and his son inherited it.**

The Gemara asks why both cases are necessary — could we not have learned the rule from Ya'ir alone? It answers that without the Pinchas case, one might have thought the peculiar distribution in Ya'ir's case was because his cities came through his wife's own direct property, not through a grandfather's estate descending after her death. The Pinchas case rules out that alternative reading. Similarly, one might have thought the Pinchas case involved property consecrated to *hekdesh* and released under unusual circumstances — *sedeh charamim* — rather than through the ordinary *rauy* mechanism. The verse in the Pinchas case uses the word *bno* — "his son" — and the Gemara reads this as emphatic: it is the property that Elazar might have been entitled to, yet it went to *bno*, his son, and not to him. Together the two cases close off alternative explanations and establish firmly that *rauy* passes to the biological heir, not to the husband.

## Husband: Ra'uy Exclusion Theological Basis

### Tosafos: Connection Severed

*Tosafos* addresses a question that arises naturally from this whole discussion: if a son inherits *rauy* from his mother — property she was in line to receive but did not yet possess — and the husband does not, what is the theoretical basis for this asymmetry? The answer *Tosafos* articulates is that once the wife has died, the connection between the husband and her family is severed. There is no biological relationship between him and her father. His entitlement was derived entirely from the marital bond, and that bond no longer exists. By contrast, the son remains the grandfather's biological grandson regardless of the mother's death. The biological chain is not broken by death; only the marital one is.

### Basar Echad vs. Korvah

This leads *Tosafos* to articulate an important conceptual distinction. There are two different categories relevant to the husband's inheritance: *she'erut* — the marital connection described as *basar echad*, one flesh — and *korvah* — biological relation. The husband inherits from his wife on the basis of *basar echad*, not *korvah*. *Tosafos* frames this as follows: *keivan she-habaal eino yoresh machamat kurva ela machamat she'erut* — since the husband inherits not by virtue of biological relation but by virtue of marital union.

The practical implication of this distinction is significant. When asking whether the husband takes priority over the son in inheriting from the wife, the answer is yes — but for a reason that illuminates the *rauy* rule as well. Consider: if there were no husband and no son, the woman's property would pass to her father, or her brother, or some other biological relative. We see clearly that the marital bond takes precedence over these biological relationships for purposes of inheritance. Why? Because the *basar echad* relationship is treated as primary — the two are considered as one, and this oneness takes priority before we begin to look at other relatives.

### Final Rule

But this same logic explains why *rauy* does not follow the same path. The *basar echad* relationship is operative at the moment of death — property the wife possesses at that moment flows to her husband before her son, because at the instant of her death they are still one. But property that comes into the estate *after* her death — property that was *rauy* but not yet *muchzak* — cannot flow to a husband who is no longer connected to that family at all. At that later moment there is no marriage, no *basar echad*, and therefore no basis for his claim. The property instead descends through the biological line to the son, who remains tied to the grandfather's estate regardless of when his mother died.

The rule that emerges is thus: a husband inherits from property his wife already possessed at the moment of her death; he does not inherit from property she was in line to receive but had not yet come into her hands. That latter category — *rauy* — goes to the biological heir, the son, through the continuing biological nexus that is not severed by her death.

## Son Before Daughter

### Bnei Achot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְנֵי אָחוֹת. תָּנָא: בְּנֵי אָחוֹת, וְלֹא בְּנוֹת אָחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And sons of sisters,** i.e., nephews born to the sisters of the deceased, inherit from their maternal uncles but do not bequeath to them. **It is taught** in a *baraita*: This *halakha* applies to **sons of sisters but not** to **daughters of sisters.**

The Gemara now introduces a rule about what happens when inheritance passes through a collateral relative — specifically a sister. If a man dies without children and without a father, the estate passes to his siblings. If he has a living brother, the brother inherits. If his only sibling is a sister, and that sister has predeceased him, the estate passes to her children. The question addressed here is whether it passes to all of her children equally or whether sons take priority over daughters.

The rule taught is *bnei achot velo bnot achot* — the sons of sisters inherit, but not the daughters of sisters, meaning that when there are sons available in that generation, they take priority over the daughters. As Rav Sheshet explains, this is a matter of *likadem* — of establishing which among the eligible heirs comes first, not of categorically excluding the daughters. If there are no male descendants in that line, the daughters will ultimately inherit. And of course, if a son in that line has died but left children, his descendants take his place and come before any living daughter at that level. The principle is the familiar one: *ben kodem l'bat* — son before daughter — applied not only at the level of direct inheritance but at every tier of the inheritance hierarchy.

### Makish Yerusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: לְקַדֵּם.
>
> תָּנֵי רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר רַב יִצְחָק קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: ״וְיָרַשׁ״ – מַקִּישׁ יְרוּשָּׁה שְׁנִיָּה לִירוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה; מָה יְרוּשָּׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת, אַף יְרוּשָּׁה שְׁנִיָּה – בֵּן קוֹדֵם לַבַּת.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to what *halakha*** was this said? It is obvious that in principle daughters have the right to inherit from their maternal uncle, as the mother inherits from him. **Rav Sheshet said:** It is said **to** teach that where there are sons as well, they **precede** the daughters in inheriting from their maternal uncle.
>
> **Rav Shmuel bar Rav Yitzḥak taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Huna:** At the end of the passage discussing inheritance of land, the verse states: "Then you shall give his inheritance to his kinsman who is next to him of his family, **and he shall inherit** it" (Numbers 27:11). The verse **juxtaposes a secondary inheritance,** that of one inheriting from other relatives, **to a primary inheritance,** that of a child inheriting from a parent. This teaches that **just as** with regard to **a primary inheritance a son precedes a daughter, so too,** with regard to **a secondary inheritance a son precedes a daughter.**

The Gemara then provides the textual basis for this rule through a *midrash halachah*. The passage in *Parashat Pinchas* enumerates the order of heirs: if a man dies and has no son, the inheritance passes to his daughter; if no daughter, to his brothers; if no brothers, to his father's brothers; and if there are none, *lish'eiro hakarov eilav veyarash otah* — to his nearest relative, and he shall inherit it. The Gemara reads the concluding phrase *veyarash otah* as establishing a link — *makish yerusha shniya liyerusha rishona* — the secondary inheritance is *hukash* to, bound by the same rules as, the primary inheritance. Just as in the primary inheritance son comes before daughter (*mah yerusha rishona ben kodem l'bat*), so too in the secondary inheritance — at any subsequent level in the chart — son comes before daughter (*af yerusha shniya ben kodem l'bat*).

### Yerusha Shniya Scope

The *Rashbam* and *Tosafos* disagree about the scope of *yerusha shniya*. According to the *Rashbam*, *yerusha rishona* refers to the first level of inheritance — the man's own sons and daughters — and *yerusha shniya* refers to the subsequent levels enumerated in the verse: the man's siblings, uncles, and so forth. At each of these collateral levels, the rule of son before daughter applies just as it does at the direct level. The *drasha* links all of these tiers to the primary tier.

*Tosafos* reads *yerusha shniya* more expansively: it encompasses not merely the lateral tiers but also the generational descent through any heir. If a nephew inherits and that nephew has descendants, the rule of son before daughter applies within his line no matter how many generations down one goes. The *drasha* covers any level of inheritance, including cases far removed from those explicitly enumerated in the *psukim*. Either way, the principle is the same: wherever one is on the inheritance chart — whether inheriting directly, through a sibling, through an uncle, or through a nephew's descendants — sons precede daughters at the same generational level, and a descendant of a son, however many generations removed, will come before the living daughter of that son's sibling.

## Din: Daytime Rule

### V'haya B'yom Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: ״וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״ – בַּיוֹם אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת, וְאִי אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה. אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, דְּשָׁכֵיב בִּימָמָא הוּא דְּיָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ, מַאן דְּשָׁכֵיב בְּלֵילְיָא לָא יָרְתִי לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabba bar Ḥanina taught** a *baraita* **before Rav Naḥman:** A verse in the passage concerning the double portion inherited by a firstborn states: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit** that which he has" (Deuteronomy 21:16). The addition of the phrase "on the day" teaches that **it is** specifically **during the day** that **you may distribute inheritances, but you may not distribute inheritances at night. Abaye said to him:** That cannot be the *halakha*, as, **if that is so,** it ought to be **that** only **one who dies during the day is** the one from **whom his children inherit,** but with regard to **one who dies at night, his children do not inherit** from **him,** and this is not the case.

The Gemara shifts to a different aspect of inheritance law: the procedural status of inheritance adjudication. A *braisa* is cited in the name of Rabba bar Chinnana, brother of Rav Nachman: *v'haya b'yom hanchilo et banav* — "it shall be on the day that he bequeaths to his sons." The *braisa* derives from this verse: *b'yom atah mapil nachalot v'ee atah mapil nachalot balayla* — in the daytime you distribute inheritances, and you do not distribute inheritances at night.

Abaye immediately challenges this reading: is the verse really saying that a man has to die during the day for his sons to inherit? *D'shachiv biyomama hu d'yarsi lei bnei, v'dah d'shachiv balayla lo yarsi lei bnei?* — If he dies during the day his sons inherit, and if he dies at night they don't? That is obviously not the meaning of the verse. The Gemara responds: *dilma din nachalot kamashma lan* — perhaps the verse is teaching us about the adjudication of inheritance cases, not the timing of the death itself.

### Dinei Mamonos Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּלְמָא דִּין נַחֲלוֹת קָא אָמְרַתְּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְחֻקַּת מִשְׁפָּט״ – אוֹרְעָה כָּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כּוּלָּהּ לִהְיוֹת דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye suggests a different interpretation of Rabba bar Ḥanina's statement: **Perhaps you said** a distinction between day and night with regard to the **adjudication of inheritances,** as judges are permitted to sit only during the day. A proof for this distinction is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A verse in the passage concerning inheritance states: **"And it shall be for the children of Israel a statute of judgment"** (Numbers 27:11), teaching that **the entire portion was placed [*ure'a*]** together **to be** considered a matter of **judgment,** subject to the procedural rules of a court matter.

The *halacha* that emerges is grounded in a second verse: *v'hayata livnei Yisrael lechukat mishpat* — "it shall be for the children of Israel as a statute of judgment." This phrase appears at the conclusion of the passage concerning the daughters of Tzelafchad. Because the Torah characterizes the laws of inheritance with the term *mishpat* — judgment — the Gemara concludes that the entire *parasha* has the status of *din*. And since it is a *din*, it is subject to the procedural rules that govern *dinei mamonos*: the adjudication must begin during the day, though it may continue into the night.

### Chidush Debate

The *Rashbam* and *Tosafos* disagree about what the *chidush* of this ruling actually is. According to the *Rashbam*, one might have thought that determining inheritance is merely a *birur* — a fact-finding exercise. If a man dies, we simply research who his closest surviving relatives are. There is no plaintiff and no defendant in the conventional sense; we are just clarifying a genealogical fact. The *chidush* is that the Torah characterizes this as a genuine *din*, with all the procedural requirements of *dinei mamonos* — including the requirement that proceedings begin during the day.

*Tosafos* finds this *chidush* too modest. It is obvious, *Tosafos* argues, that inheritance adjudication is *dinei mamonos* — of course it requires *beit din* and of course it must begin by day. The real *chidush*, according to *Tosafos*, lies elsewhere: because inheritance law is a *din*, a *beit din* that was present at the moment of death — for example, three witnesses attending a deathbed distribution — can impose its ruling on the parties. The heirs cannot simply reject that *beit din* and demand a different venue of their choosing. In a normal monetary dispute, both parties must agree on a venue; but because *nachalah* is a *din* in the full sense — not merely a transaction between consenting parties but a *Torah*-imposed transfer — a *beit din* present at the relevant moment has the authority to adjudicate without the consent of the heirs.

### Chukat Mishpat Weight

The phrase *chukat mishpat* carries additional weight beyond the procedural rules discussed here. The *Rambam* and other *rishonim* use it to establish that inheritance law is not subject to *dina d'malchuta dina* — the principle by which secular law governs monetary transactions between parties. In ordinary monetary matters, the law of the kingdom shapes the implicit terms of transactions, and *dina d'malchuta dina* can displace *Torah* law. Inheritance, however, is not a transaction between parties. It is the *Torah*'s dictation of who receives property when a person dies. The word *chok* — statute — signals that this is a Divine enactment, not a contractual arrangement that can be governed by the norms of the surrounding legal system. Some *rishonim* therefore rule that *nachalah* is categorically exempt from *dina d'malchuta dina*, and the *chukat mishpat* formulation is their textual basis. In the present context, the Gemara uses the same language more narrowly — to establish the procedural status of inheritance as *dinei mamonos* requiring daytime commencement — but the language carries this broader resonance as well.

## Night Exception

### Asukin B'Oso Inyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה, רָצוּ – כּוֹתְבִין, רָצוּ – עוֹשִׂין דִּין. שְׁנַיִם – כּוֹתְבִין וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דִּין. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם;
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Yehuda, as Rav Yehuda says:** In a case where there were **three** men **who entered** a room **to visit a sick person** and the sick person desired to write a will in order to distribute his property following his death, if the visitors **wish** to do so **they may write** his will and sign it as witnesses. And if **they wish, they may act** in **judgment,** i.e., they may act as a court in the matter, since there are three of them. Therefore, they can determine that the will has the validity of an act of court and transfer the property to the heirs in their capacity as a court. But if only **two** came to visit the sick person, **they may write** the will and sign it as witnesses, **but they may not act** in **judgment,** since three are required to form a court. **And Rav Ḥisda says:** This *halakha* was **taught only** in a case where the three came to visit him **during the day,**

Having established that inheritance cases must begin during the day, the Gemara immediately introduces an exception. The rule requires that proceedings *begin* during daytime. If a court has already commenced adjudication during lawful hours, it may continue past nightfall. This principle is illustrated through a case that involves three men visiting a dying person with the intention to act as a court for distributing his property.

### B'Chol Eis Source

The Gemara asks: *mana hanei mili* — what is the source for permitting nighttime continuation? Rav Chisda answers by citing the verse *v'shaftu es ha'am b'chol eis* — "and they shall judge the people at all times." The phrase *b'chol eis*, at all times, is read as encompassing the night: once a court has lawfully begun a case during the day, it may judge *b'chol eis* — at any time, including after dark. The rule of daytime commencement is thus not absolute; it governs when proceedings must open, not when they must close. A court already *asukin b'oso inyan* — occupied with that very matter — may see it through to resolution regardless of the hour.
