# Eruvin 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Permanent Structures

### City Boundary Structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יוֹשְׁבֵי צְרִיפִין, אֵין מוֹדְדִין לָהֶן אֶלָּא מִפֶּתַח בָּתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said: Those who dwell in huts,** i.e., in thatched hovels of straw and willow branches, are not considered inhabitants of a city. Therefore, **one measures** the Shabbat limit **for them only from the entrance to their homes;** the huts are not combined together and considered a city.

The daf begins at the bottom of 55b, continuing the discussion of which types of structures can be considered part of the boundaries of a city for purposes of *eruv techumin*. The basic principle, which Rashi reiterates consistently throughout, is that any structure made for habitation and fit for habitation counts — even if it is not currently being inhabited.

### Thatched Huts Rule

The Gemara records a ruling of Rav Huna: *yoshvei tzerifim ein modedin lahem ela mi-petach bateihem* — those who live in huts made of thatch do not have their dwellings counted as part of the city. Even if an entire settlement consists of such huts, it is not defined as a city; rather, each inhabitant is treated as an isolated individual household. The underlying assumption is that these structures are entirely non-permanent and therefore can never constitute a city, nor can they extend an existing city's boundaries.

### Desert Encampment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב חִסְדָּא: ״וַיַּחֲנוּ עַל הַיַּרְדֵּן מִבֵּית הַיְשִׁימוֹת״, וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה (אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן): לְדִידִי חֲזֵי לִי הָהוּא אַתְרָא, וְהָוֵי תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי עַל תְּלָתָא פַּרְסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda raised an objection:** The Torah states with regard to the Jewish people in the desert: **"And they pitched by the Jordan, from Beit-HaYeshimot** to Avel-Shittim in the plains of Moab" (Numbers 33:49), **and Rabba bar bar Ḥana said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: I myself saw that place, and it is three parasangs [*parsa*],** the equivalent of twelve *mil*, **by three parasangs.**

Rav Chisda raises an objection from the verse: *va-yachanu al ha-Yarden mi-Beit ha-Yeshimot* — "and they encamped on the Jordan from Beit ha-Yeshimot." Rabba bar bar Chana testified that he had personally seen that location by the Jordan River, and it measured three *parsaot* by three *parsaot*. Since a *parsa* is four *mil* and a *mil* is 2,000 *amot* — which is itself the measure of *techum Shabbat* — the encampment extended twelve *mil* by twelve *mil*. (This calculation is also the basis for the Rambam's position that the biblical *techum Shabbat* is twelve *mil*, not merely 2,000 *amot*.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: כְּשֶׁהֵן נִפְנִין — אֵין נִפְנִין לֹא לִפְנֵיהֶם, וְלֹא לְצִדֵּיהֶן, אֶלָּא לְאַחֲרֵיהֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **And it was taught** in a *baraita*: **When they would defecate** in the wilderness, **they would not defecate in front of themselves,** i.e., in front of the camp, **and not to their sides,** due to respect for the Divine Presence; **rather,** they would do so **behind** the camp.

A *Baraita* further describes the practices of the encampment: when the people needed to relieve themselves, they would not go in front of the camp or to its sides, only behind it — since they knew the camp would not retrace its path, and they did not wish to later walk over areas they had used. The relevance to Rav Huna's ruling is clear: the Israelites lived in tents in the wilderness, structures no more permanent than thatched huts, and yet the entire encampment was considered their *machaneh* — their established settlement. How can Rav Huna say that non-permanent dwellings form no city?

### Divine Command Permanence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: דִּגְלֵי מִדְבָּר קָאָמְרַתְּ? כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיב בְּהוּ: ״עַל פִּי ה׳ יַחֲנוּ וְעַל פִּי ה׳ יִסָּעוּ״ — כְּמַאן דִּקְבִיעַ לְהוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: The banners of the desert, you say?** Are you citing a proof from the practice of the Jewish people as they traveled through the desert according to their tribal banners? **Since it is written with regard to them: "According to the commandment of the Lord they remained encamped, and according to the commandment of the Lord they journeyed"** (Numbers 9:20), **it was considered as though it were a permanent** residence **for them.** A camp that is established in accordance with the word of God is regarded as a permanent settlement.

Rava resolves the contradiction: *diglei midbar kaamrat* — "you are speaking of the encampments in the desert." Since the Torah states *al pi Hashem yachanu ve-al pi Hashem yisau* — they encamped and traveled by God's word — their settlements are considered permanent, *ke-man de-kavi'a dami*. As long as God declared "remain," they remained, and the encampment therefore had the full legal status of a permanent settlement. This principle has a parallel in other areas of *hilchot Shabbat*, where the Gemara discusses why the *mishkan*'s construction can serve as a model for *melachot* despite the fact that it was constantly dismantled and reassembled — and the same logic applies: whenever God said "stay," it was permanent.

### Courtyards Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִינָּנָא בַּר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם יֵשׁ שָׁם שָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת שֶׁל שְׁנֵי בָתִּים — הוּקְבְּעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥinnana bar Rav Kahana said that Rav Ashi said: If there are three courtyards of two** properly built **houses** among a settlement of huts, **they have been established** as a permanent settlement, and the Shabbat limit is measured from the edge of the settlement.

Rav Chinana bar Rav Kahana citing Rav Ashi adds an important qualification: *im yesh sham shalosh chatzerot o shnei battim, hu kevuah* — if within a settlement of thatched huts there exists a nucleus of three courtyards, each containing two permanent houses, the entire settlement acquires the status of a city. Rashi explains that the word *bayit* here, as opposed to *tzerif*, specifically denotes a permanent structure. The problem Rav Huna identified was exclusively when the entire settlement consisted of non-permanent dwellings; if even a portion of the city meets the standard definition of urban permanence — three courtyards with two permanent houses — then the thatched huts around it are also incorporated into and serve as an extension of that city.

## Nomadic Critique

### Nomadic Life Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: יוֹשְׁבֵי צְרִיפִין וְהוֹלְכֵי מִדְבָּרוֹת — חַיֵּיהֶן אֵינָן חַיִּים, וּנְשֵׁיהֶן וּבְנֵיהֶן אֵינָן שֶׁלָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of people who dwell in huts, **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said: Those who dwell in huts,** such as shepherds who pass from one place to another and stay in a single location for only a brief period, **and desert travelers, their lives are not lives,** i.e., they lead extremely difficult lives, **and their wives and children are not** always **their** own, as will be explained below.

The Gemara continues with a broader set of observations about the nature of city life, prompted by the discussion of thatched huts and nomadic existence. Rav Yehuda citing Rav declares: *yoshei tzerifin ve-ochelei midbarot, chayeihem einam chayim* — those who live in thatched huts and those who eat and travel in the wilderness, their lives are not real lives. The statement captures the sense that a nomadic existence, lacking the amenities and social stability that constitute genuine civilized life, does not qualify as genuine living.

### Fidelity Concerns

The statement continues with a harsher dimension: *nesheihem u-veneihem einam shelahem* — their wives and children are not truly theirs. The Gemara will go on to explain the concern, but the thrust of the remark is that those living nomadic lives must entertain serious suspicions about fidelity. The remark is directed primarily from the perspective of the man's concerns — his anxiety about his wife and the paternity of his children — though as the subsequent discussion makes clear, the moral exposure cuts in multiple directions.

### Graveyard Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, אֱלִיעֶזֶר אִישׁ בִּירְיָא אוֹמֵר: יוֹשְׁבֵי צְרִיפִין כְּיוֹשְׁבֵי קְבָרִים. וְעַל בְּנוֹתֵיהֶם הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אָרוּר שׁוֹכֵב עִם כׇּל בְּהֵמָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in the following *baraita*: **Eliezer of Biriyya says: Those who dwell in huts are like those who dwell in graves. And with regard to** one who marries **their daughters,** the verse **says: "Cursed be he who sleeps with any manner of beast"** (Deuteronomy 27:21).

A *Baraita* corroborates and intensifies this view: Elazar Ish Birayah says that those who dwell in *tzerifin* are like those dwelling in graveyards — not a life at all. And regarding their daughters — meaning the women of such communities — he invokes an extreme verse: *arur shochev im kol behemah*, "cursed is he who lies with any animal." This is a strikingly harsh statement, and the Gemara will now seek to explain the underlying concern that motivates these judgments.

### Bathhouse Absence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? עוּלָּא אָמַר: שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן מֶרְחֲצָאוֹת. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּרְגִּישִׁין זֶה לָזֶה בִּטְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this harsh statement with regard to the daughters of those who dwell in huts or travel in deserts? **Ulla said: They do not have bathhouses,** and therefore the men have to walk a significant distance in order to bathe. There is concern that while they are away their wives commit adultery, and that consequently their children are not really their own. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Because they sense when one another immerses.** Similarly to the men, the women must walk a significant distance in order to immerse in a ritual bath. Since the settlement is very small and everyone knows when the women go to immerse, it is possible for an unscrupulous man to use this information to engage in adulterous relations with them by following them and taking advantage of the fact that they are alone.

The Gemara asks: *mai taama* — what is the reason for these concerns? Ulla answers: *she-ein lahem merchata'ot* — they have no bathhouses. A properly functioning city, particularly in the Roman-influenced world of the Talmud, would have public bathhouses. Without access to such facilities, people in nomadic or semi-nomadic settings have no private, regulated space for bathing.

### Mikvah Encounters

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different explanation: *mipnei shemargishim zeh lazeh bitvila* — because they become aware of one another during immersion in the *mikvah*. Rashi explains the mechanics of each concern. Regarding the absence of bathhouses: when people must bathe, they go out to the river alone, far from the settlement and without supervision — a circumstance that enables illicit encounters. Regarding the *mikvah*: because immersion is not conducted privately, it becomes known publicly that a woman has immersed, and an unscrupulous man may seize upon that knowledge to attempt seduction immediately after she emerges.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ נַהֲרָא דִּסְמִיךְ לְבֵיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical difference **between** the explanations of Ulla and Rabbi Yoḥanan? The Gemara explains: **There is** a practical difference **between them** in a case where there is **a river that is adjacent to the house,** and it is suitable for immersion but not for bathing. Consequently, the women would not have to go far to immerse themselves, but the men would still have to walk a significant distance in order to bathe.

The Gemara notes that the practical difference between Ulla's and Rabbi Yochanan's views is the case of *nahara de-samich la-birta* — a river situated close to the city. In that scenario, there would still be a *mikvah* concern (Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning would apply), but not the concern about isolation and lack of privacy that grounds Ulla's explanation, since proximity to the settlement reduces the danger. The overall picture that emerges from this section is a sharp critique — from at least some *Tannaim* — of the moral consequences of nomadic or semi-nomadic life.

## Urban Components

### Vegetables Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ יָרָק — אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לָדוּר בָּהּ. לְמֵימְרָא דְּיָרָק מְעַלְּיָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: שְׁלֹשָׁה מַרְבִּין אֶת הַזֶּבֶל, וְכוֹפְפִין אֶת הַקּוֹמָה, וְנוֹטְלִין אֶחָד מֵחֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מִמְּאוֹר עֵינָיו שֶׁל אָדָם, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן:
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned various places of residence, the Gemara cites what **Rav Huna said: Any city that does not have vegetables, a Torah scholar is not permitted to dwell there** for health reasons. The Gemara asks: **Is that to say that vegetables are beneficial** to a person's health? **Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Three** things **increase one's waste, bend** his **stature, and remove one five-hundredth of the light of a person's eyes; and they are**

The Gemara now offers additional criteria for what constitutes a livable city. Rav Huna states: *kol ir she-ein bah yerek, ein talmid chacham rashai ladur bah* — any city that does not have vegetables available is not a place where a Torah sage should reside. The reasoning is that vegetables are an inexpensive and readily available form of sustenance, important for *talmidei chachamim* who devote the bulk of their time to study and require accessible, affordable food.

### Vegetable Health Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פַּת קִיבָּר וְשֵׁכָר חָדָשׁ וְיָרָק. לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּתוּמֵי וְכַרָּתֵי, הָא — בִּשְׁאָר יַרְקֵי. כִּדְתַנְיָא: שׁוּם — יָרָק, כְּרֵישִׁין — חֲצִי יָרָק, נִרְאֶה צְנוֹן — נִרְאֶה סַם חַיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** **coarse bread,** made from coarse flour that has not been thoroughly sifted, **new beer, and vegetables.** This indicates that vegetables are harmful to one's well-being. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement of Rav Huna is referring **to garlic and leeks,** which are beneficial; **that** *baraita* is referring **to other vegetables,** which are harmful. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Garlic is** a healthy **vegetable; leeks** are **a half-vegetable,** meaning they are half as healthful. If **radish has been seen, an elixir of life has been seen,** as it is very beneficial to the body.

This raises an immediate question: does the Gemara mean to imply that vegetables are beneficial? A *Baraita* seems to contradict this, listing three things that increase excrement, cause one to become bent over, and diminish one's eyesight by one five-hundredth. The three items are *pat kibar* (coarse bran bread), *shechar chadash* (new mead or beer), and *yerek* (vegetables). Vegetables, then, would appear to be harmful, not beneficial.

### Specific Vegetables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַנְיָא: נִרְאֶה צְנוֹן — נִרְאֶה סַם הַמָּוֶת! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן — בֶּעָלִין, כָּאן — בָּאִמָּהוֹת. כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, כָּאן — בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Wasn't it taught** in a different *baraita*: If **radish has been seen, a lethal drug has been seen?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** in the *baraita* that deprecates radish, it is referring **to** its **leaves; there,** in the *baraita* that praises radish, it is referring **to the roots.** Alternatively, **here** it is referring **to the summer,** when radish is beneficial; **there,** it is referring **to the winter,** when it is harmful.

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya* — it is not difficult. One teaching refers to *tuma ve-charti* — garlic and leeks — while the other refers to other vegetables. Garlic and leeks are beneficial; other vegetables may not be. Rashi initially presents the assignment one way and then reverses himself, concluding that garlic and leeks are indeed the healthful ones — which accords with the general reputation of garlic as one of the most beneficial foods.

The Gemara then cites a *Baraita* elaborating on specific cases. Garlic is a vegetable of full standing. *Krishin* (leeks) count as half a vegetable. And then there is a dispute about the radish: one tradition says *nira tzenon nira sam chayim* — seeing a radish is like seeing the elixir of life; another says *nira tzenon nira sam mavet* — it is the draught of death. The Gemara resolves this as well: *lo kashya* — one refers to the leaves, the other to the bulb itself, and additionally the effects differ depending on whether it is summer or winter.

### Topography Effects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ מַעֲלוֹת וּמוֹרָדוֹת, אָדָם וּבְהֵמָה שֶׁבָּהּ מֵתִים בַּחֲצִי יְמֵיהֶן. מֵתִים סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: מַזְקִינִים בַּחֲצִי יְמֵיהֶן. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הָנֵי מוּלְיָיתָא דְּבֵי בֵירֵי וּדְבֵי נָרֶשׁ אַזְקְנוּן.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the attributes of different locations, **Rav Yehuda** also **said that Rav said:** In **any city that has** many **ascents and descents,** which can be taxing to the body, **people and animals die at half their days,** meaning half of their life expectancy. The Gemara expresses surprise: **Can it enter your mind** that Rav really meant to say that they **die** prematurely? Even in such cities they are known to have a regular life expectancy. **Rather, say: They grow old at half their days,** i.e., they age prematurely due to the strain of climbing up and down the inclines. Similarly, **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: The ascents** and descents **between Beit Biri and Beit Neresh,** my place of residence, **made me grow old** prematurely.

Returning to the conditions of a livable city, Rav Yehuda citing Rav states: *kol ir she-yesh bah ma'alot u-mordot* — any city with steep inclines, a very hilly terrain, *adam u-veheimah she-bah meitim ba-chatzi yemeihem* — the people and animals in it die in the middle of their days. The Gemara immediately qualifies the hyperbole: *meitim salka daatach?* — can they literally die at half their age? Rather, *mizkinim ba-chatzi yemeihem* — they age prematurely, they become old while still in their middle years, because of the constant physical exertion demanded by the terrain. One might note the irony that contemporary sensibility would reverse this judgment entirely, viewing hilly terrain as conducive to exercise and health.

### Personal Testimony

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua provides personal testimony in corroboration: *hanei milyata de-vei Beiri ve-de-vei Narash azkunun* — the hills of Beiri and Narash, two towns, made him old. This is consistent with the principle just stated.

## Compass Directions

### Constellation Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּא לְרַבְּעָהּ — מְרַבְּעָהּ בְּרִיבּוּעַ עוֹלָם, נוֹתֵן צְפוֹנָהּ לִצְפוֹן עוֹלָם וּדְרוֹמָהּ לִדְרוֹם עוֹלָם, וְסִימָנָיךְ: עֲגָלָה בַּצָּפוֹן, וְעַקְרָב בַּדָּרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the measurements of a city's boundaries, **the Sages taught** the following *baraita*: If, in order to measure the Shabbat limit, **one comes to square** a city, i.e., to extend the city's boundaries to include all of its protrusions within an imaginary square, **he squares it** so that the sides of the square align **with the four** directions **of the world. He sets the northern** side of the square **to** align with **the north of the world, and its southern** side **to** align with **the south of the world. And your sign** by which you can recognize the directions of the world is as follows: The constellation of **Ursa Major is in the north and Scorpio is in the south.** The directions of the city are determined by these constellations.

The Gemara now returns to the laws of *techumin* and addresses the procedure for squaring off a city to the cardinal directions. The ruling is: *ba le-reivah, mereiva be-reibua olam* — when squaring off a city, one aligns it to the compass points of the world. The practical significance of squaring off a city to the cardinal directions is substantial: instead of simply measuring a circle around the city, one draws a rectangle aligned to north, south, east, and west, gaining the corners and thereby extending the effective boundary considerably. A visual illustration makes this vivid — take Manhattan, square it off to the directions, and the additional area captured includes portions of Queens, Brooklyn, and New Jersey.

The directions are established by the formula *notin tzafonah le-tzafono shel olam, deromah le-deromo shel olam* — the northernmost point is set to true north, the southernmost to true south, and so forth.

### Constellation Guide

The way to identify the directions before magnetic compasses was by the constellations: *ve-simanach, egla be-tzafon* — the mnemonic is that *agala* (Ursa Major, or according to some sources Ursa Minor) is in the north, and *ve-akrav be-darom* — the scorpion constellation is in the south. According to the Soncino commentary, the scorpion is not due south but is situated in the southern sky as seen from *Eretz Yisrael*, and unlike the north star it is not always visible.

The names of the directions carry their own etymological interest: *Tzafon* (north) may derive from a root meaning "hidden," because the sun never appears there; *Darom* (south) may be read as *dar ram*, "where the sun dwells in its height," since the sun always moves toward the south for those in the Northern Hemisphere.

### Solstice Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אִם אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ לְרַבְּעָהּ בְּרִיבּוּעַ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, — מְרַבְּעָהּ כְּמִין הַתְּקוּפָה. כֵּיצַד? חַמָּה יוֹצְאָה בְּיוֹם אָרוֹךְ וְשׁוֹקַעַת בְּיוֹם אָרוֹךְ — זֶה הוּא פְּנֵי צָפוֹן. חַמָּה יוֹצְאָה בְּיוֹם קָצָר וְשׁוֹקַעַת בְּיוֹם קָצָר — זֶה הוּא פְּנֵי דָרוֹם. תְּקוּפַת נִיסָן וּתְקוּפַת תִּשְׁרֵי — חַמָּה יוֹצְאָה בַּחֲצִי מִזְרָח וְשׁוֹקַעַת בַּחֲצִי מַעֲרָב,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: If one does not know how to square** the city in alignment **with the four** directions **of the world** based upon the constellations, **he should square it based upon the seasons,** although this is less precise. **How so?** Where **the sun rises and sets on the longest day** of the year, the summer solstice, **this** route of the sun **is the face of the north.** The sun rises in the northeast and sets in the northwest, and thus travels from east to west across the north side of the world. Conversely, where **the sun rises and sets on the shortest day** of the year, the winter solstice, **this** route of the sun **is the face of the south.** Whereas at **the vernal equinox and the autumnal equinox,** when day and night are equal in length, **the sun rises in the middle of the east and sets in the middle of the west.**

Rabbi Yosi offers an alternative method for those who do not know the constellations: *mereivah ke-min ha-tekufah* — one can establish the directions by means of the season and the rising and setting of the sun. *Chama yotza be-yom aroch ve-shoka'at be-yom aroch, zehu pnei tzafon* — when the sun rises on a long day (the summer solstice) and sets on a long day, the direction it comes from indicates the north. *Ve-chama yotza be-yom katzar ve-shoka'at be-yom katzar, zehu pnei darom* — on the shortest day (the winter solstice), the direction of sunrise and sunset indicates the south.

### Sun's Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הוֹלֵךְ אֶל דָּרוֹם וְסוֹבֵב אֶל צָפוֹן״, הוֹלֵךְ אֶל דָּרוֹם — בַּיּוֹם, וְסוֹבֵב אֶל צָפוֹן — בַּלַּיְלָה. ״סוֹבֵב סוֹבֵב הוֹלֵךְ הָרוּחַ״ — אֵלּוּ פְּנֵי מִזְרָח וּפְנֵי מַעֲרָב, פְּעָמִים מְהַלַּכְתָּן וּפְעָמִים מְסַבַּבְתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is stated:** "One generation passes away and another generation comes; but the earth abides forever. The sun also rises and the sun goes down, and hastens to its place, where it rises again. **It goes toward the south, and turns about to the north;** round and round goes the wind, and on its circuits the wind returns" (Ecclesiastes 1:4–6). The verse is understood as describing the sun's movements, as follows: **"It goes toward the south" during the day; "and turns about to the north,"** on the other side of the earth, **at night. "Round and round goes the wind [*ruaḥ*];"** the word *ruaḥ* can also mean direction or side. Rabbi Yosei explains that **these are the face of the east and the face of the west; sometimes** the sun **traverses them** visibly, **and sometimes it turns about them** without being seen.

The underlying astronomical reality is as follows. At the vernal and autumnal equinoxes, the sun rises due east and sets due west. On the longer days — approaching the summer solstice — it rises from the northeast and sets in the northwest; on the shorter days — approaching the winter solstice — it rises from the southeast and sets in the southwest. If one thinks about this carefully: since the sun always moves toward the south (for those in the Northern Hemisphere), a longer path means it starts further north, hence the sunrise is more northerly on long days.

The Gemara corroborates this from the verse: *holech el darom ba-yom, ve-sovev el tzafon ba-lailah* — the sun moves toward the south during the day and circles back toward the north at night, tracing an elliptical path. The verse in *Kohelet* then says *sovev sovev holech ha-ruach* — "round and round goes the wind" — which the Gemara reads as a reference to the east and west faces, *pnei mizrach u-pnei ma'arav*, the directions that the sun sometimes traverses and sometimes goes around.

The Gemara also notes that *tekufat Tishrei* — the autumnal equinox — offers the clearest orientation: *chama yotza b'chatzi mizrach ve-shoka'at b'chatzi ma'arav* — the sun rises exactly due east and sets exactly due west. One might wonder why the tradition emphasizes the solstice method rather than the simpler vernal or autumnal equinox approach, where due east and due west are directly observable and a right angle immediately yields north and south.

### Sun Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: לֵיתַנְהוּ לְהָנֵי כְּלָלֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יָצְאָה חַמָּה מֵעוֹלָם מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית צְפוֹנִית וְשָׁקְעָה בְּקֶרֶן מַעֲרָבִית צְפוֹנִית, וְלֹא יָצְאָה חַמָּה מִקֶּרֶן מִזְרָחִית דְּרוֹמִית וְשָׁקְעָה בְּקֶרֶן מַעֲרָבִית דְּרוֹמִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mesharshiya said: There is no** validity to these **rules** established by Rabbi Yosei, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **The sun has never risen,** even during the summer, **at the northeastern corner** of the sky **and set in the northwestern corner, nor has the sun** ever **risen,** even during the winter, **at the southeastern corner and set in the southwestern corner.** Therefore, one can establish the directions of the world according to the sun's path only during the autumn and spring.

Rav Mesharshaya objects that these directional methods based on the solstices are not reliable rules. A *Baraita* states: *lo yatza chama me'olam mi-keren mizrachit tzfonit* — the sun has never truly risen from the exact northeast, nor has it ever set in the exact northwest; likewise it has never risen from the exact southeast nor set in the exact southwest. This is precisely the point noted above: while the sun moves northward in summer and southward in winter, it never reaches a full 45 degrees from due east. The maximum deviation is approximately 23 degrees. Therefore, one cannot use the solstice sunrise or sunset to determine true north or south with precision, since the sun never actually reaches the northeast or southeast positions that would be required for that calculation to work.

## Seasonal Timing

### Four Tekufot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין תְּקוּפַת נִיסָן נוֹפֶלֶת אֶלָּא בְּאַרְבָּעָה רִבְעֵי הַיּוֹם, אוֹ בִּתְחִלַּת הַיּוֹם אוֹ בִּתְחִלַּת הַלַּיְלָה, אוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַיּוֹם אוֹ בַּחֲצִי הַלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of the previous discussion with regard to calculating the directions of the world based upon the seasons, **Shmuel said: The vernal equinox occurs only at** the beginning of one of **the four quarters of a day: Either** precisely **at the beginning of the day, or** precisely **at the beginning of the night, or at midday, or at midnight.**

Shmuel, who was particularly distinguished in matters of astronomy, now presents his astronomical calculations. He states: *ein tekufat Nissan nofelet ela b'arba'ah rivei ha-yom* — the vernal equinox, *tekufat Nissan*, only ever falls at one of the four quarters of the day: either at sunrise (*tchilas ha-yom*), or at the beginning of night (sunset), or at noon (*chatzi ha-yom*), or at midnight (*chatzi ha-lailah*).

To understand this, one must grasp the four *tekufot* and their structure. The four seasonal turning points are: *Nissan* (vernal equinox), *Tammuz* (summer solstice), *Tishrei* (autumnal equinox), and *Tevet* (winter solstice). *Tevet* is the winter solstice — hence *Chanukah* always falls in the cold — and *Tammuz* is the summer solstice — hence *Shiva Asar B'Tammuz* always falls in the heat.

### Annual Shift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין תְּקוּפַת תַּמּוּז נוֹפֶלֶת אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּאַחַת וּמֶחֱצָה, אוֹ בְּשֶׁבַע וּמֶחֱצָה, בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה. וְאֵין תְּקוּפַת תִּשְׁרֵי נוֹפֶלֶת אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת אוֹ בְּתֵשַׁע שָׁעוֹת, בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה. וְאֵין תְּקוּפַת טֵבֵת נוֹפֶלֶת אֶלָּא אוֹ בְּאַרְבַּע וּמֶחֱצָה אוֹ בְּעֶשְׂר וּמֶחֱצָה, בֵּין בַּיּוֹם וּבֵין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **the summer solstice occurs only** at certain times of the day: **Either at** the conclusion of **one and a half** hours **or seven and a half** hours **of the day or night. And the autumnal equinox occurs only** at certain times: **Either at** the conclusion of **three hours or nine hours of the day or night. And the winter solstice occurs only** at certain times: **Either at** the conclusion of **four and a half** hours **or ten and a half** hours **of the day or night.**

The reason for Shmuel's rule is the following calculation. The solar year, by the Julian reckoning that Shmuel uses, is 365 and one-quarter days. Therefore, the shift from one year's vernal equinox to the next year's vernal equinox is one day and six hours beyond exactly 52 weeks. If the vernal equinox occurred on Monday at sunrise one year, then the following year it will occur on Tuesday at noon (sunrise plus six hours). The year after that, it will fall on Wednesday at sunset. The year after that, on Thursday at midnight. And then on Friday at sunrise again. Consequently, the *tekufa* can only ever fall at sunrise, noon, sunset, or midnight — cycling through these four points year after year. This same 28-year cycle is the basis for *Birkat HaChammah*, which returns to its starting point after 28 years.

It is worth noting that Shmuel's value of 365 and one-quarter days is the Julian figure. The actual solar year is slightly less than this, which is why the Gregorian calendar introduced the correction of omitting the leap year in three out of every four century years (years divisible by 100 but not by 400). The Jewish permanent calendar, meanwhile, achieves a closer approximation of the true solar year over the 19-year cycle than the Julian calendar — a remarkable achievement.

### Tammuz Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין בֵּין תְּקוּפָה לִתְקוּפָה אֶלָּא תִּשְׁעִים וְאֶחָד יוֹם וְשֶׁבַע שָׁעוֹת וּמֶחֱצָה. וְאֵין תְּקוּפָה מוֹשֶׁכֶת מֵחֲבֶרְתָּהּ אֶלָּא חֲצִי שָׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** all this is based on the principle that **there are only ninety-one days and seven and a half hours between** the beginning of **one season and the next,** as he assumed that a year is exactly 365¼ days. **And** similarly, each **season** begins precisely **one-half** planetary **hour past the** beginning of the **previous season.** There are seven heavenly bodies that are each ascendant for an hour at a time in a constant rotation: Mercury, Moon, Saturn, Jupiter, Mars, the Sun, and Venus. Each season begins half an hour later in this rotation than **the previous** season.

Having established the pattern for *tekufat Nissan*, the Gemara moves to *tekufat Tammuz*. From one *tekufa* to the next is a quarter of a year. The annual shift is one day and six hours; therefore the quarterly shift between consecutive *tekufot* is one-quarter of that, namely seven and a half hours. So *tekufat Tammuz* will always fall seven and a half hours after the corresponding *tekufat Nissan* slot. If *tekufat Nissan* can fall at midnight, then *tekufat Tammuz* can fall at midnight plus seven and a half hours, which is seven-thirty in the morning — one and a half hours into the day. The Gemara therefore states: *ein tekufat Tammuz nofelet ela o b'achat u-mechtza o b'sheva u-mechtza, bein ba-yom bein ba-lailah* — it falls either at one and a half hours or seven and a half hours, whether into the day or into the night.

### Other Tekufot

Continuing the pattern, *tekufat Tishrei* adds another seven and a half hours to the *Tammuz* slots, yielding: *nofelet o b'shalosh sha'os o b'teisha sha'os, bein ba-yom bein ba-lailah* — it falls at either three hours or nine hours into the day or night. And *tekufat Tevet* adds yet another seven and a half hours: *nofelet o b'arba u-mechtza o b'eser u-mechtza, bein ba-yom bein ba-lailah* — at four and a half or ten and a half hours into the day or night. The pattern is straightforward: each successive *tekufa* shifts by an additional hour and a half in its starting point.

### Tekufa Distances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין לְךָ תְּקוּפַת נִיסָן שֶׁנּוֹפֶלֶת בְּצֶדֶק, שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְשַׁבֶּרֶת אֶת הָאִילָנוֹת. וְאֵין לְךָ תְּקוּפַת טֵבֵת שֶׁנּוֹפֶלֶת בְּצֶדֶק, שֶׁאֵינָהּ מְיַיבֶּשֶׁת אֶת הַזְּרָעִים. וְהוּא, דְּאִיתְיְלִיד לְבָנָה אוֹ בִּלְבָנָה אוֹ בְּצֶדֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said: There is no** instance when the **vernal equinox occurs in** the planetary hour of **Jupiter** and **it does not break the trees** with its strong winds; **and there is no** instance when **the winter solstice occurs in** the planetary hour of **Jupiter** and **it does not dry up the seeds. And this** applies only **where the new moon appeared either at** the hour of the **Moon or at** the hour of **Jupiter.**

The Gemara explicitly states the distance between one *tekufa* and the next: *tish'in ve-echad ve-sheva sha'ot u-mechtza* — 91 days, seven hours, and thirty minutes. The arithmetic confirms this: 91 times four equals 364, and seven and a half hours times four equals 30 hours, which is one day and six hours. The total is therefore 365 days and 6 hours, i.e., 365 and one-quarter days — exactly the Julian solar year.

### Tekufa Precision

Shmuel adds an astronomical observation: *ein lecha tekufat Nissan she-nofelet be-tzedek she-einah meshaberet ilanos* — whenever the vernal equinox falls under the influence of *Tzedek* (Jupiter, in the seven-planet reckoning familiar to the Talmudic sages), it invariably causes trees to be broken — presumably by severe winds. Similarly, *tekufat Tevet* never falls under *Tzedek* without the seeds drying up. These observations apply specifically when the moon or sun is in ascendancy in relation to Jupiter — a framework that belongs to the astronomical and astrological system of the Talmudic period.

## Squaring Method

### Square Tableaux

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְרַבֵּעַ אֶת הָעִיר, עוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָהּ כְּמִין טַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת. וְחוֹזֵר וּמְרַבֵּעַ אֶת הַתְּחוּמִין, וְעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָן כְּמִין טַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One who squares a city** in order to determine its Shabbat limit **renders it like a square tablet, and then** he also **squares the** Shabbat **boundaries and renders them like a square tablet.** Consequently, after squaring the city, he adds additional squares of two thousand cubits to each of its sides.

The Gemara now sets out the full procedure for squaring the city and the *techumin*. The *Baraita* teaches: *ha-merubeia et ha-ir, oseh otah ke-min tavla meruba'at* — when one squares off the city, one makes it into a square tableau, aligned to the cardinal directions. Then, *ve-chozer u-merubeia et ha-tchumin, ve-oseh otan ke-min tavla meruba'at* — one squares off the *techumin* as well, creating a larger square around the inner square of the city.

### Diagonal Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁהוּא מוֹדֵד, לֹא יִמְדּוֹד מֵאֶמְצַע הַקֶּרֶן אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַפְסִיד אֶת הַזָּוִיּוֹת. אֶלָּא מֵבִיא טַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת שֶׁהִיא אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה עַל אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, וּמַנִּיחָהּ בְּקֶרֶן בַּאֲלַכְסוֹנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And when he measures** the Shabbat limit, **he should not measure** the **two thousand cubits** diagonally **from the middle of** each **corner** of the city, **because** if he were to do so, **he would lose the corners,** i.e., the limit would extend only two thousand cubits on the diagonal from each of the corners. **Rather,** he measures the boundary as though **he brought a square tablet that is two thousand cubits by two thousand cubits, and places it at** each **corner at its diagonal.**

The *Baraita* then specifies: *lo yimdod me-emtza ha-keren alpayim amah, mipnei she-hu mafsid et ha-zaviyot* — one must not measure the 2,000 *amot* at an angle from the corner, because doing so would cause one to lose the corners. The key geometric insight is this: if one measured 2,000 *amot* diagonally from a corner rather than squaring off, the resulting area would be far smaller than what one gains by squaring. Measuring straight out from each face and then completing the square captures far more territory. The additional reach afforded by the corner of a square — relative to a circle of the same radius — is the square root of two times the radius, approximately 1.4 times, which means that going diagonally one can travel some 1,200 *amot* further than going straight.

### Manhattan Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצֵאתָ הָעִיר מִשְׂתַּכֶּרֶת אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת אַמּוֹת לְכָאן וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת אַמּוֹת לְכָאן. נִמְצְאוּ תְּחוּמִין מִשְׂתַּכְּרִין שְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת אַמּוֹת לְכָאן וּשְׁמוֹנֶה מֵאוֹת לְכָאן. נִמְצְאוּ הָעִיר וּתְחוּמִין מִשְׂתַּכְּרִין אֶלֶף וּמָאתַיִם לְכָאן וְאֶלֶף וּמָאתַיִם לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** As a result, **it will be found** that **the city gains four hundred cubits in this** corner **and** another **four hundred cubits in the** opposite corner. Assuming that the city itself is round and has a diameter of two thousand cubits, as will be explained below, when the borders of the city are squared, approximately four hundred cubits are added to the city at each corner. When one then squares the Shabbat boundaries, **it is found** that the Shabbat **boundaries gain eight hundred cubits in this** corner **and eight hundred cubits in the** opposite corner. Consequently, by squaring both the city itself and its Shabbat boundaries, **it is found** that **the city and the** Shabbat **boundaries** together **gain 1,200** cubits **in this** corner **and 1,200** cubits **in the** opposite corner.

The visual illustration captures this vividly. Take Manhattan as the city. When it is squared off to the cardinal directions rather than simply surrounded by a circle, the resulting rectangle captures significant portions of what would otherwise lie outside the boundary — parts of Queens, Brooklyn, and New Jersey. Then when the 2,000-*amot* *techum* is squared off around that rectangle, still more territory is included.

### City Gain

The *Baraita* states: *nimtzet ir mishtakeret arba me'ot amah le-kan ve-arba me'ot amah le-kan* — the city itself, by virtue of being squared off, gains 400 *amot* on each side in the corners. This assumes a city of 2,000 *amot* across, meaning a radius of 1,000 *amot*. The diagonal of a square with a side of 1,000 is 1,000 times the square root of two, approximately 1,400 *amot* — a gain of 400 over the circular measurement.

### Tchumin Gain

For the *techumin*, the gain is larger: *nimtzu tchumin mishtakerin shmonah me'ot amah le-kan u-shmonah me'ot amah le-kan* — the *techumin* gain 800 *amot* on each side. Here the full span from center to the edge of the *techum* is 1,000 (city radius) plus 2,000 (*techum*) = 3,000 *amot*. The square's diagonal at that scale is approximately 4,200 *amot*, a gain of 1,200. But accounting for the inner city's own 400-*amot* gain, the net gain attributed specifically to the *techum* squaring is 800 *amot*.

### Total Diagonal Gain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּמָתָא דְּהָוְיָא תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי אַתְּרֵי אַלְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: And you find this** projection of the additions to the city's borders and Shabbat boundaries to be correct **in** the case of a round **city that is two thousand** cubits **by two thousand** cubits.

The Gemara synthesizes: *nimtzet ir u-tchumeha mishtakrin elef u-matayim le-kan ve-elef u-matayim le-kan* — taken together, city and *tchumin*, one can travel 1,200 extra *amot* when walking diagonally at a 45-degree angle. Traveling straight in any cardinal direction, one covers 1,000 (city) plus 2,000 (*techum*) = 3,000 *amot*. Traveling diagonally, one covers 3,000 times the square root of two, approximately 4,200 *amot* — an excess of 1,200 *amot*. Abaye confirms that this calculation applies when the city is 2,000 by 2,000.

## Levitical Cities

### Torah Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: תְּחוּם עָרֵי לְוִיִּם אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה. צֵא מֵהֶן אֶלֶף אַמָּה מִגְרָשׁ — נִמְצָא מִגְרָשׁ רְבִיעַ, וְהַשְּׁאָר שָׂדוֹת וּכְרָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a similar discussion with regard to the Levite cities, the forty-eight cities given to the Levites in Eretz Yisrael instead of a tribal inheritance. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Yosei, said: The boundary of the cities of the Levites** extends **two thousand cubits** in each direction beyond the inhabited section of the city. **Remove from them a thousand cubits of open space** just beyond the inhabited area, which must be left vacant. **Consequently, the open space is one quarter** of the extended area, **and the rest is fields and vineyards.**

The Gemara now turns to a *Baraita* on the cities of the *Levi'im*. Rabbi Eliezer ben Rabbi Yosi states: *tchum arei Levi'im alpayim amah* — the boundary of the Levitical cities is 2,000 *amot*. The Torah specifies two figures in proximity: one verse mentions 2,000 *amot* around the city; another mentions 1,000 *amot*. The Sages understood these two figures as follows: of the 2,000 *amot*, the inner 1,000 constitutes the *migrash* — an open, unplanted area, essentially parkland — and the outer 1,000 constitutes *sadot*, fields for grain and vineyards. The *migrash* is thus the inner ring and the *sadot* the outer ring, already suggesting that the *sadot* cover far more area than the *migrash*, since they form a much larger square.

### Quarter Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רָבָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״מִקִּיר הָעִיר וָחוּצָה אֶלֶף אַמָּה סָבִיב״, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: סַבֵּב אֶת הָעִיר בְּאֶלֶף, נִמְצָא מִגְרָשׁ רְבִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters?** From where is it derived that the open space surrounding the cities of the Levites measured a thousand cubits? **Rava said: As the verse states:** "And the open spaces of the cities, that you shall give to the Levites, shall be from the wall of the city and outward a thousand cubits round about" (Numbers 35:4). **The Torah states: Surround the city with a thousand** cubits on all sides to serve as an open space. **Consequently, the open space is one quarter** of the area.

The *Baraita* asserts: *nimtzet migrash revia* — the *migrash* turns out to be one-quarter of the whole, with the remainder being fields and vineyards. The Gemara immediately asks: where does this figure come from? Rava cites the verse: *mikir ha-ir va-chutzah elef amah saviv* — one thousand *amot* outside the city all around. This is the basis for the *migrash* being 1,000 *amot*. But the question of whether the *migrash* is truly one-quarter of the whole remains to be demonstrated.

### Square City Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְבִיעַ?! פַּלְגָא הָוֵי! אָמַר רָבָא: בַּר אַדָּא מָשׁוֹחָאָה אַסְבְּרַהּ לִי — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּמָתָא דְּהָוְיָא תְּרֵי אַלְפֵי אַתְּרֵי אַלְפֵי. תְּחוּם כַּמָּה הָוְיָא? שִׁיתְּסַר. קְרָנוֹת כַּמָּה הָוְיָין? שִׁיתְּסַר. דַּל תְּמָנְיָא דִתְחוּמִין וְאַרְבְּעָה דִקְרָנוֹת, כַּמָּה הָוֵי? תְּרֵיסַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to this calculation, how is **the open space found** to be **one quarter** of the area? **It is more than one-third.** The entire area of the extended boundary is thirty-two million square cubits and the open space occupies twelve million square cubits, which is more than one-third of the total area of the extended boundary. **Rava said: Bar Adda the surveyor explained** the calculation **to me: You will find** this **in a city that is two thousand** cubits **by two thousand** cubits. **How many** cubits is the extended **boundary** of the city itself, without the corners? **Sixteen** million square cubits. Squares measuring two thousand by two thousand cubits are appended to each of the four sides of the city. The area of each of these squares is four million square cubits, and the total area of all the additional squares is sixteen million square cubits. **How many** cubits **are the corners? Sixteen** million square cubits, as additional squares of two thousand by two thousand cubits are appended to the corners of the outer boundaries of the cities. **Subtract eight** million square cubits **from the** area of the extended **boundary** for the open space around the city; the first thousand cubits beyond the inhabited part of the city must be left as open space, which amounts to areas measuring one thousand by two thousand cubits on each of the four sides of the city, for a total of eight million square cubits. **And** subtract another **four** million square cubits **from the corners,** as sections of the corners are parallel to the open spaces. **How much is** the sum total of the area of the open spaces? **Twelve** million square cubits.

Rava bar Rav Adda reports: *meshu'acha asbara li* — the measurers (those who professionally calculate *techumin*) explained this to me. Rashi identifies these *meshu'ach'in* as the men who measure out the *techumin* for communities. Their explanation begins: *mishkachat lah be-mata d'hava trei alfi a-trei alfi* — take a city that is 2,000 by 2,000 *amot*. The *techum*, extending 2,000 *amot* in every direction, makes the entire area — including city and everything surrounding it — 8,000 by 8,000. To calculate areas conveniently, divide the total space into units of 1,000 by 1,000 each. The entire span is eight units across in each direction, giving 64 such units in total. But the relevant counting proceeds differently: the city itself occupies a 2,000 by 2,000 square, giving four units. The band of *migrash* (1,000 *amot* thick) surrounds the city, giving eight more units on the four sides (two per side), plus four corner units — twelve units for the *migrash*. The *sadot* and the remaining *techum* occupy the rest.

### Math Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא מִגְרָשׁ רְבִיעַ? טְפֵי מִתִּלְתָּא נִינְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: According to this calculation, how is **the open space found** to be **one quarter** of the area? **It is more than one-third.** The entire area of the extended boundary is thirty-two million square cubits and the open space occupies twelve million square cubits, which is more than one-third of the total area of the extended boundary.

The problem is immediate: twelve out of thirty-six (the total of city units plus surrounding units, before considering the outermost *techum* corners) is not one-quarter but one-third. Even when the four units of the city are added to yield a total of thirty-six units, twelve out of thirty-six is still one-third — *tilsha havei*, not *revia*. So how can the *Baraita* claim it is a quarter?

### Circular City Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְיתִי אַרְבְּעָה דְמָתָא, שְׁדִי עֲלַיְיהוּ. אַכַּתִּי תִּילְתָּא הָוֵי! מִי סָבְרַתְּ בְּרִיבּוּעָא קָאָמַר? בְּעִיגּוּלָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Bring the four** million square cubits **of the city** itself and **add them** to the area of the limit, and you will arrive at the correct ratio. The Gemara asks: The opens space **is still one-third,** as the total area of the city and its extended boundary is thirty-six million square cubits, and the area of the open space is twelve million square cubits. The Gemara answers: **Do you think** that this *halakha* **was stated** with regard **to a square** city? **It was** in fact **stated** with regard **to a round** city. The open space beyond the city is also round; however, the total extended boundary is squared, so that the total area of a round city with a diameter of two thousand cubits and its extended boundary is thirty-six million square cubits.

The Gemara provides the resolution: *mi savrat be-rivua kamart? be-igula kamart* — who said we are talking about a square city? We are talking about a circular city. The crucial distinction is that only the *techum* — the outermost boundary — is squared off. The city itself and the *migrash* surrounding it are circular. The squaring applies only to the final 1,000 *amot* of *sadot* and *techum*. This is a significant *chiddush*: the presumption that squaring applies at every level is incorrect; it is applied only at the outermost stage.

### Square-Circle Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּמָּה מְרוּבָּע יָתֵר עַל הֶעָגוֹל? רְבִיעַ, דַּל רְבִיעַ מִינַּיְיהוּ — פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תִּשְׁעָה. וְתִשְׁעָה מִתְּלָתִין וְשִׁיתָּא, רִיבְעָא הָוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the calculation: **How much larger is** the area of **a square than** the area of **the circle? One quarter. Subtract one quarter from** the twelve million square cubits of open space, and **nine** million square cubits **are left; and nine is** precisely **one quarter of thirty-six.**

The Gemara then invokes the geometric ratio: *ve-kamma meruba yater al ha-igul? revia* — how much larger is a square than a circle inscribed within it? One-quarter larger. This follows from the approximation that *pi* equals three. If the diameter of the circle is two (making the radius one), the area of the circle is *pi r²* = three. The area of the circumscribed square is four. The ratio is three to four — the square is one-quarter larger than the circle. The same ratio holds for circumference to perimeter: the circumference is 2*pi*r = six, while the perimeter of the square is eight. Thus the square exceeds the circle by one-quarter in both area and linear measure.

Applying this to the calculation: the city circle has a radius of 2,000 *amot* (since the city is 2,000 by 2,000 in diameter). Its area is *pi* times four million — approximately twelve million square *amot* (using *pi* = three). The inner city circle alone (radius 1,000) has area three million. The *migrash* circle, then, has area twelve minus three = nine million. But the outer square of everything is thirty-six million (6,000 by 6,000). Reducing the *migrash* from nine to nine times three-quarters (since a circle is three-quarters of the square that contains it) yields a *migrash* of effectively nine units out of thirty-six — but wait, the Gemara's calculation proceeds as follows: take the thirty-six units of the full square, remove one-quarter (nine units) because the city and *migrash* are circular rather than square, leaving twenty-seven; but the *migrash* portion before that reduction was twelve, reduced by a quarter to nine. And nine out of thirty-six is exactly one-quarter. *Dal revia mina-ihu, pashu lei tisha, ve-tisha mi-telatin ve-shisha revia havya* — remove a quarter from the thirty-six, you get nine, and nine out of thirty-six is one-quarter.

### Smaller City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ נָמֵי בְּמָתָא דְּהָוְיָא אַלְפָא בְּאַלְפָא. תְּחוּמִין כַּמָּה הָווּ? תְּמָנְיָא. קְרָנוֹת כַּמָּה הָוֵי? שִׁיתְּסַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: You will also find** that the open space is one quarter of the total area **in a city that is a thousand** cubits **by a thousand** cubits. **How many** cubits **is the** extended **boundary** of the city without the corners? It is **eight** million square cubits. Additional areas are appended along each side of the city and extending two thousand cubits beyond the city itself. Each of these areas is two thousand cubits by one thousand cubits, for a total area of two million square cubits. Since there are four of these zones, their total area is eight million square cubits. **How many** cubits **are the corners?** They are **sixteen** million square cubits, as squares of two thousand cubits by two thousand cubits are added to each of the four corners.

Abaye confirms that the same result holds when the city has a radius of only 1,000 *amot* (a city 1,000 by 1,000). *Tchumim kama havay? Temanya* — the *techum* squares yield eight units. *Kranot kama havay? Shitsar* — the corners yield sixteen. The total outside the city is twenty-four units; the *migrash* portion, before the circular correction, is eight of those. But twenty-four minus the circular correction: *dal revia mina-ihu, pashu lei shita, ve-shita mi-kaf-dalet revia havay* — remove one-quarter from twenty-four, you get six, and six out of twenty-four is one-quarter. Thus whether the city is 1,000 or 2,000 *amot* in radius, the same ratio holds.

The upshot of the entire discussion is a ruling of considerable geometric subtlety: the squaring of the *reibua olam* applies only to the *techum* — the outermost layer. The city and the *migrash* retain their circular form. It is only when computing the extended *techum* boundary that we impose the square and gain the corners. This is what makes the *migrash* exactly one-quarter of the whole.
