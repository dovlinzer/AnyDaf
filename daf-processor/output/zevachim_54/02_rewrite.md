# Zevachim 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Missing Base & Blood

### Territory Division

The Gemara on 54a opens by revisiting a territorial problem introduced at the end of the previous daf. The *mizbe'ach* in the *Mikdash* was required to sit entirely within the portion of *Binyamin*. Yet the tribe of *Yehudah*'s territory protruded slightly into the east and south, encroaching right around the altar. This was not merely the *Har Habayit* or the outer precincts: *Yehudah*'s portion included the full *azarah* — the eleven *amot* where *Yisrael* stood and the eleven *amot* of the *Kohanim* — right up to the point where the altar began. From there, *Binyamin*'s territory commenced and continued through the *Kodesh* and *Kodesh Hakodashim*. Because *Yehudah* intruded slightly from the east and south around the altar, those sides lacked the protruding *yesod* — the cubit-wide base ledge that ran around the bottom of the *mizbe'ach*. That, in brief, is where the Gemara left off.

### Bird Offering Challenge

The Gemara now poses a *meitivei* — a challenge from a *braita*. The *braita* asks: *olat ha'of keitzad hi na'aseit* — how is the bird *olah* performed? The *Kohen* would perform *melikah*, severing the bird's head from the nape of the neck with his fingernail, completely cutting through both the trachea and the esophagus, and then *u'mematzeh es damah al kir hamizbe'ach* — he would squeeze out the blood against the wall of the altar. The bird *olah* was performed at the first corner encountered, namely the southeast corner — precisely the corner opposite which there was no *yesod* below. The Gemara presses: *ve'i amrat lo hayah lo yesod b'avira b'alma hu d'ka'avid* — if there was no *yesod* there, is the blood being squeezed out into mere empty air? The assumption underlying the challenge is that just as standard *zrikat hadam* requires the blood to go *keneged hayesod* — opposite a base below — so too the squeezing out of the blood of the *olat ha'of* requires some structural element below to receive it.

### Rashi: Blood Target

Rashi's reading of the difficulty requires careful attention. One might ask: why is there a problem at all? After all, the *Kohen* performing the *olat ha'of* stood at the top of the altar, on the *soveiv* — the walk-around ledge — and the blood would strike the wall of the altar rather than flying into empty air. Rashi addresses this by noting that the issue arises specifically when the blood is squeezed out in the lower half of the altar's upper section — above the red line demarcating where the *yesod* blood applications go, but within the lower five *amot* portion. In that position, the blood runs down and there is no *yesod* to stop it from flowing to the ground below. Rashi is not treating this as a formal *halacha* requiring a *yesod* per se; rather, his concern is that the blood must have something to strike and be received by — it cannot simply cascade down into empty space. This distinction is precisely what Tosafot seizes upon: if the *olat ha'of* does not technically require a *yesod*, why does it need a protrusion to catch the blood at all?

### Airspace Solution

The Gemara's answer is provided by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *eima hachi hitnu — avir deBinyamin vekarka deYehuda*. This was the arrangement: the ground in that southeastern area belonged to *Yehudah*, but the airspace above it belonged to *Binyamin*. The blood of the *olat ha'of*, squeezed out against the corner of the altar, was being placed within *Binyamin*'s airspace — and that was sufficient. Reading this answer carefully, the real thrust of the Gemara's question becomes clearer. The concern was not primarily about the technical requirement of a *yesod*; it was about the *avodah* of the *Mikdash* being performed within the zone of *Binyamin*. If the blood was squeezed out at the southeastern corner at the lower portion of the altar, it was being placed in *Yehudah*'s airspace — since the ground there was *Yehudah*'s, and so, on the original assumption, was the air above it. The answer is that *Yehudah* effectively ceded its air rights: even though the ground was *Yehudah*'s, the airspace above belonged to *Binyamin*, making the *avodah* valid.

### Rashi's Protrusion

Rashi adds something to this picture that does not appear in the Gemara's text itself. He writes that *ve'ika lemeimar* — one may further say — that they fashioned a small protrusion, *belita ktana*, just at that one corner, positioned more than three *tefachim* above the ground (since within three *tefachim* of the ground counts as ground itself). This small ledge would catch the blood as it ran down so that none of it would fall onto *Yehudah*'s ground. Rashi is careful to note, however, that this protrusion does not constitute a *yesod* and does not render that corner eligible for the *zrikat hadam* of offerings requiring a *yesod*. It is not a *yesod*; it is merely a practical device to ensure none of the blood falls to *Yehudah*'s earth. Tosafot pushes back: if the *olat ha'of* does not require a *yesod*, why does it matter if the blood reaches *Yehudah*'s ground below, given that the *avodah* has already been completed when the blood struck the wall? Rashi's addition implies that it was important that none of the blood enter *Yehudah*'s zone at all — though the Gemara itself does not articulate a clear *halacha* to that effect. That is Rashi's elaboration, and it is the subject of a lengthy Tosafot.

## Rav vs. Levi

### Rav: No Structure

The Gemara now turns to a fundamental dispute between *Rav* and *Levi* about the nature of the missing *yesod*. Rav says *be-vinyan* — literally, in terms of construction. The protruding base simply did not exist on the south and east sides. There was no physical ledge there whatsoever.

### Levi: Off-Limits Base

*Levi* takes a different position: *be-damim* — in terms of blood. The altar was architecturally complete and symmetrical on all sides, with the *yesod* protruding fully around the perimeter. The difference was purely functional: because that portion of the base fell within *Yehudah*'s territory, it was simply off-limits for any *avodat hadam*. The structure existed; only the blood application was prohibited there. Under Levi's reading, the altar looked perfectly normal to any observer. A *Kohen* standing before it would see a full base all around — he simply knew that the southeastern *yesod* was not to receive blood.

Rav's Aramaic translation of the verse *uvein kesafav shachen* — "God will dwell between his shoulders" — reflects his position: *be-achsanteih yitbana madbecha*, in *Binyamin*'s portion the *mizbe'ach* will be built. This means no part of the altar's structure occupied *Yehudah*'s space. Levi's *targum*, by contrast, renders the verse as *be-achsanteih yitbanei mikdesha* — in his portion the sanctuary will be built — which he then glosses as *makom mekudash le-damim*, the place sanctified for blood. The altar physically extended into *Yehudah*'s portion, but only *Binyamin*'s portion was sanctified as the site of blood application. The Gemara proceeds to test these two positions against a series of proofs.

### Dimension Proof

The first challenge comes from a *braita*: *yesod hayah mehalech al pnei kol hatzafon ve-al pnei kol hama'arav — ochel badarom amah achat uvamizrach amah achat*. The base ran along the full length of the north and the full length of the west, and then it "consumed" — *ochel* — one *amah* in the south and one *amah* in the east. The plain reading of *ochel* is that it physically reached into those sides by one *amah*, leaving the remainder of the south and east without a protruding base. This supports Rav: the actual structure was absent. Levi responds by reinterpreting *ochel*: it means that only those portions of the base "consumed" blood — i.e., were eligible to receive *zrikat hadam*. The rest existed but was off-limits.

The second challenge: the *braita* describes the *mizbe'ach* as thirty-two by thirty-two. But if the base does not protrude uniformly — if, as Rav holds, it is missing on two sides — then the altar's footprint is not truly thirty-two by thirty-two. Measured accurately, the base would be closer to thirty-one by thirty-two or thirty-one by thirty-one if one accounts for the missing protrusions. The Gemara resolves this by noting that the thirty-two by thirty-two measurement was taken from two specific sides — *hacha askinan min hatzad* — and is not meant as a precise statement of total area.

### Ramp Overhang

The most architecturally interesting challenge concerns the ramp. The Gemara states *nimtza porei'ach amah al yesod v'amah al sovev* — the ramp is found to extend (*poreiach*) one *amah* over the *yesod* and one *amah* over the *soveiv*. To understand why this is a challenge to Rav, one must picture the structure. The *mizbe'ach* stepped inward as it rose: the base was thirty-two at the bottom (as measured across the top), and it inset by one *amah* on each side as it went up, yielding a thirty *amah* structure at the middle level, and so on. A *Kohen* walking from the ground at the foot of the altar up to the top would traverse thirty-two horizontal *amot* because the top overhangs the base by one *amah* on each side.

The ramp ascended from the south and sat against the southern face. If, as Rav holds, there was no physical *yesod* protruding on the south, then the ramp need only have been thirty-one *amot* at its base, overhanging by one *amah* over where the *soveiv* began — it would not need to overhang a non-existent *yesod*. So why does the Gemara describe the ramp as overhanging two *amot* — one over the *yesod* and one over the *soveiv*? Rav's supporters answer: *eima keneged amat yesod ve'al amat sovev* — the language means not that it hovered over an actual *yesod* but that it hovered over the *amah where a yesod should have been*. The ramp's base was thirty, and there was a gap of one *amah* between the base of the ramp and where the *yesod* would have protruded had it existed; then the ramp extended a further *amah* over the *soveiv*. The answer is admittedly strained — one wonders why they would not simply build the ramp base out to thirty-one and eliminate the gap entirely — but that is the Gemara's response under Rav's position.

## Altar Construction

### Layer-by-Layer Build

The Gemara now brings a *braita* that *Levi* himself taught — *detani Levi, keitzad bonin et hamizbe'ach* — how would they construct the altar? This *braita* is significant because it supports Levi's own position: the construction it describes is fully symmetrical, with the *yesod* extending uniformly on all sides.

### Base Layer

The process began by bringing a *malben* — a wooden frame or mold — thirty-two by thirty-two *amot* and one *amah* high. Into this frame they poured *chilukei avanim mefulamot* — small wet stones, whether large or small — which were *avanim shelemot*, whole, unworked stones. They then mixed together *sid*, *chunya* (a type of molten lead for hardening), and *zefet* (tar), forming a cement compound, which was then poured in among the stones. This produced the *mekom yesod* — the base layer.

### Middle & Upper Layers

Upon this, a second frame was brought: thirty by thirty *amot* and five *amot* high, again filled with whole stones and cement. The inset from thirty-two to thirty represented the one-*amah* ledge on each side — the *yesod*. Above that, a third frame: twenty-eight by twenty-eight *amot* and three *amot* high. The inset from thirty to twenty-eight produced the *soveiv* ledge. The top surface of this third layer was *mekom hama'aracha* — where the fire and wood for the offerings were arranged.

### Horn Construction

Finally, a frame one *amah* by one *amah* — a cubic *amah* — was formed, filled with *zefet* and *konya* and smoothed, and *vezehu keren* — this became a horn. The same process was repeated for each of the four corners: *ve-chen le-chol keren va-keren*.

### Whole Stone Problem

The full symmetry of this construction — base of thirty-two by thirty-two on all sides — supports Levi's position that the altar physically extended uniformly all around. The Gemara, however, raises an objection. Given that the construction uses a thirty-two by thirty-two frame and the next level is set in by one *amah* on each side, how was the inset created without chiseling the stones? The Torah requires *avanim shelemot* — whole, unworked stones — which means the builder cannot chisel or cut them to produce the recessed ledge.

### Wooden Inserts

The solution: *de-machis midi mi-tusei ve-shakil lei* — they inserted a piece of wood inside the frame before pouring in the stones and cement, and then removed it afterward. By placing a wooden insert along the inner perimeter at the appropriate dimensions, the cement and stones would fill only the remaining space. Once hardened, the insert was pulled out, leaving the desired recess without any stone having been cut. Tosafot raises the obvious question: why construct a frame of thirty-two by thirty-two and insert a shaped piece inside, rather than simply building the frame in exactly the desired final shape to begin with? The *shiur* notes this question without a ready resolution.

## Hollow Horns

### Porous Stones

The discussion of wooden inserts is not just about the ledges — it extends to the horns themselves, and it is here that the technique becomes indispensable. Rav Kahana states: *avanim shel kranot chalulot hayu* — the stones forming the horns were hollow. Rashi explains that the horns contained internal holes leading into a hollow interior, so that when the *Kohen* applied blood to the horn, the blood would be drawn into those holes and absorbed within the hollow space.

### Basin Reference

The proof is from the verse *u-mal'u ke-mizrak ke-zaviyot mizbe'ach* — "they shall be filled like a basin, like the corners of the altar." The comparison to a basin — a container that holds and collects — implies that the corners were themselves receptacles. They had holes and a hollow interior that would collect and hold the blood applied to them.

### Hollow Problem

This creates an immediate difficulty. The horns were constructed from *avanim shelemot* — whole, unworked stones. How could one create a hollow interior within a horn made of whole stones poured into a mold? Drilling or carving would violate the prohibition against working the stones.

### Insert Solution

The answer mirrors the technique used for the ledges: *ela de-machis midi mi-tusei ve-shakil lei*. Within the one-*amah* cubic mold for the horn, they placed an insert that occupied the interior space. The cement was poured around it, forming only the outer shell. Once the structure hardened, the insert was removed, leaving a hollow interior. Rashi adds that small sticks or rods were also placed in the mold, protruding in such a way that the hardened cement would set around them, and when the sticks were withdrawn, holes were left in the horn's surface — openings through which blood could flow into the hollow interior. This is what *de-machis midi mi-tusei ve-shakil lei* means in the context of the horns.

## Locating the Altar

### David & Samuel

The Gemara now shifts to a striking *derashah* of Rava, introduced by the verse *va-yelech David ve-Shmuel va-yeshvu be-Nayot ba-Ramah* — David and Shmuel went and dwelt in *Nayot* in *Ramah*. The Gemara reads this as: *v'oskin b'noyoshel olam* — they were occupied with the *hilchos Beis Hamikdash*, involving themselves in questions concerning the most beautiful place in the world. Specifically, they were trying to determine where the *Beis Hamikdash* was to be built.

### High Ground

The search was grounded in a *derashah* from the verse *v'kamta v'alita el hamakom asher yivchar Hashem* — you shall rise up and go to the place that God has chosen. The Gemara reads this not metaphorically but literally and topographically: since anyone in *Eretz Yisrael* is told to "rise up" when traveling to the *Beis Hamikdash*, the *Beis Hamikdash* must sit at the highest elevation in the entire land — *melamed sheBeis Hamikdash gavoah mikol Eretz Yisrael*.

### Boundary Search

*Lo havu yad'ei duchteih heicha* — they did not know where this highest place was. Since no topographical survey was available, they turned to *Sefer Yehoshua* and studied the boundary descriptions of the *Shevatim*. Throughout the text, the boundaries are marked by the language *vayered v'ala hagvul* — the border descends and ascends — indicating that the terrain rose and fell throughout the land.

### Benjamin's Boundary

When they reached the boundaries of *Binyamin*, they found a striking pattern: *v'ala ktiv, vayered lo ktiv* — the boundary is described as ascending, but the corresponding descent is never written. Rashi refines this observation: the description of *Yehudah*'s northern boundary around *Yerushalayim* uses the word *vaya'al* — it ascends — specifically when it reaches the area of *Gei Ben Hinnom*, adjacent to *Har Habayit*, and thereafter never again says *vaya'al* as the boundary continues. Similarly, when the southern boundary of *Binyamin* is described, the text records *vaya'al* reaching up to *Yerushalayim* but never records a *vayered* — a descent — afterward. The convergence of these readings pointed them to the southeastern area of *Binyamin*'s portion, bordering *Yehudah*, right at *Yerushalayim* and *Har Habayit*: *amri shma mina hacha hu mekomo* — they concluded, here is its place.

### Ein Eitam Rejected

Having identified the general area, they considered *Ein Eitam* — a location a few kilometers from *Yerushalayim*, somewhat higher in elevation, where there was apparently a wellspring that fed an aqueduct into the city. This was *demidlei* — a slightly elevated site. However, they rejected it, reasoning *nisasibe porsah* — let us descend a bit — drawing on the verse *uvein kesafav shachen*, "God dwells between his shoulders." The image is not of the topmost point — the head — but of the space between the shoulders: elevated, but not the absolute peak.

### Dual Border

A second argument reinforced the choice of *Yerushalayim* over *Ein Eitam*: *gemiri de-Sanhedrin be-chelko deYehuda* — it is a received tradition that the *Sanhedrin* was to sit in *Yehudah*'s portion, as the verse states *lo yasur shevet miYehudah umechokek miben raglav*. At the same time, *veshechinah be-chelko deBinyamin* — the divine presence was to rest in *Binyamin*'s portion. These two institutions had to be immediately adjacent to one another — the *Lishkat HaGazit* in *Yehudah*'s space just before the *azarah*, and the *Mikdash* proper in *Binyamin*'s space just beyond. Had they chosen *Ein Eitam*, which is not situated at the precise border between *Yehudah* and *Binyamin*, the two would have been *mitpalgi tuvei* — greatly separated. Therefore *mutav de-nisasibe porsah* — it was better to descend slightly — in order to place the *Beis Hamikdash* exactly at the junction of the two portions.

### Doeg's Jealousy

The Gemara observes: *ve'al davar ze niskanah Doeg HaEdomi* — it was over this matter that Doeg HaEdomi became consumed with jealousy. In the rabbinic imagination, *Doeg* was a formidable *talmid chacham* and a rival of *David*. His jealousy here was not territorial but intellectual: *David* and *Shmuel* had managed to derive the precise location of the *Beis Hamikdash* from a close reading of *pesukim*, besting *Doeg* in the domain of *Torah* scholarship. The verse *ki kin'at beisecha achaltani* — "the jealousy of Your house consumed me" — is read as referring to this: the jealousy over discovering where God's house belonged was what consumed *Doeg*.

### David's Vow

The Gemara then cites the verses of *Tehillim*: *zechor Hashem leDavid et kol anuso — asher nishba laHashem* — David vowed that he would not enter his own tent, would not allow sleep to his eyes or slumber to his eyelids, *ad emtza makom laHashem* — until he found a place for God. And then: *hinei shemanuha be'Efratah, metzanuha bisde ya'ar* — "we have heard of it in *Efratah*, we have found it in the fields of the forest."

### Human Effort

The Gemara interprets these verses as follows: *be'Efratah ze Yehoshua* — "in *Efratah*" refers to *Yehoshua*, who came from *Efrayim*. *Metzanuha bisde ya'ar* — "we found it in the fields of the forest" — refers to *Binyamin*, described elsewhere as a predatory wolf (*ze'ev yitrof*), and wolves dwell in forests. Thus, by searching *Sefer Yehoshua* and the portion of *Binyamin*, they discovered the location.

There is a broader religious point embedded here, drawn from the verse *lishichno tidreshu uva'ta shammah* — you shall seek out God's dwelling and come there. The plain sense seems to say that once you know where the *Mikdash* is, you simply go there — so why does it say "seek it out"? A *midrash* answers: one must first apply human intellectual effort to determine where the place should be; only then does the *navi* come to confirm and ratify that determination. The entire edifice of the *Beis Hamikdash* embodies this principle. God's presence fills all of existence — *bechol makom asher azkir et shemi* — and the concentration of that presence into one specific, sanctified location is itself contingent on human effort. *David* and *Shmuel* labored to identify and prepare that place; the prophetic confirmation followed that effort. The notion that one does not simply receive the location of the *Mikdash* from on high — that one must *darsheihu*, seek it out, and only then receive confirmation — reflects the deeper theological point that creating a *makom kadosh* for God requires human initiative before divine response.

## Asham Overview

### Kodesh Kodashim

The Gemara now transitions to the next *mishnah*. Having completed the treatment of the *chatat* and the *olah* among the *kodshei kodashim*, the *mishnah* turns to the remaining categories: *zivchei shlamai tzibur ve'ashamot* — the communal *shelamim* and the *ashamot*. While individual *shelamim* are *kodshei kalim*, the communal *shelamim* (*shlamai tzibur*) occupy the status of *kodshei kodashim*. Together with the various *ashamot*, they constitute the remaining *kodshei kodashim* to be discussed.

### Six Asham Types

The *mishnah* enumerates six types of *asham*: *asham gezelot* — the guilt offering of one who steals and then swears falsely; *asham me'ilot* — the guilt offering of one who derives benefit from *hekdesh* in violation of the laws of *me'ilah*; *asham shifcha charufa* — the guilt offering brought by a man who has relations with a maidservant who is half-freed and half-betrothed; *asham nazir* — the guilt offering of a *nazir* who became *tamei* and must restart his *nezirut*; *asham metzora* — the guilt offering brought as part of the purification process of a *metzora*; and *asham taluy* — the "suspended" guilt offering brought when a person is uncertain whether he committed a sin that would otherwise require a *chatat*, and brings an *asham* to provide atonement pending clarification.

### Common Asham Rules

All six *ashamot* share a set of common *halachot*: *shchitatam batzafon* — they are slaughtered in the north of the *azarah*; *vekiblutam bechli batzafon* — the blood is received in a vessel in the north; *ve-damam te'un shtei matanot shehen arba* — their blood requires two applications that are four, exactly as with the *olah*. These shared rules unify the otherwise diverse range of *asham* situations into a single halachic category.

### Asham Consumption

After the blood work, the flesh of all *ashamot* is *ne'echalos lifnim min hakla'im* — eaten within the curtains of the *azarah*. It is consumed *be-chol ma'achal* — in any manner of preparation, unlike the *korban Pesach* which is restricted to roasting. It may be eaten *le-yom va-layla* — during the day of offering and the following night — though rabbinically the limit is *ad chatzot*, until midnight. And it is restricted to *zichriei kehunah*, male *Kohanim* only, not female members of priestly households. With these rules established, the *mishnah*'s treatment of the *ashamot* is set to continue.
