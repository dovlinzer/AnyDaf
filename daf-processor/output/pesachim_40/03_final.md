# Pesachim 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chalitah & Chametz

### Gebrochts Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא לִיחְלוֹט אִינִישׁ תְּרֵי חִיטֵּי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, דִּילְמָא אָזְלָא חֲדָא וְיָתְבָה בְּצִירְיָא דַחֲבֶרְתַּהּ, וְלָא סָלֵיק לְהוּ דִּיקּוּלָא דְמַיָּא מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחָתָא, וְאָתֵי לִידֵי חִימּוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** A person should not scald **two wheat grains in boiling water** at the same time, **lest one move and rest in the slit [*tzirya*] of the other,** thereby **preventing the column [*dikkula*] of** boiling **water from entering** all **four sides** of the grain, **and** the grain **will come to** a state of **leavening.**

Today's daf is Pesachim 40, and we begin at the very bottom of 39b. The preceding discussion had moved from *matzah* and *maror* back to concerns about *chametz*: specifically, the practice of putting grain in water to feed chickens, and the various worries about fermentation that arise from bringing grain into contact with liquid. The *gemara* established the important principle that once grain has been baked, roasted, or even scalded, it can no longer become *chametz*. At the same time, the *gemara* closed with a ruling that a certain type of roasted grain should not be added as a thickener to soup, because it may not have been roasted thoroughly enough and could still ferment — and that concern is precisely the concern that underlies *gebrochts*. The *gebrochts* custom extends even to baked *matzos*, reflecting an anxiety that perhaps the process was not one hundred percent effective and some residual concern remains. This is the theme the *gemara* now continues.

### Rav Yosef's Scalding

*Amar Rav Yosef*: a person should not scald — that is, submerge grain in boiling hot water as a means of cooking it rapidly before it has a chance to become *chametz* — two kernels of wheat simultaneously. Why? *Dilma azla chada v'yatva b'tzira'ei d'chaverta v'lo salik lehu dikula d'maya* — perhaps one kernel will lodge itself in the crack of the other, and the stream of boiling water will fail to penetrate that area, leaving part of a kernel exposed to water that never reached the temperature needed to prevent fermentation. In other words, the two overlapping kernels create a kind of *chatzitza*, a barrier, that defeats the very mechanism on which *chalitah* relies. This makes it clear that *chalitah* demands that every kernel be completely separate from every other — and one can readily understand, in light of such concerns, why later authorities became reluctant to permit *chalitah* at all.

Indeed, the *Shulchan Aruch* rules that nowadays we cannot trust anyone to perform *chalitah* correctly, and therefore we may not rely on it as a method of avoiding *chametz* — if one performed *chalitah*, one must still assume the grain became *chametz*. It is worth noting, however, that one of the later *Acharonim* — the *Chai Adam*, if memory serves — discusses the exceptional historical circumstance in which Jews were required to prepare food for quartered troops over *Pesach* and had virtually no alternatives. In such straits, he recommends relying on *chalitah*. This is not a routine leniency; it is an acknowledgment that in the absence of any other option, one may fall back on what the *gemara* considered theoretically sound, even if current practice rejects it. The underlying issue, it seems, is not a genuine uncertainty about technique — it does not take great expertise to ensure that water is boiling — but rather a heightened sensitivity to *chametz* that later generations adopted as a matter of religious temperament rather than technical incapacity.

### Abaye's Roasting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא לִיחְרוֹךְ אִינִישׁ תְּרֵי שֻׁבְלֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, דִּילְמָא נָפְקִי מַיָּא מֵהַאי וּבָלַע אִידַּךְ, וְאָתְיָא לִידֵי חִימּוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said: A person** may **not roast two stalks together, lest water leave one and be absorbed by the other, and it come to** be **leavened.**

*V'amar Abaye*: Abaye adds a related caution regarding dry roasting. One should not roast two ears of wheat — two stalks with the kernels still on them — next to one another simultaneously, even without the involvement of any external water. Why? *Dilma nafik maya mehai u'ba l'idach* — perhaps there will be water runoff from one, since grain has an inherent moisture content, and that runoff will be absorbed by the other. *V'ati lidei chimutz* — and it may come to ferment.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָא נָמֵי, דִּילְמָא נָפְקִי מַיָּא מֵהַאי וּבָלַע אִידַּךְ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so,** one should not **even** roast **one** stalk, **lest the** liquid **leave one end** of the stalk **and be absorbed by** its **other end!**

Rava challenged this position sharply. *Ei hachi afilu chada nami* — if that is the concern, then one should not even be able to roast a single stalk. Why? *Dilma nafik mehai reisha u'ba l'idach reisha* — the water will run off from the top of the stalk and be absorbed by the bottom, or from one end to another within the same ear. If moisture content is the problem, there is no obvious reason why even a single ear of grain should be safe. Rava's point is that Abaye's concern, taken seriously, renders any roasting of wheat kernels impossible — a conclusion that seems untenable.

### Mei Peirot Doctrine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מֵי פֵירוֹת נִינְהוּ, וּמֵי פֵירוֹת אֵינָן מַחְמִיצִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** One need not be concerned about this possibility, as **it is** considered **fruit juice, and fruit juice does not leaven** grain.

*Ela amar Rava* — Rava resolves the problem differently. He says: *mei peirot ninhu* — the water runoff from grain kernels is *mei peirot*, the liquid intrinsic to a fruit or grain, and *mei peirot einan machmitzin* — *mei peirot* does not cause fermentation. The overwhelming consensus of the *Rishonim* and *Poskim* is that grain mixed only with its own intrinsic liquid, without the addition of water, does not become *chametz* at all. Reading Rava through that lens, his argument is that water running off from wheat kernels during roasting is not water in any problematic sense — it is the grain's own juice, and it cannot produce *chametz*. The concern Abaye raised simply does not exist.

What, then, was Abaye's position? The deeper question may be what qualifies as *mei peirot*. Fruit juice — orange juice, for example — contains water as a component, but is fundamentally something other than water. Grain moisture, by contrast, is largely H₂O that was absorbed from rain and the environment and is now being released. Whether absorbed water that exits the kernel retains the character of *mei peirot* or reverts to plain water is, arguably, the crux of the debate between Abaye and Rava.

### Flow vs. Absorption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵהַהִיא, דְּכׇל אַגַּב מֵדָלַיְיהוּ לָא מַחְמְצִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And Abaye retracted** his opinion with regard to **that** *halakha* of stalks, as he maintains **that anything that is absorbing** liquid will **not become leavened** unless it is fully soaked in water.

*V'hadar beh Abaye* — the *gemara* records that Abaye reversed his position. He does not, however, accept Rava's *mei peirot* reasoning. His reversal rests on a different principle: *d'chol agav madleihu* — as long as water is still flowing and running off, *lo machmitz*, it does not ferment. The key distinction is between water that flows across or off a surface and water that is absorbed and sits in contact with the grain. This principle has a useful parallel in the laws of *kashring* meat: one salts meat on an incline precisely so that the blood drawn out runs off rather than pooling beneath it. Even if the runoff from an upper piece strikes a lower piece, as long as it continues flowing away, we do not assume it is absorbed. Abaye applies the same logic here: even if the water released during roasting drips onto adjacent parts of the same ear or neighboring kernels, as long as it is in motion and draining away, it will not be absorbed and will not produce *chametz*.

### Pot Orientation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי חַצְבָּא דַאֲבִישֻׁנָא, סְחִיפָא — שְׁרֵי, זְקִיפָא — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **As Abaye said:** With regard to **this jug used for** drying the stalks by means of **roasting,** if it is **inverted it is permitted,** as the liquid that is discharged from one stalk will not be absorbed by the other stalks. However, if the vessel is **upright it is prohibited,** as the liquid retained in the vessel might be absorbed by the other stalks and leaven them.

How do we know Abaye reversed himself? Because Abaye himself subsequently taught: *hai chatzva de'avishna* — a pot used for roasting grain: if it is placed upside down in the oven (*sechifa*), it is permitted, because whatever moisture runs off the grain drains away freely. If placed right-side up (*zekifa*), it is forbidden, because the released moisture will collect at the bottom of the pot and pose a *chametz* concern.

The very fact that Abaye permits the upside-down configuration reveals that he has changed his mind — after all, even in an inverted pot with multiple kernels, the moisture drips from one kernel onto another as it flows downward. Since Abaye permits this, he must now accept that flowing water does not cause absorption. What evidently prompted the reversal was Rava's challenge: if you cannot roast two stalks together you cannot roast even one stalk, and since nobody has ever questioned the basic permissibility of roasting grain, the concern must not operate as Abaye initially feared.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ זְקִיפָא — נָמֵי שְׁרֵי, מֵי פֵירוֹת נִינְהוּ, וּמֵי פֵירוֹת אֵינָן מַחְמִיצִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even** if the jug is **upright, it is also permitted. This** liquid **is** considered **fruit juice, and fruit juice does not leaven** grain.

Rava, for his part, rules that even an upright pot is permitted — *mei peirot ninhu*, *mei peirot ein machmitzin* — because whatever collects at the bottom is still *mei peirot* and cannot ferment. The practical upshot is that Abaye permits the upside-down configuration for reasons of flowing water, while Rava permits even the upright configuration on the grounds of *mei peirot*.

## Lesisa (Bran Removal)

### Lesisa Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין לוֹתְתִין שְׂעוֹרִים בַּפֶּסַח. וְאִם לָתַת, נִתְבַּקְּעוּ — אֲסוּרוֹת, לֹא נִתְבַּקְּעוּ — מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One** may **not soak barley** in water **on Passover** to make it easier to remove the chaff from the grain. **And if one did soak** barley grain and **it split, it is prohibited.** If **it did not split, it is permitted.**

*Tanu rabbanan*: the Rabbis taught — *ein losesim se'orim be-Pesach* — one may not perform *lesisa* with barley on *Pesach*. *Lesisa* refers to the practice of moistening the outer shell of a grain kernel and rubbing or working it to remove the bran, thereby producing a cleaner, finer grain. This process inevitably brings water into contact with the grain and creates a *chametz* concern. Before proceeding, it is worth clarifying what actually defines *chametz*: from the moment flour comes into contact with water, if it is not baked within the requisite time — quantified for practical purposes as eighteen minutes — it becomes *chametz*. That eighteen-minute figure derives from a *gemara*, but the *gemara* itself acknowledges that fermentation is not so neatly quantifiable. The visual signs the *gemara* uses to detect fermentation — whether the dough has expanded, whether cracks have appeared — reflect a more nuanced reality. Moreover, a kernel of wheat or barley is very different from flour: water applied to a kernel's surface does not necessarily penetrate it and initiate fermentation in the same way it would with ground flour.

### Cracking Test

*Ve'im latat* — if one did perform *lesisa*, how does one determine whether a *chametz* problem has resulted? *Nitbaku asurot* — if the kernel has cracked, it is forbidden, because cracking indicates that water penetrated sufficiently to cause expansion. *Lo nitbaku, mutarot* — if the kernels have not cracked, they are permissible, because either the water did not penetrate or it did not penetrate enough to be consequential.

### Rabbi Yosi's Vinegar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שׁוֹרָן בְּחוֹמֶץ, וְחוֹמֶץ צוֹמְתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** If one sees that the grain is expanding, **he** should **soak it in vinegar, and** the **vinegar will cause** the grain **to contract,** thereby preventing leavening.

*Rabbi Yose omer*: Rabbi Yosi offers an additional solution. Even in cases where the kernels are beginning to expand, or perhaps even after they have cracked — the text is not entirely clear on this point, and Rashi's reading suggests it may extend even to after cracking — one can throw them into vinegar, and the vinegar will impede the fermentation process, causing the kernels to contract. Rabbi Yosi is operating in a realm of practical intervention: fermentation is not an instantaneous or irreversible process, and under certain conditions it can be arrested. Whether Abaye would agree with this approach in the case where *nitbaku* has already occurred is not explicitly addressed, though it raises a fair question.

### Stringency Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר מָר עוּקְבָא: לֹא נִתְבַּקְּעוּ מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כׇּל שֶׁאִילּוּ מַנִּיחָן עַל פִּי חָבִית וְהֵן נִתְבַּקְּעוֹת מֵאֵילֵיהֶן, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: נִתְבַּקְּעוּ מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said** that **Mar Ukva said:** When the Sages were referring to a case where the barley grain split, the prohibition does **not** apply only if **it actually split** and a crack is visible. **Rather,** this is referring even to a case where **if** the barley grains **were placed on a barrel** of wine **they** would **split by themselves,** due to the effect of the wine. **And** by contrast, **Shmuel said:** This *halakha* applies only if **it actually split.**

The *gemara* then introduces a further stringency. *Lo nitbaku mamash* — when the ruling says the kernels have not cracked, this does not mean they must literally crack before becoming forbidden. *Ella kol she-ilu menicham al pi chavit ve-hein nitbaku me-aleihen* — anything that, if placed over the mouth of a barrel (Rashi understands this as a wine barrel whose vapors would interact with the kernels and cause them to crack), would crack on its own — that too is already forbidden, even before any visible cracking occurs. *Tosafot* has a different reading of the text: not *al pi chavit* but *al pi avuv*, a metal tube used for roasting grain — so that the criterion is whether applying heat to the kernels would cause them to crack. According to *Tosafot*'s reading, the test is whether the kernels have absorbed enough water that heat would split them; if so, they are already too compromised, even if no crack is yet visible.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבַד שְׁמוּאֵל עוֹבָדָא בְּדוּרָא דְּבֵי בַּר חָשׁוּ נִתְבַּקְּעוּ מַמָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel took action** in accordance with his ruling, when he was **in the village of bar Ḥashu's house.** He prohibited only barley grains that had **actually split,** but permitted those that were about to split.

Against this stringent reading, Shmuel states: *nitbaku mamash* — it is only a problem if they have literally cracked. Shmuel's father in fact rendered a practical ruling in the village of Bar Chashush consistent with this position — grain had been subjected to *lesisa* and he ruled it permissible on the grounds that the kernels had not literally cracked, without concern that they might yet do so.

### Rabbah vs. Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ לֹא יִלְתּוֹת. מַאי אִירְיָא בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ? אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא נָמֵי, דְּהָא תַּנְיָא: אֵין לוֹתְתִין שְׂעוֹרִים בַּפֶּסַח! הָכִי קָאָמַר: בַּעַל נֶפֶשׁ — אֲפִילּוּ חִיטִּין דְּשִׁרִירִי לֹא יִלְתּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: A pious person will not soak** wheat at all during Passover. The Gemara asks: **Why** discuss **particularly a pious person?** This *halakha* should apply to **everyone as well, as it was** expressly **taught** in a *baraita*: **One** may **not soak barley on Passover.** The Gemara answers that **this is** what Rabba **is saying: A pious person** will **not even soak wheat, which is firmer** than barley and less likely to split, due to the concern that it might become leavened.

*Amar Rabbah*: Rabbah introduces the category of the *baal nefesh* — one who is particularly punctilious in religious observance (Rashi glosses this as a *chasid*, and some versions of Rashi read *yarei shamayim ha-chared al nafsho*, one who is earnest about his spiritual well-being). Such a person, Rabbah says, *lo yiltos* — should not perform *lesisa* at all. The *gemara* immediately challenges this: why speak of a *baal nefesh*? Even ordinary people are prohibited from doing this — the *braita* just said *ein losesim se'orim be-Pesach*! The *gemara* explains that Rabbah means something more: *ba'al nefesh afilu chitin* — a truly punctilious person will apply this prohibition even to wheat, not only to barley. The *braita* mentioned barley specifically; wheat, having a harder kernel, is presumably less susceptible to water penetration and is technically permitted. Rabbah says that someone who wishes to be particularly careful will refrain from *lesisa* even with wheat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן: מַאן דְּצָיֵית לֵיהּ לְאַבָּא — אָכֵיל נַהֲמָא דְעִיפּוּשָׁא. דְּהָא בֵּי רַב הוּנָא לָתְתִי, וּבֵי רָבָא בַּר אָבִין לָתְתִי. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אָסוּר לִלְתּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to** Rabba: **Anyone who listens to Abba,** Rabba's first name, **will eat moldy bread** during Passover, as flour cannot be properly prepared without soaking, and therefore *matza* should not be prepared from this flour. **As in Rav Huna's house they would soak** the grain, **and in Rava bar Avin's house they would** likewise **soak** their grain. **But Rava said:** Soaking grain should not be avoided merely for reasons of piety; rather, it is absolutely **prohibited to soak** grain.

Rav Nachman is unimpressed. *Man detzayis lei l'Abba* — whoever listens to Rabbah (a respectful form of address) will wind up eating *nahama de'ipusha*, spoiled bread — that is, whole wheat *matzah* — on *Pesach*. If one takes seriously the concern about *lesisa* even with wheat, the result is that no one can ever eat fine flour *matzah*; everyone must make do with coarsely milled whole wheat. To demonstrate that this was never the practice, Rav Nachman notes that the households of Rav Huna and Rava bar Avin routinely performed *lesisa* on their wheat. Rava himself then goes a step further than Rabbah: he states outright that *lesisa* is actually prohibited — not merely something a *baal nefesh* would avoid, but categorically *assur*.

### Rav Nachman's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין לוֹתְתִין שְׂעוֹרִים בַּפֶּסַח. שְׂעוֹרִים הוּא דְּלָא, הָא חִיטֵּי — שְׁרֵי. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא חִיטִּין, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית בֵּיהּ צִירְיָא — עָיְילִי בְּהוּ מַיָּא, אֲבָל שְׂעָרֵי דְּשִׁיעִי — אֵימָא שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **One** may **not soak barley on Passover,** indicates that **it is barley that** one may **not** soak; however, it is **permitted** to soak **wheat,** how will Rava explain this *baraita*? The Gemara explains: The *baraita* is **speaking** employing the didactic style of: **Needless to say.** It should be understood as follows: **Needless to say, wheat** may not be soaked, **since** wheat grains **have a slit through which water will enter,** and the wheat will therefore expand quickly when left to soak. **However,** in the case of **barley, which is smooth** and no water will enter the grain, one might **say** that this **seems well,** i.e., it is permitted, to soak barley. Therefore, the *baraita* **teaches us** that it is prohibited to soak even barley.

One might ask: if *lesisa* is truly prohibited even with wheat, why did the *braita* single out barley? Rava answers with a *lo mibaya* construction: it goes without saying that wheat is forbidden — wheat, despite its harder exterior, has internal cracks in the kernels (*tzirya*), and the water may work its way in through those fissures. The *braita* mentions barley precisely because one might have supposed that barley, though softer-shelled, is smooth enough on its exterior that water would not penetrate — so the *braita* comes to teach that barley too is forbidden. According to Rava, then, *lesisa* is prohibited with both barley and wheat, leaving no path to fine flour *matzah* on *Pesach*.

## Rava's Reversals

### Permit Lesisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: מוּתָּר לִלְתּוֹת. דְּתַנְיָא: יוֹצְאִין בְּפַת נְקִיָּה וְהַדְרָאָה, וְאִי אֶפְשָׁר נְקִיָּה בְּלֹא לְתִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** reconsidered and **then said:** This is not the case. Rather, **it is permitted to soak** grain, **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **One** can **fulfill** his obligation **with** a *matza* **loaf** prepared from **refined** flour **and with** *matza* baked from **coarse** flour. **And it is impossible** to produce **refined** *matza* **without soaking** the grain, as this is the only way to remove the chaff completely from the grain.

*Hadar amar Rava* — Rava then reversed himself and declared: *mutar lilsos* — *lesisa* is actually permitted. His proof is a *braita* stating that one fulfills one's *matzah* obligation whether the *matzah* is made from fine flour (*pas nekiya*) or whole wheat (*hadra'ah*) — Rashi would add even bran bread. The question is simple: fine flour matzah evidently fulfills the obligation. But how does one obtain fine flour? *V'ee efshar nekiya belo lsisah* — it is impossible to produce fine flour without performing *lesisa*. If the *braita* presupposes that fine flour *matzah* is a valid option, then the process required to produce fine flour must be permitted. One cannot say "you can use it but you can't make it." Since *lesisa* must have been performed before *Pesach* rather than during it, and since that pre-*Pesach* use would still have produced flour susceptible to *chametz* concerns once it reached the stage of dough, this is not simply a *ta'aroves* question — it is a *chametz* question. The permissibility of the result implies the permissibility of the process.

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרָבָא: הַקְּמָחִין וְהַסְּלָתוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם, שֶׁל כְּפָרִים — טְהוֹרִים, וְשֶׁל כְּרַכִּים — טְמֵאִין. דִּכְפָרִים מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא לָתְתִי, וְקָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ סוֹלֶת!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rava** from a *baraita*: With regard to **flour and refined flour** that belong **to gentiles, in villages** they **are ritually pure, and in cities** they **are ritually impure.** As for flour **in villages, what is the reason** that it is ritually pure? Is it **not due to** the fact **that they do not soak** the flour, and therefore it does not become liable to ritual impurity? **And** yet their flour **is** nonetheless **called refined flour.** It can be inferred from this that it is possible to prepare refined flour without soaking the grain.

*Eitzeivei Rav Pappa leRava* — Rav Pappa challenged this proof. He cites a *braita* dealing with *tumah* and *taharah*: flour and fine flour (*kamchim ve-hasaltos*) purchased from non-Jews in villages (*kefarim*) are *tahor* — not susceptible to *tumah* — whereas the same products purchased in cities (*krachim*) are *tamei*. What makes food susceptible to *tumah*? Contact with liquid, including water. The implication is that fine flour from villages was produced *without* water — and yet it is still called *soles*, fine flour. This would suggest that one can produce *soles* without *lesisa*, which would undermine Rava's proof that the permissibility of fine flour matzah entails the permissibility of *lesisa*.

### Flour Susceptibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּרְגּוּמָא אַקִּמְחָא. בָּתַר דְּנָפֵיק אָמַר: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אֵימָא לֵיהּ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חִיטִּין שֶׁל מְנָחוֹת אֵין לוֹתְתִין אוֹתָם, וְקָא קָרֵי לְהוּ סוֹלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava answered: You should **interpret** the distinction of the *baraita* as referring only **to** ordinary **flour,** not refined flour, which is invariably susceptible to ritual impurity due to the soaking. **After** Rava **left,** Rav Pappa **said: What is the reason** that **he did not say** a proof **from this** statement **that Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Yirmeya said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **wheat** that will be used **for meal-offerings, one** may **not soak it,** and yet it **is** nonetheless **called refined flour?** The Torah insists that the flour used for meal-offerings be prepared from refined flour.

The *gemara* attempts to deflect this by suggesting that the *braita*'s statement about *tahor* status applies only to ordinary flour (*kemach*), not to *soles* — only *soles* from cities, where higher-quality processing occurred, would have involved water. This answer is, as even the *gemara* seems to acknowledge, somewhat strained, since the *braita* explicitly mentions both *kamchim* and *saltos*. Rava resists the proof and maintains his position. After Rava departed, Rav Pappa remarked that he should have pressed a stronger challenge: the wheat used for *menachos* (Temple meal-offerings) must be *soles* — the Torah explicitly requires it — yet Shmuel rules that one should not perform *lesisa* on wheat designated for a *mincha*, precisely because *chametz* is forbidden in that context. The implication is clear: one can produce *soles* without *lesisa*. This would constitute a more decisive counter-proof to Rava's argument. Nevertheless, Rava's final ruling — that *lesisa* is permitted — stands.

### Mitzvah to do Lesisa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: מִצְוָה לִלְתּוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת הַמַּצּוֹת״ — אִי לָא דְּבָעֵי לְתִיתָה שִׁימּוּר לְמַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** reconsidered and **then said:** It is not only permitted to soak the grains; it is actually **a mitzva to soak** them, **as it is stated: "And you shall guard the *matzot*"** (Exodus 12:17). The Gemara explains this statement: **If** it is **not** the case **that** grain **requires soaking, for what** purpose is **guarding** necessary?

*Hadar amar Rava* — Rava reverses himself yet again, this time in a more striking direction: *mitzvah lelisos* — it is actually a *mitzvah* to perform *lesisa*, to use water to remove the bran before making *matzah*. This reversal is connected to the concept of *shimur* and is explained in the subsequent discussion.

## Shimur Concept

### Ushmartem Etymology

The basis for Rava's claim that *lesisa* is a *mitzvah* lies in the verse *ushmartem et ha-matzos* — "you shall watch/guard the *matzos*." The *gemara* interprets this as a directive to guard the *matzah* against becoming *chametz*. Rava argues: *ilo d'va'i lesisa* — if it were not that *lesisa* is performed at an earlier stage when water first contacts the grain, *shimur lemai* — what exactly is one guarding? The *gemara* will explore this question in detail.

### Non-Jewish Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בְּצֵקוֹת שֶׁל גּוֹיִם — אָדָם מְמַלֵּא כְּרֵיסוֹ מֵהֶן, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֹּאכַל כְּזַיִת מַצָּה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה — אִין, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה — לָא,
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Huna said:** In the case of **dough** prepared **by gentiles,** if one knows that it has not become leavened, **a person** may **fill his stomach with them** on Passover night, **provided that he eats an olive-bulk of *matza* in the end,** to fulfill the obligation to eat *matza*. The Gemara infers from this statement: With regard to the *matza* that he eats **in the end, yes,** he fulfills his obligation with this *matza*. But with regard to the *matza* he ate **in the beginning, no,** he does not fulfill the mitzva with dough prepared by gentiles.

The *gemara* invokes a teaching of Rav Huna: *betzikot shel nochrim adam memalei kriso mehen* — one may eat one's fill from dough prepared by non-Jews, provided that a visual inspection reveals the dough has not fermented. This demonstrates a striking principle: one need not have been present at the kneading to be confident the dough is not *chametz* — a visual inspection suffices to establish that. *Uvilvad she-yochal kezayis matzah b'achronah* — but in order to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *matzah*, one must ensure the final piece eaten is *matzah* that was specifically watched, from an earlier stage, by Jews. This is clearly not a *chametz* concern — after all, the non-Jewish dough is permissible to eat throughout *Pesach* based on visual inspection alone. It is rather a *kavana*, or *lishmah*, concern: the *matzah* of *mitzvah* requires a form of intentional guarding that goes beyond merely confirming the absence of fermentation.

### When Shimur Begins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא עֲבַד בְּהוּ שִׁימּוּר. וְלַעְבֵּיד לֵיהּ שִׁימּוּר מֵאֲפִיָּה וְאֵילָךְ! אֶלָּא לָאו שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, שִׁימּוּר מֵעִיקָּרָא בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** that one cannot fulfill his obligation to eat *matza* with dough prepared by gentiles? It is **because** he **did not perform** his duty to **guard** this dough. **But one** can **perform** his duty to **guard it from** the time of **baking and onward. Rather, isn't it** correct to **conclude from** this *baraita* **that** the grain **must** be guarded **from the beginning,** i.e., from the time it is soaked, which proves that soaking the grain is part of the process of preparing *matza*?

This raises the question of when such *shimur* must begin. One might think that *shimur* at the kneading stage — when water is added to flour — would be sufficient. But Rav Huna's ruling implies otherwise: if *shimur* could begin at kneading, why couldn't one simply purchase the non-Jewish dough at that point and declare one is now performing *shimur*? *V'li'avad lei shimur me'afiyah va-eilach* — why not do *shimur* from the point of baking onward? The *gemara* concludes: *ella lav shema mina shimur me'ikara ba'inan* — evidently, *shimur* must begin from the very outset of the relevant process. *Shimur* at a midpoint is insufficient.

### Early Shimur Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי לָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא. דְּאָמַר לְהוּ לְהָנְהוּ דִּמְהַפְּכִי כֵּיפֵי: כִּי מְהַפְּכִיתוּ — הַפִּיכוּ לְשׁוּם מִצְוָה, אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: שִׁימּוּר מֵעִיקָּרָא מִתְּחִלָּתוֹ וְעַד סוֹפוֹ בָּעֵינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And although** this proof was rejected, **Rava did not retract** his statement that guarding grain must begin before kneading. **For he** would **say to those who cut** and tied **the stalks [*kifei*]** of grain in the field: **When you cut** the grain, **cut it for the purpose of the mitzva. Apparently,** Rava **maintains** that **it is necessary to guard** the grain **from the outset,** i.e., **from the beginning** of its preparation **until its end.**

Rava develops this argument. If *shimur* at baking is too late, and *shimur* at kneading is also too late, then the earliest stage at which water contacts the grain — namely *lesisa* — is where *shimur* must commence. That is why Rava says it is a *mitzvah* to perform *lesisa*: not for the intrinsic value of the bran-removal process, but because doing so establishes the *shimur* at the earliest possible stage of water contact. One might reasonably object — as the *gemara* does — that this does not actually compel one to perform *lesisa*. Perhaps the *halacha* is simply that *shimur* must begin whenever water first contacts the grain, at whatever stage that occurs. If one skips *lesisa* and adds water only at kneading, then *shimur* from kneading suffices. *Lesisa* is not obligatory; it is simply one path to an earlier starting point. The *gemara* acknowledges this as a valid challenge to Rava's proof, yet notes that Rava did not reverse himself.

### Bundle Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר לְהוּ לְהָנְהוּ דִּמְהַפְּכִי כֵּיפֵי: כִּי מְהַפְּכִיתוּ — הַפִּיכוּ לְשׁוּם מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **For he** would **say to those who cut** and tied **the stalks [*kifei*]** of grain in the field: **When you cut** the grain, **cut it for the purpose of the mitzva.**

*De'amar lehu l'hanu de-mahepkhei kifi* — Rava instructed those workers who were binding the bundles of grain in the fields: *ki mahephkhei hapeikhu l'shem mitzvah* — when you bind these bundles, do so *l'shem mitzvah*, with the intention that this is for the *matzah* of the *mitzvah*. Rava thus pushed *shimur* back even further — to the binding of the sheaves in the field, before any water has been applied at all. *Alma kasavar shimur me'ikara mit'chilato v'ad sofo ba'inan* — one sees that Rava holds *shimur* must span the entire process from its very beginning to its end.

## Shimur Positions

### Shmura Label

The practical consequence of Rava's position is reflected on the label of every box of *shmura matzah* available today: *shamur mishat ktzira* — guarded from the time of cutting. This follows directly from Rava's ruling that *shimur* must begin at the very earliest stage of the grain's processing.

## Shimur Concept…

### Mar's Mother Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא מְנַקְּטָא לֵיהּ אִימֵּיהּ בְּאַרְבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates with regard to **Mar, son of Ravina,** that **his mother** would **bring him** wheat **in a trough.** In other words, she would guard the wheat grains from when they were harvested by placing them in vessels and guarding them until Passover.

*Mar breih de-Ravina* — Mar the son of Ravina: his mother would collect sheaves of grain stalks and place them in a container, watching over them and protecting them from the moment of cutting until it was time to make *matzah*. This is offered as a practical illustration of the position that *shimur* from cutting is the appropriate standard — and it is noteworthy that this practice was carried out by women as a matter of course.

## Shimur Positions…

### Machine Matzah

The question of what *shimur* actually requires has significant contemporary relevance in connection with machine *matzah*. The issue turns on the nature of *shimur*: is it fundamentally a *lishmah* or *kavana* requirement — that is, a requirement of conscious human intent directed toward producing *matzah* for the sake of the *mitzvah* — or is it simply a requirement of practical vigilance, ensuring the grain is protected from water contact throughout the process?

If *shimur* is purely a matter of practical protection and prevention of *chametz*, then the question is only whether the machinery accomplishes that protection, and a human operator who turns on the machine with the requisite intention might arguably satisfy the requirement even if the machine then runs unattended. But if *shimur* is a *lishmah* requirement, something closer to the *l'shem matzas mitzvah* declared by bakers before kneading, then continuous human engagement at each stage of the process may be necessary — and a machine running autonomously for hours, regardless of what intention the operator had at the outset, may not satisfy it.

---

## Shimur Concept……

### Shimur Distinction

It is important to distinguish between two types of *shimur* that appear in this discussion. The first is *shimur* to prevent *chametz*: ensuring the grain or dough does not ferment. This form of *shimur* can apparently be accomplished by visual inspection — as we saw with the case of non-Jewish dough, where one simply confirms no fermentation has occurred. The second is *shimur l'shem matzah*, guarding the grain specifically so that the resulting *matzah* qualifies as *matzas mitzvah*. These are distinct requirements. The first is a *chametz* concern, addressed by any adequate monitoring. The second is a *kavana* or *lishmah* concern, requiring intentional, dedicated attention from the beginning of the process. Even where one can satisfy the first by visual inspection — as with purchased non-Jewish dough — one cannot satisfy the second without prior dedicated *shimur*.

---

## Shimur Positions……

### Rishonim Timeline

The *Rishonim* debate precisely when the obligation of *shimur* begins. Some rule in accordance with the end of the *gemara*'s discussion, requiring *shimur* from the time of cutting (*mishas ketzira*). Others read the subsequent narratives — particularly the story of Mar's mother — as reflecting a praiseworthy *chumra* adopted by especially pious individuals, not as normative *halacha*, and do not require *shimur* from so early a stage. A third position holds that *shimur* is required, but not necessarily from cutting — rather, from the point at which serious exposure to water becomes a realistic concern.

### Rosh's Position

The Rosh, for example, holds that *shimur* should begin at the grinding stage, when grain is taken to the mill. His reasoning is twofold: flour is considerably more susceptible to *chametz* than whole kernels, and more critically, where did one grind wheat in the Talmudic period? At a water mill. The environment of the mill itself represents the first serious point of water exposure, and therefore that is where the obligation of *shimur* begins. This position is a middle path — earlier than the kneading stage, but not as early as the cutting of the grain.

## Submerged Trough

### Rava Permits Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא אַרְבָּא דְחִיטֵּי דִּטְבַעָא בְּחִישְׁתָּא, שַׁרְיָא רָבָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי לְגוֹיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that there was **a certain boat** carrying **wheat, which capsized** before Passover **in** the **Ḥishta** River. **Rava permitted** its owners **to sell** the recovered grain **to gentiles** before Passover.

*Hahu arva d'chiti d'tava b'chishta* — a certain trough containing wheat kernels that fell into and was submerged in a river. This is a graver situation than *lesisa*, which involves brief and controlled water contact; submersion in a river is prolonged, unmonitored, and presumably quite thorough. *Sharei Rava l'zabuni l'nochrim* — Rava permitted selling the wheat to non-Jews before *Pesach*. Since the wheat cannot be safely used and there was no intention to acquire *chametz*, the practical solution is to divest oneself of it.

### Kilayim Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבָּה בַּר לֵיוַאי לְרָבָא: בֶּגֶד שֶׁאָבַד בּוֹ כִּלְאַיִם — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִמְכְּרֶנּוּ לְגוֹי, וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה בּוֹ מַרְדַּעַת לַחֲמוֹר. אֲבָל עוֹשִׂין אוֹתוֹ תַּכְרִיכִין לְמֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Levai raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rava** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a garment in which diverse kinds,** a prohibited mixture of wool and linen, **has been lost,** i.e., a wool garment into which a linen thread was sewn or vice versa, **one** may **not sell it to a gentile; and one** may **not** even **fashion it** into **a saddlecloth for a donkey.** It is prohibited to do so lest one remove a piece of this garment and sew it onto his own clothing. **However, one** may **fashion it** into **a shroud for a dead body,** as there is no concern that he will remove it from the dead.

*Ava bar Livai* challenged Rava from the laws of *kilayim*. If one has a wool garment in which a linen thread has become embedded somewhere and cannot be located, one may not sell it to a non-Jew — *harei zeh lo yimkerenu l'nochri* — nor use it as a saddle blanket for a donkey. The concern is not that the non-Jew will wear it — it is generally understood that the prohibition of *shatnez* applies only to Jews — but that the non-Jew may eventually resell the garment to a Jew who will not realize it contains *shatnez* and will wear it. Similarly, the non-Jew who acquires the wheat may sell it back to a Jew before *Pesach*, and that Jew will not know the wheat is problematic.

### Shrouds Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְגוֹי מַאי טַעְמָא לָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲדַר מְזַבֵּין לְיִשְׂרָאֵל?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this issue: **What is the reason** that one may **not** sell it **to a gentile?** Is it **not due to** the concern **that** the gentile **will resell it to a Jew?**

The same *braita* dealing with the *kilayim* garment offers a solution: *aval osin oto tachrichin l'meit* — one may use the garment as burial shrouds. Once a garment has been designated and used as shrouds for the dead, it becomes *assur b'hana'ah* — forbidden for any benefit — taking on, as it were, the status of the corpse itself, and cannot reenter general use. The *kilayim* analogy raises a tangential point noted by *Tosafot*: the practice of removing *tzitzit* from the *tallit* of the deceased before burial, despite the *gemara*'s concern about *lo'eg la-rash* — mocking the dead who can no longer perform *mitzvot* — by displaying the fringes. *Tosafot* notes that contemporary practice relies on a line in *Masechet Semachot* in which Abba Shaul ben Batnis instructed his sons to remove his *techelet* — understood as his *tzitzit* — and bury him at the feet of his father. This leads *Tosafot* to observe that in a number of instances Ashkenazic practice follows non-Talmudic sources — *Yerushalmi*, *midrash*, or the minor tractates — in ways that diverge from the *Bavli*. This is likely connected to the historical transmission of Ashkenazic practice through Italy from *Eretz Yisrael* culture, as opposed to Sephardic tradition, which derives more directly from Babylonian Jewish culture. Sometimes *Eretz Yisrael* practices survived in Ashkenazic *minhag* in tension with, or contrary to, Babylonian rulings.

### Sell in Portions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: לְזַבִּינְהוּ קַבָּא קַבָּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּכָלְיָא קַמֵּי פִּיסְחָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava** reconsidered and **then said: He should sell** this wheat **one *kav* at a time,** i.e., in small measures, each **to** a different **Jew,** but not to any one Jew in large quantities, **so that** all of this wheat **will be used before Passover.** By selling it in this manner, all the grain will be used quickly and no one will unwittingly eat these leavened grains on Passover.

*Hadar amar Rava* — Rava accepted the critique and reversed himself again. *L'zabninhu kava kava l'Yisrael* — sell the wheat in small quantities to Jews before *Pesach*, in such a way that each individual buyer will use up their portion before *Pesach* arrives. *Ki haychi d'kalya kamei Pischa* — so that it will be consumed before *Pesach*. The logic is that people in that environment did not stockpile grain; if someone was buying wheat, they would be baking with it within days. By selling in small amounts to multiple individuals, one ensures that the problematic wheat is consumed and does not survive into *Pesach* in any Jewish household.

## Mollel (Thickening)

### Mollel Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מוֹלְלִין אֶת הַקְּדֵירָה בַּפֶּסַח. וְהָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיִּמְלוֹל — נוֹתֵן אֶת הַקֶּמַח וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹתֵן אֶת הַחוֹמֶץ. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אַף נוֹתֵן אֶת הַחוֹמֶץ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נוֹתֵן אֶת הַקֶּמַח.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One** may **not stir** flour into **a pot** of food **on Passover** to absorb the foam that has accumulated during the cooking process. **And one who wishes to stir** flour should **add the flour and afterward add vinegar,** which will prevent the flour from becoming leavened. **And some say:** One may **even add vinegar and afterward add the flour,** as vinegar prevents flour from becoming leavened even after the flour is diluted in water.

*Tanu rabbanan*: the Rabbis taught — *ein molin et ha-kederah be-Pesach* — one may not *mollel* the pot on *Pesach*. *Mollel* refers to adding flour as a thickening agent to a pot of soup or stew. The concern is obvious: flour added to a hot liquid is in contact with water under conditions that could promote fermentation, or at minimum represents an unnecessary and risky introduction of flour to a liquid environment on *Pesach*.

### Flour-then-Vinegar

*Ve-ha-rotzeh she-yimlo mah ya'aseh* — what should one who wishes to thicken the pot do? The first opinion: *nosen et ha-kemach ve-achar kach nosen et ha-chometz* — put the flour in first, then add the vinegar. One might think it would be preferable to add the vinegar first, so that the flour never contacts water unprotected. But this first opinion holds that adding the vinegar afterward is better, because at that moment the vinegar arrives undiluted and thus with its full potency to arrest any incipient fermentation.

### Vinegar-then-Flour

*Ve-yesh omrim* — there is another opinion: add the vinegar first, then the flour. *Man yesh omrim* — the *gemara* identifies this view as that of Rabbi Yehudah.

### Vinegar Power

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הָאִילְפָּס וְהַקְּדֵירָה שֶׁהֶעֱבִירָן מְרוּתָּחִין — לֹא יִתֵּן לְתוֹכָן תַּבְלִין, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ הַקְּעָרָה אוֹ לְתוֹךְ הַתַּמְחוּי. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לַכֹּל הוּא נוֹתֵן, חוּץ מִדָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ חוֹמֶץ וָצִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, as we learned** in a mishna: With regard to **a stew pot or a pot** of food **that one removed** from the fire when it was **seething,** one may **not add spices to them on Shabbat. However, one** may **add** spices **to a dish or to** the large **plate** into which the food is poured from the pot. **Rabbi Yehuda says: One** may **add** spices **to any** food that has been removed from the fire, **except to** a dish **that contains vinegar or brine,** as this food is considered as though it were still seething, due to the pungency of the vinegar or brine.

Why Rabbi Yehudah? *De-tenan*: a *mishnah* in the context of *Shabbat* laws teaches that if one removed a boiling pot or pan from the flame, one may not add spices to it on *Shabbat* because the vessel — a *kli rishon* — retains sufficient heat to cook the spices even off the flame. However, one may add spices to the soup or stew once it has been poured into a serving bowl — a *kli sheni* — which does not cook. Rabbi Yehudah adds an exception: *chutz mi-davar she-yesh bo chometz va-tzir* — except for something that contains vinegar or fish brine. Even in a *kli sheni*, the pungency of the vinegar maintains its power to act upon food. Rabbi Yehudah thus holds that vinegar retains its active properties even within a cooked mixture. It follows that if one adds vinegar to the pot first, the vinegar's power persists even after other ingredients are introduced, and placing the flour into an already-vinegar-infused environment would protect against fermentation.

The *gemara* asks: why attribute this opinion to Rabbi Yehudah rather than to Rabbi Yosi, who already said that vinegar arrests fermentation in grain? The answer is that Rabbi Yosi's position addressed undiluted vinegar applied directly to kernels — *de'isa be'inei*, when it is present in its pure form. *Aval al yedei ta'aroves* — but once the vinegar is mixed into the pot with other ingredients, diluted and blended, it may no longer retain the power to prevent fermentation. Rabbi Yosi's ruling does not necessarily extend to vinegar already incorporated into a dish. Rabbi Yehudah, by contrast, holds explicitly that vinegar maintains its potency even within a mixture — hence it is his view that supports the practice of adding vinegar first.

### Undiluted Vinegar

The critical limitation that emerges is that vinegar's power to prevent fermentation is contingent on its being undiluted and directly applied. Once mixed into the liquid environment of the pot, it may lose that capacity. This distinction governs the entire debate about which ingredient should go into the pot first.

### Ulla's Stricture

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא אָמַר: אֶחָד זֶה וְאֶחָד זֶה — אָסוּר, מִשּׁוּם: ״לֵךְ לֵךְ אָמְרִינַן נְזִירָא, סְחוֹר סְחוֹר, לְכַרְמָא לָא תִּקְרַב״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** In **both** of **these** cases, whether the vinegar is added first or afterward, its use is **prohibited,** as one must avoid scenarios that might lead to a prohibition, **as per** the well-known adage: **Go around, go around,** and **do not approach the vineyard, they say to the nazirite.**

*Ulla amar echad ze ve'echad ze assur* — Ulla holds that it makes no difference whether vinegar goes in first or last: both procedures are forbidden. One should not add flour to a pot on *Pesach* under any circumstances. His reasoning reflects a principle the *gemara* expresses with the maxim: *lech lech amrinan nezira, sechor sechor, le-karma lo tikrav* — we tell the *nazir*: go away, go around, do not come near the vineyard. A *nazir* is technically forbidden only from grapes and grape products, not from entering a vineyard per se — but we instruct him to keep his distance entirely, to put safety margins around the prohibition. The same logic applies here: the theoretical possibility of managing flour in a pot on *Pesach* using vinegar is precisely the kind of risky proximity to prohibition that we should avoid categorically, rather than parsing the technique to find a permissible pathway.

### Rav Pappi Roasted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פַּפִּי שָׁרֵי לֵיהּ לְבוּרְדִּיקִי דְּבֵי רֵישׁ גָּלוּתָא לְמִמְחֵה קְדֵירָה בַּחֲסִיסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappi permitted the cooks [*burdikei*] of the household of the Exilarch to mash roasted grains into a pot** of food, to dissolve the accumulated foam.

*Rav Pappi shari lehu le-bordakei devei Reish Galusa* — Rav Pappi permitted the cooks or bakers in the household of the *Reish Galusa* to thicken the pot with *chasisi*, which Rashi understands as roasted grain. Presumably Rav Pappi reasoned that once grain has been properly roasted, it can no longer become *chametz* — as the *gemara* established at the outset of today's daf. *Tosafot* understands *chasisi* differently, reading it as lentil flour, which would pose no *chametz* concern at all. In any event, the question is whether one can rely on proper roasting to render the thickener safe.

### Rava Practical

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אִיכָּא דְּשָׁרֵי כִּי הַאי מִילְּתָא בְּדוּכְתָּא דִּשְׁכִיחִי עָבְדִי? אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: רָבָא גּוּפָא מָחֵי לֵהּ קִידְרָא בַּחֲסִיסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is there** anyone **who permits this matter in a place where servants are found?** Servants are unlikely to be careful with regard to these prohibitions. They will use raw flour for this purpose, which will lead to a violation of the prohibition against leavened bread on Passover. **Some say** that **Rava himself** would **add roasted grains into** his own **pot.**

*Amar Rava*: Rava questioned whether one could really sanction such a practice in a household staffed by servants — *ika de-shari ki hai milsa be-duchta de-shchichi avdei*? Who can trust servants to ensure the grain is thoroughly roasted? We opened today's daf with precisely this concern: if grain is not roasted well enough, the *chametz* risk remains. Using roasted grain as a thickener, in an environment where the roasting may have been carelessly executed, seems to recreate the very problem the roasting was meant to solve.

Yet the *gemara* closes with a striking counter-note: *ika de'amri Rava gufa machi leh kedeira bechasisi* — some say that Rava himself would thicken his pot with this grain. And so the *gemara* ends as it began: moving back and forth between stringency and leniency, between those who say one must keep one's distance from any such practice and those who say that careful, attentive use of the relevant techniques is entirely permissible. The tension between these poles is not a failure of the *gemara* to reach a conclusion — it is the honest reflection of a legal system grappling with practical reality.
