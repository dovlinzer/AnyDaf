# Yoma 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Miluim & Seven Days

### Setup: Preparation Paral…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? וְהָתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״וְשָׁב הַכֹּהֵן״ ״וּבָא הַכֹּהֵן״, זוֹ הִיא ״שִׁיבָה״, זוֹ הִיא ״בִּיאָה״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **What is the practical difference** between the two terms? **Didn't the school of Rabbi Yishmael teach** a verbal analogy with regard to leprosy of houses between the verse: **"And the priest shall return [*veshav*]"** (Leviticus 14:39) and the verse: **"And the priest shall come [*uva*]"** (Leviticus 14:44)? From that verbal analogy it is derived that **this is** the *halakha* with regard to **returning,** i.e., it is after seven days; and **this is** the same *halakha* with regard to **coming,** i.e., it is also after seven days. Obviously, the less pronounced difference in grammatical forms between *tziva* and *tzavoto* should not prevent the teaching of a verbal analogy.

The *Gemara* on today's *daf* opens by addressing the question raised by the *Mishnah*: what is the biblical basis for the seven-day preparation of the *Kohen Gadol* prior to the *Avodah* on *Yom Kippur*, during which he was separated from his household? A parallel *Mishnah* in *Parah* records an identical seven-day preparation for the *kohen* who would perform the *Parah Adumah* ritual. The *Gemara* has been seeking the source for both of these practices. The answer it has been exploring derives from the verse describing the *Miluim* — the seven-day consecration period before the *kohanim* inaugurated the *Avodah* in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The verse there reads: *kaasher asah bayom hazeh tsivah Hashem la'asot lekaper alechem* — "as was done on this day, so God commanded to do, to atone for you" — which the *Gemara* interprets as a divine directive to replicate this paradigm in future contexts. Two such contexts are identified: *Parah Adumah*, derived through a *gezerah shavah* on the word *tsivah*, and *Yom Kippur*, linked through the word *lekaper*.

### Gezerah Shavah: Tzivah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין ״צִוָּה״ דְּלִפְנֵי עֲשִׂיָּה מִ״צִּוָּה״ דְּלִפְנֵי עֲשִׂיָּה, וְאֵין דָּנִין ״צִוָּה״ דִּלְאַחַר עֲשִׂיָּה מִ״צִּוָּה״ דְּלִפְנֵי עֲשִׂיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** In that case, only the sequestering prior to Yom Kippur can be derived. The Gemara rejects this, as a verbal analogy is derived only between functionally similar phrases. **One derives commanded that is** stated **before performance,** as in the portion of the heifer, **from commanded that is** stated **before performance** in the portion of the inauguration; **and one does not derive commanded that is** stated **after performance** in the portion of Yom Kippur **from commanded that is** stated **before performance.**

The connection to *Yom Kippur* via *lekaper* is admittedly broad, since *lekaper* could refer generically to any *korban*. The *Gemara* therefore adds a restriction: the referent must resemble *Yom Kippur* — it must be a special, singular event, not a routine offering. Furthermore, it must be a one-day event, which immediately rules out the *regalim*.

### Excluding Regalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁנּוֹהֵג פַּעַם אַחַת בַּשָּׁנָה מִדָּבָר הַנּוֹהֵג פַּעַם אַחַת בַּשָּׁנָה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי רְגָלִים, דְּלָאו פַּעַם אַחַת בַּשָּׁנָה נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** We derive a matter that is performed once a year, the service of Yom Kippur, **from a matter that is performed once a year,** like the inauguration, which was a one-time event, **to the exclusion of** the service on the **Festivals, which is not** performed **once a year;** rather, it is performed three times a year.

Having eliminated the three pilgrimage festivals, the *Gemara* considers whether *Shemini Atzeret* might serve as the intended one-day event. At the top of 3a, the *Gemara* raises this possibility seriously, noting that *Shemini Atzeret* does indeed stand as an independent day in six distinct respects, encapsulated in the mnemonic *Pezer Keshev*.

### Shemini Atzeret Independ…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּזֵ״ר קֶשֶׁ״ב.
>
> **Translation:** ***Peh*, *zayin*, *reish*, *kuf*, *shin*, *beit*,** an acronym for: Lottery [*payis*], as a new lottery is performed on that day to determine which priests will sacrifice the offerings that day, and the order established on *Sukkot* does not continue; the blessing of time [*zeman*]: Who has given us life, sustained us, and brought us to this time, is recited just as it is recited at the start of each Festival; Festival [*regel*], as it is considered a Festival in and of itself and there is no mitzva to reside in the *sukka* (see *Tosafot*); offering [*korban*], as the number of offerings sacrificed on the Eighth Day is not a continuation of the number offered on *Sukkot* but is part of a new calculation; song [*shira*], as the Psalms recited by the Levites as the offerings were sacrificed on the Eighth Day are not a continuation of those recited on *Sukkot*; blessing [*berakha*], as the addition to the third blessing of Grace after Meals and in the *Amida* prayer (see *Tosafot*) is phrased differently than the addition recited on *Sukkot*.

These six dimensions of independence are as follows. *Payis*: a new lottery is conducted for the *korbanot*, initiating a fresh cycle of priestly divisions. *Zman*: a new *Shehecheyanu* is recited, distinct from the final day of *Chag Hamatzot*. *Regel bifnei atzmo*: it functions as an independent *regel*, with none of the distinctive practices of *Sukkot* — no *sukkah*, no *lulav*. *Korban bifnei atzmo*: it has its own independent *korban* rather than continuing the pattern of the *Sukkot Musaf* cycle. *Shir*: the *shir shel yom* sung during the *korbanot* on *Shemini Atzeret* is distinct — *Rashi* notes that the *shir* for *Sukkot* would be based on *matanot aniyim*, whereas *Shemini Atzeret* has its own. *Bracha bifnei atzmo*: it receives a distinct blessing — *Rashi* suggests this may refer to a special blessing offered to the king, or alternatively that in *Shmoneh Esrei* and in *Birkat Hamazon* it is given its own unique appellation: *Yom Hashmini Chag Ha'atzeret hazeh*.

### Tashlumin Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן תַּשְׁלוּמִין, תַּשְׁלוּמִין דְּרִאשׁוֹן הוּא, דְּהָא תְּנַן: מִי שֶׁלֹּא חָג בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג — חוֹגֵג וְהוֹלֵךְ כָּל הָרֶגֶל כּוּלּוֹ, וְיוֹם טוֹב הָאַחֲרוֹן שֶׁל חַג.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** despite all these differences, **with regard to compensation** for failure to sacrifice the Festival offerings at the earliest opportunity, everyone agrees that **it is** a day of **compensation for** obligations not met during the **first** Festival, **as didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One who did not celebrate on the first Festival day** of *Sukkot* by sacrificing the Festival offering may **celebrate** and sacrifice the Festival offering throughout **the whole Festival in its entirety,** including **the last Festival day of the festival** of *Sukkot*. Apparently, the Eighth Day of Assembly is considered the last Festival day of Sukkot and is appended to it with regard to its obligations.

Yet despite all of these markers of independence, *Shemini Atzeret* remains unmistakably bound to *Sukkot* in one critical legal respect: it is a *tashlumin* day. The *Mishnah* teaches that one who failed to bring the *korban chagigah* on the first day of the *regel* may make it up on any subsequent day of the festival — *chogeg veholech kol haregel kulo* — and may even bring it *beyom tov acharon shel chag*, on the final day, which is *Shemini Atzeret*. The *Rambam* extends this to the *olat re'iah* as well. Accordingly, *Shemini Atzeret* functions as a makeup day for obligations incurred during *Sukkot* itself.

### Shemini Atzeret Ruled Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא שְׁמִינִי, דִּפְרִישַׁת שִׁבְעָה לְיוֹם אֶחָד הוּא! דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין קְדוּשָּׁה לְפָנָיו מִדָּבָר שֶׁאֵין קְדוּשָּׁה לְפָנָיו, וְאֵין דָּנִין דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ קְדוּשָּׁה לְפָנָיו מִדָּבָר שֶׁאֵין קְדוּשָּׁה לְפָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that the sequestering for seven days is prior to the festival of the **Eighth** Day of Assembly, as that would also be **sequestering for seven days** prior to performing a service **for one day.** The Gemara rejects this: **One derives a matter before which there is not sanctity,** Yom Kippur, which is preceded by weekdays, **from a matter before which there is not sanctity,** the day of the inauguration, which was also preceded by weekdays. **And we do not derive a matter before which there is sanctity,** the Eighth Day of Assembly, which is preceded by the seven days of *Sukkot*, **from a matter before which there is not sanctity.**

It would therefore be incoherent to require a seven-day separation before *Shemini Atzeret* while not requiring one before *Sukkot*, since *Shemini Atzeret* is clearly the culmination of *Sukkot*. This rules out *Shemini Atzeret* as a candidate, and the *Gemara* concludes that the seven-day preparation must point to *Yom Kippur*.

### Shavuot Candidate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא עֲצֶרֶת, דִּפְרִישַׁת שִׁבְעָה לְיוֹם אֶחָד הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges further: **And say** that the priest should be sequestered before the festival of ***Shavuot*,** which is a Festival preceded by weekdays, as there too **it is** a matter of **sequestering of seven** days **for one day.

The *Gemara* now turns to the next obvious candidate. Having eliminated *Pesach*, *Sukkot*, and *Shemini Atzeret*, the remaining one-day *Yom Tov* is *Atzeret* — the *Chazal*ic term for *Shavuot*. This appears structurally ideal: the *Miluim* involved seven days of preparation followed by one culminating day, and *Shavuot* is precisely a one-day event. The *Gemara* frames the question: *deparisha shiva leyoma chad* — why not say the seven-day separation for a one-day event applies to *Shavuot*?

### Rava's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: דָּנִין פַּר אֶחָד וְאַיִל אֶחָד מִפַּר אֶחָד וְאַיִל אֶחָד, לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֲצֶרֶת דִּשְׁנֵי אֵילִים נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** There is a distinction between the inauguration and *Shavuot*, as **one derives** an instance where the obligatory offering is **one bull and one ram,** Yom Kippur, **from** an instance where the obligatory offering is **one bull and one ram,** the inauguration, **to the exclusion of *Shavuot*,** when **they are two rams** that are offered.

Rava answers: *danin par echad ve'ayil echad mipar echad ve'ayil echad, le'apukei Atzeret dishnei eilim ninhu* — we derive from one *par* and one *ayil* to a case of one *par* and one *ayil*, thereby excluding *Shavuot*, which involves two *eilim*.

### Par & Ayil Comparison

The logic becomes clear upon examining the relevant verses. At the culmination of the *Miluim*, on the eighth day as recorded at the beginning of *Parshas Shmini* (Vayikra 9), *Aharon* is commanded: *kach lecha egel ben bakar lechatat ve'ayil le'olah* — one calf for a *chatat* and one ram for an *olah*. Turning to *Yom Kippur* in *Acharei Mos*, the verse reads: *bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh bepar ben bakar lechatat ve'ayil le'olah* — again, one animal from the cattle family for a *chatat* and one ram for an *olah*, both for *Aharon*. The parallel is exact.

By contrast, the special *Avodah* of *Shavuot* — not the *Musaf*, but the offering brought *al halachem* in conjunction with the *shtei halechem* as recorded in *Emor* (Vayikra 23:18) — reads: *shivat kvasim tmimim bnei shana, ufar ben bacar echad ve'eilim shnayim yihyu olah l'Hashem*. Here, first, there are seven sheep. Second, the *par* is brought as an *olah*, not a *chatat* — already a significant difference. And third, there are two *eilim*, not one. The *Gemara* therefore concludes that *Yom Kippur*, which is identical to the *Miluim* in the structure of *Aharon*'s offering, is the correct parallel, while *Shavuot* is excluded.

## Korban Details

### Miluim Offerings

To lay this out precisely: on the eighth day of the *Miluim*, *Aharon* brought an *egel ben bakar lechatat* and an *ayil le'olah*. The *Bnei Yisrael* in turn brought a *se'ir izim lechatat*, an *egel*, and a *keves bnei shana le'olah*. The structure for *Aharon* — one bovine for a *chatat* and one ram for an *olah* — is what anchors the comparison.

### Yom Kippur Offerings

On *Yom Kippur*, the parallel is precise. *Aharon* brings *par ben bacar lechatat ve'ayil le'olah* — exactly matching the *Miluim*. For *Bnei Yisrael*, the verse specifies *shnei se'irei izim lechatat ve'ayil echad le'olah*. While the communal offering is not identical to the *Miluim*'s structure for *Bnei Yisrael*, it shares a recognizable pattern. But it is *Aharon*'s offering that is the focus, since the separation being discussed pertains to the *Kohen Gadol*, and here the correspondence is complete.

It is worth noting that the *Gemara* is deliberately setting aside the *Musaf* in these comparisons. The *Musaf* of *Yom Kippur* appears in *Parshas Pinchas*, where the verse reads: *par ben bacar echad ayil echad kevasim bnei shana shiva tmimim* — a *par*, an *ayil*, and seven *kevasim*, all as *olot*. This *Musaf* is unambiguously a separate matter from the special *Avodas Yom Hakippurim* of *Acharei Mos*. Indeed, by the *Miluim* as well, the eighth day happened to be *Rosh Chodesh*, so there was a *Musaf* on that day too — but that is similarly bracketed. What is being compared is the unique *Avodah* of the day, not the standing *Musaf*.

### Shavuot Differences

Returning to *Shavuot*: the offering brought *al halachem* — the special *Avodah* tied to the *shtei halechem* — presents multiple divergences from the *Miluim*. Most obviously, there are two rams rather than one. Additionally, the *par* is an *olah* rather than a *chatat*, which is itself already a structural difference. And there are seven *kevasim* alongside. Any one of these would distinguish *Shavuot* from the *Miluim*; all three together make the comparison untenable.

### One or Two Rams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אַיִל אֶחָד הוּא, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁנֵי אֵילִים נִינְהוּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר! דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַיִל אֶחָד, הוּא הָאָמוּר כָּאן, הוּא הָאָמוּר בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵי אֵילִים הֵם, אֶחָד הָאָמוּר כָּאן, וְאֶחָד הָאָמוּר בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **This** works out **well** according **to the one who said** that the obligatory offering on **Yom Kippur is one ram; however,** according **to the one who said** that **they are two rams** that are sacrificed on Yom Kippur, **what is there to say?** According to that opinion, Yom Kippur is not comparable to the inauguration. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: One ram is the one that is mentioned here;** as it is written: "With this Aaron will come into the Sanctuary, with a young bull for a sin-offering and a ram for a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 16:3), and **it is the** same **one that is mentioned in the book of Numbers:** "And on the tenth day of the seventh month you will have a sacred gathering when you will afflict your souls; you will not do any labor, and you will offer a burnt-offering to the Lord for a sweet aroma: One young bull, one ram…" (Numbers 29:7–8). **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: They are two rams** offered on Yom Kippur, **one mentioned here** in the book of Leviticus **and one mentioned in the book of Numbers.**

The *Gemara* now raises an internal complication. The argument above — that *Yom Kippur* parallels the *Miluim* because both feature one *par* and one *ayil* — holds smoothly according to the view that there is only one *ayil* on *Yom Kippur*. But there is an opinion that on *Yom Kippur* there are in fact two *eilim* — in which case the parallel breaks down. The *Gemara* asks: *mai ika lemeimar?* — what can be said according to that opinion?

### Rabbi's View: Same Ram

A *baraita* is cited. *Rabbi* holds: *ayil echad hu ha'amur kan hu ha'amur beChumash Pikudim* — the single *ayil* mentioned in *Acharei Mos* and the *ayil* mentioned in *Chumash Pekudim* (i.e., *Pinchas*) are one and the same ram. According to *Rabbi*, therefore, the *Musaf* of *Yom Kippur* in *Pinchas* does not introduce an additional *ayil*; rather, the single *ayil* listed there is identical to the one in *Acharei Mos*, and there is in total only one ram.

### Yom Kippur Musaf

The *Musaf* passage in *Pinchas* reads: *uve'asor lachodesh hashvi'i hazeh mikra kodesh yihyeh lachem ve'inisem es nafshoseichem... vehikravtem olah la-Shem... par ben bacar echad ayil echad kevasim bnei shana shiva tmimim* — along with accompanying *menachos* and *nesachim*, followed by the qualification *milvad chatas hakippurim ve'olat hatamid uminchatah veniskeihem*. The passage is clearly structured as a *Musaf* and is fully independent of *Acharei Mos*. Moreover, the *par* here is an *olah*, whereas in *Acharei Mos* the *par* is a *lechatat* — this alone demonstrates they are different animals. And the seven *kevasim* in *Pinchas* have no parallel in *Acharei Mos*. The only potential overlap is the *ayil le'olah* that appears in both — and it is precisely about this *ayil* that *Rabbi* and *Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Shimon* disagree.

The peculiarity of *Rabbi*'s position is notable. Logic would suggest these are entirely separate offerings. One might even argue that if *Rabbi* wished to identify the *Pinchas ayil* with something in *Acharei Mos*, it would be more natural to link it with the *ayil* of *Bnei Yisrael* rather than the *ayil* of *Aharon*, since a *Musaf* is a communal offering and the *ayil le'olah* of *Bnei Yisrael* in *Acharei Mos* likewise has a communal character. Yet *Rabbi*'s stated position is that the *ayil* of the *Musaf* is the same as *Aharon*'s *ayil* — a puzzling equation that the *Gemara* leaves largely unexplained.

The larger context of the *Avodah* in *Acharei Mos* is worth recalling briefly. *Aharon* brings his *par*, performs the *viduy*, and sprinkles the blood — the famous *achas ve'sheva* — in the *Kodesh Kodashim*. The two *se'irim* of *Bnei Yisrael* play their central roles: one goes *la'Azazel*, the other undergoes the blood-sprinkling ritual *ve'chiper ba'ad kol adas Bnei Yisrael*. The *ayil le'olah* for *Aharon* and the *ayil le'olah* for *Bnei Yisrael*, by contrast, are dispatched with relative brevity — *vayatza ve'asah es olaso ve'es olas ha'am*. These *olot* are not the focal point of the day's *Avodah*.

### Rabbi Eliezer: Two Rams

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הָתָם חַד לְחוֹבַת הַיּוֹם, וְחַד לְמוּסָפִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this solution: **Even** if **you say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer, son of Rabbi Shimon,** and two rams are brought on Yom Kippur, a distinction remains between Yom Kippur and *Shavuot*. **There,** with regard to Yom Kippur, **one** ram, mentioned in the book of Leviticus, is **for the obligation of the day,** the atonement of Yom Kippur; **and one** ram, mentioned in the book of Numbers, is for the **additional offerings.** This is **to the exclusion of** the *halakha* with regard to ***Shavuot*, where both** rams **are obligations of the day.** Therefore, there is no basis for deriving the *halakha* with regard to *Shavuot* from the inauguration.

*Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Shimon* disagrees: *shnei eilim hem ha'amur kan veha'amur beChumash Pikudim* — the two references to an *ayil* are two distinct rams. According to him, *Yom Kippur* involves not one but two rams, which would undermine the claimed parallel to the *Miluim*.

### Reconciliation

The *Gemara* resolves this: *afilu teima Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Shimon* — even according to *Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Shimon*, the comparison holds. *Hasam chad dechovas hayom vechad demussafim* — there, one ram belongs to the special *Avodah* of the day (*chovas hayom*) and the other belongs to the *Musaf*. As *Tosafot* observes, there was also a *Musaf* on the eighth day of the *Miluim* — because it happened to coincide with *Rosh Chodesh* — and yet that *Musaf* is not what anchors the comparison. It is the *chovas hayom* that matters, and as regards the *chovas hayom*, even *Rabbi Eliezer beRabbi Shimon* agrees: one *par lechatat* and one *ayil le'olah* for *Aharon* — precisely mirroring the *Miluim*. The parallel therefore stands regardless of which opinion one follows on the question of one ram or two.

## Rosh Hashanah Excluded

### Rosh Hashanah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, דִּפְרִישַׁת שִׁבְעָה לְיוֹם אֶחָד הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that the requirement derived is to sequester the priest prior to **Rosh HaShana, as** there, too, **it is sequestering of seven** days **for one day.**

Having addressed *Shemini Atzeret* and *Shavuot*, the *Gemara* now raises *Rosh Hashanah* as another candidate for the one-day event to which the *Miluim* seven-day preparation might apply. *Rosh Hashanah* is a one-day *Yom Tov*, and its *Musaf* includes a *par* and an *ayil* — *par ben bacar echad ayil echad* — alongside seven *kevasim*, as recorded in *Pinchas*: *uve'echad lachodesh hashvi'i... par ben bacar echad ayil echad kevasim bnei shana shiva tmimim*. All are *olot*. If one were looking merely for a one-day occasion with a *par* and an *ayil*, *Rosh Hashanah* presents itself as a plausible candidate.

### Rav Abbahu: Tzibur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: דָּנִין פָּר וָאַיִל שֶׁלּוֹ מִפָּר וָאַיִל שֶׁלּוֹ. לְאַפּוֹקֵי עֲצֶרֶת וְרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, דְּצִיבּוּר נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said** that this too is rejected: **One derives a bull and a ram** that the High Priest brings **from his own** property on Yom Kippur **from a bull and a ram** that Aaron brought **from his own** property at the inauguration. This is **to the exclusion of *Shavuot* and Rosh HaShana,** when the bull and the ram sacrificed are **from community** property and not owned by the priest.

Rav Abbahu answers: *danin par ve'ayil shelo mipar ve'ayil shelo, le'apukei Atzeret ve-Rosh Hashanah detzibur ninhu* — we compare one person's *par* and *ayil* to another person's *par* and *ayil*, thereby excluding both *Shavuot* and *Rosh Hashanah*, whose *korbanot* are *korban tzibur*. The *Miluim* offering was *Aharon*'s own; the *Yom Kippur* offering likewise belongs to *Aharon* personally. The *Musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah*, by contrast, is a communal *korban* that any *kohen* can bring on behalf of the entire community.

### Private vs. Communal

This distinction is highly significant for the question of the separation itself. The entire seven-day preparation is a separation of the *Kohen Gadol*. The reason for that separation is directly tied to the uniquely personal character of his role — it is his *korban*, his *Avodah*, his day. The *Musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah* carries no such personal dimension; any *kohen* can bring it, funded by communal *shekalim*, on behalf of the entire *tzibbur*. The *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur* share a personal dimension that *Rosh Hashanah* does not.

### Kach Lecha Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״קַח לְךָ״ — מִשֶּׁלְּךָ, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This** works out **well** according **to the one who said** that every time the Torah utilizes the phrase: **Take you,** it means **from your own** property. **However,** according **to the one who said** that when the Torah states both phrases it means **from communal** property, **what is there to say** to distinguish between Yom Kippur and the other days?

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this reasoning. *Hani nicha leman de'amar kach lecha mishelcha* — this works well if one holds that the phrase *kach lecha* means "take from your own funds." Both the *Miluim* passage (*vayomer el Aharon kach lecha egel ben bacar*) and the *Yom Kippur* passage (*kach lecha egel ben bacar lechatat*) use this phrase, indicating a private expenditure. But what of the view that even *kach lecha* refers to communal funds? A *baraita* records this debate:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״קַח לְךָ״ — מִשֶּׁלְּךָ, וַ״עֲשֵׂה לְךָ״ — מִשֶּׁלְּךָ, ״וְיִקְחוּ אֵלֶיךָ״ — מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה. רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן אוֹמֵר: בֵּין ״קַח לְךָ״, בֵּין ״וְיִקְחוּ אֵלֶיךָ״ — מִשֶּׁל צִבּוּר, וּמָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״קַח לְךָ״ — כִּבְיָכוֹל מִשֶּׁלְּךָ אֲנִי רוֹצֶה יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁלָּהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that when the Torah states: **Take you,** it means **from your own** property, **and** when it states: **Make you,** it means **from your own** property; however, when the Torah states: **And they will bring to you,** it means **from community** property. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yoshiya. Rabbi Yonatan says** that **both** when the Torah states: **Take you, and** when the Torah states: **And they will bring to you,** it means **from community** property. **And** for **what** purpose, then, **does the verse state: Take you,** which seems to mean from your own property? It should be understood, **as it were,** that God said to Moses: **I desire** that it come **from your** property **more than** I desire it **from theirs.** Therefore, the taking was attributed to Moses even though it was actually from community property.

*Kach lecha mishelcha ve'aseh lecha mishelcha* — wherever the *Torah* says *kach lecha* or *aseh lecha*, it means private funds. The contrast is with *vyikchu eleicha* — "they shall take to you" — as in *vyikchu eleicha shemen zayit zach*, where the plural subject and the preposition *eleicha* (rather than the reflexive *lecha*) indicate communal sourcing. This is the position of *Rabbi Yoshiyah*.

*Rabbi Yonatan* disagrees: *bein kach lecha bein vyikchu eleicha mishel tzibur* — regardless of the formulation, the funding is always communal. According to *Rabbi Yonatan*, even the offering brought on the eighth day of the *Miluim* came from communal funds, and there would be no basis for the distinction Rav Abbahu drew.

### Rabbi Yonatan View

The debate between *Rabbi Yoshiyah* and *Rabbi Yonatan* bears on various other instances of *kach lecha* and *aseh lecha* throughout the *Torah*. When the *Torah* instructs regarding the *shemen hamishcha* — *ve'atah kach lecha besamim rosh mor dror* — or regarding the *chatzotzrot* — *aseh lecha shtei chatzotzrot kesef* — was Moshe required to fund these from his own resources, or were these communal expenses? *Rabbi Yoshiyah* would say private; *Rabbi Yonatan* would say communal.

### Midrashic Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא חָנָן אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ לְּךָ אֲרוֹן עֵץ״, וְכָתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וְעָשׂוּ אֲרוֹן עֲצֵי שִׁטִּים״, הָא כֵּיצַד? כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂים רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם, כָּאן בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂים רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Ḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Elazar** that **one verse says: "And make you an ark of wood"** (Deuteronomy 10:1), indicating that it should be from your own property; **and one verse says** on the same subject: **"And they shall make an ark of acacia wood"** (Exodus 25:10), meaning from the Jewish people. **How** can **this** contradiction be resolved? **Here,** the verse is referring **to a time when the Jewish people perform the will of God** and they are credited with building the Ark of the Covenant. **There,** it is referring **to a time when the Jewish people do not perform the will of God,** and construction of the Ark is attributed to Moses alone. According to that opinion, there is no difference between the offerings of Yom Kippur and other offerings.

If the funding is always communal, the *Gemara* asks, *umah talmud lomar kach lecha?* — why does the *Torah* use this personal formulation at all? The answer given is: *kevayachol, mishelcha ani rotzeh yoter mishelahem* — as it were, God expresses a preference: I would rather it come from you than from them. *Rashi* offers two readings of this *kevayachol*. The first: if it were halakhically permissible for the offering to be privately funded, God would prefer that — but since it must be communal, it remains communal, with the personal language expressing a kind of divine wish. The second: the *kevayachol* itself signals that one should not speak too plainly of God preferring the offering of an individual over that of all of *Klal Yisrael* — hence the qualification "as it were." The notion of God preferring *Moshe* or *Aharon*'s private contribution to a gift from all of Israel is theologically striking, and the *Gemara* hedges it accordingly. Yet the *Torah*'s phrasing does seem to gesture toward the singular importance of these individuals in God's design.

### Consensus: Aharon's Offe…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי אֶלָּא בְּקִיחוֹת דְּעָלְמָא וַעֲשִׂיּוֹת דְּעָלְמָא. קִיחוֹת דְּעָלְמָא — ״קַח לְךָ סַמִּים״. עֲשִׂיּוֹת דְּעָלְמָא — ״עֲשֵׂה לְךָ שְׁתֵּי חֲצוֹצְרוֹת כֶּסֶף״. אֲבָל הָנָךְ פָּרוֹשֵׁי קָא מְפָרֵשׁ דְּמִשֶּׁלְּךָ הוּא. בְּמִלּוּאִים מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וְאֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל תְּדַבֵּר לֵאמֹר קְחוּ שְׂעִיר עִזִּים לְחַטָּאת״. ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֶל אַהֲרֹן קַח לְךָ עֵגֶל בֶּן בָּקָר לְחַטָּאת״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: ״קַח לְךָ״ — מִשֶּׁלְּךָ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **They disagree only with regard to** instances of **taking in general and** instances of **making in general:** Instances of **taking in general** are as in the verse: **"Take you spices"** (Exodus 30:34); and instances of **making in general** are as in the verse: **"Make you two silver trumpets"** (Numbers 10:2). **However,** in **these** cases of inauguration and of Yom Kippur **the verses explicitly** teach that the offerings must be **from your own** property. **With regard to** the **inauguration, now, since it is written: "And to the children of** Israel **you will speak, saying: Take a goat kid for a sin-offering** and an unblemished year-old calf and lamb for burnt-offerings" (Leviticus 9:3), with regard to the verse: **"And he said to Aaron: Take you a young calf for a sin-offering"** (Leviticus 9:2), **why do I** need this clear difference between the formulation of the command to the Jewish people and the formulation of the command to Aaron? **Learn from it** that in this context the phrase: **Take you,** means **from your own** property.

The *Gemara* ultimately concludes that in the specific cases of the *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur*, there is no debate — even *Rabbi Yonatan* would agree that these are *Aharon*'s private *korbanot*. The reason is that the *Torah* in both places explicitly contrasts *Aharon*'s offering with that of *Bnei Yisrael*. At the *Miluim*, the text first instructs *Aharon* — *vayomer el Aharon kach lecha egel ben bacar* — and then pivots to the community — *ul'vnei Yisrael tedaber leimor*. This explicit juxtaposition leaves no ambiguity: *kach lecha* here unmistakably means *Aharon*'s own. The phrase *Shma mina kach lecha mishelcha hu* — hear from this that *kach lecha* means from his own — is not merely a general inference but a conclusion mandated by context.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״בְּזֹאת יָבֹא אַהֲרֹן אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ בְּפַר בֶּן בָּקָר לְחַטָּאת וְגוֹ׳ ... וּמֵאֵת עֲדַת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל יִקַּח שְׁנֵי שְׂעִירֵי עִזִּים לְחַטָּאת״. ״וְהִקְרִיב אֶת פַּר הַחַטָּאת אֲשֶׁר לוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַאי ״לוֹ״ — מִשֶּׁלּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** And **with regard to Yom Kippur, now, since it is written: "With this Aaron will come into the Sanctuary, with a young bull for a sin-offering** and a ram for a burnt-offering" (Leviticus 16:3), with regard to the verse: **"And from the congregation of the children of Israel he will take two goat kids for a sin-offering** and one ram for a burnt-offering **and** Aaron **will offer his young bull as a sin-offering"** (Leviticus 16:5–6), **why do I** need the emphasis that the goats come from the property of the children of Israel? **Learn from it** that **this** term: **His,** written with regard to the calf, means it **is from his own** property.

Similarly on *Yom Kippur*, after *Aharon* is instructed to bring *par ben bacar lechatat ve'ayil le'olah*, the next verse distinguishes *ume'et adat Bnei Yisrael yikach shnei se'irei izim lechatat ve'ayil echad le'olah*, and then the text adds: *ve'hikriv Aharon es par hachatas asher lo* — "and *Aharon* shall sacrifice the *par* of the *chatat* that is his." Why does the *Torah* restate "that is his" (*asher lo*) once *Aharon*'s offering has already been contrasted with that of the community? *Shma mina, hai lo mishelo hu* — this teaches definitively that it is funded from *Aharon*'s personal resources.

*Tosafot* raises a pointed question here: why does any of this matter? The relevant legal distinction for purposes of the comparison to the *Miluim* is not who funded the offering but for whom it comes — whose *kapparah* it achieves. Both the *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur* offer a *korban* on behalf of *Aharon* himself; the *Musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah*, by contrast, comes on behalf of the entire *tzibbur*. That distinction is self-evident and requires no derivation about funding sources. *Tosafot* leaves this as an open question. Nevertheless, the *Gemara*'s essential point stands: the *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur* share an intimate personal character — they are *Aharon*'s *korban*, for *Aharon*, from *Aharon* — that the communal *Musaf* of *Rosh Hashanah* simply does not replicate.

## Alternative Models

### Rav Ashi: Par & Ayil

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: דָּנִין פַּר לְחַטָּאת וְאַיִל לְעוֹלָה מִפַּר לְחַטָּאת וְאַיִל לְעוֹלָה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה וַעֲצֶרֶת — דְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ עוֹלוֹת נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi stated** another reason that distinguishes Yom Kippur from Rosh HaShana and *Shavuot*. **One derives** the **bull for a sin-offering and ram for a burnt-offering** written with regard to Yom Kippur **from** the **bull for a sin-offering and ram for a burnt-offering** written with regard to the inauguration, **to the exclusion of Rosh HaShana and *Shavuot*, on which both of them,** the bull and the ram, **are burnt-offerings.**

Rav Ashi now offers a cleaner and more direct answer, one that cuts through the entire question of funding: *danin par lechatat ve'ayil le'olah mipar lechatat ve'ayil le'olah, le'apukei Rosh Hashanah ve'Shemini Atzeret detarvaihu olas ninhu* — we derive from a case of *par lechatat ve'ayil le'olah* only another case sharing that identical structure, thereby excluding *Rosh Hashanah* and *Shemini Atzeret*, where the comparable offerings are both *olot* rather than a *par lechatat* paired with an *ayil le'olah*. There is no need to invoke the question of private versus communal funding. The structural dissimilarity in the *korban* itself is sufficient.

### Ravina: Kohen Gadol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: דָּנִין עֲבוֹדָה בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל מֵעֲבוֹדָה בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, לְאַפּוֹקֵי כּוּלְּהוּ קוּשְׁיָיתִין — דְּלָאו עֲבוֹדָה בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina stated** another distinction: **One derives** a matter that is restricted to the **service** performed **by the High Priest,** Yom Kippur, **from** a matter that is restricted to the **service** performed **by the High Priest,** the inauguration, which was performed by Aaron, **to the exclusion of all the difficulties** raised from the beginning of the discussion, **as** on the other potential days raised, **they are not** restricted to **service** performed **by the High Priest;** rather, the service on those days may be performed by any priest.

Ravina offers yet another approach: *danin avoda be'kohen gadol me'avoda be'kohen gadol, le'apukei kulu kushyasen d'lav avoda be'kohen gadol ninhu* — we compare an *Avodah* performed by a *Kohen Gadol* to another *Avodah* performed by a *Kohen Gadol*, thereby elegantly excluding all the problematic candidates at once. *Shemini Atzeret*, *Shavuot*, and *Rosh Hashanah* all have *Musaf* offerings that any *kohen* can perform — there is nothing unique about the *Kohen Gadol*'s role. The *Miluim*, by contrast, was *Aharon*'s singular moment of initiation as *Kohen Gadol*, and *Yom Kippur* is the one day each year on which the *Avodah* uniquely requires the *Kohen Gadol*. The separation is thus grounded not merely in a formal parallel of offerings, but in the nature of the *Avodah* itself as belonging specifically to the *Kohen Gadol*.

### Ravina Alt: Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי אָמַר רָבִינָא: דָּנִין עֲבוֹדָה תְּחִלָּה מֵעֲבוֹדָה תְּחִלָּה, לְאַפּוֹקֵי הָנֵי — דְּלָאו תְּחִלָּה נִינְהוּ. מַאי תְּחִלָּה? אִילֵּימָא תְּחִלָּה בְּכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל — הַיְינוּ קַמַּיְיתָא. אֶלָּא, עֲבוֹדָה תְּחִלָּה בַּמָּקוֹם מֵעֲבוֹדָה תְּחִלָּה בַּמָּקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** that **Ravina said: One derives** a matter that is an **initial service from an initial service, to the exclusion of all these that are not initial** services. That statement of Ravina is unclear, and the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **initial** service? **If we say** that **initial** service **means** one performed **by the High Priest; that is** identical to **the first** version of Ravina's statement. **Rather,** it may be understood as follows: One derives **the initial service** performed **in the place,** the Holy of Holies, on Yom Kippur, **from the initial service** performed **in the place,** the Tabernacle, on the eighth day of the inauguration. Therefore, it is the service of Yom Kippur alone that is derived from the inauguration.

An alternate version of Ravina's position is also recorded: *danin avoda techila bemakom me'avoda techila bemakom* — we compare an initial *Avodah* in a particular place to another initial *Avodah* in a particular place. Here the emphasis shifts from the identity of the performer to the nature of the space being inaugurated.

### Avodah Techila Explained

The *Gemara* asks: *mai techila?* — what does "initial" mean in this context? If it means "first time a *Kohen Gadol* functioned" — well, the *Miluim* was the very first time *Aharon* served as *Kohen Gadol*, and by extension, the first time anyone played that role. What corresponds on *Yom Kippur*? *Rashi* explains: the first occasion on which the *Kohen Gadol* had the opportunity to perform a *korban tzibbur* requiring uniquely the *Kohen Gadol* would have been *Yom Kippur*. But the *Gemara* notes that this formulation collapses back into the previous point about the *Kohen Gadol* — *hainu kamaisa*. The genuinely distinct reading, therefore, is: *avoda techila bemakom* — the first initiation of a place. On the *Miluim*, it was the first time the *Avodah* was performed officially with *kohanim* on the *mizbeach hachitzon*. (The seven preparatory days prior involved *Moshe*, not the *kohanim*, and were not official *Avodah* in the full sense.) On *Yom Kippur*, it was the first time an *Avodah* was performed in the *heichal* and the *kodesh kodashim*.

### Preparation Philosophy

This range of approaches is worth pausing to appreciate, because they reflect genuinely different understandings of what the seven-day preparation is for. One approach — the *Kohen Gadol* model — suggests that the preparation is about the person: this is a day that uniquely requires the *Kohen Gadol*, and he must ready himself for a role that is his alone. The preparation is an inward process of getting oneself to the appropriate level, sanctifying oneself in one's person for the singular responsibility one must bear. Another approach — the *avoda techila bemakom* model — shifts the focus from the person to the place. The preparation is about the relationship between the person and the sacred space he is about to enter. It is not simply that he needs to be prepared; he needs to be prepared *in order to enter that space properly*, because the space makes an absolute demand on whoever crosses its threshold. *Bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh* — by this shall *Aharon* enter the sanctuary — underscores that the entry itself is conditioned on a prior process. These are related but genuinely different orientations, and the shiur's final discussion will return to this distinction.

## Parah Adumah Status

### Parah Differences

Now that the *Gemara* has so thoroughly established the parallels between the *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur* — identical *korbanot* of *par lechatat ve'ayil le'olah*, both belonging personally to *Aharon*, both uniquely requiring the *Kohen Gadol*, both constituting inaugural *Avodot* in their respective sacred spaces — one element that the present discussion has quietly pushed aside becomes conspicuous by contrast: *Parah Adumah*. The *Parah Adumah* requires no *Kohen Gadol*, is not performed in the *Beit Hamikdash*, involves no *par lechatat ve'ayil le'olah*, and shares none of the features that have been marshaled to connect *Yom Kippur* to the *Miluim*. The *Gemara* now addresses this directly.

### R. Yochanan: YK Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַתְנֵי חֲדָא, [רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי] מַתְנֵי תַּרְתֵּי. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַתְנֵי חֲדָא: ״לַעֲשׂוֹת לְכַפֵּר״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia **he said: Rabbi Yoḥanan taught one** case derived from the inauguration, while **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught two.** The Gemara elaborates. **Rabbi Yoḥanan taught one: To do, to make atonement; these are the actions** performed on **Yom Kippur** that require sequestering beforehand, like the inauguration.

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *matni chada* — Rabbi Yochanan taught only one application. *La'asos lekaper eilu ma'asei Yom Hakippurim* — from the *Miluim* verse he derives only the *Avodas Yom Hakippurim*, not the *Parah Adumah*. After everything we have seen about how precisely *Yom Kippur* mirrors the *Miluim*, this position makes eminent sense. Every element of comparison has pointed to *Yom Kippur*; *Parah Adumah* shares none of them.

### R. Yehoshua: Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** [וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי] מַתְנֵי תַּרְתֵּי: ״לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה פָרָה, ״לְכַפֵּר״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught two: To do, these are the actions** performed in the burning **of the** red **heifer; to make atonement, these are the actions** performed on **Yom Kippur.** Both require sequestering.

Yet *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* taught two applications — or, according to some, it was *Reish Lakish* who taught both: *la'asos eilu ma'asei parah, lekaper eilu ma'asei Yom Hakippurim* — the word *la'asot* is taken to refer to the *Avodas Parah* and *lekaper* to the *Avodas Yom Hakippurim*. This is, of course, the reading we encountered on the previous *daf*, deriving both practices from the single verse in the *Miluim*.

### Maala B'alma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מַתְנֵי חֲדָא? וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: שִׁבְעַת יָמִים קוֹדֶם יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים, וְשִׁבְעַת יָמִים קוֹדֶם שְׂרֵיפַת הַפָּרָה! מַעֲלָה בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: And did **Rabbi Yoḥanan teach** only **one** case derived from the inauguration, i.e., Yom Kippur? **Didn't we learn** explicitly in the mishna: **Seven days prior to Yom Kippur,** and in another mishna: **Seven days prior to the burning of the heifer,** the Sages would remove the priest from his home? Apparently, there are two cases in which the priest is sequestered. The Gemara answers: With regard to sequestering the priest prior to the burning of the heifer, the Sages **merely** established **a higher standard.** They issued a decree to underscore the sanctity of the ritual after they permitted its performance by a priest who immersed that day. There is no Torah source for the sequestering of the priest in that case.

If *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that only *Yom Kippur* is derived from the *Miluim*, then the *Mishnah*'s mention of a seven-day preparation for *Parah Adumah* requires a different explanation. The *Gemara* responds: *ma'ala b'alma* — it was simply an added stringency, not a biblical derivation. The seven-day preparation for the *kohen* performing *Parah Adumah* was a rabbinic enhancement, not a requirement rooted in the *Miluim*.

### Tzaddukim Context

This fits seamlessly into the broader context of the *Parah Adumah* procedure. As *Tosafot* observes, the Rabbis had introduced a notable leniency into the *Parah Adumah* — the burning could be performed by a *tevul yom*, a *kohen* who had immersed in the *mikveh* that very day but had not yet waited until nightfall for the sunset that would complete his purification. This leniency was deliberate: *le'hotzi mi-liban shel Tzaddukim* — to confute the *Tzaddukim*, who insisted that only one who had fully completed his purification cycle (i.e., who had waited until nightfall after immersion) could perform the *Parah*. The Rabbis, by using a *tevul yom*, publicly demonstrated their rejection of the *Tzaddukim*'s position. Having accepted that unusual leniency, they compensated by heaping on a series of additional stringencies — and the seven-day separation was one of them. It was not derived from the *Miluim*; it was a *ma'ala b'alma*, a rabbinic enhancement in the context of this particular controversy.

### Yochanan Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי מִנְיוֹמֵי בַּר חִלְקִיָּה אָמַר רַבִּי מַחְסֵיָא בַּר אִידֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה בַּיּוֹם הַזֶּה צִוָּה ה׳ לַעֲשׂוֹת לְכַפֵּר עֲלֵיכֶם״, ״לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה פָרָה, ״לְכַפֵּר״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים! הָהוּא דְּרַבֵּיהּ. דְּכִי אֲתָא רָבִין, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״לַעֲשׂוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה פָרָה, ״לְכַפֵּר״ — אֵלּוּ מַעֲשֵׂה יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't Rabbi Minyomi bar Ḥilkiya say** that **Rabbi Maḥseya bar Idi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that the verse states: **"As has been done this day, so the Lord has commanded to do, to make atonement for you"** (Leviticus 8:34), from which it is derived: **To do, these are the actions** performed in the burning **of the** red **heifer; to make atonement, these are the actions** performed on **Yom Kippur?** Apparently, even Rabbi Yoḥanan taught two cases derived from inauguration. The Gemara resolves the difficulty: **That is** the opinion **of his teacher;** however, he himself disagrees. **As when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: To do, these are the actions** performed in the burning **of the** red **heifer; to atone, these are the actions** performed on **Yom Kippur.** That which Rabbi Manyumei cited in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan was the opinion of his teacher, Rabbi Yishmael.

A difficulty arises, however, from another *Amoraic* tradition. A chain of transmission — *Rabbi Minyumi bar Chelkiya* quoting *Rabbi Machasya bar Idia* quoting *Rabbi Yochanan* — records that *Rabbi Yochanan* himself interpreted the *Miluim* verse as deriving both *Parah* and *Yom Kippur*: *la'asos eilu ma'asei parah, lekaper eilu ma'asei Yom Hakippurim*. How can both traditions bear the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*?

The resolution: *hahi deRabbay* — the teaching that derives both is not *Rabbi Yochanan* speaking in his own name, but *Rabbi Yochanan* transmitting the position of his *rebbe*, namely *Rabbi*. When *Ravin* came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he clarified: *Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yishmael* — that the full *baraita*-like derivation of *la'asos eilu ma'asei parah, lekaper eilu ma'asei Yom Hakippurim* was a tradition *Rabbi Yochanan* conveyed in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael*, not his own independent ruling. *Rabbi Yochanan* in his own name derived only *Yom Kippur*.

*Tosafot* notes that the *Gemara* could simply have resolved the contradiction by saying it is a dispute among *Amoraim* about what *Rabbi Yochanan* actually held — a formula the *Gemara* uses elsewhere. The present resolution, however, is more precise: one tradition is in his own name, the other in the name of a prior authority.

Stepping back: we now have two fundamental positions. Either both *Parah Adumah* and *Yom Kippur* are derived from the *Miluim* — in which case we are looking for something more generic that ties all three together — or only *Yom Kippur* is derived from the *Miluim*, and the seven-day preparation for *Parah Adumah* is a *ma'ala b'alma* rooted in the *Tzaddukim* controversy. The latter view is in many respects more intellectually satisfying: having now demonstrated so exhaustively the precise correspondences between the *Miluim* and *Yom Kippur*, the derivation of *Yom Kippur* alone makes the analogy far more compelling and coherent. The *Parah Adumah*, for all its importance, simply does not share the profile.

## Reish Lakish Challenge

### Me'akev Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מֵהֵיכָא קָא יָלְפַתְּ לַהּ — מִמִּלּוּאִים, אִי מָה מִלּוּאִים כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי — כׇּל הַכָּתוּב בָּהֶן מְעַכֵּב בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the sequestering of the priest, **Reish Lakish said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: From where did you derive this** principle of sequestering? You derived it **from** the **inauguration. If so, just as** with regard to the **inauguration,** failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** it, **so too here,** with regard to Yom Kippur, failure to perform **all** the details **that are written in its** regard **invalidates** the Yom Kippur service. All the *halakhot* of sequestering must be precisely observed.

*Reish Lakish* now challenges *Rabbi Yochanan* directly: *mehacha kayalphat?* — is this really where you derive the *Yom Kippur* preparation? If the *Miluim* is your source, there is a significant problem. By the *Miluim*, anything written in the passage is *me'akev* — legally essential. The *Gemara* will establish this principle explicitly in a later discussion: every detail of the *Miluim* was indispensable to the validity of the process, and failure to observe any of them would have invalidated the entire consecration. If we learn the seven-day preparation from the *Miluim*, should it not likewise be *me'akev*? Should a *Kohen Gadol* who did not undergo the full seven-day separation be disqualified from performing the *Yom Kippur Avodah*?

### Backup Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכִי נָמֵי, וְהָתְנַן: ״וּמַתְקִינִין לוֹ כֹּהֵן אַחֵר״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״מַפְרִישִׁין״! וְכִי תֵּימָא: מַאי ״מַתְקִינִין״ — ״מַפְרִישִׁין״, לִיתְנֵי אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״מַתְקִינִין״, אוֹ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי ״מַפְרִישִׁין״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say: Indeed, that** is so; **didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And they would designate another priest in his stead, and it is not taught** with regard to the designated priest: Seven days before Yom Kippur **they remove** him from his house, although ultimately he may perform the Yom Kippur service. Apparently, failure to sequester the priest does not invalidate the service. **And if you say** in response: **What** is the meaning of: **They would designate?** It means: **They would remove;** that is implausible. Were that so, **let** the mishna **teach either** with regard to both **this** High Priest **and that** designated replacement: **They would designate; or** with regard to both **this** High Priest **and that** designated replacement: **They would remove.**

*Reish Lakish* adduces evidence from the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* states: *u-matkinin lo kohen acher* — a backup *kohen* is designated in case the *Kohen Gadol* becomes unfit. Now, does this substitute require a seven-day separation of his own? The *Mishnah* uses the word *matkinin* for the backup — a different verb entirely from *mafrishin*, "they separate," which is used for the *Kohen Gadol* himself. If the backup also required seven days, the *Mishnah* would have used consistent language, or at least mentioned the requirement explicitly. The absence of any mention of a seven-day preparation for the substitute — *ve-lo tani mafrishin* — is telling. The *Mishnah* presumably envisions that the backup, if called upon, performs the *Avodah* without having undergone the full seven-day preparation.

### Not Biblical

If a *Kohen Gadol* can perform the *Yom Kippur Avodah* validly without seven days of prior separation, then that seven-day preparation is clearly not *me'akev*. But if it is not *me'akev*, it cannot have been derived from the *Miluim*, where everything written is *me'akev*. *Reish Lakish* therefore concludes: the seven-day preparation cannot be learned from *Miluim* — and *Rabbi Yochanan* must look elsewhere.

## Sinai Source

### Yochanan Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא מָר מֵהֵיכָא יָלֵיף לַהּ? אָמַר: מִסִּינַי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּשְׁכּוֹן כְּבוֹד ה׳ עַל הַר סִינַי וַיְכַסֵּהוּ הֶעָנָן שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקְרָא אֶל מֹשֶׁה בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״, מַאי ״שֵׁשֶׁת יָמִים״? זֶה בָּנָה אָב: שֶׁכׇּל הַנִּכְנָס בְּמַחֲנֵה שְׁכִינָה, טָעוּן פְּרִישַׁת שִׁשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **Rather, from where do** you, **Master, derive** the *halakha* of sequestering before Yom Kippur? Reish Lakish **said to him:** I derive it **from Sinai, as it is written: "And the glory of the Lord abode upon Mount Sinai and the cloud covered him [*vaykhasehu*] six days, and He called to Moses on the seventh day** from the midst of the cloud" (Exodus 24:16). The masculine suffix *hu* in *vaykhasehu* can be interpreted either as him, referring to Moses, or as it, referring to the mountain. **Now, since it states: "And He called to Moses on the seventh day," what is** derived from the previous explicit mention of **six days? These** six days are mentioned as **a paradigm,** from which a general principle is derived **that anyone who enters the camp of the Divine Presence,** the site of the revelation at Mount Sinai, or the place where the Divine Presence rests, the Holy of Holies, **requires** prior **sequestering for six** days of sanctification.

*Rabbi Yochanan* accepts the challenge and pivots to an entirely different source: *amar mi-Sinai* — I derive it from *Har Sinai*. The seven-day preparation is not learned from the *Miluim* at all; it is learned from what happened when *Moshe* ascended to receive the *Torah*.

### Sinai Verse

The scriptural basis: *vayishkon kvod Hashem al Har Sinai vayichasehu he'anan sheshet yamim, vayikra el Moshe bayom hashvi'i* — the glory of God rested on *Har Sinai* and the cloud covered it — or covered *Moshe*, as *Rashi* understands — for six days, and on the seventh day God called to *Moshe*. Moshe was present in the divine ambient for six days, enveloped in the cloud at the foot or on the slopes of the mountain, before being summoned to ascend to the summit for the definitive encounter with God.

### Machane Shechina

The *Gemara* derives a general principle: *shekol hanichnas bemachaneh shechina teun perishat shisha* — anyone who is to enter into the *machaneh shechina* requires a six-day preparation. The *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* is entering into the most intimate divine presence — *bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh* — and therefore requires the six-day preparation that Moshe underwent before ascending *Har Sinai*.

### Machane Technical

A terminological clarification is needed here. The formal halakhic designation *machaneh shechina* refers to the sanctity of the *azarah*, the Temple courtyard. Yet ordinary *kohanim* entered the *azarah* regularly, and *Israelites* could enter it without any prior preparatory period; all that was required was *tahara*. Clearly, it is not entry into the *azarah* that mandates a six-day preparation. In this context, *machaneh shechina* carries a more restricted meaning: it refers to entry into the *kodesh* and the *kodesh kodashim* — perhaps even more precisely, only the *kodesh kodashim* itself, since ordinary *kohanim* entered the *heichal* to light the *menorah* and arrange the *shulchan* without any extended preparation.

### Seventh Day Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן שִׁבְעָה תְּנַן! מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא הִיא, דְּחָיֵישׁ לְטוּמְאַת בֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Wasn't it seven,** not six, days of sequestering that **we learned** in the mishna? Reish Lakish answered: **The mishna** that requires sequestering for seven days **is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, who is concerned about the ritual impurity of** the priest's **home,** i.e., his wife. This is done lest he become impure through relations with a menstruating woman, which is ritual impurity lasting seven days. Therefore, he is removed from his home for seven days.

If the biblical basis is six days, why does the *Mishnah* specify seven? *Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira*'s concern provides the answer: the seventh day is a rabbinic addition driven by anxiety over *tumat beito* — the potential impurity of his household, specifically his wife. The concern is that if the *Kohen Gadol* has marital relations with his wife on the sixth night — the night leading into what would be the sixth full day of preparation — his wife might become a *niddah* during or immediately after the act. In such a case, even if he had no knowledge of it at the time, he would be rendered *tamei* for seven days, since the *Torah* teaches that a man who has relations with a *niddah* is *tamei* for seven days. To guard against any possibility of this *tumah* still being present on *Yom Kippur*, the Rabbis added a seventh day of separation from his household. The six-day preparation is biblical; the seventh is rabbinic.

### Philosophy Shift

It is worth pausing on the profound conceptual difference between this source and the *Miluim* model. The *Miluim* model points to a structured process of sanctification — preparing the *kohen* for a special *Avodah*, analogous to the inauguration of the *Mishkan* itself. It carries a somewhat formal, institutional character: you are being readied for a particular role, in a particular place, through a prescribed process of consecration. There is a sense in which the preparation is about achieving the right status or condition for the *Avodah* one is about to perform.

The *Sinai* model is categorically different. *Moshe* was not being sanctified to perform a particular *Avodah*. He was preparing himself to encounter God. The six days were not about status or role — they were about the quality of presence one brings to a meeting with the divine. *Machane shechina* here is not a technical halakhic category but a description of the most intimate proximity to God's presence. The *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* is not merely performing the *Avodah*; he is entering into an encounter — *bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh*. The entry into the *kodesh kodashim* is not a procedural step but an ascent.

This distinction may illuminate how *Yom Kippur* is experienced more broadly. The days of *Elul* begin a process of drawing near; the *Aseret Yemei Teshuva* deepen it; and on *Yom Kippur* itself, one finally arrives at the summit — just as *Moshe* spent six days in the cloud before being called into the full divine presence on the seventh. The *Kohen Gadol*'s seven-day preparation, understood through the *Sinai* paradigm, becomes a map for the entire season of *teshuva* and encounter with God: a gradual approach, day by day, through the cloud, toward the moment of the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry into the *kodesh kodashim* — which is, for all of *Klal Yisrael*, the moment of standing before God.
