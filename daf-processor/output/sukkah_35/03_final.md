# Sukkah 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Hadasim/Aravot

### Excess Hadasim/Aravot

The daf picks up at the *mishnah* at the bottom of 33b. Before moving to the *mishnah* itself, two prefatory observations are worth noting. The first concerns a widespread practice — attested already among the *rishonim* and continuing to this day — of taking many more than the minimum number of *hadasim*, and sometimes also *aravot*. The obvious question this raises is how such a practice is permitted, given the general principle of *bal tosif*: adding to a *mitzvah* beyond its prescribed form.

### Tosafot: Not Bal Tosif

*Tosafot*, in the discussion beginning *arba nachal shtayim*, addresses this directly. *Tosafot* writes that *afilu noten kama hadasim ve-kama aravot be-lulav ein zeh bal tosif* — even if one takes many, many *hadasim* and *aravot*, this does not constitute *bal tosif*. The qualification is *afilu man d'tzarich eged*, that even according to the opinion that requires a binding, the problem only arises *ella im ken mosif min acher* — if one adds an extraneous species. The statement clearly reflects and seeks to justify a widespread practice.

### Min vs. Max/Yishmael

One way to understand why adding more *hadasim* and *aravot* is not a violation is to read the prescribed numbers as a minimum rather than a maximum. This reading gains support from the structure of the *arba minim* itself: the *lulav* and *etrog* are singular by nature, while the *hadasim* and *aravot* are inherently grouped as bunches, with the numbers three and two representing merely the minimum size of each bunch. This conceptual framing also helps explain the position of Rabbi Yishmael, who permits two of the *hadasim* to be trimmed at the top while one remains intact. If the *hadasim* are conceived less as discrete individual units and more as a collective bunch, this allowance becomes considerably more intuitive.

## Etrog Invalidity Cases

### Gazul/Yavesh/Ashera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֶתְרוֹג הַגָּזוּל וְהַיָּבֵשׁ — פָּסוּל. שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **An *etrog*** that was **stolen or is** completely **dry is unfit.** One **from a tree worshipped as idolatry [*asheira*] or from a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry **is unfit.**

The *mishnah* opens its treatment of the *etrog* with several cases of invalidity that parallel the structure seen in earlier *mishnayot* for the other *minim*. An *etrog ha-gazul*, a stolen *etrog*, is *pasul*. An *etrog ha-yavesh*, a dried-up *etrog*, is likewise *pasul*. An *etrog shel ashera* — from a tree used for idolatrous worship — or *shel ir ha-nidachat* — from a condemned city — is also *pasul*.

### Orlah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל עׇרְלָה — פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** An *etrog* that is **fruit that grew on a tree during the three years after it was planted [*orla*] is unfit,** because it is prohibited to eat and derive benefit from it.

The *mishnah* then introduces a category unique to the *etrog*: *shel orlah pasul* — an *etrog* from a tree within its first three years of growth is invalid. As will be developed in the *Gemara*, the *etrog* carries a distinct requirement that it be edible, or at least potentially edible, in order to be *kosher*. The *orlah* prohibition, which bars consumption of the fruit during those years, thus strikes at this foundational requirement.

### Terumah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה — פָּסוּל. שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** An *etrog* **of impure *teruma* is unfit.** With regard to an *etrog* **of pure *teruma*, one may not take it** *ab initio*, **and if one took it, it is fit,** and he fulfilled his obligation after the fact.

The *mishnah* continues: *shel trumah tmei'ah pasul* — a *tamei* *terumah* *etrog*, which cannot be eaten, is invalid. By contrast, *shel trumah tehorah* — a *tahor* *terumah* *etrog*, which may be eaten by *kohanim* — presents a different picture: *lo yitol*, one should not take it *lechatchila*, *ve-im natal kasher*, but if one did take it, it is valid. The reasons for the *lechatchila* prohibition will be examined in the *Gemara*.

### Demai Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל דְּמַאי — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי פּוֹסְלִין, וּבֵית הִלֵּל מַכְשִׁירִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an *etrog* of ***demai*,** which is produce acquired from an *am ha'aretz*, who does not reliably tithe his produce, **Beit Shammai deem it unfit, and Beit Hillel deem it fit.**

*Shel demai* — an *etrog* from an *am ha-aretz* whose tithing status is uncertain — is the subject of a dispute. Biblically, one may assume such produce is permissible, but the Rabbis required the separation of *trumot u-ma'asrot* nonetheless. The one thing one could assume had already been separated is *terumah gedolah*, so the produce occupies a kind of intermediate status. *Beit Shammai poslin* — *Beit Shammai* declares it invalid — *u-Beit Hillel machshirin* — and *Beit Hillel* declares it *kosher*. It is presumably self-evident that ordinary *tevel*, untithed produce, would be invalid since it cannot be eaten.

### Ma'aser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם — לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to an *etrog* **of second tithe in Jerusalem, one may not take it** *ab initio*; **and if he took it, it is fit.**

*Shel ma'aser sheni bi-Yerushalayim* raises a case where the *etrog* is indeed edible — in fact there is a *mitzvah* to eat it — but one's relationship to it as owner may be constrained. The *mishnah* rules *lo yitol* — do not take it *lechatchila* — *ve-im natal kasher* — but if one did take it, it is valid. The rationale for both the prohibition and the *bediavad* validity will be examined in the *Gemara*, but the recurring structure is already apparent: edibility, or the potential for it, is the central criterion.

### Aesthetic Defects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלְתָה חֲזָזִית עַל רוּבּוֹ, נִטְּלָה פִּטְמָתוֹ, נִקְלַף, נִסְדַּק, נִיקַּב וְחָסַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — פָּסוּל. עָלְתָה חֲזָזִית עַל מִיעוּטוֹ, נִטַּל עוּקְצוֹ, נִיקַּב וְלֹא חָסַר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** If **boil-like blemishes arose on** the **majority** of the *etrog*; if **its pestle**-like protuberance on the upper, blossom end **was removed;** if the *etrog* **was peeled, split, or pierced and is missing any amount, it is unfit.** However, if **boil-like blemishes arose** only **on its minority; if its stem,** which connects it to the tree, **was removed; or it was pierced** but **is** not **missing any amount, it is fit.**

The *mishnah* then turns to aesthetic defects. *Alta chazazit al rubo* — if a wart-like growth covers the majority of the *etrog* — it is invalid. *Natal pitamto* — if the *pitam*, the small mushroom-shaped protrusion at the top, has been removed — it is invalid. *Niklaf* — if peeled — *nisdak* — if cracked — *nikav ve-chasar kol shehu* — if it has a hole and something is missing as a result — these are all invalid. Conversely, if the *chazazit* is on a minority of the *etrog*, if the *oketz* (the stem) was removed rather than the *pitam*, or if there is a hole but nothing is actually missing, then *kasher* — the *etrog* remains valid.

### Color/Size Debates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶתְרוֹג הַכּוּשִׁי — פָּסוּל, וְהַיָּרוֹק כְּכַרָּתֵי — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַכְשִׁיר וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה פּוֹסֵל. שִׁיעוּר אֶתְרוֹג הַקָּטָן, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כֶּאֱגוֹז, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר כְּבֵיצָה. וּבַגָּדוֹל — כְּדֵי שֶׁיֹּאחַז שְׁנַיִם בְּיָדוֹ. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו.
>
> **Translation:** A Cushite *etrog*,** which is black like a Cushite, **is unfit.** And with regard to an *etrog* **that is leek green, Rabbi Meir deems it fit and Rabbi Yehuda deems it unfit.** What is **the** minimum **measure of a small *etrog*? Rabbi Meir says:** It may be no smaller than **a walnut-bulk. Rabbi Yehuda says:** It may be no smaller than **an egg-bulk. And in a large** *etrog*, the maximum measure is **so that one could hold two in his one hand;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says:** It is fit **even** if it is so large that he can hold only **one in his two hands.**

The *mishnah* closes with questions of color and size. *Etrog ha-kushi pasul* — a black *etrog* is invalid. If it is as green as a leek (*yarok ke-karti*), *Rabbi Meir machshir* — Rabbi Meir declares it valid — *Rabbi Yehuda posel* — Rabbi Yehuda declares it invalid. On size: *Rabbi Meir omer ke'egoz* — the minimum is the size of a walnut — while *Rabbi Yehuda omer ke-beitzah* — the minimum is the size of an egg. As for the maximum, Rabbi Yehuda requires that one be able to hold two in one hand; Rabbi Yose permits one so large it requires both hands to hold. As was noted in an earlier *Gemara*, these debates need not be read purely as disputes about *hadar* — beauty — but may reflect questions about *gmar peira*, whether the fruit is considered fully ripened and complete, or concerns about dropping it in the case of excessive size.

## Etrog Species ID

### Hadar Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״פְּרִי עֵץ הָדָר״, עֵץ שֶׁטַּעַם עֵצוֹ וּפִרְיוֹ שָׁוֶה — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה אֶתְרוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** that the verse states: **"Fruit of a beautiful tree,"** meaning, **a tree that the taste of its tree** trunk **and** the taste of its **fruit are alike.** What tree is that? **You must say it is** the ***etrog*** tree.

The *Gemara* opens at the top of 35b. *Tanu rabbanan*: *pri etz hadar* — the fruit of a beautiful tree — is interpreted as *shetaam etzo u-piryo shaveh*, a tree whose wood and fruit share the same flavor. The reading draws on the juxtaposition of *pri* and *etz*: just as there is a *midrash* on *maaseh bereishit* where God commands *etz pri oseh pri* — that the wood of the tree itself should taste like its fruit — so here the *Torah* alludes to a tree whose bark carries something of the taste of its fruit. *Hevei omer zeh etrog* — this must refer to the *etrog*. While one need not assume the bark is digestible, it does carry that characteristic taste.

### Pepper Vine Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא פִּלְפְּלִין; כִּדְתַנְיָא, הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּנְטַעְתֶּם כׇּל עֵץ״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁהוּא עֵץ מַאֲכָל? מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עֵץ מַאֲכָל״, עֵץ שֶׁטַּעַם עֵצוֹ וּפִרְיוֹ שָׁוֶה — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה פִּלְפְּלִין. לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁהַפִּלְפְּלִין חַיָּיבִין בְּעׇרְלָה. וְאֵין אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל חֲסֵרָה כְּלוּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תֶחְסַר כֹּל בָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And say** that it is referring to the **pepper** tree, since the taste of its trunk and the taste of its fruit are alike, **as it was taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "When you enter the land and plant any tree for food you shall regard its fruit as *orla*" (Leviticus 19:23). **Rabbi Meir would say** that **by inference from that which is stated** "and plant any tree," **don't I know that it is** referring to **a tree** that produces **food? Rather,** for **what** purpose **does the verse state: "Any tree for food"?** It is **to include a tree that the taste of its tree** trunk **and** the taste of its **fruit are alike. And which** tree **is this? You must say this is the pepper** tree. This comes **to teach you that the peppers,** and even its trunk, are edible, and therefore the tree is **obligated** in the prohibition of ***orla*. And Eretz Yisrael lacks nothing, as it is stated: "A land where you shall eat bread without scarceness, you shall lack nothing"** (Deuteronomy 8:9). From where, then, is it derived that the Torah commands the taking of an *etrog* as one of the four species? Perhaps the verse is referring to peppers.

The *Gemara* immediately asks: *ve-eima pilpelin* — perhaps this refers to pepper? Pepper, it turns out, is a perennial woody vine whose vine itself apparently carries a peppery flavor. The *Gemara* supports this identification from a *baraita*: *hayah Rabbi Meir omer*, since the verse dealing with *orlah* says *u-netaatem kol etz* — you shall plant any tree — and then speaks of the fruit, why must it specify *etz maachal*, a food tree? *Mah talmud lomar etz maachal* — to tell us that even the tree itself is a food, that its wood carries the taste of the fruit. *Hevei omer zeh pilpelin* — this refers to the pepper vine. The *baraita* derives two lessons from this hint: first, that peppercorns are obligated in *orlah*, lest one think that since they grow on a vine they are not considered fruit; and second, *ve-ein eretz Yisrael chasera klum* — the land of Israel lacks nothing, even peppercorns, as the verse says *lo techsar kol bo*. So one might conclude that the pepper vine, whose bark tastes like its fruit, is the *pri etz hadar*.

### Pepper Impracticality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? נִנְקוֹט חֲדָא — לָא מִינַּכְרָא לְקִיחָתַהּ. נִנְקוֹט תְּרֵי אוֹ תְּלָתָא — (אֶחָד) אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה פֵּירוֹת, הִלְכָּךְ לָא אֶפְשָׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to the four species, it is clear that the Torah is not referring to peppers, **due to** the fact **that it is not possible** to utilize peppers for this purpose. **How shall we proceed?** If **we take one** pepper, **its taking is not noticeable** due to its small size. If **we take two or three** peppers, **the Torah said one** fruit **and not two or three fruits. Therefore, it is impossible.** The verse "the fruit of a beautiful tree" cannot be referring to peppers.

The *Gemara* rejects this possibility: *mishum d'lo efshar* — it is simply not feasible. *Heichi na'avid* — what would one do? *Ninkot chada* — take one peppercorn? *Lo mitchazei lekichato* — that would not constitute a visible, evident act of taking, as the verse *u-lekachtem lachem* implies. *Ninkot tray o tlat* — take two or three? But *amar rachmana pri etz hadar* — the *Torah* says one fruit — *velo shenayim ve-shlosha peirot* — not two or three fruits. *Hilkach lo efshar* — therefore, pepper is impossible. This continues the pattern seen throughout the *arba minim*: the *Gemara* attempts to derive the identity of each species from descriptive language in the verse. As the *Rambam* notes, it is difficult to imagine that the identity of these species was genuinely unknown and discovered only through *drashot*, since the language is vague enough to admit other possibilities. The *Rambam*'s position is that the identifications were always known through tradition and the *Gemara* is retroactively anchoring them in the text.

### Dir as Pen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״הָדָר״, אֶלָּא ״הַדִּיר״. מָה דִּיר זֶה יֵשׁ בּוֹ גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים, תְּמִימִים וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין — הָכִי נָמֵי יֵשׁ בּוֹ גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים, תְּמִימִים וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין. אַטּוּ שְׁאָר פֵּירוֹת לֵית בְּהוּ גְּדוֹלִים וּקְטַנִּים, תְּמִימִין וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: עַד שֶׁבָּאִין קְטַנִּים, עֲדַיִין גְּדוֹלִים קַיָּימִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Do not read** the verse as it is written, ***hadar*,** meaning beautiful, **but rather** read it ***hadir*,** meaning the sheep pen. And it means, **just as** in **this pen there are large and small** sheep, **unblemished and blemished** sheep, **so too,** this tree **has large and small** fruits, **flawless and blemished** fruits. The Gemara wonders: **Is that to say** that **among other fruits there are not large and small** fruits, **flawless and blemished** fruits? How does this description identify the *etrog* specifically? **Rather, this is** what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying:** Just as in a pen, there are both large and small sheep together, so too, on an *etrog* tree, **when** the **small ones come** into being, the **large ones still exist** on the tree, which is not the case with other fruit trees.

*Rebbi omer*: *al tikrei hadar* — do not read it as "beautiful" — *ela ha-dir* — read it as a pen, an enclosure for animals. *Ma dir zeh*, just as a pen contains *gedolim u-ktanim*, older and younger animals, and *temimim u-va'alei mumim*, unblemished and blemished ones — *hacha nami*, so too an *etrog* comes in large and small, perfect and imperfect, and one encounters *etrogim* of widely varied shapes. The *Gemara* pushes back: *atu shear peirot leis bo gedolim u-ktanim temimim u-va'alei mumim* — don't other fruits also come in varying sizes and degrees of perfection? The *Gemara* answers: *ela hachi kama* — the real point of the *derasha* is *ad she-ba'im ktanim adayin gedolim kayamim* — while new fruits are already blossoming, the older fruits from the previous year are still present on the tree. The *dir* image, then, is not merely about variety within a single year, but about the *etrog* tree bearing fruit simultaneously from multiple growing seasons.

It is worth pausing to note the suggestive associations embedded in *Rebbi*'s formulation. This is the second time the *sugya* has forged a connection between the *lulav* and *korbanot*. The opening of the *sugya* derived the concept of *mitzvah ha-ba'ah ba-aveirah* from verses about *korbanot*, and now *Rebbi* invokes the imagery of a livestock pen — explicitly using the language *temimim u-va'alei mumim*, which is the precise vocabulary of blemish inspection applied to sacrificial animals. The *etrog* is, in a sense, the one object in contemporary religious life that receives something approaching the rigorous inspection once applied to *korbanot* in the *Beit Hamikdash*. The association with *Sukkot* more broadly is worth considering: to the extent that *Sukkot* connects to the *Mishkan* — given the approximate timing of its construction — and stands in the immediate aftermath of *Yom Kippur*, the holiday carries a pronounced *mikdash* dimension. Additionally, the seventy bulls of the *Musaf* offerings throughout *Sukkot* reinforce this linkage to the sacrificial system.

### Dir as Dwelling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״הָדָר״, אֶלָּא ״הַדָּר״ — דָּבָר שֶׁדָּר בְּאִילָנוֹ מִשָּׁנָה לְשָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said: Do not read** it ***hadar*, but rather** read it ***haddar*,** meaning one **that dwells,** referring to **an item that dwells on its tree from year to year.**

*Rabbi Abbahu amar*: *al tikrei hadar* — do not read "beautiful" — *ela hadar* — read it as "dwells." *Davar she-dar be-ilano mishana le-shana* — something that dwells on its tree from year to year. This conveys the same essential point as *Rebbi*'s teaching — that the *etrog*'s fruit persists on the tree across multiple years — but through a different reading of the root *dalet-vav-resh*, as *dar*, to dwell, rather than *dir*, a pen. Both *Rebbi* and *Rabbi Abbahu* arrive at the same conclusion by different routes, each working within the awareness that the identification of the *etrog* is the given answer.

### Idur/Water Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״הָדָר״, אֶלָּא ״אִידוֹר״, שֶׁכֵּן בְּלָשׁוֹן יְווֹנִי קוֹרִין לְמַיִם אִידוֹר, וְאֵיזוֹ הִיא שֶׁגָּדֵל עַל כׇּל מַיִם — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה אֶתְרוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** **Ben Azzai says: Do not read** it ***hadar*, but rather** read it ***idur*, as in the Greek language one calls water *idur*. And which is** the fruit **that grows on** the basis of **all water** sources, and not exclusively through irrigation or rainwater? **You must say it is an *etrog*.**

*Ben Azzai omer*: *al tikrei hadar* — do not read it as "beautiful" — *ela hadur* or *idur* — *she-ken bi-leshon Yavani korin le-mayim idur* — for in Greek, the word for water is *idur*. The *etrog* is thus described as a water tree: *ve-eizeh zeh she-gadel al kol mayim* — what is a tree that grows on all types of water, whether irrigated or rain-fed? *Hevei omer zeh etrog* — it is the *etrog*. The Greek word *idur* appears to correspond to the classical Greek *hydor*, as in *hydro-*, which helps make sense of a term that sounds nothing like modern Greek *nero*. Whether the intent is that the *etrog* requires abundant water or that it can thrive under varied water conditions is not entirely clear.

### Ramban Etymology

The *Ramban al ha-Torah* offers a complementary observation: the Aramaic word for beautiful is *mirageg*, and he speculates that the word *etrog* itself may be an Aramaic term essentially meaning "beautiful." If so, *hadar* in the verse and *etrog* as the name of the fruit may be near-synonymous — both denoting beauty — making *hadar* not merely a descriptor but, in effect, the very name of the fruit. Just as we have fruits named for their qualities (the Ramban's point evokes the way fruit names can encode descriptions), *etrog* and *hadar* may represent the same semantic content in two languages.

## Heter Achila/Din Mammon

### Ashera/Ir Hanidachat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל אֲשֵׁרָה וְשֶׁל עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת — פָּסוּל. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֵּיוָן דְּלִשְׂרֵפָה קָאֵי — כַּתּוֹתֵי מְיכַתַּת שִׁיעוּרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** An *etrog* **from a tree worshipped as idolatry or from a city** whose residents were **incited** to idolatry is unfit. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara answers: **Since** the *etrog* **is fated for burning, its** requisite **measure was crushed.** Although it has not yet been burned, its legal status is that of ashes.

Having identified the *etrog*, the *Gemara* returns to the cases in the *mishnah*. The invalidity of *ashera* and *ir ha-nidachat* — both mentioned at the *mishnah*'s opening alongside *gazul* — follows the same logic applied to the other *minim*: *keivan de-lisreifa ka'i*, since such objects are condemned to burning, *kitutei mechtat shiu'rei*, it is as though they are already reduced to nothing, their legal substance dissolved, and they can no longer function for a *mitzvah*. (The analysis of *gazul* and the question of whether invalidity flows from ownership on the first day or from *mitzvah ha-ba'ah ba-aveirah* on subsequent days was addressed in the earlier treatment of the *lulav*.)

### Orlah Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל עׇרְלָה פָּסוּל. מַאי טַעְמָא? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין וְרַבִּי אַסִּי. חַד אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה, וְחַד אָמַר: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** An *etrog* **of *orla* is unfit.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Avin and Rabbi Asi disagree** about **this** matter. **One said:** It is unfit **because there is no permission to eat** *orla*. Anything that may not be eaten is not one's property, and it is therefore unfit for use in this mitzva. **And one said:** It is unfit **because it has no monetary** value. Since it is prohibited to benefit from *orla*, it has no value, and one cannot own an item that has no value. Therefore, it does not fulfill the requirement of taking an *etrog* from one's own property.

The *Gemara* now turns to the novel requirements specific to the *etrog* — the various conditions requiring that it be fit for consumption. *Orlah pasul* — an *etrog* from *orlah* is invalid. *Mai ta'ama* — what is the reason? *Pligi ba Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba ve-Rabbi Ami* — it is the subject of a dispute between Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba and Rabbi Ami. *Chad amar lefi she-ein ba heter achila* — one holds it is invalid because the *etrog* has no *heter achila*, no halachic permission to be eaten. *Ve-chad amar lefi she-ein ba din mamon* — the other holds it is because there is no monetary value to it: *orlah* is *asur be-hana'ah*, forbidden to derive any benefit from, and anything from which one may not benefit is considered halachically worthless.

A brief note on the *orlah* case: even though there may technically be some unusual, indirect benefit one could theoretically derive, the halachic consensus is that this does not matter. Since ordinary benefit is forbidden, the object is treated as valueless. The classic illustration is a man who attempts to betroth a woman with an object of *orlah* — the betrothal does not take effect because the object lacks even a *shaveh prutah*, a penny's worth of legal value.

The two positions differ in what they emphasize about the word *lachem* — "yours" — in the verse *u-lekachtem lachem*. According to *Rashi*, followed by *Tosafot*, both reasons are variations on the same *lachem* requirement: one emphasizes the practical inability to eat it, the other the accounting sense that it has no value and thus cannot be truly "yours." According to *Rashi*, then, these are two formulations of a single ownership deficiency, which would suggest that the problem would apply only on the first day, when *lachem* is operative. The *Rambam* in *Peirush Hamishnayot* takes a markedly different approach: *heter achila* is not about ownership at all, but about the definition of *fruit*. The verse says *pri etz hadar* — how can something be called a fruit if it cannot be eaten? Even if what prevents its consumption is halachic rather than physical, that prohibition undermines its very identity as fruit. According to the *Rambam*, therefore, the inability to eat would invalidate the *etrog* on all seven days, not only the first.

### Heter/Mammon Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתֵּיהּ מַאן דְּבָעֵי הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה לָא בָּעֵי דִּין מָמוֹן, וּמַאן דְּבָעֵי דִּין מָמוֹן לָא בָּעֵי הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה. תְּנַן: שֶׁל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה — פְּסוּלָה, בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין מָמוֹן — אַמַּאי? הֲרֵי מַסִּיקָהּ תַּחַת תַּבְשִׁילוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asserts **that it may enter one's mind** to say: The **one who requires permission to eat** the *etrog* to render it fit **does not require** that it have **monetary** value, **and** the **one who requires** that it have **monetary** value **does not require permission to eat it.** On that basis, the Gemara raises a difficulty from what **we learned in the mishna:** An *etrog* **of impure *teruma* is unfit. Granted, according to the one who says** that an *etrog* of *orla* is unfit **because there is no permission to eat it,** it works out **well** that an *etrog* of impure *teruma* is unfit, as it too may not be eaten. **However, according to the one who says** that it is unfit **because it has no monetary value, why** is the *etrog* of impure *teruma* unfit? Although eating it is prohibited, a priest **burns it** as fuel **under his cooked food.** Since one may benefit from it, impure *teruma* has monetary value.

The *Gemara* proceeds on the assumption that these two positions are mutually exclusive: *man d'ba'ei heter achila lo ba'ei din mamon* — whoever requires edibility would not require monetary value — *u-man d'ba'ei din mamon lo ba'ei heter achila* — and whoever requires monetary value would not require edibility. What scenarios illustrate the difference? *Rashi* points to *ma'aser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*: one is not only permitted to eat it but obligated to do so, yet Rabbi Meir holds that *ma'aser sheni* is *mamon gavoha* — God's property — not the individual's, analogous to *kohanim* eating *korbanot* where the *mitzvah* to eat belongs to them but the *korban* does not. So *ma'aser sheni* in *Yerushalayim* represents something with *heter achila* but potentially without *din mamon*. Conversely, *Rashi* gives *tevel* as an example of something with monetary value — since once *terumos u-ma'asrot* are separated it will be fully edible, it retains real market worth — that nonetheless cannot be eaten.

### Consensus Heter Achila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: בְּהֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּבָעֵינַן, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּדִין מָמוֹן. מָר סָבַר: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה בָּעֵינַן, דִּין מָמוֹן לָא בָּעֵינַן, וּמָר סָבַר: דִּין מָמוֹן נָמֵי בָּעֵינַן. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** contrary to the previous assumption, **with regard to permission to eat** it, **everyone agrees that we require** that it be permitted to eat the *etrog*. **When they disagree is with regard to monetary** value. One **Sage holds: We require permission to eat it,** but **we do not require** that it have **monetary** value. **And** one **Sage holds: We also require** that it have **monetary** value. The Gemara asks: If so, according to this understanding, **what is the** practical halakhic difference **between them?**

The *Gemara* now tests whether the two positions are truly independent. The *mishnah* rules that *terumah tmei'ah* is invalid. *Bishlama l'man d'amar lefi she-ein bo heter achila* — if the requirement is edibility, this is straightforwardly explained: *terumah tmei'ah* cannot be eaten. But *l'man d'amar lefi she-ein bo din mamon* — what of the position that only monetary value matters? *Terumah tmei'ah* may be used as fuel to heat a pot, *masika tachat tavshilo* — surely that gives it monetary value and makes the holder an owner? The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *b'heter achila kuli alma lo pligi d'ba'ina* — everyone agrees that *heter achila* is required. All the cases in the *mishnah* are disqualified precisely because they lack *heter achila*. This is also consistent with the *Rambam*'s position that edibility is necessary to define something as fruit, and with *Rashi*'s position that edibility defines ownership in the context of *lachem*.

### Din Mammon Extra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּדִין מָמוֹן. מָר סָבַר הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה בָּעִינַן דִּין מָמוֹן לָא בָּעִינַן, וּמָר סָבַר דִּין מָמוֹן נָמֵי בָּעִינַן. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** The remaining debate, then, is narrower: **where they actually disagree is with regard to monetary value. One holds that edibility is sufficient and no additional monetary requirement is needed — one holds that even given edibility, one must also establish full technical monetary ownership. This is an additional layer beyond the agreed-upon baseline.**

The remaining debate, then, is narrower: *ki pligi* — where they actually disagree — is on *din mamon*. *Mar savar heter achila ba'inan din mamon lo ba'inan* — one holds that edibility is sufficient and no additional monetary requirement is needed — *u-mar savar din mamon nami ba'inan* — the other holds that even given *heter achila*, one must also establish full technical monetary ownership. This is an additional layer beyond the agreed-upon baseline.

### Ma'aser Sheni Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה — הֲרֵי יֵשׁ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין מָמוֹן — מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי מָמוֹן גָּבוֹהַּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the *halakha* of an *etrog* of **second tithe in Jerusalem, and according to the opinion of Rabbi Meir,** who holds that the legal status of second-tithe produce in Jerusalem is that of consecrated property. Although its owner has the right to eat it, just as he may eat from offerings that he sacrifices, it is the property of God, and he has no monetary rights to the produce. According **to the one who said:** An *etrog* of *orla* is unfit **because there is no permission to eat** it, **there is permission to eat** second tithe; therefore, according to Rabbi Meir, a second-tithe *etrog* in Jerusalem is fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva. **And** according **to the one who said:** An *etrog* of *orla* is unfit **because it has no monetary** value, **second tithe** in Jerusalem **is** consecrated **property of God** and has no monetary value to its owner. Therefore, according to Rabbi Meir, it is not fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva.

*Mai beinayhu* — what case would distinguish these two positions? *Ika beinayhu ma'aser sheni she-bi-Yerushalayim* — the difference is *ma'aser sheni* in *Yerushalayim*, and specifically *va-aliba de-Rabbi Meir*, according to Rabbi Meir who holds it is *mamon gavoha*. *Heter achila* is certainly present — there is an obligation to eat it. But according to Rabbi Meir, the ownership is God's, not the individual's. If *heter achila* alone is sufficient, one would fulfill the *mitzvah*; if *din mamon* is also required, one would not — since, per Rabbi Meir, one is not the technical monetary owner. The *Gemara* identifies this as the position of Rabbi Assi: *tistayem*, it is proven that Rabbi Assi is the one who requires *din mamon* as an additional criterion, for Rabbi Assi states explicitly that an *etrog shel ma'aser sheni*, according to Rabbi Meir, would not fulfill one's obligation on *Yom Tov*, whereas according to the *chachamim* — who hold that *ma'aser sheni* is the individual's property — it would.

## Terumah/Demai Analysis

### Terumah Tehoah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל תְּרוּמָה טְהוֹרָה לֹא יִטּוֹל, וְאִם נָטַל — כָּשֵׁר. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּכְשִׁירָהּ, וְחַד אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּפְסִידָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna stated: **And** with regard to an *etrog* **of pure *teruma*, one should not take it** *ab initio*. However, if he did, it is fit for use in fulfilling the mitzva. **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi disagreed about this.** Why may one not take it *ab initio*? **One** of them **said:** It is **because one renders it susceptible** to impurity. All vegetation cannot become ritually impure, even if it came into contact with a source of impurity, unless it was moistened by one of seven liquids (see Leviticus 11:37–38). However, once one touches the *etrog* with wet hands, which are wet because he removed the other three species from the water in which they were kept to preserve their freshness, he renders the *etrog* susceptible to impurity. The Sages prohibited taking an *etrog* of *teruma*, lest it become impure, as it is prohibited to impurify *teruma*. **And one said:** It is **because he damages it.** By handling the *etrog*, the peel is rendered disgusting, and it is prohibited to damage *teruma*.

The *Gemara* now proceeds through the individual cases of the *mishnah*. *Terumah tmei'ah* is straightforward: *leit bei heter achila* — it cannot be eaten. But *terumah tehorah* presents the more interesting question. *Bediavad* one is *yotze* because *terumah tehorah* is edible — a *kohen* can eat it — but *lo yitol lechatchila*, one should not take it initially. Why not?

### Machshira/Mafsida

The two positions are *machshira* and *mafsida*. *Chad amar mishum de-machshira* — one holds the concern is that taking the *etrog* together with the *lulav* — which is kept in water to stay fresh — causes the *etrog* to come into contact with water, thereby rendering it *mekabel tum'ah*, susceptible to *tum'ah*. Once a detached fruit comes into contact with water, it becomes susceptible. The best protection for the *terumah* is to prevent that susceptibility in the first place. *Ve-chad amar mishum de-mafsida* — the other holds the concern is that daily handling causes the rind to wear away, damaging the *terumah* through repeated use.

### Rind Exclusion Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? כְּגוֹן שֶׁקָּרָא עָלֶיהָ שֵׁם חוּץ מִקְּלִיפָּתָהּ חִיצוֹנָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּכְשִׁירָהּ — אִיכָּא. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּפְסִידָהּ — לֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: The difference would be in a case **where one accorded** the entire *etrog* **the status** of *teruma*, to the **exclusion of the peel,** whose status remains non-sacred. **According to the one who said: Because one renders it susceptible** to impurity, **there is** a prohibition, as failure to sanctify the peel as *teruma* does not prevent the fruit from becoming susceptible to impurity. However, **according to the one who said: Because he damages** the *teruma*, **there is no** prohibition, as the peel that might be damaged was never *teruma*.

*Mai beinayhu* — what would distinguish these two positions? The *Gemara* constructs the following test case: *kegon de-kara lei shem chutz mi-klipata ha-chitzonis* — suppose one designated this *etrog* as *terumah* but specifically excluded the outer rind, making only the inner flesh *terumah* while the rind retains ordinary status. Is it possible to make such a designation? Yes: one may define which portion of one's produce constitutes the *terumah* separation. Practically, when the time comes to give the *etrog* to the *kohen*, one would say: here is your *terumah* — but return the rind, which I never made *terumah*.

In this scenario: *l'man d'amar mishum de-machshira* — if the concern is susceptibility to *tum'ah* — *ika*, the problem remains. When water contacts the outer rind and renders it *mekabel tum'ah*, the *tum'ah* does not stop at the rind; the entire fruit becomes *tamei*. *Man d'amar mishum de-mafsida* — if the concern is wasting *terumah* — *leika*, no problem, since the rind is not *terumah* and wearing it away causes no loss of consecrated material.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נָטַל כְּשֵׁרָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה — הֲרֵי יֵשׁ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ דִּין מָמוֹן — הֲרֵי יֵשׁ בָּהּ דִּין מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And if he took** an *etrog* of *teruma*, **it is fit,** and he fulfilled his obligation after the fact. The Gemara explains: **According to the one who said** that one does not fulfill his obligation **because there is no permission to eat** the *etrog*, **there is permission to eat it. According to the one who said** that one does not fulfill his obligation **because it has no monetary** value, **it has monetary** value.

If one did take such a *terumah* *etrog*, *lechatchila* or *bediavad*: *lem'an d'amar mipnei she-ein bah heter achila* — if the criterion is edibility — *harei yesh bah heter achila* — it is clearly edible, whether by the *kohen* directly, or because one can give it to a *kohen* to eat. *Tosafot* addresses the implicit question here: even if the non-*kohen* owner cannot personally eat it, the fact that he controls who eats it — he can give it to a *kohen* son-in-law or the like — is sufficient to constitute the ownership relationship that *lachem* requires. *Lem'an d'amar mipnei she-ein bah din mamon* — if the criterion is also full monetary ownership — *harei yesh bah din mamon* — the owner of *terumah tehorah* is certainly its full monetary owner. The interesting edge case left unexplored is where *terumah* has been separated but not yet given to a *kohen*: there the original owner controls its disposition but is arguably no longer its true owner, raising a genuine question about whether the *lachem* requirement would be met.

### Demai Lechatchila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁל דְּמַאי. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּבֵית הִלֵּל — כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי מַפְקַר לְהוּ לְנִכְסֵיהּ וְהָוֵי עָנִי וַחֲזֵי לֵיהּ, הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי ״לָכֶם״ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ. דִּתְנַן: מַאֲכִילִין אֶת הָעֲנִיִּים דְּמַאי וְאֶת אַכְסַנְיָא דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna cites a dispute between Beit Hillel and Beit Shammai with regard to an *etrog* **of *demai*.** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale** for the opinion of **Beit Hillel,** who deem it fit? It is prohibited to eat *demai*, due to the concern that it is actually untithed produce. The Gemara answers: **Because, if one wants, he could declare all of his property ownerless, and he would be a pauper,** in which case the *demai* would be **fit for his** consumption. **Now too,** even though he did not declare it ownerless, **it is considered** to meet the criterion of "and you shall take **for yourselves." As we learned** in a mishna: **One may feed the impoverished *demai*, and** one may feed **soldiers [*akhsanya*]** whose support is imposed upon the residents of the city, ***demai*.**

*Shel demai* — the *mishnah* records a dispute between *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*. *Beit Hillel* permits the use of a *demai* *etrog* and considers one *yotze bediavad*, while *Beit Shammai* invalidates it. *Mah ta'ama de-Beit Hillel* — why does *Beit Hillel* permit it? *Kivan de-i ba'ei mafkir le-nichsei ve-havei ani ve-chazi lei* — because one could renounce all one's possessions, become legally poor, and then eat the *demai* oneself, since the Rabbis waived the tithing requirement for the poor. Therefore *lachem karena bei* — it retains the quality of "yours."

*Tosafot* points out that this reasoning is actually stronger than necessary: even without the theoretical *hefker* pathway, as long as there exists someone who can eat the *demai* — such as a poor person to whom one could give it — that should suffice for *heter achila*, just as with *terumah tehorah*. And one certainly owns it outright. The *Gemara*'s reasoning is therefore something of an overstatement.

### Ani Eats Demai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי, עָנִי לָא אָכֵיל דְּמַאי, דִּתְנַן: (אֵין) מַאֲכִילִין הָעֲנִיִּים דְּמַאי וְאֶת הָאַכְסְנָאִים דְּמַאי, וְאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תָּנָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מַאֲכִילִין אֶת הָעֲנִיִּים וְאֶת הָאַכְסְנָאִים דְּמַאי, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מַאֲכִילִין אֶת הָעֲנִיִּים דְּמַאי וְאֶת הָאַכְסְנָאִים דְּמַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why, then, do **Beit Shammai** deem it unfit? The Gemara answers: **A pauper may not eat *demai*, as we learned in** a mishna: **One may not feed the impoverished *demai* and** one may not feed **soldiers *demai*. And Rav Huna said: It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Beit Shammai say: One may not feed the impoverished *demai* and** one may not feed **soldiers** whose support is imposed upon the residents of the city, ***demai*. And Beit Hillel say: One may feed the impoverished *demai* and** one may feed **soldiers** whose support is imposed upon the residents of the city, ***demai*.** On that basis, the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel with regard to an *etrog* of *demai* is clear.

*De-tnan* — we learned in the *mishnah*: *machilin et ha-aniyim demai ve-et ha-achsenia demai* — one may feed *demai* to the poor and to quartered soldiers. This is the source that a poor person is permitted to eat *demai*. *U-Beit Shammai* — and *Beit Shammai*'s position — *ani lo achil demai* — a poor person is not permitted to eat *demai* according to *Beit Shammai*, as a *baraita* cited by Rav Huna confirms: *Beit Shammai omrim ein machilin et ha-aniyim ve-et ha-achsenia demai*, *Beit Hillel omrim machilin*. This is *Beit Shammai*'s grounds for disqualification: since *demai* cannot be eaten in its current state even by a poor person per *Beit Shammai*, there is no *heter achila*, and the *etrog* is invalid.

A clarification on the nature of the *demai* leniency for the poor: *demai* is not treated as *ma'achalot asurot* in the ordinary sense. The Rabbis imposed the obligation to tithe it, but did not transform the *cheftza* of the produce into a prohibited substance. It is a Rabbinic obligation with built-in weakness — an acknowledgment that the produce is quite likely already tithed — and so the Rabbis waived the requirement for the poor entirely. This is different from other Rabbinic prohibitions, where poverty would not be grounds for a general waiver.

### Ma'aser Sheni Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁל מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּכְשִׁירָהּ — הֲרֵי מַכְשִׁירָהּ, לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמַּפְסִידָהּ — הֲרֵי מַפְסִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: With regard to an *etrog* **of second tithe in Jerusalem, one may not take it,** *ab initio*. The Gemara explains: **According to the one who says** that an *etrog* of pure *teruma* is unfit **because one renders it susceptible** to ritual impurity, **here too, he renders it susceptible. According to the one who says** it is unfit **because one damages** the peel, **here too, he damages** the peel.

*Uma'aser sheni bi-Yerushalayim* — the *mishnah* rules one is *yotze*, and the *Gemara* has already analyzed this case at length. *Leman de-amar mamon gavoha hu lo ka machshir* — the question, per Rabbi Meir's position that it is God's property, is whether *din mamon* is required in addition to *heter achila*. The *lechatchila* prohibition parallels that of *terumah tehorah*: if the concern is susceptibility to *tum'ah*, that applies equally here since one may not render *ma'aser sheni tamei*; if the concern is causing material loss through handling, that likewise applies. *Ve-im natal keshera* — and if one took it, it is valid — with the same analysis as above applying: according to all who require only *heter achila*, one is *yotze* even according to Rabbi Meir, since the ability to eat is present; and for those who also require *din mamon*, the *chachamim* who hold that *ma'aser sheni* is the individual's property would render one *yotze*, and indeed the *mishnah*'s ruling reflects their view.

## Aesthetic Defects

### Chazazit Majority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָלְתָה חֲזָזִית. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה רַבֵּינוּ הַגָּדוֹל אֲמָרוֹ, הַמָּקוֹם יִהְיֶה בְּעֶזְרוֹ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת — כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **If boil-like blemishes arose** on the majority of the *etrog*, it is unfit. **Rav Ḥisda said: This statement was stated by our great rabbi,** Rav, **and may the Omnipresent** come **to his assistance.** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case where the blemishes are concentrated **in one place; however,** if they are distributed **in two or three places** throughout the *etrog*, **it is fit.**

Rav Chisda transmits a teaching in the name of *rabbeinu ha-gadol*, identified by *Rashi* as Rav — noting, *Hamakom yihiye b'ezro* — that Rav said: *lo shanu ela b'makom echad* — when is a *chazazit* covering the majority of the *etrog* invalid? Only when it is concentrated in one location.

### Chazazit Minority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְנוּמָּר, וּפָסוּל. אֶלָּא: אִי אִתְּמַר אַסֵּיפָא אִתְּמַר. עַל מִיעוּטוֹ — כָּשֵׁר. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דָּבָר זֶה רַבֵּינוּ הַגָּדוֹל אֲמָרוֹ, וְהַמָּקוֹם יִהְיֶה בְּעֶזְרוֹ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בִּשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת — הָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְנוּמָּר, וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Rav Ḥisda: If the blemishes are distributed in **two or three places, it is as if** the *etrog* were **speckled** with different colors in different places; it lacks beauty **and is** certainly **unfit.** **Rather, emend the text: If** this statement **was stated, it was stated concerning the latter clause** of the mishna: **If boil-like blemishes arose** only **on its minority, it is fit. Rav Ḥisda said: This statement was stated by our great rabbi,** Rav, **and may the Omnipresent** come **to his assistance.** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** if the blemishes are concentrated **in one place. However,** if they are distributed **in two or three places** throughout the *etrog*, even if their total remains a minority, **it is as if** the *etrog* were **speckled, and** it is **unfit.**

However, Rava challenges this: *aderaba* — quite the contrary — *b'shenayim u-shlosha mekomot havlei ke-manumar pasul* — if the growth is spread over two or three places, it looks spotted and mottled, which is worse, not better, and should therefore be invalid. *Ela i itmar a-seifa itmar* — Rava therefore argues that Rav's teaching was not said on the opening case of a majority, which is invalid regardless of location, but on the closing case: when *chazazit* is on a minority, the *mishnah* says it is *kosher*. On that Rav adds: *lo shanu ela be-makom echad* — this *kashrut* applies only when the warts are gathered in one place. *Aval bi-shnayim u-shlosha mekomot havlei ke-manumar u-fasul* — but if spread over two or three places, it appears spotty and mottled, and is therefore invalid even though each individual wart affects only a minority. What began as a seeming leniency — the minority case being valid — thus generates a significant stringency: even two warts in different locations invalidates.

### Chotam Strictness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְעַל חוֹטְמוֹ — וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּמַשֶּׁהוּ נָמֵי פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** If there is a blemish **on its upper,** blossom **end,** which is clearly visible and comprises the essence of the beauty of the *etrog*, **even if** the blemish **is** of **any size,** the *etrog* is **unfit.**

*De-amar Rava* — Rava further adds: *ve-al chotmo* — if a *chazazit* appears on the *chotam*, the upper slope of the *etrog* where the body begins to narrow upward toward the top — *va-afilu be-mashehu nami pasul* — even the smallest blemish there invalidates. *Rashi* identifies the *chotam* as the portion where the *etrog* begins its upward taper, literally its "nose." This strictness regarding the upper portion of the *etrog*, particularly the slope leading to the *pitam*, underlies the careful examination many are accustomed to perform. While the *Gemara* is technically discussing *chazazit*, a wart-like growth, the heightened sensitivity at the *chotam* has informed the contemporary practice of scrutinizing even very small discolorations on the upper third of the *etrog*.

### Pitam/Oketz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְּלָה פִּטְמָתוֹ. תָּנָא רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: נִטְּלָה בּוּכְנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **If its *pitam* was removed,** it is unfit. **Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar taught** a *baraita*: This means **if its pestle**-like protuberance at its upper end **was removed.**

*Nital pitamto* — if the *pitam* has been removed, the *etrog* is invalid. *Tani Rav Yitzchak ben Elazar*: *nital bochanto* — he clarifies the *pitam* by identifying it as the *bochna*, a pestle. The shape of a pestle — held at one end with a bulbous rounded knob at the other used for pounding — describes exactly the appearance of the *pitam*: a stem with a rounded protrusion at its top. This identification also makes clear which end is meant, resolving any ambiguity in the term.

### Pitam Location

Some *rishonim* actually identify the *pitom* as the opposite end — the stem end of the *etrog* rather than the apical protrusion. According to this view, the *pitom* is the portion embedded inside the fruit at the base, and its removal invalidates the *etrog*. This would then require distinguishing it from the *oketz*, the stem, whose removal the *mishnah* explicitly states does not invalidate. Those *rishonim* would say that the *oketz* is the external protrusion and the *pitom* is the internal structure, so that removing the outer stem is acceptable whereas disrupting the inner connection invalidates. The accepted *pesak*, however, follows the pestle identification: the *pitam* is the apical protrusion at the top, and this is obviously the more intuitive reading given *Rav Yitzchak ben Elazar*'s comparison to the knob end of a pestle.

One further observation regarding *Rashi* deserves mention in connection with the *terumah tehorah* case discussed earlier. *Rashi* there writes *harei yesh bah heter achila* and then adds *aval pidyon ein lah* — but it cannot be redeemed — apparently responding to an interpretation he had encountered that claimed the reason *terumah tehorah* has a *heter achila* is because one could redeem the *terumah* and then eat it freely. *Rashi* dismisses this as completely false — *terumah* cannot be redeemed — and then adds: *ve-ha-omer ken rasha hu* — and one who says so is wicked. The concern is not merely a mistaken interpretation but a dangerous one that would mislead people into thinking they could redeem *terumah* and render it permissible to non-*kohanim*. The force of *Rashi*'s language here — going beyond "incorrect" to "wicked" — reflects the severity of a ruling that would cause others to transgress.
