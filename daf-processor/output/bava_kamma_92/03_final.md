# Bava Kamma 92 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Self-Injury & Exempt Acts

### Self-Injury Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: הַחוֹבֵל בְּעַצְמוֹ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי – פָּטוּר! הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: לָא מִבַּעְיָא בּוֹשֶׁת, דְּאָדָם רַשַּׁאי לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת עַצְמוֹ; אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חֲבָלָה, דְּאֵין אָדָם רַשַּׁאי לְחַבֵּל בְּעַצְמוֹ – אֲחֵרִים שֶׁחָבְלוּ בּוֹ, חַיָּיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **and it teaches:** With regard to **one who injures himself, although it is not permitted for him** to do so, he is nevertheless **exempt** from any sort of penalty, indicating that the prohibition is in effect even with regard to humiliation. The Gemara answers: **This is** what Rabbi Akiva **said to** the man: **It is not necessary** to say with regard to **humiliation, where it is permitted for a person to humiliate himself,** that someone else who humiliated him is liable. **But even** with regard to **injury, where it is not permitted for a person to injure himself, others who injured him are liable.**

The daf picks up at the bottom of 92a, continuing Rabbi Akiva's response to the individual who had attempted to prove himself exempt from paying the heavy *boshet* fine for uncovering a woman's hair in public. Rabbi Akiva argued that the man's demonstration — showing that the woman had uncovered her own hair — was legally meaningless, because a person is not permitted to injure himself. Even granting that prohibition, however, a critical distinction applies: if one does inflict self-injury, there is no payment owed. If someone else inflicts the same injury, they are fully liable.

### Bal Tashchit Debate

The prohibition of self-injury, even where it will heal, is difficult to pin down to a precise source. The *gemara* treated it as a debate, leaving the exact grounding somewhat unclear. At minimum, self-injury that is permanent implicates *bal tashchit*. Suicide is certainly *assur*. But the question of temporary self-harm remains unresolved. What is clear is that the exemption from payment applies whether or not the underlying act was permitted.

### Sapling Cutting Case

The second example Rabbi Akiva adduced involves cutting down young saplings. One is forbidden to do so — at minimum on account of *bal tashchit* — but if one destroys one's own saplings, one incurs no liability to pay oneself. If another person does it, they must pay. The question then is the precise source and scope of the prohibition against destroying one's own saplings, a question the *gemara* will turn to at length.

### Sharecropper Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַקּוֹצֵץ נְטִיעוֹתָיו [וְכוּ׳]. תָּנֵי רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חַנָּה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב: ״שׁוֹרִי הָרַגְתָּ״, ״נְטִיעוֹתַי קָצַצְתָּ״; ״אַתָּה אָמַרְתָּ לִי לְהוֹרְגוֹ״, ״אַתָּה אָמְרַתְּ לִי לְקוֹצְצוֹ״ – פָּטוּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, לָא שְׁבַקְתְּ חַיֵּי לְבִרְיָיתָא! כֹּל כְּמִינֵּיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One who cuts** down **his** own **saplings,** although it is not permitted for him to do so, is exempt from payment. Nevertheless, others who cut down his saplings are liable. **Rabba bar bar Ḥana taught** the following *baraita* **before Rav:** If one charged another: **You killed my ox,** or: **You cut** down **my saplings,** and you are liable to pay for them, and the other responds: **You told me to kill** the ox, or: **You told me to cut** down the saplings, he is **exempt** from paying. Rav **said to him: If that is so, you let no creature exist. Is it in his** power to assert that because the other instructed him to cause damage he is exempt? With this claim, anyone who causes damage can exempt himself.

Rabba Bar Bar Chanan taught the following before Rav: if I say to another person, "You killed my ox," or "You cut down my saplings," and that person responds, "Yes, but you instructed me to do so" — he is exempt (*patur*). A first reading assumes that I have witnesses proving he did it, which makes his claim of authorization seem dubious. The *gemara* asks: does he have any *ne'emanut*, any credibility to assert that I told him to act? Rav replied that the *beraita* need not be struck — it can be explained by a specific context: *beshor omed lahariga uv'ilan omed leketzitza* — the ox had to be killed and the tree had to be cut down. The circumstances themselves lend credibility to the claim. Why did the ox have to be killed? Rashi suggests it may have been a dangerously aggressive ox, not a *shor haniskal* whose execution would fall to *beis din*, but perhaps a sick or hazardous animal whose removal was necessary for public welfare. Why did the tree have to be cut down? Rashi gives two examples: an *asherah* — a tree that had been worshipped as an idol, perhaps on land purchased from pagans — or a tree leaning over the *reshus harabbim* and threatening to collapse onto passersby.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם כֵּן, מַאי קָא טָעֵין לֵיהּ? דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא בָּעֵינָא לְמִיעְבַּד הָא מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so,** that the owner of the ox or tree acknowledges that it would be killed or cut down in any event, **what claim is he stating to him,** as there was no loss resulting from the action of the other? The Gemara answers: The case is **where he said to him: I desire to perform this mitzva** of removing a danger or destroying idolatry by myself, and therefore I am demanding payment from you for having prevented me from doing so.

## Stealing a Mitzvah

### Michael's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁפַךְ וְכִסָּה״ – מִי שֶׁשָּׁפַךְ, יְכַסֶּה. וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּאֶחָד שֶׁשָּׁחַט, וְקָדַם חֲבֵירוֹ וְכִסָּה, וְחִיְּיבוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לִיתֵּן לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה זְהוּבִים.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to one who slaughters an undomesticated animal or a bird: **"He shall pour** out the blood thereof, **and he shall cover** it with dust" (Leviticus 17:13). This teaches that **the one who poured** the blood, i.e., the one who slaughtered, **shall cover** the blood. **An incident** occurred **involving one who slaughtered** an undomesticated animal or bird, **and another preempted** him **and covered** the blood, **and Rabban Gamliel deemed him liable to give ten golden coins to** the one who slaughtered the animal, as he prevented him from performing the mitzva.

Given that the circumstances establish that the act had to be done, the *gemara* asks a sharp question: if the ox and the saplings had no real value — since they had to be disposed of anyway — what exactly is the claimant seeking? The answer: *ana bina lemevad hai mitzvah* — I wanted to perform this *mitzvah* myself, and you stole it from me. The *gemara* then asks why, even in this framing, the other person is believed that he was asked. The answer is that when something clearly had to be done and it is plausible that someone would ask another to handle it, the circumstantial evidence is sufficient to undercut the *mitzvah*-theft claim.

### Necessary Removal

The case of the tree leaning over the public domain is particularly instructive. The *mitzvah* here is what might be called *hashavas guf* — protecting the public from bodily harm. The person who saw the tree and removed it was doing something that genuinely needed to be done, and the claim that he was asked to do it is therefore plausible enough that compensation for the stolen *mitzvah* opportunity cannot be collected.

### Blood-Covering Story

The underlying legal concept — that one can owe compensation for stealing another's *mitzvah* opportunity — is established by a *beraita*: *v'shafach v'chisah*, the Torah requires that the same person who slaughters the animal shall cover its blood. A story is then cited: someone slaughtered an animal and another person ran ahead and covered the blood first. Rabban Gamliel ruled that the one who jumped in and performed the *mitzvah* must pay the slaughterer ten *zuzim*. The *gemara* treats this ruling not as an isolated curiosity but as a precedent applicable to comparable situations.

### Mitzvah vs. Berachah

The *gemara* raises the question of whether the prohibition is on account of stealing the *mitzvah* itself or the *berachah* that accompanies it. What practical difference does this make? If someone is honored to lead *birchas hamazon* and another person jumps in and leads instead, has he stolen four *berachos* and thus owes four times ten *zuzim*? The *gemara* raises this but does not fully resolve the distinction.

### Rabbeinu Tam Amen

Tosafos cites a directly parallel case. A *shatz* was called up to read from the *Sefer Torah* and another person jumped ahead and read in his place. The question was brought to Rabbeinu Tam: must the interloper pay the person he displaced? Rabbeinu Tam's response was that the wrongdoer should give the other person a chicken to slaughter in place of the two *berachos* — essentially a gesture of appeasement rather than a formal monetary award. Tosafos immediately critiques this: if a chicken suffices, what do we do with Rabban Gamliel's ruling of ten *zuzim*? That ruling treats this as a real and measurable *din*. Rabbeinu Tam then offered a second reason: the displaced person can still answer *Amen* to the *berachos* recited by the interloper, and *gadol ha'oneh Amen yoser min hamevarech* — the one who answers *Amen* receives greater reward than the one who recites the *berachah*. It is evident that Rabbeinu Tam did not wish to apply the *gemara*'s ruling directly; he was seeking a way to make peace and move on. He also invoked the principle that in *Bavel* we do not adjudicate cases that involve no concrete financial loss — and since one does not lose money by losing a *mitzvah* opportunity, the case is not actionable.

### Rosh: Din Not Knas

The Rosh responds forcefully. Rabbeinu Tam never called Rabban Gamliel's ruling a *knas*. How could one apply an arbitrary *knas* from a case of blood-covering to cases of *birchas hamazon* or tree removal? Rather, the Rosh argues: *deina hu vegamrinan minei* — it is a genuine *din* and we derive from it to other cases. Moreover, he asks, why would one impose a *knas* on someone who was trying to perform a *mitzvah*? A *knas* is a punitive measure; this is something different. The Rosh insists this is proper *din*, applicable broadly.

### IP & Copyright

What makes this discussion particularly striking is that the *gemara* is attempting to assign monetary value to something abstract — not a physical object, but a right: the right to perform a *mitzvah*. Normally, *mitzvos lav lehenos nitnu* — *mitzvos* are not the kind of thing that carry a dollar value. And yet the *gemara*, and Rabban Gamliel's ruling, push in the direction of treating as actionable theft the taking of something non-physical from someone. This has obvious resonance for the modern category of intellectual property. The question of what Torah law — as distinct from secular law — prohibits when someone photocopies an Artscroll *siddur* is a live halachic question. The *poskim* generally approach it through other avenues, but it is worth noting that *chazal* here seem to be doing exactly what intellectual property law attempts: treating as legally cognizable the taking of something that is not a tangible object. Whether this *gemara* has been explicitly employed as a precedent in that literature may be debated, but the conceptual parallel is striking.

## Fruit Tree Destruction

### Palm Tree Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: דִּיקְלָא דִּטְעַן קַבָּא – אָסוּר לְמִקְצְצֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § In connection with the prohibition against cutting down trees the Gemara notes: **Rav said** with regard to **a palm tree that** still **produces** fruit in the amount of **a *kav*,** that it is **prohibited to cut it** down due to the prohibition of: "When you shall besiege a city…you shall not destroy the trees" (Deuteronomy 20:19).

The *gemara* now turns to the prohibition of cutting down fruit trees — the concrete case in the Torah that underlies *bal tashchit*. While the *gemara* has not yet spelled out the prohibition explicitly, Rabbi Akiva's ruling that one may not cut down even one's own saplings rests on this foundation. The Torah says: *rak etz asher teda ki lo etz ma'achal hu oto tashchit v'charata* — only a non-fruit-bearing tree may you destroy. The *gemara* tries to quantify the threshold. Rav rules: *dikla de'ta'on kava* — a palm tree that bears a *kav* of dates is forbidden to cut down. Even a small amount of fruit production suffices to classify the tree as an *etz ma'achal*. This is notable: a large tree producing only a *kav* — a modest measure — still crosses the threshold.

### Olive Tree Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כַּמָּה יְהֵא בַּזַּיִת וְלֹא יִקְצְצֶ[נּ]וּ? רוֹבַע! שָׁאנֵי זֵיתִים, דַּחֲשִׁיבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rav from what was taught in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 4:10): **How much** fruit must **be on an olive tree so** that one may **not cut it** down? **A quarter**-*kav*. Why did Rav say that it must produce a full *kav*? The Gemara answers: **Olive trees are different, since they are significant.** Therefore, even a quarter-*kav* is valuable.

The *gemara* challenges Rav's formulation from a *mishnah*: with an olive tree, the relevant standard is a *rova* — a quarter-*kav* — not a full *kav*. The resolution: *shani zeisim d'chashivi* — olives are particularly significant, so even a smaller quantity suffices to render the tree categorically significant. Different species carry different thresholds.

### Death Before Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: לָא שְׁכֵיב שִׁיבְחַת בְּרִי, אֶלָּא דְּקַץ תְּאֵינְתָּא בְּלָא זִמְנַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: My son Shivḥat did not die** for any reason **other** than **that he cut** down **a fig tree before its time.**

Rav Chanina teaches: his son Shivchas did not die except because he cut down a fig tree before its time. The metaphor is as powerful as it is sobering: the tree cut down before its time; the son cut down before his time. Whatever the precise mechanism of causation implied, the teaching underscores that destroying a productive fruit tree is not merely a technical infraction — it carries an ominous symbolic and perhaps real weight.

### Financial Override

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: וְאִם הָיָה מְעוּלֶּה בְּדָמִים, מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: But if** the lumber **was greater in monetary** value than its fruits, it is **permitted** to chop it down, and this does not violate the prohibition against destroying a tree.

The *gemara* then introduces a crucial qualification: *v'im haya me'ula b'damim mutar* — if the tree is worth more as wood than it produces in fruit, cutting it down is permitted. This marks a significant shift. Until this point, the prohibition has appeared absolute — an *etz ma'achal* may not be cut down, period. Now the *gemara* abstracts the principle: the prohibition is not about the concrete category of "fruit tree" but about the concept of wastefulness. If cutting down the tree is financially justified — if you gain more by felling it than by leaving it to produce fruit — then the act is not *hashchasa* and is permitted.

One might ask how exactly to perform this calculation. Is it the immediate value of the wood versus the cumulative value of the fruit over the tree's lifetime? The principle is clear even if the precise arithmetic is left open: the governing standard is financial rationality, not categorical tree type.

### Textual Abstraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״רַק עֵץ אֲשֶׁר תֵּדַע״ – זֶה אִילַן מַאֲכָל, ״כִּי לֹא עֵץ מַאֲכָל הוּא״ – זֶה אִילַן סְרָק.
>
> **Translation:** **This** *halakha* **is also taught** in a *baraita*. The verse states: "Only the trees of which you know that they are not trees for food, them you may destroy and cut down" (Deuteronomy 20:20). **"Only the trees of which you know"; this** is referring to **a tree** that bears fruit used for **food,** and it is permitted to cut down this type of tree under certain circumstances. **"That they are not trees for food"; this** is referring to **a barren tree.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר שֶׁסּוֹפֵ[נ]וּ לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״כִּי לֹא עֵץ מַאֲכָל״? לְהַקְדִּים סְרָק לְמַאֲכָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And since** the *baraita* **will ultimately include all types** of trees, so that even a tree that produces fruit may be cut down, **what,** then, is the meaning when **the verse states: "That** they are **not trees for food,"** which indicates that it is permitted to cut down only a barren tree? The Gemara answers: It is **to give precedence** to cutting down **a barren** tree **over** a tree whose fruit is used for **food.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל אֲפִילּוּ מְעוּלֶּה בְּדָמִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״רַק״.
>
> **Translation:** One **might** have thought that he must give precedence to the cutting down of a barren tree **even** if the barren tree is **greater in monetary** value than the fruit-bearing tree. **The verse states: "Only,"** which teaches that there is an exception to the rule. Similarly, if the fruit-bearing tree itself would be worth more as lumber than for its fruits, it would be permitted for one to cut it down.

A *beraita* confirms this reading and takes it one step further, re-reading the Torah's own text. The *gemara* notes: *rak et ha'etz asher teda* — this refers to a fruit tree. *Ki lo etz ma'achal hu* — this refers to a barren tree. On a plain reading, the *pasuk* says: cut down the non-fruit-bearing tree, not the fruit-bearing one. But the *gemara* reads the two halves of the verse independently: both types of trees can be cut down under the right conditions. The *pasuk* then functions not as a categorical permission for non-fruit trees and categorical prohibition for fruit trees, but as a preference principle: if both are available, choose the barren tree. And even this preference — *l'hakdim srak l'ma'achal* — is qualified. *Talmud lomar rak*: the limiting word *rak* tells us that even the preference to cut down the barren tree over the fruit tree applies only when it makes equal financial sense. If the fruit tree's wood would fetch more, you may cut down the fruit tree.

This is one of the most striking moments in the *sugya*: an apparently concrete biblical prohibition — never destroy a fruit tree — is re-read through the lens of financial abstraction into a general prohibition on wanton destruction. The move mirrors *Bava Kamma*'s broader method of taking concrete Torah cases and deriving from them abstract legal principles.

### Wine-Tainted Dates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל אַיְיתִי לֵיהּ אֲרִיסֵיהּ תַּמְרֵי. אָכֵיל, טָעֵים בְּהוּ טַעְמָא דְחַמְרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בֵּינֵי גוּפְנֵי קָיְימִי. אָמַר: מַכְחֲשִׁי בְּחַמְרָא כּוּלֵּי הַאי? לִמְחַר אַיְיתִי לִי מִקּוֹרַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The **sharecropper of Shmuel brought him dates.** Shmuel **ate** them, and **tasted the taste of wine in them. He said to** his sharecropper: **What is this?** The sharecropper **said to him:** The date palms **stand among the grapevines** and therefore contain a taste of wine from the grapes. Shmuel **said:** Do **they weaken the wine,** i.e., the grapevines, **so much** that it is possible to taste the wine in the dates? **Tomorrow,** cut down the date palms and **bring me from their marrow** to eat.

The *gemara* illustrates with a story. Shmuel's sharecropper brought him dates. Shmuel tasted a hint of wine in them and asked why. The sharecropper explained that the date palms were growing among grapevines. Shmuel concluded that the presence of the palm trees must be causing significant injury to the vines — enough that the dates were absorbing the wine flavor. He instructed the sharecropper to bring him the trunks of the palm trees the next day, meaning: cut them down. The financial logic is that the damage being done to the vines — a more valuable crop — outweighs the value of the date palms. Cutting down the palm trees is thus not *bal tashchit* but financial prudence. This pushes the abstraction one step further: one may cut down a fruit tree not only because the wood itself is more valuable, but because the tree's presence is causing financial loss elsewhere.

### Vine-Palm Economics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא חֲזָא תָּאלֵי בֵּי גוּפְנֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לַאֲרִיסֵיהּ: עַקְרִינְהוּ; גּוּפְנֵי קָנֵי דִּקְלֵי, דִּקְלֵי לָא קָנֵי גּוּפְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **Rav Ḥisda saw date palms** growing **among grapevines** on his estate. **He said to his sharecropper: Uproot the** date palms, since one can **purchase date palms** with **grapevines,** as grapevines are more valuable, while one **cannot purchase grapevines** with **date palms.**

Rav Chisda encountered a similar situation — date palm saplings growing among grapevines. He told his sharecropper: *akrinhu* — tear them out. His explanation: *gufnei kani diklei, diklei lo kani gufnei* — with grapevines you can purchase many palm trees, but with palm trees you cannot purchase many grapevines. Grapevines are the more valuable asset; the palm trees must go. This is the halachic conclusion of the *sugya* on *bal tashchit*: the prohibition is ultimately about waste and financial rationality, not about the categorical nature of the tree.

## Mechila Beyond Pay

### Payment Insufficient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוּא נוֹתֵן לוֹ, אֵין נִמְחָל לוֹ עַד שֶׁיְּבַקֵּשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעַתָּה הָשֵׁב אֵשֶׁת וְגוֹ׳״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָחַל לוֹ, שֶׁהוּא אַכְזָרִי? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתְפַּלֵּל אַבְרָהָם אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים, וַיִּרְפָּא אֱלֹהִים אֶת אֲבִימֶלֶךְ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Despite** the fact **that** the assailant who caused damage **gives to** the victim all of the required payments for the injury, his transgression is **not forgiven for him** in the heavenly court **until he requests** forgiveness **from** the victim, **as it is stated** that God told Abimelech after he had taken Sarah from Abraham: **"Now therefore restore the wife** of the man; for he is a prophet, and he shall pray for you, and you shall live" (Genesis 20:7). **And from where** is it derived **that if** the victim **does not forgive him that he is cruel? As it is stated: "And Abraham prayed to God; and God healed Abimelech,** and his wife, and his maidservants; and they bore children" (Genesis 20:17).

The *mishnah* states: *af al pi she-noten lo, ein nimchal lo ad she-yevakesh mimenu* — even if the injurer makes full financial compensation, he is not forgiven until he requests forgiveness from the injured party. This arrives in immediate proximity to the *boshet* discussion, and it is natural to read it as applying specifically to cases of public shaming — where the psychological wound is obvious and payment alone cannot address it. But the *gemara* will raise the question of scope: does this principle apply to bodily injury without shame? Does it apply to property damage that has been fully compensated?

Consider: if someone accidentally causes a sprained ankle, pays for the lost work and suffering, and the injury was not publicly shaming — must he still seek *mechila*? Or: if someone totals another person's car and pays full replacement value — does he owe a request for forgiveness as well? These are live questions that the *gemara* opens without fully closing.

### Avimelech Example

The *mishnah* adduces the biblical precedent of Avimelech. God told him: return this man's wife, for he is a prophet, *veyispalel ba'adecha v'chayeh* — and he will pray for you and you will live. The Maharsha notes that Avimelech did in fact give Avraham financial compensation — *tzon u'vakar* — and yet the text makes plain that he also had to bring Avraham to the point of being willing to pray on his behalf. The capacity and willingness to pray for someone signals a level of reconciliation that goes beyond financial settlement. The two elements — payment and genuine forgiveness — are thus distinct and both necessary.

This case is also significant for the question of scope. The wrong done to Avraham was not a case of public shaming in the classic sense. Avimelech had taken Sarah believing she was Avraham's sister; the surrounding population thought her to be unmarried. There was no public humiliation of Avraham. Yet even in that case, financial payment was insufficient and genuine appeasement — sufficient to motivate Avraham's prayer — was required.

### Refusing Forgiveness

The *mishnah* then turns to the other side: *u'minayin she'im lo machal lo she'hu achzari* — how do we know that if the victim refuses to grant forgiveness, he is cruel? The evidence: *vayispallel Avraham el ha'Elokim vayirpa Elokim es Avimelech* — Avraham prayed and God healed Avimelech. The *gemara* uses this to establish that being prepared to pray for the one who wronged you is the standard of forgiveness, and failure to meet that standard is *achzarius* — cruelty. One might ask why Avraham's behavior sets a standard for everyone; the *gemara* here is perhaps reading in light of Rabbi Akiva's principle that even the poorest Jews are *bnei Avraham Yitzchak Veyaakov* — heirs to Avraham's character — and so the expectation of living up to Avraham's example follows from that identity.

### Mechila Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כׇּל אֵלּוּ שֶׁאָמְרוּ – דְּמֵי בוֹשְׁתּוֹ; אֲבָל צַעֲרוֹ – אֲפִילּוּ הֵבִיא כׇּל אֵילֵי נְבָיוֹת שֶׁבָּעוֹלָם, אֵין נִמְחָל לוֹ עַד שֶׁיְּבַקֵּשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָשֵׁב אֵשֶׁת הָאִישׁ כִּי נָבִיא הוּא, וְיִתְפַּלֵּל בַּעַדְךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: All these** sums **that** in the previous mishna **they said** one is liable to pay for humiliating another are the **compensation for his humiliation,** for which there is a set amount. **But** for the victim's **pain** caused by the assailant, **even if** the assailant **brings** as offerings **all the rams of Nebaioth** (see Isaiah 60:7) **that are in the world,** which are of the best quality, his transgression **is not forgiven for him** in the heavenly court **until he requests** forgiveness **from** the victim, **as it is stated: "Restore the wife of the man; for he is a prophet, and he shall pray for you"** (Genesis 20:7).

The *beraita* distinguishes the different dimensions of the wrong. The fixed values — 200, 100, or 400 *zuz* — represent *d'mei boshto*, compensation for the embarrassment. But *tza'ar*, the psychological anguish, is a separate matter. Even if one were to bring *kol eilei nevayos sheba'olam* — all the rams of Nevayos as offerings — one is not forgiven by Heaven *ad sheyivakesh mimeno* — until one asks *mechila* from the person directly.

The *beraita* thus distinguishes the *boshet* — the public embarrassment, which requires witnesses and social context — from the *tza'ar*, the inner anguish, which can exist even without an audience. Shame in the sense of public humiliation requires others to observe; but psychic pain — the experience of being dehumanized, diminished, or violated — can be felt privately, and it is that wound for which financial compensation is wholly insufficient. It is this deeper injury that requires genuine *mechila*.

## Self-Harm vs. Property

### Bodily Injury Always

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר ״סַמֵּא אֶת עֵינִי״; ״קַטַּע אֶת יָדִי״; ״שַׁבֵּר אֶת רַגְלִי״ – חַיָּיב. ״עַל מְנָת לִפְטוֹר״ – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: With regard to **one who says** to another: **Blind my eye,** or: **cut off my hand,** or: **break my leg,** and he does so, the one who performed these actions is **liable** to pay for the damage, despite having been instructed to do so. Even if he explicitly instructed him: Do so **on the condition that** you will **be exempt** from payment, he is nevertheless **liable.**

The *mishnah* now shifts to a separate ruling. *Ha'omer: sima eini, keta yadi, shaver ragli — chayav*. If I instruct someone: blind my eye, cut off my hand, break my leg — he is liable. Even if I add *al menas liftor* — on the condition that you are exempt — he remains liable. For bodily injury, neither an implicit request nor an explicit exemption clause releases the injurer from payment.

The reasoning may be that a person can never truly and sincerely mean such an exemption when it concerns permanent bodily harm — one cannot genuinely intend to waive payment for one's own blinding. Alternatively, bodily injury may be categorically different: objectively, injuring a person is always a wrong, regardless of consent, because the human body is not a piece of property subject to waiver in the same way.

### Property Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״קְרַע אֶת כְּסוּתִי״; ״שַׁבֵּר אֶת כַּדִּי״ – חַיָּיב. ״עַל מְנָת לִפְטוֹר״ – פָּטוּר. ״עֲשֵׂה כֵּן לְאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי עַל מְנָת לִפְטוֹר״ – חַיָּיב, בֵּין בְּגוּפוֹ בֵּין בְּמָמוֹנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one who says to another: **Tear my garment,** or: **break my jug,** and he does so, he is **liable** to pay for the damage. But if he instructed him explicitly: Do so **on the condition that** you will **be exempt** from payment, he is **exempt** from payment. If one says to another: **Do so,** i.e., cause damage, **to so-and-so on the condition that** you will **be exempt** from payment, and he did so, he is **liable, whether** the instructions were **with regard to** the victim **himself,** or **whether** the instructions were **with regard to his property.**

The rule for property is different. *Kera kesusi, shever kadi* — if I tell you to tear my clothing or break my pitcher, you are liable (*chayav*), even though I requested it. This is itself a *chiddush*: the mere fact that I asked you to destroy my property does not constitute an implicit *mechila* or reframe the act as non-destructive. If, however, I add an explicit exemption — *al menas liftor* — then you are exempt (*patur*). The distinction between bodily injury and property is thus twofold: for property, an explicit exemption works, whereas for bodily injury it does not; but even for property, an implicit request is insufficient to generate exemption — only an explicit *al menas liftor* suffices.

Why should a request without an explicit exemption clause be insufficient for property? Because, as the *gemara* has been developing throughout this *sugya*, whether an act of property destruction counts as *hashchasa* depends on context and purpose. If I ask you to tear my clothing with no further qualification, there is nothing in the circumstances to indicate that this is anything other than wanton destruction — nothing like the ox that had to be killed or the tree that was leaning into the public domain. Without that contextual justification, and without an explicit exemption, the act remains legally destructive and the injurer remains liable.

### No Agency Sin

If I instruct a third party — go injure so-and-so, go destroy so-and-so's property, and don't worry, I will exempt you — the third party is nonetheless liable. *Ein shliach l'dvar aveira* — there is no agency for wrongdoing. One cannot delegate a sinful act and have it rebound legally onto the sender. I am not in a position to grant *mechila* on behalf of the person who will actually be injured; I have no power to exempt someone from liability toward a third party. The *shaliach* himself is the wrongdoer and bears the liability.

## Avimelech Drashah

### Prophet Foreknowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאֵשֶׁת נָבִיא בָּעֵי אַהְדּוֹרֵי, אֵשֶׁת אַחֵר לָא בָּעֵי אַהְדּוֹרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** Having quoted the verse, the Gemara asks: Shall one infer from here **that the wife of a prophet needs to be returned,** but **the wife of another** individual **need not be returned?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: ״הָשֵׁב אֵשֶׁת הָאִישׁ״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ: ״הֲגוֹי גַּם צַדִּיק תַּהֲרֹג?! הֲלֹא הוּא אָמַר לִי אֲחֹתִי הִיא, וְהִיא גַּם הִיא אָמְרָה אָחִי הוּא!״ נָבִיא הוּא, וּכְבָר לִימֵּד: אַכְסְנַאי שֶׁבָּא לָעִיר – עַל עִסְקֵי אֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה שׁוֹאֲלִין אוֹתוֹ, אוֹ עַל עִסְקֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ שׁוֹאֲלִין אוֹתוֹ – ״אִשְׁתְּךָ הִיא?״ ״אֲחוֹתְךָ הִיא?״
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** This is how the verse should be understood: **"Restore the wife of the man" in any case,** since she is his wife. **And** with regard to that **which you,** Abimelech, **said: "Will you slay even a righteous nation? Did he not say himself to me: She is my sister, and she, even she herself, said: He is my brother?"** (Genesis 20:4–5), the answer is that you, Abimelech, are not so righteous, since the reason Abraham said that Sarah was his sister is that **he is a prophet, and he already learned** how to conduct himself based on your behavior. As with regard to **a guest [*akhsenai*] who comes to town, does one ask him about matters concerning eating and drinking, or does one ask him about matters concerning his wife?** Does one ask a guest: **Is she your wife? Is she your sister?** Abimelech was to be blamed, since Abraham thought that he intended to steal his wife.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן לְבֶן נֹחַ שֶׁנֶּהֱרָג, שֶׁהָיָה לוֹ לִלְמוֹד וְלֹא לָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara remarks: **From here** it can be derived **that a gentile is executed** for having transgressed a prohibition without awareness that the act was prohibited, **since he should have learned and he did not learn.**

The *gemara* now turns to a *drash* on the *psukim* of the Avimelech story. The verse reads: *hashiv eshet ha'ish ki navi hu* — return the man's wife because he is a prophet. The *gemara* asks: does being the wife of a prophet uniquely require her return? What of any other man's wife? Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani said in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: read the verse differently. *Hashiv eshet ha'ish mikol makom* — return the man's wife regardless, simply because she is a married woman. The phrase *ki navi hu* serves a different purpose: Avimelech protested his innocence, noting that both Avraham and Sarah had represented her as his sister. The response is: he is a prophet. The *gemara* then develops two readings of what this means. One version, found in *Makkot*, reads *ud'kvar lamad mimcha* — "he has already learned from you." The teaching: *achsana'i sheba le'ir* — when a visitor comes to a city, one asks about his food and lodging needs, not about the woman traveling with him. By inquiring *ishtecha hi, achotecha hi* — is that your wife or your sister — Avimelech's household revealed that its intentions were not pure. Avraham had already demonstrated in the earlier incident with Pharaoh that when a man presents a woman as his sister, one should not press the matter. Avimelech should have learned that lesson. The alternative version suggests that Avraham himself, in encountering a place where he perceived *ein yirat Elokim bamakom hazeh*, declared his awareness of the moral climate — and Avimelech's questions confirmed it.

The *gemara* concludes: *mikan l'ven Noach shenehreg shehaya lo lilmod v'lo lamad* — from here we learn that a *ben Noach* who is executed cannot claim ignorance, whether of the law or, significantly, of the relevant facts, because he had the means and the responsibility to know better.

### Divine Closure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי עָצֹר עָצַר ה׳״ – אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: שְׁתֵּי עֲצִירוֹת הַלָּלוּ לָמָּה? אַחַת בְּאִישׁ – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, שְׁתַּיִם בְּאִשָּׁה – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע וְלֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the verses concerning the incident of Abraham and Abimelech, the Gemara explains other related verses. **"For the Lord had obstructed [*atzor atzar*]** all the wombs of the house of Abimelech" (Genesis 20:18). **Rabbi Elazar says: Why** are **these two obstructions [*atzor atzar*]** both stated? **One** is stated **with regard to a man,** that **semen** will not be discharged, and **two** are stated **with regard to a woman,** that **semen** will not be discharged from her, **and** that she will not give **birth.**

The *gemara* turns to the verse *atzor atzar Hashem baad kol rechem* — God had completely closed every womb in the house of Avimelech. Rabbi Elazar asks: why the doubled language — *atzor atzar* — implying two closings? He answers: one closing for men (*shichvat zera* — he could not ejaculate), and two closings for women (*shichvat zera* and *leidah* — she could neither receive nor give birth).

### Four Closings Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תְּנָא: שְׁתַּיִם בְּאִישׁ – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע וּקְטַנִּים, שְׁלֹשָׁה בָּאִשָּׁה – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע וּקְטַנִּים וְלֵידָה.
>
> **Translation:** It **was taught in a *baraita*: Two** are stated **with regard to a man: Semen and urine,** i.e., that the men were unable to urinate and discharge semen; **three** are stated **with regard to a woman: Semen, and urine, and birth.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: שָׁלֹשׁ בְּאִישׁ – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע וּקְטַנִּים וּפִי טַבַּעַת, אַרְבָּעָה בְּאִשָּׁה – שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע וְלֵידָה וּקְטַנִּים וּפִי טַבַּעַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says: Three** are stated **with regard to a man: Semen, and urine, and** the **anal sphincter,** i.e., that they were unable to discharge stool as well. Consequently, they would be unable to relieve themselves at all. **Four** are stated **with regard to a woman: Semen, and birth, and urine, and** the **anal sphincter.**

A *mattnita* adds a further dimension: two for men (*shichvat zera* and the urinary passage (*ketanim*)) — and three for women: *shichvat zera*, *ketanim*, and *leidah*. Ravina then extends the count further: three closings for men — *shichvat zera*, *ketanim*, and *fi hataba'at* (the rectum, i.e., defecation was also prevented) — and four for women: *shichvat zera*, *leidah*, *ketanim*, and *fi hataba'at*. The entire household's bodily functions were suspended.

### Hens' Silence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּעַד כׇּל רֶחֶם״ – אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֲפִילּוּ תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת שֶׁל בֵּית אֲבִימֶלֶךְ לֹא הֵטִילָה בֵּיצָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse states: "For the Lord had obstructed **all the wombs** of the house of Abimelech." The Sages **of the school of Rabbi Yannai say: Even a hen of the house of Abimelech did not lay her egg** during that time.

The school of Rabbi Yannai adds a final, striking detail: *afilu tarnegolet shel beis Avimelech lo hetilah beitzatah* — even the hens in the house of Avimelech did not lay their eggs during that time. The divine closure extended to every living creature in the household, underlining the totality of the punishment and its precise, targeted character.

## Wisdom Precedents

### Prayer Others

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: כׇּל הַמְבַקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים עַל חֲבֵירוֹ וְהוּא צָרִיךְ לְאוֹתוֹ דָּבָר, הוּא נַעֲנֶה תְּחִילָּה?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a series of questions that Rava asked Rabba bar Mari, the first one being related to the previous topic of discussion. **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: From where is this matter** derived **whereby the Sages stated: Anyone who asks** for **compassion** from Heaven **on** behalf of **another, and he requires** compassion from Heaven **concerning that same matter, he is answered first?**

Rava asks Rabbah bar Mari: what is the source for the rabbinic teaching that *kol hamevakesh rachamim al chaveiro v'hu tzarich l'oto davar hu ne'eneh techilah* — whoever prays for another while himself in need of the same thing is answered first? The principle is that selfless intercession on another's behalf generates a prior divine response for the intercessor.

### Iyov Friends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דִּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ שָׁב אֶת שְׁבוּת אִיּוֹב בְּהִתְפַּלְלוֹ בְּעַד רֵעֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari **said to him** that the source for this is **as it is written: "And the Lord changed the fortune of Job, when he prayed for his friends"** (Job 42:10).

Rabbah bar Mari cites the verse: *va'Hashem shav et shevut Iyov b'hitpalalo baad re'eihu* — God returned Iyov's fortunes when he prayed on behalf of his friends. Iyov's restoration came precisely at the moment of his selfless prayer for others.

### Abraham Sarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מֵהָתָם, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא מֵהָכָא: ״וַיִּתְפַּלֵּל אַבְרָהָם אֶל הָאֱלֹהִים, וַיִּרְפָּא אֱלֹהִים אֶת אֲבִימֶלֶךְ וְאֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וְאַמְהוֹתָיו [וְגוֹ׳]״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ פָּקַד אֶת שָׂרָה כַּאֲשֶׁר אָמָר וְגוֹ׳״ – כַּאֲשֶׁר אָמַר אַבְרָהָם אֶל אֲבִימֶלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You said** the proof **from there,** from a verse in the Writings, **and I say** the proof **from here,** from a verse in the Torah. As it is written: **"And Abraham prayed to God; and God healed Abimelech, and his wife, and his maidservants,** and they bore children" (Genesis 20:17), **and it is written** immediately following that: **"And the Lord remembered Sarah, as He had said"** (Genesis 21:1), with the pronoun interpreted homiletically: **As Abraham said with regard to Abimelech.** Because Abraham prayed for Abimelech that the women of his household should give birth, Abraham himself was answered concerning that matter.

Rava responds: you derive it from there; I derive it from here — from the Avimelech passage itself. *Vayispallel Avraham el ha'Elokim vayirpa Elokim es Avimelech ve'es ishto ve'amosav vayeledu* — Avraham prayed and Avimelech's household was healed and they gave birth. Immediately following: *va'Hashem pakad es Sarah ka'asher amar* — and God remembered Sarah as He had said. The *drashah*: *ka'asher amar Avraham* — as Avraham had spoken on behalf of Avimelech. At the very moment of Avraham's selfless prayer for Avimelech, God remembered Sarah. The sequence is striking: the one who prays for another is answered first.

One might raise an obvious chronological question: if Sarah conceived at that moment, one still needs to account for the events between Avimelech's return of Sarah and her conceiving. The *gemara* does not press this, though the principle of *ein mukdam u-me'uchar ba-Torah* — the Torah does not always follow strict chronological sequence — resolves such concerns.

### Weeds Vegetables

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בַּהֲדֵי הוּצָא לָקֵי כְּרָבָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: From where is this matter** derived **whereby people say: The cabbage is damaged together with the thorn,** since the cabbage is sometimes harmed when the thorn is removed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לָמָּה תָרִיבוּ אֵלָי? כֻּלְּכֶם פְּשַׁעְתֶּם בִּי נְאֻם ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari **said to him** that the source is **as it is written: "Why will you contend with Me? You all have transgressed against Me, says the Lord"** (Jeremiah 2:29). The term "you all" includes even those who have not sinned.

Rava asks: what is the source for the popular saying *be'hadei hutza laki karba* — when the weed is struck, the vegetable alongside it is also harmed? When weeds must be uprooted, the surrounding crops can be damaged in the process. Rabbah bar Mari cites the verse: *lama tarivu elai kulchem pashatem bi* — why do you all fight against Me, you have all sinned against Me. The *kulchem* — all of you — includes the *tzaddikim* swept up together with the *resha'im*. Rava responds: you say it from there, but I say it from here — *ad ana me'antem lishmor mitzvosai v'sorosai* — the verse in which God speaks to Moshe and Aharon while addressing all of Bnei Yisrael, encompassing even Moshe and Aharon in the rebuke. Rava consistently prefers to derive these principles from earlier *psukim* in the Torah itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מֵהָתָם, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא מֵהָכָא: ״עַד אָנָה מֵאַנְתֶּם לִשְׁמֹר מִצְוֹתַי וְתוֹרֹתָי״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You said** the proof **from there,** from a verse in the Prophets, **and I say** the proof **from here,** from a verse in the Torah. The Lord said to Moses after some of the people collected the manna on Shabbat: **"How long do you refuse [*me'antem*] to keep My commandments and My laws?"** (Exodus 16:28). The term "you refuse [*me'antem*]" is written in the plural, indicating that even Moses was included, although he did not sin.

### Yosef Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי: כְּתִיב: ״וּמִקְצֵה אֶחָיו לָקַח חֲמִישָּׁה אֲנָשִׁים״ – מַאן נִינְהוּ חֲמִשָּׁה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתָן שֶׁהוּכְפְּלוּ בְּשֵׁמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: It is written** with regard to Joseph: **"And from among his brothers he took five men,** and presented them to Pharaoh" (Genesis 47:2). **Who are these five** men? Rabba bar Mari **said to him: This is what Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Those whose names were repeated** in the blessings with which Moses later blessed the twelve tribes. They are Dan, Zebulun, Gad, Asher, and Naftali (see Deuteronomy 35). Since they were weak, Joseph brought them before Pharaoh.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה נָמֵי אִיכְּפוֹלֵי מִיכְּפַל!
>
> **Translation:** Rava said: **Judah also** had his name **repeated** in the blessings, and he was strong.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְמִילְּתֵיהּ הוּא דְּאִיכְּפַל. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״יְחִי רְאוּבֵן וְאַל יָמֹת, וִיהִי מְתָיו מִסְפָּר – וְזֹאת לִיהוּדָה״?
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari **said to him: His** name **was repeated for his** own **matter, as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written** concerning Reuben and Judah in Moses' blessing of the tribes at the end of his life: **"Let Reuben live and not die in that his men become few"** (Deuteronomy 33:6), and immediately afterward in the following verse it states: **"And this for Judah,** and he said: Hear Lord, the voice of Judah, and bring him in unto his people; may his hands fight on his behalf, and You shall be a help against his adversaries" (Deuteronomy 33:7)? What is the connection between the blessing of Reuben and that of Judah, juxtaposed with the conjunction "and"?

Rava asks: the verse says *u-miketze echav lakach chamisha anashim* — Yosef took five of his brothers to present before Pharaoh. Who were these five? Rav Yochanan answers: *osam she-huchpelu bi-shemos* — those whose names are repeated in *Vezos Haberacha*. Rashi explains the repetition as an indication of weakness, suggesting these brothers needed the doubled mention as a form of strengthening. Yosef deliberately chose his weaker brothers so that Pharaoh would not be tempted to conscript his more capable brothers as royal officers.

The *gemara* raises a difficulty: if we count all the brothers whose names are repeated in *Vezos Haberacha*, there are six, not five — because Yehuda's name also appears twice. The response: Yehuda's name was doubled for an entirely different reason, unrelated to weakness.

### Yehuda Bones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אוֹתָן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר, הָיוּ עַצְמוֹתָיו שֶׁל יְהוּדָה מְגוּלְגָּלִין בָּאָרוֹן; עַד שֶׁבָּא מֹשֶׁה וּבִקֵּשׁ רַחֲמִים – אָמַר לְפָנָיו: רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, מִי גָּרַם לִרְאוּבֵן שֶׁיּוֹדֶה – יְהוּדָה!
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan said: **All those forty years that the Jewish people were in the desert, the bones of Judah,** which the Jewish people took with them from Egypt along with the bones of his brothers, **were rolling** around **in the coffin, until Moses came and asked for mercy** on Judah's behalf. Moses **said before** God: **Master of the Universe, who** served as the **impetus for Reuben that he should confess** his sin, through which he merited a blessing and was not excluded from the count of the twelve sons of Jacob (see Genesis 35:22)? It was **Judah,** as Reuben saw him confess his sin, and thereby did the same.

Rabbi Yochanan teaches in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: what is the meaning of *yechi Reuven v'al yamot* and then *v'zos l'Yehuda*? Throughout the forty years in the wilderness, the bones of Yehuda were rolling in the *aron* — the skeletal structure had collapsed and the bones were not at rest. The reason: Yehuda had said regarding Binyamin *v'chatasi l'avi kol hayamim* — I will bear the guilt before my father forever — if I do not bring him back safely. The Rashi-cited principle applies: a curse of a sage, even one spoken on a condition, retains some effect even after the condition is met. Yehuda had brought Binyamin back, satisfying the condition, and yet the *chatas* he took upon himself had not been fully released.

### Yehuda Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִיָּד: ״שְׁמַע ה׳ קוֹל יְהוּדָה״ – עָל אֵיבְרֵיהּ לְשָׁפָא. לָא הֲווֹ קָא מַסְּקִי [לֵיהּ] לִמְתִיבְתָּא דִּרְקִיעָא, ״וְאֶל עַמּוֹ תְּבִיאֶנּוּ״. לָא הֲוָה יָדַע מַאי קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן, וּלְמִשְׁקַל וּמִיטְרַח בַּהֲדֵי רַבָּנַן, ״יָדָיו רָב לוֹ״. לָא הֲוָה סָלֵיק לֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתְּתָא אַלִּיבָּא דְהִלְכְתָא, ״וְעֵזֶר מִצָּרָיו תִּהְיֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Immediately** after Moses prayed, the verse states: **"Hear, Lord, the voice of Judah"** (Deuteronomy 33:7). **His bones** then **entered** their **sockets [*leshafa*],** and his skeleton became attached. The angels still **did not elevate** him **into the heavenly study hall.** Moses then prayed: **"And bring him in unto his people"** (Deuteronomy 33:7), i.e., bring him to those in the heavenly study hall. This prayer was accepted, but **he** still **did not know what the sages were saying, and** he was unable **to deliberate** in Torah matters **with the sages.** Moses then prayed: **"May his hands fight on his behalf"** (Deuteronomy 33:7), meaning that he should have the ability to contend with them in study. But still **he was unable to draw** conclusions from **his discussion in accordance with the *halakha*.** Moses then prayed: **"And You shall be a help against his adversaries"** (Deuteronomy 33:7).

It was only when Moshe prayed on Yehuda's behalf that the situation began to change. *Mi garam l'Reuven she'yodeh?* — Who caused Reuven to confess his sin of violating his father's bed? Yehuda. Because when Yehuda publicly declared *tzadka mimeni* regarding Tamar, Reuven was moved to confess his own transgression in turn. So Moshe prayed: given what Yehuda did for Reuven, surely Yehuda deserves relief.

God heard, and the bones were restored — *al eivarei l'shafa*, the limbs returned to their sockets. But Yehuda was still not admitted to the *mesivta d'rakiya*, the heavenly academy. Moshe continued: *v'el amo t'vienu* — bring him to his people. Yehuda was admitted, but *lo hava yada mai ka'amri rabanan* — he could not follow the discussions. His situation paralleled Moshe's experience in the *beis medrash* of Rabbi Akiva: present but unable to engage fully. *Yadav rav lo* — let his hands be victorious for him; Yehuda was now able to participate in argument. But *v'lo hava salik leh shma'ata aliba d'hilchasa* — his arguments never prevailed, the *halacha* was never ruled according to his position. A final prayer: *v'ezer mitzrav tihyeh* — let him have strength against his adversaries. And so Yehuda progressed: from bones rolling in the *aron*, to restoration, to admission to the heavenly academy, to full participation in its deliberations.

### Rich-Poor Baskets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי, מְנָא הָא מִילְּתָא דְּאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בָּתַר עַנְיָא אָזְלָא עַנְיוּתָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: From where is this matter** derived **whereby people say: Poverty follows the poor?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, דִּתְנַן: עֲשִׁירִים מְבִיאִין בִּכּוּרִים בִּקְלָתוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב וְשֶׁל כֶּסֶף, וַעֲנִיִּים בְּסַלֵּי נְצָרִים שֶׁל עֲרָבָה קְלוּפָה. הַסַּלִּים וְהַבִּכּוּרִים נְתוּנִים לַכֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari **said to him: As we learned** in a mishna (*Bikkurim* 3:8): **Rich** people would **bring first fruits in baskets of gold and of silver, and poor** people would bring first fruits **in wicker baskets** made **of peeled willow,** and **they would give the baskets and the first fruits to the priests.** The rich would have their baskets returned to them, while the poor would not.

Rava asks: what is the source for the popular saying *basar anya azla anyusa* — poverty follows the poor? The rich get richer and the poor get poorer. Rabbah bar Mari cites the *mishnah* on *bikurim*: wealthy people would bring their *bikurim* in baskets of gold and silver, while poor people brought theirs in baskets of wicker made from peeled willow. When the *bikurim* were presented to the *kohanim*, the *kohanim* kept the baskets along with the produce. The rich man's gold or silver basket was returned to him; the poor man's wicker basket was kept. So not only did the poor man have to give up his produce — he also had to surrender his basket. Poverty follows the poor: even in the performance of a *mitzvah*, the poor man is further diminished.

### Metzora Self

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מֵהָתָם, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא מֵהָכָא: ״וְטָמֵא טָמֵא יִקְרָא״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You said** the proof **from there,** from a mishna, **and I say** the proof **from here,** from a verse in the Torah: The verse states with regard to one diagnosed with leprosy: "And the leper in whom the plague is, his clothes shall be rent, and the hair of his head shall go loose, and he shall cover his upper lip, **and he shall cry: Unclean, unclean"** (Leviticus 13:45). Not only must the leper suffer from the leprosy itself; he must undergo further embarrassment by publicizing his condition. This is akin to the aphorism that poverty follows the poor.

Rava prefers to derive the same principle from the verse *v'tamay tamay yikra* — the *metzora* must cry out his impurity to all around him. Not only is he expelled from the camp by the court's decree — he must then actively advertise his own *tum'ah*, pushing himself even further away and doubling his own isolation. The connection to the proverb is that the one who is already afflicted must add to his own affliction — his condition compounds itself.

### Breakfast Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַבָּה בַּר מָרִי, מְנַָא הָא מִילְּתָא דַאֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: הַשְׁכֵּם וֶאֱכוֹל בַּקַּיִץ מִפְּנֵי הַחַמָּה, וּבַחוֹרֶף מִפְּנֵי הַצִּינָּה;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rabba bar Mari: From where is this matter** derived **whereby the Sages stated: Awaken early and eat, in the summer due to the heat and in the winter due to the cold.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יִרְעָבוּ וְלֹא יִצְמָאוּ, וְלֹא יַכֵּם שָׁרָב וָשָׁמֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Mari said to him that the source is **as it is written: "They shall not hunger nor thirst, neither shall the heat nor the sun smite them"** (Isaiah 49:10), indicating that one who is not hungry or thirsty will not be affected by the weather.

A final rabbinic teaching: *hashkeim v'echol bakayitz mipnei hachama u'vachoref mipnei hatzina* — eat early in the summer on account of the heat and in the winter on account of the cold. The underlying principle is the importance of eating in the morning before exposure to the elements. The source, according to Rabbah bar Mari: *lo yir'avu v'lo yitzmau v'lo yakeim sharav vashamesh* — they shall not be hungry, nor thirsty, nor shall the heat and sun strike them. If you eat early, the sun will not overcome you.

### Rava Blessing Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ מֵהָתָם, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא מֵהָכָא: ״וַעֲבַדְתֶּם אֵת ה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״ – זוֹ קְרִיַּת שְׁמַע וּתְפִלָּה. ״וּבֵרַךְ [אֶת] לַחְמְךָ וְאֶת מֵימֶיךָ״ – זוֹ פַּת בְּמֶלַח וְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל מַיִם. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ: ״וַהֲסִירֹתִי מַחֲלָה מִקִּרְבֶּךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: You said** the proof **from there,** from a verse in the Prophets, **and I say** the proof **from here,** from a verse in the Torah. The verse states: "And you shall serve the Lord your God, and He will bless your bread and your water; and I will take sickness away from the midst of you" (Exodus 23:25). Rava explicates: **"And you shall serve the Lord your God"; this** is referring to the **recitation of *Shema* and prayer. "And He will bless your bread and your water"; this** is referring to **bread** dipped **in salt, and a flask of water** drunk after the bread, in the mornings. **From this** point **forward** the remainder of the verse applies: **"And I will take sickness away from the midst of you."**

Rava replies that he derives this from the verse *va'avadtem es Hashem Elokeichem* — which the *gemara* interprets as *krias shema* and *tefillah*. Following *davening*, one blesses one's bread and water — *u'veirach es lachmecha v'es meimecha* — which is interpreted as the morning meal of bread with salt and water. *V'hasirosi machalah mikirb'cha* — I will remove illness from you — follows naturally from this morning regimen.

### Bile Gematria

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנְיָא: מַחֲלָה – זוֹ מָרָה. וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ מַחֲלָה? שֶׁשְּׁמוֹנִים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה חֳלָאִין יֵשׁ בָּהּ – ״מַחֲלָה״ בְּגִימַטְרִיָּא הָכִי הָווּ. וְכוּלָּן, פַּת בַּמֶּלַח שַׁחֲרִית וְקִיתוֹן שֶׁל מַיִם – מְבַטְּלָן.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: **"Sickness"; this** is referring to **bile. And why is its name called sickness [*maḥala*]?** It is called this **since there are eighty-three sicknesses in** bile. The letters spelling the word ***maḥala*,** i.e., *mem*, *ḥet*, *lamed*, *heh*, **have that numerical value** of eighty-three. **And** with regard to **all of those** sicknesses, eating **bread** dipped **in salt** in the **morning and** drinking **a flask of water** afterward **negates them.**

A *beraita* reinforces this: *machalah* — illness — refers to bile or gall, and the *gematria* of *machalah* equals 83, corresponding to the 83 types of illness that bile can engender. All of these, the *beraita* teaches, are warded off by *pat bemelach shacharit vekiton shel mayim* — a morning portion of bread with salt and a flask of water.
