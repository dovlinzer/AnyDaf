# Shabbat 145 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mashkeh Haba Le'ochel

### Intro: Mashkeh for Ochel

The daf opens approximately ten lines down from the top of 145a, at the line beginning *lav mashkeh hu*. The ongoing discussion concerns the permissibility of squeezing fruit on Shabbat, and specifically the ruling that one may squeeze directly into food — or at least onto food as one squeezes. The Gemara has been exploring whether *mashkeh haba le'ochel*, liquid that is destined to go into food, is classified as *ochel* (food) or as *mashkeh* (liquid) for the relevant purposes, drawing comparisons to the definitions of *mashkeh* in the context of *tuma v'tahara*.

The previous discussion had suggested that the case of smearing grape juice onto the surface of bread might have been classified differently — not as *mashkeh haba le'ochel* at all, but as *mashkeh* going *le'ibud*, to waste — and that this is why it would not render the bread susceptible to impurity. The question is now whether, absent that consideration, liquid heading toward food would retain its status as *mashkeh*.

### Grape Juice on Bread

The Gemara now proceeds to compare this dispute to a parallel tannaitic debate. A *baraita* is cited: if one crushes olives with hands that are *tamei*, the olives become *huchshar* — rendered susceptible to impurity — because the oil that emerged is a *mashkeh* that the person wanted, and this liquid in turn makes the olives susceptible. This is the case where one crushes the olives to soften and improve their flavor; one wants the oil, even if only because it enhances the taste.

By contrast, if the crushing was done *lesafotan bemelach* — to soften the olives merely so they could be dipped in salt, without any genuine interest in the oil itself — then *lo huchshar*, the olives are not rendered susceptible, because the emerging liquid is not considered a *mashkeh* in any meaningful sense.

A further case: *lada'at im higu zeisim limsok* — if one squeezed a small amount of oil merely to test whether the olives had ripened sufficiently for harvest — *lo huchshar*, according to the first opinion. One is not truly interested in the liquid; one simply wants to assess ripeness. *Rabbi Yehuda omer huchshar* — Rabbi Yehuda disagrees and holds that the olives do become susceptible even in this case.

The Gemara asks: what is the operative dispute between these *tanna'im*? It proposes that both sides agree the person wants the liquid to emerge. The question is whether a liquid that is wanted but is going *le'ibud* — to waste, dripping onto the ground with no intention to collect it — still bears the legal status of *mashkeh*. One opinion holds *mashkeh omad le'ibud mashkeh hu*: even if the liquid will be wasted, it retains the name of *mashkeh*. The opposing view holds *lav mashkeh hu*: going to waste strips it of that status.

A student raises a sharp question: how can olives still attached to the tree become *huchshar*, since produce must be detached to be susceptible to impurity in the first place? The answer, noted as an excellent point, is that one must have plucked an olive to test it, or that the oil still rests on the olive at the time of harvesting. At any rate, the point of the *baraita* is the operative principle regarding *le'ibud*.

### Rav Huna: Le'ibud vs. Le…

*Amar Rav Huna brei de'Rav Yehoshua*: Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua clarifies that the tannaitic dispute about squeezing olives to test their ripeness is specifically a debate about *mashkeh omad le'ibud* — liquid that one wants but that is going to waste. The debate about smearing grape juice on bread, however, is a separate matter: *be'mashkeh omad letzahtzecho kamipligi* — that is a dispute about liquid intended to give a shine or polish to the surface of the bread. It is not strictly going *le'ibud*, since it is being applied to something, yet it is also not quite the normal case of liquid being added to food for flavor. The precise line between these categories is admittedly difficult to draw cleanly, but the Gemara's point is that these are two distinct debates rather than a single one.

### Practical Ruling: Into F…

The practical *halacha* that emerges from this discussion is that *mashkeh haba le'ochel* is classified as *ochel*. One may therefore squeeze a lemon onto fish or onto a salad on Shabbat, and this is permitted even without the liquid being absorbed into the food — it need only be applied directly to the food for the purpose of adding flavor. What remains prohibited, however, is squeezing into an intermediary vessel even if one ultimately intends to pour its contents into food. The liquid must go directly onto the food as one squeezes. This is the operative *halacha*. Squeezing a lemon into tea, for instance, would not be permitted, as the liquid goes directly into another liquid.

The Gemara makes this concrete: *amar Rabbi Zeira amar Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi amar Rav* — one may squeeze a cluster of grapes into a pot, but not into a bowl or plate. The pot is treated as food, or at minimum there is food already in the pot. A plate standing empty is not a food context, and the liquid would exist there as liquid, which is the forbidden category.

## Fish Brine & Pickled Veg

### Fish Brine Permissible

The Gemara now moves from squeezing fruit and its natural juices to the category of squeezing liquids that have been absorbed into foods from the outside. *Ve'dag le'tzirei* — squeezing a fish to extract its brine — *afilu le'toch ke'ara*, is permitted even into a plate. The most straightforward explanation is that nobody actually wants fish brine; one therefore has no genuine interest in the liquid that emerges, and it does not bear the status of *shem mashkeh* at all. Rashi hints at an alternative explanation — that fish brine may always carry the status of *ochel* rather than *mashkeh* — but the simpler reading is that one is entirely uninterested in what comes out.

### Rav Dimi vs. Abaye

*Yasiv Rav Dimi ve'kamar le'hai shmaita* — Rav Dimi was sitting and repeating this teaching. Abaye then said to him: you have taught this law — that fish brine may be squeezed out even into a plate — in the name of Rav, and therefore you have no difficulty. But we have received this tradition in the name of Shmuel, and for us it is problematic. *Mi amar Shmuel dag le'tzirei afilu le'toch ke'ara?* Did Shmuel truly permit squeezing a fish for its brine even into a plate? This appears to contradict another ruling attributed to Shmuel, which now requires examination.

The Gemara introduces a set of rulings: *kevashim she'sechtan* — if one squeezed pickled vegetables on Shabbat. *Amar Rav: le'gufan muttar* — Rav rules that if the squeezing is done for the sake of the vegetables themselves, to make them more edible, it is entirely permitted. One has no interest in the liquid that comes out, and there is no *sechita* concern. *Le'meimeihen* — if however one's intention is to extract the pickling liquid — *patur aval assur*: one is exempt from a *chatas* offering but the act is nevertheless rabbinically forbidden.

Why is one exempt rather than fully liable? Rashi and Tosafot take different approaches. Tosafot holds that biblical *sechita* applies only to wine and olive oil; anything else — even if one wants the liquid — yields only a rabbinic prohibition. Rashi, by contrast, explains that the exemption here is because the liquid did not naturally grow within the vegetable; it was absorbed from the outside, and squeezing out an externally absorbed liquid is not the biblical *melacha* of *sechita*. Tosafot presses a difficulty against Rashi's view: if externally absorbed liquid were not a biblical concern, why do so many cases in the Gemara treat wringing out wine-soaked cloths and similar situations as potentially serious? Tosafot answers that those cases are rabbinic — *gezeirot* — and we do sometimes enact rabbinic prohibitions on rabbinic matters. This becomes a critical discussion about what constitutes the biblical category of *sechita*.

*Ve'shlukot* — boiled vegetables — *bein le'gufan bein le'meimeihen muttar*: Rav permits squeezing boiled vegetables entirely, even when one wants the liquid that comes out. The rationale is either that the water extracted is so unlike olive oil or wine that it bears no resemblance to the biblical case, or that the liquid is so insignificant in character that even when desired it carries no *shem mashkeh*. In contrast, the pickled brine has somewhat more significance, which is why Rav treats it as *patur aval assur* when sought for its own sake.

Shmuel's view is more unified. *Echad kevashim ve'echad shelakot* — whether pickled or boiled vegetables — *le'gufan muttar*: squeezing for the sake of the vegetable itself is always fine. *Le'meimeihen patur aval assur*: but whenever one wants the liquid, regardless of whether it is pickle brine or cooking water, it is at minimum rabbinically forbidden. Shmuel draws no distinction between the two types of vegetable. For Shmuel, the moment one wants the liquid that comes out, it resembles the squeezing of fruit closely enough to trigger a rabbinic prohibition.

This creates the difficulty Abaye raised. If Shmuel says that even boiled vegetables squeezed for their water is *patur aval assur*, how can Shmuel permit squeezing a fish for its brine when one wants that liquid? On Shmuel's own logic, wanting the liquid should make it at least rabbinically forbidden. So there is a genuine contradiction within Shmuel's positions as received.

### Chain of Tradition

Rav Dimi responded to Abaye's challenge with a striking declaration: *ha'Elohim, einai ra'u velo zar* — by God, my own eyes have seen and not through another, quoting the language of the verse in Iyov. He then laid out the chain: he heard the tradition from Rabbi Yirmiya, Rabbi Yirmiya from Rabbi Zeira, Rabbi Zeira from Rav Chiya bar Ashi, and Rav Chiya bar Ashi from Rav. This is not merely a case of someone citing Rav's name from a distance — Rav Dimi is asserting that he is a direct link in the chain of transmission, *ed mi'pi ed mi'pi ed*, and therefore the attribution to Rav is certain. The contradiction is Shmuel's to resolve; the ruling about fish brine is definitively from Rav, not Shmuel, and so the problem dissolves.

A student notes the interesting tension in Rav Dimi's claim: is this really so different from the standard Talmudic formula of *amar Rav Zeira amar Rav Chiya bar Ashi amar Rav*? The answer emerges from the context: when the Gemara first introduced the statement, it attributed it to Rabbi Zeira — Rav Dimi had omitted Rabbi Yirmiya from the chain, which may have created the impression that he was not a direct link in the transmission. By now filling in the complete chain — showing that Rabbi Yirmiya stood between him and Rabbi Zeira — he demonstrates that his transmission is fully intact and that his personal testimony is being offered in the strongest possible terms.

## Rav vs. Shmuel vs. R. Yo…

### Rav's Distinction

Returning to the full statement of the dispute: *kevashim she'sechtan — amar Rav: le'gufan muttar, le'meimeihen patur aval assur*. Rav maintains that squeezing pickled vegetables to improve their edibility is entirely permitted. If done for the pickling liquid, one is exempt but it is rabbinically forbidden. *U'shlukot bein le'gufan bein le'meimeihen muttar* — boiled vegetables, even when squeezed for their water, are completely permitted. Rav's position rests on distinguishing between the two types of vegetable liquid: the pickle brine has enough character to be at least rabbinically significant when desired, while the cooking water is so negligible that even desiring it does not elevate it to a prohibited level.

### Shmuel: Unified Status

Shmuel collapses this distinction. *Echad kevashim ve'echad shelakot: le'gufan muttar, le'meimeihen patur aval assur*. Whether the vegetable is pickled or boiled, squeezing for the vegetable itself is permitted and squeezing for the liquid is rabbinically forbidden. For Shmuel, the type of liquid does not matter — any deliberate extraction of liquid from something one has squeezed is sufficiently analogous to the squeezing of fruit to warrant a rabbinic prohibition.

### R. Yochanan: All Liquids

Rabbi Yochanan's position is the most stringent. *Echad kevashim ve'echad shlukot: le'gufan muttar* — he agrees that squeezing for the food itself is permitted — *le'meimeihen chayav chatas*. Any squeezing of vegetables with the intent to extract liquid, regardless of the type of vegetable and regardless of whether the liquid was absorbed from outside or grew naturally within, renders one *chayav chatas*, biblically liable. The range of positions is therefore striking: from Rav who permits squeezing boiled vegetables even for their water, through Shmuel who makes it *patur aval assur*, to Rabbi Yochanan who makes it *chayav chatas*.

## Baraita Interpretation

### Rav Reads Baraita

The Gemara raises a *baraita* that appears to conflict with all three opinions: *sochtin kevashim be'Shabbat le'tzorech ha'Shabbat* — one may squeeze pickled vegetables on Shabbat for Shabbat use, but not for after Shabbat. *Ve'zeitim va'anavim lo yischot, ve'im sachat chayav chatas* — olives and grapes one may not squeeze, and if one did, one is *chayav chatas*. This creates problems for everyone: it suggests that only olives and grapes entail *chiyuv chatas*, contrary to Rabbi Yochanan; it does not distinguish *le'gufan* from *le'meimeihen*, unlike Rav and Shmuel; and it permits pickled vegetables without qualification.

Each *amora* therefore explains the *baraita* according to his own position. Rav reads *bameh devarim amurim le'gufan* — the permitted case of squeezing pickled vegetables refers only to squeezing for the food itself, *le'gufan*. If one wants the liquid, that is *patur aval assur*, as Rav holds. *U'shlukot* — boiled vegetables are *muttar* in both cases, a ruling the *baraita* simply did not need to state. The ruling about olives and grapes — *chayav chatas* — applies when one has squeezed them *le'meimeihen*, though the *baraita* does not make that explicit. Rav's reading is relatively natural; the main intervention is qualifying the pickled vegetable case as referring to *le'gufan*.

### Shmuel Reads Baraita

Shmuel reads *u'hu ha'din le'shlukot* — and the same applies equally to boiled vegetables. The *baraita* teaches the ruling for pickled vegetables, and Shmuel adds that boiled vegetables have the same *halacha*: permitted *le'gufan*, *patur aval assur le'meimeihen*. *Bameh devarim amurim le'gufa* — the permitted case in both categories refers to squeezing for the food itself. The ruling about olives and grapes remains *chayav chatas*. Shmuel's reading, like Rav's, requires relatively modest intervention.

### R. Yochanan Forced Read

Rabbi Yochanan's reading is the most strained. He similarly reads *bameh devarim amurim le'gufa* — the entire first part of the *baraita* speaks only of squeezing for the food itself. But then: *aval le'meimeihen lo yischot, ve'im sachat na'aseh ke'mi she'sachat zeitim va'anavim ve'chayav chatas* — if one squeezed anything for its liquid, it is as though one squeezed olives and grapes, and one is *chayav chatas*. Under Rabbi Yochanan's reading, the *baraita*'s phrase "olives and grapes" does not limit the *chiyuv* to those species; rather, it means that anything squeezed *le'meimeihen* is *ke'zeitim va'anavim*, equally liable. This is acknowledged as the most forced reading, since the most natural reading of the *baraita* is that *chiyuv chatas* is specific to olives and grapes.

## Only Grapes & Olives

### Rav Chiya: Restricted Sc…

*Amar Rav Chiya bar Ashi amar Rav: devar Torah eino chayav ela al derisat zeitim va'anavim bilvad* — biblically, one is liable only for the pressing of olives and grapes. *Ve'chein tana d'bei Menashe* — and so too it was taught in the school of Menashe: *devar Torah eino chayav ela al derisat zeitim va'anavim bilvad*. The categorical formulation is notable: it sounds as though even squeezing a Valencia orange — fruit that is commercially grown specifically for its juice — would not constitute a biblical violation, since only olives and grapes, the classical sources of *mishkeh*, carry biblical status. The word *derisa*, stomping, may further suggest that even the manner of pressing matters, though the main point is the species restriction.

### Rabbeinu Chananel Opinion

Tosafot cites Rabbeinu Chananel's view at this point, which is not how we rule. Rabbeinu Chananel held *halacha ke'Rabbi Yochanan* — we rule like Rabbi Yochanan as against Rav and Shmuel. This has sweeping consequences: not only is squeezing for the liquid forbidden in all cases, but doing so is *chayav chatas* regardless of the type of vegetable and regardless of whether it is going into food or into a vessel. Notably, Rabbeinu Chananel holds that Rabbi Yochanan does not distinguish between squeezing into a pot and squeezing into a plate — *ve'lo hifrish bein sechita bein le'kdeira le'ke'ara*. Under his view, the dispensation to squeeze into food does not apply unless the squeezing is genuinely *le'gufa*, for the food itself. This is a position that some *acharonim* treat with caution even when ruling otherwise, particularly regarding grapes and olives.

### Practical Halacha Summary

The operative *halacha* does not follow Rabbeinu Chananel. Rather, it follows the conclusion suggested by the final statement of Rav Chiya bar Ashi and the school of Menashe: only grapes and olives are *d'Oraita*. Squeezing directly into food is permitted — though some are stringent regarding grapes and olives specifically even in that context, given that it is a potential biblical violation. Vegetables — whether pickled or boiled — may be squeezed *le'gufa* without concern. If squeezed *le'meimeihen*, the prohibition is rabbinic, *patur aval assur*, and we rule in accordance with Shmuel that this applies to both boiled and pickled vegetables alike.

A remaining question concerns the many cases in the Gemara involving wringing out cloths that have absorbed wine or other liquids. The concern there — why squeezing a wine-soaked cloth is treated as serious — is not fully explained by this analysis alone. Several *rishonim* emphasize that the problem of wringing out cloths is primarily *libun*, a laundering concern, not *sechita* in the sense we have been discussing. The cases where *sechita* is itself the concern would be specifically where wine was absorbed and one wants to extract it. According to Rashi's approach, even those cases would be only rabbinic. According to Tosafot's approach, they are rabbinic as well, but for a different reason — the substance is not olive oil or wine grown within. A third view holds that when a substance was originally a liquid and was then absorbed, it retains its liquid status, making subsequent squeezing potentially *chayav* even outside the formal categories — a position closer to Rabbi Yochanan's logic.

## Edut Mipi Edut

### Baraita on Hearsay

The Gemara carries forward a second ruling from the school of Menashe alongside the one about *sechita*: *ve'ein ed mi'pi ed kasher ela le'edut isha bilvad* — hearsay testimony, *edut mi'pi edut*, is valid only for the testimony that permits a woman to remarry. In all other contexts, formal testimony requires direct witness; one witness relaying what another witness told them is not acceptable. As we saw earlier in 145a, this is not a mere rumor — the chain ends with someone who claims to have directly seen the relevant event, such as a husband's death — but the chain itself is a form of hearsay.

### Bechor Testimony Question

The Gemara then raises a question imported from tractate Bechorot: *ed mi'pi ed le'edut bechor mahu?* Is hearsay testimony valid for testimony regarding a firstborn animal's blemish? The context: *kohanim* were suspect of deliberately inflicting blemishes on *bechoros* they received from *Yisraelim*, since a blemished *bechor* can be eaten outside the *Beit HaMikdash* without being brought as a *korban* — a much more convenient outcome. If a *kohen* presents a *bechor* with a blemish and there is no evidence that the blemish existed when the *Yisrael* gave it, he may not eat from it unless he can prove the blemish arose naturally. The question is whether hearsay testimony suffices to establish that fact.

*Rav Ami assar* — Rav Ami forbids relying on such testimony, citing the *baraita* explicitly: *ein ed mi'pi ed kasher ela le'edut isha bilvad*. Rav Assi permits it.

### Rav Assi Reinterprets

*Amar lei Rav Assi* — and here Rav Assi's reinterpretation is genuinely creative: *le'edut she'ha'isha kesheira lah bilvad*. He reads the *baraita* not as saying hearsay is permitted only for the purpose of allowing a woman to remarry, but rather that hearsay is permitted in any context where women's testimony is also accepted. The rationale is that wherever the formal requirements of testimony are relaxed — as they are for *agunah* cases, where women's testimony is accepted — hearsay should be equally permitted. Indeed, the argument goes further: women's testimony is a departure from the formal rules of testimony because women are categorically excluded from formal witnessing, whereas hearsay testimony involves witnesses who could in principle testify directly. If we relax requirements for women, there is at least as much reason to accept hearsay.

For *bechor*, women's testimony is in fact accepted to establish the circumstances of a blemish. Therefore, Rav Assi reasons, hearsay testimony should likewise be accepted for *bechor*.

### Halacha: Bechor Hearsay

*Rav Yeimar* acted on this ruling in practice, permitting a *kohen* to eat a *bechor* on the basis of hearsay testimony regarding the blemish. *Maremar* referred to him somewhat pointedly as *Yeimar sharei buchra* — Yeimar the one who permits firstborns. Despite the implicit criticism, *ve'hilchisa* — the *halacha* is that *ed mi'pi ed* is valid for *bechor*. The implication is that the principle extends: wherever formal testimony requirements are relaxed — wherever we would accept a woman's testimony — we also accept hearsay.

## Olives/Grapes on Shabbat

### Honey Cakes Case

The final case in the *Mishnah* addresses a situation where honey cakes are crushed and the honey begins to flow out, having started before Shabbat and continuing on Shabbat. Rabbi Eliezer permits benefiting from that honey. The principle underlying his leniency will become clearer when compared with the *baraita* that follows.

### Baraita: Pre-Shabbat Pre…

*Ki ata Rav Hoshia mi'Narda* — when Rav Hoshia came from Narda, he brought with him a *baraita*: *zeitim va'anavim she'riskam me'erev Shabbat* — if one pressed olives and grapes before Shabbat, *ve'yatzu me'atzman asurin* — and the juices flowed out on their own on Shabbat, they are forbidden. *Rabbi Eliezer ve'Rabbi Shimon matirin* — but Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon permit benefiting from those juices.

### Rav Yosef's Question

*Amar Rav Yosef: ve'gabbei yoser ata le'ashmuenan?* — what did this *baraita* add that we did not already know from our *Mishnah*? The *Mishnah* already told us that Rabbi Eliezer permits such liquid when honey cakes are involved. Adding Rabbi Shimon to Rabbi Eliezer might seem like a small point.

### Abaye's Chiddush

*Amar lei Abaye: tuva kamashma lan* — this is a major *chiddush*, not a minor one. The cases are fundamentally different, and the *baraita* teaches something that could not have been inferred from the *Mishnah* alone. *Klum kamashma lan d'ee masnisin* — what did our *Mishnah* teach? It taught regarding honey cakes. That case differs from olives and grapes in two crucial respects.

### Abaye's Chiddush

*D'ee masnisin hava amina: hasam hu d'meikara ochla ul'sof ochla* — in the case of honey cakes, one might have thought the reason Rabbi Eliezer permits it is that the substance was food before and remains food even when dissolved, since honey itself is a food. There is no genuine transition from *ochel* to *mashkeh*; the honey is not really producing a liquid in the way grapes produce wine. One might therefore have permitted it on that ground alone, without establishing any broader principle. *Aval hacha d'meikara ochla ul'sof mashkeh* — but here, with olives and grapes, what begins as a food *does* produce a genuine *mashkeh*, and beyond that, it is a biblical concern. *Eima lo* — one might have thought that even if the juices began flowing before Shabbat, perhaps even Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon would prohibit them in this case. *Kamashma lan* — the *baraita* teaches that they permit it here as well.

### Explanation: Natural Eme…

The underlying logic is connected to the principle established much earlier in the Gemara, regarding the Beit Shammai ruling about placing a beam on olives before Shabbat: once the juice has already begun to emerge, the remaining flow is treated as though the squeezing has already occurred. Even though continued liquid emerges on Shabbat, since the process was set in motion and the juice had already started coming out, the remaining outflow does not constitute active *sechita*. Based on this reasoning, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon rule — and the *Shulchan Aruch* rules accordingly — that if the juices of olives or grapes have already started emerging before Shabbat, one may benefit from the liquid that flows out on Shabbat.

## Ain Bishul Achar Bishul

### Mishna: Resubmerging All…

The *Mishnah* states: *kol she'ba b'chamim me'erev Shabbat, shorin oto b'chamim b'Shabbat* — anything that was already submerged in hot water before Shabbat may be resubmerged in hot water on Shabbat. This is somewhat striking in context, because this principle — *ain bishul achar bishul*, there is no cooking after cooking — was central to discussions much earlier in the tractate, yet the *Mishnah* and *Gemara* here proceed almost as though those discussions never happened. The operative principle is that once something has been cooked, resubmerging it in hot water on Shabbat cannot constitute *bishul*, since the cooking has already been completed.

*Ve'chol shelo ba b'chamim me'erev Shabbat, madichin oto b'chamim b'Shabbat* — anything that was not already heated before Shabbat may be rinsed in hot water on Shabbat but may not be submerged. This raises an immediate question: if the item is raw and the water is hot, why is rinsing permitted? The answer is that *iruy*, pouring from a *kli rishon*, is apparently not treated as *bishul* in the same way as actual submersion in a *kli rishon*. This *Mishnah* is therefore implicitly staking out a position on the question of *iruy kli rishon* — whether pouring from a first vessel, as opposed to placing something into it, constitutes *bishul*. Alternatively, some read this as referring to *iruy kli sheini*. Either way, the *Mishnah* encodes without fully explaining a number of critical principles.

### Salted Fish Exception

The *Mishnah* continues: *chutz min ha'maliach ha'yashan ve'kulyas ha'ispanin* — with the exception of old salted fish and a particular variety of Iberian salted fish. *She'dichatan zu hi gmar melachtan* — for those fish, rinsing them in hot water constitutes the completion of their processing; until that rinsing, they are not fully edible, and the hot water finishes the *melacha*. This is therefore forbidden.

Rashi, at the end of his comment on the *Mishnah*, writes *she'zo gmar melachtan va'havei bishul* — this is the completion of their work and constitutes *bishul*. This raises the question of whether there is a concept of *makeh b'patish* — the concluding blow, the finishing of a productive process — with respect to food. The *Yerushalmi* does speak of *makeh b'patish* regarding food, but the *Bavli* never explicitly states it, and the overwhelming consensus among *poskim* is that *makeh b'patish* does not apply to food. If it did, a number of anomalies would follow — for instance, why is *bishul chamah*, cooking in sunlight, permitted? It would seem to complete the food's preparation without any fire. Since *makeh b'patish* is not the operative principle, Rashi translates *gmar melacha* here as *bishul* proper, even though the language sounds more like *makeh b'patish*.

### Rav Safra: Boiled Chicken

*Kegon mai?* — the Gemara asks: what is an example of something that has been so thoroughly cooked before Shabbat that resubmerging it in hot water on Shabbat is entirely unproblematic? *Amar Rav Safra: kegon tarnigolta de'Rabbi Abba* — like the chicken of Rabbi Abba. Rabbi Abba apparently prepared chicken by boiling it so extensively that it was nearly reduced to a mush. Rav Safra relates that he once visited Rabbi Abba and ate from this dish — and had Rabbi Abba not then served him wine that was three years old (*bar tlata tarfei*, literally "three leaves," meaning three growing seasons), he would have been forced to regurgitate it. The dish was, from his Babylonian perspective, thoroughly revolting.

### Cuisine Debate

The regional culinary contrast becomes explicit. *Rabbi Yochanan rayik mi'kutcha de'Bavla'ei* — Rabbi Yochanan would spit at the mere thought of Babylonian *kutach*, a fermented cheese-and-bread condiment characteristic of Babylonian cuisine. *Amar Rav Yosef: ve'li'rayik anan mi'tarnigolta de'Rabbi Abba* — Rav Yosef retorts: and we gag at the thought of Rabbi Abba's chicken.

*Ve'od amar Rav Gaza: zimna chada ikla le'hasam, ve'avdi kutcha de'Bavla'ei, ve'sha'ilu minei kol briche'i Ma'arava* — Rav Gaza relates that he once traveled to Eretz Yisrael and prepared Babylonian *kutach* there, and every sick person in Eretz Yisrael wanted a portion of it. Whatever those in Eretz Yisrael thought of the dish in principle, in practice it apparently served as a remedy for the ill. The point is made with some relish: don't dismiss Babylonian cuisine.

### Raw Items: Rinse Only

The *Mishnah*'s ruling that raw items may only be rinsed and not submerged in hot water is understood to reflect the distinction between *iruy* and full immersion in a *kli rishon*. The *Gemara* implies that pouring hot water on most raw items — even at *yad soledes bo* — may not constitute *bishul*, perhaps because *iruy kli rishon* is treated differently from actual placement in the vessel. This is not definitively resolved here, which is part of why this *Mishnah* has such significance: it addresses questions of *kli rishon*, *kli sheini*, and *iruy* in a compressed way without spelling out the underlying analysis.

### Rav Yosef: Salted Fish

*Hedich mai?* — the Gemara asks: what is the law if one did in fact pour hot water on the salted fish? *Amar Rav Yosef: hedich chayav chatas* — Rav Yosef rules that one who poured hot water on the salted fish is biblically liable. As is common throughout the tractate, the *Mishnah*'s prohibition is followed by a Talmudic ruling that violation of it entails *chiyuv chatas*. Here there is no pushback.

The liability requires explanation. One might have thought that since the general ruling permits pouring water on raw items, the salted fish case — even if distinguished — should be only rabbinic. The general explanation, consistent with Rashi's reading, is that this constitutes *bishul* proper, not *makeh b'patish*. But this pushes into the *Mishnah*'s broader logic.

### Kali Bishul Concept

The key concept that emerges is *kali bishul* — foods that cook more easily than others. The general rule distinguishes *kli rishon* from *kli sheini*: things cook in a first vessel, not in a second. But that rule is not absolute. Certain foods cook so readily that they can be cooked even in circumstances where ordinary foods would not be affected — perhaps in a *kli sheini* at high temperature, or through *iruy* alone. The salted fish are examples of *kali bishul*: they are at a stage of preparation where the finishing touch of hot water completes their cooking even under conditions that would be innocuous for other foods. This is why the *Mishnah* singles them out and why Rav Yosef rules *chayav chatas*.

The significance of this concept extends well beyond this *Mishnah*. It is the basis for the widespread practice not to place even easily dissolved foods — such as tea or certain spices — into a *kli sheini* when the water remains at *yad soledes bo*. The concern is *kali bishul*: that those foods may cook under conditions where ordinary foods would not. That entire category is already implicit in this *Mishnah*.

## Bavel vs. Eretz Yisrael

### Fatter Birds in Bavel

The *Gemara* now returns to the comparative discussion of Babylonia and Eretz Yisrael. *Yasiv Rabbi Chiya bar Abba ve'Rabbi Assi kamei de'Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and Rabbi Assi were sitting before Rabbi Yochanan, who had begun to nod off. Taking advantage of the moment, the students began to converse among themselves.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba opened: *mipnei ma ofot she'be'Vavel shemeinim* — why are the birds in Babylonia so much fatter than those in Eretz Yisrael? *Amar lei: klach le'midbar Azza ve'ar'echa shemeinim me'hem* — Rabbi Assi replied: go to the wilderness of Azza and I will show you birds fatter than those. The pattern of the exchange becomes clear: Rabbi Chiya bar Abba raises observations in which Babylonia appears superior to Eretz Yisrael, and Rabbi Assi, defending the honor of Eretz Yisrael, attempts to deflect each one. In this first case, Rabbi Assi's answer is simply that even in Eretz Yisrael there are places with fatter birds.

*Mipnei ma moadim she'be'Vavel semeichim* — why are the holidays in Babylonia so much more joyous? *Mipnei she'einan aniyim* — because they are so poor that the holiday is the only occasion for joy. This response is something of an insult: the joy of Babylonian holidays is not a sign of abundance but of the grinding poverty of ordinary days.

*Mipnei ma talmidei chachamim she'be'Vavel metzuyanin* — why do the Torah scholars of Babylonia dress so distinctively and so well? *Lefi she'einan bnei Torah* — because they do not have genuine Torah learning to recommend them, so they must rely on their clothing to earn respect.

*Is'ar behu Rabbi Yochanan* — at this point Rabbi Yochanan awoke. He addressed the students with the word *dardekei*, children, and reminded them: *emor la'chochma achoti at* — say to wisdom, you are my sister. He explained: if something is as clear to you as the prohibition of your own sister, then say it; otherwise, do not say it. The lesson is striking: even in casual conversation, one should not offer explanations unless one actually knows. This applies not to Torah rulings but to worldly wisdom — *chochma*, not Torah — and yet the standard of epistemic rigor is identical. Then, apparently without resentment, he invited them to ask him to explain these phenomena properly.

### 52-Year Exile Evidence

Rabbi Yochanan's explanation for the fatness of Babylonian birds: *mipnei shelo galu* — because they never went into exile. The Babylonian birds, like the Babylonian population, were never displaced. The proof text he cites is about Moav — *sha'anan Moav mi'ne'urav*, Moav has been tranquil from its youth, and *uvagola lo halach*, it has never gone into exile, and therefore *al ken amad ta'mo bo ve'reicho lo namar*, its taste has remained in it and its smell has not changed. Babylonia, like Moav, was not subjected to exile, and therefore its creatures remained in their native conditions and flourished. Eretz Yisrael, by contrast, went into exile, and the effects persist.

One might object: who says birds go into exile? *Ve'hacha minailan de'galu* — how do we know the creatures of Eretz Yisrael went into exile? *De'tanya: Rabbi Yehuda omer chamishim u'shtayim shana lo avar ish bi'Yehuda* — it was taught that Rabbi Yehuda says that for 52 years no man passed through Yehuda. If the land was entirely depopulated of human beings, animals presumably departed as well. And indeed, the verse explicitly states: *me'of hashamayim ve'ad behema nadadu halachu* — from the birds of the sky to the animals, they wandered and went. The exile of fauna is not inferred but stated. As for the 52 years, Rashi and Tosafot note that this figure can be derived from the historical dates of the exile without resorting to *gematria*, though the *gematria* of *behema* (50+5+40+5 = 52) is also noted.

### Animals Return Exception

*Amar Rabbi Yaakov amar Rabbi Yochanan: kulam chazru chutz mi'kuvaya d'Ispani* — all the exiled creatures returned to Eretz Yisrael except for one variety of fish, the *kuvaya d'Ispani*. The reason: *de'amar Rav: hanei madrei de'Vavel mehadri maya le'Ein Eitam* — the inclines of Babylonia feed their waters toward Ein Eitam, the wellspring in Eretz Yisrael, which is the route by which fish from Babylonia can travel to Eretz Yisrael. But the *kuvaya d'Ispani* — *keivan de'lo shari shidrei* — because its spine is not stiff enough, *lo matzi salik*, it cannot make the upstream journey, and so it alone among the exiled creatures never returned.

### Holiday Joy Difference

Rabbi Yochanan's explanation for the greater joy of Babylonian holidays: *mipnei shelo haita bahem klala* — because the curse never fell upon them. The verse he cites: *ve'hishbati kol mesosa chaga chadsha ve'shabata ve'chol moada* — God declared that He would cause all the holidays and celebrations to cease. That curse applied to Eretz Yisrael; Babylonia was not the subject of this declaration. The result is that even though Eretz Yisrael has been rebuilt and the Jewish people have returned, the residual effect of that curse continues to diminish the joy of holidays there.

A further verse is cited: *chodsheichem u'moadeichem sanea nafshi, hayu alai latorach* — your new moons and festivals, My soul hates them; they have become a burden to Me. *Amar Rabbi Elazar amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu: lo dai lahem le'Yisrael she'chotim lefanai, ela she'matrichim oti leida eizeh gzeira kasha avi aleihem* — God says: it is not enough that they sin before Me; they then burden Me with having to determine which harsh decree to bring upon them. The holidays themselves become the occasion for calculating punishment.

On a practical level, *amar Rabbi Yitzchak: ein lecha kol rega va'rega shelo ba bah boleshet le'Tzipori* — there was not a single holiday in Tzipori that did not see a detachment of royal troops arrive, not attacking but quartering themselves and demanding provisions. *Ve'amar Rabbi Chanina: ein lecha kol rega va'rega shelo ba le'Tiveria agmon ve'kamton u'va'al zmora* — and in Tiberias, on every holiday, various types of governors and officers would arrive and press the population into service.

This historical reality, that the Jews of Eretz Yisrael lived under oppressive Roman rule while the Jews of Babylonia enjoyed considerably better conditions, is not merely ancient. The contrast between Eretz Yisrael culture — which shaped Ashkenazic practice — and Babylonian culture — which shaped Sephardic practice — persists in *halachic* differences observable to this day. One significant example: Ashkenazim do not recite *Birkat Kohanim* daily, because the *Rama* explains that we lack the genuine *simcha* that *duchaning* requires outside of *Yom Tov*; Sephardim, inheritors of the Babylonian tradition of relative tranquility, recite it every day. Similarly, Ashkenazim do not recite *Shehecheyanu* at a *brit milah*, whereas Sephardim do. These differences in liturgical practice trace directly to the difference in communal joy between the two centers. A student notes that certain Spanish-Portuguese communities that settled in the Netherlands also do not recite *Birkat Kohanim* daily — evidently because they passed through *Ashkenaz* and absorbed that influence.

### Scholar's Dress Status

*Mipnei ma talmidei chachamim she'be'Vavel metzuyanin* — Rabbi Yochanan's real answer regarding the distinctive dress of Babylonian Torah scholars is quite different from the students' dismissive explanation. *Lefi she'einam bnei mekoma* — because they are not native to the place. They are exiles, not locals. *De'amri inshi: be'mata shemai* — people say: in your own city your reputation speaks for you. *Be'lo mata totvai* — in a city where you are a stranger and an outsider, you must dress in a way that makes your status visible, because no one knows who you are.

*Haba'im yashresh Yaakov, yatzitz u'farach Yisrael* — the verse speaks of those who come from afar taking root and flowering. *Tani Rav Yosef: elu talmidei chachamim she'be'Vavel* — this refers to the Torah scholars of Babylonia, *she'osim tzitzim u'frachim la'Torah* — who produce blossoms and flowers for the Torah. The distinctive dress is thus reframed entirely: it is not a sign of inadequacy but of exile, and it reflects a genuine contribution to Torah.

### Non-Jewish Stench Origin

The final question in the student exchange had nothing to do with the Babylonia-Eretz Yisrael comparison: *mipnei ma ovdei kochavim zohamim* — why do non-Jews have a stench? The students' answer — because they eat *sheratzim u'remashim*, forbidden creatures, and this produces a physical odor — is abandoned by Rabbi Yochanan in favor of a different explanation. *She'lo amdu al Har Sinai* — because they did not stand at Har Sinai. *She'bish'ah she'ba nachash al Chava* — when the snake had intercourse with Chava, in the rabbinic *midrashic* tradition, *hitil bah zohama* — it implanted in her a *zohama*, a corruption or stench. *Yisrael she'amdu al Har Sinai paska zohamatan* — when Israel stood at Har Sinai, this corruption ceased for them. According to *midrash*, just as Israel was healed at Sinai from various ailments, so too this primordial corruption was lifted. *Ovdei kochavim shelo amdu al Har Sinai lo paska zohamatan* — those who did not stand there were never cleansed of it.

### Converts' Mazal at Sinai

*Amar lei Rav Acha brei de'Rava le'Rav Ashi: geirei mai?* — what about converts? They too did not stand at Har Sinai, nor did their ancestors. How does the *zohama* cease for them? *Amar lei: af al gav de'inhu lo havu, mazlaihu havu* — even though they were not physically present, their *mazal* was there. Their spiritual presence was at Sinai, and that is what was purified. The proof text: *et asher yeshno poh imanu omed hayom lifnei Hashem Elokeinu ve'et asher einenu poh* — even those who are not physically present here are included in the covenant.

### Three Generation Theory

*U'pliga de'Rabbi Abba bar Kahana* — this position, that the *zohama* ended at Sinai, is disputed by Rabbi Abba bar Kahana. *De'amar Rabbi Abba bar Kahana: ad shlosha dorot lo paska zohama me'avoteinu* — the corruption did not end at Sinai; it ended earlier, after three generations from Avraham. *Avraham holid et Yishmael* — the first generation still produced someone outside the chosen line. *Yitzchak holid et Esav* — the second generation likewise. *Yaakov holid yud-bet shvatim shelo haya bahem shum shemetz dofi* — only with Yaakov, who fathered the 12 tribes with no admixture of anything outside the pure line, did the *zohama* fully end. According to Rabbi Abba bar Kahana, the purification was a gradual process completed by Yaakov's generation, not a sudden event at Sinai.

## Essentialism Question

### Essentialism Challenge

A student raises a thoughtful question about the broader conceptual framework underlying these teachings. The *Gemara*'s discussion has invoked notions of distinctive Jewish character, spiritual presence at Sinai, and inherited corruption removed at a historical moment — all of which seem to presuppose a form of essentialism about the Jewish people. The student notes that in other domains — particularly in contemporary biological understanding — essentialism about species is rejected; biology is understood as a continuous, fluid process. Why, then, maintain essentialism when speaking about the Jewish people?

The question is acknowledged as genuinely important, and the discussion it invites would begin from first principles: what does it actually mean to say the Jewish people are different? Does it mean different DNA? Obviously not. Does it mean a different *neshama*? What content can be given to that claim? The answer would need to be worked out from both a Torah perspective and a philosophical one — but that analysis, the discussion makes clear, is a conversation for another time.
