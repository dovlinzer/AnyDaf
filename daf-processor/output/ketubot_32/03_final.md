# Ketubot 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lashes vs. Payment

### Incest/Rape Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו כּוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ, אֵלּוּ הֵן הַלּוֹקִין: הַבָּא עַל אֲחוֹתוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אָבִיו, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִמּוֹ, וְעַל אֲחוֹת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו, וְעַל אֵשֶׁת אֲחִי אָבִיו, וְעַל הַנִּדָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: Similarly, **one who has** forced **relations with his sister,** i.e., he rapes her, **or with his father's sister,** or with his mother's sister, or with his wife's sister, or with his brother's wife, or with his father's brother's wife after they divorced, or with a menstruating woman, there is a fine paid. **And** the Gemara **raised a contradiction** from the following mishna (*Makkot* 13a): **And these** people **are flogged: One who has relations with his sister, or with his father's sister, or with his mother's sister, or with his wife's sister, or with his brother's wife, or with his father's brother's wife, or with a menstruating woman.** Anyone who intentionally has relations with any of these women is punished with lashes.

The *sugya* opens by picking up from the bottom of 31b and the beginning of 32a, in the context of a broader discussion that has been unfolding across the preceding pages. The *mishna* has established that one who rapes a woman to whom he is forbidden under penalty of *kares* — such as his sister or his father's sister — still owes the monetary fine (*knas*) applicable in cases of rape of a *na'arah*. The *gemara* has already wrestled with the position of Rabbi Nechunya ben Ha-kanah, who holds that liability for *kares* overrides monetary liability, just as liability for court-administered execution does. That discussion occupied the preceding *sugya*. Now a new dimension of the problem comes into focus.

The *gemara* introduces a *tannaitic* source from the tractate dealing with those who receive lashes: *elu hen ha-lokin* — the following people receive lashes — and proceeds to list precisely the cases of forbidden relationships that the *mishna* in Ketubot addresses: one who has relations with his sister, his father's sister, his mother's sister, his wife's sister, his brother's wife, his father's brother's wife, and a woman in a state of *niddah*. All of these carry the punishment of *kares*, and when prosecuted by the court, that punishment takes the form of lashes.

### Both Lashes & Payment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַיְימָא לַן דְּאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה וּמְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** since **we maintain** in general **that one is not** both **flogged and** liable to **pay,** if one receives lashes for having relations with his sister, why must he pay the fine as well?

The difficulty is now apparent. The *mishna* in Ketubot says these individuals pay the monetary fine. The other *mishna* says they receive lashes. And we have an established principle — *ein lokeh u-meshalem* — that the court does not administer both lashes and monetary payment to the same person for the same act. So which is it?

It is important to distinguish this problem from the one treated earlier. The principle of *kim lei bidravah minei* — the override of a lesser liability by a greater one — operates on the level of intrinsic severity: an act deserving of execution or *kares* is weighty enough to negate the concurrent monetary liability altogether, regardless of whether the court actually administers any punishment. That was Rabbi Nechunya ben Ha-kanah's position. The present question is different in character. It concerns the *actual administration* of punishment by the court: the court simply will not do both things to the same person. But this raises the question of which one yields. The easiest answer — and one the *gemara* will indeed offer — is that in our *mishna*'s case, warning (*hatra'ah*) was never given, so lashes cannot be administered, and the monetary obligation therefore stands uncontested.

### Ein Lokeh u-Meshalem

But the *gemara* presses harder: even if we assume a case where the court *was* prepared to administer lashes — where all procedural requirements including *hatra'ah* were met — how does the *mishna* justify requiring payment? Once we have a case deserving of lashes, does the rule of *ein lokeh u-meshalem* not eliminate the monetary obligation? This is the core tension the *sugya* will now explore.

## Ulla's Position

### Na'arah vs. Bogeret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ נַעֲרָה, כָּאן בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ בּוֹגֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said:** This is **not difficult; here,** the *halakha* in the mishna is **with regard to his sister** who is a **young woman,** for whom one pays a fine and is not flogged, whereas **there,** the *halakha* in the mishna is **with regard to his sister** who is a **grown woman,** for whom one does not pay a fine.

Ulla offers the first resolution. The apparent contradiction between the two *mishnayot* dissolves once we distinguish between two different scenarios. When does one pay the fine? In the case of *achoto na'arah* — his sister who is a *na'arah*, a girl between twelve and twelve-and-a-half, to whom the special *knas* of the Torah applies. When does one receive lashes? In the case of *achoto bogeret* — his adult sister, who is past the age of *na'arah*, for whom no *knas* exists. Since there is no monetary obligation in that case, lashes can be administered without conflict.

This resolution, however, implicitly reflects a significant position on Ulla's part: when both lashes and payment apply, it is the *payment* that takes precedence, not the lashes. The *mishna* that speaks of payment is teaching that even where lashes are in principle warranted — even where warning was given — the monetary obligation overrides and the lashes are not administered. Lashes appear only where there is no monetary obligation at all.

### Boshet u-Pegam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹתוֹ בּוֹגֶרֶת נָמֵי, הָא אִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם! בְּשׁוֹטָה. וְהָא אִיכָּא צַעֲרָא! בִּמְפוּתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the case of one who has relations with **his sister** who is a **grown woman, too,** although he does not pay a fine, **isn't there** compensation for **humiliation and degradation?** He should be exempt from lashes in that case as well. The Gemara answers: **There,** the *halakha* in the mishna is **with regard to** his sister who is **an imbecile,** with regard to whom there is no humiliation or degradation beyond her status as an imbecile. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** there payment for **pain** even in the rape of an imbecile? The Gemara responds: The *halakha* is **with regard to a seduced woman,** who is not entitled to payment for pain, as she engaged in relations willingly.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this. Even in the case of a *bogeret*, where the special *knas* of fifty *shekel* does not apply, there remain other monetary obligations. Under *halacha*, rape is treated as an act of physical violence against a person, subject to the same framework of compensatory payments as any such act. Even without the special fine of the *na'arah betula*, the rapist still owes *boshet* — compensation for embarrassment — and *pegam* — compensation for the injury to the victim's dignity and diminished value, assessed analogously to the way one evaluates physical damage. This *pegam* is calculated as the difference between the market value of a slave woman who is a virgin and one who is not, an admittedly imperfect but legally operative measure of the harm inflicted. Beyond these two, there is also *tza'ar* — payment for pain and suffering.

If all of these payments remain due even in the case of a *bogeret*, then even there the conflict between lashes and monetary obligation persists, and Ulla's distinction collapses.

### Consensual Sex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אֲחוֹתוֹ נַעֲרָה, בִּיתוֹמָה וּמְפוּתָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Now that you have arrived at this** explanation that the mishna is referring to a seduced woman, the mishna can be understood **even if you say** it is referring **to his sister** who is **a young woman.** The reason that the seducer does not pay the fine is that the *halakha* is **with regard to** one who is **an orphan and a seduced woman.** Were her father alive, he would receive the payment. Because he died, the payment goes to her. Since she willingly participated in the relations, she relinquished her right to the payment, and the seducer is therefore liable to receive lashes.

The *gemara* resolves this by narrowing the case further: *b'shotah* — in the case of a woman who is mentally incapacitated. Such a person would not experience embarrassment in the legally cognizable sense and therefore *boshet* would not apply. The *gemara* then anticipates the follow-up: even a *shotah* experiences physical pain, so *tza'ar* would remain. The answer: *bimfutah* — in a case where the act was consensual, not rape. In that case there is no *tza'ar* owed, and indeed no compelled harm at all.

Once the *gemara* arrives at the scenario of consensual relations, it draws a further conclusion. *Hashta d'atit l'hachi* — now that we have established that it must be a consensual case — the woman need not even be a *bogeret*. She could be a *na'arah*, provided she is a *yetomah u-mfutah* — an orphan who acts willingly. An orphan girl, even if she is only twelve years old, is legally emancipated from paternal authority since her father is deceased, and her own consent therefore governs. In such a case there is no *knas*, no *boshet*, no *tza'ar* — no monetary liability of any kind — and so lashes can be administered without conflict.

### Money Overrides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר עוּלָּא: כָּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי. מְנָא לֵיהּ לְעוּלָּא הָא? גָּמַר מֵחוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ: מָה חוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי; אַף כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara observes: **Apparently, Ulla maintains** that in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** e.g., one who has forced relations with his sister who is a young woman, one **pays money** but **is not flogged.** The Gemara asks: **From where does Ulla** derive **this** principle? The Gemara answers: He **derives** it **from** the *halakha* **of one who injures another. Just as** with regard to **one who injures another where there is** liability to both pay **money** for the injury **and** receive **lashes** for violating the prohibition "Lest he continues to strike him" (Deuteronomy 25:3), the *halakha* there is that one **pays money** but **is not flogged, so too,** in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** one **pays money** but **is not flogged.**

The upshot of Ulla's position is now fully articulated: *kol heicha de'ika mammon u-malkus, mammona meshalem malka lo laki* — wherever there is both monetary liability and liability for lashes, one pays the money and does not receive lashes. The *mishna* that speaks of payment reflects cases where money is owed; the *mishna* that speaks of lashes reflects cases where no money is owed. This is the rule according to Ulla: money overrides lashes.

The *gemara* asks: what is Ulla's source for this principle? The answer is that he derives it by analogy from the case of *chovel b'chaveiro* — one who physically injures another person. That case is paradigmatic: there is a negative prohibition against striking another person, derived by a *kal va-chomer* from the verse *lo yosif lehakkoto* which limits the lashes administered by the court, and yet the *halacha* requires monetary payment rather than lashes. This is taken as a model for the general rule that monetary obligation takes precedence over lashes.

## Chovel b'Chaveiro

### Five Payments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְחוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ — שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בַּחֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים. וְאִי מָמוֹנָא לְקוּלָּא — שֶׁכֵּן הוּתַּר מִכְּלָלוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the basis for the comparison between other cases and the case of **one who injures another?** One who injures another cannot serve as a paradigm for cases of liability for both money and lashes because the case of one who injures another is particularly stringent, **as he is liable** to pay **five types** of indemnity: Injury, pain, medical costs, loss of livelihood, and humiliation. **And if** payment of **money** is a more **lenient** form of punishment than lashes, one could infer *a fortiori*: If in the stringent case of injuring another, one receives the more lenient punishment, all the more so would he receive the more lenient punishment in less stringent cases; nevertheless, one who injures another cannot serve as a paradigm for cases of liability for both money and lashes. The reason is that there is also a lenient aspect with regard to injuring another, **as** it is **permitted,** in departure **from its norm, in court.** The court administers lashes, injuring those convicted. The leniency is that its application is selective.

The *gemara* now examines the paradigm of *chovel b'chaveiro* more carefully. One who injures another person is liable for up to five distinct categories of payment: *nezek* (damages, assessed by the difference in the injured person's value as a slave before and after the injury), *tza'ar* (pain and suffering), *boshet* (embarrassment), *ripuy* (medical expenses), and *shevet* (lost income during the period of recuperation). These five payments make the monetary obligation in cases of injury particularly weighty. At the same time, the prohibition against striking carries a potential lashes-based sanction. The *halacha*, however, requires payment rather than lashes.

### Why Money?

The *gemara* challenges Ulla's use of this case as a general paradigm. The argument from *chovel b'chaveiro* can be read in two opposite ways, depending on how one ranks lashes against monetary payment in terms of severity.

If monetary payment is considered the *more severe* punishment, then the rule that one pays rather than receives lashes in cases of injury could reflect the exceptional weightiness of the injury case — five separate payments make it an unusually heavy monetary liability — and one might therefore conclude that in less weighty cases, lashes would apply rather than payment.

Conversely, if monetary payment is the *more lenient* option, then the injury case might be exceptional for a different reason: *shekein hutrah mikhlalah b'veit din* — the prohibition against striking is not absolute, since the court itself is permitted to administer lashes. Because the prohibition is not categorical, it is not as severe a transgression, and therefore the Torah imposes the lighter sanction of monetary payment in this context. But other prohibitions — like that of incest — are absolute, and one might say the Torah would insist on the heavier sanction of lashes there.

Either way, the injury case cannot serve as a clean paradigm from which to derive a universal rule.

### Edim Zomemin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גָּמַר מֵעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין: מָה עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי; אַף כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara states that Ulla **derives** this principle **from** the *halakha* of false, **conspiring witnesses. Just as** with regard to **conspiring witnesses, where there is** liability to both pay **money,** if they falsely testified to render one liable for payment, **and** receive **lashes,** for violating the prohibition "You shall not bear false witness against your neighbor" (Exodus 20:13), and the *halakha* is that one **pays money** but **is not flogged, so too,** in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** one **pays money** but **is not flogged.**

The *gemara* then proposes a second paradigm: *edim zomemin*, false witnesses. When witnesses are found to have testified falsely, they are subject to the sanction of *ka'asher zamam* — do to them what they intended to do to the accused — which in a monetary case means paying the sum of money they sought to extract from the accused. They also transgress the prohibition of *lo sa'aneh b'rei'acha eid shaker*, which carries lashes. Yet the *halacha* requires monetary payment under *ka'asher zamam* and not lashes. Here too, then, we have a case where money overrides lashes.

If monetary payment is considered more severe, one might argue that *edim zomemin* are an exceptional case because they do not require *hatra'ah* — warning is not needed for the sanction of *ka'asher zamam* to apply. The absence of a warning requirement is itself a marker of the heightened severity of this category, and therefore it is not surprising that they receive the harsher sanction of monetary payment.

If monetary payment is considered more lenient, one might argue that *edim zomemin* are exceptional because *lo asu ma'aseh* — they committed no physical act, only speech — and therefore the Torah goes easy on them, requiring only the lighter sanction of monetary payment. But this leniency would not extend to someone who commits a physical act such as incest.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא גָּמַר מִתַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מָה הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי; אַף כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי. מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן — שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד חָמוּר, וְאִי מָמוֹנָא לְקוּלָּא הוּא — שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד הַקַּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Ulla **derives** the principle **from both of them,** the cases of one who injures another and of conspiring witnesses. **The common denominator of** both cases **is that there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes** and the *halakha* is that one **pays money** but **is not flogged; so too,** in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** one **pays money** but **is not flogged.** The Gemara asks: **What** is the basis for the comparison of other cases to **the common denominator of** both cases, **as they have an element of stringency** that does not exist in other prohibitions in that one who injures another pays five types of indemnity, and conspiring witnesses are flogged without forewarning? **And if** payment of **money** is a more **lenient** form of punishment than lashes, other cases cannot be derived from it, **as they have an element of leniency** that does not exist in other prohibitions. The prohibition in the case of one who injures another is selectively applied, as it is permitted, in departure from its norm, in court, and the case of conspiring witnesses is lenient because they performed no action.

Anticipating these objections to each individual case, the *gemara* proposes a *tzad hashaveh* — a common-denominator argument. Even if each of the two paradigms has its own special features, the one thing they share is precisely the relevant feature: each involves both monetary liability and lashes, and in each case the outcome is monetary payment rather than lashes. That common denominator should therefore yield the general rule: *kol heicha de'ika mamona u-malkus, mamona meshalem u-malka lo laki*.

## Special Features

### Chovel Exceptions

The *gemara* rejects the *tzad hashaveh* argument as well. When constructing a common-denominator argument, one must eliminate all features that distinguish the cases from each other and from the general rule one seeks to derive. But here, each case retains a distinctive feature that can explain the outcome independently. In the case of *chovel b'chaveiro*, one can point to the five payments as a distinguishing feature — whether as a marker of weightiness (if money is more severe) or as a leniency trigger (the prohibition having exceptions in the context of court-administered lashes).

### Edim Zomemin Features

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְעֵדִים זוֹמְמִין — שֶׁכֵּן אֵינָן צְרִיכִים הַתְרָאָה. וְאִי מָמוֹנָא לְקוּלָּא הוּא — שֶׁכֵּן לֹא עָשׂוּ מַעֲשֶׂה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the basis for the comparison of other cases to the case of **conspiring witnesses?** Conspiring witnesses cannot serve as a paradigm for cases of liability for both money and lashes because the case of conspiring witnesses is particularly stringent, **as they do not require forewarning.** As a rule, the courts administer punishment only to one who was forewarned not to perform the transgression. The fact that this is not a requirement in the case of conspiring witnesses indicates that it is a particularly stringent prohibition. Therefore, no proof can be cited from the case of conspiring witnesses to other cases with regard to monetary payment instead of lashes. **And if** payment of **money** is a more **lenient** form of punishment than lashes, the case of conspiring witnesses also has a lenient aspect, **as they did not perform an action** but merely spoke.

In the case of *edim zomemin*, one can point either to the absence of a *hatra'ah* requirement (a marker of severity) or to the purely verbal nature of the transgression (a marker of leniency). Each case, in other words, has a feature that explains why *that* case results in monetary payment rather than lashes.

### Not a General Rule

Because each case can be explained by its own special feature, the *tzad hashaveh* fails to establish a general rule. As Tosafot notes, taken to its logical conclusion this objection would seem to undermine the *tzad hashaveh* as a tool of derivation entirely, since one could always point to some distinctive feature of each case. Tosafot addresses this difficulty in their commentary. In any case, the *gemara* concludes here that neither paradigm individually nor their combination is sufficient to establish a universal rule. The question of where Ulla derives his position thus remains open, and the *gemara* now turns to a different type of derivation.

## Gezeirah Shavah

### Tachat Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עוּלָּא ״תַּחַת״ ״תַּחַת״ גָּמַר. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִנָּהּ״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״. מָה הָתָם — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי; אַף כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹנָא וּמַלְקוֹת — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Ulla derives** the fact that one pays and is not flogged by means of a verbal analogy between the terms **for** and **for.** The verse **states** with regard to rape: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty shekels of silver, and to him she shall be as a wife, **because [*taḥat*] he tormented her"** (Deuteronomy 22:29), **and it states there,** with regard to injury: **"An eye for [*taḥat*] an eye"** (Exodus 21:24). **Just as there,** with regard to injury, one **pays money** and **is not flogged,** so too, in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** one **pays money** but **is not flogged.**

Ulla in fact derives his rule through a *gezeirah shavah* — a verbal analogy between two verses that share a common term. The verse describing the rapist's monetary obligation reads *tachat asher innah* — because he afflicted her — using the word *tachat*. The verse establishing the principle of monetary compensation for physical injury reads *ayin tachat ayin* — an eye for an eye — also using the word *tachat*. By this *gezeirah shavah*, Ulla links the two contexts: just as *ayin tachat ayin* is interpreted by *chazal* to mean monetary payment rather than literal retaliation, and just as that monetary payment takes precedence over any lashes-based sanction, so too in the case of rape — *ayin tachat ayin* being the locus classicus for monetary payment overriding lashes — the monetary obligation of the rapist takes precedence over the lashes he would otherwise receive for the associated prohibition.

### Tosafot on Rape

Tosafot offer an important interpretive observation here. It would be a striking and counterintuitive position to hold, as a universal principle across all of Torah, that monetary payment always overrides lashes whenever both apply. The evidence for such a sweeping rule is thin. Tosafot therefore suggest that at this point in the *sugya*, the *gemara* is shifting to a more limited claim: Ulla's *gezeirah shavah* from *tachat* to *tachat* is not establishing a general principle at all. Rather, it is a derivation specific to the case of rape. When rape coincides with a prohibition that would otherwise carry lashes — such as rape of a sister — Ulla holds that the monetary obligation for the rape takes precedence and the lashes are not administered. This is analogous to the case of *chovel b'chaveiro*, where the logic is that compensating the victim takes priority over punishing the perpetrator through lashes. Ulla extends this logic to rape by means of the *gezeirah shavah*.

On this reading, Ulla may well concede the general rule that lashes override monetary payment; his claim is confined to the specific case of rape and incest occurring together. In that case alone, the monetary obligation of the rape is treated like the monetary obligation of *chovel b'chaveiro* — it takes precedence, and the lashes are not administered.

## Rabbi Yochanan

### Warning Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אֲחוֹתוֹ נַעֲרָה: כָּאן שֶׁהִתְרוּ בּוֹ, כָּאן שֶׁלֹּא הִתְרוּ בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § In proposing a different resolution to the apparent contradiction between the mishna here that rules that one pays a fine for raping his sister and the mishna in *Makkot* that rules that one is flogged in that case, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even if you say** that both *mishnayot* are referring to **his sister** who is **a young woman, there** it is referring to a case **where** the witnesses **forewarned him,** and therefore the rapist is flogged; **here,** it is referring to a case **where** the witnesses **did not forewarn him.** Since no lashes are administered without forewarning, the rapist pays the fine.

Rabbi Yochanan takes a fundamentally different position. His resolution of the conflict between the two *mishnayot* does not require distinguishing between *na'arah* and *bogeret* at all. He says: *afilu tima achoto na'arah* — even if we are dealing with his sister who is a *na'arah*, the classic case where both the *knas* and the lashes theoretically apply, there is no contradiction. *Kan she-hitru bo, kan she-lo hitru bo* — in one case warning was given, in the other it was not. When warning was given and lashes can be administered, lashes are given and money is not paid. When warning was not given and lashes cannot be administered, money is paid.

### Lashes Primary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא מָמוֹן וּמַלְקוֹת וְאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ — מִילְקָא לָקֵי, מָמוֹנָא לָא מְשַׁלֵּם. מְנָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״כְּדֵי רִשְׁעָתוֹ״, מִשּׁוּם רִשְׁעָה אַחַת אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ, וְאִי אַתָּה מְחַיְּיבוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי רִשְׁעָיוֹת. וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״אַרְבָּעִים יַכֶּנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara observes: **Apparently, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains** that in **any** case **where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes, and** the witnesses **forewarned him, he is flogged** but does not **pay money.** The Gemara asks: **From where does Rabbi Yoḥanan** derive **this** principle? The Gemara explains that he derives it from that which **the verse states** with regard to one sentenced to lashes in the court: "The judge shall cause him to lie down, and to be beaten before him, **according to the measure of his wickedness"** (Deuteronomy 25:2), from which it is inferred: **For one** act of **wickedness,** i.e., punishment, **you can render him liable, but you cannot render him liable for two** acts of **wickedness. And juxtaposed to** this it states: **"Forty he shall strike him"** (Deuteronomy 25:3), indicating that the punishment that is administered when one is liable to receive two punishments is lashes and not payment.

This means Rabbi Yochanan holds the opposite of Ulla's general rule: *kol heicha de'ika mamona u-malkuta ve-atru bei, milka laki mamona lo meshalem* — wherever there is both monetary liability and liability for lashes and warning was given, lashes are administered and money is not paid. Lashes override monetary payment, not the reverse.

The practical disagreement between Ulla and Rabbi Yochanan comes into sharp focus in the case of rape combined with incest where warning was given. For Ulla, even in that case the monetary obligation takes precedence and the man pays rather than receiving lashes. For Rabbi Yochanan, once warning was given, lashes are the operative sanction and no monetary payment is made.

## Rabbi Yochanan's Source

### Kedei Rishato

The *gemara* asks: what is Rabbi Yochanan's source for the principle that lashes override monetary payment? He derives it from the verse *kedei rishato* — according to his wickedness — which appears in the context of lashes. The verse says to administer lashes commensurate with his wickedness.

### One Wickedness

The *gemara* derives: *mishum rish'ah achat atah mechayvo* — you make him liable on account of one act of wickedness, *ve-i atah mechayvo mishum shtei rishayot* — but not for two acts of wickedness arising from the same transgression. The verse singles out *his* wickedness — *rish'ato*, in the singular — indicating that for any single act, only one form of punishment is administered. And then, immediately adjacent in the text, the verse continues with the instruction to administer lashes. The juxtaposition teaches that when a choice must be made, lashes is the punishment imposed — lashes being identified as the primary punishment for a single act of wickedness.

### Multiple Lashes

One might note an apparent tension: the *gemara* in Makkot discusses cases where a single act triggers liability for multiple prohibitions, resulting in multiple sets of lashes — if one eats a certain insect, one might be liable under several overlapping prohibitions simultaneously. So multiple lashes are possible. The principle here is narrower: it is specifically the combination of lashes and *monetary payment* that cannot coexist. Two separate lashes-based sanctions, each arising from a distinct prohibition, can both be administered. What cannot happen is lashes for one dimension of the act and monetary payment for another dimension of the same act. The verse *kedei rishato* teaches that these two distinct modalities of punishment cannot be combined.

## Personal Injury

### Perutah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ, אֲבָל אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ — מִילְקָא לָקֵי, מָמוֹנָא לָא מְשַׁלֵּם, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִכָּהוּ הַכָּאָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — לוֹקֶה. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ, אַמַּאי לוֹקֶה? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ, וְטַעְמָא — דְּלֵית בֵּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, הָא אִית בֵּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — מָמוֹנָא מְשַׁלֵּם, מִילְקָא לָא לָקֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what of the case of **one who injures another where there is** liability to both pay **money and** receive **lashes,** in which case one **pays money** but **is not flogged? And lest you say** that **this applies only when** the witnesses **did not forewarn him, but if they forewarned him** before he struck his friend he **is flogged** but **does not pay money, didn't Rabbi Ami say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If one **struck** another with **a blow that** does **not** cause damage that amounts to **the value of a *peruta*,** he is **flogged?** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of that case? **If** it is a case **where** the witnesses **did not forewarn him, why is he flogged?** No lashes are administered without forewarning. **Rather, obviously** it is a case **where they forewarned him, and the reason** he is flogged is that **there is not** damage that amounts to **the value of a *peruta*.** The damages are not quantifiable. The Gemara infers: However, if **there is** damage that amounts to **the value of a *peruta*,** he **pays money** and **is not flogged,** even though he was forewarned.

If Rabbi Yochanan's general rule is that lashes take precedence over monetary payment, how does he explain the exception of *chovel b'chaveiro*, where the *halacha* clearly requires monetary payment and not lashes? One might suggest that this exception applies only where *hatra'ah* was not given — in which case lashes cannot be administered and monetary payment simply fills the vacuum. But if *hatra'ah* was given, perhaps even in a case of personal injury, one would give lashes and not require payment.

### Any Payment

This suggestion is foreclosed by a teaching transmitted by Rav Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan himself: *haka'ah she'ein bah shaveh perutah lokeh* — if one struck another person in a manner that inflicted a wound of less than a *perutah*'s worth of damage, the perpetrator receives lashes. This ruling is striking in its irony: the most trivial act of physical aggression, one that caused essentially no quantifiable harm whatsoever — no *nezek*, no *tza'ar*, no *shevet*, no *ripuy*, no *boshet* of any monetary significance — results in forty lashes. Meanwhile, cutting off a person's arm results in monetary payment with no lashes. The more serious the injury, the more certain one is to pay rather than be lashed.

The ruling necessarily implies that the case involves *hatra'ah* — if there were no warning, lashes could not be imposed for any reason. And yet lashes are imposed. This means that even *with* warning, the general rule in cases of personal injury is monetary payment — but in the singular edge case where no monetary obligation whatsoever exists, not even a *perutah*'s worth, lashes apply. The moment there is any monetary obligation — even the smallest amount — monetary payment takes over and lashes are not administered.

### Torah Explicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין לְתַשְׁלוּמִין, הָכָא נָמֵי: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה חוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ לְתַשְׁלוּמִין. וְהֵיכָא אִיתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי אִילְעָא — אַהָא: מְעִידִין אָנוּ אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁחַיָּיב לַחֲבֵירוֹ מָאתַיִם זוּז, וְנִמְצְאוּ זוֹמְמִין — לוֹקִין וּמְשַׁלְּמִין, שֶׁלֹּא הַשֵּׁם הַמְּבִיאָן לִידֵי מַכּוֹת מְבִיאָן לִידֵי תַּשְׁלוּמִין, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** This brings Rabbi Yochanan back to his overarching position. His general rule is lashes over payment. The case of *chovel b'chaveiro* is an exception, but it requires a textual basis — the Torah must explicitly identify it as such. And indeed, as Rabi Ila states, *bifiresh ribtah Torah eidim zomemim l'tashlumin* — the Torah explicitly includes *edim zomemin* in the category of monetary payment — and the same principle applies to *chovel b'chaveiro*: *bifiresh ribtah Torah chovel b'chaveiro l'tashlumin* — the Torah explicitly mandates monetary payment in cases of personal injury, making it an explicit exception to the general rule.

This brings Rabbi Yochanan back to his overarching position. His general rule is lashes over payment. The case of *chovel b'chaveiro* is an exception, but it requires a textual basis — the Torah must explicitly identify it as such. And indeed, as Rabi Ila states, *bifiresh ribtah Torah eidim zomemim l'tashlumin* — the Torah explicitly includes *edim zomemin* in the category of monetary payment — and the same principle applies to *chovel b'chaveiro*: *bifiresh ribtah Torah chovel b'chaveiro l'tashlumin* — the Torah explicitly mandates monetary payment in cases of personal injury, making it an explicit exception to the general rule.

## Edim Zomemin Source

### False Witness Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵימָא: כׇּל הַלּוֹקֶה אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם! אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: בְּפֵירוּשׁ רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין לְתַשְׁלוּמִין. הֵיכָן רִיבְּתָה תּוֹרָה? מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״וַעֲשִׂיתֶם לוֹ כַּאֲשֶׁר זָמַם לַעֲשׂוֹת לְאָחִיו״, ״יָד בְּיָד״ לְמָה לִי? דָּבָר הַנִּיתָּן מִיָּד לְיָד, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to that mishna, the Gemara asks: **Let us say,** on the contrary, that **anyone who is flogged does not pay. Rabbi Ile'a said: The Torah explicitly amplified** the case of **conspiring witnesses for payment,** not lashes. The Gemara asks: **Where did the Torah amplify** the case of conspiring witnesses? The Gemara explains: **Now, since it states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: **"And you shall do unto him as he conspired to do unto his brother"** (Deuteronomy 19:19); **why do I** require the Torah to state in his punishment: **"A hand for a hand"** (Deuteronomy 19:21)? This indicates that the punishment that takes precedence is one in which there is **an item that is given from hand to hand, and what is that** item? It is **money.**

The *gemara* now examines where exactly the Torah makes this explicit statement regarding *edim zomemin*. The relevant verse reads: *ve-asitem lo ka'asher zamam la'asot le'achiv* — do to him as he plotted to do to his fellow. This already establishes the principle of *ka'asher zamam*. Why then does the verse continue with the phrase *yad beyad* — hand for hand?

The case of *edim zomemin* involves a range of possible sanctions, since the witnesses may have sought to impose upon the accused any manner of punishment — death, lashes, or monetary liability. The continuation of the verse, *yad beyad regel beregel ayin be'ayin*, might be read as a list of retaliatory penalties to be imposed literally. Rabi Meir indeed holds that false witnesses who sought to have the accused executed receive lashes (for the prohibition of *eidut shaker*) *and* pay monetary compensation as separate sanctions arising from distinct legal categories, since the prohibition of false testimony and the sanction of *ka'asher zamam* are legally independent of one another: one can transgress the prohibition of *lo sa'aneh* without being subject to the sanction of *ka'asher zamam* (e.g., if the procedural requirements for *hazamah* are not met), and the *ka'asher zamam* penalty is not a punishment for the personal transgression but a broader institutional mechanism for protecting the integrity of judicial proceedings. The rabbis, however — the position the *gemara* adopts — hold that one pays and does not receive lashes.

### Hand for Hand

The textual basis for the rabbinic position is derived from the phrase *yad beyad* itself. The *gemara* asks: given that the verse has already established the principle of *ka'asher zamam*, what additional meaning does *yad beyad* contribute? The answer: *davar hanitan miyad leyad* — something transferred from hand to hand, evoking the image of handing over money. The Torah thereby signals explicitly that the sanction of *edim zomemin* in monetary cases takes the form of monetary payment. And since the Torah has explicitly categorized this as a monetary obligation, and one cannot impose both lashes and monetary payment, the monetary obligation prevails and lashes are not administered.

## Chovel Source

### Injury Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ נָמֵי, מִכְּדֵי כְּתִיב: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״, ״כֵּן יִנָּתֵן בּוֹ״, לְמָה לִי? דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ נְתִינָה, וּמַאי נִיהוּ — מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The same can be said with regard to **one who injures another. Now, since it states:** "And a man who places a blemish upon his counterpart, **as he has done so shall be done to him"** (Leviticus 24:19), **why do I** require the Torah to state: "As one who places a blemish upon a man, **so shall be placed [*yinnaten*] upon him"** (Leviticus 24:20)? This teaches that this is referring to **an item that involves giving [*netina*], and what is that** item? It is **money.**

Having established the textual basis for *edim zomemin*'s exception, the *gemara* turns to *chovel b'chaveiro*. The relevant verse reads: *ka'asher asa ken ye'aseh lo* — as he has done, so shall be done to him. This appears to mandate literal retaliation for physical injury. Why, then, does the verse then add a further phrase?

### Yinaten Means Money

The verse continues: *ken yinaten bo* — so shall it be *given* in him. The *gemara* asks: given that the verse already stated *ke'asher asa ken ye'aseh lo*, why add *ken yinaten bo*? The answer derives from the word *yinaten* — *given*: *davar sheyesh bo netina*, u-*mai nihu mamon* — something that is given over, and what is that? Money. The word *yinaten*, evoking the act of handing something over, signals that the compensation for physical injury takes the form of monetary payment. This is the explicit textual basis — identified through *chazal*'s reading — for the exception of *chovel b'chaveiro*: the Torah itself has explicitly designated this as a case of monetary obligation, thereby establishing it as an exception to the general rule that lashes take precedence.

## Defending Lashes

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כְּעוּלָּא? אִם כֵּן, בִּטַּלְתָּ ״עֶרְוַת אֲחוֹתְךָ... לֹא תְגַלֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan, what is the reason** that **he did not say** the same *halakha* **as Ulla,** that where there is liability to both pay money and receive lashes, one pays money but is not flogged? The Gemara answers: **If so,** if that were the case, **you have rendered moot** the prohibition **"The nakedness of your sister…you shall not uncover"** (Leviticus 18:9) in that contrary to the standard prohibitions, no lashes would be administered for its violation.

With Rabbi Yochanan's general principle and its two explicit exceptions now established, the *gemara* presses a different challenge. Rabbi Yochanan holds that in the case of rape combined with incest, lashes take precedence — to pay money rather than administer lashes would, in his view, undermine the weight of the prohibition of incest, *bataltah ervat achoto lo tigaleh*. If money replaces lashes, the prohibition loses its deterrent force and its appropriate gravity as a sanction.

But this reasoning creates a difficulty with the very exceptions Rabbi Yochanan acknowledges. If paying money rather than lashing undermines the weight of a prohibition, why does he accept the exceptions of *chovel b'chaveiro* and *edim zomemin*? Does permitting monetary payment in those cases not equally undermine the weight of those prohibitions?

What lies beneath this exchange is significant. The technical disagreements between Ulla and Rabbi Yochanan about how to read certain verses are not occurring in a vacuum. Rabbi Yochanan's preference for lashes reflects a substantive jurisprudential commitment: serious sins require serious punishment, and monetary payment — whatever its practical benefits — is not an adequate substitute for the punitive weight of lashes when a genuine prohibition has been violated. This underlying philosophy shapes how he reads the relevant verses.

### Ben Gerusha Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחוֹתוֹ נָמֵי, אִיכָּא לְקַיּוֹמַהּ בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ בּוֹגֶרֶת! אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַאי ״תַּחַת אֲשֶׁר עִינָּהּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְאַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** (From Ketubot 33a) The Gemara raises a difficulty: If so, with regard to **one's sister as well,** the lashes **can be fulfilled** in the case of one who had forced relations **with his sister** who is **a grown woman.** Since there is no fine in that case, he is flogged and the verse is not moot. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said to you** that he does not agree with Ulla because **this** verse: **"Because he tormented her"** (Deuteronomy 22:29), is not available for a verbal analogy, as **he requires it to** derive **in accordance with that** which **Abaye** said.

Rabbi Yochanan responds to the challenge regarding *edim zomemin* as follows: *efshar lekiyumah b'ven gerusha u'ven chalutza* — it is possible to fulfill the prohibition's lashes-based sanction in the case of a *ben gerusha* or a *ben chalutza*. If the false witnesses testified that a certain *kohen* was a *ben gerusha* or *ben chalutza* — that is, born of a union that disqualifies priestly lineage — the sanction of *ka'asher zamam* cannot be applied in the normal way, because one cannot impose a status-based disqualification on witnesses who are themselves not *kohanim* or who already bear that disqualification. When *ka'asher zamam* is inapplicable, no monetary payment is due, and the witnesses instead receive lashes for the prohibition of bearing false witness. Thus the prohibition of *eidut sheker* remains one for which lashes can and do apply in certain configurations; the fact that monetary payment takes over in other configurations does not strip the prohibition of its lashes-based dimension. The underlying force of the prohibition is not undermined.

### Perutah Edge Case

The edge case of *haka'ah she'ein bah shaveh perutah* — a wound of less than a *perutah*'s worth — demonstrates that the prohibition against striking another person retains its lashes-based sanction. In the rare theoretical scenario where no monetary obligation whatsoever arises from the act, lashes are administered. One can therefore say that the prohibition is not undermined: it remains, at least in principle and in rare practice, a prohibition that carries the sanction of lashes. The fact that in most cases monetary payment takes over does not negate the underlying lashes-based character of the prohibition.

### Incest Lashes

Rabbi Yochanan now turns the argument back in his own favor. If the logic for maintaining lashes as the appropriate sanction for those two categories is that there exist configurations in which lashes do apply — and this is considered sufficient to preserve the weight of the prohibition — then the same reasoning actually *strengthens* his position regarding incest. In the case of incest, the *majority* of cases will result in lashes: whenever the act is consensual, or whenever the woman is a *bogeret*, there is no monetary obligation and lashes are administered. The configuration where both rape and incest coincide — the scenario Ulla would resolve in favor of monetary payment — is a minority of cases. The prohibition of incest is overwhelmingly a lashes-based prohibition. To say that in this particular configuration money should replace lashes undermines a prohibition that in most of its applications carries lashes. Rabbi Yochanan therefore insists that even in the rape-and-incest case, lashes must prevail.

## Conclusion

### Tachat Already Used

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוּלָּא? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִדְּרָבָא. דְּאָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָתַן הָאִישׁ הַשּׁוֹכֵב עִמָּהּ לַאֲבִי הַנַּעֲרָה חֲמִשִּׁים כָּסֶף״, הֲנָאַת שְׁכִיבָה חֲמִשִּׁים, מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם.
>
> **Translation:** (From Ketubot 33a) The Gemara asks: **And Ulla,** who uses this verse to derive a verbal analogy, from where does he derive the *halakha* stated by Abaye? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the statement of **Rava,** who derives that *halakha* from the same verse, **as Rava said** that **the verse states: "And the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the young woman fifty shekels of silver"** (Deuteronomy 22:29). The superfluous phrase "who lay with her" teaches that it is for **the pleasure of lying** with her that he pays **fifty** shekels, i.e., fifty *sela*; **by inference** one may conclude **that** beyond the fine, **there is** compensation for **humiliation and degradation.**

The *gemara* concludes by addressing why Rabbi Yochanan does not adopt Ulla's *gezeirah shavah* from *tachat* to *tachat*. Rabbi Yochanan would say: *hai tachat asher innah* — the word *tachat* in the rape verse — *mibaya lei lekhed'Abaye*, I need that word to teach something else entirely, as Abaye derives from it a different *halacha* altogether.

### Ulla's Deduction

Abaye derives from the phrase *tachat asher innah* that the fifty *shekel* fine paid by the rapist constitutes payment specifically for the act of sexual violation itself — and that this very specification implies, *michlal de'ika boshet u-pegam*, that there are additional payments owed beyond the fifty *shekel*, namely *boshet* and *pegam*, which remain as independent obligations. Since Rabbi Yochanan uses the word *tachat* for this derivation, he cannot simultaneously use it for Ulla's *gezeirah shavah*. Ulla, for his part, derives the same teaching about additional payments from a different verse, attributed to Rava: *venatan ha'ish hashochev imah la'avi ha-na'arah chamishim kasef* — the man who lay with her shall give fifty silver pieces to the father of the young woman. The phrase *hanat shechiva chamishim* — the fifty is for the pleasure of the act itself — indicates by implication that *boshet* and *pegam* are paid separately. With this derivation available to him, Ulla does not need *tachat asher innah* for that purpose and is therefore free to use it for the *gezeirah shavah*.

### Bottom Line

The final picture is this. The general rule, as Rabbi Yochanan establishes from *kedei rishato*, is that when one must choose between lashes and monetary payment, lashes take precedence. The court administers lashes and does not collect monetary payment. There are two explicit exceptions — *chovel b'chaveiro* and *edim zomemin* — where the Torah itself mandates monetary payment through textual signals (*yinaten* and *yad beyad* respectively), making these deliberate exceptions to the general rule. In the case of rape combined with incest, Rabbi Yochanan holds that the general rule applies: lashes are administered and monetary payment is not collected. Ulla disagrees, holding — through a *gezeirah shavah* of *tachat* to *tachat* — that in the specific case of rape, as in *chovel b'chaveiro*, the monetary obligation takes precedence. On Tosafot's reading, this is Ulla's limited claim: not a universal principle, but a rule confined to the rape case alone, derived through the verbal analogy and justified by the same logic that protects the victim's interests in cases of personal injury.
