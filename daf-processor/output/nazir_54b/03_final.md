# Nazir 54b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishna Review

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֲבָל הַסְּכָכוֹת, וְהַפְּרָעוֹת, וּבֵית הַפְּרָס, וְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים, וְהַגּוֹלֵל, וְהַדּוֹפֵק, וּרְבִיעִית דָּם, וְאֹהֶל, וְרוֹבַע עֲצָמוֹת, וְכֵלִים הַנּוֹגְעִים בְּמֵת, וּבִימֵי סְפָרוֹ, וּבִימֵי גְמָרוֹ — עַל אֵלּוּ אֵין הַנָּזִיר מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The previous mishna listed the sources of ritual impurity for which a nazirite must shave. This mishna adds: **However,** the nazirite does not shave for these: **The hanging** branches over a corpse, i.e., a tree overhanging a body that a nazirite passes, but it is uncertain which branches are over a corpse; **and the projecting** stones from fences when the place of the impurity is unknown; **and a *beit haperas*,** a place that contained a grave and was plowed. In the latter case, the entire area around the grave is impure from a corpse due to an uncertainty, as it might contain human bones. **And the land of the nations,** i.e., a nazirite left Eretz Yisrael for another land. The Sages decreed that all land outside of Eretz Yisrael is ritually impure. **And the grave cover; and the grave walls** upon which the cover rests; **and a quarter-***log* **of blood** from a corpse; **and a tent; and a quarter-***kav* **of bones** of a corpse; **and vessels that are touching a corpse. And** if the nazirite is **in the days of his counting,** i.e., the seven days a leper must count after purification from his leprosy; **or in his days of full** leprosy, when he is a full-fledged leper, **for these the nazirite does not shave.** This is the case even if he is rendered impure by one of the sources listed in the previous mishna.

We begin on 54b, five lines down in the *Gemara*. The *Mishnah* presented a list of categories of *tumah* for which a *Nazir* is not *megale'ach* — does not shave his head, does not lose the earlier days of his *nezirus*, and does not bring a *korban*. Some of these exclusions apply because the *tumah* in question is only *d'rabbanan*; others apply even to *tumah* that is nominally *d'oraisa*, such as *tumas meis*, but that nevertheless fall outside the category that triggers *nazir megale'ach*. An important parallel principle, found not in our *Gemara* but in *Masechet Smachot*, states that whatever does not cause a *Nazir* to shave also does not fall within the prohibitions incumbent upon a *Kohen* — a point of great significance to which we will return.

### Categories Overview

The *Mishnah*'s list of exclusions from *nazir megale'ach* divides into two broad categories. The first consists of cases where there is no doubt that *tumah* is present, but the mechanism by which it is transmitted is only *d'rabbanan*. The second consists of cases where the *tumah* itself may be *d'oraisa* in origin but is nonetheless excluded from the operative category. The upcoming discussion focuses primarily on the first type: cases where a genuine dead body may well be present, but the manner of transmission does not rise to the biblical standard.

### Trees & Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַזֶּה בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי וּבַשְּׁבִיעִי,
>
> **Translation:** **And** in those cases listed that involve ritual impurity from a corpse, **one sprinkles** the purification water upon him **on the third and on the seventh** days of his purification,

Two examples of rabbinically-based *tumah* appear in the *Mishnah*: overhanging trees and outcropping walls. In the case of the trees — most plausibly trees growing over a cemetery — *Tosafot* explains that while there is indeed a corpse buried underneath, the branches and leaves in any given span do not constitute a *tefach* by *tefach* surface and therefore cannot form a *d'oraisa ohel*. In the case of the outcropping stone walls, the scenario involves something buried on the outside of the cemetery wall — sometimes fetuses, buried there due to uncertainty about their status. Because the burial is uncertain, the *tumah* is a *safek tumah b'reshut harabbim*, and the person remains biblically *tahor*. Both cases thus represent categories where the *Nazir* does not shave, and where — by the parallel principle of *Masechet Smachot* — the *Kohen* is not formally prohibited.

## Beis Hapras

### Intro

We now take up a new category in the *Mishnah*'s list: *beis hapras* and *eretz ha'amim*. *Beis hapras* refers to a field through which a grave was accidentally plowed, scattering fragments of bone throughout. Biblically, there would ordinarily be no sufficient basis for *tumah* without actual contact with the bone itself, but rabbinically one may not walk through such a field. The *Gemara* gives this only brief treatment here; it is *eretz ha'amim* — the lands outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, which are deemed *tamei* — that will occupy the bulk of our discussion.

### Air vs. Ground

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְאֵלּוּ הֵן הַסְּכָכוֹת — אִילָן הַמֵּיסֵךְ עַל הָאָרֶץ. וּפְרָעוֹת — הַיּוֹצְאוֹת מִן הַגָּדֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains the terms hangings and projections by citing a mishna (*Oholot* 8:2). **And these are the hangings: A tree that overhangs the earth. And** the **projections** are stones **that protrude from a fence.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים, מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא גָּזְרוּ עָלֶיהָ, אוֹ דִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָּׁא גָּזְרוּ עָלֶיהָ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna further taught: **And the land of the nations** does not render a nazirite obligated to shave, despite the fact that it is considered ritually impure. The Gemara inquires with regard to the nature of this impurity. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Did the Sages **decree the land of the nations** impure **with regard to the air,** i.e., is one rendered impure merely by being there? **Or perhaps they decreed** it impure **with regard to the earth,** i.e., one who touches the ground or overlays it becomes impure.

The *Gemara* poses a pointed question about the nature of *tumas eretz ha'amim*: *eretz ha'amim ibaya lehu* — they raise the question: *mishum avira gazru aleha* — was the decree directed at the airspace itself? Or *mishum gusha gazru aleha* — was it directed at the ground?

To understand the stakes: if the concern is the ground (*gusha*), then the *tumah* operates within the standard framework of *tumas meis* and *ohel*. Just as one who stands over a corpse is *tamei* by virtue of being in the same *ohel*, so too one who walks or stands over the ground of *eretz ha'amim* would be *tamei* if we assume the ground harbors the dead. Critically, however, if one were inside a structure or on an elevated platform that effectively forms an *ohel* between oneself and the ground, one would be protected — the floor below serving as an *ohel* separating the person from whatever lies beneath.

### Avira & Gusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וּמַזֶּה בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי וּבַשְּׁבִיעִי. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא, הַזָּאָה לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לָאו, מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָּׁא? לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא, וְכִי קָתָנֵי — אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a resolution to this question from the mishna, which lists one who enters the land of the nations and states: **And one sprinkles** on him **on the third and on the seventh** days. **And if you say** that the decree was **with regard to the air, why do I** need this **sprinkling?** He neither touched something ritually impure nor overlaid it. **Rather, is it not with regard to the earth** that the land of the nations was decreed impure? The Gemara rejects this proof: **No, actually I** could **say to you** that the decree is **due to the air, and when** the mishna **teaches** that he receives the sprinkling, it is referring **to the other** items listed in the mishna, e.g., the grave cover, the grave walls, and a quarter-*log* of blood.

*Avira*, by contrast, means that even this protection is unavailing. Even if one is entirely shielded from what is beneath — sealed above an *ohel*, standing on the second floor of a building — one remains *tamei* by virtue of being within the airspace of *eretz ha'amim*. There are, after all, no dead bodies floating in the air; there is no physical rationale for *tumas avira*. This observation itself points toward something important about the nature of this decree: it cannot be explained as a straightforward extension of ordinary *tumas meis* and *ohel*. It must be motivated by something else.

## Tosafot Airspace

### Transport Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי: וְכֵלִים הַנּוֹגְעִים בְּמֵת. הָנֵי כֵּלִים בְּנֵי הַזָּאָה אִינּוּן? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַשְּׁאָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **So too, it is reasonable** that this is the case, **from** the fact **that** the mishna also **teaches: And vessels that are touching a corpse.** Now do people who touch **these vessels require sprinkling?** Does one who touches a vessel that is ritually impure from a corpse require the sprinkling of purification water? This individual is impure only until that evening. **Rather, learn from this** that the *halakha* that he is sprinkled on the third and on the seventh days does not refer to all the cases in the mishna but only **to** some of **the others.**

*Tosafot* introduces precisely the cases the *Gemara* will shortly bring: *k'gon mahalach b'shidah tevah u'migdal* — one who is transported through *eretz ha'amim* inside a large box, wagon, or tower. The box must be larger than forty *se'ah* — the size of a *mikveh* — because below that volume it qualifies as a *kli*, a vessel, and as a vessel it could not serve as an *ohel* and would itself be susceptible to *tumah*. Assume instead a massive box, like the crate a large appliance comes in, with an open top. If you are standing inside it, elevated on a platform within it, then the floor of the box constitutes an *ohel* between you and the ground of *eretz ha'amim*. You should, by normal *ohel* logic, be protected. Similarly, if you travel through *eretz ha'amim* by wagon or boat, the structure of the vessel stands between you and the ground.

### Stricter Rules

The key discussion of *Tosafot* on the stringencies applied to *eretz ha'amim* is found in the parallels drawn between different frameworks. *Tosafot* states: *she'hechmiru vo yoter mib'ohel hameis* — the Sages were more strict regarding the airspace of *eretz ha'amim* than the ordinary rules of *ohel* and corpse-*tumah* would require. Even though one is above an *ohel* that would normally be fully protective, the mere fact of being within the airspace renders one *tamei*. This is a stringency that has no parallel in standard *halachot* of *tumah*.

### Policy Rationale

The key phrase in *Tosafot* is: *shelo yeitzei me'eretz l'chutz la'aretz* — so that people should not leave *Eretz Yisrael* for *chutz la'aretz*. This is a remarkable statement. The *tumah* of *eretz ha'amim* is not grounded in a practical concern about contact with the dead. It is a policy instrument — an application of *halacha* as a form of social legislation designed to discourage emigration from *Eretz Yisrael*. The immediate practical effect falls most heavily on *Kohanim*, for whom *tumah* carries formal prohibitions, but the designation of the outside world as *eretz tmei'ah* presumably carries a broader cultural weight as well, reinforcing the attitude that *chutz la'aretz* is categorically inferior — a place one should not wish to inhabit.

There is already a separate prohibition, *assur latzeit me'eretz Yisrael l'chutz la'aretz*, applicable to all Jews. One might ask: is that not sufficient? Perhaps. But *Kohanim*, it seems, were especially responsive to the framework of *tumah v'taharah*, and the designation of *eretz ha'amim* as *tamei* would have had particular force for them. Beyond the formal prohibition, the very label of *eretz tmei'ah* shapes how one relates to the outside world. This stands in interesting tension with the recognition — which we noted based on a student's observation — that *Eretz Yisrael* itself has, through the millennia of invasion and massacre, become saturated with the dead. Every building project today in Israel uncovers old graves. That irony only reinforces the conclusion that the fundamental motivation of *tumas eretz ha'amim* must be this *kedei shelo yeitzu* rationale, whatever form it ultimately takes in *halachic* application.

## Gusha Alternative

### Dead Bodies

*Tosafot* also considers the *gusha* position: that the decree operates only through the ground and the normal laws of *ohel*, without the additional stringency of *avira*. On this reading, one would be *tamei* by walking over or being within a normal *ohel* relationship with the ground of *eretz ha'amim*, but protected if one stands above an *ohel* that intervenes. The question is what bodies would ground this *gusha*-based *tumah*. *Tosafot* offers two answers: *mipnei metei mabul* — because of the dead of the Flood, who, according to certain *gemarot*, were swept into *Bavel* (identified with *Shin'ar*) and are buried beneath its ground; and *mipnei rov Yisrael shenehergu b'chutz la'aretz* — because the majority of Jews who were killed throughout history died outside of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Practical Issues

Both answers face serious difficulties. The *mabul* dead were not Jewish, and the standard ruling is that only Jewish corpses convey *tumas ohel*. One might argue that the first answer assumes we rule that non-Jews also convey *tumas ohel* — but that is far from settled. Moreover, *Bavel* is not all of *chutz la'aretz*. As for the second answer: even granting the tragic historical claim that many Jews perished outside of *Eretz Yisrael*, the major mass deaths of the *Beis HaMikdash* period occurred predominantly within *Eretz Yisrael* itself. There is no clear empirical basis for assuming that Jewish dead are uniformly distributed throughout *chutz la'aretz* in quantities sufficient to ground a *gusha*-based *tumah* with the force of *tumas ohel*. Rightly understood, even the *gusha* account does not rest on solid physical reasoning — it cannot be a straightforward application of ordinary *tumah* law.

### Policy Underlying

This is why *Tosafot*'s policy rationale — *kedei shelo yeitzu* — is likely the underlying motivating force regardless of which account one adopts. The question between *avira* and *gusha* is then not whether the decree is policy-driven, but rather how far the Sages extended it: did they apply ordinary *tumas ohel* logic with the ground as the source (*gusha*), or did they go further and declare the very airspace *tamei* even when one is otherwise protected by an *ohel* (*avira*)? Both represent departures from purely physical *tumah* reasoning; *avira* simply departs further.

## Nazir-Kohen Link

### Principle

At this point the broader principle connecting *nazir megale'ach* and *kohen muzhar* comes into focus. The principle, as formulated in *Masechet Makkos* and echoed here, is: *kol tumah she'ein ha'nazir megale'ach aleha, ein ha'kohen muzhar aleha* — any *tumah* that does not cause a *Nazir* to shave does not trigger the formal biblical prohibition for a *Kohen*. This linkage is structurally important: it means that the entire question of what constitutes operationally significant *tumah* — the *tumah* that the Torah treats as the most serious kind — is defined by the *Nazir*'s shaving requirement.

### Application

The application to *eretz ha'amim* and *beis hapras* follows directly. Because these are *d'rabbanan* forms of *tumah* that do not cause a *Nazir* to shave, a *Kohen* entering *eretz ha'amim* or *beis hapras* does not incur *malkus d'oraisa* and is not in violation of a biblical prohibition. There remain *issurei d'rabbanan* — the rabbinic prohibitions are real — but the *Kohen* is not *muzhar* in the full biblical sense, does not receive lashes, and is not subject to a *d'oraisa issur*. This distinction between the biblical and rabbinic levels of prohibition will prove critical in the discussions that follow.

## Gemara Resolution

### Sprinkling Proof

The *Gemara* now attempts to resolve the *avira*-versus-*gusha* question from a ruling found in the *Mishnah* itself: *u'mazei bashlishi uvashvi'i* — one who entered *eretz ha'amim* must be sprinkled with the purification waters of the *parah adumah* on days three and seven of the purification process. The *Gemara* reasons: *v'ei amart mishum avira* — if the *tumah* is based purely on the airspace, then this is a novel, free-standing form of *tumah* with no basis in the framework of *tumas meis*. If it is not grounded in *tumas meis*, there is no logical basis for applying the *haza'ah* on *shlishi v'shvii* — the sprinkling cycle is inherently part of the *tumas meis* purification process. On the *avira* account, the Sages invented a new *tumah*; they cannot then borrow the ritual mechanics of an entirely different *tumah* to define its purification.

### Conclusion

The *Gemara* initially appears to conclude that the sprinkling requirement points to *gusha* — the *tumah* operates within the *tumas meis* framework, and therefore the standard purification ritual applies. But the *Gemara* immediately rejects this inference: *le'olam eima lach mishum avira* — one can still say it is *avira*-based. The sprinkling mentioned in the *Mishnah* does not apply to every item on the list. The *Mishnah* mentions *shlishi v'shvii* with respect to some categories, such as *sechachos*, *pera'os*, and *beis hapras*, which are extensions of *tumas meis* and therefore appropriately purified through *haza'ah*. But other items on the list — *metzora* being the clearest example, since the *parah adumah* sprinkling is obviously irrelevant to *tzara'as* — are not subject to *shlishi v'shvii*. On the *avira* reading, *eretz ha'amim* falls into the second category: it is a distinct *tumah* to which *shlishi v'shvii* does not apply.

The *Gemara* then adds that this conclusion is supported by another consideration: *mideka tanni v'keilim hanog'im b'meis* — the *Mishnah* also includes vessels that touched a dead body among the categories for which a *Nazir* does not shave. *Hani keilim benei haza'ah nin?* — Are such vessels subject to sprinkling on days three and seven? Clearly not, in the *Gemara*'s assumption. This demonstrates that the *Mishnah*'s mention of *shlishi v'shvii* cannot be read as applying to every item enumerated. Therefore, *eretz ha'amim*'s exemption from *shlishi v'shvii* is consistent with the *avira* interpretation and does not refute it.

## Cherev Ke'Chalal

### Vessels Assumption

This raises a significant question. The *Gemara* assumes without explanation that *keilim* that touched a dead body would not require *shlishi v'shvii* sprinkling. Let us examine why. Under ordinary *halachot* of *tumah*, a vessel that touches a *meis* becomes an *av hatumah*. A person who then touches that vessel becomes a *rishon l'tumah* — a first-level derivative — and is *tamei* for only one day. The one-day *tumah* has no connection to the seven-day purification process and *shlishi v'shvii*; those apply only to *tumas shivah*, the full seven-day *tumah* associated with direct contact with a corpse or its equivalent. So if we are talking about ordinary vessels, the logic is clear: no *shlishi v'shvii*. But then a different question arises: if it is such a routine form of *tumah*, why does the *Mishnah* bother mentioning it at all?

### Tosafot Question

This is precisely *Tosafot*'s question. *Tosafot* asks: *v'im tomar* — why not read the *Mishnah* as referring to metal vessels under the principle of *cherev harei hu k'chalal* — a sword is like a corpse? Under *cherev k'chalal*, a metal vessel that was in contact with a corpse is treated as if it were the corpse itself — an *av hatumah* in the fullest sense. Anyone who touches it becomes *tamei* with a seven-day *tumah* and requires *shlishi v'shvii* sprinkling. If that is the *Mishnah*'s intent, then two things follow: first, it is a genuine *chiddush* worthy of mention, and second, the person *would* require *shlishi v'shvii*, so the *Mishnah*'s reference to sprinkling would apply here too, undercutting the *Gemara*'s proof.

Why, then, does the *Gemara* assume the *Mishnah* is not talking about metal vessels under *cherev k'chalal*?

### Rabbeinu Tam

*Rabbeinu Tam*'s answer is direct: *de'al karchach lo miktamah masnisin b'klei matechos* — the *Mishnah* cannot be speaking of metal vessels under *cherev k'chalal*, because *de'im kein hanazir megale'ach, d'cherev harei hu k'chalal* — if it were, the *Nazir* would be *megale'ach*. If the vessel is treated like the corpse itself and the *Nazir* who touches it is thereby *tamei tumas shivah*, there is no known *Gemara* that exempts such a *Nazir* from shaving. *Rabbeinu Tam*'s position is that *cherev k'chalal* would indeed trigger *nazir megale'ach*, and therefore the *Mishnah*, which teaches that the *Nazir* does *not* shave for *keilim hanog'im b'meis*, cannot be discussing *cherev k'chalal* at all. The *Mishnah* must be speaking of ordinary vessels under ordinary *tumah* rules.

## Rabbeinu Chaim

### House Metaphor

*Tosafot* then records the famous exchange between *Rabbeinu Tam* and *Rabbeinu Chaim HaKohen*. *Rabbeinu Chaim* sent *Rabbeinu Tam* a letter, opening with the verse *aizeh bayis asher tivnu li, v'aizeh makom menuchati* — "What house can you build for Me, and what place shall be My resting place?" — invoking the divine rhetorical question to pose one of his own: he is a *Kohen*, and *Rabbeinu Tam* is telling him that any metal vessel that was ever in a house with a *meis* is now itself like the *meis* — and the *Kohen* is biblically prohibited from being in the same space as it. *Aizeh bayis asher tivnu li* — what house can you build for me in which I could ever live?

### Metal Problem

The argument is a *reductio ad absurdum*. *Rabbeinu Chaim* invokes the principle *kol tumah she'hanazir megale'ach aleha kohen muzhar aleha* — everything that requires *nazir megale'ach* also triggers the *Kohen*'s prohibition. He observes: *v'ein lecha bayis she'ein bo shum klei machos she'hayah b'ohel hameis o shum masmer* — there is no house that does not contain some metal vessel or nail that was at some point under the same *ohel* as a dead body. In the Middle Ages nails were not manufactured fresh from a factory; they were reused from house to house, passed through markets, carried across generations. Any such nail, if *cherev k'chalal* applied in full force, would render the *Kohen* biblically prohibited from entering any ordinary home. The implication is patently impossible as a practical matter.

### Conclusion

*Rabbeinu Chaim HaKohen* therefore concludes that even metal vessels — even when *cherev k'chalal* applies and the person who touches them is *tamei tumas shivah* — do not cause a *Nazir* to shave and do not trigger the formal biblical prohibition for a *Kohen*. This is what the *Mishnah* is teaching when it lists *keilim hanog'im b'meis* among the cases where a *Nazir* does not shave: the *chiddush* is specifically about metal vessels under *cherev k'chalal*, and the ruling is that even there, the *Nazir* is not *megale'ach* and the *Kohen* is not *muzhar*. This position represents the accepted *halacha*.

## Rambam Position

### All Vessels

The *Rambam* takes the discussion in a striking direction. Not only does he affirm *cherev k'chalal*, but he extends its scope beyond metal vessels. In *Hilchos Tumas Meis* 5:3, he writes: *keilim she'nitme'u b'meis bein b'maga bein b'ohel, harei hanogei'a bahen k'nogei'a b'meis atzmo* — vessels that became *tamei* through a corpse, whether through direct contact or through *ohel*, render one who touches them as if one had touched the corpse itself. Just as a corpse conveys seven-day *tumah* whether to a person or to vessels, so too do vessels that became *tamei* through the corpse — whoever or whatever subsequently touches them is *tamei tumas shivah*.

### Derivation

And the *Rambam* derives this not only from tradition (*mi'pi hashmu'ah*) but from a novel reading of the verse. He asks: *v'chi ya'aleh al da'atcha* — would it enter your mind that someone who killed another person would be *tamei* for seven days even if he had no physical contact with the body? Surely the verse *kol horeg nefesh* cannot mean one who shot an arrow from a distance. It must refer to killing with a sword — *horeg nefesh b'cherev* — in which case the verse teaches that the sword itself becomes like the corpse, and its bearer is *tamei tumas shivah* through the contact with the sword. From there the *Rambam* derives that all vessels that touched a *meis* — *bein klei matechos, bein klei shetef uvgadim*, whether metal, cloth, or other materials — share this status.

### Three Positions

The discussion thus yields three distinct positions. The first restricts *cherev k'chalal* to metal vessels (*klei matechos*) and holds that touching them creates *tumas shivah*; the question then is what follows for a *Nazir* and a *Kohen*. The second position — *Rabbeinu Tam*'s, which is rejected *l'halacha* — holds that if *cherev k'chalal* applies to metal vessels, the *Nazir* is *megale'ach* and the *Kohen* is *muzhar*; therefore the *Mishnah* cannot be speaking of metal at all. The third position — *Rabbeinu Chaim HaKohen*'s, representing the accepted ruling — holds that *cherev k'chalal* does apply to metal vessels and does create *tumas shivah*, but the *Nazir* does not shave and the *Kohen* is not *muzhar* even for metal. And the *Rambam* goes furthest of all: *cherev k'chalal* applies to all vessels regardless of material, but here too the *Nazir* does not shave. The *Rambam*'s additional ruling — that even the obligation of *shlishi v'shvii* sprinkling does not apply to a *Nazir* in this case, despite the seven-day *tumah* — is, frankly, a striking *halacha* that demands further attention of its own.

The common thread across all positions that have halachic standing is the impossibility of the alternative. If touching any vessel or any metal vessel ever in contact with a *meis* were to impose biblical prohibitions on *Kohanim* with the full force of *tumas meis*, no *Kohen* could function in ordinary life. The *reductio ad absurdum* of *Rabbeinu Chaim HaKohen* — what house can you build for me? — captures the underlying principle: *halacha* cannot be read in a way that makes normative life for a *Kohen* simply impossible.

## Tannaitic Debate

### Box Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי: הַנִּכְנָס לְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל, רַבִּי מְטַמֵּא, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מְטַהֵר. מַאי לָאו: רַבִּי סָבַר מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָּׁא?
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say** that this is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught: With regard to **one who enters the land of the nations** not on foot but **in a chest, a box, or a cabinet, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **deems** him ritually **impure. And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, deems** him **pure. What, is it not** correct to say that they disagree in this regard: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who deems him impure, **holds** that the Sages decreed impurity **with regard to the air, and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, holds** that the Sages decreed impurity **with regard to the earth,** and consequently he is not impure, as the container prevents him from overlying the impurity?

The *Gemara* now proposes to resolve the underlying *avira*-versus-*gusha* question by reference to a *tannaitic* dispute: *lema katannai* — let us say this is a debate among *Tanna'im*. The case is: *haniknas l'eretz ha'amim b'shidah tevah u'migdal* — one who enters *eretz ha'amim* while enclosed in a large box, crate, or tower. *Rabbi* (Judah HaNasi) says he is *tamei*; *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* says he is *tahor*. On its face, this looks like an exact parallel to the *avira*-versus-*gusha* question. The box, as we have noted, would constitute an *ohel* between the person and the ground. If one holds *gusha*, the person should be protected and *tahor*. If one holds *avira*, the open-topped box leaves the person exposed to the airspace and *tamei*. *Rabbi*'s position that he is *tamei* would thus reflect *avira*, and *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda*'s position that he is *tahor* would reflect *gusha*.

### Initial Interpretation

This is the straightforward reading — *mai lav*, the *Gemara* asks, is *Rabbi* not holding *mishum avira*, and *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* holding *mishum gusha*? If so, we have our resolution: the *tannaitic* debate maps directly onto the *avira*-versus-*gusha* question, and the debate remains unresolved at the *tannaitic* level. But the *Gemara* does not rest with this.

### Ohel Zaruk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשּׁוּם גּוּשָּׁא. מָר סָבַר: אֹהֶל זָרוּק — שְׁמֵיהּ אֹהֶל, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא שְׁמֵיהּ אֹהֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: This is **not** necessarily the correct interpretation of their dispute, as one can say **that everyone** agrees that the decree is **with regard to the earth,** and their dispute concerns only the case of one who enters in a chest, a box, or a cabinet. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **a moving tent,** an item that serves as a tent as it passes over ritual impurity, **is called a tent,** and therefore a person who enters the land of the nations in a large container is protected from its impurity. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **a moving tent is not called a tent.** Consequently, nothing separates this individual from the impurity, and he becomes impure by overlying the land of the nations.

*De'chulei alma mishum gusha* — the *Gemara* proposes that in fact both *Tanna'im* agree the issue is the ground, not the airspace. The disagreement is instead about whether the box, which is being transported, constitutes a valid *ohel*. A moving *ohel* — one that is being carried rather than standing stationary — is called an *ohel zaruk*, a "thrown" or "projected" *ohel*. The question is: *mar savar ohel zaruk shemei ohel u'mar savar lo shemei ohel* — one *Tanna* holds that a moving structure constitutes a valid *ohel* and therefore protects the person inside; the other holds it does not. On this reading, the dispute is not about the source of *tumas eretz ha'amim* at all, but about a general principle in *hilchos ohel* — one that has implications far beyond *eretz ha'amim*, including, as was noted, the question of aircraft today.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: תֵּיבָה שֶׁהִיא מְלֵאָה כֵּלִים, וּזְרָקָהּ עַל פְּנֵי הַמֵּת בְּאֹהֶל — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם הָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: A box that is full of utensils that one threw over a corpse in a tent,** in such a manner that it overlay the corpse, **is impure,** and everything inside it is also rendered ritually impure, as it does not provide the protection of a tent. **And if it was placed down** and positioned as a tent over a corpse, **it is pure,** and its contents are protected from the impurity. This shows that according to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, a moving tent is not considered a tent, which contradicts the above claim.

A *baraita* is then cited in which *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* himself teaches that if a box filled with *keilim* is thrown in front of a *meis* so that it flies over it as an *ohel*, the *keilim* inside are *tamei* — because the flying box does not constitute an *ohel*. If the same box were stationary, suspended on poles above the *meis*, the *keilim* inside would be *tahor*, because then it is a genuine *ohel*. This *baraita* appears to demonstrate that *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* himself holds *ohel zaruk lo shemei ohel* — and that would seemingly contradict his position that the person in the transported box in *eretz ha'amim* is *tahor*.

*Tosafot* resolves this by distinguishing between degrees of *zaruk*. A box that is literally thrown through the air — or a *talis* flapping in the wind with no support from the ground — is a classic *ohel zaruk* and is *lo shemei ohel* by everyone's agreement. A box being carried by bearers who stand on the ground is a more ambiguous case: it is moving, and thus not fully stationary, but it is anchored to the ground through the bodies of those carrying it. The debate between *Rabbi* and *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* may then concern precisely this intermediate case of a transported, but ground-anchored, box — not the extreme case of a box in free flight.

## Transport Baraita

### Status Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִשּׁוּם אַוֵּירָא, וּמָר סָבַר: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא — לָא גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן, וּמָר סָבַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא שְׁכִיחָא — גְּזַרוּ בֵּיהּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** In light of this argument, the Gemara concedes that the previous explanation of the dispute is incorrect. **Rather,** one must say **that everyone** agrees that the decree of impurity concerning the land of the nations is **with regard to its air, and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **since it is not common** for one to move around in an enclosure, **the Sages did not decree** impurity **with regard to** this case. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **holds** that **although it is not common the Sages** nevertheless **decreed** impurity **with regard to it.**

The *Gemara* then advances to a third possible framework for the debate, and before presenting the *baraita* that supports it, it is worth flagging a conceptual difficulty in the second framework (the *lo shechiach* account). The principle *davar lo shchiach lo gazru beih rabbanan* — that the Sages did not apply a rabbinic decree to unusual, rare cases — is a rule about prohibiting *actions*. When the Sages prohibit a category of conduct, they intend their prohibition for the normal range of cases; if a situation is so unusual that it was simply not contemplated, the prohibition may not apply. But applying this principle to the *status* of *tumas avira* is conceptually jarring. If the Sages declared the airspace of *eretz ha'amim* to be *tamei*, that is a categorical assignment of *tumah* to a space — it is not a prohibition of a particular action. To then say that one can be within that airspace through an unusual method and thereby escape the *tumah* is a strange application of the *lo shechiach* principle: it is not prohibiting a rare action, it is selectively withdrawing a status based on the rarity of the method of contact.

### Transport Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַתַּנְיָא: הַנִּכְנָס לְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל — טָהוֹר, בְּקָרוֹן וּבִסְפִינָה וּבְאִיסְקַרְיָא — טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And it is taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Oholot* 18:5) in accordance with this explanation of the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda: **One who enters the land of the nations in a chest, a box, or a cabinet** is **ritually pure.** If he was **in a wagon [*karon*], boat, or raft [*iskareya*],** he is **ritually impure.** The difference is that the latter vessels are commonly used to convey people.

The *baraita* reads: *haniknas l'eretz ha'amim b'shidah tevah u'migdal, tahor* — one who enters *eretz ha'amim* in a box or tower is *tahor*. *U'vakaron u'visfinah u'veis'karya, tamei* — but one who enters by wagon, boat, or *eis'karya* (apparently the mast or rigging of a ship) is *tamei*. The distinction the *baraita* draws between these two groups is the central question.

### Normalcy Test

One might initially think the difference relates to *ohel* status — perhaps a wagon or boat does not constitute an *ohel* while the box does. But this analysis does not hold. Both a box and a wagon can provide the same physical separation from the ground. Both can, in principle, be analyzed under the *ohel zaruk* framework. The only consistent difference between the two groups is the normalcy of the mode of transport. How does one normally enter *eretz ha'amim*? By wagon, by boat, by ship. These are the standard means of travel. Being carried through *eretz ha'amim* enclosed in a large box is by contrast an entirely unusual scenario. The *baraita*'s distinction, then, supports the interpretation that the *tumah* is *avira*-based, but was decreed only for normal modes of entry into the airspace — not for the unusual case of the box.

## Leaning Theory

### Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָכָא שֶׁמָּא יוֹצִיא רֹאשׁוֹ וְרוּבּוֹ לְשָׁם פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** an alternative explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda. Both agree that the ritual impurity of the land of the nations is with regard to the earth, and a moving tent is considered a tent. Therefore, the person in question should be ritually pure according to both opinions. However, **here they disagree** with regard to a different issue, the concern **lest he remove his head and the majority of his body** from the chest, box, or cabinet **into there,** i.e., the land of the nations.

The *Gemara* offers yet a third possibility: *ve'iba'it eima, hacha shema yotzi rosho verubo lesham pligi* — if you wish, say that the debate concerns a different issue altogether. On this reading, everyone agrees that *eretz ha'amim*'s *tumah* is *gusha*-based, and everyone agrees that the box constitutes an *ohel* that would normally protect its occupant. Nevertheless, one might say the person in the box is *tamei* because of a presumption that he will lean out — that he will extend his head or the majority of his body outside the box, at which point even the *gusha*-based *tumah* would reach him through normal *ohel* logic. The *Hachamim* who rule him *tamei* immediately may be acting on this presumption: we are sufficiently confident he will lean out that we treat him as *tamei* from the outset.

### Yosei Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַנִּכְנָס לְאֶרֶץ הָעַמִּים בְּשִׁידָּה תֵּיבָה וּמִגְדָּל — טָהוֹר, עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא לְשָׁם רֹאשׁוֹ אוֹ רוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** likewise in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: One who enters the land of the nations in a chest, a box, or a cabinet is pure, unless he** actually **removes his head or the majority of his body into** the land of the nations. By contrast, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi renders him ritually impure due to concern that one's head might protrude from the container.

*Tosafot* brings a *baraita* in which *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* explicitly addresses this: *hanichnas l'eretz ha'amim b'shidah tevah u'migdal tahor, ad sheyotzi lesham rosho o rubo* — one who enters in a box is *tahor* until he actually extends his head or the majority of his body outside, at which point he becomes *tamei*. This formulation strongly suggests that the operative concern is indeed the act of leaning out, and that *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* refuses to presume it in advance — he requires it to actually occur. The *Hachamim* who rule *tamei* immediately presumably accept the presumption and act on it preemptively.

One might ask: if this is really about *gusha* and *ohel* logic, why does it require specifically *rosho verubo* — the head or majority of the body — to trigger *tumah*? Even a small part of the body extending beyond the *ohel* might seem sufficient. The *Gemara* does not fully resolve this tension, and it remains a difficulty with the *gusha*-leaning interpretation.

### Summary

We thus have a range of interpretive possibilities for the *tannaitic* debate and for the underlying question of *tumas eretz ha'amim*. The first possibility: the debate is directly about *avira* versus *gusha* — *Rabbi* holds *avira* and therefore rules the person in the box *tamei* despite the *ohel*, while *Rabbi Yosei bar Rabbi Yehuda* holds *gusha* and rules him *tahor* because the *ohel* protects him. The second possibility: both agree the issue is *gusha*, but disagree whether the transported box constitutes an *ohel zaruk* that fails to protect. The third, supported by the *baraita* about wagon, boat, and mast: both agree the issue is *avira*, but the decree applies only to normal modes of entry — the box is too unusual a method to have been included. The fourth: both agree the issue is *gusha* and the box is a valid *ohel*, but one *Tanna* presumes the occupant will lean out and rules him *tamei* preemptively, while the other requires the leaning to actually occur. Each of these frameworks carries different implications for how broadly or narrowly *tumas eretz ha'amim* applies — and each illuminates a different dimension of what the Sages were trying to accomplish with this remarkable decree.
