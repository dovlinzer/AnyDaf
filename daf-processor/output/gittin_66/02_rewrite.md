# Gittin 66 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two/Three Person Agency

### Language Assumptions

Today's study begins at the final line of Gittin 66a, where the *mishna* turns to a question that will occupy the bulk of the *daf*: when a husband appoints others to write and deliver a *get*, does his appointment bind those individuals personally, or may they in turn delegate that responsibility to someone else? The *mishna* explores this question through a series of cases distinguished by the number of people addressed and the precise language used.

### Two People Direct Action

The *mishna* opens: *amar lishnayim tnu get l'ishti* — if a husband says to two people, "give a *get* to my wife," and since one cannot give a *get* without first writing it, the instruction to give necessarily implies writing as well. Similarly, if he says to three people *kitvu get utnu l'ishti* — "write a *get* and give it to my wife" — then *harei elu yichtevu veyitnu*: those people themselves must both write and deliver it. They may not hand that responsibility to others.

### Three People/Beit Din

Here, however, the *mishna* introduces an important distinction, and it is precisely this distinction that explains why the *mishna* treats the two-person *tnu* case separately from the three-person *tnu* case. If a husband says to three people *tnu get l'ishti* — "give a *get* to my wife" — without specifying *kitvu*, the word *tnu* alone can plausibly be understood as "take responsibility to see that it is given," rather than as a directive to perform every step personally. When said to only two people, we would not interpret the instruction that way, because two people do not constitute a body that normally supervises and delegates; they would be expected to act themselves. Three people, however, constitute a *beis din*, and a *beis din* can be understood as a supervisory body that need not execute matters directly. Therefore, when a husband tells three people *tnu* without the concrete specification of *kitvu*, the *mishna* rules: *harei elu yomru l'acherim veyichtevu* — they may instruct others to write the *get*. This is because *she'asan beis din* — he has cast them in the role of a *beis din* — and that is the ruling of Rabbi Meir.

The *mishna* adds that this is also the *halacha* brought down by Rabbi Chanina Ish Ono from prison — a transmission from Rabbi Akiva, who was then incarcerated under the Roman decrees: *mekubal ani b'omer lishlosha tnu get l'ishti, yomru l'acherim veyichtevu, mipnei she'asan beis din* — I have received the tradition that when one says to three people "give a *get* to my wife," they may delegate to others, because he has made them a *beis din*.

### Rabbi Yose's View

Rabbi Yose, however, disputes this emphatically. *Nominu l'shaliach* — using an archaic term for "we said to the agent" — he declares: I too have a tradition. And his tradition is that *afillu amar l'beis din hagadol shebirushalayim tnu get l'ishti* — even if one says to the Great Court in Jerusalem, "give a *get* to my wife," since he used the word *tnu* and not *kitvu*, and those justices are obviously a *beis din* and one would never expect them to take out parchment and begin scribing themselves — nevertheless, if this *get* is to be given through this appointment, *she'yilmedu veyichtevu veyitnu*: they themselves must learn how to write a *get* and do it. They may always decline the appointment, but they cannot simply supervise and pass the task to others. *Harei elu yichtevu veyitnu* — it is they who must act.

This is a striking position. Rabbi Yose seems unconcerned with the husband's apparent intent, or at least he holds that whatever one's intent, the words of appointment bind only those who directly received them.

### Ten People Distribution

Shifting to a different scenario, the *mishna* takes up the case where the husband says to ten people *kitvu utnu get l'ishti* — "write a *get* and give it to my wife." Since he specifies *kitvu*, it is clear he does not mean mere supervision; he means they should execute it. How does this work in practice when addressing a group? *Echad kotev ushnayim chotmin* — one person writes, two people sign. The phrase "write and give," addressed to many, means simply: from among you, some combination should accomplish this. One person writes, two sign as witnesses, one delivers.

### Kulhon Requirement

A further case: *kulhon kitvu* — if he explicitly says "all of you write a *get*." Here, *echad kotev v'chulam chotmin* — one person writes and all of them sign. This prompts a question that will recur in the *Gemara*: if all of them must write, how is it that only one person does the actual scribing? The answer the *Gemara* will give is that when the husband says *kulhon*, the requirement is not that each person physically holds the pen, but that all are present while one writes — a kind of representative action, analogous to a *shaliach tzibur* who prays on behalf of the entire congregation. Everyone is present and constitutes the body; one person is the instrument through which the group acts.

### Invalidation by Death

The *mishna* closes this section with a consequential ruling: *im meit echad meihen harei zeh get batel* — if one of the ten dies before the *get* is written and delivered, the *get* is invalid. This flows directly from the husband's demand that all of them be part of the process. What is notable here — and worth pausing over — is how powerfully the husband's *da'at* and *ratzon* shape the very validity of the *get*. One might have assumed that a *get* has a fixed form: a specific text, signed by two witnesses, properly delivered. But in fact the husband controls far more than that. If he specified that ten people must all be involved and that requirement is not fulfilled, the *get* itself is *batel*. His will is not merely a preference; it is constitutive of the document's validity.

## Milei/Shaliach Question

### Scribe vs. Witness

The *Gemara* opens with a remarkable historical note: Rabbi Yirmiyah bar Abba reports that they sent a question *mi'bei Rav l'Shmuel* — from the academy of Rav to Shmuel. The very fact that the students of Rav would send a question to Shmuel is itself striking, given that the two were contemporaries generally regarded as peers.

The question: *amar l'shnayim kitvu utnu get l'ishti, v'amru l'sofer v'chatav v'chatmu hem mahu* — if a husband says to two people "write and give a *get* to my wife," and they do not write it themselves but instead have a *sofer* write it while they sign it as witnesses — what is the *halacha*? The question, at its core, is: when the husband says *kitvu* to these two people, does he mean that they must do the actual scribing, or does *kitvu* mean that they must sign? If it means scribing, they may not have the *sofer* do it. If it means signing, then having the *sofer* write it is merely finding someone to perform a task the husband implicitly left open.

Shmuel's reply: *teitzei v'hadavar tzarich talmud* — if she remarried on the basis of this *get*, she cannot remain with her second husband because the *get* is of questionable validity, but the matter requires further study. It is a *safek get*.

### Shmuel: No Delegation

The *Gemara* now asks: what exactly requires further investigation? One possibility is that the doubt concerns whether they are permitted to pass on their verbal appointment to someone else — the principle of *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*, that words cannot be delegated to an agent. But this possibility is immediately challenged: *veha'amar Shmuel amar Rav halacha k'Rabbi Yose de'amar mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — Shmuel himself rules that we follow Rabbi Yose, who holds that a verbal appointment cannot be passed on. If that is already settled, why is there any doubt?

### Kitvu Meaning Debate

The *Gemara* therefore refocuses: Shmuel's real question is not about delegation but about the meaning of *kitvu* itself. When the husband says to the two people "write it," does he mean *kesav yadan* — that they must sign it — or does he mean *kesav haget* — that they must actually scribe the document? This is the live question.

### Ten-Person Proof

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve this from the *mishna* itself: *amar l'shnayim tnu get l'ishti, o lishlosha kitvu utnu get l'ishti, harei elu yichtevu veyitnu* — when the *mishna* says they must write it, doesn't that clearly mean they must scribe it? The *Gemara* rejects this, noting that the question about the meaning of *kitvu* is precisely what is at issue; the *mishna* cannot serve as its own resolution. More useful is the end of the *mishna*, where in the ten-person case the language *echad kotev ushnayim chotmin* suggests that someone in the group must be the actual scribe — which would imply that *kitvu* does include scribing.

### Signing-Only Rejected

The *Gemara* pushes toward clarification: *pishita dekesav haget hu* — it is obvious that *kitvu* means scribe the document. The proof: the *mishna* cites Rabbi Yose as saying that even the *beis din hagadol* in Jerusalem would have to *she'yilmedu veyichtevu* — learn how to write a *get* and do it themselves. If *kitvu* meant only signing, it would be absurd to say that the Supreme Court might not know how to sign their own names. That they would need to learn how to sign makes no sense; that they might need to learn the technical craft of scribing a *get* is at least intelligible.

The *Gemara* deflects this: *in, ika beis dina chadata* — perhaps it is a newly constituted court whose members have not yet signed anything. This is a forced answer, acknowledged as such, and the question remains live.

### Finding a Scribe

The *Gemara* now restates the issue in its proper form. If *kitvu* means sign — not scribe — then having the *sofer* do the writing is not a case of passing on one's agency (*mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*); rather, it is a case where the two people are implicitly authorized to find a *sofer*, and their communication to that *sofer* is simply informing him that the husband has appointed him. That is the concept of *omer imru* — the husband is telling the two people, in effect, "tell the *sofer* to write it," meaning the husband is appointing the *sofer* through the intermediary of the two witnesses. The *sofer* has not received a sub-delegated appointment from the witnesses; he has received the husband's appointment, communicated through messengers. The question Shmuel is raising, then, is whether *omer imru* is permissible — and that depends on whether the *sofer* can be validly appointed through intermediaries rather than through direct communication from the husband himself.

## Milei vs. Omer Imru

### Milei Principle

The distinction being developed here is fundamental. *Mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — words cannot be delegated to an agent — addresses the following situation: I appoint Reuven, and Reuven, rather than performing the task himself, sub-delegates it to Shimon. Reuven has taken his own appointment, his *shlichus*, and attempted to pass it on. The question is whether he has the authority to do that. By a *get*, especially given the centrality of the husband's *da'at* and the verse *v'chasav lah sefer krisus* — "and he shall write for her a bill of divorce" — there are strong grounds to say that the appointment must remain with the original appointee. Reuven cannot make Shimon his substitute.

### Omer Imru Concept

*Omer imru* is a structurally different situation. Here the husband says to Reuven and Shimon: "go tell the *sofer* to write, and go tell Ploni and Ploni to sign." The husband himself is appointing the *sofer* and the witnesses; he is simply using Reuven and Shimon as messengers to communicate that appointment. They are not delegating their own agency; they are transmitting the husband's appointment to third parties. The question is whether this indirect form of appointment — *shlichus shelo befanav*, appointment not made in the presence of the appointee — is valid for a *get*.

## Chasam Sofer Issue

### Sofer V'Ed Valid

The *Gemara* now introduces a *mishna* from elsewhere that will generate significant discussion. Rabbi Yirmiyah reports: *chasam sofer shaninu* — the *mishna* speaks of a case where the *sofer* himself signs as one of the witnesses, together with one other witness. That configuration — *chasam sofer v'ed* — is valid.

### Validity Problem

But this raises an immediate difficulty. Rav Chisda states: *matnisin mani Rabbi Yose hi* — that *mishna* follows the position of Rabbi Yose. How so? Because the validity of *chasam sofer v'ed* presupposes that everything was arranged in a particular way, and the question is how that arrangement can be assumed to be legitimate under Rabbi Yose's stringent requirements.

### Embarrassment Scenario

The *Gemara* works through a troubling scenario. Suppose the husband, through *omer imru*, tells two intermediaries: "tell the *sofer* to write it and tell Ploni and Ploni to sign it." The intermediaries go to find the appointed witnesses, but the *sofer* objects — he is embarrassed that he is deemed fit to write the *get* but not fit to sign as a witness. Moved by concern for the *sofer's* dignity (*mishum kisufa d'sofer*), one of the intermediaries makes an adjustment: he has the *sofer* serve as one of the witnesses alongside only one of the originally designated witnesses. *U'va'al lo amar hachi* — but the husband said no such thing. The *get* is therefore *pasul*, yet those who rely on it may not know this and will assume it is *kasher*.

### Shouldn't Do/Valid

This is precisely the concern: if *omer imru* is permissible, one can readily imagine this corruption arising. The *Gemara* responds: *kivan d'amar mar kasher v'lo ta'aseh chen b'Yisrael* — since we rule that *chasam sofer v'ed* is valid but one should never do this in Israel, meaning the *sofer* himself is required to refuse such an arrangement. Since this practice is prohibited and the *sofer* ought not comply, we are not afraid that this scenario will in fact unfold. The prohibition makes the bad outcome sufficiently uncommon that we need not treat it as a standing concern.

### Witness Sign Alternative

But the *Gemara* is not satisfied. A second scenario: suppose the husband directly appoints the witnesses themselves and tells them to sign — he does not use *omer imru* for the witnesses at all — but he simply asks them to find someone to be the *sofer*. They are themselves committed to signing; only the scribing is left open. Now one might think this is clearly acceptable. Yet the same problem can arise: the witnesses, concerned about the *sofer's* feelings, have him sign as a witness instead of one of them — and again the husband never authorized this arrangement.

### Rava's Position

Rava concludes: *ha nami kasher v'lo ta'aseh hu* — this second scenario also falls under the rubric of *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. One may not do this *lechatchila*, and the *sofer* should refuse to participate as a witness in such a circumstance. Since both scenarios are prohibited, neither presents a practical danger that should lead us to invalidate *chasam sofer v'ed* entirely.

### Rabbi Yose Explains It

At this point the *Gemara* draws a clean conclusion: *Rabbi Yose tarti amar* — Rabbi Yose holds both positions simultaneously. Not only does he hold *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* — that a verbal appointment cannot be sub-delegated — he also holds that *omer imru* is invalid. No one can represent the husband in the writing, signing, or delivery of a *get* unless that person heard the appointment directly from the husband himself. This dual position is, upon reflection, entirely coherent. When the *mishna* says that even the *beis din hagadol* in Jerusalem must do it themselves, the implicit situation is one where the husband almost certainly intended them to delegate — and yet Rabbi Yose disallows it. This is not only about sub-delegation; it is about the principle that only those who heard directly from the husband can be part of the process. That is *omer imru* logic as much as *mili* logic.

## Milei vs. Omer Imru…

### Reconciliation

With Rabbi Yose's position now fully articulated, the *Gemara* returns to Shmuel's ruling: *halacha k'Rabbi Yose de'amar mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*. The question is whether Shmuel adopts Rabbi Yose's position entirely — both *mili* and *omer imru* — or only partially. To understand the answer, it is worth being precise about the conceptual difference between the two principles.

*Mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach* addresses the internal authority of an agent: if I appointed Reuven, does Reuven have the power to take that appointment and transfer it to Shimon? The answer is no — the appointment stays with Reuven. *Omer imru* addresses a different question: can the husband appoint someone who is not in his presence, by having a messenger communicate the appointment? This is the question of *shlichus shelo befanav* — can you create agency in someone who did not directly hear the appointment from the principal?

These are distinct. In the first case, the appointed agent is fully empowered but lacks the authority to transfer his role. In the second case, the question is whether a valid appointment was ever created in the first place.

### Rabbi Yose Both Rules

Rabbi Yose holds that both are prohibited, and this is what makes his position internally consistent and explains the full force of the *mishna's* ruling about the *beis din hagadol*. Even in that case — where the implicit intent seems clearly to delegate — Rabbi Yose says no: the only people who can act are those who directly heard the appointment from the husband. *Mili lo mimsaran* prevents sub-delegation; the *omer imru* prohibition prevents indirect appointment altogether.

### Shmuel's Split

Shmuel, however, splits the difference. *U-Shmuel savar lah kevaseih b'chada u-palig aleih b'chada* — Shmuel agrees with Rabbi Yose on one point and disagrees on the other. Shmuel holds *mili lo mimsaran l'shaliach*: if you are the appointed agent, you cannot pass your appointment on. But Shmuel disagrees regarding *omer imru*: the husband may appoint someone through an intermediary. An indirect appointment, where a messenger informs the prospective agent that the husband is appointing him, is valid. The appointee need not hear it directly from the husband himself.

This split is the key that unlocks Shmuel's original question. When the husband says to two people *kitvu utnu get l'ishti*, and Shmuel asks whether *kitvu* means sign or scribe — here is what depends on the answer. If *kitvu* means the actual scribing, then having the *sofer* do it would require them to transfer their own appointment to the *sofer*, which is *mili lo mimsaran*, and Shmuel would rule it invalid. But if *kitvu* means sign, then the husband never appointed these two to scribe at all; he implicitly left the scribing open, to be arranged through them as intermediaries. That is *omer imru*, which Shmuel permits. They are not sub-delegating their appointment; they are communicating the husband's appointment of the *sofer*. Hence the *get* would be valid. Shmuel's uncertainty about whether the *get* is valid — his response of *teitzei v'hadavar tzarich talmud* — reflects precisely his uncertainty about what *kitvu* means in this context.

## Lishnayim Agency

### Indirect Appointment

The *Gemara* now takes up a case directly related to *omer imru*. The husband says to two people: *imru l'sofer veyichtov, uleploni ploni veyachtimu* — tell the *sofer* to write it and tell so-and-so and so-and-so to sign it. Here the husband himself is not directly appointing anyone; he is using two intermediaries to communicate all of his appointments. Everything is transmitted indirectly.

### Concern: Bribed Witnesses

Rav Huna says in the name of Rav: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh chen b'Yisrael* — it is valid, but one should not do this in Israel. The *Gemara* asks: if it is valid, why should one not do it? The answer: *chayishinan shema tiskor edim* — we are concerned lest the woman have hired witnesses. If the husband does not directly appoint anyone and instead two intermediaries come to the *sofer* and the prospective witnesses claiming that the husband instructed them, there is no independent verification. Perhaps the woman bribed these two people to fabricate the husband's appointment, enabling the *get* to be written without the husband's genuine consent.

### Trust in Documents

The *Gemara* challenges this concern: is it not true that witnesses who sign a bill of sale or a *get* are trusted, and the Sages have no concern about forgery? *Edim hachatumim al sadeh mekach v'al get ishah, lo chashu chachamim l'davar zeh* — witnesses signed on a deed of sale or on a woman's *get*, the Sages had no such worry.

### Words vs. Writing

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: *ma'aseh lo avdi* — people will not actually forge a document. We are not afraid they will put their names to a fraudulent *get*. But *dibura ka'amri* — spoken messages are another matter. Finding someone willing to pass on a fabricated verbal message, without personally signing anything, is a realistic concern. The two intermediaries are not personally implicated by any written document; they are merely transmitting words. That is where the potential for corruption lies, and that is why the *sofer* and the witnesses should not agree to be appointed through such an indirect chain.

### Witnesses Self-Commit

The *Gemara* then considers a modified scenario: *amar lishnayim imru l'sofer veyichtov v'atem chatomu* — the husband tells the two directly that they themselves will sign, and only asks them to communicate to the *sofer* that he should write the *get*. Here the witnesses are personally committed — they are signing — so there is no concern that the woman hired them. The *sofer* can have confidence that the witnesses' word is genuine, since they are putting their own names on the document.

### Amoraic Dispute

Rav Chisda rules: *keshera v'lo ta'aseh* — even this is valid but should not be done, as a precautionary measure to prevent sliding into the broader prohibited practice. The *Gemara* then records a remarkable range of *Amoraic* opinions. Rabbah bar bar Chana says: *kasher veta'aseh* — it is permitted outright. Rav Nachman: *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Sheshet: *kasher veta'aseh*. Rabbah: *kasher v'lo ta'aseh*. Rav Yosef: *kasher veta'aseh*. And the *Gemara* adds that some reverse the attributions to Rabbah and Rav Yosef. What emerges is that in practice we follow Rav Yosef, but the picture is complicated by Shmuel's earlier ruling, which acknowledged *omer imru* as technically valid while the subsequent *Amoraic* discussion introduces pragmatic concerns about reliability that counsel against it even where it would be *bedieved* valid.

## Counting Names

### Kulkhem Distinction

The *Gemara* now takes up the *braita* elaborating on the ten-person case from the *mishna*. *Amar l'asarah kitvu get utnu l'ishti, echad kotev al yedei kulam* — if he says to ten people "write a *get* and give it to my wife," one writes on behalf of all. By contrast, *kulchem kitvu* — if he says "all of you write it" — then *echad kotev bema'amad kulam*: one writes but all must be present.

### One Writes Distribution

The same logic applies to delivery. *Holichu get l'ishti, echad molich al yedei kulam* — if he says "bring the *get* to my wife," one person delivers on behalf of all. *Kulchem holichu* — if he specifies all of you — then *echad molich bema'amad kulam*: one delivers, but all must be present. The analogy to the *shaliach tzibur* is instructive: just as the prayer leader represents the entire congregation in *tefillat hatzibur* — each member present, one person as the mouthpiece — so here, one person performs the act while all are present as the constitutive body.

### Counting Effect

This leads to a novel question: *mana otan mahu* — if the husband does not say *kulchem* explicitly but instead counts them — one, two, three, four, five, six, seven, eight, nine, ten — does that counting carry the same legal weight as saying *kulchem*? Does enumerating each person individually signal an intent that all of them must participate, or does it merely mean "from among you"?

### Huna: No Effect

Rav Huna rules: *mana eino ke'kulam* — counting is not equivalent to saying *kulchem*. The counting does not impose the requirement that all must be present.

### Yochanan: Does Mean

Rav Yochanan, citing Rabbi Elazar from Rome, rules: *mana harei hu ke'kulam* — counting is indeed equivalent to *kulchem*. By identifying each person individually, the husband signals that each of them is specifically included and required.

### Papa Resolves

Rav Papa steps in to resolve the dispute: *v'lo pligi* — they do not actually disagree. The distinction is whether the husband counted all the people in the room or only some of them. *Ha d'mana kulhu ha d'mana miktzatayhu* — one ruling applies when he counted everyone present, and the other applies when he counted only some. Which case yields which ruling is itself debated — *amri lah l'hai gisa v'amri lah l'hai gisa* — with some holding that counting everyone means *kulchem* and counting some means "from among you," while others hold the reverse. The underlying logic in each direction is not entirely transparent, but the operative distinction is whether the counting was exhaustive or selective.

## Takanah for Formula

### Fixed Nusach

Rav Yehuda instituted a *takanah* to address the practical hazards created by the *kulchem* requirement. He established a fixed *nusach* — a standard formula — for the husband to use when appointing agents to write and deliver a *get*. The formula: *ktovu o kulchem o kol chad v'chad minchon, chatomu shtein o kulchem o kol trei minchon, ovilu o kulchem o kol chad v'chad minchon* — write it, whether all of you or any one of you; sign it, whether all of you or any two of you; deliver it, whether all of you or any one of you. By explicitly including the alternative of any individual, the formula ensures that the *get* will not be invalidated by the death or absence of one of the appointees.

### Rava's Caution

Rava raises a concern with this formula. *Lo zimnin degayiz lei l'diburei, v'omar kulchem v'lo amar kol chad v'chad minchon* — it sometimes happens that a person will cut off what he is saying and will say *kulchem* without completing the rest of the formula. If the husband says "all of you" and fails to add "or any one of you," the *get* will become *pasul*.

### Better Wording

Rava therefore proposes an alternative: do not begin with *kulchem* at all. *Ktovu kol chad minchon, chatomu kol trei minchon, ovilu kol chad minchon* — simply establish a text that opens with "any one of you" and does not introduce *kulchem* in the first place. If the formula never raises the problem of an all-inclusive requirement, there is no risk that the husband will inadvertently impose one. The word *kulchem* is the source of the difficulty; the better practice is to teach people a formula that avoids it entirely.
