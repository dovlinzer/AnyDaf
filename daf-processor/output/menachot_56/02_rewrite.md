# Menachot 56 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nasi's Chatat & Tzafon

### V'Shachatat Derivation

The daf begins by wrapping up a lengthy digression that grew out of a discussion about *klal u'prat hamruchakim ze mize* — the question of whether two verses that form a *klal u'prat* are so distant from one another that the interpretive principle cannot apply. The case in point involved the verses *lo ta'aseh* and *lo ta'aseh chametz*, which the *Gemara* reads not as a *klal u'prat* but as indicating that every stage of a process — every act of making the *minchah* as *chametz* — carries its own liability. One of the reasons the *Gemara* had for rejecting the *klal u'prat* reading was that those verses are too far apart. That led into a discussion about whether the requirement that certain *korbanot* be slaughtered in the north (*tzafon*) could be established through *klal u'prat*, and that in turn gave rise to a discussion about the *seir nachshon* — the *chatat* brought by the *nesi'im* during the inauguration of the *mishkan* — and the word *oto*, which seemed to limit the application of certain laws to that particular offering.

Now the *Gemara* closes that loop. The verse *v'shachatat et hachatat* teaches that every *chatat* must be slaughtered in the north, and the repetition of that requirement in the context of a *nasi*'s *chatat* indicates that it is *me'akev*. Given this, the *Gemara* asks: what is the word *oto* — "it" — in that passage coming to exclude?

The *Gemara* answers: *oto bitzafon v'ein seir nachshon bitzafon*. The word *oto* is telling us that only the standard *chatat* of a *nasi* — the one that applies for all future generations — must be in the north. The *chatat* brought by *Nachshon ben Aminadav* and the other *nesi'im* during the twelve days of the inauguration of the *mishkan* does not require *tzafon*.

### Free-Will Chatat Anomaly

This is a striking ruling, and it invites a moment of reflection. The offering brought by the *nesi'im* during the *chanukat hamishkan* is peculiar in the extreme. *Rashi* notes that it was a free-will *chatat* — not brought to atone for any specific sin, not brought for purposes of *taharah* in any conventional sense, but offered voluntarily. Whoever heard of a free-will *chatat*? The anomalous character of these offerings forces the *Gemara* to ask how they conform to the normal laws governing a *chatat* at all. This exceptional status underlies the *Gemara*'s reluctance to assume that the standard laws governing *korbanot* apply to it. The assumption is not merely that we cannot learn permanent law from a one-time event (*sha'ah m'doros*), but that this particular offering is so extraordinary in nature that no standard law can be assumed to apply unless it is explicitly stated.

### Smichah Derivation

The *Gemara* then asks: how do we know that *smichah* — the laying on of hands — was performed at all by the *seir nachshon*? A *braita* is cited in answer: the verse states *v'samach yado al rosh haseir*, "and he shall place his hand on the head of the goat," rather than the simpler *v'samach yado al rosho*. The use of the phrase *al rosh haseir* — specifying "the goat" rather than simply "its head" — is understood as including another goat brought by a *nasi*, namely the *chatat* brought by the *nesi'im* during the inauguration of the *mishkan*. The word *nasi* appears in both contexts, and the extra language serves to include the *seir nachshon* in the requirement of *smichah*. This is the view of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Rabbi Yehuda vs Shimon

*Rebbe Shimon* disagrees. He holds that the extra language *al rosh haseir* does not refer to the *seir nachshon* at all; it comes rather to include the goats brought as a *chatat* when the *beit din* errs in a ruling involving *avodah zarah* — the *se'irim* of *he'elam davar*. *Tosafot* notes that this would seem straightforward, since those goats are *l'doros* and already connected to other *chataot* and presumably follow the normal rules in any case. The force of the *drashah*, then, is stronger as applied to the *seir nachshon*, the anomalous case. But according to *Rebbe Shimon*, the *Torah* simply would not go out of its way to teach a one-time *halacha* about what the *nesi'im* had to do during that unique event. Whatever the *Torah* says, it says for all future generations.

The practical consequence of this dispute is as follows: according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, who holds that *smichah* applies to the *seir nachshon*, one might think that the north requirement applies as well, and therefore the word *oto* is needed to exclude it. According to *Rebbe Shimon*, who holds that *smichah* is not established for the *seir nachshon*, there is no basis to think that north would apply either, so one might ask: what does *oto* come to exclude?

### Exceptional Status Logic

*Ravina* raises precisely this challenge: the word *oto* is well-understood according to *Rabbi Yehuda*, but what does *Rebbe Shimon* do with it?

*Mar Zutra the son of Rebbe Mari* responds with a sweeping argument — and a striking one. He says: in truth, *oto* is not needed even according to *Rabbi Yehuda*. The *seir nachshon* is such an exception to normal law that the correct posture is *mai d'israbis israbis u'mai d'lo israbis lo israbis* — whatever was explicitly included is included, and whatever was not explicitly included is not. The very fact that a special verse was needed to include *smichah* proves the point: if the default were to assume that all standard laws apply, why would a verse be required to establish *smichah*? The very need for that verse demonstrates that the default is the opposite — we assume nothing applies unless stated. If so, *tzafon* is automatically excluded without any need for *oto*, because no verse ever includes it.

The *Gemara* affirms this reasoning: *sha'ah m'doros lo yalpinan* — we do not derive permanent law from what was done during a one-time, exceptional moment in history. That is why *smichah* required an explicit verse to be established for the *seir nachshon*, and that is why *tzafon* needs no verse to be excluded — there is simply nothing to include it.

What makes this particularly remarkable, as noted, is not merely the principle of *sha'ah m'doros* in itself, but the combination of that principle with the fundamentally anomalous character of this offering. It was not just a one-time event; it was a free-will *chatat*, a category that strains the very definition of that *korban*. The *Gemara* is saying that an offering so far outside the norm cannot have any standard law assumed of it.

### Oto: Animal Not Shochet

If *oto* does not come to exclude *tzafon* from the *seir nachshon* in either of the earlier proposed ways, then what does it come to teach? The *Gemara* answers: *oto batzafon v'ein hashocheit omed batzafon*. The animal must be in the north; the *shochet* need not stand in the north.

This is significant because *shechita* is not an *avodah* — it does not require a *kohen* and is not a formal priestly act. The *shochet* can even stand in the *Ezrat Nashim* and slaughter with a long knife extended over the line. But at the moment of *shechita*, the animal is being designated in its sanctity as *kodshei kodashim*, and that designation must occur in the place designated for *kodshei kodashim* — the north. The focus is on the animal, not on the actor.

### Rav Achya's Distinction

One might object that this is already known from elsewhere. The *Gemara* cites a *braita* in the name of *Rav Achya*: the verse *v'shachat oto al yerech hamizbe'ach tzafona* — "and he shall slaughter it on the north side of the altar" — teaches by contrast with the case of *kabalah*, receiving the blood. Regarding *kabalah*, we have established that not only must the act of receiving take place in the north, but the *kohen* himself must be standing in the north. If he stands in the south and receives the blood with a vessel placed in the north, it is invalid. Since such a requirement exists for the *mekabel*, one might have thought the same applies to the *shochet* — that he too must stand in the north. The verse therefore states *oto* — "it," the animal — to teach that only the animal must be in the north, not the *shochet* himself.

So the *Gemara* returns to the question: if we know from *Rav Achya*'s teaching that the *shochet* need not stand in the north, what new information does the *oto* in the verse about the *nasi*'s *chatat* provide?

### Exception for Shechita

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent redundancy by reinterpreting the thrust of *Rav Achya*'s teaching. *Rav Achya* is not, in fact, primarily teaching that the *shochet* need not stand in the north. Rather, his point is the reverse: he establishes by contrast that the *mekabel* — the one who receives the blood — *does* require that the *kohen* stand in the north, not merely that the vessel be there. The teaching that the *shochet* need not be in the north is the premise, not the conclusion. The conclusion is that despite *shechita*'s leniency in this regard, *kabalah* is more demanding.

Where, then, does *Rav Achya* derive that the *mekabel* must stand in the north? The *Gemara* points to the dual formulation *velakach* and *ulakachah* in the verse concerning the *par*'s blood — *velakach midam hapar* — which conveys both that the *kohen* is present in the north and that the act of *kabalah* takes place in the north. However, the *Gemara* notes that one Amora does not read significance into this doubled formulation, and for him the requirement that the *mekabel* stand in the north comes instead from *oto* in the verse of the *nasi*'s *chatat*.

Thus the full picture emerges: the word *oto* in the verse *v'shachat oto* teaches that *shechita* requires only that the animal be in the north — establishing an exception to the broader rule that wherever the *kohen*'s person must be in the north, as is the case for *kabalah*. All of this enormous digression was ultimately in service of the broader point that the two verses *lo ta'aseh* and *lo ta'aseh chametz* are not read as a *klal u'prat*, and now the *Gemara* turns its attention back to the main topic: the prohibition of making the *minchah* as *chametz*, and the separate liability incurred at each stage of that process.

## Stages of Chametz

### Rav Papa's Two Lashes

The *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s ruling: *vechayav al lishatah v'al arichatah v'al afiyatah* — one is liable for kneading it, for arranging it, and for baking it. *Rav Papa* now adds a sharp ruling: if one bakes a *chametz* offering, he receives two sets of lashes — one for *arichah* and one for *afiyah*. This is not simply observing that he transgressed two separate stages sequentially; the *chiddush* is that in a single act of baking, both *arichah* and *afiyah* are simultaneously transgressed.

### Arichah Defined

To understand this, one must appreciate what *arichah* means. The word refers to the act of shaping and fitting the bread, and in the baking practice of the ancient world, this was not simply a preparatory step done at the workbench before the bread went into the oven. The *tanur* of the time was a cylindrical clay oven whose interior walls served as the baking surface. Bread was literally pressed and fitted against the wall of the oven — a process that both gave the bread its final shape and initiated the baking itself. *Rashi* seems to suggest, somewhat ambiguously, that perhaps the very act of baking, insofar as it finalizes and sets the form of the bread, itself constitutes a form of *arichah*. But it is easier, and more in line with *Tosafot*'s explanation, to understand that there is a physical act of *arichah* — pressing the dough against the oven wall — that is distinct from the *afiyah* proper, even though both occur at or near the same moment when the bread is placed in the oven. The oven wall itself becomes a kind of mold or baking pan, and fitting the bread to it is the *arichah*; the subsequent heat-driven transformation is the *afiyah*. This also connects to discussions elsewhere about *rediyat hapat batanur* — the act of retrieving the bread from the oven wall without it collapsing — which reflects how intimate the contact between bread and oven wall truly was.

### Arichah & Afiyah Tension

The *Gemara* raises a difficulty. The *mishnah*'s ruling — that one is liable for *afiyah bifnei atzmah*, baking as a distinct and independent transgression — depends on treating *afiyah* as a self-contained act. But if every act of baking always includes *arichah* as well, how can we treat *afiyah* as an independent paradigm of transgression? The two always come together; they cannot be separated.

### Separate Actors Solution

The *Gemara* resolves this by distinguishing between two scenarios: one in which the same person performs both *arichah* and *afiyah*, and one in which one person performs the *arichah* and a second person comes along and does the *afiyah*. In the latter case — where, say, Reuven has already shaped the bread and handed it off, and Shimon now places it in the oven — it is immediately clear that Shimon's act is straightforwardly an act of *afiyah* divorced from any *arichah* he himself performed. *Rashi* and *Tosafot* both resist the naïve reading that this scenario produces only *one* liability. On the contrary, it is precisely in this case that two liabilities are produced, because the act of placing the bread against the oven wall constitutes a discrete act of *arichah* in addition to the *afiyah*.

### Afiyah Independent

The case where one person does both *arichah* and *afiyah* is the more conceptually interesting one. Here, the *Gemara* resolves the tension as follows: when one person performs both stages, the small *arichah* involved in pressing the bread to the oven wall is treated as a continuation and completion of the earlier *arichah* performed at the workbench. The two acts of shaping are conceptually merged into one *arichah*. What then stands alone is the *afiyah* — the baking itself — and it is this that serves as the paradigm for *afiyah bifnei atzmah*, an independent act of *afiyah* for which one is separately liable. The *Torah*'s insistence that *afiyah* as such carries its own liability means that even when the *arichah* portion of the oven-placement is absorbed into the earlier act of shaping, the *afiyah* remains an independent transgression. This is why *Rav Papa*'s ruling carries a real *chiddush*: the act of baking, even in a single motion, generates dual liability — one for the *arichah* embedded within it, and one for the *afiyah* proper.

## Mechametz Achar Mechametz

### Universal Consensus

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* says in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: all authorities agree that one who performs an act of *mechametz achar mechametz* — who further leavens a *minchah* that is already *chametz* — is liable. The verse *lo te'afeh chametz* establishes that the prohibition is framed around the act of making, not around the result of having made. Since each stage of the process is an act of making the bread as *chametz*, one transgresses at each stage even if the bread is already *chametz* before that stage begins. Whatever improvement or elaboration one adds to *chametz* that is already *chametz* is still a transgression, because the act itself is prohibited, not merely the outcome.

### Matzah Application

This has an important conceptual implication. One might have understood the prohibition as: do not allow this bread to become *chametz*. Under that understanding, once the bread has become *chametz*, no further violation is possible — the prohibited result already exists, and nothing one does subsequently changes that fact. But the *Gemara* is unambiguous: the prohibition is framed as *lo ta'aseh*, "do not make" — an act-focused prohibition. Every act by which one makes the bread more thoroughly or definitively *chametz* is independently prohibited, regardless of the bread's prior state. The transgression lies in the doing, not in the becoming.

This principle raises a broader question that the *Gemara* will explore through parallel cases: across *halacha* generally, how do we determine whether a prohibition is focused on preventing a certain result — in which case, once the result exists, further action is not independently prohibited — or on prohibiting a certain type of act, in which case each performance of the act is independently forbidden even if the result has already come about?

A useful analogy, found in a different *Gemara* in Nazir, involves a *kohen* who is already *tamei meit*. Is it prohibited for him to become *tamei meit* again? One position holds that at the moment of new contact, his level of *tumah* actually increases — he becomes *tamei bechiburin* — and therefore there is a real change in status, independently justifying the prohibition. Another position holds that even absent any such change, the act of bringing oneself into *tumah* is itself the prohibited category, not merely the resultant state of *tumah*. The question of whether the prohibition tracks the act or the result is, as the *Gemara* here makes clear, one that must be analyzed individually for each *halacha*.

## Blemish on Bechor

### Bloodletting Necessity

The *Gemara* introduces the case of a *bechor* — a firstborn animal — that has suffered a blood clot and requires bloodletting to survive. Bloodletting requires cutting the skin, and depending on where the cut is made, it may or may not leave a permanent blemish. If the cut is made in most places on the body, healing will occur and no lasting *mum* will result. If the cut is made on the ear, however, the *mum* will remain permanently. The *Gemara* examines where, if at all, bloodletting is permitted, and what the consequences are if it creates a *mum*.

### Rebbe Meir's Stringency

*Rebbe Meir* takes a straightforward position: one may let blood, but only in a place where it will not create a lasting *mum*. Making a *mum* in a *bechor* — *matil mum b'bechor* — is prohibited, and the fact that the animal needs bloodletting does not override this prohibition if it entails creating a *mum*.

### Chachamim's Leniency

The *chachamim* disagree. They hold that one may let blood even in a place where it will create a *mum* — with one critical condition: *u'vilvad she-lo yishchat al oto mum*, one may not subsequently slaughter the *bechor* on account of that *mum*. The background to this condition is that a *bechor* with a *mum* may be slaughtered and eaten at home without being brought as a *korban* — a status the *kohanim* were naturally eager to achieve for their *bechoros*. Allowing bloodletting in a place that creates a *mum* could therefore become a massive loophole: anyone could claim medical necessity as a pretext for creating a *mum* on their *bechor* and then simply eat it at home. The *chachamim*'s response is to close this loophole by prohibiting the owner from *using* that *mum* — he may not slaughter the *bechor* on account of it. This also creates a practical disincentive: the animal now cannot be brought as a *korban* (because of the *mum*) and cannot be slaughtered at home on account of the *mum* (because of the rabbinic prohibition), so it is in a kind of suspended state until it develops another *mum* through natural causes.

The *chachamim*'s position is implicitly grounded in the principle of *davar she-eino miskavein* — the act of bloodletting is not intended to create a *mum*, and were it not for the concern that one might benefit from the resulting *mum*, it would be straightforwardly permitted. Indeed, a closer analysis suggests that the resulting *mum* constitutes a *psik reisha* — an inevitable consequence — and normally *psik reisha d'lo nicha lei*, an inevitable but unwanted result, carries at most rabbinic concern. Here, the *chachamim* address that concern not by prohibiting the bloodletting but by prohibiting the benefit derived from its consequence.

## Mechametz Achar Mecham…

### Castration Analogy

The *Gemara* now establishes through a parallel case that the principle of *mechametz achar mechametz* — liability for the act even when the prohibited result already exists — reflects a universal consensus. The parallel case is *mesares achar mesares*: castrating an animal after it has already been castrated. The prohibition against castrating animals (*sirus*) is severe, and the verse enumerates multiple forms of this act: *u'ma'uch v'chatut v'natuk v'charut* — crushed, cut, severed, excised — each representing a different form of damage to the reproductive organs. A *midrash halacha* asks: if one is liable for the most drastic form (*karut*), certainly one is liable for the lesser forms — why enumerate them all? The answer: *ela lehavi notek achar karut* — to include the case where one person partially severs and another fully removes. The second person is liable even though the animal was already rendered sterile and non-functional by the first person's act.

*Rashi* describes this as a case where the first person detaches the testicles while they remain within the scrotum, and the second person removes the scrotum itself. *Tosafot* describes it as partial and then full detachment of the testicles. Either way, the *Gemara* rules that the second person is fully liable.

### Result vs Action

This is perhaps the most arresting application of the principle. If ever there were a case where one might say the harm has already been done and further action is inconsequential, it would be here. An animal already rendered sterile, already functionally and biologically castrated — and yet the *Gemara* rules unequivocally that further mutilation is independently prohibited and independently liable. All authorities agree on this.

The reason, it seems, is that the prohibition of *sirus* is not merely about achieving sterility. It is about the act of mutilating an animal in this particular way. Each act of mutilation, each distinct physical harm done to the animal in this form, is its own transgression. The result — sterility — is incidental to the prohibition; the act itself is what the *Torah* forbids. This is what makes *mesares achar mesares* unambiguously liable: even when the result already exists, each additional act of mutilation is a fresh transgression.

All authorities agree on this case. The dispute, as the *Gemara* will now make clear, concerns only a somewhat different configuration.

## Blemish on Bechor…

### Second Blemish Consensus

The *Gemara* states the consensus position: *hakol modin* — all agree — that one is liable for *matil mum achar mum*, creating a second blemish in an already-blemished *bechor*. The dispute between *Rebbe Meir* and the *chachamim* concerns a distinct question, to be addressed shortly.

### Tamim Yihiyeh Principle

The verse cited in this context is *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon* — "it shall be perfect for acceptance." The verse *kol mum lo yihiyeh bo* — "no blemish shall be in it" — is also relevant. *Rebbe Meir* derives from *kol mum lo yihiyeh bo* that even a blemish added to an already-blemished animal is prohibited, since the word *kol* — "any" — encompasses even additional blemishes layered on existing ones. The *chachamim*, by contrast, read the prohibition as tied to *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon*: the prohibition of creating a *mum* applies only when the animal is still *tamim*, still eligible to be brought upon the *mizbe'ach*. Once it is already blemished and therefore ineligible, there is nothing left to protect, and a second *mum* is not prohibited.

The *Gemara* asks: does *Rebbe Meir* not also hold by the verse *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon*? He does — but he reads that verse differently.

### Redeemed Animals Excepti…

According to *Rebbe Meir*, *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon* comes to exclude *psulei hamukdashim achar pidyon* — sanctified animals that bore a *mum*, were redeemed, and are no longer formally *kodshim* though they retain certain restrictions. *Rebbe Meir* holds that normally, even an already-blemished animal with *kedushah* remains subject to the prohibition of *matil mum*. However, once that animal has been redeemed — once its *kedushah* for the *mizbe'ach* has been formally dissolved — there is no longer any prohibition of creating a *mum* in it.

One might have thought otherwise: even after redemption, such an animal retains certain restrictions. The verse *vezavachta v'achalta* — and you shall slaughter and eat — is interpreted by the *Gemara* as implying *lo gizzah v'lo chalav*: you may not shear it and you may not milk it; you may only slaughter and eat it. Given these ongoing restrictions, one might have supposed that the prohibition of *matil mum* also continues. *Rebbe Meir* teaches, through the verse *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon*, that this is not so: once the animal is no longer *ra'uy le-mizbe'ach* — no longer in the category of that which is destined for the altar — the prohibition of creating a *mum* falls away, even though other restrictions remain.

The *chachamim* by contrast hold that *tamim yihiyeh le-ratzon* teaches the more general point that the *mum* prohibition applies only to animals that are currently *tamim*, and once a *mum* already exists, no second *mum* is prohibited — even before any redemption. The *chachamim* would therefore require a different verse for the exclusion of *psulei hamukdashim*, or would simply say that since the animal is already *ba'al mum*, it is automatically outside the *tamim* category and thus unprotected. The *Gemara* notes that an animal born with a *mum* or sanctified while already blemished is so obviously outside the *mezbe'ach* category that it is *dikla b'alma hu* — no better than a palm tree — and no verse is needed to exclude it.

### Indirect Blemishing

The *Gemara* now turns to an entirely different dimension of the *mum* prohibition: indirect blemishing. Even if one accepts that actively creating a *mum* is prohibited, one might think that engineering a *mum* through indirect means — causing it to come about through the actions of another — would be permitted. The verse *kol mum lo yihiyeh bo* teaches otherwise.

A *braita* expounds: *ein li ela she-lo yehe bo mum* — I know only that you may not actively place a blemish in the animal. *Minayin she-lo yigrom lo al yedei acherim* — how do I know you may not cause a blemish to come about through others? The *braita* provides a vivid illustration: *she-lo yani'ach batzek o deveilah al gabei ha-ozen k'dei she-yavo ha-kelev v'yitlenu* — one may not place dough or pressed figs against the ear of the *bechor* so that a dog will come and bite them off, in the process tearing the ear and creating a permanent *mum*.
