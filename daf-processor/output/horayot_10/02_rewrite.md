# Horayot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chatu Ad Shelo Nitmanu

### Mishnah Setup

The third chapter of *Horayot*, continuing on 10a, takes up a comparison between the *kohen mashiach* and the *nasi* with respect to transgressions committed before either figure assumed his appointed role. The *mishnah* poses the case of *chatu ad shelo nitmanu* — they sinned before they were appointed. We are discussing the *kohen mashiach* and the *nasi* (who, as we shall see, is the king), and the question is: what is the status of a transgression — one whose deliberate commission would carry *karet* and whose inadvertent commission requires a *chatat* — committed before these individuals rose to their respective positions? *Ve-achar kach nitmanu* — and afterward they were appointed — *harei elu ke-hedyotot*: they are treated as ordinary persons and bring the sacrifice of an ordinary person.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

Rabbi Shimon refines this ruling. *Im noda lahem ad shelo nitmanu chayavin, u-mishe-nitmanu peturin.* For a transgression to generate liability, two elements are required: the act itself and the awareness of it. If both the act and the awareness occur before appointment, Rabbi Shimon holds the individual liable. If the awareness comes only after appointment, he is entirely exempt. The entire deliberation thus turns on precisely when the relevant status takes effect and how long it endures.

### Status Duration

The two offices differ in a critical respect established in prior discussion. The *kohen mashiach*, from the moment he is anointed, retains that status continuously — even during periods when he is not actively performing his duties. The *nasi*, by contrast, holds the relevant status only while he is actively exercising his role. As soon became apparent in that earlier *gemara*, the intermediate period raised its own question: what of the person who is a *kohen* but has not yet formally undergone the inauguration that would make him *kohen mashiach*? Rabbi Shimon addresses exactly this case — if such a person somehow became aware of his transgression in that liminal period, the *tanna kamma* would not hold him liable, since the full status of *kohen mashiach* had not yet been conferred.

### Who is the Nasi

The *mishnah* then asks directly: *eizehu nasi? Zeh melech* — the *nasi* in question is the king. The source is Leviticus 4:22, which speaks of one who violates *mikol mitzvot Hashem elohav* — from all the commandments of the Lord his God — and the *mishnah* reads this as meaning that there is no human authority above him, only God. He is therefore the highest temporal figure, and this identification will be important in the analysis that follows.

## Kal VaComer Deriv

### Leviticus 4:3 Source

The *gemara* asks *mena hani mili* — from where do we derive the rule about sinning before appointment? A *baraita* cites Leviticus 4:3: *im ha-kohen ha-mashiach yecheta* — if the anointed priest will sin. The *baraita* understands this as a *prat*, an exclusion, limiting the *kohen mashiach*'s special liability to transgressions committed while he already holds that status. Any transgression predating his anointment is treated as that of an ordinary person.

### Kal VaComer Rejected

But the *gemara* immediately questions the need for an explicit verse: *ve-halo din hu* — could we not derive this through a *kal va-chomer*? The *nasi* brings an offering only for *shgagat ma'aseh* — the inadvertent performance of an act — and does not bring for his prior transgressions. The *kohen mashiach*, whose threshold for liability is even more demanding (requiring *he'elem davar* — unawareness of the law — together with *shgagat ma'aseh*), should certainly not be liable for prior transgressions. Yet the *gemara* rejects this immediately: the *kal va-chomer* could be reversed. One might equally argue the other direction — the *kohen mashiach* brings a *chatat* for transgressions he committed while in his current status, whereas the *nasi* does not; perhaps therefore the *nasi* is more lenient and the comparison breaks down. The two cases are sufficiently different that neither straightforwardly implies the other, and the *kal va-chomer* cannot be sustained in either direction without challenge.

### Im vs. Asher

The resolution is that Scripture provides explicit language for both cases. Regarding the *kohen mashiach*, the verse says *im* — "if" — *ha-kohen ha-mashiach yecheta*: the conditional "if" signals that the obligation arises only when he transgresses in his current capacity, not for past actions. The *gemara* then concludes: *keshe-hu mashiach mevi, keshe-hu hedyot eino mevi* — when he is *kohen mashiach* he brings the offering, when he was still a *hedyot* he does not bring it.

The parallel is drawn for the *nasi* as well. *Ve-tanya nami gabi nasi ke-hai gavna: asher nasi yecheta — prat le-kodmot.* The verse says *asher nasi yecheta* — that the *nasi* transgresses — and this too excludes prior transgressions, since he must be *nasi* at the time of the sin for the special liability to apply. The push in both directions, from the *kohen mashiach* to the *nasi* and back again, eventually settles by showing that each case requires its own textual grounding, and the back-and-forth confirms that neither figure can fully derive from the other through logical inference alone.

### Asher Nasi Yecheta

The *gemara* now pauses to examine the precise language of the verse *asher nasi yecheta* more carefully, and this is a striking passage. *Yakhol gezeirah* — perhaps the word *asher* ("that") implies an inevitability, a divine decree that the *nasi* must sin? After all, unlike the *kohen mashiach*, where the verse uses *im* ("if") — a pure conditional — the *nasi*'s verse uses *asher*, which in other contexts carries a declarative or resultative force. Is the Torah telling us that it is simply inevitable that the king will transgress?

The *gemara* resolves this by reference to the *kohen mashiach*: *mah le-halan li-kshe-yecheta, af kan li-kshe-yecheta* — just as "if" in the earlier verse means "when and if this occurs," so too *asher* in the *nasi*'s verse means "when this occurs," not that it is decreed to occur. We learn the meaning of *asher* from the parallel context of *im*, even though the words differ.

But the *gemara* presses further: where else might we find a *gezeirah* — a divine decree — that someone will inevitably sin? The answer offered is Leviticus 14:34, the verse about *tzaraat* afflicting homes in the land: *ve-natati nega tzaraat be-veit eretz achuzatchem* — "I will place a plague of *tzaraat* in the houses of the land of your possession." The *gemara* notes, in the name of Rabbi Yehuda, that this is a *bsorah* — a tidings — and we know the tradition that it was ultimately good news, since dismantling these homes would reveal hidden treasures. Nonetheless, on its face it appears as a divine declaration of something that will occur.

Rabbi Shimon, however, limits the scope of this verse: *prat le-nig'ei onsin* — it does not apply to *tzaraat* that comes about through circumstances beyond the person's control, such as through supernatural forces. The *gemara* then asks whether *nig'ei onsin* at all render one impure, since regarding the *zav* the principle is that he does not become impure through *ones*. The comparison is pressed through a *kal va-chomer* between the *zav* and *nega'im*: *mah zav min ha-dibur va-eilach, af nega'im min ha-dibur va-eilach* — both become operative only from the giving of the Torah onward. This argument is rejected, however, because the *zav* does not become impure through *ones* while *nega'im* do, making the comparison asymmetric. The verse *adam ki yihiyeh* in Leviticus 13:2 establishes the *nega'im* rules as applicable from the time of *matan Torah* and going forward.

Rav Papa then distinguishes within Rabbi Shimon's ruling: when Rabbi Shimon exempts *nig'ei onsin*, he means specifically *nig'ei ruchot* — afflictions caused by spirits — and *nig'ei keshafim* — afflictions caused by sorcery. These supernatural causes are categorically different from ordinary circumstances of coercion, and it is only regarding these that Rabbi Shimon holds no impurity is transmitted.

## Choleh & Tzaraat

### Prat L'Choleh

Returning to the *nasi*, a *baraita* derives another limitation from the words *asher nasi yecheta*: *prat le-choleh* — this excludes a *nasi* who is sick. The logic is that if he is ill, he is not functioning as *nasi* at that moment, and the special liability of the *nasi* therefore does not apply. One might ask, however, just how serious must the illness be to remove a person from *nasi* status? Surely a cold or a headache does not suspend the kingship.

### Tzaraat = Spiritual

The *gemara* addresses this directly. *Amar Rav Avdimi bar Chama: prat le-nasi she-nitztzara* — we are not speaking of ordinary illness at all, but of a *nasi* who has been stricken with *tzaraat*. The understanding is that *tzaraat* is a spiritual affliction, not merely a physical one. Something has gone profoundly wrong — not a passing indisposition, but a condition that speaks to the person's very capacity to function in his role.

### Uzziah Story

The *gemara* cites the proof from Kings 2, chapter 15, verse 5: *va-yenagga Hashem et ha-melech va-yehi metzora ad yom moto va-yeshev be-veit ha-chofshit ve-Yotam ben ha-melech al ha-bayit* — God struck the king and he was a *metzora* until the day of his death, and he sat in *beit ha-chofshit*, while his son Yotam was over the royal household. This is the case of King Uzziah of Judah. Once stricken with *tzaraat*, he was removed from active kingship, and it was his son who effectively governed.

### Beit HaChutzfiyot

The *gemara* now pauses on the name *beit ha-chofshit* — the house of freedom — and asks a pointed question: *miklal de-ad hashta eved havah* — if we are now told that he went to the house of freedom, does that imply that until now he was a slave? The answer, and the transition it opens, is precisely yes: Rabban Gamliel will later say to his students, *avdut ani noten lachem* — I am giving you a form of slavery — and the understanding is that *serara*, the position of authority, is not a privilege but a burden. The king sitting in the *beit ha-chofshit* is now free — free from the crushing weight of royal responsibility. The phrase *chofshi min ha-mitzvot*, free from obligations, also resonates here, though the primary meaning in context is freedom from the responsibilities of governance.

## Boat & Comet

### Ship Provisions

The *gemara* develops this theme through the famous story of Rabban Gamliel and Rabbi Yehoshua traveling together on a boat. *Be-hadei de-Rabban Gamliel havah pita, be-hadei de-Rabbi Yehoshua havah pita ve-solta* — Rabban Gamliel had only bread for provisions, while Rabbi Yehoshua had both bread and fine flour, *solet*, something like cream of wheat. When Rabban Gamliel's bread ran out — *shalim pita de-Rabban Gamliel* — he relied on the *solet* that Rabbi Yehoshua had brought. Astonished, he asked him: *mi havah yad'at de-havah lan akuva kulei hai de-aitit solta* — how did you know the journey would be delayed so long that you brought extra provisions?

### Comet Knowledge

Rabbi Yehoshua's answer is remarkable. *Kochav echad le-shiv'im shanah oleh u-mateh et ha-sapanim* — there is a star that rises once every seventy years and confuses the navigators of ships, and he had calculated that it might rise during their voyage and lead them off course, so he came prepared. This is widely recognized as an early reference to what we now call Halley's Comet. According to calculations, the comet passed in the year 66, and this story is attributed to that period. Rabbi Yehoshua knew of this phenomenon and planned accordingly — it was, some have noted, Rabbi Yehoshua's comet long before Halley gave it his name centuries later.

There is also a *mashal* embedded in the story, developed by the *Maharsha*, who connects this narrative with the *aggadot* of Rabbah bar bar Chana at the end of *Sanhedrin*. In that reading, the *sfina* — the boat — represents the turbulent ocean of ideological conflict, and the seventy years alludes to *shivim shnot adam*, the span of a human life. Rabban Gamliel's *pita* represents *Torah*, which is solid and sufficient, while Rabbi Yehoshua's *pita* and *solet* together represent *Torah* augmented by other forms of wisdom — the kind needed to navigate and prevail in arguments with outside movements. The fuller treatment of this *mashal* warrants its own study.

### Gamliel's Question

Rabban Gamliel, deeply impressed by Rabbi Yehoshua's knowledge, exclaims: *kol kach be-yadcha ve-ata oleh be-sfina* — you have such knowledge and yet you travel on a boat like a common person? The implication is that someone of Rabbi Yehoshua's stature deserves far better circumstances.

### Two Students

Rabbi Yehoshua deflects the praise and redirects it: *ad she-ata tameh alai, tameh al shnei talmidim she-yesh lecha ba-yabashah* — rather than marvel at me, marvel at two students you have on dry land. He names them: Rabbi Elazar Chisma and Rabbi Yochanan ben Gudgada. *She-yod'im li-sha'er kama tipot yesh ba-yam* — they can estimate how many drops of water are in the ocean — and yet *ein lahem pat le'echol ve-lo beged lilbosh* — they have not a piece of bread to eat nor a garment to wear. Here are men of extraordinary wisdom and learning, living in absolute poverty.

Rabban Gamliel resolved then and there — *natan da'ato le-hoshivam ba-rosh* — to place them at the head of an academy or a position of communal responsibility. Rashi explains the purpose: *k'dei she-yihyu mitparnesim* — so that they would have *parnassah*, livelihood. The *Tosafot ha-Rosh*, however, notes pointedly: *ve-hoshivam — mai parnassah ika?* — if he is seating them in the *yeshiva*, what *parnassah* is actually there? This tension between Torah leadership and material sustenance was already alive in the *gemara*'s own time.

### Serara as Slavery

When Rabban Gamliel returned from the sea, he sent for them. They did not come — they did not wish for *serara*, for authority or position. He sent again and they came. He then delivered the essential teaching, which connects directly back to King Uzziah and the *beit ha-chofshit*: *ki-mdumin atem she-serara ani noten lachem? Avdut ani noten lachem* — do you think I am giving you authority? I am giving you slavery. The proof text follows immediately.

## Kingdom Split

### Elders' Counsel

The verse cited is from Kings 1, chapter 12, the pivotal moment when the united kingdom stood at its breaking point. The elders spoke to Rehoboam, son of Solomon: *im ha-yom tihyeh eved la-am ha-zeh va-avadtam va-anitam ve-dibarta aleihem devarim tovim ve-hayu lecha avadim kol ha-yamim* — if today you will be a servant to this people, serve them, answer them, speak good things to them, they will be your servants forever. This is the counsel of the elders: genuine leadership is servitude. Authority is not taken but earned through self-abnegation before the people one leads.

### Rehoboam's Hubris

Rehoboam rejected this counsel and delivered his notorious response: *avi yisser etchem ba-shotim va-ani ayasser etchem ba-akrabim* — my father chastised you with whips, but I will chastise you with scorpions. He refused to accept the position of servant-king that his advisors described.

### Schism Result

The consequence was the division of the kingdom. By refusing to serve, Rehoboam lost the unified throne. Jeroboam ben Nevat took the ten northern tribes and established the separate kingdom of Israel, while Rehoboam retained only Judah. The trajectory that followed was devastating: the northern kingdom drifted into idolatry. The lesson is stark — the willingness to accept *serara* as *avdut*, as a burden of service rather than a privilege of power, is what holds a people together.

## Asher → Ashrei

### Ashrei Drasha

The *gemara* returns once more to the words *asher nasi yecheta* and extracts yet another layer of meaning. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakai: ashrei ha-dor she-ha-nasi shelo mevi korban al shigagato* — happy is the generation whose *nasi* brings a sacrifice for his inadvertent transgression. Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakai reads *asher* not as the relative pronoun "that" but hears in it *ashrei* — the formula of happiness or blessing. If the king is the kind of person who brings a *korban* even for an accidental sin, what does that tell you about the generation? *Im nasi shelo mevi korban, tzarich ata lomar mah hedyot* — if even the king brings a sacrifice, of course the common person will do so as well. *Ve-im al shigagato mevi korban, tzarich ata lomar mah zedono* — and if he brings a sacrifice for an inadvertent transgression, how much more will he do *teshuvah* for an intentional one. What an extraordinary standard of moral accountability for a king to set.

### Rava's Pushback

Rava challenges this *drasha* sharply. If we are going to hear *ashrei* every time the Torah uses *asher*, what do we do with the negative appearances of the same word? Jeroboam ben Nevat himself is described with *asher chata va-asher hecheti* — who sinned and caused others to sin. Are we to read that as *ashrei*? The parallelism would be absurd. More pointedly, if the word *asher* alone triggers the *ashrei* reading in Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakai's *drasha*, it should do so consistently across all contexts.

### Context Rules

The resolution is that context determines meaning. Here, in the verse *asher nasi yecheta*, we have an additional textual signal: the verse is being read against the backdrop of *im ha-kohen ha-mashiach yecheta*, where the parallel construction is unmistakably conditional and not a *gezeirah*. It is precisely this deliberate parallelism between the two verses that licenses the *drasha*. The same word *asher* in a completely different context — such as the description of Jeroboam's sin — carries no such interpretive invitation. The *drasha* belongs to this verse, in this context, and cannot be generalized.

## Righteous vs. Wicked

### Eccl 8:14 Cited

Rav Nachman bar Rav Chisda expounds a verse from Kohelet, Ecclesiastes, chapter 8, verse 14: *yesh hevel asher na'aseh al ha-aretz asher yesh tzaddikim asher magia aleihem ke-ma'asei ha-resha'im, ve-yesh resha'im she-magia aleihem ke-ma'asei ha-tzaddikim* — there is a futility that occurs on earth: there are righteous people who receive what would befit the conduct of the wicked, and there are wicked people who receive what would befit the conduct of the righteous. Solomon concludes: I say this too is futility. The translation of *hevel* as "frustration" is offered in some editions, though it is worth noting that *hevel* more typically evokes vanity or nothingness — the famous *hevel havalim* of Kohelet.

### First Interp

The initial *drasha* on this verse reads it as describing a providential arrangement across worlds. *Ashreihem la-tzaddikim she-magia aleihem ke-ma'aseh ha-resha'im shel olam ha-ba ba-olam ha-zeh* — happy are the righteous to whom happens in this world what the wicked will experience in the world to come — i.e., suffering. *Oy lahem la-resha'im she-magia aleihem ke-ma'aseh ha-tzaddikim shel olam ha-ba ba-olam ha-zeh* — and woe to the wicked to whom happens in this world what the righteous will experience in the world to come — i.e., prosperity. On this reading, the righteous suffer now so that their full reward waits intact in the *olam ha-ba*, while the wicked enjoy now and will suffer then.

### Rava's Fix

Rava objects: *atu tzaddiki, i achlei terei olamei misnei lahu?* — is it really harmful for the righteous to enjoy two worlds? Why must they suffer in this one? The framing of the first interpretation implies that enjoyment in *olam ha-zeh* somehow diminishes the reward in *olam ha-ba*, and Rava finds no basis for that assumption. He therefore reframes the *drasha* entirely. *Ella amar Rava: ashreihem la-tzaddikim she-magia aleihem ke-ma'aseh ha-resha'im shel olam ha-zeh ba-olam ha-zeh* — happy are the righteous to whom happens in this world what the wicked experience in this world — that is, prosperity, enjoyment, material success. *Oy lahem la-resha'im she-magia aleihem ke-ma'aseh ha-tzaddikim shel olam ha-zeh ba-olam ha-zeh* — and woe to the wicked to whom happens in this world what many righteous people experience in this world — that is, hardship and suffering. On Rava's reading, the *felicity* belongs entirely in *olam ha-zeh* and the comparison is strictly this-worldly.

### Small Wealth

The *gemara* then illustrates Rava's teaching through an encounter. Rav Pappa and Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua came before Rava. He asked them: had they mastered this tractate and that tractate? They said yes. Had they become a little wealthy — *iatartu purta*? They said yes — *de-zavnan ketina de-ara* — they had purchased a small plot of land. Rava then applied to them the verse: *ashreihem la-tzaddikim she-magia aleihem ke-ma'aseh ha-resha'im shel ha-olam ha-zeh ba-olam ha-zeh* — happy are the righteous to whom happens in this world what the wicked experience in this world.

One of the commentators notes that the key word in Rava's question is *purta* — a little. He did not ask whether they had become rich; he asked whether they had become a *little* rich. And their answer is equally measured — a small plot of land, nothing extravagant. The implication is that modest *parnassah* alongside Torah learning is a blessing, but excessive wealth poses its own danger. The *Meiri* captures this with a striking metaphor: pursuing both Torah and *parnassah* is like trying to hunt two deer simultaneously. Hunting even one deer is extraordinarily difficult — they fast, they are alert, they hide. Chasing two at once approaches the impossible. A measure of worldly success is *ashrei*; unbounded pursuit of it is another thing entirely.

## Hosea Path

### Hosea 14:10

Rabbah bar bar Chana cites Rabbi Yochanan on a verse from Hosea, chapter 14, verse 10: *ki yesharim darchei Hashem, tzaddikim yelchu vam u-fosh'im yikashlu vam* — for the paths of God are straight; the righteous walk in them, but the wicked stumble in them. The provocative feature of this verse is that it is one path. The same road that the righteous walk successfully is the road in which the wicked stumble. This is not two different paths producing two different outcomes. It is the identical path producing opposite results depending on who is walking it. The *gemara* now undertakes to find concrete examples that capture this dynamic — situations where the very same act is a *mitzvah* for the righteous person and a transgression for the wicked one.

### Pesach Example

The first example is the *Pesach* offering. Two people slaughter and eat the *Pesach* sacrifice. One eats it *lishum mitzvah* — for the sake of the commandment. One eats it *lishum achilah gassah* — for the sake of gluttony. For the one who eats it as a *mitzvah*, this is *tzaddikim yelchu vam*. For the one who eats it for gluttony, this is *u-fosh'im yikashlu vam*. But Reish Lakish challenges this illustration: *rasha karit leih?* — you are calling him wicked? *De-nehi de-lo avid mitzvah min ha-muvchar* — granted he did not perform the *mitzvah* in the most elevated manner — *Pesach miha ka-achil* — but he still ate the *Pesach* offering. He fulfilled the commandment, even if not ideally. Calling him a *rasha* for that seems disproportionate. The example is therefore abandoned.

### Wife/Sister

The *gemara* proposes a more dramatic illustration. Two men are each at home with their wife and their sister. In one case, *nizdamna lo ishto* — it so happened that his wife became available to him; in the other, *nizdamna lo achoto* — it so happened that his sister became available to him. For the first man: *tzaddikim yelchu vam*. For the second: *u-fosh'im yikashlu vam*. The same moment of opportunity, the same domestic setting — one path, two outcomes.

This too is rejected. *Mi dami? Anan ka-amrinan chada derech, ve-hacha shtei drachim* — the comparison fails because we are looking for one path, and here there are in fact two: a wife and a sister are two different people, two different circumstances, two different choices. The verse demands a case where it is genuinely the same act, the same path, producing opposite effects.

### Lot's Daughters

The *gemara* arrives at its preferred example: *mashal le-Lot u-shtei benotav*. Lot's daughters, believing themselves to be the last surviving humans on earth, caused their father to drink and lay with him in order to preserve humanity. Their intention was *leshem mitzvah* — for the sake of a commandment, the perpetuation of human life. *Tzaddikim yelchu vam.* Lot himself, the *gemara* maintains, was acting *leshem aveirah* — for the sake of transgression. *U-fosh'im yikashlu vam.* This is the case: the exact same act, the exact same night, one path — and it is a *mitzvah* for the daughters and a transgression for the father.

## Lot Deep Dive

### Lot's Intent

One might immediately push back: perhaps Lot, too, intended a *mitzvah*. He was, after all, drunk. He did not consciously choose anything. Perhaps we should extend him the same charitable reading as we do to his daughters — that whatever happened was for the sake of preserving humanity.

### Entire Story Evil

Rabbi Yochanan will not allow this. *Kol ha-pasuk ha-zeh leshem aveirah ne'emar* — the entire story, from beginning to end, is told for the sake of transgression. And to prove it, Rabbi Yochanan does not stay in chapter 19 of *Bereishit*, where the cave episode occurs. He goes all the way back to chapter 13, to the moment Lot separated from Avram and surveyed the Jordan valley.

### Gaze Analogy

The verse in Genesis 13 reads: *va-yisa Lot et einav va-yar et kol kikar ha-Yarden — ki kulloh mashke* — Lot lifted his eyes and saw the entire plain of the Jordan, that it was all well-watered. Rabbi Yochanan deconstructs this verse through a series of *gezeirot shavot* and parallel expressions that reveal its true moral coloration. *Va-yisa* — he lifted — is compared to *va-tissa eishet adonav et eineiha*, the wife of Potiphar lifting her eyes to gaze upon Joseph with desire. *Et einav* — his eyes — is compared to Samson's declaration, *hi yashrah be-einai*, she is right in my eyes, his desire for a forbidden woman. *Va-yar* — and he saw — parallels *va-yar otah Shechem ben Chamor*, Shechem seeing Dinah before violating her. *Et kol kikar ha-Yarden* — the entire plain — is compared through the word *kikar* to the verse in Proverbs, *ki be'ad isha zonah ad kikar lachem*, describing the man who squanders himself for a prostitute. *Ki kulloh mashke* — that it was all well-watered — is connected to the theme of *pat be-salo*, bread in one's basket, from the Joseph story. Each phrase of Lot's simple geographic observation in chapter 13 is thus shown to carry the imprint of desire and transgression. This is how Rabbi Yochanan reads the whole story as oriented toward *aveirah* from the very outset.

### Second Night

The objection of *ones* — that Lot was simply a victim of circumstances — is addressed by two proofs. First, the Talmud notes that the word *bekumah* in the phrase *ve-lo yada be-shichbah u-ve-kumah* — he did not know when she lay down or when she arose — carries a special textual mark (*nakud*). *Tana mishum Rabbi Yosi bar Rabbi Choni: lama nakud al vav she-be-kuma shel bechirah?* The implication is: *be-shichbah lo yada, aval be-kumah yada* — when she lay down he did not know, but when she arose he did know. His ignorance was not complete.

Second, and more decisive: *nafka mina de-le-fanya acharina lo iba'ei leih le-mishtei* — the practical implication of holding him responsible is that on the second night he should not have drunk again. The first incident might be excused as something that happened to him. But after waking and knowing, he drank a second time. That choice cannot be attributed to *ones*. *Mai de-hava hava* — what had already happened, happened — but the second night was his.

### Separation = Shame

Rava adds a further dimension by expounding a verse from *Mishlei*: *ach nifsha mi-kiryat oz u-midyanim ke-veriach armon* — a brother who has betrayed a strong city, and their contentions are like the bars of a castle. Rava reads the first half as referring to Lot: *she-nifrad me-Avram* — he separated himself from Avram. And the second half — *she-hetil midyanim bein Yisrael le-Ammon* — that he cast conflicts between Israel and the Ammonites. It was Lot's separation from Avraham — his choice to go his own way, to prefer the well-watered plains of Sodom over the company and influence of Avraham — that set in motion the entire tragic sequence culminating in his disgrace. His name, his shame, was made public precisely because his daughter named her son *Moav* — from my father — rather than conceal what happened, as one might expect.

### David's Lineage

Yet the *gemara* does not leave the story in disgrace alone. The daughters' boldness, their willingness to do what they believed was necessary for the perpetuation of life, carried forward into history in a remarkable way. *Lo yavo Amoni u-Moavi bi-khal Hashem* — an Ammonite and a Moabite shall not enter the congregation — and yet King David's great-grandmother is Ruth the Moabite. The halakhic resolution — that the exclusion applies to males only, *Amoni* but not *Amonit*, *Moavi* but not *Moaviyah* — is well known. But some commentators go further, suggesting that precisely this lineage, descending from Lot's daughters through Moav and then through Ruth, contributed something essential to the Davidic dynasty. The kings of Israel could not be built from *chesed* alone; they also needed qualities of boldness, initiative, and the willingness to act decisively in extremity — qualities that the daughters of Lot, for all the circumstances in which they expressed them, possessed in full.
