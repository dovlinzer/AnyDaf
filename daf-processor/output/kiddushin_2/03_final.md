# Kiddushin 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Kiddushin

### Masechet Overview

We begin the final *masechet* of *Nashim*: *Masechet Kiddushin*. Like *Gittin*, this is a tractate studied intensively in *yeshivas*, with a rich tradition of *rishonim* and subsequent literature, owing in part to its practical *halachic* relevance to questions of marriage and *kiddushin*. Yet it is equally important to appreciate the remarkably diverse range of topics it encompasses — a diversity comparable to what we encountered in *Gittin*.

A few introductory observations are in order. *Gittin* contained eight or nine *perakim* and extended to ninety *dapim*. *Kiddushin*, by contrast, has only four *perakim* and runs to eighty *dapim* — nearly the same length with roughly half the *perakim*. Alongside the laws of *kiddushin* proper, one finds discussions of parental and filial obligations, *kibbud av va'em*, and other subjects a reader might not expect to encounter in *Masechet Kiddushin*.

### First Perek Structure

The first *perek* is, by any measure, the largest *perek* in *Shas* — forty *dapim*, half the entire *masechet* — and its scope extends well beyond *kiddushin*. The opening *mishna* reads *ha-isha nikneit b'shalosh drachim*, a woman is acquired in three ways, and by the end of that same *mishna* the topic shifts to *ha-yevamah nikneit*, how a *yevamah* is acquired. From there, the *mishnayot* of the first *perek* proceed systematically: how an *eved ivri* is acquired, how an *eved kena'ani* is acquired, how one purchases real estate, how one acquires chattel — all organized around the theme of *kinyanim*, of transactions and acquisitions. The operative word throughout is *nikneit* — acquired. So while the *masechet* is named *Kiddushin* and is named for the institution of betrothal, the first *perek* is devoted not solely, and perhaps not even primarily, to *kiddushin* itself, but to the broad question of how various things and persons are acquired.

The second *perek* returns to a direct focus on *kiddushin* with the formulation *ha-ish mekadesh*, the man betroths — and immediately after that, *bo u'vshlucho*, through himself or through his agent. Where *Gittin* took the institution of *shlichus* somewhat for granted (while raising particular questions about who qualifies as an agent), *Kiddushin* gives *shlichus* a sustained and direct treatment in the second *perek*. The third *perek* engages with questions of *tna'im*, conditions, which we also encountered extensively in *Gittin*. And the fourth *perek* — *asara yuchasin* — addresses lineage: questions of *mamzerut*, uncertain parentage, and related issues of status. A wide and diverse *masechet*, then, and hence even four *perakim* amounts to eighty *dapim*.

## Ha-Isha Nikneit Mishna

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית בְּשָׁלֹשׁ דְּרָכִים, וְקוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ בִּשְׁתֵּי דְרָכִים. נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, בִּשְׁטָר, וּבְבִיאָה. בְּכֶסֶף: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים בְּדִינָר וּבְשָׁוֶה דִּינָר, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: בִּפְרוּטָה וּבְשָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וְכַמָּה הִיא פְּרוּטָה – אֶחָד מִשְּׁמֹנָה בָּאִיסָּר הָאִיטַלְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A woman is acquired** by, i.e., becomes betrothed to, a man to be his wife **in three ways, and she acquires herself,** i.e., she terminates her marriage, **in two ways.** The mishna elaborates: **She is acquired through money, through a document, and through sexual intercourse.** With regard to a betrothal **through money,** there is a dispute between *tanna'im*: **Beit Shammai say** that she can be acquired **with one dinar or with** anything that is **worth one dinar. And Beit Hillel say:** She can be acquired **with one *peruta*,** a small copper coin, **or with** anything that is **worth one *peruta*.** The mishna further clarifies: **And how much is** the value of **one *peruta*,** by the fixed value of silver? The mishna explains that it is **one-eighth of the Italian *issar*,** which is a small silver coin.

The first *mishna* reads: *ha-isha nikneit b'shalosh drachim*, a woman is acquired in three ways; *v'konah et atzmah b'shtei drachim*, and she acquires herself — her own freedom, if you will — in two ways. Then: *nikneit b'kesef b'shtar u'v'viah*, she is acquired through money, through a document, and through sexual relations.

### Rashi's Explanation

Rashi explains the phrase *v'konah et atzmah* — "acquires herself" — as meaning *lihyot birshutah*, to be under her own control, and specifically *lihinasay l'acher*, to be free to marry someone else. There are, of course, other dimensions to the change in her status — for instance, a husband may annul his wife's vows, and her earnings belong to him. But Rashi focuses on the most fundamental consequence of *kiddushin*: that she is now bound to her husband in the sense that she may not marry or have relations with another man. That is the core change effected by *kiddushin*, and accordingly that is what "she acquires herself" primarily refers to: the dissolution of that bond, restoring her freedom to remarry. The precision of Rashi's formulation will become clearer as we proceed.

### Three Methods

The three modes of *kiddushin* are *kesef*, *shtar*, and *biah* — money, document, and sexual relations.

### Kesef Method

*Kesef* involves giving an object of value accompanied by the declaration *harei at mekudeshet li* — behold, you are betrothed to me. Also essential, though not stated explicitly in the *mishna*, is the requirement of *eidim*, witnesses — not merely to prove that the act occurred, but to constitute it as a legally binding act, just as we saw by *gittin*.

### Ring Practice

The traditional practice of using a ring is not mentioned anywhere in the *Gemara*. Its first recorded appearance is in a work called *Sefer ha-Chilukim*, a Gaonic-era compilation that documents observed differences in practice between the Jews of *Mizrach* — Babylonia — and the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael*. There it states that the Jews of the East did not use a ring in *kiddushin*, whereas the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael* did. This appears to reflect a regional divergence. In any event, neither the Bavli nor the Yerushalmi mentions a ring; *kesef* simply means the giving of some object of value.

### Shtar Method

*Shtar* is a written document — at its simplest, a piece of paper on which is written *harei at mekudeshet li* — given to the woman in the presence of *eidim*. It is worth noting that while *shtar kiddushin* is fully treated as a valid *halachic* mechanism, there is virtually no evidence that it was ever actually practiced. The *Gemara* contains many accounts of *kesef kiddushin* — a man in a marketplace, for instance, handing a woman a basket of fruit and declaring *harei at mekudeshet li* — but no stories involving a *shtar kiddushin*. It appears never to have been used in practice.

One might be tempted to identify the *ketubah* as the *shtar* referred to here, or the *yichud* room as the *biah*. This is a common misunderstanding, but a complete *halachic* error: the *ketubah* and *yichud* pertain to *nisu'in*, not to *kiddushin*. That said, the ancient *ketubot* from the Cairo Genizah are instructive. Scholars such as Mordechai Friedman have noted that some of those documents include language in which the groom declares *harei at mekudeshet li*, and there is reason to think that at least some communities conceptualized the *ketubah* as bearing some relation to a *shtar kiddushin* — even though *halachically* it is not one, and even though the *ketubah* is fundamentally a document recording mutual obligations associated with *nisu'in*.

### Biah Method

*Biah* — sexual relations — constitutes the third mode of *kiddushin*. The witnesses presumably need not be in the room itself. Crucially, it must be an act of sexual relations performed specifically with the intention of creating *kiddushin*, of establishing the couple as betrothed. Not any act of intimacy — the intent must be directed toward this legal and personal transformation.

## Kiddushin vs. Nisu'in

### Kiddushin Defined

The mention of *biah* as a mode of *kiddushin* is an apt occasion to clarify — as it bears repeating — the fundamental distinction between *kiddushin* and *nisu'in*. *Kiddushin* is the act that renders a woman legally a married woman. This means that if she were to accept *kiddushin* from another man, it would have no effect; and if she were to be intimate with another man, it would constitute adultery. However, *kiddushin* does not yet mean that the husband and wife are living together, establishing a household, or having an ongoing sexual relationship. All of that belongs to *nisu'in*, to the moment of *chuppah*, when they actually begin life together.

### Nisu'in Defined

*Nisu'in* is treated in *Masechet Ketubot*, which addresses what constitutes *chuppah*, the obligations it creates — the husband's obligations of *shear, kesut, v'onah* (sustenance, clothing, and conjugal rights) as specified in the *Torah*, as well as the reciprocal obligations running in the other direction, including the wife's earnings going to the husband — and all the legal dimensions of actually establishing a shared household.

### Historical Context

The existence of *kiddushin* and *nisu'in* as two distinct stages reflects a social reality. In the *alte heim*, marriages were often arranged by parents when children were very young. What prevented one side from backing out? In later practice, *tna'im* — formal betrothal agreements with severe financial penalties for withdrawal — served that purpose. But originally, the binding mechanism was *kiddushin* itself. The groom, who had to be of age, would give an object of value, and the legal status of marriage was thereby created — even if the couple would not begin living together for years. The girl could have been betrothed by her father even as a minor. The actual commencement of married life, the *nisu'in*, might follow a decade later.

It is also worth noting that *Masechet Kiddushin* is ordered after *Ketubot* only because it is shorter — four *perakim* versus *Ketubot*'s many more — but conceptually *kiddushin* precedes *nisu'in*, and the ordering reflects size rather than logical sequence. *Sotah*, similarly, follows *Gittin* despite a different conceptual sequence.

### Rashi's Precision

This historical context illuminates the precision of Rashi's earlier comment. When Rashi says *v'konah et atzmah* refers specifically to the ability to marry another man, he is deliberately not mentioning vow annulment or the right to her earnings — because those consequences arise only from *nisu'in*, not from *kiddushin*. The only thing that *kiddushin*, in the absence of *nisu'in*, actually accomplishes is the prohibition on marrying or having relations with another man. All other ways in which a wife is legally under her husband's authority are products of *nisu'in*. Rashi is, as always, precise.

### Biah Distinction

The same precision applies to the *biah* of *kiddushin*. This is not the ongoing sexual relationship that characterizes *nisu'in* and married life. It is a single, discrete act performed with the specific intent of creating the status of *kiddushin* — after which the couple might not begin living together for years. The act of *biah* that constitutes *kiddushin* and the sexual relationship of *nisu'in* are entirely different in kind: the former is a one-time legal act, the latter is the ongoing reality of a shared life.

## Kesef Dispute

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

The *mishna* continues: *nikneit b'kesef* — acquired through *kesef*. What is meant by *kesef*? *Beit Shammai* say: a *dinar* or its equivalent. *Beit Hillel* say: a *perutah* or its equivalent. The *dinar* was the silver coin of the Roman Empire — and this usage of *kesef* was, literally, silver. The additional *chiddush* of the phrase "or its equivalent" is that the object need not actually be a coin or even metal; it need only be of equivalent value.

### Perutah Definition

*Beit Hillel*'s position — a *perutah* or its equivalent — involves a copper coin, or its equivalent. This of course already demonstrates that *kesef* need not mean silver at all. The *mishna* then provides a gloss on *Beit Hillel*'s view: *kamah hi perutah? Echad m'shmoneh b'isar ha-italki* — one-eighth of an Italian *isar*. The sources indicate that the Italian *isar* was itself a copper coin — *nechoshet* — a point worth noting. If *Beit Hillel* were trying to anchor the *perutah* in the language of silver (*kesef*), one would expect the reference coin to be silver. The fact that it is copper reinforces that according to *Beit Hillel*, *kesef* in this context simply means something of value, however minimal.

This also illuminates the structure of the *mishna* itself. The earlier, core formulation — *nikneit b'kesef, b'shtar, u'v'biah* — reads as a tight, apodictic statement. The discussion of *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* is a gloss: a later layer of *tannaitic* commentary asking what that word *kesef* means. And the definition of the *perutah* as one-eighth of an Italian *isar* is, in turn, a gloss upon the gloss. Recognizing this layering is important not only for understanding the *mishna*'s structure but also for appreciating what *kesef* might have meant before *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* introduced their debate.

### Kesef Evolution

Without the *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* gloss, one reads simply: *ha-isha nikneit b'kesef* — a woman is acquired with money. How much money? One might naturally assume something like the *chamishim kesef* of the *Torah* — the standard *mohar ha-betulot*, the fifty silver pieces that constituted the standard bride-price paid, typically to the father, when a virgin was married. That would be a significant, meaningful sum — one that would make the transaction feel genuinely transactional, genuinely like a *kinyan*.

What happened to that fifty *kesef*, which became two hundred *zuz* in rabbinic reckoning? It migrated to the *ketubah*. It became not a sum paid upfront but a sum recorded as a debt, payable upon death or divorce — designed to protect the woman's financial interests and, according to *Chazal*, to slow a husband from divorcing precipitously by ensuring there would be real financial consequences. By moving this amount to the back end, the upfront act of *kiddushin* became symbolic rather than substantial. That is precisely what prompts *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel*: if we are no longer giving the *mohar* upfront, what does *kesef* in *kiddushin* actually mean? A *dinar*? A *perutah*?

Once that shift occurs — once the act of *kiddushin* involves only something symbolic rather than a significant purchase price — the institution stops being referred to as *kinyan* and is called exclusively *kiddushin*. The linguistic change tracks an evolution in how the institution itself is conceptualized.

### Kesef in Context

The contrast with other applications of *kesef* as a mode of acquisition sharpens this point. When the *mishna* later teaches that *nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut* — real estate — is acquired through *kesef, shtar, u'chazakah*, what does *kesef* mean there? Market value. Whatever is being asked for. Nobody imagines that a *perutah* suffices to purchase a house. The *kesef* of real estate acquisition is the actual going rate. So if the opening *mishna* had said simply *ha-isha nikneit b'kesef* without the *Beit Shammai* and *Beit Hillel* gloss, the natural reading would be: whatever the agreed-upon price is — presumably something like the *mohar* of the *Torah*. The introduction of a *dinar* or a *perutah* as the operative amount fundamentally transforms how we understand the nature of this transaction.

## Intro to Kiddushin (II)

### Kinyan vs. Kiddushin

This brings us to a foundational conceptual tension that will run throughout the entire first *perek*: the *kinyan* model versus the *kiddushin* model of understanding marriage.

*Masechet Kiddushin* is named *Kiddushin*. And in every *mishna* throughout the *masechet* — with a single exception — the institution of betrothal is referred to using the language of *kiddushin*: *mekadesh*, *mekudeshet*, *kiddushin*. The one exception is this very first *mishna*, which uses the language of *kinyan*: *nikneit*, acquired.

One model — the *kinyan* model — understands marriage as a form of acquisition. Not the acquisition of a slave in the full sense of an *eved kena'ani*, but an acquisition nonetheless: the man acquires the woman in certain defined ways. What would he be acquiring? Not her labor — that right is rabbinic, not biblical. But perhaps sexual rights: not only are other men prohibited from her, but he has a claim to her. The *Gemara* discusses the husband's *chiyuv* of *onah*, his obligation to have sex with his wife — but notably never derives from a verse that the wife is obligated to have sex with her husband. The obligation is simply assumed, perhaps because it is built into the very concept of what *kinyan* in marriage means. Similarly, children born of the marriage are considered his in a fundamental way — reflected in the *Torah*'s ruling that one who strikes a pregnant woman causing miscarriage pays the husband, not the mother.

The *kiddushin* model, by contrast, is not primarily about possession at all. It is about personal status — the transformation of a woman from single to married, the creation of an exclusive relationship that renders her *asurah* to other men. It is about a status that emerges through a relationship, not about ownership of a person.

The first *perek* of *Kiddushin* will be the arena in which this tension is worked through. The early, tight collections of *mishnayot* — *ha-isha nikneit*, *ha-yevamah nikneit*, *eved ivri*, *eved kena'ani*, *nechasim she-yesh lahem achrayut* — all structured around "here is the object, here are the modes of acquisition," reflect an early stratum in which the *kinyan* model predominated. The later development — moving the substantial monetary payment to the *ketubah*, reducing the upfront amount to something purely symbolic, and renaming the institution *kiddushin* — reflects a fundamental reconceptualization. A ring that is worth a *perutah* does not feel like a purchase price. It feels like a symbol. And once it becomes a symbol, the institution is no longer called *kinyan*. It is called *kiddushin*.

## Nikneit vs. Mekadesh

### Terminology Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּתָנֵי ״הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּתָנֵי ״הָאִישׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ״?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that **a woman** can be **acquired** in three ways. The Gemara asks: **What is different here that** this mishna **teaches: A woman is acquired,** using the language of acquisition, **and what is different there,** in the beginning of the next chapter (42a), **which teaches: A man betroths,** using the language of betrothal?

The *Gemara* opens with a direct question: *mai shna hacha detani ha-isha nikneit u'mai shna hatam detani ha-ish mekadesh?* What is the difference between here, where the *mishna* says *ha-isha nikneit* — the woman is acquired — and there, the second *perek*, where the language is *ha-ish mekadesh* — the man betroths? Why does the language of *kinyan* appear only here?

### Kinyan from Efron

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי כֶּסֶף, וְכֶסֶף מְנָא לַן – גָּמַר ״קִיחָה״ ״קִיחָה״ מִשְּׂדֵה עֶפְרוֹן. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״כִּי יִקַּח אִישׁ אִשָּׁה״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״נָתַתִּי כֶּסֶף הַשָּׂדֶה קַח מִמֶּנִּי״,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation: **Because he wanted to teach** about betrothal through **money,** which is the standard means of exchange in an act of acquisition. **And from where do we** derive that betrothal is accomplished by means of giving **money?** It is **derived** by means of a verbal analogy between the term expressing **taking** stated with regard to betrothal and **from** the term expressing **taking** with regard to **the field of Ephron.** How so? **It is written here,** with regard to marriage: **"When a man takes a woman"** (Deuteronomy 24:1), **and it is written there,** concerning Abraham's purchase of the field of the Cave of Machpelah from Ephron the Hittite: **"I will give money for the field; take it from me"** (Genesis 23:13). This verbal analogy teaches that just as Ephron's field was acquired with money, so too, a woman can be acquired with money.

The *Gemara* answers: *mishum dekaba'i lemisnei kesef* — because the *mishna* is about to discuss *kesef*, and the concept of *kesef* as a mode of acquisition is derived from the language of *kinyan* used in connection with Avraham's purchase of the field of Efron. That is why we open with the vocabulary of acquisition.

### Gezerah Shavah

The derivation is a *gezerah shavah* of *kicha kicha* — the word "taking" appearing in two contexts. *Ketiv hacha: ki yikach ish isha* — when a man takes a woman. *U'ketiv hatam: natatti kesef ha-sadeh kach mimeni* — I have given the money for the field, take it from me. The same root, *kicha*, appears in both the acquisition of a wife and the acquisition of Efron's field. This creates the hermeneutical link between *kiddushin* and the language of *kinyan*.

### Field Acquisition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקִיחָה אִיקְּרִי ״קִנְיָן״, דִּכְתִיב: ״הַשָּׂדֶה אֲשֶׁר קָנָה אַבְרָהָם״. אִי נָמֵי, ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ״, תָּנֵי ״הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** the **taking** of Ephron's field **is called an acquisition** in the Torah, **as it is written** with regard to the same issue: **"The field which Abraham acquired"** (Genesis 25:10). **Alternatively,** it can be proven that purchasing a field with money is called an acquisition from the verse: **"They shall acquire fields with money"** (Jeremiah 32:44). Consequently, as the *tanna* wanted to teach that a woman can be betrothed with money, **he taught: A woman is acquired.** This explains why the terminology of acquisition is used in this mishna.

To complete the *gezerah shavah*: not only is the same word *kicha* used in both contexts, but the field of Efron is explicitly referred to as a *kinyan* — *ha-sadeh asher kanah Avraham*, the field that Avraham purchased. And a further verse confirms that *kesef* is what effects the acquisition of fields: *sadot b'kesef yiknu* — fields are purchased with money. Tosafot raises the possibility that one might argue the money was merely the price, while the actual act of *kinyan* was accomplished through something else — *chazakah*, perhaps. The verse *sadot b'kesef yiknu* forecloses that: the giving of money is itself the act that acquires the field. So when we learn *kiddushin* from this parallel, we know both that *kesef* is a valid mode of acquisition for a wife and that the giving of *kesef* is itself the operative act.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי הָתָם ״הָאִישׁ קוֹנֶה״! מֵעִיקָּרָא תָּנֵי לִישָּׁנָא דְאוֹרָיְיתָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף תָּנֵי לִישָּׁנָא דְרַבָּנַן, וּמַאי לִישָּׁנָא דְרַבָּנַן? – דְּאָסַר לַהּ אַכּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let** the mishna **teach there,** in the next chapter: **A man acquires.** The Gemara explains: **Initially,** the mishna **taught** using **the language of the Torah,** in which betrothal is called taking. **And ultimately,** in the next chapter, **it taught** using **the language of the Sages. And what** is the reason that betrothal is called *kiddushin*, literally, consecration, in the **language of the Sages?** The reason is **that** through betrothal the husband **renders her forbidden to everyone like consecrated** property. Therefore, this act is referred to as consecration.

The *Gemara* then asks: why not be consistent and use *kinyan* language throughout the entire *masechet*? The answer: *me-ikara tani lishna d'Oraita* — at the outset, the *mishna* uses biblical language. *Kinyan*, as we have seen, is the biblical conceptualization, rooted in the *gezerah shavah* from Efron's field. *U'levasof tani lishna d'Rabbanan* — but subsequently, the *mishna* uses rabbinic language. The rabbinic vocabulary is no longer *kinyan* but *kiddushin*. Words are not arbitrary. The shift from *kinyan* to *kiddushin* across the *masechet* reflects a genuine conceptual evolution in how the institution was understood.

## Kiddushin Meaning

### Hekdesh Model

What does the rabbinic term *kiddushin* mean? The *Gemara* explains: *d'asar lah a'kuly alma k'hekdesh* — he renders her forbidden to all other men, as *hekdesh* renders an object forbidden. Just as sanctifying an animal or object to the Temple removes it from the domain of all ordinary use, so too a man who is *mekadesh* a woman renders her off-limits to all other men. This is the core of the *kiddushin* model: not ownership, not possession, but a status of exclusivity — she is removed from the general domain of eligibility.

### Tosafot Relationship

This formulation, while legally precise, is not entirely satisfying as a description of marriage. As one observes immediately: *hekdesh* is off-limits even to the person who sanctified it. If I sanctify an animal, it becomes *asur* to me as well. Furthermore, I can only sanctify something *to* the Temple if I already own it — the act of *hekdesh* flows from prior ownership. But a man does not own the woman before *kiddushin*. So what gives him the right to make her *hekdesh*? And she certainly does not become *asurah* to him.

Tosafot addresses this directly and adds the crucial missing piece. Tosafot asks: in the formula *harei at mekudeshet li*, what does the word *li* — to me — contribute? Tosafot answers: *li yehei li* — she becomes *mekudeshet* to the world *bishvili*, because of her connection to me. Just as one might say *harei hem mekudashim l'Shamayim* — these are sanctified to Heaven — meaning they belong to Heaven and therefore become forbidden to all else, so too *mekudeshet li* means: because you are mine, because you are in relationship with me, you are therefore forbidden to everyone else. Tosafot thus adds the relational dimension: the exclusivity that *kiddushin* creates is grounded not in a transfer of ownership but in a relationship that, once established, forecloses all other relationships.

### Kinyan to Status

What emerges is a fundamental transition: from a world of *kinyan* — possession, control, acquisition of objects — to a world of *kiddushin* — personal status arising through a relationship. The man is still the initiating party; *kiddushin* is still something the man does. But the framework shifts from "I am acquiring her" to "I am entering into an exclusive relationship with her, and the consequence is that she is removed from the domain of all other men." The *li* — the relational dimension — is what drives the prohibition, not an act of possession.

This is reinforced by the comparison to *hekdesh* itself. One might understand *hekdesh* as a transfer of ownership to God. But one can also sanctify an animal as a *korban* — a sacrifice — in a sense that is not simply about God owning the animal in the way a person owns property. The sanctification establishes a relationship of dedication. So too with *kiddushin*: the model of *kiddushin as hekdesh* can be read not as a transfer of ownership but as a dedication — an act of exclusive consecration that establishes a relationship and, as its consequence, shuts out all others.

### Woman's Agency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי הָכָא ״הָאִישׁ קוֹנֶה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנָא סֵיפָא: ״וְקוֹנָה אֶת עַצְמָהּ״ בְּדִידַהּ, תְּנָא נָמֵי רֵישָׁא בְּדִידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to the difference in wording between the two *mishnayot*: **And let it teach here,** as in the following chapter: **A man acquires.** Why does this mishna teach: The woman is acquired, with the woman as the subject of the sentence? The Gemara answers: This is **because** the *tanna* **wanted to teach** in **the latter clause** of the mishna: **And she acquires herself,** which is stated **with regard to her.** Therefore, the *tanna* **also taught** the *halakha* stated **with regard to her** in **the first clause.**

This leads directly to a further point the *Gemara* will make explicit. The *mishna* says *ha-isha nikneit* — the woman is acquired — using the passive voice and making the woman the grammatical subject. The *Gemara* observes that this is not accidental. While the entire structure of the first *perek*'s *mishnayot* focuses on the object being acquired (the woman, the *yevamah*, the *eved ivri*, the *eved kena'ani*, the real estate), the choice to say *ha-isha nikneit* rather than *ha-ish koneh* — the man acquires — has a deliberate legal implication: *kiddushin* requires the woman's *da'at*, her consent and desire. *Mida'atah in, she-lo mida'atah lo* — with her knowledge, yes; without it, no. The *mishna* makes her a subject, not merely an object.

### Voice Answer and Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי ״הָאִישׁ קוֹנֶה וּמַקְנֶה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא מִיתַת הַבַּעַל, דְּלָאו אִיהוּ קָא מַקְנֵי – מִן שְׁמַיָּא הוּא דְּמַקְנִי לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But** if this is the reason, the mishna could have been formulated entirely differently. **Let it teach: The man can acquire** a woman **and transfer** authority, i.e., grant her the release from marriage in the form of a bill of divorce. The Gemara answers: The mishna could not use the expression: Transfer, **because there is** the case of **the husband's death, in** which **it is not he who transfers** authority. Rather, **it is from Heaven that** her freedom **is transferred to her.** Therefore, the mishna could not issue a general statement that the man can actively transfer to the woman her release from marriage.

This is a striking interpretive move. The same *mishna* that uses the language of *kinyan*, and whose derivation is from the purchase of a field, is read by the *Gemara* as emphasizing the woman's subjectivity and agency. The *Gemara* is aware, of course, that in the *Torah* the father could marry off his daughter entirely without her consent. *Chazal* interpreted that power as applying only to a *k'tanah*, a minor — once she reached maturity, she could not be married off without her own participation. And the *Gemara* reads the very language of *ha-isha nikneit* as encoding this requirement of consent, even within a *mishna* that speaks the language of acquisition.

### Medatah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: אִי תְּנָא ״קוֹנֶה״, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ – תְּנָא ״הָאִשָּׁה נִקְנֵית״, דְּמִדַּעְתָּהּ – אִין, שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead another explanation. **If** the mishna had **taught:** The man **acquires** the woman, **I would say** that he can acquire her **even against her will,** as indicated by the expression: He acquires. One might have assumed that the betrothal depends on the husband, without the need for the woman's consent. Therefore the mishna **taught: The woman is acquired,** from which it may be inferred **that with her consent, yes,** he can acquire her as a wife, but **when** he acts **without her consent, no,** she is not betrothed to him.

Because, the *Gemara* responds, when the husband dies, it is not he who releases her — *lav ihu ka-maknei lah, min shamayim hu d'ka-maknei lah* — it is Heaven that releases her. His death is not an act of *makneh* on his part. So the structure cannot be *ha-ish koneh u'makneh*.

But beyond this technical point, the *Gemara* adds a deeper reason: *iba'it eima* — if you prefer, the language *ha-isha nikneit* is used to make a substantive legal point. Had the *mishna* said *ha-ish koneh*, one might have thought that a man could perform *kiddushin* even without the woman's knowledge or against her will — *afilu be'al korchah*. The *mishna* says *ha-isha nikneit* precisely to emphasize that *kiddushin* is only valid *mida'atah* — with her desire and consent — and not *she-lo mida'atah*.

### Father's Rights

This is a striking move by the *stam*. In the *Torah*, *kiddushin* — or at least the *erusin* of the *Torah* — was not *mida'atah* in any general sense. The father married off his daughter; she was not asked. Only in the account of Rivkah's family (*nikra la-na'ara v'nish'ala et piha*) does the *Torah* depict the bride being consulted, and that itself is presented as exceptional. Ordinarily, in the *Torah*, the woman was entirely the object of a transaction between men — father and husband.

*Chazal* limited the father's authority to marry off his daughter to her minority alone. Once she became an adult, she could only be married with her own participation and consent. And what the *Gemara* does here is remarkable: it reads the language of *ha-isha nikneit* — a *mishna* that uses the most objectifying possible vocabulary, the *kinyan* of acquisition — as encoding the requirement of her consent. The same *mishna* that says "the woman is acquired" is read as insisting that she is a subject, not merely an object, that she must desire and agree to what is happening to her. The linguistic vehicle of *kinyan* is being turned, at the moment of its very first deployment, into an affirmation of her agency.

## Shalosh Grammar

### Grammar Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״שָׁלֹשׁ״? לִיתְנֵי ״שְׁלֹשָׁה״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״דֶּרֶךְ״ וְ״דֶרֶךְ״ לְשׁוֹן נְקֵבָה הוּא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהוֹדַעְתָּ לָהֶם אֶת הַדֶּרֶךְ יֵלְכוּ בָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to analyze the style of the mishna: **And why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach: Three [*shalosh*]** ways, formulated in the feminine? **Let it teach: Three [*shelosha*]** ways, formulated in the masculine. The Gemara explains: The mishna uses this form **because it wants to teach** the word **way [*derekh*], and *derekh* is formulated** in the **feminine, as it is written: "And you shall show them the way [*derekh*] in which [*bah*] they must walk"** (Exodus 18:20). The term *bah*, which is referring to *derekh*, is formulated in the feminine.

The *Gemara* now turns to a grammatical question that will occupy the balance of the *daf*: why does the *mishna* say *bi-shalosh drachim* rather than *bi-shlosha drachim*? The number three takes different forms depending on the grammatical gender of the noun it modifies — *shalosh* for feminine nouns, *shlosha* for masculine. If the *mishna* says *shalosh*, it is treating *derech* as feminine. But is *derech* feminine?

### Derech Feminine

Yes: *derech* is a feminine noun. The proof: *v'hoda'ta lahem et ha-derech yelchu vah* — you shall make known to them the path that they shall walk *bah*, using the feminine pronoun *bah*. The feminine agreement of the pronoun confirms that *derech* is feminine, which is why *shalosh* is correct.

### Derech Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: ״בְּשִׁבְעָה דְּרָכִים בּוֹדְקִין אֶת הַזָּב״, נִיתְנֵי ״שֶׁבַע״! מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְמִיתְנֵי ״דֶּרֶךְ״, וְאַשְׁכְּחַן ״דֶּרֶךְ״ דְּאִיקְּרִי לְשׁוֹן זָכָר, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּדֶרֶךְ אֶחָד יֵצְאוּ אֵלֶיךָ וּבְשִׁבְעָה דְרָכִים יָנוּסוּ לְפָנֶיךָ״. אִי הָכִי, קָשׁוּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי! וְקַשְׁיָא נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין אַהֲדָדֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** with regard to **that which is taught** in a mishna (*Nazir* 65b): **One examines a *zav* in seven [*shiva*] ways [*derakhim*],** where *shiva* is formulated in the masculine, **let it teach: Seven [*sheva*]** ways, formulated in the feminine. The Gemara answers: The mishna uses the masculine formulation of the term seven **because it wanted to teach: *Derekh*, and we find** that the word ***derekh* is referred to** in the **masculine form, as it is written: "They shall come out against you one way [*derekh*], and shall flee before you seven [*shiva*] ways"** (Deuteronomy 28:7). The Gemara asks: **If so, the verses contradict each other,** as in one verse the term *derekh* is masculine, and in the other verse it is feminine. **And** furthermore, **the *mishnayot* contradict each other,** as in one mishna *derekh* is masculine while in the other it is feminine.

But then the *Gemara* notes an apparent contradiction. Another verse uses *derech* with masculine agreement: *be-derech echad yetz'u eilecha u've-shiva derachim yanusu lefanecha* — they shall come out against you by one way and flee before you by seven ways. Here *echad* (masculine) is used, not *achat* (feminine), suggesting *derech* is masculine. And there is a corresponding inconsistency in the *mishnayot*: our *mishna* says *shalosh drachim* (feminine), while another *mishna* — about checking a *zav* — says *shiva derachim* (masculine, using *shiva* rather than *sheva*).

So the *pesukim* seem to contradict one another, and the *mishnayot* seem to contradict one another.

### Context Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי לָא קַשְׁיָין: הָכָא, דִּבְתוֹרָה קָאֵי, וְתוֹרָה אִיקְּרִי לְשׁוֹן נְקֵבָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״תּוֹרַת ה׳ תְּמִימָה מְשִׁיבַת נָפֶשׁ״ – כָּתַב לָהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן נְקֵבָה. הָתָם, דִּבְמִלְחָמָה קָאֵי, דְּדַרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת מִלְחָמָה וְאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה לַעֲשׂוֹת מִלְחָמָה – כָּתַב לָהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The verses do not contradict each other. Here,** that verse: "The way in which they must walk" (Exodus 18:20), **is referring to the Torah,** i.e., the way mentioned here is referring to the path of the Torah, **and Torah is referred to** in the **feminine form, as it is written: "The Torah of the Lord is perfect [*temima*], restoring the soul"** (Psalms 19:8). The word *temima* is in the feminine. Consequently, in reference to the Torah the verse **writes:** *Derekh*, **formulated** in the **feminine. There, that** verse: "Shall flee before you seven ways" (Deuteronomy 28:7), **is referring to war,** and **as it is the way of a man to wage war and it is not the way of a woman to wage war,** it is appropriate to speak in the masculine. Therefore, the verse **writes** the word *derekh* **formulated** in the **masculine.**

The *Gemara* resolves: *kra'ei ahadadi lo kashya* — the verses do not actually contradict. The grammatical gender of *derech* is context-dependent. The verse *v'hoda'ta lahem et ha-derech yelchu vah* is speaking about the way of the *Torah*. And *Torah* itself is a feminine noun — *Torat Hashem temima meshivat nafesh*. A *derech* that represents *Torah* takes the feminine form; hence *bah*, and hence *shalosh*. The verse *be-derech echad yetz'u eilecha... be-shiva derachim yanusu* is about war — and war is the domain of men (*darko shel ish la'asot milchama, v'ein darchah shel isha la'asot milchama*). A *derech* that represents the masculine domain of warfare takes the masculine form, hence *echad* and *shiva*.

## Mishnah Application

### Ha-Isha Gender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין אַהֲדָדֵי לָא קַשְׁיָין: הָכָא, דִּלְגַבֵּי אִשָּׁה קָאֵי – קָתָנֵי לַהּ בִּלְשׁוֹן נְקֵבָה. הָתָם, דִּלְגַבֵּי אִישׁ קָאֵי, דְּדַרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ לִיבָּדֵק וְאֵין דַּרְכָּהּ שֶׁל אִשָּׁה לִיבָּדֵק, דְּהָא אִשָּׁה נָמֵי בְּאוֹנֶס מִיטַּמְּאָהּ – תָּנֵי לְשׁוֹן זָכָר.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, the ***mishnayot* do not contradict each other: Here, where it is referring to a woman, the mishna teaches** *derekh* **formulated** in the **feminine. There,** with regard to the examination of a *zav*, **where it is referring to a man, as** it is **common for a man to undergo an examination** to determine if his emission has a cause other than a gonorrhea-like discharge [*ziva*] **but it is not common for a woman to undergo an examination, since,** unlike a man, **a woman is rendered impure even by circumstances beyond** her **control, it taught** and used the word *derekh* **formulated** in the **masculine.** Even if a woman has an emission of blood for a reason other than illness, she is still impure. Consequently, in her case there is no reason for an examination to see what might have caused her discharge.

The same principle resolves the inconsistency between the *mishnayot*. Our *mishna* — *ha-isha nikneit bi-shalosh drachim* — is about a woman. Since the subject is female, *derech* takes its feminine form, and the number three appears as *shalosh*.

### Zav Gender

The *mishna* about the *zav* — *bi-shiva derachim bodkim et ha-zav* — uses the masculine form *shiva* because the checking procedure applies exclusively to men.

### Zav Rationale

Why are only men subject to this examination? Because the investigation of whether a discharge constitutes *zivah* of the *Torah* — contingent on its not having been triggered by sleep, food, accident, or some other external cause — applies only to men. If a woman has a bleeding, she is automatically rendered *tamei*, whether it was caused by an outside force or not. The source of the bleeding does not determine her status. Therefore *d'darko shel ish le-hivadek, v'ein darchah shel isha le-hivadek* — the checking pertains to the male domain — and so *derech* takes masculine agreement: *shiva derachim*, not *sheva derachim*.
