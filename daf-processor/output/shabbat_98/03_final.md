# Shabbat 98 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intent & Carrying

### Eight vs Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא נִתְכַּוֵּון לִזְרוֹק שְׁמֹנֶה וְזָרַק אַרְבַּע — הֲרֵי כָּתַב ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** It is **obvious** that **one who intended to throw** an object **eight** cubits in a public domain **and** actually **threw** it only **four** cubits is liable **because** that case is similar to a case where **one wrote** the word ***shem*,** the first two letters **of** the name **Shimon.**

The Gemara opens with a case it labels *pshita* — obvious. If one intended to throw an object eight *amos* through a *reshus harabim* but it traveled only four, one is *chayav*. The reasoning: *harei kasav shem mi-Shimon* — it is analogous to one who intended to write the name Shimon but wrote only the *shin* and *mem*. One is still *chayav* for writing two letters, because the intent to write the complete name subsumed the intent to write its component letters. Intending to throw eight *amos* includes within it the four, so when the object traveled four, that was encompassed in the original intent.

### Four vs Eight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְכַּוֵּון לִזְרוֹק אַרְבַּע וְזָרַק שְׁמֹנֶה — מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן: הָא אַפֵּיק לֵיהּ, אוֹ דִילְמָא: הֵיכָא דְּבָעֵי, הָא לָא נָח.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the *halakha* if **one intended to throw** an object **four** cubits **and threw** it **eight? Do we say he did** indeed **carry** the object, **or perhaps** we say that ultimately the object **did not land where he wanted** it to land?

The Gemara immediately reverses course and poses the harder question: *niskaven lizrok arba ve'zarak shmonah* — one intended to throw only four *amos* but the object traveled eight. What is the *halacha*?

The Gemara entertains two possibilities. On one side, *ha-apik lei* — one did ultimately remove the object from its place, and perhaps the very significance of four *amos* lies in the concept that four *amos* constitutes a person's personal space. To transport an object four *amos* is to remove it from its space, and whether it came to rest at 4.1 or 8.0 *amos*, that fundamental act of removal was accomplished. On the other side, *heicha d'ba'ei hanacha* — the act of carrying is defined not only by movement but by where the object comes to rest. If the intended resting place was after four *amos* and the object came to rest after eight, the act performed was not the act intended.

### Shimon Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ הֲרֵי כָּתַב ״שֵׁם״ מִשִּׁמְעוֹן, מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם כַּמָּה דְּלָא כְּתִיב ״שֵׁם״ לָא מִכְּתִיב לֵיהּ ״שִׁמְעוֹן״, הָכָא כַּמָּה דְּלָא זָרֵיק אַרְבַּע — לָא מִיזְדַּרְקִי לֵיהּ תַּמְנֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, the first case, which seems obvious, also requires clarification. **And that which you said,** that this is similar to a case where **one wrote** the word ***shem*,** the first two letters **of** the name **Shimon, is** it in fact **similar? There, as long as** the letters of ***shem*,** *shin* and *mem*, **are not written,** the name **Shimon cannot be written. Here,** where one intended to throw the object eight cubits and he threw it only four, is it true that **as long as it was not thrown four** cubits **it cannot be thrown eight?** An object can be thrown eight cubits without first landing after four cubits. The question remains unresolved.

The Gemara notes that this very question was addressed implicitly in the previous discussion, where Ravina answered Rav Ashi's case — in which an object passed from a house into a *reshus harabim* and continued four *amos* — by saying the person is *chayav* only *b'omer kol makom she'tirtzeh tanuach*, when the thrower explicitly declared indifference to where the object would land. This answer is telling. Why was that qualification necessary? If intending to carry more includes carrying less, or if carrying less *mechayev* one for the additional distance as well, one could have answered more simply: the intent to place the object upon entering the *reshus harabim* included the act of crossing the boundary, and therefore one is *chayav* for both. Or conversely, intending to travel four *amos* subsumes the crossing of the line. The fact that the Gemara required the explicit declaration of indifference demonstrates that none of these simpler answers were available — that intent in carrying must be precise.

### Carrying Requires Precise Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ קְרָשִׁים אַפּוּתַיְיהוּ הֲוָה מַנַּח לְהוּ? אַחוּדָּן מַנַּח לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Do you maintain** that they **would place the beams on their width? They would place them on their depth,** which was one cubit wide, and therefore there was a greater distance between the rows.

The Gemara therefore reverses even the initial *pshita*: even if one intended eight and the object traveled only four, one would be *exempt*. And it explains why the *Shimon* analogy fails. *Mi dami?* — how can you compare them? *Hasam*, there, writing *Shimon* certainly includes writing the *shin* and *mem*, because one cannot write *Shimon* without first writing those letters. *Hacha*, here, is it true that one cannot throw eight *amos* without having first thrown four? One might say yes — but the critical distinction is that carrying, unlike writing, is defined by its conclusion. The act of carrying four *amos* means picking something up, walking four *amos*, and putting it down. The act of carrying eight *amos* means picking something up, walking eight *amos*, and putting it down at eight. These are not the same act with one nested inside the other; they are distinct acts, each defined by a specific resting place.

This reflects a well-established principle: carrying places a heightened demand on *kavannah*. Across the *lamed-tet melachos*, we find requirements of *meleches machsheves* — intentional, considered action — but by carrying the concern is particularly acute, likely because carrying is a *melacha* defined more by process than by result. One must intend not merely the act of transporting, but the precise destination. If the object travels further than intended, or less far, or comes to rest in a different location, the act performed differs from the act intended, and one is *patur* — unless one explicitly declared indifference to where the object would land, the scenario of *kol makom she'tirtzeh tanuach*, in which case liability obtains regardless of where it ultimately rests.

## Reshuyot Combining

### Public-Private-Public

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַזּוֹרֵק מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד בָּאֶמְצַע, אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב, פָּחוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** With regard to **one who throws** an object on Shabbat **from the public domain to the** other **public domain through the private domain, he is liable** if he throws an object a total of **four cubits** in both parts of the public domain. If he throws it **less than four cubits,** he is **exempt,** as he is neither liable for carrying from domain to domain nor for carrying in the public domain.

A *Tanna* teaches: if one threw an object from one *reshus harabim* to another *reshus harabim* with a *reshus hayachid* in between — a house, for instance, whose airspace the object passed through — and the object traveled four *amos* in the *reshus harabim*, one is *chayav*; less than four *amos*, one is *patur*.

### Through vs Resting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: רְשׁוּיוֹת מִצְטָרְפוֹת — וּדְלָא אָמְרִינַן קְלוּטָה כְּמָה שֶׁהוּנְּחָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is he teaching us** with this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: **He is teaching us the following** two things. First, that **domains join** together; even though one public domain is separated from the other by a private domain, they are treated as one domain. **And** second, **that we do not say** that **an object in airspace is considered at rest.** The object is not considered to have landed in a private domain, and therefore the one who threw it is exempt.

The Gemara asks: *mai kamashma lan* — what novelty does this teach? Surely if the object traveled four *amos* in a *reshus harabim* one is *chayav*. The Gemara answers with two teachings embedded in the ruling. First, it is *not* teaching that passage through the house triggers liability — that is, we do not rule *kluta k'mi she-huncha*, that the object's passing through the airspace of the house is treated as if it came to rest there. That principle, associated with Rabbi Akiva and others, is here rejected.

### Reshuyos Mitztarfos

The second and remarkable teaching is: *reshuyos mitztarfos* — two separate *reshus harabim* domains combine. If the object traveled two *amos* in the first *reshus harabim*, passed through the house, and traveled two *amos* in the second *reshus harabim*, those two segments combine to constitute the required four *amos*, and one is *chayav*.

This is a striking *chiddush*. Two *amos* in a *reshus harabim* is not merely below the threshold — it is a fully permitted act. One is *mutar l'chatchila* to carry an object fewer than four *amos* in a *reshus harabim*; the prohibition attaches only at four. This is grounded in the concept that four *amos* represents a person's personal space — less than four *amos* and the object has not left its domain. Two such permitted acts, separated by an entirely different domain, somehow combine to constitute a single prohibited act. The *chiddush* is not merely quantitative combination but the joining of two legally independent and individually innocuous movements across a discontinuous path.

One might note an interesting implicit assumption: the Gemara speaks throughout as if a given segment of road can be *reshus harabim* while an adjacent segment, if roofed, is not. The question of whether a partial covering redefines the entire *reshus harabim* is not explicitly addressed here, though we will encounter it shortly. And regarding Rabbi's position — that passing through the house is treated as *hachnasa* and renders one *chayav* for entering — it seems unlikely that Rabbi would simultaneously affirm *reshuyos mitztarfos*, since defining the passage through the house as an independent act of *hachnasa* would be difficult to reconcile with also treating the two segments as halves of a single act of *ha'avara*.

## Roofed Public Domain (II)

### Roofed = Exempt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב אַבָּא, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַב: הַמַּעֲבִיר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְקוֹרֶה — פָּטוּר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה לְדִגְלֵי מִדְבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said** that **Rav Abba said** that **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said: One who carries** an object **four cubits in the covered public domain is exempt because it is not similar to the flags of** the camp of Israel in **the desert,** which were not covered.

Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said in the name of Rav Abba bar Avira in the name of Rav Huna in the name of Rav: *hamavir arba amos b'reshus harabim mikora patur* — one who carries four *amos* in a *reshus harabim* that has a roof overhead is exempt.

This applies to any roofed public thoroughfare — an underpass, a vaulted street, a covered arcade. The ruling has contemporary relevance as well. The *Geonim* derived from the concept that *reshus harabim* must resemble conditions in the desert the requirement that 600,000 people traverse a street for it to constitute a biblical *reshus harabim* — a standard that allows most modern communities to permit an *eiruv*. Where 600,000 people do gather, poskim have considered whether the presence of underpasses or overpasses along a major route — sections that are *mekora* — might suffice to remove the entire street from the category of *reshus harabim*. However, the Gemara here implies nothing so sweeping: it treats the roofed segment as losing its *reshus harabim* status locally, not as disqualifying the broader thoroughfare. As will become apparent momentarily, the Gemara assumes that adjacent unroofed sections retain their *reshus harabim* identity even when roofed sections intervene.

### Not Diglei Midbar

The Gemara's answer is grounded in the principle that a *reshus harabim* derives its legal status from the wilderness encampments of Israel during their desert wanderings. Why is one who carries in a roofed *reshus harabim* exempt? *Lefi she-eina doma le-diglei midbar* — because it does not resemble the encampments of the Israelites in the desert, the paradigmatic *reshus harabim* from which we derive the laws of *hotza'ah*. The desert road was open to the sky. A vaulted space is more like a house; once covered, it loses the defining character of a *reshus harabim*.

This raises an important question for *eiruv* construction. The *tzurat hapetach* — the formal gate-like structure of post and lintel that permits carrying — is predicated on the assumption that we are dealing not with a biblical *reshus harabim* but only with a *karmelit*. If a biblical *reshus harabim* exists, a *tzurat hapetach* is insufficient. The partial *mikora* argument — that some coverage somewhere along the route transforms the entire domain — thus has practical stakes, but the Gemara does not endorse it.

## Roofed Public Domain (II)

### Wagons Were Covered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא עֲגָלוֹת דִּמְקוֹרוֹת הָוְיָין, וְאָמַר רַב מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי חִיָּיא: עֲגָלוֹת, תַּחְתֵּיהֶן וּבֵינֵיהֶן וְצִידֵּיהֶן רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **Is that so? Weren't** the **wagons** on which they transported the beams of the Tabernacle **covered?** The beams formed a covering over the wagons. **And** even so, **Rav said in the name of Rabbi Ḥiyya:** The areas that were **beneath the wagons, and between them, and** on **their sides** are all considered to have been **the public domain.**

The Gemara immediately challenges its own ruling. *Eini?* — is that really true, that the desert camps had no covering? *Ve-ha agalot de-mechusot havu* — but the wagons were covered! Rav in the name of Rabbi Chiyya states: the areas *tachteihen u-veineihen ve-tzideihen* — underneath the wagons, between the wagons, and at their sides — all constituted *reshus harabim*. But this only raises the underlying difficulty: if the wagons bearing the *kerashim* created a covered space, how can one maintain that the desert encampment was the paradigmatic uncovered *reshus harabim*?

## Levite Wagon Setup

### Spaces Around Wagon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רַב בְּדָרָאתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When Rav said** that the space beneath the wagons had the legal status of a public domain, he was referring to when the beams were arranged **in stacks.** The beams did not cover the entire area of the wagon. There was space between the stacks.

The Gemara establishes certain physical assumptions about the wagons. Each wagon was five *amos* long and the beams were ten *amos* long, so each beam overhanging two and a half *amos* on each side. The wagons traveled in pairs, and the beams from each pair of wagons would overhang toward each other — potentially meeting in the middle and creating a roof over the space between them. This would mean that the area between the wagons was in fact covered, directly undermining the claim that the desert road was open.

The Gemara answers: *ki kama Rav bedadarta* — Rav's statement refers to the gaps between the beams as they lay on the wagon. The wagon was five *amos* long; each beam was an *amah* and a half wide (as specified in the Torah). Laid across the width of the wagon, one could fit three beams — taking up four and a half *amos* — leaving a half *amah* to be distributed across the two gaps between the three beams. According to Rashi, the beams were stacked in vertical columns with gaps between them, and those gaps constituted the *reshus harabim*. According to Tosafot, the word *bedadarta* refers specifically to the bottom row before subsequent rows were staggered, at which point the gaps would disappear.

### Three Beams & Gaps

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, אוּרְכָּא דַעֲגָלָה כַּמָּה הֲוַאי — חֲמֵשׁ אַמִּין, פּוּתְיָא דְקֶרֶשׁ כַּמָּה הֲוַאי — אַמְּתָא וּפַלְגָא, כַּמָּה מוֹתֵיב — תְּלָתָא, פָּשׁ לֵיהּ פַּלְגָא דְאַמְּתָא, כִּי שָׁדֵי לֵיהּ מָר בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי — כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **After all, how much was the length of** a **wagon?** It was **five cubits. How much was the width of** a **beam?** It was **a cubit and a half. How many** beams could one **place** on a wagon? One could place **three** stacks of beams, totaling four and a half cubits. If so, **half a cubit** of open space **remained. When the Master distributes** half a cubit **between** the stacks of beams **it is considered *lavud*,** attached, as the space between each stack was less than three handbreadths.

The Gemara, however, immediately challenges this. *Orcha de'agala kama havya* — how long was the wagon? Five *amos*. *Pusya de-kersh kama havya* — how wide was a beam? An *amah* and a half. *Kama mosiv* — how many fit? Three — totaling four and a half *amos*. *Pashlei palga de'amsa* — you are left with half an *amah* for the two gaps, meaning each gap is a *tefach* and a half. And a *tefach* and a half is subject to the rule of *lavud* — objects or surfaces within three *tefachim* of each other are treated as connected. The gaps are thus halakhically non-existent, and the beams are treated as one continuous surface. There is no *reshus harabim* between them.

### Four Beams Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף סוּמְכָא דְקֶרֶשׁ כַּמָּה הָוֵי — אַמְּתָא, כַּמָּה הֲוָה מוֹתִיב — אַרְבְּעָה, פָּשָׁא לַהּ אַמְּתָא, כִּי שָׁדֵי לַהּ מָר בֵּינֵי וּבֵינֵי, כְּלָבוּד דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately, how much was the depth of a beam?** It was **one cubit. How many** stacks **would** they **place?** They would place **four** stacks. **One cubit** of open space **remained. When the Master distributes** one cubit **between** the four stacks of beams **it is considered *lavud*,** as two handbreadths separated each stack.

The Gemara then suggests placing the beams on their depth rather than their width. Each beam was an *amah* deep; placed thus, four could fit on a five-*amah* wagon. Three gaps would divide the remaining one *amah* — six *tefachim* — giving each gap exactly two *tefachim*. But two *tefachim* still falls within *lavud*, so the problem remains. *Sof sof sumcha de-kersh kama havya* — the depth of a beam is one *amah*; *arba* — four fit; *pashla amta* — one *amah* left; *ki shadlei mar beini u'veini* — dividing it among three gaps yields two *tefachim* each — *ke-lavud dami* — still treated as connected.

### Angled Beams Taper

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר קְרָשִׁים מִלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, מִלְּמַעְלָן כָּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין עַד כְּאֶצְבַּע — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה כָּךְ מִלְּמַעְלָן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **This** statement of Rav works out **well according to** the opinion of **the one who said** that **the beams** in the Tabernacle **were one cubit thick at the bottom,** and **they narrowed to a fingerbreadth as they reached the top.** According to that opinion, there was a space larger than three handbreadths at least between the tops of the beams, and therefore the area beneath that part of the wagon was not covered. **However, according to** the opinion of **the one who said** that **just as they were one cubit thick at the bottom, so too,** the beams **were one cubit thick on top, what can be said?** In that case, the space between the stacks was less than three handbreadths, and area beneath the wagon had the legal status of a covered public domain.

The Gemara now introduces the opinion of *leman de-amar krashim mil-matan ovyan ama u-mil-malah kalin ve-holchin ad ke-etzba* — that the beams were one *amah* deep at the base but tapered toward the top until they were only a finger-width in depth. This is the position we will later see associated with Rabbi Yehuda. If the beams taper as they rise, then the portions of the beams extending beyond the wagon become progressively thinner. By the time they overhang the space between the wagons, the beams are nearly nothing — a point — and the gaps between them have grown correspondingly. What was a two-*tefach* gap at the base becomes open airspace above. The *lavud* problem disappears and meaningful uncovered space — *reshus harabim* — exists between the beams.

### Atzbe'i Columns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: בְּאַטְבְּעֵי! אַטְבְּעֵי הֵיכָא מַנַּח לְהוּ — אַגַּבָּא דַעֲגָלָה, עֲגָלָה גּוּפַהּ מְקוֹרָה הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said:** When we said that the underside of the wagon was considered to be a public domain, the statement was not referring to when the beams were stacked on them. When the wagon was empty and consisted of the **frames** that held the beams in place, beneath the wagon was an uncovered public domain. The Gemara asks: But **where would** they **place** the frames? **On top** of the **wagon** when the beams were already stacked on it and the **wagon itself was** already **covered** by the beams, as stated above (*ge'onim*).

For the opinion that the beams maintained uniform depth throughout — *keshem she-mil-matan ovyan ama kach mil-malah ovyan ama* — Rav Kahana offers an alternative: *be-atzbai*. Rashi explains this (with two possible readings) as grouping the beams into combined columns at the two ends of the wagon, rather than distributing them evenly. Instead of four evenly spaced columns with two-*tefach* gaps, two beams are paired at each end, leaving a full *amah* — six *tefachim* — between the central paired groupings. Six *tefachim* exceeds the *lavud* threshold; that central space is halakhically open and qualifies as *reshus harabim*.

The Gemara then asks: *atzbai heichi manchat lehu* — but where exactly are these combined columns placed? *Agava de-agala* — on the floor of the wagon itself.

### Frame Without Floor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲגָלָה גּוּפַהּ מְקוֹרָה הֲוַאי!
>
> **Translation:** But the **wagon itself is** already **covered** by the beams.

This prompts a further question: *agala gufei mikoreh havai* — but the wagon itself is roofed. Even if one has now solved the problem of the gaps between the beams, the entire base of the wagon covers the space beneath it, just as the undercarriage of any vehicle covers the ground below. The Gemara answers: *amar Shmuel bi-teidoth* — Shmuel says the wagons were constructed of pegs or a frame, without a solid floor. The wagon was a structural frame onto which the beams were loaded, but the space between the frame members had no covering. The area beneath the wagon therefore remained open, and the ground-level space under and around the wagon retained its character as *reshus harabim*.

Tosafot raises a broader conceptual objection: given that the majority of the area under the wagon train was in fact covered by the overhanging beams, why focus on the small uncovered gaps as definitive of the *reshus harabim*? The Gemara's implicit answer is that it is the uncovered portion that establishes the domain's identity, not the covered portions created by the cargo in transit. This itself is a significant assumption. One might equally have argued that the structural reality of the space — open sky when the wagons are absent — should define its identity, and the Gemara could have dismissed the entire difficulty on those grounds. It is striking that the Gemara does not take that simpler route, instead engaging seriously with the question of whether the wagon coverings redefine the space beneath them.

## Mishkan Beam Design

### R. Yehuda: Tapered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: קְרָשִׁים מִלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, וּמִלְּמַעְלָן כָּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין עַד כְּאֶצְבַּע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יִהְיוּ תַמִּים עַל רֹאשׁוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״תַּמּוּ נִכְרָתוּ״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** The Tabernacle **beams were one cubit thick at the bottom,** and **they narrowed to a fingerbreadth as they reached the top, as it is stated:** "And they shall match at the bottom, and together **they will be ended [*tamim*] at the top** toward a single ring; so shall it be for them both, they shall form the two corners" (Exodus 26:24). **And below,** when the children of Israel crossed the Jordan River, **it says:** "And those who went down toward the Sea of Arava at the Dead Sea **came to an end [*tamu*]"** (Joshua 3:16). *Tam* means finished or terminated. Here, too, the beams narrowed as they reached the top until they were virtually terminated; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The Gemara now turns from the halakhic implications of the wagon configuration to a description of the *kerashim* themselves. *Tanu Rabbanan*: the beams were one *amah* deep at the base — *mil-matan ovyan ama* — but toward the top they tapered to near-nothing — *mil-malah kalin ve-holchin ad ke-etzba*, until at the top they were only a finger-width. The prooftext: *yihyu tmimim al rosho* — the word *tmimim* is read not as "complete" or "whole" but in the sense of coming to an end, as in *tammu nichratu* and *v'yitmu yemei b'chi evel Moshe* — they conclude, they cease. Thus the beams conclude at the top, coming to a point. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda.

### R. Nechemya: Uniform

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, כָּךְ מִלְּמַעְלָן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַחְדָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya says: Just as they were one cubit thick at the bottom, so too, they were one cubit thick at the top, as it is stated: Together.**

Rabbi Nechemya disagrees: *keshem she-lemattan ovyan ama, kach mil-malah ovyan ama* — the same way they were one *amah* deep at the bottom, so at the top they remained one *amah* deep. The beams were uniform rectangular solids throughout. The prooftext he invokes is *yachdav* — together, meaning one consistent unified form from bottom to top.

Each disputant must address the other's textual support. On the word *tmimim*, Rabbi Nechemya reads it as requiring that each beam be made from a single whole piece of wood — *shlemim ve-lo de-nisra* — complete, not assembled from separate pieces. On the word *yachdav*, Rabbi Yehuda reads it as the simpler instruction that the beams be placed flush and even in a consistent row, not staggered — *d'lo lishlachfinu me-hadadi* — together, aligned.

### Corner Configuration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כְּשֵׁם שֶׁמִּלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה כָּךְ מִלְּמַעְלָן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״וּלְיַרְכְּתֵי הַמִּשְׁכָּן יָמָּה תַּעֲשֶׂה שִׁשָּׁה קְרָשִׁים. וּשְׁנֵי קְרָשִׁים תַּעֲשֶׂה לִמְקֻצְעֹת״ — דְּאָתֵי פּוּתְיָא דְהָנֵי, מְמַלֵּי לֵיהּ לְסוּמְכָא דְהָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **Granted, according to the one who said: Just as they were one cubit thick at the bottom, so too, they were one cubit thick at the top,** it is understandable **why it is written: "And for the back of the Tabernacle westward you shall make six beams. And you shall make two beams for the corners** of the Tabernacle in the back" (Exodus 26:22–23). This means **that the width of these** beams **comes** and **covers the** remaining **thickness of those.**

The Gemara now addresses the corner beams. The Torah specifies six beams for the western wall plus two corner beams — *u-shnei krashim ta'aseh l'miktzo'ot*. The interior of the *Mishkan* was thirty *amos* long by ten *amos* wide. The beams along the length stood side by side, each an *amah* and a half wide. Six beams on the western wall gave nine *amos*; the remaining *amah* on each side was supplied by the corner beams. Each corner beam contributed half an *amah* of interior width to the western face, completing the ten *amos*, while its remaining *amah* of width extended outward, connecting flush with the depth of the lengthwise beams.

This configuration works elegantly according to Rabbi Nechemya, whose beams have uniform depth of one *amah* throughout. *D'ati putya d'hanei u-mamli leh l'sumcha d'hanei* — the width of the corner beam fills in the depth of the lengthwise beam. The corner beam's width on the exterior is one *amah*, the depth of the adjacent lengthwise beam is one *amah*, and they meet perfectly, producing a clean exterior and interior configuration.

### Tapered Corner Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִלְּמַטָּן עוֹבְיָין אַמָּה, מִלְּמַעְלָן כָּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין עַד כְּאֶצְבַּע, הַאי עָיֵיל וְהַאי נָפֵיק! דְּשָׁפֵי לְהוּ כִּי טוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **However, according to the one who said** that **they were one cubit thick at the bottom and they narrowed to a fingerbreadth as they reached the top,** they would not be perfectly aligned, as at the corners **this** beam **goes in and this** beam **goes out.** Part of the beam would protrude out of the Tabernacle. The Gemara answers **that** it was not only the thickness of the beam that narrowed. **One pared** the width of the beams as well so they were sloped **like mountains** and did not protrude.

According to Rabbi Yehuda, however, whose beams taper toward the top, the corner configuration becomes problematic. The corner beam tapers in one direction while the adjacent lengthwise beam tapers in the perpendicular direction. They will not meet cleanly at the top; one will be coming to a point in one direction while the other tapers in a crossing direction, producing an awkward and irregular junction. *Miman kol nevuchadnetzar behai ayeil u-vehai nafak* — one goes in one direction, the other goes out another, and they do not resolve.

The Gemara answers: *de-shapi lehu ke-turei* — at the corners they would fashion the beams to taper in both directions simultaneously, so that the corner beams rose to a point like a mountain peak, meeting the tapering of both adjacent walls cleanly. While no explicit verse mandates this particular treatment of the corners, the Gemara accepts it as the implied solution.

## Pole System

### Central Pole Rings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַבְּרִיחַ הַתִּיכֹן בְּתוֹךְ הַקְּרָשִׁים״, תָּנָא: בְּנֵס הָיָה עוֹמֵד.
>
> **Translation:** Following the dispute over the Tabernacle beams, the Gemara interprets other verses according to the two positions. It is written: **"And the middle bar in the midst of the beams** shall pass through from end to end" (Exodus 26:28). One of the Sages **taught:** It **stood by** means of **a miracle,** as this verse indicates that the middle bar was a single rod that ran along the length and width of the Tabernacle. The middle bar was miraculously bent through the beams on three sides.

*Ve-ha-bariach ha-tichon betoch ha-kerashim* — the Torah describes a central pole running through the rings of the beams. The *kerashim* had rings affixed to their exterior faces, and poles were threaded through these rings to bind the beams together into a stable wall, functioning like long bolts running the length of each wall — preventing the individual beams from swaying.

### Three-Way Miracle

*Tana benes haya omed* — a tradition records that the central pole's placement was miraculous. Rashi explains: the verse implies that a single pole ran through rings in all three directions simultaneously — along the southern wall, the northern wall, and the western wall, turning the corners in one continuous passage, which is physically impossible by natural means. Tosafot notes, however, that the *brayta* of *melechet ha-Mishkan* describes three separate poles running along three separate walls, and on that reading the miracle mentioned is a different one. The Gemara does not resolve this dispute but proceeds with the broader description.

## Mishkan Curtains

### Inner Curtains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְאֶת הַמִּשְׁכָּן תַּעֲשֶׂה עֶשֶׂר יְרִיעֹת ... אֹרֶךְ הַיְרִיעָה הָאַחַת שְׁמֹנֶה וְעֶשְׂרִים בָּאַמָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a verse with regard to the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Neḥemya: **"And you shall make the Tabernacle with ten curtains…the length of each curtain shall be twenty-eight cubits** and the width of each curtain four cubits; all the curtains shall have the same measurement" (Exodus 26:1–2).

The Torah instructs: *ve-es ha-Mishkan ta'aseh eser yeriot* — you shall make ten curtains for the *Mishkan*. *Orech ha-yeri'ah ha-achat shmoneh ve-esrim ba-amah* — each curtain was twenty-eight *amos* long and four *amos* wide. The ten curtains together formed the inner layer of the *Mishkan*'s covering.

### Width-Wise Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁדִי אוּרְכַּיְיהוּ לְפוּתְיָא דְמִשְׁכָּן, כַּמָּה הָוְיָא — עֶשְׂרִין וְתַמְנֵי. דַּל עֲשַׂר לְאִיגָּרָא — פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תֵּשַׁע לְהַאי גִּיסָא וְתֵשַׁע לְהַאי גִּיסָא. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִיגַּלְּיָא אַמָּה דַאֲדָנִים, לְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מִיגַּלְּיָא אַמָּה דִקְרָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Place their length across the width of** the **Tabernacle. How much was** their length? It was **twenty-eight** cubits. **Subtract ten** cubits **for** the width of **the roof** of the Tabernacle, and **nine** cubits **remain on this side, and nine on that side.** According **to Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that the beams narrowed to a fingerbreadth, **the cubit** of the **sockets was exposed,** as the beams were ten cubits high and the bottom cubit of the beams was placed in the sockets. According **to Rabbi Neḥemya,** the cubit at the top of the beams must be added to the overall width of the Tabernacle. In addition to the cubit of the sockets, a **cubit** of the **beams** themselves **was exposed.**

Orienting the curtains lengthwise across the width of the *Mishkan*: each curtain was twenty-eight *amos* long; the interior width of the *Mishkan* was ten *amos*. Subtracting ten for the interior ceiling leaves eighteen *amos*, or nine *amos* falling on each side. The beams were ten *amos* tall and the *adanim* — the silver sockets — added one *amah* at the base.

### R. Yehuda vs R. Nechemya

Here the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Nechemya determines the precise coverage. According to Rabbi Yehuda, whose beams taper toward the top, the curtain placed over the top of the wall need not traverse the full one-*amah* depth of the beam; it slopes down naturally from the tapering apex. The curtain therefore descends nine *amos* on each side, covering the full ten-*amah* beam height and leaving only the *adanim* — the sockets at the base — exposed. *Rav Yehudah megalei amah de-adanim* — according to Rabbi Yehuda, the sockets are the only exposed element.

According to Rabbi Nechemya, whose beams maintain uniform depth, the curtain must first traverse the full one-*amah* depth of the beam at the top before it can begin to descend. This consumes one of the nine *amos*, leaving only eight to fall along the side. *Rav Nechemya megalei amah de-krashim* — an entire *amah* of the beam itself is left exposed, in addition to the exposed socket below.

### Length-Wise Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁדִי פּוּתַיְיהוּ לְאוּרְכֵּהּ דְּמִשְׁכָּן, כַּמָּה הָוְיָא — אַרְבְּעִין. דַּל תְּלָתִין לְאִיגָּרָא — פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ עֲשַׂר, לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, מִכַּסְּיָא אַמָּה דַאֲדָנִים לְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מִיגַּלְּיָא אַמָּה דַאֲדָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Place their width across the length** of the **Tabernacle. How much was** their width? It was **forty** cubits. **Subtract thirty** cubits **for** the length of **the** Tabernacle's **roof** and **ten** cubits **remain.** According **to Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that the beams narrowed to a fingerbreadth, the curtain hung down the western side of the Tabernacle and the **cubit** of the **sockets was covered.** According **to Rabbi Neḥemya,** the **cubit** of the **sockets** was **exposed.**

The same calculation applies along the length. The curtains' combined width — ten curtains of four *amos* each — totaled forty *amos*. The interior of the *Mishkan* was thirty *amos* long. After accounting for thirty *amos* of interior ceiling, ten *amos* remained to drape down the rear wall. According to Rabbi Yehuda the tapering beam required no depth adjustment, and the full ten *amos* descended the back wall, reaching the *adanim* and covering even the sockets. According to Rabbi Nechemya the one-*amah* beam depth consumed one *amah* of the drape, leaving nine descending the rear wall and the sockets exposed.

### Goat Hair Curtains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְעָשִׂיתָ יְרִיעֹת עִזִּים לְאֹהֶל וְגוֹ׳ אֹרֶךְ הַיְרִיעָה הָאַחַת שְׁלֹשִׁים בָּאַמָּה וְגוֹ׳״. שְׁדִי אוּרְכַּיְיהוּ לְפוּתְיָא דְמִשְׁכָּן, כַּמָּה הָוְיָא — תְּלָתִין, דַּל עֲשַׂר לְאִיגָּרָא, פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ עֲשַׂר לְהַאי גִּיסָא וַעֲשַׂר לְהַאי גִּיסָא. לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִיכַּסְּיָא אַמָּה דַאֲדָנִים, לְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מִיגַּלְּיָא אַמָּה דַאֲדָנִים.
>
> **Translation:** It is also written: **"And you shall make curtains from goat hair for a tent** over the Tabernacle; eleven curtains you shall make them. **The length of each curtain shall be thirty cubits** and the width of each curtain four cubits; each of the eleven curtains should have the same measurement" (Exodus 26:7–8). **Place their length across the width of** the **Tabernacle. How much was** their length? It was **thirty** cubits. **Subtract ten for** the width of **the roof** and **there will remain ten on this side and ten on that side.** According **to Rabbi Yehuda,** the **cubit** of the **sockets** was **covered.** According **to Rabbi Neḥemya,** the **cubit** of the **sockets** was **exposed.**

Above the embroidered inner curtains lay a second layer of coarser curtains made of goat's hair: *ve-asita yeriot izim le-ohel*. These differed in two respects: each was thirty *amos* long rather than twenty-eight, and there were eleven rather than ten. Combined width was thus forty-four *amos*; combined length thirty *amos*.

Applying the same width-wise calculation: thirty *amos* oriented across the ten-*amah* interior leaves ten *amos* per side. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the full ten descends each side, *mechase amta de-adanim* — covering even the sockets. According to Rabbi Nechemya, one *amah* is consumed by the beam depth and nine descend, *megalya amta de-adanim* — the sockets remain exposed. This is corroborated by the *brayta*: *v'ha-amah mizeh v'ha-amah mizeh b'odef* — the Torah's explicit mention of the extra *amah* on each side of the goat's hair curtain. According to Rabbi Yehuda, *l'chasot amta de-adanim* — this extra *amah* covers the sockets. According to Rabbi Nechemya, *l'chasot amta de-krashim* — it covers the exposed beam, not the socket.

### Doubled Front Curtain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: ״וְהָאַמָּה מִזֶּה וְהָאַמָּה מִזֶּה בָּעֹדֵף״ — לְכַסּוֹת אַמָּה שֶׁל אֲדָנִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: לְכַסּוֹת אַמָּה שֶׁל קְרָשִׁים. שְׁדִי פּוּתַיְיהוּ לְאוּרְכֵּיהּ דְמִשְׁכָּן, כַּמָּה הָוְיָא? אַרְבְּעִין וְאַרְבְּעֵי. דַּל תְּלָתִין לְאִיגָּרָא — פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ אַרְבֵּסְרֵי. דַּל תַּרְתֵּי לְכַפְלָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְכָפַלְתָּ אֶת הַיְרִיעָה הַשִּׁשִּׁית אֶל מוּל פְּנֵי הָאֹהֶל״ — פָּשׁוּ לְהוּ תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **That was also taught** in a *baraita*. The verse states, **"And the cubit on the one side, and the cubit on the other side of what remains** of the length of the curtains of the tent shall hang over the sides of the Tabernacle, on this side and on that side to cover it" (Exodus 26:13). What remains of the length of the curtains is **to cover the cubit of the sockets;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Neḥemya says:** It is **to cover the cubit of the beams. Place their width across the length** of the **Tabernacle. How much was** their width? It was **forty-four** cubits. **Subtract thirty for the roof,** and **fourteen remain. Subtract two for the doubling** of the sixth curtain, **as it is written: "And you shall double the sixth curtain over the front of the tent"** (Exodus 26:9), and **twelve remain.**

In the length-wise direction, the eleven goat's hair curtains yielded forty-four *amos* combined. Thirty *amos* covered the interior ceiling; fourteen remained. Of these, two *amos* were designated to be folded over at the front — *v'chifalta et ha-yeri'ah ha-shishit el mul pnei ha-ohel* — doubled down over the entrance, leaving twelve *amos* for the rear.

### Train Behind Mishkan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַיְינוּ דִּכְתִיב: ״חֲצִי הַיְרִיעָה הָעֹדֶפֶת תִּסְרַח״. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה, מַאי ״תִּסְרַח״? תִּסְרַח מֵחַבְרוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** according **to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that the beams narrowed to a fingerbreadth, and therefore the top of the beams did not take up any of the width of the curtains, which enabled the curtain to cover the entire wall of the Tabernacle with part of the curtain on the ground, it is understandable why **it is written:** "And as for the overhanging part that remained from the curtains of the tent, **the half curtain that remained shall hang** over the back of the Tabernacle" (Exodus 26:12). **However,** according **to Rabbi Neḥemya,** who maintains that it is necessary for the width of the curtains to cover the thickness at the top of the beams, **what** is the meaning of the phrase **shall hang?** The Gemara answers: It means that **it will hang more than the others.**

Of the twelve *amos* remaining to descend the back wall: according to Rabbi Yehuda, all twelve fell down the rear. Ten covered the full height of the beams; two *amos* extended onto the ground behind the *Mishkan*. This is the referent of the verse *chatzi ha-yeri'ah ha-odefet tisrach* — the half of the extra curtain trails beyond. *Tisrach* connotes extension or trailing.

### Mishkan as Bride

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לְמָה מִשְׁכָּן דּוֹמֶה — לְאִשָּׁה שֶׁמְהַלֶּכֶת בַּשּׁוּק וְשִׁפּוּלֶיהָ מְהַלְּכִין אַחֲרֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** In his opinion, this curtain is two cubits longer than the other curtains covering the Tabernacle. With regard to this, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: To what is the Tabernacle similar?** It is similar **to a woman walking in the marketplace with her skirts following after her.**

According to Rabbi Nechemya, the one-*amah* beam depth consumes one *amah*, eleven descend the rear wall — one covering the socket and ten covering the beam — and only one *amah* trails on the ground. Thus even according to Rabbi Nechemya something trails, though less than according to Rabbi Yehuda. *Tisrach mechavroteha* — it trails beyond its companions, longer than the other curtains, even if only by one *amah* onto the ground.

The Gemara closes with an image from the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *l'ma ha-Mishkan domeh* — to what is the *Mishkan* comparable? *L'isha she-mehalechet ba-shuk ve-shuleha mehalchin achareha* — to a woman — a bride — walking through the marketplace with her train trailing behind her. The doubled-over curtain at the entrance served as a veil; the trailing length at the rear was the train. With this image the daf concludes.
