# Yevamot 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Issur Mitzvah/Ervah

### Four Brothers Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן אֲסוּרָה עַל הָאֶחָד אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — אָסוּר בָּהּ וּמוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי אָסוּר בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **one of** the sisters **was forbidden to one** of the brothers due to a **prohibition against forbidden relatives** because she was a relative of his wife or a relative from his mother's side, then **he is prohibited from** marrying **her but permitted to** marry **her sister.** But **the second** brother, who is not a close relative of either sister, **is prohibited from** marrying **both of them.**

The daf begins at the very last line of 28a, with the conclusion of the *mishna* dealing with four brothers, two of whom died having been married to two sisters. The case under discussion is one in which the surviving brothers face the problem of *achos zekukaso* — the sister of a *zekuka*, a quasi-wife. Ordinarily, because each surviving brother has a *zika* (a quasi-marital bond) to each of the deceased brothers' widows, and those widows are sisters to one another, neither brother can perform *yibum* or *chalitza*, since each woman is the sister of the other's *zekuka*. The *mishna* teaches, however, that if one of the women is an *ervah* to one of the brothers — forbidden to him by a biblical incest prohibition — that *zika* is dissolved, and the other brother may then perform *yibum* with the remaining woman.

The canonical illustration: if Rachel is Levi's daughter, there is no *zika* between Levi and Rachel — she is an *ervah* to him outright — and therefore Leah is not Levi's *achos zekukaso*. He may perform *yibum* with Leah. One might wonder how Rachel and Leah can be sisters if one is Levi's daughter and the other is not. The answer, as the *gemara* will clarify, is that they share the same mother but have different fathers — Rachel's father being Levi himself, and Leah's father being another man.

### Issur Mitzvah vs. Ervah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה וְאִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת! הָתָם, אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה לְחוֹדַהּ, הָכָא, אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה, וַאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva** or **a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity** will be transgressed through the levirate marriage, then the woman must **perform *ḥalitza* and she may not enter into levirate marriage.** The Gemara answers: There is a novelty here: **There,** where the *halakha* is reviewed in general terms, it speaks of **a prohibition due to a mitzva alone.** One woman happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage and it is only as a result of this prohibition that she is prevented from entering levirate marriage. **Here,** there is **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva and,** in addition, **her sister** happened before the *yevamin* for levirate marriage together with her.

The *gemara* on 28b opens by noting that we have already been taught the case of *issur mitzvah* and *issur kedushah* in a simpler context: *issur mitzvah choletzet v'lo misyabbemet* — a woman who is forbidden only by rabbinic prohibition, or by a *lav* such as the prohibition against a *kohen* marrying a *grushah*, does *chalitza* and does not do *yibum*. That earlier teaching addressed the straightforward scenario of a single deceased brother; the present teaching introduces the more complex hybrid case where two sisters are *zekukos* to two surviving brothers, and one of those sisters is an *issur mitzvah* — a rabbinic-level prohibition — to one of the brothers.

### Hava Amina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: לֵיקוּם אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה בִּמְקוֹם אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — וְתִתְיַיבֵּם, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It might enter your mind to say: Let the prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva stand in** the same **place,** i.e., level of severity, **as the prohibition against forbidden relatives.** Consequently, the woman who is forbidden to the *yavam* is considered a forbidden relative and her sister is permitted to him. Despite the fact that the prohibition resulting from a mitzva is not as severe, here it is given equal status, **and** we might say: **Let** her sister **enter into levirate marriage.** Therefore, **this comes to teach us** that this is not the case.

The *gemara* raises a compelling *hava amina*: since the entire problem of *achos zekukaso* is itself only a *derabbanan* prohibition — Rachel was never fully his wife, only a *zekuka*, a quasi-wife — perhaps if Rachel is an *issur derabbanan* to Levi, that should be sufficient to remove her from the picture. After all, we are operating entirely within the rabbinic domain: the *zika* is a rabbinic construct, and Rachel's forbidden status is also rabbinic. Let one *derabbanan* solve another *derabbanan* problem, and allow Levi to perform *yibum* with Leah.

### Rejection of Hava Amina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִתְיַיבֵּם? כֵּיוָן דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא רַמְיָא קַמֵּיהּ, קָא פָגַע בַּאֲחוֹת זְקוּקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why in fact **does** her sister not **enter into levirate marriage?** The Gemara answers: Her sister is forbidden to him **because by Torah** law the prohibited woman **is** still **set before** *the yavam* for levirate marriage. Were he to take the sister in levirate marriage **he would** essentially **be encountering the sister of the woman with whom he has** a levirate bond.

The *gemara* rejects this: *keivan demide'oraita ramya kamei* — since, on the biblical level, Levi still has a *zika* to Rachel, she remains his *zekuka de'oraita*, and Leah is therefore still the sister of his *zekuka* even if the *zika* is only actualized rabbinically. Rachel does not disappear from the scene merely because she is an *issur derabbanan* to him.

### Mishum Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: מִשּׁוּם מִצְוָה עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **It might enter your mind to say** that **due to the mitzva** of levirate marriage, **the Sages did as they did** and nullified their decree prohibiting the sister of a woman with whom the *yavam* has a levirate bond from entering levirate marriage, when the original sister is forbidden only as the result of a mitzva. Therefore, it **teaches us** that they did not cancel their decree in this situation.

One might further suggest: since the entire problem is rabbinic, and the *rabbanan* instituted *achos zekukaso* in service of the *mitzva* of *yibum*, perhaps they would relax it in order to facilitate *yibum* in a case where the woman is already an *issur derabbanan*. The *gemara* dismisses this as well — *kamashma lan* — we are taught that the *rabbanan* would not override the *zika* problem in this fashion.

## Variations & Case Logic

### Pedagogical Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי, הַיְינוּ הָךְ: מָה לִי לְחַד מָה לִי לִתְרֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I need this** *halakha* **as well, for this is** identical to **that** which was taught earlier, when the mishna stated that if one of the sisters was forbidden to one of the brothers due to a prohibition against forbidden relatives, then he is forbidden to marry her but permitted to marry her sister. However, the second brother, who is not a close relative of either sister, is prohibited from marrying both of them. Once the mishna taught that the *yavam* is permitted to marry the woman to whom he is not related, **what is the** difference **to me** if there is **one** brother **or two** brothers? If this happened to both brothers, clearly both should be allowed.

The *gemara* now asks why the *mishna* needed to state both the case where one of the two women is an *ervah* and the case where both women are each an *ervah* to the respective other brother. Is not the second case simply a doubled version of the first? *Hainu hach* — is it not the same point? The *gemara* answers that both cases are pedagogically necessary, and proceeds to explain the distinctive logical function each one serves.

### One Ervah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְועִינַן הָתָם: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא שֵׁנִי דְּקָא מוֹכַח, אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּלֵיכָּא שֵׁנִי — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **necessary** that this be said here as well, **for if it taught us** this *halakha* only **there** in its more general formulation (*Yevamot* 20a), then I might have said: The *yavam* is permitted to marry the sister **because there is no** reason **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to a second** brother. There, there is only one *yavam*, to whom one sister is permitted and the other is forbidden. **Here, however,** in the case of two brothers, **where there is** reason **to issue** a rabbinic **decree due to the second** brother, lest he consummate the levirate marriage as well, **I would say** that we do **not** allow even the first brother to perform levirate marriage, and both sisters-in-law should be forbidden to both brothers.

If the *mishna* had taught only the case where one woman is an *ervah* — say, Rachel is Levi's daughter, and Levi therefore performs *yibum* with Leah — one might fail to recognize that this is only permitted because of the special circumstance of *ervah*. Without the second case to illuminate the point, an observer might conclude that in any such configuration, the surviving brother is simply permitted to perform *yibum* with the other woman, regardless of whether an *ervah* relation is actually present. The singular case, standing alone, might be mistakenly read as establishing a general rule.

### Two Ervos Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמוּעִינַן הָכָא: אַדְּרַבָּה, תַּרְוַויְיהוּ מוֹכְחִי אַהֲדָדֵי, אֲבָל אִידַּךְ — לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the opposite could also be said: **If it taught us** the ruling only **here,** in the case of two brothers, one might have said: **On the contrary, they both indicate** the nature of **each other's** status. Each of the brothers married a specific sister-in-law, i.e., the woman who was not his close relative, indicating he does not have a levirate bond with the other sister. **But in the other** case, where one brother is allowed to consummate the levirate marriage and the second brother is forbidden to both sisters, I might say that **no,** we would not allow marriage to the first brother either. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state the *halakha* in both instances.

The case of two *ervos*, where each woman is an *ervah* to the respective other brother, allows each brother to perform *yibum* with the woman who is not his *ervah*. The presence of the second brother here is itself probative: because Yehuda performs *yibum* with Rachel and Levi performs *yibum* with Leah — each with a different woman — the mutual arrangement makes evident that something special is operative. This cross-pattern demonstrates that only because of the *ervah* relationship is each brother permitted to act.

### Sheni's Evidentiary Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ וְכוּ׳. ״זוֹ הִיא״ לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? לְמַעוֹטֵי אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה לָזֶה וְאִיסּוּר מִצְוָה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna: **And this is** the case that was referred to when **they said:** When her sister is also her *yevama*, she either performs *ḥalitza* or enters into levirate marriage. The Gemara asks: **What does** the expression: **This is,** come to **exclude?** The Gemara answers: **It excludes** the case where there is **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva for this** one **and a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva for that** one. Although each woman is forbidden to a different brother due to a prohibition resulting from a mitzva, they may not both enter into levirate marriage.

The *gemara* introduces the principle of *sheni demuchach* — the second one proves. In the case of two *ervos*, the second brother's restricted behavior demonstrates that a special circumstance accounts for why the first brother is permitted. Each brother's situation illuminates the other's. Conversely, if the *mishna* had taught only the two-*ervah* case, one might think the reason both brothers can perform *yibum* is self-evident from the symmetry alone, without realizing that each *yibum* is contingent on the *ervah* relation operating in that particular direction. The case of one *ervah* is then needed to establish that the permission is not structural but derives from the specific *ervah* status.

This analysis extends further: the *gemara* asks about the final line of the *mishna* — *zu hi she'amru*, "this is what they said" — and concludes that it comes to exclude the case where both women are an *issur mitzvah* to the respective brothers, rather than a true *ervah*. One might have thought that if each brother has a *derabbanan ervah* to one woman, and each can therefore perform *yibum* with the other, the symmetry of the situation should allow it — since neither brother is being disadvantaged and there is no concern about the *sheni*. The *mishna* teaches that even in this symmetric case, the *issur derabbanan* does not suffice to overcome the *zika*, and *yibum* remains forbidden.

## Fifteen Arayos Debate

### All Arayos Apply

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וְכֵן תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: בְּכוּלָּן אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהֶן, הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה, וַאֲחוֹתָהּ כְּשֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ — אוֹ חוֹלֶצֶת אוֹ מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** § Incidental to this *halakha*, the Gemara cites the following statement: **Rav Yehuda said that Rav said, and similarly Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** in a *baraita*: **With regard to all of those** women enumerated in the first mishna of the tractate, who are forbidden to the *yevamin* as forbidden relatives, the following situation could arise: These women could also be two sisters who were married to two brothers who happen before their *yevamin* for levirate marriage while each one is a forbidden relative to one of the *yevamin*. In these situations, **I could apply** the ruling that **she who is forbidden to this** brother **is permitted to that** brother, **as well as** the ruling that **when her sister is also her *yevama* she may either perform *ḥalitza* or enter into levirate marriage.**

The *gemara* now turns to a broader principle, one that may be recalled from early in the *masechet*. A *braita* teaches in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav, and likewise taught by Rabbi Hiyya: *v'chulan ani korei bahen ha'asurah lazeh muteret lazeh v'achosah k'shehi yevimtah o choletzes o misyabbemet* — in all of the fifteen forbidden relationships enumerated in the opening *mishna* of the *masechet*, one can construct the scenario in which the forbidden woman is forbidden to one brother but permitted to the other, and her sister who is simultaneously a sister-in-law may either do *chalitza* or *yibum*.

The puzzle being posed is essentially a combinatorial one: for each of the fifteen *arayos*, can one construct a configuration in which two sisters are each married to two brothers, such that one sister is an *ervah* to one brother but not the other, while the other sister is an *ervah* to the second brother but not the first? The claim is that this can be worked out for all fifteen cases. A simple illustration: if Leah is the mother-in-law of Yehuda (i.e., the mother of Yehuda's wife), she is an *ervah* to Yehuda but has no relationship to Levi. Similarly, Rachel is Levi's mother-in-law but not Yehuda's. Since Rachel and Leah are sisters through their father, the entire configuration is achieved.

### Rav Yehuda Excludes Six

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יְהוּדָה מְתַרְגֵּם מֵחֲמוֹתוֹ וְאֵילָךְ, אֲבָל שִׁיתָּא בָּבֵי דְרֵישָׁא — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rav Yehuda interprets** these principles as applying only to those cases found in the list **from** the **mother-in-law of** the *yavam* **and onward, but not to the six cases at the beginning,** which include his daughter, and his wife's daughter, and their descendants.

However, Rav Yehuda himself restricts the application of this principle. He will only apply it beginning from the case of *chamot* — mother-in-law — and onward through the list. The first six cases of the *mishna* — which all derive from the category of *bat*, daughter — he excludes: *aval sheis bavi d'reisha lo*. He does not wish to teach that these six cases can be applied to the scenario of *achos zekukaso*.

### Daughter Cases Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא — כֵּיוָן דְּבִתּוֹ בְּאוּנְסִין הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, בְּנִשּׂוּאִין לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for Rav Yehuda's distinction? **This is because you find** the scenario where two women who are candidates for levirate marriage are both sisters as well as the daughters of the two *yevamin* only in a case where the women are the daughters of the *yevamin* **through rape, but you cannot find it** in a case where they are his daughters **through marriage.**

The difficulty with the daughter cases is as follows. For Rachel to be Levi's daughter but not Yehuda's daughter, while Rachel and Leah are sisters, they must share the same mother. But if they share the same mother, and Levi fathered Rachel through that woman, then Leah — as the daughter of the same woman — would be Levi's wife's daughter, which is itself a forbidden relationship. The only way to avoid this is if Levi was never married to the mother. In other words, the case of daughter within this framework can only be constructed if the daughter was born out of wedlock. The *gemara* formulates this as: *keivan de'vita ba'onsin hu d'mishkachat lah, benisuin lo mishkachat lah* — the case of daughter is only workable when the child was born through an illicit union, not through marriage. Rav Yehuda, who prefers to speak only of cases that arise through legitimate marriage, excludes all six daughter-derived categories.

### Abayei Includes Daughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי מְתַרְגֵּם אַף בִּתּוֹ (בָּאָה) מֵאֲנוּסָתוֹ, כֵּיוָן דְּאַשְׁכּוֹחֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּאוּנְסִין תֶּיהְוֵי, אִי בָּעֲיָא — בְּנִשּׂוּאִין תִּהְוֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye interprets** these principles as applying **even to his daughter from a woman he raped. Since you** can **find this** scenario, **if** the statement **wants** to deal with cases of **rape, let it; if it wants** to deal with cases of **marriage, let it.**

Abaye disagrees and extends the principle to all fifteen cases, including the daughter categories. His reasoning is pragmatic: *keivan de'ashkachei mishkachat lah, ei ba'onsin teihavei ei benisuin teihavei* — since the scenario is achievable, whether through an out-of-wedlock birth or otherwise, it is legitimate to include it. Abaye is willing to speak even about cases that require positing an illicit origin for the daughter, provided the legal structure works.

### Eishet Achiv Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ — לָא. כֵּיוָן דְּאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הוּא דְּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ — בִּפְלוּגְתָּא לָא קָמַיְירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **However, it cannot** be explained as referring to **the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist.** Even according to the opinion of Abaye, this case cannot be included, **since you can find** a situation where two sisters happen before two brothers for levirate marriage while each woman is the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist only **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, while** such a case **cannot be found according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis.** And Rabbi Ḥiyya **does not deal with** cases that are subject to **dispute.**

Abaye, however, carves out one exception of his own: the case of *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* — the wife of a brother who was not yet born when his brother was alive and married. Abaye says this case cannot be applied to the *achos zekukaso* scenario, because the only way to construct such a configuration is according to the position of Rabbi Shimon, and not according to the *rabbanan*. Since this is a matter of *machloket*, Abaye does not wish to build the scenario on a disputed foundation: *biflugeta lo ka'mira* — I do not speak of a case that is subject to dispute.

## Meis Nolad Yibem

### Initial Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב סָפְרָא מְתַרְגֵּם אַף אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו שֶׁלֹּא הָיָה בְּעוֹלָמוֹ, וּמַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּשִׁיתָּא אַחֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וְסִימָנָיךְ: מֵת נוֹלַד וְיִיבֵּם, מֵת נוֹלַד וְיִיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Safra interprets** these principles as applying to the **wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, and you** can **find this** scenario **in the case** of the **six brothers and according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. And your mnemonic** to remember how this might come about **is** as follows: **Died, born, consummated the levirate marriage; died, born, consummated the levirate marriage.**

The *gemara* then records that Rav Safra disagrees with Abaye on this last point, insisting that the case of *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* can indeed be applied to the *achos zekukaso* scenario — but only according to Rabbi Shimon, and it requires six brothers. The mnemonic given is *meis, nolad, v'yibem; meis, nolad, v'yibem* — died, born, did *yibum*; died, born, did *yibum*. The *gemara* then elaborates the case fully.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רְאוּבֵן וְשִׁמְעוֹן נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, לֵוִי וִיהוּדָה נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי נׇכְרִיּוֹת. מֵת רְאוּבֵן, נוֹלַד יִשָּׂשכָר, וְיִיבֵּם לֵוִי. מֵת שִׁמְעוֹן, נוֹלַד זְבוּלֻן, וְיִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה. מֵתוּ לֵוִי וִיהוּדָה בְּלֹא בָּנִים, וְנָפְלוּ לְהוּ קַמֵּי יִשָּׂשכָר וּזְבוּלֻן.
>
> **Translation:** How could this come about? There were two brothers, **Reuven and Shimon,** who were **married** to **two sisters.** And there were two other brothers, **Levi and Yehuda,** who were **married** to **two unrelated women. Reuven died, Yissakhar was born, and Levi consummated the levirate marriage** with Reuven's wife. Afterward, **Shimon died, Zevulun was born, and Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage** with Shimon's wife. Later, **Levi and Yehuda died childless and** these two sisters **happened before Yissakhar and Zevulun for levirate marriage.**

We begin with four brothers: Reuven and Shimon, who are married to two sisters — Rachel and Leah respectively. A second pair of brothers, Levi and Yehuda, are married to two unrelated women, Devorah and Channah.

### Stage 1: Reuven Dies

In the first stage, Reuven dies. In the second stage, a new brother, Yissachar, is born. Because Yissachar was born after Reuven had already died, Rachel — Reuven's widow — is an *ervah* to Yissachar as *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*: the wife of a brother who was never alive at the same time as him.

### Stage 2: Levi Yibums

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה. וְהָאֲסוּרָה לָזֶה — מוּתֶּרֶת לָזֶה. וַאֲחוֹתָהּ שֶׁהִיא יְבִמְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In this scenario, the woman who had been Reuven's wife **is forbidden to this** brother, Yissakhar, as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, because he was not alive at the same time as Reuven, and yet **she is** nevertheless **permitted to this** brother, Zevulun, because when Zevulun was born this woman was already the wife of Levi, a brother with whom he did coexist. According to Rabbi Shimon, the fact that she had previously been the wife of Reuven, a brother with whom he did not coexist, is not taken into account. Similarly, with regard to Shimon's wife, we find that **she who is forbidden to this** one **is permitted to that** one. Shimon's wife is forbidden to Zevulun as the wife of a brother with whom he did not coexist, but she is nevertheless permitted to Yissakhar. Accordingly, the situation of **her sister who is her *yevama*,** i.e., two sisters who are *yevamot* and yet are allowed to perform levirate marriage, can be applied to this case as well.

In the third stage, Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel. He was already married to Devorah; now he takes Rachel as well through *yibum*. Rachel remains an *ervah* to Yissachar even after Levi's *yibum*, since the *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* prohibition is rooted in the relationship between Rachel and Reuven, not in Rachel's current marital status.

### Stages 3-4: Shimon Dies

In the fourth stage, Shimon dies. In the fifth stage, another new brother, Zevulun, is born. By the time Zevulun is born, Rachel is already married to Levi — she is not available as a *zekuka* to Zevulun, and therefore she is not an *ervah* to him. Leah, however, was Shimon's wife, and since Zevulun was born after Shimon died, Leah is *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* to Zevulun. Leah, by contrast, is not an *ervah* to Yissachar: Shimon and Yissachar were contemporaries, so there is a standard *zika* relationship — a *mitzva* obligation — not an *ervah*.

### Stage 5: Yehuda Yibums

In the sixth stage, Yehuda performs *yibum* with Leah. Now, when Levi and Yehuda die childless, their widows — Rachel and Leah — fall before Yissachar and Zevulun for *yibum*. The result is the desired cross-pattern: Rachel is an *ervah* to Yissachar (*eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*) but permitted to Zevulun; Leah is an *ervah* to Zevulun (*eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo*) but permitted to Yissachar. Thus, *asurah lazeh muteret lazeh v'asurah lazeh muteret lazeh* — each woman is forbidden to one brother and permitted to the other. The full structure of the *achos zekukaso* ruling with *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* is thereby demonstrated.

### Rabbi Shimon Restriction

This construction, as was anticipated, works only according to Rabbi Shimon. The *rabbanan* would hold that even though Rachel was already married to Levi by the time Zevulun was born, Rachel's status as *eishet achiv shelo hayah be'olamo* vis-à-vis Reuven remains intact, and therefore she would be an *ervah* to Zevulun as well. Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, holds that once Rachel entered a fully consummated *yibum* with Levi — who was alive when Reuven was alive — her status with respect to Zevulun is redefined by her current marriage, not by her original connection to Reuven. Hence the scenario only functions according to Rabbi Shimon's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי יִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה? בְּלָא יִיבֵּם יְהוּדָה נָמֵי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need to assume that this is referring to a scenario where **Yehuda consummated the levirate marriage? You can find this** possibility **in** a case where **Yehuda did not consummate the levirate marriage as well.**

The *gemara* then asks why Yehuda's *yibum* with Leah was necessary to the construction, since it seems one could arrive at the same result without him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם צָרָה. הָא תִּינַח צָרָה, צָרָה דְצָרָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **due to the rival wife.** It was taught in this way in order to make the principle applicable not only to the wives, but also to their rival wives. In this scenario, Levi and Yehuda were previously married to two sisters who then became the rival wives of a wife of a brother with whom one did not coexist. This allows the ruling to apply to the case of rival wives as well. The Gemara notes: **This works out well** if the mishna comes to include only **a rival wife** herself, but **what can be said** about **the rival wife of a rival wife?**

The answer is that Yehuda's presence is needed to generate the case of *tzarat ervah* — the co-wife of an *ervah* — as well. Without Yehuda in the picture, both women would be in an *ervah* relationship with the same brother, and there would be no way to introduce the co-wife scenario. The *gemara* further notes that with the addition of two more brothers, Gad and Asher, one can extend the case to include *tzarat tzarah* — the co-wife of a co-wife of an *ervah* — as well, though the details are left to the reader.

## Three Brothers Mishna

### Basic Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶן נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבִתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבַת בִּתָּהּ, אוֹ אִשָּׁה וּבַת בְּנָהּ — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to** close relatives, e.g., **two sisters; or a woman and her daughter; or a woman and her daughter's daughter; or a woman and her son's daughter,** if the two brothers who were married to two close relatives died and their wives happened before a third brother for levirate marriage, **then these** two women must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as each of them is a relative of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond. **And Rabbi Shimon exempts** them even from the obligation to perform *ḥalitza*.

A new *mishna* opens: *sheloshah achim, shenayim meihem nesuim shtei achayos* — three brothers, two of them married to two sisters. The case is structurally similar to the four-brother case discussed previously, now simplified to three brothers. The third brother is *mufneh* — unattached, single.

### Types of Arayos

The *mishna* extends the ruling to apply not only to cases where the two women are sisters but also to any analogous *ervah* relationship between them: *o ishah u-vittah, o ishah u-vas bittah, o ishah u-vas binnah* — or a woman and her daughter, or a woman and her granddaughter (through the daughter or through the son). Any pair of women who could not both be married to the same man may stand in this configuration.

### Consensus Halacha

The baseline ruling of the *mishna* — consistent with what we have established — is that when such a configuration exists, *harei eilu choltzos v'lo misyabbemet*: these women do *chalitza* and do not do *yibum*. Since neither surviving brother can perform *yibum* with either woman without encountering the problem of *zika* to the other, they must each do *chalitza*. This is the standard position of the *rabbanan*, grounded in the rabbinic prohibition of *achos zekukaso*.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן מִן הַחֲלִיצָה וּמִן הַיִּיבּוּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִשָּׁה אֶל אֲחוֹתָהּ לֹא תִקָּח לִצְרוֹר״. בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ צָרוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ — לֹא יְהֵא לְךָ לִיקּוּחִין אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon exempts both of them from *ḥalitza* and from levirate marriage, as it is stated: "And you shall not take a woman to her sister, to be a rival to her"** (Leviticus 18:18). This indicates that **at the time that** a situation arises wherein **they are to become rival wives to each other,** e.g., two sisters happen before one *yavam* for levirate marriage, **you shall not have** the ability **to take even one of them** in marriage. In his opinion, both women are exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza* at the moment that they happen before the *yavam*, just as a forbidden relative is exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

Rabbi Shimon, however, takes a strikingly different position: *Rabbi Shimon poter* — Rabbi Shimon exempts entirely. In his view, both women are completely exempt from both *chalitza* and *yibum*. They simply walk away. This is a remarkable ruling, standing in sharp contrast to the majority position. Where the *rabbanan* treat *achos zekukaso* as a rabbinic prohibition requiring *chalitza* as a safeguard, Rabbi Shimon regards the entire configuration as generating a *de'oraita* exemption from the *yibum* obligation altogether. The *gemara* will proceed to examine his reasoning.

---

## Rabbi Shimon's Basis

### Prooftext Citation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ צָרוֹת זוֹ לָזוֹ — לֹא יְהֵא לְךָ לִיקּוּחִין אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** This indicates that **at the time that** a situation arises wherein **they are to become rival wives to each other,** e.g., two sisters happen before one *yavam* for levirate marriage, **you shall not have** the ability **to take even one of them** in marriage.

The *gemara* cites the *braita*: Rabbi Shimon derives his position from the verse *v'ishah el achota lo tikach litzror* — "you shall not take a woman alongside her sister to create rivalry between them." This verse, which is the source of the prohibition against marrying two sisters simultaneously, is applied by Rabbi Shimon to the *yibum* context as well.

### Tzarot Principle

Rabbi Shimon reasons: *besha'ah shena'asu tzarot zu l'zu* — at the moment when these two women become *tzarot* to one another, co-sisters-in-law, each bound by the *mitzva* of *yibum* to different brothers who have now both died, they are as though they are co-wives competing for the same household. In such circumstances, *lo yihyeh lachem afilu b'achat meihen* — there is no *mitzva* relationship with even one of them. The prohibition of taking two sisters simultaneously extends, for Rabbi Shimon, to the *yibum* context, where the *zika* of each brother to each woman creates a functional co-wife relationship between the two sisters. Therefore, rather than a mere rabbinic enactment, Rabbi Shimon holds that there is a *de'oraita* exemption: the *mitzva* of *yibum* simply does not apply, and consequently neither does the obligation of *chalitza*.

### De'Oraita Status

This is a critical theological distinction. For the *rabbanan*, *achos zekukaso* is a *derabbanan* problem: biblically, one might argue that *yibum* should be performed with one of the women, and the rabbinic enactment merely prevents it for fear of complication. The *rabbanan* therefore require *chalitza* to discharge the residual *zika*. For Rabbi Shimon, the exemption is *de'oraita*: the *yibum* obligation does not arise at all in this configuration, and without a *mitzva* of *yibum*, there is no *yibum* obligation to discharge and therefore no need for *chalitza* either.

### Hava Amina Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת לֹא חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת, לִגְזוֹר מִשּׁוּם שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת דְּעָלְמָא, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary** to state this again here **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon. It might enter your mind to say: Since Rabbi Shimon said** that **two sisters may not perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as the very fact that they require levirate marriage from the same man and stand to become rival wives to each other renders them forbidden, **we should issue** a rabbinic **decree** in this case as well. Although in this case only one of the sisters is eligible for levirate marriage, as the other is a forbidden relative, perhaps there should be a rabbinic decree prohibiting the *yavam* from marrying the permitted sister, **due to** the similarity to the case of **two sisters in general** who happen before him for levirate marriage. **This teaches us** that even Rabbi Shimon does not hold that there is a rabbinic decree in this case.

Given the severity of Rabbi Shimon's position — that this is a *de'oraita* exemption, and that performing *yibum* in such a case would constitute an actual *issur* of *eishet achiv* — one might have thought that even in the case where one of the women is a genuine *ervah* to one of the brothers, Rabbi Shimon would prohibit *yibum* with the other woman as well. The concern would be a *gezeirah*: if we allow *yibum* in this case, people might come to permit *yibum* in the ordinary case of *achos zekukaso*, which for Rabbi Shimon is a *de'oraita* prohibition.

### Rejection Gezeirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה אַחַת מֵהֶן אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו אִיסּוּר עֶרְוָה — אָסוּר בָּהּ, וּמוּתָּר בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one of them was forbidden to him,** the third brother, due to **a prohibition against forbidden relatives,** then **he is prohibited from** marrying **her but is permitted to** marry **her sister.**

The *gemara* rejects this *hava amina*: *kamashma lan* — we are taught that even Rabbi Shimon agrees that when one woman is a genuine *ervah* to one brother, the other brother may perform *yibum* with the remaining woman. There is no *gezeirah* in that case. When one woman is an *ervah* and is entirely removed from the picture, the remaining woman is simply a standard *yevama* to the other brother, and there is no circumstance that could generate a misunderstanding.

## Issur Mitzvah Gezeirah

### Public Perception Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה אוֹ אִיסּוּר קְדוּשָּׁה — חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** However, if one of the women was forbidden due to **a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva or a prohibition** stemming from **sanctity,** then **they** must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna* continues: when one of the women is an *issur mitzvah* — a rabbinic-level prohibition, or a *lav* such as the prohibition against a *kohen* marrying a *grushah* — *choltzos v'lo misyabbemet* — both women do *chalitza* and neither does *yibum*. This ruling applies even according to Rabbi Shimon. The *gemara* notes that this seems surprising: in a plain case of two sisters with no other *issur* present, Rabbi Shimon says they walk away without *chalitza* at all. Why, when one of them is an *issur mitzvah*, should Rabbi Shimon require *chalitza*?

### Grusha/Kohen Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם אִיסּוּר מִצְוָה דְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is **a rabbinic decree due to the general case of a prohibition** resulting from **a mitzva.**

The *gemara* answers: it is a *gezeirah* on account of *issur mitzvah d'alma* — a protective measure for the general category of *issur mitzvah* cases. Consider the following scenario: one of the women happens to be a *grushah* who was married to a *kohen*. Now, if both women are simply allowed to walk away without *chalitza* — as Rabbi Shimon would normally permit in a case of two sisters — observers will not focus on the sisters-and-brothers dimension of the case. They will focus on the specific fact that was salient to them: the woman was a *grushah* and the surviving brother was a *kohen*. They will conclude: "Apparently, when a woman is forbidden as an *issur mitzvah* to a *kohen*, no *chalitza* is required."

### Both Get Chalitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא תִּינַח אִיהִי, אֲחוֹתָהּ מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? גְּזֵירָה אֲחוֹתָהּ מִשּׁוּם לַתָּא דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well** for her, as **she** is forbidden due to the prohibition resulting from a mitzva. It is appropriate to require *ḥalitza* with her, to demonstrate that *ḥalitza* is generally applied in cases where there is a prohibition resulting from a mitzva. But with regard to **her sister, what is there to say?** Why must she perform *ḥalitza* as well? The Gemara answers: There is **a rabbinic decree** with regard to **her sister due to her.**

This would be a catastrophically wrong inference, since the *halacha* requires *chalitza* whenever a woman is an *issur mitzvah* to the *yavam* in a standard *yibum* situation. To prevent this misunderstanding, the *rabbanan* require *chalitza* from both women even in the case of *achos zekukaso* where one is an *issur mitzvah* — specifically so that no one will infer that *issur mitzvah* cases are generally exempt from *chalitza*. The woman who is the *issur mitzvah* must do *chalitza* to remove the fear of a wrong public inference. And since we are requiring *chalitza* from her, the *gezeirah* extends to require *chalitza* from her sister as well.

### Ervah Clarity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא גַּבֵּי עֶרְוָה לָא גָּזְרִינַן? שָׁאנֵי עֶרְוָה דְּמִגְמָר גְּמִירִי לַהּ אִינָשֵׁי, וְקָלָא אִית לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If Rabbi Shimon issues a decree in this case requiring *ḥalitza* for the sister, out of concern for confusion, then there is a difficulty: **But, with regard to a forbidden relative we do not issue a decree** requiring *ḥalitza* due to her sister; why does Rabbi Shimon issue a rabbinic decree requiring *ḥalitza* with a woman forbidden due to a prohibition resulting from a mitzva and not with a woman who is a forbidden relative? The Gemara answers: **Forbidden relatives are different, as people learn** the *halakhot* pertaining to **them** because they are stated explicitly in the Torah and are well known to all, **and** the ruling **has publicity.** Therefore, there is no concern lest people mistakenly confuse this case with cases of *yevamot* who are not sisters. Prohibitions resulting from mitzvot are not, however, explicit in the Torah, nor are they generally known.

The *gemara* then asks: in the case of a real *ervah* — say, the woman was Levi's daughter — Rabbi Shimon does not make a *gezeirah* and does not require *chalitza* from the sister. Why is a *gezeirah* not similarly needed there? The answer: *shani ervah d'mifarsma l'milsa* — the case of *ervah* is publicly known. When the woman is someone's daughter, or another clear *ervah* relationship, the nature of the prohibition is evident to all, and observers will correctly understand why the parties departed without *chalitza*. The confusion that would arise in the *issur mitzvah* case — where the reason is subtle and easily misattributed — does not arise when the *ervah* status is obvious. Therefore the *gezeirah* applies to *issur mitzvah* but not to genuine *ervah*.

## Ma'amar Before Death

### Setup Ma'amar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת וְאֶחָד מוּפְנֶה. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְעָשָׂה בָּהּ מוּפְנֶה מַאֲמָר. וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָחִיו הַשֵּׁנִי —
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two** of whom **were married to two sisters, and one who was single,** the following occurred: **The husband of one of the sisters died** childless, leaving behind his wife, **and the single** brother **performed levirate betrothal [*ma'amar*]** to this wife. The single brother performed an act of betrothal to the *yevama* but did not yet consummate the marriage by engaging in sexual intercourse. **Afterward, the second brother died,** and therefore the second brother's wife, the sister of the betrothed, happened before the single brother for levirate marriage as well.

A new *mishna* introduces a fresh scenario. Three brothers again: two married to two sisters — call them Reuven married to Rachel, and Shimon married to Leah — and a third brother, Levi, who is *mufneh*, unattached. Reuven dies. Now Levi is the *yavam* for Rachel. At this point, before Shimon has died, there is no problem of *achos zekukaso*: Levi can straightforwardly perform *yibum* or *chalitza* with Rachel. Levi, instead of consummating *yibum*, performs *ma'amar* with Rachel — the rabbinic form of *kiddushin* that is a partial fulfillment of the *yibum* obligation. After this, Shimon dies.

### Ma'amar Strength

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ, וְהַלֵּזוּ תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט וּבַחֲלִיצָה, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה. זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אוֹי לוֹ עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאוֹי לוֹ עַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** In this case, **Beit Shammai say: His wife remains with him.** The woman he betrothed is considered like his wife, and he is not required to divorce her. **And this other** woman **leaves** the *yavam* and is exempt from levirate marriage **as the sister of a wife. Beit Hillel say:** Being as he had not yet entered into marriage with the first woman, he is required to perform levirate marriage with both women. Therefore, **he divorces his wife,** i.e., the woman to whom he performed levirate betrothal, **with a bill of divorce,** which nullifies levirate betrothal, **and by *ḥalitza*,** which nullifies the levirate bond. **And,** he sends away **the wife of his** second **brother with *ḥalitza*** as well. They comment: **This is** the case **that** was referred to when the Sages **said: Woe unto him for his wife and woe unto him for the wife of his brother.** Due to the combination of circumstances, he loses them both.

The question now before us is whether the *ma'amar* that Levi performed with Rachel is strong enough to alter the legal reality. Specifically: does the *ma'amar*, combined with the original *zika*, create a bond sufficiently robust to treat Rachel as Levi's wife for *de'oraita* purposes? If so, then once Shimon dies and Leah falls before Levi, Leah is the sister of Levi's wife — *achos isha* — and would simply depart without *chalitza*. If not, then Leah remains Levi's *zekuka* and there remains a *yibum* obligation to her, which cannot be discharged without *chalitza*.

There are really two separate questions embedded here. First, at the rabbinic level: does the *ma'amar* suffice to overcome the *derabbanan* problem of *achos zekukaso*, so that Levi may proceed to consummate *yibum* with Rachel? Second, at the *de'oraita* level: has the *ma'amar* transformed Rachel's status into that of a full wife, such that Leah — as the sister of Levi's wife — requires no *chalitza* at all?

### Beit Shammai View

Beit Shammai rules: *ishto imo v'halazu taitzei mishum achos isha* — Rachel is his wife, and Leah departs on account of being the sister of a wife. Beit Shammai grants the *ma'amar* full operative weight: it is sufficient to definitively establish Rachel as Levi's wife at the *de'oraita* level, such that Leah — now the sister of a married woman — is simply exempt from both *yibum* and *chalitza*. There is no *mitzva* and no need to discharge any bond.

### Beit Hillel View

Beit Hillel rules otherwise: *motzi et ishto be-get u-ve-chalitza, v'eshet achiv be-chalitza* — Levi must give Rachel a *get* on account of the *ma'amar*, and also *chalitza* on account of the residual *zika*; and he must give Leah *chalitza* as well. Beit Hillel refuses to grant the *ma'amar* the weight that Beit Shammai does. At the *de'oraita* level, Rachel is not yet Levi's wife in any full sense; the *zika* has not been converted into actual marriage. Therefore Leah still has a *zika* to Levi, which requires *chalitza*. And Rachel, whose relationship with Levi was deepened by the *ma'amar*, requires a *get* to dissolve the rabbinic *kiddushin*, plus *chalitza* to dissolve the *de'oraita* *zika*. Now, moreover, since Shimon has died and Leah is in the picture, the *achos zekukaso* problem is fully active: Levi cannot consummate with Rachel because Leah is her sister and Leah is his *zekuka*. Both women require *chalitza*; neither can be taken in *yibum*.

### Consequences Harm

This leads to the poignant statement of the *mishna*: *oy li al ishto v'oy li al eshet achiv* — woe to his wife and woe to his brother's wife. According to Beit Hillel, both Rachel and Leah are harmed. Rachel, who had the *ma'amar* and was on her way to being fully married, cannot be taken in *yibum* because of *achos zekukaso*; she must take a *get* and *chalitza* and goes away with nothing. Leah, who did nothing, is trapped and must also receive *chalitza* before she can remarry. According to Beit Shammai, by contrast, Levi consummates his marriage with Rachel and Leah departs freely.

One might ask — as indeed arises in the *gemara* — how, according to Beit Hillel, the *ma'amar* can be strong enough to require a *get* but not strong enough to solve the *achos zekukaso* problem. The answer, as the *gemara* makes clear, is that the *ma'amar* operates only at the rabbinic level, generating the rabbinic parallel to *kiddushin* that requires a *get* to dissolve — but the *de'oraita* *zika* to Leah persists and is not resolved by any amount of rabbinic *kiddushin* with Rachel. The *gemara* had already established this principle earlier: a *derabbanan ervah* does not resolve a *derabbanan* problem of *achos zekukaso* if the *de'oraita* *zika* remains intact.

## Ma'amar Analysis

### Partial Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לָא תֵּימָא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר, דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי סַגִּי לַהּ בְּגִיטָּא, אֶלָּא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the main issue of levirate betrothal, it would seem from this mishna that Beit Shammai hold that levirate betrothal has the legal force of levirate marriage. However, **Rabbi Elazar said: Do not say** that **levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **as a full-fledged acquisition according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** such that the first woman becomes his wife to the extent **that if he wants to divorce** her **a bill of divorce would suffice** without an act of *ḥalitza*. **Rather, for Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes a rival wife** from entering levirate marriage. The legal force of levirate betrothal is limited to preventing the rival wife of the sister from performing levirate marriage; she is exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

Rabbi Elazar now clarifies the position of Beit Shammai more precisely. One should not say that *ma'amar*, according to Beit Shammai, creates a *kinyan gamur* — a complete, total acquisition — in the full sense of *yibum* consummation.

### Divorce Test

The test case is divorce. When a man consummated *yibum* with his *yevama* and subsequently wishes to divorce her, a *get* alone suffices — *chalitza* is not needed once the marriage has been consummated. If *ma'amar*, according to Beit Shammai, were a complete *kinyan gamur*, then dissolving it should similarly require only a *get*. But that is not the case.

### Partial Effect

Rather, the *ma'amar* according to Beit Shammai is sufficient to push Leah away and to permit consummation with Rachel — but it does not fully transform Rachel's legal status in every dimension. The relationship, while operative enough to displace the co-sister-in-law, has not fully converted from *yibum*-track to ordinary-marriage-track in every respect.

### Ma'amar Dachaya

Rabbi Elazar articulates the principle: *ma'amar le-Beit Shammai eino koneh ella lidchot be-tzarah bilvad* — the *ma'amar*, according to Beit Shammai, acquires only to the extent of pushing off the co-woman. This is *ma'amar dachaya*: the *ma'amar* operates by displacing Leah's claim rather than by fully constituting Rachel as Levi's wife in the ordinary sense. The relationship with Rachel has been meaningfully strengthened by the *ma'amar*, but it has not crossed the threshold of a *kinyan gamur* in the fullest sense.

## Ma'amar Solution Problem

### Why Not Ma'amar?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? מַאֲמָר דְּהֵיתֵרָא דַחִיא — אֲבָל מַאֲמָר דְּאִסּוּרָא לָא דַחִיא.
>
> **Translation:** [This would be inferred from a principle found elsewhere:] The *gemara* answers by drawing a fundamental distinction: *ma'amar deheitera dachi* — only a *ma'amar* that was performed permissibly, without a competing *zika* problem at the time it was done, can have the effect of displacing the co-woman.

A sharp question now arises. If *ma'amar*, according to Beit Shammai, is strong enough to push Leah off the scene and allow Levi to consummate with Rachel — then why, in a standard case of *achos zekukaso* where no *ma'amar* has yet been done, should Levi not simply perform *ma'amar* with Rachel first, thereby displacing Leah, and then consummate *yibum* with Rachel? This would solve the entire *achos zekukaso* problem from the outset, without the need to suffer through the situation at all.

### Deheitera vs. Issura

In the case in our *mishna*, the *ma'amar* was performed with Rachel while Shimon was still alive. At that moment, there was no problem: Levi had a *zika* only to Rachel, Shimon was still married to Leah, and there was no *achos zekukaso* difficulty. The *ma'amar* was therefore a *ma'amar deheitera* — a permissible *ma'amar* done in legitimate circumstances. It is this *ma'amar* that, according to Beit Shammai, retains its effect even after Shimon dies and Leah enters the picture.

By contrast, if Levi were to perform *ma'amar* with Rachel after both brothers have already died — when there is already a *zika* to Leah and a *zika* to Rachel simultaneously, and *yibum* with Rachel is prohibited because of *achos zekukaso* — that *ma'amar* would be a *ma'amar de'issura*, performed in circumstances where the underlying *yibum* is forbidden. Such a *ma'amar* cannot have operative legal effect.

### Ma'amar Works Cleanly

The principle is that *ma'amar* is only meaningful when it is performed in a context where *yibum* itself would be permitted. The *ma'amar* is the rabbinic anticipation of *yibum* — a partial enactment of the relationship. If the relationship itself cannot be consummated because of a competing *zika* and the *achos zekukaso* prohibition, the *ma'amar* cannot bootstrap itself into creating legal reality.

### Zika Interference

Once Leah's *zika* is on the scene and *yibum* with Rachel is prohibited by reason of *achos zekukaso*, performing *ma'amar* with Rachel is itself problematic — it is a *ma'amar de'issura*. Such a *ma'amar* cannot create full marital status and cannot displace Leah's claim. The effect of *ma'amar* depends entirely on the legitimacy of the context in which it was done. This is why the *mishna*'s case is one where the *ma'amar* was done before the second brother died — that timing is not incidental but is the entire legal basis on which Beit Shammai's ruling operates.

## Ma'amar Status TBD

### Kinyan Degree

Having established that the *ma'amar* in the *mishna* was a *ma'amar deheitera* and that this is why it has operative effect according to Beit Shammai, the question remains: what precisely is the weight of that *ma'amar*? Does it create full marital status — a *kinyan gamur* — such that if Levi and Rachel subsequently wished to part ways, only a *get* would be needed? Or is it a partial status, sufficient to displace Leah, but insufficient to fully transform the relationship — so that *chalitza* would still be required in addition to a *get* if dissolution were sought?

### Divorce Implications

This question is not merely theoretical. It determines the practical consequences for the couple. If the *ma'amar deheitera* creates a *kinyan gamur*, then Levi and Rachel are fully married once he consummates, Leah walks away, and any future divorce would require only a *get*. If the *ma'amar deheitera* creates only a partial *kinyan* — enough to displace Leah but not enough to fully convert the relationship — then even after consummation, *chalitza* would remain a requirement should they ever divorce.

### Ongoing Debate

Rabbi Elazar's initial formulation — *ma'amar le-Beit Shammai eino koneh ella lidchot be-tzarah bilvad* — suggests it is not a full *kinyan*. However, the full weight of that ruling, and whether Beit Shammai could be read as granting a complete *kinyan gamur* under some interpretation, remains a point of ongoing discussion in the *gemara*. The daf concludes with Rav Ashi's version of Rabbi Elazar's statement beginning to be introduced, and the full analysis of the *ma'amar*'s degree of acquisition according to Beit Shammai will continue in the next shiur.
