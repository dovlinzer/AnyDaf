# Hullin 136 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Hefsek Taharah Basics

### Hefsek vs. Weekly Checks

The *hefsek taharah* is fundamentally different from the *bedikot* a woman performs throughout the *shiva nekiyim*. The distinction lies in purpose: the *hefsek taharah* is performed to establish that bleeding has ceased. The *bedikot* conducted during the seven clean days, by contrast, are performed to verify that nothing has occurred in the interim — that no bleeding took place since the previous check.

### Bedikot Theories

There is, however, a second and quite different way of understanding the function of the weekly *bedikot*. According to this view, the purpose of the *bedikot* is not primarily to detect any blood that may have emerged unnoticed, but rather to direct the woman's conscious attention to her bodily state at that moment. The very act of performing a *bedikah* creates a moment of mindful awareness: is anything coming out? Under this reading, the verse *v'safra lah shivat yamim* requires not merely that seven days pass, but that on each of those days the woman has been cognitively present — attentive, in some form, to whether she is bleeding. Even if something occurred between checks and was undetected, that is of secondary concern; what matters is that on each day there was a moment of focused awareness.

### Washing Protocol

This distinction between the two purposes produces a concrete *nafka mina* in practical law. Before performing a *hefsek taharah*, the woman should wash beforehand — cleaning internally and externally — so that any blood present from prior bleeding does not contaminate the check. Since the entire purpose is to confirm that bleeding has stopped *right now*, earlier blood is irrelevant, and we want nothing from minutes or hours prior to obscure the result. During the *shiva nekiyim*, however, washing immediately before a *bedikah* is problematic. The concern is not only whether bleeding is occurring at this precise moment, but whether any bleeding occurred at all that day. Washing right before could eliminate evidence of bleeding that happened that morning, which is precisely what we need to detect.

As a practical matter, a woman may wash or bathe during the *shiva nekiyim*, but she should wait approximately ten to fifteen minutes before performing the *bedikah*. If she is still bleeding, something will have emerged in that interval; if nothing does, it may be concluded that no bleeding occurred.

## Hefsek: D'Oraita or D'Ra…

### Source of Obligation

A threshold question must be asked: is the *hefsek taharah* — understood as a *bedikah*, performed as thoroughly as possible, reaching high internally and around the sides to ensure nothing is missed — required by Torah law? And more fundamentally: is the obligation of *hefsek taharah* at all a *d'oraita* requirement? On this question several distinct positions emerge.

### Niddah Needs Hefsek

One view holds that for a *niddah*, a *hefsek taharah* is required by Torah law for the following reason: the conclusion of the seven days of *niddah* does not by itself establish that bleeding has ended, because the woman may in fact have become a *zavah*. She may still be bleeding. Therefore, the Torah insists not only that the seven days have elapsed, but that she actively confirm the bleeding has stopped. The *hefsek taharah* is that confirmation. Without it, she cannot be certain she has exited the state of *niddah* and has not entered the state of *zavah*.

### Seven Days Suffice

Against this position, one can argue the converse: by a *zavah*, where she must count *shiva nekiyim*, the very structure of those seven clean days ultimately ensures she will know the bleeding stopped. If tomorrow she bleeds, she cannot count that day; if she does not bleed, she counts it. Eventually, by the time she completes seven clean days, she will know by default that the bleeding ended. Why, then, would she need to establish cessation at the outset before beginning the count? If blood appears tomorrow, the count does not begin anyway; if it does not, the count proceeds. The *hefsek taharah* appears superfluous for a *zavah*, since the *shiva nekiyim* themselves serve as ongoing verification.

### Zavah Needs Hefsek

The opposing view holds the exact reverse. For a *niddah*, the argument runs: *ve-chi le-olam tireh?* — is she going to bleed forever? This is the language of the Chatam Sofer, who traces it to Tosafot. A *niddah* bleeds for a predictable span — three days, four days, five days — and the seven days of *niddah* will certainly outlast that bleeding. There is no practical need for her to verify cessation, because cessation is a foregone conclusion well before the seven days are up. The Torah simply says: if you become a *niddah*, wait seven days. That suffices. If bleeding persists beyond seven days, we address that when it happens; but ordinarily, it is simply over.

For a *zavah*, however, the situation is fundamentally different. *Zivah* is, in the Ramban's framing, a form of illness — an abnormal physiological condition. Because the duration of *zivah* bleeding is uncertain and unpredictable, and because her *taharah* depends on counting *shiva nekiyim* — seven consecutive clean days — she genuinely needs to know that *today* ended without bleeding, so that *tomorrow* can count as day one. The *hefsek taharah* is, for the *zavah*, a prerequisite for initiating the count. The Ramban explains further why *niddah* requires only seven days while *zavah* requires *shiva nekiyim*: *niddah* is a natural biological process, and one need only wait out the maximum duration; *zavah* is unnatural, and the body must be shown to have fully recovered — hence seven consecutive clean days are required, and hence the need to establish clearly when clean days begin.

### Practical Nafka Mina

This dispute carries significant practical consequences. If *niddah* requires a *hefsek taharah* as a matter of Torah law, then leniencies are effectively unavailable. Even if we might otherwise invoke a *derabbanan* framework, a woman who is unambiguously a *niddah d'oraita* cannot benefit from such reasoning. Consider a case — not at all uncommon — where a woman has undergone a pap smear during the week of her *niddah*. She knows her period has stopped, but there is no possibility of performing a *bedikah* and finding it clean; the examination has caused bleeding that will persist for two or three days. Under this scenario, if *niddah* requires a *hefsek taharah d'oraita*, the posek has almost no room to maneuver. The standard *heterim* — those that apply to a *kesem*, or where there was no *hargashah*, or where blood derives from a wound — are simply unavailable for someone who is clearly a *niddah*.

If, on the other hand, we follow the view that *niddah* does not require a *hefsek taharah d'oraita* — because *ve-chi le-olam tireh*, she does not bleed indefinitely — then the *hefsek taharah* for *niddah* is only *derabbanan*. And since *zavah* in our day is always *derabbanan* as well — because a woman who observes *shiva nekiyim* every month can never be a *zavah d'oraita* (by *mima nafshach*, each month is either *niddah* or follows a previous *zavah* that was already resolved) — both the *niddah* dimension and the *zavah* dimension of the *hefsek taharah* are *derabbanan*. That opens the door to applying the range of *kulas* available when we are operating entirely within the *derabbanan* framework.

## Torah Level Hefsek

### Bedikot Requirement

Even granting that *hefsek taharah* is required at the Torah level — whether for *niddah* or for *zavah* — a further question must be asked: what precisely does the Torah require? Does it require *bedikot* — a physical internal examination? Or does it merely require that the woman know she has stopped bleeding?

The question deserves serious attention. Why should the Torah insist on a physical examination at all? Perhaps all that is needed is knowledge that bleeding has ceased. And how might a woman know that? By *hargashah* — by awareness in her body. If she has no sensation of bleeding, if she feels nothing emerging, perhaps that is sufficient *d'oraita*. She need not physically examine herself; she need only concentrate: is anything happening? If the answer is no, she has established, at the Torah level, that the bleeding has stopped.

Under this reading, the obligation of physical *bedikot* — the insertion of cloth, the reaching internally, the checking of all sides and crevices — is entirely *miderabbanan*. And once we are in the realm of *derabbanan*, the familiar tools of *pesika* become available: a wound, a *makkah*, the absence of *hargashah*, atypical circumstances — all of these can potentially be relied upon. The practical significance is considerable.

### Hargasha Approach

This is precisely the position of the Chatam Sofer and the Noda B'Yehuda, albeit arrived at through somewhat different conceptual routes. Both authorities maintain that what the Torah requires for *hefsek taharah* is not a physical examination but rather *hargashah* — the woman's concentrated, undistracted attention to whether she senses any bleeding. She must take a moment, clear her mind of all other thoughts, and focus solely on whether she feels anything. If she does not, she has performed a valid *hefsek taharah* at the Torah level.

The Chatam Sofer articulates this in vivid terms. His language, which he takes from the same *vein* as Tosafot, is *ve-chi le-olam tireh?* — was she going to bleed forever? The *niddah* stops bleeding; that is simply what happens. What the Torah requires is a moment of genuine, focused awareness that confirms this. *Chazal*, the Chatam Sofer adds, then mandated physical *bedikot* as a *d'rabbanan* overlay, because this degree of mental concentration is extraordinarily difficult to achieve — *bedika kasha me'od* — and so a physical examination was required as a more accessible substitute. But where physical examination is impossible, we may fall back on the Torah-level standard.

### Hargasha Definition

Despite their agreement on the basic principle, the Chatam Sofer and the Noda B'Yehuda in fact dispute what *hargashah* means — and this dispute explains why their positions diverge in certain cases.

The Chatam Sofer holds that *hargashah* is *pesicha pi ha-makor* — the sensation of the cervix or uterine opening opening. The Noda B'Yehuda holds that *hargashah* is *zivat davar lach* — the sensation of a liquid dripping or flowing. This difference in definition has precise implications for the *hefsek taharah* question. According to the Noda B'Yehuda, the *hargashah*-based method works for *hefsek taharah* as readily as for the weekly *bedikot*: if she feels no dripping, there is no *hargashah*, and the *hefsek taharah* is valid. According to the Chatam Sofer, however, a problem arises specifically at the *hefsek taharah* stage. During the *shiva nekiyim*, the method works well: if the cervix had closed when bleeding stopped, one would not feel it opening again unless bleeding resumed. But at the time of *hefsek taharah*, the cervix has been open throughout the days of bleeding — it may simply still be open, without any sensation of a new opening. How, then, can the woman confirm that the bleeding has stopped by attending to *hargashah*, if the very structure of *hargashah* as *pesicha pi ha-makor* cannot detect an absence of new bleeding from a cervix that was already open? That is the Chatam Sofer's objection. The Noda B'Yehuda's position does not encounter this difficulty, because *zivat davar lach* — the presence or absence of a dripping sensation — is relevant regardless of whether the cervix was already open.

### Makkah Case

The Chatam Sofer applies his position to a specific and practically significant case. A woman is a *niddah* and, at some point during or at the end of her *yemei nidosah*, develops a *makkah* — some internal wound or infection. The doctor has identified a problem, and performing a *bedikah* will inevitably yield blood from that *makkah*. What can be done?

The Chatam Sofer reasons as follows. Since for *niddah* there is no *hefsek taharah d'oraita* — because *ve-chi le-olam tireh?* — and since physical *bedikot* are only *derabbanan*, then when physical *bedikot* are impossible, we may fall back on the Torah-level alternative: the woman concentrates intently, clears her mind, and attends to whether she senses any bleeding. If she does not, that constitutes a valid *hefsek taharah* at the Torah level, and the *derabbanan* requirement of a physical *bedikah* is waived under these circumstances.

However, the Chatam Sofer himself raises a harder case. What if the *makkah* was already present *before* her period began, and she has been bleeding continuously — from both the *makkah* and the *niddah* — for an extended period? Now the problem is compounded: she does not even have a clear baseline of when her period began and ended. The Chatam Sofer expresses hesitation in applying the *hargashah*-based *hefsek taharah* in this situation, since the very uncertainty about the origin and duration of the bleeding complicates the assessment of cessation. Most later authorities, however, dispute this reservation. They argue that even in such a case, the woman typically knows her own body well enough to distinguish her period from the *makkah* bleeding, and knows when the periodic bleeding stopped. The concern the Chatam Sofer raises does not, in practice, undermine the reliability of the *hargashah*-based approach.

## Practical Solutions

### Rav Ovadia Position

Rav Ovadia Yosef rules decisively that one may rely on the broad consensus of *acharonim* who invoke *ve-chi le-olam tireh*. That principle — that a *niddah* does not bleed indefinitely and the cessation of her period is a known and predictable event — grounds a solid basis for permitting the *hargashah*-based *hefsek taharah* in difficult cases. Rav Ovadia specifically endorses the approach of the Chatam Sofer and the Noda B'Yehuda — the method of sitting, clearing the mind of all distraction, and concentrating entirely on whether one senses any bodily discharge. He holds that *ve-chi le-olam tireh* applies equally whether the *makkah* preceded or followed the onset of *niddah*, and that the Chatam Sofer's hesitation about the earlier *makkah* case need not be followed in practice.

## Torah Level Hefsek…

### Chazon Ish Method

The Chazon Ish approaches the same question from a different angle. Even if one accepts that the Torah requires some physical evidence of cessation — not merely mental concentration — it does not follow that the Torah requires an internal *bedikah* in the full *halachic* sense. The Chazon Ish suggests that repeated external wiping, performed at intervals over a sustained period, would constitute sufficient proof at the Torah level that bleeding has stopped. The logic is straightforward: if a woman wipes herself every several minutes over the course of half an hour (or at similarly regular intervals over some meaningful span of time), and finds nothing, the bleeding must have ceased. There is nowhere for the blood to go; if it were still flowing, it would appear. A single wipe might miss a drip that is still forming, or catch something that had been present earlier. But sustained, repeated wiping over time eliminates that concern. The Chazon Ish is clear that even by this standard, all the Torah ever required was knowledge that bleeding has ceased — and serial external wiping achieves that physically, without requiring an internal examination.

## Practical Solutions…

### Rav Moshe Ruling

Rav Moshe Feinstein is known to have ruled, at least in one case, that a woman should employ the Chatam Sofer's method: she should sit quietly, remove all other thoughts from her mind, and concentrate solely on whether she senses any bleeding. The Chatam Sofer's own language is precise on this point:

> *ela she-taamod sha'ah achas mesichas da'ata mikol hirhurim u-machshavot u-mashgicha al atzma ve-yoda'at she-lo niftach mekora*

She should stand — meaning remain still — for a period, completely withdrawn from all thoughts and preoccupations, attending only to herself and knowing that her *makor* has not opened. The Chatam Sofer acknowledges immediately that this is an extraordinarily demanding form of *bedikah* — *bedikah kasha me'od* — which is why *Chazal* required physical examination. But where physical examination is genuinely impossible, this is the fallback the Torah itself provides.

One might ask: if the Chatam Sofer's method is valid, does it not also eliminate the need for the weekly *bedikot* during the *shiva nekiyim*? The answer is that the Chatam Sofer himself holds that physical *bedikot* certainly work during the week, and the *hargashah*-based mental check works as well. In cases of difficulty — such as extra staining caused by an IUD, residual effects from a medical procedure, or other circumstances where normal *bedikot* are complicated — one might reduce the physical *bedikot* to one or two during the week, on days when examination is feasible, and rely on the mental *hargashah* check on other days. The Chatam Sofer's approach is a *b'dieved* fallback, not an ideal — but it is a genuine and grounded *halachic* option where the alternative is impossible.

### Pad Method Case

The following practical case illustrates how these principles come together. A woman preparing for marriage completed her seven clean days and performed *bedikot* during that week. When asked about her *hefsek taharah*, she realized she had never formally performed one. The question was whether she could proceed to the *mikvah*.

When asked how she had known her period stopped — that is, on what basis she had begun counting *shiva nekiyim* — she explained that she wears pads constantly, day and night. She had not seen anything on those pads since her period ended. Asked whether she checked the pads before discarding them, she confirmed that she had.

This constitutes, without question, a valid *hefsek taharah min haTorah*. It is no worse than the Chazon Ish's standard of repeated external wiping — indeed, it is arguably more comprehensive, since the pads were in continuous contact and she had attended to each one. The *hefsek taharah* is established *min haTorah*, and under the pressing circumstances of an imminent *chuppah* and the serious concerns associated with *chuppat niddah*, there is every reason to be *somech* on this. While it would be an overstatement to say that others explicitly rule this way, the ruling accords entirely with what the major authorities say about *hefsek taharah*, and *lechatchila* one should certainly perform the *hefsek taharah* formally with cloth.

### Tampon Usage

The question of a tampon as *hefsek taharah* is worth addressing directly. The standard *halachic* concern with internal examination is that the cloth or *bedikah* implement must reach the sides and crevices of the vaginal canal — not merely go straight up through the center — since blood may have dripped and caught on the sides. A tampon, being narrow and essentially cylindrical, might seem to miss those areas.

However, if the tampon is left in place for several minutes, it absorbs whatever discharges are present and expands, conforming to the surrounding tissue. This renders it functionally similar to a *moch dachuk* in its coverage, and it is therefore a valid medium for *hefsek taharah*. A woman who specifically intends to perform a *hefsek taharah* using a tampon should leave it in for approximately five to ten minutes.

Moreover, a tampon that was inserted during the day without any formal *kavana* of *hefsek taharah* — and was later found to be clean when removed — can retroactively serve as the *hefsek taharah*. The reasoning is that the very act of removing the tampon and examining it constitutes the *bedikah*; the woman obviously had the checking in mind at the moment she looked at it. That is precisely what *hefsek taharah* requires. If a woman returns home in the evening, reports that she never performed a formal *hefsek taharah*, but mentions that she had a tampon in all day and it came out clean — the tampon provides the *hefsek taharah*, and it was performed during the day, satisfying the timing requirement. *Lechatchila*, of course, a *hefsek taharah* should be performed deliberately and with *kavana*, with prior washing and with time for multiple attempts if necessary. But the tampon case is entirely valid *b'dieved*, and the *hefsek taharah* it represents occurred at the moment she removed and checked it.

## Timing of Hefsek

### Evening Timing

Turning to the question of timing: when should the *hefsek taharah* be performed? The *Mechaber* in siman 196 writes that the *shiva nekiyim* of the *zavah* begin on the day following the day she ceased bleeding, and that accordingly, on the day she stops, she examines herself in order to *tifsok b'taharah* — to conclude in a state of purity.

### Bein Hashmashot Role

The Vilna Gaon in his *Biur HaGra* explains that the requirement to perform the *bedikah* close to evening is a *chumra lechatchila*. The logic is as follows: what I want to establish is not merely that I found myself clean at some point during the day, but that nothing occurred between that examination and the onset of the next day. To achieve this most effectively, I should perform the *hefsek taharah* as close to *bein hashmashot* as possible. The reason I cannot do so *after* the onset of *bein hashmashot* is critical: if I wait until *bein hashmashot* has begun, I lose the day. The day of *hefsek taharah* must count as "yesterday" — a complete, clean day — so that tomorrow can begin as the first of the *shiva nekiyim*. If I wait until *bein hashmashot* is already underway, I cannot be certain that today, as a full day, was clean, and I cannot commence counting tomorrow.

Performing the *hefsek taharah* immediately before *bein hashmashot* solves this: all of today was unproblematic, and tomorrow begins — from the onset of *bein hashmashot*, *misafek* — as day one. The Beis Yosef had already noted that what the sources refer to is not *bein hashmashot mamash* but *samuch l'bein hashmashot* — in the vicinity of *bein hashmashot*, shortly before it begins.

## Timing of Hefsek…

### Morning Bedieved

The Rama rules that *b'dieved*, even if the woman examined herself only in the morning and found herself clean, that suffices. This ruling reflects a deeper truth about the physiology involved.

### Blood Flow Science

It is worth understanding why this is so. Prior to the discoveries of William Harvey regarding the circulatory system — that blood circulates through the entire body and returns via capillaries — it was commonly imagined that blood simply pooled and sloshed around within the body. The uterus, under this mental model, might have been conceived as something like a reservoir: full, it drips; emptied, it stops. Under such an image, one might indeed fear that the bleeding could stop at one moment and restart at another, as if a valve had opened and closed.

But this is not how uterine bleeding actually works. What we call menstrual flow is the atrophying of the uterine wall's vascular lining — a process by which blood vessels that had engorged in preparation for a fertilized egg gradually break down, releasing their contents through the cervix, which is normally open to approximately two millimeters. Once that process is complete, the blood does not "stop" in the sense of a valve closing; it is simply finished. There is no such thing as catching the precise moment of cessation. If the flow has stopped, it is because it is done. There is no closed/open dynamic to be monitored; the question is only whether the blood is dripping or not.

The Chatam Sofer already understood this reality. Given this understanding, the concern that undergirds requiring the *bedikah* close to *bein hashmashot* — namely, that something might have occurred between the morning examination and the evening — is largely theoretical. If the bleeding stopped, it stopped. A clean examination in the morning gives us genuine grounds to know that the next seven days will also be clean. The seven *bedikot* of *shiva nekiyim* are required not because the *hefsek taharah* might have missed something that later restarted, but because the Torah requires *shiva nekiyim* — seven days during which the woman is cognitively attentive to her status.

### Early Check Strategy

The practical conclusion from all of this is counterintuitive relative to the common practice of waiting until late afternoon. Doing the *hefsek taharah* as early as possible is actually advisable, because it leaves time to perform it again — and again, if necessary — if the first check does not come out clean. If a woman waits until immediately before *bein hashmashot* and that *bedikah* is problematic, she has no recourse. If she performs the *hefsek taharah* early in the afternoon and encounters a *shayla*, there is time to redo it. If it stopped bleeding today, it is done — and a clean examination at two in the afternoon is no less valid than one at six. The concern that drove the requirement of performing it close to evening was based on a model of uterine function that does not reflect reality. *Lechatchila* one certainly takes the *halachic* framework seriously, but *b'dieved* the morning is valid, and practically speaking, earlier is better.

There is an additional strategic consideration: in our day, a woman cannot begin counting *shiva nekiyim* until day five of the niddah period. But cessation of bleeding often occurs before then. Performing the *hefsek taharah* on day three or day four — even though counting will not begin until day six — has the advantage that if day five is cut off from the waiting period for any reason, that day five can be counted as day one of *shiva nekiyim*. There is no reason to delay the *hefsek taharah*; the cost of performing it early is negligible and the benefit is real.

## Moch Dachuk Discussion

### Why Moch Dachuk

Returning to the text of the *Mechaber* in siman 196, a further requirement is introduced. The *Mechaber* writes that a husband should instruct his household that the woman should perform her *hefsek taharah* using a *moch dachuk* — a tightly packed cloth insert — which should remain in place throughout *bein hashmashot*. The text reads:

> יִלְמַד אָדָם בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁתְּהֵא בּוֹדֶקֶת בְּיוֹם הַפְסֵק טָהֳרָתָהּ בְּמוֹךְ דָּחוּק וְשֶׁיְּהֵא שָׁם כָּל בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת שֶׁזּוֹ הַבְּדִיקָה מוֹצִיאָה מִיַּד כָּל סָפֵק.

The function of the *moch dachuk* is to ensure that all crevices and folds of the vaginal canal are covered. Even after washing, a drop of blood may have dripped and lodged in a crevice in the interval between washing and inserting the *bedikah* cloth. A single *bedikah* with cloth going straight up the center might miss blood caught on the sides. The *moch dachuk* — tightly packed, expanding to fill the entire space — and left in place throughout *bein hashmashot*, addresses this comprehensively. As the *Mechaber* puts it, this examination removes all doubt.

### Moch Optional

The *moch dachuk* is, however, a *lechatchila* standard — an ideal, not an obligation. It is not required, and if it causes any discomfort, pain, or irritation, it should not be done. The practice creates a heightened standard of certainty, but it is not the baseline requirement; ordinary *bedikot* with cloth suffice.

### Against Moch Dachuk

After considerable experience with the questions (*shailos*) that the *moch dachuk* generates in practice, the conclusion of several authorities is simply: do not do it. The problems it creates outweigh the benefits. Blood from irritation caused by the *moch dachuk* itself creates new *shailos* about whether the woman has become *tamei*; husbands and wives come with questions that would never have arisen had the *moch dachuk* not been used. The *moch dachuk* is supposed to eliminate *safek* — but it generates more *sfeikot* than it resolves.

Rabbi Abadi, in his *Hilchos Niddah*, rules explicitly that the *moch dachuk* should not be done. It is a *chumra* that hurts people and causes complications, and despite its theoretical virtue of removing all doubt, the practical consequence is the opposite: it multiplies uncertainty. Similarly, Rav Tuvia Goldstein — a contemporary and close colleague of Rav Moshe Feinstein, and a major *posek* in his own right — when asked about the *moch dachuk*, responded simply that it causes irritation, conveying through that terse characterization his lack of enthusiasm for the practice. The practical recommendation is thus clear: *kallah* teachers should explain that the *moch dachuk* is strictly *lechatchila* and optional, and that if it is in any way uncomfortable or problematic, it should be avoided entirely.

## One-Day Bleeding

### One-Day Rule

An entirely different set of considerations applies when a woman has only a single day of bleeding. Ordinarily this is not the situation with *niddah*, but it can occur — for instance, with hormonal changes caused by a new contraceptive, which involves genuine hormonal activity and therefore genuine *niddah* blood rather than the blood of a *makkah*. If she ceased bleeding on that same day, the *Mechaber* rules in Halacha 2 of siman 196 that she must perform the *hefsek taharah* using a *moch dachuk* which remains in place throughout *bein hashmashot*. In this case, the *moch dachuk* is not merely *lechatchila* — it is required. The reason is that when bleeding occurred for only a single day, there is particular uncertainty whether that day concluded without bleeding. The full coverage of *kol bein hashmashot* is necessary to establish with confidence that the day ended cleanly, so that tomorrow can begin as day one of *shiva nekiyim*.

The Rama adds that *b'dieved*, if she examined herself *samuch l'bein hashmashot* and found herself clean, even without the *moch dachuk* remaining throughout *bein hashmashot*, this is valid. The reasoning is that *kol bein hashmashot* was never a *halachic* obligation that any authority actually imposed; it was acknowledged as an ideal — the Gemara's point is that if one were to require checking the entire *bein hashmashot*, that would be understandable, but no one ever required it. What Rav Yehuda required was *samuch l'bein hashmashot*, and it is that standard which applies *b'dieved* in the one-day case. The morning alone does not suffice here, because the one-day scenario generates genuine uncertainty not present when the bleeding has extended over multiple days.

### Kesem Leniencies

A further distinction applies when the single-day "bleeding" takes the form of a *kesem* — a stain — rather than clearly identifiable flowing blood. The *halachic* treatment of *kesamim* differs from that of significant bleeding. When the disruption of the *shiva nekiyim* was caused by a *kesem*, it is plausible to treat the discharge as residue from the earlier bleeding rather than as the onset of a new episode. If what was found is only a *kesem* — discovered on underwear or in toilet water rather than experienced as a clear flow — there is genuine room to be lenient and to hold that a *moch dachuk* is not required when counting resumes, particularly if the amount was not significant. Where there is clearly flowing blood, however, this distinction cannot be applied, and a new start with full requirements applies.
