# Berakhot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishna Categories

### Welcome & Overview

Today's daf is 35, and with it we enter *Kaitzad Mevarkhim* — what can fairly be called the second half of the masechet. The first half was devoted to the fixed daily liturgy: *Shema* and *Shemoneh Esreh*. From this point forward, the masechet turns to *brachot* that arise at various moments throughout the day outside of any fixed liturgy, with the primary focus on blessings before and after eating, followed later by other *brachot* that may occur during the course of daily life.

### Mishna: Blessings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מְבָרְכִין עַל הַפֵּירוֹת? עַל פֵּירוֹת הָאִילָן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָעֵץ", חוּץ מִן הַיַּיִן, שֶׁעַל הַיַּיִן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הַגֶּפֶן". וְעַל פֵּירוֹת הָאָרֶץ הוּא אוֹמֵר: "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה", חוּץ מִן הַפַּת, שֶׁעַל הַפַּת הוּא אוֹמֵר: "הַמּוֹצִיא לֶחֶם מִן הָאָרֶץ". וְעַל הַיְּרָקוֹת הוּא אוֹמֵר: "בּוֹרֵא פְּרִי הָאֲדָמָה". רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: "בּוֹרֵא מִינֵי דְשָׁאִים".
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: This mishna discusses the blessings recited over various foods. **How does one recite a blessing over fruits? Over** different **fruits** that grow on a **tree one recites: Who creates fruit of the tree, with the exception of wine.** Although wine is produced from fruit of the tree, due to its significance, its blessing differs from other fruits of the tree. **Over wine one recites: Who creates fruit of the vine. Over fruits** that grow from **the earth, one recites: Who creates fruit of the ground, with the exception of bread.** Bread, too, is significant and its blessing differs from other fruits of the ground, **as over bread one recites: Who brings forth bread from the earth. Over** herbs and leafy **vegetables one recites: Who creates fruit of the ground. Rabbi Yehuda says** that there is room to distinguish between fruits that grow from the earth, herbs, and leafy vegetables. Although they are all fruit of the ground, since they have different qualities, the blessing on the latter is: **Who creates various kinds of herbs.**

The *mishna* at the top of 35a opens: *kaitzad mevarkhim al ha-perot* — how does one make a blessing over fruit? *Al perot ha-ilan hu omer borei pri ha-etz*: for fruit from the tree, one recites *borei pri ha-etz*. The *mishna* already presupposes that even when fruit is transformed into juice, one would naturally assume the blessing remains *borei pri ha-etz* in its original form. The *mishna* must therefore specify the exception: *chutz min ha-yayin* — for wine one recites *borei pri ha-gefen*. God, after all, did not create wine directly; human processing was involved. Therefore, even in reciting a special *bracha* for this transformed form, we refer to the fruit in its natural state and say *borei pri ha-gefen*.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

One notable feature of the *mishna*'s language is its use of the word *ilan* for tree in the body of the rule, while the *bracha* text itself employs *etz*. As one of the participants observed, *ilan* does not appear in the *mikra*, whereas *etz* — as in *etz pri oseh pri le-mino* — is biblical language. This reflects a broader principle: the formulations of *brachot*, particularly their *chatimot*, draw heavily on the language of *psukim*. The same dynamic explains the well-known *machlokret* among *Sefardim* over whether to pronounce *borei pri ha-gafen* or *borei pri ha-gefen* — the form *gefen* appears in *Tanakh* mid-sentence, while *gafen* is the pausal form for end-of-verse; since *gafen* as such never appears as a final word in *Tanakh*, some hold that one must use only the form that actually appears in the text.

### Wine & Bread

Continuing the *mishna*: *ve-al perot ha-aretz hu omer borei pri ha-adama* — for fruit of the ground, one says *borei pri ha-adama* — with the exception of bread. Here too the *mishna* assumes that since bread derives from wheat, one might say *borei pri ha-adama*, as one would for wheat eaten in its natural form. But for bread, one says *ha-motzi lechem min ha-aretz*.

### Hamotzi Prayer

The *bracha* of *ha-motzi* is interesting because it does approach the idea of the finished product — it says *lechem*, bread, not wheat. Yet the emphasis remains on the divine act of bringing forth the wheat from the earth, rather than on the human process of baking. The *bracha* is thus a dual acknowledgment: it names the final human product while directing our attention to God's creative role in producing the grain.

### Categories: Earth & Veg

The *mishna* then states: *ve-ha-yerakot hu omer borei pri ha-adama* — for vegetables one also recites *borei pri ha-adama*, the same *bracha* as for what it earlier called *perot ha-aretz*. This naturally raises the question: what distinguishes *perot ha-aretz* from *yerakot* if both receive the same *bracha*? One way to draw the distinction — and *Rashi* addresses this as well — is that *perot ha-aretz* refers to things that grow above the ground but are not fruits of a tree, while *yerakot* grow in the ground. That said, *Rashi* also includes *kitniyot* (legumes) within the vegetable category, which complicates a simple above-ground versus below-ground division. The precise scope of *perot ha-aretz* versus *yerakot* remains a point of discussion as the *gemara* continues.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

Rabbi Yehuda dissents on the last category, holding that one should not say *borei pri ha-adama* over vegetables but rather *borei minei deshaim* — creator of types of herbs, or literally grasses. This formulation suggests that the vegetable category extends beyond things that grow below the ground and encompasses a broader range of low-growing plants.

What this *mishna* accomplishes overall is the laying out of the various categories of *brachot*: that *brachot* are differentiated according to what one is eating, that a transformed form of a food in many cases retains the original *bracha*, and that wine and bread are notable exceptions receiving their own distinct *brachot*. What the *mishna* does not yet address is the source of the obligation to make *brachot* in the first place — that is precisely where the *gemara* begins.

## Kodesh Hilulim & Neta

### Kodesh Hilulim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: "קֹדֶשׁ הִלּוּלִים לַה׳", מְלַמֵּד שֶׁטְּעוּנִים בְּרָכָה לִפְנֵיהֶם וּלְאַחֲרֵיהֶם. מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיִּטְעוֹם כְּלוּם קוֹדֶם שֶׁיְּבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: Concerning the fundamental basis for blessings, the Gemara asks: **From where are these matters,** the obligation to recite a blessing before eating, derived? The Gemara answers: **As the Sages taught** in the *Sifra*: With regard to saplings, it is stated that in their fourth year their fruit will be: **"…sanctified for praises before the Lord"** (Leviticus 19:24). This verse **teaches** that **they require** praise of God in the form of a **blessing** both **beforehand and thereafter,** as the verse says praises in the plural. **From here, Rabbi Akiva said: A person is forbidden to taste anything before he recites a blessing,** as without reciting praise over food, it has the status of a consecrated item, from which one is forbidden to derive pleasure.

The *gemara* opens with the foundational question: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for making *brachot* before eating? *Detanu rabbanan*: the Torah's discussion of *neta revai* — the fourth-year fruit, which must be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there in a manner akin to *maaser sheni* — includes the phrase *kodesh hilulim l'Hashem*, sanctified as a praising to God. This is interpreted to mean: *melamed she-te'unin bracha lifneihem u-le'achareihem* — it teaches that such produce requires a blessing both before and after eating. This is the initial scriptural basis for *brachot* over food.

### Rabbi Akiva Stringency

Beyond this, Rabbi Akiva states: *assur le-adam she-yitom klum kodem she-yevarech* — it is forbidden for a person to taste anything before reciting a blessing. This formulation is striking. Until now the language has been about obligation and praise; here Rabbi Akiva introduces the concept of prohibition, which points toward a qualitatively different concern — that one is not entitled to eat at all without first acknowledging the divine source of the food. One of the participants astutely noted that Rabbi Akiva's emphasis on the prohibition may be rooted in the structure of the *pasuk* itself: three years of *orlah* in which eating is forbidden, followed by the fourth year of *hilulim*, followed by the fifth year in which one may eat. The sequence suggests: forbidden, then praise, then permitted — echoing Rabbi Akiva's insistence that the blessing is a precondition for eating.

### Kodesh Hilulim Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי "קֹדֶשׁ הִלּוּלִים" לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא? הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ חַד דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא אַחֲלֵיהּ וַהֲדַר אִכְלֵיהּ. וְאִידָךְ דָּבָר הַטָּעוּן שִׁירָה, טָעוּן חִלּוּל, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ טָעוּן שִׁירָה, אֵין טָעוּן חִלּוּל. וְכִדְרַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה אֶלָּא עַל הַיַּיִן? — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַתֹּאמֶר לָהֶם הַגֶּפֶן הֶחֳדַלְתִּי אֶת תִּירוֹשִׁי הַמְשַׂמֵּחַ אֱלֹהִים וַאֲנָשִׁים", אִם אֲנָשִׁים מְשַׂמֵּחַ, אֱלֹהִים בַּמֶּה מְשַׂמֵּחַ? מִכָּאן שֶׁאֵין אוֹמְרִים שִׁירָה אֶלָּא עַל הַיַּיִן!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** did **this** verse: **"Sanctified for praises," come for that** purpose? **This** verse **is necessary** to derive other matters. **One** being **that the Merciful One said: Redeem it and then eat it.** This midrash interprets *hillul*, praise, as *ḥillul*, redemption. **And the other** matter derived from this verse is: **An object which** is offered upon the altar and **requires a song** of praise when it is offered, as is the case with the libation of wine, **requires redemption. And that which does not require a song** of praise, all other fruits, **does not require redemption. And** this is **in accordance with** the opinion that **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said, as Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: From where** is it derived that **one only recites a song** of praise in the Temple **over the** libation of **wine** on the altar? **As it is stated: "And the vine replied: Should I leave my wine, which gladdens God and man,** and go and wave above the trees?" (Judges 9:13). **If** wine **gladdens people, in what** way **does it gladden God?** Rather, derive **from here that one only recites a song** of praise **over wine,** as wine gladdens God when offered as part of the service in the Temple.In any case, other *halakhot* have been derived from this verse. From where, then, is the requirement to recite blessings derived?

The *gemara* now questions whether the phrase *kodesh hilulim* is actually being used to teach the obligation to make *brachot*. The word *hilulim* is written in a doubled form and may be carrying multiple halakhic messages simultaneously. The *gemara* therefore asks: is this really the source for *brachot*, or does the verse need to be used for other teachings?

### Hilulim as Chillulim

The *gemara* offers two other things that *hilulim* comes to teach. First, reading the *heh* as a *chet* — the two letters are phonetically related as gutturals and visually similar — gives us *chillulim*, meaning redemption or transfer of sanctity. This teaches that the law of *neta revai* parallels *maaser sheni*: if one does not wish to bring the fourth-year produce to Jerusalem, one may redeem its value onto money and then bring the money to Jerusalem. *Achlei u-vahadar achlei*: transfer the sanctity, then eat — consistent with the following *pasuk* which speaks of eating.

### Shira Over Wine

The second teaching from *hilulim* concerns the scope of *neta revai* altogether. The *gemara* cites the principle: *davar ha-ta'un shira ta'un chillul* — something over which one sings *shira* falls within the category of *neta revai*, while something over which one does not sing is not subject to this law. The question becomes: what is it that one sings over? Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani says in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *minayin she-ein omrim shira ela al ha-yayin* — how do we know that *shira* is recited only over wine? The source is the parable in *Shoftim* (Judges), where in the famous allegory of the trees choosing a king, the grapevine declares: have I given up my wine *that gladdens the hearts of God and people* that I should have to become king? The *gemara* asks: if wine gladdens people, in what sense does it gladden God? The answer is that it is through the *shira* itself — the Levitical singing performed in the Temple during the wine libation that accompanied each *korban*.

### Wine Gladdens Hearts

This point has significant practical implications. The *Levi'im* sang *shira* not during the slaughter of the sacrifice, not during the sprinkling of the blood, not during the placing of the limbs on the altar — but only at the very end of the *avodah*, when the wine of the libation was being poured. *Ein omrim shira ela al ha-yayin*. This is also the halakhic model for *Kiddush* and *Havdalah* — rituals of verbal praise and sanctification are performed specifically over wine. The *gemara* here already knew of *Kiddush*, *Havdalah*, and *Birkat Hamazon* as wine-based rituals; the expansion to *Sheva Brachot*, *Birkat Erusin*, and *Birkat Brit Milah* over wine, while not sourced in the *gemara*, further reflects this same principle that *shira* and formal praise carry greatest ritual weight when performed over wine.

The upshot of this approach is that *hilulim* is being used to teach that only grapevines fall within the scope of *neta revai*, since only over wine is *shira* recited. If so, the word *hilulim* has already been fully deployed for this teaching and cannot simultaneously be used to derive the obligation of *brachot*.

### Wine Limitation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּתָנֵי "נֶטַע רְבָעִי". אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּתָנֵי "כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי" מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִתְּמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּרַבִּי, חַד תָּנֵי "כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי", וְחַד תָּנֵי "נֶטַע רְבָעִי".
>
> **Translation:** Indeed, **this** works out **well according to the one who taught,** as a rule: **A fourth-year sapling** in the *mishnayot* dealing with the prohibition to eat fruits produced during the first three years of a tree's existence and the sanctity of the fruit produced in its fourth year; as, in his opinion, fourth-year fruits that grow on all trees must be redeemed. **However, according to the one who taught,** as a rule: **A fourth-year grapevine, what can be said?** Indeed, he derives the *halakha* that only wine that is accompanied by a song of praise requires redemption, from the interpretation of *hillul* as *ḥillul*. **As it was stated: Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Shimon, son of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **one taught** these *mishnayot* using the term: **A fourth-year grapevine, and one taught** using the term: **A fourth-year sapling.**

The *gemara* therefore states: *haniicha le-man de-tani neta revai* — this works out well according to the version of the *mishnah* that uses the term *neta revai*, fourth-year planting, implying the *mitzvah* applies broadly and is not limited to the vine. If that is the case, the word *hilulim* is not needed to limit the category to wine, and it remains available to teach the obligation to make *brachot*. But according to the version that reads *kerem revai* — fourth-year vine — meaning the *mitzvah* is limited to grapevines from the outset, then *hilulim* must be used to explain *why* it is limited to the vine, and cannot simultaneously teach *brachot*. This debate is recorded explicitly: Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon *berebbi* disagree — one transmits *kerem revai*, the other *neta revai*.

### Gezeirah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּתָנֵי "כֶּרֶם רְבָעִי" הָנִיחָא אִי יָלֵיף גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן "לְהוֹסִיף לָכֶם תְּבוּאָתוֹ", וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן "וּתְבוּאַת הַכָּרֶם". מָה לְהַלָּן כֶּרֶם, אַף כָּאן כֶּרֶם. אִייַּתַּר לֵיהּ חַד "הִלּוּל" לִבְרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And according to the one who taught: A fourth year grapevine, this** works out **well if he derives** this matter **from a verbal analogy [*gezera shava*],** and therefore need not derive this *halakha* from the term *hillulim*. **As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: It is stated here** with regard to the laws of the prohibition of fruit for the tree's first three years: "But in the fifth year you may eat its fruit, **so that it may increase your produce [*tevuato*];** I am the Lord your God" (Leviticus 19:25). **And it is stated below,** with regard to the laws of diverse kinds: "You shall not sow your vineyard with two kinds of seed, lest the growth of the seed that you have sown be forfeited **with the produce [*utevuat*] of the vineyard"** (Deuteronomy 22:9). Based on a verbal analogy, it can be derived: **Just as below,** with regard to the laws of diverse kinds, the produce is that which grows in **vineyards; so too, here,** with regard to the *halakhot* of the fruits of a sapling, the produce is that which grows in **vineyards.** Consequently, according to the one who holds this verbal analogy, **one extra *hillul*** remains from which to derive **the blessing.** Since he derives that the laws of fourth-year saplings apply only to grapes from the verbal analogy, he can derive the requirement to recite blessings before partaking of food from the word *hillulim*.

The *gemara* then proposes a way out for the opinion that holds *kerem revai*: even according to that view, perhaps the limitation of the fourth-year law to the vine is derived not from *hilulim* at all but from a *gezeirah shavah*. A *braita* cites Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: the *pasuk* in the context of *orlah* and *neta revai* says *le-hosif lachem tevu'ato* — to increase for you its yield — and elsewhere the Torah speaks of *tevu'at ha-kerem*, the yield of the vine. The word *tevu'ah* appears in both places; this *gezeirah shavah* links the two and can be used to establish that the fourth-year category is vineyard-specific. If the limitation to wine is learned from the *gezeirah shavah*, then *hilulim* is freed up to teach something else — namely, the requirement of a *bracha*.

### Beyond Wine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָא יָלֵיף גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה, בְּרָכָה מְנָא לֵיהּ? וְאִי נָמֵי יָלֵיף גְּזֵרָה שָׁוָה, אַשְׁכְּחַן לְאַחֲרָיו, לְפָנָיו מִנַּיִן?
> 
> הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר: כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׂבֵעַ מְבָרֵךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא רָעֵב — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he does not derive** this *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy,** he must derive this *halakha* from the term *hillulim*, in which case, **from where does he derive** the mitzva **to recite a blessing** before partaking of food? **And even if he derives** this *halakha* by means of **a verbal analogy, we found** a source for the obligation to recite a blessing **after** eating, similar to the obligation stated in the verse: "And you will eat and be satisfied and then you shall bless." However, **from where** is it derived that there is an obligation to recite a blessing **beforehand?** From one *hillul*, the fundamental *halakha* of redemption of fourth-year saplings is derived.
> 
> The Gemara answers this: This is **not difficult, as it** may be **derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference: If when he is satiated,** after eating, **he** is obligated to **recite a blessing** over food, **when he is hungry,** before eating, **all the more so** that he is obligated to recite a blessing over food.

The *gemara* is quite strict about this principle: once a word has been used to derive one *halacha*, it generally cannot also be used to derive another. As the *gemara* presses further, even granting the *gezeirah shavah*, the word *hilulim* appears in doubled form, but one usage has already been spent on the law of redemption — *chillul*. At most, then, one *bracha* can be derived. And if only one *bracha* is available, the *gemara* reasons it should be the *bracha* after eating, since we already have a precedent for that in *Birkat Hamazon*.

This leaves the *bracha* before eating without a source from *hilulim*. But the *gemara* responds: *ha lo kashya, de-ati be-kal vachomer* — that is not a difficulty, since we derive it by *kal vachomer*. *Keshehu save'a mevarech keshehu ra'ev lo kol sheken* — if one makes a *bracha* when sated, how much more so when hungry? When hungry, the appreciation of God's gift of food is more immediate and powerful; after eating, one has more fully experienced the blessing but perhaps with somewhat less intensity of anticipation. Therefore, a *bracha* before eating follows logically from the *bracha* after. The *gemara* at this stage seriously entertains the possibility that this *kal vachomer* has the force of a biblical derivation.

But even granting all of this, we have only established *brachot* for wine and grapevines. *She'ar minin minayin* — how do we extend the requirement to all other foods?

## All Foods Blessing

### Kal Vachomer Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן כֶּרֶם, שְׁאָר מִינִין מִנַּיִן?
> 
> דְּיָלֵיף מִכֶּרֶם: מָה כֶּרֶם דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה וְטָעוּן בְּרָכָה, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה — טָעוּן בְּרָכָה.
> 
> אִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לְכֶרֶם שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיב בְּעוֹלֵלוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: In that way, **we found** a source for the obligation to recite a blessing over the produce of **vineyards,** but **from where** is it derived with regard to **other types of** produce?
> 
> The Gemara responds: **It is derived** by means of the hermeneutic principle: What do we find, **from** the produce of a **vineyard: Just as** the fruit of the **vineyard is an item** from **which one derives benefit and it requires a blessing, so too, any item** from **which one derives benefit, requires a blessing.**
> 
> The Gemara rejects this proof: **This** derivation **can be refuted,** as a vineyard is unique: **What** is unique about a **vineyard, that it is obligated in** the mitzva requiring to give **small, incomplete clusters of grapes [*olelot*]** to the poor? That is a stringency that does not apply to other fruits. Perhaps the blessing is also a stringency that applies only to grapes.

The *gemara* attempts to extend the requirement broadly: *di-yalfi mi-kerem* — let us derive from the case of the vine. Just as the vine is something from which one derives benefit and over which a *bracha* is required, so too everything from which one derives benefit should require a *bracha*. But the *gemara* challenges this: perhaps the vine is distinctive because of the law of *olelot* — the unformed clusters that must be left for the poor, a law unique to the vineyard. Maybe the requirement of a *bracha* is connected to that distinctive feature, even if there is no logical reason why it should be. This is the *gemara*'s standard mode of objection when trying to prevent one case from becoming a paradigm: the mere existence of a distinctive characteristic, regardless of its logical relevance, blocks a generalization.

### Grain & Seven Species

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָמָה תּוֹכִיחַ. מָה לְקָמָה שֶׁכֵּן חַיֶּיבֶת בְּחַלָּה!
> 
> כֶּרֶם יוֹכִיחַ. וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן: דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה וְטָעוּן בְּרָכָה, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה — טָעוּן בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In that case, **standing grain can prove** that the *halakha* of *olelot* is not a factor in the obligation to recite a blessing. One is obligated by Torah law to recite a blessing after eating bread, even though the *halakha* of *olelot* does not apply to grain. The Gemara rejects this proof: **What** is unique about **ripe grain, that it is obligated in** the mitzva of separating ***ḥalla*** from the dough? That is a stringency that does not apply to other foods.
> 
> The Gemara responds: In that regard, **vineyards can prove** that the *halakha* of *ḥalla* is not a factor in the obligation to recite a blessing. In summary: **And the derivation has reverted** to its starting point. However, at this point the *halakha* is derived from a combination of the two sources: **The aspect of this is not like the aspect of that, and the aspect of that is not like the aspect of this; the common denominator is:** Both are **items** from **which one derives benefit and** each **requires a blessing.** A general principle may be derived: **So too, any item** from **which one derives benefit, requires a blessing.**

The *gemara* therefore brings in a second case: *kama tochiach* — let grain prove it. We know a *bracha* is required after eating grain because the *pasuk* says *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta*, the source for *Birkat Hamazon*. Grain and wine together prove that the principle — deriving benefit obligates a *bracha* — is not about *olelot*. But then grain has its own distinctive feature: *chayevet be-challah*, the obligation to separate *challah* applies to grain and not to wine. Perhaps the *bracha* requirement is tied to *challah*. So the *gemara* invokes the vine to prove it is not about *challah*, and grain to prove it is not about *olelot* — and the reasoning goes in a circle.

### Grain & Seven Species (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בּוֹ צַד מִזְבֵּחַ. וְאָתֵי נָמֵי זַיִת דְּאִית בֵּיהּ צַד מִזְבֵּחַ.
> 
> מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא: מָה לְהַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן שֶׁכֵּן יֵשׁ בָּהֶן צַד מִזְבֵּחַ! אֶלָּא, דְּיָלֵיף לַהּ מִשִּׁבְעַת הַמִּינִין: מָה שִׁבְעַת הַמִּינִין דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה וְטָעוּן בְּרָכָה, אַף כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנֶּהֱנֶה טָעוּן בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** Again, the Gemara objects: **What** is unique about **the common denominator** between grapes and grain that prevents utilizing it as a paradigm for other food items? Grapes and grain **have an aspect** of being offered upon the **altar,** and perhaps that is the reason that they require blessings. Based on that reasoning, although all other food items cannot be derived from the common denominator, **an olive may also be derived as it too has an aspect** of being offered upon the **altar,** as olive oil is one of the components of a meal offering.
> 
> The Gemara returns to the issue at hand, noting that **in any case, it is difficult: What** is unique about **the common denominator** between grapes and grain? That they **possess an aspect** of being offered upon the **altar. Rather, it is derived from** the obligation to recite a blessing **upon the seven species.** After the verse speaks of the seven species, it states: "And you will eat and be satisfied and then you shall bless." This is a paradigm for all other foods, that they too require a blessing: **Just as the seven species are items** from **which one derives benefit and require a blessing, any item** from **which one derives benefit, requires a blessing.**

The *gemara* next attempts to derive the obligation from *shivat ha-minim*, the seven species. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* describing the Land of Israel enumerates the seven species and concludes with the promise *lo techsar kol bah* — you shall lack nothing — followed by the verse *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta*. If that verse of *bracha* applies not only to bread but to all seven species, then we have a biblical source for *brachot* after eating all seven species. And then, by the earlier *kal vachomer*, if one blesses after eating, one certainly blesses before. This would give us *brachot* before and after for the full set of seven species.

### Tzad Hashaveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה לְשִׁבְעַת הַמִּינִין שֶׁכֵּן חַיָּיבִים בְּבִכּוּרִים? וְעוֹד: הָתִינַח לְאַחֲרָיו, לְפָנָיו מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** Again, the Gemara rejects this: **What** is unique **about the seven species? That one is obligated in** the mitzva **of first fruits.** However, other produce with regard to which one is not obligated in the mitzva of first fruits, from where is it derived that they require a blessing? **Furthermore,** even if the seven species can serve as a paradigm, **this** works out **well** with regard to the blessing **thereafter;** but **from where** is the obligation to recite a blessing **beforehand** derived?

But the *gemara* again objects: *mah le-shivat ha-minim she-kein chayavin be-bikurim* — the seven species are distinctive because one brings *bikurim* only from them. Therefore the *bracha* obligation might be tied to that unique characteristic. The *gemara* had tried a similar move earlier with grain and wine, using the *tzad hashaveh* — the common denominator — when individual cases failed. When both grain and wine were brought as sources, the *gemara* had observed that both have a *tzad mizbeach*, an aspect of being offered on the altar. Perhaps the *bracha* requirement applies only to foods that have this altar-connection.

### Olives Included

The *gemara* notes that at most one could extend this to olives, since olive oil is also brought on the *mizbeach*. The *gemara* briefly considers deriving this from the word *kerem* in *kerem revai* — one *pasuk* in *Shoftim* speaks of *kerem zayit*, an olive orchard. But Rav Papa rules: *kerem zayit ikri, kerem stam lo ikri* — it may be called an olive *kerem* with a qualifier, but *kerem* unmodified refers only to the vine. So even including olives via the *tzad hashaveh* argument, the challenge stands: the obligation might be limited to things that have an *altar* dimension.

### All Foods Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא דְּאָתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר: כְּשֶׁהוּא שָׂבֵעַ מְבָרֵךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא רָעֵב — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן.
> 
> וּלְמַאן דְּתָנֵי "נֶטַע רְבָעִי", הָא תִּינַח כֹּל דְּבַר נְטִיעָה. דְּלָאו בַּר נְטִיעָה, כְּגוֹן בָּשָׂר בֵּיצִים וְדָגִים מְנָא לֵיהּ? אֶלָּא סְבָרָא הוּא: אָסוּר לוֹ לָאָדָם שֶׁיֵּהָנֶה מִן הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בְּלֹא בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds to the question: This is **not difficult, as it** may be **derived by means of an *a fortiori* inference: If when he is satiated,** after eating, **he** is obligated to **recite a blessing** over food, **when he is hungry,** before eating, **all the more so** he is obligated to recite a blessing over food.
> 
> In any case, this is not an absolute proof. Furthermore, even **according to the one who taught: A fourth-year sapling** in all the relevant *mishnayot*, **it** works out **well** with regard to **everything that can be planted,** that one is obligated to recite a blessing. However, with regard to **items that cannot be planted, such as meat, eggs, and fish, from where does he** derive the *halakha* that one is obligated to recite a blessing? **Rather,** all previous attempts at deriving this *halakha* are rejected. The fundamental obligation to recite a blessing over food is founded on **reason: One is forbidden to derive benefit from this world without a blessing.**

Having exhausted the *tzad hashaveh* approach, the *gemara* attempts one more avenue: *ela de-yalif lah mi-shivat ha-minim*. If the verse *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta* applies to all seven species and gives us a *bracha* after eating all of them, and we apply the *kal vachomer* to get a *bracha* before, perhaps that is broad enough. The *gemara* responds again: *mah le-shivat ha-minim she-kein chayavin be-bikurim* — again, the *bikurim* connection is distinctive. And critically, the *gemara* adds: *ve-od hatenei le-acharav, le-fanav minayin* — all of this still only gives you a *bracha* afterwards; how would you know about a *bracha* before? The *kal vachomer* answers that, as before. But the underlying point is that even for the seven species we cannot escape the objection based on their distinctive status.

There is something conceptually rich about why *bikurim* and *tzad mizbeach* keep appearing as obstacles. Both *bikurim* and the altar offerings involve bringing food to the *Mikdash* with words of praise — the very model that the *hilulim* verse evoked at the outset. The connection between *bracha* and *kodesh* runs throughout: perhaps *brachot* are obligatory specifically for things that enter the sanctified space of the Temple, and not for the ordinary foods of daily life.

### Sevara: Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אָסוּר לוֹ לָאָדָם שֶׁיֵּהָנֶה מִן הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בְּלֹא בְּרָכָה. וְכׇל הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בְּלֹא בְּרָכָה מָעַל. מַאי תַּקַּנְתֵּיהּ? — יֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל חָכָם.
> 
> יֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל חָכָם?! מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הָא עֲבַד לֵיהּ אִיסּוּרָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: יֵלֵךְ אֵצֶל חָכָם מֵעִיקָּרָא וִילַמְּדֶנּוּ בְּרָכוֹת כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָבֹא לִידֵי מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *Tosefta*: **One is forbidden to derive benefit from this world,** which is the property of God, **without** reciting **a blessing** beforehand. **And anyone who derives benefit from this world without a blessing,** it is as if he is guilty of **misuse** of a consecrated object. The Gemara adds: **What is his remedy? He should go to a Sage.**
> 
> The Gemara is puzzled: **He should go to a Sage; what will he do to him?** How can the Sage help after **he has already violated a prohibition? Rather, Rava said,** this is how it should be understood: **He should go to a Sage initially,** in his youth, **and** the Sage **will teach him blessings, so that he will not come to** be guilty of this type of **misuse** of a consecrated object in the future.

At this point the *gemara* sets aside the entire derivational enterprise. *Ela sevara hu* — it is simply a matter of logic. *Assur lo le-adam she-yehene min ha-olam ha-zeh belo bracha* — it is forbidden for a person to derive benefit from this world without a blessing. This echoes and grounds Rabbi Akiva's formulation from the outset, but now with a different emphasis. The earlier approaches centered on *hilulim* — on praising God for His gifts — and could conceivably be satisfied by a *bracha* after eating alone, or at most an obligation before that flows from a *kal vachomer*. Here the emphasis is categorically different: one is simply *not entitled* to eat without first acknowledging that the food comes from God. The *bracha* is not just an expression of gratitude; it is the act of obtaining permission.

The *Tosafot* at the bottom of the *daf* makes this explicit: *asmachta be-alma* — the verse of *hilulim* was only ever a scriptural support for what the *gemara* now understands to be the real foundation. The *gemara* originally considered that the obligation before eating might be fully biblical, whether from *hilulim* directly or through a *kal vachomer*. That possibility is now set aside, and the conclusion is that the *bracha* before eating is rabbinic — grounded in the *sevara* that one may not derive benefit from God's world without acknowledgment — while the *Tosafot* at the top of the *daf* notes the irony: the *gemara* had to work hard to justify why *Shema* starts with "when" rather than "whether," but here no such question arises. Of course one makes *brachot* before eating; it is self-evident that one does not derive benefit without blessing.

## Me'ilah Principle

### Forbidden Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בְּלֹא בְּרָכָה כְּאִילּוּ נֶהֱנָה מִקׇּדְשֵׁי שָׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ".
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: One who derives benefit from this world without a blessing, it is as if he enjoyed objects consecrated to the heavens, as it is stated: "The earth and all it contains is the Lord's,** the world and all those who live in it" (Psalms 24:1).

### Me'ilah Equivalence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי לֵוִי רָמֵי: כְּתִיב "לַה׳ הָאָרֶץ וּמְלוֹאָהּ", וּכְתִיב "הַשָּׁמַיִם שָׁמַיִם לַה׳ וְהָאָרֶץ נָתַן לִבְנֵי אָדָם"! לָא קַשְׁיָא כָּאן קוֹדֶם בְּרָכָה, כָּאן לְאַחַר בְּרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Levi expressed this concept differently. **Rabbi Levi raised a contradiction: It is written: "The earth and all it contains is the Lord's," and it is written** elsewhere: **"The heavens are the Lord's and the earth He has given over to mankind"** (Psalms 115:16). There is clearly a contradiction with regard to whom the earth belongs. He himself resolves the contradiction: This is **not difficult. Here,** the verse that says that the earth is the Lord's refers to the situation **before a blessing** is recited, **and here,** where it says that He gave the earth to mankind refers to **after a blessing** is recited.

The force of this is considerable. Everything in the world belongs to God; God's property is, in a sense, sanctified. Just as one who uses property belonging to the Temple without authorization commits *me'ilah* — theft of sanctified objects — so too one who eats without a *bracha* has taken from that which is God's without permission. The *bracha* functions as the mechanism of authorization. This introduces a profound dimension of *kedusha* into the act of eating: just as the earlier *hilulim* framework evoked the *Mikdash* experience of bringing produce with songs of praise, here too the language of *me'ilah* brings the sanctity of the Temple into the domain of the everyday meal.

### Learn from Sage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: כׇּל הַנֶּהֱנֶה מִן הָעוֹלָם הַזֶּה בְּלֹא בְּרָכָה כְּאִילּוּ גּוֹזֵל לְהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא וּכְנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "גּוֹזֵל אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ וְאוֹמֵר אֵין פָּשַׁע חָבֵר הוּא לְאִישׁ מַשְׁחִית". וְאֵין "אָבִיו" אֶלָּא הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הֲלֹא הוּא אָבִיךְ קָּנֶךָ", וְאֵין "אִמּוֹ" אֶלָּא כְּנֶסֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "שְׁמַע בְּנִי מוּסַר אָבִיךָ וְאַל תִּטּוֹשׁ תּוֹרַת אִמֶּךָ".
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa said: Anyone who derives benefit from this world without a blessing, it is as if he stole from God and the community of Israel, as it is stated: "Whoever robs his father and his mother and says: It is no transgression, he is the companion of a destroyer"** (Proverbs 28:24). The phrase, **his father, refers to none other than God, as it is stated: "Is He not your Father Who created you,** Who made you and established you" (Deuteronomy 32:6). The phrase **his mother refers to none other than the community of Israel, as it is stated: "Hear, my son, the discipline of your father, and do not forsake the Torah of your mother"** (Proverbs 1:8). The mention of the Torah as emanating from the mouth of the mother, apparently means that your mother is the community of Israel.

The *gemara* asks: *mai takantei* — what is the remedy if one derived benefit without a *bracha* and has thereby committed *me'ilah*? The initial suggestion is *yelech etzel chakham* — one should go to a sage. But the *gemara* immediately objects: *mai avid lei* — what can the sage do? *Ha avi issura* — the transgression has already occurred. *Ela amar Rava*: one should go to a sage *me'ikara* — beforehand — *ve-yilamdenu brachot*, so that one learns all the *brachot* properly, *ke-dei she-lo yavo lidei me'ilah*, so as not to come to transgress *me'ilah*. The *Maharsha* adds that this instruction carries particular weight because the laws of *brachot* are genuinely complex — which *bracha* applies to which food, and in what form. Even making the wrong *bracha*, one that does not fulfill the obligation, may leave the person having derived benefit without a valid *bracha*, and thus still subject to the *me'ilah* concern.

### God Owns All

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "חָבֵר הוּא לְאִישׁ מַשְׁחִית"? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: חָבֵר הוּא לְיָרׇבְעָם בֶּן נְבָט שֶׁהִשְׁחִית אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **What** is the meaning of the continuation of the verse: **He is the companion of a destroyer? Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa said:** He is **a companion of Jeroboam ben Nevat, who corrupted Israel before their Father in heaven** by sinning and causing others to sin.

### Before & After

### Steal from God

The *Tosafot* and *Rashi* commentary explain: one who dismisses the importance of *brachot*, announcing that it is trivial, teaches others to do the same and spreads neglect of the obligation.

### Yeravam Comparison

Such a person is a companion to *Yerovam ben Nevat* — *she-hishchit et Yisrael le-avihem she-ba-shamayim*, who corrupted Israel before their Father in Heaven. This comparison may seem extreme, but its logic runs deeper than it first appears. *Yerovam ben Nevat*'s defining sin was preventing the Jewish people from making *aliyah la-regel* — from ascending to the Temple, from bringing their produce, their grain and wine and oil and animals, to the *Mikdash* in acknowledgment and gratitude to God. One who dismisses *brachot* does the same thing at the level of daily life: he severs the sanctity of eating from its connection to divine acknowledgment. He steals not just from God but from *Knesset Yisrael*, because — as *Rashi* elsewhere notes, echoing the *neta revai pasuk* — it is the act of acknowledgment that calls forth continued blessing upon the land. Remove the *brachot*, and you remove the blessing for the entire people.

The two frameworks the *gemara* has now presented — *hilulim* as praise, and *sevara* as the prohibition of unauthorized benefit — both point toward the same profound sanctification of everyday eating. The first evokes the *Mikdash* model: we bring food before God, we sing over it, we acknowledge its source. The second makes an even starker claim: the food was never ours to begin with. Only by blessing do we acquire the right to consume what is, at its foundation, God's property.

## Torah & Livelihood

### Ve'asafta Deganecha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאָסַפְתָּ דְגָנֶךָ״ מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? — לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָמוּשׁ סֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֶּה מִפִּיךָ״ — יָכוֹל דְּבָרִים כִּכְתָבָן, תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאָסַפְתָּ דְגָנֶךָ״ — הַנְהֵג בָּהֶן מִנְהַג דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What** is the meaning of that which **the verse states: "And you shall gather your grain"? Because it is stated: "This Torah shall not depart from your mouths,** and you shall contemplate in it day and night" (Joshua 1:8), **I might** have thought **that these matters are** to be understood **as they are written;** one is to literally spend his days immersed exclusively in Torah study. Therefore, **the verse states: "And you shall gather your grain,** your wine and your oil," **assume in their** regard, **the way of the world;** set aside time not only for Torah, but also for work. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

*Tanu Rabbanan* raises the verse *ve-asafta deganecha* — you shall gather your grain — from the second paragraph of *Shema*. Given the command in *Sefer Yehoshua* that *lo yamush sefer ha-Torah ha-zeh mi-picha ve-hagita bo yomam va-layla* — the book of Torah shall not depart from your lips, you shall meditate in it day and night — one might conclude that the Torah mandates uninterrupted, exclusive Torah study. *Yakhol devarim ki-chtavam* — perhaps this is literal? The *gemara* answers: *talmud lomar ve-asafta deganecha* — no, you must also gather your grain. *Hanhag ba-hen minhag derech eretz* — alongside Torah one must conduct oneself in the way of normal economic life.

### Rabbi Yishmael Path

This is the position of Rabbi Yishmael: Torah study and *derech eretz* — ordinary work and livelihood — must coexist. The verse *ve-asafta deganecha* appears within *Shema* itself, immediately following the commandments of love and service of God, signaling that gathering grain is not a concession to human weakness but an integrated part of a life properly lived.

### Rabbi Shimon Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי אוֹמֵר: אֶפְשָׁר אָדָם חוֹרֵשׁ בִּשְׁעַת חֲרִישָׁה, וְזוֹרֵעַ בִּשְׁעַת זְרִיעָה, וְקוֹצֵר בִּשְׁעַת קְצִירָה, וְדָשׁ בִּשְׁעַת דִּישָׁה, וְזוֹרֶה בִּשְׁעַת הָרוּחַ, תּוֹרָה מַה תְּהֵא עָלֶיהָ? אֶלָּא בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם — מְלַאכְתָּן נַעֲשֵׂית עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָמְדוּ זָרִים וְרָעוּ צֹאנְכֶם וְגוֹ׳״, וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאֵין יִשְׂרָאֵל עוֹשִׂין רְצוֹנוֹ שֶׁל מָקוֹם — מְלַאכְתָּן נַעֲשֵׂית עַל יְדֵי עַצְמָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאָסַפְתָּ דְגָנֶךָ״. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא שֶׁמְּלֶאכֶת אֲחֵרִים נַעֲשֵׂית עַל יָדָן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָבַדְתָּ אֶת אוֹיְבֶךָ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: Is it possible that a person plows in the plowing season and sows in the sowing season and harvests in the harvest season and threshes in the threshing season and winnows in the windy season,** as grain is separated from the chaff by means of the wind, and is constantly busy; **what will become of Torah? Rather,** one must dedicate himself exclusively to Torah at the expense of other endeavors; as **when Israel performs God's will, their work is performed by others, as it is stated: "And strangers will stand and feed your flocks,** and foreigners will be your plowmen and your vinedressers" (Isaiah 61:5). **When Israel does not perform God's will, their work is performed by them** themselves, as it is stated: **"And you shall gather your grain." Moreover,** if Israel fails to perform God's will, **others' work will be performed by them, as it is stated: "You shall serve your enemy** whom God shall send against you, in hunger, in thirst, in nakedness and in want of all things" (Deuteronomy 28:48).

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai disagrees sharply. Is it truly possible, he asks, that a person plows at plowing time, sows at sowing time, harvests at harvest time, threshes at threshing time, and winnows when the wind blows — *Torah mah te-hei aleiha*? When will Torah be learned? If one's days are consumed by agricultural labor across all its seasons, serious Torah study becomes impossible. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai therefore holds that when Israel does God's will, *melachtan na'asit al yedei acherim* — others will do their work for them, as the verse says *ve-amdu zarim ve-ra'u tzonechem*, foreigners will shepherd your flocks. When Israel fails to do God's will, they must do their own work — *ve-asafta deganecha* — and worse, they will serve their enemies.

### Practical Success

The two positions thus represent not merely different philosophies but different visions of what a Torah community looks like in practice.

### Abaye Assessment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַרְבֵּה עָשׂוּ כְּרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וְעָלְתָה בְּיָדָן. כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי, וְלֹא עָלְתָה בְּיָדָן.
>
> **Translation:** Summing up this dispute, **Abaye said:** Although there is room for both opinions, **many have acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** and combined working for a living and learning Torah, **and** although they engaged in activities other than the study of Torah, **were successful** in their Torah study. **Many have acted in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai and were not successful** in their Torah study. They were ultimately forced to abandon their Torah study altogether.

*Amar Abaye*: many followed the path of Rabbi Yishmael and it succeeded for them — *alta be-yadam*, they managed both livelihood and Torah. Many followed the path of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and it did not succeed — *lo alta be-yadam*. The failure was likely twofold: they could not sustain themselves materially, and as a consequence their Torah learning was compromised as well.

### Rava Compromise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא לְרַבָּנַן: בְּמָטוּתָא מִינַּיְיכוּ, בְּיוֹמֵי נִיסָן וּבְיוֹמֵי תִּשְׁרֵי לָא תִּתְחֲזוֹ קַמַּאי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא תִּטַּרְדוּ בִּמְזוֹנַיְיכוּ כּוּלָּא שַׁתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rava said to the Sages** who would attend his study hall: **I implore you; during** the months of **Nisan and Tishrei,** the crucial agricultural periods, **do not appear before me.** Engage in your agricultural work then **so that you will not be preoccupied with your sustenance all year.**

*Amar Rava le-rabbanan*: please, do not appear before me during Nissan and Tishrei — the months of harvest and planting. He wanted his students to devote those two months to working in the fields so that they could support themselves, and thus have the remaining ten months free to study Torah without distraction. This is a practical compromise, tilting heavily toward learning while acknowledging that some period of sustained work is necessary. It implicitly acknowledges the *Maharsha*'s point: Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's path is an ideal appropriate only for a very small number of exceptional individuals. For the many — *harbe asu ke-Rabbi Yishmael ve-alta be-yadam* — Rabbi Yishmael's model is the one that actually works.

The *Rambam*'s treatment in *Pirkei Avot* and *Mishneh Torah* covers similar ground, criticizing those who sit and learn while expecting others to support them, while simultaneously, at the end of *Hilkhot Shemitah ve-Yovel*, holding out the ideal of one wholly devoted to knowing God and freed from material preoccupation — *kodesh kodashim*. These two poles map onto the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, with the broad community falling under Rabbi Yishmael's model and perhaps a rare few under Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's.

## Earlier vs Later Gen

### Early Gen Priority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאִי: בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים. דּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים עָשׂוּ תּוֹרָתָן קֶבַע וּמְלַאכְתָּן עֲרַאי, זוֹ וָזוֹ נִתְקַיְּימָה בְּיָדָן. דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים שֶׁעָשׂוּ מְלַאכְתָּן קֶבַע וְתוֹרָתָן עֲרַאי, זוֹ וָזוֹ לֹא נִתְקַיְּימָה בְּיָדָן.
>
> **Translation:** Summarizing these statements, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of** the *tanna* **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi El'ai: Come and see that the latter generations are not like the earlier generations;** rather they are their inferiors. **The earlier generations made their Torah permanent and their work occasional,** and **this,** Torah study, **and that,** their work, **were successful for them.** However, **the latter generations who made their work permanent and their Torah occasional, neither this nor that was successful for them.**

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yehuda be-rebbi Ilai*: come and see that the later generations are not like the earlier generations. The earlier generations made their Torah *keva* — their primary, defining endeavor — and their work *arai*, secondary and incidental. They did follow Rabbi Yishmael's model: they worked. But Torah was the center of their identity and their time, and work was what they did on the side because they had to.

### Late Gen Priority

*Doros ha-acharonim*, by contrast, made their work *keva* — primary in time, identity, and focus — and their Torah *arai*. They define themselves by their occupation and squeeze Torah in when they can. The consequence: *zu ve-zu lo niskayma be-yadam* — neither succeeded. They neither achieved great material success nor serious Torah learning. The critique is not merely quantitative but structural: when work becomes the center, it reshapes how one approaches everything else, including religious obligation.

### Loophole Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי אִלְעַאִי: בֹּא וּרְאֵה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים. דּוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הָיוּ מַכְנִיסִין פֵּירוֹתֵיהֶן דֶּרֶךְ טְרַקְסְמוֹן כְּדֵי לְחַיְּיבָן בְּמַעֲשֵׂר, דּוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים מַכְנִיסִין פֵּירוֹתֵיהֶן דֶּרֶךְ גַּגּוֹת, דֶּרֶךְ חֲצֵרוֹת, דֶּרֶךְ קַרְפֵּיפוֹת, כְּדֵי לְפׇטְרָן מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אֵין הַטֶּבֶל מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּמַעֲשֵׂר עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה פְּנֵי הַבַּיִת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בִּעַרְתִּי הַקֹּדֶשׁ מִן הַבַּיִת״.
>
> **Translation:** Along these lines, **Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi El'ai: Come and see that the latter generations are not like the earlier generations.** In the **earlier generations,** people **would bring their fruits into their courtyards through the main gate in order to obligate them in tithes.** However, **the latter generations bring their fruits through roofs, through courtyards and through enclosed courtyards,** avoiding the main gate **in order to exempt them from** the mitzva of **tithing. As Rabbi Yannai said: Untithed produce is not obligated in** the mitzva of **tithing until it sees the front of the house** through which people enter and exit, and it is brought into the house that way **as it is stated** in the formula of the confession of the tithes: **"I have removed the consecrated from the house"** (Deuteronomy 26:13), as the obligation to tithe produce whose purpose has not yet been designated takes effect only when it is brought into the house.

A second teaching from the same chain of transmission sharpens this critique concretely. The earlier generations would bring their produce in through the *traksmon* — the main gate of the house — specifically in order to render it obligated in *maaser*. They actively sought to fulfill the *mitzvah*. The later generations bring their produce in through rooftops, courtyards, and side passages — *derech gagot, derech chatzerot, derech chapifot* — in order to exempt it from *maaser*. Since the *maaser* obligation is triggered by the produce entering the house through its main entrance, using back routes is a technical loophole that avoids the obligation. They could still eat the produce in an *achilas arai* manner — feeding it to animals, eating it casually — but could not eat it as a fixed meal (*achilas keva*) without first separating *maaser*.

### Maaser Obligation

*Amar Rabbi Yannai*: the *tevel* does not become obligated in *maaser* until it sees *pnei ha-bayit* — the face of the house, the main entrance — as the *pasuk* says *bi'arti ha-kodesh min ha-bayit*, I have removed the sanctified portion from the house, implying the produce entered through the normal main entrance and thereby became obligated. Rabbi Yochanan adds that even *chatzer* — the courtyard — creates the obligation, since the verse *ve-achlu bi-she'arecha ve-save'u* speaks of eating within one's gates, not merely within one's house. Some hold that this latter position is only rabbinic, but it expands the concept of *pnei ha-bayit* beyond the doorway of the house itself.

### Money & Observance

The critique lands precisely here. The later generations are so focused on their earnings — *melachtan keva* — that this orientation corrupts their observance across the board. They find halakhic workarounds for *maaser* obligations, driven purely by economic calculation. The *keva* of work does not merely crowd out time for Torah; it reconfigures one's entire relationship to *mitzvot*, making one alert to exemptions and loopholes rather than to fulfillment. That which was meant to be *keva* — Torah and its obligations — becomes *arai*, while that which was meant to be *arai* — making a living — becomes the organizing principle of life.
