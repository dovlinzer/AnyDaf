# Berakhot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Mishna Categories

### Welcome & Overview

Today's daf is 35, and with it we enter *Kaitzad Mevarkhim* — what can fairly be called the second half of the masechet. The first half was devoted to the fixed daily liturgy: *Shema* and *Shemoneh Esreh*. From this point forward, the masechet turns to *brachot* that arise at various moments throughout the day outside of any fixed liturgy, with the primary focus on blessings before and after eating, followed later by other *brachot* that may occur during the course of daily life.

### Mishna: Blessings

The *mishna* at the top of 35a opens: *kaitzad mevarkhim al ha-perot* — how does one make a blessing over fruit? *Al perot ha-ilan hu omer borei pri ha-etz*: for fruit from the tree, one recites *borei pri ha-etz*. The *mishna* already presupposes that even when fruit is transformed into juice, one would naturally assume the blessing remains *borei pri ha-etz* in its original form. The *mishna* must therefore specify the exception: *chutz min ha-yayin* — for wine one recites *borei pri ha-gefen*. God, after all, did not create wine directly; human processing was involved. Therefore, even in reciting a special *bracha* for this transformed form, we refer to the fruit in its natural state and say *borei pri ha-gefen*.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

One notable feature of the *mishna*'s language is its use of the word *ilan* for tree in the body of the rule, while the *bracha* text itself employs *etz*. As one of the participants observed, *ilan* does not appear in the *mikra*, whereas *etz* — as in *etz pri oseh pri le-mino* — is biblical language. This reflects a broader principle: the formulations of *brachot*, particularly their *chatimot*, draw heavily on the language of *psukim*. The same dynamic explains the well-known *machlokret* among *Sefardim* over whether to pronounce *borei pri ha-gafen* or *borei pri ha-gefen* — the form *gefen* appears in *Tanakh* mid-sentence, while *gafen* is the pausal form for end-of-verse; since *gafen* as such never appears as a final word in *Tanakh*, some hold that one must use only the form that actually appears in the text.

### Wine & Bread

Continuing the *mishna*: *ve-al perot ha-aretz hu omer borei pri ha-adama* — for fruit of the ground, one says *borei pri ha-adama* — with the exception of bread. Here too the *mishna* assumes that since bread derives from wheat, one might say *borei pri ha-adama*, as one would for wheat eaten in its natural form. But for bread, one says *ha-motzi lechem min ha-aretz*.

### Hamotzi Prayer

The *bracha* of *ha-motzi* is interesting because it does approach the idea of the finished product — it says *lechem*, bread, not wheat. Yet the emphasis remains on the divine act of bringing forth the wheat from the earth, rather than on the human process of baking. The *bracha* is thus a dual acknowledgment: it names the final human product while directing our attention to God's creative role in producing the grain.

### Categories: Earth & Veg

The *mishna* then states: *ve-ha-yerakot hu omer borei pri ha-adama* — for vegetables one also recites *borei pri ha-adama*, the same *bracha* as for what it earlier called *perot ha-aretz*. This naturally raises the question: what distinguishes *perot ha-aretz* from *yerakot* if both receive the same *bracha*? One way to draw the distinction — and *Rashi* addresses this as well — is that *perot ha-aretz* refers to things that grow above the ground but are not fruits of a tree, while *yerakot* grow in the ground. That said, *Rashi* also includes *kitniyot* (legumes) within the vegetable category, which complicates a simple above-ground versus below-ground division. The precise scope of *perot ha-aretz* versus *yerakot* remains a point of discussion as the *gemara* continues.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

Rabbi Yehuda dissents on the last category, holding that one should not say *borei pri ha-adama* over vegetables but rather *borei minei deshaim* — creator of types of herbs, or literally grasses. This formulation suggests that the vegetable category extends beyond things that grow below the ground and encompasses a broader range of low-growing plants.

What this *mishna* accomplishes overall is the laying out of the various categories of *brachot*: that *brachot* are differentiated according to what one is eating, that a transformed form of a food in many cases retains the original *bracha*, and that wine and bread are notable exceptions receiving their own distinct *brachot*. What the *mishna* does not yet address is the source of the obligation to make *brachot* in the first place — that is precisely where the *gemara* begins.

## Kodesh Hilulim & Neta

### Kodesh Hilulim Source

The *gemara* opens with the foundational question: *mena hanei milei* — what is the source for making *brachot* before eating? *Detanu rabbanan*: the Torah's discussion of *neta revai* — the fourth-year fruit, which must be brought to Jerusalem and eaten there in a manner akin to *maaser sheni* — includes the phrase *kodesh hilulim l'Hashem*, sanctified as a praising to God. This is interpreted to mean: *melamed she-te'unin bracha lifneihem u-le'achareihem* — it teaches that such produce requires a blessing both before and after eating. This is the initial scriptural basis for *brachot* over food.

### Rabbi Akiva Stringency

Beyond this, Rabbi Akiva states: *assur le-adam she-yitom klum kodem she-yevarech* — it is forbidden for a person to taste anything before reciting a blessing. This formulation is striking. Until now the language has been about obligation and praise; here Rabbi Akiva introduces the concept of prohibition, which points toward a qualitatively different concern — that one is not entitled to eat at all without first acknowledging the divine source of the food. One of the participants astutely noted that Rabbi Akiva's emphasis on the prohibition may be rooted in the structure of the *pasuk* itself: three years of *orlah* in which eating is forbidden, followed by the fourth year of *hilulim*, followed by the fifth year in which one may eat. The sequence suggests: forbidden, then praise, then permitted — echoing Rabbi Akiva's insistence that the blessing is a precondition for eating.

### Kodesh Hilulim Query

The *gemara* now questions whether the phrase *kodesh hilulim* is actually being used to teach the obligation to make *brachot*. The word *hilulim* is written in a doubled form and may be carrying multiple halakhic messages simultaneously. The *gemara* therefore asks: is this really the source for *brachot*, or does the verse need to be used for other teachings?

### Hilulim as Chillulim

The *gemara* offers two other things that *hilulim* comes to teach. First, reading the *heh* as a *chet* — the two letters are phonetically related as gutturals and visually similar — gives us *chillulim*, meaning redemption or transfer of sanctity. This teaches that the law of *neta revai* parallels *maaser sheni*: if one does not wish to bring the fourth-year produce to Jerusalem, one may redeem its value onto money and then bring the money to Jerusalem. *Achlei u-vahadar achlei*: transfer the sanctity, then eat — consistent with the following *pasuk* which speaks of eating.

### Shira Over Wine

The second teaching from *hilulim* concerns the scope of *neta revai* altogether. The *gemara* cites the principle: *davar ha-ta'un shira ta'un chillul* — something over which one sings *shira* falls within the category of *neta revai*, while something over which one does not sing is not subject to this law. The question becomes: what is it that one sings over? Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani says in the name of Rabbi Yonatan: *minayin she-ein omrim shira ela al ha-yayin* — how do we know that *shira* is recited only over wine? The source is the parable in *Shoftim* (Judges), where in the famous allegory of the trees choosing a king, the grapevine declares: have I given up my wine *that gladdens the hearts of God and people* that I should have to become king? The *gemara* asks: if wine gladdens people, in what sense does it gladden God? The answer is that it is through the *shira* itself — the Levitical singing performed in the Temple during the wine libation that accompanied each *korban*.

### Wine Gladdens Hearts

This point has significant practical implications. The *Levi'im* sang *shira* not during the slaughter of the sacrifice, not during the sprinkling of the blood, not during the placing of the limbs on the altar — but only at the very end of the *avodah*, when the wine of the libation was being poured. *Ein omrim shira ela al ha-yayin*. This is also the halakhic model for *Kiddush* and *Havdalah* — rituals of verbal praise and sanctification are performed specifically over wine. The *gemara* here already knew of *Kiddush*, *Havdalah*, and *Birkat Hamazon* as wine-based rituals; the expansion to *Sheva Brachot*, *Birkat Erusin*, and *Birkat Brit Milah* over wine, while not sourced in the *gemara*, further reflects this same principle that *shira* and formal praise carry greatest ritual weight when performed over wine.

The upshot of this approach is that *hilulim* is being used to teach that only grapevines fall within the scope of *neta revai*, since only over wine is *shira* recited. If so, the word *hilulim* has already been fully deployed for this teaching and cannot simultaneously be used to derive the obligation of *brachot*.

### Wine Limitation Debate

The *gemara* therefore states: *haniicha le-man de-tani neta revai* — this works out well according to the version of the *mishnah* that uses the term *neta revai*, fourth-year planting, implying the *mitzvah* applies broadly and is not limited to the vine. If that is the case, the word *hilulim* is not needed to limit the category to wine, and it remains available to teach the obligation to make *brachot*. But according to the version that reads *kerem revai* — fourth-year vine — meaning the *mitzvah* is limited to grapevines from the outset, then *hilulim* must be used to explain *why* it is limited to the vine, and cannot simultaneously teach *brachot*. This debate is recorded explicitly: Rabbi Chiyya and Rabbi Shimon *berebbi* disagree — one transmits *kerem revai*, the other *neta revai*.

### Gezeirah Shavah

The *gemara* then proposes a way out for the opinion that holds *kerem revai*: even according to that view, perhaps the limitation of the fourth-year law to the vine is derived not from *hilulim* at all but from a *gezeirah shavah*. A *braita* cites Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: the *pasuk* in the context of *orlah* and *neta revai* says *le-hosif lachem tevu'ato* — to increase for you its yield — and elsewhere the Torah speaks of *tevu'at ha-kerem*, the yield of the vine. The word *tevu'ah* appears in both places; this *gezeirah shavah* links the two and can be used to establish that the fourth-year category is vineyard-specific. If the limitation to wine is learned from the *gezeirah shavah*, then *hilulim* is freed up to teach something else — namely, the requirement of a *bracha*.

The *gemara* is quite strict about this principle: once a word has been used to derive one *halacha*, it generally cannot also be used to derive another. As the *gemara* presses further, even granting the *gezeirah shavah*, the word *hilulim* appears in doubled form, but one usage has already been spent on the law of redemption — *chillul*. At most, then, one *bracha* can be derived. And if only one *bracha* is available, the *gemara* reasons it should be the *bracha* after eating, since we already have a precedent for that in *Birkat Hamazon*.

### Beyond Wine

This leaves the *bracha* before eating without a source from *hilulim*. But the *gemara* responds: *ha lo kashya, de-ati be-kal vachomer* — that is not a difficulty, since we derive it by *kal vachomer*. *Keshehu save'a mevarech keshehu ra'ev lo kol sheken* — if one makes a *bracha* when sated, how much more so when hungry? When hungry, the appreciation of God's gift of food is more immediate and powerful; after eating, one has more fully experienced the blessing but perhaps with somewhat less intensity of anticipation. Therefore, a *bracha* before eating follows logically from the *bracha* after. The *gemara* at this stage seriously entertains the possibility that this *kal vachomer* has the force of a biblical derivation.

But even granting all of this, we have only established *brachot* for wine and grapevines. *She'ar minin minayin* — how do we extend the requirement to all other foods?

## All Foods Blessing

### Kal Vachomer Logic

The *gemara* attempts to extend the requirement broadly: *di-yalfi mi-kerem* — let us derive from the case of the vine. Just as the vine is something from which one derives benefit and over which a *bracha* is required, so too everything from which one derives benefit should require a *bracha*. But the *gemara* challenges this: perhaps the vine is distinctive because of the law of *olelot* — the unformed clusters that must be left for the poor, a law unique to the vineyard. Maybe the requirement of a *bracha* is connected to that distinctive feature, even if there is no logical reason why it should be. This is the *gemara*'s standard mode of objection when trying to prevent one case from becoming a paradigm: the mere existence of a distinctive characteristic, regardless of its logical relevance, blocks a generalization.

The *gemara* therefore brings in a second case: *kama tochiach* — let grain prove it. We know a *bracha* is required after eating grain because the *pasuk* says *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta*, the source for *Birkat Hamazon*. Grain and wine together prove that the principle — deriving benefit obligates a *bracha* — is not about *olelot*. But then grain has its own distinctive feature: *chayevet be-challah*, the obligation to separate *challah* applies to grain and not to wine. Perhaps the *bracha* requirement is tied to *challah*. So the *gemara* invokes the vine to prove it is not about *challah*, and grain to prove it is not about *olelot* — and the reasoning goes in a circle.

### Grain & Seven Species

The *gemara* next attempts to derive the obligation from *shivat ha-minim*, the seven species. The *pasuk* in *Devarim* describing the Land of Israel enumerates the seven species and concludes with the promise *lo techsar kol bah* — you shall lack nothing — followed by the verse *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta*. If that verse of *bracha* applies not only to bread but to all seven species, then we have a biblical source for *brachot* after eating all seven species. And then, by the earlier *kal vachomer*, if one blesses after eating, one certainly blesses before. This would give us *brachot* before and after for the full set of seven species.

### Tzad Hashaveh

But the *gemara* again objects: *mah le-shivat ha-minim she-kein chayavin be-bikurim* — the seven species are distinctive because one brings *bikurim* only from them. Therefore the *bracha* obligation might be tied to that unique characteristic. The *gemara* had tried a similar move earlier with grain and wine, using the *tzad hashaveh* — the common denominator — when individual cases failed. When both grain and wine were brought as sources, the *gemara* had observed that both have a *tzad mizbeach*, an aspect of being offered on the altar. Perhaps the *bracha* requirement applies only to foods that have this altar-connection.

### Olives Included

The *gemara* notes that at most one could extend this to olives, since olive oil is also brought on the *mizbeach*. The *gemara* briefly considers deriving this from the word *kerem* in *kerem revai* — one *pasuk* in *Shoftim* speaks of *kerem zayit*, an olive orchard. But Rav Papa rules: *kerem zayit ikri, kerem stam lo ikri* — it may be called an olive *kerem* with a qualifier, but *kerem* unmodified refers only to the vine. So even including olives via the *tzad hashaveh* argument, the challenge stands: the obligation might be limited to things that have an *altar* dimension.

### All Foods Challenge

Having exhausted the *tzad hashaveh* approach, the *gemara* attempts one more avenue: *ela de-yalif lah mi-shivat ha-minim*. If the verse *ve-achalta ve-savata u-verachta* applies to all seven species and gives us a *bracha* after eating all of them, and we apply the *kal vachomer* to get a *bracha* before, perhaps that is broad enough. The *gemara* responds again: *mah le-shivat ha-minim she-kein chayavin be-bikurim* — again, the *bikurim* connection is distinctive. And critically, the *gemara* adds: *ve-od hatenei le-acharav, le-fanav minayin* — all of this still only gives you a *bracha* afterwards; how would you know about a *bracha* before? The *kal vachomer* answers that, as before. But the underlying point is that even for the seven species we cannot escape the objection based on their distinctive status.

There is something conceptually rich about why *bikurim* and *tzad mizbeach* keep appearing as obstacles. Both *bikurim* and the altar offerings involve bringing food to the *Mikdash* with words of praise — the very model that the *hilulim* verse evoked at the outset. The connection between *bracha* and *kodesh* runs throughout: perhaps *brachot* are obligatory specifically for things that enter the sanctified space of the Temple, and not for the ordinary foods of daily life.

### Sevara: Forbidden

At this point the *gemara* sets aside the entire derivational enterprise. *Ela sevara hu* — it is simply a matter of logic. *Assur lo le-adam she-yehene min ha-olam ha-zeh belo bracha* — it is forbidden for a person to derive benefit from this world without a blessing. This echoes and grounds Rabbi Akiva's formulation from the outset, but now with a different emphasis. The earlier approaches centered on *hilulim* — on praising God for His gifts — and could conceivably be satisfied by a *bracha* after eating alone, or at most an obligation before that flows from a *kal vachomer*. Here the emphasis is categorically different: one is simply *not entitled* to eat without first acknowledging that the food comes from God. The *bracha* is not just an expression of gratitude; it is the act of obtaining permission.

The *Tosafot* at the bottom of the *daf* makes this explicit: *asmachta be-alma* — the verse of *hilulim* was only ever a scriptural support for what the *gemara* now understands to be the real foundation. The *gemara* originally considered that the obligation before eating might be fully biblical, whether from *hilulim* directly or through a *kal vachomer*. That possibility is now set aside, and the conclusion is that the *bracha* before eating is rabbinic — grounded in the *sevara* that one may not derive benefit from God's world without acknowledgment — while the *Tosafot* at the top of the *daf* notes the irony: the *gemara* had to work hard to justify why *Shema* starts with "when" rather than "whether," but here no such question arises. Of course one makes *brachot* before eating; it is self-evident that one does not derive benefit without blessing.

## Me'ilah Principle

### Forbidden Benefit

*Tanu rabbanan*: it is forbidden for a person to derive benefit from this world without a blessing. The *gemara* now restates the principle in full formal terms, having established it as the operative basis in the preceding analysis.

### Me'ilah Equivalence

*Ve-khol ha-nehene min ha-olam ha-zeh belo bracha ma'al* — whoever derives benefit from this world without a *bracha* has transgressed *me'ilah*, the prohibition of deriving benefit from sanctified property. The force of this is considerable. Everything in the world belongs to God; God's property is, in a sense, sanctified. Just as one who uses property belonging to the Temple without authorization commits *me'ilah* — theft of sanctified objects — so too one who eats without a *bracha* has taken from that which is God's without permission. The *bracha* functions as the mechanism of authorization. This introduces a profound dimension of *kedusha* into the act of eating: just as the earlier *hilulim* framework evoked the *Mikdash* experience of bringing produce with songs of praise, here too the language of *me'ilah* brings the sanctity of the Temple into the domain of the everyday meal.

### Learn from Sage

The *gemara* asks: *mai takantei* — what is the remedy if one derived benefit without a *bracha* and has thereby committed *me'ilah*? The initial suggestion is *yelech etzel chakham* — one should go to a sage. But the *gemara* immediately objects: *mai avid lei* — what can the sage do? *Ha avi issura* — the transgression has already occurred. *Ela amar Rava*: one should go to a sage *me'ikara* — beforehand — *ve-yilamdenu brachot*, so that one learns all the *brachot* properly, *ke-dei she-lo yavo lidei me'ilah*, so as not to come to transgress *me'ilah*. The *Maharsha* adds that this instruction carries particular weight because the laws of *brachot* are genuinely complex — which *bracha* applies to which food, and in what form. Even making the wrong *bracha*, one that does not fulfill the obligation, may leave the person having derived benefit without a valid *bracha*, and thus still subject to the *me'ilah* concern.

### God Owns All

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: whoever derives benefit from this world without a *bracha* is as though he derived benefit from *kodshei shamayim* — sanctified property of Heaven. The *pasuk* says *la-Hashem ha-aretz u-mlo'ah* — the earth and all its fullness belongs to God.

### Before & After

But Rabbi Levi raises an apparent contradiction. That same verse affirms *la-Hashem ha-aretz u-mlo'ah* — it all belongs to God. Yet another *pasuk* says *ha-shamayim shamayim la-Hashem ve-ha-aretz natan li-vnei adam* — the heavens are God's, but the earth He has given to humanity. *Lo kashya*: the contradiction is not difficult. *Kan kodem bracha, kan le-achar bracha* — before the *bracha*, the earth belongs entirely to God; after the *bracha*, having acknowledged that this is God's gift, we have license to use it. The act of blessing transforms our relationship to the food: without it, we are taking what is not ours; with it, we receive what has been given.

### Steal from God

*Amar Rabbi Chanina bar Papa*: whoever derives benefit from this world without a *bracha* is as though he stole from both God and from *Knesset Yisrael*. The *pasuk* says *gozel aviv ve-imo ve-omer ein pasha, chaver hu le-ish mashchit* — one who steals from his father and mother and says it is no sin is a companion to a destructive person. *V-ein aviv ela ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu* — the father is God (*halo hu avicha kanecha*); *ve-ein imo ela Knesset Yisrael* — the mother is the Jewish people (*shma bni musar avicha ve-al titosh torat imecha*). The phrase *ve-omer ein pasha* — saying it is no sin — is key. *Rashi* explains: one who dismisses the importance of *brachot*, announcing that it is trivial, teaches others to do the same and spreads neglect of the obligation.

### Yeravam Comparison

*Mai chaver hu le-ish mashchit*? *Amar Rabbi Chanina bar Papa*: such a person is a companion to *Yerovam ben Nevat* — *she-hishchit et Yisrael le-avihem she-ba-shamayim*, who corrupted Israel before their Father in Heaven. This comparison may seem extreme, but its logic runs deeper than it first appears. *Yerovam ben Nevat*'s defining sin was preventing the Jewish people from making *aliyah la-regel* — from ascending to the Temple, from bringing their produce, their grain and wine and oil and animals, to the *Mikdash* in acknowledgment and gratitude to God. One who dismisses *brachot* does the same thing at the level of daily life: he severs the sanctity of eating from its connection to divine acknowledgment. He steals not just from God but from *Knesset Yisrael*, because — as *Rashi* elsewhere notes, echoing the *neta revai pasuk* — it is the act of acknowledgment that calls forth continued blessing upon the land. Remove the *brachot*, and you remove the blessing for the entire people.

The two frameworks the *gemara* has now presented — *hilulim* as praise, and *sevara* as the prohibition of unauthorized benefit — both point toward the same profound sanctification of everyday eating. The first evokes the *Mikdash* model: we bring food before God, we sing over it, we acknowledge its source. The second makes an even starker claim: the food was never ours to begin with. Only by blessing do we acquire the right to consume what is, at its foundation, God's property.

## Torah & Livelihood

### Ve'asafta Deganecha

*Tanu Rabbanan* raises the verse *ve-asafta deganecha* — you shall gather your grain — from the second paragraph of *Shema*. Given the command in *Sefer Yehoshua* that *lo yamush sefer ha-Torah ha-zeh mi-picha ve-hagita bo yomam va-layla* — the book of Torah shall not depart from your lips, you shall meditate in it day and night — one might conclude that the Torah mandates uninterrupted, exclusive Torah study. *Yakhol devarim ki-chtavam* — perhaps this is literal? The *gemara* answers: *talmud lomar ve-asafta deganecha* — no, you must also gather your grain. *Hanhag ba-hen minhag derech eretz* — alongside Torah one must conduct oneself in the way of normal economic life.

### Rabbi Yishmael Path

This is the position of Rabbi Yishmael: Torah study and *derech eretz* — ordinary work and livelihood — must coexist. The verse *ve-asafta deganecha* appears within *Shema* itself, immediately following the commandments of love and service of God, signaling that gathering grain is not a concession to human weakness but an integrated part of a life properly lived.

### Rabbi Shimon Path

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai disagrees sharply. Is it truly possible, he asks, that a person plows at plowing time, sows at sowing time, harvests at harvest time, threshes at threshing time, and winnows when the wind blows — *Torah mah te-hei aleiha*? When will Torah be learned? If one's days are consumed by agricultural labor across all its seasons, serious Torah study becomes impossible. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai therefore holds that when Israel does God's will, *melachtan na'asit al yedei acherim* — others will do their work for them, as the verse says *ve-amdu zarim ve-ra'u tzonechem*, foreigners will shepherd your flocks. When Israel fails to do God's will, they must do their own work — *ve-asafta deganecha* — and worse, they will serve their enemies.

### Practical Success

The two positions thus represent not merely different philosophies but different visions of what a Torah community looks like in practice.

### Abaye Assessment

*Amar Abaye*: many followed the path of Rabbi Yishmael and it succeeded for them — *alta be-yadam*, they managed both livelihood and Torah. Many followed the path of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai and it did not succeed — *lo alta be-yadam*. The failure was likely twofold: they could not sustain themselves materially, and as a consequence their Torah learning was compromised as well.

### Rava Compromise

*Amar Rava le-rabbanan*: please, do not appear before me during Nissan and Tishrei — the months of harvest and planting. He wanted his students to devote those two months to working in the fields so that they could support themselves, and thus have the remaining ten months free to study Torah without distraction. This is a practical compromise, tilting heavily toward learning while acknowledging that some period of sustained work is necessary. It implicitly acknowledges the *Maharsha*'s point: Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's path is an ideal appropriate only for a very small number of exceptional individuals. For the many — *harbe asu ke-Rabbi Yishmael ve-alta be-yadam* — Rabbi Yishmael's model is the one that actually works.

The *Rambam*'s treatment in *Pirkei Avot* and *Mishneh Torah* covers similar ground, criticizing those who sit and learn while expecting others to support them, while simultaneously, at the end of *Hilkhot Shemitah ve-Yovel*, holding out the ideal of one wholly devoted to knowing God and freed from material preoccupation — *kodesh kodashim*. These two poles map onto the debate between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, with the broad community falling under Rabbi Yishmael's model and perhaps a rare few under Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's.

## Earlier vs Later Gen

### Early Gen Priority

*Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Yehuda be-rebbi Ilai*: come and see that the later generations are not like the earlier generations. The earlier generations made their Torah *keva* — their primary, defining endeavor — and their work *arai*, secondary and incidental. They did follow Rabbi Yishmael's model: they worked. But Torah was the center of their identity and their time, and work was what they did on the side because they had to.

### Late Gen Priority

*Doros ha-acharonim*, by contrast, made their work *keva* — primary in time, identity, and focus — and their Torah *arai*. They define themselves by their occupation and squeeze Torah in when they can. The consequence: *zu ve-zu lo niskayma be-yadam* — neither succeeded. They neither achieved great material success nor serious Torah learning. The critique is not merely quantitative but structural: when work becomes the center, it reshapes how one approaches everything else, including religious obligation.

### Loophole Examples

A second teaching from the same chain of transmission sharpens this critique concretely. The earlier generations would bring their produce in through the *traksmon* — the main gate of the house — specifically in order to render it obligated in *maaser*. They actively sought to fulfill the *mitzvah*. The later generations bring their produce in through rooftops, courtyards, and side passages — *derech gagot, derech chatzerot, derech chapifot* — in order to exempt it from *maaser*. Since the *maaser* obligation is triggered by the produce entering the house through its main entrance, using back routes is a technical loophole that avoids the obligation. They could still eat the produce in an *achilas arai* manner — feeding it to animals, eating it casually — but could not eat it as a fixed meal (*achilas keva*) without first separating *maaser*.

### Maaser Obligation

*Amar Rabbi Yannai*: the *tevel* does not become obligated in *maaser* until it sees *pnei ha-bayit* — the face of the house, the main entrance — as the *pasuk* says *bi'arti ha-kodesh min ha-bayit*, I have removed the sanctified portion from the house, implying the produce entered through the normal main entrance and thereby became obligated. Rabbi Yochanan adds that even *chatzer* — the courtyard — creates the obligation, since the verse *ve-achlu bi-she'arecha ve-save'u* speaks of eating within one's gates, not merely within one's house. Some hold that this latter position is only rabbinic, but it expands the concept of *pnei ha-bayit* beyond the doorway of the house itself.

### Money & Observance

The critique lands precisely here. The later generations are so focused on their earnings — *melachtan keva* — that this orientation corrupts their observance across the board. They find halakhic workarounds for *maaser* obligations, driven purely by economic calculation. The *keva* of work does not merely crowd out time for Torah; it reconfigures one's entire relationship to *mitzvot*, making one alert to exemptions and loopholes rather than to fulfillment. That which was meant to be *keva* — Torah and its obligations — becomes *arai*, while that which was meant to be *arai* — making a living — becomes the organizing principle of life.
