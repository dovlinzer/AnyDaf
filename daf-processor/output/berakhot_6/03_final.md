# Berakhot 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prayer in Synagogue

### Prayer Heard in Shul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אַבָּא בִּנְיָמִין אוֹמֵר: אֵין תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל אָדָם נִשְׁמַעַת אֶלָּא בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִשְׁמֹעַ אֶל הָרִנָּה וְאֶל הַתְּפִלָּה״. בִּמְקוֹם רִנָּה — שָׁם תְּהֵא תְּפִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Abba Binyamin said: One's prayer is only** fully **heard in a synagogue, as it is stated** with regard to King Solomon's prayer in the Temple: "Yet have You turned toward the prayer of Your servant and to his supplication, Lord my God, **to listen to the song and the prayer** which Your servant prays before You on this day" (I Kings 8:28). The following verse concludes: "To hear the prayer Your servant directs toward this place" (I Kings 8:29). We see that one's prayer is heard specifically in the Temple, of which the synagogue is a microcosm (Rav Yoshiyahu Pinto). It may be inferred that **in a place of song,** a synagogue where God's praises are sung, **there prayer should be.**

The daf opens in the middle of daf 6 with a series of statements attributed to Abba Binyamin. Having already encountered his teaching about the mazikim, we now pick up with a new Baraita: *Tanya Abba Binyamin omer, ein tfilato shel adam nishmaat ela b'beit haknesset* — a person's prayer is heard primarily in the synagogue. The proof text is *lishmoa el harina v'el hatefilla*, "to listen to the rejoicing and to the prayer," from which the Gemara derives: *b'makom rina sham t'hei tfillah* — in the place of singing, there should be prayer.

The significance of this statement is twofold, and its context is worth appreciating. In the period of the Gemara and the Mishnah, the rabbis were not the institutional leaders of the synagogues. There was a distinct leadership structure for the beit haknesset, separate from the rabbinic enterprise centered in the *Beit HaMidrash*, and archaeological evidence supports this as well. This creates a broader question about the relationship between tefillah and Talmud Torah, and between the beit haknesset and the *Beit HaMidrash* as competing or complementary loci of religious life. There are in fact other Gemaras that speak of a person's prayer being heard where he learns — not necessarily in the beit haknesset at all. And the statement here is not even specifically about minyan: one might daven at home alone, or in shul alone, or with a minyan at home or in shul. This is a statement about location, not quorum, and it is not self-evident that the synagogue is the superior location.

### Rina & Singing

Given that context, what makes the synagogue the preferred place? The answer the Baraita gives is *rina* — and *rina* is clearly something distinct from *tefillah*. Rashi explains: *beit haknesset shesham omrim hatzibur shirot v'tishbachot b'ne'imat kol arev* — the synagogue is where the community recites songs and praises with a pleasing and sweet voice. The communal singing is not incidental; it is the very quality that elevates prayer in the synagogue over prayer elsewhere. The Maharsha adds a parallel observation: just as Shmoneh Esrei is preceded by *shevach v'tishbacha*, the praises of P'sukei D'zimra, so too the rina of the congregation functions as a proper lead-in and accompaniment to the central act of *bakasha*.

### Shul vs. Beit Midrash

There is also a historical and liturgical context to the prominence of *rina*: the rabbis, particularly after the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, were consciously trying to recreate something of the Temple experience. The *Shirat HaLevi'im* — the Levitical singing with instruments — was a defining feature of the Temple service, and the characterization of the beit haknesset as a *mikdash me'at* makes it entirely coherent that communal singing would be understood as one of the central features distinguishing it as a place of prayer.

## God's Presence

### God in Congregation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מָצוּי בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** In explaining Abba Binyamin's statement, **Rabin bar Rav Adda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, is located in a synagogue? As it is stated: "God stands in the congregation of God;** in the midst of the judges He judges" (Psalms 82:1). The congregation of God is the place where people congregate to sing God's praises, and God is located among His congregation.

### Ten to One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן לַעֲשָׂרָה שֶׁמִּתְפַּלְּלִין שֶׁשְּׁכִינָה עִמָּהֶם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהִים נִצָּב בַּעֲדַת אֵל״. וּמִנַּיִן לִשְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין בַּדִּין שֶׁשְּׁכִינָה עִמָּהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּקֶרֶב אֱלֹהִים יִשְׁפֹּט״. וּמִנַּיִן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁיּוֹשְׁבִין וְעוֹסְקִין בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁשְּׁכִינָה עִמָּהֶם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז נִדְבְּרוּ יִרְאֵי ה׳ אִישׁ אֶל רֵעֵהוּ וַיַּקְשֵׁב ה׳״ וְגוֹ׳. מַאי ״וּלְחֹשְׁבֵי שְׁמוֹ״? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָשַׁב אָדָם לַעֲשׂוֹת מִצְוָה, וְנֶאֱנַס, וְלֹא עֲשָׂאָהּ — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִילּוּ עֲשָׂאָהּ. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד שֶׁיּוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁשְּׁכִינָה עִמּוֹ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּכָל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר אַזְכִּיר אֶת שְׁמִי אָבוֹא אֵלֶיךָ וּבֵרַכְתִּיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived that **ten people who pray, the Divine Presence is with them? As it is stated: "God stands in the congregation of God,"** and the minimum number of people that constitute a congregation is a quorum of ten. **From where** is it derived **that three who sit in judgment, the Divine Presence is with them?** It is derived from this same verse, **as it is stated: "In the midst of the judges He judges,"** and the minimum number of judges that comprises a court is three. **From where** is it derived **that two who sit and engage in Torah** study, **the Divine Presence is with them? As it is stated: "Then they that feared the Lord spoke one with the other, and the Lord listened,** and heard, and a book of remembrance was written before Him, for them that fear the Lord, and that think upon His name" (Malachi 3:16). The Divine Presence listens to any two God-fearing individuals who speak with each other. With regard to this verse, the Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase, **"And that think upon His name"? Rav Ashi said:** If **a person intended to perform a mitzva, but due to** circumstances **beyond** his **control, he did not perform it, the verse ascribes him** credit **as if he performed** the mitzva, as he is among those that think upon His name. **And from where** is it derived **that when even one who sits and engages in Torah** study, **the Divine Presence is with him? As it is stated: "In every place where I cause My Name to be mentioned, I will come to you and bless you"** (Exodus 20:21); God blesses even a single person who mentions God's name, a reference to Torah study (*Iyyun Ya'akov*).

From this foundation, the Gemara proceeds through a well-known descending series, familiar from the Mishnah in Pirkei Avot. *U'minayin l'asara shemitpallelim she'Shechina imahem* — when ten people pray, the Shechina is with them, as proven again from *Elohim nitzav b'adat el*, where the divine presence accompanies the congregation. *U'minayin l'shlosha sheyoshvim b'din she'Shechina imahem* — when three sit in judgment, God is with them, as it says *b'kerev Elohim yishpot*, "in the midst of God they shall judge." *U'minayin l'shnayim sheyoshvim v'oskim baTorah she'Shechina imahem* — when two sit and engage in Torah, God hears and listens, as it says *az nidberu yirei Hashem ish el re'ehu, vayakshev Hashem vayishma, vayikatev sefer zikaron* — then those who feared God spoke one to his fellow, God hearkened and heard, and a book of remembrance was written.

The end of that pasuk, *v'yirei Hashem u'lechoshvei shemo*, prompted an aside from Rav Ashi: *chashav adam la'asot mitzvah v'ne'enas v'lo asah, ma'aleh alav hakatuv k'ilu asah* — if a person intended to perform a mitzvah and was prevented by circumstances beyond his control, Scripture accounts it as if he had done it. The criterion is *ne'enas* — a genuine attempt frustrated by circumstance, not merely an unfulfilled intention among many. This is parenthetical to the main thread, however.

The series then concludes: *u'minayin she'afilu echad sheyoshev v'osek baTorah she'Shechina imo* — even a single individual learning Torah has the divine presence with him, as it says *b'chol hamakom asher azkir et shemi avo elecha u'verachticha* — in any place where I will cause My name to be mentioned, I will come to you and bless you. One might note that this pasuk speaks of God causing His name to be mentioned, not of the human actor doing so; but through the lens of Chazal it is read as referring to Torah study, by which God's name is invoked. It is also worth noting, as one might observe, that the pasuk could very plausibly have been read as referring to tefillah — *mazkir shem Hashem*, the invocation of God's name, is a natural description of prayer. Yet Chazal read it as Torah. This connects to a broader tension running through this entire passage: the eliding and overlapping of tefillah and Talmud Torah as different modes of connecting to God — and the question of whether, at the experiential level, learning Torah is being understood here as a form of tefillah, a direct personal encounter with God rather than a purely intellectual activity.

### Two vs. One Learning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּאֲפִילּוּ חַד, תְּרֵי מִבַּעְיָא?! תְּרֵי — מִכַּתְבָן מִלַּיְיהוּ בְּסֵפֶר הַזִּכְרוֹנוֹת, חַד — לָא מִכַּתְבָן מִלֵּיהּ בְּסֵפֶר הַזִּכְרוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since** the Divine Presence rests **even** upon **one** who engages in Torah study, **was it necessary** to say that the Divine Presence rests upon **two** who study Torah together? The Gemara answers: There is a difference between them. **Two** people, **their words** of Torah **are written in the book of remembrance,** as it is stated: "And a book of remembrance was written"; however **a single** individual's **words** of Torah **are not written in a book of remembrance.**

What does it mean for words to be "written in the book of remembrance"? If the concern were simply receiving reward for learning, one would receive that whether learning alone or with a partner. The Maharsha offers a compelling answer: when two people learn together, each challenges and corrects the other, and the conclusions they reach are therefore more likely to be accurate and truthful. It is those conclusions — precisely reasoned, tested by dialogue, closer to the truth — that are worth recording. The single learner may produce interesting thoughts, but without the corrective pressure of a study partner, those ideas may not be reliable enough to merit that kind of recording. The merit of the individual is recorded; but the *ideas* themselves are what get written when two learn together. This reflects a broader principle well-established in the Gemara: learning in partnership is the superior form of Torah study.

There is a further dimension here. The progression in this passage moves from tefillah (at ten) to din (at three) to Torah (at two and one). The shift is notable: the list begins with prayer as the paradigmatic religious act and ends with Torah learning. One might observe that when learning is done alone, it can have the quality of a spiritual, personal, almost prayerful activity — an intimate encounter with God. When two learn together, the dynamic becomes more dialogical and intellectual, *az nidberu*, they "spoke" to one another — a different mode of encounter, more public, more corrective, perhaps less purely interior.

### Din as Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּאֲפִילּוּ תְּרֵי, תְּלָתָא מִבַּעְיָא?! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: דִּינָא שְׁלָמָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וְלָא אָתְיָא שְׁכִינָה, קָמַשְׁמַע לָן דְּדִינָא נָמֵי הַיְינוּ תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Since** the Divine Presence rests **even** upon **two** who engage in Torah study, is it **necessary** to mention **three?** The Gemara answers: Here too, a special verse is necessary **lest you say that judgment is merely to** keep the **peace** among the citizenry, **and the Divine Presence does not come** and rest upon those who sit in judgment as they are not engaged in Torah study. Rabin bar Rav Adda **teaches us that** sitting in **judgment is also Torah.**

This is a striking statement. There is a real temptation — perhaps especially strong in the rabbinic world — to draw a sharp line between the beit midrash, where "real" Torah takes place, and the courtroom, where practical disputes are resolved. One might imagine that the talmidei chachamim are engaged in the genuinely religious work, while the dayyanim adjudicating everyday cases between litigants are performing something more mundane, however socially necessary. The Gemara rejects that dichotomy emphatically. Why is din Torah? In one sense, because to judge correctly requires mastery of halacha — you cannot sit as a dayan without knowing the relevant Torah. But there is a deeper sense as well: Torah is not only the intellectual process of learning; it is that learning *actualized* in the world, shaping society toward justice and toward what God intended. Din is Torah brought into reality. As the Tur opens Choshen Mishpat citing the Midrash: *kol she'oseh din l'amito ke'ilu na'aseh shutaf l'Hakadosh Baruch Hu bema'aseh Bereishit* — anyone who renders true judgment becomes, as it were, a partner with God in the work of creation. A judge spending his days adjudicating disputes may feel far from the beit midrash, but the Gemara insists that he is performing *avodas Hashem* in the fullest sense.

### God Awaits Ten

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מֵאַחַר דַּאֲפִילּוּ תְּלָתָא, עֲשָׂרָה מִבַּעְיָא?! עֲשָׂרָה — קָדְמָה שְׁכִינָה וְאָתְיָא. תְּלָתָא — עַד דְּיָתְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Since** the Divine Presence rests **even** upon **three,** is it **necessary** to mention **ten?** The Gemara answers: **The Divine Presence arrives before a group of ten,** as the verse: "God stands in the congregation of God," indicates that when the ten individuals who comprise a congregation arrive, the Divine Presence is already there. For a group of **three** judges, however, the Divine Presence does not arrive **until they sit** and begin their deliberations, as in the midst of the judges He judges. God aids them in their judgment, but does not arrive before them.

Finally, the Gemara distinguishes between ten and three: *asara kadim Shechina v'atya* — for ten, God arrives before they do. God is already present, waiting in anticipation. *V'ad d'ati tlata* — for three, God's presence comes only once the activity is underway. The difference is not merely quantitative; it reflects the urgency and eagerness with which the divine presence anticipates communal prayer. *Elohim nitzav b'adat el* — God is *standing* there, not hovering above, but present as an active, anticipating participant. This image, of God already at the synagogue waiting for the minyan to arrive, will carry forward into the Gemara's subsequent discussion of what happens when the expected minyan does not materialize.

## God's Tefillin

### God's Tefillin Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִנַּיִן שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִשְׁבַּע ה׳ בִּימִינוֹ וּבִזְרוֹעַ עֻזּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another aggadic statement: **Rabbi Avin bar Rav Adda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: From where** is it derived **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, wears phylacteries? As it is stated: "The Lord has sworn by His right hand, and by the arm of His strength"** (Isaiah 62:8). Since it is customary to swear upon holy objects, it is understood that His right hand and the arm of His strength are the holy objects upon which God swore.

This transition is not arbitrary. The image of *Elohim nitzav b'adat el* — God not merely present but *standing* in the congregation, as an equal participant — is what prompted the discussion of God wearing tefillin, since God is imagined as fully present and personally engaged in the life of the community. That same image now animates the next set of teachings.

### Swearing by Torah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בִּימִינוֹ״ — זוֹ תּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִימִינוֹ אֵשׁ דָּת לָמוֹ״, ״וּבִזְרוֹעַ עֻזּוֹ״ — אֵלּוּ תְּפִילִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ עֹז לְעַמּוֹ יִתֵּן״. וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁהַתְּפִילִּין עוֹז הֵם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָאוּ כׇּל עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ כִּי שֵׁם ה׳ נִקְרָא עָלֶיךָ וְיָרְאוּ מִמֶּךָּ״. וְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ תְּפִילִּין שֶׁבָּרֹאשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Specifically, **"His right hand" refers to the Torah, as it is stated** in describing the giving of the Torah: **"From His right hand, a fiery law for His people"** (Deuteronomy 33:2). **"The arm of His strength,"** His left hand, **refers to phylacteries, as it is stated: "The Lord gave strength to His nation"** (Psalms 29:11), in the form of the mitzva of phylacteries. **And from where** is it derived **that phylacteries provide strength for Israel? As it is written: "And all the nations of the land shall see that the name of the Lord is called upon you, and they will fear you"** (Deuteronomy 28:10). **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: This is** a reference to **the phylacteries of the head,** upon which the name of God is written in fulfillment of the verse: "That the name of the Lord is called upon you."

The proof begins with a pasuk from Yeshayahu: *nishba Hashem biyemino uvizroa uzo* — God swore by His right hand and by His powerful arm. *Yimino zu Torah* — His right hand is the Torah, as it says *mi'yimino esh dat lamo*, from God's right hand a fiery law for His people. The *zroa uzo*, the powerful arm, must then refer to something in God's left hand. What is God holding when He swears — analogous to a person holding a Torah scroll when taking an oath? The Gemara identifies it as tefillin, on the basis of *Hashem oz l'amo yiten* — God gives *oz*, strength, to His people — and *oz* is identified with tefillin through the pasuk *v'ra'u kol amei ha'aretz *ki shem Hashem* nikra alecha v'yeru mimeka*, all peoples of the earth will see that God's name is called upon you and they will fear you, which a Baraita in the name of Rabbi Eliezer HaGadol applies to the *tefillin she'barosh*.

Tosafot engages with a nuance here: why specifically the head tefillin? One might suppose the *shin* on the bayit and the *dalet* formed by the knot of the straps spell out part of God's name, making those features the source of the pasuk's *ki shem Hashem nikra alecha*. But Tosafot rejects this, pointing out that the straps have the status of *tashmishei kedushah* — accessories that serve the sacred object — rather than themselves being a *davar shebikdushah*. If the *dalet* formed by the straps were considered an actual written letter of God's name, the straps would require the same level of sanctity as the *klafim* themselves; but it is clear that the bayit has greater sanctity than the straps on account of the *shin*, which means the *dalet* does not carry halachic weight as a letter of God's name. What the pasuk means, Tosafot explains, is simply that the *tefillin shel rosh* is visible — worn high on the head, in plain sight — whereas the *tefillin shel yad* is covered by the sleeve and concealed. It is the visibility of the head tefillin that makes it the locus of *v'ra'u kol amei ha'aretz*.

### Mutual Declaration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: הָנֵי תְּפִילִּין דְּמָרֵי עָלְמָא מָה כְּתִיב בְּהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ ״וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל גּוֹי אֶחָד בָּאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin: What is written in the phylacteries of the Master of the world? Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin replied:** It is written: **"Who is like Your people, Israel, one nation in the land?"** (I Chronicles 17:21). God's phylacteries serve to connect Him, in a sense, to the world, the essence of which is Israel.

The Gemara then asks: *hani tefillin d'marei alma mai ketiv behu* — what is written in God's tefillin? Just as we know what is written in our tefillin, what text does God's contain? The answer: *u'mi k'amcha Yisrael goy echad ba'aretz* — who is like Your people Israel, one nation on earth?

The parallel is deliberate and profound. We recite *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* — we declare God's oneness. The word *echad* here may be best understood not merely as the arithmetical claim that there is one God, but as *God alone* — He is our God, and no other. In this reading, the Shema is a declaration of exclusive allegiance: only God is our God. The parallel in God's tefillin is *goy echad ba'aretz* — not that Israel is the only nation on earth, but that Israel is the one uniquely chosen nation, the nation with whom God has an exclusive relationship. Just as we proclaim our singular devotion to God, God's tefillin proclaims His singular devotion to us.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי מִשְׁתַּבַּח קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא בְּשִׁבְחַיְיהוּ דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל? אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶת ה׳ הֶאֱמַרְתָּ הַיּוֹם״. וּכְתִיב: ״וַה׳ הֶאֱמִירְךָ הַיּוֹם״, אָמַר לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְיִשְׂרָאֵל: אַתֶּם עֲשִׂיתוּנִי חֲטִיבָה אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם, וַאֲנִי אֶעֱשֶׂה אֶתְכֶם חֲטִיבָה אַחַת בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak continues: **Is the Holy One, Blessed be He, glorified through the glory of Israel?** Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin answered: **Yes,** as indicated by the juxtaposition of two verses; **as it is stated: "You have affirmed, this day,** that **the Lord** is your God, and that you will walk in His ways and keep His laws and commandments, and listen to His voice." **And the** subsequent **verse states: "And the Lord has affirmed, this day,** that **you** are His treasure, as He spoke to you, to keep His commandments" (Deuteronomy 26:17–18). From these two verses it is derived that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, said to Israel: You have made Me a single entity [*ḥativa*] in the world,** as you singled Me out as separate and unique. **And** because of this, **I will make you a single entity in the world,** and you will be a treasured nation, chosen by God.

The Gemara makes this mutuality explicit through the pasuk in Devarim: *et Hashem hemarta hayom* — you have elevated and declared God today — *v'Hashem hemircha hayom* — and God has elevated and declared you today. *Amar lehem HaKadosh Baruch Hu l'Yisrael: atem asituni chativa achat ba'olam, va'ani e'eseh etchem chativa achat ba'olam.* You have made Me the one singular unit in the world — that is the *Shema*, *Hashem Echad* — and I will make you the one singular chosen unit in the world — that is *u'mi k'amcha Yisrael goy echad ba'aretz*.

There is something genuinely beautiful about this image. The tefillin we wear is a *levush*, a garment of identity: *v'ra'u kol amei ha'aretz *ki shem Hashem* nikra alecha* — they see you and they see God's name upon you. The tefillin identifies us as belonging to God. The Gemara now imagines God wearing a corresponding *levush* — a garment that identifies God, in some sense, as belonging to us. God, in His essence, transcends all particularity and is the God of the entire universe; but in the *levush* He wears, in the relationship He has put on, there is a unique bond with Israel. The power of anthropomorphism here is not to diminish God but to give extraordinary resonance and weight to the religious acts we perform. If God swears by His Torah when He must swear, then our act of swearing by the Torah participates in something that God Himself does. If God wears tefillin, then our daily donning of tefillin is an act that parallels what God does — an act of mutual declaration.

### Four Compartments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תִּינַח בְּחַד בֵּיתָא, בִּשְׁאָר בָּתֵּי מַאי? אָמַר לֵיהּ: ״כִּי מִי גוֹי גָּדוֹל״, ״וּמִי גּוֹי גָּדוֹל״, ״אַשְׁרֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״, ״אוֹ הֲנִסָּה אֱלֹהִים״, ״וּלְתִתְּךָ עֶלְיוֹן״. אִי הָכִי, נְפִישִׁי לְהוּ טוּבֵי בָּתֵּי? אֶלָּא: ״כִּי מִי גוֹי גָּדוֹל״ ״וּמִי גּוֹי גָּדוֹל״ דְּדׇמְיָין לַהֲדָדֵי — בְּחַד בֵּיתָא, ״אַשְׁרֶיךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״ ״וּמִי כְּעַמְּךָ יִשְׂרָאֵל״ — בְּחַד בֵּיתָא, ״אוֹ הֲנִסָּה אֱלֹהִים״ — בְּחַד בֵּיתָא, ״וּלְתִתְּךָ עֶלְיוֹן״ — בְּחַד בֵּיתָא. וְכוּלְּהוּ כְּתִיבִי בְּאֶדְרָעֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava said to Rav Ashi: It works out well** with regard to the contents of **one** of the four **compartments** of God's phylacteries of the head. However, all four compartments of Israel's phylacteries of the head contain portions of the Torah that praise God. **What** portions in praise of Israel are written in **the rest of the compartments** of God's phylacteries of the head? Rav Ashi **said to him:** In those three compartments it is written: **"For who is a great nation,** to whom God is close, like the Lord our God whenever we call upon Him?" (Deuteronomy 4:7); **"And who is a great nation,** who has righteous statutes and laws, like this entire Torah which I set before you today?" (Deuteronomy 4:8); **"Happy are you, Israel,** who is like you? A people saved by the Lord, the shield of your help, and that is the sword of your excellence. And your enemies shall dwindle away before you, and you shall tread upon their high places" (Deuteronomy 33:29); **"Or has God attempted** to go and take for Himself a nation from the midst of another nation, by trials, by signs and by wonders" (Deuteronomy 4:34); **"And to elevate you** above all nations that He has made, in praise, in name and in glory; that you may be a holy people to the Lord, your God, as He has spoken" (Deuteronomy 26:19). Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, raises an objection: **If** all of these verses are included in God's phylacteries of the head, **there are too many compartments** as more than four verses of praise were listed. **Rather,** the portions in God's phylacteries must be arranged as follows: The verses **"For who is a great nation" and "And who is a great nation"** are included **in one compartment,** as they are similar. **"Happy are you, Israel" and "Who is like your people, Israel" are in one compartment. "Or has God attempted" is in one compartment and "And to elevate you" is** in one **compartment** in the phylacteries of the head, where there are four separate compartments. **And all** of the verses **are written** together on one parchment **in** the phylacteries of the **arm,** which has only one compartment.

The Gemara is not finished, however. *Tenach *b'chad beita*, shear batei mai* — granted, that is in one compartment; but what is in the other compartments? This question has almost the quality of a *reductio ad absurdum* — having entertained the notion of God's tefillin at all, the Gemara now presses the logic through and asks what fills all four compartments. In doing so, it naturalizes the entire premise: very well, God has tefillin with four compartments, so let us determine their contents.

The Gemara identifies four passages from Devarim, each praising Israel's unique relationship with God. *Ki mi goy gadol asher lo Elohim krovim eilav* — what great nation has God so close to it? *Ashrecha Yisrael *mi kamocha*, am nosha b'Hashem* — praised are you Israel, who is like you, a nation saved by God? *O hanissa Elohim lavo lakachat lo goy mikerev goy* — has God ever done such a thing as to take one nation out of another? *U'letitcha elyon al kol hagoyim* — to place you supreme above all the nations. These are all passages from Devarim that express the theme of Israel's chosen and elevated status among the nations.

## Regular Attendance

### Missing a Day

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא, אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כׇּל הָרָגִיל לָבֹא לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת וְלֹא בָּא יוֹם אֶחָד, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מְשָׁאֵיל בּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מִי בָכֶם יְרֵא ה׳ שֹׁמֵעַ בְּקוֹל עַבְדּוֹ אֲשֶׁר הָלַךְ חֲשֵׁכִים וְאֵין נֹגַהּ לוֹ״. אִם לִדְבַר מִצְוָה הָלַךְ — נוֹגַהּ לוֹ, וְאִם לִדְבַר הָרְשׁוּת הָלַךְ — אֵין נוֹגַהּ לוֹ. ״יִבְטַח בְּשֵׁם ה׳״ מַאי טַעְמָא? — מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לִבְטוֹחַ בְּשֵׁם ה׳, וְלָא בְּטַח.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **Rabin bar Rav Adda said** that **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: One who is accustomed to come to the synagogue and did not come one day, the Holy One, Blessed be He, asks about him,** as it were, to determine what happened to him, **as it is stated: "Who among you fears the Lord? Who hears the voice of His servant? Though he walks in darkness and has no light,** let him trust in the name of the Lord, and rely upon his God" (Isaiah 50:10). In other words, God asks, who among you fears the Lord yet did not come to hear the voice of His servant the prayer leader, who engages in the service of God? He who went out before dawn and walks in darkness before prayer. **If it is for a matter** involving **a mitzva that he went** and absented himself from prayer in the synagogue, then, despite the darkness, **there is light for him,** the aura of his mitzva will protect him. **But if it is for an optional matter,** some mundane purpose, **that he went** and absented himself from prayer in the synagogue, then, even once the day begins, **there is no light for him** (Maharsha). The verse continues: **"Let him trust in the name of the Lord."** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that God is so exacting with this person? The Gemara answers: **Because he should have relied on the name of the Lord,** and trusted that he would not incur any loss if he postponed dealing with his mundane matters until after prayer in the synagogue, **and he did not rely** on God.

*Amar Rabin bar Rav Adda amar Rabbi Yitzchak: kol haragil lavo l'veit haknesset v'lo ba yom echad* — anyone who regularly comes to the synagogue and one day does not come — *HaKadosh Baruch Hu mash'il bo* — God asks after him: where is so-and-so today? He is usually here. The proof text: *mi vachem yirei Hashem shome'a b'kol avdo* — who is it that fears God, listens to the voice of His servant — *asher halach chasheichim v'ein nogah lo* — who has now gone into darkness with no light? If he went for the sake of a mitzvah, then *nogah lo* — the mitzvah is light, and there is no concern. But if he went for something that is merely *reshus* — perhaps a business matter, an early meeting — *ein nogah lo*, it will not be light for him. *Yivtach b'shem Hashem* — the end of the pasuk counsels: trust in God's name. The conclusion of the Gemara: *mishum d'hava lei livtoach b'shem Hashem v'lo batach* — because he should have trusted in God rather than missing shul for the sake of his livelihood.

This is a striking Gemara. It speaks to an entirely recognizable reality: the regular shul-goer who one morning does not appear. What is notable, though, is the asymmetry. What of the overwhelming majority of the community that never comes to shul in the morning? The Gemara says nothing about them — no divine inquiry, no concern expressed. It is precisely the person who has established a pattern of attendance whose absence registers. His regular presence has created expectations. God, as it were, knows this person, sees what he is committed to, and notices when he breaks that pattern. There is something both demanding and tender about this: God is upset when the regular shul-goer is absent, but that upset is the upset of one who cares, who tracks, who has a relationship with that person. *HaKadosh Baruch Hu medakdek im chasidav k'chut hasa'arah* — God holds those close to Him to the finest standard — and here too, it is not even that this person is more righteous than others in general, but that he has engaged in a practice that creates a particular set of expectations.

### God's Anger at No Minyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בָּא בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, וְלֹא מָצָא בָּהּ עֲשָׂרָה — מִיָּד הוּא כּוֹעֵס, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מַדּוּעַ בָּאתִי וְאֵין אִישׁ קָרָאתִי וְאֵין עוֹנֶה״.
>
> **Translation:** On this same topic, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: When the Holy One, Blessed be He, enters a synagogue and does not find ten** people there, **He immediately becomes angry, as it is stated: "Why, when I came, was there no one? When I called, there was no one to answer…**Behold, with My rebuke I dry up the sea, I make the rivers a wilderness" (Isaiah 50:2).

*Amar Rav Yochanan: besha'ah she'HaKadosh Baruch Hu ba b'veit haknesset v'lo matza bah asarah, miyad hu ko'es* — when God comes to the synagogue at the appointed time for a minyan and does not find ten people there, He is immediately angry. The proof text: *madua basi v'ein ish, karasi v'ein oneh* — why did I come and no one was here, I called out and there was no answer.

One might note a slight tension: the earlier teaching said that God arrives *before* the minyan, already waiting. Presumably the intent here is that God arrives at the time when the minyan is expected, and people are still straggling in — six present, seven, waiting for others to arrive — and this failure to be ready provokes divine displeasure. The reaction is thoroughly human in its expression — and, one might add, thoroughly recognizable to anyone who has ever waited for a minyan to form.

The halachic weight of this aggadic teaching is not negligible. One of the Rishonim invokes this Gemara in connection with a famous episode where it was necessary to free a slave named Tevvi in order to complete a minyan. The general prohibition against freeing one's slave was overridden, according to the Ramban, because the gravity of the divine anger when ten are expected and not present was sufficient to justify even transgressing that prohibition. Whatever further questions that raises, it reflects the understanding that this is not merely a rhetorical flourish but a statement with practical halachic implications about the overriding importance of completing a minyan.

## Fixed Place (Makom Kavua)

### Fixed Prayer Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: כׇּל הַקּוֹבֵעַ מָקוֹם לִתְפִלָּתוֹ — אֱלֹהֵי אַבְרָהָם בְּעֶזְרוֹ. וּכְשֶׁמֵּת, אוֹמְרִים לוֹ: ״אֵי עָנָיו, אֵי חָסִיד, מִתַּלְמִידָיו שֶׁל אַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning another aspect of the constancy of prayer, **Rabbi Ḥelbo said that Rav Huna said: One who sets a fixed place for his prayer, the God of Abraham assists him.** Since prayer parallels the Temple service, it is a sign of respect to set a fixed place for this sacred rite (Rabbi Yoshiyahu Pinto). The God of Abraham assists him because this pious custom evokes Abraham's conduct. **When he dies,** those who eulogize one who set a fixed place for his prayer **say about him: "Where is the humble** one, **where is the pious** one, **of the disciples of our father Abraham?"** Presumably, one who sets a fixed place for prayer is a disciple of Abraham in every respect, including humility and piety (Rabbi Yoshiyahu Pinto).

*Amar Rabbi Chelbo amar Rav Huna: *Kol *hakovei'a makom* litfillato*, Elohei Avraham be'ezro* — anyone who establishes a fixed place for his prayer, the God of Abraham will come to his aid. When he dies, they say over him at his hesped: *Ei anav, ei chasid, mitalmidav shel Avraham Avinu* — where is the humble one, where is the righteous one, from the students of Avraham Avinu. This formulation, *ei anav, ei chasid*, appears to have been a standard liturgical trope of the period, a classic eulogy phrase used for those known for their seriousness in prayer and their commitment to a fixed place.

One point worth noting: *makom kavua* is not simply equivalent to *beit haknesset*. The teaching says *makom litfillato*, a fixed place for his prayer — not *beit haknesset*. For whatever reason a person cannot go to shul — distance, illness, circumstance — the obligation and virtue of *makom kavua* still applies. The two conversations, that of the beit haknesset and that of the *makom kavua*, are related but distinct.

### Abraham's Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבְרָהָם אָבִינוּ מְנָא לַן דִּקְבַע מָקוֹם? דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיַּשְׁכֵּם אַבְרָהָם בַּבֹּקֶר אֶל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר עָמַד שָׁם״, וְאֵין ״עֲמִידָה״ אֶלָּא תְּפִלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעֲמֹד פִּינְחָס וַיְפַלֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive **that Abraham our father set a fixed place** for his prayer? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And Abraham rose in the morning to the place where he had stood** before God" (Genesis 19:27), **and** the verb **"standing" means nothing other than prayer, as it is stated: "And Pinehas stood and prayed"** (Psalms 106:30).

*Avraham Avinu minalan dekava makom* — from where do we know that Abraham established a fixed place? *Dechtiv vayashkem Avraham baboker el hamakom asher amad sham* — Abraham arose in the morning and went to the place where he had stood. The word *amad*, stood, means he returned to the same place he had been before. *V'ein amidah ela tefillah* — standing (*amidah*) always connotes prayer, as demonstrated from *vaya'amod Pinchas vayipalel*, Pinchas stood and prayed.

The significance of *makom kavua* goes beyond mere practicality. When a person has a fixed place, his prayer is not something done haphazardly — not done here today, somewhere else tomorrow. There is an identity that accumulates in a place. We are territorial creatures, and we make associations: this place is where I have stood before God, morning after morning, year after year. The place carries the history of those encounters. It is *my* place in relation to God. This is well evidenced by the seriousness with which *makom kavua* is treated in halachic practice — those sitting shivah must move from their usual place, as a sign of disruption; the intensity of feeling when someone occupies one's accustomed seat in shul is not merely territorial pettiness but reflects something real about the relationship between person, place, and prayer.

The symbolism of Abraham as the model is also worth reflecting upon. Abraham represents the very pillar of faith — *Elohei Avraham be'ezro* — who held his ground through every trial. His going back to the same place is not only a technical precedent for *makom kavua*; it evokes his character as one who, even in his prayer and his arguments with God, did not waver. The one who fixes a place for prayer aspires to be *mitalmidav shel Avraham Avinu* — a student of Abraham not only in the matter of location but in that quality of unwavering, grounded faith that Abraham's return to his place represents.

## Synagogue Decorum

### Leaving Shul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת אַל יַפְסִיעַ פְּסִיעָה גַסָּה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא לְמִיפַּק. אֲבָל לְמֵיעַל — מִצְוָה לְמִרְהַט, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִרְדְּפָה לָדַעַת אֶת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥelbo said** that **Rav Huna said: One who leaves the synagogue should not take large strides** because it creates the impression that he is eager to leave. **Abaye** explained Rav Huna's statement and **said:** This *halakha* **was only said** with regard **to leaving** the synagogue, where large strides seem particularly disrespectful. **However,** with regard **to entering** a synagogue, **it is a mitzva to run** and one is permitted to rush and take large strides (Rabbi Yoshiyahu Pinto). **As it is said:** "And let us know, **eagerly strive to know the Lord"** (Hosea 6:3). One who eagerly enters a synagogue displays his enthusiasm to follow the path of God.

*Amar Rav Huna: ha'yotzei mi'beit haknesset al yifsi'a pesi'ah gassah* — one who leaves the synagogue should not take large steps. The concern is not mere decorum but the message one communicates: taking large strides toward the exit signals eagerness to leave, which is unbecoming of someone departing from a place of divine presence.

*Amar Abaye: lo amran ela le'meifa* — this applies only to leaving. *Aval le'meito, mitzvah le'mirhatz* — but going into shul, it is a mitzvah to run, as the pasuk says *nirdofah lada'at et Hashem* — let us run to know God. The contrast is deliberate: when leaving, one shows reluctance; when arriving, one demonstrates eagerness. Neither is about decorum in the narrow sense — both express the value of what is happening in the synagogue and one's relationship to it.

### Running In

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: מֵרֵישׁ כִּי הֲוָה חֲזֵינָא לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּקָא רָהֲטִי לְפִרְקָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא, אָמֵינָא: ״קָא מְחַלַּיִין רַבָּנַן שַׁבְּתָא״. כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁמַעְנָא לְהָא דְּרַבִּי תַּנְחוּם אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לְעוֹלָם יָרוּץ אָדָם לִדְבַר הֲלָכָה וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַחֲרֵי ה׳ יֵלְכוּ כְּאַרְיֵה יִשְׁאָג״ וְגוֹ׳, אֲנָא נָמֵי רָהֵיטְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: Initially, when I saw the Sages running to the** Rabbi's **lecture on Shabbat, I said: These Sages are desecrating Shabbat.** One is prohibited from running on Shabbat in deference to the sanctity of the day. **Once I heard that which Rabbi Tanḥum said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** said: **One should always run for a matter of *halakha*, even on Shabbat, as it is stated: "They shall walk after the Lord, who will roar like a lion"** (Hosea 11:10). In other words, one should rush as though he were chased by a lion (*Birkat Hashem*), **I too run.**

The apparent tension is real: there is a prohibition against running on Shabbos, and there is a sense of the decorum appropriate to the beit haknesset. Yet running toward *dvar Hashem* — running *for* Torah — is not a violation of what Shabbos or the synagogue is about; it is, in fact, the most authentic expression of what those things are about. The eagerness to receive Torah is more in keeping with the spirit of Shabbos than any formal decorum that would impede it.

### Running for Torah

*Amar Rav Zeira: agra de'pirka rahata* — the reward of attending the public *drasha* is the running. This is an honest and somewhat arresting observation: most people attending a public lecture do not sufficiently understand or retain what is being taught. Rashi makes this explicit — *ki rubam einam mevinim*, most of them do not really understand what is being said, nor will they later be able to repeat it accurately. What they do earn reward for is the effort of showing up, the act of running to be there. As Rashi formulates it: the one who can repeat a teaching some time later — that is the real mark of understanding.

Since the discussion has turned to running to the *drasha*, the Gemara continues in this vein with a series of statements about where the reward lies in various forms of communal engagement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: אַגְרָא דְפִרְקָא — רִהֲטָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַגְרָא דְכַלָּה — דּוּחְקָא. אָמַר רָבָא: אַגְרָא דִשְׁמַעְתָּא — סְבָרָא. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אַגְרָא דְבֵי טַמְיָא — שְׁתִיקוּתָא. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: אַגְרָא דְתַעֲנִיתָא — צִדְקְתָא. אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אַגְרָא דְהֶסְפֵּדָא — דַּלּוֹיֵי. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַגְרָא דְבֵי הִלּוּלֵי — מִילֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said: The reward for** attending **the lecture is** for **running.** Since most individuals attending the lecture did not fully understand the material taught, the primary reward for attendance was given for their intention to hear the Torah being taught, as evidenced by their rush to arrive. Similarly, **Abaye said: The reward for** attending **the *kalla* is** for **the crowding.** Due to the large crowd, study was difficult, so the primary reward was given for their effort to hear and understand some part of the lecture. Similarly, **Rava said: The reward for** learning the halakhic **traditions** of the *amora'im* **is** for **the logical** analysis, as the primary reward for studying Talmud was not given for knowing the halakhic conclusions, but for the logical reasoning that led to those conclusions. **Rav Pappa said: The** primary **reward for** attending **a house of mourning [*bei tammaya*] is** for the **silence,** which is the optimal manner for those consoling the mourners to express their empathy. **Mar Zutra said: The** primary **reward for fasting is** for the **charity** given to the poor on the fast day (see Isaiah 58). **Rav Sheshet said: The** primary **reward for** delivering **a eulogy is** for causing those in attendance to **raise their voices** and cry, as that increases the grief over the deceased. **Rav Ashi said: The** primary **reward for** participating in **a wedding is** for **the words,** i.e., the good wishes with which the guests regale the bride and groom.

## Praying Behind Shul

### Rewards for Engagement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הַיּוֹצֵא מִבֵּית הַכְּנֶסֶת אַל יַפְסִיעַ פְּסִיעָה גַסָּה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אֲמַרַן, אֶלָּא לְמִיפַּק. אֲבָל לְמֵיעַל — מִצְוָה לְמִרְהַט, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִרְדְּפָה לָדַעַת אֶת ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Back to the topic of deference for a synagogue, the Gemara records that **Rav Huna said: One who prays behind the synagogue is called wicked,** as while the entire congregation is facing one direction to pray, he faces the opposite direction creating the impression that he is treating the synagogue and its congregation with contempt. **As it is stated: "The wicked walk round about,** when vileness is exalted among the sons of men" (Psalms 12:9). In other words, only the wicked walk round about the synagogue in order to pray.

*Amar Rav Huna: kol hamispallel achorei beit haknesset nikra rasha* — whoever prays behind the synagogue is called wicked, as it says *saviv resha'im yis'hallechun* — the wicked walk around on the outside. The more natural interpretation is that this refers to someone praying outside the building entirely, though interpretations vary.

### Behind Shul is Wicked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא מַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ לְבֵי כְּנִישְׁתָּא, אֲבָל מַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ לְבֵי כְּנִישְׁתָּא — לֵית לָן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This** *halakha* **was said only** in a case **where one does not turn his face toward the synagogue. But** where **he turns his face toward the synagogue** and prays **we have no** prohibition **in** that case.

*Amar Abaye: lo amran ela d'lo mahadar apei l'bei knishta* — this is said only when one is not turning one's face toward the synagogue. If a person is outside the shul but facing in the same direction as those praying inside — facing toward Jerusalem just as the congregation does — *leit lan bah*, it is not this terrible thing described. The problem is specifically turning one's back to the shul while praying, as though rejecting its direction and its community.

### Face Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא גַבְרָא דְּקָא מְצַלֵּי אֲחוֹרֵי בֵּי כְּנִישְׁתָּא, וְלָא מַהְדַּר אַפֵּיהּ לְבֵי כְּנִישְׁתָּא. חֲלַף אֵלִיָּהוּ. חַזְיֵיהּ, אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּטַיָּיעָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּדוּ בָר קָיְימַתְּ קַמֵּי מָרָךְ?! שְׁלַף סַפְסֵרָא וְקַטְלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** To reinforce the gravity of this prohibition, the Gemara relates: **A certain individual prayed behind the synagogue and did not turn to face the synagogue.** **Elijah** the Prophet **passed by and he saw him. He appeared to** him **as an Arab [*taya'a*].** Elijah **said: "This is how [*kadu bar*] you stand before your Master?"** Elijah **drew a sword and killed him.**

The Gemara illustrates with a story: *hahu gavra d'kamtzallei achorei bei knishta v'lo mahadar apei l'bei knishta* — there was a man praying behind the synagogue with his back to it. *Chalif Eliyahu* — Elijah passed by, or, according to another version of the text, a certain nomad passed by; in either reading, the figure appeared in the guise of a *taya*, an Arab traveler. He said to the man: *kadu bar kayyams k'mei marach* — is this how you stand before your Master? Everyone is inside praying; you stand out here with your back to the shul, your back to God as it were. *Shalaf sapsira v'katlei* — he drew his sword and killed him. That is the entirety of the story, delivered without further comment.

### Elijah's Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְרַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב בִּיבִי לְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי ״כְּרֻם זֻלּוּת לִבְנֵי אָדָם״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁעוֹמְדִים בְּרוּמוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם, וּבְנֵי אָדָם מְזַלְזְלִין בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna already explained the beginning of the verse, "The wicked walk round about." The Gemara explains the end of the verse: "When vileness is exalted among the sons of men." **One of the Sages said to Rav Beivai bar Abaye, and some say Rav Beivai** said to **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: What is** the meaning of: **"When vileness is exalted among the sons of men"?** He said to him: **These are matters of utmost importance,** exalted, i.e., mitzvot or prayer, **which people** nonetheless **treat with contempt,** vileness among the sons of men.

The Gemara then asks: *mai k'rum zalus livnei adam?* — what is meant by the *krum* of the *zalut* of things before people? *Amar lei: elu devarim she'omdim berumo shel olam u'vnei adam mezalzelim bahen* — these are things that stand at the very heights of the world, things of the utmost importance, and people treat them lightly. The specific example given is prayer: *kegon tefillah she'olah lemala* — prayer, which ascends on high.

This is a significant statement, particularly in the context of Berakhot. Throughout this daf there is an ongoing tension between tefillah and Talmud Torah. Here, in what may be the most direct acknowledgment of that tension in this passage, the Gemara acknowledges that some people treat prayer as secondary — finish davening quickly, whatever, and move on to more time with learning or with other activities. The Gemara insists: prayer stands at the heights of the world. It *olah lemala*. To treat it lightly — to pray hastily behind the synagogue with one's back turned — is to misunderstand profoundly what prayer is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: עוֹף אֶחָד יֵשׁ בִּכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם, וּ״כְרוּם״ שְׁמוֹ, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁחַמָּה זוֹרַחַת מִתְהַפֵּךְ לְכַמָּה גְּווֹנִין. רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: כְּאִלּוּ נִדּוֹן בִּשְׁנֵי דִינִים, אֵשׁ וּמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִרְכַּבְתָּ אֱנוֹשׁ לְרֹאשֵׁנוּ בָּאנוּ בְּאֵשׁ וּבַמַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** to Babylonia from Eretz Yisrael **he said: There is a bird in the cities by the sea called *kerum* and when the sun rises,** the bird **changes several colors.** Similarly, one who becomes dependent upon others blushes in embarrassment. **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi both said:** One who becomes dependent upon others, **it is as if he was punished with two punishments: Fire and water. As it is stated: "You have caused men to ride over our heads; we have gone through fire and water"** (Psalms 66:12).

The word *krum* is explained through a subsequent teaching. *Ki ata Rav Dimi amar: of echad yesh b'charkei hayam v'krum shemo* — there is a certain bird in the islands called *krum*, *v'keivan shechama zorachat mehapech l'chama gevanin* — when the sun shines upon it, it turns into many colors. When a person is in a state of *zalut*, of being lowly in the eyes of others, his face changes colors like this bird — shifting and exposed under scrutiny.

*Rav Ami v'Rav Assi de'amri tarveihu: keivan shenitzrach adam labriyot, zalut lifnei adam* — when a person needs the assistance of other people, he becomes lowly in their eyes. And then *ke'ilu nidon bishnei dinim esh u'mayim* — it is as though he is judged by two judgments: fire and water. The proof text: *hirkavta enosh leroshenu banu va'esh uvamayim* — You have placed people over our heads; we have entered fire and water.

The image is one of compounded suffering. It is not enough that a person has come to a place of material need or dependence; on top of that, he must endure the social humiliation of turning to others for help. The diminishment is not only material but existential — the person's face changes colors, he is tried in two courts at once. This is offered as an interpretation of the same phrase *k'rum zalut livnei adam* with which the passage began, now read through the lens of human dependence rather than the specific case of prayer. The daf closes on this image of vulnerability and its double cost.
