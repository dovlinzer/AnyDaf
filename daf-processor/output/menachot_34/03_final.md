# Menachot 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Doorframe Structure (II)

### Desolate Doors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי פִּיתְחֵי שִׁימָאֵי פְּטוּרִין מִן הַמְּזוּזָה. מַאי פִּיתְחֵי שִׁימָאֵי? פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב רִיחוּמִי וְאַבָּא יוֹסֵי, חַד אָמַר: דְּלֵית לְהוּ תִּקְרָה, וְחַד אָמַר: דְּלֵית לְהוּ שְׁקוֹפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says:** With regard to **these broken entrances [*pitḥei shima'ei*],** which lack the proper form of doorways, one is **exempt from the** obligation of placing **a *mezuza*.** The Gemara asks: **What are broken entrances? Rav Riḥumi and Abba Yosei disagree with regard to this. One says that they do not have** a proper **ceiling, and one says that they do not have lintels [*shakofei*]** above the openings.

The *sugya* opens by completing a passage from the bottom of 33b. Rava teaches: *hanei pitchai shamai* — those doors that are desolate or abandoned — are exempt from *mezuzah*. The Gemara asks: what exactly are these desolate doors? Rav Rechumi and Abaye Yosei debate the matter. One says the door lacks a *tikra* — a ceiling or roof element — and the other says it lacks *shikufi*.

Rashi reads these terms fairly literally. The first opinion concerns a room without a ceiling; the second concerns the absence of a lintel — the horizontal structural element at the top of the door — and the absence of doorposts. The alternative reading of Rashi assumes that a missing ceiling is not itself disqualifying, and therefore interprets the first term as well to mean missing lintel, and the second as referring to missing doorposts. However one resolves the internal ambiguity within Rashi, the underlying principle is clear: it is not sufficient that a space merely happens to take the *form* of a doorway. There must be an actual architectural structure functioning as a frame.

### Frame vs. Shape

This brings us to a foundational debate between Rashi and Tosafot with significant practical ramifications. In many homes, the passageway between a living room and a dining room is shaped like a doorway — formed by the way the walls terminate — but has no actual inset wooden or metal frame. We affix a *mezuzah* to such openings. Is that halachically justified?

Rashi, read strictly, would seem to require an actual physical structure — a true doorframe — not merely the incidental shape of a doorway. Tosafot, however, reads the Gemara differently. Tosafot to *d'leisu lei tikra* explains: *peirush makom shehapetach shokef sham milmala eino shaveh* — the issue is that the top of the opening is not flat or straight. The stones coming from the arch above are jutting in and out — *even nichneset ve'even yotzet* — making it jagged rather than even. According to Tosafot, the disqualification is not about the absence of a physical frame but about the absence of the correct *shape*. So long as the lines are straight — even if the opening is simply the natural termination of two walls — a *mezuzah* is required. The practice to affix *mezuzot* on frameless interior openings follows the position of Tosafot.

This distinction — physical frame versus shape alone — recurs throughout the *sugya* and will resurface in the discussion of the *akarna* corner door.

## Achsadra & Entrances (II)

### Achsadra Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שֵׁילָא, אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: אַכְסַדְרָה פְּטוּרָה מִן הַמְּזוּזָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לָהּ פַּצִּימִין. הָא יֵשׁ לָהּ פַּצִּימִין חַיָּיב? לְחִיזּוּק תִּקְרָה הוּא דַּעֲבִידִי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ פַּצִּימִין – פְּטוּרָה, שֶׁאֵין עֲשׂוּיִין אֶלָּא לְחִיזּוּק לַתִּקְרָה. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: חֲזֵינָא לְהוּ לְאִיסְפְּלִידִי דְּבֵי מָר, דְּאִית לְהוּ פַּצִּימֵי וְלֵית לְהוּ מְזוּזָתָא. קָסָבַר: לְחִיזּוּק תִּקְרָה הוּא דַּעֲבִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Sheila says** that **Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard to **a portico,** i.e., a structure at the entrance to a house that is entirely open on its front side, one is **exempt from the** obligation of placing **a *mezuza*, because it does not have doorposts [*patzimin*]** on its sides. The Gemara questions this reason: This indicates **that** if **it has doorposts,** one would be **obligated** to place a *mezuza*. But that is not logical, as these doorposts are not there to serve as an entrance; rather, **they are made to strengthen the ceiling.** In that case, why should one be obligated? The Gemara answers: **This** is what Rav Ḥisda **is saying: Even if it has doorposts,** one is **exempt** from the obligation to place a *mezuza* there, **because they are made only to strengthen the ceiling,** not as an entrance. Similarly, **Abaye said: I saw the porticos [*le'ispelidei*] of the house of the Master,** Rabba, **that they had doorposts but they did not have *mezuzot*.** Rabba evidently **holds** that its doorposts **are made to strengthen the ceiling.**

Rabbah bar Shila said in the name of Rav Chisda: an *achsadra* — a portico, open on one end, with walls or columns on three sides — is exempt from *mezuzah*, because *ein lah pitzimin*: it does not have doorposts. Since the entire side is open, there is no frame, no doorway, and no defined entrance at all.

The Gemara immediately presses: does this imply that if the *achsadra* did have columns, it would be *chayav*? The Gemara rejects this inference: *le-chizuk tikrah hu de-avidi* — even those columns are there only to support the ceiling, not to structure a doorway. They do not functionally constitute a *pitzimin*. The Gemara therefore corrects the formulation: even if an *achsadra* had such columns, it would still be exempt, since those columns do not serve the function of doorposts. Abaye then reports that he saw the *ispalidi* — some manner of portico or esplanade — of the master's house, which had columns and yet had no *mezuzah*, consistent with the ruling that such columns, being there only to support the roof, do not transform the structure into a proper doorway.

## Doorway Function

### Achsadra and the Role of Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: בֵּית שַׁעַר, אַכְסַדְרָה וּמִרְפֶּסֶת – חַיָּיבִין בִּמְזוּזָה! בְּאַכְסַדְרָה דְּבֵי רַב. אַכְסַדְרָה דְּבֵי רַב כְּאִינְדְּרוֹנָא מְעַלַּיְיתָא הוּא! בְּאַכְסַדְרָה רוֹמְיָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: With regard to **a gatehouse, a portico, and a balcony,** one is **obligated to** place **a *mezuza*.** The Gemara answers: The *halakha* of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to** a specific type of portico, **the portico of a study hall,** which is closed on all sides, but its walls do not reach the ceiling. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **A portico of a study hall is like a full-fledged room [*inderona*],** and therefore it should not be labeled a portico with regard to the *halakhot* of *mezuza*. The Gemara answers: The *halakha* of the *baraita* is stated **with regard to a Roman portico,** which is more open than the portico of a study hall, as it is built with many windows instead of proper walls. The *baraita* is teaching that one is obligated to place a *mezuza* on this type of portico.

The Gemara raises an apparent contradiction. A *baraita* states that a *beit shaar*, an *achsadra*, and a *mirpeset* are all *chayavin bemezuzah*. But we just established that an *achsadra* is exempt because it has no doorframe. The Gemara resolves this: the *achsadra* referred to in the *baraita* is the *achsadra debei Rav* — the portico of Rav's house. Rashi glosses *indrona* — the term the Gemara uses to characterize it — with *chambra*, i.e., chamber. The Gemara is saying that whatever Rav's *achsadra* was, it was in reality a true enclosed chamber with four walls and a doorway, not an open portico at all. It thus tells us nothing new that such a structure requires a *mezuzah*.

The Gemara then offers the real answer: the *baraita* refers to an *achsadra rumisa* — a Roman-style *achsadra*. Rashi describes this as a structure with many windows, something resembling a gazebo in feel — very open — but nonetheless possessing a defined entranceway. Unlike a standard *achsadra* whose entire side is open, this structure has a proper doorway. It therefore requires a *mezuzah*. The principle emerging is that what matters is not the name or general character of the structure, but whether it has a functioning doorway — and, of course, its connection to a *beit dirah*.

The Gemara raises a deeper principle here that extends beyond the physical structure: even if a space happens to have the shape of a doorway, that shape must be the result of deliberate architectural function. It cannot be accidental. This applies equally to the debate between Rashi and Tosafot — whether one requires a physical structure or merely the correct shape — because in either case, the shape must be intentional, not merely incidental. A space that happens to look like a doorway because of how stone walls happen to fall is not the same as one designed to serve as a doorway.

The word *mezuzah* in the verse *al mezuzot beitecha u'visharecha* does not refer to the parchment scroll but to the doorframe itself. The obligation attaches to that doorframe, and the frame must be recognizable as such — not only structurally but functionally.

Beyond structure, there is a second requirement: *beit dirah*. A *mezuzah* is obligated only on a dwelling or on something directly appended to a dwelling. This is why a *beit midrash* or *beit kenesset* does not require a *mezuzah* *mid'oraita* — these are not places of living. The *achsadra* or portico, though not itself a *beit dirah*, does have a connection to one, and the Tosafot at the bottom of the page addresses this directly. The Tosafot cites a *baraita* from Yoma which might have excluded a *beit shaar*, *achsadra*, and *mirpeset* from the obligation. The resolution offered by Mar bar Rav Ashi is that *mid'oraita* such structures are indeed exempt — since one does not live in them — but *miderabbanan* they do require a *mezuzah* because they serve or connect to a *beit dirah*. The principle is therefore layered: architectural form, intentional function, and relationship to a dwelling all determine the obligation.

## Bei Chaziki

### Two Mezuzot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַחֲבָה, אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בֵּי הַרְזִיקֵי חַיָּיב בִּשְׁתֵּי מְזוּזוֹת. מַאי בֵּי הַרְזִיקֵי? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא סָבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: בֵּית שַׁעַר הַפָּתוּחַ לְחָצֵר, וּבָתִּים פְּתוּחִין לְבֵית שַׁעַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Raḥava says** that **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **a *bei harziki*,** one is **obligated to** place **two *mezuzot*.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **a *bei harziki*? Rav Pappa the Elder says in the name of Rav:** It is **a gatehouse that opens to a courtyard, and houses** also **open** directly **to** the **gatehouse.** It requires two *mezuzot*, one for the entrance from the courtyard to the gatehouse, and one for the entrance from the gatehouse to the houses.

Rechava said in the name of Rav Yehuda: *bei haziki chayav bishtei mezuzot* — the *bei haziki* requires two *mezuzot*. The Gemara asks: what is a *bei haziki*? Rav Papa said in the name of Rav: it is a *beit shaar* — a gatehouse — that opens into a *chatzer*, a shared courtyard, with houses that open off that *beit shaar*. The structure thus has two doors: one connecting the *chatzer* to the *beit shaar*, and one connecting the *beit shaar* to the residential area.

The *chiddush* is that both doors require a *mezuzah*. The inner door, from the *beit shaar* to the houses, is straightforwardly connected to the dwelling. But the outer door, from the *chatzer* into the *beit shaar*, is not yet at the threshold of any individual home. Nevertheless, since the *beit shaar* has become an extension — an appendage — of the residential quarters, even the outer door is drawn into the obligation. The connectedness to a *beit dirah* is sufficient.

### Beit Shaar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בֵּית שַׁעַר הַפָּתוּחַ לְגִינָּה וּלְקִיטוֹנִית – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נִידּוֹן כְּקִיטוֹנִית, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: נִידּוֹן כְּבֵית שַׁעַר. רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִגִּינָּה לְבַיִת – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּחַיָּיב, מַאי טַעְמָא? בִּיאָה דְּבַיִת הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a gatehouse that** has two entrances, as it **opens** both **to a garden,** which is exempt from a *mezuza*, **and to a small room [*ulekitonit*], Rabbi Yosei says: Its** halakhic **status is like** that of **a small room,** and it requires a *mezuza*, **and the Rabbis say:** Its halakhic status **is like** that of **a gatehouse,** and it does not require a *mezuza*. There is a difference of opinion among *amora'im* with regard to this dispute. **Rav and Shmuel both say:** With regard to the entrance through which one enters **from the garden to the house,** i.e., the entrance of the gatehouse to the small room, **everyone agrees** that one is **obligated** to place a *mezuza*. **What is the reason? It is** the way of **entering** the **house,** and the house requires a *mezuza*.

The Gemara then introduces a *baraita* addressing a smaller-scale version of the same question. A *beit shaar* that opens both into a garden (*gina*) and into a small private house (*kaitonit*) has two doors — one facing the garden, one facing the house. Which of them, if any, requires a *mezuzah*?

Rabbi Yosi says the *beit shaar* is treated as an extension of the *kaitonit* — the small house — and therefore requires a *mezuzah*. The rabbis say it is treated as a *beit shaar*, which in Rashi's reading means it is associated with the garden and is therefore exempt.

The Gemara's underlying question is whether the *beit shaar* is judged by its connection to the garden or to the house, and whether that judgment changes depending on which door one considers. The function and association of the space — not just its physical structure — governs the analysis.

### Rav vs. Raba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי מִבַּיִת לְגִינָּה, מָר סָבַר: קִיטוֹנִית עִיקָּר, וּמָר סָבַר: גִּינָּה עִיקָּר. רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִבַּיִת לְגִינָּה, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּפָטוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? פִּיתְחָא דְּגִינָּה הוּא. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – מִגִּינָּה לְבַיִת, מָר סָבַר: בִּיאָה דְּבַיִת הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: כּוּלַּהּ אַדַּעְתָּא דְּגִינָּה הוּא דַּעֲבִידָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav and Shmuel continue: **When they disagree** it is with regard to the entrance through which one enters **from** the **house to the garden,** i.e., the entrance of the gatehouse to the garden. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that the **small room** into which the gatehouse opens is the **main** area, and therefore the gatehouse, which is used for entering the small room, is considered like a regular gatehouse to a house, and all its entrances require a *mezuza*. **And** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **hold** that the **garden** is the **main** area, and therefore this entrance does not require a *mezuza*. Conversely, **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** With regard to the entrance through which one enters **from** the **house to the garden,** i.e., the entrance between the gatehouse and the garden, **everyone agrees** that one is **exempt** from placing a *mezuza*. **What is the reason? It is** the **entrance to the garden,** and the garden does not require a *mezuza*. **When they disagree** it is with regard to the entrance **from** the **garden to the house,** i.e., the entrance between the gatehouse and the small room. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yosei, **holds** that **it is** the way of **entering the house,** and the house requires a *mezuza*, **and** one **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **the entire** area **is made for the purpose of** reaching the **garden,** not for entering the house, and therefore even with regard to the entrance between the gatehouse and small room, one is exempt from placing a *mezuza* at the entrance of the small room.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא עָבְדִי כְּרַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף, וְרַב אָשֵׁי עָבֵיד כְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל לְחוּמְרָא, וְהִילְכְתָא כְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Abaye and Rava would act in accordance with** the explanation of **Rabba and Rav Yosef,** i.e., they would not place a *mezuza* on the two entrances of a gatehouse, neither to the garden nor to the small room, in accordance with the ruling of the Rabbis. **And Rav Ashi would act in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav and Shmuel, stringently,** i.e., following the ruling of Rabbi Yosei that both entrances require a *mezuza*. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the explanation of **Rav and Shmuel, stringently.**

Rav and Shmuel both rule: *mi-gina l'bayis* — when going from the garden toward the house — everyone agrees it is *chayav*. This refers to the door connecting the *beit shaar* to the *kaitonit*: that door is plainly an entry into the dwelling. The debate applies only to the door connecting the *beit shaar* to the garden. One opinion holds *kaitonit ikar* — even that door is seen as an appendage of the house and is *chayav*. The other holds *gina ikar* — that door is associated with the garden and is *patur*.

Raba and Rav Yosef take the opposite and more lenient position: *mi-bayis l'gina* — everyone agrees the garden-facing door is *patur*, as it is plainly an opening into the garden. The debate applies only to the door connecting the *beit shaar* to the house — one opinion says it is *bei d'bayis hu* and is *chayav*; the other says even that door should be seen as an exit from the garden, not an entrance into the house, and is *patur*.

Abaye and Rava ruled like Raba and Rav Yosef, who were their teachers. Rav Ashi ruled like Rav and Shmuel, the more stringent position. The *halacha* follows Rav and Shmuel *l'chumra*, which means both doors — the one toward the house and the one toward the garden — require a *mezuzah*. The takeaway is clear: the obligation of *mezuzah* depends not only on architectural form but on function and connectedness to a *beit dirah*, and even individual doors within the same structure may be judged differently based on what each one is seen as serving.

## Internal Doors

### Lul to Attic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: לוּל פָּתוּחַ מִן הַבַּיִת לַעֲלִיָּיה, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ פֶּתַח אֶחָד – חַיָּיב בִּמְזוּזָה אַחַת, אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי פְּתָחִין – חַיָּיב בִּשְׁתֵּי מְזוּזוֹת. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ מִדְּרַב הוּנָא, הַאי אִינְדְּרוֹנָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ אַרְבְּעָה בָּאבֵי – חַיָּיב בְּאַרְבַּע מְזוּזוֹת. פְּשִׁיטָא! לָא צְרִיכָא, אַף עַל גַּב דִּרְגִיל בְּחַד.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** With regard to **an aperture that opens from** the ceiling of **a house** occupied by one person **to a loft** occupied by another, with a walled staircase leading from the lower floor to the loft, **Rav Huna says: If** the staircase **has one entrance,** i.e., one doorway, either from the house or from the upper story, one is **obligated to** affix **one *mezuza*; if it has two entrances,** both from below and above, one is **obligated to** affix **two *mezuzot*. Rav Pappa says:** One can **learn from that** statement **of Rav Huna** that with regard to **this** type of **room that has four gates,** one is **obligated to** affix **four *mezuzot*.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious?** The Gemara explains: **No,** it is **necessary** to teach that **even though one is accustomed to** using **one** particular gate, nevertheless, all four require a *mezuza*.

The Gemara discusses a *lul* — a passage connecting a ground-floor house to an upper-floor *aliya*, an attic or upper dwelling rented to a separate family. Since the upper-floor tenants had no independent street exit, they would descend through the main house. To preserve the privacy of both households, a small enclosed stairwell would be constructed with doors at both the upper and lower landings — somewhat like a private lobby. When someone from the *aliya* descended, they would arrive on a landing and knock on the door to enter the main house; similarly, entering from the main house into the *aliya* required passing through the landing door.

Rav Huna rules: if the *lul* has one door, one *mezuzah* is required; if it has two doors, two *mezuzot* are required. However many doors, that is how many *mezuzot*. The door connecting the stairwell to the main house is, by any measure, not the primary door of that house. It is not how the homeowner enters or exits. Yet it is still *chayav in mezuzah*.

### Four Doors

Rav Papa derives from Rav Huna's ruling a broader principle: a chamber — *androna* — that has four doors leading in four different directions is *chayav* in four *mezuzot*. This is striking, because one might have thought that only the primary entrance requires a *mezuzah*, or that secondary doors are exempt. The Gemara says *pshita* — obviously — and then clarifies: *lo tzricha af al gav d'ragil b'chad* — the novelty is that even though one door is used most frequently, the others still require *mezuzot*.

This raises the question of how to reconcile this with a story elsewhere in the Gemara about Rebbe, who had a small private door to the *beit midrash* that did not require a *mezuzah*. Perhaps Rebbe's door was exempt because it was a private door for personal use, as opposed to a door available for general use. Alternatively, perhaps that case involved multiple doors all leading into the same room — where only the primary door matters — whereas here we are speaking of doors leading into different spaces. The practical implication is clear: one should affix *mezuzot* to every doorway, and this is indeed the common practice.

### Akarna Corner

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַמֵּימָר: הַאי פִּיתְחָא דְּאַקַּרְנָא חַיָּיב בִּמְזוּזָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְאַמֵּימָר: וְהָא לֵית לֵיהּ פַּצִּימִין! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עָדֵי פַּצִּימֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said:** With regard to **this entrance which** is located **at the corner** of a house, one is **obligated to** affix **a *mezuza*. Rav Ashi said to Ameimar: But it does not have doorposts.** Ameimar **said to him: These [*adei*] are its doorposts,** i.e., the end of the walls serve as its doorposts.

Meimar rules that a *pitcha d'akarna* — a corner door — is *chayav bemezuzah*. Rav Ashi challenges him: does it not lack *pitzimin*? A door cut into a corner of a house does not present a naturally shaped doorframe; two walls simply converge without forming a classic rectangular opening.

Meimar responds: *adai ptzimi* — there are indeed *pitzimin*. Rashi explains: *eilu rashei ha'ktalim hen hen ptzimin* — the ends of the walls themselves serve as the doorframe. The point where one wall terminates on each side constitutes the two doorposts of the opening. This is significant on two levels. First, it confirms that a separate physical structure is not required — the walls themselves suffice. Second, even an unconventionally shaped opening — two walls meeting at an angle rather than framing a standard rectangular doorway — can constitute a valid doorframe by using the wall terminations as its posts.

This is somewhat in tension with the stricter reading of Rashi from the earlier discussion, where Rashi seemed to require a physical doorframe structure. It is possible that the earlier Rashi was not absolute, and that in principle Rashi also agrees with Tosafot that the shape suffices, even if described differently. What Rashi here clearly demonstrates is both that a physical separate structure is not required, and that an unusual configuration can still qualify.

The Gemara continues. Rav Papa visited the house of Mar Shmuel and observed a door that had only one *ptzima*, a doorpost structure on the left side, and nevertheless a *mezuzah* had been placed. Rav Papa challenged this: like whom? Like Rabbi Meir? But Rabbi Meir only permits a *mezuzah* on a one-sided doorframe when the *single* post is on the *right* — where the *mezuzah* is placed. If the only post is on the left, even Rabbi Meir would not permit it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי מָר שְׁמוּאֵל, חֲזָא הָהוּא פִּיתְחָא דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ אֶלָּא (פַּצִּים אֶחָד) [חַד פַּצִּימָא] מִשְּׂמָאלָא, וַעֲבִידָא לֵיהּ מְזוּזָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? אֵימַר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר מִיָּמִין, מִשְּׂמֹאל מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Pappa happened** to come **to the house of Mar Shmuel,** where **he saw a certain entrance that had only one doorpost to the left** of the entrance, **and** yet Mar Shmuel **had affixed a *mezuza* to** that doorpost. Rav Pappa **said to him: In accordance with whose** opinion did you do this? Did you act **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who deems one obligated to affix a *mezuza* to an entrance that has only one doorpost? But one can **say that Rabbi Meir says** that one must do so only in a case where the doorpost is **to** the **right** of the entrance. **Does he say** that it requires a *mezuza* if the entrance is **to** the **left?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא: ״בֵּיתֶךָ״ – ״בִּיאָתְךָ״, מִן הַיָּמִין. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר מִן הַיָּמִין, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא מִשְּׂמֹאל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בֵּיתֶךָ״. מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא? אָמַר רַבָּה: דֶּרֶךְ בִּיאָתְךָ מִן הַיָּמִין, דְּכִי עָקַר אִינִישׁ כַּרְעֵיהּ דְּיַמִּינָא עָקַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the source for **this** requirement that the *mezuza* be affixed to the right side? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: "And you shall write them upon the doorposts of **your house [*beitekha*]"** (Deuteronomy 6:9), the word *beitekha* is interpreted as ***biatekha*,** your entry, i.e., the *mezuza* must be affixed to the side by which you enter, which is **from the right. Do you say** it is **from the right, or** is it **only from the left?** Therefore, **the verse states: Your house [*beitekha*].** The Gemara asks: **What is the** biblical **derivation** here? **Rabba says:** The *mezuza* is affixed in **the way that you enter** the house, which is **from the right, as when a person lifts his foot** to begin walking, **he lifts his right** foot first. Therefore, the *mezuza* is affixed to the right side of the doorway.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר אַחָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר, מֵהָכָא: ״וַיִּקַּח יְהוֹיָדָע הַכֹּהֵן אֲרוֹן אֶחָד וַיִּקֹּב חֹר בְּדַלְתּוֹ וַיִּתֵּן אוֹתוֹ אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ מִיָּמִין בְּבוֹא אִישׁ בֵּית ה׳ וְנָתְנוּ שָׁמָּה הַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרֵי הַסַּף אֶת כׇּל הַכֶּסֶף הַמּוּבָא בֵית ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shmuel bar Aḥa said before Rav Pappa in the name of Rava bar Ulla** that the requirement that the *mezuza* be affixed to the right of the entrance is derived **from here: "And Jehoiada the priest took a chest, and bored a hole in the lid of it, and set it beside the altar, on the right side as one comes into the House of the Lord; and the priests that kept the threshold put in there all the money that was brought into the House of the Lord"** (II Kings 12:10). This indicates that an object designed for those entering a house is placed to the right of the one entering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי רַבִּי מֵאִיר? דְּתַנְיָא: בַּיִת שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא פַּצִּים אֶחָד, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב בִּמְזוּזָה, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן? ״מְזוּזוֹת״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further inquires: **What** is this statement of **Rabbi Meir,** referred to by Rav Pappa, that he deems one obligated to place a *mezuza* on an entrance that has only one doorpost? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a house that has only one doorpost, Rabbi Meir deems** one **obligated to** affix **a *mezuza*, and the Rabbis deem** him **exempt** from affixing a *mezuza*. The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of the Rabbis? It is written:** "And you shall write them upon the **doorposts** of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:9), in the plural, which indicates that there must be two doorposts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? דְּתַנְיָא: ״מְזוּזוֹת״ – שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי מִיעוּט מְזוּזוֹת שְׁתַּיִם, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״מְזוּזוֹת״ בְּפָרָשָׁה שְׁנִיָּה, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר, הָוֵי רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי, וְאֵין רִיבּוּי אַחַר רִיבּוּי אֶלָּא לְמַעֵט, מִעֲטוֹ הַכָּתוּב לִמְזוּזָה אַחַת, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Rabbi Meir,** that one doorpost suffices to obligate one to affix a *mezuza*? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: When it states **"doorposts"** (Deuteronomy 6:9), **I** would **derive the minimum** number **of doorposts,** which is **two. When it says "doorposts" in the second passage** (Deuteronomy 11:20), this also serves to teach a *halakha*, **as** otherwise there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this. This **is** one **amplification following** another **amplification, and** the principle is that **an amplification following an amplification is** stated **only** in order **to restrict** its extent. In this manner **the verse restricted** the minimum number **to one doorpost.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״עַל הַמַּשְׁקוֹף וְעַל שְׁתֵּי הַמְּזוּזֹת״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שְׁתֵּי״, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שְׁתֵּי״? זֶה בָּנָה אָב, כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״מְזוּזוֹת״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא אַחַת, עַד שֶׁיִּפְרֹט לְךָ הַכָּתוּב שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** This proof **is not necessary.** Rather, **when** the verse **states:** "And strike **the lintel and the two doorposts"** (Exodus 12:22), one can claim that there is a superfluous word in this verse, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state "two,"** since the minimum of doorposts is two. **What** is the meaning when **the verse states "two"? This established a paradigm** that **anywhere where it is stated "doorposts,"** it means **only one** doorpost, **unless the verse specifies** that it is referring to **two** doorposts.

The Gemara then explains Rabbi Meir's position. The *baraita* teaches: a house that has only one doorpost — *bayit she'eino ella ptzim echad* — Rabbi Meir says it is *chayav bemezuzah*; the sages say it is *patur*. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is derived from a *kal vachomer* on the word *mezuzot*: the Torah uses both the form *mezuzat* (singular or defective) and *mezuzot* (plural or full), which in rabbinic hermeneutics signals a *ribui achar ribui* — a doubling of inclusion — which by the rule *ein ribui achar ribui ella lema'eit* actually narrows the requirement. The conclusion is that even one doorpost suffices.

Rabbi Yishmael explains Rabbi Meir's position through this analysis. Rabbi Akiva does not need this derivation, since he learns it from the use of *shtei hamezuzot* — the two doorposts — in the context of *Pesach Mitzrayim*, which by being explicit there (*al hamashkof v'al shtei hamezuzot*) teaches that ordinarily *mezuzot* does not presuppose two. Until the Torah specifies "two," a single post suffices.

A related *drasha* establishes that the *mezuzah* is placed on the right side of the entryway. From *bi'atcha min hayamin* — you enter leading with your right — and supported by a verse in Melachim where Yehoyada the Kohen placed the charity box *miyamin* at the entrance to the *beit Hashem*, we learn that entry is associated with the right side, and the *mezuzah* follows accordingly. The question of what defines the doorpost — whether it requires a physical structure or merely the shape — thus runs through this entire section and remains live.

## Writing Mezuzah

### Parchment Writing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ – יָכוֹל יִכְתְּבֶנָּה עַל הָאֲבָנִים? נֶאֱמַר כָּאן כְּתִיבָה, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן כְּתִיבָה – מָה לְהַלָּן עַל הַסֵּפֶר, אַף כָּאן עַל הַסֵּפֶר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And you shall write them** upon the doorposts of your house, and upon your gates" (Deuteronomy 6:9). One **might** have thought that **one writes** a *mezuza* **on the stones** of the entrance. To counter this, an expression of **writing is stated here,** with regard to a *mezuza*, **and** an expression of **writing is stated there. Just as there** the mitzva of writing means **on a book,** i.e., parchment, **so too,** a *mezuza* must be written **on a book.**

The Gemara now concludes the laws of *mezuzah* with the requirement that the text be written on parchment. *Uchtavtam* — "and you shall write them" — one might think means to write directly on the stones of the doorpost, which is what the plain reading of *al mezuzot beitecha* seems to suggest. The Gemara therefore introduces a *gezerah shavah*.

### Gezerah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ כְּלָךְ לְדֶרֶךְ זוֹ: נֶאֱמַר כָּאן כְּתִיבָה, וְנֶאֱמַר לְהַלָּן כְּתִיבָה, מָה לְהַלָּן עַל הָאֲבָנִים, אַף כָּאן עַל הָאֲבָנִים! נִרְאֶה לְמִי דּוֹמֶה? דָּנִין כְּתִיבָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת מִכְּתִיבָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת, וְאֵין דָּנִין כְּתִיבָה הַנּוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת מִכְּתִיבָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ נוֹהֶגֶת לְדוֹרוֹת, וּכְמוֹ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר לְהַלָּן: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לָהֶם בָּרוּךְ מִפִּיו יִקְרָא אֵלַי אֵת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה וַאֲנִי כֹּתֵב עַל הַסֵּפֶר בַּדְּיוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* suggests: **Or** perhaps, **go [*kalekh*] this way,** i.e., one can suggest a different interpretation: An expression of **writing is stated here,** with regard to a *mezuza*, **and writing is stated there,** with regard to the mitzva of writing the words of the Torah on stones upon the entry to Eretz Yisrael (Deuteronomy 27:3). **Just as there,** the words are written **on the stones** themselves, **so too here,** the *mezuza* should be written **on the stones.** The *baraita* continues: **Let us see to which it is similar,** i.e., which comparison appears more apt. **We derive writing that is performed in** all **generations,** i.e., that of a *mezuza*, **from** another **writing that is performed in** all **generations, but we do not derive writing that is performed in** all **generations from writing that is not performed in** all **generations. And** furthermore, a *mezuza* must be written with ink, **as it is stated below: "And Baruch said to them: He dictated all these words to me, and I wrote them with ink in the scroll"** (Jeremiah 36:18).

The word *ktivah* — writing — appears both in the context of *mezuzah* and in the context of a *get* (where the Torah says *vechatav lah sefer keritut*) or, according to Tosafot, in the context of *sotah*. The *gezerah shavah* teaches: just as writing there refers to writing on a *sefer* — a parchment document — so too here, writing on *mezuzah* must be on parchment. Alternatively, one might propose a *gezerah shavah* from the writing on the stones at Gilgal, where the Torah says *al ha'avanim* — suggesting the *mezuzah* text should be inscribed directly on the stone doorpost. The Gemara resolves this by ruling that one applies a *gezerah shavah* from writing that is practiced for all generations (*ktivah hanohaget ledorot*) to another obligation that spans all generations, not from a one-time commandment like the writing on the stones, which was fulfilled at a specific historical moment.

Rav Acha the son of Rava raises a striking challenge to Rav Ashi: but the Torah says explicitly *al mezuzot* — on your doorposts! How can you override the plain meaning with a *gezerah shavah*? Rav Ashi responds that the derivation is not solely from the *gezerah shavah*. The word *uchtavtam* itself implies *ktivah tamah* — a complete, perfect writing. One cannot write perfectly on stone. Therefore the *pasuk* itself instructs: write it properly — which means on *klaf*, parchment — and *then* place it on the *mezuzah*. The *gezerah shavah* adds the further point that writing nicely on a flat stone and then affixing the stone to the doorpost would not suffice either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: רַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״עַל מְזוּזֹת״, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ נֵילַף כְּתִיבָה כְּתִיבָה? אָמַר קְרָא ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ – כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה, וַהֲדַר ״עַל הַמְּזוּזוֹת״. וּמֵאַחַר דִּכְתִיב [״וּכְתַבְתָּם״], הַאי גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמָה לִי? אִי לָאו גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא לִיכְתְּבַהּ אַאַבְנָא וְלִיקְבְּעַהּ אַסִּיפָּא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi: The Merciful One states: "Upon the doorposts,"** which indicates that a *mezuza* should be written on the doorposts themselves, **and** yet **you say: Let us derive** a verbal analogy between **"writing"** and **"writing,"** to teach that one writes it on parchment. Why isn't the verse interpreted in accordance with its straightforward meaning? Rav Ashi said to him: **The verse states: "And you shall write them [*ukhtavtam*],"** which means that it should first be **complete writing [*ketiva tamma*],** i.e., the full passages written down, **and** only **then** should one place them **"upon the doorposts"** of the house. The Gemara asks: **And since it is written:** "And you shall write them," from which it is derived that the *mezuza* should be written first and then placed on the doorpost, **why do I** need **this verbal analogy** between "writing" and "writing"? The Gemara explains that **were it not** for the **verbal analogy, I would say** that one **should write** the passages of a *mezuza* **on a stone, and** afterward **affix** the stone **to the doorpost.** To counter this, the verbal analogy **teaches us** that a *mezuza* must be written on a scroll.

It is somewhat striking that of all the *mitzvot* in this section, this is the one that draws the challenge "but the Torah says it explicitly." After all, *ukeshtartam* — "and you shall bind them" — in the context of *tefillin* is also not taken literally as tying the text to one's body, and yet no such objection is raised there. The Gemara here, in any case, offers the resolution and moves forward.

### Four Parshiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבַּע פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁבַּתְּפִילִּין מְעַכְּבוֹת זוֹ אֶת זוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּתָב אֶחָד מְעַכְּבָן. פְּשִׁיטָא! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְקוֹצוֹ שֶׁל יוּד. וְהָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לְאִידַּךְ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל אוֹת שֶׁאֵין גְּוִיל מוּקָּף לָהּ מֵאַרְבַּע רוּחוֹתֶיהָ, פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The mishna teaches: With regard to the four passages that are in the phylacteries,** i.e., the two passages that are written in the *mezuza* and two additional passages (Exodus 13:1–9, 11–16), **the absence of each prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with the others, and** the absence of **even one letter prevents fulfillment of the mitzva with** the rest of **them.** The Gemara asks: Isn't it **obvious** that the inclusion of every letter is necessary? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** It is **necessary** to state this ruling **only** to teach **that** even the absence of **the thorn,** i.e., a small stroke, **of** a letter ***yod*** prevents fulfillment of the mitzva. The Gemara asks: **But** isn't **this also obvious,** since the letter is not formed properly? **Rather, it is necessary only according to another** statement **that Rav Yehuda** says. **As Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Any letter that is not encircled with** blank **parchment on** all **four of its sides,** as its ink connects to the letter above it, below it, preceding it, or succeeding it, is **unfit.**

The Gemara then states: the four *parshiyot* of *tefillin* are interdependent — each is indispensable to the others, and even a single letter invalidates the whole. The Gemara says this is *pshita* — obvious. The novelty is established more precisely: Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav that even the *kotzo shel yud* — the tiny tagin or serifs of the letter *yud* — is indispensable. This was already discussed in the context of *mezuzah*, and the Gemara pushes further: even that the letters must be *mukaf gevil* — surrounded by parchment on all four sides, with no letter touching another — is required, as established earlier.

## Tefillin Intro

### Totafot Etymology

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לְטֹטֶפֶת״ ״לְטֹטֶפֶת״ ״לְטוֹטָפוֹת״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן אַרְבַּע, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, ״טַט״ בְּכַתְפִּי שְׁתַּיִם, ״פַּת״ בְּאַפְרִיקִי שְׁתַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the number of compartments in the phylacteries of the head, the verse states: "It shall be for a sign upon your hand, and **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (Exodus 13:16), with the word *totafot* spelled deficient, without a *vav* before the final letter, in a way that can be read as singular; and again: "They shall be **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8), spelled as a singular word; and again: "They shall be **for *totafot*** between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 11:18), this time spelled plene, with a *vav* before the final letter, in a manner that must be plural. **There are four** mentions of *totafot* **here,** as the third one is written in the plural and therefore counts as two. Consequently, it is derived that the phylacteries of the head must have four compartments. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Akiva says: There is no need** for this proof, as the requirement of four compartments can be derived from the word *totafot* itself: The word ***tot* in** the language of **Katfei** means **two,** and the word ***pat* in** the language of **Afriki** also means **two,** and therefore *totafot* can be understood as a compound word meaning four.

The Gemara now turns to *tefillin* and derives the requirement for four *parshiyot* and four compartments from the word *totafot*. The word appears three times in the Torah: twice in a defective spelling (without a *vav*) and once in the full spelling. The Gemara counts: *l'totafot l'totafot l'totafot* — here are four. Rabbi Yishmael derives four from the numerical value suggested by these three appearances. Rabbi Akiva does not rely on this: *kat d'kafti shtayim, pat b'afriki shtayim* — in a certain language the element *tat* or *kat* means two, and in African the element *pat* means two, yielding four together. The Torah uses a word whose linguistic roots in two foreign languages each contribute the number two.

Tosafot notes a difficulty with Rabbi Yishmael's count — the one time *totafot* is written "fully," the additional *vav* appears between the two *tets*, not as a standard plural marker, and does not obviously imply plurality. But regardless of the technical derivation, the tradition was clearly that four *parshiyot* and four compartments are required; the *drasha* serves to anchor that tradition in the text.

### Meaning of Terms

Tosafot to *l'totafot* offers a fascinating observation. The word *totafot* may refer to the location on the head where *tefillin* are placed — above the forehead, between the eyes. Tosafot also raises the possibility that in Aramaic the word relates to seeing or being seen — from a root meaning *l'hatzit*, to gaze. In that reading, *totafot* parallels the *tzitz* of the Kohen Gadol, which bore God's name on the forehead. The *tefillin* too bear God's name on the head, and both are associated with being visibly marked as God's.

Tosafot concludes by favoring the reading that *totafot* refers to what others see: *v'ra'u kol amei ha'aretz ki shem Hashem nikra alecha* — all the nations of the earth will see that God's name is called upon you. The *tefillin* are testimony, worn visibly on the body as evidence of Israel's relationship with God.

The word *tefillin* itself — a rabbinic term not found in the Torah — is the singular of *tefilla*, prayer. Though in Talmudic times *tefillin* were worn all day and not specifically limited to the time of prayer, Tosafot offers a deeper explanation for the name. About two-thirds down the relevant Tosafot, the word *tefillin* is connected to the root of *plilim* — argumentation or justification before a court. *Tefillin* are *eidut v'hochacha* — testimony and proof — that God's name is called upon us, that we are His people, and that this inspires *yir'ah* among the nations. Wearing *tefillin* is an act of bearing witness to the covenant, and the term reflects that quality.

### Shel Rosh Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָכוֹל יִכְתְּבֵם עַל אַרְבָּעָה עוֹרוֹת, וְיַנִּיחֵם בְּאַרְבָּעָה בָּתִּים בְּאַרְבָּעָה עוֹרוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּלְזִכָּרוֹן בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״, זִכָּרוֹן אֶחָד אָמַרְתִּי לָךְ, וְלֹא שְׁנַיִם וּשְׁלֹשָׁה זִכְרוֹנוֹת. הָא כֵּיצַד? כּוֹתְבָן עַל אַרְבָּעָה עוֹרוֹת וּמַנִּיחָן בְּאַרְבָּעָה בָּתִּים בְּעוֹר אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: One **might** have thought that a scribe **should write** the passages of the phylacteries of the head **on four** separate **hides,** i.e., parchments, **and place them in four compartments of four hides,** one passage in each compartment. Therefore, **the verse states: "And for a memorial between your eyes"** (Exodus 13:9). This teaches: **I said to you** that the phylacteries are **one memorial, but not** that they are **two or three memorials,** i.e., the phylacteries themselves must be one unit. **How so?** One **writes** the passages **on four hides and places them in four compartments** fashioned **of one hide.**

The Gemara now specifies the construction of the *shel rosh*. A *baraita* teaches: one might think the four *parshiyot* should be written on four separate parchments and placed in four completely separate compartments — four independent *batim* in no way connected. The Torah therefore says *ul'zikaron bein einecha* — it must be a single *zikaron*, one unified object, not several separate ones. How then is this achieved? One writes the four *parshiyot* on four separate parchments (*arba orot*) and places them in four compartments (*arba batim*) constructed out of a single piece of leather (*b'or echad*). Four compartments, but one object.

## Shel Rosh Build

### Spaced Parchments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם כְּתָבָן בְּעוֹר אֶחָד, וְהִנִּיחָן בְּאַרְבָּעָה בָּתִּים – יָצָא, וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּהֵא רֶיוַח בֵּינֵיהֶן, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ. וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁנּוֹתֵן חוּט אוֹ מְשִׁיחָה בֵּין כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. וְאִם אֵין חֲרִיצָן נִיכָּר – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** a scribe **wrote** all four of **them on one hide and placed them in four compartments** by slitting the parchment between each of the passages, one who dons these phylacteries has **fulfilled** his obligation. **And** in such a case it is **necessary for there to be a space between** each of the passages, so that each can be placed in a separate compartment; this is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **And the Rabbis say: It is not necessary** for there to be a space between them. **And** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis **agree that one places a string or** a thicker **band between each and every one** of the four compartments. The *baraita* adds: **And if their furrows,** i.e., the lines marking the separation between the compartments, **are not noticeable** from the outside, the phylacteries **are unfit.**

The Gemara raises a further possibility: what if one wrote all four *parshiyot* on a single parchment and inserted it into the four *batim*? Rabbi — Rebbe — says this is valid (*yatza*), provided one leaves sufficient space between the columns of text (*revach beineihem*) so that the parchment can be separated into four portions, each fitting into its respective compartment. The way Rashi understands this: one writes the four *parshiyot* in four vertical columns on one parchment, then folds or inserts each column into its corresponding compartment. The parchment, remaining connected at its base, spans all four. Since the *bayit* itself is one unit made from one hide, a single parchment similarly unified should be acceptable.

The rabbis disagree and say no space is required. This is somewhat puzzling — it is not entirely clear how the rabbis envision the parchment fitting into four separate compartments without some division. Nevertheless, all agree on the next point.

### Visible Grooves

Both Rabbi and the rabbis agree that one must place a cord or demarcation between each compartment (*chut o meshicha bein kol achat v'achat*), and crucially: *im ein charitzah nikeret, pesulot* — if the groove or incision between compartments is not visible from the outside, the *tefillin* are invalid. The division between the four compartments must be discernible externally. Looking at a *shel rosh* one should see the four sections clearly delineated. An interior division that is invisible from without does not suffice.

## Shel Yad Build

### Gluing Parchments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד כּוֹתְבָן? תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל יָד כּוֹתְבָהּ עַל עוֹר אֶחָד, וְאִם כְּתָבָהּ בְּאַרְבַּע עוֹרוֹת וְהִנִּיחָהּ בְּבַיִת אֶחָד – יָצָא. וְצָרִיךְ לְדַבֵּק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהָיָה לְךָ לְאוֹת עַל יָדְךָ״, כְּשֵׁם שֶׁאוֹת אַחַת מִבַּחוּץ – כָּךְ אוֹת אַחַת מִבִּפְנִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How does** a scribe **write them?** With regard to **the phylacteries of the arm, he writes it on one hide. But if he wrote it on four** separate **hides and placed it in one compartment,** one who wears it has **fulfilled** his obligation. **And** in such a case **it is necessary to attach** the four parchments, **as it is stated: "And it shall be for a sign for you upon your arm"** (Exodus 13:9). This teaches that **just as** the phylacteries of the arm are **one sign on the outside,** as the compartment is fashioned from a single hide, **so too,** they must be **one sign on the inside,** i.e., the four passages must be on a single parchment. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: It is not necessary** to attach the passages.

The Gemara now addresses the *shel yad*. A *baraita* teaches: the *shel yad* is written on one parchment (*or echad*). If one wrote it on four parchments and placed them in the single *bayit* of the *shel yad*, one is *yotzei* — but one must glue them together (*tzarich ledabek*). The reason: *vehayah lecha le'ot al yadecha* — it must be a single *ot* on your hand. Just as the outside presents one unified appearance, the inside must be one unified object. This is the position of Rabbi Yehudah.

### Separate Parchments

Rabbi Yosi disagrees: *eino tzarich* — gluing is not required. Four separate parchments placed inside the *shel yad* without being glued are entirely valid. This is a remarkably lenient ruling — one that runs counter to what most would intuitively expect.

### Converting Structures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: וּמוֹדֶה לִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרִיבִּי, שֶׁאִם אֵין לוֹ תְּפִילִּין שֶׁל יָד וְיֵשׁ לוֹ שְׁתֵּי תְּפִילִּין שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, שֶׁטּוֹלֶה עוֹר עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן וּמַנִּיחָהּ. מוֹדֶה? הַיְינוּ פְּלוּגְתַּיְיהוּ! אָמַר רָבָא: מִדְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי חָזַר בּוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: And Rabbi Yehuda the Distinguished [*Beribbi*] concedes to me that if one does not have phylacteries of the arm but has two phylacteries of the head, that he covers one of them with patches of hide,** to render it like one compartment, **and places it** on his arm. The Gemara asks: How can Rabbi Yosei say that Rabbi Yehuda **concedes** to him in this case? **This is** the very situation in which **their dispute** applies, as they disagree over whether or not the passages of the phylacteries of the arm may be written on separate parchments. **Rava said: From Rabbi Yosei's statement** one can infer that **Rabbi Yehuda retracted** his opinion and accepted Rabbi Yosei's ruling.

Rabbi Yosi then adds that Rabbi Yehudah would agree with him in the following case: if one lacks a *shel yad* but has two *shel rosh tefillin*, one may take one of the *shel rosh* units, stretch a piece of leather (*or*) over it so that on the outside it appears as a single compartment, and wear it as a *shel yad*. Rabbi Yosi argues that Rabbi Yehudah, having retracted his position on gluing, would accept this as valid. The Gemara objects: but this appears to be precisely the point of their dispute. Rava resolves this: from the very fact that Rabbi Yosi said Rabbi Yehudah would agree, we infer that Rabbi Yehudah in the end retracted his position and agreed that separate parchments in the *shel yad* are acceptable.

This yields a remarkable picture. The *shel rosh*, which is four compartments constructed from one hide, presents four visible divisions on the outside — the *charitzim* are essential. The *shel yad*, which is one compartment, can in practice contain four separate parchments on the inside, as long as the exterior presents the appearance of a single unit. The outside appearance of the *shel rosh* matters greatly; the interior reality of the *shel yad* allows significant flexibility.

### Kedushah Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא שְׁלַח רַב חֲנַנְיָה מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: תְּפִלָּה שֶׁל יָד עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ שֶׁל רֹאשׁ, וְשֶׁל רֹאשׁ אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ שֶׁל יָד, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מוֹרִידִין מִקְּדוּשָּׁה חֲמוּרָה לִקְדוּשָּׁה קַלָּה. לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא בְּעַתִּיקָתָא, הָא בְּחַדְתָּתָא. וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר הַזְמָנָה מִילְּתָא הִיא, דְּאַתְנִי עֲלַיְיהוּ מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei said that all agree that one can convert phylacteries of the head into phylacteries of the arm. The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rav Ḥananya sent** the following ruling **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If one has **phylacteries of the arm,** he **can convert it to** phylacteries **of the head, but** if one has phylacteries **of the head,** he **cannot convert it to** phylacteries **of the arm, because one does not reduce** the sanctity of an item **from** a level of **greater sanctity** of phylacteries of the head **to** a level of **lesser sanctity** of phylacteries of the arm. The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** ruling is stated **with regard to old** phylacteries, which have already been worn on one's head and therefore have a greater level of sanctity, whereas **that** ruling is stated **with regard to new** phylacteries, which have not yet been used. The Gemara adds: **And according to the one who says** that **designation is significant,** i.e., once one designates an item for use in fulfilling a particular mitzva, it assumes the sanctity of an item used for mitzvot, this ruling is stated with regard to a case **where he stipulated with regard to them from the outset** that he may convert it from phylacteries of the head to phylacteries of the arm, and only in this circumstance it is permitted to convert them.

The Gemara now raises a difficulty. Rabbi Chanina transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a *shel yad* may be converted into a *shel rosh*, but a *shel rosh* may not be converted into a *shel yad*. The reason is *ein moridin mikedushah chamurah likedushah kalah* — one does not descend from a higher *kedushah* to a lower one. The *shel rosh* is considered holier: it sits higher on the body, and — more concretely — it bears two-thirds of God's name on the *bayit* itself. The *shin* appears on the box; the *dalet* is formed by the knot; only the *yud* is formed by the knot of the *shel yad*. The *shel rosh* thus carries greater intrinsic sanctity.

But if that is so, how did we just permit converting a *shel rosh* into a *shel yad* by stretching leather over it? The Gemara answers: *lo kashya — ha b'atikta ha b'chadta* — the ruling about conversion applies to old, used *tefillin*, while the permission to stretch leather applies to new *tefillin* that have never yet been used as a *shel rosh*. An unused *shel rosh* has not yet acquired the formal status of *shel rosh*, and so descending from it presents no problem.

### Designation Exception

A further answer applies to those who hold *hazmana milta* — that designation alone, without actual use, confers halachic status. According to that opinion, even unused *tefillin* designated as a *shel rosh* would already carry its status and could not be downgraded. The resolution is that if one stipulated from the very outset (*atin ala me'ikara*) — at the time of making the *tefillin* — that they might be converted or used differently, then the designation was conditional from the start and the *kedushah* of *shel rosh* was never fully conferred.

The *sugya* thus ends with a picture of far greater flexibility in *tefillin* construction than one might have assumed, combined with a strict hierarchy of sanctity once objects have been properly designated and used. We stop here, as the next two lines open the famous debate of Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam regarding the order of the *parshiyot* in the *tefillin* — which will be the subject of the next shiur.
