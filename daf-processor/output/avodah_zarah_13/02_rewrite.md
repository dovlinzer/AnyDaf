# Avodah Zarah 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Decorated Stores

### Market vs Holiday

The Mishna on 12b, which sets the stage for today's daf, continues the Gemara's sustained engagement with the question of commercial dealings with non-Jews in contexts touching on *avodah zarah*. The opening discussion concerns a city in which *avodat kochavim* is present. A foundational dispute between Rashi and Tosafot turns on what precisely this means. Rashi understands the case to concern a *yom eid* — an actual pagan holiday — and the Mishna is accordingly addressing the permissibility of conducting business on such a day. Tosafot, by contrast, understands the case to concern not a holiday at all but a market day specifically dedicated to a particular pagan god: business conducted within that market is somehow connected to the deity in whose honor the market was established. It is this latter understanding — the *shuk shel avodah zarah* — that will guide the analysis going forward.

### Beis She'an Case

Within that market, the Mishna distinguishes between stores that are decorated (*me'utarot*) and those that are not. The case, we are told, arose in Beis She'an, a cosmopolitan city with a mixed Jewish and non-Jewish population. The ruling of the Sages is: *hame'utarot asurot veshe'einan me'utarot mutarot* — stores that are bedecked in honor of the pagan god are forbidden, while those that are undecorated are permitted. The implication is that although the market day as a whole is dedicated to the god, not every transaction within it is thereby tainted. If the particular store shows no visible sign of devotion to the deity, one may transact business there.

### Selling vs Buying

Rashi, who reads this Mishna as pertaining to a *yom eid* rather than merely a dedicated market day, must reckon with a difficulty Tosafot had raised at the outset of the tractate: is it not problematic to do business with non-Jews on their *yom eid* regardless of whether the individual store is decorated? Even an undecorated store is still operating on a pagan holiday. This leads Rashi to invoke a distinction — mentioned by Tosafot at the very beginning of the tractate — between selling items to a non-Jew, which risks those items being used in their worship and is therefore forbidden, and purchasing items *from* them, which may be permissible. Whether buying from non-Jews on their *yom eid* is indeed permitted remains a matter of ongoing discussion. Under the simpler reading, however — that the Mishna concerns a dedicated market day rather than a holiday — the decorated stores are forbidden specifically because of their connection to the pagan god, and the analysis proceeds from there.

## Deriving Benefit

### Myrtle & Roses

The Gemara opens its analysis with a teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *lo shanu ela me'utarot bevered vehadas* — the prohibition applies only when the stores are decorated with roses and myrtle. The reason is *dekamtahni meireicha* — one derives benefit from their fragrance. The roses and myrtle in question are presumably objects connected directly to the idol — perhaps cultivated in a garden consecrated to it, or even offered as a presentation before it — and are therefore *assur behana'ah*, forbidden as a source of benefit. Entering such a store and inhaling their scent constitutes *nehane*, deriving benefit from something forbidden. By contrast, if the decoration consists only of fruit — *me'utarot bepeirot* — which emits no fragrance, the store is permitted, since one is not actually deriving any benefit.

### Nehane Assur

The legal basis is drawn from the verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem* — nothing of that which is under the ban shall cleave to your hand. From this the Gemara derives a foundational principle: *nehane hu d'asur, aval mehane shari* — receiving benefit from something connected to an idol is forbidden, but giving benefit to *avodah zarah* is permitted. This is a striking claim, and one that requires careful scrutiny. Can one truly make a donation to the temple of a pagan god without any prohibition? Tosafot registers strong discomfort, pointing to multiple passages in the Gemara where giving benefit to *avodah zarah* is clearly treated as forbidden. Tosafot therefore limits the statement, suggesting that Reish Lakish — whose opinion this reflects — must be speaking of a specific and unusual scenario, as will be clarified below.

### Smell & Hana'ah

Before proceeding to that larger question, Tosafot raises a pointed inquiry about the threshold case: is smell even considered *hana'ah* in the halakhic sense? One might assume so — bodily pleasure is bodily pleasure, and one recites a *berachah* over fragrant objects — but another Gemara introduces some ambiguity. Tosafot resolves the question with two principles. First, for objects whose normal mode of benefit is precisely their aroma — roses and myrtle being paradigmatic examples — smelling them is unambiguously considered *derech hana'ah*, the standard way of deriving benefit, and is therefore forbidden when those objects are *assur behana'ah*. Second, even for objects where smell is not the primary benefit, *avodah zarah* may be uniquely stringent. This stringency flows from the language of the verse: rather than using the word *achilah* — eating — as the operative term, as is done with, say, *basar bechalav* (*lo tevashel*) or *kilayim* (*tikdash*), the Torah employs the phrase *lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem*, language suggesting total exclusion from any form of connection. That formulation may prohibit even atypical modes of benefit — *lo derech hana'ato* — from *avodah zarah*, whereas for other forbidden categories, only the normal mode of deriving benefit would be proscribed. The upshot for the case of roses and myrtle: smelling them is forbidden both because fragrance is their ordinary benefit, and because even if it were not, *avodah zarah* may forbid even non-standard forms of *hana'ah*.

## Mehane Assur

### Kal Vachomer

Rabbi Yochanan takes a broader position. Even if the store is decorated only with fruit — which emits no fragrance and from which one derives no *hana'ah* — he maintains it is still forbidden: *afilu me'utarot bepeirot nami asur*. His reasoning takes the form of a *kal vachomer*: if receiving benefit from something connected to *avodah zarah* is forbidden, surely actively conferring benefit upon *avodah zarah* — doing something that raises it up, that signals support of it — should be forbidden all the more so. The *kal vachomer* is logically compelling. Whether this prohibition rises to the level of a biblical *issur* is less clear. The verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem* might encompass giving as well as receiving — after all, to give to something is also a form of cleaving to it — but the precise scriptural grounding is not fully articulated. At a minimum, there is something powerfully symbolic about the act: giving monetary support to a pagan temple constitutes a form of affiliation with it, independent of any personal benefit derived.

### Tax & Ownership

This raises the question: in what way does ordinary commercial activity in this market constitute *mehane* — giving benefit to the idol? Rashi offers two explanations, each with distinct implications. The first: on this market day dedicated, say, to Dionysus, a tax of some percentage is levied on every sale — not a standard sales tax but, as it were, a Dionysus surcharge — and that levy flows directly to the temple of Dionysus. Even though the buyer plays no conscious role in directing the payment, the transaction necessarily channels funds to the pagan cult. The second explanation: the store is actually owned by the temple of Dionysus itself, so that all profits accrue to the institution of *avodah zarah*.

### Intent & Use

Each of these explanations opens its own set of questions about the parameters of what makes *mehane* problematic. Is the concern triggered by money explicitly earmarked for the idol, or does it extend to any profit that flows to those associated with it? Does it matter whether the funds will be used to build the temple or merely to feed the priests? Does the buyer's intent or awareness factor in, or is the objective result sufficient? Is the problem the directness of the connection to the idol itself, or the institutional support it represents? These ambiguities may help explain why Reish Lakish maintained that in certain configurations *mehane* is permissible — Tosafot acknowledges the force of the contrary Gemaras but concedes that the full parameters are genuinely complex.

## Crown & Tax

### Crown Offer

A *baraita* is cited in the name of Rabbi Natan that brings the tension into sharp practical relief. Rabbi Natan describes a day on which the *avodat kochavim* waives the ordinary sales tax — not by imposing a special cultic surcharge, but by suspending the standard *meches* for those who honor the deity. The announcement is made: *kol mi shenotel atarah veyaniach berosho uverosh chamoro* — whoever places a wreath upon his head and upon the head of his donkey, in honor of the god, will have his tax waived. Those who do not participate will be required to pay in full.

### Jew's Dilemma

The Gemara then poses the dilemma facing the Jew who finds himself in this market: *mah yaaseh?* What should he do? If he puts on the wreath, *nimtsa neheneh* — he ends up deriving benefit, since the wreath is presumably made of roses or myrtle connected to the idol. Rashi understands the *nehane* to lie precisely in this sensory experience — smelling the aromatic wreath that is associated with the idol. Tosafot considers but does not fully adopt the alternative reading that the *nehane* consists in having the tax waived, i.e., retaining one's own money, since Tosafot understands that not being charged is not strictly *nehane* in the technical sense. The Rashi-based reading prevails: it is the physical pleasure of the wreath itself that constitutes the forbidden benefit. If the Jew declines to put on the wreath, *nimtsa mehaneh* — he pays the tax, and that payment presumably reaches the temple of the pagan god, making him guilty of conferring benefit upon *avodah zarah*. Neither path appears open.

Rabbi Natan's conclusion: the Jew may neither pay the tax-exempt price by donning the crown, nor conduct business on that day at all. And the consequences for transgressing are severe: *behemah te'akeir* — animals purchased are to be maimed; *peirot kesut vekeilim yirakevu* — fruit, garments, and vessels are left to rot; *ma'ot uchelei matachot yolicheim leyam hamelach* — money and metal objects are thrown into the Dead Sea.

### Idolatry Concern

Here a striking question presents itself — and it is notable that neither Rashi nor Tosafot appears to address it directly. The entire discussion frames the prohibition in terms of *nehane* and *mehane* — benefit derived from or conferred upon the idol. Yet surely the act of placing a wreath on one's head in explicit honor of a pagan deity — *lichvod avodat kochavim* — is not merely a question of monetary benefit. The cultural context may not be fully recoverable, but the act seems to constitute at minimum a public gesture of participation in the cult, something resembling the acts classified among those by which one shows honor to *avodah zarah*. The *mishnakek* — hugging, kissing, bowing, showing deference — these are forms of idolatrous worship, and putting on a wreath in honor of the god sounds as though it should fall somewhere in that spectrum, close to an act of *avodah zarah* in its own right. That the Gemara frames the prohibition exclusively in terms of *hana'ah* and *mehane*, without raising the more fundamental concern of participation in idolatrous practice, is genuinely puzzling and warrants closer examination of the full cultural and legal context.

Reish Lakish responds that he holds in accordance with the Rabbis who dispute Rabbi Natan — and according to Tosafot, this is the only case in which Reish Lakish permits *mehane*. The key, Tosafot explains, is the symbolic meaning of the act. Paying the full tax because one *refuses* to don the crown is not an expression of honor to the god — it is, in a sense, an act of disrespect, a public declaration of non-participation. That the money ends up reaching the temple does not transform this into *mehane* in the culpable sense, because the manner of its transmission is one of principled refusal rather than willing support. Tosafot therefore limits Reish Lakish's leniency to this specific configuration: he would not permit *mehane* in the ordinary case where a tax is charged and collected as a straightforward cultic surcharge. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, holds that even the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Natan — no such paying of the tax is permissible — and that Rabbi Natan's ruling is not contested.

## Baraita Permission

### Buy & Register

The Gemara then raises a challenge to Rabbi Yochanan's blanket prohibition: *veha tanya* — a *baraita* was taught. This *baraita* permits precisely the kind of commercial engagement Rabbi Yochanan sought to prohibit: *holchin leyarid shel ovdei kochavim* — one may go to the market days of idolaters, *velokechin meihem behemah avadim ushfachot batim sadot ucheramim* — and purchase from them cattle, male and female slaves, houses, fields, and vineyards. Moreover — and this is a notable extension — *vechoteiv uma'aleh be'arkaot shelahem*, one may register the transaction documents in their courts, without concern about the prohibition of using non-Jewish legal institutions (*bechukoteihem* or the principle of *eileh hamishpatim*).

### Matzil Miyadam

The permitting principle is stated explicitly: *mipnei shehu kematzil miyadam* — because doing so is like salvaging these assets from their hands. This concept, *matzil miyadam*, is the animating rationale for a range of special dispensations in this chapter and those that follow, oriented toward preserving and reinforcing Jewish ownership and presence.

### Land Context

The broader context of this *baraita* is the land of Israel. The specific categories enumerated — cattle, slaves, houses, fields, vineyards — are not arbitrary. They correspond precisely to the kinds of ownership and productive control that constitute a meaningful presence in the land: agricultural property, animals needed to work that land, and the labor to maintain it. The *baraita* does not say that one may purchase clothing or food in this market; it is these particular assets, strategically connected to the *yishuv* in Eretz Yisrael, that are the subject of the dispensation. Indeed, as later sections of this chapter will make clear, parallel restrictions prohibit *selling* these very categories of property to non-Jews — the logic being symmetrical: strengthen Jewish control, prevent its erosion.

### Kohen Abroad

A digression follows from within the *baraita* itself. Even a *kohen* may become *tamei* in *chutz la'aretz* — which carries rabbinic-level impurity — in order to go abroad and litigate or defend his land claims in the courts of the nations: *la'dun u'le'ar'er imahem*, to contest their legal claims against him. And just as he may become *tamei* by going to *chutz la'aretz* for this purpose, so too may he enter a *beit haperas* — an area where a grave has been disturbed, creating a rabbinically impure zone due to concern about small fragments of bone — for the same purpose.

The Gemara then extends the dispensation: a *kohen* may also go to *chutz la'aretz* and incur this rabbinic *tuma* for the sake of learning Torah — *u'mitamei lilmod Torah ve'lisa ishah* — or for the purpose of marriage. Rabbi Yehudah limits this: the permission to go abroad for Torah study applies only *bi'zman she'ein motzei lilmod*, when one cannot find adequate instruction in Israel. If suitable Torah study is available locally, the *kohen* may not incur *tuma* for the sake of going abroad. Rabbi Yossi disagrees: even when there is a suitable institution in Israel, *yitamei* — the *kohen* may still go, *lefi she'ein adam zocheh lilmod mikol* — because a person does not necessarily merit learning from every teacher equally. One may be uniquely suited to flourish in a specific setting or under a specific master, even if another master is theoretically available nearby. Rabbi Yossi himself acted on this principle, and the Gemara records an instance of Yosef ha-Kohen who followed his master to Tzidan for the sake of Torah study. Rabbi Yochanan rules in accordance with Rabbi Yossi.

## Yochanan Reconciles

### Challenge

The *baraita* permitting the purchase of cattle, slaves, and real estate at the pagan market day clearly poses a problem for Rabbi Yochanan's position. Rabbi Yochanan had ruled, on the basis of his *kal vachomer*, that even buying from stores decorated only with fruit is forbidden — that *mehane* is prohibited. Yet this *baraita* openly sanctions commercial activity at the *yarid shel ovdei kochavim* across a wide range of categories. The Gemara establishes that Rabbi Yochanan was aware of this *baraita*, which means the apparent contradiction must be resolved rather than dismissed.

One might object that the *baraita*'s permission is exceptional rather than general — it applies specifically to the categories tied to the *yishuv* in Eretz Yisrael by virtue of the *matzil miyadam* principle, and does not license purchasing ordinary commodities. But this does not fully answer the challenge: even if the permission is narrow and purposive, Rabbi Yochanan had formulated a blanket prohibition, and the *baraita* makes clear that business in this market is at least sometimes permissible. Moreover, the *baraita* includes cattle, and the preceding *baraita* of Rabbi Natan had specifically subjected illicitly purchased animals to *behemah te'akeir* — meaning the two *baraitot* appear to contradict each other on the case of animals as well. The Gemara therefore presses: do the Rabbis in fact dispute Rabbi Natan, as Reish Lakish claimed? *Alma pligi?*

### Merchant vs Homeowner

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *leolam lo pligi* — they do not actually dispute one another. The resolution lies in a distinction between two types of sellers: *kan belokeach min hatagar* — where the prohibition applies, one is purchasing from a merchant, a professional participant in the market; *kan belokeach miba'al habayit* — where the permission applies, one is purchasing from a homeowner, someone selling privately and not in a commercial capacity. The operative difference is the *meches*: merchants have a tax assessed upon their sales that flows to the temple of the pagan god; homeowners in a non-commercial, flea-market capacity do not. When there is no tax being channeled to *avodah zarah*, the *mehane* concern does not arise, and the transaction is permitted.

This resolution is, however, somewhat unsatisfying, and the difficulty is worth pressing. The entire force of the second *baraita*'s permission — the reason it seemed like a remarkable dispensation — was the *matzil miyadam* rationale: even though there would normally be a problem, the overriding concern of maintaining Jewish control over land and livestock in Eretz Yisrael justifies the transaction. But if the Gemara's resolution is that the permission only applies in the homeowner case where there is no *meches* and no *mehane* issue, then no special dispensation is really needed. If one is not paying a tax that benefits *avodah zarah* and is not in a store visibly honoring the god, why would such a purchase require a special justification in the first place? The *matzil miyadam* rationale seems to lose its bite. The more natural reading would have been that the *baraita* permits these purchases even where a *mehane* concern would normally exist, precisely because of the compelling countervailing interest in the land. The Gemara's resolution restructures the picture so that the permission is limited to cases where there is no baseline problem — which rather diminishes the novelty of the ruling.

### Church Taxes

Tosafot uses the underlying principle of *mehane assur* to address a concrete reality of medieval Jewish life, citing the view of Rabbeinu Baruch. There were markets at which priests or monks collected a portion of the sales tax, and that portion was explicitly designated for *vinyan avodat kochavim* — the construction of a church building. Rabbeinu Baruch rules: if the cultic surcharge is publicly labeled as such — the seller says the price is, for instance, ten *perutot* and additionally one *perutah* for the church building fund — it is forbidden. The explicit earmarking of even a small sum for church construction makes the transaction a direct act of *mehane* to *avodah zarah*, which Rabbi Yochanan forbids. Even if the priest does not verbally announce the designation but tacks on an additional charge after stating the base price, Tosafot inclines toward the view that it remains forbidden — because everyone understands that the additional amount is a building-fund contribution, and the seller's reticence is merely out of sensitivity to the Jewish buyer's discomfort.

### Christianity & AZ

This discussion is embedded in a broader question about the status of Christianity that Tosafot navigates with care. Tosafot had earlier established — on the basis of a separate discussion at the beginning of the tractate — that Christians are not classified as *ovdei avodah zarah* in the full sense, which permits ordinary commercial dealings between Jews and Christians. But Tosafot's discussion here assumes that Christianity remains *avodah zarah* as an institution, even if individual Christians are not treated as sincere idolaters. This distinction has practical weight: it permits business *with* Christians while potentially still prohibiting direct financial benefit *to* the Church as an institutional entity.

This raises a genuinely difficult question: if Christianity is *avodah zarah* for Jews but not for non-Jews — as later developments in halakhic thinking, attributed by subsequent authorities to a different Tosafot passage, would suggest — how precisely does the prohibition of *mehane* operate? One answer is that *mehane* is ultimately about my relationship to the forbidden thing, not about whether I am enabling someone else's sin. The verse *lo yidbak b'yadcha m'uma min hacharem* speaks in terms of what cleaves *to you* — and if for me Christianity constitutes *avodah zarah*, then my direct financial support of the Church connects *me* to something under the ban, regardless of whether that worship is permitted for the non-Jews who receive my money. Under that reading, even if Christianity is permissible for non-Jews, the Jewish donor's connection to the institution should remain problematic.

The Rav Moshe mi-Kotzi cited by Tosafot introduces a further nuance: where the designation of funds is ambiguous — an additional sum charged without explicit statement of its purpose — there may be grounds for leniency, since one cannot be certain the money will go to the building fund; perhaps it will be used for charitable purposes, a soup kitchen, or other communal needs. The question of where exactly the money goes — to the construction of the sanctuary, to the salaries of the clergy, or to social services — significantly affects the analysis, and reasonable authorities have taken differing positions. This Tosafot was written before the distinction between *avodah zarah for Jews only* and *avodah zarah for everyone* had crystallized into its later formulations; it operates on the assumption that Christianity is *avodah zarah* in principle for all, even if Christians themselves are not classified as sincere *ovdei avodah zarah*. The practical ramifications of the subsequent distinction — particularly regarding donations toward church reconstruction, support for interfaith relief efforts, and analogous situations — remain a live area of halakhic discussion.

## Property Destruction

### Punishment Rules

The Gemara now turns to the severe consequences that apply when someone transgresses and conducts business in the market dedicated to *avodah zarah*. The Mishna and *baraita* prescribe a graduated set of punishments calibrated to the type of object involved: animals are maimed (*behemah te'akeir*), fruit and garments and vessels are left to rot (*peirot kesut vekeilim yirakevu*), and money together with metal objects are thrown into the Dead Sea (*yam hamelach*). The *ikur* — the maiming — is defined as severing the tendons from the ankle downward, and Tosafot clarifies that even this degree of disability is sufficient; more extensive damage is not required.

### Tzaar Debate

The Gemara raises an immediate objection: *ve'ika tzaar baalei chayim* — does not this maiming involve causing unnecessary suffering to an animal, which is prohibited? Abaye answers from a verse in the context of Yehoshua's conquest of Canaan, where God commanded that the horses of the defeated nations be hamstrung (*ve'sus'eihem te'aker*). This provides a scriptural basis — albeit a loose one, since that was a specific divine command in a unique military context — for the principle that objects connected to *avodah zarah* may be treated this way.

Tosafot reports a sharp challenge from Rav Chananel: those who hold that *tzaar baalei chayim* is a biblical prohibition should find the verse from Yehoshua unpersuasive. That was an explicit divine command — *al pi haDibbur* — and therefore a direct exception. How can a rabbinic *knas* override a biblical prohibition against causing animal suffering? Tosafot's answer is that the Sages do, in certain circumstances, have the authority to override even biblical norms when they judge it sufficiently warranted — *yesh koach be'yad chakhamim la'akor heicha d'nira lahem ktzat la'akor*. This position is consistent with Tosafot's approach to an analogous case discussed earlier in the tractate, where an animal was killed as part of the ritual conducted at the death of kings: there too, Tosafot identified the allowance as a specific exception — either because of the honor of all Israel or because of the need to uproot *avodah zarah* — rather than a general principle that *tzaar baalei chayim* yields whenever there is human benefit to be gained.

This stands in marked contrast to the position of the Rambam and the *Trumat HaDeshen*, as well as the direction in which later *halacha* developed, which hold that *tzaar baalei chayim* is generally displaced whenever the animal's suffering serves a legitimate human purpose. For Tosafot, the permission is narrow: the only cases in which *tzaar baalei chayim* is overridden are those involving special countervailing values — the honor of all Israel, the need to make a statement against *avodah zarah* — not the general category of human benefit. According to the opposing *rishonim*, by contrast, Rav Chananel's question would never have gotten off the ground; if human benefit is always a valid override, the *knas* of *te'aker* would straightforwardly justify the suffering, and there would be nothing to reconcile.

## Property Destruction (II)

### Abaye vs Rava

When the same punishment scheme — *behemah te'akeir*, fruit and vessels to rot, metal objects to the Dead Sea — is applied in a different context, namely where someone improperly consecrates an animal as *hekdesh* in the present era when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, a further question arises. Here the *baraita* defines *ikur* differently: instead of actual maiming, one locks the animal in an enclosure and it dies of starvation — *noel delet lefaneiha u'meisah mei'eileiha*, like the *chatat hameitah*. Why the difference? Abaye and Rava each offer a distinct rationale for why one may not directly mutilate an animal that has been consecrated.

Abaye's reason: *mishum bizui kodashim* — it would be disrespectful to actively mutilate something that carries sanctity. Locking it up until it dies of its own accord is a less overtly degrading fate than physical maiming, even though the outcome is similarly destructive. Rava's reason: directly blemishing a consecrated animal *nira ke'matil mum bekodashim* — it appears as though one is making a blemish in a sacrifice, which is a specific biblical prohibition.

### Slaughter Risk

The Gemara then asks: if these concerns prevent direct mutilation, why not simply slaughter the animal (*venishchetei nishchat*)? Slaughter is not maiming, and it is a dignified form of dispatch. The answer: *atu behu lidei takalah* — people would see the slaughtered animal and think it is kosher meat, leading them to eat it and thus benefit from *hekdesh* in a prohibited manner.

Tosafot raises a further question: why would slaughtering this consecrated animal not constitute *shechutei chutz* — the biblical prohibition against slaughtering a sacrificial animal outside the *Beit HaMikdash*? Rashi's reading of the *baraita* resolves this: the animal was consecrated for *bedek habayit* — Temple maintenance — and not as a *korban*. Since it lacks the sanctity of a sacrificial offering, no prohibition of *shechutei chutz* applies. Tosafot, however, appears to contemplate cases where the animal might have been consecrated as a *korban*. In that scenario, Tosafot reaches a striking conclusion: the prohibition of *shechutei chutz* may not apply when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, since the defining element of the offense is the bringing of what *could* be offered inside outside instead — and when there is no inside, the framework of the violation collapses. Whether this reasoning is entirely satisfying is another matter.

## Sanctified Property

### Bizui Kodashim

The Gemara now examines Abaye's rationale — *bizui kodashim* — more carefully. Why, asks the Gemara, is letting an animal die of starvation by locking it in an enclosure not itself considered *bizui*? Surely slowly starving a consecrated animal is also disrespectful. The Gemara's implied answer is that active mutilation carries a qualitatively different kind of disrespect — a deliberate, hands-on degradation — compared to the passive act of enclosure, even if the latter also ends in death. The underlying principle of *bizui kodashim* thus operates asymmetrically: it prohibits direct acts of degradation but does not extend to every means by which sanctified property is ultimately rendered inert.

### Lock Up

The *baraita* that defines *ikur* as locking up rather than mutilating is central to Abaye's analysis. Its logic is: since directly blemishing or killing the animal would constitute disrespect to the sanctified object, the alternative ordained by the Sages is to sequester the animal — removing it from use and benefit — while permitting it to perish through the more passive mechanism of deprivation. The same logic that forbids one from directly *making* a blemish (*matil mum*) on a sanctified animal applies here by analogy.

### Verse Proof

Rava's rationale introduces a distinct scriptural basis. He points to the verse *lo ta'asun ken l'Hashem Eloheichem* — do not do thus to the Lord your God. This verse, in its original context, prohibits the Israelites from treating the altars and sanctuaries of God the way the nations are commanded to treat their pagan altars — i.e., it forbids the active destruction of sanctified objects. From this verse the Sages derive that one may not actively destroy consecrated property. Thus, even though we wish to ensure that the *hekdesh* animal yields no benefit to anyone — the whole purpose of the rabbinic *knas* being to prevent a *me'ilah* violation — we cannot accomplish this through active demolition or mutilation of the consecrated object. The permissible path is the indirect one: lock it away and let nature take its course.

### Mum Appearance

Rava's own stated rationale, as noted, is that direct mutilation *nira ke'matil mum bekodashim* — it has the appearance of making a blemish in a sacrificial animal. The Gemara presses: if this animal genuinely has the sanctity of a *korban*, and one actually makes a blemish in it, this is not merely *nira ke'matil mum* — it *is* a real blemish, a *mum me'alya*. So the prohibition should be framed not as one of appearance but as the actual biblical offense.

### Blemish Conditions

The Gemara refines the analysis by specifying the conditions under which the prohibition of making a *mum* in consecrated property applies. The prohibition is operative when the animal in question — even if already blemished — retains some path toward contributing to the sacrificial system. The paradigmatic case is a *baal mum*, a blemished sacrifice: even though it cannot itself be offered on the altar, so long as the *Beit HaMikdash* stands, it can be redeemed — its sanctity transferred through the redemption money to another animal that *can* be brought — and the proceeds used to purchase a valid *korban*. In that case, *lidmei chazi* — it is suitable in terms of the monetary value it can contribute — and therefore the prohibition of making a further *mum* still applies, since a legal and functional path to the altar remains.

But when there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, even an unblemished consecrated animal stands in a fundamentally different position: *d'lo chazi l'gufei v'lo lidmei chazi* — neither the animal itself nor any proceeds from it can currently be brought as a *korban*. In that situation, there would be no biblical violation in making a *mum*, since the entire framework presupposes a functional sanctuary that can receive the offering or its redemption proceeds. All the more so in our case, where the animal is merely *bedek habayit* hekdesh rather than a *korban* — the concern is not actual *mum* but merely *nira ke'matil mum*, an appearance of impropriety that might confuse onlookers. For that concern of appearance, one does not directly mutilate; instead one locks the animal away to die passively, thus removing it from benefit without committing what could be misread as an act of blemishing a sacrifice.

## Non-Jewish Slaves

### Slave Destruction

The Gemara now turns to a case involving human beings. Rabbi Yonah poses a question to Rabbi Ilai at the gateway of Tzur: if a person transacts business in the market of *avodah zarah* in violation of the prohibition, and the consequence for an animal is *behemah te'akeir* — maiming — what is the consequence for a slave purchased in that same market? The question specifically concerns a non-Jewish slave (*eved oved kochavim*); the case of a Jewish slave purchased there is not the subject of inquiry. How far does the *knas* extend? Is the slave also subject to some form of degradation?

### Not Saving Idolaters

The answer is swift: *tanya ovdei kochavim ve'ro'ei behemah dakah lo ma'alin ve'lo moridin* — there is a *baraita* that teaches that with respect to idolaters and shepherds of small cattle, one does not pull them up from the pit but also does not push them down. This establishes the principle: one may not *directly* kill a non-Jew, even under these circumstances. Whatever the *knas* demands, actively taking the life of a human being is not within its scope. The logical consequence is that the question of maiming does not arise in the same way — if one cannot kill the slave, certainly one cannot treat him as one would a *behemah* subject to *te'akeir* purely as a rabbinic penalty. The *knas* framework simply does not extend to that conclusion.

### Under Shekhinah

The Gemara then turns to the question that arises in a different context: what is the positive reason for purchasing a non-Jewish slave in the permissive *baraita* that allows such purchases at the pagan market? The question is posed between Rav Yirmiya and Rav Zeira: when the *baraita* permits buying slaves at the *yarid*, does it mean Jewish slaves — whose Jewish status obviously constitutes a sufficient reason for the extraordinary dispensation — or does it extend to non-Jewish slaves as well? Rav Yirmiya assumes the permission must refer to Jewish slaves; otherwise, *de'ei eved oved kochavim lemai ibai lei* — what purpose does acquiring a non-Jewish slave serve? Ravin, however, transmits in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi that it extends even to a non-Jewish slave, *mishum demachnisan tachat kanfei hashechinah* — because acquiring them as a Jewish-owned slave brings them under the protection of the Divine Presence, making them obligated in certain *mitzvot* and thus bringing them closer to the community of Israel. One might find this framing somewhat surprising — is there an obligation, or even a strong preference, to proactively acquire non-Jewish slaves in order to bring them into a state of partial obligation in *mitzvot*?

### Me'utaihu

Rav Ashi raises a more fundamental objection: *atu behemah mai tachat kanfei hashechinah ika lemeimar?* Does one bring a cow under the wings of the *Shekhinah* by purchasing it? The *kanfei hashechinah* rationale cannot be the animating reason for the *baraita*'s permission, because the permission extends equally to cattle — and cattle are not candidates for spiritual elevation. The true reason, Rav Ashi argues, is *mishum mi'utaihu* — the goal is the diminishment of non-Jewish wealth and presence, the strategic reduction of the resources and holdings in non-Jewish hands. Purchasing cattle, slaves, fields, and vineyards serves the same purpose in each case: it moves control over these assets from the non-Jewish sphere into the Jewish sphere, further entrenching the *yishuv* in Eretz Yisrael. Rashi renders *mi'utaihu* in terms of generally diminishing the wealth of idolaters (*she'mame'it ovdei kochavim umedalelan*), though the more contextually precise reading — consistent with the land-centered logic running through this entire chapter — seems to be about the specific project of consolidating Jewish presence in the land of Israel. The fact that Rashi does not explicitly limit his formulation to Eretz Yisrael may leave the question somewhat open, but the parallel material throughout the chapter strongly points in that direction.

## Sandal & Bread

### Mutual Criticism

The Gemara closes with a vivid narrative that brings the entire framework to life. Rav Yaakov purchased a sandal at one of the market days of *avodah zarah*, and Rav Yirmiya purchased bread. When each encountered the other, each said to the other: *yatama avad rabbach hachi?* — "You orphan! Would your master have acted this way?" The epithet is pointed: to call someone an orphan in this context is to say, essentially, you are without guidance, without a teacher you honor, without the discipline that a living rabbinic relationship instills. And yet both men had acted in precisely the way they were criticizing the other for acting.

### Ironic Suspicion

The irony is rich and deliberate. Each knew, of course, that his own purchase was entirely in order — he had bought from a *ba'al habayit* and paid no *meches*, no tax that could benefit *avodah zarah*. Yet seeing his colleague at the same market, each immediately assumed the worst: that the other had bought from a merchant, had paid the cultic tax, and had transgressed. Neither applied the charitable standard of *dan le'chaf zekhut*, judging one's fellow favorably. The Gemara makes no effort to soften this. It is a striking admission that even great *amoraim* in their ordinary interactions could fail to extend to one another the presumption of correctness that they surely would have extended had they paused to think.

### Three Categories

The resolution — already established above but now grounded in this incident — distinguishes three categories of sellers at the pagan market. The first is the *tagar*, the professional merchant, from whom a *meches* is collected on every sale and flows to the cultic institution; purchasing from such a seller is forbidden. The second is the *ba'al habayit*, the ordinary homeowner or flea-market seller, not in business as a merchant; in the typical case, no *meches* is taken from such a person, and purchasing from him is permitted. The third category — introduced here to resolve the specific case of Rav Yaakov and Rav Yirmiya — is the *ba'al habayit she'eino kavua*, the homeowner who is not a regular or registered participant at the market, someone selling ad hoc and passing through rather than occupying a designated stall. Rav Abba the son of Rav Chiya bar Abba clarifies that on a particular market day, Rabbi Yochanan had observed that even homeowners had a *meches* levied on their sales — meaning the distinction between merchant and homeowner is not absolute but depends on the local practice of that particular market. In such a place, Rabbi Yochanan would have forbidden purchases even from homeowners. In the case of Rav Yaakov and Rav Yirmiya, what saved both of them was that they had purchased specifically from this third category — sellers so peripheral to the organized market that even the tax collectors did not assess them. Each had acted correctly; each had been too quick to assume his colleague had not.
