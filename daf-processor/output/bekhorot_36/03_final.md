# Bekhorot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Halacha: Testimony Rules

### Rav Nachman's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, רָבָא אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated: Rav Naḥman says** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** that a priest is deemed credible to testify about the blemished firstborn animal of another priest, even if he is a household member. **Rava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** that one's household members are not deemed credible to testify.

The daf opens at the bottom of 35b, picking up a sustained discussion about who may be believed when testifying that a *mum d'bechor* — a blemish on a firstborn animal — occurred naturally and was not inflicted by the owner. The previous discussion surveyed a range of positions: some authorities held that believability depends on whether the witness stands to benefit, so that the owner himself cannot be trusted; others, most notably Rabbi Meir, held that *kohanim* as a class are suspect in this area and cannot testify about a *bechor* at all, even concerning someone else's animal. The *Gemara* now turns to the practical question of how we rule.

The language of *itmar* introduces two competing rulings from Amoraic authorities. Rav Nachman states: *halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel. Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel's position is that a person is believed about someone else's *bechor*, even if that person is a relative. The sole restriction is that one is not believed about the blemishes on one's own *bechor*.

### Rava's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** that one's household members are not deemed credible to testify.

Rava disagrees: *halacha k'Rabbi Yosi* — we rule in accordance with Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi's position is a refinement of Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel's: family members are not believed about one another's animals. A relative may not testify about the *bechor* of another family member, though a complete stranger would be believed.

### Owners Outside Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: בְּעָלִים עוֹמְדִים עִמָּנוּ בַּחוּץ, נִכְנַס שָׁלֵם וְיָצָא חָבוּל — מְעִידִין עָלָיו! אֵימָא: כׇּל בְּעָלִים עוֹמְדִים, וְלָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rava** actually **say this? But doesn't Rava say:** If the priestly **owner** of a firstborn animal was **standing outside with us,** and the animal **entered** the house **whole and emerged injured,** the household members **may testify about it** that the blemish was not caused by a person. Evidently, Rava maintains that the members of the priest's household are deemed credible to testify about the priest's firstborn. The Gemara answers: One can **say** that Rava is referring to a case where **all the owners,** i.e., the household members, **are standing outside,** while the shepherd alone remains inside. When the firstborn animal emerges injured, the shepherd is deemed credible to testify about the blemish **and we are not concerned** that he is lying.

The *Gemara* immediately questions whether Rava truly maintained this position, citing another ruling attributed to him. Rava had stated that if witnesses testify that the owners were standing outside — *ba'alim omdim imanu* — and the sheep entered the house uninjured and emerged with a blemish, those inside may testify about it. This seems to imply that family members present in the house would be believed, even though the owner was not, directly contradicting Rabbi Yosi's restriction.

### Shepherd Testimony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נֵיחוּשׁ לַחֲשָׁדָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְדַוְקָא בְּנוֹ וּבִתּוֹ, אֲבָל אִשְׁתּוֹ לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִשְׁתּוֹ כְּגוּפוֹ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** that all of the household members were outside, **what** is the purpose **of stating** that the shepherd is deemed credible? This ruling is obvious. The Gemara answers: This ruling is necessary **lest you say** that **we should entertain a suspicion** that the shepherd himself caused the blemish. Rava therefore **teaches us** that this is not so, and the shepherd is deemed credible. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel,** that a priest's household members may testify about his blemished firstborn offering. **And** this applies **specifically** to **his son and daughter, but his wife** may **not** testify. **What is the reason? One's wife is as oneself.**

The *Gemara* resolves the tension: the case Rava described involves a scenario where *all* the owners were outside. In that setting, presumably a shepherd was inside, and it is the shepherd — not a family member — who is testifying. Nor are we concerned that the shepherd was bribed or conspired with the owners, since all the owners were demonstrably absent. The *Gemara* asks: is that not obvious? Why would Rava need to state it at all? The answer is that one might have thought we should nonetheless entertain a concern — *nichosh l'chashada* — that perhaps the owners arranged the trap beforehand, or that the shepherd is being compensated for his testimony. Rava's ruling teaches us that we do not entertain that suspicion when all the owners are outside.

### Final Rule & Spouse

Having worked through the competing positions, the *Gemara* issues its definitive ruling: *v'hilchata k'Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel. One is believed about someone else's *bechor*, and this extends even to family members. The one exception is a spouse. *V'davka bno u'vito* — specifically one's son or daughter may testify; but *ishto*, one's wife, may not testify on his behalf. The reason offered is *ishto k'gufo dami* — a man's wife is like himself. The underlying principle, well known in other halachic contexts, is that the interests of husband and wife are so thoroughly identified with one another that the spouse is treated as if she were the owner herself. This is not understood as a statement about women in particular but about the nature of the marital relationship: there are things a person will do for a spouse that they would not do for anyone else, and that shared interest compromises the reliability of spousal testimony in this specific context.

### Bechor vs. Kashrus

It is worth pausing to observe that this entire framework of suspicion and restricted testimony is strikingly different from ordinary areas of *kashrus* and *issur v'heter*. In those domains, a person is generally believed about the status of their own food even when they have a direct personal interest. There is no requirement to produce independent witnesses before eating food that one testifies was purchased from a *kosher* establishment, and one's spouse is trusted on these matters as a matter of course. Why should *bechor* be treated more stringently?

Two factors emerge. The first is the concept of *beyado* — within one's power. In many areas of *kashrus*, the person asserting the permissibility of something could have ensured its proper status to begin with: one could have purchased from a reliable store, or supervised the *shechita* properly. That control lends credibility to the subsequent claim. In the case of *bechor*, however, it is not *halachically* within one's power to produce a *mum*. One cannot simply arrange for the animal to develop a valid blemish. The person begins with an animal of fixed status and has no legitimate means of changing it.

The second and perhaps more fundamental factor is that the area of *bechor* was, historically, one where there was rampant abuse. The *yetzer hara* in this domain was evidently powerful: an owner is stuck with an animal he cannot use, must continue to feed and maintain it indefinitely, and cannot benefit from it in any way until it develops a qualifying blemish. This created conditions in which widespread dishonesty took hold to such an extent that the entire class of *kohanim* came under suspicion, as we saw in Rabbi Meir's position. Even the authorities who do not adopt Rabbi Meir's sweeping ruling acknowledge that the immediate owner cannot be trusted. This is a special case in which normal presumptions of credibility are displaced by the documented reality of abuse in this particular area. The most lenient ruling — that of Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel — is still more restrictive than the baseline rule for *issur v'heter* generally.

## Kohanim as Judges

### Rav Pappa Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר הֶחָשׁוּד עַל הַדָּבָר לֹא דָּנוֹ וְלֹא מְעִידוֹ, וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר הֶחָשׁוּד לְדָבָר אֶחָד חָשׁוּד לְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ, כֹּהֲנִים הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא דָּיְינִי דִּינָא? וְהָכְתִיב ״וְעַל פִּיהֶם יִהְיֶה כׇּל רִיב וְכׇל נָגַע״!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Pappa said to Abaye: According to Rabbi Meir, who says that** a priest **who is suspect about the matter** of causing a blemish may **neither adjudicate nor testify** in cases involving that matter even on behalf of another, **and** in addition **Rabbi Meir says** that **one who is suspected of** transgressing **one matter,** i.e., who is known to have committed one transgression, **is suspected of** transgressing **the entire Torah,** it should follow that **priests** should **also not** be allowed to **administer judgment** at all. **But isn't it written** with regard to the priests: **"And according to their word shall every controversy and every stroke be"** (Deuteronomy 21:5)?

Rav Pappa poses a sharp question to Abaye: Rabbi Meir rules that *hachashud al hadavar lo dano v'lo meido* — one who is suspect concerning a matter may not serve as a judge in that matter and may not testify about it. Rabbi Meir also holds, as the *Gemara* established several *dafim* ago, that *hachashud l'davar echad chashud l'chol haTorah kullach* — one who is suspect for one transgression is to be considered suspect across the entire Torah. If we combine these two Midrashic premises with Rabbi Meir's position that *kohanim* as a class are suspect with respect to *bechor*, then *kohanim hachi nami d'lo dayni dina* — do *kohanim* therefore never sit on a *Beis Din*? Do we simply disqualify *kohanim* from all judicial and testimonial functions? And yet the *pasuk* says *v'al pihem yihyeh kol riv v'chol naga* — that all disputes and all plague decisions are adjudicated through them. Could one really mean to say that the very people the Torah designates as the religious and judicial authorities are to be blanket-disqualified from that role?

### Rabbi Meir Paradox

This is a genuinely striking *Gemara*. It presses the logical tension inherent in accepting both of Rabbi Meir's principles simultaneously. On the one hand, the *Gemara* had no hesitation about attributing a class-level suspicion to *kohanim* with respect to *bechor*. On the other hand, taking that suspicion to its logical conclusion under Rabbi Meir's framework leads to an absurd and unacceptable result: the complete exclusion of *kohanim* from religious authority.

One might think that the challenge is conflating two different things — suspecting individual *kohanim* who are judges with saying that all judges happen to be *kohanim*. But the *Gemara* here is working with the Torah's explicit framework, in which *kohanim* are positioned as the religious adjudicators. Even if in the post-*Beis HaMikdash* world that role has largely passed to the sages, the Torah still designates *kohanim* for this function, and one cannot simply declare the entire class disqualified.

It is worth noting that this question also arises in other areas of *halacha*. In standard *issur v'heter* contexts, a person with a financial stake in an outcome is nevertheless believed. A butcher who tells you his meat is *kosher* is believed even though he has an obvious financial interest in that claim. *Ed echad ne'eman b'issurin* — one witness is believed in matters of religious prohibition — applies regardless of personal gain. The *bechor* case is exceptional because the *kohanim* have specifically earned a reputation for dishonesty in this domain. But the existence of that class-level suspicion in one particular area, where it is well documented, does not mean we export that suspicion everywhere.

### Suspicion Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דְּאָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר לַחֲשָׁשָׁא, לְאַחְזוֹקִינְהוּ מִי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: One can **say that Rabbi Meir says** that priests are suspect with regard to causing blemishes only to the extent **that** there is **a concern** that they might have caused a blemish. But **did he say** this ruling **to establish them** as those who definitely cause blemishes? Certainly not.

The resolution turns on a crucial distinction in how Rabbi Meir's two rules operate. When Rabbi Meir says that someone suspect for one thing is suspect for everything, this applies specifically to *l'achzukonei* — when there is established, concrete knowledge about a particular individual: we have seen this person repeatedly violate, we know them specifically to be dishonest in a documented way. In that circumstance, Rabbi Meir argues that a person who allows themselves to violate in one area will violate in others, because the internal barriers are already broken.

The suspicion applied to *kohanim* with respect to *bechor*, by contrast, is *l'chashasha* — a class-level concern, not established fact about any specific individual. We know that *kohanim* as a group have, in the post-*Beis HaMikdash* reality, been prone to dishonesty in this particular area. But we do not have documented evidence about any specific *kohen* before us. Since that is a *chashasha* and not an established *chazaka* of personal dishonesty, we cannot apply Rabbi Meir's principle that disqualifies someone from everything.

### When Fully Disqualified

This distinction has important practical implications. If I personally witness someone regularly eating *treif* — not merely a suspicion but observed habitual violation — then, according to Rabbi Meir, I can no longer trust that person about anything. We do not rule like Rabbi Meir in this regard; the accepted halacha is that people compartmentalize. A person may rationalize eating non-*kosher* food while being scrupulously careful about *Shabbat*, or may rationalize dishonesty in one domain while maintaining high standards in another. But Rabbi Meir's position — that documented, consistent violation of one prohibition destroys credibility across the board — only even arises when we have concrete personal evidence of regular violation.

Where there is only a class suspicion — *kohanim* in general, not this *kohen* in particular — even Rabbi Meir would not extend the disqualification to all areas. We remain concerned about *bechor* specifically because the historical record of abuse in that area is clear. We do not therefore declare *kohanim* untrustworthy about *negaim*, or *kashrus*, or anything else. The two principles of Rabbi Meir operate at different thresholds and cannot be combined in a way that destroys the entire category of *kohanim* as religious authorities.

## Hearsay Testimony

### Hearsay Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: עֵד מִפִּי עֵד, מַהוּ לְעֵדוּת בְּכוֹר? רַב אַסִּי אָסַר, וְרַב אָשֵׁי שָׁרֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַסִּי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא תָּנָא דְבֵי מְנַשֶּׁה: אֵין עֵד מִפִּי עֵד כָּשֵׁר אֶלָּא לְעֵדוּת הָאִשָּׁה!
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: With regard to **testimony** based on **hearsay,** where one repeats the testimony of another witness, **what is** the *halakha* **in** a case of such **testimony** about a blemished **firstborn** animal? **Rav Asi deems prohibited** the slaughter of the animal based on such testimony, **and Rav Ashi deems** it **permitted. Rav Asi said to Rav Ashi: But isn't it taught in** a *baraita* in the **school of Menashe** that **hearsay testimony is valid only for** the **testimony of a woman,** where one testifies that her husband is dead? This indicates that such testimony is not accepted in all other instances.

The *Gemara* raises a further question: *ed mi-pi ed* — what is the status of second-hand testimony for *bechor*? In ordinary legal contexts requiring formal testimony, second-hand testimony is invalid: if Michael witnessed the *mum* occurring naturally but is not present, and I come to testify about what Michael told me, that does not constitute valid testimony. But *bechor* cases may not require the same formal evidentiary standards. Can second-hand testimony serve as a sufficient basis to permit the animal?

It is worth appreciating how difficult the underlying evidentiary challenge really is. The very fact that testimony is demanded at all makes these cases extraordinarily hard to establish. For a *mum* to permit the slaughter of a *bechor*, one needs to demonstrate that the blemish occurred naturally — not merely to demonstrate the absence of infliction, which cannot be proven, but to have actually witnessed the event. How often will someone be present at the precise moment an animal injures itself? The question of *ed mi-pi ed* therefore has enormous practical significance: allowing second-hand testimony would substantially relieve the nearly impossible evidentiary burden.

### Assi vs. Ashi

The passage shows Rav Assi saying *assur* — second-hand testimony is forbidden. Rav Ashi says *shari* — it is permitted. The *Gemara* offers a mnemonic: Assi says *assur*, Ashi says *shari*.

### Agunah Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנִי: אֶלָּא לְעֵדוּת שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה כְּשֵׁרָה לָהּ בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi responded: Emend the *baraita* and **teach** it like this: Testimony based on hearsay is valid **only for** that **testimony for which** the testimony of **a woman is valid.** According to Rav Ashi, the *baraita* is teaching that any case where the testimony of a woman is valid, hearsay testimony is valid as well, and this includes testimony concerning a blemished firstborn animal.

Rav Assi then challenges Rav Ashi from a *baraita*: the school of Menashe taught that *ein ed mi-pi ed kasher ela l'eidus isha* — second-hand testimony is valid only for testimony concerning a woman. This refers to the *agunah* situation: if a husband died in a distant location and the only evidence of his death comes through hearsay, are we to leave the wife an *agunah* indefinitely for want of direct testimony? The rabbis permitted second-hand testimony in this case alone, precisely because of the severity of the *agunah* problem. According to Rav Assi's reading, this exceptional dispensation does not extend to *bechor*.

### Women's Testimony

The *Gemara* corrects the reading of the *baraita*. The phrase *eidus isha* does not mean testimony *about* a woman or her husband specifically. Rather, it means testimony that is the kind *for which a woman is qualified*. The *baraita* teaches that second-hand testimony is accepted in the same categories of cases where a woman's testimony is accepted. Women are excluded from formal judicial testimony — the two-witness requirement for civil, capital, and similar cases — but are accepted as witnesses in matters of *issur v'heter* and *kashrus*. The principle *ed echad ne'eman b'issurin* applies regardless of the gender of the witness. A woman is fully believed when she testifies about the *kashrus* status of food, about the status of a *bechor*, and so forth. These are not matters requiring the formal apparatus of two male witnesses.

By this logic, since formal two-witness testimony is not required in such matters, and since women may testify in them, there is no principled reason to demand that even the single witness testify directly rather than through a reliable report. Second-hand testimony is a relaxation of the same formal requirements that are already relaxed in these contexts.

It is worth noting a parenthetical observation relevant here. Tosafot in *Gittin* make the point that a husband routinely trusts his wife about what she places on the dinner table — the *kashrut* status of food in one's own home is accepted on the word of one's spouse without further inquiry. The formal question about women serving in institutional roles of *hashgacha* is distinct and concerns authority rather than credibility, as Rav Moshe addresses in a *teshuvah*. But the baseline principle is clear: women are fully believed in areas of *issur v'heter*, and second-hand testimony follows the same logic.

### Hearsay Accepted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יֵימַר אַכְשַׁר עֵד מִפִּי עֵד בִּבְכוֹר, קָרֵי עֲלֵיהּ מָרִימָר: ״יֵימַר שָׁרֵי בּוּכְרָא״. וְהִלְכְתָא: עֵד מִפִּי עֵד כָּשֵׁר לְעֵדוּת בְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **Rav Yeimar deemed fit testimony** based on **hearsay with regard to** a blemished **firstborn** animal. **Mareimar** condescendingly **called him: Yeimar** who **permits firstborn** animals. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that **testimony** based on **hearsay is valid** in the case **of a firstborn** animal.

Rav Yeimar stated explicitly that *ed mi-pi ed* is permitted in *bechor* cases. Mareimar reportedly called him *Yeimar sharei buchra* — Yeimar the one who permits *bechor* — which Rashi notes was a disparagement. Nevertheless, the *Gemara* rules definitively: *v'hilchata ed mi-pi ed kasher l'eidus bechor* — second-hand testimony is valid for purposes of *bechor* testimony.

## Self-Disclosure Case

### Unknown Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: לֹא הָיוּ מוּחְזָקִין בּוֹ שֶׁהוּא בְּכוֹר, וּבָא אֶחָד וְאָמַר שֶׁהוּא בְּכוֹר וּמוּמוֹ עִמּוֹ — נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses a related matter. **Rabbi Ile'a says:** In a case where a blemished animal **was not established as being a firstborn, and one** priest **came** to an expert **and said that it is a firstborn** animal **and** yet **its blemish is with it,** i.e., it was unintentionally blemished, **he is deemed credible,** and the expert may deem the animal fit for slaughter based on the priest's testimony.

Rabbi Elazar presents the following scenario: an animal's status as a *bechor* was not publicly known — *lo hayu muchzakin bo she-hu bechor*. A *kohen* comes forward and declares that it is a *bechor*, and simultaneously presents the animal with a blemish, claiming the blemish occurred on its own. Should he be believed?

This case has important practical implications. Much of the earlier discussion about the impossibility of procuring testimony rests on the assumption that the animal's status as a *bechor* is already established and known. But very often, no one outside the owner's household knows which animals in a flock are *bekhorot*. If the *kohen* is the first to disclose that it is a *bechor*, the evidentiary landscape shifts entirely. Rabbi Elazar rules: *ne'eman* — he is believed.

### Same Mouth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, שֶׁהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר? תְּנֵינָא: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה ״אֵשֶׁת אִישׁ הָיִיתִי וּגְרוּשָׁה אֲנִי״ נֶאֱמֶנֶת, שֶׁהַפֶּה שֶׁאָסַר הוּא הַפֶּה שֶׁהִתִּיר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rabbi Ile'a **teaching us?** Is he teaching us the principle **that the mouth that prohibited** it **is the mouth that permitted** it, i.e., when the only source that an item was prohibited is the statement of one who says that it is now permitted, his claim is accepted? But **we learn** this in a mishna (*Ketubot* 22a): With regard to **a woman who said: I was a married woman and** now **I am a divorcée, she is deemed credible** and permitted to remarry, **as the mouth that prohibited** her by establishing that she was married **is the mouth that permitted** her by establishing that she was divorced.

The *Gemara* asks: what is the *chiddush*? Is this not obvious from the principle of *she-hapes asar hu hapes hitir* — the same mouth that forbids is the same mouth that permits? Since without the *kohen*'s declaration I would have had no reason to treat this animal as a *bechor*, and I only know it is a *bechor* because the *kohen* told me, I should believe his entire account. Having credited him on the more restrictive side — acknowledging the animal's sacred status — I should credit him on the permissive side as well, namely that the blemish occurred naturally.

### Divorced Woman

The *Gemara* confirms this principle is already taught in a *mishnah*: a woman who comes forward and says, "I was married but I am now divorced," is believed — *ne'emenet, she-hapes asar hu hapes hitir*. We had no prior knowledge that she was ever married; she herself created the restriction by disclosing her married status, and the same declaration includes the resolution of that restriction. Since we have no contradicting information, we accept both parts of her statement together.

### Forced Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָתָם הוּא דְּאִי בָּעֲיָא לָא אָמְרָה. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּלָא סַגִּיא דְּלָא אָמְרָה, דְּקָדָשִׁים בְּחוּץ לָא אָכֵיל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Ile'a's statement is necessary, **lest you say it is** only **there,** in the case of the mishna, that the woman's testimony is accepted, **as, if she wants** to remarry illegally, **she** does **not** need to **say** anything at all about previously being married. **But here,** with regard to the firstborn animal, it is different, **as,** if the priest wishes to eat the meat of the animal, **it not sufficient** for him **not to** bring the animal to an expert, i.e., he has no recourse other than to **say** that it is a firstborn animal which has a blemish requiring examination, **as** he cannot determine on his own whether this blemish permanently disqualifies the firstborn animal from sacrifice, and **he would not eat sacrificial** meat **outside** the Temple courtyard, which is punishable by *karet*.

The *Gemara* rejects the analogy, however. In the case of the divorced woman, she had a genuine choice: *de-ba'ei lo amrah* — had she wished, she could simply have said nothing. Since we had no prior knowledge of her marital status, she could have presented herself as single without any declaration whatsoever. Her voluntary disclosure of both the restriction and its resolution gives her claim credibility precisely because she was not compelled to speak.

In the *bechor* case, the *kohen* was not free to remain silent. *De-lo sagi de-lo amrah* — he had no alternative but to acknowledge that the animal was a *bechor*, because *kadshim bachutz lo achil* — he would not permit himself to eat *kodshim* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. He had to bring the animal to a *chacham* to have the blemish assessed; that process necessarily involved identifying the animal as a *bechor*. His admission that it was a *bechor* was therefore not freely given. Since he was compelled to make that disclosure regardless, the principle of *she-hapes asar hu hapes hitir* does not apply: we cannot treat his admission of *bechor* status as a voluntary act that lends credibility to his accompanying claim about how the blemish occurred.

### Won't Eat Invalid

The underlying psychology here is important. Even granting that *kohanim* are suspect for inflicting blemishes on their animals, we do not suspect them of the far more serious offense of eating *kodshim bachutz*, which carries *chiyuv karet*. The *kohen* will rationalize making a *mum* on his own, but he will not rationalize eating a *korban* that has no valid blemish outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. This is why, when the *kohen* presents the animal to a *chacham* for inspection, we trust that he genuinely believes the blemish is valid: he would not eat the animal if he did not. People compartmentalize their rationalizations, and the *kohen*'s rationalization extends to producing a *mum* but not to the much graver violation of *kodshim bachutz*.

### Challenge Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שְׁנָא מֵהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאוֹגַר לֵיהּ חֲמָרָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא תֵּיזִיל בְּאוֹרְחָא דִּנְהַר פְּקוֹד דְּאִיכָּא מַיָּא, זִיל בְּאוֹרְחָא דְּנַרֶשׁ דְּלֵיכָּא מַיָּא. אֲזַל בְּאוֹרְחָא דִּנְהַר פְּקוֹד וּמִית חֲמָרָא, וַאֲתָא וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּאוֹרְחָא דִּנְהַר פְּקוֹד אָזְלִי, וּמִיהוּ מַיָּא לָא הֲווֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi objects to this: What is different** between this case and **that** incident involving **a certain man who rented a donkey to another?** The owner **said to** the renter: **Look, do not go on the path of Nehar Pekod, where there is water** and the donkey is likely to drown. Instead, **go on the path of Neresh, where there is no water.** The renter **went on the path of Nehar Pekod and the donkey died. When he came** back, **he said:** Yes, **I went on the path of Nehar Pekod, but there was no water** there, and therefore the donkey's death was caused by other factors.

Mar bar Rav Ashi raises a further challenge. If the entire argument for believing the *kohen* is that he could have stayed silent — and therefore, having spoken, must be telling the truth — the *Gemara* invokes a parallel case. If someone rents a donkey to a friend and warns him not to take the route by the Wadi of Nahar Pekod because there is water there, and to take instead the route of Nash where there is none, and the borrower disobeys and takes the dangerous route, the borrower becomes liable for negligence if the water caused the donkey's death. He would be exempt, however, if the Wadi happened to be dry that day. The borrower now claims that he did indeed take the forbidden route but that there was no water. He could have simply said he took the safe route and avoided the problem entirely — *iy ba'ei amar be-orcha de-Nash azli*. Rava holds that since he did not have to tell this complicated story, we believe him: *mah lei leshaker*.

### Challenge Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲמַר רָבָא: מָה לוֹ לְשַׁקֵּר, אִי בָּעֵי אָמַר: בְּאוֹרְחָא דְּנַרֶשׁ אֲזַלִי. וַאֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: ״מָה לוֹ לְשַׁקֵּר״ בִּמְקוֹם עֵדִים לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava said:** The renter's claim is accepted, due to the reasoning of: **Why would he lie** and state this claim? In other words, **if** this man **wanted** to lie, **he** could have **told** the donkey's owner: **I went on the path of Neresh,** as the owner instructed. **And Abaye said to** Rava: **We do not say** the principle of: **Why would he lie, in a place** where there are **witnesses.** Since witnesses can be summoned to establish conclusively whether there was water along the path of Nehar Pekod, the reasoning that the renter could have stated a different claim is not employed. Similarly, the priest's contention that the blemish occurred inadvertently should not be deemed credible, as it is known that priests are suspected of causing blemishes.

### Suspicious = Honest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם וַדַּאי אִיכָּא מַיָּא, הָכָא וַדַּאי שָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא — חֲשָׁשָׁא הוּא, וּבִמְקוֹם חֲשָׁשָׁא אָמְרִינַן ״מָה לוֹ לְשַׁקֵּר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** with regard to the path of Nehar Pekod, **there is certainly water there, but** here, the possibility **that** the priest **caused a blemish in** the firstborn animal **is** only **a concern, and in a place of** mere **concern we** do **say** the reasoning of: **Why would he lie?** The Gemara relates that **Ravina was sitting and saying this *halakha*** of Rabbi Ile'a **anonymously. Rava Zuti said to Ravina: We learned this** *halakha* **in the name of Rabbi Ile'a.**

The *Gemara* draws the crucial distinction. In the case of the wadi, we say *vadai ika maya* — the water is definitely there. The borrower's claim is simply false, and *mah lei leshaker* cannot override something patently true. But in the *bechor* case: *vadai shadei bei muma*? Do we say with certainty that this *kohen* definitely inflicted the blemish himself? Of course not. The suspicion against *kohanim* is a *chashasha* — a concern that some *kohanim* sometimes do this — not a certainty that every *kohen* always does. *U-ve-makom chashasha amrinan mah lei leshaker*: to counteract a suspicion, not a certainty, we do apply the principle that having no reason to lie lends credibility. We assume this particular *kohen* is among the honest ones, and his voluntary disclosure supports that.

There is a further point. Even if one were to press the suspicion further, there is an additional reason to believe the *kohen* in this case: *havei meshanei be-mumo de-nikkar* — if he were dishonest and simply wanted to make the animal permissible, he would have made an obvious, unambiguous blemish, not one that required a *chacham*'s ruling. The very fact that this blemish required expert adjudication suggests it was accidental. A *kohen* engineering a fraudulent *mum* would not put himself through the *tzaros* of having to bring the animal to town and submit it for inspection; he would simply inflict an unmistakable blemish and be done with it. The borderline nature of the blemish is itself evidence of honesty.

## Tzadok Story

### Tzadok's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי צָדוֹק הֲוָה לֵיהּ בּוּכְרָא, רְמָא לֵיהּ שְׂעָרֵי בְּסַלֵּי, בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָאָכֵיל אִיבְּזַע שִׂיפְתֵּיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אָמַר לוֹ: כְּלוּם חִילַּקְנוּ בֵּין חָבֵר לְעַם הָאָרֶץ? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Tzadok,** an erudite priest, **had a firstborn** animal. **He placed barley in** wicker **baskets for it,** and **while it was eating, its lip split,** rendering the animal blemished. Rabbi Tzadok **came before Rabbi Yehoshua,** to ask whether or not he is suspected of intentionally causing a blemish in his firstborn animal offering. Rabbi Tzadok **said to him: Didn't we differentiate between** a priest who is **a *ḥaver*,** i.e., learned, **and** a priest who is **an ignoramus,** with regard to their credibility about blemishes found on a firstborn animal? **Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: Yes,** we did. Since you are a learned priest, you are deemed credible to testify that this blemish was caused inadvertently.

The *Gemara* now presents a narrative that brings the theoretical debates into vivid relief. Rabbi Tzadok, who was a *kohen*, had a *bechor* in his possession. He placed barley in a basket made of woven reeds or similar sharp-edged material — the precise description of the material is a matter of textual variants — and as the animal was eating from the basket, its lip was cut on the edge of the wicker. The blemish occurred entirely accidentally, in the course of ordinary feeding. Rabbi Tzadok came before Rabbi Yehoshua to ask about his case.

### Yehoshua Yes

Rabbi Yehoshua responded affirmatively: *kelum chilaknu bein chaver le'am ha'aretz*? — Do we make a distinction between a *kohen* who is a *chaver*, meaning a trusted *talmid chacham* or member of the community of precision in matters of *tahara*, and a *kohen* who is simply an *am ha'aretz*? The implied answer Rabbi Yehoshua gave was *hein* — yes, we do make such a distinction. A *kohen* of standing and learning, someone who is "one of us," can be trusted in a way that an ordinary *kohen* off the street cannot.

### Gamliel No

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, אָמַר לוֹ: חִילַּקְנוּ בֵּין חָבֵר לְעַם הָאָרֶץ? אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: לָא. אָמַר לוֹ: (והא) [וַהֲלֹא] רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אָמַר לִי הֵן! אָמַר לוֹ: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלוּ בַּעֲלֵי תְרִיסִין לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Tzadok then **came before Rabban Gamliel,** the *Nasi* and head of the academy of Yavne at the time. Rabbi Tzadok **said to him:** Didn't **we differentiate between** a priest who is **a *ḥaver* and** a priest who is **an ignoramus** with regard to credibility about blemishes found on a firstborn animal? **Rabban Gamliel said to him: No,** we did not. Rabbi Tzadok **said to him: But Rabbi Yehoshua said to me** that **yes,** we did differentiate in this manner. Rabban Gamliel **said to** Rabbi Tzadok: **Wait until the masters of the shields [*ba'alei terisin*],** a reference to the Torah scholars who battle in the war of Torah, **enter the study hall,** at which point we will discuss this issue.

Unsatisfied with this answer, or perhaps uncertain, Rabbi Tzadok brought the same question to Rabban Gamliel. Rabban Gamliel's ruling was the opposite: *lav* — no, we do not make such a distinction. All *kohanim* are in the same category with respect to *bechor* suspicion, regardless of their learning or communal status.

### Public Humiliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, עָמַד הַשּׁוֹאֵל וְשָׁאַל: כְּלוּם חִילַּקְנוּ בֵּין חָבֵר לְעַם הָאָרֶץ? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לָאו. אָמַר לוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: וַהֲלֹא מִשִּׁמְךָ אָמְרוּ לִי הֵן! יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, עֲמוֹד עַל רַגְלֶיךָ, וְיָעִידוּ בְּךָ. עָמַד רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עַל רַגְלָיו וְאָמַר: הֵיאַךְ אֶעֱשֶׂה? אִילְמָלֵי אֲנִי חַי וְהוּא מֵת — יָכוֹל הַחַי לְהַכְחִישׁ אֶת הַמֵּת, עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאֲנִי חַי וְהוּא חַי — הֵיאַךְ חַי יָכוֹל לְהַכְחִישׁ אֶת הַחַי?
>
> **Translation:** **When** the Torah scholars **entered the study hall, the questioner stood** before everyone present **and asked:** With regard to blemishes found on a firstborn animal, **didn't we differentiate between** a priest who is **a *ḥaver* and** a priest who is **an ignoramus? Rabbi Yehoshua said to him: No,** we did not. **Rabban Gamliel said to him: But they said to me in your name** that **yes,** we did differentiate. Rabban Gamliel continued: **Yehoshua, stand on your feet and they will testify against you** that you did, in fact, say that we differentiated in such a case. **Rabbi Yehoshua stood on his feet and said: How should I act** in this situation? **If I were alive and** Rabbi Tzadok **were dead, the living can contradict the dead,** and I could deny issuing that ruling. **Now that I am alive and he is alive, how can the living contradict the living?** I have no choice but to admit that I said it.

When Rabbi Tzadok reported that Rabbi Yehoshua had given the opposite ruling, Rabban Gamliel did not simply note the disagreement and move on. Instead he said: *hamten ad she-ya'alu ba'alei terisin* — wait until the shield-bearers arrive, until the students of the *Beis HaMidrash* enter. The phrase *ba'alei terisin* invokes a military metaphor — the scholars who fight the battles of Torah — and its use signals that Rabban Gamliel intended to address this publicly. Once the students assembled in the *Beis HaMidrash*, Rabbi Tzadok stood and posed the question again: Do we make a distinction between a *chaver* and an *am ha'aretz*?

Rabbi Yehoshua, reading the situation and anticipating where this was heading, now answered *lav* — reversing what he had said privately. But Rabban Gamliel was not satisfied with this reversal. He pressed forward: *v'hala mishmecha amru li hein* — I have received a report that you said yes. He then commanded: *Yehoshua amod al raglecha v'ya'idu bach* — Rabbi Yehoshua, stand on your feet and let them testify against you that you ruled otherwise than my ruling.

Rabbi Yehoshua rose and, standing exposed before the entire assembly, said: *heich e'eseh* — what can I do? *Im l'chai ani v'hu meis* — had I been alive and he were dead, *yachol ha-chai l'hach'chish et ha-meit* — the living could perhaps have denied what the dead had said and there would have been no one to contradict. *Aval achshav she-ani chai v'hu chai* — but now that I am alive and he is alive, *heich yachol ha-chai l'hach'chish et ha-chai*? How can I contradict him? He acknowledges he is caught; there is living testimony against him and no way out.

Rabban Gamliel then continued his lecture with Rabbi Yehoshua standing there, singled out and exposed, *omeid al raglav* — still on his feet.

### Crowd Reaction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָיָה רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל עוֹמֵד וְדוֹרֵשׁ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ עוֹמֵד עַל רַגְלָיו, עַד שֶׁרִינְּנוּ כׇּל הָעָם וְאָמְרוּ לְחוּצְפִּית הַמְתוּרְגְּמָן: עֲמוֹד! וְעָמַד.
>
> **Translation:** In the meantime, **Rabban Gamliel was standing and lecturing, and Rabbi Yehoshua** all the while **was standing on his feet,** as Rabban Gamliel did not instruct him to sit. He remained standing in deference to the *Nasi*. This continued for some time, **until** it aroused great resentment against Rabban Gamliel, and **all of the people** assembled began **murmuring and said to Ḥutzpit the disseminator: Stop** conveying Rabban Gamliel's lecture, **and he stopped.**

The assembled students and public could not accept what they were witnessing. *Ad she-rin'nu kol ha-am* — until everyone began murmuring. They turned to Chutzpis the *meturgeman* — Rabban Gamliel's reciter and projector, who, as Tosafot notes, was one of the *Asarah Harugei Malchus* — and said to him: *amod* — stop. They effectively silenced Rabban Gamliel by cutting off his public voice, refusing to allow him to continue the lecture.

This is only half the story. In *Berachot*, the full account is recorded in the context of a dispute over whether *Tefillas Arvit* is obligatory or voluntary. Exactly the same sequence of events takes place: Rabbi Yehoshua states one position privately, is reported to Rabban Gamliel, and is publicly humiliated in the *Beis HaMidrash*. The *Gemara* there explicitly connects the events: the people's patience had been exhausted by Rabban Gamliel's repeated mistreatment of Rabbi Yehoshua — citing among the offenses the incident on *Rosh Hashanah* and the treatment of Rabbi Yehoshua in the *Bechoros* story, referred to there as *ha-ma'aseh de-Bechoros be-Rabbi Tzadok*. The cumulative effect led to a broader confrontation with Rabban Gamliel's authority, with attempts to dethrone him as *Nasi*.

### Power Dynamics

Beyond the surface narrative, there is a possible subtext worth considering. Why did this particular question — whether to distinguish between a *kohen chaver* and a *kohen am ha'aretz* — generate such charged confrontation? On one level, it may simply reflect Rabban Gamliel's well-documented tendency to assert his authority regardless of the substance of the debate, just as he did over the question of *Tefillas Arvit*.

But there may be something more structural at work. The post-*Beis HaMikdash* period witnessed a gradual displacement of *kohanim* as religious authorities, with that role passing increasingly to the *talmidei chachamim* and the *chaverim*. Simultaneously, as the *Gemara* has documented, *kohanim* became suspect in the area of *bechor* — precisely because they had been dishonest about it. These two developments are not unrelated: the decline in the religious authority of *kohanim* and the loss of public trust in them as a class happened in tandem.

If we were to credit a *kohen* who is a *talmid chacham* with special trustworthiness — as Rabbi Yehoshua suggested — that would be crediting him, at least in part, because of his status as a *kohen*, not only because of his learning. It would be an indirect way of preserving something of the *kohen*'s traditional religious authority. Rabban Gamliel's insistence that no such distinction be drawn — that all *kohanim* are equally suspect — can be read as a way of making absolutely clear that the religious authority of *kohanim* as such has no standing in this domain. He, who combined the role of *Nasi* with that of head of the *Beis HaMidrash*, had a particular stake in ensuring that the competing claim of *kehunah* to religious authority was not rehabilitated, even partially.

## Kohen Credibility

### Genuine Blemish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נֶאֱמָן הַכֹּהֵן לוֹמַר: הֶרְאֵיתִי בְּכוֹר זֶה וּבַעַל מוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A priest is deemed credible to say: I showed this firstborn** animal to an expert **and** he ruled that **it is blemished.**

The *Gemara* now presents a series of closely related rulings about what precisely a *kohen* is and is not believed about when it comes to *bechor* blemishes. The *mishnah* states: *ne'eman ha-kohen lomar her'eiti bechor zeh al mum l'chacham u-va'al mum hu* — a *kohen* is believed when he says, "I showed this *bechor* to a *chacham* and he ruled that it has a valid blemish." Note the precision: even though a *kohen* is generally suspect about how the blemish arose, he is believed that the blemish is genuine and has received a *chacham*'s approval. We will examine why these two questions — whether the blemish is real, and how it came about — receive different treatment.

### Source Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: נֶאֱמָן הַכֹּהֵן לוֹמַר ״בְּכוֹר זֶה נָתַן לִי יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמוּמוֹ״. מַאי טַעְמָא? כֹּל מִילְּתָא דַּעֲבִידָא לְאִיגַּלּוֹיֵי לָא מְשַׁקְּרִי בַּהּ אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A priest is deemed credible to say: An Israelite gave me this firstborn** animal **with its blemish** already inflicted upon it. **What is the reason?** With regard to **any matter that is likely to be revealed, people do not lie about it.** Since the Israelite can be questioned with regard to the veracity of the priest's claim, it is assumed that the priest will not risk lying.

Rav Yehuda citing Rav adds a further ruling: *ne'eman ha-kohen lomar bechor zeh natan li Yisrael b'mumo* — a *kohen* is also believed when he states that a *Yisrael* gave him this *bechor* with the blemish already present. This claim goes to the question of how the blemish arose, at least indirectly, and yet the *kohen* is believed. Why?

### Uncoverable Truth

The reason offered is the principle: *kol milta d'avida l'igluyei lo meshakrei bei inshi* — anything that will be uncovered, people do not lie about. If the *kohen* says he received the animal from a particular *Yisrael* with the blemish already there, I can simply ask that *Yisrael* whether this is true. The checkability of the claim is what lends it credibility.

### Won't Eat Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, נֶאֱמָן הַכֹּהֵן לוֹמַר ״הֶרְאֵיתִי בְּכוֹר זֶה וּבַעַל מוּם הוּא״. מַאי טַעְמָא? לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּאָמְרִינַן כֹּל מִילְּתָא דַּעֲבִידָא לְאִיגַּלּוֹיֵי לָא מְשַׁקְּרִי בַּהּ אִינָשֵׁי? הָתָם הוּא דְּקָדָשִׁים בְּחוּץ לָא אָכֵיל, אֲבָל הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דַּחֲשִׁידִי — חֲשִׁידִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: We learn** a similar principle in the mishna **as well: A priest is deemed credible to say: I showed this firstborn** animal to an expert **and** he ruled that **it is blemished. What is the reason** the priest's claim is deemed credible? **Is it not because we say** that with regard to **any matter that is likely to be revealed, people do not lie about it?** The Gemara rejects the claim that the mishna's ruling is based on the principle that people do not lie about a matter that is likely to be revealed: Perhaps **there,** in the case of the mishna, the reason the priest's claim is accepted is that **he** would **not eat sacrificial** meat **outside** the Temple courtyard. If an expert had not permitted the meat, the priest would not eat it. **But here,** in Rav's case, where the priest claims an Israelite gave him an already blemished animal, **since** priests **are suspected** of causing blemishes and claiming that it was inadvertent, **they are** also **suspected** of causing a blemish and claiming it was given to them in that state by an Israelite.

Rav Ashi attempts to derive the same principle from the *mishnah* itself: the *kohen* is believed that he showed the animal to a *chacham* and received approval — is this not also because the *chacham* can be asked? But the *Gemara* rejects that inference. The reason the *kohen* is believed about the *chacham*'s ruling is not because it can be verified, but because *kadshim bachutz lo achil* — he will not eat *kodshim* outside the *Beis HaMikdash*. The *kohen*'s only suspect motivation is the temptation to make a *mum* in order to use the animal. He has no comparable temptation to misrepresent whether the blemish is genuine, because he himself has no desire to eat an animal that lacks a valid blemish — the transgression is far too severe. His credibility about the validity of the blemish is thus grounded in his own self-interest as much as in the checkability of the claim.

### How It Occurred

When it comes to the question of *how* the blemish occurred, the *kohen* is not believed. Here his financial interest and the documented class-level suspicion are fully operative. Even if the claim can be checked — even if, theoretically, one could verify whether the *Yisrael* transferred the animal with the blemish already present — the fact that the *kohen* is suspect in this specific area means we do not rely on checkability alone.

### Ma'aser Messenger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שֵׁיזְבִי: הָאוֹמֵר לְמִי שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱמָן עַל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר ״קַח לִי מִמִּי שֶׁהוּא נֶאֱמָן״ אוֹ ״מִמִּי שֶׁהוּא מְעַשֵּׂר״ — אֵינוֹ נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizevi raises an objection** to Rav's ruling from a mishna (*Demai* 4:5): In the case of **one who says to someone who is not trusted with regard to tithe: Purchase** produce **for me from someone who is trusted** with regard to tithes, i.e., one who does not purchase produce from an ignoramus, who generally does not separate tithes; **or** if he says: Purchase produce for me **from someone who tithes,** i.e., even from one who purchases produce from an ignoramus but is careful to set aside tithes upon purchasing the produce, the agent **is not deemed credible** to claim that he fulfilled the condition of the one who appointed him. According to Rav, **why** isn't the agent deemed credible? **Say** the principle that with regard to **any matter that is likely to be revealed, people do not lie about it.**

Rav Shizbi challenges the principle of *avida l'igluyei* from a case involving *ma'aser*. If one instructs a messenger who is not himself careful about *ma'aser* to purchase food only from a vendor who is reliable about it — or from a vendor who tithes for himself — the messenger is *not* believed when he reports having done so. Why? Because despite the fact that I could theoretically check up on his story, he knows that I probably will not bother, or that even if I do, he can always claim he believed the vendor to be reliable when perhaps he was not. The checkability of the claim does not here produce credibility.

### Fear & Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְאִשְׁתְּמוֹטֵי. סֵיפָא וַדַּאי מְסַיְּיעָא לֵיהּ: מֵאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי — הֲרֵי זֶה נֶאֱמָן. הָתָם, כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לֵיהּ תּוֹבֵעַ, מִירְתַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is different there,** **as he has** the easy option **of extricating himself** from the accusation that he did not fulfill the condition of the one who appointed him, by claiming that in his opinion the individual from whom he purchased the produce was to be trusted. Since the issue of the third party's trustworthiness is subjective, the agent is not afraid to lie. The Gemara suggests: The **latter clause** of this mishna **certainly supports** the ruling of Rav, that people do not lie with regard to any matter that will be revealed. How so? The latter clause states that if the one instructed the agent to purchase produce specifically **from so-and-so,** who is reliable with regard to tithes, then **this** agent **is deemed credible** to claim that he adhered to the instructions. Since the one who appointed him mentioned a specific individual, the agent is not suspected of lying, as that individual can be subsequently questioned. The Gemara rejects this proof: **There,** in the mishna, **since** the agent **has a claimant, he is afraid** to lie. In other words, since the one who appointed him specified an individual, the agent presumes that he intends to verify the matter, and therefore he will not risk lying. By contrast, in Rav's case, since it was the priest who volunteered the information that a specific Israelite gave him an already blemished firstborn animal, he is not concerned that the examiner might seek out the Israelite to confirm the actual facts.

The *Gemara* resolves: *shani hatam d'it lei l'ishtamutei* — that case is different because he has an easy escape. The messenger can always say he thought the vendor was trustworthy. The ability to equivocate and wriggle out of accountability means that even if his story is technically checkable, the risk of being caught is effectively neutralized. *Mah lei leshaker be-makom eidim* does not prevent lying when the liar can maneuver around the contradiction.

However, the *seifa* — the closing clause of that same *baraita* about *ma'aser* — supports the principle. If I instruct you to buy specifically from this particular person — naming a specific store or a specific vendor — then *harei zeh ne'eman*, you are believed. Why? Because *keivan d'it lei tveiya mirtat* — since I am pressing you with a specific demand, you are afraid. You understand that I will follow up precisely, and the social and practical fear of being found to have disobeyed specific instructions keeps you honest. Specificity and the clear communication of accountability produce credibility in a way that general instructions do not.

### Borderline Blemishes

The *Gemara* leaves us with an important open question for the following daf. The *peshat* of our *mishnah* is that the *kohen* is believed that the *mum* is a genuine *mum* because he is not suspect about eating *kodshim bachutz* — he will not eat the animal without a real blemish, so his report of the *chacham*'s approval is trustworthy. But this leaves a difficulty unresolved: if the *kohen* is believed only because he would not want to eat an invalid *korban*, what happens when the blemish is of a borderline nature — a *mum over*, a transient blemish that may pass? The *kohen* cannot be certain it will not resolve. Why should we believe him about the validity of something that is, by its nature, uncertain and not *avida l'igluyei* in any straightforward sense? This question carries us into the next daf.
