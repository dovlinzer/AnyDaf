# Menachot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kometz & Vessel Hold (II)

### Rava: Ground Vessel OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי, קוֹמֵץ מִכְּלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּסִילּוּק בָּזִיכִין. מְקַדְּשִׁין מִנְחָה בִּכְלִי שֶׁעַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּסִידּוּר בָּזִיכִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **obvious to me** that a priest may **remove a handful from a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground, as we find** such an instance **in** the case of **the removal of** the **bowls** of frankincense from the Table of the shewbread, since the Table is resting upon the ground of the Sanctuary when they are removed. Similarly, **one can sanctify a meal offering in a vessel that** is resting **upon** the **ground, as we find** such an instance **in** the case of **the arrangement of** the **bowls** of frankincense upon the Table of the shewbread.

The daf begins in the middle of 7b, approximately six lines from the top, where the Gemara has been exploring whether the *kli sharet* used in the *kometz* process must be held by a *kohein* or whether it may rest on the ground. Rava opens with a declaration: *peshita li* — it is obvious to me — that the *kometz* may be taken from a *kli sharet* that is resting on the ground. His proof is drawn from the *siluk bazeichin*: when the small spoons of incense are removed from the *shulchan*, those spoons function as the *kometz* of the *lechem hapanim*, insofar as the twelve loaves correspond to the *shirayim* of a *mincha* that are eaten, and the *bazikim* correspond to the portion that is burned. Even though there is no flour involved, the structural role they play mirrors that of the *kometz*. Since the *bazikim* are removed from a table that rests on the ground, we learn that the *kometz* may likewise be taken from a ground-based vessel.

Rava extends this logic further: one may also place the *mincha* into a *kli sharet* resting on the ground for the purpose of *kidush kli*, the initial sanctification. The proof again is from the *sidur bazeichin* — the original arrangement of the loaves and spoons on the *shulchan*, which is itself a ground-based vessel. So both the initial placing of the *mincha* into the *kli* and the subsequent taking of the *kometz* from it are established as valid even when the vessel is on the ground.

### Placing Kometz Stage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: קִידּוּשׁ קוֹמֶץ מַאי? מִמִּנְחָה יָלְפִינַן לַהּ, אוֹ מִדָּם יָלְפִינַן לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **sanctification of a handful** by placing it in a vessel that is resting upon the ground, **what** is the *halakha*? Do **we derive** this *halakha* **from** the sanctification of **a meal offering,** in which case one can sanctify a handful in this manner, just as he can do so with a meal offering? **Or** do **we derive it from** the collection of the **blood** of an offering, in which case one cannot do so, just as the blood of an offering may not be collected in a vessel that is resting upon the ground?

However, one stage of the process remains unaddressed: after the *kometz* has been taken, it must be placed into a second *kli sharet*. May that vessel also be on the ground? Here the logic shifts. From the moment the *kometz* is taken, the process of bringing it to the *mizbe'ach* has effectively begun — this is the stage at which *pigul* considerations become relevant, and the *kohein* is actively engaged in the *avoda*. When a vessel is left on the ground, the forward motion of the *avoda* is, in a sense, interrupted; the offering is no longer being actively brought. One might therefore argue that at this later stage, the vessel must be held by a *kohein*.

### Rava's Query

This is precisely Rava's question: *bai rava kidush kometz mai* — what is the law regarding the *kidush* of the *kometz* at this placement stage? Do we continue to learn from the earlier stages of the *mincha* itself, where the *kli* may be on the ground? Or do we now shift our analogy and learn from *dam* — from the blood of an animal *korban*? From the moment of *kmitza* onward, there is indeed a structural analog to *korbanot*: the *kometz* is caught in a *kli* and brought to the *mizbe'ach*, just as blood is caught in a vessel and brought to the *mizbe'ach*. If we draw that parallel, then the *kli* cannot be on the ground, since by blood the *avoda* of *kabbalas hadam* requires an active physical holding of the vessel — without that, there is no valid act of reception.

It is worth noting an important distinction: by blood, the *avoda* is defined as *kabbalah* — catching. Without holding the vessel, there is no act. By a *mincha*, however, the *avoda* is *netina* — placing. One can perform the act of placing even if the vessel rests on the ground. This difference could have justified treating the two cases differently. Nevertheless, Rava's question is whether, from this point on, we follow the analogy to blood.

### Learn from Dam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: מִדָּם יָלְפִינַן לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **then resolves** the dilemma: **We derive it from** the collection of the **blood.**

The Gemara records: *hadar pashat* — Rava ultimately resolved his question and concluded *midamya yalfinan*, that we do in fact learn from blood at this stage. The resolution is presented without detailed reasoning; it appears to rest on the logical observation that once the *kmitza* is taken and the *avoda* begins in earnest, the relevant model is that of animal *korbanot*, and accordingly the vessel must be held by a *kohein*. This means two *kohanim* are involved at that stage — one cannot hold the vessel in one's own other hand, given the requirement to use the right hand.

## Kometz Two Vessels

### Chalako B'Shnei Kelim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רָבָא הָכִי? וְהָא אִתְּמַר: קוֹמֶץ שֶׁחִלְּקוֹ בִּשְׁנֵי כֵּלִים, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, וְרָבָא אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rava** really **say this,** that the *halakha* with regard to the sanctification of a handful is derived from the collection of the blood? **But it was stated:** With regard to a full measure of **a handful that** a priest **divided** and placed **in two vessels, Rav Naḥman says** that **it is not sanctified, and Rava says** that it is **sanctified.**

The Gemara immediately challenges whether Rava genuinely holds that we learn from *dam*, by citing a separate tradition: if one divided the *kometz* between two vessels — placing half into one *kli sharet* and half into another, rather than placing the entire *kometz* into a single vessel — what is the law? Rav Nachman says *eino kadosh*: it is not sanctified. Rava says *kadosh*: it is sanctified.

The relevance of this dispute to Rava's earlier position becomes clear when we trace the chain of reasoning that underlies it. The principle that the minimum required amount must all be caught in a single vessel derives from the law of *haza'ah* by *parah adumah*. From there it is extended to the *kabalah* of *dam* of a *korban* — the minimum amount necessary for sprinkling must all be caught in a single vessel. If Rava holds that the *kidush kometz* is learned from *dam*, one would expect him to require the entire *kometz* to be placed into a single vessel, just as the minimum amount of blood must be caught in a single vessel. Yet here Rava permits dividing the *kometz* between two vessels. The Gemara presses: if Rava truly learns from *dam*, why does he not apply the requirement of a single-vessel minimum to the *kometz* as well?

## Kometz Two Vessels (II)

### Rava Reverses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם אִיתָא, לֵילַף מִדָּם! הֲדַר בֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵהַהִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is so** that the *halakha* of the handful is derived from the collection of the blood, then **let** Rava **derive from blood** that the handful is not sanctified in this manner, just as the blood is not sanctified when divided into two. The Gemara responds: **Rava retracted that** statement and ruled that a handful is not sanctified when divided and placed into two vessels.

The Gemara records that *hadar beih Rava me-hahi* — Rava retracted from his position. Rashi explains that Rava reversed his ruling that the *kometz* placed in two vessels is *kadosh*, and ultimately agreed that one must place the entire *kometz* into a single vessel, consistent with learning from *dam*. This is the direction the Gemara has been building toward: the analogy to blood is the governing principle, and the requirement of a minimum amount in a single vessel applies equally to the *kometz*.

### Dam Minimum Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וָדָם מְנָלַן דְּלָא קָדוֹשׁ לַחֲצָאִין? דְּתָנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בֶּן שָׁאוּל: קִידֵּשׁ פָּחוֹת מִכְּדֵי הַזָּאָה בִּכְלִי זֶה, וּפָחוֹת מִכְּדֵי הַזָּאָה בִּכְלִי זֶה – לֹא קִידֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further discusses the *halakha* with regard to the collection of the blood. **And** with regard to **blood, from where do we** derive **that it is not sanctified in halves,** i.e., when collected in two vessels? It is derived from **that** which **Rav Taḥlifa ben Shaul teaches** with regard to the water of purification: If the priest **sanctified** the water in two vessels in such a manner that he sanctified **less than the amount of sprinkling in this vessel,** i.e., there was not enough water into which he could dip a bundle of hyssop and sprinkle the water with it, **and** he sanctified **less than the amount of sprinkling in that vessel,** then **he has not sanctified** the water. Even if he subsequently combines the contents of both vessels into a single vessel, the water is not sanctified.

Having established that Rava reverses to align with the blood analogy, the Gemara turns to ask: where do we know in the first place that blood is not sanctified if less than the minimum amount is caught in each of two vessels? The source is a *braitah* taught by Rav Tachlipa bar Shmuel, which we will examine in the next section.

## Kometz & Vessel Hold (II)

### Learn or Not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּדָם מַאי? הִלְכְתָא הִיא, וּמֵהִלְכְתָא לָא יָלְפִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **With regard to** the collection of **blood** of a sin offering for the purpose of sprinkling it upon the altar, **what** is the *halakha*? **Is** the ruling with regard to the water of purification **a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai, in which case the *halakha* with regard to blood may not be derived from it, as **we do not derive** other cases **from a *halakha*** transmitted to Moses from Sinai?

Before proceeding to that *braitah*, the Gemara raises a broader question: does Rabbi Eliezer in fact learn the laws of *mincha* from *dam*? This question is prompted by a teaching attributed to Rabbi Eliezer regarding the *chavitei Kohen Gadol*, which will be treated at greater length below. As the Gemara eventually establishes, Rabbi Eliezer does not learn *mincha* from *dam* at all — rather, he learns *mincha* from *mincha*, that is, from the *lechem hapanim*. This distinction is critical: it means that Rabbi Eliezer's rulings about the *mincha* cannot be challenged on the basis of what we know about blood, and conversely, what we know about blood cannot be straightforwardly imported into the *mincha* context according to his view.

## Dam One Vessel Rule

### Parah Minimum Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא הָתָם מַאי טַעְמָא – דִּכְתִיב ״וְטָבַל בַּמַּיִם״, הָכָא נָמֵי הָכְתִיב ״וְטָבַל בַּדָּם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the other side of the dilemma: **Or perhaps, there,** in the case of the water of purification, **what is the reason** that it is not sanctified? It is possible that the reason is **that it is written** in a verse that is referring to the water initially placed in the vessel: **"And dip it in the water"** (Numbers 19:18). If this verse is the source of the *halakha* that the hyssop may be dipped in the water of purification only when there was initially enough water in the vessel for sprinkling, then **here too,** in the case of the blood of a sin offering, **isn't it written: "And** the priest **shall dip** his finger **in the blood"** (Leviticus 4:6)?

The Gemara now presents the *braitah* of Rav Tachlipa bar Shmuel, which anchors the entire discussion. The *haza'ah* of *parah adumah* requires a minimum amount of liquid — enough to be absorbed by the hyssop and then sprinkled. If one sanctified less than that minimum amount in one vessel and less than that minimum amount in a second vessel, the water has not been sanctified, and one may not combine the two and use them for sprinkling. The entire minimum amount must have been present together at the moment of sanctification.

### Dam Same Law?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי זְרִיקָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אַף בְּדָם לֹא קִידֵּשׁ. אָמַר רָבָא: תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, בִּפְנֵי כֹּהֵן מַנִּיחַ, ״וְטָבַל״ וְלֹא מְסַפֵּיג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And it was stated** with regard to this dilemma: **Rabbi Zerika says** that **Rabbi Elazar says: Even in** the case of the **blood, one has not sanctified** it if he collected less than a full measure of blood in a single vessel. **Rava said** that **this is also taught in** a *baraita* **with regard to** the bull of **the anointed priest.** The verse states: "And the priest shall dip his finger in the blood, and sprinkle of the blood" (Leviticus 4:6). From the term **"And** the priest **shall dip"** it is derived that there must be enough blood inside the vessel in which to dip his finger, **and** there should **not** be so little blood that he must resort to **wiping** his finger along the walls of the vessel.

This rule is established for *parah adumah*, but the question is immediately raised: does the same law apply to the blood of a *korban*? One might argue that the *parah adumah* rule is a *halachah l'Moshe miSinai* — a technically arbitrary law transmitted from Sinai — and from such laws we do not extrapolate to other areas. Or perhaps the *parah adumah* rule derives from a *pasuk*, in which case the same *pasuk* or parallel reasoning would apply to *dam* as well.

### V'Taval B'Dam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּדָּם״ – שֶׁיְּהֵא בַּדָּם שִׁיעוּר טְבִילָה מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, ״מִן הַדָּם״ – מִן הַדָּם שֶׁבָּעִנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: Additionally, from the term **"in the blood"** it is derived **that there should initially be in** the vessel containing **the blood a measure** fit for **dipping** his finger. Furthermore, it is derived from the term **"of the blood"** that he must sprinkle **from the blood of the matter,** as will be explained.

The Gemara resolves this by examining the verse governing how the *kohein* dips his finger in the blood: *v'taval* — he shall dip — and *badam* — in the blood. The Gemara demonstrates that both terms are necessary and that each conveys a distinct requirement. Had the Torah written only *v'taval*, one would know that the act of dipping requires immersion in collected liquid rather than merely running the finger around the rim — what the Gemara calls *maspig*, a sponging motion — but one might have thought it permissible to collect a little blood in one vessel and a little in another, then combine them before dipping. Had the Torah written only *badam*, one would know that the minimum amount must be present from the outset, but one might have permitted the sponging motion. Both terms together establish: first, the act must be a genuine dipping in pooled liquid; and second, the minimum amount must have been present in the vessel from the beginning.

### Dam Original Amount

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״וְטָבַל״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״בַּדָּם״, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״וְטָבַל״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֹא קִיבֵּל שִׁיעוּר טְבִילוֹת, דְּהַיְינוּ הַזָּאָה שֶׁבַע פְּעָמִים מֵעִיקָּרוֹ, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״בַּדָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And it was necessary** for the Merciful One **to write: "And** the priest **shall dip," and it was necessary** for the Merciful One **to write: "In the blood,"** despite the fact that both terms are referring to the amount of blood that must be in the vessel. **Because if the Merciful One had written** only: **"And** the priest **shall dip," I would say** that it is enough if the vessel contains enough blood for even one sprinkling, **even though** the priest **did not initially collect a measure** fit for all of the **sprinklings, that is,** enough with which to **sprinkle seven times.** Therefore, **the Merciful One writes: "In the blood."**

Rava confirms this reading with a supporting *braitah*: *v'taval* — not *maspig*; *badam* — *shete'hei badam shiur tvila m'ikaro*, that the minimum amount required for dipping must have been in the vessel originally, from the moment the blood was caught. This is now established as a *pasuk*-based rule applicable to the blood of *korbanot*, not merely to *parah adumah*, and it is on this basis that we apply the same requirement to the *kometz* — assuming one learns *kometz* from *dam*.

## Finger Remnants

### Fresh Dam Each Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִן הַדָּם״ שֶׁבָּעִנְיָן, לְמַעוֹטֵי מַאי? אָמַר רָבָא: לְמַעוֹטֵי שִׁירַיִם שֶׁבָּאֶצְבַּע, מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: שִׁירַיִם שֶׁבָּאֶצְבַּע פְּסוּלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the last statement of the *baraita*, that the priest must sprinkle **from the blood of the matter.** This statement serves **to exclude what? Rava said:** It serves **to exclude** the **remainder** of blood **on** his **finger** from the previous sprinkling, i.e., the priest must dip his finger into the blood before each sprinkling; he may not sprinkle with the blood that remains on his finger from the previous sprinkling. The Gemara notes: This **supports** the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar, who says** that the **remainder** of blood **that** remained **on** the priest's **finger is unfit** for sprinkling.

The Gemara then turns to the final phrase of the *braitah*: *min hadam shebe'inyan* — from the blood that is the subject of the matter. What does this phrase come to exclude? Rava explains: *l'mutei shirayim shebe'etzba* — it excludes the remnant blood left on the finger. If the *kohein* must perform multiple sprinklings — for example, the four corner applications of a *chatas*, or the seven sprinklings toward the *parochet* for an inner *chatas* — he may not use the blood remaining on his finger from the previous sprinkling. Rather, for each application he must return to the vessel and dip anew. The verse says *min hadam shebe'inyan* — from the blood that is in the vessel — meaning each sprinkling must draw from the original collected blood, not from whatever remains on the finger. This supports the ruling of Rabbi Elazar: *shirayim shebe'etzba psulin* — the remnant blood on the finger is invalid for further sprinklings.

### Splattered Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא לְרָבָא: אָמְרִי תַּלְמִידָךְ אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם, תַּנְיָא: הָיָה מַזֶּה וְנִתְּזָה הַזָּאָה מִיָּדוֹ, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא הִזָּה – טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס, מִשֶּׁהִזָּה – אֵין טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravin bar Rav Adda said to Rava: Your students say** that **Rav Amram says** that **it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where a priest **was sprinkling** from the blood of a sin offering **and** the blood of the **sprinkling sprayed from his hand** onto a garment, the *halakha* is as follows: **If** the blood sprayed onto the garment **before he sprinkled,** the garment **requires laundering,** as is the *halakha* when the blood of a sin offering that is fit for sprinkling fell on a garment. But if the blood sprayed onto the garment **after he** had already **sprinkled,** it **does not require laundering.**

At this point, a student of Rava named Ravin bar Rav Adda raises a challenge. His fellow students cite a *braitah* in the name of Rav Amram that appears to contradict this conclusion. The *braitah* states: if one was sprinkling blood and some blood fell from his hand, the question is whether the garment on which it falls requires the special ritual laundering prescribed by the Torah for blood of a *chatas*. The answer given is: if the blood fell from the finger *before* the sprinkling, laundering is required; if it fell *after* the sprinkling, laundering is not required.

### Single Sprinkling Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא, עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָצְתָה מִיָּדוֹ הַזָּאָה – טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס, וּמִשֶּׁיָּצְאָה הַזָּאָה מִיָּדוֹ וְנִתְּזָה מִמַּה שֶּׁנִּשְׁאַר – אֵין טָעוּן כִּיבּוּס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** the *baraita* is saying that if the blood sprayed from his hand onto the garment **before** any particular **sprinkling left his hand,** the garment **requires laundering. But** if the blood was sprayed **after a sprinkling left his hand,** in which case the blood **sprayed from that which remained** on his finger following that sprinkling, then the garment **does not require laundering,** as the blood left on his finger was already rendered unfit for sprinkling.

The Gemara rejects this reading. There is no reason to interpret the *braitah* as describing a series of sprinklings at all. The far more natural reading is: before the finger releases the blood in a single sprinkling, any blood that splatters on clothes requires laundering; after the finger has discharged the blood in that single sprinkling, blood that splatters from the finger does not. The *braitah* is simply describing the before-and-after of one sprinkling, not the beginning and end of a multi-sprinkling series. The challenge therefore dissolves: there is no evidence that *shirayim shebe'etzba* are valid for continued use.

### Parah Hand/Finger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: גָּמַר מִלְּהַזּוֹת – מְקַנֵּחַ יָדוֹ בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה. גָּמַר – אִין, לֹא גָּמַר – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised an objection to** Rava from a mishna discussing the red heifer (*Para* 3:9): When the priest has **completed sprinkling** the blood of the red heifer toward the entrance to the Sanctuary, **he wipes his hand** from the blood **on the body of the heifer.** Abaye explains his objection: The mishna states that when the priest **completed** all the sprinklings, then **yes,** he wipes his hand. It may be inferred that if he **did not complete** the sprinklings, he does **not** wipe his hand, even though blood remains on his finger from each preceding sprinkling. This proves that the blood that remains on his finger is fit for sprinkling.

A related question is raised by Abaye from the procedure of *parah adumah*, where the *kohein* sprinkles blood seven times toward the *Mikdash*. The *braitah* states: *gamar milhazot mekanuach yado begufah shel parah* — when finished with the sprinkling, he wipes his hand on the body of the *parah* itself, since the verse requires all the blood to be burned together with the animal. But *lo gamar mekanuach etzba'o* — when he is not yet finished, he cleans only his finger.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: גָּמַר – מְקַנֵּחַ יָדוֹ, לֹא גָּמַר – מְקַנֵּחַ אֶצְבָּעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: The mishna means that when the priest **completed** all of the sprinklings, **he wipes his hand.** If he has **not** yet **completed** all of them, then he does not wipe his hand but he must **wipe his finger** to remove the blood after each sprinkling, as that blood is no longer fit for subsequent sprinklings.

Abaye reads this as evidence that the *kohein* performed all seven sprinklings in succession from one dipping, and only after finishing did he wipe his hand on the *parah*. The intermediate action — cleaning the finger — would then refer only to grooming between sprinklings, not to re-dipping.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא גָּמַר מְקַנֵּחַ יָדוֹ בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְשָׂרַף אֶת הַפָּרָה לְעֵינָיו״, אֶלָּא לֹא גָּמַר מְקַנֵּחַ אֶצְבָּעוֹ, בְּמַאי מְקַנֵּחַ? דְּאִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה, אִיבְּעִי לֵיהּ לְמִיתְנֵי: מְקַנֵּחַ יָדוֹ וְאֶצְבָּעוֹ בְּגוּפָהּ שֶׁל פָּרָה! מִדְּלָא קָתָנֵי הָכִי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ דְּלָא בָּעֵי קִינּוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Granted,** after **he completed** all the sprinklings **he wipes his hand on the body of the heifer, as it is written: "And the heifer shall be burned in his sight;** her skin, and her flesh, and her blood" (Numbers 19:5), which indicates that the blood of the red heifer must be burned together with its flesh. **But** if when the priest had **not** yet **completed** the sprinklings **he wipes his finger,** then **with what** does he **wipe? Because if you say** that he wipes his finger **on the body of the heifer,** the mishna **should have taught:** He **wipes his hand and his finger on the body of the heifer.** Rather, **from** the fact **that** the mishna **does not teach this,** that he wipes his finger on the body of the heifer, one can **conclude from** this mishna **that** his finger **does not require wiping** between sprinklings.

The Gemara rejects this inference. The contrast is not between mid-series and end-of-series, but rather between two different cleaning actions: when *finished*, he wipes his entire hand on the *parah*, because the blood remaining on his whole hand must be burned; when *not yet finished*, he cleans only his finger — specifically, at the rim of the vessel (*b'sfat mizrak*), so that the finger is clean for the next dipping.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׂפַת מִזְרָק, כְּדִכְתִיב: ״כְּפוֹרֵי זָהָב וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** He wipes his finger **on the edge of** the **bowl** containing the blood, **as it is written: "Atoning bowls [*keforei*] of gold"** (Ezra 1:10), which is referring to the bowls containing the blood. The root *kafar* can also mean to wipe.

The Gemara cites Abaye's own answer: the vessels used in this process are called *kipurei zahav*, golden *kipurim*, and *kipur* implies cleansing — accordingly, the rim of the vessel is precisely the place designed for cleaning the finger between dippings.

The implication is striking: even for the seven sprinklings of *parah adumah*, the *kohein* must re-dip between each sprinkling, cleaning his finger at the rim of the vessel before dipping anew. The *shirayim shebe'etzba* are invalid, consistent with Rabbi Elazar's ruling and Rava's derivation from *min hadam shebe'inyan*.

## Chavitei Split Sanctify

### Yochanan vs. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הָכִי? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל – רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינָהּ קְדוֹשָׁה לַחֲצָאִין, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁקְּרֵבָה לַחֲצָאִין, קְדוֹשָׁה לַחֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And did Rabbi Elazar** really **say this,** that blood may not be sanctified in halves? **But it was stated** with regard to the **griddle-cake** offering **of** the **High Priest, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **it is not sanctified in halves, and Rabbi Elazar says: Since it is sacrificed in halves,** as half of the meal offering is sacrificed in the morning and half in the afternoon, it may likewise be **sanctified in halves.**

The Gemara now turns to the substantive debate about the *chavitei Kohen Gadol* — the daily *mincha* offering of the *Kohen Gadol*, which was a minimum-size *mincha* of one *isaron* brought in two halves, half in the morning and half in the afternoon. The question is whether the *mincha* could be initially prepared and sanctified in two separate half-*isaron* portions, or whether it must first be combined as a whole and then divided.

Rabbi Yochanan says *enah kedushah l'chatza'in*: the *mincha* cannot be sanctified in halves; it must be sanctified as a whole. Rabbi Eliezer says *mitoch shekreva l'chatza'in kedushah l'chatza'in*: since the *mincha* is brought in halves, it may also be sanctified in halves. The *kli sharet* can function on each half separately.

### Mincha Min Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחָה מִמִּנְחָה יָלֵיף, מִנְחָה מִדָּם לָא יָלֵיף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Elazar does **derive** the *halakha* with regard to **a meal offering from** that of another **meal offering;** the shewbread is considered a meal offering. But **he does not derive** the *halakha* with regard to **a meal offering from** that of **blood.**

This debate prompts the Gemara to ask whether Rabbi Eliezer can hold this position consistently with his ruling about blood — for if one learns the functioning of the *kli sharet* from *dam*, then just as the minimum amount of blood must all be caught in a single vessel, the *mincha* must all be placed in a single vessel. The Gemara resolves this by establishing that Rabbi Eliezer does not learn *mincha* from *dam* at all. Rather, he learns *mincha mimincha* — from one type of *mincha* to another. When Rabbi Eliezer validates *kmitza* performed in the *heichal* rather than the *azara*, his proof is *shekein matzinu b'sidur bazeichin*: the *bazikim* are placed on the *shulchan* inside the *heichal*, and this act corresponds to the *kmitza* of the *mincha*. He learns *mincha* from *lechem hapanim* — one type of *mincha* from another — but he does not cross the conceptual boundary from *mincha* to *dam*. Therefore, the rules that govern the functioning of the *kli sharet* in the context of blood cannot simply be transposed onto the *mincha* according to Rabbi Eliezer's view.

### Bread Breaking Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנְחָה מִמִּנְחָה מִי יָלֵיף? וְהָתַנְיָא: עַד שֶׁלֹּא פֵּרְקָהּ נִפְרַס לַחְמָהּ – הַלֶּחֶם פָּסוּל, וְאֵין מַקְטִיר עָלָיו אֶת הַבָּזִיכִין. מִשֶּׁפֵּרְקָהּ נִפְרַס לַחְמָהּ – הַלֶּחֶם פָּסוּל, וּמַקְטִיר עָלָיו אֶת הַבָּזִיכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does** Rabbi Elazar **derive** the *halakha* of one **meal offering from** that of another **meal offering? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **before** the priest **detached** the arrangement of shewbread and the bowls of frankincense from upon the Table, the **bread broke** into pieces, **the bread** is **unfit** for consumption **and** the priest **does not burn the** frankincense contained in the **bowls on** account of **it.** If the **bread broke after** the priest **detached it, the bread is unfit but** the priest **burns the** frankincense contained in the **bowls on** account of **it.**

The Gemara proceeds to examine whether Rabbi Eliezer consistently learns *mincha* from *mincha* by testing it against a *braitah* about the *lechem hapanim*. The *braitah* states: if before the *bazikim* were removed from the *shulchan* a loaf of the *lechem hapanim* broke, the bread is invalid and the *bazikim* may not be burned. If after the *bazikim* were removed a loaf broke, the bread is invalid — the *kohanim* may not eat it — but the *bazikim* may still be burned; the *korban* retains its validity in that respect.

The parallel to a *mincha* is as follows: if part of the *mincha* was lost before the *kmitza*, the entire *korban* is invalid. If the *kometz* was taken and then part of the *shirayim* was lost, the *kometz* remains valid and may be burned on the *mizbe'ach*, but the *shirayim* may not be eaten and must be burned. This correspondence is clean and direct.

### Higia Zmanah Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא פֵּרְקָהּ מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לְפָרֵק, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פֵּרְקָהּ, כְּמִי שֶׁפֵּרְקָהּ דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And Rabbi Elazar says:** When the *baraita* refers to the detachment of the shewbread, it does **not** mean that the priest **actually detached it. Rather,** it means that **once** the **time to detach it has arrived, even though he has not** yet **detached it** and has not removed the bowls, **it is considered as though he has detached it.**

Rabbi Elazar adds an important gloss to the *braitah*: the phrase "after the *bazikim* were removed" does not require that they were physically removed — it means only that the time to remove them has arrived. *Keivan shehigia zmana lefarek, af al pi shelo parka, kemi sheparka dami*: once the time to remove them came, it is as though they were already removed, even if the physical act had not yet occurred.

This principle — *higia zmanah* — cannot be applied to an ordinary *mincha*, and the Gemara explains why. In a *mincha*, the *kometz* has not yet been defined; it is a portion that must be taken from an undifferentiated mass of flour, and no one knows in advance which handful will be taken. One therefore cannot say "the time has come for the *kometz* to be taken" in a way that renders it already separated. With the *lechem hapanim*, by contrast, the *bazikim* are physically distinct objects sitting alongside the bread. They are clearly defined, identifiable, and separable. Once the time arrives to remove them, it is conceptually coherent to treat them as already removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? תֶּיהְוֵי כְּמִנְחָה שֶׁחָסְרָה קוֹדֶם קְמִיצָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its question: **And** if Rabbi Elazar derives the *halakha* of one meal offering from another, **why** does he say that frankincense contained in the bowls is burned in a case where the shewbread broke when the time to detach the bread had arrived? **It should be like** the case of **a meal offering that became lacking** in its measure **before the removal of the handful.** Such a handful is not removed and is not sacrificed upon the altar. Likewise, the frankincense was still on the Table when the shewbread broke and should therefore be disqualified.

A second difference, not raised by the Gemara but noted in the shiur: unlike a private individual's *mincha*, which may be brought at any time, the *lechem hapanim* has a fixed and precise time — Shabbat afternoon — at which the removal must occur. This fixed time makes it possible to invoke *higia zmanah* in a meaningful way.

### Machatzitah Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, מִנְחָה לָא בְּרִיר (בְּרֵירָה) קוֹמֶץ דִּידַהּ, וְהָא בְּרִיר (בְּרֵירָה) קוֹמֶץ דִּידַהּ, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לְפָרֵק – כְּמַאן דְּפָרְקַהּ דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This** is **not difficult,** as there is a difference between these meal offerings. In the case of **a meal offering** that became lacking before the removal of a handful, **its handful was not clearly designated.** Consequently, if the meal offering became lacking before a handful was removed, one may no longer remove a handful from it. **But** in the case of the shewbread and the bowls of frankincense, **its handful,** i.e., the frankincense, **was clearly designated** at the time when the frankincense was placed in the bowls, since the frankincense is in a separate container from the bread. **And** therefore, **once** the **time to detach** the bread **has arrived, it is considered as though he has detached it.**

The Gemara now turns to the textual basis for Rabbi Yochanan's position. Rav Acha cites the verse: *mincha machatzitah baboker umachatzitah bein ha'arbayim* — the *mincha*, half of it in the morning and half of it in the afternoon. The word *machatzitah* — "half of it" — indicates that one begins with a whole *mincha* and then takes half. The verse thus presupposes the existence of a complete *mincha* at the outset, before the division occurs.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? אָמַר קְרָא: ״מִנְחָה מַחֲצִיתָהּ״, הָבֵיא מִנְחָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ חֳצֵיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan? The verse states: "A meal offering** perpetually, **half of it** in the morning, and half of it in the evening" (Leviticus 6:13). This means: First **bring** a whole **meal offering, and** only **afterward divide it** into halves.

A *braitah* reinforces this reading: if the verse had said *mincha machatzit* — "a half-*mincha*" — one might have thought the *Kohen Gadol* brings half an *isaron* in the morning and makes it into a *mincha*, and another half an *isaron* in the afternoon and makes it into a separate *mincha*. But the verse says *machatzitah* — half of *it* — indicating that you start with one whole *mincha* and divide it. *Mechtza mishalem hu meivi*: he brings a half of a whole.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: חֲבִיתֵּי כֹּהֵן גָּדוֹל לֹא הָיוּ בָּאוֹת חֲצָאִין, אֶלָּא מֵבִיא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם וְחוֹצֵהוּ; וְתַנְיָא: אִילּוּ נֶאֱמַר ״מִנְחָה מַחֲצִית״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: מֵבִיא חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ שַׁחֲרִית וּמַקְרִיב, חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן מִבֵּיתוֹ עַרְבִית וּמַקְרִיב; תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מַחֲצִיתָהּ בַּבֹּקֶר״ – מֶחֱצָה מִשָּׁלֵם הוּא מֵבִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar. The mishna teaches (50b): The **griddle-cake** offering **of** the **High Priest did not come in halves. Rather,** the High Priest **brings a full tenth** of an ephah **and** then **divides it** into two. **And it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to this mishna: **If it were stated: A meal offering, half** in the morning, and half in the evening, **I would say: He brings half of a tenth from his home** in the **morning and sacrifices** it, and another **half of a tenth from his home** in the **evening and sacrifices** it. Therefore, **the verse states: "Half of it in the morning,"** indicating that **he brings a half from a whole,** and he does not bring a half by itself.

Against Rabbi Eliezer, the Gemara further notes that the Torah uses the language of *chok* — a fixed statutory requirement — in connection with the *chavitim*. Normally in the context of *korbanot*, the Torah's detailed specifications are not all *me'akeiv*, and failure to observe them does not necessarily invalidate the offering unless the Torah repeats or emphasizes the requirement, or uses the word *chok*. Since *chok* appears here, one might argue that the specific procedure of bringing a whole *mincha* and dividing it is indispensable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמִצְוָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב גְּבִיהָא מִבֵּי כְתִיל לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְהָא ״חוּקָּה״ כְּתִיב בַּהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לַהֲבִיאָהּ שָׁלֵם מִבֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rabbi Elazar maintains that the verse requires that a whole meal offering be brought in the morning only **for a mitzva,** i.e., *ab initio*. Nevertheless, if half of a tenth was brought in the morning it is valid after the fact. **Rav Geviha from Bei Katil said to Rav Ashi: But** the term **"statute" is written with regard to** the griddle-cake offering, as the verse states: "A statute forever" (Leviticus 6:15), and there is a principle that whenever the Torah calls a mitzva a statute, the details of its performance are indispensable. Rav Ashi **said to him: It was necessary** for the Torah to define this mitzva as a statute **only with regard to** the requirement that the High Priest **bring a full** tenth **from his home.** With regard to its sanctification in a service vessel, it can be sanctified in halves.

Rabbi Eliezer responds: the *chok* requirement applies only to the stage of bringing a whole *isaron* from one's home before the *mincha* is sanctified. The obligation is that the flour, before it enters the *kli sharet*, must consist of a full *isaron*. Once that threshold is met, Rabbi Eliezer maintains that the *kli sharet* may sanctify each half independently. This is admittedly a striking answer, since one would ordinarily expect the whole-*mincha* requirement to govern the sanctification stage as well as the preparatory stage. But Rabbi Eliezer's underlying principle is that the question of *kedushah* — whether the *kli sharet* functions to sanctify — is analytically separate from the question of how the *avoda* must be performed. Even if the *avoda* ideally requires a whole *mincha* that is then divided, the *kli sharet* may still effect *kedushah* on portions presented to it separately.

## Partial Amounts

### Rav Half Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָא אִיתְּמַר: הִפְרִישׁ חֲצִי עִשָּׂרוֹן, וְדַעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף – רַב אָמַר: אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** really **say this? But it was stated:** If one **set aside half a tenth** of an ephah for any meal offering, **and his intention** was **to add** to the half in order to reach a full tenth, **Rav says** that **it is not sanctified,** as he did not bring a full tenth, **and Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is **sanctified.**

The final section of the daf takes up a related question: what if a *kohein* places half an *isaron* into a *kli sharet* with the intention to add the remaining half subsequently? Is even this partial amount sanctified by the *kli*? Rav says *eino kadosh*: it is not sanctified, even with intent to add.

### Yochanan Half Valid

This is itself a surprising position from the perspective of Rabbi Yochanan, who has insisted that the whole *mincha* must be brought and then divided. Yet here Rabbi Yochanan says *kadosh*: even a half-measure placed with the intent to complete the full amount is sanctified by the *kli sharet*.

### Yosi Intent Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִילְּתָא מִמִּילְּתָא לָא יָלֵיף, וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּהֵיכָל כְּשֵׁרִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מֵעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan does not derive** the *halakha* of one **matter** with regard to consecrated items **from** that of another **matter,** that is difficult: **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: Peace offerings that** were **slaughtered in the Sanctuary are valid, as it is written: "And slaughter it at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2), i.e., in the courtyard. Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: It is logical **that** the *halakha* with regard to the **minor** area, i.e., the courtyard, **should not be more stringent than** the *halakha* with regard to the **major** area, the Tent of Meeting. Evidently, Rabbi Yoḥanan derives a *halakha* with regard to the Sanctuary from the Temple courtyard.

Rabbi Yosi refines the position with a crucial distinction: *bizman she'ein da'ato lehosif* — when one has no intention of adding more — the *kli* does not sanctify a partial amount. *Aval bizman sheda'ato lehosif, rishon rishon kadosh* — but when one intends to add more, each portion added becomes sanctified immediately as it is placed in the vessel. This is itself a *chiddush*: one might have thought that the full amount would become sanctified only at the end of the process, once the required measure is complete. Rabbi Yosi teaches that, since the entire process constitutes a single purposive act of filling the vessel, each increment is sanctified the moment it enters.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף שָׁאנֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: ״מְלֵאִים״ – אֵין ״מְלֵאִים״ אֶלָּא שְׁלֵמִים (כְּלוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא עִשָּׂרוֹן שָׁלֵם), וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵימָתַי? בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף, אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁדַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן קָדוֹשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan does in fact derive the *halakha* of one matter from another, and therefore he learns the *halakha* with regard to all meal offerings from the griddle-cake offering, that in general they are not sanctified in halves. But a case where one expresses **his intention to add** to the half measure **is different, as it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "Both of them **full** of fine flour" (Numbers 7:13). **"Full"** is a reference **only** to **full** measurements; **that is to say,** the flour **is not sanctified until there is a full tenth** inside the vessel. **And Rabbi Yosei said: When** is it the *halakha* that the flour is sanctified only if a full tenth is inside the vessel? It is **at a time when his intention** was **not** initially **to add** to that which he placed inside the vessel. **But at a time when his intention** was initially **to add, each initial** bit of flour is **sanctified** by the vessel.

### Rav Like Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, בְּחָבִיתִין כְּמַאן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ? אִי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, לֵילַף מֵחֲבִיתִּין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rav,** who holds that standard meal offerings are not sanctified in halves even if one's initial intention was to add to the half measure, **with regard to a griddle-cake** offering, **in accordance with whose** opinion **does he hold? If** he holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** who says that a griddle-cake offering can be sanctified in halves, then **let him derive from** the *halakha* of **griddle-cake** offerings that all meal offerings may be sanctified in halves.

The Gemara demonstrates that Rav is willing to learn from one *mincha* context to another. Rav rules that a *mincha* may be sanctified in the *kli sharet* before the oil has been added, because the *lechem hapanim* — which has no oil — is nonetheless sanctified. Similarly, a *mincha* may be sanctified without the *levona* having been placed on it, because the *minchat nesachim* — the *mincha* accompanying animal offerings — has no *levona*. And a *mincha* may even be sanctified without either oil or *levona*, because we find such a case in the *minchat chotei*, for which the verse explicitly prohibits placing oil or *levona* on it. Since Rav is willing to draw these analogies across different *mincha* contexts, the Gemara presses: why does Rav not apply the case of *chavitim* — where Rabbi Eliezer would permit sanctification in halves — to permit sanctification of a half-*isaron* in other *mincha* contexts as well?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא רַב מִילְּתָא מִמִּילְּתָא לָא יָלֵיף, וְהָאָמַר רַב: מִנְחָה קְדוֹשָׁה בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן (וּבְלֹא לְבוֹנָה) – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים. בְּלֹא לְבוֹנָה – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **Rav does not derive** the *halakha* of one **matter from** that of another **matter,** that is difficult: **But doesn't Rav say:** A standard **meal offering,** which is brought with oil and frankincense, **is sanctified** by a service vessel even **without** its **oil** and without its frankincense. It is sanctified without its oil, **as we find** such a *halakha* **with regard to the shewbread,** which is sacrificed without oil and is nevertheless sanctified by a service vessel. Similarly, it is sanctified even **without** its **frankincense, as we find** such a *halakha* **with regard to the meal offering** accompanying the **libations** of an offering, which is sacrificed without frankincense and is nevertheless sanctified by a service vessel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן וּבְלֹא לְבוֹנָה – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא, עַל כׇּרְחָיךְ רַב כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Finally, a standard meal offering is sanctified by a service vessel even **without** its **oil and without** its **frankincense, as we find with regard to the meal offering of a sinner,** which includes neither of these. This indicates that Rav does derive the *halakha* of a meal offering from other meal offerings. Rather, **perforce,** with regard to the griddle-cake offering, **Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** that this offering is not sanctified in halves, and it is derived from there that no meal offerings are sanctified in halves.

The Gemara's answer: *al korchacha Rav k'Rabbi Yochanan svira lei* — Rav must necessarily hold like Rabbi Yochanan, who rules that the *chavitim* must also be sanctified as a whole and may not be sanctified in halves. Since Rav applies the rule of the *chavitim* to other *mincha* offerings, and since Rav requires the full measure for those other offerings, Rav's position is only internally consistent if he agrees with Rabbi Yochanan that the *chavitim* themselves require the full measure at the time of sanctification.

The overarching theme that emerges across all these discussions is the functioning of *klei sharet* as the mechanism of *kedushah*. The questions of whether the vessel must be held, whether the minimum amount must be present, whether a half-measure can be sanctified, and whether *da'ato lehosif* makes a difference are all, at their root, questions about how *klei sharet* operate — not merely about the correct procedural form of the *avoda*. Rabbi Eliezer's position on the *chavitim* illustrates this cleanly: he agrees that the *avoda* should be performed by bringing a whole *mincha* and dividing it; his dispute is solely about whether, if that procedure was not followed, the *kli sharet* can nonetheless have effected *kedushah* on the portions presented to it. The *kedushah* of the *kli sharet* and the ritual correctness of the *avoda* are related but distinct categories, and much of the daf's analysis turns on keeping them carefully separate.
