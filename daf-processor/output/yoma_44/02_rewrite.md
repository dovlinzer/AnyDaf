# Yoma 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita & Blood

### Shechita

The *mishna* that opens the daf picks up in the middle of 43b, returning to the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur*. The *Kohen Gadol* has completed his second *viduy* — the first having been for himself and his household alone, the second extending to all the *kohanim* — in fulfillment of the principle *yavo zakai veyechaper al hachayav*: he who is pure should come to bring atonement for others. As was noted, this already places extraordinary weight on the *viduy* itself, identifying him as *zakai* even before any *korban* has been brought.

With the *viduy* concluded, the *mishna* proceeds: *shachato* — he performed the *shechita* of the cow. *Kibeyl bamazrak et damo* — he himself received the blood in the *mizrak*. Every act of the *avodah* is performed by the *Kohen Gadol* personally.

### Blood Stirring

There is, however, one task he is not required to perform: continuously stirring the blood to prevent it from congealing. This is not itself an *avodah*, but it is a practical necessity — under normal circumstances, blood would be dashed immediately upon the altar, but here the *Kohen Gadol* must first bring the *ketores* into the *Kodesh Hakodashim* before attending to the blood. The *mishna* therefore states that he hands the blood to another, *lemishehu memareis vo*, who stirs it *al harovat harevi'i shebeheichal* — on the fourth step of the *Heichal* — so that it should not congeal. The precise location of this step will require discussion.

### Fire Pan Prep

Next, *nato machta* — he took a fire pan — *ve'ala lerosh hamizbeach*, ascended to the top of the outer altar, and pushed the coals to either side, digging out from the fully consumed inner embers. This coal will be used for a separate fire pan dedicated to the *ketores* of *Yom Kippur*. Indeed, as will emerge, a distinct wood pile was created specifically for this purpose — a special fire on top of the great outer altar, separate from the fires already burning there, whose sole function was to generate coals for the incense that would be brought into the *Kodesh Hakodashim*.

## Fire & Vessels

### Special Fire

The *mishna* now elaborates at length on the contrasts between the normal daily service and what is done on *Yom Kippur*, in a sustained back-and-forth that constantly pulls the reader between the ordinary and the extraordinary. The first such contrast concerns the fire pile itself. He placed the gathered coals *al harovat harevi'i sheba'azarah* — on the fourth step in the *Azarah* — which is presumably the same set of steps viewed from the other direction: one being the fourth step descending from the *Heichal*, and the other the fourth step ascending from the *Azarah*.

### Silver/Gold Pans

*Bechol yom hayah choteh beshel kesef umeareiv betoch shel zahav* — every ordinary day, a *kohen* would shovel out the coals using a silver fire pan and then transfer them into a gold one, bringing the gold pan into the *Heichal* to place upon the golden altar. The silver pan is used for the initial digging because shoveling burning coals with gold is damaging to the metal; silver is more durable. Both, of course, are *klei shareit*.

*Uvayom hazeh choteh beshel zahav uvah hayah machnis* — but today, he would not perform two transfers at all. He would dig out the coals directly with a gold fire pan and carry that same pan straight into the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. The *Gemara* will explain that the elimination of the double transfer is a matter of *chulsha d'Kohen Gadol* — sparing the *Kohen Gadol* unnecessary exertion — since the subsequent *avodah* of placing the incense from vessel to hands while holding the fire pan is already extraordinarily demanding.

The *Rashash*, however, suggests a different reading: that this is simply the *pshat* of the *psukim*. The Torah states *v'lakach mlo hamachta gachalei esh me'al hamizbeach... u'mlo chofnav ktoret samim daka v'hevi mibeit laparochet* — the very fire pan from which the coals are taken is the one brought inside. The *Rashash* further argues that *hayom* — "today" — when contrasted with "every day" throughout this *mishna*, refers not merely to the special *Yom Kippur ketores* of the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, but to all the *ketorot* performed that day, including those of the morning and afternoon. *Rashi*, however, maintains that the contrast applies only to the special inner *ketores* of *Yom Kippur*, and the subsequent discussion follows *Rashi*'s assumption.

### Coal Measures

*Bechol yom choteh b'shel arba'ah kavin umeareiv betoch shlosha kabin* — every ordinary day he would start with a pan that held four *kavin* and pour into one that held three. The reason is practical: when transferring between vessels, some spillage is inevitable, so you begin with more than the target amount. *Vehayom choteh bishlosha kabin uvahen hayah machnis* — but today, since he uses only one pan throughout, he digs out three *kavin* and that same pan is carried inside.

*Rabbi Yose* differs on the normal-day quantities: *bechol yom choteh b'shel se'ah* — ordinarily he would begin with a full *se'ah*, which is six *kavin*, and pour into three. Today, by contrast, three *kavin* are both dug and carried, the same bottom line as the first opinion.

### Pan Weight

*Bechol yom hayta kveda* — every ordinary day the fire pan was heavy. *Vehayom kala* — today it was lighter, made of thinner gold. This is not merely a gesture toward the *Kohen Gadol*'s comfort in general; it is a necessity given the extreme physical demands of the *avodah* that follows. He must carry both the fire pan and a vessel of incense, and then transfer the incense from the vessel into both of his hands while still holding the fire pan — manipulating the pan with his arms and elbows. Every reduction in weight and every increase in maneuverability matters enormously.

### Pan Color

Accompanying the difference in weight is a difference in the handle: *bechol yom hayta yadah ketzarah* — ordinarily the handle was short; *vehayom aruchah* — today it was long. A longer handle allows for better leverage, makes the pan easier to carry at the elbow, and facilitates the difficult manipulation of transferring the incense from vessel to hands.

*Bechol yom haya zehava yarok* — on ordinary days the gold of the pan was *yarok*. It should be noted that *yarok* in *mishnaic* usage encompasses both green and yellow, as *Tosafot* observes elsewhere from the *mishna* concerning an *etrog* that requires the specification *yarok ke-karti* — green like a leek — which would be unnecessary if *yarok* unambiguously meant green. Here, *yarok* likely indicates a yellowish-green tone. *Vehayom adom* — today it was red gold, a richer and more distinguished hue. This is attributed to *Rabbi Menachem*.

### Incense Quality

*Bechol yom makriv pras b'shacharit u'fras bein ha'arbayim* — every day a half-*maneh* of incense was burned on the golden altar in the morning and a half-*maneh* in the afternoon. *Vehayom mosif melo chofnav* — and today an additional *melo chofnav*, a double handful, was burned in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. *Bechol yom hayta daka* — ordinarily the incense was ground very finely. *Vehayom daka min ha-daka* — today it was the finest of fine. On this point, virtually everyone agrees that the contrast refers specifically to the special *Yom Kippur* incense.

### Kohen Movement

*Bechol yom kohanim olim b'mizracho shel kevesh v'yordim b'maaravo* — ordinarily, *kohanim* would ascend on the eastern side of the ramp and descend on the western side. This is because of the general principle of always turning to one's right: walking forward, your right is to your east, and continuously facing the center while circling the altar produces a counter-clockwise movement. This is the basis for many analogous practices in Jewish life.

*Vehayom Kohen Gadol oleh b'emtza v'yored b'emtza* — but today the *Kohen Gadol* would go up and down through the middle. Since the entire *Beit HaMikdash* is cleared of other *kohanim* during his *avodah*, there is no practical need to stay to one side; and beyond the practical, this expresses the *kavod* of the *Kohen Gadol* — as *Rashi* puts it, he is like a *ben bayit*, at home, free to walk wherever he wishes.

It is worth noting a variant reading found in the *Yerushalmi* and the *Rambam*: *vehayom olim b'emtza v'yordim b'emtza*, in the plural. This is understood to mean that a retinue of *kohanim* would precede the *Kohen Gadol* up the altar — not performing any *avodah*, but serving as his honor guard. This presents an entirely different picture: rather than an empty *Beit HaMikdash* with a solitary figure ascending, one imagines ranks of *kohanim* marching before him, which would itself be a powerful expression of *kevod Kohen Gadol*.

*Rashi* also mentions, though its source is not immediately evident in the *Gemara*, that the *Kohen Gadol* could ascend the altar even *shelo le-tzorech* — even when not strictly required to do so.

*Rabbi Yehuda* disagrees with the premise: *l'olam Kohen Gadol oleh b'emtza v'yored b'emtza* — at all times, not only on *Yom Kippur*, the *Kohen Gadol* may ascend in the middle. For him, *Yom Kippur* does not create this privilege; the *Kohen Gadol* always enjoys it.

### Washing

*Bechol yom Kohen Gadol mekadesh yadav v'raglav min hakiyor* — every day he would sanctify his hands and feet at the *kiyor*, the communal laver. *Vehayom min hakiton shel zahav* — but today, since he undergoes multiple immersions and would wash his hands and feet adjacent to each *tevillah*, a special golden vessel filled with water was brought to him at the site of the *mikveh*. He would not need to descend to the communal *kiyor*.

*Rabbi Yehuda* again applies this distinction universally: the *Kohen Gadol* always washes from his own golden vessel, not the shared *kiyor* — an executive privilege, as it were, befitting his station.

### Fire Piles

*Bechol yom hayu sham arba ma'arachot* — ordinarily there were four wood piles with fire on the top of the outer altar. *Vehayom chamesh* — and today five. The fifth is the special pile created exclusively to produce the coals for the *ketores* of *Yom Kippur*. This is the view of *Rabbi Meir*. *Rabbi Yose* holds that ordinarily there were three, and today four. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that ordinarily there were two, and today three. All opinions agree, however, that one additional pile was added specifically for today's coal-gathering, making the total one more than usual.

## No One Present

### Stirrer Problem

Having completed the *mishna*'s survey of contrasts between ordinary days and *Yom Kippur*, the *Gemara* turns to a difficulty embedded in the description of the blood-stirrer. He is positioned on the fourth step of the *Heichal* — yet the verse states *ve-chol adam lo yihyeh b'Ohel Mo'ed* — no one may be present in the *Ohel Mo'ed* when the *Kohen Gadol* enters *lechaper bakodesh*, to perform the *kapparah* in the sanctuary.

*Amar Rabbi Yehuda tani shel heichal* — the answer is that these are not steps within the *Heichal* itself, but steps *leading to* the *Heichal*. The matter is clarified when one considers that no actual steps inside the *Heichal* are known to have existed. The *Heichal* and the *Ulam* are treated as a single unit; the steps in question are those ascending from the *Azarah* toward the *Ulam*-*Heichal* complex. The blood-stirrer stands outside this unit entirely, on the fourth step going down — which corresponds to the *rovat revi'i sheba'azarah* mentioned in the *mishna* as the step of the *Azarah*, the same steps viewed from below.

### No One in Ohel

A *baraita* then explores the scope of the prohibition systematically. The verse *ve-chol adam lo yihyeh b'Ohel Mo'ed* — one might have thought this applied even to the *Azarah* at large. The verse therefore says *be-Ohel Mo'ed*, limiting the prohibition to the *Ohel Mo'ed* proper. One might then have thought it applies only to the *Ohel Mo'ed* of the desert generation; the verse adds *bakodesh* — *b'vo'o lechaper bakodesh* — extending it to the sanctified space of future generations, including *Shilo* and the *Beit Olam*.

*Tosafot* notes a broader question: why do some laws require an explicit verse to include future *batei mikdash* while others are assumed automatically to apply? Among *Tosafot*'s answers is that *tumah*-related laws may differ: since the *Ohel Mo'ed* was portable, its actual tent structure could become *tamei*, while the *Mikdash*'s sanctity inheres in the *makom* itself — even after the structure is gone. These distinctions may be why *tumah*-related derivations sometimes require explicit inclusion.

### Blood Offerings

The *baraita* continues: from the phrase *be-sha'at haktoret* one might have learned only that the prohibition applies during the burning of the incense. How do we know it applies equally during *matan damim* — during the sprinkling of the blood of the *par* and the *sa'ir* on the golden altar and in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*? *Talmud lomar b'vo'o lechaper* — the verse says *b'vo'o lechaper*: all acts of *kapparah* performed in the sanctified space are included.

One might then have thought the prohibition ends once the *Kohen Gadol* enters. The verse adds *ad tzeito* — until he leaves. The *baraita* then delineates the order of precedence: his own *kapparah* precedes his household's, his household's precedes that of his *kohen* brethren, and the *kohanim*'s precedes that of the entire community — corresponding respectively to the two *viduyim* on the *par* and the *viduy* on the *sa'ir*. The *Gra* notes that this formulation is slightly problematic, since the *Kohen Gadol* never actually separates his personal *kapparah* from that of his household — his first *viduy* already reads *ani u'veiti*. The *Gra* accordingly emends the text to read simply him-and-his-household before the *kohanim*.

The *Gemara* then asks: why would one have initially assumed the verse refers only to the burning of the *ketores*? The phrase *lechaper bakodesh* seems more naturally associated with blood than with incense. *Rava*, and similarly *Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi* and *Rav Elazar*, answer from the continuation of the verse: *ve-chipper ba'ado u've'ad beito u've'ad kol kehal Yisrael*. What single act of *kapparah* on *Yom Kippur* covers all of these simultaneously — him, his household, the *kohanim*, and all of Israel? Only the burning of the *ketores* in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, which is performed once and for all. The blood offerings are staggered — the *par* for himself and the *kohanim*, the *sa'ir* for all Israel — but the *ketores* is brought once, encompassing everyone equally.

## Ketores Atonement

### Ketores Mekapper

This leads to a pointed question: *ve-ketoret mechaperet?* — since when is the *ketores* understood to be a vehicle of *kapparah*? We know blood effects atonement; blood is explicitly connected to *kapparah* throughout the Torah. But the *ketores*? Other rituals performed in the *Mikdash* — the lighting of the *Menorah*, the *Lechem Hapanim* — are not associated with *kapparah*. The act of burning the *ketores*, conceptually, seems to belong to an altogether different register: it is about the *encounter* with God, about the conditions that allow the *Kohen Gadol* to enter the *Kodesh Hakodashim* in safety. The entire opening of the *parasha* — *bizot yavo Aharon el hakodesh*, *ki be'anan era'eh al ha-kaporet* — frames the *ketores* not as atonement but as the modality of divine encounter. This is why the question is genuinely sharp.

The *Gemara* answers: *in, detani Rabbi Chananya* — yes, we learn that *ketores* atones. The proof is from the episode following the rebellion of *Korach*, where *Aharon* runs into the midst of a plague and *vayiten et ha-ketores vaykhaper al ha-am* — he placed the *ketores* and it atoned for the people. The *ketores* demonstrably has the power to effect *kapparah*. While this observation is correct, it should not be taken to reduce the entire role of the *Yom Kippur ketores* to this one function; the *pshat* of the *psukim* clearly invests it with a much richer significance. The *kapparah* function is real but not exhaustive.

### Lashon Hara

*Tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* specifies what the *ketores* atones for: *lashon hara*. The connection to the *Korach* episode is evident — the sin there was the misuse of speech, the inciting of people through rhetoric, the pernicious power of language deployed destructively. *Lashon hara* in *Chazal*'s usage is broader than mere gossip; it encompasses any wicked and harmful deployment of speech.

Furthermore, *yavo davar she-bachashai* — something that is conducted in private, in concealment. The *ketores* is burned in the covered space of the *Heichal*, within the innermost sanctum, away from public view. *Ve-yechaper al ma'aseh chashai* — let it atone for things done in private, for slander whispered away from the public eye. One might observe that while this connection between *lashon hara* and the *ketores* is deeply instructive as *mussar*, the *pshat* of *Yom Kippur* invests the *ketores* with something far more profound.

## Ulam/Mizbeach

### Separation Rule

The *Gemara* now cites a *mishna* from tractate *Keilim* that discusses different levels of *kedusha*: *porshin mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach* — at the time of the burning of the *ketores*, people would separate not only from the *Heichal* and *Ulam* (which are already covered by the prohibition of *ve-chol adam lo yihyeh*) but from the entire space between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach* as well. This space *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach* has its own elevated level of *kedusha* within the *Azarah*, and that status expresses itself through this requirement of separation. The *Gemara* will ultimately conclude that this requirement is rabbinic in character — though, as will be seen, the matter is somewhat nuanced.

*Tosafot* raises the obvious question: if no one can be in this space during the *ketores*, what of the man stirring the blood, who is standing on one of those very steps? The answer is that the rabbinic prohibition applies to those present without purpose; someone actively engaged in a necessary task is not in violation.

### Rav Elazar

*Amar Rav Elazar*: the statement of the *mishna* — that people must vacate the space *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach* at the time of the *ketores* — does not refer to the *ketores* of *Yom Kippur* burned in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. *Lo shanu ela besha'at haktarat heichal* — it refers specifically to the daily *ketores* burned on the golden altar inside the *Heichal*. *Aval besha'at haktarah di-lifnai vela-lifnim* — but when the *ketores* is burned in the innermost sanctum on *Yom Kippur*, *meheichal parshi* — people must vacate the *Heichal* and *Ulam* — *mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach lo parshi* — but they need not vacate the outer space between the *Ulam* and the *mizbe'ach*.

This is a striking and counterintuitive ruling. One would naturally expect that the most significant *avodah* of the year — the *ketores* burned in the *Kodesh Hakodashim* on *Yom Kippur* — would require the greatest degree of separation, extending outward as far as possible. Yet *Rav Elazar* argues the reverse: the closer the burning is to the *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach*, the more it triggers the requirement to vacate that space. When the burning occurs right there in the *Heichal*, the proximity creates an obligation to clear even the adjacent outer area. When the burning occurs deep within the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — a distinct and more remote zone — it does not project outward to compel evacuation of the space near the *mizbe'ach*. The logic is one of proximity: the outer zone must be cleared when the sacred act is nearby; when the act is in a different, more inward domain, that zone is not affected.

The implication is also that on a daily basis — not only *Yom Kippur* — whenever the morning or afternoon *ketores* is burned on the golden altar, everyone must vacate the space *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach*. Twice every day, this separation is required.

### Rabbi Yose

A *baraita* is then cited from *Rabbi Yose*: *keshem she-porshin mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach besha'at haktarah, kach porshin besha'at matan dam par kohen mashiach u-par helem davar shel tzibbur u-se'irei avodah zarah*. Just as one separates from this space during the burning of the *ketores*, so too does one separate when the blood of the *par kohen mashiach* — the special *korban* brought when the *Kohen Gadol* himself sins — or the *par helem davar shel tzibbur* — the communal sin-offering when the entire community acts in error — or the *se'irei avodah zarah* — the goats brought when a *beit din* rules erroneously on matters of *avodah zarah* — is brought into the inner sanctum.

The conceptual principle underlying this extension is *lechaper bakodesh*: any act of *kapparah* performed within the *kodesh*, whether on *Yom Kippur* or at another time of year, triggers the same requirement of separation. The daily *ketores*, according to *Rav Elazar*, also qualifies because it too is an act of *kapparah* — specifically, as the *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* taught, for *lashon hara*. Any *kapparah* performed in the *Heichal* obligates evacuation not only of the *Heichal* itself but of the outer space as well.

## Kedusha Levels

### Heichal Status

Having established this picture, the *baraita* continues with a question of its own: *u-mah ma'ala yesh bein ha-heichal le-vein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach?* — if one must vacate *both* the *Heichal* and the outer space during these acts of *kapparah*, in what respect is the *Heichal* on a higher level of *kedusha* than the space *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach*? If both must be vacated at the same moments, what distinguishes them?

### Two Contexts

The *baraita* answers: *ela shebeheichal porshin bein besha'at haktarah bein shelo besha'at haktarah* — because from the *Heichal* one must separate whether it is the time of the *ketores* or not. *U-mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach ein porshin ela besha'at haktarah* — but the outer space must be vacated only during the *ketores*. The *Heichal* must be cleared even at times that are not the burning of the *ketores* — that is, when blood offerings are being brought inside during these other occasions of *kapparah* (the sprinklings of the *par* and *sa'ir* on *Yom Kippur*, and the blood of the special *korbanot* enumerated above). The outer space, by contrast, need only be cleared during the actual burning of incense.

This is the practical expression of the *Heichal*'s higher status: it must be vacated more broadly, including at moments when the outer zone is not affected.

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* then challenges *Rav Elazar*'s position. If *Rav Elazar* is correct that the *mishna*'s reference to *ketores* means specifically the daily *ketores* of the *Heichal* — and not the *Yom Kippur ketores* of the *Kodesh Hakodashim* — then the *baraita*'s statement of the *Heichal*'s superior status is incomplete. According to *Rav Elazar*, there is an additional distinction: you must vacate the *Heichal* when the *ketores* is burned in the *Kodesh Hakodashim* (on *Yom Kippur*), but you do *not* need to vacate the outer space at that time. Why doesn't the *baraita* cite *this* as the most telling difference between the two zones?

*Besha'at haktoret miha parshi* — even *Rav Elazar* agrees that the outer space must be vacated during the *ketores*. *Mai lav besha'at haktoret di-lifnai vela-lifnim?* — could it not be that this refers to the *Yom Kippur ketores* of the *Kodesh Hakodashim*? If so, the *baraita* should have said: the superiority of the *Heichal* lies in the fact that you must vacate the *Heichal* even when the *ketores* is being burned in the innermost sanctum, while the outer space need not be vacated at that time.

### Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves this: *lo, besha'at haktarah deheichal* — *Rav Elazar* insists that when the *baraita* says *besha'at haktarah*, it means the *ketores* of the *Heichal*. That is indeed the reference point throughout. The *Gemara* then asks: *ei hachi, ha-ma'ala bekulo?* — if so, why doesn't the *baraita* articulate the *full* superiority of the *Heichal*? Specifically, the *Gemara* formulates the missing distinction: *de'ilu meheichal parshi bein besha'at haktarah dideih bein besha'at haktarah di-lifnai vela-lifnim* — you vacate the *Heichal* whether you are burning the *ketores* there or burning it in the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. But *mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach lo parshi ela besha'at haktarah deheichal* — from the outer zone you must vacate only when the *ketores* is burning in the *Heichal* proper.

The *Gemara* answers: *hachi katani* — this is precisely what the *baraita* is saying. When it states that you vacate the *Heichal* both *besha'at haktarah* and *shelo besha'at haktarah*, the word *haktarah* is being used consistently to refer to the daily burning on the golden altar. On this reading, *besha'at haktarah* means: during the burning of the *ketores* on the golden *mizbe'ach*; *shelo besha'at haktarah* means: at some other time — namely, when the *ketores* is burned inside the *Kodesh Hakodashim* on *Yom Kippur*, or when blood is being sprinkled. The *Heichal* must be vacated during both. But the outer zone need only be cleared during the first — the *ketores* of the *Heichal*. So the *baraita* does capture exactly the distinction *Rav Elazar* requires; it simply does so implicitly, by using *haktarah* in the narrow sense throughout.

The *Gemara* adds a further nuance: *ve-ika hai ma'ala* — there is yet another dimension of the *Heichal*'s superior status that could be articulated, namely a conceptual one: *de'ilu meheichal parshi bein bi-kdushah dideih bein bi-kdushah di-lifnai vela-lifnim* — you vacate the *Heichal* whether the sanctifying act is its own *avodah* or the *avodah* of the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. *Ve'ilu mibein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach lo parshi ela bi-kdushah deheichal* — the outer zone must be cleared only when the sanctifying act belongs to the *Heichal*'s own domain.

### Rava's Argument

*Amar Rava*: there is a proof from all this that the *kedushah* of the *Ulam* and the *Heichal* are *chada milta* — a single level. *De'isalkadachta shetei kedushot ninhu* — if one were to say they are two distinct grades of holiness, then the requirement to vacate the *Ulam* on *Yom Kippur* (which the *Gemara* earlier treated as falling under the *de'oraita* prohibition of *ve-chol adam lo yihyeh*) would be rabbinic. And if vacating the *Ulam* is rabbinic, then the requirement to vacate the space *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach* would be a rabbinic decree resting upon another rabbinic decree — *gezeirah li-gezeirah* — which is impermissible. Therefore, the *Ulam* and the *Heichal* must share one unitary level of *kedushah*, both governed by the *de'oraita* prohibition.

### One Space

The *Gemara* responds with a different structural resolution: *lo, Ulam u-vein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach chada kedushah hi; Heichal ve-Ulam shetei kedushot* — the conceptual unity is not between the *Ulam* and the *Heichal*, but between the *Ulam* and the outer space beyond it. The *Heichal* and the *Ulam* are, in fact, two distinct levels of *kedushah*. The outer space and the *Ulam* share one level — and therefore the requirement to vacate both is based on a single *halacha*, not a compound of two separate rabbinic enactments.

The structural picture, then, is as follows: the *Heichal* proper and the *Ulam* are physically one complex — one roofed, enclosed space leading into the next. The outer zone, partly open, shares the *Ulam*'s grade of *kedushah* by virtue of its proximity and the absence of any intervening barrier. *Kivan d'lo mafsik midi* — there is nothing separating the outer space from the *Ulam*; the transition is invisible, making it easy to inadvertently step in. The *mizbe'ach ha-chitzon*, by contrast, serves as a natural physical and conceptual divider between that zone and the rest of the *Azarah*, making the boundary obvious. This is why the rabbinic requirement stops at *bein ha-ulam vela-mizbe'ach* and does not extend to the entire *Azarah*: the *mizbe'ach* itself is *mafsik*, and the danger of accidental entry into the *Ulam* does not threaten one standing at a safe distance behind it.
