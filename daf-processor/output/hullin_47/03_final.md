# Hullin 47 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Sirchos & Lobe Connection

### Definition of Sirchos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי אוּנֵּי דִּסְרִיכָן לַהֲדָדֵי, לֵית לְהוּ בְּדִיקָה. וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא כְּסִדְרָן, אֲבָל כְּסִדְרָן – הַיְינוּ רְבִיתַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: These two lobes** of the lung **that adhere to one another** by thin strands **have no** need for **inspection,** since it is certain that these adhesions arose due to a perforation of the lung, rendering the animal a *tereifa*. The Gemara adds: **And we said** this *halakha* **only** with regard to adhesions **that are out of order,** where a lobe adhered to a non-adjacent lobe. **But** with regard to adhesions that are **in order, that is their** normal manner of **growth,** and the animal is kosher.

Today's daf is 47. We pick up at the very bottom of 46b, with the words *amar Rava*, three lines from the bottom, where the Gemara finally turns to the issue of *sirchos* — adhesions. The *unot* (also *onot*) are the lobes of the lung: smaller sacs connected to the major lung structure. That major sac is called the *uma* (from the same root as *em*, meaning mother or womb) — the principal lung body to which the lobes are attached. For now, the discussion concerns these smaller lobes.

*Amar Rava hanei tarti unai desrichan lehadadi* — if two lobes are connected to one another by a film or membrane that has grown between them, that membrane is a *sircha*. The Gemara is not always fully explicit about why *sirchos* are a problem, but the convergence of evidence across multiple passages points to one central concern: a hole formed in the lung, and the *sircha* grew to cover it. As we have seen in earlier discussions about other organs, a membrane that comes to cover a hole after the fact is halachically ineffective — the animal remains a *treifah*, since we assume the covering will eventually tear. The presence of a *sircha*, therefore, raises the suspicion that a hole exists beneath it, and if a hole did exist, the animal is a *treifah*.

### Leit Lehu Bdika

*Leis lehu bdika* — you cannot check these adhesions, meaning that even if one attempted to verify whether a hole actually exists, such verification would not suffice to permit the animal. This is notable because the Gemara elsewhere does allow checking in certain contexts — blowing up the lung, placing it in water to see if bubbles form, and so on. The reason checking fails here, as will become clearer, is that the entire problem is predicated on the assumption that a hole already exists beneath the *sircha*. The *sircha* has covered it, but that covering does not cure the *treifah*. Any checking procedure tests only the current state, not the pre-existing condition the *sircha* is concealing.

### Kesidra vs. Shelo Kesidra

The Gemara immediately qualifies: *velo amran ela shelo kesidra* — this ruling applies only when the lobes are connected out of order. What does out of order mean? As the Gemara will spell out shortly, there are three lobes on the right side of the lung and two on the left. Out of order would mean a lobe from the right connected to a lobe from the left, or, on the right side, lobe one connected to lobe three with lobe two not involved.

*Aval kesidra* — but if the lobes are connected in order — lobe one to lobe two, or lobe two to lobe three on the right, or the two lobes on the left connecting to each other — *hainu revuyseyhu*, that is their normal manner of growth, and there is no problem. The precise meaning of *hainu revuyseyhu* is the subject of a significant dispute between Rashi and Tosafot.

### Rashi: Healing Through Proximity

Rashi reads this Gemara as follows. In the out-of-order case, the assumption is that there was a hole, and the *sircha* grew to cover it — but because these lobes are not naturally adjacent, they will eventually pull apart, the covering will tear, and the hole will reassert itself. The animal is therefore a *treifah*.

In the in-order case, Rashi takes a more striking position: even if there was a hole, because the lobes are naturally adjacent and pressed close together, the combination of the membrane and the physical pressure of the two lobes against one another successfully and permanently closes the hole. The animal was, at some point, in a state that would have constituted *treifah* — but it healed. This invokes a principle encountered earlier in the tractate: where another organ lies directly against the site of a hole, that adjacency can constitute a genuine cure. Here, the membrane combined with the natural proximity of the lobes accomplishes the same result. The animal stops being a *treifah* once the healing is complete and permanent.

This reading raises a question about the phrase *leis lei livdika* — if the lobes are adjacent and the healing is successful, why can one not simply check? It is not entirely clear from Rashi's position, and the matter is discussed among the *rishonim*.

### Tosafot: Future vs. Present

Tosafot reads the Gemara quite differently. Tosafot notes that the Gemara here never mentions a hole at all. Tosafot therefore proposes that the problem of a *sircha* connecting out-of-order lobes is not that it covers an existing hole — rather, it is that these lobes, being non-adjacent, will naturally pull apart over time, and since they are connected by a *sircha*, that pulling apart will rip the tissue and create a hole. The animal is a *treifah* now, at the time of slaughter, because a hole is foreseen as inevitable.

This reading fits well with the phrase *leis lei livdika* — even if one checks and finds no hole at present, that is irrelevant, because the animal is already classified as a *treifah* by virtue of the inevitable hole that the adhesion will produce. Checking for a current hole cannot remedy a future one.

When the lobes are in order — naturally adjacent — the concern evaporates: they will remain pressed together and will not pull apart, so no hole will be created, and there is no basis to call the animal a *treifah*.

Tosafot does acknowledge, however, that numerous other Gemaras indicate that the concern of a *sircha* is not about a future hole but about an existing one. So while Tosafot's reading fits the local text elegantly, the broader body of evidence in the Gemara tends to support the understanding that a *sircha* indicates a present hole, which is also the assumption visible in Rashi's comments and, as we will see, in the *rishonim* who follow.

### Uma Connection Issue

A substantial case that the Gemara does not discuss at all is what happens when a lobe adheres not to another lobe but to the *uma* — the major lung body itself. This case appears in Rashi at the very bottom of 46b. Nine lines from the bottom, beginning with the words *dehi etzla*, Rashi records the following:

*Dehi etzla maaseh ba leyadi* — this case came before me. *v'shaalti et pi rabbeinu yaakov bar yakar* — I asked my teacher, and he told me both his own reasoning and the reasoning of those who would forbid. *ha'ossrin omrim harei nekev* — those who forbid begin from the premise that a *sircha* presupposes a hole. *v'lo matzinu she'hutar ela b'oni* — we have explicit Gemara permitting the case of lobes connected to each other; we have no such source permitting a lobe connected to the *uma*. Therefore, they argue, in the absence of a Gemara-based permission, the starting assumption that a hole exists governs — and the burden of proof lies with those who would permit, not those who would forbid.

Rashi's teacher, however, ruled it permissible. His reasoning appears later in that same Rashi: *lo matzinu uma baGemara* — the Gemara never discusses the *uma*, meaning that the reason given for permitting in-order lobes applies equally here. The Gemara's reason, as Rashi reads it, is *hainu revuyseyhu* — they grow naturally side by side, and because they grow together, they do not pull apart. As he puts it explicitly: *kach hen gedilot zu im zu v'ein nifrakot zu mizu* — they grow alongside one another and do not naturally separate. *hilchach lo shna oni v'lo shna uma* — it therefore makes no difference whether the *sircha* connects lobe to lobe or lobe to *uma*: the same logic applies.

This case illustrates a broader principle that recurs throughout the laws of *treifos*: the Gemara does not discuss every possible case, and when a case arises that is not explicitly addressed, the extrapolation is inevitably disputed, because the very rationale of the Gemara's ruling is often itself contested.

### Rabbeinu Tam Stringency

Rabbeinu Tam — who wrote an entire work on *treifos* and who is Rashi's grandson — not only disagrees with Rashi's permissive ruling here but employs unusually sharp language in doing so. The relevant Tosafot appears at the top of 47a, the long entry beginning with the word *yakar*. After summarizing Rashi's position, Tosafot records: *pasak l'issur* — Rabbeinu Tam ruled it forbidden. And further: *v'omer d'chol hamachil ona s'rucha b'uma, maachil trefot b'Yisrael* — whoever feeds meat from an animal whose lobe was adhered to the *uma* is feeding *treifah* meat to the Jewish people. That is quite strong language, especially directed, even implicitly, against a position his own grandfather held.

The underlying structure of the debate is clear: those who forbid maintain that once we know a *sircha* indicates a hole, the burden falls on those who permit to show an explicit Gemara-based mechanism by which that hole is remedied. Such a mechanism exists for in-order lobes — their natural adjacency and the principle of *hainu revuyseyhu* — but whether that mechanism applies equally to a lobe adhering to the *uma* depends entirely on how one understands the Gemara's rationale. And as is so often the case in these laws, the disagreement about the rationale maps directly onto a disagreement about the application.

## Multiple Blisters

### Two Blisters Indicate Hole

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי בּוּעֵי דִּסְמִיכָן לַהֲדָדֵי, לֵית לְהוּ בְּדִיקוּתָא. חֲדָא וּמִתְחַזְיָא כְּתַרְתֵּי – מַיְיתִינַן סִילְוָא וּבָזְעִינַן לַהּ, אִי שָׁפְכָן לַהֲדָדֵי – חֲדָא הִיא וּכְשֵׁרָה, וְאִי לָא – תַּרְתֵּי נִינְהוּ וּטְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says: These two cysts that are adjacent to one another** on the lung **have no** need for **inspection.** The animal is definitely a *tereifa*, since it is presumed that the cysts formed around a perforation in the lung. But if there is only **one** cyst that **looks like two,** due to a depression in the middle, **we bring a thorn and pierce it** to remove the fluid inside. **If** the fluids from either side **empty into one another,** this indicates that **it is one** cyst, **and** the animal is **kosher. And if not, they are two** separate cysts, **and** the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

*V'amar Rava hanei tarti bo'ei d'smichi l'hadadi* — Rava also says: if you have two blisters on the lung that are close to one another, *leis lehu bdikusa* — you cannot check them, and the animal is a *treifah*. Tosafot specifies that anything more than one hair's breadth separating two blisters would be considered distant; if they are within one hair's breadth of each other, that counts as close.

The reasoning, which Rashi supplies though the Gemara does not state it explicitly, is that two adjacent blisters indicate that there was some underlying irritation — and the most probable source of such irritation is the presence of a hole. A single blister might be dismissed; two blisters together constitute evidence of a hole beneath them. Just as with a *sircha*, if a hole is assumed to underlie the blisters, the blisters themselves do not fix anything: they merely cover what is already there. Hence *leis lehu bdikusa* — checking whether air currently escapes or not is beside the point. We begin from the assumption of a hole, and the blisters no more remedy that hole than a *sircha* does.

It is worth noting that across both this passage and the preceding one about *sirchos*, the Gemara never once uses the word "hole." It speaks only of *sirchos*, of *bo'os*, and of *bdika* — yet the references to checking procedures that involve blowing up the lung and looking for air movement make clear, as the *rishonim* universally understand, that holes are the underlying concern throughout.

## Lung Anatomy & Lobes

### Standard Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא: חָמֵשׁ אוּנֵּי אִית לַהּ לְרֵיאָה, אַפַּהּ כְּלַפֵּי גַּבְרָא – תְּלָתָא מִיַּמִּינָא וְתַרְתֵּי מִשְּׂמָאלָא. חַסִּיר אוֹ יַתִּיר אוֹ חֲלִיף – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rava says: The lung has five lobes [*unnei*].** When the animal hangs by its legs with **its face toward the person** checking it, he sees **three** lobes **on** his **right and two on** his **left.** If the animal is **missing** a lobe **or** has **an extra** lobe, **or** if the lobes were **switched,** with two on the right and three on the left, the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

*V'amar Rava hei uni it lei l'rei'a* — Rava states that the lung has five lobes. *arba klapei gavra* — oriented with respect to a person facing the animal. Rashi clarifies: this means the animal suspended by its legs after slaughter, facing the butcher. In that orientation, the butcher's left corresponds to the animal's left — which is counterintuitive but follows from the inversion of the hanging animal.

*Tleta min yamina, vetartei mishmala* — three lobes on the right side of the lung and two on the left. *chasar o yatir* — if a lobe is missing or if there is an extra lobe, *o chalif* — or if the distribution is reversed, with three on the left and two on the right, *treifah* — the animal is a *treifah*.

### Extra Lobes Problem

The case of an extra lobe is particularly striking. The Gemara later invokes the principle of *kol yeter kechasar dami* — an extra limb is considered equivalent to a missing one, so if a missing lobe would render the animal a *treifah*, an extra lobe does too. This principle was a significant source of bewilderment among the *rishonim*, because the empirical evidence seemed to run against it: animals with extra lobes can clearly survive.

The question ties into the broader debate about *treifah einah chayah* — the principle that a *treifah* cannot survive. One might ask how an animal can be three years old with an extra lobe if the extra lobe makes it a *treifah*, and a *treifah* cannot live. The Rashba recognizes one possible resolution: that *treifah einah chayah* means only in the large majority of cases, not in every instance — which would dissolve a great many practical difficulties. However, the Rashba rejects this approach based on the Gemara's evidence, concluding that the principle means no animal will ever survive with one of these conditions. One is then left with the empirical problem, which is real.

One might also ask whether the categories of *treifah* that derive from formal principles — like *kol yeter kechasar* — should be treated differently from *treifos* that concern injuries and holes, since the latter have a clear biological basis for the animal's death while the former are more structural or formal in character. The *rishonim* do not appear to have explicitly proposed such a distinction, but it points to the genuine tension between the legal framework and observed reality that runs throughout the laws of *treifos*.

### Mareimar Extra Lobe Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא יַתִּירְתָּא דַּאֲתַאי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּמָרִימָר, הֲוָה יָתֵיב רַב אַחָא אַבָּבָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֲמַר לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַכְשְׁרַהּ נִיהֲלִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲדַר עַיְּילַהּ קַמֵּיהּ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אֵימָא לֵיהּ לְמַאן דְּיָתֵיב אַבָּבָא, לֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא בִּיתֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a certain** lung that **had an extra** lobe **was brought before Mareimar. Rav Aḥa was sitting at the door** of Mareimar's house. When the animal's owner was leaving, Rav Aḥa **said to him: What did** Mareimar **say to you?** The man **said to him:** Mareimar **deemed it kosher.** Rav Aḥa was surprised by this, because it contradicts Rava's statement, so **he said to him: Turn around** and **bring** the animal **before him.** The owner did so. Mareimar realized why he was being asked twice, and **said to him: Go tell whomever is sitting at the door: The *halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava in** the case of an animal that has **an extra** lobe.

A practical case is recorded in the Gemara. *hahu yoseirta d'asai l'kamei d'mareimar* — an extra lobe case was brought before Mareimar for a ruling. *havi yasiv rav acha b'vava* — Rav Acha was sitting at the entrance of Mareimar's home. He saw someone entering with a *shaila* and learned what the question was. When the person emerged, Rav Acha asked: *mai amar lach* — what did Mareimar tell you? The man replied: *achshurei achshara nihalei* — he said it was kosher, he was not troubled by the extra lobe. Rav Acha responded: *hadar ul l'kamei* — go back, I think there was a misunderstanding, ask him again.

When the butcher returned to Mareimar and relayed this exchange, Mareimar said: *zil eima lei l'man d'yasiv b'vava* — tell the one sitting at the door: *lav k'hilchasa k'vosei d'Rava b'tarpa* — the ruling is not in accordance with Rava; regarding an extra lobe, the animal is permitted. The rhyme between *b'vava* and *b'tarpa* is noted by the Gemara, and may reflect deliberate editorial composition.

### Red Boat Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי – דְּקָיְימָא בְּדָרָא דְּאוּנֵּי, אֲבָל בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only **when** the extra lobe **stands in line with the** other **lobes,** on the left or right. **But** if it is **in between** the two sides, the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

*V'hani mili d'kayma b'dara d'onei* — this permissibility of an extra lobe applies only when the extra lobe appears within the row of lobes. If the three on the right and two on the left are arranged in their proper row, and the extra lobe appears as an additional one within that same row, it is not a *treifah*. *aval beini beini treifa* — but if it appears out of the row, it is a *treifah*.

### Beini Beini & Wild Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא בֵּינֵי בֵּינֵי דַּאֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי, סָבַר רַב אָשֵׁי לְמִיטְרְפַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא מָר בַּר אַוְיָא: כֹּל הָנֵי חֵיוֵי בָּרָיָיתָא הָכִי אִית לְהוּ, וְקָרוּ לַהּ טַבָּחֵי עֵינוּנִיתָא דְּוַורְדָּא. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי מִגַּוַּאי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates that **a certain** animal with an extra lobe **in between** the two sides **was brought before Rav Ashi. Rav Ashi thought to deem it a *tereifa*. Rav Huna Mar bar Avya said to him: All those animals** that graze **outside** in the fields **have** extra lobes like **this, and butchers call it the little rose lobe.** The Gemara adds: **And this statement** applies only when the extra lobe is on the **inside** face of the lung, facing the heart.

*Hahu beini beini d'asai l'kamei d'Rav Ashi* — a case of precisely this sort — an extra lobe appearing between the rows — came before Rav Ashi. *savar rav ashi l'mitrefah* — Rav Ashi was inclined to declare it a *treifah*, consistent with the rule just stated.

But Rav Huna bar Mar Avia said to him: *k'hanei chivei baraysa hachi it lehu* — all the animals that go out in the field have these, meaning this is a common occurrence in non-domesticated or field-raised animals. *v'karu lei tufchei v'ninusa d'varda* — the butchers call it a little red boat, meaning it has a recognized name among practitioners, indicating that it is a known and normal variant of anatomy.

Rashi's analysis of this point is careful. The basis for permitting it is that this is a normal feature — a naturally occurring variant. But that logic has limits. Rashi reads the "little red boat" as appearing specifically at the bottom, between the three lobes and the two lobes, in a particular location. If the extra lobe were in a different location, it would not qualify. If there were two such extra lobes, or if the lobe were large rather than small, Rashi holds those cases would remain a *treifah*, because the permission is grounded in normality: only this specific, recognized, common variant is treated as part of the normal range of anatomy.

There is also a practical question this raises: if it turns out that the majority of animals have this extra lobe, does the absence of that lobe then itself become a *chisaron* — a deficiency? And conversely, can such an extra lobe count toward the three that belong on the right side, to compensate for a lobe that is missing? Rashi rejects both extrapolations, though both are subjects of debate among the *rishonim*. Similarly, while the term *baraysa* might suggest wild versus domesticated animals, Rashi reads it as *ro'os ba'afar* — animals of the field generally — and the Gemara does not appear to place significant weight on a species distinction.

### Face of Lung Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אַגַּבַּהּ, אֲפִילּוּ כְּטַרְפָּא דְאָסָא – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if it is **on the back** of the lung, **even** if it is as small **as a myrtle leaf,** the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

*V'hani mili migavai* — all the foregoing applies when the extra lobe appears on the inside, meaning in the space between the rows of lobes. *aval agabai* — but if it appears on the face of the lung itself, on the main surface of the *uma* rather than in the area where lobes normally grow, *afilu k'tarfa d'asa treifa* — even if it is as small as the leaf of a *hadas* (myrtle), it is a *treifah*. Tosafot adds: what if it is smaller than that leaf? Perhaps it would not be a *treifah*. But any extra lobe appearing directly on the face of the major lung is a *treifah* regardless of its position within the row — the location alone determines the ruling.

## Lung Deformations & Coloration

### Chopped Wood (Ufta)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַפְרָם: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּדָמְיָא לְאוּפְתָּא – טְרֵפָה. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בַּחֲזוּתָא, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי בְּגִישְׁתָּא. אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי דִּנְפִיחָה, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי דִּפְחִיזָא, וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי דְּשִׁיעָא, דְּלֵית לַהּ חִיתּוּכָא דְאוּנֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rafram says: This lung that is similar to a chip** of wood renders the animal **a *tereifa*. There are** those **who say** that the similarity lies **in** its **appearance,** i.e., if it is pale like wood. **And there are** those **who say** that it lies **in** its **feeling,** i.e., if it is hard like wood. **There are** those **who say that it is swollen. And there are** those **who say that it is light. And there are** those **who say that it is** completely **smooth, that it has no sectioning of lobes.**

*Amar Rafram: hai reia d'damya l'ufta treifa* — if a lung looks like *ufta*, chopped wood, it is a *treifah*. The Gemara records several interpretations of what this means in practice.

*Ika d'amri* — some say it means the face of the lung has turned white, like the exposed face of chopped wood. *ika d'amri b'gishta* — others say it refers to the feel of the lung, which has become hard. *ika d'amri deneficha* — others explain it means the lung appears somehow expanded or blown up, giving it a white or stretched appearance. *ve'ika d'amri depachiza* — and others say it means hard, returning to the question of feel versus appearance.

Finally, *ve'ika d'amri deshia* — some explain *ufta* as meaning completely smooth, with no indentations: the five lobes are indistinguishable from the main lung body, *delais bei chituchei de'unei* — the lines or cuts that normally demarcate the lobes are absent. All the *rishonim* hold that the lobes must be distinguishable by some indentation, however slight; if the lung is so fused as to show no differentiation at all, it is a *treifah*.

### Blue vs. Black

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּכוּחְלָא – כְּשֵׁרָה, כִּדְיוֹתָא – טְרֵפָה, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: שָׁחוֹר אָדוֹם הוּא, אֶלָּא שֶׁלָּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava says:** If the lung assumed a dark color **like eye shadow,** the animal is **kosher.** If its color is black **like ink,** the animal is **a *tereifa*. As Rabbi Ḥanina says:** Menstrual blood that appears **black is** actually **red, except that it decayed.** Black color is therefore a sign of decay, and the lung is assumed to be defective.

*Amar Rava: kechuchla keshera* — if the lung has a bluish appearance, it is kosher. *kediyusa treifa* — if it has a black appearance, it is a *treifah*. The basis for this is a statement of Rabbi Chanina in the laws of *niddah*: *shachor adom hu ela shelaka* — black is simply red that has become altered, exposed to the air and darkened. Just as black menstrual blood is treated as red because it was red before it oxidized, so too a black lung tells us that what appears there was once red — meaning blood, meaning a wound. The presence of that wound suffices to render the animal a *treifah*.

It is worth noting that wound-based *treifah* through coloration is not explicitly enumerated in the *mishna*, which focuses on holes. The extension to wounds evidenced by color represents an application of the underlying principle to a different physical presentation.

### Red & Green Kosher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְרוּקָּה – כְּשֵׁרָה, מִדְּרַבִּי נָתָן. אֲדוּמָּה – כְּשֵׁרָה, מִדְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַכְתִּי לִכְרַכֵּי הַיָּם, בָּאתָה אִשָּׁה אַחַת לְפָנַי שֶׁמָּלָה בְּנָהּ רִאשׁוֹן וָמֵת, שֵׁנִי וָמֵת, שְׁלִישִׁי – הֱבִיאַתּוּ לְפָנַי. רְאִיתִיו שֶׁהָיָה אָדוֹם, אָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: בִּתִּי, הַמְתִּינִי לוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּבָּלַע בּוֹ דָּמוֹ. הִמְתִּינָה לוֹ וּמָלָה אוֹתוֹ וְחָיָה, וְהָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתוֹ נָתָן הַבַּבְלִי עַל שְׁמִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: If the lung was **green,** the animal is **kosher,** as can be derived **from** the episode **of Rabbi Natan.** And if it was **red,** it is **kosher,** as can be derived **from** the episode **of Rabbi Natan. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Natan says: Once I went to the cities overseas,** where **one woman came before me who circumcised her first son and he died,** and she circumcised her **second** son **and he died,** and out of concern that circumcising her **third** son might cause him to die as well, **she brought him before me. I saw that he was red,** so **I said to her: My daughter, wait for him until his blood is absorbed into him. She waited for him until** his blood was absorbed into him **and** then **circumcised him, and he survived. And they would call him Natan the Babylonian after my name.** This incident indicates that a red lung can heal.

*Yaroka keshera* — a green lung is kosher, *miderabi Nasan* — derived from a story of Rabbi Nasan. *aduma keshera* — a red lung is also kosher, again *miderabi Nasan*. This requires explanation: if black is a problem because it indicates a wound that was once red, how can red itself be kosher? The answer is that the red here refers not to the natural redness of the lung tissue but to a reddishness that indicates blood vessels have ruptured beneath the surface — like a bruise. However, unlike black, which signals a wound that will not heal, this type of redness, and the green coloration, indicate conditions that will resolve on their own. They are temporary and reversible, and therefore the animal is not a *treifah*. Once something turns black, by contrast, it indicates a wound that has already progressed beyond repair.

Both rulings — red kosher and green kosher — are supported by stories about Rabbi Nasan.

### Red = Excess Blood

*Detanya, Rabbi Nasan omer* — in a Baraita, Rabbi Nasan says: once I went to the islands of the sea, and a woman came to me who had circumcised her first son and he died, her second son and he died. She brought her third son to me. *ra'isi shehayah adom* — I saw that the child was red. I said to her: *hamtini lo ad sheyibala damo* — wait for his blood to be absorbed. There was an excess of blood pooled between the flesh and the skin, indicating a precarious physiological state in which a wound could prove fatal. She waited for his color to normalize, then performed the *bris milah*, and the child survived. They named the child after Rabbi Nasan — Nathan the Babylonian.

Rashi's reading here is that the excess blood had not yet been absorbed into the flesh — the child was in a state where any wound risked fatal hemorrhage. Once the blood normalized, the danger passed. Applied to the lung: a reddish lung indicates that excess blood has pooled there, but that condition is temporary and will resolve, and therefore the animal is kosher.

### Green = Insufficient Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוּב פַּעַם אַחַת הָלַכְתִּי לִמְדִינַת קַפּוֹטְקְיָא, בָּאתָה אִשָּׁה לְפָנַי, שֶׁמָּלָה בְּנָהּ רִאשׁוֹן וָמֵת, שֵׁנִי וָמֵת, שְׁלִישִׁי הֱבִיאַתּוּ לְפָנַי, רְאִיתִיו שֶׁהָיָה יָרוֹק, הֵצַצְתִּי בּוֹ וְלֹא הָיָה בּוֹ דַּם בְּרִית, אָמַרְתִּי לָהּ: בִּתִּי, הַמְתִּינִי לוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל בּוֹ דָּמוֹ, הִמְתִּינָה לוֹ וּמָלָה אוֹתוֹ וְחָיָה, וְהָיוּ קוֹרִין אוֹתוֹ נָתָן הַבַּבְלִי עַל שְׁמִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Natan further related: **And on another occasion I went to the state of Cappadocia,** in Asia Minor. A similar situation: two sons had died from *bris milah*, and the third was brought to him. **I saw that he was green,** i.e., pale. **I looked at him and** saw that **he did not have the blood of circumcision in him,** i.e., he had a deficiency of blood such that no blood would emerge from the circumcision. The phrase *dam bris* — blood of circumcision — is noteworthy; the concern here is not about the ritual significance of blood in *milah* but about the biological danger to the child. Without adequate blood circulation, a wound would not heal, and the child would die. **I said to her: My daughter, wait until his blood enters him. She waited for his** blood to increase **and** then **circumcised him, and he survived. And they would call his name Natan the Babylonian after my name.** This incident indicates that a green lung can heal as well.

*Veshuv pa'am achat halachti limdinat Kaputkia* — and on another occasion Rabbi Nasan went to Cappadocia, in Asia Minor. A similar situation: two sons had died from *bris milah*, and the third was brought to him. *vera'itiv shehayah yarok* — I saw that he was greenish. *hitzatzti bo velo hayah bo dam bris* — I looked closely and saw that he lacked sufficient blood flowing through him. The phrase *dam bris* — blood of circumcision — is noteworthy; the concern here is not about the ritual significance of blood in *milah* but about the biological danger to the child. Without adequate blood circulation, a wound would not heal, and the child would die. Rabbi Nasan told the mother: *himtini lo ad sheyipol bo damo* — wait until his blood gets flowing into him. She waited, the blood normalized, the child was circumcised and survived, and again the child was named Nathan the Babylonian.

The green coloration thus indicates insufficient blood circulation — a condition that is temporary and will resolve. Applied to the lung: a greenish lung signals a state of insufficient blood flow, but since this corrects itself, the animal is not a *treifah*. The contrast with black is again the point: both red and green are conditions in flux, moving toward resolution; black is the residue of a wound that has run its course — and that course leads to *treifah*.

### Liver vs. Flesh Color

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: כְּכַבְדָּא – כְּשֵׁרָה, כְּבִשְׂרָא – טְרֵפָה, וְסִימָנָיךְ: ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says:** If the lung has an appearance **like the liver,** the animal is **kosher.** If it has an appearance **like flesh,** the animal is **a *tereifa*. And your mnemonic** to remember that the latter is a *tereifa* is the verse: "You shall not eat any **flesh that is torn of animals [*tereifa*] in the field"** (Exodus 22:30).

*Amar Rav Kahana: kecheveda keshera* — if the lung resembles the liver in color, it is kosher. *kevisra treifa* — if it resembles flesh in color, with a pinkish tone, it is a *treifah*. And as a mnemonic: *uvasar basadeh treifa* — flesh is a *treifah*. If the lung takes on the color of raw meat, something has gone wrong, and the animal is a *treifah*.

### Yellow vs. Green Hues

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב סַמָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּדָמְיָא כִּכְשׁוּתָא, וּכְמוֹרִיקָא, וּכְגוֹן בֵּיעֲתָא – טְרֵפָה. אֶלָּא יְרוּקָּה דִּכְשֵׁרָה הֵיכִי דָּמְיָא? כְּכַרָּתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sama, son of Rava, says: This lung** whose appearance **resembles dodder, or saffron, or** has a yellow color **such as** that of **an egg** yolk, renders the animal **a *tereifa*.** Since all of these appearances are various shades of green, the Gemara asks: **But the green** lung **that is kosher, what is it like?** The Gemara responds: It is **like a leek.**

*Amar Rav Sama brei deRava: hai reia dedamya kechashuta* — if a lung resembles *chashuta* (some type of grain or foodstuff), *vechimrika* — or saffron, *uche'ein beita* — or an egg (presumably the yolk), *treifah*. These are all bright yellow hues, and a lung with this type of coloration is a *treifah*.

This prompts the question: we said *yaroka keshera* — green is kosher. What type of green is kosher? *kecharti* — like a leek. The Gemara's color vocabulary here reflects what appears to be a broader ancient phenomenon in which a single term, *yarok*, covers a spectrum that includes both what we would call yellow and what we would call green. The Gemara therefore distinguishes within that spectrum: the yellow end — saffron-like, egg-like — is a *treifah*; the green end — leek-like — is kosher. This is consistent with the use of *kecharsi* in the context of *esrog* in tractate *sukkah*, where the green of a leek is likewise the reference point for the green that is acceptable. Tosafot reads the kosher green as extending into a green-blue range, which would be continuous with the blue (*kechuchla*) that Rava earlier declared kosher.

## Obstructions & Testing

### Obstruction Testing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: אָטוּם בְּרֵיאָה, מַיְיתִינַן סַכִּינָא וְקָרְעִינַן לַהּ, אִי אִית בַּהּ מוּגְלָא – וַדַּאי מֵחֲמַת מוּגְלָא הוּא, וּכְשֵׁרָה, וְאִי לָא – מוֹתְבִינַן עֲלַהּ גַּדְפָּא אוֹ רוּקָּא, אִי מְבַצְבְּצָא – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִי לָא – טְרֵפָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravina says:** If there was **a sealed** area **in the lung** that does not inflate, **we bring a knife and tear it open. If there is pus in** the sealed area, then **it** was **definitely** sealed **due to the pus, and** the animal is **kosher. But if** we do **not** find pus there, **we lay a feather or saliva on** the opening and inflate the lung. **If** the saliva **bubbles** or the feather moves, the animal is **kosher,** since some air does reach the area, **and if not,** there is some defect in the lung, and the animal is **a *tereifa*.**

*Amar Ravina: atum bereiya* — if there is an obstruction inside the lung. *mayitina sakina vekarina lei* — we take a knife and cut it open. *ei it bei mugla* — if it contains pus, then *vadai machmas mugla hu* — it formed as a result of the pus, *veksheira* — and it is kosher, as this is a temporary condition that would heal. *ve'ilo* — and if there is no pus, then *motvinan lah gadva o roka* — we apply a feather or spittle. *im mevatva ksheira* — if there is some movement or flutter, that indicates the obstruction is not fully hardened, still has some airflow through it, and is therefore not a permanent serious condition, and the animal is kosher. *ve'ilo* — but if nothing moves at all, indicating the obstruction is completely hard and closed off, then *treifah*.

### Membrane Coverage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: קְרוּם שֶׁעָלָה מֵחֲמַת מַכָּה בָּרֵיאָה – אֵינוֹ קְרוּם, וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הַאי רֵיאָה דְּאָוְושָׁא, אִי יָדְעִינַן הֵיכָא אָוְושָׁא – מַנְּחִינַן עֲלַהּ גַּדְפָּא אוֹ גִילָא אוֹ רוּקָּא, אִי מְבַצְבְּצָא – טְרֵפָה, וְאִי לָא – כְּשֵׁרָה, וְאִי לָא יָדְעִינַן לַהּ – מַיְיתִינַן מְשִׁיכְלְתָא דְּמַיָּא פָּשׁוֹרֵי וּמוֹתְבִינַן לַהּ בְּגַוַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef says: A membrane that appeared due to a wound in the lung,** i.e., a scab that covered a perforation through the lung, **is not** considered a true **membrane,** since it does not last. **And Rav Yosef says:** With regard to **this lung that emits a sound** when inflated, **if we know** from **where it emits a sound, we set a feather, or straw, or saliva on** that point. **If** the saliva **bubbles** when the lung is inflated, the animal is **a *tereifa*,** since this proves that the lung is perforated through both membranes. **And if not,** the animal is **kosher. And if we do not know** from where **it** emits a sound, **we bring a basin of tepid water and set** the lung **inside it.**

*Amar Rav Yosef: krum sheala machmat maka bereiya eino krum* — a membrane that grew over a wound in the lung does not count as a membrane. This restates the principle we have been working with throughout: a membrane or skin that comes to cover a hole after the hole has already formed is halachically ineffective. The *treifah* status created by the hole is not cured by subsequent coverage. The animal remains a *treifah*.

### Whistling Lung Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּחַמִּימֵי לָא – דְּכָוְוצִי, בְּקָרִירֵי לָא – דִּמְטָרְשִׁי, אֶלָּא בָּדְקִינַן לַהּ בְּפָשׁוֹרֵי. אִי מְבַצְבְּצָא – טְרֵפָה, וְאִי לָא – כְּשֵׁרָה. תַּתָּאָה אִינְּקִיב, עִילָּאָה לָא אִינְּקִיב, וְהַאי דְּאָוְושָׁא – זִיקָא דְּבֵינֵי וּבֵינֵי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** One **cannot** place it **in hot water, as it** causes the lung to **contract,** closing the perforation. And one **cannot** place it **in cold water, as it hardens** the lung and may cause it to crack. **Rather, we check it in tepid water. If** the water **bubbles,** the animal is **a *tereifa*. And if not,** the animal is **kosher,** since it is apparent that only **the inner** membrane **is perforated** and **the outer** membrane **is not perforated, and the** fact **that it emits a sound is** due to **the air** moving in the space **between** the two membranes.

*V'amar Rav Yosef: hai reiya de'avsha* — if a lung makes a whistling sound when blown up, and *ei yadinan heicha avsha* — if you know where the sound is coming from, *manchinan ala gadva o gila o roka* — place a feather, straw, or spittle precisely at that spot. *im meva'be'a treifah* — if it flutters or bubbles, the animal is a *treifah*, because the movement indicates that air is passing through a hole all the way to the outside. *v'ei lo ksheirah* — if there is no such movement, the animal is kosher.

### Water Bubble Test

*V'ei lo, v'lo yadainan leh* — if you do not know where the sound is coming from, *maytainan shichusha d'maya pshurei* — you take a container of lukewarm water and *u'motavinan lah b'gavah* — submerge the lung in it. *b'chamimei lo* — not hot water, *d'chafti* — because hot water causes holes to contract, which would falsify the test. *u'v'kriri lo* — and not cold water, *d'matarshi* — because cold water makes the skin hard and could cause cracking where there would not naturally be a hole. *ela d'vadkinan lah b'pshurei* — only lukewarm water. *im meva'be'a treifah* — if bubbles form, the animal is a *treifah*. *v'ei lo ksheirah* — if not, kosher.

### Whistling Explanation

*V'ha ka avsha* — but then why does the lung make sound at all if it is kosher? *mishum d'tatai inkiv* — because the lower membrane has a hole in it, *ilai lo inkiv* — but the upper membrane does not. *v'ha d'avsha, zika d'veini beini hu* — the whistling sound comes from the wind that has escaped through the lower membrane and is now moving between the two membranes. It has exited the inner cavity but has not breached the outer wall. As long as air does not pass through the outer membrane, there is no hole to the outside, and the animal is kosher.

### Dissolved Flesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רֵיאָה שֶׁנִּשְׁפְּכָה כְּקִיתוֹן, כְּשֵׁרָה. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: חִסָּרוֹן מִבִּפְנִים לָא שְׁמֵיהּ חִסָּרוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** An animal with **a lung that** liquefied and can be **poured** out **like** water from **a jug** is **kosher.** The Gemara notes: **Evidently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **holds** that **a deficiency on the inside** of an organ **is not considered a deficiency.** Only a deficiency in the wall or membrane of an organ renders an animal a *tereifa*.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan: reiyah she'nishp'chah k'kiton ksheirah* — if the flesh of the lung has dissolved internally to the point of being liquid, like a pitcher of liquid, the animal is nonetheless kosher. *alma kasavar chisaron mibifnim lo sh'meih chisaron* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that an internal deficiency, however severe, does not constitute a *chisaron* in the halachic sense, as long as there is no hole breaching the outer wall.

### Rabbi Yochanan Ruling

The Gemara challenges this from a Baraita: *hareiyah she'nikvah o she'chasrah* — the lung that has a hole or that is lacking. The question is: what does "lacking" mean? *ilima mibachutz* — if it means lacking to the outside, *hainu nikvah* — that is the same as a hole. So how can lacking be a separate category from hole unless it means internal lacking? *shma minah chisaron mibifnim sh'meih chisaron* — this seems to prove that internal deficiency is itself a problem, contra Rabbi Yochanan.

## Holes vs. Deficiency

### Chisaron vs. Nikvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: ״הָרֵיאָה שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה אוֹ שֶׁחָסְרָה״. מַאי חָסְרָה? אִילֵימָא מִבַּחוּץ – הַיְינוּ נִיקְּבָה! אֶלָּא לָאו מִבִּפְנִים, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חִסָּרוֹן מִבִּפְנִים שְׁמֵיהּ חִסָּרוֹן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba raised an objection to Ulla** from the mishna, which states: **The lung that was perforated or that was missing** a piece renders the animal a *tereifa*. **What** is the case of a lung that was **missing** a piece? **If we say** that it was missing a piece **on the outside, this is** the same as if it **was perforated,** since any missing piece of the lung wall would constitute a perforation. **Rather, is it not** referring to a missing piece **on the inside?** If so, **learn from** the mishna that **a deficiency on the inside** of an organ **is considered a deficiency** to render the animal a *tereifa*.

The challenge rests on the apparent redundancy of the Baraita's formulation: if *nikvah* means a hole going through to the outside and *chasrah* means a deficiency going to the outside, they are the same thing. The Baraita must mean that *chasrah* refers to something internal — and if internal deficiency is mentioned as a ground for *treifah*, Rabbi Yochanan's ruling that internal deficiency does not count seems directly contradicted.

### Inside vs. Outside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם מִבַּחוּץ, וּדְקָא אָמְרַתְּ הַיְינוּ נִיקְּבָה – לָא צְרִיכָא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, דְּאָמַר: עַד שֶׁתִּנָּקֵב לְבֵית הַסִּמְפּוֹנוֹת. הָנֵי מִילֵּי נֶקֶב דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ חִסָּרוֹן, אֲבָל נֶקֶב דְּאִית בֵּיהּ חִסָּרוֹן – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מוֹדֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No,** the mishna **actually** is referring to a missing piece **on the outside. And** with regard to **that which you said: This is** the same as if it **was perforated,** one may respond that **no,** it is **necessary** to mention both cases to account **for** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** in the mishna, **who says:** It is not a *tereifa* **until** the lung **is perforated** through **to the bronchi.** By mentioning both cases, the mishna teaches that **this statement** of Rabbi Shimon applies only to a small **perforation that does not constitute a deficiency. But** in the case of **a perforation** so large **that** it **constitutes a deficiency, even Rabbi Shimon concedes** that it renders the animal a *tereifa* even if the perforation does not reach the bronchi.

The Gemara resolves this: *l'olam mibachutz* — both *nikvah* and *chasrah* refer to conditions that reach the outside. *u'd'ka'amart hainu nikvah* — and to the question of why they are not identical, the answer is that the distinction serves to address Rabbi Shimon's opinion.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

*Lo tsricha ela l'Rabbi Shimon, d'amar ad she'tinnakev l'veit hasimponot* — we need both terms only to speak to Rabbi Shimon, who holds that a hole in the lung is a *treifah* only if it reaches the *simponot* — the branches of the trachea where they divide within the lung. According to Rabbi Shimon, a hole that goes to the outside of the lung but does not reach the trachea branches is not a *treifah*.

### Hole with Flesh Loss

Given Rabbi Shimon's position, the Baraita now distinguishes two cases. *hanei milei nikvah d'leis bah chisaron* — Rabbi Shimon's leniency applies to a hole that simply pierces the wall without removing any tissue. *aval nikvah d'it bah chisaron* — but if the hole is accompanied by actual removal of tissue — some of the flesh of the lung is gone — *afilu Rabbi Shimon modeh* — even Rabbi Shimon agrees that the animal is a *treifah*, regardless of whether the hole reaches the trachea branches.

This resolution also preserves Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: his statement that internal dissolution is not a problem refers to a case where the outer shell remains intact. There is no hole. The question of whether internal deficiency without a hole is a *chisaron* is resolved in his favor — it is not.

### Rav Acha Question

*Amlei Rav Acha brei deRava l'Rav Ashi: minay yadin* — Rav Acha asked Rav Ashi: how does one know whether the trachea branches inside have dissolved or not? The lung's outer shell may be intact, the flesh inside may have liquefied — but how can one determine whether those internal branches remain or have dissolved along with the rest of the tissue?

### Lead Vessel Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: מַיְיתְצִינַן צָעָא דְּקוּנְיָא, וְשָׁפְכִינַן לַהּ בְּגַוֵּיהּ. אִי אִית בַּהּ שׁוּרְיָיקֵי חִיוָּרֵי – טְרֵפָה, וְאִי לָא – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi **said to him: We bring** a glazed **earthenware vessel** with no cracks, so the contents can be observed, **and we pour** the lung **into it. If there are white streaks in it,** the animal is **a *tereifa*,** as the white streaks are remains of the liquefied bronchi. **And if not,** the animal is **kosher.**

*Amar lei: mayitsinan tzo'ada d'kunya* — you take a lead-lined vessel, *v'shafchinan lah b'gavah* — and pour the liquefied contents of the lung into it. *iy basey s'riki chivrey treifa* — if white lines appear in what pours out, those white lines are the trachea branches themselves. Their presence in the liquid means they have dissolved and separated from the structure — they are no longer part of the lung's architecture. The animal is a *treifah*. *v'ilo ksheira* — if no white lines appear, the branches have not dissolved; they remain embedded in the remaining structure, and the animal is kosher.

## Softening & Cavities

### Softened Lung Cavities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: רֵיאָה שֶׁנִּימּוֹקָה וּקְרוּם שֶׁלָּהּ קַיָּים – כְּשֵׁרָה. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: רֵיאָה שֶׁנִּימּוֹקָה וּקְרוּם שֶׁלָּהּ קַיָּים, אֲפִילּוּ מַחְזֶקֶת רְבִיעִית – כְּשֵׁרָה. נִיטְּלָה
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says: If the lung** was partially **atrophied** and only part of the flesh remains, **but its membrane** still **exists,** the animal is **kosher.** The Gemara notes: **This is also taught** in a *baraita*: **If the lung was atrophied, but its membrane** still **exists, even** if the space vacated by the atrophied lung can **hold a quarter-***log* of fluid, the animal is **kosher.**

*Amar Rav Nachman: rei'ah she'nimokah v'krum shelah kayam k'sheira* — if the lung has become *nimokah* — somewhat mushy or softened, not fully liquefied — and the outer shell remains intact, it is kosher. Rashi notes that *nimokah* is a lesser condition than full dissolution: it has created some interior cavitation or softening without complete liquefaction. *tanya nami hachi* — a Baraita supports this: *rei'ah she'nimokah v'krum shelah kayam* — if it has softened and the shell remains, *afilu machazekes revi'is k'sheira* — even if the interior cavity could hold a *revi'is* (approximately three ounces), the animal is kosher, because there is no hole through the outer wall.

### Missing Womb

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִיטְּלָה שַׁלְפּוּחִית שֶׁלָּהּ – כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** If **its womb was removed,** the animal is **kosher.**

*Nitla shalpushis* — if the womb is absent, *k'sheira* — the animal is kosher. Tosafot notes that this is the first mention of the womb in this entire discussion, reflecting the broader reality that many organs are not addressed by the Mishnah or Gemara in their list of *treifos*. What happens when other organs not on the formal list have holes or are missing? Tosafot raises, for instance, the question of a hole in the urethra, and tentatively suggests it might fall under the category of *dakin* — small intestines. But as Tosafot himself acknowledges, the urethra is not the small intestine, and many such questions remain without clear resolution because the Gemara simply did not address them.

### Wormy Liver Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִתְלִיעַ כָּבֵד שֶׁלָּהּ – זֶה הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְעָלוּ עָלֶיהָ בְּנֵי עַסְיָא שְׁלֹשָׁה רְגָלִים לְיַבְנֶה, לְרֶגֶל שְׁלִישִׁי הִתִּירוּהָ לָהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** If **its liver became infested by worms,** with regard to **this** there **was an incident, and the residents of Asia** Minor **went up** on **three occasions to** the great Sanhedrin in **Yavne** to inquire with regard to the *halakha*. On the first two occasions they did not receive an answer; on **the third occasion,** after the Sanhedrin had deliberated, **they permitted** the animal **to them.**

*Hitli'ah kaveid shelah* — if the liver has become wormy. *zeh hayah maaseh, v'alu aleha bnei asya gimel regalim l'yavneh* — this was an actual case, and the people of Asya (Asia Minor) came to Yavneh over three *regalim* to seek a ruling. *l'regel shelishi hitiru lahem* — by the third *regel* they finally received a definitive answer: even a wormy liver is kosher. This may seem surprising — a wormy liver sounds like a severe pathological condition — but the analogy to the missing liver is instructive: we have already established that a liver from which tissue is missing is kosher as long as a *kezayis* remains. A worm-infested liver that still has its basic structure apparently falls within the same logic.
