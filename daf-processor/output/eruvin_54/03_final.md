# Eruvin 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bruriah & Language

### Yosi & Bruriah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הֲוָה קָא אָזֵיל בְּאוֹרְחָא, אַשְׁכְּחַהּ לִבְרוּרְיָה אֲמַר לַהּ: בְּאֵיזוֹ דֶּרֶךְ נֵלֵךְ לְלוֹד? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: גָּלִילִי שׁוֹטֶה, לֹא כָּךְ אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אַל תַּרְבֶּה שִׂיחָה עִם הָאִשָּׁה?! הָיָה לְךָ לוֹמַר: ״בְּאֵיזֶה לְלוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili was walking along the way,** and **met Berurya. He said to her: On which path shall we walk** in order to get **to Lod? She said to him: Foolish Galilean, didn't the Sages say: Do not talk much with women? You should have said** your question more succinctly: **Which** way **to Lod?**

The *aggadeta* of Eruvin 54 opens in the midst of a thematic discussion that grew out of the very first word of the *mishnah* — a discussion about language, precision, and economy of expression. The *gemara* picks up four lines from the bottom of 53b. Rabbi Yosi HaGlili was traveling on the road (*hava ka'azil be'orcha*) when he encountered Bruriah, the wife of Rabbi Meir, a figure renowned not only for her erudition but for her characteristic sharpness of mind.

He asked her: *be'eizeh derech nelech le'Lud* — "which path should we go to get to Lud?" It is worth noting, as an alert listener pointed out, that he phrased his question in the plural, *nelech*, which carried its own impropriety — implying that they might travel together. But Bruriah's rebuke focused on a different excess.

### Bruriah's Critique

*Glili shoteh* — stupid Galilean, she called him. The epithet is pointed: the tractate has already established that the Galileans were known for imprecision and carelessness with language, and Rabbi Yosi HaGlili was himself a Galilean. She then asked: did the sages not say *al tarbeh sichah im ha'ishah* — one should not speak excessively with a woman? (The statement appears in *Pirkei Avot*, Perek 1, Mishnah 5, attributed to Yosi ben Yochanan of Jerusalem, one of the *Zugot* — predating Rabbi Meir's era considerably.) You should have simply said *be'eizeh le'Lud* — "which way to Lud?" Two words instead of seven. You wasted words.

There is an obvious irony here that the *gemara* seems to leave deliberately unspoken: Bruriah is the one citing the prohibition against speaking excessively with women — and she is a woman whose intellectual stature makes her a peer of the sages. The ironic deployment of that statement by Bruriah herself feels intentional, a kind of witty self-awareness about her own anomalous position.

## Learning Aloud

### Whisper Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרוּרְיָה אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְהַהוּ תַּלְמִידָא דַּהֲוָה קָא גָרֵיס בִּלְחִישָׁה. בְּטַשָׁה בֵּיהּ, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לֹא כָּךְ כָּתוּב ״עֲרוּכָה בַכֹּל וּשְׁמוּרָה״, אִם עֲרוּכָה בִּרְמַ״ח אֵבָרִים שֶׁלְּךָ — מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת, וְאִם לָאו — אֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Berurya came across a certain student who was whispering his studies** rather than raising his voice. **She kicked him** and **said to him: Isn't it written as follows: "Ordered in all things and secure"** (ii Samuel 23:5), which indicates that **if** the Torah **is ordered in your 248 limbs,** i.e., if you exert your entire body in studying it, **it will be secure, and if not, it will not be secure.**

Bruriah then encountered a certain student (*ashkachatih le'hahu talmida*) who was learning in a whisper (*de'hava ka garis belachisha*) — quietly, murmured, as one might study in a library. She kicked him (*batsha beih*) and rebuked him: does the verse not say *arucha vakol ushmura* — "it is arranged with everything and protected"? If the Torah is *arucha be'ramach evarim shelcha* — laid out within your 248 limbs, meaning if you engage your entire body in the act of study, then *mishtameret* — it will be retained. And if not, it will not be retained.

The specific emphasis on vocalizing — saying Torah out loud — will be developed more explicitly in the *baraitot* that follow. But Bruriah's formulation is perhaps broader: what enables Torah to be retained is a kind of full-bodied engagement, where not just the mind but the entire physical self is enlisted in the process of learning. The *shoklen*, the movement, the voice — all of it is part of how Torah becomes truly internalized.

### Undertone Student

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד הָיָה לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁהָיָה שׁוֹנֶה בְּלַחַשׁ, לְאַחַר שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים שָׁכַח תַּלְמוּדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was** similarly **taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer had a student who would study quietly,** and **after three years he forgot his studies.**

A *tanna* taught: Rabbi Eliezer had a student who would learn in an undertone (*shehayah shoneh belachash*). Three years later, he had forgotten everything he had learned. This confirms Bruriah's point and connects directly to the earlier discussion about precision of language as a means of preserving Torah in memory: how one physically engages with learning is determinative of whether that learning endures.

### Deserving Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא, תַּלְמִיד אֶחָד הָיָה לוֹ לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר שֶׁנִּתְחַיֵּיב בִּשְׂרֵיפָה לַמָּקוֹם. אָמְרוּ: הַנִּיחוּ לוֹ אָדָם גָּדוֹל שִׁמֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Eliezer had a student who was liable for** the punishment of death by **burning,** for his sins **against God,** but the Rabbis **said: Let him** alone and do not punish him as he deserves, because **he served a great person.**

A second *baraita* reports that Rabbi Eliezer had another student who was considered deserving of divine death by burning (*shenitchayav bisreifah la'Makom*). It is not entirely clear from the text how this was known. The *Maharsha* suggests the connection to a teaching that one who causes himself to forget something he has learned is liable to a form of heavenly death — so this student's failing was presumably in that domain. Nevertheless, *amru* — it was said in heaven — *hinichu lo*, give him a pass: *adam gadol shimesh*, he had attended upon and served a great rabbi, Rabbi Eliezer. The principle seems to be that serving *talmidei chachamim* carries its own profound merit, sufficient to outweigh serious spiritual liability. Whether the liability was understood as death at the hands of heaven or at the hands of man, the phrasing *nitchayev srefa la'Makom* — liable to burning before God — points clearly to the former.

### Open Your Mouth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, פְּתַח פּוּמָּיךְ קְרִי, פְּתַח פּוּמָּיךְ תְּנִי, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִתְקַיַּים בָּיךְ וְתוֹרִיךְ חַיֵּי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי חַיִּים הֵם לְמֹצְאֵיהֶם וּלְכׇל בְּשָׂרוֹ מַרְפֵּא״, אַל תִּקְרֵי ״לְמֹצְאֵיהֶם״ אֶלָּא ״לְמוֹצִיאֵיהֶם בַּפֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: Keen scholar [*shinnana*], open your mouth and read** from the Torah, **open your mouth and study** the Talmud, **in order that** your studies **should endure in you and** that **you should live a long life, as it is stated: "For they are life to those who find them, and health to all their flesh"** (Proverbs 4:22). **Do not read: "To those who find them [*lemotzeihem*]," but** rather **"to those who express them [*lemotzi'eihem*]," with** their **mouth.**

Shmuel said to his student Rav Yehuda, whom he affectionately addressed as *Shinena* — "O sharp one" — *petach pumach ukrei*: open your mouth and read the verses. And again: *petach pumach tani* — open your mouth and speak the *mishnah*, so that it should be established within you (*ki heichi detitkayem bach*) and your life should be lengthened. The instruction is not metaphorical: do not learn in your head, do not mumble — speak it aloud. The benefit Shmuel promises is both mnemonic and metaphysical, as the *gemara* now elaborates.

### Words of Life

The verse cited is from Mishlei: *ki chayim hem lemotzeihem ul'chol besaro marpe* — "for they are life to those who find them and healing to all their flesh." The *gemara* applies a *al tikrei* reading: do not read *lemotzeihem*, "to those who find them," but *lemotzi'eihem bepeh* — "to those who bring them out through the mouth." When one actually speaks words of Torah, they become a source of life — retained in memory and productive of longevity. The movement here is from what Bruriah said about embodiment and retention all the way to the metaphysical dimension: articulating Torah with the mouth is itself a life-giving act.

## Enjoying Life

### Grab & Eat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, חֲטוֹף וֶאֱכוֹל חֲטוֹף וְאִישְׁתִּי, דְּעָלְמָא דְּאָזְלִינַן מִינֵּיהּ כְּהִלּוּלָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda,** his beloved student: **Keen scholar, grab and eat, grab and drink, as the world from which we are departing is like a wedding feast,** whose joy is only temporary, and one who does not take pleasure in it now will not be able to do so in the future.

Since the *gemara* has now assembled a series of things Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda, it continues with another teaching from that pair. Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: *Shinena* — *chatof ve'echol, chatof ve'ishti* — grab and eat, grab and drink, *de'alma de'azlinan minei ke-hilula dami* — for this world that we are leaving is like a wedding party. Rashi does not soften this. What it means, Rashi says, is: do not be an ascetic. Do not deny yourself pleasure. There will be no physical pleasure in the World to Come. The opportunity to enjoy this world is here and now, like a party that eventually ends. This does not mean hedonism or the unbridled pursuit of pleasure; it means one should not adopt a life of self-denial and asceticism. The echo of *Kohelet* — "eat, drink, and be merry" — is unmistakable, and here it is presented not as a philosophical blind alley but as a genuine teaching about how to live.

### Do Good

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא: בְּנִי אִם יֵשׁ לָךְ — הֵיטֵב לָךְ, שֶׁאֵין בַּשְּׁאוֹל תַּעֲנוּג, וְאֵין לַמָּוֶת הִתְמַהְמֵהַּ. וְאִם תֹּאמַר אַנִּיחַ לְבָנַי — חוֹק בַּשְּׁאוֹל מִי יַגִּיד לָךְ: בְּנֵי הָאָדָם דּוֹמִים לְעִשְׂבֵי הַשָּׂדֶה הַלָּלוּ נוֹצְצִין וְהַלָּלוּ נוֹבְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to Rav Hamnuna: My son, if you have** money, **do well for yourself.** There is no point waiting, **as there is no pleasure in the netherworld, and death does not tarry. And if you say: I will** save up in order to **leave for my children, who told you the law of the netherworld,** i.e., how do you know which of you will die first? **People are similar to grass of the field,** in that **these blossom,** i.e., grow, and their actions are blessed, **and these wither** and die.

Rav said to his student Rav Hamnuna: *beni, im yesh lecha heitiv lach* — my son, if you have the means, do good for yourself. Buy yourself nice clothing, good food, pleasant furnishings. Don't deny yourself. *She'ein bish'ol ta'anug* — there is no pleasure waiting in the depths, and *ein lamaves hitmahmeha* — death does not tarry. You may not have the opportunity later; do not defer gratification on the assumption that tomorrow will come.

And if one argues: *aniach livni choki* — I want to leave my wealth to my children — Rav responds: *bish'ol mi yagid lecha* — who will tell you in the depths whether your children made use of it? *Bnei adam domim le'isvei hasadeh* — people are like the grass of the field, *halalu notzetzim vehalalu novlim* — some sprout and some wither. Who knows whether one's children will live to benefit?

This is a notably frank teaching, and the lecturer rightly observes that it resonates with several passages in *Kohelet* — passages we typically read as Shlomo's philosophical explorations of paths eventually abandoned. Here, however, the same themes are presented as positive guidance: do not go to the extreme of total self-denial, neither for the sake of the World to Come nor for the sake of an inheritance. *Chazal* speak elsewhere of the importance of leaving something to one's children; the point here is not to abandon that value but to guard against the opposite excess of denying oneself everything present.

## Torah Medicine

### Body Cures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הַמְהַלֵּךְ בַּדֶּרֶךְ וְאֵין עִמּוֹ לְוָיָיה — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לִוְיַת חֵן הֵם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: One who is walking along the way without a companion** and is afraid **should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated** with regard to the words of Torah: **"For they shall be a graceful wreath [*livyat ḥen*]** for your head, and chains about your neck" (Proverbs 1:9). The word *livyat* is understood here as a reference to *levaya*, accompaniment, so that the verse is interpreted to mean that Torah is a graceful accompaniment to one who is traveling.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָשׁ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ — יַעֲסוֹק בְּתוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לִוְיַת חֵן הֵם לְרֹאשֶׁךָ״. חָשׁ בִּגְרוֹנוֹ — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲנָקִים לְגַרְגְּרוֹתֶיךָ״. חָשׁ בְּמֵעָיו — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״רִפְאוּת תְּהִי לְשָׁרֶּךָ״. חָשׁ בְּעַצְמוֹתָיו — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְשִׁקּוּי לְעַצְמוֹתֶיךָ״. חָשׁ בְּכׇל גּוּפוֹ — יַעֲסוֹק בַּתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְכׇל בְּשָׂרוֹ מַרְפֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **One who feels** pain **in his head should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "For they shall be a graceful wreath for your head." One who feels** pain **in his throat should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "And chains about your neck." One who feels** pain **in his intestines should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "It shall be health to your navel"** (Proverbs 3:8). **One who feels** pain **in his bones should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "And marrow to your bones"** (Proverbs 3:8). **One who feels** pain **in his entire body should engage in Torah** study, **as it is stated: "And health to all their flesh"** (Proverbs 4:22).

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught: if one is walking on the road with no companion (*ein lo levayah*), which is dangerous, one should engage in Torah study, for the verse says *ki livyat chen hem* — they are a gracious accompaniment. If one has a headache, one should study Torah — *ki livyat chen hem l'roshecha*, they are like an ornament for your head. If one has a sore throat — Torah, for *va'anakim l'gargerotecha*, like a necklace around your throat. If one has a stomachache — Torah, for *rif'ut tehi l'sharecha*, healing for your navel. If your bones are weary — Torah, for *v'shikui l'atzmotecha*, strengthening for your bones. If your whole body aches — Torah, for *ul'chol besaro marpe*, it heals all your flesh.

The *Maharsha* notes some tension between this *gemara* and the principle elsewhere stated that *ein mitrap'in b'divrei Torah* — one may not use Torah as a medical remedy. He suggests that this passage should be understood either as a metaphysical statement — Torah protects and heals through a supernatural mechanism — or as a more psychological one: when a person immerses fully in Torah study with whole-body engagement, as Bruriah described, the focus becomes so complete that headaches and bodily discomforts cease to register. The practical or metaphysical mechanism is not made explicit. What is clear is that the theme continues: Torah is meant to permeate the entire body, not to remain a merely cerebral activity.

### Human vs Divine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: בֹּא וּרְאֵה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּמִדַּת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וְדָם. מִדַּת בָּשָׂר וָדָם: אָדָם נוֹתֵן סַם לַחֲבֵירוֹ — לָזֶה יָפֶה, וְלָזֶה קָשֶׁה. אֲבָל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אֵינוֹ כֵּן: נָתַן תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל — סַם חַיִּים לְכׇל גּוּפוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּלְכׇל בְּשָׂרוֹ מַרְפֵּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, said: Come and see that the attribute of flesh and blood is unlike the attribute of the Holy One, Blessed be He. The attribute of flesh and blood** is that when **a person gives a drug to his fellow, it is good for this** part of his body **and it is harmful to that** other part of his body. **But** the attribute of **the Holy One, Blessed be He, is not so; He gave the Torah to the Jewish people,** and **it is a drug of life for one's entire body, as it is stated: "And health to all their flesh."**

Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiya elaborated: *bo ure'eh shelo k'middat haKadosh Baruch Hu middat basar vadam* — come and see that the way of God differs from the way of flesh and blood. A human physician gives a patient medicine that may help one ailment but harm another — what is good for the liver may be bad for the eye. But God gave Israel the Torah as *sam chayim l'chol gufo* — a medicine of life for the entire body, as the verse says *ul'chol besaro marpe*. It is not a remedy effective for one part and dangerous for another; it is beneficial throughout.

### Belly & Lips

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַמֵּי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כִּי נָעִים כִּי תִשְׁמְרֵם בְּבִטְנֶךָ יִכּוֹנוּ יַחְדָּיו עַל שְׂפָתֶיךָ״, אֵימָתַי דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה נְעִימִים — בִּזְמַן שֶׁתִּשְׁמְרֵם בְּבִטְנֶךָ, וְאֵימָתַי תִּשְׁמְרֵם בְּבִטְנֶךָ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁיִּכּוֹנוּ יַחְדָּו עַל שְׂפָתֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ami said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "For it is a pleasant thing if you keep them within you; let them be firmly attached together to your lips"** (Proverbs 22:18)? **When are words of Torah pleasant? When you keep them within you** and know them. **And when will you keep them within you? When they will be attached together to your lips,** i.e., when you articulate them audibly and expound them.

Rabbi Ami asked: what is the meaning of the verse *ki na'im ki tishmerem bevitcha, yikonu yachdav al sefatecha* — "for they are pleasant when you keep them in your belly; they shall be established together on your lips"? When are words of Torah pleasant? When you keep them in your belly — when you internalize them. And when do you succeed in keeping them in your belly? When they are arranged on your lips — when you speak them aloud. Again the pattern: verbalization is not the goal in itself but the means by which Torah becomes genuinely internal, truly part of one's body.

### Joy in Speaking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״שִׂמְחָה לָאִישׁ בְּמַעֲנֵה פִיו וְדָבָר בְּעִתּוֹ מַה טּוֹב״, אֵימָתַי שִׂמְחָה לָאִישׁ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁמַּעֲנֶה בְּפִיו. לָשׁוֹן אַחֵר: אֵימָתַי שִׂמְחָה לָאִישׁ בְּמַעֲנֵה פִיו — בִּזְמַן שֶׁדָּבָר בְּעִתּוֹ מַה טּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said** that this idea is derived **from here: "A man has joy in the answer of his mouth; and a word in due season, how good it is"** (Proverbs 15:23). **When does a man have joy? When an answer** related to Torah study **is in his mouth. Another version: When does a man have joy in the answer of his mouth? When** he experiences the fulfillment of: **A word in due season, how good it is,** i.e., when he knows when and how to address each issue.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״כִּי קָרוֹב אֵלֶיךָ הַדָּבָר מְאֹד בְּפִיךָ וּבִלְבָבְךָ לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ״, אֵימָתַי קָרוֹב אֵלֶיךָ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁבְּפִיךָ וּבִלְבָבְךָ לַעֲשׂוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said** that this idea is derived **from here: "But the matter is very near to you, in your mouth and in your heart, that you may do it"** (Deuteronomy 30:14). **When** is it **very near to you? When it is in your mouth and in your heart, that you may do it,** i.e., when you articulate your Torah study.

Rabbi Zeira derived a similar principle from a different verse: *simcha le'ish bema'aneh fiv, vedavar be'ito mah tov* — "a joy to a person is in the response of his lips, and a matter spoken in its time, how good is it." When does a person have joy? When there is response on his lips — when the Torah is expressed through his speech, which both internalizes it and brings genuine satisfaction. An alternative formulation (*lashon acher*) takes the first half of the verse as a unit: joy comes when something is said in its proper time. Rashi offers two readings of this: either that one's learning is appropriate to the season of the year (studying *Hilchos Pesach* on Pesach, etc.), or more broadly that the learning is somehow fitting for the moment.

Rabbi Yitzchak derived the same principle from yet another verse: *ki karov eilecha hadavar me'od, beficha uvelevavecha la'asoto* — "for the matter is very close to you, in your mouth and in your heart to do it." When is it close to you — when do you truly own it? When it is on your lips and in your heart to act upon it. The addition here goes slightly beyond verbalization: it includes the commitment to act, the *uvelevavecha la'asoto*, which is itself a dimension of how Torah becomes embodied.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר מֵהָכָא: ״תַּאֲוַת לִבּוֹ נָתַתָּה לּוֹ וַאֲרֶשֶׁת שְׂפָתָיו בַּל מָנַעְתָּ סֶּלָה״, אֵימָתַי תַּאֲוַת לִבּוֹ נָתַתָּה לּוֹ — בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֲרֶשֶׁת שְׂפָתָיו בַּל מָנַעְתָּ סֶּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that this idea is actually derived **from here: "You have given him his heart's desire, and have not withheld the request of his lips, Selah"** (Psalms 21:3). **When have You given him his heart's desire? When You have not withheld the request of his lips, Selah,** i.e., when he converses in words of Torah.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״תַּאֲוַת לִבּוֹ נָתַתָּה לּוֹ״. וּכְתִיב ״וַאֲרֶשֶׁת שְׂפָתָיו בַּל מָנַעְתָּ סֶּלָה״. זָכָה — תַּאֲוַת לִבּוֹ נָתַתָּה לוֹ. לֹא זָכָה — וַאֲרֶשֶׁת שְׂפָתָיו בַּל מָנַעְתָּ סֶּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised an** internal **contradiction** in that very verse: In the beginning of the verse **it is written: "You have given him his heart's desire,"** implying that it is enough for one to request in his heart, whereas in the end of the verse **it is written: "And You have not withheld the request of his lips, Selah,"** indicating that one must express his prayers verbally. Rava himself resolved the contradiction: If one **is fortunate, "You have given him his heart's desire,"** even if he does not give verbal expression to his wants. But if he **is not fortunate,** at least **"You have not withheld the request of his lips, Selah."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נֶצַח״ ״סֶלָה״ ״וָעֶד״ — אֵין לוֹ הֶפְסֵק עוֹלָמִית. נֶצַח, דִּכְתִיב: ״כִּי לֹא לְעוֹלָם אָרִיב וְלֹא לָנֶצַח אֶקְּצוֹף״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the end of this verse, a Sage **of the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov taught** the following *baraita*: **Wherever it states *netzaḥ***, **Selah,** or ***va'ed***, the matter **will never cease. *Netzaḥ*, as it is written: "For I will not contend forever; neither will I be eternally [*lanetzaḥ*] angry"** (Isaiah 57:16), which demonstrates that *netzaḥ* bears a similar meaning to forever.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כַּאֲשֶׁר שָׁמַעְנוּ כֵּן רָאִינוּ בְּעִיר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת בְּעִיר אֱלֹהֵינוּ אֱלֹהִים יְכוֹנְנֶהָ עַד עוֹלָם סֶלָה״. וָעֶד, דִּכְתִיב: ״ה׳ יִמְלוֹךְ לְעוֹלָם וָעֶד״.
>
> **Translation:** **Selah, as it is written: "As we have heard, so have we seen in the city of the Lord of Hosts, in the city of our God; may God establish it forever, Selah"** (Psalms 48:9), which demonstrates that Selah means forever. ***Va'ed*, as it is written: "The Lord shall reign forever and ever [*va'ed*]"** (Exodus 15:18).

Rava raised a contradiction within a single verse: *ta'avat libo natata lo, va'areshet sefatav bal manata selah* — "You have given him the desire of his heart, and the expression of his lips You have not withheld, forever." The first half implies that it is sufficient to desire something internally — God gives it. The second half implies that one must express it through the lips. These appear to conflict.

Rava's resolution: *zacha* — if one merits it, then *ta'avat libo natata lo* — the desire of his heart is given even without verbal expression. *Lo zacha* — if one does not merit it, then one must reach *va'areshet sefatav bal manata selah* — one must actually verbalize and exert effort to receive what is desired. Rashi, notably, does not read this in the context of *tefilla* but in purely this-worldly terms: some fortunate people have good things fall into their lap without effort, while others must labor strenuously for what they seek. Why one person merits the former and another the latter is left unexplained — that is simply how it is.

The *gemara* here adds a teaching from the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov: wherever the Torah uses the words *netzach*, *selah*, or *va'ed*, it connotes absolute permanence — *ein lo hefsek olami*, no cessation whatsoever. This is demonstrated through verses where each of these words appears in parallel with *olam*, confirming that all three carry the meaning of "forever."

## Student Virtues

### Necklace

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (סִימָן: ״עֲנָקִים״ ״לְחָיָיו״ ״לוּחוֹת״ ״חָרוּת״). אָמַר רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר): מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וַעֲנָקִים לְגַרְגְּרוֹתֶיךָ״, אִם מֵשִׂים אָדָם עַצְמוֹ כַּעֲנָק זֶה שֶׁרָף עַל הַצַּוָּאר וְנִרְאֶה וְאֵינוֹ נִרְאֶה — תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ. וְאִם לָאו — אֵין תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In light of the previous discussion, the Gemara cites several expositions of verses proposed by Rabbi Eliezer, while first providing them with a **mnemonic: Chains, cheeks, tablets, engraved. Rabbi Eliezer said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "And chains about your neck"** (Proverbs 1:9)? **If a person makes himself like a chain that hangs loosely on the neck,** i.e., if a scholar is not pushy and disruptive to others, **and** he is also **seen but not seen,** i.e., just as a chain is covered by clothes and hair, so too, the scholar does not let himself be seen, **his** Torah **study will endure. But if not,** if he acts in a rude and arrogant manner, **his** Torah **study will not endure.**

Rav Elazar asked: what is the meaning of the verse *va'anakim legargrotacha* — "and a necklace around your throat"? If a person makes himself like a necklace — *sharaf al hatzavar*, loose around the neck rather than a choker — and *venireh ve'eino nireh*, sometimes visible and sometimes not, then *talmudo mitkayem beyado* — his Torah learning will be established in his hand. And if not, it will not.

What does it mean to be visible and not visible? Rashi offers an interesting reading: a man wearing a necklace may have his beard cover it, so it is not always prominently on display. Applied to the Torah scholar, the point seems to be twofold: be easy and undemanding in your interactions with others, not choking those around you, and spend much of your time sequestered in the *beit midrash* rather than constantly out in public. The person who is relaxed and not always demanding to be seen is the one whose Torah will endure.

### Flowerbed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לְחָיָו כַּעֲרוּגַת הַבּוֹשֶׂם״, אִם מֵשִׂים אָדָם עַצְמוֹ כַּעֲרוּגָה זוֹ שֶׁהַכֹּל דָּשִׁין בָּהּ, וּכְבוֹשֶׂם זֶה שֶׁהַכֹּל מִתְבַּשְּׂמִין בָּהּ — תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer** also **said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "His cheeks are like a bed of spices"** (Song of Songs 5:13)? **If a person makes himself** humble **like this** garden **bed upon which everyone treads, and like this spice with which everyone perfumes himself,** i.e., which benefits not only the one who wears it, **his** Torah **study will endure. But if not, his** Torah **study will not endure.**

Rav Elazar continued: what is the meaning of *lechayav ka'arugat habosem* — "his cheeks are like a flowerbed of fragrance"? If a person makes himself like a flowerbed (*arugah*) upon which *shehakol dashim bah* — everyone walks, everyone tramples — and like fragrance, *shekol mitbashmim bah* — everyone wants to be near it and enjoy its scent — meaning: if you are not haughty, not demanding of your own honor (*kavod*), not *makpid*, and you are genuinely pleasant and desirable to those around you — then *talmudo mitkayem*, your learning will be established. And if not, it will not.

The lecturer flags this as a genuinely puzzling connection. Unlike the straightforwardly practical advice about speaking aloud or reviewing what one has learned, the link between humility and *talmudo mitkayem* is not self-evident on a practical level. There are surely haughty scholars who sit alone in their rooms and retain their Torah. The *gemara* seems here to be framing this more as a dimension of divine reward — that the quality of character one brings to learning is itself part of what sustains it — rather than as a mechanistic causal claim.

### Stone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר): מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לוּחוֹת אֶבֶן״, אִם אָדָם מֵשִׂים עַצְמוֹ אֶת לְחָיָיו כְּאֶבֶן זוֹ שֶׁאֵינָהּ נִמְחֵית — תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Eliezer** further **said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "Tablets [*luḥot*] of stone"** (Exodus 31:18)? **If a person makes his cheeks [*leḥayav*] like this stone that does not wear away, his** Torah **study will endure. But if not,** i.e., if he is not diligent in his studies, **his** Torah **study will not endure.**

Rav Elazar again: the verse *luchot even* — the tablets of stone — if one reads *luchot* as *lechayav*, one's cheeks (or more broadly, one's manner of speech in Torah), and makes those *ke'even zu she'eina nimcheit* — like stone that is not worn away — meaning one constantly speaks in Torah, one's learning is never eroded, never interrupted — then *talmudo mitkayem beyado*. Rashi confirms: the point is constant engagement in speaking and reviewing Torah, so that it never fades from one's lips and is therefore never worn away.

### Broken Tablets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר): מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״חָרוּת עַל הַלּוּחוֹת״, אִלְמָלֵי לֹא נִשְׁתַּבְּרוּ לוּחוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת — לֹא נִשְׁתַּכְּחָה תּוֹרָה מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **And,** lastly, **Rabbi Eliezer said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written:** "And the tablets were the work of God, and the writing was the writing of God, **engraved upon the tablets"** (Exodus 32:16)? This teaches that **had the first tablets,** the subject of this verse, **not been broken, the Torah would never have been forgotten from the Jewish people,** as the Torah would have been engraved upon their hearts.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: אֵין כׇּל אוּמָּה וְלָשׁוֹן שׁוֹלֶטֶת בָּהֶן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״חָרוּת״ — אַל תִּיקְרֵי ״חָרוּת״, אֶלָּא חֵירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** Had the tablets not been broken, **no nation or tongue would** ever **have ruled over them, as it is stated: "Engraved**"; **do not read** it **engraved** [***ḥarut]* but** rather **freedom [*ḥeirut*].**

The same Rav Elazar extended the verse in a more sweeping direction: *charut al haluchot* — engraved on the tablets. *Ilmalei lo nishtabru luchot harishonot, lo nishtachacha Torah miYisrael* — had the first tablets not been broken, Torah would never have been forgotten. The tablets as stone are a symbol of permanence, and Rav Elazar understands them symbolically: when they were engraved, Torah was meant to be permanently inscribed — not only in stone but in the minds and souls of Israel. The breaking of the tablets introduced, in some metaphysical sense, the reality of forgetting. This does not refer merely to individual students who forget their learning, as one listener noted; Rav Acha bar Yaakov adds that had the tablets not been broken, *ein kol uma u'lashon sholelet bahen* — no other nation would ever have had dominion over Israel. The breaking of the tablets was the first great national sin, and as this week's *parasha*, *Behar-Bechukotai*, makes clear, sin leads to *galus*. The possibility of exile entered history precisely at the moment the tablets shattered — indeed, the tablets were broken at the very moment the Torah was first given, so that fallibility and its consequences arrived together with the gift itself.

The *gemara* adds: *al tikrei charut ela cheirut* — do not read "engraved" (*charut*) but "freedom" (*cheirut*). Had the tablets never been broken, the Torah would have represented complete freedom from subjugation by other nations.

## Wilderness Gift

### Rav Matna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״וּמִמִּדְבָּר מַתָּנָה״, אִם מֵשִׂים אָדָם עַצְמוֹ כְּמִדְבָּר זֶה שֶׁהַכֹּל דָּשִׁין בּוֹ — תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ. וְאִם לָאו — אֵין תַּלְמוּדוֹ מִתְקַיֵּים בְּיָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Mattana said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written:** "The well that the princes dug out, that the nobles of the people delved, with the scepter, with their staves. **And from the wilderness they went to Mattanah"** (Numbers 21:18)? **If a person makes himself** humble **like this wilderness,** which is open to all and **upon which everyone treads, his** Torah **study will endure** and be given to him as a gift [*mattana*]. **And if not, his** Torah **study will not endure.**

Rav Matna asked: what is the meaning of the verse *umimidbar matana* — "and from the wilderness, a gift" — from the song of the well? If a person makes himself like the wilderness, *shehakol dashim bo* — that everyone tramples upon it, for the wilderness is open to all and no one honors it or protects it — then *talmudo mitkayem beyado*, his Torah learning will be established in his hand, and moreover the Torah will be given to him as a *matana*, a gift. Humility — the willingness to be like open, trampled ground — is the condition under which Torah is truly received.

### Rava Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ מִלְּתָא לְרַב יוֹסֵף בַּהֲדֵיהּ, כִּי מְטָא מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי אֲמַר: אֵיזִיל וַאֲפַיְּיסֵיהּ. אֲזַל, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ דְּקָא מָזֵיג לֵיהּ כָּסָא. אֲמַר: הַב לִי וְאֶימְזְגֵיהּ אֲנָא. יְהַב לֵיהּ, מַזְגֵיהּ. כִּדְטַעְמֵיהּ אָמַר: דָּמֵי הַאי מְזִיגָא לִמְזִיגָא דְּרָבָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף בַּר חָמָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef had a grievance against Rava, son of Rav Yosef bar Ḥama,** who is usually referred to in the Gemara simply as Rava, and as a result of the grievance the two would never meet. **When the eve of Yom Kippur arrived,** Rava **said: I will go and appease him. He went and found** Rav Yosef's **attendant mixing him a cup** of wine. **He said to** the attendant: **Give** it **to me, and I will mix** it. **He gave it to** Rava, and Rava **mixed it.** Rav Yosef was blind and could not see his visitor, but **when he tasted** the wine **he said: This mixture is similar to the mixture** of **Rava, son of Rav Yosef bar Ḥama,** who would add extra water to the wine. Rava **said to him: It is I.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא תִּתֵּיב אַכַּרְעָיךָ עַד דִּמְפָרְשַׁתְּ לִי הָנֵי קְרָאֵי. מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמִמִּדְבָּר מַתָּנָה וּמִמַּתָּנָה נַחֲלִיאֵל וּמִנַּחֲלִיאֵל בָּמוֹת וּמִבָּמוֹת הַגַּיְא״?
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: Do not sit on your knees until you have explained these verses to me: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "And from the wilderness to Mattanah; and from Mattanah to Nahaliel; and from Nahaliel to Bamoth; and from Bamoth to the valley** in the field of Moab, to the top of Pisgah, which looks out toward the desert" (Numbers 21:19–20)?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִם אָדָם מֵשִׂים עַצְמוֹ כְּמִדְבָּר זֶה שֶׁהַכֹּל דָּשִׁין בּוֹ — תּוֹרָה נִיתְּנָה לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁנִּיתְּנָה לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה — נְחָלוֹ אֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִמַּתָּנָה נַחֲלִיאֵל״. וְכֵיוָן שֶׁנְּחָלוֹ אֵל — עוֹלֶה לִגְדוּלָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִנַּחֲלִיאֵל בָּמוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: If a person makes himself** humble **like this wilderness,** which is open to all and **upon which everyone treads, the Torah will be given to him as a gift [*mattana*]. And once it is given to him as a gift, he inherits it [*neḥalo*]** and **God [*El*]** makes it His inheritance, **as it is stated: "And from Mattanah to Nahaliel." And once God has made it His inheritance, he rises to greatness, as it is stated: "And from Nahaliel to Bamoth,"** which means heights.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מֵגֵיס לִבּוֹ — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַשְׁפִּילוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִבָּמוֹת הַגַּיְא״. וְאִם חוֹזֵר בּוֹ — הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַגְבִּיהוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל גֶּיא יִנָּשֵׂא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he becomes haughty, the Holy One, Blessed be He, lowers him, as it is stated: "And from Bamoth to the valley." And if he repents, the Holy One, Blessed be He, raises him** back **up, as it is stated: "Every valley shall be exalted"** (Isaiah 40:4).

On *Erev Yom Kippur*, Rava — the son of Rav Yosef bar Chama — went to appease Rav Yosef, with whom he had a quarrel. Finding Rav Yosef's attendant preparing a cup of wine, Rava took over and mixed it himself, exactly as Rav Yosef preferred. Rav Yosef, who was blind, recognized the mixture and said: it tastes just like the way Rava the son of Rav Yosef bar Chama used to mix it. Rava revealed himself — *ana hu*, it is I — and Rav Yosef told him: don't sit down until you explain these verses to me. What is the meaning of *umimidbar matana, umimatana Nachaliel, umiNachaliel Bamoth, umiVamoth Hagai*?

Rava explained: if a person makes himself like the wilderness that everyone tramples, the Torah is given to him as a gift — *umiMidbar Matana*. Once it is given as a gift, God becomes his inheritance — *umiMatana Nachaliel*. Once God is his inheritance, he rises to greatness — *umiNachaliel Bamoth*. But if he then becomes haughty — *ve'im megis libo* — God brings him low, *umiVamoth Hagai*, from the heights to the valley. And if he then does *teshuvah*, God raises him up again — *kol ge yinnase*, all the valleys will be lifted.

The lecturer observes that there is likely a subtext running beneath this exchange. Was Rava confessing that he himself had acted with excessive pride and was now doing *teshuvah* — and was that the meaning of the reconciliation? Or was he perhaps subtly directing the interpretation toward Rav Yosef, suggesting that one who holds a grudge may be too *makpid* on his own *kavod*? The text leaves this deliberately open. What is clear is that Rav Yosef himself invited the interpretation, so the conversation is doing double work: reconciling a personal dispute while expounding a teaching about humility and its consequences.

## Torah Metaphors

### Fig Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״נוֹצֵר תְּאֵנָה יֹאכַל פִּרְיָהּ״, לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה כִּתְאֵנָה, מָה תְּאֵנָה זוֹ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאָדָם מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בָּהּ — מוֹצֵא בָּהּ תְּאֵנִים. אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאָדָם הוֹגֶה בָּהֶן — מוֹצֵא בָּהֶן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "He who guards the fig tree shall eat its fruit"** (Proverbs 27:18)? **Why were matters of Torah compared to a fig tree? Just as this fig tree,** whenever a person searches it for figs to eat, **he finds figs in it,** as the figs on a tree do not ripen all at once, so that one can always find a recently ripened fig, **so** too, with **matters of Torah. Whenever a person meditates upon them, he finds in them** new **meaning.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: what is the meaning of *notzer te'ena yochal pirya* — "one who tends a fig tree will eat its fruit"? Why is Torah compared to a fig tree? Because figs do not all ripen simultaneously: anytime you reach into a fig tree and feel around, you will find ripe figs — perhaps hidden, requiring searching, but always there. So too Torah: *kol zman she'adam hoge bahen motze bahen ta'am* — the more one immerses in Torah, deeply contemplating and analyzing it, the more one finds reason and meaning.

This marks a subtle shift in the *gemara*'s emphasis. The bulk of the discussion until this point has concerned *talmudo mitkayem* — retention and memory. What you do to internalize Torah, to keep it from being forgotten. Here the *gemara* moves toward a different dimension: understanding. The more you delve into Torah, the more it yields. It is not merely a matter of remembering what you learned; it is an inexhaustible source of insight for those who search it.

### Doe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אַיֶּלֶת אֲהָבִים וְיַעֲלַת חֵן וְגוֹ׳״, לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה לְאַיֶּלֶת? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה אַיָּלָה רַחְמָהּ צַר, וַחֲבִיבָה עַל בּוֹעֲלָהּ כׇּל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה כְּשָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה — אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה חֲבִיבִין עַל לוֹמְדֵיהֶן כׇּל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה כְּשָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "A loving hind and a graceful roe,** let her breasts satisfy you at all times, and be you ravished always with her love" (Proverbs 5:19)? **Why were matters of Torah compared to a hind? To tell you** that **just as** with **a hind, its womb is narrow and it is cherished by its mate each and every hour like the first hour, so** too, **matters of Torah are cherished by those who study them each and every hour like the first hour.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְיַעֲלַת חֵן״ — שֶׁמַּעֲלָת חֵן עַל לוֹמְדֶיהָ. ״דַּדֶּיהָ יְרַוֻּךָ בְכׇל עֵת״ — לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלוּ דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה כְּדַד? מָה דַּד זֶה כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַתִּינוֹק מְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בּוֹ — מוֹצֵא בּוֹ חָלָב, אַף דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאָדָם הוֹגֶה בָּהֶן — מוֹצֵא בָּהֶן טַעַם.
>
> **Translation:** **"And a graceful roe"** is expounded as follows: **That** the Torah **bestows grace upon those who study it. "Let her breasts satisfy you at all times"; why were matters of Torah compared to a breast? Just as** with **a breast, whenever a baby searches it** for milk to suckle, **he finds milk in it, so** too, with **matters of Torah. Whenever a person meditates upon them, he finds** new **meaning in them.**

Rav Shmuel bar Nachmani asked: why is Torah compared to a doe (*ayelet ahavim v'ayelet chen*)? Because the doe (*rachmah tzar*) — and the *gemara* uses physiological language here quite explicitly — is always desired by its mate. So too, *divrei Torah chavivin al lomdeihem bechol sha'ah v'sha'ah kisha'ah rishonah* — words of Torah are beloved to those who study them at every moment just as at the first moment. Torah does not grow stale or tiresome for the devoted learner; every encounter with it is like the first.

The lecturer acknowledges that the metaphor is striking — even jarring — in its physicality, and notes the student observation that this is exactly the kind of image Bruriah might have objected to. But the point being illustrated is a serious one, connecting back to the teaching *asher anochi metzavecha hayom* — that each day Torah should feel as fresh as the day it was given (*bechol yom vayom yihyu b'einecha kachadashim*). The earlier discussion spoke of how one retains what one has learned; here the *gemara* speaks of how, if one truly throws oneself into Torah, it never loses its freshness or power.

The verse continues: *va'ayelet chen* — and she who lifts favor — meaning that Torah *shemaalet chen al lomdeha*, it causes those who study it to find favor in the eyes of others.

### Breast

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba continued with the next part of the verse: *dadeha yeravucha bechol et* — "her breasts will satisfy you at all times." Why is Torah compared to a breast? Just as an infant who nurses on a breast — who handles it and suckles from it — always finds milk, so too with Torah: *kol zman she'adam hoge bahen motze bahen ta'am* — every time a person immerses in Torah, he finds nourishment. The same principle as the fig tree, now expressed through a different natural image — this one shifting from the erotic register to the maternal, to the nourishment of an infant by its mother.

## Torah Metaphors (II)

### Elazar Passion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בְּאַהֲבָתָהּ תִּשְׁגֶּה תָּמִיד״ — כְּגוֹן רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר) בֶּן פְּדָת. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבִּי (אֱלִיעֶזֶר) שֶׁהָיָה יוֹשֵׁב וְעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה בַּשּׁוּק הַתַּחְתּוֹן שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי, וּסְדִינוֹ מוּטָל בַּשּׁוּק הָעֶלְיוֹן שֶׁל צִיפּוֹרִי. (תַּנְיָא) אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר: פַּעַם אַחַת בָּא אָדָם לִיטְּלוֹ, וּמָצָא בּוֹ שָׂרָף.
>
> **Translation:** **"And be you ravished always with her love";** your love for Torah should always distract you from worldly matters, **as was** the case with **Rabbi Elazar ben Pedat. They said of him, of Rabbi Elazar, that he would sit and engage in Torah** study **in the lower marketplace of Tzippori, and his cloak was lying in the upper marketplace of Tzippori.** His mind was so focused on Torah study that he would act in this unusual manner. In this regard, the Gemara relates that **it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yitzḥak ben Elazar said: One time a person came to take** this cloak for himself **and found a serpent on it** guarding it.

The verse concludes: *be'ahavatah tishgeh tamid* — "in its love you will constantly be intoxicated." What does this passionate attachment look like in practice? The *gemara* cites Rabbi Elazar ben Padat as its example. They used to say about him: he would sit and engage in Torah in the lower marketplace of Tzippori while his cloak lay forgotten in the upper marketplace. He was so consumed by his learning — so *meshugah* for Torah — that he was entirely oblivious to his surroundings and possessions. And a *baraita* adds: once someone came along and found the cloak, thinking to take it for himself, and found a serpent (*saraf*) inside — God was protecting the garment so that it would be there when Rabbi Elazar eventually returned for it.

## Scholars Classes

### White Donkeys

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַב עָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״רוֹכְבֵי אֲתוֹנוֹת צְחוֹרוֹת יוֹשְׁבֵי עַל מִדִּין [וְהוֹלְכֵי עַל דֶּרֶךְ שִׂיחוּ]״, ״רוֹכְבַי אֲתוֹנוֹת״ — אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמְּהַלְּכִין מֵעִיר לְעִיר וּמִמְּדִינָה לִמְדִינָה לִלְמוֹד (בּוֹ) תּוֹרָה. ״צְחוֹרוֹת״ — שֶׁעוֹשִׂין אוֹתָהּ כְּצָהֳרַיִם. ״יֹשְׁבֵי עַל מִדִּין״ — שֶׁדָּנִין דִּין אֱמֶת לַאֲמִיתּוֹ. ״וְהוֹלְכֵי״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִקְרָא. ״עַל דֶּרֶךְ״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מִשְׁנָה. ״שִׂיחוּ״ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי תַלְמּוּד, שֶׁכׇּל שִׂיחָתָן דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In further praise of the Torah and those who study it, a Sage **of the school of Rav Anan taught: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "You that ride on white donkeys, you that sit on rich cloths, and you that walk by the way, tell of it"** (Judges 5:10)? **"You that ride on white donkeys"; these are Torah scholars, who travel from city to city and from province to province to study Torah. "White [*tzeḥorot*]"** are those **who make it** clear **as noon** [***tzahorayim***], i.e., who make the Torah comprehensible. **"You that sit on couches [*midin*]"** refers to those **who judge [*danin*] an absolutely true judgment. "And you that walk"; these are the masters of Bible,** who are the least important of the scholars. **"By the way"; these are the** more important **masters of Mishna. "Tell of it"; these are the masters of Talmud,** the most important of all, **as all their conversation is** about **matters of Torah.**

A *tanna* from the school of Rav Anan taught: what is the meaning of the verse from *Shirat Devorah* (*Shoftim*): *rochvei atonot tzechorot, yoshvei al midin, veholechi al derech sichu* — "those who ride on white donkeys, who sit upon *midin*, and those who walk on the road have spoken"?

*Rochvei atonot* — those who ride on donkeys — these are *talmidei chachamim* who travel from city to city and from province to province to learn Torah. *Tzechorot* — white — they make Torah as clear as the noon-day sun (*katzahorayim*); through their effort and dedication, Torah is clarified and illuminated. *Yoshvei al midin* — reading *midin* as *din* — these are those who sit in judgment and render a true ruling (*din emet la'amito*): Torah is not merely learned but applied. *Holechi al derech* — those who walk on the path — these are *ba'alei mikra*, masters of the written text; they are on the right path. *Sichu* — those who speak — these are *ba'alei Talmud*, masters of Talmud, *shekol sichatan divrei Torah* — for whom all conversation consists of words of Torah.

The progression here is significant. *Mikra* is the foundation; *mishnah* is knowing and transmitting the law; *Talmud* — meaning not the compiled tractate but the enterprise of analysis — is the capacity to penetrate and investigate. And the mark of a true *ba'al Talmud* is that the analysis has permeated his entire being, so that *kol sichato divrei Torah*: there is no idle speech, because his mind is always engaged in the substance of Torah. This is the culmination of the *gemara*'s long discussion about embodying Torah in the whole body — the person whose entire verbal life is Torah.

## Repetition

### Hunter Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא יַחֲרוֹךְ רְמִיָּה צֵידוֹ״ — לֹא יִחְיֶה וְלֹא יַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים צַיָּיד הָרַמַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheizvi said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "The slothful man [*remiyya*] will not roast [*yaḥarokh*] his catch"** (Proverbs 12:27)? **The deceitful [*rammai*] hunter will not live [*yiḥyeh*] a long life [*ya'arikh*].** A deceitful hunter continues to hunt more and more animals without holding on to the animals he has already caught. Similarly, someone who continues to study new material without reviewing what he has already learned will not be successful.

Rav Shizbi said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: what is the meaning of *lo yachroch remiyah tzeido* — the sly one will not char his catch? Reading *yachroch* as a combination of *lo yichyeh velo ya'arich yamim* — the deceptive hunter will not live long and will not have length of days. And who is the deceptive hunter? Rashi understands this as someone who learns a great deal but does not review (*chazer*). All that accumulation is deceptive — perhaps even self-deceptive — if it is never consolidated. It will not pay off.

### Break Wings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: צַיָּיד הָרַמַּאי יַחֲרוֹךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said:** Will **a deceitful hunter** have something to **roast?** One who acts in this way is a fool, but it is hard to describe him as deceitful.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: מָשָׁל לְצַיָּיד שֶׁצָּד צִפֳּרִים, אִם רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן מְשַׁבֵּר כְּנָפָיו — מִשְׁתַּמֵּר, וְאִם לָאו — אֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said: This is comparable to a hunter who is hunting birds; if he breaks** the **wings** of the birds **one by one** as he captures them so that they will be unable to fly off again, his prey **will be secured, and if not,** they **will not be secured.** According to this explanation, the word *rammai* is interpreted as cunning rather than deceitful. A cunning hunter secures his prey; similarly, a cunning student reviews each lesson and thereby retains that which he learns.

Rav Sheshet reads the same verse more positively: the crafty hunter — *tzeid haramai* — if he is truly clever about his catch, *yachroch* — he will be able to preserve it. And how is one crafty about retaining what one has learned? When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael* he offered a parable: it is like a hunter who catches birds. If after catching the first one he immediately breaks its wings — does something to fix it in place — then when he pursues the second bird, the first one stays. *Ve'im lav ein mishtamer* — if he does not, it will not be kept. After learning a piece of Torah, do something to break its wings — review it, repeat it — so that it cannot fly away before you go on to the next thing.

### Bundles Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר (רַבָּה) אָמַר רַב סְחוֹרָה אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״הוֹן מֵהֶבֶל יִמְעָט וְקוֹבֵץ עַל יָד יַרְבֶּה״, אִם עוֹשֶׂה אָדָם תּוֹרָתוֹ חֲבִילוֹת חֲבִילוֹת, — מִתְמַעֵט, וְאִם לָאו — קוֹבֵץ עַל יָד יַרְבֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rabba said that Rav Seḥora said that Rav Huna said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "Wealth gotten through vanity** [***hevel***] **shall be diminished; but he that gathers little by little shall increase"** (Proverbs 13:11)? **If a person turns his Torah into bundles** [***ḥavilot*,** derived from the word *hevel* by replacing the *heh* with a *ḥet*], studying large amounts at the same time, his Torah **will diminish. And if not,** i.e., if he learns little by little and reviews what he has learned, **he that gathers little by little shall increase.**

Rav Sachora said in the name of Rav Huna: what is the meaning of *hon mehevel yimat, vekoveitz al yad yirbeh* — "wealth gathered by vanity will be diminished, and one who gathers hand by hand will increase"? Reading *hevel* as *chavila* — a bundle: if a person makes his Torah into bundles upon bundles, piling on more and more without pausing to internalize what he has already accumulated, *mitma'et* — it will diminish, it will be lost. But *vekoveitz al yad* — if one gathers slowly, hand by hand, piece by piece — *yirbeh*, it will increase and be retained.

### Rava's Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: יָדְעִי רַבָּנַן לְהָא מִלְּתָא, וְעָבְרִי עֲלַהּ. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אֲנָא עֲבַדְתַּהּ, וְאִיקַּיַּים בִּידַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said: The Sages know this, but** nevertheless **transgress it,** i.e., they fail to heed this advice. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: I did this,** learning little by little and regularly reviewing what I had learned, **and** my learning **has** indeed **endured.**

Rava stated: *yad'u rabanan lehai milta ve'avru alah* — the rabbinic students know this principle and ignore it anyway. It is always more appealing to press forward to the next page than to slow down and review what has just been learned. The observation drew a pointed remark from the room about the *Daf Yomi* cycle — if any context makes this critique vivid, it is that one. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, however, testified: *ana avidtah* — I actually did it the right way, learning slowly with review, *vekayma beyadai* — and it was established in my hands. The method works.

## Sinai Transmission

### Order at Sinai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, כֵּיצַד סֵדֶר מִשְׁנָה: מֹשֶׁה לָמַד מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה. נִכְנַס אַהֲרֹן, וְשָׁנָה לוֹ מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקוֹ, נִסְתַּלֵּק אַהֲרֹן וְיָשַׁב לִשְׂמֹאל מֹשֶׁה. נִכְנְסוּ בָּנָיו, וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ בָּנָיו, אֶלְעָזָר יָשַׁב לִימִין מֹשֶׁה, וְאִיתָמָר לִשְׂמֹאל אַהֲרֹן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לְעוֹלָם אַהֲרֹן לִימִין מֹשֶׁה חוֹזֵר. נִכְנְסוּ זְקֵנִים וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ זְקֵנִים, נִכְנְסוּ כׇּל הָעָם וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן מֹשֶׁה פִּירְקָן. נִמְצְאוּ בְּיַד אַהֲרֹן אַרְבָּעָה, בְּיַד בָּנָיו שְׁלֹשָׁה, וּבְיַד הַזְּקֵנִים שְׁנַיִם, וּבְיַד כׇּל הָעָם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss methods of Torah study. **The Sages taught** the following *baraita*: **What was the order of teaching** the Oral Law? How was the Oral Law first taught? **Moses learned** directly **from the mouth of the Almighty. Aaron entered** and sat before him, **and Moses taught him his lesson** as he had learned it from God. **Aaron moved** aside **and sat to the left of Moses.** Aaron's **sons entered, and Moses taught them their lesson** while Aaron listened. Aaron's **sons moved** aside; **Elazar sat to the right of Moses and Itamar** sat **to the left of Aaron. Rabbi Yehuda** disagreed with the first *tanna* with regard to the seating arrangements and **said: Actually, Aaron would return to** sit to **the right of Moses. The elders entered and Moses taught them their lesson. The elders moved** aside, and **the entire nation entered and Moses taught them their lesson. Therefore, Aaron had** heard the lesson **four times, his sons** heard it **three** times, **the elders** heard it **twice, and the entire nation** heard it **once.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִסְתַּלֵּק מֹשֶׁה וְשָׁנָה לָהֶן אַהֲרֹן פִּירְקוֹ. נִסְתַּלֵּק אַהֲרֹן שָׁנוּ לָהֶן בָּנָיו פִּירְקָן. נִסְתַּלְּקוּ בָּנָיו, שָׁנוּ לָהֶן זְקֵנִים פִּירְקָן. נִמְצָא בְּיַד הַכֹּל אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Moses** then **departed** to his tent, **and Aaron taught** the others **his lesson** as he had learned it from Moses. **Aaron** then **departed** and **his sons taught** the others **their lesson. His sons** then **departed** and **the elders taught** the rest of the people **their lesson. Hence everyone,** Aaron, his sons, the elders and all the people, heard the lesson taught by God **four times.**

The *gemara* asks: *keitzad seder mishnah* — what was the order of the *mishnah*, meaning how was the Torah transmitted when God gave it at *Har Sinai*? Moshe received the Torah from God. Aaron then entered and Moshe taught him his portion (*shanah lo Moshe pirko*). Aaron then sat to Moshe's left — not to his right, because sitting on someone's right when there is no one at his left implies a degree of presumptuousness. Aaron's sons then entered, and Moshe taught them; Elazar sat to Moshe's right (since Aaron was at his left) and Itamar to Aaron's left. Rabbi Yehuda maintains that once there was someone to sit at Moshe's left, Aaron moved to Moshe's right. The elders then entered and Moshe taught them. Finally all of Israel entered and Moshe taught them as well.

The result: Aaron heard the material four times, Aaron's sons three, the elders twice, and all of Israel once. Moshe then stepped back, and Aaron repeated the teaching; Aaron's sons then taught it; then the elders taught it to everyone. By the end of the day, everyone had heard the material at least four times.

### Four Times Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: חַיָּיב אָדָם לִשְׁנוֹת לְתַלְמִידוֹ אַרְבָּעָה פְּעָמִים. וְקַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה אַהֲרֹן שֶׁלָּמַד מִפִּי מֹשֶׁה, וּמֹשֶׁה מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה — כָּךְ, הֶדְיוֹט מִפִּי הֶדְיוֹט — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **From here Rabbi Eliezer said: A person is obligated to teach his student** his lesson **four times. And** it follows by way of **an *a fortiori* inference: If Aaron, who learned from Moses** himself, **and Moses** had received the Torah directly **from the mouth of the Almighty,** needed **this** regimen; **an ordinary** student learning **from the mouth of an ordinary** teacher, **how much more so** must he review his studies four times.

From this, Rabbi Elazar derived: *chayav adam lishnot letalmido arba pe'amim* — a person is obligated to teach his student four times. The *kal vachomer* is straightforward: if Aaron, who learned from Moshe, who learned from God (*mipi ha-Gevurah*), required four repetitions before the material was truly his — how much more so an ordinary student learning from an ordinary teacher?

### Akiva Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁחַיָּיב אָדָם לִשְׁנוֹת לְתַלְמִידוֹ עַד שֶׁיִּלְמָדֶנּוּ — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלַמְּדָהּ אֶת בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״, וּמִנַּיִן עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא סְדוּרָה בְּפִיהֶם — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שִׂימָהּ בְּפִיהֶם״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: From where** do we derive **that a person is obligated to teach his student until he learns** the material and understands it? **As it is stated:** "Now therefore write this song for you, **and teach it to the children of Israel;** put it in their mouths, that this song may be a witness for me against the children of Israel" (Deuteronomy 31:19). This verse indicates that one must teach Torah to others. **And from where** do we derive that one must teach his students **until** the material **is organized in their mouths? As it is stated: "Put it in their mouths,"** so that they should be capable of teaching it to others.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִין שֶׁחַיָּיב לְהַרְאוֹת לוֹ פָּנִים — שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֵלֶּה הַמִּשְׁפָּטִים אֲשֶׁר תָּשִׂים לִפְנֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** do we derive **that** a teacher **must show** his students **the reasons** for the teachings? **As it is stated: "Now these are the judgments which you shall set before them"** (Exodus 21:1), which indicates that the lesson must be set out in logical fashion for the students.

Rabbi Akiva adds a further dimension: *minayin she-chayav adam lishnot letalmido ad she-yilmadennu* — how do we know that one must teach until the student actually learns, however many repetitions that requires? From *ve-lamdah et bnei Yisrael* — "teach it to the children of Israel," without limit. The *Maharsha* understands Rabbi Akiva as rejecting the idea that four repetitions is a fixed, fulfillable obligation; rather, you teach until they know it.

Moreover: *minayin ad she-tehei sedurah befihem* — how do we know the material must be *arranged* in the student's mouth, not merely vaguely known? From *simah befihem* — "place it in their mouths." And: *minayin she-chayav le-har'ot lo panim* — how do we know one must not only transmit the text but explain and illuminate it, which is the function of the *gemara* relative to the *mishnah*? From *ve-eleh hamishpatim asher tasim lifnehem* — "these are the judgments which you shall place before them," like a set table, as the famous Rashi reads it. Teaching is not complete with four recitations. The student must be able to say it back; must be able to articulate it clearly; and must understand it.

### Moshe's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְמְרוּ כּוּלְּהוּ מִמֹּשֶׁה! כְּדֵי לַחֲלוֹק כָּבוֹד לְאַהֲרֹן וּבָנָיו, וְכָבוֹד לַזְּקֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the manner in which the Oral Law was taught, the Gemara asks: **They should all have studied from Moses** himself four times. The Gemara answers: The teaching was divided in this manner **in order to give honor to Aaron and his sons, and** also to give **honor to the elders.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵיעוּל אַהֲרֹן וְנִיגְמַר מִמֹּשֶׁה, וְלִיעַיְילוּ בָּנָיו וְלִיגְמְרוּ מֵאַהֲרֹן, וְלִיעַיְילוּ זְקֵנִים וְלֵילְפוּ מִבָּנָיו, וְלִיעַיְילוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל וְלֵילְפוּ מִן הַזְּקֵנִים. כְּדִבְרֵי הַרִאשׁוֹנוֹת לִשְׁנִיּוֹת! כֵּיוָן דְּמֹשֶׁה מִפִּי הַגְּבוּרָה גָּמַר, מִסְתַּיְּיעָא מִלְּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks why a different method was not adopted, one which would have involved less trouble for Moses: **Aaron should have entered and studied from Moses; his sons should** then **have entered and studied from Aaron; the elders should** then **have entered and studied from** Aaron's **sons; and** then **they should have gone** out **and taught all of the Jewish people.** The Gemara answers: **Since Moses had studied** directly **from the mouth of the Almighty, it would be** more **effective** for everyone to hear the Torah at least once from Moses himself.

A question arises: why did Moshe not simply teach each group four times himself, so that Aaron would hear it four times directly from Moshe, the sons four times from Aaron, and so on down the chain? The *gemara* answers: *kedei lachlok kavod le-Aaron* — to give honor to Aaron, to the sons, and to the elders. By stepping back and allowing each tier to teach the next, Moshe ensured that the transmission of Torah carried dignity for all of its transmitters.

But a further question presses: why not have Aaron enter and hear it four times straight from Moshe, then the sons enter and hear it four times straight from Aaron? The *gemara* responds: *keivan deMoshe mipi hagevurah gamar, mistayeia milta* — since Moshe himself had received the Torah directly from God, there is a *siata d'shmaya*, a divine assistance, that accompanies Moshe's teaching. The people benefit from hearing it from Moshe himself; the very fact that he received it from the mouth of the Almighty lends his transmission a quality that carries through to those who receive it from him directly.
