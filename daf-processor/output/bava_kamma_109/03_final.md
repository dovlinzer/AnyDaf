# Bava Kamma 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Swearing & Returning Sto…

### Torah Section Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״אָבַד״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁאֲכָלוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן. [הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.]
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the owner asked the bailee: **Where is my deposit?** And the bailee **said to him:** It was **lost.** And the owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen,** therefore accepting the oath; **and the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he consumed it,** then he must **pay** the **principal.** If the bailee **admitted on his own** that he had taken a false oath, then he must **pay** the **principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 108b situates us within a broader *parsha* in the Torah addressing one who swears falsely about an object and subsequently admits to the theft. Upon admission, he is obligated to return the object, add a fifth of its value (*chomesh*), and bring a *korban asham*. The *gemara* on 109a will take up the more complex case in the *mishna*, but to understand it properly, the two foundational cases must first be laid out.

### Object Lost Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֵיכָן פִּקְדוֹנִי?״ אָמַר לוֹ: ״נִגְנַב״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״, וְהָעֵדִים מְעִידִים אוֹתוֹ שֶׁגְּנָבוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל. הוֹדָה מֵעַצְמוֹ – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן, חוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** If the owner asked the bailee: **Where is my deposit,** and the bailee **said to him: It was stolen;** and the owner said: **I administer an oath to you, and** the bailee **said: Amen,** therefore accepting the oath; **and the witnesses testify about** the bailee **that he stole it,** he must **pay** the **payment of double** the principal. If the bailee **admitted on his own** that he had taken a false oath, then he must **pay the principal,** the additional **one-fifth** payment, **and** bring **a guilt-offering.**

The first case: a depositor asks his *shomer*, "Where is the object I entrusted you with?" The *shomer chinam* responds that it was lost — a claim that, if true, would exempt him. The depositor imposes an oath (*mashbi'acha ani*) and the *shomer* answers *amein*, affirming the oath. Witnesses subsequently testify that he had in fact consumed the object. In this scenario, because the theft was exposed by witnesses rather than by the *shomer*'s own admission, he pays only the principal (*keren*). The special *parsha* of *keren, chomesh*, and *asham* applies exclusively when the admission is voluntary (*hodeh mei'atzmo*). If he does come forward on his own and confesses, he pays the principal and the fifth and brings a *korban asham*. This is not simply a heavier penalty — it is the path to atonement.

### Object Stolen Case

The second case: the depositor asks the same question, and the *shomer* claims the object was stolen (*nignav*). Again an oath is administered, again he answers *amein*. But witnesses then testify that he himself had kept the object. Because the *shomer* advanced the claim of *ta'anas gannav* — asserting it was stolen — the *halacha* treats him as a *gannav* himself, and he therefore pays *tashlumei kefel*, double, just as a *gannav* would. By contrast, if he admits on his own, the case falls under the *parsha* of the *shevuah* rather than the laws of *geneiva*, and he pays *keren, chomesh*, and *asham* — not *kefel*. These distinctions are clear and will not be further examined by the *gemara*; the focus shifts to what follows.

## Son Inherits Father's Es…

### Repayment Recipients

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַגּוֹזֵל אֶת אָבִיו, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ, וָמֵת – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם לְבָנָיו אוֹ לְאֶחָיו. וְאִם אֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה אוֹ שֶׁאֵין לוֹ – לֹוֶה וּבַעֲלֵי חוֹב בָּאִין וְנִפְרָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In the case of **one who robs his father** and the father demands that he return the stolen item, **and he takes an oath to** his father that he did not rob him; **and** then the father **dies;** and then the son admits that he robbed him and took a false oath, necessitating the return of the principal and the giving of the additional one-fifth payment to his father's heirs, of which he is either one of several or the only one; what should he do? **This** son **pays** the **principal and** the additional **one-fifth** payment **to his** father's **sons or brothers, and** brings **a guilt-offering** and does not keep his own share. **And if he does not want** to forfeit his share **or where he does not have** sufficient funds to pay the other heirs while forfeiting his share, he **borrows** money in the amount of the value of the stolen item **and the creditors come and are repaid** in part from his share in the stolen item.

The *mishna* now presents the case at the center of the *gemara*'s analysis: *gezel ha-av*. A son steals from his father, swears to him falsely, and the father then dies — leaving the son as an heir. The son is obligated to pay *keren, chomesh*, and *asham*, but to whom does he pay? The *mishna* states that he pays *l'vanav o l'echav* — to his sons or to his brothers. Because this formulation admits of multiple readings, the *rishonim* interpret it in substantially different ways.

### Rambam's View

The Rambam's *girsa* reads the *mishna* as *l'echav o l'vanav* — first his brothers, then his sons. According to this reading, the obligation is to get the stolen object, or its monetary equivalent, entirely out of his possession. Even if he would have inherited a portion of it had he never stolen it, that consideration is irrelevant. He has a *mitzvah* to return what he stole, and he does so by giving it to his brothers, who now represent the deceased father as the primary heirs. If there are no brothers, he gives it to his own son, who as the grandson of the deceased represents the father's estate to some degree. The critical point for the Rambam is that the item must leave his possession. Even if he is the sole heir and would have inherited everything, the theft and the *shevuah* created an independent obligation of return.

If he is unwilling or, in the Rambam's reading of *ein lo*, has neither brothers nor sons — meaning there is truly no one who represents the father — the *mishna* provides a mechanism: *loveh u'vaal chov ba v'nifra*. He borrows money, and when the creditors come to collect, he pays them with the stolen property, identifying it as *gezel aviv* — the thing he stole from his father. The act of giving it over, even to creditors, constitutes the required *hashava*. The essential requirement is that the object leaves his hands.

### Rashi's View

Rashi reads the case differently, focusing on the son's entitlement to the portion he would have inherited. Had the theft never occurred, he would have been entitled to a share of the object as part of the estate. Rashi therefore reads the *mishna* as providing a way for the son to preserve that legitimate share while still discharging the obligation of return. He gives the full value to his brothers or to his father's brothers — those who would inherit from the father — and then borrows money to facilitate this payment. He gives the full amount to the brothers and, when the creditors seek repayment, directs them to collect back his rightful portion from the brothers, paying the remainder himself. The mechanics of how this actually operates are admittedly not fully transparent, but the animating principle is that the son is not required to forfeit the share that was rightfully his.

### Tosafot's View

Tosafot takes yet a third approach. In Tosafot's reading, the son need not give up any of the stolen object's value at all. He borrows money against the stolen property, designating it as collateral for the loan. He then gives the object to the brothers. When the creditors arrive to collect, they exercise their liens on the object and take it from the brothers. The son retains his borrowed funds and surrenders nothing personally. The difficulty with this reading is obvious: it seems to leave the brothers worse off precisely because the theft occurred, and the son ends up keeping what he stole. How this constitutes a genuine *hashava* is difficult to explain on Tosafot's terms. Nonetheless, these three approaches — Rambam, Rashi, and Tosafot — represent three fundamentally distinct understandings of the nature of the obligation.

## Neder & Inheritance

### Neder Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לִבְנוֹ: ״קוּנָּם אִי אַתָּה נֶהֱנֶה מִשֶּׁלִּי״, אִם מֵת – יִירָשֶׁנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **one who says to his son** in a vow: It is forbidden **like an offering [*konam*],** and for that reason **you may not derive benefit from my property, if** the father then **dies** the son **inherits** from **him,** because it is no longer the father's property once he dies.

The *mishna* then presents a parallel case involving a *neder*. A father declares to his son, *konam ee ata nehene misheli* — he takes a vow prohibiting his son from deriving any benefit from his property.

### Inheritance Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּחַיָּיו וּבְמוֹתוֹ, אִם מֵת – לֹא יִירָשֶׁנּוּ, וְיַחְזִיר לְבָנָיו אוֹ לְאֶחָיו. וְאִם אֵין לוֹ – לֹוֶה וּבַעֲלֵי חוֹב בָּאִים וְנִפְרָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the father stated in his vow that his son may not derive benefit from his property **in his life and in his death,** then even **if** the father then **dies** the son **does not inherit** from **him,** as the prohibition is still in effect. **And** instead of taking his inheritance, **he returns** his portion in the estate **to his sons or to his brothers. And if he does not have** sufficient funds to subsist without his inheritance, he **borrows** money in the amount of the value of his share in the inheritance **and** the **creditors come and are repaid** from his share.

The general rule is that if the father dies, the son may still inherit. The logic is straightforward: the *neder* applies while the property belongs to the father; once he dies, the property is no longer his, and the son is not benefiting from something that belongs to the father. However, if the father specified *bechayav u'vimoto* — that the prohibition extends both during his lifetime and after his death — then the son cannot inherit, because doing so would constitute deriving benefit from property that the *neder* forbids him from benefiting from even after death. In that case, the property must pass to the father's other heirs: *l'vanav v'l'echav*, his sons or his brothers.

### No Heirs Scenario

If there are no other heirs — either in the Rambam's sense, that no one else exists to receive the property, or in Rashi's and Tosafot's sense, that the son cannot afford to forgo the inheritance — the *mishna* again provides the mechanism of *loveh u'va'alei chov ba'im v'nifra'im*: he borrows money and the creditors exercise their liens against the property. In this case, the logic is cleaner. The son borrows ten thousand dollars against the property; the property sits in his possession technically untouched; and when he fails to repay, the creditors exercise their liens and take the property directly. Because the son never directly derived benefit from the property itself — he used the borrowed cash — and it was the creditors who benefited from the property by exercising their liens, he is not considered to have violated the *neder*. The creditors are the ones taking the property, not him.

## Debt vs. Ritual Act

### Rav Yosef Charity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: אֲפִילּוּ לְאַרְנָקִי שֶׁל צְדָקָה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר: ״זֶה גֶּזֶל אָבִי״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the *halakha* written in the mishna that one who robs his father pays the principal and the additional one-fifth payment to the father's heirs, **Rav Yosef says:** One may **even** put this money **in a charity purse** if he so chooses. **Rav Pappa says: And** when giving the money **he must say: This** is what was **robbed** from **my father.**

The *gemara* opens on 109a with a statement of Rav Yosef: *afilu l'arnakei shel tzedaka* — even giving the stolen property to a charitable fund suffices to discharge the obligation, in a case where there is no one to return it to. This extends the *mishna*'s mechanism of paying creditors: the essential requirement, in the absence of an identified recipient, is simply to get the property out of one's possession. Giving it to *tzedaka* accomplishes exactly that.

### Identification Required

Rav Pappa adds a qualification: *v'tzarich she'yomar zeh gezel avi* — for the giving to constitute a valid *hashava*, one must verbally identify it as the property stolen from his father. The act of transfer alone is insufficient; it must be accompanied by the declaration that this is what he stole, so that the giving has the character of a genuine return rather than an unrelated transaction.

### Self-Forgiveness Challen…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי? נִמְחֲלֵיהּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ! מִי לָא תְּנַן: מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא מָחַל לוֹ עַל הַחוֹמֶשׁ? אַלְמָא בַּר מְחִילָה הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara questions the mishna's ruling that one who robs his father must return the stolen item to others even if he is the sole heir. **Why** must he do this? **Let him forgive** the debt **to himself;** as the heir, to whom payment is due, he should be able to forgive it. **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (103a) that in the case of a robber who took a false oath claiming to be innocent and later admitted his liability: If the owner **forgave him concerning the principal, but did not forgive him concerning the** additional **one-fifth** payment, he need not pursue him to repay the remaining debt. **Apparently,** even the principal **is subject to forgiveness,** so why doesn't he forgive the obligation to himself?

This leads the *gemara* to press a more fundamental question. The discussion up to this point has concerned cases where the stolen property has been destroyed and what remains is essentially a monetary obligation — a debt. In such a case, why is any of this necessary? If the son is an heir and the obligation is simply a debt, why can he not simply waive it for himself? If he has two brothers, why can he not say: the third that would have come to me I hereby release — and be done with it? And if he is the sole heir, there is no one he owes the money to — so why must he give it to *tzedaka*? The question points to what is at stake: is the obligation of *hashava* in the context of a *shevuah* purely a financial debt, or does it have an independent dimension requiring a ritual act of transfer?

Support for the debt-only view comes from an earlier *mishna*: *machalu al ha-keren v'lo machalu al ha-chomesh* — if the person who was stolen from waives the principal, the obligation is extinguished. This shows that *mechila* — forgiveness — works. If the debt is waived, there is no additional need to perform an act of returning. The *gemara* notes that this earlier *mishna* treats the obligation as fully subject to *mechila*, and asks: why, then, does our *mishna* create such an elaborate structure? If the father dies and the son is his heir, it is simply a debt he owes himself — let him waive it. The *gemara* attributes this tension to a *tannaitic* dispute.

## Yose vs. Akiva Debate

### Extreme Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, הָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאִם אֵין לָאִישׁ גֹּאֵל לְהָשִׁיב הָאָשָׁם״ – וְכִי יֵשׁ אָדָם בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גּוֹאֲלִים? אֶלָּא בְּגֶזֶל הַגֵּר הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר –
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This is **not difficult. That** mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** who says that this obligation can be forgiven, while **this** mishna, ruling that a son who robbed his father and is his sole heir cannot forgive the obligation to himself, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** who holds that this obligation cannot be forgiven. Rabbi Yoḥanan explains: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 10:16) that the verse states with regard to one who steals from one who then died: **"But if the man has no kinsman** to whom **restitution may be made for the guilt,** the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, even the priest's" (Numbers 5:8). The *baraita* asks: **But is there** any **Jewish person who has no kinsmen?** Since every Jew descends from Jacob our forefather, all Jews have relatives to inherit from them. **Rather,** it must be that **the verse is speaking of robbery of a convert** who never married or had children as a Jew, and in any case is no longer legally related to his gentile family; he has no heirs. In this situation the Torah instructs one who robbed from a now-deceased convert to return the stolen item to a priest.

Rabbi Yochanan resolves the contradiction by identifying the two *mishnayot* with two opposing *tannaim*: the earlier *mishna* where *mechila* works reflects the view of Rabbi Yose HaGlili, while our *mishna*, which demands an act of giving even when the son is the sole heir, reflects the view of Rabbi Akiva. A *beraita* provides the textual basis for the dispute: *v'im la'ish go'el l'hashiv ha'asham eilav* — if the person stolen from has no redeemer (*go'el*) to receive the restitution, it is brought to the *Kohen*. The *gemara* notes: is there anyone in Israel who has no relatives? It must be speaking of *gezel ha-ger* — stealing from a convert who dies without heirs — and in that case the money is given to the *Kohanim*.

### Ger Death Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל הַגֵּר, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ; וְשָׁמַע שֶׁמֵּת הַגֵּר, וְהָיָה מַעֲלֶה כַּסְפּוֹ וַאֲשָׁמוֹ לִירוּשָׁלַיִם; וּפָגַע בְּאוֹתוֹ הַגֵּר, וּזְקָפוֹ עָלָיו בְּמִלְוָה, וּמֵת – זָכָה הַלָּה בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּיָדוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין לוֹ תַּקָּנָה עַד שֶׁיּוֹצִיא גְּזֵילוֹ מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: In the case of one **who robbed a convert and took** a false **oath to him** saying that he did not rob him, **and** then **heard that the convert died; and he was bringing his money,** for the principal and the additional one-fifth payment, **and his guilt-offering up to Jerusalem** to pay his debt to the priests and sacrifice his offering, **and he encountered that same convert,** who in fact had not died; **and,** instead of having the robber pay the money right away the convert **established it as a loan for** the robber, **and** then the convert **died,** leaving his property ownerless, as he had no heirs; **this** robber **acquires** all **that** is **in his possession,** as it is now a loan, not money to pay back the theft; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says:** The robber **has no remedy until he will remove his stolen item from his possession.**

The *beraita* then describes a case: someone steals from a *ger* and swears falsely; he hears that the *ger* has died and begins traveling to Jerusalem with his money and his *korban asham* to give to the *Kohanim*; on the road he encounters the *ger* — very much alive. The *ger*, in a generous spirit, then says there is no need to hand over the money now; he may keep it as a loan and pay later. Then the *ger* dies. *Zacha lo b'ma sheb'yado* — he acquires what is in his hand. So rules Rabbi Yose HaGlili: the man keeps the money.

### Yose: Debt Erased

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – לָא שְׁנָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ לָא שְׁנָא לַאֲחֵרִים, מָצֵי מָחֵיל; וּלְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – לָא שְׁנָא לַאֲחֵרִים וְלָא שְׁנָא לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, לָא מָצֵי מָחֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan explains their respective opinions. **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** there **is no difference** if the one to whom payment is due forgives the return of a stolen item **to himself,** such as in this case of a convert who died or in the case of the mishna where a son robbed his father who then died, and there **is no difference** if he forgives the return of a stolen item **to others.** In either case, he **can forgive** payment. **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** there **is no difference** if the one to whom payment is due forgives the return of a stolen item **to others** and there **is no difference** if he forgives the return of a stolen item **to himself.** In either case, he **cannot forgive** payment.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili's reasoning rests on the principle that when a *ger* dies without heirs, any debts owed to him are erased — whoever holds the property at that moment acquires it. Because the *ger* had converted the original theft-debt into a loan, and then died, the debt to the *ger* was erased at the moment of death. Rabbi Yose HaGlili concludes that once the debt aspect of the obligation disappears, there is no independent requirement to perform any ritual act of giving. The obligation is entirely debt-based, and when the debt is gone, so is the obligation.

### Akiva: Act Required

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *ein lo takanah she'yotzi gezeilo mi'tachas yado* — there is no remedy for this man until he actually removes the stolen property from his possession. The fact that he no longer has a living creditor to whom he owes the money is irrelevant. The obligation of return is not purely financial; it requires an affirmative act of giving. No matter how the debt was extinguished — through the death of the *ger*, through *mechila*, through inheritance — the ritual dimension of *hashava* remains and must be fulfilled.

The *gemara* initially frames the dispute as maximally extreme. Rabbi Yose HaGlili holds that *mechila* dissolves the obligation in every configuration — whether someone else forgives the debt or whether one inherits it and effectively owes it to oneself — and *lo shna l'nafshei lo shna l'acherim*: there is never a need for a ritual act of giving. Rabbi Akiva holds the opposite extreme: no form of erasure — whether by third-party forgiveness or by automatic extinction through death — ever suffices. An act of physical transfer is always required. On this reading, the earlier *mishna* where *mechila* works is Rabbi Yose HaGlili, and our *mishna* where the sole heir must still give it away is Rabbi Akiva.

The *gemara* then pushes further to clarify the extreme reading: according to Rabbi Yose HaGlili, the *beraita* did not even need to describe the scenario in which the *ger* converted the debt into a loan. Even without that conversion, once the *ger* dies, the debt is erased and the man is exempt — because the debt that existed from the theft itself would have been wiped out at the *ger*'s death regardless. The *beraita* described the loan scenario only to highlight the power of Rabbi Akiva's position: even when the *ger* actively said "you will owe me the money" — which might have been taken as an act analogous to giving and receiving it back — Rabbi Akiva says it is still insufficient. The stolen property must physically leave the thief's possession.

## Rav Sheshet Challenge

### Limited Examples Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: אִי הָכִי, לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי – לַשְׁמְעִינַן לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן לַאֲחֵרִים! לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא – לַשְׁמְעִינַן לַאֲחֵרִים דְּלָא מָצֵי מָחֵיל, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ דְּלָא מָצֵי מָחֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to this** interpretation of the *baraita*: **If so,** then **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, let** the mishna on 103a, which indicates that one can forgive the principal payment of a robbery, **teach us** instead that the robbery victim can forgive the obligation **to himself, and** it would be understood that **all the more so** one can forgive the obligation **to others.** And **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** which Rabbi Yoḥanan explains to be the opinion stated in the mishna here, **let it teach us that one cannot forgive** the obligation **to others, and** it would be understood **all the more so** with regard to the robber, **that** he **cannot forgive** the obligation **to himself.**

Rav Sheshet challenges Rabbi Yochanan's extreme framing of the dispute. If Rabbi Yose HaGlili truly holds that the obligation is purely a debt — such that even self-forgiveness works — he should have stated his position in its strongest form: the case in our *mishna* where the son is the sole heir and there is no one else involved. That case, where the man effectively owes the money to himself and simply waives it, is the most radical application of the debt-only view. From that case it would follow *a fortiori* that when an external party — the *nigzal* — forgives the debt, that certainly works. Why, then, does Rabbi Yose HaGlili's position appear only in the less dramatic case of the *nigzal*'s *mechila*?

### Both Need Act

The same challenge applies in reverse to Rabbi Akiva. If he holds that a ritual act of giving is always required regardless of how the debt is dissolved, he should have stated his position in its strongest form: even when the *nigzal* actively and explicitly forgives the obligation, the thief still must hand over the money. That would be a stunning *chiddush* — and from it, the case of the sole heir would follow *a fortiori*. Yet Rabbi Akiva's position appears only in our *mishna*, which addresses the case of the heir. Rav Sheshet concludes that the debate is therefore not as extreme as Rabbi Yochanan suggested, and the *gemara* must find a more calibrated reading.

## Abaye Resolution

### Mechila As Returning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי; כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי דְּמָצֵי מָחֵיל – לַאֲחֵרִים, אֲבָל לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ – לָא מָצֵי מָחֵיל. אֶלָּא אַמַּאי זָכָה הַלָּה בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּיָדוֹ? מִשּׁוּם דִּזְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Sheshet said: That** mishna **and this** mishna are both in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. When Rabbi Yosei HaGelili is saying that one can forgive** the return of a stolen item, he meant specifically forgiving the obligation **to others, but** he **cannot forgive** the obligation **to himself.** The Gemara asks: **But** if that is the *halakha*, **why did this** one who robbed a convert who subsequently died **acquire** all **that** is **in his possession? Because** the convert **established it as a loan for him,** and henceforth it is no longer considered stolen property, but a standard loan debt.

Abaye resolves the tension by reading both *mishnayot* as the position of Rabbi Yose HaGlili. Rabbi Yose HaGlili does not hold that the obligation is purely a debt with no dimension of giving; rather, he agrees that there must be some act of returning. His leniency is that he is willing to accept *mechila* by the other party as constituting such an act. When the *nigzal* says "I forgive you," that counts for Rabbi Yose HaGlili as sufficient — it is an interaction with the party to whom the debt is owed, and it stands in for the act of returning. Similarly, when the *ger* converts the obligation into a loan and then dies, that too counts as a form of return, since it involved an exchange between the two parties.

### Sole Heir Problem

The case of the sole heir is categorically different. There is no other party; there is no act that can plausibly be characterized as a return. The son simply owes the money to no one. And in that case, even Rabbi Yose HaGlili is not satisfied. The mechanism of *mechila* requires that there be someone else to grant the forgiveness; without that, no act of return has occurred, and the obligation remains. Our *mishna*, which requires the sole heir to find some way to give the property away, is therefore also Rabbi Yose HaGlili.

### Loan Conversion

Returning to the *ger* case: why, on Abaye's reading, does the scenario of converting the debt to a loan matter? Because Rabbi Yose HaGlili distinguishes between the original *gezel* debt — an obligation to return what was stolen — and the new loan arrangement. When the *ger* says "pay me later," it is as though the thief paid the *ger* and the *ger* lent the money back. That constitutes an act of return in the same way that *mechila* by the *nigzal* does: there was a genuine interaction between the parties around the obligation. It is this interaction that Rabbi Yose HaGlili accepts as fulfilling the requirement. Rabbi Akiva, in Abaye's reading, would not accept even this — any act of returning must be explicit and direct.

## Rava Resolution

### Third Party Mechila

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא; כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דְּלָא מָצֵי מָחֵיל – לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֲבָל לַאֲחֵרִים – מָצֵי מָחֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers a different explanation. **Rava said: That** mishna **and this** mishna are both in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva. When Rabbi Akiva says that** he **cannot forgive** the return of a stolen item he meant specifically forgiving the obligation **to himself,** as in the case of the convert, **but** he **can forgive** the obligation **to others.**

Rava offers a parallel resolution that assigns both *mishnayot* instead to Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva does not hold that no form of *mechila* is ever sufficient. His position is more precisely targeted: he only insists on an actual act of return when the debt is extinguished automatically — by operation of law, as when the *ger* dies — without any active participation by the party to whom the obligation was owed. When the *nigzal* actively grants *mechila*, Rabbi Akiva agrees that this constitutes an adequate act of return. The earlier *mishna* where *mechila* works is therefore also Rabbi Akiva.

### Automatic Erasure

The case of the *ger* is where Rabbi Akiva draws the line. The *ger*'s death automatically erases the debt; the *ger* did nothing, chose nothing, and granted nothing. For Rabbi Akiva, that automatic extinction — however complete it may be as a matter of financial law — does not constitute the required act of return. The stolen property has not been handed over; it has simply ceased to be owed. That is insufficient. Rabbi Yose HaGlili, on this reading, would say that even the automatic erasure is enough, since the obligation is entirely debt-based and needs no act of its own.

The upshot is three distinct ways of framing the *tannaitic* dispute. In the extreme reading: Rabbi Yose HaGlili treats everything as a debt and any erasure suffices; Rabbi Akiva always demands a ritual act regardless. In Abaye's reading: both are Rabbi Yose HaGlili, who requires some act but accepts *mechila* by another party as that act — except when there is no other party. In Rava's reading: both are Rabbi Akiva, who requires some act but accepts *mechila* by the other party as that act — except when the extinction occurs automatically.

## Money to Kohanim

### Timing Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: אֲפִילּוּ לְנַפְשֵׁיהּ נָמֵי מָצֵי מָחֵיל?! אֶלָּא גֶּזֶל הַגֵּר דְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא נְתִינָה לְכֹהֲנִים, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions Rava's explanation: **By inference** does it follow **that Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds** that he **can forgive** the return of the stolen item **even to himself? But** if so, payment for **robbery of a convert** who dies without heirs, about **which the Merciful One says** that it requires **giving** repayment **to the priests, how can you find these** circumstances if in every case where one robs a convert and the convert dies the robber can forgive the return of the item to himself?

A pointed question now emerges from Rava's framework. According to Rabbi Yose HaGlili's view — as articulated in the extreme reading — the entire obligation of *gezel ha-ger* is purely a debt, and when the *ger* dies, that debt is automatically erased. But if that is so, when would the stolen money ever reach the *Kohanim*? The Torah explicitly provides that when the stolen property has no *go'el* to receive it, it is given to the *Kohen* (*ha'asham ha'mushav la-Kohen*). Yet every time the *ger* dies, the debt appears to evaporate. Under a purely debt-based framework with no independent requirement of *hashava*, how can the law of *gezel ha-ger* going to the *Kohanim* ever be operative?

### Post-Death Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּשֶׁגָּזַל אֶת הַגֵּר, וְנִשְׁבַּע לוֹ, וּמֵת הַגֵּר, וְהוֹדָה לְאַחַר מִיתָה; דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּאוֹדִי – קְנָאוֹ הַשֵּׁם וּנְתָנוֹ לְכֹהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: With what are we dealing here?** With a case **where one robbed the convert and took an oath to him** that he did not rob him, **and** then **the convert died, and** the robber **admitted** his false oath **after** the convert's **death,** such **that at the time that he admitted** to it, **the Name,** i.e., God, **acquired** the principal and the additional one-fifth payment **and gave it to the priests.** Once the right to the payment is transferred to the priests the robber can no longer forgive it. By contrast, if he admitted his false oath while the convert was still alive and then the convert dies, the convert can forgive the obligation to himself, since he already took ownership of it.

Rava answers: the scenario that triggers the payment to the *Kohanim* is one in which the thief did not admit (*hodeh*) until after the *ger* had already died. When the *ger* was still alive, the thief had not confessed, and so no formal debt had crystallized. Once the *ger* dies and the thief then comes forward with his admission, the debt comes into existence for the first time — but at that moment, the *ger* is gone. There is no *ger* to whom it can be owed; accordingly, *d'be'idna de'hode kano Hashem u'nesano l'Kohanim* — at the moment of his admission, the obligation vests in the *Kohanim* directly. God, so to speak, acquires it on behalf of the *Kohanim* at the instant the obligation arises. Had the admission come while the *ger* was alive, the debt would have been owed to the *ger*, and its extinction upon the *ger*'s subsequent death would have released the thief entirely. It is only the post-death admission that routes the obligation to the *Kohanim* from the outset.

## Female Ger Question

### Gender Restriction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבִינָא: גֶּזֶל הַגִּיּוֹרֶת, מַהוּ? ״אִישׁ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – וְלֹא אִשָּׁה, אוֹ דִלְמָא אוֹרְחֵיהּ דִּקְרָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** § **Ravina raises a dilemma:** With regard to payment for **robbery of a female convert, what is** the *halakha*? **The Merciful One states** in the Torah: "But if the **man** has no kinsman" (Numbers 5:8), indicating that the *halakha* applies to a male convert, **but not** to **a woman,** i.e., a female convert; if she dies after being robbed, payment is not given to the priests. **Or perhaps it is the manner of the verse** to employ masculine wording, but the *halakha* applies in the case of a female convert as well?

Ravina raises a final question: does the law of *gezel ha-ger* — including the provision that the stolen property goes to the *Kohanim* when there is no *go'el* — apply equally when one steals from a female convert, a *giyores*? The *pasuk* states *im ein la'ish go'el* — "if the man has no redeemer." The word *ish* might be read as deliberately limiting the law to male converts, constituting a *gzeiras ha-kasuv* that excludes women. Alternatively, *ish* may simply reflect the Torah's standard mode of address and carries no exclusionary force.

### Beraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַהֲרֹן לְרָבִינָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישׁ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ; אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״הַמּוּשָׁב״ – הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aharon said to Ravina: Come** and **hear** a solution to your dilemma, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that the verse states in its entirety: "But if the **man** has no kinsman to whom restitution may be made for the guilt, the restitution for guilt that is made shall be the Lord's, even the priest's; besides the ram of the atonement, whereby atonement shall be made for him." **I have** derived **only** that it is referring to **a man; from where** is it derived that this applies equally to **a woman? When it states:** "The restitution for guilt **that is made," there are two** instances of the phrase "that is made" **here,** as the verse mentions making restitution twice, to include a female convert in this *halakha*.

Rav Aaron — a notably rare name in the *gemara* — directs Ravina to a *beraita* that resolves the question. The *beraita* begins: *ish ein li ella ish* — from the word *ish* I know only a man. *Isha minayin* — how do I derive a woman? The *beraita* resolves this from the phrase *ha'asham ha'mushav*, "the guilt-offering that is returned" — the superfluous word *ha'mushav* extends the rule to additional cases, including a female convert. The inclusion is thus derived from the textual expansion, not from the word *ish*, which is then available to teach something else.

### Minor Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״אִישׁ״? אִישׁ אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר אַחֲרָיו אִם יֵשׁ לוֹ גּוֹאֲלִים אִם לָאו, קָטָן אִי אַתָּה צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר אַחֲרָיו; בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁאֵין לוֹ גּוֹאֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states** specifically **"man"?** The *baraita* explains: To pay a convert who is **a man, you need to investigate concerning him** to determine **if he has a kinsman or not,** but to pay a convert who is **a minor you do not need to investigate concerning him. It is known that he has no kinsman,** since as a convert he has no kin among his birth family, and as a minor he does not have children.

The *beraita* then asks: if the law already applies to women by virtue of *ha'mushav*, what is the purpose of the word *ish* at all? The *beraita* explains: the word *ish* teaches that when the deceased *ger* was an adult male, an investigation must be conducted to determine whether he left any heirs. Since an adult man may have fathered children, one cannot assume there is no *go'el* without inquiry. A minor, however, requires no such investigation — it is inherently certain that a minor could not have fathered children, and therefore there is no need to search for potential heirs. The stolen property goes directly to the *Kohanim* without further inquiry.
