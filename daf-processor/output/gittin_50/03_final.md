# Gittin 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketubah & Tikkun Olam

### Prior Discussion Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיבּוּרִית. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא מִיַּתְמֵי, אֲבָל מִינֵּיהּ דִּידֵיהּ – בְּבֵינוֹנִית.
>
> **Translation:** Payment of **a woman's marriage contract** is collected **from inferior-quality land. Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said: We said** that a woman collects her marriage contract from inferior-quality land **only** when her husband died and she collects payment **from** the **orphans** who inherit his estate. **But** if she was divorced and she collects payment **from** the husband **himself,** then she collects it **from intermediate-quality land.**

The *daf* opens in the midst of a broader discussion about *tikkun olam* and the question of whether a *ketubah* is collected from grade A, grade B, or grade C property. The *mishnah* had established that a woman collects her *ketubah* from *ziboris* — grade C, the lowest quality land. The reason offered is to incentivize men to marry: since the husband bears the substantial financial obligations of the *ketubah*, the Rabbis made the collection somewhat less burdensome by limiting it to the least valuable property. However, Mar Zutra had proposed a much narrower reading of the *mishnah*, arguing that the limitation to *ziboris* applies only when the woman collects from *yesomim* — orphans — where grade C collection is the norm in any case. According to Mar Zutra, when the woman collects from the husband himself in a case of divorce, she collects from grade B, *beinonis*.

The *gemara* is attempting to resolve this debate. In the course of doing so, one proposed explanation of a problematic *braitah* was that the *mishnah* was not speaking of the woman collecting from the principal debtor — the husband or his orphaned heirs — but rather from an *areiv*, a guarantor. This explanation opened a significant digression into the laws of guarantors and their obligations, which the *gemara* now pursues at length before returning to the central question.

### Areiv & Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דְּעָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה לֹא מִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But let him derive** this *halakha* from another *halakha* that states that **the guarantor** of a marriage contract **does not become responsible** for the payment of the marriage contract from his own property.

The *gemara* on the bottom of 49b raises an immediate difficulty with explaining the *mishnah* as a case of collecting from a guarantor: *v'teipuk lei d'areiv d'ketubah lo mishtabeid* — a guarantor of a *ketubah* does not become legally obligated to pay. The reason goes to the heart of what creates a guarantor's liability. In an ordinary loan, when a person agrees to serve as guarantor, the lender actually extends money to the borrower in reliance on the guarantor's word. Because actual funds changed hands based on the guarantor's commitment, the guarantor acquires a genuine sense of indebtedness — it is as though the money passed through him. That sense of real obligation, of *gemirat da'at*, is what makes the guarantee legally binding.

### No Money Exchange

By contrast, a *ketubah* involves no transfer of money at all. The husband simply obligates himself in the abstract; the wife does not hand over funds that the husband promises to return. Since no money is disbursed based on the guarantor's word, the mechanism that would ordinarily create a binding guarantee is entirely absent. One might argue that the couple agreed to marry on the guarantor's say-so, and that this ought to suffice — but the Rabbis hold that even if the marriage arguably would not have taken place without the guarantor's assurance, the specific financial obligation of the *ketubah* is not something that came into being through any concrete monetary act dependent on the guarantor's word. Accordingly, a simple *areiv* of a *ketubah* does not become legally indebted.

## Areiv vs. Kablan

### Kablan on Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּקַבְּלָן.
>
> **Translation:** We are dealing here **with an unconditional guarantor,** i.e., one who accepted unconditional responsibility for the obligation, allowing his daughter-in-law to collect payment of her marriage contract either from his son or from him, as she so desires.

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by distinguishing between an *areiv* and a *kablan*. The word *kablan* derives from *lekabel*, to receive — it denotes a guarantor who actually takes the money himself before passing it on to the debtor. In an ordinary loan, this means that the lender gives the funds directly to the *kablan*, who in turn transfers them to the borrower. Rashi explains how a *kablan* can be created even in the context of a *ketubah*, despite the absence of any actual debt being incurred at the time of the marriage.

### Ritual Pre-Payment

Rashi's formulation (*she'na'aseh lah kablan she'tafsah bo mataltelin le'chtubatah ve'hi masartam le'yado be'torat kablanut*) describes a deliberate ritual arrangement. The prospective husband gives his bride a sum of money as a pre-payment against her *ketubah* obligation. She holds this sum momentarily and then, rather than returning it directly to the groom, transfers it to the intended *kablan* — who receives it on behalf of the loan arrangement and then passes it to the groom. Through this process, the *kablan* has physically received money. He is no longer a mere promissory guarantor; he has accepted the funds, and the debt now rests on him. This transforms his status into a *kablan* and creates the legal obligation that a simple *areiv* would lack. The ritual may seem formal, but it accomplishes something real in *halachah*: money has genuinely changed hands through the *kablan*'s possession.

### Kablan in Loans

In the context of an ordinary loan, the *kablan* arrangement is more straightforward. The lender, rather than trusting the borrower, gives the money directly to the *kablan*, who then transfers it to the borrower. The lender's money moved not on the borrower's account but on the *kablan*'s account; the *kablan* is the one who first received the funds and is therefore the primary obligant in a meaningful sense.

### Creditor Collection

Indeed, there are opinions that precisely because the *kablan* is the one who directly received the money, the creditor may go straight to the *kablan* to collect — without first being required to attempt collection from the borrower himself. Tosafot engages with this point and works through the implications of the *kablan*'s status as the primary recipient.

## Kablan Obligation Debate

### No Property Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: קַבְּלָן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ נִכְסֵי לְלֹוֶה – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, שַׁפִּיר; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, אִי לֵית לֵיהּ – לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **This works out well according to the one who says** that **an unconditional guarantor becomes responsible** for repayment of the loan **even if the borrower does not have property** of his own. Therefore, it works out **well** to explain the case in this manner. **But according to the one who says** that **if** the borrower **has** property of his own at the time of the loan, then the unconditional guarantor **becomes responsible,** but **if** the borrower **does not have** property of his own at the time of the loan, then the unconditional guarantor **does not become responsible, what is there to say** in a case where the son did not have any property at the time of the marriage? In such a case, the father never became responsible for his son's obligations.

Having established the *kablan* framework, the *gemara* turns to a further complication. It poses the question: *hani nicha le'man de'amar kablan af al gav de'leit lei nichsei le'loveh mishta'abed shapir* — this works out nicely according to the opinion that holds a *kablan* obligates himself even when the original borrower has no property at the time of the loan. The woman then cannot collect from the orphans because the estate is insufficient, so she turns to the *kablan* — and according to this view he is bound regardless of the borrower's financial state at the time.

But according to the other opinion — *ei it lei mishta'abed, ei leit lei lo mishta'abed* — a *kablan* only obligates himself if the original borrower had property at the time the loan was made. If the borrower had no property, the *kablan*'s commitment is treated as a kind of *asmachta*, an overreach: since there is no realistic expectation that the borrower will ever repay, the *kablan* cannot truly have committed himself with full seriousness — it is too extreme an obligation for him to have genuinely meant it. Under this view, how can the *mishnah*'s scenario hold? If the borrower had property, why is the woman going to the *kablan* rather than directly to the orphans?

### Property at Loan Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּדַהֲווֹ לֵיהּ וְאִישְׁתְּדוּף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that it is a case **where** the son **had** property of his own at the outset, **but** afterward **it was blighted.**

The *gemara* answers simply: *eima d'hava lei v'ishtadif* — he had property at the time of the loan but it subsequently deteriorated or was lost, leaving insufficient funds in the estate. This is precisely the normal function of a guarantor: the obligation was undertaken when the principal debtor was solvent, and the guarantee becomes operative when the estate can no longer cover the debt. The woman therefore comes to the *kablan* not because there was never any property, but because what was there has since dissipated.

### Woman & Guarantor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כֹּל לְגַבֵּי בְּרֵיהּ, שַׁעְבּוֹדֵי מְשַׁעְבַּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish say:** With respect to **anything relating to his son,** it is common for a father to **pledge himself** absolutely, even when the son has no property of his own.

With this in place, the *mishnah*'s scenario is coherently reconstructed. The woman is collecting her *ketubah* not directly from the orphans — which would ordinarily be limited to *ziboris* — but from a *kablan* who had been arranged at the time of the marriage. The husband had property when the *kablan* took on his obligation, that property has since diminished, and the woman now turns to the *kablan* for collection.

### Choson's Father

The *gemara* then offers an alternative explanation: *v'iba'is eima, kol legabei breih shibudei m'shabeid nafshieh* — one may alternatively say that the guarantor in the *mishnah*'s scenario is the groom's father. Even if one holds that a *kablan* only obligates himself when the borrower has property, a father is willing to go further for his son's sake. A father obligates himself with greater seriousness and completeness than an ordinary guarantor would, and therefore his guarantee holds even in situations where an ordinary guarantor might not be bound.

## Areiv/Kablan Analysis

### Areiv Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: עָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד; קַבְּלָן דְּבַעַל חוֹב – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד; עָרֵב דְּבַעַל חוֹב, וְקַבְּלָן דִּכְתוּבָּה – פְּלִיגִי;
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos a statement attributed to Mar Zutra, the Gemara cites another *halakha* in his name on a similar topic. It **was stated** that **everyone agrees** that **a guarantor** who signs **a marriage contract does not** thereby **become responsible** for the payment of the marriage contract from his own property. **Everyone** also **agrees that an unconditional guarantor for** the repayment of **a creditor becomes responsible** for payment of the borrower's debt. The Sages **disagree** with regard to the following cases: **A guarantor for** the repayment of **a creditor and an unconditional guarantor for** payment of **a marriage contract.**

The *gemara* now presents a comprehensive analytical framework, working through the four possible combinations of the two relevant factors: whether money changed hands (*ba'al chov*) and whether the guarantor is a *kablan* (one who directly received the funds) or merely an *areiv* (one who did not). A simple *areiv* of a *ketubah* — *areiv d'kesubah divrei hakol lo mishta'abeid* — does not obligate himself under any view. Both factors that would create a genuine legal indebtedness are absent: no money was actually given out on the guarantor's word, and the guarantor never received any funds himself. The *ketubah* obligation arose entirely in the abstract, through the marriage arrangement, not through any concrete monetary act.

### Kablan Loan

At the opposite extreme, a *kablan* of an ordinary loan — *kablan d'ba'al chov, divrei hakol mishta'abeid* — obligates himself under every view. Here both positive factors converge: the creditor actually disbursed funds based on the guarantor's involvement, and the *kablan* himself was the one who first received those funds before passing them to the borrower. With both factors present, there is no question of *asmachta* or insufficient *gemirat da'at*; the obligation is fully formed.

### Middle Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ נִכְסֵי לְלֹוֶה – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, וְאִיכָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, אִי לֵית לֵיהּ – לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** There is one** Sage **who says: Although the borrower does not have property** of his own at the time of the loan, the guarantor **becomes responsible** for payment of the obligation. **And there is one** Sage **who says: If** the obligated party **has** property of his own, then the guarantor **becomes responsible; if he does not have** property of his own, **he does not become responsible.**

The two intermediate cases — an *areiv* of an ordinary loan and a *kablan* of a *ketubah* — each possess only one of the two factors, and these are where the debate arises. The *areiv d'ba'al chov* has the positive factor that real money was lent on his word, creating the creditor's reliance that grounds an obligation. But the *areiv* himself never received the funds. The *kablan d'kesubah*, set up through the ritual described by Rashi, has the positive factor that the *kablan* directly received money during the arrangement — but the underlying situation is a *ketubah*, meaning that ultimately no party is genuinely out of pocket in any lasting way.

For these middle cases, there is a debate: some hold that a *kablan* or *areiv* in such cases obligates himself even when the original debtor has no property, while others hold that the obligation only arises when the debtor had property at the time — reasoning that without such property the commitment takes on the character of an *asmachta*, a not-quite-serious undertaking. The logic may seem counterintuitive — surely if the debtor has no resources the *kablan* should be all the more firmly obligated, since everyone knows collection will fall on him — but the Rabbis treat a guarantee in such extreme circumstances as lacking the requisite *gemar da'at* to constitute a true legal undertaking.

### Halacha on Kablan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִילְכְתָא: בְּכוּלְּהוּ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד; בַּר מֵעָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית לֵיהּ – לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *halakha*** is: **In all of** these cases, **although** the obligated party **does not have** property of his own at the time he assumes the obligation, the guarantor **becomes responsible** for payment of that obligation. This is so **except in** the case of a **guarantor for** payment of **a marriage contract, as** in that case, **even if** the husband **has** property of his own when he gives a marriage contract, the guarantor **does not become responsible** for payment of the obligation.

The *halachah* resolves this: *v'hilchata b'chulhu, af al gav d'leit lei mishta'abeid* — in all cases where at least one of the two factors is present, the *kablan* or *areiv* obligates himself even if the original debtor had no property at the time.

### Areiv Ketubah Exception

The single exception, as already established, is the *areiv d'kesubah*, where neither factor is present. In that case, *d'af al gav d'ait lei lo mishta'abeid* — even if the original debtor does have property, the guarantor does not incur any legal obligation. Without either the concrete monetary reliance of the creditor or the direct receipt of funds by the guarantor, no legally binding debt can be created.

### Tosafot & Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? מִצְוָה הוּא דַּעֲבַד, וְלָא מִידֵּי חַסְּרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? The intention of the guarantor **is performing a mitzva** of facilitating a marriage by encouraging the woman to consent to the marriage as a result of his involvement, but he does not truly intend to obligate himself. **And** furthermore, the woman **does not lose** anything in exchange for which the guarantor would accept responsibility, as the husband does not borrow money from the woman. Therefore, the Sages instituted that the guarantor does not become responsible for payment of the marriage contract from his own property.

At this juncture the *gemara* adds an explanation that had not previously appeared in the analysis: *mitzvah hu d'avid v'lo midi chasrah* — the guarantor was merely performing a *mitzvah*, helping a couple get married, and no one was left financially worse off. This reason is presented as the rationale for why the *areiv d'kesubah* incurs no obligation.

Tosafot seizes on this and derives an important *chiddush*. Until this point the analysis had focused entirely on whether money changed hands and whether the *kablan* directly received funds. The introduction of the *mitzvah* factor opens a new dimension. Tosafot argues: *tartei ba'inan* — both conditions must be present to exempt the *areiv* from obligation. The absence of financial loss (*lo midi chasrah*) alone is not sufficient. If the guarantor makes his promise *after the marriage has already taken place* — *she'na'aseh areiv achar hanisusin* — then the *mitzvah* motivation is no longer operative. The couple is already married; no one is being encouraged toward the *mitzvah* of marriage by this promise. In that case, even though no money changed hands, the guarantor *does* obligate himself, because his word in that context cannot be explained away as mere *mitzvah*-encouragement. Conversely, the case where both factors are present — a post-marriage guarantee where money also changed hands — is obviously an obligation.

This is striking. Prior to this line in the *gemara* the entire framework rested on whether something concrete happened — money lent, money received — to create the sense of a real legal debt. According to Tosafot, a bare post-marriage promise, with no money changing hands at all, suffices to create legal obligation. This seems to be a significant *chiddush*, and one might find it difficult to derive from the *gemara*'s overall reasoning, which consistently required some concrete financial act to ground the obligation.

## Mishnah Resolution

### Incentive for Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: תָּא שְׁמַע מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּתַקַּנְתִּין – יוֹתֵר מִמַּה שֶּׁהָאִישׁ רוֹצֶה לִישָּׂא, אִשָּׁה רוֹצָה לִינָּשֵׂא. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ מִיַּתְמֵי, הַאי מִשּׁוּם דְּיַתְמֵי הוּא! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּמָר זוּטְרָא תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Come** and **hear** a proof concerning the question of whether the mishna's ruling that payment of a woman's marriage contract is collected from inferior-quality land applies only when the woman collects from orphans or if it applies even when she collects from her husband. The proof is **from the beginning of our ordinance:** Why did the Sages say that a woman's marriage contract is collected from inferior-quality land? It is because **more than a man wants to marry** a woman, **a woman wants to become married** to a man. **And if it enters your mind** that this *halakha* applies only when the woman collects **from orphans,** then the reason given for **this** *halakha* should be that it is **because they are orphans,** not because a woman wants to become married. This indicates that the mishna's ruling applies even when she collects her marriage contract during her husband's lifetime, contrary to the opinion of Mar Zutra. The Gemara affirms: **The refutation** of the opinion **of Mar Zutra is a conclusive refutation.**

After the extended digression through the laws of *areiv* and *kablan*, the *gemara* finally returns to the original question of whether Mar Zutra's position can be sustained. Ravina offers a conclusive proof: *ta shma — me'ikara de-takantin* — from the very rationale of the *takanah*. The *takanah* that a *ketubah* is collected from *ziboris*, grade C, was explained precisely because *yoser mi-mah she-ish rotzeh lisa, ishah rotzah lihinasei* — women are more eager to marry than men, and we must therefore incentivize men to take on the financial obligations of marriage by making those obligations less burdensome. If that is the established reason for the *takanah*, the entire logic presupposes that we are speaking of the man's obligation during his lifetime — what a man considers when deciding whether to assume the financial weight of marriage.

### From Husband Direct

The incentive rationale makes sense only when the *ketubah* is collected from the husband himself. It is what the husband anticipates paying that needs to be made manageable. The prospect of one's orphans someday paying *ziboris* rather than *beinonis* is not the calculation running through a man's mind when he contemplates marriage.

### Mar Zutra Refuted

Accordingly, *v'i salka da'atcha mi-yasmi, hai mishum de-yasmi hu* — if the only rationale for *ziboris* were the protection of orphans, then the reason given in the *gemara* — incentivizing men to marry — would be irrelevant. Since the *gemara* explicitly grounds the *takanah* in the incentive rationale, it follows that the *takanah* operates when collecting from the husband himself, not merely from orphans. *Tiyuvta d'Mar Zutra tiyuvta* — Mar Zutra's position is definitively refuted.

### Balance Protection

This resolution reflects a broader tension running through the entire topic of *kesuvot*. The institution of the *ketubah* represents a comprehensive system of protections for the wife — ongoing obligations during the marriage and a lump-sum payment upon dissolution through divorce or death. Yet the *gemara* recognizes that these protections cannot be made so onerous that they deter marriage altogether. The balance being struck is real and consequential: yes, the woman receives significant protection, but it is calibrated so as not to be so burdensome as to discourage men from assuming those obligations.

### Shidduch Crisis

This concern may strike modern readers as exaggerated, but there is contemporary evidence for how demographic and financial incentives genuinely affect marriage rates. Research into the so-called *shidduch* crisis has identified a clear demographic source: in much of the non-*chassidic* Orthodox community, men typically marry women two to three years younger. Combined with a positive birth rate, this means that for every hundred men seeking marriage partners in a given cohort, there are approximately 112 women in the younger cohort they are seeking to marry. The resulting imbalance creates real delays and missed matches. The *chassidic* world, where couples marry within their own age cohort, does not experience this phenomenon. The *gemara*'s concern about incentivizing men toward marriage is thus not a theoretical nicety — sometimes incentive structures genuinely matter, and the *takanah* limiting *ketubah* collection to *ziboris* reflects that awareness.

## Collection Orphans

### Note Grade A

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: שְׁטַר חוֹב הַיּוֹצֵא עַל הַיְּתוֹמִין, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ שֶׁבַח, אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos a statement attributed to Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, the Gemara cites another *halakha* in his name on a similar topic: **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Naḥman, said in the name of Rav Naḥman:** If **a promissory note is presented to orphans** for repayment of a loan taken by their father, then **although an enhancement** of the creditor's ability to collect, enabling the collection of even superior-value land, **is written in it,** the creditor **can collect only from inferior-quality land.**

The *gemara* now shifts to a new question brought by Mar Zutra son of Rav Nachman in the name of Rav Nachman, dealing specifically with collecting from orphans. The basic rule is that all collection from orphans is limited to *ziboris*, grade C — a protection for the orphans who have inherited their father's estate. The question raised is what happens when a *shtar chov*, a promissory note, explicitly granted the creditor a lien on the debtor's grade A property, *idis*. Does that explicit upgrade carry over to collection from orphans? The *halachah* rules clearly: *shtar chov ha-yotzeh al yesomim af al pi she-kasuv bo shevach, eino goveh ela min ha-ziboris* — even if the document specifies the highest-quality land, collection from orphans is limited to grade C regardless.

### Abaye Downgrade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תִּדַּע – דְּבַעַל חוֹב דִּינֵיהּ בְּבֵינוֹנִית, וּמִיַּתְמֵי בְּזִיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Know** that this is the case, **as** the ***halakha*** governing **a creditor** is that he collects **from intermediate-quality land, but from orphans** he collects only **from inferior-quality land.** If one cannot collect even from intermediate-quality land of orphans, all the more so one cannot collect from their superior-quality land.

Abaye offers a proof for this rule: *d'ba'al chov dinei b'veinonis, u-me'yasmi b'ziboris* — a normal creditor is entitled to grade B from the debtor himself, yet when collecting from orphans he is downgraded to grade C. By analogy, even someone who was explicitly upgraded to grade A in his document loses that upgrade when collecting from orphans and reverts to grade C. If the standard downgrade from B to C is applied to orphans, the same logic should apply to reduce grade A to grade C as well.

### Rava Torah Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! בַּעַל חוֹב דִּינֵיהּ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּזִיבּוּרִית! כִּדְעוּלָּא – דְּאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה, בַּעַל חוֹב בְּזִיבּוּרִית, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּחוּץ תַּעֲמֹד וְהָאִישׁ וְגוֹ׳״ – מָה דַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל אִישׁ לְהוֹצִיא, פָּחוּת שֶׁבַּכֵּלִים; וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ בַּעַל חוֹב בְּבֵינוֹנִית? כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּנְעוֹל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לֹוִין – וְגַבֵּי יַתְמֵי אוֹקְמוּהּ רַבָּנַן אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: How can** these two cases **be compared? By Torah law,** the ***halakha*** governing **a creditor** is that he collects **from inferior-quality land, in accordance with** the opinion **of Ulla, as Ulla says: By Torah law, a creditor** collects **from inferior-quality land, as it is stated: "You shall stand outside, and the man** to whom you have lent shall bring forth the pledge out to you" (Deuteronomy 24:11). **What would a man ordinarily take out** as a pledge? **The least** valuable **of** his **vessels.** This demonstrates that the creditor is entitled only to the least valuable of the debtor's vessels. Similarly, when he collects from land, he collects only from inferior-quality land. **And for what reason did** the Sages **say** that **a creditor** collects **from intermediate-quality land? So as not to lock the door before** potential **borrowers,** i.e., so that people do not refrain from extending loans. **And with regard to orphans, the Sages left** the *halakha* **as** it is stated **in the Torah.**

Rava challenges this analogy sharply: *hachi hashta?* — is this really a comparable case? To understand Rava's position, one must first understand why a normal creditor collects from orphans at grade C at all. Rava's analysis, following Ulla, begins from the Torah's baseline rule. Ulla establishes: *devar Torah ba'al chov b'ziboris* — from a Torah perspective, a creditor is only entitled to grade C even from the debtor himself. The source is the verse (*Devarim* 24:11): *ve-ha'ish asher attah nosheh bo yotzi elecha et ha'avot ha-chutzah* — the debtor himself brings out the collateral, and naturally, *mah darko shel ish l'hotzi — pachos she-bakelim* — he brings out his least valuable items. From this the Rabbis derived that the Torah's default entitlement for a creditor is the lowest-quality property.

Why then is a creditor normally entitled to grade B from a living debtor? *K'dei she-lo tina'el deles bifnei lovin* — to ensure that the door is not locked to borrowers, the Rabbis enacted that creditors collect grade B, creating sufficient incentive to lend money. This is particularly necessary in a *halachic* framework where interest is prohibited, loans are subject to *shemitah* cancellation, and lenders need additional assurance. But this upgrade from C to B is entirely a rabbinic *takanah* enacted for a policy reason.

### Explicit Lien Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָכָא, כֵּיוָן דְּדִינֵיהּ מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּעִידִּית, אֲפִילּוּ מִיַּתְמֵי נָמֵי בְּעִידִּית!
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** a stipulation enhancing the creditor's ability to collect was included in the promissory note. Therefore, **since by Torah law the *halakha*** governing this creditor is that he collects **from superior-quality land** as stipulated in the loan agreement, then **he should also** collect **from superior-quality land even** when collecting the debt **from orphans.**

With this foundation, Rava constructs his counter-argument. *Ve-gabei yasmi ukmi rabanan ad'oraita* — when it comes to orphans, the Rabbis simply restored the Torah's baseline: the rabbinic *takanah* that upgraded creditors to grade B was not applied to the case of orphans, and so the creditor falls back to the Torah's default of grade C. This is not a special protective enactment *for* orphans — it is simply the reversion to the underlying Torah law in the absence of the incentive-based rabbinic upgrade.

But Rava's critical point follows: *ella hacha, keivan d'dinei m'deoraisa b'idis* — in the case where it is written explicitly in the document that the debtor granted a lien on grade A property, the creditor's entitlement to grade A is not a rabbinic upgrade but a Torah-level right, because the debtor explicitly created that encumbrance. *Af me'yasmi nami b'idis* — therefore, when collecting from orphans, the creditor should be entitled to grade A even from them, since the right to grade A is grounded in the Torah and not in a rabbinic *takanah* that was withheld from the orphan context.

The distinction Rava is drawing is precise: when the creditor has grade B by virtue of the rabbinic *takanah*, orphans restore him to the Torah default of grade C. But when the creditor has grade A because the document explicitly created a Torah-level lien, there is nothing to revert from — he is already at the Torah baseline or above it, and that right persists even against orphans.

## Damages & Orphans

### Damages Grade C

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּלְרָבָא, וְהָתָנֵי אַבְרָם חוֹזָאָה: אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִין אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית, וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן נִיזָּקִין. וְהָא נִיזָּקִין – דִּינָן מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בְּעִידִּית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** this explanation of **Rava,** there is a difficulty: **Didn't** the Sage **Avram Ḥoza'a teach** a *baraita* that states: A father's debt **can be collected from the property of the orphans only from inferior-quality land?** The Gemara infers: **And** this ruling applies **even** if the property is collected for payment to **injured** parties. **But isn't** it true that **by Torah law** the ***halakha*** governing **injured** parties is that they collect **from superior-quality land?** This indicates that even when by Torah law collection should be from superior-quality land, the Sages instituted that one may collect from orphans only inferior-quality land.

The *gemara* presses Rava further: if his theory is correct that orphans merely restore us to the Torah baseline, then collection of *nezikin* — damages — from orphans should yield grade A, since the Torah entitles a *nizak* to collect *meitav sadehu*, the best quality field. Why does the *braitah* cited by Avram Choza — *ein nifroin min nechsei yesomim ela min ha-ziboris* — apply even to damage claims?

### Special Case Damage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה עִידִּית דְּנִיזָּק כְּזִיבּוּרִית דְּמַזִּיק;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what** case **are we dealing here** in the *baraita* taught by Avram Ḥoza'a? We are dealing with a case **where superior-quality land of the injured** party **is equal** in quality **to inferior-quality land of the one who caused the damage,** i.e., the orphans, and the one who caused the damage also has land of superior quality.

The *gemara* resolves this: *hacha be-mai askinan k'gon d'havya idis d'nizak k'ziboris d'maziq* — the *braitah* addresses a special case in which the best property of the *nizak* (the damaged party) is equivalent in value to the worst property of the *maziq* (the damaging party). In this scenario, the Torah entitlement of *meitav sadehu* actually translates to grade C of the *maziq*'s holdings, not grade A.

### Yishmael Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא בִּדְנִיזָּק שָׁיְימִינַן, וּמִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן בִּדְמַזִּיק – וְגַבֵּי יַתְמֵי אוֹקְמוּהּ רַבָּנַן אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *baraita* is taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, who says: By Torah law we appraise** the property **of the injured** party, and therefore the one who caused the damage can pay with his inferior-quality fields that are equal in quality to the superior-quality land owned by the injured party. **And for the betterment of the world, the Sages instituted** that we appraise the property **of the one who caused the damage,** and he pays with his superior-quality land. **But with regard to orphans, the Sages left** the *halakha* **as** it is stated **in the Torah;** they pay from their inferior-quality land that is equal in quality to superior-quality land belonging to the injured party. By contrast, a creditor who holds a promissory note stipulating that the debt can be collected from superior-quality land can collect superior-quality land even from orphans, as stipulated in the loan agreement.

This reading follows Rebbi Yishmael, who holds that *meitav sadehu* is assessed based on the *nizak*'s best property, not the *maziq*'s. Under this view, the *nizak* cannot collect property of higher quality than his own best field. If the *nizak*'s top-quality land is equivalent to the *maziq*'s lowest-quality land, then from a Torah perspective the *nizak* is entitled only to grade C of the *maziq*. The rabbinic *tikkun olam* enactment upgraded this to allow collection from grade A of the *maziq*, but *gabei yasmi okmuha rabanan ad'oraita* — for orphans, they reverted to the Torah baseline, which in this special case is grade C. The *braitah* thus does not refute Rava's general theory; it represents a narrow case where the Torah entitlement itself is grade C.

## Abaye vs. Rava

### Baraita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר נָיוֹתָאָה: אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנִּכְסֵי יְתוֹמִים אֶלָּא מִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית, וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן עִידִּית. מַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ הֵן עִידִּית״? לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דִּכְתִיב עִידִּית בִּשְׁטָרָא?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Eliezer Nayota'a teach** a *baraita* that states: A father's debt **can be collected from the property of the orphans only from inferior-quality land, even if it is superior-quality land?** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **Even if it is superior-quality land? Is it not** the following: **Although superior-quality land is written in the** promissory **note,** the debt can be collected from the orphans only from inferior-quality land.

Despite Rava's elegant explanations, the *gemara* continues to press. Rebbi Eliezer Nivta'ah's teaching states: *ein nifroin min nechsei yesomim ela min ha-ziboris — v'afilu hen idis* — you collect from orphans only from grade C, even if it is grade A property. The *gemara* reads this as apparently meaning: even if the document grants the creditor an explicit lien on grade A property, collection from orphans is still limited to grade C. This would be a direct refutation of Rava's position.

### Shafay Idis Redefined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; מַאי ״עִידִּית״ – שְׁפַאי עִידִּית, כִּדְרָבָא –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No, what** is the meaning of **superior-quality land?** Orphans can repay the debt even from **strips of superior-quality land** that due to their location cannot be cultivated or guarded and that consequently have a lower market value than inferior-quality land, **as** in the statement **of Rava.**

The *gemara* deflects the challenge through a reinterpretation: *mai idis? Shafay idis* — when the *braitah* says "even grade A," it does not mean explicitly-documented grade A liens. Rather, it refers to *shafay idis* — the marginal edges of an otherwise high-quality field, land situated near a river that is periodically flooded and is therefore actually grade C in value, despite being physically part of a top-grade estate. Tosafot explains: fertile land along a riverbank that is regularly inundated by overflow is essentially grazing land — it looks like part of a prime field but functions as grade C. This land, though nominally associated with an *idis* parcel, is reclassified as *ziboris*. The *braitah* is therefore saying: even when collecting from orphans, one may not take even the *shafay idis* of the estate at above its true grade C value. This interpretation preserves Rava's thesis intact.

## Damages & Orphans (II)

### Shafay Idis Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רָבָא: הִזִּיק זִיבּוּרִית – גּוֹבֶה מִן הָעִידִּית. שְׁפַאי עִידִּית, גּוֹבֶה מִן הַבֵּינוֹנִית. וְגַבֵּי יַתְמֵי אוֹקְמוּהּ רַבָּנַן אַדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rava says:** If **one damaged inferior-quality land,** the injured party **collects from superior-quality land.** The one who caused damage is penalized and made to pay from land of much higher quality than that which he damaged. If he damaged **strips of superior-quality land** that cannot be cultivated, the injured party **collects from intermediate-quality land,** as the land that was damaged is inferior in value even to inferior-quality land, and therefore it suffices if compensation is paid from intermediate-quality land. And why did Rabbi Eliezer Nayota'a teach that orphans may pay their father's debt from land of the lowest quality? **With regard to orphans, the Sages left** the *halakha* as it is written **in the Torah,** that a creditor collects from inferior-quality land.

Rava has his own distinct teaching about *shafay idis* in the context of damage law, which goes beyond the orphan discussion. If someone damaged grade C land (*ziboris*), the *nizak* ordinarily collects from grade A. But if the damaged land was *shafay idis* — the flooded margins of an *idis* field, which is in effect sub-grade-C, a kind of grade D — then *goveh min ha-beinonis*, the *nizak* collects only from grade B, not from grade A. The logic appears to be that the degraded nature of the damaged land correspondingly reduces the quality from which compensation is drawn. This ruling of Rava is striking — ordinarily a *nizak* always collects from the best property, even if it was the worst property that was damaged. The *shafay idis* case represents an exception whose underlying rationale is not entirely transparent, but Rava's position is clear.

The conclusion of the orphan discussion, however, remains as stated: all collection from orphans, whatever its Torah-level basis, is limited to *ziboris*. Rava's position that Torah-level entitlements are preserved against orphans is logically coherent, but the weight of the *baraisos* consistently pushes back toward a blanket grade C rule for orphans in all cases. Rava manages to reinterpret each challenge, but the plain reading of the *baraisos* consistently supports Abaye's position: regardless of what one is entitled to under Torah law, the Rabbis instituted a uniform *ziboris* rule for orphans.

## Adult vs. Minor

### Scope Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין. בָּעֵי רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: ״יְתוֹמִים״ שֶׁאָמְרוּ – קְטַנִּים, אוֹ אֲפִילּוּ גְּדוֹלִים?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one who owed money died and his children inherited his property, the father's debt **can be collected from the property of the orphans only from inferior-quality land. Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami raised a dilemma** with regard to the meaning of this statement: Do the **orphans of which they spoke** refer specifically to **minors or even** to **adults?**

The *gemara* now turns to a question raised by Rav Acha bar Adda: *mi yesomim she'amru — ketanim o afilu gedolim?* When the Rabbis said one collects from orphans only *ziboris*, were they speaking specifically of minor orphans, or does this apply even to adult orphans?

### Minors Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּקַּנְתָּא הִיא דַּעֲבוּד רַבָּנַן גַּבֵּי יַתְמֵי – לִקְטַנִּים עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן, לִגְדוֹלִים לָא עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן;
>
> **Translation:** Is **this an ordinance that the Sages instituted for** the benefit of the **orphans,** in which case it may be argued that **the Sages instituted** it **for minors** but **they did not institute** it **for adults?**

One approach: the Rabbis made this enactment to protect orphans, and minors are the paradigmatic case requiring protection. Their interests cannot be adequately safeguarded by themselves, and the *takanah* ensures they are not taken advantage of. Adults, by contrast, can manage their own affairs — once they reach majority they can appear in court, negotiate, and advocate for themselves. Under this reading, the *takanah* applies only to minors.

### Lack Incentive

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא מַסֵּיק אַדַּעְתָּא דְּמַלְוֶה דְּמִית לֹוֶה וְנָפְלִי נִכְסֵי קַמֵּי יַתְמֵי; דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ נוֹעֵל דֶּלֶת; הִלְכָּךְ אֲפִילּוּ גְּדוֹלִים נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Or, perhaps** the reason is different. The reason that a creditor collects from intermediate-quality land is so that the door not be locked before potential borrowers. Here, this is not a concern, **since it does not enter the mind of the creditor** that he should not lend money as perhaps **the borrower** will **die and** his **property will fall to the orphans. As** only if he were to consider this possibility, would it be **that this would lock the door** before potential borrowers. Consequently, the ordinance that a creditor collects from intermediate-quality land was instituted only in the standard case, where the creditor collects from the debtor himself, but not where he collects from the heirs. **Therefore,** the ordinance that a debt can be collected from the property of orphans only from inferior-quality land should apply **also** to **adult** orphans.

The alternative approach reframes the entire question. Perhaps the grade C rule for orphans is not a proactive protective *takanah* at all, but simply the absence of the incentive that normally upgrades creditors to grade B. The reasoning: a creditor's entitlement to grade B rests on the rabbinic desire to incentivize lending — *lo tina'el deles bifnei lovin*. But the creditor's calculation when deciding whether to lend money centers on the normal case of collection from a living debtor. The relatively rare scenario that the debtor will die before repaying and the creditor will have to collect from the estate was not the eventuality that the Rabbis needed to incentivize against. Therefore, for collection from an estate inherited by orphans, no upgrade was given — not as a protection for orphans per se, but simply because the *takanah* upgrading creditors to grade B was never extended to that scenario. Under this view, even adult orphans would be subject to grade C collection, because the question is not about their age or capacity for self-protection, but about whether the incentive-based upgrade applies to this type of collection at all.

### Damages Implication

If the rationale is the absence of a creditor incentive, however, a difficulty arises with the earlier *gemara* that applied grade C even to damage claims collected from orphans. Damage collection is not about incentivizing creditors — there is no *ba'al chov* seeking encouragement to lend money. If the grade C rule for orphans is merely the absence of an incentive enactment, it should not apply to *nezikin* at all. Yet the earlier discussion showed that even damage claims are limited to grade C when collected from orphans. This suggests the rule *is* a proactive protection of orphans, not merely an incentive absence — which would in turn support the view that it applies only to minor orphans.

### Oath Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי אַבָּיֵי קַשִּׁישָׁא: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁאָמְרוּ – גְּדוֹלִים, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר קְטַנִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear what Abaye the Elder taught:** With regard to **the orphans of which** the Sages **spoke,** the phrase is referring to **adults, and needless to say** it is referring also to **minors.**

The *gemara* attempts to resolve the question through a *tannaitic* teaching: *yesomim she'amru — gedolim, v'ein tzarich lomar ketanim* — the very orphans the Rabbis spoke of are adults, and this is certainly true of minors as well. The *gemara* challenges this proof, arguing that perhaps this rule was stated only with respect to the requirement of a *shevuah* — the oath a creditor must swear that the debt was never paid. When collecting from orphans, the creditor must swear that their father never discharged the debt. In that context, *degadol bemilei de'avuh kekatan dami* — even an adult, with respect to his father's financial dealings, is like a minor: he has no personal knowledge of whether the debt was paid, and therefore the *shevuah* requirement applies regardless of the orphan's age. But this logic is specific to the *shevuah* — perhaps when it comes to grade C property, the rule applies only to minor orphans.

### Halacha All Orphans

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁאָמְרוּ – גְּדוֹלִים, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר קְטַנִּים; בֵּין לִשְׁבוּעָה בֵּין לְזִיבּוּרִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha*** is: With regard to **the orphans of which** the Sages **spoke,** the phrase is referring to **adults, and needless to say** it is referring also to **minors, whether with regard to** the *halakha* that a debt can be collected from the property of orphans only with **an oath,** or **whether with regard to** the *halakha* that a debt can be collected from the property of orphans only from **inferior-quality land.**

The *gemara* rules: *v'hilchata — yesomim she'amru gedolim, v'ein tzarich lomar ketanim, bein le-shevuah bein le-ziboris* — the law applies to both adults and minors, both with respect to the oath and with respect to limiting collection to grade C property.

### Gemara Tension

The adoption of this ruling, however, creates a tension with the logic presented earlier in the *sugya*. If the grade C rule applies even to adult orphans, the explanation that it is merely the absence of a creditor incentive gains force — since the protection-of-orphans rationale would not naturally extend to adults who can protect themselves. But the earlier *gemara* applied grade C even to *nezikin* collected from orphans, which fits the proactive-protection theory, not the incentive-absence theory. These two *gemara* passages thus pull in different directions: the ruling that adults are included pushes toward the incentive-absence theory, while the application to damage claims pushes toward the proactive-protection theory. The two positions do not appear fully reconciled.

## Encumbered Property

### Sold Property Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נִפְרָעִין מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Payment of a debt or other obligation **is not collected from liened property** that has been sold to a third party **when** the debtor still **has unsold property, even** when this unsold property is inferior-quality land.

The *mishnah* establishes: *ein nifra'in mi-nechasim meshubadim b'makom she-yesh bnei chorin* — one may not collect from encumbered property that has been sold to a third party as long as the debtor still has free assets available. Even if the creditor is entitled to grade B property and the debtor sold off all his grade B land, the creditor may not pursue the purchasers while the debtor still holds grade C assets. The creditor must exhaust the debtor's own holdings first.

### Gift Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: בְּמַתָּנָה הֵיאַךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥadevoi bar Ami raised a dilemma: What** is the *halakha* **with regard to** liened property that the debtor gave to another person as **a gift?** Is it the *halakha* that the debt is not collected from liened property that has been given as a gift to a third party when the debtor still has property?

The question then raised is whether this rule extends to gifts. The case of a sale is governed by clear policy logic: a creditor cannot pursue a buyer's land as long as other assets remain. But does the same protection extend to someone who received land as a *matanah*, a gift?

### Buyer Protection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּקַּנְתָּא הוּא דַּעֲבוּד רַבָּנַן – מִשּׁוּם פְּסֵידָא דְלָקוֹחוֹת, אֲבָל מַתָּנָה, דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא דְלָקוֹחוֹת – לָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents the two sides to this dilemma: Is the *halakha* that payment is not collected from liened property that has been sold **an ordinance that the Sages instituted due to the loss of the buyers,** who would lose the money that they had paid for the property? **But** in the case of **a gift, where there is no** issue of **loss to the buyers,** i.e., the recipients, as the recipients paid nothing for the property, the *halakha* does **not** apply.

One approach holds that the protection was enacted specifically for buyers: *takanata hi d'avdu rabanan mishum psida d'lekochot* — the Rabbis were concerned that commerce would be undermined if purchasers could not rely on the security of their acquisitions. People must be willing to buy land, and they will only do so if they know the seller's creditors cannot come after them except as a last resort. This rationale applies to buyers, but a recipient of a gift has paid nothing and has no comparable reliance interest. Perhaps the Rabbis saw no need to protect gift recipients from having their received property seized by a creditor.

### Both Protected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא מַתָּנָה נָמֵי, אִי לָאו דְּאִית לֵיהּ הֲנָאָה מִינֵּיהּ – לָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ מַתָּנָה, וְהִלְכָּךְ כִּי פְּסֵידָא דְלָקוֹחוֹת דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps** the *halakha* applies to **a gift as well, as, were it not for** some **benefit** that the donor derives **from** the recipient, **he would not have given** him **the gift. And therefore** this loss to the recipient **is considered** to be **like the loss to buyers** and payment of the debt is not collected from the recipient of the gift when the debtor still has property in his possession.

The alternative holds that gifts too are protected: *matanah nami* — even a gift is an institution the Rabbis wish to preserve.

### Gift Institution

The reasoning is that gift-giving is not truly random or gratuitous: *i lav d'is lei hana'ah minei lo yehav lei matanah* — one does not give a gift without some benefit or relationship that motivated the giving. Whether through friendship, gratitude, or some form of mutual relationship, gifts express something real in the social fabric. The institution of gift-giving, like the institution of commerce, is something the Rabbis want to protect. If recipients of gifts cannot rely on keeping what they received, the willingness to give gifts — and the social bonds that gifts express — would be undermined. Therefore, even a gift recipient is protected by the rule that the creditor must collect from the debtor's remaining assets before seizing encumbered property, whether that property was sold or given away.

## Deathbed Gifts

### Proportional Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, תָּא שְׁמַע, שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר: ״תְּנוּ מָאתַיִם זוּז לִפְלוֹנִי, ושְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת לִפְלוֹנִי״ – אֵין אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בִּשְׁטָר זָכָה; לְפִיכָךְ, יָצָא עָלָיו שְׁטַר חוֹב – גּוֹבֶה מִכּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Batra* 9:6): If **a person on his deathbed said: Give two hundred dinars to so-and-so, and three hundred to so-and-so, and four hundred to so-and-so,** in this case one **does not say** that **whoever appears first in the deed acquires** his money first. **Therefore,** if **a promissory note emerged against** the one who gave the gifts, and it becomes clear that the money given was pledged to a creditor, then the creditor **collects from all of them.**

Mar Kashisha son of Rav Chisda raises a *braitah* concerning a *shchiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — who instructs that specific sums be distributed to multiple beneficiaries: 200 zuz to Reuven, 300 to Shimon, 400 to Levi. The estate, however, contains only 700 zuz, insufficient to cover the total of 900. The rule: *ein omrim kol ha-kodem ba-shtar zachah* — one does not apply a first-come-first-served rule simply because one beneficiary was mentioned before another. All three distributions were declared simultaneously, and we cannot treat the order of speaking as creating priority. Accordingly, the 700 zuz is divided proportionally: Rashi explains this as dividing the estate into nine units, with Reuven receiving two-ninths, Shimon three-ninths, and Levi four-ninths.

### Debt Effect All

If a creditor then comes forward with a *shtar chov* — a promissory note establishing that the deceased owed him money — the debt is deducted from the total estate before distribution. All three beneficiaries lose proportionately, since they share the estate on equal footing from the moment of the declaration.

### Sequential Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִם אָמַר: ״תְּנוּ מָאתַיִם זוּז לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַחֲרָיו לִפְלוֹנִי, וְאַחֲרָיו לִפְלוֹנִי״ – אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל הַקּוֹדֵם בִּשְׁטָר זָכָה. לְפִיכָךְ, יָצָא עָלָיו שְׁטַר חוֹב – גּוֹבֶה מִן הָאַחֲרוֹן. אֵין לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִשֶּׁלְּפָנָיו. אֵין לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִשֶּׁלִּפְנֵי פָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the person on his deathbed **said: Give two hundred dinars to so-and-so, and after him to so-and-so, and after him to so-and-so,** then one **says: Anyone who appears first in the deed acquires** his money first. **Therefore,** if **a promissory note emerged against him,** the creditor first **collects from the last** one of the recipients. If **he does not have** enough to repay the debt, **he collects from the previous** person. If **he does not have** enough to repay the debt, **he collects from** the person listed **before the previous** person.

The rule changes if the deathbed declaration included the word *acharav* — "after him." If the dying person said: give 200 to Reuven, and after him give 300 to Shimon, and after him give 400 to Levi — *omrim kol ha-kodem ba-shtar zachah* — here priority does apply. Reuven receives his full 200 zuz first; Shimon receives his 300 from whatever remains; Levi receives his 400 from whatever remains after that.

### Debt Last Only

When a creditor presents a *shtar chov* in this sequential case, the debt is deducted from the end of the line. Only Levi, the last-named beneficiary, loses out: since the sequential structure means we work backward, and since the estate is treated as having been encumbered from the deceased's lifetime, it is as though Levi's share was always the most vulnerable. The logic is that the prior beneficiaries had already received their portions sequentially, and it is Levi's final share that bears the shortfall.

### Backup Collection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּקַמָּא בֵּינוֹנִית וּבָתְרָא זִיבּוּרִית – מִזִּיבּוּרִית גָּבֵי, מִבֵּינוֹנִית לָא גָּבֵי. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, בְּמַתָּנָה נָמֵי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara infers: **And** this is the *halakha* **even if** the property given **to the first** recipient is **intermediate-quality land and** the property given to **the last** recipient is **inferior-quality land.** Although a creditor is entitled to collect his debt from intermediate-quality land, the creditor in this case **collects from inferior-quality land,** as he collects from the last person to receive his gift, and **he does not collect from the intermediate-quality land** given to the first recipient. **Conclude from** the *baraita* that **the Sages also instituted** this **ordinance in** the case of **a gift,** and that payment of a debt is not collected from liened property that has been given as a gift to a third party when the debtor still has property that has not been sold or given away as a gift. The reason is that the second gift is considered to be unsold property in relation to the first gift because, when the first gift was given, the second gift was still in the donor's possession.

If Levi's portion is entirely exhausted and still does not cover the debt, the creditor then goes back to Shimon — *goveh mishi-lefanav*. If Shimon's portion is also insufficient, the creditor proceeds back to Reuven. The sequential priority cuts both ways: first priority in receiving, but also last exposure to the creditor — working through the sequence in reverse when collection is needed.

### Baraita Evidence

The *gemara* cites this *braitah* as evidence bearing on the earlier question of whether gift recipients are protected from creditors in the same way purchasers are. Since the *shchiv mera* scenario involves gifts — the dying person is distributing his estate as gifts to the beneficiaries — and the *braitah* discusses how creditors collect from those gifts, the *braitah*'s structure reveals assumptions about when and how *matanah* recipients can be reached by creditors. The full implications of this *braitah* for the gift-protection question will be explored in the continuation of the *sugya*.
