# Eruvin 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wall Between Courtyards

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה וְרוֹחַב אַרְבָּעָה — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **a wall between two courtyards is ten** handbreadths **high and four** handbreadths **wide,** the residents of the courtyard **establish two *eiruvin*,** a separate one for each courtyard, **but they** may **not establish one *eiruv*.**

Daf 77 continues the geometrically rich new *perek* that began on the previous daf. The *mishna* at the bottom of 76b takes up a scenario that the earlier discussion only touched upon: the status of a wall standing between two distinct courtyards and the halachic implications of its dimensions, its surface, and its relationship to the courtyards on either side.

### Wall Dimensions

The *mishna* opens with *kotel shebein shtei chatzerot gavoha asara verochav arba* — a wall between two courtyards that is ten *tefachim* high and four *tefachim* wide. The height of ten *tefachim* already ensures that the two courtyards are fully separated: without a properly sized window or door, they cannot be unified under a single *eruv* even if both parties wish it. But the wall's additional width of four *tefachim* gives it an independent halachic identity: it is a *reshut hayachid* in its own right, a four-by-four domain standing between the two courtyards.

### Courtyard Separation

Because the wall is ten *tefachim* high with no legitimate opening, the *mishna* rules *me'arvin shenayim ve'ein me'arvin echad*: the two courtyards must each maintain a separate *eruv* and cannot be treated as one. This ruling is independent of the wall's width; the sheer height without a proper aperture is sufficient to mandate separation.

### Fruit on Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ פֵּירוֹת — אֵלּוּ עוֹלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין, וְאֵלּוּ עוֹלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יוֹרִידוּ לְמַטָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **If there was produce on top** of the wall, **these** residents of one courtyard **may ascend from this side and eat** from it, **and those** residents of the other courtyard **may ascend from that side and eat** from it, **provided that they do not lower** the produce **down** from on top of the wall to one of the courtyards.

Given that the wall constitutes its own *reshut hayachid*, the *mishna* turns to the question of fruit resting on top of it. The ruling is *eilu olim mikan ve'ochlim ve'eilu olim mikan ve'ochlim*: residents of either courtyard may ascend to the top of the wall and eat there. The surface of the wall is its own domain, and carrying within that domain — among objects already located there — is entirely permitted. One may stand on top of the wall and consume fruit that was already there; this raises no *shabbat* carrying concern so long as the fruit remains on the wall's surface.

### Wall Carrying Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִפְרְצָה הַכּוֹתֶל, עַד עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת — מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם, וְאִם רָצוּ מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּפֶתַח. יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן — מְעָרְבִין אֶחָד וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **If the wall was breached,** the following distinction applies: If the breach was **up to ten cubits** wide, **they establish two *eiruvin*, and if they desire, they** may **establish one *eiruv*, as it is similar to an entrance,** like any opening less than ten cubits wide. If the breach was **more than this, they establish one *eiruv*, and they** may **not establish two,** as a breach of this size nullifies the partition and joins the two courtyards into a single domain.

What is forbidden, however, is *yaridu lemata* — bringing those items down from the wall into either courtyard. The wall is a distinct *reshut* from each of the adjoining courtyards, and since no joint *eruv* has been established between the wall and either courtyard, carrying from one to the other is prohibited. The wall becomes, in effect, a no-man's-land: each courtyard has equal and symmetrical access to its top, but neither may transfer items to or from it. The *gemara* notes that this outcome is not entirely self-evident; as we shall see, there are scenarios in which the wall becomes more closely associated with one courtyard than the other, and that asymmetry has halachic consequences.

## Wall Breaches

### Ten Amah Breach

The *mishna* now turns from the wall's dimensions to its integrity. *Nifratz hakotel ad eser amot* — if a breach opens in the wall of up to ten *amot* (approximately fifteen feet), the ruling is *me'arvin shenayim ve'im ratzu me'arvin echad*: the two courtyards may still maintain separate *eruvin*, but if they wish they may also be treated as one. The reasoning is *mipnei shehu ke-petach* — a gap of up to ten *amot* is halachically comparable to a doorway, and a doorway creates an option without imposing an obligation.

### Destroyed Wall

If the breach exceeds ten *amot*, the calculus reverses entirely: *me'arvin echad ve'ein me'arvin shenayim*. The wall no longer functions as a wall. The two courtyards are treated as one and cannot be separated for *eruv* purposes.

### Ten Amah Limit

This ten-*amah* threshold carries significant weight beyond the immediate context of the *mishna*. The question of how large a gap may be in the walls enclosing a *reshut hayachid* was addressed already in the first *perek*, and the same principle applies here. The Rambam rules that even a *tzurat hapetach* cannot close a gap larger than ten *amot*, which would require a *tzurat hapetach* every fifteen feet in any city *eruv* — a position that no contemporary urban *eruv* follows. That dispute, however, belongs to the earlier *perek*; in the present *mishna*, the point is simply that a breach beyond ten *amot* destroys the wall's status as a divider and forces the courtyards into a single unit.

## Thin Walls Debate

### Non-Independent Domain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה מַאי? אָמַר רַב: אֲוִיר שְׁתֵּי רְשׁוּיוֹת שׁוֹלֶטֶת בּוֹ, לֹא יָזִיז בּוֹ אֲפִילּוּ מְלֹא נִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: If this wall **is not four** handbreadths in width, **what** is the *halakha*? **Rav said:** In this case, **the air of two domains controls it.** Since the wall is not broad enough to be regarded a domain of its own, the top of the wall is seen as belonging to both courtyards and is then prohibited to both of them. Accordingly, **one may not move** anything **on** top of the wall, **even** as much as **a hair's breadth.**

The *gemara* now introduces a new scenario: *ein bo arba amos* — what if the wall between the two courtyards is not four *tefachim* wide? Such a wall is ten *tefachim* high and therefore still divides the courtyards, but without the four-by-four dimension it cannot constitute its own *reshut hayachid*. In the language familiar from earlier discussions, it would normally be classified as a *makom patur*. The question is what legal status it carries in this context.

### Rav's Position

Rav rules: *avir shtei reshuyot sholet bo, lo yaziz bo afilu malei nima*. Because this thin wall is not an independent domain, it is considered contested territory — claimed simultaneously by courtyard A and by courtyard B. Each courtyard asserts ownership, neither has exclusive rights, and no *eruv* unites them. The consequence is total prohibition: one may not move so much as a hairsbreadth on top of this wall. Courtyard A cannot use it because B also lays claim to it, and vice versa.

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵלּוּ מַעֲלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין, וְאֵלּוּ מַעֲלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: These** residents of one courtyard **may raise** food **from** their courtyard to the top of the wall **and eat** it there, and they may lower the food from the wall to the courtyard; **and those** residents of the other courtyard **may raise** food **from** their courtyard **and eat** it there, and they may lower the food from the wall to the courtyard. This is because the wall is considered nonexistent, and its domain is viewed as part of the two courtyards.

Rabbi Yochanan takes the opposite view: *eilu maalin mikan ve'ochlim ve'eilu maalin mikan ve'ochlim*. Precisely because the thin wall is not an independent domain, it is *shayach* — connected — to both courtyards simultaneously. Rather than being forbidden to both, it is available to both. When courtyard A wishes to use it, the wall functions as an extension of A; when B wishes to use it, it functions as an extension of B. And crucially, this is not merely passive access: the term is *maalin*, not merely *olim*, meaning one may actively transfer items between the courtyard and the wall in either direction.

### Olim vs. Maalin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אֵלּוּ עוֹלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין, וְאֵלּוּ עוֹלִין מִכָּאן וְאוֹכְלִין. עוֹלִין — אִין, מַעֲלִין — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If there was produce on top of the wall, **these** residents of one courtyard **may ascend from this side and eat** from it, **and those** residents of the other courtyard **may ascend from that side and eat** from it. The Gemara infers from this: To **ascend, yes,** it is permitted, but to **raise** food from the courtyard to the top of the wall, **no,** it is not permitted. This presents a challenge to Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion.

The *gemara* notes the precise terminological distinction between the *mishna* and Rabbi Yochanan's ruling. The *mishna*, dealing with a four-by-four wall that *is* its own *reshut hayachid*, uses the term *olim* — one may *go up* to the wall and eat there. Transfer of items between the courtyard and the wall is not permitted. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, speaks of *maalin* — one may *bring up* items to the wall, or bring them down from it. The *mishna*'s *olim* without *maalin* thus belongs to the case of the four-by-four wall, while Rabbi Yochanan's *maalin* applies to the thin wall that lacks independent domain status.

### Four-by-Four Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: יֵשׁ בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה, עוֹלִין — אִין, מַעֲלִין — לָא. אֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה — מַעֲלִין נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this is what** the mishna **is saying:** If the top of the wall between the two courtyards **is four by four** handbreadths, then to **ascend, yes,** it is permitted. However, to **raise** food, **no,** it is prohibited, because in that case the top of the wall is considered a domain in its own right. But if **it is not four by four** handbreadths, it is an exempt domain, and therefore **they may raise** their food onto the wall **as well.**

The *gemara* clarifies explicitly: *yesh bo arba'ah al arba'ah* — if the wall is four-by-four and ten *tefachim* high, it is a *reshut hayachid* unto itself, and therefore *olim in, maalin lo* — you may go up to it but not transfer items to and from it. *Ein bo arba'ah al arba'ah* — if it lacks that width, *maalin nami* — the transfer in both directions is permitted, at least according to Rabbi Yochanan.

## Makom Patur

### Makom Patur Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּכִי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבָּעָה — מוּתָּר לִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְלִבְנֵי רְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד לְכַתֵּף עָלָיו, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יַחֲלִיפוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan followed his** line of **reasoning** in this regard, **as when Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** With regard to **a place that does not have** an area of **four by four** handbreadths and is situated between a public and a private domain, **it is permissible for** both **the people in the public domain and for the people in the private domain to** adjust the burden on their **shoulders upon it, provided they do not exchange** objects between them from one domain to the other domain. This demonstrates that in the case of an exempt domain, Rabbi Yoḥanan was not concerned that one might carry from one domain to another, and permitted members of both domains to use it.

The *gemara* now extends Rabbi Yochanan's position to the broader concept of *makom patur*, a no-man's-land that is not four-by-four *tefachim* and thus lacks independent domain status. When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he transmitted Rabbi Yochanan's view: *makom she'ein bo arba'ah al arba'ah* — a space without four-by-four — is *mutar livnei reshut harabbim velivnei reshut hayachid lekhatef alav*, permitted both to the people of *reshut harabbim* and to the people of *reshut hayachid* for carrying upon it. Consider a small platform or post standing in the middle of a public thoroughfare — too small to be a *reshut hayachid* by virtue of lacking the four-by-four dimension. Such a platform is a *makom patur*. If it abuts both a *reshut harabbim* and a *reshut hayachid*, the people of each domain may place objects on it and retrieve objects from it, because when each domain uses it the platform is considered connected to that domain. It does not constitute an independent domain standing between them as an obstacle.

### No Exchange Rule

There is, however, one constraint: *uvilvad shelo yachlifu* — the one thing that is forbidden is the exchange. A person from *reshut harabbim* may not place an object on the *makom patur* for a person from *reshut hayachid* to retrieve, and vice versa. Even though the transfer is indirect — no single person has carried from *reshut harabbim* to *reshut hayachid* — this already constitutes a prohibited circumvention and would lead inevitably to the direct transfer. The *gemara* notes that strictly speaking such an indirect transfer would be only a rabbinic problem, but it is prohibited nonetheless.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַב דִּימִי? אִי בִּרְשׁוּיוֹת דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — הָכִי נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **Rav,** who prohibits carrying in that case even as much as a hair's breadth, **not accept** the opinion **of Rav Dimi** in this matter? The Gemara answers: **If** this referred **to** an exempt domain situated between two **domains by Torah law,** i.e., between a public and a private domain, **so too,** Rav would agree that the members of both domains may adjust their burdens there.

The *gemara* then asks: does Rav not accept Rav Dimi's formulation? The concept of *makom patur* connecting to adjacent domains was established in the tractate of *Shabbat* and appeared to command universal agreement. Why does Rav dispute its application to the courtyard wall? The *gemara* answers: *ei birshuyot deoraisa hachi nami* — when dealing with biblical domains, *reshut harabbim* and *reshut hayachid*, Rav also agrees that a *makom patur* may be used by both sides.

### Eruvin Stringency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — בִּרְשׁוּיוֹת דְּרַבָּנַן. וַחֲכָמִים עָשׂוּ חִיזּוּק לְדִבְרֵיהֶם יוֹתֵר מִשֶּׁל תּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** However, **with what are we dealing here,** in the case of the wall? We are dealing **with domains by rabbinic law, and the Sages reinforced their statements** even **more than those of the Torah.** Due to their severity, Torah laws are generally observed. Therefore, there is no need to impose decrees and enactments in order to preserve them. The same is not true of rabbinic decrees; if people ignore the preventive measures, they might come to violate the entire enactment.

*Hacha bamai askinan birshuyot derabbanan* — here, however, we are dealing with two courtyards, both of which are *reshut hayachid*. The only issue between them is the rabbinic requirement to establish an *eruv*. Since the underlying domain status is entirely rabbinic in its application here, a different standard applies.

*Vechachamim asu chizuk ledivreihem yoter mishel Torah*: the rabbis sometimes impose greater stringency on their own enactments than on biblical law. One might reason that since both courtyards are in fact *reshut hayachid*, and the only issue is the absence of an *eruv*, the whole matter is minor enough to be treated leniently. To prevent precisely this dismissive attitude toward the *eruv* system, Rav rules stringently: even a *makom patur* between two *reshut hayachid* domains that lack a shared *eruv* is treated as contested territory and is off-limits entirely.

This move is worth pausing over, because it runs counter to the general principle *halacha kedivrei hameikel be'eruv* — that in *eruv* matters we rule leniently when there is a dispute. Tosafot notes the apparent tension and resolves it as follows: the tendency toward leniency governs the resolution of specific disputes about *eruv* requirements; the principle of rabbinic reinforcement governs structural features of the system itself. Tosafot adds a further qualification: Rav's stringency about *makom patur* applies only when the underlying issue is an *eruv* issue — two *reshuyot hayachid* that have not made a joint *eruv*. If instead one were dealing with a *karmelit* alongside a *reshut harabbim* with a *makom patur* in between, that falls within the ambit of the biblical carrying prohibitions (even if the *karmelit* itself is rabbinic), and there the *makom patur* would be treated leniently even according to Rav. Rabbi Yochanan, consistent with his position throughout, is lenient in both cases.

## Unequal Height Walls

### Wall Height Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה (אָמַר) רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, צִידּוֹ אֶחָד גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים וְצִידּוֹ אֶחָד שָׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ — נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁשָּׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** that **Rav Huna said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** With regard to **a wall that is between two courtyards,** and **one side** facing one courtyard is **ten handbreadths high, and the other side is level with the ground** of the second courtyard, i.e., the second courtyard is built on a higher plane, so that the wall is less than ten handbreadths above its floor, in this case the Sages **grant** the use of the top of the wall on Shabbat only **to the** courtyard in which the wall is **level with the ground.**

The *gemara* records a ruling transmitted by Rabbah bar Rav Huna in the name of Rav Nachman: *kotel shebein shtei chatzerot tziddo echad gavoha asara tefachim vetziddo echad shaveh la'aretz* — a wall between two courtyards that is ten *tefachim* high on one side but whose other side is close to level with the ground. The wall has been treated until now as neutrally positioned between the two courtyards, belonging to neither exclusively. But when one side is effectively accessible — when the top of the wall is nearly at ground level from one courtyard's perspective — the wall's neutrality breaks down.

### Easier Access Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּקָשֶׁה. וְכׇל לָזֶה בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה בְּקָשֶׁה — נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** The reason is **because the use of** the wall **is convenient for one** side, i.e., the higher courtyard, **but difficult for the other** side. The wall can be used more conveniently by the residents of the higher courtyard. **And** the principle is that in **any** case with regard to Shabbat where an action is **convenient for one** party **and difficult for another,** the Sages **grant it to the one for whom its use is convenient.**

*Notnin oto lazeh sheshaveh la'aretz mishum dehavi leh lezeh tashmisho benachas ulezeh tashmisho bekoshi* — we assign the wall to the courtyard from which the ground is closer, because for that courtyard use of the wall is easy, *benachas*, while for the other it is burdensome, *bekoshi*. The principle is *tashmisho benachas*: where access is easy and natural, the object or space belongs halachically to the domain that enjoys that easy access. This applies even though technically the wall may still be ten *tefachim* high on the far side. The experiential accessibility from one side is sufficient to assign the wall to that courtyard, and the people of that courtyard may therefore carry between their courtyard and the wall.

It is worth noting the precise conditions: this is not simply a matter of one side being twelve *tefachim* and the other being ten. The key is that from one side the top of the wall is *below* ten *tefachim* of effective height — close enough to ground level that from that perspective the wall does not function as a full wall at all. Tosafot addresses the related question of a courtyard surrounded by a wall that is less than ten *tefachim* from the inside but more than ten from the outside: even if *halachically* such a wall is still considered a valid enclosure, the experiential height determines which courtyard the wall is connected to.

### Ditch Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁיזְבִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: חָרִיץ שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, צִידּוֹ אֶחָד עָמוֹק עֲשָׂרָה וְצִידּוֹ אֶחָד שָׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ — נוֹתְנִין אוֹתוֹ לָזֶה שֶׁשָּׁוֶה לָאָרֶץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ לָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּנַחַת וְלָזֶה תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בְּקָשֶׁה וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **Rav Sheizvi said** that **Rav Naḥman said:** In the case of **a ditch between two courtyards, one side of which is ten** handbreadths **deep, and the other side of which is level with the ground** of the second courtyard, i.e., is less than ten handbreadths below it, the Sages **grant** the use of the ditch **to the** courtyard in which the ditch is **level with the ground.** This is **because it is** a case in which **the use of** the ditch **is convenient for one** side, where it is close to level with the ground, **and difficult for the other,** where the ditch is ten handbreadths deep, and whenever use of an item is convenient for one party and inconvenient for another, it is granted to the one for whom it is convenient.

Shizbi transmits a parallel ruling from Rav Nachman concerning a *charitz* — a ditch between two courtyards. A ditch ten *tefachim* deep and four *tefachim* wide creates the same separation as a wall: the space at the bottom is its own *reshut hayachid* and the courtyards cannot be unified. The analogy with the wall is precise. Now, if one side of the ditch drops ten *tefachim* but the other side is closer to the level of the ditch floor, *notnin oto lazeh sheshaveh la'aretz* — we assign the ditch floor to the courtyard from whose perspective it is less than ten *tefachim* deep, again by the principle of *tashmisho benachas*.

### Why Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי. דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן כּוֹתֶל, מִשּׁוּם דִּבְגוּבְהָא מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁי אִינָשֵׁי. אֲבָל חָרִיץ, בְּעוּמְקָא לָא מִשְׁתַּמְּשִׁי אִינָשֵׁי — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** it was **necessary** to cite both of these cases, as it would not have been possible to learn one from the other, **since, had** Rav Naḥman **taught us** only the case **with regard to a wall,** one could have said that the *halakha* applies only in that case, **because people use an elevated** surface. Even for the residents of the lower courtyard, it is relatively easy to use this wall. **However,** with regard to **a ditch, people do not use a deep** surface, as it is troublesome to bend down and place an item in a ditch. In that case, one might **say** that it may **not** be used by the residents of either courtyard.

The *gemara* asks why both cases — the wall and the ditch — need to be stated explicitly. *Vetzricha*: each case contains an assumption the other might not share.

Had the *gemara* stated only the wall case, one might have reasoned: people readily use elevated surfaces, so a wall that is accessible from one side naturally becomes that courtyard's domain. But a ditch is different — *be'omka lo mishtamshi inshi*, people do not typically make practical use of the bottom of ditches. Even if it is not very deep from one side, I might have thought the ditch floor remains unassigned to either courtyard. The ditch case is therefore necessary to teach that even for below-ground spaces, easier access determines assignment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּחָרִיץ, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא בְּעִיתָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא. אֲבָל כּוֹתֶל דִּבְעִיתָא תַּשְׁמִישְׁתָּא — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** likewise, **had he taught us** only the case **with regard to a ditch,** one could have said that the *halakha* applies only in that case, **because its use does not cause worry,** as anything placed in the ditch is protected. **However,** with regard to **a wall, the use of which causes worry** that the objects placed there are liable to fall, one might **say** that it may **not** be used by the residents of either courtyard. Therefore, it was **necessary** to state both of these rulings.

Conversely, had the *gemara* stated only the ditch case, one might have argued: things stored in a ditch are hidden and safe, making the ditch genuinely useful to the adjacent courtyard. But items placed atop a wall are exposed, *deba'ita tashmisha* — you are afraid to leave things there unprotected — and perhaps that fear of use means the wall is not truly accessible even from the easier side. The wall case is therefore necessary to teach that practical accessibility, not security, governs the determination. Since neither case could have been derived from the other, both must be stated.

## Lowering Wall Method

### Platform Building

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לְמַעֲטוֹ, אִם יֵשׁ בְּמִיעוּטוֹ אַרְבָּעָה — מוּתָּר לְהִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בְּכׇל הַכּוֹתֶל כּוּלּוֹ. וְאִם לָאו — אֵין מִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ אֶלָּא כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּיעוּט.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a wall between two courtyards, the Gemara states: If **one comes to diminish** the height of the wall by placing a stone next to it, or by building a platform in order to permit its use or to utilize it as a passageway to another courtyard, the following distinction applies: **If the diminished** section **is** at least **four handbreadths** wide, **it is permitted to use the entire wall.** This area has the status of an entrance and the two courtyards are considered one, which renders the entire wall permitted. **However, if** the diminished section is **not** at least four handbreadths wide, **one** may **use only the** area of the wall **opposite** the **diminished** section, but no more.

The *gemara* now turns to a different technique for accessing the wall: not natural ground asymmetry, but deliberate construction. *Ba lema'ato* — you wish to lower the wall. Rather than removing material from the top of the wall, the easier approach is to raise the ground on one side by building a platform or earthen mound. Once the floor level is raised sufficiently, the effective height of the wall above that floor drops below ten *tefachim*, and the wall can then be considered accessible to that courtyard.

### Platform Width

The critical question is how wide the platform must be to affect the wall's entire length. *Im yesh bemi'uto arba'ah* — if the raised section is four *tefachim* wide, *mutar lehishtamesh bechol hakotel kulo* — one may use the entire length of the wall, not just the section immediately opposite the platform. The reasoning follows from the rules of openings: four *tefachim* is the minimum width of a halachically significant doorway or window. A four-*tefach*-wide section at the lower height constitutes a valid *petach* into the wall, and once that aperture exists, it creates access to the entire wall, just as a single door in a long wall creates access to whatever lies beyond it. Pictorially, one can understand this as creating a situation analogous to the window-in-a-wall discussed on the previous daf: the raised platform produces a four-*tefach*-wide section where the effective height is below ten *tefachim*, which is precisely equivalent to having a four-*tefach* window at the ten-*tefach* limit.

### Limited Access

If the platform is less than four *tefachim* wide — say, only two *tefachim* — the ruling is *ein mishtamesh ela keneged hami'ut*: one may only use the section of the wall directly opposite the narrow platform, not the rest of the wall. This is internally consistent: if the narrow section does not rise to the level of a proper *petach*, it grants access only to what lies directly adjacent.

### Platform vs. Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה נַפְשָׁךְ? אִי אַהֲנִי מִעוּטָא — בְּכוּלֵּיהּ כּוֹתֶל לִישְׁתַּמֵּשׁ. אִי לָא אַהֲנִי — אֲפִילּוּ כְּנֶגֶד הַמִּיעוּט נָמֵי לָא. אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁעָקַר חוּלְיָא מֵרֹאשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this ruling: **Whichever way you** look at it, this ruling is difficult. **If the diminishing was effective, although** it extends less than four handbreadths, **let him use** the entire **wall;** and **if** the diminishing **was not effective, even** the area **corresponding to the diminished** section should **also not** be permitted for use, as this section is insignificant. **Ravina said:** Here, it refers to a case **where one removed a segment** of stone **from the top** of the wall. Since the wall is actually less than ten handbreadths high along that section, it is fit for use as is an opening in the wall.

Rav Ashi objects to this formulation as internally inconsistent. If we consider those two *tefachim* sufficient to grant access to the section of the wall above them, by the same logic that access should extend to the entire wall, as it does when the platform is four wide. And if two *tefachim* is genuinely insufficient to constitute an opening, then even the section directly opposite should be inaccessible. Ravina resolves this by specifying that the case of limited access involves not a platform raising the ground, but *akar chulya merosho* — physically removing a section from the top of the wall itself. When a small section of the top of the wall is literally removed, that section becomes lower than ten *tefachim* and is connected to the courtyard below it; but because the wall itself remains structurally ten *tefachim* high everywhere else, the removed section does not create a doorway that opens the entire wall. It is a localized lowering, not a *petach*. Rashi explains: a doorway is either a doorway or it is not; there is no such thing as a partial doorway. When you build a raised platform, you are creating an opening below ten *tefachim*, which either meets the four-*tefach* threshold (granting access to the whole wall) or does not (granting no access at all). But when you remove stone from the wall itself, the lowered section is simply a thinner part of an otherwise full wall, and its connectivity to the courtyard does not propagate to the rest of the wall's length.

## Temporary Objects (II)

### Basket Not Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְחִיאֵל: כָּפָה סֵפֶל — מְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeḥiel said:** If **one overturned a basin** and placed it next to a wall, such that the wall is no longer ten handbreadths high, **it** effectively **diminishes** the height of the wall.

Rav Yitzchak introduces a further scenario: what if instead of building a permanent earthen mound, one simply overturns a basket or sturdy box against the wall? *Kafa sefel mema'et* — intuitively, this might also serve to lower the effective height of the wall. The *gemara* immediately raises an objection.

### Removability Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי? דָּבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת הוּא, וְדָבָר הַנִּיטָּל בְּשַׁבָּת אֵינוֹ מְמַעֵט. לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּחַבְּרֵיהּ בְּאַרְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And why** should this be so? Isn't this basin **an item that may be moved on Shabbat,** i.e., something that one is permitted to handle? **And** the principle is that **an item that may be moved on Shabbat does not diminish** a wall. Since it can be removed at any moment on Shabbat, such an object cannot be viewed as a permanent part of the wall. The Gemara answers: **No, it is necessary;** this basin can be considered a permanent part of the wall in a case **where he attached** the basin **to the ground** by covering it with soil. The basin may then not be moved on Shabbat due to the prohibition of handling soil.

*Davar hanital b'Shabbat hu, ve'davar hanital b'Shabbat eino mema'et* — an overturned basket is an object that can be removed on *Shabbat* itself, and anything removable on *Shabbat* does not alter the halachic configuration of the wall. Because the basket is impermanent — one can simply lift it away during *Shabbat* — it does not change how we regard the wall. The wall's status is determined by its stable, structural configuration, not by objects temporarily propped against it.

The *gemara* then refines the case: what if the box has been pushed into the dirt, or its edges driven into the ground, making removal less trivial? Perhaps in that case it should be considered sufficiently permanent? And the *gemara* continues: even if the box has a lip (*ogna*) on the bottom, so that removing it disturbs the dirt around it, does that change things?

### Incidental Movement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי חַבְּרֵיהּ בְּאַרְעָא, מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָא תַּנְיָא: פַּגָּה שֶׁהִטְמִינָהּ בְּתֶבֶן, וַחֲרָרָה שֶׁהִטְמִינָהּ בְּגֶחָלִים, אִם מְגוּלָּה מִקְצָתָהּ — נִטֶּלֶת בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **And if one attached** the basin **to the ground, what of it? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **an unripe** fig **that one buried in straw** intended for kindling, so that it would ripen, **or a cake that one buried in coals** before Shabbat, and the coals were extinguished, **if part of** either one **is visible, it may be moved on Shabbat.** This is not prohibited, although as a result one will move the straw or the coals, which are set aside.

To address this, the *gemara* invokes a *baraita*: if an unripe fig was buried in straw — straw that is *muktzah* because it is not animal fodder but construction material — or a wafer was placed in dead coals, *im megula miktzato nitalat b'Shabbat*, if even a small portion of the buried item is exposed, the entire thing may be moved on *Shabbat*. The incidental movement of the surrounding *muktzah* material is not prohibited; this is *tiltul min hatzad*, incidental displacement of *muktzah* for the sake of the non-*muktzah* item, which is permitted. The same reasoning applies to the box with a lip: removing the box incidentally disturbs the dirt, but that incidental disturbance is not a prohibition, and therefore the box remains movable on *Shabbat* and does not alter the wall's halachic configuration.

### Serious Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּאִית לֵיהּ אוֹגְנַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a case where the top of the basin **has a rim** that is fully buried in the ground, so that removing the basin will dislodge the earth under which it is buried in a manner similar to digging, which is prohibited on Shabbat.

The *gemara* continues, which leads to the question of whether such serious burial truly prevents removal. The *gemara* finally arrives at a case where the object truly cannot be removed on *Shabbat*: *d'ba'ei mara v'chatzina* — it requires a hoe and a shovel to extract it. In that case, removal would constitute either the prohibited labor of *choresh* (plowing) or *soter* (demolishing), and the object genuinely cannot be moved on *Shabbat*. Only then does its presence alter the configuration of the wall and permit use of the wall's surface.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אִית לֵיהּ אוֹגְנַיִים, מַאי הָוֵי? וְהַתְנַן: הַטּוֹמֵן לֶפֶת וּצְנוֹן תַּחַת הַגֶּפֶן, בִּזְמַן שֶׁמִּקְצָת עָלִין מְגוּלִּין אֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ לֹא מִשּׁוּם כִּלְאַיִם, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֵׂר, וְלֹא מִשּׁוּם שְׁבִיעִית, וְנִיטָּלִין בַּשַּׁבָּת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further challenges this explanation: **And if** the basin **has a rim, what of it? Didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **one who buries a turnip or a radish** in the ground **beneath a vine** for safekeeping, **when some of the leaves** of the turnip or radish **are showing, he need not be concerned due to diverse kinds,** i.e., that he violated the prohibition of planting food crops in a vineyard, as he did not intend to commit an act of planting; **nor due to tithes,** i.e., there is no concern lest the turnip or radish grew further, in which case he would be obligated to tithe it; **nor due to** the prohibition against working the land during **the Sabbatical Year,** and similarly, he need not be concerned that they grew further and that the additional growth is prohibited as produce that grew during the Sabbatical Year. **And** therefore, the turnip or radish **may be taken** from the ground **on Shabbat.** Even if most of the turnip or radish is underground, it is permitted to pull it from the ground on Shabbat. If so, in the case described in the mishna here, even if the basin buried in the ground has a rim, it should nevertheless be permitted to move it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּבָעֵי מָרָא וַחֲצִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** Rabbi Yeḥiel's ruling **is necessary** in a case where the basin was so firmly attached to the ground **that one would need a hoe [*mara*] or a spade** to remove it, as this action would certainly involve digging, which is prohibited on Shabbat. Therefore, since the basin cannot be removed on Shabbat, it is considered fixed in its place and effectively diminishes the height of the wall.

## Ladder Types

### Egyptian Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוּלָּם הַמִּצְרִי אֵינוֹ מְמַעֵט וְהַצּוֹרִי מְמַעֵט. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי סוּלָּם הַמִּצְרִי? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אַרְבָּעָה חֲווֹקִים.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in a *baraita*: **An Egyptian ladder,** which is small, **does not diminish** the height of a wall, whereas **a** larger, **Tyrian** ladder effectively **diminishes** its height. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of an **Egyptian ladder;** i.e., what characterizes it? **The** scholars of the **school of Rabbi Yannai said: Any** ladder **that does not have four rungs.**

The *gemara* continues with a related case: can a ladder placed against the wall lower its effective height and make the wall accessible? *Sulam hamitzri eino mema'et* — an Egyptian ladder does not lower the wall. As the school of Rabbi Yannai explains, *kol she'ein lo arba chivakin* — it is a small ladder that lacks four rungs. It is essentially a compact step ladder, comparable in halachic terms to the upturned basket: it is portable, temporary, and does not alter the structural configuration of the wall.

### Tyrian Ladder

*Hatzuri mema'et* — a Tyrian ladder, from *Tzur*, does lower the wall. The question is what distinguishes the two. Rav Acha bar Adda transmits in the name of Rav Hamnuna the same principle already established: the Egyptian ladder is disqualified *mishum d'havei lei davar she'nittal b'Shabbat*, because it is the kind of thing that can be removed on *Shabbat*. If so, the objection pushes further: surely a larger ladder can also be moved?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ צוֹרִי נָמֵי! הָתָם כּוֹבְדוֹ קוֹבְעוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** this should apply **even** to **a Tyrian** ladder **as well,** as a large ladder is also not set-aside and may be handled on Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a Tyrian ladder, **its heaviness establishes it** as part of the wall. Even though one is permitted to move it, since due to its weight it is not moved easily, it effectively diminishes the height of the wall.

### Practical Permanence

The *gemara* responds: *kovo kovo* — in practice, it is left in place. A large, heavy roof ladder is not the sort of thing one routinely folds up and relocates. Even if it could theoretically be moved, if in actual use it has a fixed location and stays there, it has sufficient practical permanence to alter the halachic configuration of the wall. The standard, then, is not theoretical movability but practical, behavioral permanence: does the object actually remain in place during *Shabbat* as a matter of ordinary usage? If yes, it functions as a structural modification; if no, it does not.

## Creating Opening

### Lavud Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כּוֹתֶל שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, וְהִנִּיחַ סוּלָּם רָחָב אַרְבָּעָה מִכָּאן וְסוּלַּם רָחָב אַרְבָּעָה מִכָּאן, וְאֵין בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים — מְמַעֵט. שְׁלֹשָׁה — אֵינוֹ מְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** If **a wall between two courtyards** is **ten handbreadths high, and one placed a ladder four** handbreadths **wide** against the wall **on one side,** in one courtyard, **and** another **ladder four** handbreadths **wide on the other side,** in the other courtyard, **and there are less than three handbreadths between them,** i.e., the two ladders on the opposite sides of the wall are within three handbreadths of each other, even if they are not directly opposite each other, this **diminishes** the height of the wall. The pair of ladders is regarded as a valid passageway between the two courtyards. However, if the gap between the two ladders is **three** handbreadths or more, this **does not diminish** the height of the wall.

Abaye now introduces a different application of ladders: not lowering the wall so that one courtyard may use it, but creating a proper *petach* — an opening — in the wall that would allow both courtyards to establish a single joint *eruv*. *Kotel shebein shtei chatzerot gavoha asara tefachim* — you have a wall ten *tefachim* high, preventing any combined *eruv*. You place a ladder four *tefachim* wide on one side of the wall — lowering the effective height on that side below ten *tefachim* for a four-*tefach* section — and another ladder four *tefachim* wide on the other side of the wall. Each ladder creates a half-window, as it were, on its respective side. If the gap between the two ladders — measuring across the width of the wall — is less than three *tefachim*, the principle of *lavud* applies: gaps of less than three *tefachim* are treated as closed, the two half-windows are considered joined, and the combined opening constitutes a valid four-*tefach* window traversing the full thickness of the wall. With such a window, the two courtyards may combine their *eruvin* into one.

If the gap between the two ladders is three *tefachim* or more, *lavud* does not apply, the window does not span the wall, and the courtyards remain separated.

### Wide Wall Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא הָוֵי כּוֹתֶל אַרְבָּעָה, אֲבָל הָוֵי כּוֹתֶל אַרְבָּעָה — אֲפִילּוּ מוּפְלָג טוּבָא, נָמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And we only said** this qualification if the **wall was less than four** handbreadths wide. However, **if** the **wall was** at least **four** handbreadths wide, then **even** if one ladder was **greatly distanced** from the other, this **likewise** renders it permitted. Since it is possible to walk along the thickness of the wall, the pair of ladders constitutes a passageway between the two courtyards.

This analysis applies only when the wall is less than four *tefachim* wide. If the wall is four *tefachim* wide or more, *afilu muflag tuva nami* — even if the two ladders are very far apart from each other (measured across the wall's top surface), it still works. The reason is that a four-*tefach*-wide wall is itself walkable surface: a person can climb up to the top of the wall, walk across it, and descend on the other side. The wall's own surface provides the traversal that *lavud* provides in the thin-wall case. Therefore the distance between the ladders is irrelevant; the top of the wall itself constitutes the passage, and the combined structure permits the courtyards to form a single *eruv*.

## Creating Opening (II)

### Dual Ladder Setup

To be explicit about the mechanics: the dual-ladder setup requires each ladder to be four *tefachim* wide, because four *tefachim* is the minimum width for a halachically significant opening. *Lavud* is invoked only to close the gap between the two sides and establish continuity through the wall's thickness; it does not contribute to the width of the opening. Each ladder must independently meet the four-*tefach* threshold. Where the wall is wide enough to walk upon, its own surface supplies the continuous passage that makes the measurement of inter-ladder separation irrelevant.

## Stacked Structures

### Stacked Platforms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: בָּנָה אִיצְטְבָא עַל גַּב אִיצְטְבָא, אִם יֵשׁ בָּאִיצְטְבָא הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה — מְמַעֵט. אִי נָמֵי אֵין בַּתַּחְתּוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה, וְיֵשׁ בָּעֶלְיוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה, וְאֵין בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה — מְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: If one built a** wooden **platform** next to the wall **above** another **platform,** then **if the lower platform is four** handbreadths wide, **it diminishes** the height of the wall to below ten handbreadths. **Alternatively,** if **the lower** one **is not four** handbreadths wide, **but the upper** one **is four** handbreadths wide, **and there is** a gap of **less than three** handbreadths **between them, it diminishes** the height of the wall, as the two platforms are considered as one.

Rav Bibi bar Abaye introduces a further variant: *bana itztava al gabei itztava* — a platform built on top of another platform. The question returns to the context of lowering the wall for use by one courtyard (not creating a combined opening). A single four-*tefach* platform on the ground lowers the wall effectively. What if you have two stacked platforms, neither of which alone may be positioned as ideally as a single ground-level platform? *Im yesh be'itztava tachtona arba'ah mema'et* — if the bottom platform is four *tefachim* wide, the top platform is irrelevant; the bottom platform alone suffices, is connected to the ground, and lowers the wall's effective height.

### Top Platform Rule

The *chiddush* is the reverse case: the bottom platform is less than four *tefachim* wide, but the top platform is four *tefachim* wide. Does the top platform lower the wall? It depends entirely on the gap between the two platforms. *Ve'ein bein zeh le'zeh shalosha mema'et* — if the space between the two platforms is less than three *tefachim*, the two are considered joined by *lavud*, the top platform is treated as connected to the lower one and through it to the ground, and the four-*tefach* top platform lowers the wall. If the gap between the two platforms is three *tefachim* or more, the top platform is considered to float in mid-air, unconnected to the ground, and it does not alter the wall's configuration regardless of its width.

### Step Ladder Rungs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: סוּלָּם שֶׁשְּׁלִיבוֹתָיו פּוֹרְחוֹת, אִם יֵשׁ בַּשְּׁלִיבָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה — מְמַעֵט. אִי נָמֵי אֵין בַּשְּׁלִיבָה הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה וְיֵשׁ בַּשְּׁלִיבָה הָעֶלְיוֹנָה אַרְבָּעָה, וְאֵין בֵּין זֶה לָזֶה שְׁלֹשָׁה — מְמַעֵט.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** In the case of **a ladder whose rungs are disconnected, if the bottom rung is four** handbreadths wide, **it diminishes** the height of the wall. **Alternatively,** if **the bottom rung is not four** handbreadths wide, **but the upper rung is four** handbreadths wide, **and there is** a gap of **less than three** handbreadths **between them, it diminishes** the height of the wall, because the principle of *lavud* joins the two rungs together.

Rav Nachman citing Rabbah bar Avuha applies the same analysis to a ladder with *porchot* rungs — flying rungs. Rashi understands this as a standard ladder with separate rungs. Tosafot understands it as a step ladder whose platforms are separated from one another with gaps between them. On Tosafot's reading, the case is structurally identical to the stacked platforms: *im yesh bishliva tachtona arba'ah mema'et* — if the bottom step is four *tefachim* wide (and is within three *tefachim* of the ground so as to be considered connected by *lavud*), it lowers the wall. If the bottom step is less than four *tefachim* wide but the next step up is four *tefachim* wide, it lowers the wall only if that upper step is within three *tefachim* of the lower step — *ve'ein bein zeh le'zeh shelosha mema'et* — so that the upper step is considered connected through the lower step to the ground. Beyond three *tefachim* of separation, the upper step is unanchored and has no halachic effect on the wall's height.

This same analysis, finally, connects back to the combined-*eruv* scenario: if such a ladder is placed on both sides of a wall in the configuration Abaye described, and the rungs on both sides meet within three *tefachim* of each other, the courtyards may be combined under a single *eruv*.
