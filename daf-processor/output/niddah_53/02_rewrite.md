# Niddah 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketamim Overview

### Ketamim Definition

The daf begins at the Mishnah towards the bottom of 52b, marking a full return to the discussion of *niddah* and shifting into the area of *ketamim* — stains found on a woman's garment. The historical context is important: at a time when laundry was not done daily and blood was prevalent in the marketplace from slaughtered animals, the question of whether a discovered stain genuinely originated from the woman herself was far from settled. Even when we do attribute the stain to her, we do not do so with certainty, and she is therefore rendered only rabbinically *teme'ah* — not a biblical *niddah* — precisely because of that inherent element of doubt.

### Mishnah Haro'ah Ketem

The Mishnah states: *haro'ah ketem harei zo mekukeles ve-chosheshes le-mishum zov* — if a woman finds a stain on her garment, she is *mekukeles* and must be concerned about *zov*. This is the position of Rabbi Meir.

### Mekukeles Explained

The term *mekukeles* — literally "disrupted" or "thrown into disorder" — refers specifically to the disruption of the woman's menstrual cycle counting. The mechanism is as follows: whether a woman is a *niddah* or a *zavah* depends entirely on when she last saw blood. She is a *niddah* for seven days following any bleeding; during the subsequent eleven days, any bleeding renders her a *zavah*, and three consecutive days of bleeding render her a *zavah gedolah*. It is therefore critical to know precisely when she last bled.

When a woman discovers a stain and cannot determine when it appeared on her garment — it could have been today, yesterday, or two weeks ago, since the last time she laundered — she must assume rabbinically that the stain came from her body. She is thus a rabbinic *niddah*. But now, if she bleeds again, she has no way of knowing whether she is bleeding as a *niddah* or as a *zavah*, because she cannot locate the stain in her cycle. That is the meaning of *mekukeles*: her cycle is completely disoriented, and the only way to restore orientation is to wait eighteen days without any bleeding. Any bleeding in the interim leaves her status indeterminate.

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir goes further, adding that she must also be concerned about *zov* — that is, if the stain is large enough, she must worry that it represents three separate episodes of bleeding, potentially all occurring during her *zavah* period, assuming she has not laundered since then. If so, she may be a *zavah*. This reflects Rabbi Meir's maximally stringent approach: he takes the worst-case interpretation of what the stain could represent.

### Rabbis Counter

The *chakhamim* respond: *ein be-ketamim mishum zov* — stains do not carry the concern of *zov*. Their reasoning is one of proportion. This woman is not even a biblical *niddah*; we cannot be certain the stain is from her at all. Since the entire *tumah* here is rabbinic, it would be an overreach to treat the stain as evidence that she is a *zavah*. The Rabbis do not explicitly address whether they agree with the *mekukeles* consequence, leaving open the question of whether their disagreement is with both aspects or only with the *zavah* concern.

## Chanina ben Antignos

### Chanina Identity

The Gemara immediately asks: *man chachamim* — who are these Rabbis who deny the *zavah* concern? The answer is Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, as established from a *baraita* in which he is cited explicitly.

### 3 Garments Case

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos states: *ketamim ein bahem mishum zav* — stains do not carry the *zov* concern. However, he concedes that there are circumstances in which stains can indeed lead to *zivah* status. He illustrates: *lavsha gimel chalukos badukos la* — if she wore three garments that had been pre-checked and laundered, confirmed clean before she put them on, and she subsequently found a stain on each of them on three separate days, then there is compelling evidence that each stain came from her on a different day. In that case, she must be concerned that she is a *zavah*, or at least a *safek zavah*.

### 2 Days Bleeding

A second case is presented: if she had two days of direct bodily flow during her *zavah* period — genuine *re'iyos* from her body — and then on the third day found a stain on a pre-checked garment, this is even clearer evidence. The Gemara asks why this second case needed to be stated: if three stained garments on three separate days suffice to create concern, surely two confirmed days of direct bleeding followed by a stained garment would do so as well?

The Gemara answers: one might have thought that given the enormity of the supporting evidence in this second case — two confirmed direct *re'iyos* — we should go further and treat her as a *vadai zavah*, allowing her to bring a *korban* and eat it. The *baraita* therefore teaches that even here she remains only a *safek zavah*. The fact that one of the three *re'iyos* is a *ketem* prevents us from ever treating the full status as certain, even when the evidence is overwhelming. This underscores the consistent halakhic principle: a *ketem*, even a well-supported one, always retains an element of *safek*.

### Rava Assessment

Rava remarks: *be-ha zakinu Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos le-rabbanan* — in this argument, Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos prevailed over the Rabbis. The implicit force of his position is more powerful than it might initially appear.

### 3 Beans Argument

Rava articulates Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's implicit challenge to the Rabbis' position. The Rabbis, following Rabbi Meir, would treat a stain large enough to be divided into three *gris* sizes as evidence of three separate days of bleeding, and thus a *zavah* concern. Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos presses the question: *mai shna pachos mi-gimel grisin be-makom echad de-lo chaishinan* — why are you not concerned if the stain is smaller than three *grisin* in one location? The answer must be that you say it is only two stains, not three. But then, even if the stain equals three *grisin*, why not say that two and a half came from her body and the remainder is attributable to insects — to the *zuhama*, the discharge, that attracts lice? Once you allow that logic at all, it should apply even to a stain of three sizes.

## Gris Measurement

### Gris Definition

The *gris* is a measurement used in the laws of *ketamim* to discount smaller stains. Because lice were prevalent and their blood could appear on garments, any stain smaller than the size of a *gris* — a particular type of bean — is automatically attributed to lice and does not render the woman *teme'ah* at all. Furthermore, the operative threshold is not merely a *gris* but a *gris ve-od* — slightly more than a *gris* — in order for the stain to demand halakhic attention.

### 3 Gris Threshold

The Rabbis (following Rabbi Meir) hold that if a stain is large enough to be divided into three portions each the size of a *gris ve-od*, the woman must be concerned that it represents three separate episodes of bleeding on three separate days. She is therefore treated as a *zavah*, at least rabbinically, even though the stain appears in a single location on the garment.

### Chanina Challenge

Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos challenges this reasoning. His argument, as articulated by Rava, is as follows: *mai shna pachos mi-gimel grisin be-makom echad de-lo chaishinan* — you concede that a stain smaller than three *grisin* in one spot does not require *zavah* concern. Why? Presumably because you say it represents only two separate bleedings, not three. But then *shelosha grisin be-makom echad nami neima* — even when it is the size of three *grisin*, why not say the same? Why not say two and a half portions came from her body, while the remainder — *agav zuma dama* — was attracted by the stench, and the blood of insects contributed the rest? This argument is somewhat unusual in construction, since the simpler objection would be that the entire large stain came from a single episode on one day. The framing as "two and a half from her body plus lice" is admittedly strange, and it is equally strange that the very premise for discounting small stains in the first place is the concern for lice — yet here lice are invoked selectively only for one portion. Nevertheless, Rava presents this as the thrust of Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's challenge, and he considers it a strong one.

### Rabbis Respond

The Rabbis reply: *kivan de'ika le-miplagei ke-chagris ve-yoter be-chol yoma, lo talinan* — since the stain is large enough that one could allocate a *gris ve-od* per day across three days, we do not attribute any portion to an outside cause. Once it reaches that threshold, we assume three separate *re'iyos* on three separate days, rather than any combination of human blood and insect blood. In other words, the size is itself the deciding factor: once divisible into three minimum-sized portions, the worst-case scenario is invoked and the woman is treated as a potential *zavah*.

---

## Spot Location Debate

### Spot vs. Spots

The Gemara now reframes the entire dispute by distinguishing between a single large stain in one location and stains in three separate locations on the same garment. If Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos's challenge about one large spot carries such force, his implied concession is equally significant: *gimmel grisin be-makom echad lo chaishinan* — if it is all one spot, we should not be concerned it is three separate bleedings. This implies, by contrast, that *ha be-gimel mekomos chaishinan* — if the stains appear in three distinct locations on the garment, he would agree it is a concern.

### Concession Terms

However, this creates a difficulty, because Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos was quoted earlier as saying that only three separate garments worn on three separate days suffice to create concern — not three separate spots on the same garment. The Gemara resolves this: when Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos makes his argument against the Rabbis, he argues *le-divreichem* — according to the Rabbis' own logic. Even by their reasoning, a single large spot should not be treated as three separate bleedings. But as for his own position: *li-didi, gimmel chalukin in* — for me, only three separate garments on separate days constitute sufficient evidence. *Gimmel mekomos lo* — three spots on a single garment, even by me, is not enough.

### Settlement

The final resolution of the dispute is that Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos presses the Rabbis: even granting your own framework of treating stains by size, you must at least concede that a single unified spot cannot be presumed to represent three separate bleedings. The Rabbis decline to concede, maintaining that since the stain is divisible into three *gris ve-od* portions, we invoke the three-day assumption regardless of whether it appears as one spot or multiple. Throughout all of this, it must be emphasized that even Rabbi Meir's stricter view is operating entirely in the realm of *de-rabbanan*: there is always an underlying element of *safek* with *ketamim*, and at most, exceptionally well-supported cases may rise to the level of *safek de-oraita*.

## Baraita 3 Gris

### Majority Ruling

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *ha-ro'ah ketem im yesh bo kedei le-chalek gimel grisin va-od — she-hen ke-gris va-od* — if a woman sees a stain large enough to divide into three portions each of *gris va-od*, she must be concerned that she is a *zavah*. If the stain does not reach that threshold, she need not be concerned. This represents the majority position, aligned with Rabbi Meir, and the *stam* formulation of the *baraita* lends it greater authority — which may explain why the Gemara earlier referred to Rabbi Meir's view as the position of the *rabbanan*.

### Minority Stricter

Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra, citing Rabbi Yosi, states: *achas zu ve-achas zu chosheshes* — in both cases, whether the stain is large or small, she must be concerned. Even a stain that is only the size of two *grisin* — not three — requires concern. This would seem to invoke *zavah gedolah* concern even from what appears to be only two *re'iyos*, which is striking since a *zavah gedolah* requires three.

### Rebbi Analysis

Rabbi Rebbi comments: *nirin divrei Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra be-shelo badkah* — the position of Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra makes sense when the woman did not check. When there was no checking at a particular time, we must be more concerned, and therefore even two apparent *re'iyos* might constitute three. When the woman did check — *ve-divrei chachamim be-she-badkah* — the Rabbis' position, limiting the concern to three-sized stains, applies. The Gemara will now unpack precisely what "checking" and "not checking" mean in this context.

## Bein Hashmashos

### Twilight Effect

The mechanism by which two stains can count as three *re'iyos* is now explained. If a woman bleeds during *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period between one day and the next — that single episode counts as two separate days of *re'iyah*: once for the outgoing day and once for the incoming day. If she then sees on the following full day, she has accumulated three *re'iyos* in total, rendering her a *zavah gedolah*, despite only two episodes of visible bleeding.

This is admittedly a surprising ruling, since ordinarily *bein hashmashos* is treated as an unresolved doubt — it is either day or night, but one or the other, not both. One might therefore argue that even if bleeding occurred during *bein hashmashos*, it cannot count as two days simultaneously, since the bleeding itself is of a minimum *gris* size and did not definitively span both sides of the transition. The Gemara does not pause to unpack this assumption but proceeds with it: once we have discounted the lice concern by virtue of the stain's minimum size, we attribute the entire *gris* to her body, and if it can be assumed to have straddled the transition of the day, it counts as two.

This immediately raises the question of what "checking" means in Rabbi Rebbi's formulation. It now emerges that the critical question is: can we eliminate the *bein hashmashos* possibility from the table? If we can, then a stain of only two *grisin* counts as only two *re'iyos* and no *zavah gedolah* concern arises. If we cannot eliminate it, then even two *re'iyos* may in fact be three, and concern is warranted.

### Rava Discussion

Rava reports that he came upon yeshiva students who were framing the debate as follows: *kegon she-badkah atzmah ve-lo badkah chalukah* — she examined herself directly but did not examine her garment. This is the normal *ketem* scenario — there is no direct evidence of bodily flow, only a stain of uncertain origin. The students then specify the precise constructed case: *de-af atzmah lo badkah ela bein hashmashos de-Rabbi Yehuda, u-vein hashmashos de-Rabbi Yosi lo badkah* — she examined herself only during the *bein hashmashos* of Rabbi Yehuda, but not during the *bein hashmashos* of Rabbi Yosi.

### Twilight Periods

The reference here is to the well-known dispute in *Shabbat* about the duration of *bein hashmashos*. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *bein hashmashos* lasts for half a *mil* — approximately nine minutes — beginning from sunset. After those nine minutes, it is definitively night. Rabbi Yosi, by contrast, holds that *bein hashmashos* is *ke-heref ayin* — a split second — and the Gemara here assumes that this split second occurs immediately after Rabbi Yehuda's nine-minute period concludes. So from sunset: nine minutes of Rabbi Yehuda's *bein hashmashos*, and then a single moment of Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashos*, after which it is indisputably night.

### Constructed Case

In the constructed case, the woman examined herself throughout Rabbi Yehuda's nine-minute *bein hashmashos*, but not during the moment immediately following. The Rabbis hold that *bein hashmashos de-Rabbi Yosi layla hu* — by Rabbi Yosi's period it is already definitely night, so she has effectively eliminated the *bein hashmashos* possibility entirely. At most she had two *re'iyos*, and no *zavah* concern arises unless the stain is three-sized. Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra in the name of Rabbi Yosi dissents: *bein hashmashos de-Rabbi Yosi safek hu* — that split-second moment is still a genuine doubt, and since she was not checking during it, we cannot rule out that a *re'iyah* occurred then. Therefore even two apparent *re'iyos* must be treated as potentially three.

Rava notes that this framing is itself problematic. The easy version of the debate would simply be: do we care about *bein hashmashos* at all? But the students are saying that everybody agrees *bein hashmashos* is on the table — the only question is whether it can be removed from consideration, and if so, by what means.

### Eliminate Twilight

Rava responds to the students: *de-maliya yadah kol bein hashmashos shafir d'mei* — if she was examining herself continuously for the entire nine-minute *bein hashmashos*, inserting her hand throughout that entire period, then one could argue she has eliminated the *bein hashmashos* possibility. But the students' scenario — checking "during the *bein hashmashos* of Rabbi Yehuda" — might mean only occasional checking during that period, not continuous. In that case, *shema mi-she-asi yadah ra'asah* — perhaps she bled in the moment between one check and the next. That cannot be the scenario being debated, since such a case would always remain unresolved.

The students respond to Rava: *ke-she-nishnis yadah bein eineiha kol bein hashmashos* — yes, she was checking herself continuously throughout the entire nine minutes. The image is striking: continuous self-examination for the duration of that window, analogous to keeping one's finger in one's eye without a break. The Gemara acknowledges that this is a highly constructed scenario, but proceeds on its terms. The debate then is not over whether *bein hashmashos* matters, but over whether a woman who has conclusively eliminated Rabbi Yehuda's nine-minute *bein hashmashos* has also eliminated Rabbi Yosi's single-second *bein hashmashos*, which may or may not fall within the same window.

## Rebbi Twilight

### Rebbi Concedes

Rabbi Rebbi states: *nirin divrei Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra be-she-lo badkah* — I acknowledge that the position of Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra is correct when there was no checking. When *bein hashmashos* has not been accounted for, the concern of three *re'iyos* is justified. *Ve-divrei chachamim be-she-badkah* — and the Rabbis' lenient position applies when she did check.

### Conditions

The Gemara presses: *mai lo badkah* — what exactly constitutes "not checking"? If it means she checked during Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes but not during Rabbi Yosi's single second, *michlal de-Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra savar af al gav de-lo badkah be-travaihu lo chaisha* — it would imply that Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra holds that even without checking either period there is no concern. But *ha lo badkah* — she did not check at all! How can there be no concern? The Gemara concludes that "not checking" must mean she did not check during either period — neither Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes nor Rabbi Yosi's moment. And "checking" — for purposes of when Rabbi Rebbi concedes to the Rabbis — must mean checking both windows.

### Contradiction Found

With this framing, a difficulty emerges: *kashya de-Rebbi ad Rebbi* — Rabbi Rebbi contradicts himself. When he says he concedes to Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra in the case of "no checking," and to the Rabbis in the case of "checking," it seems that one of his concessions implies a ruling about *bein hashmashos* that conflicts with what the other implies. Specifically, if he concedes to the Rabbis only when both periods were checked, then checking only Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes would leave him aligned with Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra's concern — meaning Rabbi Rebbi views Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashos* as a genuine *safek*. But then, when he says he concedes to Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra only when there was no checking at all, he implies that checking Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes suffices to resolve the concern — contradicting the previous implication.

### No Checking Agreement

The Gemara resolves this: *hachi ka'amar* — here is the correct reading of Rabbi Rebbi's statement. Rabbi Rebbi is not staking out a middle position between the two disputants; rather, he is clarifying the precise boundaries of their dispute. The Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra disagree only in the case where Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes were checked but Rabbi Yosi's moment was not. In that constructed case, the Rabbis say *bein hashmashos* is off the table, while Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra says it remains a concern. But when she did not check at all — not even the nine minutes — *af chachamim lo nechleku alav*, even the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra that *bein hashmashos* remains a genuine concern. The entire dispute is confined to that single precise scenario: checked Rabbi Yehuda's window, missed Rabbi Yosi's moment.

---

## Two Versions

### Baraita Conflict

The Gemara now raises a *remiya* — a contradiction from another *baraita*. That *baraita* states: *ha-ro'ah ketem, damyah merubah chosheshes, damyah mu'etes einah chosheshes* — if the stain is large, she must be concerned; if small, she need not be. This is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra in the name of Rabbi Yosi. But this directly contradicts the position we have been attributing to Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra — namely, that even a smaller stain (two sizes rather than three) requires concern, because of the *bein hashmashos* possibility.

### Rebbi Small Stain

In that same *baraita*, Rabbi Rebbi states: *ani shamati mimenu she-achas zu ve-achas zu chosheshes* — I heard directly from Rabbi Yosi that in both cases — large stain and small stain alike — she must be concerned. Rabbi Yosi then explained this to him with an analogy: a *niddah* who did not perform a *hefsek tahara* — a bodily examination — toward the end of day seven, from *minchah* onward, cannot assume she is clean and immerse that night. Even if she checked earlier in the week, we assume worst-case and require a fresh examination near day's end, lest she restarted bleeding in the interim. Similarly, a woman who already has evidence of staining should be treated with the same worst-case orientation, and we do not relax the concern for *bein hashmashos* simply because the stain is small.

Rabbi Rebbi then adds: *nirin devarav — ve-nirin dvarav, keshebadkah* — I find his position persuasive, and specifically it applies when she did check. And now the Gemara applies the same analytical pressure that it did before: *mai badkah* — what constitutes checking? If it means checking during Rabbi Yehuda's period but not Rabbi Yosi's, then Rabbi Rebbi's concession would imply that Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra holds there is no concern even without checking Rabbi Yosi's moment. But *ha lo badkah* — she did not check that period. And on the other side, if Rabbi Rebbi says he concedes to the Rabbis when she did check, his concession implies that the Rabbis would hold there is no concern even without any checking — which is equally untenable. The same contradiction recurs. *Kashya de-Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra a-de-Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra* — Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra contradicts himself.

### Two Tannaim

The Gemara resolves: *trei tannai ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra* — there are two different Tannaim each transmitting a different version of Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra's ruling.

### Twilight Timing

The two versions reflect a disagreement about the precise temporal relationship between Rabbi Yehuda's *bein hashmashos* and Rabbi Yosi's. One Tanna holds: Rabbi Yosi's moment comes *after* Rabbi Yehuda's nine minutes — it is a distinct period that follows sequentially. Therefore, even if a woman checked the entire nine minutes, she has not covered Rabbi Yosi's *bein hashmashos*, and the concern for a three-count remains. The other Tanna holds: *bein hashmashos de-Rabbi Yosi mishach shaycha be-de-Rabbi Yehuda* — Rabbi Yosi's moment falls *within* Rabbi Yehuda's nine-minute window, not after it. If that is so, then a woman who checked the full nine minutes has covered Rabbi Yosi's moment as well, and *bein hashmashos* is entirely eliminated from consideration. The disagreement between the two versions of Rabbi Yehuda ben Agra's ruling is thus not about the substance of the *zavah* concern, but about this underlying dispute regarding the sequential or overlapping relationship of the two *bein hashmashos* periods.

## Retroactive Tumah

### Rabbi Strict

The Gemara transitions to a *baraita* addressing retroactive *tumah* from *ketamim*: how far backward in time does a discovered stain render the woman *teme'ah* with respect to things she may have touched? The general principle for a woman who actually saw blood is twenty-four hours retroactively. But for *ketamim*, because the stain may have been on the garment for an extended period — potentially since the last time she laundered — Rabbi Rebbi holds that she is retroactively *teme'ah* for herself and for *kodashim* going all the way back to the last laundering. This is in one respect *more* strict than a direct *re'iyah*.

### Shimon Lenient

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees: *shelo yehei ketamah chamur me-reiyatah* — the law of stains should not be more strict than the law of actual seeing. When she actually saw blood, retroactive concern extends only twenty-four hours. For a stain, she should be no more concerned than that.

### Sacrifice Problem

The Gemara challenges: but you yourself, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, acknowledge that *kodashim* she touched are affected going all the way back to the last laundering. That is more strict than the twenty-four-hour rule of actual seeing. You are already conceding a greater stringency.

### Full Correction

The Gemara therefore corrects the text of the *baraita*: *tani hachi* — read it as follows. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: *af kodashim einah metama'ah, shelo yehei ketamah chamur me-reiyatah le-chol davar* — even *kodashim* she is not required to declare retroactively *teme'ah* beyond what the rules of actual seeing would impose. There is no dimension — not for herself, not for *kodashim* — in which *ketamim* should impose greater strictness than a direct *re'iyah*. Whether Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would also impose at least twenty-four hours retroactively, or whether his position is even more lenient, remains unresolved.

## Stain Connection

### 24 Hour Rule

The Gemara cites another *baraita*: *ra'asah ketem ve-achar kach ra'asah dam, tola ketamah be-reiyatah me-eis le-eis, divrei Rabbi* — if a woman found a stain and then, within twenty-four hours, she saw actual bleeding from her body, she may attribute the stain retroactively to that bleeding episode. This is enormously consequential: rather than worrying that the stain had been on her garment for weeks, she can assume it arrived just now, connected to this *re'iyah*. And further — since we are now confident enough to treat the stain as the beginning of her *niddah* period, her counting of the seven days begins from the time of the stain, not from the time of the direct bleeding. This is an instance where a *ketem* is treated with the weight of genuine *niddah* blood, potentially even on a *de-oraita* level, given how much supporting evidence we have.

### Same Day Stricter

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar disagrees: *yomo* — the stain and the bleeding must occur on the same halakhic day, not merely within twenty-four hours. If she found the stain on Wednesday and bled on Thursday — even if fewer than twenty-four hours elapsed — he does not connect them.

### Rebbi Agrees

Rabbi Rebbi comments: *me-ein dvarav mi-dvarai* — the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar makes more sense than my own, *she-hu metaknah va-ani me-avtah* — he is helping her while I am burdening her. Rabbi Rebbi acknowledges that a position more favorable to the woman is preferable.

### Consequences

The Gemara pushes back: how is Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar helping her? By limiting the connection to the same halakhic day, he is actually giving her *less* time to make the attribution — twenty-four hours is more generous than "same day only." Ravina suggests reversing the attributions. Rav Nachman, however, argues that no reversal is necessary: *lo le'olam lo teipach*. There is a dimension in which Rabbi Rebbi's twenty-four-hour rule is in fact more strict than Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's same-day rule, and it concerns the transition from *niddah* to *zavah* status. If the stain is found on Tuesday and bleeding follows on Wednesday, Rabbi Rebbi — who allows a twenty-four-hour connection — will say that her *niddah* counting began Tuesday. If she then bleeds again on the following Tuesday, that falls in her *zavah* period and renders her a *zavah*. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, who does not connect across halakhic days in this case, would start her *niddah* count from Wednesday's direct bleeding — meaning the subsequent Tuesday's bleeding falls within her *niddah* period, not her *zavah* period, and she may immerse that night. So *she-hu metakein hilchos niddah li-yedei zivah* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar fixes the *niddah* laws in a way that avoids pulling her into *zavah* status, while Rabbi Rebbi's rule can inadvertently do so.

## Hefsek Tahara

### Question Asked

Rav Zeira poses a question to Rav Assi: *ketamim tzerichin hefsek tahara o lo* — does a woman who is *teme'ah* by virtue of a stain require a *hefsek tahara* before immersing in the *mikvah*? The issue is significant: for a *niddah*, a *hefsek tahara* — a bodily examination at the end of day seven — is required before immersion. But a woman made *teme'ah* by a *ketem* is only rabbinically *teme'ah*, and since there is always doubt whether the stain genuinely came from her, one might argue she need not perform the examination. Alternatively, since it was not a direct *re'iyah* but only a small stain, perhaps the requirement to actively confirm cessation of bleeding does not apply.

### Silence

Rav Assi was silent and gave no answer.

### Debate Later

On a subsequent occasion, Rav Zeira found Rav Assi engaged in learning and presenting the ruling of Rabbi Rebbi: that a woman who finds a stain and then bleeds within twenty-four hours may retroactively connect the stain to the bleeding. In connection with this, Rav Assi cited the dispute between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan.

### Reish Lakish

Reish Lakish holds: *ve-hu she-badkah* — the ruling that we connect the stain to the subsequent bleeding and begin the *niddah* count from the stain applies only when she checked herself on the night between the stain and the direct *re'iyah*. By performing that check — in preparation, as it were, to immerse — she herself demonstrates that she treats the stain as the beginning of her *niddah* period. Without such a check, she has implicitly indicated that she does not attribute the stain to herself, and therefore the stain cannot retroactively serve as the opening of her *niddah* count.

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *af al pi she-lo badkah* — even if she did not check, the objective fact of the stain followed by bleeding within twenty-four hours is sufficient to connect them. Her personal conduct — whether or not she checked — is irrelevant to the objective halakhic determination.

### Recognition

Rav Zeira then said to Rav Assi: from the fact that Reish Lakish requires a checking — *mikhlal de-komi tzricha hefsek tahara* — you can infer that *ketamim* do indeed require a *hefsek tahara*, because the checking that Reish Lakish references *is* the *hefsek tahara*. Rav Assi confirmed: *in* — yes, you are right. Rav Zeira then noted that he had asked Rav Assi this very question many times and received no answer. Rav Assi replied: *agav shitpach asya li* — it arose through the flow of my learning. Caught in the rhythm of the discussion, the answer surfaced in a way it had not before. Whether the *hefsek tahara* required for *ketamim* operates in precisely the same way as for a full *niddah* status — given that in the Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan case there is an actual subsequent *re'iyah*, making it at least partially *de-oraita* — is a question that *Tosafot* takes up separately, but the basic principle is established here: *ketamim* do require a *hefsek tahara*.
