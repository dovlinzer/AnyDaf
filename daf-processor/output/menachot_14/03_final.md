# Menachot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbi Yosi vs. Chachamim

### Pigul Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁחַט שְׁנֵי כְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל אַחַת מִן הַחַלּוֹת לְמָחָר, הִקְטִיר שְׁנֵי בָּזִיכִין לֶאֱכוֹל אֶחָד מִן הַסְּדָרִים לְמָחָר, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אוֹתָהּ הַחַלָּה וְאוֹתוֹ הַסֵּדֶר שֶׁחִישֵּׁב עָלָיו – פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, וְהַשֵּׁנִי פָּסוּל וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וָזֶה פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one slaughtered** the **two lambs** that accompany the two meal offering loaves sacrificed on *Shavuot* with the intent **to partake of one of the** two **loaves the next day,** or if **one burned** the **two bowls** of frankincense accompanying the shewbread with the intent **to partake of one of the arrangements** of the shewbread **the next day, Rabbi Yosei says: That loaf and that arrangement of which he intended** to partake the next day are ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** their consumption, **and the second** loaf and arrangement are **unfit, but there is no** liability to receive ***karet* for** their consumption. **And the Rabbis say: This** loaf and arrangement **and that** loaf and arrangement are both ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** their consumption.

The daf begins well into the discussion of *pigul*, a topic that occupied much of Masechet Zevachim and that in Menachot affords even richer opportunities for analysis — not only regarding *pigul* in its own right, but using *pigul* as a lens through which to examine the relationships between the various components of a *korban*. How do the *kometz* and the *levonah*, for instance, operate jointly or independently? These structural questions animate the sugya at every turn.

### Shavuot Lambs & Loaves

The Mishnah addressed the case of the two lambs brought on Shavuot — *shenei kevasim* — whose slaughter renders the two loaves of bread, the *shetei halechem*, permitted for consumption. Similarly, the two bowls of frankincense on the *shulchan* (*bazikhin*) permit the *lechem hapanim* to be eaten. These cases raise the question of *mefaglin be-chatzi matir* — whether a *pigul* thought entertained during only one of the two lambs, or one of the two bowls, suffices. But the primary debate introduced by the Mishnah is a different one: if the *kohen* performing the service intends to eat one of the two loaves at the wrong time, does *pigul* attach to both loaves or only to the one he was thinking about?

The general principle is that if one thinks during the service of a *korban* about eating even a minimal amount at the wrong time, the *pigul* status applies to the entire *korban*. The question here is how to define the "object" — what counts as a single unit for this purpose. *Rabbi Yosi* holds that the *pigul* status attaches only to the loaf one was thinking about; the *Chachamim* hold that it attaches to everything, treating both loaves as a single unit.

### Rabbi Yosi's Narrow View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, פִּיגֵּל בַּיָּרֵךְ שֶׁל יָמִין – לֹא נִתְפַּגֵּל הַיָּרֵךְ שֶׁל שְׂמֹאל. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא: לָא עֲדִיפָא מַחְשָׁבָה מִמַּעֲשֵׂה הַטּוּמְאָה, אִילּוּ אִיטַּמִּי חַד אֵבֶר מִי אִיטַּמִּי לֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ? וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא: ״וְהַנֶּפֶשׁ הָאֹכֶלֶת מִמֶּנּוּ עֲוֹנָהּ תִּשָּׂא״ – מִמֶּנּוּ וְלֹא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna says: Rabbi Yosei would say,** in accordance with his opinion that intent of *piggul* with regard to one loaf or one arrangement does not render the second loaf or arrangement *piggul*, that if one **had intent of *piggul* with regard to the right thigh,** i.e., he slaughtered an offering with the intent to partake of the right thigh the next day, then **the left thigh has not become *piggul*** and one is not liable to receive *karet* for its consumption. **What is the reason** for this? **If you wish, propose a logical argument, and if you wish, cite a verse.** Rav Huna elaborates: **If you wish, propose a logical argument:** Disqualifying **intent is no stronger than an incident of ritual impurity,** and **if one limb** of an offering **became impure, did the entire** offering then **become impure?** Accordingly, one limb can be rendered *piggul* without the other. **And if you wish, cite a verse** that addresses *piggul*: **"And the soul that eats of it shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 7:18). The verse indicates that one who eats specifically **"of it,"** i.e., from one part, shall bear his iniquity, **and not** one who eats **from the other** part of the offering.

The Gemara initially attempts to read Rabbi Yosi's position in an even more radical direction. On this reading, Rabbi Yosi would not merely distinguish between separate loaves as distinct objects; he would maintain that *pigul* is so localized that even within a single *korban*, only the precise portion one was thinking about becomes *pigul*. So if one intended to eat specifically from the right thigh, the left thigh would remain unaffected. The Gemara pursues this interpretation, though — as the lecturer makes clear from the outset — it will ultimately be rejected. The conclusion toward which the entire discussion moves is this: everyone agrees that *pigul* applies to the whole defined object; the debate is exclusively about how that object is defined.

### Half-Kezayit Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתִיבֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב הוּנָא: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים, לְעוֹלָם אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן בִּכְזַיִת. בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן – אִין, בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman raised an objection to** Rav **Huna** from a *baraita*: **And the Rabbis say** that **there is never** liability to receive ***karet*** for partaking of the two loaves **unless one has intent of *piggul* with regard to an olive-bulk of both of them,** i.e., one's intent renders both loaves *piggul* only if he slaughters the lambs with the intent to consume an amount equal to an olive-bulk from both loaves combined outside their proper time. One can infer from this that if he had intent **with regard to both of them, yes,** both loaves are *piggul*. But if his intent was **with regard to** only **one of them, no,** the other loaf is not *piggul*.

The Gemara advances a *baraita* as evidence that even Rabbi Yosi would agree that *pigul* applies to the whole unit when properly defined. The *baraita* states that if one thinks about eating a *kezayit* drawn jointly from both loaves — half a *kezayit* from each — both loaves become *pigul*. The Gemara reasons: this case cannot reflect the position of the *Chachamim*, because according to the *Chachamim*, a *pigul* thought about one loaf alone would already render both loaves *pigul* — there would be no need to combine the two loaves into a shared *kezayit*. Therefore, the *baraita* must reflect the position of Rabbi Yosi, who normally treats the loaves as distinct. And yet Rabbi Yosi allows that if one conceptually joins them — by thinking about a *kezayit* spanning both — they become a single unit, and *pigul* attaches to the whole. This would demonstrate that Rabbi Yosi agrees in principle that *pigul* applies to the entire unit; his dispute is only about unit definition.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? אִילֵּימָא רַבָּנַן – אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן נָמֵי! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חַד גּוּפָא הוּא – מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מִצְטָרֵף, אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ תְּרֵי גּוּפֵי נִינְהוּ – מִי מִיצְטָרְפִי? הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת מֵחַלָּה זוֹ, וְכֵן חֲבֵירוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת מֵחַלָּה זוֹ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי שֶׁזֶּה כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman continues: In accordance with **whose** opinion is the *baraita*? **If we say** that it is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis** of the mishna, then **even** if his intent was **with regard to** only **one of them,** both loaves are rendered *piggul*. **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Now, **granted, if you say** that according to Rabbi Yosei the left and right thighs of an offering are considered **one body,** and consequently *piggul* intent with regard to one thigh renders the other thigh *piggul* as well, then **due to that** reason it is understandable that if the *piggul* intent was for an amount equal to one total olive-bulk from both of the loaves, then the intent with regard to each loaf is **combined** with the other. **But if you say** that Rabbi Yosei holds that the right and left thighs of an offering **are** considered **two** distinct **bodies,** and therefore *piggul* intent with regard to one does not render the other *piggul*, then in the case of the two loaves, **would** the intentions concerning both loaves **combine** to render them both *piggul*? One cannot infer anything from this *baraita* with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, as in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **as it is taught** in another *baraita*: With regard to **one who slaughters** one of **the lambs** brought as peace offerings on *Shavuot* with the intent **to consume half an olive-bulk from this loaf** the next day, **and similarly,** he slaughtered **the other** lamb with the intent **to consume half an olive-bulk from that** second **loaf** the next day, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say that this** offering is **valid,** as his intentions do not combine.

The Gemara, however, deflects this proof. It suggests that the *baraita* may not reflect Rabbi Yosi's position at all, but rather that of *Rebbi* (Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi). A *baraita* is cited: one who slaughters the first lamb with the intent to eat a half-*kezayit* from one loaf, and then slaughters the second lamb with the intent to eat a half-*kezayit* from the other loaf, is told by Rebbi: *omer ani shezeh kasher* — I say this is entirely valid, no *pigul* has been created.

### Chatzi Matir Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר ״חֲצִי חֲצִי״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״כְּזַיִת מִשְּׁתֵּיהֶן״ – מִצְטָרֵף. וְרַבִּי, אַלִּיבָּא דְּמַאן? אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן – אֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן נָמֵי! אִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – הָדַר קוּשְׁיַין לְדוּכְתֵּיהּ. לְעוֹלָם אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן, וְלָא תֵּימָא ״עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן״, אֶלָּא [אֵימָא] ״עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בִּשְׁנֵיהֶן״, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן. וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: מְפַגְּלִין בַּחֲצִי מַתִּיר, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna continues: It may be inferred that **the reason** why the priest's intentions do not combine is **that** his intent was **said** with regard to **a half** and **a half,** i.e., he slaughtered each lamb with the intent to consume half an olive-bulk from one loaf the next day. **But** if he **said** during the slaughter of each of the lambs that he is slaughtering it with the intent to consume **an olive-bulk from both of them,** then the halves **combine** to render the offering *piggul*. The Gemara asks: **And** as for **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who says that the two loaves are *piggul* only if he has intent with regard to an amount equal to an olive-bulk from both of them combined, **in accordance with whose** opinion is his statement? **If** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis** of the mishna, who hold that *piggul* intent with regard to one loaf renders both loaves *piggul*, then **even** if his intent was **with regard to** only **one of them,** both loaves should be *piggul*. And **if** it is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that *piggul* intent with regard to one loaf does not render the second loaf *piggul*, then **our difficulty returns to its place:** If Rabbi Yosei holds that the right and left thighs are considered two distinct bodies, how can intentions with regard to two halves of an olive-bulk combine to render both loaves *piggul*? The Gemara responds: **Actually,** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement is **in accordance with** the opinion **of the Rabbis. And do not say** that the *baraita* states: It is not *piggul* **unless one has** the **intent of *piggul* with regard to both of them [*bishteihen*],** in the feminine form, whereby the *baraita* would be referring to the loaves. **Rather,** the *baraita* states: **With regard to both of them [*bishneihen*],** in the masculine form, i.e., unless he slaughters both lambs with *piggul* intent, **and** in such a case, **even** if his intent was with regard to only **one of the** loaves, the offering is *piggul*. The Gemara adds: **And** this *baraita* serves **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir, who says** in the mishna on 16a: **One renders** an offering ***piggul*** by means of intent during the sacrifice **of half a permitting factor,** e.g., if one slaughtered one of the lambs with the intent to consume the two loaves the next day, the loaves are *piggul*. This *baraita* **teaches us that** this is **not** the *halakha*.

The reason Rebbi gives is instructive. It is not merely that the two half-*kezayit* thoughts fail to combine; it is that the thought during each lamb was entertained during only a *chatzi matir* — half of the operative act. Since both lambs together constitute the *matir* that permits the loaves, each lamb individually is only half of that enabling act. A *pigul* thought during half of a *matir* does not create *pigul*, in accordance with the principle of *ein mefaglin be-chatzi matir*.

### Same Thought Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי ״לְעוֹלָם״? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן וּבִשְׁנֵיהֶן, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וּמִדְּרַבָּנַן קָאָתֵי – הַיְינוּ דְּקָאָמַר ״לְעוֹלָם״. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ רַבָּנַן, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, מַאי ״לְעוֹלָם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** then **what** is the meaning of the emphasis in the *baraita*: There is **never** liability? **Granted,** this phrase is understandable **if you say** that the *baraita* means that the loaves are not *piggul* unless he has intent **with regard to both of the** loaves **and both of the** lambs, i.e., they are *piggul* only if he slaughters both lambs with the intention to partake of both loaves the next day. In that case the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, and it comes to exclude** the statements **of** both **Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis,** and **this is** the reason **that** the *baraita* **states:** There is **never** liability, to emphasize that Rabbi Yosei disagrees with both of these opinions. **But if you say** that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis and** that it serves **to exclude** only the opinion **of Rabbi Meir,** for **what** reason does the *baraita* stress: There is **never** liability? The *tanna* would not use such a word to exclude merely one opinion. Rather, it must be that the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. If so, then the difficulty raised against Rav Huna, who says that *piggul* intent concerning the right thigh does not render the left one *piggul*, remains unresolved.

The Gemara then clarifies what *would* create *pigul* according to Rebbi: if during the slaughter of each lamb one had the very same thought — "I intend to eat a *kezayit* taken jointly from both loaves" — then both lambs carried the identical *pigul* intention throughout the complete act. This resolves the *chatzi matir* problem, since the same intent persisted through both halves of the *matir*, and *pigul* would result. The Gemara thus distinguishes sharply between two scenarios: thinking a half-*kezayit* from each loaf separately during each respective lamb (no *pigul*, due to *chatzi matir*), versus thinking the same combined *kezayit* from both loaves during each lamb (genuine *pigul*).

This means the *baraita* that seemed to prove Rabbi Yosi's position was actually about Rebbi's position and the principle of *ein mefaglin be-chatzi matir* — the requirement that one have the *pigul* thought during the full *matir*, not merely during one half of it. The word *bishneihem* (masculine, referring to the lambs) rather than *bishteihen* (feminine, referring to the loaves) is the key: Rebbi requires the thought during both lambs, not necessarily about both loaves. This, the Gemara concludes, reflects the view against Rabbi Meir, who holds that *mefaglin be-chatzi matir*. Once this is understood, the *lamed-vav* — the word *l'olam* ("never") — in the formulation makes sense: it flags that two separate requirements must both be met, against two opposing positions: against Rabbi Meir who holds one lamb suffices, and against the *Rabbanan* who hold one loaf suffices.

The reinterpretation of the *baraita* as belonging to the *Rabbanan* (not Rabbi Yosi), and as addressing the lamb requirement (not the loaf combination), means the proof for Rabbi Yosi's agreement with the whole-unit principle has dissolved. *Hadra kusha l'duchta* — the question returns to its place.

### Rashi on Lebona

At this juncture, Rashi raises a point that demands attention. If we accept the principle of *ein mefaglin be-chatzi matir*, a question arises about our paradigm case of *pigul*: how can taking the *kometz* of a *mincha* create *pigul*, given that there is a second *matir* — the *levonah* — which has not yet been burned? Would not the *kemitza* alone constitute only a *chatzi matir*?

Rashi answers that the concern about *chatzi matir* applies only at the stage of *haktara* — the burning. At that stage, both the *kometz* and the *levonah* must be burned, and having a *pigul* thought during the burning of one without the other raises the *chatzi matir* issue. But at the *kemitza* stage, no parallel act exists for the *levonah*: the Torah does not describe any *avoda* of gathering the *levonah* prior to burning it. The only *avoda* prescribed for the *levonah* is the burning itself. Therefore, during *kemitza*, *holecha*, or *nesinat kelli*, there is only one thing happening — the acts associated with the *kometz* — and the *chatzi matir* concern simply does not apply at that stage. The *levonah* enters the picture only at the *haktara* stage, when both must be burned together.

This explanation, while elegant, leaves open the question of why the gathering of the *levonah* — which some authorities consider a valid *avoda* — would not also count as a parallel *matir* act alongside *kemitza*. Rashi's position is noted here without full resolution.

## Proofs for Whole Unit

### Inner Chatat Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: פִּיגֵּל בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ – פִּיגֵּל, בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בִּפְנִים – לֹא פִּיגֵּל. כֵּיצַד? הָיָה עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט עַל מְנָת לְהַזּוֹת מִדָּמוֹ לְמָחָר״ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בַּחוּץ בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בְּפָנִים. הָיָה עוֹמֵד בִּפְנִים וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי מַזֶּה עַל מְנָת לְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִין לְמָחָר וְלִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִים לְמָחָר״ – לֹא פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בִּפְנִים בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ. הָיָה עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵינִי שׁוֹחֵט עַל מְנָת לִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִים לְמָחָר וּלְהַקְטִיר אֵימוּרִין לְמָחָר״ – פִּיגֵּל, שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה בַּחוּץ בְּדָבָר הַנַּעֲשֶׂה בַּחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, didn't Rav Ashi say: Come** and **hear** a refutation of the opinion of Rav Huna from a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says in the name of Rabbi Yosei** that if, while performing the sacrificial rites for the bulls or goats which are burned as an offering, the priest had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul* with regard to a matter that is performed outside** the Sanctuary, i.e., in the Temple courtyard, **he has rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** If his intention was **with regard to a matter that is performed inside** the Sanctuary or the Holy of Holies, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If **he was standing outside** when slaughtering the animal **and said: I hereby slaughter** the animal **with the intention of sprinkling its blood tomorrow** inside the Sanctuary, **he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*.** The reason is **that** when one has **an intention outside with regard to a matter that is performed inside,** he has not rendered the offering *piggul*. Likewise, if he **was standing inside** when sprinkling, **and said: I hereby sprinkle** the blood of the sin offering **in order to burn** its **sacrificial portions** on the external altar **tomorrow and to pour out** its **remainder** on the base of the altar **tomorrow, he has not rendered** the offering ***piggul*, as** he had **an intention inside with regard to a matter that is performed outside.** **But** if **he was standing outside and said: I hereby slaughter** the animal **with the intention to pour out** the **remainder** of its blood **tomorrow, or to burn** its **sacrificial portions tomorrow, he has rendered** the offering ***piggul*, as** he had **an intention outside with regard to a matter that is performed outside.**

The Gemara now turns to positive proof that Rabbi Yosi agrees *pigul* applies to the whole unit — that his dispute with the *Chachamim* is exclusively about unit definition, not about whether *pigul* spreads through a single defined unit. The first proof is introduced with the formula *de-ha amar Rav Ashi ta shma* — "did not Rav Ashi say, come and hear." This framing is slightly unusual: ordinarily one who says *ta shma* is the one answering a question. The suggestion is that at an earlier editorial stage, Rav Ashi responded to the question about Rabbi Yosi's view by citing this *baraita*, and the later editorial framing has embedded that citation into its current context.

The *baraita* cited by Rav Ashi is from the *Sifra* (Torat Kohanim) and concerns *pigul* by the inner *chatat* — *chatat ha-pnimiot*. The inner *chatat* involves sprinkling blood inside the *Heichal* (toward the *parochet*) and burning the meat outside the *Mikdash*. Examples include the Yom Kippur bull and the *par he'elam davar* brought when the *Beit Din* rules incorrectly and the entire community acts upon that erroneous ruling.

The paradigm for *pigul* in the Torah is *shelamim*, where both the slaughter and the blood service occur outside the *Heichal*, on the outer *mizbe'ach*. Rabbi Yosi (as quoted by Rebbi) derives from this that *pigul* by the inner *chatat* requires a precise structural parallel: the *pigul* thought must occur during an *avoda* performed outside the *Heichal*, and that thought must concern an act that is likewise performed outside the *Heichal*.

The *baraita* enumerates the cases: if one stands outside the *Heichal* and says "I am slaughtering this with the intent to sprinkle its blood tomorrow" — no *pigul*, because although the thought occurred outside, the intended act (the sprinkling) is performed inside the *Heichal*. If one stands inside the *Heichal* and says during the sprinkling "I will burn the *imurin* tomorrow" or "I will pour the *shirayim* tomorrow" — acts done on the outer *mizbe'ach* — again no *pigul*, because although the intended act is outside, the thought occurred inside. Only when one stands outside during *shechita* and intends to pour the *shirayim* on the outer *mizbe'ach* tomorrow, or to burn the *imurin* tomorrow, does *pigul* result: thought outside, intended act outside.

### Blood Spilling Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִשְׁפּוֹךְ שִׁירַיִים, לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי מַאי? אִילֵימָא לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי דָּם – דָּם מִי מִיפַּגַּל? וְהָתְנַן: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל: הַקּוֹמֶץ, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשִׁיחַ, וְהַדָּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: With regard to the case where one slaughtered the offering with the intent **to pour the remaining** blood the next day, **what could be rendered *piggul*? If we say** the **blood could be rendered *piggul*,** one can ask: **Does blood become *piggul*? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 42b): **These are** the **items for which one is not liable** to receive *karet* **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*: The handful; the frankincense; the incense; the meal offering of priests; the meal offering** accompanying the **libations** brought with an animal offering; **the meal offering of** the **anointed priest; and the blood?**

A sharp question now emerges. When one slaughters with the intent to pour the *shirayim* at the wrong time, what exactly becomes *pigul*? The *shirayim* themselves? The *dam* (blood)? The Gemara considers each possibility methodically. Could it be the *dam* that becomes *pigul*? No — *dam lo mifagal*. Blood does not become *piggul*. This is established by a *mishna* that lists the items that cannot become *pigul* even when one has had a *pigul* thought during their service: the *kometz*, the *levonah*, the *ketores*, the *minchat kohen*, the *minchat nesachim*, the *minchat kohen mashiach*, and the blood. The reason: all these items are themselves *matirs* — they are the enabling acts that permit the *korban* to proceed to its next stage. *Pigul* operates by rendering *pigul* what the *matir* is working upon, not the *matir* itself. Thus the blood, which is the *matir* enabling the meat to be eaten and the fats to be burned, cannot itself become *pigul*.

### Meat Becomes Pigul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי בָּשָׂר, הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם דְּלָא חַשֵּׁיב בֵּיהּ בְּבָשָׂר גּוּפֵיהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי מִיפַּגַּל, הָכָא דְּחַשֵּׁיב בֵּיהּ בְּזֶבַח גּוּפֵיהּ, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן דְּפִיגֵּל בְּיֶרֶךְ יָמִין פִּיגֵּל בְּיֶרֶךְ שְׂמֹאל?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is **obvious** that the *baraita* means that it is the **meat** of the offering that **could be rendered *piggul*. Now** consider: **And if there,** in the *baraita*, **where he did not have intent with regard to the meat itself,** as his intention was not to partake of the meat the next day but to pour the remaining blood the next day, and yet **Rabbi Yosei said** that the meat **is rendered *piggul*;** then **here, where he has intent** to partake of the right thigh the next day, which is part **of the offering itself,** is it **not all the more so that** if **he had intent of *piggul* with regard to the right thigh, he has rendered the left thigh *piggul*** as well?

If the blood cannot become *piggul*, what can? The *basar* — the meat. And here lies the proof. One was thinking about the *shirayim* (blood), yet what becomes *piggul* is the meat. The *pigul* thought was about one component of the *korban*, and the *pigul* status attached to a completely different component. The Gemara draws the inference explicitly: *hashta u'mah hasam d'lo chashiv beih basar gufei amar Rabbi Yose mesagel* — "now, if even there, where one did not think about the meat at all but only about spilling the blood at the wrong time, Rabbi Yosi says *pigul* applies — then here, where one is thinking about the *zevach* itself, specifically about one thigh, how much more so should *pigul* spread to the other thigh." The *a fortiori* argument is powerful: if a *pigul* thought about the blood — a distinct component from the meat — renders the meat *piggul*, certainly a *pigul* thought about one part of the meat renders the other part *piggul* as well.

One might respond, as was noted during discussion, that the blood has a unique relationship to the whole *korban* as its *matir* — so naturally a *pigul* thought about the *matir* affects everything dependent upon it. But when one thinks about a specific piece of the edible meat, perhaps the effect is limited to that piece. The Gemara acknowledges this implicitly by adducing a second proof.

### Kometz Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָאָמַר רָבִינָא: תָּא שְׁמַע, הַקּוֹמֵץ אֶת הַמִּנְחָה לֶאֱכוֹל שְׁיָרֶיהָ אוֹ לְהַקְטִיר קוּמְצָהּ לְמָחָר, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בָּזוֹ שֶׁפִּיגֵּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. לְהַקְטִיר קוּמְצָהּ, לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי מַאי? אִילֵימָא לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי קוֹמֶץ, קוֹמֶץ מִי מִיפַּגַּל? וְהָתְנַן: אֵלּוּ דְּבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל: הַקּוֹמֶץ כּוּ׳! אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא לְאִיפַּגּוֹלֵי שִׁירַיִים. הַשְׁתָּא וּמָה הָתָם, דְּלָא חַשֵּׁיב בְּהוּ בְּשִׁירַיִים גּוּפֵיהּ, מִיפַּגְּלִי – הָכָא דְּחַשֵּׁיב בְּהוּ בִּזְבִיחָה גּוּפַהּ, לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, doesn't Ravina say: Come** and **hear** a refutation of the statement of Rav Huna from the mishna (13a): In the case of **one who removes a handful from the meal offering** with the intent **to partake of its remainder or to burn its handful** on **the next day, Rabbi Yosei concedes in this** instance **that he has rendered** the offering ***piggul* and he is liable** to receive ***karet* for** partaking of **it.** Ravina continues: When one removes the handful with the intent **to burn its handful, what could be rendered *piggul*? If we say** that **the handful could be rendered *piggul*, does the handful become *piggul*? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 42b): **These are** the **items for which one is not liable** to receive *karet* **due to** the prohibition of ***piggul*: The handful, etc. Rather,** it is **obvious** that the **remainder could be rendered *piggul*. Now** consider: **And if there,** in the mishna, **where he did not have intent with regard to** the **remainder itself,** i.e., to partake of the remainder the next day, and yet Rabbi Yosei said that the remainder **is rendered *piggul*;** then **here, where he has intent** to partake of the right thigh the next day, which is part **of** the **offering itself,** is it **not all the more so** that both thighs should become *piggul*?

Ravina adds another demonstration, introduced again with the formula *amar Ravina ta shma*. The case: one takes the *kometz* of a *mincha* with intent to eat the *shirayim* tomorrow, or to burn the *kometz* tomorrow. Rabbi Yosi agrees — *modu Rabbi Yose b'zo shepigel vechayavin alav karet* — that *pigul* results and one is liable to *karet* for eating it. This is not in doubt. The Gemara then probes: what becomes *piggul*? Can it be the *kometz*? No — the *kometz* is a *matir* and, as established, *matirs* themselves do not become *piggul*. Therefore, the *shirayim* — the remainder of the *mincha* — become *piggul*. And the inference follows: *hashta u'ma hatam d'lo chashivei be'shirayim gufei mifagli* — even though one was thinking only about the *kometz*, the *shirayim* become *piggul*. If that is so when the thought concerned the *matir* itself, certainly when one thinks about one part of the edible matter the *pigul* status should spread to the whole *korban*.

### Unit Definition

Both proofs carry the same structural limitation noted in discussion: one could argue that in both cases — blood and *kometz* — the *pigul* thought concerns the *matir*, and it is natural for a *pigul* thought about the *matir* to affect everything the *matir* enables. This is a less compelling proof of what happens when the thought concerns part of the edible portion itself. Nonetheless, the Gemara treats the two proofs as decisive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – הַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן גּוּף אֶחָד, וְהַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן שְׁנֵי גּוּפִין; גּוּף אֶחָד – דִּמְעַכְּבִי אַהֲדָדֵי, שְׁנֵי גּוּפִין – דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: הָא לְחוֹדַהּ עֲבִידָא וְהָא לְחוֹדַהּ עֲבִידָא. עָרְבִינְהוּ – מִתְעָרְבִין, דְּהַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן גּוּף אֶחָד; פַּלְגִינְהוּ מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּהַכָּתוּב עֲשָׂאָן שְׁנֵי גּוּפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** Rabbi Yosei holds that intent of *piggul* with regard to one thigh renders the other thigh *piggul* as well, as they are of one body. Similarly, with regard to two loaves, Rabbi Yosei is of the opinion that if one intends to consume an amount equal to an olive-bulk from both loaves, both loaves are rendered *piggul*. And as for his statement that intent of *piggul* with regard to one loaf does not render the other loaf *piggul*, **this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosei: The verse renders** the two loaves **one body, and the verse** also **renders them two bodies.** The verse renders them **one body** in the sense **that they preclude one another,** i.e., neither loaf is valid without the other. The verse also renders them **two bodies, as the Merciful One states: This** loaf **is prepared alone and that is prepared alone,** i.e., the kneading and arrangement of each loaf must be performed separately. Therefore, if the priest **mixed them** together by intending to consume an olive-bulk from both of them, then **they are mixed** and they are both *piggul*, **as the verse renders them one body.** But if **he separated them** by having intent with regard to only one loaf, in that case **they are separated** and only that loaf is *piggul*, **as the verse renders them two bodies.**

The conclusion, attributed to Rabbi Yochanan, is unambiguous: *hainu ta'ama de'Rabbi Yose* — this is the reasoning of Rabbi Yosi. He agrees entirely in principle that *pigul* applies to the whole unit. *Ha-katuiv asan guf echad ve-ha-katuiv asan shnei gufim* — the Torah itself treats the *shetei halechem* both as one unit and as two units. The debate between Rabbi Yosi and the *Chachamim* is therefore not a fundamental dispute about whether *pigul* spreads through a unit, but a specific dispute about the unit definition of the *shetei halechem* and *lechem hapanim*.

## One and Two Units

### Torah's Dual Language

Rabbi Yochanan's resolution is built on a close reading of the Torah's language regarding the *shetei halechem*. The Torah employs both singular and plural designations for these two loaves. One verse uses the singular *lechem* — "you shall bring *lechem* of waving" — treating the two loaves as a single entity. Another verse specifies *shtayim* and *shnei esronim* — two loaves, two tenth-*ephah* portions — treating them as distinct objects. The same principle is then extended by *seder* to the *lechem hapanim*, which the Gemara notes is derived (*yalfinan*) from the *shetei halechem*.

### Interdependence

The Torah's treatment of the loaves as one unit is evidenced by their mutual interdependence: *de-ma'akvi ahadadi* — they prevent one another from being valid. If only one loaf is present, the *korban* is not fulfilled. The *korban* requires both; neither is complete without the other. This functional unity justifies viewing them as a single *guf* in one respect.

### Separate Creation

At the same time, the Torah treats them as two separate units — *shnei gufim* — because, as the Gemara notes, *de-amar Rachmana hai lachudei avidah ve-hai lachudei avidah*: the Torah indicates that each loaf is made independently. One does not prepare a single large dough and divide it; each loaf requires its own separate dough, kneaded and formed individually. This physical and procedural distinctness is what makes each loaf its own *guf* in another respect.

### Conceptual Combination

The practical rule that emerges from Rabbi Yosi's position is as follows. *Ervinhu mitarvin* — if one conceptually combines the loaves, by framing one's *pigul* intention in terms of a *kezayit* drawn from both together, one has invoked the Torah's recognition of them as a single unit. Since the Torah does treat them as one *guf* in the sense that each requires the other, this conceptual joining is legitimate, and *pigul* attaches to both. *Pilginhu mefalgi* — if one separates them conceptually, thinking only of a *kezayit* from one loaf without reference to the other, then the Torah's alternative recognition of them as two separate *gufim* governs, and *pigul* attaches only to the loaf one was thinking about.

This is the precise scope and logic of Rabbi Yosi's position. He does not limit *pigul* to a fragment of a unit; he limits it to the unit as defined. The dispute with the *Chachamim* is therefore about which model — the Torah's single-unit reading or its two-unit reading — is the operative default when one has not explicitly combined or separated the loaves in one's intention.

### Generalized Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פִּיגֵּל בְּלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה מַהוּ, בְּמִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה מַהוּ? תְּנָא לֵיהּ רַב תַּחְלִיפָא מִמַּעְרְבָא: וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּלַחְמֵי תוֹדָה, וְכֵן אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בְּמִנְחַת מַאֲפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raises a dilemma:** If one **had intent of *piggul* with regard to** one type of loaf from the **loaves of a thanks offering, what is** the *halakha* concerning the remaining types of loaves, i.e., are they rendered *piggul* as well? Similarly, if one had intent of *piggul* **with regard to** either the loaves or the wafers of **baked meal offerings, what is** the *halakha* with regard to the remaining type? **Rav Taḥlifa from the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **taught him** a *baraita* that states: **And likewise you say with regard to the bread of a thanks offering, and likewise you say with regard to a baked meal offering,** that the *halakha* is a matter of dispute between Rabbi Yosei and the Rabbis.

Rabbi Yochanan then poses the question of how far Rabbi Yosi's principle extends. *Ba'ei Rabbi Yochanan: pigul be-lachmei toda mahu? Be-minchat ma'aseh mahu?* Does the same logic apply to the forty loaves of *lachmei toda*, or to the *minchat ma'aseh tanur* whose breads come in two distinct forms — loaves and wafers? Are those collections each internally one unit, or are they multiple distinct units?

The question was answered when Rav Tachlifa from the west — from Eretz Yisrael — presented a *baraita* that explicitly extended Rabbi Yosi's ruling to these other cases: *vechen ata omer be-lachmei toda, vechen ata omer be-minchat ma'aseh*. Rabbi Yosi consistently applies his framework across all breads brought as *korbanot*: where the Torah provides grounds for categorizing collections of breads as distinct, a *pigul* thought about one collection does not affect another. So the two columns of *lechem hapanim*, the four varieties of *lachmei toda*, the two forms of *minchat ma'aseh tanur* — each collection is treated as its own unit, and *pigul* on one does not spread to another unless one has conceptually combined them.

## Combining Avodot

### Shechita & Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁעַת שְׁחִיטָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת, וּבִשְׁעַת זְרִיקָה חִישֵּׁב לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת – פִּיגּוּל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁשְּׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If **at the time of** the **slaughter** of an offering one **had intent to consume half an olive-bulk** of its meat the next day, **and at the time of** the **sprinkling** of the blood **he had intent to consume half** of another **olive-bulk** of meat the next day, the offering is ***piggul*, as** intentions that occur during the **slaughter and sprinkling combine** to render an offering *piggul*.

The Gemara now shifts to a related but distinct question: can *pigul* thoughts entertained during two separate *avodot* — not during two halves of the same *matir*, but during two complete and distinct *avodot* — combine to constitute a single *pigul* intention? A *baraita* addresses this directly: *beshe'at shechita chishav le'echol chatzi zayit, u-veshe'at zerika chishav le'echol chatzi zayit* — during *shechita* one intended to eat a half-*kezayit*, and during *zerika* one again intended a half-*kezayit*. The ruling is *pigul*, because *shechita* and *zerika* combine: *mipnei she-shechita u-zerika mitztarfin*.

It is worth pausing to distinguish this case from the earlier discussion of *chatzi matir*. There, the concern was that the *avoda* being performed constituted only half the enabling act — one lamb out of two required, one bowl of frankincense out of two. Here, each *avoda* is complete in itself — the *shechita* is the full *shechita*, the *zerika* is the full *zerika* — but the *pigul* intention at each stage covered only half a *kezayit*. The question is whether these two half-intentions, spread across two separate complete *avodot*, combine to form a full *pigul* intention.

### Which Avodot Combine

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: שְׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה, דְּתַרְוַויְיהוּ מַתִּירִין – אִין, קַבָּלָה וְהוֹלָכָה – לָא. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: הָנָךְ דִּמְרַחֲקָן, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן הָנֵי דִּמְקָרְבָן.
>
> **Translation:** There is a dispute between *amora'im* with regard to the *halakha* of this *baraita*: **Some say** that in the case of intentions that occur specifically during the **slaughter and sprinkling, as both of them are permitting factors** of the offering, **yes,** the intentions combine. But intentions that occur during the **collection** of the blood in a service vessel **and** the **conveying** of the blood to the altar do **not** combine, as neither rite is a permitting factor. **And some say** that if intentions during **those** rites **that are distant** from one another, i.e., the slaughter and sprinkling, combine, **all the more so** intentions during **these** rites **that are close** to one another, i.e., collection and conveying, certainly combine.

The *baraita* establishes that *shechita* and *zerika* combine, but it leaves open whether other pairs of *avodot* do as well.

### Matirin Opinion

One opinion limits the combination to *shechita* and *zerika* specifically, because both belong to the same category: both are *matirs*. *Shechita* enables the blood to proceed to the *mizbe'ach*; *zerika* enables the meat to be eaten and the fats to be burned. Since they share this enabling function, their respective *pigul* intentions may combine. *Kabala* and *holecha*, by contrast, are necessary *avodot* but are not themselves *matirs* in the same sense, and therefore cannot combine.

### Distant Avodot View

A second opinion inverts the logic. If even *shechita* and *zerika* — the most distant of the four *avodot*, the first and the fourth — combine, then *kabala* and *holecha*, which stand between them and are adjacent in sequence, certainly combine as well. On this view, proximity in the chain of *avoda* is a stronger basis for combination than functional category.

### Levi's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא תָּנֵי לֵוִי: אַרְבַּע עֲבוֹדוֹת אֵין מִצְטָרְפוֹת לְפִיגּוּל, שְׁחִיטָה וּזְרִיקָה, קַבָּלָה וְהוֹלָכָה! אָמַר רָבָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא רַבִּי, הָא רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** i.e., that intentions during the slaughter and sprinkling combine? **But Levi teaches** in a *baraita*: Intentions that occur during the **four** sacrificial **rites do not combine to** render an offering ***piggul*,** and those rites are: **Slaughter and sprinkling, collection and conveying. Rava said:** It is **not difficult; this** statement of Levi is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, whereas **that** *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

The Gemara presents a challenge from a *baraita* taught by Levi: *arba avodot ein mitztarfin le-pigul* — the four *avodot* do not combine at all for *pigul*. This directly contradicts the first *baraita*, which permitted the combination of *shechita* and *zerika*.

### Rabbi vs. Rabbis

Rava resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya — ha Rabbi, ha Rabbanan* — these are not conflicting rulings but represent two sides of an ongoing debate between Rebbi and the *Rabbanan*. The *baraita* that permits combination reflects one position; Levi's *baraita* reflects the other.

### Rabbi's View Clarified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַכֶּבֶשׂ לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת מֵחַלָּה זוֹ, וְכֵן חֲבֵירוֹ לֶאֱכוֹל חֲצִי זַיִת מֵחַלָּה זוֹ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אוֹמֵר אֲנִי שֶׁזֶּה כָּשֵׁר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֵימַר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי – חֲצִי מַתִּיר וַחֲצִי אֲכִילָה, כּוּלּוֹ מַתִּיר וַחֲצִי אֲכִילָה מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the two loaves and two lambs sacrificed on *Shavuot*: With regard to **one who slaughters** one of **the lambs** brought as peace offerings on *Shavuot* with the intent **to consume half an olive-bulk from this loaf** the next day, **and similarly,** he slaughtered **the other** lamb with the intent **to consume half an olive-bulk from that** second **loaf** the next day, **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: I say that this** offering is **valid.** Clearly, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that intentions that occur during the performance of two permitting factors do not combine to render an offering *piggul*. **Abaye said to** Rava: You can **say that you have heard that Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi holds that intentions do not combine to render an offering *piggul* when each intention is concerning **half a permitting factor and half** a measure of **consumption,** i.e., one lamb and half an olive-bulk. But in a case where one had intentions during the performance of **an entire permitting factor,** i.e., during the slaughter and sprinkling, **and** concerning **half** a measure of **consumption, did you hear him** say that such intentions do not combine?

Abaye, however, objects to how Rava has characterized Rebbi's position. The *baraita* used to identify Rebbi's view — the case of slaughtering one lamb with intent to eat half a *kezayit* of one loaf and the second lamb with intent to eat half a *kezayit* of the other — is not a case of two separate complete *avodot* with a half-*kezayit* thought during each. That case involves *chatzi matir* and *chatzi achila* simultaneously: not only was the *pigul* intention only half a *kezayit* each time, but each *shechita* was itself only half the *matir*. Abaye insists the case Rava is trying to address is fundamentally different: *kulo matir ve-chatzi achila* — a complete *avoda*, with a half-*kezayit* intention during each. That is the genuine question of whether half-*kezayit* thoughts during two separate complete *avodot* combine, and Rebbi's ruling in the lamb case sheds no light on it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא בַּר רַב חָנָן לְאַבָּיֵי: וְאִי אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי כּוּלּוֹ מַתִּיר וַחֲצִי אֲכִילָה, לִגְזוֹר חֲצִי מַתִּיר וַחֲצִי אֲכִילָה אַטּוּ כּוּלּוֹ מַתִּיר וַחֲצִי אֲכִילָה, דְּהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי גָּזַר וְרַבָּנַן גָּזְרִי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Ḥanan said to Abaye: But if Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is of** the opinion that intentions during the performance of **an entire permitting factor and** concerning **half** a measure of **consumption** combine to render an offering *piggul*, why does he rule that when one slaughters each lamb with the intent to consume half an olive-bulk from one loaf the next day the offering is entirely valid? **Let him decree** that intentions during **half a permitting factor and** concerning **half** a measure of **consumption** disqualify an offering, **due to** the fact that intentions during **an entire permitting factor and** concerning **half** a measure of **consumption** render the offering *piggul*, **as** one finds in similar instances that **Rabbi Yosei decreed and the Rabbis decreed** in this manner.

Rava bar Rav Chanan then presses further, noting that if Rebbi's position on the complete-*matir* case could be inferred from the absence of a rabbinic decree (*gezeira*), one might be able to establish indirectly what Rebbi holds — though this argument is introduced at the very close of the daf and its full development extends beyond the present passage.
