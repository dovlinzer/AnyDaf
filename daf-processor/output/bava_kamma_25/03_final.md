# Bava Kamma 25 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tarfon: Full Damages

### Tarfon's Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹר הַמַּזִּיק בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק, כֵּיצַד? נָגַח, נָגַף, נָשַׁךְ, רָבַץ, בָּעַט; בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק. בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן אוֹמֵר: נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is** the case of the **ox that causes damage** while **on the property of the injured** party, mentioned in an earlier mishna (15b) that listed animals that are forewarned? If the animal **gored, pushed, bit, squatted** upon, or **kicked** another animal **in the public domain,** the owner is liable to **pay half** the cost of **the damage** if the ox was innocuous, but if it acted while **on the property of the injured** party, **Rabbi Tarfon says:** He must pay the **full** cost of **the damage, and the Rabbis say:** He must pay **half** the cost of **the damage,** as in any other case classified as Goring.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: וּמָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁהֵקֵל עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, שֶׁהוּא פָּטוּר; הֶחְמִיר עֲלֵיהֶן בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – לְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם; מְקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר עַל הַקֶּרֶן – בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, לְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק; אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנַּחְמִיר עָלָיו בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – לְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon said to the** Rabbis: **If in a place where** the Torah **was lenient with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and with regard to Trampling,** specifically **in the public domain, as** the owner **is exempt** from liability, nevertheless the Torah was **strict with regard to these** forms of damage if they occurred **on the property of the injured** party, requiring him **to pay** the **full** cost of **the damage,** then in **a place where** the Torah **was strict with regard to** cases of damage classified as **Goring,** specifically **in the public domain,** requiring the owner **to pay for half** the cost of **the damage, is it not right that we** should **be strict with regard to this** form of damage if it occurs **on the property of the injured** party to likewise require the owner of the animal **to pay** the **full** cost of **the damage?**

The *mishna* opens with the dispute between Rabbi Tarfon and the *Chachamim* regarding an ox that gores in the *reshut hanizak* — the property of the injured party. Rabbi Tarfon argues that even on the first offense, when the animal would ordinarily be liable for only half-damages (*chatzi nezek*), the owner must pay full damages when the goring occurs in the *reshut hanizak*. He derives this through a *kal vachomer* from *shen* and *regel*: in the *reshut harabim*, *shen* and *regel* are entirely exempt; yet in the *reshut hanizak*, they are liable for the full amount. *Keren*, which pays half in the *reshut harabim*, should certainly — *kal vachomer* — be liable for the full amount in the *reshut hanizak*.

### Rabbis' Dayo Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין – לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן. מָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חֲצִי נֶזֶק, אַף בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** Although there is an *a fortiori* inference being applied here, still **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source,** meaning that the *halakha* cannot be stricter with the inference than it is with the case that serves as the source of the inference. Therefore, **just as** one is liable to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** classified as Goring **in the public domain, so too,** for damage classified as Goring **on the property of the injured** party he is liable to pay only **half** the cost of **the damage.**

The *Chachamim* respond with the *dayo* principle: *dayo l'avo min hadin lihyot k'nidon* — it is sufficient that the conclusion derived from the *kal vachomer* equal the case from which it was derived. Since Rabbi Tarfon's *kal vachomer* derives *keren* in the *reshut hanizak* from *keren* in the *reshut harabim*, and *keren* in the *reshut harabim* is half-damages, the most the *kal vachomer* can establish for the *reshut hanizak* is half. The greater severity of the *reshut hanizak* is acknowledged, but *dayo* prevents the conclusion from exceeding its source.

### Tarfon's Second Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא אָדוּן קֶרֶן מִקֶּרֶן, אֲנִי אָדוּן קֶרֶן מֵרֶגֶל – וּמָה בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁהֵקֵל עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל, בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – הֶחְמִיר בַּקֶּרֶן; מָקוֹם שֶׁהֶחְמִיר עַל הַשֵּׁן וְעַל הָרֶגֶל, בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנַּחְמִיר בַּקֶּרֶן?
>
> **Translation:** **will not derive an inference** with regard to **Goring from** a different case of **Goring. I will** instead **derive an inference** with regard to **Goring from Trampling: And if in a place where** the Torah **was lenient with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** specifically **in the public domain,** as the owner is exempt from liability, nevertheless the Torah was **strict with regard to** damage classified as **Goring,** requiring him to pay half the cost of the damage, then in **a place where** the Torah **was strict with regard to** damage classified as **Eating and Trampling,** specifically **on the property of the injured** party as the animal's owner is obligated to pay the full cost of the damage, **is it not right that we** should **be** equally **strict with regard to** damage classified as **Goring** and require payment of the full cost of the damage in this case as well?

Rabbi Tarfon then shifts his framing. Rather than comparing *keren* in one domain to *keren* in another — which immediately invites the *dayo* limitation — he focuses on the domains themselves. The *reshut harabim* is a place where one sometimes pays nothing (*shen* and *regel*) and sometimes pays half (*keren*). The *reshut hanizak* is a place where one pays the full amount for *shen* and *regel*. It therefore follows that *keren* in the *reshut hanizak* should also be full, since the *reshut hanizak* is inherently a place of full liability. The comparison is between *shen* and *regel* in the *reshut hanizak* — where the payment is full — and *keren* in the *reshut hanizak*, which should likewise be full. On this framing, *dayo* need not constrain us, since we are not comparing *keren* to *keren*.

### Rabbis' Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין – לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן. מָה בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – חֲצִי נֶזֶק, אַף בִּרְשׁוּת הַנִּיזָּק – חֲצִי נֶזֶק.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him:** Here as well, **it is sufficient for the** conclusion that **emerges from** an *a fortiori* **inference to be like** its **source,** and therefore, **just as** one is liable to pay **half** the cost of **the damage** classified as Goring **in the public domain, so too,** for damage classified as Goring **on the property of the injured** party he will be liable to pay only **half** the cost of **the damage,** as ultimately your inference still depends on the fact that for Goring in the public domain one pays half the cost of the damage.

The *Chachamim* reject this reformulation. At its core, they argue, the *kal vachomer* is still fundamentally a comparison of *keren* to *keren*, and therefore *dayo* applies regardless of how the argument is framed. That is the scope of the debate as presented in the *mishna*.

## Tarfon & Dayo

### Dayo Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן לֵית לֵיהּ דַּיּוֹ?! וְהָא דַּיּוֹ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **And** is it true that **Rabbi Tarfon does not accept** the principle of: **It is sufficient** for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source? **But** that cannot be, as the principle that begins with: **It is sufficient, is** an aspect **of Torah law.**

The *Gemara* now steps back from the *mishna* itself and probes the underlying structure of Rabbi Tarfon's reasoning. The *Gemara* asks: *v'Rabbi Tarfon leise lei dayo* — does Rabbi Tarfon simply not accept the *dayo* principle? The question arises because in his original formulation, Rabbi Tarfon appeared entirely unbothered by *dayo*, arguing directly that *keren* which pays half in the *reshut harabim* should certainly pay full in the *reshut hanizak*. Only when the *Chachamim* pressed him did he shift to the second approach. This implies that in his own view, the *kal vachomer* could simply produce a result beyond its source without any constraint.

### Dayo Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: מִדִּין קַל וְחוֹמֶר – כֵּיצַד? ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל מֹשֶׁה: וְאָבִיהָ יָרֹק יָרַק בְּפָנֶיהָ, הֲלֹא תִכָּלֵם שִׁבְעַת יָמִים?!״ – קַל וְחוֹמֶר לַשְּׁכִינָה, אַרְבָּעָה עָשָׂר יוֹם! אֶלָּא דַּיּוֹ לַבָּא מִן הַדִּין לִהְיוֹת כַּנִּדּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The Sages said that one of the ways in which the Torah may be interpreted is **by an *a fortiori* inference. How** is this **so?** It is written (Numbers 12:14): **"And the Lord said to Moses: If her father had but spit in her face, should she not hide in shame seven days?** Let her be shut up seven days outside the camp, and after that she shall be brought in again," and therefore, using **an *a fortiori*** inference it can be derived **that** if **the Divine Presence** reprimanded her, she should hide in shame for **fourteen days.** Why was Miriam banished for only seven days? **Rather,** it is because **it is sufficient** for the conclusion **that emerged from the** *a fortiori* **inference to be like the source** of the inference.

This prompts a further challenge: *v'ha dayo de-oraita hu* — the *dayo* principle is itself a biblical law. Beyond the logical problem of knowing how far a *kal vachomer* can extend (since it establishes only a minimum, not a maximum), the principle is grounded in the *Torah* itself. The *Gemara* cites a *braita* from the list of the thirteen hermeneutic principles (*Yud Gimmel Middot*) through which *halacha* is derived from the *Torah*, and demonstrates that *dayo* is one of them with an explicit scriptural basis.

### Miriam Case

The *braita* sources the *dayo* principle from the episode of Miriam's *tzara'at*. The *pasuk* states: *v'aviha yarok yarak b'faneha halo ticalem shivat yamim* — if her father were to spit in her face, would she not be embarrassed for seven days? The *pasuk* then concludes: *tisager michutz lamachaneh shivat yamim* — let her be closed outside the camp for seven days. When Miriam contracted *tzara'at* through divine displeasure, a *kal vachomer* would seem to argue: if a father's spitting causes seven days of isolation, then God's rebuke should certainly produce *arba-asar* — fourteen days. Yet in practice, Miriam was confined for only seven.

### Dayo Limits Kal Vachomer

The *braita* explains: *ela dayo l'avo min hadin lihyot k'nidon* — it is enough that the conclusion of the *kal vachomer* equal the case from which it was derived. Since the source case (the father's spitting) involves seven days, the *kal vachomer* regarding divine displeasure cannot produce more than seven. Even though the case of God is weightier, *dayo* limits the result to seven. This is the scriptural grounding of *dayo* as a *de-oraita* principle.

### Tarfon Rejects Dayo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי לֵית לֵיהּ דַּיּוֹ – הֵיכָא דְּמִפְּרִיךְ קַל וָחוֹמֶר; הֵיכָא דְּלָא מִפְּרִיךְ קַל וָחוֹמֶר – אִית לֵיהּ דַּיּוֹ. הָתָם – שִׁבְעָה דִּשְׁכִינָה לָא כְּתִיבִי; אֲתָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר אַיְיתִי אַרְבֵּסַר, אֲתָא דַּיּוֹ אַפֵּיק שִׁבְעָה, וְאוֹקֵי שִׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When** Rabbi Tarfon **does not accept** the principle: **It is sufficient,** it is **where** this principle completely **refutes** the ***a fortiori*** inference, leaving no *halakha* derived from it. But **where it does not** completely **refute** the ***a fortiori*** inference, Rabbi Tarfon **accepts** the principle: **It is sufficient.** Consequently, **there,** with regard to Miriam, the **seven** days during which she deserved to be banished from the camp due to the rebuke **of the Divine Presence were not written,** so **the *a fortiori*** inference **came** and **brought** those days plus additional days, adding up to a total of **fourteen,** and then the principle: **It is sufficient, came** and **removed seven** days **and left seven** days intact. Consequently, the *a fortiori* inference was effective with regard to the seven days during which she was banished from the camp.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction in Rabbi Tarfon's position with a precise formulation: *ki leise lei dayo heicha de-mifrach kal vachomer* — Rabbi Tarfon rejects *dayo* only in cases where applying *dayo* would entirely undermine the *kal vachomer*. Conversely, *heicha de-lo mifrach kal vachomer, isay dayo* — where the *kal vachomer* still yields a meaningful result after applying *dayo*, Rabbi Tarfon accepts *dayo*.

### Tarfon Principle Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָכָא – חֲצִי נֶזֶק כְּתִיב; וַאֲתָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר וְאַיְיתִי חֲצִי נֶזֶק אַחֲרִינָא, וְנַעֲשָׂה נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם; אִי דָּרְשַׁתְּ דַּיּוֹ – אִפְּרִיךְ לֵיהּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר!
>
> **Translation:** **But here,** payment for **half** the cost of **the damage is written** explicitly in the Torah with regard to the *halakha* of Goring in the public domain, **and the *a fortiori*** inference **comes and brings an additional** payment for **half** the cost of **the damage, forming** a payment of **the full** cost of the **damage. If you interpret** the *halakha* employing the principle: **It is sufficient,** to reduce the payment to half the cost of the damage, this completely **refutes** the ***a fortiori*** inference, as no *halakha* would be derived from the inference; the initial payment for half the cost of the damage was written explicitly in the Torah. Consequently, in this case, Rabbi Tarfon does not employ the principle: It is sufficient.

The logic of this distinction requires careful unpacking. In most *kal vachomers*, one is deriving from a known case (A) and applying to an unknown case (B). The *kal vachomer* establishes that whatever is true of A is certainly true of B. Even when *dayo* is applied and prevents going beyond A, the *kal vachomer* still "bore fruit" — it established that the law of A applies to B, even if B cannot exceed A. In the case of Miriam, we had no independent source establishing that divine displeasure produces seven days of isolation. The *kal vachomer* from the father's spitting was what brought us to the number seven. Even after *dayo* limits the result (preventing us from going to fourteen), the *kal vachomer* still accomplished something: it taught us that the law of seven days applies in the divine case. *Dayo* removed the additional seven but preserved the base seven — *apik shiva ve-okei b'atrah*.

In the case of the goring ox, however, the situation is entirely different. The *Torah* explicitly establishes that a goring ox pays half-damages — *aval hacha chatzi nezek ksiv*. There was never any reason to think this rule was limited to the *reshut harabim*; half-damages applies universally, regardless of where the goring occurs. So even before the *kal vachomer* is applied, we already knew that *keren* in the *reshut hanizak* is half-damages. The *kal vachomer* from *shen* and *regel* attempts to elevate this to full damages — *ve-asa nezek shalem* — by showing that the *reshut hanizak* is a more stringent domain. But if *dayo* is then applied, it collapses us right back to what we already knew: half-damages. The *kal vachomer* would produce nothing — *ei darashta dayo, ifrach leh kal vachomer*. Since *dayo* would entirely destroy the *kal vachomer*, Rabbi Tarfon holds that in this case *dayo* does not apply. In its place, one may draw on other evidence — such as the fact that *shen* and *regel* pay full damages in the *reshut hanizak* — to determine how far the *kal vachomer* extends.

### Rabbis Defend Dayo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, שִׁבְעָה דִשְׁכִינָה כְּתִיבִי – ״תִּסָּגֵר שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** understand this matter? How do they respond to Rabbi Tarfon's reasoning? The Gemara answers: In their opinion, the **seven** days during which Miriam had to be banished due to the reprimand she received **from the Divine Presence** are in fact **written** in the Torah: **"Let her be shut up seven days."** This indicates that the *a fortiori* inference is not required to teach the *halakha* of the seven days she was banished, as it would have added only the extra seven days. This means that according to the Rabbis, there is a source in the Torah that the principle: It is sufficient, is employed even when it refutes the *a fortiori* inference completely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, הָהוּא ״תִּסָּגֵר״ – דְּדָרְשִׁינַן דַּיּוֹ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what would **Rabbi Tarfon** say about that reasoning? The Gemara answers: He would say that **this** verse: **"Let her be shut up** seven days," is necessary to teach us the basic fact **that we interpret** the *halakha* according to the principle: **It is sufficient** for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, כְּתִיב קְרָא אַחֲרִינָא – ״וַתִּסָּגֵר מִרְיָם״. וְרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן, הָהוּא – דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּעָלְמָא דָּרְשִׁינַן דַּיּוֹ, וְלֹא תֹּאמַר: הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹדוֹ שֶׁל מֹשֶׁה, אֲבָל בְּעָלְמָא לָא; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what would **the Rabbis** say in response? The Gemara answers: They would point out that **a different verse is written** about Miriam: **"And Miriam was shut up** outside the camp seven days" (Numbers 12:15). The Gemara asks: **And** how would **Rabbi Tarfon** respond to that? The Gemara answers: **That** verse teaches **that we interpret** the *halakha* according to the principle: **It is sufficient, even generally,** and not only in this specific case. **And** this point is necessary so that **you do not say: Here** the principle: It is sufficient, is employed **due to respect for Moses, but generally** that is **not** done. This verse therefore **teaches us** that this is not so.

One might then ask: how can the *Chachamim* apply *dayo* even in the Miriam case, given that they appear to already know seven days applies to God — since the *pasuk* itself concludes with *tisager shivat yamim*? The *Gemara* explains the *Chachamim*'s position: a second *pasuk* states *v'tisager Miriam* — and Miriam was sealed up — for seven days, repeating the number. The *Chachamim* argue that this repetition teaches that God's displeasure warranted seven days *independently* of the *kal vachomer*. Since seven days was already established for the divine case, the *kal vachomer* still "bore fruit" even after *dayo* — it confirmed what we already knew rather than leaving us with nothing. Therefore, *dayo* applies and limits the result to seven, and we do not go to fourteen.

### Tarfon's Response

Rabbi Tarfon, however, reads the second *pasuk* differently. He does not understand the repetition as establishing an independent source for seven days in the divine case. Rather, *hahu d'afilu b'alma darshinan dayo* — the repetition is there to teach that *dayo* is a general principle applicable in all cases, not one that was invoked here merely as a special concession to Moses. The concern the *pasuk* is preempting is this: one might have thought that ordinarily *dayo* is not applied at all, but that God made an exception here out of honor for Moses — limiting Miriam's punishment to seven rather than fourteen so as not to distress Moses excessively. The *pasuk* therefore repeats the seven-day limit to signal that *dayo* is a universal hermeneutic rule, not a one-time accommodation. *V'lo tomar hacha mishum kvodo shel Moshe, aval b'alma lo, kamashma lan* — lest one say that this principle only operates here because of God's regard for Moses, the *Torah* clarifies that *dayo* applies universally whenever the *kal vachomer* would otherwise bear fruit.

The upshot is that Rabbi Tarfon fully accepts *dayo* and accepts it in all cases — but only when applying *dayo* still leaves the *kal vachomer* with something to show for itself. Where *dayo* would nullify the *kal vachomer* entirely, Rabbi Tarfon holds that *dayo* does not apply.

## Pappa's Challenge

### Keri B'Zav Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְהָא הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּלָא דָּרֵישׁ דַּיּוֹ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִפְּרִיךְ קַל וָחוֹמֶר! דְּתַנְיָא: קֶרִי בְּזָב מִנַּיִין? וְדִין הוּא, מָה טָהוֹר בְּטָהוֹר – טָמֵא בְּטָמֵא; טָמֵא בְּטָהוֹר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָמֵא בְּטָמֵא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Abaye:** Is the fundamental principle: It is sufficient for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source, actually accepted by all authorities? **But** there is **this *tanna*, who does not interpret** the *halakha* in accordance with the principle: **It is sufficient, even though** it is a case where the principle **does not refute** the ***a fortiori*** inference completely. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that the **semen of a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*]** imparts ritual impurity by someone carrying it as well as by coming into contact with it, just as the actual gonorrhea-like discharge does? **It is a logical derivation** from an *a fortiori* inference: **Just as** spittle, which is **ritually pure** when coming **from** a person who is **ritually pure,** is **impure** when coming **from** someone like a *zav* who is **impure, is it not logical that** semen, which is **impure** when coming **from** someone who is **pure, should be impure** when coming **from** someone who is **impure?**

Having established Rabbi Tarfon's principle, the *Gemara* raises a challenge. *Amar lei Rav Pappa l'Abaye*: Rav Pappa said to Abaye that there exists a *tanna* who does not apply *dayo* even in a case where the *kal vachomer* would bear fruit — that is, even where *dayo* would still leave the *kal vachomer* with a meaningful result. The *Gemara* cites a *braita* asking: *keri b'zav minayin* — from where do we derive that the seminal emission (*keri*) of a *zav* is *tamei*?

### Semen Kal Vachomer

To appreciate the question, one must understand the underlying *halachot*. A *zav* is a person who has experienced a discharge (*ziva*) from the male organ on three occasions of sufficient quality to render him severely *tamei* — he is an *av hatumah* whose *tumah* persists for seven days and whose bodily fluids carry significant *tumah* as well. The *Torah* explicitly states that if a *zav* spits upon a *tahor* person, that person becomes *tamei* — *v'chi yarok hazav al hatahor* — establishing that a *zav*'s spit is itself an *av hatumah*. The question is how we extend this to his semen (*shichvat zera*).

The *braita* constructs a *kal vachomer*: spit, which is ordinarily *tahor* when it comes from a *tahor* person, becomes *tamei* when it comes from a *zav*. Semen (*shichvat zera*), which is *tamei* even when it comes from a *tahor* person, should certainly be *tamei* when it comes from a *zav*. This is the *kal vachomer*: *ma tahor b'tahor tamei b'tamei, tamei b'tahor eino din shehei tamei b'tamei* — that which is *tahor* when it comes from a *tahor* person becomes *tamei* when it comes from a *zav* (spit), then that which is *tamei* when it comes from a *tahor* person (semen) certainly becomes *tamei* when it comes from a *zav*.

### Tamei by Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ בֵּין לְמַגָּע בֵּין לְמַשָּׂא. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: אַהֲנִי קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְמַגָּע, אַהֲנִי דַּיּוֹ לְאַפּוֹקֵי מַשָּׂא!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* **brings** this derivation and applies it **whether** discussing ritual impurity imparted **by contact** or **whether** discussing impurity imparted **by carrying. And why** is this the case? **Let us say: The *a fortiori*** inference **is effective in** teaching that the semen of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity **by contact** alone, as is the *halakha* with regard to the semen of a pure man, and the principle: **It is sufficient, is effective** by limiting the scope of the *a fortiori* inference **to exclude** the semen of a *zav* from imparting ritual impurity **by carrying.** Since the *tanna* does not formulate his derivation in this manner, it appears that he rejects the principle: It is sufficient, in all situations.

But the *braita* does not stop there. It goes further and applies the resulting law — that the *keri* of a *zav* is *tamei* — not merely by touch (*maga*) but also by carrying (*massa*): *v'kameisi la bein l'maga bein l'massa*. The distinction matters because the *ma'ayanos* of a *zav* — his bodily fluids, including his spit — are *tamei* in the weightier sense that even carrying them (without direct contact) conveys *tumah*. The ordinary *keri* of a *tahor* person, by contrast, conveys *tumah* only through touch, not through carrying. The *braita* is thus applying the *kal vachomer* to establish not only that the *keri* of a *zav* is *tamei*, but that it is *tamei* in the more stringent manner — like the spit — through carrying as well.

### Dayo Question 2

This is where Rav Pappa's challenge bites. The *Gemara* asks: *ve-amai neima hanei kal vachomer lemaga* — why is *dayo* not applied? Even granting that *dayo* would limit the *kal vachomer*, the *kal vachomer* would still bear fruit. If *dayo* were applied here, we would know that the *keri* of a *zav* is *tamei* by touch — *lemaga* — which is something we supposedly did not know before the *kal vachomer*. *Dayo* would then preclude the additional stringency of *tumah* through carrying. The *kal vachomer* would not be undermined; it would still teach us something meaningful. And yet the *braita* does not apply *dayo* and goes all the way to *tumah* through carrying. This appears to contradict Rabbi Tarfon's principle — this *tanna* seems to reject *dayo* even where the *kal vachomer* is not destroyed by it.

### Source Pasuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לְמַגָּע לָא אִצְטְרִיךְ קַל וָחוֹמֶר, דְּלָא גָּרַע מִגַּבְרָא טָהוֹר; אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: ״מִקְּרֵה לָיְלָה״ כְּתִיב – מִי שֶׁקִּרְיוֹ גּוֹרֵם לוֹ; יָצָא זֶה, שֶׁאֵין קִרְיוֹ גּוֹרֵם לוֹ – אֶלָּא דָּבָר אַחֵר גָּרַם לוֹ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that the ***a fortiori*** inference **was not necessary** to teach that the seminal emission of the *zav* imparts ritual impurity **by contact, as it is** certainly **no less** impure **than** if it had come from **a man** who was **pure,** so the inference was necessary only to teach that the semen of a *zav* imparts impurity by carrying, this is not correct. In fact, it **was necessary** to teach this point, as it may **enter your mind to say** that since **it is written** in the verse: "If there be among you any man that is not clean because of something that **happens to him by night"** (Deuteronomy 23:11), this means that a seminal emission is ritually impure if it came from **someone who** had something **happen to him,** and this **causes him** to experience the emission, but the verse is **excluding this** *zav*, who did **not** have something **happen to him** to **cause him** to experience the emission, but **rather another matter,** i.e., his gonorrhea-like condition, **caused him** to experience the emission. Consequently, it **teaches us** that this is incorrect.

The *Gemara* addresses the challenge. The assumption underlying Rav Pappa's question is that *dayo* would still leave the *kal vachomer* with something to teach — namely, that the *keri* of a *zav* is *tamei* by touch — and therefore *dayo* should apply. But the *Gemara* responds: *vechi teima lemaga lo itztrich kal vachomer* — perhaps no *kal vachomer* was needed at all for *tumah* by touch. *Dila gara migavra tahor* — the *keri* of a *zav* is no less *tamei* than the *keri* of a *tahor* person, and we know the *keri* of a *tahor* person is *tamei* by touch from the *Torah*. Since there is no reason to think the *keri* of a *zav* would be less *tamei* than that of a *tahor* person, we already know it is *tamei* by touch without any *kal vachomer*.

One might push back: *salka daitcha amina*, I might have thought that the *Torah*'s law of *tumah* for seminal emission — *mikreh laylah ksiv* — applies only to one whose seminal emission is what *causes* him to become *tamei*, *mi shekeiryo gorem lo*. It would then exclude a *zav* — *yatza zeh she-ein keiryo gorem lo* — since his semen does not cause his *tumah* (his *ziva* does). The *kal vachomer* is therefore needed even for *tumah* by touch. And if so, *dayo* should limit us to touch alone, and the *braita*'s extension to carrying would be unwarranted.

### Semen Tamei by Touch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִידֵּי וְלֹא דָּבָר אַחֵר כְּתִיב?! 
>
> **Translation:** Abaye responded: In the verse "something that happens to him by night," **is it** also **written: But not another matter?** This limiting clause is not written in the verse, and therefore the *halakha* that the semen of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity by contact can be understood from the explicit verse in the Torah, and there is no need to derive it from an *a fortiori* inference. Therefore, the only function of the *a fortiori* inference is to teach the halakha that the semen of a *zav* imparts impurity by carrying. Applying the principle: It is sufficient, would refute the *a fortiori* inference completely, so there is no proof that the *tanna* would apply the principle in all circumstances.

The *Gemara* dismisses this reasoning. *Midei velo davar acher ksiv*? The *Torah* does not say that only the semen caused the *tumah* and nothing else. The *pasuk* speaks of semen causing *tumah*, but this does not exclude cases where *some other factor* also causes *tumah* — *ela davar acher gorem lo*. The original *pasuk* about *ba'al keri* therefore already includes both a *tahor* person and a *zav*; the *keri* of a *zav* was already known to be *tamei* by touch through the original *pasuk* itself, not through the *kal vachomer*. The *kal vachomer* serves only to elevate the *keri* of a *zav* to the status of the *zav*'s spit — *tamei* even through carrying.

This now maps perfectly onto Rabbi Tarfon's structure. We already knew that *keri* is *tamei* by touch for all people, including a *zav*. The *kal vachomer* pushes us toward a higher level of *tumah* — *tumah* through carrying — by analogy with the spit. If *dayo* were applied, it would collapse us back to *tumah* by touch, which we already knew. The *kal vachomer* would produce nothing. Precisely as Rabbi Tarfon holds, *dayo* does not apply when it would entirely nullify the *kal vachomer*.

### Tanna Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ דְּאָמַר: שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁל זָב מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא? לָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. דִּתְנַן: שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע שֶׁל זָב מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַגָּע, וְאֵין מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: אַף מְטַמְּאָה בְּמַשָּׂא, לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר בְּלֹא צִחְצוּחֵי זִיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Once the Gemara raised the issue, it clarifies: **And who is the *tanna*** about whom **you heard that he said: Semen of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity by carrying?** It was **not Rabbi Eliezer and not Rabbi Yehoshua. As we learned** in a *baraita*: **Semen of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity by contact but** it **does not impart ritual impurity by carrying;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. And Rabbi Yehoshua says:** It **also imparts ritual impurity by carrying, as it is impossible** for semen to emerge **without small drops of gonorrhea-like discharge [*ziva*]** accompanying it.

Having established that the *braita* operates according to Rabbi Tarfon's principle, the *Gemara* asks whose position it represents. *U-man tanna de-shamiesh lei d'amar shichvat zera shel zav metamei be-massa* — who is the *tanna* who holds that the *shichvat zera* of a *zav* is *tamei* by carrying in this intrinsic sense? The *Gemara* answers: *lo Rabbi Eliezer ve-lo Rabbi Yehoshua* — it is neither Rabbi Eliezer nor Rabbi Yehoshua.

### Kelim Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם – אֶלָּא שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר בְּלֹא צִחְצוּחֵי זִיבָה, הָא לָאו הָכִי – לָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua says there** that the semen of a *zav* imparts ritual impurity by carrying **only because it is impossible** for semen to emerge **without small drops of *ziva*** accompanying it. This indicates that **if not for this** reason, the semen would **not** impart ritual impurity by carrying, according to the opinions of both Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabbi Eliezer.

A *mishna* in Kelim makes the positions of these two *tanna'im* clear. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *shichvat zera* of a *zav* is *tamei* only by touch and not by carrying — *metamei be-maga ve-eino metamei be-massa* — entirely rejecting the *braita*'s conclusion. Rabbi Yehoshua does hold that the *shichvat zera* of a *zav* is *tamei* by carrying, but only because *lefi she-i efshar belo tsichtsuchei ziva* — it is impossible for the semen to be emitted without some trace drops of *ziva* mixed into it. Those drops of *ziva* are themselves *tamei* by carrying, and their presence in the semen causes the whole to be *tamei* by carrying.

The *Gemara* therefore notes: *ad kan lo ka-amar Rabbi Yehoshua hatam ela lefi she-i efshar belo tsichtsuchei ziva* — Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling is grounded entirely in the physical admixture of *ziva*; *ha lav hachi, lo* — were it not for that admixture, Rabbi Yehoshua would not hold that the semen is intrinsically *tamei* by carrying. His position thus does not reflect the *kal vachomer* reasoning of the *braita*, which treats the *keri* of a *zav* as independently and intrinsically *tamei* by carrying.

### Braita Confirmed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הַאי תַּנָּא הוּא – דִּתְנַן: לְמַעְלָה מֵהֶן... זוֹבוֹ שֶׁל זָב, וְרוּקּוֹ, וְשִׁכְבַת זַרְעוֹ, וּמֵימֵי רַגְלָיו, וְדַם הַנִּדָּה – מְטַמְּאִין בֵּין בְּמַגָּע בֵּין בְּמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara therefore concludes that the *braita* represents a distinct *tanna*, **as we learned** in a mishna listing the sources of ritual impurity (*Kelim* 1:3): **Of a greater degree than** the ritual impurities listed previously in the mishna, i.e., the impurity of a creeping animal, semen, and one who contracted ritual impurity from a corpse, are the ***ziva* of a *zav*, and his spittle, and his semen, and his urine, and the blood of a menstruating woman,** all of which **impart impurity both by touching and by carrying.**

The *Gemara* therefore concludes that the *braita* represents a distinct *tanna* — not Rabbi Eliezer and not Rabbi Yehoshua — who uses the *kal vachomer* to derive that the *shichvat zera* of a *zav* is intrinsically *tamei* by carrying, without applying *dayo*, precisely because applying *dayo* would undermine the *kal vachomer* entirely. This *tanna* thus operates in accordance with Rabbi Tarfon's principle, providing a compelling parallel to the dispute about the goring ox.

## Acha's Challenge

### Mapatz & Meis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: וְהָא הַאי תַּנָּא לָא דָּרֵישׁ דַּיּוֹ, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מִפְּרִיךְ קַל וָחוֹמֶר! דְּתַנְיָא: מַפָּץ בְּמֵת מִנַּיִין? וְדִין הוּא, וּמָה פַּכִּין קְטַנִּים – שֶׁטְּהוֹרִים בְּזָב, מְטַמֵּא בְּמֵת; מַפָּץ – שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא בְּזָב, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּטַמֵּא בְּמֵת?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the primary discussion concerning the principle: It is sufficient for the conclusion that emerges from an *a fortiori* inference to be like its source. **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina: But** there is **this *tanna*** who **does not apply** the principle: **It is sufficient,** at all, **even though** such application would **not refute** the entire ***a fortiori*** inference. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that a thick **mat** can contract ritual impurity **from a corpse? It is a logical derivation** based on an *a fortiori* inference: **And just as small** clay **vessels,** into which even a finger cannot be inserted because of their narrow opening and **which** remain **ritually pure** despite contact **with a *zav*,** nevertheless **become impure** if present under one roof **with a corpse,** then **is it not right that a mat, which can become impure** due to contact **with a *zav*, will** similarly **become impure** if present under one roof **with a corpse?**

The *Gemara* now presents a second challenge. *Amar lei Rav Acha mi-Difti le-Ravina*: Rav Acha mi-Difti said to Ravina that there is yet another *tanna* who appears to reject *dayo* even where the *kal vachomer* would bear fruit. The case concerns a *mapatz* — a woven or wicker mat — and its *tumah* status in relation to a *meis* (corpse).

### Vessels Kal Vachomer

A *braita* asks: *mapats be-meis minayin* — from where do we know that a *mapatz* becomes *tamei* through contact with a corpse? A *mapatz* is not among the standard categories of objects susceptible to *tumah* — it lacks a *beit kibul* (receptacle), it is not metal, not food, and not a standard garment. Yet it is known to become *tamei* in one context: *mishkav* by a *zav*. The *Torah* states that all surfaces upon which a *zav* lies or sits — *kol ha-mishkav asher yishkav alav* — become *tamei*, and a *mapatz*, being something designed to be sat or lain upon, falls within this category.

### Vessel Exemption

The *braita* constructs a *kal vachomer*. Certain small pottery vessels — think of a miniature perfume vial with a very narrow opening — are *tahor* by a *zav* yet *tamei* by a *sheretz*. The reason they are *tahor* by a *zav* is that a pottery vessel can only become *tamei* through contact on its inside, and a *zav*'s ability to convey *tumah* by touch or by carrying (*massa*) requires physical contact of a type that would make the vessel *tamei* — an impossibility when the opening is too small for any part of the *zav*'s body to enter. A *sheretz*, however, can convey *tumah* even through a particle the size of a lentil (*be-ka'adashah*), and can therefore penetrate even a tiny vessel.

### Mapatz Kal Vachomer

From this the *kal vachomer* is drawn: these small pottery vessels are *tahor* by a *zav* yet *tamei* by a *sheretz*; a *mapatz*, which is *tamei* by a *zav* (through *mishkav*), should certainly be *tamei* by a *meis*. The reasoning is that a *meis* conveys *tumah* not only through touch but through being under the same roof (*tumah b'ohel*) — a more penetrating and comprehensive form of *tumah*. What a *zav* can make *tamei*, a *meis* can certainly make *tamei*.

### Dayo Limitation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמַיְיתֵי לַהּ בֵּין לְטוּמְאַת עֶרֶב, בֵּין לְטוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה. וְאַמַּאי? אֵימָא: אַהֲנִי קַל וָחוֹמֶר לְטוּמְאַת עֶרֶב, וְאַהֲנִי דַּיּוֹ לְאַפּוֹקֵי טוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה!
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *tanna* **cites this** *a fortiori* inference **both** to derive the *halakha* that the mat becomes ritually impure **with impurity** that lasts until **nightfall,** as it would if it were to come into contact with a *zav*, **and** to derive that the mat becomes ritually impure **with impurity** that lasts for **seven** days, as would any vessel that contracted ritual impurity from a corpse. **And why** does he do so? **Say** instead that the ***a fortiori*** inference **is effective** in deriving the *halakha* that the mat can contract ritual impurity from a corpse, but only **with regard to** rendering it **impure until nightfall,** and also say that the principle: **It is sufficient, is effective** by limiting the scope of the *a fortiori* inference **to exclude** the possibility that the mat would be rendered **impure for seven** days.

But the *braita* goes further and applies this *tumah* for seven days — the standard duration for *tumah* imparted by a corpse — even though the original source of the *kal vachomer*, the *zav*, makes things *tamei* for only one day. This is Rav Acha's challenge: *ve-amai* — why is *dayo* not applied? Even if *dayo* prevented us from going all the way to seven days, the *kal vachomer* would still produce a meaningful result: we would know that a *mapatz* is *tamei* by a *meis* for one day. The *kal vachomer* is not undermined by *dayo*; it still bears fruit. And yet the *braita* does not apply *dayo* and awards the full seven-day *tumah*. This appears to be a *tanna* who rejects *dayo* even when the *kal vachomer* would survive it.

### Sheretz Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבָר רְמָא נִיהֲלֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר זְכַרְיָה לְאַבָּיֵי, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: תַּנָּא – מִמַּפָּץ בְּשֶׁרֶץ מַיְיתֵי לַהּ, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מַפָּץ בְּשֶׁרֶץ מִנַּיִין? וְדִין הוּא, וּמָה פַּכִּין קְטַנִּים – שֶׁטְּהוֹרִים בְּזָב, טְמֵאִין בְּשֶׁרֶץ; מַפָּץ – שֶׁטָּמֵא בְּזָב, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהֵא טָמֵא בְּשֶׁרֶץ?
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to** Rav Aḥa: This question **was already raised** by **Rav Naḥman bar Zekharya to Abaye, and Abaye said to him** in response: This ***tanna*** does not derive the *halakha* with regard to the mat through an *a fortiori* inference from the case of a *zav*. Rather, he **derives it from** the *halakha* of **a mat** that came into contact **with** the carcass of **a creeping animal, and this is** what **he is saying:** The first derivation is as follows: **From where** is it derived that **a mat** is susceptible to impurity imparted **by** the carcass of **a creeping animal? And it is a logical derivation** based on an *a fortiori* inference: **And just as small vessels, which** remain **pure** despite contact **with a *zav*,** nevertheless become **impure from** contact with the carcass of **a creeping animal, is it not right that a mat, which** becomes **impure from the impurity of a *zav*, will** likewise become **impure from** contact with the carcass of **a creeping animal?**

Ravina responds to Rav Acha mi-Difti by citing Abaye's resolution of the same question — *kevar ramya nihali Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak le-Abaye* — Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak had already posed this very challenge to Abaye, and Abaye gave the answer. Abaye's response is that the *braita* does not derive the *tumah* of a *mapatz* by a *meis* directly from the *zav* at all. Rather, the derivation proceeds in two stages, beginning with *sheretz*.

### Sheretz Kal Vachomer

*Tanna mimapatz be-sheretz mayisi lah* — the *tanna* first derives that a *mapatz* is *tamei* through contact with a *sheretz*. The *kal vachomer* for this is: those small pottery vessels are *tahor* by a *zav* but *tamei* by a *sheretz*; a *mapatz*, which is *tamei* by a *zav*, is certainly *tamei* by a *sheretz*. This *kal vachomer* establishes *tumah* for one day, consistent with the one-day *tumah* of a *sheretz*. And here *dayo* does not present any problem: the *kal vachomer* yields something new — we now know the *mapatz* is *tamei* through contact with a *sheretz*, which we did not know before — and *dayo* is satisfied because we stay within the one-day framework of the source case.

### Gezerah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַפָּץ בְּמֵת מִנַּיִין? נֶאֱמַר ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ בְּשֶׁרֶץ, וְנֶאֱמַר ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ בְּמֵת; מָה ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ הָאָמוּר בְּשֶׁרֶץ – מַפָּץ טָמֵא בּוֹ, אַף ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ הָאָמוּר בְּמֵת – מַפָּץ טָמֵא בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The second derivation is as follows: **But from where** is it derived that **a mat** contracts impurity **from a corpse?** It is derived through a verbal analogy. **With regard to** impurity imparted by the carcass of **a creeping animal, it is stated:** "Or **a garment or leather"** (Leviticus 11:32), **and with regard to** impurity imparted by **a corpse it is stated: "A garment, or** anything made of **leather"** (Numbers 31:20). **Just as** with regard to the terms **"garment" and "leather" stated with regard to a creeping animal, a mat** becomes **impure from it, so too** with regard to the terms **"garment" and "leather" stated with regard to a corpse, a mat** becomes **impure from it.**

Now that we have established a *mapatz* as *tamei* by a *sheretz*, the *braita* can move to the *meis*. *Ne'emar beged v'or be-sheretz* — the *Torah* uses the words *beged* (garment) and *or* (leather) in the context of *sheretz*; *ve-ne'emar beged v'or be-meis* — and uses the same words *beged* and *or* in the context of *meis*. A *gezerah shavah* is drawn: just as *beged* and *or* in the context of *sheretz* makes the *mapatz* *tamei*, so too *beged* and *or* in the context of *meis* makes the *mapatz* *tamei*.

### Extra Words Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּפְנֶה; דְּאִי לָא מוּפְנֶה, אִיכָּא לְמִפְרַךְ: מָה לְשֶׁרֶץ – שֶׁכֵּן מְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, תֹּאמַר בְּמֵת – שֶׁאֵין מְטַמֵּא בְּכַעֲדָשָׁה, אֶלָּא בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: It must be that the words "garment" and "leather" are **free,** i.e., those terms must be extraneous in their contexts, and the Torah included them for the express purpose of establishing the verbal analogy, as a verbal analogy that is based on otherwise extraneous terms cannot be logically refuted. **Because if** these terms are **not free,** the verbal analogy **can be refuted: What** is unique **about a creeping animal?** It is unique in **that** it **renders** items **impure** even **by means of** contact with **a lentil-bulk** of a creeping animal. **Shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to a corpse,** whose *halakhot* are less strict **in that** it **does not render** items **impure by means of** contact with **a lentil-bulk** of a corpse but **rather by means of** contact with **an olive-bulk?** Unless the terms are free, the analogy can be refuted.

The *Gemara* notes that this *gezerah shavah* is *mufneh* — the linking words are "free," meaning they are extra and not needed for their own plain reading of the *pasuk*. This is crucial because a *gezerah shavah* based on extra words is impervious to challenge. If the linking words were needed for their primary meaning, a counterargument — *pircha* — could undermine the analogy. But when the words are extra, they exist specifically to create the linkage, and no *pircha* can dislodge it.

### Pasuk Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָאיֵי אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֶה – מִכְּדֵי שֶׁרֶץ אִתַּקַּשׁ לְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא וְגוֹ׳״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל שֶׁרֶץ״; וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע: ״וְכׇל בֶּגֶד וְכׇל עוֹר אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה עָלָיו שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara affirms: **Indeed,** the terms **are free.** The Gemara proves that the terms "garment" and "leather" are extraneous in their context. **Now, since** ritual impurity imparted by contact with **a creeping animal is juxtaposed to** ritual impurity imparted by contact with **semen, as it is written:** "And one who comes into contact with anyone impure with impurity imparted by a corpse, **or a man from whom** semen **is emitted"** (Leviticus 22:4), **and juxtaposed to that** verse is the verse: **"Or a man who touches any creeping animal** which makes him impure, or a person who may make him impure with any impurity that he has" (Leviticus 22:5); **and it is written with regard to** the impurity of **semen: "And every garment and all leather that has semen on it** shall be washed with water and will be impure until evening" (Leviticus 15:17), since the verses appear next to each other, the *halakhot* of each can be derived from the other.

Why are the words *beged* and *or* considered extra by *sheretz*? The *Gemara* explains through a textual observation: *sheretz itkesh le-shichvat zera de-chsiv* — the *Torah* juxtaposes *sheretz* with *shichvat zera* (semen) in a single *pasuk*: *o ish asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zera* ... *o ish asher yiga be-chol sheretz*. And in the *pasuk* about *shichvat zera*, the *Torah* already states explicitly: *ve-chol beged ve-chol or asher yihyeh alav shichvat zera* — any garment or leather that has semen on it is *tamei*. Since *sheretz* is textually linked to *shichvat zera* in the same passage, and *shichvat zera* already specifies *beged* and *or*, one would have known from the linkage alone that *beged* and *or* are *tamei* by *sheretz*. The *Torah*'s explicit mention of *beged* and *or* in the *sheretz* context is therefore redundant — it is there to serve as the linking word for the *gezerah shavah*.

### Ironclad Gezerah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּשֶׁרֶץ – לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, **why do I** need the words **"garment" and "leather" that the Merciful One wrote with regard to a creeping animal?** The relevant *halakha* could be derived from the *halakhot* of seminal impurity. Consequently, **learn from it** that "garment" and "leather" were mentioned **to render them free,** i.e., extraneous, but the Torah included them for the express purpose of establishing the verbal analogy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד הוּא! הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד – לְמֵידִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין, שַׁפִּיר; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמֵידִין וּמְשִׁיבִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And still,** the terms "garment" and "leather" **are free** only **from one side** of the verbal analogy, because although the terms "garment" and "leather," stated with regard to ritual impurity imparted by a creeping animal, are extraneous in their context, as the relevant *halakha* could have been derived in another manner, those terms stated with regard to impurity imparted by a corpse are not extraneous in their context. **This works out well according to the one who says** that with regard to a verbal analogy that is **free from** only **one side, one can derive from it and** one **cannot refute** it logically; **it is well. But according to the one who says** that **one can derive** a *halakha* **from** a verbal analogy of this kind **and** one **can** also **refute** it logically, **what can be said?** It has already been established that the carcass of a creeping animal is stricter in some senses than a corpse.

A *gezerah shavah* is ironclad only when both sides of the analogy feature extra words. Having established that *beged* and *or* are extra by *sheretz*, the *Gemara* asks whether they are also extra by *meis* — for only then would the *gezerah shavah* be fully *mufneh mi-shnei tzaddim*, free on both sides, and unassailable even by those who hold that a *gezerah shavah* free on only one side remains vulnerable to challenge.

### Meis Pasuk Connection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד הוּא! הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד – לְמֵידִין וְאֵין מְשִׁיבִין, שַׁפִּיר; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לְמֵידִין וּמְשִׁיבִין, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּמֵת נָמֵי אִפְּנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֶה – מִכְּדֵי מֵת אִתַּקַּשׁ לְשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהַנּוֹגֵעַ בְּכׇל טְמֵא נֶפֶשׁ, אוֹ אִישׁ אֲשֶׁר תֵּצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ וְגוֹ׳״; וּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע: ״וְכׇל בֶּגֶד וְכׇל עוֹר אֲשֶׁר יִהְיֶה עָלָיו שִׁכְבַת זָרַע״; ״בֶּגֶד וָעוֹר״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמֵת – לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי, וְהָוֵי מוּפְנֶה מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The terms "garment" and "leather," as stated **with regard to** impurity imparted by **a corpse, are also free. Since** the impurity of **a corpse is juxtaposed with** that of **semen, as it is written: "And one who comes into contact with anyone impure with impurity imparted by a corpse, or a man from whom** semen **is emitted"** (Leviticus 22:4), **and it is written with regard to semen: "And every garment and all leather that has semen on it"** (Leviticus 15:17), **why do I** need the terms **"garment" and "leather" that the Merciful One wrote with regard to** impurity imparted by **a corpse? Learn from it** that they are mentioned in order **to render them free, and** therefore **it is free from both sides.**

The *Gemara* demonstrates that *beged* and *or* are equally extra in the *meis* context. *Michdi meis itkash le-shichvat zera* — a *meis* is also textually juxtaposed with *shichvat zera* in the same *pasuk*: *ve-hanogei be-chol tmei nefesh* — one who touches someone made *tamei* by a corpse — *o ish asher tetzei mimenu shichvat zera* — or a man from whom semen is emitted. Since both *meis* and *sheretz* are juxtaposed with *shichvat zera* in the same textual context, and *shichvat zera* already carries *beged* and *or*, the explicit mention of *beged* and *or* by *meis* is likewise redundant. Both sides of the *gezerah shavah* are therefore extra. The *gezerah shavah* is fully ironclad: a *mapatz* that is *tamei* by *sheretz* is equally *tamei* by *meis* — and no *pircha* can challenge it.

### Seven Days Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְכִבַּסְתֶּם בִּגְדֵיכֶם בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁבִיעִי״ – כׇּל טְמָאוֹת שֶׁאַתֶּם מִטַּמְּאִין בְּמֵת, לֹא יְהוּ פְּחוּתִין מִשִּׁבְעָה.
>
> **Translation:** In response to this, **Rava said:** This opinion does not lead to that conclusion, as there is a Torah edict indicating otherwise. **The verse states** (Numbers 31:24): **"And you shall wash your garments on the seventh day."** This teaches that **any ritually impure** items **that you render impure** due to contact **with a corpse must** remain impure for a period of **no less than seven** days.

We have now established, through the *kal vachomer* to *sheretz* and then the *gezerah shavah* to *meis*, that a *mapatz* is *tamei* when in contact with a corpse. But how do we arrive at *seven days* of *tumah*? The difficulty is that the *mapatz* was brought into the *meis* framework by means of a *gezerah shavah* from *sheretz*, and *sheretz* imparts only one day of *tumah*. If one holds the principle of *don minah u-minah* — learn from it and carry along its original context — then a *mapatz* made *tamei* by *meis* through a *sheretz*-derived *gezerah shavah* would remain at one day of *tumah*, mirroring the duration of *sheretz*. The *gezerah shavah* would transport only the law, not the severity specific to *meis*.

The alternative principle, *don minah ve-okei be-atrah* — learn from it and then situate the conclusion within the context of the destination case — produces a different result. Under this approach, one learns from *sheretz* that a *mapatz* is susceptible to *tumah* through *meis*, and then applies the general rules of *meis* *tumah* in full, including its seven-day duration.

### Final Pasuk

For those who hold *don minah u-minah* and would therefore limit the *mapatz* to one day of *tumah* even when made *tamei* by a *meis*, Rava cites a *pasuk* that resolves the matter: *ve-chibastem bigdeichem ba-yom ha-shvi'i* — you shall wash your garments on the seventh day. *Kol tumah she-atem mitamim be-meis lo yifchatu mi-shiva* — any *tumah* contracted through a *meis* is a minimum of seven days. This *pasuk* functions as a blanket rule: whatever the source of the *kal vachomer* or *gezerah shavah* that brought a particular object into the category of *tamei* by *meis*, the *tumah* itself carries the full seven days characteristic of *meis* *tumah*. The original one-day framework of *sheretz* is thus superseded by the explicit *pasuk* that sets seven days as the floor for any *tumah* derived from a corpse.
