# Yevamot 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Givonim & David

### Intro: Netinim

The daf begins in the middle of 78b, following a relatively brief discussion in the Gemara concerning *mamzer* status. The Mishnah now turns to the *netinim* — the designation applied to the *givonim*, the Gibeonites, who are forbidden in marriage. The *givonim* were members of one of the *shivat ha-amamim*, the seven Canaanite nations, who had deceived Yehoshua into making a pact with them. They are assumed to have converted at some point — otherwise they would simply be non-Jews — and so the question becomes one of their status as a distinct group even after conversion.

### Lo Titchaten Bam

One might suggest that the source of the prohibition is the biblical verse *lo titchaten bam* — the prohibition against intermarrying with the seven Canaanite nations. There is an earlier discussion in the tractate regarding whether this prohibition applies even after the *shivat ha-amamim* have converted. That debate bears on the *givonim* as well, but the Gemara now proceeds to explore an additional basis for the prohibition, rooted in a later episode in the narrative of Tanakh.

### David's Gezeirah

The Gemara introduces the following teaching: *Amar Rabbi Chana bar Adda: netinim David gazar aleihem* — it was David who issued a decree concerning the *netinim*, presumably prohibiting intermarriage with them. The proof-text is drawn from Shmuel, where the narrative of a famine in David's time opens: *vayikra hamelech lagivonim vayomer aleihem* — the king called the Gibeonites and spoke to them. The verse also notes, *veha-givonim lo mibnei Yisrael heim* — they were not of the Jewish people. The question the Gemara then pursues is: *mai ta'ama gazar aleihem* — why did David enact this decree?

### Three-Year Famine

The basis for the decree emerges from the account of a three-year famine during David's reign: *vayehi ra'av bimei David shalosh shanim shana acharei shana* — there was a famine, year after year, for three consecutive years. The Gemara understands that David actively investigated the cause each of those years.

In the first year, David told the people: *shema ovdei avodah zarah yesh bachem* — perhaps there are idolaters among you. The scriptural basis is the verse warning that serving other gods will cause God to close the heavens and withhold rain. They investigated — *badku velo matzu* — and found nothing.

In the second year, he said: *shema ovrei aveirah yesh bachem* — perhaps there are sexual sinners among you. The verse cited describes God withholding the rains and invokes the imagery of the brazen forehead of a wayward woman — a punishment associated with sexual sin. Again they investigated and found nothing.

In the third year, he said: *shema porskei tzedakah b'rabim yesh bachem ve'einam notnim* — perhaps there are those who make public pledges at an appeal and do not fulfill them. The verse speaks of clouds and wind that promise rain but deliver none, analogous to the person who praises himself with a false donation. They investigated and again found nothing.

### Divine Inquiry

At this point David declared, *ein hadavar taluy ela bi* — it must be up to me to determine the cause — and immediately sought God directly: *vayevakesh David et pnei Hashem*. The Gemara asks how David sought God in this manner, and Resh Lakish answers: *she-sha'al ba-Urim veTummim* — he inquired through the Urim and Thummim. Rabbi Elazar confirms this through a *gezerah shavah* on the word *pnei*: the verse here says *vayevakesh David et pnei Hashem*, and elsewhere the Torah says regarding the Urim and Thummim, *vesha'alu lo b'mishpat ha-Urim lifnei Hashem* — the shared word *pnei* / *lifnei* connects the two passages.

God's response was: *Vayomer Hashem sha'ul v'el beit hadamim* — it is on account of Shaul and the house of blood, *al asher hemit hagivonim* — because he killed the Gibeonites. The Gemara then identifies two causes implicit in this divine response: (1) *al Shaul shelo nispas ke-halacha* — Shaul was not properly eulogized, and (2) the killing of the Gibeonites. The motivation to read a secondary theme of the eulogy into this passage comes from the end of the narrative in Shmuel, where David retrieves and reinters the bones of Shaul and Yehonatan alongside those of the executed sons — indicating that the proper treatment of Shaul's remains was part of what this entire episode addressed.

### Saul's Culpability

The Gemara then asks: *ve-chi heichan matzinu b'Shaul shehemit et hagivonim* — where do we ever find explicitly that Shaul killed the Gibeonites? The verse in this story is the first such mention. The Gemara answers: *ela mitoch sheharag Nov ir Kohanim* — because Shaul destroyed Nov, the city of priests. The Gibeonites, who served the priests as hewers of wood and drawers of water — a role assigned to them by Yehoshua and later connected to the altar — were thereby deprived of their livelihood and sustenance. *Ma'aleh alav ha-katuv k'ilu haragam* — Scripture accounts it to Shaul as though he had killed them outright. This reading notably softens what the plain reading of the Shmuel narrative implies — that Shaul had actually attempted to decimate the Gibeonites — reducing it to an indirect harm caused by the destruction of those they depended upon.

### Dual Punishment

This raises an apparent difficulty: the text assigns punishment for two things simultaneously — Shaul's lack of proper eulogy, and the harm done to the Gibeonites. How can both be true together? Is Shaul a victim in one sense and a perpetrator in another? Resh Lakish resolves this through a verse: *bikshu et Hashem kol anvei ha-aretz asher mishpato sham pa'alu* — seek God, all the humble of the earth, where God's justice is, there too are God's righteous deeds. The same point where justice is exacted is also where the person's due is rendered. David reflected that Shaul had gone twelve months — indeed thirty years — without a proper *hesped*, and that while the formal mourning period had long passed and it was no longer the ideal time for a eulogy, *velo dacha le-mispedei* — he had not pressed to make it happen. His more immediate task was to address the Gibeonites: *nikrina u-nepaysinu* — let us call them and attempt to appease them.

### Givonim Demand

The king called the Gibeonites and asked: *mah e'eseh lachem u-bamah achaper* — what shall I do for you and how can atonement be made? The Gibeonites responded that they wanted neither silver nor gold, nor any claim against the broader Jewish people — only seven of Shaul's sons: *yutan lanu shiv'ah anashim mibanav ve-hokanum le-Hashem* — give us seven of his sons, and we will impale them before God. David attempted to negotiate some other form of appeasement — *mifas lo faysinu* — but they would not be moved.

### Jewish Virtues

Confronted with this display of implacable cruelty, David reflected on the defining characteristics of the Jewish people. He declared: *shloshah simanim yesh b'umah zu* — the Jewish nation has three identifying marks: *ha-rachmanim* — they are compassionate, *ha-bayshanim* — they are capable of shame and embarrassment, and *gomlei chasadim* — they perform acts of loving-kindness.

Each characteristic is grounded in a scriptural source. Compassion: *ve-nasan lecha rachamim ve-richamcha* — God will instill compassion in you. Shame: *ba-avur tihyeh yir'aso al pneichem le-vilti techetau* — God's awe should be upon your faces so that you do not sin, implying a non-brazen, humble bearing. Kindness: *le-ma'an asher yetzaveh et banav ve-et beiso acharav ve-shamru derech Hashem la'asos tzedakah u-mishpat* — to walk in God's ways and do righteousness and justice.

The principle then follows: *kol she-yesh bo shloshah simanim elalu ra'uy le-hidabek b'umah zu* — whoever possesses these three traits is fit to attach themselves to the Jewish people. For a nation that lacks them, intermarriage is not appropriate even if they have converted and are technically Jewish.

### Conclusion: Unfit

This observation — made in the context of the Gibeonites' heartless demand — is what leads David to his conclusion. Having seen their cruelty firsthand, David determined that they were unfit to intermarry with Israel, and *gazar aleihem* — he issued his decree. Some derive the prohibition from the very verse introducing the Gibeonites in this narrative, *ve-ha-givonim lo mibnei Yisrael heim* — not merely that they were not originally of Israel, but that they were never to be integrated into the people through marriage.

Tosafos identifies three possible understandings of what David's *gezeirah* actually entailed. One view holds that the biblical prohibition of *lo titchaten bam* ceases to apply after the *shivat ha-amamim* convert, and David's *gezeirah* was a rabbinic or extra-legal decree filling that gap — prohibiting intermarriage even post-conversion. A second view suggests that David's contribution was to extend Yehoshua's designation of the Gibeonites as servants beyond the specific conditions Yehoshua had set, and since slaves cannot be married into the congregation, the marriage prohibition follows indirectly from their servitude. A third view, that the biblical prohibition applies even after conversion, would leave David's decree focused purely on the question of servitude rather than intermarriage directly.

The plain sense of the Gemara here, it should be noted, concerns only marriage — there is no hint in this passage of the servitude question. The discussion is entirely embedded in a tractate about who may or may not marry whom. The servitude dimension will enter later.

## Execution & Kiddush

### Selection Process

The seven descendants of Shaul were identified through a specific selection process. The verse specifies that David took the two sons of Ritzpah daughter of Ayah — Armoni and Mephiboshes — as well as five sons of Michal daughter of Shaul whom she bore to Adriel ben Barzillai ha-Mecholasi. This Mephiboshes is not the son of Yehonasan, as the verse immediately prior notes that David had compassion on Yehonasan's son Mephiboshes on account of the oath he had sworn to Yehonasan — there were two individuals of that name.

Rav Huna teaches that the selection of which seven to hand over was determined by passing them before the *aron*: *kol she-ha-aron kolto le-misah* — whoever was drawn in by the Ark was designated for death; *kol she-ein ha-aron kolto le-chayim* — whoever was not drawn in was spared. This parallels a similar methodology used in another episode. The Gemara then raises a difficulty from the verse *vayachmol ha-melech al Mephiboshes ben Yehonasan* — the king had compassion on Mephiboshes son of Yehonasan — which implies that it was David's personal discretion rather than an objective test that determined his fate.

The Gemara resolves this in stages. First it is suggested that the compassion was simply that David did not submit Yehonasan's Mephiboshes to the test at all — that was the act of mercy. But then it is objected: *ve-chi maso panim yesh badavar* — is it permissible to show favoritism in capital matters? One ought to let God decide. The Gemara then revises: Mephiboshes was in fact submitted to the test, but David prayed that he not be drawn in — *bikaesh alav rachamim shelo yikletenu ha-aron* — and he was spared.

But the objection resurfaces: *ve-achti maso panim yesh badavar* — even that involves favoritism, since sparing one through prayer effectively sends another in his place. Rashi articulates this point with striking force: by praying that Mephiboshes be released, David was implicitly assigning someone else to death in his stead. The Gemara ultimately resolves this by clarifying that David's prayer preceded the test entirely — he prayed from the outset that Mephiboshes would not be drawn in, rather than praying for his release after the Ark had already claimed him, meaning no other individual was displaced.

### Children Punished

The Gemara asks the obvious question: *ve-haktiv lo yumtu avot al banim* — does the Torah not prohibit putting children to death for the sins of their fathers? These seven sons were being executed for what Shaul had done. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mutav she-te'aker ot achat min ha-Torah u-val yitchalel Shem Shamayim be-farhesya* — it is better that one letter of the Torah be uprooted than that the divine name be publicly desecrated.

One might have supposed that executing innocent people is itself a desecration of the divine name — but Rashi explains the calculus differently. The concern was what the nations would say: *she-yehu ha-umot omrim ein umah zo re-uyah le-hidabek bah* — this is not a nation worthy of joining, because they allowed these converts to be killed without their food being provided and without any vengeance being taken on their behalf. The perception of Israel as a nation that abandoned vulnerable converts in their midst would constitute a *chilul Hashem*. To prevent that, even a violation of the prohibition against punishing children for their fathers' sins was permitted — a genuinely radical application of the concern for how Israel presents itself to the outside world, and one where the concern extends specifically to non-Jewish perception.

### Ritzpah's Vigil

After the execution, Ritzpah daughter of Ayah, the mother of two of the executed sons, took sackcloth and spread it by the rock, keeping vigil from the beginning of the harvest season until rain finally fell: *lo natnah of ha-shamayim lanoach aleihem yomam ve-chayat ha-sadeh laylah* — she did not let the birds of the heaven rest on the bodies by day, nor the beasts of the field approach them by night.

This raises another halakhic problem: *ve-haktiv lo talin nivlato al ha-etz* — does the Torah not prohibit leaving a body hanging on a tree overnight? Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak answers with the same formulation: *mutav she-te'aker ot achat min ha-Torah ve-yitkadesh Shem Shamayim be-farhesya* — it is better that one letter of the Torah be uprooted than that the divine name be desecrated in public. Here the argument inverts: what is gained by leaving the bodies exposed is not the avoidance of *chilul Hashem* but the active achievement of *kiddush Hashem*.

### Kiddush Hashem

The mechanism of *kiddush Hashem* is spelled out explicitly. Passersby would stop and ask: *mah tivan shel elu* — who are these people? Why are they hanging here? And they would be told: *hallalu b'nei melachim hem* — these are the sons of kings, princes. *U-mah asu* — and what did they do? *Pashtu yadeihem be-geirim gerurim* — they acted against lowly converts. The word *gerurim* here refers to their status as converts who had entered under deception — not the most sincere or prestigious of converts, yet still the objects of such punitive concern.

The conclusion drawn by the onlookers: *ein le-cha umah she-re-uyah le-hidabek bah ke-zo* — there is no nation as worthy of joining as this one. *U-mah b'nei melachim kach* — if that is what they do to princes for harming such people, *b'nei hedyotot al achat kamah ve-chamah* — how much more would they demand of ordinary citizens who harm the vulnerable.

The narrative is striking in how it inverts the moral positions established earlier. Shaul had committed a terrible act against the Gibeonites. Yet ultimately, it is the Gibeonites themselves who are shown to be heartless — demanding the lives of seven men — while Israel emerges as the nation of exceptional ethical standards. Tosafos notes a beautiful *midrash* on the phrase *va-tatehu lah el ha-tzur*: the word *ha-tzur* alludes to the *tzidduk ha-din*, the acceptance of divine judgment — *Ha-Tzur Tamim Pe'alo* — suggesting that Ritzpah, in her grief, affirmed the justice of what had occurred.

### Converts Arrive

As a direct result of this demonstration of Israel's moral standards, 150,000 people converted: *nisvasfu Yisrael me'ah va-chamishim elef*. The scriptural basis is drawn from the account of Shlomo's workforce: *vayihi li-Shlomo shiv'im elef nosei saval u-shmonim elef chotzeiv ba-har* — 70,000 burden-bearers and 80,000 hewers in the mountain, for the construction of the Beit HaMikdash.

The Gemara questions whether these might simply have been native-born Israelites. The objection is raised: *u-mibnei Yisrael lo nasan Shlomo eved* — Shlomo did not impose servitude on the children of Israel — yet this workforce was engaged in labor that resembles servitude, so they must have come from the gentile nations. A further verse makes this explicit: *vayispor Shlomo kol ha-anashim ha-geirim asher be-eretz Yisrael vayimmatze'u me'ah va-chamishim elef* — Shlomo counted all the resident aliens in the land of Israel and found 150,000, placing 70,000 as burden-bearers and 80,000 as hewers.

## Netinim Status

### Yehoshua's Decree

The Gemara now turns to the legal status of the *netinim* in terms of servitude, tracing its origins back to Yehoshua. The verse records: *vayitneim Yehoshua ba-yom ha-hu chotevei eitzim ve-sho'avei mayim la-eidah u-le-mizbeach Hashem* — Yehoshua designated them on that day as hewers of wood and drawers of water for the congregation and for the altar of God. This is the foundational act that established the Gibeonites as servants in relation to the Beit HaMikdash.

### Moshe vs. Yehoshua

The Gemara then asks whether Moshe might have preceded Yehoshua in this designation, citing the verse *mi-chotev eitzecha ad sho'ev meimecha* — from the hewer of your wood to the drawer of your water — which appears in the Torah itself. The Gemara thus suggests that Moshe had already in some sense identified a people for this role, even before the conquest of Canaan and before the episode with the Gibeonites. The question of how this works precisely — since the Gibeonites had not yet made their pact with Yehoshua in Moshe's time — is left somewhat open. The conclusion is: *Moshe gazar le-hu dara* — Moshe's decree was for that generation only; *David gazar le-chulei dara* — David extended it to all future generations.

### David's Extension

Yehoshua's decree, the Gemara explains, was conditioned on the existence of the Temple: *Yehoshua gazar bi-zman she-Beit HaMikdash kayam* — the verse says *le-mizbeach Hashem*, "for the altar of God," tying the servitude to the Temple's operation. Since there was no Temple in David's time — only David extended their status to periods when there is no Temple: *David gazar bi-zman she-ein Beit HaMikdash kayam*. This extension is what David added beyond Yehoshua's designation.

The question of what exactly David was extending — the servitude status, or the prohibition of intermarriage — becomes complicated here. The Gemara seems to intertwine the two, and Tosafos proposes that the intermarriage prohibition may follow naturally from slave status, since one cannot marry a slave. The simpler reading of the earlier Gemara, however, would suggest that David's decree was directly about intermarriage, with no dependence on the slave question.

### Repeal Attempt

In the time of Rabbi, an attempt was made to rescind the *netinim* status: *bikshu le-hatir nesinim* — they sought to permit the *netinim*, at least by releasing them from servitude. Rabbi responded: *chelkeinu natia* — we can release our portion, the claim the Jewish people hold over them, through the principle of *hefker beit din hefker*. But *chelek mizbe'ach mi yatish* — who are we to release the Temple's portion? The Temple's share in their servitude is not ours to annul.

### Shares Doctrine

This position is in tension with a view of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *chelek adam le-olam assur* — the people's portion is forever forbidden unless released; *chelek mizbe'ach bi-zman she-Beit HaMikdash kayam assur* — the Temple's portion is forbidden when the Temple stands, *ein Beit HaMikdash kayam shari* — but when there is no Temple, the Temple's portion is no longer binding. According to this view, the people could in theory release their portion, and the Temple's portion would lapse in the absence of the Beit HaMikdash, potentially opening the door for the *netinim* to be freed from their status entirely — and perhaps, by extension, permitted for intermarriage. The Gemara remains somewhat ambiguous about whether the ultimate focus is on servitude or on the marriage prohibition, with the two questions running through each other throughout this passage.

## Saris & Ailonit

### Saris Traditions

The Mishnah now shifts to a discussion of *chalitzah* and *yibum* as they apply to a *saris* — a eunuch — and to an *ailonit*, a woman who cannot bear children. Rabbi Yehoshua opens with a declaration: *shamati she-saris choletz* — I have heard that a *saris* performs *chalitzah*, and *choltzin le-ishto* — that one performs *chalitzah* with the wife of a *saris* who dies. This runs against intuition: if a man cannot have children, why should he or his wife be relevant to *yibum* and *chalitzah*, whose entire purpose is *le-hakim shem achiv* — to perpetuate the name of the deceased brother?

Rabbi Yehoshua then adds: *ve-saris lo choletz ve-lo choltzin le-ishto* — I also heard the opposite, that a *saris* neither performs *chalitzah* nor is *chalitzah* performed with his wife. He acknowledges he cannot reconcile the two traditions.

### Akiva's Distinction

Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Yehoshua's student, offers to resolve the contradiction: *ani afareish* — I will explain. *Saris adam choletz ve-choltzin le-ishto* — a man who became a *saris* through human agency performs *chalitzah* and one performs *chalitzah* with his wife. *Saris chamah lo choletz ve-lo choltzin le-ishto* — a man who is a *saris* by nature does not perform *chalitzah* and one does not perform *chalitzah* with his wife.

### Sha'at Kosher

The basis for Rabbi Akiva's distinction is the concept of *sha'at kosher* — a prior moment of valid capacity. A *saris adam* — one whose condition was caused by a later physical event, analogous to *ptzua daka* or *khrut shofcha* — at least began life with the capacity to father children. Even if that capacity has been entirely and permanently eliminated, his personal identity is not intrinsically that of someone excluded from the category of procreation. He had *sha'at kosher*, and so in terms of his halakhic *gavra* status he remains within the *parshah* of *yibum* and *chalitzah* — even though fathering children is currently and permanently impossible.

A *saris chamah*, by contrast — one born with the condition, whose entire life has been that of someone incapable of reproduction — never had *sha'at kosher*. His personal identity is intrinsically that of someone excluded from the category, and therefore *yibum* and *chalitzah* do not apply.

### Eliezer's View

Rabbi Eliezer, Rabbi Yehoshua's colleague and a fellow teacher of Rabbi Akiva, disagrees and inverts the ruling. *Saris chamah choletz ve-choltzin le-ishto* — a natural *saris* performs *chalitzah* and one performs *chalitzah* with his wife, *mipnei she-yesh lo refua* — because his condition may be curable. If the incapacity arose from natural circumstances — a condition one was born into — perhaps it is something that could yet resolve itself. *Saris adam lo choletz ve-lo choltzin le-ishto mipnei she-ein lo refua* — a *saris adam* cannot perform *chalitzah* and one does not perform it with his wife, because when something anatomically decisive has been done through human agency — severed, as in *khrut shofcha*, or damaged, as in *ptzua daka* — it is permanent and admits of no remedy.

The underlying logic of the two positions is thus symmetrical but opposite. Rabbi Akiva focuses on prior personal identity: was there ever a moment when this person stood in the category of one who can have children? Rabbi Eliezer focuses on present and future reality: is there any possibility, however remote, that this condition is reversible? Both agree that the immediate practical impossibility of conception is not the operative question — the *saris* who cannot have children right now and never will again can nonetheless, under the right analytical framework, remain within the halakhic category. What differs is what places one inside or outside that category.

### Ben Magusas Case

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Betira testified regarding a certain Ben Magusas who lived in Jerusalem: he was a *saris adam*, and when he died, *yibum* was performed with his wife — *veyibemu et ishto le-kayem divrei Rabbi Akiva*. The testimony was not issued in order to affirm Rabbi Akiva's position, but it functions as corroboration. Here was an actual case of a *saris adam* where *yibum* — not merely *chalitzah* — was performed with the deceased's wife, consistent with Rabbi Akiva's ruling that *saris adam* retains full standing in the laws of *yibum* and *chalitzah*.

### Final Ruling

The Mishnah then presents what appears to be a later, independent formulation: *ha-saris lo choletz ve-lo meyabem* — the *saris* does not perform *chalitzah* and does not perform *yibum*. And similarly: *ve-chen ailonit lo choletzet ve-lo mityabemet* — an *ailonit* does not perform *chalitzah* and is not subject to *yibum*. This categorical statement makes no distinction between types of *saris* and seems to ignore the entire preceding debate among Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Eliezer. The most natural reading is that this was an earlier, undifferentiated *stam* teaching, upon which the later *tannaim* built their distinctions — similar to other cases in *Shas* where an early undifferentiated Mishnah is followed by a debate that constitutes the various *tannaim*'s interpretations of its terms.

### Sex vs. Chalitzah

Regarding the *saris* who nevertheless goes ahead and performs *chalitzah* with his *yevamah*: *lo pasla* — it does not invalidate her for a *kohen*. The act is halakhically meaningless; it accomplished nothing. But if he had sexual relations with her — *pasla* — he has invalidated her. Since there is no *mitzvat yibum* with a *saris*, the act is one of *arayot* — it constitutes *eshet ach* in a context where the *mitzvah* does not apply — and she is therefore rendered a *chalalah*, *mipnei she-hi be'ilat zenut*. It is more precise to say *be'ilat arayot* — an act of forbidden relations.

The distinction is thus clear: meaningless *chalitzah* leaves her status unchanged, while sexual relations in the absence of a valid *mitzvat yibum* renders the act prohibited and invalidates her.

### Ailonit Rules

The same framework applies to an *ailonit*. If one of the brothers performed *chalitzah* with her: *lo pasluha* — they did not invalidate her for a *kohen*, since the act was halakhically meaningless in the absence of any *mitzvat yibum* or *mitzvat chalitzah*. If they had sexual relations with her — *pasluha* — they invalidated her, since without *mitzvat yibum* the act is one of *arayot*, *mipnei she-be'ilatah be'ilat zenut*.

An *ailonit* is not simply a woman who is currently infertile or who has not yet conceived. The parallel to the *saris* is instructive: just as the relevant question for the *saris* is not whether conception is practically possible at this moment but whether his personal identity places him within the category, so too the *ailonit* must be someone whose identity is intrinsically that of a woman outside the category — not an *akara*, a woman who happens not to be bearing children, but an *ailonit*, defined by specific physical characteristics that place her categorically elsewhere.

## Gemara Problem

### Prohibition Paradox

The Gemara now raises a striking analytical problem with Rabbi Akiva's position. We know that Rabbi Akiva holds *chayavei lavin ke-chayavei keritot dami* — those who violate a *lav* are treated as those who violate an *issur karet*. This means, among other consequences, that the offspring of such unions is a *mamzer*, that *kiddushin* is ineffective, and — crucially for our context — that the *mitzvah* of *yibum* and *chalitzah* is entirely excluded, just as it is excluded in cases of *arayot*. The very first Mishnah in the tractate established that if a woman is forbidden to her *yavam* by an *issur karet*, she is exempt from *yibum* and *chalitzah* altogether.

But a *saris adam* — the category in which Rabbi Akiva says *chalitzah* and *yibum* apply — is presumably someone in the category of *ptzua daka u-khrut shofcha*, who is forbidden by a *lav* to marry a Jewish woman. If Rabbi Akiva treats a *lav* like an *issur karet*, then the *saris adam*'s wife should be entirely excluded from *yibum* and *chalitzah*, not included. The contradiction is direct: *ve-chayvei keritot lav benei chalitzah ve-yibum ninhu* — those subject to *issur karet* are not within the category of *yibum* and *chalitzah* at all.

### Convert Resolution

Rabbi Ammi resolves the problem: *hakha be-mai askinan ke-gon she-nasa achiv giyoret* — we are discussing a case where the deceased brother's wife is a convert. There is no prohibition of *ptzua daka* applying to a *giyoret* — the *lav* of *lo yavo ptzua daka be-kehal Hashem* does not prohibit marriage with a convert.

### Kahal Gerim

Rabbi Akiva follows the position of Rabbi Yossi, who holds *kehal gerim lo ikrei kehal* — the congregation of converts is not considered *kehal* for this purpose. Therefore, the prohibition does not apply to the *saris adam* who married a convert, there is no *lav*, and consequently the relationship is not treated as *chayvei keritot* by Rabbi Akiva — leaving *chalitzah* and *yibum* applicable.

### Yibum Question

A follow-up question arises: if the prohibition is entirely absent in the case of a *giyoret*, why does the Mishnah speak only of *chalitzah* rather than *yibum* as well? If there is no *lav*, why not simply perform *yibum*?

### Restriction Scope

The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yehoshua, who reported the two traditions in the first place, would hold that the prohibition of *ptzua daka* applies even to *geirim* — he does not accept Rabbi Yossi's position that *kehal gerim* is not *kehal*. Therefore, Rabbi Yehoshua had to frame the teaching as *chalitzah* only, since in his view there remains a prohibition even vis-à-vis a convert. Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, would have no objection in principle to *yibum* as well in this case. When Rabbi Akiva stated *choletz* — he used the language of Rabbi Yehoshua's formulation, but in principle according to his own view, *yibum* could also be performed.

### Testimony Inference

This is confirmed by the testimony regarding Ben Magusas: when Ben Magusas, a *saris adam*, died, *yibum* was performed with his wife — not merely *chalitzah*. The Gemara infers: *shema mina* — this is evidence that even *yibum* was considered possible for a *saris adam*, supporting the Gemara's conclusion that the case involves a *giyoret* and that there is no operative prohibition preventing *yibum*.

## Saris Obligations

### Permission Principle

The Gemara now presents a *baraita* that lays out the full application of the laws of *yibum* and *chalitzah* for those with physical defects or advanced age. *Ptzua daka u-khrut shofcha*, as well as an elderly man who is no longer capable of reproduction — whether such a person died leaving a wife, or whether he is the surviving brother obligated in *yibum* — the ruling is: *o chaltzin o meyabmin* — either *chalitzah* or *yibum* may be performed. These men are not categorically excluded from the framework of *yibum* and *chalitzah*.

### Valid Acts

The *baraita* elaborates: whether it is the *saris adam* or elderly man who died and left a *yevamah* behind for his brothers, or whether he is the living brother, all legal acts are fully valid. *Maamar*, *get*, *chalitzah* — and *yibum* itself — are all binding and effective: *mah she-asu asui*. The Gemara stresses this applies regardless of whether the deceased was in this condition or the surviving brother is.

### Prohibition Remains

Yet the *baraita* adds: *ve-assur le-kayanam* — they are nevertheless forbidden to remain married. Even though *yibum* is valid and effective, the husband cannot maintain the marriage, because the prohibition of *lo yavo ptzua daka u-khrut shofcha be-kehal Hashem* still applies. The Gemara notes that here we are dealing with a case where the wives are not converts — the prohibition does apply — which raises anew the question of how Rabbi Akiva can permit *yibum* and *chalitzah* in the presence of a *lav* that he treats as equivalent to an *issur karet*.

## Later Defects

### Rava's Question

Rava proposes a resolution. The scenario described in the *baraita* — where *yibum* and *chalitzah* apply despite the *lav* of *ptzua daka* — is one where the physical condition arose after the husband's death: *ke-gon she-naflo lo be-sof niftzu* — the condition occurred at the end, meaning after the original obligation of *yibum* was already established. At the moment the brother died and the *mitzvah* of *yibum* attached, everything was normal. The *saris* condition of the surviving brother arose only afterward.

### Rava's Proposal

Rava's reasoning is that if the *issur* came after the *mitzvah* had already taken hold, it does not retroactively dissolve the obligation. The *yevamah* was already fully obligated in *yibum* or *chalitzah* at the moment of the brother's death; a condition that subsequently afflicts the surviving brother should not undo what had already been established.

### Abaye's Objection

Abaye challenges this reasoning: *ve-dachi issur ptzua ve-nidchei issur asei de-yibum* — why should the newly arising prohibition of *ptzua daka* not override and displace the positive obligation of *yibum*, even if it came later? He brings proof from an established teaching: *min lo tanan Rabban Gamliel omer im mianah mianah* — Rabban Gamliel ruled regarding a man married to an underage girl whose sister fell before him for *yibum*. Rabban Gamliel said that if the underage wife performs *mi'un* — refusal — the *yibum* obligation with her sister can proceed.

### Underage Wife Case

The mechanics of Abaye's proof: when the brother died, his wife's sister was only rabbinically married to the surviving brother, not yet a full halakhic *ishah*. The prohibition of *achot isha* — a man's wife's sister — had not yet fully crystallized. At that moment, the *mitzvah* of *yibum* applied to the deceased's wife. But if the surviving brother waits until the underage girl matures, she becomes his full halakhic wife, and her sister — the *yevamah* — becomes his *achot isha*, canceling the obligation of *yibum*. Rabban Gamliel rules: *tamtin ad she-tagdil ve-tetzei halazu mishum achot isha* — wait until she grows up, and the *yevamah* must leave on account of the prohibition of a wife's sister.

### Prohibition Cancellation

This case demonstrates that even when the *mitzvah* of *yibum* was established at the moment of death, a prohibition that arises afterward — here *achot isha* — can come along and override the obligation. *Alema ati issur achot isha ve-dachi* — we see that a later *issur* can displace an earlier *mitzvah* of *yibum*. Therefore, says Abaye, the same should be true here: even if the *saris* condition arose after the brother's death, the resulting prohibition of *ptzua daka* should be sufficient to cancel the *mitzvah* of *yibum*. Rava's proposed resolution is therefore inadequate.

## Simple Lav

### Rav Yosef Answer

Rav Yosef provides the definitive answer: *hai tanna d'vei Rabbi Akiva hi de-amar me-chayavei lavin d'she'er havei mamzer, me-chayavei lavin geridei lo havei mamzer* — this teaching follows the position of the school of Rabbi Akiva, which distinguishes between two types of *lavin*.

### Simple Lav Category

When Rabbi Akiva says that *chayavei lavin* are treated like *chayavei keritot* — producing *mamzer* status and being excluded from *yibum* and *chalitzah* — he does not apply this to every *lav* universally. The prohibition of *ptzua daka* is a *lav gerei* — a simple, freestanding prohibition — that has nothing to do with kinship or relational categories. Rabbi Akiva's equation of *lavin* with *keritot* applies only in a more limited domain.

### Kinship Lavim

The category of *lavin* that Rabbi Akiva treats as equivalent to *issur karet* — producing *mamzer* status and excluding the relationship from *yibum* — is specifically *chayavei lavin d'she'er*: prohibitions rooted in the relational category of *she'er*, kinship. These are the prohibitions that neighbor the domain of *arayot* — unions forbidden because of who the parties are to one another, not merely because of some personal defect or disability. *Ptzua daka* is not in this category. It is a prohibition about the man's physical status, entirely unrelated to kinship between the parties. Therefore, even Rabbi Akiva does not treat it as producing *mamzer* status or as excluding the couple from the framework of *yibum* and *chalitzah*. The *lav* remains — they may not stay married — but it does not rise to the level of an *issur karet* analog that would dissolve the *yibum* obligation entirely.
