# Bava Batra 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah: Trees & Land

### Two Trees Case

The *daf* opens with a *mishnah* several lines from the top of 81a: *hakoneh shne ilanot betoch sdei chaveiro* — one who purchases two trees situated within his friend's field. This case was already referenced earlier in the tractate, but it is now presented in full. The ruling is *harei zeh lo kanah karka*: the buyer acquires only the trees themselves, not the ground. This means not merely that he fails to acquire the land between the two trees, but that he does not even acquire the soil directly beneath each tree. He has the right to have the trees standing there — the landowner cannot demand that he remove them — but, as the *mishnah* will make clear, that right is tied entirely to the trees themselves. Rabbi Meir dissents: *Rabbi Meir omer kanah karka* — in his view, purchasing two trees implies the acquisition of a plot of land as well, and the *gemara* will later examine precisely how much land that entails.

### Branch Rights

Returning to the position of the *chachamim* — or equivalently, to the case where the buyer does not own the land — the *mishnah* addresses what happens when the branches grow and spread: *higdilu, lo yishpeh*. The landowner may not trim the canopy, nor may he compel the tree-owner to do so. The reason is straightforward: the landowner cannot tell the tree-owner to confine his tree to his own property, because the tree-owner has no property of his own. Implicitly, the right to have the tree growing there entails the right to draw nutrients from the soil, to shade the surrounding land, and to allow the branches to spread wherever they reach — even if this inconveniences the landowner's farming. This is, paradoxically, an upside of not owning land: the absence of a defined plot means the tree's needs take precedence across the entire surrounding field.

### Trunk & Roots

The *mishnah* then draws a precise distinction regarding what grows from the tree itself. *V'ha-oleh min ha-geza shelo* — whatever sprouts from the trunk belongs to the tree-owner, since he clearly owns the tree. But *u-min ha-shorashim she-ba-karka* — whatever grows from the roots belongs to the landowner. Because the roots are underground and the buyer did not acquire the ground, the roots themselves are considered to remain part of the landowner's domain, and anything that emerges from them follows accordingly. This is a counterintuitive consequence: one might have assumed that roots, being part of the tree, belong to whoever owns the tree, but the *mishnah* treats roots as inseparable from the land in which they are embedded.

### Tree Death

The *mishnah* concludes the two-tree case with *v'im mesu ein lo karka*: if the trees die, the buyer has no remaining claim. He cannot argue that he should be permitted to plant replacement trees. His purchase was of those specific trees and the right to have them there — nothing more. Since he owns no land, there is nothing for him to retain once the trees are gone.

### Three Trees Case

By contrast, *kanah shlosha kanah karka* — if one purchases three trees (in the view of the *chachamim*), land is indeed acquired, and the *gemara* will specify how much. This land ownership, however, comes with a corresponding limitation: *higdilu yishpeh* — if the branches spread beyond the bounds of that owned plot, the landowner now has the right to trim them, because he can legitimately say that the tree-owner has rights only within his defined parcel. On the other hand, *v'ha-oleh min ha-geza u-min ha-shorashim shelo* — whatever grows from both the trunk and the roots belongs to the tree-owner, since he owns the full tree and the ground beneath it. And *v'im mesu yesh lo karka* — if the trees die, he retains the land and may plant new trees in their place.

## Bikkurim Obligation

### No Recitation

The *gemara* now cites a *mishnah* from tractate Bikkurim: *hakoneh shne ilanot betoch sdei chaveiro mevi v'eino korei* — one who purchases two trees in his friend's field brings *bikkurim* from the fruit, but does not recite the accompanying *pesukim*. The recitation includes the phrase *ha-adamah asher natata li* — thanking God for the land He has given — and since this buyer owns no land, that declaration would be false. The fruit itself may qualify as *bikkurim*, so the physical act of bringing is performed, but the verbal declaration is omitted.

### Rabbi Meir View

Rabbi Meir's position in that same *mishnah* is *mevi v'korei*: with two trees, he holds, the buyer does acquire land, as established in our *mishnah* in Bava Batra, and therefore the full *bikkurim* obligation — including the recitation of *ha-adamah asher natata li* — applies. This appears to be a straightforward application of his position on land acquisition, but the *gemara* finds the repetition curious.

### Extreme Position

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel*: Rav Yehuda, citing Shmuel, advances a more radical reading of Rabbi Meir's view. Rabbi Meir, he claims, would obligate *bikkurim* with recitation even for one who purchases fruit in the marketplace — *lokeiach peiros min ha-shuk* — having bought neither trees nor land at all. On this reading, Rabbi Meir's position is not simply about deriving land acquisition from the purchase of two trees; it extends the *bikkurim* obligation far beyond any conventional understanding of land ownership.

### Extra Mishnah

The basis for this extreme reading is textual: *mimai midkatni mishnah yateirah* — why was an additional *mishnah* needed in Bikkurim? Given that the dispute in Bava Batra already establishes whether two trees carry land acquisition, the *bikkurim* implications follow automatically. According to the *chachamim*, two trees means no land, which means *mevi v'eino korei*. According to Rabbi Meir, two trees means land, which means *mevi v'korei*. All of this is directly derivable from our *mishnah*. Perhaps the *chidush* in the Bikkurim *mishnah* lies in clarifying that even according to the *chachamim*, the buyer of two trees must still *bring* — lest one think that without land there is no *bikkurim* obligation at all. But then why does the Bikkurim *mishnah* need to tell us Rabbi Meir's position? For Rabbi Meir, *mevi v'korei* is self-evident from Bava Batra. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the extra *mishnah* must be teaching something more: Rabbi Meir really holds that even without any tree ownership, even with fruit purchased in the marketplace, one would bring and recite.

This is a striking *gemara*. Beyond the substantive oddity of the ruling — what makes marketplace fruit *bikkurim* in any recognizable sense? — the derivation itself is puzzling. Bikkurim predates Bava Batra in the ordering of tractates, so the more natural reading is that Rabbi Meir's teaching originated in the context of Bikkurim and was extracted therefrom when Bava Batra addressed questions of sale. To infer from a "doubling" of sources that the earlier source must contain a novel ruling beyond what the later source teaches is methodologically unusual. The *gemara* itself will challenge this position.

The *gemara* first tries to defend the extreme reading against apparent scriptural objections. *Ve-hachtiv asher tavi me'artzecha* — doesn't the verse say "from your land"? The response is that this phrase excludes *chutz la-aretz*, not that it requires personal land ownership. *Ve-hachtiv admasecha* — "your ground"? That excludes fruit grown on a non-Jew's land (*admat akum*), but does not require the individual to own the land personally. And when the declaration says *asher natata li* — "which You gave me" — the *gemara* suggests this can be read broadly: God gave me the money, I bought the fruit, all of it ultimately comes from God. The *gemara* pushes this reasoning quite far before the challenge arrives.

## Refutation

### One Tree Braita

*Masiv Rabba* — Rabba raises an objection from a *braisa*: *hakoneh ilan echad betoch shel chaveiro mevi v'eino korei lefi shelo kana karka divrei Rabbi Meir* — one who purchases a single tree in his friend's field brings *bikkurim* but does not recite, because he has not acquired land — and this is the ruling of Rabbi Meir. This *braisa* provides precisely the test case needed. One tree, no land ownership — and Rabbi Meir says *mevi v'eino korei*, not *mevi v'korei*. For Rabbi Meir, what one tree achieves is what two trees achieve for the *chachamim*: you bring without reciting. The *braisa* thus demonstrates that Rabbi Meir does require land ownership for the recitation — he merely draws the threshold differently.

### Tiyuvta Proof

The *gemara* declares *tiyuvta* — a conclusive refutation. The extreme reading advanced by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, that Rabbi Meir would obligate *bikkurim* with full recitation even for marketplace fruit, cannot be sustained. It is clear from the *braisa* that even Rabbi Meir conditions the recitation on land ownership; he simply holds that one tree, not two, is sufficient to establish that ownership.

## Central Question

### Elazar's Response

With the extreme position refuted, a fundamental question remains: why does one who owns a single tree (according to Rabbi Meir) or two trees (according to the *chachamim*) bring *bikkurim* at all? If the declaration *ha-adamah asher natata li* cannot be made because there is no land ownership, why is the act of bringing required? *Amar lei Rav Shimon ben Yakim le-Rabbi Elazar* — at the opening of 81b, Rav Shimon ben Yakim poses exactly this question to Rabbi Elazar: what is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir in the one-tree case, and what is the reasoning of the *rabbanan* in the two-tree case?

Rabbi Elazar's response is remarkable: *davar she-rishonim lo amru lo bo ta'am tishaleni be-veit ha-midrash kedei le-vaysheni* — "You are asking me in public about something for which earlier authorities gave no reason, as if to embarrass me." The earlier generations who transmitted these rulings from Rabbi Meir and the *rabbanan* did not supply an explanation, and the question was well known precisely because no satisfactory answer had been offered. It is a candid and disarming response — and, it must be said, one that sits in some tension with the principle that a person who is ashamed to ask will never learn. But clearly Rabbi Elazar felt that this was a question whose difficulty was notorious and whose public airing without a ready answer was genuinely embarrassing.

### Rava's Solution

The *gemara*, undeterred, offers its own resolution through Rava. *Dilma Rabbi Meir be-ilan echad sfukei mesafke leih, rabbanan bi-shnei ilanos sfukei mesafke lehu* — perhaps Rabbi Meir in the one-tree case, and the *rabbanan* in the two-tree case, each hold that it is *possible* — not certain — that land is acquired in those circumstances. The question of land ownership in those borderline cases is genuinely uncertain.

When it comes to property law — *dinei mamonot* — the principle of *hamotzi me-chavero alav ha-ra'ayah* applies: the one seeking to extract money or property from another bears the burden of proof. Since the tree-buyer cannot prove he owns the land, the presumption favors the original landowner, and the buyer acquires nothing for purposes of civil law. But because the matter is doubtful, and because in the realm of ritual obligation doubt demands stringency, the buyer must bring *bikkurim* — perhaps the land really is his, and if so, the obligation would apply. He does not recite, however, because the declaration might be false — he cannot confidently say *ha-adamah asher natata li* when it is uncertain whether God gave him the land.

This is an elegant but not simple solution. It treats the obligation to bring and the obligation to recite as resting on different thresholds: bringing requires only the possibility of land ownership, while reciting requires its certainty. When the *gemara* is pressed on whether the language of the *braisa* — *lefi shelo kana karka*, "because he did not acquire land" — implies definitive non-ownership rather than mere doubt, it answers that the phrase means *shema lo kana*, "perhaps he did not acquire" — a tentative formulation, not a conclusive one.

## Safek & Sanctification

### Chulin ba-Azarah

The *gemara* presses further: *ve-leihush she-dilma lav Bikkurim ninhu ve-ka ma'ail chulin la-azarah* — if these fruits are only doubtfully *bikkurim*, perhaps they are not *bikkurim* at all, in which case one would be bringing *chulin* — non-sanctified produce — into the *azarah*, the Temple courtyard, which is prohibited.

What exactly is the prohibition of *chulin ba-azarah*? The classic case is slaughtering a non-sacrificial animal within the Temple precincts, treating the *azarah* as an ordinary abattoir. Whether this prohibition is *d'oraisa* or *d'rabbanan* is itself a matter of discussion, but it is well established as a serious concern. The Rashbam explains that it creates confusion — people will observe treatment of meat inconsistent with how *kodshim* are handled — or, more fundamentally, it blurs the essential boundary between *chulin* and *kodshim*, between what belongs inside the *beit hamikdash* and what belongs outside it.

One might wonder: does bringing a piece of fruit into the *azarah* really implicate *chulin ba-azarah*? Is it not comparable simply to carrying one's food through the Temple grounds? Tosafot addresses this directly. The concern is not merely physical presence in the *azarah*, but performing an act *ke'ein hakravah* — one analogous to the ritual acts of sacrifice. The *gemara* will specify that *bikkurim* involves *hagashah* or *tenufa* — bringing the fruit near the *mizbe'ach* or waving it — acts that parallel the ritual of *korbanot*. It is this ritual treatment of the fruit, performed with something that may not be *bikkurim* at all, that raises the problem of *chulin ba-azarah*.

### Sanctification

The *gemara*'s solution is *demakdish lehu* — one sanctifies the fruit to the *beit hamikdash* before bringing it. The declaration is conditional: if this fruit qualifies as *bikkurim*, it is being treated as such; if not, it is being sanctified as Temple property. In either case, the fruit is no longer *chulin*, and the concern of *chulin ba-azarah* is resolved. One might ask whether sanctifying fruit to the Temple really permits performing *bikkurim*-like ritual acts with it, given that ordinary sanctified property — a chair, for instance, dedicated to the Temple — would never be subject to waving. The *gemara*'s implicit response seems to be that as long as the object is not *chulin*, the integrity of the Temple's space is preserved, even if the ritual acts remain somewhat awkward.

### Redemption

A further complication arises: *bikkurim* are ultimately consumed by the *kohen*. If the fruit has been sanctified to the *beit hamikdash*, how can the *kohen* eat property belonging to the Temple? The *gemara* answers *deparkinehu* — the fruit is redeemed. After the ritual is performed, the sanctity is transferred onto a monetary substitute — say, twenty dinars — which is given to the Temple treasury, and the fruit itself reverts to ordinary status and may be eaten by the *kohen*. The *gemara* continues from here to work through additional implications of this *safek* framework, but the essential resolution has been reached: the doubtful *bikkurim* are brought, sanctified, the ritual is performed without recitation, and the fruit is then redeemed and given to the *kohen* for eating.

## Conceptual Analysis

### Bikkurim Status

Before concluding, two important Tosafot from the previous *amud* deserve attention. The first addresses a question that arises from the *gemara*'s discussion: why does the verse *asher tavi me'artzecha* need to exclude *chutz la-aretz* from the *bikkurim* obligation? Is not *bikkurim* a *mitzvah hatluya ba-aretz* — a commandment dependent on the Land — and therefore automatically inapplicable outside it, just as *terumot* and *ma'aser* do not apply to produce grown abroad?

Tosafot, citing the Rashbam, answers that *bikkurim* is in fact *not* a *mitzvah hatluya ba-aretz* in the classic sense, because it is not analogous to *terumah*, *ma'aser*, or *challah*. The key distinction lies in the nature of the obligation. With *terumah* and *ma'aser*, the obligation begins with the object — the food itself. Grain grown in *Eretz Yisrael* acquires the status of *tevel*, untithed produce, which is forbidden to eat and which demands separation. The *kedushah* — the sanctity demanding action — inheres in the food and derives from the fact that it grew from the holy soil of *Eretz Yisrael*. This is why these commandments are genuinely *tluya ba-aretz*: they start with the ground and flow to the food.

### Personal Obligation

*Bikkurim*, by contrast, works differently. If one fails to bring *bikkurim*, the fruit is not forbidden. There is no status of *tevel*, no prohibition on eating the fruit. The obligation does not inhere in the food as a *cheftza* — as an object with a legal status — but rather in the *adam*, the person. It is a *chovas haguf*, a personal obligation: the person who owns this fruit must bring it to the Temple. The food's own status is not altered by the failure to bring it.

### Early Sanctification

This distinction is further reinforced by an additional difference. *Terumot* and *ma'asrot* become obligatory only when the produce reaches a certain stage of completion — specifically, when the grain pile is smoothed over after winnowing (*mismareiach bakeri*). The obligation is tethered to the food reaching its final processed state, confirming that it is the food's status that drives the obligation. *Bikkurim*, however, work in the opposite direction: the obligation begins *be-odan ba-ilan*, while the fruit is still on the tree. When one sees the first ripening fruits, one ties a ribbon designating them as *bikkurim* — before they are even harvested. The obligation is thus triggered by the person's recognition and dedication, not by the food reaching any stage of completion. These two features together — the absence of a *tevel*-like status and the early onset of the obligation while fruit is still on the tree — confirm that *bikkurim* is a *chovas haguf*, a personal obligation, and not a *kedushah* emanating from the ground in the manner of classic *mitzvot hatluya ba-aretz*. A scriptural exclusion is therefore necessary to establish that *bikkurim* does not apply to produce from *chutz la-aretz*.

## Gerim & Bikkurim

### Ger Exclusion

The second Tosafot addresses a variant reading that the Rashbam had before him: that the phrase *lema'utei admat akum* — excluding land owned by a non-Jew — also extends to land owned by a *ger*, a convert. This leads Tosafot into a broader discussion of whether a *ger* brings *bikkurim* with or without the recitation.

### Rabbeinu Tam

The starting point is an explicit *mishnah* in tractate Bikkurim: *ha-ger mevi v'eino korei* — a convert brings *bikkurim* but does not recite. The reason given is *lefi she-eino yachol lomar asher nishba la-avoseinu lases lanu* — because he cannot say "which [God] swore to our forefathers to give us," since the covenant with the patriarchs was not made with his ancestors.

Rabbeinu Tam drew a practical ruling from this *mishnah*: a *ger* should not lead *birkat ha-zimun* — the communal blessing after meals — because the text includes the phrase *she-nachalata la-avoseinu eretz tova u-richava*, "which You gave as an inheritance to our forefathers, a good and spacious land," language that a *ger* cannot truthfully utter. The *mishnah* in Bikkurim further specifies that when a *ger* prays privately, he should say *Elohei avos Yisrael* — "God of the fathers of Israel" — rather than *Eloheinu v'Elohei avoseinu*, always marking his distinct status. And when serving as *shliach tzibbur*, he says *Elohei avoseichem* — "God of your forefathers." The ruling is consistent but carries a striking human cost: a person who has fully embraced the Torah and the Jewish people is perpetually reminded, even in his private prayer, that he is other than native-born. This sits in sharp tension with the principle of *kager ka-ezrach* and the Torah's repeated admonition *v'lo sonu*.

### Yerushalmi View

The *Ri* — a later Tosafist — disagrees. He holds that a *ger* may indeed say *la-avoseinu*, citing a different authority: Rabbi Yehudah, whose position is recorded in the Yerushalmi. Rabbi Yehudah rules that a *ger* brings *bikkurim* and recites the full declaration — *ger atzmo mevi v'korei*. The reasoning is drawn from a verse: *ki av hamon goyim netaticha* — "I have made you the father of many nations." Abraham was not merely the father of Aram; from that moment forward he was designated as the father of all nations. A *ger* who accepts the covenant is counted among the spiritual descendants of Abraham, and Abraham's patriarchal relationship extends to him.

### Halacha Ruling

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi affirms: *halacha ke-Rabbi Yehudah* — the law follows Rabbi Yehudah. A *ger* is considered a descendant of Abraham in every spiritually meaningful sense, may say *Eloheinu v'Elohei avoseinu*, and need not maintain a formal linguistic distinction marking his origins. This ruling coheres with the broader practice of identifying converts as *ben Avraham* or *bat Sarah*, acknowledging Abraham as their spiritual progenitor.

### Rambam Letter

The Rambam addressed this question directly in his famous letter to Ovadia HaGer, who inquired how his prayer should reflect his status as a convert. The Rambam ruled in full accordance with the position of the *Ri*: Ovadia HaGer is the spiritual heir of Abraham and may say *Eloheinu v'Elohei avoseinu* with complete integrity. The Rambam's ruling is grounded not only in the legal tradition traced through Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi but in the moral imperatives of *lo tonu* and *kager ka-ezrach* — the Torah's insistence that the convert be treated as fully equivalent to the native-born Israelite. The *daf* concludes here, leaving us with this profound question about the nature of belonging and spiritual inheritance that the *halacha* of *bikkurim* brings to the surface.
