# Yoma 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Assur vs. Chayav Kares

### Perek Introduction

We begin the final *perek* of Tractate Yoma, moving beyond the *avoda* of the *Kohen Gadol* and turning to Yom Kippur itself from our perspective — focused on its prohibitions. The discussion of *tefillah* will come later; we open at the very top of 74a.

### Mishnah Language

The issue that launches the *gemara* is the language of the *mishnah*, which describes it as *assur* to eat and drink on Yom Kippur. The *gemara* finds this something of an understatement: the prohibition is biblical in force and carries an *issur kares*. Why would the *mishnah* reach for the word *assur*, which typically connotes something lighter — indeed, something closer to a rabbinic prohibition — rather than the weightier *chayav*, or at minimum *chayav kares*?

### Rabbi Yochanan vs. Reish…

The *gemara's* answer is that *assur* is the appropriate term when discussing eating less than the minimum *shiur* — *chatzi shiur*. This introduces the famous debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *chatzi shiur* is *assur min haTorah* — forbidden by the Torah itself. Reish Lakish holds it is only rabbinically prohibited. Either way, the term *assur* fits, because we are speaking of something forbidden but not deserving the full weight of *chayav kares*, since the act involved is less than the minimum *shiur*.

### Shvua and Treifot

Within this broader debate arises a specific question involving *shvuot*. If someone takes an oath not to eat and then eats *treifus*, the *chachamim* (in contrast to Rabbi Shimon) hold that the person has still violated the oath. But one might ask: should the person not already be *mushba ve'omed* — fore-sworn from Sinai — against eating *treifa*? Why does the additional oath take effect at all?

---

## Shvua and Mushba Ve'Omed

### Kulel Explanation

One answer offered is the principle of *kolel*: the oath was a general one not to eat any food. Even though part of what it encompasses — eating *treifa* — is already covered by biblical prohibition, the oath also encompasses many other foods not otherwise forbidden. Since the oath takes effect on the broader category, it takes effect entirely, and eating *treif* food now violates not only the prohibition of *treif* but also the oath itself.

### Reish Lakish Alternative

Reish Lakish offers an alternative resolution: the case involved an oath specifically not to eat *chatzi shiur* — less than a *kezayis*. Since eating half a *kezayis* of forbidden food is not a biblical violation, the person was never biblically fore-sworn against it, and therefore the oath can take effect on that amount.

### Rabbi Akiva View

Rabbi Akiva goes further, holding that one need not even specify *chatzi shiur* explicitly. Any normal oath not to eat implies even the smallest amount. So either by explicit formulation or by implication, the person is not *mushba ve'omed* against *chatzi shiur*, and the oath therefore takes hold.

### Rabbinic Prohibitions Qu…

This raises a pointed question: according to Reish Lakish, who holds that *chatzi shiur* is at least rabbinically forbidden — since rabbinic prohibitions exist under the broader biblical mandate not to violate the words of the Rabbis — shouldn't the person still be *mushba ve'omed* even rabbinically? How can the oath take effect on something that is at minimum rabbinically prohibited?

---

## Biblical vs. Rabbinic

### Keivan D'Is Heter

The *gemara* addresses this with a principle: *keivan de'is lei heter min haTorah* — since biblically it is permissible, the oath can take effect, and therefore a *korban* would result from violating it. Though the Rabbis have forbidden it, the biblical permissibility is sufficient for the oath to operate.

The *gemara* then immediately challenges this principle: if a rabbinic prohibition suffices to block an oath in some contexts, the same should apply here.

### Shvuat Ha'edut

To illustrate this challenge, the *gemara* introduces the case of *shvuat ha'edut* — the oath of testimony. In the absence of formal subpoena, the Torah provides a mechanism by which one may impose an oath upon a person believed to possess relevant testimony. If that person knows the testimony and withholds it, the verse states *im lo yagid venasa avono* — if he does not testify, he bears the burden of his sin. The question becomes: on whom does this oath-obligation operate?

### Mishnah: Eligible Witnes…

The *mishnah* states that *shvuat ha'edut* applies only to those who are *re'uyim lehiahid* — eligible to testify. The *mishnah* had already explicitly excluded women, minors, and *gazlanim*. So the additional phrase "eligible to testify" must be excluding someone beyond those already listed.

---

## Psulei Edut

### Le'ma'utei Mai

The *gemara* asks: *le'ma'utei mai* — what additional category does the phrase "eligible to testify" come to exclude?

### Rav Papa: King

Rav Papa says it excludes a king. A king cannot be brought to court to testify, nor can testimony be given against him — *melech lo dan ve'lo danin*. Though this is not explicitly stated in the *mishnah*, it is implied by the additional qualifying phrase.

### Rav Acha: Gamblers

Rav Acha bar Yaakov says it excludes those who are rabbinically disqualified — specifically, dice players and gamblers — *mesachek b'kivya* — who are disqualified by rabbinic decree, not biblical law.

### Gamblers Disqualified

Two reasons are given for their disqualification. The first is that gamblers are not engaged in *yishuvo shel olam* — productive labor that contributes to the world — making them susceptible to bribery and corruption of testimony. The second reason is more technical: gambling involves a kind of *gezeila*, because the loser of the bet was never truly prepared to part with his money; the bet rests on an *asmachta lo kanya*, a commitment not fully intended. Either way, the disqualification is rabbinic in nature. Nevertheless, when a *shvuat ha'edut* is imposed on such a person, the oath does not take effect — even though biblically he is fully qualified to testify, the rabbinic disqualification suffices to exempt him from any liability.

---

## Rabbinic Prohibition Eff…

### Paradox Raised

This creates a striking difficulty. We see that a rabbinic disqualification from testimony is enough to prevent a *shvuat ha'edut* from taking hold. If so, why doesn't the rabbinic prohibition of eating *chatzi shiur* similarly prevent the oath from taking effect there? In both cases, something is rabbinically off-limits; in both cases, a biblical oath is at play. Why is the result different?

### Practical Ability Requir…

The *gemara* answers by pointing to the precise language of the verse: *im lo yagid* — if he does not testify. The Torah imposes liability only on someone who *has the practical ability* to testify and chooses not to. The gambling dice player, rabbinically barred from court, has no such practical ability — if he were to appear and offer testimony, the court would turn him away. *Ve'hai lav bar hagadah hu klal* — he simply cannot testify in any practical sense. The verse cannot be talking about him.

### Practical vs. Theoretical

The distinction the *gemara* draws is between practical inability and theoretical qualification. Whether the obstacle is a rabbinic disqualification or a physical impossibility — say, the witness is imprisoned — the result is the same: if he cannot actually appear and be accepted in court, the oath does not bind him. It is irrelevant that theoretically, under biblical law, he would be qualified. What matters is whether he has the genuine, operative ability to testify.

### Food Application

In the case of food, the analysis is entirely different. The question of whether one is *mushba ve'omed* — fore-sworn — is about the status of the object: is it something already biblically off-limits? Eating *chatzi shiur* of *chazir* is not biblically forbidden. It may be rabbinically restricted, but one is not biblically fore-sworn against it. Therefore, the oath takes effect on it. The two cases operate on entirely different axes: *shvuat ha'edut* turns on practical ability to act; *mushba ve'omed* turns on biblical prohibition of the object. A rabbinic food restriction is thus not analogous to a rabbinic testimonial disqualification.

As an aside, this analysis opens a broader question regarding witnesses who are *pesulei edut derabbanan* in other halachic contexts — such as witnesses at a wedding or a *get*. There the witnesses are not appearing before a court to testify; their function is to give legal weight and substance to the act of *kiddushin* or *geirushin*. The question of whether a rabbinically disqualified witness renders the act invalid *de'oraisa* is a significant debate among the *rishonim*, and it turns on whether the efficacy of witnesses in those contexts is tied to their practical ability to appear in court or to some other criterion.

---

## Assur and Braisah

### Principle Established

With the foregoing in place, the *gemara* asserts its broader principle: *vechol heicha de'tani enosh kares lo tani assur* — wherever the language of punishment by *kares* applies, the *mishnah* would not employ the word *assur*. This is in line with the Rambam's practice in *Hilchos Shabbos*, where he is more systematic than the *gemara* itself, consistently using *assur* for rabbinic prohibitions and *chayav* for biblical ones. The *gemara* is not always so rigorous, but the basic point stands: *assur* carries an implication of leniency — at most something like *patur aval assur* — and is out of place as a description of a prohibition carrying *kares*.

### Braisah Text

The *gemara* immediately challenges this principle from a *braisah*: *af al pi she'amru assur b'chulan* — even though they said it is forbidden for all of them — referring precisely to our *mishnah* about Yom Kippur, which lists eating, drinking, bathing, anointing, wearing sandals, and marital relations — *lo amru enosh kares ela ochel v'shoseh v'oseh melachah bilvad*. The only activities punishable by *kares* are eating, drinking, and performing *melachah*. So the *braisah* itself acknowledges that the word *assur* appears in the *mishnah* for activities that carry *kares* — and it is the *braisah* that then clarifies the different weight of the items on the list.

### Different Weights

The *braisah* thus confirms that the same word *assur* is used for the entire list, while noting that eating and drinking (and *melachah*) carry *kares* while the others do not. Far from supporting the *gemara's* principle, the *braisah* seems to demonstrate that *assur* can be used even for things punishable by *kares* — it is the *braisah's* clarifying role to explain the differential gravity.

### Chatzi Shiur Interpretat…

The *gemara* responds with what is admittedly a forced rereading of the *braisah*. It maintains its position that *assur* would never properly apply to a prohibition carrying *kares*, and therefore reinterprets the *braisah* as follows: when the *mishnah* says *assur* regarding eating and drinking, it is referring specifically to *chatzi shiur* — less than the minimum amount. For a full *shiur*, one incurs *kares*. The *braisah* comes to point out that of the categories listed, only eating, drinking, and *melachah* can ever reach the level of *kares* — but the use of *assur* in the *mishnah* itself applies only to the *chatzi shiur* case. This is, as noted, quite a strained reading, since the entire point of the *braisah* was precisely to acknowledge that the same term covers activities of differing weight.

---

## Assur for Other Items

### Assur Justification

The *gemara* offers an alternative explanation, which is considerably more compelling and resolves the difficulty with the *braisah* cleanly. It proposes: *ki tani assur asha'ara* — when the *mishnah* uses the word *assur*, it does so because that term is appropriate for the *other* items on the list. The *mishnah* enumerates bathing, anointing, wearing sandals, and marital relations alongside eating and drinking. For all of those other items, *assur* is exactly the right word — they are forbidden but do not carry *kares*. Since the *mishnah* is composing a unified list and needs a term that fits the majority of its items, it employs *assur* even for eating and drinking, which would otherwise warrant the stronger *chayav* or *chayav kares*.

---

## Assur for Other Items…

### Braisah Clarifies

The *braisah* then performs its natural function on this reading: it confirms that yes, the same word *assur* applies to everything on the list, but it clarifies that the first two categories — eating, drinking, and *melachah* — are of a categorically different and more severe character than the rest, and it is those that carry *chayav kares*. On this reading, the *mishnah* and *braisah* work harmoniously together, with no need to reread *assur* as a reference to *chatzi shiur*.

---

## Source for Inuyim

### Inuyim Introduction

Having established the terminological framework of the *mishnah*, the *gemara* now turns to the sources for the additional *inuyim* — the prohibitions beyond eating and drinking that apply on Yom Kippur: washing, anointing, wearing sandals, and marital relations.

### Sifrei and Shabbaton

Rabba and Rav Yosef teach, in the name of the other books of the House of Rav — *bishe'ar Sifrei d'vei Rav* — that the source for these additional Yom Kippur prohibitions is the word *Shabbaton*, as written in the verse: *Shabbat Shabbaton hi lachem ve'initem et nafshoteichem*.

### Sifrei D'vei Rav

The phrase *she'ar Sifrei d'vei Rav* warrants brief explanation. Rashi clarifies that it refers to the other *tannaitic midrashim* — those that are not *Torat Kohanim*, i.e., not the *Sifra* on Vayikra. The *Sifrei*, covering Bamidbar and Devarim, is sometimes labeled *Sifrei d'vei Rav* because Rav, as one of the earliest *amoraim*, is regarded as its editor. The very name *Sifrei* — "the books" — is secondary, a contrast to *Sifra*, meaning "the book."

### Sifra and Torat Kohanim

The *Sifra*, also known as *Torat Kohanim*, is the primary *tannaitic* midrash halacha on Vayikra. The fact that it is both called "the book" (*Sifra*) and bears the programmatic title *Torat Kohanim* reflects its centrality among the halachic midrashim. The *Sifrei*, comprising the midrash halacha on Bamidbar and Devarim, thus earns its name as "the other books," in contrast to this preeminent work.

---

## Shabbaton Concept

### Rashi's Parallel

Rashi's comment on the use of *Shabbaton* as the source for the additional *inuyim* is rich and carefully structured. Rashi notes that the verse *Shabbat Shabbaton hi lachem ve'initem* connects *Shabbaton* directly to *inui*. Just as the word *Shabbaton* in the context of Shabbat was used by the Rabbis to extend prohibitions beyond the core *melachot* — to other activities that are, in Rashi's striking phrasing, *she'ar melachot* of a lesser grade (*shelo hayu b'mishkan ve'einam melacha gmura*) — so too here, the word *Shabbaton* in proximity to *inui* comes *lehosif al inui achila ushtiya*: to add to the primary affliction of not eating and drinking these additional forms of self-restraint.

### Biblical or Rabbinic?

Rashi's formulation is deliberately ambiguous on the question of whether the additional *inuyim* are biblical or rabbinic. On the one hand, he uses the language *asmechu bei Rabbanan* — the Rabbis used this as their peg — suggesting an *asmachta*, a rabbinic enactment with a verse serving as a support. On the other hand, he writes that the *pasuk* is coming *lehosif* — to add — which suggests the verse itself is the operative source of the additional prohibitions, not merely a hanger for a rabbinic decree. This tension leaves Rashi's position genuinely ambiguous on the biblical-rabbinic question.

### Rambam and Ramban

The *rishonim* engage this question directly. Both Rambam and Ramban hold that the additional *inuyim* are not simply rabbinic with a verse as a mere *asmachta*. Rather, the process of rabbinic legislation here gives form and definition to a biblical mandate — the concept of *Shabbaton* — that extends beyond the specific prohibitions enumerated. The Rabbis are thus not legislating independently but articulating the content of an underlying biblical imperative.

Interestingly, the same dynamic applies by Shabbat, where both Rambam and Ramban understand the rabbinic restrictions on Shabbat as giving flesh to the biblical concept of *Shabbaton* that exists alongside the *melachot*. The *derabbanans* on Shabbat are not free-standing rabbinic inventions but the concrete expression of this broader biblical category.

### Ramban vs. Rambam

The debate between Ramban and Rambam concerns the nature of *Shabbaton* itself. The Ramban holds that the core concept of *Shabbaton* in the Torah — beyond *melachah* — is about refraining from exertion and labor-like activity, a category related to but conceptually distinct from the specific *melachot*. The Rambam, by contrast, holds that *Shabbaton* instructs us to prohibit not only the *melachot* proper but anything that leads toward *melachah* or resembles it — such as reading by candlelight, or performing an act with a *shinui*. On the Rambam's view, the rabbinic restrictions are defined by proximity and similarity to the *melachot*; on the Ramban's view, they represent a separate biblical category of withdrawal from exertion.

Applied to Yom Kippur, the question becomes whether the concept of *inui* is best understood through the lens of *Shabbaton* — as a form of withdrawal from the physical world — or whether it is a fundamentally different idea rooted in affliction, pain, and the *teshuvah* process. The Rambam, who titles his laws of Yom Kippur *Hilchos Shevisas Asor* — the resting on the tenth — sees the day as a *shabbat*-like stepping back from the physical world: just as one ceases *melachah*, one ceases eating, drinking, and bodily gratification. The *inui* is not about self-inflicted pain but about liberating oneself from anchoring in the physical world. This contrasts with the more intuitive understanding of *inui* as suffering and self-mortification in the service of *teshuvah* and *kapparah*.

## Chatzi Shiur Analysis

### Debate Restated

The *gemara* returns *gufa* — on its own terms — to the debate of Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish regarding *chatzi shiur*. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *assur min haTorah* — eating less than the minimum *shiur* of a forbidden food is biblically prohibited. Reish Lakish holds: *mutar min haTorah* — it is biblically permitted, though rabbinically forbidden.

### Chazi L'Itztarufei

Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning is: *keivan d'chazi l'itztarufei* — since the *chatzi shiur* is capable in principle of combining with an additional *chatzi shiur* to form a full *shiur*, *issura ka achil* — what one is eating is still the forbidden substance. Notably, Rabbi Yochanan does not say that one has performed an act of eating; rather, he says one has engaged with a forbidden object.

### Act of Eating

Reish Lakish's reasoning is: *achila amar Rachmana* — the Torah prohibits eating, *d'leka* — and here there has been no act of eating. Until one consumes a *kezayis*, the action does not qualify as an act of eating, and the prohibition therefore does not apply.

---

## Two Models of Shiur

### Model One: Transgression

The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish maps onto two fundamentally different ways of understanding why a minimum *shiur* is required for liability in the first place.

The first model holds that the transgression exists regardless of quantity: when the Torah says do not eat *chazir*, even nibbling *chazir* technically violates that command. The *shiur* is a threshold not for the transgression itself but for punishment — one does not receive *malkot* or *kares* below the minimum, but one has still done something wrong. This model sits very naturally with Rabbi Yochanan's position, since on this reading *chatzi shiur assur min haTorah* flows directly from the nature of the prohibition: the act of transgression occurs even below the *shiur*, only the penal threshold has not been met.

### Model Two: Act Itself

The second model holds that the *shiur* is constitutive of the act itself. There is no act of eating until one has consumed a *kezayis*; below that threshold, one has not yet performed the defined act that the Torah prohibits. You might have tasted or nibbled, but you have not *eaten* in the halachically operative sense. This is Reish Lakish's position: *achila amar Rachmana d'leka* — the Torah prohibited eating, and that act simply has not occurred.

### Model Implications

Rabbi Yochanan's position may be understood in a way that transcends both models. He appears not to claim that one has technically performed an act of *achilah* with a *chatzi shiur* — that is Reish Lakish's point, and Rabbi Yochanan does not explicitly contest it. Rather, his argument, captured in the phrase *issura ka achil* — one has ingested a forbidden substance — is that even absent a full act of *achilah*, there is something independently wrong about placing a forbidden object into one's body. It is not simply about whether the act of eating was complete; it is about the inherently problematic character of the object. The *chazi l'itztarufei* formulation supports this: the fact that two *chatzi shiurim* combine demonstrates that each one is already engaging with the forbidden substance, and that engagement is itself what the Torah regards as problematic, even if the act of *achilah* is only consummated at the *kezayis*.

---

## Kol Chelev Derasha

### Challenge Presented

Rabbi Yochanan presses his position against Reish Lakish by citing a *braisah* that derives a *pesuk*-based source for *chatzi shiur*. The verse regarding *chelev* states: *kol chelev lo tochlu* — do not eat any fat — and separately: *ki kol ochel chelev venichretas* — whoever eats fat will be subject to *kares*. From this juxtaposition, the *braisah* derives: I know only that those subject to punishment are subject to the prohibition; what about those not subject to punishment?

### Kol and Non-Punishment

The *braisah* identifies two such categories: one who eats *chelev* of a *koy* (discussed below), and one who eats *chatzi shiur*. In both cases, there is no *kares* — yet might we think there is also no prohibition? The verse therefore states *kol chelev* — any fat — teaching that even these non-punishment categories remain prohibited. Rabbi Yochanan cites this to demonstrate that *chatzi shiur* carries a biblical prohibition.

### Koy and Chatzi Shiur

The *koy* is an animal whose status as *chayah* or *behemah* is indeterminate. Because the prohibition on *chelev* applies to *behemah*, and the *koy* may or may not be a *behemah*, one who eats its *chelev* does not incur *kares* — the uncertainty prevents punishment. Similarly, *chatzi shiur* is below the punishable threshold. The verse *kol chelev* includes both: even these borderline cases remain in the sphere of prohibition.

### Response: Rabbinic

The *gemara* responds: *talmud lomar mi-drabbanan ve-kra asmachata b'alma* — this is actually a rabbinic enactment, and the verse is merely a *peg* upon which the Rabbis hang their decree. The prohibition of *chatzi shiur* is not, in fact, derived biblically from *kol chelev*.

---

## Koy Problem

### Koy Question

The *gemara* now challenges the alternative reading. If the prohibition is indeed biblical and *kol chelev* is the operative source, why would the verse need to include the *koy* at all? The *koy's* status is uncertain to us, not to the Torah. God knows perfectly well whether the *koy* is a *chayah* or *behemah*. The verse's inclusion of the *koy* would be saying: even this thing which you don't know the status of — it is still prohibited. But that seems to say nothing that logic would not already dictate: if it might be *chelev behemah*, of course one should be cautious. Why would divine legislation be needed to state that?

### Koy Its Own Category

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the possibility that the author of this *braisah* holds *koy beria bifnei atzma hi* — the *koy* is its own independent category, neither *chayah* nor *behemah*. If that is so, a genuine question arises: the Torah prohibited *chelev behemah* and permitted *chelev chayah* — but what about something that is categorically neither? On this view, the *koy's* chelev is not simply a case of uncertainty; it is a qualitative borderline case, a quasi-*behemah*, and the verse *kol chelev* genuinely extends the prohibition to it. The verse thus carries its own biblical work, and *chatzi shiur* may similarly be included through the same verse on biblical grounds.

### Dam Parallel

A parallel discussion supports this reading. Rav Idi bar Avin notes that the verse *kol dam lo tocheilu* — do not eat any blood — uses the word *kol*, which the *gemara* says comes to include the blood of a *koy*. Here again the question arises: why would the verse need to include the *koy's* blood, since the Torah prohibits both the blood of *chayah* and *behemah*? The answer, again, is that if the *koy* is its own category, its blood might otherwise not be covered. *Birya shani* — an independent category is treated differently — and the verse therefore extends explicitly to it. The same logic applies in the *chelev* case, and *kol chelev* may therefore carry genuine biblical force in its inclusion of *chatzi shiur*.

---

## Chatzi Shiur Scope

### Foods Only?

Two further questions about *chatzi shiur* emerge from the preceding analysis.

The first is whether the prohibition of *chatzi shiur* applies only to foods or extends to other categories of Torah prohibition as well. If, for instance, one carries on Shabbat half the minimum *shiur*, or melts half the minimum amount of metal, has one biblically violated Shabbat? A number of *poskim* hold that *chatzi shiur* is limited to foods. The reason is that the biblical prohibition derives from the verse *kol chelev lo tochlu* — which, even when extended conceptually, speaks of eating and ingestion. One can broaden that conceptually to all *ma'achalot assurot* and even to all eating, but there is no basis in the verse to extend it to entirely different categories of prohibition such as *melachot* on Shabbat. Accordingly, the biblical prohibition of *chatzi shiur* would not apply there. (We *pasken* like Rabbi Yochanan, and *chatzi shiur* is *de'oraisa*; however, many *poskim* would limit this to food-related prohibitions.)

An additional conceptual justification for limiting *chatzi shiur* to foods is the logic of *issura ka achil* — one has ingested a forbidden substance. When there is a forbidden *cheftza*, a taboo object, the mere ingestion of any part of it is problematic, because the object itself carries a prohibition. When the prohibition is about an *act* rather than an *object* — such as the act of *melachah* — the logic does not transfer: half an act of *melachah* is not an engagement with a forbidden object; it is simply an incomplete act.

### Generic or Subcategory?

The second question is whether *chatzi shiur* constitutes a single generic prohibition — something like a blanket rule derived from *kol chelev* that any *chatzi shiur* of any forbidden food is prohibited — or whether it is a subcategory of each individual prohibition. That is: is *chatzi shiur* of *chelev* a quasi-violation of the *chelev* prohibition, *chatzi shiur* of *chazir* a quasi-violation of the *chazir* prohibition, and so forth — each anchored in its own issur?

### Ramban's Question

The Ramban already raises a practical consequence of this question. The halacha is that when one must violate a prohibition to save a life, the lesser prohibition is preferred. Suppose both a *chatzi shiur* of *dam* (whose full *shiur* carries *kares*) and a *chatzi shiur* of *chazir* (whose full *shiur* carries a lesser prohibition) are available to sustain a dangerously ill person. Is there a preference for the *chazir*? Or are both instances of *chatzi shiur* simply the same generic prohibition and therefore equivalent? The answer hinges on whether *chatzi shiur* is a subcategory of its underlying *issur* — in which case quasi-*dam* is more severe than quasi-*chazir* — or a uniform generic prohibition.

### Issur Cheftza

This connects to the broader distinction between *issur cheftza* — a prohibition rooted in the forbidden character of the object — and other forms of prohibition. The logic of *chatzi shiur* is most naturally at home in the domain of *issur cheftza*, where any engagement with the taboo substance is itself problematic, because the problem lies in the object rather than exclusively in the act.

---

## Eating Prohibition Nature

### State Not Object

The analysis of *chatzi shiur* connects naturally to the prohibitions of Yom Kippur but also points to an important conceptual difference. The standard categories of *ma'achalot assurot* are paradigm cases of *issur cheftza*: the object — *chazir*, *chelev*, *dam* — is itself the forbidden thing, and any engagement with it is problematic. On Yom Kippur, by contrast, the prohibition of eating is not about the object being forbidden. Any food is permitted at any other time; the problem is not the food but the *state* of the person — whether one is fasting or not, satiated or hungry. The Torah does not declare bread or meat a taboo substance on Yom Kippur; it demands that one remain in a state of *inui*.

### Conceptual Challenge

This distinction makes it conceptually harder to simply transfer the *chatzi shiur* logic from *ma'achalot assurot* to Yom Kippur. The argument *issura ka achil* — you have engaged with a forbidden substance — does not apply in the same way, since the food is not forbidden as an object. Whether and how Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning about *chatzi shiur* extends to Yom Kippur therefore remains a genuine conceptual question.

---

## Eating Prohibition Source

### Te'anu Meaning

Where does the Torah's prohibition of eating and drinking on Yom Kippur come from? The verse says *teanu et nafshoteichem* — afflict yourselves. But *inui* could mean many things. Perhaps it means sitting in the sun, or sitting in the cold, or other forms of bodily discomfort. How do we know it specifically means refraining from food and drink?

### First Braisah

The first *braisah* addresses this by juxtaposing the command of *inui* with the adjacent verse *vechol melachah lo taasu* — do not perform any *melachah*. The *braisah* reasons: just as refraining from *melachah* operates by *shev ve'al taaseh* — by sitting still and not doing — so too *inui nefesh* must operate through *shev ve'al taaseh*: a form of affliction achieved by passively not doing something rather than by actively doing something painful. This rules out sitting in the sun or deliberately exposing oneself to discomfort, which are active forms of self-affliction.

---

## Parallel to Melacha

### Refraining Logic

The logic of the parallel is direct: *melachah* is defined as something one refrains from; *inui* must likewise be defined as refraining. The *gezeira shava* between the two is not a formal lexical one but a structural parallel: they appear together in the verse, and so the character of one illuminates the character of the other.

### No Distinction

One might attempt to preserve a role for active *inui* in certain cases — for instance, if one is already comfortable, perhaps one must actively put oneself in a situation of discomfort. The *gemara* rejects this: just as there is no such distinction by *melachah*, there is no such distinction by *inui*. There is no category of *melachah* that sometimes requires active engagement; *melachah* is always defined by abstention. So too *inui* is always defined by abstention.

### Rashi: Passive Active

Rashi sharpens the point. The distinction the *braisah* draws is between passive and active affliction. By *melachah*, abstention is always passive — one simply does not perform the act. The parallel to *inui* teaches that *inui* must similarly be always achievable through passivity. If the only way to afflict oneself in certain circumstances would be to actively seek out discomfort — sitting in the sun, or staying in the cold — that does not qualify as the Torah's *inui*, because it cannot be framed as *shev ve'al taaseh*.

## Second Braisah

### Activity Standard

A second *braisah* takes a different approach to deriving what *inui* means. Again juxtaposing *inui* and *melachah*, it reasons: just as *melachah* on Shabbat is an activity that is punishable *elsewhere* — that is, *melachah* is defined as the kind of act that has halachic consequence and carries punishment in other contexts (as indeed the *melachot* are defined by their roles in other halachic systems) — so too *inui nefesh* must be an activity that is punishable *elsewhere*. The criterion is not merely that it is unpleasant, but that it constitutes the kind of act that in some other context carries liability. This rules out sitting in the sun or cold: there is no halachic context in which one is punished for sitting in the sun.

### Piggul and Notar

What type of eating is punishable elsewhere? The *braisah* answers: eating *piggul* and *notar* — sacred foods rendered invalid through improper intent or left past their allotted time. These carry *kares*, just as performing *melachah* on Shabbat carries *kares*. So the *braisah* establishes its initial category of *inui nefesh* as referring to eating — specifically, a kind of eating that is punishable, analogous to *melachah*.

### Expanding Punishment

But *kares* is not the only form of punishment in the Torah. The *braisah* extends the category through the double language of *te'anu ve'inisem* to include foods punishable by *misa biyedei shamayim* — heavenly death — even where the punishment does not reach *kares*, as with *tevel*. The doubled expression broadens the operative category of prohibited eating.

---

## Narrowing Scope

### Neveilah Inclusion

From foods punishable in some context, the *braisah* moves through a *kal vachomer* to include *neveilah* and other foods carrying a simple *lav* — a biblical prohibition without *kares* or *misa biyedei shamayim*. Even these, the *braisah* establishes, fall under the prohibition of *inui nefesh* through the expansive language of *te'anu ve'inisem*.

### All Foods Included

Once forbidden foods are included, the *braisah* reasons further: all foods must be included. The prohibition of *inui* on Yom Kippur is not merely a prohibition on eating food that is otherwise forbidden; it encompasses ordinary, permitted food. The expansive language of *te'anu* continues to broaden the category.

### Obligatory Foods

The *braisah* continues: having included discretionary eating, one might think that foods one is obligated to eat — such as *terumah*, which carries a *mitzvah* for the *kohen* to consume — might be excluded. The *braisah* includes these as well through the repeated formulation of the verse.

### Kodashim

Finally, the *braisah* addresses *kodashim* — the most obligatory category of eating, where there is not only a *mitzvah* to eat but a positive prohibition of *bal totiru* — leaving the sacred food past its time. One might have thought that where there is an active mandate and a corresponding prohibition against not eating, perhaps eating on Yom Kippur would be permitted or even required. The *braisah* rules otherwise: *ta'anu et nafshoteichem* includes even this case.

What this *braisah* achieves, conceptually, is grounding the prohibition of eating on Yom Kippur in the world of *ma'achalot assurot* — forbidden eating. The idea is: how do we know *te'anu* means don't eat? Because elsewhere in the Torah, eating can be a prohibited, punishable act. The Torah here is prohibiting that same category of activity — eating — but in the broadest possible scope: all eating, even obligatory eating. This is a conceptually striking move: the *inui* of Yom Kippur is understood as a species of forbidden eating, albeit one universalized to encompass all food. Notably, this model — rooting *inui* in the paradigm of *ma'achalot assurot* — connects directly to the model of *melachah* introduced in the first *braisah*, where the parallel was more about *shev ve'al taaseh*. The two *braisos* thus reflect two distinct conceptual frameworks for understanding what *inui* is and where it sits in the halachic system.

## V'Avadeti Source

### Verse Quoted

The *gemara* now presents an alternative source for the prohibition of eating and drinking on Yom Kippur: *ve'ha'avadeti et hanefesh hahi* — I will destroy that soul — referring to the one who does not afflict themselves.

### Body Wasting

The *braisah* derives from this verse not a punishment but a definition: *inui she'hu avedat hanefesh* — the type of *inui* being referred to is one that involves the wasting away, the losing, of the body. The word *avadeti* — destruction — signals that the *inui* the Torah has in mind causes a literal diminishment of the body.

### Loss of Calories

What activity causes actual bodily wasting? *Achila ve'shtiya* — eating and drinking. Refraining from food and drink depletes the body of its sustenance, causing a measurable, physical diminishment. This is the third conceptual model for *inui*: not withdrawal (*shev ve'al taaseh*), not a species of *ma'achalot assurot*, but genuine self-affliction in the sense of allowing the body to waste. This model aligns most naturally with the intuitive understanding of Yom Kippur as a day of suffering and *kapparah* — real, bodily discomfort as an instrument of *teshuvah*.

---

## Multiple Sources Why

### Question Raised

The *gemara* asks: why are multiple sources needed? Given the first derivation, why not simply rely on that?

### Arayos Problem

The problem with relying solely on the first derivation — that *inui* must be something punishable elsewhere, leading us to eating — is that there is another activity punishable elsewhere that could plausibly qualify: *arayot*, forbidden sexual relations. Sexual relations with prohibited partners carry severe punishment in many contexts. One might therefore argue that *te'anu* mandates refraining not from food but from marital relations, or that it mandates refraining from both. The first source alone does not clearly exclude this possibility.

### Arayos Excluded

The verse *ve'ha'avadeti et hanefesh* resolves this. The destruction of the body — the wasting signaled by *avadeti* — uniquely identifies eating and drinking as the subject. Sexual relations, while prohibited in many contexts, do not cause bodily wasting of the kind described by *ve'ha'avadeti*. The verse therefore specifically and exclusively points to *achila ve'shtiya* as the operative *inui*, while simultaneously excluding *arayot* from the scope of the prohibition.

## Re'avon Parallel

### Gezerah Shava

A final source is presented in the name of the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *ne'emar kan inui ve'ne'emar le-hallan inui* — the word *inui* is stated here, in the Yom Kippur context, and the word *inui* is stated elsewhere in another context. This constitutes a *gezerah shava* — a derivation based on the use of the same word in two separate contexts.

### Inui Re'avon

The parallel context is in Devarim, where God's affliction of Israel in the desert is described: *va-ye'anecha va-yar'ivecha* — He afflicted you and made you go hungry. Here *inui* appears directly alongside *re'avon* — hunger, starvation. The juxtaposition is explicit: affliction is hunger.

### Starving Conclusion

By *gezerah shava*, the *inui* of Yom Kippur is interpreted in light of this parallel: just as the *inui* mentioned in Devarim means going hungry — literal starvation — so too the *inui* of Yom Kippur means the same. *Te'anu et nafshoteichem* is a command to go hungry, to starve, and that means refraining from *achila ve'shtiya*.
