# Bava Metzia 72 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ribbit via Non-Jew

### Intro: Ribbit Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מַלְוֶה יִשְׂרָאֵל מְעוֹתָיו שֶׁל נׇכְרִי מִדַּעַת הַנׇּכְרִי, אֲבָל לֹא מִדַּעַת יִשְׂרָאֵל, כֵּיצַד? יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלָּוָה מָעוֹת מִן הַגּוֹי בְּרִבִּית וּבִיקֵּשׁ לְהַחְזִירָם לוֹ, מְצָאוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֵר וְאָמַר לוֹ: תְּנֵם לִי וַאֲנִי אַעֲלֶה לָךְ כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲלֶה לוֹ – אָסוּר. וְאִם הֶעֱמִידוּ אֵצֶל גּוֹי – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught in a *baraita*: **A Jew may lend a gentile's money** as a middleman to another Jew **with the knowledge of the gentile, but not with the knowledge of a Jew. How so?** In the case of **a Jew who borrowed money with interest from a gentile and** then **wanted to return it to him,** but at that point **another Jew found** the borrower **and said to him:** Since you do not need this money anymore and I do need it, **give it to me and I will pay you** with the addition of interest **in the same way that you pay** the gentile, this is **prohibited,** since this is interest paid to a Jew. **But if** the borrower **presented** the second Jew **to the gentile** and the gentile agreed to this arrangement, it is **permitted.**

The daf picks up at the bottom of 71b and carries into 72a, addressing a question of enormous practical significance — particularly in the medieval context — namely, whether a non-Jew can serve as an intermediary through whom one Jew effectively lends money to another Jew with interest. The *Tanna'im* and *Amoraim* were acutely aware that the prohibition of *ribbit* could be circumvented by routing a loan through a non-Jewish third party, and the *Gemara* works carefully to identify exactly where the line falls. The discussion builds on the preceding analysis — where Rabbeinu Tam found ways to limit the scope of the Rabbinic restriction on lending to a non-Jew with interest — and now turns to the reverse manipulation: using a non-Jew as a cutout to lend to a Jew with interest.

### Case 1: Jew Borrows

The *Beraita* (*Tanu Rabbanan*) presents two parallel cases. In the first: a Jew — call him Reuven — has borrowed money from a non-Jew, Christopher, with interest. The loan has matured and Reuven wishes to repay the full amount, principal plus accrued interest. Shimon, another Jew, appears and says to Reuven: "Give me the money instead. I will pay you the same interest you were paying Christopher." This is clearly *assur*. Whatever the arrangement between Reuven and Christopher, the operative transaction is now Shimon paying interest to Reuven — a Jew paying a Jew — and that is forbidden *ribbit*.

### Two Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן גּוֹי שֶׁלָּוָה מָעוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל בְּרִבִּית וּבִיקֵּשׁ לְהַחְזִירָם לוֹ, מְצָאוֹ יִשְׂרָאֵל אַחֵר וְאָמַר לוֹ: תְּנֵם לִי וַאֲנִי אַעֲלֶה לְךָ כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲלֶה לוֹ – מוּתָּר. וְאִם הֶעֱמִידוֹ אֵצֶל יִשְׂרָאֵל – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And similarly, if a gentile borrowed money with interest from a Jew and** then **wanted to return it to him,** and **another Jew found** the gentile **and said to him: Give it to me and I will pay you** interest **in the same way that you pay** the Jewish lender, this is **permitted,** since he pays interest to a gentile and not to a Jew. But **if** the gentile borrower **presented** the second Jew **to** the Jew who lent him the money and the Jewish lender agreed to this arrangement, it is **prohibited.**

The *Beraita* then introduces a permitted alternative involving the same parties. Instead of the above arrangement, Reuven and Shimon go together to Christopher's location. Standing in Christopher's presence, Reuven hands the repayment money — rather than to Christopher directly — to Shimon. Shimon then assumes the loan, owing Christopher the money with interest. This, the *Beraita* states, is *mutar*. The underlying logic is that by virtue of occurring in Christopher's presence, the transaction is treated as if Reuven repaid Christopher and Christopher in turn lent the money to Shimon. Shimon thus owes interest to Christopher — a Jew owing interest to a non-Jew — which is permissible.

### Permitted Method

The *heter* in this case turns on the idea that the physical context of the transaction — Reuven handing money to Shimon in Christopher's presence, with Christopher's apparent consent — causes the debt to be reconstituted as a loan from Christopher to Shimon. The *chiddush* is that we do not simply follow the money and say "Reuven gave the money to Shimon, therefore Shimon owes Reuven." Rather, we ask whose money was it originally and through whose act of lending was it placed in circulation. When the full repayment sum is transferred to a new party in the lender's presence and with the lender's assent, the law treats it as if the original loan was repaid and a new loan was made to the new party.

### Case 2: Non-Jew Borrows

The parallel case runs in the opposite direction. Christopher has borrowed money from Reuven with interest. Christopher wishes to repay. Shimon appears and says: "Christopher, give me the money; I will pay back Reuven what you owe him with interest." This is *mutar*, because Shimon is paying interest to Christopher, and that is permissible. However, if instead Reuven, Christopher, and Shimon all go to Reuven's location and Christopher, standing before Reuven, hands the repayment to Shimon, that is *assur* — because we now treat it as if Christopher repaid Reuven and Reuven lent the money to Shimon. Shimon now owes interest to Reuven, a Jew paying a Jew.

### Impermissible Method

The symmetry of the two cases rests on a single operative principle: when the total sum changes hands in the presence of the original lender or creditor, we conceptualize the transaction as if the original debt was discharged and a new loan was made to the new recipient by that creditor. When the creditor is Jewish, this is *assur*; when the creditor is a non-Jew, it is *mutar*. The presence of the original lender is what determines who is deemed to have made the new loan.

### Conceptual Challenge

This raises a genuine conceptual difficulty. At the end of the day, in Case 1 (the *mutar* scenario), it is Reuven who physically gives the money to Shimon — not Christopher. Christopher never handed anything to Shimon. So how does it follow that Shimon's debt and interest obligation run to Christopher rather than to Reuven? Similarly, in Case 2 (the *assur* scenario), it is Christopher who physically gives the money to Shimon — not Reuven. Reuven never gave Shimon anything. Why, then, do we treat it as if Reuven is Shimon's lender?

The *Gemara*'s answer rests on a foundational framing: we do not determine the nature of the loan simply by observing who happens to hand over the cash. We ask, rather, who was the original owner of the funds and through whose act of lending were those funds extended. The physical delivery of cash is merely the mechanism; the legal character of the loan is determined by identifying from whose capacity the money was placed in play. When the entire sum — not just an interest payment but the full principal and interest — is transferred to a new party in the original lender's presence and with that lender's assent, we treat it as if the lender received the repayment and then extended a fresh loan.

### Tosafot's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא סֵיפָא, לְחוּמְרָא. אֶלָּא רֵישָׁא: כֵּיוָן דְּאֵין שְׁלִיחוּת לְגוֹי, אִיהוּ נִיהוּ דְּקָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ רִבִּיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the *baraita*: **Granted, the latter clause** of the *baraita*, which says that it is prohibited for a Jew to pay the gentile borrower when the Jewish lender is aware of the transaction, can be explained as **a stringency** due to concern about the prohibition of interest, **but in the first clause** the Jewish lender presents the Jewish borrower to the gentile before handing him the money, and the fact that this is permitted indicates that he acts as the gentile's agent, not on his own. But **since** the *halakha* is that **there is no agency for a gentile,** this means that the Jew **is** the one **who takes the interest from** the second borrower. Why, then, does the *baraita* permit it?

*Tosafot* presses the conceptual question further. One might argue that the simplest test for who owes the *ribbit* is to ask: who is actually handing over the interest payments? In Case 1, Christopher continues to receive his five percent. Why not simply say that since Shimon is delivering the interest to Christopher, Shimon owes interest to Christopher — and since that is Jew-to-non-Jew, it is fine — regardless of how we conceptualize the loan structure? *Tosafot*'s point is that the question of "who pays the interest" and "to whom is the interest owed" are not necessarily the same question.

The response is that we cannot determine the character of the loan merely from the direction of cash flow. Theoretically, Shimon might owe his interest to Reuven, and merely be delivering it to Christopher as a shortcut — since Reuven himself owes Christopher and Shimon's payment to Christopher could simply represent Shimon paying Reuven who in turn pays Christopher. The principle of *shib'uda d'Rabbi Natan* does allow A to pay C directly when A owes B and B owes C, but even that principle does not collapse the intermediate stages — it simply allows the cash to be delivered to the end of the chain. The conceptual debt structure still runs through each link. So the question of whose money Shimon actually took — and therefore to whom his loan obligation runs — must be determined at the outset, not inferred from who ends up holding the interest payments.

## Role of Presence

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַנִּיחֵם עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע וְהִיפָּטֵר. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ said in the name of Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika: Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the gentile **said to** the Jew when he returned the money: **Place it upon the ground and be dismissed,** and afterward the other Jew went and took it. Therefore, the loan was transacted directly between the second Jew and the gentile. The Gemara asks: **If that is so, what** is the purpose **of stating** it? In this scenario it is obvious that there are two separate loans and the first Jewish lender has nothing to do with the loan to the second. This is certainly permitted.

The *Gemara* now turns to its central analytical question: the *Beraita* treats presence — of the original lender at the moment the money changes hands — as the operative legal factor. But why should mere presence accomplish anything? In Case 1, Reuven hands the money to Shimon. Whether or not Christopher is standing in the room, the money went from Reuven to Shimon. How does proximity to Christopher transform the nature of what just happened?

### No Shlichus for Non-Jew

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁנָּטַל וְנָתַן בַּיָּד. וְאַכַּתִּי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא?! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: גּוֹי גּוּפֵיהּ כִּי עָבֵיד – אַדַּעְתָּא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל קָא גָמֵיר וְיָהֵיב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said:** It must be speaking about a case **where** the gentile **took** the money from the first Jew **and gave** it to the second Jew **by hand.** The Gemara asks: **But still, what** is the purpose **of stating** it? In this case also, the second loan is clearly transacted directly with the gentile lender. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: The gentile himself, when he does this, he gives it with the knowledge of the Jew,** as he trusts the second borrower due to the mediation of the first, and therefore one might have thought he is deemed involved in the loan. To counter this, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The *Gemara* raises the obvious difficulty with the *mutar* case. *Bishlama seifa lechumra* — the *assur* ruling in Case 2 can be explained by a desire to be strict: even though Christopher gave the money to Shimon and not Reuven, we will treat it as though Reuven lent Shimon the money. But the *reisha*, the *mutar* ruling, is harder. When Reuven hands the money to Shimon in Christopher's presence, the *Gemara* notes: *ein shlichus l'nochri*. A non-Jew cannot serve as a *shaliach*. Therefore Christopher cannot appoint Reuven as his *shaliach* to deliver funds to Shimon on his behalf. When Reuven hands the money to Shimon, he is not acting in Christopher's capacity — there is no legal mechanism for that. He is handing over his own money. So from Shimon's perspective, he has simply received money from Reuven. Christopher never lent Shimon anything. How, then, can we say this is *mutar* on the grounds that Shimon owes his interest to Christopher?

### Rav Huna's Answer

The *Gemara* immediately objects: *ei hachi, mai l'meimra?* If that is the mechanism, what is the *chiddush*? The *Beraita* is telling us something we already know: if Christopher took Reuven's money and lent it to Shimon, then of course Shimon owes Christopher. That is simply a straightforward loan. There is no novelty in stating that fact. The innovation was supposed to be that *presence* of the non-Jew transforms an otherwise impermissible transaction — but under Rav Huna's reading, there is nothing special about the scenario at all. Rav Papa similarly suggests that Christopher took the money from Reuven and handed it directly to Shimon — but the *Gemara* again dismisses this as stating the obvious.

### Full Payment Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** To counter this, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the case.

The *Gemara* then offers the *chiddush* it was looking for. One might have thought: when Reuven and Shimon come before Christopher, and Reuven — at Shimon's request and in the context of facilitating Shimon's new loan — hands the money to Shimon, perhaps Christopher in this arrangement is acting merely as Reuven's facilitator. That is, maybe Christopher's role here is just to carry out Reuven's wish that Shimon receive the money, and the underlying structure remains that it is Reuven's money going to Shimon. *Ka mashma lan* — the *Beraita* teaches us this is not so: when conducted properly, the transaction is recognized as Christopher lending Shimon the money, not as Reuven. This is the *chiddush*.

But the *Gemara*'s conclusion, emerging from its rejection of the intermediate answers, is that mere proximity to Christopher is insufficient. The transaction must actually be completed — the money genuinely repaid to Christopher and then re-lent by Christopher — in order for the loan to be reconstituted between Christopher and Shimon. Simply performing the handoff in Christopher's room, without a genuine two-step of repayment and new lending, does not accomplish the legal restructuring.

This conclusion has important practical implications. It means that the permissible structure — a Jew borrowing from a non-Jew and then having a second Jew take over the loan — requires that the full repayment genuinely occur, and then the non-Jew separately lend the funds to the new borrower. The non-Jew need not even be present when the new loan is made; he could send the money via Christopher to the new borrower separately. Conversely, even if everyone is in the same room, if there is no genuine intermediate step of repayment and re-lending, the transaction does not shift the loan relationship to the non-Jew.

### Rav Ashi's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: כִּי אָמְרִינַן אֵין שְׁלִיחוּת לְגוֹי – הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּתְרוּמָה, אֲבָל בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ – יֵשׁ שְׁלִיחוּת לְגוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** It is possible to explain the *baraita* in a different way. **When we say** that the *halakha* is **that there is no agency for a gentile, this matter** applies **concerning** the separation of ***teruma*,** the portion of produce designated for a priest. It is in this context that the *halakha* that there is no agency for a gentile is derived, **but concerning the rest of the** *halakhot* of the **Torah, there is agency for a gentile.**

Rav Ashi offers a creative alternative. He proposes: when we say *ein shlichus l'nochri* — that a non-Jew cannot be a *shaliach* — this rule applies only in the context of *terumah*. In all other areas of *halacha*, a non-Jew can in fact serve as an agent. Therefore, in the *mutar* case, when Christopher instructs Reuven to give the money to Shimon, Reuven acts as Christopher's *shaliach* — and that *shlichus* is legally valid. Shimon thereby receives the money from Christopher through his agent, and Shimon's loan runs to Christopher.

### Rejection of Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּרוּתָא הִיא: מַאי שְׁנָא תְּרוּמָה דְּלָא, דִּכְתִיב ״אַתֶּם״ – ״גַּם אַתֶּם״: מָה אַתֶּם בְּנֵי בְּרִית – אַף שְׁלוּחֲכֶם נָמֵי בְּנֵי בְּרִית. שְׁלִיחוּת דְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ נָמֵי מִתְּרוּמָה גָּמְרִינַן לַהּ. אֶלָּא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּרוּתָא הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And this** opinion **of Rav Ashi is an error,** since **what is different** about ***teruma*** that a gentile **cannot** be appointed an agent? **As it is written** concerning *teruma*: "So you also shall set apart a gift unto the Lord of all your tithes" (Numbers 18:28); once the verse states **"you,"** the addition of the word "also" in the phrase **"you also"** serves to include an agent. The Sages additionally derive: **Just as you,** those who appoint agents, are **members of the covenant,** i.e., Jews, **so too, your agents** must be **members of the covenant.** A gentile cannot separate *teruma* even if appointed as an agent by a Jew. The concept of **agency with regard to the rest of the** *halakhot* of the **Torah is also** a matter **we learn** through tradition by a derivation **from *teruma*,** as this is the source for the *halakha* that the legal status of one's agent is like that of himself. Therefore, the same *halakhot* apply to agency in all matters. **Rather,** it must be concluded that the opinion expressed **by Rav Ashi is an error.**

The *Gemara* dismisses Rav Ashi's proposal bluntly — on one version it says *bruta hi* and on another *bedusa hi* — both expressions indicating that the idea is without merit. The rejection proceeds as follows: the rule that *ein shlichus l'nochri* is derived from the verse regarding *terumah*, *"atem gam atem"* — "you, even you" — which teaches that just as *you* (the one separating *terumah*) are members of the covenant, so too your *shaliach* must be a member of the covenant. But the laws of *shlichus* for the entire *Torah* are derived from *terumah*. Therefore, *ein shlichus l'nochri* is not a rule limited to *terumah* — it is a universal rule derived from *terumah* and applied throughout *halacha*.

Rav Ashi's second formulation — that while a non-Jew cannot be *our* *shaliach*, we can be *their* *shaliach* — is similarly rejected. The derivation from *"atem gam atem"* means that for *shlichus* to operate, the *meshale'ach* (the one appointing the agent) must be a member of the covenant. If Christopher appoints Reuven as his *shaliach*, Christopher is the *meshale'ach*, and Christopher is not a member of the covenant. The verse requires that the *atem* — the party who sends — be Jewish. Since Christopher cannot satisfy that condition, the appointment of Reuven as his *shaliach* is legally void regardless of Reuven's status. The *Gemara* therefore concludes: *ela hadar bei Rav Ashi* — Rav Ashi retracted.

### Ravina's Zechiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: נְהִי דִּשְׁלִיחוּת לְגוֹי לֵית לֵיהּ, זְכִיָּה מִדְּרַבָּנַן אִית לֵיהּ, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵי אַקָּטָן. קָטָן לָאו אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ שְׁלִיחוּת, אִית לֵיהּ זְכִיָּה מִדְּרַבָּנַן. הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina says** another answer: **Granted, a gentile is not** included in the category of **agency.** But **he has** the power, **by rabbinic law,** to **acquire** an item through an act of acquisition performed by another. This is **similar to** the *halakha* concerning a Jewish **minor.** As with **a minor, is it not** the case **that even though he is not** included in the category **of agency,** nevertheless, **he has** the power, **by rabbinic law,** to **acquire** an item by means of an act of acquisition performed by another? **Here, also,** it **is not different;** the Jew can perform an act of acquisition on behalf of the gentile even though there is no agency for gentiles.

Ravina proposes an alternative that sidesteps *shlichus* altogether. He invokes the principle of *zechiyah* — specifically *zachin l'adam shelo befanav*, the rule that one may acquire something on behalf of another person even without that person's explicit agency. The idea is: Christopher does not need to appoint Reuven as his *shaliach*. Instead, when Reuven hands the money to Shimon, Shimon acquires the money on behalf of Christopher. Shimon is *zocheh* in the money for Christopher. Now Christopher holds the money — through Shimon's act of *zechiyah* — and simultaneously lends it to Shimon. Shimon thus owes the debt to Christopher.

The model for *zechiyah* without *shlichus* is a minor child. A *katan* cannot appoint a *shaliach* — he lacks the legal capacity for *shlichus* — yet one can be *zocheh* in something on behalf of a minor. For example, if someone wishes to give a *Bar Mitzvah* gift to a child who has not yet reached his *Bar Mitzvah*, a third party can take *kinyan* on the child's behalf. The acquisition is valid even though the child could not himself make that person his *shaliach*. *Miderabbanan*, by Rabbinic enactment, *zechiyah* operates independently of *shlichus*. Ravina argues the same should apply to a non-Jew: Shimon can be *zocheh* in the money for Christopher through *zechiyah miderabbanan*, even though Christopher cannot make Shimon his *shaliach*.

### Rejection of Zechiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא: יִשְׂרָאֵל אָתֵי לִכְלַל שְׁלִיחוּת, נׇכְרִי לָא אָתֵי לִכְלַל שְׁלִיחוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this comparison: **But** that **is not so.** A minor **Jew will** eventually **reach** the stage of eligibility for **agency,** but **a gentile will not reach** eligibility **for agency.** Consequently, the Sages did not establish the power for gentiles to acquire an item by means of an act of acquisition performed by a Jew.

The *Gemara* rejects this as well: *v'lo hi*. The operative principle is: *Yisrael ati l'chlal shlichus, akum lo ati l'chlal shlichus*. The minor child will eventually reach adulthood and will then be capable of *shlichus*. *Zechiyah* is grounded in, or at minimum dependent upon, the eventual potential for *shlichus*. It applies only to someone who will one day enter the framework of *shlichus*. A non-Jew will never be in the *parsha* of *shlichus* — Christopher can never make Shimon a *shaliach* in any context. Therefore, *zechiyah* on behalf of a non-Jew does not function. Shimon cannot be *zocheh* in the money for Christopher.

The conclusion of this entire discussion is that there is no formal legal mechanism — neither *shlichus* nor *zechiyah* — by which Reuven's direct handoff of money to Shimon, however it is framed, can be reconstituted as a loan from Christopher to Shimon. The only genuinely valid structure is the one that actually involves Christopher taking possession of the money and then separately lending it to Shimon. *Tosafot*'s underlying question — whether the direction of interest payments should determine the identity of the loan — is answered by demonstrating that the question of loan ownership must be determined at the level of who actually extended the credit, and the legal tools for reassigning that relationship are narrow and specific.

## Conversion & Ribbit

### Reconsolidation Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלָּוָה מָעוֹת מִן הַנׇּכְרִי בְּרִבִּית, וּזְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה, וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר. אִם קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר זְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְגוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית. וְאִם לְאַחַר שֶׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר זְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** In the case of **a Jew who borrowed money with interest from a gentile, and** the gentile lender **established** the interest **as a loan for** the Jewish borrower, i.e., he added the amount of interest to the principal and consolidated it into a single debt, **and** then the gentile **converted,** the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. **If** the gentile **established it as a loan for him before he converted, he may collect the principal** from the borrower **and he may** also **collect the interest.** Since it had already been consolidated into a single debt, it is as though he already collected the interest while he was still a gentile. **But if he established it as a loan for him after he converted, he may collect the principal but may not collect the interest,** as it still had the status of interest when he became a Jew.

The *Gemara* now introduces a new *Beraita* dealing with *ribbit* in the context of conversion. A Jew — Reuven — has lent money to a non-Jew, Christopher, with interest. Over time, the principal and interest together amount to, say, twelve hundred dollars. Reuven and Christopher now agree to consolidate: rather than maintain a loan of one thousand dollars with two hundred dollars of interest outstanding, they write a new *shtar* acknowledging a fresh loan of twelve hundred dollars. The interest is folded into the principal and the debt is reconstituted as a single sum. Christopher then converts to Judaism and becomes Chaim. The question: can Chaim now pay Reuven the full twelve hundred dollars, or does the two hundred dollars that was originally interest remain uncollectible?

### Before Conversion

If the loan was reconsolidated — the twelve hundred dollar *shtar* was written — *before* Christopher converted, then Chaim may pay Reuven the full twelve hundred dollars. At the moment the new *shtar* was written, Christopher was still a non-Jew, and the two hundred dollars was legitimately owed as interest in a Jew-to-non-Jew loan. The new *shtar* of twelve hundred dollars is simply a valid debt instrument, and when Chaim pays Reuven twelve hundred dollars, he is paying a loan — not interest. *Goveh et ha'keren ve'goveh et ha'ribbit*.

### After Conversion

If the reconsolidation occurred *after* Christopher converted — that is, at the moment of conversion he owed Reuven a thousand dollars of principal and two hundred dollars of interest, and only thereafter did they write a new *shtar* of twelve hundred dollars — then Chaim may only pay the principal. He may not pay the two hundred dollars. *Goveh et ha'keren ve'eino goveh et ha'ribbit*.

### Interest Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן נׇכְרִי שֶׁלָּוָה מָעוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל בְּרִבִּית וּזְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְגַּיֵּיר זְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְגוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית. אִם מִשֶּׁנִּתְגַּיֵּיר זְקָפָן עָלָיו בְּמִלְוֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly,** in the case of **a gentile who borrowed money with interest from a Jew and** the Jewish lender **established** the interest **as a loan for** the gentile borrower, **and** then the gentile borrower **converted, if he established it as a loan before he converted,** the Jew **may collect the principal and may** also **collect the interest. If he established it as a loan for** the gentile **after he converted, he may collect the principal but may not collect the interest,** as it still had the status of interest when he became a Jew.

This ruling has an interesting nuance worth examining. One might point out that the two hundred dollars of interest accrued while Christopher was still a non-Jew — not a single shekel of new interest was generated after his conversion to Chaim. Why, then, should the two hundred dollars be uncollectible? The answer lies in the timing of the reconsolidation. At the moment Chaim stood before Reuven as a Jew, the outstanding balance was framed as "a thousand dollars plus two hundred dollars of interest." It was at that moment — while he was Jewish — that the sum was characterized as interest. Repackaging it into a twelve hundred dollar loan does not undo that characterization. The fact that the interest accrued during his non-Jewish period is part of the *chiddush*: even though the interest never accrued as a Jew, since it was *framed* as interest owed while he was Jewish, it cannot be collected. The reconsolidation cannot launder the character of the obligation.

### Reverse Scenario

The *Gemara* then presents the parallel case in reverse: a non-Jew, Christopher, borrows money from Reuven with interest. The loan similarly reaches a point where a thousand dollars of principal and two hundred dollars of interest are outstanding, and the parties reconsolidate into a twelve hundred dollar *shtar*. Christopher then converts to Chaim. The same two possibilities exist: reconsolidation before conversion, or reconsolidation after conversion. And the same ruling applies symmetrically: if consolidated before conversion, the full twelve hundred is payable; if after, only the principal.

### Rabbi Yossi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: נׇכְרִי שֶׁלָּוָה מָעוֹת מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל בְּרִבִּית, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְגוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yosei says: If a gentile borrowed money with interest from a Jew** and converted, **whether** in **this** circumstance **or whether** in **that** circumstance, i.e., regardless of when the lender consolidated the interest and principal into a single debt, the Jewish lender **may collect the principal and he may** also **collect the interest.**

Rabbi Yossi dissents with respect to this second case — where the non-Jew was the *borrower* from the Jew. Rabbi Yossi holds that *bein kach u'vein kach goveh et ha'keren ve'goveh et ha'ribbit* — whether the reconsolidation occurred before or after conversion, Chaim always pays the full twelve hundred dollars. This applies regardless of when the new *shtar* was written.

### Rava's Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ בִּשְׁבִיל מְעוֹתָיו נִתְגַּיֵּיר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says** that **Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav Huna says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rava says: What is the reasoning** behind the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei?** What is the justification for collecting interest from a Jew? It is **in order that people will not say: This** individual **converted due to** concern for **his money.** People will suspect that he converted in order to avoid paying the interest.

Rava explains: *Amar Rava amar Rav Chisda amar Rav Huna: halacha ke'Rabbi Yossi*. We rule like Rabbi Yossi. What is Rabbi Yossi's reasoning? *Kedei she'lo yomru bishvil me'otav nitgayer zeh* — so that people should not say that Christopher converted to Judaism in order to avoid paying his debts. If converting to Judaism meant that a non-Jewish borrower could shed his interest obligations, the integrity of *geirut* would be compromised. People would allege that conversions are financially motivated — that Christopher became Chaim not out of sincere religious commitment but to avoid paying Reuven two hundred dollars. To prevent this perception, Rabbi Yossi rules that the non-Jewish borrower who converts must pay the full amount regardless of when the reconsolidation occurred.

One might ask: how is this technically permitted? After all, once Chaim is Jewish, paying interest to a Jew would seem to be *ribbit*. The answer may lie in the point noted earlier: the interest was never accrued as a Jew. No new interest was generated after the conversion. Since the two hundred dollars represents an obligation that arose entirely during Christopher's non-Jewish period, it may be that *me'ikar ha'din* there is no real *ribbit* problem with paying it — and the various rulings in this *sugya* are, at least in part, shaped by considerations of appearances and incentives rather than strict *issur*.

## Written Ribbit

### Document Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: שְׁטָר שֶׁכָּתוּב בּוֹ רִבִּית – קוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ, וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְלֹא אֶת הָרִבִּית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה אֶת הָרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught:** In the case of a promissory **note in which** the details of a loan with **interest were written, we penalize** the lender, **and** therefore **he may not collect the principal and may not collect the interest;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: He may collect the principal but he may not collect the interest.**

The *Gemara* turns to a *Beraita* dealing with a *shtar* that has *ribbit* explicitly written into it — a loan document that specifies both principal and an interest rate. Such a document concretizes the *ribbit* arrangement in written form, and the question is what legal effect that document has. Can it be used in court to collect anything at all?

### Rabbi Meir Position

Rabbi Meir rules: *konesin oto ve'eino goveh lo et ha'keren ve'lo et ha'ribbit*. The lender is penalized (*kenas*), and may collect neither the principal nor the interest. The document is entirely voided. Rabbi Meir's reasoning is that the *shtar* itself is the vehicle of an *aveirah* — the *ribbit* is embedded in the document — and therefore the entire document is torn up. Interestingly, this is not simply that the interest portion is unenforceable; rather, even using the *shtar* as evidence for the principal is forbidden. If the lender had independent witnesses who could testify that a loan was made, those witnesses could in principle be used to collect the principal — but the *shtar* itself, having been used to effect an *aveirah*, is entirely discarded as a legal instrument. This position has a certain compelling integrity: allowing a court to recognize a *ribbit* document for any purpose compromises the court's standing and provides only weak disincentive against the practice.

### Chachamim Position

The *Chachamim* disagree: *goveh et ha'keren ve'eino goveh et ha'ribbit*. The principal is collectible; the interest is not. The *shtar* may be used in court, but only to enforce the portion that represents a legitimate debt.

### Debate Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: קָנְסִינַן הֶתֵּירָא מִשּׁוּם אִיסּוּרָא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: לָא קָנְסִינַן הֶתֵּירָא מִשּׁוּם אִיסּוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do they disagree?** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Meir holds: We penalize** him with regard to that which is **permitted due to** that which is **prohibited, and the Rabbis hold: We do not penalize** him with regard to that which is **permitted due to** that which is **prohibited.**

The *Gemara* identifies the underlying dispute: *Rabbi Meir savar konesin heter mishum issur* — Rabbi Meir holds that we fine a person with respect to the permitted portion (the principal) on account of the forbidden portion (the interest). By lending with *ribbit* and concretizing it in a document, the lender forfeits even his right to collect the principal via that document. *Rabbanan savri lo konesin heter mishum issur* — the *Chachamim* hold we do not extend a penalty into the permitted domain. The principal is owed regardless of how the *shtar* was written, and we can separate the legitimate from the illegitimate.

### Predated Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: שְׁטָרֵי חוֹב הַמּוּקְדָּמִין – פְּסוּלִין, וְהַמְאוּחָרִין – כְּשֵׁרִין. מוּקְדָּמִין אַמַּאי פְּסוּלִין? נְהִי דְּלָא גְּבוֹ מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, נִיגְבּוֹ מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Shevi'it* 10:5): **Antedated promissory notes,** in which the date written in the document is earlier than the date the loan was actually transacted, **are not valid, but postdated** promissory notes **are valid.** The Gemara asks: **Why** are **antedated** documents **not valid? Granted, they cannot** be used to **collect from the first date,** the date written in them, because this could cause loss to people who purchased land from the borrower in the time between the date written on the promissory note and the time the loan was actually granted. The land they purchased would be subject to a lien when it fact it should not be. But at least **they should** be able to be used to **collect from the second date,** when the loan was actually granted.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from a ruling taught elsewhere — in Gittin, Rosh Hashanah, and related contexts — that predated documents (*shtarot hamukdamim*) are invalid, while postdated documents are kosher. The problem with a predated *shtar* is that it allows the lender to collect liens from property sold by the borrower between the false date and the actual date of the loan — property that was sold without any actual encumbrance at the time of sale. An innocent purchaser who checked the records and found no outstanding debts against the seller would find his property seized through the fraud of the predated *shtar*.

The *Gemara* asks: why not apply the *Chachamim*'s logic to the predated *shtar* as well? Even under the *Chachamim*, who do not penalize the permitted for the forbidden, one could argue: simply disallow the lender from collecting any liens prior to the true date of the loan, but permit collection from the valid date forward. In other words, split the document — enforce the later portion, void the earlier portion.

### Severability Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: בְּמַחְלוֹקֶת שְׁנוּיָה, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר הִיא. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן – גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִגְבֶּה מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says:** This mishna **is subject to dispute, and it is** taught in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who said that the lender is penalized with respect to that which is permitted due to that which is prohibited. Here too, since he wrote an incorrect date, the entire document is invalidated as a penalty. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: You** may **even say** that this *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** as here there is a rabbinic **decree** invalidating the document **lest he collect from the first date.** If the document is not invalidated, the lender will depend on it and will come to collect repayment according to the date written on it.

Reish Lakish suggests that the voiding of the entire predated *shtar* is in fact a Tannaic dispute and represents Rabbi Meir's position. Rabbi Yochanan disagrees and says: even the *Rabbanan* — who do not penalize the permitted for the forbidden in the case of the *ribbit* document — would void the entire predated *shtar*. The reason is a *gezeirah*: we fear that if a predated *shtar* is allowed to circulate in any form, the lender may come to use it to collect liens from the earlier, fraudulent date. The predated *shtar* does not reveal on its face that it was predated — it simply states "this loan was made on November 1st" and there is no indication within the document itself that this date is false. A court examining the document cannot tell the difference between a validly dated and fraudulently predated document. Therefore, permitting any use of such a document is too dangerous; the entire *shtar* must be voided.

The case of the *ribbit shtar* is fundamentally different. There, the document itself plainly states the interest terms. The forbidden portion and the permitted portion are clearly delineated within the *shtar* itself. A court can read the document and separate what is enforceable from what is not. That severability makes it possible, under the *Chachamim*'s view, to enforce the principal while ignoring the interest. The predated *shtar*, by contrast, is non-severable in a practical sense — there is no way to know which liens are valid and which are fraudulent without additional testimony, and the document itself would mislead. Hence the two cases diverge.

## Property Ruse Case

### Story Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּמַשְׁכֵּין לֵיהּ פַּרְדֵּיסָא לְחַבְרֵיהּ, אַכְלַהּ תְּלָת שְׁנֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מְזַבְּינַתְּ לַהּ נִיהֲלִי – מוּטָב, וְאִי לָא – כָּבֵישְׁנָא לַהּ לִשְׁטַר מַשְׁכַּנְתָּא וְאָמֵינָא: ״לְקוּחָה הִיא בְּיָדִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who mortgaged his orchard to another** as security for a loan, and the lender **consumed** the produce of the orchard for **three years.** At the end of this time the lender **said to** the borrower: **If you sell me the** orchard, that is **good. But if not, I will hide the document of the mortgage and I will say: It is purchased,** and that is why it is **in my possession,** meaning I will claim I purchased the field and lost the deed. Since the land has been in my possession for three years I do not have to bring any other proof, as the *halakha* is that after three years of use of a field there is a presumption of ownership for the one who uses it (see *Bava Batra* 29b).

The *Gemara* now presents a complex and colorful narrative (*ma'aseh*). A creditor has taken a vineyard as *mashkanta* — collateral — from a debtor. The arrangement is of the *mashkanta de-nachita* type: the creditor takes possession of the vineyard and effectively deducts something nominal from the loan while consuming the produce of the vineyard for the duration. After three years of eating the vineyard's grapes, the creditor approaches the debtor with a threatening proposition: either sell me your vineyard at my price, or I will suppress the evidence that I held the vineyard as *mashkanta* — I will claim instead that I purchased it from you three years ago, have been in possession for three full years, and thereby have a *chazakah* of ownership.

### Transfer to Minor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל קָם אַקְנְיַיהּ לִבְנוֹ קָטָן, וַהֲדַר זַבְּנַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** When the borrower saw that he could not protect himself against the ruse, he devised a scheme: **He went and transferred** ownership of the field **to his minor son** by means of a deed of gift, **and afterward sold** the field **to** the lender. Subsequently, he demanded that the sale be annulled, since when he sold the field, it was not his.

The debtor, facing this extortion, devises a countermove. Before the creditor can execute his scheme, the debtor transfers title to the vineyard to his minor son. He then — no longer being the owner — sells the vineyard to the creditor. The creditor receives a document of sale and money changes hands. The creditor is now in possession of the vineyard legitimately through purchase, but crucially, the sale comes from the debtor *after* the debtor has already transferred ownership to his son. So the sale is of property the debtor no longer owned.

### Ruse Discovered

When the ruse is discovered, the creditor's original leverage is entirely destroyed. His threat was to claim he purchased the vineyard three years ago. But now a document exists showing he purchased it just recently — from the debtor himself. The predated-purchase theory is impossible to sustain. The ruse has worked perfectly from the debtor's perspective: he has broken the creditor's leverage and sold him property he did not own, thereby recovering money (the purchase price) while undermining any claim of long-standing ownership.

### Liens Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְבִינֵי וַדַּאי לָא הָווּ זְבִינֵי. זוּזֵי כְּמִלְוֶה בִּשְׁטָר דָּמוּ וְגוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, אוֹ דִילְמָא כְּמִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה דָּמוּ וְאֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the *halakha*: **The sale was certainly not a sale,** since the field was not his to sell, but the question is: Are **the dinars** that the buyer paid **similar to a loan with** a promissory **note, and** therefore the buyer **can collect** the money he paid even **from liened property** that has been sold? **Or perhaps it is similar to a loan by oral** agreement, **and** therefore **he cannot collect** it **from liened property** that has been sold.

The creditor has now paid money for a vineyard he will not get to keep — the title was in the minor son, not the seller. The creditor wants his money back. The question becomes: does the *shtar mechirah* — the document of sale — which of course contained the standard clause of *achrayut* (warranty of title, entitling the buyer to reimbursement if the property is lost), give the creditor the power of liens? That is, can he collect not just from the debtor's current assets, but from property the debtor may have sold to third parties in the interim — treating the *shtar* as equivalent to a *shtar chov* with *meshubadim*? Or, since the *shtar* was written for a sale that was fundamentally invalid — the seller did not own the property — is it a meaningless document that creates no liens at all, leaving the creditor only with a claim for reimbursement from the debtor's unencumbered assets?

### Abaye's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: וְלָאו הַיְינוּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְגוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Is this** question **not** the same as that of **Rabbi Asi?** As **Rabbi Asi says:** In the case of a debtor who **admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order to collect the debt, **and he** can therefore use the document to **collect** the debt even **from liened property** that has been sold. In the present case as well, the seller admits that he received the money; therefore the document of sale should enable the buyer to collect his money from liened property.

Abaye draws a comparison to a ruling of Rabbi Assi: *modeh b'shtar shektavo eino tzarich l'kaymo ve'goveh minichsei meshubadei*. If David presents a *shtar* against Dov, and the *shtar* has not been externally validated by witnesses, but Dov himself acknowledges the *shtar* is genuine — Dov admits he owes the money — then David can collect from *meshubadim*, from property Dov sold to third parties, even though the validation came only from the borrower himself. This seems analogous: a *shtar* of limited standing can nonetheless create liens. By loose analogy, Abaye suggests the *shtar* of this bogus sale — even though the sale was invalid — might still function to create liens for reimbursement.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם נִיתַּן לְהִכָּתֵב. הָכָא לֹא נִיתַּן לְהִכָּתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: Are** these cases **comparable? There,** the matter detailed in the document **may be written,** as it is a substantive matter; the document attests to true events and it is therefore possible to use the promissory note to collect the debt. But **here,** the matter detailed in the document **may not be written,** as the entire sale was not genuine since it was done against the will of the seller. Consequently, this document is completely invalid and cannot be used to collect from liened property.

Rava rejects the comparison. *Mi dami?* There the *shtar* in Rabbi Assi's case was entirely legitimate as a debt instrument — a genuine loan was made, the *shtar* was properly written, it simply lacked external validation. Nobody questions that it *should* have been written; the only issue is whether Dov's own acknowledgment is sufficient validation. *Nitan likateiv* — it was proper to write such a *shtar*. Here, the *shtar mechirah* purports to sell property the seller did not own. A document of sale for property one does not own is *lo nitan likateiv* — it had no right to exist at all. It was a meaningless document from the moment of its writing. Such a document cannot generate liens.

### Shevach Karka'ot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: לְשֶׁבַח קַרְקָעוֹת כֵּיצַד? הֲרֵי שֶׁגָּזַל שָׂדֶה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ וּמְכָרָהּ לְאַחֵר וְהִשְׁבִּיחָהּ, וַהֲרֵי הִיא יוֹצְאָה מִתַּחַת יָדוֹ. כְּשֶׁהוּא גּוֹבֶה – גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַקֶּרֶן מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, וְשֶׁבַח גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But** how does one understand **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **What** is the case in which one appropriates property **for** the **enhancement of land?** It is a case where **one robbed another of a field and sold it to another** and that buyer **enhanced it, and it is appropriated** by the court **from his possession. When** the buyer **collects** payment from the robber, **he collects the principal,** i.e., the money he paid for the field itself, even **from liened property** that the robber had sold in the interim, **and he collects** the value of the **enhancement from** the robber's **unsold property.**

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from another *Beraita* dealing with *shevach karka'ot*. If someone steals a field, sells it to Gloria, and Gloria improves it — and then the original owner reclaims the field from Gloria — Gloria seeks reimbursement from the thief for both the purchase price and her improvements. The *Beraita* rules: Gloria can collect the principal purchase price from the thief's *meshubadim* — from property he sold to third parties — but she cannot collect the enhancement value (*shevach*) from those *meshubadim*; she can collect only from the thief's unencumbered (*benei chorin*) property. The reasoning for the distinction is that third parties could anticipate being subject to a lien for the known purchase price when they bought property from the thief, but they could not know how much Gloria would spend on improvements — that is an unspecified and unforeseeable amount, and it would be unfair to extend the lien that far.

The relevance to our case: Gloria collected the purchase price from *meshubadim* based on a *shtar mechirah* of a stolen field — a sale of property the thief did not legitimately own — and yet that *shtar* generated liens. This seems to contradict Rava's position that a *shtar* written for a sale of property one does not own has no legal force. If the *shtar* in Gloria's case generates liens, why can't the *shtar* in our case do the same?

### Legitimate vs. Ruse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵיב מָרִימָר וְקָאָמַר לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְמָרִימָר: אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִגְבֶּה מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, נֵימָא: לֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב! אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא. הָתָם נְהִי דְּלֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן, אֲבָל נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב מִזְּמַן שֵׁנִי. הָכָא לֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Mareimar sat and stated this *halakha*. Ravina said to Mareimar: But** if Rava's answer is accepted, then with regard to **that which Rabbi Yoḥanan said** concerning an antedated loan document, that there is a rabbinic **decree** invalidating the document **lest he collect from the first date, let us say** that there is a better rationale, as Rava stated: The antedated document is invalid, as it **may not be written.** Mareimar **said to him: How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the antedated document, **granted, it may not be written from the first date, but it may be written from the second date. Here, it may not be written at all.**

Mareimar presents Rava's response, which Ravina then engages with and extends. The key distinction between Gloria's case and our case is *intent*. When the thief sold the field to Gloria, even if he knew or suspected the title might be challenged, he genuinely *wanted* the sale to be valid and legitimate. He did not want Gloria to go around complaining that he had defrauded her. Either because he did not want to be called a *gazlan*, or because he wanted to uphold his reputation and honor his commitments, he had a genuine interest in ensuring that Gloria's purchase was protected and that she would be reimbursed if anything went wrong. His *intent* behind the sale, and behind the *achrayut* clause, was sincere — he wanted the *shtar* to have legal force and for Gloria to have genuine protection. *Mafiyes lei l'marei* — he would even try to pacify the original owner, to buy off the title claim so Gloria could keep the property. Therefore, even though the *shtar* was technically for property he could not transfer, it carries genuine legal weight because the seller *meant* for it to be valid.

In our case, the situation is categorically different. The debtor transferred his vineyard to his minor son and then "sold" the vineyard to the creditor as a deliberate *ruse* — not because he wanted the sale to be valid, but precisely because he wanted it to be exposed as invalid. His entire purpose was to undermine the creditor's leverage, not to create a genuine sale. *Lav revucha minei ka michavein* — he was not intending to benefit the creditor; he was trying to escape the creditor's extortion. The *shtar* was not written with sincere intent to convey rights. It was a tactical maneuver in a dispute. Therefore the *shtar mechirah* in this case is not merely technically deficient — it is a document without *kavana*, without genuine transactional intent — and it creates no liens whatsoever.

The principle that emerges is that the validity of a document of sale for generating liens does not depend solely on whether the seller technically owned the property. It also depends on whether the seller *intended* for the document to carry legal force. A sale of stolen property, made by someone who genuinely wished to honor his obligations to the buyer, can create liens. A sale designed from the outset as a ruse — made precisely because the seller wanted it to be void — cannot.
