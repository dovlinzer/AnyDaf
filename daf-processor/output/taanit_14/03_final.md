# Taanit 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Final Seven Fasts

### Shift to Late Fasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה אֵלּוּ יְתֵירוֹת עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת, אֶלָּא שֶׁבְּאֵלּוּ מַתְרִיעִין וְנוֹעֲלִין אֶת הַחֲנוּיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **How are these** seven fast days **more** stringent **than the first ones? Rather,** the difference is **that on these** days, in addition to all the earlier stringencies, **they sound the alarm and they lock the stores.**

The Gemara on 14a opens with the question *ma elu yeterot al harishonot* — in what ways are the final seven fasts weightier than the earlier ones? The preceding discussion has already established a gradation of seriousness across the three sets of fasts. In the first three, one fasts only during the day. In the middle set, the fast begins the night before and additional *inuyim* are observed. By the time we reach the final seven, the stores are closed, the fast begins from the previous night, and most significantly, the nature of the *tefillot* themselves is transformed. The *mishnah* states: *ela shebe'elu matri'in veno'alin et hachanutot* — the distinguishing features are *matri'in* and the closing of the stores. The community shuts down entirely, with everyone focusing on prayer. It is these two features — and particularly the term *matri'in* — that the Gemara now proceeds to examine.

### Matri'in Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי מַתְרִיעִין? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת, וְרַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב אָמַר: בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **With what do they sound the alarm? Rav Yehuda said: With *shofarot*. And Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat, said in the name of Rav: With** the ***Aneinu*** prayer.

The Gemara asks: *bema matri'in* — what exactly is the nature of this *matri'in*? Rav Yehuda says *beshofarot* — it refers to shofar blasts. This reading is intuitive, since the word *matri'in* derives from *teruah*, which we naturally associate with the shofar. Rav Yehuda the son of Rav Shmuel bar Shilat, however, citing Rav, says *be'anenu* — the *matri'in* refers to the special prayer of *anenu*, the crying out to God, rather than to any instrumental blast. On this reading, *teruah* is being understood not as a shofar sound but as a form of wailing or calling out through prayer.

### Rashi on Shofar

Rashi's commentary illuminates both positions. On the opinion of *be'anenu*, Rashi explains that the community would cry out *bekol ram* — loudly — saying *anenu avinu anenu*, *anenu elohei Avraham anenu*, *anenu elohei Yitzchak anenu*, and so forth. This is, as Rashi notes, the very text one recognizes from the end of *selichot* — *hakol kefi she'omerim besof haselichot*. It is worth pausing here: this *anenu* liturgy is among the earliest organized prayers we have outside of the *shemoneh esrei* itself, and its structure centers on invoking God's past responsiveness to the patriarchs. Rashi also clarifies that this is not the standard *anenu* blessing said on fast days as a separate *berachah* within the *amidah*, which is said even on the first three fasts and even by an individual. Rather, this refers to the elaborated *anenu* structure tied to the additional six *berachot* of the final seven fasts. It may be, as Rashi suggests, that they also said it outside the *shemoneh esrei* entirely.

On the shofar position, Rashi notes that the word *hatra'ah* is the language of *teruah*, a blast preceded and followed by a *tekiah*, as we know from tractate Rosh Hashanah. The scriptural basis is the verse *utkatem bachatzotzrot al hatzar hatzorer etchem* — you shall sound the trumpets when an enemy oppresses you. Rashi draws two ideas together: the shofar is a mode of calling out to God and of being remembered before Him, and it is simultaneously a means of awakening the community to *teshuvah*, causing hearts to be subdued by its sound and stirring people away from their sins. These are recognizably the same two functions developed in the context of Rosh Hashanah — one directed heavenward, one directed inward.

### Shofar + Anenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּן, מַאן דְּאָמַר בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״ — לָא אָמַר בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת — לָא אָמַר בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מִשֶּׁבַע תַּעֲנִיּוֹת עַל הַצִּבּוּר, שֶׁבָּהֶן שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה הַתְרָעוֹת. וְסִימָן לַדָּבָר: יְרִיחוֹ. וִירִיחוֹ שׁוֹפָרוֹת הֲוָה, וּתְיוּבְתָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the dispute: **It** might **enter our mind** to say that **the one who said** that the community sounds the alarm **by** reciting ***Aneinu*,** i.e., Rav, **did not say** that they cry out **with *shofarot*, and** likewise **the one who said** that they do cry out **with *shofarot*,** Rav Yehuda, **did not say** that they sound the alarm **by** reciting ***Aneinu*. But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The court **does not** decree **fewer than seven fasts on the community, which include eighteen** acts of **sounding the alarm. And a mnemonic for** this **matter is Jericho. And** as **there were** many episodes of sounding the ***shofarot* in Jericho,** this is **a conclusive refutation of the one who said** that according to the opinion of Rav they sound the alarm only **by** reciting ***Aneinu*.**

The Gemara initially assumes the two opinions are mutually exclusive — that whoever says *matri'in* means *anenu* does not require shofar blasts, and vice versa. A *baraita* quickly challenges this: *ein pochtim misheva taniyot al hatzibbur shebahen yud chet hatra'ot* — one may not have fewer than seven communal fasts, and within them no fewer than eighteen *hatra'ot*. The *siman* for remembering this is *Yericho*. Why eighteen? To understand this, one must look ahead to the *mishnah* later in the tractate, where the additional six *berachot* of the final seven fasts are enumerated. Each *berachah* is structured around a *mi she'anah* formula — *mi she'anah Avraham avinu behar hamoriyah hu ya'aneh etchem* — and ends with its own *chatimah*. If there are six such *berachot*, and each is accompanied by a *tekiah teruah tekiah* — three blasts per *berachah* — the total comes to eighteen *hatra'ot*. This *baraita* clearly presupposes shofar blasts as integral to the seven fasts, constituting a refutation of any view that would exclude them entirely.

### Matri'in Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּקָרֵי לַהּ הַתְרָעָה. כִּי פְּלִיגִי בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״. מָר סָבַר: קָרֵי לַהּ הַתְרָעָה, וּמָר סָבַר: לָא קָרֵי לַהּ הַתְרָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara explains that the dispute must be understood differently: **With regard to *shofarot*, everyone,** i.e., Rav and Rav Yehuda, **agrees** that the mishna **calls this: Sounding the alarm. When they disagree,** it is **with regard to** the ***Aneinu*** prayer. One **Sage,** Rav, **holds** that **this** too **is called sounding the alarm, and** one **Sage,** Rav Yehuda, **holds** that reciting *Aneinu* **is not called sounding the alarm.**

The Gemara therefore revises its understanding of the dispute: *beshofar kulei alma lo* — everyone agrees that shofar blasts are performed and that the term *hatra'ah* refers to them. The disagreement is only about *anenu*: does the word *matri'in* in the *mishnah* encompass *anenu* as well, or does the *mishnah*'s emphasis on *matri'in* as the distinguishing feature of the final seven refer exclusively to the shofar blasts?

With this reframing, the positions are clarified. According to the view that *matri'in* means *be'anenu*, the *mishnah* is highlighting *anenu* as the distinctive feature — but of course shofar blasts are performed as well, since everyone agrees on that. According to the view that *matri'in* means *beshofarot*, the *mishnah* highlights the shofar as the distinctive feature, and whether *anenu* is also added remains unaddressed by the word *matri'in* itself.

There is a deeper question worth raising here: is this dispute purely terminological — what the word *matri'in* technically denotes — or does it reflect a genuine disagreement about what the community actually does? Given that the subsequent *mishnah* clearly describes both the shofar blasts and the *anenu* structure in the context of the final seven fasts, it is more plausible that both were always practiced. The real debate is which practice the *mishnah* is calling attention to as the defining difference from the earlier fasts. Is it the instrumental blast that signals a new level of urgency, or is it the transformed mode of prayer? This is ultimately a question about what is considered the more powerful and fundamental way of calling out to God — the ritual object of the shofar or the emotional register of the *anenu* cry.

### Tzoa'kin vs Matri'in

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: וּשְׁאָר כׇּל מִינֵי פּוּרְעָנוּיוֹת הַמִּתְרַגְּשׁוֹת, כְּגוֹן: חִיכּוּךְ, חָגָב, זְבוּב וְצִירְעָה וְיַתּוּשִׁין, וְשִׁילּוּחַ נְחָשִׁים וְעַקְרַבִּים — לֹא הָיוּ מַתְרִיעִין אֶלָּא צוֹעֲקִין. מִדִּצְעָקָה בַּפֶּה, הַתְרָעָה בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against this conclusion. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **And** with regard to **all other types of calamities** than drought **that break out, for example scabs,** plagues of **locusts, flies, or hornets, or mosquitoes, or infestations of snakes or scorpions, they would not sound the alarm, but they** would **cry out. From** the fact that **crying out** is, according to all opinions, a prayer recited **with** one's **mouth,** it follows that **sounding an alarm** must be **with *shofarot*.** This *baraita* indicates that sounding the alarm with *shofarot* is the response to a serious situation, whereas the *Aneinu* prayer is recited on less worrisome occasions.

A *baraita* is cited that sharpens the distinction further: *u'vesh'ar kol minei pur'aniyot hamitragshot* — for other types of anticipated calamities, such as infestations of locusts, flies, wasps, mosquitoes, or the sending of snakes and scorpions, *lo hayu matri'in* — they would not perform *matri'in*. Instead, *ela tzo'akin* — they would only cry out verbally. The Gemara draws the logical conclusion: since *tzo'akin* here unambiguously means verbal crying out, the term *matri'in* is being set in explicit contrast to it. *Hatra'ah beshofar* — the *hatra'ah* referred to by *matri'in* can only mean shofar blasts, not verbal outcry. This seems to resolve the matter decisively in favor of the shofar interpretation.

### Matri'in Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: עַל אֵלּוּ מַתְרִיעִין בְּשַׁבָּת, עַל עִיר שֶׁהִקִּיפוּהָ גַּיִיס אוֹ נָהָר, וְעַל סְפִינָה הַמְטוֹרֶפֶת בַּיָּם. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אֹמֵר: לְעֶזְרָה, אֲבָל לֹא לִצְעָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as we learned** in a mishna: **For the following** calamities **they sound the alarm** even **on Shabbat: For a city that is surrounded by** an enemy **army or** in danger of being flooded by **a river, or for a ship tossed about at sea. Rabbi Yosei said:** An alarm may be sounded on Shabbat **to** summon **help, but** it may **not** be sounded **for crying out** to God.

The Gemara responds, however, that this is a dispute among *tanna'im*. A second *baraita* records: *al elu matri'in beshabbat* — for the following situations one may perform *matri'in* even on Shabbat: a city surrounded by troops, a river threatening to overflow its banks, or a ship endangered at sea. Rabbi Yossi qualifies: *l'ezra* — one may sound the alarm to summon help, *aval lo l'tze'akah* — but not as an act of prayer addressed to God. This statement is striking in its this-worldly practicality: when a city is besieged, the more urgent priority is mobilizing human assistance, not divine petition. One may sound whatever signal summons people to help.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִילֵימָא בְּשׁוֹפָרוֹת — שׁוֹפָרוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת מִי שְׁרֵי? אֶלָּא לָאו, בַּ״עֲנֵנוּ״, וְקָרֵי לַהּ הַתְרָעָה, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies this case. **With what** do they sound the alarm? **If we say with *shofarot*, is** the sounding of ***shofarot* permitted on Shabbat?** Even when Rosh HaShana occurs on Shabbat, one must refrain from sounding the *shofar* on that day. **Rather, is it not** the case that this is referring to the recitation of the ***Aneinu*** prayer, **and** yet the mishna **calls this** recitation: **Sounding the alarm. Conclude from** this that there is a *tanna* who maintains that sounding of the alarm is in fact performed by prayer, as claimed by Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat.

The Gemara presses: when Rabbi Yossi speaks of *matri'in* for help, what is being sounded? *Ilema beshofaro* — if it is a shofar, *shofaro beshabbat mi sharei* — is a shofar even permitted on Shabbat? The Gemara is not suggesting that pikuach nefesh would fail to override the prohibition; rather, the earlier position contemplated sounding the alarm as a form of *tefillah*, and we do not blow the shofar on Shabbat even for prayer — which is precisely why we do not blow on Rosh Hashanah when it falls on Shabbat. The conclusion is therefore: *ela lav be'anenu* — clearly what is being referred to here is *anenu*, verbal calling out, and the *baraita* uses the term *matri'in* to describe it. *Ve'kari lei hatra'ah* — and it uses the language of *hatra'ah*. This proves that for some *tanna'im*, *matri'in* does include verbal calling out, not only shofar blasts.

### Matri'in Resolution

The upshot is that *matri'in* as a technical term definitively refers to shofar blasts — this is what the *baraita* about *hatra'ot* and the numerical calculation of eighteen establish beyond dispute. The debate between *tanna'im* is only about whether the word can *also* encompass the *anenu* cry. In practice, both are performed during the final seven fasts: shofar blasts accompany the six additional *berachot*, each *berachah* structured around the *mi she'anah* formula, and the *anenu* prayer gives expressive content to each segment. The *mishnah*'s use of the word *matri'in* emphasizes the shofar dimension, though the *anenu* dimension is simultaneously present. The question that lingers is fundamentally one of emphasis and theology: is the essential mode of urgent supplication an instrumental one, calling upon the power of the shofar to reach God and stir the heart, or is it a personal one, the raw crying out of the human voice?

## Post-13 Cessation

### Rabbi Yehuda Nesia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁנֵי דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה הֲוָה צַעֲרָא, גְּזַר תְּלָת עַשְׂרֵה תַּעֲנִיּוֹת וְלָא אִיעֲנִי. סְבַר לְמִיגְזַר טְפֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ אֵין מַטְרִיחִין אֶת הַצִּבּוּר יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **During the years of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia there was a trouble** that afflicted the community. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia **decreed thirteen fasts, but he was not answered. He considered decreeing more** fasts until they would be answered. **Rabbi Ami said to him** that **they said: One does not trouble the community excessively,** and therefore you should not impose more than thirteen fasts.

In the years of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia — grandson of Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi, and thus an Amora — there was some form of communal distress. He decreed the full complement of thirteen fasts, *velo i'anu* — and they were not answered. The *mishnah* itself describes what happens in this situation: the community enters a posture of *aveilut*. Rabbi Yehuda Nesia, however, *savar lemigzar tefei* — contemplated decreeing additional fasts beyond the thirteen. Rabbi Ami objected: *harei amru ein matrihin al hatzibbur yoter midai* — the stated principle is that one does not burden the community beyond what is reasonable, and thirteen fasts is the prescribed limit.

This exchange opens a deeper question about the rationale for stopping at thirteen. Is the limit one of communal burden — at a certain point, enough is enough and life must go on — or is it specific to rain, because after a certain point in the season even answered prayer would come too late? The answer matters considerably: if the limit is about communal burden, it is universal and applies to all types of distress. If it is about the futility of late rain, it applies only to drought-related fasts and other calamities would require fasting indefinitely until relief arrives.

### Rain-Specific Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: רַבִּי אַמֵּי דַּעֲבַד — לְגַרְמֵיהּ הוּא דַּעֲבַד. אֶלָּא הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִגְשָׁמִים, אֲבָל לִשְׁאָר מִינֵי פוּרְעָנוּיוֹת — מִתְעַנִּין וְהוֹלְכִין עַד שֶׁיֵּעָנוּ מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, said: When Rabbi Ami acted** and issued this ruling, **he did** so **on his own** authority, as it went against the majority opinion. **Rather, Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said as follows: They taught only** that the community observes a maximum of thirteen fasts when they are praying **for rain. However,** with regard **to other types of calamities, they continue to fast until they are answered from Heaven.**

Rav Abba the son of Rav Hiyya bar Abba responds that Rabbi Ami's ruling about communal burden, while not incorrect, reflects only his own view — *Rabbi Ami de'avad legarmeh hu de'avad*. The real teaching, as stated by Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, is: *lo shanu ela ligshamim* — the limit of thirteen fasts was said only with respect to rain. *Aval lish'ar minei pur'aniyot*, for all other forms of calamity, *mit'anim v'holchim ad she'ya'anu min hashamayim* — one fasts and continues until Heaven responds. There is no ceiling. One never despairs of eventually being answered.

There is something deeply important in this formulation. The end of the *mishnah* describing the post-thirteen state — the community sitting as those rejected by God, wrapped in mourning — conveys an atmosphere of near-despair, a severed relationship. Rabbi Yochanan's teaching restores that relationship. The reason one stops praying for rain after thirteen is not theological resignation; it is simply that rain after a certain point in the season is no longer beneficial. It is analogous to praying for a person's recovery after he has already died — one stops not because God cannot act, but because the specific request is no longer applicable. For every other calamity, however, the distress is ongoing, the request remains relevant, and hope is never abandoned. One continues indefinitely until relief comes.

### Tannaitic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַמֵּי! אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי אַמֵּי: תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין גּוֹזְרִין יוֹתֵר מִשְּׁלֹשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה תַּעֲנִיּוֹת עַל הַצִּבּוּר, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין מַטְרִיחִין אֶת הַצִּבּוּר יוֹתֵר מִדַּאי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא מִן הַשֵּׁם הוּא זֶה, אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּצָא זְמַנָּהּ שֶׁל רְבִיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that this** *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabbi Ami.** The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ami** could have **said to you** that this **is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One does not decree more than thirteen fasts on the community, as one does not trouble the community excessively.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: This** *halakha* **is not for** that **reason.** Rather, it is **due to** the fact that after thirteen fasts the **time of the rainfall has** already **passed,** and there is no reason to fast for rain after the rainy season has ended.

A *baraita* confirms that this very dispute has *tanna'itic* roots. Rabbi holds: *ein gozrim yoter mishlosh esrei ta'aniyot al hatzibbur lefi she'ein matrihin et hatzibbur yoter midai* — the reason for stopping at thirteen is the principle of not overburdening the community, and this principle applies universally. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel disagrees: *lo min hashem hu zeh* — that is not the reason at all. Rather, *mipnei sheyatzah zemana shel revi'ah* — it is because the season for beneficial rainfall has passed. The dispute of Rav Nachman and Rabbi Yochanan among the Amoraim thus maps precisely onto the dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel among the *tanna'im*. Even Rabbi's reasoning — that we stop to avoid burdening the community — is considerably more humane and relationally intact than the stark language of *benei adam hanizufim lamakom*. To say "reality must eventually reassert itself" is a far cry from "God has rejected you."

## Regional Rain Requests

### Nineveh Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי נִינְוֵה לְרַבִּי: כְּגוֹן אֲנַן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בִּתְקוּפַת תַּמּוּז בָּעֵינַן מִטְרָא, הֵיכִי נַעֲבֵיד? כִּיחִידִים דָּמֵינַן אוֹ כְּרַבִּים דָּמֵינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a story on a similar topic: **The inhabitants of Nineveh sent** a question **to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: People **such as us, who require rain even during the season of Tammuz,** and who live in areas where rain falls all year round, **what** should we **do** when there is a drought during the summer? Are we **likened to individuals or** are we **likened to a community?**

The Gemara now addresses a question related to the timing of prayer for rain in unusual geographic circumstances. The people of Nineveh — a striking choice of locale, given Nineveh's forthcoming role elsewhere in the tractate as a paradigm of *teshuvah* — sent a question to Rabbi: *kegon anan d'afilu b'tekufat Tammuz ba'inan mitra* — we are a community that needs rain even in the summer months. Normally the communal request for rain ceases at Pesach time. But our region regularly requires rainfall well beyond that point. *Heichi na'avid* — what should we do? *K'yechidim daminan o k'rabbim daminan* — should we be treated as individuals or as the community?

### Shomeah Tefillah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַח לְהוּ: כִּיחִידִים דָּמֵיתוּ וּבְ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **He sent** his answer **to them: You are likened to individuals and** therefore you pray for rain **in** the blessing: **Who listens to prayer.**

The Gemara's interpretation of this question is revealing. It does not read the question as being about whether to fast — whether to treat this as a *ta'anit yachid* or a *ta'anit tzibbur*. The question is not framed in crisis terms at all: the people of Nineveh are simply asking about the regular structure of their prayer throughout the year. *Kegon anan d'afilu b'tekufat Tammuz* — as a matter of regular course, we need rain even then. The question is therefore one of liturgical placement: should we say *v'sein tal umatar l'vracha* as individuals would — in *shomeah tefillah*, the catch-all blessing that hears individual petitions — or should we treat ourselves as the community does with the rain prayer incorporated directly into *birkat hashanim*, the ninth blessing?

Rabbi responds: *ki'yechidim damitu* — you are to be treated as individuals, *uv'shomeah tefillah* — and you say it in *shomeah tefillah*. The underlying principle is that the calendar of communal liturgy — including when *v'sein tal umatar* is inserted into and removed from *birkat hashanim* — is fixed for all of *Klal Yisrael*. Regional variations exist, as is well known: *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel* do not share the same starting date for *v'sein tal umatar*. But once the standard cessation date arrives at Pesach, that applies universally. A community whose rainfall needs extend beyond that date has a legitimate need, but addresses it through the individual's recourse of *shomeah tefillah*, not by altering the communal liturgical calendar.

### Standard Calendar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: אֵימָתַי — בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַשָּׁנִים כְּתִיקְנָן וְיִשְׂרָאֵל שְׁרוּיִן עַל אַדְמָתָן. אֲבָל בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה — הַכֹּל לְפִי הַשָּׁנִים, הַכֹּל לְפִי הַמְּקוֹמוֹת, הַכֹּל לְפִי הַזְּמַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַתְנִיתָא רָמֵית עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי?! רַבִּי תַּנָּא הוּא וּפָלֵיג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said: When** do the *halakhot* concerning the times during which the prayer for rain is recited apply? **When the years,** i.e., the climate, **are as they ought to be and the Jewish people are living in their land. However, nowadays,** when the Jewish people are dispersed around the world, and the climate is not always as it ought to be, **all is in accordance with the year,** i.e., the local climate, **all is in accordance with the place** in question, and **all is in accordance with** the particular **time,** and therefore one prays for rain in the blessing of the years, as necessary for the local climate. **He said to him: You raise a contradiction from a *baraita* against Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi? **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi himself **is a *tanna*, and** consequently has the authority **to dispute** the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.

The Gemara raises a challenge from a *baraita*: Rabbi Yehuda is quoted as saying that the standard schedule applies *bizman shehashanim ketiknan v'Yisrael sheruyim al admatam* — when the years are normal and Israel dwells on its own land. But *bizman hazeh* — in the present era — the rule shifts: *hakol lefi hashanim hakol lefi hamkomos v'hakol lefi hazman* — everything follows the specific conditions of that year, that place, and that time. This would seem to say that the liturgical calendar for *v'sein tal umatar* is not fixed at all, but fluctuates community by community and year by year based on actual rainfall needs — a remarkable position. The Gemara deflects this challenge: *Rabbi Tanna hu u'falig* — Rabbi is himself a *Tanna* and may simply disagree with this *baraita*.

### Amoraic Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: בְּבִרְכַּת הַשָּׁנִים. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״. וְהִלְכְתָא: בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic conclusion **was** reached **about** this matter? **Rav Naḥman said:** One prays for rain **in the blessing of the years,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. **Rav Sheshet said:** One prays **in** the blessing: **Who listens to prayer,** as stated by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. The Gemara concludes: **And** the ***halakha*** is that if rain is required when it is not the rainy season in Eretz Yisrael, one prays for rain **in** the blessing: **Who listens to prayer.**

Among the Amoraim, the matter is debated: Rav Nachman holds *b'birkat hashanim* — a community with unusual rain needs may continue saying *v'sein tal umatar* within *birkat hashanim*, while Rav Sheshet holds *b'shomeah tefillah* — it must be said in *shomeah tefillah*. The Gemara rules: *v'hilcheta b'shomeah tefillah*. Even if a community's need for rain persists past Pesach, the universal cessation of *v'sein tal umatar* in *birkat hashanim* at Pesach time holds for everyone. Late rainfall needs are addressed through *shomeah tefillah*.

The Rosh encountered precisely this issue when he relocated to Toledo and became its rabbinic authority. The communities there not only needed rain regularly after Pesach — they were actually holding communal fasts for lack of rain — yet were not saying *v'sein tal umatar l'vracha*. The Rosh argued vigorously that the case of Nineveh involves a single city, while an entire country the size of Spain represents a different category entirely. Just as the calendar differs between *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel*, so too Spain should have its own calendar for *v'sein tal umatar*. He writes that *tzaveach k'kruchiya v'lo ishtakach bi* — he cried out like a crane and was not heeded. The halachic consensus rejected his position. Even an entire country with consistently late rainfall needs does not shift the placement of *v'sein tal umatar* from *shomeah tefillah* back into *birkat hashanim* — though if one accidentally said it in *birkat hashanim* in such circumstances, one need not repeat the *shemoneh esrei*.

## Final Seven Fasts…

### Store Closures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּשֵּׁנִי מַטִּין עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה וּבַחֲמִישִׁי כׇּל הַיּוֹם מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ, הֵיכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּשֵּׁנִי מַטִּין עִם חֲשֵׁיכָה, וּבַחֲמִישִׁי כׇּל הַיּוֹם — מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת, אוֹ דִילְמָא: בַּשֵּׁנִי מַטִּין, וּבַחֲמִישִׁי פּוֹתְחִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **On Monday** they open the stores **a little at nightfall, and on Thursday** they are permitted to open the stores **all day, in deference to Shabbat. A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **How is** this **taught,** i.e., what is the meaning of this ruling? Does it mean that **on Monday** the storeowners open their doors **a little at nightfall, and on Thursday** they likewise open their doors just a little, but do so **all day, in deference to Shabbat? Or perhaps,** the mishna means that **on Monday** they open their doors **a little,** but all day, **and on Thursday they open** their doors wide **the entire day?**

The *mishnah* states: *b'sheni matin min hachasheicha u'vachamishi kol hayom mipnei kvod Shabbat* — on Mondays the stores are partially opened toward evening, and on Thursdays they are open all day out of honor for Shabbat. The Gemara raises an *ibaya*: does the verb *matin* — meaning to incline or make available — apply to both Monday and Thursday, with the only difference being the duration? Or does Thursday represent a fundamentally different status, where the stores are *potchin* — fully open for normal business — while only Monday maintains the partial-availability posture?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: בַּשֵּׁנִי מַטִּין עַד הָעֶרֶב, וּבַחֲמִישִׁי פּוֹתְחִין כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ מִפְּנֵי כְּבוֹד הַשַּׁבָּת. הָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנֵי פְתָחִים — פּוֹתֵחַ אֶחָד וְנוֹעֵל אֶחָד. הָיָה לוֹ אִצְטְבָא כְּנֶגֶד פִּתְחוֹ — פּוֹתֵחַ כְּדַרְכּוֹ וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear** a resolution of this dilemma, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **On Monday** they open their doors **a little until the evening, and on Thursday they open** them **the entire day, in deference to Shabbat.** If one's shop **had two entrances,** he **opens one and locks one,** thereby demonstrating that his store is not open in the normal manner. If **he had a platform opposite his entrance** which conceals the door to his store, **he may open in his** usual **manner without concern,** as it is prohibited to open one's store not due to work, but only so that it not appear as though people are eating and drinking on this day.

A *baraita* resolves this: *b'sheni matim ad ha'erev* — on Mondays they operate in that limited, inclined manner until evening, *u'vachamishi potchin kol hayom kulo* — and on Thursdays they are fully open all day, *mipnei kvod Shabbat*. Thursdays are therefore qualitatively different: the store closure does not apply at all on Thursdays. Two refinements follow. If a storekeeper has two entrances, he opens one and locks the other — even if fully open for business, some visible expression of the fast's atmosphere is maintained. If, however, there is a booth or stall in front of the entrance, *poteach k'darko v'eino choshesh* — he may open in his normal manner without concern. Rashi reads this extension broadly: *afilu b'sheni* — even on Monday, if a booth stands before the entrance and the open doors are not fully exposed to the street, the store may be fully opened. This functions as a significant practical accommodation. The principle underlying the store closures is that the entire community must visibly and collectively shut down during the final seven fasts; the accommodations are calibrated to allow life to continue where the communal signal is already sufficiently attenuated.

## Avelut Practices

### Avelut Restrictions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָבְרוּ אֵלּוּ וְלֹא נַעֲנוּ — מְמַעֲטִין בְּמַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן, בְּבִנְיָן וּבִנְטִיעָה. תָּנָא: בְּבִנְיָן — בִּנְיָן שֶׁל שִׂמְחָה. נְטִיעָה — נְטִיעָה שֶׁל שִׂמְחָה. אֵי זֶהוּ בִּנְיָן שֶׁל שִׂמְחָה — זֶה הַבּוֹנֶה בֵּית חַתְנוּת לִבְנוֹ. אִי זוֹ הִיא נְטִיעָה שֶׁל שִׂמְחָה — זֶה הַנּוֹטֵעַ אַבְּווֹרַנְקֵי שֶׁל מְלָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **If these** fasts have passed **and they have not been answered, they decrease** their engagement **in** business **negotiations** and **in building and planting.** It was **taught** in the *Tosefta* (*Megilla* 5:2): **Building** means **joyful building,** not building in general. Likewise, **planting** means **joyful planting,** not all planting. The *Tosefta* elaborates: **What is joyful building? This** is referring to **one who builds a wedding chamber for his son.** It was customary upon the marriage of a son to build him a small house where the marriage feast was held and where the newlywed couple would live for a certain period of time. **What is joyful planting? This** is referring to **one who** plants **a splendid, royal garden** that does not serve practical purposes, but is only for ornamentation.

After the thirteen fasts have passed without response, the community enters a period characterized not by continued fasting but by behaviors expressive of *aveilut*. The *mishnah* states: *mema'atim b'masa umatan u've'vinyan u've'neti'ah* — one curtails commerce, construction, and planting. A *tanna* clarifies that these limitations are not absolute. *Vinyan shel simcha* — only a construction of joyous character is prohibited. Specifically: *zeh haboneh beit chatnut livno* — one who builds a wedding house for his son, whether that means a home for the newlywed couple or a special wedding canopy structure. Ordinary construction for practical needs — repairing a leaking roof, for example — is not included. Similarly, *neti'ah shel simcha* — joyful planting is what is forbidden. *Zeh hanotea avzarnaki shel melachim* — this refers to the kind of decorative royal orchard or pleasure garden planted for aesthetic enjoyment rather than sustenance. Essential agricultural work is not prohibited. The limitation is thus defined quite narrowly: what is curtailed is the category of activity undertaken for pleasure, celebration, and expansive living — precisely the activities incompatible with a community in mourning.

### She'ilat Shalom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חֲבֵרִים, אֵין שְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם בֵּינֵיהֶם. עַמֵּי הָאָרֶץ שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲלִין — מַחְזִירִין לָהֶם בְּשָׂפָה רָפָה, וּבְכוֹבֶד רֹאשׁ. וְהֵן מִתְעַטְּפִין וְיוֹשְׁבִין כַּאֲבֵלִים וְכִמְנוּדִּין, כִּבְנֵי אָדָם הַנְּזוּפִין לַמָּקוֹם, עַד שֶׁיְּרַחֲמוּ עֲלֵיהֶם מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § **And** the mishna further taught that they decrease **greetings** between one another. **The Sages taught: *Ḥaverim*,** members of a group dedicated to the precise observance of mitzvot, **do not extend greetings between each other** at all. ***Amei ha'aretz*,** common, uneducated people, **who extend greetings** to *ḥaverim*, do so while unaware that this is inappropriate. The *ḥaverim* **answer them in an undertone and in a solemn manner. And** *ḥaverim* **wrap themselves and sit as mourners and as ostracized ones, like people who have been rebuked by God, until they are shown mercy from Heaven.**

The practices of *aveilut* extend to social interaction as well. The period restricts *she'ilat shalom* — the formal inquiry after another's welfare, a greeting more substantial than a simple hello, implying *ma shlomcha*, wishing another peace and asking after their condition. On Tisha B'Av, for instance, even simple greetings are suspended. The *gemara* emphasizes that *she'ilat shalom* carries the word *shalom* within it and conveys a quality of engagement and warmth that is incompatible with communal mourning.

A distinction is drawn between two groups. *Chaverim ein she'ilat shalom beineihem* — those of the knowledgeable and scrupulous rabbinic class do not engage in any inquiry after welfare at all. *Amei ha'aretz she'sho'alim* — the common people, unaware of the restriction, may still greet you. What should you do when they approach you with a cheerful greeting? *Machzirim lahem b'safah rafah u'vechoved rosh* — you answer them in a soft voice and with a heaviness of head. Brevity and solemnity without rudeness.

### Mourning Garb

Beyond the curtailment of activity and greeting, the community adopts visible expressions of mourning. *V'hem mit'atefim v'yoshvim ke'aveilim u'che'menudim* — they wrap themselves in their *tallit* and sit as mourners and as those in a state of *niduy*, *kivnei adam hanizufim lamakom* — like people who have been rejected by God — *ad sheyerachamu aleihem min hashamayim* — until Heaven shows compassion. Two distinct purposes are served by this practice. On one level, it is the honest owning of the religious significance of what is happening — not carrying on as though nothing is amiss, but allowing the external expression to match the internal reality of communal distress. On another level, there is an implicit theology here: making oneself so visibly and thoroughly miserable may itself move God to compassion. *Ad sheyerachamu* — until He takes pity. The possibility that the *ta'anit* itself functions partly in this way — that self-imposed suffering invites divine mercy — is not far beneath the surface.

## Ostentatious Behavior

### Falling on Face

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין אָדָם חָשׁוּב רַשַּׁאי לִיפּוֹל עַל פָּנָיו, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נַעֲנֶה כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ אֶל יְהוֹשֻׁעַ קֻם לָךְ לָמָּה זֶּה אַתָּה נֹפֵל עַל פָּנֶיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: An important person is permitted to fall on his face** and humiliate himself in front of the community **only if he** is certain that he **will be answered like Joshua, son of Nun, as it is stated: "And the Lord said to Joshua, Get you up, why are you fallen upon your face?"** (Joshua 7:10). One who is not absolutely certain that he will be answered may not fall on his face in public, as if he is unanswered he will become an object of derision.

Rabbi Eleazar offers a series of rulings that together form a critique of ostentatious religious behavior during this period — a topic implicitly connected to the earlier theme of *yechidim* who continue to fast and to set themselves apart from the community. He says: *ein adam chashuv rashai lipol al panav ela im ken ne'ena kiyehoshua bin nun* — an important person is not permitted to fall on his face in prayer unless he is confident of being answered as Yehoshua bin Nun was. The proof text: *vayomer Hashem el Yehoshua kum lach lama zeh ata nofel al panecha* — God said to Yehoshua, rise, why are you fallen on your face? The verse confirms that Yehoshua did fall on his face and was addressed by God — meaning he was answered. Unless a person has that level of assurance about his standing before God, the demonstrative act of falling on one's face amounts to *yuhara* — ostentatious religiosity.

This connects to the earlier portrait of the *yachid* who continues to fast. Being a *yachid* means setting oneself apart from the community in a way that carries spiritual weight. But there is a difference between private persistence in fasting and theatrical expressions of piety. The comparison to Choni Hame'agel — who drew a circle and demanded rain from God — captures the dangerous edge of that kind of self-presentation: it can tip from genuine *avodah* into a kind of religious *chutzpah*. Rabbi Eleazar is setting a firm limit on the demonstrative behaviors appropriate even for *gedolim*.

### Sackcloth Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין אָדָם חָשׁוּב רַשַּׁאי לַחֲגוֹר שַׂק אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נַעֲנֶה כִּיהוֹרָם בֶּן אַחְאָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כִשְׁמֹעַ הַמֶּלֶךְ אֶת דִּבְרֵי הָאִשָּׁה וַיִּקְרַע אֶת בְּגָדָיו וְהוּא עֹבֵר עַל הַחֹמָה וַיַּרְא הָעָם וְהִנֵּה הַשַּׂק עַל בְּשָׂרוֹ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar said: An important person is permitted to gird** himself in **sackcloth** as a sign of mourning and to pray for mercy **only if he** is certain that he **will be answered like Jehoram, son of Ahab, as it is stated: "And it came to pass, when the king heard the words of the woman, that he rent his clothes, now he was passing by upon the wall, and the people looked, and, behold, he had sackcloth** within **upon his flesh"** (II Kings 6:30). Although he was wicked, Jehoram was later answered and the suffering of the Jews was alleviated.

Rabbi Eleazar adds: *ein adam chashuv rashai lachgor sak ela im ken ne'ena kiyoram ben Achav* — an important person is not permitted to gird himself in sackcloth unless he expects to be answered as Yoram ben Achav was. This is a striking choice of exemplar, since Yoram was a wicked king. Yet the point is precisely that: even Yoram, when he tore his garments and wore sackcloth in response to dire news, was answered. The proof text: *vayehi kishmoa hamelech et divrei ha'isha vayikra et bgadav vehu over al hachoma vayar ha'am vehineh hasak al bsaro* — the king tore his garments as he walked the wall, and the people saw the sackcloth on his flesh. Unless you have reason to believe you will be answered — even as Yoram was — putting on sackcloth publicly is not appropriate for a person of standing.

### Varied Expressions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא הַכֹּל בִּקְרִיעָה וְלֹא הַכֹּל בִּנְפִילָה. מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרוֹן — בִּנְפִילָה, יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב — בִּקְרִיעָה. מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן בִּנְפִילָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּפּוֹל מֹשֶׁה וְאַהֲרֹן עַל פְּנֵיהֶם״. יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְכָלֵב בִּקְרִיעָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ בִּן נוּן וְכָלֵב בֶּן יְפֻנֶּה קָרְעוּ בִּגְדֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar** further **said: Not all** are worthy to petition God **by rending** their garments, **and not all** are worthy **of falling** on their faces in times of trouble. **Moses and Aaron** were worthy of petitioning God **by falling** on their faces, whereas their students **Joshua and Caleb** prayed **by** only **rending** their garments. The Gemara elaborates: **Moses and Aaron** petitioned God **by falling** on their faces, **as it is written: "Then Moses and Aaron fell on their faces"** (Numbers 14:5). **Joshua and Caleb** prayed **by rending** their garments, **as it is written** in the next verse: **"And Joshua, son of Nun, and Caleb, son of Jephunneh,** who were of those who spied out the land, **rent their garments"** (Numbers 14:6).

Rabbi Eleazar extends his observation beyond the specific behaviors to a general principle: *lo hakol bikriyah velo hakol binefilah* — not everyone in the biblical record used both rending of garments and falling on one's face. Different biblical figures employed different modes of expressing grief and supplication. *Moshe ve'Aharon binefilah* — Moshe and Aharon fell on their faces; *Yehoshua veCalev bikriyah* — Yehoshua and Calev rent their garments. The proof texts are familiar from the story of the *meraglim*: *vayipol Moshe ve'Aharon al pneihem*, and *vYehoshua bin nun veCalev ben Yefuneh kare'u bigdeihem*. It is worth noting that in context, Yehoshua and Calev were not tearing their garments as an act of prayer addressed to God; they were expressing grief at the people's behavior. This is entirely consistent with where the community now finds itself in the post-thirteen-fast stage: the focus is not on achieving an answered prayer but on owning the tragedy. The acts of Yehoshua, Calev, Moshe, and Aharon in response to the catastrophe of the *meraglim* are the appropriate models for this phase of *aveilut*, not for the earlier phase of urgent supplication.

### Challenge & Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: אִי הֲוָה כְּתִיב ״יְהוֹשֻׁעַ״ — כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב: ״וִיהוֹשֻׁעַ״, הָא וְהָא עָבֵיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira strongly objects to this** interpretation, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** who objected: **Had** the verse **written** only: **Joshua** and Caleb, the meaning would be **as you said,** that Moses and Aaron fell upon their faces whereas Joshua and Caleb only rent their garments. However, **now that it is written: "And Joshua,"** it is possible that the connecting word "and" indicates that Moses and Aaron merely fell upon their faces, while Joshua and Caleb **did** both **this and that,** i.e., they rent their clothing in addition to falling upon their faces.

Rabbi Zeira — or some say Rabbi Shmuel bar Nachmani — challenges Rabbi Eleazar's distinction. If the verse had simply written *Yehoshua* without the prefix *vav*, you could argue that Yehoshua and Calev did one thing while Moshe and Aharon did another. But since the verse writes *vYehoshua* — with the connective *vav* — it implies that Yehoshua and Calev did both: they fell on their faces as Moshe and Aharon did, *and* they rent their garments. *Ha veha avdi.* The *vav* connects their action to that of Moshe and Aharon rather than contrasting it, suggesting they performed both expressions. Doing more than one demonstrative act is therefore not so problematic after all — though the pushback does not entirely undo Rabbi Eleazar's larger concern about ostentatious behavior.

### Rising & Bowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא הַכֹּל בְּקִימָה וְלֹא הַכֹּל בְּהִשְׁתַּחֲוָיָה. מְלָכִים — בְּקִימָה, וְשָׂרִים — בְּהִשְׁתַּחֲוָיָה. מְלָכִים בְּקִימָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ גֹּאֵל יִשְׂרָאֵל קְדוֹשׁוֹ לִבְזֹה נֶפֶשׁ לִמְתָעֵב גּוֹי לְעֶבֶד מֹשְׁלִים מְלָכִים יִרְאוּ וָקָמוּ״. וְשָׂרִים בְּהִשְׁתַּחֲוָיָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״שָׂרִים וְיִשְׁתַּחֲווּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar** further **said: Not all** dignitaries will worship God in the messianic age **by rising, and not all** will do so **by bowing.** Rather, **kings** will serve God **by rising, and ministers by bowing.** The Gemara elaborates: **Kings by rising, as it is written: "Thus says the Lord, the Redeemer of Israel, his Holy One, to he who is despised of men, to he who is abhorred of nations, to a servant of rulers: Kings shall see and arise"** (Isaiah 49:7); **and ministers by bowing, as it is written,** in the same verse: **"Ministers, and they shall bow."**

Rabbi Eleazar makes one further observation, moving beyond the context of grief and prayer to forms of honor and respect: *lo hakol bekima velo hakol behishtachavaya* — not everyone rises, and not everyone bows down. Different forms of expressing honor are appropriate for different people. *Melachim bekima* — kings rise: *melachim yiru vekamu* — kings will see and rise up before you, as the verse in Isaiah promises. *V'sarim behishtachavaya* — princes bow down: *sarim veyishtachavu* — and princes will prostrate themselves.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי: אִי הֲוָה כְּתִיב ״וְשָׂרִים יִשְׁתַּחֲווּ״ — כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, הַשְׁתָּה דִּכְתִיב ״שָׂרִים וְיִשְׁתַּחֲווּ״ — הָא וְהָא עֲבוּד.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Zeira strongly objects to this** interpretation, **and some say** it was **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani** who objected. **Had** the verse **written: And ministers shall bow,** the meaning would have been **as you said.** However, **now that it is written: "Ministers, and they shall bow,"** this indicates that ministers will **do this and that,** i.e., they will both arise and bow.

Rabbi Zeira challenges this as well: the verse writes *sarim veyishtachavu* — with a *vav* before the verb — which implies they will also rise first and then bow down, performing both acts. *Ha veha avdi* — they do both. The point is parallel to the earlier challenge: the *vav* in these formulations connects rather than contrasts, suggesting that combining expressions is natural rather than excessive.

### Righteous & Upright

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק, אַף אֲנִי אוֹמֵר: לֹא הַכֹּל לְאוֹרָה, וְלֹא הַכֹּל לְשִׂמְחָה. צַדִּיקִים — לְאוֹרָה, וִישָׁרִים — לְשִׂמְחָה. צַדִּיקִים לְאוֹרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״אוֹר זָרֻעַ לַצַּדִּיק״. וְלִישָׁרִים שִׂמְחָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּלְיִשְׁרֵי לֵב שִׂמְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: I too say** a similar idea. **Not all** are fit **for light, and not all** are fit **for gladness. The righteous** are fit to be rewarded **with light, and the upright** are fit to be rewarded **with gladness. The righteous** are fit to be rewarded **with light,** as it is written: **"Light is sown for the righteous"** (Psalms 97:11), **and the upright** are fit to be rewarded **with gladness, as it is written,** in the same verse: **"And gladness for the upright in heart."**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak appends a final observation: *af ani omer, lo hakol le'orah velo hakol lesimcha* — let me add, not everyone will see the same end. *Tzadikim le'orah* — the righteous will have light; *lisharim lesimcha* — the upright will have joy. *De'tzadikim le'orah dichsiv or zarua latzadik* — for the righteous, light is sown; *u'le'yesharim simcha dichsiv u'le'yishrei lev simcha* — and for the upright of heart, joy. The observation ends the section on an unexpectedly positive note, though it also raises a question worth sitting with: does a *tzadik* not receive joy? Does a *yashar* not receive light? What exactly distinguishes these two categories?

Whatever the precise answer, this ending coheres with the larger thrust of the section. The entire discussion — Rabbi Eleazar's warnings against falling on one's face, wearing sackcloth, rending garments, and the various challenges about whether combining expressions is acceptable — reflects a sustained concern with authentic versus performative religiosity. The *yachid* who privately persists in fasting occupies a legitimate and honored role. What Rabbi Eleazar resists is the public theater of piety: the layering of demonstrative acts that signal one's special standing before God, when in truth only someone of Yehoshua's or even Yoram's stature has grounds for such confidence. The *perek* opens with the gradations of fasting that escalate with communal urgency and closes with a caution that even in the most intense stages, inward sincerity must govern outward expression.
