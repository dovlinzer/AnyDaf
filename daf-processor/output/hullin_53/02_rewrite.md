# Hullin 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Derusah Mechanism

### Derusah Principle

The daf begins five lines from the bottom of 52b, at the words *bemakom she-ein matzilin ein derusah* — "where there is no one to intervene, there is no *derusah*." The background to this passage is the discussion introduced on the previous daf: smaller animals such as a cat or weasel can render a victim *treif* through an act of *derusah*, which involves attacking a smaller animal in a manner that injects poison and thereby condemns the prey. The Mishnah had spoken of a wolf as the paradigmatic predator, but the Gemara extended this concern to smaller predators attacking proportionally smaller prey — young sheep, young goats, and the like. A *brayta* had complicated this picture by appearing to deny *derusah* to these smaller animals altogether. The resolution offered was that these animals attack viciously, injecting their poison, specifically when they fear that their prey will be saved by intervention. When no such intervention is anticipated, they do not inject their poison with the same force.

### Chicken & Cat Case

The Gemara now challenges this resolution with a case from the house of Rav Kahana. A cat was chasing a chicken, which fled into an interior chamber — the word *indara*, an uncommon Aramaic term, is helpfully glossed by the marginal note with a reference to the Targum on the verse where Yosef withdraws to an inner room. The door was slammed shut in the cat's face, leaving it alone with the chicken. The cat then struck the door with its claws, and five drops of blood were found on the door — apparently one per claw — indicating that poison had been injected. But if the entire concern is about the fear of intervention, and here the cat was utterly alone with its prey in a sealed room, why would it have injected its poison?

The Gemara answers: *hatzalat atzmah nami ke-hatzalat acharina dami* — the chicken's potential escape from its own predicament is treated like a rescue by an external party. The cat knew that people were pursuing it, and that it had been chased into the room precisely because of human intervention. It was therefore in a heightened state of agitation and acting vicious even within the sealed chamber.

### Tanna Kamma vs Rebbi

The Gemara then records a second version of this entire discussion — *ika de-amri*, some say — in which the positions of *tanna kamma* and Rebbi are reversed. In the first version, the view that small animals *do* cause *derusah* under certain conditions was attributed to Rebbi, while *tanna kamma* denied it. In the second version, it is *tanna kamma* who holds that small animals always cause *derusah*, and Rebbi who restricts the concern to cases where the predator fears intervention. The substantive ruling is identical; only the attribution changes.

The same story of the cat and chicken in Rav Kahana's house is then recruited to challenge Rebbi's position in this second version — if Rebbi says there is no *derusah* when there is no one to intervene, how do we explain the five drops of blood? The Gemara gives the same answer: the cat's self-protective agitation constitutes a form of concern equivalent to fear of external intervention.

The Gemara also addresses the view of the Sages who deny *derusah* entirely to these smaller animals, even in light of the physical evidence of blood. Their response is *zei'ah ra'itah ve-lo kali zikei* — what was observed was not genuine poison but merely a form of excretion; the small animal's venom does not burn with sufficient intensity to cause the kind of damage that would render an animal *treif*.

## Rav Kahana Questions

### Cat & Weasel Debate

The Gemara now transitions to the top of 53a, where Rav Kahana posed a series of questions to Rav. He asked: is there *derusah* for a cat, or is there no *derusah* for a cat? Rav replied cryptically: even a weasel, which is smaller, causes *derusah* — implying that the cat question is trivially obvious. Rav Kahana then asked: is there *derusah* for a weasel? Rav replied: there is not even *derusah* for a cat — implying the opposite, that the question is equally trivial but in the other direction. Finally Rav Kahana combined the questions: do a cat and weasel have *derusah* or not? Rav answered: for a cat yes, for a weasel no.

### Rav's Answers

The apparent contradiction resolves once the Gemara introduces a third variable — the nature of the prey. When Rav said that even a weasel causes *derusah*, he was speaking of birds (*b'ofot*), against which even the smaller weasel is effective. When he said that even a cat does not cause *derusah*, he was speaking of large sheep (*b'imrei ravrevei*), against which neither animal is effective. When he split his ruling — cat yes, weasel no — he was addressing an intermediate category: young goats and young lambs (*bi-g'dayim u-t'layim*). Tosafot elaborates on why Rav chose to answer cryptically, effectively encoding all three cases within three sequential responses. The key principle is that *derusah* depends not only on which predator is attacking but also on what it is attacking. The size of the prey is as determinative as the species and capacity of the predator.

## Birds of Prey

### Non-Kosher Birds

Rav Ashi asked a further question: what about other non-kosher birds — birds of prey other than those explicitly mentioned in the Mishnah? The Mishnah had mentioned the *netz* (sparrowhawk) and the *gas* (buzzard). Do other predatory birds also cause *derusah* when they attack smaller birds? Rav Hillel responded in the name of Rav Kahana: yes, other non-kosher birds of prey do cause *derusah*. One might object from the Mishnah's specific language, which singles out the sparrowhawk — but just as in the parallel case of the wolf, the Mishnah's specificity serves not to exclude other capable predators but to convey something particular about the species mentioned, while larger predators are certainly included.

### Sparrowhawk Superior

The Gemara then explains the precise significance of the Mishnah's mention of the sparrowhawk. According to the first version (*ika de-amri*): the sparrowhawk causes *derusah* even in birds of comparable size (*bidekhvateih*), while other birds of prey cause *derusah* only in birds smaller than themselves. According to the second version: the sparrowhawk causes *derusah* even in birds larger than itself (*bidarabi mineih*), while other birds of prey cause *derusah* only in birds of comparable size. In both versions the sparrowhawk is distinguished as the more formidable attacker; the question is whether its superior capacity extends to equal-sized prey or to prey larger than itself.

## Fox Derusah

### Fox No Derusah

Rav Kahana then transmitted in the name of Rav Shimi bar Ashi: there is no *derusah* for a fox. This is striking. We have just established that a cat and weasel — smaller animals — can cause *derusah* against appropriately sized prey. A fox is a larger and more powerful animal. The basis for the distinction, as will become clearer, is that *derusah* is about clawing and the injection of poison through the claw, and the relevant animals are those whose claws carry this poison. A fox, despite its capacity to cause serious injury through biting, may not share this particular mechanism.

### Bathhouse Story

The ruling is immediately challenged. When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael he reported: there was a story of a fox and a ewe in a bathhouse of Beit Heini — *ma'aseh be-shual ve-rachel be-mirkhatz shel Beit Heini* — and the case came before the Sages, who ruled that there is a problem of *derusah*. This appears to directly contradict the position that a fox cannot cause *derusah*.

The Gemara then presents a competing version of both the ruling and the story. According to the first version, Rav Kahana had said there is *no* *derusah* for a fox, and the bathhouse ruling had confirmed *derusah*. According to the second version (*ika de-amri*), Rav Ashi had actually said there *is* *derusah* for a fox, and the bathhouse story had ruled that there is *no* *derusah*. Both the transmitted ruling and the story are each reported in contradictory forms.

### Fox vs Cat

In each version, Rav Safra offers a resolution by identifying the animal in the bathhouse story not as the species assumed. In the first version — where it was ruled that *derusah* applies and this contradicts the denial of fox *derusah* — Rav Safra says: that bathhouse animal was not a fox but a cat. In the second version — where *derusah* was denied despite Rav Ashi holding that fox *derusah* applies — Rav Safra says: that animal was not a fox but a dog (*kalba*). This last identification points ahead to the next major topic: dogs do not cause *derusah* because *derusah* is specifically about clawing, not biting, and dogs lack the relevant claws. The canine family — foxes and wolves among them — presents the interesting puzzle that the Mishnah treats the wolf as the paradigmatic *derusah* predator, yet the fox is contested. Both, in zoological terms, are canines. The resolution must lie in the specific mechanism of poison injection through the claw.

## Nature of Derusah

### Foreleg Claws

Rav Yosef states as an operating principle: *ein derusah le-chelev* — dogs do not cause *derusah*. Abaye then articulates the structural logic underlying all of the preceding discussions: *ein derusah ela beyad* — *derusah* is only through the foreleg, not the hind leg; and *ein derusah ela be-tziporen* — only through the claw, not through the teeth (*la'apukei shen dlo*). This is why dogs are excluded: despite their capacity for serious injury, dogs do not attack primarily through retractable claws that inject poison, and so the mechanism of *derusah* does not apply to them.

One might have thought teeth would be more dangerous from the standpoint of poison injection — after all, snakes inject venom through their bite. But the Gemara's assumption is that the specific poison of these predatory animals is delivered through the claw. This is why the concern is limited to the foreleg (where retractable claws are located), to the claw itself, and to certain species identified as carrying this poison: cats, weasels, wolves, and certain birds of prey.

It is worth noting that if a dog bite or any other attack caused a wound that in turn raised concern about perforation of an internal organ, that would be a separate *treif* problem — not *derusah* but a question of *nekuva* or *drusah* in a different sense. The *derusah* discussed here is specifically the concern about clawing and poison injection.

### Intent & Withdrawal

The Gemara continues to define precisely when *derusah* applies. *ein derusah ela mida'at* — *derusah* requires that the animal consciously and intentionally attack with its claw (*la'apukei shelo mida'at dlo*). If the claws were extended and the animal accidentally made contact with another animal, no poison would be transmitted. *ein derusah ela mi-chayyim* — *derusah* requires that the attacking animal be alive at the time of the attack, not that a dead animal with extended claws fell upon another.

The Gemara notes that the ruling about death seems superfluous: if we already know that the animal must be acting intentionally, a dead animal obviously cannot act intentionally, and so the case of the dead animal would already be excluded. The Gemara therefore clarifies that the case being addressed is where the animal clawed another, and then its foreleg was severed. The animal itself is still alive, but the severed limb is functionally dead. One might have thought that the poison was injected in the act of clawing and so would have been transferred during the clawing motion itself. The Gemara teaches: *be-hide de-shalif shadi zeharei* — the poison is deposited not as the claw enters but as it is *withdrawn*. Since the severed limb cannot be withdrawn in a living, intentional way, no poison is injected, and there is no *derusah*.

## Lion Among Oxen

### Detached Nail

Rabbah bar Avuna citing Rav presents the following scenario: a lion entered among oxen, and afterward a claw (*tziporen*) was found embedded in the back of one of them. Should we be concerned that the lion attacked that ox, rendering it *treif* through *derusah*?

The Gemara rules: we are not concerned. Two reasons are offered. First, *rov arayos dorsin u-mi'utan ein dorsin* — Tosafot explains this not as a claim that most lions actually attack at any given moment, but that most lions have the capacity to cause *derusah*, while a minority are not vicious or capable in this way. The mere presence of a lion does not establish that this particular lion attacked. Second, *ve-chol ha-dores ein tziporen nishmemet* — any animal that actually claws another does not leave its claw behind in the process. The very evidence being offered — a claw in the ox's back — counts against the hypothesis of clawing, because clawing does not produce detached claws.

### Wall Rubbing

The Gemara then introduces an alternative explanation for how the claw came to be embedded in the ox: *eimar be-kosel nis-chatech* — perhaps the lion was scratching itself against a wall, and in the process its claw became detached and fell. This detached claw then somehow came to be lodged in the ox's back. The argument is that lions do sometimes shed claws in this way, and the claw's presence in the ox does not require the hypothesis of attack.

### Resolve Nail

The Gemara then reverses course and argues the other direction: *adarabba* — in fact, one could argue from the same evidence that the ox *was* attacked. The counter-argument runs as follows. *rov shvarim mis-chatchin u-mi'utan ein mis-chatchin* — most oxen do rub their backs against walls, but not all. And even those that do, *ein tziporen yosheves lo be-gavlo* — rubbing against a wall does not result in a claw becoming embedded in the ox's back. Similarly, clawing an animal does not result in the claw being left behind. Neither mechanism produces the observed evidence cleanly, yet the ox does have a claw in its back. So one could argue from this that the lion must have attacked.

The Gemara concludes: *ika le-meimar hachi ve-ika le-meimar hachi* — arguments can be made in both directions. When evidence admits of competing interpretations, the principle is *okmei milta a-chazakah* — we maintain the animal in its presumptive status. The animal has a *chazakah* of fitness; this is a case of *safek derusah*, and Rav holds *ein chosheshin le-safek derusah* — in doubtful cases we rely on the *chazakah* of fitness and do not declare the animal *treif*.

This raises a broader question about the Gemara's overall approach: in many cases concerning *treifot*, the Gemara appears extremely stringent, refusing to permit reliance on a membrane covering a hole or other mitigating factors. Yet here it is lenient based on *chazakah*. These approaches are not contradictory. Where a problem is established — a hole is known to exist — the Gemara does not easily permit the assumption that it has been resolved by other means. But where the question is whether a problem ever arose in the first place, the Gemara consistently permits reliance on *chazakah* to conclude that it did not. The strictness is reserved for cases where an issue is known; the leniency operates before a problem has been formally established. There is also a further question about the nature of *chazakah* here — whether it describes a physical state (the animal began its life without being attacked) or a halachic state (the animal began its life as forbidden). The Gemara is clear that once the animal has been properly slaughtered, it carries a *chezkat hetter*.

The Gemara then introduces a qualification in the name of Bivi: *lo amru ela tziporen* — the leniency where we find a detached claw applies specifically when the claw is found protruding. But *mekom tziporen* — if one finds a hole clearly shaped by a claw, one is obviously concerned. Furthermore, even a protruding claw is only dismissed if it is moist (*lacha*); a dried claw (*yevesha*) might have been naturally shed. And even a moist claw: if only one is found, it is dismissed, but two or three claws found together (*tarti ve-tlat*), especially if they are arranged in the pattern of claws on a paw (*de-kaymi be-dardei de-chuchpa*), constitute clear evidence of having been attacked.

## Safek Derusah

### Rav Chazakah

The Gemara now presents the formal dispute between Rav and Shmuel regarding *safek derusah* in general. Rav says: *ein chosheshin le-safek derusah* — in doubtful cases of *derusah*, we are not concerned; we rely on the *chazakah* of the animal's fitness. The practical implication is that unless there is clear evidence of an attack, the animal is presumed undamaged.

### Shmuel Concern

Shmuel says: *chosheshin le-safek derusah* — we are concerned even in cases of doubtful *derusah*. Once there is sufficient basis to raise the doubt, the *chazakah* of fitness does not override it.

### Limit Debate

The Gemara works to define the precise boundaries of this dispute. Certain cases are excluded from the debate because they do not rise to the level of a genuine *safek*. If it is unknown whether the predator even entered the herd, *de-kulei alma safek al safek lo al* — everyone agrees that a doubt compounded upon a doubt does not require concern. This is understood through the concept of *leidat ha-safek* — the formal "presentation" of a doubt that obliges us to address it. A doubt that is too remote — we do not know if the lion entered, and even if it did we do not know if it attacked — is not a sufficiently concrete *safek* to trigger halachic concern. Similarly, if it is unknown whether the animal in question was a dog or a cat, and it is only a cat that causes *derusah*, then since we do not even know there was a cat present, the doubt has not formally presented itself.

The Gemara then adds a case with more contextual texture: the predator entered among the sheep and simply sat there (*aylei ve-yativi beineihu*) — perhaps, as Rashi notes, they had been domesticated to coexist, or perhaps, in a striking image, the lion lay down with the lamb. That alone does not create concern. Even if the lion decapitated one animal — which is certainly a *neveilah* — Shmuel would not require concern about the remaining animals merely because a violent lion was present. The case that does generate the debate is more specific.

## Lion & Sheep

### No Attack

The cases examined so far — the lion merely present, the lion making noise, the lion that decapitated one animal — do not reach the threshold that even Shmuel would consider a *safek*. The Gemara considers the scenario of a lion that has actively roared and the sheep that have responded with distressed sounds, and rules: *meva'atei ahadadei* — they are frightening each other; the sounds indicate mutual fear, not necessarily an actual attack. None of these scenarios suffice to trigger Shmuel's concern.

### Silent Lion

The case that divides Rav and Shmuel is identified at the top of 53b: *de-ihu ka shatik ve-inhu ka mekarkeran* — the lion is silent while the sheep are bleating. The dispute is in the interpretation of the sheep's cries. One authority holds *maisa ka'avid* — the lion is clearly doing something to them; the silence of the lion combined with the distress of the sheep indicates active attack. The other holds *be-machmat bi'uta hu de-ka'avid* — the sheep are bleating from sheer terror at the lion's presence, not because they are being attacked. It is this specific, nuanced circumstance — a silent predator and distressed prey — that constitutes the case where Rav and Shmuel disagree about whether to apply the *chazakah* of fitness.

## Practical Ruling

### Amemar Rules

Amemar rules: *hilchata kavatei de-choshishin le-safek derusah* — the *halacha* follows Shmuel's position, and in cases that present a sufficient basis for doubt (of the kind delineated above), we are concerned about *safek derusah* and do not simply rely on *chezkat hetter*.

### Ashi Questions

Rav Ashi challenged Amemar: what about Rav's opposing position that we do not worry about *safek derusah* and do rely on *chazakah*? How can one simply rule against Rav without addressing his view?

### Amemar Response

Amemar replied: *lo shmia li* — I have not heard of Rav's position. The Gemara immediately glosses this: *k'lomar, lo sevira li* — "I have not heard it" is a way of saying "I do not find it persuasive." The idiom is that of a student who is technically aware of the teaching but simply declines to give it weight. Amemar is not claiming ignorance; he is registering disagreement.

## Rav Reversal

### Basket Birds

The Gemara raises the possibility that Rav himself reversed his position: *hadar bei Rav legabei Shmuel* — Rav ultimately came around to agree with Shmuel. The evidence offered is that a certain basket (*sharkafa*) — Rashi explains this as a basket of birds — that presented a question of *safek derusah* was brought before Rav, and Rav sent it to Shmuel. The argument is: if Rav still held that *safek derusah* is not a concern, why would he send it to Shmuel, who was his disputant on precisely this question?

### Shmuel Action

Shmuel received the basket and acted decisively: he strangled the birds and threw them into the river — *chankinhu ve-shadinhu be-nahara*. As noted elsewhere in the Gemara, a *posek* who rules that an animal is forbidden and physically feeds it to dogs thereby takes responsibility for the financial loss. Shmuel's act of strangling and discarding the birds effectively constituted such an act, and he did so in keeping with his consistent ruling that *safek derusah* is a genuine concern.

The Gemara then challenges the inference that Rav reversed himself: *aval mah hadar bei* — even if he did reverse himself, why send it to Shmuel? If Rav now holds it is forbidden, he should simply rule it forbidden himself. The Gemara responds: *asrei de-Shmuel hava* — it was in Shmuel's locale, his city. Rashi presents two readings of this answer. In the first, the answer simply deflects the proof: because it was Shmuel's city, Rav sent the question to him out of deference to the *rav ha-makom*, and we therefore cannot infer anything about Rav's own ruling. In the second — which Rashi presents as more striking — the deference to the local authority does not override the financial interests of the questioner, and therefore if Rav had genuinely held the birds were permitted, he would have ruled so even in Shmuel's city to save the person from a loss. The fact that he sent it rather than ruling leniently shows that he had reversed himself. Rashi ultimately favors the *p'shat* that we cannot determine whether Rav reversed himself; the sending of the basket is explained by the fact that it was Shmuel's locale.

### Publicity

The Gemara then asks why Shmuel specifically strangled the birds and threw them into the river, rather than using simpler methods. Could he not simply have thrown them into the river without strangling? The birds would have flown away and been eaten by unknowing individuals. Could he not have waited and sold them to non-Jews? The concern there is *asi lizvinehu le-Yisroel* — the non-Jews might resell them to Jews who would not know of the problem. Could he not simply have thrown them in the garbage or fed them to dogs? The Gemara's answer: *ela le-farsumei milsa d'isura* — Shmuel wanted to publicize the seriousness of the prohibition. As Rashi explains, *derusah* is not always easy to detect, and Shmuel's dramatic action was a one-time measure designed to make clear to everyone that this category of doubt is genuine and serious. It is not presented as a general policy recommendation.

### Twelve Month Test

The Gemara raises the question of why Shmuel did not simply observe whether the birds lived for twelve months. The principle is that any animal that is genuinely *treif* will not survive a full year; if an animal of *safek* status lives out twelve months, this demonstrates retroactively that it was not *treif*.

### Why Not Wait

The answer is *asi behu lidei takala* — holding a basket of birds for twelve months would lead to others stumbling, as someone would inevitably eat one before the year was out. The twelve-month test, while theoretically available, was not practically viable in this case given the number of birds and the difficulty of maintaining control over them for so long.

## Goose Case

### Goose Incident

A goose belonging to the household of Rav Ashi went into the reeds (*al beini kanya*) and returned with a bloody neck (*nafak ata ki mimasmas kovei d'may*). The question arose: was the neck injured by a cat lurking among the reeds, which would create a concern of *derusah*, or was it cut by the sharp reeds themselves?

### Reeds Injury

Rav Ashi resolved the case: *lo amran safek chavsa ve-shunra eimar chavsa* — in a case where one does not know whether the culprit was a dog or a cat, even Shmuel would agree that the doubt has not formally presented itself, because we have no established evidence of a cat being present. Similarly here: *safek kanya safek shunra* — it is uncertain whether the wound was caused by the reeds or by a cat among the reeds. *eimar kanya amchayei* — it is reasonable to conclude that it was the sharp reeds that struck the neck. The doubt therefore does not reach the threshold of a *safek* requiring concern, even according to Shmuel's more stringent position.

## Inspection Methods

### Check Organs

The sons of Rabbi Chiya taught: *derusah she'amru tzricha bedikah keneged bnei me'ayim* — the *derusah* referred to by the Sages requires inspection opposite the area of the inner digestive organs. If an animal was attacked by a predator, one checks whether the outer skin opposite the stomach and intestinal area has turned red. Rashi explains the mechanism: the concern with *derusah* is that the poison injected through the claw is acidic in nature and will eat its way through to the inner organs, creating a hole. The relevant location to inspect is therefore the skin opposite those organs, since that is where the damage will manifest. If, upon inspection, that area shows no redness, the animal is not condemned. The language of the teaching — *tzricha bedikah*, "requires inspection" — sounds like a demand rather than a leniency, but both Rashi and Tosafot read it as providing a mechanism to *permit* the animal: even in a case of known *derusah* or *safek derusah*, a clean inspection can establish fitness.

### Shmuel Method

Rav Yosef notes that this teaching of the sons of Rabbi Chiya had already been articulated by Shmuel: *d'amar Shmuel mishum Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, derusah she'amru tzricha bedikah keneged bnei me'ayim* — in the name of Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos, Shmuel had taught the identical rule about inspecting opposite the digestive organs.

### Neck Area

Ilfa raised two distinct questions. First: *yesh derusah la-simanim o ein derusah la-simanim?* — is there *derusah* when the attack was at the neck, in the area of the *simanim* (trachea and esophagus), rather than the abdominal area? Rabbi Zeira replied that this had already been answered by Rav Chanan bar Rava in the name of Rav: the inspection must cover not just the area opposite the digestive organs but *keneged beit ha-chalal kulo* — the entire body cavity, including, by implication, the area of the *simanim*.

Ilfa's second question is formally separate but is presented in the same context: *simanim she-nidaldelu be-ruban mahu* — when the *simanim* have been uprooted from their proper position in the flesh of the neck for the majority of their length, what is the ruling? Rabbi Zeira replied that Rabbah bar bar Chana in the name of Shmuel had already addressed this: if the *simanim* are uprooted (*nidaldelu*) from their proper place for the majority of their length, it is a disqualifying condition.

## Reddened vs Rot

### Bloody Flesh

Rabbi Ami raised a question about flesh that is *martema* — somewhat bloody or reddened in appearance. In the context of evaluating *derusah*, when one inspects the area opposite the inner organs, what is the significance of flesh that appears bloody or discolored? Rabbi Zeira replied that this too had already been explained — the Gemara here continues the pattern of *kvar pirshah*, "this was already explained."

### Reddened Evidence

The explanation comes from Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav: *bi-drusah, ad she-ya'adim basar keneged bnei me'ayim* — in a case of *derusah*, the determining evidence is that the flesh opposite the inner organs has actually turned red (*ya'adim*). Redness of the skin in this location is the indicator that poison has penetrated and is working its way toward the inner organs. Rashi explains that the redness signals that the acidic poison has reached that level of the body wall, and from there it will continue to the organs. This is what makes *derusah* dangerous and what the inspection is designed to detect.

### Rotting Treatment

A separate but related question concerns flesh that has *nitmasmeis* — that has decomposed, become putrid, or is dissolving. The rule is: *ha-basar ro'enu k'ilu einu* — such flesh is treated as if it were not present, as if it had been removed. The inquiry then shifts from *derusah* specifically to a general question about *treifot*: if that flesh were removed entirely, would the animal be *treif* or not? *derusah* operates through the mechanism of poison penetration, so evidence of redness in a particular location of the outer wall indicates ongoing danger to the inner organs. Rotting flesh operates differently: one simply assesses whether the animal, absent that flesh, would be *treif* based on the standard criteria for the affected area.

## Rotting Definition

### Medical Removal

Rav Huna son of Rav Yehoshua defines rotting flesh precisely: *kol she-rofe godro u-ma'amido al basar chai* — any flesh that a physician would surgically excise in order to expose and treat the healthy tissue beneath. Flesh in this condition has the halachic status of removed flesh, and the animal is evaluated as though that flesh had been cut away.

### Lung Example

Rav Ashi reported that while he was in the house of Rav Kahana, a lung was brought before them. When placed on a table it appeared normal, but when lifted, it would crumble and fall apart in pieces. Rav Kahana ruled it *treif* (*ve-tarfina lah*) on the basis of Rav Huna's principle. A lung with a hole is *treif*; this lung's tissue was so decomposed that it would disintegrate upon handling, meaning the substance of the lung was functionally absent in those areas. Since the rotting tissue is treated as if removed, the lung is evaluated as having openings — and a lung with openings is not fit.

## Derusah vs Puncture

### Thorn Perforate

Rav Nachman articulates a crucial distinction: *be-kotz, ad she-tenakev le-chalal* — if a thorn or needle pierced the animal's body wall, it is only a concern if it penetrated all the way through the abdominal wall into the inner cavity. A thorn merely embedded in the outer flesh, without reaching the cavity, is not a problem. The rationale is straightforward: a thorn is simply a sharp object causing mechanical injury, and the danger is only if it actually reaches the inner organs.

### Claw Reddening

The case of *derusah* is categorically different: *bi-drusah, mishe-ta'adim basar keneged bnei me'ayim* — if the skin opposite the inner organs has turned red, that is sufficient evidence of a problem, even if the claw did not physically penetrate all the way through the body wall. The claw's poison, injected into the tissue upon withdrawal, is not a purely mechanical injury. The redness indicates that the poison is actively working through the tissue, and the assumption is that it will continue through to the organs. Thus the evidentiary threshold for *derusah* is lower than for a thorn: redness suffices, while full perforation is not required.

### Simanim Exception

Rav Zavid teaches a qualification regarding the *simanim*. In the case of *derusah* to the body generally, redness of the outer skin opposite the inner organs is the evidentiary standard. But when the concern involves the *simanim* specifically — the trachea and esophagus in the neck — the *simanim* themselves must turn red (*ad she-ta'adimu simanim atzamam*). The outer skin of the neck alone turning red is insufficient; one needs to see the redness in the *simanim* themselves. The reasoning is that these structures are more robust and are not as immediately affected by the poison spreading from the outer layers. Throughout all of this, the question persists — noted by both Rashi and Tosafot — whether the inspection method applies only to *safek derusah*, as a way to determine whether an attack actually occurred, or whether it applies equally to a *vaday derusah*, as a means of establishing that despite the confirmed attack, the poison has not reached the critical organs and the animal remains fit.
