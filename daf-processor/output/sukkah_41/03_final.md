# Sukkah 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lulav Obligation History

### Original Law

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הָיָה לוּלָב נִיטָּל בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ שִׁבְעָה, וּבַמְּדִינָה יוֹם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Originally,** during the Temple era, the ***lulav* was taken in the Temple** for **seven** days, **and in the** rest of the **country** outside the Temple it was taken for **one day.**

The *mishnah* opens a new subject: *barishona hayah lulav nital bamikdash shiva u-vamedina yom echad* — originally, the *lulav* was taken in the *beit hamikdash* for all seven days, and in the *medina* for only one day. Having spent the entirety of the preceding *perek* discussing the four species, the act of *hallel*, and the special case of *shemitta*, the *mishnah* finally arrives at the fundamental question of the nature and scope of the *lulav* obligation itself.

### Medina Definition

What exactly is the *medina*? The term stands in contrast to *mikdash*, and the most natural reading is that it refers to anywhere outside the *beit hamikdash*. However, the Rambam, in his *Perush Hamishnayot* here and in several analogous *mishnayot*, interprets *mikdash* to encompass not merely the Temple precinct itself but all of *Yerushalayim*. A parallel case involves blowing the *shofar* on *rosh hashana* that falls on *shabbos*: this was permitted in the *mikdash* but not in the *medina*, and for the Rambam that privilege extended throughout *Yerushalayim*. The majority of authorities, however, understand *mikdash* in the strict sense — the Temple compound alone — and *medina* as everywhere outside it.

### Biblical Source

The biblical source for the seven-day obligation in the *mikdash* is the verse *v'likachtem lachem bayom harishon* — take for yourselves on the first day — followed at the end of the passage by *v'semachtem lifnei Hashem elokeichem shivat yamim*, rejoice before Hashem your God for seven days. The phrase *lifnei Hashem*, before God, is understood to mean in the *beit hamikdash* — or, according to the Rambam, in the *mikdash* and *Yerushalayim* — and that presence before God carries a seven-day *mitzvah*. Outside the *mikdash*, only the first day applied.

### Post-Destruction Enactment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, הִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי שֶׁיְּהֵא לוּלָב נִיטָּל בַּמְּדִינָה שִׁבְעָה, זֵכֶר לַמִּקְדָּשׁ. וְשֶׁיְּהֵא יוֹם הֶנֶף כּוּלּוֹ אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Once the Temple was destroyed, **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted** an ordinance **that the *lulav*** should **be taken** even **in the** rest of the **country** for **seven** days, in **commemoration of the Temple. And** for similar reasons, he instituted an ordinance **that** for **the entire day of waving** the *omer* offering, **it** should **be prohibited** to eat the grain of the new crop.

Once the *beit hamikdash* was destroyed, Raban Yochanan ben Zakai enacted that the *lulav* should be taken all seven days everywhere — *sheyehei lulav nital bamedina shiva* — as a *zecher lamikdash*, a remembrance of the Temple. Since the seven-day *mitzvah* could no longer be performed in the *mikdash*, it would henceforth be observed universally. The *mishnah* then adds a second element: *v'yehei yom hanaf kulo asur*, the day of the waving of the *omer* should be entirely forbidden. The juxtaposition of these two rulings — the *lulav* and the *omer* prohibition — may seem surprising, but Tosafot suggests that both were enacted by Raban Yochanan ben Zakai as *takanot* of *zecher lamikdash*, and these happen to be the two recorded in our *mishnah*.

## Zecher Lamikdash Theology

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא לַן דְּעָבְדִינַן זֵכֶר לַמִּקְדָּשׁ? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״כִּי אַעֲלֶה אֲרוּכָה לָךְ וּמִמַּכּוֹתַיִךְ אֶרְפָּאֵךְ נְאֻם ה׳ כִּי נִדָּחָה קָרְאוּ לָךְ צִיּוֹן הִיא דּוֹרֵשׁ אֵין לָהּ״. ״דּוֹרֵשׁ אֵין לָהּ״, מִכְּלַל דְּבָעֲיָא דְּרִישָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that **we institute** ordinances in **commemoration of the Temple? Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that it is **as the verse states: "For I will restore health unto you and I will heal you of your wounds, says the Lord; because they have called you an outcast, she is Zion, there is none that seeks her"** (Jeremiah 30:17). From the fact that the verse states: "There is none that seeks her," it can be learned **by inference that it requires seeking,** i.e., people should think of and remember the Temple.

The *gemara* does something striking: it completely bypasses the fascinating question of the one-day versus seven-day *lulav* obligation — a topic that will be taken up at the beginning of the next *perek* — and leaps directly to the concept of *zecher lamikdash*. The *gemara* asks: *minalan d'avdinan zecher lamikdash* — what is the source for the idea that one should institute practices to remember the *beit hamikdash*?

### Jeremiah Source

Rabbi Yochanan answers with a verse: *ki nidchah kar'u lach, Tziyon hee doreish ein lah* — they have called you a castaway, Zion that no one seeks after. The verse is a lament over the fate of *Yerushalayim* after the *churban*, bemoaning that no one is seeking it out. The *gemara* reads this negatively — *doreish ein lah*, no one seeks after it — as implying a positive obligation: *miklal d'ba'i doreish*, we are in fact required to seek after it, to maintain practices that keep the memory of the *churban* and of *Yerushalayim* alive.

### Ben Zakkai's Vision

The concept of *zecher lamikdash* invites a broader reflection on what Raban Yochanan ben Zakai was actually doing. He is typically understood as being deeply preoccupied with ensuring that the *churban habayit* remain at the forefront of Jewish consciousness. Yet the same Raban Yochanan ben Zakai was precisely the figure who helped the Jewish people navigate the transition away from a *mikdash*-centered Judaism toward a *Torah*-centered Judaism. The famous story in *Gittin* recounts how he negotiated with Vespasian and requested: *tenu li Yavne v'chachameha* — give me Yavneh and its scholars. Rabbi Akiva later criticized this as a missed opportunity to save *Yerushalayim*, but the *gemara* explains that Raban Yochanan ben Zakai regarded saving *Yerushalayim* as a lost cause and sought to preserve what could actually be preserved.

This tension is illustrated by two parallel stories about walking past the ruins of the *har habayit*. In the story involving Rabbi Akiva, the sages weep upon seeing foxes emerging from the site of the *beit hamikdash*, while Rabbi Akiva laughs, explaining that the fulfillment of the prophecy of destruction assures him the prophecy of rebuilding will also be fulfilled. It is a deeply optimistic vision, but one that remains centered on the *mikdash* — the *mikdash* was destroyed, and the *mikdash* will be rebuilt. In the parallel account in *Avos de-Rabbi Noson*, Raban Yochanan ben Zakai walks past the ruins and laughs. When his students ask why, he does not speak of the future rebuilding. Instead he invokes the verse *chesed chafatzti v'lo zavach* — I desire loving-kindness, not sacrifice — arguing that prayer, *Torah*, and *maasim tovim* are not mere substitutes for the *mikdash* but are independently what God desires. The center of religious life has genuinely shifted, not simply relocated.

### Paradox & Effect

Against this backdrop, the *takanot* of *zecher lamikdash* take on a paradoxical quality. They ostensibly exist to remind us of the *beit hamikdash* and to keep its loss present to consciousness. Yet if one were to ask today why we take the *lulav* for seven days, the natural answer would be: because it is a seven-day *mitzvah*. The practice does not typically evoke the sense of loss or yearning for the *mikdash*; if anything, it creates a full and expansive seven-day religious experience that stands entirely on its own. Rather than heightening the sense of absence, the enactment actually generates a vibrant religious practice that operates independently of the *mikdash*. Indeed, by extending the seven-day *mitzvah* to all of Israel rather than confining it to the Temple compound and a handful of participants, the *takana* arguably produced something richer than what had existed before.

This is the deeper signature of Raban Yochanan ben Zakai's project. The *takanot* framed as *zecher lamikdash* do not in practice cultivate ongoing yearning for the *mikdash* — they supplant that yearning with a flourishing Judaism that needs no *mikdash* to sustain it. The ostensible purpose and the actual effect run in opposite directions.

## Yom Hanaf Prohibition

### Problem Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁיְּהֵא יוֹם הֶנֶף. מַאי טַעְמָא? מְהֵרָה יִבָּנֶה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְיֹאמְרוּ: אֶשְׁתָּקַד מִי לֹא אָכַלְנוּ בְּהֵאִיר מִזְרָח? הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי נֵיכוֹל. וְאִינְהוּ לָא יָדְעִי דְּאֶשְׁתָּקַד דְּלָא הֲוָה בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ — הֵאִיר מִזְרָח, הִתִּיר. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאִיכָּא בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ — עוֹמֶר מַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: Rabban Yoḥanan instituted **that** for **the entire day of waving** the *Omer* offering, it is prohibited to eat the grain of the new crop. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this ordinance? It is that **soon the Temple will be rebuilt, and** people **will say: Last year,** when the Temple was in ruins, **didn't we eat** of the new crop **as soon as the eastern** horizon **was illuminated,** as the new crop was permitted immediately with the advent of the morning of the sixteenth of Nisan? **Now, too, let us eat** the new grain at that time. **And they do not know that** although **last year, when there was no Temple, the illuminating of the eastern** sky **permitted** one to eat the new grain immediately, **now that there is a Temple, the *omer*** offering **permits** one to eat the new grain. Until the *omer* offering is sacrificed, the new grain is not permitted.

The *mishnah* also includes Raban Yochanan ben Zakai's ruling that *yom hanaf* — the sixteenth of *Nissan*, the day the *omer* was waved — should be *kulo asur*, entirely forbidden for consumption of the new grain. The underlying law is *ad yom havi'achem et korban Elokeichem* — the new grain is prohibited until the *korban* of the *omer* is brought on the second day of *Pesach*. In the absence of a *beit hamikdash*, there is no *korban* to bring, and a separate verse — *v'lechem vekali v'karmel lo tochlu ad etzem hayom hazeh* — is understood to mean that when there is no *omer*, the grain becomes permissible as soon as the day itself begins, i.e., at nightfall entering the sixteenth. Paradoxically, the absence of the *mikdash* makes the grain permissible earlier than when the *mikdash* stands, since it is the bringing of the *omer* that creates the permission.

### Rebuilding Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיבְּנִי אֵימַת? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיבְּנִי בְּשִׁיתְּסַר, הֲרֵי הִתִּיר הֵאִיר מִזְרָח! אֶלָּא דְּאִיבְּנִי בַּחֲמֵיסַר, מֵחֲצוֹת הַיּוֹם וּלְהַלָּן תִּשְׁתְּרֵי, דְּהָא תְּנַן: הָרְחוֹקִים, מוּתָּרִין מֵחֲצוֹת הַיּוֹם וּלְהַלָּן, לְפִי שֶׁאֵין בֵּית דִּין מִתְעַצְּלִים בּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **When** is it **that** the Temple **will be rebuilt** in this scenario? **If we say that it will be rebuilt on the sixteenth** of Nisan, since in the morning the Temple was not yet built, the **illuminating of the eastern sky permitted** one to eat the new grain, as the *omer* offering could not yet be brought. **Rather,** say **that it will be rebuilt on the fifteenth** of Nisan or on some earlier date, in which case the new grain would not become permitted by the **illuminating of the eastern sky.** In that case, **from midday and onward let it be permitted** to eat the new grain, **as we learned** in a mishna in tractate *Menaḥot*: **The** people **distant** from Jerusalem, who are unaware of the precise time when the *omer* was brought, **are permitted** to eat the new grain **from midday and onward because** the members of the **court are not indolent with regard to** the *omer* and would not postpone bringing the offering after midday.

Why then did Raban Yochanan ben Zakai forbid the new grain for the entire day? The *gemara* explains with a remarkably concrete scenario: *meheira yibaneh beit hamikdash* — when the *beit hamikdash* is quickly rebuilt — people will say that last year they were eating new grain from daybreak of the sixteenth, *he'ir mizrach hitir*. When the *mikdash* is rebuilt and they must now wait for the *omer* to be brought, they will not understand the distinction and will continue eating from daybreak, not realizing that last year there was no *mikdash* and so daybreak sufficed, whereas now the *omer* must be brought. The *gemara* then asks with characteristic precision: when exactly does this concern arise? If the *beit hamikdash* were rebuilt on the sixteenth itself, then since it had not yet been built at daybreak, the grain was already permissible at that moment — so there is nothing problematic about having eaten at daybreak. Rather, the concern is that the *beit hamikdash* will be rebuilt on the fifteenth, on *yom tov* itself.

### Noon Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, דְּאִיבְּנִי בְּלֵילְיָא, אִי נָמֵי סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **No,** it is **necessary** to institute the ordinance only in the case where the Temple **will be rebuilt at night,** on the evening of the sixteenth, and there was no opportunity to cut the *omer* that night. **Alternatively,** it was necessary to institute the ordinance in the case where the Temple was built **adjacent to sunset** on the fifteenth because there would not be sufficient time to complete all the preparations and sacrifice the offering by noon the next day.

This raises a further difficulty. A *mishnah* teaches that those who lived far from *Yerushalayim* were permitted to eat new grain from noon onward — *mechatzot hayom u'lehallan* — because *beit din* was never delinquent in ensuring the *omer* was brought by noon at the very latest. If the *beit hamikdash* is rebuilt on the fifteenth and the *omer* will invariably be brought by noon, why not simply establish the practice of refraining until noon? Whatever happens when the *mikdash* is rebuilt, people will know not to eat until noon — which will be after the *omer* is brought in any case. Why must the prohibition extend through the entire day?

### Night-Before Solution

The *gemara* resolves this with a specific scenario: *d'ivni b'leilya* — the *beit hamikdash* is built on the night entering the sixteenth. More precisely, it is completed right before sunset at the end of the fifteenth. In that case, when dawn breaks on the sixteenth, the *mikdash* already stands and the new grain requires the *omer* before it may be eaten. However, bringing the *omer* was not a simple act — it required going out to the fields, identifying and cutting the wheat, grinding it, and performing thirteen separate siftings. This preparatory process demanded substantial lead time. If the *mikdash* was completed only the previous night, there would be insufficient time to complete all preparations by noon. The *omer* could not be brought by noon, and the new grain would remain forbidden past midday. Had the practice been to permit eating from noon — since the *omer* is always brought by then — people in this scenario would eat at noon and in fact violate the prohibition, not realizing the situation was different. Therefore the practice had to be to forbid the new grain the entire day.

### Rashi on Future Temple

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (אָמַר) רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי בְּשִׁיטַת רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֲמָרָהּ, דְּאָמַר: מִן הַתּוֹרָה הוּא אָסוּר, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַד עֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״ — עַד עִיצּוּמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם, וְקָסָבַר ״עַד״ וְעַד בַּכְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** That is not the reason; rather, **Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai stated** his ordinance **in** accordance with **the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, who said: It is prohibited by Torah law** to eat the new grain until the seventeenth of Nisan, **as it is written: "And you shall eat neither bread, nor roasted grain, nor fresh grain, until this selfsame [*etzem*] day,** until you have brought the offering of your God" (Leviticus 23:14), indicating **until the essence [*itzumo*] of the day,** and not the night before. **And he holds that** when the verse states: **"Until,"** the word **until is inclusive,** meaning that the grain is permitted only after the conclusion of the sixteenth.

Rashi and Tosafot both address an obvious question: building the *beit hamikdash* is prohibited on *yom tov*, and also at night — so how can the *gemara* contemplate a scenario in which the *mikdash* is built at night or on *yom tov*? Rashi explains: *hani mili binyan habanui bidei adam* — those restrictions apply to a *mikdash* built by human hands. But the future *mikdash* that we await — *mikdash ha'asid she'anu mitzapin* — is already built and perfected, and it will be *yigale veyavo mishamayim*, revealed and descend from heaven, as the verse says *Mikdash Hashem konenu yadecha*, the Temple that God's own hands established. This understanding — that the future *mikdash* is already complete and awaits only its revelation — carries significant theological weight regarding the respective roles of human initiative and divine action in redemption.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּווֹתֵיהּ? וְהָא מִפְלָיג פְּלִיג עֲלֵיהּ, (דְּתַנְיָא:) מִשֶּׁחָרַב בֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, הִתְקִין רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי שֶׁיְּהֵא יוֹם הֶנֶף כּוּלּוֹ אָסוּר, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: וַהֲלֹא מִן הַתּוֹרָה הוּא אָסוּר, דִּכְתִיב: ״עַד עֶצֶם הַיּוֹם הַזֶּה״ — עַד עִיצּוּמוֹ שֶׁל יוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **hold in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda? **But doesn't he disagree with him, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Once the Temple was destroyed, Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai instituted that** for **the entire day of waving** the *omer* offering, **it** should **be prohibited** to eat the grain of the new crop. **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: Isn't it prohibited by Torah law, as it is written: "Until this selfsame day,"** which means: **Until the essence of the day?**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak proposes an entirely different reading of the *mishnah*. Rather than understanding Raban Yochanan ben Zakai's ruling as a *gezeira* — a rabbinic fence against confusion — he argues that Raban Yochanan ben Zakai was following the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the all-day prohibition is in fact *min haTorah*, a biblical law. The basis is the verse *ad etzem hayom hazeh* — until the very essence of this day — which Rabbi Yehuda reads as *ad itzumo shel yom*, until the day itself has completely passed. The word *ad* is understood as inclusive: the new grain is forbidden through the entirety of the day, unless the *omer* is brought, in which case it becomes permissible earlier. Under this reading, when the *omer* is brought it does not delay permission — it accelerates it. There is a certain logical elegance to this: rather than the presence of the *korban* imposing a stricter regime, the *korban* actively creates an earlier *heter*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הוּא דְּקָא טָעֵי, הוּא סָבַר מִדְּרַבָּנַן קָאָמַר. וְלָא הִיא, מִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קָאָמַר. וְהָא ״הִתְקִין״ קָאָמַר! מַאי ״הִתְקִין״ — דָּרַשׁ וְהִתְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It is Rabbi Yehuda who is mistaken. He thought** that Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **is saying** it is prohibited **by rabbinic law. And that is not** so; **he is saying** it is prohibited **by Torah law.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't** the mishna **say:** Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai **instituted,** indicating that it is a rabbinic ordinance? The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of **instituted?** It means that **he interpreted** the verses in the Torah **and instituted** public notice for the multitudes to conduct themselves accordingly.

The *gemara* challenges this by noting that Rabbi Yehuda explicitly objects to Raban Yochanan ben Zakai's ruling elsewhere, suggesting he disagrees with it. The *gemara* resolves this by saying Rabbi Yehuda misunderstood Raban Yochanan ben Zakai: he thought Raban Yochanan ben Zakai was issuing a new *d'rabbanan* prohibition, and he responded that no new enactment is needed since the all-day prohibition already exists biblically. But Raban Yochanan ben Zakai was in fact doing something different — *darash v'hitkin* — he was expounding and publicizing what the biblical law actually is. Since there had always been a *mikdash*, no one had ever needed to know what the *halacha* was in its absence. Once the *mikdash* was destroyed, Raban Yochanan ben Zakai taught the masses the existing biblical rule. The word *hitkin* here means he expounded and established the practice, not that he created new rabbinic legislation.

## First Day on Shabbat

### Pre-positioning Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת, כׇּל הָעָם מוֹלִיכִין אֶת לוּלְבֵיהֶן לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת. לַמׇּחֳרָת מַשְׁכִּימִין וּבָאִין, כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד מַכִּיר אֶת שֶׁלּוֹ וְנוֹטְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **the first** day **of** the **festival of *Sukkot* occurs on Shabbat, all of the people bring their *lulavim* to the synagogue** on Shabbat eve, as it is prohibited to carry in a public domain on Shabbat. **The next day,** on Shabbat, everyone **rises early and comes** to the synagogue. **Each and every one recognizes his** *lulav* **and takes it.**

The *mishnah* turns to the case of *yom tov rishon shel chag she-chal lihyot be-Shabbos* — the first day of *sukkos* that falls on *shabbos*. We noted earlier that the first day of *lulav* is biblically mandated everywhere, and in the era when this *mishnah* was operative, people would in fact take the *lulav* even on *shabbos* if the first day fell then. The challenge was how to get the *lulav* to *shul* without carrying on *shabbos*. The solution was to prepare in advance: on *erev shabbos*, everyone would bring their *lulavim* to the synagogue. The next morning, people would rise early — *mashkimin u-va'in* — and each person would identify and take his own *lulav*, *kol echad ve-echad makir shelo ve-notel lo*, since the sages ruled that one cannot fulfill the obligation on the first day with someone else's *lulav*.

### Ownership Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן בְּלוּלָבוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, וּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַחַג — אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּלוּלָבוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** This emphasis that each and every one recognizes his own *lulav* and takes it is **because the Sages said: A person does not fulfill his obligation** to take the *lulav* **on the first day of the Festival with the *lulav* of another, and** on **the rest of the days of the Festival a person fulfills his obligation** even **with the *lulav* of another.**

The *mishnah* states explicitly: *ein adam yotzei yedei chovaso be-yom tov rishon be-lulavo shel chaveiro* — a person does not fulfill his obligation on the first day of *yom tov* with his friend's *lulav*. This is why early arrival mattered: with many *lulavim* gathered together, each person had to identify and retrieve his own. For the remaining days of *chol hamoed*, however, a person may use his friend's *lulav*; the ownership requirement is specific to the first day.

### Accidental Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת, וְשָׁכַח וְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַלּוּלָב לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים — פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוֹצִיאוֹ בִּרְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** If **the first day of** the **Festival occurs on Shabbat, and he forgot and carried the *lulav* out into the public domain, he is exempt** from liability to bring a sin-offering for this unwitting transgression **because he carried it out with permission,** i.e., he was preoccupied with the performance of the mitzva and carried it out.

Rabbi Yosi addresses a case of someone who did not bring his *lulav* to *shul* before *shabbos* and then, on the morning of *yom tov shabbos*, absentmindedly picked it up and walked out of his house. He realizes only after stepping outside that he has just carried in a public domain on *shabbos*. Rabbi Yosi rules *patur* — exempt from a *korban chatas* — because the carrying occurred in the context of performing a *mitzvah*. This reflects the general principle of *toeh bidvar mitzvah*: one who commits an unintentional transgression while engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from the *korban*. This same debate appears in the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. A classic parallel is a *mohel* who on the eighth day inadvertently performs *milah* on the wrong infant; he is engaged in a *mitzvah* and acted without intent — the accidental *aveira* in the course of the *mitzvah* exempts him from the *korban*.

### Plural vs Singular

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וּלְקַחְתֶּם״, שֶׁתְּהֵא לְקִיחָה בְּיַד כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. ״לָכֶם״, מִשֶּׁלָּכֶם — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַשָּׁאוּל וְאֶת הַגָּזוּל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **From where are these matters** derived, that one does not fulfill his obligation with the *lulav* of another on the first day of the Festival? It is **as the Sages taught** that it is written: **"And you shall take** for yourselves on the first day the fruit of a beautiful tree, branches of a date palm, and boughs of a dense-leaved tree, and willows of the brook" (Leviticus 23:40). The use of second person plural in the phrase: "And you shall take," indicates **that there should be taking in the hand of each and every** person. The word **yourselves** in the phrase "take for yourselves" means: **From your own, to exclude a borrowed or stolen** *lulav*.

The *gemara* asks: what is the source for the requirement of individual ownership specifically on the first day? The *gemara* derives it from careful attention to grammatical number in the verse. *Ulekachtem lachem* is in the plural, and the counterintuitive principle that emerges is that plural language in the *Torah* means each individual is separately obligated, while singular language addresses the community as a corporate whole. This can be seen in the two *mitzvot* of counting: *usefartem lachem* — the plural form — is the *mitzvah* of counting the *omer*, which falls on each individual. *Vesafarta lecha* — the singular form — is the counting for the *yovel*, which is an obligation of *beit din* on behalf of the entire community. The plural addresses each member of the group individually; the singular addresses the collective as one entity. Applied here: had the verse said *ulekachta lecha*, one person could have taken the *lulav* on behalf of all. *Ulekachtem lachem* means every individual must take it personally.

### Biblical Ownership Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: אֵין אָדָם יוֹצֵא יְדֵי חוֹבָתוֹ בְּיוֹם טוֹב הָרִאשׁוֹן שֶׁל חַג בְּלוּלָבוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן נְתָנוֹ לוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **From here the Sages stated: A person does not fulfill his obligation on the first day of** the **Festival with the *lulav* of another unless** the other **gave it to him as** a full-fledged **gift,** as in that case it belongs to him.

The second element derived from *ulekachtem lachem* is the word *lachem* — for yourselves — which is read as *mishelachem*, from what belongs to you. This excludes a borrowed or stolen *lulav* and *esrog*. *Mikan amru chachamim* — from this the sages derived — that one does not fulfill the obligation on the first day of the holiday with a *lulav* belonging to someone else, unless it has been given as an outright gift: *ela im ken nesano lo be-matana*.

## Four Rabbis Story

### Story Narrative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעֲשֶׂה בְּרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁהָיוּ בָּאִין בִּסְפִינָה, וְלֹא הָיָה לוּלָב אֶלָּא לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בִּלְבַד, שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ בְּאֶלֶף זוּז. נְטָלוֹ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וְיָצָא בּוֹ, וּנְתָנוֹ לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בְּמַתָּנָה. נְטָלוֹ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ וְיָצָא בּוֹ, וּנְתָנוֹ לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה בְּמַתָּנָה. נְטָלוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה וְיָצָא בּוֹ, וּנְתָנוֹ בְּמַתָּנָה לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. נְטָלוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְיָצָא בּוֹ, וְהֶחְזִירוֹ לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** There was **an incident involving Rabban Gamliel, and Rabbi Yehoshua, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and Rabbi Akiva, who were** all **traveling on a ship** during the festival of *Sukkot* **and only Rabban Gamliel had a *lulav*, which he** had **bought for one thousand *zuz*. Rabban Gamliel took it and fulfilled** his obligation **with it and then gave it to Rabbi Yehoshua as a gift. Rabbi Yehoshua took it and fulfilled** his obligation **with it and gave it to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya as a gift. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya took it and fulfilled** his obligation **with it and gave it to Rabbi Akiva as a gift. Rabbi Akiva took it and fulfilled** his obligation **with it and returned it to Rabban Gamliel.**

The *mishnah* then records a story that illustrates and sharpens this principle. Rabban Gamliel, Rabbi Yehoshua, Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, and Rabbi Akiva were traveling together on a ship — there is interesting speculation that this journey may have been connected to one of the delegations to the Roman government in that period. Rabban Gamliel was the only one who had brought a *lulav* and *esrog*, and he had purchased it for a thousand *zuz* — an extraordinary sum, reflecting how precious the *mitzvah* was to him. Who else on a ship was selling *lulavim ve-esrogim*? Apparently the one person who had one charged accordingly.

Rabban Gamliel took the *lulav* and *esrog* and fulfilled his obligation. He then gave it to Rabbi Yehoshua *be-matana* — as a gift, not a loan, since a loan would not allow him to fulfill the obligation. Rabbi Yehoshua was *yotzei*, and then gave it as a gift to Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, who in turn gave it as a gift to Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva was *yotzei* and then returned it to Rabban Gamliel. It is, in the spirit of the season, an early documented instance of regifting.

### Conditional Gift

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמֵימַר הֶחְזִירוֹ? מִלְּתָא אַגַּב אוֹרְחֵיהּ קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: מַתָּנָה עַל מְנָת לְהַחְזִיר — שְׁמָהּ מַתָּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **to say** that Rabbi Akiva **returned** the *lulav* to Rabban Gamliel? The crux of the story is that each of the Sages fulfilled his obligation with the same *lulav* after receiving it as a gift. The Gemara answers: By including that detail, the *tanna* **teaches us** another **matter in passing,** namely that **a gift** given **on the condition that it be returned is considered** a full-fledged **gift.**

The *gemara* asks why the *mishnah* bothers telling us it was returned to Rabban Gamliel — this seems like an incidental detail. The *gemara* answers: *agav orcha kamashma lan* — it is teaching us something in passing. The return was not simply Rabbi Akiva's courtesy; it reflects the implicit understanding that the gift was given *al menat le-hachzir* — on the condition that it would ultimately be returned to Rabban Gamliel. And the *chiddush* is: *matana al menat le-hachzir shema matana* — even a conditional gift of this kind constitutes a genuine *matana*. One might have argued that if you give something only on the condition it comes back, it is really a loan, not a gift, and the recipient is therefore not *yotzei*. 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רָבָא: ״הֵא לְךָ אֶתְרוֹג זֶה עַל מְנָת שֶׁתַּחְזִירֵהוּ לִי״. נְטָלוֹ וְיָצָא בּוֹ, הֶחְזִירוֹ — יָצָא, לֹא הֶחְזִירוֹ — לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that which Rava said,** that in the case of one who says to another: **Here is an *etrog* for you on condition that you return it to me, and** the recipient **took it and fulfilled his obligation with it,** if **he returned** the *etrog*, **he fulfilled** his obligation of taking the *etrog*. However, if **he did not return** the *etrog*, **he did not fulfill** his obligation. Since he did not fulfill the condition, retroactively he never acquired the gift at all.

Rava states explicitly: if one says *helach lecha esrog ze al menat she-tachzirehu li* — here is this *esrog* on condition you return it to me — and the recipient takes it and fulfills the *mitzvah*, he is indeed *yotzei*. If he returns it, the condition is satisfied and the *yetzia* stands. If he does not return it, *lo yatza* — he was not *yotzei* — because the condition's failure retroactively negates the gift, meaning he was in effect taking an object that was not his.

An interesting nuance from the story: Rabban Gamliel's condition was not that the first recipient return it immediately, but that it eventually find its way back. Each subsequent recipient was receiving a genuine gift for the duration of his use of it.

### Communal Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמָה לִי לְמֵימַר שֶׁלְּקָחוֹ בְּאֶלֶף זוּז? לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כַּמָּה מִצְוֹת חֲבִיבוֹת עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **to say that** Rabban Gamliel **bought** this *lulav* **for one thousand *zuz*?** The Gemara answers: It is **to inform you how beloved mitzvot were to them** to the extent that he was willing to pay an exorbitant sum to purchase a *lulav*.

Tosafot draws out two important practical applications. First, Tosafot notes that a partnership *esrog* — one owned jointly by multiple people — also does not satisfy the *mitzvah* on the first day; sole ownership is required. Tosafot then addresses the historical reality in European communities, where individuals could not afford a *lulav* and *esrog* and communities would purchase a set with communal funds. How could this work if shared ownership disqualifies? The answer is that the implicit arrangement — and the practice was to make it explicit — was that when each person's turn came to take the *esrog*, every other member of the partnership was simultaneously giving his share to that person as a *matana al menat le-hachzir*. At the moment of taking, the individual is the sole owner of all the shares, satisfying the requirement of *lachem*. This is the implicit, if not always explicit, understanding operative in such communal purchases: *stama d'milsa k'ilu peirshu dami* — by default, the situation is treated as though all the terms were spelled out.

An analogous question arises with communal *tallitot* in a synagogue. Taking a *shul's tallis* and reciting a *bracha* depends on whether one has acquired it sufficiently — some treat the arrangement as a *matana al menat le-hachzir* or a similar form of temporary acquisition. The same logic applied historically to communal wedding rings, large decorative rings found in Jewish museums, which communities owned jointly but transferred temporarily to the groom as sole owner at the moment of *kiddushin*.

### Practical Lending

The practical *halacha* for someone who needs to borrow a *lulav* and *esrog* on the first day of *yom tov* follows directly from these principles. One cannot simply lend it — that would leave the borrower without ownership. The owner should give it as a genuine *matana al menat le-hachzir*, on the condition that it be returned. Ideally, the condition can be stated explicitly. Tosafot in the next passage raises a separate concern: whether giving something *al menat le-hachzir* requires satisfying the technical requirements of stipulations derived from the *tnai b'nei Gad u'v'nei Re'uven* — that the condition must precede the action, must be stated in a double form with both the positive and negative clause, and so on. That Tosafot suggests these conditions must be satisfied, while elsewhere the same Tosafot indicates that where the intent is clear, the conditions may be assumed. In the communal purchase scenario, Tosafot had already concluded that an implicit understanding suffices; the question of technical *tnai* requirements is more acute when one is making an explicit conditional gift on the spot.

## Holding During Prayer

### Ameimar Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר בַּר אַמֵּימָר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אַבָּא צַלּוֹיֵי קָא מְצַלֵּי בֵּיהּ. מֵיתִיבִי: לֹא יֹאחַז אָדָם תְּפִילִּין בְּיָדוֹ וְסֵפֶר תּוֹרָה בְּחֵיקוֹ וְיִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Mar bar Ameimar said to Rav Ashi:** My **father would pray with** the four species in his hand in an expression of his love for the mitzva. The Gemara **raises an objection: A person** should **not hold phylacteries in his hand or a Torah scroll in his lap and pray** while doing so.

Mar bar Ameimar reports to Rav Ashi that his father, Ameimar, would hold his *lulav* and *esrog* while he was *davening* — that is, he held onto the four species throughout his prayer. The *gemara* immediately raises an objection from a *baraita*: a person should not hold *tefillin* in his hand or a *sefer Torah* in his chest while *davening*, because he will be distracted — divided between focusing on his prayer and ensuring he does not drop these sacred objects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: סַכִּין וּקְעָרָה, כִּכָּר וּמָעוֹת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** With regard to **a knife, a bowl** full of food, **a loaf** of bread, **or money, these** items **are similar to those** mentioned above; since he is concerned lest these items fall from his hand, he is distracted and he unable to concentrate on his prayers.

Shmuel extends the concern to non-sacred objects as well: a knife, a plate, bread, or money — all of these are things one fears dropping during prayer, and therefore they too constitute a distraction.

### No Distraction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם לָאו מִצְוָה נִינְהוּ, וּטְרִיד בְּהוּ. הָכָא מִצְוָה נִינְהוּ, וְלָא טְרִיד בְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **There,** in the cases listed above, **they are not** related to performance of **a mitzva, and he is preoccupied with them.** Therefore, that preoccupation distracts his focus from his prayers. **Here,** in the case of the four species, **they are** related to performance of **a mitzva,** so **he is not preoccupied with them** in a manner that will distract him from his prayers.

How then could Ameimar hold a *lulav* and *esrog* while *davening*? The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by distinguishing between the nature of the objects. In the cases of *tefillin*, a *sefer Torah*, and the objects listed by Shmuel, there is no *mitzvah* in the holding itself — *lav mitzvah ninhu l'ochzan* — and as a result the holding is experienced as a burden and a distraction. Rashi explains: *v'havu lehu l'masa* — psychologically, holding them is a weight; one is anxious about maintaining hold of them, and that anxiety pulls focus away from *davening*. But with the *lulav* and *esrog*, the holding itself is the *mitzvah*: *netilata u'lekichata mitzvah hi*. And because the *mitzvah* is beloved — *mitech shechaviva mitzvah alav* — it does not feel like a burden. The holding is not an additional concern layered on top of prayer; it is itself a cherished act, and therefore psychologically it does not distract. This is admittedly counterintuitive — one still needs to avoid dropping it — but Rashi's explanation is that when an act is experienced as *chaviv*, the anxiety of maintaining it does not register as a drain on attention.

### Jerusalem Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בַּר צָדוֹק אוֹמֵר: כָּךְ הָיָה מִנְהָגָן שֶׁל אַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, אָדָם יוֹצֵא מִבֵּיתוֹ — וְלוּלָבוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. הוֹלֵךְ לְבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת — לוּלָבוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. קוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — וְלוּלָבוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. קוֹרֵא בַּתּוֹרָה וְנוֹשֵׂא אֶת כַּפָּיו — מַנִּיחוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי קַרְקַע. הוֹלֵךְ לְבַקֵּר חוֹלִים וּלְנַחֵם אֲבֵלִים — לוּלָבוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. נִכְנַס לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ — מְשַׁגֵּר לוּלָבוֹ בְּיַד בְּנוֹ, וּבְיַד עַבְדּוֹ, וּבְיַד שְׁלוּחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites support for the custom mentioned above, as **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok says: This was the custom of the people of Jerusalem** during the festival of *Sukkot*. **A person leaves his house, and his *lulav* is in his hand; he goes to the synagogue,** and **his *lulav* is in his hand; he recites *Shema* and prays, and his *lulav* is in his hand; he reads the Torah and** a priest **lifts his hands** to recite the priestly benediction, and **he places it on the ground** because he cannot perform those tasks while holding the *lulav*. **He goes to visit the ill or to console mourners,** and **his *lulav* is in his hand; he enters the study hall** to study Torah, and **he sends his *lulav*** home **in the hands of his son, in the hands of his slave, or in the hands of his agent.**

A *baraita* records that this was in fact the established practice of the people of *Yerushalayim*. Rabbi Elazar bar Tzadok testifies: *adam yotzei mibeito v'lulavo beyado* — a person would leave his home with his *lulav* in hand; he would walk to the synagogue holding his *lulav*; he would recite *krias shema* and *daven* holding his *lulav*. This is consistent with the assumption that dominates the preceding discussion throughout this *perek*: the primary and natural context for taking the *lulav* is in *shul* during *hallel* and prayer. The people of *Yerushalayim* took this further by holding the *lulav* not just during the designated moments but throughout the day.

### Placing Down

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כַּמָּה הָיוּ זְרִיזִין בְּמִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the *baraita* **teaching us** by relating all these details that appear to establish the same practice? The Gemara explains: It is **to inform you how vigilant they were in** the performance of **mitzvot** and how much they cherished them.

Two activities required setting the *lulav* down. When called up for a *Torah* reading, one needs both hands free to open and roll the *Torah*. And when a *kohen* performed *duchening* — the *priestly blessing* — his hands obviously needed to be free and raised. In those moments, the *lulav* was placed on the ground. Upon entering the *beit midrash* for learning, a person would send the *lulav* home with a son, a servant, or a messenger rather than leave it on the floor, since abandoning it there would be disrespectful. The *gemara* notes that the entire *baraita* serves *l'hodiacha kama hayu zerizin bemitzvot* — to show how zealous the people of *Yerushalayim* were in the performance of *mitzvot*. The practice also reflects the understanding that holding the *lulav* is not exhausted in the single moment of *netila* — *ulekachtem lachem*, the act of taking, continues as a *mitzvah* throughout the duration of holding it.

## Exemption Conditions

### Abaye Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁלֹּא יָצָא בּוֹ, אֲבָל יָצָא בּוֹ — חַיָּיב. הָא מִדְּאַגְבְּהֵיהּ נְפַק בֵּיהּ! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: כְּשֶׁהֲפָכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught** that he is exempt **only** in a case **where he did not** yet **fulfill** his obligation. **However,** if **he** already **fulfilled** his obligation and he carries the *lulav* out, he is **liable** to bring a sin-offering. The Gemara asks: Is it possible to carry the *lulav* without fulfilling one's obligation? **Didn't he, from** the moment **that he lifted it, fulfill** his obligation **with it? Abaye said:** It is referring to a case **where he overturned it** and lifted it.

The *gemara* returns to the case of Rabbi Yosi: one who accidentally carries the *lulav* out into the public domain on the *shabbos* of the first day of *sukkos* is *patur* because he was *toeh bidvar mitzvah*. Abaye qualifies this: *lo shanu ela shelo yatza bo* — the exemption applies only if at the moment of carrying he had not yet been *yotzei*. If he was *already yotzei* and then stepped outside with the *lulav* in hand, *chayav* — he is liable for the sin offering, since the act of carrying was no longer part of the *mitzvah* itself.

### Upside Down

This raises an immediate difficulty: as soon as one picks up the *lulav* and *esrog*, one is *yotzei*, so when would it ever be the case that one carries it outside and has not yet been *yotzei*? Abaye answers: *keshehafacho* — when one picked it up upside down. As discussed earlier, one turns the *lulav* upside down before the *bracha* in order not to be *yotzei* prematurely; the *mitzvah* of *lekicha* requires the *lulav* to be in its proper orientation. So if a person grabbed his *lulav* upside down, intending to flip it right before the *bracha* in *shul*, and in that state walked out of his house, he had not yet been *yotzei* and the exemption of *toeh bidvar mitzvah* would apply.

### Vessel Carrying

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא הֲפָכוֹ, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹצִיאוֹ בִּכְלִי. וְהָא רָבָא הוּא דְּאָמַר: לְקִיחָה עַל יְדֵי דָּבָר אַחֵר — שְׁמָהּ לְקִיחָה! הָנֵי מִילֵּי דֶּרֶךְ כָּבוֹד, אֲבָל דֶּרֶךְ בִּזָּיוֹן — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even if you say that** it is referring to a case where **he did not invert it, with what are we dealing here?** It is with a case **where he took** the *lulav* **out in a vessel** and did not fulfill his obligation. The Gemara asks: **But isn't Rava the one who said** that **taking by means of another object is considered taking?** The Gemara answers: **This applies only** when the addition is in **a deferential manner,** for wrapping or ornamentation. **But** if the addition is in **a degrading manner,** as in this case, where one places the *lulav* into a vessel and carries it that way, **no,** it is not considered taking.

Rava offers an alternative: *afilu teima shelo hafacho* — even if the *lulav* was held right-side up, the exemption can still apply. *Kegon shehotzio bikli* — if the person was carrying it out inside a vessel or container, and he is not *yotzei* because he was not holding it directly. The *gemara* presses back: but Rava himself had said *lekicha al yedei davar acher shema lekicha* — taking through an intermediary object still counts as taking. Rava responds: that is only true *derech kavod* — if the manner of taking is respectful. If it is *derech bizayon*, a disrespectful manner, it does not count. Rashi clarifies that holding the *lulav* in a fine silk handkerchief as a handle, for example, is *derech kavod* and constitutes valid *lekicha*, whereas placing it inside an ordinary vessel is not *derech bizayon* per se but also lacks the quality of *kavod* required. For indirect *lekicha* to be valid, it must be conducted in a manner that reflects respect for the *mitzvah*. In most ordinary circumstances — walking out of one's house with the *esrog* in its box and the *lulav* in its holder — the act likely does not meet the threshold of *lekicha derech kavod*, which is why one does not automatically become *yotzei* through such casual handling.

## Toeh Bidvar Mitzvah

### Olah-Chatat Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, עוֹלַת הָעוֹף שֶׁנִּמְצֵאת בֵּין אֲגַפַּיִם, וּכְסָבוּר חַטַּאת הָעוֹף הִיא וַאֲכָלָהּ — פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna said** that **Rabbi Yosei would say:** In the case of **a bird** sacrificed as **a burnt-offering that is found among** other **birds** in one of the corners of the altar, **and** the priest **thought that it was a bird** sacrificed as **a sin-offering and he ate it,** as sin-offerings are eaten by priests, **he is exempt** from liability to bring a guilt-offering for misuse of consecrated items.

Rav Huna extends the application of Rabbi Yosi's principle of *toeh bidvar mitzvah*. He cites the following case: if a *kohen* finds a bird *olah* among the bird *chatat* offerings near the base of the *mizbeach* — they are normally in separate corners, but due to overflow one has ended up in the wrong place — and he reasonably assumes it is a *chatat* and eats it, *patur*. He is exempt from the *korban*, since a *kohen* eating a *chatat* bird is a *mitzvah*, and his error occurred in the course of intending to perform that *mitzvah*.

### Exemption Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דְּטָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה — פָּטוּר? הַיְינוּ הָךְ! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָתָם הוּא דְּטָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה פָּטוּר — הַיְינוּ דַּעֲבַד מִצְוָה. אֲבָל הָכָא, דְּטָעָה בִּדְבַר מִצְוָה וְלָא עֲבַד מִצְוָה — אֵימָא לָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav Huna **teaching us?** Is it **that** if **one erred in the matter of a mitzva, he is exempt? This is** identical to **that** statement of Rabbi Yosei; what novel element is introduced by Rav Huna? The Gemara answers: It is **lest you say** that **it is** only **there,** in the case of carrying the *lulav*, **that one who erred in a matter of a mitzva is exempt** and **that is** because **he performed a mitzva; however, here,** with regard to mistakenly identifying the bird-offerings, **where he erred in the matter of a mitzva but did not perform a mitzva** at all, **say no,** in that case he would not be exempt from liability to bring a guilt-offering. Therefore, Rav Huna **teaches us** that he is in fact exempt.

The *gemara* asks: what is novel here? We already know from Rabbi Yosi's case that *toeh bidvar mitzvah* exempts. The *gemara* answers: one might have thought the exemption applies only when the error leads to the actual performance of a *mitzvah* — the person was trying to take a *lulav* and in the process committed an unintentional *aveira*, but he did end up performing the *mitzvah* of *lulav*. In Rav Huna's case, the person intended to eat a *chatat* bird as a *mitzvah*, but what he actually ate was an *olah* — he committed only an *aveira* and never performed any *mitzvah* at all. The *chiddush* is that even in this case, the intention to perform a *mitzvah* is sufficient; the exemption of *toeh bidvar mitzvah* does not require the error to culminate in the actual fulfillment of a *mitzvah*.

### Tamid Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הַשּׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַתָּמִיד שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְבוּקָּר כְּהִלְכָתוֹ בַּשַּׁבָּת — חַיָּיב חַטָּאת, וְצָרִיךְ תָּמִיד אַחֵר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection. Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to **one who slaughters the daily offering on Shabbat that is not properly inspected,** and a blemish is discovered that disqualifies the sacrifice, he unwittingly performed the prohibited labor of slaughtering on Shabbat. **He is liable** to bring **a sin-offering, and he needs** to bring **a different daily offering.** Even though he erred in a matter of a mitzva, he is liable.

The *gemara* raises an objection from a *mishnah*: Rabbi Yosi himself rules that one who slaughters a *tamid she'eino mevukar* on *shabbos* — a daily offering animal that had not been inspected for blemishes four days in advance as required — is *chayav chatas*, liable for the sin offering, and must bring an additional *tamid* since the offering was invalid. But this is the same Rabbi Yosi who exempts *toeh bidvar mitzvah*. Here too, the person was performing what he believed to be the *mitzvah* of the *tamid* and yet wound up not performing a valid *mitzvah* — just as in the *olah-chatat* case. Why is he liable?

### Negligence Resolve

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא. דְּהָא אִתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר חַתַּאי אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא סָבָא אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַשְׁיָאן אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהֱבִיאוֹ מִלִּשְׁכָּה שֶׁאֵינָן מְבוּקָּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna **said** in response to the objection: Proof may be cited, **except** not **from that** *baraita*, **as it was stated concerning** that *baraita* that **Rav Shmuel bar Ḥatai said** that **Rav Hamnuna Sava said** that **Rav Yitzḥak bar Ashian said** that **Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** It is referring to **a case where they brought** the sheep for the daily offering **from a chamber** in which there were sheep **that are not inspected.** Since under no circumstances should one take a sheep for the daily offering from uninspected sheep, his error cannot be attributed to preoccupation with the mitzva. Therefore, although he was engaged in performance of a mitzva, he is not exempt from liability to bring a sin-offering.

The *gemara* resolves the contradiction through a tradition transmitted in a chain of authorities: Rav Shmuel bar Shilas in the name of Rav Hamnuna Saba, in the name of Rabbi Yitzchak bar Ashyan, in the name of Rav Huna, in the name of Rav — the case of the *tamid* involved an animal *shehevi'o milishkah she'eino mevukarim*, brought from a pen of animals that had not been pre-inspected. This was not an innocent error; it constituted genuine negligence. The *tamid* offering process was sufficiently structured that bringing an animal from an un-inspected pen reflects a failure of care that cannot be excused under the rubric of *toeh bidvar mitzvah*. The exemption covers genuine inadvertent error in the course of a *mitzvah*, but not errors that arise from one's own negligence in carrying out the requirements of the *mitzvah* properly. Whether the disqualifying factor is the negligence alone, or the combination of negligence and the failure to culminate in an actual *mitzvah*, the *halacha* is clear: in a case of *toeh bidvar mitzvah* that stems from the person's own carelessness, the exemption does not apply.

*Chazak.*
