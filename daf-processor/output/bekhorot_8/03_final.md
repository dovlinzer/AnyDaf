# Bekhorot 8 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Honey & Beeswax

### Honey: Exception or Definition?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דָּג טָמֵא — מַשְׁרִיץ, דָּג טָהוֹר — מֵטִיל בֵּיצִים, כָּל הַמּוֹלִיד — מֵנִיק, וְכׇל הַמֵּטִיל בֵּיצִים — מְלַקֵּט, חוּץ מֵעֲטַלֵּף שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמֵּטִיל בֵּיצִים — מֵנִיק.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** that **a non-kosher fish spawns** its offspring, while **a kosher fish lays eggs. Any** animal **that gives birth** to its offspring **nurses** them, **and any** animal **that lays eggs gathers** food and feeds it to its young. This applies to all animals **except for a bat [*atallef* ], as although it lays eggs, it nurses** its young.

The *daf* before us opens with a question that flows directly from the preceding discussion: what is the precise basis for the permissibility of bee honey? Two positions present themselves. The first holds that bee honey is permitted as a *gezerat hakatuv* — a scriptural decree that carves out an exception for this one product alone, leaving intact a broad rule that anything secreted by or emerging from an animal is forbidden. The second position maintains that the permissibility of honey reflects a definitional principle: only something that is genuinely *produced* by the animal in a meaningful sense is prohibited. Under this view, bee honey is not an exception to a general rule but rather a demonstration of what the rule actually encompasses.

The *Rambam* sides with the latter position. According to him, honey is permitted not because the *Torah* granted it a special dispensation, but because the relevant category — "produced by the animal" — must be defined narrowly. On the *Rambam*'s view, the permissibility extends not only to bee honey but to honey of other insects as well, since the principle itself is the determining factor, not an isolated scriptural exception.

### Royal Jelly

Royal jelly, the substance secreted by worker bees and fed to the queen, occupies a more complicated position. Unlike ordinary bee honey — which, under the *Rambam*'s framework, may not fall within the prohibited category at all — royal jelly is genuinely secreted and produced by the bee in a direct physiological sense. Accordingly, the consensus among *poskim* is that royal jelly is forbidden. It is worth noting that some authorities who hold the *gezerat hakatuv* view have suggested that the scriptural dispensation for honey might encompass royal jelly as well, since whatever the *Torah* permitted it permitted broadly. However, this remains a decidedly marginal position, and the operative assumption is that royal jelly is not permitted.

### Beeswax Permitted

Beeswax presents the next case. Like royal jelly, beeswax is directly secreted by the bee — and this is precisely what makes the question difficult. If the secretion of an insect is what renders a product problematic, beeswax should be forbidden. Yet incidental evidence from early sources suggests that beeswax was never treated as a concern, and many *poskim* accept its permissibility without hesitation.

The explanation rests on a distinction drawn from the closing passage of the *Gemara* discussed previously: certain substances produced by animals are classified as *pirsha* — waste or refuse — rather than food. The *Gemara* there noted that a placenta, despite being produced by the animal, is regarded as something closer to wood or refuse, not as nourishment. Beeswax falls squarely into this category. It has no taste, provides no nutrition, and — as Rav Moshe notes — is not even digested by the body; it passes through entirely. This combination of factors removes beeswax from any meaningful definition of "food," and therefore it does not trigger the prohibition.

### Shellac & Lac

This same reasoning extends to shellac, which is derived from the lac insect — a non-kosher species — and used as a coating to produce the familiar gloss on certain fruits and confectionery. Shellac is secreted by a non-kosher insect and, strictly speaking, should be problematic. Nevertheless, the general consensus among *poskim* is that it is permitted on the same grounds as beeswax: it has no taste, no smell, it is not nutritive, and it is not digested by the body. Although it is applied to food, its function is entirely cosmetic, and it does not meet the definition of a foodstuff in any halakhically meaningful sense. Some authorities maintain that ideally one should seek out products whose glazing derives from kosher sources, but the practical ruling is that shellac and similar coatings do not constitute a prohibition.

### Summary

To summarize the halakhic upshot of this entire discussion: the foundational question is whether the permissibility of bee honey reflects a narrow scriptural exception or a broader definitional principle governing what it means to be "produced" by an animal. That dispute has practical consequences — most notably for royal jelly, which everyone agrees is forbidden because it is genuinely secreted by the bee. For beeswax and shellac, the relevant distinction is not primarily about production but about the final character of the substance: things that are not food in any real sense, regardless of their origin, are not subject to the prohibition. The *Gemara*'s own discussion of *pirsha* supplies the conceptual framework for this result.

## Fish within Fish

### Mishna: Fish Swallowing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דָּג טָמֵא שֶׁבָּלַע דָּג טָהוֹר, מוּתָּר בַּאֲכִילָה, וְטָהוֹר שֶׁבָּלַע דָּג טָמֵא, אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ גִּידּוּלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a non-kosher fish that swallowed a kosher fish, consumption** of the kosher fish is **permitted. And** in the case of **a kosher** fish **that swallowed a non-kosher fish, consumption** of the non-kosher fish is **prohibited due to** the fact **that** the host fish **is not** the place of **its development.**

The *Mishnah* on 7b presents a direct application of the principle that not everything found inside an animal is defined by that animal. A non-kosher fish that has swallowed a kosher fish: the kosher fish found inside is permitted for consumption. Conversely, a kosher fish that has swallowed a non-kosher fish: the non-kosher fish found inside remains forbidden. The reason given is *she'eino gidulo* — it did not grow there, it was not produced there. It is simply present inside the host animal; it retains its own identity and is not transformed by its location.

### Seen Swallowing?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דַּחֲזֵינֵיהּ דִּבְלַע, הָא לָא חֲזֵינֵיהּ דִּבְלַע — אָמְרִי: אַשְׁרוֹצֵי אַשְׁרֵיץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers from the language of the mishna: In the case of a non-kosher fish that swallowed a kosher fish: **The reason** consumption of the kosher fish is permitted is **that we saw that** the non-kosher fish **swallowed** the kosher fish. **But** if **we did not see it swallow** the kosher fish **we say** that perhaps the non-kosher fish **spawned** the kosher fish, and it is forbidden.

The *Gemara* initially assumes that the *Mishnah* is describing a case where one actually witnessed the swallowing. On this reading, *taama dechazina debala* — the reason the ruling holds is because we saw it swallow. The implication would be that if one simply opened the fish and found a smaller fish inside, without having witnessed the act of swallowing, we might be concerned that the inner fish was actually spawned there — produced by the outer fish — and would therefore be governed by it. The *Gemara* then asks for the scriptural or textual basis for this distinction.

### Baraita: Spawning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: דָּג טָמֵא מַשְׁרִיץ, דָּג טָהוֹר מֵטִיל בֵּיצִים. אִי הָכִי, כִּי חָזֵינָא דִּבְלַע אָמְרִינַן: הַאי אִיעַכַּלָא, וְהַאי אַשְׁרוֹצֵי אַשְׁרֵיץ!
>
> **Translation:** **From where do we** derive that we must have this concern? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A non-kosher fish spawns** offspring, while **a kosher fish lays eggs** from which the offspring hatch. The Gemara challenges: **If so,** even **when we saw it swallowing** a kosher fish **we** should **say** that **that** fish that it swallowed **was digested, and this** one, which was found inside of the non-kosher fish, it **spawned,** and should be forbidden.

A *baraita* is cited: *dag tamei mashritz* — a non-kosher fish spawns its offspring internally — while a kosher fish lays eggs externally. The significance of this for the *Gemara*'s concern is now apparent: if non-kosher fish do indeed produce their young internally, then when one opens a non-kosher fish and finds a smaller fish inside, there is at least a theoretical basis for thinking the inner fish was spawned there rather than swallowed. The *Gemara* therefore asks: even in the case where we witnessed the swallowing, why do we not say that the swallowed kosher fish was digested and what we now find is the non-kosher fish's own spawn? The question cuts in both directions and calls for a more careful account of how we distinguish ingestion from production.

### Spawned Inside?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: כְּגוֹן שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ דֶּרֶךְ הָרֶיעִי. רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ דֶּרֶךְ בֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה. רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: כְּגוֹן שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ שָׁלֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says:** It is referring to a case **where** one **found** the kosher fish in **the digestive tract,** which is not where the non-kosher fish normally spawns its offspring. **Rav Pappa says** similarly: It is referring to a case **where** one **found** the kosher fish in **the passageway of the gullet. Rav Naḥman says:** It is referring to a case **where** one **found it whole** and in complete form, and not as a newly hatched fish.

The *Gemara* raises the objection sharply: even when we saw the fish swallow, perhaps what appears inside is not the swallowed fish at all but rather the non-kosher fish's own progeny. Three *Amoraim* offer answers rooted in physical location and appearance. Rav Sheshet says the case involves finding the inner fish along the digestive tract — *derech hare'i* — which clearly indicates it was ingested and is being processed, not spawned. Rav Papa offers a parallel answer: one found the fish in the region of the throat — *beit habli'ah* — again indicating ingestion. Rav Nachman proposes that one found the inner fish whole and intact; a fish in the process of natural development inside the body would not yet be in such a formed state.

### Amoraim Answers

These answers share a common approach: they restrict the *Mishnah*'s ruling to circumstances where the physical evidence itself establishes that the inner fish was swallowed rather than spawned. Each answer, in its own way, attempts to preserve the *Gemara*'s initial assumption that some form of verification is required.

### Rav Ashi: Own Kind

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: רוֹב דָּגִים בְּמִינָן מַשְׁרִיצִין, וּכְמִי שֶׁבָּלַע לְפָנֵינוּ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** Do not infer from the mishna that it is referring to a case where the non-kosher fish was seen swallowing the kosher fish. Rather, the reason it is permitted to consume the swallowed fish is that **the majority of fish spawn** offspring **of their** own **species, and** therefore the case of a kosher fish discovered inside a non-kosher fish **is considered as if** the non-kosher fish had **swallowed** the kosher fish **in our presence.**

Rav Ashi's answer is decisive and general. He states: *rov dagim beminom mashritzin* — the vast majority of fish spawn their own kind. Even granting the *Gemara*'s broader cosmological assumption that unusual cross-species births are theoretically possible, they remain the rare exception. If one opens a non-kosher fish and finds a kosher fish inside, the straightforward inference is that it was swallowed. We may treat the situation *uchenish shebalua lefaneinu dami* — as though we had seen it swallow before our eyes. No actual observation is required. This is the answer that resolves the earlier assumption and establishes the ruling of the *Mishnah* on firm ground: the inner fish retains its own identity because, as a matter of statistical reality, it belongs to a different species and therefore must have come from outside.

## Reproduction & Gestation

### Fish: Internal vs. External

The *Gemara* now transitions into a sustained *baraita*-based discussion of animal reproduction and physiology. The *baraita* repeats the distinction already encountered: *dag tamei mashritz* — non-kosher fish spawn internally — while *dag tahor metil beitzim* — kosher fish lay eggs externally.

### Birth vs. Eggs

The *baraita* then articulates a general rule: *kol hamolid menika* — any creature that gives birth to live young also nurses those young — and *kol hametil beitzim melaket* — any creature that lays eggs does not produce milk but gathers food for its offspring. The correspondence is intuitive: live birth and nursing belong together as characteristics of the same reproductive strategy, as do egg-laying and food-gathering.

### Bat Exception

The *baraita* immediately introduces an exception: *chutz me'atalef* — except for the bat. The bat, it says, lays eggs yet nurses. This assertion is zoologically problematic. Bats are mammals, and the only egg-laying mammals known to science are the platypus and the several species of echidna, all native to Australia. The *Gemara*'s identification of the bat as an egg-laying nurturer does not correspond to contemporary zoological understanding, and this is one of several points in this *daf* where the ancient natural-historical record diverges from what modern observation would establish.

### Dolphins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַדּוֹלְפָנִין פָּרִין וְרָבִין כִּבְנֵי אָדָם. מַאי דּוֹלְפָנִין? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: בְּנֵי יַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **The *dulfanin* reproduce like people.** The Gemara asks: **What are *dulfanin*? Rav Yehuda says:** They are creatures that are called **sons of the sea.**

The *baraita* continues: *hadolfanim parin veravin* — dolphins procreate and multiply. The key textual variant here is significant. Our standard text reads *kibnei adam* — like human beings — meaning that dolphins procreate and nurse in the manner of humans, which is accurate: dolphins are mammals that nurse their young. However, Rashi and Tosafot have a different *girsa*: *mibnei adam* — from human beings — implying that dolphins can actually produce offspring with humans. This is not supported by biology, and an examination of the *Tosefta*, where this passage originates, consistently shows the text *kibnei adam* — like humans, not from humans. Tosafot's claim that their version reflects the *Tosefta*'s text does not appear to be corroborated by the surviving manuscripts of the *Tosefta*.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai dolfinin* — what exactly is meant by dolphins? Rav Yehuda answers: *bnei yama* — children of the sea, or perhaps more broadly, sea mammals.

### Male Organ Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁבֵּיצָיו מִבַּחוּץ — מוֹלִיד, וְכֹל שֶׁבִּפְנִים — מֵטִיל בֵּיצִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: In the case of **any** male animal **whose testicles are external,** the female **gives birth** to live offspring, **and** in the case of **any** male animal **whose testicles are internal,** the female **lays eggs.**

The *baraita* then offers a general anatomical principle: *kol shebeitzav mibachutz molid* — any animal whose testes are on the outside gives birth to live young — *vekol shebifnim matil beitzim* — and any animal whose testes are internal lays eggs. The *Gemara* immediately challenges this.

### Geese & Ducks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲוָוז וַאֲוָוז בָּר כִּלְאַיִם זֶה בָּזֶה, וְהָוֵינַן בָּהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: זֶה בֵּיצָיו מִבַּחוּץ, וְזֶה בֵּיצָיו מִבִּפְנִים, וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ מְטִילֵי בֵּיצִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Shmuel say** that a domestic **goose and a wild goose are** considered **diverse kinds,** and one may not mate them **with each other. And we discussed it: What is the reason? Abaye said:** In the case of **this** one, the male wild goose, **its testicles are external, and** in the case of **that** one, the domestic goose, **its testicles are internal.** The Gemara comments: **And** yet **both** geese **lay eggs.** Evidently, the fact that the male's testicles are external does not prove that the female gives birth.

The challenge comes from a ruling cited in the name of Shmuel: a domestic goose and a wild goose are considered *kilayim* with respect to one another — they are prohibited from cross-breeding as distinct species. When this ruling was previously discussed, Abaye had explained the distinction: one has its testes externally and the other internally, which suffices to define them as separate species. But the *Gemara* now points out the difficulty — *u'travaihu matilei beitzim* — both of them lay eggs. How then can the anatomical distinction between internal and external testes correlate with the distinction between live birth and egg-laying? The *Gemara* therefore refines the principle: the relevant feature is not the testes but the male reproductive organ. *Kol shezachruso mibachutz molid* — any animal whose male organ is external gives live birth — and if it is internal, the animal lays eggs. Geese and ducks, it turns out, do indeed have the male organ situated internally, though it extends outward during mating.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, כֹּל שֶׁזַּכְרוּתוֹ מִבַּחוּץ — מוֹלִיד, מִבִּפְנִים — מֵטִיל בֵּיצִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* must mean the following: With regard to **any** animal **whose male** reproductive **organ** is **external,** the female **gives birth,** and in the case of any male animal whose reproductive organ is **internal,** the female **lays eggs.** Although the testicles of the male wild goose are external, its reproductive organ is internal.

## Copulation Timing

### Day Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם — יוֹלֵד בַּיּוֹם, בַּלַּיְלָה — יוֹלֵד בַּלַּיְלָה, כֹּל שֶׁתַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בֵּין בַּיּוֹם בֵּין בַּלַּיְלָה — יוֹלֵד בֵּין בַּיּוֹם בֵּין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* continues to discuss matters of animal procreation: **Any** species **whose sexual intercourse** occurs only **during the daytime gives birth** only **during the daytime;** any species whose intercourse occurs only **at night gives birth** only **at night; any** species **whose intercourse** occurs **both during the daytime and at night gives birth both during the daytime and at night.**

The *baraita* turns to a further generalization: *kol shetashmisho bayom yoled bayom* — any species that copulates exclusively during the day gives birth during the day.

### Night Birth

Correspondingly, *balaila yoled balaila* — any species that copulates at night gives birth at night.

### Any Time Birth

And *kol shetashmisho bein bayom bein balaila yoled bein bayom bein balaila* — any species that copulates day or night gives birth at any time.

### Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בַּיּוֹם יוֹלֵד בַּיּוֹם — תַּרְנְגוֹל, בַּלַּיְלָה יוֹלֵד בַּלַּיְלָה — עֲטַלֵּף, תַּשְׁמִישׁוֹ בֵּין בַּיּוֹם בֵּין בַּלַּיְלָה יוֹלֵד בֵּין בַּיּוֹם בֵּין בַּלַּיְלָה — אָדָם וְכֹל דְּדָמֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: The statement that any species whose **intercourse** occurs **during the daytime gives birth during the daytime** is referring to **a chicken.** The statement that any species whose intercourse occurs **at night gives birth at night** is referring to **a bat.** The statement that any species whose **intercourse** occurs **both during the daytime and at night gives birth both during the daytime and at night** is referring to **a human and any** creature **that is similar to him.**

The *baraita* identifies the three categories by example. Roosters and hens are daytime copulators and therefore lay during the day only. The bat, being nocturnal, copulates and gives birth at night. Humans may copulate at any hour and therefore give birth at any hour — *adam* — and *vechol dedami lei*, and any species similarly unrestricted in its timing.

## Yom Tov Egg Case

### Egg Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְכִדְרַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: בָּדַק בְּקִינָּה שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלִין מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְלֹא מָצָא בָּהּ בֵּיצָה, וּלְמָחָר הִשְׁכִּים וּמָצָא בָהּ בֵּיצָה — מוּתֶּרֶת בַּאֲכִילָה בְּיוֹם טוֹב, אֵימַר: לֹא בָּדַק יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** halakhic **difference is** there whether an animal gives birth during the daytime or at night? The Gemara answers: The difference is **with regard to that which Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana,** said. **As Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, said:** If one **examined a chicken's nest on a Festival eve and did not find an egg in it, and the following day,** on the Festival, **he rose early,** before dawn, **and found an egg in it, consumption** of the egg is **permitted on the Festival.** It is not considered an egg that was laid on the Festival, which is forbidden (see *Beitza* 2a), as chickens do not lay eggs at night. Although he examined the nest before the Festival and failed to find an egg there, one is compelled to **say** that **he did not examine** the nest **carefully.**

The *Gemara* asks: *lemai nafkamina* — what is the practical halakhic implication of knowing that hens lay only during the day? The answer comes from a ruling of Rav Mari the son of Rav Kahana. The case: one checked the hen coop on the eve of *Yom Tov* and found no egg. The following morning — before daybreak, on *Yom Tov* itself — one checked again and found an egg.

### Hens Day-Only

The ruling is *muteret be'achila be'Yom Tov* — it may be eaten on *Yom Tov*. The concern that would otherwise apply is *nolad* — an object that came into existence on *Yom Tov* is *muktzah* and forbidden for use. If the egg was laid at night, it would have been produced during *Yom Tov* and would be *nolad*. However, since hens lay only during the day, the egg cannot have been produced during the night of *Yom Tov*. Therefore it must have been laid before *Yom Tov* began, and it is permitted.

### Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא בָּדַק יָפֶה! אֵימַר: יָצְתָה רוּבָּהּ וְחָזְרָה הֲוָה, וְכִדְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֵּיצָה שֶׁיָּצְתָה רוּבָּהּ מֵעֶרֶב יוֹם טוֹב וְחוֹזֶרֶת — מְוֻתֶּרֶת לְאוֹכְלָהּ בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But he did examine** the nest **carefully,** as the *baraita* states that he examined it. The Gemara explains: One must **say** that this **was** a case where **most of** the egg **emerged** on the eve of the Festival **and returned** inside its mother before the examination. **And** this ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case where **most of the egg emerged on the eve of the Festival and returned** inside its mother it is **permitted to eat it on the Festival,** as once most of it emerged it is considered to have been laid already.

The *Gemara* presses: but you checked and found nothing — how can you say it was laid before *Yom Tov*?

### Re-absorption Theory

The *Gemara* answers: *eimur yatza rubo v'chazar havei* — one must say that the majority of the egg emerged before *Yom Tov* and then the egg was re-absorbed. The halakhic principle here is supplied by Rav Yochanan: *beitza sheyatza rubah me'erev Yom Tov vechazra muteret be'achila be'Yom Tov* — an egg whose majority emerged before *Yom Tov* and then retreated is considered to have been substantially laid before *Yom Tov*, and is therefore permitted on *Yom Tov*. Since we have established that hens cannot lay at night, and since one found the egg in the morning, the only available inference is that the majority emerged before *Yom Tov* and was re-absorbed when the coop was checked — the hen simply had not yet fully discharged the egg. This is an example of a case where the scientific premise shapes the halakhic conclusion: the re-absorption theory is invoked because the alternative — that a hen laid the egg at night — is ruled out on natural-historical grounds.

### Tosafot Ruling

Tosafot, in the brief comment at the top of the page — *eimur yatza rubo v'chazar* — draws out a practical ruling from this *Gemara*. *Mikan matirim liknot beitzim b'leil sheni shel Yom Tov shel galuyot*: from here it is permitted to acquire eggs on the second night of *Yom Tov* of the *diaspora*. If one goes to the coop on the second night of *Yom Tov* and finds an egg, one may assume it was laid during the first day of *Yom Tov* — since hens do not lay at night — and an egg laid on the first day of *Yom Tov* is permitted on the second day. This is a striking application of a scientific generalization to a concrete *Yom Tov* ruling, and Tosafot accepts it *le'halacha*.

## Gestation Parallels

### Hens 21 Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: תַּרְנְגוֹלֶת — לְעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד יוֹם, וּכְנֶגְדָּהּ בָּאִילָן — לוּז, כֶּלֶב — לַחֲמִשִּׁים יוֹם, וּכְנֶגְדּוֹ בָּאִילָן — תְּאֵינָה, חָתוּל — לַחֲמִשִּׁים וּשְׁנַיִם יוֹם, וּכְנֶגְדּוֹ בָּאִילָן — תּוּת, חֲזִיר — לְשִׁשִּׁים יוֹם, כְּנֶגְדּוֹ בָּאִילָן — תַּפּוּחַ, שׁוּעָל וְכׇל מִינֵי שְׁרָצִים — שִׁשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים, וּכְנֶגְדָּם בָּאִילָן — תְּבוּאָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a *baraita* that discusses the length of gestation for various animals. **The Sages taught** that **a chicken** hatches **after twenty-one days, and corresponding to it** in length of gestation **with regard to trees is the almond,** which ripens twenty-one days after the budding of the flower. **A dog** gives birth **after fifty days, and corresponding to it with regard to trees is the fig. A cat** gives birth **after fifty-two days, and corresponding to it with regard to trees is the mulberry. A pig** gives birth **after sixty days,** and **corresponding to it with regard to trees is the apple. A fox and all types of creeping animals** give birth after **six months, and corresponding to them with regard to trees,** i.e., plants, **is grain.**

The *baraita* now catalogues gestation and incubation periods across species and pairs each animal with a corresponding plant whose ripening cycle runs for the same duration. Hens incubate for 21 days. The parallel plant is *luz* — identified by Rashi as a hazelnut tree and by Tosafot as possibly an almond; the *amigdoli*, almond, was Tosafot's alternative suggestion.

### Dogs 50 Days

Dogs gestate for 50 days. The parallel is the fig tree — *te'enah* — which takes approximately 50 days from blossoming to bearing fruit. The reason the *baraita* draws these parallels is not entirely clear; the *Gemara* does not explain the purpose of the comparison, though the ultimate function will become apparent when the serpent is reached — the one creature for which no parallel exists.

### Cats 52 Days

Cats gestate for 52 days. The parallel is *tut* — berry or mulberry — described here as a tree. Tosafot raises the question prompted by this identification: should one recite *borei pri ha'etz* on berries, since the *baraita* calls *tut* a tree? Tosafot answers that no proof can be drawn from here, since the *baraita* is also about to refer to grain — *tevuah* — as a tree; clearly "tree" in this context is used loosely to mean any plant. Tosafot then distinguishes: berries that grow on actual trees do require *borei pri ha'etz*, but *tutei sadeh* — field berries, such as strawberries — do not, and *borei pri ha'adama* is recited over them.

### Pigs 60 Days

Pigs gestate for 60 days. The parallel is the apple tree — *tapuach* — whose fruit takes approximately 60 days from blossoming to completion.

### Foxes 6 Months

Foxes and all types of *sheratzim* — reptiles — gestate for six months. The parallel is grain — *tevuah* — which runs from planting around *Sukkot* to harvest around *Pesach*, a period of approximately six months. The *baraita*'s use of *tevuah* as a parallel "tree" is, as noted, imprecise, but Tosafot's observation already addressed this.

### Sheep/Goat 5 Months

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּהֵמָה דַּקָּה טְהוֹרָה — לַחֲמִשָּׁה חֳדָשִׁים, וּכְנֶגְדָּן בָּאִילָן — גֶּפֶן. בְּהֵמָה גַּסָּה טְמֵאָה — לִשְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חוֹדֶשׁ, וּכְנֶגְדּוֹ בָּאִילָן — דֶּקֶל. טְהוֹרָה — לְתִשְׁעָה חֳדָשִׁים, וּכְנֶגְדָּהּ בָּאִילָן — זַיִת. הַזְּאֵב וְהָאֲרִי וְהַדּוֹב וְהַנָּמֵר וְהַבַּרְדְּלָס וְהַפִּיל וְהַקּוֹף וְהַקִּיפוֹף — לְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, וּכְנֶגְדָּן בָּאִילָן — בְּנוֹת שׁוּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Small kosher livestock,** such as sheep or goats, give birth **after five months, and corresponding to them with regard to trees is the grapevine. Large non-kosher livestock,** such as camels or donkeys, give birth **after twelve months, and corresponding to them with regard to trees is the date palm.** Large **kosher** livestock, such as cows, give birth **after nine months, and corresponding to them with regard to trees is the olive. The wolf, and the lion, and the bear, and the leopard, and the *bardelas*, and the elephant, and the monkey, and the long-tailed ape** give birth **after three years, and corresponding to them with regard to trees is the white fig.**

Kosher small animals — *behema daka tehora*, such as sheep and goat — gestate for five months. The parallel is the vine — *gefen* — with grapes taking approximately five months from flowering to harvest.

### Donkey 12 Months

Large non-kosher animals — *behema gassa tme'ah*, the classic example being the donkey — gestate for twelve months. The parallel is the date palm — *dekel* — which takes twelve months for the new dates to develop and ripen.

### Cow 9 Months

Large kosher animals — *behema gassa tehora* — such as cows, gestate for nine months. The parallel is the olive tree — *zayit*.

### Predators 3 Years

Finally, the major predators: the wolf, lion, bear, *namer*, *bardelas*, elephant, monkey, and *kipof* — three years. The parallel plant is *bnot shuach*, a category of trees whose identification is uncertain, though some suggest mulberries. The precise factual accuracy of the three-year figure for all these species is questionable, but the *baraita* presents it as a unified category.

## Snake's Curse

### Adder 70 Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶפְעֶה לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, וּכְנֶגְדּוֹ בָּאִילָן — חָרוּב. חָרוּב זֶה, מִשְּׁעַת נְטִיעָתוֹ עַד שְׁעַת גְּמַר פֵּירוֹתָיו — שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה, וִימֵי עִיבּוּרוֹ שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים. נָחָשׁ לְשֶׁבַע שָׁנִים, וּלְאוֹתוֹ רָשָׁע לֹא מָצִינוּ חָבֵר. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: מוֹכְסָסִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **A viper** gives birth **after seventy years, and corresponding to it with regard to trees is the carob.** In the case of **this carob,** the period **from the time of its planting until the time of the ripening of its fruit is seventy years, and the length of its gestation is three years. A snake** is born **after seven years, and for that wicked** animal **we have not found a counterpart** among trees. **And some say** that ***mukhsasim*** are the equivalent, as they ripen after seven years.

The *baraita* continues with the *ef'eh* — the adder — and states that it takes seventy years. The *Gemara* clarifies: this does not mean seventy years of gestation inside the body, but rather that the adder does not begin giving birth until it is seventy years old. The parallel in the plant world is the carob tree — *charuv* — which famously takes seventy years from planting before it produces its first fruit. Once it does begin to produce, the *baraita* says that *chamur ze mishe'as netiyaso ad she'as gemar peirosav shivim shana* — seventy years from planting to first fruit — while *yemei ibburo shalosh shanim* — the gestation period itself, once it begins, is three years.

### Snake 7 Years

The serpent — *nachash* — gestates for seven years. And *ule'oso rasha lo matzinu chaver* — for that wicked creature, no parallel can be found. This may well be the entire purpose of the preceding catalogue: to establish by contrast that the snake alone stands outside the order of nature, with no corresponding plant or phenomenon. The curse of the snake expressed itself, among other ways, in a gestation uniquely prolonged, uniquely unparalleled.

### Source Curse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, וּמָטוּ בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָרוּר אַתָּה מִכׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה וּמִכֹּל חַיַּת הַשָּׂדֶה״. אִם מִבְּהֵמָה נִתְקַלְּלָה, מֵחַיָּה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where is this matter,** that the gestation period of a snake is seven years, derived? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says, and** some **determined it** to be **in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya:** It is derived from **that** which **is stated** with regard to the punishment of the snake for causing Adam and Eve to sin: **"Cursed are you from among all animals, and from among all beasts of the field"** (Genesis 3:14), in that your gestation period should be longer than all of them. Now, why does the verse mention the beasts of the field, i.e., undomesticated animals? **If** the snake **was cursed** more **than the domesticated animals,** then **all the more so** is it **not** clear that it was cursed more **than the undomesticated animals?** The shortest gestation period of domesticated animals, which is five months for a goat, is longer than the shortest gestation period among undomesticated animals, which is fifty-two days for a cat.

The *Gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — where do we derive that a snake gestates for seven years? The answer comes in the name of Rav Yehuda citing Rav, transmitted through Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya, based on the verse in Bereshit: *arur ata mikol habeheima umikol chayas hasadeh* — cursed are you from all the *beheima* and from all the *chaya* of the field. The assumption underlying the derivation is that since Chava's curse concerned childbirth and pregnancy, the snake's curse is similarly understood to bear on its reproductive duration — the period of gestation is the expression of the curse.

### Ratio Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, לוֹמַר לָךְ: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּתְקַלְּלָה הַבְּהֵמָה מֵחַיָּה, אֶחָד לְשִׁבְעָה, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? חֲמוֹר מֵחָתוּל, כָּךְ נִתְקַלֵּל הוּא מִבְּהֵמָה, אַחַת לְשֶׁבַע, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ שַׁב שְׁנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the verse mentions the beasts of the field **to tell you: Just as the domesticated animals were cursed** more **than the undomesticated animals by** a proportion of **one to seven;** the Gemara interjects: **And** with regard to **what** case **is that?** It is with regard to **a donkey,** whose gestation period as mentioned is twelve months, compared **to a cat,** whose gestation period is fifty-two days. Seven times longer than fifty-two days is 364 days, which means that the gestation period of the donkey, which is one year, is almost exactly seven times longer than that of the cat. Rav Yehuda continues: **So too,** the snake **was cursed** more **than domesticated animals,** i.e., the donkey, in a proportion of **one to seven, which is** a total of **seven years.**

The derivation proceeds as follows. The verse says the snake is cursed *from all the beheima and from all the chaya*. The longest-gestating *beheima* is the donkey at twelve months — approximately 365 days. The shortest-gestating *chaya* class would be the cat at approximately 52 days. The ratio of 365 to 52 is approximately seven to one. The *baraita* proposes: just as the *beheima* gestates seven times as long as the *chaya* — specifically, a donkey compared to a cat — so too the snake was cursed to gestate seven times as long as the longest *beheima*, which is seven times twelve months, yielding seven years.

### 1:3 vs 1:7

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּתְקַלְּלָה חַיָּה מִבְּהֵמָה, אַחַת לְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? אֲרִי מֵחֲמוֹר, כָּךְ נִתְקַלֵּל הוּא מֵחַיָּה אַחַת לְשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Say** that the verse can be interpreted as follows: **Just as the undomesticated animals were cursed** more **than the domesticated animals** by a measure of **one** year **to three years;** the Gemara interjects: **And** in **what** case **is that?** It is in the case of **a lion,** whose gestation period is three years, compared **to a donkey,** whose gestation period is one year. The Gemara continues its challenge: **So too,** the snake **was cursed** more **than the undomesticated animals,** i.e., the lion, by a proportion of **one** year **to three years, which is nine years.**

The *Gemara* challenges this derivation. Why must the ratio be seven to one? There is also a three-to-one ratio available: a goat gestates for five months and a cat for approximately 52 days — roughly a three-to-one relationship. And among the *chaya* category, the lion gestates for three years, while the donkey gestates for one year — also approximately a three-to-one ratio. One could therefore argue the snake should gestate three times three years, yielding nine years, which would actually represent a *greater* curse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי כְּתִיב ״מִכׇּל הַחַיָּה וּמִכׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה״? ״מִכׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה וּמִכֹּל הַחַיָּה״ כְּתִיב! אָרוּר הוּא מִבְּהֵמָה שֶׁנִּתְקַלְּלָה מֵחַיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Is it written:** Cursed are you **from among all beasts, and from among all animals?** No, **it is written: "From among all animals, and from among all beasts,"** meaning that **it is cursed from among the domesticated animals, which were cursed from among the undomesticated animals.**

The *Gemara* offers two responses. *Ibeis eima mikol habeheima kesiv* — the verse says "from all the *beheima*," implying the most extreme comparison, which points to the seven-to-one ratio. *Ve'ibeis eima klala hu, klala shadi lei* — alternatively, when interpreting a curse, one interprets it to produce the largest curse, and the seven-to-one reading from donkey to cat, yielding seven years, is identified as the maximum.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁנִּתְקַלְּלָה בְּהֵמָה מֵחַיָּה אֶחָד לְשָׁלֹשׁ, וּמַאי נִיהוּ? עֵז מֵחָתוּל, כָּךְ נִתְקַלֵּל הוּא מִבְּהֵמָה אֶחָד לְשָׁלֹשׁ, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ חֲמֵיסַר יַרְחֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: Why must it mean that the snake was cursed more than the donkey in the same proportion that the donkey was cursed more than the cat? **But say** instead that **just as the domesticated animals were cursed** more **than the undomesticated animals** by a measure of **one to three;** the Gemara interjects: **And** in **what** case **is that?** It is in the case of **a goat,** whose gestation period is five months, compared **to a cat,** whose gestation period is fifty-two days. The Gemara continues its challenge: **So too,** the snake **was cursed** more **than the domesticated animals,** i.e., the goat, by a proportion of **one to three, which is fifteen months.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: ״מִכׇּל הַבְּהֵמָה״ כְּתִיב. אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: קְלָלָה הוּא, קְלָלָה שְׁדִי עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that **it is written:** "Cursed are you **from among all animals,"** including the most accursed of them, i.e., the donkey. **If you wish, say** instead that since **it is a curse** that the snake received here, the verse **imposes upon it** the most extreme **curse** that can be derived from the text.

### Curse Discussion

This passage naturally invites reflection on the nature of the curses recorded in Bereshit. The verse reads: *harba arbeh itzvonech veheronech, be'etzev teldi vanim* — I will greatly increase your pain and your pregnancy; in pain you shall bear children. The *Gemara* and the *baraita* appear to read the curse of woman as concerning duration — the length of pregnancy — as well as pain. One might ask whether it is theologically appropriate to speak of pregnancy itself as a punishment. An insightful reading, apparently not from a named Talmudic source but from more recent commentary, suggests that the pain of childbirth is not a punishment *per se* but a natural consequence of the choice to eat from the *Etz HaDa'at*: increased knowledge correlates with larger human brains, which in turn make childbirth more physically demanding. The consequence flows from the choice, but it is not an arbitrary punitive imposition.

On a related note, the Catholic Church has historically opposed pain relief during childbirth on the grounds that the curse mandates suffering, and one might wonder whether a similar argument could be made regarding other instances of mitigating natural hardship. Rav Soloveitchik, however, addressed this directly: there is no *mitzvah* to live under a curse. On the contrary, our obligation in this world is to transcend the curses — to deploy human knowledge and capacity not to accept the curse as the permanent order of things but to negate it wherever possible.

## Copulation & Pregnancy

### Caesar's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ קֵיסָר לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: נָחָשׁ לְכַמָּה מִיעַבַּר וּמוֹלֵיד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְשַׁב שְׁנֵי. וְהָא סָבֵי דְּבֵי אַתּוּנָא אַרְבְּעִינְהוּ, וְאוֹלִיד לִתְלָת! הָנְהוּ מִיעַבְּרִי הֲווֹ מֵעִיקָּרָא אַרְבַּע. וְהָא קָמְשַׁמְּשִׁי שַׁמּוֹשֵׁי! אִינְהוּ נָמֵי מְשַׁמְּשִׁי כְּאָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the gestation time of a snake, the Gemara relates that **the** Roman **emperor said to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya:** In the case of **a snake, after how long** a period of **gestation does it give birth?** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to him: After seven years.** The emperor said to him: **But the elders,** i.e., the sages, **of the school of Athens bred** snakes **and they gave birth after three** years. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya responded: **Those** snakes **were** already **pregnant from beforehand** for **four** years. The emperor asked: **But they engaged in intercourse,** and animals do so only in order to give birth, not when they are already pregnant. Rabbi Yehoshua responded: Snakes **also engage in intercourse like people,** i.e., they do not do so solely for reproduction.

The *Gemara* returns to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya and the claim that snakes gestate for seven years. Caesar poses the question directly to him: how long do snakes take to gestate and give birth? Rabbi Yehoshua answers: seven years, as established.

### Athens Experiment

Caesar replies: but the elders of Athens bred snakes — *arbi'unu* — and those snakes gave birth in three years, not seven. This is presented as an empirical refutation: controlled breeding conducted by the wise men of Athens produced results inconsistent with Rabbi Yehoshua's claim.

### Already Pregnant

Rabbi Yehoshua's response is immediate and precise: those snakes must have already been four years pregnant when the Athenians began breeding them. The Athenians did not know this; they simply introduced males and females. But the females had already been carrying young for four years, so three more years brought them to the full seven-year term. The empirical observation is entirely consistent with the tradition — the Athenians simply did not control for prior pregnancy.

### Like Humans

But the *Gemara* raises the obvious objection: if the snakes were already pregnant, why were they copulating? Normally animals do not mate when already pregnant. Rabbi Yehoshua's answer: *inhu nami ke-vnei adam meshamshei tolda* — snakes, like humans, engage in intercourse not solely for the purpose of procreation. The *Gemara* earlier noted that dolphins and monkeys share this characteristic with humans. Snakes, it is now said, are similarly exceptional in this regard. This resolution simultaneously preserves the seven-year tradition against the Athenian experiment and integrates the snake's unusual sexual behavior as part of what sets it apart from other animals.

## Athens Story Part 1

### Caesar Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא חַכִּימֵי אִינְהוּ? אֲנַן חֲכִימִינַן מִינַּיְיהוּ. אִי חֲכִימַתְּ, זִיל זַכִּינְהוּ וְאַיְתִינְהוּ לִי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כַּמָּה הָווּ? שִׁיתִּין גַּבְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The emperor said to him: **But** how can you disagree with the sages of Athens? **Aren't they wise?** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya responded: **We are wiser than they.** The emperor said: **If you are wiser** than they, then **go defeat them** in debate **and bring them to me.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: How many are there?** The emperor answered: **Sixty men.**

The passage continues with an extended narrative in which Caesar challenges Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya to defeat the elders of Athens in a contest of wisdom and bring them back. The framing is significant: the wisdom of Athens — characterized by empirical inquiry and testing — is being set against the wisdom of Judea, characterized by Torah-derived knowledge and tradition. The outcome of their contest is structured to demonstrate the superiority of the latter.

### Boat Request

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עֲבֵיד לִי סְפִינְתָּא דְּאִית בַּהּ שִׁיתִּין בָּתֵּי, וְכֹל בֵּיתָא אִית בַּהּ שִׁיתִּין בִּיסְתַּרְקֵי. עֲבַד לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya **said to him: Construct a ship that has sixty rooms for me, and each room** should **have sixty mattresses [*bistarkei*] in it.** The emperor **constructed** it **for him.**

Rabbi Yehoshua agrees, but makes an unusual request: build me a boat with sixty compartments, each containing sixty cushions or chairs. Caesar complies. The significance of this request becomes clear only at the end of the episode.

### Butcher Trick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא לְהָתָם לְבֵי טַבָּחָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְהָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּקָא פָשֵׁיט חֵיוְתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רֵישָׁךְ לְזַבּוֹנֵי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּכַמָּה? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בְּפַלְגָא דְּזוּזָא. יְהַב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua then set out on the ship for Athens. **When** he **arrived there,** he entered **a butcher shop** and **found a certain man,** the butcher, **flaying an animal.** Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: Is your head for sale?** The butcher **said to him: Yes** it is. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: For how much** is it being sold? The butcher **said to him: For half a dinar.** Rabbi Yehoshua **gave him** the money.

Arriving in the vicinity of Athens, Rabbi Yehoshua goes to a butcher whom he finds skinning an animal. He says to the butcher: *reishach le'zvonei* — how much to buy your head? The butcher understands this as a request to buy the head of the animal, quotes a price of half a *zuz*, and receives payment. Rabbi Yehoshua then clarifies: he said *your* head, not the animal's head. By accepting payment, the butcher has sold his own head — and with it, his life — for half a *zuz*.

### Hidden Entrance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא רֵישָׁא דְּחֵיוְתָא אֲמַרִי לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי בָּעֵית דְּאֶשְׁבְּקָךְ סַגִּי אַחְוִי לִי פִּיתְחָא דְּבֵי אַתּוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Rabbi Yehoshua paid the butcher, **he said to him:** Did **I say to you** that I wanted **the head of the animal?** I was referring to your head, and you must now keep your word and give me your head. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: If you wish for me to let you be, go** and **show me the entrance to the school of** the sages of **Athens.**

Rabbi Yehoshua then leverages this ownership. If you want me to release you from this obligation, he tells the butcher, show me the entrance to the city of Athens. The city was fortified and hidden; its entrance was not publicly known and no one was permitted to reveal it.

### Guards System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִסְתְּפֵינָא דְּכֹל דְּמַחְוֵי קָטְלִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּרִי כְּרִיכָא דִּקְנֵיא, וְכִי מָטֵית לְהָתָם זִקְפֵהּ כְּמַאן דְּקָא מִתְּפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The butcher **said to him: I am afraid, as they kill anyone who shows** its location to another. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to him: Carry a bundle of reeds, and when you arrive there, stand it up like one who is resting,** to mark the location.

The butcher is understandably terrified. He explains: *mistafina dechol d'machvei katlei lei* — anyone who reveals the entrance is put to death. Rabbi Yehoshua instructs him to carry a load of wood to the entrance, and when he arrives, to set it down as if resting — *keman d'kamnapash* — and that action will signal to Rabbi Yehoshua the location of the entrance. The butcher does so.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכַּח דַּרְבְּנָאֵי מִגַּוַּאי וְדַרְבְּנָאֵי מִבָּרַאי, דְּאִי חָזוּ כַּרְעָא דְּעָיְילָא — קָטְלִי לְהוּ לְבָרַאי, וּדְנָפְקָא — קָטְלִי לְהוּ לְגַוַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **found guards** stationed **on the inside and guards** stationed **on the outside** to ensure that no one could enter or exit. They also spread sand on the ground in the entranceway so that they could detect if anyone entered or left. **If they saw footsteps that were entering** they would **kill the outer** guards for allowing people to enter, **and** if they saw footsteps **that were exiting** they would **kill the inner** guards for allowing people to leave.

Rabbi Yehoshua then surveys the scene and discovers that the city has guards posted both inside and outside. The system was designed with harsh accountability: if footprints in the sand or sawdust near the entrance revealed that someone had entered without being detected, the outer guards were executed for negligence; if footprints showed someone had exited, the inner guards were executed.

### Backwards Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַפְכֵהּ לְסַנְדָּלֵיהּ — קָטְלִי לְהוּ לְגַוָּאֵי, אַפְכֵהּ לְסַנְדָּלֵיהּ — קָטְלִי לְהוּ לְכוּלְּהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **reversed his sandal** so it was facing away from the entrance, walked on the sand, and snuck away, thereby creating the appearance of someone who had left the building. When the authorities saw the footsteps, **they killed the inner** guards. Rabbi Yehoshua then returned, **reversed his sandal,** and made footsteps in the sand indicating that someone had entered the building. They then **killed all** the guards, including the outer ones, and Rabbi Yehoshua succeeded in entering the building.

Rabbi Yehoshua's solution is elegant. He approaches the entrance with his sandals on backwards. Standing in the doorway, his footprints pointed inward — as if someone had come from inside the city and passed through outward — and the inner guards were killed for apparently having allowed an exit. He then reversed his sandals, and by some further maneuver created the impression of unauthorized entry, so that the outer guards were killed as well. With all the guards eliminated — and no time for replacements — he was able to enter the city.

### Young Above, Old Below

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכַּח יָנוֹקֵי מִלְּעֵיל, סָבֵי מִלְּתַחַת. אֲמַר: אִי יָהֵיבְנָא שְׁלָמָא לְהָנֵי, קָטְלִי לִי הָנֵי, סָבְרִי: אֲנַן עֲדִיפִינַן, דַּאֲנַן קַשִּׁינַן טְפֵי וְאִינְהוּ דַּרְדַּקֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **found the younger** sages sitting in the **upper,** more prominent section, and **the elder** ones in the **lower** section. **He said** to himself: I must first greet the younger sages, as they are sitting in the upper section, prior to the elder sages; but **if I greet these** younger sages first, **those** elder sages will **kill me,** as **they maintain: We are better, because we are older and they are children.**

Inside, Rabbi Yehoshua finds a striking arrangement: *yanukei mile'eil savai miletachat* — the young scholars were positioned above, and the elders below. This inversion of the expected hierarchy is potentially significant: in a society that prizes innovation and empirical discovery, the younger generation — whose knowledge is more recent and who have built on the accumulation of what came before — receives the higher position, while the elders, the source of the foundational wisdom, are placed below. One is reminded of the image, wherever it originates, of later generations as midgets on the shoulders of giants: the younger ones can see further, but only because they stand upon those beneath them.

### Greeting Dilemma

Rabbi Yehoshua faces a dilemma that is both social and philosophical. If he greets the elders first, the younger scholars — sitting above him — will take offense that the superior position was not acknowledged. If he greets the young scholars first, the elders will protest: *savi anan adifu de'anan kashishei* — we are the more important ones, we are the elders. He cannot show deference to one group without implicitly slighting the other.

### Equal Greeting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר: שְׁלָמָא לְכוּ. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: מַאי עֲבִידְתָּיךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲנָא חַכִּימָא דִיהוּדָאֵי אֲנָא, בָּעֵינָא לְמִיגְמַר חֻכְמְתָא מִינַּיְיכוּ. אִי הָכִי, נִיבְעֵי לָךְ! אֲמַר לְהוּ: לְחַיֵּי, אִי זָכֵיתוּ — כֹּל דְּבָעֵיתוּ עֲבִידוּ בִּי, וְאִי זָכֵינָא בְּכוּ — אִיכְלוּ גַּבַּאי בִּסְפִינְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **said: Greetings to you,** but did not directly address either group. **They said to him: What are you doing** here? Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: I am a Sage of the Jews,** and **I desire to learn wisdom from you.** They said to him: **If so, we will ask you** questions and see if you are worthy of this privilege. Rabbi Yehoshua **said to them: Very well. If you win, you may do to me anything you wish, and if I defeat you,** then **eat with me on my ship.**

He resolves the dilemma by greeting everyone simultaneously — *shlama lechu* — neither group first, neither group second.

### Contest Terms

The elders ask what he is doing there. He introduces himself: *chatima de-yehudaye ana* — I am one of the wise men of Judea, and I have come to learn from your wisdom. They respond: then let us test you. He agrees, with the following terms: if they best him in the contest, they may do with him as they wish; if he bests them, they must come onto his boat and dine with him. It seems an entirely lopsided wager in their favor — what could they lose? — and they agree.

## Athens Story Part 2

### Marriage Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאָזֵיל וּבָעֵי אִתְּתָא וְלָא יָהֲבוּ לֵיהּ, מַאי חֲזֵי לֵיהּ דְּאָזֵיל הֵיכָא דְּמִדְּלוּ מִינָּיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The sages of Athens **said to him:** In the case of **a certain man who goes and asks** to marry **a woman and** her family **does not give** her **to him, why would he see** fit **to go to** a family **that is greater than** the first?

The first question the elders pose: a certain man went and sought to marry a woman of equal status, and her parents refused to give her to him. Why then does he subsequently pursue women of *higher* status? If he cannot obtain a woman at his own level, what leads him to seek someone above it?

### Why Higher Status

The question is subtle and pointed. It implies an internal contradiction in human — or perhaps specifically Jewish — aspiration: if you are denied what matches you, what is the logic of reaching for what surpasses you?

### Peg Metaphor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁקַל סִיכְּתָא, דָּצַהּ לְתַתַּאי — לָא עָאל, לְעִילַּאי — עָאל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua **took a peg** and **stuck it** into the **lower** part of the wall, but it did **not go in.** He then stuck it **into the upper** portion of the wall where there was a hole, and it **went in.**

Rabbi Yehoshua's answer takes a physical form. *Shakil sikhsa* — he picked up a peg — *datsa letata, lo al* — he tried to drive it into the lower part of the wall and it would not go in — *le'ila, ve'al* — he drove it into a higher part of the wall and it went in. The answer to their question is embedded in the demonstration: sometimes the lower placement does not work; the higher one does. There is no inherent contradiction in seeking something beyond what one's level seemed to dictate, because the level itself may have been wrong.

### Olam HaBa Interpretation

The *Maharal* offers a reading of this exchange that resonates with the story's broader theme. The Athenians, he suggests, are making an implicit argument: the Jews have no success in *olam hazeh* — in this world. In the ancient and classical world, worldly success was widely understood as a sign of divine favor; prosperity, power, and visible achievement marked those whom the gods had blessed. The Jews, by this standard, appear to be out of favor. If you cannot secure even what belongs to your own station in *olam hazeh*, on what basis do you claim reward in *olam haba*?

Rabbi Yehoshua's answer with the peg is: the lower position did not work — that is true. But that does not mean the higher position is also foreclosed. On the contrary, it is precisely there that entry is found. The Jews' reward is not of this world; *olam haba* is what is appropriate for them, and the fact that the peg did not go into the lower part of the wall does not mean it will not go into the higher. The story will continue.
