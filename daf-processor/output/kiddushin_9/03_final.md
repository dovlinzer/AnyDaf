# Kiddushin 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rejection via Condition

### Dog Distraction Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּכִכָּר״ – ״תְּנֵהוּ לְכֶלֶב״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְאִם הָיָה כֶּלֶב שֶׁלָּהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. בָּעֵי רַב מָרִי: כֶּלֶב רָץ אַחֲרֶיהָ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with a loaf** of bread, and she said to him: **Give it to a dog, she is not betrothed. And if** this **dog was hers, she is betrothed. Rav Mari raises a dilemma:** If **a dog** was **chasing her** to bite her, and she said to him: Give the loaf to the dog, **what is** the *halakha*?

The sugya begins at the bottom of 8b with a case left unresolved — a *teiku* — by Rav Mari: a man offers a woman bread for *kiddushin*, and she responds by telling him to give the bread to a dog in order to distract it from biting her. That case was left open. The Gemara now moves to a related scenario.

### Poor Person Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּכִכָּר״ – ״תְּנֵהוּ לְעָנִי״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, אֲפִילּוּ עָנִי הַסָּמוּךְ עָלֶיהָ. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דִּמְחַיַּיבְנָא בֵּיהּ אֲנָא, הָכִי מְחַיְּיבַתְּ בֵּיהּ אַתְּ.
>
> **Translation:** If a man said to a woman: **Be betrothed to me with a loaf** of bread, and she said: **Give it to a poor person, she is not betrothed, even** if it was **a poor person who is dependent upon her,** i.e., a poor person who regularly receives food from that woman. **What is the reason** for this? She could **say to him: Just as I am required** to give charity to **him, so too you are required** to give charity to **him.** Therefore, this donation is not an indication that she has agreed to the betrothal.

*Hitkadshi li bekikar tenahu le'ani* — the man says, "Be married to me with this loaf of bread," and the woman replies, "Give it to that poor person." The ruling is *eina mekudeshes* — she is not betrothed. This applies even in the case of *afilu ani hasamuch aleha*, even if the poor person is one who regularly relies on *tzedakah* that she provides.

### Tzedakah Obligation

The reason, *mai ta'ama*, is articulated as follows: *amra leh ki heichi demichayavat beih ana hachi michayavat beih at* — she is saying to him, "Just as I am obligated to support him, so too are you." In other words, we each bear the same obligation to give *tzedakah*. Her instruction to him to give the bread to the poor person is therefore a rejection of the *kiddushin* — she is not accepting the bread on her own behalf at all. It is worth noting that *halakha* does recognize a certain precedence in giving *tzedakah* to those who regularly depend on a particular individual, to the extent that it takes on the character of a near-obligation. And yet even that special relationship is insufficient to transform her redirection of the bread into an act of acceptance.

## Double Language

### Hava Mehava (Buttons)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא מְזַבֵּין חוּמְרֵי פְּתַכְיָיתָא. אֲתַאי הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי חַד שׂוֹכָא״. אֲמַר לַהּ: ״אִי יָהֲבִינָא לִיךְ מִיקַּדְּשַׁתְּ לִי״? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״הַבָה מֵיהֲבָה״. אָמַר רַב חָמָא: כֹּל ״הַבָה מֵיהֲבָה״ לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who was selling beads [*ḥumrei*] of glass [*petakhyata*]. A certain woman came** and **said to him: Give me one string. He said to her: If I give you** this string **will you be betrothed to me** with it? **She said to him: Give, give. Rav Ḥama said: Any** use of the expression: **Give, give, is nothing.** Although she said: Give, give, she did not agree to the condition, as she was mocking him and had no intention of actually becoming betrothed.

The Gemara introduces a series of cases involving *lashon kaful* — double language. *Hahu gavra dehavah kamizban chumrei pitchita* — there was a man selling buttons, or some kind of trinkets. Rashi identifies these as tin buttons (*butnin she'osin shel bedil*), and this is the first Rashi on 9a. A woman approached him and said, *havli chad shuka* — give me one button or necklace. He replied: *ee havinah lach mekudeshet li* — if I give it to you, will you be married to me? Her response: *hava me-hava* — give it, give it — a doubled formulation. Rav Chama rules: *kol hava me-hava lav klum hee* — when the response is phrased with this double *lashon*, it does not constitute acceptance of *kiddushin*.

The interpretive question is whether *hava me-hava* expresses consent or rejection. The double formulation signals that she is simply brushing him off and demanding the item without accepting his condition. Tosafot engages this discussion as well, and the point is that a doubled imperative of this kind reflects insistence on receiving the object rather than agreement to the proposed *kiddushin*.

### Ashkey Ashkayan (Wine)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא שָׁתֵי חַמְרָא בְּחָנוּתָא. אֲתַאי הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״הַב לִי חַד כָּסָא״. אֲמַר לַהּ: ״אִי יָהֵיבְנָא לִיךְ מִיקַּדְּשַׁתְּ לִי״? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״אַשְׁקוֹיֵי אַשְׁקְיַין״. אָמַר רַב חָמָא: כֹּל ״אַשְׁקוֹיֵי אַשְׁקְיַין״ לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: There was **a certain man who was drinking wine in a store. A woman came** in and **said to him: Give me one cup** of wine. **He said to her: If I give you** a cup of wine will **you be betrothed to me** with it? **She said to him: Give to drink, give** it **to me to drink. Rav Ḥama said** that **any** use of the expression: **Give to drink, give** it **to me to drink, is nothing,** i.e., she certainly did not intend to accept the condition and she is not betrothed.

A parallel case: *hahu gavra dehavay shatey chamra be-chanuta* — a man was drinking wine in the market. A woman came and asked, *havli chad kasa* — give me a cup of wine. He responded: *ee havinah lach mekudeshet li* — if I give it to you, will you be married to me? She replied: *ashkey ashkayan* — let me drink, let me drink — again a double *lashon*. Rav Chama rules identically: *kol ashkey ashkayan lav klum hee* — this double formulation does not count as acceptance.

### Shadi Mishadey (Dates)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דַּהֲוָה קָא שָׁדֵי תַּמְרֵי מִדִּקְלָא. אֲתַאי הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא, אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״שְׁדִי לִי תַּרְתֵּי״! אֲמַר לַהּ: ״אִי שָׁדֵינָא לִיךְ מִיקַּדְּשַׁתְּ לִי״? אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: ״שְׁדִי מִישְׁדָּא״. אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: כֹּל ״שְׁדִי מִישְׁדָּא״ לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further relates: There was **a certain man who was picking dates from a date tree. A certain woman came** and **said to him: Throw me two. He said to her: If I throw** two dates **to you** will **you be betrothed to me** with them? **She said to him: Throw, throw. Rav Zevid said: Any use** of the expression: **Throw, throw, is nothing,** and she is not betrothed.

A third case: *hahu gavra dehavay kashadi tamrey midikla* — a man was picking dates from a palm tree and throwing them down, the method by which dates were harvested. A woman came and asked, *shadi lee tartey* — throw me down two dates. One might note that she asks for two rather than one, as in the other cases; it has been suggested this is because a single date might not reach the value of a *perutah*, or simply that one date would not suffice. He responds: *ee shadina lach mekudeshet lee* — if I throw them down to you, will you be married to me? She replies: *shadi mishadey* — throw it down, throw it down. Rav Zvid rules: *kol shadi mishadey lav klum hee* — again, the double *lashon* does not count.

### Single Language Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: ״הַב״, ״אַשְׁקִי״, ״וּשְׁדִי״, מַהוּ? – אָמַר רָבִינָא: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַב סַמָּא בַּר רַקְתָּא אָמַר: תָּגָא דְמַלְכָּא! אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְהִלְכְתָא: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If she said: **Give,** or: **Give to drink,** or: **Throw,** without the additional emphasis of the repetition, **what is** the *halakha*? Does this straightforward statement indicate that she actually meant him to give it to her in accordance with his stated condition, or does she not agree to betrothal even here? **Ravina said: She is betrothed. Rav Sama bar Rakta said** in the form of an oath: **By the king's crown! She is not betrothed.** The Gemara states: **And the *halakha*** is that **she is not betrothed.**

This naturally raises the question: what if she had responded with only a single *lashon* — simply *hav*, or *ashkey*, or *shadi*? *Iba'ya lehu hav ashkey veshadey mahu?* Ravina rules *mekudeshes* — she would be betrothed. Rav Sama bar Rakta disagrees and says *eina mekudeshes*, swearing by *taga de-malka* — the crown of the king — a striking oath not typically encountered in the Gemara, where one more commonly finds *chai Elokim*. This is a *machloket Amoraim*.

Rabbeinu Chananel, quoted in the Rashba, explains *taga de-malka* to mean: even if the man had given her the crown of the king, an object of extraordinary value, she still would not be considered betrothed in this context — because the manner of her response demonstrates that she was not genuinely agreeing to the marriage at all. The logic in each of these cases, whether single or double *lashon*, is that when a woman asks for something and a man offers it conditionally on marriage, and she responds in a way that merely demands the item without clearly accepting his condition, that is a rejection. The double formulation makes it unambiguous; the single formulation is where the *machloket* lies.

## Hilchata Rulings

### Summary Hilchata

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: שִׁירָאֵי לָא צְרִיכִי שׁוּמָא. וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וְהִלְכְתָא כְּרָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara issues further rulings concerning the previous cases. **And the *halakha*** is: With regard to **silk** garments that are worth more than one *peruta*, **appraisal** is **not necessary** before a woman can be betrothed with them. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar,** that if a man promised a woman one hundred dinars as betrothal money and gave her only a dinar, she is betrothed. **And the *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava,** who said that **Rav Naḥman said** that if he promised one hundred dinars and gave her only collateral, this is not a valid betrothal.

The Gemara concludes the preceding *sugyot* with a series of *hilchata* rulings. *V'hilchasa eina mekudeshes* — the ruling in the cases just discussed is that she is not betrothed.

### Silk, Rings, Diamonds

*V'hilchasa shirai lo tzrichi shuma* — silk does not require assessment of its value for purposes of *kiddushin*. This resolves the *machloket* between Rabba and Rav Yosef: Rav Yosef had maintained that a piece of silk used for *kiddushin* would require a formal appraisal of its worth, while Rabba disagreed. The ruling follows Rabba.

The Gemara also rules *v'hilchasa k'Rabbi Eliezer* — in the case discussed previously where a man says *itkadshi li b'maneh v'nasan lah dinar*, meaning he promised 100 *dinar* and gave only one, she is *mekudeshes* but he owes the remainder. And *v'hilchasa k'Rava amar Rav Nachman* — in the case of the *mashkon*, where a man promises 100 *dinar* but gives her only collateral and no actual *kesef kiddushin*, the ruling is *maneh ein kan mashkon ein kan* — neither the collateral nor the promised money constitutes valid *kiddushin*.

### Precious Stones Issue

One might ask: if the ruling is that silk requires no assessment, why do some authorities raise concerns about using a diamond ring under the *chuppah*? This is precisely the concern raised by the Ri in Tosafot. The Ri distinguishes between silk, whose value is relatively familiar and not subject to gross error (*v'ein regilin lito'os bo kol kach*), and other items such as precious stones and gems (*avanim tovos u-margaliyos*), whose value may be entirely opaque. A stone that appears magnificent may in truth be nearly worthless — the example of a polished cubic zirconia is apt — while giving a woman what appears to be a diamond ring, she naturally assumes it carries significant value.

Rabbeinu Tam responds that the ruling regarding silk teaches specifically that silk does not require assessment *because* its value is sufficiently known. Other items, especially precious stones, may well require more care. The concern, then, is not one of *shaveh prutah* — a properly cut diamond is certainly worth more than a *perutah* — but rather of the woman's subjective understanding of the value of what she is receiving. If she would later discover that the ring is worth dramatically less than she understood, she might argue that she only accepted on the assumption of a certain value. This Tosafot is the source for the common practice of using a plain band under the *chuppah* rather than a stone-set ring.

Fundamentally, if a man gives his bride an object of clear and unambiguous value, worth more than a *perutah*, with witnesses present and full awareness on all sides, there is no intrinsic halachic difficulty. The practice of using a plain ring has developed from an abundance of caution born of precisely this Tosafot, combined with the symbolic resonances — wholeness, the chain of tradition reaching back to *Rivka Imeinu* — that have attached themselves to the ring in *Ashkenazi* communities.

## Shtar Kiddushin

### Writing the Shtar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּשְׁטָר כֵּיצַד? כָּתַב לוֹ עַל הַנְּיָיר אוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְרוּטָה, ״בִּתְּךָ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, ״בִּתְּךָ מְאוֹרֶסֶת לִי״, ״בִּתְּךָ לִי לְאִינְתּוּ״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: How is** betrothal performed **with a document?** If he **wrote** the following **for** a young woman's father **on paper or earthenware, despite** the fact **that** the paper or earthenware **is not worth one *peruta*: Your daughter is betrothed [*mekuddeshet*] to me,** or: **Your daughter is betrothed [*me'oreset*] to me,** or: **Your daughter is to me as a wife,** then **she is betrothed.** There is no requirement for the paper or earthenware to be worth one *peruta*, as she is not betrothed through the value of the paper or earthenware.

The Gemara turns to the second mode of *kiddushin*: acquisition through a written document. *Tanu rabbanan bishtar keitzad* — how does one effect *kiddushin* through a *shtar*? The *braita* states: *katav lo al haniyar o al hacheres af al pi she'ein bo shaveh prutah* — one writes on paper or on an ostracon, a shard of pottery used for writing in the ancient Near East, and the document effects *kiddushin* even though the physical medium is worth less than a *perutah*. The *shtar kiddushin*, in other words, derives its efficacy entirely from its content, not from the intrinsic value of the material.

It is notable that the first *braita* quoted frames the document as written between the husband and the father — *bitecha mekudeshet li*, *bitecha me'ureset li*, *bitecha li le'intu* — "your daughter is married to me," in three variant formulations, and the ruling is *harei zo mekudeshes*. This contrasts with the *braita* on *kesef kiddushin*, which focused on the interaction between the man and the adult woman directly. Here the *braita* envisions a younger woman, a minor daughter, for whom the father is the relevant party.

As Rabbi Linzer noted at the outset of this *masechta*, *kiddushei shtar* was likely never widely practiced, and consequently many of its *halachot* are underdeveloped. The *sugya* is comparatively brief.

A related question noted by the Rashba concerns what minimal content a *shtar kiddushin* must contain. The Rashba holds that a *shtar* must include at minimum the names of the parties and the date, as with any proper *shtar*. The Ritva and many other *Rishonim* disagree, holding that the *shtar kiddushin* is a more abstract instrument: a piece of material bearing the words *harei at mekudeshet li* is sufficient, and nothing further is required. Whether one even needs to pronounce *harei at mekudeshet li* verbally when handing over the *shtar* is itself debated — since the words are already written in the document, perhaps the *shtar* itself embodies the *amirah*, and no separate declaration is required.

One further observation: the directionality of *kiddushei shtar* is the reverse of what one might expect from a commercial analogy. In a sale of land, the seller writes *sadi mechura lach* — "my field is sold to you" — and hands the document to the buyer, who thereby acquires the field. In *kiddushin*, however, it is the husband who writes and delivers the *shtar*, not the woman or her father. The woman, in a certain sense analogous to the seller of the field, is nonetheless not the one writing the document. This reversal is the challenge pressed by Rabbi Zeira.

### Zeira's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא בַּר מֶמֶל: הָא לָא דָּמֵי הַאי שְׁטָרָא לִשְׁטַר זְבִינֵי! הָתָם, מוֹכֵר כּוֹתֵב לוֹ ״שָׂדִי מְכוּרָה לָךְ״, הָכָא בַּעַל כּוֹתֵב ״בִּתְּךָ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira bar Memel objects to this** description of the writing of the document: **But this document is not comparable to a bill of sale. There,** in the case of a bill of sale, the **seller** is the one who **writes to** the buyer: **My field is sold to you. Here,** the **husband,** who is akin to a buyer, is the one who **writes: Your daughter is betrothed to me.**

*Matkif lah Rabbi Zeira* — Rabbi Zeira challenges: *ha lo dami hai shtar leh shtar zvini* — this *shtar kiddushin* is not analogous to a deed of sale. In a sale, *hatam mocher kotev lo sadi mechura lach* — the seller writes "my field is sold to you." But here, *hacha baal kotev bitcha mekudeshet li* — it is the husband who writes "your daughter is married to me." The directionality is inverted relative to what the analogy to commercial transactions would suggest. How, then, does it work?

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָתָם מֵעִנְיָינָא דִּקְרָא וְהָכָא מֵעִנְיָינָא דִּקְרָא. הָתָם כְּתִיב ״וּמָכַר מֵאֲחֻזָּתוֹ״ – בְּמוֹכֵר תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא, הָכָא כְּתִיב ״כִּי יִקַּח״ – בְּבַעַל תְּלָה רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: There,** in the case of a sale, the formulation of the document is taken **from the context of the verse, and here,** in the case of betrothal, the formulation of the document is likewise taken **from the context of the verse.** Rava elaborates: **There,** with regard to a sale, **it is written: "And sells of his ancestral land"** (Leviticus 25:25), which indicates that **the Merciful One renders** the transaction **dependent on the seller. Here, it is written: "If** a man **takes** a woman" (Deuteronomy 22:13), meaning that **the Merciful One renders** the betrothal **dependent on the husband.**

Rava resolves this by appeal to scriptural derivation. *Amar Rava hatam me-inyana de-kra ve-hacha me-inyana de-kra* — each case follows its own scriptural source. In the case of a field sale, the Torah writes *u-machar me-achuzato* — "and he shall sell of his ancestral land" — and *be-mocher tala rachmana*, the Torah places legal emphasis on the seller. Accordingly, the seller writes the deed. In *kiddushin*, the Torah writes *ki yikach* — "when a man takes" — and *be-baal tala rachmana*, the Torah places emphasis on the husband's act of taking. Therefore it is the husband who writes the *shtar*.

The Gemara attempts to push back. In the context of land sales, one may also read *sados be-kesef yiknu* — "fields are bought with money" — with different vowels (*kari bei yaknu*), implying the buyer also has legal standing in the transaction. Similarly in *kiddushin*, perhaps the relevant *pasuk* should be read *ki yukach* — "when she is taken" — emphasizing the woman's role. But this is rejected from the *pasuk* *es biti nasati la-ish ha-zeh* — "I gave my daughter to this man" — in the context of *motzi shem ra*, where it is the father who acts as giver, not the husband who takes.

### Biblical Sources Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם נָמֵי כְּתִיב ״שָׂדוֹת בַּכֶּסֶף יִקְנוּ״! קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״יַקְנוּ״. מַאי טַעְמָא קָרֵית בֵּיהּ ״יַקְנוּ״ – מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״וּמָכַר״, הָכִי נָמֵי קְרִי בֵּיהּ ״כִּי יַקַּח״, דִּכְתִיב ״אֶת בִּתִּי נָתַתִּי לָאִישׁ הַזֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **There,** in the case of a sale, **it is also written: "Men shall buy [*yiknu*] fields for money"** (Jeremiah 32:44), which indicates that the matter depends upon the buyer. The Gemara answers: **Read into** the verse: Shall sell **[*yikkanu*].** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that **you read it** as ***yikkanu*; because it is written** in the verse in Leviticus: **"And sells,"** and there is a preference to have the verse from the Prophets accord with that of the Torah? **So too,** instead of: "If a man takes [*ki yikaḥ*]" (Deuteronomy 22:13), **read into** the verse: When he is given **[*ki yakiaḥ*], as it is written: "I gave my daughter to this man"** (Deuteronomy 22:16), so that the verses will accord with each other.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא נִינְהוּ וְאַסְמְכִינְהוּ רַבָּנַן אַקְּרָאֵי. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָתָם נָמֵי כְּתִיב ״וָאֶקַּח אֶת סֵפֶר הַמִּקְנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** There is no proof from the verses for these rulings, as **they are a *halakha*** received through tradition, **and the Sages based them on** the **verses. And if you wish, say: There too,** in the case in Jeremiah, **it is written** with regard to the buyer: **"And I took the deed of purchase"** (Jeremiah 32:11), thereby indicating that it is the seller who writes the document.

The Gemara's resolution is ultimately that the scriptural derivations are not fully adequate, and Rava concludes: *ela amar Rava hilchasa ninhu* — these are *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, transmitted traditions from Sinai, *ve-asmachinhu rabbanan a-kra* — and the Rabbis simply attached them to *psukim* as mnemonics, but the underlying law is a received tradition.

Tosafot raises an objection: it cannot be that both cases are *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*, because the commercial case of a seller writing the deed of sale works perfectly from the *psukim* without needing recourse to tradition. Tosafot therefore limits the claim: only the directionality of *shtar kiddushin* — that it is the husband who writes the document — is *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai*. The Rambam's framework is relevant here: *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* refers specifically to those laws that are entirely arbitrary from the perspective of the text, laws one could never derive through reading and deduction — such as the minimum size of a *kezayit* or the forty *se'ah* of a *mikveh* — as opposed to *perushim ha-mekubalim mi-pi Moshe Rabbeinu*, received interpretations that could in principle be recovered if lost.

A further alternative resolution is offered: *ve-iba'it eima hatam nami ketiv va'ekach et sefer ha-miknah* — perhaps after all one may say that the buyer's writing of the deed of sale is also supported scripturally, from the verse in Yirmiyahu where Yirmiyahu himself took the deed of purchase when buying his uncle Hanam'el's field in Anatot. In that episode, Yirmiyahu's act of purchasing ancestral land during the Babylonian crisis was a prophetic gesture of hope — a public declaration that the exile would end and the people would return. This *pasuk* comes from *Ketuvim*-adjacent material in Nevi'im, however, which raises its own difficulty: as a general principle, *halakha* is not derived from Nevi'im and Ketuvim in the same way as from the Torah, and many *Rishonim* hold that any derivation from Nevi'im and Ketuvim is automatically an *asmachta*.

## Shtar Requirements

### Minor Daughter Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: כָּתַב לוֹ עַל הַנְּיָיר אוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְרוּטָה, ״בִּתְּךָ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, ״בִּתְּךָ מְאוֹרֶסֶת לִי״, ״בִּתְּךָ לִי לְאִינְתּוּ״, בֵּין עַל יְדֵי אָבִיהָ בֵּין עַל יְדֵי עַצְמָהּ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת מִדַּעְתּוֹ, וְהוּא שֶׁלֹּא בָּגְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava says** that **Rav Naḥman says:** If he **wrote** the following **for him on paper or earthenware, despite** the fact **that** the paper or earthenware **is not worth one *peruta*: Your daughter is betrothed [*mekuddeshet*] to me,** or: **Your daughter is betrothed [*me'oreset*] to me,** or: **Your daughter is to me as a wife, whether** he gave it **to her father or whether** he gave it directly **to her, she is betrothed with the consent** of her father. **And this** is the *halakha* provided **that she has not** yet **reached her majority,** before which her father alone has the authority to betroth her.

The Gemara presents further *halachot* regarding the writing and transmission of *shtar kiddushin*. Rav Amram in the name of Rav Nachman rules: *katav lah al haniyar o al hacheres af al pi she'ein bo shaveh prutah* — if one writes on paper or pottery, even without the medium reaching the value of a *perutah*, and writes *bitecha mekudeshet li*, *bitecha me'ureset li*, or *bitecha li le'intu*, the *kiddushin* is effective *bein al yedei aviha bein al yedei atzmah mida'ato* — whether the *shtar* is delivered to the father or directly to the daughter herself, provided it is done with the father's *da'at*. This applies *vehu shelo bagra* — so long as the daughter has not yet reached *bagrus* (12½ years of age). When a father marries off his minor daughter, the operative legal standing is the father's own, not his daughter's; he is not merely a proxy for her but rather the primary actor in a distinct mechanism of *kiddushin*.

### Adult Woman Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב לָהּ עַל הַנְּיָיר אוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְרוּטָה, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לִי לְאִינְתּוּ״, ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְאוֹרֶסֶת לִי״ – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, בֵּין עַל יְדֵי אָבִיהָ, בֵּין עַל יְדֵי עַצְמָהּ, מִדַּעְתָּהּ, וְהוּא שֶׁבָּגְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **If he wrote for her on paper or earthenware, despite** the fact **that** the paper or earthenware **is not worth one *peruta*: You are hereby betrothed [*mekuddeshet*] to me,** or: **You are hereby to me as a wife,** or: **You are hereby betrothed [*me'oreset*] to me,** then **she is betrothed whether** he gave it **to her father** or **to her,** as long as this was **with her consent. And this is** the *halakha* provided **that she has reached her majority** and is under her own authority.

When she has reached majority, the *shtar* must be written *harei at mekudeshet li*, *harei at li le'intu*, or *harei at me'ureset li*, and it may be delivered *bein al yedei aviha bein al yedei atzmah mida'atah* — either to her or to her father, with her consent, *vehu shebagra* — in the case where she has come of age, meaning she is older than twelve and a half.

This text is somewhat problematic: it appears to say that the father could accept *kiddushin* on behalf of a *bogeret*, which is inconsistent with other sources in this Gemara that make clear that once a woman has reached majority, her father no longer has independent authority over her *kiddushin*. All the *Rishonim* therefore explain that *mida'atah* here must mean that she has specifically appointed her father as a *shaliach kabbalah* on her behalf. Without such an appointment, delivery to the father of a *bogeret* would not constitute valid *kiddushin*.

### Leshem Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: שְׁטַר אֵירוּסִין שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָהּ, מַהוּ? הֲוָיוֹת לִיצִיאוֹת מַקְּשִׁינַן, מָה יְצִיאָה בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ – אַף הֲוָיָיה בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא הֲוָיוֹת לַהֲדָדֵי מַקְּשִׁינַן, מָה הֲוָיָיה דְכֶסֶף לָא בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ – אַף הֲוָיָיה דִשְׁטָר לָא בָּעֵינַן לִשְׁמָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish raises a dilemma:** With regard to **a document of betrothal that was written not for her sake,** i.e., not for this particular woman, **what is** the *halakha*? Do **we juxtapose** the *halakhot* of the **modes of becoming** betrothed **to** the *halakhot* of the **modes of leaving** a marriage, i.e., divorce? If so, one should say: **Just as we require** that the document of **leaving,** i.e., a bill of divorce, must be written specifically **for her sake, so too, we require** that the document of **becoming** betrothed be written **for her sake. Or perhaps we juxtapose** the different **modes of becoming** betrothed **to each other** and say: **Just as we do not require** that **becoming** betrothed **with money** must be carried out with coins minted **for her sake, so too, we do not require** that **becoming** betrothed **with a document** must be with a document written **for her sake.**

Reish Lakish poses the central question of the section: *shtar eirusin she-katavo shelo lishma, mahu?* — what is the law if a *shtar kiddushin* was written *shelo lishma*, that is, not written with the intent of betrothing this particular woman? We know that a *get* must be written *lishma* — it must be written for the sake of this specific woman for the purpose of this specific divorce. Does the same apply to *shtar kiddushin*?

The question is framed as an alternative: *havayah l'yetziah makshinan?* — do we apply the principle of *hekesh havayah l'yetziah*, connecting marriage to divorce? If so, just as *get* requires *lishma*, so too *shtar kiddushin* would require *lishma*. Or perhaps: *havayah l'hadadi makshinan* — do we instead compare different modes of marriage to one another? If so, just as *kesef kiddushin* requires no *lishma* (how would one even make money *leshema*? One cannot mint coins *leshem kiddushin*), so too *shtar kiddushin* would require no *lishma*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּבַעְיָא הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ: הֲוָיָיה לִיצִיאָה מַקְּשִׁינַן, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **After he raised the dilemma,** Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish **then resolved it. We juxtapose becoming** betrothed **to leaving** a marriage, **as the verse states: "And she departs** out of his house, and goes **and becomes"** (Deuteronomy 24:2). This shows that the *halakhot* of a betrothal document are derived from those of a bill of divorce, and therefore a document of betrothal must also be written for her sake.

After raising the question, Reish Lakish resolves it himself: *havayah l'yetziah makshinan*, and we derive this from the *pasuk* *v'yatz'a v'haysa*. Reish Lakish is consistently the champion of *hekesh havayah l'yetziah* throughout the *masechta*, holding that *kiddushin* and *gerushin* are deeply and structurally connected such that many *halachot* of one may be derived from the other. Rabbi Yochanan will take a more limited view when the dispute is revisited at length on 44a. The Gemara on 44a recounts colorfully that when Rabbi Yochanan and the *chachamim* ruled against *hekesh havayah l'yetziah*, Reish Lakish rose and proclaimed *tzavech k'kruchiya* — squawking like a goose — *havayah l'yetziah, havayah l'yetziah, havayah l'yetziah*, so committed was he to this principle.

The broader stakes of this *machloket* are significant. *Gerushin* is not simply an undoing of *kiddushin* — a woman who has been divorced does not revert to her pre-marital status in all respects. She acquires a new *halachic* identity as a *gerushah*, with distinct consequences for *terumah* and for the prohibition of marrying a *kohen*. Whether *kiddushin* and *gerushin* are mirror images of one another or rather two distinct processes with their own logic is therefore a question with deep structural implications.

Since *shtar kiddushin* was never practically employed, many of these *halachot* were never fully *paskened*, and a number of foundational questions — including whether *shtar kiddushin* must include names and a date like a *get*, as the Rashba holds, or not, as the Ritva holds — remain contested without definitive ruling.

### Writing Knowledge Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: כְּתָבוֹ לִשְׁמָהּ, וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ – רָבָא וְרָבִינָא אָמְרִי: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַב פָּפָּא וְרַב שֵׁרֵבְיָא אָמְרִי: אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידְהוּ וְאֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידִי. אֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידְהוּ – דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ – מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָיה לִיצִיאָה, מָה יְצִיאָה לִשְׁמָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ, אַף הֲוָיָיה נָמֵי לִשְׁמָהּ וְשֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that *amora'im* disagreed with regard to the following issue: If a man **wrote** a document of betrothal **for her sake but without her consent,** i.e., she did not know at the time that they were writing it but accepted it afterward, **Rava and Ravina say: She is betrothed. Rav Pappa and Rav Sherevya say: She is not betrothed. Rav Pappa said: I will say their reason and I will say my reason. I will state their reason, as it is written: "And she departs** out of his house, and goes **and becomes,"** by which the verse **juxtaposes becoming** betrothed **to leaving** a marriage. **Just as** a bill of divorce, written for **leaving** a marriage must be written **for her own sake but** can be written **without her consent, so too,** a document written for **becoming** betrothed must be written **for her own sake and** can even be **without her consent.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא טַעְמָא דִידִי: ״וְיָצְאָה״ ״וְהָיְתָה״ – מַקִּישׁ הֲוָיָיה לִיצִיאָה, מָה יְצִיאָה בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת מַקְנֶה, אַף הֲוָיָיה בָּעֵינַן דַּעַת מַקְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And I will say my reason:** The verse says: **"And she departs** out of his house, and goes **and becomes."** The verse **juxtaposes becoming** betrothed **to leaving** a marriage. **Just as** with regard to a bill of divorce, written for **leaving** a marriage, **we require the consent of the one transferring** ownership, i.e., the man, as he divorces and transfers authority of the woman to herself, **so too,** with regard to a document written for **becoming** betrothed, **we require the consent of the one transferring** ownership, which in this case is the woman, who must agree to the marriage.

*Ismar: kesavo lishma v'shelo mada'atah* — what is the law if the *shtar kiddushin* was written *lishma* but without the woman's knowledge? Rava and Ravina rule *mekudeshes* — the *kiddushin* is valid. Rav Papa and Rav Sharvya rule *eina mekudeshes* — it is not valid.

Rav Papa then explains both positions from the same source. *Eima ta'ama didhu* — the reasoning of Rava and Ravina: from *v'yatz'a v'haysa*, applying *hekesh havayah l'yetziah*, we see that a *get* may be written *lishma v'shelo mida'ata* — a woman need not consent to, or even know about, her own divorce. By parallel, a *shtar kiddushin* written *lishma* is effective even without her knowledge. *Eima ta'ama didi* — Rav Papa's own reasoning against this: the same *hekesh* cuts the other way. In *gerushin*, we require *da'as makneh* — the consent of the one who is transferring. In divorce, that is the husband, and his consent is indeed required. By parallel, in *kiddushin*, the *da'as makneh* belongs to the woman or her father — they are the ones effecting the transfer — and therefore *their* consent is required, including at the stage of writing the *shtar*. Note that this argument does not apply to *kesef kiddushin*, which has little connection to the structure of *gerushin* in the first place.

### Tannaim Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין שְׁטָרֵי אֵירוּסִין וְנִשּׂוּאִין אֶלָּא מִדַּעַת שְׁנֵיהֶן. מַאי לָאו שְׁטָרֵי אֵירוּסִין וְנִשּׂוּאִין מַמָּשׁ! לָא, שְׁטָרֵי פְּסִיקָתָא. וְכִדְרַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב גִּידֵּל אָמַר רַב: ״כַּמָּה אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לְבִנְךָ״? – ״כָּךְ וְכָךְ״, ״לְבִתְּךָ״? – ״כָּךְ וְכָךְ״, עָמְדוּ וְקִדְּשׁוּ – קָנוּ, הֵן הֵן הַדְּבָרִים הַנִּקְנִים בַּאֲמִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Bava Batra* 167b) against the opinion that she is betrothed if the document was written without her consent. **One writes documents of betrothal and marriage only with the consent of both** the man and woman. **What, is** the mishna **not** referring to **actual documents of betrothal and marriage,** which indicates that the document must be written with the woman's consent? The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** this is referring to **documents of stipulation,** which contain the details of the dowry. **And** this statement is **in accordance with** that which **Rav Giddel** says that **Rav says.** **As Rav Giddel says** that **Rav says:** If the father of one member of the couple says to the father of the other: **How much are you giving to your son?** And he answers: **Such and such,** and adds: How much are you giving **to your daughter?** And the other responds: **Such and such,** then if **they,** the couple, subsequently **arose and** became **betrothed, they acquire** everything that was promised. **These are the matters that are acquired through speech,** and they do not require an act of acquisition. The documents of betrothal mentioned here that require the woman's consent are those which contain this type of monetary obligation, not actual documents of betrothal.

The Gemara then cites a *braita* in apparent support of Rav Papa: *ein kotvim shtarei eirusin u-nisuin ela mi-da'at sheneihem* — one does not write *shtarei eirusin* or *nisuin* except with the knowledge and consent of both parties. The Gemara asks: does this not directly support the position that a *shtar kiddushin* requires the woman's prior knowledge?

The response is that the *braita* is not discussing *shtar kiddushin* itself. *Mai lav shtarei eirusin u-nisuin mamash lo — shtarei pasikta.* The *braita* refers to *shtarei pasikta* — the engagement agreement, what we call *tannaim*, recording the financial commitments of both families, how much each side will contribute, and related arrangements. Rashi explains: *shtarei pasikta ha-tannaim she-beineihem u-mammon she-porskin zeh al zeh* — the conditions set between them and the financial obligations each side undertakes. These are monetary contracts in the ordinary sense, and of course monetary contracts require the *da'at* of both parties. The *braita* therefore says nothing about *shtar kiddushin* proper.

This is supported by the dictum of Rav Gidel in the name of Rav: if the families verbally agree — how much will you give your son, how much will you give your daughter — and the couple then proceeds to perform *kiddushin*, the *chalot* of the *kiddushin* act cements the verbal agreements. *Hen ve-hen ha-devarim ha-niknim be-amirah* — this is one of the rare instances in *halakha* where a verbal commitment alone creates binding legal obligation. It is worth noting that in Talmudic times, *kiddushin* served a function much closer to what we now call an engagement — it was performed months before the wedding celebration — yet because of its full *halachic* force, breaking it off required a *get*. By the time of Rav, there was already a practice of having some form of prior agreement (*shidduchei*) before the *kiddushin* itself.

## Bi'ah Kiddushin

### Be'ulat Ba'al Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְבִיאָה. מְנָא לַן? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״בְּעֻלַת בַּעַל״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנַּעֲשֶׂה לָהּ בַּעַל עַל יְדֵי בְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that a woman can be betrothed **through sexual intercourse.** The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this? **Rabbi Abbahu said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that **the verse states:** "If a man be found lying with **a married woman [*beulat ba'al*]"** (Deuteronomy 22:22). This **teaches that he becomes her husband [*ba'al*] by means of sexual intercourse [*be'ila*].

The Gemara now turns to the third mode of *kiddushin*: *bi'ah*, acquisition through sexual intercourse. *Menalan?* — from where is this derived? Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan cites the *pasuk* *be'ulat ba'al* from the context of *na'arah ha-me'orasah* — the case of a betrothed girl who is raped. The *pasuk* uses the phrase *be'ulat ba'al*, teaching that a man becomes her *ba'al* through *be'ilah*: *melamed she-na'asait lo ba'al al yedei be'ilah*.

### Zeira's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּעוּרָה זוֹ שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבִּי ״וּבְעָלָהּ״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה? אִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא עַד דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ וַהֲדַר בָּעֵיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Abbahu, and some say** it was **Reish Lakish** who said this **to Rabbi Yoḥanan:** Is **this** other proof, **taught by Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **unacceptable:** "When a man takes a woman **and engages in sexual intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1)? This verse **teaches** that **she can be acquired through intercourse.** The Gemara answers that the verse cited by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is insufficient proof that a woman can be betrothed via intercourse, as, **if** this *halakha* were derived only **from there, I would say** that she is not considered his wife **unless he** first **betroths** her through money, indicated by the phrase "takes a woman," **and then engages in intercourse** with her. This is the only valid mode of betrothal, and intercourse alone is not enough. Therefore, the verse states "a married woman [*beulat ba'al*]" and **teaches us** that intercourse by itself is a valid means of betrothal.

Rabbi Zeira challenges Rabbi Abbahu: why derive this from the *na'arah ha-me'orasah* context at all? The *pasuk* in the *parashah* of *kiddushin* itself says *ki yikach ish isha u've'alah* — this is the verse cited by Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi (*Rebbi*) to teach that *kiddushin* may be effected through *bi'ah*. Why do we need the additional *pasuk* of *be'ulat ba'al*?

The response: *ee meihatam hava amina ad demikadesh v'hadar be'il* — if we learned exclusively from *ki yikach ish isha u've'alah*, we might have supposed that *kiddushin* requires both the *kinyan* — money or *shtar* — *and* a sexual act, that the two together constitute *kiddushin*. The *pasuk* of *be'ulat ba'al* teaches that *bi'ah* alone, without prior *kesef* or *shtar*, suffices as the *kinyan* of *kiddushin*.

### Na'arah Ha-Me'orasah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: אִם כֵּן, נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה, דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא בִּסְקִילָה, הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אִי דְּאקַדֵּישׁ וַהֲדַר בְּעֵיל בְּעוּלָה הִיא, אִי דְּאקַדֵּישׁ וְלָא בְּעֵיל לָאו כְּלוּם הוּא! אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, כְּגוֹן שֶׁבָּא עָלֶיהָ אָרוּס שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel objects to this:** The above suggestion, that both money and sexual intercourse are required for betrothal, cannot be the correct interpretation of the verse: "When a man takes a woman and engages in sexual intercourse with her." This is because, **if** it is **so,** that a woman can be acquired only through both betrothal money and intercourse, the case of one who engages in intercourse with **a betrothed young woman,** concerning which **the Merciful One states** in the Torah that he is punished **by stoning** (see Deuteronomy 22:23–24)**, how can you find** a case where he is liable to be punished in this manner? Rabbi Abba bar Memel elaborates: **If** this is referring to a case **where he betrothed** her with money **and then engaged in sexual intercourse** with her, **she is a non-virgin,** and the punishment of stoning applies only to one who engages in intercourse with a betrothed young virgin. **If** it is referring to a case **where he betrothed** her with money **and did not engage in intercourse** with her, this **is nothing,** as the betrothal has not been completed. **The Rabbis said before Abaye: You find it** in a case **where** he betrothed her with money and then the **betrothed man engaged in intercourse with her in an atypical manner,** i.e., anal intercourse. Despite the fact that she is still a virgin, the betrothal has taken effect by means of this type of sexual intercourse.

Rabbi Abba bar Memel then presses a challenge from the other direction. If it were true — as the rejected *hava amina* supposed — that one must first perform *kiddushin* and then have intercourse, how would there ever be a case of *na'arah ha-me'orasah* of the kind the Torah subjects to *skilah* rather than *chenek*? The Torah distinguishes this case as a special category: a betrothed virgin who is raped receives a different punishment than an ordinary married woman.

But within the *hava amina*, there is a dilemma. *Ei d'akdesh v'hadar be'il* — if he did *kiddushin* and then had (vaginal) intercourse, *be'ulah hi* — she is already *be'ulah*, no longer a virgin, and the rapist would be liable for *chenek*, not *skilah*. *Ei d'akdesh v'lo be'il* — and if he did *kiddushin* but had not yet had intercourse, *lav klum hu* — that is also nothing, since within the *hava amina*, *kiddushin* without subsequent intercourse would not be complete. How then would the case of *na'arah ha-me'orasah* with its special *skilah* penalty ever arise?

### Anal Sex Resolution

The *rabbanan* before Abaye propose a resolution: *mishkachat lah k'gon she'ba aleha arus shelo k'darkah* — the case could arise if the betrothed man had engaged in anal intercourse with her. She would remain physically a virgin in the relevant physiological sense, and the subsequent rapist would still face the *na'arah ha-me'orasah* case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ אַבָּיֵי: עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי וְרַבָּנַן אֶלָּא בְּאַחֵר, אֲבָל בַּעַל – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אִם בָּא עָלֶיהָ שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ – עֲשָׂאָהּ בְּעוּלָה!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** those Sages: The verse cannot be explained in that manner, as **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and the Rabbis disagree only with regard to another** man, i.e., whether a woman is considered to be a virgin after engaging in anal intercourse with another man. **But** with regard to her **husband, everyone agrees** that **if he engages in intercourse in an atypical manner with her he has rendered her a non-virgin.** If so, she is no longer considered a virgin with regard to the *halakha* of a betrothed young woman.

Abaye rejects this: *ad kan lo pligi Rebbi v'rabanan ela b'acher* — the *machloket* between *Rebbi* and the *Rabbanan* (in the *braita* from Sanhedrin about ten men raping a woman one after another, with the first punishable by *skilah* and all subsequent ones by *chenek* according to *Rebbi*) concerns only the case of *acher*, another man who is not the husband. But as for the husband himself: *aval ba'al divrei hakol im ba aleha shelo k'darkah as'ah be'ulah* — everyone agrees that if the husband himself has anal intercourse with his wife, she is rendered *be'ulah*, regardless of the absence of hymenal change. A marital act of sex — whether vaginal or anal — renders her a *be'ulah* in law.

### Shtar Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ כְּגוֹן שֶׁקִּדְּשָׁהּ בִּשְׁטָר, הוֹאִיל וְגוֹמֵר וּמוֹצִיא, גּוֹמֵר וּמַכְנִיס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** a different answer to Rabbi Abba bar Memel's question: **You find** a situation where a man who engages in intercourse with a betrothed young woman is punished by stoning in a case **where he betrothed her with a document.** Everyone agrees that **since** a document, i.e., a bill of divorce, **completely removes** a woman from her husband, without the need for an additional act, it also **completely brings** her into the state of betrothal. If a young woman is betrothed by means of a document, she can be a betrothed young woman while remaining a virgin.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak offers a different resolution to the dilemma of *na'arah ha-me'orasah*: *mishkachat lah k'gon she'kideish b'shtar* — the case may arise if the *kiddushin* was effected through a *shtar* alone. A *shtar kiddushin* is powerful enough to complete the entire *kinyan* of *kiddushin* without any *bi'ah* at all. Thus, within the *hava amina* that *kesef* plus *bi'ah* were required together, a *shtar kiddushin* would stand independently, and a woman betrothed solely through a *shtar* would be *me'ureset* — and if subsequently raped, the case of *na'arah ha-me'orasah* would apply perfectly, without any complication about whether she remains a virgin.

### Wife vs. Shifcha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הַאי ״וּבְעָלָהּ״, מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ – זוֹ נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה, וְאֵין אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה. סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא תֵּיתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִיְבָמָה: וּמָה יְבָמָהּ שֶׁאֵין נִקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, נִקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה, זוֹ, שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּכֶסֶף, אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנִּקְנֵית בְּבִיאָה? מָה לִיבָמָה, שֶׁכֵּן זְקוּקָה וְעוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the different derivations of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabbi Yoḥanan. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan,** who maintains that the mode of betrothal through intercourse is derived from the verse: "If a man be found lying with a married woman [*beulat ba'al*]" (Deuteronomy 22:22), what does he **do with this** verse: "When a man takes a woman **and engages in sexual intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 24:1)? The Gemara answers: **He requires that** verse for a different *halakha*, as he maintains that it teaches that **this** woman **can be acquired through intercourse, but a Hebrew maidservant cannot be acquired through intercourse.** As it might **enter your mind to say: Let** the *halakha* of a Hebrew maidservant **be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the *halakha* of **a *yevama*: Just as a *yevama*, who cannot be acquired through money** at all, nevertheless **can be acquired through intercourse,** which indicates that the ability of an act of sexual intercourse to effect acquisition is greater than that of money, **is it not logical that this** Hebrew maidservant, **who can be acquired through money, can** also **be acquired through intercourse?** The Gemara rejects this opinion: **What** is unique **about a *yevama* is that she is bound and standing** waiting for the *yavam*, i.e., there is already a connection between them. Perhaps it is for this reason that intercourse enables a *yavam* to acquire a *yevama*, and the same cannot be said of a maidservant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּכְתִב ״אִם אַחֶרֶת יִקַּח לוֹ״, הִקִּישָׁהּ הַכָּתוּב לְאַחֶרֶת. מָה אַחֶרֶת מִיקַּנְיָא בְּבִיאָה – אַף אָמָה הָעִבְרִיָּה מִיקַּנְיָא בְּבִיאָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְרַבִּי, הַאי סְבָרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא ״וּבָעַל״, מַאי ״וּבְעָלָהּ״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, it might **enter your mind to say** a different claim: **Since it is written** with regard to a the master of a Hebrew maidservant: **"If he take himself another** wife" (Exodus 21:10), this **verse juxtaposes** a Hebrew maidservant **with another** woman that a master marries: **Just as another** woman that a master marries **can be acquired through intercourse, so too, a Hebrew maidservant can be acquired through intercourse. Therefore,** the verse **teaches us,** with the phrase "and engages in sexual intercourse with her," that this is not the case. The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who learns that betrothal can be effected through sexual intercourse from this verse, **from where does he** derive **this conclusion** that a Hebrew maidservant cannot be acquired through intercourse? The Gemara answers: **If so,** that this verse is teaching only one *halakha*, **let the Merciful One write** simply: **And he engages in sexual intercourse. What** is the meaning of the phrase **"And he engages in sexual intercourse with her"? Learn two** *halakhot* **from it.** One can learn from this verse both that a woman can be acquired through intercourse, and that an ordinary woman can be betrothed through intercourse but a Hebrew maidservant cannot be acquired through intercourse.

Finally, Rabbi Yochanan's use of the *pasuk* *u've'alah* is addressed. Once the Gemara establishes that the derivation comes from *be'ulat ba'al*, the question is: *mai avid lei* Rabbi Yochanan with the *pasuk* of *u've'alah* from *ki yikach ish isha u've'alah*? The answer: *hahu miba'ei lei* — he uses it to derive that a wife may be acquired through *bi'ah*, but an *amah ha-ivriyah*, a Hebrew maidservant given over by her father as an indentured servant, is not acquired through *bi'ah*.

One might have thought to derive the contrary by *kal va-chomer* from *yevamah*: a *yevamah* cannot be acquired through money, yet she is acquired through *bi'ah*; surely a woman who *can* be acquired through money — *kesef kiddushin* — should also be acquirable through *bi'ah*, and by extension so too an *amah ha-ivriyah*. The rejection: *mah leyevamah sheken zekukah ve'omedes* — a *yevamah* is already in a distinct state of *zikah* to the *yavam*, which makes her case unique and unsuitable for generalization.

Furthermore, one might have argued from the *pasuk* *im acheres yikach lo* in the laws of *amah ha-ivriyah* — the Torah there connects her to another wife, suggesting that the *amah ha-ivriyah* is in some sense analogous to a wife, and just as a wife may be acquired through *bi'ah*, so too an *amah ha-ivriyah*. *Ka mashma lan* — it is to exclude precisely this inference that Rabbi Yochanan requires the *pasuk* of *u've'alah*: marriage to a wife is effected through *bi'ah*, but the *kinyanim* of *amah ha-ivriyah* are not.
