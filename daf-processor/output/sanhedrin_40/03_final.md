# Sanhedrin 40 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Witness Inte (III)rrogation Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתָן בְּשֶׁבַע חֲקִירוֹת: בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁבוּעַ, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁנָה, בְּאֵיזוֹ חוֹדֶשׁ, בְּכַמָּה בַּחֹדֶשׁ, בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹם, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁעָה, בְּאֵיזֶה מְקוֹם. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹם, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁעָה, בְּאֵיזֶה מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court **would examine** the witnesses in capital cases **with seven interrogations,** i.e., interrogatory questions, and they are: **In which seven-year** period, that is, in which cycle of seven years within a jubilee did the event occur; **in which year** of the Sabbatical cycle did the event occur; **in which month** did the event occur; **on which** day **of the month** did the event occur; **on which day** of the week did the event occur; **at which hour** did the event occur; and **in what place** did the event occur. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The court would examine the witnesses with only three interrogations: **On which day** did the event occur, **at which hour,** and **in what place.**

The fifth *perek*, *Hayu Bodkin*, opens at the top of Sanhedrin 40a with the *mishnah*. Having dealt in the previous *perek* with the early stages of the court process — accepting witnesses and the general framework of capital proceedings — the *mishnah* here addresses specifically the rules governing the *chakiros* and *drishot*, or *bedikos*: the different types of questions the court must put to witnesses. Some of these questions are mandatory; others are discretionary. Some are sufficiently precise that if the witnesses err in answering them, the entire testimony is disqualified; others leave room for ignorance or even contradiction without invalidating the testimony. Crucially, this framework of *drisha ve-chakira* applies only to *dinei nefashot* — capital cases. As established in the first *perek*, the Rabbis did not apply this regime to *dinei mamonos*, civil suits, not because it would not be required by Torah law but because the rigors of the process would make it impractical for people to resolve monetary disputes. The economic cost of making litigation too cumbersome was a serious concern the Rabbis sought to avoid.

## Seven Chakiros: Tim (III)e/Place Questions

### Seven Questions Listed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיוּ בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתָן בְּשֶׁבַע חֲקִירוֹת: בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁבוּעַ, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁנָה, בְּאֵיזוֹ חוֹדֶשׁ, בְּכַמָּה בַּחֹדֶשׁ, בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹם, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁעָה, בְּאֵיזֶה מְקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The court **would examine** the witnesses in capital cases **with seven interrogations,** i.e., interrogatory questions, and they are: **In which seven-year** period, that is, in which cycle of seven years within a jubilee did the event occur; **in which year** of the Sabbatical cycle did the event occur; **in which month** did the event occur; **on which** day **of the month** did the event occur; **on which day** of the week did the event occur; **at which hour** did the event occur; and **in what place** did the event occur.

The *mishnah* states: *hayu bodkin otan be-sheva chakirot* — the judges would interrogate the witnesses with seven distinct categories of questions. These are: *be-eizo shavua* — in which cycle of seven years within the *yovel* cycle? Here *shavua* refers not to a week of days but to a *shemita* cycle, a seven-year period; *be-eizo shana* — which year within that seven-year cycle? *Be-eizo chodesh* — which month of the year? *Be-khamma ba-chodesh* — which day of the month? *Be-eizo yom* — which day of the week? *Be-eizo sha'ah* — at what hour of the day? And the seventh: *be-eizo makom* — in what place? Six of the seven questions concern time, and the seventh concerns location. The question of how precise the answers need to be — whether "in New York City" suffices or whether the floor of a particular building must be specified — is itself a matter the *gemara* will probe.

### Rabbi Yosi: Three Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹם, בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁעָה, בְּאֵיזֶה מָקוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** The court would examine the witnesses with only three interrogations: **On which day** did the event occur, **at which hour,** and **in what place.**

*Rabbi Yose omer be-eizo yom be-eizo sha'ah be-eizo makom* — Rabbi Yosi holds that only three *chakiros* are required: which day of the week, at what hour, and in what place. His position stands in direct contrast to the majority view of seven, and the question of what drives this disagreement will become central to the *gemara*'s later analysis of the biblical sources.

### Act Details: Idol & Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכִּירִים אַתֶּם אוֹתוֹ? הִתְרֵתֶם בּוֹ? הָעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה: אֶת מִי עָבַד, וּבַמֶּה עָבַד?
>
> **Translation:** They would also ask: **Do you recognize him** as the man who committed the transgression? **Did you warn him?** They would then ask the witnesses about the particulars of the incident. For example, in the case of **one who** is an accused **idol worshipper,** they ask the witnesses: **Whom,** i.e., which idol, **did he worship, and in what manner did he worship** it, and so on.

The *mishnah* continues with a set of questions that the *Rambam*, in his *Perush ha-Mishnayos*, regards as even more fundamental than the *chakiros* proper — so fundamental, in fact, that without them there is no testimony at all, since they go to the very content of the act being testified about. These are: *makirin atem oto* — do you, the witnesses, recognize him? This includes whether they could identify the defendant as a Jew, since Jewish identity bears on culpability. *Hitartem bo* — did you administer a warning (*hatra'ah*) about the consequences of the act? In *dinei nefashot* the witnesses must have warned the person that the specific act he was about to perform carries a death penalty. In the *mishnah*'s context of idolatry, they would have had to say explicitly: if you worship this idol you will be liable to *skila*.

Beyond these preliminary questions, if the case involves *avodah zarah*, the court asks *et mi avad* — which idol did he worship? — and *u-ve-mah avad* — by what mode of worship? This second question matters because *avodah zarah* has two distinct tracks of culpability. First, four categories of worship — sacrifice, libation, incense-offering, and prostration — are forbidden regardless of what the idol's normal mode of worship is, because these replicate the modes of divine service. Second, if an idol has a characteristic mode of worship native to it, performing that mode is likewise prohibited. The *gemara*'s classic example is *Markulis*, likely the Roman god Mercury, worshipped by throwing or placing stones at the idol's location or at crossroads. Even one who throws a stone at *Markulis* sarcastically, intending it as an act of contempt, has nonetheless performed its characteristic form of worship and is culpable. The question *bameh avad* therefore needs to establish which of these tracks applies.

## Purpose of Chakiros: Hazimah & Evidentiary Quality

### Purpose: Hazimah

*Rashi* makes a point of emphasizing the fundamental purpose of the *chakiros*: they exist specifically in order to create testimony that is *edut sheyachol lehazimah* — testimony susceptible to being exposed as conspiratorial. The mechanism of *hazimah*, the disqualification of *eidim zomemim*, which we will reach in the first *perek* of *Masechet Makkos*, works by a counterclaim of *imanu hayitem* — "you, the witnesses, were with us elsewhere at the very time you claim to have witnessed the crime." For such a counterclaim to be possible, the testimony must be anchored to a specific time and place. Without those coordinates, there is no basis for *hazimah*. *Rashi*'s point is therefore that the *chakiros* are not merely procedural safeguards but are constitutive of valid testimony in capital cases: the time and place of the event are not themselves elements of the *aveira* but are essential to the testimonial quality that allows the judicial process to function with integrity.

## Discretionary Examinations

### Bdikot Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַמַּרְבֶּה בִּבְדִיקוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה מְשׁוּבָּח. מַעֲשֶׂה וּבָדַק בֶּן זַכַּאי בְּעוּקְצֵי תְּאֵנִים.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all** judges **who increase** the number of **examinations,** i.e., who add questions about the details of the event, **this is praiseworthy,** as this may clarify that the witnesses are lying. **An incident** occurred **and ben Zakkai examined** the witnesses by questioning them **about** the color and shape of **the stems of figs** in order to unearth a contradiction between the witnesses.

The *mishnah* continues: *kol hamarbeh bivdikot harei zeh meshubach* — one who multiplies *bedikot* questions is praiseworthy. The *bedikot* are the discretionary questions, left to the court's judgment, and an illustrative case is cited: *maaseh uvadak Ben Zakkai be'uktzei te'einim* — there was an incident in which Rabbi Yochanan Ben Zakkai examined witnesses about the fig stems at the site of a murder committed under a fig tree. Different varieties of figs have stems of different thicknesses, and Ben Zakkai asked the witnesses to describe them. This *bdikah* was designed to verify whether two witnesses were truly describing the same tree and the same scene. Naturally, each witness was examined separately — the entire system of interrogation requires the witnesses to be questioned apart from one another so that they cannot coordinate answers — and if one described thick stems while the other described thin ones, their accounts would be revealed as incompatible, exposing the inconsistency in their testimony.

## Chakiros vs. Bdikot: Consequences of Ignorance

### Chakiros vs. Bdikot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָה בֵּין חֲקִירוֹת לִבְדִיקוֹת? חֲקִירוֹת – אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״, עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה. בְּדִיקוֹת – אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ שְׁנַיִם אוֹמְרִים ״אֵין אָנוּ יוֹדְעִין״, עֵדוּתָן קַיֶּימֶת. אֶחָד חֲקִירוֹת וְאֶחָד בְּדִיקוֹת, בִּזְמַן שֶׁמַּכְחִישִׁין זֶה אֶת זֶה – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna explains: **What** is the difference **between interrogations and examinations?** With regard to **interrogations,** if **one** of the witnesses **says: I do not know** the answer, **their testimony is void** immediately. With regard to **examinations,** if **one says: I do not know** the answer, **and even** if **two say: We do not know** the answer, **their testimony** still **stands. Both** with regard to **interrogations and examinations, at a time when** the witnesses **contradict one another, their testimony is void.**

*Mah bein chakiros livdikot* — what is the difference between *chakiros* and *bedikot*? The *mishnah* answers: for *chakira* questions, if even one witness says *eini yodea* — "I don't know" — *edutan beteilah*, the testimony is entirely invalidated. The logic follows from *Rashi*'s principle: if a witness cannot specify when and where the event occurred, the testimony loses its quality of *edut sheyachol lehazimah*. A witness who says "I don't know what day of the week it was" has left an opening that prevents any subsequent *hazamah*.

For *bedikot*, by contrast, *afilu shenayim omrim ein anu yode'im edutan kayemet* — even if both witnesses say they do not know, the testimony stands. *Bdikot* are designed to catch witnesses in a contradiction, not to affirm the factual foundation of the testimony. So if one witness says the stems were thick and the other says he was not paying attention to the stems at all, that is perfectly acceptable — there is no contradiction and no deficiency in the core testimony. The *mishnah* then adds that both *chakiros* and *bedikot* share one rule: *bizman shemachchishim ze et ze edutan beteilah* — if the witnesses contradict one another, the testimony falls regardless of which type of question produced the contradiction.

## Temporal Discrepancies

### Month/Day Errors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׁנַיִם בְּחֹדֶשׁ״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה בַּחֹדֶשׁ״ – עֵדוּתָן קַיֶּימֶת, שֶׁזֶּה יוֹדֵעַ בְּעִיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל חֹדֶשׁ, וְזֶה אֵינוֹ יוֹדֵעַ בְּעִיבּוּרוֹ שֶׁל חֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna clarifies: If **one** witness **says** the event occurred **on the second of the month, and one** witness **says** that the event occurred **on the third of the month,** this is not regarded as a contradiction and **their testimony stands,** since it is possible to say **that this** witness **knows of** the **addition of** a day to the previous **month,** and according to his tally the event occurred on the second of the month, **and that** witness **does not know of** the **addition of** a day to the previous **month,** and according to his tally the event occurred on the third of the month. Their testimony is not considered incongruent.

*Echad omer bishnayim ba-chodesh ve-echad omer bishlosha ba-chodesh* — if one witness says the event occurred on the second of the month and the other says on the third, *edutan kayemet*. The testimony stands, because *sheze yodea be-iburo shel chodesh veze eino yodea be-iburo shel chodesh* — one witness may have known whether the previous month had thirty days or only twenty-nine, while the other did not. Months varied in length, and early in the month, when the precise day count since the month's beginning was not yet settled in everyone's mind, an off-by-one error is a reasonable human mistake. Notably, *Elul* was the one exception — since the time of Ezra, *Elul* never carried thirty days and was always twenty-nine — so there could be no ambiguity about *Elul* dates. But for other months, a one-day discrepancy at the beginning of the month is treated charitably.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בַּחֲמִשָּׁה״ – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, if **this** witness **says** the event occurred **on the third** of the month **and one** witness **says** the event occurred **on the fifth** of the month, **their testimony is void,** as this disparity cannot be attributed to a mere error. Therefore, their testimony is not congruent.

*Ze omer bishlosha ve-echad omer bachamisha* — but if one witness says the third and the other says the fifth, *edutan beteilah*. A two-day discrepancy is not a reasonable error, because months are always either twenty-nine or thirty days, never more. A person of the sort who would come to testify in court is expected to track the passage of days of the month, and being off by two days exceeds the latitude the court extends.

### Hour Errors & Pesach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בִּשְׁתֵּי שָׁעוֹת״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בְּשָׁלֹשׁ שָׁעוֹת״ – עֵדוּתָן קַיֶּימֶת. אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בְּשָׁלֹשׁ״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״בְּחָמֵשׁ״ – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, if **one** witness **says** that the event occurred **at two hours,** i.e., the second hour of the day from sunrise, **and one** witness **says** that the event occurred **at three hours, their testimony stands,** as one could reasonably err this amount in estimating the hour of the day. By contrast, if **one says** that the event occurred **at three** hours, **and one says** that the event occurred **at five** hours, **their testimony is void.**

*Echad omer bishtei sha'ot ve-echad omer bishalosh sha'ot* — one says it was during the second hour, the other says during the third hour: *edutan kayemet*. Without mechanical timepieces, witnesses tracked the hour by the sun's position, and a one-hour error is an ordinary human mistake. *Echad omer bishalosh ve-echad omer bachamesh* — one says the third hour, the other says the fifth: *edutan beteilah*. A two-hour discrepancy, being off by one hour in either direction, is beyond reasonable error.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: קַיֶּימֶת. אֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּחָמֵשׁ״, וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״בְּשֶׁבַע״ – עֵדוּתָן בְּטֵילָה, שֶׁבְּחָמֵשׁ חַמָּה בַּמִּזְרָח וּבְשֶׁבַע חַמָּה בַּמַּעֲרָב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** Also in this case their testimony **stands,** as one could reasonably err concerning even this length of time. Rabbi Yehuda adds: But if **one says** that the event occurred **at five** hours, **and one says** that the event occurred **at seven** hours, **their testimony is void.** Here the difference is recognizable to all, **since at five** hours **the sun** is **in the east and at seven the sun** is **in the west,** and one could not err concerning this. Therefore, their testimony is not congruent.

*Rabbi Yehuda omer kayemet* — Rabbi Yehuda, however, holds that even a two-hour discrepancy leaves the testimony valid, because Rabbi Yehuda's view is that a two-hour error is within the range of normal human imprecision. This position has interesting implications for *Pesach*-related questions that *Tosafot* raises. The *de'oraita* prohibition on eating *chametz* takes effect at noon on *erev Pesach*. The Rabbis added a prohibition at the sixth hour to safeguard against encroaching on the seventh. They further prohibited eating *chametz* during the fifth hour — the concern being that on a cloudy day one might confuse the fifth hour for the sixth. This yields an apparent difficulty: we generally do not issue a rabbinic decree to protect another rabbinic decree, *gezeira al ha-gezeira*. Various resolutions are offered; perhaps the fifth-hour prohibition is actually safeguarding the seventh hour, the *de'oraita* threshold, which would make it a two-hour protective buffer. Yet according to the *chachamim* who reject Rabbi Yehuda's position, a two-hour error is not a normal human mistake — so why enact a two-hour buffer? These are among the questions the issue raises.

### 5th-7th Hour Boundary

*Echad omer bachamesh ve-echad omer besheva* — one says the fifth hour and the other says the seventh: *edutan beteilah*, even according to Rabbi Yehuda, because *shebachamesh chama bamizrach u-vesheva chama bama'arav* — in the fifth hour the sun is in the east, while by the seventh hour it has already crossed to the western half of the sky. No reasonable person could mistake one for the other, since the sun's position has shifted unmistakably from one side to the other. This marks the outer limit beyond which even Rabbi Yehuda concedes the discrepancy is fatal to the testimony.

## Post-Interrogation P (II)

### Acquittal Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַחַר כָּךְ מַכְנִיסִין אֶת הַשֵּׁנִי וּבוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ. אִם נִמְצְאוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶם מְכֻוָּונִים, פּוֹתְחִין בִּזְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **And afterward,** after the court examines the first witness, **they bring in the second** witness **and examine him. If the statements** of the witnesses **are found** to be **congruent,** the court begins to deliberate the matter. **They open** the deliberations **with** an appeal to anyone who can find a reason to **acquit** the accused.

After the *chakiros* and *bedikot* have been conducted and the witnesses' answers found consistent — *im nimtze'u divreihem mechuvannim* — the court proceeds to deliberation. Even at this point, with two witnesses testifying to the defendant's guilt, the *mishnah* prescribes *poteach bizchut* — the proceedings open with a presumption toward acquittal. The court actively seeks reasons to find the defendant innocent before rendering any judgment.

### Student Elevation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אֶחָד מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים: ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עָלָיו זְכוּת״ – מַעֲלִין אוֹתוֹ וּמוֹשִׁיבִין אוֹתוֹ בֵּינֵיהֶם, וְלֹא הָיָה יוֹרֵד מִשָּׁם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ. אִם יֵשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו, שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ. וַאֲפִילּוּ הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״יֵשׁ לִי לְלַמֵּד עַל עַצְמִי זְכוּת״, שׁוֹמְעִין לוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיֵּשׁ מַמָּשׁ בִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** But if **one of the students said: I can to teach** a reason to **acquit him, they raise him** to the seat of the court **and seat him among them, and he would not descend from there the entire day,** but would sit and participate in their deliberations. **If the statement of** that student **has substance,** the court **listens to him. And** if **even** the accused **says: I can teach** a reason to **acquit me,** the court **listens to him** and considers his statement, **provided that his statement has substance.**

If, by contrast, one of the *talmidim* says *yesh li lelamed alav z'chut* — he has a reason to find the defendant innocent — *maalin oto*, the court brings him up and seats him among the judges for the remainder of the day. The entire proceeding in *dinei nefashot* reflects the obligation of *v'hitzilu ha-edah* — the community's affirmative duty to seek the defendant's acquittal — a principle derived from the laws of *ir miklat* but applied across all capital cases.

*Im yesh mamash b'divrav* — if there is genuine substance to what the *talmid* says, *shom'in lo*, the court listens to him. Remarkably, *afilu hu omer yesh li lelamed al atzmi z'chut* — even if the accused himself raises a ground for his own defense — *shom'in lo*, the court may hear him, *u-vilvad she-yesh mamash b'divrav*, provided there is actual substance to the claim. The accused is not presenting himself as a witness and is not testifying about facts; he is offering, in something like a hybrid procedural role, a ground that might tip the scales toward innocence. The *rishonim* debate exactly what legal role the accused plays in this moment, but the principle is clear: any opening toward acquittal is to be pursued. The courts will not, however, let this become a mechanism for indefinitely prolonging proceedings through empty claims — once a pattern of groundless assertions emerges, the court will cease to engage.

### Adjournment & Pairs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת, פְּטָרוּהוּ. וְאִם לָאו, מַעֲבִירִין אוֹתוֹ לְמָחָר, וּמִזְדַּוְּוגִין זוּגוֹת זוּגוֹת. הָיוּ מְמַעֲטִין מִמַּאֲכָל, וְלֹא הָיוּ שׁוֹתִין יַיִן כׇּל הַיּוֹם, וְנוֹשְׂאִין וְנוֹתְנִין כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה. וְלַמׇּחֳרָת מַשְׁכִּימִין וּבָאִין לְבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the court **found** it fit to **acquit him** during the deliberations, as all or a majority of the judges agreed to acquit him, **they excuse him. But if** a majority does **not** find it fit to acquit him, **they delay** his verdict **to** the **following day, and** they then **assign pairs** of judges to discuss the matter with each other. **They would minimize** their **food** intake **and they would not drink wine all day. And they** would **deliberate all night, and the following day they** would **arise early and come to court** and then vote again and tally the votes of the judges.

*Im matzu lo zechu petaruhu* — if they find a reason to acquit, they release him. *Ve-im lav, maavirin oto lemachar* — if they have found no exculpatory reason, they still do not render a final judgment. They adjourn until the following day. In the interim, *mizdavvegim zugot zugot* — the judges pair off with one another. Given that the court must have an odd number of members, at least one group of three must have formed.

### Judge Restrictions

During this interlude the judges observe certain austerities. *Hayu memaatim bemaachal* — they eat less, avoiding any suggestion of indulgence while bearing the weight of a capital decision. *Velo hayu shotin yayin kol ha-yom* — they drink no wine throughout the entire day, since a judge is disqualified from rendering a decision after consuming wine, and this rule remains in force across the entire deliberative period. *Kol ha-yom ve-nos'in ve-notenim kol ha-layla* — they engage in deliberation throughout the day and the entire night. Whether *kol ha-layla* means they must remain entirely awake throughout the night or means only that they should be occupied in discussion as much of the night as possible is a question raised by commentators. There is a suggestive parallel to the *Kohen Gadol*, who must remain awake the entire night before *Yom Kippur* — perhaps a broader parallelism exists between the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry into the *avoda* on *Yom Kippur* and the judges' entry into capital deliberation.

### Day Two: Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְזַכֶּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי מְזַכֶּה, וּמְזַכֶּה אֲנִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״. וְהַמְחַיֵּיב אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי מְחַיֵּיב, וּמְחַיֵּיב אֲנִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **One who** yesterday was of the opinion to **acquit** the defendant **says: I** said to **acquit, and I acquit in my place,** i.e., I stand by my statement to acquit. **And one who** yesterday was of the opinion to **deem** him **liable says: I** said to **deem** him **liable, and I deem** him **liable in my place.**

*U-le-macharot*, on the second day, *mashkimim u-va'in le-veit din* — the judges rise early and return to the *beit din*. Each judge then declares his position. *Hamezakeh omer: ani mezakeh u-mezakeh ani bi-mkomi* — one who votes for innocence declares: "I find him innocent and I remain in my place," meaning his position has not changed. *Ve-hamachayev omer: ani machayev u-machayev ani bi-mkomi* — one who votes guilty makes the corresponding declaration of consistency.

### Verdict Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה מְלַמֵּד זְכוּת, אֲבָל הַמְלַמֵּד זְכוּת אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחְזוֹר וּלְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **One who** yesterday **taught** a reason to deem him **liable may** then **teach** a reason to **acquit, but one who** yesterday **taught** a reason to **acquit may not then teach** a reason to deem him **liable.**

A judge who previously said guilty may now have been persuaded to say innocent — that switch is permitted. But *hamlamed zechut eino yachol lachzor lelamed chova* — a judge who previously argued for innocence cannot change to guilty. Even from the first day's deliberations, this asymmetry is operative: verdicts may only move in one direction, from conviction toward acquittal, never the reverse.

This creates a striking catch-22. If on the first day the entire court unanimously found the defendant guilty, and the following day some judges switch to innocent, that switch — although formally permitted — would actually move the verdict from the status of non-execution (since a unanimous guilty verdict results in acquittal) to possible execution. The precise *psak* in such a case is genuinely unclear.

## Voting & Court Expan (II)

### No Unanimous Guilt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָעוּ בַּדָּבָר – שְׁנֵי סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין מַזְכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ. אִם מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת – פְּטָרוּהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – עוֹמְדִים לַמִּנְיָן. שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מְזַכִּין וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין – זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** If **they erred in the matter,** as one of the judges forgot what he had said the previous day, **two judges' scribes,** who recorded the statements of the judges, **remind him. If** the court then **found** it fit to **acquit him** unanimously, **they excuse him, and if not** all of the judges determine to acquit, **they stand to count** the vote. If **twelve** judges vote to **acquit** him **and eleven** judges **deem** him **liable,** he is **acquitted.**

The rule that a unanimous guilty verdict results in acquittal rather than execution warrants attention. If all twenty-three judges find the defendant guilty, he is paradoxically set free. The concern underlying this rule is *groupthink* and the fear of external pressure — if every single member of a court has been convinced unanimously, something may be distorting the deliberation. Whether the basis is *gezeirat ha-katuv* or grounded in a realistic assessment of how consensus-without-dissent emerges, the rule reflects a deep suspicion of unanimity in capital cases. Statistical analysts in other contexts have similarly noted that 100% agreement itself is a signal that something is amiss in the process.

### Scribes' Role

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָעוּ בַּדָּבָר – שְׁנֵי סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין מַזְכִּירִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **they erred in the matter,** as one of the judges forgot what he had said the previous day, **two judges' scribes,** who recorded the statements of the judges, **remind him.**

*Shnei sofrei ha-dayanim mazkirin oto* — two court scribes keep the record of each judge's declared position, and their role is specifically to prevent a judge from switching his verdict in the prohibited direction. If a judge who had voted innocent attempts to shift to guilty, the scribes' record serves as the check against that impermissible change.

### First Vote

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם מָצְאוּ לוֹ זְכוּת – פְּטָרוּהוּ, וְאִם לָאו – עוֹמְדִים לַמִּנְיָן. שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מְזַכִּין וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין – זַכַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** If **the court then** **found** it fit to **acquit him** unanimously, **they excuse him, and if not** all of the judges determine to acquit, **they stand to count** the vote. If **twelve** judges vote to **acquit** him **and eleven** judges **deem** him **liable,** he is **acquitted.**

After all deliberations, *omdim le-minyan* — the court rises to a formal vote count. *Im matzu lo zechut petaruhu* — if they find grounds for innocence, he is freed. *Ve-im lav* — if not (note again the circuitous phrasing, avoiding the direct statement that he is guilty) — *omdim le-minyan* — they proceed to the formal tally.

### 23-Judge Voting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְזַכִּין, וַאֲפִילּוּ אַחַד עָשָׂר מְזַכִּין וְאַחַד עָשָׂר מְחַיְּיבִין וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״, וַאֲפִילּוּ עֶשְׂרִים וּשְׁנַיִם מְזַכִּין אוֹ מְחַיְּיבִין וְאֶחָד אוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ״ – יוֹסִיפוּ הַדַּיָּינִין. וְכַמָּה מוֹסִיפִין? שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם, עַד שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In a case where **twelve** judges **deem** him **liable and eleven** judges **acquit; or even** if **eleven** judges **acquit and eleven deem** him **liable and one** judge **says: I do not know; or even** if **twenty-two** judges **acquit or deem** him **liable and one** judge **says: I do not know,** the judge who said he does not know is disregarded, and **the judges add** additional judges to the court until they reach a definitive ruling. **And how many** judges **do they add?** They add **pairs of two** judges each time they do not reach a ruling **until** there are **seventy-one** judges, but no more than that.

*Shenayim asar mazakin ve-achad asar mechayavin* — if twelve say innocent and eleven say guilty, *zakai*, he is acquitted, since twelve constitutes a majority. *Shenayim asar mechayavin ve-achad asar mazakin* — if twelve say guilty and eleven innocent, that is likewise inconclusive as a final verdict: conviction requires a majority of three, meaning at least thirteen against ten in a court of twenty-three. *Ve-afilu achad asar mazakin ve-achad asar mechayavin ve-echad omer eini yodea* — even if it is eleven to eleven with one abstention, *afilu esrim u-shenayim mazakin o mechayavin ve-echad omer eini yodea*, or even if twenty-two vote one way and one judge says *eini yodea*, I cannot decide — then *yosifu ha-dayanim*, more judges must be added. It may seem puzzling that adding judges resolves the difficulty created by one judge who abstains: the abstaining judge's own position will not be changed by the addition of new judges. How this actually resolves the problem is itself a question the *gemara* will address.

### Adding Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה מוֹסִיפִין? שְׁנַיִם שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And how many** judges **do they add?** They add **pairs of two** judges each time.

*Ve-chamah mosifin — shenayim shenayim* — judges are added two at a time, since the court must always remain odd-numbered. The court expands incrementally in pairs.

### 71-Judge Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד שִׁבְעִים וְאֶחָד. שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה מְזַכִּין וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה מְחַיְּיבִין – זַכַּאי. שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה מְחַיְּיבִין וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וַחֲמִשָּׁה מְזַכִּין – דָּנִין אֵלּוּ כְּנֶגֶד אֵלּוּ, עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה אֶחָד מִן הַמְחַיְּיבִין דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **until** there are **seventy-one** judges, but no more than that. **At that point, if** **thirty-six** judges **acquit and thirty-five** judges **deem** him **liable,** he is **acquitted.** If **thirty-six** judges **deem** him **liable and thirty-five** judges **acquit, they** continue to **deliberate** the matter, **these** judges **against those** judges, **until one of those who deems** him **liable sees** the validity of **the statements of those who acquit** and changes his position.

*Ad shivim ve-echad* — until the court reaches seventy-one members, the maximum permitted. At seventy-one: *sheloshim ve-shisha mazakin u-sheloshim ve-chamishah mechayavin* — thirty-six for innocence and thirty-five for guilt yields an acquittal. *Sheloshim ve-shisha mechayavin u-sheloshim ve-chamishah mazakin* — thirty-six for guilt and thirty-five for innocence produces a deadlock, because to execute one needs a majority of three, meaning thirty-seven against thirty-four, and thirty-six against thirty-five is insufficient. Yet a majority also cannot simply acquit since more judges voted for guilt. In this deadlock, *danin elu keneged elu ad she-yireh echad min ha-mechayavin divrei ha-mazakin* — the two sides argue with one another until one of those voting guilty is persuaded to shift to innocent. It is, in effect, a procedural filibuster: the court cannot proceed to execution without achieving the required supermajority, and the burden falls on the side of guilt to yield.

## Biblical Source: Gezeirah Shavah

### Three Contexts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְדָרַשְׁתָּ וְחָקַרְתָּ וְשָׁאַלְתָּ הֵיטֵב״, וְאוֹמֵר ״וְהֻגַּד לְךָ וְשָׁמָעְתָּ וְדָרַשְׁתָּ הֵיטֵב״, וְאוֹמֵר ״וְדָרְשׁוּ הַשּׁוֹפְטִים הֵיטֵב״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? What is the source for these seven interrogations? **Rav Yehuda says:** It is **as the verse states** with regard to an idolatrous city: **"And you shall inquire, and investigate, and ask diligently"** (Deuteronomy 13:15); **and** it **states** with regard to one who worships idols: **"If it be told to you and you have heard it and inquired diligently"** (Deuteronomy 17:4); **and** it **states** with regard to conspiring witnesses: **"And the judges shall inquire diligently"** (Deuteronomy 19:18). Taken together, there are seven interrogations alluded to in these verses by each instance of the terms inquire, investigate, and diligently.

The *gemara* now turns to the biblical derivation of the seven *chakiros*. *Mena hanei milei* — where is this learned from? *Amar Rav Yehudah d'amar kra* — Rav Yehudah the *amora* derives it from three distinct biblical passages, two of which appear in last week's *parasha* and one in this week's. The three contexts are: *ir ha-nidachat* — the wayward city that turns to *avodah zarah*; an individual case of *avodah zarah*; and the case of *eidim zomemim* — conspiring witnesses. Before the biblical analysis, however, the *Rambam* in the first chapter of *Hilchot Edut* should be noted. *Halacha* 4 states: *mitzvat aseh lidrosh ha-edim ve-lachkor otam* — there is a positive *mitzvah* on the court to conduct the *derishah* and the *chakirah*. *U-leharbot bi-she'elatan* — to multiply the questions. *U-medakdekin aleihem* — to be exacting with the witnesses. *U-masi'in otam me-inyan le-inyan betoch ha-she'elah* — to move fluidly between topics during the questioning in order to disorient witnesses who are fabricating testimony, since such interrogation techniques — familiar from any serious investigative context — are effective at revealing inconsistency and deception. The *Rambam* adds that the *dayanim* must be careful not to inadvertently coach the witnesses by revealing information that could help them construct a more coherent false account. The *Rambam* then enumerates the seven *chakiros* as in our *mishnah*, adding that there are further questions beyond the seven that also carry the status of *chakiros* — though, as noted, in his *Perush ha-Mishnayos* the *Rambam* seems to treat those additional questions as a separate category, raising interesting questions about the consequences of a witness's failure to answer them.

### Gezeirah on 'Heitiv'

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאֵימָא: חֲדָא חֲדָא כְּדִכְתִיבָא? דְּאִם כֵּן, לִיכְתְּבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא בַּחֲדָא! כֵּיוָן דְּכוּלְּהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי כְּתִיבָא, מֵהֲדָדֵי יָלְפִי, וְכֵיוָן דְּיָלְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי, כְּמַאן דִּכְתִיב בַּחֲדָא דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that **each one** is **as it is written,** and conclude that the Torah requires, respectively, three, two, and two interrogations in the three cases of idol worship, an idolatrous city, and conspiring witnesses, discussed in those verses? **As, if** it is **so,** i.e., if there is a requirement of seven interrogations in all cases of capital law, **let the Merciful One write them in one** place, and it would be derived from there to other cases. The Gemara answers: **Since they are all written together,** i.e., they all employ analogous terminology, **one learns** the *halakhot* of **one from the other, and once one learns** the *halakhot* of **one from the other,** they **are considered as if they are written in one** place.

The three *pesukim* are: *ve-darashta ve-chakarta ve-sha'alta heiteiv* — regarding *ir ha-nidachat*, in which the requirement to investigate, search further, and ask very well uses three distinct verbs plus the word *heiteiv*; *ve-hugad l'cha ve-shamata ve-darashta heiteiv* — regarding individual *avodah zarah*, where the court is told it was reported, you heard, and you investigated thoroughly; and *ve-darshu ha-shoftim heiteiv* — regarding *eidim zomemim*, where the judges are required to investigate very well.

If we simply count the operative words across the three *pesukim*, we might say each passage contributes its own set of requirements independently. One might then ask: why not treat each passage in isolation and conclude, as Rabbi Yosi does, that each contributes only three requirements? *De-im kein l'chitivinhu Rachmana b'chada* — the *Torah* could have stated the requirement of investigation once, in a single place. The fact that all three appear in separate *halachic* contexts, each with its own word *heiteiv*, signals that they are meant to be read together — *keivan d'culhu bahadi hadadi ktivi, mehadadi yalfei* — and since they are learned from one another, it is *k'man d'chtiv b'chada dami*, as though written together as a unified law. This is not quite a *binyan av* but something related — splicing together three separate *pesukim* to generate the number seven.

### Case Distinctions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָא לָא דָּמְיָאן לַהֲדָדֵי? (סִימָן: פָּלֵט, סַיִיף, הַתְרָאָה.)
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** those three instances of idol worship, an idolatrous city, and conspiring witnesses **are not similar to each other,** so how can one derive the *halakhot* of one from the other? The Gemara records **a mnemonic** device for the following terms: **Escapes, sword, forewarning.**

The obvious objection is that the three cases are not analogous, and ordinarily we would not combine non-analogous cases into a single legal construct. A *siman* captures the three differences: *palat, sayif, hatra'ah* — property, sword, and warning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת לְהָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי לָא דָּמְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן מָמוֹנָן פָּלֵט. עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה לְהָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי לָא דָּמְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן בְּסַיִיף. עֵדִים זוֹמְמִין לְהָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי לָא דָּמְיָא, שֶׁכֵּן צְרִיכִים הַתְרָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** What are the differences? The case of **an idolatrous city** is **not similar to those two,** i.e., idol worship and conspiring witnesses, **because** for the others **their money escapes** their fate. Although the court executes those transgressors, their money is not confiscated. By contrast, in the case of an idolatrous city, not only are the residents executed, all of their property is destroyed. **Idol worship** is **not similar to those two, as** the other two transgressions are judged **by the sword,** while the punishment for idol worship is death by stoning. The case of **conspiring witnesses** is **not similar to those two, as** those who transgress the two other transgressions **require forewarning** in order to be liable for the transgression, while conspiring witnesses are not forewarned before they testify.

*Ir ha-nidachat l'hanach tarti lo damya* — *ir ha-nidachat* is unlike the other two: *sheken mamonam palat* — in *avodah zarah* and *eidim zomemim* the participants' property is preserved, but in *ir ha-nidachat* the property of the city's inhabitants is incinerated along with the city itself.

*Avodah zarah l'hanach tarti lo damya* — *avodah zarah* is unlike the other two: *sheken b'sayif* — both *ir ha-nidachat* and *eidim zomemim* (in the context of a murder conviction) are executed by beheading with a sword, while *avodah zarah* carries execution by *skila*, stoning.

*Eidim zomemim l'hanach tarti lo damya* — *eidim zomemim* is unlike the other two: *sheken tzrichim hatra'ah* — both other cases require that the offender be warned in advance, but *eidim zomemim* famously does not require *hatra'ah*. This is nearly unique among capital crimes; presumably the nature of a conspiracy to corrupt the judicial process is such that a formal warning is considered superfluous or inapplicable.

Since each of the three cases has a distinctive feature that sets it apart from the other two, we cannot simply combine them as parallel cases through a general literary construction.

### Gezeirah Mufneh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* בְּ״הֵיטֵב״ ״הֵיטֵב״ יָלְפִינַן מֵהֲדָדֵי, וּגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה מוּפְנָה. דְּאִי לָא מוּפְנָה, אִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: This is not an association based on conceptual similarity alone; rather, **we learn one from the other based on** a verbal analogy employing the words **"diligently"** and **"diligently."** "Diligently" is used in all three verses. The Gemara comments: **And** this **verbal analogy** must be **free,** i.e., these terms must be superfluous in their context. The Torah included them for the express purpose of establishing the verbal analogy. A verbal analogy that is based on otherwise extraneous terms cannot be logically refuted. This is **because if** these terms are **not free,** the verbal analogy **can be refuted.**

*Be-heiteiv heiteiv yalfinan mehadadi u-gzeirah shavah mufneh* — the resolution is that the word *heiteiv* appears in each of the three contexts, enabling a *gezeirah shavah* that equates the three cases despite their differences. A *gezeirah shavah* operates on a different principle than a *binyan av*: it does not require the cases to be similar; it requires only that the identical word appear in each context, with a *masorah* supporting the connection. Because it is a *gezeirah shavah*, the underlying dissimilarities between the cases do not defeat the *drasha*.

The *gezeirah shavah* must be *mufneh* — open-ended, meaning the word used in the comparison is extraneous to its immediate context, present specifically to be available for the *drasha*. *Rashi* explains: a *gezeirah shavah ha-mufneh lemedim ve-ein meshivin* — a *gezeirah shavah* that is *mufneh* cannot be challenged; it is as watertight a proof as exists in the system of *drashot*. But if it is not *mufneh*, it is open to refutation, which would explain how Rabbi Yosi can disagree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לָאיֵי, אַפְנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֵי. מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִיכְתַּב ״וְדָרְשׁוּ וְחָקְרוּ״, וְשַׁנִּי קְרָא בְּדִיבּוּרֵיהּ בְּ״הֵיטֵב״, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: This is **not so [*la'ei*],** i.e., the verbal analogy cannot be refuted, as **they are free.** The Gemara explains: **Since** the Torah **could have written: And they inquire and investigate, and the verse modified its statement by** writing **"diligently," learn from it** that this term's function is **to have it be free,** to enable a verbal analogy.

How is *heiteiv* shown to be extraneous? In two of the three cases the word is obviously unnecessary: the *pesukim* already have *ve-darashta ve-chakarta* — "you shall investigate and you shall search" — and adding *heiteiv*, "very well," is redundant. The *Torah* could have said *darosh tidrosh* or *chakor tachkor*, using the doubled-verb form that Hebrew uses for emphasis, and the requirement to investigate thoroughly would have been conveyed. By choosing *heiteiv* instead, the *Torah* signals that this word is present specifically to be available for the *gezeirah shavah* — *shma mina le-afnu'ei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאַכַּתִּי מוּפְנֶה מִצַּד אֶחָד הוּא! בִּשְׁלָמָא הָנָךְ תַּרְתֵּי מוּפְנֶה הוּא, מִשּׁוּם דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב. אֶלָּא עִיר הַנִּדַּחַת, מַאי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב? הָא כְּתִיבָא כּוּלְּהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this explanation: **And yet it** is **free on** only **one side,** as the term is superfluous in two of the verses but not in all three of them. The Gemara explains: **Granted,** with regard to **these two,** idol worship and conspiring witnesses, **it is free, because it could have** been **written** only: And you inquire and you investigate, but instead the word "diligently" also appears in the verse. **But** in the verses concerning **an idolatrous city, what could it have written** differently? **They are all written.**

One might object that *heiteiv* is extraneous only in two of the three *pesukim*, not all three — making it *mufneh mitzad echad*, open-ended from only one side. The *gemara* responds: *bishlama hanech tarti mufneh* — the two cases of *avodah zarah* and *eidim zomemim* are clearly *mufneh* given their alternative formulations. For *ir ha-nidachat*, however, *mai havah leih le-michtav* — what alternative phrasing would have been available that would not have used *heiteiv*? The answer is the same: it could have used the doubled-verb form, *darosh tidrosh* or *chakor tachkor*, for emphasis. Since it did not, and instead chose *heiteiv*, that too is *mufneh* — *shma mina le-afnu'ei* — open for use in the *drasha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָתָם נָמֵי אַפְנוֹיֵי מוּפְנֵה, מִדַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמִכְתַּב ״דָּרוֹשׁ תִּדְרֹשׁ״ אוֹ ״חָקוֹר תַּחְקֹר״, וְשַׁנִּי קְרָא בְּדִיבּוּרֵיהּ בְּ״הֵיטֵב״ – שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לְאַפְנוֹיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** it is **also free,** as the term is superfluous in that verse as well. **Since** the Torah **could have written: You shall inquire, or: You shall investigate,** in an emphatic form by doubling the verb, **and the verse modified its statement by** writing: "And you shall inquire and investigate **diligently," learn from it** that this term's function is **to have it be free** in order to enable a verbal analogy. By way of this verbal analogy, the Gemara has derived the requirement for seven interrogations in cases of capital law where the punishment is either death by stoning or death by the sword.

Having established the *gezeirah shavah mufneh* and combined the three *pesukim*, the three *pesukim* yield the following words with independent semantic weight: seven operative words across the passages, producing the seven *chakiros*. Three uses of *heiteiv* provide the basis for the *gezeirah shavah* itself. The next matter is whether the cases of *nechnakin* — strangulation — and *nisrafin* — burning — can be derived from these same sources or require further derivation.

### Kal Vachomer and Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְאָתוּ נֶחְנָקִין בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִנִּסְקָלִין וּמִנֶּהֱרָגִין, וְאָתוּ נִשְׂרָפִין בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר מִנִּסְקָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** one can **learn** that this applies to **those** transgressors **who are strangled** as well, **by** means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from those** transgressors who are **stoned and from those** who are **killed** by the sword. Just as in these cases, where the transgression is severe as evidenced by their severe mode of execution, seven interrogations are required, the requirement should apply all the more so to those transgressors who are strangled, who committed a less severe transgression. **And** one can **learn** that this applies to **those** transgressors who are **burned** as well, **by** means of **an *a fortiori*** inference **from those** transgressors who are **stoned.**

*Ve-atu nechnakin be-kal va-chomer mi-skilin u-miharugin* — those executed by strangulation can be learned by *kal va-chomer* from the contexts of stoning and beheading. *Ve-atu nisrafin be-kal va-chomer mi-skilin* — burning is learned by *kal va-chomer* from stoning.

The structure of this *kal va-chomer* deserves attention. The argument is: if even in cases of the most severe punishment, where we might be most motivated to convict, we nonetheless impose the rigor of *drisha ve-chakira* that works to protect the accused, then *kal va-chomer*, in cases of lesser punishment where we have less motivation to convict, the same requirement certainly applies. The direction is perhaps counterintuitive — learning from the more severe to the less severe in a way that appears to impose *additional* requirements — but the logic holds when the *drisha ve-chakira* is understood as a protective mechanism rather than as a precondition for punishment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָנִיחָא לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי סְקִילָה חֲמוּרָה, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר שְׂרֵיפָה חֲמוּרָה, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this last inference: **This works out well according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that **stoning** is more **severe** than burning (see 49b). Consequently, it is possible to derive by means of an *a fortiori* inference the *halakha* of a transgression whose punishment is burning from the *halakha* of a transgression whose punishment is stoning. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who says** that **burning** is more **severe** than stoning, **what is there to say?**

*Ha niicha le-Rabbanan d'amri skila chamura* — this *kal va-chomer* works smoothly according to the Rabbis, who hold that *skila* is the most severe form of capital punishment, so learning from stoning to burning presents no difficulty. *Ella le-Rabbi Shimon d'amar sreifa chamura* — but Rabbi Shimon holds that burning is the most severe execution. If burning is more severe than stoning, then the *kal va-chomer* from stoning to burning cannot work, since one cannot learn from a less severe case to a more severe one in this direction. *Mai ika le-meimar* — how does Rabbi Shimon account for *nisrafin*?

### Rav Yehudah: 11 Words & Heitiv

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: ״וְהִנֵּה אֱמֶת נָכוֹן״, ״וְהִנֵּה אֱמֶת נָכוֹן״ – הָא חַד סְרֵי. שֶׁבַע – לְשֶׁבַע חֲקִירוֹת, דַּל תְּלָת – לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה, פָּשָׁא לְהוּ חֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yehuda said:** There are an additional two phrases in the verses that are used to teach the *halakha* of the seven interrogations. The verse states with regard to the idolatrous city: "And you shall inquire, and investigate, and ask diligently **and behold it be truth,** the matter **certain"** (Deuteronomy 13:15). Additionally, the verse states with regard to idol worship: "You have inquired diligently **and behold it be truth,** the matter **certain"** (Deuteronomy 17:4). When one adds the two usages of "truth" and "certain" to the seven expressions stated earlier, **consequently,** there are **eleven** expressions; **seven** are used **to** teach the *halakha* of the **seven interrogations,** and of the four that remain, **remove three,** one for each verse, **for** the purpose of the **verbal analogy,** and **one remains.**

*Ella amar Rav Yehudah: ve-hinei emet nachon ha-davar* — Rav Yehudah now offers an alternative *drasha*. The *pasuk* says *ve-hinei emet nachon ha-davar* — "and behold, the matter is true and established" — which adds additional words to the count of operative terms across the three *pesukim*. Tallying carefully: *sheva le-sheva chakirot* — seven of the eleven words derived from the combined *pesukim* provide the source for the seven *chakiros*. *Tlat le-gezeirah shavah* — three words (the three appearances of *heiteiv*) are reserved for the *gezeirah shavah*. *Pash lihu chada le-Rabbi Shimon le-atuyei nisrafin* — one word remains, and this remaining word serves, for Rabbi Shimon, as the explicit textual inclusion of *nisrafin*, since Rabbi Shimon cannot use the *kal va-chomer* that the Rabbis rely upon.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לְאֵתוֹיֵי נִשְׂרָפִין. לְרַבָּנַן, מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר – טָרַח וְכָתַב לַהּ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** this additional expression serves **to include** the *halakha* that the seven interrogations applies to **those** transgressors who are **burned. According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis** as well, who learned this from an *a fortiori* inference, the additional expression teaches this same *halakha*, as with regard to **a matter that can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, the **verse** nevertheless **takes the trouble and writes it** explicitly.

For the Rabbis, who could have learned *sreifa* by *kal va-chomer*, the fact that the *Torah* explicitly includes it anyway reflects the general principle that *milta d'atya be-kal va-chomer tarach ve-chatav lah kra* — the *Torah* sometimes writes out explicitly even what could have been derived logically, and the Rabbis simply count that explicit inclusion as an expression of the same principle. The concern about *ein onshin min ha-din* — that one cannot derive punishments from *kal va-chomer* — does not apply here, since the *drisha ve-chakira* regime is itself oriented toward acquittal rather than punishment; its purpose is to protect the accused, not to impose liability.

### Rabbi Abbahu Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מְגַדֵּף בַּהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: אֵימָא לְאֵתוֹיֵי שְׁמֹנֶה חֲקִירוֹת! וּשְׁמֹנֶה חֲקִירוֹת מִי אִיכָּא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu ridiculed this** explanation: **Say** this additional expression serves **to add** that there are **eight interrogations.** The Gemara asks: **And are there eight interrogations?**

*Megaded beh Rabbi Abbahu* — Rabbi Abbahu challenges Rav Yehudah's accounting. If the Rabbis have an eleventh word available and use it to explicitly include *nisrafin* even though they could have derived it by *kal va-chomer*, why not instead use that eleventh word to generate an *eighth chakira* rather than seven? Rabbi Abbahu's question is pointed: Rav Yehudah's resolution of the problem for Rabbi Shimon works only if the Rabbis commit that eleventh word to *nisrafin*. But why is that the necessary use of the word rather than expanding the number of *chakiros*?

## Eight Chakiros: Proposals & Objections

### Baraita: 8 Chakiros

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּשְׁמֹנֶה חֲקִירוֹת מִי אִיכָּא?! אַלְּמָה לָא? וְהָאִיכָּא לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּכַמָּה בְּשָׁעָה. וְתַנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הָיוּ בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ בִּשְׁמֹנֶה חֲקִירוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** What interrogation can be added? The Gemara suggests: And **why not? But there is** the possibility **to add** this question to the interrogation: **When in the hour,** i.e., at what time within the hour. **And this is also taught** in a *baraita*: **They would examine him with eight interrogations.**

*Shmoneh chakirot mi ika* — the *gemara* asks whether there is any *tanna* who actually holds that there are eight *chakiros*. *Ve-ha ika le-atuyei b'khama b'sha'ah* — the answer is: there is indeed a possible eighth question, namely how far into the hour the event occurred. Beyond the hour itself, one could ask witnesses to specify how many *dakin* or *chalakim* — the Rabbis' more precise sub-hour units — had elapsed. *Chalakim* are units of three and a third seconds each, too fine for any witness to track in a criminal event, but some sub-hourly precision might in principle be demanded.

*Ve-tanya nami hachi* — and indeed there is a *baraita* that reflects this position: *hayu bodkin oto bi-shmoneh chakirot* — they would examine him with eight questions.

### Rav Meir's Perception of Time

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* הָנִיחָא לְאַבָּיֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אֵין אָדָם טוֹעֶה וְלֹא כְּלוּם, וּלְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא נָמֵי דְּאָמַר: אָדָם טוֹעֶה מַשֶּׁהוּ – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְאַבָּיֵי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם טוֹעֶה חֲצִי שָׁעָה, וּלְרָבָא דְּאָמַר: טָעוּ אִינָשֵׁי טוּבָא – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This works out well according to** the explanation of **Abaye of** the opinion **of Rabbi Meir, who says,** in explaining Rabbi Meir's opinion: **A person does not err at all,** i.e., when witnesses state an hour in which an event occurred, it is assumed that they are completely accurate. **And also according to the version** of his statement **that** Abaye **says: A person errs a bit;** it is **well.** Consequently, there is a reason to ask at what time during the hour the event occurred. **But according to** the explanation of **Abaye of** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, who says,** in explaining Rabbi Yehuda's opinion: **A person errs** up to **half an hour, and according to** the opinion of **Rava, who says: People err** even **more** than that; **what can be said?** There is no reason to ask at what time during the hour the event occurred, as any inconsistency will be ascribed to an innocent error.

*Ha nicha le-Abaye aliba de-Rabbi Meir d'amar ein adam toeh ella khlum* — this works well according to Abaye's reading of Rabbi Meir, which is that a person knows precisely what time it is, even within the hour. *U-le-hach lishna nami d'amar adam toeh mashehu* — and it also works under the version that holds a person might err by only a small amount within the hour. *Ella le-Abaye aliba de-Rabbi Yehuda d'amar adam toeh chatzi sha'ah* — but according to Abaye's reading of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that a person can err by as much as half an hour, and *u-le-Rava d'amar ta'u inshei tuvei* — according to Rava, who interpreted the *mishnah*'s handling of a two-hour discrepancy as reflecting that people err substantially — how could an eighth question about the sub-hourly interval even be asked if witnesses routinely cannot be held to precise hour-level accuracy?

### Yovel Cycle as 8th Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּכַמָּה בַּיּוֹבֵל. הַיְינוּ בְּאֵיזֶה שָׁבוּעַ! אֶלָּא, לְאֵתוֹיֵי בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹבֵל. וְאִידַּךְ: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר בְּאֵיזֶה שָׁבוּעַ, לָא בָּעֵי בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** there is the possibility **to add** the question: **In how many** years **within the jubilee,** i.e., in which of the fifty years, the event occurred. The Gemara questions this explanation: **This is** exactly the same question as: **In which seven-year** period of the jubilee did the event occur, as one can calculate the year within the jubilee by knowing the seven-year period. The Gemara suggests: **Rather,** there is the possibility **to add** the question of: **In which jubilee** cycle did the event occur, in the current cycle or the previous cycle? **And the other** Sage, i.e., Rabbi Abbahu, who assumes that there cannot be an eighth interrogation, holds that **once** the witness **said in which seven-year** period of the jubilee the event occurred, there is **no need** to ask **in which jubilee** it occurred, as the court would have no reason to think that he is testifying about an event that occurred fifty years prior.

The *gemara* proposes another candidate for a possible eighth *chakira*: *ela le-atuyei b'eizeh yovel* — perhaps the eighth question is not about the sub-hourly time but rather about which *yovel* cycle it is. The first *chakira* already asks *be-eizo shavua*, in which seven-year cycle within the *yovel* period. A logically distinct question would be *be-eizeh yovel* — in which fifty-year *yovel* cycle, counting from the inception of the *yovel* system, did this event occur? The *gemara* then considers whether this is truly a distinct question from *be-eizo shavua* — and concludes that it is: *hainu be-eizeh shavua* is asking about the position within a *yovel* cycle, while *be-eizeh yovel* asks which *yovel* cycle altogether.

### Yovel Tracking Ended

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר בְּאֵיזֶה שָׁבוּעַ לָא בָּעֵי בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **once** the witness **said in which seven-year** period of the jubilee the event occurred, there is **no need** to ask **in which jubilee** it occurred.

However, this eighth question is not asked in practice, and the reason is historical. *Keivan d'amar be-eizeh shavua lo ba'ei be-eizeh yovel* — since after a certain point in history, no one was tracking which *yovel* cycle they were in, the question becomes unanswerable and therefore moot. Already during the *Bayit Rishon* period there are indications that tracking of *yovel* years had lapsed — though there is some debate about precisely when — and certainly by the *Bayit Sheni* period the *yovel* was not observed. A question no witness could be expected to answer cannot form part of the mandatory *chakiros*. There is perhaps also a polemical dimension here: the authors of *Sefer Yovelot* — the Book of Jubilees, a work associated with sectarian groups outside the rabbinic tradition — placed enormous emphasis on tracking *yovel* cycles. A quiet rabbinic dismissal of *be-eizeh yovel* as an unanswerable question may carry some polemical resonance against those groups.

## Rabbi Yosi's Challenge

### Yosi Rejoinder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תַּנְיָא, אָמַר לָהֶם רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לַחֲכָמִים: לְדִבְרֵיכֶם, מִי שֶׁבָּא וְאָמַר ״אֶמֶשׁ הֲרָגוֹ״, אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁבוּעַ?״, ״בְּאֵיזוֹ שָׁנָה?״, ״בְּאֵיזֶה חֹדֶשׁ?״, ״בְּכַמָּה בַּחֹדֶשׁ?״
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* the reasoning behind this dispute: **Rabbi Yosei said to the Rabbis: According to your statement** that the court asks all seven interrogations, when there is a witness **who came** to court **and said:** The accused **killed** the victim **last night,** should the judge **say to him** in his examination: **In which seven-year** period of the jubilee, **in which year, in which month, on which** day **of the month** did the accused murder the victim? What would be the purpose of asking these questions? It suffices that they ask him about the day, the time, and the place.

*Tanya, amar lahem Rabbi Yosei la-chachamim* — the *baraita* then records Rabbi Yosi's rhetorical challenge to the *chachamim*: *le-divreichem mi sheba ve-amar emesh harag* — according to your position, if a witness comes to court and says "yesterday he committed murder," do you intend to ask him *be-eizeh shavua be-eizeh shana be-eizeh chodesh be-kama ba-chodesh* — which seven-year cycle, which year, which month, which day of the month? The questions are plainly unnecessary when the event occurred *yesterday*; asking which year or which month is ludicrous when there is no ambiguity whatsoever.

### Rabbis' Counter-Rejoinder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, מִי שֶׁבָּא וְאָמַר ״עַכְשָׁו הֲרָגוֹ״, אוֹמֵר לוֹ ״בְּאֵיזֶה יוֹם?״, ״בְּאֵיזֶה שָׁעָה?״, ״בְּאֵיזֶה מָקוֹם?״
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to him: According to your statement,** when there is a witness **who came** to court **and said:** The accused **killed** the victim just **now,** should the judge **say to him** in his examination: **On which day, at which hour, in what place** did the accused murder the victim? The witness already stated that the murder had just occurred.

*Amru lo* — the Rabbis respond in kind: *le-vareicha mi sheba ve-amar achshav harag* — according to *your* position, Rabbi Yosi, if someone comes to court and says "he murdered him just now, today," would you still ask *be-eizeh yom be-eizeh sha'ah be-eizeh makom* — which day of the week, at what hour, in what place? The questions are equally unnecessary in such a case; it happened moments ago in an identifiable location. Each side, Rabbi Yosi's and the Rabbis', can identify a scenario where the other's required questions are pointless.

### Pressure Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא צָרִיךְ, רָמֵינַן עֲלֵיהּ כִּדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. הָכָא נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא צָרִיךְ, רָמֵינַן עֲלֵיהּ כִּדְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis continue: **Rather, even** according to your opinion, **although it is not necessary** to ask these questions in this particular case, **we impose** the obligation to answer all the interrogations **on** the witnesses, **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** that the court beleaguers the witnesses in attempt to confuse them (see 32b). **Here too, although it is not necessary** to ask these questions in this particular case, **we impose** the obligation to answer all the interrogations **on** the witnesses, **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar.**

*Ela af al gav de-lo tzarich, raminin alei* — the Rabbis' answer is that even when the questions are technically unnecessary, the court imposes them regardless, *ke-di'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — in accordance with the principle stated by Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. *Lehatricham* — the purpose is to put pressure on the witnesses. *De-amar mesin hayu et ha-edim mi-makom le-makom* — Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's teaching is that part of the formal court proceedings involved moving witnesses from place to place, physically relocating them during the process as a form of deliberate pressure. Formal interrogation, even with questions whose answers are already known, serves the same function: the atmosphere of scrutiny and the demand to answer precisely creates psychological pressure that exposes deception in ways that a more casual inquiry would not.

### Yosi's Contextual Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי: ״אֶמֶשׁ הֲרָגוֹ״ – שְׁכִיחַ בְּרוֹב עֵדִיּוֹת, ״עַכְשָׁיו הֲרָגוֹ״ – לָא שְׁכִיחַ בְּרוֹב עֵדִיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what would **Rabbi Yosei** respond to this claim? The Gemara explains: He holds that the witness testifying: The accused **killed** the victim **last night, is common in most testimonies.** Therefore, this testimony is taken into account when deciding the content of the interrogations. By contrast, the witness testifying: The accused **killed** the victim just **now, is not common in most testimonies,** i.e., it is relatively rare. Therefore, this testimony is not taken into account when deciding the content of the interrogations.

Rabbi Yosi, however, distinguishes between the two scenarios: *emesh harag shachiach le-rov edyot* — "yesterday he committed murder" is a type of testimony that actually arises with some frequency in ordinary court proceedings; a witness who saw something yesterday and comes today to testify is a common occurrence. *Achshav harag lo shachiach le-rov edyot* — "he committed murder right now, this instant" is not a normal form of testimony. Because the scenario of "yesterday" is common in practice, Rabbi Yosi is willing to concede that it requires the formal procedure even when technically redundant. The extreme edge case of the murder that literally just occurred does not shape the formal rule.
