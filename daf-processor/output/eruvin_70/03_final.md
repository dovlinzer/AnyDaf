# Eruvin 70 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Recent Issues: Labor & S…

### Non-Jew Labor, Mitzvah

Before turning to the *mishna* proper, it is worth summarizing two significant halachic issues that arose over the course of the preceding days, as not everyone may have been present for those discussions.

The first concerned the heating of water for an infant on *Shabbos* and the attendant question of whether a non-Jew may be instructed to perform such an act. This naturally opens onto the broader question of what categories of need or purpose can justify *amira l'goi* — instructing a non-Jew to perform a *melacha*. Where there is *sakanas nefashos*, the matter is clear. Similarly, for a *choleh kol gufo* — a genuinely ill person, not merely someone with a minor complaint, though without mortal danger — one may have a non-Jew perform a *melacha*. The question of whether one may directly perform a *derabonon* oneself in such circumstances is debated separately. Instructing a non-Jew is generally easier, since one is not personally performing any act. When the purpose is the performance of a *mitzvah*, however — such as facilitating a *bris milah* — a range of opinions comes into play.

### BaHaG & Opinions

The spectrum of *poskim* on this question extends from the *BaHaG*, who permits instructing a non-Jew to perform even a *d'oraisa* for the sake of a *mitzvah*, to the more restrictive view that *amira l'goi* may be employed only for a *derabonon* — or more precisely, only for a double *derabonon* — or that the leniency is limited specifically to *milah* rather than extending to every *mitzvah*. The practical tendency adopted in many communities is to permit even a direct performance of a double *derabonon* for the sake of any *mitzvah*, and under certain circumstances *poskim* do rely on the position of the *BaHaG* as well. This also bears on what is permissible on *Yom Tov* in analogous situations.

### Mechallel Shabbat Status

The second major issue from the preceding day concerned the status of a *mechallel Shabbos* — a habitual public violator of *Shabbos* — with respect to participation in the *eruv* process through *bittul reshous*. The *Gemara* addressed this initially through the case of a *Tzedoki*, and there is reason to think the *Tzedoki*'s disqualification might stem from a very local cause: since a *Tzedoki* does not accept the rabbinic framework of *eruv* altogether, he cannot be part of a system he categorically rejects. The *Gemara* then explored whether the disqualification of a *mechallel Shabbos* might be limited to matters pertaining specifically to *Shabbos* laws, or whether it extends universally. This was in fact a disputed question among *tannaim*, and it was only the final position of the *Gemara* that treats it as excluding the *mechallel Shabbos* across all areas.

### Rambam & Rav Moshe

Even that final conclusion in the *Gemara* is framed in terms of *mumar* — an apostate — rather than the categorical formulation that follows in the *Rambam*, who states that a *mechallel Shabbos* is *harei hu kegoy l'chol davar*, equivalent to a non-Jew in every respect. Rav Moshe questions whether that degree of absolute universality is actually supported by the *Gemara*, both in terms of its categorical nature and in terms of the specific comparison to a non-Jew.

### Tzedoki & Habitual

Within this discussion, an important distinction was noted regarding the *Tzedoki* and, by extension, the Karaites and analogous groups in our own time. The question is whether someone who operates within an entirely different halachic or theological system — rather than simply transgressing the accepted one — should be treated the same as an ordinary *mechallel Shabbos*. Additionally, it was emphasized that the disqualification of a *mechallel Shabbos* requires habitual violation, not a single act, and the question of whether this applies even to violations of rabbinic law tends toward inclusion.

### Chashad Distinction

Finally, a crucial conceptual distinction was drawn between the question of *status* exclusion — whether the *mechallel Shabbos* is categorically excluded from certain legal frameworks — and the separate question of *chashad*, untrustworthiness. The latter concerns whether a person can be relied upon to testify or be believed regarding certain matters, and this is an independent consideration that should not be conflated with the question of categorical status.

## Forgotten Eruv & Waivers

### Forgotten One: Private H…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר שֶׁשָּׁכַח אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְלֹא עֵירַב — בֵּיתוֹ אָסוּר מִלְּהַכְנִיס וּמִלְּהוֹצִיא לוֹ וְלָהֶם, וְשֶׁלָּהֶם מוּתָּרִין לוֹ וְלָהֶם. נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּתָן — הוּא מוּתָּר, וְהֵן אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one of the residents of a courtyard forgot and did not participate in an *eiruv*** with the other residents before Shabbat, and on Shabbat he renounced his rights in the courtyard to the other residents, **his house is prohibited** both **to him,** who forgot to establish an *eiruv*, **and to them,** the other residents, **to bring in** objects from the courtyard to his house **or to take** them **out** from his house into the courtyard. **But their** houses **are permitted** both **to him and to them,** for taking objects out into the courtyard and for bringing them in. **If they gave** away **their rights** in the courtyard **to him,** i.e., if they renounced their rights in his favor, **he is permitted** to carry from his house into the courtyard, **but they are prohibited** from doing so.

We now turn to the *mishna*, which begins on 69b. The *mishna* states: *anshei chatzer sheshachach echad mehem v'lo eirev* — the residents of a courtyard, one of whom forgot and did not make an *eruv*. The consequence is *beiso assur le'hachnis u'lehotzi lo v'lahem* — his house is forbidden for carrying in and out, both for him and for them.

The reason he himself cannot use his house in connection with the *chatzer* is, as we have been discussing, that doing so would constitute a reassertion of his rights in the *chatzer*. The reason the others cannot carry in and out of his house requires elaboration, and the *Gemara* will address it shortly. The operating assumption — which frames the *mishna* as following the position of the *Rabbonon* rather than Rabbi Eliezer, a debate discussed earlier — is that he waived only his rights in the *chatzer*, not in his house. His house remains a separate, private domain, and therefore neither he nor anyone else can carry between his house and the *chatzer*. The *mishna* continues: *veshelachem mutarim lo v'lahem* — their houses they may all carry in and out of freely, as these form one collective domain together with the *chatzer*.

### All Waive to One Person

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם — אוֹסְרִין זֶה עַל זֶה. שֶׁאֶחָד נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּת וְנוֹטֵל רְשׁוּת, שְׁנַיִם נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּת וְאֵין נוֹטְלִין רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **If two** residents of the courtyard forgot to establish an *eiruv*, and the others renounced their rights in the courtyard in their favor, **they prohibit one another.** In this scenario, the courtyard would belong to both of them, but each individual house remains the domain of its owner. It would therefore be prohibited for each of these residents to carry into the courtyard. **For one** resident **may give away and receive rights** in a domain, whereas **two** residents **may** only **give away rights** in a domain, **but they may not receive rights** in a domain. Since they did not establish an *eruv*, it is unreasonable for the other residents of the courtyard to give away their rights in the domain, as the two who are prohibited because they did not participate in the *eruv* render it prohibited for each other to carry.

The *mishna* then presents a reverse scenario: *natnu lo rishusan* — the other residents waived their rights to him. Perhaps he had a particular pressing need to use the *chatzer*, and so the others transferred their rights to him. Now the entire *chatzer* is effectively his; he alone has rights, without having made an *eruv*, but with everyone else having waived to him. In this case, *hu muttar* — he may carry in and out of his house — *v'hen asurin* — but they are forbidden to carry from his house into the *chatzer*. The *Gemara* will explain the reasoning: while one person can be considered a guest in the collective domain of many, the many cannot plausibly be considered guests in the domain of one. The fiction that the entire *chatzer* belongs to a single individual is tenuous enough; once the others begin carrying from his house, they are effectively reasserting their own presence.

### Two Receivers Problem

The *mishna* continues: *hayu shnayim osrin zeh la'zeh* — if there were two people who both failed to make an *eruv*, and all the other residents waived their rights to these two, the courtyard remains problematic. These two individuals are not a unified collective; they are two separate people with competing and unresolved domains within the *chatzer*. Their presence continues to prohibit carrying.

### Give & Receive Rules

The *mishna* then articulates the governing principle: *she'echad noten reshut v'notel reshut* — one person can both give rights and receive rights. *Shnayim notnim reshut* — two people can give rights. *V'ein notlin reshut* — but two people cannot receive rights, since two non-participants remaining in the *chatzer* still leaves unresolved domains.

### Shammai vs. Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵאֵימָתַי נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּת? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם. בֵּית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה. מִי שֶׁנָּתַן רְשׁוּתוֹ וְהוֹצִיא, בֵּין בְּשׁוֹגֵג בֵּין בְּמֵזִיד — הֲרֵי זֶה אוֹסֵר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּמֵזִיד אוֹסֵר, בְּשׁוֹגֵג אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna poses a general question: **When may one give** away **rights** in a domain? **Beit Shammai say: While it is still day,** i.e., before the onset of Shabbat; **and Beit Hillel say: Even after nightfall,** when it is already Shabbat. The mishna cites another dispute: If **one gave away his rights** in his courtyard to the other residents of the courtyard, renouncing them after having forgotten to establish an *eiruv* with them the previous day, **and** then **he carried** something **out** from his house into the courtyard – **whether unwittingly,** forgetting that he had renounced his rights, **or intentionally, he** renders carrying **prohibited** for all the residents of the courtyard, for his action cancels his renunciation; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says:** If he acted **intentionally, he** renders carrying **prohibited;** but if he acted **unwittingly, he does not** render carrying **prohibited.**

The *mishna* also addresses the timing of *bittul reshout*: *Beis Shammai omrim miba'od yom* — Beis Shammai holds that the waiver must be performed while it is still day before *Shabbos* begins. The logic is that if you intend to create a new status for the *chatzer*, it must be done before *Shabbos* takes effect — though one might wonder why the person couldn't simply participate in the *eruv* in the first place. *U'Beis Hillel omrim mishe'chashechah* — Beis Hillel permits the waiver even after nightfall, after *Shabbos* has already begun, which is the assumption operative throughout the surrounding discussions.

### Reassertion Debate

Finally, the *mishna* addresses what happens if a person waives his rights and then carries out from his domain. *Bein b'shogeg bein b'mezid harei zeh oser divrei Rabbi Meir* — whether he did so intentionally or unintentionally, he has thereby reasserted his rights and has now made carrying forbidden for everyone else, in the view of Rabbi Meir. *Rabbi Yehuda omer: b'mezid oser, b'shogeg eino oser* — Rabbi Yehuda distinguishes between intentional and unintentional reassertion: only a deliberate act of carrying reclaims his rights, while an inadvertent one does not.

It was noted earlier in the *Gemara* that all of this is only a concern if the other members of the *chatzer* had not already established their own claim. Once they have staked their claim in the *chatzer*, one person's subsequent reassertion does not undo what they have already acquired. One might also raise the question of whether, having reasserted rights by carrying out, the person could then perform a second *bittul* — whether *mevattelin v'chozrin u'mevattelin* applies here as well. The further question of whether, according to the opinions that permit reassertion, a *bittul* after a reclaiming of previously-waived rights would be effective remains open.

## Gemara: House vs. Courty…

### Separate Domains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בֵּיתוֹ הוּא דְּאָסוּר, הָא חֲצֵירוֹ שַׁרְיָא. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּבַטֵּיל, בֵּיתוֹ אַמַּאי אָסוּר. אִי דְּלָא בַּטֵּיל, חֲצֵירוֹ אַמַּאי שַׁרְיָא? הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן שֶׁבִּיטֵּל רְשׁוּת חֲצֵירוֹ, וְלֹא בִּיטֵּל רְשׁוּת בֵּיתוֹ. וְקָא סָבְרִי רַבָּנַן: הַמְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת חֲצֵירוֹ — רְשׁוּת בֵּיתוֹ לֹא בִּיטֵּל, דְּדָיַיר אִינִישׁ בְּבַיִת בְּלָא חָצֵר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara first analyzes the language of the mishna. It states: It is prohibited to bring in objects from the courtyard to his house and to take them out from his house into the courtyard. It can be inferred from this that **it is carrying** to and from **his house** that **is prohibited, but carrying** to and from **his** share of the **courtyard is permitted** to the other residents of the courtyard. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where this ruling applies? **If** the resident who forgot to establish an *eiruv* **renounced** his rights, **why is his house** rendered **prohibited?** And **if he did not renounce** his rights, **why is his courtyard permitted?** The Gemara explains: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a special case, **where he renounced his rights in his courtyard** to the others **but did not renounce his rights in his house** to them. **And the Rabbis hold that one who renounces his rights in his courtyard has not renounced his rights in his house,** as it is common **for people to reside in a house without a courtyard.**

The *Gemara* opens by asking what seems like an obvious question but turns out to require careful analysis. The *mishna* states that when one person forgets the *eruv* and waives his rights to the *chatzer*, his house remains forbidden even to the other residents. The *Gemara* asks: *beiso hu d'asar* — his house is what is forbidden — *ha chatzeiro sharei* — the *chatzer* itself is permitted; that is explicit in the *mishna*. So what exactly is the case?

The *Gemara* frames this as a dilemma: *heichi dami* — what are the circumstances? If he waived his rights to his house entirely, then why is his house forbidden to the others? And if he did not waive his rights to the *chatzer* at all, why is the *chatzer* permitted? The resolution — *hacha b'mai askinan* — is that the case involves someone who waived his rights to the *chatzer* but did not waive his rights to his house. He waived the *chatzer reshut* alone, so the *chatzer* is open to all; but his house retains its separate status as his private domain, and therefore neither he nor the others may carry between his house and the *chatzer*.

### House Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁלָּהֶם מוּתָּר לוֹ וְלָהֶם, מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָוֵי אוֹרֵחַ לְגַבַּיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proceeds in its analysis of the mishna: It states that carrying in and out of **their** houses **is permitted for him and for them.** The Gemara poses a question: **What is the reason** that their houses are permitted to him? The Gemara answers: **For he is** regarded **like a guest of theirs,** i.e., he is subordinate to them and may carry wherever they may do so.

This leads to a more general principle. *Kasavari rabbanan*, the position of the *Rabbonon* is that *hamvattel reshus chatzeiro* — one who waives his rights to the *chatzer* — *reshus beiso lo bitel* — has not thereby waived the rights to his house. The reason, as the *Gemara* explains, is *dedayri inshi beveita belo chatzer* — people can and do live in houses without a *chatzer*. Since a house can exist as an independent dwelling independently of a courtyard, when someone waives rights over the *chatzer*, the natural assumption is that he was focused solely on the *chatzer* and retains his house as his own domain. It is not that he must have explicitly stated he was keeping his house; the implication is simply that nothing he said touched upon his house.

The *Gemara* then turns to the reverse scenario from the *mishna*: *veshelhen muttar lo u'lahem* — their houses are permissible for him and for them. The question is: *mai taama* — what is the reason that he, the one who forgot the *eruv*, may carry in and out of their houses? The *Gemara* answers: *dehavei oreiach legabeihu* — he has the status of a guest relative to them. Since the *chatzer* and their houses form one collective domain, and he has waived his own rights, he enters their shared space as a guest. A guest may carry in and out of a host's house without thereby asserting proprietorial rights.

### Guest Status Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּתָן — הוּא מוּתָּר וְהֵן אֲסוּרִין. וְנֶהֱוֵי אִינְהוּ לְגַבֵּיהּ כִּי אוֹרְחִין! חַד לְגַבֵּי חֲמִישָּׁה — הָוֵי אוֹרֵחַ, חֲמִשָּׁה לְגַבֵּי חַד — לָא הָוֵי אוֹרֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **If** the other residents **gave** away **their rights** in the courtyard **to him, he is permitted** to carry from his house into the courtyard, **but they are prohibited** from doing so. The Gemara asks: **But let them,** the ones who renounced their rights in the courtyard, **be** regarded as **guests of his,** which would enable them to carry as well. The Gemara answers: **One vis-à-vis five is** considered **a guest,** whereas **five** or more **vis-à-vis one are not** ordinarily viewed as **guests.**

This reasoning then forces a closer examination of the reverse scenario: *nasnu lo reshusan* — when they all waived their rights to him — *hu muttar v'hen asurin* — he may carry, but they cannot, even from his house. The question is: *venihavu inhu legabeih ke-orchin* — why cannot they also be treated as guests relative to him? The *Gemara*'s answer is: *chad legabeih chamisha havei oreiach* — one person can be a guest relative to a collective of many people; *chamisha legabeih chad lo havei oreiach* — but many people cannot be guests relative to one. The numbers here are illustrative rather than precise; the point is that when a large group is treated as belonging to the domain of a single individual, the fiction becomes too tenuous. The moment those many people start carrying from his house into the *chatzer*, they are effectively reasserting their presence, because it is simply implausible that a large group could genuinely be subsumed under the single domain of one person the way one person can disappear into the collective domain of many.

### Conditional Waiver Fails

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ, מְבַטְּלִין וְחוֹזְרִין וּמְבַטְּלִין. הָכִי קָאָמַר: נָתְנוּ לוֹ רְשׁוּתָן מֵעִיקָּרָא — הוּא מוּתָּר וְהֵן אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara attempts to draw another inference from the wording of the mishna: Shall we not **learn from this,** from the order of events in the mishna, that **one may renounce** his rights in favor of another when he needs it, **and then** the latter **may renounce** his rights in favor of the former when he needs it? For the mishna first describes a case in which the one who forgot to establish an *eiruv* renounces his rights in favor of the others, at which stage they may use the courtyard, and then afterward recounts that the other residents renounce their rights in favor of the one who forgot to establish an *eiruv*, leaving it permitted for him and prohibited for them. The Gemara answers: No proof can be brought from here, for **this is** what the mishna **is saying: If they gave away their rights** in the courtyard **to him at the outset,** it is **permitted** for **him and** it is **prohibited for them.** In other words, this is not a continuation of the previous clause, but a separate case.

The *Gemara* briefly considers whether the *mishna*'s two scenarios — he waives to them, and then they waive to him — represent a single sequential case, from which one could infer that *mevattelin v'chozrin u'mevattelin* is permitted. The *Gemara* rejects this reading: *hachi kaamar*, the *mishna* is presenting two independent cases. The first, *nasnu lo reshusan me-ikara*, is simply the scenario where they waived to him from the outset — not as a sequel to a prior waiver in the other direction.

Returning to the problem of two people neither of whom made an *eruv* — if everyone in the *chatzer* waives their rights to both of them, the *chatzer* remains forbidden, as two unresolved domains persist. The *Gemara* notes that this might seem obvious — *peshita* — but the ruling is needed for the following case: *de-hadar chad mineihu bittel lei le-chavreih* — what if after everyone waives to the two, one of them then turns and waives to the other? You might think — *mahu de-teima lishteri* — that now it should be permissible, since effectively one person is the sole rights-holder. *Ka mashma lan*: it does not work. The reason is that *be-idna de-vittel* — at the moment the others performed their waiver — the *chatzer* was not yet usable by those two, since they constituted two separate unresolved domains. The initial waiver was therefore ineffective from the very first moment. Since the fiction of *bittul reshut* operates not by genuine transfer but by a legal relinquishment, it must be effective immediately. If the waiver did not take effect at the moment it was performed, nothing was actually conferred, and there is nothing that one of the two can then pass along to the other.

Similarly, a proposal involving a stipulated condition — *knei al menat lehaknot* — acquiring on condition to subsequently transfer — also fails. Even if the others specified that they were waiving to one person on condition that he would then transfer the rights to the second, this does not work. At the initial moment, two people still had unresolved rights, so the waiver was ineffective, and no subsequent transfer can remedy what was never validly established.

## Braita Scenarios

### Case 1: Non-Participant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב, שְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב, וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ, אוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection** from the following *baraita*: **One** resident of a courtyard **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce his rights** in the courtyard **in favor of one who did establish an *eiruv*. Two** courtyard residents **who established an *eiruv* may** also **renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of one who did not establish an *eiruv*. And** similarly, **two** courtyard residents **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of two** residents **who did establish an *eiruv* or in favor of one** resident **who did not establish an *eiruv*.**

The *Gemara* now turns to a *braita* that presents a series of cases governing who may give and receive *bittul reshut*. The *braita* is admittedly intricate — there are a great many cases — but the underlying logic, once traced through, is coherent.

Case 1: *echad she-lo eirev noten reshuto le-echad she-eirev* — one person who did not make an *eruv* may give his rights to one person who did make an *eruv* (that is, one member of the *eruv* collective). This case, as we will see, immediately raises difficulties in how it is to be understood.

### Case 2: Two Give to One

Case 2: *shnayim she-eirvu notnim reshusan le-echad she-lo eirev* — two people who made an *eruv* together can give their rights to one person who did not make an *eruv*. Since there will then be only one person with unresolved rights in the *chatzer*, the situation is resolved. The *braita* uses the plural *notnim* — they give — which the *Gemara* will take as indicating that each of them must individually perform the waiver.

### Case 3: Both Give & Rece…

Case 3: *shnayim she-lo eirvu notnim reshusan li-shnayim she-eirvu* — two people who did not make an *eruv* may give their rights to two people who did make an *eruv*. Alternatively — a sub-case — those two non-participants may give their rights to one non-participant, since then only one person's rights remain unresolved.

### Case 3b: Two Give to Non…

This sub-case — two people who did not make an *eruv* giving their rights to one person who also did not make an *eruv* — is important. One might have thought that since nobody in this scenario made an *eruv*, the entire arrangement should be prohibited as an inducement to abandon *eruv* practice altogether. The *Gemara* will return to this concern below.

### Case 4: Participant Cannot…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל לֹא אֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב, וְאֵין שְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ, וְאֵין שְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But one** courtyard resident **who did establish an *eiruv* may not renounce his rights** in the courtyard **in favor of one** resident **who did not establish an *eiruv*, nor may two** residents **who established an *eiruv* renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of two** other residents **who did not establish an *eiruv*, nor may two** residents **who did not establish an *eiruv* renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of two** residents **who did not establish an *eiruv*.**

Case 4: *aval ein echad she-eirev noten reshuto le-echad she-lo eirev* — but one person who made an *eruv* cannot give his rights to one person who did not make an *eruv*. The implicit reasoning is that the others who also made the *eruv* are still present and have not waived their rights; only one of them is acting, and all members of the *eruv* must give individually. Since only one person is waiving rather than all of them, the act is insufficient.

### Case 5: Cannot Give to T…

Case 5: *v'ein shnayim she-eirvu notnim reshusan li-shnayim she-lo eirvu* — two people who made an *eruv* cannot give their rights to two people who did not make an *eruv*. Even if they both waive, two non-participants remain in the *chatzer* with competing unresolved domains, and the problem persists.

### Case 6: Two to Two Failu…

Case 6: *v'ein shnayim she-lo eirvu notnim reshusam li-shnayim she-lo eirvu* — two people who did not make an *eruv* cannot give their rights to two other people who also did not make an *eruv*. Again, two unresolved domains remain at the conclusion of the act. This is the mirror image of case 3, and like case 5 it fails because one cannot be left with two people whose rights have not been resolved.

## Abaye vs. Rava Debate

### Rava: Deaths Hypothesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת רֵישָׁא: אֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב — נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלֵיכָּא אַחֲרִינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ, בַּהֲדֵי מַאן עֵירַב? ... אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא — דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרִינָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ, וְקָתָנֵי: לְאֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב! וְרַבָּה: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דַּהֲוָה וּמִית.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event the first clause is teaching: One** resident of a courtyard **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce his rights** in the courtyard **in favor of one who did establish an *eiruv*. What are the circumstances** surrounding this case? **If there is no other** resident **with him,** i.e., if there were only two people living in the courtyard, **with whom did he,** the other resident, **establish an *eiruv*?** He could not have established an *eiruv* on his own. **Rather,** it is **obvious that there is another** resident **with him,** apart from the one who failed to establish an *eiruv*, **and** yet **it states:** He may renounce his rights in the courtyard **in favor of one who did establish an *eiruv*,** which implies that it is enough for him to renounce his rights in favor of one of the residents. He does not have to renounce his rights in favor of all of them. The Gemara now asks: **And** how does **Rabba** understand this teaching? The Gemara answers: Rabba can say as follows: **With what are we dealing here?** This is a special case, **where there was** another person in the courtyard with whom he established the *eiruv*, **but** that person **died** in the meantime, leaving only one who established an *eiruv*, to whom the one who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce his rights.

The *braita* generates an immediate tension. Case 1 — one non-participant giving to one participant — seems to imply that a single member of the *eruv* collective can receive the waiver on behalf of the whole group. But case 3 — two non-participants giving to two participants — uses the language *li-shnayim*, to two, which seems to require that both members of the receiving collective individually receive.

Rava resolves the difficulty by positing that in Case 1, *de-havu u'meit* — the other members of the *eruv* collective had died. That is why only one remains: the *chatzer* at the time of the *bittul* had a single surviving member of the *eruv* group. This is admittedly a significant qualification to leave unstated, but Rava holds it is the only way to read the *braita* consistently.

The *Gemara* challenges Rava by pointing out that the cases in the *braita* appear to be consistent scenarios, not isolated edge cases requiring each to be understood under different circumstances. If the latter cases of the *braita* clearly assume the *eruv* members are alive — since the language *li-shnayim* requires two receivers — then consistency demands the same for Case 1. Rava responds that the cases need not be read as internally consistent — *hachi isa v'hachi isa* — each is an independent scenario and may obtain under different circumstances.

The *Gemara* then notes that Rava can adduce positive support from Case 3 itself: the fact that the *braita* says *li-shnayim she-eirvu* — to the two who made an *eruv* — using the plural, implies that *li-shnayim in, le-echad lo* — giving to two is permitted but giving to only one of the two is not. This, argues Rava, demonstrates that everyone in the receiving group must individually receive.

### Abaye: Collective Repres…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּדַע, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא דְּרֵישָׁא: וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ, נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ. לִשְׁנַיִם אִין! לְאֶחָד לָא. וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר: מַאי ״לִשְׁנַיִם״ — לְאֶחָד מִשְּׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Know** that this *baraita* does not only deal with one state of affairs, **for the last** part **of the first clause teaches: And two** courtyard residents **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of two** residents **who did establish an *eiruv*.** It can be inferred from this that **in favor of two** residents, **yes,** they may renounce their rights, but **in favor of one, no,** they may not. This clearly indicates that they must renounce their rights in the courtyard in favor of both of them. **And Abaye** can **say: What** is the meaning of **in favor of two? In favor of one of the two,** for this is as effective as renouncing their rights in favor of both of them.

Abaye disagrees. He argues that when Case 3 says *li-shnayim she-eirvu*, this should be read as *le-echad mi-shnayim* — to one representative of the two, one person acting on behalf of the collective. The *braita* is not requiring that both receive individually; it is simply specifying that the rights go to the collective, which is adequately represented by one of its members.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges Abaye: *i hachi* — if so — *lisni le-echad she-eirev o le-echad she-lo eirev* — then why does the *braita* say "two"? Why not simply say "one"? This remains a *kashya* against Abaye's reading. However, the difficulty against Rava's reading — requiring the implausible assumption that four people died in Case 1 — is arguably greater.

### Case Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי לִיתְנֵי: לְאֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב אוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב. קַשְׁיָא. אֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב נוֹתֵן רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁעֵירַב. לְאַבָּיֵי דְּאִיתֵיהּ, וְקָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: דְּאֵין צָרִיךְ לְבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד. לְרַבָּה דַּהֲוָה וּמִית, וְלָא גְּזוּר זִימְנִין דְּאִיתֵיהּ. וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא עֵירַב — לִיקְנְסֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וּשְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ נוֹתְנִין רְשׁוּתָן לִשְׁנַיִם שֶׁעֵירְבוּ. לְרַבָּה — תְּנָא סֵיפָא לְגַלּוֹיֵי רֵישָׁא, לְאַבָּיֵי — שְׁנַיִם שֶׁלֹּא עֵירְבוּ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךָ אָמֵינָא: לִגְזַר דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְבַטּוֹלֵי לְהוּ. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** If so, **let it teach** that the two courtyard residents who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce their rights in the courtyard **in favor of one** resident **who established an *eiruv* or in favor of one** resident **who did not establish an *eiruv*,** from which one would understand that there are two present, for otherwise there could be no *eiruv*. The Gemara concludes: This is indeed **difficult** according to Abaye's opinion, although it does not completely refute his opinion. The *Gemara* now explains the need for each clause of the *baraita*. The *baraita* opens: **One** resident of a courtyard **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce his rights in favor of one who did establish an *eiruv*. According to Abaye,** this refers **to** a case **where there is** another person present, **and it teaches us** that **he need not renounce his rights** in the courtyard **in favor of each and every one** of the others. **According to Rabba,** this refers to a case **where there was** another person in the courtyard, with whom he established the *eiruv*, **but** that person **died** in the meantime, **and** the novel teaching is that the Sages **did not issue a decree** due to the concern that **sometimes** that other person **is** still **present.** The *baraita* continues: **Two** courtyard residents **who established an *eiruv* may renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of one who did not establish an *eiruv*.** The Gemara poses a question: Isn't this **obvious?** What new *halakha* is being taught here? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **since he did not establish an *eiruv*, we should penalize him** by insisting that he renounce his rights in their favor and not the reverse, therefore the *baraita* **teaches us** that it is permitted even for the ones who established an *eiruv* to renounce their rights in his favor. It was further taught in the *baraita*: **And** similarly, **two** courtyard residents **who did not establish an *eiruv* may renounce their rights** in the courtyard **in favor of two** residents **who established an *eiruv*. According to Rabba,** the *baraita* **taught the latter clause to shed light on the first clause.** As the latter clause teaches that one must renounce rights to every resident in the courtyard, the first clause must refer to the case where the additional resident passed away, for otherwise, he would not be able to renounce his rights to only one of the residents of the courtyard. **According to Abaye,** it was **necessary** for the mishna to teach the *halakha* in the case of **two who did not establish an *eiruv*.** For **it could enter your mind to say** that **we should issue a decree** determining that the two residents who did not establish an *eiruv* may not renounce their rights in favor of the two residents who established an *eiruv*, **lest** the two who established an *eiruv* **come to renounce** their rights **in favor of** the two who did not. The *baraita*, therefore, **teaches us** that we do not issue such a decree.

The *Gemara* then proceeds case by case, explaining how each reading — Abaye's and Rava's — accounts for the *chiddush* of each scenario.

Case 1, according to Abaye: the other members of the *eruv* are still alive, and the *chiddush* is precisely that one need not waive to every individual member of the collective — giving to one suffices. According to Rava: the others died, and the *chiddush* is that we are not concerned (*delo gozrinan*) that allowing a waiver in a case where others happened to die will lead people to mistakenly think they can give to only one member of a living collective.

Case 2: the *chiddush* according to Rava and Abaye alike is that we do not penalize the non-participant by refusing to allow him to be the recipient. One might have thought — *mahu de-teima* — that the person who failed to make an *eruv* should be disqualified from receiving rights as a form of fine. *Ka mashma lan* that no such fine applies.

Case 3: according to Rava, the *chiddush* is that the receivers must be both of the *eruv* participants — *li-shnayim* is read literally — making explicit what Case 1 left implicit. According to Abaye, the *chiddush* of Case 3 is not about the receivers but about the givers: one might have thought that since neither of the two giving parties made an *eruv*, we should prohibit the entire arrangement, either to penalize them or out of concern that allowing *bittul* where nobody made an *eruv* will cause *torat eruv* to be forgotten altogether. *Ka mashma lan* that we do not apply this concern.

Case 3b — two non-participants giving to one non-participant — according to Abaye, the *chiddush* is the same: we are not concerned that permitting *bittul* in a case where nobody participated in an *eruv* will lead to the abandonment of *eruv* practice.

Case 4: according to Abaye, this case is the mirror of Case 1, and its *chiddush* is to clarify what Case 1 said: when we said in Case 1 that one could give to one member of the collective, we meant one receiver is sufficient — but one giver is not. A single member of the *eruv* collective cannot give on behalf of all the others, because each giver must act individually. According to Rava, this case simply parallels Case 1 in structure and its logic is self-explanatory given the framework already established.

Case 5: the *chiddush* is that even if one of the two receivers subsequently waives his share to the other — producing what looks like a single effective recipient — this still does not work, because at the moment of the original waiver, the *chatzer* was not usable by those two. The initial act was therefore ineffective, and no subsequent act can repair it.

Case 6: the *chiddush* is the same as Case 5 but with the added stipulation — *knei al menat lehaknot* — that even a conditional waiver (receiving on condition of passing along) fails, because the initial transfer was not immediately effective.

### Givers Individual, Recei…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אוֹ לְאֶחָד שֶׁלֹּא עֵירַב. לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — הֵיכָא דְּמִקְצָתָן עֵירְבוּ וּמִקְצָתָן לֹא עֵירְבוּ. אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּכוּלָּן לֹא עֵירְבוּ — לִיקְנְסִינְהוּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּשְׁתַּכַּח תּוֹרַת עֵירוּב, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Or** they may renounce their rights **in favor of one who did not establish an *eiruv*.** The Gemara poses a question: **Why do I** need this addition? The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **these** permissive **rulings** with regard to renunciation **apply only** in a case **where some of** the residents **established an *eiruv* and some of them did not establish an *eiruv*. But** in a case **where none of** the residents **established an *eiruv*, we should penalize them** by not allowing renunciation, **so that the** halakhic **category of *eiruv* should not be forgotten** by those who come after them. The *baraita*, therefore, **teaches us** that we are not concerned about this.

The conclusion that emerges from this entire analysis is striking: there is, in fact, broad agreement on one side of the question. All agree that when the *eruv* collective is giving away rights, every individual member of that collective must personally perform the *bittul* — no one person can represent the group as a giver. The practical analogy offered is that when people give away property, one person cannot relinquish what belongs to another. The disagreement between Abaye and Rava centers exclusively on the receiving end: must every individual member of the receiving collective personally receive, or can one representative of the collective receive on behalf of all?

## Heir's Bittul Reshut

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן: יוֹרֵשׁ מַהוּ שֶׁיְּבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman:** With regard to **an heir, what is** the *halakha* regarding whether **he** may **renounce rights** in a courtyard? If a person who had forgotten to establish an *eiruv* died on Shabbat, may his heir renounce his rights in his stead?

The *Gemara* now shifts to a question posed by Rava to Rav Nachman. The question involves an *yoresh* — an heir. Consider the case where a father owned a house in a *chatzer* but did not make an *eruv* with the other residents. The father then dies on *Shabbos*, and the son inherits the house at that point. The question is: *yoresh mahu she'yevatel reshus* — may the heir perform *bittul reshut*?

At the onset of *Shabbos*, the son was not a member of the *chatzer* and had no stake in it. He was not in a position to participate in an *eruv*, nor could he have performed *bittul*. Now that he has inherited the property mid-*Shabbos*, can he waive his newly acquired rights?

### Continuation vs. New

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכָא דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְעָרוֹבֵי מֵאֶתְמוֹל מָצֵי מְעָרֵב — בַּטּוֹלֵי נָמֵי מָצֵי מְבַטֵּל, אֲבָל הַאי כֵּיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְעָרוֹבֵי מֵאֶתְמוֹל — לָא מָצֵי מְעָרֵב, לָא מָצֵי מְבַטֵּל. אוֹ דִּלְמָא: יוֹרֵשׁ כַּרְעֵיהּ דַּאֲבוּהּ הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two sides of the question: On the one hand, perhaps only in a case **where, if** the person **wanted to establish an *eiruv* on the previous day he could have established an *eiruv*, he can also** renounce his rights on Shabbat. **But this** heir, **since, if he wanted to establish an *eiruv* the previous day he could not have established an *eiruv*,** as he was not then a resident of the courtyard, therefore, today **he cannot renounce** his rights either. **Or perhaps an heir is** like **his father's foot,** i.e., he is considered an extension of his father and substitutes for him in all regards, which means that just as his father could have renounced his rights, so can he.

Two framings present themselves. On one reading, the heir is simply a newcomer who acquired a house on *Shabbos* — someone who had no connection to the *chatzer* when *Shabbos* began, and therefore cannot do *bittul*, just as any other newcomer who acquires a stake in a *chatzer* after *Shabbos* begins cannot retroactively regularize the situation. On the other reading, *yoresh kera d'avuha hu* — an heir is the legs of his father, a continuation of the father's identity and ownership. Under this conception, the heir is not a new legal entity at all; he is the same abstract owner, merely in a different person, and therefore what his father could have done he may also do.

### Rav Nachman Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנִי אוֹמֵר מְבַטֵּל, וְהָנֵי דְּבֵי שְׁמוּאֵל תָּנוּ אֵין מְבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman **said to him: I** myself **say** that an heir **can** indeed **renounce** rights in a courtyard, **while those** scholars of **the school of Shmuel taught: He cannot renounce** rights in a courtyard.

Rav Nachman answers: *ani omer mevatel* — I would say he can perform *bittul*. However, a *braita* from the school of Shmuel states the opposite: *ein mevatel* — he cannot perform *bittul*. So there is a genuine dispute on the question, turning on whether the heir is to be seen as a continuation of the father or as an independent legal personality.

## Status at Shabbat Start

### Begin Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: זֶה הַכְּלָל, כׇּל שֶׁמּוּתָּר לְמִקְצָת שַׁבָּת — הוּתַּר לְכׇל הַשַּׁבָּת, וְכׇל שֶׁנֶּאְסַר לְמִקְצָת שַׁבָּת — נֶאְסַר לְכׇל הַשַּׁבָּת חוּץ מִמְּבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised an objection to** Rav Naḥman from the following *baraita*: **This is the principle: Anything that is permitted for part of Shabbat is permitted for all of Shabbat, and anything that is prohibited for part of Shabbat is prohibited for all of Shabbat, apart from one who renounces his rights** in a courtyard, for renunciation can provide an allowance halfway through Shabbat.

The *Gemara* now introduces a broad principle, which is brought as a challenge to the preceding discussion. The principle states: *kol shehutar lemiktzas Shabbos hutar lechol haShabbos* — anything that was permissible for any part of *Shabbos* remains permissible for all of *Shabbos*. Conversely: *kol sheneesar lemiktzas Shabbos neesar lechol haShabbos* — anything that was forbidden at any part of *Shabbos* remains forbidden throughout *Shabbos*. The operative principle is the status at the beginning: what begins as permitted continues as permitted; what begins as forbidden remains forbidden.

### Sealed Openings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁהוּתַּר לְמִקְּצָת שַׁבָּת, מוּתָּר לְכׇל הַשַּׁבָּת — כְּגוֹן עֵירַב דֶּרֶךְ הַפֶּתַח, וְנִסְתַּם הַפֶּתַח. עֵירַב דֶּרֶךְ חַלּוֹן, וְנִסְתַּם חַלּוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now explains each element of the *baraita*: **Anything that is permitted for part of Shabbat is permitted for all of Shabbat. For example,** if **an *eiruv* was established** between two adjacent courtyards that are connected **via an opening** between them, **and** that **opening was closed up** on Shabbat, the *eiruv* is valid. Alternately, if **an *eiruv* was established** between the two courtyards that are connected **via a window** opening from one to the other, **and** that **window was closed up** on Shabbat, the *eiruv* is valid. As carrying from one courtyard to another was permitted at the beginning of Shabbat, it is permitted throughout Shabbat.

The *Gemara* illustrates the permissive side of the principle. *Eruv derech pesach* — an *eruv* made through a door connecting two courtyards, *venistam hapetach* — and then the door was sealed on *Shabbos*. One might have thought the *eruv* is now broken, since the connection has been severed. But since the *eruv* was valid when *Shabbos* began, it remains valid and carrying between the two courtyards continues to be permitted. The same applies to *eruv derech chalon venistam chalon* — an *eruv* made through a window, with the window subsequently sealed on *Shabbos*. Since it was valid at the start, it remains valid.

### Walls Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל — לְאֵתוֹיֵי מָבוֹי שֶׁנִּיטְּלוּ קוֹרוֹתָיו אוֹ לְחָיָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The words **this is the principle** come **to include** the case of **an alleyway whose** cross **beams or side posts were removed** on Shabbat, teaching that one may nonetheless use the alleyway, as it had been permitted at the outset of Shabbat.

The principle extends further. *Mavoi shenitlu korosav o lechayav* — an alleyway whose beam (*korah*) or side-post (*lechi*) was removed on *Shabbos*. This is actually a significant *chiddush*: one might have thought that the removal of these structural markers — which in the first chapters of the tractate were shown to be what defines the alleyway as a domain permitting carrying — would fundamentally alter the legal status of the space. But the principle holds: since the *eruv* was valid and carrying was permitted when *Shabbos* began, the removal of the *lechi* or *korah* does not retroactively invalidate the *eruv*. Carrying may continue. One need not check on *Shabbos* whether the physical markers are still in place if they were verified before *Shabbos*. (This, of course, does not extend to actual walls: if two walls of an alleyway fall, there is nothing left to carry in, and no principle can save that.)

### Begin Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל שֶׁנֶּאְסַר לְמִקְצָת שַׁבָּת, נֶאְסַר לְכׇל הַשַּׁבָּת כּוּלָּהּ — כְּגוֹן שְׁנֵי בָתִּים בִּשְׁנֵי צִידֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, וְהִקִּיפוּם גּוֹיִם מְחִיצָה בְּשַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its explanation of the *baraita*: **Anything that is prohibited for part of Shabbat is prohibited for all of Shabbat. For example,** if there were **two houses on two sides of a public domain, which gentiles enclosed with a wall on Shabbat,** the enclosed area remains prohibited. Even though a partition of this kind is considered a proper one with regard to Shabbat domains, it is prohibited to carry objects from either house into the enclosed area, even if the owner of the first house renounces his rights in the area in favor of the owner of the second house, as they could not have established an *eiruv* between them before Shabbat.

The converse is stated with equal force: *kol sheneesar lemiktzas Shabbos neesar lechol haShabbos kullah* — anything that was forbidden at the outset of *Shabbos* remains forbidden throughout. Again, the key is the status at the moment *Shabbos* begins.

### Wall Built on Shabbat

The illustration offered for the forbidden side of the principle is the following: two houses on opposite sides of a *reshus harabbim*, and non-Jews who, on *Shabbos* itself, surrounded the public domain with a *mechitza* — a partition — thereby converting it halachically into a *reshus hayachid*. One might think that now the two homeowners should be able to perform *bittul reshut* to each other and regularize their domains. The *Gemara* says this is not so. Since at the beginning of *Shabbos* they were not in a position to make an *eruv* or perform *bittul* — the *reshus hayachid* did not exist yet — the status that began as forbidden does not change, and they may not now do *bittul*. (Rashi notes an important caveat: if only one person were involved, the wall built by the non-Jews on *Shabbos* would genuinely create a *reshus hayachid*, and that individual would be the sole occupant — a different matter altogether. The issue of two unresolved parties is what blocks the remedy here.)

### Bittul Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָתָנֵי: חוּץ מִמְּבַטֵּל רְשׁוּת. אִיהוּ — אִין, יוֹרֵשׁ — לָא. אֵימָא: חוּץ מִתּוֹרַת בִּיטּוּל רְשׁוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the *baraita* **teaches: Apart from one who renounces his rights** in a courtyard, which teaches that a person may renounce his rights in a courtyard even on Shabbat, despite the fact that the courtyard was prohibited prior to his renunciation. The Gemara infers: **He** himself, i.e., the original owner, **yes,** he may renounce his rights even on Shabbat, but with regard to his **heir, no,** he may not renounce his rights on Shabbat, which contradicts Rav Naḥman's opinion. Rav Naḥman replied: **Say** that the *baraita* must be understood as follows: **Apart from** anyone who falls **into the** halakhic **category of one who renounces his rights** in a domain. In other words, the *baraita* is not referring to a particular person who renounces his rights, but rather to the category of renunciation in general, which includes an heir.

The principle as stated, however, carries a crucial exception: *chutz mimvatel reshus* — except for the case of waiving rights. *Bittul reshut* is the one case in which *Shabbos* began in a state of prohibition — the *chatzer* was forbidden because someone sat out of the *eruv* — and yet a subsequent act of *bittul* in the middle of *Shabbos* converts that forbidden status into permission. This is precisely what makes *bittul reshut* singular: it is the exceptional mechanism that operates against the general principle.

## Heir Death Timing

### Dies Before Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חָצֵר שֶׁמֵּת, וְהִנִּיחַ רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּק, מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — אוֹסֵר, מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה — אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **raised a** further **objection to** the opinion of Rav Naḥman from a different *baraita*: If **a resident of a courtyard died and left his domain,** the use of his house, **to one from the marketplace,** i.e., a non-resident of the courtyard, the following distinction applies: If he died **while it was still day,** i.e., before Shabbat, the one from the marketplace renders carrying **prohibited,** for it is assumed that he received his portion before the onset of Shabbat and should have joined in an *eiruv* with the others. Since he failed to establish an *eiruv* with the other residents of the courtyard, he renders carrying prohibited in the entire courtyard. If, however, he died **after nightfall, he does not** render carrying **prohibited,** for so long as it was permitted to carry for part of Shabbat it remains permitted for the entirety of Shabbat.

The *Gemara* now applies these principles to the question of the heir through a series of scenarios. First: *echad mibnei chatzer shemeis vehiniach reshusso leachad min hashuk* — one of the residents of the *chatzer* dies and bequeaths his property to a stranger, someone from outside, who had no connection to this *chatzer*. If the resident died *mibe'od yom* — before *Shabbos* began — then the stranger enters the *chatzer* on *Shabbos* as a new party who never made an *eruv* and never had the opportunity to regularize his status before *Shabbos*. The stranger's presence makes carrying forbidden, and *bittul reshut* is not available to him, because the status began forbidden.

### Dies on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּק שֶׁמֵּת וְהִנִּיחַ רְשׁוּתוֹ לְאֶחָד מִבְּנֵי חָצֵר, מִבְּעוֹד יוֹם — אֵינוֹ אוֹסֵר, מִשֶּׁחָשֵׁיכָה — אוֹסֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **And** alternatively, if **one from the marketplace** who owned a residence in the courtyard but did not dwell there **died and left his domain to a resident of the courtyard** who does live there and usually joins in an *eiruv* with his neighbors, the following distinction applies: If the person from the marketplace died **while it was still day,** i.e., before Shabbat, the courtyard resident **does not** render carrying **prohibited,** as when he establishes his *eiruv* it includes his new residence as well. If, however, the person from the marketplace died **after nightfall** without having established an *eiruv*, the deceased renders carrying **prohibited.** As this residence was prohibited at the beginning of Shabbat, it can no longer be permitted on that Shabbat.

In contrast: *mishe'chashecha eino osser* — if the same courtyard member died after *Shabbos* began, his property passes to the stranger mid-*Shabbos*. But since the *chatzer* was already in a permitted state when *Shabbos* began — the deceased was a member of the *eruv* — the principle *kol shehutar lemiktzas Shabbos hutar lechol haShabbos* applies. The status of permission is locked in from the beginning of *Shabbos* and continues; the stranger inheriting mid-*Shabbos* does not disrupt what was already established as permitted.

### Forbidden Until Bittul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמַּאי אוֹסֵר? נִיבַטֵּל! מַאי ״אוֹסֵר״ נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי — עַד שֶׁיְּבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava's question is based on the first case discussed in the *baraita*: According to Rav Naḥman, **why does** the heir render carrying **prohibited** in this case? **Let him renounce** his rights in the courtyard to the other residents, as Rav Naḥman maintains that an heir may renounce rights. Rav Naḥman replied: **What is** the meaning of the word **prohibits that** the *baraita* **teaches** here? It means he renders carrying prohibited **until he renounces** his rights, i.e., although there is no way of rectifying the situation by means of an *eiruv*, it can be corrected by way of renunciation.

The *Gemara* then clarifies what *osser* — forbidden — means in this context. It does not mean irreparably forbidden for all of *Shabbos* with no exit. Rather, *mai osser nami d'tani ad she'yevateil* — it means it is forbidden *until* the person performs *bittul reshut*. Once he does perform *bittul*, the status changes. This is, of course, the exception already identified — *chutz mimvatel reshus* — *bittul reshut* is precisely the mechanism that uniquely permits a change from forbidden to permitted mid-*Shabbos*.

One might note that the *Gemara* here does not draw an explicit distinction between the case of a direct heir — someone who is *kera d'avuha*, a continuation of the father — and a stranger who receives a bequest. That distinction, which lay at the heart of Rava's original question, would seem to argue powerfully in favor of the heir's ability to perform *bittul*: unlike a pure stranger, the heir carries a kind of continuity of identity with the original owner. It is notable that the *Gemara* does not press this distinction, and the question of whether the heir may perform *bittul* will continue to be examined.
