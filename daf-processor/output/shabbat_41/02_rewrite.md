# Shabbat 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kli Rishon/Kli Sheni

### Rebbi & Oil Flask

The *gemara* opens with the account of Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi, who relates: "One time I followed Rebbe into the bathhouse." Presumably it was Shabbos, and Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi wished to place a flask of oil into the bath in order to warm it up. Rebbe's response was immediate: take water in a second vessel and place the flask of oil into that second vessel — do not place it directly into the bath itself.

The reasoning is foundational. The bath is considered a *kli rishon* — the vessel in which the water was initially heated — and objects are capable of being cooked in a *kli rishon*. By contrast, placing the flask into a *kli sheni*, a second vessel into which the hot water has been transferred, removes the concern of cooking. Today's *gemara* is, in fact, one of the primary sources for this entire distinction.

A question immediately arises: what was Rebbe doing in the bathhouse in the first place? The *gemara* established earlier that one may not fully immerse in hot water on Shabbos. Rashi notes, and the *gemara* itself will shortly confirm, that the bath must have been *chamei Teverya* — the naturally heated springs of Tiberias, warmed not by fire but by the earth itself.

### Three Inferences

From Rebbe's instruction, the *gemara* derives three *halachic* conclusions: *shema mina shemen yesh bo mishum bishul* — it is possible to violate the prohibition of cooking with respect to oil, resolving the uncertainty discussed in the previous session. Second: *kli sheni eino mevashel* — a second vessel does not cook; this is of decisive importance for later *halacha*. Third: *hafshara zehu bishulo* — mere dissolution, the liquefying of a solid, may itself constitute the act of cooking.

This third inference, however, sits in some tension with the conclusion reached previously, where it was established that cooking a liquid requires it to reach the temperature of *yad soledet bo*. If mere dissolution — even at a lower temperature — were sufficient, that earlier conclusion would be undermined. The *gemara* therefore notes that in practice, even for oil, we require *yad soledet bo*, and the third inference does not represent the final ruling.

### Chamei Teverya

The *gemara* now raises the inference of Ravina: it sounds as though one who cooks in *chamei Teverya* on Shabbos is biblically liable — *hamevashel b'chamei Teverya b'Shabbos chayav*. This would contradict the established principle that heating by a non-fire source is not a biblical violation. Ravina's reasoning is that Rebbe's story must have occurred after the earlier rabbinic prohibition was in place, meaning that Rebbe could only have been in water not heated by fire — i.e., *chamei Teverya* — and yet his concern about placing oil in the bath seemed to rise to the level of biblical prohibition.

The *gemara* rejects this inference, citing Rav Chisda: *hamevashel b'chamei Teverya b'Shabbos patur* — one who cooks in *chamei Teverya* on Shabbos is exempt. The apparent biblical concern in the story must therefore refer only to *makat mardut*, a rabbinic lashing, not a Torah-level violation. What remains is the rabbinic restriction against cooking something in *chamei Teverya* — a form of *toldos chamah* — and it was this concern that prompted Rebbe to insist on using a *kli sheni*.

### Torah in Bathhouse

Before returning to the *halachic* substance, the *gemara* raises a procedural difficulty: how was Rebbe permitted to speak words of *Torah* in the bathhouse at all? The Gemara cites Rabba bar bar Chana in the name of Rabbi Yochanan that it is forbidden to think about *Torah* in a bathhouse or lavatory.

One might suppose the answer is that Rebbe spoke in a non-Hebrew language, but the *gemara* anticipates this and rejects it. Abaye states clearly: *devarim shel chol mutar l'omram b'lashon hakodesh* — secular matters may be said in Hebrew even in a bathhouse, and sacred content may not be said even in the vernacular. The criterion is the content, not the language.

Yet one might still object: what Rebbe actually said was not an explicit *halachic* ruling — he simply instructed his attendant to use a different vessel. The *gemara* treats even this implicit reference to *kli sheni* as constituting speech of *Torah*. How, then, was it permitted?

The *gemara* answers: *afrushei mei'isura shani* — it is different when one is acting to prevent another from committing a transgression. In such a case, speaking words of *Torah* in the bathhouse is permitted. A parallel case is adduced: a student of Rabbi Meir followed him into the bathhouse on Shabbos and wished to wash the floor; Rabbi Meir told him *ein medichin* — it is forbidden. He then wished to apply oil to the floor, and Rabbi Meir told him *ein sachin* — that too is forbidden, lest it lead to smoothing over holes in the ground. In both cases, Rabbi Meir spoke clear *halachic* prohibitions in the bathhouse, and this was permitted because he was preventing a transgression. The principle is established: *afrushei mei'isura shani*.

### Connected Systems

Returning to the student's original question — if the bath was heated by *chamei Teverya* rather than by fire, why should it be considered a *kli rishon* at all? The springs never cooked anything in the bathtub per se. Rashi addresses this directly: the bath, into which the hot water is drawn continuously from the wellsprings, is considered like a *kli rishon* in which the water was boiled — *af al pi she'he'eviru me'al ha'ur mevashel* — because a *kli rishon*, even once removed from the fire, retains its status.

There is still an apparent difficulty: the bathtub was never itself placed over a fire. But Rashi's understanding seems to be that when hot water flows continuously from its source into a connected vessel, the entire system functions as a single unit. This has significant contemporary implications. When one opens the hot water tap in a home on Shabbos, drawing water directly from a hot water heater, some authorities argue that the water flowing through the pipe remains part of a single connected system and retains the status of *kli rishon*. The cup into which it is poured may itself be considered part of that connected unit rather than a separate *kli sheni*.

This reflects a broader question about where precisely the conceptual break between *kli rishon* and *kli sheni* occurs. One authority cited holds that when water flows directly from a hot water urn through a spigot into a cup, since the water is still in contact with the heat source at the moment of flow, the cup itself might carry *kli rishon* status — in contrast to the classic case of *kli sheni*, where the vessel has already been removed from the fire before the transfer occurs.

### Cooking Definition

This discussion sets up the two central criteria for *bishul* on Shabbos that have emerged across this *daf*: *yad soledet bo* as the required temperature, and the requirement that the act occur in a *kli rishon* rather than a *kli sheni*. The question naturally arises: if the water is equally hot in both vessels, why should this distinction matter?

Two approaches address this. The first is a formalistic distinction. Following the view of the Ramban that paradigmatic *bishul* is cooking directly over a fire, one can extend this to a *kli rishon* on the grounds that its contents were themselves heated by the fire and it is still, in a sense, connected to that reality. A *kli sheni*, however, is entirely detached from the fire and therefore falls outside the formal definition of cooking, regardless of the practical outcome.

The second approach, that of Tosafos, is more practical. The walls of a *kli rishon* retain heat absorbed directly from the fire, continuing to warm the contents even after removal. In a *kli sheni*, by contrast, the heat of the transferred water dissipates outward through the walls — heat is being lost rather than retained or introduced. This directional difference in heat transfer means that items placed in a *kli sheni* will not cook effectively.

The difference between these two approaches becomes consequential when considering *kalei habishul* — things that cook easily, such as tea or couscous. If the distinction is formal, then even easily cooked foods remain exempt in a *kli sheni* regardless of practical outcome. If the distinction is practical, one might argue that certain foods can in fact be cooked even in a *kli sheni*, and this is the question the *gemara* will continue to examine.

Thus the *daf* has established three primary criteria for *bishul* on Shabbos: that the heat source must be fire rather than solar heat or *chamei Teverya* to create a biblical violation; that the temperature must reach *yad soledet bo*; and that the act must occur in a *kli rishon*.

## Swimming Rules

### Lifting Feet

Rabbi Zeira relates that he observed Rabbi Abbahu swimming in a bath — *deshat b'ambati* — and could not tell whether Rabbi Abbahu lifted his feet off the ground or not. The *gemara* responds that this is not a genuine uncertainty: *pshita d'lo akar* — obviously Rabbi Abbahu did not lift his feet. The reason is a *braita* that explicitly prohibits swimming in a pool filled with water even in a private courtyard — *lo yashut adam b'vreicha meleiah mayim v'afilu omedet b'chatzer* — even where there is no concern of carrying in a public domain.

### Flotation Concern

The reason for this prohibition, as the *gemara* in *Beitzah* explains, is a concern that one will come to construct some type of flotation device or raft. The primary context for this concern is swimming in a river, where such improvisational construction might seem natural. The question then becomes: at what point does a pool, rather than a river, become the relevant category?

### Lip Distinction

The *gemara* resolves this with the criterion of *gidudei* — a lip or raised rim around the edge of the pool. According to Rashi's preferred explanation, a pool with a raised lip is more analogous to a vessel, and thus clearly distinct from a river; the rabbinic concern about constructing flotation devices applies to river-like bodies of water, not to enclosed pool-like structures. A pool without a lip resembles open ground-water more closely and remains prohibited. Thus, if a pool has a raised lip along its edge, swimming in it on Shabbos is permitted, since it does not resemble the river context that generated the original concern.

### Ashkenazi/Sefardi

On the basis of this ruling, *Sefardim* in practice do permit swimming in a pool on Shabbos, provided the water was not heated by fire — even water heated by solar means, such as through concentrated solar energy, would not create a biblical prohibition. *Ashkenazim*, however, follow a post-*gemara* rabbinic enactment, dating back approximately a thousand years, prohibiting full immersion in water on Shabbos even in cold water. The concerns underlying this *minhag* include the prohibition against squeezing water from one's hair and the practical difficulty of wringing out a towel, as well as the broader logic of the *gemara* that even in a private courtyard — where carrying is not an issue — immersion poses other problems. The *gemara*'s own ruling that maintaining one's head above water does not resolve the prohibition is assumed to apply equally to the *Ashkenazi minhag*, though the precise boundary requires further examination.

## Modesty/Covenant

### Abbahu Covering

The *gemara* continues with another observation of Rabbi Zeira regarding Rabbi Abbahu: when entering the water, Rabbi Abbahu placed his hand in front of his genital area. Rabbi Zeira reports uncertainty about whether Rabbi Abbahu incidentally touched himself in doing so. The *gemara* resolves this immediately: *pshita d'lo naga* — obviously he did not touch himself. The basis is the teaching of Rabbi Eliezer that one who holds himself while urinating is as if he brought a flood upon the world, given the concerns of erection, improper thoughts, and *zera levatala*.

### Distraction Principle

Abaye, however, offers a more nuanced defense of Rabbi Abbahu's practice. He invokes the principle of *boleshet* — the scenario of troops entering a city. The *mishnah* in *Beitzah* teaches: if troops enter a city during peacetime, open barrels of wine are forbidden because one fears the non-Jewish soldiers may have helped themselves; sealed barrels are permitted. But if the troops are in the midst of battle — *besha'at milchama* — even open barrels are permitted, because *ein penai l'nasach*, they have no time to perform a libation to *avoda zara*, and perhaps not even time to drink. The operative principle is: *kivan d'bi'isi, lo menaschi* — since they are frightened, they do not perform the *nisuch*. 

Similarly, says Abaye, when Rabbi Abbahu was covering himself, his thoughts were distracted by other concerns — fear, in this context — and therefore there was no concern that the act of covering himself would lead to the problematic outcomes otherwise feared.

### Two Concerns

The *gemara* asks: what precisely was this fear? — *bisusa de-nahara* — the fear of entering a river. One who is entering a body of water has enough to occupy his attention that the distraction principle applies.

However, the *gemara* immediately raises a competing concern. Rav, citing Rav Huna citing Rav, states: *kol hamanich yado k'neged panav she-lemata k'ilu kofer bivriso shel Avraham Avinu* — one who places his hand so as to cover his genital area is as if he denies the covenant of Abraham our father. In the Hellenistic and Roman context, where circumcision was a mark of social difference, covering oneself could appear as an act of concealment or shame regarding one's circumcision. This creates a direct tension with the concern for modesty.

### Rava/Zeira Practices

The *gemara* resolves the conflict with a situational distinction: *ha kinachis, ha kisali* — it depends on whether one is descending into the river or ascending from it. When descending, one's back faces observers, so there is no concern of appearing to hide one's circumcision, and one should therefore stand upright. When ascending and facing the onlookers, modesty justifies — and indeed requires — covering oneself.

This produces different reported practices. Rava would crouch — *shachi* — while Rabbi Zeira would stand straight — *zakif*. The *gemara* does not specify at which moment each posture was adopted, but the resolution implies that both concerns are legitimate and context-dependent.

### Rav Ashi's Rabbis

The rabbis of the house of Rav Ashi provided the most precise formulation of how to reconcile these competing obligations. When descending into the river — and thus not facing people — they stood upright, *zakif*, so as not to appear to be concealing their circumcision. When ascending and facing those nearby, they bent over, *shachi*, out of concern for modesty. One student offered the observation that these differences in practice may partly reflect geographic context: in Eretz Yisrael, where Hellenistic influence made circumcision a more charged social marker, the concern about appearing to deny one's *brit* would have been heightened — consistent with Rabbi Zeira's practice of standing upright. In Babylonia, where that social pressure was less acute, the modesty concern could take precedence, consistent with Rava's practice of bending over.

## Zeira's Departure

### Avoiding Rav Yehuda

The *gemara* now transitions — as we have already encountered in *Berachos* — to the account of Rabbi Zeira's evasion of Rav Yehuda. Rabbi Zeira wished to make *aliyah* to Eretz Yisrael, but Rav Yehuda opposed the move on the basis of the verse *Bavel'ah yuva'u ve'shammah yihyu* — "to Babylon they were brought and there they shall remain" — interpreted as a prophetic command prohibiting emigration to Eretz Yisrael until the final redemption. Rav Yehuda held that one must remain in exile until *Mashiach* comes miraculously, while Rabbi Zeira held that making *aliyah* was not only permitted but meritorious.

Rather than confront Rav Yehuda directly, Rabbi Zeira avoided him. Yet before departing, he resolved to extract one last teaching. He located Rav Yehuda in the bathhouse, overhearing him instruct his attendant in several matters.

### Bathhouse Instructions

Rav Yehuda told his attendant: bring me lye (a cleansing agent), bring me a comb; open your mouths and let the hot vapor out; and bring me water from the bathhouse to drink. Rabbi Zeira declared that had he come for no other purpose than to hear these words, it would have been sufficient — *ilmalei basi ella lishmo'a davar zeh dayi*.

### Hebrew Speech

The *gemara* asks what *Torah* was embedded in each instruction. From "bring me the lye and the comb," one learns that *devarim shel chol mutar l'omram b'lashon hakodesh* — secular matters may be stated in Hebrew even in a bathhouse. As established earlier in this *daf*, the prohibition against speaking *Torah* in a bathhouse is about content, not language. Speaking ordinary requests in Hebrew is entirely permitted.

### Air & Health

From the instruction to open one's mouth and expel the hot vapor, one learns the principle articulated by Shmuel: *havla mafik havla* — air drives out air. The good air of the bathhouse comes in and displaces the bad air within, with the additional benefit that sweating releases impurities through the skin.

The drinking instruction is the occasion for a broader statement of health principles: one who eats without drinking — *achal v'lo shasa* — renders his eating like blood and initiates digestive illness. One who eats without walking four *amos* afterwards allows his food to putrefy, which leads to bad breath. One who needs to relieve himself but eats before doing so is compared to a *tanur* that has been ignited over unswept ashes — the waste product is present and new material is piled on top. And one who washes in hot water without drinking from hot water is like an oven that has been heated on the outside but not on the inside: the external application is only half the remedy.

### Drinking After Bath

Two further principles follow. One who bathes in hot water without following with a cold rinse is like iron that has been placed in fire and not subsequently immersed in cold water — without the cold water to harden the metal after it has been heated and shaped, the process is incomplete. And one who bathes without applying oil afterward is like water running off the outside of a barrel — the oil is necessary to seal in the benefits of washing.

Rabbi Zeira's observation is striking: the content of what he learned was less about grand *Torah* principles and more about health, hygiene, and the appropriate use of the Hebrew language. Yet these, too, represent authentic *Torah* transmitted by example. The poignancy of the episode is heightened by Rabbi Zeira's position: he was about to depart in a manner that defied his teacher's most fundamental ruling, yet he could not leave without grasping the last available teaching. Far from distancing himself from Rav Yehuda, he clung to every word, even these seemingly mundane instructions, as irreplaceable *Torah*.

## Heat-Retaining Vessels

### Mulyar Rules

The *gemara* now introduces two vessels whose heat-retention properties govern their permissibility on Shabbos: the *mulyar* and the *antiki*. The *mulyar* is a thermos-like vessel with water on the inside and coals on the outside, maintaining heat through this surrounding arrangement. The *mishnah* rules: if the *mulyar* is *garuph* — swept out, meaning the coals have been removed — *shotin heimenu b'Shabbos*, one may drink from it on Shabbos. Its residual heat does not render it problematic once the coals are removed.

### Antiki Rules

The *antiki*, by contrast, is a vessel with more aggressive heat retention. One description, cited by Rava, identifies it as *bei kirei* — a vessel embedded within or closely associated with an oven. Another description, by Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, identifies it as *bei dudi*, a vessel with coals positioned underneath rather than on the sides. According to Rav Nachman, *bei dudi* alone would not be problematic, but *bei kirei* — which is more intensely heated — certainly would be. The *braita* supports Rav Nachman: an *antiki*, even when swept out and its ashes covered over (*gruphah u'ktumah*), may not be drunk from on Shabbos — *ein shotin heimenu* — because its copper or metal base retains heat so intensely, or (according to Rashi's alternative reading) because the floor of the vessel itself, having been heated, continues to radiate warmth.

### Kirah/Tanur Parallel

The structure of this ruling directly parallels the earlier distinction between the *kirah* and the *tanur*. A *kirah*, once swept out, may be used on Shabbos; a *tanur*, because of its superior heat retention, may not — even swept out. Similarly, the *mulyar* corresponds to the *kirah* and the *antiki* to the *tanur*. In both cases the underlying concern is either that one will come to stoke the coals, that the appearance of cooking on Shabbos is troubling, or — as Tosafos adds — that the water retained will remain at *yad soledet bo* and could cook wine when mixed with it, which is prohibited. In the case of the *antiki*, because of the intensity of its heat retention, none of these concerns is resolved by sweeping out.

## Kettle Water Rules

### Mishnah Rules

The *mishnah* introduces the case of a *meicham* — a hot water kettle — ruling that one may not add a small amount of cold water into it *bishvil sheyeichammu*, so that the water will be heated up. However, one may add a large amount of water into it or into a cup, *kedei lehafshiran*, merely to warm it. The distinction tracks precisely what was discussed earlier regarding placing water near a fire: warming is permitted; actual cooking — bringing to *yad soledet bo* — is not. Yet the *mishnah* leaves ambiguous the precise scenario: what exactly is the state of the *meicham*, and what determines whether added water will heat up or merely warm?

### Rav Adda Interpretation

Rav Adda bar Matnah's reading is as follows: *hamicham shepinah mimenu mayim* — the kettle from which the water has been poured out, leaving an empty but very hot vessel. Into this empty hot kettle, one may not add a little cold water, because the little water will quickly reach *yad soledet bo* — that is *bishul*. But one may add a large amount of cold water, because the volume of cold water will cool the vessel sufficiently that the water will only warm, not cook.

### Abaye's Version

Abaye objects on grammatical grounds: the *mishnah* does not say *shepinah mimenu mayim* — that water was removed from the kettle — but *shepinahu*, that the kettle was removed. This means: the kettle was taken off the fire, with its hot water still inside. Into this kettle — containing hot water but no longer on the fire — one may not add a little cold water, since the little water will become very hot. But one may add a large amount, since the combined effect will merely warm the overall contents without reaching *yad soledet bo*. The practical ruling is the same as Rav Adda's, but the scenario differs.

### Tempering Problem

Abaye's reading has an additional advantage: it avoids the problem of *mitzaref* — tempering. When one pours cold water into an empty, intensely hot metal vessel, the rapid cooling of the hot metal is the same process as tempering in metalwork — hardening a vessel by plunging it in cold water — which constitutes the completion of a vessel-making process and is potentially a biblical prohibition on Shabbos. If the *mishnah* is speaking of an empty hot vessel, then the permission to pour in a large amount of cold water would require permitting *mitzaref*, which is difficult. But according to Abaye, since the vessel already contains hot water, the act of adding cold water does not constitute *mitzaref*: tempering occurs specifically when a hot empty metal vessel is immersed in cold water, not when cold water is added to an already-filled vessel.

Abaye therefore adds a further ruling: if the vessel truly is empty — *meicham shepinah mimenu mayim* — then one may not add cold water at all, on account of *mitzaref*.

### Rav/Shmuel Dispute

The *gemara* records a dispute between Rav and Shmuel. Rav rules: it is only permitted if one intends merely to warm (*lehafshier*), but if the amount of water is sufficient to temper the vessel (*shiur letzaref*), it is forbidden. Shmuel rules: even an amount sufficient to temper the vessel is permitted.

The underlying question is whose view each authority follows on the principle of *davar she'eino mitkaven* — an unintended act. Rav holds like Rabbi Yehuda, who rules that an unintended act is prohibited even if one did not intend it, and therefore pouring in enough cold water that *mitzaref* becomes a real possibility is forbidden. Shmuel holds like Rabbi Shimon, who rules that an unintended act is permitted, and therefore as long as one does not intend to temper the vessel, the amount of cold water poured in is irrelevant.

One might ask whether *mitzaref* is in any case a *pesik reisha* — an inevitable consequence — which would be prohibited even according to Rabbi Shimon. The *gemara*'s implicit answer is that it is not inevitable: pouring cold water into a hot vessel will not necessarily produce the effect of tempering, and therefore Rabbi Shimon's leniency applies. The question of whether Shmuel consistently holds like Rabbi Shimon will be taken up on the following *daf*.
