# Bava Batra 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shetar & Publicity

### Three Owners & Shetar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה לָקוֹחוֹת מִצְטָרְפִין. אָמַר רַב: וְכוּלָּם בִּשְׁטָר.
>
> **Translation:** **Three buyers combine** to establish the presumption of ownership. If one purchased a field and sold it to another, who then sold it to a third party, and in total, the three of them worked and profited from the field for three years, the third one has established the presumption of ownership. **Rav says: And** this is the *halakha* only if they **all** purchased the land **with a bill** of sale. As a result of the bill of sale, the prior owner will know that it is not the case that each of them worked and profited from the field for only one year and abandoned it, which would explain why he did not bother to lodge a protest.

The Gemara opens Daf 42 by returning to the fundamental mechanism of *chazaka* — the three-year adverse possession that establishes a presumption of ownership over land. The baseline requirement is that a single person must occupy the land continuously for three years, for it is precisely this uninterrupted, visible presence that serves as sufficient provocation for the original owner to protest. Were three separate people each there for only one year, no individual *chazaka* would be established.

Rav, however, carves out an important exception. If Reuven occupied the land for a year, sold it to Shimon, and Shimon sold it to Levi, the three years of possession may be aggregated into a single continuous *chazaka* — provided the transfers are linked by *shetarot*. The logic is that the combined chain of possession is understood as a continuous assertion of Reuven's original ownership claim. But this works only if the original owner could plausibly have known that the connection among the three possessors existed. If the transfers were accomplished merely through witnesses, without *shetarot*, the original owner sees only three random people on his land, one after another — insufficient provocation to demand a response. It is the *shetar* that creates the public record, that generates the *kol* — the "voice," the notoriety — which puts the original owner on notice. Shimon and Levi must therefore be able to produce their respective *shetarot*, and only then does the original owner's silence across three years establish a *chazaka* against him.

### Shetar vs. Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַב: שְׁטָר אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא, וְעֵדִים לֵית לְהוּ קָלָא? וְהָאָמַר רַב: הַמּוֹכֵר שָׂדֶה בְּעֵדִים, גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים! הָתָם, לָקוֹחוֹת אִינְהוּ אַפְסִידוּ אַנַּפְשַׁיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rav holds** that **a bill** of sale generates **publicity, but witnesses do not** generate **publicity? But doesn't Rav say** that with regard to **one who sells a field in** the presence of **witnesses,** and that field is later seized by the creditors of the seller, the buyer **collects from** the **liened property** that the seller had sold to others, to be reimbursed for his seized field? If not for the fact that the sale in the presence of witnesses generates publicity, those who later purchased land from the seller would not have been aware that the property they are purchasing is liened to the first buyer. The Gemara answers: **There,** with regard to **buyers,** **they caused their own loss** by not investigating whether there was a lien on the property that they intended to buy.

The Gemara immediately presses on this requirement: why must the transfers be documented by *shetar*? Surely witnesses to a sale would also generate talk, would also produce a *kol* sufficient to put the original owner on notice? The Gemara rejects this. Only a *shetar* generates the requisite publicity; witnesses alone do not. And while witnesses to a land transaction may be sufficient to put a prospective *purchaser* on notice — since a buyer about to spend money has a duty of due diligence, caveat emptor — the original owner standing by passively and observing three successive people on his land cannot be presumed to know the internal legal connections among them unless there is documentary evidence in the form of *shetarot*.

### Liens & Documentation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב הָכִי? וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁטָר – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים, עַל יְדֵי עֵדִים – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And did Rav,** in fact, **say this,** that one can collect sold property by means of witnesses alone? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (175a): **One who lends money to another with** a promissory **note collects** his debt **from liened property** that had been sold after the loan, if the debtor has no other property; but if one lent **by means of witnesses** without a promissory note, he **collects** his debt only **from unsold property?**

This distinction between *shetar* and witnesses is not merely an invention of the present passage; it is rooted in a well-known *mishnah*. If one lends money with a *shetar*, the lender may collect from encumbered property — that is, from land the borrower has since sold to a third party — because the *shetar* was a public matter and any purchaser of the borrower's land is on notice that liens may exist. If the loan was made only with witnesses, however, the lender may collect only from the borrower's own unencumbered property (*nichsei bnei chorin*), because a purchaser cannot reasonably be expected to discover liens that were created without any public documentation. This rule is the accepted *halacha* and presents no difficulty.

### Loan vs. Sale Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: רַב תַּנָּא הוּא וּפָלֵיג, וְהָא רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁים וְלֹא מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **Rav is a *tanna*, and** as such has the authority to **dispute** the determination in the mishna, **but didn't Rav** himself **and Shmuel both say:** One who is owed a debt due to **a loan by oral** contract **does not collect** liened property, **not from the heirs** of the debtor **nor from the buyers,** despite the fact that there are witnesses?

The challenge the Gemara now raises is a tension within Rav's own rulings. It is well established — and Rav and Shmuel both hold this — that a loan made only orally (*milveh al peh*) creates no liens enforceable against either purchasers or heirs. That much is standard. The striking *chiddush* of Rav and Shmuel is that this limitation extends even to heirs: just as the lender cannot enforce liens against a purchaser of the borrower's land absent a *shetar*, so too the lender cannot enforce liens against the borrower's heirs absent a *shetar*. This is noteworthy because the case for protecting heirs seems weaker than the case for protecting purchasers. A purchaser actively spends money and deserves assurance that he can investigate existing liens; we therefore restrict liens to those created through the publicity of a *shetar*. Heirs, by contrast, passively inherit an estate — they have not spent anything — and intuitively one might think the lender should be able to collect from the estate as long as he can prove the debt through witnesses. Nevertheless, Rav and Shmuel apply the same rule symmetrically: if the loan creates no liens against a purchaser, it creates no liens against heirs either. This is not the accepted *psak*, but it is their *chiddush*, and what matters for present purposes is that Rav himself clearly accepts the distinction between *shetar* and witnesses in the context of loans.

Yet Rav also holds that when one sells land with *achrayut* — a warranty creating a lien on the seller's remaining property, so that if the sold land is later seized the buyer can collect reimbursement from the seller — those liens are created even if the sale was conducted only with witnesses and no *shetar*. How can Rav simultaneously maintain that loan-based liens require a *shetar* while sale-based liens created through witnesses are enforceable? This is the Gemara's question, and it demands a principled resolution.

### Privacy & Publicity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִלְוֶה אַזְּבִינֵי קָא רָמֵית?! מִלְוֶה, כִּי קָא יָזֵיף – בְּצִנְעָא קָא יָזֵיף, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלָא לִיתַּזְלוּ נִכְסֵיהּ עֲלֵיהּ. זְבִינֵי, מַאן דְּזָבֵין אַרְעָא – בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא זָבֵין, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִיפּוֹק לַהּ קָלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Are you raising a contradiction** from a case of **a loan to** a case of **a sale?** They are not comparable. In the case of **a loan, when** one **borrows** money he **borrows discreetly, in order that his property not be devalued,** as people will pay less for his property if they know that he is pressed for capital. Since a loan is issued discreetly, the presumption is that the buyers were unaware of the loan. Therefore, the creditor does not collect from sold property. By contrast, in the case of **a sale, one who sells land sells** it **in public in order that publicity be generated with regard to it.** Therefore, the cases of loans and sales are not comparable.

The Gemara's answer is elegant and draws on a fundamental difference in social context. When people borrow money, they borrow in private. A borrower does not want his neighbors to know that he needed a loan: it may carry a degree of social embarrassment, and beyond that, it carries a concrete financial danger. If it becomes known that a person is cash-poor and under pressure to raise funds, buyers of his property will lowball him. Knowing he is distressed and must sell, they will offer less than market value. Therefore, the borrower actively keeps the loan quiet, and even the lender may have an interest in protecting the borrower's financial standing — since the lender's own ability to be repaid depends on the borrower's assets retaining their value. When a loan is conducted with only witnesses, then, it is reasonable to assume the matter remains private. Even a diligent purchaser of the borrower's land, asking around about possible encumbrances, may simply have no way of discovering the loan. Witnesses who were sworn to discretion are not going to volunteer the information. To protect such a purchaser, the law therefore holds that liens attach only if there is a *shetar* — a formal, public document that the purchaser can discover through due diligence.

The case of a sale is entirely different. When a person sells land, he sells in public. He wants a bidding war; he posts notices, he tells people, he seeks the best price. The very transaction that generates the *achrayut* and its associated liens is by its nature conducted in the open. Therefore, even if the sale was accomplished with witnesses rather than a *shetar*, it is something a subsequent purchaser of the seller's property could discover — the original sale having generated its own *kol*. The lender's formula, *milveh ki ka yazif b'tzina ka yazif* — a borrower borrows in private — is exactly inverted for the seller: *man d'zavin, ihu befarsya zavin*, one who sells, sells in public. The result is that the legal treatment of liens tracks the actual epistemic situation of anyone who might later need to investigate: loan-based liens require a *shetar* because loans are kept quiet; sale-based liens are enforceable even with only witnesses because sales are conducted openly.

## Father & Son Chazaka

### Father-Son Succession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֲכָלָהּ הָאָב שָׁנָה וְהַבֵּן שְׁתַּיִם; הָאָב שְׁתַּיִם וְהַבֵּן שָׁנָה; הָאָב שָׁנָה, וְהַבֵּן שָׁנָה, וְהַלּוֹקֵחַ שָׁנָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues the discussion of the establishment of the presumption of ownership by successive possessors. **The Sages taught:** If **the father** worked and **profited** from the land for one **year and the son** who inherited it from him worked and profited from it for **two** years, or if **the father** worked and profited from the land for **two** years **and the son** worked and profited from it for one **year,** or if **the father** worked and profited from the land for one **year and the son** worked and profited from it for one **year, and the buyer,** who purchased it from the son, worked and profited from it for one **year, this is** sufficient to establish the **presumption** of ownership.

The Gemara now turns to a *baraita* that extends the principles of *chazaka* to intergenerational chains of possession. The *mishnah* having already addressed how possession by a father and son aggregates into a single *chazaka*, the *baraita* systematically enumerates the permutations: the father occupied for one year and the son for two; the father for two and the son for one; or even a three-way division in which the father held for one year, the son for one year, and then a purchaser (*lokeach*) for a final year. In all these configurations, the *baraita* rules: *harei zu chazaka* — this constitutes a valid *chazaka*.

Before reaching the question about the *lokeach*, it is worth pausing over the *chiddush* embedded in the simple father-son case. That a son who has just inherited an estate is immediately expected to know all of his father's land holdings and dealings — and that his failure to protest an adverse possessor's presence constitutes legally meaningful silence — is a genuinely striking ruling. The son is an adult, but coming up to speed with a deceased parent's business affairs takes time, and yet the *halacha* treats his silence as legally equivalent to the father's.

### Sale as Protest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּלוֹקֵחַ אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא?! וּרְמִינְהִי: אֲכָלָהּ בִּפְנֵי הָאָב שָׁנָה וּבִפְנֵי הַבֵּן שְׁתַּיִם; בִּפְנֵי הָאָב שְׁתַּיִם וּבִפְנֵי הַבֵּן שָׁנָה; בִּפְנֵי הָאָב שָׁנָה, וּבִפְנֵי הַבֵּן שָׁנָה, וּבִפְנֵי לוֹקֵחַ שָׁנָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ לוֹקֵחַ אִית לֵיהּ קָלָא, אֵין לְךָ מְחָאָה גְּדוֹלָה מִזּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that** with regard to **a buyer,** the transaction generates **publicity? And** one can **raise a contradiction** from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 2:8): If one worked and **profited** from the land **in the presence of the father,** the prior owner, for one **year, and in the presence of the son,** who then inherited it from the father, for **two** years; or **in the presence of the father** for **two** years **and in the presence of the son** for one **year;** or **in the presence of the father** for one **year and in the presence of the son** for one **year and in the presence of the buyer,** who purchased it from the son, for one **year; this is** sufficient to establish the **presumption** of ownership. **And if it enters your mind** that with regard to **a buyer,** the transaction generates **publicity, there is no greater protest than this.** By selling his land to someone else, the son of the prior owner is clearly stating that it does not belong to the possessor.

The Gemara now notices a tension with the three-way case involving a *lokeach*. In that scenario, the original owner's son sells the land to a third party. Ought that sale not function as a *mecha'ah* — a protest asserting the original owner's (or his heir's) claim — which would thereby interrupt the *chazaka*? If a sale *does* generate a *kol* (as we established earlier, that sales are public matters), then the person holding the land adversely should have been informed of the protest. But if the sale of the son to the *lokeach* does not function as a *mecha'ah*, it must be because sales do not generate a *kol* — in which case how can we say, as we did earlier, that purchasing land is a public matter that puts third parties on notice? The Gemara articulates this tension sharply: if a *lokeach* has a *kol* (and thus the adverse possessor should have known about the protest), then there is no greater *mecha'ah* than selling one's land to someone else. But if the *lokeach* has no *kol*, then how can the continuity of the *chazaka* be established through the chain of transfers in the first place?

### Unspecified vs. Specific

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּי תַּנְיָא הָהִיא – בְּמוֹכֵר שְׂדוֹתָיו סְתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** That is not a contradiction, as **when that** *baraita* **is taught,** it is taught **with regard to** one who **sells his fields without specification.** The son of the prior owner sold his fields without clarifying which fields he was selling. Since he did not specify the field from which the possessor is profiting, the possessor had no reason to assume that this field was being sold and that he needed to hold on to his deed, and he establishes the presumption of ownership despite the sale. In a case where the son of the prior owner stated explicitly that he was selling the field in question, the sale would serve as his protest.

Rav Papa resolves the contradiction by distinguishing between a sale that identifies the disputed land specifically and one that does not. In the *baraita*'s case, the son sells not this particular plot but all of his father's properties collectively — a generic conveyance without specifying any individual field. From the perspective of the adverse possessor, who holds that the disputed land is no longer part of the father's estate (since it was purchased from the father), such a blanket sale of "all my father's land" does not include the disputed property and therefore cannot function as a *mecha'ah* against him. He hears the news of a sale and reasonably thinks: that sale doesn't encompass my land. But if the sale specifically identified the disputed plot, it would constitute an effective *mecha'ah*, because the adverse possessor could no longer claim ignorance.

Tosafos notes that a simpler resolution would have invoked the *shetar* versus witnesses distinction: a sale accompanied by a *shetar* generates a *kol* and functions as a *mecha'ah*; a sale with only witnesses does not. Rav Papa's answer, however, adds a further layer: even with a *shetar*, if the disputed parcel is not individually identified, the sale cannot serve as a *mecha'ah* for that specific land. The adverse possessor's silence during the three-year period therefore remains unimpeached, and the *chazaka* stands.

## Exclusions from Chazaka

### Craftsmen & Chattel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוּמָּנִין, וְהַשּׁוּתָּפִין, וְהָאֲרִיסִין, וְהָאַפּוֹטְרוֹפִּין – אֵין לָהֶם חֲזָקָה. לֹא לָאִישׁ חֲזָקָה בְּנִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְלֹא לָאִשָּׁה חֲזָקָה בְּנִכְסֵי בַּעֲלָהּ; וְלֹא לָאָב בְּנִכְסֵי הַבֵּן, וְלֹא לַבֵּן בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Craftsmen** who are in possession of items that they are repairing, **and partners, and sharecroppers, and stewards [*veha'apotropin*] do not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership with regard to property in their possession, as their possession is not indicative of ownership. Similarly, **a man does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to his wife's property, and a wife does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to her husband's property. And a father** similarly **does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to a son's property, and a son does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership **with regard to a father's property.** A husband and wife, or son and father, use each other's property freely. Possession is therefore not indicative of ownership.

The next *mishnah* shifts to enumerate categories of persons whose possession of property does not give rise to any *chazaka*. The first category consists of *umnim* — craftsmen. Unlike the three-year *chazaka* of land, the *chazaka* applicable to *metaltelin* (moveable goods) is immediate: anything found in a person's possession is presumed to be his. An *uman*, however, enjoys no such presumption with respect to the very goods that are the objects of his trade. A dry cleaner holding David's suit, a shoemaker holding a pair of shoes left for repair — these cannot claim ownership merely through possession. The professional context of the relationship is itself the explanation for why the item came to be in the craftsman's hands, and that context entirely defeats any presumption of ownership. Similarly, objects that an owner regularly lends or rents out do not generate a presumption of ownership in the hands of the borrower or renter, since the owner's practice of lending explains the possession without implying a transfer of title.

### Partnership & Status

The *mishnah* then lists *shutfin* (partners), *arisin* (sharecroppers), and *apotropin* (trustees or administrators) as persons who cannot establish a *chazaka* over land. In each case, the pre-existing relationship between the parties provides an independent explanation for why one person is working or managing the other's land, an explanation that completely undercuts the inference that would otherwise flow from uncontested possession. A partner who works the entire field for three years might plausibly have done so under some arrangement — perhaps taking the first three years' produce while his partner takes the next three. A sharecropper who retains all of the harvest for three years rather than splitting it with the landowner might simply be operating under a restructured payment arrangement. A trustee or administrator (*apotropos*, more precisely: one with power of attorney or fiduciary authority) who manages an estate is by definition on the land in a representative capacity. In all these cases, the contextual relationship so thoroughly explains the possession that no adverse possession claim can arise.

### Family Relations

The *mishnah* continues with spousal and filial relationships. A husband working his wife's *nichsei melog* — property over which she retains title but whose produce he is entitled to during the marriage — obviously does so by virtue of his marital right to the *peirot*, not because he claims ownership. The inference runs so transparently that the *mishnah* states it almost as a matter of course. More interesting is the converse: a wife who takes over the management and administration of her husband's land cannot claim a *chazaka*, and the Rashbam adds that since the husband is obligated to provide *mezonos* for her, she may well be working the land to secure that provision rather than to assert ownership. The same principle applies between father and son in either direction: familial relationships so naturally explain joint use and management of property that no presumption of ownership through possession can arise. The practical consequence the *mishnah* draws is direct — if one actually did purchase land from a family member, it is essential to preserve the *shetar*, because the possession itself will not speak for you.

## Types of Chazaka

### Benefit & Presumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בְּמַחְזִיק; אֲבָל בְּנוֹתֵן מַתָּנָה, וְהָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ, וְהַמַּחְזִיק בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר – נָעַל וְגָדֵר וּפָרַץ כׇּל שֶׁהוּא – הֲרֵי זוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **In what** case **is this statement,** that one establishes the presumption of ownership after profiting from the property for a certain duration, with the exception of the above people, **said?** It is said **in** a case of one who **has** mere **possession** of the property, which does, in some cases, serve as proof of ownership. **But in** a case where another person **gives** one **a gift, or** there are **brothers who divided** their inheritance, **or** there is one who **takes possession of** the **property of a convert** who died without heirs and his property is now ownerless, as soon as one **locked** the door of the property, **or fenced** it **or breached** its fence even **a bit, this is** considered **taking possession** of the property, and it effects acquisition.

Having catalogued the exclusions from *chazaka*, the *mishnah* now asks: to what type of *chazaka* do these exclusions apply? The answer is: to the *chazaka* of demonstrating ownership — the three-year possession that creates a presumption of title. This is the *chazaka* in which one derives *benefit* from the land: harvesting crops, reaping produce. That sustained pattern of benefiting from land is what provides the original owner with sufficient provocation to protest, and therefore, if the original owner fails to protest, his silence yields the adverse possessor's claim.

### Effectuating Ownership

But the *mishnah* immediately pivots to a different category of *chazaka* altogether — one that does not merely *demonstrate* ownership but actually *effectuates* it. When land is given as a gift, when brothers divide an inheritance and each takes possession of his share, or when one takes possession of the estate of a convert who died without heirs (and whose property therefore becomes *hefker*), the question is not one of demonstrating pre-existing ownership through sustained use but of completing a legal transaction. Land transfers by means of *kesef*, *shetar*, or *chazaka* — payment, document, or a concrete act of possession. In these contexts, the *chazaka* functions as the act that closes the transaction.

### Acts of Improvement

The *mishnah* specifies the acts that constitute this transactional *chazaka*: *na'al* — locking the gate; *gader* — building a fence or wall; *paratz* — opening or creating a necessary breach in a wall, done not destructively but constructively. Even *kol shehu* — even a minimal act of this kind performed on even a small portion of the land — constitutes a valid *chazaka* of possession. This is worth contrasting with the *chazaka* of demonstrating ownership: there, what matters is the *taking* of benefit from the land — harvesting, reaping — since only that sufficiently provokes the original owner to respond. Here, what matters is the *giving* of benefit to the land — improvement, enclosure, construction. The two modes of *chazaka* are thus structurally inverse: the three-year presumption of ownership rests on deriving benefit; the transactional *chazaka* rests on bestowing it.

## Shmuel's Position

### Partners Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל וְלֵוִי תָּנוּ: שׁוּתָּף אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן אוּמָּן. שְׁמוּאֵל תָּנֵי: אוּמָּן אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה, אֲבָל שׁוּתָּף יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Shmuel's father and Levi taught: A partner does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership of property in his possession, **and, all the more so,** this inability applies to **a craftsman** as well. But **Shmuel teaches: A craftsman does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership of property in his possession, **but a partner** does **have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership.

Shmuel's father and Levi taught that a *shutaf* (partner) has no *chazaka* — and that an *uman* (craftsman) certainly has no *chazaka*, a case so obvious it need not even be stated. Shmuel, however, directly disputes his father. Shmuel's version of the *mishnah* includes the *uman* explicitly and excludes the *shutaf*: an *uman* has no *chazaka* (obvious enough to warrant explicit mention), but a *shutaf* does have a *chazaka* under certain circumstances. Shmuel thus removes partners from the categorical exclusion and holds that three years of uncontested possession by one partner against the other can indeed establish ownership.

### Partners as Guardians

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא שְׁמוּאֵל לְטַעְמֵיהּ – דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַשּׁוּתָּפִין מַחְזִיקִין זֶה עַל זֶה, וּמְעִידִין זֶה עַל זֶה, וְנַעֲשִׂים שׁוֹמְרֵי שָׂכָר זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Shmuel follows his** line of **reasoning, as Shmuel says: Partners establish** the **presumption** of ownership with regard to the property of **each other, and** they **testify for each other and become paid bailees** of their joint property with regard **to each other.** In terms of these issues, Shmuel considers partners to be independent parties.

Shmuel's broader position on partnership law is laid out in an expansive statement: *ha'shutfin machzikin zeh al zeh u'me'idin zeh al zeh ve'na'asim shomrei sachar zeh la'zeh* — partners can establish *chazaka* against one another, can testify on behalf of one another, and are considered *shomrei sachar* (paid guardians) with respect to one another's share. Each of these three rulings is striking. The *chazaka* point is Shmuel's central thesis here; the testimony point seems to violate the rule against self-interested testimony (*nogei'a b'edut*) and the Gemara flags it as something to be addressed. The *shomer sachar* point has immediate practical consequences: if two partners share a boat and one partner watches it on the other's day off — each taking a turn at supervision, with the "payment" being the reciprocal watch — then the watching partner is a *shomer sachar* on the other's half. If the boat is stolen on his watch, he is liable to compensate his partner's entire half, whereas absent this rule both partners would simply share the loss proportionally.

### Implied Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה בִּמְעָרְתָּא דְּבֵי רַב זַכַּאי, מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שׁוּתָּף יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲזָקָה? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: שׁוּתָּף כְּיוֹרֵד בִּרְשׁוּת דָּמֵי. לָאו לְמֵימְרָא דְּשׁוּתָּף אֵין לוֹ חֲזָקָה? לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דִּנְחֵית לְכוּלַּהּ, הָא דִּנְחֵית לְפַלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba raises a contradiction to Rav Yehuda in the case of Rav Zakkai's house: Did Shmuel** actually **say** that **a partner has** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership? **But doesn't Shmuel say: A partner is** considered **as** one who **enters** the field **with permission,** such as a sharecropper? **Isn't that to say that a partner does not have** the ability to establish the **presumption** of ownership? The Gemara answers: It is **not difficult. This** is referring to **where** he **enters all of** the field, and **that** is referring to **where** he **enters half** of the field.

But there is an apparent contradiction within Shmuel's own rulings. On one hand, Shmuel holds that a partner can have a *chazaka* — implying that prolonged sole use of the land by one partner should provoke the other to protest. On the other hand, Shmuel also holds that a *shutaf ke-yored birshut dami* — a partner is always considered to act with implicit permission. If a partner's presence on the land is always deemed permitted, then his presence can never be adverse, and there should be no *chazaka*. The Gemara resolves this tension by distinguishing between the *scope* of the partner's occupation: whether he has descended upon the whole field (*nachas le-kula*) or only upon half (*nachas le-palga*).

### Full vs. Half Field

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְהַאי גִּיסָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Some say it in this manner and some say it in that manner.** On the one hand, it is possible to explain that if he entered half of the field he establishes the presumption of ownership with regard to that half, but if he entered the entire field he is merely acting as a partner. On the other hand, one could explain that entering half of the field does not establish the presumption of ownership at all, while entering the entire field does establish it.

Here the Gemara records a dispute — *amri lei le-hai gisa ve-amri lei le-hai gisa*, some say it one way and some say the other. One view holds that if Chanan has been working only his half of the field for three years while the other partner is present and working his own half, that *does* constitute a *chazaka*: since both are present and the division of the field's use is visible, the partner working his half exclusively for three years without any rotation or protest provides a genuine basis for claiming that half as his own through purchase. If, on the other hand, Chanan has been working the entire field for three years, that cannot be a *chazaka*, because the other partner can simply say: we have an informal arrangement by which you work it for a few years and I work it for the next few years. The other view inverts the logic: if Chanan is working his half and the partner is visibly working his own half, this simply reflects the ordinary state of a working partnership, and nothing about it should provoke a protest or create a *chazaka*. But if Chanan is working the *entire* field for three years — including the portion that the other partner ought to be cultivating — that *should* provoke the partner to act, and his failure to do so is meaningful silence that supports a *chazaka*.

### Field Divisibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא דִּנְחֵית לְכוּלַּהּ; וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דְּאִית בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ, הָא דְּלֵית בָּהּ דִּין חֲלוּקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina stated** a different resolution to the contradiction: **Both this and that** are referring to a case **where** he **enters the entire** field, **and** it is **not difficult. This** is referring to a case **where** the field **is of** sufficient area to be subject to the ***halakha* of division.** In this case, his being in possession of the other half of the field as well, which belonged to his partner, establishes the presumption of ownership. **That** is referring to a case **where** the field **is not of** sufficient area to be subject to the ***halakha* of division.** Since the property will not be divided but will remain co-owned, he is merely possessing it as a partner and does not establish the presumption of ownership.

Ravina offers a further resolution that cuts across the debate differently. Rather than distinguishing between half and whole, Ravina distinguishes based on whether the field is subject to *din chaluka* — whether it is large enough that either partner could compel a division. If the field cannot be divided (*leit bei din chaluka*) because it is too small for each half to be independently viable, then it is entirely expected that one partner will work it alone for a period of time while the other waits for his turn; sole occupation is thus explained by the field's indivisibility and cannot constitute a *chazaka*. If the field is large enough to be divided (*it bei din chaluka*), then joint cultivation of separate halves is practical and normal, and if one partner instead monopolizes the entire field for three years without any participation from the other and without protest, that extended sole use *does* constitute a *chazaka*. Ravina's resolution returns us to the beginning of the tractate and its discussion of *mishpat chaluka* — the law of partition — now deployed here to calibrate when a partner's possession is sufficiently adverse to support a *chazaka* claim.

The upshot of Shmuel's position, taken as a whole, is that the categorical exclusion of partners from the law of *chazaka* stated in the *mishnah* is not absolute. There are circumstances — defined by what portion of the field is at issue and whether that field is subject to division — in which a partner's uncontested possession over three years does ripen into a valid *chazaka*, because the other partner's silence is legally meaningful rather than merely passive acquiescence within the normal latitude of partnership.
