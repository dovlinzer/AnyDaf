# Gittin 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Core Get Language

### Mishna & R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״וְדֵן, דְּיֶהֱוֵי לִיכִי מִינַּאי – סֵפֶר תֵּירוּכִין, וְאִגֶּרֶת שִׁבּוּקִין, וְגֵט פִּטּוּרִין; לִמְהָךְ לְהִתְנְסָבָא לְכֹל גְּבַר דִּיתִצְבִּיִּין״. גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ (בַּת) [בֶּן] חוֹרִין״; ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לְעַצְמָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** that there is also another essential sentence: **And this that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce, and a letter of leave, and a bill of dismissal to go to marry any man that you wish.** And **the basic** element **of a bill of manumission** for a maidservant is: **You are hereby a free woman,** or: **You are hereby your own.**

The *daf* begins in the middle of 85b, where the *mishna* establishes that the key line of a *get* is *harei at muteret le-chol adam* — "behold, you are permitted to all men." Rabbi Yehuda, however, requires that the *get* be more explicit and self-referential, stating *v'dein* — "and this" — should serve as an instrument of divorce. Not only does his formulation contain additional language, but it explicitly declares that the document itself is functioning as the instrument of divorce.

### Leatzimech Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא, אָמַר לָהּ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ בַּת חוֹרִין״ – לֹא אָמַר וְלֹא כְּלוּם; אָמַר לָהּ לְשִׁפְחָתוֹ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״ – לֹא אָמַר וְלֹא כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is obvious** that if one **stated** in a bill of divorce **for his wife: You are hereby a free woman, he has stated nothing,** as that is not a statement of divorce. Similarly, if one **stated** in a bill of manumission **for his maidservant: You are hereby permitted to** marry **any man, he has stated nothing,** as that is not a statement of manumission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהּ לְאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לְעַצְמָךְ״, מַהוּ? לִגְמָרֵי קָאָמַר לַהּ, אוֹ לִמְלָאכָה קָאָמַר לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If **he stated** in a bill of divorce **for** his **wife: You are hereby your own, what is** the *halakha*? **Is he saying to her:** You are **entirely** your own, meaning that she has full control over her status and may marry another man? **Or is he saying to her:** You are your own with regard **to work,** meaning that she may keep the revenue from her work? If so, this is not a statement of divorce.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, דִּתְנַן: גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ (בַּת) [בֶּן] חוֹרִין״; ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לְעַצְמָךְ״. וּמָה עַבְדָּא, דִּקְנִי לֵיהּ גּוּפֵיהּ, כִּי אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ לְעַצְמָךְ״ – קְנִי גּוּפֵיהּ; אִשָּׁה, דְּלָא קְנִי גּוּפַהּ – לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a solution **that we learned** in the mishna: **The basic** element **of a bill of manumission is: You are hereby a free woman,** or: **You are hereby your own. And just as** with regard to **a slave, whose body is acquired** by the master, **if** the master **states** in a bill of manumission **for** the slave: **You are hereby your own,** the slave **acquires his body, all the more so** with regard to **a wife, whose body is not acquired** by her husband, is it **not** clear that by his writing in a bill of divorce: You are hereby your own, she is completely released from their marriage?

The *Gemara* then explores which formulations are valid and which are not. If a man says to his wife *harei at bat chorin* — "you are a free woman" — that is meaningless as a *get*, because *bat chorin* carries the specific meaning of being free as opposed to being a slave, and is not a language of divorce. Conversely, if he says to his female slave *harei at muteret le-chol adam*, that too is ineffective, since the language of permissibility to all men is not relevant to the slave's status of bondage or freedom.

The more interesting case arises when a man says to his wife *harei at le-atzmech* — "you are under your own control." This formulation works for freeing a slave, but is it general enough to function as divorce language as well? The *Gemara* frames the question: does *le-atzmech* mean *legamrei* — fully and completely — such that it severs all connection including marriage, or does it mean only that he is releasing her from his rights to her labor, which would be relevant only to a slave?

Ravina raises a proof for Rav Ashi from the *mishna*: the essence of a *get shichrur* is either *harei at bat chorin* or *harei at le-atzmech*. The *Gemara* reasons: *uma avda dekani lei gufei* — by a slave, over whom the master owns the body entirely, if one says *harei at le-atzmech*, the slave acquires ownership of himself or herself. This demonstrates that the phrase means a full withdrawal of control, not merely a waiver of labor rights. *Isha d'lo kani lei gufei* — by a wife, over whom the husband's control is less comprehensive than that of a master over a slave — *kal va-chomer* that *harei at le-atzmech* constitutes a complete removal of ownership. The *Gemara* thus establishes that any formulation lacking the specific semantic content of "slave versus free person" — meaning one that expresses a full severance of ownership and control in general terms — can function for both manumission and divorce.

### Ein Li Esek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: אָמַר לוֹ לְעַבְדּוֹ: ״אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בָּךְ״, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi:** If **he stated** in a bill of manumission **for his slave: I have no business with you, what is** the *halakha*? Is this a valid statement of manumission?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנִין לְרַב אָשֵׁי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב חָנִין מָחוֹזְנָאָה לְרַב אָשֵׁי: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר עַבְדּוֹ לְגוֹי – יָצָא לְחֵירוּת, וְצָרִיךְ גֵּט שִׁחְרוּר מֵרַבּוֹ רִאשׁוֹן. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – שֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ, אֲבָל כָּתַב עָלָיו אוֹנוֹ – זֶהוּ שִׁחְרוּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanin said to Rav Ashi, and some say** that it was **Rav Ḥanin from Meḥoza** who said this **to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** a solution **that we learned** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who sells his slave to a gentile,** the slave **is emancipated but** nevertheless **requires a bill of manumission from his first master. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is **when** the master **did not write his document for** the slave when he sold him; **but** if **he wrote his document for him, that is** the slave's bill of **manumission.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אוֹנוֹ״? אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ: ״לִכְשֶׁתִּבְרַח מִמֶּנּוּ, אֵין לִי עֵסֶק בָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the phrase: **His document,** referring to? **Rav Sheshet says that he writes for him** as follows: **When you will escape from** the gentile to whom I sold you, **I have no business with you.** Consequently, as soon as he escapes his gentile master in any manner, he is entirely emancipated. It may be derived from here that the statement: I have no business with you, is valid with regard to manumission.

Ravina then poses to Rav Ashi a further question: if a man says to his slave *ein li esek bach* — "I will have no dealings with you" — what is the status? This language sounds like a waiver of rights rather than a positive grant of freedom or a fundamental change of status. Rav Chanan, or according to another version Rav Chanin of Mechuza, brings a proof from a *baraita*: one who sells his slave to a non-Jew — the slave goes free, and the first master must still write a *get shichrur*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies: when is a second document needed? Only when the master had not already written a document at the time of sale. If he had written one, *zehu shichruro* — that earlier document serves as the writ of manumission. What was this document? Rav Sheshes explains: it was a document stating that should the slave ever escape from the non-Jew, *ein li esek bach* — "I waive all rights to you." This establishes that *ein li esek bach* functions as a valid *shtar shichrur*.

This is a genuine *chiddush*, because the phrase "I will have nothing to do with you" does not on its face convey a positive transfer of self-ownership or a change of status — it sounds more like a unilateral withdrawal. Yet we find that it works for a slave. The natural follow-up question is whether it similarly works in a bill of divorce, and given the *Gemara*'s reasoning that a language effective for freeing a slave — as long as it does not carry the specific meaning of slave-versus-free — is sufficient for divorce as well, the presumptive answer is yes.

### Explicit vs Implicit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״וְדֵן, דְּיֶהֱוֵי לִיכִי מִינַּאי – סֵפֶר תֵּירוּכִין וְאִגֶּרֶת שִׁבּוּקִין״. בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? רַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: יָדַיִם שֶׁאֵין מוֹכִיחוֹת הָוְיָין יָדַיִם, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא כְּתַב לָהּ ״וְדֵן״ – מוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא דִּבְהַאי גִּיטָּא קָא מְגָרֵשׁ לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says** that in addition to the statement: You are hereby permitted to marry any man, there is another essential sentence in the bill of divorce: **And this [*veden*] that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce and a letter [*ve'iggeret*] of leave.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to what** principle **do** Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis **disagree?** The Gemara answers: **The Rabbis hold** that **ambiguous intimations are valid intimations.** An incomplete statement is sufficient as long as the intention is clear from the context. **And** therefore, **even if he does not write for her** explicitly: **And this** that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce and a letter of leave, the bill of divorce is valid as **it is evident that he is divorcing her with this bill of divorce.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: יָדַיִם שֶׁאֵין מוֹכִיחוֹת לָא הָוְיָין יָדַיִם, וְטַעְמָא דִּכְתַב לַהּ ״וְדֵן״ – דְּמוֹכְחָא מִילְּתָא דִּבְהַאי גִּיטָּא קָא מְגָרֵשׁ לַהּ; אֲבָל לָא כְּתַב לָהּ ״וְדֵן״ – אָמְרִי: בְּדִיבּוּרָא גָּרְשַׁהּ, וּשְׁטָרָא רְאָיָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **ambiguous intimations are not valid intimations, and** therefore **the reason** that the bill of divorce **is** valid is **that he writes for her: And this** that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce and a letter of leave, **so it is evident that he is divorcing her with this bill of divorce. But** if **he does not write for her: And this** that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce and a letter of leave, people **will say** that **he is divorcing her orally,** and will mistakenly assume that the husband's statement to her when he hands her the bill of divorce is what causes the divorce to take effect, **and** that **the document is merely evidence** of the divorce, rather than the divorce itself.

The *Gemara* now addresses the heart of the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *tanna kamma*. Rabbi Yehuda requires the *get* to state *v'dein di yehavi lichi minai sefer teiruchin v'igeret shivukin* — "and this shall be to you from me a book of divorce and a writ of release." One might ask why any document should need to declare itself the instrument it constitutes — a standard *shtar mechira* simply says *sadi mechura lach* and is handed over; a *shtar kiddushin* says *harei at mekudeshet li*. Neither describes itself as the instrument of transfer. The *Gemara*, however, does not frame the debate this way.

*Rabbanan savri yadayim she-einan mochichos haviyan yadayim* — the Rabbis hold that even an implicit or non-explicit indication (*yad*) suffices. The document need not explicitly state that it is the instrument of divorce; it is sufficiently clear from context. *Mochiach milta de-be-hai gitta kama-garesh lah* — the fact that this document is being given as a *get* makes the matter apparent. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds *yadayim she-ein mochichos lav havyan yadayim* — an implicit indication that falls short of being fully evident does not qualify. Without the explicit *v'dein*, one might argue *be-dibura girsheha* — he is divorcing her verbally — *ve-shtara re'aya be-alma* — and the document is merely serving as evidence of that verbal act. Rabbi Yehuda thus requires both that the *get* make clear it is the instrument of divorce and that it do so explicitly. Both of these are genuine *chiddushim*, but the first — that a *get* must identify itself as the instrument at all — is arguably the more striking one.

## Nusach & Letters

### Spelling Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי מַאן דְּכָתֵב גִּיטָּא, לָא לִכְתּוֹב ״וְדֵין״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע וְדִין; אֶלָּא ״וְדֵן״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates several rulings concerning the precise terminology to be used in writing a bill of divorce. **Abaye said: This person who writes a bill of divorce should not write** the word meaning: **And this,** by spelling it *vav*, *dalet*, *yod*, *nun*, **as** that can be misread as having the vowel of a *ḥirik* under the letter *dalet*, not a *tzeire*. Read with a *ḥirik*, it **indicates: And** there is **a law** that we should get divorced. **Rather,** he should make sure to write the word meaning: **And this,** without a *yod*, so that it is clear that it should be read with a *tzeire*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא לִכְתּוֹב ״אִיגֶּרֶת״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע (אִיגֶּרֶת) [אִיגָּרָא]; אֶלָּא ״אִגֶּרֶת״. וְלָא לִכְתּוֹב ״לִימְהָךְ״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע לִי מֵהָךְ; וְלָא לִכְתּוֹב ״לִמְחָךְ״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע כִּי חוּכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And he should not write** the word meaning: **A letter,** by spelling it *alef*, *yod*, *gimmel*, *reish*, *tav*, **as** that can be confused with another identically spelled word that **indicates a roof. Rather,** he should write the word meaning: **A letter,** without a *yod*. **And he should not write: To go,** by spelling it *lamed*, *yod*, *mem*, *heh*, *khaf*, **as** that could be read as a conjunction which **indicates: For me from this. And he should** be sure **not to write *limḥakh*,** i.e., he must be careful that the letter *heh* not look like a *ḥet*, **as that indicates** that it is **like a joke.**

The *Gemara* now turns to extreme precision in the drafting of the *get*. Abaye enumerates a series of spelling hazards. The word *v'dein* must be written without a *yod* — *v'den* — lest it be read as *v'din*, suggesting "it is the law" that the *get* be given, which is both inaccurate and misleading. The word for letter or writ must be written *ageret* without a *yod*, not *igeret* with one, lest it be misread as *igra* — a roof — yielding the absurd meaning "this should be to you a roof of divorce." The word for "to go," *le-meihach*, must be written with a *heh*, not *le-mihach* with a *chet*, lest it be read as *le-mihach* — "to mock" — turning the *get* into something of a Purim joke.

### Rashi Nusach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״דִּיתִיהְוִייִין״, ״דִּיתִיצְבִּייִין״ – תְּלָתָא תְּלָתָא יוֹדִין; דְּמַשְׁמַע תֶּהֶוְיָין וְתִצְבְּיָין. וְלוֹרְכֵיהּ לְוָיו ״דְּתֵירוּכִין״, וּלְוָיו ״דְּשִׁבּוּקִין״; דְּמַשְׁמַע תְּרִיכִין וּשְׁבִיקִין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: In the clause: That you shall be permitted to go marry any man that you wish, the words ***ditihevyin*** and ***dititzviyin*** must include **three** instances of the letter ***yod*** in a row in each word, **as** with only two instances of the letter *yod* these words **indicate:** That **they shall be [*tehevyan*], and:** That **they wish [*titzviyan*],** referring to other women. **And he should extend the *vav* of *teirukhin* and the *vav* of *shevukin*, as** otherwise, the *vav* may be mistaken for a *yod*, and those words spelled with a *yod* **indicate divorced [*terikhin*] and left [*shevikin*]** women. In other words, it will change the meaning from describing the document as one that divorces or sends away to describing the women as divorced and sent away.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹרְכֵיהּ לְוָיו ״דְּכַדּוּ״; דְּמַשְׁמַע ״וּכְדִי״. וְלָא לִיכְתּוֹב ״לְאִיתְנְסָבָא״ – דְּמַשְׁמַע לָא יִתְנַסְבָא, אֶלָּא ״לְהִתְנְסָבָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in the clause: And now [*ukhedu*] I have dismissed and ousted and divorced you, **he should extend the *vav* of *khedu*, as** otherwise, the *vav* may be mistaken for a *yod*, and spelled with a *yod* it **indicates: And with nothing [*ukhedi*]. And** in the expression: To go marry [*lehitnasseva*] **he should not write *le'itnasseva*** with an *alef* and a *yod*, **as,** if he leaves space between the letters it will **indicate: Will not get married [*la yitnasseva*]. Rather,** he should write ***lehitnasseva*,** with a *heh* and without a *yod*, so there will be no room for this error.

Rashi provides the complete standard *nusach ha-get*, which runs as follows. *Petirus ychi lichi* — I have freed you to yourself — *ant plonit bat ploni*, you, such-and-such daughter of such-and-such, *de-haveit inteti min kadmat de-na* — who were my wife prior to this — *ve-chadu* — and with this — *petirat u-shvakat ve-taricat ychi* — I have released and divorced you — *di te-haveyan rasha'a u-shalta'a be-nafshichi* — that you should be permitted and under your own authority — *le-meihach u-le-hitnasba le-chol man di tetzbayan* — to go and be married to whomever you desire — *ve-inash lo yimchei be-yadaichi* — and no man shall protest or stand in your way — *min yoma de-nan u-le-alam* — from this day forward — *ve-den di ye-havei lichi minai* — and this shall be to you from me — *get piturin ve-sefer teiruchin ve-igeret shevukin* — a bill of release, a book of divorce, and a writ of abandonment — *ke-dat Moshe ve-Yisrael*.

One notable observation: Rashi's *nusach* does not explicitly include *harei at muteret le-chol adam*, even though the *mishna* identifies this as the core line of the *get*. The complete *nusach* as cited by both Rashi and the *Rif* is clearly the product of a received *mesora*, which is why Tosafot references the concept of a *tikun gittin* — an established authoritative text for the formulation of a *get*.

The *Gemara* continues with a requirement that the *get* contain three *yudin* in the word *de-yihavyan* — "that you should be" — and three in *de-yitzbayan* — "that you should desire." The reasoning is that with only two *yudin*, the phrase could be read impersonally — that women generally may desire or may be permitted — whereas three *yudin* make the language personal and direct, referring specifically to this woman. Tosafot adds that there must be five *yudin* in total: three at the end of *de-yihavyan* and one each before and after the *taf* of *de-yitzbayan*.

### Tosafot Letters

Tosafot raises several fine points regarding letter formation. The *vav* in *teiruchin* must be written long enough that it is not mistaken for a *yod*; if read as a *yod*, the word would become *terichin* — divorced women — rather than the abstract noun meaning "of divorce," yielding the absurd phrase "a book of divorcees." Similarly, the *vav* in *chadu* must be sufficiently elongated that it is not confused with a *yod*, which would transform *chadu* — "with this" — into *chadi*, meaning "for nothing" or "for naught." The word *le-hitnasba* must be written with a *heh* rather than an *alef*, lest *l'itnasva* be misread as *lo yitnasva* — "she shall not marry" — precisely the opposite of the intended meaning. The *heh* in *le-meihach* must be written with its leg sufficiently separated from the roof that it does not look like a *chet*.

Tosafot is careful to clarify what these requirements actually entail. The *vav* need not be dramatically elongated relative to other letters — it simply must not be so short as to resemble a *yod*. Similarly, the leg of the *heh* need not be made especially prominent; it must simply not be attached to the roof so as to resemble a *chet*. As for whether extreme elongation is required or merely preferred, Tosafot concludes that *vadai ein ha-get pasul im lo he'erikh* — certainly the *get* is not invalid if the letter was not given extra emphasis, as long as it does not actually look like the wrong letter.

This last point leads to a practical ruling. Tosafot records an actual incident: a *get* was brought before Rabbi Eliyahu in which it was unclear whether a particular letter was a *vav* or a *yod*. Rabbi Eliyahu employed the *tinok* test — bringing a child who is neither especially bright nor especially slow-witted to read the word, paralleling the procedure described in *Menachot* regarding letters in *tefillin*. If the child reads it correctly, the *get* is *kasher*; if not, it is *pasul*. This is the same standard applied to *sifrei Torah*, and it can raise real questions in the context of *gittin*.

### Rava Takana

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בָּעֵינַן ״וְדֵן״, אוֹ לָא בָּעֵינַן ״וְדֵן״?
>
> **Translation:** § **A dilemma was raised before them: Do we need** to write: **And this** that you shall have from me is a scroll of divorce, and a letter of leave, and a bill of dismissal to go to marry any man that you wish, **or do we not need** to write the clause beginning with the words: **And this?** Is the *halakha* in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda or the Rabbis?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאַתְקֵין רָבָא בְּגִיטֵּי: ״אֵיךְ פְּלָנְיָא בַּר פְּלָנְיָא, פְּטַר וְתָרֵיךְ יָת פְּלוֹנִיתָא אִינְתְּתֵיהּ, דַּהֲוָת אִינְתְּתֵיהּ מִן קֳדָם דְּנָא – מִיּוֹמָא דְּנַן וּלְעָלַם״. וְאִילּוּ ״וְדֵן״ לָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a solution, **as Rava instituted** the following wording **in bills of divorce:** We saw **how so-and-so, son of so-and-so, dismissed and divorced so-and-so, his wife, who was his wife from beforehand, from this day and forever. But he did not state** the clause beginning with the words: **And this.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, כּוּלְּהוּ מִי קָאָמַר? אֶלָּא בָּעֵינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי בָּעֵינַן;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this solution: **And according to your reasoning, did** Rava **state all** of the other necessary clauses of a bill of divorce? **Rather, we need** to write them even though they were not mentioned explicitly in Rava's formulation. **Here too, we need** to write the clause beginning with the words: And this, even though it was not mentioned specifically by Rava.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִיּוֹמָא דְּנַן״ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דְּאָמַר: זְמַנּוֹ שֶׁל שְׁטָר מוֹכִיחַ עָלָיו;
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara analyzes the wording instituted by Rava: The expression: **From this day,** is written **to the exclusion of** the statement **of Rabbi Yosei, who said: The date** written **in a document proves** when **it** takes effect, and therefore it is not necessary to write the expression: From this day. Rava added this expression to take the opposite opinion into account.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּלְעָלַם״ – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדִּבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רָבָא מֵרַב נַחְמָן – דְּאָמַר: ״הַיּוֹם אִי אַתְּ אִשְׁתִּי וּלְמָחָר אַתְּ אִשְׁתִּי״.
>
> **Translation:** was instituted **to the exclusion of that which Rava asked Rav Naḥman,** with regard to a case **where** the husband **said** to his wife while handing her the bill of divorce: **Today you are not my wife and tomorrow you are my wife.** Does this limitation take effect? Although the conclusion of that inquiry was that once the wife is divorced she is divorced forever, Rava instituted the addition of this expression in the text of the bill of divorce to remove any uncertainty with regard to such a case.

The *Gemara* then asks whether we rule like Rabbi Yehuda — whether the *v'dein* line is in fact required. The *Gemara* notes that Rava, in formulating his *takana* for the standard *nusach* of a *get*, stated: *eich planya bar planya pattar ve-tarich yat plonisa ittati de-havas ittati min kadmat de-na, min yoma den u-le-alam* — so-and-so releases and divorces so-and-so my wife who has been my wife until now, from this day forward — but did not include the *v'dein* line. This would suggest he did not rule like Rabbi Yehuda.

The *Gemara* pushes back: *u-le-ta'ameicha kulhu mi ka'amar?* — by your reasoning, did Rava say everything? That formulation was only one particular addition Rava was establishing; the absence of *v'dein* from his stated formulation cannot serve as proof that he excluded it, any more than the absence of *harei at muteret le-chol adam* means he excluded that. *Ela ba'inan* — we require the other parts as well even though he did not mention them, and *hachi nami ba'inan* — similarly, perhaps we also require *v'dein* and he simply did not mention it. The *Gemara*'s conclusion, given the extraordinary attention to every letter and every word throughout this discussion, is that the *nusach* requires explicit inclusion of *v'dein de-yihavi lichi minai*, ruling in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda. Whether the absence of *v'dein* renders the *get* invalid *bedi'avad* is perhaps less clear, but *lechatchila* the standard demands full explicitness.

What Rava did explicitly insist upon, however, are two additions. First, *miyoma de-nan* — from this day — *la-apoukei mi-d'Rabbi Yosei*, to avoid relying on Rabbi Yosei's position that the date of the document implicitly establishes its effective date, making explicit retroactive conditions unnecessary. Rava required that the effective date be stated unambiguously. Second, *u-le-olam* — and forever — *la-apoukei mi-de-ba'ei minei Rava me-Rav Nachman*, in order to preempt the kind of question Rava once posed to Rav Nachman about whether one could be divorced only for a single day. Even though the *halacha* ultimately holds that such a conditional divorce does not work, Rava wanted the *get* to state explicitly that the divorce is permanent, so that no one could raise the claim that she was only divorced for that day.

## Three Invalid Gittin

### Handwriting No Edim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִשֵּׂאת הַוּוֹלָד כָּשֵׁר –
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Three bills of divorce are invalid** *ab initio*, **but if** the woman **marries** another man on the basis of one of these bills of divorce the lineage of **the offspring** from this marriage **is unflawed.** In other words, she is not considered to be a married woman who engaged in sexual intercourse with another man, which would impair the lineage of their child.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ, וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים; יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים, וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן; יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן, וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד; הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה גִּיטִּין פְּסוּלִין, וְאִם נִשֵּׂאת – הַוָּלָד כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** These three bills are: A bill of divorce that the husband **wrote in his handwriting but has no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document at all, a case where **there are** signatures of **witnesses on** the document **but there is no date** written **on it,** and a case where **there is a date** written **on it, but it contains only one witness. These are** the **three invalid bills of divorce** with regard to which the Sages said: **And if she marries,** the lineage of **the offspring is unflawed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, אֶלָּא שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לָהּ בִּפְנֵי עֵדִים – כָּשֵׁר, וְגוֹבָה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים; שֶׁאֵין הָעֵדִים חוֹתְמִים עַל הַגֵּט אֶלָּא מִפְּנֵי תִּיקּוּן הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: Even though there are no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document, **but he handed it to her in the presence of** two **witnesses,** it **is a valid** bill of divorce. **And** on the basis of this bill of divorce the woman **can collect** the amount written for her in her marriage contract even **from liened property, as** Rabbi Eliezer maintains that **the witnesses sign the bill of divorce only for the betterment of the world.** If no witnesses sign a bill of divorce the husband can contest its validity at any time by denying that he wrote it. Nevertheless, the witnesses' signatures are not an essential part of a bill of divorce.

The *mishna* states: *shelosha gittin pesulim ve-im nisset ha-vlad kasher* — there are three invalid *gittin* such that nevertheless the children are not *mamzerim* if the woman remarried on the basis of them. The *Gemara* will discuss whether this *mishna* goes against Rabbi Meir — who holds that any deviation from the rabbinic standard of *gittin* renders the child a *mamzer* — or whether it represents Rabbi Meir's own concession that these particular cases, while invalid, do not produce a *mamzer*.

This is striking, because some of these defects seem far more serious than defects Rabbi Meir was willing to hold produce a *mamzer* in earlier contexts. Rabbi Meir had ruled that even dating a *get* according to the wrong foreign government or according to the *binyan beit ha-mikdash* renders the child a *mamzer* — extraordinarily exacting standards. Yet here, with defects that seem more substantive, the child will not be a *mamzer*.

The first case: *katav bi-chtav yado ve-ein alav eidim* — it was written in the husband's own handwriting but has no witnesses. Whose position is this? The *mishna* ends by introducing Rabbi Eliezer with his *edei mesira kartei* position, which implies that the beginning of the *mishna* is not Rabbi Eliezer's view. Two possibilities are offered. The first — attributed to Tosafot — is that this reflects Rabbi Meir's view that *edei chasima kartei*, signatures are required; yet the husband's own handwriting constitutes a form of *eidus*, since *hoda'as ba'al din ke-me'ah eidim dami* — the admission of a litigant is equivalent to a hundred witnesses. One's own handwriting is one's own testimony. The second — attributed to Rashi — is a more radical suggestion: perhaps no *eidus* is required in a *get* at all. If written by the husband himself as his own direct expression of divorce, perhaps that suffices without any testimony. Rashi entertains this as a possibility, which is, as noted, quite shocking given that the necessity of *eidus* has been among the most stable assumptions of the entire tractate. The more natural reading, as Tosafot argues, is that one's own handwriting constitutes self-testimony.

### No Date/One Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְתוּ לֵיכָּא?! וְהָא אִיכָּא גֵּט יָשָׁן! הָתָם לֹא תֵּצֵא, הָכָא תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But are there no other** invalid bills of divorce? **But isn't there an outdated bill of divorce,** where the husband and wife were secluded after it was written and before it was handed to her? The Gemara answers that there is a difference between the cases: **There,** in the case of an outdated bill of divorce, if the woman remarries on the basis of that bill of divorce **she** need **not leave** her husband; **here, she must leave** him.

The second case: *yesh alav eidim ve-ein bo zman* — it has witnesses but no date. Dating was critical, as extensively discussed, to establish precisely when the *get* took effect. The third case: *yesh bo zman ve-ein bo ela eid echad* — it has a date but only one witness.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: ״כְּתַב יָדוֹ״ שָׁנִינוּ. אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַרֵישָׁא – פְּשִׁיטָא! ״כְּתַב יָדוֹ״ קָתָנֵי! וְאֶלָּא אַמְּצִיעֲתָא – הֲרֵי יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים! אֶלָּא אַסֵּיפָא: יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד –
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna: A bill of divorce that the husband **wrote in his handwriting, but has no** signatures of **witnesses on it** at all, is invalid; nevertheless, if the woman remarries, the lineage of the child born from her second husband is unflawed. **Rav says: We learned** that this is referring to **his handwriting.** The Gemara asks: **With regard to which** clause of the mishna is this referring? **If we say** that it is referring **to the first clause,** what Rav said **is obvious,** as the case of **his handwriting is** explicitly **taught** in that clause. **And** if it is **rather** referring **to the middle clause,** i.e., an undated bill of divorce, why does it matter whether it was written in the husband's handwriting? **Aren't there** signatures of **witnesses on it? Rather,** it must be referring **to the latter clause** in the mishna, i.e., a bill of divorce that **has a date** written **on it but has only** the signature of **one witness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדַוְקָא כְּתַב יָדוֹ וְעֵד; אֲבָל כְּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav is saying that **specifically** when the bill of divorce is written in **his handwriting and** has the signature of one **witness,** the woman's child from her second husband is of unflawed lineage. **But** if it is written in **a scribe's handwriting,** even if it includes the signature of one **witness,** the child's lineage is **not** unflawed, but rather he is a *mamzer*, as the bill of divorce is completely invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ כְּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד, שֶׁהֲרֵי שָׁנִינוּ: כָּתַב סוֹפֵר וְעֵד – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Even** where it is written in **a scribe's handwriting and** it has the signature of **a witness,** the lineage of the child is unflawed, **as we learned** in a mishna (87b): In the case of a bill of divorce in which **the scribe's handwriting** is recognizable **and** there is one **witness** signed, it is **valid.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב – מִי דָּמֵי?! הָתָם תִּינָּשֵׂא לְכַתְּחִילָּה, הָכָא דִּיעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav** would respond: **Is** that case **comparable? There,** in that case, the mishna indicates that the woman **may marry *ab initio*.** Therefore, it must be a case where the scribe also signed as a second witness. But **here,** in the case of this mishna, the bill of divorce is invalid and the woman may not remarry; it is only if she remarried and had a child that his lineage is rendered unflawed **after the fact.** Therefore, it must be referring to a case where the husband wrote it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל – לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא בְּסָפְרָא דְּמוּבְהַק, וְהָא בְּסָפְרָא דְּלָא מוּבְהַק.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel,** who holds that both cases are referring to a bill of divorce that was written by a scribe but not signed by the scribe as one of the witnesses, would respond that the contradiction is **not difficult. That** mishna, where the bill of divorce is rendered valid *ab initio*, is referring **to** the handwriting of **an expert scribe,** who can presumably be relied upon to have written the bill of divorce properly and only according to the husband's instructions, **and this** mishna is referring **to** the handwriting of **a scribe who is not expert.** Since the bill of divorce was not signed by two witnesses, there is concern that the scribe did not write it properly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״כְּתַב יָדוֹ״ שָׁנִינוּ. אָמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הֲרֵי יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַסֵּיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in accordance with the opinion of Rav: **We learned** that the mishna is referring to **his handwriting. Rabbi Elazar,** assuming that Rabbi Yoḥanan was explaining the middle clause, **said to him: Aren't there** signatures of **witnesses in** the bill of divorce? Why is the husband's handwriting necessary? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** I was referring **to the latter clause,** in which there is only one witness.

Regarding the third case, the *Gemara* presents a debate. Rav holds that *ktav yado shaninu* — what the *mishna* is discussing is the husband's own handwriting combined with one witness, not the scribe's handwriting with one witness. His reasoning: if a *get* is found in the street and a single witness attaches his name to it, one cannot know whether it is genuine. The scribe's handwriting alone carries no testimonial weight about the validity of the *get*. But if the husband himself wrote it in his own hand, that handwriting functions as his own testimony, and adding one witness creates a combination of his testimony and one external witness — sufficient *bedi'avad*.

Shmuel disagrees: *afilu ktav sofer ve-eid* — even the scribe's handwriting combined with one witness is *kasher bedi'avad*, relying on a later *mishna* that validates such a combination. Shmuel understands that later *mishna* as dealing with the scribe's handwriting, and holds that the scribe's writing counts as one form of testimony plus one signature equals two.

Rav responds that the later *mishna* is not discussing *ktav sofer* — the scribe's handwriting — but *chatam sofer* — the scribe's signature alongside one other witness. The proof: that later *mishna* says the woman may remarry *lechatchila*, whereas in our *mishna* she may only do so *bedi'avad*. Clearly they discuss different cases. Rabbi Yochanan agrees with Rav's position that *ktav yadcha shaninu* — it is the husband's handwriting that is at issue in the third case of our *mishna*.

Shmuel responds that there is no contradiction: *ha be-sofer mufhak ha be-sofer delo mufhak* — when the later *mishna* permits remarriage *lechatchila*, it refers to an expert scribe, *sofer mufhak*, whose writing of the *get* carries implicit testimonial weight because an expert scribe would not allow a *get* to leave his hands unless it was going to be properly used. A non-expert scribe might write a *get* for practice and discard it — his handwriting carries no such presumption. The scribe's handwriting combined with one witness therefore suffices *lechatchila* only when the scribe is *mufhak*.

### Rav Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זִימְנִין אָמַר רַב: תֵּצֵא, זִימְנִין אָמַר רַב: לֹא תֵּצֵא. הָא כֵּיצַד? יֵשׁ לָהּ בָּנִים – לֹא תֵּצֵא, אֵין לָהּ בָּנִים – תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the three invalid bills of divorce mentioned in the mishna, **sometimes Rav said** that if the woman remarries based on one of these bills of divorce **she must leave** her second husband, and **sometimes Rav said** that **she** need **not leave.** **How so?** How can the contradiction between Rav's statements be explained? If **she** already **has children** from her second husband then **she** need **not leave** him, as, if she is required to leave him there will be slander that their children are *mamzerim*. But if **she does not have children** from him, **she must leave** him.

Rav's two positions can be reconciled as follows: *zimnin amar Rav tetzei* — sometimes Rav said that if a woman married on the basis of any of the three *gittin* in the *mishna*, she must be divorced — and *zimnin amar Rav lo tetzei* — sometimes he said she does not need to be divorced. The resolution is circumstantial: *yesh la banim lo tetzei* — if she has already had children, she does not need to be divorced, because forcing a divorce would cause people to declare the children *mamzerim*. *Ein la banim tetzei* — if she has not yet had children, she must obtain a proper *get*.

### Tannaitic Proofs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב מָר זוּטְרָא בַּר טוֹבִיָּה: וְכוּלָּן, שֶׁהָיוּ בָּהֶן סְפֵק קִידּוּשִׁין אוֹ סְפֵק גֵּירוּשִׁין – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַבְּמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra bar Toviya raises an objection** to Rav's opinion from a mishna (*Yevamot* 30b). With regard to a woman whose husband died childless [*yevama*] but who is prohibited from marrying her late husband's brother [*yavam*] because he is a forbidden relative to her, not only is she herself exempt from levirate marriage, but she causes any rival wives of hers to be exempt as well, even if they are not forbidden relatives to the brother. The mishna lists fifteen examples of such forbidden relatives (*Yevamot* 2a). **And** if **any of these** fifteen women **had undergone a betrothal or divorce** whose status is **uncertain** with the deceased brother, **those** rival wives **must perform the ritual through which the *yavam* frees the *yevama* of her levirate bonds [*ḥalitza*] and** may **not enter into levirate marriage,** since they are possibly the rival wives of a forbidden relative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד סְפֵק קִידּוּשִׁין? זָרַק לָהּ קִידּוּשִׁין – סָפֵק קָרוֹב לָהּ, סָפֵק קָרוֹב לוֹ, זֶהוּ סְפֵק קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **How** could there be **a betrothal** whose status is **uncertain?** If **he threw her** money or a document as **betrothal** in the public domain, and the item was **possibly closer to her,** whereby she could acquire the object and become betrothed, and **possibly closer to him,** preventing her from acquiring it, **this is** a case of **a betrothal** whose status is **uncertain.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְפֵק גֵּירוּשִׁין – כָּתַב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ וְאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים, יֵשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים וְאֵין בּוֹ זְמַן, יֵשׁ בּוֹ זְמַן וְאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא עֵד אֶחָד – הֲרֵי זֶה סְפֵק גֵּירוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** How can there be **a divorce** whose status is **uncertain?** It is in the cases of invalid bills of divorce mentioned in the mishna here: A bill of divorce that the husband **wrote in his handwriting but has no** signatures of **witnesses on** the document at all, a case where **there are** signatures of **witnesses on** the document **but there is no date** written **on it,** and a case where **there is a date** written **on it but it contains only one witness.** In each of the three cases, **this is** considered **a divorce** whose status is **uncertain.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ לֹא תֵּצֵא, צָרָתָהּ אָתְיָא לְיַבּוֹמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that despite this woman's problematic divorce **she** need **not leave** her second husband, **her rival wife** from her first marriage might **come to enter into levirate marriage** after the first husband's death, assuming that she was already divorced. This would violate the ruling of the mishna in tractate *Yevamot* that she may not do so, as the status of the divorce is uncertain and therefore it is uncertain whether she is the rival wife of a forbidden relative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּתְיַבֵּם, וְאֵין בְּכָךְ כְּלוּם; חֲשָׁשָׁא דְרַבָּנַן הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **She may enter into levirate marriage, and** there is **no** problem **with that,** as the difficulty with the bill of divorce **is** merely **a concern by rabbinic law.** By Torah law it is a complete bill of divorce, and the rival wife is fit for levirate marriage.

A *baraita* is cited as a possible challenge to the position that she does not need to be divorced: all cases involving doubtful betrothal or doubtful divorce — *sefek kiddushin* or *sefek gerushin* — require *chalitzah* but not *yibbum*. The *baraita* specifies that the three *gittin* of our *mishna* constitute *sefek gerushin*. If she does not need to be divorced upon remarriage, the *Gemara* objects, then people will conclude she is fully divorced and a *yavam* may come to perform *yibbum* with her co-wife, thinking the marriage to the deceased brother's daughter was certainly severed. The *Gemara* deflects: *tityabem bein kach u-vein kach* — let him do *yibbum* either way; since *bedi'avad* the divorce is merely a rabbinic problem, the marriage was effectively severed and *yibbum* would be technically permissible. The concern is only a *chashasha de-rabbanan*, a rabbinic precaution.

The *Gemara* also examines whether these three *gittin* should be listed together with other problematic *gittin* such as *get yashan* — an old *get*, written before the couple later cohabited and then given afterwards — or *get kerei'ach* — the bald *get* in which not all of a folded *get*'s folds were signed. Neither fits neatly with the three of our *mishna*. A *get yashan* is not listed because, according to the view that in our *mishna* she must be divorced, the *get yashan* is less weighty — once given, she may even remarry *lechatchila*. A *get kerei'ach* is not listed because there the child is a *mamzer*, whereas here the child is *kasher*. However, the *Gemara* notes a tension: if our *mishna* follows the *Rabbanan*, who never declare a child a *mamzer*, then the distinction from *get kerei'ach* must rest on whether *tetzei* applies. This works only for the opinion that in our *mishna* she does not need to be divorced; if she does need to be divorced here just as in *get kerei'ach*, then there is no basis to omit *get kerei'ach* from the list according to the *Rabbanan*. The *Gemara* is forced to concede: *okimta ke-Rabbi Meir* — one can explain the separate lists only by attributing the *mishna* to Rabbi Meir, who maintains two distinct categories, one in which the *vlad* is a *mamzer* and one in which it is not. By the *Rabbanan*, who never declare a *mamzer*, the lists should be combined, and no fully satisfying answer is given unless one holds that in our *mishna* she does not need to be divorced.

The *mishna* also closes with Rabbi Eliezer's position: even a *get* that bears no witnesses at all is valid if it was given in the presence of witnesses — *edei mesira kartei* — and the woman may even collect from encumbered property on the basis of it, since the witnesses at the moment of delivery confer upon it the full legal weight of a witnessed document.

### Remarriage & Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם לֹא תֵּצֵא. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְעוֹלָם לֹא תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** Contrary to Rav's opinion that a woman who remarries based on one of these bills of divorce must leave her second husband unless they have children, **Levi says** that **she** is **never** required to **leave** him, whether or not they have children. **And similarly, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **she** is **never** required to **leave** him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לִבְנֵי רַבִּי חֲלַפְתָּא דְּמִן הוּנָא, הָכִי אָמַר אֲבוּכוֹן: לְעוֹלָם לֹא תֵּצֵא; וְקַרְצִית שֶׁבֶּעָמִיר אֵינָהּ פּוֹסֶלֶת בְּמֵי חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And similarly, Rabbi Yoḥanan said to the sons of Rabbi Ḥalafta of Huna: Your father said the following** two statements that I heard from him: That **she** is **never** required to **leave** him, **and** that **a *kartzit* in the sheaf does not disqualify water of purification** (see Numbers 19:17–19). Even if a *kartzit* drinks from this water, it may still be sprinkled on a person in order to purify him from ritual impurity imparted by a corpse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קַרְצִית? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דִּידְבְּתָא דְּבֵינֵי כֵיפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is a *kartzit*? Abaye said** that it is a species of **fly [*didveta*] that is** found **between the sheaves.**

Levi states: *l'olam lo tetzei* — she never needs to be divorced, regardless of whether she has had children. Rabbi Yochanan agrees: *l'olam lo tetzei*. Rabbi Yochanan conveyed this also to the sons of Rav Chalafta of Minhuna — "this is what your father said: *l'olam lo tetzei*." Once a woman has remarried on the basis of one of these three *gittin*, she does not need to be divorced, with or without children, *l'olam* meaning even absent children.

The *daf* concludes with a brief appendix conveyed alongside this ruling — Rabbi Yochanan also told the sons of Rav Chalafta another ruling in their father's name: a certain type of locust (*kartzi*) that dwells among bundles of wheat does not invalidate the waters of a *parah adumah* if it drinks from them. The *Gemara* asks what a *kartzi* is, and identifies it as a small insect or flying grasshopper found between grain bundles. A challenge is raised from a *baraita* stating that all birds invalidate *mei chatat* if they drink from them, with the sole exception of the dove, which sucks in and does not spit back. If the *kartzi* were always valid, the *baraita* should have listed it alongside the dove. The *Gemara* resolves: the ruling is not absolute — *gedola lo pasla, ketana pasla* — a large one does not invalidate the water but a small one does. How small must it be to still pose a problem? Until the size of an olive, according to Rav Yirmiya, or according to another version, Rav Ami. This brief digression aside, the substantive *halacha* of the *daf* concludes with the ruling of Levi and Rabbi Yochanan: once a woman has remarried on the basis of any of the three *gittin* of our *mishna*, she does not need to be divorced — *l'olam lo tetzei*.
