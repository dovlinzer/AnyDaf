# Makkot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Escaped Convicts & Sanhedrin Authority

### Escaped Convict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ, וּבָרַח, וּבָא לִפְנֵי אוֹתוֹ בֵּית דִּין – אֵין סוֹתְרִין אֶת דִּינוֹ. כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיַּעַמְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״מְעִידִים אָנוּ בְּאִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֵדָיו״ – הֲרֵי זֶה יֵהָרֵג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One whose verdict was delivered** and he was sentenced to death **and he fled, and he came before the same court** that sentenced him, **they do not overturn his verdict.** Rather, the court administers the previous verdict. Consequently, in **any place where two witnesses will stand and say: We testify with regard to a man** called **so-and-so that his verdict was delivered** and he was sentenced to death **in the court of so-and-so, and so-and-so and so-and-so** were **his witnesses, that person shall be executed** on the basis of that testimony.

The second chapter of *Makkot* opens with a *mishna* concerning a convicted person who escapes and is subsequently apprehended: *mi shenigmar dino uvarach* — one whose *din* has been concluded and who has fled. If he returns and appears before the same *beit din*, *ein sotrin et dino* — the court does not retry his case. The *mishna* continues: wherever two witnesses come forward and declare that such-and-such a person was sentenced in the *beit din* of so-and-so, with these and these as his witnesses, *harei ze ye'hareg* — that person is put to death on the basis of their testimony alone, without any new proceeding.

### Sanhedrin Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַנְהֶדְרִין נוֹהֶגֶת בָּאָרֶץ וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ. סַנְהֶדְרִין הַהוֹרֶגֶת אֶחָד בְּשָׁבוּעַ נִקְרֵאת חוֹבְלָנִית. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה. רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמְרִים: אִילּוּ הָיִינוּ בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין לֹא נֶהֱרַג אָדָם מֵעוֹלָם. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הֵן מַרְבִּין שׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: The mitzva to establish **a Sanhedrin** with the authority to administer capital punishments **is in effect** both **in Eretz Yisrael and outside Eretz** Yisrael. **A Sanhedrin that executes** a transgressor **once in seven years is characterized as a destructive** tribunal. Since the Sanhedrin would subject the testimony to exacting scrutiny, it was extremely rare for a defendant to be executed. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** This categorization applies to a Sanhedrin that executes a transgressor **once in seventy years. Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva say: If we had been** members **of the Sanhedrin,** we would have conducted trials in a manner whereby **no person would have ever been executed. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In adopting that approach, **they too** would **increase** the number of **murderers among the Jewish people.** The death penalty would lose its deterrent value, as all potential murderers would know that no one is ever executed.

The *mishna* then introduces a new topic: *sanhedrin noheget ba'aretz uvachutz la'aretz* — a *sanhedrin* can be established whether in *Eretz Yisrael* or outside of it. Based on the context of the *gemara*, this refers to a *sanhedrin* of twenty-three. The *mishna* also introduces the famous teaching that a *sanhedrin* which executes one person in seven years is called a *chavlanit* — destructive.

### Same vs. Different Court

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לִפְנֵי אוֹתוֹ בֵּית דִּין הוּא דְּאֵין סוֹתְרִין, הָא לִפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין אַחֵר – סוֹתְרִין. הָא תָּנֵי סֵיפָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁיַּעַמְדוּ שְׁנַיִם וְיֹאמְרוּ: ״מְעִידִין אָנוּ אֶת אִישׁ פְּלוֹנִי שֶׁנִּגְמַר דִּינוֹ בְּבֵית דִּין פְּלוֹנִי, וּפְלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי עֵדָיו״ – הֲרֵי זֶה נֶהֱרָג!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara infers: **It is** in the case of one who comes **before the same court that they do not overturn** the verdict, **but if one comes before a different court they overturn** the verdict and retry the case. The Gemara asks: **Isn't it taught in the latter clause** of the mishna: **Any place where two witnesses will stand and say: We testify with regard to a man** called **so-and-so that his verdict was delivered** and he was sentenced to death **in the court of so-and-so, and so-and-so and so-and-so** were **his witnesses, that person shall be executed** on the basis of that testimony? This indicates that the verdict is not overturned and the defendant is not retried even before another court.

The *gemara* opens by inferring from the *reisha* of the *mishna*: it is specifically before the *same* *beit din* that the case is not retried, which implies that before a *different* *beit din* the case would be reopened and the court would seek grounds for acquittal. Yet the *seifa* seems to contradict this, since it states that even when two witnesses appear anywhere and testify to the sentence, the condemned is executed immediately without any retry. How are these two clauses reconciled?

### Escape Across Borders

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, כָּאן בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This apparent contradiction is **not difficult, as here,** in the first clause of the mishna, from which it was inferred that the second court overturns the initial verdict, **in Eretz Yisrael. There,** in the latter clause, which indicates that the second court sustains the initial verdict and does not retry the defendant, **outside Eretz** Yisrael.

*Abaye* resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan b'Eretz Yisrael, kan b'chutz la'aretz* — the *seifa* is speaking of *Eretz Yisrael*, where a different *beit din* would indeed reopen the case, while the *reisha* speaks of outside *Eretz Yisrael*, where even a different *beit din* does not retry the matter. The asymmetry is striking, and it raises a genuine conceptual question: why should the mere fact of escape trigger a renewed search for acquittal? The logic of the institution is not immediately apparent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן דּוֹסְתַּאי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח: בָּרַח מֵאָרֶץ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ – אֵין סוֹתְרִין אֶת דִּינוֹ. מֵחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ לָאָרֶץ – סוֹתְרִין אֶת דִּינוֹ, מִפְּנֵי זְכוּתָהּ שֶׁל אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*, that **Rabbi Yehuda ben Dostai says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Shataḥ:** If a convicted defendant **fled from Eretz Yisrael to outside Eretz** Yisrael **one does not overturn his verdict.** Rather, they implement the initial verdict. But if one fled **from outside Eretz** Yisrael **to Eretz** Yisrael, **one overturns his verdict** and the defendant is retried. Perhaps, **due to the merit of Eretz Yisrael,** the court will discover a reason to exonerate him.

A *braita* is cited in support: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Dostai* says in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Shetach* that if one escapes from *Eretz Yisrael* to *chutz la'aretz*, the case is not retried; but if one escapes from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael*, the case is retried — *mipnei zechutah shel Eretz Yisrael*, because of the merit of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Merit of Eretz Yisrael

*Rashi* explains the concept of *zechutah shel Eretz Yisrael* as follows: *ulai toil limtzo lo petach shel zechut* — perhaps the merit of *Eretz Yisrael* will somehow influence the proceedings and the court will find a basis for acquittal. There is something counterintuitive here: the stronger and more skilled a *dayan* is, the more capable he is of finding grounds for acquittal. This is precisely what *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* claimed — *ilu hayinu b'Sanhedrin, lo neherag adam me'olam* — had they sat on the *sanhedrin*, they would always have found a way to exonerate the accused. And *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* responded: *af hem marbin shofchei damim beYisrael* — by publicly announcing such a policy, they would only be increasing bloodshed in Israel, since the deterrent effect of capital punishment would be eliminated. The parallel concern applies here: allowing a convicted person to escape from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael* and obtain a new hearing creates an obvious incentive for flight.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַנְהֶדְרִין נוֹהֶגֶת כּוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהָיוּ אֵלֶּה לָכֶם לְחֻקַּת מִשְׁפָּט לְדֹרֹתֵיכֶם״, לָמַדְנוּ לְסַנְהֶדְרִין שֶׁנּוֹהֶגֶת בָּאָרֶץ וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: The mitzva to establish **a Sanhedrin** with the authority to administer capital punishments **is in effect** both in Eretz Yisrael and outside Eretz Yisrael. The Gemara clarifies: **From where are these matters** derived? It is **as the Sages taught:** It is written with regard to the sentencing of murderers: **"And these shall be for you as a statute of justice for your generations in all your dwelling places"** (Numbers 35:29), from which **we learn that** the mitzva to establish **a Sanhedrin is in effect** both **in Eretz Yisrael and outside Eretz** Yisrael.

The *gemara* then turns to the derivation that a *sanhedrin* of twenty-three operates both in *Eretz Yisrael* and outside it. The *tanna'im* cite the verse *v'hayu eleh lachem l'chukat mishpat l'doroteichem* — these shall be for you a statute of judgment for your generations. The verse continues with *b'chol moshvoteichem* — in all of your dwellings — which extends the obligation to establish courts beyond *Eretz Yisrael* alone.

### Court Jurisdiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ״? בִּשְׁעָרֶיךָ אַתָּה מוֹשִׁיב בָּתֵּי דִינִים בְּכׇל פֶּלֶךְ וָפֶלֶךְ וּבְכׇל עִיר וָעִיר, וּבְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ אַתָּה מוֹשִׁיב בְּכׇל פֶּלֶךְ וָפֶלֶךְ, וְאִי אַתָּה מוֹשִׁיב בְּכׇל עִיר וָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **If so, what** is the meaning when **the verse states:** "Judges and officers you shall appoint **in all your gates"** (Deuteronomy 16:18), which indicates that the mitzva to establish a Sanhedrin is in effect where the gates are yours, i.e., only in Eretz Yisrael? The explanation is as follows: **In your gates,** in Eretz Yisrael, **you establish courts in each and every district and in each and every city, and outside Eretz Yisrael you establish** courts **in each and every district, but you do not establish** courts **in each and every city.** The requirement to establish courts in every city is only in Eretz Yisrael.

If so, the *gemara* asks, why does the *pasuk* also use the word *b'sharecha* — in your gates — which ordinarily refers specifically to the gates of cities in *Eretz Yisrael*? The answer is that the two formulations do different work: *b'sharecha atah moshiv batei dinim b'chol pelch u'pelch u'v'chol ir va'ir* — in *Eretz Yisrael* you establish courts in every district and in every city, whereas *u'v'chutz la'aretz atah moshiv b'chol pelch u'pelch ve'i atah moshiv b'chol ir va'ir* — outside of *Eretz Yisrael* you establish a *beit din* in every province but not in every individual city. The *sanhedrin* presumably defines what constitutes a *pelch*, and on that basis determines where courts are to be set up.

## Destructive Sanhedri (II)

### One Per Seven Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַנְהֶדְרִין הַהוֹרֶגֶת אֶחָד בְּשָׁבוּעַ נִקְרֵאת חוֹבְלָנִית.
>
> **Translation:** **A Sanhedrin that executes** a transgressor **once in seven years is characterized as a destructive** tribunal.

The *mishna* teaches: *sanhedrin hahoreget echad be'shavua nikret chavlanit* — a *sanhedrin* that executes one person in seven years is called destructive.

### Seventy-Year Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** This categorization applies to a Sanhedrin that executes a transgressor **once in seventy years.**

*Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah* then offers an alternative: one execution every seventy years rises to the level of being called *chavlanit*.

### Unresolved Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: אַחַת לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה נִקְרֵאת חַבְּלָנִית, אוֹ דִלְמָא אוֹרַח אַרְעָא הִיא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya saying that a Sanhedrin that executes **once in seventy,** rather than seven, **years is characterized as a destructive** tribunal? **Or perhaps** he is saying that **standard conduct is** for a Sanhedrin to execute once in seventy years, and only if it executes more than one person during that period is it characterized as destructive? The Gemara concludes: The dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* records the question: when *Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah* says one in seventy years, does he mean that even that rate is called *chavlanit*, or does he mean that one per seventy years is *orach ar'a* — the proper and reasonable standard? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teku*.

### Tarfon & Akiva View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמְרִים: אִילּוּ הָיִינוּ בְּסַנְהֶדְרִין לֹא נֶהֱרַג אָדָם מֵעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva say: If we had been** members **of the Sanhedrin,** we would have conducted trials in a manner whereby **no person would have ever been executed.**

*Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* declare: *ilu hayinu b'Sanhedrin, lo neherag adam me'olam* — had we sat on the *sanhedrin*, we would never have allowed anyone to be executed.

## Capital Conviction I (II)

### Witness Interrogation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי הֲווֹ עָבְדִי? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רְאִיתֶם טְרֵיפָה הָרַג, שָׁלֵם הָרַג?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **How would they have acted** to spare the accused from execution if witnesses testified that he intentionally committed murder? **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar both say** that they would have asked the witnesses: **Did you see** whether the accused **killed a *tereifa*,** i.e., a person with a condition that would lead to his death within twelve months, **or if he killed** someone who was **intact?** The halakhic status of a *tereifa* is like that of one who is dead, in the sense that one who kills him is not executed. Since no witness can be certain with regard to the victim's physical condition, they would invalidate any testimony to a murder.

The *gemara* immediately asks: *heichi havu avdei* — how would they have actually achieved this? This is not merely a moral position; it is a question about procedure. *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Elazar* both answer that they would interrogate the witnesses about the victim's physical status.

### Tereifah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר שָׁלֵם הֲוָה, דִּלְמָא בִּמְקוֹם סַיִיף נֶקֶב הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** Even **if you say** that they examined him postmortem and **he was intact** the testimony could be challenged, as **perhaps in the place** that the **sword** pierced the victim's body **there was a perforation** in one of the organs that renders the person a *tereifa*, but which was rendered undetectable by the wound caused by the sword.

In a case of murder, the judges would ask the witnesses: *re'item terefah* — did you see that the victim was a *terefah*, someone with a mortal internal condition who is in any case destined to die imminently? *Horeg shalem horeg* — or did you see that the person was whole and healthy? *Rav Ashi* adds a further complication: even if the witnesses claim the victim was *shalem* — complete and uninjured prior to the killing — one might still ask whether the precise location where the sword struck happened to coincide with an existing *terefah* wound, leaving the matter unresolved.

## Destructive Sanhedri (II)

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אַף הֵן מַרְבִּין שׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In adopting that approach, **they too** would **increase** the number of **murderers among the Jewish people.** The death penalty would lose its deterrent value, as all potential murderers would know that no one is ever executed.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* responds to the position of *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva*: *af hem marbin shofchei damim beYisrael* — by pursuing a policy of never convicting anyone, they would effectively increase bloodshed in Israel, since the absence of any credible threat of capital punishment removes the deterrent entirely.

## Capital Conviction I (II)

### Shechita Comparison

*Tosafot* raise a pointed question: if we are truly concerned that a victim might have been a *terefah* before the killing, why do we not raise the same concern in the context of *shechita*? When one slaughters an animal, we do not require the *shochet* to demonstrate that the animal was not already a *terefah* prior to slaughter. Both situations involve a *chazaka* that a living creature is presumed not to be a *terefah*. If that *chazaka* is sufficient for *shechita*, why is it not sufficient in capital cases to establish that the victim was *shalem*? Clearly the concern cannot be absolute, because if it were, the *Torah* could never sanction an execution — which would effectively nullify the entire institution of capital punishment, much as the Talmud elsewhere discusses with *ben sorer u'moreh* and *ir ha-nidachat*.

### Unknown Answer Disqualification

*Rabbeinu Tam* addresses the issue differently. He explains that the strategy of *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* did not rest on the *terefah* question per se. Rather, the method was *she-marbe be-vedikot* — to multiply questions put to the witnesses until eventually one witness contradicts the other, producing a *hakchashah* that disqualifies their testimony entirely. The key principle *Rabbeinu Tam* articulates is this: if the witnesses are asked a question and respond *ein anu yodim* — we don't know — this response itself disqualifies them. Witnesses who cannot provide complete answers to the court's examination may become *pesulim*, and the *edut* collapses. This is why the phrase *lo neherag adam me'olam* is *lav davka* — not strictly literal. Most of the time, *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* would succeed in tripping up the witnesses through extensive questioning; occasionally they would not. It follows, as *Tosafot* make clear, that this is not a claim to literally abolish the death penalty, but rather a description of a vigorous commitment to evidentiary scrutiny.

### Sexual Relations Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבוֹעֵל אֶת הָעֶרְוָה הֵיכִי הֲווֹ עָבְדִי? אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: רְאִיתֶם כְּמִכְחוֹל בִּשְׁפוֹפֶרֶת? וְרַבָּנַן, הֵיכִי דָּיְינוּ? כִּשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בִּמְנָאֲפִים – מִשֶּׁיִּרְאוּ כִּמְנָאֲפִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **With regard to one who engages in intercourse with a forbidden relative, how would they have acted** to spare the accused from execution? **Abaye and Rava both say** that they would have asked the witnesses: **Did you see** the intercourse, **like a brush entering into a tube?** Since witnesses rarely witness the act that closely, one could claim that the testimony is incomplete. The Gemara asks: **And concerning the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Tarfon and Rabbi Akiva, **how would they** have **adjudicated** that case? The Gemara answers: They hold **in accordance with** the statement of **Shmuel, as Shmuel says: In cases involving** adulterers one can testify and convict them **from when they will appear as adulterers,** without any need for him to witness the act in graphic detail.

The *gemara* turns to a different category of capital crime: *boel et ha-erva* — one who has illicit sexual relations. *Heichi avdei* — how would *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* have handled this case? *Abaye* and *Rava* both state that the witnesses would be asked: *re'item k'michul ba-shfoferet* — did you see the actual act of penetration, like a brush entering a tube? This is the standard metaphor used throughout the Talmud for the act of intercourse. If the witnesses could not give such direct testimony, there would be no conviction.

### Sufficient Appearance

The *rabbanan*, however, hold a different standard. The *gemara* cites *Shmuel* who says: *bimna'afim, mi-she-yiru k'mna'afim* — in cases of illicit relations, once witnesses see the parties behaving in the manner of those engaged in the act, that is sufficient. The question this raises is significant: if the *rabbanan* do not require actual testimony of penetration, how can they convict? The answer is that *Shmuel*'s principle allows conviction on the basis of contextual observation, without requiring the witnesses to testify to the act itself. The *Rambam* rules in accordance with the *rabbanan*, and the *Shulchan Aruch* follows the *Rambam* on this point.

## Accidental Homicide

### Exile Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵלּוּ הֵן הַגּוֹלִין: הַהוֹרֵג נֶפֶשׁ בִּשְׁגָגָה, הָיָה מְעַגֵּל בַּמַּעְגֵּילָה וְנָפְלָה עָלָיו וַהֲרָגַתּוּ, הָיָה מְשַׁלְשֵׁל בֶּחָבִית וְנָפְלָה עָלָיו וַהֲרָגַתּוּ, הָיָה יוֹרֵד בַּסּוּלָּם וְנָפַל עָלָיו (וַהֲרָגַתּוּ) [וַהֲרָגוֹ] – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה. אֲבָל אִם הָיָה מוֹשֵׁךְ בַּמַּעְגֵּילָה וְנָפְלָה עָלָיו וַהֲרָגַתּוּ, הָיָה דּוֹלֶה בֶּחָבִית וְנִפְסַק הַחֶבֶל וְנָפְלָה עָלָיו וַהֲרָגַתּוּ
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **These are** the people **who are exiled:** Anyone **who kills a person unintentionally.** Whether one is liable to be exiled depends on the particular circumstances of the case: If **one was rolling a roller** to smooth the covering of mortar that he applied to seal his roof **and** the roller **fell upon** a person **and killed him,** or if **one was lowering a barrel from the roof and it fell on** a person **and killed him,** or if he **was descending a ladder and he fell on** a person **and killed him,** in all of these cases, **he is exiled. But if one was pulling a roller toward him and it fell from his hands upon** a person **and killed him,** or if **one was lifting a barrel and the rope was severed and it fell upon** a person **and killed him,**

With the conclusion of the first chapter, the *gemara* begins the second: *elu hen ha-golin* — these are those who go into exile. The Torah provides for *arei miklat*, sanctuary cities, to which a person who accidentally kills another must flee. There he is protected from the *go'el ha-dam*, the blood avenger from the victim's family, who is otherwise permitted to kill the manslayer. The chapter enumerates who is and who is not included in this obligation.

### Downward Motion Cases

If one was rolling a log along a roof — *Rashi* explains this was a common method of sealing rooftops with pitch — and the log fell and killed someone below, that person is exiled. Similarly, if one was lowering a barrel — *meshalshel be-chavit* — and it fell and killed someone. And if one was descending a ladder and fell onto someone and killed them, *harei zeh goleh* — that person is exiled.

### Upward Motion Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּסּוּלָּם וְנָפַל עָלָיו וַהֲרָגוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁבְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ – גּוֹלֶה, וְשֶׁלֹּא בְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** or if **one was climbing a ladder and he fell upon** a person **and killed him, that unintentional murderer is not exiled. This is the principle: Any** murderer **who** kills unintentionally **through his downward** motion **is exiled, and one who** kills **not through his downward** motion **is not exiled.**

By contrast, the *mishna* lists cases that are excluded from exile: if one was pulling the log upward and it fell and killed someone, or one was raising the barrel and the rope broke, or one was ascending a ladder and fell and killed someone — in all these cases, *harei zeh eino goleh* — that person is not exiled.

### General Rule

The *mishna* then articulates the governing principle: *kol she-be-derech yeridato goleh, ve-shelo be-derech yeridato eino goleh* — any case in which the harmful event occurs through a downward motion incurs exile; any case in which it does not occur through a downward motion does not incur exile.

### Negligence vs. Exile

This raises an important conceptual distinction. In several of the excluded cases — for instance, pulling a barrel upward when the rope snaps — the person may well be negligent and could be liable to pay damages. The exclusion from *galut* does not necessarily mean full exemption from legal responsibility; it means specifically that the obligation to go to an *ir miklat* does not apply. The *gemara* will make clear that in some excluded cases liability for negligence remains, while in others the *gemara* excludes the individual altogether. There appear to be two dimensions to *ir miklat*: protection from the blood avenger, and a kind of existential atonement for having caused a death, however unintentionally. Both dimensions are operative in the institution, which makes the precise contours of the *derech yeridato* rule all the more significant.

### Biblical Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיַּפֵּל עָלָיו וַיָּמֹת״ – עַד שֶׁיִּפּוֹל דֶּרֶךְ נְפִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks with regard to the principle that one is exiled only if he killed unintentionally through a downward motion: **From where are these matters** derived? It is derived from a verse, **as Shmuel says that the verse states** with regard to those exiled to a city of refuge: **"And he cast it down upon him and dies"** (Numbers 35:23), indicating that one is not liable to be exiled **unless** the item **falls in a downward** motion.

*Shmuel* derives the entire principle from the verse *va-yipol alav va-yamot* — and it fell upon him and killed him — implying that exile is incurred only when the killing occurs through the natural manner of falling, which is a downward motion.

## Excluded Categories

### Intentional Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בִּשְׁגָגָה״ – פְּרָט לְמֵזִיד. ״בִּבְלִי דַּעַת״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* derivations from verses written with regard to the unintentional murderer: **"Unintentionally"** (Numbers 35:11); to **exclude** from exile **the one who kills intentionally. "Unawares"** (Deuteronomy 19:4); to **exclude** from exile **the one who kills with intent.**

The *braita* proceeds to expound the relevant *pesukim* term by term. The word *bishgaga* — unintentionally — comes to exclude the *mezid*, someone who kills deliberately. This person is not exiled because he is *bar katla* — subject to execution, not exile.

### Omer Mutar Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵזִיד? פְּשִׁיטָא, בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אֵימָא פְּרָט לְאוֹמֵר מוּתָּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִי אוֹמֵר מוּתָּר, אָנוּס הוּא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר, הָאוֹמֵר מוּתָּר – קָרוֹב לְמֵזִיד הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is a derivation necessary to exclude one who kills **intentionally?** It is **obvious** that he is not exiled; **he is subject to** the **death** penalty. **Rather, Rava said: Say** that the type of intentional killer referred to is meant to **exclude the one who says that it is permitted to kill** the victim. The verse teaches that this person is neither executed nor is he exiled. **Abaye said to Rava: If the reference is to one who says that it is permitted, he is an** *oness* **— a victim of circumstances beyond his control, as he did not know any better. How could that be characterized as intentional?** Rava **said to him:** That is not a problem, **as I say that with regard to one who says that it is permitted,** his action **borders on the intentional.**

The *gemara* notes that excluding the *mezid* on the basis of *bishgaga* seems obvious — of course an intentional killer is not sent to an *ir miklat*. *Rava* therefore proposes a more refined reading: *bishgaga* comes to exclude the *omer mutar* — one who believes that killing is permitted and kills on that basis. Such a person is not simply a *mezid* in the usual sense, yet *Rava* argues he is *karov le-mezid* — close to being intentional — and therefore the *braita*'s exclusion applies to him.

*Abaye* pushes back: if someone genuinely believes killing is permitted, he is an *oness* — acting under a form of compulsion arising from mistaken belief — and ought not be labeled a *mezid* at all. *Rava* responds that while the *omer mutar* is not a full *mezid*, his proximity to intentionality is sufficient for the *braita* to treat him as falling outside the category of *shogeg* entitled to exile. Importantly, *karov le-mezid* is not identical to *mezid*: such a person would not be executed, but neither would he receive the protection of *galut*. How one could genuinely believe that murder is permitted is indeed difficult to conceive; the case is perhaps most analogous to one who mistakenly believes his victim is a *rodef*, though that remains a distinct *halachic* category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בִּבְלִי דַעַת״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין. מִתְכַּוֵּין? פְּשִׁיטָא, בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! אָמַר רַבָּה: פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין לַהֲרוֹג אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה וְהָרַג אֶת הָאָדָם, לַגּוֹי וְהָרַג אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, לַנֵּפֶל וְהָרַג בֶּן קַיָּימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **"Unawares"; to exclude** from exile **the one who kills with intent.** The Gemara asks: **With intent?** It is **obvious** that he is not exiled; **he is subject to** the **death** penalty. **Rabba said:** The reference is to **exclude the one who acted with the intent to kill an animal and he killed a person** inadvertently, **or one who acted with the intent to** kill **a gentile and he killed a Jew,** or **one who acted with the intent to kill a non-viable newborn and he killed a viable** newborn.

A parallel analysis applies to the phrase *bivli da'at* — without knowledge — which excludes one who *intended* to kill. That exclusion, too, seems obvious since an intentional killer is *chayav mita*. *Rabba* therefore reinterprets it as excluding one who intended to kill an animal and accidentally killed a person, or intended to kill a *kuti* and killed a *Yisrael*, or intended to kill a child who was not halachically viable and killed a viable one. We know from elsewhere that *ha-mitkaven la-harog zeh ve-harag zeh patur* — one who intends to kill one person and kills another is exempt from execution. One might nonetheless have thought such a person incurs *galut* — the *braita* tells us he does not. Notably, the *braita* does not address the case where one intends to kill one *Yisrael* and kills a different *Yisrael* — leaving open the possibility that in that case, *galut* does apply.

### Corner Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אִם בְּפֶתַע״ – פְּרָט לְקֶרֶן זָוִית. ״בְּלֹא אֵיבָה״ – פְּרָט לְשׂוֹנֵא. ״הֲדָפוֹ״ – שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ. ״אוֹ הִשְׁלִיךְ עָלָיו״ – לְהָבִיא יְרִידָה שֶׁהִיא צוֹרֶךְ עֲלִיָּה. ״בְּלֹא צְדִיָּה״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין לְצַד זֶה וְהָלְכָה לָהּ לְצַד אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* based on the verse written with regard to an unintentional murderer: "And if suddenly, without enmity, he thrust him or cast upon him any vessel without lying in wait" (Numbers 35:22). **"If suddenly"; this serves to exclude** one who unintentionally kills another that he encounters at **a corner. "Without enmity"; this serves to exclude** one who unintentionally kills his **enemy,** as even if the act appears unintentional, the presumption is that it was not. **"He thrust him"; this indicates that even if he unintentionally shoved him with his body** and killed him, **he is liable to be exiled. "Or cast upon him"; this serves to include** the case of **a downward motion that is for the purpose of an upward** motion, e.g., if one bent down in order to lift an item from the ground, and in the process of bending down he killed another unintentionally, he is exiled. **"Without lying in wait"; this serves to exclude** one who **had the intent to throw a stone to this side and it went to a different side** and killed a person.

The *pasuk* uses the word *be-feta* — suddenly — and the *braita* derives from this that the two parties must be in close proximity to one another. *Befeta* implies immediacy and closeness. This comes to exclude the case of *keren zavit* — a corner. *Rashi* explains that *leshon feta hu be-samuch* — the language of *feta* implies the two are near one another, *betakif*, immediately accessible. The case: one person is walking out of one alleyway and entering another, turning left or right, with a knife in hand — and another person comes around the corner and is accidentally stabbed. In this case there is no *galut*, because the parties were not in a position to see and be seen by one another; the word *feta* implies proximity of awareness, not merely physical proximity at the moment of impact.

This is admittedly counterintuitive. Walking through a public space with a knife outstretched represents a high degree of negligence; the case of two people rounding a corner and colliding might seem more like a genuine accident. Yet the *halacha* holds that the corner case is excluded from *galut* — though liability for damages may still apply — while the straight-line encounter could incur exile. The *derech yeridato* principle, combined with the *befeta* requirement, generates results that are not always intuitively obvious.

### Enmity Exclusion

The phrase *be-lo eivah* — without prior hatred — excludes the *soneh*, someone who harbored enmity toward the victim. If a person who hates someone else "accidentally" kills that person, the preexisting hatred disqualifies the act from being classified as pure *shgaga*, and the killer does not receive the protection of *galut*.

### Intent Mismatch

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַאֲשֶׁר לֹא צָדָה״ – פְּרָט לְמִתְכַּוֵּין לִזְרוֹק שְׁתַּיִם, וְזָרַק אַרְבַּע. ״וַאֲשֶׁר יָבֹא אֶת רֵעֵהוּ בַיַּעַר״ – מָה יַעַר רְשׁוּת לַנִּיזָּק וְלַמַּזִּיק לִיכָּנֵס לְשָׁם, אַף כֹּל רְשׁוּת לַנִּיזָּק וְלַמַּזִּיק לִיכָּנֵס לְשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** And it is written with regard to an unintentional murderer: **"And who did not lie in wait"** (Exodus 21:13); this serves to exclude one who **had intent to throw a stone two cubits and he inadvertently threw it four** cubits and killed a person. And it is written concerning an unintentional murderer: **"And as one who goes with his neighbor into the forest"** (Deuteronomy 19:5), from which it is derived: **Just as a forest** is ownerless property, and there is **permission for the victim and for the assailant to enter there, so too,** unintentional murder that occurs in **any place where there is permission for the victim and for the assailant to enter there,** is punishable with exile. The unintentional murder of one who entered the assailant's property without his permission is not cause for the murderer to be exiled.

The *braita* then addresses a case involving a mismatch between intent and result by way of *hadafo* — pushing — establishing that the act must be *bishgaga*, without deliberate intent, and the harmful motion must be a downward one. The *braita* includes in the category of *chayav galut* a downward motion that is *tzorech aliyah* — functionally necessary to an upward motion. One might have thought that because the downward swing is merely a means to an upward motion, it should be classified as *aliyah* and thus exempt from *galut*. The *braita* teaches otherwise: *lehavi yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — this case is included.

### Wrong Target/Distance

The phrase *be-lo tzediyah* — without premeditation — comes to exclude two specific situations: one who intends to hit one side of a person and actually strikes another side, and one who intends to throw something two *amot* and actually throws it four. In both cases, the person is not *chayav galut*. Whether this is because the degree of negligence is too low or too high relative to the *galut* threshold is a matter of judgment; in either case, the person likely retains liability for damages.

### Mutual Access

Finally, the verse *ve-asher yavo et re'eihu ba-ya'ar* — and he goes with his fellow into the forest — generates a general principle: *mah ya'ar reshut le-nizak ule-mazik lehikaneis lesham* — just as in a forest both the damaged and the damager have permission to enter, so too *galut* liability requires that both parties had equal right of access to the location where the accident occurred.

## Ladder Case

### Rung Falling Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָיָה עוֹלֶה בַּסּוּלָּם וְנִשְׁמְטָה שְׁלִיבָה מִתַּחְתָּיו, וְנָפְלָה וְהָרְגָה, מַהוּ? כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא עֲלִיָּה הִיא, אוֹ יְרִידָה הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a related discussion. **Rabbi Abbahu raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If **one was ascending a ladder and the rung was displaced from beneath him, and the rung fell and killed** another, **what is the** *halakha* **with regard to his being exiled?** **In a case like this, is it considered killing in an upward** motion, as he was climbing the ladder, **and therefore he is exempt, or is it considered killing in a downward** motion, as when he stepped on the rung he pushed it down, **and therefore he is liable?**

*Rabbi Abbahu* poses a question to *Rabbi Yochanan*: *haya oleh be-sulam ve-nishmeta ha-shleiva mitachtav ve-naflah ve-hargah mahu* — if one is climbing up a ladder and, as he pushes down on a rung, the rung is dislodged and falls and kills someone, what is the law? Is this considered a downward motion — *yeridah* — triggering *galut*, or an upward motion — *aliyah* — exempting him?

### Downward for Upward

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּבָר נָגַעְתָּ בִּירִידָה שֶׁהִיא צוֹרֶךְ עֲלִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** In the scenario you described, **you already touched upon** the case in the *baraita* cited above, the case of unintentional murder that is performed with **a downward motion that is for the purpose of an upward** motion. The ruling in the *baraita* is that one is liable to be exiled in that case.

*Rabbi Yochanan* replies: *kevar nagata bi-yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — you have already touched upon this in the *braita*. The *braita* explicitly stated that a downward motion which is necessary for the purpose of going up — *yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — is included in the category of *chayav galut*. The act of pushing down on the rung in order to propel oneself upward is precisely this: a downward motion performed in service of ascent. It is therefore *chayav galut*.

### Mishna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁבְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ – גּוֹלֶה, שֶׁלֹּא בְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ – אֵינוֹ גּוֹלֶה. שֶׁלֹּא בְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי? לָאו לְאֵיתוֹיֵי כְּהַאי גַוְונָא? וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, כָּל שֶׁבְּדֶרֶךְ יְרִידָתוֹ, לְאֵיתוֹיֵי מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **raised an objection to** the explanation of Rabbi Yoḥanan from the mishna, which states: **This is the principle: Any** murderer **who kills** unintentionally **through his downward motion is exiled, and one who** kills **not through his downward** motion **is not exiled.** Rabbi Abbahu clarifies: With regard to the phrase in the mishna: One **who** kills **not through his downward** motion, **what case that was not already specified does this serve to add?** **Is it not to add a case like this,** and to teach that even though the death was caused by the falling rung, since the assailant was ascending the ladder at the time, he is not exiled? Rabbi Yoḥanan replied: **And according to your reasoning,** that this apparently extraneous phrase serves to include a case that is not addressed explicitly, with regard to the previous phrase in the mishna: **Any** murderer **who** kills unintentionally **through his downward** motion, **what case that was not already specified does this serve to add?**

An objection is raised from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* formulates the general rule as *kol she-be-derech yeridato goleh, ve-shelo be-derech yeridato eino goleh*. What is the phrase *she-lo be-derech yeridato* coming to include? The challenger suggests it comes to include precisely the ladder case — i.e., climbing up and having a rung fall — as an instance of something that is *not* in the way of descent and is therefore *eino goleh*. If so, the *mishna* itself contradicts *Rabbi Yochanan*'s answer.

### Butcher Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קַצָּב, הָכָא נָמֵי לְאֵיתוֹיֵי קַצָּב. דְּתַנְיָא: קַצָּב שֶׁהָיָה מְקַצֵּב, תָּנָא חֲדָא: לְפָנָיו – חַיָּיב, לְאַחֲרָיו – פָּטוּר. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: לְאַחֲרָיו – חַיָּיב, לְפָנָיו – פָּטוּר. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בֵּין לְפָנָיו בֵּין לְאַחֲרָיו – חַיָּיב, וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: בֵּין לְפָנָיו בֵּין לְאַחֲרָיו – פָּטוּר. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one may say that it serves **to add** the case of **a butcher. Here too,** in the latter clause, the phrase: One who kills **not through his downward** motion, serves **to add** the case of **a butcher, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a butcher who was chopping** animal limbs with a cleaver, **one** Sage **taught** that if he killed a person **in front of him,** he is **liable** to be exiled; if he killed a person **behind him,** he is **exempt. And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If he killed a person **behind him,** he is **liable;** if he killed a person **in front of him,** he is **exempt. And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: Both** if he killed a person **in front of him and** if he killed a person **behind him,** he is **liable. And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **Both** if he killed a person **in front of him and** if he killed a person **behind him,** he is **exempt. And** although these *baraitot* appear contradictory, the apparent contradiction is **not difficult.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* turns the argument around: *ule-ta'amach*, according to your reasoning, what does the positive clause — *kol she-be-derech yeridato* — come to include? You need both clauses to be adding something. *Rabbi Yochanan* resolves this by reading both clauses of the *mishna*'s general rule as referring to the case of a *katzav* — a butcher chopping meat.

### Four Butcher Cases

A series of four *braitot* addresses the butcher case. The first teaches: *lefanav chayav, le-achorav patur* — liable if the blade falls in front of him, exempt if it falls behind him. The second teaches the reverse. The third holds liable in both directions; the fourth exempts in both. The apparent contradictions among these four *braitot* are resolved by attention to the direction of the butcher's arm motion at the precise moment the blade is released.

### Motion Direction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּאן – בִּירִידָה שֶׁלְּפָנָיו, וַעֲלִיָּה שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו. כָּאן – בַּעֲלִיָּה שֶׁלְּפָנָיו, וִירִידָה שֶׁלְּאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** When a butcher is about to cut the meat of an animal, he raises the cleaver and lowers it behind him in order to create momentum that will generate power. Then he raises the cleaver from behind him and brings it down forcefully onto the meat. In that process, the butcher lowers the cleaver in front of him and behind him and raises the cleaver both in front of him and behind him. Each of the four *baraitot* addresses a different stage of the process. **Here,** the *baraita* that says he is liable if he unintentionally murders a person in front of him and exempt if he unintentionally murders a person behind him, is referring to a case where the butcher swings the cleaver **with a downward motion in front of him and an upward** motion **behind him. There,** the *baraita* that says he is exempt if he unintentionally murders a person in front of him and liable if he unintentionally murders a person behind him, is referring to a case where the butcher swings the cleaver **with an upward motion in front of him and a downward** motion **behind him.**

The resolution: whenever the *braita* says *chayav lefanav*, it refers to a downward motion in front — *yeridah she-lefanav*; whenever it says *patur le-achorav*, it refers to an upward motion behind — *aliyah she-le-achorav*. Systematically, upward motions result in *patur*, downward motions in *chayav*. There are four possible combinations — down-front, down-back, up-front, up-back — and the four *braitot* address them in differing combinations, all consistent with the overarching principle of *derech yeridato*.

### Tannaitic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּאן – בִּירִידָה שֶׁלְּפָנָיו וְשֶׁל אַחֲרָיו, כָּאן – בַּעֲלִיָּה שֶׁלְּפָנָיו וְשֶׁל אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, **here,** the *baraita* where he is liable in both instances, is referring to a case where the butcher swings the cleaver with a downward motion both in front of him and behind him. There, the *baraita* where he is exempt in both instances, is referring to a case where the butcher swings the cleaver with an upward motion both in front of him and behind him. The two phrases that constitute the principle in the mishna teach that the determining factor is whether the motion is upward or downward, not whether it is in front of him or behind him. This is Rabbi Yoḥanan's reply to the objection of Rabbi Abbahu.

The *gemara* then raises the possibility: *leima katanai hi* — perhaps the dispute between *Rabbi Abbahu* and *Rabbi Yochanan* reflects an underlying *tannaitic* dispute. The very case they debated — one who is climbing up a ladder and a rung falls — appears in two conflicting *braitot*, one ruling *chayav* and one ruling *patur*. Perhaps one *tanna* holds it is *yeridah* and the other holds it is *aliyah*.

### Two Liability Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא עֲלִיָּה הִיא. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לִנְיזָקִין, כָּאן לְגָלוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects that parallel: **No,** that is not the point of contention, **as everyone agrees** that **it is an upward** motion, **and** there is no dispute between the Sages in the *baraitot*. Nevertheless, the apparent contradiction between them is **not difficult, as here,** in the *baraita* that rules him liable, the reference is **to** payment of **damages, as with regard to one's liability to pay damages, there is no difference between upward and downward motions, or between intentional and unwitting damage; and there,** in the *baraita* that rules him exempt, the reference is **to exile,** from which one who murders unintentionally in an upward motion is exempt by Torah edict.

The *gemara* rejects this interpretation: *lo, de-chulei alma aliyah hi, ve-lo kashya — kan le-nizkin, kan le-galut*. Both *tanna'im* may agree that the motion is fundamentally *aliyah* — ascent — and there is no contradiction, because the two *braitot* are addressing different legal frameworks: one speaks of liability in *nezikin*, where one may be *chayav* to pay damages even for an upward motion involving negligence; the other addresses *galut*, where the *aliyah* classification exempts one from exile.

### Rung Sagging

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא הָא וְהָא לְגָלוּת, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאַתְלַיע, הָא דְּלָא אַתְלַע.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** instead that in both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* the reference is **to exile, and** it is **not difficult, as this** *baraita*, where the ruling is that he is liable, is referring to a case **where** the rung of the ladder **was worm infested** and he should have been cautious before stepping on it, **and that** *baraita*, where the ruling is that he is exempt, is referring to a case **where** the rung **was not worm infested,** as in that case it is murder due to circumstances beyond his control, as he could not have anticipated that the rung would be displaced.

Alternatively, both *braitot* may indeed be discussing *galut*, and the distinction is factual: *ha de-itlia, ha de-lo itlia* — one case involves a rung that had been eaten by worms and therefore sagged slightly under the person's weight before breaking off. That sagging constitutes a downward motion, making the rung's fall *derech yeridah*, and the person is *chayav galut*. The other *braita* involves a rung that had not been so compromised, with no sagging — and that person is *patur*.

### Poor Fastening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא וְהָא דְּלָא אַתְלַע, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּמִיהַדַּק, וְהָא דְּלָא מִיהַדַּק.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** that in both **this** *baraita* **and that** *baraita* the reference is to a rung that **was not worm infested, and** it is **not difficult, as this** *baraita*, where the ruling is that he is exempt, is referring to a case **where** the rung **was tightly inserted into the ladder,** and therefore he could not have foreseen that it would be displaced, **and that** *baraita*, where the ruling is that he is liable, is referring to a case **where** the rung **was not tightly inserted** and he should have anticipated its displacement.

A third resolution: *ha de-mihadek ve-ha de-lo mihadek* — one case involves a rung that was not well fastened in the first place. When stepped upon, it too would sag under the pressure before coming loose, again constituting a downward motion at the critical moment. A well-fastened rung that simply snapped without any preliminary downward motion would leave the climber *patur* from *galut*. The practical implication is that liability may turn on fine distinctions in the physical condition of the ladder — distinctions that are difficult to assess in advance, which is precisely why the question of whether one must clear the area before climbing even a new ladder remains genuinely open.
