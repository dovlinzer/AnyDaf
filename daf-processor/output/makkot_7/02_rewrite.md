# Makkot 7 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Escaped Convicts & Sanhe…

### Escaped Convict

The second chapter of *Makkot* opens with a *mishna* concerning a convicted person who escapes and is subsequently apprehended: *mi shenigmar dino uvarach* — one whose *din* has been concluded and who has fled. If he returns and appears before the same *beit din*, *ein sotrin et dino* — the court does not retry his case. The *mishna* continues: wherever two witnesses come forward and declare that such-and-such a person was sentenced in the *beit din* of so-and-so, with these and these as his witnesses, *harei ze ye'hareg* — that person is put to death on the basis of their testimony alone, without any new proceeding.

### Sanhedrin Authority

The *mishna* then introduces a new topic: *sanhedrin noheget ba'aretz uvachutz la'aretz* — a *sanhedrin* can be established whether in *Eretz Yisrael* or outside of it. Based on the context of the *gemara*, this refers to a *sanhedrin* of twenty-three. The *mishna* also introduces the famous teaching that a *sanhedrin* which executes one person in seven years is called a *chavlanit* — destructive.

### Same vs. Different Court

The *gemara* opens by inferring from the *reisha* of the *mishna*: it is specifically before the *same* *beit din* that the case is not retried, which implies that before a *different* *beit din* the case would be reopened and the court would seek grounds for acquittal. Yet the *seifa* seems to contradict this, since it states that even when two witnesses appear anywhere and testify to the sentence, the condemned is executed immediately without any retry. How are these two clauses reconciled?

*Abaye* resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan b'Eretz Yisrael, kan b'chutz la'aretz* — the *seifa* is speaking of *Eretz Yisrael*, where a different *beit din* would indeed reopen the case, while the *reisha* speaks of outside *Eretz Yisrael*, where even a different *beit din* does not retry the matter. The asymmetry is striking, and it raises a genuine conceptual question: why should the mere fact of escape trigger a renewed search for acquittal? The logic of the institution is not immediately apparent.

### Escape Across Borders

A *braita* is cited in support: *Rabbi Yehuda ben Dostai* says in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Shetach* that if one escapes from *Eretz Yisrael* to *chutz la'aretz*, the case is not retried; but if one escapes from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael*, the case is retried — *mipnei zechutah shel Eretz Yisrael*, because of the merit of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Merit of Eretz Yisrael

*Rashi* explains the concept of *zechutah shel Eretz Yisrael* as follows: *ulai toil limtzo lo petach shel zechut* — perhaps the merit of *Eretz Yisrael* will somehow influence the proceedings and the court will find a basis for acquittal. There is something counterintuitive here: the stronger and more skilled a *dayan* is, the more capable he is of finding grounds for acquittal. This is precisely what *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* claimed — *ilu hayinu b'Sanhedrin, lo neherag adam me'olam* — had they sat on the *sanhedrin*, they would always have found a way to exonerate the accused. And *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* responded: *af hem marbin shofchei damim beYisrael* — by publicly announcing such a policy, they would only be increasing bloodshed in Israel, since the deterrent effect of capital punishment would be eliminated. The parallel concern applies here: allowing a convicted person to escape from *chutz la'aretz* to *Eretz Yisrael* and obtain a new hearing creates an obvious incentive for flight.

### Biblical Source

The *gemara* then turns to the derivation that a *sanhedrin* of twenty-three operates both in *Eretz Yisrael* and outside it. The *tanna'im* cite the verse *v'hayu eleh lachem l'chukat mishpat l'doroteichem* — these shall be for you a statute of judgment for your generations. The verse continues with *b'chol moshvoteichem* — in all of your dwellings — which extends the obligation to establish courts beyond *Eretz Yisrael* alone.

### Court Jurisdiction

If so, the *gemara* asks, why does the *pasuk* also use the word *b'sharecha* — in your gates — which ordinarily refers specifically to the gates of cities in *Eretz Yisrael*? The answer is that the two formulations do different work: *b'sharecha atah moshiv batei dinim b'chol pelech u'pelech u'v'chol ir va'ir* — in *Eretz Yisrael* you establish courts in every district and in every city, whereas *u'v'chutz la'aretz atah moshiv b'chol pelech u'pelech ve'i atah moshiv b'chol ir va'ir* — outside of *Eretz Yisrael* you establish a *beit din* in every province but not in every individual city. The *sanhedrin* presumably defines what constitutes a *pelech*, and on that basis determines where courts are to be set up.

## Destructive Sanhedri (II)

### One Per Seven Years

The *mishna* teaches: *sanhedrin hahoreget echad be'shavua nikret chavlanit* — a *sanhedrin* that executes one person in seven years is called destructive.

### Seventy-Year Standard

*Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah* then offers an alternative: one execution every seventy years rises to the level of being called *chavlanit*.

### Unresolved Dispute

The *gemara* records the question: when *Rabbi Eliezer ben Azariah* says one in seventy years, does he mean that even that rate is called *chavlanit*, or does he mean that one per seventy years is *orach ar'a* — the proper and reasonable standard? The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teku*.

### Tarfon & Akiva View

*Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* declare: *ilu hayinu b'Sanhedrin, lo neherag adam me'olam* — had we sat on the *sanhedrin*, we would never have allowed anyone to be executed.

## Capital Conviction I (II)

### Witness Interrogation

The *gemara* immediately asks: *heichi havu avdei* — how would they have actually achieved this? This is not merely a moral position; it is a question about procedure. *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Elazar* both answer that they would interrogate the witnesses about the victim's physical status.

### Tereifah Status

In a case of murder, the judges would ask the witnesses: *re'item terefah* — did you see that the victim was a *terefah*, someone with a mortal internal condition who is in any case destined to die imminently? *Horeg shalem horeg* — or did you see that the person was whole and healthy? *Rav Ashi* adds a further complication: even if the witnesses claim the victim was *shalem* — complete and uninjured prior to the killing — one might still ask whether the precise location where the sword struck happened to coincide with an existing *terefah* wound, leaving the matter unresolved.

## Destructive Sanhedri (II)

### Shimon ben Gamliel

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* responds to the position of *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva*: *af hem marbin shofchei damim beYisrael* — by pursuing a policy of never convicting anyone, they would effectively increase bloodshed in Israel, since the absence of any credible threat of capital punishment removes the deterrent entirely.

## Capital Conviction I (II)

### Shechita Comparison

*Tosafot* raise a pointed question: if we are truly concerned that a victim might have been a *terefah* before the killing, why do we not raise the same concern in the context of *shechita*? When one slaughters an animal, we do not require the *shochet* to demonstrate that the animal was not already a *terefah* prior to slaughter. Both situations involve a *chazaka* that a living creature is presumed not to be a *terefah*. If that *chazaka* is sufficient for *shechita*, why is it not sufficient in capital cases to establish that the victim was *shalem*? Clearly the concern cannot be absolute, because if it were, the *Torah* could never sanction an execution — which would effectively nullify the entire institution of capital punishment, much as the Talmud elsewhere discusses with *ben sorer u'moreh* and *ir ha-nidachat*.

### Unknown Answer Disqualif…

*Rabbeinu Tam* addresses the issue differently. He explains that the strategy of *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* did not rest on the *terefah* question per se. Rather, the method was *she-marbe be-vedikot* — to multiply questions put to the witnesses until eventually one witness contradicts the other, producing a *hakchashah* that disqualifies their testimony entirely. The key principle *Rabbeinu Tam* articulates is this: if the witnesses are asked a question and respond *ein anu yodim* — we don't know — this response itself disqualifies them. Witnesses who cannot provide complete answers to the court's examination may become *pesulim*, and the *edut* collapses. This is why the phrase *lo neherag adam me'olam* is *lav davka* — not strictly literal. Most of the time, *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* would succeed in tripping up the witnesses through extensive questioning; occasionally they would not. It follows, as *Tosafot* make clear, that this is not a claim to literally abolish the death penalty, but rather a description of a vigorous commitment to evidentiary scrutiny.

### Sexual Relations Evidence

The *gemara* turns to a different category of capital crime: *boel et ha-erva* — one who has illicit sexual relations. *Heichi avdei* — how would *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* have handled this case? *Abaye* and *Rava* both state that the witnesses would be asked: *re'item k'michul ba-shfoferet* — did you see the actual act of penetration, like a brush entering a tube? This is the standard metaphor used throughout the Talmud for the act of intercourse. If the witnesses could not give such direct testimony, there would be no conviction.

### Sufficient Appearance

The *rabbanan*, however, hold a different standard. The *gemara* cites *Shmuel* who says: *bimna'afim, mi-she-yiru k'mna'afim* — in cases of illicit relations, once witnesses see the parties behaving in the manner of those engaged in the act, that is sufficient. The question this raises is significant: if the *rabbanan* do not require actual testimony of penetration, how can they convict? The answer is that *Shmuel*'s principle allows conviction on the basis of contextual observation, without requiring the witnesses to testify to the act itself. The *Rambam* rules in accordance with the *rabbanan*, and the *Shulchan Aruch* follows the *Rambam* on this point.

## Accidental Homicide

### Exile Category

With the conclusion of the first chapter, the *gemara* begins the second: *elu hen ha-golin* — these are those who go into exile. The Torah provides for *arei miklat*, sanctuary cities, to which a person who accidentally kills another must flee. There he is protected from the *go'el ha-dam*, the blood avenger from the victim's family, who is otherwise permitted to kill the manslayer. The chapter enumerates who is and who is not included in this obligation.

### Downward Motion Cases

The *mishna* presents several paradigmatic cases of accidental homicide that do incur exile. If one was rolling a log along a roof — *Rashi* explains this was a common method of sealing rooftops with pitch — and the log fell and killed someone below, that person is exiled. Similarly, if one was lowering a barrel — *meshalshel be-chavit* — and it fell and killed someone. And if one was descending a ladder and fell onto someone and killed them, *harei zeh goleh* — that person is exiled.

### Upward Motion Cases

By contrast, the *mishna* lists cases that are excluded from exile: if one was pulling the log upward and it fell and killed someone, or one was raising the barrel and the rope broke, or one was ascending a ladder and fell and killed someone — in all these cases, *harei zeh eino goleh* — that person is not exiled.

### General Rule

The *mishna* then articulates the governing principle: *kol she-be-derech yeridato goleh, ve-shelo be-derech yeridato eino goleh* — any case in which the harmful event occurs through a downward motion incurs exile; any case in which it does not occur through a downward motion does not incur exile.

### Negligence vs. Exile

This raises an important conceptual distinction. In several of the excluded cases — for instance, pulling a barrel upward when the rope snaps — the person may well be negligent and could be liable to pay damages. The exclusion from *galut* does not necessarily mean full exemption from legal responsibility; it means specifically that the obligation to go to an *ir miklat* does not apply. The *gemara* will make clear that in some excluded cases liability for negligence remains, while in others the *gemara* excludes the individual altogether. There appear to be two dimensions to *ir miklat*: protection from the blood avenger, and a kind of existential atonement for having caused a death, however unintentionally. Both dimensions are operative in the institution, which makes the precise contours of the *derech yeridato* rule all the more significant.

### Biblical Source

*Shmuel* derives the entire principle from the verse *va-yipol alav va-yamot* — and it fell upon him and killed him — implying that exile is incurred only when the killing occurs through the natural manner of falling, which is a downward motion.

## Excluded Categories

### Intentional Exclusion

The *braita* proceeds to expound the relevant *pesukim* term by term. The word *bishgaga* — unintentionally — comes to exclude the *mezid*, someone who kills deliberately. This person is not exiled because he is *bar katla* — subject to execution, not exile.

### Omer Mutar Dispute

The *gemara* notes that excluding the *mezid* on the basis of *bishgaga* seems obvious — of course an intentional killer is not sent to an *ir miklat*. *Rava* therefore proposes a more refined reading: *bishgaga* comes to exclude the *omer mutar* — one who believes that killing is permitted and kills on that basis. Such a person is not simply a *mezid* in the usual sense, yet *Rava* argues he is *karov le-mezid* — close to being intentional — and therefore the *braita*'s exclusion applies to him.

*Abaye* pushes back: if someone genuinely believes killing is permitted, he is an *oness* — acting under a form of compulsion arising from mistaken belief — and ought not be labeled a *mezid* at all. *Rava* responds that while the *omer mutar* is not a full *mezid*, his proximity to intentionality is sufficient for the *braita* to treat him as falling outside the category of *shogeg* entitled to exile. Importantly, *karov le-mezid* is not identical to *mezid*: such a person would not be executed, but neither would he receive the protection of *galut*. How one could genuinely believe that murder is permitted is indeed difficult to conceive; the case is perhaps most analogous to one who mistakenly believes his victim is a *rodef*, though that remains a distinct *halachic* category.

A parallel analysis applies to the phrase *bivli da'at* — without knowledge — which excludes one who *intended* to kill. That exclusion, too, seems obvious since an intentional killer is *chayav mita*. *Rabba* therefore reinterprets it as excluding one who intended to kill an animal and accidentally killed a person, or intended to kill a *kuti* and killed a *Yisrael*, or intended to kill a child who was not halachically viable and killed a viable one. We know from elsewhere that *ha-mitkaven la-harog zeh ve-harag zeh patur* — one who intends to kill one person and kills another is exempt from execution. One might nonetheless have thought such a person incurs *galut* — the *braita* tells us he does not. Notably, the *braita* does not address the case where one intends to kill one *Yisrael* and kills a different *Yisrael* — leaving open the possibility that in that case, *galut* does apply.

### Corner Exception

The *pasuk* uses the word *be-feta* — suddenly — and the *braita* derives from this that the two parties must be in close proximity to one another. *Befeta* implies immediacy and closeness. This comes to exclude the case of *keren zavit* — a corner. *Rashi* explains that *leshon feta hu be-samuch* — the language of *feta* implies the two are near one another, *betakif*, immediately accessible. The case: one person is walking out of one alleyway and entering another, turning left or right, with a knife in hand — and another person comes around the corner and is accidentally stabbed. In this case there is no *galut*, because the parties were not in a position to see and be seen by one another; the word *feta* implies proximity of awareness, not merely physical proximity at the moment of impact.

This is admittedly counterintuitive. Walking through a public space with a knife outstretched represents a high degree of negligence; the case of two people rounding a corner and colliding might seem more like a genuine accident. Yet the *halacha* holds that the corner case is excluded from *galut* — though liability for damages may still apply — while the straight-line encounter could incur exile. The *derech yeridato* principle, combined with the *befeta* requirement, generates results that are not always intuitively obvious.

### Enmity Exclusion

The phrase *be-lo eivah* — without prior hatred — excludes the *soneh*, someone who harbored enmity toward the victim. If a person who hates someone else "accidentally" kills that person, the preexisting hatred disqualifies the act from being classified as pure *shgaga*, and the killer does not receive the protection of *galut*.

### Intent Mismatch

The *braita* then addresses a case involving a mismatch between intent and result by way of *hadafo* — pushing — establishing that the act must be *bishgaga*, without deliberate intent, and the harmful motion must be a downward one. The *braita* includes in the category of *chayav galut* a downward motion that is *tzorech aliyah* — functionally necessary to an upward motion. One might have thought that because the downward swing is merely a means to an upward motion, it should be classified as *aliyah* and thus exempt from *galut*. The *braita* teaches otherwise: *lehavi yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — this case is included.

### Wrong Target/Distance

The phrase *be-lo tzediyah* — without premeditation — comes to exclude two specific situations: one who intends to hit one side of a person and actually strikes another side, and one who intends to throw something two *amot* and actually throws it four. In both cases, the person is not *chayav galut*. Whether this is because the degree of negligence is too low or too high relative to the *galut* threshold is a matter of judgment; in either case, the person likely retains liability for damages.

### Mutual Access

Finally, the verse *ve-asher yavo et re'eihu ba-ya'ar* — and he goes with his fellow into the forest — generates a general principle: *mah ya'ar reshut le-nizak ule-mazik lehikaneis lesham* — just as in a forest both the damaged and the damager have permission to enter, so too *galut* liability requires that both parties had equal right of access to the location where the accident occurred.

## Ladder Case

### Rung Falling Question

*Rabbi Abbahu* poses a question to *Rabbi Yochanan*: *haya oleh be-sulam ve-nishmeta ha-shleiva mitachtav ve-naflah ve-hargah mahu* — if one is climbing up a ladder and, as he pushes down on a rung, the rung is dislodged and falls and kills someone, what is the law? Is this considered a downward motion — *yeridah* — triggering *galut*, or an upward motion — *aliyah* — exempting him?

### Downward for Upward

*Rabbi Yochanan* replies: *kevar nagata bi-yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — you have already touched upon this in the *braita*. The *braita* explicitly stated that a downward motion which is necessary for the purpose of going up — *yeridah she-hi tzorech aliyah* — is included in the category of *chayav galut*. The act of pushing down on the rung in order to propel oneself upward is precisely this: a downward motion performed in service of ascent. It is therefore *chayav galut*.

### Mishna Challenge

An objection is raised from the *mishna* itself: the *mishna* formulates the general rule as *kol she-be-derech yeridato goleh, ve-shelo be-derech yeridato eino goleh*. What is the phrase *she-lo be-derech yeridato* coming to include? The challenger suggests it comes to include precisely the ladder case — i.e., climbing up and having a rung fall — as an instance of something that is *not* in the way of descent and is therefore *eino goleh*. If so, the *mishna* itself contradicts *Rabbi Yochanan*'s answer.

### Butcher Example

*Rabbi Yochanan* turns the argument around: *ule-ta'amach*, according to your reasoning, what does the positive clause — *kol she-be-derech yeridato* — come to include? You need both clauses to be adding something. *Rabbi Yochanan* resolves this by reading both clauses of the *mishna*'s general rule as referring to the case of a *katzav* — a butcher chopping meat.

### Four Butcher Cases

A series of four *braitot* addresses the butcher case. The first teaches: *lefanav chayav, le-achorav patur* — liable if the blade falls in front of him, exempt if it falls behind him. The second teaches the reverse. The third holds liable in both directions; the fourth exempts in both. The apparent contradictions among these four *braitot* are resolved by attention to the direction of the butcher's arm motion at the precise moment the blade is released.

### Motion Direction

The resolution: whenever the *braita* says *chayav lefanav*, it refers to a downward motion in front — *yeridah she-lefanav*; whenever it says *patur le-achorav*, it refers to an upward motion behind — *aliyah she-le-achorav*. Systematically, upward motions result in *patur*, downward motions in *chayav*. There are four possible combinations — down-front, down-back, up-front, up-back — and the four *braitot* address them in differing combinations, all consistent with the overarching principle of *derech yeridato*.

### Tannaitic Dispute

The *gemara* then raises the possibility: *leima katanai hi* — perhaps the dispute between *Rabbi Abbahu* and *Rabbi Yochanan* reflects an underlying *tannaitic* dispute. The very case they debated — one who is climbing up a ladder and a rung falls — appears in two conflicting *braitot*, one ruling *chayav* and one ruling *patur*. Perhaps one *tanna* holds it is *yeridah* and the other holds it is *aliyah*.

### Two Liability Types

The *gemara* rejects this interpretation: *lo, de-chulei alma aliyah hi, ve-lo kashya — kan le-nizkin, kan le-galut*. Both *tanna'im* may agree that the motion is fundamentally *aliyah* — ascent — and there is no contradiction, because the two *braitot* are addressing different legal frameworks: one speaks of liability in *nezikin*, where one may be *chayav* to pay damages even for an upward motion involving negligence; the other addresses *galut*, where the *aliyah* classification exempts one from exile.

### Rung Sagging

Alternatively, both *braitot* may indeed be discussing *galut*, and the distinction is factual: *ha de-itlia, ha de-lo itlia* — one case involves a rung that had been eaten by worms and therefore sagged slightly under the person's weight before breaking off. That sagging constitutes a downward motion, making the rung's fall *derech yeridah*, and the person is *chayav galut*. The other *braita* involves a rung that had not been so compromised, with no sagging — and that person is *patur*.

### Poor Fastening

A third resolution: *ha de-mihadek ve-ha de-lo mihadek* — one case involves a rung that was not well fastened in the first place. When stepped upon, it too would sag under the pressure before coming loose, again constituting a downward motion at the critical moment. A well-fastened rung that simply snapped without any preliminary downward motion would leave the climber *patur* from *galut*. The practical implication is that liability may turn on fine distinctions in the physical condition of the ladder — distinctions that are difficult to assess in advance, which is precisely why the question of whether one must clear the area before climbing even a new ladder remains genuinely open.
