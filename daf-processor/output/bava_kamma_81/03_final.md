# Bava Kamma 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yehoshua's Ten Stipulations

### Ten Takkanot Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, עֲשָׂרָה תְּנָאִין הִתְנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: שֶׁיְּהוּ מַרְעִין בָּחוֹרָשִׁין; וּמְלַקְּטִין עֵצִים בִּשְׂדוֹתֵיהֶם; וּמְלַקְּטִים עֲשָׂבִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם – חוּץ מִתִּלְתָּן; וְקוֹטְמִים נְטִיעוֹת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם – חוּץ מִגְּרוֹפִיּוֹת שֶׁל זַיִת; וּמַעְיָן הַיּוֹצֵא בַּתְּחִילָּה – בְּנֵי הָעִיר מִסְתַּפְּקִין מִמֶּנּוּ; וּמְחַכִּים בְּיַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִפְרוֹס קֶלַע וְיַעֲמִיד אֶת הַסְּפִינָה;
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Joshua stipulated ten conditions** when he apportioned Eretz Yisrael among the tribes: **that** people **shall** have the right to **graze** their animals **in forests,** even on private property; **and** that **they** shall have the right to **gather wood from** each other's **fields,** to be used as animal fodder; **and** that **they** shall have the right to **gather** wild **vegetation** for animal fodder in any place **except for** a field of **fenugreek; and** that **they** shall have the right to **pluck off a shoot anywhere** for propagation and planting, **except for olive shoots; and** that **the people of the city** shall have the right to **take supplies** of water from a spring on private property, even **from a spring that emerges for the first time; and** that **they** shall have the right to **fish in the Sea of Tiberias,** i.e., the Sea of Galilee, **provided that** the fisherman **does not build an** underwater **fence** to catch fish, thereby **causing an impediment to boats.**

The *gemara* on Bava Kamma 81b opens with a *baraisa*: *asarah tenayim hitna Yehoshua* — Yehoshua made ten stipulations when the Jewish people entered the land of Israel. These were conditions assumed to have been agreed upon at the time of the conquest, governing the entire society going forward. What is immediately striking is how different these *takkanot* are from the mishna's preceding discussions of tort law. Rather than restricting behavior to prevent damage to others, these stipulations grant the public a degree of freedom even with respect to the private property of others.

### Grazing & Gathering

The first stipulation is *she-yehei mar'in b'chorshin* — that people may graze their animals in the forests, including privately owned ones. As noted earlier in the tractate, one is permitted to own sheep and goats provided they are grazed in forests rather than in fields. Here Yehoshua extends this principle to allow grazing even in someone else's private forest. The presumption seems to be that the vegetation poses no real concern to the owner — the forest is not being used for crops, and the vegetation will grow back.

The second and third stipulations allow gathering sticks and grasses from others' property. One may collect *etzim* — sticks — from another person's fields, presumably for firewood or fodder. One may also collect *asavim* — grasses — from anywhere, to feed one's animals. Yehoshua's logic throughout seems to be that wherever the owner suffers no real loss, the public cannot rightfully be excluded. The *gemara* will qualify each of these substantially, because there are plainly situations where the owner does stand to lose something.

### Water & Fishing Rights

The fourth stipulation concerns *mayan ha-yotzei ba-techilla* — a wellspring that emerges on its own. Even though the spring surfaces on private land, the townspeople have a right to use it. This is described as a remarkable ruling: water was a desperate public necessity, and Yehoshua declared that even a naturally occurring spring on private property is subject to public usage rights. The owner may retain some form of ownership — Rabba bar Rav Huna argues that one must pay for the water — but the *gemara* rejects this view and holds that no payment is required. The precise scope of this right (how much water, for what uses) is left open, but the principle is clear: the private owner cannot simply exclude the public.

The fifth stipulation permits *mechakin b'yama shel Tverya* — cast hook fishing in the Sea of Tiberias — even from shores that border private land. The qualifier is *bilvad she-lo yifros kela ve-ya'amid et ha-sefinah* — one may not spread out box traps or similar devices that would obstruct the boats. Nets, however, are permitted.

### Paths & Privacy Rights

The sixth stipulation, *niftin le-achorei ha-geder* — one may relieve oneself behind a stone fence — applies even in a field full of saffron, the most precious commodity of the ancient world. The allowance is clearly grounded in *kavod ha-briyot*, human dignity, and perhaps the recognition that necessity admits of no argument.

The seventh stipulation concerns *shvilei ha-reshut* — either paths that private owners have designated for passage or simply paths on privately owned land — which the public may use until the second rainfall. The background is that after the harvest, a field lies fallow and passage causes no harm. Once the second rain falls after seeding, however, the growing crops are at risk, and the right of passage lapses. Rashi interprets *reshut* as referring to paths that owners have explicitly permitted; others understand it simply to mean privately owned paths.

The eighth stipulation, *u-mistalkin le-tzidei ha-derachim mipnei yesados ha-derachim*, permits walking onto private property at the edges of a road when the hardened mud of the path — having dried into sharp, uneven ridges after the rainy season — makes the center of the road difficult to traverse.

### Lost in Vineyard & Meis Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְנִין לַאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׂדֶה מְלֵיאָה כַּרְכּוֹם; וּמְהַלְּכִים בִּשְׁבִילֵי הָרְשׁוּת, עַד שֶׁתֵּרֵד רְבִיעָה שְׁנִיָּה; וּמִסְתַּלְּקִין לְצִידֵּי הַדְּרָכִים מִפְּנֵי יְתֵידוֹת הַדְּרָכִים; וְהַתּוֹעֶה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים – מְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד; וּמֵת מִצְוָה קוֹנֶה מְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues the list of Joshua's ten conditions: **And** people shall have the right to **relieve themselves** outdoors **behind a fence, even in a field** that is **full of saffron [*karkom*]; and they** shall have the right to **walk in permitted paths,** i.e., those paths that cut through a private field, throughout the summer **until the second rainfall,** when crops begin to sprout; **and they** shall have the right to **veer off to the sides of the roads** onto private property **because of hard protrusions [*yeteidot*] of the road; and one who becomes lost among the vineyards** shall have the right to **cut down branches and enter** an area of the vineyard, or **cut down branches and exit** an area of the vineyard, until he finds his way back to the road; **and** that **a corpse with no one to bury it [*met mitzva*] acquires its place** and is buried where it was found.

The ninth and tenth stipulations complete the list. *Ha-to'eh bein ha-keramim* — one who is lost among the vines of a vineyard — may cut his way out, whether upward or downward, *mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored*, until he reaches a road or a town. And *meis mitzvah koneh mekomo* — an unidentified corpse acquires, as it were, its place of death. Wherever an unknown person dies, he has the right to be buried there, regardless of whose property it is.

## Grazing Qualifications

### Animal Size & Forest Density

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיְּהוּ מַרְעִין בָּחוֹרָשִׁין. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דַּקָּה בְּגַסָּה, אֲבָל דַּקָּה בְּדַקָּה וְגַסָּה בְּגַסָּה – לָא. וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן גַּסָּה בְּדַקָּה דְּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The first condition mentioned in the *baraita* is **that** people shall have the right to **graze** their animals **in forests. Rav Pappa said: We said** this **only** with regard to **small** domesticated animals, i.e., sheep or goats, that graze **in** a forest of **large** trees. Small animals grazing in a forest of this kind would not destroy it. **But** in the case of **small** animals grazing **in** a forest with **small** growth, or **large** animals, e.g., oxen, grazing **in** a forest of **large** trees, these practices are **not** permitted, as in either of these scenarios the practice would destroy the forest. **And, all the more so,** it is understood **that large** animals grazing **in** a forest with **small** growth is **not** permitted.

The *gemara* now unpacks each stipulation in detail. Regarding the first — grazing in forests — Rav Papa qualifies: *lo amran ela daka b'gasah* — this was only said of small animals (*daka*), such as sheep and goats, grazing in a thick, densely wooded forest (*gasah*). Three other combinations are excluded. Small animals in a thin forest (*daka b'daka*) — not permitted, because the sparse vegetation could be significantly damaged. Large animals in a dense forest (*gasah b'gasah*) — also not permitted, because even dense growth cannot withstand large animals without real harm to the owner. And *kol she-kein gasah b'daka* — certainly large animals in a thin forest are not permitted. All of this, of course, applies only to someone else's forest; an owner may do as he pleases on his own land.

### Gathering Sticks Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְלַקְּטִין עֵצִים מִשְּׂדוֹתֵיהֶם. לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּהִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי, אֲבָל בִּשְׁאָר עֵצִים – לָא. וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּהִיזְמֵי וְהִיגֵי – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בִּמְחוּבָּרִין, אֲבָל בִּתְלוּשִׁין – לָא. וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְחוּבָּרִין – לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּלַחִין, אֲבָל בִּיבֵשִׁין – לָא. וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְשָׁרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the next condition mentioned in the *baraita*: **And** that **they** shall have the right to **gather wood from** each other's **fields,** to be used as animal fodder. The Gemara comments: **We said** this **only with regard to** twigs of **thorns and shrubs,** as the field's owner does not care about these. **But** with regard to **other** types of **wood,** it is **not** permitted. **And even with regard to** twigs of **thorns and shrubs, we said** this **only when** they are **attached** to the ground. **But when** they have been **detached** by the owner it is **not** permitted, as he has already claimed them for himself. **And even when** they are **attached we said** this **only when** the twigs are still **moist, but when** they are completely **dry** it is **not** permitted, as the owner requires these for firewood. Moreover, it is permitted only **provided that one does not uproot** the thorn bush or shrub from the ground, but it is prohibited to pull them out with their roots.

Regarding gathering sticks, the *gemara* layers qualification upon qualification. *Lo amran ela b'hizmei ve-higni* — it applies only to thornbushes, not to other wood, which the owner may well want for firewood. Even for thornbushes, the allowance applies only to *mechubarim* — things still attached to the ground — because detached thornbushes (*telushin*) may already have been pulled out by the owner for his own use. And even if the thornbushes are still attached, the allowance is only for *lach* — moist ones — not dry ones, since dry thornbushes are much more readily usable as firewood and the owner may have plans for them. Finally, even in permitted cases, *u-vilvad she-lo yesharesh* — one may not uproot the thornbush in a way that damages the soil.

The pattern here is instructive. Most of Yehoshua's stipulations are operative only where the owner suffers no real loss. The moment a genuine loss to the owner comes into view, the permission evaporates. The exceptions are the cases that override even tangible damage — the meis mitzvah, relieving oneself — and those are grounded in overriding concerns of dignity or the sacred.

### Fenugreek & Grasses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְלַקְּטִין עֲשָׂבִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, חוּץ מִשְּׂדֵה תִּלְתָּן. לְמֵימְרָא דְּתִלְתָּן מְעַלּוּ לַהּ עֲשָׂבִים? וּרְמִינְהִי: תִּלְתָּן שֶׁעָלְתָה עִם מִינֵי עֲשָׂבִים – אֵין מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ לַעֲקוֹר! אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן לְזֶרַע, כָּאן לְזִירִין. לְזֶרַע – קָשׁוּ לַהּ עֲשָׂבִים, דְּמַכְחֲשִׁי לַהּ; לְזִירִין – מְעַלֵּי לַהּ, דְּכִי קָיְימִי בֵּינֵי עֲשָׂבִים – מִירַכְּבָא. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כָּאן לְאָדָם, כָּאן לִבְהֵמָה. דְּכֵיוָן דְּלִבְהֵמָה הוּא דְּזַרְעַהּ, עֲשָׂבִים נָמֵי מִיבְּעֵי לַהּ. וּמְנָא יָדְעִינַן? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שָׁארְיָה מְשָׁארֵי – לְאָדָם, לָא שָׁארְיָה מְשָׁארֵי – לִבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further discusses Joshua's conditions: **And** that **they** shall have the right to **gather** wild **vegetation** for animal fodder in any place **except for** a field of **fenugreek.** The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that** wild **vegetation is good for fenugreek,** and therefore the owner wants it to be left in his field? The Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna that deals with diverse kinds (*Kilayim* 2:5): With regard to **fenugreek that sprouted alongside** various **types of** wild **vegetation, one is not required to uproot** the vegetation. Although it is generally prohibited to grow different species of vegetables together in one patch, if the species have a negative impact on each other's growth, it is not prohibited. In this case the wild vegetation may be left alongside the fenugreek because it is harmful to it, and the *halakhot* of diverse kinds do not apply in a case of this kind. **Rav Yirmeya said:** This is **not difficult. Here,** the mishna is referring to a case where the fenugreek was planted **for** its **seeds; there,** the *baraita* that lists Joshua's conditions is referring to fenugreek planted **for** its **stalks.** When it is planted **for** its **seeds,** wild **vegetation is bad for it, as it weakens** the fenugreek and lowers its yield. But when it is planted **for** its **stalks,** wild vegetation is **good for it, as when it is situated among vegetation it climbs on it** and thereby grows to a larger size. **If you wish, say** instead another answer: **Here,** the mishna is referring to a case where the fenugreek was planted **for human** consumption; and **there,** the *baraita* is referring to fenugreek planted **for animal** consumption. **Since** the owner **sowed** the fenugreek **for animal** consumption, the wild **vegetation is also required by him,** as this too can be used for animal fodder. **And how can we know** if a particular fenugreek field was planted for human or animal consumption, and thereby know whether the vegetation may be picked and taken? **Rav Pappa said:** If he planted it **in rows [*mesharei*],** it is **for human** consumption; if he did **not** plant **in rows** but planted haphazardly, it is **for animal** consumption.

Regarding grasses, the *gemara* notes the exception of *tiltan*, fenugreek: grasses may be gathered everywhere *chutz mi-sdeh tiltan* — except from a fenugreek field. This prompts a question: are we saying that grasses actually benefit fenugreek? But a *baraisa* implies the opposite — if fenugreek grows up alongside other grasses, we do not require the owner to uproot either the fenugreek or the grasses on account of *kilayim*, the prohibition of mixed species, precisely because the mixture arose on its own without deliberate planting. The implication of that *baraisa* is that the grasses actually harm the fenugreek, not help it.

Rav Yirmiyah resolves the tension: *lo kashya, kan le-zera, kan le-zirin* — no difficulty. It depends on whether the fenugreek is being grown for its seeds or for its stalks. For the seed, the grasses are harmful — *kashu leh asavim de-machchesh lah* — they weaken the seeds. That is the case in the *baraisa*, where the mixture arose incidentally; since the grasses damage the fenugreek-for-seed, the owner does not want them growing together, and there is no *kilayim* concern. But for the stalk, *ma'alu leh* — the grasses are beneficial, because when the fenugreek grows up amid grasses, *mirkava*, it spreads across the surrounding vegetation and the stalk develops more robustly. In that case the grasses are wanted, and removing them would hurt the owner.

Alternatively, the *gemara* offers a second distinction: *kan le-adam, kan li-vhemah* — whether the fenugreek is grown for human consumption or for animal consumption. If it is grown for animals, then *de-zara asavim nami miba'ei leh* — the grower wants the grasses too, as additional feed. In that case, gathering the grasses hurts the owner and Yehoshua's *takkana* does not apply. Rav Papa adds a practical marker: if the fenugreek is planted in rows, it is grown for human consumption; if not in rows, it is for animals.

## Branch Cutting Rules

### Olive Tree Branches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקוֹטְמִים נְטִיעָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, חוּץ מִגְּרוֹפִיּוֹת שֶׁל זַיִת. פֵּירְשׁוּ רַבִּי תַּנְחוּם וְרַבִּי בִּרְיָיס מִשּׁוּם זָקֵן אֶחָד: בְּזַיִת – כְּבֵיצָה. בְּקָנִים וּבִגְפָנִים – מִן הַפְּקָק וּלְמַעְלָה. וּשְׁאָר כׇּל הָאִילָנוֹת – מִן אוּבּוֹ שֶׁל אִילָן, וְלֹא מִן חוּדּוֹ שֶׁל אִילָן; מִן חָדָשׁ שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה פֵּירוֹת, וְלֹא מִן יָשָׁן שֶׁהוּא עוֹשֶׂה פֵּירוֹת; מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה אֶת הַחַמָּה,
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches: **And** that **they** shall have the right to **pluck off a shoot anywhere** for propagation and planting, **except for olive shoots,** as this would cause damage to the olive tree. **Rabbi Tanḥum and Rabbi Berayes explained** this **in the name of a certain elder: With regard to olive** trees, one must leave a shoot **the size of an egg** on the trunk when detaching it from the trunk; in the case **of reeds and grape vines,** he may take shoots only **from the** place of the first **knot and above. And** with regard to **all other trees,** shoots may be taken only **from the thick** part **of** the **tree,** where there are many branches growing, **but not from the thin** part **of** the **tree. Furthermore, one may take** **from a new** branch, **which does not** yet **produce fruit, but not from old** branches, **which do produce fruit;** and one may take **from a place that does not face the sun,**

Regarding the fifth item in the original list — *ve-kotmim neti'ah be-chol makom chutz mi-grufiyot shel zayit* — the public may break off a small twig anywhere, from any tree, except from the shoots of an olive tree. Rav Tanchum and Rav Birai, citing an elder, specify: for an olive tree the relevant threshold is *ke-beitzah* — egg-sized. The precise application of this measurement is itself debated, but one reading is that a twig of up to that size may be taken, though the surrounding prohibition on olive shoots makes this complicated.

### Other Tree Branches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא מִמְּקוֹם שֶׁהוּא רוֹאֶה אֶת הַחַמָּה; שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּמִמֶּגֶד תְּבוּאֹת שָׁמֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** **but not from a place that faces the sun,** where the fruits grow copiously, **as it is stated: "And for the precious things of the fruits of the sun"** (Deuteronomy 33:14).

For other trees — reeds and grapevines — one may cut from *min ha-paka u-le-ma'alah*, from the knuckle-like joint upward. For all other trees, one must cut from *ovyo shel ilan ve-lo me-chudo* — from the inner, less exposed part of the tree, not from the tip or the top. Rashi explains this as meaning one must take from the interior side.

Two further restrictions apply regardless of tree type. One may only cut a *chadash* — a new twig that is not yet bearing fruit — and not from a branch that is actively producing. And one must take from a location that is *eino ro'eh et ha-chamah* — not exposed to sunlight — since sun-exposed growth produces the best fruit and is exactly what the owner would wish to preserve. The *gemara* cites the verse *u-mi-meged tevu'ot shamesh* — from the sweetness of the produce of the sun — as the basis. The aggregate principle is plain: the twig may be taken only in a manner that causes the owner no meaningful loss.

## Water & Fishing

### Wellspring Rights

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַעְיָן הַיּוֹצֵא תְּחִילָּה – בְּנֵי הָעִיר מִסְתַּפְּקִין מִמֶּנּוּ. אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ דָּמִים. וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: **And the people of the city** shall have the right to **take supplies** of water from a spring on private property, even **from a spring that emerges for the first time. Rabba bar Rav Huna says: And** although one may draw water from that spring, **he** must **give money to** reimburse the owner of the property. The Gemara concludes: **But the *halakha* is not in accordance with his** opinion, as the water may be taken without payment.

*U-ma'inah yotzei techilat benei ha'ir mistapkim mimenu* — if a new wellspring emerges naturally on someone's land, the townspeople have a right to draw from it. Rabba bar Rav Huna rules that payment must nonetheless be made to the owner. The *gemara* rejects this ruling: *v'leit hilchata ke-vateih* — we do not rule in accordance with his position. The owner retains some formal ownership, but the public's right of use is not conditioned on payment. The exact parameters of this right — how much water, for what purposes — are not spelled out, but the underlying principle reflects the ancient understanding of water as a communal necessity that private ownership cannot fully obstruct.

### Fishing Methods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְחַכִּין בְּיַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִפְרוֹס קֶלַע וְיַעֲמִיד אֶת הַסְּפִינָה – אֲבָל צָד הוּא בִּרְשָׁתוֹת וּבְמִכְמָרוֹת. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה הִתְנוּ שְׁבָטִים זֶה עִם זֶה שֶׁלֹּא יִפְרוֹס קְלִיעָה וְיַעֲמִיד אֶת הַסְּפִינָה, אֲבָל צָד הוּא בִּרְשָׁתוֹת וּבְמִכְמָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The next of Joshua's conditions is: **And** that **they** shall have the right to **fish in the Sea of Tiberias, provided that** the fisherman **does not build an** underwater **fence** to catch fish, thereby **causing an impediment to boats.** The Gemara comments: **But one may fish with nets and with traps. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Initially, the tribes stipulated with each other that one may not build** an underwater **fence** to catch fish, thereby **causing an impediment to boats, but one may fish with nets and with traps.**

The *gemara* elaborates on the fishing stipulation. Tosafot explains that there are three tiers of access. Cast hook fishing — ordinary line fishing from shore — is available to anyone, even on shores that front private land. The large box traps, *kela*, which would catch passing boats, are prohibited to everyone, even the landowner whose shore it is. Net fishing — *reshatot u-michmeret* — occupies the middle tier: permitted only to the landowner.

### Kinneret Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יַמָּהּ שֶׁל טְבֶרְיָא – בְּחֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל נַפְתָּלִי הָיְתָה. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁנָּטַל מְלֹא חֶבֶל חֵרֶם בִּדְרוֹמָהּ, לְקַיֵּים מַה שֶּׁנֶּאֱמַר ״יָם וְדָרוֹם יְרָשָׁה״. תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: תְּלוּשִׁין שֶׁבֶּהָרִים – בְּחֶזְקַת כׇּל הַשְּׁבָטִים הֵן עוֹמְדִים, וּמְחוּבָּרִים – בְּחֶזְקַת אוֹתוֹ הַשֵּׁבֶט. וְאֵין לְךָ כׇּל שֵׁבֶט וְשֵׁבֶט מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהָר וּבַשְּׁפֵלָה וּבַנֶּגֶב וּבָעֵמֶק, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״פְּנוּ וּסְעוּ לָכֶם וּבֹאוּ הַר הָאֱמוֹרִי וְאֶל כׇּל שְׁכֵנָיו; בָּעֲרָבָה, בָהָר, בַּשְּׁפֵלָה וּבַנֶּגֶב וּבְחוֹף הַיָּם וְגוֹ׳. וְכֵן אַתָּה מוֹצֵא בַּכְּנַעֲנִים וּבַפְּרִיזִּים וּבָאֱמוֹרִיִּים שֶׁלִּפְנֵיהֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאֶל כׇּל שְׁכֵנָיו״ – אַלְמָא שְׁכֵנָיו הָכִי הָווּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The Sea of Tiberias was** located **in the portion of** the tribe of **Naphtali. Moreover,** the tribe of Naphtali **received** in addition a small stretch of land equal to **the full** length of the **rope of** a fish **trap.** This stretch of land was located **to the south** of the sea, where the members of this tribe could spread out their fishing nets, **to fulfill that which is stated:** "And of Naphtali he said: O Naphtali, satisfied with favor, and full with the blessing of the Lord; **possess the sea and the south"** (Deuteronomy 33:23). **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: Detached** items **that are** found **in the mountains** at the time of conquest **are** considered to be **in the possession of all the tribes** equally, as spoils of war; **but** that which is **attached,** e.g., trees, **are** considered to be **in the** sole **possession of that tribe** that would receive the land where the tree was found. The *baraita* adds: **And you do not have a single tribe of Israel that did not have** in its portion at least some land **in the mountains, and** some **in the lowland, and** some **in the countryside, and** some **in the valley, as it is stated: "Turn and take your journey, and go to the hill-country of the Amorites and to all their neighbors, in the Arabah, in the hill-country, and in the lowland, and in the countryside, and by the seashore"** (Deuteronomy 1:7). **And you find similarly with regard to the Canaanites and Perizzites and Amorites who** inhabited the land **before** the Jews, **as it stated** in the above verse: "The Amorites **and to all their neighbors." Apparently,** the Amorites and **their neighbors** all **had this** variety of types of land in their respective territories.

A *baraisa* records that from the outset, the tribes made a mutual condition among themselves not to use the *kela* traps, but that net fishing was freely permitted.

A further *baraisa* provides the historical background: the Sea of Tiberias fell within the tribal portion of Naftali. Moreover, Naftali received an additional strip — *melo chevel cherem bi-droma* — a measured section of the southern shore, even though his primary territory lay to the north and east, in fulfillment of the verse *yam ve-darom yerashah* — he shall inherit the west (i.e., the western shore of the Kinneret) and the south.

On a related point, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches: detached objects found in the mountains — *telushin she-be-harim* — were held collectively in the presumptive ownership of all the tribes, while attached things — *mechubarim* — belonged to the specific tribe in whose territory they lay. Rashi understands this broadly: movable property seized in the conquest of the land was distributed equally among all Israel regardless of where it was found, while land and its attachments were divided by tribal portion.

The *gemara* adds that every tribe received a portion encompassing the full range of landscapes: *har*, *shefela*, *negev*, and *emek* — mountains, lowlands, dry terrain, and valleys. The verse in Devarim supports this: *ba-aravah ba-har u-va-shefela u-va-negev u-va-chof ha-yam* — in the plain, in the mountain, in the lowland, in the south, and at the shore of the sea. Not every tribe received coastal access literally, but the distribution was designed to give each tribe a variety of terrain. The *gemara* notes that this pattern preceded Israel as well, having characterized the holdings of the Canaanites, Perizzites, and Emorites before them, as the verse implies: *ve-el kol shecheinav* — and to all its neighbors.

## Relief & Passage

### Relieving Oneself

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְנִין לַאֲחוֹרֵי הַגָּדֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׂדֶה שֶׁהִיא מְלֵאָה כַּרְכּוֹם. אָמַר רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: לֹא נִצְרְכָה אֶלָּא לִיטּוֹל הֵימֶנּוּ צְרוֹר. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׁבָּת. מָר זוּטְרָא חֲסִידָא שָׁקֵיל וּמַהְדַּר, וַאֲמַר לֵיהּ לְשַׁמָּעֵיהּ (לִמְחַר): זִיל שִׁירְקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the next of Joshua's conditions: **And** people shall have the right to **relieve themselves** outdoors **behind a fence, even in a field** that is **full of saffron. Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** It goes without saying that one may relieve himself when necessary; this stipulation **is necessary only** to permit the one relieving himself **to take a stone out of** a wall in the field with which to clean himself. **Rav Ḥisda said: And** it is permitted to remove a stone from a wall for this purpose **even on Shabbat. Mar Zutra the Pious would take** a stone in this manner on Shabbat **and replace** it in the wall, **and say to his attendant** after Shabbat: **Go and plaster it over,** so that it would fit securely back in the wall.

*Ve-niftin le-achorei ha-geder* — one may relieve oneself behind a fence — *va-afilu bi-sdeh she-mlei'ah karkom* — even in a field full of saffron. Rav Acha bar Yaakov observes that the core *chiddush* here is not the obvious permissibility of relieving oneself (whether obvious because of *kavod ha-briyot* or because the act arguably fertilizes the field), but rather that one may *take a stone from the stone wall* to use for wiping. That — removing a stone from another's fence — is the genuinely novel ruling.

### Kavod Habriyot on Shabbat

Rav Chisda extends this further: *va-afilu b'Shabbat* — even on Shabbat. The stone is *muktzeh*, yet *kavod ha-briyot* overrides this *de-rabbanan* restriction. Mar Zutra Chasida, being a *chasid*, would take the stone, use it, and then return it to the wall. He would then instruct his attendant — naturally, not on Shabbat itself — to plaster over that section, so that the used side would not remain exposed to others.

### Paths of Reshut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְהַלְּכִים בִּשְׁבִילֵי הָרְשׁוּת, עַד שֶׁתֵּרֵד רְבִיעָה שְׁנִיָּה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְהַאי דִּידַן – אֲפִילּוּ טַל קָשֵׁי לֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further states: **And they** shall have the right to **walk in permitted paths,** i.e., those paths that cut through a private field, throughout the summer **until the second rainfall,** when crops begin to sprout. **Rav Pappa said: And** with regard to **these** fields that we have in Babylonia, **even dew** that settled the previous night **is bad for them.** Even after a night of dew, the field is sufficiently moistened that trampling on it will cause damage, and therefore this condition does not apply if there was dew the previous night.

The stipulation *u-mehalchin bi-shvilei ha-reshut ad she-teired reviah shniyah* — one may walk through the private paths until the second rainfall — reflects the agricultural calendar. After the harvest the field lies fallow; passage causes no harm and is therefore permitted. Once the second rain falls after sowing, the seedlings are vulnerable and the right lapses.

### Hardened Mud Paths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְסַלְּקִין לְצִידֵּי הַדְּרָכִים, מִפְּנֵי יְתֵידוֹת הַדְּרָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** The next item on the list of conditions is: **And they** shall have the right to **veer off to the sides of the roads** onto private property **because of hard protrusions of the road.**

Rav Papa qualifies this for Babylonian conditions: *va-hadidan afilu talya di-kashla* — in their fields, even a light dew suffices to make crossing damaging. The underlying principle is the same: passage is permitted only when it causes no harm. If the field is in any condition where crossing would injure it, the right does not apply. The stipulation about hardened mud — *yesados ha-derachim* — concerns the summer months when the path itself becomes treacherous: dried into sharp, uneven ridges, the center of the road is difficult and potentially injurious to walk on. In that situation, one may step off the road onto the private field margin.

## Walking on Field Edges

### Shmuel & Rav Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל וְרַב יְהוּדָה הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי וְאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא, הֲוָה מִסְתַּלַּק שְׁמוּאֵל לְצִידֵּי הַדְּרָכִים. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה: תְּנָאִין שֶׁהִתְנָה יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, אֲפִילּוּ בְּבָבֶל?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר, אֲפִילּוּ בְּחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel and Rav Yehuda,** who lived in Babylonia, **were** once **walking along the road,** and **Shmuel veered off to the sides of the road** onto private property. **Rav Yehuda said to him:** Do the **conditions that Joshua stipulated** apply **even in Babylonia?** Shmuel **said to him:** Indeed so, **as I say** that they apply **even outside of Eretz** Yisrael.

A narrative illustrates this. Shmuel and Rav Yehuda were walking along a road, and Shmuel stepped off to the side — onto private land — to avoid the hardened, treacherous mud of the path in the dry season. This is, of course, a stipulation Yehoshua made for the Land of Israel. But here we are outside of Eretz Yisrael, in Babylonia.

### Outside Eretz Yisrael Question

Rav Yehuda challenged Shmuel directly: *tenayim she-hitna Yehoshua afilu b'Bavel?* — are you claiming that the conditions Yehoshua stipulated apply even in Bavel? Yehoshua's conditions were manifestly limited to Eretz Yisrael. Shmuel replied: *sha'ani omer afilu b'chutz la-aretz* — I say they apply even outside the land. Whether Shmuel is claiming that Yehoshua himself intended universal application, or that any organized Jewish community operates with these implicit norms, is left somewhat open. It is worth noting that the *gemara* earlier applied the restrictions of the *mishna* regarding small animals to Babylonia as well, so there is a broader tendency to treat Babylonian Jewish society as operating by similar principles.

### Rebbi vs. Ben Kenusa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי וְרַבִּי חִיָּיא הֲווֹ שָׁקְלִי וְאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא, אִסְתַּלַּקוּ לְצִידֵּי הַדְּרָכִים. הֲוָה קָא מְפַסַּיע וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן (קְנוֹסָא) [נְקוֹסָא] קַמַּיְיהוּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מִי הוּא זֶה שֶׁמַּרְאֶה גְּדוּלָּה בְּפָנֵינוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא: שֶׁמָּא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן (קְנוֹסָא) [נְקוֹסָא] תַּלְמִידִי הוּא, וְכׇל מַעֲשָׂיו לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם. כִּי מְטוֹ לְגַבֵּיהּ, חַזְיֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָאו יְהוּדָה בֶּן (קְנוֹסָא) [נְקוֹסָא] אַתְּ, גְּזַרְתִּינְהוּ לְשָׁקָךְ בְּגִיזְרָא דְפַרְזְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **and Rabbi Ḥiyya were** once **walking along the road,** and **they veered off to the sides of the road. Rabbi Yehuda ben Kanosa was taking broad steps** on the road, to avoid the protrusions without going off to the side of the road, **while walking in front of them. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said to Rabbi Ḥiyya: Who is this** man **who is showing off** his supposed **greatness in our presence?** By acting more stringently than required by *halakha*, he is displaying insolence. **Rabbi Ḥiyya said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: **Perhaps it is my student Rabbi Yehuda ben Kanosa. And** if so, **all of his actions are** undertaken **for the sake of Heaven;** he is not acting out of haughtiness. **When they reached him** and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **saw him, he said to him: If you were not Yehuda ben Kanosa I would have cut off your legs with iron shears,** i.e., I would have excommunicated you for your impudence.

A parallel episode is related involving Rebbi — Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi — and Rebbi Chiya, who were walking together in Eretz Yisrael and similarly moved to the side of the road. Coming toward them, moving directly through the middle of the road, was Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, navigating the difficult terrain by hopping from one stable spot to the next rather than stepping onto private property.

Rebbi turned to Rebbi Chiya and asked: *mihu zeh she-mapgin gedulah b'faneinu?* — who is this person displaying his greatness before us? The implied affront was clear: here stand Rebbi, the foremost rabbinic and political leader of the generation, and Rebbi Chiya, alongside him — both walking on the side of the road — and this person ostentatiously refuses to do so, implicitly casting their practice into question.

Rebbi Chiya responded: perhaps it is Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, my student — *ve-chol ma'asav le-shem shamayim* — and all his actions are *le-shem shamayim*. He is not performing. He is simply that careful. When they drew close and saw who it was, they confirmed this was indeed the case. In Rashi's reading, Rebbi's initial remark was sufficiently sharp that had it been anyone else, it would have warranted *nidui* — excommunication. But given that it was Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, and his intentions were transparently pure, no such measure was appropriate.

## Lost in Vineyard

### Self-Rescue in Vineyard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַתּוֹעֶה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד מְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָרוֹאֶה חֲבֵירוֹ תּוֹעֶה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, מְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד, עַד שֶׁמַּעֲלֵהוּ לָעִיר אוֹ לַדֶּרֶךְ. וְכֵן הוּא שֶׁתּוֹעֶה בֵּין הַכְּרָמִים, מְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד עַד שֶׁיַּעֲלֶה לָעִיר אוֹ לַדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* further teaches: **And one who becomes lost among the vineyards** shall have the right to **cut down [*mefaseig*] branches and enter** an area of the vineyard, or **cut down branches and exit** an area of the vineyard, until he finds his way back to the road. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* that this stipulation extends further: With regard to **one who sees another** person **lost among the vineyards, he may cut down branches and enter** an area of the vineyard, or **cut down branches and exit** an area of the vineyard **until he** reaches him and **brings him** back **up to the city or to the road. And similarly,** if **he** himself is the one **who is lost among the vineyards, he may cut down branches and enter** an area of the vineyard, or **cut down branches and exit** an area of the vineyard **until he comes** back **up to the city or to the road.**

*Ha-to'eh bein ha-keramim mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — one who is lost among the vines may cut his way upward or downward through the vineyard until he reaches a town or a road. A *baraisa* extends this explicitly to two scenarios.

### Rescuing the Lost

The first scenario is a bystander who sees another person lost in the vineyard: *ha-ro'eh chaveiro to'eh bein ha-keramim mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — he too may cut a path in and out in order to extract the lost person and bring him back to a town or a road. The second scenario is the lost person himself: he may likewise cut his own way out.

### Logic of Rescuing the Lost

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְכֵן״? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, חֲבֵירוֹ הוּא דְּיָדַע לְהֵיכָא מְסַלֵּק – דְּנִיפְסוֹג; אֲבָל הוּא, דְּלָא יֵדַע לְהֵיכָא קָא סָלֵיק – לָא נִיפְסוֹג, נַהְדְּרֵיהּ (נֶהְדַּר) [מִהְדָּר] בֵּי מִיצְרֵי; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to this *baraita*: **What** is the point of the clause that begins with: **And similarly?** It is obvious that a lost individual himself has the same right to cut down branches as one who assists him to find his way out. The Gemara answers: That is taught **lest you say** that it is only **another** person who is permitted to cut down branches, **as,** having seen the lost party, **he knows** exactly **where he is going** to rescue the other and help him leave the vineyard, and **that** is why **he may cut down branches; but** with regard to the lost person himself, **who does not know where he is going,** one might have said that **he may not cut down branches** but must **go** all the way **back to the boundary** of the vineyard. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that one who is lost may cut down branches in his quest to find his own way to the nearby town or road.

One might ask why the *gemara* needs to tell us the second case separately — if even a bystander may cut through the vineyard to rescue someone, surely the person himself may do the same. The *gemara* anticipates this: one might have thought the difference matters. The bystander, coming from outside, knows where he is going — he can cut a relatively direct and limited path. The lost person, by contrast, has no idea of his orientation, and granting him the right to cut in any direction he imagines correct might lead to far greater destruction. Perhaps we should have required him to make his way to the edges of the vineyard and follow the perimeter out, even if that seems to him to be a far longer route. The *gemara* rules against this: *ka mashma lan* — the stipulation applies to the lost person as well, and he may cut in the direction he judges best.

### Deoraisa vs. Derabanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: הֲשָׁבַת גּוּפוֹ מִנַּיִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַהֲשֵׁבוֹתוֹ״! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא דְּקָאֵי בֵּי מִיצְרֵי, אֲתָא הוּא תַּקֵּין דִּמְפַסֵּיג וְעוֹלֶה מְפַסֵּיג וְיוֹרֵד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a further question: Why was it necessary for Joshua to stipulate that one may find his way out in this manner? After all, **this** *halakha* applies **by Torah law.** When one is lost, whoever can assist in helping him find his way must do so by Torah law, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: There is a mitzva to return lost items to their owner. **From where** is it derived that the requirement applies even to **returning his body,** i.e., helping a lost person find his way? **The verse states: "And you shall restore it** to him" (Deuteronomy 22:2), which can also be translated as: And you shall restore himself to him. If this is required by Torah law, why did Joshua stipulate a condition to this effect? The Gemara answers: **By Torah law** one is required only to walk in a roundabout path **along the boundaries,** without damaging another's vines by cutting off branches. Joshua **came and instituted** the stipulation **that one may** go even further and **cut off branches and ascend** or **cut off branches and descend,** thereby leaving through the most direct route.

The *gemara* then raises a pointed question: is this stipulation even necessary? Rescuing a person is a *de-oraisa* obligation. A *baraisa* teaches: *hashavat gufo minayin* — how do we know there is an obligation to return a person to himself, in the same manner as one is obligated to return lost property? From the verse *va-hashevoto* — you shall return him. So there is already a biblical obligation to rescue the lost. Why does Yehoshua's *takkana* add anything?

### Yehoshua's Tikkun

The *gemara* answers: *de-oraisa hu de-kai be-metzarei* — the biblical obligation is satisfied by having the rescuer go along the edges of the vineyard, without causing destruction to the vines. The *de-oraisa* obligation of *hashavah* does not, by itself, permit cutting through and damaging another's property. What Yehoshua came to establish — *atah tikken de-mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — is that even destructive passage through the vineyard is permitted in order to effect the rescue. That is Yehoshua's *takkana*: to authorize a form of rescue that would otherwise constitute prohibited damage to private property.
