# Bava Kamma 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yehoshua's Ten Stipulati…

### Ten Takkanot Overview

The *gemara* on Bava Kamma 81b opens with a *baraisa*: *asarah tenayim hitna Yehoshua* — Yehoshua made ten stipulations when the Jewish people entered the land of Israel. These were conditions assumed to have been agreed upon at the time of the conquest, governing the entire society going forward. What is immediately striking is how different these *takkanot* are from the mishna's preceding discussions of tort law. Rather than restricting behavior to prevent damage to others, these stipulations grant the public a degree of freedom even with respect to the private property of others.

### Grazing & Gathering

The first stipulation is *she-yehei mar'in b'chorshin* — that people may graze their animals in the forests, including privately owned ones. As noted earlier in the tractate, one is permitted to own sheep and goats provided they are grazed in forests rather than in fields. Here Yehoshua extends this principle to allow grazing even in someone else's private forest. The presumption seems to be that the vegetation poses no real concern to the owner — the forest is not being used for crops, and the vegetation will grow back.

The second and third stipulations allow gathering sticks and grasses from others' property. One may collect *etzim* — sticks — from another person's fields, presumably for firewood or fodder. One may also collect *asavim* — grasses — from anywhere, to feed one's animals. Yehoshua's logic throughout seems to be that wherever the owner suffers no real loss, the public cannot rightfully be excluded. The *gemara* will qualify each of these substantially, because there are plainly situations where the owner does stand to lose something.

### Water & Fishing Rights

The fourth stipulation concerns *mayan ha-yotzei ba-techilla* — a wellspring that emerges on its own. Even though the spring surfaces on private land, the townspeople have a right to use it. This is described as a remarkable ruling: water was a desperate public necessity, and Yehoshua declared that even a naturally occurring spring on private property is subject to public usage rights. The owner may retain some form of ownership — Rabba bar Rav Huna argues that one must pay for the water — but the *gemara* rejects this view and holds that no payment is required. The precise scope of this right (how much water, for what uses) is left open, but the principle is clear: the private owner cannot simply exclude the public.

The fifth stipulation permits *mechakin b'yama shel Tverya* — cast hook fishing in the Sea of Tiberias — even from shores that border private land. The qualifier is *bilvad she-lo yifros kela ve-ya'amid et ha-sefinah* — one may not spread out box traps or similar devices that would obstruct the boats. Nets, however, are permitted.

### Paths & Privacy Rights

The sixth stipulation, *niftin le-achorei ha-geder* — one may relieve oneself behind a stone fence — applies even in a field full of saffron, the most precious commodity of the ancient world. The allowance is clearly grounded in *kavod ha-briyot*, human dignity, and perhaps the recognition that necessity admits of no argument.

The seventh stipulation concerns *shvilei ha-reshut* — either paths that private owners have designated for passage or simply paths on privately owned land — which the public may use until the second rainfall. The background is that after the harvest, a field lies fallow and passage causes no harm. Once the second rain falls after seeding, however, the growing crops are at risk, and the right of passage lapses. Rashi interprets *reshut* as referring to paths that owners have explicitly permitted; others understand it simply to mean privately owned paths.

The eighth stipulation, *u-mistalkin le-tzidei ha-derachim mipnei yesados ha-derachim*, permits walking onto private property at the edges of a road when the hardened mud of the path — having dried into sharp, uneven ridges after the rainy season — makes the center of the road difficult to traverse.

### Lost in Vineyard & Meis…

The ninth and tenth stipulations complete the list. *Ha-to'eh bein ha-keramim* — one who is lost among the vines of a vineyard — may cut his way out, whether upward or downward, *mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored*, until he reaches a road or a town. And *meis mitzvah koneh mekomo* — an unidentified corpse acquires, as it were, its place of death. Wherever an unknown person dies, he has the right to be buried there, regardless of whose property it is.

## Grazing Qualifications

### Animal Size & Forest Den…

The *gemara* now unpacks each stipulation in detail. Regarding the first — grazing in forests — Rav Papa qualifies: *lo amran ela daka b'gasah* — this was only said of small animals (*daka*), such as sheep and goats, grazing in a thick, densely wooded forest (*gasah*). Three other combinations are excluded. Small animals in a thin forest (*daka b'daka*) — not permitted, because the sparse vegetation could be significantly damaged. Large animals in a dense forest (*gasah b'gasah*) — also not permitted, because even dense growth cannot withstand large animals without real harm to the owner. And *kol she-kein gasah b'daka* — certainly large animals in a thin forest are not permitted. All of this, of course, applies only to someone else's forest; an owner may do as he pleases on his own land.

### Gathering Sticks Rules

Regarding gathering sticks, the *gemara* layers qualification upon qualification. *Lo amran ela b'hizmei ve-higni* — it applies only to thornbushes, not to other wood, which the owner may well want for firewood. Even for thornbushes, the allowance applies only to *mechubarim* — things still attached to the ground — because detached thornbushes (*telushin*) may already have been pulled out by the owner for his own use. And even if the thornbushes are still attached, the allowance is only for *lach* — moist ones — not dry ones, since dry thornbushes are much more readily usable as firewood and the owner may have plans for them. Finally, even in permitted cases, *u-vilvad she-lo yesharesh* — one may not uproot the thornbush in a way that damages the soil.

The pattern here is instructive. Most of Yehoshua's stipulations are operative only where the owner suffers no real loss. The moment a genuine loss to the owner comes into view, the permission evaporates. The exceptions are the cases that override even tangible damage — the meis mitzvah, relieving oneself — and those are grounded in overriding concerns of dignity or the sacred.

### Fenugreek & Grasses

Regarding grasses, the *gemara* notes the exception of *tiltan*, fenugreek: grasses may be gathered everywhere *chutz mi-sdeh tiltan* — except from a fenugreek field. This prompts a question: are we saying that grasses actually benefit fenugreek? But a *baraisa* implies the opposite — if fenugreek grows up alongside other grasses, we do not require the owner to uproot either the fenugreek or the grasses on account of *kilayim*, the prohibition of mixed species, precisely because the mixture arose on its own without deliberate planting. The implication of that *baraisa* is that the grasses actually harm the fenugreek, not help it.

Rav Yirmiyah resolves the tension: *lo kashya, kan le-zera, kan le-zirin* — no difficulty. It depends on whether the fenugreek is being grown for its seeds or for its stalks. For the seed, the grasses are harmful — *kashu leh asavim de-machchesh lah* — they weaken the seeds. That is the case in the *baraisa*, where the mixture arose incidentally; since the grasses damage the fenugreek-for-seed, the owner does not want them growing together, and there is no *kilayim* concern. But for the stalk, *ma'alu leh* — the grasses are beneficial, because when the fenugreek grows up amid grasses, *mirkava*, it spreads across the surrounding vegetation and the stalk develops more robustly. In that case the grasses are wanted, and removing them would hurt the owner.

Alternatively, the *gemara* offers a second distinction: *kan le-adam, kan li-vhemah* — whether the fenugreek is grown for human consumption or for animal consumption. If it is grown for animals, then *de-zara asavim nami miba'ei leh* — the grower wants the grasses too, as additional feed. In that case, gathering the grasses hurts the owner and Yehoshua's *takkana* does not apply. Rav Papa adds a practical marker: if the fenugreek is planted in rows, it is grown for human consumption; if not in rows, it is for animals.

## Branch Cutting Rules

### Olive Tree Branches

Regarding the fifth item in the original list — *ve-kotmim neti'ah be-chol makom chutz mi-grufiyot shel zayit* — the public may break off a small twig anywhere, from any tree, except from the shoots of an olive tree. Rav Tanchum and Rav Birai, citing an elder, specify: for an olive tree the relevant threshold is *ke-beitzah* — egg-sized. The precise application of this measurement is itself debated, but one reading is that a twig of up to that size may be taken, though the surrounding prohibition on olive shoots makes this complicated.

### Other Tree Branches

For other trees — reeds and grapevines — one may cut from *min ha-paka u-le-ma'alah*, from the knuckle-like joint upward. For all other trees, one must cut from *ovyo shel ilan ve-lo me-chudo* — from the inner, less exposed part of the tree, not from the tip or the top. Rashi explains this as meaning one must take from the interior side.

Two further restrictions apply regardless of tree type. One may only cut a *chadash* — a new twig that is not yet bearing fruit — and not from a branch that is actively producing. And one must take from a location that is *eino ro'eh et ha-chamah* — not exposed to sunlight — since sun-exposed growth produces the best fruit and is exactly what the owner would wish to preserve. The *gemara* cites the verse *u-mi-meged tevu'ot shamesh* — from the sweetness of the produce of the sun — as the basis. The aggregate principle is plain: the twig may be taken only in a manner that causes the owner no meaningful loss.

## Water & Fishing

### Wellspring Rights

*U-ma'inah yotzei techilat benei ha'ir mistapkim mimenu* — if a new wellspring emerges naturally on someone's land, the townspeople have a right to draw from it. Rabba bar Rav Huna rules that payment must nonetheless be made to the owner. The *gemara* rejects this ruling: *v'leit hilchata ke-vateih* — we do not rule in accordance with his position. The owner retains some formal ownership, but the public's right of use is not conditioned on payment. The exact parameters of this right — how much water, for what purposes — are not spelled out, but the underlying principle reflects the ancient understanding of water as a communal necessity that private ownership cannot fully obstruct.

### Fishing Methods

The *gemara* elaborates on the fishing stipulation. Tosafot explains that there are three tiers of access. Cast hook fishing — ordinary line fishing from shore — is available to anyone, even on shores that front private land. The large box traps, *kela*, which would catch passing boats, are prohibited to everyone, even the landowner whose shore it is. Net fishing — *reshatot u-michmeret* — occupies the middle tier: permitted only to the landowner.

### Kinneret Ownership

A *baraisa* records that from the outset, the tribes made a mutual condition among themselves not to use the *kela* traps, but that net fishing was freely permitted.

A further *baraisa* provides the historical background: the Sea of Tiberias fell within the tribal portion of Naftali. Moreover, Naftali received an additional strip — *melo chevel cherem bi-droma* — a measured section of the southern shore, even though his primary territory lay to the north and east, in fulfillment of the verse *yam ve-darom yerashah* — he shall inherit the west (i.e., the western shore of the Kinneret) and the south.

On a related point, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches: detached objects found in the mountains — *telushin she-be-harim* — were held collectively in the presumptive ownership of all the tribes, while attached things — *mechubarim* — belonged to the specific tribe in whose territory they lay. Rashi understands this broadly: movable property seized in the conquest of the land was distributed equally among all Israel regardless of where it was found, while land and its attachments were divided by tribal portion.

The *gemara* adds that every tribe received a portion encompassing the full range of landscapes: *har*, *shefela*, *negev*, and *emek* — mountains, lowlands, dry terrain, and valleys. The verse in Devarim supports this: *ba-aravah ba-har u-va-shefela u-va-negev u-va-chof ha-yam* — in the plain, in the mountain, in the lowland, in the south, and at the shore of the sea. Not every tribe received coastal access literally, but the distribution was designed to give each tribe a variety of terrain. The *gemara* notes that this pattern preceded Israel as well, having characterized the holdings of the Canaanites, Perizzites, and Emorites before them, as the verse implies: *ve-el kol shecheinav* — and to all its neighbors.

## Relief & Passage

### Relieving Oneself

*Ve-niftin le-achorei ha-geder* — one may relieve oneself behind a fence — *va-afilu bi-sdeh she-mlei'ah karkom* — even in a field full of saffron. Rav Acha bar Yaakov observes that the core *chiddush* here is not the obvious permissibility of relieving oneself (whether obvious because of *kavod ha-briyot* or because the act arguably fertilizes the field), but rather that one may *take a stone from the stone wall* to use for wiping. That — removing a stone from another's fence — is the genuinely novel ruling.

### Kavod Habriyot on Shabbat

Rav Chisda extends this further: *va-afilu b'Shabbat* — even on Shabbat. The stone is *muktzeh*, yet *kavod ha-briyot* overrides this *de-rabbanan* restriction. Mar Zutra Chasida, being a *chasid*, would take the stone, use it, and then return it to the wall. He would then instruct his attendant — naturally, not on Shabbat itself — to plaster over that section, so that the used side would not remain exposed to others.

### Paths of Reshut

The stipulation *u-mehalchin bi-shvilei ha-reshut ad she-teired reviah shniyah* — one may walk through the private paths until the second rainfall — reflects the agricultural calendar. After the harvest the field lies fallow; passage causes no harm and is therefore permitted. Once the second rain falls after sowing, the seedlings are vulnerable and the right lapses.

### Hardened Mud Paths

Rav Papa qualifies this for Babylonian conditions: *va-hadidan afilu talya di-kashla* — in their fields, even a light dew suffices to make crossing damaging. The underlying principle is the same: passage is permitted only when it causes no harm. If the field is in any condition where crossing would injure it, the right does not apply. The stipulation about hardened mud — *yesados ha-derachim* — concerns the summer months when the path itself becomes treacherous: dried into sharp, uneven ridges, the center of the road is difficult and potentially injurious to walk on. In that situation, one may step off the road onto the private field margin.

## Walking on Field Edges

### Shmuel & Rav Yehuda

A narrative illustrates this. Shmuel and Rav Yehuda were walking along a road, and Shmuel stepped off to the side — onto private land — to avoid the hardened, treacherous mud of the path in the dry season. This is, of course, a stipulation Yehoshua made for the Land of Israel. But here we are outside of Eretz Yisrael, in Babylonia.

### Outside Eretz Yisrael Qu…

Rav Yehuda challenged Shmuel directly: *tenayim she-hitna Yehoshua afilu b'Bavel?* — are you claiming that the conditions Yehoshua stipulated apply even in Bavel? Yehoshua's conditions were manifestly limited to Eretz Yisrael. Shmuel replied: *sha'ani omer afilu b'chutz la-aretz* — I say they apply even outside the land. Whether Shmuel is claiming that Yehoshua himself intended universal application, or that any organized Jewish community operates with these implicit norms, is left somewhat open. It is worth noting that the *gemara* earlier applied the restrictions of the *mishna* regarding small animals to Babylonia as well, so there is a broader tendency to treat Babylonian Jewish society as operating by similar principles.

### Rebbi vs. Ben Kenusa

A parallel episode is related involving Rebbi — Rebbi Yehuda HaNasi — and Rebbi Chiya, who were walking together in Eretz Yisrael and similarly moved to the side of the road. Coming toward them, moving directly through the middle of the road, was Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, navigating the difficult terrain by hopping from one stable spot to the next rather than stepping onto private property.

Rebbi turned to Rebbi Chiya and asked: *mihu zeh she-mapgin gedulah b'faneinu?* — who is this person displaying his greatness before us? The implied affront was clear: here stand Rebbi, the foremost rabbinic and political leader of the generation, and Rebbi Chiya, alongside him — both walking on the side of the road — and this person ostentatiously refuses to do so, implicitly casting their practice into question.

Rebbi Chiya responded: perhaps it is Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, my student — *ve-chol ma'asav le-shem shamayim* — and all his actions are *le-shem shamayim*. He is not performing. He is simply that careful. When they drew close and saw who it was, they confirmed this was indeed the case. In Rashi's reading, Rebbi's initial remark was sufficiently sharp that had it been anyone else, it would have warranted *nidui* — excommunication. But given that it was Rebbi Yehuda ben Kenusa, and his intentions were transparently pure, no such measure was appropriate.

## Lost in Vineyard

### Self-Rescue in Vineyard

*Ha-to'eh bein ha-keramim mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — one who is lost among the vines may cut his way upward or downward through the vineyard until he reaches a town or a road. A *baraisa* extends this explicitly to two scenarios.

### Rescuing the Lost

The first scenario is a bystander who sees another person lost in the vineyard: *ha-ro'eh chaveiro to'eh bein ha-keramim mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — he too may cut a path in and out in order to extract the lost person and bring him back to a town or a road. The second scenario is the lost person himself: he may likewise cut his own way out.

One might ask why the *gemara* needs to tell us the second case separately — if even a bystander may cut through the vineyard to rescue someone, surely the person himself may do the same. The *gemara* anticipates this: one might have thought the difference matters. The bystander, coming from outside, knows where he is going — he can cut a relatively direct and limited path. The lost person, by contrast, has no idea of his orientation, and granting him the right to cut in any direction he imagines correct might lead to far greater destruction. Perhaps we should have required him to make his way to the edges of the vineyard and follow the perimeter out, even if that seems to him to be a far longer route. The *gemara* rules against this: *ka mashma lan* — the stipulation applies to the lost person as well, and he may cut in the direction he judges best.

### Deoraisa vs. Drabanan

The *gemara* then raises a pointed question: is this stipulation even necessary? Rescuing a person is a *de-oraisa* obligation. A *baraisa* teaches: *hashavat gufo minayin* — how do we know there is an obligation to return a person to himself, in the same manner as one is obligated to return lost property? From the verse *va-hashevoto* — you shall return him. So there is already a biblical obligation to rescue the lost. Why does Yehoshua's *takkana* add anything?

### Yehoshua's Tikkun

The *gemara* answers: *de-oraisa hu de-kai be-metzarei* — the biblical obligation is satisfied by having the rescuer go along the edges of the vineyard, without causing destruction to the vines. The *de-oraisa* obligation of *hashavah* does not, by itself, permit cutting through and damaging another's property. What Yehoshua came to establish — *atah tikken de-mafaseg ve-oleh mafaseg ve-yored* — is that even destructive passage through the vineyard is permitted in order to effect the rescue. That is Yehoshua's *takkana*: to authorize a form of rescue that would otherwise constitute prohibited damage to private property.
