# Yevamot 18 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zika & Mother

### Yibum & Inheritance

The previous discussion concluded with a remarkable line in the *gemara*: *yibum b'nachala tala rachmana* — the obligation of *yibum* is somehow directly connected to the concept of inheritance. That connection is worth keeping in mind as we proceed, because it will circle back in subsequent discussions. We now turn to a new topic — or rather, one that is not entirely new — which brings us back to the end of the *mishna* that dealt with the concepts of *zika* and *maamar*. Before that, however, we had revisited the case of *eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo*, derived from the word *yachdav*, which teaches us that *yibum* applies only when both brothers were alive at the same time, and that the patrilineal relationship alone — even without shared maternity — suffices to create the *yibum* bond.

### Zika Concept

The *gemara* opens with the statement of Rav Huna in the name of Rav. The concept at the center of this discussion is *zika* — a quasi-marital connection that the surviving brother inherits from his deceased brother with respect to the *yevama*. This is not a concept that appears explicitly anywhere in the *psukim*; the *psukim* simply legislate the *mitzvah* of *yibum*. Yet *Chazal* understand *zika* to be the underlying biblical mechanism — the metaphysical or legal reality that makes *yibum* possible. It is what allows the *yibum* act to be a continuation of the previous relationship rather than a new *issur* of *eshes achiv*. Because of this *zika*, the *yevama*'s status is transferred in some quasi-marital sense to the surviving brother, and this transfer then requires actualization through either *yibum* or *chalitza*. Importantly, *zika* is understood as a *d'oraisa* concept, not *derabbanan* — though, as we shall see, the question of exactly which implications flow from it at the *d'oraisa* level is precisely what is being debated.

### Shomeres Yavam Dies

Against this backdrop, Rav Huna states in the name of Rav: *shomeres yavam shemeisa muteres b'ima* — if a *shomeres yavam* dies, the *yavam* is permitted to marry her mother. The case is straightforward: Reuven is married to Rachel, Reuven dies, and Shimon now stands obligated in *yibum* to Rachel. Rachel, in her waiting status, is called a *shomeres yavam*. She then dies. The question is whether Shimon may now marry Rachel's mother.

One might think this is prohibited, since if Shimon had actually been married to Rachel and she died, he could not marry her mother — the prohibition of *isha v'ima* applies even to a deceased wife's mother. The question, then, is whether Rachel's status as Shimon's *zekuka* — as the woman to whom he has a *zika*, a quasi-marital connection inherited from Reuven — is sufficient to render her mother forbidden as the mother of his *zekukaso*, presumably at the *d'oraisa* level.

Rav's ruling is that this is permitted. The ruling is interpreted as reflecting the position of *ein zika*: either the concept of *zika* is denied altogether, or — more precisely — whatever *zika* exists is not substantial enough to carry the implication of rendering the woman's relatives forbidden as *erva*. There is also a distinct possible reading: even if one accepts *yesh zika* while the *shomeres yavam* is alive, one might argue that once she dies, the *mitzvah* of *yibum* no longer exists and the *zika* dissolves with it. After all, *zika* was tied to an obligation that needed to be actualized; once she is dead, there is no longer anything to actualize. However, as we will see, the *gemara* here is not limiting its statement in that way. It understands Rav's ruling as reflecting *ein zika* categorically — or at least, *ein zika* in any sense meaningful enough to create prohibited relationships.

### Ein vs. Yesh Zika

The *gemara* then asks: given that this ruling reflects *ein zika*, why not simply state the general principle — *halacha k'divrei ha'omer ein zika* — and be done with it? Why construct a specific case about marrying the mother?

The *gemara* answers with a two-step narrowing argument. First, had we only ruled *ein zika* in the context of the case involving two brothers — a case where Levi is engaged to Leah (Rachel's sister) but has not yet consummated the marriage, while Shimon also stands obligated to Rachel — one might have said: *ein zika* applies there because with two brothers, the *zika* is indeterminate. It is not clear that Rachel is specifically bound to Levi, since Shimon might be the one to perform *yibum* or *chalitza*. Perhaps with only one surviving brother, the *zika* is fully concentrated in him, and we would say *yesh zika* in that case. Therefore, Rav had to state a case of a single surviving *yavam* to demonstrate *ein zika* even there.

Second, had Rav simply ruled *ein zika* even in the case of one brother — without limiting his ruling to the scenario after her death — one might conclude that Shimon may marry Rachel's mother even while Rachel is still alive. So Rav deliberately chose the case of Rachel having died, teaching us: after her death, yes; while she is alive, no. But why not? If we hold *ein zika* even when she is alive, what prevents it? The answer is *mishum de'assur levatel mitzvos yevamin* — one may not nullify the *mitzvah* of *yibum*. In principle, there is no *issur* of *zika* even while Rachel is alive, but if Shimon were to marry Rachel's mother while Rachel still lives, he would have rendered himself unable to perform *yibum* with Rachel (since she would now be his *eshet ima*), and the *mitzvah* would simply vanish. The *gemara*, following Rashi, holds that if the *yevama* becomes an *erva* after the *yibum* obligation begins, she is treated like one who was an *erva* from the outset — neither *yibum* nor *chalitza* applies. Therefore, marrying the mother would entirely eliminate both options. This is considered *assur lechatchila* as a negation of the *mitzvah* of *yibum*, though *bediavad* the marriage would take effect, since there is no actual *issur* of *ein zekukaso* according to this position.

The precise rationale for the prohibition of *bitul mitzvat yevamin* remains somewhat difficult to define sharply. It is something like the principle that where a *mitzvah* stands to be performed, one should not actively cause it to disappear — though whether it rises to more than a *lechatchila* concern is unclear from the *gemara* here.

### Zika vs. Maamar

At this point it is worth clarifying the distinction between *zika* and *maamar*. *Maamar* is a rabbinic institution — an act of *kiddushin* performed by the *yavam* with the *yevama* prior to *yibum*, giving the relationship a character more closely resembling conventional marriage before the consummating act. It is purely *derabbanan*. *Zika*, by contrast, is *Chazal*'s understanding of the underlying biblical mechanism operative in *yibum* law: at the moment the husband dies, his surviving brothers inherit a quasi-marital connection to his wife, and this connection — this *zika* — is what must be resolved through either *yibum* or *chalitza*. The question of whether *zika* has significant legal implications — rendering the woman's relatives *erva*, or making her sister forbidden as *achot zekukaso* — is precisely the question the *gemara* is now exploring.

The *gemara* then presents a potential proof from a *mishna* that states: *yevamto shemeisa mutar b'achosah* — if the *yevama* dies, he may marry her sister. The *mishna* specifies only the sister, implying he may not marry the mother, which would suggest *yesh zika*. The *gemara* dismisses this: *hu hadin de'afilu b'imah* — in truth he could even marry the mother. The *mishna* only mentioned the sister in parallel with the opening clause, which addressed the case of one's actual wife dying — where only the sister is permitted and the mother remains forbidden *d'oraisa* regardless. The *seifa* follows that language pattern without implying the same restriction.

Against this, Rav Yehuda rules: *shomeres yavam shemeisa assur b'imah* — even after she dies, it is forbidden to marry her mother — reflecting the position of *yesh zika*. The *gemara* again asks why Rav Yehuda could not simply state *yesh zika* as a general principle with reference to the case of Levi and Leah. The answer mirrors the logic above: had he stated *yesh zika* only for that case (which involves two brothers), one might have thought *yesh zika* applies only with one brother. But had he said *yesh zika* even with one brother, one might have thought it applies only while the woman is alive — *mechayim* — but that once she dies, *paka la zika*, the *zika* dissolves. Therefore Rav Yehuda had to specify explicitly the case after death: *kamashma lan d'zika b'chadi lo paka* — *zika* does not simply disappear on its own. Even after the woman has died, the *zika* persists, and the mother remains forbidden.

## Two Brothers Case

### Setup: Sisters

We now turn to the scenario involving two brothers. Reuven is married to Rachel. Levi has performed *kiddushin* — but not yet *nisu'in* — with Leah, Rachel's sister. Reuven then dies. The question facing Levi is stark: may he proceed to consummate his marriage with Leah?

### Achot Zekukato

The problem is that Rachel, as *shomeres yavam*, now stands in a *zika* relationship with Levi. Leah, Levi's fiancée, is Leah's sister. If *zika* creates a quasi-marital bond, then Leah is the sister of Levi's *zekuka*, making her *achot zekukato* — analogous to *achot ishto*. Consummating the marriage with Leah while Rachel remains in a *zika* relationship with Levi would therefore be problematic.

The position discussed — attributed ultimately to *mishum Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira* — is that Levi must wait: *hamtein ad she-ya'aseh achicha ma'aseh* — wait until your brother Shimon performs *yibum* or *chalitza* with Rachel. Once Rachel is removed from the picture — either through *yibum* with Shimon or through *chalitza* — Levi is free to consummate his marriage with Leah.

Note that this case differs from the mother case in two respects, both of which the *gemara* will address. First, here Rachel is still alive, which gives the concept of *zika* more intuitive force — there is still a live obligation that must be actualized. Second, however, there is somewhat less reason to apply *zika* specifically to Levi in this case, since there are two surviving brothers, Shimon and Levi, and the *zika* is not concentrated in Levi alone. It is Shimon who bears the primary obligation: *mitzvah begadol leyabem*, the older brother has precedence. To the extent that the *mitzvah* lies primarily with Shimon, the *zika* binding Rachel to Levi specifically is more attenuated, which gives additional grounds for arguing *ein zika* in this case.

### Maamar Specificity

The *gemara* asks why the *mishna* in the case of *eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo* specifies the scenario where Shimon performed *maamar* with Rachel. If *yesh zika*, then even without *maamar*, Shimon's mere *zika* to Rachel should have been sufficient to render Sarah (*tzarat zekukaso*) problematic for Levi after Shimon's death.

The *gemara* responds through Rava: *hu hadin de'af al gav delo avad ba maamar, shniya mechlatz chaltza yibumi lo meyabma* — even without *maamar*, *zika* alone is sufficient to create the quasi-marital relationship, and therefore Levi may only perform *chalitza* with Sarah, not *yibum*. Why, then, did the *mishna* specify *maamar*? *Le'apokei mi-Beis Shammai de'amar maamar koneh kinyan gamur* — to exclude the position of Beis Shammai, who holds that *maamar* effects a complete acquisition equivalent to *yibum* itself. According to Beis Shammai, *maamar* with Rachel would make Sarah a full *tzarat erva* for Levi, precluding even *chalitza*. The *mishna* therefore specifies *maamar* to teach that even in that case, Levi must perform *chalitza* — contra Beis Shammai, who would say it is an outright *tzarat erva* requiring nothing.

### Zika Alone Sufficient

The critical conclusion is thus that *zika* alone — without any formal act of *maamar* — is sufficient to create a quasi-marital relationship with significant halachic implications. Even if Shimon did absolutely nothing after Reuven died, the *zika* he acquired to Rachel means that after Shimon's death, Rachel functions as a quasi-*erva* for Levi, rendering Sarah a *tzarat erva al yedei zika*. Levi may therefore perform only *chalitza* with Sarah, not *yibum*.

This conclusion emerges from the *gemara*'s analysis of the *mishna*. The *braisa* introduced by Abaye, which describes the exact scenario — Shimon had a *zika* to Rachel but died before performing *maamar*, and Levi was born only afterward — confirms this: regarding Rachel, Levi is exempt (*achot zekukaso shelo haya b'olamo*), while regarding Sarah, *o choletzes o misyabemes* — Levi may perform either *chalitza* or *yibum*. This seems to prove the contrary — that without *maamar*, Sarah is not a problem and Levi may even do *yibum* with her. The *gemara* acknowledges this directly: *ve-i amarta yesh zika, havya la tzoras eshes achiv shelo haya baolamo b'zika* — if *zika* were operative, Sarah should be the *tzara* of Shimon's quasi-wife, and Levi should be unable to perform *yibum*.

## Not Born Yet

### Stage Setup

The case of *eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo* now takes center stage in a more complex form. The setup is as follows: Reuven is married to Rachel; Shimon is alive and married to Sarah; Reuven dies with no children. At that moment, Rachel becomes a *shomeres yavam* — obligated in *yibum* or *chalitza* with Shimon. Subsequently, Levi is born. Since Levi was not yet alive when Reuven died, Rachel is *eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo* with respect to Levi — she carries no *mitzvat yibum* in relation to him. Shimon then dies.

### Zika Without Maamar

The question now is: what is Levi's status regarding Sarah, Shimon's wife? Regarding Rachel, it is clear — she is an *erva* to him as *eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo*. But what about Sarah? After Shimon's death, Sarah falls into *yibum* with Levi as the surviving brother. The complication is that Shimon had a *zika* to Rachel — a quasi-marital bond. If *yesh zika*, then Rachel functions quasi as Shimon's wife; Levi is then obligated in *yibum* to Rachel, but she is an *erva* to him. Sarah would then be *tzarat erva al yedei zika* — the co-wife of Shimon's quasi-wife — and Levi could not do *yibum* with her.

Crucially, in this scenario, Shimon performed no *maamar* with Rachel. The *zika* existed but was never concretized by any formal act. The question is whether *zika* alone — without *maamar* — suffices to render Sarah problematic for Levi.

The *gemara* initially assumes that based on our analysis (*zika* alone is sufficient), Levi should indeed be prohibited from *yibum* with Sarah, since Rachel's *zika* to Shimon casts her in a quasi-*erva* role. Yet the *mishna* in question specifies the scenario of *maamar*, implying that without *maamar* there is no such restriction. This tension is what drives the inquiry.

### Braisa: Levi Yibum OK

Abaye raises a challenge from a *braisa*: *shnei achim b'olam echad, u-meis echad mehem b'lo valad* — two brothers exist in the world, one dies without children. Shimon was about to perform *maamar* with Rachel but did not manage to do so before Levi was born, and then Shimon died. The *braisa* rules: *ha-rishona yotza mishum eshes achiv shelo haya b'olamo* — Rachel is exempt from *yibum* with Levi; *v'ha-shniya o choletzes o misyabemes* — regarding Sarah, Levi may perform either *chalitza* or *yibum*. Sarah is not rendered problematic.

This is a direct challenge: if *yesh zika* and *zika* alone suffices even after death, then Sarah should be *tzarat erva al yedei zika* and Levi should be unable to do *yibum* with her. Yet the *braisa* permits it. Evidently, in this case — where Shimon had only a *zika* and no *maamar* — Sarah is not a problem for Levi.

### Ein Zika After Death

The *gemara* concludes that this *braisa* reflects the position of *ein zika* — at minimum, *ein zika l'achar mita*, that *zika* does not persist after the death of the *yavam* who held it. The *braisa* is attributed to Rabbi Meir, who holds *ein zika*. However, this attribution is immediately complicated: the *gemara* will show that a *stam mishna* — presumed to reflect Rabbi Meir's view — appears to operate on the assumption of *yesh zika*. The *braisa*'s position, then, is not necessarily the final ruling.

## Four Brothers Case

### Four Brothers Setup

To test this further, the *gemara* turns to a *mishna* about four brothers. Reuven and Levi were married to Rachel and Leah, who are sisters. Both Reuven and Levi die. The two surviving brothers — let us call them Yissachar and Zevulun — now face obligations of *yibum* to both women.

### Achot Ishah Risk

The problem is immediate. Yissachar has a *zika* to both Rachel and Leah — each came from a different deceased brother, but both obligations now fall on the surviving brothers. Similarly for Zevulun. If Yissachar were to perform *yibum* with Rachel, he would thereby be having relations with *achot zekukaso* — Leah is his *zekuka*, and Rachel is her sister. Since *zika* creates a quasi-marital bond, consummating *yibum* with Rachel would be like having relations with the sister of his quasi-wife. The same logic applies in reverse if he were to go to Leah first. It seems as though *yibum* cannot be performed at all.

### Chalitza Required

The *mishna* rules: *harei elu chotzot v'lo mityabmot* — both women perform *chalitza* and may not undergo *yibum*. Why *chalitza* rather than each brother doing *yibum* with a different sister — Yissachar with Rachel and Zevulun with Leah? The *gemara* explains: *dilma ad-meyabbem chad u-met idach* — perhaps after Yissachar performs *yibum* with Rachel, Zevulun dies before performing *yibum* or *chalitza* with Leah. Now Yissachar has done *yibum* with Rachel, so Leah is *achot ishto* — forbidden to him as his wife's sister. No one can perform *yibum* or *chalitza* with Leah. She exits without any resolution. That is *bitul mitzvat yevamim* — the *mitzvah* obligation with respect to Leah is entirely negated. To prevent even the possibility of that outcome, both women must do *chalitza*. That way, if one *chalitza* has been performed, the surviving brother can still perform *chalitza* for the other woman, since *chalitza* does not create the *achot ishto* prohibition.

One might ask: is it not also a case of *bitul mitzvat yevamim* if someone dies in the middle of the *chalitza* proceedings? The answer is that if Yissachar performs *chalitza* with Rachel, the prohibition of *achot ishto* does not apply — he has merely released her, not married her — and he can still perform *chalitza* with Leah as well.

### Rabbi Meir Ein Zika

The *gemara* now confronts a tension: if Rabbi Meir holds *ein zika*, as we concluded from the *braisa* above, why does this *stam mishna* — presumed to be Rabbi Meir — operate on the assumption that *yesh zika*? If *ein zika*, why can't Yissachar simply do *yibum* with Rachel and Zevulun with Leah? They come from two different households (*mitrei batei ka'atyan*) — there is no *tzarat erva* issue. Each brother could do *yibum* with a different woman.

The *gemara* resolves this: *le'olam ein zika*. Rabbi Meir does indeed hold *ein zika*. The prohibition against doing *yibum* in this case is not because of *achot zekukato* but because of *bitul mitzvat yevamin* — the concern that if one brother does *yibum* and the other then dies, the second woman loses her *mitzvah* entirely. Rabbi Meir is so sensitive to this concern that even in a merely possible (not certain) scenario of *bitul mitzvat yevamim*, he requires *chalitza* over *yibum*.

## Rabban Gamliel

### Underage Wife Case

To support the proposed connection between the positions of *ein zika* and permissibility of *bitul mitzvat yevamin*, the *gemara* cites Rabban Gamliel from a *mishna*: Reuven is married to Rachel; Levi is married to Leah, who is underage and whose marriage has only *derabbanan* validity (having been arranged by her mother and brothers, such that *mi'un* — retroactive rejection — remains an option). Reuven dies. The question is what Levi should do.

### Gamliel's Ruling

Rabban Gamliel rules: *im miana miana* — if Leah performs *mi'un* and retroactively annuls the marriage, that is ideal; Levi is then free to perform *yibum* with Rachel. *Ve-im lav tamtin ad she-tagdil* — if she does not perform *mi'un*, wait until she grows up. Levi continues living with Leah; when Leah reaches adulthood, their relations constitute *kiddushin d'oraisa*, and at that point Rachel — as Leah's sister and Levi's *achot ishto* — is released from any *yibum* obligation without *chalitza*, as she has become an outright *erva* to him.

### Gamliel Proves Ein Zika

The proof that Rabban Gamliel holds *ein zika* lies in this ruling: during the period of waiting for Leah to grow up, Levi continues having relations with Leah despite the fact that Rachel is his *zekuka*. If *zika* were significant, Leah — as *achot zekukaso* — would be forbidden to him during this period. Yet Rabban Gamliel raises no objection. This demonstrates that Rabban Gamliel holds *ein zika*: the *zika* to Rachel carries no implications that would render Leah problematic.

### Gamliel's Two Positions

Moreover, Rabban Gamliel's ruling effectively ensures that the *mitzvah* of *yibum* with Rachel will disappear entirely. Once Leah grows up and *kiddushin d'oraisa* is in force, Rachel becomes *achot ishto* — an outright *erva* — and neither *yibum* nor *chalitza* applies. Rabban Gamliel is entirely untroubled by this outcome. He holds both *ein zika* and that *bitul mitzvat yevamin* is not a concern, even when the disappearance of the *mitzvah* is certain.

The *gemara* thus presents Rabban Gamliel as holding both positions together, in contrast to Rabbi Meir, who was said to hold *ein zika* but still to be concerned about *bitul mitzvat yevamin* even in cases of doubt. The *gemara* suggests that these two positions may be logically linked: if *ein zika*, perhaps there is no conceptual basis for worrying about *bitul mitzvat yevamin* either, since the whole idea of something that must be "actualized" or "transformed" — the underpinning of the *bitul* concern — derives from the concept of *zika* itself.

### Not Necessarily Connected

The *gemara* pushes back on this proposed connection: *Rabban Gamliel ad Rabbi Meir ka ramit?* — what justification is there for comparing Rabban Gamliel's two positions to Rabbi Meir's? Perhaps they are not logically connected after all. Perhaps Rabbi Meir holds *ein zika* and yet still holds *assur levatel mitzvat yevamin*. The *gemara* concedes that these positions need not be linked. What it offers instead is a characterological distinction: *Rabbi Meir chayish afilu l'sfeika* — Rabbi Meir is concerned about *bitul mitzvat yevamin* even in a merely doubtful case (e.g., the possibility that one brother might die mid-process in the four-brothers scenario). *Rabban Gamliel afilu l'vadai lo chayish* — Rabban Gamliel is unconcerned even where the *bitul* is certain (as in the underage-wife case, where Leah's eventual maturation will definitively eliminate the obligation toward Rachel). The difference between them, then, is one of degree in how much weight they assign to *bitul mitzvat yevamin*, not necessarily a matter of logical entailment from *ein zika*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Attribution Issue

The *gemara* now returns to the original ruling with which the discussion opened. Abaye says to Rav Yosef: *ha de-Rav Yehuda de-Shmuel hi* — the ruling that the *shomeres yavam* who died renders her mother forbidden (i.e., *yesh zika*) is properly attributed to Shmuel, not to Rav. The initial ruling permitting the marriage of the mother was stated as *Rav Huna amar Rav*, reflecting *ein zika*. The opposing ruling — forbidding it — was attributed to Rav Yehuda. Since Rav Yehuda was a student of both Rav and Shmuel, the attribution was ambiguous. If Rav Yehuda was transmitting in the name of Rav, we would have two conflicting traditions about Rav's position. Abaye clarifies: the ruling of Rav Yehuda that it is *assur* is actually Shmuel's position, not Rav's.

### Shmuel Consistent

This attribution is consistent with another known ruling of Shmuel: *amar Shmuel halacha ke-Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira* — Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira that we instruct Levi to wait before consummating his marriage with Leah until his brother Shimon resolves the *yibum* or *chalitza* with Rachel. Both positions — the one about the mother and the one about the sister — reflect the same underlying view: *yesh zika*, and the *zika* creates genuine restrictions on relationships with the *zekuka*'s relatives.

### Explicit Tradition

Rav Kahana reports that when he related this discussion before Rav Zvid of Nehardea, Rav Zvid responded that in their tradition there was an explicit formulation: *amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel, shomeres yavam shemeisa assur b'imah* — Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel that if the *shomeres yavam* died it is forbidden to marry her mother. There is thus no need to infer the attribution from Shmuel's other rulings; it was transmitted explicitly. *Alma kasavar yesh zika* — he holds *yesh zika*.

The *gemara* explains why both statements of Shmuel were necessary. Had Shmuel only ruled about the mother case, one might have thought that *yesh zika* applies only where there is a single *yavam*, since in that scenario the *zika* is fully concentrated in him. Perhaps in the case of two brothers (the Levi-Leah-Shimon case), the indeterminate nature of the *zika* would render it insufficient — *ein zika* in that case, and Levi could consummate his marriage with Leah without waiting for Shimon. Therefore Shmuel also had to rule like Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira, applying *yesh zika* even with two brothers. Conversely, had Shmuel only ruled about the two-brothers case, one might have concluded that *yesh zika* applies only while the *zekuka* is alive — *mechayim* — but that after her death *paka la zika*, the *zika* dissolves. Shmuel therefore also had to rule in the mother case, which arises specifically *after* her death, teaching us *kamashma lan d'zika b'chadi lo paka* — *zika* does not simply dissolve on its own.

### Final Conclusion

The *gemara*'s conclusion is thus broad and sweeping. *Yesh zika* — the *gemara* adopts this position comprehensively. *Zika* persists even after the *zekuka* has died. It applies even in cases where there are multiple brothers and the *zika* is not exclusively concentrated in one of them. And it carries genuine halachic implications — rendering the *zekuka*'s mother forbidden, and requiring Levi to wait before consummating his marriage with *achot zekukato* until the *zika* has been resolved through *yibum* or *chalitza*. This is the position of Shmuel, affirmed by explicit tradition, and it appears to be the *gemara*'s accepted ruling.
