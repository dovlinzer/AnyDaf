# Shabbat 146 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Breaking Barrel

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹבֵר אָדָם אֶת הֶחָבִית לֶאֱכוֹל הֵימֶנָּה גְּרוֹגְרוֹת וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יִתְכַּוֵּין לַעֲשׂוֹת כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A person may break a barrel** on Shabbat in order **to eat dried figs from it, provided he does not intend to make a vessel.**

Daf 146 opens a new *mishnah*, one that stands significantly apart from those preceding it. The prior *mishnayot* in this chapter dealt with related but distinct food-handling concerns: the first addressed a barrel that broke, the attendant concern of *s'chitah* (squeezing), and issues around squeezing foods more generally; the next treated reheating food and the *bishul achar bishul* question. Now we turn to a *mishnah* that is, in many ways, the central Talmudic source for one of the most practically pressing questions of Shabbat observance in contemporary life: opening packages.

### Mishnah Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין נוֹקְבִין מְגוּפָה שֶׁל חָבִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין, וְלֹא יִקְּבֶנָּה מִצִּדָּהּ. וְאִם הָיְתָה נְקוּבָה, לֹא יִתֵּן עָלֶיהָ שַׁעֲוָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מְמָרֵחַ. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי בַּעֲרָב, וְאָמַר: חוֹשְׁשַׁנִי לוֹ מֵחַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not perforate the plug of a barrel** to extract wine from it; rather, one must remove the plug entirely to avoid creating a new opening for the barrel. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis permit** puncturing the plug, but they too restrict this leniency and say that **one may not perforate** the plug of the barrel **on its side. And if it was** already **perforated, one may not apply wax to it** to seal the hole, **because** in doing so **he spreads** the wax evenly on the barrel and thereby violates the prohibited labor of smoothing. **Rabbi Yehuda said: An incident** of that kind **came before Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai in** the city of **Arav, and he said: I am concerned for him,** because he may be liable **to** bring **a sin-offering** as a result of this.

The *mishnah* reads: *shover adam es hachavis leachol heimena grogrot uval shelo yis'kavein la'asos kli* — a person may break open a sealed barrel in order to eat the pressed figs inside, provided he does not intend to make a vessel (*kli*) from it. The permission is thus twofold: one may break off the top of the barrel to access the food, but the act of breaking must not itself constitute the fashioning of a functional vessel. The *mishnah* thus generates its central interpretive tension immediately: what precisely distinguishes permissible destructive access from prohibited vessel-creation?

### Packaging Issues

The rough contemporary analogy is the opening of packaging — ripping into a bag, a carton, or a container to get to the food inside — provided one is not trying to make a vessel from what remains. The key question is at what point such an act crosses the line into *asiyat kli* (making a vessel). Consider the old-fashioned milk carton or a bag of potato chips: once opened, does the resulting opening transform the container into a vessel? Does it matter that in practice these are disposable? The *Gemara* itself does not address disposability directly, but it is a significant contemporary consideration. The fact that modern packaging is generally designed to be discarded, not repurposed, places it in a meaningfully different category than the barrel of the *mishnah*, which was itself a genuine vessel before it was sealed. The barrel's prior vessel-status is precisely what raises the question: is puncturing or breaking it simply accessing its contents, or reconfiguring it as a new kind of vessel? A sealed barrel contains its food; once broken open in the right way, it serves and distributes. The can that once held tuna fish is the same conceptual question in industrial form.

### Stopper Debate

The *mishnah* continues: *v'ein nokvin megufah shel chavis* — one may not make a hole in the stopper (*gufah*) of the barrel — *divrei Rabbi Yehuda*, these are the words of Rabbi Yehuda. *V'chachamim matirin* — the Sages, however, permit it. The visual that aids comprehension here is not a flat-topped barrel but something closer to a large jug with a substantial cork inserted into the top. Making a hole through that cork — rather than removing it — is what Rabbi Yehuda prohibits and the Sages allow. The conceptual distinction between removing a cork entirely and puncturing it to create a pour-hole is not immediately obvious, and the *Gemara* will spend considerable effort clarifying exactly what types of holes in what locations carry what degree of concern.

### Side Hole Issue

The *mishnah* then adds: *v'lo yikevena mitzidah* — one should not make a hole on the side of it. The *Gemara* will debate whether "the side" refers to the side of the cork or the side of the barrel itself. This restriction, it appears, reflects the position of the Sages — the same authorities who permitted a hole in the top of the cork — now prohibiting a hole in the side. The progression thus moves from total destruction (permitted), to a hole on top (debated between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages), to a hole on the side (prohibited even according to the Sages).

### Wax Smoothing

The *mishnah* addresses a further scenario: *v'im hayta nekuva* — if the barrel already had a hole — *lo yiten aleha sha'ava* — one may not place wax over it to seal it. Why? *Mipnei shehu memare'ach* — because doing so involves *memare'ach*, the act of smoothing. This is a *toldah* (derivative) of *memachek*, the primary *melacha* of scraping and smoothing, as in the preparation of leather. When one smooths wax over a hole, one is not merely depositing material but creating a functionally smooth, sealed surface — and that act of smoothing is itself a prohibited labor.

This raises broader questions about the application of *memare'ach* to contemporary substances. Thick ointments like Vaseline, Desitin, or Aquaphor, which require spreading across a surface, present a genuine concern. If something has the consistency of oil, there is no issue at all — applying oil-like substances to the body on Shabbat involves no *melacha*. But thicker creams that must be smoothed out raise the question of whether that smoothing constitutes *memare'ach*. The distinction that seems compelling is this: classic *memare'ach*, like the smoothing of wax over a hole, is done specifically so that the smooth surface serves a function — sealing, protection, evenness. Applying Vaseline or Desitin, by contrast, is about coverage, not about creating a smooth surface as such. Nevertheless, the practical ruling tends toward stringency with such substances. The additional concern of *p'sik reishah* when applied in a therapeutic context — itself raising questions of *choleh she'ein bo sakanah* — only adds further complexity, and those considerations are distinct from the *memare'ach* question proper.

### R. Yochanan Case

To underscore the gravity of the *memare'ach* prohibition, Rabbi Yehuda in the *mishnah* reports: *ma'aseh bo lifnei Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai b'Arev* — a case came before Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai in the town of Arev involving someone who had smoothed wax over the hole of a barrel. The response: *v'amar chosheshani lo mechatat* — I am concerned he may be liable for a *chatat* offering, meaning this is a potential biblical violation. Most of the *mishnayot* in the preceding chapters simply prohibit an action without specifying whether the prohibition is rabbinic or biblical; the *Gemara* then asks, and typically someone asserts it rises to biblical status. Here the *mishnah* itself, through Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai's statement, makes the biblical dimension explicit.

## Muktzeh Issue

### Pressed Figs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּרוּסוֹת, אֲבָל מְפוֹרָדוֹת — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Oshaya said: They only taught** that it is permitted to break open a barrel when the figs were **pressed** together. This is because in that case it is permissible to use a utensil to separate the figs, that utensil may also be utilized to break open the barrel. **However,** if the figs were already **separated,** it is **not** permitted to handle a utensil for the sole purpose of breaking the barrel.

Rav Hoshaya introduces a qualification that approaches the *mishnah*'s permission from an entirely unexpected angle. *Lo shanu ela drusot aval mefuradot lo* — the permission to break the barrel applies only when the figs inside are pressed together (*drusot*), but not when they are separated (*mefuradot*). This has nothing to do with the question of vessel-creation; it is a concern about *muktzeh*. The logic is as follows: in order to break the barrel, one needs to pick up a knife. On Shabbat, a *kli she'melachto l'issur* — an implement whose primary function is a prohibited labor — may ordinarily be handled for permitted purposes. However, there is a position that a vessel may only be used for its standard designated purpose. If the figs are pressed together and need to be cut apart, then picking up the knife serves a legitimate standard purpose (cutting the figs), and one may then use it also to break open the barrel. If the figs are already separate, there is no legitimate reason to pick up the knife in the first place, and one may not handle it merely to break the barrel. This is the position of Rabbi Nechemia, as the *Gemara* will clarify.

### Sword Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מֵבִיא אָדָם אֶת הֶחָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן וּמַתִּיז רֹאשָׁהּ בְּסַיִיף, וּמַנִּיחָהּ לִפְנֵי הָאוֹרְחִים בְּשַׁבָּת וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on a *baraita*: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: A person may bring a barrel of wine and cut off** the **top** of the barrel **with a sword and place it before the guests on Shabbat without concern** that it is prohibited to move the sword or that doing so constitutes the creation of a new vessel, which is prohibited.

The *Gemara* challenges Rav Hoshaya's position from a *braita*: *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer, mevi adam chavit shel yayin umatiz rosha besayif* — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says one may take a barrel of wine and use a sword to chop off its top, and then place it before guests on Shabbat. The dramatic gesture — drawing a sword and striking off the barrel's head — appears to have been a display of generosity, a performance for the guests. Significantly, the case involves wine, not pressed figs. Since wine does not need to be cut, there is no reason to pick up the sword for its standard cutting purpose, yet Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel permits it.

### R. Nechemia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהִיא רַבָּנַן, מַתְנִיתִין רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **That** *baraita*, which cites the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** whereas **our mishna is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya,** who said that it is prohibited to move any utensil on Shabbat for any purpose other than that for which the utensil is designated.

The *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *hahu rabbanan* — that case of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel reflects the position of the Rabbis, who do not restrict the use of a vessel to its standard purpose only. *Matnitin Rabbi Nechemia hi* — our *mishnah*, however, follows Rabbi Nechemia, who holds that a vessel may only be used for its designated purpose. Therefore the *mishnah* requires that the figs be pressed, so that the knife has a legitimate standard purpose before it may be used to break the barrel.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי דּוּחְקֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא לְאוֹקוֹמֵי מַתְנִיתִין כְּרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה וּבִדְרוּסוֹת, לוֹקְמַהּ בִּמְפוֹרָדוֹת וְרַבָּנַן! אָמַר רָבָא, מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ: מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״גְּרוֹגְרוֹת״? לִיתְנֵי ״פֵּירוֹת״! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בִּדְרוּסוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what forced Rabbi Oshaya to establish the mishna in accordance with** the minority opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya** and to say that it is referring only **to** the case of a **pressed** dried figs? **Let him establish** that the mishna is referring even **to separated** figs **and** is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. Rava said: The mishna** posed a **difficulty for him; why did** the *tanna* **teach particularly** about **dried figs? Let him teach** a more general *halakha* with regard to **fruit. Rather, learn from here that** the mishna is referring specifically to **pressed** dried figs, and it is because one requires a utensil to separate them that he may use it to open the barrel as well.

This prompts a further question: *u'mai duchkeia de'Rabbi Oshia le'ukmi matnitin ke'Rabbi Nechemia u'be'drusot* — what forced Rav Hoshaya to interpret the *mishnah* as following Rabbi Nechemia and as speaking specifically of pressed figs? Why not explain the *mishnah* as speaking of any fruit, in accordance with the majority rabbinic opinion? Rava answers: *matnitin kashya lei* — Rav Hoshaya was troubled by the *mishnah* itself. Specifically, *mai irya detani grogrot* — why does the *mishnah* choose the particular example of pressed figs? Wine is far more common a case and appears in the *braita*; or alternatively the *mishnah* could have spoken generically of fruit. The very specificity of "pressed figs" signals that this detail carries halachic weight — pressed figs are figs that have been pressed together, which is precisely the condition that gives the knife a legitimate use and makes Rav Hoshaya's reading compelling.

### Fig Baskets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא חֲדָא: חוֹתָלוֹת שֶׁל גְּרוֹגְרוֹת וְשֶׁל תְּמָרִים — מַתִּיר וּמַפְקִיעַ וְחוֹתֵךְ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: מַתִּיר אֲבָל לֹא מַפְקִיעַ וְלֹא חוֹתֵךְ! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — רַבָּנַן, הָא — רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ תַּרְווֹד וַאֲפִילּוּ טַלִּית וַאֲפִילּוּ סַכִּין — אֵין נִיטָּלִין אֶלָּא לְצוֹרֶךְ תַּשְׁמִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in one** *baraita*: If one has sealed, wicker **baskets of dried figs or of dates, one may untie** the basket's knot on Shabbat, and **unbraid** the basket **and cut** it open. **And it was taught in another** *baraita*: **One may untie** the knot, **but one may not unbraid or cut** the basket. There is a contradiction between these two *baraitot*. The Gemara resolves this contradiction: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita*, which permits all of these actions, is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis. That** *baraita*, which prohibits unbraiding and cutting, is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥemya. As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Neḥemya says: Even a large spoon and even a cloak and even a knife may only be taken** on Shabbat **for their** designated **use,** and it is therefore prohibited to take a knife to cut open baskets of fruit.

The *Gemara* turns to a related set of *braitot* concerning the wicker baskets (*chotalot*) used to wrap pressed figs and dates to facilitate their ripening — a rough ancient analogue to placing produce in a paper bag. The first *braita* states: *chotalot shel grogrot veshel tamarim — matir u'mafki'a ve'chotech* — one may undo the knot tying on the cover, tear into the basket itself, or cut the string. Rashi in a parallel *Gemara* clarifies that the knot in question is a *kesher she'eino shel kayama*, a temporary knot made to be undone. A second *braita*, however, contradicts: *matir aval lo mafkia velo choteich* — one may undo the string, but may not tear the basket or cut the string. Rather than explaining the apparent contradiction through differences in the nature of the *melacha* involved, the *Gemara* simply says *lo kashya — ha rabbanan ha Rabbi Nechemia*: the more permissive *braita* follows the Rabbis, and the more restrictive one follows Rabbi Nechemia, for whom the knife may only be taken up for its standard use. *Detanya Rabbi Nechemia omer, afilu tarvai va'afilu talif va'afilu sakin — ein nitlin ela letzoirech tashmishan* — even a shovel, a garment, or a knife may only be handled for the purpose for which they are normally used. We do not rule like Rabbi Nechemia.

## Hole Making

### Puncturing Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעוֹ מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מַהוּ לְמִיבְרַז חָבִיתָא בְּבוּרְטְיָא בְּשַׁבְּתָא?
>
> **Translation:** The students **raised a dilemma before Rav Sheshet: What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether or not it is permitted **to perforate a barrel with a spear [*burtiya*] on Shabbat?**

Having addressed the *muktzeh* tangent, the *Gemara* returns to the central concern of the *mishnah*: the making of openings. The question posed is *mahu limvraz chavisa bevurtya beShabbasa* — what is the rule regarding puncturing a barrel with a *burtya* on Shabbat? The *burtya* is understood as something like a spear or awl — a pointed implement that creates a hole in the side of the barrel, making it possible to draw wine out through that hole. This seems paradigmatically like fashioning a functional opening, an act of vessel-creation.

### Rashi View

Rashi's interpretation of the case is distinctive. He understands the *burtya* not as the standard instrument used for making such holes, but rather as something dramatic and non-standard — like a large spear brought down off the wall, rather than the proper awl one would ordinarily use for the purpose. In Rashi's reading, the very choice of an unusual implement signals something about the actor's intent: if one truly meant to create a standard functional spout, one would use the standard tool. The use of an oversized or improvised implement suggests instead a performative gesture — the same spirit as chopping off the barrel's top with a sword to show one's generosity.

### Intent Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִיתְחָא קָמִיכַּוֵּין, וַאֲסִיר, אוֹ דִילְמָא: לְעַיִן יָפָה קָמִיכַּוֵּין, וּשְׁרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Is the assumption that one intends to** make **an opening** in the barrel **and** it is therefore **prohibited, or perhaps** is the assumption that **one** merely **intends to** display **generosity and it is permitted?**

This framing generates the *Gemara*'s two-pronged question: *lipitcha ka mechavein ve'assur* — is the person's intention to create a functional opening (*pitcha*), in which case it is forbidden — *o le'ayn yafa ka mechavein veshari* — or is the intention merely for aesthetic display (*l'ein yafa*), in which case it would be permitted? The phrase *l'ein yafa* — literally, "for a pleasing appearance" — captures the idea of a grand, generous gesture that gives access to the contents without constituting the deliberate crafting of a functional vessel-opening.

### Rav Sheshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: לְפִיתְחָא קָא מְכַוֵּין, וַאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet **said to them: He intends to** make **an opening** in the barrel **and it is prohibited.**

Rav Sheshet rules definitively: *lipitcha ka mechavein ve'assur* — the intention is to make an opening, and it is therefore forbidden. His use of the word *assur* without further specification invites the question of whether this is a biblical or rabbinic prohibition; Rashi's framing tends toward treating it as a *d'oraita* concern, but other positions hold that all of these hole-making questions are rabbinic in nature.

### Gamliel Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: מֵבִיא אָדָם חָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן וּמַתִּיז רֹאשָׁהּ בְּסַיִיף? הָתָם — וַדַּאי לְעַיִן יָפָה קָמִיכַּוֵּין, הָכָא — אִם אִיתָא דִּלְעַיִן יָפָה קָמִיכַּוֵּין — לִפְתְּחֵיהּ מִיפְתָּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on that which was taught in the *baraita* that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One may bring a barrel of wine** on Shabbat **and cut off its top with a sword.** This contradicts Rav Sheshet's opinion that opening a barrel with a spear is prohibited? He answered them: **There,** in the case of the sword, since one essentially destroys the barrel by cutting off its top, **he certainly intends to** display **generosity** by breaking the barrel open in his guests' honor. However, **here,** in the case of spearing a hole in the barrel, **if it were** true that **he intends** to display **generosity, let him open** the top of the barrel by removing its plug. By perforating the barrel, he indicates that he specifically wants there to be a small hole.

A challenge is raised from Rabban Gamliel's case of cutting off the barrel's top with a sword. The *Gemara* resolves: there, *vaday le'ayn yafa ka mechavein* — it is certain that the intent is aesthetic and performative, not the creation of a functional spout. But when one makes a hole specifically in the side of the barrel — even with an unusual implement — the logic of *l'ein yafa* does not hold. *Liphthachay miphtach* — if one wanted to simply show generosity and give access, one would open the top. Making a side hole, however non-standardly, indicates a purposeful intent to create a functional pour-spout, and it is therefore prohibited.

## Top/Side Debate

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין נוֹקְבִין מְגוּפָה וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת לְמַעְלָה, אֲבָל מִן הַצַּד — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אָסוּר. וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי: ״לֹא יִקְּבֶנָּה מִצִּדָּהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna: **And one may not perforate the plug** of a barrel; this is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda, and the Rabbis permit it. **Rav Huna said:** This **dispute** is only with regard to a case where one seeks to make a perforation **on top** of the plug; **however,** if he seeks to perforate it **from the side, everyone agrees that it is prohibited,** because people sometimes puncture a barrel beneath the plug in this way. **And that is** what the mishna **is teaching: One may not perforate it on its side.**

The *mishnah*'s reference to "the side" now requires disambiguation. Rav Huna explains: *machlokes lemala* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages concerns the top of the cork. Making a hole straight down through the top of the cork — the equivalent of a corkscrew hole — is non-standard enough that the Sages permit it. *Aval min hatzad — divrei hakol assur* — but a hole on the side of the cork is forbidden according to everyone, because that is a more standard and functional type of opening.

### Rav Chisda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: מַחֲלוֹקֶת מִן הַצַּד, אֲבָל עַל גַּבָּהּ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מוּתָּר. וְהָא דְּקָתָנֵי ״לֹא יִקְּבֶנָּה מִצִּדָּהּ״ — הָתָם בְּגוּפָהּ דְּחָבִית.
>
> **Translation:** Whereas **Rav Ḥisda said:** This **dispute** is with regard to a case where one seeks to perforate it **from the side; however,** if one seeks to perforate it **on top, everyone agrees that it is permitted, and** with regard to **that which** the mishna **is teaching: One may not perforate it on its side, there** it is referring to perforating **the barrel itself,** not the plug.

Rav Chisda refines the picture: *machlokes min hatzad* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and the Sages is actually about the side of the cork, not the top. On the top of the cork, *divrei hakol mutar* — everyone agrees it is permitted, because that is clearly not a standard functional opening. The debate is reserved for the side of the cork, where Rabbi Yehuda prohibits and the Sages permit.

### Sidebar

Given Rav Chisda's reading, the *mishnah*'s phrase *v'lo yikevena mitzidah* — "do not make a hole on its side" — must be referring to something else. *Hatam begufa dechavit* — that prohibition concerns the side of the barrel itself, not the cork. Making a hole in the body of the barrel is the most serious case, because that directly reconfigures the barrel as a distribution vessel in a standard functional way.

### Functionality

The underlying conceptual distinction emerges more clearly once we understand these cases as involving a barrel rather than a jug. A hole in the top of the cork is non-standard and almost certainly not a genuine *asiyat kli*. A hole in the side of the cork may or may not reach the threshold of vessel-creation, hence the dispute. A hole in the side of the barrel itself — creating what would amount to a spigot-point — most clearly transforms a storage vessel into a serving vessel, and that transformation is what the prohibition targets. The barrel before the hole is a vessel for containing wine; after the hole it becomes a vessel for distributing wine. The transition from containment to distribution is the halachically significant act.

## New/Old Holes

### Braita Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין נוֹקְבִין נֶקֶב חָדָשׁ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאִם בָּא לְהוֹסִיף — מוֹסִיף. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מוֹסִיפִין. וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁנּוֹקְבִין נֶקֶב יָשָׁן לְכַתְּחִילָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not create a new hole** in a vessel **on Shabbat. And if one seeks to add** to and widen an already existing hole, **one may add** to it; **and some say** that **one may not** even **add** to an already existing hole. **And** all opinions, even those who generally prohibit creating new holes, agree **that one may perforate** the seal over **an old hole,** even ***ab initio*.

The *Gemara* cites a *braita*: *ein nofkin nekiv chadash beShabbat* — one may not make a new hole on Shabbat — *ve'im ba lehosif, mosif* — but if one wishes to enlarge an existing hole, one may do so. *Veyesh omrim ein mosif* — and some say one may not even enlarge an existing hole. *Veshavin shenokvin nekiv yashan l'chatchila* — and all agree that one may repuncture an old hole that had been sealed, so that removing the seal and reopening what was already there is universally permitted.

### Expand Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא: מַאי שְׁנָא מִנֶּקֶב חָדָשׁ דְּלָא — דְּקָא מְתַקֵּן פִּיתְחָא, אוֹסוֹפֵי נָמֵי — קָא מְתַקֵּן פִּיתְחָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** the Gemara asks: **What is different** about perforating the seal over an old hole that makes it permitted, whereas **creating a new hole is not** permitted? Is it because in creating the new hole **he is creating an opening?** If so, by **adding** to an already existing hole **he is also creating an opening.**

The *Gemara* presses: the *Tanna Kamma* prohibits making a new hole because *de'ka mataken pitcha* — one is fashioning an opening. But if one adds to an existing hole, *hosefa nami ka mataken pitcha* — one is also fashioning an opening, since the enlarged hole is itself new in its enlarged form. Why, then, does the *Tanna Kamma* permit expansion?

What is striking here is that the *Gemara*'s mode of analysis has shifted. Rather than examining whether an act transforms a vessel's functionality — the macro-level question of vessel-creation — the *Gemara* now zeroes in on the act of hole-making itself as the operative *melacha*. Making holes is a specific form of *boneh* (building): one builds a wall and makes a hole to insert a window, or bores a hole to receive a peg. The act of creating the hole, not merely its effect on the vessel's functional status, is what the *Gemara* treats as the problem.

### Rava Layers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה — כׇּל פֶּתַח שֶׁאֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי לְהַכְנִיס וּלְהוֹצִיא — אֵינוֹ פֶּתַח, וְרַבָּנַן הוּא דִּגְזוּר מִשּׁוּם לוּל שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלִין, דְּעָבֵיד לְעַיּוֹלֵי אַוֵּירָא וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי הַבְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said:** Actually, even creating a new hole is not prohibited, because **by Torah law, any opening that is not made to** both **insert and to remove is not** considered **an opening,** and a hole that one perforates in a barrel is intended exclusively to remove the contents of the barrel. **And it was the Sages who issued a decree** that one may not perforate a vessel **because** it is similar to perforating **a chicken coop,** which is designated for use in both directions, e.g., **to let in air and to let out heat,** and it is therefore prohibited by Torah law.

Rava introduces a crucial distinction. *Devar Torah* — biblically — *kol pitcha delo avid le'achnosi u'le'apoki lav pitcha hu* — any opening that is not made for two-directional flow (things going in *and* out) does not constitute a halachic *pitcha* and is not a biblically prohibited act of *boneh*. *Ve'Rabbanan hu de'gazru mishum lul shel tarnegolim* — it is the Rabbis who decreed against even one-directional holes, by reason of the chicken coop.

### Chicken Coop

The chicken coop (*lul shel tarnegolim*) is the paradigmatic case of a two-directional hole. Air is brought in for the chickens — *da'avid le'ulei le'avira* — and foul air is expelled — *u'le'apoki hevla*. Such a hole is genuinely two-directional and therefore biblically prohibited to create. But making this case might not be intuitively obvious to everyone. The concern is that if the Rabbis permit one-directional holes, people will mistakenly think they may make the chicken-coop hole as well. Therefore, the Rabbis prohibited even one-directional holes as a *gezeira* against the two-directional case. The analysis has moved entirely away from the question of vessel-functionality and into a narrowly defined taxonomy of hole-types.

### Weasel Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם בָּא לְהוֹסִיף — מוֹסִיף, אוֹסוֹפֵי וַדַּאי בְּלוּל שֶׁל תַּרְנְגוֹלִים — לָא אָתֵי לְאוֹסוֹפֵי מִשּׁוּם רִיחְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** therefore we learned that **if one seeks to add** to an existing hole **one may add** to it. There is no reason to prohibit this due to concern that one may do so in a chicken coop, because **one will certainly not come to add to** an already existing hole **in a chicken coop,** **because** one does not want **vermin** to enter the coop.

Yet this *gezeira* has a built-in limit. *Osufi vadai be'lul shel tarnegolim lo atei le'osufi* — with regard to enlarging a hole, no one would ever enlarge the hole in a chicken coop. Why? *Mishum richsha* — because of weasels. Enlarging the chicken-coop hole would allow weasels and other predators to enter, and no one would do that. Therefore the *gezeira* against enlarging holes does not extend to the chicken-coop context, and since the *gezeira* was enacted specifically because of chicken coops, enlarging holes generally falls outside its scope. This is a remarkably fine-grained piece of reasoning: the rabbinic prohibition against one-directional holes was enacted on account of a specific case, and whether the *gezeira* extends to enlargement turns on whether enlargement would be a concern *in that specific case*.

### Expansion Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים אֵין מוֹסִיפִין — זִימְנִין דְּלָא תַּקְּנֵיהּ מֵעִיקָּרָא, וְאָתֵי לְאַרְווֹחֵי בֵּיהּ. דָּרֵשׁ רַב נַחְמָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲלָכָה כְּיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** when we learned that **some say** that **one may not add** to a preexisting hole, that ruling is also due to a decree lest one come to do so in a chicken coop, as **sometimes one does not properly** create the opening **initially, and comes to expand it** so that it will be fit for use. In that case, widening the opening is considered its completion. **Rav Naḥman taught in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the statement cited as: **Some say.**

*Veyesh omrim ein mosifin* — some, however, hold that one may not even enlarge an existing hole. Their reasoning: *zimnin delo taknah mei'ikara* — sometimes the original hole in a chicken coop was not made properly to begin with, and *ve'atei le'arvuchei bah* — one might come to enlarge it, making the *gezeira* against enlargement relevant after all.

*Rav Nachman mishmeih d'Yochanan amar halacha ke'yesh omrim* — the ruling follows the more restrictive view: one may not enlarge even an existing hole.

## Wax Sealing

### Wax Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשָׁוִין, שֶׁנּוֹקְבִין נֶקֶב יָשָׁן לְכַתְּחִילָּה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּמְקוֹם הֶעָשׂוּי לְשַׁמֵּר, אֲבָל לְחַזֵּק — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the *baraita*: **And** everyone **agrees that one may perforate an old hole** that was sealed ***ab initio*. Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: They only taught** this **in a place where** the hole **is made to strain** the wine from the sediment; **however,** if it was made **to reinforce** the barrel, it is **prohibited** (*ge'onim*).

Returning to the universally permitted case — repuncturing a sealed hole — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel qualifies: *lo shanu ela b'makom asui l'shamer* — this applies only when the wax was placed there to *protect* (seal against air, dust, contamination), not when it was placed there to *strengthen* (*l'chazek*) the vessel. If the wax has become structurally integrated into the vessel itself, repuncturing it is equivalent to making a new hole.

### Wine Level Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְשַׁמֵּר, הֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְחַזֵּק? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לְמַעְלָה מִן הַיַּיִן — זֶהוּ לְשַׁמֵּר, לְמַטָּה מִן הַיַּיִן — זֶהוּ לְחַזֵּק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** in which a hole is considered to have been made **to strain** and **what are the circumstances** in which a hole is meant **to reinforce** the barrel? **Rav Ḥisda said:** If one perforates the barrel **above** the level of **the wine, that is** a hole designated **to strain** the wine, and if one perforates the barrel **below** the level of **the wine, that is** a hole designated **to reinforce** the barrel.

Rav Chisda proposes a practical criterion: *l'ma'ala min hayayin zehu l'shamer* — wax placed above the wine line is there only to protect, since there is no structural pressure at that point. *L'mata min hayayin zehu l'chazek* — wax placed below the wine line is there to strengthen, because it is preventing the wine from seeping out through a compromised section of the vessel, and thus functions as part of the vessel's structure.

### Rava Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: לְמַטָּה מִן הַיַּיִן נָמֵי זֶהוּ לְשַׁמֵּר. וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי לְחַזֵּק — כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּיקְּבָה לְמַטָּה מִן הַשְּׁמָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** A hole made **below** the level of **the wine is also** designated **to strain** the wine, **and what are the circumstances** in which a hole is meant **to reinforce** the barrel? It is in a case **where one perforated it below the sediment.**

Rava disagrees: *l'mata min hayayin nami zehu l'shamer* — even wax below the wine line is merely protective. Sealing a crack on the side through which wine would leak is not the same as providing structural support; it is still only a seal.

### Real Strengthening

What, then, is the true case of *l'chazek*? *K'gon she'nikva l'mata min hashamarim* — when the crack or hole is at the very bottom of the vessel, beneath the sediment. There, the weight of the wine pressing down on the crack creates genuine structural risk: without the wax, the vessel might fracture entirely. In that case, the wax is not merely a seal but a structural element, and repuncturing it constitutes making a new hole.

### House Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרָבָא, תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ: בַּיִת סָתוּם — יֵשׁ לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו — אֵין לוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. בַּיִת סָתוּם — אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו, פָּרַץ אֶת פַּצִּימָיו — מְטַמֵּא כׇּל סְבִיבָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rava:** A *baraita* **was taught that supports your** opinion that any opening retains its status unless it is absolutely clear that it is no longer used for that purpose. When dividing a courtyard between the owners of the houses in it, in addition to an equal share of the courtyard, each receives the four cubits adjoining each entrance leading from his house to the courtyard. **A house** with a **sealed** entrance still **has** the **four cubits** adjoining that entrance, because the entrance can be reopened. It is only if **one broke its doorposts** and sealed the entrance that the entrance has been completely negated and **it does not have** the **four cubits** adjoining it. This applies similarly to the *halakhot* of ritual impurity: A house in which there is a corpse transmits ritual impurity only through its doorways. **A house** with **sealed** entrances **does not render all of its surroundings ritually impure;** the ritual impurity only extends opposite the entrances. However, if **one broke its doorposts,** it is no longer considered an entrance, and **it renders all of its surroundings ritually impure.**

Abaye cites a *braita* in support of Rava's general principle — that the threshold for something losing its functional halachic status requires a substantial structural change. *Bayit satum yesh lo arba amot* — if one seals off a doorway of a house, the sealed doorway still counts as a *petach* (opening) for the purpose of entitling its owner to four *amot* of walking space before it. *Paratz et ptzimav* — only when the doorposts and lintel are actually demolished does it lose that status. Similarly in *tum'ah*: a sealed house does not render all those who touch its exterior walls *tamei*, but once the doorframe is physically destroyed it does. The principle is that sealing something does not readily strip it of its functional legal character; only structural destruction accomplishes that. This supports Rava's position that wax filling a hole retains its character as a stopper rather than a new vessel-component unless something far more radical has occurred.

## Spigot Issue

### Gubsa Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּבְתָּא, רַב אָסַר וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. מְחַתֵּךְ לְכַתְּחִלָּה — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר. אַהְדּוֹרֵי — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי. כִּי פְּלִיגִי דַּחֲתִיכָה וְלָא מְתַקְּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to inserting a **reed** through a hole in a barrel in order to draw wine through it, **Rav prohibited** doing so on Shabbat, **and Shmuel permitted** doing so. The Gemara elaborates: With regard to **cutting** and inserting the reed ***ab initio*, everyone agrees** that **it is prohibited,** because this is considered fashioning a utensil. With regard to **restoring** a prepared reed to its place, **everyone agrees that it is permitted. When they disagree is** in a case where the reed **has been cut but not** yet **inserted** into the barrel.

The *Gemara* turns to the question of the *gubsa* — a pipe or tube inserted into a hole in the barrel to serve as a functioning spigot. Rav prohibits inserting it; Shmuel permits it.

### Cutting Pipe

On one point there is universal agreement: *mechatech l'chatchila — d'chuli alma lo pligi d'assur* — cutting the pipe to the right shape on Shabbat is forbidden by all. This involves both the possible *melacha* of *mechatech* (cutting to a specific size) and the fashioning of a new vessel-component.

### Reinstalling

On a second point there is also universal agreement: *ahdorei — d'chuli alma lo pligi d'shari* — if a pipe that had previously been installed falls out, one may reinsert it. Since the pipe's function in that hole has already been established, putting it back changes nothing.

### First Insert

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי פְּלִיגִי דַּחֲתִיכָה וְלָא מְתַקְּנָא: מַאן דְּאָסַר גָּזְרִינַן דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְמִיחְתַּךְ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי לָא גָּזְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **The one who prohibits** doing so, Rav, holds that **we issue a decree** that prohibits inserting the reed due to the concern that **perhaps one will come to cut** the reed ***ab initio*** for this purpose; **and the one who permits** doing so, Shmuel, holds that **we do not issue a decree** in that situation.

The dispute concerns an intermediate case: *b'chaticha v'lo misakna* — the pipe has already been cut to the appropriate shape, but has never yet been inserted into a hole. Is inserting it for the first time on Shabbat prohibited?

### Rav Concern

The one who prohibits — Rav — does not claim that inserting a pre-cut pipe constitutes making a vessel. The pipe slides in and can be removed; it does not physically alter the barrel. Rather: *gazrinan dilma ati l'michtach l'chatchila* — we issue a *gezeira* lest one come to cut the pipe to shape on Shabbat itself. Permitting insertion of a pre-cut pipe might lead, in the mind of the less-careful, to cutting the pipe fresh.

### Shmuel Allow

Shmuel, who permits, holds *lo gazrinan* — we do not extend a *gezeira* here, since people understand the difference between handling an already-prepared pipe and fashioning one from scratch.

### Tanai View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: אֵין חוֹתְכִין שְׁפוֹפֶרֶת בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּשַׁבָּת. נָפְלָה — (אֵין) מַחֲזִירִין אוֹתָהּ בְּשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר בְּיוֹם טוֹב. וְרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה מֵיקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that Rav and Shmuel's dispute is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*: One may not cut a** reed **tube on a Festival, and needless to say,** it is prohibited **on Shabbat.** And if it was already cut but **fell, one may restore it** to its place **on Shabbat, and needless to say,** it is permitted to do so **on a Festival. And Rabbi Yoshiya is lenient** in this matter.

The *Gemara* notes this parallels a *tannaitic* dispute: *ein chotchin shififerta b'Yom Tov v'ein tzarich lomar b'Shabbos* — one may not cut a tube/pipe on *Yom Tov* and certainly not on Shabbat. *Nafla — machzirin oto b'Shabbos v'ein tzarich lomar b'Yom Tov* — if it falls, one may reinsert it on Shabbat and certainly on *Yom Tov*.

### Rabbi Yoshi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַרֵישָׁא — הָא קָמְתַקֵּן מָנָא? אֶלָּא אַסֵּיפָא — תַּנָּא קַמָּא נָמֵי מִישְׁרֵא קָשָׁרֵי? אֶלָּא דַּחֲתִיכָה וְלָא מְתַקְּנָא אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the statement of **Rabbi Yoshiya: On which** section of the *baraita* is he commenting? **If you say** that he is referring to **the first clause** of the *baraita*, which discusses cutting a tube, **isn't he creating a vessel,** which is a prohibited labor? How could Rabbi Yoshiya permit doing so? **Rather,** say that he is referring **to the latter clause** of the *baraita*, which discusses restoring a fallen tube to its place; **the first *tanna* also permits** doing so, leaving no room for further leniency by Rabbi Yoshiya. **Rather,** it is with regard to a case in which **it was cut but not inserted** into the barrel that **there is** a practical difference **between them.**

Rabbi Yoshi is described as taking a lenient position — *meikel*. The *Gemara* asks: to which of the three cases does his leniency apply? It cannot be the first (cutting the pipe), since everyone agrees that is forbidden — *ha kamisaken mana*, one is clearly fashioning a vessel. It cannot be the third (reinserting a fallen pipe), since everyone permits that as well. Therefore Rabbi Yoshi's leniency must apply to the intermediate case of inserting a pre-cut pipe for the first time — where one *Tanna* decrees against it and Rabbi Yoshi does not.

### Sheshet Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרַשׁ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, taught** publicly **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoshiya.**

*Darash Rav Sheshet brei d'Rav Idi mishmei d'Rav Yochanan halacha k'Rabbi Yoshi* — the ruling follows Rabbi Yoshi's lenient position, and one may insert a pre-cut pipe into a barrel hole on Shabbat.

## Myrtle Leaf

### Leaf Spigot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם הָיְתָה נְקוּבָה וְכוּ׳. מִישְׁחָא — רַב אָסַר, וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. מַאן דְּאָסַר — גָּזְרִינַן מִשּׁוּם שַׁעֲוָה. וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי — לָא גָּזְרִינַן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר בַּר חָנָה לְרַב יוֹסֵף: בְּפֵירוּשׁ אֲמַרְתְּ לַן מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב — מִישְׁחָא שְׁרֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **And** we also learned in the mishna: **And if** the barrel **was** already **perforated,** one may not seal the hole with wax. There is a dispute among the *amora'im* whether or not it is permitted to seal that hole with **oil: Rav prohibited** doing so, **and Shmuel permitted** doing so. The Gemara explains: **The one who prohibits** doing so, Rav, holds that **we issue a decree** prohibiting oil **due to** concern lest one come to seal the hole with **wax. And the one who permits** doing so, Shmuel, holds that **we do not issue a decree** prohibiting oil due to concern lest one come to use wax to seal the hole. **Rav Shmuel bar bar Ḥana said to Rav Yosef:** How is Rav cited as prohibiting the sealing of the hole in a barrel with oil on Shabbat? **You told us explicitly in Rav's name: Oil is permitted** for use in sealing a hole.

A related case is now introduced. The wife of Rav Yosef reports in the name of Shmuel: *tarpa d'assa d'assur* — using a myrtle leaf folded into the shape of a pipe as a spigot to draw wine from the barrel is forbidden. The practice would involve taking a myrtle leaf, folding it over on itself to form a channel, and inserting it into the barrel's hole to direct the flow of wine.

### Gutter Fear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ טָבוּת רִישְׁבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי טִרְפָא דְאַסָּא — אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? רַב יֵימַר מִדִּפְתִּי אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה מִשּׁוּם מַרְזֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a statement that **Tavut the hunter said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to **this myrtle leaf, it is prohibited** to insert it into a hole in a barrel to be used as a spout. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? **Rav Yeimar of Difti said:** It is **a decree** issued **due to** the concern lest one come to make **an** actual **spout** on Shabbat.

Rav Yeimar of Difti explains: *gezeira mishum marzev* — a rabbinic decree on account of gutters or elaborate piping systems. If one is permitted to fold a leaf and use it as a makeshift conduit, one may come to construct more elaborate drainage or pipe systems on Shabbat. The *gezeira* is quite expansive in scope.

### Tosafot Query

Tosafot, citing the Ri, raises the obvious difficulty: we just ruled that inserting a pre-cut pipe into the barrel is permitted. If a rigid pre-formed pipe is allowed, why is a folded myrtle leaf — a far simpler improvisation — worse? *V'kashya d'begufsa nami nigzar* — if the concern is about pipe-systems, it should apply equally to inserting the pipe.

### Tosafot Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִקְטוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** It is **a decree** issued **lest one cut** a myrtle leaf from its branch on Shabbat for this purpose.

Tosafot's resolution is elegant: *d'ki shakil alya d'hadas u'machpelo k'ein marzev* — when one takes a myrtle leaf and *folds* it into the shape of a pipe, one is performing an act that creates its functionality anew. The leaf was not already a pipe; by folding it, one is in effect manufacturing the pipe-shape on Shabbat itself, which is *nira k'oseh marzev* — it appears as if one is constructing a gutter or conduit. *Aval begufsa — she'eino oseh bo shum maaseh — lo gazrinan* — inserting a pre-cut pipe, by contrast, involves no act of fashioning whatsoever; one simply places an already-functional object into position, and there is no concern.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative reason for prohibiting the folded myrtle leaf: *gezeira shema yiktom* — lest one come to cut the leaf from a branch. Rashi notes that this concern applies even to a branch that is no longer growing, because the act of cutting a leaf specifically for this purpose resembles a kind of *tikkun* (fixing or completing an object for use).

### Detached Leaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דִּקְטִים וּמַנַּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** in a case in which the leaves **were** already **cut and placed,** as in that case, Rav Ashi's concern would not apply, but Rav Yeimar would still rule stringently.

The practical difference between Rav Yeimar's reason and Rav Ashi's reason emerges in the following case: a myrtle leaf that is already detached — not cut from a branch on Shabbat, but simply found lying there. According to Rav Yeimar's reason (the *gezeira* against gutters), even a detached leaf may not be folded for use as a spigot. According to Rav Ashi's reason (the concern about cutting from the branch), if the leaf is already detached, there is no concern, and folding it would be permitted.

## Blanket/Food

### Blanket Carry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּי סַדְיָא, רַב אָסַר וּשְׁמוּאֵל שָׁרֵי. בְּרַכִּין — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דִּשְׁרֵי. בְּקָשִׁין — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דַּאֲסִיר. כִּי פְּלִיגִי — בְּמִיצְעֵי: מַאן דְּאָסַר — מִיחְזֵי כְּמַשּׂוֹי, וּמַאן דְּשָׁרֵי לָא מִיחְזֵי כְּמַשּׂוֹי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another dispute between Rav and Shmuel: With regard to **felt cloths** upon which people typically sit but that may also be worn as cloaks, the Sages disagreed whether or not one may wrap himself in them on Shabbat and transfer them from one place to another through a public domain. **Rav prohibited** doing so, **and Shmuel permitted** doing so. The Gemara elaborates: **With regard to** articles made of **soft** felt, **everyone agrees** that **it is permitted,** as their legal status is that of other garments. **With regard to** articles made of **hard** felt, **everyone agrees** that **it is prohibited. When they disagree,** it is **with regard to** felt articles that have an **intermediate** level of softness. **The one who prohibits** wearing them, Rav, holds that one who wears these as a garment **appears as** one carrying **a burden, and the one who permits** wearing them, Shmuel, holds that **it does not appear as** one carrying **a burden,** because the material appears to be a garment.

A series of independent debates between Rav and Shmuel is introduced. The first concerns a type of folded blanket or mat that one wishes to transport through a public domain. Since carrying is prohibited, one might consider wearing the blanket as a garment — draped over one's shoulders — and walking with it to its destination. Does wearing it in this manner constitute legitimate clothing-wearing, or is it obviously a ruse to transport a burden?

### Soft/Stiff

The *Gemara* qualifies the dispute: *b'rachin — d'kulei alma lo pligi d'shari* — if the blanket is soft, everyone agrees it is permitted, because wearing a soft textile is a normal enough act that it genuinely resembles wearing clothing. *U'vekashin — d'kulei alma lo pligi d'assur* — if the blanket is stiff and board-like, everyone agrees it is forbidden, because no one would normally wear such a thing as clothing. The dispute between Rav and Shmuel is limited to the intermediate case of a medium-grade blanket: *man d'assur mechzi k'masoi* — the one who prohibits holds that it looks like a burden regardless of how it is worn; *u'man d'shari lo mechzi k'masoi* — the one who permits holds that wearing it looks sufficiently like normal clothing.

### Rav Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּרַב, לָאו בְּפֵירוּשׁ אִיתְּמַר אֶלָּא מִכְּלָלָא אִיתְּמַר. דְּרַב אִיקְּלַע לְהָהוּא אַתְרָא דְּלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ רַוְוחָא, נְפַק יְתֵיב בְּכַרְמְלִית. אַיְיתוֹ לֵיהּ בֵּי סַדְיָא, לָא יְתֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out that **this** opinion attributed **to Rav was not stated explicitly** by him; **rather, it was stated by inference,** i.e., based on Rav's conduct, it was concluded that this is his opinion. **As Rav happened** to come **to a certain place** where **there was not** enough **room** for him and his students inside the house, and therefore, **he went** out **and sat in a *karmelit*. They brought him felt** cloths by wearing them outside, and **he did not sit** on them.

The *Gemara* notes that Rav's position was not stated explicitly but was inferred from an incident. Rav visited a place where there was insufficient seating. He went out and sat in a *karmelit*. The people brought him a blanket — wearing it out to him — and he refused to sit on it. The observer assumed Rav was demonstrating that wearing blankets out is prohibited.

### Respect Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דַּחֲזָא סָבַר מִשּׁוּם דְּבֵי סַדְיָא אֲסִיר, וְלָא הִיא, דְּרַב אַכְרוֹזֵי מַכְרִיז: בֵּי סַדְיָא שְׁרֵי, וּמִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד רַבּוֹתֵינוּ לֹא יָשַׁב עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **One who saw** this incident **assumed** that he refused to sit on them **because it is prohibited** to move **cloths** in this manner; **however, that is not so,** as **Rav** used to **proclaim** that **it is permitted** to move articles made of **felt** in this manner. **And it was in deference to our Rabbis** who were there that **he did not sit on it,** as he did not want to sit on a higher and more distinguished surface than they did.

But the *Gemara* clarifies: *v'lohi* — that was not the reason. Rav had in fact publicly announced that wearing such a blanket out is permissible: *d'Rav achruzei machriz bisaya sharei*. His refusal was for an entirely different reason. *Umishum kvod raboseinu lo yashav alav* — out of respect for his colleagues, Rav Kahana and Rav Assi, who were present and for whom no blankets had been brought. He would not accept a comfort denied to his peers.

## Food Preservation (II)

### Pit Cooling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נוֹתְנִין תַּבְשִׁיל לְתוֹךְ הַבּוֹר בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיְּהֵא שָׁמוּר, וְאֶת הַמַּיִם הַיָּפִים בָּרָעִים, בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּצָּנוּ. וְאֶת הַצּוֹנֵן בַּחַמָּה בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיֵּחַמּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may place a** cooked **dish into an** empty **pit** on Shabbat **so that it will be protected** from the heat, **and** similarly, one may place **good** potable **water** into a vessel and place the vessel **into bad,** non-potable water **so that** the potable water **will cool off. And** one may also place **cold water** out **in the sun so that** it **will be heated.**

The *mishnah* continues with a cluster of food-preservation cases. *Nosnin tavshil l'soch habor bishvil sheyehei shamur* — one may lower a cooked stew into an empty pit to keep it cool — a rudimentary form of refrigeration. The pit's cool air preserves the food.

### Submersion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ כֵּלָיו בַּדֶּרֶךְ בַּמַּיִם — מְהַלֵּךְ בָּהֶן וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ. הִגִּיעַ לֶחָצֵר הַחִיצוֹנָה — שׁוֹטְחָן בַּחַמָּה, אֲבָל לֹא כְּנֶגֶד הָעָם.
>
> **Translation:** They also taught: **Someone whose garments fell into water** while walking **on the road may** replace them and continue to **walk** wearing **them and need not be concerned** about violating the prohibitions against wringing or laundering. When he **reaches the outer courtyard** of a place where he can leave his clothes, **he spreads them in the sun** to dry, **but not opposite the masses,** as they will suspect him of laundering on Shabbat.

*V'es hamayim hayafim b'ra'im* — one may also submerge a vessel of good water within bad (polluted) water *bishvil sheyitznanu* — in order to keep the good water cool. And *v'es hatzonin b'chama* — one may place cold items in the sun *bishvil sheyeichamu* — to warm them. The concern about heating does not apply to solar warming, since there is no prohibition of *bishul chama* — cooking by sunlight.

## Food Preservation (II)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: נִיגְזַר מִשּׁוּם אַשְׁווֹיֵי גוּמּוֹת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֶת הַמַּיִם הַיָּפִים בָּרָעִים. פְּשִׁיטָא! סֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. וְאֶת הַצּוֹנֵן בַּחַמָּה, הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְתֵימָא: נִיגְזוֹר דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְאַטְמוֹנֵי בְּרֶמֶץ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara is puzzled by the *halakha* that was taught about placing food in a pit: It is **obvious** that it is permitted. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: Let us issue a decree** prohibiting this **due to** concern lest one perform the prohibited labor of **leveling holes** in the pit when he places the pot in it, **it teaches us** that no decree was issued due to this concern. We also learned in the mishna: One may place **good water into bad water** on Shabbat in order to cool it off. The Gemara says: It is **obvious** that it is permitted. The Gemara explains: It is **necessary** to mention **the latter clause** of the mishna, which states that it is permitted to place **cold water** out **in the sun.** The Gemara expresses surprise: **This, too, is obvious.** The Gemara answers: **Lest you say that we should issue a decree** prohibiting this action, because **perhaps one will come to insulate it in hot ashes** to warm it up, which also heats the water without actually placing it on the fire, **it teaches us** that we do issue a decree in that case.

The *Gemara* addresses the permissibility of these acts in sequence. Lowering food into a pit seems obvious — *pshita*. The *Gemara* explains that the ruling was needed lest one think a *gezeira* applies: *nigzar mishum ashvuyei gumos* — perhaps permitting this will lead one to smooth out the bottom of the pit (a prohibited labor). The ruling clarifies that no such concern applies. Submerging good water in bad water is likewise obvious on its face; the *Gemara* notes that the novelty lies in the final case. Placing cold items in the sun: this too might have seemed problematic, lest *nigzar dilma asi litmoni b'remetz* — one might come to bury food in ashes for warming, which is prohibited because of concern that one will stir the ashes (*hatmanah* concerns). The ruling establishes that no such *gezeira* is enacted here.

## Wet Clothes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנָּשְׁרוּ כֵּלָיו בַּדֶּרֶךְ בַּמַּיִם — מְהַלֵּךְ בָּהֶן וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ. הִגִּיעַ לֶחָצֵר הַחִיצוֹנָה — שׁוֹטְחָן בַּחַמָּה, אֲבָל לֹא כְּנֶגֶד הָעָם.
>
> **Translation:** They also taught: **Someone whose garments fell into water** while walking **on the road may** replace them and continue to **walk** wearing **them and need not be concerned** about violating the prohibitions against wringing or laundering. When he **reaches the outer courtyard** of a place where he can leave his clothes, **he spreads them in the sun** to dry, **but not opposite the masses,** as they will suspect him of laundering on Shabbat.

### Walking Wet

The *mishnah* shifts from food to wet clothing. *Mi shenishru kelav baderech b'mayim* — if one's clothes became soaked while traveling, perhaps by crossing a river or being caught in rain — *mehalach bahen v'eino choshesh* — one may continue walking in them without concern. The potential concern is incidental squeezing (*s'chitah*), but this is not treated as a prohibition in this context.

### Drying Rules

*Higia l'chatzer hachitzona* — when one arrives at one's outer courtyard, *shotchan bachama* — one may spread the clothes in the sun to dry, *aval lo kineged ha'am* — but not in a place visible to passersby. The reason for this restriction is *maris ayin*: observers who see wet clothes hung out to dry will assume one laundered them on Shabbat.

### Inner Rooms

The *Gemara* will address the obvious follow-up question — if the concern is *maris ayin*, does spreading clothes in an inner room fully resolve it? That discussion will continue on the following *amud*. The *mishnah*'s formulation — *sheta b'chadrei chadarim*, spread them in inner rooms — anticipates this resolution, but the *Gemara*'s analysis of why inner rooms sufficiently address the *maris ayin* concern is left for the next day's learning.

## Package Summary

### Framework

The daf's analytical arc moves from a broad foundational question — when does access to food constitute prohibited vessel-creation? — through increasingly granular sub-categories. The opening *mishnah* establishes that destructive access to food is permitted when vessel-creation is not the intent. The *Gemara* then examines various configurations: holes in corks, holes in barrel-sides, the difference between tops and sides, and the distinction between making new holes and enlarging existing ones.

### Permitted

The daf permits a substantial range of acts: completely destroying the packaging or barrel to access food; cutting off the top of a barrel (even dramatically with a sword), which is understood as an act of display rather than functional vessel-creation; making a hole in the top of the cork, which does not constitute a standard functional opening; and reinserting a pre-cut pipe that had fallen from an existing hole.

### Prohibited

Prohibited are: making a hole in the side of the barrel in a manner that creates a standard functional pour-spout; smoothing wax over any hole (*memare'ach*); making any new hole, whether or not it rises to the biblical threshold of *boneh* — at minimum it is rabbinically prohibited; and folding a myrtle leaf to create a pipe-spigot, since the act of creating its functionality is itself the problem.

### Analytical Shift

Perhaps the most intellectually striking feature of the daf is the mid-discussion pivot in mode of analysis. The first part of the *sugya* examines the macro question of whether an act transforms a vessel's functional character — turning a storage barrel into a serving vessel. The latter part — particularly Rava's analysis — zooms in entirely on the micro-act of hole-making, classifying holes by directionality (two-directional holes are biblical; one-directional holes are rabbinic by *gezeira*) and ruling on expansion of existing holes through a chain of reasoning about chicken coops and weasels. These two frameworks — vessel-functionality and hole-taxonomy — are not always easy to reconcile, and the *Gemara* does not fully integrate them.

### Modern Cases

The daf's practical implications for contemporary Shabbat observance are considerable and genuinely unresolved at their edges. Disposable packaging — designed to be discarded after a single use — is significantly more lenient than the barrel of the *mishnah*, which was itself a genuine vessel before sealing. Many authorities hold that disposable packaging creates no vessel-creation concern at all. Resealable containers — designed after opening to be closed again and reused — present a far more serious problem, since the opening explicitly confers new functionality. The question of cans (tuna fish, soda) turns in large part on whether the sealed top is more analogous to protective wax (*l'shamer*) or to structural integration (*l'chazek*), and whether the can was a vessel before the top was applied. Given that all of these hole-making concerns may be rabbinic rather than biblical — a position that the *Gemara* by the latter part of the *amud bet* seems to lean toward — there is a basis for leniency in cases of genuine uncertainty. Nevertheless, practice varies considerably, and the *sugya* of 146 makes clear why: the underlying categories are subtle, the analogies to modern materials imperfect, and the two analytical frameworks of the daf not always pointing in the same direction.
