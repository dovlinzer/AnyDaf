# Yevamot 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Eshet Ach & Achot Isha

### Braita Setup

The Gemara on 32a, approximately six lines below where the daf begins, introduces a *braita* that shifts the discussion into entirely new terrain — away from the specific case of two brothers and two sisters, and into the broader question of *issur chal al issur*, whether one prohibition can take effect on top of another. The *braita* presents the following scenario: a man's brother married his wife's sister. The woman is thus, to this man, simultaneously *eshet ach* (his brother's wife) and *achot isha* (his wife's sister) — two independent prohibitions apply to the very same person. He then has relations with her. What is his liability?

### R. Yose vs. R. Shimon

The *braita* records a dispute between Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yose takes the straightforward position: he has transgressed two prohibitions, *eshet ach* and *achot isha*, and is liable for both. Rabbi Shimon disagrees, holding that he is liable for only one. Before even analyzing the reasoning behind this dispute, however, the Gemara immediately confronts a contradiction: a second version of Rabbi Shimon's ruling states the exact opposite — that he is liable only for *achot isha*, not for *eshet ach*. So Rabbi Shimon consistently holds that only one prohibition applies, but the two versions disagree about which one.

### Order Matters

The Gemara resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya*. The two versions reflect two different factual sequences. In one case, the brother who ultimately dies married her first — making her *eshet ach* before she became *achot isha*. In the other case, the surviving brother married first, making her *achot isha* before she became *eshet ach*. Rabbi Shimon's position is that you follow whichever status was established first. This is the key principle: the first prohibition defines her status, and the second cannot displace it.

To make this concrete: Reuven married Rachel, and Shimon married Leah. Reuven then dies. From Shimon's perspective, Rachel is both *eshet ach* and *achot isha*. If Reuven married Rachel before Shimon married Leah, then Rachel's first status relative to Shimon was *eshet ach*; the subsequent marriage to Leah that created the *achot isha* status came later and, according to Rabbi Shimon, cannot take effect. Conversely, if Shimon married Leah first, Rachel was already *achot isha* before Reuven ever married her, and the subsequent *eshet ach* status cannot displace the earlier one.

### Ein Issur Chal

The operative principle behind Rabbi Shimon's position is *ein issur chal al issur*: one prohibition does not take effect on top of a prohibition that is already in force. The second status, whatever it may be, cannot replace or supplement the first. Rabbi Shimon applies this categorically — whichever status came first is the one that counts, and only its attendant consequences apply.

It is worth pausing to note what is practically at stake. Both prohibitions carry *karet*. The difference is not about going to hell once versus twice, as it were, but rather about whether one who acted *beshogeg* is obligated to bring one *korban* or two, and potentially about other related consequences. These are real *halakhic* distinctions that make the debate substantive.

### Two Scenarios

This also raises an important side point regarding *yibum*. According to Rabbi Shimon, if Reuven died without children, one might think Shimon could perform *yibum* with Rachel — after all, in the scenario where Rachel's first status was *eshet ach*, the *achot isha* prohibition never took effect. But Rav Ashi explains that the *issur* of *achot isha* is, as it were, hovering above, latent but present. Were the *issur eshet ach* to fall away — precisely what *yibum* would accomplish — the *issur achot isha* would immediately descend and take hold. Because performing *yibum* would only usher in the next prohibition, the first prohibition does not dissolve, and Rachel remains forbidden. This is a striking mechanism: the very act that would remove one prohibition would instantaneously activate another, so the system locks in place.

## R. Yose's Position

### Both Prohibitions

The Gemara now turns to Rabbi Yose, who says that both prohibitions apply simultaneously. Does Rabbi Yose truly hold that *issur chal al issur* — that one prohibition can take effect on top of another? This seems to be a striking claim, and the Gemara immediately raises a contradiction from Rabbi Yose's own rulings elsewhere.

### Two Deaths Braita

A *braita* teaches: if one commits a sin that carries two different death penalties, the court imposes only the more severe one — *nidon b'chamura*. Rabbi Yose disagrees: *nidon b'zika harishona haba'ah alav*, one is judged according to the first relationship that defined his liability. The term *zika* — connection or relationship — is significant here. Rabbi Yose says that the first relationship is what defines the punishment.

### Mother-in-Law Case

The *braita* then illustrates Rabbi Yose's view with a concrete case. A mother-in-law is subject to the penalty of *sreifa* (burning), while an *eshet ish* (married woman) is punishable by *chenek* (strangulation), the less severe death. Now consider a woman who is both a man's mother-in-law and a married woman — a natural scenario, since she is presumably married to his father-in-law.

### Zika Rishona

The *tanna kamma* would say he is punished by *sreifa*, the more severe penalty. Rabbi Yose says no — go by the first relationship. If she was his mother-in-law first and subsequently became a married woman (for instance, she was widowed or divorced and then remarried), then the first *zika* is mother-in-law, and he is punished by *sreifa*. But if she was a married woman first — that is, she was already married before he ever married her daughter — then the first *zika* is *eshet ish*, and he is punished only by *chenek*, not by *sreifa*.

This is the critical insight. The only way Rabbi Yose can arrive at the lighter penalty of *chenek* is if he genuinely holds that the mother-in-law status, though technically applicable, does not create a separate legal reality. He is not saying merely that we pick the lighter punishment while acknowledging both — he is saying the later status never took effect. This is structurally identical to *ein issur chal al issur*.

### R. Yose's Stance

But then we have the contradiction. The same Rabbi Yose who says in the mother-in-law case that you go by the first relationship — which implies *ein issur chal al issur* — says in the *eshet ach*/*achot isha* case that you are liable for both. Does Rabbi Yose hold *issur chal al issur* or not?

## Mosif vs. Kolel

### Issur Mosif Defined

The Gemara's answer, articulated by Rabbi Abahu, is: *modeh Rabbi Yosi be-issur mosif* — Rabbi Yose concedes that a second prohibition *can* take effect when it constitutes an *issur mosif*, a prohibition that broadens the scope of persons to whom the forbidden object or person is prohibited.

The concept requires careful exposition. The general rule is *ein issur chal al issur*: a second prohibition cannot descend onto an object already carrying a prohibition. But there is an exception: if the second prohibition extends the reach of that object's forbidden status to a wider class of people, it *can* take effect even on top of the existing one.

Consider the *eshet ach*/*achot isha* scenario. If Shimon married Leah first, Rachel became *achot isha* to Shimon. At that point, she was forbidden only to Shimon. Then Reuven married Rachel. By becoming *eshet ach*, Rachel's forbidden status now extends to all of Reuven's brothers — not just to Shimon, but to any other brothers as well. The scope of her prohibition has expanded: she was forbidden to one person, and now she is forbidden to a broader class of people. This is an *issur mosif*, and because it genuinely adds something — it reaches people who were not previously affected — it can take effect even on top of the existing *achot isha* prohibition. So in this sequence, Shimon is liable for both.

Why does it matter to Shimon that she is now forbidden to other brothers? The conceptual logic is that the new status has real operative content regarding other parties; since it takes effect vis-à-vis those others, it takes effect as a status altogether, including with respect to Shimon, even though she was already forbidden to him personally. The *issur mosif* creates an objectively new prohibition, not merely redundant with the one already in place.

If, on the other hand, Reuven married Rachel first, she was *eshet ach* from the outset, forbidden to all the brothers. Then Shimon married Leah. Now Rachel is *achot isha* — but the *achot isha* prohibition adds no new persons to whom Rachel is forbidden. She was already forbidden to all the brothers as *eshet ach*; the *achot isha* prohibition merely covers Shimon again, who was already covered. This is not an *issur mosif*, and thus the second prohibition cannot take effect.

The Gemara confirms: in the sequence where the surviving brother married first (wife's sister established first) and then the deceased brother married her (brother's wife established second), *migo de-itosif issur legabei achim* — since the new status broadens her prohibition to encompass the other brothers, *to-isif issur legabei dideih* — it also reinforces the prohibition with respect to Shimon himself. Both apply. In the reverse sequence, no such broadening occurs, and only the first prohibition counts.

### Issur Kolel Defined

Now the Gemara turns to a second category: *issur kolel*. This is a subtler and more contested concept. Whereas *issur mosif* means the forbidden object itself becomes prohibited to a wider class of people, *issur kolel* means the forbidden person finds themselves included within a larger population of people who are all now subject to the same prohibition — without the scope of the object's prohibition having changed at all.

The distinction can be illustrated as follows. With *issur mosif*: Rachel begins as *achot isha* — forbidden to Shimon. She then becomes *eshet ach* — now forbidden to all the brothers. Rachel herself has become prohibited to more people. Her own status has expanded outward. That is *issur mosif*.

With *issur kolel*: Rachel begins as *eshet ach* — forbidden to all the brothers. Shimon then marries Leah. Shimon is now forbidden not just to Rachel but to all of Leah's sisters. Rachel, as one of Leah's sisters, now finds herself among a larger class of women who are all forbidden to Shimon. But Rachel's own status has not changed — she was forbidden to all the brothers before, and she remains forbidden to all the brothers after. What changed is not Rachel's reach but Shimon's prohibitions: he is now subject to a broader class of women he cannot marry, and Rachel happens to be one of them. She becomes part of a *kolel*, a broader collective category, without her own status having expanded.

To crystallize the difference: *issur mosif* — the forbidden object is now prohibited to more people. *Issur kolel* — the person subject to the prohibition is now prohibited regarding a broader class of objects. The Gemara articulates this explicitly: *hai issur kolel hu* — this is an *issur kolel*, not an *issur mosif*. The object's scope has not changed; the subject's prohibited field has widened, sweeping the original object in.

### Why Mosif Works

Why does *issur mosif* allow a second prohibition to take effect but *issur kolel* does not? The Gemara's logic is that for the second prohibition to take effect on something already prohibited, it must bring something genuinely new that attaches to the object itself. With *issur mosif*, the object — Rachel — genuinely acquires a new dimension: she is now forbidden to people who were not previously forbidden from her. The new prohibition is a real, operative status change in the object. With *issur kolel*, the object itself has not changed; Rachel is still forbidden to exactly the same people as before. The change is in the subject's broader landscape of prohibitions, and that is not enough to generate a second, independent *issur* on the object.

The Gemara therefore concludes that Rabbi Yose holds of *issur mosif* but not *issur kolel*. This resolves the apparent contradiction: in the *eshet ach*/*achot isha* case, if the sequence creates an *issur mosif* — wife's sister first, then brother's wife — Rabbi Yose says both apply. In the mother-in-law and *eshet ish* case, when *eshet ish* comes first, mother-in-law is a narrower scope prohibition that cannot take effect on top of the broader one, so only the first applies.

The Gemara then raises a remaining difficulty: what does Rabbi Yose mean when he says both apply in the *eshet ach*/*achot isha* case in the opposite sequence, where no *issur mosif* exists? Rava answers: *ma'aleh ani alav ke'ilu asah shta'im bein chayav ela achas* — we consider it as if he committed two transgressions, even though he is formally liable for only one. This means that while the second prohibition does not create a second technical liability in terms of punishment, it still speaks to who this person is. The same formulation is confirmed by Rabbi Yochanan when Ravin transmitted his teaching: *ma'aleh ani alav ke'ilu asah shta'im ve'eino chayav ela achas*.

The Gemara asks: *mai nafka mina* — what is the practical consequence of this statement, which seems to say nothing more than "it's as if"? The answer is *likvoro bein resha'im gemurim* — it affects where he is buried. When the court executes a person, the severity of the sentence affects the section of the cemetery in which they are interred. The "as if" formulation means that in terms of his personal moral identity — what Briskers might call the *gavra* dimension — he is treated as one who committed two *aveiros*, even though the formal consequences of the act are determined by only one. This is how Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon are in fact arguing: Rabbi Shimon says it is purely one, even in this moral-identity sense; Rabbi Yose says it carries the character of two.

## R. Chiya vs. Bar Kappara

### Zar on Shabbos

The Gemara now connects the *issur chal al issur* discussion to a parallel series of debates between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara. *Uviflukta* — this controversy, the Gemara says, is linked to another dispute. The first case: *zar she-shimesh be-Shabbos* — a non-*kohen* who performs the Temple service on *Shabbos*. The *halakha* is that a *zar* who performs the Temple service is liable with death *bidei shamayim*, and that performing *melacha* on *Shabbos* also incurs the death penalty, *bidei adam*. This person has done both: he has performed the Temple service as a non-*kohen*, and he has done so on *Shabbos*. Is he liable twice?

### Two vs. One

Rabbi Chiya says *chayav shta'im* — twice. Bar Kappara says *eino chayav ella achas* — only once. What follows is one of the more colorful passages in this section of the Gemara. Rabbi Chiya jumps up and takes an oath — *ha'avodah*, by the divine Temple service — that he heard from Rebbi (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) that the liability is twice. Bar Kappara immediately jumps up and takes his own oath — *ha'avodah* — that he heard from Rebbi exactly the opposite, that the liability is only once. Two Tannaim, two oaths, two contradictory reports. Rather than simply deferring to one or the other, both proceed to offer logical arguments in support of their positions.

### R. Chiya's Logic

Rabbi Chiya argues: *Shabbos* was forbidden to everyone. When it was permitted in the Temple — that is, when the Temple service overrides *Shabbos* — that dispensation was granted *etzel kohanim*, specifically to *kohanim*. For non-*kohanim*, no such dispensation was ever extended. Therefore, *yesh kan mishum zarus ve-yesh kan mishum Shabbos* — he has violated both the prohibition against a *zar* performing the Temple service and the prohibition of *Shabbos*.

### Bar Kappara Logic

Bar Kappara counters: *Shabbos* was forbidden to everyone; when it was permitted in the Temple, it was permitted *in the Temple* — that is, within the context of the Temple service, *melacha* on *Shabbos* does not apply at all. The premise of the *Shabbos* prohibition is simply set aside entirely in this context, not merely waived for *kohanim* specifically. Therefore, *ein kan ella zarus* — there is only the prohibition of *zar* performing the service; the *Shabbos* violation simply does not exist here.

This is a strange position on its face. One might ask: does this mean anyone can walk into the Temple and light a match on *Shabbos*? Bar Kappara's logic seems to push in that direction, and the Gemara is aware of the difficulty. The debate is ultimately about how to conceptualize the nature of the *heter*: is it a targeted dispensation for *kohanim* doing the *avodah*, leaving the *Shabbos* prohibition intact for everyone else? Or does the Temple context itself dissolve the *Shabbos* prohibition as an operative category, such that there is simply no *Shabbos* issue for any action performed there?

### Baal Mum & Tamei

The second parallel case: *baal mum she-shimesh be-tumah* — a *kohen* with a physical blemish who performs the Temple service in a state of *tumah* (ritual impurity), specifically in the context of a *korban tzibur* (communal offering). As background: a *baal mum* is normally forbidden from performing the *avodah*, and serving in a state of *tumah* is also normally forbidden. However, *tumah* is overridden for a *korban tzibur* — when the entire community is *tamei*, the communal sacrifice may still be brought. So were this *kohen* unblemished, the *tumah* would not be a problem. But he is a *baal mum*, so he cannot perform the *avodah* in any case. Has he violated only the prohibition of *baal mum*, or has he also violated the prohibition of *tumah*?

Rabbi Chiya says twice; Bar Kappara says once. They take the same oaths as before. Rabbi Chiya's argument: *tumah* was forbidden to all; when it was permitted in the Temple for a *korban tzibur*, that dispensation was granted *lekohanim tmimim* — to unblemished *kohanim* capable of performing the *avodah*, not to a *baal mum*. Since this *baal mum* was not the intended beneficiary of the dispensation, the *tumah* prohibition still applies to him in full. He therefore violates both. Bar Kappara's argument: when the Torah permits *tumah* in the Temple for a *korban tzibur*, it permits it for all Temple service in that context — the *tumah* prohibition is simply not operative in this situation, regardless of who is performing the *avodah*. Therefore only the *baal mum* prohibition applies.

It is worth noting that Bar Kappara's position here is somewhat more coherent than in the *Shabbos* case. The principle of *tumah hutra be-tzibur* — that *tumah* is broadly set aside when communal sacrifices are required — is a recognized *halakhic* category with genuine *sugyot* behind it, and one can plausibly argue that in such a context the prohibition of *tumah* is not merely waived for eligible *kohanim* but is categorically suspended.

### Melikah Case

The third case: *zeh she-achal melikah* — a non-*kohen* who ate from a bird *korban* that had been slaughtered by the *kohen* through *melikah* (the method of severing the bird's spine with the fingernail from behind the neck). *Melikah* is the valid method for bringing a bird sacrifice; when performed by a *kohen* on a qualifying *korban*, it renders the bird a legitimate offering, edible by *kohanim*. But this particular bird offering — a *chatas* — may only be eaten by *kohanim*. A non-*kohen* who eats it has certainly violated the prohibition against *zarim* eating from a *korban chatas*.

But has he also violated the prohibition of eating *neveilah*? Because *melikah*, unlike *shechita*, does not involve cutting the neck with a knife in a way that would render the bird a properly slaughtered animal for ordinary consumption. A bird slaughtered in this fashion outside the Temple context would indeed be a *neveilah*. Is this bird, then, simultaneously a kosher *korban* (permitted to *kohanim*) and a *neveilah* (with respect to *zarim*)? Rabbi Chiya says twice; Bar Kappara says once. The same oaths, the same structure.

Rabbi Chiya's argument: *neveilah* was forbidden to all; when it was permitted in the Temple through the vehicle of *melikah*, that permission was granted to *kohanim* specifically — *lekohanim hutra, velo le-zarim*. For this non-*kohen*, it remains a *neveilah*. He therefore violates both. Bar Kappara's argument: when the Torah established *melikah* as the mode of bringing a bird sacrifice, it was declaring that an animal prepared this way *is not a neveilah* — not that it is a *neveilah* for which there happens to be a special permit. The Torah redefined the category: done this way, in this context, it simply is not a *neveilah* at all. Therefore there is no *neveilah* issue, and only the *zarus* prohibition applies.

Bar Kappara's position in this third case is arguably the most compelling of the three. There is a coherent reading of *melikah* as a mode of preparation that is *sui generis* — the Torah, by prescribing it, defined the result as *not neveilah* rather than as *neveilah with a dispensation*. This is a meaningful conceptual distinction.

Despite the local coherence of each of Bar Kappara's arguments, the Gemara is going to reframe all three cases as expressing a single, unified *halakhic* debate rather than three separate local questions.

## Simultaneous Prohibi (II)

### All Three Are Kolel

The Gemara now asks: *be-mai kamiflagi* — what is the underlying dispute between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara across all three cases? The answer proposed: *be-issur kolel ve-aliba de-Rabbi Yosi* — they are debating *issur kolel* according to Rabbi Yose. Recall that we established Rabbi Yose holds of *issur mosif* but the question remained whether he holds of *issur kolel*. All three cases, the Gemara now suggests, are structured as instances of *issur kolel*.

---

## Simultaneous Prohibi (II)

### R. Chiya on Kolel

Rabbi Chiya holds: *Rabbi Yosi be-issur kolel mechayev tartei* — Rabbi Yose says that even an *issur kolel* can take effect, generating liability for two prohibitions. This is why Rabbi Chiya rules twice across all three cases — he understands them as *issur kolel* scenarios in which Rabbi Yose would impose dual liability.

### Bar Kappara on Kolel

Bar Kappara holds: *lo mechayev ella chada* — even Rabbi Yose does not permit the second prohibition to take effect when it is only *issur kolel*. The second prohibition must genuinely expand the scope of the object itself (*issur mosif*); merely sweeping the object into a broader class of the subject's prohibitions (*issur kolel*) is insufficient. Hence only one.

The Gemara then demonstrates how each of the three cases can be understood as *issur kolel*. In the case of the *zar* on *Shabbos*: initially, the *zar* could perform *melacha* freely on weekdays — his only prohibition was the *avodah* in the Temple. When *Shabbos* arrives, he becomes forbidden in *melacha* generally; the class of things he cannot do expands, sweeping the Temple *avodah* into that broader class. His own forbidden status — the *avodah* prohibition — has not changed; he was forbidden from the *avodah* all along. But the range of activities he cannot engage in has broadened, and because the *avodah* falls within the same category of activity as *melacha*, it is now doubly prohibited, so to speak — once from *zarus* and once because it is a form of *melacha* that he cannot perform on *Shabbos*. This is *issur kolel*: the subject's prohibited activities have expanded, sweeping the already-forbidden act into a broader category.

In the case of the *baal mum* who became *tamei*: a *baal mum* cannot perform the *avodah* but may eat *korbanot*. Once he becomes *tamei*, he loses the ability to eat *korbanot* as well. Because he is now restricted in eating — and being restricted in eating *korbanot* is linked to being restricted in performing the *avodah* — the *tumah* status adds an additional dimension to his prohibition against performing the *avodah*. More of his activities are now forbidden; the *avodah* prohibition gets swept into this broader set of restrictions.

### Bat Achat Issue

But here a fundamental problem emerges with the *melikah* case. The Gemara states: *ella melikah be-vat achat hi de-mishkachat lah — be-issur kolel lo mishkachat lah*. In the *melikah* case, you cannot construct any sequence at all. The moment the *kohen* performs *melikah*, two things happen simultaneously: the bird becomes a *neveilah* (were it not a *korban*) and it becomes a *korban* prohibited to *zarim*. There is no prior moment when one prohibition existed without the other. You cannot say the *zarus* prohibition came first and the *neveilah* prohibition was added later, or vice versa — they arise in the same instant. The very act of *melikah* introduces both *issurin* in *bat achat*, at one and the same moment. *Issur kolel* requires at minimum a conceptual sequence; if both prohibitions arise simultaneously, *issur kolel* cannot be the operative framework.

### Real Debate

The Gemara therefore proposes a reframing: *kamiflagi be-issur bat achat* — the debate between Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara is actually about what happens when two prohibitions arise simultaneously. And the debate is *aliba de-Rabbi Yosi* — according to Rabbi Yose, who is already known to permit *issur chal al issur* in the case of *issur mosif*. Would Rabbi Yose also say that when two prohibitions arise at exactly the same moment — *bat achat* — both take effect? Rabbi Chiya says yes: *Rabbi Yosi be-issur bat achat mechayev tartei*. Bar Kappara says no: *lo mechayev ella chada* — even simultaneous prohibitions yield only one.

## Mosif vs. Kolel (II)

### Application to Yevamot

To close the loop back to our original case, we must now apply the framework of *issur mosif* and *issur kolel* to the question of *eshet ach* and *achot isha* that opened the discussion. We established that *issur mosif* works when the second prohibition broadens the scope of persons to whom the object is forbidden. In the sequence where *achot isha* comes first — she is forbidden to Shimon alone — and then she becomes *eshet ach* by marrying Reuven, she is now forbidden to all of the brothers. That expansion of scope constitutes *issur mosif*, and Rabbi Yose says both prohibitions apply. In the reverse sequence, where *eshet ach* comes first — she is already forbidden to all the brothers — the subsequent *achot isha* status, which merely sweeps her into the broader class of sisters forbidden to Shimon, constitutes *issur kolel*. Since Rabbi Yose does not hold of *issur kolel*, only the first prohibition applies, and the *ke'ilu asah shta'im* formulation of Rava addresses the residual moral dimension in that sequence.

## Reconciling Testimony

### The Contradiction

The Gemara returns to the most pressing structural puzzle: how can both Rabbi Chiya and Bar Kappara have taken oaths that they heard opposite rulings from the same teacher, Rebbi? They are not simply disagreeing about the law; each claims a direct personal tradition. Somebody heard incorrectly, but the Gemara resists attributing this to dishonesty — hence the need to reconstruct a scenario in which both could have genuinely misunderstood. And crucially, the Gemara is not satisfied with an explanation that works in only one direction; it needs an account that explains how each side could have innocently reached the wrong conclusion.

### Theory One

The first proposed resolution: *ki asnei l'dideih aliba de-Rabbi Yosi* — when Rebbi taught Rabbi Chiya, he was teaching according to Rabbi Yose, and said twice. *Ki asnei le-Bar Kappara aliba de-Rabbi Shimon* — when he taught Bar Kappara, he was presenting the view of Rabbi Shimon, and said once. Each student heard Rebbi's statement correctly but took it as Rebbi's definitive ruling rather than as a presentation of a particular Tanna's position. Rabbi Chiya heard twice and remembered twice; Bar Kappara heard once and remembered once. The mistake was one of attribution — each attached the ruling to Rebbi's own authority rather than to the Tanna whose position Rebbi was presenting.

### Theory Two

The Gemara is not fully satisfied with this explanation, however, and proposes a second framework. *Ela kamifligi be-issur bat achat aliba de-Rabbi Shimon* — perhaps the real debate is about *bat achat*, simultaneous prohibitions, according to Rabbi Shimon, not Rabbi Yose. The logic: Rabbi Yose is already known to permit *issur chal al issur* in certain cases. Rabbi Shimon categorically says *ein issur chal al issur*. Perhaps, however, even Rabbi Shimon would permit dual liability in the case of *bat achat* — when the prohibitions arise simultaneously, there is no moment where one preceded the other, and the logic of "one cannot take effect on top of the other" simply does not apply. They took hold together. Rabbi Chiya swears twice, maintaining that even Rabbi Shimon holds of liability in a *bat achat* scenario. Bar Kappara swears once, holding that Rabbi Shimon's position is consistent: even *bat achat* yields only one.

### Unresolved Problem

Yet the Gemara identifies a persistent difficulty with both frameworks. In either formulation — whether the debate is *bat achat aliba de-Rabbi Yosi* or *bat achat aliba de-Rabbi Shimon* — one can explain how one of the two scholars might have misunderstood. Under theory one, Rabbi Chiya could understand Bar Kappara's mistake: Rebbi said once according to Rabbi Shimon, and Bar Kappara mistakenly took it as Rebbi's general ruling; Rabbi Chiya knows the twice ruling was according to Rabbi Yose. But under the same theory, it is harder to explain from Bar Kappara's vantage point how Rabbi Chiya could have innocently arrived at twice if, according to Bar Kappara, even Rabbi Yose only yields once for *bat achat* — what would twice even be based on?

Under theory two, the problem is similarly asymmetric. If the debate is whether Rabbi Shimon allows *bat achat* to generate dual liability, then Rabbi Chiya is swearing that Rabbi Shimon permits twice in such a case, a position that contradicts the well-established understanding of Rabbi Shimon. One can understand why Bar Kappara would say once — that is simply Rabbi Shimon's known position. But *why* would Bar Kappara have needed to swear to that? It is the obvious, unremarkable reading of Rabbi Shimon. The oath adds nothing. And conversely, it becomes difficult to construct a plausible innocent mistake in the other direction. The Gemara leaves this *kashya* unresolved — *aliba de-Bar Kappara Rabbi Chiya shikurei ka-mishaker?* is asked but not answered to the Gemara's own satisfaction, and the discussion will continue.
