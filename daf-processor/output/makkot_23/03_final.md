# Makkot 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Malkot Procedure

### Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ? כּוֹפֶה שְׁתֵּי יָדָיו עַל הָעַמּוּד הֵילָךְ וְהֵילָךְ, וְחַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת אוֹחֵז בִּבְגָדָיו, אִם נִקְרְעוּ – נִקְרְעוּ, וְאִם נִפְרְמוּ – נִפְרְמוּ, עַד שֶׁהוּא מְגַלֶּה אֶת לִבּוֹ. וְהָאֶבֶן נְתוּנָה מֵאַחֲרָיו. חַזַּן הַכְּנֶסֶת עוֹמֵד עָלָיו וּרְצוּעָה בְּיָדוֹ, שֶׁל עֵגֶל, כְּפוּלָה אֶחָד לִשְׁנַיִם וּשְׁנַיִם לְאַרְבָּעָה, וּשְׁתֵּי רְצוּעוֹת שֶׁל חֲמוֹר עוֹלוֹת וְיוֹרְדוֹת בָּהּ. יָדָהּ טֶפַח וּרְחָבָה טֶפַח, וְרֹאשָׁהּ מַגַּעַת עַל פִּי כְרֵיסוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How do they flog him? He ties** the **two hands** of the person being flogged **on this** side **and that** side **of a post, and the attendant of the congregation takes hold of his garments** to remove them. **If they were ripped** in the process, **they were ripped, and if they were unraveled, they were unraveled,** and he continues **until he bares his chest. And the stone** upon which the attendant stands when flogging **is situated behind** the person being flogged. **The attendant of the congregation stands on it with a strap in his hand.** It is a strap **of calf** hide, and is **doubled, one into two, and two into four, and two straps of donkey** hide **go up and down** the doubled strap of calf hide. The length of **its handle** is **one handbreadth,** and the **width** of the straps is **one handbreadth, and** the strap must be long enough so that **its end reaches the top of his abdomen,** i.e., his navel, when he is flogged from behind.

Makkot 23 concludes with what is, in many respects, a deeply unsettling passage to study. The final two *mishnayot* of the tractate deal with the precise mechanics of *malkot*—how lashes are actually administered—and then pivot to some of the most profound theological statements in the entire *masechta*. We begin at the bottom of 22b, with the *mishnah* that opens: *keitsad malkin oto*—how do we administer the lashes?

### Binding & Position

The procedure begins with binding the offender's hands to a post. The post stands roughly one and a half to two *amot* high, and the person receiving the lashes leans against it—not standing upright and not sitting, but tilted sideways—so that lashes can be delivered both to the front and to the back. The *mishnah* states: *kofet shtei yadav al ha-amud heylach ve-heylach*—his two hands are tied to the post on either side, so that he cannot resist or interfere with the procedure.

### Degradation Principle

The *chazzan ha-knesset*—the attendant—grabs hold of the offender's garments. *Im nikreu nikreu ve-im nifremu nifremu ad she-hu megaleh et libo*: if they are torn, they are torn; if they unravel, they unravel—until the person's heart is exposed. The exposure is required so that one can see exactly where the lashes land, but the *mishnah* is careful to specify that this exposure goes only as far as is necessary. The principle at work throughout this entire procedure is *nikleh*—degradation. There is a deliberate point to the *bizayon*, the humiliation, but it is bounded: degradation is the vehicle, not the destination.

This dynamic is reminiscent of what we find with the *sotah*, where a comparable form of controlled exposure takes place. Here too, the underlying principle is that the offender must undergo a defined experience of *nikleh* and emerge from it redeemed—as we will see later, the *pasuk* concludes *veniklah achicha le'einecha*, "and your brother will be degraded before your eyes," which the *gemara* reads as: once he has been degraded, he becomes *achicha*—your brother once again.

### Strap Construction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַכֶּה אוֹתוֹ שְׁלִישׁ מִלְּפָנָיו וּשְׁתֵּי יָדוֹת מִלְּאַחֲרָיו. וְאֵינוֹ מַכֶּה אוֹתוֹ לֹא עוֹמֵד וְלֹא יוֹשֵׁב, אֶלָּא מוּטֶּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִפִּילוֹ הַשֹּׁפֵט״,
>
> **Translation:** **And** the attendant **flogs him** with **one-third** of the lashes **from the front of him,** on his chest, **and two** one-third **portions from behind him,** on his back. **And he does not flog him** when the one receiving lashes is **standing, nor** when he is **sitting; rather,** he flogs him when he is **hunched, as it is stated: "And the judge shall cause him to lie down,** and strike him" (Deuteronomy 25:2), which indicates that the one receiving lashes must be in a position that approximates lying down.

Behind the attendant, a stone is placed on which he stands—*ve-even netunah me-achorav, ve-hazan ha-knesset omed aleha*. In his hand is a strap of calf skin, doubled over: *retzuah be-yado shel egel kfulah*. It is doubled once and then again—*echad li-shnayim ve-shnayim le-arba'ah*—so that it has multiple layers. On the sides of this strap hang two additional straps of donkey leather—*shtei retzuot shel chamor olot ve-yordot*—that rise and fall with each strike. Based on Rashi, these elements appear to have been woven together, though the Steinsaltz illustrations present their own reconstruction.

### Measurements

The handle of the strap has a specific measurement: *yad tefach ve-rochvah tefach*—a *tefach* in length and a *tefach* in width. The tip of the strap should reach as far as the belly of the person being lashed: *ve-roshah maga'at al pi kreiso*. The distribution of lashes is likewise precisely defined: *u-makeh oto shlish mil-fanav ve-shtei yadot me-achorav*—one third of the lashes on the front (over the heart) and two thirds on the back.

### Striking Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמַּכֶּה מַכֶּה בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת בְּכׇל כֹּחוֹ, וְהַקּוֹרֵא קוֹרֵא: ״אִם לֹא תִשְׁמֹר לַעֲשׂוֹת וְגוֹ׳״, ״וְהִפְלָא ה׳ אֶת מַכֹּתְךָ וְאֵת מַכּוֹת וְגוֹ׳״ וְחוֹזֵר לִתְחִלַּת הַמִּקְרָא: ״וּשְׁמַרְתֶּם אֶת דִּבְרֵי הַבְּרִית הַזֹּאת וְגוֹ׳״ וְחוֹתֵם: ״וְהוּא רַחוּם יְכַפֵּר עָוֹן״, וְגוֹ׳, וְחוֹזֵר לִתְחִלַּת הַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the** attendant **flogging** the one receiving lashes **flogs [*makeh*] him with one hand with all his strength, and the** court **crier recites** the verses: **"If you do not observe to perform** all the words of this law that are written in this book, that you may fear this glorious and awesome name, the Lord your God. **And the Lord will make your plagues [*makkotekha*] outstanding, and the plagues** of your descendants, and even great plagues, and of long continuance, and severe sicknesses, and of long continuance" (Deuteronomy 28:58–59). **And** then **he returns to the beginning of the verse.** He also recites: **"And you shall observe the matters of this covenant,** and do them, that you may make all that you do to prosper" (Deuteronomy 29:8), **and concludes** with the verse: **"And He is merciful and shall atone for transgression, and destroys not;** and many a time does He turn His anger away, and does not stir up all His wrath" (Psalms 78:38), **and** then **returns to the beginning of the verse** that starts: "If you do not observe to perform."

The flogger strikes with one hand and with full force: *ve-hamakeh beyado achat bechol kocho*. This is derived from the *pasuk* of *vehippilo ha-shofeyt*—"the judge shall fell him"—which, as we will discuss at greater length, yields two separate teachings from a single word. Counterintuitively, the tradition understands a one-handed strike as producing greater force than a two-handed strike, and that is the standard applied here.

### Verses & Word Count

Three individuals are present during the procedure: the *makeh* (the one who strikes), a *koreh* (one who reads *psukim* aloud), and a third who counts the lashes and coordinates the strikes. The *koreh* recites the following verses throughout the procedure.

The first passage is from Devarim (28:58–59): *im lo tishmor la'asot et kol divrei ha-torah ha-zot... vehifla Hashem et makotecha*—"if you do not observe all the words of this Torah... Hashem will make your plagues extraordinary." After completing this section, *vechozer le-tichilat ha-mikra*—he returns to the beginning of the reading. The second passage is from Devarim (29:8): *ushmartem et divrei ha-brit ha-zot va'asitem otam*—"and you shall observe the words of this covenant and fulfill them." The procedure closes with the verse from Tehillim (78:38): *ve-hu rachum yechaper avon velo yashchit*—"and He, being merciful, atones for iniquity and does not destroy," again followed by a return to the beginning of the reading.

One of the commentators notes that the *teivot*—the word count of these *psukim*—can be arranged to reach the number 39. One reckoning: the first passage from Devarim runs to 21 words, then *vehifla Hashem et makotecha* adds 13 more, for 34. Add the first five words of *ushmartem et divrei ha-brit ha-zot*, and one arrives at 39. Another reckoning, advanced by the Maharsha, is that the three closing verses are each precisely 13 words, totaling 39. Both calculations are plausible and appear in the sources.

There is a notable element of *teshuvah* embedded in this structure. The procedure does not conclude on a note of pure punishment; rather, *ushmartem et divrei ha-brit ha-zot*—"guard the words of this covenant"—is placed deliberately to give the offender an orientation toward repentance, not merely chastisement. The closing verse of *ve-hu rachum* similarly frames the moment as one of divine mercy rather than naked retribution. The juxtaposition is striking: *ve-hu rachum, yechaper avon*—He is merciful—is declared even as the strike lands. One might read this as an expression of the principle that punishment in this system functions as education and purification, in the spirit of *chosech shivto soneh beno*—one who withholds the rod hates his child.

A question naturally arises about how the verses were coordinated with the strikes—whether each word corresponded to one strike, or whether the *psukim* were recited continuously as a backdrop to the grouped lashes of 13, 13, and 13. This remains unclear. The *mishnah* itself notes that the *koreh* adjusts his pace: *bezman she-makah merubah ma'arich, bezman she-makah mu'etet mekatzeir*—when there are more lashes he prolongs the reading, when fewer he shortens it. This suggests that the verse-reading was not a rigid one-word-per-strike system, but rather something timed broadly to the duration of the procedure, though the precise mechanics are not spelled out.

### Death Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם מֵת תַּחַת יָדוֹ – פָּטוּר. הוֹסִיף לוֹ עוֹד רְצוּעָה אַחַת וּמֵת – הֲרֵי זֶה גּוֹלֶה עַל יָדוֹ. נִתְקַלְקֵל, בֵּין בְּרֵיעִי בֵּין בְּמַיִם – פָּטוּר, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָאִישׁ – בְּרֵיעִי, וְהָאִשָּׁה – בְּמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the one being flogged **dies at** the **hand** of the attendant, the latter is **exempt,** because he acted at the directive of the court. If the attendant **added for him an additional** lash with **a strap and he died,** the attendant **is exiled** to a city of refuge **on his account,** as an unwitting murderer. **If** the one being flogged involuntarily **sullies himself,** due to fear or pain, **whether with excrement or with urine,** he is **exempt** from further lashes. **Rabbi Yehuda says** that the threshold of shame for men and women is different: **The man** is exempted if he sullies himself **with excrement, and the woman** is exempted even **with urine.**

The *mishnah* continues: *umet tachat yado patur*—if the person being lashed dies in the course of the procedure, the flogger is exempt from liability. This is because the process was preceded by a medical assessment that determined the person was fit to receive the prescribed lashes. If death occurred within the parameters of that authorized procedure, the flogger bears no criminal responsibility.

However, if the flogger added even one extra lash beyond what was prescribed—*hosif lo od retzuah achat va-met*—and the person died as a result, the flogger is subject to *galut*, exile to an *ir miklat*, the same consequence as one who kills *be-shegegah*, unintentionally.

### Nikleh Dismissal

The *mishnah* introduces a final basis for dismissal from the lashing: *nitkalkel bein be-rei bein be-mayim, patur*—if the offender becomes soiled, whether by what one would delicately call number two or number one, the lashing stops immediately. The reasoning is tied to the principle of *nikleh*: the degradation has already occurred, and continuing the lashes would exceed what the Torah intended. Rabbi Yehudah draws a distinction between men and women in this regard—for a man, the dismissal is triggered by the former; for a woman, by the latter. The implications of this distinction are discussed in greater detail below.

## Smichuyot & Sources

### Why Nikleh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא – מִשּׁוּם ״נִקְלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the attendant rips the garments of the person about to be flogged. The Gemara explains: **What is the reason** for this? It is **due to** the verse: "Forty he shall flog him…and your brother shall be **debased** before you" (Deuteronomy 25:3), as tearing his garments debases him.

The *gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishnah* with a foundational question: *mai ta'ama? mishum nikleh*—what is the reason for this entire procedure? It is because of *veniklah achicha le'einecha*, the *pasuk* in Devarim that frames the lashing in terms of degradation. The idea that the offender becomes *achicha*—your brother—after undergoing this humiliation is what gives the entire procedure its internal coherence. Everything that looks gratuitously harsh—the tearing of the garments, the exposure, the leaning posture—is in the service of *derech bizayon*, a defined path of degradation that, when completed, restores the offender to the community.

### Calf-Skin Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: מִנַּיִן לִרְצוּעָה שֶׁהִיא שֶׁל עֵגֶל – דִּכְתִיב ״אַרְבָּעִים יַכֶּנּוּ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״לֹא תַחְסֹם שׁוֹר בְּדִישׁוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet says in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: From where** is it derived **with regard to the strap** used for flogging **that it is** a strap from the hide **of a calf?** It is **as it is written: "Forty he shall flog him," and juxtaposed to it** is written: **"You shall not muzzle an ox in its threshing"** (Deuteronomy 25:4), indicating that the strap is from the hide of an ox.

A more difficult question follows. Rav Sheshet in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah asks: *minayin la-retzuah she-hi shel egel*—from where do we derive that the strap must be made of calf skin? The answer: *de-chtiv arba'im yakenu ve-samich lei lo tachsom shor be-disho*—the *pasuk* of "forty lashes shall be given to him" (*arba'im yakenu*) is written in proximity to the *pasuk* of "you shall not muzzle an ox while it threshes" (*lo tachsom shor be-disho*). From the proximity of these two verses, we learn something about the material of the strap—a *shor* (ox) is connected, and *shor* is connected to *egel* (calf). Admittedly, this requires some inferential steps that are not fully transparent to us, and the *gemara* itself is working from a received tradition here. There is an additional resonance: the tradition that the *parah adumah* (red cow) atones for the sin of the *egel ha-zahav* (the golden calf). One commentator suggests that *egel* etymologically connects to *ma'agal*—going in circles, self-absorption—while *parah* relates to *lifroth*, to be fruitful, to give outward. Whether that etymology illuminates the connection to the lashing strap or not, it remains a suggestive piece of the larger picture.

### Smichuyot Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: מִנַּיִן לִיבָמָה שֶׁנָּפְלָה לִפְנֵי מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין, שֶׁאֵין חוֹסְמִין אוֹתָהּ – דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא תַחְסֹם שׁוֹר בְּדִישׁוֹ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״כִּי יֵשְׁבוּ אַחִים יַחְדָּו וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet says in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: From where** is it derived **with regard to a *yevama* who happened before** a *yavam* **afflicted with boils, that one does not compel her** to enter into that levirate marriage? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "You shall not muzzle an ox in its threshing," and juxtaposed to it** is written: **"When brothers dwell together"** (Deuteronomy 25:5), which is the passage dealing with levirate marriage. The *yevama* is not muzzled, as it were, when she states that she does not want to enter into levirate marriage with him.

The technique being employed here is *smichuyot*—the principle that *psukim* placed in proximity to one another in the text illuminate each other. When two commandments or narratives stand side by side, the Sages understood that the juxtaposition itself carries legal or homiletical significance. This is a well-established mode of deriving *halacha* and *aggadah* in rabbinic literature, though the specific connections are not always fully recoverable. We possess the conclusions; some of the intermediate reasoning belongs to the *Torah she-be'al peh* that was not written down and has been lost to us.

### Smichuyot Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: כׇּל הַמְבַזֶּה אֶת הַמּוֹעֲדִים כְּאִילּוּ עוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, דִּכְתִיב ״אֱלֹהֵי מַסֵּכָה לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה לָּךְ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״אֶת חַג הַמַּצּוֹת תִּשְׁמֹר״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet says in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya:** Concerning **anyone who treats the Festivals with contempt, it is as though** he is **worshipping idols, as it is written: "Molten gods you shall not make for yourself"** (Exodus 34:17), **and juxtaposed to it** is written: **"The festival of Passover you shall observe"** (Exodus 34:18).

Rav Sheshet in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah brings several further examples of *smichuyot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: כׇּל הַמְסַפֵּר לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, וְכֵן הַמְקַבֵּל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע, וְכׇל הַמֵּעִיד עֵדוּת שֶׁקֶר – רָאוּי לְהַשְׁלִיכוֹ לִכְלָבִים, דִּכְתִיב ״לַכֶּלֶב תַּשְׁלִכוּן אֹתוֹ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ ״לֹא תִשָּׁא שֵׁמַע שָׁוְא וְגוֹ׳״, קְרִי בֵּיהּ נָמֵי ״לֹא תַשִּׂיא״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheshet says in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya:** Concerning **anyone who speaks malicious speech, and anyone who accepts malicious speech** as the truth, **and anyone who testifies a false testimony, it is fit to throw him to the dogs, as it is written: "To the dog you shall cast it"** (Exodus 22:30), **and juxtaposed to it** is written: **"You shall not accept [*lo tissa*] a false report;** do not join with the wicked to be a false witness" (Exodus 23:1). In addition to prohibitions against false testimony and against accepting malicious speech, Rav Sheshet **also reads into** the verse the meaning: **You shall not relate [*lo tassi*]** a false report.

The first: *minayin le-yevamah she-nafelah lifnei muke shchin she-ein chosmin otah*—from where do we learn that a *yevamah* who falls before a *yavam* whom she finds repulsive (described here as *muke shchin*, afflicted with a severe skin condition) cannot be compelled to marry him? The answer: *lo tachsom shor be-disho* is in textual proximity to *ki yeshvu achim yachdav*—the opening of the laws of *yibbum*. Just as we do not muzzle the ox in its moment of labor, so too we do not "muzzle" the *yevamah* by forcing her into a marriage that is intolerable to her. The *smichut*, the proximity, teaches the one from the other.

The second: *kol ha-mevazeh et ha-mo'adim ke-ilu oved avodah zarah*—anyone who treats the *mo'adim* with contempt is as though he has worshipped idols. The *mo'adim* referred to here are understood to be *Chol Ha-Mo'ed*, the intermediate days of Pesach and Sukkot—for the full *yom tov* days carry their own explicit obligations. The textual basis: *Elohei masechah lo ta'aseh lach* ("do not make molten gods") is in proximity to *et chag ha-matzot tishmor* ("you shall observe the festival of matzot"). The verse prohibiting idolatry stands directly beside the command to observe the festival, and from this proximity we derive the severity of degrading *Chol Ha-Mo'ed*.

The third: *kol ha-mesaper lashon ha-ra, ve-chol ha-mekabel lashon ha-ra, ve-chol ha-me'id edut sheker—ra'uy le-hashlicho la-klavim*—one who speaks *lashon ha-ra*, one who receives it, and one who gives false testimony, all deserve to be thrown to the dogs. The textual basis: *basar ba-sadeh tereifah lo tochelu la-kelev tashlichun oto* ("flesh torn in the field, you shall not eat; throw it to the dogs") stands in proximity to *lo tisa shema shav* ("do not carry a false report"), which is read homiletically as *lo tashi*—do not cause others to carry it. The letter in *tisa* can be read as either an *ayin* or an *aleph*, and the *gemara* here is willing to play on that ambiguity. The connection between discarding treife meat and discarding false speech is made through the proximity of these two *psukim*.

One cannot help but notice a certain tension: in other contexts in the *gemara*, great precision is brought to bear on individual letters—distinguishing an *ayin* from an *aleph*, a *yud* from a *vav*—and entire legal edifices are built on those distinctions. Here, the *gemara* itself seems to wave away the philological precision in the service of the *smichut*. This is, in a sense, the honest face of *Torah she-be'al peh*: we have the tradition, we have the conclusion, and we work with what we have, even when the derivation raises more questions than it answers.

### Donkey Strap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁתֵּי רְצוּעוֹת וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא: שֶׁל חֲמוֹר. כִּדְדָרֵישׁ הָהוּא גָּלִילָאָה עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא: ״יָדַע שׁוֹר קֹנֵהוּ וַחֲמוֹר אֵבוּס בְּעָלָיו יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא יָדַע וְגוֹ׳״, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: יָבֹא מִי שֶׁמַּכִּיר אֵבוּס בְּעָלָיו, וְיִפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַכִּיר אֵבוּס בְּעָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And two straps** go up and down the doubled strap of calf hide. The Sage **taught: And** they are straps **of donkey** hide. **As a certain Galilean interpreted before Rav Ḥisda:** It is written: **"The ox knows its owner, and the donkey its master's trough; but Israel does not know,** My people does not consider" (Isaiah 1:3). **The Holy One, Blessed be He, says: Let the one who recognizes its master's trough,** an ox and donkey, **come and exact retribution,** through lashes with a strap of ox and donkey hide, **from one who does not recognize his Master's trough** and performs transgressions.

The *mishnah* mentioned that alongside the doubled calf-skin strap, there are *shtei retzuot shel chamor*—two straps of donkey leather. The *gemara* explains this through a teaching cited by a certain Galilean in the presence of Rav Chisda: *yada shor konehu ve-chamor evus ba'alav*—"an ox knows its owner, and a donkey its master's feeding trough" (Yeshayahu 1:3). The *chamor* is the animal that recognizes its provider and appreciates its sustenance. *Yisrael lo yada*, says the prophet—Israel did not recognize theirs. The *gemara* therefore says: *amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu, yavo mi she-makir evus ba'alav ve-yipara mi-mi she-eino makir evus ba'alav*—let the one who recognizes its master's trough come and call to account the one who does not. The donkey strap becomes a kind of testimony: an animal that knows its provider administers the reminder to the human being who forgot his.

## Size & Attendants

### Tefach Sizing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָדָהּ טֶפַח וְכוּ׳. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כֹּל חַד וְחַד לְפוּם גַּבֵּיהּ עָבְדִינַן לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִם כֵּן נַפֵּישׁ לְהוּ רְצוּעוֹת טוּבָא! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אַבְקָתָא אִית לַיהּ, כִּי בָּעֵי מְיקַטַּר בַּיהּ, כִּי בָּעֵי מְרַפֵּה בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: The length of **its handle** is **one handbreadth,** and the width of the straps is one handbreadth, and the strap must be long enough so that its end reaches the top of his abdomen. **Abaye said: Conclude from it** that **for each and every one, we craft** the strap **according to** the size of **their back. Rava said to him: If so, there will be numerous straps** in court **for them. Rather, Rava said: It has loops; when** the attendant **wants, he ties** the loops to shorten the strap, and **when** the attendant **wants, he loosens** the loops to lengthen the strap. The length of the strap can be adjusted to correspond to the height of the person being flogged.

Abaye observes: *shema mina, kol chad ve-chad le-fum gabeih avdinan lei*—from this we infer that the strap is sized individually to the person being lashed. Since *tefach* varies by person, a bespoke strap must be fashioned for each individual, ensuring that neither too many nor too few lashes are effectively delivered.

Rava immediately raises the practical problem: *im ken, nafishu lehu retzuot tuva*—if so, the *beit din* would need an enormous stock of straps, one for every possible size. He resolves the difficulty: *avakta it leih, ki ba'ei miktar beih, ki ba'ei marpei leih*—the strap has a loop; when the attendant needs it tighter, he tightens it; when he needs it looser to fit the individual, he loosens it. One adjustable strap suffices for all.

### Distribution Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְהִפִּילוֹ הַשֹּׁפֵט וְהִכָּהוּ לְפָנָיו כְּדֵי רִשְׁעָתוֹ בְּמִסְפָּר״ – רִשְׁעָה אַחַת מִלְּפָנָיו, שְׁתֵּי רִשְׁעָיוֹת מֵאַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One flogs him** with one-third of the lashes from the front of him and two one-third portions from behind him. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **Rav Kahana said:** It is derived from a verse, **as the verse states: "And the judge shall cause him to lie down, and strike him before him in accordance with his wickedness, by number"** (Deuteronomy 25:2), indicating that the attendant strikes him in accordance with **one** portion of **wickedness from the front of him,** and **two** portions of **wickedness from behind him.**

The Gemara then asks: from where do we derive the specific distribution of one third to the front and two thirds to the back? Rav Kahana answers from the *pasuk*: *ve-hippilo ha-shofeyt ve-hikkahu le-fanav kedei rish'ato be-mispar*. The word *le-fanav*—"before him"—is read to indicate one portion to the front. The construction of the verse implies the remainder goes to the back: *rish'ah achat mil-fanav, shtei rishot me-achorav*—one punishment to the front, two to the back.

### Doubled Strap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִן לִרְצוּעָה שֶׁהִיא מוּכְפֶּלֶת – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְהִפִּילוֹ״. וְהָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְגוּפֵיהּ! אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״יַטֵּיהוּ״, מַאי ״הִפִּילוֹ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And he does not flog him** when the one receiving lashes is **standing, nor** when he is **sitting; rather,** he flogs him when he is **hunched, as it is stated: "And the judge shall cause him to lie down." Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From where** is it derived with regard **to the strap that it is doubled?** It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated: And he shall cause him to lie down [*vehippilo*],** which is interpreted based on the similar Aramaic root *ayin*, *peh*, *peh*, meaning double. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't he require** that verse **for** the fundamental *halakha* **itself,** as the mishna teaches: He flogs him when he is hunched. The Gemara answers: **If so, let the verse write: Shall bend him. What** is the meaning of: **"Shall cause him to lie down"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it:** The *halakha* that the person being flogged must be hunched, and the allusion to the doubling of the strap.

The *gemara* then asks further: from where do we derive that the strap must be doubled? Rav Chisda in the name of Rabbi Yochanan says: from the same word *ve-hippilo*. The word can be read as *ve-hikpilo*—and he shall double it. But this raises a question: if we use *ve-hippilo* to teach doubling, how can we simultaneously use it to derive the posture (that the judge "fells" him)? The *gemara* resolves: *shema mina tartei*—we derive both teachings from the same word. The single term yields two conclusions: the doubled strap and the tilted posture.

### Attendant Qualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמַּכֶּה מַכֶּה בְּיָדוֹ. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מַעֲמִידִין חַזָּנִין אֶלָּא חֲסֵירֵי כֹחַ וִיתֵירֵי מַדָּע. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ חֲסֵירֵי מַדָּע וִיתֵירֵי כֹּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the** attendant **flogging** the one receiving lashes **flogs** him **with** one **hand** with all his strength. **The Sages taught:** For the administration of lashes, the court **appoints only attendants** who are **lacking in strength and** are **exceedingly knowledgeable** in Torah. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The court may appoint **even** those who are **lacking in knowledge and** are **exceedingly strong.**

The *baraita* adds: *ein ma'amidin chazanin ela chasrei koach vi-yeterei mada*—we appoint only those who are lacking in physical strength but rich in knowledge. The reasoning is that such a person will be careful, attentive to the condition of the offender, and restrained enough not to exceed the prescribed measure.

### Attendant Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מִסְתַּבְּרָא, דִּכְתִיב ״לֹא יוֹסִיף... פֶּן יוֹסִיף״, אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא חֲסֵירֵי מַדָּע – הַיְינוּ דִּצְרִיךְ לְאַזְהוֹרֵי. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ יְתֵירֵי מַדָּע – מִי צְרִיךְ לְאַזְהוֹרֵי? וְרַבָּנַן: אֵין מְזָרְזִין אֶלָּא לַמְזוֹרָז.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is reasonable** to rule **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is written:** "Forty he shall flog him; **he shall not exceed, lest he continue** to beat him" (Deuteronomy 25:3). He explains: **Granted, if you say** that even people **lacking in** halakhic **knowledge** are appointed, **that is** why **it is necessary to warn** him not to add lashes. **But if you say** only people who are **exceedingly knowledgeable** are appointed, **does** the Torah **need to warn** the attendant? Apparently, even a person lacking in knowledge can be appointed as an attendant. **And** according to **the Rabbis,** that is no proof, as there is an expression that **one implores only one who is** already **implored.** In other words, only one who is already cognizant of a *halakha* can be effectively warned to observe it.

Rabbi Yehudah disagrees: *afilu chasirei mada vi-yeterei koach*—one may appoint even someone deficient in knowledge, provided he is physically strong. Rava supports Rabbi Yehudah's position from the *pasuk*: *lo yosif pen yosif*—"he shall not add, lest he add." This redundant warning—"do not add, lest you add"—would be unnecessary if the attendant were already a thoughtful, learned person. The very fact that the Torah warns against excess implies the attendant is someone who needs to be told. The Sages who favor the learned attendant respond: *ein mezarzin ela la-mezoraz*—we caution even the cautious. The reminder is appropriate regardless of the attendant's disposition.

### Striking Mechanics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: כְּשֶׁהוּא מַגְבִּיהַּ – מַגְבִּיהַּ בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדָיו, וּכְשֶׁהוּא מַכֶּה – מַכֶּה בְּיָדוֹ אַחַת, כִּי הֵיכִי (דְּלֵיתֵא) [דְּתֵיתֵי] מִדִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught: When** the attendant **raises** the strap to administer the lashes, **he raises** it **with both hands, and when he flogs** the one receiving lashes, **he flogs with one hand, so that** the lashes **will come from him** in a deliberate manner.

The *baraita* specifies the mechanics further: *keshe-hu magbia, magbia bi-shtei yadav; u-keshe-hu makeh, makeh be-yado achat*—when raising the strap, he raises with two hands; when striking, he strikes with one. This ensures the blow is measured and controlled rather than carried by the momentum of a two-handed swing.

### Three Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַקּוֹרֵא קוֹרֵא וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבַּדַּיָּינִין קוֹרֵא, הַשֵּׁנִי מוֹנֶה, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי אוֹמֵר: ״הַכֵּהוּ״! בִּזְמַן שֶׁמַּכָּה מְרוּבָּה – מַאֲרִיךְ, בִּזְמַן שֶׁמַּכָּה מוּעֶטֶת – מְקַצֵּר. וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: חוֹזֵר לִתְחִלַּת הַמִּקְרָא! מִצְוָה לְצַמְצֵם, וְאִי[ם] לֹא צִמְצֵם – חוֹזֵר לִתְחִלַּת הַמִּקְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And the** court **crier recites** the verse beginning: "If you do not observe to perform," as well as other verses. **The Sages taught: The most prominent of the judges recites** the verses, **the second** most prominent judge **counts** the lashes, **and the third** most prominent **says** to the attendant: **Strike him. When the lashes are numerous,** the one reciting the verses **extends** his recitation; **when the lashes are few,** he **curtails** his recitation by reciting it faster. In both cases, he does so to coordinate the recitation with the duration of the lashes. The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: And then **he returns to the beginning of the** first **verse,** indicating that one could read the passage several times? The Gemara answers: The **mitzva is to precisely** coordinate recitation of the verses with the flogging, **and if he did not precisely** coordinate between them, and he completed the recitation of the verses before completing the lashes, **he returns to the beginning of the** first **verse.**

Three *dayanim* are present at the procedure. *Ha-gadol she-ba-dayanim korei*—the most senior judge reads the *psukim*. *Ha-sheni moneh*—the second counts the lashes. *Ve-ha-shlishi omer hakehu*—the third gives the signal to strike.

The *baraita* adds: *be-zman she-makah merubah ma'arich, be-zman she-makah mu'etet mekatzeir*—when the number of lashes is large, the *koreh* prolongs his reading; when the number is small, he shortens it. The reading is calibrated to the duration of the procedure as a whole.

## Dismissal Rules

### Maka Raba

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מַכָּה רַבָּה״, אֵין לִי אֶלָּא מַכָּה רַבָּה, מַכָּה מוּעֶטֶת מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״לֹא יוֹסִיף״. אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״מַכָּה רַבָּה״? לִימֵּד עַל הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁהֵן מַכָּה רַבָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another *baraita* with regard to the number of lashes. **The Sages taught:** From the verse: "He shall not exceed, lest he continue to beat him beyond these, **a great flogging"** (Deuteronomy 25:3), **I have** derived **only** a prohibition with regard to **a great flogging; from where** do I derive that even **a minimal** excessive **flogging** is prohibited? I derive it from **the verse** that **states: "He shall not exceed,"** at all. The Gemara asks: If so, **why** must **the verse state: "A great flogging"?** This **teaches that the initial** lashes must be administered **as a great flogging,** with all of the attendant's strength.

The *gemara* now turns to the *baraita* on the prohibition against adding lashes. The *pasuk* itself says *maka raba*—a great blow. One might think this prohibition applies only to exceeding by a significant amount. The *baraita* clarifies: *maka raba ein li ela maka raba, maka mu'etet minayin—talmud lomar lo yosif*—even adding a single small lash is prohibited, as the verse states "he shall not add." If so, what does *maka raba* add? The *baraita* answers: it teaches us that even the prescribed lashes themselves already constitute *maka raba*—a great blow. One should not minimize or dismiss what the offender has already endured.

### Nitkalkel Dismissal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַלְקֵל וְכוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֶחָד הָאִישׁ וְאֶחָד הָאִשָּׁה בְּרֵיעִי וְלֹא בְּמַיִם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הָאִישׁ בְּרֵיעִי, וְהָאִשָּׁה בְּמַיִם. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֶחָד הָאִישׁ וְאֶחָד הָאִשָּׁה – בֵּין בְּרֵיעִי בֵּין בְּמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the one being flogged **sullies himself,** with excrement or urine, he is exempt from further lashes. Rabbi Yehuda says: The man is exempted with excrement, and the woman is exempted even with urine. **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: For **both a man and a woman,** they are exempted if they sully themselves **with excrement, but not** if they do so **with urine;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: The man with excrement, and the woman** even **with urine. And the Rabbis say: Both a man and a woman** are exempt from further lashes **whether** they sullied themselves **with excrement or with urine.**

The *gemara* returns to the question of dismissal through *nitkalkel*—soiling. *Tanu rabanan: echad ha-ish ve-echad ha-isha be-rei, ve-lo be-mayim—divrei Rabbi Meir*—the *baraita* records that according to Rabbi Meir, both men and women are dismissed from lashing only in the case of the more severe soiling (number two), not the lesser. Rabbi Yehudah distinguishes: for a man it is number two, for a woman it is number one. The Sages say: for both men and women, either form of soiling is sufficient grounds for dismissal.

### Meir vs. Yehudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד הָאִישׁ וְאֶחָד הָאִשָּׁה – בְּרֵיעִי! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: שְׁנֵיהֶם שָׁוִין בְּרֵיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks with regard to the opinion attributed to Rabbi Yehuda: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Both a man and a woman** are exempted **with excrement,** indicating that Rabbi Yehuda holds that a woman is not exempted with urine. **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said:** There is no contradiction; that *baraita* is merely stating that according to Rabbi Yehuda **both are equal with regard to excrement.** That does not mean that Rabbi Yehuda holds that a woman is not exempted with urine.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak resolves an apparent contradiction in Rabbi Yehudah's position by explaining that while there may appear to be leniency extended to the woman, the operative conclusion is that for both men and women, the more severe soiling dismisses them from the procedure. *Shneihem shavim be-rei*—both are equal with respect to that standard.

### Escape & Breakage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּפָתוּהוּ, וְרָץ מִבֵּית דִּין – פָּטוּר. מֵיתִיבִי: קָלָה, בֵּין בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה בֵּין בַּשְּׁנִיָּה – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. נִפְסְקָה רְצוּעָה בַּשְּׁנִיָּה – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ, בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה – אֵין פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. אַמַּאי? לֶהֱוֵי כְּרָץ! הָתָם רָץ, הָכָא לָא רָץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says:** If **they bound him** to be flogged **and he fled from** the **court,** he is **exempt. The Gemara raises an objection** from a *baraita*: If he **was debased** with excrement, **whether during** the **first** lash **or during** the **second** lash, the court **exempts him.** But in a case where the **strap was severed** during the course of the flogging, if this occurred **during** the **second** lash **they exempt him,** but if it happened **during** the **first** lash, **they do not exempt him. Why** is he not exempted during the first lash? **Let** his status **be like one who fled** from the court before the flogging began, in which case he is exempt. The Gemara answers: **There,** in that case, **he fled** from the court and he is not compelled to return; **here, he did not flee,** and therefore he is not exempted without being flogged.

The *gemara* raises a delightful case. Shmuel states: *kefatuhu ve-ratz mi-beit ha-din, patur*—if the offender was bound and somehow escaped from the court, he is exempt. The logic is that the act of escaping—in full view of everyone—already constitutes sufficient *bizayon*. The humiliation has occurred; there is nothing more to administer.

A parallel case distinguishes strap breakage from escape. If the strap broke during the second lash, the offender is dismissed. But if it broke during the very first lash, he is not—*barishona ein potrim oto*. Why? Because unlike the person who escaped—who actively underwent the humiliation of fleeing bound from the court—the person whose strap broke at the first strike has experienced only one lash, and the degradation associated with the process has not yet been sufficiently established. *Hatam ratz, hacha lo ratz*—in the one case, he ran; in the other, he did not.

### Sequence Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֲמָדוּהוּ לִכְשֶׁיִּלְקֶה קָלֶה – פּוֹטְרִין אוֹתוֹ. לִכְשֶׁיֵּצֵא מִבֵּית דִּין קָלֶה – מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ. וְלֹא עוֹד אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ קָלָה בַּתְּחִלָּה – מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִכָּהוּ וְגוֹ׳ וְנִקְלָה״ וְלֹא שֶׁלָּקָה כְּבָר בְּבֵית דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** If **they assessed** concerning **him that when he is flogged he** will be **debased** with excrement, **they exempt him,** as the court does not administer a punishment that will lead to debasing the one being flogged beyond the shame generated by the lashes themselves. But if they assessed concerning him **that** it is only **when he will leave the court** that **he** will be **debased** with excrement, **they flog him. Moreover, even if he was debased initially,** before any lashes were administered, **they** nevertheless **flog him, as it is stated: "And strike him…and** your brother **shall be debased"** (Deuteronomy 25:2–3), indicating that the reference is to one debased as a result of the lashes, **and not** to **one who was already debased in court** prior to being flogged.

A final *baraita* addresses the timing of dismissal relative to the order of events. *Amduhu le-shilkeh, kala potrim oto*—if the court assessed him, and in the course of the assessment he became degraded, he is exempt. However: *le-cheshe-yatza mi-beit ha-din, kala malkin oto*—if he was degraded after leaving the court but before the lashing began, he is flogged anyway. *Ve-lo od ella afilu kala ba-techila malkin oto*—even if the degradation came at the very outset, he is still subject to lashing in that case.

The *pasuk* establishes the necessary sequence: *ve-hikkahu... ve-niklah*—first the lashing, then the degradation. If the degradation preceded the lashing entirely, it does not fulfill the Torah's intended order. The offender must first receive the lashes and then emerge from the procedure degraded—not the reverse. *Ve-lo she-lakah kvar be-veit din*—the lashing in the *beit din* must precede the *nikleh*.

## Malkot & Karatah

### Kareit Absolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת שֶׁלָּקוּ – נִפְטְרוּ יְדֵי כְּרִיתָתָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִקְלָה אָחִיךָ לְעֵינֶיךָ״ – כְּשֶׁלָּקָה הֲרֵי הוּא כְּאָחִיךָ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All those liable** to receive ***karet* who were flogged are exempted from their** punishment of ***karet*, as it is stated: "And your brother shall be debased before your eyes"** (Deuteronomy 25:3), indicating: **Once he is flogged he is as your brother,** as his sin has been atoned and he is no longer excised from the Jewish people; this is **the statement of Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel.**

We arrive at the final *mishnah* of *Makkot*, and it is a remarkable one. *Kol chayavei keritot she-laku nifteru al yedei kritatan*—all those who are liable for *kareit*, if they received lashes, are absolved of their *kareit*. The textual basis: the proximity of the *kareit* passages to the verse *veniklah achicha le'einecha*—"and your brother will be degraded before your eyes." Once he has been degraded, *harei hu ke-achicha*—he is restored as your brother. These are the words of Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel.

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מָה אִם הָעוֹבֵר עֲבֵירָה אַחַת נוֹטֵל נַפְשׁוֹ עָלֶיהָ, הָעוֹשֶׂה מִצְוָה אַחַת עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה שֶׁתִּנָּתֵן לוֹ נַפְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel says: And if** for **one who performs one transgression his soul is taken for it,** as one's soul can be uprooted from the world for one transgression, for **one who performs a single mitzva,** it is **all the more so** the case **that his soul will be given to him,** as the reward for performing mitzvot is greater than the punishment for performing transgressions.

Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel continues: *mah im over aveirah achat noten nafsho aleha, ha-oseh mitzvah achat al achat kamah va-chamah she-tinaten lo nafsho*—if one who commits a single transgression may forfeit his life on account of it, then one who performs a single *mitzvah* surely receives his life back, and more, *kal va-chomer*.

### Reward Ratio

Rashi's comment on this *kal va-chomer* is illuminating. The phrase *she-tinaten lo nafsho* reflects the principle that *midah tovah merubah mi-midat pur'anut*—the measure of divine goodness exceeds the measure of divine punishment. The ratio is *echad me-chamesh me'ot*—one to five hundred. How so? In the measure of punishment, the verse states *poked avon avot al banim al shileshim ve-al rive'im*—sin is visited upon the sons to the third and fourth generation, which gives us the factor of four. In the measure of goodness, the verse states *oseh chesed la-alafim*—kindness is done to thousands. The minimum plural of "thousands" is two thousand. Two thousand divided by four equals five hundred. The measure of goodness therefore exceeds the measure of punishment by a factor of at least five hundred to one.

### Shimon Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: מִמְּקוֹמוֹ הוּא לָמֵד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְנִכְרְתוּ הַנְּפָשׁוֹת הָעֹשֹׂת וְגוֹ׳״
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: It is derived from its** own **place** in the Torah, **as it is stated** at the conclusion of the passage discussing intercourse with forbidden relatives, which is punishable with *karet*: **"And the souls that perform** them **shall be excised"** (Leviticus 18:29), **and it states** toward the beginning of that chapter:

Rabbi Shimon offers an alternative derivation that dispenses with the *kal va-chomer* entirely. He says: *mimkomo hu lomed*—we learn it from the verses themselves. The *pasuk* of *ve-nichretu ha-nefashot ha-osot* (those who commit transgressions shall be cut off) stands in proximity to *asher ya'aseh otam ha-adam va-chai ba-hem*—"which a person shall do and live by them." We are now at the top of 23b. The juxtaposition of *kareit* and *chai bahem* teaches directly that life is the reward for observance, without any inferential structure required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֲשֶׁר יַעֲשֶׂה אֹתָם הָאָדָם וָחַי בָּהֶם״. הָא הַיּוֹשֵׁב וְלָא עָבַר עֲבֵירָה נוֹתְנִין לוֹ שָׂכָר כָּעוֹשֶׂה מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **"That a person shall perform and live by them"** (Leviticus 18:5). It is inferred **that** with regard to **one who sits and did not perform a transgression,** God **gives him a reward like** that received by one who **performs a mitzva.**

This leads to a striking corollary: *ha kol ha-yoshev ve-lo avar aveirah notnim lo sachar ke-oseh mitzvah*—anyone who sits and refrains from a transgression is given the reward as though he had performed a positive *mitzvah*. The mere absence of sin, the deliberate non-act, is credited as a positive achievement.

### Blood Abstention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״רַק חֲזַק לְבִלְתִּי אֲכֹל (אֶת) הַדָּם כִּי הַדָּם הוּא הַנָּפֶשׁ וְגוֹ׳״ וּמָה אִם הַדָּם, שֶׁנַּפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל הָאָדָם קָצָה מִמֶּנּוּ – הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִמֶּנּוּ מְקַבֵּל שָׂכָר, גָּזֵל וַעֲרָיוֹת, שֶׁנַּפְשׁוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם מִתְאַוָּה לָהֶן וּמְחַמַּדְתָּן – הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מֵהֶן עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה שֶׁיִּזְכֶּה לוֹ וּלְדוֹרוֹתָיו וּלְדוֹרוֹת דּוֹרוֹתָיו עַד סוֹף כׇּל הַדּוֹרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon bar Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** that as the verse **states: "Only be steadfast to not eat the blood, as the blood is the soul"** (Deuteronomy 12:23), it can be derived *a fortiori*: **And if** with regard to **the blood, which a person's soul loathes, one who abstains from its** consumption **receives a reward** for that action, as it is written in a subsequent verse: "You shall not eat it, so that it shall be good for you and for your children after you" (Deuteronomy 12:25); then concerning **robbery and** intercourse with **forbidden relatives, which a person's soul desires and covets, one who abstains from their** performance and overcomes his inclination, **all the more so that he and his descendants and the descendants of his descendants until the end of all generations will merit** a reward.

Rabbi Shimon the son of Rabbi extends this principle with a *kal va-chomer* of his own. The *pasuk* states *rak chazak le-vilti achol ha-dam ki ha-dam hu ha-nafesh*—"only be strong not to eat the blood, for the blood is the life." Blood is something that most people find instinctively repulsive; one would not eat it without being commanded otherwise. Yet by refraining from blood, one receives a reward. *Kal va-chomer*: *gezel va-arayot she-nafsho shel adam mitavah lahem*—theft and sexual immorality, which the soul genuinely desires—one who removes himself from those receives reward extending to his children, his children's children, and to the end of all generations: *al achat kamah va-chamah she-yizkeh lo u-le-dorotav ve-dorot dorotav*.

### Chananya ben Akashya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן עֲקַשְׁיָא אוֹמֵר: רָצָה הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא לְזַכּוֹת אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל, לְפִיכָךְ הִרְבָּה לָהֶם תּוֹרָה וּמִצְווֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״ה׳ חָפֵץ לְמַעַן צִדְקוֹ יַגְדִּיל תּוֹרָה וְיַאְדִּיר״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Akashya says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, sought to confer merit upon the Jewish people; therefore, He increased for them Torah and mitzvot,** as each mitzva increases merit, **as it is stated: "It pleased the Lord for the sake of His righteousness to make the Torah great and glorious"** (Isaiah 42:21). God sought to make the Torah great and glorious by means of the proliferation of mitzvot.

The *mishnah* closes with one of the most frequently cited statements in all of rabbinic literature: *Rabbi Chananya ben Akashya omer: ratza Hakadosh Baruch Hu le-zakot et Yisrael, le-fichach hirbah lahem Torah u-mitzvot*—the Holy One, Blessed be He, wished to confer merit upon Israel, and therefore multiplied for them Torah and *mitzvot*, as it is written: *Hashem chafetz le-ma'an tzidko, yagdil Torah ve-yadir*—"Hashem desired, for His righteousness, to make the Torah great and glorious."

The implication of Rabbi Chananya ben Akashya's teaching is that the abundance of *mitzvot* is itself an act of divine beneficence. A Torah with fewer commandments would have given us fewer opportunities for merit. Even those *mitzvot* that require little effort—or whose transgression is instinctively avoided—provide an occasion for reward. The comprehensive scope of *halacha* is thus not a burden but a gift, a divine strategy for maximizing the *zechuyot* of Israel.

## Kareit Dispute

### Yochanan Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חֲלוּקִין עָלָיו חֲבֵרָיו עַל רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב, תְּנֵינַן: אֵין בֵּין שַׁבָּת לְיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אֶלָּא שֶׁזֶּה זְדוֹנוֹ בִּידֵי אָדָם וְזֶה זְדוֹנוֹ בְּהִכָּרֵת. וְאִם אִיתָא, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בִּידֵי אָדָם הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel's colleagues are in disagreement with him** and hold that lashes do not exempt the sinner from *karet*. **Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that this is so, as **they say** in **the school of Rav** that **we learned** in a mishna (*Megilla* 7b): **The difference between Shabbat and Yom Kippur** with regard to the labor prohibited on those days **is only that** in **this** case, Shabbat, **its intentional** desecration is punishable **by human hands,** as he is stoned by a court based on the testimony of witnesses who forewarned the transgressor, **and** in **that** case, Yom Kippur, **its intentional** desecration is punishable at the hand of God, **with *karet*. And if** the statement of Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel **is so,** in both **this** case, Shabbat, **and that** case, Yom Kippur, the punishment would be **by human hands.** Apparently, the *tanna* of the mishna, the Rabbis, disagrees with Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel.

The *gemara* opens with a sharp rejoinder: *amar Rabbi Yochanan, chalukim alav chaveirav shel Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel*—Rabbi Yochanan states that Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel's colleagues dispute his ruling. The position that *malkot* can absolve *kareit* is not universally accepted. One can immediately see why the ruling is controversial: it opens the door to a kind of calculus whereby a person who is liable for *kareit* might seek to receive lashes—perhaps even engineering an early exit from the procedure through *nitkalkel*—as a means of shedding the more severe heavenly penalty.

### Yitzchak Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: הָא מַנִּי – רַבִּי יִצְחָק הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַלְקוֹת בְּחַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת לֵיכָּא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: חַיָּיבֵי כָרֵיתוֹת בַּכְּלָל הָיוּ, וְלָמָּה יָצָאת כָּרֵת בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ – לְדוּנוֹ בְּכָרֵת וְלֹא בְּמַלְקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says:** There is no proof from here that Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel's colleagues disagree with him, as in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna taught? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, who says: There are no lashes in** cases of **those liable** to receive ***karet*. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** All **those liable** to receive ***karet*** in cases of forbidden relations **were included in** the principle: "For whoever shall commit any of these abominations, even the people who commit them shall be cut off from among their people" (Leviticus 18:29). **And why was *karet*** in the case of relations with **one's sister excluded** from this verse and mentioned independently (Leviticus 20:17)? It is **to sentence** one who transgresses a prohibition punishable with *karet* **to** be punished **with *karet*** alone, **and not with lashes.** Other Sages disagree with Rabbi Yitzḥak (see 13b).

The *gemara* then invokes Rabbi Yitzchak: *de-amar malkut be-chayavei keritot leika*—Rabbi Yitzchak holds that *malkot* simply cannot be applied to those liable for *kareit*. They are not interchangeable punishments.

### Shabbat vs. Yom Kippur

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, זֶה – עִיקַּר זְדוֹנוֹ בִּידֵי אָדָם, וְזֶה – עִיקַּר זְדוֹנוֹ בִּידֵי שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yitzḥak and hold that there are lashes even in cases where there is liability for *karet*, there is no proof that Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel's colleagues disagree with him. The mishna can be understood as follows: In **this** case, Shabbat, the **primary** punishment for **its intentional** desecration is **by human hands, and** in **that** case, Yom Kippur, the **primary** punishment for **its intentional** desecration is *karet*, which is a punishment **at the hand of Heaven.** If he was flogged, he is exempt from *karet*.

The *gemara* frames the underlying conceptual issue through the well-known distinction between Shabbat and Yom Kippur: *ein bein Shabbat le-Yom Ha-Kippurim ella she-zeh zedono bi-dei adam ve-zeh zedono be-kareit*—the difference between them is that willful transgression of Shabbat is punished *bi-dei adam* (by a human court, through *skilah*), while willful transgression of Yom Kippur carries *kareit*, a heavenly punishment. If Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel's position were accepted fully—that *malkot* can substitute for *kareit*—then both Shabbat and Yom Kippur would ultimately be reducible to human-court punishments, collapsing the distinction between them. Rabbi Yitzchak's position is adduced from a *baraita*: *chayavei keritot be-chlal hayu*—those liable for *kareit* were originally included in the general category of lashing. The fact that the *kareit* of *achoto* (sleeping with one's sister) was singled out teaches that it is to be judged by *kareit* specifically, and not converted into *malkot*.

### Ashi Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava says** that **Rav says: The *halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel,** who ruled that lashes exempt the sinner from *karet*.

Rav Ashi offers a resolution that cuts through the apparent contradiction. *Zeh ikar zedono bi-dei adam, ve-zeh ikar zedono bi-dei shamayim*—in the case of Shabbat, the primary punishment is by human authority; in the case of Yom Kippur, the primary punishment is by the heavenly court. But "primary" does not mean "exclusive." There remains a dimension of *bi-dei adam* even in a *kareit* offense—and it is to that dimension that *malkot* speaks. The two punishments are not simply exchanged; rather, the *malkot* addresses the human-court dimension of the offense while the *kareit* dimension before heaven remains separately adjudicated. This preserves the distinction between Shabbat and Yom Kippur while still leaving room for Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel's ruling to operate.

### Halacha Decision

Rav Adda bar Ahava rules definitively: *halacha ke-Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel*—the *halacha* follows Rabbi Chananya ben Gamliel. Where the conditions are met—warning was given, *malkot* was administered—the *kareit* is absolved.

## Heavenly Court

### Three Decrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאן סְלֵיק לְעֵילָּא וַאֲתָא וַאֲמַר?! אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים עָשׂוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה וְהִסְכִּימוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה עַל יָדָם – מַאן סְלֵיק לְעֵילָּא וַאֲתָא וַאֲמַר? אֶלָּא: קְרָאֵי קָא דָרְשִׁינַן, הָכָא נָמֵי, קְרָאֵי קָא דָרְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yosef said: Who ascended on high and came and said** to you that one who is flogged is exempted from *karet*? That is not dependent upon the decision of an earthly court. **Abaye said to** Rav Yosef: **But** according to your reasoning, then with regard to **that which Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** There are **three matters** that the **earthly court implemented and the heavenly court agreed with them,** the same question applies: **Who ascended on high and came and said** to him that this is so? **Rather,** in arriving at Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's conclusion **we** homiletically **interpret verses. Here too,** with regard to lashes and *karet*, **we** homiletically **interpret verses.**

Rav Yosef raises the obvious question: *man salik le-eila ve-ata ve-amar*—who went up to the heavens and returned to tell us that the heavenly court agrees with our arrangements? Abaye responds: we know from what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi taught. But the same question applies to his teaching too. The *gemara* resolves: *ela kraika darshinan*—we derive it from *psukim*. The claim that heavenly agreement was obtained is itself grounded in textual inference.

### Megillah Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים עָשׂוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַטָּה וְהִסְכִּימוּ בֵּית דִּין שֶׁל מַעְלָה עַל יָדָם, [אֵלּוּ הֵן]: מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה, וּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם [בַּשֵּׁם], וַהֲבָאַת מַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to **the** matter **itself, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** There are **three matters** that the **earthly court implemented and the heavenly court agreed with them, and these are they: Reading the Scroll** of Esther on Purim, **and greeting** another **with the name of God, and bringing** the first **tithe** to the Temple treasury in Jerusalem. From where is it derived that the heavenly court agreed with them?

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi teaches: *shelosha devarim asu Beit Din shel Mata, ve-hiskimu Beit Din shel Ma'ala al yadeihem*—three things were enacted by the earthly court, and the heavenly court ratified them.

One must immediately ask: why only three? Surely there are many *rabbinic* decrees. One resolution offered is that these three instances occurred during the era of prophecy, when a direct channel between the earthly and heavenly realms still existed. Other rabbinic enactments were made after prophecy had ceased, and their authority rests on different grounds. (We are familiar with other instances of *bat kol*—in the case of *tanuro shel Achnai*, or the *bat kol* about *Shammai and Hillel*, *eilu ve-eilu divrei Elokim chayim*—but those are not numbered among these three.)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקְרָא מְגִילָּה – דִּכְתִיב ״קִיְּמוּ וְקִבְּלוּ הַיְּהוּדִים״ – קִיְּימוּ לְמַעְלָה מַה שֶּׁקִּבְּלוּ לְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reading the Scroll** of Esther is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "The Jews confirmed, and they took upon themselves"** (Esther 9:27). The verse could have simply said: They took upon themselves. From the formulation of the verse it is interpreted: **They confirmed above** in Heaven that **which they took upon themselves below** on earth.

The first: *mikra megillah*—the reading of the *Megillah* on Purim. The textual basis is the verse at the end of the *Megillah*: *kiyemu ve-kiblu ha-yehudim*—"the Jews fulfilled and accepted." The duality is read as: *kiyemu le-ma'alah mah she-kiblu le-mata*—they fulfilled on high what they accepted below.

One immediately wants to ask: if the *Megillah* reading received heavenly ratification, why did *Hanukkah* not? The *gemara* does not address this here; it enumerates only these three.

### Shalom Greeting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁאֵילַת שָׁלוֹם – דִּכְתִיב ״וְהִנֵּה בֹעַז בָּא מִבֵּית לֶחֶם וַיֹּאמֶר לַקּוֹצְרִים ה׳ עִמָּכֶם״, וְאוֹמֵר ״ה׳ עִמְּךָ גִּבּוֹר הֶחָיִל״. מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא בּוֹעַז הוּא דַּעֲבַד מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ, וּמִשְּׁמַיָּא לָא אַסְכִּימוּ עַל יְדֵיהּ – תָּא שְׁמַע: וְאוֹמֵר ״ה׳ עִמְּךָ גִּבּוֹר הֶחָיִל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And greeting** another with the name of God is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "And presently Boaz came from Bethlehem and said to the harvesters: The Lord is with you, and they said to him: May the Lord bless you"** (Ruth 2:4). **And it states:** "And the angel of the Lord appeared to him and said to him: **The Lord is with you, mighty man of valor"** (Judges 6:12). The Gemara asks: **What** is the reason that the Gemara cites the additional source about Gideon, introduced with the phrase: **And it states?** Why was the proof from Boaz's statement to the harvesters insufficient? The Gemara explains: **And if you would say: It is Boaz who did** so **on his own, and from Heaven they did not agree with him; come** and **hear** proof, **and it says: "The Lord is with you, mighty man of valor."** The angel greeted Gideon with the name of God, indicating that there is agreement in Heaven that this is an acceptable form of greeting.

The second: *she'ilat shalom be-shem*—greeting another person using the divine name. The basis is two verses. First, from Megillat Rut: *ve-hineh Boaz ba mi-Bet Lechem va-yomer la-kotzrim Hashem imachem*—Boaz greeted the reapers in the field with "Hashem be with you." Second, from the episode of Gidon: *Hashem imcha gibor ha-chayil*—an angel greeted Gidon in precisely the same terms. The *gemara* anticipates the objection: perhaps Boaz acted on his own initiative and the heavens never affirmed it. The proof from the angelic greeting to Gidon—which occurred six generations earlier—establishes that this form of greeting carries divine sanction. It is a striking argument: the earlier angelic precedent retrospectively validates Boaz's practice.

### Ma'aser Decree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲבָאַת מַעֲשֵׂר – דִּכְתִיב ״הָבִיאוּ אֶת כׇּל הַמַּעֲשֵׂר אֶל בֵּית הָאוֹצָר וִיהִי טֶרֶף בְּבֵיתִי וּבְחָנוּנִי נָא בָּזֹאת אָמַר ה׳ צְבָאוֹת אִם לֹא אֶפְתַּח לָכֶם אֵת אֲרֻבּוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם וַהֲרִיקֹתִי לָכֶם בְּרָכָה עַד בְּלִי דָי״. מַאי ״עַד בְּלִי דָּי״? אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר רַב: עַד שֶׁיִּבְלוּ שִׂפְתוֹתֵיכֶם מִלּוֹמַר ״דָּי״.
>
> **Translation:** From where is it derived that the heavenly court agreed to the **bringing** of the first **tithe** to the Temple treasury in Jerusalem? It is derived from a verse, **as it is written: "Bring you the whole tithe into the storehouse, that there may be food in My house, and try Me now with this, says the Lord of hosts, if I will not open for you the windows of heaven and pour you out a blessing, that there shall be more than sufficiency [*ad beli dai*]"** (Malachi 3:10). This indicates that the heavenly court agreed that the first tithe should be brought to the Temple treasury. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of **"*ad beli dai*"? Rami bar Rav says:** It means that the abundance will be so great **that your lips will be worn out [*yivlu*], from saying enough [*dai*].**

The third: *hava'at ma'aser*—the bringing of *ma'aser* as enacted in the time of Ezra, when *kohanim* as well as *levi'im* were permitted to receive the tithe. The textual basis is from Malachi: *havi'u et kol ha-ma'aser el beit ha-otzar*—"bring all the *ma'aser* to the storehouse." The verse continues: *va-chanuni pa ba-zot amar Hashem tzeva'ot, im lo eftach lachem et arubot ha-shamayim*—"test me in this, says Hashem of Hosts, if I shall not open for you the heavenly channels." *Va-harikoti lachem bracha ad bli dai*—"and I will pour out for you blessing without limit." The *gemara* asks: what is *ad bli dai*? Rami bar Rav answers: you will have so much blessing that your lips will grow tired of saying *dai*—"enough"—and still the blessing will come.

## Ruach Hakodesh

### Three Instances

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הוֹפִיעַ רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ: בְּבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁם, וּבְבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁמוּאֵל הָרָמָתִי, וּבְבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁלֹמֹה. בְּבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁם – דִּכְתִיב ״וַיַּכֵּר יְהוּדָה וַיֹּאמֶר צָדְקָה מִמֶּנִּי״. מְנָא יָדַע? דִּלְמָא כִּי הֵיכִי דַּאֲזַל אִיהוּ לְגַבַּהּ אֲזַל נָמֵי אִינָשׁ אַחֲרִינָא [לְגַבַּהּ]! יָצָאת בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: מִמֶּנִּי יָצְאוּ כְּבוּשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a somewhat similar statement. **Rabbi Elazar says: In three places the Divine Spirit appeared** before all to affirm that the action taken was appropriate: **In the court of Shem, in the court of Samuel the Ramathite, and in the court of Solomon.** The Gemara elaborates: This occurred **in the court of Shem, as it is written** in the context of the episode of Judah and Tamar: **"And Judah acknowledged them and said: She is more righteous than I [*mimmenni*]"** (Genesis 38:26). **How did** Judah **know** that Tamar's assertion that she was bearing his child was correct? **Perhaps, just as he went to her** and hired her as a prostitute, **another person went to her** and hired her **as well,** and he is not the father. Rather, **a Divine Voice emerged and said:** It is **from Me [*mimmenni*]** that these **secrets emerged.** God affirmed that her assertion was correct and that it was His divine plan that Judah would father a child from Tamar.

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *be-shelosha mekomot hofia Ruach HaKodesh*—in three places the divine spirit made itself known. The same numerical structure as the previous *sugyah* applies here, and the same implicit challenge: why only three? These are the three that the tradition has preserved for us, and each one involves a *bat kol* playing a decisive role in a judicial proceeding.

### Shem & Yehudah

The first: *be-veit dino shel Shem*—in the court of Shem, the son of Noah, who according to tradition was still alive in the time of the Patriarchs and served as a *beit din*. The episode is the story of Yehudah and Tamar. When Yehudah recognized the pledges Tamar sent him, he declared *tzadka mimeni*—"she is more right than I." But how did he know the child was his? One could easily imagine that after Yehudah departed, another man came to her. *Dilma ki heichi de-azal ihu le-gabah, azal nami inish achrina le-gabah?*—perhaps after he left, someone else came?

A *bat kol* emerged and declared: *mimeni yatze'u kevushim*—from Me, from heaven, these hidden things went forth. Two interpretations appear in Rashi. One: *devarim ne'elamim halalu me'iti yatze'u*—these mysterious things emanated from the divine will; the *bat kol* confirmed that Yehudah's words *tzadka mimeni* were not merely a human admission but a divinely affirmed truth. The implication is that the lineage emerging from this union—including the royal house of David and, ultimately, the *mashiach*—was itself decreed from above.

### Shmuel Witness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁמוּאֵל – דִּכְתִיב ״הִנְנִי עֲנוּ בִי נֶגֶד ה׳ וְנֶגֶד מְשִׁיחוֹ אֶת שׁוֹר מִי לָקַחְתִּי... וַיֹּאמְרוּ לֹא עֲשַׁקְתָּנוּ וְלֹא רַצּוֹתָנוּ... וַיֹּאמֶר עֵד ה׳ וְעֵד מְשִׁיחוֹ... כִּי לֹא מְצָאתֶם בְּיָדִי מְאוּמָה וַיֹּאמֶר עֵד״, ״וַיֹּאמֶר״? ״וַיֹּאמְרוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! יָצָאת בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: אֲנִי עֵד בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, this occurred **in the court of Samuel, as it is written: "Here I am; testify against me before the Lord and before His anointed: Whose ox have I taken…And they said: You have neither defrauded us nor oppressed us…And he said to them: The Lord is witness against you, and His anointed is witness this day, that you have not found anything in my hand. And he said: He is witness"** (I Samuel 12:3–5). Based on the context, instead of the singular: **"And he said,"** the plural: **And they said, should have** been written, as the verse appears to be the reply of the Jewish people to Samuel's challenge, attesting to the truth of his statement. Rather, **a Divine Voice emerged and said: I,** God, **am witness to this matter.**

The second: *be-veit dino shel Shmuel HaRamati*—in the court of Samuel the prophet. When Samuel came before the people of Israel to install Shaul as king, he demanded that they testify: had he ever taken anything from them unjustly? They answered *vayomeru*—in the plural: *lo ashaktanu ve-lo ratzotanu*—"you did not oppress us and did not wrong us." Samuel then called upon Hashem as witness. The Torah records the response as *vayomer ed*—in the singular, not *vayomeru*. The singular verb is unexpected. A *bat kol* emerged and said: *ani ed ba-davar ha-zeh*—I am the witness to this matter. The shift from plural to singular marks the transition from the human response to the divine ratification.

### Shlomo & Mothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּבֵית דִּינוֹ שֶׁל שְׁלֹמֹה – דִּכְתִיב ״וַיַּעַן הַמֶּלֶךְ וַיֹּאמֶר תְּנוּ לָהּ אֶת הַיֶּלֶד הַחַי וְהָמֵת לֹא תְמִיתֻהוּ (כִּי) הִיא אִמּוֹ״. מְנָא יָדַע? דִּלְמָא אִיעָרוּמֵא מְיעָרְמָא! יָצָאת בַּת קוֹל וְאָמְרָה: ״הִיא אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** This occurred **in the court of Solomon,** when the Divine Spirit appeared in the dispute between two prostitutes over who was the mother of the surviving child, **as it is written: "And the king answered and said: Give her the living child, and do not slay him; she is his mother"** (I Kings 3:27). **How did** Solomon **know** that she was the mother? **Perhaps she was devious** and was not the mother of the surviving child at all. Rather, **a Divine Voice emerged and said: She is his mother.**

The third: *be-veit dino shel Shlomo*—in the judgment of Solomon. In the famous case of two women and a single living infant (with one dead), Solomon declared: *tenu lah et ha-yeled ha-chai*—give the living child to her. *Minaha yada?*—how did he know which woman was the true mother? Perhaps the woman who claimed the living child was clever enough to deceive him. A *bat kol* emerged and declared: *hi imo*—she is his mother. The *bat kol* confirmed the verdict.

### Abba Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מִמַּאי? דִּלְמָא יְהוּדָה, כֵּיוָן דְּחַשֵּׁיב יַרְחֵי וְיוֹמֵי וְאִיתְרְמִי – דְּחָזֵינַן מַחְזְקִינַן, דְּלָא חָזֵינַן לָא מַחְזְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: From where** do you draw these conclusions? None of these proofs is absolute. **Perhaps** in the case of **Judah, once he calculated** the passage of the **months and the days** from when he engaged in intercourse with Tamar **and it happened** to correspond with the duration of her pregnancy, he realized that her assertion is correct. There is no room to suspect that another man was the father, as the principle is: Based on that **which we see, we establish presumptive status;** based on that **which we do not see, we do not establish presumptive status.**

At this point, a voice identified as *Abba* challenges all three instances, arguing that no *bat kol* was necessary in any of them.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי, כּוּלְּהוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל קָרֵי לְהוּ בִּלְשׁוֹן יְחִידִי, דִּכְתִיב ״יִשְׂרָאֵל נוֹשַׁע בַּה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Samuel too,** no proof may be cited from the use of the singular, as on occasion the **entire Jewish people is referred to in the singular, as it is written,** e.g.: **"The Jewish people is saved by the Lord"** (Isaiah 45:17).

Regarding Samuel: *Shmuel nami kulhu Yisrael kari lehu bi-lashon yachidi*—the Torah often uses a singular verb to refer to *Yisrael* collectively. *Shema Yisrael*, *ve-ahavta*—singular verb, plural referent. There was nothing remarkable about *vayomer* requiring a *bat kol* to explain it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹמֹה נָמֵי – מִדְּהָא קָא (מְרַחַמְתָּא) [מְרַחֲמָא] וְהָא לָא קָא (מְרַחַמְתָּא) [מְרַחֲמָא]! אֶלָּא גְּמָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **Solomon too,** perhaps he reasoned that **due to** the fact **that this** woman **is merciful** and seeks to spare the baby **and this** woman **is not merciful,** it is evident that the former is its mother. **Rather,** Rava concludes: There is no proof from the verses that a Divine Spirit appeared in those circumstances; rather, there is **a tradition** that this is the case.

Regarding Solomon: one of the women was merciful and did not want the child killed; the other was not. *Midai hacha mirachma merachmata, ve-ha-lo kamerachmata*—it was obvious from their behavior which one was the mother. No *bat kol* was needed; natural reasoning was sufficient.

### Tradition Resolution

The *gemara*'s response is a single word: *gemara*. This is our received tradition. These are instances in which the *gemara* attests that a *bat kol* resolved the case, and we accept that tradition even in the face of logical challenges that might make the *bat kol* seem unnecessary. The arguments of *Abba* are noted and not refuted on their merits—they are perfectly reasonable—but the tradition stands nonetheless.

This is, in a sense, the fitting conclusion to a *masechta* that has repeatedly shown us the limits of what we can fully reconstruct from the text alone. We have the tradition; we work with what we have; and where logical reconstruction falls short, we return to what was transmitted. *Ela, gemara.*
