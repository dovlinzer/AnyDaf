# Sanhedrin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Vote Switching Rules (II)

### Monetary vs Capital

The Yerushalmi for Perek 4 Halacha 4 parallels the Bavli material we examine today. The Yerushalmi opens with a fundamental distinction between *dinei mamonot* and *dinei nefashot* regarding who may argue for which side. In monetary cases, *hakol melamdim zechut vechova* — anyone may argue either for the benefit or the detriment of a litigant, and a judge who argued one way may turn around and argue the other. In capital crimes, however, while anyone may argue for the defendant's innocence, not everyone may argue for guilt. More specifically: in capital cases, a judge who argued for guilt may reverse himself and argue for innocence, but one who argued for innocence *cannot* reverse himself and argue for guilt — *hamelamed zechut eino yachol lachazor u'lamed chova*.

### Yerushalmi Position

The critical question is at what stage this rule applies. Is it during the deliberations — the *masa u'matan* — or only at the moment of the final ruling, the *gmar din*? The Yerushalmi addresses this briefly.

*Rebbi* states: *ubilvad shelo yehu mechusarim lomar ish ploni atah zakai ve'atah chayav* — the restriction applies specifically when the judges are at the very last moment of the final ruling, where all that remains is the formal pronouncement of innocent or guilty. If, however, they are still in the midst of *masa u'matan*, then one may change positions. According to *Rebbi*, therefore, during the theoretical deliberations judges may argue both sides freely, but once the vote is being finalized, a judge who earlier expressed a view of innocence cannot shift to guilt. What precisely "locks in" a judge at that stage is not entirely clear from the text — whether it is having voted formally, or having stated at any point that he thinks the defendant innocent — but the effect is to strongly bias the final vote toward acquittal.

### Rabbi Yose Stricter

*Rabbi Yose ben Chanina* takes a stricter position: *afilu mechusarim b'masa u'matan* — even during the deliberations themselves, if a judge has argued for innocence, he cannot reverse himself and argue for guilt. His restriction thus applies not only at the moment of the final vote but throughout the entire debate.

The Yerushalmi's overall picture, then, is that by the time of the final vote everyone agrees you are locked in, and according to *Rabbi Yose ben Chanina* you are locked in even during the deliberations. The Bavli, as we shall see, presents almost the mirror image of this structure.

## Recalling Cases

### No Recall Guilt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מַחְזִירִין לְחוֹבָה. אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁטָּעָה בְּדָבָר שֶׁאֵין הַצַּדּוּקִין מוֹדִין בּוֹ, אֲבָל טָעָה בִּדְבַר שֶׁהַצַּדּוּקִין מוֹדִין בּוֹ – זִיל קְרִי בֵּי רַב הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **but** they **do not bring** him **back** to be judged with a claim **to find** him **liable.** Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: And this** is the *halakha* only in a case **where** the judge **erred with regard to a matter** for **which the Sadducees do not admit to its** validity, i.e., he erred in a matter learned from tradition or established by the Sages. **But** if the judge **erred in a matter** for **which the Sadducees admit to its** validity, i.e., a matter that is written explicitly in the Torah, **it is** a topic that you could **go learn in a children's school,** and such an error negates the verdict and is reversed.

Before turning to the Bavli's treatment of vote-switching, the *gemara* addresses a related but distinct topic: the doctrine of recalling a case. The rule stated is *ve'ein machzirin lachovah* — in capital crimes, you do not reopen a case in order to find someone guilty. This is not limited to situations where new evidence is brought; even a judge's recognition that he made a legal error does not automatically permit revisiting a conviction of innocence, based on the verse *venaki vetzaddik al taharog*. Once someone has been found innocent — rendered a *tzaddik* by the court — the prohibition against double jeopardy applies. Conversely, the obligation to avoid executing the genuinely innocent (*naki al taharog*) means that one always reopens a case if new grounds for innocence emerge.

### Mistrial Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טָעָה בְּנוֹאֵף וְנוֹאֶפֶת מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְּמוֹקְדָךְ יְקִיד, זִיל קוּץ קָרָךְ וּצְלִי. אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: טָעָה בְּנוֹאֵף – חוֹזֵר. אֶלָּא הֵיכִי דָּמֵי אֵין חוֹזְרִין? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁטָּעָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba asked of Rabbi Yoḥanan: What is** the *halakha* in the case of a judge who **erred with regard to** the judgment of **an adulterer and adulteress,** by ruling that only the man is liable but not the woman? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: While your fire is burning, cut your squash and roast it,** i.e., seize the opportunity to add this case to the principle I taught you earlier. It **was also stated: Rabbi Ami says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Concerning a judge who **erred with regard to an adulterer,** the court **revokes** the verdict. The Gemara asks: **Rather, what are the circumstances** where the court **does not revoke** the acquittal? **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In a case **where he erred** and acquitted the adulterer who engaged in sexual intercourse **in an atypical manner,** i.e., anal intercourse. The *halakha* that this is considered sexual intercourse is not explicit in a verse. Therefore, if a court acquits one so accused, the verdict is not revoked.

*Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *vehu she'to'ah bidavar she'ein haTzedukim modim bo* — the rule permitting recall (even for guilt in a capital case) applies specifically when the error was so blatant that even the *Tzedukim* would not have agreed with the ruling. This is actually a more extreme standard than *to'eh bidvar mishnah* — making a mistake against explicit, black-letter law. Since the *Tzedukim* generally reject the oral law, a mistake that even they would recognize as wrong is tantamount to ruling against an explicit biblical verse. That level of error is so fundamental that it constitutes no ruling at all — it is a mistrial — and the case may be retried even for guilt.

This has an important corollary: a *to'eh bidvar mishnah*, a violation of unanimous and explicit rabbinic law, is *not* sufficient grounds to reopen a capital case for guilt. Although the *gemara* elsewhere suggested that such an error might be so severe as to be considered no ruling at all, in the context of capital crimes even that level of error produces a binding result — or at minimum, precludes reopening the case. Only a mistake so gross that even the *Tzedukim* would call it wrong — analogous to what in a different context one might call a mistrial — permits retry.

The case of *to'eh bidvar she'haTzedukim modim bo* — error in something the Sadducees would recognize as settled law — is treated as *zil krei be'rav hu*, the kind of thing you could learn from a child, and is so utterly without basis as a ruling that the case is completely open to be reheard.

### Adultery Mistakes

The discussion of adultery cases reveals that even in areas of law that seem clear and unambiguous, the *Tzedukim* might still disagree with how certain details are defined. The question raised is whether a court that erred in a case of adultery would be permitted to recall the case — and the answer hinges on exactly what kind of error was made. If the court erred about what constitutes intercourse (such as whether atypical intercourse counts), that is not the kind of error that even the Sadducees would recognize as wrong, and therefore the case cannot be recalled. Only if the court made an error that the Sadducees would clearly recognize — a truly fundamental mistake — would recall be permitted.

The broader principle thus stands: the threshold for mistrial in capital cases is not mere *to'eh bidvar mishnah* but an error so fundamental that even the *Tzedukim* would agree it was wrong.

## Vote Switching Rules (II)

### Rav Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת: הַמְלַמֵּד כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁעַת מַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן, אֲבָל בִּשְׁעַת גְּמַר דִּין – מְלַמֵּד זְכוּת חוֹזֵר וּמְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: In cases of **capital law, one who** initially **teaches** a reason to deem the accused liable may then teach a reason to acquit, but one who initially teaches a reason to acquit him may not return and teach a reason to deem him liable. **Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to the time of** the **deliberations** of the court, **but at the time of** the **verdict,** one who initially **teaches** a reason to **acquit may return and teach** a reason to deem him **liable.**

The Bavli now presents a position that is almost the mirror image of the Yerushalmi. *Rav* says: *lo shanu ela b'sha'at masa u'matan* — the rule that you cannot reverse a position in favor of innocence applies specifically *during the deliberations*. But *aval b'sha'at gmar din* — at the time of the final vote — *melamed zechut chozer u'melamed chovah*: one who argued innocence may reverse himself and vote for guilt. In other words, whereas the Yerushalmi locked in votes at the *gmar din* but allowed free argument during *masa u'matan*, Rav holds the opposite: during *masa u'matan* you are locked in to the side you argued, but at the moment of final voting you may vote your conscience.

### Morning Vote Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: לְמׇחֳרָת מַשְׁכִּימִין וּבָאִים. הַמְזַכֶּה אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי הַמְזַכֶּה, וּמְזַכֶּה אֲנִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״. הַמְחַיֵּיב אוֹמֵר: ״אֲנִי הַמְחַיֵּיב, וּמְחַיֵּיב אֲנִי בִּמְקוֹמִי״. הַמְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה מְלַמֵּד זְכוּת, אֲבָל הַמְלַמֵּד זְכוּת אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְלַמֵּד חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (40a): **The following day,** i.e., the day after the initial deliberations, the judges would **arise early and come** to court. **One who** yesterday was of the opinion to **acquit says: I** said to **acquit, and I acquit in my place,** i.e., I stand by my statement to acquit. **And one who** yesterday was of the opinion to **deem** him **liable says: I** said to **deem** him **liable, and I deem** him **liable in my place. One who** yesterday **taught** a reason to deem him **liable may** then **teach** a reason to **acquit, but one who** yesterday **taught** a reason to **acquit may not** then **teach** a reason to deem him **liable.**

A *braita* describes the reconvening procedure for capital cases: since a capital verdict cannot be finalized on the same day deliberations occur, the court reconvenes the next morning. Each judge states his position: *hamazakeh omer ani hamazakeh u'mezakeh ani bimkomi* — I voted for innocence and I maintain my position; *hamachayiv omer ani hamachayiv u'mechayiv ani bimkomi* — I voted for guilt and I maintain mine. Then deliberations resume. Critically, *hamelamed chovah melamed zechut* — one who argued for guilt may now argue for innocence — *aval hamelamed zechut eino yachol lachazor u'lelamed chovah* — but one who argued for innocence cannot reverse to argue guilt.

### Challenge Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לַמׇּחֳרָת גְּמַר דִּין הוּא, וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, לַמׇּחֳרָת מַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן מִי לֵיכָּא? כִּי קָתָנֵי בִּשְׁעַת מַשָּׂא וּמַתָּן. תָּא שְׁמַע: דָּנִין אֵלּוּ כְּנֶגֶד אֵלּוּ, עַד שֶׁיִּרְאֶה אֶחָד מִן הַמְחַיְּיבִין דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּין. וְאִם אִיתַהּ, לִיתְנֵי נָמֵי אִיפְּכָא! תַּנָּא אַזְּכוּת קָא מְהַדַּר, אַחוֹבָה לָא קָא מְהַדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **But the following day is** at the time of **the verdict,** and the mishna rules that a judge who had said to acquit may not change his opinion. The Gemara questions this reading of the mishna: **And according to your reasoning, are there no deliberations on the following day?** The deliberations may resume on the next day. Therefore, one can say that **when** the mishna **teaches** that the judge may not change his opinion, it is **with regard to the time of** the **deliberations.** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the continuation of that mishna, which teaches that if the number of judges who deem him liable is one more than the number of judges who acquit, **they** continue to **deliberate** the matter, **these** judges **against those** judges, **until one of those who deems** him **liable sees** the validity of **the statements of those who acquit** and changes his position, as the court does not condemn someone to death by a majority of one judge. The Gemara states its proof: **And if it is so** that one who initially **teaches** a reason **to acquit may return and teach** a reason to deem him **liable, let** the mishna **also teach the opposite** possibility. The Gemara explains: **The *tanna* is searching for** scenarios of **acquittal,** he **is not searching for** scenarios of **liability.** It may be that the *halakha* is the same in the opposite case, but the *tanna* prefers to employ an example of acquittal.

This *braita* appears to challenge Rav's position, since it describes what happens the next morning when the court reconvenes for the final vote — and it says you cannot switch from innocence to guilt even then. The *gemara* responds: *u'leta'ameich lemacharas masa u'matan leka?* — just because it is the next day does not mean we are at the *gmar din*. Even on the second day there is still deliberation; what is described is still *masa u'matan*, not the final vote. The *gemara* acknowledges this is not what the passage plainly sounds like, but it is Rav's reading.

A further challenge: the *braita* describes the court working to get one of the judges who voted guilty to switch to innocence — *ad sheyireh echad min hamachayivim divrei hamazakin* — until one of those who voted guilty comes to see the arguments of those who voted innocent. If it were truly possible to switch from innocence to guilt in the final vote, why does the *braita* not describe the reverse scenario — getting one who voted innocent to switch to guilty? The *gemara* responds: *kama zechut kama hadar, chovah lo kama hadar* — the *braita* simply prefers to describe the case where the outcome is acquittal. In principle, it is equally possible for a vote to shift the other way.

### Dead Student Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: אֶחָד מִן הַתַּלְמִידִים שֶׁזִּיכָּה וָמֵת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ חַי וְעוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ. וְאַמַּאי? נֵימָא: אִילּוּ הֲוָה קַיָּים, [הֲוָה] הָדַר בֵּיהּ! הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ. וְהָא שְׁלַחוּ מִתָּם: לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, מוּצָא מִכְּלַל רַבֵּינוּ! ״אֵין מוּצָא״ אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **As Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina says:** In a case where there was **one of the students who** argued to **acquit and** then **died,** the court **views him as if** he were **alive and standing in his place** and voting to acquit. The Gemara asks: **But why?** According to the opinion of Rav, that a judge may change his opinion at the time of the verdict, **let us say:** Perhaps **if** that student **were alive,** he would **retract** his opinion and find the accused liable. The Gemara explains: **Now, in any event,** he **did not retract from** his opinion. The assumption is that he would not have changed his opinion, although one can do so. The Gemara questions this explanation: **But** the Sages **sent** a statement **from there,** Eretz Yisrael: **According to** this version of **the statement of Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina,** he engages in **a dispute with our teacher,** i.e., Rav. Apparently, the previous explanation, which reconciles their opinions, is incorrect. The Gemara answers: That tradition was not accurate, and it **was stated** that he does **not** engage in **a dispute** with Rav.

*Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina* teaches: *echad min hatalmidim shezika u'mes, ro'in oto ke'ilu chai ve'omeid bimkomo* — if one of the students argued for a defendant's innocence and then died, we regard him as though he were still alive and still holding his position. One might ask: since when does a student receive a vote? The answer appears in context — *Rashi* says this applies *l'inyan haminyan*, for purposes of the count — which raises questions we do not fully resolve here. The scenario seems to be one where a student has declared that he has an argument for innocence (perhaps as the defendant is being led out for execution), and then dies before articulating it; the court reopens the case nonetheless.

The challenge to Rav is this: if you can switch your vote at the *gmar din*, why does the ruling of the dead student remain fixed? Perhaps, had he lived, he would have reversed himself. The *gemara* answers: *hashta miha lo hadar bei* — since in fact he did not reverse himself before dying, we do not assume he would have, and we treat his position as fixed. This resolves the challenge to Rav without conceding his position.

A message was sent from *Eretz Yisrael*: *l'divrei Rabbi Yose bar Chanina motzi miklal rabbeinu* — the ruling of *Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina* seems to argue against our master Rav. The *gemara* responds: *ein motzi mi'itmar* — it does not in fact refute Rav's position, for the reasons just given. Nevertheless, the weight of the evidence runs against Rav, and his position that the final vote may be freely reversed stands in tension with the entire body of sources discussed.

## Recording Arguments

### Changed Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנֵי סוֹפְרֵי הַדַּיָּינִין עוֹמְדִים לִפְנֵיהֶם, אֶחָד מִן הַיָּמִין וְאֶחָד מִן הַשְּׂמֹאל, וְכוֹתְבִין דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּים וְדִבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא דִּבְרֵי הַמְחַיְּיבִין, לִמְחַר חָזוּ טַעְמָא אַחֲרִינָא וּבָעוּ לְמֶעְבַּד הֲלָנַת דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: After the initial deliberations, **two judges' scribes stand before** the court, **one on the right, and one on the left, and they write the statements of those who acquit** the accused **and the statements of those who find** him **liable.** The Gemara explains the proof: **Granted,** they write the **statements of those who find** the accused **liable** even though they may not change their opinions, as **on the following day they** may **see another reason** to find the accused liable, not the reason they gave the day before. **And** once this new reason is given, the court **is required to perform a suspension of the trial** until the following day, as they may not issue a verdict in cases of capital law on the same day as the deliberations.

The *gemara* addresses the role of the court stenographers. Two scribes stand before the judges — one on the right, one on the left — and record all the arguments made for innocence and all the arguments made for guilt: *v'kotvin divrei hamazakin v'divrei hamachayivin*. The morning after the first day of deliberations, these records are read back to the court.

One might assume the reason for recording is simply to preserve arguments so they are not forgotten. The *gemara*, however, offers a more precise and striking rationale for recording the arguments of those who voted for guilt. The reason is: *lemachar chazu ta'ama achrina u'ba'ei l'meivad halanat din* — because if the next day a judge who voted guilty now articulates a *different reason* for guilt, this triggers a renewed requirement of *halana*, the mandatory overnight waiting period before finalizing a verdict.

The principle is remarkable: even if a judge says guilty today and guilty tomorrow, if his *reason* has changed, today's reasoning must itself be slept upon. The logic is that *halana* is required not merely for the vote but for the specific reasoning underlying the vote. If yesterday I found the defendant guilty for reason X, and today I believe it is reason Y that makes him guilty, I must give myself another night to reconsider reason Y — perhaps upon reflection reason Y will also fall away. The record of yesterday's reasoning is therefore essential so that the court can determine whether a judge's current reasoning is continuous with or different from his prior reasoning, and whether an additional *halana* is required.

### Double Counting Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דִּבְרֵי הַמְזַכִּים, מַאי טַעְמָא? לֹא, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יֹאמְרוּ שְׁנַיִם טַעַם אֶחָד מִשְּׁנֵי מִקְרָאוֹת. כְּדִבְעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָמְרוּ שְׁנַיִם טַעַם אֶחָד מִשְּׁנֵי מִקְרָאוֹת, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין מוֹנִין לָהֶן אֶלָּא אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But what is the reason** the scribes write **the statements of those who acquit** the accused? **No,** the reason they write their statements is **so that two** of the judges **should not say one explanation** to acquit **from two** different **verses.** If two judges each say the same reason to acquit, but derive their reason from different verses, they are not counted as two votes. **As Rabbi Asi asked of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If **two** of the judges **say one explanation** to acquit **from two** different **verses, what is** the *halakha*? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to him: We count them only as one,** as it is clear that one of the derivations is in error.

The rationale for recording the arguments of those who voted for innocence is more complex. One might think: since a judge who argued innocence is locked into that vote and cannot reverse, why does it matter to record his reasons? The *gemara*'s answer is that the record is needed to prevent double-counting of votes. If two judges both argue that the defendant is innocent based on the same *halacha* but derive that *halacha* from different verses, this does not count as two votes — it counts as one.

The *gemara* formulates this as follows: *kedei she'lo yomru shnayim ta'am echad mi'shnei mikra'ot* — so that two judges should not count as giving separate votes when they offer the same reason derived from two different verses. The rationale: the Torah is not redundant. If one *halacha* is derivable from verse A, it cannot *also* be derived from verse B — that would make one verse superfluous. Therefore, while both judges agree on the *halacha*, only one of them is correct about its biblical source. Since one of the two derivations must be mistaken, the two positions collapse into one, and only a single vote is counted.

The record of reasoning is thus necessary to identify such cases: if I know that yesterday I voted innocent based on *halacha* X learned from verse A, and today you are voting innocent based on *halacha* X learned from verse B, the court knows to count us as only one vote, not two.

### Two Verses Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״אַחַת דִּבֶּר אֱלֹהִים שְׁתַּיִם זוּ שָׁמָעְתִּי כִּי עֹז לֵאלֹהִים״. מִקְרָא אֶחָד יוֹצֵא לְכַמָּה טְעָמִים, וְאֵין טַעַם אֶחָד יוֹצֵא מִכַּמָּה מִקְרָאוֹת. דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל תָּנָא: ״וּכְפַטִּישׁ יְפֹצֵץ סָלַע״ – מָה פַּטִּישׁ זֶה מִתְחַלֵּק לְכַמָּה נִיצוֹצוֹת, אַף מִקְרָא אֶחָד יוֹצֵא לְכַמָּה טְעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses the ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **From where is this matter** derived? **Abaye says: As the verse states: "God has spoken once, twice I have heard this; that strength belongs to God"** (Psalms 62:12). Abaye explains: **One verse** is stated by God and from it **emerge several explanations, but one explanation does not emerge from several verses.** Alternatively, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught** that the verse states: "Is not My word like as fire? says the Lord; **and like a hammer that breaks the rock in pieces"** (Jeremiah 23:29). **Just as this hammer breaks** a stone **into several fragments, so too, one verse** is stated by God **and** from it **emerge several explanations.**

*Rav Assi* posed this directly to *Rabbi Yochanan*: *amru shnayim ta'am echad mi'shnei mikra'ot mahu?* — if two judges give the same *halacha* derived from two different verses, what is the ruling? *Rabbi Yochanan* replied: *ein monin lahem ela echad* — it counts as only one vote.

The *gemara* asks: where does this principle come from? *Abaye* derives it from the verse: *achas diber Elokim shnayim zu shamati* — God spoke one thing and I heard two things from it (*ki oz le'Elokim*). This establishes: *mikra echad yotze le-kama ta'amim* — from one verse you can derive multiple *halachot*. But the converse follows: *ve'ein ta'am echad yotze mi'kama mikra'ot* — one *halacha* cannot emerge from multiple verses. The school of *Rabbi Yishmael* taught similarly: *u'kfatzish yefotzetz sela* — just as a hammer shatters a rock into many sparks, one verse yields many laws. But you do not need multiple verses for a single law.

### Sacrifices Digression

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי טַעַם אֶחָד מִשְּׁנֵי מִקְרָאוֹת? אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, כְּדִתְנַן: מִזְבֵּחַ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הָרָאוּי לוֹ. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לָאִשִּׁים, אִם עָלָה – לֹא יֵרֵד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הָעֹלָה עַל מוֹקְדָה״. מָה עוֹלָה שֶׁהִיא רְאוּיָה לָאִשִּׁים, אִם עָלְתָה – לֹא תֵּרֵד, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לָאִשִּׁים, אִם עָלָה – לֹא יֵרֵד. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, אִם עָלָה – לֹא יֵרֵד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הִיא הָעֹלָה עַל מוֹקְדָה עַל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ״. מָה עוֹלָה שֶׁהִיא רְאוּיָה לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ – אִם עָלְתָה לֹא תֵּרֵד, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהוּא רָאוּי לְמִזְבֵּחַ – אִם עָלָה לֹא יֵרֵד. וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ מַאי קָמְרַבּוּ? פְּסוּלִין. מָר מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִ״מּוֹקְדָה״, וּמָר מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִ״מִּזְבֵּחַ״. וְהָא הָתָם מִיפְלָג פְּלִיגִי, דְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֵין בֵּין דִּבְרֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶלָּא הַדָּם וְהַנְּסָכִים, שֶׁרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לֹא יֵרְדוּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: יֵרְדוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What is considered one explanation from two** different **verses? Rav Zevid says: As we learned** in a mishna (*Zevaḥim* 83a): With regard to certain items that are disqualified from being sacrificed *ab initio*, once they have been placed on the altar they are nevertheless sacrificed, but **the altar sanctifies** only items **that are suited for it,** as the Gemara will explain. The *tanna'im* disagree as to what is considered suited for the altar. **Rabbi Yehoshua says: Any** item **that is suited to** be consumed by **the fire** on the altar, e.g., burnt-offerings and the portions of other offerings burned on the altar, **if it ascended** upon the altar, even if it is disqualified from being sacrificed *ab initio*, **it shall not descend.** Since it was sanctified by its ascent upon the altar, it is sacrificed upon it, **as it is stated:** "It is **the burnt-offering on the pyre** upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:2), from which it is derived: **Just as** with regard to **a burnt-offering that is suited to** be consumed by **the fire** on the altar, **if it ascended, it shall not descend, so too** with regard to **any** item **that is suited to** be consumed by **the fire** on the altar, **if it ascended, it shall not descend.** **Rabban Gamliel says:** With regard to **any** item **that is suited to** ascend upon **the altar,** even if it is not typically consumed, **if it ascended, it shall not descend,** even if it is disqualified from being sacrificed *ab initio*, **as it is stated: "It is the burnt-offering on the pyre upon the altar,"** from which it is derived: **Just as a burnt-offering that is fit for the altar, if it ascended, it shall not descend, so too any** item **that is fit for the altar, if it ascended, it shall not descend.** Rav Zevid explains: **And what do the two of them include** by means of these explanations? **Disqualified** offerings, teaching that if they ascend they do not descend. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yehoshua, **brings** proof for **this** *halakha* **from** the term **"on the pyre," and** one **Sage,** Rabban Gamliel, **brings** proof to **this** *halakha* **from** the term "upon the **altar."** This is an example of one explanation from two different verses. The Gemara questions this example: **But there,** Rabbi Yehoshua and Rabban Gamliel **disagree,** and their explanations cannot therefore be identical. **As the latter clause** of that mishna **teaches: The difference between the statement of Rabban Gamliel and the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua is only** with regard to disqualified **blood and libations,** which are not consumed by the fire, but do ascend onto the altar, **as Rabban Gamliel says: They shall not descend,** as they are fit to ascend on the altar, **and Rabbi Yehoshua says: They shall descend,** as they are not burned on the altar.

The *gemara* now attempts to provide a concrete example of two authorities deriving the identical *halacha* from two different verses, in the context of sacrificial law. The *mishna* in *negaim* (used here for this purpose) states: *mizbeach mekadesh et hara'uy lo* — the altar sanctifies what is fit for it. Specifically, an invalid sacrifice (*pasul*) that has been placed on the altar is not brought back down.

*Rabbi Yehoshua* says: *kol hara'uy le'ishim im ala lo yered* — anything fit to be burned on the fire, once placed on the woodpile, is not brought back down. He derives this from *ha'ola al mukdah hamizbeach* — the *olah* on the woodpile — learning from the extra word *al hamukdah* that anything placed on the fire as an *olah* is treated accordingly.

*Rabban Gamliel* says: *kol hara'uy lamizbeach im ala lo yered* — anything fit for the altar, once placed up, is not brought back down. He derives this from the phrase *hi ha'ola al mukdah al hamizbeach*, emphasizing *al hamizbeach* — on the altar — rather than the woodpile.

The *gemara* asks: what are they each including that the other does not? Both seem to be including invalid *korbanot*. One learns from *mukdah* and one from *mizbeach* — same *halacha*, two verses. However, the *gemara* notes that in fact they are *not* saying the identical thing: *detani seifa* — the end of the *mishna* states *ein bein divrei Rabban Gamliel l'divrei Rabbi Yehoshua ella hadam vehanessachim*. Blood and wine libations are placed on the altar but are not burned on the fire. Therefore, according to *Rabban Gamliel* (who emphasizes the altar) they would stay up once placed there; according to *Rabbi Yehoshua* (who emphasizes fire) they would be brought back down. The two positions are not identical in scope.

### One Vote Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כָּל הַנּוֹגֵעַ בַּמִּזְבֵּחַ יִקְדָּשׁ״ – שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי, בֵּין רָאוּי בֵּין שֶׁאֵינוֹ רָאוּי? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כְּבָשִׂים״ – מָה כְּבָשִׂים רְאוּיִין, אַף כֹּל רָאוּי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״עוֹלָה״ – מָה עוֹלָה רְאוּיָה, אַף כֹּל רָאוּי. וְתַרְוַיְיהוּ מַאי קָא מְרַבּוּ? זְרִיקָה. מָר מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מִ״דָּם יֵחָשֵׁב״, וּמָר מַיְיתֵי לַהּ מֵ״אוֹ זָבַח״. וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: שָׁחַט וְזָרַק אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת; לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, חַיָּיב שְׁתַּיִם. הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אַף לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נָמֵי אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״שָׁם תַּעֲלֶה עֹלוֹתֶיךָ וְשָׁם תַּעֲשֶׂה״. עָרְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא לְכוּלְּהוּ עֲשִׂיּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa says:** An example of one explanation from two different verses is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: From the fact that it is stated: "Whatsoever touches the altar shall be sanctified"** (Exodus 29:37), **I** would **derive** that this applies to every item, **whether** it is **suited** to be an offering, **or unsuited** to be an offering. **The** following **verse states:** "Now this is that which you shall offer upon the altar: Two **lambs** of the first year day by day continually" (Exodus 29:38); from this I derive: **Just as lambs are suited** to be offerings, **so too, everything** that is **suited** to be an offering is included in this *halakha*. The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Akiva says:** The offerings discussed in this passage are each referred to as **a burnt-offering** (see Exodus 29:42). Therefore, I derive: **Just as a burnt-offering is suited** for the altar, **so too, everything** that is **suited** for the altar is included in this *halakha*. Rav Pappa explains: **What do the two of them include** by means of these explanations? One who performs **sprinkling** of the blood outside the Tent of Meeting. One **Sage,** Rabbi Yishmael, **brings** proof for **this** *halakha* **from** the phrase **"blood shall be imputed," and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Akiva, **brings** proof for **this** *halakha* **from** the term **"or sacrifice."** This is an example of one explanation from two different verses. The Gemara questions this example: **But doesn't Rabbi Abbahu say** that the difference **between them** is with regard to one who **slaughtered** the offering **and sprinkled** the blood, as **according to the statement of Rabbi Yishmael he is liable** for **only one** transgression, and **according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva** he is **liable** for **two** transgressions? The Gemara responds: **Wasn't it stated with regard to that** *baraita* that **Abaye says: Even according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva, he is liable** for **only one** transgression, **as the verse states:** "But in the place that the Lord shall choose in one of your tribes, **there you shall offer your burnt-offerings, and there you shall do** all that I command you" (Deuteronomy 12:14). **The Merciful One combined all the actions** with regard to offerings as one transgression. According to the explanation of Abaye, there is in fact no practical dispute between Rabbi Yishmael and Rabbi Akiva with regard to this matter, and it serves as an example of one explanation from two different verses.

*Rav Papa* then cites a *braita* involving *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* and *Rabbi Akiva*. *Rabbi Yose HaGlili* derives the rule — that only things *fit* for the altar remain up — from the verse *kol hanogeya bamizbeach yikdash* together with the subsequent verse about lambs (*kevasim*): *ma kevasim re'uyim, af kol ra'uy* — just as lambs are fit, so anything fit remains up. *Rabbi Akiva* derives the same rule from *olah*: *ma olah re'uya, af kol ra'uy*. The *gemara* asks: what are they each excluding? Presumably things completely unfit for the altar at all (*psulei*). One learns the exclusion from *kevasim* and one from *olah* — and perhaps here we have the same *halacha* from two different verses.

But *Rav Adda bar Ahava* points out: *olat ha'of psulah ika beinaihu* — they actually disagree about an invalid bird *olah*. The one who learns from *kevasim* (land animals) would exclude bird sacrifices from the "remains up" rule; the one who learns from *olah* would include even bird sacrifices. So again there is a difference in scope.

*Rav Ashi* then turns to a case involving *shchutei chutz* — slaughtering a sacrifice outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. One verse states *dam yeichashev la'ish hahu dam shafach*; another says *o zevach*. *Rabbi Yishmael* derives from *dam yeichashev* that one who merely throws blood on an outside altar also violates the prohibition — *lerabbot et hazorek*. *Rabbi Akiva* derives the same inclusion of throwing blood from *o zevach*. Both are including *zerika* — throwing blood — one from the verse about slaughtering, one from the verse about offering. *Rav Papa* identifies a practical difference: if a person both slaughtered and threw blood outside the *Beit HaMikdash* *beshogeg*, for *Rabbi Yishmael* (who learns from the slaughter-verse) they constitute a single prohibition and require one *korban*; for *Rabbi Akiva* (who learns from a separate verse about the offering) they are two separate acts and require two *korbanot*.

*Abaye*, however, disagrees: *afilu lederebbi Akiva nami eino chayav ela achat* — even according to *Rabbi Akiva* one brings only one *korban*. Why? *De'amar kra sham ta'aleh olotecha ve'sham ta'aseh* — the verse says all your sacrificial acts must be done *there*, in the *Beit HaMikdash*. *Arvinhu Rachmana le'chulei asiyot* — the Torah groups all forms of sacrificial action under a single prohibition, so even distinct acts in the same transgression produce only one *korban*.

According to *Abaye's* reading, there is now a genuine example of the same *halacha* — the inclusion of *zerika* in the *shchutei chutz* violation — derived from two different verses, with no practical difference between the two positions in terms of scope. This would, in the context of a capital case, constitute a situation where two judges both vote innocent for *exactly* the same *halacha* but base it on different verses — and such votes would count as only one.

The *gemara* notes that this phenomenon — where two *amoraim* disagree only about which verse a *halacha* is derived from, while agreeing entirely on the *halacha* itself — is a common form of debate in the *gemara*, sometimes described as *mashmaut dorshim ika beinaihu*. What is striking here is that it took such an extended discussion through sacrificial law to arrive at a workable example. One further puzzle remains: why does a *difference in scope* between two otherwise similar positions allow each to count as a separate vote, given that here too one must be wrong — either the *halacha* is as broad as position A or as narrow as position B? Once there is even a slight divergence in scope, however, the positions are not *identical*, and the principle of *ein ta'am echad yotze mi'kama mikra'ot* does not apply to them. They count as two votes, even though, logically, only one can be fully correct.

## Day vs Night Judgment

### Mishna Timing Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם וְגוֹמְרִין בַּלַּיְלָה. דִּינֵי נְפָשׁוֹת דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם וְגוֹמְרִין בַּיּוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The *mishna* states: In cases of **monetary law,** the court **judges during the day and may conclude** the deliberations **at night.** **In cases of capital law,** the court **judges during the day and concludes** the verdict **during the day** (though not on the same day).

The *mishna* states: *dinei mamonot danin bayom ve'gomrim balaylah* — monetary cases are tried in the day and may be concluded at night. *Dinei nefashot danin bayom ve'gomrim bayom* — capital cases are tried and concluded during the day (though not on the same day). The *gemara* asks: where do we derive these rules?

### Verse Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר פָּפָּא: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְשָׁפְטוּ אֶת הָעָם בְּכׇל עֵת״. אִי הָכִי, תְּחִלַּת דִּין נָמֵי? כִּדְרָבָא, דְּרָבָא רָמֵי: כְּתִיב ״וְשָׁפְטוּ אֶת הָעָם בְּכׇל עֵת״, וּכְתִיב ״וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״. הָא כֵּיצַד? יוֹם לִתְחִלַּת דִּין, לַיְלָה לִגְמַר דִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** concerning the time of the deliberations derived? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Pappa says: As the verse states: "And let them judge the people at all times"** (Exodus 18:22), indicating that the judgment can be during the day or at night. The Gemara challenges this explanation: **If that is so** that this is the source of the *halakha*, the court should be able to conduct **the initial** stage **of the trial** at night, **as well.** The Gemara explains: It is possible to resolve the matter **in accordance with** the statement **of Rava, as Rava raises a contradiction** between two verses: It **is written** in one verse: **"And let them judge the people at all times,"** indicating that the judgment can be during the day or at night, **and** it **is written** in another verse: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit** that which he has" (Deuteronomy 21:16), indicating that cases of inheritance are judged only during the day. Rava explains: **How** can these texts be reconciled? The verse referring to the **day** is stated **with regard to the initial** stage **of the trial,** and the verse that includes the **night** is stated **with regard to the verdict.**

The source is *v'shaftu et ha'am b'chol eit* — they shall judge the people at all times — which implies even at night. But another verse, *v'haya b'yom hanchilo et banav* — on the day he bequeaths to his sons — suggests judgment must occur in the day. *Rava* reconciles these: *yom l'tchilas din, layla l'gmar din* — the day establishes when proceedings must begin, while the verse permitting "all times" allows for concluding at night. The Bavli's *braita* also notes that *bedieved*, if a monetary case was judged entirely at night, *v'shaftu et ha'am b'chol eit* teaches that it is valid after the fact.

The *gemara* then notes: *matnisin d'lo k'Rabbi Meir* — our *mishna* does not accord with the view of *Rabbi Meir*.

### Rabbi Meir Linking

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּתַנְיָא: הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל פִּיהֶם יִהְיֶה כׇּל רִיב וְכׇל נָגַע״? וְכִי מָה עִנְיַן רִיבִים אֵצֶל נְגָעִים? אֶלָּא מַקִּישׁ רִיבִים לִנְגָעִים: מָה נְגָעִים בַּיּוֹם, דִּכְתִיב ״וּבְיוֹם הֵרָאוֹת בּוֹ״, אַף רִיבִים בַּיּוֹם. וּמָה נְגָעִים שֶׁלֹּא בְּסוֹמִין, דִּכְתִיב ״לְכׇל מַרְאֵה עֵינֵי הַכֹּהֵן״, אַף רִיבִים שֶׁלֹּא בְּסוֹמִין. וּמַקִּישׁ נְגָעִים לְרִיבִים: מָה רִיבִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְרוֹבִים, אַף נְגָעִים שֶׁלֹּא בִּקְרוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir would say: What** is the meaning when **the verse states** with regard to the priests: **"According to their word shall every dispute and every leprous sore be"** (Deuteronomy 21:5)? **And what do disputes have to do with leprous sores?** **Rather,** the verse **juxtaposes disputes to leprous sores,** teaching that **just as leprous sores** are viewed by a priest only **in the daytime, as it is written: "And on the day when** raw flesh **appears in him** he shall be impure" (Leviticus 13:14), **so too disputes** are judged only **in the daytime. And just as leprous sores** are viewed by a priest who can see, but **not by blind** priests, **as it is written: "As far as appears to the priest"** (Leviticus 13:12), **so too disputes** are judged by sighted judges, **not by blind judges. And** the verse **juxtaposes leprous sores to disputes,** teaching that **just as disputes** are judged by independent judges, **not by** judges who are **relatives** of the litigants, **so too leprous sores** are viewed **by** a priest who is **not a relative** of the afflicted person.

*Rabbi Meir* holds that matters of judgment (*rivim*) must be conducted under the same conditions as rulings on *nega'im* (*tzara'at* spots). His source is the verse *al pihem yihiyeh kol riv v'chol nega* — by their (*kohanim*'s) mouth shall be all disputes and all *nega'im*. Since the same verse governs both, *Rabbi Meir* reads them as linked by a *gezera shava*: the rules applying to one apply to the other.

### Gezera Shava

The linkage operates in both directions. Regarding *nega'im*, the verse *u'vayom hira'ot bo* — on the day it appears — and the phrase *l'chol mareh einei hakohen* — to the full vision of the *kohen*'s eyes — clearly establishes that rulings on *tzara'at* must be made in full daylight and with full sight. *Ma nega'im bayom, af rivim bayom* — just as *nega'im* are judged by day, so disputes must be adjudicated by day. This means, for *Rabbi Meir*, that not only must *dinei nefashot* begin and end by day (as the *mishna* holds), but *dinei mamonot* as well — you cannot even conclude monetary cases at night.

The linkage then runs in the other direction: *ma rivim shelo bekerovim, af nega'im shelo bekerovim* — just as relatives are disqualified from sitting in judgment, a *kohen* who is a relative of the afflicted person cannot rule on his *nega'im*. And: *im harivim bishlosha, af nega'im bishlosha* — if monetary cases require three judges, perhaps *nega'im* also require three *kohanim*? Here the comparison is cut off by the verse *u'va el Aharon hakohen o el echad mibbanav hakohanim* — he shall come to Aharon or to *one* of his sons — establishing that a single *kohen* may rule on a *nega*.

### Blind Judges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מָה רִיבִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה – אַף נְגָעִים בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה? וְדִין הוּא: מָמוֹנוֹ בִּשְׁלֹשָׁה, גּוּפוֹ לָא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהוּבָא אֶל אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֵן אוֹ אֶל אֶחָד״. הָא לָמַדְתָּ שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ כֹּהֵן אֶחָד רוֹאֶה אֶת הַנְּגָעִים. הָהוּא סַמְיָא דַּהֲוָה בְּשִׁבְבוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, דַּהֲוָה דָּאֵין דִּינָא, וְלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְלָא מִידֵּי. הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כִּסְתַם מִשְׁנָה, וּתְנַן: כׇּל הַכָּשֵׁר לָדוּן כָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד, וְיֵשׁ שֶׁכָּשֵׁר לְהָעִיד וְאֵין כָּשֵׁר לָדוּן. וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְאֵתוֹיֵי סוֹמֵא בְּאַחַת מֵעֵינָיו. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סְתָמָא אַחְרִיתָא אַשְׁכַּח: דִּינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת דָּנִין בַּיּוֹם, וְגוֹמְרִין בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **If** these two matters are juxtaposed, why not say that **just as disputes** are judged specifically **by three** judges, **so too leprous sores** are viewed **by three** priests? **And this** would be supported by **a logical inference:** If a case involving one's **money** is judged **by three** judges, is it **not** clear **all the more so** that the person **himself** should be viewed by three priests? To counter this, **the verse states: "And he shall be brought to Aaron the priest or to one** of his sons, the priests" (Leviticus 13:2). From this **you** have **learned that even one priest views leprous sores.** In any event, as opposed to the mishna, Rabbi Meir holds that disputes are judged only during the day. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain blind man who was** living **in the neighborhood of Rabbi Yoḥanan who would serve as a judge, and Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say anything to him.** The Gemara asks: **How did he do this,** i.e., allow the blind man to judge? **But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself **say:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the ruling of **an unattributed mishna, and we learned** in an unattributed mishna (*Nidda* 49b): **Anyone who is fit to judge** is **fit to testify, but there are** those **who** are **fit to testify but not fit to judge. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The latter clause serves **to include** one who is **blind in one of his eyes,** who is fit to testify but is not fit to judge. All the more so Rabbi Yoḥanan would agree that the unattributed mishna holds that one blind in both eyes is disqualified from serving as a judge. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Yoḥanan found another unattributed mishna,** i.e., the mishna here, which indicates that a blind man can serve as a judge: In cases of **monetary law,** the court **judges during the daytime and may conclude** the deliberations and issue the ruling even **at night.** Accordingly, judging cases of monetary law is not compared to viewing leprous sores, which is the source for disqualifying a blind judge.

*Rabbi Meir*'s position that *l'chol mareh einei hakohen* — the full vision of the *kohen*'s eyes — is required for *nega'im* extends, via the *gezera shava*, to courts: *af rivim shelo besumim* — just as ruling on *nega'im* requires full sight, so judges may not be blind.

The *gemara* notes that a blind man in *Rabbi Yochanan*'s neighborhood served as a judge, and *Rabbi Yochanan* did not object. This seems puzzling, since *Rabbi Yochanan* himself rules that the *halacha* follows an unattributed *stam mishna*, and a *stam mishna* states: *hakol kasher ladun kasher leha'id* — anyone fit to testify is fit to judge — *v'yesh she'kasher leha'id v'ein kasher ladun* — but there are those fit to testify who cannot serve as judges. *Rabbi Yochanan* himself identified this as including *suma b'achas me'einav* — one blind in one eye — who, he held, can testify but cannot judge, apparently based on *Rabbi Meir*'s requirement of full binocular vision derived from *l'chol mareh einei hakohen*.

The resolution is that *Rabbi Yochanan* found a different *stam mishna* that supersedes this one: our very *mishna* here, which permits concluding monetary cases at night. Since *Rabbi Meir* derived both the nighttime restriction and the blindness restriction from the same *gezera shava* to *nega'im*, a *mishna* that rejects the nighttime restriction by implication rejects the entire linkage to *nega'im*. Once the link to *nega'im* is severed, there is no basis for excluding blind judges from monetary courts.

### Why Mishna Precedence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אוּלְמֵיהּ דְּהַאי סְתָמָא מֵהַאי סְתָמָא? אִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: סְתָמָא דְּרַבִּים עֲדִיף, וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָתָנֵי לַהּ גַּבֵּי הִלְכְתָא דְּדִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what** way is **the strength of this unattributed** mishna greater **than** the strength of **that unattributed** mishna? Why would Rabbi Yoḥanan rule in accordance with this one and not that one? The Gemara explains: **If you wish, say** that **an unattributed** mishna that records the opinion **of many** Sages is **preferable,** as the mishna in tractate *Nidda* is written in accordance with the individual opinion of Rabbi Meir of the *baraita*. **And if you wish, say** it is **because** this unattributed mishna **teaches this** *halakha* **in the context of the *halakhot* of judgment.** Since this chapter is the primary source for all *halakhot* of judgments, the ruling written here carries greater weight.

Why does our *mishna* take precedence? Two reasons are offered: *stama d'rabim adif* — our *mishna* reflects the majority position, whereas the opposing *stam* is the individual view of *Rabbi Meir*; and *mishum d'tanei l'gabei hilchasa d'dina* — rulings that appear in the context of their relevant subject matter carry greater weight, and since our *mishna* directly addresses the laws of *din*, it is presumed more precise.

### Cloudy Days Inheritance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַאי ״וְשָׁפְטוּ אֶת הָעָם בְּכׇל עֵת״ מַאי דָּרֵישׁ בֵּיהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: לְאֵיתוֹיֵי יוֹם הַמְעוּנָּן. דִּתְנַן: אֵין רוֹאִין אֶת הַנְּגָעִים שַׁחֲרִית, וּבֵין הָעַרְבַּיִם, וְלֹא בְּתוֹךְ הַבַּיִת, וְלֹא בְּיוֹם הַמְעוּנָּן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכֵּהָה נִרְאֵית עַזָּה. וְלֹא בַּצׇּהֳרַיִם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁעַזָּה נִרְאֵית כֵּהָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its discussion of these opinions. **And what** does **Rabbi Meir interpret** from **this** verse: **"And let them judge the people at all times"**? **Rava said:** He interprets that it serves **to include a cloudy day,** teaching that although a priest does not view a leprous sore on a cloudy day, the court may judge a case on a cloudy day. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Nega'im* 2:2): A priest **does not view leprous sores** during the early **morning** when the sun is not in full force, **and** not during the late **afternoon, and not in a house, and not on a cloudy day.** This is **because a dull** white sore **appears bright,** and a bright white sore is deemed ritually impure. **And** a priest does **not** view leprous sores **at midday, because a bright** white spot **appears dull** and the priest will mistakenly deem it ritually pure. The priest views the leprous sores during the late morning or early afternoon.

Since *Rabbi Meir* derives the daytime requirement from *nega'im*, one might wonder what he does with the verse *v'shaftu et ha'am b'chol eit*, which implies judgment at all times. *Rava* explains: *l'atuyei yoma me'unana* — *Rabbi Meir* uses that verse to include cloudy days, which he would otherwise have thought to exclude based on the *nega'im* parallel. The *mishna* in *Nega'im* explicitly excludes examining *nega'im* on cloudy days (*velo b'yom me'unana, mipnei she'keheis nir'eit k'aza*), since conditions that make a dull spot appear bright or a bright spot appear dull compromise the assessment. *Rabbi Meir* does not extend that restriction to courts, because the verse *v'shaftu et ha'am b'chol eit* comes to permit even cloudy days for judgment.

### Bequest Day Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַאי ״בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתָנֵי רַבָּה בַּר חֲנִינָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: ״וְהָיָה בְּיוֹם הַנְחִילוֹ אֶת בָּנָיו״ – בַּיּוֹם אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת, וְאִי אַתָּה מַפִּיל נַחֲלוֹת בַּלַּיְלָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, מַאן דְּשָׁכֵיב בִּימָמָא יִרְתוּן לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ, וּמַאן דְּשָׁכֵיב בְּלֵילְיָא לָא יִרְתוּן לֵיהּ בְּנֵיהּ? דִּילְמָא דִּין נַחֲלוֹת קָאָמְרַתְּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְהָיְתָה לִבְנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל לְחֻקַּת מִשְׁפָּט״ – אוּרְעָה כָּל הַפָּרָשָׁה כֻּולָּהּ לִהְיוֹת דִּין. כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְבַקֵּר אֶת הַחוֹלֶה, רָצוּ – כּוֹתְבִין, רָצוּ – עוֹשִׂין דִּין. שְׁנַיִם – כּוֹתְבִין וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דִּין. וְאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בַּיּוֹם, אֲבָל בַּלַּיְלָה כּוֹתְבִין וְאֵין עוֹשִׂין דִּין, מִשּׁוּם דְּהָווּ לְהוּ עֵדִים, וְאֵין עֵד נַעֲשֶׂה דַּיָּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** does **Rabbi Meir interpret** from **this** verse: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit"?** He already derived from the juxtaposition to leprous sores that the court cannot issue a verdict at night. The Gemara answers: **He requires that** verse **to** teach the *halakha* **that Rabba bar Ḥanina taught in the presence of Rav Naḥman:** The verse states: **"Then it shall be on the day that he causes his sons to inherit** that which he has" (Deuteronomy 21:16). The addition of the term "on the day" teaches that it is specifically **during the day that you** can **distribute inheritances, but you cannot distribute inheritances at night.** Rav Naḥman **said to him:** That cannot be the *halakha*, as, **if that is so,** then it ought to be that it is only in the case of **one who dies during the day** that **his children inherit** from **him but** that with regard to **one who dies at night, his children do not inherit** from **him,** and this is not the case. Rav Naḥman suggests: **Perhaps you are stating** a distinction between day and night with regard to the **adjudication of inheritances.** A proof for this distinction is **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: A verse in the passage concerning inheritance states: **"And it shall be for the children of Israel a statute of judgment"** (Numbers 27:11), teaching that **the entire portion is placed** together **to be** considered a matter of **judgment,** subject to the procedural rules that apply to a matter of the court. **And** this is **in accordance with** the statement **that Rav Yehuda** says that **Rav says, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** In a case where there were **three people who entered** a room **to visit an ill person,** and the ill person desires to write a will in order to distribute his property following his death, if the visitors **wish** to do so, **they** can **write** his will and sign it as witnesses, and if **they wish, they** can **act** in **judgment,** i.e., they can act as a court in the matter, since they are three. Therefore, they can determine that the will has the validity of an act of the court and transfer the property to the heirs in their capacity as a court. But if only **two** came to visit the ill person, **they** can **write** the will and sign it as witnesses, **but they cannot act** in **judgment,** since three are required to form a court. **And Rav Ḥisda says:** This *halakha* was **taught only** in a case where the three came to visit him **during the day; but** if the three of them came **at night, they** can **write** the will and sign it as witnesses, **but they cannot act** in **judgment.** What is the reason that they cannot act in judgment on the next day? It is **because they are** already **witnesses** to the will of the deceased, **and** there is a principle that **a witness cannot become a judge,** i.e., one who acts as a witness in a particular matter cannot become a judge with regard to that same matter.

As for the verse *v'haya b'yom hanchilo et banav*, since *Rabbi Meir* does not need it to establish the daytime requirement (which he derives from *nega'im*), he applies it differently: like *Rabba bar Chanina* taught before *Rav Nachman*, *beyoma ata mapil nachalos ve'ei ata mapil nachalos balaylah* — rulings about inheritance are made during the day, not at night.

One might read this as saying that inheritance itself only takes effect in the day — that a person who dies at night does not transmit his estate to his heirs. *Rav Nachman* dismisses this immediately: *uman deshachiv beleily lo yarsun lei banav?* Surely the heirs of someone who dies at night inherit no less than any other heirs. What the verse must mean, rather, is: *din nachalos kamart* — the *adjudication* of inheritance must occur in the day.

This is the principle articulated by *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*: three people who enter to visit a sick man and find him distributing his property — and he then dies — may, if they choose, record what they heard as a *shtar* (documentary testimony) or function as a *beit din* and issue a judgment directly. If only two were present, they may only record testimony; they cannot issue a judgment. *Rav Chisda* qualifies this further: *lo shanu ela beyom* — this applies only if they heard the bequest during the day. *Aval balaylah* — if they heard it at night — *kosvin ve'ein osin din*, they can record testimony but cannot function as a *beit din*, because at night they are not a *beit din* but witnesses, *ve'ein eid na'aseh dayan* — a witness cannot become a judge.

Thus, for *Rabbi Meir*, the verse about bequests establishes not the daytime requirement for courts (he derives that from *nega'im*) but rather this specific rule: people who witness a deathbed bequest by night are witnesses only, and cannot convert their role into that of *dayanim*. The overall picture is that the *chachamim* permit monetary cases to conclude at night and allow blind judges, since they reject the linkage to *nega'im*, while *Rabbi Meir* maintains that linkage throughout, requiring daytime proceedings and full binocular vision for all forms of judicial ruling.
