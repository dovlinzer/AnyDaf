# Ketubot 54 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Daughters & Ketubah Stat…

### Bat Arusah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: בַּת אֲרוּסָה, יֵשׁ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, אוֹ אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת? כֵּיוָן דְּאִית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה — אִית לַהּ, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן כְּתוּבָּה עַד שְׁעַת נִישּׂוּאִין — לֵית לַהּ, תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** In the case of the **daughter of a betrothed woman,** i.e., a man betrothed a woman, fathered a daughter with her, and then died, does the daughter **have** the right to receive **sustenance** from his estate, **or** does **she not have** the right to receive **sustenance? Since** the mother **has a marriage contract,** as in this case the man wrote her a marriage contract after betrothing her, it may be argued that the daughter **has** the right to receive sustenance. **Or perhaps, since the Sages did not enact** any requirement for a man to provide his wife with **a marriage contract until the time of marriage,** the stipulations of the marriage contract do not apply until then, and therefore the daughter of this woman **does not have** the right to receive sustenance. Again, the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The previous session concluded with a series of cases involving doubtful *ketubah* situations — among them the case of *shneiyot*, women forbidden to marry *derabbanan*, where the Gemara left the question of whether their daughters receive *mezonot* unresolved with a *teiku*. We now turn to the opening of 54a, four lines from the top.

*Ba'ei Rava* — Rava poses a question: *bat arusah yesh lah mezonot o ein lah mezonot?* Does the daughter of a woman who was only *arusah* — engaged but not yet fully married — receive *mezonot*? The assumption here is that these are women who followed the practice of *nishei Yehuda*, who did not live together with their husbands before the formal *nisuin*, so the marriage had not yet been consummated.

The question turns on two competing considerations. On one hand, *keivan de-it lah ketubah it lah* — since an *arusah* does have the right to a *ketubah*, perhaps the *tnai ketubah* are operative, including the *mezonot* provision for daughters. On the other hand, *keivan de-la takinu rabanan ketubah ad sh'at nisuin leit lah* — perhaps since the Rabbis did not actually mandate the writing of the *ketubah* until the time of *nisuin*, this daughter does not benefit from those provisions, having never reached that threshold. The Gemara leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Bat Anusah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: בַּת אֲנוּסָה, יֵשׁ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, אוֹ אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת? אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּאָמַר יֵשׁ לָהּ כְּתוּבָּה מָנֶה. כִּי תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: יָצָא כֶּסֶף קְנָסָהּ בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa raises a dilemma:** With regard to **the daughter** of **a raped** woman, i.e., a man raped a young woman, married her, had a child, and died, does **she have** the right to receive **sustenance** from her father's estate **or** does **she not have** the right to receive **sustenance** from his estate? The Gemara comments: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, do not raise this dilemma, as he said** that a raped woman **has a marriage contract of one hundred dinars,** and therefore she is entitled to the stipulations of a marriage contract, one of which is that if she has a daughter with her husband, the daughter receives sustenance from the husband's estate. Rather, **let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that the **money of her fine fulfilled** his obligation to provide **her** with a **marriage contract,** i.e., since she has already received the fine in compensation for the rape, she is not entitled to further payment in the form of a marriage contract. According to this opinion, **what** is the *halakha*?

Rav Papa, an Amora from Bavel, now raises a related question: *bat anusah yesh lah mezonot o ein lah mezonot?* Does the daughter of a woman whose marriage was contracted because her husband raped her — an *anusah* — receive *mezonot*?

To understand the difficulty, one must recall the Torah's framework for this case: a man who rapes an unmarried young woman must pay a penalty and marry her, and is forbidden to divorce her. Given that he has no power of divorce, the usual protective function of the *ketubah* — serving as a financial deterrent against casual divorce — does not apply in the same way. The question, then, is whether a *ketubah* grounded in this situation has full legal force for purposes of the *tnai ketubah*, or whether its unusual nature diminishes the daughter's entitlement to *mezonot*. One might also note that upon the husband's death, the *ketubah* does become relevant, so the underlying concern is how we understand the nature and purpose of the *ketubah* in this case.

### Ketubat vs. Knas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה, לֵית לַהּ מְזוֹנֵי, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כְּתוּבָּה טַעְמָא מַאי — כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ, וְהָא — לָא מָצֵי מַפֵּיק לַהּ. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: It may be argued that **since** the mother **does not have a marriage contract,** the daughter **does not have** the right to receive **sustenance** from her father's estate. **Or perhaps** one should consider the following: **What is the reason** that **the marriage contract** was enacted? **So that** his wife **will not be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her. And this one,** his rape victim, **he cannot divorce her** by Torah law, as it is stated: "He may not send her away all his days" (Deuteronomy 22:29). It was therefore unnecessary for the Sages to require that he provide the woman with a marriage contract. However, the reasons for the stipulations included in a marriage contract, e.g., that his daughter receives sustenance from his estate, still apply in this case. Consequently, the Sages stipulated that these provisions still be granted. Yet again the Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

The Gemara clarifies that according to Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, there is no question: *de-amar yesh lah ketubah ketubat maneh* — he holds that the *anusah* receives a standard *ketubah* of a *maneh*, like any woman who enters marriage as a non-virgin. Since a proper *ketubah* is in place, the *tnai ketubah* apply and there is no question regarding *mezonot* for her daughter.

The question only sharpens according to the position of the *rabbanan*, who hold *yatza kesef knasah b'ketubatah* — the money paid as a *knas* by the rapist effectively stands in place of the *ketubah*. While this payment is technically made to the father, the assumption is that the father acts as a protector of his daughter's interests. Nevertheless, as the Gemara itself presses: the *rabbanan* may simply be saying that one cannot require multiple payments — the *knas* already paid counts, so no additional *ketubah* can be extracted. Whether this arrangement actually protects the wife's interests in the same fashion as a conventional *ketubah* is a separate question.

Given this ambiguity, the Gemara asks according to the *rabbanan*: perhaps *keivan d'leit lah ketubah leit lah mezonei* — if she effectively has no standard *ketubah*, the daughter receives no *mezonot*. But a counter-argument follows: *o dilma ketubah ta'ama mai kedei shelo tehei kalah be'einav l'hotzia* — perhaps the entire rationale of a standard *ketubah* is that it should not be easy for the husband to send his wife away. Since *v'ha lo matzi mapik lah* — this husband cannot divorce her regardless — those aspects of the *ketubah* concerned with deterring divorce are indeed inapplicable. Yet other *tna'im* of the *ketubah*, which exist for reasons unrelated to the divorce deterrent, might still be in force. The Gemara again concludes with *teiku*.

### House & Resources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתְּ תְּהֵא יָתְבָא בְּבֵיתִי וְכוּ׳. תָּנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״בְּבֵיתִי״ — וְלָא בְּבִיקְתִּי, אֲבָל מְזוֹנֵי אִית לַהּ. מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ מְזוֹנֵי נָמֵי לֵית לַהּ. וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught that one of the stipulations of a marriage contract is: **You will sit in my house** and be sustained from my property all your days as a widow. **Rav Yosef taught: In my house, and not in my hovel [*bikati*].** If there is no room for her in his house, the heirs are not obligated to allow her to stay there. **However,** even in this case, **she has** the right to receive her **sustenance** from the heirs. **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: She does not even have** the right to receive her **sustenance,** as she is entitled to sustenance only when she lives in her husband's house. If she resides elsewhere, for whatever reason, she does not receive this payment. The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Mar bar Rav Ashi.**

The Gemara now cites a *piska* — a passage from the *Mishnah* — which introduces the standard *tnai ketubah* formulation: *at tehei yatva b'veiti* — "you shall sit in my house" all the days of your widowhood. Rav Yosef transmits a *tannaic* tradition on this: *b'veiti v'lo b'vikati* — in my house, but not in my shack. That is, if the estate lacks a proper dwelling, the widow cannot be housed there, and she must presumably return elsewhere. However, *aval mezonei it lah* — even in such a case she retains her right to *mezonot*, which must be provided regardless.

Mar bar Rav Ashi goes further, taking a stricter position: *afilu mezonei nami leit lah* — even *mezonot* lapses if there is no house, since the absence of a house indicates an absence of resources altogether, and the *tnai ketubah* is therefore inoperative. The Gemara rules: *v'leit hilchata k'Mar bar Rav Ashi* — we do not follow this view, even though Mar bar Rav Ashi is among the latest Amoraim. This is a striking ruling, and it reflects the principle that even later authorities can be overruled when there is sufficient basis.

## Widow's Mezonot

### Shmuel's Proposal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: תְּבָעוּהָ לְהִנָּשֵׂא וְנִתְפַּיְּיסָה — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. הָא לֹא נִתְפַּיְּיסָה, יֵשׁ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת? אָמַר רַב עָנָן: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל: אָמְרָה ״מֵחֲמַת פְּלוֹנִי בַּעְלִי״ — יֵשׁ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת, ״מֵחֲמַת בְּנֵי אָדָם שֶׁאֵינָן מְהוּגָּנִין לָהּ״ — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** If a man **proposed to marry** a widow **and she agreed,** even if she has not yet married him, **she no** longer **has** the right to receive **sustenance** from the heirs of her previous husband. The Gemara comments: It may be inferred from here that if she had **not agreed,** even if the man had proposed marriage, **she** still **has** the right to receive **sustenance. Rav Anan said: It was explained to me personally by Mar Shmuel** that the *halakha* varies in different cases. If **she said:** I will not marry you **due to so-and-so, my** deceased **husband,** i.e., she still feels connected to him, **she** still **has** the right to receive **sustenance** from his estate. However, if she refused the offer **because** the men who approached her are **people who are unsuitable for her, she does not have** the right to continue to receive **sustenance,** as she has shown that in principle she is willing to remarry.

The Gemara now turns to the topic of a widow's right to *mezonot* during the period she remains in her late husband's household. Rav Nachman cites Shmuel: *tavu'ah lehinasei v'nitpaisah ein lah mezonot* — if a man proposed marriage to the widow and she agreed to marry him, she loses her *mezonot*. The reverse follows by inference: *ha lo nitpaisah yesh lah mezonot* — if she did not agree, she retains her entitlement.

But this raises an immediate difficulty. She has already entered the marriage market in some sense — if someone has proposed to her, is she not already in a position of potential remarriage? Rav Anan clarifies: *ledidi mefarsha li minei d'Mar Shmuel* — I can explain what Shmuel actually intended. If she declines the proposal by saying *amrah machamat ploni ba'ali* — "I cannot remarry because it would dishonor the memory of my late husband," then she is still considered to be living in true widowhood and retains *mezonot*. But if she declines because *machamat bnei adam she'einam mehuganim lah* — because the suitor is beneath her station — then she loses *mezonot*.

The distinction is between a refusal rooted in *lichvod hamet*, honoring the deceased, and a refusal grounded in personal social calculations. A widow who says "no one is worthy of replacing my husband" is in a different category from one who says "this particular suitor is not in my league." The latter indicates a willingness in principle to remarry, and once that is apparent, the basis for continuing to draw on the late husband's estate is diminished.

### Beautification Tests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: זִינְּתָה — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כִּיחֲלָה וּפִירְכְּסָה — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said:** If **she engaged in licentious sexual relations she does not have** the right to continue receiving **sustenance** from his estate, as she is not acting in a manner befitting a widow. **Rav Yosef said:** If **she painted** her eyes **and dyed** her hair she has clearly done so to attract men for the purposes of marriage, and therefore **she does not have** the right to receive **sustenance** from her husband's estate.

Rav Chisda presents a further case: *zanta ein lah mezonot* — if the widow has an illicit sexual encounter, she loses *mezonot*. Rav Yosef goes a step further in one direction: *kichla ufirkesa ein lah mezonot* — if she begins to adorn herself, put on cosmetics, and make herself attractive to potential suitors, that alone suffices to end her *mezonot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר זִינְּתָה, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן כִּיחֲלָה וּפִירְכְּסָה. מַאן דְּאָמַר כִּיחֲלָה וּפִירְכְּסָה, אֲבָל זִינְּתָה אִית לַהּ, מַאי טַעְמָא — יֵצֶר אַנְסַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: According to **the one who says** that a widow who **engaged in licentious sexual relations** loses her sustenance, **all the more so** if **she painted** her eyes **and dyed** her hair she loses her sustenance, as her intention to marry is evident. However, according to **the one who says** that if a widow **painted** her eyes **and dyed** her hair she forfeits her right to receive sustenance from her husband's estate, this ruling applies only to that particular situation. **However,** if **she engaged in licentious sexual relations she** still **has** the right to receive sustenance from his estate. **What is the reason?** Her evil **inclination forced her,** i.e., she did not make a decision to remarry but merely succumbed to temptation.

The Gemara then explores how these two positions relate to each other. The one who holds that a sexual liaison forfeits *mezonot* would certainly hold that beautifying oneself to attract suitors also forfeits *mezonot* — *kol she-ken*. But the one who holds that beautification alone suffices might still allow *mezonot* even after a single illicit encounter: *mahu aval zanta is lah. mai ta'ama yetzer ansa* — the *yetzer hara* overwhelmed her in a moment of weakness, but this does not necessarily indicate a settled intention to leave her widowed state.

This is a remarkable passage. It reflects either a profound sensitivity to the impulsive nature of human behavior, or a very sharp assessment of what deliberate preparation for remarriage actually signals. The Gemara then sweeps all of these rulings aside: *v'lo hi hilchata k'chol hani shema'tata* — the *halacha* follows none of these opinions.

### Court Demand Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵית הִלְכְתָא כְּכֹל הָנֵי שְׁמַעְתָּתָא, אֶלָּא כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ בְּבֵית דִּין — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **And the *halakha* is not in accordance with all of these statements. Rather,** the *halakha* **is, in accordance with that which Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: One who claims** the payment specified in **her marriage contract in court does not have** the right to continue receiving **sustenance,** as she has thereby demonstrated her desire to sever her ties with her late husband.

The operative *halacha* is instead the tradition of Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel — a different transmission from Shmuel than the one cited earlier, which the *Tosafot* notes. The ruling: *hatova'at ketubatah beveit din ein lah mezonot* — a widow who formally demands her *ketubah* in *Beit Din* loses her *mezonot* from that point forward. This and only this is the moment that ends her entitlement.

### Mortgaging Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: מָכְרָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, וּמִשְׁכְּנָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, עָשְׂתָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ אַפּוֹתֵיקֵי לְאַחֵר — אֵין לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. הָנֵי — אִין, אֲבָל תּוֹבַעַת — לָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does she **not** receive sustenance? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who sold her marriage contract, or used her marriage contract as collateral, or established her marriage contract as designated repayment [*apoteiki*] for** a debt owed to **another** individual, **she does not have** the right to receive **sustenance** from her husband's estate? The Gemara infers from this *baraita*: In **these** cases, **yes,** she forfeits her right to continue to receive sustenance. **However,** in the case of a widow who **claims** her marriage contract, **no,** she does not lose the right to continue to receive sustenance.

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge from a *Braita* listing additional cases in which a widow forfeits *mezonot*: *machra ketubatah* — she sold her *ketubah*; *mishkena ketubatah* — she pledged it as collateral for a loan; *asta ketubatah apotiki le'acher* — or she designated her *ketubah* as an *apotiki*, a form of security tied to a specific asset. In all of these cases, *ein lah mezonot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי, בֵּין בְּבֵית דִּין בֵּין שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין. תּוֹבַעַת, בְּבֵית דִּין — אִין, שֶׁלֹּא בְּבֵית דִּין — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this argument is incorrect, and one should make the following inference instead: In **these** cases listed in the *baraita*, she forfeits her marriage contract **whether** she took the action **in court** or **whether** she did **not** do so **in court.** However, with regard to **one who claims** her marriage contract, if she issues this claim **in court, yes,** she loses the right to continue to receive her sustenance, but if her claim was **not** issued **in court, no,** she has not forfeited this right.

The Gemara reconciles this: *hani bein beveit din bein shelo beveit din* — these *Braita* cases apply regardless of whether they were done through a formal *Beit Din* proceeding or not. The reason is that in each of these cases, the widow has effectively transferred legal control of her *ketubah* to another party, thereby demonstrating that she has moved beyond her dependent status. By contrast, the case of formally demanding the *ketubah* in court does not involve relinquishing control in this way. Therefore: *tova'at beveit din in* — if the demand is made through *Beit Din*, it is sufficient to remove *mezonot*; *shelo beveit din lo* — but an informal, out-of-court demand does not have that effect, and she continues to receive *mezonot*.

The broader question of how these laws apply today, given that the relevant *leshonot* of *tnai Beit Din* no longer appear in our standard *ketubot*, is a practical question that touches on the enforceability of the *ketubah* in contemporary legal settings — a matter the *acharonim* address.

## Geographic Variation

### Rav vs. Shmuel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכָךְ הָיוּ אַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם וְכוּ׳. אִתְּמַר, רַב אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּאַנְשֵׁי יְהוּדָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּאַנְשֵׁי גָלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna taught: **And the residents of Jerusalem** and of the Galilee **would** write the marriage contract **in this manner,** i.e., that if the woman is widowed, she may remain in her husband's house and receive her sustenance from his property throughout her widowhood. Conversely, the residents of Judea would write that she may live in his house and be sustained from his estate until the heirs decide to give her the marriage contract. **It was stated** that the *amora'im* argued over this issue. **Rav said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the custom of **the residents of Judea, and Shmuel said** that **the *halakha* is in accordance with** the custom of **the residents of the Galilee** and Jerusalem.

The Gemara now introduces a significant geographic dimension to the laws of the widow's *mezonot*, returning to the *Mishnah*'s text: the formulation that a widow may sit in the husband's house all the days of her widowhood. The *Mishnah* itself records two versions — that of *Anshei Yerushalayim* and *Anshei HaGalil*, according to whom the widow remains until she chooses to leave or until she demands her *ketubah*, and a parallel tradition tied to the wishes of the *yorshim*, the heirs.

*Itmar* — an Amoraic dispute is recorded. *Rav amar halacha ke'Anshei Yehuda* — Rav holds that the *halacha* follows the position in which the inheritors may proactively pay out the *ketubah* and thereby terminate the widow's *mezonot* and right of residence. *Ushmuel amar halacha ke'Anshei Galil* — Shmuel holds that it is the widow's choice, and she may remain until she decides to leave based on the criteria discussed above.

### Mechoza Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּבֶל וְכֹל פַּרְווֹדֶהָא נְהוּג כְּרַב, נְהַרְדְּעָא וְכֹל פַּרְווֹדֶהָא נְהוּג כִּשְׁמוּאֵל. הָהִיא בַּת מָחוֹזָא דַּהֲוָת נְסִיבָא לִנְהַרְדְּעָא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, שַׁמְעַהּ לְקָלַהּ דְּבַת מָחוֹזָא הִיא. אֲמַר לְהוּ: בָּבֶל וְכׇל פַּרְווֹדֶהָא נְהוּג כְּרַב. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָא לִנְהַרְדְּעָא נְסִיבָא! אֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי הָכִי, נְהַרְדְּעָא וְכׇל פַּרְווֹדֶהָא נְהוּג כִּשְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Babylonia and all** of its surrounding **towns [*parvadaha*] act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav; Neharde'a and all** of its **towns act in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel.** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain woman of Meḥoza who was married to** a man from **Neharde'a. They came before Rav Naḥman** to discuss her marriage contract. **He heard from her voice that she was from Meḥoza,** whose residents had a distinctive accent. Rav Naḥman **said to them: Babylonia and all** of its **towns act in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav. They said to him: But she is marrying** a resident of **Neharde'a. He said to them: If so, Neharde'a and all** of its **towns act in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel.**

The practical application of this dispute depends on geography: *Bavel ukhol parvadah nahug ke-Rav* — the city of Bavel and its surrounding environs follow Rav's ruling. *Nehardea ukhol parvadah nahug ke-Shmuel* — Nehardea and its surrounding area follow Shmuel.

An illustrative case is then presented: a woman from Mechoza — which falls in the Bavel-and-environs region — married a man from Nehardea. When her husband died and the question of *mezonot* arose, the parties came before Rav Nachman. Hearing her accent, he recognized her as being from Mechoza and initially ruled: *Bavel ukhol parvadah nahug ke-Rav* — her home region follows Rav, meaning the heirs could end her *mezonot* by paying out the *ketubah*. But when told that she had been married to someone from Nehardea, he reversed course: *Nehardea ukhol parvadah nahug ke-Shmuel*. The husband's geographic origin is the determining factor, since it is his unstated *tna'im* — his assumed cultural expectations at the time of writing the *ketubah* — that govern the terms of the agreement.

### Kav Measurement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד הֵיכָא נְהַרְדְּעָא — עַד הֵיכָא דְּסָגֵי קַבָּא דִנְהַרְדְּעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And until where** is the boundary of **Neharde'a? Up to** any place **where the *kav*** measurement **of Neharde'a is used.** The entire area that utilizes the system of Neharde'a measurements is considered part of its surroundings for the purposes of this *halakha*.

One might ask: how far does the Nehardean *parvadah* extend? The Gemara answers: *ad heicha de-sagei kava de-Nehardea* — as far as the Nehardean *kav*, their local unit of volume measurement, is in use. The geographic jurisdiction of Nehardea's custom is coextensive with the use of Nehardea's particular standard of measurement — a practical and elegantly concrete means of defining a region's legal culture.

## Widow's Clothing

### Count or Not?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר. אַלְמָנָה, רַב אָמַר: שָׁמִין מַה שֶּׁעָלֶיהָ. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֵין שָׁמִין מַה שֶּׁעָלֶיהָ. אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָבִין: וְחִילּוּפָהּ בְּלָקִיט.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated** that the *amora'im* also argued about the *halakha* of **a widow. Rav said:** When she receives the payment of her marriage contract, the court **appraises** the clothes **that are upon her** and deducts their value from the payment she receives. **And Shmuel said** that the court **does not appraise** the clothes **that are upon her. Rav Ḥiyya bar Avin said: And the reverse** is the case **with regard to a hired worker.** With regard to a hired laborer who lived with his employer and the latter bought clothes for him, Rav and Shmuel disagreed as to whether these garments are appraised and their value deducted from the worker's salary when he leaves his employer's service. However, in this case Rav claims that his clothes are not appraised, whereas Shmuel maintains that they are appraised.

A new dispute: *itmar — almanah: Rav amar shamin ma she'aleha, veShmuel amar ein shamim ma she'aleha* — when a widow comes to collect her *ketubah*, do we assess and deduct the value of the clothes she is wearing? Rav says yes, we count them. Shmuel says no, we do not. The underlying question is whether clothing provided to the wife during the marriage — or in the period of her widowhood, during which the estate was obligated to provide *ksut* — now belong to her outright or remain, in some sense, chargeable against her *ketubah*.

### Hired Worker

Rav Chiya bar Avin notes a striking inversion: *u'vechilufa belakit* — the positions are reversed when it comes to a hired worker who received clothing from his employer during his term of work. Whatever position Rav held regarding the widow, he held the opposite for the worker, and similarly for Shmuel. One can understand the logic running in both directions: for a wife, one might say that items provided by the husband during the marriage were intended as gifts and become her property, or conversely that they are chargeable expenses; for a worker, one might say that his salary was fixed and his clothing are extraneous costs, or that they are simply tools of his labor and not to be deducted. The reversal implies a genuine structural difference between the two cases, though the precise reasoning behind each sage's position is not made fully explicit.

### Orphan & Widow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא מַתְנֵי: וְכֵן בְּלָקִיט. וּמַנַּח בַּהּ סִימָנָא: ״יַתְמָא וְאַרְמַלְתָּא שְׁלַח וּפוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** Conversely, **Rav Kahana** would **teach: And likewise with regard to a hired worker,** their respective opinions are the same in this case as well. Rav rules that one appraises the garments, while Shmuel claims that one does not. **And he would apply a mnemonic** device for Rav's opinion: **An orphan and a widow, disrobe and remove** them. In other words, Rav maintains that both a widow and a hired worker, dubbed an orphan due to his typical poverty, should disrobe, as it were, when the court evaluates the payment to which they are entitled.

Rav Kahana preserves a *siman* — a mnemonic — for the ruling: *yatma v'almana shlach u'fok* — the orphan and the widow, strip off their clothes and leave. This striking formulation, attributed to Rav's position, captures the rule that both the worker (the *lakit*, referred to here by the charged term *yatma*) and the widow must account for what they are wearing. The *lashon* is notably unsentimental given the Torah's repeated concern for the widow and orphan, and the Gemara does not soften it. Rashi explains that the term *yatma* is applied to the worker because in being sent away without his clothing, he is accorded a kind of orphan's treatment.

### Rav Nachman Resolves

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אַף עַל גַּב דִּתְנַן בְּמַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב. דִּתְנַן: אֶחָד הַמַּקְדִּישׁ נְכָסָיו, וְאֶחָד הַמַּעֲרִיךְ אֶת עַצְמוֹ — אֵין לוֹ לֹא בִּכְסוּת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְלֹא בִּכְסוּת בָּנָיו, וְלֹא בְּצֶבַע שֶׁצָּבַע לִשְׁמָן, וְלֹא בְּסַנְדָּלִים חֲדָשִׁים שֶׁלָּקַח לִשְׁמָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: Even though we learned in a mishna in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, the *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav. As we learned** in a mishna (*Arakhin* 24a): With regard to **both one who consecrates his property and one who valuates himself** by donating his fixed value to the Temple, the Temple treasurer has the right to take **neither his wife's clothing, nor his children's clothing, nor** new **dyed** clothing **that he dyed** specially **for them,** even if they have yet to wear them, **nor new sandals that he bought for them.** This mishna is apparently in accordance with the opinion of Shmuel that a woman's garments are not considered her husband's property.

Rav Nachman rules that even though the *Mishnah* appears on a superficial reading to support Shmuel's position, the *halacha* follows Rav. The relevant *Mishnah* states that one who consecrates all his possessions, or makes a vow assessing his own value, does not count in that calculation the clothing he purchased for his wife and children, the dyes he bought for them, nor the new shoes he acquired for them — implying these items belong to them, not to him.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְכִי מֵאַחַר דִּתְנַן מַתְנִיתִין כְּווֹתֵיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אַמַּאי הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִכְאוֹרָה כִּשְׁמוּאֵל רְהִיטָא, כִּי מְעַיְּינַתְּ בָּהּ — הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Once we have learned a mishna in accordance with** the opinion of **Shmuel, why is the *halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav? He said to him: It would seem [*likhora*]** that this mishna agrees **with** the opinion of **Shmuel** when it is **skimmed through** and read superficially. However, **when you examine it** you will see that **the *halakha* is** in fact **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav.**

Rava challenges Rav Nachman: *v'chi me'achar d'tnan matnitin k'vatei d'Shmuel amai hilchata k'vatei d'Rav?* If the *Mishnah* reads like Shmuel, why rule like Rav? Rav Nachman responds: *l'ichora k'Shmuel rahita ki maayant bah hilchata k'vatei d'Rav* — at a surface reading it appears to favor Shmuel, but on deeper examination the *halacha* is actually like Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּי אַקְנִי לַהּ, אַדַּעְתָּא לְמֵיקַם קַמֵּיהּ. אַדַּעְתָּא לְמִשְׁקַל וּלְמִיפַּק — לָא אַקְנִי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reason** for this? **When he bought her** these clothes he did so **with the intention that she should stand before him** and wear them when she is with him. **He did not buy** them for **her with the intention that she should take** them **and leave** him. The reason for the mishna's ruling is in fact that the husband acquires the clothes on behalf of his wife, but this applies only if she is living with him. Consequently, if she is living with him, the treasurer has no right to them. However, if she leaves him, she has no right to them, in accordance with the opinion of Rav.

The reasoning: *ki aknei lah adata d'meikam kamei, adata d'meikam u'lmeifak la aknei lah* — when the husband purchased these items, he did so with the expectation that he would see his wife and children wearing them, with him present. He did not purchase them with the intention that they would take these valuables and leave his household. This reasoning connects to a broader theme running through this chapter: the Gemara consistently attempts an *umdan da'at* — an assessment of the presumed intent behind legal acts — to determine what a person actually meant when giving, writing, or committing to something. The clearest analogue, noted here as well, is the case of a husband who becomes mentally incapacitated versus one who abandons his family: in the former case *mezonot* continues because we assume he would have wanted it; in the latter it does not because his abandonment signals otherwise.

One might press the point: does this logic apply even to items given during the husband's lifetime, not just after his death? The *Mishnah* appears to discuss things given during life. The answer suggested by the discussion is that even so, the husband never intended those items to be extracted from his estate and carried away — whether in a divorce or upon predeceasing. A plausible counter-argument exists that this reasoning might apply specifically to divorce but not to the case where the husband dies and the wife is simply collecting what she is owed, though the Gemara does not pursue that distinction here.

### Court Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּלְּתָא דְּבֵי בַּר אֶלְיָשִׁיב הֲוָה קָא תָּבְעָה כְּתוּבְּתַהּ מִיַּתְמֵי. הֲוָה קָא מַמְטְיָא לְהוּ לְבֵי דִינָא, אָמְרִי: זִילָא לַן מִילְּתָא דְּתֵיזְלִי הָכִי. אֲזַלָא לְבֵישְׁתִּינְהוּ וְאִיכַּסִּתִינְהוּ לְכוּלֵּיהּ מָנָא. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבִינָא, אֲמַר לְהוּ: הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב, דְּאָמַר: אַלְמָנָה שָׁמִין מַה שֶּׁעָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: The **daughter-in-law of the house of the son of Elyashiv was claiming** payment of **her marriage contract from the orphans,** and **she was** in the process of **bringing them to the court.** The orphans **said** to her: It is **demeaning for us that you should go** dressed **in this manner,** in house clothes. We would rather you come in more suitable attire. **She went** and **dressed and covered herself with all of her clothes. They came before Ravina, who said to them: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav,** who **said** that with regard to **a widow,** the court **appraises** the clothes **that are upon her.** Therefore, the court takes everything she is wearing into account in the calculation of her marriage contract payment.

An illustrative story is recorded. A widow from the household of Rav Bar Eliyashiv was seeking to collect her *ketubah* from the *yetomim* and brought them to *Beit Din*. The orphans, wishing to make a favorable impression — or perhaps shrewdly aware of what was coming — told her it would be embarrassing for her to appear in such plain attire and encouraged her to dress well for court. She obliged, putting on all her finest garments and jewelry. When the parties came before Ravina, he ruled: *hilchata k'vatei d'Rav de'amar almana shaminan mah she'aleha* — the *halacha* follows Rav, who says we assess what the widow is wearing. The implication is that what she had on her at that moment was counted against her *ketubah*. It appears from the context that only what she is actually wearing at the time — not the entire contents of her wardrobe — is assessed. The story also illustrates the kind of tactical maneuvering that the law can invite, and one might observe that familiarity with the relevant *halachot* would have served the widow well here.

The ruling in favor of Rav in a monetary dispute is itself worth noting — the general principle is that Shmuel's rulings prevail in financial matters, making this a notable exception.

## Dowry & Prices

### Wine & Dowry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״נְדוּנְיָא לִבְרַת״. זַל נְדוּנְיָא. אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: פּוּרְנָא לְיַתְמֵי. הָהוּא דַּאֲמַר לְהוּ: ״אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזֵי מִן חַמְרָא לִבְרַת״. אִיַּיקַּר חַמְרָא. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: רַוְוחָא לְיַתְמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates another incident: **A certain** person **said to** his heirs, in his will: Give **a dowry to** my **daughter.** There was an established custom for the amount of money spent on a dowry, including clothing and jewelry. In the meantime, the cost of **a dowry depreciated,** i.e., all these items could be acquired with less money. The question arose concerning the difference between the amount the father wished to give her when he wrote the will and the sum they paid in practice. **Rav Idi bar Avin said:** The **profit [*purna*],** i.e., this difference in price, goes **to the** male **orphans,** not to the daughter. The Gemara cites a related incident: **A certain** person **said to** his heirs, in his will: **Four hundred dinars of** this **wine** you should give **to** my **daughter,** and **the wine** subsequently **appreciated** in value, so that some money remained. **Rav Yosef said:** The **gain** goes **to the** male **orphans,** i.e., they are entitled to the leftover sum.

A man who stipulated that his daughter should receive a standard dowry (*nidunya*) upon his death — without specifying an amount — and then died: if the standard dowry has decreased in value by the time of collection, Rav Idi bar Avin rules *purna leyatmei* — the orphans retain the benefit; the daughter receives the lower amount now in effect. Conversely, if a man said give my daughter four hundred *zuz* worth of wine as her dowry, and the price of wine subsequently rose, leaving her with less wine for the same nominal sum — Rav Yosef rules *revacha leyatmei* — again the orphans gain; they retain the appreciated commodity and the daughter receives only what the monetary value at the time of death would have provided.

### Stepmother Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָרִיבֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הֲוָה לְהוּ אִיתַּת אַבָּא דַּהֲוָה קָמַפְסְדָה מְזוֹנֵי. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֵיזִילוּ וֶאֱמַרוּ לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּכוֹן דִּנְיַיחֵד לַהּ אַרְעָא לִמְזוֹנַהּ. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁרִיבָּה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא אָמַר הָכִי! אֲמַר לְהוּ: זִילוּ הַבוּ לַהּ. וְאִי לָא, מַפֵּיקְנָא לְכוּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מֵאוּנַּיְיכוּ. אֲתוֹ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לְהוּ: מָה אֶעֱשֶׂה? שֶׁכְּנֶגְדִּי חָלוּק עָלַי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **The relatives of Rabbi Yoḥanan had a wife of** their **father who would diminish** his resources by spending wastefully on her **sustenance. They came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** to ask his advice. **He said to them: Go and say to your father that he should set aside** a certain portion of **land for her sustenance.** If she agrees to accept this land for her sustenance she has thereby relinquished her claim to the rest of the estate. **They approached** the court, but unfortunately heard from **Reish Lakish, who said to them: All the more so, he has increased** the sources of **sustenance** available **to her.** In other words, they are still obligated to support her from their father's estate according to the lifestyle she is accustomed to living, and if they do not, she may use the field to supplement what they give her. **They said to him: But Rabbi Yoḥanan did not say so. He said to them: Go** and **give her** all she requires, **and if not, I will remove for you Rabbi Yoḥanan from your ears,** i.e., I will treat you so harshly that you will forget Rabbi Yoḥanan's ruling. **They approached Rabbi Yoḥanan** to complain, but **he said to them: What can I do?** I cannot impose my opinion, **as** a man **equal to me disagrees with me.**

The relatives of Rabbi Yochanan — who had appeared before him on a previous occasion with a legal dilemma and received advice he later regretted — returned with a new problem: their stepmother, their father's still-living wife, was consuming the estate's resources at an alarming rate. They came to Rabbi Yochanan, who advised them: *izilu ve-imru le-avuchon de-nyached lah ara le-mezonei* — tell your father to designate a specific field from which her *mezonot* will be drawn. Once that field is exhausted, her claim on the estate ends.

The father followed this advice. When he later died, the stepmother demanded more than the field could provide. The orphans brought the matter before Resh Lakish, who ruled against them: *kol she-ken she-riba lah mezonot* — by specifying the field, the father actually added to her *mezonot* entitlement, on top of what she would have received in any case. The orphans protested: this advice came from Rabbi Yochanan himself. Resh Lakish was unmoved: *zilu havu lah, ve-ee la mapikna lechu Rabbi Yochanan mi-onaichu* — go and pay her, or I'll take Rabbi Yochanan right out of your ears. The *aggadah* recalls that Resh Lakish's history lent his threats a certain credibility.

The parties then returned to Rabbi Yochanan, who demurred: *mah e'eseh she-kenavchi chaluk alai* — what can I do if colleagues disagree with me?

### Grammatical Nuance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: לְדִידִי מִפָּרְשָׁא לִי מִינֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אָמַר ״לִמְזוֹנוֹת״ — רִיבָּה לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת. אָמַר ״בִּמְזוֹנוֹת״ — קָצַץ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** This matter **was explained to me personally by Rabbi Yoḥanan.** All depends on the husband's description of the land. If **he said** that he is giving her land **for** her **sustenance, he has** thereby **increased** the sources of **sustenance** available **to her.** He will continue to provide her sustenance, but if that amount is insufficient he has also set aside an area of land specifically for that purpose. However, if **he said** to her that he is designating the land **as** her **sustenance, he has** thereby **fixed** this plot of land as the only source of **sustenance** available **to her,** and she can take no more.

Rabbi Abahu then steps in to explain what Rabbi Yochanan had actually intended his advice to convey. The distinction is linguistic: *amar li-mezonot riba la-mezonot* — if the father says *li-mezonot* (this field is **for** her *mezonot*, i.e., designated as an additional provision), he has added to her entitlement. But *amar bi-mezonot* — if he says *bi-mezonot* (this field **is** her *mezonot*, i.e., in place of it), then *katzatz la-mezonot* — he has actually capped and shortened her *mezonot*, limiting her to the yield of that field. What Rabbi Yochanan advised the sons to say was *bi-mezonot*, thereby limiting the stepmother's claim to that field alone. Through a miscommunication, the father said *li-mezonot* — and Resh Lakish correctly ruled that this formulation expanded, rather than curtailed, her rights. The difference between a *lamed* and a *beit* — between two prepositions — carries that much legal weight.

## Ketubah Standards

### Mishnah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ בְּתוּלָה גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם וְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה, אִם רָצָה לְהוֹסִיף אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה מָנֶה — יוֹסִיף. נִתְאַרְמְלָה אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשָׁה, בֵּין מִן הָאֵרוּסִין בֵּין מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין — גּוֹבָה אֶת הַכֹּל. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה אוֹמֵר: מִן הַנִּשּׂוּאִין — גּוֹבָה אֶת הַכֹּל, מִן הָאֵירוּסִין — בְּתוּלָה גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם וְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה, שֶׁלֹּא כָּתַב לָהּ אֶלָּא עַל מְנָת לְכוֹנְסָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Although they said** as a principle that **a virgin collects two hundred** dinars as payment for her marriage contract **and** that **a widow** collects **one hundred dinars, if** the husband **wishes to add even** an additional **ten thousand** dinars, **he may add** it. If **she is** then **widowed or divorced, whether from betrothal** or **whether from marriage, she collects the entire** amount, including the additional sum. **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya says:** If she is widowed or divorced **from marriage, she collects the total** amount, but if she is widowed or divorced **from betrothal, a virgin collects two hundred** dinars **and a widow one hundred** dinars. This is **because he wrote** the additional amount **for her** in the marriage contract **only in order to marry her.**

The tractate's next chapter opens with a *Mishnah* that synthesizes several themes encountered throughout the preceding discussion: *af al pi she-amru betulah gova matayim ve-almanah maneh, im ratzah le-hosif afilu me'ah maneh yosif* — even though the fixed amounts are two hundred *zuz* for a virgin and one hundred for a widow, a husband may add as much as he wishes, even another hundred *maneh*. Furthermore: *nit'almanah oh nitgarshah, bein min ha-eirusin bein min ha-nisu'in, gova et ha-kol* — whether widowed or divorced, whether the dissolution comes from *eirusin* or from *nisu'in*, she collects the full amount, including the *tosefet*.

Rabbi Elazar ben Azariah qualifies this: *min ha-nisu'in gova et ha-kol, min ha-eirusin betulah gova matayim ve-almanah maneh* — the *tosefet* is only collectible if the marriage proceeded to *nisu'in*; if it ended at the stage of *eirusin*, she receives only the statutory amount. His reasoning: *she-lo katav lah ela al menat le-kochzah* — the husband's additions were given on condition that he would actually marry her; if *nisu'in* never occurred, the condition was never fulfilled.

### Yehuda's Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם רָצָה כּוֹתֵב לִבְתוּלָה שְׁטָר שֶׁל מָאתַיִם, וְהִיא כּוֹתֶבֶת ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ מָנֶה״, וּלְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה וְהִיא כּוֹתֶבֶת ״הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי מִמְּךָ חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז״. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַפּוֹחֵת לִבְתוּלָה מִמָּאתַיִם וּלְאַלְמָנָה מִמָּנֶה — הֲרֵי זוֹ בְּעִילַת זְנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says** a related *halakha* with regard to the marriage contract: **If he wishes, he may write for a virgin a document for two hundred** dinars as is fitting for her, **and she may** then **write** a receipt stating: **I received one hundred dinars from you.** Even though she has not actually received the money, the receipt serves as a means for her to waive half of the amount due to her for her marriage contract. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the financial commitment in the marriage contract is a right due to the wife, which she may waive if she chooses to do so. **And** similarly, **for a widow** he may write **one hundred dinars** in the contract **and she may write** a receipt stating: **I received from you fifty dinars.** However, **Rabbi Meir says:** It is prohibited to do this, as **anyone who reduces** the amount of the marriage contract to less than **than two hundred** dinars **for a virgin or one hundred dinars for a widow, this** marital relationship amounts to **licentious sexual relations** because it is as if he did not write any marriage contract at all.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a practical mechanism for reducing the *ketubah* obligation by mutual consent: a husband may write a *ketubah* of two hundred *zuz* for a virgin, and she may simultaneously write him a receipt stating she has already received one hundred — even if she has not actually received it. Similarly for an *almanah*: he writes one hundred, she writes a receipt for fifty. This functions as a kind of *mechilah* — a formal waiver — on her part, enabling a couple to proceed with marriage even when the husband cannot realistically commit to the full statutory amount.

This appears to place Rabbi Yehuda in *machloket* with Rabbi Meir, who states that anyone who gives less than the full two hundred to a *betulah* or less than one hundred to an *almanah* renders the relationship *be'ilat zenut*. One might attempt to resolve the contradiction by noting that Rabbi Yehuda does technically write the full amount — it is her written receipt that reduces the effective obligation — but the Gemara reads them as genuinely disputing positions.

### Tosefet Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: קִיצּוּתָא עֲבַדוּ רַבָּנַן שֶׁלֹּא לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. אִם רָצָה לְהוֹסִיף כּוּ׳. ״רָצָה לִכְתּוֹב לָהּ״ לָא קָתָנֵי, אֶלָּא ״רָצָה לְהוֹסִיף״. מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי אַיְבוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַיְבוּ אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: תְּנַאי כְּתוּבָּה כִּכְתוּבָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna states that if he wishes to add to the obligation in the marriage contract, he may do so. The Gemara asks: **Isn't** it **obvious** that if he wishes to add he may do so? The Gemara explains: **Lest you say** that **the Sages instituted a fixed** ceiling on the amount one may designate in the marriage contract, in order **not to embarrass one who does not have** sufficient funds, the mishna therefore **teaches us** that the Sages were not specific about this. If he wishes to add to the amount, he may do so. § The mishna states that **if he wishes to add** even ten thousand dinars to the marriage contract, he may do so. The Gemara comments: **It does not teach: He wishes to write for her, rather: He wishes to add.** This language indicates that the additional sum he wrote is added to the marriage contract itself, as opposed to being an independent obligation. **This supports** the opinion of **Rabbi Aivu,** who said what **Rabbi Yannai said, as Rabbi Aivu said** that **Rabbi Yannai said: The stipulation in the marriage contract** as well as additional amounts he chooses to add to the contract **are comparable to the marriage contract** itself.

The Gemara asks: why does the *Mishnah* need to tell us that a husband may add to the *ketubah*? Surely anyone may give money to whomever they wish. The answer: one might have thought the Rabbis fixed a maximum, not just a minimum, in order not to embarrass those who cannot afford to give more. Hence, *kamashma lan* — the *Mishnah* teaches that additions are permitted without limit.

Furthermore, the *Mishnah*'s choice of language is telling: it says *ratza le-hosif* — he wishes to **add** — rather than *ratza lichtov* — he wishes to **write**. This linguistic precision supports the principle articulated by Rabbi Ibo in the name of Rabbi Yannai: *tnai ketubah ke-ketubah damei* — the additions to a *ketubah* have the full legal status of the *ketubah* itself. The word *le-hosif* signals that the addition is being appended to the *ketubah*, not recorded as a separate gift. As Rashi explains: had the text said *ratza lichtov*, one might have understood the *tosefet* as a voluntary gift with its own independent legal character, carrying no *shem ketubah*; the formulation *lehosif al ha-ketubah* makes clear that the addition takes on the full identity of the *ketubah*.

### Nine Applications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ: לְמוֹכֶרֶת; וּלְמוֹחֶלֶת; לְמוֹרֶדֶת; וּלְפוֹגֶמֶת; לְתוֹבַעַת; וּלְעוֹבֶרֶת עַל דָּת; לְשֶׁבַח; לִשְׁבוּעָה; וְלִשְׁבִיעִית; וּלְכוֹתֵב כׇּל נְכָסָיו לְבָנָיו; לִגְבּוֹת מִן הַקַּרְקַע וּמִן הַזִּיבּוּרִית; וְכׇל זְמַן שֶׁהִיא בְּבֵית אָבִיהָ; וְלִכְתוּבַּת בְּנִין דִּכְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** This principle produces **a practical difference** with regard to many issues. It is relevant **to one who sells** her marriage contract, indicating that such a sale includes the additional sum of the marriage contract; **and to one who waives** her marriage contract to her husband or his heirs, teaching that the additional sum is included in this relinquishing of rights to payment of the contract; and **to a rebellious** woman, from whom the court deducts a specific amount from her marriage contract each week until there is nothing left. These deductions come from the additional sum as well. **And** this principle also results in a practical difference **to one who vitiates** her marriage contract, as one who states that she received part of the payment for her marriage contract does not receive the remainder without taking an oath, and the additional sum is included in this *halakha*; **to one who demands** payment for her marriage contract, as the Sages ruled that from the time she begins to demand the payment she waives her right to further sustenance, and this applies with regard to one who demands the additional sum as well; **and to one who violates the precepts** of *halakha* or of Jewish custom, who may be divorced without receiving payment for her marriage contract, including the additional sum. It is also relevant **to an increase in value,** as she does not collect the main or additional sums from the increased value of the property in a case where the husband's estate was not sufficient at the time of his death to pay the entire cost of her marriage contract but the heirs later increased the value of the property; **to an oath,** because if the wife is required to take an oath in order to receive her marriage contract, the additional sum is also included in that oath; **and to the Sabbatical Year,** as the marriage contract is not annulled with other debts in the Sabbatical Year, and this includes the additional sum as well. **And** with regard **to one who writes** a document transferring **all of his property to his sons** and leaves his wife a specific plot of land for her marriage contract, this teaches that she receives both the main and the additional sums of her contract only from that land. The aforementioned *halakha* also teaches that she **collects** the payment only **from land, and** specifically **from land of inferior quality; and** that a widow loses her ability to collect the main and additional sums **as long as she is in her father's home** for more than twenty-five years after her husband's death; **and** the principle also applies **to** the stipulation in the **marriage contract** that the **male offspring** inherit their mother's dowry when her husband passes away in addition to the inheritance they receive together with their other brothers. These *halakhot* apply equally to the additional sum of the marriage contract.

The Gemara enumerates the practical consequences of the rule that *tosefet* carries the legal status of the *ketubah* proper. If she sells her *ketubah* — the *tosefet* is sold with it. If she is a *moredet* — a wife who rebels — the reductions apply beginning from the *tosefet* and work downward. If she is *pogemet* — she has collected part and written a receipt — the *tosefet* is subject to the same *shevuah* requirement as the principal *ketubah*. If she demands her *ketubah* in *Beit Din*, this includes the *tosefet*, and the *mezonot* entitlement ends. If she violates religious norms and thereby forfeits her *ketubah*, she forfeits the *tosefet* as well. If the property appreciated in value after the husband's death, she collects the *tosefet* at the value fixed at the time of death, just as with the principal *ketubah*; the first-born sons similarly do not receive a double share of post-mortem appreciation, whether of the *ketubah* or the *tosefet*. The *tosefet* is likewise not annulled in *shemitah*, as the *ketubah* itself is not subject to annulment during the seventh year. If the husband wrote all his property to his sons, the *ketubah* — together with the *tosefet* — is affected; Rashi notes that if he additionally designated even a minimal piece of land for his wife, she forfeits her *ketubah*, a *sugya* developed in Bava Batra 132. Collection of *tosefet* follows the same land-quality rules as the *ketubah* — from *karka* and from *ziborit*. And for purposes of *ketubat benin dichrin* — the provision by which sons of one wife inherit her *ketubah* before division of the estate — the *tosefet* is included. Finally, the time limit on collection when the widow is in *beit aviah* — her father's house — applies equally to the *tosefet*: after twenty-five years, neither the *ketubah* nor the *tosefet* may be collected.
