# Hullin 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Oser Davar She'eino Shelo

### Ulla & R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל זֶבַח לָא. וְאִי אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ, מַאי אִרְיָא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף? אֲפִילּוּ חַטַּאת בְּהֵמָה נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּקַנְיָא לֵיהּ לְכַפָּרָה כְּדִידֵיהּ דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in the case of an animal **offering** there is **no** way in which one can violate all three prohibitions simultaneously. **But if a person does not render forbidden an item that is not his, why** must the *tanna* teach the *halakha* **specifically** with regard to **a bird sin offering?** The same *halakha* would apply **even** in the case of **an animal sin offering.** This is because cutting one *siman* for idolatry does not render the animal forbidden, as the priest has the exclusive right to derive benefit from it, so it does not belong to the owner anymore. Therefore, one would violate the three prohibitions simultaneously. The Gemara answers: **Since** one who brings a sin offering **acquires** the animal **for his atonement,** its status is **like** that of an animal that **is his,** and he renders it forbidden with the first cut at the beginning of the slaughter.

The sugya opens four lines from the top of 41a with the words *ta shema*, and it presupposes the debate established in the preceding discussion. *ulla* in the name of *rabbi yochanan* had articulated the following principle: one cannot render forbidden something that does not belong to him — but only where the *halachic* status in question is not grounded in a straightforward physical reality. Where a physical reality is created, there is no issue: if one cooks another's meat together with milk, the mixture is genuinely transformed, and the *issur* follows as a matter of physical fact. The question arises only where the *issur* depends on a designation or classification rather than a material change. Even so, *ulla* and *rabbi yochanan* held that if one performs an *action* — an act of physical control over the object — one can render it forbidden even if it does not belong to him. The paradigm case is *shechita* performed with intent for *avodah zarah*: the act of slaughter is sufficient to impose a forbidden status.

Arrayed against this position are *rav nachman*, *rav amram*, and *rav yitzchak*, who maintained that even a physical action cannot render another's property forbidden. Within that position there had been a subsidiary discussion about the threshold of *shechita* — whether even the beginning of the act suffices — but the present section focuses on the fundamental dispute: can an act performed upon another's property alter its *halachic* status?

### Shnayim Ochzin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּסַכִּין וְשׁוֹחֲטִין, אֶחָד לְשׁוּם אֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ, וְאֶחָד לְשׁוּם דָּבָר כָּשֵׁר – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּאִית לֵיהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת בְּגַוַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another objection from the mishna: If there were **two** people **grasping a knife** together **and slaughtering** an animal, **one** slaughtering **for the sake of one of all those** enumerated in the first clause of the mishna **and one** slaughtering **for the sake of a legitimate matter,** their **slaughter is not valid.** Based on the formulation of the mishna, the one slaughtering with improper intent is not necessarily the owner of the animal. How, then, can he render the animal forbidden? Apparently, one can render forbidden an item that is not his. The Gemara answers: **What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where** the one with improper intent **has a partnership** share **in** the animal, so he is rendering his own animal forbidden.

The *gemara* now brings its first *ta shema* from the *mishna* of *shnayim ochazin basakkin* — two people holding a knife together and performing *shechita*, one with intent for one of the listed problematic designations (*avodah zarah* or the like), and one with intent for a legitimate purpose. The *mishna* rules *shechitato pesulah* — the *shechita* is invalid.

The implied question is immediate: if one cannot render forbidden that which is not his, why does the intent of one party invalidate the *shechita*? After all, since two people are performing it, we cannot simply assume the animal belongs to either one of them — or at least to the one with the problematic intent.

### Shutafut Basho


The *gemara* resolves this with the principle of *shutafut*: *hacha b'mai askinan de-ika shutafut begavah* — we are dealing with a case in which there is a partnership in the animal. Just as when a single individual performs *shechita* we assume he is the owner, when two people perform *shechita* together we assume they are joint owners. Since the problematic party holds at least a partial share in the animal, he has sufficient standing to affect its status. The *chiddush* of the *mishna* is therefore twofold: first, that even a partial owner — not the sole owner — can affect the *halachic* status; and second, that even a single intent, among two simultaneous intents, is enough to disqualify.

Crucially, however, this *mishna* does not serve as proof that one can render forbidden something that is genuinely not his own. The *gemara* limits the case to one of partial ownership, and the group of *amoraim* who deny any such power even through an action can therefore deflect the challenge.

### Hezek She'eino Nikar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמְטַמֵּא, וְהַמְדַמֵּעַ, וְהַמְנַסֵּךְ – בְּשׁוֹגֵג פָּטוּר, בְּמֵזִיד חַיָּיב. הָכָא נָמֵי דְּאִית לֵיהּ שׁוּתָּפוּת בְּגַוַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another objection from a mishna (*Gittin* 52b): With regard to **one who renders** another's food **impure, and one who mixes** *teruma* with another's non-sacred produce, **and one who pours** another's wine as a libation before an idol, if he did so **unwittingly,** he is **exempt** from payment of damages, even though he caused the other monetary loss. If he did so **intentionally,** he is **liable** to pay damages. Apparently, one can render forbidden an item that is not his. The Gemara answers: **Here too** it is a case **where** the one who caused the damage **has a partnership** share **in** the produce.

A second *ta shema* is brought from the famous *mishna* of *hametamei, hamidamea, vehamenasech* — one who renders another's *teruma* ritually impure (*tamei*), one who mixes *teruma* into *chullin*, and one who pours another's wine as a libation to *avodah zarah*. These cases invoke the category of *hezek she'eino nikar* — damage that leaves no visible physical trace — which raises its own set of analytical complexities.

*Halacha* in *bava kamma* imposes strict requirements on what constitutes actionable damage. Two conditions are particularly constraining. First, the damage must be direct — *grama*, indirect causation, generally exempts. Second, the change effected must be physically discernible; damage that is invisible does not qualify as *hezek* at the biblical level. This creates apparent gaps. If one renders an animal psychologically incapable of functioning — through fright or trauma — without any visible physical injury, that too would constitute *hezek she'eino nikar*, and technically no compensation would be owed. The Rabbis addressed these gaps through two mechanisms: the category of *garmi* — a form of quasi-direct causation for which one is nonetheless liable — and a special rule that *hezek she'eino nikar*, while not biblically actionable, triggers liability as a *knas* when done deliberately. If the damage was accidental, the victim goes uncompensated by virtue of the legal exemption; but if the perpetrator acted intentionally, the Rabbis refused to allow him to escape liability through a technicality.

This is precisely what the *mishna* encodes. Making someone's *teruma tamei* — a purely metaphysical transformation, invisible to the eye — carries no biblical liability. Similarly, mixing *teruma* and *chullin* creates no physical alteration, only a halakhic one: the combined grain must now be treated as *teruma* and sells for less. And pouring another's wine as *nisuch* to *avodah zarah* renders it entirely forbidden — again, invisibly. In all three cases: *beshogeig patur* — if unintentional, one is exempt; *bemezid chayav* — if willful, one is liable.

Now, a distinction must be drawn. In the cases of *tum'ah* and *midameia*, the objection of *oser davar she'eino shelo* does not apply in its sharpest form. Rendering something *tamei* is simply a metaphysical reality that occurs when a *tamei* person contacts *teruma* — there is no act of classification or designation at work. Similarly, mixing *teruma* and *chullin* creates a physical reality of commingling. Neither case depends on the perpetrator *defining* the object's status; the status follows automatically from what has occurred. The case of *menasech* is different. Pouring wine as a libation does not alter the wine physically; the wine becomes forbidden because it is *defined* as an offering to *avodah zarah*. And here the question of ownership is acutely relevant: what right does the perpetrator have to define someone else's wine as a sacrificial offering to his god? This is precisely where *oser davar she'eino shelo* bites.

### Nokhri Menasech Yayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: גּוֹי שֶׁנִּיסֵּךְ יֵינוֹ שֶׁל יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – אֲסָרוֹ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא מַתִּירִין אוֹתוֹ מִפְּנֵי שְׁנֵי דְבָרִים: אֶחָד – שֶׁאֵין מְנַסְּכִין יַיִן אֶלָּא בִּפְנֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְאֶחָד – שֶׁיָּכוֹל לוֹמַר לוֹ ״לֹא כׇּל הֵימֶנָּךְ שֶׁתֶּאֱסֹר יֵינִי לְאוֹנְסִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the dispute whether one who slaughters another's animal for idol worship renders the animal forbidden, in accordance with Rav Huna, or does not render it forbidden, in accordance with Rav Naḥman, Rav Amram, and Rav Yitzḥak, is **parallel to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a gentile who poured a Jew's wine** as an idolatrous **libation,** but **not in the presence of** an object of **idol worship, he has rendered** the wine **forbidden. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba permit** drinking the wine **due to two factors: One** is **that** the presumption is that idol worshippers **pour wine as** an idolatrous **libation only in the presence of** an object of **idol worship. And** the other **one** is **that** even if the gentile poured the wine as an idolatrous libation, the Jew **can say to** the gentile: It is **not within your** power **to render my wine forbidden against my will.**

The *gemara* now cites what appears to be a *mishna* or *braita*: a case in which a non-Jew pours Jewish wine as a libation to his god — but *shelo bifnei avodat kochavim*, not in the presence of the idol. The question is whether this renders the wine forbidden.

The anonymous first opinion holds that it does: *asaro* — he renders it forbidden.

### Ein Adam Oser Davar


*Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* and *rabbi yehuda ben baba* disagree, ruling that the wine remains permitted — *matirin* — for two independent reasons.

First: *she'ein menaschin yayin ela bifnei avodat kochavim* — libations are not poured to idols except in the presence of the idol. Since the idol was not present, we do not believe the non-Jew was genuinely making an offering. Whatever he said, he was simply lying — attempting to harm the Jew by claiming to consecrate his wine.

Second, and more fundamental: *lav kol heimenecha she-te'aser yeini l'onsi* — you have no standing to render my wine forbidden against my will. Even granting that his intent was genuine, the wine belongs to the Jew, and the non-Jew has no authority to define its status. *ein adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot render forbidden that which is not his.

This of course raises a significant question: according to *rabbi yehuda ben beteira* and *rabbi yehuda ben baba*, how is it that a non-Jew's contact ever renders a Jew's wine forbidden? One might speculate that the rabbinic extension of *yayin nesech* addresses this, or that even these authorities would acknowledge liability when there is reason to believe the idol was present — perhaps concealed on the person — but that question properly belongs to *masechet avodah zarah* and is left aside here.

What matters for our purposes is that the *tannaitic* dispute maps precisely onto the *amoraic* debate. One *tannatic* position holds that a non-Jew can render a Jew's wine forbidden through the act of *nisuch*; the other holds he cannot, because one cannot render forbidden that which is not one's own. The fundamental question of *oser davar she'eino shelo* is thus a genuine *tannaitic* controversy.

A further question arises from the discussion: does *nisuch* require a physical act? The answer is yes — *tikkrovet* to *avodah zarah*, dedication as a sacrificial gift, requires an actual act of worship or offering. A mere declaration does not suffice for this purpose, just as *hekdesh* — sanctification — does not apply to *avodah zarah* by declaration alone.

### Afilu Man De'amar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב נַחְמָן וְרַב עַמְרָם וְרַב יִצְחָק אָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי גּוֹי, אֲבָל יִשְׂרָאֵל לְצַעוֹרֵיהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Naḥman, and Rav Amram, and Rav Yitzḥak say:** Although Rav Huna's opinion is compatible only with the opinion of the first *tanna* in the *baraita* and not with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira and Rabbi Yehuda ben Baba, we can state our opinion **even according to the one who says** that **a person renders forbidden an item that is not his,** e.g., by pouring his wine as a libation or slaughtering his animal for idol worship. **This statement** applies only in a case where **a gentile** pours the libation or slaughters the animal. **But** if **a Jew** pours the wine or slaughters the animal, presumably **he intends to torment** that other person, and not to engage in idol worship. Therefore, a Jew does not render the animal forbidden.

*Rav Nachman*, *rav amram*, and *rav yitzchak* — the three *amoraim* who deny that any action can render another's property forbidden — now refine their position in a striking way. Rather than simply asserting their position in the abstract, they concede ground while preserving their practical conclusion. They say: *afilu man de'amar adam oser davar she'eino shelo* — even if we accept the view that a person can render forbidden something that is not his own through an action, that principle applies in the case of a non-Jew acting upon a Jew's wine. But when it comes to a Jew acting upon another Jew's property — slaughtering another Jew's cow with intent for *avodah zarah*, or pouring another Jew's wine as a libation — *urei k'mahavsei*: he is simply trying to give his fellow a hard time, to spite him, to cause him harm. We do not assume that a Jew is sincerely devoted to *avodah zarah*. And since sincerity is absent, even if one accepts that a genuine act of *nisuch* can render wine forbidden, this particular act — performed by a Jew against another Jew — would not do so, because we are unwilling to credit the requisite intent.

This is a significant conceptual shift. These *amoraim* are no longer claiming the broad principle that actions can never impose forbidden status on another's property. They are claiming something narrower and in some ways more radical: that even when the principle might otherwise apply, the specific case of a Jew claiming to worship *avodah zarah* is presumptively incredible, and therefore cannot carry the legal weight needed to render the object forbidden.

### Mumar Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּסַכִּין וְשׁוֹחֲטִין, אֶחָד לְשׁוּם אֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ וְאֶחָד לְשׁוּם דָּבָר כָּשֵׁר – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a contradiction to that distinction from the mishna: If there were **two** people **grasping a knife** together **and slaughtering** an animal, **one** slaughtering **for the sake of one of all those** enumerated in the first clause of the mishna **and one** slaughtering **for the sake of a legitimate matter,** their **slaughter is not valid.** As the mishna is discussing a case involving Jews, this indicates that even a Jew who slaughters an animal for idol worship renders it forbidden. The Gemara answers: **What are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with the case **of a Jewish transgressor** whose intent when declaring that his slaughter is for the sake of mountains, hills, or other natural entities is for idol worship.

The *gemara* now revisits the *mishna* of *shnayim ochazin basakkin* in light of this argument. If we are supposed to presume that a Jew's claimed devotion to *avodah zarah* is insincere — mere spite rather than genuine worship — then why does the *mishna* rule that when one of two joint owners slaughters with intent for *avodah zarah* the *shechita* is disqualified? One might respond that since both parties are joint owners, the one with the problematic intent would harm himself as much as his partner, and therefore spite is less plausible. But the *gemara* does not rely on that reasoning, for it is easy to construct cases where causing harm to oneself is worthwhile if the harm to the other is greater.

The *gemara* answers instead: *b'Yisrael mumar* — the *mishna* is speaking of a Jew who is a known apostate, one who has actually converted to *avodah zarah*. In such a case there is no presumption of insincerity; the individual's devotion to *avodah zarah* is established. Accordingly, we do credit his intent, and the *shechita* is disqualified.

The same answer is given when the same question is posed with respect to the *mishna* of *hametamei, hamidamea, vehamenasech*: *hacha nami b'Yisrael mumar* — there too, we are dealing with a known apostate, and sincerity is not in doubt.

### Hitru Bo V'kibel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הִתְרוּ בּוֹ וְקִבֵּל עָלָיו הַתְרָאָה, מַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הִתִּיר עַצְמוֹ לְמִיתָה קָאָמְרַתְּ? אֵין לָךְ מְשׁוּמָּד גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Rav Ashi:** With regard to a Jew who is not a transgressor but declared that he is slaughtering another's animal for idolatry, if those who heard his declaration **forewarned him** that doing so is prohibited by Torah law and is punishable by death, **and he acknowledged the forewarning** and said: It is in full knowledge of the prohibition and the punishment that I do so, **what** is the *halakha*? Does he render the animal forbidden in that case? Rav Ashi **said to him: Are you saying** a case where **he subjected himself to death** by acknowledging the forewarning? **You have no transgressor greater than that,** and he certainly renders the animal forbidden.

*Rav Acha the son of Rav* then poses a sharper test case to *rav ashi*. Suppose the individual in question has no prior history of *avodah zarah* — he is not a known apostate — but at this very moment he is performing the slaughter, and witnesses warned him beforehand: if you slaughter this animal as an offering to *avodah zarah*, you will be liable to the death penalty. And he responded: I know, and I intend to do it anyway — *hitir atzmo le'misah*, he has effectively accepted upon himself that death. What is the ruling in this case?

*Rav Ashi* replies: *ein lecha mumar gadol mizeh* — you have no greater example of a genuine apostate. One who accepts the death penalty upon himself and proceeds is unambiguously sincere. He cannot be dismissed as acting out of spite. In this case, he would render another's property forbidden through his act.

The *gemara*'s use of the phrase *hitir atzmo le'oso misah* — he has given himself over to that death — reflects a broader conception in which *hatra'ah* (the formal warning required before capital punishment) functions not merely as a procedural requirement but as the individual's own acceptance of the penalty. This explains how the court can proceed: in a sense, he himself has authorized the execution. There is also an observation worth noting: the threshold of accepting *hatra'ah* is so demanding that in practice it may have been nearly impossible to meet, which is itself one of the mechanisms by which the Rabbis effectively curtailed application of the death penalty.

The conclusion of this section: as a matter of practical *halacha*, the ruling follows the position that through an action one *can* render another's property forbidden — *adam oser davar she'eino shelo* through an act is operative. The qualification introduced by *rav nachman* and his colleagues survives only in the specific case of a Jew acting upon another Jew's property, where sincerity of *avodah zarah* intent must be affirmatively established — either by the individual being a known *mumar* or by his having accepted *hatra'ah*.

## Ein Shochtin Lo L'toch

### Yamim Neharot Kelim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לֹא לְתוֹךְ יַמִּים, וְלֹא לְתוֹךְ נְהָרוֹת, וְלֹא לְתוֹךְ כֵּלִים, אֲבָל שׁוֹחֵט הוּא לְתוֹךְ עוּגָה שֶׁל מַיִם. וּבִסְפִינָה, עַל גַּבֵּי כֵלִים. אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לַגּוּמָּא כׇּל עִיקָּר, אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה גּוּמָּא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס הַדָּם לְתוֹכָהּ. וּבַשּׁוּק לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, שֶׁלֹּא
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow, **neither into seas, nor into rivers, nor into vessels,** as in all those cases it appears that he is slaughtering the animal in the manner of idolaters. **But one may slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a round excavation** containing **water. And on a ship,** one may slaughter an animal **onto vessels** as it is clear that his objective is to avoid sullying the ship. **One may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground **at all, but one may fashion a small hole inside his house so that the blood will enter into it. And in the marketplace one may not do so,** so **that** he will **not**

Having completed the discussion of genuine intent for *avodah zarah*, the *mishna* now turns to a related category: acts that *look* like *avodah zarah* but may not be accompanied by any such intent. The *mishna* states: *ein shochtin lo letoch yamim velo letoch neharot velo letoch kelim* — one may not slaughter into oceans, rivers, or vessels.

The reasoning for each prohibition follows from its association with idolatrous practice. Slaughtering into an ocean or river appears to be an offering to the spirit of those waters — a genuinely attested form of ancient worship. Slaughtering into a vessel appears to be a collection of blood for use in a sacrificial rite, such as sprinkling — a practice alluded to in the *torah*'s prohibition of *lo tochlu al hadam*, which many commentators understand as referring to pagan blood-worship rituals.

### Ugah Shel Mayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל שׁוֹחֵט הוּא לְתוֹךְ עוּגָה שֶׁל מַיִם
>
> **Translation:** **But one may slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a round excavation** containing **water.**

The *mishna* immediately qualifies the prohibition: *aval shachat hu letoch ugah shel mayim* — but one may slaughter into a small puddle or depression filled with water. Since the blood immediately mixes with water and is diluted, no one would realistically consider using such a mixture in a ritual context. There is, moreover, no spirit associated with puddles in the way there is with oceans and rivers. The concern therefore does not arise.

### Bespina Al Gabei Kelim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִסְפִינָה, עַל גַּבֵּי כֵלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And on a ship,** one may slaughter an animal **onto vessels** as it is clear that his objective is to avoid sullying the ship.

If one is traveling by boat and wishes to avoid bloodying the deck, the *mishna* permits slaughtering *al gabei kelim* — over the back of vessels rather than into them. As *rashi* explains, the vessels are positioned and angled so that the blood runs off them and flows into the ocean rather than being collected inside. Since the blood is not being gathered into the vessel — it merely passes over the exterior surface — the appearance of ritual blood collection is avoided, even though the blood ultimately drains into the sea.

### L'gumah Kol Ikkar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לַגּוּמָּא כׇּל עִיקָּר
>
> **Translation:** **One** may **not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground **at all.**

The *mishna* rules: *ein shochtin legumah kol ikkar* — one may never slaughter directly into a hole, under any circumstances. The concern is the same as with vessels: a hole in the ground can function as a collection basin for blood, and slaughtering directly into it evokes the imagery of gathering blood for ritual use.

### Guma Betoch Beito

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל עוֹשֶׂה גּוּמָּא בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁיִּכָּנֵס הַדָּם לְתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But one may fashion a small hole inside his house so that the blood will enter into it.**

The *mishna* does, however, permit a related arrangement: *aval oseh gumah betoch beito bishvil she-yikanes hadam letocha* — one may make a hole in one's house or yard for the purpose of channeling the blood. The key distinction is that one does not slaughter *into* the hole directly. Rather, one slaughters on level or slightly angled ground nearby, and the blood flows on its own into the hole. The purpose is hygienic — keeping the floor or yard clean — and this purpose is evident from the domestic context.

### Bashuk Lo Ya'aseh Ken

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבַשּׁוּק לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, שֶׁלֹּא יְחַקֶּה אֶת הַמִּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And in the marketplace one may not do so,** so **that** he will **not** appear to **emulate [*yeḥakkeh*] the heretics.**

This dispensation does not extend to the public marketplace: *u'vashuk lo ya'aseh kein* — in the marketplace one may not create even an offset hole into which the blood runs. The reasoning is that in the marketplace one has no obvious personal interest in maintaining cleanliness — the street belongs to no one in particular, and civic-mindedness of this sort would not be the natural assumption. If one is nevertheless going to the trouble of creating a blood-drainage arrangement in the marketplace, observers will more readily assume a ritual motivation than a hygienic one. Therefore, in the marketplace, one must slaughter on flat, open ground where the blood simply spreads — with no arrangement that could suggest collection.

### Shelo Yechakeh Haminim


The concluding line of the *mishna* provides the unifying rationale for all of these restrictions: *shelo yechakeh et ha-minim* — so as not to imitate the *minim*. The printed text of many editions reads *tzedukim* — Sadducees — but this is a product of medieval censorship. The original reading was *minim*, a term that here refers not to sectarians in general but specifically to Jews who had abandoned Judaism for idolatrous practice, the Jewish apostates who were suffused in and identified with the surrounding pagan world.

*Rashi* notes that the verb in the *mishna* derives from *chok* — a ritual statute — rather than from *chikui* in the sense of mere imitation, though in post-Mishnaic Hebrew *chikui* came to mean mimicry. The prohibition is thus not simply about how one appears to others, but about performing acts that are structurally the acts of *minim* — rituals, as it were, of the idolatrous world.

### Chikui HaMinim Today


This distinction between *maris ayin* and *shelo yechakeh et ha-minim* is worth pausing over. *maris ayin* is fundamentally a concern about external perception — what other people will think when they see me doing this. *shelo yechakeh et ha-minim* is a different register: it speaks to one's own sense of religious identity and propriety, the feeling that certain actions are simply not acts a committed Jew performs, regardless of whether anyone is watching. This is reminiscent of the principle of *harchik min ha-ki'ur umin ha-domeh lo* — keep distant from what is repugnant, and even from that which merely resembles it. The concern here is not primarily reputational but existential: some acts should not be done because of what they are, not merely because of how they look.

The fact that the *mishna* draws a distinction between performing the offset-hole arrangement at home (permitted) versus in the marketplace (forbidden) might suggest that perception is indeed relevant — the same act reads differently in different contexts. But this can also be understood as a recognition that *my own sense* of what is appropriate shifts with context, not only what others think of me.

This prohibition carries contemporary resonance. In recent decades — and with increasing frequency in recent years — the phrase *chikui ha-minim* has been deployed in *halachic* responsa to resist practices that are technically permitted but that have been adopted by non-Orthodox Jewish denominations. The argument, stated explicitly in some of these *teshuvot*, is that even if a given practice is *halachically* unobjectionable, it is forbidden because it constitutes *chikui ha-minim* — mimicry of those who have departed from Orthodox Judaism, identified by the authors with the *minim* of the *mishna*. One concrete example is the inclusion of the mother's name alongside the father's in a *ketubah*. Those who oppose the practice do not always ground their opposition in a *halachic* deficiency; they argue instead that it is a marker of non-Orthodox Judaism, and that adopting it therefore constitutes forbidden imitation of the movements they regard as having broken with tradition.

This application of the concept is, to put it plainly, deeply troubling. The *mishna*'s prohibition is directed at practices whose origins lie in *avodah zarah* — in a world saturated with active paganism. To abstract the principle and apply it to *halachically* permissible practices simply because those practices have been adopted by other denominations is to prohibit that which is permitted. Moreover, the analogy is strained: there is a categorical difference between imitating rituals rooted in idol worship and following practices embraced by Jews who are themselves committed to *halacha* as they understand it. That said, the phenomenon is real — this language does appear in contemporary responsa, and ignoring it would leave a significant dimension of the *mishna*'s ongoing legal life unaddressed.

### Mai Shna L'toch Yamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לֹא לְתוֹךְ וְכוּ׳. מַאי שְׁנָא לְתוֹךְ יַמִּים דְּלָא? דְּאָמְרִי: לְשָׂרָא דְּיַמָּא קָא שָׁחֵיט. לְתוֹךְ עוּגָה שֶׁל מַיִם נָמֵי אָמְרִי: לְבָבוּאָה קָא שָׁחֵיט. אָמַר רָבָא: בַּעֲכוּרִים שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: **One may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow, **neither into** seas, nor into rivers, nor into vessels, but one may slaughter an animal and have its blood flow into a round excavation containing water. The Gemara asks: **What is different** about slaughter **into seas?** Is it **that** one may **not** perform it, **as** onlookers will **say: He is slaughtering to the angel of the sea?** If so, slaughter **into a round excavation** containing **water** should **also** be prohibited, as onlookers will **say: He is slaughtering to** his **reflection [*bavua*],** which is also similar to idolatry. **Rava said:** The *tanna'im* **taught** that *halakha* **in** the case of **murky** water, in which no reflection can be seen.

The *gemara* now analyzes the *mishna*'s distinctions. It asks: *mai shna letoch yamim d'lo* — why is it specifically forbidden to slaughter into the ocean? The answer is: *d'amri l'sara d'yamma ka shachit* — because observers will say he is slaughtering to the spirit of the sea.

The *gemara* then raises an obvious difficulty: if the puddle case is permitted, why? After all, *l'toch ugah shel mayim nami amri l'babu'ah ka shachit* — even in a puddle of water one might say he is slaughtering to the image reflected in the water, to a spirit manifested in the reflection.

*Rava* resolves this: *b'achurim shanu* — the *mishna* is speaking of murky, cloudy water. There is no clear reflection in murky water, and therefore no one would associate the act with any water-spirit or reflection-spirit. The puddle is permitted because the water is opaque.

### Rbutz D'Rava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לַגּוּמָּא וְכוּ׳. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ: אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לַגּוּמָּא כְּלָל? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רֵישָׁא בְּגוּמָּא שֶׁבַּשּׁוּק. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא ״וּבַשּׁוּק לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן״, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו בְּשׁוּק עָסְקִינַן!
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **One** may **not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground at all, but one may fashion a small hole inside his house so that the blood will enter into it. The Gemara asks: How is it permitted to slaughter and have the blood flow into a hole inside his house? **But didn't you** initially **say** that **one may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground **at all? Abaye said: The first clause** of the mishna, where there is a blanket prohibition against having the blood flow into a small hole, is referring **to a small hole that is in the marketplace.** **Rava said to him: But** isn't it so that **from** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: And in the marketplace one may not do so, it may be inferred that** in **the first clause we are not dealing with the marketplace?**

The *gemara* then addresses the apparent internal contradiction in the *mishna*: the *mishna* says one should not slaughter *legumah* at all, and yet it also says one may make a hole in one's house. *abaye* initially suggests that the prohibition of the hole applies only in the marketplace, while the home is always permitted. *rava* rejects this reading on the basis of the *mishna*'s structure: since the final clause explicitly prohibits the practice *in the marketplace*, the earlier ruling must have been speaking of a different context entirely — namely, even the home setting.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין לַגּוּמָּא כׇּל עִיקָּר, וְהָרוֹצֶה לְנַקֵּר חֲצֵרוֹ, כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? עוֹשֶׂה מָקוֹם חוּץ לַגּוּמָּא וְשׁוֹחֵט, וְדָם שׁוֹתֵת וְיוֹרֵד לַגּוּמָּא, וּבַשּׁוּק לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, שֶׁלֹּא יְחַקֶּה אֶת הַמִּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that **this** is what the mishna **is saying: One may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground **at all. And one who wishes to clean his courtyard** and ensure that it will not be sullied in blood, **how does he do** so? **He fashions a place** with an incline or a furrow **outside the small hole, and slaughters** the animal there, **and the blood flows and descends into the hole. And in the marketplace one may not do so,** so **that** he will **not** appear to **emulate the heretics.**

*Rava* therefore offers the definitive reading: *ein shochtin legumah kol ikkar* — one never slaughters directly into a hole, even in one's own home. The permitted domestic arrangement is not slaughtering *into* the hole but slaughtering on a smooth or angled surface adjacent to the hole, allowing the blood to trickle down: *oseh makom chutz la-gumah v'shochet, ve-hadam shotet ve-yored la-gumah*. In the marketplace, even this is forbidden — *u'vashuk lo ya'aseh kein* — on account of the prohibition against mimicking the practices of *minim*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָיָה מְהַלֵּךְ בִּסְפִינָה וְאֵין לוֹ מָקוֹם בַּסְּפִינָה לִשְׁחוֹט – מוֹצִיא יָדוֹ חוּץ לַסְּפִינָה וְשׁוֹחֵט, וְדָם שׁוֹתֵת וְיוֹרֵד עַל דּוֹפְנֵי הַסְּפִינָה, וְאֵין שׁוֹחֵט לַגּוּמָּא כׇּל עִיקָּר. וְהָרוֹצֶה לְנַקֵּר חֲצֵרוֹ, כֵּיצַד הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? עוֹשֶׂה מָקוֹם חוּץ לַגּוּמָּא וְשׁוֹחֵט, וְדָם שׁוֹתֵת וְיוֹרֵד לַגּוּמָּא. וּבַשּׁוּק לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה כֵּן, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְחֻקֹּתֵיהֶם לֹא תֵלֵכוּ״, וְאִם עָשָׂה כֵּן – צָרִיךְ בְּדִיקָה אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rava:** If one **was traveling on a ship and he has no place on the ship to slaughter** an animal, **he extends his hand** with a knife, holds the head of the animal **outside** the walls of **the ship, and slaughters** the animal there; **and the blood flows and descends** down **the sides of the ship.** He may not slaughter an animal and have its blood flow directly into the sea. **And one may not slaughter** an animal and have its blood flow **into a small hole** in the ground **at all.** **And one who wishes to clean his courtyard** and ensure that it will not be sullied with blood, **how does he do** so? **He fashions a place** with an incline or a furrow **outside the small hole, and slaughters** the animal there, **and the blood flows and descends into the hole. And in the marketplace, one may not do so, as it is stated: "Neither shall you follow their statutes"** (Leviticus 18:3). **And if he did so, he requires examination after his** actions to ascertain whether he is a heretic.

A *braita* is cited in corroboration of *rava*'s ruling. The *braita* addresses the case of a boat: if one is traveling by sea and has no room on the deck to slaughter properly, one may extend the animal's neck over the side of the boat and slaughter there, so that the blood first strikes the hull of the boat before flowing into the sea — since the blood does not go directly into the ocean, the prohibition is not violated. And by the same principle as *rava*'s ruling: *ein shochtin legumah kol ikkar*, and if one wishes to keep one's yard clean, one creates a smooth channel outside the hole so that the blood runs into it of its own accord. In the marketplace, even this is forbidden — *mishum she-ne'emar u'vchukoteihem lo telechu* — because of the *torah*'s prohibition against following the statutes of the nations.

The invocation of *u'vchukoteihem lo telechu* raises broader questions about the scope of the *chukat ha-goyim* prohibition. The most significant and uncontroversial application is to practices that are sourced in *avodah zarah*. The prohibition is not triggered by the mere fact that non-Jews engage in an activity — otherwise it would be impossible to slaughter animals at all, since pagans also slaughter animals. What makes an act fall under *bechukoteihem* is either that it is specifically a ritual of the idolatrous world, or that by its circumstances it so closely evokes such a ritual that it becomes indistinguishable from one. The *gemara*'s final notation — *v'im asah kein tzarich bedikah acheret* — underscores this: one who is observed slaughtering in a manner that evokes ritual blood-collection thereby raises suspicion about his overall religious status, warranting further investigation.

## Hashochet L'shem Olah

### L'shem Olah L'shem Zvach…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה, לְשֵׁם זְבָחִים, לְשֵׁם אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, לְשֵׁם פֶּסַח, לְשֵׁם תּוֹדָה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal and asserts that he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a burnt offering, for the sake of a peace offering, for the sake of a provisional guilt offering, for the sake of a Paschal offering,** or **for the sake of a thanks offering, his slaughter is not valid,** as it appears that he is consecrating animals and slaughtering sacrificial animals outside the Temple. **And Rabbi Shimon deems** his slaughter **valid.**

The final *mishna* of the *perek* addresses a distinct category of problematic *shechita*: not intent for *avodah zarah*, but intent to designate an ordinary animal as a *korban* — a sacrifice — to be offered outside the *beit haMikdash*. This is the prohibition of *kodshim bachutz*, sacrificing outside the Temple precincts. The *mishna* lists: *hashochet l'shem olah, l'shem zevachim, l'shem asham talui, l'shem pesach, l'shem todah* — one who slaughters designating the animal as a *olah*, a peace-offering (*zevachim* = *shelamim*), a conditional guilt-offering (*asham talui*), a Passover sacrifice, or a thanksgiving offering. In all these cases: *shechitato pesulah* — the *shechita* is invalid.

The reason, though not stated explicitly by the *mishna*, is that these are all offerings that could in principle be brought voluntarily — they are *nidvarim*, free-will offerings — and therefore the slaughter has the appearance of *kodshim bachutz*. Even though the animal has not been formally sanctified as a *korban*, the stated intent creates the appearance of a prohibited sacrificial act performed outside the Temple, and that appearance itself invalidates the *shechita*.

### Rebbi Shimon Machshir


*Rabbi Shimon* rules *machshir* — he validates the *shechita* in all these cases. Two approaches are offered in the *rishonim*. *rashi* explains that *rabbi shimon* is simply not concerned with appearances in this context. *tosafot* offer a more technically grounded explanation: *rabbi shimon* holds that if one explicitly designates an animal as an *olah* to be brought *outside* the *beit haMikdash*, the sanctification is void — *harei zo olah al menat she'avienah chutz la-Beit HaMikdash* has no effect according to *rabbi shimon*. Since any putative sanctification would be nullified, the act does not even superficially resemble *kodshim bachutz*, and there is therefore no basis for invalidation.

### Shnayim Ochzin L'shem Ol…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנַיִם אוֹחֲזִין בְּסַכִּין וְשׁוֹחֲטִין, אֶחָד לְשׁוּם אֶחָד מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ, וְאֶחָד לְשׁוּם דָּבָר כָּשֵׁר – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** If there were **two** people **grasping a knife** together **and slaughtering** an animal, **one** slaughtering **for the sake of one of all those** enumerated in the first clause of the mishna **and one** slaughtering **for the sake of a legitimate matter,** their **slaughter is not valid.**

The *mishna* then extends the ruling to the case of *shnayim ochzin b'sakin*, two people holding a knife together and slaughtering, one with intent for one of these problematic *korban* designations, and one with entirely kosher intent. As in the earlier case involving *avodah zarah*, *shechitato pesulah* — the *shechita* is invalid, because the problematic intent of one party, even combined with the legitimate intent of another, is sufficient to disqualify.

### Chatat Asham Bechor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת, לְשֵׁם אָשָׁם וַדַּאי, לְשֵׁם בְּכוֹר, לְשֵׁם מַעֲשֵׂר, לְשֵׁם תְּמוּרָה – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **one who slaughters** an animal **for the sake of a sin offering, for the sake of a guilt offering** for **a definite** transgression, **for the sake of** the offering of **a firstborn, for the sake of** the offering of animal **tithe,** or **for the sake of a substitute** for a sacrificial animal, **his slaughter is valid.** All of these offerings may be brought only as obligations and not as gifts. Therefore, there is no concern that he consecrated the animals.

The *mishna* then lists a contrasting category: *hashochet l'shem chatas, l'shem asham vadai, l'shem bechor, l'shem ma'aser, l'shem tmurah* — one who slaughters with intent for a sin offering, a definite guilt offering, a firstborn animal, the animal tithe, or an exchange. In all these cases: *shechitato kesheirah* — the *shechita* is valid.

### Kol Davar Shenidar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל דָּבָר שֶׁנִּידָּר וְנִידָּב – הַשּׁוֹחֵט לִשְׁמוֹ אָסוּר, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נִידָּר וְנִידָּב – הַשּׁוֹחֵט לִשְׁמוֹ כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** For **any item,** i.e., offering, **which** is consecrated as a voluntary **vow or gift,** in the case of **one who slaughters for its sake** the animal is **forbidden. And** for any offering **that is not** consecrated as a voluntary **vow or gift** but is an obligation that is incumbent upon him, in the case of **one who slaughters for its sake** the animal is **permitted.**

The unifying principle is stated immediately: *kol davar shenidar v'nidav, hashochet l'shmo assur* — any offering that can be brought by way of a free-will vow, if one slaughters with that designation, it is forbidden. *v'she'eino nidar v'nidav, hashochet l'shmo kasher* — any offering that cannot be brought by voluntary vow — that is, one whose incidence is determined by obligation or by contingent circumstance — if one slaughters with that designation, it is valid.

The rationale is intuitive. A free-will offering — an *olah*, *shelamim*, *todah* — is within anyone's power to create at any time simply by making a vow. When someone slaughters an animal claiming it is for such an offering, the claim is plausible, and the act genuinely resembles *kodshim bachutz*. But a *chatas* can only arise from sin, an *asham vadai* from specific categories of transgression, a *bechor* only by being a genuine firstborn, *ma'aser* only through the counted tenth of a flock, and *tmurah* only by exchanging an already-sanctified animal. None of these can be created by an arbitrary act of will. When someone claims to be slaughtering for such a designation, the claim is inherently implausible — it looks like nothing recognizable, not a real *korban* — and therefore creates no appearance of *kodshim bachutz*.

### Asham Talui B'chol Yom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה. אָשָׁם תָּלוּי בַּר נִידָּר וְנִידָּב הוּא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מִתְנַדֵּב אָדָם אָשָׁם תָּלוּי בְּכׇל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: In the case of **one who slaughters** an animal and asserts that he is slaughtering it **for the sake of a burnt offering,** for the sake of a peace offering, for the sake of a provisional guilt offering, for the sake of a Paschal offering, or for the sake of a thanks offering, the slaughter is not valid. This is because one who slaughters for the sake of any type of offering that is consecrated as a vow or as a gift renders the animal forbidden. The Gemara asks: **Is a provisional guilt offering fit** to be consecrated as **a vow or** as **a gift?** A provisional guilt offering is brought only when one is obligated to do so due to uncertainty whether or not he is liable to bring a sin offering. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *halakha* in the mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says** in a mishna (*Karetot* 25a): **A person donates a provisional guilt offering every day** if he chooses, due to concern that perhaps he violated a prohibition.

The *gemara* immediately raises a difficulty with the *mishna*'s first list. *asham talui* was included among the offerings that create an invalidating appearance — but an *asham talui* is not a free-will offering. It arises under specific circumstances: one performed an act that may have been a sin, and one is uncertain. This should place it in the second category, where slaughtering with that designation is *kasher*, not *pasul*.

*Rabbi Yochanan* resolves this: *hai mani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — the *mishna* reflects the position of *rabbi eliezer*, who taught *mitnadev adam asham talui b'chol yom* — one may voluntarily bring an *asham talui* every single day. According to *rabbi eliezer*, an abstract, daily concern about possible unknown sins is sufficient basis to bring an *asham talui* without any specific concrete doubt. This transforms the *asham talui*, for *rabbi eliezer*, into something close to a free-will offering — one that anyone could genuinely be bringing at any time. Therefore, if someone slaughters and states it is for an *asham talui*, the claim is plausible, the appearance of *kodshim bachutz* is real, and the *shechita* is invalidated.

### Pesach Pashuto Kol Hasha…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פֶּסַח בַּר נִידָּב וְנִידָּר הוּא? זִמְנָא קְבִיעָא לֵיהּ! אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: שָׁאנֵי פֶּסַח, הוֹאִיל וְהַפְרָשָׁתוֹ כׇּל הַשָּׁנָה כּוּלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is a Paschal offering fit** to be consecrated as **a vow or** as **a gift? The time is fixed for its** offering on Passover eve, when bringing it is an obligation, and it may not be brought on any other day. **Rabbi Oshaya said: The Paschal offering is different, since** although the date for bringing and slaughtering it is the fourteenth of Nisan, **its designation** can be performed **throughout the entire year.**

The same problem arises with *pesach*. A *korban pesach* can only be offered on the fourteenth of *nisan*; it is not a free-will offering that can be brought at will. Why, then, does the *mishna* list it among the invalidating designations?

*Rabbi Yehoshua* answers: *shani pesach ho'il u'fashuto kol ha-shanah kulah* — the *pesach* is different, because one can designate an animal for the *korban pesach* months in advance. It is not the case that the *pesach* can only become relevant at the moment of offering. From the moment of designation, the animal acquires a sacred character, and if it were slaughtered before *pesach* it would be treated as a *shelamim*. Therefore it is entirely plausible that someone designating an animal as a *pesach* and slaughtering it now — even in *tishri* — is engaged in a genuine act involving a *korban*. The appearance of *kodshim bachutz* is therefore real, and the *shechita* is invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא תְּמִימִים, אֲבָל בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין – מִידָּע יְדִיעַ. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעֲלֵי מוּמִין נָמֵי, זִימְנִין דִּרְמֵי לֵיהּ מִידֵּי אַמּוּמָא וְלָא יְדִיעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yannai says:** The Sages **taught** that slaughter of an animal for the sake of an offering is not valid **only** if the animals were **unblemished. But** with regard to animals with **blemishes,** the slaughterer **knows** that they are blemished and disqualified from sacrifice. Therefore, despite his declaration, there is no concern that his actual intent was to slaughter the animal for that purpose. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Slaughter of an animal for the sake of an offering is not valid **even** if the animals were **blemished as well,** as there are **times that an item is cast over the blemish** and covers it, **and he does not know** that the animal is blemished.

The *gemara* adds a further qualification (*rabbi yannai*): the concern about appearances applies only if the animal is unblemished (*tmimim*). If it is a *ba'al mum* — a blemished animal — it is self-evidently disqualified as a *korban*, and no observer would take seriously the claim that it is being offered. *rabbi yochanan* qualifies even this: even a blemished animal can sometimes create an appearance problem, since a blemish can be hidden by wool or covering, and the blemish might not be apparent to observers. Therefore the concern extends even to some blemished animals.

### L'shem Chatosi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשּׁוֹחֵט לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת, אֲבָל מְחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת – אֵימָא לְשׁוּם חַטָּאתוֹ הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. וְהָא לָא קָאָמַר ״לְשֵׁם חַטָּאתִי״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״לְשֵׁם חַטָּאתִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: With regard to **one who slaughters** an animal **for the sake of a sin offering,** his slaughter is valid, as, since one cannot voluntarily contribute a sin offering, there is no concern that the onlookers will draw the wrong conclusion. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The Sages **taught** that slaughter of an animal for the sake of a sin offering is valid **only** with regard to a slaughterer **who is not liable** to bring **a sin offering. But** a slaughterer who knows that he is **liable** to bring **a sin offering,** his slaughter is not valid. **Say** that **he is performing** consecration and slaughter **for the sake of his sin offering.** The Gemara asks: **But** didn't he say before the slaughter: For the sake of a sin offering, and **he did not say: For the sake of my sin offering?** Why, then, is that a concern? **Rabbi Abbahu said:** Indeed, the reference is to a case **where he says:** I am slaughtering this animal **for the sake of my sin offering.**

The *gemara* examines the ruling regarding *chatas*. The *mishna* holds that slaughtering *l'shem chatas* is *kasher* — a sin offering is not a free-will offering and cannot be created at will. *rabbi yochanan* qualifies: the dispensation applies only *she'ino mekhu-yav chatas* — when the person is not known to be obligated in a *chatas*. If, however, the person *is* known to have a *chatas* obligation — say, everyone knows he violated a *torah* prohibition inadvertently and has not yet brought his *chatas* — then the general statement *l'shem chatas* raises a real concern, because it might actually correspond to a genuine obligation he is now fulfilling outside the *beit haMikdash*.

But the *gemara* pushes back: even if he is obligated, when he says *l'shem chatas* generically rather than *l'shem chatasi* — for the sake of *my* sin offering — the designation is not sufficiently specific to suggest he is actually bringing the *korban* owed. *rava* resolves: the *mishna*'s implicit case is *b'omer l'shem chatasi* — where he explicitly says "for the sake of my sin offering." In that circumstance, when he is in fact obligated and states a specific personal reference, the act genuinely resembles bringing his obligated *chatas* outside the *beit haMikdash*, and the *shechita* is invalid.

### L'shem Tmurah Tmirah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשֵׁם תְּמוּרָה, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ זֶבַח בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ זֶבַח בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ – אֵימַר אֲמוֹרֵי אֲמַיר בֵּיהּ. וְהָא לָא קָאָמַר ״לְשֵׁם תְּמוּרַת זִבְחִי״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״לְשֵׁם תְּמוּרַת זִבְחִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: With regard to one who slaughters **for the sake of a substitute** for a sacrificial animal, his slaughter is valid. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The Sages **taught** that the slaughter is valid **only** in a case **where he does not have** an animal consecrated as **an offering inside his house** for which it can be the substitute, **but** if **he has** an animal consecrated as **an offering in his house, say** that he is **substituting** this animal **for it,** and the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara asks: **But** didn't he say before the slaughter: For the sake of a substitute for a sacrificial animal, and **he did not say: For the sake of a substitute for my** animal consecrated as an **offering?** Why, then, is that a concern? **Rabbi Abbahu said:** Indeed, the reference is to a case **where he says:** I am slaughtering this animal **for the sake of a substitute for my** animal consecrated as an **offering.**

Similarly, the *gemara* examines the case of *tmurah*. The *mishna* rules that slaughtering *l'shem tmurah* does not invalidate — an exchange (*tmurah*) is not something one can create by free-will vow; it requires an already-sanctified animal to exchange against. *rabbi elazar* qualifies: this applies only *she'ein lo zevach betoch beiso* — when he does not have a consecrated animal in his home. If he does have a *korban* in his house, then when he says *l'shem tmurah*, he might genuinely mean it — the claim is plausible, and the *shechita* should be invalid.

The *gemara* again pushes back: even with a *korban* in the house, the generic phrase *l'shem tmurah* does not establish that this is the *tmurah* of that particular animal. *rava* again resolves: the case is *b'omer l'shem tmurat zivchi* — where he explicitly says "for the sake of the exchange of my consecrated animal." In that circumstance the act genuinely appears to be a real *tmurah* transaction, and it creates the appearance of *kodshim bachutz*.

### Olas Nazir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״זֶה הַכְּלָל״ לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי עוֹלַת נָזִיר, דְּמַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָא לָא נְדַר, אֵימַר נֶדֶר בְּצִינְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna states that **this is the principle:** For any offering that is consecrated as a voluntary vow or gift, in the case of one who slaughters for its sake, the animal is forbidden. The Gemara asks: **What** case does this clause **add?** The list in the mishna appears to be comprehensive. The Gemara answers: The clause serves **to add** the **burnt offering of a nazirite. As, lest you say** there is no concern in that case, **as that** person did **not vow** to become a nazirite and could not possibly be obligated to bring that offering, therefore, the *tanna* teaches that there is a concern if he said he was slaughtering for the sake of the burnt offering of a nazirite. **Say** that perhaps **he vowed** to become a nazirite **in private,** and no one else was aware of it.

The *mishna*'s concluding principle — *zeh ha-kelal* — is intended to include additional cases. The *gemara* identifies one: *olas nazir*, the burnt offering brought by a *nazir* upon completing his vow. One might have thought this case presents no concern: if the individual never took a *nezirut* vow, obviously no one will think he is genuinely bringing an *olas nazir*. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning: *mahu d'teima halo nadar* — even without publicly known *nezirut*, others might assume he has taken a vow privately. We cannot be certain he is not a *nazir*. Therefore, slaughtering *l'shem olas nazir* creates a genuine appearance of *kodshim bachutz*, and it is included in the category of invalid *shechita*.

### Olas Yoledes Eima Ipulei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ נִידָּר וְנִידָּב, לְאֵתוֹיֵי עוֹלַת יוֹלֶדֶת. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אִשָּׁה, אֲבָל יֵשׁ לוֹ אִשָּׁה – אֵימַר לִשְׁמָהּ הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. וְהָא לָא קָאָמַר ״לְשֵׁם עוֹלַת אִשְׁתִּי״? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״לְשֵׁם עוֹלַת אִשְׁתִּי״. פְּשִׁיטָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אִם אִיתָא דְּיָלְדָה – קָלָא הֲוָה לֵיהּ, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: אֵימַר אַפּוֹלֵי אַפִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: What is added by the second half of the principle: **And** for any offering **that is not consecrated** as a voluntary **vow or** as **a gift,** in the case of one who slaughters for its sake the animal is permitted? The Gemara answers: It serves **to add the burnt offering of a woman after childbirth.** If one slaughters an animal for the sake of a burnt offering of a woman after childbirth, the slaughter is valid, as it is an obligation. **Rabbi Elazar says:** The Sages **taught** that the slaughter is valid **only** in a case **where he does not have a wife. But** if **he has a wife, say that he performs** consecration and slaughter of the animal **for her sake** and therefore the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara asks: **But** didn't he say before the slaughter: For the sake of the burnt offering of a woman after childbirth, and **he did not say: For the sake of the burnt offering of my wife** after childbirth? Why, then, is that a concern? **Rabbi Abbahu said:** Indeed, the reference is to a case **where he says:** I am slaughtering this animal **for the sake of the burnt offering of my wife** after childbirth. The Gemara objects: This is **obvious.** Clearly, if he explicitly declared that the slaughter is for the sake of the burnt offering of his wife after childbirth the slaughter is not valid. The Gemara answers: **Lest you say: If it is so that** his wife **gave birth, it would have** generated **publicity** and been common knowledge; therefore, one might conclude that the slaughter is valid even if he declared that the slaughter is for the sake of the burnt offering of his wife after childbirth, as in fact she did not give birth. To counter this, Rabbi Elazar **teaches us** that the slaughter is not valid. **Say** that his wife **miscarried** and is liable to bring an offering, but it is not common knowledge, because the baby was not born alive.

The *gemara* then identifies the excluded case on the *kasher* side of the principle — *v'she'eino nidar v'nidav la'asuyei*: the example offered is *olas yoledes*, the burnt offering brought by a woman who has given birth. This would seem to fall into the permitted category — a post-partum offering is not something anyone can bring by free-will vow; it is contingent on having given birth.

*Rabbi Elazar* raises an objection: if he has a wife, the concern exists — *eima l'shemah ka avid*, perhaps he is doing this for his wife. Once again, *rava* resolves: the concern arises only if he says *l'shem olas ishti* — explicitly for the sake of his wife's post-partum offering. The generic designation *olas yoledes* would not suffice even if his wife recently gave birth, because without the specific reference to his wife, no one would associate the act with her.

But the *gemara* raises a further question: even if he explicitly says it is for his wife, perhaps it is obvious to all that his wife has not recently given birth — in which case the claim is self-evidently absurd and creates no appearance. The *gemara* answers: *ippulei appil* — perhaps she miscarried. The *halacha* is that a woman who miscarries remains obligated in an *olas yoledes*, even though the birth was not carried to term and may not be publicly known. This is a genuinely sad closing case: because the possibility of a private miscarriage exists, the slaughter with explicit reference to one's wife's *olas yoledes* retains its problematic appearance, and it remains in the category of *shechita pesulah*.

*Chazak chazak ve-nitchazek.* We have completed the fourth *perek* of *masechet zevachim*. *hadran alach beit shammai.*
