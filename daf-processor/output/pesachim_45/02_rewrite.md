# Pesachim 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mixture Review

### Heter Mitztaref

Today's daf is 45. We pick up in the middle of 44b, deep in the middle of a *sugya* about *heter mitztaref l'issur* and *ta'am k'ikar*. Before shifting focus to *ta'am k'ikar*, a brief review of what was covered previously is in order, as the *sugya* is challenging and has generated considerable discussion among the *Rishonim*.

The first concept is *heter mitztaref l'issur*, stated by Rabbi Abbahu in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, which applies to a *nazir* who dips his bread in wine. The *gemara* also applies it to the *kutach habavli* of Rabbi Eliezer, who holds one is *chayav* for *kutach habavli*. The concept is this: you have a mixture — say, wine with bread soaked in it, or a dip containing pieces of *chametz* — and if you eat a *kezayit* of the combination, you are *chayav*. The *heter*, the permissible component, combines with the *issur* to render everything you eat *assur*. Presumably you actually transgress the original prohibition of *chametz* or of *yayin*. A notable complication is that Rabbi Eliezer appears to hold that one does not incur *karet* for *ta'aroves chametz*, but that can be bracketed for the moment.

### Kezayit Measurement

*Kezayit b'chedei achilat pras* is an entirely different principle. It holds that you do not combine the *heter* at all. Rather, what you have transgressed is measured by the actual quantity of *issur* present in the mixture. If you eat enough of the dip so that the aggregate amount of actual *chametz* within it equals a *kezayit*, and you consume that *kezayit* within the time it takes to eat half a loaf of bread, you are *chayav* — not for a *kezayit* of the dip as a whole, but for a *kezayit* of the *chametz* particles themselves.

The *gemara* noted this cannot happen practically with *kutach habavli*, because one simply cannot eat that much dip in the relevant time. Consequently, a person eating such a mixture has either consumed *chametz* but less than a full *kezayit* — making it *chatzi shiur* — or, according to Rabbi Eliezer, has transgressed a separate prohibition derived from the verse *kol machmetzes*, a prohibition called *ta'aroves chametz*, which carries a negative commandment but not *karet*.

### Ta'am K'Ikar Principle

The third category is *ta'am k'ikar*: when a mixture contains the taste of something forbidden, even without the substance itself, it is prohibited. A piece of *chazir* falls into chicken soup; even if the *chazir* is removed, the taste that remains is forbidden. Rashi's version of the scenario involves grapes soaked in water — after removing the grapes, the grape-flavored water is still forbidden. This is *ta'am k'ikar*, and it underlies most of the laws of mixtures in *halacha*: the question of whether something can be tasted, whether it is less than or more than one part in sixty, is essentially a measure of whether the forbidden item imparts taste.

It is important to note that *ta'am k'ikar* appears to be a new and independent prohibition rather than the original one. The original prohibition requires a *kezayit* of actual *issur*; *ta'am k'ikar* addresses a lesser category where only taste, not substance, is present. The universally familiar one-in-sixty ratio is the standard measure for determining whether such taste is present. This prohibition is generally understood not to carry *karet*.

## Ta'am Sources Debate

### Basar B'Chalav Evidence

The *gemara* raises the question: where does Rabbi Akiva derive the principle of *ta'am k'ikar*? Since for Rabbi Akiva, *mishrat* — bread dipped in wine, or grapes soaked in water — teaches the more severe and localized principle of *heter mitztaref l'issur* by a *nazir*, he cannot simultaneously use it for the universal principle of *ta'am k'ikar*. So where does he derive the universal prohibition?

The *gemara* proposes: *yalif mi-basar b'chalav* — he learns it from the prohibition of meat and milk. When you cook meat in milk, remove the meat, and are left only with the taste of the meat in the milk, that mixture is nonetheless forbidden. The taste has transferred; there is no longer any actual meat present in the milk, yet it is *assur*. *Hacha nami lo shna* — here too, it should make no difference. From this we see that taste counts as equivalent to the substance itself.

### Meat-Milk Uniqueness

The *rabbanan* reject this argument: *mi-basar b'chalav lo gamrinan* — you cannot derive a general principle from meat and milk, because *d'chidush hu*, it is a legal innovation that does not follow normal rules.

The *gemara* explores what makes it unusual. One possibility: each ingredient individually is permissible, yet together they become forbidden — the inverse of ordinary logic. But *kilayim* follows the same pattern: wheat and grape seeds, each permissible alone, become forbidden when planted together. So that cannot be the distinctive feature.

The true innovation, says the *gemara*, is this: *de'ei tarlei kulei yoma b'chalba*, if you immerse cooked meat in cold milk for an entire day, the meat soaks up the milk and you have a full interpenetration of the two — and yet it is biblically permissible. It is only forbidden when they are cooked together: *bashlei bishulei assur*. This is a striking point. A complete physical mixture of meat and milk is permitted; only the act of cooking together triggers the prohibition.

### Cooking vs. Taste

This observation points to something deeper about the nature of the *basar b'chalav* prohibition. One might assume the concern is the mixture of tastes — that the taste of meat present in the milk renders it as though actual meat were there. But the *gemara* here suggests an entirely different conceptual framework: the prohibition is not about the presence of taste at all, but about the act of cooking. The *Torah* forbids the *cooking* of meat and milk together, not their mixture. The proof: soaking meat in milk produces a total mixture of the two substances without any biblical violation. What triggers the prohibition is the act of heating them together — an act of cooking, not an act of tasting.

On this understanding, *basar b'chalav* cannot serve as evidence for the general principle that taste counts like substance. The prohibition is not structured around taste at all. It is about a specific act — *bishul* — performed upon a specific combination. Accordingly, the *rabbanan* conclude, one cannot extrapolate from *basar b'chalav* to a universal *ta'am k'ikar* principle.

### Non-Kosher Vessels

Having set aside *basar b'chalav*, the *gemara* offers Rabbi Akiva a second source: *yalif mi-gei'ulei nochrim* — he learns from the laws of non-kosher vessels seized in the war against Midian. The *Torah* in those verses — *kol asher yavo va-esh ta'aviru va-esh ve-taher* — is interpreted by the *gemara* as establishing the requirement to *kasher* vessels used with *treif* food: vessels used over fire are koshered with fire; vessels used with boiling water are koshered with boiling water.

Why does this vessel require *kashering* at all? If you cooked pork in a pot yesterday and today use it for vegetable soup, there is no pork in the soup. Even granting the mechanism by which taste of pork is absorbed into the walls of the vessel and subsequently released into the soup, what enters the soup is only *ta'am* — taste — not the pork itself. Yet the *Torah* requires *kashering*. This demonstrates, argues Rabbi Akiva, that the *Torah* recognizes the legal significance of absorbed taste. *Gei'ulei nochrim lav ta'ama b'alma hu, ve'assur* — all you have is taste from non-kosher vessels, and it is forbidden. From this Rabbi Akiva derives the universal principle of *ta'am k'ikar*, leaving *mishrat* free to teach the more severe and localized principle of *heter mitztaref l'issur* by *nazir*.

### Vessels Category

The *rabbanan* respond with a parallel objection: *gei'ulei nochrim nami chidush hu* — the case of non-kosher vessels is itself a legal anomaly and cannot serve as a general source. Their argument rests on the principle *nosein ta'am lifgam mutar* — ordinarily, when something imparts a degraded or off-putting taste, it does not create a forbidden mixture. This is the basis for the well-known ruling that a pot used more than twenty-four hours ago imparts only *ta'am lifgam* and therefore does not render what is cooked in it forbidden.

Now, the *gemara* notes: absorbed taste that has been in a vessel wall and then leached back out is always somewhat degraded — *kedeira bas yoma nami lo efshar delo pagma purta*, even on the same day the taste is slightly off. If *ta'am lifgam* were always permissible, the vessels prohibition should never apply. The fact that it does apply despite the off-putting quality of the released taste indicates that this is a special category — a prohibition of *non-kosher vessels* as such, not an application of the general *ta'am k'ikar* principle. This is the position of Rav Chiya son of Rav Huna, who held that the *Torah* only biblically forbids using a vessel on the same day it was used, when the taste is still good — meaning the prohibition is grounded in real taste, not degraded taste. The *rabbanan* counter that even same-day taste is somewhat off, making the vessel prohibition an independent category regardless.

The practical and conceptual upshot is significant: we generally assume that the law of non-kosher vessels is in fact an application of *ta'am* principles — which is why we permit, at least *b'di'avad*, a pot that has not been used for more than a day. But the *gemara* here raises the possibility that this leniency rests on a contested premise. According to the *rabbanan*, the vessel prohibition may be its own independent category, not derived from general taste principles at all.

## Two Pesukim Debate

### Rav Aha Challenge

The discussion now shifts to a question raised by Rav Aha son of Rav Avya to Rav Ashi. The logic of the *rabbanan*'s position raises an interesting inference. The *rabbanan* use *mishrat* — the case of the *nazir*'s bread dipped in wine — to derive the universal principle of *ta'am k'ikar*, applicable throughout the *Torah*. Since the *nazir* prohibition is relatively lenient, the principle derived from it may be broadened universally.

By the same logic, asks Rav Aha, why not apply *mishrat* universally for Rabbi Akiva as well? Rabbi Akiva uses *mishrat* to derive *heter mitztaref l'issur* — the more severe principle that the permissible component combines with the forbidden one — but limits it to the case of *nazir* alone. Given that the *rabbanan* can take *mishrat* and make it a universal rule, why does Rabbi Akiva not likewise universalize *heter mitztaref l'issur* across all prohibitions in the *Torah*?

### Two Pesukim Answer

Rav Ashi responds, and with this we arrive at the beginning of today's daf, 45a: *mishum de-havei Nazir ve-hatat shenei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-ehad ve-ein melamdin* — because there are two passages in the *Torah* that teach *heter mitztaref l'issur*, one involving *nazir* and one involving the *hatat*, the sin offering, and when the *Torah* states the same principle in two separate contexts, those two cases are treated as exceptions rather than as the paradigmatic rule. This is the principle of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad ein melamdin*: when the *Torah* bothers to write something twice, it signals that these are the anomalous cases; if it were a universal rule, one statement would have sufficed.

### Chattat Absorption

The *gemara* then asks: what is the *hatat* case that teaches *heter mitztaref l'issur*? It cites a *baraita*: *kol asher yigga bi-vesarah yikdash* — anything that touches the meat of the *hatat* shall be sanctified. If, for example, a piece of another *korban* or even ordinary bread touches the hot *hatat* and absorbs some of its juices, that bread acquires the legal status of the *hatat* itself. It must be eaten within the same time constraints as a *hatat* — only until the following night, only by male *kohanim*, only within the *Beit HaMikdash*. And if the *hatat* was *pasul* — disqualified through *pigul*, *notar*, or some other defect — then the bread that absorbed from it is likewise disqualified and must be burned.

The *gemara* adds the qualification: *yakhol afilu lo bala* — one might have thought that mere contact, even without absorption, suffices. The verse therefore specifies *bi-vesarah* — in its meat — indicating that actual absorption is required. Tosafot notes a textual difficulty in the direction of the absorption implied by the verse, though the *gemara* does not dwell on it.

On Rabbi Akiva's reading, this *hatat* case is an instance of *heter mitztaref l'issur*: the bread, which is otherwise permissible, combines with the *hatat* it absorbed, such that every *kezayit* of the mixture is subject to the laws of *hatat*. One who eats from such a mixture — if the *hatat* was already *notar* — receives lashes for a *kezayit* of the combined substance.

### Rabbi Akiva Stand

Rabbi Akiva's position can now be stated precisely. He knows the principle of *ta'am k'ikar* — that taste counts like substance — from the prohibition of non-kosher vessels. That being established, when *mishrat* appears in the context of *nazir*, it cannot be teaching *ta'am k'ikar*, since Rabbi Akiva already has a source for that. Therefore *mishrat* must be teaching *heter mitztaref l'issur* in the specific context of *nazir*.

Then the *Torah* teaches the same idea — *heter mitztaref l'issur* — in the context of the *hatat*. Rabbi Akiva reads both *mishrat* and the *hatat* verse as teaching *heter mitztaref l'issur*. Because the *Torah* gives two instances of this principle, the rule of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad* applies: these two are the exceptions, and *heter mitztaref l'issur* does not apply universally. It is confined to the *nazir* case and the *hatat* case.

Why could the *Torah* not have conveyed this with one verse? The *gemara* explains: *bishlama d'chesav Rachmana b'hatat* — had the *Torah* written this principle only by the *hatat*, it could not have been extended to *nazir*, because *d'chulin mi'kodashim lo gamrinan* — we do not derive rules for non-sacrificial contexts from the world of *kodashim*. The *hatat* verse alone would have left the *nazir* case undetermined. The *Torah* therefore had to write both. But since it wrote both, Rabbi Akiva concludes they are exceptions.

### Rabbanan Stand

The *rabbanan* offer a different reading. They agree that the *hatat* verse teaches *heter mitztaref l'issur*, and that this principle is limited to *kodashim* — it is not derived outside the world of sacrifices. But they read *mishrat* differently: for them, *mishrat* teaches *ta'am k'ikar*, not *heter mitztaref l'issur*. Since they do not derive *ta'am k'ikar* from non-kosher vessels — which they hold to be an independent category — they need *mishrat* to establish the universal *ta'am k'ikar* principle applicable throughout the *Torah*.

On this reading, the two verses are *not* teaching the same thing. *Mishrat* teaches *ta'am k'ikar* universally; the *hatat* verse teaches *heter mitztaref l'issur* but only within *kodashim*. Since the two verses serve distinct purposes, *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad* does not apply — there is no redundancy, and there are thus no two identical exceptions to generate a limiting rule.

Rabbi Akiva responds: *traveihu l'heter mitztaref l'issur* — both verses are teaching *heter mitztaref l'issur*. Since he derives *ta'am k'ikar* from vessels, he does not need *mishrat* for that purpose, and *mishrat* thus joins the *hatat* verse as a second statement of the same principle. That redundancy is precisely what invokes *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im k'echad* and limits the principle to its two stated contexts.

The practical upshot is that all authorities agree to the universal prohibition of *ta'am k'ikar* and agree that *heter mitztaref l'issur* is confined to a narrow range of cases. The debate is only over the sourcing: whether *mishrat* teaches *ta'am k'ikar* universally (the *rabbanan*) or teaches *heter mitztaref l'issur* locally (Rabbi Akiva), with *ta'am k'ikar* coming instead from vessels.

Returning briefly to the question of *ta'aroves chametz*: according to everyone, if a *chametz* mixture has *ta'am* — which it would in an *eino mino* context — it is at minimum subject to a prohibition of taste. The debate about *ta'aroves chametz* concerns whether one can transgress a more serious *lav*, namely, whether one has actually consumed a *kezayit* of *chametz* itself within *chedei achilat pras*. Rabbi Eliezer holds there is an independent prohibition for *ta'aroves chametz* derived from *kol machmetzes*, carrying a *lav* but not *karet*, even when a full *kezayit* of *chametz* has not been consumed. Whether the *halacha* follows Rabbi Eliezer on this point, and exactly where the thresholds fall, remains a substantial debate among the *Rishonim*.

## Combination Rules

### Bread-Wine Combination

Rav Ashi raises a question to Rav Kahana based on a *baraita*: *micol asher yei'aseh mi-geffen ha-yayin mei'chartzanim v'ad zag* — anything made of the vine, from the skin to the pit — is cited to teach that the various prohibited items for a *nazir* combine with one another. A little bit of grape skin, some pits, and some juice all combine to reach the minimum quantity required for lashes. But if this is so, Rav Ashi asks, *hashta l'Rabbi Akiva issur v'heter mitztarfin* — if bread (a *heter*) combines with wine (an *issur*), surely it goes without saying that *issur* and *issur* should combine? Why does the *Torah* need to tell us that grape skins and pits and juice all combine when we already know that a *heter* combines with an *issur*?

### Eaten Together Rule

The *gemara* answers by drawing a crucial distinction. *Issur v'heter b'vas achas* — a *heter* combines with an *issur* only when they are eaten together simultaneously, as a single intake. If you soak bread in wine and eat that piece — the bread and wine entering your mouth as one — the bread combines with the wine. But if you separate the bread from the wine and eat them sequentially, they do not combine.

*Issur v'issur*, by contrast — grape skin, pits, and juice — combine even *achar zeh achar zeh*, even when eaten sequentially, provided it is within the requisite time. Therefore the *Torah* does need a separate verse to tell us that these multiple *issurim* for a *nazir* combine across sequential eating. *Heter mitztaref l'issur* is thus a more constrained principle than it might first appear: it operates only when the *heter* and *issur* are consumed together in one act, not across separate sequential intakes.

## Dough in Cracks

### Mishnah Rule

The *gemara* now shifts to a new *mishnah*, though still within the broader theme of ensuring one's home is free of *chametz*. *Batzek she-bi-sidkei ariva* — dough that has become lodged in the cracks of a kneading trough. *Im yesh kezayit b'makom echad chayav leva'er* — if in any single location there is a *kezayit*, one is obligated to remove it. *Ve-im lav, batel b'mi'uto* — and if not, it is nullified by its smallness.

Rashi notes that even if the accumulated total from all the cracks combined equals a *kezayit*, if no single location contains a *kezayit*, the dough loses its status as *chametz* and becomes integrated into the vessel — one may ignore it. The *mishnah* evidently assumes that a *kezayit* in one place is accessible and can be removed; the case where *ein kelev yachol lachapes acharav* — it is so inaccessible that not even a dog could search it out — would be a separate matter. Similarly, the *mishnah* here is not discussing mere crumbs (*perurim*); the dough in question, while scattered, may represent substantial quantities in individual crevices.

### Chatzitzah Status

The *mishnah* then addresses a related issue: *ve-chen le-inyan ha-tum'ah* — similarly with respect to impurity. *Im makpid alav chotzetz* — if the vessel owner objects to the dough being there, the dough constitutes a *chatzitzah*, a barrier, for purposes of *mikvah*. *Ve-im rotzeh b'kiyumo harei hu ke-ariva* — if the owner wants it there, it becomes like the vessel itself and is not a *chatzitzah*.

The *mishnah* thus hinges on the owner's desire. The dough filling a crack in the vessel wall functions as a kind of putty; if the owner wants it there — because it structurally fills the crack — it becomes part of the vessel and creates no barrier. If the owner does not want it there, it remains extraneous and is a *chatzitzah*. Left unresolved by the *mishnah* is the intermediate case: what if the owner has no particular opinion, neither wanting nor not wanting the dough there? This ambiguity is significant in the broader laws of *chatzitzah*, where the question of whether indifference suffices — or whether affirmative desire is required — is a central dispute.

## Strengthening Factor

### Yehuda's Ruling

The *gemara* now introduces an additional variable. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *lo shanu ela b'makom she'ein asuy l'chazek* — the rule that a *kezayit* requires removal applies only in a location where the dough does not structurally strengthen the vessel. *Aval b'makom she'asuy l'chazek* — but if the dough is lodged in a crack where it genuinely reinforces the vessel, like putty reinforcing a joint, *eino chayiv l'va'er* — one is not obligated to remove it, even if it is a *kezayit*, because it has truly become part of the vessel.

The *gemara* infers from this statement: *miklal d'pachot mikezayit afilu b'makom she'ein asuy l'chazek eino chayiv l'va'er* — by implication, less than a *kezayit* in a place that does not strengthen the vessel is likewise not a problem. The upshot is that two factors interact: whether the location structurally reinforces the vessel (*asuy l'chazek* or not), and whether the quantity reaches a *kezayit*. To constitute a problem requiring removal, both must be unfavorable: it must be in a non-reinforcing location *and* amount to a *kezayit*.

### Baraitot Contradiction

*Ika d'matni lah asseifa* — an alternative tradition applies Rav Yehuda's principle to the *seifa* of the *mishnah*, the clause about *pachot mikezayit*, and yields a far stricter ruling. On this reading: when does the rule *batel b'mi'uto* — that less than a *kezayit* is not a problem — apply? Only *b'makom asuy l'chazek* — in a location where the dough is strengthening the vessel. *Aval b'makom she'ein asuy l'chazek* — if the location does not reinforce the vessel, then even *pachot mikezayit*, even less than a *kezayit*, requires removal.

The inverse inference follows: a *kezayit*, even *b'makom she'asuy l'chazek*, even where the dough is reinforcing — *chayav l'va'er*, one must still remove it.

We thus have two conflicting readings. The lenient position holds that a problem requires *both* factors to be unfavorable: a *kezayit* in a non-reinforcing location. Any other combination — a *kezayit* in a reinforcing location, or less than a *kezayit* anywhere — is acceptable. The strict position holds that a *kezayit* is always a problem, even in a reinforcing location; the only leniency (*ein asuy l'chazek* being acceptable) is when the quantity falls below a *kezayit* and the location is reinforcing.

These two positions correspond to two *baraitot* that appear to contradict one another. The *gemara* confirms the contradiction — *kashya ahadadi* — and records that Rav Huna proposed simply eliminating the lenient *baraita* in the presence of the strict one: *sami kalta mikamaita*. Since we are dealing with *chametz* and *Pesach*, rule like the stricter position.

Rav Yosef objects to this approach: *tannayei shakalta me'hai alma* — you cannot simply erase a *baraita* as though you have removed all *tannaim* from the world. These are two competing *tannaic* positions and must be treated as a *machlokes tannaim*. To demonstrate that an explicit *tannaic* dispute underlies the two *baraitot*, the *gemara* cites a *baraita*: *ha-pas she'ifsha chayav l'va'er mipnei she'ra'uya l'shochakah u'l'chamei'a bah kama isos acheros* — bread that has become moldy must still be removed, because it can be ground up and used as a sourdough starter for other batches of dough; even if no longer fit for human consumption in its current form, it retains enough utility to matter.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: *bameh devarim amurim b'mkuyemes l'achilah* — when is that true? Only when it is still situated in a context of food use. But *aval kupas se'or sheyichada l'yeshivah* — if the vessel containing sourdough in its cracks has been permanently repurposed as a seat, entirely removed from the world of food use, *batlah* — it is annulled. The *Tanna Kamma* implicitly disagrees.

The *gemara* reads this dispute as bearing on the question of whether a *kezayit* can ever be nullified. The *Tanna Kamma* apparently holds: *kol kezayit af al gav de-mevatel lo batel* — once a *kezayit* is present, no act of nullification — not repurposing the vessel, not even treating the dough as though it has become part of the vessel — avails. Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar holds that repurposing suffices to nullify even a *kezayit* under certain conditions.

Abaye then challenges this resolution: *taratzta b'kezayit, pachot mikezayit mai taratzta?* — you have found a *tannaic* dispute about whether a *kezayit* can be nullified, but what about the question of less than a *kezayit* in a non-reinforcing location? No *tanna* has been explicitly shown to hold that *pachot mikezayit* in a non-reinforcing location is still a problem. You cannot claim that this dispute covers both *baraitot*.

### Location Explanation

*Ella ha be-Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar hi* — Abaye concludes that in fact both *baraitot* represent the position of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and the contradiction between them is not a dispute at all. *V'lo kashya — ha be-makom lisha, ha shelo be-makom lisha* — the two *baraitot* are discussing different locations on the vessel, and there is no contradiction once one distinguishes between those locations.

Rashi explains that there are effectively three locations to consider on a kneading trough. The floor of the vessel is the most load-bearing and reinforcing part; a crack in the floor is most properly described as *asuy l'chazek*. The walls of the vessel — *makom lisha*, where the kneading actually occurs — are intermediate: they bear some structural role but are less critical. The lip or edge of the vessel — *shelo b'makom lisha* — performs no real structural function.

The first *baraita* compares the floor (calling it *asuy l'chazek*) and the wall (calling it, relatively, *ein asuy l'chazek*). On this comparison, the wall is the weaker case: dough there is only acceptable if it is *pachot mikezayit*, while dough in the floor is fine even if it is a *kezayit*. The second *baraita* compares the wall (calling it *asuy l'chazek*) and the lip (calling it *ein asuy l'chazek*). On this comparison, the lip is always a problem, while the wall is acceptable only when *pachot mikezayit*. The two *baraitot* are consistent — they simply apply their terms to different pairs of locations, with the wall occupying an intermediate position in both.

Rav Ashi adds a further precision: *lo teima shelo b'makom lisha agab d'agna* — do not think that the case of *shelo b'makom lisha*, where even a small amount is problematic, refers only to the outermost back of the lip. *Ela a-sipsa d'agna* — even the inner edge of the lip itself is in this category. One might have thought that the lip sometimes receives dough during kneading and should therefore count as *makom lisha*. Rav Ashi teaches that this is not so: the lip is never treated as a place of kneading, and even minimal dough there creates a problem.

## Rav Nachman Debate

### Shimon Ben Elazar Rule

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Rav: halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — Rav Nachman rules in the name of Rav that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar: a *kezayit* in a genuinely *asuy l'chazek* location is considered part of the vessel and need not be removed.

### Plastering Requirement

An alternative teaching in the name of Rav is reported by Rav Yitzchak bar Ashi: *im tach pania b'tit bitlah* — if one plasters over the surface of the vessel with clay, the dough underneath is nullified. The implication of *tach in, lo tach lo* is pointed: only if one actively plasters over the dough does it become nullified; without plastering, it is not sufficient that the dough happens to be in a reinforcing location. The *gemara* resolves this by noting that these are two separate traditions transmitted in the name of Rav, reflecting a dispute among *Amoraim* as to what Rav actually held. One tradition has Rav ruling like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar without requiring plastering; another has Rav requiring active plastering over the dough to achieve nullification.

An alternative version reverses the *halacha*: *amar Rav Nachman amar Rav ein halacha k'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — we do not rule like Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar at all. On this reading, the requirement to plaster reported by Rav Yitzchak bar Ashi is the more lenient position, while outright rejection of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar's ruling means that even a *kezayit* in a structurally reinforcing location is a problem regardless.

### Connection Test

*Amar Rav Nachman amar Shmuel: shnei chatzei zeisim v'chut shel batzek beineihem* — if two half-*kezayit* pieces of dough are connected by a thin strand of dough, are they considered a single *kezayit* in one place, or two separate half-quantities?

The *gemara* provides a practical test: *ro'im* — one assesses whether *kol she'ilu yinatel ha-chut v'nitlin imo* — if, when the connecting strand is lifted, both halves are lifted with it, then they are considered one connected *kezayit* and one is *chayav l'va'er*. If not — if the halves separate when the strand is lifted — they are treated as two distinct, insufficient quantities.

## House Application

### House Stringency

*Amar Ulla: lo amrin ela b'areiva* — Ulla rules that all the lenient principles discussed above — that dough below a *kezayit*, or dough in a structurally reinforcing location, may be disregarded — apply only in the kneading trough. *Aval b'bayis chayav l'va'er* — but if the dough is embedded in the walls of a house, one is obligated to remove it regardless.

### Risk of Accumulation

*Mai ta'ama?* — What is the reason for this distinction? *De-zimnin d'kanish lehu v'nafal gabi hadadei* — because with a house, there is a realistic concern that the dough will fall from the walls or be swept up from various locations and accumulate in one spot, ultimately creating a *kezayit* in a single place. The leniency for the kneading trough relied on the dough being truly embedded and integrated into the vessel; in a house, the structural integration is far less assured, and the practical risk of accumulation is too great to permit the same leniency.

### Unresolved Cases

Several practical questions were then raised in the West, in *Eretz Yisrael*: *bayis v'aliya mahu* — what if there is half a *kezayit* of dough embedded in the wall of the lower floor and half a *kezayit* in the wall of the upper floor? Does the concern about accumulation apply across stories? *Bayis v'achsadra mahu* — what of a house and its adjacent portico? *Shnei batim ze lifnim mize mahu* — what of two rooms, one inside the other? The *gemara* does not resolve any of these questions. *Teiku.*

### Rosh Perspective

The Rosh observes that all of this theoretical analysis — the debates over *asuy l'chazek*, *kezayit*, sequential versus simultaneous eating, the unresolved questions about multiple rooms and stories — has little practical bearing. He writes: *v'eini tzorich lichtov kol machlokes ha-Rishonim lefi she-Yisrael kedoshim heim v'gorrin u-mevarin kol chametz ha-nimtza afilu kolshehu v'afilu im nidbak b'kosei ha-bayis* — I need not record all the disputes of the *Rishonim*, because the Jewish people are holy and scrape out and destroy all *chametz* they find, however small, even if it is embedded in the walls of the house.

What is notable here is the perspective the Rosh provides: the practice of being extraordinarily stringent about *chametz* — going far beyond what any theoretical *halacha* would require — was already firmly established in the era of the *Rishonim*. The *gemara* itself, by contrast, engaged quite carefully with the precise parameters: when exactly is embedded dough a problem, what role does structural reinforcement play, how do the different locations on a vessel compare? It is a more measured and legally precise analysis than the blanket stringency that came to characterize Jewish practice. The *Rosh* acknowledges both — recording the *gemara*'s careful reasoning while also noting that in practice, it does not change what Jews do.
