# Kiddushin 63 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Davar Shelo Ba L'olam

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָתֵר עַל כֵּן אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״פֵּירוֹת עֲרוּגָה זוֹ תְּלוּשִׁין יִהְיוּ תְּרוּמָה עַל פֵּירוֹת עֲרוּגָה זוֹ מְחוּבֶּרֶת״, ״פֵּירוֹת עֲרוּגָה זוֹ מְחוּבֶּרֶת יִהְיוּ תְּרוּמָה עַל פֵּירוֹת עֲרוּגָה זוֹ תְּלוּשִׁין לִכְשֶׁיָּבִיאוּ שְׁלִישׁ וְיִתָּלְשׁוּ״, וְהֵבִיאוּ שְׁלִישׁ וְנִתְלְשׁוּ – דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Moreover, Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov said** that **even** if **one said: The detached produce of this garden bed will be *teruma* for the attached produce of this garden bed,** or: **The attached produce of this garden bed will be *teruma* for the detached produce of this garden bed when they,** i.e., the attached produce, **will reach one-third** of their growth **and are detached,** although at the time they had yet to ripen which means that the obligations of *terumot* and tithes do not apply to them, when **they will reach one-third** of their growth **and are detached, his statement is upheld.**

The sugya on this daf continues a discussion that began at the bottom of 62b, concerning the principle of *davar shelo ba l'olam* — a legal act performed in relation to something that has not yet come into existence. The preceding mishna established that if a man says to a woman, "You are married to me after you are divorced," or "after your husband dies," or "after I convert" or "after you convert," the *kiddushin* does not take effect. The *gemara*, however, has been exploring whether there are circumstances in which such an act might nonetheless be valid — particularly where the missing component is *b'yado*, within the person's control, or where its coming into being is naturally anticipated.

It is useful to distinguish three categories of *davar shelo ba l'olam*. The first is something not yet present that the person himself can bring about — *b'yado* — where even under normal rules some authorities allow the transaction to work. The second is something that will naturally occur if circumstances proceed as expected: grain will ripen, a pregnant woman will give birth. Though not *b'yado*, these are reasonably anticipated. The third and most extreme category encompasses things whose occurrence is genuinely uncertain and unpredictable — being freed from slavery, a husband dying, obtaining *chalitzah* from a *yavam*. These represent the hardest cases of *davar shelo ba l'olam*.

### R. Eliezer b. Yaakov

The *gemara* had been focused on the position of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, who recognizes validity even in the middle category — things that will happen naturally and as anticipated, though not within the actor's direct control. This position now serves as the launching point for Abaye's broader observation about the landscape of *tannaitic* opinion on the subject.

## Three Tannaim

### Abaye's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב, וְרַבִּי, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ – אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לְעוֹלָם. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. רַבִּי – דְּתַנְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says: Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and Rabbi Meir all hold** the following principle: **A person can transfer an entity that has not yet come into the world.** That is, one can perform an act of acquisition for an item that is not yet in existence. The opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov** is referring to **that which we** just **said,** that one can separate *teruma* even for produce not yet included in this mitzva. Where does **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi state a similar opinion? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*:

Four lines from the bottom of 62b, the *gemara* records: *Amar Abaye* — Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi (*Yehuda HaNasi*) — *kulhu sevira lehu adam makneh davar shelo ba l'olam*, all three share the view that a person can transfer ownership or effect a legal transaction with respect to something or someone not yet in existence. Abaye characteristically identifies *tannaim* who, though not in identical cases, share a common legal disposition, and groups them together. The three positions are not identical, but they reflect a shared underlying approach.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov's case has just been discussed: fruit not yet ripened, not *b'yado*, but naturally expected to come about. That is the most accessible form of *davar shelo ba l'olam*.

### Rabbi & Slave

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא תַסְגִּיר עֶבֶד אֶל אֲדֹנָיו״, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: בְּלוֹקֵחַ עֶבֶד עַל מְנָת לְשַׁחְרְרוֹ הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: כְּגוֹן דִּכְתַב לֵיהּ: ״לִכְשֶׁאֶקָּחֲךָ, הֲרֵי עַצְמְךָ קָנוּי לְךָ מֵעַכְשָׁיו״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"You shall not deliver a slave to his master"** (Deuteronomy 23:16), **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The verse is speaking of one who acquires a slave,** not to enslave him but **in order to emancipate him.** The court may not deliver the slave to this master, as this master has no right to enslave him. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: For example, if** the new master **wrote to** the slave in his bill of manumission: **When I will purchase you, you are hereby acquired to yourself from now.** In that case the slave acquires himself from that moment, despite the fact that the buyer does not own him. This shows that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintains that a person can transfer an item that has not yet entered the world, as one can emancipate his slave before acquiring him.

What is Rabbi's position? The *gemara* cites a *braita* (top of 63a): *lo tashgir eved el adonav* — you shall not hand over a slave to his master. Rabbi interprets this verse as referring to one who purchases a slave with the stipulation that he will free him. If the master then changes his mind, the community — the *beit din* — cannot return the slave to his master. The community must enforce and protect the slave's freedom.

### Freeing Slave

The *gemara* asks: *heichi dami* — what exactly is the case? Rav Dimi bar Yitzchak explains: it is a case where the purchaser wrote to the slave before acquiring him, *lech-she-ekkachecha harei atzmecha knuya lach me-achshav* — "when I acquire you, you are hereby freed, as of now." The transaction of liberation was performed before the purchaser actually owned the slave. Moreover, the outcome was not *b'yado* in any full sense — the seller was not necessarily going to complete the sale — and yet the act of liberation takes effect.

This is precisely what Abaye uses to demonstrate Rabbi's position: the fact that Rabbi is willing to read the verse this way, choosing to understand *lo tashgir* as enforcing an actual, effective transaction, shows that Rabbi presupposes the legal validity of such an act. The *gemara* clarifies: it cannot be that the purchaser merely promised to free the slave, because a moral promise is not legally binding under *halacha*, and the Torah's command would then be protecting something legally insubstantial. Nor can it be that the master freed the slave outright and simply changed his mind, because the verse says "do not return the slave to his master" — implying that in some sense he remains a slave at the time the verse speaks. The only coherent reading, therefore, is that the act of liberation was executed against a not-yet-existing ownership, and the Torah commands us to honor it.

### Me'achshav

Tosafot raises an important question about the word *me-achshav* — "as of now." Two difficulties present themselves. First, all other cases of *davar shelo ba l'olam* in the *sugya* do not use this language; if anything, they use formulations pointing toward the future, such as *lich-she-yitlashu* — when they are picked. Second, logically, how can an act take effect *now* when the necessary components are not yet in place? The slave is not yet owned; a woman being offered *kiddushin* is still married to another man.

Tosafot offers two explanations. One is that *me-achshav* carries no independent legal force; it is simply a clarification of Rabbi's intent rather than a technically operative term. The other, more developed explanation draws on a general principle that when an act is intended to take effect in the future, something of that act — some remnant or trace — must still be operative at the moment it is supposed to take effect. So if one says "this item will be yours in thirty days," there must be some continuing connection between the transaction and the object at the time it activates. Tosafot suggests that *me-achshav* serves to give the act of liberation sufficient force and permanence that it does not require such a remnant later. Even if the *shtar* of liberation were subsequently lost or torn, the act would still take effect — because the word *me-achshav* establishes it with the full force of a present act, even though it cannot literally take present effect.

This does not mean the *shtar* need not be given at all. The entire mechanism of *davar shelo ba l'olam* requires that the transactional instrument — the ring, the *shtar* — be given now, to a slave who belongs to another, or to a woman still married to another man. That is precisely the striking feature of the principle: you give the *shtar* to a slave owned by someone else, and it takes effect once the necessary circumstances align. But without *me-achshav*, the recipient would need to retain the *shtar* for the transaction to remain viable. With *me-achshav*, the act is sufficiently anchored that even if the *shtar* is lost, the liberation holds.

## Rabbi Meir Extreme

### Extreme Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶתְגַּיֵּיר״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּתְגַּיְּירִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁאֶשְׁתַּחְרֵר״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתִּשְׁתַּחְרְרִי״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיָּמוּת בַּעְלִיךְ״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁתָּמוּת אֲחוֹתִיךְ״, ״לְאַחַר שֶׁיַּחְלוֹץ לָךְ יְבָמִיךְ״ – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir** also holds that one can transfer an entity that has not yet come into the world, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me after I convert,** or: **After you convert,** or: **After I am emancipated,** or: **After you are emancipated,** or: **After your husband dies,** or: **After your sister dies,** or: **After your *yavam* performs *ḥalitza* for you, she is not betrothed. Rabbi Meir** disagrees and **says: She is betrothed.**

Rabbi Meir is classically associated with the principle of *davar shelo ba l'olam*. The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *ha'omer l'isha harei at mekudeshet li l'achar she'isgayer l'achar she'tisgayri l'achar she'ishtachrer l'achar she'tishtachrari* — if he says to a woman, "You are married to me after I convert, after you convert, after I am freed from slavery, after you are freed from slavery." None of these are *b'yado*, none are naturally anticipated occurrences. The *braita* continues: *l'achar sheyamus ba'aleich l'achar shetamus achoseich* — after your husband dies, after your sister dies (the latter presumably relevant where he had once been married to the sister). *L'achar sheyachalotz l'yevameich* — after your *yavam* performs *chalitzah*, a provision that itself presumes a *yevamah* cannot receive *kiddushin* while still awaiting *yibbum* or *chalitzah*, contrary to the way we rule.

### Rabbi Meir Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא אוֹמֵר: מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ ״אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת״ – מִשּׁוּם אֵיבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says** that in some of these cases, by Torah law **she is betrothed. And** for **what reason did** the Sages **say she is not betrothed? Due to enmity.** Giving betrothal to a married woman that will take effect after her husband dies can engender enmity between the woman and her husband. Giving betrothal to one sister that depends on the death of the other can engender enmity between the sisters. Nevertheless, Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi maintain that one can betroth a woman who is currently forbidden to him.

*Eina mekudeshet* — the *tanna kamma* rules that none of this works. *Rabbi Meir omer mekudeshet* — Rabbi Meir says she is betrothed. This is a remarkable position. It is striking enough that the necessary components are entirely absent and their arrival uncertain. But consider further: a non-Jew proposing *kiddushin* is not even a legal participant in the institution of *kiddushin*, which is exclusively a Jewish framework. The institution of marriage recognized for non-Jews is a different legal structure entirely. Yet Rabbi Meir maintains that such an act is nonetheless legally operative. Similarly, effecting *kiddushin* to a woman who is still an *eishes ish* — still married to another man — goes to the very heart of what *kiddushin* is designed to establish. That these extreme cases should nonetheless work under the principle of *davar shelo ba l'olam* is, to say the least, difficult to absorb.

### Afkeinhu Rabanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא! הַיְינוּ רַבִּי, הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us also count Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi** among those who maintain that a person can acquire an entity that has not yet come into the world. The reason it asks is that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is merely called Rabbi above. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **is** the same as **Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi;** they are one person.

*Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi omer mekudeshet* — Rabbi (Yehuda HaNasi) agrees with Rabbi Meir: the *kiddushin* works in principle. However, the *gemara* asks: *uma ta'am amru eina mekudeshet* — why then did the Sages say it does not take effect? The answer is *mishum eivah* — because of enmity. Rashi explains this in reference to two of the cases: betrothal conditioned on the woman's husband dying, or on her sister dying. Recognizing such *kiddushin* would create deeply dangerous social tensions — a man waiting for, or perhaps arranging, the death of a husband or a sister-in-law. These cases therefore cannot be recognized, even if technically valid, as a matter of social policy. This reflects the principle of *afkinhu rabanan l'kiddushin* — the Sages retroactively annul the *kiddushin*. Rabbi agrees with Rabbi Meir as a matter of law, and departs only on grounds of social necessity in these two specific instances.

The *gemara* then asks: since Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi agrees with Rabbi Meir, why not add him to Abaye's list alongside Rabbi Meir? The *gemara* answers: *hainu Rabbi hainu Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi* — they are the same person; Rabbi was already cited in connection with the slave case above.

## Rabbi Akiva

### Woman's Vow

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאֲנִי עוֹשָׂה לְפִיךָ״ – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְהָפֵר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר, שֶׁמָּא תַּעֲדִיף עָלָיו יָתֵר מִן הָרָאוּי לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us also count Rabbi Akiva, as it is taught** in a mishna (see *Nedarim* 85a): If a woman stated a vow to her husband: **I will** not **produce** anything **for you,** including the work that she is obligated to do for him according to the terms of her marriage contract, as **that is *konam*,** i.e., forbidden like an offering, for me, her husband **need not nullify** the vow at all. It is automatically void, since she is obligated to perform those tasks. **Rabbi Akiva says: He should** nevertheless **nullify** the vow, as **perhaps she will exceed** the required amount of work and do **more for him than is fitting for him** to receive.

The *gemara* suggests adding Rabbi Akiva to the list of *tannaim* who accept *davar shelo ba l'olam*. A *braita* is cited: a woman declares *konam she'ani oseh le'picha* — a *konam* vow, functioning like *hekdesh*, forbidding her husband any benefit from her labor. Under the laws of the *ketubah*, a husband holds rights to his wife's labor; she is not generally in a position to sever those rights through a vow. *Rabbi Akiva omer yafer* — Rabbi Akiva rules that the husband should nonetheless annul the vow. Why? *Shema ta'adif alav yoter min ha'ra'uy lo* — perhaps she will produce more than the amount to which he is entitled. The *ketubah* obligates a certain measure of labor; any excess belongs to her. If she vows to forbid everything she produces, the vow might take hold over that excess which is genuinely hers to dispose of, and the husband's benefit from what he is entitled to might thereby be compromised, since one cannot easily distinguish which labor is the obligated portion and which is the surplus. Therefore, the husband should annul the vow to prevent it from affecting the extra that is hers.

Tosafot asks: why can the husband annul in the first place? The mechanism of a husband annulling a wife's vow applies to vows that affect the marital relationship. But if the surplus labor is entirely her own property with no marital obligation attached, why would her vow over it be subject to his annulment at all? Tosafot answers that in practice one cannot precisely calculate whether a given moment of labor is within the obligated portion or beyond it. The presence of the vow therefore intrudes on his access even to what he is entitled to, since it cannot be determined in real time whether any given unit of output falls on one side of the line or the other. On this basis, even the surplus is considered to fall within the ambit of the marital relationship, and the husband can annul.

### Hands Exist

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: בְּאוֹמֶרֶת: ״יִקְדְּשׁוּ יָדַי לְעוֹשֵׂיהֶם״, וְיָדַיִם אִיתַנְהוּ בָּעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Wasn't it** already **stated with regard to** this ruling that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, says:** This is referring **to** a woman **who says: Let my hands be sanctified to their Maker?** She sanctifies her actual hands, **and** these **hands exist in the world;** therefore the vow takes effect.

The *gemara* then raises a challenge to the very identification of Rabbi Akiva's position with *davar shelo ba l'olam*. The labor has not yet been performed; how can a vow forbid something that does not yet exist? *Ha itmar alah amar Rav Huna breih de'Rav Yehoshua* — it was already stated regarding this: *b'omeres yikdeshu yadai l'oseihen* — the vow is formulated as "may my hands be consecrated for what they produce." The *neder* is not imposed on the as-yet-unproduced labor, but on the hands themselves — the hands as instruments for their eventual output. *Ve'yadayim itnhu b'olam* — and the hands are already in the world. There is therefore no *davar shelo ba l'olam*; the subject of the vow exists right now.

This prompts a further reflection: if one were to declare forbidden to oneself the fruit of a particular tree before those fruit have grown, would that be a *davar shelo ba l'olam*? The more apt formulation — and one that would avoid the problem — would be to direct the vow at the tree itself, in terms of its capacity to produce fruit, much as the woman directs the vow at her hands rather than at the as-yet-nonexistent produce. Such a vow would take hold of an existing entity. Whether a vow formulated over an entire class of objects, including those not yet in existence, raises similar questions, is left as an open matter.

## Father's Approval

### Service Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר לָאִשָּׁה ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֲדַבֵּר עָלַיִךְ לַשִּׁלְטוֹן״, ״וְאֶעֱשֶׂה עִמָּךְ כְּפוֹעֵל״, דִּבֵּר עָלֶיהָ לַשִּׁלְטוֹן וְעָשָׂה עִמָּהּ כְּפוֹעֵל – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me on the condition that I will speak** in **your** favor **to the authorities,** e.g., to help her address some legal matter, or: On the condition that **I will act for you as a laborer,** if **he spoke** in **her** favor **to the authorities or acted for her as a laborer, she is betrothed. But if not, she is not betrothed.**

The *gemara* now turns to a new mishna. *Ha'omer l'isha harei at mekudeshet li al menat she'adaber alayich lashilton* — if a man says to a woman, "You are hereby married to me on the condition that I speak to the governor on your behalf," or *ve'e'eseh imach k'poel* — "on the condition that I perform a day's labor for you." If he followed through — *diber aleha lashilton* or *asah imah k'poel* — she is betrothed; if not, she is not. The mishna appears straightforward. Resh Lakish comments: *v'hu she'natan lah shaveh pruta* — and this applies only where he also gave her the value of a *pruta*. In other words, Resh Lakish reads the mishna as follows: the act of *kiddushin* was performed in the normal way — he gave her a ring or coin — and the service functions only as a *tnai*, a condition, not as the *kessef kiddushin* itself.

### Fee for Service

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּתַן לָהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וּבְשָׂכָר לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁהִרְכַּבְתִּיךְ עַל הַחֲמוֹר שֶׁהוֹשַׁבְתִּיךְ בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Reish Lakish says: And this** *halakha* applies only **if he gave her** an item **worth one *peruta*** at the time of the betrothal. She is not betrothed via his recommendation to the authorities or via the work itself, as these are merely conditions appended to the betrothal. The Gemara asks: **And** is she **not** betrothed **by the** monetary **value** of his recommendation or action? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if one says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me **by** the monetary **value** you received **when I had you ride on a donkey,** or: By the monetary value you received **when I had you sit on a wagon [*karon*], or:** By the monetary value you received when I had you ride **in a boat, she is not betrothed,** as she has already done the action and therefore owes him this debt, and one cannot betroth a woman with a loan.

The *gemara* challenges this: *u'vischar lo* — are you saying one cannot betroth a woman with the fee for a service? The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *bischar she'hirkavtich al hachamor* — with the fee for the donkey ride I gave you, or the wagon ride, or the ferry ride — *eina mekudeshet*. That does not work because the service was already rendered; the woman owes him money, and betrothing with that debt is *mekadesh b'milveh* — betrothing with a loan — which is ineffective since the money is already hers. But: *bischar she'oshivich al hachamor* — with the fee for the donkey ride I *will* give you — *mekudeshet*. He performs the service, she receives value that was not yet owed, and that value constitutes the *kesef kiddushin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׂכַר שֶׁאַרְכִּיבִךְ עַל הַחֲמוֹר, שֶׁאוֹשִׁיבִךְ בְּקָרוֹן אוֹ בִּסְפִינָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכָא נָמֵי בְּדִיהַב לַהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְהָא בִּשְׂכַר קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** But if he says to her: **By** the monetary **value** you will receive **when I will have you ride on a donkey,** or: By the monetary value you will receive **when I will have you sit on a wagon, or:** By the monetary value you will receive when I will have you ride **in a boat, she is betrothed. And if you would say** that **here too,** it is referring **to** a case **where he gave her** an item **worth one *peruta*,** the *baraita* **says: By** the monetary **value,** which indicates that she is betrothed via this sum.

A second *braita* reinforces this: if a woman says to a man, *shevi imi b'tzavta ve'ekadesh lach* — sit with me and keep me company, and I will be betrothed to you for that — or *sechok lefanai*, perform a comedy routine; *rekod lefanai*, do a dance; *aseh kidmus hazeh*, construct some structure — *shamin*, one assesses the value. If the service is worth a *pruta*, she is betrothed; if not, she is not. This is striking also because the formulation here has the woman initiating the transaction — there is no statement of *harei at mekudeshet li* from the man; she herself declares *ekadesh lach*, and the betrothal follows from that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: שֵׁב עִמִּי בְּצַוְותָּא וְאֶקַּדֵּשׁ לָךְ, שְׂחוֹק לְפָנַי, רְקוֹד לְפָנַי, עֲשֵׂה כַּדִּימוֹס הַזֶּה – שָׁמִין, אִם יֵשׁ בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָכָא נָמֵי בְּדִיהַב לַהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְהָא שָׁמִין אוֹתוֹ קָאָמַר! תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is taught** in a *baraita* that if a woman says to a man: **Sit together with me and I will be betrothed to you,** or: **Perform entertainment before me,** or: **Dance before me,** or: **Perform** work for me **like this layer [*dimus*]** of a building, the court **appraises** the monetary value of his action. **If** the action he performs for her **is worth the value of one *peruta*, she is betrothed, but if not, she is not betrothed. And if you would say** that **here too,** it is referring to a case **where he gave her** an item **worth one *peruta*,** and the betrothal is with that; **but** the *baraita* **says:** The court **appraises it.** If this were merely a condition, there would be no need for an appraisal. Isn't this **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Reish Lakish?**

The *gemara* presses: perhaps in these *braitot* too, he had given her a ring and the service was merely a condition? The *gemara* dismisses this. In the first case, the language is explicitly *bischar* — with the fee. In the second, the *braita* says *shamin*, they assess the value — clearly the *kiddushin* is effected through the service itself and nothing else.

### Accrual vs. End

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לְךָ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַאי תַּנָּא בָּרָא סָבַר: אֵינָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת אֶלָּא לְבַסּוֹף. וְתַנָּא דִידַן סָבַר יֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **Reish Lakish** could have **said to you: This *tanna* of the *baraita* maintains** that the obligation to pay a person's **wage** is incurred **only at the end** of the period for which he was hired. Consequently, she is betrothed via money rather than by a loan. **And the *tanna* of our** mishna **maintains** that the obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end.** Since the debt accumulates throughout the duration that the service is performed, by the time he has finished the work she owes him the entire sum, and a debt cannot be used for a betrothal.

*Tiyuvta deResh Lakish* — this is a refutation of Resh Lakish's reading. But Resh Lakish deflects: the *tanna* of those *braitot* holds *einah lisechirut ela levasof* — the obligation for labor or service accrues only at the conclusion of the work. Under this view, when the service is complete, a discrete value exists that can be given as a gift to the woman and constitutes *kessef kiddushin*. But the *tanna* of our mishna holds *yeshnah lisechirut mitechilah ve'ad sof* — the obligation accrues incrementally from the very beginning of the service through its completion.

Under the view that payment accrues from beginning to end, every infinitesimal increment of service immediately generates a corresponding infinitesimal fraction of a *pruta* of obligation. No completed service of a *pruta*'s worth is ever given as a unified act; instead, the value is parceled out across the duration in fractions too small to constitute *kessef kiddushin* at any given moment. Each fraction, as it accrues, immediately becomes a debt — *milveh* — and cannot then function as *kessef kiddushin*. The cumulative amount never coheres into a single transferable gift of a *pruta* because by the time each increment exists, it has already become her property as a debt.

### Resh Lakish's Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי דּוּחְקֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְאוֹקוֹמַיהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין בְּיֶשְׁנָהּ לִשְׂכִירוּת מִתְּחִילָּה וְעַד סוֹף, וּבְדִיהַב לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what forces Reish Lakish to establish the mishna in** accordance with the opinion **that** the obligation to pay **a wage is** incurred continuously **from the beginning** of the period he was hired **to** its **end, and** that it is referring to a case **where he gave her** the value of one *peruta*? Why does he reject the possibility that the mishna is referring to one who did not give an extra *peruta*, and instead he betrothed her with the value of the service he performed, in accordance with the opinion that the obligation to pay a person's wage is incurred only at the end of the period for which he was hired?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין קְשִׁיתֵיהּ, מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״עַל מְנָת״? נִיתְנֵי ״בִּשְׂכַר״, אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: כֹּל ״עַל מְנָת״ הֵיכָא דִּיהַב לַהּ הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara replies: **Rava said** that **the mishna was difficult for** Reish Lakish: **Why does** the *tanna* **specifically teach: On the condition? Let him teach: By** the monetary **value,** as stated in the *baraita*. **Rather, conclude from** it that **any** statement of: **On the condition,** is referring to a stipulation, and therefore the mishna must be dealing with a case **where he has** already **given her** the value of one *peruta*. Consequently, the value of his service cannot be the money for the betrothal itself, and instead serves as a later condition.

This leads to the *gemara*'s further question: *u'mai dukhkheih d'Resh Lakish* — what compelled Resh Lakish to read the mishna this way at all? Why not simply read the mishna as a case where the service is the *kessef kiddushin*? Rava answers: *matnisin kashya lei* — Resh Lakish was bothered by the plain language of the mishna. The mishna says *al menat she'adaber alayich lashilton* — on the condition that I speak to the governor. If the service were the *kessef kiddushin*, the mishna should have said "with the fee for my speaking to the governor." The use of *al menat*, "on the condition," is the consistent formulation throughout the preceding mishnayot wherever a condition is added to an independent act of *kiddushin*. Resh Lakish simply reads the *pshat* of the mishna: the service is a condition; the *kessef kiddushin* was given separately.

## Father's Silence

### Silence Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּרְצֶה אַבָּא״, רָצָה הָאָב – מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – אֵינָהּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מֵת הָאָב – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת. מֵת הַבֵּן – מְלַמְּדִין הָאָב לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to one who betroths a woman and says to her that the betrothal is: **On the condition that** my **father will want** this betrothal, if his **father wants** it, **she is betrothed; but if not, she is not betrothed.** If **the father dies, she is betrothed,** despite the fact that he did not reveal his wishes. If **the son dies, one instructs the father to say that he does not want** the betrothal, so that the betrothal will never have taken effect, thereby enabling her to avoid the requirement of levirate marriage.

The mishna continues: *al menat sheyirtze abba* — on the condition that Father agrees. If the father agrees, she is betrothed; if not, she is not. If the father dies, she is betrothed. If the son (the groom) dies, one instructs the father to say he did not consent, so that the girl will not be left as a widow — for her situation is far preferable if the *kiddushin* never took effect than if she is now an *almanah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּרְצֶה אַבָּא״? אִילֵּימָא עַד דְּאָמַר אַבָּא ״אִין״, אֵימָא מְצִיעֲתָא: מֵת הָאָב – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְהָא לָא אֲמַר ״אִין״! אֶלָּא:
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the meaning of: **On the condition that** my **father will want** this betrothal? **If we say** it means they are not married **until** his **father says yes,** indicating his agreement, then **say the middle clause** of the mishna: If **the father dies, she is betrothed. But** how can she be betrothed? After all, **he did not say yes,** and the condition was not fulfilled. **Rather,**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוֹק אַבָּא. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: מֵת הַבֵּן – מְלַמְּדִין אֶת הָאָב לוֹמַר שֶׁאֵינוֹ רוֹצֶה. אַמַּאי? וְהָא שְׁתֵיק! אֶלָּא אֲמַר לַהּ ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא יְמַחֶה אַבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** the phrase: On the condition that my father wants this betrothal, means: **On the condition that** my **father will remain silent** and will not object when he hears of the betrothal. But if this is the case, **say the last clause** of the mishna: If **the son died, one instructs the father to say that he does not want** the betrothal. **Why** should this matter? After all, **he remained silent** when he first heard about it. Clearly his mere silence does not constitute agreement. **Rather,** one must say that the phrase: On the condition that my father wants this betrothal, means that **he said to her: On the condition that** my **father will not object** to this betrothal at any point in time.

The *gemara* asks what *sheyirtze abba* — "that Father agrees" — actually means. If it means that the father must verbally affirm his consent, then in the case where the father dies without speaking, why is she betrothed? He never said yes. This forces a reinterpretation: the condition must mean *sheyishtok abba* — that the father is silent, that he does not object. If he dies, he never objected, and the *kiddushin* takes effect.

But now the *gemara* faces a difficulty with the end of the mishna. If silence constitutes acceptance, then when the son dies and the father is momentarily silent before we instruct him, has he not already implicitly accepted? Why can we still instruct him to object? The *gemara* therefore recasts the condition again: *al menat shelo yimche abba* — not that he is silent for a moment, but that he never actively objects. Silence alone does not seal the matter; only active objection terminates it. Under this reading: if the father verbally accepts, the *kiddushin* is firmly established — though one might think that if the condition is only "as long as he doesn't object," he could theoretically object even after verbal acceptance. This is precisely what troubles the *gemara* about the *reisha*: how can verbal consent make the *kiddushin* definitively binding if the real condition is simply non-objection?

### Rav Yannai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא בְּחַד טַעְמָא וּמְצִיעֲתָא וְסֵיפָא בְּחַד טַעְמָא! אָמַר רַבִּי יַנַּאי: אִין. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ לִדְרַבִּי יַנַּאי דָּחֲקִינַן וּמוֹקְמִינַן מַתְנִיתִין בִּתְרֵי טַעֲמֵי וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּחַד תַּנָּא וְלָא מוֹקְמִינַן בִּתְרֵי תַּנָּאֵי וּבְחַד טַעְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Wouldn't this mean that **the first clause** of the mishna describes **one reason,** i.e., one set of circumstances, **and the middle clause and last clause** describe **one** other **reason? Rabbi Yannai said: Yes,** this is so. **Reish Lakish said: Conclude from** his statement that **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yannai one forces a mishna** to be explained as **following two reasons, and in accordance with** the opinion **of one *tanna*, rather than establishing** a mishna as following the opinions of **two *tanna'im* and one reason.** In other words, an interpretation that maintains the unified attribution of one mishna is preferable, even if one is thereby forced to say that it addresses different situations.

Rav Yannai resolves the apparent inconsistency between the beginning and end of the mishna by positing that the two clauses describe different formulations. In the *reisha*, where verbal agreement renders her betrothed, the groom used the language *sheyishtok abba* — on the condition that the father is silent. Under that formulation, a moment of silence following the father's learning of the marriage seals the *kiddushin*. In the *seifa*, where active objection is what terminates it, the groom used the language *shelo yimche abba* — on the condition that the father not object. Under that formulation, verbal acceptance does not definitively close the matter; the condition remains open until the father actively objects or the timeframe expires.

Resh Lakish draws a methodological inference from Rav Yannai's willingness to read the beginning and end of the mishna as different scenarios: *dachkinan umokminan matnitin bitrei ta'amei ve'aliba dechad tana* — we would rather strain the mishna to present two different circumstances under one *tanna*'s opinion than posit a hidden dispute between two *tannaim* expressed within a single anonymous mishna.

### Thirty Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר אַמֵּי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם חַד טַעְמָא הוּא, וּמַאי ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁיִּרְצֶה אַבָּא״ – עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא יְמַחֶה אַבָּא מִכָּאן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Ami said: Actually,** the entire mishna **is** addressing **one reason,** i.e., one case, **and what** is the meaning of: **On the condition that** my **father wants** the betrothal? It means: **On the condition that** my **father will not object from now and until thirty days** have elapsed. His silence for thirty days indicates his acceptance of the betrothal.

Rav Yosef bar Ami proposes a simpler resolution: *le'olam chada ta'ama hu* — it is actually a single scenario throughout. The groom's formulation was *al menat shelo yimche abba mikan ve'ad shloshim yom* — on the condition that the father does not object within the next thirty days. A time limit resolves every difficulty. In the *reisha*, when the father verbally affirms consent, the *kiddushin* is established because no further objection is possible — or at minimum, the condition has been resolved favorably. The middle case, where the father dies: even within the thirty-day window, his death ensures he will never object before it expires, so she is immediately betrothed. The final case, where the son dies: the thirty-day clock is still running, and one may still instruct the father to object within that window, thereby preventing the *kiddushin* from taking effect.

## Father's Ignorance

### Father Believed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״קִדַּשְׁתִּי אֶת בִּתִּי וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ לְמִי קִידַּשְׁתִּיהָ״, וּבָא אֶחָד וְאָמַר ״אֲנִי קִידַּשְׁתִּיהָ״ – נֶאֱמָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a man who said: **I betrothed my** minor **daughter** to someone **but I do not know to whom I betrothed her, and one** man **came** forward **and said: I betrothed her,** his claim is **deemed credible.**

The mishna proceeds: *kidashti et biti ve'eini yodei lemi kidashtiya* — I married off my daughter, and I do not know to whom I married her. The underlying premise of this mishna — left implicit but relied upon by the *gemara* — is that a father is believed to claim that he married off his daughter, even without producing witnesses. This is derived from the *pesukim* about the father who says *et biti natati la'ish hazeh* — I gave my daughter to this man. Despite the fact that *kiddushin* normally requires witnesses, the father's testimony is credited by virtue of this special *ne'emanut*.

If the father cannot identify the groom, the daughter is in a dire situation: she may not remarry since she might be an *eishes ish*. The mishna therefore says: if one man comes forward and says *ani kidashtiya* — I was the one who betrothed her — *ne'eman*, he is believed. This raises its own question: the Torah granted the father special *ne'emanut*; on what basis does a random claimant receive credibility?

### Single Claimant

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: – נֶאֱמָן לִיתֵּן גֵּט, וְאֵין נֶאֱמָן לִכְנוֹס. נֶאֱמָן לִיתֵּן גֵּט, אֵין אָדָם חוֹטֵא וְלֹא לוֹ. וְאֵין נֶאֱמָן לִכְנוֹס? אֵימָא יִצְרוֹ תּוֹקְפוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says:** When the mishna states that the claim of one who says that he betrothed this girl is deemed credible, it means that he is **deemed credible** to the extent that he **may give** her **a bill of divorce, but** he is **not deemed credible to marry** her. He is **deemed credible to give** her **a bill of divorce,** as there is a presumption that **a person sins only for his own** benefit. No one is suspected of sinning when he does not stand to gain from it. Since he is not allowed to marry her himself, we do not say that he lied without purpose. **But he is not deemed credible to marry** her, as we **say** that **his inclination** might have **overcome him,** leading him to falsely claim that he is the one who betrothed her.

Rav rules: *ne'eman liten get ve'eino ne'eman liknos* — the claimant is believed to give her a *get* and thereby free her to marry someone else, but he is not believed to marry her himself. The distinction rests on *ein adam chotei velo lo* — a person does not sin without personal benefit. If the man is lying, and we prohibit him from marrying her, he gains nothing. The only beneficiary of his false testimony would be some future husband, who might be committing adultery without knowing it. The claimant has no personal gain, and therefore we credit him. But *eino ne'eman liknos* — he cannot marry her himself — because *shema yitzro takpo*: if he has a vested personal interest, his *yetzer hara* may overpower him, and people do sometimes lie even about grave sins when they stand to benefit.

### Two Claimants

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אַף נֶאֱמָן לִכְנוֹס. וּמוֹדֶה רַב אַסִּי בְּאוֹמֶרֶת ״נִתְקַדַּשְׁתִּי וְאֵינִי יוֹדַעַת לְמִי נִתְקַדַּשְׁתִּי״ וּבָא אֶחָד וְאָמַר ״קִידַּשְׁתִּיהָ״ שֶׁאֵין נֶאֱמָן לִכְנוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi** disagrees with Rav's interpretation of the mishna and **says: He** is **deemed credible even to marry** her, as he would not dare to lie directly to the father of the girl he claims to have betrothed. **And Rav Asi concedes in** the case of a woman **who says: I became betrothed but I do not know to whom I became betrothed, and one** man **came and said: I betrothed her, that he is not deemed credible to marry her.** In this situation she might have agreed to collude with him, as the Gemara will explain.

Rav Ashi disagrees: *af ne'eman liknos* — he is even believed to marry her. Two considerations support this: first, the sin would be severe and falls on himself; second, he would be afraid of being exposed as a liar, since the father might look at him and say the groom was of a different appearance entirely, or there may be other corroborating details that would give him away.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: רָצוּ – אֶחָד נוֹתֵן גֵּט וְאֶחָד כּוֹנֵס, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב! אָמַר לְךָ רַב: שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּכֵיוָן דְּאִיכָּא אַחֵר בַּהֲדֵיהּ – אִירְתוֹתֵי מִירְתַת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna that if **they** so **desire, one** of them **gives** her **a bill of divorce and** the other **one may marry** her. This is apparently **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav,** who says that he may not marry her. The Gemara answers: **Rav** could have **said to you: There,** in our mishna, **it is different, as, since there is another** person **with him** also claiming to have betrothed her, **he is afraid** to lie.

The mishna's case of two claimants: *shneihem notnim get* — both must give a *get*, since they cannot both be the husband, and one of them is certainly lying; to allow either to remain with her risks adultery. *Ve'im ratzu echad noten get ve'echad kones* — alternatively, if they choose, one may give a *get* and the other may marry her, on the grounds of *mimah nafshach*: whichever one is the true husband, the woman is covered. If the one who stays with her is the true husband, all is well; if it was the other, she is at least divorced from him first. The *gemara* notes this appears to contradict Rav, who would not allow the claimant to marry. Rav replies: *shani hatam dekivan de'ika achrina bahadei mirtat* — the case of two claimants is different, because each man knows the other is present, and the risk of being exposed as a liar in front of the other claimant and the father is much greater. This heightened social pressure provides sufficient deterrent that we credit the one who steps forward to marry. Rashi and Tosafot note that we treat the one willing to give the *get* as more likely to be the liar — a quasi-*Shlomo HaMelech* resolution — though the logic remains somewhat difficult.

## Woman's Ignorance

### Woman's Claim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁאָמְרָה ״נִתְקַדַּשְׁתִּי וְאֵינִי יוֹדַעַת לְמִי נִתְקַדַּשְׁתִּי״ וּבָא אֶחָד וְאָמַר ״אֲנִי קִידַּשְׁתִּי״ – אֵין נֶאֱמָן לִכְנוֹס. מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהִיא מְחַפָּה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* cites another case: With regard to **a woman who said: I became betrothed but I do not know to whom I became betrothed, and one** man **came and said: I betrothed** her, he is **not deemed credible to marry** her. Why not? **Because she is protecting him.** Perhaps she decided only now that she wants to marry him and is therefore identifying him as the man who betrothed her, although she knows it was someone else. The same suspicions do not arise in the case of a father, who has no inclinations in this regard.

The *gemara* states that Rav Ashi himself concedes the following case: *hi she'amrah nitkadashti ve'eini yoda'at lemi nitkadashti* — if a woman herself says, "I was betrothed and I do not know to whom," and a man comes forward claiming *ani kidashtich* — I betrothed you — *eino ne'eman liknos*, he is not believed to marry her. This is a striking asymmetry: the father's claim generates a *ne'emanut* that can be extended to a male claimant; the woman's own claim does not generate an equivalent *ne'emanut* for a claimant.

### Woman's Interest

The reason given is: *mipnei shehi mechapa alav* — because she will cover for him. The logic is as follows: part of what renders the claimant credible in the father's case is the assumption that if he is lying, he risks being identified and exposed — the father will call him out. But when the woman herself is the one who said she was betrothed to an unknown person, and a man appears claiming to be that person, the woman has a powerful vested interest not to expose him. If she is not betrothed to this man, she remains bound to the unknown original *mekadesh*, cannot remarry, and faces a life as an *agunah*. The temptation to go along with the false claimant — or to genuinely convince herself that this must be the right person — is far stronger for the woman in her own predicament than for a father concerned for his daughter, however genuine that concern may be. Because the woman's *ne'emanut* cannot constrain the claimant in the same way the father's does, the claimant lacks the fear of exposure that otherwise supports his credibility, and we therefore do not credit him to marry her.
