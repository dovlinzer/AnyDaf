# Sotah 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Previous Perek Review

### Eliezer vs. Yehoshua

Before turning to the new *perek*, it is worth revisiting two points from the material just completed. The *Mishnah* presented a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding the circumstances under which a husband would divorce his wife and provide a *kesuvah*. Rabbi Eliezer held that even a rumor as tenuous as what one might overhear from a passing bird would suffice. Rabbi Yehoshua required something more substantial — people actually witnessed the two together in circumstances suggesting impropriety.

Rashi understood this dispute as relating to the earlier debate about what constitutes valid *stira* — the act of seclusion required to trigger the *sotah* process. That reading, however, sits awkwardly with the plain text of the *Mishnah*. Tosafos offers a considerably cleaner approach: the two Tannaim are actually debating something entirely different. In the cases they discuss, there is no *kinuy* and no *stira* at all — only rumors. The debate concerns what kind of rumors are sufficient to warrant advising the husband to divorce his wife. Since there is no proper halachic basis for the *sotah* procedure, the husband must in any event provide a *kesuvah*. Tosafos's reading is the more natural one.

### Witnesses & Timing

The second point concerns the case of two witnesses who contradict one another — one testifying that the woman became *temei'ah* and the other that she did not. The question is whether they are neutralized by one another only when they testify simultaneously, or also when one comes after the other.

Rashi explains "simultaneously" as meaning within *toch k'dei dibbur* — the brief interval considered legally contemporaneous with speech. Tosafos disagrees. According to Tosafos, once *beis din* has already heard and ruled on the first testimony, a subsequent contradicting witness carries no weight. But if both witnesses appear before *beis din* before any ruling is issued, they neutralize one another, regardless of whether one spoke a moment before the other.

### Elu Ne'emarim Intro

With those clarifications in place, we now open a new *perek* — *Elu Ne'emarim*. While it begins with a point about *sotah*, the *perek* is in fact broad and consequential, addressing the *halacha* of reciting texts: which texts must be recited specifically in *lashon hakodesh*, and which may be said in translation.

---

## Bechol Lashon

### Sotah & Ma'aser

The *Mishnah* opens: *elu ne'emarim bechol lashon* — the following may be recited in any language. The first two items are *Parshas Sotah* and *viduy ma'aser*.

*Parshas Sotah* refers to the verbal component of the *sotah* procedure — the priest's address to the woman, her response of *amen amen*, and so forth. This is striking: the text is written in Hebrew, and the document is scraped into the water in Hebrew, yet the recitation itself may be in any language.

*Viduy ma'aser* is the declaration made at the end of the third year, in which the person states that all accumulated *ma'aser* has been properly distributed and removed from his home. The *Torah* provides a specific text for this declaration. That both of these — cases where the *Torah* itself specifies a formal text — may nonetheless be recited in translation is a considerable *chiddush*.

### Shema & Tefillah

The *Mishnah* continues its list: *Krias Shema*, *tefillah* (meaning *Shemoneh Esrei*), and *Birkas HaMazon* may all be recited in any language. One might pause and ask: for *tefillah* and *Birkas HaMazon*, the *Torah* provides no fixed text — it simply requires that one thank and bless God. Why would I have assumed these must be in Hebrew at all? Perhaps the *Mishnah*'s point is that even where there is no prescribed text, one might have thought there exists an inherent sanctity to the Hebrew language that demands its use even in open-ended recitation. The *Mishnah* dispels this: in all these cases, any language is valid.

### Shvuas Ha'Eidus

The *Mishnah* also lists *shvuas ha'eidus* and *shvuas hapikadon* as permissible in any language. *Shvuas ha'eidus* arises when someone adjures witnesses to testify on his behalf, and they falsely deny having seen anything relevant — they are liable to bring a *korban* for this oath. *Shvuas hapikadon* arises when someone denies having received an object for safekeeping, swears falsely to that effect, and later admits the truth — similarly requiring a *korban*. Both may be administered and taken in any language.

### Tosafot: Shomeia

Tosafos makes an important observation about the phrase *elu ne'emarim bilshonam* — they are recited in *their* language. He explains: *u'peirush kol adam bilshono she'hu shomeia* — the meaning is that each person recites in a language he actually understands. The example he gives is telling: one cannot fulfill the obligation by reciting in a foreign language he does not comprehend. Interestingly, however, Tosafos concedes that Hebrew — even if not understood — would still be valid, which opens a broader question about *kavana* and the degree of comprehension required for each individual area of *halacha*.

Tosafos then raises a tension: the word *bechol lashon* in the *Mishnah* seems to imply any language whatsoever, without any requirement of understanding — *bein shomeia bein eino shomeia*. And yet, as the *Gemara* will show regarding *Shema*, understanding does seem to matter. Tosafos thus flags the question of whether one can recite a translated text in a language he does not understand and still fulfill his obligation.

### Tosafot: Other Items

Tosafos raises a further question: the *Mishnah* lists certain items that may be said in any language, but omits a great many others — *Hallel*, *Shir HaShirim*, *Kiddush* of Shabbos, *brachos* before food, *birkas hamitzvos*, and so forth. Why are these not listed?

One might suggest the *Mishnah* limits itself to *de-Oraisa* obligations, but Tosafos dismisses this: *tefillah* is most likely *de-Rabbanan*, *Krias Shema* is *de-Rabbanan* according to some views, while *Kiddush* is *de-Oraisa*. The categories do not align neatly enough to support such a distinction.

### Tosafot: No Shomeia

Tosafos resolves this by drawing a distinction between two types of items. The items the *Mishnah* lists — *Parshas Sotah*, *viduy ma'aser*, *Krias Shema* — may be said in any language, but only if the speaker understands what he is saying. The items Tosafos mentions as omitted — *brachos* before food, *birkas hamitzvos*, and the like — may be said in any language even without understanding. Because those items operate differently, they are not comparable to the *Mishnah*'s list and were therefore not included. This distinction is admittedly difficult to explain on principled grounds, but Tosafos acknowledges it clearly.

## Bilshon Hakodesh

### Hebrew Texts List

The *Mishnah* then turns to texts that must be recited specifically in *lashon hakodesh*: *Mikra Bikkurim* (the declaration of *aram oveid avi* when bringing first fruits), *chalitza* (including the woman's declaration of *kacha ye'aseh la'ish* and the accompanying shoe removal and spitting), *brachos* and *klalos* on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, *Birkas Kohanim*, the blessing of the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* when he reads from the *Sefer Torah*, *Parshas Hamelech* at *Hakhel*, and *Parshas Eglah Arufah* — the declaration of the elders: *yadeinu lo shafchu es hadam hazeh*.

One item that stands out as particularly surprising is the address of the *mashuach milchama* — the *kohen* anointed for war — when he speaks to the troops. He informs them: whoever has built a new house, planted a vineyard, or betrothed a wife may return home. This is not a ritual recitation in any conventional sense; it is a practical communication designed specifically to be understood. If there is any context in which we would expect translation to be permitted — indeed required — it would be this one. And yet this too must be in *lashon hakodesh*.

It should be noted: just because the recitation must be in Hebrew does not necessarily mean the *chalitza* itself is invalid if the text is not recited in Hebrew. That is a separate question treated elsewhere.

As for *brachos* and *klalos* on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*: this is not an ongoing *halachic* obligation but a historical event. The reason it is mentioned in the *Mishnah* is that it serves as the exegetical basis — the *gehezera shava* anchor — for the other items requiring *lashon hakodesh*, as the *Gemara* will now explain.

### Mikra Bikkurim

How do we know that *Mikra Bikkurim* must be in *lashon hakodesh*? The *Gemara* points to the juxtaposition of two phrases. By *bikkurim* the *Torah* says *ve'anita ve'amarta lifnei Hashem elokecha* — you shall respond and say before God. By the *brachos* and *klalos* on the mountains, it says *ve'anu haLevi'im ve'amru* — the Levites shall respond and say. The combination of the verbs "respond" and "say" appears in both contexts. Just as the *brachos* and *klalos* on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* were in *lashon hakodesh*, so too *Mikra Bikkurim* must be in *lashon hakodesh*.

### Chalitza

The same *gezera shava* applies to *chalitza*. By *chalitza*, the *Torah* writes *ve'aneta ve'amra* — she shall respond and say. By the Levites on the mountains, it says *ve'anu haLevi'im ve'amru*. The same dual-verb construction creates the link: just as the mountains required *lashon hakodesh*, so does *chalitza*.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees. He holds that by *chalitza* there is no need to derive the Hebrew requirement from the mountains via this *gezera shava*. The *Torah* itself says *ve'amra kacha* — she shall say "thus," meaning she must say it in precisely this manner — *ad she'tomar balashon hazeh*, until she says it in this very language. The word *kacha* itself implies the exact wording, which is inherently the Hebrew text as given.

## Har Gerizim & Eival

### Geography

Having established that the *brachos* and *klalos* on *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* serve as the foundation for the other *lashon hakodesh* requirements, the *Mishnah* now describes that ceremony in detail. It begins with the geography.

Once the Jewish people crossed the Jordan, they came to *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival*, which are in *Shomron*, alongside *Shechem*, near *elonei Moreh*. The *Torah* provides these geographic markers, and the *Gemara* explains how we know that *elonei Moreh* refers to *Shechem*: the *Torah* elsewhere says that Avram passed through the land *ad mekom Shechem ad elon Moreh*. Just as *elon Moreh* in that earlier verse refers to *Shechem*, so the *elon Moreh* mentioned in connection with *Har Gerizim* and *Har Eival* likewise refers to *Shechem*.

### Arrangement

Six tribes ascended *Har Gerizim* and six ascended *Har Eival* — those on *Har Gerizim* for the *bracha*, those on *Har Eival* for the *kelala*. It is worth noting that even today *Har Gerizim* is covered with vegetation while *Har Eival* is entirely barren — a striking natural correspondence to the roles each mountain was assigned.

The *kohanim*, *Levi'im*, and the *aron* stood below in the middle, between the two mountains. The *kohanim* encircled the *aron* as an inner protective ring; the *Levi'im* encircled the *kohanim*. All the remaining Israelites stood on both sides — six tribes on each mountain. This is supported by the verse in *Yehoshua*: *vechol Yisrael uzekanav veshotrav veshofetav omdim mizeh umizeh la'aron*.

### Brachos & Klalos

The *Mishnah* then describes the recitation. They turned toward *Har Gerizim* and opened with the *bracha*: *baruch haish asher lo yaaseh pesel umasecha* — blessed is the man who does not make a graven image. Both groups — those on *Har Gerizim* and those on *Har Eival* — responded *amen*. This is notable: despite the two mountains representing two opposing paths, the *amen* response came from all of Israel together. Then they turned toward *Har Eival* and recited the corresponding *klala*: *arur haish asher yaaseh pesel umasecha* — and again, both groups responded *amen*.

The sequence alternated — *bracha*, *klala*, *bracha*, *klala* — until all the *brachos* and *klalos* were completed. This interleaving is natural and deliberate: the two choices are set before the people together, blessing and curse side by side.

### 70 Languages

After completing the *brachos* and *klalos*, they brought the stones and built the *mizbeiach* — the *Mishnah* understands that the inscribed stones and the altar stones were one and the same — plastered them over with lime and wrote upon them *kol divrei haTorah beshivim lashon*, the entire *Torah* in 70 languages. The source for this is the *Torah*'s instruction to write these words *be'er heitev* — very clearly and well explicated.

What makes this deeply significant in context is the juxtaposition. The *brachos* and *klalos* — which must be in *lashon hakodesh* — serve as the basis for other things that must be in *lashon hakodesh*. And yet standing right alongside them, at this very same moment of national commitment, is the writing of the *Torah* in all 70 languages. This will become directly relevant when the *Gemara* later discusses the principle of *kol haTorah kulah bechol lashon ne'emra*.

After the ceremony, the stones were dismantled, and the people went and spent the night in their place — Rashi identifies this as *Gilgal*. There is some complexity here, as *Yehoshua* describes twelve stones taken from the feet of the *kohanim* at the crossing of the *Yarden*, which seem to be a distinct monument erected at *Gilgal* to commemorate the crossing. A degree of conflation between the two accounts exists, but the *Mishnah* does not address it directly.

## Gezera Shava

### Sotah Language

The *Gemara* now asks: how do we know that *Parshas Sotah* may be recited in any language? The source is the verse *ve'amar hakohen laisha* — the priest shall say to the woman. Rashi cites the parallel verse *ve'amar el haisha* — to the woman — which implies that the recitation is about reaching her, communicating to her in a way she takes to heart. The word *amar* is a generic verb of communication, as opposed to the double-verb construction (*ve'anu ve'amru*) that signals a specific formal text. The sense of *el ha'isha* is *devarim hanichnasim belibah* — words that enter her heart — meaning any language that she understands.

### Woman's Understanding

A *baraisa* makes this explicit: *mashmi'in osah bechol lashon shehi shoma'as* — she is informed in any language that she understands. This connects to Tosafos's earlier point about *shomeia*: in translation, the requirement of understanding the language is operative.

### Four Topics

The *baraisa* then specifies the four topics that must be communicated to her: *al ma hi shotah uvmah hi shotah, al mah nitmes uvmah hi nitmes* — why she is drinking and in what vessel, and for what she has become *tamei* and by what means.

These four topics are not uniform in character. *Al ma hi shotah* — why she is drinking — refers to the formal halachic criteria: there was *kinuy* and *stira*; this is why the procedure is taking place. *Uvmah hi shotah* — in what she is drinking — refers to the earthenware vessel used, a detail chosen for its demeaning quality, signaling the diminished state she has brought upon herself.

*Al mah nitmes* — for what she became *tamei* — is not halachic criteria but *mussar*: *al iskei schok veyaldus*, it comes from frivolity and immaturity, from conduct that opens the door to such situations. *Uvmah hi nitmes* — by what means — returns to halachic territory: *beshogeg o bemeizid, be'ones o beratzon* — was it inadvertent or deliberate, coerced or willing? This matters greatly because only the willful and uncoerced transgression is tested by the waters.

The reason all of this is spelled out to her — at least the last point — is *shelo lehotzi la'az al hamayim hamarim*: so she should not emerge and claim the waters have no power, having drunk them without effect. She must understand that certain circumstances — *shogeg*, *ones* — exempt from the trial, and her survival in those cases reflects nothing about the efficacy of the waters.

### Praise & Faults

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai teaches: a person should speak his own praises in a low voice, and his own faults loudly. How do we know praises should be in an undertone? From *viduy ma'aser*: the declaration is in essence a statement of everything one did correctly — *asisi kechol asher tzivisani, hashkifa mimon kodshecha* — yet the *Torah* does not say *ve'anu ve'amru*, the language of proclamation; it simply says *ve'amarta*, you shall say. Quietly, without fanfare.

And how do we know one's pain or difficult circumstances should be proclaimed? The *Gemara* says: from *Mikra Bikkurim*. There, *matchil bignus* — one begins with the difficult opening, *arami oveid avi*, evoking the suffering of our ancestors and the precarious origins of our people — and this is recited publicly and aloud.

### Viduy as Praise

The *Gemara* observes that the parallel drawn here is somewhat imprecise. *Viduy ma'aser* is straightforwardly self-praise, but *Mikra Bikkurim* is not exactly *gnus* in the sense of personal fault. It is more in the category of *tza'ara* — difficulty, pain, a less than triumphant chapter of one's story. The teaching therefore is refined: one should not merely hide sinful conduct; rather, one should feel free to share one's suffering openly, because doing so invites others to pray on one's behalf. This is the deeper point: *kol mi she'ira bo davar tzarich lehodi'a lerabbim, verabbim mevakshim alav rachamim* — whoever experiences hardship should make it known, so that others may seek mercy on his behalf.

This is illustrated by *vetamei tamei yikra*: the *metzora* sent outside the camp is required to announce his condition — *tzarich lehodi'a tza'aro lerabbim verabbim mevakshim alav rachamim*. The pastoral point is powerful: people who are suffering often conceal it, either out of pride or a reluctance to burden others. The *Torah* and *Chazal* take a different view — sharing one's pain opens the door for communal support and prayer.

### Tefillah Undertone

Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai, teaches: why was *tefillah* established as a silent, undertone recitation? We know the model from *Chana* — *vekolah lo yishama* — but why did *Chazal* adopt that model? *Kedei shelo levayesh es ovrei aveirah* — in order not to embarrass those who have sinned. When a person stands before God in *tefillah*, he should feel free to speak honestly about his failures and transgressions. If *tefillah* were recited aloud, people would be inhibited. The intimate, private character of silent *tefillah* allows for genuine, unguarded honesty.

This observation also carries with it an implicit point: *tefillah* is not merely the recitation of a fixed text. One is expected to add personal words within *Shemoneh Esrei* — to speak to God about one's own life, needs, and failures. The structure exists to enable that conversation, not to replace it.

### Chatas & Olah

The same principle — protecting the sinner from public embarrassment — is illustrated from the laws of *korbanos*. A *chatas* and an *olah* are both slaughtered in the northern part of the *azarah*. Because they share the same location, a bystander watching someone bring an animal cannot tell from the place of *shechita* whether that person has sinned and is bringing a *chatas* or has done nothing wrong and is bringing an *olah*. The *halacha* itself is structured to protect a sinner's dignity.

### Blood Distinction

The *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge: is the distinction truly concealed? The application of the blood differs — *dam chatas lemala*, the blood of a *chatas* is applied to the horns at the top of the *mizbeiach*, while *dam olah lemata*, the blood of an *olah* is cast on the lower walls. A careful observer could discern the difference.

The *Gemara* responds: *hasam kohen hu deyada* — only the *kohen* knows. By the time the blood is being applied, it is the *kohen* performing the service, and the layperson watching from the *azarah* may not readily follow the technical details of blood application. Tosafos, however, raises the obvious question: are we really to assume that observers are so inattentive? The *Gemara* accepts the answer but the difficulty is real.

### Gender Concealment

The *Gemara* raises a further challenge: a *chatas* must be a female animal and an *olah* must be male. If observers can see which sex of animal a person hands to the *kohen*, the distinction is transparent.

The *Gemara* answers: *chasuy mechasya be-alyah* — the fat tail of a sheep covers and conceals the genitals, so passersby cannot readily determine the animal's sex. The *Gemara* thus assumes that for sheep specifically, the distinction is not obvious. Tosafos again notes the somewhat strained assumption that people are carefully observing these details.

### Avodah Zarah

The *Gemara* then presses the point: what about a *chatas* brought specifically for *avodah zarah*? That *korban* may only be a female goat, and a goat has no concealing fat tail. The sex of the animal is visible, and therefore the nature of the sin is apparent to anyone watching. How does the principle of concealment apply here?

The *Gemara* responds: *hasam nichsof ve-nechsi ki heichi de-niskapar lei* — in the case of *avodah zarah*, a measure of additional embarrassment is not only appropriate but perhaps necessary. The severity of the sin of *avodah zarah* demands a more complete process of atonement and shame, and the public visibility of one's *korban* contributes to that process. For ordinary sins, the *Torah* protects the sinner's dignity; for *avodah zarah*, it does not — and this is entirely fitting.

## Shema Language

### Shema Any Language

The *Gemara* turns to *Krias Shema*. How do we know it may be recited in any language? From the word *shema* — *be-chol lashon she-ata shomea* — "hear" means: in any manner and language in which you can understand. The *Gemara* is treating the first word of *Krias Shema* as itself a legal instruction, indicating that understanding is central and therefore any language that conveys understanding is valid.

It should be noted that this *Gemara* treats *Krias Shema* as *de-Oraisa*. As Tosafos discusses at length, other *Gemoras* seem to suggest it may be *de-Rabbanan*, and no fully settled position emerges from the *stam* of the *Gemara*. But the present sugya proceeds on the assumption that at least the first *pasuk* or first paragraph carries biblical force.

### Rabbi vs. Rabbanan

A *baraisa* records the dispute directly. Rabbi says: *Krias Shema ki-ksava* — it must be read exactly as it is written, meaning in Hebrew, and cannot be recited in translation. The Sages say: it may be recited in any language.

### Rabbi's Ve'hayu

Rabbi's reason: the verse says *ve'hayu hadevarim ha-eileh al levavecha* — "and these words shall be upon your heart." The word *ve'hayu* — they shall be — implies that the words must remain exactly as they are, in the form in which they were given. This is analogous to the principle of *kacha* in *chalitza*: the formulation demands the precise original text.

### Rabbanan Shema

The Sages, by contrast, emphasize *shema* — hear, understand. The central obligation is comprehension, and comprehension is achievable in any language. As for *ve'hayu* — the Sages use it to derive a different *halacha* entirely: *shelo yikra lamafreia* — one may not recite *Krias Shema* out of order. The words, the sentences, the paragraphs must follow their proper sequence. *Ve'hayu* thus governs order rather than language.

Where does Rabbi derive the prohibition against reciting *Krias Shema* out of order, given that he uses *ve'hayu* for the language requirement? The *Gemara* answers: from the doubling of the expression — *ve'hayu hadevarim*, rather than simply *ve'hayu devarim*. The additional *heh* signals an additional requirement. The Sages do not accept this kind of inference from the extra *heh* and therefore have no need to overload *ve'hayu* with both requirements.

### Hearing Requirement

What does Rabbi do with the word *shema*? *Lehashmia oznecha mah she-ata motzi mi-picha* — you must let your ears hear what comes out of your mouth. This is the *halacha* of audibility: one must recite *Shema* at a volume that at minimum is audible to oneself.

The Sages, on the other hand, hold in accordance with the view that *hakore es Shema v'lo hishmia l'ozno yatza* — one fulfills the obligation even if one does not hear oneself. For the Sages, *shema* means language comprehension, not audibility.

The result is that Rabbi is strict on both dimensions: Hebrew is required, and audibility is required. The Sages are lenient on both: any language is valid, and audibility is not required. One might have expected the correlations to run differently — those who require the formal Hebrew text might care less about audibility (since the focus is on the text, not comprehension), while those who permit translation might specifically demand audibility (since comprehension is the goal). Tosafos notes and explores these possibilities, but the *Gemara* as it stands presents Rabbi and the Sages each internally consistent on their respective positions.

## Torah Bechol Lashon

### Deduction

The *Gemara* now raises a broader question flowing from the dispute between Rabbi and the Sages. Can we infer from Rabbi's position — which requires *Krias Shema* in Hebrew and derives this from *ve'hayu* — that Rabbi holds *kol haTorah bechol lashon ne'emra*, that the general rule is that Torah texts may be recited in any language? For if Rabbi's default were that everything must be in Hebrew, why would he need *ve'hayu* to establish that *Krias Shema* must be in Hebrew? That would simply follow from the general rule.

The *Gemara* deflects: the verse was still necessary because of the word *shema*, which could have implied that even Rabbi permits any language here. *Ve'hayu* is therefore needed to counter that inference and confirm that even in this case, the general requirement of Hebrew applies.

Similarly, one might infer from the Sages' position that they hold the default is Hebrew — otherwise, why would they need the word *shema* to permit any language? The *Gemara* deflects again: *shema* was still necessary because *ve'hayu* could have been read as an exception demanding Hebrew even for those who in principle permit any language. Both proofs are thus deflected, and neither Rabbi nor the Sages can be definitively assigned to one side of the broader principle.

### Default Rule

The principle of *kol haTorah kulah bechol lashon ne'emra* — Torah was given in all languages — is best understood as a question about the default rule. When the *Torah* requires a text to be recited, is the baseline assumption that any language will serve, or is the baseline Hebrew? The position of *bechol lashon ne'emra* holds that all languages are inherently valid; items requiring specifically *lashon hakodesh* are exceptions to that default. The opposing view holds that *lashon hakodesh* is the default, and explicit permission is needed to allow translation.

As the Tosafot Shantz explains, the practical implication is this: when the *Torah* provides a text for recitation and says nothing explicit about language, does the text remain inherently open to translation, or does it presumptively require Hebrew?

### 70 Languages Link

This entire discussion connects powerfully to what the *Mishnah* described earlier. At the very moment the Jewish people crossed the *Yarden* and committed themselves anew to the *Torah*, the first thing they did was write the entire *Torah* in 70 languages — *beshivim lashon*, with the instruction *be'er heitev*, to write it clearly and accessibly. This is an astonishing counterpoint to the *brachos* and *klalos* that must be in *lashon hakodesh* and that serve as the basis for other requirements of Hebrew. Standing alongside those requirements is the vision of *Torah* written in all the languages of the world.

This suggests that *kol haTorah kulah bechol lashon ne'emra* is not merely a legal default rule but reflects something fundamental: the *Torah* is inherently accessible to all peoples in all languages. Where the *Torah* demands *lashon hakodesh* specifically — as it does in the cases derived in this *perek* — that is an exception, requiring its own source and justification. Where no such demand is made, the *Torah*'s native posture is openness to every language.

It is worth noting that Rashi and Tosafos raise the question of whether this principle relates to whether a *Sefer Torah* itself may be written in another language — a separate and well-known debate. But that does not appear to be what the *Gemara* here is discussing. The present discussion concerns something more fundamental: whether Torah recitation, as a category, was always understood to allow for translation — a position grounded in the very act with which Bnei Yisrael began their life in the land.
