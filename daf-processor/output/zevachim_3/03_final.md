# Zevachim 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Stam Kodshim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לָרַב פָּפָּא: לָא הֲוֵית גַּבַּן בְּאוּרְתָּא בִּתְחוּמָא בֵּי חַרְמָךְ – דְּרָמֵי רָבָא מִילֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא אַהֲדָדֵי, וְשַׁנִּי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Pappa:** Since **you were not with us last night within the** Shabbat **limit of Bei Ḥarmakh,** you did not hear **that Rava raises a contradiction between** two **superior** mishnaic **statements and teaches their** resolution.

### Ravina & Rav Pappa

The daf opens with Ravina recounting to Rav Pappa a pair of contradictions and resolutions that Rava had presented the previous evening in the beit medrash. The first contradiction had pitted two domains against each other: the laws of *kodshim* and the laws of *gittin*. By *kodshim*, if a *kohen* performs *shechita* without any specific intent — neither for the right *korban* nor for the wrong one — the offering is valid. By *get*, however, a document written without specific intent for the particular woman is invalid, even if written in a generic fashion.

### Kodshim vs. Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּזְבְּחוּ שֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן כּוּ׳. טַעְמָא דְּשֶׁלֹּא לִשְׁמָן; הָא סְתָמָא – עָלוּ נָמֵי לַבְּעָלִים לְשֵׁם חוֹבָה; אַלְמָא סְתָמָא נָמֵי כְּלִשְׁמָן דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **All slaughtered offerings that were slaughtered not for their sake** are fit, but they did not satisfy the obligation of the owner. Rava infers: **The reason** they do not satisfy the obligation of the owner is specifically **that** they were slaughtered **not for their sake. But** if offerings were slaughtered **without specification** of intent, **they, as well, satisfied the obligation of the owner. Apparently,** if one performs any action **without specification** of intent, it is **also considered as** if he performed it expressly **for its sake.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: כׇּל הַגֵּט שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִשָּׁה – פָּסוּל. וּסְתָמָא נָמֵי פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **raises a contradiction** from another mishna (*Gittin* 24a): **Any bill of divorce that was written not for the sake of** the **woman** in question is **not valid. And** it is derived from the continuation of that mishna that if a bill of divorce was written **without specification** as to which woman it is referring, it is **also not valid.**

These two rulings appear flatly inconsistent. In both cases, a legally significant act is performed without directed intent. Why should *kodshim* be forgiving of generic performance while *gittin* is not?

### Kedusha Defines Act

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁנִּי: זְבָחִים – בִּסְתָם לִשְׁמָן עוֹמְדִין, אִשָּׁה – בִּסְתָמָא לָאו לְגֵירוּשִׁין עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **resolves** the contradiction: **Ordinary slaughtered offerings stand** designated **for their** own **sake.** From the time that the offering is consecrated, its presumed end is that it will be slaughtered for the type of offering for which is was consecrated. Therefore, even if the one slaughtering it has no particular intention, it is in effect considered slaughtered for its own sake. By contrast, **an ordinary wife does not stand** designated **for divorce.** Therefore, a bill of divorce is never presumed to be referring to a given woman unless it is specified explicitly.

The resolution, as Rava had explained, turns on a fundamental difference in the nature of the act. By *kodshim*, the animal arrives already endowed with *kedusha* — it has an identity. *Stam kodshim lishman hem omdim*: sanctified animals stand as already defined by their consecration. The act of *shechita* therefore does not require the officiant's intent to define it; the *kedusha* itself defines the act. One need only ensure the absence of a disqualifying thought, not the presence of a specifying one. By *get*, no such identity inheres in the raw materials. A piece of parchment and ink have no intrinsic relationship to any woman. Only the scribe's specific intent invests the document with its identity. Absent that intent, the *get* is unformed, and *stama* — generic performance — is disqualifying. This contrast — *stam kodshim lishman* versus the requirement of directed intent for a *get* — was the first major resolution of the previous session.

## Minah vs. Lo Minah

### Chatat: Olah vs. Chulin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תּוּ רָמֵי מִילְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי – מִי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה – פְּסוּלָה, שְׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חוּלִּין – כְּשֵׁירָה; אַלְמָא דְּמִינַהּ מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ, דְּלָאו מִינַּהּ לָא מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** § Ravina continued to inform Rav Pappa of Rava's statements: Rava **further raises another contradiction: Did Rav Yehuda say** that **Rav says** that **a sin offering that one slaughtered for the sake of a burnt offering** is **unfit,** whereas if one **slaughtered** the animal **for the sake of** consumption of **non-sacred** meat, it is **fit? Apparently,** only improper intent that is **of its type,** i.e., for the sake of another offering, **ruins** the offering and renders **it** unfit, whereas wrong intent that is **not of its type,** i.e., for the sake of consumption of non-sacred meat, is disregarded and **does not ruin it.**

With that recap established, the *gemara* turns to a second contradiction raised by Rava. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: a *chatat* that was slaughtered *leshem olah* — with intent that it be an *olah* — is invalid; but a *chatat* slaughtered *leshem chulin* — with intent that it be a non-consecrated animal — is valid. At first glance this is counterintuitive: an *olah* is at least a sacred offering, while *chulin* is entirely outside the domain of *korbanot*. One might have expected the more radical departure to be more disqualifying, not less.

### Principle Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהוּ: כׇּל הַגֵּט שֶׁנִּכְתַּב שֶׁלֹּא לְשֵׁם אִשָּׁה – פָּסוּל. וַאֲפִילּוּ לְשֵׁם גּוֹיָה נָמֵי פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **raises a contradiction** from the aforementioned mishna (*Gittin* 24a): **Any bill of divorce that was written not for the sake of** the **woman** who is being divorced with it **is not valid; and** by inference, **even** if it was written **for the sake of a gentile woman,** it is **not valid.** With regard to the matter of divorce, a gentile woman is not of the same type as a Jewish woman, as the *halakhot* of bills of divorce are irrelevant to her.

The *gemara* draws the principle explicitly: *d'mina machriv bah, d'lo mina lo machriv bah* — something of its own kind can destroy it; something of a completely different kind cannot. The threat is always greatest from that which is closest. A thought about *chulin* is so far outside the world of *kodshim* that it has no traction, no purchase on the act. It is as irrelevant as a ruling that contradicts an explicit verse — it simply does not register. A thought about an *olah*, however, remains within the sacred domain and therefore has the power to displace the *chatat*'s identity.

### Get Challenge

This principle is immediately challenged from the laws of *get*. The rule there is that any *get* written *shelo lishmo* — not for the sake of this particular woman — is invalid. And the *gemara* presses: presumably, this applies even if the *get* was written for a non-Jewish woman, who has no place in the halachic world of *gittin* whatsoever. A non-Jewish woman would seem as irrelevant to *gittin* as *chulin* is to *kodshim*. So why does thinking of a non-Jewish woman still invalidate the *get*?

### Stama Pasul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁנִּי: גֵּט – דַּל גּוֹיָה מִינֵּיהּ הָוֵה לֵיהּ סְתָמָא, וּסְתָמָא פָּסוּל. קָדָשִׁים – דַּל חוּלִּין מִינַּיְיהוּ הָוֵה לֵיהּ סְתָמָא, וּסְתָמָא כְּשֵׁירִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **resolves** the contradiction: If **a bill of divorce** is written for the sake of a gentile woman it is not valid, because if you **remove** the intent for the sake of **a gentile woman from it, it is** considered to be **without specification** of the woman who is divorced with it, **and** a bill of divorce written **without specification is not valid.** But if **sacrificial** animals are slaughtered for the sake of consumption of non-sacred meat, they remain fit, since if you **remove** the **non-sacred** intent **from them, they are** considered to be **without specification, and** offerings slaughtered **without specification are fit.**

The answer is a near-softball given what was established the day before. By *get*, thinking of a non-Jewish woman does not disqualify because of its content — it is indeed irrelevant. But its irrelevance means only that it is not a *positive* disqualifying thought. The problem remains that one lacked the *required* specific intent for the right woman. *Stama pasul* — generic performance is disqualifying for a *get*. The absence of a wrong thought does not help if the right thought was never present. By *kodshim*, as established, *stama keshera* — generic performance is acceptable, because the *kedusha* already defines the act. The two domains thus remain fully consistent with the principle: the structure of each domain determines what counts as disqualifying, and within *kodshim*, only something *minah* — of the same sacred genus — can destroy.

## Kli Cheres & Tocho

### Shratzim & Tumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״תּוֹכוֹ״ – וְלֹא תּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּלִי שֶׁטֶף מַצִּיל!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: The Torah states with regard to ritually impure carcasses of creeping animals: "And every earthen vessel into **whose interior** any of them falls, anything that is in its interior shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:33). From the word "interior" it is derived that only food in its interior is rendered impure, **but not** any food that is in **the interior of its interior,** i.e., in another vessel within the earthenware vessel. **And** this is the *halakha* not only if the inner vessel is an earthenware vessel; **even** if it is a metal or wooden **vessel,** which is purified though **rinsing** in a ritual bath and is therefore susceptible to impurity from its exterior, it nevertheless **protects** food that is inside it from being rendered impure. Evidently, even a vessel that is not of the same type serves to nullify the status of the interior of an earthenware vessel.

The *gemara* now introduces a new contradiction bearing on the same principle, drawing from the laws of *tum'ah*. The Torah states, concerning a *kli cheres* — a pottery vessel — that if a *sheretz* falls into its interior (*el tocho*), everything within it (*kol asher be-tocho*) becomes *tamei*. The critical feature of a *kli cheres* is that it conveys *tum'ah mei-aviro* — through its airspace — rather than through direct contact. The *sheretz* defiles the vessel's interior atmosphere, and that atmosphere in turn defiles whatever is suspended within it, even without physical touching.

### Tocho Concept

The *halacha* thus operates on two levels: the *sheretz* falls *el tocho*, into the airspace of the *kli cheres*, rendering the vessel *tamei*; and then *kol asher be-tocho yitma* — anything within that airspace becomes *tamei* as well, even without direct contact with the *sheretz*.

### Vessel Inside Vessel

The *gemara* cites a *beraita*: *tocho ve-lo toch tocho* — the airspace of the vessel, but not the airspace within the airspace. Moreover, *afilu kli shetef matzil* — even a non-pottery vessel placed inside the *kli cheres* saves its contents. To picture this: a drinking glass, open at the top, is placed inside a *kli cheres*, with the rim of the glass extending above the rim of the *kli cheres*. Food inside the glass is considered to be *within the glass*, not within the *kli cheres*, and is therefore not *tamei*. The glass need not be sealed; as long as its opening is above the rim of the outer *kli cheres*, the food is considered in its own separate airspace.

This raises the precise question the *gemara* has been building toward. A glass vessel is *lav mina* — not of the same genus as a *kli cheres*. Based on the principle that only something *minah* has a meaningful impact, one might argue that from the perspective of the *kli cheres*, the glass is irrelevant; the food should still be considered as residing within the *kli cheres*'s airspace, and *tamei*. Yet the *halacha* is that even a glass vessel saves. How does this square with *d'lo mina lo machriv bah*?

### Vessel vs. Wall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁנִּי: חוּלִּין אֵצֶל קָדָשִׁים – כִּמְחִיצָה אֵצֶל תַּנּוּר; מָה מְחִיצָה אֵצֶל תַּנּוּר לָא מַהְנְיָא לַהּ כְּלָל, אַף חוּלִּין אֵצֶל קֳדָשִׁים לָא מַהְנְיָא לֵיהּ כְּלָל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rava **resolves** the contradiction as follows: A vessel purified through rinsing is actually considered of the same type as an earthenware vessel, since they are both vessels. Concerning the *halakhot* of impurity, the equivalent of intent with regard to the consumption of non-sacred meat is an item that is not a vessel at all; the Sages **rendered non-sacred** meat **with regard to sacrificial** animals **like a partition with regard to** an earthenware **oven: Just as a partition in an oven is not effective at all** in preventing the transmission of impurity from one side to the other since it is not a vessel, **so too,** slaughtering an offering for the consumption of **non-sacred** meat **is not effective at all with regard to** rendering **sacrificial** animals unfit.

The *gemara* resolves this with a pointed analogy: *asu chulin etzel kodshim k'mechitzah etzel tannur* — *chulin* vis-à-vis *kodshim* is comparable to a partition (*mechitzah*) within an oven. A glass vessel is not truly *lo mina*. Whatever its material, it remains a vessel, a *kli*, and that is precisely the relevant category. The question of whether food is inside the *kli cheres* or in a separate airspace is a question defined by the world of *keilim* — vessels. A glass is still a *kli*. That is sufficient to create a distinct relational space: the food is in vessel B, not in vessel A.

The truly *lo mina* case is a wall dividing the oven. A wall is not a vessel at all. It is entirely irrelevant to the world of *keilim*. If one drives a plank through a *kli cheres* and places the *sheretz* on one side, the food on the other side is still *tamei* — the wall accomplishes nothing. Walls define separate spaces in the domain of *ohalim*, of buildings, but not in the domain of vessels. The *machloket* between type-*olah* and type-*chatat* maps onto the distinction between vessel-and-vessel; *chulin* vis-à-vis *kodshim* maps onto wall-within-vessel. The principle is intact.

## Tannur & Mechitzah

### Tannur Division

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: תַּנּוּר שֶׁחֲצָצוֹ בִּנְסָרִים אוֹ בִּירִיעוֹת, וְנִמְצָא שֶׁרֶץ בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – הַכֹּל טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Kelim* 8:1): In the case of **an oven that** one **divided with boards or with curtains, and** the carcass of **a creeping animal was found in one place, all** of the food in the oven, including that on the other side of the partition, is rendered **impure.**

The *gemara* makes this concrete by citing a *mishna*: *tannur shechatzatzo b'nesarim* — an oven that was divided by planks, or by *biriyot* (curtains). If a *sheretz* is found in one section (*nimtza sheretz b'makom echad*), the ruling is *hakol tamei* — everything in the oven is *tamei*. A wall or partition does not create separate airspaces within a *kli*; only a separate *kli* does.

### Keiveret Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כַּוֶּורֶת שֶׁהִיא פְּחוּתָה וּפְקוּקָה בְּקַשׁ, וּמְשׁוּלְשֶׁלֶת לַאֲוִיר הַתַּנּוּר – שֶׁרֶץ בְּתוֹכָהּ, הַתַּנּוּר טָמֵא; שֶׁרֶץ בַּתַּנּוּר, אֳוכָלִין שֶׁבְּתוֹכָהּ טְמֵאִין. וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In the case of **a round barrel that is broken and plugged up with straw and lowered into the airspace of the oven,** if the carcass of **a creeping animal** is **inside** the barrel, **the oven** is rendered **impure.** And if the carcass of **a creeping animal** is **in the oven,** the **food that is inside** the barrel is rendered **impure.** The broken barrel is not considered a vessel despite its being plugged up with straw, and therefore it does not prevent the transmission of impurity between the creeping animal and the oven. **And Rabbi Eliezer deems** the food in the barrel **pure.**

The *mishna* then introduces the *keiveret* case. A *keiveret* is a large cylindrical vessel — its name suggests a beehive shape — that has been *pechutah u'pekukah b'kash*: it had a hole large enough to deprive it of vessel status, and that hole was stuffed with straw, a non-permanent repair. The result is that the *keiveret*, despite its vessel-like shape and enclosed interior, is not halachically a *kli*. It functions, for these purposes, as a *mechitzah* — a partition — not as a vessel. When this *keiveret* is suspended (*meshulshelet*) in the airspace of the oven, the question is whether it creates a protected inner zone. The ruling: if the *sheretz* is inside the *keiveret*, the oven is *tamei*; if the *sheretz* is in the oven and the food is inside the *keiveret*, the food is *tamei*. Since the *keiveret* is halachically not a vessel, it operates as a wall — and walls, as established, are *lo mina* in the world of *keilim* and have no separating power.

### R. Eliezer's Kal Vchomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: קַל וָחוֹמֶר! אִם הִצִּיל בְּמֵת – הֶחָמוּר, לֹא תַּצִּיל בִּכְלִי חֶרֶס – הַקַּל?!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Eliezer said:** My opinion can be inferred ***a fortiori*: If** a barrel or any other partition between some item and a corpse under the same roof **protects** the item **from** becoming impure, even though impurity imparted by **a corpse** is **severe** in that it lasts seven days, **shouldn't** a partition **protect** food **in** the airspace of **an earthenware vessel** from impurity imparted by the carcass of a creeping animal, **which is mild** by comparison?

Rabbi Eliezer, however, disagrees and rules the food *tahor*. He advances a *kal v'chomer*: if a wall saves from *tumat hameis* — the most severe form of *tum'ah* — should it not certainly save within the more lenient domain of a *kli cheres*? The argument is compelling on its face. In the context of *ohel*, of houses and buildings, a wall is so powerful that it stops even *tumat meis* from spreading to the adjacent space. If walls can do that, why can they not divide an oven?

### Rabbis Respond

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא; אִם הִצִּיל בְּמֵת חָמוּר – שֶׁכֵּן חֲלוּקָה בְּאוֹהָלִין, תַּצִּיל בִּכְלִי חֶרֶשׂ הַקַּל – שֶׁאֵין חֲלוּקִין בְּאוֹהָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: The Rabbis **said to him:** Your inference is **not** correct. **If** a partition **protects** an item **from** impurity imparted by **a corpse,** which is **severe,** this is only **because** such impurity is unique in that it is imparted to that which is in the same tent, i.e., under the same roof, and **tents are divided** by partitions. If so, **should** a partition **protect** food from impurity imparted **in an earthenware vessel, which,** although **mild, is not divided by** partitions, like **tents** are?

The *Rabbanan* reject the *kal v'chomer*. By *ohel* — houses — walls are the natural mechanism for defining separate spaces. It is entirely appropriate that a wall divides a house into distinct zones, and that is why it blocks *tumat meis*. But a *kli cheres* is a vessel, and the natural mechanism for defining a distinct interior space within the domain of vessels is another vessel, not a wall. *She'ein chalukin b'ohalim* — vessels are not divided by walls the way buildings are. Walls are *lo mina* vis-à-vis vessels; they are structurally irrelevant to the category and therefore have no force there.

### Both Positions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתִינַח לְרַבָּנַן; לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר קַל וָחוֹמֶר קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The comparison between non-sacred meat vis-à-vis offerings and a partition in an earthenware vessel **works out well according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who hold that a partition does not prevent transmission of impurity inside an earthenware vessel. But **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who maintains that a partition is effective in an earthenware oven, **what can be said?** How is this compatible with the principle that an item is not affected by something not of its type? The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Eliezer** agrees with this principle. Yet he **says** that a partition in an earthenware vessel is effective due to his ***a fortiori*** inference, which overrides the principle that an item is not affected by something not of its type.

The *gemara* clarifies both positions in a precise formulation. The *Rabbanan* hold to the principle *d'lo mina lo machriv bah* — something of a different category simply has no impact — and apply it consistently: walls are not the mechanism of division for vessels, so they accomplish nothing. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, accepts the general principle in theory but is willing to deploy a *kal v'chomer* to override it. He agrees that walls are not the natural dividing mechanism for vessels, but argues that their demonstrated power against *tumat meis* is sufficient to import them into this domain by logical inference.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הָתָם נָמֵי לֵימָא קַל וָחוֹמֶר: קֳדָשִׁים מְחַלְּלִין קֳדָשִׁים – חוּלִּין לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If that is so,** that an *a fortiori* inference overrides this principle, **let us say an *a fortiori*** inference **there as well,** with regard to a sin offering that was slaughtered for the consumption of non-sacred meat: If slaughtering sacrificial animals for the sake of other **sacrificial** animals **desecrates** those **sacrificial** animals, **all the more so** is it **not** clear that slaughtering them for the consumption of **non-sacred** meat desecrates them?

That imports a question. If Rabbi Eliezer uses a *kal v'chomer* to override *lo mina* in the case of walls within a *kli cheres*, why not deploy the same logic by *kodshim* and say that a thought about *chulin*, though irrelevant in principle, should still invalidate the *korban* via *kal v'chomer* — if *kodshim* can desecrate *kodshim*, surely *chulin* can? 

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַב – כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב? ״וְלֹא יְחַלְּלוּ אֶת קׇדְשֵׁי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל אֵת אֲשֶׁר יָרִימוּ לַה׳״ – קֳדָשִׁים מְחַלְּלִין קֳדָשִׁים, וְאֵין חוּלִּין מְחַלְּלִין קֳדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rather, the reasoning** behind the statement **of Rav** that a sin offering slaughtered for the consumption of non-sacred meat is fit is not in accordance with this principle at all, but it is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar. As Rabbi Elazar says: What is the reasoning** behind the statement **of Rav?** The verse: **"And they shall not desecrate the sacred items of the children of Israel, which they set apart unto the Lord"** (Leviticus 22:15), teaches that only improper intentions for the sake of **sacred items,** i.e., offerings, **desecrate sacred items, but non-sacred** intent **does not desecrate sacred items.**

The *gemara* responds: *ata kra afkei mikal v'chomer* — a verse comes and removes us from the *kal v'chomer*. The verse in *terumah*, *v'lo yechalelu et kodshei b'nei Yisrael*, is read not only as a prohibition but as a structural statement: *chulin* simply does not have the power to *mechallel kodshim*. Only *kodshim* can desecrate *kodshim*. The verse blocks the *kal v'chomer* in that domain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָא אֲתָא קְרָא אַפְּקֵיהּ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר; הָכָא נָמֵי – לֵיתֵי ״תּוֹכוֹ״ לַפְּקֵיהּ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Apparently,** an inference from **a verse** can **come to rule out** a conflicting ***a fortiori*** inference. If so, **here too,** with regard to a partition in an oven, **let** the verse "and every earthen vessel into **whose interior** any of them falls" **come to rule out** Rabbi Eliezer's ***a fortiori*** inference that a partition prevents food in an oven from becoming impure.

By *kli cheres*, one might similarly expect a verse to block the *kal v'chomer*. And indeed, the word *tocho* appears to be available. *Tocho* — the interior — could be read as limiting the *mechitzah*'s efficacy: only what is genuinely in a separate inner vessel is saved, not what is merely behind a wall. However, the *gemara* explains that this extra deployment of *tocho* is already occupied by a different *halacha* entirely: *ochlim shegibevam b'tit*. That case, and the *derashah* from *tocho*, is addressed in the next section.

## Food Wrapped

### Plaster Wrap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״תּוֹכוֹ״ – מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לָאֳוכָלִין שֶׁגִּיבְּלָן בְּטִיט, וְהִכְנִיסָן לַאֲוִיר תַּנּוּר. סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּבִנְגִיעָה לֹא מְטַמֵּא, בַּאֲוִירוֹ נָמֵי לָא מְטַמּוּ [קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן].
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **This** expression: **"Whose interior,"** does not indicate that a partition is ineffective, as it **is necessary to** teach another *halakha*, namely, that **food that one kneaded with clay,** covering it from all sides, **and put in the airspace of an oven** that had the carcass of a creeping animal in it is impure. Because it might **enter your mind to say** that **since** the food **cannot become impure by touching** an impure item, as the clay serves as an interposition, **it also cannot become impure** by being put **in the airspace of** an impure oven. The phrase "in whose interior any of them falls" **teaches us** that the food does contract impurity.

The word *tocho* that the *gemara* turns to in this context addresses the following scenario: food that has been encased in plaster — *ochlim shegibevam b'tit* — and brought into the airspace of a *kli cheres*. The food is entirely within the vessel's airspace, but it is wrapped and sealed. There is no separate vessel; it is simply food encased in a non-vessel covering.

### Touch vs. Air

One might have reasoned as follows: since a *kli cheres* does not transmit *tum'ah* by direct contact to the outer surface of the plaster — the plaster is not itself susceptible — perhaps the airspace mechanism also cannot reach through to the food within. *Ho'il u'venegi'ah lo metamei*, since touch does not reach the wrapped food, *be-avira nami lo metamu*, perhaps the airspace also does not defile it.

### Tocho Teaches

The *gemara* answers that the extra word *tocho* comes precisely to teach that even this food remains *tocho* — within the interior of the *kli cheres* — and is therefore *tamei*. The wrapping changes nothing. The *kli cheres* operates through airspace, not through contact, and wrapping the food in plaster does not remove it from the vessel's interior domain. It is emphatically still *tocho*. This is the purpose for which the extra *tocho* is expended — to include wrapped food — and it therefore cannot simultaneously serve to exclude *mechitzot* from saving, leaving Rabbi Eliezer's *kal v'chomer* without a scriptural counter.

## Shinui Kodesh

### Rav's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר אַמֵּי רָמֵי שִׁינּוּי קוֹדֶשׁ אַשִּׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים, וּמְשַׁנֵּי. מִי אָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ לְשֵׁם חַטַּאת – כְּשֵׁירָה, לְשֵׁם עוֹלָה – פְּסוּלָה; אַלְמָא דְּלָאו מִינַּהּ מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ, דְּמִינַּהּ לָא מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Yosef bar Ami raises a contradiction** between Rav's statement with regard to **deviation** with regard to the type **of offering,** i.e., slaughtering for the sake of a different type of offering, **and** Rav's statement with regard to **deviation** with regard to the **owner,** i.e., slaughtering for the sake of someone other than the offering's owner, **and** he then **resolves** the contradiction. The contradiction is as follows: **Did Rav say** that **a sin offering that one slaughtered for the sake of** another **sin offering,** i.e., one that the owner is obligated to bring for a different transgression, is **fit,** but that if one slaughtered it **for the sake of a burnt offering** it is **unfit? Apparently,** slaughtering an offering with an improper intention **not of its type ruins it;** whereas intent that is **of its type does not ruin it.**

Rav Yosef bar Ami now presents a further contradiction. Rav has taught: a *chatat* slaughtered *leshem chatat* — even for a different kind of *chatat* than the one the owner is obligated in — is *kesherah*; slaughtered *leshem olah*, it is *pesulah*.

### Contradiction

This appears to invert the principle that has been guiding the analysis. The cases can be specified precisely. David is obligated in a *chatat* for eating *chelev*. The *kohen* slaughters it with intent for a *chatat* for eating *dam* — a different sin of the same type. That, says Rav, is valid. But if the *kohen* slaughters it with intent for an *olah*, it is invalid. Now, on the principle *d'mina machriv bah*, this is puzzling. A *chatat* for *dam* is closer to a *chatat* for *chelev* than an *olah* is. Closer proximity should mean greater destructive potential. Yet the closer change — *chatat* to a different kind of *chatat* — produces a valid offering, while the more radical change — *chatat* to *olah* — produces an invalid one.

## Shinui Be'alim

### Owner Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב חַטָּאת – פְּסוּלָה, עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב עוֹלָה – כְּשֵׁרָה; אַלְמָא דְּבַר מִינַּהּ מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ, דְּלָאו מִינַּהּ לָא מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **But doesn't Rav say** that **a sin offering that one slaughtered for** a person who is not its owner but **who** nevertheless **is obligated** to bring **a sin offering** is **unfit,** whereas if one slaughtered it **for** a person **who is obligated** to bring **a burnt offering,** it is **fit? Apparently,** an improper intention that is **of its type ruins** the offering, whereas intent **not of its type does not ruin it.**

Rav has taught a parallel rule for *shinui be'alim*: a *chatat* slaughtered *al mi shemechuyav chatat* — for someone else who is also obligated in a *chatat* — is *pesulah*; slaughtered *al mi shemechuyav olah* — for someone obligated only in an *olah* — is *kesherah*.

### Gloria vs. Dov

Two figures are introduced to clarify the cases. David has a *chatat* obligation. Gloria has no *chatat* obligation; she is donating a voluntary *olah*. Dov has a *chatat* obligation of his own. If the *kohen* performs *shechita* on David's *chatat* while thinking of Gloria — who is not in the world of *chatat* at all — it is valid. If he thinks of Dov — who is also obligated in a *chatat* — it is invalid.

### Relevance Levels

On first impression, this again seems to contradict the principle. Thinking of Gloria, who is completely outside the world of *chatat*, should be more threatening than thinking of Dov, who at least shares the same type of obligation. Yet it is the thought of Dov — the closer, more *minah* thought — that disqualifies, while the thought of Gloria, the *lo minah* thought, is harmless. This is entirely consistent with the principle. Thinking of Gloria is so irrelevant — she is not in the *parsha* of *chatat* at all — that the thought has no purchase on the act. It is like thinking about picking up one's children. It registers as nothing and the *shechita* proceeds as it should. Thinking of Dov, however, is a meaningful substitution: Dov is in the same category, is *mechuyav* in the same type of *kaparah*, and the intent to serve his obligation is a real displacement of the intent required for David.

## Shinui Kodesh…

### Precision Level

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי: הָתָם ״וְשָׁחַט אֹתָהּ לְחַטָּאת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וַהֲרֵי חַטָּאת לְשֵׁם חַטָּאת נִשְׁחֲטָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he **resolves** the contradiction as follows: **There,** with regard to deviation from the type of offering, **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"And slaughter it for a sin offering"** (Leviticus 4:33). **And** here **a sin offering was slaughtered for the sake of a sin offering,** and therefore although it was for the sake of a different sin offering, it remains fit.

The apparent contradiction in *shinui kodesh* — why a *chatat* slaughtered for a different *chatat* is valid while one slaughtered *leshem olah* is invalid — is resolved by attention to what the Torah actually demands. The verse states *v'shachat osah l'chatat*: one must slaughter it *as a chatat*. The Torah specifies intent for a *chatat* generically — not for a particular type of *chatat*, not for the specific sin that generated the obligation. When the *kohen* slaughters David's *chatat* while thinking of a *chatat* for *dam* rather than *chelev*, he has in fact satisfied the Torah's demand. He intended a *chatat*. The specific variety is below the threshold of precision that the Torah requires.

This is not a case where proximity renders something less dangerous despite the principle. Rather, the Torah does not demand that degree of exactitude in the first place. The thought about a different kind of *chatat* is not a disqualifying thought at all — it satisfies the requirement. Only the shift to *olah* is a genuine violation of the requirement to intend a *chatat*.

## Shinui Be'alim…

### Vekipper Alav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא ״וְכִפֶּר עָלָיו״ כְּתִיב – עָלָיו וְלֹא עַל חֲבֵירוֹ; חֲבֵירוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִידֵיהּ, שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב כַּפָּרָה כְּמוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Here,** concerning deviation with regard to the owner, it **is written** in the Torah concerning a sin offering: **"And he shall be forgiven"** (Leviticus 4:26), indicating that specifically **he,** the owner, **but not another** person, shall be forgiven. Therefore, if a sin offering is slaughtered for the sake of another person, it is unfit. **The other** person to whom this is referring is presumably **similar to him,** the owner of the sin offering, in **that he is obligated** to make **atonement similar to** that of the owner. Therefore, if the other person is obligated to bring a burnt offering and not a sin offering, this disqualification does not apply.

The parallel resolution for *shinui be'alim* is drawn from a different verse. The Torah states *vekipper alav* — atonement must be effected *for him*, the specific owner. This creates an explicit demand for intent directed at the right person. The *kohen* must have in mind the correct individual. When he intends Dov rather than David, he has violated *vekipper alav* — he has directed the atoning act toward the wrong person, and both are within the world of *chatat* obligation. When he thinks of Gloria, however, the thought is so categorically irrelevant that it fails to constitute a redirection at all. Gloria is not *mechuyav kaparah* in a *chatat*, and the thought about her does not rise to the level of an alternative *vekipper alav*. It is simply noise. The act proceeds as if the thought never occurred.

## Rav Chaviva's Q

### Glass Vessel Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲבִיבָא רָמֵי שִׁינּוּי בְּעָלִים אַתּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ, וּמְשַׁנֵּי. וּמִי אָמַר רַב: חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׁחָטָהּ עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב [חַטָּאת – פְּסוּלָה, עַל מִי שֶׁמְּחוּיָּיב] עוֹלָה – כְּשֵׁירָה; אַלְמָא דְּמִינַּהּ מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ, לָאו מִינַּהּ לָא מַחֲרִיב בַּהּ?! וְהָתַנְיָא: ״תּוֹכוֹ״ – וְלֹא תּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ, אֲפִילּוּ כְּלִי שֶׁטֶף מַצִּיל!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥaviva raises a contradiction** between Rav's statement concerning **deviation** with regard to the **owner and** the aforementioned *baraita* concerning **the interior of its interior,** i.e., a vessel placed in an earthenware vessel, **and resolves** the contradiction. The contradiction is as follows: **But did Rav say** that **a sin offering that one slaughtered for** a person other than its owner but **who is** also **obligated** to bring **a sin offering** is **unfit,** whereas if one slaughtered it **for** a person **obligated** to bring **a burnt offering,** it is **fit? Apparently,** an improper intention that is **of its type ruins it,** whereas intent that is **not of its type does not ruin it.** **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that if the carcass of a creeping animal is found inside an earthenware vessel, the vessel's **interior** is rendered impure **but not the interior of its interior,** and **even a vessel** purified through **rinsing,** if placed in the earthenware vessel, **protects** food inside it from contracting impurity? Evidently, impurity in the airspace of an earthenware vessel can be contained by something not of its type.

Rav Chaviva now poses the final challenge of the daf, combining the *shinui be'alim* analysis with the *tocho* discussion. The question is elegant: Rav has ruled that slaughtering David's *chatat* while thinking of Gloria is valid, on the grounds that Gloria is *lo mina* — not in the world of *chatat* obligation — and therefore the thought is irrelevant. But if a thought about something *lo mina* is truly harmless, why does a glass vessel inside a *kli cheres* save the food? A glass vessel is equally *lav mina* relative to the *kli cheres*. If *lo mina* thoughts and entities are genuinely without impact, the glass vessel should not create a separate space; the food should still be *tamei*.

### True Distinction

The question is sharp, and Tosafot makes it sharper still. When the *gemara* earlier resolved the glass-vessel question, it answered that a glass vessel is still *minah* — it is still a *kli*, still in the world of vessels, even if not a pottery vessel. That answer worked when the comparison was between *kodshim* and *chulin*, or between a wall and a vessel. But here, the *lo mina* of Gloria vis-à-vis Dov is not like *chulin* vis-à-vis *kodshim*. It is more like the difference between an *olah* and a *chatat* — they are both *kodshim*, yet that difference suffices for the ruling. Similarly, Gloria and Dov are both human beings; the distinction is only that one is *mechuyav chatat* and the other is not. If that relatively small difference is enough for Gloria to be *lo mina* and harmless, why is the glass vessel — which differs from the *kli cheres* only in material — still considered *minah* enough to protect?

### Tocho Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְשַׁנֵּי: אַרְבְּעָה ״תּוֹכוֹ״ כְּתִיבִי (תּוֹכוֹ) – ״תּוֹךְ״, ״תּוֹכוֹ״, ״תּוֹךְ״, ״תּוֹכוֹ״. חַד – לְגוּפֵיהּ; וְחַד – לִגְזֵירָה שָׁוָה; חַד – תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל זֶה וְלֹא תּוֹכוֹ שֶׁל אַחֵר; אִידַּךְ – ״תּוֹכוֹ״ וְלֹא תּוֹךְ תּוֹכוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ כְּלִי שֶׁטֶף מַצִּיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav Ḥaviva **resolves** the contradiction as follows: The expression: **Whose interior [*tokho*], is written four** times. In other words, in the verse: "And every earthen vessel into whose interior [*tokho*] any of them falls, anything that is in its interior [*tokho*] shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:33), the word *tokho* is mentioned twice, and each time, the verse could have written: The interior. Since the possessive: Its, is added to each instance, the verse is interpreted exegetically as though the word interior [*tokh*] were mentioned four times: **Interior [*tokh*], whose interior [*tokho*], interior [*tokh*],** and **its interior [*tokho*].** **One** of these is necessary **to** teach the matter **itself,** that an impure earthenware vessel imparts impurity to food in its airspace; **and one** instance is used **for a verbal analogy** between the two instances of the word interior, from which it is derived that an earthenware vessel itself contracts impurity from impure items in its airspace. **One** indicates that **the interior** airspace **of this,** i.e., an earthenware vessel, renders food impure, **but not the interior of another** type of vessel, which imparts impurity only through contact; and **the other** instance indicates that **its interior, but not the interior of its interior,** imparts impurity, **and** that **even a vessel** purified through **rinsing,** if placed in the earthenware vessel, **protects** food inside it from becoming impure. Consequently, it is derived from a verse that inside an earthenware vessel, other vessels not of its type can block the transmission of impurity. Since this halakha is derived from a verse written in that context, there is no reason to assume that a similar *halakha* would apply to slaughter.

The *gemara*'s answer draws on the multiplicity of the word *tocho* in the relevant passages. The word *tocho* appears, with its various forms, four times in the verses: *asher yipol meihem el tocho* and *kol asher be-tocho yitma*. Each appearance of *toch*/*tocho* yields two counts, producing four total. One is required to establish the basic *halacha* — that *tum'ah* operates through the interior airspace. A second serves as the basis of a *gezeira shava* establishing that not only is the food *tamei* but the vessel itself becomes *tamei*. A third teaches *tocho shel zeh ve-lo tocho shel acher* — that food inside an inner vessel is not considered inside the outer vessel's airspace. And the fourth — the apparently extra word — teaches that even a glass vessel, despite being *lav minei* a *kli cheres*, still functions to save the food within it. This extra verse is not an application of the *minah* principle but a specific *limud* that extends the saving power of inner vessels to all vessels, even non-pottery ones. The *gemara* thus affirms the principle while acknowledging that in the domain of *keilim*, the Torah has provided an additional verse to widen the category of what counts as a protecting inner vessel.

## Summary & Principle

### General Rule

The overarching principle that has animated the entire daf is *minah machriv, lo minah lo machriv* — closer things are more destructive, and entirely irrelevant things have no impact. This has been demonstrated consistently across the domains of *kodshim*, *gittin*, *kli cheres*, and *korbanot*: a thought or entity that belongs to the same category as the act in question has the power to displace or disqualify it; one that is categorically foreign does not register.

### Exception: Precision

The apparent exceptions are not genuine exceptions but rather applications of a complementary principle: the Torah does not always demand the maximum degree of precision. Where the Torah specifies only that one must intend a *chatat* — not the particular variety of *chatat* — the principle of *minah machriv* simply has no domain of application for intra-*chatat* distinctions. The Torah has defined the relevant category broadly, and staying within the broad category satisfies the requirement. This is not *lo mina* failing to cause harm; it is the Torah declining to recognize the distinction as halachically operative.
