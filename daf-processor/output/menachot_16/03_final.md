# Menachot 16 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pigul & Chatzi Matir

### Kometz/Levonah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ פִּיגֵּל בַּקּוֹמֶץ וְלֹא בַּלְּבוֹנָה, בַּלְּבוֹנָה וְלֹא בַּקּוֹמֶץ – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בְּכׇל הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the burning of the handful of a meal offering and the frankincense, both of which render the meal offering permitted for consumption: If the priest had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul* during** the burning of **the handful but not during** the burning of **the frankincense,** or **during** the burning of **the frankincense but not during** the burning of **the handful,** i.e., he burned one of them with the intention to eat the remainder of the offering beyond its designated time, **Rabbi Meir says:** The offering is ***piggul* and one** who eats it is **liable** to receive ***karet*** for its consumption. **And the Rabbis say: There is no** liability to receive ***karet* in this** case **unless he renders** the offering ***piggul* during** the sacrifice of **the entire permitting factor,** i.e., the burning of both the handful and the frankincense.

We begin today's daf at the very top of 16a, where a new *mishnah* opens. We remain in the extended discussion of *pigul*, and we now arrive at the *mishnah* that directly addresses the principle of *mephaglin b'chatzi matir* — whether one can render a *korban pigul* through an intent expressed during only half of the *matir*.

The *mishnah* states: *Pigel b'kometz v'lo bi'levonah* — if someone had a *pigul* thought while offering up the *kometz* on the *mizbeach* but not during the *levonah*, or conversely, had the thought during the *levonah* but not the *kometz* — *Rabbi Meir omer pigul v'chayavin alav karet*: Rabbi Meir holds it is *pigul* and one incurs *karet* for eating the *shirayim*. *V'chachamim omrim ein bo karet ad she'yefagel b'chol ha'matir*: the Sages hold there is no *karet* until the *pigul* intent encompasses the entire *matir*. The terminology implicit in the *mishnah* itself — *chatzi matir* versus *kol ha'matir* — captures the heart of the dispute. It should also be noted that even according to the Sages, who deny *karet*, the *korban* is presumably *pasul* at the rabbinic level, a point the *Gemara* addressed two *dapim* earlier, where it established certain *de'rabbanan* rulings of *pasul* precisely because of the proximity to a genuine *de'oraita* violation.

### Rabbi Meir's View

Rabbi Meir's position is that a *pigul* thought during only half of the *matir* — here, either the *kometz* or the *levonah* alone — is sufficient to render the *korban pigul* and to obligate one who eats the *shirayim* in *karet*. He employs the principle of *mephaglin b'chatzi matir*: even partial *pigul* intent, applied to one of two necessary acts, is halakhically operative.

### Chakhamim's View

The Sages disagree. Their position is that *pigul* takes effect only when the intent to consume the *korban* outside its proper time encompasses the entire *matir*. In the case of the *minchah*, both the *kometz* and the *levonah* must be offered upon the *mizbeach* before the *shirayim* become permissible for eating. Each of these acts is therefore only half of what is required. If one had the *pigul* intent for only one and not the other, the Sages hold that the *pigul* is incomplete and *karet* does not apply.

### Chakhamim's Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוֹדִים חֲכָמִים לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא וּבְמִנְחַת קְנָאוֹת, שֶׁאִם פִּיגֵּל בַּקּוֹמֶץ, שֶׁהוּא פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, שֶׁהַקּוֹמֶץ הוּא הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Meir in** the case of **a meal offering of a sinner and in** the case of **a meal offering of jealousy** of a *sota* **that if one had intent of *piggul* during** the burning of **the handful, that** the meal offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet*** for its consumption, **as** here **the handful is the** sole **permitting factor.**

The *mishnah* then records: *u'modim Chachamim l'Rabbi Meir b'Minchat Choteh u'vi'Minchat Kenaot* — the Sages concede to Rabbi Meir's ruling in the case of the *minchah* of a sinner (*Minchat Choteh*) and the *minchah* of jealousy (*Minchat Kenaot*, brought by the *sotah*). In these offerings there is no *levonah*; the *kometz* alone is the *matir*. Accordingly, if one had a *pigul* thought during the *kometz*, the *korban* is *pigul* and *karet* applies, *she'ha'kometz hu ha'matir* — because the *kometz* is the sole *matir* and thus constitutes the entire *matir* on its own. The Sages have no objection to the *kometz* in isolation; their dispute is only where a second act is also required and the intent did not extend to it. The *Gemara* had noted earlier that this clarification, though seemingly obvious, was stated in order to explain why even at the stage of half a *matir* the Sages impose a *de'rabbanan* disqualification — because in cases such as *Minchat Kenaot* the same act would be a fully operative *de'oraita pigul*.

### Shtei Hakvashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט אֶחָד מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל שְׁתֵּי חַלּוֹת לְמָחָר, הִקְטִיר אֶחָד מִן הַבָּזִיכִין לֶאֱכוֹל שְׁנֵי סְדָרִים לְמָחָר – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בְּכׇל הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered one of the** two **lambs** sacrificed with the two loaves on *Shavuot* with the intent **to partake of** the **two loaves the next day,** or if **one burned one of the bowls** of frankincense with the intent **to partake of two arrangements** of shewbread **the next day, Rabbi Meir says:** The meal offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet*** for its consumption, **and the Rabbis say: There is no** liability to receive ***karet* unless he has intent of *piggul* during** the sacrifice of **the entire permitting factor.**

The *mishnah* then introduces what becomes one of its most instructive case studies: the *shtei ha'kevasim*, the two lambs brought together with the *shtei ha'lechem* on *Shavuot*. The lambs of *Shavuot* function as the *matir* of the two loaves — the *shtei ha'lechem* can only be eaten once the lambs have been sacrificed. The bread may be conceptually primary (it is what the *Shavuot* offering is fundamentally about, since one does not bring leavened bread on the *mizbeach*), but in terms of what actually effects its elevated status as a *korban* to enable its consumption, it is the *hakravah* of the two lambs that achieves this. The relationship is analogous to the *korban Pesach*: from the beginning of *Zevachim* we learn that the *achilas* (eating) is primary, yet the *korban* only attains its status as *korban Pesach* through the *zerikas ha'dam*. The question of what is most significant is not the same as the question of what constitutes the *matir*.

The *mishnah* presents the case: *Shachat echad min ha'kevasim l'echol shtei chalot le'machar* — he slaughtered one of the two lambs with the intent to eat the two *challot* of the *shtei ha'lechem* the following day, which is a *pigul* thought. Similarly: *hiktir echad min ha'bazichim l'echol shtei sedarim le'machar* — he burned one of the two *bazichim* of *levonah* for the *lechem ha'panim* with intent to eat the bread the next day. In both cases, Rabbi Meir holds it is *pigul* and *karet* applies; the Sages hold *ein bo karet ad she'yefagel b'chol ha'matir* — you need the intent to encompass both lambs, or both *bazichim*, for the *pigul* to be complete.

### Three Perspectives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁחַט אֶחָד מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים לֶאֱכוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ לְמָחָר – הוּא פִּיגּוּל, וַחֲבֵירוֹ כָּשֵׁר; לֶאֱכוֹל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לְמָחָר – שְׁנֵיהֶם כְּשֵׁרִים.
>
> **Translation:** If **one slaughtered one of the lambs** with the intent **to partake of it the next day, that** lamb is ***piggul* and the other is a fit** offering. If he slaughtered one lamb with the intent **to partake of the other the next day, both** lambs **are fit** offerings, as one permitting factor does not render another permitting factor *piggul*.

What makes the *shtei ha'kevasim* case so rich analytically is that the two lambs and the loaves illuminate *chatzi matir* from three distinct angles simultaneously.

First, relative to the bread: together the two lambs function as the *matir* of the *shtei ha'lechem*, so each lamb individually is only a *chatzi matir* for the bread. This is the case under debate between Rabbi Meir and the Sages.

Second, relative to the lamb itself: the *mishnah* states *shachat echad min ha'kevasim l'echol heimenu le'machar* — he slaughtered one lamb with intent to eat from that same lamb the next day. Here the *avodah* performed with respect to that lamb — slaughtering and then sprinkling its blood — is the complete *matir* for the lamb's own meat. This is not a *chatzi matir* at all; it is a full *pigul* intent, rendering that lamb *pigul* while the other remains *kasher*: *hu pigul v'chaveiro kasher*.

Third, relative to the other lamb: *le'echol me'chaveiro le'machar* — if one slaughtered a lamb with intent to eat from the *other* lamb tomorrow, *shneihem kesheirim* — both are *kasher*. This is because of the principle *ein matir mefagel matir*: one lamb has no halakhic dependency on the other. The bread depends on both lambs; the lambs have no dependency on one another. Hence one lamb's *avodah* cannot affect the status of the other.

The *mishnah* thus offers three clean perspectives from a single pair of lambs: each lamb is a *chatzi matir* for the bread; each lamb is a full *matir* for its own meat; and the lambs have no relationship to each other as potential *matirim*.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנָּתַן אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ בִּשְׁתִיקָה, וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה בְּמַחְשָׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav says:** The **dispute** between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis with regard to a case where one has intent of *piggul* by either the handful or the frankincense applies only, for instance, **when he placed the handful** upon the altar **in silence,** i.e., without specific intent, **and** thereafter placed **the frankincense with intent** to partake of the remainder the next day. In such a case, it is evident that his intent relates only to the frankincense.

The *Gemara* now records the dispute between Rav and Shmuel regarding how to delimit the case under debate. Rav says: *machlokes she'natan et ha'kometz b'shtika v'et ha'levonah b'machshava* — the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies specifically when the *kometz* was performed in silence, without any *pigul* thought, and the *levonah* was performed with the *pigul* thought. We assume the *kometz* was done first, though as noted earlier this is not strictly required. That is the case where we debate *mephaglin b'chatzi matir*.

However, says Rav: *aval natan ha'kometz b'machshava v'et ha'levonah b'shtika* — if the *kometz* was performed first with the *pigul* thought, and then the *levonah* was performed in silence, *divrei hakol pigul* — everyone agrees it is *pigul*. The reason: *she'kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh* — one who performs subsequent acts does so under the framing of the initial intent. Once the *pigul* intent has been articulated at the first stage, the subsequent act of silence is interpreted as proceeding under that same mindset, and accordingly the entire *matir* is encompassed by the *pigul* intent. It is therefore not a case of *chatzi matir* at all.

### Shmuel's Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נָתַן הַקּוֹמֶץ בְּמַחְשָׁבָה, וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה בִּשְׁתִיקָה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פִּיגּוּל, שֶׁכׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת רִאשׁוֹנָה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה. וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עֲדַיִין הִוא מַחְלוֹקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if **he placed the handful with** the **intent** to partake of the remainder the next day **and** then placed **the frankincense in silence, all agree** that the meal offering is ***piggul*, as anyone who performs** the rites in such a manner **performs** them **in accordance with** his **initial intent. And Shmuel says:** Even in such a case, there **is still a dispute** between the Rabbis and Rabbi Meir.

Shmuel disagrees: *u'Shmuel omer adayin hi machlokes* — it is still a dispute. Unless a fresh *pigul* intent is expressed or held at the second stage, one cannot assume the second act is defined by the first. Shmuel thus holds that even when the *pigul* thought preceded, subsequent silence does not carry the first thought forward. The case remains one of *chatzi matir* and the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages applies.

### Mindset vs. Speech

This dispute reveals something philosophically important about the nature of *pigul* intent. Both Rashi and Tosafot hold that *pigul* thought must be articulated — it is not sufficient to merely think it internally. Yet even granting this, there is a crucial distinction between articulation as an expression of thought and a pure speech act in the manner of *hakdashah* or *kri'as ha'Torah*. If *pigul* were purely a speech act — like a legal declaration — it could not be said that silence following such a declaration constitutes a repetition of it. One simply did not speak. The most one could say is that having articulated what one was thinking at the first stage, it is clear one continues to hold that thought, or that the second act is framed by the prior statement. This is precisely what Rav claims: *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh* is a claim about mindset, not about repeated utterance. The first articulation reveals and frames the operative *da'as*; subsequent silence is governed by it. Shmuel denies this framing: without an explicit expression of intent at each stage, there is no basis to carry it forward.

The very structure of this debate — Rav appealing to *da'as rishona* and Shmuel rejecting its reach — demonstrates that *pigul*, even for those who require articulation, is fundamentally about *da'as*, about mental orientation, not about a performative speech act. The articulation is a vehicle for revealing and concretizing the intent, but the halakhic substance is the intent itself.

### Tosefta Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּקְמִיצָה, וּבְמַתַּן כְּלִי, וּבְהִילּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement,** that intent of *piggul* with regard to only the handful renders the meal offering *piggul*, **said?** It is stated in a case where one had such intent **during the removal of the handful, or during the placement** of the handful in a service **vessel, or during the conveying** of the vessel to the altar. Since these rites are not performed with the frankincense, during these stages the handful is the only relevant permitting factor.

A *Tosefta* is now cited that bears directly on this debate. The *Tosefta* first supplies important framing for a question implicit in the earlier *mishnah*: if we have always discussed *pigul* arising at the stage of *kemitzah*, why was the *levonah* never considered? The *Tosefta* clarifies: *bameh devarim amurim* — when is the *kometz* alone sufficient and the *levonah* not a concern? During *kemitzah*, *matan kli*, and *hiluch*. But *aval bau l'haktarah* — when it comes time to burn: there the *levonah* matters, because at the stage of *haktarah* the *levonah* is an *avodah* and a *matir*. It is only then that *natan et ha'kometz b'shtika v'et ha'levonah b'machshava* — performing the *kometz* in silence and the *levonah* with the *pigul* thought, or *et ha'kometz b'machshava v'et ha'levonah b'shtika* — performing the *kometz* with the *pigul* thought and the *levonah* in silence, generates the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לְהַקְטָרָה, נָתַן אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ בִּשְׁתִיקָה וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה בְּמַחְשָׁבָה, אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ בְּמַחְשָׁבָה וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה בִּשְׁתִיקָה – רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בְּכׇל הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: But once the priest **comes to** perform **the burning** of the handful, then if he **placed the handful** on the fire of the altar **in silence and** he placed **the frankincense with intent** of *piggul*, or if he placed **the handful with intent and the frankincense in silence, Rabbi Meir says:** It is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** its consumption, **and the Rabbis say: There is no** liability to receive ***karet* unless he has intent of *piggul* during** the sacrifice of **the entire permitting factor.**

Two reasons explain why earlier stages do not implicate *chatzi matir*. First, there is no *avodah* of the *levonah* at the stage of *kemitzah* or *hiluch* — the Torah's discussion of the *levonah* concerns only its burning. Second, and more conceptually: the *matir* in the phrase *chatzi matir* refers not merely to a necessary *avodah* in the process, but to an act that *effects a change of status*. The *kometz* and *levonah*, when burned, change the status of the *shirayim* — they render the remainder permissible. Nothing that precedes the *haktarah* effects such a change. A necessary *avodah* is not the same as a *matir* in the operative sense. The principle at stake is not *mephaglin b'chatzi avodah* but *mephaglin b'chatzi matir*.

Now, the *Tosefta*'s relevance to the Rav-Shmuel debate: the *Tosefta* presents the two scenarios — *kometz* with thought then *levonah* in silence, and *kometz* in silence then *levonah* with thought — as equally subject to the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages. Even where the *pigul* thought came first, the Sages do not declare it *pigul* by virtue of *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh*. They still treat it as *chatzi matir*. This is direct evidence against Rav and in favor of Shmuel.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: נָתַן אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ בְּמַחְשָׁבָה וְאֶת הַלְּבוֹנָה בִּשְׁתִיקָה, וּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Aḥa bar Rav Huna explains his objection: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches:** If he **placed the handful with intent and the frankincense in silence, and** yet the Rabbis **disagree** with Rabbi Meir and they do not say that one performs the rite of the frankincense with one's initial intent.

The *Gemara* presses: *ktani mihat* — let us focus on this one point — that the debate applies even in the case of *natan et ha'kometz b'machshava v'et ha'levonah b'shtika*, the *pigul* thought having come first, yet the Sages still debate and do not rule it unambiguously *pigul*. This refutes Rav's contention that *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* resolves the question.

An attempt is made to reread the *Tosefta*: perhaps *natan et ha'kometz b'machshava* means that the *kometz* was described first but actually performed second — i.e., the *levonah* had already been done in silence before the *kometz* with the thought. The *Gemara* raises two objections — *shtei teshuvot ba'davar*. First, this would be redundant with the preceding case. Second, other *baraitot* explicitly use the language *v'achar kach*, "and afterwards," referring to the *levonah* in silence, confirming that the *levonah* followed rather than preceded. So the reread fails.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּרְגְּמַהּ רַב חֲנִינָא בִּשְׁתֵּי דֵּיעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina interpreted** this *baraita* in accordance with the opinion of Rav: This *baraita* is referring to a case **of two intentions,** i.e., there were two priests, the first one of whom burned the handful with intent of *piggul*, and the second burned the frankincense in silence. Since the intent of one priest is entirely independent of the other, it cannot be said that the second priest burns the frankincense in accordance with the intent of the first priest.

Rav Chanina defends Rav differently: *targema be'vei de'ot* — interpret the *Tosefta* as describing two *kohanim*, two separate individuals. *Kohen* A performed the *kometz* with the *pigul* thought, and *Kohen* B performed the *levonah* in silence. In that case, *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* would not apply because one *kohen*'s intent does not define another's. The scenario remains a genuine *chatzi matir* not because of anything about the sequence of intent, but because we cannot project one person's *da'as* onto another's acts. The *Gemara* allows this but proceeds to further challenge Rav from other evidence.

## Evidence Against Rav

### Yom Kippur Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? בְּדָמִים הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַחִיצוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **Come** and **hear** a proof for the opinion of Shmuel from a *baraita* that addresses *piggul* during the sprinkling of the blood. **In what** case **is this statement,** that the offering is rendered *piggul* even when he intends only while performing the first placement to eat it beyond its designated time, **said?** It is rendered *piggul* **in** the case of **blood that is placed on the external altar,** where one placement renders the offering permitted.

The *Gemara* introduces a strong *ta shema* from a *baraita* concerning the blood sprinklings of *Yom Kippur*. A preliminary clarification: while for blood placed on the outer *mizbeach* the first application is generally determinative and subsequent applications do not affect *pigul* status, the inner blood applications — those of the *par* and *se'ir* on *Yom Kippur* — are different. Every single application of the inner blood is *me'akev*, indispensable. A *pigul* thought at any one of these applications is thus potentially operative.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל דָּמִים הַנִּיתָּנִין עַל מִזְבֵּחַ הַפְּנִימִי, כְּגוֹן אַרְבָּעִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, וְאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שֶׁל פַּר כֹּהֵן מָשׁוּחַ, וְאַחַת עֶשְׂרֵה שֶׁל פַּר הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר שֶׁל צִיבּוּר, פִּיגֵּל בֵּין בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה בֵּין בַּשְּׁנִיָּה וּבֵין בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: פִּיגּוּל וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת עַד שֶׁיְּפַגֵּל בְּכׇל הַמַּתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to the **blood placed** inside, in the Holy of Holies, on the Curtain, and **on the inner altar, for example,** the **forty-three** presentations of the blood **of** the bull and goat of **Yom Kippur, and the eleven** presentations of the blood **of** the **bull for** an unwitting sin of the **anointed priest, and** the **eleven** presentations of the blood **of** the **bull for an unwitting communal sin,** if the priest had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul*, whether in the first** set of presentations, **whether in the second** set, **or whether in the third** set, i.e., in any of the requisite sets of presentations, e.g., in the case of the Yom Kippur bull in the Holy of Holies, on the Curtain, and on the inner altar, **Rabbi Meir says:** The offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for its** consumption. **And the Rabbis say: There is no** liability to receive ***karet* unless he** had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul* during** the performance of **the entire permitting factor.**

The *baraita* identifies the inner blood of *Yom Kippur*: *mem gimel shel Yom HaKippurim* — the 43 applications of *Yom Kippur*, as well as the 11 of the *par kohen mashiach* from *Horayot* and the 11 of the *par helam davar* from *Horayot*. Working through the count for *Yom Kippur*: inside the *Kodesh Hakodashim*, eight applications (one up, seven down) are made for the *par* and the *se'ir* separately, yielding 16. Then, against the *paroches*, again separately for each, yielding 32. After the bloods are mixed, the four corners and seven on top of the *mizbeach ha'zahav* yield another 11, for a total of 43.

Regarding the 11 for the regular cases: seven applications toward the *paroches* and four on the four corners of the *mizbeach ha'zahav*, totaling 11.

The *baraita* then states: *pigel bein b'rishona bein b'shniya bein b'shlishis* — whether the *pigul* thought occurred at the first set (inside the *Kodesh Hakodashim*), the second set (against the *paroches*), or the third set (on the *mizbeach ha'zahav*) — Rabbi Meir says it is *pigul* and *karet* applies; the Sages say *ein bo karet ad she'yefagel b'chol ha'matir*. And *u'pligi* — they debate.

The critical observation: even when the *pigul* thought occurred at the first set — *rishona* — the Sages still debate. The *baraita* makes no distinction based on whether the thought came first. This is clear evidence against Rav: we do not say *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא: פִּיגֵּל בֵּין בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, בֵּין בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וּבֵין בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית, וּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **In any event,** this *baraita* **teaches:** If the priest had an intention that can **render** the offering ***piggul*, whether in the first** set of presentations, **whether in the second** set, **or whether in the third** set; **and** Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis **disagree** in this case as well. Evidently, the Rabbis are not of the opinion that anyone who performs a rite performs it in accordance with his initial intent.

The attempt is made to rescue Rav by appealing to the scenario of *shtei de'ayos* — two *kohanim*, two intellects — suggesting that perhaps the *Kohen Gadol* performed the first set with a *pigul* thought and then died or was replaced, and a second *Kohen Gadol* performed the subsequent sets. In that case, *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona* would not apply since they are different individuals. However, the *Gemara* notes that this depends on a disputed point: *hani'a l'man de'amar b'par va'afilu b'dam moshe par* — this works according to the opinion that a successor *Kohen Gadol* may continue the *Yom Kippur* service mid-process even with the blood of the original animal. But according to the opinion that holds *b'par v'lo b'dam moshe par* — that a new *Kohen Gadol* must begin again with a new living animal — the scenario of two *kohanim* completing the same blood sequence cannot arise. Yet the *baraita* presents the case without qualification, apparently applying the debate even where a single *Kohen Gadol* carried through all three sets. *Mah ika l'meimar* — what can one say?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, הָכָא נָמֵי בִּשְׁתֵּי דֵּיעוֹת – הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּפַר״ וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״בְּפַר״ וְלֹא בְּדָמוֹ שֶׁל פַּר, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **here too,** the *baraita* is referring to a case **of two intentions,** e.g., one High Priest performed the initial presentation and was thereafter disqualified from performing the other presentations, and a second priest replaced him and performed the remaining presentations, there is still a difficulty: **This works out well according to the one who says** that the verse: "With this shall Aaron come into the sacred place: **With** a young **bull"** (Leviticus 16:3), indicates that a High Priest may enter the Sanctuary **even with the blood of a bull,** i.e., he may continue the presentations with the blood of the offerings slaughtered by another High Priest. **But according to the one who says** that the verse indicates that a High Priest may enter **"with** a young **bull," but not with the blood of a bull,** i.e., a replacement High Priest must slaughter another bull and begin the presentations again, **what can be said?** If so, it is impossible for these presentations to be performed by two priests.

### Rava's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁפִּיגֵּל בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה, וְשָׁתַק בַּשְּׁנִיָּה, וּפִיגֵּל בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית, דְּאָמְרִינַן: אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת רִאשׁוֹנָה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה, מִיהְדָּר פַּיגּוֹלֵי בַּשְּׁלִישִׁית לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** According to Rav, **here,** in the *baraita*, **we are dealing with** a case **where** the High Priest **had intent of *piggul* during the first** set of presentations **and was silent during the second** set, **and** again **had intent of *piggul* during the third** set. In such a case, **we say: If** it **enters your mind** to say that **anyone who performs** a rite **performs** it **in accordance with his initial intention,** then **why do I** need the High Priest to **repeat** his intent of ***piggul* during the third** set? The fact that he repeats his intention during the third set indicates that he did not perform the second set in accordance with his initial intent. Accordingly, the Rabbis hold that the offering is not *piggul*, as he did not have intent of *piggul* during the presentation of the entire permitting factor.

Rava attempts to save Rav's position with an interpretive move: *kegon she'pigel b'rishona v'shatak b'shniya u'pigel b'shlishis* — the case described is one where the *Kohen Gadol* had a *pigul* thought at the first set, was silent at the second, and then had a *pigul* thought again at the third. In this configuration, Rav could concede that the second act of silence is not governed by the first thought, since the *Kohen Gadol* himself went on to articulate the *pigul* thought explicitly at the third stage — thereby demonstrating retroactively that his silence at the second stage was not mere continuation of the first intent. *De'amrinan ei salka da'atach kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh, mehadar pigulei b'shlishis lamah li* — if subsequent acts were automatically governed by the initial *da'as*, why would he have bothered to repeat the *pigul* thought explicitly at the third stage? The fact that he did so reveals that he understood his silence at the second stage to signify something different, not a continuation of his original intent. Thus, this is a legitimate case of *chatzi matir* even where the *pigul* thought preceded.

### Rav Ashi's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי: מִידֵּי שָׁתַק קָתָנֵי? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁפִּיגֵּל בָּרִאשׁוֹנָה וּבַשְּׁנִיָּה וּבַשְּׁלִישִׁית, דְּאָמְרִינַן: אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה עַל דַּעַת רִאשׁוֹנָה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה, מִיהְדָּר פַּגּוֹלֵי בַּשְּׁנִיָּה וּבַשְּׁלִישִׁית לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this** explanation: **Does** the *baraita* **teach** that the High Priest was **silent? Rather, Rav Ashi said: Here we are dealing with** a case **where he had** explicit **intent of *piggul* during the first, second, and third** presentations, and was silent during the subsequent presentations. In such a case, **we say: If** it **enters your mind** to say that **anyone who performs** a rite **performs** it **in accordance with his initial intention, why do I** need the High Priest to **repeat** his intent of ***piggul* during the second and third** presentations?

Rav Ashi objects: *midi shatak tani?* — does the *baraita* say anything about silence? The *baraita* says *bein b'rishona bein b'shniya bein b'shlishis* — whether at the first, second, or third. This formulation does not describe the complex scenario of thought–silence–thought; it simply states that the *pigul* thought occurring at any one of these stages — the first, or the second, or the third — triggers the debate. Rava's reading is not what the *baraita* says.

Rav Ashi therefore proposes his own reading: *kegon she'pigel b'rishona u'v'shniya* — the case is where the *pigul* thought was present at the first and second sets but not at the third. Some textual versions add *v'shatak b'shlishis* explicitly. This fits the *baraita*'s language better: *bein b'rishona bein b'shniya bein b'shlishis* means: whether the thought was present in the first and second (but not the third), or whether it was present only in the third. The same principle then applies: *ei salka da'atach kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh, mehadar pigulei b'shniya lamah li* — if the first thought governed everything, why articulate the *pigul* thought again at the second stage? The repetition at the second stage demonstrates that silence genuinely means a different mindset, not a continuation.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״בֵּין בֵּין״ קָתָנֵי! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty against Rav Ashi's interpretation: **But** the *baraita* **teaches: Whether** during the first presentation or **whether** during the second, which indicates that the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Meir even with regard to a case where the priest had intent of *piggul* during any one of the presentations. The Gemara notes: Indeed, this poses **a difficulty.**

The *Gemara* concludes: *v'ha bein bein ktani* — the *baraita* simply says "whether at the first, whether at the second, whether at the third," indicating that any single one of these stages, alone, suffices to create the case under debate. Neither Rava's reading nor Rav Ashi's reading actually fits the plain text. *Kashya* — it remains a difficulty.

The conclusion is clear: all the evidence aligns with Shmuel against Rav. The debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages regarding *mephaglin b'chatzi matir* applies regardless of whether the *pigul* thought came first or later. The principle of *kol ha'oseh al da'as rishona hu oseh* — that subsequent acts are governed by an initial articulated intent — does not operate in this domain. Silence after an expressed *pigul* thought does not carry that thought forward. This has significance beyond *pigul*: the extent to which prior articulated intent frames subsequent silence is a live question in other halakhic contexts as well. Here, the *Gemara* firmly rules it does not apply.

## Technical Clarifications

### Completion Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר פִּגּוּל, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו כָּרֵת. מִכְּדֵי, כָּרֵת לָא מִיחַיַּיב עַד שֶׁיִּקְרְבוּ כׇּל הַמַּתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to the discussion of the *baraita* itself. **The Master said** above: If the priest had intent of *piggul*, whether in the first set of presentations, whether in the second set, or whether in the third set, **Rabbi Meir says:** The offering is ***piggul* and one is liable** to receive ***karet* for** its consumption, despite the fact that he performed the rest of the rite silently. The Gemara asks: **Now** consider, **one is not liable** to receive ***karet* unless all the permitting factors** of the offering **have been sacrificed,** i.e., if the whole service is completed, including the presentation of the blood.

Returning to the earlier-cited *baraita* about the *Yom Kippur* blood, the *Gemara* examines an important foundational principle: *karet lo mechayev ad she'yakrivu kol ha'matirim* — one does not become obligated in *karet* for eating *pigul* unless all the acts that would normally permit the *korban* have been properly completed. This is not a point of dispute but a precondition shared by all opinions. The verse *pigul hu lo yeiratzeh* — "it is *pigul*, it will not find favor" — is read to require that the *korban* must have been in a position to "find favor," i.e., it must have been performed correctly in all other respects. *Hartza'as kasher kach hartza'as pasul*: just as a *kasher korban* requires completion of all the *matirim*, so too *pigul* requires that all those same acts be completed — save for the *pigul* thought itself, which is the sole defect.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר מָר: ״יֵרָצֶה״ – כְּהַרְצָאַת כָּשֵׁר, כָּךְ הַרְצָאַת פָּסוּל; מָה הַרְצָאַת כָּשֵׁר עַד שֶׁיִּקְרְבוּ כׇּל הַמַּתִּירִין, אַף הַרְצָאַת פָּסוּל עַד שֶׁיִּקְרְבוּ כׇּל הַמַּתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides the source for this claim. **As the Master said** that the verse states with regard to *piggul*: **"It shall** not **be accepted"** (Leviticus 7:18), which indicates that **the acceptance of a disqualified** offering is **like the acceptance of a valid** offering, of which the verse states: "It shall be accepted" (Leviticus 22:27), and **just as** there is no **acceptance of a valid** offering **unless all** its **permitting factors have been sacrificed, so too** there is no lack of **acceptance of a disqualified** offering, i.e., it is not rendered *piggul*, **unless all** its **permitting factors have been sacrificed.** That is to say, in the absence of one of its permitting factors it does not become *piggul*.

### Blood Mid-Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי, כֵּיוָן דְּחַשֵּׁיב בֵּהּ בִּפְנִים – פַּסְלֵיהּ, כִּי מַדֵּי בְּהֵיכַל – מַיָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דְּקָא מַדֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, the Gemara challenges: **And** with regard to **this** case, of the blood of the bull and goat brought on Yom Kippur, **since he had intent** of *piggul* **with regard to it** when he was presenting the blood **inside** the Holy of Holies, **he has disqualified it.** If so, **when he sprinkles** the blood again later **in the Sanctuary,** on the Curtain and the inner altar, it is as though **he is merely sprinkling water,** and not the blood of the offering. Consequently, the permitting factors of the offering have not been sacrificed, and therefore the offering should not be rendered *piggul*.

This principle generates a sharp question regarding the *Yom Kippur* case. Suppose the *Kohen Gadol* had a *pigul* thought while sprinkling blood inside the *Kodesh Hakodashim*. *Keivan de'chashav bah bifnim, paslah* — that thought at the inner stage has invalidated the blood. When he then exits and approaches the *heichal* to sprinkle toward the *paroches*, *maya be'alma deka mati* — it is as though he is sprinkling water: the blood is no longer *kasher* and the subsequent *avodah* is not being performed with a valid *korban*. How then can the *pigul* be considered completed when the very blood used for subsequent stages has been rendered invalid by the initial *pigul* thought?

Rashi raises the obvious counter-question: if this reasoning were applied universally, *pigul* could never take effect. If a *kohen* slaughters the animal with a *pigul* thought, the blood thereby becomes blood of a *pigul* animal — so when he proceeds to the *zrikah*, he is sprinkling the blood of something invalid. Yet we know *pigul* does apply! Rashi explains that the principle must be that we bracket *pigul* and set it aside: we evaluate the subsequent *avodot* as though the *pigul* defect did not occur, and ask whether they were otherwise properly performed.

The question then is whether this bracketing applies within a single, continuous process involving the same blood. Perhaps we can bracket *pigul* only when the *avodah* in question is a distinct subsequent *avodah*, but when the very same blood is being applied in multiple stages, we cannot set aside the defect that arose mid-process.

### Bracketing Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא פַּר אֶחָד וְשָׂעִיר אֶחָד, לְפַגּוֹלֵי מְרַצֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: You** may **even say** that the *baraita* is referring to a case of only **one bull and one goat,** and the remaining blood was in fact disqualified. Nevertheless, **with regard to rendering** an offering ***piggul*,** the presentations performed with the disqualified blood **effect acceptance,** as though the entire permitting factor was performed in its proper manner. In other words, even though the High Priest sprinkled the blood inside the Holy of Holies with improper intent, and thereby disqualified the offering, nevertheless, since he completed the service he is considered as having sacrificed all the permitting factors with regard to *piggul*.

Rava resolves this: *diskomi meratzi* — for the purpose of assessing *pigul*, the defect is bracketed. When we ask whether *hartza'as kasher k'hartza'as pasul* — whether the *korban* was performed in a manner that could have found favor — we mean: setting aside the *pigul* problem itself, was everything else done correctly? This bracketing applies even in the midst of a single extended process. The answer to the question is therefore straightforward: yes, even when the *pigul* thought arose during the inner blood applications, we evaluate the subsequent applications as if they were being performed with valid blood, and the *pigul* is complete once all applications are done. Rava had also briefly noted another theoretical resolution — that one might have used multiple animals (*arba'ah parim v'arba'ah se'irim*), refreshing the blood at each stage — but the operative answer is the bracketing principle.

### Blood Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעִים וְשָׁלֹשׁ, וְהָתַנְיָא: אַרְבָּעִים וָשֶׁבַע! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: מְעָרְבִין לִקְרָנוֹת, וְהָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: אֵין מְעָרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* mentioned that there are **forty-three** presentations of the blood of the bull and the goat sacrificed on Yom Kippur. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** otherwise in a different *baraita*, which states that there are **forty-seven** presentations of that blood? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement, that there are forty-three presentations, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that the High Priest **mixes** the blood of the bull and the goat before placing it **on the corners** of the inner altar, rather than placing the blood of each one separately. **And that** statement, that there are forty-seven presentations, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that the High Priest **does not mix** the two types of blood before placing them on the corners, but sprinkles four times from the blood of the bull and another four times from the blood of the goat, and only afterward mixes the blood of the two animals for placement on the top of the altar.

The *Gemara* addresses a discrepancy in the count of blood applications on *Yom Kippur*. We cited the number 43. But a *baraita* counts 47. *Lo kashya*: the debate hinges on whether the bloods of the *par* and *se'ir* are mixed before or after the four corner-applications of the *mizbeach ha'zahav*. All agree they are mixed before the seven applications on top of the *mizbeach*. But if the mixing occurs only after the four corners, then those four applications are performed separately for the *par* and the *se'ir*, adding four more applications and yielding 47.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמוֹנָה! לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: שִׁירַיִם מְעַכְּבִין, הָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר: שִׁירַיִם לֹא מְעַכְּבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in yet another *baraita* that there are **forty-eight** presentations? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement, that there are forty-eight presentations, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that the pouring of the **remainder** of the blood on the base of the external altar **is indispensable,** and therefore this act is added to the total. **That** statement, that there are only forty-seven presentations, is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that the pouring of the **remainder** of the blood **is not indispensable.**

A further *baraita* counts 48. *Lo kashya*: this reflects the dispute whether the *shirayim* — the pouring of the remaining blood on the base of the outer *mizbeach* — is *me'akev* in the inner *chatat*. Normally, *shirayim* are not considered indispensable. But there is an opinion that because these are *chatat penimiyot*, the *shirayim* retain their *me'akev* status even when poured on the outer *mizbeach*. According to that view, there is one additional application that is halakhically significant, bringing the count to 48.

## Nature of Matir

### Pigul in Holachah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: פִּיגֵּל בְּהוֹלָכָה, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If one had intent of *piggul* during the sacrifice of only part of the permitting factors, e.g., during the burning of the handful but not during the burning of the frankincense, the Rabbis rule that the offering is not *piggul*. Concerning this, **a dilemma was raised before** the Sages: If one **had intent of *piggul* during** the **conveying** of the handful to the altar but not during the conveying of the frankincense, **what is** the *halakha*?

The *Gemara* now addresses the question that the earlier discussion made acute. We established from the *Tosefta* that the *levonah* is not a concern during the earlier stages of *kometz* processing — *kemitzah*, *matan kli*, and *hiluch*. The *Tosefta* explained that the debate of *chatzi matir* arises only at *haktarah*. But we also noted from an earlier *Gemara* that the collecting of the *levonah* from atop the *minchah* — the *likkut* of the *levonah* — may itself constitute an *avodah*, classified as a kind of *holachah* that brings the *levonah* to its place. If there is indeed a *holachah* of *levonah*, then at the stage of *holachah* of the *kometz*, there is a parallel *avodah* of *levonah* taking place. Does this generate a *chatzi matir* issue?

### Rabbi Yochanan's Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הוֹלָכָה כִּקְמִיצָה, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: הוֹלָכָה כְּהַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The *halakha* with regard to **conveying** the handful is **like** that of the **removing of the handful.** Just as intent of *piggul* with regard to removing only the handful renders the offering *piggul*, as it is the sole permitting factor with which the rite of removal is performed, the same applies to conveying. **And Reish Lakish says:** The **conveying** of the handful is **like** its **burning.** Just as intent of *piggul* is required during the burning of both the handful and the frankincense for the offering to be rendered *piggul*, as both of them are burned on the altar, the same *halakha* applies to conveying.

Rabbi Yochanan rules: *holachah ke'kemitzah* — *holachah* is like *kemitzah*. The *levonah* is irrelevant; the *pigul* thought during *holachah* of the *kometz* is a full *pigul*, without any issue of *chatzi matir*. Even granting that there is a *holachah* of *levonah*, this does not create a *chatzi matir* problem at the stage of *holachah*.

### Reish Lakish's Theory

Reish Lakish disagrees: *holachah ke'haktarah* — *holachah* is like *haktarah*. Since there is a *holachah* of *levonah*, the *holachah* of the *kometz* is only one of two parallel *avodot* being performed at this stage, and is therefore a *chatzi matir*. *Bishlama l'Reish Lakish ika nami holachah de'levonah* — Reish Lakish's position is internally coherent: the *holachah* of the *levonah* exists, and the *kometz*'s *holachah* is thus only half.

Rava explains Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning: *kasavar Rabbi Yochanan, kol avodah she'einah materes, avodah chashuvah hi le'fagel aleha bifnei atzmah* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that any *avodah* that does not itself effect a *matir*, does not produce a change of status, is to be considered a self-sufficient *avodah* in its own right for purposes of *pigul*. It is not half of anything; it is the entirety of what that *avodah* is. The concept of *chatzi matir* — doing only half of what is needed — only applies when the *avodah* in question is functioning as something that effects a change of status. When the *avodah* is producing an end result, when it is a *matir* in the operative sense, then we can say it has done only half of what is needed, because the entire point of the act is to effect a certain outcome and that outcome requires a complementary act. But when the *avodah* is simply a necessary step — a *hiluch* that is not yet producing any change — it is entirely itself. The existence of a parallel *avodah* is simply irrelevant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, קָסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מַתֶּרֶת, עֲבוֹדָה חֲשׁוּבָה הִיא לְפַגֵּל עָלֶיהָ בִּפְנֵי עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Yoḥanan holds** that if one performed **any** sacrificial **rite that does not permit** the offering, e.g., conveying, even if he performed it with only one of the permitting factors, such as with the handful and not with the frankincense, **it is** considered **a significant rite with regard to rendering** the offering ***piggul* on account of it, by itself.** It is not comparable to a case of intent of *piggul* during the sacrifice of only part of the permitting factors, as this rite of conveying does not render the offering permitted.

Conceptually, Rabbi Yochanan's position distinguishes sharply between the logic of process and the logic of result. When an act is understood primarily as a necessary process step, its identity is defined by what it does, not by what else must happen. When an act is understood primarily as producing a result — effecting a change of status — then its adequacy is measured by whether it has fully achieved that result, and if it needs a complementary act to do so, it is only half adequate.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: הֲרֵי שְׁחִיטַת אֶחָד מִן הַכְּבָשִׂים, דַּעֲבוֹדָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ מַתִּירָתָהּ, וּפְלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **But** what about **the slaughter of one of the lambs** brought with the two loaves on *Shavuot*, **which** is **a rite that does not permit** the offering, as neither the sacrifice of its portions designated for burning upon the altar nor the consumption of the meat of the offering and the two loaves is permitted by this slaughter, **and** yet the Rabbis **disagree** with Rabbi Meir in this case?

Abaye challenges Rabbi Yochanan: *harei shechitat echad min ha'kevasim, de'avodah she'einah matirathah* — consider the slaughter of one of the two Shavuot lambs relative to the *shtei ha'lechem*. The *shechitah* of the lamb does not render the bread edible; the bread becomes permissible only through the *zerikas ha'dam*. Yet the *mishnah* records the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages even for this case — *shachat echad min ha'kevasim v'al shtei ha'chalot le'machar*, with the Sages requiring the intent to encompass both lambs. How can Rabbi Yochanan say *chatzi matir* only applies when effecting a change of status, if the *shechitah* does not yet change the status of the bread?

### Rabbi Yochanan's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ לֶחֶם בְּתַנּוּר קָדוֹשׁ? שְׁחִיטַת כְּבָשִׂים מְקַדְּשָׁא לֵיהּ, וְהַבָּא לְקַדֵּשׁ כְּבָא לְהַתִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to** Abaye: **Do you maintain** that the two loaves of **bread** are already **sanctified** from when they are **in the oven,** and require only the sprinkling of the blood to render them permitted for consumption? In fact, **the slaughter of the lambs sanctifies** the loaves, **and** an act **that comes to sanctify is considered like** that which **comes to permit.** Since the slaughter of the two lambs sanctifies the loaves, the slaughter of each lamb is considered half a permitting factor; the slaughter of each lamb independently cannot render the loaves *piggul*.

Rabbi Yochanan responds: *mi savrat lechem ba'tanur kadosh?* — do you assume the bread is already fully sanctified from when it was baked in the *Mikdash*? *Shechitat kvasim mikadsha lei* — the slaughter of the lambs effects a status change in the bread itself. It may be that the bread has *kedushas damim* before the lambs are slaughtered, and *kedushas ha'guf* — full sanctification as a *korban* — only after. The point is that the *shechitah* of the lambs actively elevates the bread's sanctified status. *De'ha ba l'kadesh k'ba l'hatir dami* — coming to sanctify is halakhically equivalent to coming to render permissible. *Matir* in this analysis is not limited to making something edible; it means effecting a status change. Any act that effects such a change is a *matir* in the relevant sense, and if two such acts are jointly required to produce the full change, each is a *chatzi matir*.

### Korban Pesach Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי: אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: הִקְדִּים מוּלִים לַעֲרֵלִים – כָּשֵׁר, הִקְדִּים עֲרֵלִים לְמוּלִים – פָּסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ashi raises an objection** from a *baraita* that discusses the slaughter of the Paschal offering. The first *tanna* rules that if one slaughtering the Paschal offering intended for it to be consumed by both disqualified individuals, e.g., uncircumcised males, and fit individuals, e.g., circumcised males, the offering is not disqualified. ***Aḥerim* say:** In a case where one slaughtered a Paschal offering and severed one of the two organs of ritual slaughter with a disqualifying intention, and severed the other organ with a valid intention, then if one's intent with regard to **circumcised** males **preceded the** intent with regard to the **uncircumcised** males, the offering is **valid.** But if the intent with regard to the **uncircumcised** males **preceded** the intent with regard to the **circumcised** males, it is **disqualified.**

Rav Shimi bar Ashi raises a further challenge from a *baraita* in the name of *Acheirim* concerning *korban Pesach*: *hikdim moolim l'areilim kasher, hikdim areilim l'moolim pasul* — if one slaughtered the *korban Pesach* first with intent for those who are circumcised and then for the uncircumcised, it is *kasher*; if the uncircumcised came first in one's intent, it is *pasul*. The *Gemara* in Pesachim establishes that this debate turns on the issue of *chatzi matir*: specifically, the two *simanim* — the trachea and the esophagus — of the *shechitah* of the *korban Pesach*. A *pigul* intent during one sign but not the other constitutes a *chatzi matir* case.

But the *shechitah* of the *korban Pesach* is not yet a *matir*: the *pesach* becomes *mutar* only through the *zerikas ha'dam*, not the *shechitah*. How then does Rabbi Yochanan's principle apply?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקַיְימָא לַן, דְּבַחֲצִי מַתִּיר פְּלִיגִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי סָבְרַתְּ דָּם בְּצַוַּאר בְּהֵמָה קָדוֹשׁ? דַּם – סַכִּין מְקַדְּשָׁא לֵיהּ, וְהַבָּא לְקַדֵּשׁ כְּבָא לְהַתִּיר דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi continues: **And we maintain that** the first *tanna* and *Aḥerim* **disagree with regard to** whether the sacrifice of **half a permitting factor** with disqualifying intent disqualifies the entire offering. In this case, although this slaughter does not sanctify anything, the severing of one of the organs is considered half of a permitting factor. Rava **said to** Rav Shimi bar Ashi: **Do you maintain** that the **blood** is already **sanctified** while **inside the neck of** the **animal?** In fact, the **knife sanctifies** the **blood, and that which comes to sanctify is considered like** that which **comes to permit.** Consequently, the slaughter of the animal is a permitting factor, and the severing of one of its organs is half a permitting factor.

The response parallels what was said about the Shavuot lambs: *amar lei mi savras dam b'tzavar beheimah kadosh?* — do you assume the blood in the animal's neck is already sanctified at the appropriate level? *Dam sakin mikadsha lei* — the knife, the act of *shechitah*, sanctifies the blood. The blood goes from being an ordinary fluid in the animal's neck to being blood with *kedushas damim* — eligible to be offered on the *mizbeach*. This is an active status change. *De'ha ba l'kadesh k'ba l'hatir dami* — coming to sanctify is like coming to render permissible. The two *simanim* of the *shechitah* jointly effect this status change on the blood, and thus each is a *chatzi matir* for it. This reasoning also lends support to the view among the *Rishonim* that the knife used for *shechitah* must be a *kli sharet*.

### Tannaitic Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – בִּקְמִיצָה, וּבְמַתַּן כְּלִי, וּבְהִילּוּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **In what** case **is this statement,** that intent of *piggul* with regard to only the handful renders the meal offering *piggul*, **said?** It is said in a case where one had such intent **during the removal of the handful, or during the placement** of the handful in a service **vessel, or during the conveying.**

A *baraita* is cited that appears to strongly support Rabbi Yochanan: *bameh devarim amurim b'kemitzah u'vmatan kli u'vehiluch* — the clause limiting the debate to stages prior to *haktarah*, and stating that only at *haktarah* does the *levonah* matter, seems to indicate that even at the stage of *holachah* — where Reish Lakish posits a *holachah* of *levonah* — there is no *chatzi matir* issue. *Mai lav hiluch de'haktarah* — does this not refer to the *holachah* that leads to the *haktarah*, the real *avodah*? If so, the *baraita* directly contradicts Reish Lakish, who would say that at this stage the *holachah* of *levonah* creates a *chatzi matir*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי לָאו הִילּוּךְ דְּהַקְטָרָה? לָא, הִילּוּךְ דְּמַתַּן כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the proof: **What, is it not** correct to say that the *baraita* is referring to the **conveying** of the handful **for burning** upon the altar, in which case it is teaching that intent of *piggul* with regard to the handful during its conveying renders the offering *piggul*? The Gemara responds: **No;** the *baraita* is referring to the **conveying of** the handful before its **placement** into the service **vessel** that sanctifies it. Since the frankincense is not placed into a vessel, in this case the conveying of the handful alone is considered the performance of the entire rite.

### Reish Lakish Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בְּמַתַּן כְּלִי וּבְהִילּוּךְ? בְּהִילּוּךְ וּבְמַתַּן כְּלִי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, תָּנֵי הָכִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does the *baraita* state: **During the placement** of the handful in **a vessel or during the conveying?** This indicates that it is referring to an act of conveying that occurs after the placement. **It should have** stated: **During the conveying or during the placement** of the handful in **a vessel.** The Gemara responds: **This** is **not difficult,** as one should emend the *baraita* and **teach** it in **that** order, i.e., with the conveying before the placement.

Reish Lakish attempts to reread the *baraita*: *lo, hiluch de'matan kli* — the *hiluch* mentioned is not the *holachah* that is the real *avodah*, but rather the movement involved in transferring the *kometz* from the *minchah* into the *kli sharet*. This would be a preparatory step, not the *avodah* of *holachah* itself, and so the *baraita* would not be speaking about the stage where the *levonah*'s *holachah* matters.

The *Gemara* objects on two grounds. First: *ei hachi, b'matan kli u'vehiluch*, the *baraita* lists *matan kli* first and *hiluch* second — which implies that the *hiluch* comes after the *matan kli*, i.e., it is the real *avodah* of *holachah* that follows the placing of the *kometz* in the vessel. If it were the *hiluch* of placing it into the vessel, the order would be reversed: first the *hiluch*, then the *matan kli*. Second: the *baraita* then says *ba lo l'haktarah* — it came to *haktarah* — implying the stage it just described is prior to *haktarah*, i.e., the *holachah* to the *mizbeach* for burning. According to Reish Lakish, the *baraita* should have said *ba lo l'holachah* as the pivot point where *chatzi matir* begins, since that is where the *holachah* of *levonah* becomes relevant. *Kashya* — this remains difficult for Reish Lakish.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּא לוֹ לְהַקְטָרָה? בָּא לוֹ לְהוֹלָכָה מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא, כֵּיוָן דְּהוֹלָכָה צוֹרֶךְ דְּהַקְטָרָה הִיא, קָרֵי לַהּ הַקְטָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection from the next clause of the same *baraita*: Once **he comes to** perform **the burning** of the handful, there is no liability to receive *karet* unless he has intent of *piggul* during the sacrifice of the frankincense as well. The Gemara asks: According to Reish Lakish, the *baraita* **should have** stated: Once **he comes to** perform **the conveying** and to perform the burning. Reish Lakish maintains that the conveying mentioned previously in the *baraita* occurs earlier, before its placement into a service vessel, which means that there is another act of conveying. The Gemara responds: **This** is **not difficult; since** this **conveying is for the purpose of burning, it is called burning** in the *baraita*.

Furthermore: *ela natan et ha'kometz b'shtika* — the *baraita* phrases the debate in terms of *natan et ha'kometz*, the placing of the *kometz*, in silence. According to Reish Lakish, it should have said *holich*, in the *holachah*, in silence. The *baraita*'s language does not support Reish Lakish's reading. The strong evidence of the *baraita* thus aligns with Rabbi Yochanan: the only stage at which the *levonah*'s presence creates a *chatzi matir* issue is the *haktarah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, נָתַן אֶת הַקּוֹמֶץ בִּשְׁתִיקָה – הוֹלִיךְ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further challenges: **But** the *baraita* also states: If he **placed the handful** on the altar **in silence,** and he placed the frankincense with intent of *piggul*. According to Reish Lakish, **it should have** also stated: If **he carried** the handful in silence, and the frankincense with intent of *piggul*, as according to Reish Lakish it is the act of conveying that he is performing at this stage. The Gemara responds: Indeed, this is **a difficulty.**

## Conceptual Conclusion

### Rabbi Yochanan Prevails

Rabbi Yochanan's principle prevails. The concept of *chatzi matir* — doing only half of what is required — applies exclusively to acts that effect a change of status. An *avodah* that is merely a necessary step in the process, without itself producing a change of status in any object or substance, is a self-sufficient *avodah* for *pigul* purposes. The fact that a parallel *avodah* exists at the same stage is immaterial unless both of those parallel acts are effecting a change of status jointly.

Reish Lakish's position — that any parallel *avodah* generates a *chatzi matir* problem — cannot be reconciled with the *baraita*'s explicit ruling that only *haktarah* triggers the debate.

### Minchah Application

Applied to the *minchah*: the only stage at which the burning of the *kometz* and the *levonah* function as a *chatzi matir* — effecting the change of status of the *shirayim* to render them permissible for eating — is the *haktarah*, the burning. No earlier stage changes the status of the *shirayim*. The *kemitzah*, the *matan kli*, and the *holachah* are all necessary *avodot*, but they do not themselves effect a change of status in conjunction with anything else. Accordingly, the debate of Rabbi Meir and the Sages over *mephaglin b'chatzi matir* applies, in the case of the *minchah*, only at the stage of *haktarah*.
