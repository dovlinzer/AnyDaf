# Yevamot 29 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Maamar Status (Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel)

### Maamar as Kinyan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת וְאֶחָד מוּפְנֶה. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְעָשָׂה בָּהּ מוּפְנֶה מַאֲמָר. וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָחִיו הַשֵּׁנִי — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ, וְהַלֵּזוּ תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: מוֹצִיא אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט וּבַחֲלִיצָה, וְאֵשֶׁת אָחִיו בַּחֲלִיצָה. זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: אוֹי לוֹ עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְאוֹי לוֹ עַל אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two** of whom **were married to two sisters, and one who was single,** the following occurred: **The husband of one of the sisters died** childless, leaving behind his wife, **and the single** brother **performed levirate betrothal [*ma'amar*]** to this wife. The single brother performed an act of betrothal to the *yevama* but did not yet consummate the marriage by engaging in sexual intercourse. **Afterward, the second brother died,** and therefore the second brother's wife, the sister of the betrothed, happened before the single brother for levirate marriage as well. In this case, **Beit Shammai say: His wife remains with him.** The woman he betrothed is considered like his wife, and he is not required to divorce her. **And this other** woman **leaves** the *yavam* and is exempt from levirate marriage **as the sister of a wife. Beit Hillel say:** Being as he had not yet entered into marriage with the first woman, he is required to perform levirate marriage with both women. Therefore, **he divorces his wife,** i.e., the woman to whom he performed levirate betrothal, **with a bill of divorce,** which nullifies levirate betrothal, **and by *ḥalitza*,** which nullifies the levirate bond. **And,** he sends away **the wife of his** second **brother with *ḥalitza*** as well. They comment: **This is** the case **that** was referred to when the Sages **said: Woe unto him for his wife and woe unto him for the wife of his brother.** Due to the combination of circumstances, he loses them both.

The sugya opens by returning to the bottom of 29a in order to reframe the central question and correct a slightly imprecise reading from the previous session. The *mishna* had described a case involving three brothers — Reuven, Shimon, and Levi — where Reuven and Shimon are each married to one of two sisters, Rachel and Leah. Reuven dies, and before Shimon dies, Levi performs *maamar* with Rachel. Then Shimon dies. The dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel turns on the legal weight of that *maamar*.

Beit Shammai holds that Leah simply walks away — she is free without *yibum* or *chalitzah* — while Beit Hillel requires both a *get* and *chalitzah* with Leah (to counteract the *maamar*) and a *get* and *chalitzah* with Rachel. Beit Shammai's position implies that *maamar* carries a real *de-Oraita* status: it not only removes the problem of *achot zikukato*, which is itself only *derabbanan*, but it frees Leah to walk away entirely, as though she were an *erva* in the full sense. The *gemara* now wants to determine exactly how powerful that *maamar* is.

### Pushes Off Co-wife

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לָא תֵּימָא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר, דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְאַפּוֹקֵי סַגִּי לַהּ בְּגִיטָּא, אֶלָּא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת בַּצָּרָה בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the main issue of levirate betrothal, it would seem from this mishna that Beit Shammai hold that levirate betrothal has the legal force of levirate marriage. However, **Rabbi Elazar said: Do not say** that **levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **as a full-fledged acquisition according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai,** such that the first woman becomes his wife to the extent **that if he wants to divorce** her **a bill of divorce would suffice** without an act of *ḥalitza*. **Rather, for Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes a rival wife** from entering levirate marriage. The legal force of levirate betrothal is limited to preventing the rival wife of the sister from performing levirate marriage; she is exempt from both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza*.

The *gemara* introduces this question through Rabbi Elazar: *lo teima maamar l'Beit Shammai koneh kinyan gamur* — do not say that *maamar* according to Beit Shammai is a complete *kinyan*, equivalent in every respect to the consummation of *yibum* itself. The proof of a complete *kinyan gamur* would be that if Levi now wished to divorce Rachel, he would need only a *get*, just as in an ordinary marriage after *yibum*, where the *yibum* dimension is fully spent and no *chalitzah* is required for dissolution. Rabbi Elazar rejects this. Even if *maamar* frees Leah entirely, it does not complete the relationship with Rachel to that degree. Should Levi wish to divorce Rachel, he would require both a *get* and *chalitzah*, because there remains a residual dimension of the *yibum* bond.

The formulation is precise: *maamar l'Beit Shammai eino koneh ela lidechot bi-tzarah bilvad* — what the *maamar* accomplishes is to push off Leah, to give her a sufficiently strong *erva* status that she can walk away, while the relationship with Rachel remains not yet fully concluded. It is strong enough to free Leah but not strong enough to fully discharge the *yibum* obligation with Rachel.

### Maamar de-Heitera

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יְקַיְּימוּ. יְקַיְּימוּ אִין — לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר: זֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִקְנֶה, וְזֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִקְנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin said: We, too, learn** in the mishna a proof that levirate betrothal is not a full-fledged act of acquisition, even according to Beit Shammai. The mishna at the beginning of this chapter (26a) teaches that when two sisters who are *yevamot* happen before two brothers for levirate marriage, if the brothers married their wives before consulting the court, then **Beit Shammai say: They may maintain them** as their wives. From here it can be deduced: If they already married them, **yes, they may maintain them.** However, marrying them ***ab initio*, no,** this is prohibited due to the prohibition against marrying the sister of a woman with whom one has a levirate bond.

Rav Avin then attempts to find support for Rabbi Elazar's position from the *mishna* itself. The earlier *mishna* had discussed a case where both brothers died more or less simultaneously, or where no *maamar* had been performed before either death, creating a situation in which Levi and Yehuda cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel and Leah because each is *achot zikukato* of the other. Beit Shammai says that if they went ahead and consummated the *yibum* anyway (*yekayemu*), they may remain married — *bidi'avad* yes, *l'chatchilah* no.

Rav Avin's argument is this: if *maamar* according to Beit Shammai were a *kinyan gamur*, there would be a ready solution in that lower case even *l'chatchilah*. Since nobody prohibits performing *maamar* — the prohibition is only on the act of intercourse with *achot zikukato* — one could simply do *maamar* with Rachel, thereby fully acquiring her and removing Leah from the *zika* picture entirely, and then consummate with Rachel. Then the other brother would do the same with Leah. If *maamar* were truly 100%, this should resolve the problem.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי? דּוֹחֶה דְּחִיָּיה גְּמוּרָה — זֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִדְחֶה, וְזֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִדְחֶה. אֶלָּא מַאי אִית לָךְ לְמֵימַר: מַאֲמָר דְּהֶתֵּירָא — דָּחֵי, דְּאִיסּוּרָא — לָא דָּחֵי, הָכִי נָמֵי מַאֲמָר, אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מַאֲמָר קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר: מַאֲמָר דְּהֶתֵּירָא קָנֵי, דְּאִיסּוּרָא לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Rather, what** then? If levirate betrothal does not serve to fully acquire the woman, what is the alternative? It must be that according to Beit Shammai levirate betrothal **precludes** the rival wife from entering levirate marriage with **a complete preclusion.** If so, there is an additional potential scenario: **This one should perform levirate betrothal** with one sister **and preclude** the second sister from entering levirate marriage, **and that one should perform levirate** betrothal with the second sister **and preclude** the first sister from entering levirate marriage. In this way, the *yevamin* can consummate the levirate marriage with the sisters *ab initio* as well. Yet the mishna does not allow for this possibility. **Rather, what have you to say?** One must assert that **levirate betrothal** performed **in** circumstances where it is **permitted** to perform levirate marriage, i.e. where there is no prohibition with regard to the close relative of a woman with whom the *yavam* has a levirate bond, completely **precludes** the rival wife from entering levirate marriage. However, levirate betrothal performed **in** circumstances where it is **forbidden** to perform levirate marriage, **does not preclude** entering levirate marriage. Therefore, in the case of two sisters who happen simultaneously before two brothers for levirate marriage, the sisters cannot be precluded from entering levirate marriage via levirate betrothal. **So too** here, with regard to **levirate betrothal** performed by the third brother, the same distinction can be made. **Even according to the one who said that levirate betrothal acquires** a woman **as a full-fledged acquisition,** just like marriage, there is the following difference: **Levirate betrothal under permitted** circumstances **acquires** the woman, but levirate betrothal **under forbidden circumstances does not acquire** the woman. Therefore, there is no proof as to the nature of levirate betrothal according to the opinion of Beit Shammai from the first mishna in the chapter.

The *gemara* presses back: this objection applies equally whether you say *maamar* is *kinyan gamur* or whether you say it is merely *dechiya gemura* — that it fully pushes Leah off without constituting a complete acquisition of Rachel. Either way, the same question stands: why not use *maamar* to push the co-wife out of the picture and then consummate? The *gemara* therefore concludes that neither formulation can draw a proof from the structure of the case below, and offers a different resolution entirely.

The resolution is *maamar de-heteira dachi, de-issura lo dachi* — or in the parallel formulation, *maamar de-heteira kanei, de-issura lo kanei*. The *maamar* that Levi performed with Rachel in the *mishna* was done before Shimon died, at a point when *yibum* with Rachel was fully permissible. A *maamar* introduced into an uncomplicated, permitted situation carries legal force. In the case below, however, there is already a prohibition in place — the *achot zikukato* problem means *yibum* is already forbidden — and a *maamar* performed in that context of *issura* does not carry the same weight and cannot resolve the entanglement. This is what distinguishes the workable case from the unworkable one, regardless of whether *maamar* is *kinyan gamur* or merely *dechiya gemura*.

The upshot of the entire discussion to this point is that the core question remains open within Beit Shammai: is *maamar* a *kinyan gamur*, such that only a *get* would be needed from Rachel; or is it *dechiya gemura*, fully pushing Leah off but leaving the *yibum* dimension with Rachel incomplete, such that both a *get* and *chalitzah* would still be required?

### Meshayer Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי מַתְנֵי הָכִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לָא תֵּימָא מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי דָּחֵי דְּחִיָּיה גְּמוּרָה, וְצָרָתָהּ חֲלִיצָה נָמֵי לָא בָּעֲיָא, אֶלָּא דּוֹחֶה וּמְשַׁיֵּיר הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi teaches** this discourse in **this** manner: **Rabbi Elazar said: Do not say** that **according to Beit Shammai levirate betrothal precludes** entering levirate marriage as **a complete preclusion,** so that **the rival wife** of the woman who received levirate betrothal **does not require even *ḥalitza*. Rather, it precludes** entering levirate marriage **and** yet **leaves a remnant** of the levirate bond in place. Accordingly, levirate betrothal precludes the rival wife's entering levirate marriage to the degree that the *yavam* is not required to divorce the woman he betrothed, but the levirate bond with the rival wife remains to the extent that he must still perform *ḥalitza* with her.

Rav Ashi now introduces a third, yet more restricted reading of Beit Shammai's position. Where the previous discussion debated whether *maamar* achieves *kinyan gamur* or merely *dechiya gemura* vis-à-vis Rachel, Rav Ashi teaches that the question is not about Rachel at all but rather about Leah. *Lo teimar maamar l'Beit Shammai dachi dechiya gemura* — do not say that *maamar* pushes Leah off completely, freeing her without any remaining need for *chalitzah*. Rather, *doche u-meshayyer hu* — the *maamar* pushes Leah off enough that Levi is not guilty of *achot zikukato* when he consummates with Rachel, but it leaves a residual connection to Leah such that she would still require *chalitzah* to be fully freed.

This reading is significantly more restrictive than what the *mishna* appears to say, and the *gemara* will challenge it on precisely those grounds.

### Meshayer Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין, אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים יְקַיְּימוּ. יְקַיְּימוּ — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי דּוֹחֶה דְּחִיָּיה גְּמוּרָה, זֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִדְחֶה, וְזֶה יַעֲשֶׂה מַאֲמָר וְיִדְחֶה! וְאֶלָּא הָא קָתָנֵי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ, וְהַלֵּזוּ תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Avin said: We,** too, **learn** this statement from the mishna, where it states: **Beit Shammai say: They may maintain them** as their wives. From here it can be deduced: **Yes, they may maintain them** as their wives after the fact; **no,** they are not allowed to marry them ***ab initio*. And if it enters your mind** to say that, **according to Beit Shammai, levirate betrothal precludes entering** levirate marriage as **a complete preclusion,** then **this** brother **should perform levirate betrothal and preclude** one sister from levirate marriage, **and that** brother **should perform levirate betrothal and preclude** the other sister from levirate marriage. The Gemara challenges this conclusion: **But didn't** the mishna **teach** that **Beit Shammai say: His wife remains with him, and this other is sent away due to** her status **as a sister of his wife?** This indicates that she is exempt from levirate marriage and does not even require *ḥalitza*.

Rav Avin responds by pointing directly to the *mishna*'s language. The *mishna* states explicitly: *ishto imo v'halazu teitzei mishum achot isha* — his wife Rachel remains with him, and Leah walks away as his wife's sister. The *mishna* says she walks away. There is no mention of any *chalitzah* required of Leah. The language of *teitzei* is unambiguous, and it undermines the *meshayer* position entirely. According to Beit Shammai as read in the *mishna*, Leah simply goes free; the *maamar* has effected a real *de-Oraita* change in her status.

### Yevama Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: יְבָמָה דְּחַזְיָא לְכוּלְּהוּ — חַזְיָא לְמִקְצָתה, יְבָמָה דְּלָא חַזְיָא לְכוּלְּהוּ — לָא חַזְיָא לְמִקְצָתה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** one must resolve this by asserting that **a *yevama* who is suitable for all of** the aspects of levirate marriage **is** also **suitable for part of it.** If a *yevama* is eligible for both levirate marriage and *ḥalitza* when she happens before the brothers, as in the case cited in the present mishna where the woman who happened before the third brother was permitted to enter into levirate marriage, she is eligible for part of it. This indicates that if the *yavam* does not take her in levirate marriage but performs only levirate betrothal, the levirate betrothal has sufficient legal force to preclude the rival wife completely. However, **a *yevama* who is not suitable for all of the** aspects of levirate marriage **is** also **not suitable for a part of it.** If the *yevama* is not eligible for levirate marriage, such as in the case referred to in the first mishna of this chapter, where both women happened simultaneously so that the *yavam* is not permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with either woman, she is not suitable for part of it. In that case, if the *yavam* performs levirate betrothal, it does not have the legal force to affect a total preclusion.

The *gemara* does not abandon the *maamar de-heteira / maamar de-issura* framework but instead uses it to explain why the *meshayer* position fails in the lower case. The principle articulated is: *yevama de-chazya le-kulhu chazya le-miktzata* — a *yevama* who was eligible for *yibum* (from this *yavam*) can be affected by even a partial *maamar*. *Yevama de-lo chazya le-kulhu lo chazya le-miktzata* — a *yevama* who was ineligible for *yibum*, because the prohibition of *achot zikukato* was already in place, is not affected even by a partial *maamar*. This is simply another formulation of the *maamar de-heteira* principle.

The *gemara*'s final accounting on the *meshayer* question is that it clearly runs against the *mishna*. The *mishna* shows Leah walking away entirely. Of the remaining positions, the *gemara* has left open whether Beit Shammai holds *kinyan gamur* — in which case only a *get* is needed from Rachel — or *dechiya gemura* — in which case Leah is fully freed but a *get* and *chalitzah* are still needed from Rachel because the *yibum* dimension has not been fully discharged.

One might ask how a *maamar*, widely understood to be *derabbanan*, could produce the *de-Oraita* effect of freeing Leah without *chalitzah*. The *Yerushalmi* addresses this directly. It derives a biblical basis for Beit Shammai's position from the verse *yevamah yavo aleha ve-lakacha lo le-isha ve-yibbmah* — the *Yerushalmi* reads *ve-lakacha lo le-isha* as a reference to *maamar*-style acquisition, and *ve-yibbmah* as the act of *yibum* itself, identifying *lekicha l'ishah* as a *de-Oraita* component of the *yibum* process according to Beit Shammai.

## Maamar Completeness

### Nisuin Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבָּה: מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי, נִישּׂוּאִין עוֹשֶׂה, אוֹ אֵירוּסִין עוֹשֶׂה? אָמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to Beit Shammai's opinion about levirate betrothal, **Rabba asks: Does levirate betrothal, according to** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, create** a full-fledged **marriage** bond? **Or, does it** merely **create betrothal,** similar to all other acts of betrothal? **Abaye said to him: With regard to what *halakha*** do you ask this?

Rava now pushes the inquiry in an entirely different direction. Assuming Beit Shammai holds *maamar* to be a real *kinyan* — perhaps even *kinyan gamur* — the question becomes not merely whether it is stronger than standard *kiddushin* but whether it might accomplish something even beyond *kiddushin*: does *maamar* according to Beit Shammai achieve *nisuin* status, or only *eirusin*?

This is a striking question, given that *maamar* consists of a verbal declaration analogous to *kiddushin* and involves no act of consummation. But Rava is probing whether the unique *de-Oraita* framework of Beit Shammai might assign it a higher categorization.

### Abaye's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילֵּימָא לְיוֹרְשָׁהּ וְלִיטַמֵּא לָהּ וּלְהָפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ, הַשְׁתָּה אֲרוּסָה בְּעָלְמָא תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: אִשְׁתּוֹ אֲרוּסָה לֹא אוֹנֵן וְלֹא מִטַּמֵּא לַהּ, וְכֵן הִיא לֹא אוֹנֶנֶת וְלֹא מִטַּמֵּאת לוֹ, מֵתָה — אֵינוֹ יוֹרְשָׁהּ, מֵת הוּא — גּוֹבָה כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **If we say** it pertains **to inheriting** from **her** when she dies, **and to becoming ritually impure for her** if he is a priest, **and to nullifying her vows,** all of which are rights and obligations acquired by marriage, this is difficult. **Now,** with regard to **a betrothed woman in general,** i.e., a woman who was betrothed by Torah law, **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** in a *baraita*: **One does not enter acute mourning** on the day of the death of **his betrothed wife, nor can he become ritually impure for her** if she dies if he is a priest. **Similarly, she does not enter acute mourning for him and** is **not** obligated **to become ritually impure for him.** If **she dies, he does not inherit from her;** if **he dies, she collects** the payment of **her marriage contract,** In a case where **he performed** only **levirate betrothal with her, is it necessary to say** that he does not inherit from her, nor does he become ritually impure for her? Therefore, this *halakha* is obvious and Rabba's question seems superfluous.

Abaye asks: *le-mai hilchata* — what practical difference are you asking about? If the question is whether the rights associated with *nisuin* — inheritance, the husband's right to become *tamei* for her as a *kohen*, and annulment of vows without the father's participation — if that is what you mean, Abaye says those cannot apply to *maamar*.

### Eirusin Rights

The proof lies in the status of ordinary *eirusin*. A *baraita* taught in the school of Rabbi Chiyya specifies the rights that do *not* yet apply after *kiddushin* alone: the husband does not have *onen* status upon her death, does not become *tamei* for her even if he is a *kohen*, does not inherit her, and she does not inherit him (though she may collect her *ketuvah* if one was written). All of these belong to the *nisuin* stage.

Abaye's argument follows a *kal va-chomer*: if standard *eirusin*, which is *de-Oraita*, does not confer these rights, how could *maamar* — which is at most a *de-Oraita* institution of a different and arguably lesser character — do so? The *Ritva* presses on this, noting that within the *sugya* we are operating according to Beit Shammai who considers *maamar* to have biblical force, and therefore the question is not about its rabbinic versus biblical status but about its intrinsic weight. The *Ritva* points to another *gemara* indicating that a woman who has relations with another man after *maamar* but before the act of *yibum* does not incur the full penalty of *eishet ish*, even according to Beit Shammai — which shows that even where *maamar* may be *de-Oraita*, it does not carry the full gravity of an ordinary marital relationship.

### Chuppah Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן מְסִירָה לְחוּפָּה, מַאי? נִישּׂוּאִין עוֹשֶׂה — וְלָא בָּעֲיָא מְסִירָה לְחוּפָּה, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֵירוּסִין עוֹשֶׂה — וּבָעֲיָא מְסִירָה לְחוּפָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the question must relate **to the matter of delivery** of the woman **to** the husband under **a wedding canopy. What is** the ruling with regard to this? Is the *yavam* obligated to enter the wedding canopy in the manner that he would with any other betrothed woman, or not? This is the dilemma: Does levirate betrothal **create** a full-fledged **marriage** bond, whereby the *yevama* **would not require** further **delivery to a wedding canopy? Or, does** levirate betrothal **perhaps create** only **betrothal,** whereby **she would require delivery to a wedding canopy?**

Rava refines the question. If *nisuin* rights are off the table regardless — since even *eirusin de-Oraita* does not produce them — then what is the actual question? The question, Rava clarifies, is about *mesirah la-chuppah*: does *maamar* itself constitute something equivalent to *chuppah*, so that no subsequent act of *nisuin* is required? Or does it function only as *eirusin*, meaning that after *maamar*, before proceeding to *yibum*, a formal act of *nisuin* — entrance under the *chuppah* — would also be required?

This question is not merely abstract. If, having performed *maamar*, the *yavam* is understood to have stepped onto the *kiddushin* track rather than the *yibum* track, then perhaps the rabbinic structure of *yibum* demands that *nisuin* follow *maamar* before consummation, just as *chuppah* follows *kiddushin* in ordinary marriage. Rava is asking: having done *maamar*, must one also perform an act of *nisuin* before proceeding?

### Bi'ah as Chuppah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַשְׁתָּא לָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר, כְּתִיב: ״יְבָמָהּ יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ״ — בְּעַל כׇּרְחָהּ, עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him** that this question is not necessary, as well: **Now,** if **he did not perform levirate betrothal with her** at all, **it is written: "Her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5). It is deduced from this verse that he can take her even **against her will. Is** it **necessary** to say **that** if **he performed levirate betrothal with her,** even without a wedding canopy, that he is allowed to engage in sexual intercourse with her?

The *gemara* responds by observing that in the ordinary case of *yibum* — where no *maamar* is performed — the *Torah* states *yevamah yavo aleha*, and this can take place *be'al korchah*, even against her will. The act of intercourse itself constitutes the completion of the *yibum* and brings with it whatever the equivalent of *nisuin* would be. This is because any consensual cohabitation naturally serves as *chuppah*, and even non-consensual *yibum* is halachically effective — a point the *gemara* acknowledges is deeply difficult but which it does not soften. The implication is that the act of intercourse itself, in any context of *yibum*, serves as *chuppah*.

Therefore, even if *maamar* places the parties on the *kiddushin* track, the subsequent act of *yibum* — the bi'ah — would itself constitute *chuppah*. The question of whether one would separately need an act of *mesirah la-chuppah* appears to dissolve, at least in the case without *maamar*, since bi'ah inherently is *chuppah*.

### Zikat Yevamim Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לָךְ, שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הָעוֹשֶׂה מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ פָּרְחָה מִמֶּנּוּ זִיקַּת יְבָמִין, וְחָלָה עָלָיו זִיקַּת אֵירוּסִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him:** This question is relevant, as **I say** that **anyone who performs levirate betrothal with his *yevama* causes the levirate bond to be removed from him,** and he is no longer considered subject to the *halakhot* of levirate marriage. Instead, a standard **bond of betrothal applies to him.** Therefore, it is appropriate to ask if this act of levirate betrothal is similar to a standard betrothal insofar as the wedding canopy is concerned, and consequently the woman would be required to enter the wedding canopy. Alternatively, perhaps the *halakhot* of levirate marriage still apply somewhat, in which case the woman would not be required to enter a wedding canopy in order to become married, similar to a standard *yevama* to whom levirate betrothal was not performed. Does the performance of levirate betrothal weaken the capacity of intercourse to establish levirate marriage on its own? **What** is the halakhic ruling here?

The deeper answer emerges from Rava's own statement, introduced with *sh'ani omer*: *kol ha-oseh maamar b'yevamto, parcha mimenu zikat yevamin* — whoever performs *maamar* with his *yevama*, the bond of *zikat yevamin* actually departs from him entirely, *v'chal alav zikat eirusin* — and is replaced entirely by the bond of *eirusin*. According to this view, once *maamar* is performed, the parties are no longer in the *yibum* track at all. The *zika* is gone and the relationship has been converted entirely into an *eirusin* relationship. This is a remarkable restructuring: the *maamar* does not supplement *yibum* but displaces it entirely and initiates a new marital trajectory.

If that is true, then the question of *chuppah* is genuine: they are now in an *eirusin* state, not a *yibum* state, and the subsequent act of consummation is no longer the act of *yibum be'al korchah* but must be framed as a *nisuin* within the ordinary marital model. This remains among the most fascinating theoretical positions within Beit Shammai's account of *maamar* — the idea that *maamar* does not merely strengthen or initiate the *yibum* process but fundamentally redirects it.

## Hafarat Nedarim

### Shomeret Yavam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם, בֵּין יָבָם אֶחָד בֵּין שְׁנֵי יְבָמִין — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: יָפֵר. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: לְאֶחָד וְלֹא לִשְׁנַיִם. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא לְאֶחָד וְלֹא לִשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an answer from what is taught in a mishna (*Nedarim* 74a): What is the ruling with regard to the nullification of vows for **a widow** who is **waiting for her *yavam*, whether** she is waiting for **a single *yavam* or two *yevamin*? Rabbi Eliezer says: Let him nullify** her vows. The *yavam* may nullify her vows as though he were her husband. **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** This holds true only if she is bonded to **a single *yavam*, but not** to **two. Rabbi Akiva says:** It does **not** hold true, neither **to one** *yavam* **nor to two** *yevamin*. They may not nullify her vows.

The *gemara* now introduces a case involving *hafarat nedarim* — annulment of vows — as a means of probing the status of *maamar*. The case concerns a *shomeret yavam*, a woman waiting for her *yavam* to perform either *yibum* or *chalitzah*, who makes a vow. The question is whether the *yavam* (or *yevamin*, if there are more than one) can annul her vows.

### Rabbinic Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָוֵינַן בַּהּ: בִּשְׁלָמָא, רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: אֵין זִיקָּה אֲפִילּוּ לְחַד. לְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לְחַד יֵשׁ זִיקָּה, לִתְרֵי אֵין זִיקָּה. אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, נְהִי נָמֵי דְּקָסָבַר יֵשׁ זִיקָּה, בִּשְׁלָמָא לְחַד מֵיפֵר, אֶלָּא לִתְרֵי אַמַּאי?
>
> **Translation:** **And we discussed this** issue, interpreting the various opinions: **Granted, Rabbi Akiva holds that the levirate bond is not substantial, even in** the case of **one** *yavam*. In his opinion, the levirate obligation does not create a marriage bond at all, even if there is only a single *yavam*. And according **to Rabbi Yehoshua, the levirate bond with one** *yavam* **is substantial.** The *yevama* undoubtedly requires this *yavam* for levirate marriage, and therefore she is considered to be like his wife. **But with two** *yevamin*, **the levirate bond is not substantial,** since it is not clear which brother will consummate the levirate marriage with her. **However, Rabbi Eliezer, if** he **indeed holds that the levirate bond is substantial,** his opinion is difficult. **Granted,** if there is **one** *yavam*, **he can nullify** her vows, **but** if there are **two,** why should only one of them suffice to nullify her vows, as it is not yet clear which of them will eventually marry her?

Three *tanna'im* dispute this. Rabbi Eliezer says *yafer* — he can annul the vows. Rabbi Yehoshua says that only if there is a single *yavam* can he annul; if there are two *yevamin*, neither can annul. Rabbi Akiva says that not even a single *yavam* can annul — the *zika* alone is insufficient to confer the right of *hafarat nedarim*.

The *gemara* notes that Rabbi Akiva's position is internally coherent: he holds *ein zika* — the bond of *zika* is legally insufficient, even with a single *yavam*. Rabbi Yehoshua is also coherent: with one *yavam* there is *zika* directed clearly at him and he may annul; with two the *zika* is diffuse and neither has a strong enough individual relationship to annul alone. The difficulty is with Rabbi Eliezer, who evidently allows annulment even when there are two *yevamin*. One can understand why a single *yavam* could annul, but if there are two, neither has an exclusive claim — how can either one annul?

### Maamar Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי (בַּר אַהֲבָה): הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — כְּגוֹן דַּעֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר, וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַאֲמָר קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ami bar Ahava said: With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** one of them **performed levirate betrothal with her, and** this **is** in accordance with **the opinion of Beit Shammai, who say: Levirate betrothal acquires** her **as a full-fledged acquisition.**

Rabbi Ami bar Abba resolves this by identifying the precise case under discussion: *kegon she-asah bah maamar* — it is a case where one of the two *yevamin* has already performed *maamar* with the woman, and the dispute reflects the position of Beit Shammai that *maamar koneh kinyan gamur*. The *maamar* singles out one of the *yevamin* as the one with the active relationship, which is why Rabbi Eliezer permits him to annul.

### Nisuin vs Eirusin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא נִישּׂוּאִין עוֹשֶׂה — מִשּׁוּם הָכִי מֵיפֵר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ אֵירוּסִין עוֹשֶׂה — הֵיכִי מָצֵי מֵיפֵר, וְהָתְנַן: נַעֲרָה הַמְאוֹרָסָה — אָבִיהָ וּבַעְלָהּ מְפִירִים נְדָרֶיהָ! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי מֵיפֵר — מֵיפֵר בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת.
>
> **Translation:** This interpretation can resolve Rabba's question. **Granted, if you say** levirate betrothal **creates** a full-fledged **marriage, it is due to that** reason that the *yavam* **can nullify** her vows just as a full-fledged husband does. **However, if you say** that levirate betrothal merely **creates betrothal, how can he nullify** the vows? Even in standard cases of betrothal by Torah law the husband cannot nullify her vows, **for didn't we learn** in a mishna: With regard to **a betrothed young woman,** only **her father and her husband** together can **nullify her vows?** How could a *yavam* nullify the vows without the father of the *yevama*, if he is not considered a full-fledged husband? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** that this proof can be rejected by interpreting the mishna as follows: **What is** the meaning of the phrase: He **nullifies? That he nullifies** her vows only **in conjunction** with her father.

This identification has immediate implications. *Ei amart bishlama nisuin osah, mishum hachi mefer* — if *maamar* achieves *nisuin* status, one can readily explain why the *yavam* who performed *maamar* can annul her vows on his own, since a husband in the *nisuin* stage annuls alone. But if *maamar* only achieves *eirusin* status, there is a difficulty: the law (*mishna* in *Nedarim*) is that for a *na'arah me'orasah*, vows are annulled jointly by the *arus* and the father. If the *maamar* puts her in an *eirusin* state, the *yavam* should need the father's participation and could not annul alone.

This appears to be strong evidence that *maamar* achieves *nisuin*, not merely *eirusin*: the ability to annul alone is the hallmark of *nisuin* status.

### Partnership Annulment

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak challenges the inference. *Mai mefer? Mefer b'shutfut* — when the *tanna* says *yafer*, perhaps it means the *yavam* annuls in partnership with the father. The word *yafer* does not necessarily mean he annuls alone; it could be read as his participation in a joint annulment. On this reading, *maamar* achieves only *eirusin*, and the *yavam* annuls together with the father, which fully accords with the law of *na'arah me'orasah*.

### Meshayer & Hafara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: מַאֲמָר לְבֵית שַׁמַּאי אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת לַצָּרָה בִּלְבַד, אַמַּאי מֵיפֵר בְּשׁוּתָּפוּת? אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵימַר דַּאֲמַרִי אֲנָא אֵינוֹ קוֹנֶה אֶלָּא לִדְחוֹת לַצָּרָה בִּלְבַד, דְּלָא סַגִּי לַהּ בְּגִיטָּא אֶלָּא בָּעֵי נָמֵי חֲלִיצָה, לְהָפֵר נְדָרֶיהָ מִי אָמְרִינַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this reading of the mishna: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who said** that **for Beit Shammai levirate betrothal acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes a rival wife** from entering levirate marriage, and it is not a full-fledged acquisition, **why should** the *yavam* **nullify** her vows, even if he does so **in conjunction** with her father? The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Eliezer** could have **said to you:** One can **say that** when **I said** that levirate betrothal **acquires** the woman **only** insofar **as it precludes the rival wife** from entering levirate marriage, it was to emphasize **that a bill of divorce would not suffice for her, but rather she also requires *ḥalitza*.** However, with regard **to** the matter of **nullifying her vows, do we say** that levirate betrothal is not effective, and that he cannot nullify her vows alone?

The *gemara* then returns to Rabbi Elazar's position — the earlier view that *maamar* only pushes the co-wife off and does not even constitute *kinyan gamur* with Rachel. If that is the framework, how does *hafarat nedarim* work at all, even in partnership with the father? The *kinyan gamur* assumption was the prerequisite for any *eirusin*-level analysis; if we are below even that, neither alone nor with the father should suffice.

Rabbi Elazar responds: his statement — *eino koneh ela lidechot b'tzarah bilvad* — was only about the consequence of needing a *get* and *chalitzah* rather than just a *get* from Rachel. It does not mean the *kiddushin* itself is incomplete or lacking. The *kiddushin* component is fully present; what has not yet been discharged is the *yevama* dimension. On this reading, one can say she is simultaneously a full *arusah* — in which case he can annul with the father — while also retaining a residual status as *yevama* that requires *chalitzah* to discharge. These are two parallel statuses rather than a partially completed single status. This is a nuanced position: not partial acquisition, but dual status.

### Court Support Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: וּלְרַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מִי נִיחָא? מִי קָתָנֵי ״יָפֵרוּ״? ״יָפֵר״ קָתָנֵי. אֶלָּא, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן — שֶׁעָמְדָה בַּדִּין, וּפָסְקוּ לָהּ מְזוֹנוֹת מִשֶּׁלּוֹ, וְכִדְרַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רַב פִּנְחָס מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: כׇּל הַנּוֹדֶרֶת — עַל דַּעַת בַּעְלָהּ הִיא נוֹדֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** an alternate explanation: **Rabbi Elazar** could have **said to you: And as for Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak, does this** resolution that the mishna is only referring to a case where the father and the *yavam* can nullify her vows together **work out well? Did the** mishna **teach** that **they** can **nullify** her vows? **It teaches** that **he** can **nullify** her vows in the singular, implying that he nullifies the vows alone and not in conjunction with anyone else. **Rather,** the mishna must be explained differently: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case where the *yavam*, regardless of whether or not he performed levirate betrothal, did not want to consummate the levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza*. Therefore, the *yevama* **stood in court** so as to compel him to consummate the levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza*, **and it ruled that he** must supply **her sustenance.** Because she is bound to him and cannot marry another, the court ruled that he was responsible for her livelihood. **And** this is in accordance with the statement that **Rav Pineḥas** said **in the name of Rava, as Rav Pineḥas said in the name of Rava: Any woman who makes a vow, makes her vow with the consent of her husband.** Because she is dependent upon her husband for her livelihood, she does not act without his consent. In this case, because the *yavam* is responsible to supply the *yevama* with sustenance, it is assumed that her vows are also made with his consent. It is for this reason that he can nullify her vows without her father. Consequently, no conclusive proof can be derived from here with regard to the strength of acquisition through levirate betrothal.

If this reading still seems strained — since Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak's proposed answer requires him to annul in partnership, but the *baraita* uses the singular *yafer* — the *gemara* offers a different account of the entire case. *She'amda b'din u-fasku lah mezonot mishelo* — this is a case where the woman appeared before a court and the court ordered the *yavam* to support her financially until he performs either *yibum* or *chalitzah*. In that context, the *hafara* is not a formal act of *hafarat nedarim* at all but rather reflects the principle stated by Rav Pinchas in the name of Rava: *kol ha-noderet al da'at ba'alah hi noderet* — a woman who makes a vow does so implicitly with her husband's implicit consent in mind, and therefore vows made under conditions where she is financially dependent on him and seeking to preserve the relationship are understood as conditional on his agreement from the outset. This is not a technical *hafarat nedarim* within the *Torah*'s framework but an implicit stipulation, and the reason it is confined to this unusual case rather than applied universally is precisely that it depends on the particular type of relationship and financial dependency involved.

The broader takeaway from this entire section is a rich menu of possible standings for *maamar* according to Beit Shammai: full *kinyan gamur* requiring only a *get*; *kiddushin* with a residual *yevama* status requiring both *get* and *chalitzah*; and the radical option that *maamar* removes *zikat yevamin* entirely and places the couple on a pure *eirusin* track — potentially even requiring a formal *chuppah* before consummation.

## Complex Sibling Cases

### Case 1: Rachel Then Leah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְכָנַס נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וּמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצְאָה מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה, וּשְׁנִיָּה מִשּׁוּם צָרָתָהּ. עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּמֵת — נׇכְרִית חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: **The husband of one of the sisters died** childless, **and** the brother **who was married to the unrelated woman married,** i.e., performed levirate marriage with, the deceased brother's **wife and** later **died** himself, childless. In this situation, both women happen for levirate marriage before the other, remaining, brother. **The first** woman **is dismissed due to** the prohibition proscribing **the sister of one's wife,** as she is the sister of this brother's wife, **and the second** woman is dismissed **due to** her status as the first woman's **rival wife.** Following the first levirate marriage, this second woman became the rival wife of the sister, and is therefore exempt from levirate marriage as well. If, however, the brother married to the unrelated woman **performed** only **levirate betrothal,** but had not yet consummated the levirate marriage with the sister, **and he died, the unrelated woman,** whose halakhic status with regard to *yibbum* is similar to that of a sister's rival wife, must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.**

The *mishna* now turns to a series of cases involving three brothers, two of whom are married to two sisters, and one of whom is married to an unrelated woman (*nochrit*). In the first case, Reuven is married to Rachel, Shimon is married to Leah, and Levi is married to Devorah.

In the sequence: Reuven dies; Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel (he is the only eligible *yavam*); then Levi dies. Now Shimon survives with two women before him: Rachel (Levi's now-wife, formerly Reuven's wife) and Devorah (Levi's original wife).

### Case 1 Result

*Ha-rishona yotzei'a mishum achot isha* — Rachel walks away because she is now Shimon's wife's sister (the sister of Leah, who is Shimon's wife). *Ve-ha-shniya mishum tzaratah* — Devorah requires *chalitzah* and may not have *yibum* because she is a co-wife of a woman who is forbidden to Shimon.

The *mishna* then addresses the scenario where Levi performed *maamar* rather than *yibum* with Rachel before dying. In that case, since we are now operating within the framework of Beit Hillel (not Beit Shammai), the *maamar* creates only a quasi-marital relationship. Rachel, being the quasi-wife of Levi, is effectively an *erva* in the *yibum* context for Shimon. Devorah therefore becomes a quasi-*tzarat erva*. The ruling is *nochrit choletzet ve-lo mityabemet* — Devorah must perform *chalitzah* and cannot have *yibum*, because the *maamar* has implicated her as a co-wife of a quasi-forbidden woman.

### No Zikat Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דַּעֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר, הָא לָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר — נׇכְרִית יַבּוֹמֵי נָמֵי מְיַיבְּמָה. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת אֵין זִיקָּה, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּחַד אַחָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara deduces the following *halakha* from the second clause of the mishna: **The reason** that the mishna requires *ḥalitza* is specifically **because he,** the brother who was married to the unrelated woman, **performed levirate betrothal with** the sister. **Consequently, had he not performed levirate betrothal with her, the unrelated woman** would be permitted to **enter into levirate marriage as well.** This is true despite the fact that the levirate bond could potentially render her the rival wife of his wife's sister. **Rav Naḥman said: That is to say, the** levirate **bond is not substantial;** the woman requiring levirate marriage is not considered married to the *yavam*. **And** this is true **even** if the levirate bond was **with a single brother,** as this widowed sister happened for levirate marriage only before the brother who was married to the unrelated woman; her levirate bond was with him alone.

The *gemara* extracts a further inference. *Ta'ama de-avad bah maamar* — the reason Devorah is in a problematic status is specifically because Levi performed *maamar* with Rachel, creating that quasi-marital bond. *Ha lo avad bah maamar, nochrit yibumi nami mityabma* — had Levi not done *maamar*, the implication of the *mishna* is that Shimon could go ahead and perform full *yibum* with Devorah. But this requires attention: even without *maamar*, Levi had a *zika* with Rachel — a bond of the *yibum* relationship. After Levi dies with that *zika* intact, does that *zika* not create a quasi-marital connection to Rachel that would implicate Devorah as a quasi-*tzarat erva*?

The *mishna*'s implication that Shimon could do *yibum* with Devorah if no *maamar* was done answers this question. Rav Nachman draws the conclusion: *zot omeret ein zika va-afilu b'chad acha* — this demonstrates that there is no operative *zika*, even with a single *yavam*. The *zika* alone, without *maamar*, does not create a strong enough relationship to render Rachel a quasi-wife and Devorah a quasi-*tzarat erva*. This principle — *ein zika* — is one of the major *machlokot* in *masechet Yevamot*, and Rav Nachman here identifies this *mishna* as evidence for that position.

### Case 2: Devorah First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. מֵת הַנָּשׂוּי נׇכְרִית, וְכָנַס אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ וּמֵת — הָרִאשׁוֹנָה יוֹצֵאת מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה, וּשְׁנִיָּה — מִשּׁוּם צָרָתָהּ. עָשָׂה בָּהּ מַאֲמָר וּמֵת — נׇכְרִית חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: **He who was married to the unrelated woman died, and one of the husbands of the sisters married his wife, and** then **died** childless as well. **The first** woman, i.e., the sister who was originally married to the brother who performed levirate marriage, **is dismissed** and is exempt from levirate marriage **due to** her status as **the sister of his wife. And the second** woman, i.e., the unrelated woman who had entered into levirate marriage, is dismissed **as her rival wife.** If, however, **he performed levirate betrothal** with the unrelated woman, **and** then **died,** then this **unrelated woman** must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** as levirate betrothal rendered her status with regard to *yibbum* as similar to the rival wife of his wife's sister.

The second configuration: Levi (married to Devorah) dies first. Reuven, who is married to Rachel, performs *yibum* with Devorah. Then Reuven dies. Now Shimon has before him: Leah (his own wife, who is an *erva* to Reuven — his wife's sister — but is not a *yibum* candidate for Shimon since she is his own wife), Rachel (his wife's sister, an *erva* in the *yibum* context), and Devorah (Reuven's *yibum*-wife).

### Case 2 Result

The ruling: *ha-rishona yotzei'a mishum achot isha* — Rachel walks away as Shimon's wife's sister. *Ve-ha-shniya mishum tzaratah* — Devorah requires *chalitzah* as the co-wife of Rachel, who is an *erva*. As in case one, if Reuven had performed *maamar* with Devorah rather than *yibum* before dying, the result is that Devorah requires *chalitzah* and cannot have *yibum* — she is now the co-wife of a quasi-*erva*.

The structural difference from case one: in case one, the *maamar* was performed with Rachel (who is the *erva* — Shimon's wife's sister), and Devorah was implicated as *tzarat erva*. In case two, the real wife — Rachel, who is genuinely Shimon's wife's sister — is the *erva*, and Devorah is implicated through the *maamar* as a quasi-*tzarat erva*.

### Kal Va-Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הַיְינוּ הָךְ! הַשְׁתָּא: וּמָה הָתָם דַּאֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה הָוְיָא צָרָה לְנׇכְרִית — אָמְרַתְּ נׇכְרִית אֲסוּרָה, הָכָא דְּנׇכְרִית הָוְיָא צָרָה לַאֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I need this** mishna **as well? This** principle **is identical** to the principle behind the ruling in the previous mishna, and therefore this ruling can easily be deduced from the previous ruling. **Now, just as there, when** his **wife's sister became rival wife of the unrelated woman** who was already the brother's wife, **you say** that **the unrelated woman is forbidden** despite the fact that the forbidden relative joined later, **here, where the unrelated woman became the rival wife of** his **wife's sister** afterward, is it **not all the more so** clear that she is exempt as a rival wife?

The *gemara* questions why the *mishna* needed to teach both cases. *Hainu hach* — are they not essentially the same ruling? The *gemara* responds with a *kal va-chomer*. In case one, the *erva* is the quasi-wife — brought in through *maamar* — and yet that was sufficient to render Devorah (who is the genuine *yibum* candidate, the real wife) forbidden to *yibum*. If even a quasi-*erva* suffices to block *yibum* with the real wife, then certainly in case two — where the *erva* is the full, genuine wife and Devorah is only quasi-implicated through *maamar* — one would expect the prohibition to apply. Case two, then, is the more obvious of the two, derivable by *kal va-chomer* from case one.

Why, then, were both cases taught? The *gemara* explains: *tana hacha tana bereisha* — the second case (case two, where the *erva* is the real wife) was actually taught first. And the reason is that the author initially entertained the possibility that in case one, where the basic *de-Oraita* obligation of *yibum* runs toward Devorah and the *erva* is only quasi-connected through *maamar*, perhaps the genuine *yibum* obligation should override the *maamar*-generated complication and *yibum* should be permitted. The *tanna* thought about it and then ruled no — even in that case, *chalitzah* is required. Because that was the more novel ruling — the greater *chiddush* — it was placed first. And once it was placed first, the subsequent *mishna* was retained even though it is technically superfluous, since once you prohibit the harder case, the easier case follows automatically. *Tosafot* notes that one might normally expect the formula *zu ve-ein tzarich lomar zu* to handle such redundancy, but that usage applies to a brief additional phrase, not an entire separately formulated *mishna* restating a full scenario.

## Status Lock at Death

### Sequence of Deaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְכָנַס נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: **One of the husbands of the sisters died, and he who was married to the unrelated woman married** the deceased husband's **wife, and** then **the wife of the second** brother, the other one of the sisters, **died. Afterward,** the brother **who was married to the unrelated woman died,** leaving two women for levirate marriage before the remaining brother: The unrelated woman and the woman who was previously prohibited as the sister of his deceased wife. In this case, the sister **is forbidden to him forever.** She is not forbidden due to her status as his wife's sister, as his wife already died and one's wife's sister is permitted after the wife's death. However, **since she was** already **forbidden to him at one time,** she is forbidden to him forever. When she first happened before the brothers for levirate marriage, before the third brother married her, she was forbidden to the second brother as his wife's sister. Therefore, she is forbidden to him forever. In addition, she exempts her rival wife, the unrelated woman, from levirate marriage.

A third scenario unfolds. Once again: three brothers, two married to sisters, one to an unrelated woman. Reuven dies. Before Levi acts — before any *yibum* or *maamar* is performed — Leah dies. Then Levi dies. Now Rachel and Devorah stand before Shimon.

The question: Rachel was Shimon's wife's sister when Reuven died. But Leah has since died, and Leah's death dissolved the *achot isha* prohibition. By the time Levi dies and the *yibum* obligation crystallizes for Shimon, Rachel is no longer Shimon's wife's sister. May Shimon now perform *yibum* with Rachel?

### Olamit Forbidding

The *mishna* answers with striking finality: *harei zu asurah alav olamit ho'il ve-nesrah alav sha'ah achat* — she is permanently forbidden to him, because she was forbidden to him for even one moment. At the moment Reuven died — *sha'ah achat* — Rachel was Shimon's wife's sister and *yibum* was impossible. That original moment of prohibition is determinative. Even though the *achot isha* prohibition subsequently fell away with Leah's death, the status has been locked.

### Rav Yehuda's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל יְבָמָה שֶׁאֵין אֲנִי קוֹרֵא בָּהּ בִּשְׁעַת נְפִילָה ״יְבָמָהּ יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ״ — הֲרֵי הִיא כְּאֵשֶׁת אָח שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ בָּנִים, וַאֲסוּרָה. מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said** a principle on this matter: **Any *yevama* to whom the verse "Her brother-in-law will have intercourse with her"** (Deuteronomy 25:5) **cannot be applied at the time that she happens** before him for levirate marriage because she was forbidden to him at that moment, **is then** forever considered to be **like the wife of a brother** with whom **she has children, and she is forbidden** to him. The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav **teaching us** with this statement? **We** already **learned** this in the mishna: **She is forbidden to him forever, since she was forbidden to him at one time.**

Rav Yehuda formulates a general principle in the name of Rav: *kol yevama she-eina nikraat bah bi-sha'at nefila yevama yavo aleha, harei hi ke-eshet ach she-yesh lah banim ve-asurah* — any *yevama* who cannot have the verse *yevama yavo aleha* applied to her at the moment she becomes a *yevama* (i.e., at the moment of the husband's death) is treated like a brother's wife who has children from her husband — an *eshet ach* for whom *yibum* is forbidden — and she remains permanently forbidden.

The logic is precise. The moment Reuven died, two things happened to Rachel simultaneously: she became a candidate for *yibum* with any eligible *yavam*, and she was prohibited as *achot isha* to Shimon. Since at that moment of *nefila* the verse *yevama yavo aleha* could not be applied to her in relation to Shimon, she acquired the status of an *eshet ach* forbidden as such — not merely the contingent status of *achot isha* — and that status does not dissolve when the *achot isha* prohibition later falls away.

### Attribution Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אִיחַזְיָא לַהּ בִּנְפִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִיחַזְיָא לַהּ בִּנְפִילָה רִאשׁוֹנָה — אֵימָא תִּישְׁתְּרֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** Rav **teaching us** with this statement? **We** already **learned** this in the mishna: **She is forbidden to him forever, since she was forbidden to him at one time.** The Gemara answers: This was necessary **lest you say that this** ruling **applies** only in cases **where she was not eligible** at all **during** the **first** time that she **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage. Such is the case in the mishna, when she was forbidden to the *yavam* as his wife's sister the entire time that she was eligible for levirate marriage. Even though his wife died after the other *yavam* married this woman, because she was forbidden to him that entire time, she is forbidden to him forever. **But** in cases **where she was eligible** at some point **during** the **first** time she **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage, such as in the scenario where the brother's wife died prior to the time when his other brother married her, one could **say** that **she would be permitted.** In that case, since the prohibition had in the meantime been canceled and she was indeed rendered eligible for levirate marriage with him during the period of the first time she happened before him, one might think that she would now be permitted. It is for this reason that Rav **teaches us** that even in this scenario she would be forbidden to him forever.

The *gemara* asks: *mai kamashma lan?* What is Rav Yehuda adding beyond what the *mishna* itself already states? The *mishna* explicitly says *harei zu asurah lo olamit ho'il ve-nesrah alav sha'ah achat*. That is precisely what Rav Yehuda said.

### Status Finality

The *gemara* answers that Rav Yehuda's *chiddush* addresses a case not covered by the *mishna*. The *mishna* describes a situation where Rachel was continuously *asurah* to Shimon throughout the entire period that she was a *yevama* — from Reuven's death through Levi's *yibum* — and only after Levi married her did Leah die. Her *asur* status was unbroken. One might say this unbroken status is what makes the *issur* permanent.

But what if the status changed while she was still in the *yibum* process — before any *yavam* acted? For instance: Reuven dies, and then — before anyone performs *yibum* or *chalitzah* — Leah dies. Rachel's *achot isha* status dissolves. She is no longer *asurah* to Shimon. And she is still available for *yibum* from Levi. Perhaps now, since her status vis-à-vis Shimon has changed while she is still in the open *yibum* period, she should be permitted to Shimon if Levi later dies. This is the case Rav Yehuda rules on: even here, the status at the moment of death controls, and she remains forbidden.

### Death Status Lock

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: שְׁנֵי אַחִים נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, מֵת אֶחָד מֵהֶם, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵתָה אִשְׁתּוֹ שֶׁל שֵׁנִי — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֲסוּרָה עָלָיו עוֹלָמִית, הוֹאִיל וְנֶאֶסְרָה עָלָיו שָׁעָה אַחַת! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: הָתָם הוּא דְּאִידְּחַי לַהּ מֵהַאי בֵּיתָא לִגְמָרֵי, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא אִידְּחַי לַהּ מֵהַאי בֵּיתָא לִגְמָרֵי, אֵימָא: מִיגּוֹ דְּחַזְיָא לְהַאי נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית, חַזְיָא נָמֵי לְהַאי — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises an objection: **We learned** this **as well,** as a later mishna (32a) states: In the case of **two brothers who were married to two sisters,** if **one of them,** i.e., one of the brothers, **died and afterward the wife of the second** brother **died, then she,** the surviving wife, **is forbidden to him,** the surviving brother, **forever, since she was forbidden to him** during the period she happened before him **at one time.** The Gemara answers: One cannot learn the halakhic principle from that case. **Lest you say that there** she is forbidden forever because of the following argument: When she was forbidden to the brother, **she was precluded from** entering **this household completely,** i.e., from the entire obligation of levirate marriage. She received total exemption from the mitzva of levirate marriage because this obligation applied only to the one remaining brother, and she was forbidden to him at the time that she happened before him for levirate marriage. But **here, however,** in the case Rav is referring to, **where she was not completely precluded from** entering **this household** because she still required levirate marriage with another brother, one could **say: Since she is eligible** and permitted **to this** brother, **who was married to the unrelated woman, she is eligible for this** second brother following the death of his wife **as well,** in other words, she was not rendered completely exempt from the obligation of levirate marriage. Lest one make this argument, Rav **teaches us** that under any circumstances she who was forbidden at one time is forbidden forever.

The *gemara* challenges this by citing yet another *mishna*: *shenei achim nesu'im shtei achayot* — two brothers married to two sisters. One dies; the other cannot perform *yibum* because his *yevama* is his wife's sister. Then his wife (the surviving sister) dies. Now the *yevama* is no longer his wife's sister. The *mishna* rules: *harei zu asurah lo olamit ho'il ve-nesrah alav sha'ah achat*. This is the same *halacha*, and it covers the very case Rav Yehuda is addressing — where the *achot isha* prohibition disappears before any *yibum* is done, and there is only one brother who could theoretically perform *yibum*.

The *gemara* distinguishes: in the case of only one surviving brother, when the first husband dies and the sole surviving brother cannot act because of *achot isha*, we might say the entire *yibum* process is finished for her vis-à-vis that household — she has been completely pushed away. The *mishna* tells us even then she remains forbidden. In Rav Yehuda's case, however — with multiple brothers — when Reuven dies, Rachel has not been completely rejected from the household. Levi remains and can perform *yibum*. She is still in process, still within the *yibum* framework. One might think that since she has not been closed off from the household entirely, a subsequent change in her *achot isha* status could permit her to Shimon. Rav Yehuda teaches that this reasoning fails: the status at the moment of *nefila* is final, and the process of being in flux does not reopen the question.

## Divorce Case

### Divorce Permits Yibum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד נָשׂוּי נׇכְרִית. גֵּירַשׁ אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמֵת נְשׂוּי נׇכְרִית, וּכְנָסָהּ הַמְגָרֵשׁ וָמֵת — זוֹ הִיא שֶׁאָמְרוּ: וְכוּלָּן שֶׁמֵּתוּ אוֹ נִתְגָּרְשׁוּ — צָרוֹתֵיהֶן מוּתָּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and one who was married to an unrelated woman,** the following occurred: Shimon, **the husband of one of the sisters, divorced his wife, and** then Levi, **who was married to the unrelated woman, died, and** Shimon, **the man who divorced** his wife, **married,** i.e., performed levirate marriage with, **her,** i.e., this unrelated woman. **And** then Shimon himself later **died,** so that the unrelated woman happened for levirate marriage before Reuven, the third brother, who is married to the second sister. In this scenario, Reuven is allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with the unrelated woman. **This is** the case that was referred to when **they said: And** with regard to **all those** fifteen forbidden relatives **who died or were divorced, their rival wives are permitted** to enter into levirate marriage. This is because at the time that they happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage they were no longer the rival wives of a forbidden relative.

The final *mishna* of this sequence introduces a variant: instead of death, *divorce*. The setup is the same — three brothers, Reuven married to Rachel, Shimon married to Leah, Levi married to Devorah. Now Shimon divorces Leah. Then Levi dies. Can Shimon perform *yibum* with Devorah?

Yes. Shimon divorced Leah; Devorah is not *achot ishto* in the relevant sense, and there is no obstacle to *yibum*. *Ve-chanas ha-migaresh* — Shimon performs *yibum* with Devorah. Then Shimon dies.

### Life vs Divorce

The prohibition of *achot isha* applies only *bechayeha* — while the sister-wife lives. Divorce has the same structural effect as death in removing the *achot isha* prohibition: once Leah has been divorced, Rachel ceases to be *achot ishto* in the operative sense (for purposes of the *yibum* framework), just as she would cease to be *achot ishto* if Leah had died. The *mishna* here demonstrates that it is the ongoing existence of the marital bond — not merely the woman's physical life — that generates the prohibition for the co-wife.

### All Permit Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ טַעְמָא דְּגֵירַשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת, אֲבָל מֵת וְאַחַר כָּךְ גֵּירַשׁ — אֲסוּרָה. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: יֵשׁ זִיקָה, אֲפִילּוּ בִּתְרֵי אַחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara deduces from here that **the reason** for this *halakha* is specifically **that** Shimon **divorced** his wife **and after that** Levi **died** and Shimon married the unrelated woman. **But** if Levi had **died** first, **and later** Shimon **divorced** his wife, then the unrelated woman would be **forbidden** to Reuven due to the levirate bond that existed between her and Shimon prior to the latter's divorce. She would be considered the rival wife of the divorced woman who is the sister of Reuven's wife. **Rav Ashi said: That is to say, the** levirate **bond is substantial, even with two brothers.** Although the unrelated woman required levirate marriage with two brothers, the levirate bond is substantial enough to create a relationship between the unrelated woman and Shimon such that the unrelated woman is considered the rival wife of the divorced woman, i.e., the sister of Reuven's wife.

The *mishna* concludes: *kulan she-metu o she-nitgareshu tzaroteihen mutarot* — in all the cases, if the sisters died or were divorced, their co-wives are permitted. This is the general principle: the *tzara* stands forbidden only as long as the *achot isha* prohibition is active — whether because the sister-wife is alive and still married. Once death or divorce removes that prohibition, the co-wife is freed, and there is no doctrine of *sha'ah achat* applying here to lock in a prior status. The *sha'ah achat* principle is operative only where a *yibum* status crystallized at a moment of death; divorce, operating prospectively, does not retroactively implicate the *tzara*.
