# Chullin 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mishnah Structure & Them…

### L'chatchila/B'diavad Int…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, אִיכָּא ״הַכֹּל״ לְכַתְּחִלָּה, וְאִיכָּא ״הַכֹּל״ דִּיעֲבַד. אֶלָּא ״הַכֹּל״ דְּהָכָא, מִמַּאי דִּלְכַתְּחִלָּה הוּא דְּתִקְשֵׁי לָךְ? דִּלְמָא דִּיעֲבַד הוּא, וְלָא תִּקְשֵׁי לָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Indeed, there are** instances where the word: **Everyone,** indicates ***ab initio*, and there are** instances where the word: **Everyone,** indicates **after the fact. Rather,** concerning the term: **Everyone, that** appears **here** in the mishna, **from where** can it be determined **that it is** an expression indicating that it is permitted ***ab initio*,** creating an apparent contradiction in the mishna **that will be difficult for you? Perhaps** it is an expression indicating that everyone's slaughter is valid **after the fact, and** there will **not** be a contradiction in the mishna that **will be difficult for you.**

We pick up on the bottom of Daf 2b. The first discussion in the Gemara may have felt somewhat frustrating, in that it created what seems like an artificial question about the Mishnah — is it talking about le'chatchila or be'di'avad? — and then forced itself toward the answer that the Mishnah is addressing both: le'chatchila and be'di'avad. That said, it is worth stepping back to appreciate what the Gemara is doing here, because the answer illuminates a great deal about what is to come.

### Opening Sugya Pattern


This is a classic move at the beginning of a masechet. The Gemara will often focus on what appears to be a side issue — sometimes purely linguistic, sometimes a question that does not seem to demand much attention — and use it as a vehicle for introducing major themes that will recur throughout the masechet. One finds this pattern across numerous masechtos. The beginning of *bava kamma*, for instance, uses the question of arba avos and tolada as an occasion to survey all the categories of nezikin. In some cases, one has the sense that the opening sugya was constructed quite late, specifically for this introductory purpose.

That is precisely what is happening here. We insisted that the Mishnah first indicates something le'chatchila and then something be'di'avad, and we are going to use that framework to examine four or five different answers — each of which lays out a different understanding of the le'chatchila and be'di'avad of shechita. Taken together, they serve as an introduction to the central questions of the masechet: who can shecht, under what circumstances, what constitutes le'chatchila, what constitutes be'di'avad, and where the debates lie. That is the agenda for the remainder of this daf.

## Tamei & Tuma Mechanics

### Tamei Source & Distincti…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי טָמֵא, דְּאִיטַּמָּא בְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאִיטַּמִּי בְּמֵת, ״בַּחֲלַל חֶרֶב״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא,
>
> **Translation:** **This ritually impure** person mentioned in the mishna is one **who became impure with what** form of impurity? **If we say that he became impure with** impurity imparted by **a corpse,** this is difficult, as **the Merciful One states:** "And whosoever in the open field touches **one slain with a sword"** (Numbers 19:16).

### Cherev ke-Chalal Princip…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֶרֶב הֲרֵי הוּא כֶּחָלָל, אַב הַטּוּמְאָה הוּא, לְטַמְּיֵיהּ לְסַכִּין, וַאֲזַל סַכִּין וְטַמִּיתֵיהּ לְבָשָׂר!
>
> **Translation:** It is derived from the juxtaposition of "slain" to "sword" that the halakhic status of **a sword** or any other metal vessel that comes into contact with a corpse **is like** that of **a corpse** itself. Similarly, if a metal utensil comes in contact with a person impure with impurity imparted by a corpse, it assumes his status. Therefore, since the impure person **is a primary source of ritual impurity, let him render the knife impure,** rendering it as well a primary source of impurity, **and the knife** then **goes and renders the flesh impure.**

The Gemara raises the case: ileima de-itma be-meis — if he became tamei through a met, a dead body. And here the Gemara cites the halakhic principle of cherev harei hu ke-chalal, derived from the pasuk *be-chalal cherev*, at the top of Daf 3a. This is a special rule in Hilchot Tumas Meis. Normally, tuma descends in level as it transfers from one object to another. The corpse itself occupies the highest level, avi avot hatuma — the granddaddy of tumot. The person who touches it becomes an av hatuma; what that person touches becomes a rishon, then a sheni, and so on. But by tumas meis, there is a remarkable exception: a metal object that contacts a met does not descend a level — it retains the status of avi avot hatuma. And by extension, if a person who is an av hatuma by virtue of having touched a met then touches a metal object, that metal object retains av hatuma status rather than descending to rishon.

How does this bear on our case? We are assuming a metal knife is being used for shechita, which is the natural assumption. If the shochet became tamei through contact with a met, he is an av hatuma. He then takes his metal knife to shecht — and by the principle of *cherev ke-chalal*, that knife does not descend a level; it too is an av hatuma. Even if the shochet never directly touches the meat, the knife is touching the meat, and an av hatuma contacting food renders it tamei. How then can we say that the shechita is permissible?

Both Rashi and Tosafot note that the special rule about metal is not even strictly necessary to generate the problem. Even with a non-metal knife, the shochet as av hatuma would render the knife a rishon, and the knife touching the meat would still make the meat tamei. The Gemara acknowledges this: *letamyei lesakin* *vetimtei lebasar* — the knife will be rendered tamei and the knife will render the meat tamei. So how does it help that the shochet does not personally touch the meat?

### Tamei via Sheretz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא דְּאִיטַּמִּי בְּשֶׁרֶץ, וְאִי בָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם דְּאִיטַּמִּי בְּמֵת, וּכְגוֹן שֶׁבָּדַק קְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה וְשָׁחַט בָּהּ, דְּתַנְיָא: בַּכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִים, בֵּין בְּצוֹר בֵּין בִּזְכוּכִית בֵּין בִּקְרוּמִית שֶׁל קָנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it is a case **where** the person **became impure with** impurity imparted by **a creeping animal;** as he assumes first-degree ritual impurity status and does not render vessels impure, the knife remains ritually pure. **And if you wish, say** instead **that actually he became impure with** impurity imparted by **a corpse, and** it is a case **where one examined the stalk of a reed,** which is a flat wooden vessel that does not become ritually impure, to ensure that it is perfectly smooth with no nicks, **and slaughtered with it, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **One may slaughter** an animal **with any** sharp object, **whether with a flint, or with glass** shards, **or with the stalk of a reed.**

The Gemara's first answer: *de'itmei besheretz* — he did not become tamei through a met, but through a sheretz, a dead rodent or animal carcass. In that case, the animal is an av, not an avi avot — only tumas meis begins at that elevated level. If I touch a sheretz, I become a rishon. And a rishon cannot be metamei a kli — a vessel. So if I touch my knife, I cannot render the knife tamei at all. Consequently, the knife remains tahor and there is no problem.

### Shechita Objects


The second answer takes a different route: ve'iba'it eima, *le'olam de'itmei bemeis* — even if he did become tamei through a met, the case is ukegon shebadak kerumit shel kaneh veshachat bah — he checked a reed, like a hard bamboo reed, sharpened it sufficiently, and used it to shecht. And here the Gemara takes the opportunity to cite a braita: *bechol shochtin* — you may shecht with anything — bein betzur bein bizchuchit, whether a rock or a piece of glass, *bein bikrumit shel kaneh*, or a reed — as long as it is sharp and smooth and does the job.

This is already significant in its own right: you do not need a knife per se. There are no formal requirements as to what object you must use. It simply has to be sharp enough and smooth enough to perform the shechita.

### Kli Requirement Debate


Tosafot raises an important question here. There is a principle, derived from the pasuk concerning Avraham and Yitzchak — vayikach es hama'achelet lishchot es beno — that shechita requires a kli. Tosafot asks two things. First: the objects listed in the braita — a sharp rock, a piece of glass — are not recognized as kelim for the purposes of tuma law. They do not carry the formal halakhic designation of a vessel. So if we say shechita requires a kli, we evidently do not mean a kli in the technical sense recognized in other legal contexts. We just mean something fashioned to serve the purpose — which renders the requirement nearly circular, because it reduces to: the object has to be capable of doing the job. For all practical purposes, there is no formal specification of what the shechita implement must be. There is no defined "shechita knife" with prescribed characteristics; it only needs to be sharp and smooth.

Second, Tosafot questions whether even for shechitat kodashim, where the pasuk of the ma'achelet might impose stricter requirements, the implement must qualify as a kli shareit. Tosafot finds this genuinely difficult, noting that some of the objects that appear to be valid for shechita would not normally qualify as kli shareit. This is also not fully settled in the Rambam. The question is complicated further by other Gemarot that speak of the shechita knife as mekadesh — sanctifying — the animal, which would suggest a more formal status for the implement. The matter remains unresolved, but the key practical point is established: for ordinary shechita, there is no prescribed knife and no requirement that the implement be a formally recognized vessel.

## Abaye: Kuti & Supervision

### Kuti Status & Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: הָכִי קָתָנֵי – הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוּתִי. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? כְּשֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל עוֹמֵד עַל גַּבָּיו, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס – לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in resolution of the apparent contradiction in the mishna **that this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Everyone slaughters, and even a Samaritan. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where a Jew is standing over him** and ensuring that he slaughters properly; **but** if the Jew merely **exits and enters** and does not have a constant presence, the Samaritan **may not slaughter** the animal.

Abaye now offers his reading of the Mishnah, moving away from tuma entirely and toward the more central question of who may perform shechita. He focuses on the person doing the shechita: Abaye amar *hachi katani* — here is how you read the Mishnah. Hakol shochtin, everybody may shecht — va'afilu Kuti, even a Samaritan.

The Kuti here is used literally, not as a euphemism. There are several distinct questions surrounding the Samaritan's status. The first is whether Samaritans are Jewish at all. The book of Yirmiyah records that Nevuchadnezzar settled the Samaritans in the Land of Israel, and that they worshiped both their own god and God. Whether they genuinely converted or not is a matter of halakhic dispute. The second question is: even granting that they are Jewish, can we trust that they follow the same system of *shechita laws* as we do? It was well-known that Samaritans kept many Jewish laws throughout the Second Temple period, and so this is a reasonable assumption. But the third and most pointed concern is this: even if they follow the same *shechita laws* for themselves, do they care about causing other people to sin? Given the well-documented antagonism between the Samaritans and the Jewish community, one cannot simply assume that a Kuti shechting meat for non-Samaritan Jews will be careful about their interests. He might not hold by the prohibition of lifnei iver lo titein michshol — the prohibition against placing a stumbling block before others. He may not feel any obligation to prevent them from eating treif, and may even, in some cases, wish to do so.

So the concern is not that the Kuti does not know the laws or is technically incompetent. The concern is one of intent and motivation when shechting for others.

### Supervision & Salvage Te…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם שָׁחַט, חוֹתֵךְ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ. אֲכָלוֹ – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, לֹא אֲכָלוֹ – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if** the Samaritan **slaughtered** the animal without supervision, the Jew **cuts an olive-bulk of meat** from the slaughtered animal **and gives it to** the Samaritan to eat. If the Samaritan **ate it,** it is **permitted** for the Jew **to eat** meat **from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.** Since Samaritans are meticulous with regard to the meat that they eat and eat meat only from an animal that was slaughtered properly, the Jew may partake of the meat. But if the Samaritan **did not eat** the meat, there is concern that the animal was not slaughtered properly, and it is **prohibited to eat from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.**

With that background, Abaye parses the Mishnah as follows: *hachi katani* — hakol shochtin, everybody may shecht, va'afilu Kuti, even a Samaritan. *b'meh devarim amurim* — when is this true? Kshe'Yisrael omeid al gabav — when a Jew is standing over him, in direct and constant supervision. Under those conditions, even though the Kuti's motivations might be suspect, the direct observation ensures he is performing the shechita correctly. That is the le'chatchila. Aval *yotzei v'nichnas* *lo yishchot* — but if the supervision is merely yotzei ve'nichnas — the supervisor popping in and out at intervals rather than maintaining constant presence — that is not sufficient le'chatchila.

### Meat-Eating Test


Then the be'di'avad: ve'im shachat — if he went ahead and shechted without the required constant supervision, there is still a mechanism to salvage the shechita. Choteich kezayit basar, you cut a piece of the meat, *v'nosein lo*, and offer it to the Kuti himself. *achal lo* — if he eats it, mutar le'echol mishchitato — you may eat the rest of the shechita. *lo achal lo* — if he refuses to eat it, assur le'echol mishchitato — you may not. The logic is precise: we assume the Kuti knows the laws and is technically competent; the only concern is whether he would willfully feed others treif. If he himself eats from it, we know he performed it correctly, because he would not eat neveilah.

### Cheresh Shoteh Katan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּחוּלִּין גְּרֵידֵי, דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁהוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִדְרְסוּ, וְשֶׁמָּא יַחֲלִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And it teaches: This is the *halakha* with regard to all people **except for a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, who, even** if they slaughtered **only non-sacred** animals, their slaughter is **not** valid **even after the fact.** The reason the Sages deemed such slaughter not valid is **lest** people in these categories **interrupt** the slaughter, **lest they press** the knife in the course of slaughter, **and lest they conceal** the knife beneath the windpipe or the gullet in the course of an inverted slaughter.

*chutz mecheresh shoteh vekatan* — excluded from this dispensation are the deaf-mute, the mentally incompetent, and the minor. For them, even if there is no direct supervision, the meat-eating test will not work. Ve'afilu be'di'avad nami lo — even after the fact, there is no way to salvage it. Why? Shema yish'hu shema yidresu ve'shema yachlidu — we are concerned that they may have paused, applied pressure, or performed the shechita in such a way as to invalidate it. The distinction is fundamental. For the Kuti, the concern is one of motivation and honesty — he knows what to do, and the question is whether he will do it faithfully for others. For the cheresh, shoteh, and katan, the concern is one of technical competence — they may have committed a technical violation without even knowing it. Therefore, even the meat-eating test is irrelevant, since their own assessment of what they did carries no weight.

### Vechulan Difficulty

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ, אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – עֲלַהּ קָאֵי, ״וְאִם שָׁחֲטוּ״ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the clause in the mishna that follows: **And any of them who slaughtered** an animal and others see and supervise them, their slaughter is valid, **to which** case in the mishna is it referring? **If we say** that the reference is to the case **of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** since that is the *halakha* to which **it stands adjacent,** the *tanna* **should have** formulated the phrase: **And if they slaughtered,** instead of: And any of them who slaughtered.

The final phrase of the Mishnah — *vechulan sheshachatu* ve'acherim ro'in otam, anyone who shechted while others directly observed is valid — creates a difficulty for Abaye. Ilema *cheresh shoteh vekatan* — if this phrase is referring to *cheresh shoteh vekatan*, we were just discussing them; it should have simply said ve'im shachatu. Ela akuti — so perhaps it refers back to the Kuti. But ha amart kesheyisrael omeid al gabav shachat afilu le'chatchila — Abaye himself already established that with direct supervision the Kuti's shechita is good even le'chatchila. Why then restate it in a way that makes it sound like this is only a be'di'avad dispensation? Kashya — this is a difficulty that Abaye's reading cannot fully resolve.

## Rava: Yotzei Ve-Nichnas

### Wine Precedent Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וְיוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא? וְהָתְנַן: הַמַּנִּיחַ נָכְרִי בַּחֲנוּתוֹ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס – מוּתָּר! הָתָם מִי קָתָנֵי ״מַנִּיחַ״? ״הַמַּנִּיחַ״ קָתָנֵי, דִּיעֲבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: And** in a case where a Jew **exits and enters,** is it **not** permitted for the Samaritan to slaughter the animal ***ab initio*? But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Avoda Zara* 69a): In the case of **one who leaves a gentile in his store** in which there is wine, **and a Jew exits and enters,** the wine is **permitted?** Just as there, the sporadic presence of the Jew is sufficient to ensure that the gentile will refrain from touching the wine, it should be sufficient in the case of a Samaritan who slaughters an animal as well. The Gemara rejects that proof. **There,** in the case of the store, **does** the *tanna* **teach: One leaves** a gentile *ab initio*? The *tanna* **teaches: One who leaves, after the fact.** Consequently, there is no proof from there that the Jew's sporadic presence is sufficient to permit slaughter by a Samaritan *ab initio*.

Rava now uses Abaye's position as an opportunity to challenge the threshold of required supervision. Amar Rava: ve'yotzei ve'nichnas le'chatchila lo? — are you really saying that periodic supervision is insufficient? Ve'hatnan — we have a Mishnah regarding yayin nesech: ha'maniach oved kochavim be'chanuto ve'yisrael yotzei ve'nichnas — if you leave a non-Jew in your wine store and you yourself are simply popping in and out, mutar — the wine remains permitted. Periodic supervision is enough to keep the non-Jew honest, since you might catch him in the act at any moment. So Rava asks: why does Abaye demand omeid al gabav for the Kuti performing shechita, when yotzei ve'nichnas evidently suffices in the wine case?

### L'chatchila vs B'diavad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: אֵין הַשּׁוֹמֵר צָרִיךְ לִהְיוֹת יוֹשֵׁב וּמְשַׁמֵּר, אֶלָּא אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁיּוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס – מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** proof can be cited **from** the mishna **here** (*Avoda Zara* 61a): In a case where barrels of wine belonging to a Jew are in the possession of a gentile, and a Jew was tasked with supervising those barrels, **the supervisor need not be** continuously **sitting and supervising** to ensure that the gentile does not touch the wine; **rather, even if** the supervisor **exits and enters,** the wine is **permitted.** This mishna clearly indicates that exiting and entering is sufficient even *ab initio*.

The key distinction that emerges, which explains Abaye's position, concerns both opportunity and the nature of the task. In the wine case, the non-Jew has no legitimate basis for handling the wine. It belongs to someone else. If he were caught opening a barrel or touching the wine, he would have no excuse — it is immediately apparent that he is doing something wrong. Yotzei ve'nichnas works precisely because the illicit act is easy to catch and there is no pretext for it. In the shechita case, the situation is entirely different: the Kuti has been given the animal to shecht. He has every right to be handling it. Any error — whether deliberate or negligent — can happen in a single instant, in the moment when the supervisor steps out. There is no opportunity to catch him red-handed. Additionally, in the shechita case the person actually processing the food has a direct role in the outcome; the potential for impropriety is woven into the very act he has been assigned to perform. This is why Abaye holds that yotzei ve'nichnas cannot suffice for shechita even if it suffices for wine storage.

### Guarding vs Processing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ כּוּתִי. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? כְּשֶׁיִּשְׂרָאֵל יוֹצֵא וְנִכְנָס, אֲבָל בָּא וּמְצָאוֹ שֶׁשָּׁחַט – חוֹתֵךְ כַּזַּיִת בָּשָׂר וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, אֲכָלוֹ – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, לֹא אֲכָלוֹ – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** in resolution of the apparent contradiction similar to the resolution proposed by Abaye, that **this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Everyone slaughters, and even a Samaritan. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where a Jew exits and enters; but** if the Jew does not exit and enter and instead **came and found that** the Samaritan **slaughtered** the animal, the Jew **cuts an olive-bulk of meat** from the slaughtered animal **and gives it to** the Samaritan to eat. If the Samaritan **ate it,** it is **permitted** for the Jew **to eat** meat **from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.** But if the Samaritan **did not eat** the meat, it is **prohibited to eat from** what the Samaritan **slaughtered.**

Rava accordingly re-reads the Mishnah on his own terms. Ela amar Rava, *hachi katani*: hakol shochtin va'afilu kuti — everybody may shecht, even a Samaritan. *b'meh devarim amurim* — when? Keshe'yisrael yotzei ve'nichnas — when a Jew is periodically checking in. That, says Rava, is sufficient le'chatchila. Aval ba umatza sheshachat — if you arrived and found that he had already shechted, with no supervision at all, then chotech kezayit basar ve'nosein lo — you cut a piece of the meat and give it to him. Achlo — if he eats it, mutar le'echol mishchitato. *lo achlo* — if he refuses, assur. *chutz mecheresh shoteh vekatan* — de'afilu be'di'avad nami lo, with the same reasoning as before. And the final phrase *vechulan sheshachatu* again generates the same difficulty under Rava's reading that it did under Abaye's — kashya.

## Rav Ashi: Mumar

### Rava's Re-Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: הָכִי קָתָנֵי – הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד. מְשׁוּמָּד לְמַאי? לֶאֱכוֹל נְבֵילוֹת לְתֵיאָבוֹן, וְכִדְרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: יִשְׂרָאֵל מְשׁוּמָּד אוֹכֵל נְבֵילוֹת לְתֵיאָבוֹן
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** in resolution of the apparent contradiction in the mishna that **this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Everyone slaughters, and even a Jewish transgressor [*meshummad*].** The Gemara asks: **A transgressor of what** sort? The Gemara answers: It is one whose transgression is **to eat** unslaughtered **animal carcasses to** satisfy his **appetite,** i.e., for his convenience. **And** the ruling of the mishna is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rava, as Rava says:** In the case of **a Jewish transgressor** whose transgression is that he **eats** unslaughtered **animal carcasses to** satisfy his **appetite,** if he seeks to slaughter an animal,

Rav Ashi now offers a third reading: Rav Ashi amar *hachi katani*. Hakol shochtin va'afilu Yisrael mumar — everybody may shecht, even a Jew who is a mumar, a habitual violator. In the Gemara's usage, mumar does not mean heretic in the theological sense; it refers to someone who regularly transgresses a particular law. There are two types. A mumar le'te'avon violates out of desire and appetite — he simply cannot resist the temptation, or finds it economically convenient, even while being otherwise careful about halacha. A *mumar lehach'is*, by contrast, has no particular desire for the forbidden thing but does it out of spite — as an ideological act of defiance against God. The *mumar lehach'is* is categorically worse, because his violation reflects an active axe to grind, which may translate into other behaviors as well.

The specific violation in question here is eating neveilot — non-shechted meat — and this is directly relevant to shechita. If a person is willing to eat meat that has not been shechted properly, that raises an immediate concern about his reliability as a shochet. The question is: can we trust such a person to shecht correctly for others?

### Mumar Types & Neveilot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹדֵק סַכִּין וְנוֹתֵן לוֹ, וּמוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ. אֲבָל לֹא בָּדַק וְנָתַן לוֹ – לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, וְאִם שָׁחַט – בּוֹדֵק סַכִּינוֹ אַחֲרָיו. נִמְצֵאת סַכִּינוֹ יָפָה – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **one examines a knife** to ensure that it is perfectly smooth with no nicks **and gives it to** the transgressor, **and** it is **permitted to eat from** what **he slaughtered. But if one did not examine** the knife **and give it to** the transgressor, the transgressor **may not slaughter** an animal *ab initio*. **And if** the transgressor **slaughtered** an animal, **one examines his knife after his** slaughter. If **his knife is found** to be **perfectly** smooth, it is **permitted to eat** meat **from** what **he slaughtered, and if not,** it is **prohibited to eat from** what **he slaughtered.**

The Gemara cites a statement of Rava: Yisrael mumar ochel neveilot le'te'avon — a Jew who habitually eats neveilot out of desire — bodek sakin ve'nosein lo, u'mutar le'echol mishchitato — if you check the knife, ensure that it is sharp and smooth, and give it to him, you may eat from his shechita.

The reasoning is elegant. A mumar le'te'avon only violates when there is some gain — the meat tastes good, or it is cheaper, or there is some other benefit to eating the non-kosher over the kosher. Here, you have pre-checked the knife and handed it to him. The most demanding part of a shochet's responsibility — ensuring the knife is in proper condition — has been taken off his hands. He has no effort to exert, and no incentive to do the shechita incorrectly, because doing it incorrectly provides him no benefit. Since he would just as readily do it correctly, and since he knows the laws, we can trust that he did. This is a striking ruling. We are speaking of a person who is a known regular violator of the laws of shechita — who eats non-shechted meat — and yet we are prepared to designate him a shochet, without supervision, provided only that the knife has been checked beforehand.

### Mumar Le'Te'avon


Aval lo badak ve'natan lo — but if the knife was not pre-checked and the responsibility was left to him, *lo yishchot* — he should not shecht le'chatchila, because checking a knife is an effort, and he might not be motivated to do it properly. Ve'im shachat — if he did shecht, bodek sakino acharav — you check the knife afterwards. The knife can only become worse in the process of shechita, never better — it can become duller or nicked, but it cannot become sharper or smoother on its own. So if the post-shechita inspection finds the knife in good condition, it was certainly in good condition before and during. Nimtza sakin yafeh — mutar le'echol mishchitato. Ve'im lav, assur le'echol mishchitato — and if the knife is not good afterwards, or if it cannot be located to be checked, the shechita is invalid. The unavailability of the knife for post-facto checking thus creates a serious problem that even direct supervision cannot remedy: if you do not have the knife, even if you observed the physical motions of the shechita perfectly, you cannot rule out the possibility that the knife had a nick — dilma be'sakin pguma ka'shachat.

### Post-Facto Knife Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁהוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִדְרְסוּ, וְשֶׁמָּא יַחֲלִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And it teaches: This is the *halakha* with regard to all people **except for a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, whose** slaughter is **not** valid **even after the fact.** The reason the Sages deemed such slaughter not valid is **lest** people in these categories **interrupt** the slaughter, **lest they press** the knife in the course of slaughter, **and lest they conceal** the knife beneath the windpipe or the gullet in the course of an inverted slaughter.

As in the previous readings, *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* are excluded — de'afilu be'di'avad nami lo. Even with a pre-checked knife, even with direct supervision, the concern for the cheresh, shoteh, and katan is not one of motivation or honesty but of technical performance. Shema yish'heh shema yidros ve'shema yachlid — they may have paused, applied downward pressure, or driven the knife laterally in an invalid manner, without knowing it.

### Cheresh Shoteh Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ – אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן – עֲלַהּ קָאֵי, ״וְאִם שָׁחֲטוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the clause in the mishna that follows: **And any of them who slaughtered** an animal and others see and supervise them, their slaughter is valid, **to which** case in the mishna is it referring? **If we say** that the reference is to the case **of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** since that is the *halakha* to which **it stands adjacent,** the *tanna* **should have** formulated the phrase: **And if they slaughtered,** instead of: And any of them who slaughtered.

The final phrase of the Mishnah — *vechulan sheshachatu* — poses the same difficulty as before. Ilima *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* — if it refers to the cheresh, shoteh, and katan, halo ka'amar, ve'im shachatu mibei lei — it should have said ve'im shachatu. Ella Yisrael mumar — so it must be referring to the mumar with direct supervision. But ai d'badak sakina ve'yahiv lei, ha amart shochet le'chatchila — if the knife was checked, Rav Ashi already said he may shecht le'chatchila even without direct supervision. And if the knife was not checked, then direct observation of his physical technique does not solve the knife problem: d'leita le'sakina, ki acherim ro'in oto mai havei — if the knife is not available to check, what does it matter that observers watched him? Kashya — this too remains difficult.

## Ravina: Expertise & Expe…

### Vechulan Difficulty Again

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָכִי קָתָנֵי – ״הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין״, הַכֹּל מוּמְחִין שׁוֹחֲטִין, מוּמְחִין וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מוּחְזָקִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said** in resolution of the apparent contradiction in the mishna **that this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Everyone slaughters,** i.e., **everyone** who is **an expert** in the *halakhot* of ritual slaughter **slaughters;** all **experts** are qualified to slaughter, **and** this is the *halakha* **even if they are not established** as accustomed to slaughter with a steady hand and without fainting.

Ravina now offers a fourth reading of the Mishnah, one that does not focus on unusual categories of people — tamei, Kuti, mumar — but on the general requirements that apply to any person who wishes to shecht. Ravina proposes two distinct criteria, each representing a separate version of his statement. The first is that of the mumcheh — expertise in the law. Ravina amar *hachi katani*: hakol shochtin, hakol mumchin shochtin — everybody may shecht who is mumcheh, expert in the laws of shechita. Ve'af al pi she'ein muchzakin — even without experience, provided that knowledge is established.

### Ravina I: Expertise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁיּוֹדְעִין בּוֹ שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹמַר הִלְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה, אֲבָל אֵין יוֹדְעִין בּוֹ שֶׁיּוֹדֵעַ לוֹמַר הִלְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה – לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, וְאִם שָׁחַט – בּוֹדְקִין אוֹתוֹ, אִם יוֹדֵעַ לוֹמַר הִלְכוֹת שְׁחִיטָה – מוּתָּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְאִם לָאו – אָסוּר לֶאֱכוֹל מִשְּׁחִיטָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where** people **know about him that he knows** and is able **to recite the *halakhot* of ritual slaughter. But if** people **do not know about him that he knows** and is able **to recite the *halakhot* of ritual slaughter, he may not slaughter** an animal *ab initio*. **And if he slaughtered** an animal, **one examines him; if he knows** and is able **to recite the *halakhot* of ritual slaughter** it is **permitted to eat** meat **from** what **he slaughtered, and if not,** it is **prohibited to eat from** what **he slaughtered.**

*B'meh devarim amurim* — when is this true? She'yodim bo she'yode'a lomar *hilchot shechitah* — when it is known that he can articulate the halachot of shechita, meaning he has been tested and demonstrated his knowledge. Aval ein yodim bo she'yode'a lomar *hilchot shechitah* — if his expertise has not been established, *lo yishchot* — he may not shecht.

Ve'im shachat — if he shechted without prior testing, bodkin oto — you test him after the fact. Im yode'a lomar *hilchot shechitah* — if he passes the test, mutar le'echol mishchitato. We assume he did not learn the laws in the last ten minutes, so passing the post-facto test is meaningful evidence that he knew the laws at the time of the shechita. Ve'im lav — if he fails the test or cannot be tested at all, assur le'echol mishchitato.

### Testing Mechanism

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חוּץ מֵחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא, שֶׁמָּא יִשְׁהוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִדְרְסוּ, וְשֶׁמָּא יַחֲלִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** And it teaches: This is the *halakha* with regard to all people **except for a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor, whose** slaughter is **not** valid **even after the fact.** The reason the Sages deemed such slaughter not valid is **lest** people in these categories **interrupt** the slaughter, **lest they press** the knife in the course of slaughter, **and lest they conceal** the knife beneath the windpipe or the gullet in the course of an inverted slaughter.

Chutz mecheresh shoteh ve'katan — ve'afilu be'di'avad nami lo. And here there is a notable twist: even if the cheresh, shoteh, or katan could somehow pass a knowledge test, their shechita remains invalid. Shema yish'eh shema yidros shema yachlid — for them, we are not merely concerned with what they know abstractly, but with what they are capable of doing in practice. Their age or disability makes it specifically likely that they will be unable to execute the shechita technically even if they can articulate the laws. This is a meaningful distinction: they might pass the exam in flying colors, yet still not be trusted to hold the knife.

### Cheresh Knowledge Gap

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ – אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן עֲלַהּ קָאֵי, וְאִם שָׁחֲטוּ מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the clause in the mishna that follows: **And any of them who slaughtered** an animal and others see and supervise them, their slaughter is valid, **to which** case in the mishna is this referring? **If we say** that the reference is to the case **of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** since that is the *halakha* to which **it stands adjacent,** the *tanna* **should have** formulated the phrase: **And if they slaughtered,** instead of: And any of them who slaughtered.

On Ravina's reading, the final phrase *vechulan sheshachatu* ahai — what does it refer to? Ilima *cheresh shoteh ve'katan* — it cannot mean them, because we were just discussing them. Ella she'ein mumchin — it refers to someone who has not been tested as an expert. Be'bodkin oto sagi — for him, post-facto testing suffices. But de'leika kaman de'livdekei — if he is no longer around to be tested, then direct observation of his shechita is sufficient. And here is an important principle: someone who was not tested beforehand or afterward is no worse than a cheresh, shoteh, or katan in this regard. As long as the shechita was directly observed and performed correctly, it stands. An implicit assumption throughout is that the act of shechita, when directly observed, yields sufficient information to determine whether it was done correctly — in combination, of course, with checking the knife.

Tosafot raises a sharp question here: the Gemara speaks of a case where the shochet cannot be found afterward. But it does not discuss a more extreme case — where the shochet is found and fails the test. Tosafot points out that the Gemara should have noted this even more obvious case: if direct observation of the shechita is what saves the situation when the person cannot be tested at all, then surely it would save the situation even when he was tested and failed. Tosafot does not fully resolve this, but the implication seems to be that direct observation works for all cases where the concern is knowledge-based — which would include the adult who does not know the laws — just as it works for the cheresh, shoteh, and katan.

### Vechulan Under Ravina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי, רָבִינָא אָמַר: הָכִי קָתָנֵי – ״הַכֹּל שׁוֹחֲטִין״, הַכֹּל מוּחְזָקִין שׁוֹחֲטִין, מוּחְזָקִין אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מוּמְחִין. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ לְפָנֵינוּ שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים וְלֹא נִתְעַלֵּף, אֲבָל לֹא שָׁחַט לְפָנֵינוּ שְׁתַּיִם וְשָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים – לֹא יִשְׁחוֹט, שֶׁמָּא יִתְעַלֵּף. וְאִם שָׁחַט וְאָמַר: ״בָּרִי לִי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְעַלַּפְתִּי״ – שְׁחִיטָתוֹ כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that **Ravina said** that **this** is what the mishna **is teaching: Everyone slaughters,** i.e., **everyone** who is **established** as accustomed to slaughter with a steady hand and without fainting **slaughters;** all those **established** concerning this are qualified to slaughter, **even** if it is **not** known if **they are experts. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said in a case **where they slaughtered before us two or three times and did not faint. But if he did not slaughter before us two or three times he may not slaughter** an animal *ab initio*, **lest he faint. And if he slaughtered** an animal **and said: It is clear to me that I did not faint, his slaughter is valid.**

Ika de'amri — others say a different version of Ravina's statement: hakol muchzakin shochtin — everybody who is experienced may shecht. Muchzakin af al pi she'ein mumchin — experience suffices even without formal knowledge testing. This is the inverse of the first version: where the first version said knowledge without experience is sufficient, the second says experience without verified knowledge is sufficient.

The Gemara is careful to explain what muchzak means here: *she'shachtu lefanenu shtei ve'shalosh pe'amim*, ve'lo nitalef — they have shechted before us two or three times and did not faint in the process. The concern about fainting is specific: if a shochet faints during the shechita, the interruption itself invalidates the shechita through the prohibition of shehiyah — pausing. So the requirement for witnessed prior experience is designed to establish that this person can see the shechita through without losing composure.

### Ravina II: Experience


Aval lo shachat lefanenu shtei ve'shalosh pe'amim — if prior experience has not been witnessed, *lo yishchot* — he should not shecht, shema yitalef — lest he faint. The concern is not only about the dramatic case of full unconsciousness; even a momentary loss of control or a reflexive recoil upon first confronting the act could cause an invalid pause or pressure.

### Experience Witnessed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכוּלָּן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ – אַהֵיָיא? אִילֵּימָא אַחֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן עֲלַהּ קָאֵי, ״וְאִם שָׁחֲטוּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, with regard to the clause in the mishna that follows: **And any of them who slaughtered** an animal and others see and supervise them, their slaughter is valid, **to which** case in the mishna is this referring? **If we say** that the reference is to the case **of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, and a minor,** since that is the *halakha* to which **it stands adjacent,** the *tanna* **should have** formulated the phrase: **And if they slaughtered,** instead of: And any of them who slaughtered.

Ve'im shachat ve'amar bari li she'lo nitalafti — if he went ahead and shechted and declares, "I know I did not faint," *shechitato kesheira* — his shechita is valid be'di'avad. We take his word for it. This is a considerable chiddush. Someone presents himself to shecht — we have not tested his knowledge, and we have not witnessed his prior experience — he shechts, and he reports that he performed correctly without fainting. Be'di'avad, we trust him. The underlying principle the Gemara will invoke momentarily is rov hametzuyim eitzel shechitah mumchin hein — most people who have the confidence to step forward and shecht actually know what they are doing. Nobody would typically approach a large animal with a knife unless they had at least some grounding in the laws and practice. This rov provides the baseline of reliability that makes the self-report meaningful.

Chutz mecheresh shoteh ve'katan — de'afilu be'di'avad nami lo, shema yish'hu shema yidresu ve'shema yachlidu. For them, the concern is not whether they fainted but whether they are capable of performing the technical act correctly at all — and that cannot be resolved through their own report.

Ve'chulan sheshachtu ve'acherim ro'in otan — and anyone who shechted with direct observation is valid. The same difficulty about this phrase recurs. Ilima cheresh, shoteh, ve'katan lo kai — we were discussing them. Ela ashe'eino munchzak — it is referring to someone who is not known to be experienced. Ve'amarta be'bari li sagi — his self-report would suffice if he were present. D'leita d'nishaylei — but if he is no longer around to report, direct observation suffices.

## Reconciliation & Debate

### Self-Report Be'diavad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ כְּרַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא לָא אָמְרִי, לְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ הָכָא עִיקָּר – אַדְּרַבָּה, הָתָם עִיקָּר, דִּבְקָדָשִׁים קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **All of** the other *amora'im* **did not say** a resolution to the apparent contradiction in the mishna **like** that of **Rabba bar Ulla,** who interprets the mishna as referring to the case of a ritually impure person; **according to that version that you said:** The mishna **here** is the **primary** source for the *halakha* of a ritually impure person who slaughtered a sacrificial animal, the other *amora'im* reject this interpretation because, **on the contrary,** the mishna **there** is the **primary** source, **as it is standing in** tractate *Zevaḥim*, which deals with **sacrificial** animals.

The Gemara now turns to explain why each Amora did not adopt the approaches of the others. Ravina and Rabbah bar Ulla — the latter having offered the first answer regarding tuma — did not use the readings of Abaye, Rava, and Rav Ashi because, as we noted, the phrase vechulan remained difficult in all of those readings. As for why the others did not say like Rabbah bar Ulla's tuma answer: le'hach lishna de'amart hacha ikkar — according to the version that says the tuma ruling is primarily located here, the response is addaraba hatam ikkar — on the contrary, it properly belongs in Zevachim where kodashim are discussed. And le'hach lishna de'amart hatam ikkar — according to the version that says the tuma ruling belongs in Zevachim and was brought here only incidentally — ve'hacha aidi de'tana tamei be'chullin tana nami tamei be'mukdashim — the deeper problem is that tamei be'chullin gufah lo itztaricha leh. There is no need for the Mishnah to address a tamei person shechting chullin at all. Even chullin she'na'asu al taharat kodesh — chullin treated with the purity standards of hekdesh — lav ke'kodesh dami. It does not attain the halakhic status of kodesh. There is no genuine prohibition for a tamei person to engage with it. The entire tuma framework is simply not relevant to chullin, which is why the others found that answer uninteresting and inapplicable.

### Tuma'ah Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוּלְּהוּ כְּרָבִינָא לָא אָמְרִי, לְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר: מוּמְחִין אִין, שֶׁאֵין מוּמְחִין לָא – רוֹב מְצוּיִים אֵצֶל שְׁחִיטָה מוּמְחִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **All of** the other *amora'im* **did not say** a resolution to the apparent contradiction in the mishna **like** that of **Ravina; according to that version that he said: Experts, yes,** may slaughter *ab initio*, but those **that** it is **not** known that they are **experts, no,** they may not slaughter *ab initio*, the other *amora'im* disagree because they hold that **the majority** of those **associated with slaughter are experts.** Therefore, even if it is not known whether they are experts, their slaughter is valid.

As for why the others did not adopt Ravina's readings — le'hach lishna de'amar mumchin in she'ein mumchin lo — according to the version that knowledge is required and must be verified: rov metzuyin eitzel shechitah mumchin hein. Most people who present themselves to perform shechita do in fact know the laws. It would be extraordinary for someone to step forward and attempt to shecht without any knowledge of what is required. We therefore operate with the presumption that knowledge exists and do not require formal testing to establish it. The other Amoraim agree that knowledge is essential — they simply do not agree that we must actively verify it. The presumption suffices.

### Ravina Rejection I

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהַךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר: מוּחְזָקִין אִין, שֶׁאֵין מוּחְזָקִין לָא – לְעִלּוֹפֵי לָא חָיְשִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **According to that version that** Ravina **said:** People who are **established** as accustomed to slaughter without fainting, **yes,** they may slaughter *ab initio*, but people who are **not established** as accustomed to slaughter without fainting, **no,** they may not slaughter *ab initio*, the other *amora'im* disagree because they hold that **we are not concerned for** the possibility of **fainting.**

Le'hach lishna de'amar muchzakin in she'ein muchzakin lo — according to the version that experience must be witnessed — le'allufei lo chashinan — we are not concerned about fainting. This is, frankly, the most surprising of the conclusions. It holds that someone who has mastered the laws of shechita may be handed a knife and entrusted to shecht an animal for the first time — with no prior observed practice — and his shechita is valid. The reasoning is that knowledge of the law implies practical competence. If someone truly knows what is required at every stage of the shechita, we trust that he will be able to execute it correctly. The concern about fainting or loss of composure is not applied to someone who is established as knowing the laws. This is a far-reaching chiddush: the knowledge criterion and the experience criterion are not independent — knowledge is held to entail practical ability, at least to the degree the halacha requires.

### Ravina Rejection II

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי לָא אָמַר כְּתַרְוַיְיהוּ, קָסָבַר: כּוּתִים גֵּרֵי אֲרָיוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi did not say** a resolution **like** that of **both of them,** Abaye and Rava, because **he holds: Samaritans are converts** who converted under duress due to the threat posed by **lions,** and their conversion is void; therefore, their halakhic status is that of a gentile, whose slaughter is not valid.

Rav Ashi did not adopt the approaches of Abaye or Rava regarding the Kuti: kasavar Kuti gerei arayot hein — he holds that Kutim are not genuine converts but gerei arayot, converts motivated by fear of the lions, as recorded in Yirmiyahu. They are therefore not Jewish. If they are not Jewish, they are categorically excluded from shechita regardless of supervision level, and the entire framework built around the Kuti's reliability is irrelevant.

### Rav Ashi on Kuti

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי לָא אָמַר כְּרַב אָשֵׁי, לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ הָא דְּרָבָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye did not say** a resolution **like** that of **Rav Ashi,** who interprets the mishna as referring to the case of a Jewish transgressor whose transgression is that he eats unslaughtered animal carcasses to satisfy his appetite, because **he does not hold that which Rava** said, that such a transgressor may slaughter *ab initio* if his knife is examined beforehand by someone reliable.

Abaye did not adopt Rav Ashi's reading about the mumar: lo svira lei haderava — he does not hold like Rava's ruling that pre-checking the knife suffices to render the mumar's shechita trustworthy. Abaye holds that a person who regularly eats non-kosher meat cannot simply be handed a pre-checked knife and trusted to shecht for others. The removal of the incentive through the pre-checked knife is insufficient in Abaye's view; direct supervision remains necessary.

### Abaye on Mumar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא רָבָא, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָמַר כִּשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ? לִדְבָרָיו דְּאַבָּיֵי קָאָמַר, וְלֵיהּ לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** as for **Rava, what is the reason** that **he did not say** a resolution **in accordance with his** own statement of ***halakha*** with regard to the slaughter of a transgressor and explain the mishna in the manner that Rav Ashi did? When Rava explained that the *tanna* in the mishna is referring to the slaughter of a Samaritan, **he stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Abaye** in order to resolve the difficulty that Abaye raised; **but he himself does not hold accordingly.**

Finally — ale Rava, mai tama lo amar kishmayse? — why did Rava himself not explain the Mishnah as being about the mumar, as Rav Ashi did, given that Rava was the one who stated the original ruling about the mumar and the pre-checked knife? The Gemara answers: lidvarei de'Abaye ka'amar velei lo svira lei — Rava was speaking polemically, responding to Abaye's position. Rava's argument about yotzei ve'nichnas was directed at Abaye, challenging his requirement for constant supervision in the Kuti case. Rava was not laying out his own preferred reading of the Mishnah in that context, but arguing against Abaye's framework. Had Rava been presenting his own reading of the Mishnah independently, he would have explained it as Rav Ashi did — in terms of the mumar and the pre-checked knife. In that reading he personally agrees, but his contribution to this sugya was a polemical one, responding to what he saw as an overreach in Abaye's supervision requirements.

What has emerged from today's daf is a comprehensive introduction to some of the central concerns of Masechet Chullin. Through the fiction of explaining the Mishnah's le'chatchila and be'di'avad structure, the Gemara has surveyed the formal requirements of the shechita implement, the question of who may shecht, the degree of supervision required to establish reliability, and the interplay between knowledge, experience, and presumption in determining who may be entrusted with the act of shechita.
