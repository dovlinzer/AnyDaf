# Niddah 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Niddah Blood & Tumah

### Daf Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' דַּם הַנִּדָּה, וּבְשַׂר הַמֵּת — מְטַמְּאִין לַחִין, וּמְטַמְּאִין יְבֵשִׁין. אֲבָל הַזּוֹב, וְהַנִּיעַ, וְהָרוֹק, וְהַשֶּׁרֶץ, וְהַנְּבֵלָה, (והשכבת) [וְשִׁכְבַת] זֶרַע — מְטַמְּאִין לַחִין, וְאֵין מְטַמְּאִין יְבֵשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The blood of a menstruating woman and the flesh of a corpse transmit impurity** by contact and by carrying when they are **moist, and** likewise **transmit impurity** when they are **dry. But** with regard to **the** gonorrhea-like **discharge of a *zav* [*ziva*], and the mucus and the saliva** of a *zav*, **and** the carcass of **a creeping animal, and an animal carcass, and semen,** all **transmit impurity** when they are **moist but do not transmit impurity** when they are **dry.**

Daf 55a opens a new *perek*, and while the transitions from topic to topic can be difficult to track at first glance, the underlying thematic logic is clear. The discussion has shifted away from the status of the woman as a *niddah* and toward a related but distinct question: the blood she produces, the menstrual blood that renders her a *niddah*, is itself a source of *tumah* for anyone who touches it. From there, the *Gemara* broadens its inquiry to cover other substances that are sources of *tumah*, examining each one with a two-part analysis: first, how do we know the substance is *tamei* at all, and second, in what state must it be in order to transmit *tumah*?

### Mishna Categories

The *Mishna* established that only two substances remain a source of *tumah* even when dried out: the blood of a *niddah* and the flesh of a corpse (*basar hamet*). All other substances on the list are only *tamei* when they are moist, not when they have dried. The *Gemara* proceeds through the list, treating each substance in turn.

## Basar Hamet

### Resh Lakish vs R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְשַׂר הַמֵּת, מְנָלַן? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אָמַר קְרָא ״לְכֹל טֻמְאָתוֹ״ — לְכׇל טְמָאוֹת הַפּוֹרְשׁוֹת מִמֶּנּוּ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״אוֹ בְעֶצֶם אָדָם אוֹ בְקָבֶר״ — אָדָם דּוּמְיָא דְּעֶצֶם: מָה עֶצֶם יָבֵשׁ, אַף כָּאן יָבֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And the flesh of a corpse** transmits impurity both when moist and when dry. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? **Reish Lakish said** that this is as **the verse states:** "Or whoever touches any creeping thing by which he may be made impure, or a man from whom he may be made impure, **from any impurity that he has"** (Leviticus 22:5). The term "from any" is an amplification, indicating that one may become impure **from any impurities that come from** a dead person, whether they are moist or dry. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that this *halakha* is derived from the verse: "And whoever touches in the open field one who is slain with a sword, or one who died, **or the bone of a man, or a grave,** shall be impure seven days" (Numbers 19:16). The verse indicates that the impurity of a dead **man** is **similar to** the impurity of **a bone: Just as a bone** is **dry, so too here,** with regard to the impurity of a corpse, it transmits impurity even when it is **dry.**

The *Gemara* begins with *basar hamet* — the flesh of a corpse — and asks: *minalan*, where do we know that the flesh of a dead body is itself *tamei*? The Torah's discussion of a *meit* speaks of the corpse as a whole; it is evident that one who touches a corpse or is under the same roof as one becomes *tamei*. But what is the source that individual portions of the body, specifically the flesh, are *tamei* as well?

Two *Amoraim* offer competing derivations. Resh Lakish cites the verse *l'chol tumato* — "for all of his impurities" — understood to mean that all things which separate from the body of a *meit* are also sources of *tumah*. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, derives it from the phrase *etzem adam* — "the bone of a man" — read as an equation: a bone is compared to the man himself. Just as a bone is *tamei* even when dry, so too any part of the body is *tamei* even in a dry state.

Critically, both *Amoraim* are not merely proving that flesh is *tamei*; they are simultaneously proving that it is *tamei* regardless of its state, whether moist or desiccated. Resh Lakish achieves this through the inclusive language of *l'chol tumato*, which encompasses the substance in any condition. Rabbi Yochanan achieves it by analogy to bone, which is inherently dry and yet *tamei*, establishing that dryness is no bar to *tumah*.

### Crumbling Flesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאִפְּרִיךְ אִפְּרוֹכֵי. מֵיתִיבִי: בְּשַׂר הַמֵּת שֶׁהוּפְרַךְ טָהוֹר! הָתָם דְּאַקְמַח וְהָוֵי עַפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between** the inferences of Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan? The Gemara answers that the practical difference **between them** is the case of a corpse which is so dry **that it crumbles.** Reish Lakish maintains it is impure, as the term "from any" indicates that a corpse transmits impurity in any form, whereas Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains it is ritually pure, as it is unlike a bone, which does not crumble. The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Reish Lakish from a *baraita*: **The flesh of a corpse that crumbled** is **ritually pure.** The Gemara answers that this is not difficult, as the *baraita* **there** is referring to where the flesh is so dry **that it has become** like **flour and is** therefore classified as **dust.**

The *Gemara* then asks: *mai beinaihu*, what practical difference does it make whether we derive the rule from *l'chol tumato* or from the analogy to bone? The answer is a case of *d'afri aprichei* — flesh so desiccated that it will crumble or shatter upon contact. On the one hand, *l'chol tumato* is maximally inclusive and would seem to encompass even such pulverized flesh. On the other hand, such crumbling flesh is no longer analogous to a bone, which is dry but solid; therefore, if one derives the rule solely from the bone analogy, this extreme case would not be *tamei*.

The *Gemara* then raises a challenge from a *Mishna* in *Ohalot*: *basar hamet shehuphrach* — flesh of a corpse that has been so thoroughly dried that it crumbles — is *tahor*. Does this not show that the degree of dryness does matter? The *Gemara* resolves this: the *Mishna* in *Ohalot* refers to flesh that has disintegrated to the point of becoming actual dust (*d'ikmach v'havei afra*). In that case, everyone agrees it is no longer considered flesh at all and is *tahor*. The dispute between Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan concerns only an intermediate case: flesh so dry it shatters, but not so far gone as to become dust.

### Body Parts & Intrinsicness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: כֹּל שֶׁבַּמֵּת מְטַמֵּא, חוּץ מִן הַשִּׁינַּיִם, וְהַשֵּׂעָר, וְהַצִּפּוֹרֶן, וּבִשְׁעַת חִבּוּרָן — הַכֹּל טָמֵא! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: דּוּמְיָא דְּעֶצֶם, מָה עֶצֶם שֶׁנִּבְרָא עִמּוֹ, אַף כֹּל שֶׁנִּבְרָא עִמּוֹ. וְהָאִיכָּא שֵׂעָר וְצִפּוֹרֶן, שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ עִמּוֹ וּטְהוֹרִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinions of both Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yoḥanan from a mishna (*Oholot* 3:3): **Everything that is in a corpse transmits impurity, except for the teeth, and the hair, and the nails.** This is the *halakha* only when these items are separated from the body, **but when they are attached** to the corpse they are **all impure.** According to Rabbi Yoḥanan teeth should transmit impurity because they are similar to bones, while according to Reish Lakish they should be included in the term "from any." **Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** Only items that are **similar to a bone** transmit impurity: **Just as a bone** is an item **that was created with him** at the time of birth, **so too, all** items that transmit impurity are those **that were created with him,** whereas teeth are not present at the time of birth. The Gemara asks: **But are there not** the cases of **hair and nails, which were created with him, and** yet the mishna states that **they are ritually pure?**

The *Gemara* next cites a separate *Beraita*: *kol shebames metamei* — everything in a dead body causes *tumah* — *chutz min hashinayim v'hase'ar v'hatzipporen*, with the exception of teeth, hair, and fingernails. However, *b'sha'at chiburan hakol tamei* — while they are still attached, everything is *tamei*; only when separated do these three fail to convey *tumah*.

What makes these three items special? The question is pressing particularly for Resh Lakish, whose source — *l'chol tumato* — is maximally inclusive and should, it would seem, encompass teeth, hair, and fingernails as much as anything else. Even Rabbi Yochanan's more limited derivation from *etzem adam* requires explanation, since one might have included these parts under the general category of body components.

### Gizo Machlif Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: דּוּמְיָא דְּעֶצֶם, מָה עֶצֶם שֶׁנִּבְרָא עִמּוֹ וְאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, אַף כֹּל שֶׁנִּבְרָא עִמּוֹ וְאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף. יָצְאוּ הַשִּׁינַּיִם — שֶׁלֹּא נִבְרְאוּ עִמּוֹ, יָצְאוּ שֵׂעָר וְצִפּוֹרֶן — שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּבְרְאוּ עִמּוֹ, גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Adda bar Ahava said** a different explanation: Only those items that are **similar to a bone** transmit impurity: **Just as a bone** is an item **that was created with him and its root does not renew** itself, i.e., if a bone is removed a new bone does not grow in its place, **so too, any item that was created with him and whose root does not renew** itself transmits impurity. **The teeth were excluded** from this category, **as they were not created with him.** The **hair and nails were excluded, as even though they were created with him** their **roots renew** themselves, since they grow again after they are cut off.

Rav Adda bar Ahava offers the controlling principle. He explains that even according to the inclusive derivation, there is a limiting criterion derived from the comparison to *etzem* — bone. Bone is *tamei* because it has two properties simultaneously: (1) it is created with the person (*nivra imo*), and (2) it does not replace itself (*ein gizo machlif*). For something to be an intrinsic part of the corpse that conveys *tumah* when separated, it must share both characteristics.

This phrase *gizo machlif* — literally, "its trunk replaces itself" — is familiar from the realm of *berachot*, where the same terminology distinguishes between trees whose trunk survives from year to year (*ein gizo machlif*, and one recites *borei pri ha-etz*) and plants that die back and regrow annually (*gizo machlif*, and one recites *borei pri ha-adamah*). Here, the principle is applied analogously to parts of the human body: a part that will regrow after being cut off is not considered truly intrinsic to the body in the same way that bone is.

Teeth are excluded because they are not created with a person — a newborn has no teeth — satisfying the first criterion for exclusion. But what of hair and fingernails? They are indeed created with a person. The *Gemara* therefore amends Rav Adda bar Ahava's ruling: the exclusion of hair and fingernails rests on the second criterion — *gizo machlif*. Cut hair grows back; cut fingernails grow back. Because they regenerate, they are not considered as integral to the body as bone or flesh, and therefore when separated they do not convey *tumah*.

### Skin's Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי עוֹר, דְּגִזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, וּתְנַן: הַגְּלוּדָה — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַכְשִׁיר וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִין. וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבָּנַן לָא קָפָסְלִי אֶלָּא דְּאַדְּהָכֵי וְהָכֵי שָׁלֵיט בַּהּ אַוֵּירָא וּמִתָה, וּלְעוֹלָם גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף. וּתְנֵינַן: אֵלּוּ שֶׁעוֹרוֹתֵיהֶם כִּבְשָׂרָן — עוֹר הָאָדָם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But isn't there** the case of **skin, whose root renews** itself, **and** this is as **we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 54a): In the case of an animal **whose hide was removed [*hageluda*], Rabbi Meir deems** it **kosher,** as the skin renews itself, **and the Rabbis deem** it an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*] and **unfit** for consumption. The Gemara explains: **And even the Rabbis deem** it **unfit only** due to the fact **that in the meantime,** between the removal of the old hide and the growth of the new one, **the air affects it and** as a result **it** will **die, but** they concede that **actually** the skin's **root renews** itself. **And** nevertheless **we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 122a): **These** are the entities **whose skin** has the same halakhic status **as their flesh: The skin of a** dead **person,** and the skin of a domesticated pig…and the skin of the hump of a young camel, etc.

With this principle established, the *Gemara* raises the question of skin (*or*): *de-gizo machlif* — the skin replaces itself, does it not? If you suffer a minor cut, the skin heals over. And yet, a *Mishna* elsewhere states that *or haadam*, the skin of a human being, is *tamei* like flesh. How can this be, if skin regrows and therefore should be classified alongside hair and fingernails as *tahor* when separated?

The *Gemara* marshals evidence from *Ohalot* that skin replaces itself. A *Mishna* regarding *treifot* records a debate about an animal that has been completely flayed (*hagluda*): Rabbi Meir rules it is still kosher (*machshir*), while the Sages rule it is a *treifa* (*poslin*). The *Gemara* initially suggests that even the Sages' ruling that it is a *treifa* does not mean they believe skin cannot regenerate; rather, they hold that while the skin would eventually grow back, the animal would be fatally exposed to the elements in the interim and would die first (*d'adehachi vahachi shalta beih avira u'mait*). Accordingly, everyone may agree that skin regenerates. Yet despite this, a separate *Mishna* includes human skin among things whose *or* is *kivsaran* — like their flesh — in terms of *tumah*. The question therefore stands: why is skin *tamei* if it grows back?

### Ulla on Skin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה — עוֹר אָדָם טָהוֹר, וּמַאי טַעְמָא אָמְרוּ טָמֵא? גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם עוֹרוֹת אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ שְׁטִיחִין לַחֲמוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **it was stated with regard to that** mishna that **Ulla said: By Torah law, the skin of** a dead **person is ritually pure. And what is the reason** the Sages **said** that it is **impure?** It is a rabbinic **decree lest a person should fashion rugs for a donkey** out of **the skins of his** deceased **father and mother.**

The *Gemara* cites a statement of Ulla that reframes the entire issue: *devar Torah, or adam tahor* — biblically speaking, the skin of a human being is actually *tahor*. The *Mishna*'s ruling that skin is *tamei* is therefore not a biblical *din* but a rabbinic enactment.

### Skin Gezerah

The reason for this rabbinic measure is startling. The *Gemara* explains: *gezeira shema ya'aseh adam orot aviv v'immo shetichin lachamor* — there was a concern that a person might take the skin of his father and mother and fashion it into a pad for a donkey's saddle. To forestall this grotesque possibility, the Sages declared human skin *tamei*, thereby ensuring that people would keep their distance from it rather than processing it for utilitarian purposes.

This explanation raises a natural question, which *Tosafot* addresses: is one not already prohibited from deriving benefit from a corpse (*issurei hanaah*)? *Tosafot* offers two answers. First, in certain contexts — particularly among *kohanim* and those who were especially rigorous about *tumah va-taharah* — the concern of *tumah* was in practice more effective as a deterrent than other prohibitions. The *Gemara* elsewhere notes similarly that the Sages declared *pigul* to be *tamei* because this was more likely to deter *kohanim* from the violation than the direct biblical prohibition itself; the psychology of *tumah* as a category of taboo carries a practical weight that transcends the formal severity of the prohibition. Second, *Tosafot* suggests that perhaps skin may not be included in the prohibition of deriving benefit from a corpse in the first place — paralleling the very question that is being discussed here about what constitutes an intrinsic part of the body for purposes of *tumah*.

### Skin as Leather

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אִיתְּמַר דְּעוּלָּא אַסֵּיפָא אִיתְּמַר, ״וְכוּלָּן שֶׁעִבְּדָן אוֹ שֶׁהִילֵּךְ בָּהֶן כְּדֵי עֲבוֹדָה טְהוֹרִין, חוּץ מֵעוֹר אָדָם״, וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה עוֹר אָדָם כִּי עִבְּדּוֹ טָהוֹר, וּמָה טַעַם אָמְרוּ טָמֵא? גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם עוֹר אָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ שְׁטִיחִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **when** the opinion **of Ulla was stated, it was stated with regard to the latter clause** of that mishna: **And** for **all of** these skins, in a case **where one tanned them or where one** spread them on the ground and **trod on them** for the same amount of time it takes **for tanning,** they are no longer classified as flesh and **are ritually pure, except for the skin of** a dead **person,** which maintains the status of flesh. **And** with regard to this **Ulla said** that **by Torah law the skin of** a dead **person, when one tanned it, is ritually pure. And what is the reason** the Sages **said** it is **impure?** It is a rabbinic **decree lest a person should fashion rugs** out of **the skin of his** deceased **father and mother.**

At this point, the *Gemara* clarifies where Ulla's statement was originally made. It was not a freestanding comment about skin's *tumah* status in general; rather, it was said in response to a specific clause at the end of the *Mishna* dealing with different types of skin. That *Mishna* states: *v'kullan she'ibdan o she'hilach bahen*, all those categories of skin that are *tamei* — including human skin — if one were to process them into leather or subject them to the treatment used to convert skin into leather (*kedei avodah*), they become *tahor*, since they have transformed into a new identity as leather. The single exception is *chutz me'or adam* — human skin, which even after processing remains *tamei*.

### Skin Regeneration

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי: הֲרֵי עוֹר, דְּאֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, וּתְנַן: וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִין, וַאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי מֵאִיר לָא קָא מַכְשַׁר אֶלָּא דְּקָרִיר בִּשְׂרָא וְחָיֵיא, וּלְעוֹלָם אֵין גִּזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף. וְאָמַר עוּלָּא: דְּבַר תּוֹרָה עוֹר אָדָם טָהוֹר!
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** a different version of the above discussion: **Isn't there** the case of **skin, whose root does not renew** itself, **and** this is as **we learned** in a mishna (*Ḥullin* 54a): In the case of an animal whose hide was removed, Rabbi Meir deems it kosher, **and the Rabbis deem** it a *tereifa* and **unfit** for consumption, as its skin does not regrow? The Gemara explains: **And even Rabbi Meir deems** it **fit only because the flesh cools and** the animal **heals, but** he concedes that **actually** the skin's **root does not renew** itself. Accordingly, the skin of a corpse should be impure. **But Ulla said: By Torah law, the skin of** a dead **person is ritually pure.**

The *Gemara* then presents an alternative version (*ika de'amri*) of the entire preceding discussion that reverses the initial premise. In this version, the *Gemara* begins by assuming that skin does *not* regenerate. How is this established? From the very same *treifa* debate — but now read differently. According to this version, the Sages who rule the flayed animal a *treifa* do so because they genuinely believe the skin will not grow back. And even Rabbi Meir, who rules it is not a *treifa*, does not hold that the skin regenerates; rather, he holds that the exposed flesh itself will toughen and harden (*de-kari bisra ve-chaya*), providing adequate protection for the animal to survive, even without skin regrowth.

If skin does not replace itself, then according to the first criterion it should be *tamei de'oraita* — biblically *tamei* — and the *Mishna*'s ruling presents no difficulty. The difficulty is now with Ulla's statement that skin is biblically *tahor*. The resolution is the same as before: Ulla's statement refers specifically to skin that has been processed into leather. Once converted to leather, human skin is fundamentally *tahor* — this is the biblical rule. Only the rabbinic *gezeira* keeps it *tamei* even after processing, to discourage people from converting their relatives' skin into leather goods. The full legal picture, then, is: untreated human skin is *tamei de'oraita* (since it does not regenerate), while processed human skin is *tahor de'oraita* but *tamei mide'rabbanan* due to the *gezeira*.

The *Gemara*'s willingness to pivot entirely between these two versions based on competing readings of the *treifot* debate is itself striking — empirical certainty about whether skin regenerates is effectively resolved by which way one reads a *tannaitic* dispute, rather than by direct observation, though it is noted that the practical reality likely depends on the extent of the wound: minor abrasions heal without scarring, while large areas of lost skin require grafting.

### Flesh Healing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי בָּשָׂר, דְּגִזְעוֹ מַחְלִיף, וְטָמֵא! אָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: בָּשָׂר נַעֲשֶׂה מְקוֹמוֹ צַלֶּקֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't there** the case of **flesh, whose root renews** itself, as when one's flesh is cut it regrows and heals, **and** yet it is **impure? Mar bar Rav Ashi says: Flesh** does not renew itself, as although when someone is cut his flesh regrows and heals, **a scar is formed** in **its place.**

The *Gemara* finally addresses the last apparent difficulty: flesh (*basar*) itself heals — if you lose a piece of flesh from a cut or wound, does it not grow back, placing it in the same category as hair and fingernails? Mar bar Rav Ashi responds: *basar na'asa mekomo tzaleket* — when flesh heals, the area becomes a scar. Healing and complete regeneration are not the same thing. The flesh does not truly replace itself; it leaves a scar, meaning the original tissue is gone and has not been fully reconstituted. This distinguishes flesh from hair and fingernails, which do fully replace themselves, and confirms that flesh remains in the category of *tamei* even when separated from the corpse.

## Zov Tumah

### Zovo Tamei Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הַזּוֹב. זוֹב מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא״ — לִימֵּד עַל הַזּוֹב שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But *ziva*** transmits impurity when moist, although not when dry. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that ***ziva*** transmits impurity? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that discusses the verse: "When any man has an issue out of his flesh, **his issue,** it is **impure"** (Leviticus 15:2). This **taught with regard to *ziva* that it is impure.**

Having completed the discussion of *basar hamet*, the *Gemara* turns to the second category of the *Mishna*: substances that are only *tamei* when moist, not when dried. The first of these is *hazov* — the penile discharge that renders a man a *zav*. The question is not whether the *zav* himself is *tamei* — that is explicit in the Torah — but whether the discharge itself is a source of *tumah* that makes others who come into contact with it *tamei*.

The *Beraita* cites the verse: *ish ish ki yihyeh zav mibsaro, zovo tamei hu* — "a man, a man who has a flow from his flesh, his flow is *tamei*" (Vayikra 15:2). This appears to state directly that the *zov* itself is *tamei*, not merely the man who experiences it.

### Kal Vachomer & Scapegoat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דִין הוּא: לַאֲחֵרִים גּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה, לְעַצְמוֹ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁגּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה לַאֲחֵרִים, וְהוּא עַצְמוֹ טָהוֹר. אַף אַתָּה אַל תִּתְמַהּ עַל זֶה, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁגּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה לַאֲחֵרִים — הוּא עַצְמוֹ טָהוֹר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא״ — לִימֵּד עַל הַזּוֹב שֶׁהוּא טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: Why is this derivation necessary? **Could this not** be derived through an *a fortiori* **inference?** Since *ziva* **causes impurity to others,** i.e., to the one who emitted the discharge, is it **not all the more so** that *ziva* **itself** is impure? The *baraita* replies that the case of the **scapegoat** brought on Yom Kippur **may prove** that this inference is not valid, **as it causes impurity to others,** i.e., the dispatcher of the scapegoat is rendered impure, **and** yet the goat **itself is pure. So too, you should not be surprised about this,** the discharge of *ziva*, **that even though it causes impurity to others,** *ziva* **itself is pure.** Therefore, **the verse states: "His issue,** it is **impure."** This **taught with regard to *ziva* that it is impure.**

But the *Beraita* itself raises a prior question: is a verse even necessary? Could one not derive the *tumah* of *zov* through a *kal vachomer*? If the *zov* causes the man to become *tamei*, surely the *zov* itself should be an *av hatumah*. The *Beraita* deflects this *kal vachomer* with the case of the *seir hamishtaleach*, the scapegoat sent off the cliff on Yom Kippur: the person who dispatches the goat becomes *tamei*, yet the goat itself is *tahor* to the touch. Here, then, is a case where something causes another to become *tamei* while itself remaining *tahor*. The same might be true of *zov*: the man becomes *tamei* through it, yet the discharge itself might be *tahor*. Hence the explicit verse *zovo tamei hu* is needed to establish that the *zov* is itself a source of *tumah*.

### Zov Tumah Severity

The verse teaches that *zov* is *tamei*, but knowing that *zov* is *tamei* does not yet tell us to what degree it is *tamei*. There are three ascending levels of *tumah* in this domain. At the lowest level, a substance is *tamei* only through touch, and the clothes one is wearing do not become *tamei* — analogous to a *sheretz* (dead rodent). At the middle level, when one carries the substance, both the person and his clothes become *tamei*, but touching it alone does not make one's clothes *tamei* — analogous to a *neveilah* (a carcass of an animal). At the highest level, even touching the substance makes both the person and his clothes *tamei* simultaneously — as is the case with a *zav*, a *zavah*, a *niddah*, and a *meit*.

### Zov vs. Semen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמַגָּע, אֲבָל בְּמַשָּׂא לָא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַשֶּׁרֶץ! אָמַר רַב בִּיבִי בַּר אַבָּיֵי: בְּמַגָּע לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא, דְּלָא גָּרַע מִשִּׁכְבַת זֶרַע, כִּי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא לְמַשָּׂא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** one **may say** that **this statement,** that the discharge of *ziva* transmits impurity, applies only to transmitting impurity **by contact. But** with regard to transmitting impurity **by carrying** it does **not** transmit impurity, **just as it is with regard to** the carcass of **a creeping animal,** which transmits impurity by contact but not by carrying. **Rav Beivai bar Abaye said: A verse was not necessary** to teach that *ziva* transmits ritual impurity **by contact, as** the *halakha* with regard to *ziva* is **no less** stringent **than** with regard to **semen,** which transmits impurity by contact. **When the verse was necessary,** it was **to** teach that *ziva* transmits impurity by **carrying.**

Rav Bibi bar Abaye responds: we do not need a verse to establish that touching *zov* makes one *tamei*, because that is already derivable from *shichvat zera* — seminal emission. The Torah explicitly states that semen is *tamei* to the touch. Since the *zov* of a single flow renders the man temporarily equivalent to a *baal keri* (one who has experienced a seminal emission), we know that the *zov* is at least as *tamei* as semen. Therefore the additional verse — *zovo tamei hu* — must be doing more work: it is elevating the *tumah* of *zov* beyond touch alone, to include the category of carrying (*masa*) as well.

But even this may not bring us to the highest level. Perhaps *zov* is in the middle category — *tamei* by *masa* (such that both person and clothes are affected when carrying it), but not by mere touch. That is analogous to the *neveilah*: carrying it makes both person and clothes *tamei*, but touching it only affects the person. How do we know *zov* rises to the highest level?

### Hazav Et Zovo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא: אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״הַזָּב אֶת זוֹבוֹ לַזָּכָר וְלַנְּקֵבָה״ — מַקִּישׁ זוֹבוֹ לוֹ, מָה הוּא לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בֵּין מַגָּעוֹ לְמַשָּׂאוֹ לְטַמֵּא אָדָם וּלְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים, אַף זוֹבוֹ כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This could **not enter your mind, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that ***Aḥerim* say,** with regard to the verse: "This is the law of him that has an issue…And of her who experiences the flow of her menstrual impurity, and **of one who emits his issue [*zovo*], in the case of a male or a female"** (Leviticus 15:32–33). The verse **juxtaposes** the impurity of **the issue of the *zav* to** the impurity of the *zav* **himself: Just as** with regard to **him you did not distinguish between** impurity transmitted by **contact with him and** impurity transmitted by **carrying him, as** in both cases the *zav* **transmits impurity to a person and transmits impurity to** his **garments, so too,** with regard to **the issue of the *zav*** the *halakha* is **the same.**

The *Gemara* cites a *Beraita* from the school of Rabbi Yishmael interpreting the verse: *Hazav et zovo*, "the one who flows his flow." The juxtaposition of the *zav* and his *zov* in this phrase creates a direct equation: just as the *zav* himself transmits *tumah* through both touch and carrying, and in both cases makes both the person and his clothes *tamei*, so too the *zov* is exactly as *tamei* as the *zav* himself. By this equation, *zov* reaches the highest level of *tumah* — an *av hatumah* that makes both person and clothes *tamei* even through touch alone.

### Both Verses Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּנָפְקָא לַן מֵ״הַזָּב אֶת זוֹבוֹ״, ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא״ לְמָה לִי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה מִדִּסְקַרְתָּא: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא שָׂעִיר הַמִּשְׁתַּלֵּחַ יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁגּוֹרֵם טוּמְאָה לַאֲחֵרִים וְהוּא עַצְמוֹ טָהוֹר. וְאִי מִשּׁוּם ״הַזָּב אֶת זוֹבוֹ״, לְמִנְיָינָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And now that we have derived** the impurity of the discharge of a *zav* **from** the verse: "And **of one who emits his issue"** (Leviticus 15:33), **why do I** need the verse: "When any man has an issue out of his flesh, **his issue,** it is **impure"** (Leviticus 15:2)? **Rav Yehuda of Diskarta said:** This verse **was necessary,** as it might **enter your mind to say** that the case of the **scapegoat may prove** that the discharge of a *zav* is not impure, **as** the scapegoat **causes impurity to others and** yet the goat **itself is pure.** Likewise, one might conclude that although the discharge of a *zav* causes impurity to others, *ziva* itself is pure. **And if** one were to dismiss this possibility, **due to** the verse: "And **of one who emits his issue [*zovo*],** in the case of a male or a female" (Leviticus 15:33), one could reply **that** it does not indicate the impurity of *ziva*. Rather, **it comes to** teach the **number** of emissions by which one becomes impure with the impurity of a *zav*.

This raises an immediate question: if the verse *Hazav et zovo* already establishes that *zov* is fully equivalent to the *zav* in *tumah*, why is the earlier verse *zovo tamei hu* still needed?

Rav Yehuda of Diskarta answers: without *zovo tamei hu*, one would never have concluded that the *zov* is itself *tamei* in the first place. The *seir hamishtaleach* objection applies: one could maintain that the *zov* makes the *zav tamei* while itself remaining *tahor*. In that case, the verse *Hazav et zovo* would carry an entirely different interpretive burden — it would be read not as a statement about the *tumah* of *zov*, but as the source for determining how many instances of *zov* one must experience and whether *oness* (flow due to external causes) counts. Specifically: *zov* (one flow) — *zovo* (two flows) — *Hazav et zovo l'zachar v'l'nekeivah* provides the basis for distinguishing between the first two flows (where only non-*oness* counts, the "male" standard) and the third flow (which must be brought as a *korban* even if due to *oness*, equated with the "female" standard). The entire verse, in this reading, is about quantifying and qualifying the flows, not about the *tumah* status of the *zov* as a substance.

Therefore *zovo tamei hu* is indispensable: it first establishes that the *zov* is itself *tamei*. Only once that is known can we return to *Hazav et zovo* and apply it to determine the *degree* of *tumah* — that the *zov* is as *tamei* as the *zav* himself. Both verses are necessary and neither is redundant.

No verse is needed to restrict *tumah* of *zov* to its moist state alone; this follows naturally from the description of the substance as a "flow." If there is no liquid flow, there is nothing being described by the verse, and dried residue would simply not be encompassed by the verse's terms.

## Spit & Phlegm

### Rok Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָרוֹק. רוֹק מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכִי יָרוֹק״ — יָכוֹל אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נָגַע? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בַּטָּהוֹר״ — עַד שֶׁיִּגַּע בַּטָּהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: And the mucus **and the saliva** of a *zav* transmit impurity when moist but not when dry. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive the ritual impurity of the **saliva** of a *zav*? The Gemara answers that this is derived **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And if** one who has an issue **spits** on the pure person, then he shall wash his clothes, and bathe himself in water, and he is impure until the evening" (Leviticus 15:8). One **might** have thought that the saliva transmits impurity even if the *zav* merely spat in the direction of a pure person, **despite** the fact **that it did not** come into **contact** with him. Therefore, **the verse states:** "And if one who has an issue spits **on the pure person,"** which teaches that **unless** the saliva comes into **contact with the pure person** he is not rendered impure.

The *Gemara* now turns to *rok* — the spit of a *zav* — which the *Mishna* lists among the substances that are *tamei* only when moist. As before, the *Gemara* first establishes the source of *rok*'s *tumah* and then its degree. The *Beraita* cites the verse: *vechi yarok hazav batahor vechibes begadav verachatz bamayim vetamei ad ha'arev* — "if the *zav* spits upon a *tahor* person, he shall wash his garments, immerse in water, and be *tamei* until evening" (Vayikra 15:8). This verse makes *rok* the most directly scripturally attested of all the fluids under discussion, since the Torah speaks about spitting explicitly.

### Spitting & Yibuma

One might have thought that the mere act of a *zav* spitting in someone's direction — without the spit actually making contact — renders that person *tamei*, on analogy with the *yibuma* case, where the act of spitting (*yareka b'fanav*, "she shall spit before him") does not require the spit to physically strike the *yavam*. The *Beraita* therefore reads *batahor* — "upon the *tahor*" — to indicate that actual contact is required: *ad sheyiga batahor*, it must touch the *tahor* person. *Tosafot* notes that once we establish from this verse that *rok* must actually make contact to convey *tumah*, one might revisit the *yibuma* case and argue the spit must hit there too — though the *Beraita* accepts the distinction.

### Phlegm Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא רוּקּוֹ, כִּיחוֹ וְנִיעוֹ וּמֵי הָאַף שֶׁלּוֹ מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְכִי יָרוֹק״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **I have** derived **only** that **his saliva** is impure. **From where** is it derived that **his phlegm, his mucus, and his nasal fluids** are also impure? **The verse states: "And if** one who has an issue **spits."** The superfluous "and" is an amplification, indicating that these substances also transmit impurity.

The verse teaches that *rok* itself is an *av hatumah*. But what about related secretions? *Keicho* and *niaro* — different types of phlegm (Rashi identifies them as phlegm produced by a deep cough versus a surface cough) — and *mei ha-af* — mucus — are not explicitly mentioned in the verse. The *Beraita* derives their inclusion from the prefix *v'* in *vechi yarok*, a form of expansion: anything similar to spit that fits the description of *mit'agel v'yotzei* (collecting in the body and then coming out) is included.

### Rok Severity

Having established that *rok* is *tamei*, the *Gemara* proceeds through the same three-level analysis used for *zov*. Perhaps *rok* is only *tamei* at the lowest level — touch only, like a *sheretz*?

### Drashot Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵימַר: הָנֵי מִילֵּי בְּמַגָּע, אֲבָל בְּמַשָּׂא — לָא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַשֶּׁרֶץ! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָמַר קְרָא: ״בַּטָּהוֹר״ — מַה שֶּׁבְּיַד טָהוֹר טִמֵּאתִי לָךְ. וְאֵימָא: בְּמַשָּׂא מְטַמֵּא אָדָם וּבְגָדִים, בְּמַגָּע אָדָם לְטַמֵּא בְּגָדִים לָא לְטַמֵּא, מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַמַּגַּע נְבֵלָה! אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, וְכֵן תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אָמַר קְרָא ״בַּטָּהוֹר״ — טׇהֳרָה שֶׁטִּהַרְתִּי לְךָ בְּמָקוֹם אַחֵר, טִמֵּאתִי לְךָ כָּאן, וְאֵיזֶה זֶה? מַגַּע נְבֵלָה. וְאֵימָא כְּמַשָּׂא דְּשֶׁרֶץ! אִם כֵּן נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא ״בָּאָדָם״, מַאי ״בַּטָּהוֹר״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And** yet one **may say** that although the saliva of a *zav* transmits impurity both by carrying and by contact, nevertheless there is the following difference between them: **By carrying** the saliva of a *zav*, **it transmits impurity** to **a person and** to his **garments,** but **by contact,** although it **transmits impurity** to the **person** who touches it, **it does not transmit impurity** to his **garments, just as is** the *halakha* **with regard to contact** with an unslaughtered **animal carcass.** **Reish Lakish said, and so** too **the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught,** that **the verse states:** "And if one who has an issue spits **on the pure person."** The term "on the pure person" teaches that a case of **purity,** i.e., that **which I deemed pure for you in a different case, I have deemed impure for you here. And which** case **is this? This is** referring to **contact** with **an animal carcass,** which renders only the one touching it impure, but not his garments, whereas the saliva of a *zav* renders them both impure. The Gemara objects: **But** one can **say** that the case deemed pure elsewhere but deemed impure here is that **of carrying** the carcass of **a creeping animal.** Perhaps the verse is teaching that whereas a creeping animal does not transmit impurity by carrying, the saliva of a *zav* does transmit impurity by carrying. By contrast, contact with the saliva of a *zav* renders only the one touching it impure, but not his garments, as is the *halakha* with regard to an animal carcass. The Gemara responds: **If that is so, let the verse write:** If one who has an issue spits **on the person. What** is the reason the verse states specifically: **"On the pure person"? Conclude two** conclusions **from it,** both that the saliva of a *zav* transmits impurity by carrying and that contact with the saliva renders impure both the person and his garments.

Resh Lakish, in the name of the school of Rabbi Yishmael, derives from the word *batahor* in the verse a series of escalating conclusions. First, the verse uses *batahor* rather than *b'adam*, which would have been the natural phrasing if the Torah only meant to say the spit must hit the person directly. The prefix *b-* in *batahor* is read to mean "on something belonging to the *tahor* person" — i.e., something he is holding or carrying. This establishes that even if the spit hits only an object the *tahor* person is carrying, that person becomes *tamei* — moving *rok* to the middle level where *masa* is sufficient.

But the verse goes further: by using the word *batahor* — literally "on the *tahor* one" — it invokes the concept of *taharah* in a context where *tumah* results, signaling that even a case where one would normally be *tahor* is here made *tamei*. The *Gemara* identifies this as the case of *maga neveilah*: normally, touching a *neveilah* makes only the person *tamei*, not his clothes. Here the verse teaches that even this level of contact — mere touching, as opposed to carrying — will make both the person and his clothes *tamei*. This elevates *rok* to the highest level of *tumah*.

The *Gemara* confirms: *mai batahor shma mina tarti* — from *batahor* we derive two things simultaneously: both the *masa* rule (that even carrying an object struck by the spit conveys *tumah*) and the highest-level rule (that even touching makes clothes *tamei*). This is a somewhat compressed derivation, and the *Gemara* resolves the apparent redundancy by noting that these two conclusions are extracted together from the single term.

As with *zov*, the moist-state requirement for *rok* needs no explicit verse; the substance is inherently liquid by definition and the verse would not encompass dried residue.

### Mucus Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵי הָאַף. מַאי מֵי הָאַף? אָמַר רַב: בְּנִגְרָרִין דֶּרֶךְ הַפֶּה, לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לְמֵי הָאַף בְּלֹא צִחְצוּחֵי הָרוֹק. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּנִגְרָרִין דֶּרֶךְ הַחוֹטֶם. אַלְמָא קָסָבַר: מַעְיָין הוּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא רַבְּיֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The aforementioned *baraita* teaches: **And the nasal fluids** of a *zav* also transmit impurity. The Gemara asks: **What** are these **nasal fluids? Rav says:** This is referring to fluids **that are emitted via the mouth** of a person. They are impure **because** it is **impossible for the nasal fluids** to flow through the mouth **without** containing **traces of saliva,** which are impure. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The *baraita* is referring **even** to fluids **that are emitted via the nose. Evidently,** Rabbi Yoḥanan **maintains** that nasal fluids **are** categorized as **a flow** of bodily fluids, **and the Merciful One included it** among the impure bodily fluids of a *zav*, by the amplification: "And if one who has an issue spits."

The *Gemara* pauses on the case of *mei ha-af* — mucus — which appears in the *Beraita* but is conspicuously absent from the *Mishna*'s list of the nine fluids of a *zav*, as will become apparent. Rav limits the *tumah*-conveying status of mucus to a specific circumstance: *b'nigrar derech ha-peh* — when it drains through the mouth, i.e., when nasal secretions drain into the back of the throat and are expelled through the mouth. In that case, Rav explains, it is *tumah*-bearing not because the mucus itself is a *mayan* (wellspring of *tumah*), but because *lefi she-ee efshar l'mei ha-af b'lo tzichtzuchei ha-rok* — mucus that comes out through the mouth cannot come without traces of saliva (*rok*) mixed in. The *tumah* of the mucus in this case is effectively the *tumah* of the *rok* it carries.

### R. Yochanan vs. Rav

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu v'nigran derech ha-chotem* — even when mucus comes out through the nose, it is *tamei*, because *alma kasavar mayan hu* — he holds that mucus is itself a genuine *mayan*, an independent source of *tumah* for a *zav*, not merely a vehicle for *rok*.

### Tears Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב, נַחְשׁוֹב נָמֵי דִּמְעַת עֵינוֹ! דְּאָמַר רַב: הַאי מָאן דְּבָעֵי דְּלִסְתַּמֵּיה (לְעֵינֵיהּ) — לִיכְחוֹל מִגּוֹי. וְלֵוִי אֲמַר: הַאי מָאן דְּבָעֵי דְּלֵימוּת — לִיכְחוֹל מִגּוֹי. וְאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר גּוּרְיָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַב דְּלָא אָמַר הָא ״מַאן דְּבָעֵי דְּלֵימוּת״, הוֹאִיל וְיָכוֹל לְגוֹרְרָן וּלְהוֹצִיאָן דֶּרֶךְ הַפֶּה? וְרַב: נְהִי דְּזִיהֲרָא נָפֵיק, דִּמְעֲתָא גּוּפַאּ לָא נָפֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to **Rav,** who maintains that nasal fluids are impure because they contain traces of saliva, this is difficult: **Let** the mishna **also count** among the impure bodily fluids of the *zav* **the tears of his eye, as Rav said: He who desires to blind his eye should have a gentile paint [*likhḥol*]** his eye, as the gentile is apt to add a substance that causes blindness. **And Levi said: He who desires to die should have a gentile paint** his eye, as the gentile is apt to add poison to the paint. The Gemara continues: **And Rav Ḥiyya bar Gurya said: What is the reasoning of Rav, that he did not say** like Levi: **He who desires to die** should have a gentile paint his eye? Rav did not say this because he maintains one can prevent the poisonous substances from killing him, **since he can suck** the poisonous substances into his mouth by inhaling **and emit them via the mouth.** This indicates that the fluids of the eye can also be emitted through the mouth, and if so, the tears of a *zav* should likewise transmit impurity. The Gemara answers: **And Rav** would say that **although the poison [*dezihara*]** placed in one's eye can **exit** the body via the mouth, **the tears themselves do not exit** the body through the mouth.

Given Rav's position, the *Gemara* asks: if Rav's rule is that mucus is *tamei* only when it passes through the mouth (because of admixed *rok*), why does he not also mention tears (*dimat eino*) — tears can also drain through the nasal passages into the mouth? The *Gemara* resolves this by citing Rav's own view: *hai man d'ba'i listmiah le'einei* — Rav believed that poison inserted through the eye could be expelled by draining it out through the mouth. This reflects an assumption that the tear ducts can serve as a channel to the mouth. However, the *Gemara* clarifies: Rav distinguished between foreign substances (like poison) that might be pulled through this channel, and the tears themselves. *Nehi d'zhiri nafik, dimato gufah lo nafik* — while foreign fluids inserted into the eye might be extractable through the mouth, the tears themselves do not flow that way. Therefore Rav did not mention tears: they do not pass through the mouth, they carry no *rok*, and by Rav's reckoning they are not *tamei* as a *mayan*.

## Nine Fluids

### Three by Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: תִּשְׁעָה מַשְׁקֵה הַזָּב הֵן — הַזֵּיעָה, וְהַלֵּיחָה סְרוּחָה, וְהָרֶיעִי — טְהוֹרִין מִכְּלוּם; דִּמְעַת עֵינוֹ, וְדַם מַגֵּפָתוֹ, וַחֲלֵב הָאִשָּׁה — מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאַת מַשְׁקִין בִּרְבִיעִית; אֲבָל זוֹבוֹ, רוּקּוֹ, וּמֵימֵי רַגְלָיו — מְטַמְּאִין טוּמְאָה חֲמוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: With regard to the above dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan, **come** and **hear** a *baraita*: **There are nine** types of **fluids** of **the *zav*. The sweat, the foul secretion** from an infected wound, **and the excrement are pure from any** form of impurity. The **tears** that emerge **from his eye,** the **blood from his wound, and the milk of a woman** who is a *zava* all **transmit the ritual impurity of liquids,** i.e., they render food and liquids impure, as other impure liquids do, in a situation **where** there is at least **a quarter**-*log* of the fluid. **But his *ziva*, his saliva, and his urine** all **transmit a severe** form of **ritual impurity.**

The *Gemara* now presents a comprehensive classification of the nine fluids (*tisha mashkin*) of a *zav*, organized into three groups of three based on their *tumah* status. This classification clarifies the full landscape of which bodily fluids are *tamei* and to what degree.

### Non-Mayanot

The first category comprises three fluids that are entirely *tahor*: *haze'a* (sweat), *leicha serucha* (rancid or putrid liquid, identified by some as pus), and *ha-re'i* (excrement, or more liquid discharge). These three are *tehorin miklum* — completely *tahor* — for two independent reasons. First, they are *she'einan mayanot*: they do not collect and then emerge as a concentrated flow; they are not of the type described as *mit'agel v'yotzei*. Second, and independently, they are not considered *mashkin* — liquids — at all in the halachic sense, since drinking and liquids are never associated with these substances in any halachic context. Because they fail on both counts, they convey no *tumah* whatsoever.

### Liquids Not Mayanot

The second category comprises three fluids that are considered *mashkin* (liquids) but are not *mayanot* (wellsprings of *tumah*): *dimat eino* (tears), *dam agpato* (blood from a wound), and *chalav isha* (milk of a nursing woman who is a *zavah*). These convey *tumah* only in the sense that any liquid does when touched by a *zav* or *zavah* — they become a *rishon l'tumah*. Furthermore, this *tumah* applies only when there is a minimum measure of *reviit* (approximately 3.5 ounces). They are not themselves *mayanot* because they do not arise through the process of *mit'agel v'yotzei* — tears flow immediately from the eye, wound blood flows immediately from the wound, and milk, while it does accumulate, is debated; the *Gemara* notes the question without resolving it fully, suggesting that milk perhaps does not flow autonomously in the same way.

### True Mayanot

The third and highest category comprises three fluids that are genuine *mayanot* and are themselves *av hatumah*: *zovo* (the discharge itself), *roko* (his spit), and *mei raglav* (his urine). These three convey *tuma chamura* — the severest form of *tumah* — making both person and clothes *tamei* even through touch alone.

### Spit Subcategories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִילּוּ ״מֵי הָאַף״ לָא קָתָנֵי. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב, לָא קָתָנֵי דְּלָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ לְמִתְנֵי, זִימְנִין דְּאָתֵי דֶּרֶךְ הַפֶּה, וְזִימְנִין דְּאָתֵי דֶּרֶךְ הַחוֹטֶם, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לִיתְנֵי. וּלְטַעְמָיךְ, ״כִּיחוֹ וְנִיעוֹ״ מִי קָתָנֵי? אֶלָּא תְּנָא רוֹק, וְכֹל דְּאָתֵי מֵרִבּוּיָא. הָכָא נָמֵי תְּנָא רוּקּוֹ, וְכֹל דְּאָתֵי מֵרִבּוּיָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The *baraita* lists many substances, **and yet it does not teach** that **nasal fluids** are impure. **Granted, according to** the opinion of **Rav,** the *tanna* of the *baraita* **does not teach** that nasal fluids are impure, **as he could not teach it categorically,** since **sometimes** the nasal fluid **comes via the mouth,** and is impure, **and sometimes it comes via the nose,** and is not impure. **But according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan, let** the *tanna* **teach** that nasal fluids are impure. The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, does** this *tanna* **teach** that **his phlegm and his mucus** are impure? **Rather, he taught** that **saliva** is impure, **and** this includes the impurity of **all** substances **that are derived from** its **amplification,** e.g., his phlegm and his mucus. **Here too,** with regard to nasal fluids, the *tanna* **taught** the *halakha* of **his saliva, and** this includes **all** substances **that are derived from** its **amplification,** including nasal fluids.

A careful reader will notice that the list of nine fluids does not include *kicho*, *niaro*, or *mei ha-af* — the phlegm types and mucus discussed earlier in the *Gemara*. The *Gemara* explains: *tana roko v'chol d'asi meiribuiya* — the list says "his spit" and subsumes under that heading everything derived from the expansive *drashot* on the verse about spit. Phlegm (*kicho* and *niaro*) and, according to Rabbi Yochanan, mucus (*mei ha-af*), are halachically subsumed under *rok* by virtue of being derived from the same verse. They are not enumerated separately because they are extensions of the *rok* category, not independent entries.

### Drinking Verses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּמְעַת עֵינוֹ — דִּכְתִיב ״וַתַּשְׁקֵמוֹ בִּדְמָעוֹת שָׁלִישׁ״. וְדַם מַגֵּפָתוֹ — דִּכְתִיב ״וְדַם חֲלָלִים יִשְׁתֶּה״. מָה לִי קַטְלֵיהּ כּוּלֵּיהּ, מָה לִי קַטְלֵיהּ פַּלְגֵיהּ. חֲלֵב הָאִשָּׁה — דִּכְתִיב ״וַתִּפְתַּח אֶת נֹאוֹד הֶחָלָב וַתַּשְׁקֵהוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that in the case of a *zav* or *zava*, the tears of their eyes and the blood from their wounds, as well as the milk of a *zava*, all transmit the ritual impurity of liquids. The Gemara cites the source for these cases of impurity: **The tears of his eye** are classified as a liquid, **as it is written:** "You have fed them with the bread of tears, **and given them tears to drink in great measure"** (Psalms 80:6). **And** the **blood from his wound** is classified as a liquid, **as it is written: "And he shall drink the blood of the slain"** (Numbers 23:24). Although the verse is referring to the blood of a dead person, and not to the blood of a wound, **what** difference does it make **to me** if one **killed all of him,** and **what** difference does it make **to me** if one **killed part of him,** i.e., wounded him? With regard to the **milk of a woman,** it too is classified as a liquid, **as it is written: "And she opened the bottle of milk, and gave him to drink"** (Judges 4:19).

For the middle category — the three fluids that are *mashkin* but not *mayanot* — the *Gemara* provides the scriptural basis for calling each of them a *mashke* (liquid), since halachically a substance is considered a *mashke* only if it is associated with the act of drinking. Tears: the verse in *Tehillim* speaks of being made to *drink* tears — *v'tashkem bidmaot shalish* — associating tears with drinking. Blood from wounds: the verse says *v'dam chalalim yishteh* — "and the blood of the slain he shall drink" — and since we derive that a wound is comparable to a partial death (*mah li kulei mah li palgei*), wound blood is covered. Milk: the verse in *Shoftim* records Yael giving Sisera milk to drink — *vatiftach et nod ha-chalav vatashkehu* — establishing milk as a *mashke* for this purpose. In each case, an association with drinking in scriptural language is sufficient to place the fluid in the category of *mashke*, even if it is not ordinarily consumed as a beverage.

### Urine Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵימֵי רַגְלָיו — מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא... וְזֹאת״ — לְרַבּוֹת מֵימֵי רַגְלָיו לְטוּמְאָה. וַהֲלֹא דִּין הוּא: וּמָה רוֹק הַבָּא מִמְּקוֹם טׇהֳרָה טָמֵא, מֵימֵי רַגְלָיו הַבָּאִים מִמָּקוֹם טָמֵא — אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיְּהוּ טְמֵאִין? דָּם הַיּוֹצֵא מִפִּי הָאַמָּה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁבָּא מִמָּקוֹם טָמֵא וְטָהוֹר. אַף אַתָּה אַל תִּתְמַהּ עַל זֶה, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁבָּא מִמְּקוֹם טוּמְאָה יִהְיֶה טָהוֹר. תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זוֹבוֹ טָמֵא... וְזֹאת״ — לְרַבּוֹת מֵימֵי רַגְלָיו לְטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* teaches that the urine of a *zav* transmits a severe form of ritual impurity, as do his *ziva* and his saliva. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive that the **urine** of a *zav* is impure? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When any man has an issue out of his flesh, **his issue,** it is **impure. And this** shall be his impurity" (Leviticus 15:2–3). The term "and this" comes **to include his urine with regard to** a severe form of **ritual impurity.** The *baraita* asks: **And could this not be** derived through an *a fortiori* **inference? If saliva, which comes from a place of purity,** i.e., one's mouth, whose secretions are not usually impure, is nevertheless **impure,** then with regard to **his urine, which comes from a place of impurity,** the same place that emits *ziva*, **is it not logical that it should be impure?** The *baraita* answers: The case of **blood that issues from the opening of the penis may prove** that this inference is invalid, **as** the blood **comes from a place of impurity and** yet **it is pure. Likewise, you should not be surprised about this,** the urine of a *zav*, **that even though it comes from a place of impurity it should be pure.** Therefore, **the verse states:** "When any man has an issue out of his flesh, **his issue,** it **is impure. And this** shall be his impurity" (Leviticus 15:2–3). The term "and this" comes **to include his urine with regard to** the severe form of **ritual impurity.**

Urine (*mei raglav*) belongs to the highest category but requires its own derivation since it is not derived from the verses about *zov* or *rok*. The *Beraita* cites: *zovo tamei v'zot tihiyeh tumato* — "his flow is *tamei*, and this shall be his *tumah*." The phrase *v'zot tihiyeh tumato* adds another category of *tumah* to the *zav* beyond the *zov* itself, and this is interpreted as a reference to urine — the other primary excretion from the same area of the body.

### Urine Kal Vachomer

The *Beraita* raises the question: could one not derive the *tumah* of urine through a *kal vachomer*? Spit, which comes from the mouth — a place of *tahara* — is *tamei*; surely urine, which comes from the same place as the *zov* — an area already associated with *tumah* — should certainly be *tamei*. The *Beraita* deflects this *kal vachomer* with a counter-example: blood that exits from the penis (*dam hayotzei mipi ha'ama*) is *tahor*, despite coming from the same source as *zov*. If blood from that area can be *tahor*, there is no automatic inference that urine must be *tamei* simply by virtue of its origin. Hence the explicit verse *zovo tamei v'zot* is necessary to include urine in the *tumah* of the *zav*.

It is worth noting that this derivational structure — specific verses for *zov*, specific verses for *rok* and its subsets, and a specific verse for urine — raises a broader point. The conceptual framework of *mit'agel v'yotzei* may describe the *character* of the substances that qualify as *mayanot*, but it is not itself the operative halachic mechanism. What places each fluid in the highest category of *tumah* is ultimately its explicit scriptural derivation. The absence of a clear verse for mucus may thus be as significant as any conceptual difference between mucus and the other substances — and may explain why the *Gemara* does not fully resolve the debate between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan on that point.
