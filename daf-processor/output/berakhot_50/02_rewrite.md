# Berakhot 50 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Nevarech vs. Barchu

### Shmuel's Principle

Today's daf begins at the very bottom of 49b, where the *Gemara* takes up the discussion that followed the *Mishnah*'s treatment of *zimun*. The *Mishnah* had established the different texts for *zimun* — the distinction between *nevarech* and *barchu*, between groups of three, ten, one hundred, and so on — with increasing appellations of God for larger assemblages. That, as we will see, was the position of Rabbi Yose HaGelili, while Rabbi Akiva held that once ten is reached, the text remains constant regardless of size.

The *Gemara* opens with a statement from *Shmuel*: *le'olam al yotzi adam atzmo min ha-klal* — a person should never exclude himself from the community. This is a striking first principle, with implications well beyond the laws of *zimun*. The *Mishnah*, however, seems to present a difficulty: *bishlosha vehu omer barchu* — when three people eat together, the leader says *barchu*, which is addressed to the others and explicitly excludes the speaker. Does this not contradict *Shmuel's* principle?

### Gemara's Resolution

The *Gemara* resolves the tension by reinterpreting the *Mishnah*: *af barchu* — the *Mishnah* means that even *barchu* is acceptable, but it is not the ideal formulation. *Umikol makom nevarech adif* — nevertheless, *nevarech* is better, because it includes the speaker in the blessing.

The *Gemara* then adduces support from a *braita* cited in the name of Rav Adda bar Ahava, quoting Rav Yannai: if one has six people, they may divide into two groups of three. This is permitted up until the threshold of ten, at which point division is no longer allowed. Now, the *Gemara* reasons: *iye amart nevarech adif* — if *nevarech* is at least as good as *barchu*, then dividing six into two groups of three makes sense, since neither group loses anything by moving from a potential *barchu* to a *nevarech*. But if *barchu* were actually superior, why would you divide and lose the opportunity to say *barchu*? The fact that division is permitted therefore demonstrates that *nevarech* is at least equivalent. *Shema mina nevarech adif*.

It is worth noting that the word *adif* here does not necessarily mean "better" in an absolute sense. *Tosafot* reads *adif* as meaning "equivalent," while *Rashi* goes further: once you establish equivalence, and you add *Shmuel's* principle that one should not separate oneself from the community, it follows that *nevarech* is genuinely preferable. The *Gemara*'s proof from the permissibility of dividing six into two groups establishes at minimum that *nevarech* is no worse — and *Shmuel's* principle then tips the balance in its favor.

### Braita Support

The *Gemara* then cites a *braita* that states directly: *bein she'amar barchu bein she'amar nevarech, ein tofsin oto al kach* — whether one said *barchu* or *nevarech*, one does not "jump on him" for it, meaning neither formulation is grounds for correction. This language of equivalence seems to somewhat soften the earlier suggestion that *nevarech* is superior, though it certainly confirms that *nevarech* is not inferior.

The *braita* then adds: *vehanidkanim tofsin oto al kach* — the nitpickers do jump on him. The commentary on the side records an interesting philological note: the variant reading *ha-nikranim*, explained via an Arabic cognate as a term for someone who pecks and picks at minor details, like a rooster scratching through a garbage heap. The word, in other words, is simply a colorful term for a nitpicker. According to this reading, the *braita* is actually trivializing any preference between *barchu* and *nevarech* — it is only the nitpickers who make something of it. At the very least, *nevarech* is certainly not inferior, and in light of *Shmuel's* principle, it is the preferred formulation.

It is worth noting in passing that the question of why the *Shulchan Aruch* uses *barchu* in certain contexts, and why the familiar Aramaic formulation *hav lan nivareich* is used in other settings, deserves further investigation — the *Gemara* here does not address those distinctions directly.

## Talmidei Chachamim Tests

### Uvetuvo Debate

The *Gemara* continues by citing the *Tosefta*: *umibirkasav shel adam nikkarim talmid chacham hu im lav* — from the way a person recites blessings, one can tell whether he is a *talmid chacham* or not. This was an era before fixed *siddurim*, when the texts of *brachot* were not yet uniformly standardized, and meaningful variation existed. The *Gemara* then offers a series of examples that distinguish learned from unlearned formulations.

The first example: if one says *uvetuvo chayinu* — "and through His goodness we have lived" — that is the formulation of a *talmid chacham*. If one says *umituvo chayinu* — "from some of His goodness we have lived" — that is the formulation of a *bur*, an ignoramus. The difference lies in the preposition: *uvetuvo* expresses that one's entire life flows from God's goodness without qualification, while *umituvo* implies that one has received only a portion of God's beneficence, suggesting an incomplete or insufficiently expansive gratitude.

The *Gemara* raises a challenge from Abbaye to Rav Dimi: the verse states *umibirchasecha yevoreich beis avdecha le'olam* — "and from Your blessing let the house of Your servant be blessed forever." That verse uses the partitive *mi-*, implying not all but some of God's blessing. The *Gemara* responds: *beshe'eila shani* — in the context of a request, the phrasing is different. When petitioning God, one approaches in humility and asks for what one might receive. *Rashi* frames this beautifully: one should ask *k'ani ha'omeid al ha-pesach*, like a poor person standing at the door, who does not raise his head and make grandiose demands.

But even in the context of petition, the *Gemara* cites the verse *harcheiv picha va'amaleihu* — "open your mouth wide and I will fill it" — which suggests one should request maximally. The *Gemara* resolves this by saying that expansive requests are appropriate for Torah: *hahu b'divrei Torah kesiv*. When asking for Torah one may ask without limit, but when requesting material blessing, the proper posture is that of humble supplication. The principle that emerges is that in gratitude one should express the fullness of divine beneficence — *uvetuvo* — while in petition one may be more restrained, though even there the *Gemara* directs us toward ambitious requests in the spiritual domain.

One might observe a tension running through this entire passage: what begins as a guide to precise liturgical formulation quickly becomes a framework for publicly categorizing people as learned or ignorant. It is worth pausing on this. The *Gemara* could have simply stated the correct formulation and moved on. The choice to frame precision as a test of a person's worth raises a genuine question about the purpose of liturgical exactitude — whether it is ultimately about the proper orientation of the heart toward God, or whether it has become a social sorting mechanism. That tension, already present in the *Tosefta* that the *Gemara* is quoting, is something that remains worth sitting with.

### Chayinu vs. Chayim

A second example of the learned-versus-ignorant test: *uvetuvo chayinu* — "we live through His goodness" — is the formulation of a *talmid chacham*, while *uvetuvo chayim* — "there is life through His goodness" — marks a *bur*. The difference, as *Rashi* explains, is that *chayinu* is personal and inclusive — we ourselves live by God's goodness — whereas *chayim* is an abstract theological statement about the existence of life in general. Personal, embodied gratitude is considered more sophisticated than a detached doctrinal claim.

A dissenting position is attributed to one *Nahvilai*, or according to some textual variants, transmitted differently, who held the reverse: *chayim* is actually preferable because it encompasses all creatures, not just Jews or humans. By saying *chayim* rather than *chayinu*, one expresses a more universal divine beneficence. However, the *Gemara* rules: *ve-leit hilchata ke-Nahvilai* — we do not rule like *Nahvilai*. The formulation *chayinu* is the correct one.

### She'achalnu Mishelo

Rabbi Yochanan offers a third test: *nevarech she'achalnu mishelo* — "let us bless the One from whose [food] we have eaten" — is the formulation of a *talmid chacham*. *L'mi she'achalnu mishelo* — "to the One from whom we have eaten" — is the formulation of a *bur*.

The distinction is subtle. *Rashi* explains that the phrase *l'mi she'achalnu* — "to the one from whom we ate" — could be heard as referring to the *ba'al habayit*, the host who provided the meal, rather than to God. The ambiguity arises because *l'mi* suggests singling out a particular individual among others: "that person from whom we ate" — which naturally evokes the host rather than God. By contrast, *she'achalnu mishelo* without the *l'mi* directs the blessing toward God without that ambiguity.

Rav Acha the son of Rava challenges this from Rabbi Yochanan himself, who elsewhere uses the phrase *l'mi she'asah la'avoteinu v'lanu et kol ha-nissim ha'eleh* — "to the One Who performed all these miracles for our ancestors and for us." Does that not use *l'mi* without confusion? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam mochcha milta* — there it is obvious, because *man avid nissi, Kudsha Brich Hu* — who performs miracles? Only God. When the context makes the referent unambiguous, *l'mi* poses no problem. But in the context of eating, where a host is present, the phrase *l'mi she'achalnu* might indeed be misread as gratitude to the person who fed you rather than to God. The contrast between *nevarech she'achalnu mishelo* and *l'mi she'achalnu mishelo* thus turns on whether the blessing's target is clear.

### Al HaMazon Question

A fourth example: Rabbi Yochanan teaches that *baruch she'achalnu mishelo* — "blessed is the One from whose [food] we have eaten" — is the formulation of a *talmid chacham*, while *baruch al ha-mazon she'achalnu mishelo* — "blessed for the food from which we ate" — marks a *bur*. The insertion of *al ha-mazon* limits and focuses the blessing: you are blessing for the food specifically, rather than blessing God in an unlimited sense. When you say simply *baruch she'achalnu mishelo*, your gratitude for the food becomes the vehicle for blessing God comprehensively. When you say *al ha-mazon*, you narrow your blessing to a transaction — God gave food, I thank for the food.

*Tosafot* offers a related reading: *d'mashma d'mevarech l'ba'al habayit hama'achilo* — it sounds as though you are blessing the host who fed you. The reasoning is that of God, *d'ilu Rachmana lav ha-mazon mazkir* — God is not defined by the food; *belo mazon yesh harbe l'varcho*, there is much to bless God for apart from food. When you emphasize *al ha-mazon*, you are implicitly saying the sole reason you are blessing is the food — which fits the *ba'al habayit* perfectly but is a diminished way to approach God.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua then refines the problem: the issue with *al ha-mazon* arises specifically when there are only three and God's name is not mentioned, so it is ambiguous whether you are addressing God or the host. But when ten are present and one says *nevarech Elokeinu*, the inclusion of *Elokeinu* removes all ambiguity — *mochach milta*, it is obvious who is being addressed. In that case, *al ha-mazon* is acceptable because the context has already clarified the referent. This is actually borne out by our *Mishnah* itself, which includes the phrase *al ha-mazon she'achalnu* only after the full divine address — *Baruch Hashem Elokei Yisrael Elokei Tzvaot yoshev ha-keruvim*.

One voice in the discussion observes that rather than declaring certain formulations grounds for being called a *bur*, the *Gemara* might simply have stated the correct text. The fact that each "incorrect" formulation attracts a dissenting voice — someone arguing that it is in fact legitimate or even superior — does somewhat soften the sting. Still, the framework of *talmid chacham* versus *bur* is a striking way to communicate liturgical preference, and it is worth acknowledging the tension between cultivating precision in blessing and keeping the orientation of prayer focused on God rather than on the speaker's erudition.

It is also of interest that this emphasis on precision in liturgical language appears in a *Tosefta*, which is an *Eretz Yisrael* source. The broader historical pattern tends toward greater fluidity around liturgical text in *Eretz Yisrael* and greater rigidity in *Bavel* — and yet here it is the *Tosefta* itself that insists on precision and categorizes deviations. The comparison also arises naturally: is the threshold of ten for *zimun* analogous to a *davar shebikdusha*? The *Gemara* does not explicitly invoke that concept here, but comparisons to the *minyan* threshold appear nearby in the sugya, and several *Rishonim* do understand the ten for *zimun* along those lines. The implications for who counts toward the ten — a question raised by, among others, Rav Soloveitchik, who reportedly held that the ten for *zimun* is not based on the *devarim shebikdusha* model and that women could in theory count toward it — will deserve further exploration when we reach the relevant sugya in *Megillah*.

## Mishnah Contradiction

### Self-Contradiction

The *Gemara* now turns to what it identifies as an internal contradiction within the *Mishnah* itself. The *Mishnah* opens by stating: *echad asarah ve-echad asarah ribbo* — whether ten or ten myriad, it is all the same formulation. Yet the *Mishnah* then proceeds to give distinct texts for one hundred, one thousand, and ten thousand, with different divine appellations for each threshold. The *Gemara* states plainly: *hagufa kashya* — the body of the *Mishnah* is a self-contradiction.

### Rav Yosef's Resolution

Rav Yosef resolves the contradiction: *ha Rabbi Yose HaGelili ha Rabbi Akiva* — the two parts of the *Mishnah* reflect two different positions, those of Rabbi Yose HaGelili and Rabbi Akiva respectively. The editing of the *Mishnah* has placed two originally distinct and opposing traditions side by side without fully smoothing the tension between them. This is a point worth dwelling on: the *Mishnah* as we have it was not composed from scratch by Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi but rather edited from pre-existing oral texts. Those texts were in some cases conflated, and the rough edges were not always resolved. The *Gemara* itself acknowledges this, and it is one of the more candid moments of *meta*-textual awareness in the *sugya*.

### Rabbi Yose HaGelili

Rabbi Yose HaGelili's position is that the text of *zimun* changes with the size of the group: *lefi rov ha-kahal hem mevarchin* — you follow the size of the assembled congregation. His source is the verse *bimkhelot barchu Elohim* — "in gatherings, bless God" — which he reads as indicating that different types of gatherings call for different modes of blessing. The plural *mkhelot* implies that the diversity of assembly sizes is itself significant and calls for varied expression.

### Rabbi Akiva's Position

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *mah matzinu bevet ha-kneset* — just as in the synagogue there is no distinction between a gathering of ten and a gathering of ten thousand, so too with *zimun*, once you have reached ten, the formulation does not change. Rabbi Akiva thus understands ten as a categorical threshold — once crossed, the *halachic* category is fixed regardless of how many more are added.

What does Rabbi Akiva do with Rabbi Yose HaGelili's proof-text, *bimkhelot barchu Elohim*? He applies it elsewhere: *mibaye lei le-chidetanya* — the verse is needed for the teaching that even fetuses in their mothers' wombs sang the *shirah* at the sea — *shene'emar bimkhelot barchu Elohim Hashem mimkor Yisrael*, "from the source (*mekor*) of Israel," where *mekor* evokes the womb. Rabbi Yose HaGelili can derive the fetus-song from the word *mekor* alone, leaving *mkhelot* for his own teaching about varying assembly sizes. Rabbi Akiva needs *mkhelot* for the fetus, so it is unavailable to support the graduated-text position.

Rava rules: *halacha ke-Rabbi Akiva* — the law follows Rabbi Akiva. *Rabbeinu Chananel*, quoted by *Tosafot*, codifies this accordingly: whether ten, one hundred, or ten myriad, the formulation is the same — *nevarech Eloheinu al ha-mazon she'achalnu mishelo*. He notably includes *al ha-mazon*, though as we discussed, this phrasing is only appropriate when *Eloheinu* has already been invoked. For three, one does not mention *al ha-mazon*.

The question arises whether the threshold of ten for *zimun* is analogous to a *davar shebikdusha*. Interestingly, Rabbi Akiva himself draws the comparison to the *beit knesset*, which is precisely a context of *devarim shebikdusha*. Several *Rishonim* do understand the ten for *zimun* along those lines, which also bears on the question — not resolved here — of whether women count toward that ten.

## Dividing Groups

### Rav Chama's Question

The *Gemara* now relates that Ravina and Rav Chama bar Buzi came to the house of the *Reish Galuta*. Rav Chama began going around trying to assemble exactly one hundred people, so that the *mezamen* could say *nevarech Hashem Elokeinu*. Ravina stopped him: *lo tzrichet* — you need not bother. *Hachi amar Rava, halacha ke-Rabbi Akiva* — Rava has ruled that the law follows Rabbi Akiva, so there is no significance to the difference between ninety-nine and one hundred. The formulation does not change.

### Groups of Three

Rava himself relates: when eating bread at the house of the *Reish Galuta*, where the feasts were very large and very noisy, the practice was to divide into small groups of three and bench privately — *mevarchinan gimmel gimmel*. The *Gemara* asks: why not divide into groups of ten so that one could include *Elokeinu*? Rava explains: *shama Reish Galuta ve-ikpid* — the *Reish Galuta* would be offended if he saw groups of ten forming to leave, as it would signal publicly that people were departing. Groups of three are discreet; a group of ten making its own *zimun* would draw attention. The smaller groupings allowed people to fulfill their obligation without causing offense.

### Hearing Requirement

The *Gemara* then asks: why not simply wait and be included in the *Reish Galuta's* own *zimun*? The answer: *aidi d'avshu kulei alma lo sham'ei* — because the noise was so great that no one could actually hear the *mezamen*. And to be *yotzei zimun*, one must actually hear what the *mezamen* is saying. *Zimun* is not retroactive and cannot be fulfilled through mere presence; active auditory participation is required. This *halacha* has obvious practical implications — at large weddings and gatherings, the question of whether one has genuinely heard the *mezamen* is entirely live.

## Zimun Participation

### Benching Ahead

Rabbah Tosfa'ah teaches: if three people ate bread together and one of them went ahead and benched on his own — without waiting for the *zimun* — the other two can still use him for the *zimun*. Since he ate with them and was part of the original group, he counts toward the *zimun* even though he has already benched. However, he himself does not fulfill his obligation of *zimun* through their subsequent *zimun*: *lefi she-ein zimun le-mafrea* — *zimun* does not work retroactively. He ate with them, he can make them a *zimun*, but the *zimun* they make for him comes after he has already benched, and that does not count. He thus falls short of his own obligation.

This case, which will have occurred at any number of meals across the generations when someone inadvertently begins benching before the group has gathered, illustrates a general principle: the *chiyuv* of the individual who was part of the group remains in force, so he can still serve as part of the *zimun* for others — but his own *zimun* obligation cannot be satisfied after the fact.

## Two Groups One House

### Seeing Criterion

The *Mishnah* states: if two groups are eating in one house, when some of each group can see the others — *miktzatan ro'im zeh et zeh* — they combine for *zimun*. If they cannot see one another, each group makes its own *zimun* separately. The key criterion is visibility: you need not have eaten together, need not have felt yourself part of the same gathering, need not have shared food. Mere visual access to one another is sufficient to constitute a single group for purposes of *zimun*.

*Tosafot* notes that *bayit* here need not mean a single room — it can include adjacent rooms or spaces where people can see or hear one another. This has practical implications for large gatherings where people spill across multiple rooms, a balcony, or an outdoor area: the question of whether they can see one another becomes the operative *halachic* test.

### Shamash Role

The *Gemara* adds from a *braita*: *im yesh shamash beineihem, shamash mitzarfan* — if there is a waiter moving between the two groups, the *shamash* combines them. The *shamash* serves as a living link between the groups: even if the two groups cannot see one another directly, the presence of a servant moving between them creates a sufficient connection to render them a single group for *zimun*.

## Wine and Water

### Rabbi Eliezer's View

The *Mishnah* concludes with what at first glance appears to be a non-sequitur: a dispute about wine and water. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *ein mevarichin al ha-yayin ad she-yiten le-tocho mayim* — one does not recite *borei pri hagafen* over wine until water has been mixed into it. The wine of antiquity was extremely potent and was routinely diluted — the practice was called *mizigah* — and in its undiluted state it was not considered drinkable wine in the normal sense.

### Rabbis' View

The Rabbis disagree: *bein kach u-vein kach, mevarichin alav borei pri hagafen* — whether or not water has been added, one recites *borei pri hagafen*. According to the Rabbis, undiluted wine retains the status of wine for purposes of blessing regardless of the *mizigah*.

The placement of this *halacha* at the end of the chapter on *zimun* is noted as somewhat puzzling. The likely connection is that *zimun* at such meals was often performed over a cup of wine, and so the question of what blessing one recites over that cup is a natural appendage — though the *Mishnah* does not make the connection explicit.

## Zimun Participation (II)

### Three From Three Groups

The *Gemara* now returns to the topic of *zimun* with a case that at first appears paradoxical. *Rav Huna* teaches: *shlosha she-ba'u mi-shalosh chavurot einam resha'in lachalek* — three people who came from three different groups may not separate from one another. The scenario is as follows: each person was eating with his own group. Each then left — perhaps running out to the marketplace — while his original group remained. Now these three individuals from three separate groups find themselves together. Because each of them was individually obligated in *zimun* as part of his original group, they can together constitute a new *zimun*. The obligation attaches to the individual who was part of a *zimun*-obligated group, and three such individuals can now combine to fulfill that obligation together.

*Rav Chisda* adds a condition: *ve-hu she-ba'u mi-shalosh chavurot shel shlosha bnei adam* — this applies only when each person came from a group of at least three, meaning a group that was itself obligated in *zimun*. Each individual must have been part of a proper *zimun*-obligated group; only then can the three individuals regroup and make their own *zimun*.

This case raises a genuine conceptual question about *zimun*: the entire institution has been described as something rooted in the shared experience of eating together as a group. Yet here, three people who never ate together — each of whom ate with entirely different people — are combining to make a *zimun*. The *chiyuv* of *zimun* appears to be something that attaches to the individual who participated in a qualifying meal, rather than something that inheres exclusively in the group as such. Whether this represents a principled extension of *zimun* or a more pragmatic accommodation is a genuine question.

### Rava's Limitation

Rava then limits the case significantly: *velo amran ela de-lo akdimu hanhu ve-azminu aleihu beduchtaihu* — this applies only when the group these individuals left behind has not already made a *zimun* for them. If the group they left behind already performed *zimun* and included these absent members — meaning the absent members had already answered to the *zimun* before departing, as described earlier in the *sugya* where a person waits to answer *zimun* and then runs to the marketplace — then *parach zimun mineihu*, the *zimun* has "flown away" from them. They have already been discharged through that first *zimun* and can no longer regroup for another.

*Rashi* explains that "the group included them in the *zimun*" specifically means the absent members actively answered to the *zimun* before leaving, waiting until *ha-zan et ha-kol* was said. In that case they participated in a *zimun* and their obligation is fulfilled; they cannot count toward a second one.

Rava supports this ruling with an analogy to the laws of *tum'ah*: if a bed is split in half — whether stolen, lost, or divided between brothers or partners — it loses its *tum'ah*. *Keivan de-palguha, parach la tum'ah minah* — once divided, the *tum'ah* departs permanently, and if reassembled, the bed only receives new *tum'ah* from that point forward, not retroactively. Similarly, once a person's *zimun* obligation is discharged through the *zimun* of his original group, it is gone — *parach zimun mineihu* — and cannot be reconstituted.

*Tosafot* notes that the analogy to the bed is particularly apt because these beds were assembled from component parts and could be physically disassembled and reassembled. The parallel to an individual being taken out of one *chaburah* and potentially reconstituted with another is structural: just as the reassembled bed does not recover its prior *tum'ah*, the person who has already been part of a *zimun* does not recover a *zimun* obligation by joining a new group.

## Wine and Water

### Before Water

The *Gemara* returns to the *Mishnah's* closing topic and cites the relevant *braita* in full. Before water has been added, the *braita* states, one does not recite *borei pri hagafen* over wine — only *borei pri ha'etz*. *Tosafot* notes in passing that this confirms the general principle that fruit juices, at least when they are significant enough not to be *she-hakol*, receive the blessing of *borei pri ha'etz*. Undiluted wine has not yet been transformed into the improved product that justifies the higher blessing of *borei pri hagafen*. Additionally, before water is added, such wine may be used for washing hands — because its legal status at this stage is closer to water than to wine.

### After Water

Once water has been added, the status reverses. Now the mixture is considered wine: *mevarichin alav borei pri hagafen* — one recites *borei pri hagafen*. And, somewhat counterintuitively, once it is wine it can no longer be used for washing hands: *ve-ein notlin heimenu le-yadayim*. Adding water has simultaneously elevated it to wine and removed it from the category of water available for *netilat yadayim*.

### Whose Position

The *Gemara* then asks: *ke-man azla ha de-amar Shmuel?* — whose position aligns with *Shmuel's* ruling that *oseh adam kol tzarchav be-fat*, a person may use bread for any of his needs? This principle addresses the broader question of *bizayon ochlin* — whether using food for purposes other than eating constitutes a disrespect for food. *Shmuel* holds that even bread, which commands the greatest degree of respect, may be used for other purposes. The *Gemara* concludes that *Shmuel's* position aligns with Rabbi Eliezer, who explicitly permitted using undiluted wine for washing hands. The Rabbis, who held that wine is wine regardless of dilution and thus never permitted it for hands, do not necessarily disagree with *Shmuel's* general principle — but that principle clearly emerges from Rabbi Eliezer's position.

*Tosafot* complicates this: the *Gemara* elsewhere rules like *Shmuel* but with a qualification — food may be used for other purposes only when it is not rendered *ma'us* (repulsive or inedible) in the process. If using food for a secondary purpose ruins it as food, *Shmuel* would not permit it. Rabbi Eliezer, by allowing wine to be used for washing hands even though the hands might make it *ma'us*, goes further than *Shmuel*. So the correct reading is not that Rabbi Eliezer simply reflects *Shmuel's* position, but that Rabbi Eliezer actually extends beyond it.

### Kos Shel Bracha

Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Chanina teaches an important qualification: *modin chachamim le-Rabbi Eliezer be-kos shel bracha* — even the Rabbis agree with Rabbi Eliezer when it comes to a *kos shel bracha*, a ritual cup used for *Kiddush*, *Havdalah*, or *Birkat Hamazon*. For a *kos shel bracha*, water must be added before reciting *borei pri hagafen*. *Rav Oshaya* explains: *be'ina mitzvah min ha-muvchar* — when performing a *mitzvah*, we require the best possible fulfillment, and diluted wine is considered superior for that purpose. The Rabbis may hold that the blessing is valid over undiluted wine in ordinary drinking, but for a *mitzvah* cup, the higher standard applies.

This harmonization is also significant for understanding the placement of the *Mishnah's* closing *halacha*. The *Mishnah* has been discussing the *zimun* over wine — and that wine is almost certainly a *kos shel bracha*. On the level of a *kos shel bracha*, there is actually no dispute: all authorities require the water. The apparent debate in the *Mishnah* may thus be more limited in practical scope than it initially appears.

### Medicinal Purpose

The *Gemara* then asks: according to the Rabbis, who hold that undiluted wine is wine regardless, and who do not permit it for washing hands — for what purpose, then, is undiluted wine fit, given that the *Gemara* seems to suggest it is not normally drunk as-is? *Rav Zeira* answers: *chazi le-kuraitei* — it is suitable for a medicinal preparation involving dates, some sort of curative mixture. The *Gemara* is saying that the Rabbis' position, which classifies undiluted wine as wine and assigns it *borei pri hagafen*, is somewhat strained since they admit people do not normally drink it straight. One recites *borei pri hagafen* over it only in the context of medicinal use, which makes Rabbi Eliezer's position — that undiluted wine is not yet properly wine — the more intuitively compelling one.

## Respect for Food

### Four Prohibitions

The *Gemara* now cites a *braita*: *arba'ah devarim ne'emru be-fat* — four things were said regarding bread. One may not place raw meat on bread, lest the blood soak in and render the bread unpleasant. One may not pass a full cup directly over bread, lest it spill. One may not throw bread. And one may not use bread as a support for a plate or table — for instance, wedging a piece under an uneven surface to stabilize it. All four reflect the principle of *kavod* for bread, the staple of life, which commands a level of respect not automatically extended to other foods.

### Throwing Food

The prohibition on throwing food raises the question of whether it applies to foods other than bread. The *Gemara* relates that *Amemar*, *Mar Zutra*, and *Rav Ashi* were eating bread together when dates and pomegranates were brought. *Mar Zutra* tossed a piece to *Rav Ashi*. *Rav Ashi* challenged him: *lo savar lei mar le-ha de-tanya, ein zorkin et ha-ochlin* — does he not hold the teaching that one may not throw food? *Mar Zutra* replied: *hahi be-fat tanya* — that teaching refers only to bread. Other foods are not subject to the same stringency.

### Mar Zutra Story

The *Gemara* then challenges *Mar Zutra's* position from a *braita* that states: just as one may not throw bread, so too one may not throw other foods. But another *braita* states: one may not throw bread, but one may throw other foods. Two *braitot* appear to contradict each other.

### Unpalatable Standard

The resolution: *lo kashya — ha bi-midi de-mimas, ha bi-midi de-lo mimas*. The *braita* that prohibits throwing other foods refers to foods that will become *ma'us* — repulsive or inedible — when thrown. The *braita* that permits throwing other foods refers to foods that will not be ruined in the process. A pomegranate in its shell, for example, can be thrown without the seeds being damaged; the food is not rendered unfit. But something that will get dirty or otherwise become disgusting when thrown falls under the prohibition.

The question of throwing bread at the *Shabbat* table — a practice observed in some communities, where pieces of *challah* are thrown to those seated around the table — arises naturally here. The argument for permissibility would be that the bread is not rendered unfit in the process. However, *Tosafot* makes an explicit and significant distinction: *ve-ein zorkin et ha-pas, afilu lo ma'is* — bread may not be thrown even when it does not become repulsive. Bread is categorically different; it may not be thrown regardless of outcome. This would appear to present a genuine problem for the practice of throwing *challah* at the *Shabbat* table.

### Pouring and Throwing

The *Gemara* continues: *mamshichin yayin be-tzinorot lifnei chatan ve-lifnei kallah* — wine may be poured through channels or funnels before a bride and groom. *Rashi* explains this is permitted because the wine can be collected in vessels and is not ruined in the process — the food is not made *ma'us*. Similarly, one may throw roasted seeds and nuts before them in the summer, but not in the rainy season when they would fall into mud. *Tosafot* raises the question of whether nuts in the shell really become *ma'us* even in mud, since the shell protects the contents — and suggests that *nimas* may not require that people refuse to eat the food, but that allowing it to become muddy is itself a lack of respect, regardless of edibility.

The *Gemara* further states: one may not throw pieces of fine bread or cake (*gluska'ot*) even in summer, because that will be ruined. These distinctions collectively build a coherent principle: food may be used for purposes other than eating, and may even be thrown, so long as it is not rendered unfit. The exception is bread, which may never be thrown.

## Food in Mouth

### Three Options

*Rav Yehuda* teaches: if one forgot and placed food in one's mouth without having recited a blessing, one should move it to the side of the mouth and recite the blessing. The *Gemara* then cites three *braitot* that appear to disagree. The first says one should swallow the food. The second says one should spit it out. The third agrees with *Rav Yehuda* that one should move it aside.

### Reconciling Braitot

The *Gemara* reconciles the three positions: *lo kashya*. The *braita* that says swallow refers to liquids — one cannot hold liquid to the side of the mouth and recite a blessing without choking. The *braita* that says spit it out refers to solid food that will not become *ma'us* when spat out — something like a hard candy that has not yet been chewed, which can be removed from the mouth, placed aside, the blessing recited, and the food returned to the mouth without any disgust involved. The *braita* that says move it to the side refers to solid food that would become *ma'us* if spat out — one therefore moves it to the side of the mouth, recites the blessing, and continues eating.

### Waste Priority

The *Gemara* presses further: even for the category of food that would not become *ma'us* when spat out — the case assigned to the "spit it out" *braita* — why not simply move it to the side of the mouth? That would be equally valid. The answer emerges from a key principle: when one has liquids in the mouth and the choice is between swallowing without a blessing or spitting out and wasting the liquid, the ruling is to swallow — even *belo bracha*. This is because *bizayon ochlin*, the imperative not to waste food, takes precedence over the obligation not to derive benefit from the world without a blessing. One does not make a blessing after the fact in such a case; the obligation has been superseded by the imperative to avoid waste.

### Mouth Empty Standard

The *Gemara* closes with a teaching transmitted by Rabbi Yitzchak Kaskasa before Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mishum she-ne'emar yimaleh pi tehilatekha* — because it is written, "My mouth shall be filled with Your praise." The mouth should be empty and available for praise when one recites a blessing. This is the ideal: one's mouth should be clear, unencumbered, and fully directed toward God when giving praise. It is this standard that explains why spitting out is preferred when possible, and why moving the food to the side is considered a workable but secondary solution. The ideal of *yimaleh pi tehilatekha* — the mouth full of praise alone — is the normative aspiration against which the practical accommodations of swallowing and moving-aside must be measured.
