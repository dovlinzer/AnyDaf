# Bava Batra 145 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shushbinut Overview

### Mishna Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאַחִים שֶׁעָשׂוּ מִקְצָתָן שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת בְּחַיֵּי הָאָב, חָזְרָה שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת – חָזְרָה לָאֶמְצַע; שֶׁהַשּׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת נִגְבֵּית בְּבֵית דִּין. אֲבָל הַשּׁוֹלֵחַ לַחֲבֵירוֹ כַּדֵּי יַיִן וְכַדֵּי שֶׁמֶן – אֵין נִגְבִּין בְּבֵית דִּין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן גְּמִילוּת חֲסָדִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: It was common practice for friends of a groom to give him gifts in order to help cover the expenses of the wedding feast. These gifts are known as gifts of groomsmen, and would be reciprocated in turn. While the groom and the groomsman were at times the recipient and the giver of the gifts, respectively, the gifts were at times provided by the father of the groomsman and received by the father of the groom. In the case of **brothers, some of whom brought gifts of groomsmen in their father's lifetime,** which were provided by their father, when **the gifts of groomsmen are reciprocated** after the father's death, when one of the brothers gets married, **they are reciprocated to the middle,** i.e., the gift is divided among the brothers. This is **because gifts of groomsmen are** a legal debt owed to the father, **collectible in court. But** with regard to **one who sends his friend jugs of wine or jugs of oil,** a reciprocal gift **is not collectible in court, because they are** considered **acts of kindness.**

The Mishna on 144b introduced the institution of *shushbinut* — the gifts brought by close friends of the groom to a wedding, together with the reciprocal obligation those gifts create. The scenario the Mishna describes is as follows: one family sends a son as representative to a neighbor's son's wedding, bearing a gift on behalf of the entire family. The father of that son subsequently dies. When the representative son himself later marries, and the other family sends a gift in return, the question arises: to whom does that return gift belong? Is it the personal property of the son who originally represented the family, or does it belong to the family estate and get divided among all the heirs?

The Mishna rules that the gift belongs to the entire family — *chazra l'emtza*, it returns to the middle — because the son was acting only as a representative of the family, not in his own capacity. The Mishna further contrasts this with a case where a son brings a purely personal gift to a friend's wedding. In that case, the return gift is not collectible in *beis din* at all (there is no legally enforceable obligation of reciprocity the way there is with *shushbinut* proper), and the implication is that whatever does come back would belong to the son personally rather than to the family estate.

### Gift Reciprocity

The institution of *shushbinut* is built on a mechanism of community-wide gift reciprocity: when you receive a *shushbinot* gift at your wedding, you are legally obligated to reciprocate when the giver's family makes a wedding. This is not merely a social expectation but an enforceable legal debt. One might think of it as analogous to a communal cost-sharing arrangement in which the expense of the wedding is distributed across a network of families, each bearing the cost in turn. The gifts given by the *shushbinin* to the groom are understood to carry this reciprocal obligation, and it is precisely because of that legal structure that when the son brought the gift on behalf of the family, he was seen as acting for the family — and therefore, the return gift flows back to the family, not to him individually.

## Braita Reconciliation

### Braita's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּרְמִינְהִי: שָׁלַח לוֹ אָבִיו שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת, כְּשֶׁהִיא חוֹזֶרֶת – חוֹזֶרֶת לוֹ. נִשְׁתַּלְּחָה לְאָבִיו שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת, כְּשֶׁהִיא חוֹזֶרֶת – חוֹזֶרֶת מִן הָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** between the mishna's ruling that the gifts of groomsmen are reciprocated to the middle, and the first clause of a *baraita*: If **a father sent** his son with **gifts of groomsmen, when they are reciprocated** when that son gets married, **they are reciprocated to** that son. If **his father was sent gifts of groomsmen** for the wedding of one of his sons, **when they are reciprocated** from the father's estate, **they are reciprocated from the middle,** i.e., the cost of the gift is divided among the brothers.

The Gemara now raises a tension from a *braita*. The *braita* states: *shalach lo aviv shushbinut, keshe'hi chozeret chozeret lo* — if a father sent his son as *shushbin* to the wedding of another, when the gift is repaid, it comes back specifically to that son. This appears to contradict the Mishna, which holds that the return gift goes to the entire family.

Before the Gemara resolves the contradiction, it presents an intermediate case: if a son received the *shushbinut* gift on behalf of his family while his father was still alive, and the father then died before the obligation was reciprocated, when the obligation to reciprocate falls due, it falls upon the entire estate. The rationale is symmetric: the gift was received as a gift to the family; therefore the obligation to return it is borne by the family. The Gemara notes, however, that the *braita*'s framework is actually directionally asymmetric — the gift flows from the entire family to the individual groom, and the reciprocal obligation flows from that individual groom back. This creates the tension with the Mishna, which seemed to assume a family-to-family dynamic.

### Rabbi Assi's Rewrite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי תְּנַן נָמֵי מַתְנִיתִין, נִשְׁתַּלְּחָה לְאָבִיו תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When we learned the mishna as well, we learned** it as teaching the *halakha* with regard to gifts of groomsmen that **were sent to** the **father** for the wedding of one of his sons, not with regard to gifts of groomsmen sent by the father.

Rabbi Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan attempts to resolve the conflict by rewriting the Mishna to match the *braita*. His argument runs as follows: rather than reading the Mishna as a case where the son went and gave a gift on behalf of the family, read it instead as the opposite — the son was the *recipient* of the *shushbinut*, and the Mishna is telling us that when the obligation to reciprocate arises, it falls upon the entire family (*min ha'emtza*). In this reading, the Mishna and *braita* would describe the same basic rule — the gift goes specifically to the groom who received it — but the Mishna would be addressing the separate question of who bears the repayment obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״אַחִים שֶׁעָשׂוּ מִקְצָתָן שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת״ קָתָנֵי! תְּנִי: ״לְמִקְצָתָן״. וְהָא ״חָזְרָה שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: חָזְרָה לִגְבּוֹת – נִגְבֵּית מֵאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But** the mishna **teaches** the *halakha* with regard to **brothers, some of whom brought gifts of groomsmen.** The Gemara responds: Emend the text of the mishna and **teach** it as saying: With regard to brothers, **to some of whom** gifts of groomsmen were brought. The Gemara objects: **But** the mishna explicitly **teaches** the *halakha* if **the gifts of groomsmen are reciprocated.** The Gemara responds: **This is** what the mishna **is saying: When it is collected** from the brothers **in return, it is collected from** the **middle.**

The Gemara immediately pushes back. The text of the Mishna is explicit: *ha'achim she'asu miktzatan shushbinut* — the brothers who performed the *shushbinut*, which clearly indicates that they were the givers, not the recipients. Rabbi Assi's attempt to rewrite the Mishna as a case where they were the recipients cannot be sustained without fundamentally altering the text. *Hachi katani* — the Gemara tries to reparse the language to make it fit, but ultimately the rewrite is recognized as untenable. As the Gemara notes with uncharacteristic directness: this reading requires one to erase and rewrite the Mishna wholesale.

### Rav Ashi's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר: לָא קַשְׁיָא; כָּאן בִּסְתָם, כָּאן בִּמְפָרֵשׁ – כִּדְתַנְיָא: שָׁלַח לוֹ אָבִיו שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת, כְּשֶׁהִיא חוֹזֶרֶת – חוֹזֶרֶת לוֹ. שָׁלַח אָבִיו שׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת סְתָם, כְּשֶׁהִיא חוֹזֶרֶת – חוֹזֶרֶת לָאֶמְצַע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi says:** Even if the text of the mishna is not emended, it is **not difficult** and can be reconciled with the *baraita*. **Here** in the mishna it is referring to where the father sent the gifts of groomsmen **without specifying** which son should be ascribed credit for bringing the gifts. **There** in the *baraita*, it is referring to **where** the father **specified** that the credit for the gifts of groomsmen should be ascribed to a specific son, who receives gifts in return, **as it is taught** in another *baraita*: If **his father sent gifts of groomsmen on his,** i.e., the son's, behalf, then **when** the gift **is reciprocated, it is reciprocated to him,** i.e., to that specific son. If **his father sent gifts of groomsmen without specification,** then **when** the gift **is reciprocated, it is reciprocated to the middle.**

Rav Ashi offers a far more elegant resolution: *lo kashya — kan bistam, kan bimfaresh*. There is no real contradiction, because the two sources describe two different factual contexts. When the father *designated* a specific son to go and serve as *shushbin* — *shalach lo aviv shushbinut* — that act of designation focuses the gift on the son personally, and therefore when the gift is returned, it returns to that son. When the father sent a son as a generic, unnamed representative of the family — *shelach aviv shushvinut stam*, simply sending the *shushbinut* without specifying a particular child — then the son is understood as a mere conduit for the family's obligation, and the return gift goes to the whole family.

### Rashbam's Distinction

The Rashbam unpacks Rav Ashi's distinction in precise terms. When the father gives the son money and specifically dispatches *him* to serve as *shushbin* — *natan lo aviv ma'ot v'shalcho la'asot shoshvinut* — the son's personal involvement is highlighted, and the reciprocal gift accordingly returns to him alone (*chozeret lo levado*). By contrast, when the father sends the *shushbinut* generically, without designating one son in particular — *af al pi she'al yedei echad mi'banav shelach* — even though it ultimately happens to be carried out by one of the sons, he is acting as an anonymous family representative. The return gift therefore goes back to the entire family estate. This parallels the principle established in the preceding Mishna: when a son is hired for labor, whether his wages belong to him or to the family depends on whether the employer engaged him personally or simply hired whomever the family sent.

### Samuel's Yibum Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: הָכָא בְּיָבָם עָסְקִינַן, שֶׁאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says: Here** in the mishna **we are dealing with a man whose married brother died childless [*yavam*].** The son who brought the gifts of groomsmen died childless, his brother entered into levirate marriage with the widow, and the gifts of groomsmen were reciprocated at the wedding of the *yavam*. Although the *yavam* inherits the property of his brother, since the reciprocal gifts were not extant when his brother died, they do not belong solely to him; rather, they are divided among the brothers. This is because the *yavam* **does not take** in inheritance the property **due** the deceased **as** he does the property the deceased already **possessed.**

Samuel offers a different reading of the Mishna altogether. He maintains that the fundamental rule is as the *braita* states — the *shushbinut* always returns to the specific groom. The case in the Mishna where it returns to the entire family (*chazra l'emtza*) is a special scenario involving *yibum* (levirate marriage). As the Rashbam explains: *u'matnitin diktani chozeret le'emtza — hani mili she'met haben*. The case in the Mishna is one where the son who originally received the *shushbinut* gift died, and his brother has now performed *yibum* with the son's widow. The *yavam* claims that as part of his acquisition of the deceased brother's estate through *yibum*, he is entitled to the *shushbinut* that is owed to that estate. Samuel rules that this claim fails, because the *yavam* cannot take *b'ra'uy k'vemuchzak* — he does not receive anticipated assets as though they were already in hand. The *shushbinut* that has not yet been paid back is *ra'uy*, not *muchzak*, and therefore it does not pass to the *yavam* but is distributed among all the heirs.

## Yibum & Rav'ui

### Yabam Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּאִידַּךְ – מְשַׁלֵּם? לֵימָא: ״תְּנוּ לִי שׁוֹשְׁבִינִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is it possible to conclude **by inference** from Shmuel's statement **that the other** side, i.e., a friend who had received gifts of groomsmen from the deceased, is obligated to **repay** the gifts of groomsmen he received from the deceased? Why should this be so? **Let him say: Give me my groomsman and I will rejoice with him.** Since the one who gave him the gifts is deceased, he is not obligated to reciprocate.

Samuel's *yibum* case raises a further question. If the *yavam* cannot collect the *shushbinut* gift because it is merely *ra'uy* — an anticipated but not yet possessed asset — one might accept that he cannot claim it exclusively. But the Gemara presses further: why is *anyone* obligated to send the gift back at all? The *shushbinut* system is built on the expectation of a wedding celebration, of spending the seven days of *sheva brachot* together with the groom. The groom is dead. No wedding is taking place. Why should the opposing family feel obligated to send a gift to the estate of a man who has died before *nisuin*?

The Gemara frames this in parallel to a separate case being introduced: *leima tnu li shushvini ve'esmakh immo* — let the obligated party say, "Give me back my friend who is getting married and I will rejoice with him, and then I will bring the gift." Just as a bride can make the claim *tnu li ba'ali ve'esmakh immo* — "give me my husband and I'll celebrate with him" — to avoid having to return *kesef kiddushin* when the husband dies, so too here the gift-giver might argue that his obligation has not ripened because the wedding never truly concluded.

### Obligation Trigger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁשָּׂמַח עִמּוֹ שִׁבְעָה יְמֵי מִשְׁתֶּה, וְלֹא הִסְפִּיק לְפוֹרְעוֹ עַד שֶׁמֵּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: With what are we dealing here?** Shmuel's statement is referring to a case **where** the original recipient, in reciprocation, **rejoiced with** the brother who brought the gifts of groomsmen during the **seven days of** the wedding **feast, but did not suffice to repay him before** the brother **died.** Since it was the usual practice to send the gifts of groomsmen after the groomsman rejoiced with the betrothed man for seven days, he is obligated to reciprocate the gifts of groomsmen, and the *yavam* cannot claim them for himself.

The Gemara resolves this through Rav Yosef: *kegon she'samach immo shiva yemei mishteh v'lo hispik lifro'o ad shemeis* — the Mishna's *yibum* case is specifically one where the groom lived through the full seven days of wedding celebration, the *sheva brachot* were completed, and only then did he die before the opposing family had the chance to repay. Once both parties have gone through the full week of celebration together, the legal obligation to repay the *shushbinut* has fully crystallized. The *tnu li shushvini* argument no longer applies, because he did in fact get to rejoice at the wedding. The obligation is thus fully enforceable — only the question of *who* collects it (the *yavam* alone or the whole family) remains, and the answer is that since the gift had not yet been received before death, it is *ra'uy* and therefore goes to the whole family rather than exclusively to the *yavam*.

## Kiddushin Returns

### Woman Dies Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״. הָכָא נָמֵי, נֵימָא: ״תְּנוּ לִי שׁוֹשְׁבִינִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** **Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him,** i.e., it is not my fault that we are not getting married, she is not required to return the betrothal money. **Here too, let him say: Give me my groomsman and I will rejoice with him.**

The Gemara's exploration of the *tnu li ba'ali ve'esmakh immo* argument leads into an extended discussion of the rules governing the return of *kesef kiddushin* when the betrothal is disrupted before *nisuin*. A *braita* is cited: in a place where the custom (*minhag*) is to return *kiddushin* money, it is returned; in a place where the custom is not to return it, it is not returned. Rav Yosef bar Abba citing Mar Ukva citing Samuel adds a crucial qualification: *lo shanu ela shemeisa hi* — this rule (that it depends on *minhag*) applies only when *she* dies. *Aval meis hu* — if *he* dies, the bride's family need not return the *kiddushin*, because she can say *tnu li ba'ali ve'esmakh immo*: I am willing to proceed with the marriage; it is not my fault he died.

### Le-Tibu'in Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא; דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יְכוֹלָה שֶׁתֹּאמַר: ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״. וּדְמִית הוּא – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי – שֶׁמֵּתָה הִיא; וְהָכָא בְּקִדּוּשִׁין לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי נָתָן סָבַר: קִדּוּשִׁין לָאו לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא סָבַר: קִדּוּשִׁין לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **No, everyone** agrees that **she can say: Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him, and** in a case **where he died, everyone agrees** that she does not have to return the betrothal money. **They disagree** in a case **where she died, and here** it is **with regard to** the question of whether **betrothal** money **is given as a sunk** cost, i.e., that it is not returned even if the betrothal is not consummated, that **they disagree. Rabbi Natan holds** that **betrothal** money **is not given as a sunk** cost, and in a place where people were accustomed to return it, they return it. **And Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds** that **betrothal** money **is given as a sunk** cost.

The Gemara now identifies this as a point of dispute among *Tannaim*. The underlying question is whether *kiddushin* money is *le-tibu'in nitnu* — given to be "done with," in the sense that once transferred, it belongs to the woman absolutely and is not subject to recovery under any circumstances — or whether it retains some conditional character. The Rashbam explains *le-tibu'in nitnu*: if the woman dies and he never merits marrying her, the money is nonetheless gone; it does not return to him. The opposing view holds that the money is not simply consumed upon transfer and may, under certain circumstances, need to be returned.

### Rabbi Yehuda vs. Noson

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״ אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – וְחַסּוֹרֵי מְחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַמְאָרֵס אֶת הָאִשָּׁה, בְּתוּלָה – גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם, וְאַלְמָנָה – מָנֶה. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים – דַּהֲדַר בֵּיהּ אִיהוּ; אֲבָל מֵתָה – מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַחְזִיר, מַחְזִירִין; מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְהַחְזִיר, אֵין מַחְזִירִין. וְדַוְקָא שֶׁמֵּתָה הִיא, אֲבָל מֵת הוּא – אֵין מַחְזִירִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Shall we say** that Shmuel's statement that a betrothed woman can claim: **Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him, is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*? As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who betrothed a woman** and then he divorces her or dies, if she is **a virgin,** she **collects two hundred** dinars in payment of her marriage contract. **And** if she is **a widow,** she collects **one hundred dinars.** In **a place where** people **were accustomed to return the betrothal** money when the betrothed man or woman died, **they return** it. In **a place where** people **were accustomed not to return the betrothal** money, **they do not return** it. This is **the statement of Rabbi Natan. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says: Actually they said:** In **a place where** people **were accustomed to return** it, **they return** it; in **a place where** people **were accustomed not to return** it, **they do not return** it. **And** this applies **specifically** to **where she died; but** if **he died, they do not return** it. **What is the reason** for this? It is because **she can say: Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him.** This is the statement of Rabbi Natan.

The *braita* records a dispute between Rabbi Noson and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Rabbi Noson holds that whether *kiddushin* is returned depends on local custom (*minhag*), which seems identical to the opening of the *braita*. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi states: *be'emes amru* — "in fact they said" — *makom she'nahagu lehachzir machzirin*, which also seems merely to echo the same rule. The Gemara therefore presses: *hainu Tanna Kama* — isn't Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi simply repeating the *Tanna Kama*?

The Gemara resolves this by arguing that the two statements address different scenarios, and that the *braita* must be read as missing some words (*chasurei mechasra*). The first part of the *braita* addresses the case where *he* dies — in which case the base *kesubah* payment (two hundred *zuz* for a virgin, one hundred for a widow) falls due. It is in this context that Rabbi Noson states the *minhag*-based rule for returning *kiddushin*. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi then comes and says: *bein mes hu uvein mesah hi* — whether he dies or she dies, whether one must return the ring is a function of local custom. In other words, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi explicitly rejects the *tnu li ba'ali* argument even in the case where he dies, insisting that even then it is purely a question of *minhag*.

### Gemara Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲתָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הַנָּשִׂיא לְמֵימַר, בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: בֵּין מֵת הוּא, וּבֵין מֵתָה הִיא; מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַחְזִיר – מַחְזִירִין, מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ שֶׁלֹּא לְהַחְזִיר – אֵין מַחְזִירִין; וְלָא מָצְיָא אָמְרָה: ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi came to say: Actually they said: Whether he died or whether she died,** in **a place where** people **were accustomed to return** it, **they return** it; in **a place where** people **were accustomed not to return** it, **they do not return** it, **and she cannot say: Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him.**

The Gemara then reverses itself. It proposes that in fact, *kuli alma* — everyone agrees — that if he dies, she can invoke *tnu li ba'ali ve'esmakh immo* and never has to return the ring. The disagreement between Rabbi Noson and Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is therefore not about the case where he dies, but about the case where *she* dies. This requires rereading the *braita* with an internal gap: the first clause (two hundred or one hundred *zuz* payment) concerns the case of his death; then there is an implicit shift to the case of her death; and it is in the context of her death that the *minhag* debate about return of *kiddushin* is situated. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, on this reading, is saying that even when she dies, whether the ring must be returned depends on *minhag* — and *v'lo masi amrah tnu li ba'ali ve'esmakh immo*, she cannot invoke the *tnu li ba'ali* argument in that case, since she is the one who has died.

### Core Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַחְזִיר מַחְזִירִין״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְסִבְלוֹנוֹת – וַדַּאי מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַחְזִיר, מַחֲזִירִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But doesn't** the *baraita* **teach** that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, in **a place where** people **were accustomed to return** it, **they return** it? This indicates that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi does not hold that the betrothal money was given as a sunk cost. The Gemara replies: This is not referring to the betrothal money, which is not returned in any event. **This is** what the *baraita* **is saying: But** with regard to **presents,** which the betrothed man sent his betrothed following the betrothal, **certainly** in **a place where** people **were accustomed to return** them, **they return** them.

With this restructuring established, the Gemara identifies the root of the disagreement: *b'kiddushin le-tibu'in nitnu kamiplegi* — the debate turns on whether *kiddushin* money is given to be simply consumed. Rabbi Noson holds that it is *not le-tibu'in nitnu*, and therefore in the case where she dies, whether the groom recovers the money is a function of *minhag*. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that it *is le-tibu'in nitnu*, meaning the ring is hers absolutely once given, and it never returns — even when she dies.

The Gemara then asks: but Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi himself says *makom she'nahagu lehachzir machzirin* — he explicitly allows for return in places where that is the custom. This seems to contradict the claim that *kiddushin* is *le-tibu'in nitnu*. The Gemara resolves this by distinguishing between *kiddushin* proper and *savlonot*. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's concession to *minhag* applies only to the *savlonot* — the gifts sent by the groom to the bride or her family during the *eirusin* period. Those gifts, which are tied to the anticipation of *nisuin*, are not *le-tibu'in nitnu* and do go back depending on *minhag*. The *kesef kiddushin* itself, the ring or its equivalent, is *le-tibu'in nitnu* on Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's view, and never returns.

### Rabbi Yosi Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי – כְּהָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי, דְּתַנְיָא: קִדְּשָׁהּ בְּכִכָּר – בְּתוּלָה גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם, וְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בְּתוּלָה גּוֹבָה מָאתַיִם, וְאַלְמָנָה מָנֶה; וּמַחְזֶרֶת לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: קִדְּשָׁהּ בְּעֶשְׂרִים – נוֹתֵן לָהּ שְׁלֹשִׁים חֲצָאִין. קִדְּשָׁהּ בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים – נוֹתֵן לָהּ עֶשְׂרִים חֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The dispute between **these *tanna'im*,** in the following *baraita*, **is like** the dispute between **those *tanna'im*** just mentioned, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case where the man **betrothed** a woman **with a talent** of silver, equivalent to six thousand dinars, if she was **a virgin** she **collects** in payment of her marriage contract **two hundred** dinars over and above this amount, **and** if she was **a widow** she collects **one hundred dinars** over and above this amount. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yehuda says: A virgin collects two hundred** dinars **and a widow one hundred dinars, and she returns the rest** of the betrothal money **to him. Rabbi Yosei says:** If **he betrothed her with twenty, he gives her thirty halves;** if **he betrothed her with thirty, he gives her twenty halves,** as the Gemara will explain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא שֶׁמֵּתָה, מִי אִית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה?! וְאֶלָּא שֶׁמֵּת הוּא – אַמַּאי מַחְזֶרֶת לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר? וְנֵימָא: ״תְּנוּ לִי בַּעְלִי וְאֶשְׂמַח עִמּוֹ״! וְאֶלָּא בְּאֵשֶׁת יִשְׂרָאֵל שֶׁזִּינְּתָה. וּבְמַאי? אִי בְּרָצוֹן – מִי אִית לַהּ כְּתוּבָּה? וְאֶלָּא בְּאוֹנֶס – מִישְׁרֵא שַׁרְיָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the *baraita*: **With what are we dealing? If we say** the *baraita* is referring to **when she died, is she entitled** to receive payment of her **marriage contract? Rather,** perhaps the *baraita* is referring to **where he died,** in which case **why** does Rabbi Yehuda state that **she returns the rest** of the betrothal money **to him? Let her say: Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him. Rather,** perhaps the *baraita* is referring to the case **of the wife,** i.e., the betrothed, **of an Israelite, who committed adultery,** who cannot claim that she is available to marry her betrothed, as it is prohibited for her to engage in intercourse with him. The Gemara challenges: This is also difficult, as **with what** circumstance is the *baraita* dealing? **If** it is referring to **where** she committed adultery **willingly, is she entitled** to receive payment of her **marriage contract? Rather,** it is referring **to** a case of **rape.** But in that case, she **is permitted to him.** Why should she return the betrothal money?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא בְּאֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן שֶׁנֶּאֶנְסָה, וּבְקִדּוּשִׁין לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: קִדּוּשִׁין לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ, וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: לָאו לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – מְסַפְּקָא לֵיהּ אִי לְטִיבּוּעִין נִיתְּנוּ אִי לָא,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* is referring **to the wife,** i.e., the betrothed, **of a priest, who was raped** and is prohibited from engaging in intercourse with her betrothed. She therefore cannot claim: Give me my husband and I will rejoice with him, yet she is entitled to receive payment of her marriage contract, **and** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to** whether **betrothal** money **is given as a sunk** cost: **Rabbi Meir holds that betrothal** money **is given as a sunk** cost. **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that it **is not given as a sunk** cost. **And Rabbi Yosei is uncertain if** it **is given as a sunk** cost **or not.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכָּךְ קִדְּשָׁהּ בְּעֶשְׂרִים – נוֹתֵן לָהּ שְׁלֹשִׁים חֲצָאִין. קִדְּשָׁהּ בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים – נוֹתֵן לָהּ עֶשְׂרִים חֲצָאִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains Rabbi Yosei's opinion: **And** since it is uncertain whether or not she is required to return the betrothal money, **therefore,** she returns only half the money: If **he betrothed her with twenty** *sela*, which are equivalent to eighty dinars, she owes him forty dinars. Yet, if she is widowed or divorced, she is entitled to one hundred dinars in payment of her marriage contract, so he or his estate **gives her thirty halves** of a *sela*, which are equivalent to sixty dinars, so that she receives one hundred dinars in all. If **he betrothed her with thirty** *sela*, which are equivalent to one hundred twenty dinars, she owes him sixty dinars. Since she is entitled to one hundred dinars in payment of her marriage contract, he **gives her twenty halves** of a *sela*, equivalent to forty dinars.

Rabbi Yosi is uncertain which position is correct — *mesafeka lei ei le-tibu'in nitnu ei lav* — and therefore splits the difference. His ruling is expressed through two parallel cases. If the man betrothed her with twenty *shekalim* (worth eighty *zuz*): Rabbi Yosi awards her thirty "halves" (*shloshim chatza'in*), meaning thirty half-*shekalim*, which equals sixty *zuz*. The logic is that she is conceptually treated as having half of the *kiddushin* returned, so she is left with forty *zuz* worth of *kiddushin*, and the *kesubah* balance owed to her is correspondingly sixty *zuz*. Conversely, if he betrothed her with thirty *shekalim* (worth one hundred and twenty *zuz*): she is awarded twenty half-*shekalim* (forty *zuz*), on the same logic of splitting the uncertainty down the middle.

### Rav Yosef Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף בַּר מִנְיוֹמֵי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַחְזִיר – מַחְזִירִין. וְתַרְגּוּמָא: נְהַרְדְּעָא. שְׁאָר בָּבֶל מַאי? רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מוּהְרֵי הָדְרִי, קִדּוּשֵׁי לָא הָדְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef bar Minyumi says** that **Rav Naḥman says: In every place where they were accustomed to return** the betrothal money, **they return** it. **And the interpretation** of this statement is that it is referring to **Neharde'a.** The Gemara asks: With regard to **the rest of Babylonia, what** is the *halakha*? **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say:** The **presents are returned** and the **betrothal** money **is not returned.**

Rav Yosef bar Minyumi citing Rav Nachman rules: *bechol makom she'nahagu l'hachzir machzirin* — wherever the *minhag* is to return, one returns. The Gemara qualifies that this ruling applies specifically to *Nehardea* and communities like it, where the practice was indeed to return *kiddushin* in cases of the woman's death, and therefore the operative rule is not *le-tibu'in nitnu* but rather follows local custom.

### Babylonian Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, הִלְכְתָא: בֵּין שֶׁמֵּת הוּא, בֵּין שֶׁמֵּתָה הִיא, וַהֲדַר בֵּיהּ הוּא – מוּהְרֵי הָדְרִי, קִדּוּשֵׁי לָא הָדְרִי. הֲדַרָא בָּהּ אִיהִי – אֲפִילּוּ קִדּוּשֵׁי נָמֵי הָדְרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: The *halakha*** is that **whether he died** or **whether she died or** whether **he retracted** his agreement to the betrothal and divorced her, the **presents are returned** and the **betrothal** money **is not returned.** If **she retracted** her agreement to the betrothal and requested a divorce, **even** the **betrothal** money **is returned.**

Rabbah and Rav Yosef both say, with respect to the rest of *Bavel* outside those communities: *ma'ori hadri, kiddushei lo hadri* — the *savlonot* (here called *ma'ori*, a term the Rashbam explains as related to *mohar*, a gift sent by the groom during the *eirusin* period and noted in the *kesubah*) are returned, but *kiddushin* is not. The Rashbam observes the interesting semantic evolution: the biblical *mohar* was *kesef kiddushin*; here the word has shifted to denote the *savlonot*-type gifts, reflecting a transition in which the *kesubah* absorbed and reframed the original *mohar* obligation, while the term migrated to describe the betrothal-period gifts.

### Rav Pappa Final

Rav Pappa states the *halacha*: *bein shemet hu bein shemetah hi v'hadri behu* — whether he dies, whether she dies, or whether either party calls off the engagement, the *ma'ori* (the *savlonot* gifts) are returned, and *kiddushin* is not. The *kesef kiddushin* is *le-tibu'in nitnu* and belongs to the woman under all circumstances — unless she is the one actively initiating the cancellation, in which case Rav Pappa suggests that even the *kiddushin* itself would have to be returned.

### Amimar Dissent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמֵּימָר אָמַר: קִדּוּשֵׁי לָא הָדְרִי, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בַּאֲחוֹתָהּ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: גִּיטָּהּ מוֹכִיחַ עָלֶיהָ. וְהָא דְּרַב אָשֵׁי בְּדוּתָא הִיא, דְּאִיכָּא דְּשָׁמַע בְּהָא וְלָא שָׁמַע בְּהָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ameimar said:** The **betrothal** money **is not returned.** This is a rabbinic **decree, lest** people **say** that **betrothal takes effect with her sister.** If the betrothal money is returned, people are likely to think that the betrothal is retroactively nullified and that the man can betroth her sister, whereas in fact the betrothal was not retroactively nullified and he cannot betroth her. **Rav Ashi said: Her bill of divorce proves for her** that she was betrothed, so this concern is not in effect. The Gemara comments: **And this** statement **of Rav Ashi is a mistake, since there are** those **who heard about this,** i.e., the return of the betrothal money, **but did not hear about that,** i.e., the bill of divorce.

Amimar disagrees with Rav Pappa's exception. Even if she is the initiating party, *kiddushei lo hadri* — the *kiddushin* ring does not go back. The reason is a *gzeira: shema yomru kiddushin tofsin b'achotah* — lest people say that *kiddushin* takes effect with her sister. The concern is that if the ring is visibly returned, onlookers will conclude that the *kiddushin* never took place or was fully undone, and the man might proceed to betroth the woman's sister without realizing that a *get* is required first. Once he has given a *get*, he would ordinarily be forbidden to her sister only as long as she is a *gerusha* — but absent a *get*, if people believe there was no binding *kiddushin*, he might believe himself free to marry the sister outright. To prevent this confusion, the ring must never be returned, regardless of who initiates the cancellation.

Rav Ashi initially disagreed with this *gzeira*, arguing that *gita mochiach alah* — the existence of the *get* makes it self-evident that *kiddushin* had taken place. But the Gemara reports that Rav Ashi retracted (*vehadar Rav Ashi bediburei*), because *d'ika desham'a beha v'lo sham'a beha* — not everyone hears everything; one person may have seen the ring returned but not heard about the *get*, and the confusion Amimar feared is entirely realistic. Amimar's protective *gzeira* is therefore the accepted position.

## Shushbinut Properties

### Collectible Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהַשּׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת נִגְבֵּית בְּבֵית דִּין. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים נֶאֶמְרוּ בְּשׁוֹשְׁבִינוּת: נִגְבֵּית בְּבֵית דִּין, וְחוֹזֶרֶת בְּעוֹנָתָהּ, וְאֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם רִבִּית,
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the gifts of groomsmen are reciprocated after the father's death, they are reciprocated to the middle, **because gifts of groomsmen are** a legal debt owed to the father, **collectible in court. The Sages taught** (*Tosefta* 10:8): **Five statements were said with regard to gifts of groomsmen:** They are **collectible in court; and** they are **reciprocated** only **at their** relevant **time,** i.e., at the time of the groomsman's own wedding; **and they are not subject to** the prohibition of **interest,** i.e., it is permitted to reciprocate with a gift of greater value than the original gift;

Having completed the extended digression through *kiddushin* and *savlonot*, the Gemara returns to *shushbinut* and enumerates its distinctive legal characteristics. *Shushbinos nigbis b'veit din* — *shushbinut* is collectible in *beis din*. This is a striking ruling: unlike an ordinary gift, which carries no legal obligation of reciprocity, *shushbinut* functions as an enforceable debt. The irony, as the Gemara immediately makes clear, is that while it is collectible like a loan, it is simultaneously a debt with no fixed amount and no fixed payment schedule.

### Five Characteristics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הַשְּׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתָּהּ, וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּהּ פִּי שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **and the Sabbatical** Year **does not abrogate them; and the firstborn does not take a double** portion **of them.**

*Tanu Rabbanan*: the Rabbis taught that five things were said about *shushbinut*. First, *nigbis b'veit din* — it is collectible in court. Second, *chozeres b'onasah* — it is returned specifically at its proper time: when the gift-giver himself makes a wedding, and not before; one may not prepay the obligation. Third, *ein bah mishum ribis* — there is no prohibition of *ribis* (prohibited interest), even if the amount returned differs from the amount originally given. Fourth, *eina shevi'is m'shamatata* — the *shemitta* year does not annul it. Fifth, *ein habechor notel bah pi shenayim* — the firstborn son does not receive a double portion of *shushbinut* assets in an inheritance.

### Collectibility Explained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִגְבֵּית בְּבֵית דִּין – מַאי טַעְמָא? כְּמִלְוֶה דָּמְיָא. וְאֵין בָּהּ מִשּׁוּם רִבִּית – דְּלָאו אַדַּעְתָּא דְּהָכִי יְהַב לֵיהּ. וְאֵין הַשְּׁבִיעִית מְשַׁמַּטְתָּהּ – דְּלָא קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ: ״לֹא יִגֹּשׂ״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states that gifts of groomsmen **are collectible in court. What is the reason** for this? It is that they are considered **similar to a loan.** The *baraita* states: **And they are not subject to** the prohibition of **interest.** The reason is **that** it was **not** with **that in mind** that he gave him a larger gift. Rather, he did so on account of his joy at his friend's wedding. The *baraita* states: **And the Sabbatical** Year **does not abrogate them.** The reason is **that one cannot read** the verse concerning the abrogation of debts during the occurrence of the Sabbatical Year: **"He shall not exact** it of his neighbor and his brother, because the Lord's release has been proclaimed" (Deuteronomy 15:2), **with regard to** gifts of groomsmen. Since one cannot choose to exact the gifts until the time of one's own wedding, they are not addressed by this verse.

The Gemara explains each ruling. *Nigbis b'veit din mai ta'ama? K'milveh damya* — why is it collectible in court? Because it functions like a loan. This analogy to a loan is what places it within the jurisdiction of *beis din*, even though the social dynamics of the relationship are not those of a creditor and debtor.

There is no *ribis* because *d'lav ada'ata d'hachi yahav lei* — it was not given with the understanding that an exact equivalent amount would be returned. The parties understood from the outset that the repayment amount might differ. Since the original gift was not extended as a commercial loan with a fixed repayment expectation, returning a larger or smaller amount does not violate the prohibition of *ribis*.

*Shemitta* does not annul it. The Rashbam explains this on technical grounds: *she'eino yachol l'dachko b'veitin b'shvi'is she-harei lo higia zmano* — if the recipient has not yet married and hosted his own wedding, the time for repayment has not yet arrived at all. *Shemitta* can only cancel a debt whose due date has passed; a debt whose due date has not yet arrived at the onset of *shemitta* remains in force. There is also a deeper structural point, which *Tosafot* notes in anticipation of the Gemara's own language: *d'lo kari bei lo yigos* — the verse prohibiting creditor pressure (*lo yigos et re'eihu v'et achiv*) does not apply here, because the power dynamic of borrower and lender is absent from *shushbinut*. The obligation exists between friends and equals, not between those in the coercive relationship of creditor and debtor.

### Bechor Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם – דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ רָאוּי, וְאֵין הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל בָּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* states: **And the firstborn does not take a double** portion from them. The reason is **that they constitute potential** inheritance, **and the firstborn does not take** in inheritance the property **due** the deceased **as** he does the property the deceased **possessed.**

Finally, the firstborn does not receive a double portion of *shushbinut* assets — *d'hava lei ra'uy, v'ein habechor notel b'ra'uy k'v'muchzak*. In the case where the return gift flows to the entire family estate (as in the *yibum* case discussed earlier, or in the case of a generic family representative), the *shushbinut* owed back to that estate has not yet been paid; it is money anticipated but not yet in hand. The *bechor* receives a double portion only of assets that are *muchzak* — already in the possession of the estate at the time of inheritance — not of assets that are merely *ra'uy*, awaiting collection. Since the reciprocal *shushbinut* gift had not yet been paid at the time the estate came into being, it is *ra'uy*, and the *bechor* receives only his single proportional share along with the rest of the heirs.
