# Hullin 86 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chullin Ba'azarah Status

### Shechita She'eina Re'uya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: לֹא לַכֹּל אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה, מוֹדֶה רַבִּי מֵאִיר שֶׁאֵין מַתִּירָתָהּ בַּאֲכִילָה. פְּשִׁיטָא! טְרֵפָה בִּשְׁחִיטָה מִי מִישְׁתַּרְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **The Master said** that **not with regard to all** *halakhot* did **Rabbi Meir say** an act of **slaughter that is not fit is considered** an act of **slaughter,** as **Rabbi Meir concedes that** such an act of slaughter **does not render** the meat of the animal **permitted for consumption.** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious? Can a *tereifa* become permitted** for consumption **through slaughter?**

The daf begins at the bottom of 85b, picking up with Rav Papa's question to Abaye. The discussion concerns *shechita she'eina re'uya* — a *shechita* that is technically valid but not fit for eating — and its application to the prohibition of *chullin ba'azarah*, the slaughter of non-consecrated animals within the Temple precincts. The specific context involves Rabbi Shimon's position: he holds that a *shechita she'eina re'uya* is nonetheless sufficient to prevent the slaughtered animal from being classified as a *neveilah*. This means that even if a *treifa* was slaughtered in the *azarah* — an act of *chullin ba'azarah* — Rabbi Shimon counts that act as a valid enough *shechita* to prevent the animal from being considered *neveilah*, while simultaneously exempting the one who performed it from the prohibition of *chullin ba'azarah* on account of the *shechita she'eina re'uya* not being a fully effective *shechita*. The *gemara* now turns to examine whether this analysis presupposes that Rabbi Shimon holds *chullin ba'azarah* to be a biblical prohibition.

### Biblical vs. Rabbinic Lo Ta'amod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, וְהָתְנַן רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: חוּלִּין שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטוּ בַּעֲזָרָה יִשָּׂרְפוּ בָּאֵשׁ, וְכֵן חַיָּה שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה בָּעֲזָרָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – הַיְינוּ דְּגָזְרִינַן חַיָּה אַטּוּ בְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to** Rav Pappa: **Yes** he does. **And we learned** in a mishna (*Temura* 33b) that **Rabbi Shimon says: Non-sacred** animals **that were slaughtered in the** Temple **courtyard shall be burned in the fire. And likewise, an undomesticated animal that was slaughtered in the Temple courtyard** shall be burned in the fire. Abaye continues: **Granted, if you say** the meat of a non-sacred domesticated animal slaughtered in the Temple courtyard is forbidden **by Torah law, this is** the reason why **we decree** that the meat of **an undomesticated animal** is prohibited as well, **due to** the Torah prohibition against deriving benefit from the meat of **a domesticated animal.**

The *gemara* asks: *v'savar Rabbi Shimon chullin ba'azarah d'oraita hi?* Does Rabbi Shimon truly hold that *chullin ba'azarah* is a biblical concern? The question arises from a subtle but important assumption embedded in his ruling. If *chullin ba'azarah* were only a rabbinic prohibition, it is hard to see why the formal technicality of *shechita she'eina re'uya* would be relevant at all.

To appreciate this point fully, one must first understand the nature of the prohibition itself. One does not find an explicit verse in the Torah stating that one may not slaughter a non-consecrated animal within the Temple. What the Torah does say is *vezavachta ki yirchak mimcha hamakom* — you shall slaughter when the place is far from you. The implication, read as a negative derived from a positive, is that slaughter within the precincts, when one is not far from the *Beit HaMikdash*, is not permitted. Whether this implicit prohibition carries full biblical force, or is merely a rabbinic enactment supported by an *asmachta*, is precisely the question at stake. There are also two dimensions to the question: whether the *act* of slaughtering a non-consecrated animal inside the *azarah* is biblically prohibited, or whether the *result* — the prohibition on eating the meat of such an animal — is what is biblical.

What makes this *gemara* especially illuminating is its implicit assumption about the relationship between biblical law and formal technicality. The *gemara* assumes that if *chullin ba'azarah* is a biblical prohibition, then formal definitions matter: we must ask precisely whether this act constitutes a *shechita* or not, and if it does not fully qualify — because it is a *shechita she'eina re'uya* — then the prohibition is not triggered. A biblical prohibition operates through its formal criteria, and if those criteria are not met, the prohibition does not apply. By contrast, if *chullin ba'azarah* were only rabbinic, the *gemara* reasons that we would look to the underlying concern rather than the technical definition. The rabbinic concern behind the prohibition is presumably the fear of confusion — that slaughtering ordinary animals inside the *azarah* will blur the boundary between *chullin* and *kodashim*, leading people to treat consecrated animals as ordinary food or to consume *kodashim* outside the Temple. If that is the animating concern, then the technicality of whether this constitutes a fully valid *shechita* or not is entirely beside the point. The act of slaughtering inside the *azarah* still creates precisely the kind of confusion the rabbis were trying to prevent, regardless of the *treifa* status of the animal.

This is a striking insight that runs beneath the surface of the *gemara*'s technical question. When dealing with Torah law, we operate formalistically — we do not presume to know precisely why the Torah commanded what it did, and we do not reshape the law based on our understanding of its purpose. *Lo darshinan ta'ama dikra*: there is a certain humility in not deriving conclusions from the reasons behind biblical commandments, because we cannot be certain we have identified those reasons correctly. But when dealing with rabbinic legislation, where the legislators' purposes are in principle accessible and the law is built around those purposes, the formal technicalities should yield to whether the goal is being achieved or not. Since Rabbi Shimon does apply the technicality of *shechita she'eina re'uya* to exempt from *chullin ba'azarah*, this implies he holds the prohibition to be biblical in character.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דְּרַבָּנַן, בְּהֵמָה מַאי טַעְמָא? דִּילְמָא אָתֵי לְמֵיכַל קָדָשִׁים בַּחוּץ, הִיא גּוּפַהּ גְּזֵרָה, וַאֲנַן נֵיקוּם וְנִגְזוֹר גְּזֵירָה לִגְזֵירָה?
>
> **Translation:** **But if you say** the meat of domesticated animals is forbidden **by rabbinic law** then the mishna is difficult: **What is the reason** the meat of a non-sacred **domesticated animal** is forbidden? It is forbidden **lest one** see someone consuming this meat outside the Temple courtyard and **arrive** at the erroneous conclusion that it is permitted **to partake of sacrificial** meat **outside** the Temple courtyard. If so, the prohibition against deriving benefit from the meat of a non-sacred domesticated animal is itself a rabbinic **decree, and will we** then **proceed to issue a decree** prohibiting one to derive benefit from a non-sacred undomesticated animal in order **to** prevent the violation of **a decree?**

The *gemara* affirms that yes, Rabbi Shimon holds *chullin ba'azarah* to be biblical — and brings a proof from a *mishna*. Rabbi Shimon states: *chullin she'nishchetu ba'azarah yisarfu ba'eish* — non-consecrated animals slaughtered in the *azarah* must be burned. And further: *v'chen chaya she'nishcheta ba'azarah* — even a *chaya*, a wild animal such as a deer, which can never itself be brought as a *korban*, is similarly burned if slaughtered in the *azarah*.

### Gezeira vs. Biblical Law

The *gemara* uses this extension to the *chaya* as the decisive proof. If *chullin ba'azarah* is biblical, we can readily understand why Rabbi Shimon extends the prohibition to include a *chaya*. Even though a deer can never be a *korban*, the biblical prohibition applies broadly, or at minimum a one-degree rabbinic extension from a biblical law — a straightforward *gezeira* — is well within halachic bounds. Indeed, Rashi notes that if the prohibition is truly biblical, the *chaya* may not even require a *gezeira* at all; the biblical prohibition would simply extend to it formally.

But if the entire structure of *chullin ba'azarah* is itself rabbinic — enacted to prevent confusion between *chullin* and *kodashim* — then the extension to a *chaya* becomes untenable. The *gemara* frames it as follows: if the prohibition on slaughtering a non-consecrated cow in the *azarah* is already a rabbinic safeguard, *hi gufa gezeira*, then prohibiting a deer becomes *gezeira ligezeira* — a safeguard upon a safeguard — which the rabbis do not enact. A deer cannot be confused with a *korban* directly; the concern would only be that one might confuse a deer with a cow, and the cow with a *korban*. That is two degrees of removal, and rabbinic legislation does not reach that far. Since Rabbi Shimon does extend the prohibition to a *chaya*, this proves he understands the prohibition as biblical in nature, where a single-degree rabbinic extension is permissible.

## Bird Blood & Shechita

### Treifa Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא נְפַל לֵיהּ יָאנִיבָא בְּכִיתָּנֵיהּ, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁקוֹל עוֹפָא וּשְׁחוֹט עַל בּוּבִיתָא דְּמַיָּא, דְּמוֹרַח דְּמָא וְשָׁבֵיק לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one who stabs an undomesticated animal or bird, or who tears loose its windpipe and gullet, is exempt from covering the blood. With regard to this the Gemara relates: **Moths infested the flax of Rabbi Ḥiyya. He came before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi to ask how to get rid of the moths. Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Take a bird and slaughter** it **over the tub of water** in which the flax was soaked, **as** the moths will **smell the blood and leave** the flax.

The *gemara* now transitions to an account involving Rabbi Chiya, which brings us back to the central topic of *kisuy hadam*, the covering of blood. Rabbi Chiya had an infestation of a worm or pest in his flax, and the remedy was to use the blood of a bird, as the creatures would smell the blood and abandon the flax. He came before Rabbi to ask what to do.

The immediate problem: if one performs a proper *shechita* on the bird, one is obligated to cover the blood. A *baraita* states explicitly that even *hashochet v'tzarich l'dam* — one who slaughters and wants only the blood, not the meat — is still obligated to cover the blood. The obligation of *kisuy hadam* is not contingent on wanting to eat the meat; it is contingent only on the *shechita* being one that is fit for the meat to be eaten. So if the bird is perfectly healthy and one performs a valid *shechita*, the blood must be covered — and pouring it directly onto the flax and water would be impossible.

Rabbi's solution: *shekol of ushchot al bubita d'maya* — take a bird and slaughter it over the water in which the flax is soaking. Rashi explains that this refers to the water used to soak the flax. The blood flows directly into the water, the pests smell it and flee.

But then how is this permitted? The *gemara* asks: *heichi avid hachi?* How could Rabbi advise this? The answer given is: *o nochro o okro* — either do not perform a *shechita* at all and kill the bird in some other fashion, or tear out its *simanim* in a manner that is not *shechita*. By killing the bird in a way that does not constitute *shechita*, the blood that emerges is not blood that requires covering.

### Two Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: הַשּׁוֹחֵט וְצָרִיךְ לַדָּם – חַיָּיב לְכַסּוֹת. כֵּיצַד יַעֲשֶׂה? אוֹ נוֹחֲרוֹ, אוֹ עוֹקְרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How could** Rabbi Ḥiyya **do this? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who slaughters** a bird or an undomesticated animal, **and** does so only because he **needs the blood, is** nevertheless **obligated to cover** the blood and may not use it for any other purpose? Rather, **how should** one who needs the blood **act?** He **either stabs it or tears loose** its windpipe and its gullet, so that it is not killed with an act of slaughter.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported Rabbi's instruction differently: *tzei t'rof* — go make the bird a *treifa* first, and then perform the *shechita*. According to Ravin's version, Rabbi said: *tzei nechor* — go and kill the bird without *shechita* at all. So there are two distinct transmissions of what Rabbi advised, and the *gemara* must reconcile them.

### Shechita for Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: "צֵא טְרוֹף" אֲמַר לֵיהּ, כִּי אֲתָא רָבִין אָמַר: "צֵא נְחוֹר" אֲמַר לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya: **Go out** and **render** the bird **a *tereifa*,** and then slaughter it, as the mishna teaches that one who slaughters a *tereifa* is not required to cover its blood. **When Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said** that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to** Rabbi Ḥiyya: **Go out** and **tear loose** the bird's windpipe and gullet.

The *gemara* asks: according to Rav Dimi, who transmits that Rabbi said to make the bird a *treifa* first, why did he not simply say to kill the bird without *shechita*? The *gemara* entertains the possibility: perhaps Rabbi held *ein shechita l'of min haTorah* — that the Torah does not require *shechita* for birds at all, and killing a bird in any fashion renders the meat fit for eating. If so, even a bird killed without *shechita* would require its blood to be covered, since the meat would be considered fit to eat. In that case, simply killing the bird without *shechita* would not solve the problem; one would still be obligated to cover the blood. The only way to guarantee that the blood need not be covered would be to first render the bird a *treifa*, making the meat unfit, and then perform the *shechita* — a *shechita she'eina re'uya*, which here would not trigger the obligation of *kisuy hadam*.

### Kaasher Tziviticha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, קָסָבַר אֵין שְׁחִיטָה לָעוֹף מִן הַתּוֹרָה, וּנְחִירָתוֹ זוֹ הִיא שְׁחִיטָתוֹ, וְהָתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: "כַּאֲשֶׁר צִוִּיתִךָ" – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁנִּצְטַוָּה מֹשֶׁה עַל הַוֶּשֶׁט וְעַל הַקָּנֶה, וְעַל רוֹב אֶחָד בָּעוֹף, וְעַל רוֹב שְׁנַיִם בַּבְּהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** the reason is because Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **slaughter of a bird is not** obligatory **by Torah law, and** consequently **the tearing loose** of **its** windpipe and gullet **is** considered **its slaughter** such that he would be required to cover the blood, this is untenable. As **isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The verse states: "And you shall slaughter…**as I have commanded you"** (Deuteronomy 12:21). This **teaches that Moses was** previously **commanded** concerning the mitzvot of slaughter, i.e., he was taught **about the gullet and the windpipe,** that the cutting of these *simanim* constitutes slaughter, **and about** the requirement to cut the **majority of one** *siman* **of a bird, and about** the requirement to cut **the majority of two** *simanim* **of an animal.** If so, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that the slaughter of a bird is obligatory by Torah law.

But the *gemara* immediately rejects this: *vehatanya* — we have a *baraita* in which Rabbi himself states that *kaasher tziviticha*, the verse in Devarim meaning "as I have commanded you," teaches that Moshe was commanded at Sinai regarding the specific laws of *shechita*: the cutting of the *veshet* and *kaneh*, the esophagus and trachea, the majority of one *siman* in a bird, and the majority of two *simanim* in a large animal. This is the only verse that alludes to an oral tradition of *shechita* procedure. Tosafot famously notes a play on the word *kaasher*: reading *k-a-sh-r* in reverse, one can derive *rubo shel echad kamohu* — the majority of one *siman* is equivalent to the whole — as a hint embedded in the text.

Since Rabbi himself is the source of the ruling that *shechita* for birds is a biblical requirement, it cannot be that Rav Dimi's version of Rabbi's advice was motivated by the possibility that *shechita* is not biblically required for birds. The *gemara* therefore resolves the matter differently, at the top of 86a.

### Blood Covering Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא "צֵא נְחוֹר" דְּלָאו שְׁחִיטָה הִיא כְּלָל, אֲבָל "צֵא טְרוֹף" אֵימָא שְׁחִיטָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ רְאוּיָה שְׁמָהּ שְׁחִיטָה וְלִיבְעֵי כִּסּוּי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן כִּדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav Dimi **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary.** That is, **it is not necessary** to teach: **Go out** and **tear loose, since** it is obvious that **this is not** considered **slaughter at all** and one is not required to cover the blood. **But** with regard to the instruction: **Go out** and **render** the bird **a *tereifa*, I would say** that an act of **slaughter that is not fit** to render the meat permitted **is** nevertheless **considered** an act of **slaughter, and** the blood of this bird **should require covering.** Therefore, Rav Dimi **teaches us in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** (85a), that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds with regard to the mitzva of covering the blood that an act of slaughter that is not fit to render the meat permitted is not considered an act of slaughter, and one is therefore not required to cover the blood of this bird.

The *gemara* explains both versions using the principle of *lo mibaya kaamar* — it goes without saying. According to Rav Dimi's version: of course the simple solution is to kill the bird without *shechita* at all — that goes without saying. But Rabbi came to teach a *chiddush*: even if you make it a *treifa* and then perform a proper *shechita*, you are not obligated to cover the blood, because as Rabbi Chiya bar Abba reported in Rabbi Yochanan's name, when it comes to *kisuy hadam*, even Rabbi — who generally rules that *shechita she'eina re'uya shema shechita*, that such a *shechita* counts — agrees that here, because the verse uses the word *ve'achalta*, indicating the meat must be fit to eat, only a *shechita re'uya* obligates *kisuy hadam*. So by making the bird a *treifa* first, the *shechita* is not *re'uya*, and there is no obligation to cover the blood.

According to Ravin's version, Rabbi said kill it without *shechita*. That version's *chiddush* is the opposite: of course making it a *treifa* first would work — everyone knows that. The novelty is that even simply killing it without any *shechita* works, because Rabbi holds *shechita* for birds is biblically required. One might have thought that even a non-*shechita* killing of a bird counts as its *shechita* in some sense, and perhaps the blood would still require covering. Rabbi therefore teaches that no — killing without *shechita* is entirely outside the framework of *kisuy hadam* and the blood need not be covered.

The practical upshot is as follows. The desire to use the blood does not exempt one from *kisuy hadam*. The meat being fit to eat — not whether one actually intends to eat it — is what triggers the obligation. To avoid the obligation, one must either perform a *shechita she'eina re'uya* by making the bird a *treifa* first and then slaughtering, or kill the bird in a manner that does not constitute *shechita* at all. Either route removes the obligation to cover.

## Merit & Suffering

### Infestation Paradox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי נְפַל לֵיהּ יָאנִיבָא בְּכִיתָּנֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר רָבִין בַּר אַבָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין בַּר שְׁבָא: מִשֶּׁעָלוּ בְּנֵי הַגּוֹלָה פָּסְקוּ הַזִּיקִין וְהַזְּוָעוֹת וְהָרוּחוֹת וְהָרְעָמִים, וְלֹא הֶחְמִיץ יֵינָם, וְלֹא לָקָה פִּשְׁתָּנָם, וְנָתְנוּ חֲכָמִים עֵינֵיהֶם בְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא וּבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara questions the very occurrence of the incident involving Rabbi Ḥiyya: **And could moths have infested his flax? But doesn't Ravin bar Abba say, and some say Rabbi Avin bar Sheva says: From when the people of the Exile ascended** from Babylonia to Eretz Yisrael **there ceased** to be **meteors, earthquakes,** storm **winds, and thunder; and their wine did not sour, and their flax was not stricken** with an infestation of moths; **and the Sages placed their eyes,** i.e., attributed these phenomena, **to** the merit of **Rabbi Ḥiyya and his sons,** who ascended from Babylonia? If so, how was Rabbi Ḥiyya's flax affected?

The *gemara* pauses to ask a striking question about the story itself. How could Rabbi Chiya have had a worm infestation in his flax in the first place? A tradition is cited — in the name of Ravin bar Abba, or some say Rav Avin bar Shaba — that from the time righteous people came up from the exile to Eretz Yisrael, certain natural calamities ceased: *pasku hazikin ve'hazva'os ve'haruchos ve'hara'amim* — harmful celestial phenomena, violent winds, and storms subsided. And further: *velo laka pishtanam* — the flax was free from infestation. Rashi explains that this refers to a later period of migration by the *Tannaim*, not the original return from Babylonian exile. The *gemara* notes that the *chachamim* cast their eyes upon Rabbi Chiya and his sons as the primary individuals whose merit accounted for this protection. How, then, could Rabbi Chiya himself have suffered from precisely the infestation his merit was supposed to prevent?

### Chananya's Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מַהְנְיָא זְכוּתַיְיהוּ – אַעָלְמָא, אַדִּידְהוּ – לָא, כִּדְרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּכׇל יוֹם וָיוֹם בַּת קוֹל יוֹצֵאת וְאוֹמֶרֶת: כָּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ נִיזּוֹן בִּשְׁבִיל חֲנִינָא בְּנִי, וַחֲנִינָא בְּנִי דַּי לוֹ בְּקַב חָרוּבִין מֵעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת לְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **When their merit is effective,** it is effective **for the** rest of the **world but not for themselves.** And this is **in accordance with** the statement **that Rav Yehuda says** in the name of **Rav, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Each and every day a Divine Voice emerges and says: The entire world is sustained in the merit of Ḥanina** ben Dosa, **My son, and** yet for **Ḥanina, My son, a *kav* of carobs,** i.e., a very small amount of inferior food, **is sufficient** to sustain him **from** one **Shabbat eve to** the next **Shabbat eve.** Similarly, the merit of Rabbi Ḥiyya and his sons was effective for others but not for themselves.

The *gemara* answers simply: *ki mahani zchusaihu a'alma, adidideh lo* — his merit benefited the entire world, but it did not benefit himself. The shoemaker's children go unshod.

A parallel is drawn from a statement of Rav Yehuda in Rav's name: every single day a heavenly voice proclaims that the entire world is sustained in the merit of Chananya my son, and yet Chananya himself subsists on merely a *kav* of carob pods from one *Shabbos* eve to the next. The force of this *gemara* is not simply that Chananya chose an ascetic lifestyle. The point seems to be that he had no more than this to sustain himself — yet his merit upheld the entire world.

### Righteous Self-Sacrifice

The philosophical weight of this is worth pausing over, even if the *gemara* does not belabor it. There is a certain type of *tzaddik* who gives everything to others and places himself last. The sustenance flows outward and not inward. In a metaphysical register, the *gemara* seems to be saying that this is sometimes precisely what keeps the righteousness pure — that it operates entirely for others and not for oneself. There is also a more prosaic truth embedded here: people who devote themselves entirely to the needs of the community often neglect their own, and the very dedication that benefits others leaves them personally wanting. The *gemara* imported this teaching from its primary locations in Taanis and Berachot, citing it here only for the parallel it draws to Rabbi Chiya's situation.

## Cheresh Shoteh Katan

### Observation & Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חֵרֵשׁ שׁוֹטֶה וְקָטָן שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ, וַאֲחֵרִים רוֹאִין אוֹתָם – חַיָּיב לְכַסּוֹת. בֵּינָן לְבֵין עַצְמָן – פָּטוּר מִלְּכַסּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor who slaughtered** an undomesticated animal or a bird, **and others saw them** and ensured that the slaughter was properly performed, in which case the slaughter is valid (see 2a), one who oversaw the slaughter is **obligated to cover** the blood. If they slaughtered the animals **among themselves** without supervision, one is **exempt** from the obligation to cover the blood.

The *gemara* now returns to the opening *mishna* of the *masechet*, which ruled: *cheresh shoteh vekatan she'shachatu ve'acherim ro'im otam* — if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor performed *shechita* while others observed them and verified that the *shechita* was done properly, the *shechita* is valid. If observed and found correct, *chayav lechasot* — one is obligated to cover the blood.

### Blood Covering Concern

If, however, they slaughtered *beinam levein atzmam* — without anyone watching — we do not know whether the *shechita* was performed correctly. The *mishna* rules *patur milechasot* — exempt from covering the blood. The logic is that if one covers the blood, observers will assume the *shechita* was valid and will eat from the meat. Rather than cover the blood without a *beracha* as one might assume would be the natural response to a *safek*, it is better not to cover the blood at all, so as not to create a false impression that the meat is permitted.

### Oto Ve'et Beno

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן אוֹתוֹ וְאֶת בְּנוֹ, שֶׁשָּׁחֲטוּ וַאֲחֵרִים רוֹאִין אוֹתָן – אָסוּר לִשְׁחוֹט אַחֲרֵיהֶם, בֵּינָן לְבֵין עַצְמָן – רַבִּי מֵאִיר מַתִּיר לִשְׁחוֹט אַחֲרֵיהֶן, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹסְרִים, וּמוֹדִים שֶׁאִם שָׁחַט – שֶׁאֵינוֹ סוֹפֵג אֶת הָאַרְבָּעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And likewise with regard to the matter of** slaughtering **a mother and its offspring** on the same day, if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor **slaughtered** an undomesticated mother animal **and others saw them,** it is **prohibited to slaughter** its offspring **after them.** If they slaughtered the mother animal **among themselves, Rabbi Meir deems** it **permitted to slaughter** its offspring **after them and the Rabbis deem** it **prohibited. And** the Rabbis **concede that if one slaughtered** the offspring thereafter **that he does not incur the forty** lashes, as it is possible the mother was not properly slaughtered.

The *mishna* continues to a parallel case: *oto ve'et beno*, the prohibition against slaughtering an animal and its offspring on the same day. If a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* slaughtered the mother animal while others observed, the *shechita* is considered valid, and it is *assur lishchot achareihem* — forbidden to slaughter the offspring on that same day. If they slaughtered without observation, then we have uncertainty. Here, Rabbi Meir permits slaughtering the offspring on the same day — a position that initially seems startling, because perhaps the first *shechita* was valid, in which case slaughtering the child is a violation of *oto ve'et beno*. The *chachamim*, however, prohibit slaughtering the offspring that day.

### Active vs. Passive

The distinction between the two cases of the *mishna* rests on the difference between action and inaction. When one covers the blood, one is performing an act that sends a clear message — here is a valid *shechita*. When one simply refrains from slaughtering the offspring that day, no such message is sent. A bystander who sees that no *shechita* is occurring today has no particular reason to conclude that the earlier *shechita* was valid; one might simply not be hungry, or prefer a different food. The absence of *shechita* is invisible and sends no signal. Thus the *chachamim* can prohibit covering the blood — because the act carries a message — without being inconsistent in declining to prohibit refraining from *shechita*.

### Prohibition Overrides

A second and perhaps more fundamental distinction applies. Covering the blood is a positive *mitzvah*. The rabbis are empowered to instruct a person not to perform a positive *mitzvah* when circumstances warrant — and saying one is *patur* from the *mitzvah* is, in effect, such an instruction. But in the case of *oto ve'et beno*, the concern is the possible violation of a prohibition. Even if one were worried about sending the wrong message by refraining from *shechita*, the rabbis cannot grant license to possibly transgress a *lav*. When there is a genuine concern about violating a prohibition, one must be strict and refrain, regardless of any messaging considerations. Hence the *chachamim* say: do not slaughter the offspring today.

The *mishna* concludes with a point on which all agree: *she'eino sofeg et ha'arba'im* — if one did slaughter the offspring on the same day after the unobserved *shechita* of the *cheresh shoteh vekatan*, one does not receive the forty lashes. Since there is doubt whether the first *shechita* was valid, there is no certainty of a prohibition having been violated, and one is not lashed for a *safek*.

## Reisha and Seifa

### Apparent Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבָּנַן, מַאי שְׁנָא רֵישָׁא דְּלָא פְּלִיגִי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא סֵיפָא דִּפְלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, what is different** about **the first clause** of the mishna that discusses the covering of the blood, **where they do not disagree** with the statement that if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor slaughtered an animal without supervision one is exempt from the obligation to cover the blood, which indicates the Rabbis hold that such an act of slaughter is not considered an act of slaughter; **and what is different** about **the latter clause** of the mishna that discusses the prohibition against slaughtering a mother and its offspring on the same day, **where they disagree** with Rabbi Meir and hold that if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor slaughtered a mother animal without supervision one is prohibited to subsequently slaughter its offspring, indicating they hold that such an act of slaughter is in fact considered an act of slaughter?

The *gemara* asks: *rabanan mai shena reisha delo paligi u'mai shena seifa depaligi* — why do the rabbis take a position in the opening clause of the *mishna*, ruling that the blood need not be covered, but then also take a position in the closing clause, ruling that the offspring may not be slaughtered? The two rulings seem to operate on different principles. In the first case, the rabbis act as if the *shechita* was definitively invalid, refusing to cover the blood. In the second case, they act as if it might be valid, forbidding the slaughter of the offspring. The *gemara* explores whether these positions are internally consistent.

The *gemara* presses the point. In the first case — covering the blood — one might say: cover the blood without a *beracha*, since it is a *safek*. But the rabbis say do not cover, apparently because doing so would send the message that the *shechita* was good and people would eat the meat. But if that logic applies in the first case, why does it not apply in the second? If the rabbis say do not slaughter the offspring, does that not equally send the message that the first *shechita* was valid and thereby lead people to eat from that meat?

The *gemara* attempts to deflect: perhaps by not slaughtering, no message is sent — *amrei bisra delo ka mibaya lei*, he simply does not want meat today. But then the *gemara* presses from the other side: in the first case too, perhaps covering the blood sends no unambiguous message — one might be cleaning up the yard. The *gemara* counters: if someone comes to the rabbi and asks whether to cover the blood, and the rabbi says yes, that ruling clearly sends the message that it was a valid *shechita*. Symmetrically, if someone asks whether to slaughter the offspring today and the rabbi says no, that ruling also sends the message of a valid first *shechita*. The cases seem perfectly parallel, and the *gemara* cannot find a principled distinction between them using the messaging rationale alone.

### Rabbis Ignore Messages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, רַבָּנַן אַכּוּלַּהּ מִילְּתָא פְּלִיגִי, וְנָטְרִי לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר עַד דְּמַסֵּיק לַהּ לְמִילְּתָא, וַהֲדַר פְּלִיגִי עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes: **Rather,** it must be that **the Rabbis disagree concerning the entire matter,** i.e., they disagree with regard to covering the blood as well, and hold that if a disqualified person slaughtered an animal while unsupervised, one must cover the blood; **and** the Rabbis **waited for Rabbi Meir until he concluded his statement, and then they disagreed with him** on both accounts.

The resolution the *gemara* reaches is elegant: *ela rabbanan a'kulhu pligi* — the rabbis in fact argue against Rabbi Meir's entire framework, and they do not concern themselves with the messaging question at all. The rabbis treat the *shechita* of a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* as a genuine *safek* — a doubt — in both directions. Because it is a *safek*, they act consistently and strictly in both directions: cover the blood, because it might have been a valid *shechita*; do not slaughter the offspring, for the same reason. They do not exempt from covering the blood in order to send a message of invalidity, and they do not worry that not slaughtering the offspring sends a message of validity. Messages are simply not part of their calculus.

The *mishna* as we read it presented what appeared to be Rabbi Meir and the *chachamim* arguing only in the second clause, with agreement in the first. The *gemara* now explains that in fact, the *mishna* let Rabbi Meir complete his entire position before recording the rabbis' dissent: *d'shavkuhu l'Rabbi Meir ad d'masik lei l'miltei, v'hadar pligi alei* — they allowed Rabbi Meir to finish his full presentation and only then recorded their dispute. The apparent agreement in the first clause does not represent the rabbis' actual view; it represents Rabbi Meir's position being stated, and the dispute with the rabbis extends to everything.

### Rabbi Meir's Rov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רַבָּנַן לְחוּמְרָא, אֶלָּא רַבִּי מֵאִיר – מַאי טַעְמָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **granted,** the opinion of the **Rabbis** is understandable, as they consistently rule **stringently.** That is, although it is prohibited to consume the meat of an unsupervised slaughter performed by a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, the Rabbis require one to cover the blood and prohibit one to slaughter the offspring, due to concern that the person may have performed a valid slaughter. **But** with regard to the opinion of **Rabbi Meir** that one is exempt from covering the blood and that one may slaughter the offspring on the same day, **what is the reason** he does not rule stringently due to uncertainty?

What, then, is Rabbi Meir's position? Rabbi Yaakov said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: Rabbi Meir would hold that one who eats from the meat of an unobserved *shechita* of a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* is liable as if having eaten *neveilah* — the *shechita* is considered definitively invalid, not merely doubtful. The basis is *rov*: the majority of cases in which a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* performs an unobserved *shechita* are cases where they do it incorrectly. Following the majority, Rabbi Meir rules the *shechita* invalid. On the basis of that definitive ruling, he permits not covering the blood and permits slaughtering the offspring on the same day.

## Minority & Chazaka

### Meir's Minority Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְחַיֵּיב הָיָה רַבִּי מֵאִיר עַל שְׁחִיטָתָן מִשּׁוּם נְבֵלָה. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הוֹאִיל וְרוֹב מַעֲשֵׂיהֶן מְקוּלְקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Rabbi Meir would deem** one **liable** to receive lashes **for** eating from **the slaughter of** a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor, **due to** violation of the prohibition against eating from **an animal carcass.** According to Rabbi Meir there is no uncertainty with regard to such slaughter, and it is not considered an act of slaughter at all. Consequently, one may become liable to receive lashes for its consumption. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason? Rabbi Ami says: Since the majority of actions** of a deaf-mute, imbecile, and a minor **are bungled,** i.e., they are performed incompetently, it can be assumed that their slaughter was performed improperly as well.

A sharp challenge immediately arises. Rabbi Meir is famously the authority who is *choshesh l'miyuta* — who gives weight to minority cases and rules strictly even when a minority of situations present a concern. If so, even if the majority of *cheresh shoteh vekatan* perform *shechita* incorrectly, there is still a significant minority who do it correctly. Rabbi Meir's characteristic stringency should lead him to attend to that minority and forbid eating the meat on account of the minority possibility that it was valid — not to discard the minority and declare the meat definitively prohibited as *neveilah*. The argument seems to run in exactly the wrong direction.

### Rov Cannot Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא לְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: מַאי אִירְיָא רוֹב? אֲפִילּוּ מִיעוּט נָמֵי, דְּהָא רַבִּי מֵאִיר חָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא, סְמוֹךְ מִיעוּטָא לַחֲזָקָה, וְאִתְּרַע לֵיהּ רוּבָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, and some say** that **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** said **to Rav Pappa: Why** did Rabbi Ami **specifically** state that the reasoning of Rabbi Meir is based on the assumption that the **majority** of their actions are bungled? **Even** if only **a minority** of their actions are bungled and the majority are performed competently, Rabbi Meir would **also** maintain that the animal is considered a carcass, **as Rabbi Meir is concerned for a minority** when it can be combined with a presumptive status. If so, **append the minority to the presumptive status** of an animal prior to its slaughter, i.e., that it is prohibited for consumption, **and the majority** of competent acts of slaughter **is** thereby **weakened.**

The *gemara* resolves this with a subtle but important principle: *semoch miyuta l'chazaka ve'itrei lei rova* — attach the minority to the *chazaka*, the presumptive status quo, and the majority is undermined. Here is the logic. Before it was slaughtered, the animal had a *chazaka* — a presumptive status — of not being a valid *shechita*, of being a living non-*shechted* animal. Changing that status requires an effective act of *shechita*. Now, even if the majority of cases in which a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* slaughters unobserved result in a valid *shechita*, Rabbi Meir gives weight to the minority. That minority possibility, that the *shechita* was invalid, combines with the pre-existing status quo of the animal: it had not been validly slaughtered, and the minority possibility that it remains so is enough — combined with the *chazaka* — to prevent the majority from changing its status. *Rov* alone cannot overcome *chazaka* when the minority possibility has independent weight.

This is the productive irony of Rabbi Meir's position. His characteristic stringency in attending to minority possibilities is precisely what generates this result. By taking the minority seriously, he ensures that a *rov* cannot simply override a status quo. Normally one might think: most cases are valid, therefore assume validity. But Rabbi Meir says: the minority cannot be ignored, and a *rov* that faces a *chazaka* reinforced by the minority is not strong enough to change that status.

### Infant & Dough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: תִּינוֹק שֶׁנִּמְצָא בְּצַד הָעִיסָּה וּבָצֵק בְּיָדוֹ, רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְטַהֵר, וַחֲכָמִים מְטַמְּאִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁדַּרְכּוֹ שֶׁל תִּינוֹק לְטַפֵּחַ. וְאָמְרִינַן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? קָסָבַר: רוֹב תִּינוֹקוֹת מְטַפְּחִין, וּמִיעוּט אֵין מְטַפְּחִין, וְעִיסָּה זוֹ בְּחֶזְקַת טׇהֳרָה עוֹמֶדֶת, סְמוֹךְ מִיעוּטָא לַחֲזָקָה וְאִיתְּרַע לֵיהּ רוּבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proves that Rabbi Meir is concerned for the minority: **As we learned** in a mishna (*Teharot* 3:8): In the case of a ritually impure **child who is found alongside** ritually pure **started dough, and** he has **risen dough in his hand** that may have been removed from the larger portion of started dough, **Rabbi Meir deems** the started dough **pure.** This is because there is no proof the child touched it; he might have been given the piece by someone else. **And the Rabbis deem it impure,** as they assume he touched the started dough. The child is presumed to be impure **because it is the manner of a child to handle** items. **And we say** with regard to this mishna: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Meir? He holds** that **a majority of children handle** items, in this case the dough, that are within reach, **and a minority do not handle** items within reach, **and** the **dough** itself **retains a presumptive status of purity** since its impurity has not been definitively determined. One **appends** the **minority** of children who do not handle items within reach **to the presumptive status** of purity of the dough, **and** consequently the force of the **majority** of children who handle items within reach **is weakened.**

The *gemara* finds a proof for this principle in a *mishna* regarding *tumah*: *tinok shenimtza b'tzad ha-issa u'vatzek b'yado* — an infant found next to a batch of dough with a piece of dough in its hand. The *chachamim* rule the dough *tamei*, because the majority of infants pat and touch everything around them, and since the infant is *tamei* (Rashi: because the infant touched an insect; Tosafot: because the infant's mother, who typically handles the infant, is most often a *niddah*, rendering the infant *tamei*), the majority dictates that the infant touched and contaminated the rest of the dough. Rabbi Meir, however, rules the dough *tahor*. His reasoning: true, the majority of infants touch everything around them, but there is a minority who do not. And the dough began in a status of *taharah*. Attaching the minority possibility to the *chazaka* of *taharah*, one cannot allow the majority to change the status quo. The dough remains *tahor*.

### Ruling vs. Action

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם אָמְרוּ סְפֵק טוּמְאָה לְטַהֵר, יֹאמְרוּ סְפֵק אִיסּוּר לְהַתִּיר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds that the two cases are not comparable: **If they said** one may append the minority to the presumptive status with regard to a case of **uncertain ritual impurity** in order **to render** the dough **pure, will they say** that one may rely on a minority in the case of **an uncertain prohibition** in order **to permit** it? In other words, without the fact that a majority of the acts of slaughter of a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor are bungled, Rabbi Meir could neither deem one exempt from covering the blood nor allow one to slaughter the offspring immediately. Consequently, it is due only to the majority that Rabbi Meir deems one liable for violation of the prohibition against consuming an animal carcass when consuming meat from their slaughter.

The *gemara* presses: if Rabbi Meir says the *chazaka* combined with the minority suffices to render the dough *tahor* by the infant case, why in our case does the *gemara* say Rabbi Meir needs the *majority* to have done the *shechita* badly? Even if only a minority of *cheresh shoteh vekatan* did it badly, combined with the *chazaka* that the animal was not validly slaughtered, that should be enough for Rabbi Meir to rule the *shechita* definitively invalid.

The *gemara* answers: *im amru safek tumah letaher, yomru safek issur lehatir?* Just because a *safek* in *tumah* can result in a lenient ruling does not mean that the same logic licenses a lenient ruling when it comes to potential prohibitions. There is a categorical difference. By the dough, the ruling is simply about the status of the dough itself: it retains its existing status of *taharah*. By the *cheresh shoteh vekatan* and the offspring, the question is not merely maintaining the status of the slaughtered animal, but actively licensing a new act — going ahead and slaughtering the offspring today. Allowing some new behavior based on this logic is a larger step than simply affirming a status quo, and the *gemara* treats it as categorically different.

### Tosafot on Tahara

Tosafot adds a further distinction. The laws of *tumah ve'taharah* operate according to their own internal logic, including certain leniencies that apply specifically to that domain — particularly the well-known rule that a *safek tumah birshut harabbim* is *tahor*. It is possible that for Rabbi Meir, the *chazaka* plus minority amounts to a kind of fifty-fifty, which in the domain of *tumah ve'taharah* is enough to be lenient. But in other areas of law, where such specific leniencies for *safek* do not apply, a fifty-fifty doubt does not automatically generate a lenient outcome. Tosafot thus suggests that it is the peculiar rules of *tumah ve'taharah* that allow Rabbi Meir's logic to produce a lenient ruling for the dough, while the same logic does not transfer to permit slaughtering the offspring.

The upshot: certainly if Rabbi Meir holds that the majority of unobserved *shechitot* by a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* are invalid, he can confidently rule the *shechita* definitively not good and draw all the resulting consequences — no obligation to cover the blood, permission to slaughter the offspring on the same day, and liability for *neveilah* if one eats the meat.

## Rabbi's Final Ruling

### Conflicting Rulings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹרָה רַבִּי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְהוֹרָה רַבִּי כַּחֲכָמִים. הֵי מִינַּיְיהוּ דְּאַחֲרִיתָא?
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the dispute in the mishna, the Gemara notes: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **ruled in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who held that if a deaf-mute, an imbecile, or a minor slaughtered a mother animal, one may subsequently slaughter its offspring; **and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi also **ruled in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who held that it is prohibited to slaughter it. The Gemara asks: **Which of** these two rulings **is the later,** definitive ruling, and which ruling is the retracted one?

The *gemara* now reports that Rabbi issued rulings in both directions. On one occasion he ruled like Rabbi Meir — treating the unobserved *shechita* as definitively invalid. On another occasion he ruled like the *chachamim* — treating it as a genuine *safek* requiring strict treatment in both directions. This is not without precedent; great authorities sometimes issue different rulings to different questioners or at different stages of their lives.

### Which is Achreita?

The question raised in the *beis medrash* is: *hei mineihu debatrayta* — which ruling was Rabbi's last, and therefore his authoritative, final position? This is a standard question in *halachic* methodology: when a *posek* issues conflicting rulings, the later one is presumed to supersede.

### Missed Opportunity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא וְרַבִּי זֵירָא הֲווֹ קָיְימִי בְּשׁוּקָא דְּקֵיסָרִי, אַפִּתְחָא דְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא. נְפַק רַבִּי אַמֵּי אַשְׁכְּחִינְהוּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָאו אָמֵינָא לְכוּ בְּעִידָּן בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא לָא תְּקִימוּ אַבָּרַאי, דִּילְמָא אִיכָּא אִינָשׁ דְּמִיצְטַרְכָּא לֵיהּ שְׁמַעְתָּא וְאָתֵי לְאִיטְּרוֹדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from an incident: **Rabbi Abba, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba, and Rabbi Zeira were standing in the marketplace of Caesarea, at the entrance to the study hall. Rabbi Ami exited** the study hall **and found** the two of **them** standing there. Rabbi Ami **said to them: Have I not told you** that **at the time** when the **study hall** is in session **you should not stand outside,** as **perhaps there is a person** inside the study hall **who requires** clarification of **a *halakha*, and he will become bothered** by it because you will not be inside to assist in offering the proper explanation?

The *gemara* recounts: Rav Abba the son of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba and Rabbi Zeira were standing in the marketplace of Caesarea, outside the entrance to the *beis medrash*. Rabbi Ami came out and reproached them for not being inside during *beis medrash* hours — someone might need help with a difficult topic and would be left without guidance. Rabbi Zeira listened and went in. Rabbi Abba did not. Later, those inside the *beis medrash* were grappling with precisely this question of Rabbi's final ruling. Rabbi Zeira said: *lo shvakitun li de'isha'il lesaba* — had I known this was the question, I would have asked the elder, referring to Rabbi Abba who had remained outside. Why would Rabbi Abba know? Because he might have heard from his father, Rabbi Chiya bar Abba, who was the student of Rabbi Yochanan and reviewed his learning before Rabbi Yochanan every thirty days — a relationship of constant, meticulous review. Through that chain, the tradition might have been preserved.

### Elazar's Message

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הָוֵי עֲלַהּ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּשָׁלַח רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לַגּוֹלָה: הוֹרָה רַבִּי כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** conclusion **was** reached **about it?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof: **Rabbi Elazar sent** a message **to the** Jews in **exile,** i.e., Babylonia: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **ruled in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir.**

The answer eventually came from another source. Rabbi Elazar sent word to the exile: *hora Rabbi k'Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi ruled like Rabbi Meir. Since we also have a tradition that Rabbi ruled like the *chachamim*, the *gemara* concludes: *shma mina hada achreita* — since this was the ruling sent abroad by Rabbi Elazar (which typically reflects the authoritative, final position), this ruling like Rabbi Meir was Rabbi's last and definitive ruling. The result is somewhat surprising: although common intuition might have assumed the *shechita* of a *cheresh shoteh vekatan* is a *safek*, the authoritative ruling is that it is considered definitively invalid when unobserved. Consequently, one does not cover the blood, and one may even slaughter the offspring on the same day.

## Multiple Animals & Blood

### Single Covering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁחַט מֵאָה חַיּוֹת בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, מֵאָה עוֹפוֹת בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, חַיָּה וָעוֹף בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שָׁחַט חַיָּה – יְכַסֶּנָּה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִשְׁחוֹט אֶת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **slaughtered one hundred undomesticated animals in one place, one covering** of the blood suffices **for all** the animals and there is no obligation to cover the blood of each animal separately. Likewise, if one slaughtered **one hundred birds in one place, one covering** of the blood suffices **for all** the birds. If one slaughtered **an undomesticated animal and a bird in one place, one covering for all of** the blood is sufficient. **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one **slaughtered an undomesticated animal, he should cover its** blood immediately **and** only **thereafter he should slaughter the bird.**

The *gemara* closes the daf with a brief but important *mishna* on the mechanics of *kisuy hadam*. If one slaughtered a hundred *chayot* in the same place, *kisuy echad lekulan* — a single covering suffices for all of them. One need not cover each animal's blood separately. The blood collects together and is covered collectively.

### Mixed Animals

Similarly, if one slaughtered a hundred birds in the same place, a single covering suffices. The *mishna* further extends this: if one slaughtered a *chaya* and a bird — for example, a deer and a chicken — in the same place, *kisuy echad lekulan*, one covering may serve for both.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חַיָּה – כֹּל מַשְׁמַע חַיָּה, בֵּין מְרוּבָּה וּבֵין מוּעֶטֶת; עוֹף – כֹּל מַשְׁמַע עוֹף, בֵּין מְרוּבֶּה וּבֵין מוּעָט; מִכָּאן אָמְרוּ: שָׁחַט מֵאָה חַיּוֹת בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, מֵאָה עוֹפוֹת בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן, חַיָּה וָעוֹף בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד – כִּסּוּי אֶחָד לְכוּלָּן. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שָׁחַט חַיָּה – יְכַסֶּנָּה, וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִשְׁחוֹט אֶת הָעוֹף, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "חַיָּה אוֹ עוֹף".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to the mitzva of covering the blood: "An undomesticated animal or bird" (Leviticus 17:13). **"Undomesticated animal"** is inclusive, i.e., **any** number of animals is **included** in the term **undomesticated animal, whether many or few.** Likewise, **"bird"** is inclusive, i.e., **any** amount is **included** in the term **bird, whether many or few. From here** the Rabbis **stated:** If one **slaughtered one hundred undomesticated animals in one place, one covering** of the blood suffices **for all** the animals. Likewise, if one slaughtered **one hundred birds in one place, one covering** of the blood suffices **for all** the birds. If one slaughtered **an undomesticated animal and a bird in one place, one covering for all of** the blood is sufficient. The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If one **slaughtered an undomesticated animal, he should cover its** blood immediately **and** only **thereafter he should slaughter the bird, as it is stated: "An undomesticated animal or bird"** (Leviticus 17:13). The term "or" indicates that each type must be attended to separately.

Rabbi Yehuda disagrees with this final ruling. He holds: *shachat chaya yachasena ve'achar kach yishchot es ha'of* — one should first cover the blood of the *chaya* and only then proceed to slaughter the bird. His reasoning is that the Torah, in addressing *kisuy hadam*, mentions *chaya* and *of* separately — *shachat chaya o of*, using the disjunctive *o*, implying that each requires its own distinct act of covering. Since the Torah distinguishes between them, the covering for a *chaya* and the covering for a bird must be separate acts; one cannot serve as the covering for both simultaneously.
