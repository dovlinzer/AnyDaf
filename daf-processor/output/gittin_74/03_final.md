# Gittin 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mehayom Im Mati Status

### Opening Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה הִיא בְּאוֹתָן הַיָּמִים? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: כְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ לְכׇל דְּבָרֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת:
>
> **Translation:** **What is** her status **during these days? Rabbi Yehuda says:** She is **like a married woman with regard to all of her matters. Rabbi Yosei says:** It is uncertain whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced.**

The daf opens at the bottom of 73b with the question *mai hi be'otan hayamim* — what is a woman's status during the interim period when a *get* has been given with the formula *mehayom im mati*, "as of today if I die"? The previous discussion had already established that *yichud* should be avoided during this period, lest it be construed as an act of *kiddushin bi'ah* that would retroactively nullify the *get*. The natural assumption is that her status during the interim is that of a *safek megureshet*: if he dies she will have been divorced retroactively from the moment the *get* was given; if he does not die, she was never divorced at all. That is the straightforward reading — but the *mishnah* reveals a genuine debate.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* says she is *ke'eshet ish lechol devaraha* — not merely that she must conduct herself as a married woman out of caution, but that she is a *vadai eshet ish* in every respect. The *gemara* will elaborate: for example, if she had relations with another man during this period *bishogeg*, she would bring a *chatat* — even if he ultimately died and the *get* took retroactive effect. She is not a *safek* at all; she is definitively married. The obvious difficulty is: how can this be? If he dies in the end, she should retroactively have been *megureshet* from the moment the *get* was given — in which case she should have been a *vadai megureshet*, not a *vadai eshet ish*.

### Rabbi Yossi Position

*Rabbi Yossi* says *megureshet ve'eina megureshet* — she is divorced and not divorced. On its face this sounds equivalent to *safek*, which is roughly what we would have expected. But the *gemara* will clarify that *Rabbi Yossi*'s position is not quite the same as a resolvable *safek*, and a third category — *taluy*, suspension — will be introduced to sharpen the distinctions.

### Rava's Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: יָמִים שֶׁבֵּינָתַיִם – בַּעְלָהּ זַכַּאי בִּמְצִיאָתָהּ, וּבְמַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ, וּבַהֲפָרַת נְדָרֶיהָ; וְיוֹרְשָׁהּ, וּמִטַּמֵּא לָהּ. כְּלָלוֹ שֶׁל דָּבָר: הֲרֵי הִיא כְּאִשְׁתּוֹ לְכׇל דָּבָר – אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In a case where the husband said: This is your bill of divorce from now if I die from this illness, **during the days between,** before he dies, **her husband is entitled to** anything that **she finds,** i.e., any lost item that cannot be returned to its owner, in accordance with the rabbinic principle that any lost item found by a wife belongs to her husband. **And** he is entitled to the profits from **her earnings, and** he is entitled to **annul her vows** (see Numbers 30:7–9), **and he inherits** from **her** if she predeceases him, **and he becomes ritually impure for her** if he is a priest. **The principle of the matter** is as follows: At that time **she is like his wife in every sense, but she does not require a second bill of divorce from him** when he dies. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

To understand *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position that she is a *vadai eshet ish*, the *gemara* first raises the objection: *u'lchi mayis havi gita* — if you say the *get* only takes effect when he dies, that amounts to *ein get le'achar mita*, which is inadmissible. So *Rava* says the case must involve a husband who said *me'et she'ani ba'olam* — the *get* should take effect at the very last moment he is still alive, a moment before his death.

### Rashi's Reading

This is where *Rashi* and *Tosafot* diverge sharply. *Rashi* accepts the *gemara*'s text as given and understands that *Rava* is not explaining *mehayom im mati* but is rather redirecting the *mishnah* entirely to a different case. According to *Rashi*, the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and the *chachamim* does not concern *mehayom im mati* at all; rather, it concerns a case where the husband explicitly said the *get* should take effect the moment before he dies. In that scenario, *Rabbi Yehuda* holds she was a *vadai eshet ish* throughout the entire preceding period — consistently with the *get* taking effect only at the very last instant. The opposing view holds that even in that scenario she retains some status of *safek* throughout the interim period, since while she was living through it she experienced it as uncertain. This is an admittedly very difficult reading: the *mishnah* had been discussing *mehayom im mati* throughout, and suddenly, without transition, *Rashi* has the *gemara* pivot to an entirely different case. One might ask why any opinion would deny that she was a *vadai eshet ish* when the husband had explicitly stipulated the *get* takes effect only at the very last instant of his life — she should have a *chezkat eshet ish* and in retrospect should clearly be *vadai eshet ish*. The position that even then she retains a *safek* retroactively is difficult to defend.

### Tosafot's Girsa

*Tosafot*, following *Rabbeinu Tam* and *Rabbeinu Chananel*, resolve the difficulty by emending just one element of the text. Their reading — which *Tosafot* claim reflects the original *girsa* — has *Rava* say not a description of a different case but rather *na'aseh ke'omer me'ishtani ba'olam*: we treat the husband's formula *mehayom im mati* as if he said it should take effect at the last moment he is alive. This is the interpretive principle at stake in the *mishnah* itself. When a husband says *mehayom im mati*, he is in some sense giving a conditional *get* effective "as of today." But what does he really intend? The *gemara* reasons that if one were to ask this husband — knowing he is going to die — when he truly wants his wife to be divorced, he would say: at the very last minute, of course, but I was told I must say *mehayom* because *ein get le'achar mita*. On *Tosafot*'s reading, *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that we interpret *mehayom im mati* to mean the *get* takes effect at the very last moment he is alive, and therefore throughout the entire prior period she is a *vadai eshet ish*. The opposing position is: no, we read the words as they stand — "as of today, if I die" — meaning if he dies, it takes effect retroactively from the moment of the giving. During the interim, it is a *safek* of all or nothing.

### Yamim Shebeintayim

A *braita* then teaches the particulars of *yamim shebeintayim*, the days in between. If one follows *Rabbi Yehuda*'s approach — that she is *ke'eshet ish lechol devaraha* — then during this period her husband is entitled to her *metziah* (found objects), her *ma'asei yadeha* (earnings), he may annul her vows, he inherits her if she dies, if he is a *kohen* he becomes *tamei* for her, and she eats *terumah*. The general rule is *harei hi ke'ishto lechol davar* — she is as his wife in every respect — *ela she'eina tzricha heimenu get sheni*, except that if he dies she does not need a second *get*, because the *get* takes effect at the last moment. That is the position of *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Taluy vs. Safek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: בְּעִילָתָהּ תְּלוּיָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: בְּעִילָתָהּ סָפֵק. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיָּמוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Meir says:** If she engages in sexual intercourse with another man, the legal status of **her sexual intercourse depends** on whether or not her husband dies from this illness. If he dies, she is considered to have been divorced from the time the bill of divorce was given, and her sexual intercourse is not deemed adulterous. **Rabbi Yosei says: Her sexual intercourse** has an **uncertain** status. **And the Rabbis say:** There is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced,** and this is an uncertainty **provided that he dies** from this illness. If he does not, they are certainly not divorced.

*Rabbi Meir* says *be'ilata tluyah* — if she has relations with another man, the act is suspended (*taluy*). Depending on whether he dies, it will turn out retroactively either to be adultery or not. This is the straightforward position: the *get* is contingent on his death, and if he dies it takes effect from the day it was given, so retroactively she was *megureshet* and there was no sin. *Rabbi Yossi*, by contrast, says *be'ilata safek* — her act is in *doubt*, not merely in suspension. These sound identical, and the *gemara* asks *mai beinaihu*.

### Asham Taluy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִיכָּא בֵּין רַבִּי מֵאִיר לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אָשָׁם תָּלוּי אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ; לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – לָא מַיְיתֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, וּלְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי – מַיְיתֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **What** difference **is there between** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** who says that the status of her sexual intercourse depends on whether or not her husband dies from his illness, **and** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who says that the status of her sexual intercourse is uncertain? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The** practical difference **between them is** with regard to the bringing of **a provisional guilt-offering,** which is brought by one who is uncertain as to whether he committed a sin that requires a sin-offering. **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** the paramour **does not bring a provisional guilt-offering** (see Leviticus 5:17–19), because, although there was uncertainty at the time of the act of sexual intercourse, the matter will eventually be clarified once the husband either dies or recovers from his illness. If the husband survives her paramour must bring a sin-offering, and if the husband dies the other man is exempt from bringing any offering. **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei** her status is uncertain, and even if the husband dies from this illness Rabbi Yosei is uncertain when the bill of divorce took effect. Therefore, her paramour must **bring a provisional guilt-offering.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* answers: *asham taluy*. Assuming he does eventually die, does she bring an *asham taluy* for having had relations with another man during the interim period? An *asham taluy* is brought when one performs an act that may constitute an *aveira* — one that would normally require a *chatat* — but it is uncertain whether it did. According to *Rabbi Meir*, once he dies, we look back retroactively: she was *vadai* divorced from the day the *get* was given, the act was not adultery, no sin occurred — she brings nothing. According to *Rabbi Yossi*, even after he dies the question remains permanently unresolved. *Rabbi Yossi*'s position is that the phrase *mehayom im mati* is inherently ambiguous: it could mean the *get* takes effect as of the day it was given (the *Rabbi Meir* reading) or at the very last moment (the *Rabbi Yehuda* reading), and *Rabbi Yossi* cannot determine which is correct. Since the statement is inherently indeterminate, no subsequent fact — not even his death — ever resolves which reading is right. Therefore even in retrospect she is in an irresolvable *safek*, and she brings an *asham taluy*.

This is noted as a *chiddush* in two respects. First, an *asham taluy* is usually associated with a doubt that is in principle resolvable — one had two pieces before oneself and did not know which one was eaten. An abstract *safek* that can never be verified is a less standard case. Second, there is a debate whether an *asham taluy* applies to abstract doubt at all, though there are positions that permit it. *Tosafot* note that *Rabbi Yossi* is not saying retroactive reassessment is impossible in principle; he is saying the original statement is ambiguous in a way that no future event can adjudicate. She therefore brings an *asham taluy* because it is, in effect, a *safek* that is permanently sealed.

### Chachamim Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. חֲכָמִים הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי! אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר יִרְמְיָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים ״מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת וְאֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת״ – בַּעְלָהּ חַיָּיב בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* taught: **And the Rabbis say:** There is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced.** The Gemara comments: Apparently, the opinion of **the Rabbis is** the same as the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** Why are they recorded as having a dispute? The Gemara answers: **There is** a practical difference **between them** with regard to the halakhic ruling transmitted **by Rabbi Zeira. As Rabbi Zeira says** that **Rabba bar Yirmeya says** that **Shmuel says: Wherever the Sages said:** There is uncertainty whether **she is divorced or** whether **she is not divorced, her husband is obligated to provide her** with **sustenance.** This formulation is stated only by the Rabbis, and Rabbi Zeira's *halakha* would apply only according to them, not according to Rabbi Yosei.

The *mishnah* also records the position of *chachamim*, who say *megureshet ve'eina megureshet*. The *gemara* notes that *chachamim* are essentially aligned with *Rabbi Yossi* — both hold the status is permanently indeterminate. The distinction lies in what *Rabbi Zeira* taught in the name of *Shmuel*: *kol makom she'amru chachamim megureshet ve'eina megureshet, ba'al chayav bimezonoteha* — wherever the *chachamim* use the formula *megureshet ve'eina megureshet*, the husband remains obligated to support his wife. This is a genuine *nafka mina* from *Rabbi Yossi*'s formulation: *Rabbi Yossi* might say the husband is not obligated (perhaps applying the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*), whereas the *chachamim*, by invoking this established formula, affirm that even in an irresolvable state of *safek megureshet*, the husband's support obligation continues.

## Al Menat Gitin

### Get Conditional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״ – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְתִתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a husband says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars,** then **she is divorced and must give** two hundred dinars in order to fulfill the condition of the bill of divorce.

The next *mishnah* introduces the case of *harei zeh gitayich al menat she'titni li matayim zuz* — "here is your *get* on the condition that you give me two hundred *zuz*." It is worth pausing on the figure: two hundred *zuz* is precisely the value of a standard *ketubbah*. The case is therefore not simply a random monetary condition but something more pointed — the husband is effectively saying: give up your *ketubbah* and I will give you the *get*. This is permissible when the wife is seeking the divorce but cannot compel it, and the husband, who has no obligation to divorce her in such circumstances, may set conditions. Were he compelled to divorce her — in a case of *kefiyah* — this kind of condition would not be allowed. But absent that, he retains the unilateral power to decide whether to divorce at all, and he may condition it accordingly.

The critical feature of the case is that he already hands her the *get*. He does not say "first give me the money and then I'll give the *get*." He gives her the *get* with the condition embedded in it. The question is entirely about how the *al menat* condition operates: does the *get* take effect retroactively from the moment it was given once the condition is fulfilled, or does it take effect only at the moment of fulfillment?

### Get Time Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מִיכָּן וְעַד שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם״ – אִם נָתְנָה לוֹ בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת; וְאִם לָאו, אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** If a husband says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **on the condition that you will give me** money **from now until** the conclusion of **thirty days, if she gives** the money **to him within thirty days she is divorced. And if not she is not divorced.**

The *mishnah* continues: if he says *al menat she'titni li mikhan ad shloshim yom*, on the condition she give it within thirty days — *natnah lo betoch shloshim yom megureshet, ve'im lav einah megureshet*. If she gives within thirty days she is divorced; if not, she is not. The *gemara* observes this seems obvious, and we will see the discussion of whether the thirty-day limit is strictly required or merely advisory.

### Rav Huna Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״וְתִתֵּן״? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: וְהִיא תִּתֵּן. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: לִכְשֶׁתִּתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches: She is divorced and must give the money. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **And must give? Rav Huna says:** She is divorced immediately, once the bill of divorce has been given over to her, **and she must give** him the money subsequently in order to fulfill the condition. The bill of divorce takes effect retroactively, from the time when it was given, after she gives the money. **Rav Yehuda says:** She is divorced only **when she gives** him this sum.

The *mishnah* then uses the phrase *ve'titein* — "and she gives" — rather than the more expected *titein ve'tehei megureshet*, "let her give and she will be divorced." The *gemara* asks *mai ve'titein*: what does this phrasing mean? *Rabbi Yehuda* reads it as *lechshe'titein* — only when she gives the money does the *get* take effect. This is a significant position: *al menat* means the *get* is effective only at the moment the condition is fulfilled, not retroactively from the moment it was given.

*Rav Huna* takes the opposite view: *ve'hi titein* means she gives the money and that satisfies the condition, but the *get* takes effect retroactively from the moment it was given.

### Nifka Mina Torn Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ שֶׁנִּתְקָרַע הַגֵּט אוֹ שֶׁאָבַד; רַב הוּנָא אָמַר ״וְהִיא תִּתֵּן״ – אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי; רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר ״לִכְשֶׁתִּתֵּן״ – צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between** the opinion of Rav Huna and the opinion of Rav Yehuda? The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them if the bill of divorce was torn or lost** after she received it but before she gave the money. According to the opinion of **Rav Huna,** who **says: And she must give** but the bill of divorce takes effect retroactively, if it was torn or lost then **she does not require a second bill of divorce from him.** By contrast, **Rav Yehuda says** that the divorce takes effect only **when she gives** him the sum. Therefore, if the document is torn or lost **she requires a second bill of divorce from him,** as in his opinion it is not yet a valid bill of divorce until she has given him the money.

The practical difference between these two positions is illustrated by a case where the *get* was torn or lost in the interim. According to *Rav Huna*, since the *get* takes effect from the moment it was originally given, the subsequent fate of the document is irrelevant — *ein tzricha heimenu get sheni*, she does not need a second *get*. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, since the *get* only takes effect at the moment she hands over the money, the document must still be present and valid at that moment — *tzricha heimenu get sheni*, she requires a second *get* if the original was lost or destroyed. This flows from the principle that when an act takes effect at a future moment, the instrument or object through which it was created must still be present at that moment. (By contrast, money given as *kesef kiddushin* does not need to still be held at the moment the condition is fulfilled, because she remains in a state of having been enriched by it; but a document must physically persist.)

### Kiddushin Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְנַן נָמֵי גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא – דִּתְנַן: הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשָּׁה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי, עַל מְנָת שֶׁאֶתֵּן לִיךְ מָאתַיִם זוּז״ – הֲרֵי הִיא מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת, וְיִתֵּן. וְאִיתְּמַר: מַאי ״וְיִתֵּן״? רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: וְהוּא יִתֵּן. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it was also taught** in the mishna **with regard to betrothal** that Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda also disagreed about **a case like this, as we learned** in a mishna (*Kiddushin* 60a): If **one says to a woman: You are hereby betrothed to me on the condition that I will give you two hundred dinars,** then **she is betrothed, and he must give** the sum. **And it was stated** that Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda disagreed with regard to **what** is the meaning of: **And he must give. Rav Huna says: And he must give,** meaning that she is betrothed immediately, provided that the husband gives what he promised. **Rav Yehuda says:** She is betrothed only **when he gives.**

An analogous debate applies in the realm of *kiddushin*. If a man says to a woman *harei at mekudeshet li al menat she'etein lach matayim zuz* — you will be betrothed to me on the condition that I give you two hundred *zuz* — *Rav Huna* holds that once he gives the money the *kiddushin* takes effect retroactively from when the ring was given. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds it takes effect only at the moment the money is given. The *nafka mina* here: if in the interim she accepted a ring from another man. According to *Rav Huna*, she would be retroactively married from the moment of the first ring, so the second ring has no effect. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, she was not yet married when she accepted the second ring, and she is married to the second man, not the first. A third *nafka mina* — applicable in both *gittin* and *kiddushin* — is whether relations with another man during the interim constitute adultery.

### Psychology Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ: אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ – שֶׁפָּשְׁטָה יָדָהּ וְקִיבְּלָה קִידּוּשִׁין מֵאַחֵר. רַב הוּנָא אָמַר ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״ – תְּנָאָה בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, מְקַיֵּים תְּנָאֵיהּ וְאָזֵיל. רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר ״לִכְשֶׁיִּתֵּן״ – לְכִי יָהֵיב לַהּ הוּא דְּהָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין, הַשְׁתָּא לָא הָווּ קִידּוּשִׁין. וּצְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין, בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא ״וְהוּא יִתֵּן״ – מִשּׁוּם דִּלְקָרוֹבַהּ קָאָתֵי; אֲבָל גַּבֵּי גֵירוּשִׁין – דִּלְרַחוֹקַהּ קָאָתֵי, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה; וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן גַּבֵּי גֵירוּשִׁין; בְּהָא קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא ״וְהִיא תִּתֵּן״ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא כְּסִיף לְמִיתְבְּעַהּ; אֲבָל גַּבֵּי קִידּוּשִׁין, דִּכְסִיפָא לְמִיתְבְּעֵיהּ, אֵימָא מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ לְרַב יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** in a case **where she extended her hand and received betrothal from another** man before the first man gave her the money that he promised. **Rav Huna says: And he must give,** meaning that **it is merely a condition** that he accepted upon himself. **He fulfills his condition and moves on,** and the woman is betrothed to him immediately. Therefore, her betrothal to the second man has no validity. **Rav Yehuda says: When he gives,** which means that **when he gives her** the money **it will be** a valid **betrothal.** But **now it is not** a valid **betrothal** and therefore the betrothal from the second man takes effect. The Gemara comments: **And** it is **necessary** to mention this dispute in both the case of divorce and the case of betrothal. **As if it would have taught us** their opinions only **with regard to betrothal,** then one may think that it is specifically **with regard to this** case that **Rav Huna says: And he should give, because he comes to draw her close** in marriage. Consequently, he does this willingly, and therefore there is an assumption that his intention was for the betrothal to take effect immediately. **But with regard to divorce, where he comes to distance her,** there is an assumption that he does so unwillingly, so that he makes the divorce dependent on a condition in order to delay the matter. Therefore, **say** that Rav Huna **concedes to Rav Yehuda** that the divorce does not take effect until the condition has been fulfilled. **And if it would have taught us** their opinions only **with regard to divorce,** then one may think that it is specifically **with regard to this** case that **Rav Huna says: And she must give, because he is not embarrassed to demand** the money **from her** afterward to fulfill the condition of the bill of divorce, and his intent was to divorce her immediately. **But with regard to betrothal, where she is embarrassed to demand** the money that he promised would come **from him,** she may not agree to be betrothed to him until she actually receives the money. Therefore, you might **say** that Rav Huna **concedes to Rav Yehuda** that the betrothal is valid only once he gives her the money.

The *gemara* explains why both cases need to be stated explicitly, because one might have argued opposite conclusions in each case and therefore wrongly assumed that *Rav Huna* and *Rabbi Yehuda* would agree in one of the two contexts.

Had the debate only been stated in *kiddushin*, one might have reasoned: *Rav Huna* says it is retroactive there *mishum d'likrova ka'ati* — he is trying to draw her close, he wants to marry her, so naturally we read his words to mean the *kiddushin* takes effect as soon as possible. But in *gittin*, where *d'lerachoka ka'ati* — he is trying to distance himself from her — perhaps even *Rav Huna* would agree with *Rabbi Yehuda* that we read the words to mean it takes effect only when the money changes hands, since he is not eager to divorce. On the other hand, one might argue the opposite: in *gittin*, where *d'lo kassif lemisbei* — he has no embarrassment about demanding the money — he is not threatened by saying it takes effect immediately, because he is confident he can always collect. In *kiddushin*, where the woman is in a somewhat more hesitant position and *kassif lemisbei* — she is reluctant to press him for the money — she might want the *kiddushin* to take effect only when the money changes hands, to preserve her leverage.

Similarly, had the debate been stated only in *gittin*, one might have argued the reverse logic from *Rabbi Yehuda*'s side. The *gemara* therefore says *tzricha* — both formulations are necessary. The deeper point is that *al menat* should not require any case-by-case psychological analysis at all. *Rav Huna* has a consistent rule: *al menat* means retroactive, as of the moment the *get* was given. *Rabbi Yehuda* has a consistent rule: *al menat* means the act takes effect only when the condition is fulfilled. The circumstantial psychology can always be argued both ways, which is precisely why *halacha* requires a stable rule rather than ad hoc intent-inference.

### Proof from Braitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁנִּקְרַע הַגֵּט אוֹ שֶׁנֶּאֱבַד – מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת; וּלְאַחֵר לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא עַד שֶׁתִּתֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** based on what was taught in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 7:5): If one says to a woman: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars,** then **even though the bill of divorce was torn or lost she is divorced. And she may not marry another** man **until she gives** her first husband the money.

The *gemara* then challenges *Rabbi Yehuda* from two *braitot*. The first teaches: *harei zeh gitayich al menat she'titni li matayim zuz* — *af al pi she'nikra ha'get o she'ne'evad*, even if the *get* was torn or lost in the interim, *megureshet* — she is divorced once she gives the money. This is clearly consistent with *Rav Huna*: the *get* takes effect retroactively from when it was given, and the subsequent destruction of the document is irrelevant. The *braita* adds the practical advice not to remarry until the money is actually given, since if she can never produce it the *get* will never take effect.

### Second Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד תַּנְיָא: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וָמֵת, נָתְנָה – אֵינָהּ זְקוּקָה לְיָבָם; לֹא נָתְנָה – זְקוּקָה לְיָבָם. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֶנֶת לְאָבִיו אוֹ לְאָחִיו אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַקְּרוֹבִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught further** in that same *baraita*: If one says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give two hundred dinars to me, and** the husband **died** childless, if she had already **given** him the money, then **she is not bound** with a levirate bond **to the *yavam*** because she was already divorced from her husband through the bill of divorce that he gave her. But if **she had not given** him the money, then **she is bound** with a levirate bond **to the *yavam*** because the bill of divorce did not take effect and she is a widow. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: She should give** the money **to** her late husband's **father, or to his brother, or to one of the relatives** who inherit from him, and by doing so she fulfills the condition and is released from the levirate bond.

A second *braita* addresses the case where the husband died before the money was given. *Tanna kamma* holds that if she gave the money before he died, she is free from the *yavam* (she was divorced); if she did not give it before his death, she is not divorced and is *zekuka le'yavam*. *Rabban Gamliel* adds that even after his death she may give the money to his father, his brother, or another relative, and that satisfies the condition. The key insight from *Rabban Gamliel*'s position is that it presupposes universal agreement that the *get* takes effect retroactively — the only debate is whether giving the money to his heirs satisfies the condition he formulated as "give it to me." Both *tannaim* agree that once the money is given, the *get* takes effect from the moment it was handed over, not from the moment of payment. This is a clear *tiyuvta* against *Rabbi Yehuda*.

### Rabbi Yehuda Challenged

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי – אֶלָּא דְּמָר סָבַר: ״לִי״ – וְלָא לְיוֹרְשַׁי, וּמָר סָבַר: ״לִי״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ לְיוֹרְשַׁי; וְכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא מִיהָא תְּנָאָה הָוֵי – תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב יְהוּדָה! אָמַר לָךְ רַב יְהוּדָה: הָא מַנִּי, רַבִּי הִיא – דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי. וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ, וַאֲנָא דַּאֲמַרִי כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: The first *tanna* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **disagree only with regard to** the meaning of the condition. One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that when the husband said: On the condition that she will give **to me,** he meant: To me specifically **and not to my heirs.** Consequently, she cannot fulfill the condition by giving the money to his heirs. **And** one **Sage,** Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, **holds** that when the husband said that she will give the money **to me,** he meant **and even to my heirs. But in any event, everyone** agrees that this **is** a valid **condition,** yet its fulfillment does not change the date that the bill of divorce takes effect. This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Yehuda,** who said that the bill of divorce takes effect only when she gives. **Rav Yehuda** could have **said to you:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Anyone who states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** is **like** one who **states:** The agreement will take effect retroactively **from now,** even though the condition is fulfilled only later on. **And the Rabbis disagree with him. And I say** my ruling **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis.**

*Rabbi Yehuda* responds: *ha mani Rabbi* — these *braitot* follow *Rabbi*. For *Rav Huna* himself stated his rule in the name of *Rabbi*: *kol ha'omer al menat ke'omer me'achshav dami* — whoever says "on the condition that" is treated as having said "as of now, on the condition that." *Rabbi Yehuda* thus concedes that the *braitot* are against him, but claims there are other *tannaim* who dispute *Rabbi*'s principle. The *gemara* presses him: who are these *tannaim*? At this point *Rabbi Yehuda* is in a very weak position — the *braitot* are against him, *Rabbi*'s principle is against him, and no *tanna* can be produced to support his view.

### Eretz Yisrael Consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בְּבָבֶל אָמְרִינַן, הָא דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַבִּי: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי – פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ. כִּי סְלִיקִי, אַשְׁכַּחְתֵּיהּ לְרַבִּי אַסִּי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בְּאוֹמֵר ״עַל מְנָת״ – כְּאוֹמֵר ״מֵעַכְשָׁיו״ דָּמֵי. לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בְּ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Zeira says: When we were in Babylonia we** would **say** with regard to **this** following statement **that Rav Huna says** that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: Anyone who states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** is **like** one who **states:** The agreement will take effect retroactively **from now,** even though the condition is fulfilled only later on; we would say that **the Rabbis disagree with him. When I ascended** to Eretz Yisrael **I found Rabbi Asi, who was sitting and saying in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Everyone concedes that with regard to** anyone who **states** a condition employing the language: **On the condition,** he is **like** one who **states:** The agreement will take effect retroactively **from now;** and the Rabbis **disagreed** with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **only in** a case where the bill of divorce included the condition: **From today and after** my **death.**

*Rabbi Zeira* relates that when he was still in *Bavel*, the tradition there was that *Rav Huna*'s ruling in the name of *Rabbi* was disputed by other *tannaim*, and that is why *Rav Yehuda* felt free to disagree. But *ki selik* — when *Rabbi Zeira* made *aliyah* to *Eretz Yisrael* — *ashkachtei le'Rabbi Assi* — he found *Rabbi Assi* sitting and declaring in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *hakol modim be'omer al menat ke'omer me'achshav dami* — everyone agrees that *al menat* is equivalent to saying "as of now." This is not merely the position of *Rabbi* — it is the universal consensus. In *Eretz Yisrael*, where knowledge of *tannaitic* positions was most authoritative, there was no tradition of any dissent on this point.

### Conclusion Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: ״מֵהַיּוֹם וּלְאַחַר מִיתָה״ – גֵּט וְאֵינוֹ גֵּט, דִּבְרֵי חֲכָמִים; רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: כָּזֶה – גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a husband says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce **from today and after** my **death,** then this is both a valid **bill of divorce and not** a valid **bill of divorce.** This is **the statement of the Rabbis. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi disagrees and **says:** A case **like this** is a valid **bill of divorce** without any uncertainty. This teaches that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis disagree only in this case, but everyone agrees that when he employed the language: On the condition, it is as though he stipulated: From now.

The *gemara* concludes with a precise formulation: *lo nechelku ela be'hayom u'le'achar mita* — the only genuine *tannaitic* debate was not about *al menat* at all. *Al menat* universally means retroactive, effective as of the day it was given. The debate was about the formula from the previous *mishnah* — *mehayom u'le'achar mita* — "as of today and after my death." That phrase sounds contradictory on its face: "today" and "after death" cannot both be the operative time. The *chachamim* read it as an irresolvable internal contradiction that renders it *safek megureshet ve'eina megureshet*. *Rabbi* is prepared to read even that formulation as a valid *tnai* — as if the husband is saying the *get* should take effect today, on the condition he dies — and therefore declares *kaze get*, it is a valid *get* once he dies.

A final observation resolves an apparent structural question in the *braita*: why does the debate between *Rabbi* and the *chachamim* use the *mehayom u'le'achar mita* case rather than the seemingly easier *al menat* case? The *gemara* answers *kocho de'heteira adif lei*: the greater *chiddush* belongs to the *tanna* prepared to read the difficult case permissively. The *chachamim* who restrict are only being *machmir* — they are rendering the *get* invalid. The remarkable ruling is *Rabbi*'s willingness to validate even the strained language of *mehayom u'le'achar mita*. Stating the debate on that harder case demonstrates the full reach of *Rabbi*'s interpretive generosity.

## Lost Cloak Case

### Itzaliti Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּצַיְדָּן, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁאָמַר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ, עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי אִיצְטְלִיתִי״, וְאָבְדָה אִיצְטְלִיתוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: תִּתֵּן לוֹ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said:** There was **an incident in** the city of **Tzaidan involving one who said to his wife: This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me my coat [*itztaliti*], and she lost his coat,** so that she could not give it to him. **And the Rabbis said** that **she must give him the value** of the coat, and by doing so she fulfills the condition and is divorced.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* relates a story (*ma'aseh b'Tzidan*): a man said to his wife *harei zeh gitayich al menat she'titni li itzaliti* — here is your *get* on the condition you give me back my cloak. The cloak was lost (*ve'avda itzalito*). The *chachamim* ruled that she may give him the monetary value of the cloak and that counts as fulfilling the condition. This is a striking *chiddush*. Even in a case involving a specific personal item — a garment, something to which the husband might have personal attachment and not merely economic interest — the *chachamim* allow money to substitute for the object once the object is gone.

### Missing Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי מַעֲשֶׂה? חַסּוֹרֵי מִיחַסְּרָא, וְהָכִי קָתָנֵי: אִם אָמַר לָהּ: ״עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי אִיצְטְלִיתִי״, וְאָבְדָה אִיצְטְלִיתוֹ – אִיצְטְלִיתִי דַּוְקָא קָאָמַר לַהּ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: תִּתֵּן לוֹ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ. וְאָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: מַעֲשֶׂה נָמֵי בְּצַיְדָּן, בְּאֶחָד שֶׁאָמַר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי אִצְטְלִיתִי״, וְאָבְדָה אִצְטְלִיתוֹ, וְאָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: תִּתֵּן לוֹ אֶת דָּמֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** did Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **teach** before, such **that he** now **teaches** and cites **an incident** which is similar to it? This incident does not seem to be referring to what was stated immediately before in the mishna. The Gemara answers: The mishna **is incomplete and this is what it is teaching: If** the husband **said to** his wife: This is your bill of divorce **on the condition that you will give me my coat, and she lost his coat,** then since **he specifically stated to her:** Give me **my coat,** she cannot give him its value instead. And since she has lost the coat, the bill of divorce is not valid; this is the opinion of the Rabbis. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In such a case **she can give him its value. And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said** in support of his statement: There was **an incident also in** the city of **Tzaidan involving one who said to his wife: This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me my coat, and she lost his coat. And the Rabbis said** that **she should give him its value.**

The *gemara* asks *mai ta'ama d'katani ma'aseh* — why does the *mishnah* simply give the story without first stating the underlying rule? The *gemara* answers *chasuri mechasra ve'hachi katni*: words are missing, and the *mishnah* should be reconstructed as follows. The implicit rule being illustrated is: if he said *al menat she'titni li itzaliti*, specifying the cloak, and the cloak was lost — one might have thought *itzaliti davka ka'amar lah*, he meant that specific cloak and nothing else can substitute, so the condition can never be met. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* comes to teach *lo titten lo et dameha* — no, she gives him the monetary equivalent and that satisfies the condition.

### Mechulim Lach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי אַסִּי מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הֲרֵי זֶה גִּיטִּךְ עַל מְנָת שֶׁתִּתְּנִי לִי מָאתַיִם זוּז״, וְחָזַר וְאָמַר לָהּ ״מְחוּלִים לָךְ״, מַהוּ? תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן; תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Asi raised a dilemma before Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If a man says to his wife: **This is your bill of divorce on the condition that you will give me two hundred dinars, and** afterward **he returned and said to her: It is waived for you,** then **what is** the *halakha*? He explained his question in detail: **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis,** and **let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

The *gemara* then introduces a related question posed by *Rav Assi* to *Rabbi Yochanan*: what if the husband said *harei zeh gitayich al menat she'titni li matayim zuz*, and then later said to her *mechulim lach* — I waive it, you do not have to give me the money? Does that cause the *get* to take effect?

The *gemara* frames this question as applicable across the two positions: one may ask it according to the *Rabbanan* (who do not allow money to substitute for the cloak) and according to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* (who does allow substitution).

### Object vs. Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְרַבָּנַן – עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן הָתָם, אֶלָּא דְּלָא אַחֲלַהּ גַּבָּהּ; אֲבָל הָכָא, הָא קָאָמַר לַהּ: ״מְחוּלִים לָךְ״; אוֹ דִלְמָא, אֲפִילּוּ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל לָא קָאָמַר, אֶלָּא דְּקָא מְפַיְּיסָה לֵיהּ בִּדְמֵי; אֲבָל לִגְמָרֵי – לָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Let the dilemma be raised according to** the opinion of **the Rabbis:** One can say that **only there** do **the Rabbis state** their opinion that she is not divorced if the condition is not fulfilled and she gives him his coat, because **he did not waive** his claim to it; **but here he says to her: It is waived for you,** so they are divorced. **Or perhaps** one can say that **even Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel states** that she can be divorced even if he does not receive the coat **only there, as she appeases him by** giving him **money,** i.e., the condition is considered to be fulfilled due to the fact that he received the value of the coat; **but** in a case where he waives the condition **entirely, no,** because he does not receive anything from her. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him** in response: **She is not divorced,** because the condition has not been fulfilled.

Taking the *Rabbanan* first: one might think that even they would allow *mechulim lach* to work. After all, the *Rabbanan* said money cannot substitute for the cloak — but that was because the husband was not waiving the condition; he simply declined to accept money as equivalent to the specific object. Perhaps if he explicitly says "I waive it," that is different — he is not claiming the money equals the cloak but is actively relinquishing his claim. On the other hand, one might argue that even *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* would not accept *mechulim lach*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* permitted money as a substitute precisely because it can purchase the item in the marketplace — a functional equivalence argument. But if nothing at all is given, there is no basis to say the condition was met. He permitted functional equivalence; he did not permit outright waiver.

### No Waiving Answer

*Rabbi Yochanan* answers: *einah megureshet* — the *get* does not take effect. Saying *mechulim lach* does not work, and this applies even according to *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*. It is one thing to accept money as the functional equivalent of a lost object; it is another thing entirely to simply declare the condition void without anything being given or treated as given.

## Neder Al Tnai

### Konam Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״קֻוֽנָּם שֶׁאַתָּה נֶהֱנֶה לִי, אִם אִי אַתָּה נוֹתֵן לִבְנִי כּוֹר אֶחָד שֶׁל חִטִּין וּשְׁתֵּי חָבִיּוֹת שֶׁל יַיִן״, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אָסוּר עַד שֶׁיִּתֵּן; וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אַף זֶה – יָכוֹל לְהַתִּיר אֶת נִדְרוֹ שֶׁלֹּא עַל פִּי חָכָם, וְאוֹמֵר: ״הֲרֵינִי כְּאִילּוּ הִתְקַבַּלְתִּי״!
>
> **Translation:** **He raised an objection to his** opinion from a mishna (*Nedarim* 63b): In the case of **one who says to another: Benefiting from me is *konam* for you,** meaning it is prohibited for you to derive benefit from me, **if you do not give my son one *kor* of wheat and two barrels of wine** as a wedding gift, **Rabbi Meir says:** It is **prohibited** for this other person to benefit from the person who took the vow **until he gives** his son the gift. **And the Rabbis say: Even this** individual who took the vow **can dissolve his vow without** the **consent of a halakhic authority, and** he does this by **saying: I hereby** consider it **as though I have received** the gift from you. Based on this mishna, it would seem that if the husband forgives his wife the money that she owes him, it should be considered as if he received it, and the bill of divorce should be valid.

Following from *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling, a challenge is raised from a *braita* involving a *neder al tnai*. The case: a man says to his fellow *konam she'atah neheneh li im atah noten livni kor echad shel chittin u'shtei chavyot shel yayin* — I make a vow that you may derive no benefit from anything of mine, unless you give my son a quantity of wheat and two barrels of wine.

### Vow Absolution

*Rabbi Meir* rules *asur ad sheyitein* — the other person remains forbidden from benefiting from this man's property until the condition is actually fulfilled. *Chachamim* say *af ze yachol lehatir et nidro shelo al pi chacham* — the one who made the vow can release it without the formal process of *hatarat nedarim* before a *chacham*.

### Ke'ilu Treatment

How? By saying *hareini ke'ilu hitkabalti* — I will treat it as if I have already received what was promised. So by the *chachamim*'s ruling, the person who made the vow can declare "it is as if I received the wheat and wine," and the *neder* is thereby dissolved. This appears to be precisely the mechanism the husband would need in the *get* case — saying "it is as if I received the two hundred *zuz*." If the vow-maker can invoke this formula, why can the husband who gave the *get* not do likewise?

### Intent Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם לְצַעוֹרַהּ קָא מְכַוֵּין – וְלָא צַיעֲרַהּ. הָכָא מִשּׁוּם הַרְוָוחָה הוּא, וְהָא לָא אִיצְטְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of a bill of divorce, **he intends to vex her, and** until she pays him **he did not vex her.** If he forgives this condition, his initial intention has not been fulfilled. **Here,** in the case of the vow, the reason the father took the vow is **for the sake of profit;** he wants his son to receive a valuable gift, **but** subsequently the one who took the vow decides that **it was not necessary.** Therefore, he can waive his claim.

The *gemara* asks *hachi hashta* — how can these cases be compared?

### Tzurah vs. Revacha

The *gemara* draws a fundamental distinction: *hatam letzi'ura ka michaven, hacha lerevacha ka michaven ve'lo letzi'ura*. In the *get* case, when the husband says "on the condition you give me two hundred *zuz*," we interpret his intent as being primarily about making her suffer — *letzi'ura*. It is not really about the money as money; it is about the principle, which in the context of an adversarial divorce we read as his desire to impose hardship on her. Therefore, even if he declares "I consider myself as having received the money," the condition is not satisfied, because the condition was never really about his benefit in isolation — it was about her suffering. Since she has not suffered (no money changed hands, no hardship was imposed), his declaration accomplishes nothing toward fulfilling the *tnai*.

In the *neder* case, by contrast, the one who made the vow wanted his son provided for — *lerevacha*, for benefit, not to impose suffering on the other party. Since the entire purpose of the condition was his own gain, he has the power to declare "I consider that gain as received," and that declaration genuinely resolves the condition.

### Final Conclusion

What emerges clearly from the *gemara* is a precise and important principle: one is not in a position to simply *waive* a *tnai*. A condition stands as it was articulated; it cannot be unilaterally dissolved. What one *may* be able to do — under specific circumstances — is to declare that the condition has been treated as fulfilled, *ke'ilu hitkabalti*. But that option is available only when the condition was framed entirely around one's own benefit (*revacha*), so that one may credibly say "I consider that benefit received." When the condition is framed around the other party's suffering (*tzi'ura*), no such declaration is meaningful — the condition is not met, and the *get* does not take effect.
