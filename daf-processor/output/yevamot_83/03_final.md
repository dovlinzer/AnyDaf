# Yevamot 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel & Rav Positions

### Dvei Rav Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס וּבְהַרְכָּבָה, וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: בְּקוֹשִׁי וּבְקִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages of **the school of Rav said in the name of Rav** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** both **with regard to** the *halakha* of **a hermaphrodite and with regard to** the *halakha* of **grafting. And Shmuel says:** The *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion **with regard to** the laws of **protracted labor and forfeiture.**

The discussion picks up where it left off. *Dvei Rav* — the school or circle of Rav — had established that Rav rules in accordance with Rabbi Yose in two distinct cases: the case of the *androgynos* (hermaphrodite), which has been the central subject of the chapter, and the case of *harkava* (grafting), which the Gemara illustrated with a concrete example. In addition, a statement in Shmuel's name was cited regarding a woman who bleeds as a result of contractions or some physiological event connected to pregnancy. The question there is whether such bleeding constitutes *zivah* (the type of abnormal discharge that triggers a more stringent impurity), and Shmuel rules in accordance with Rabbi Yose. Shmuel also rules like Rabbi Yose in the case of *kiddush* — the subject to which the Gemara now turns.

## Kiddush Hakerem

### Kiddush Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִידּוּשׁ — דִּתְנַן: הַמְסַכֵּךְ גַּפְנוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי תְּבוּאָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ — הֲרֵי זֶה קִדֵּשׁ, וְחַיָּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתוֹ, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמְרִים: אֵין אָדָם אוֹסֵר דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ שֶׁלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** What is this *halakha* of **proscription? As we learned** in a mishna (*Kilayim* 7:4): If **one causes his grapevine to overshadow the grain of another, he has proscribed** it, rendering it forbidden as a food crop in a vineyard, **and he bears** financial **responsibility for it,** i.e., he must compensate the other individual for the loss of the produce that has been rendered forbidden. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Shimon say: A person cannot render forbidden an item that is not his.** Since the grain does not belong to him, he cannot render it forbidden. According to Shmuel, the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabbi Yosei in this case as well.

The Gemara introduces the concept of *kiddush hakerem* through a *mishnah*: *hamsakech gafno al gabei tevuato shel chaveiro harei ze kideish vechayav be'achrayuto divrei Rabbi Meir* — one who drapes his grapevine so that it overhangs his fellow's grain crop has created a situation of *kil'ei hakerem*, a forbidden mixture of vine and other species. The result is that the grain beneath becomes *kodesh* in the sense of being forbidden for use and benefit; it must be burned. Rabbi Meir further holds that this person bears financial liability for his fellow's losses. Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon, however, take a different view: *ein adam oseir davar she'eino shelo* — a person cannot render *asur* that which does not belong to him. Although *kilayim* is fundamentally a state-of-being violation rooted in the physical proximity of two different species, Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon maintain that the prohibition has a proprietary dimension: one cannot go around making someone else's field *kodesh* by extending one's own vine over it.

The connection of this case to the broader list is worth noting. Both Rashi and Tosafot observe that in every instance where Rav rules like Rabbi Yose, Rabbi Shimon is a co-signatory to that same position. These are not cases of Rabbi Yose alone; they are consistently cases where Rabbi Yose and Rabbi Shimon form a pair — a fact that draws these otherwise disparate topics more tightly into a single conceptual grouping.

## Shmuel & Rav Positions…

### Shmuel & Androgynos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס מָה לִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב עָנָן: לֵיתַהּ לְבָרַיְיתָא מִקַּמֵּי מַתְנִיתִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **As for a hermaphrodite, what did Shmuel say?** It was stated in the name of Rav that the *halakha* is ruled in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei with regard to a hermaphrodite, but no ruling was attributed to Shmuel concerning this case. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Shmuel said to Rav Anan: The *baraita* is not** to be relied upon **in the presence of the mishna.** This indicates that Shmuel rejects Rabbi Yosei's opinion as stated in the *baraita* that a hermaphrodite is considered a creature unto himself.

The Gemara asks: *androgynos ma li amar Shmuel?* — in the case of the *androgynos*, what is Shmuel's position? The answer: *de'amar Shmuel leRav Anan leita livraita mikamei matnitin* — Shmuel told Rav Anan that the *baraita* does not stand in the face of the *mishnah*. The *baraita* in question is the one in which Rabbi Yose says that an *androgynos* is *birya bifnei atzmah* — its own category — and that the *chachamim* never rendered a definitive judgment as to whether it is male or female. The *mishnah*, by contrast, presents Rabbi Yose as holding that an *androgynos* is male in status.

This raises a significant question that Tosafot develops at length: is the *androgynos* simply a case of *safek* (unresolved doubt) that the *chachamim* never adjudicated, or is it a genuine third category, a *birya bifnei atzmah mamash*, a distinct gender that is neither male nor female? Tosafot tend toward the view that in this *baraita* Rabbi Yose means a genuinely independent third category, while acknowledging that other *mishnaic* passages treat the *androgynos* as a *safek*. The tension remains unresolved. In any case, Shmuel's ruling that the *mishnah* prevails over the *baraita* signals that the authoritative statement of Rabbi Yose is the one found in the *mishnah* — which implies that Shmuel endorses that position, since there would be little reason to evaluate which formulation of Rabbi Yose is authoritative if Shmuel did not intend to rule accordingly.

### Grafting & Shemita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַרְכָּבָה מָה לִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל? תָּא שְׁמַע, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב עָנָן: תְּנִי כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר שְׁלֹשָׁה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: Concerning **grafting, what did Shmuel say?** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear, as Shmuel said to Rav Anan** that he should **teach in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who said** that it is prohibited to plant for **thirty-three** days before Rosh HaShana of the Sabbatical Year. Evidently, he ruled on this matter in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, and not that of Rabbi Yosei.

The Gemara then asks: *harkavah mah li amar Shmuel?* — in the case of grafting, what is Shmuel's position? Again Shmuel speaks to Rav Anan: *tanei keman de'amar sheloshah ushloshim* — teach the *baraita* in accordance with the one who says thirty-three days. To appreciate this, one must recall the background. The question concerns how much time before the *shemita* year one must complete one's final grafting. One opinion holds that the cutoff is thirty days before the beginning of the *shemita* year; another holds that three days suffice. But Rav Nachman clarified that the real discussion concerns not the month of Elul — in which everyone agrees that such agricultural work is forbidden — but rather the month of Tammuz, when the question is how much lead time the graft needs before entering the month of Elul. Shmuel rules in accordance with the position that three days are sufficient for a graft to take root, which means one may graft as late as three days before the end of Tammuz.

One might ask: why does grafting appear in a list otherwise dominated by issues of personal status and sacrificial law? The answer is organizational rather than thematic. In an oral culture, a useful mnemonic device is to group rulings together not by subject but by the authority who issued them. Remembering that "Rav rules like Rabbi Yose in the following cases" — and the same for Shmuel — provides a framework for retaining disparate *psakim* across entirely unrelated domains.

The Gemara now turns to Rav's positions on the remaining items in the list. On *koshi* — the question of whether a woman's bleeding during labor is associated with her contractions or constitutes independent *zivah* — Rav's position is simply unknown. *Teku.* On *kiddush*, the vine overhanging another's field, the Gemara proceeds to a dispute.

## Kiddush Hakerem…

### Rav Yosef & Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִידּוּשׁ מָה לִי אָמַר רַב? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי חָזֵית דְּסָמְכַתְּ אַהָא? סְמוֹךְ אַהָא! דְּאָמַר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further inquires: With regard to **proscription, what did Rav say? Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear, as Rav Huna said** that **Rav said** that the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Abaye said to him: What did you see that** led **you** to **rely on that** source? **Rely on this** source; **as Rav Adda said** that **Rav said** that the ***halakha* is** in fact **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei.** There is, then, a tradition that Rav accepted Rabbi Yosei's view.

On the question of Rav's ruling in *kiddush hakerem*, two conflicting traditions emerge. Rav Yosef cites a tradition in the name of Rav Huna, who said in Rav's name: *ein halacha ke'Rabbi Yose* — the *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Yose. Abaye, Rav Yosef's student, challenges this: why privilege that tradition? There is an equally valid statement in the name of Rav Ada bar Ahava, who likewise transmitted in Rav's name: *halacha ke'Rabbi Yose* — the *halacha* does follow Rabbi Yose. The result is an apparent contradiction between two reliable transmitters of Rav's positions.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב״ — מַנּוּ? רַב הוּנָא, וְרַב הוּנָא אָמַר: אֵין הֲלָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** When it is stated that the Sages of **the school of Rav said** a teaching, to **whom** is it referring? The reference is to **Rav Huna. And** it was **Rav Huna** who **said** that the ***halakha* is not** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei. It may be presumed, then, that Rav Adda's version of Rav's ruling is in error, as preference is given to the report of Rav's preeminent disciple, Rav Huna.

The resolution draws on the identity of *Bei Rav* — the school that originally established the list of cases in which Rav rules like Rabbi Yose. The Gemara concludes that *Bei Rav* refers to Rav Huna. Since it is Rav Huna himself who is the source of the tradition that Rav rules like Rabbi Yose in those enumerated cases, and since that same Rav Huna states explicitly that Rav does *not* rule like Rabbi Yose in the case of *kiddush hakerem*, the implication is clear: *kiddush hakerem* was never part of that list to begin with. Rav Huna's tradition, which defines the scope of the list, takes precedence over Rav Ada's contrary report. Rav does not rule like Rabbi Yose in the case of the vine overhanging another's field.

## Tumtum & Yibum

### R. Yehuda Tumtum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְטוּמְטוּם דְּבֵירֵי, דְּאוֹתְבוּהּ אַבֵּי כּוּרְסְיָה וְאִיקְּרַע, וְאוֹלֵיד שְׁבַע בְּנִין! וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אָמַר לָךְ: חַזֵּר עַל בָּנָיו מֵאַיִן הֵם.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** If **a *tumtum*,** whose external sexual organs are indeterminate, was torn open so that his genitals were exposed, and he was found to be a male, he must not perform *ḥalitza* because he is treated like a eunuch. **Rabbi Ami said: What** would **Rabbi Yehuda do with the *tumtum*** living in the town **of Biri, who was placed in a seat** for an operation, **and** the tissue covering his genitals **was torn** open **and** he later **fathered seven children?** Evidently, a *tumtum* who was torn open is not necessarily sexually impotent. The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yehuda** could have **said to you:** Go and **inquire about his children** and find out **from where they came.** He did not believe that they were fathered by this man but rather by someone else.

The Gemara now returns to the *mishnah* on 81a. Rabbi Yehuda states: *tumtum shenikra venimtza zachar lo yachlotz mipnei shehu kesaris* — a *tumtum*, a person whose genitalia are concealed by an undifferentiated membrane such that one cannot determine the sex, who undergoes a tearing or rupturing (*nikra*) of that membrane and is found to be male, should nonetheless not perform *chalitza*, because such a person is like a *saris* (eunuch). The logic: since the *tumtum* is presumed to be a *saris*, he is exempt from *yibum*, and consequently he cannot perform *chalitza* either.

### Birei's Case

Rabbi Ami challenges Rabbi Yehuda's position with a concrete case: *mai avid leh Rabbi Yehuda letumtum devirei* — what would Rabbi Yehuda do with the famous *tumtum* from Birei, who was placed on a special chair for the procedure, underwent the rupturing, was found to be male, and then fathered seven children? How can one maintain that every *tumtum* who is found to be male is necessarily a *saris*?

Rabbi Yehuda's response is striking: *chazor al banav me'ayin hem* — examine his children carefully; you will find they are not his. In Rabbi Yehuda's view, it is established medical fact that *tumtums* revealed to be male are sterile. The *chazaka* that children born to a married woman belong to her husband does not apply here, because once you are no longer dealing with a standard case — once you have a man known to be infertile — the ordinary presumption of paternity cannot be maintained. The *tumtum* who appears to have fathered children has not undermined the principle; rather, something else has occurred that the community was charitable enough not to investigate.

### R. Yose b'R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם לֹא יַחְלוֹץ, שֶׁמָּא יִקָּרַע וְנִמְצָא סְרִיס חַמָּה. אַטּוּ כֹּל דְּמִקְּרַע זָכָר הָוֵי? הָכִי קָאָמַר: שֶׁמָּא יִקָּרַע וְנִמְצָא נְקֵבָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ נִמְצָא זָכָר — שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא סָרִיס חַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: A *tumtum* must not perform *ḥalitza*,** as **perhaps he will be torn** open **and found** to be **a eunuch by natural causes.** The Gemara asks: Why did he formulate his teaching in this manner? **Is that to say** that **every** *tumtum* who is torn open is **a male?** It is certainly possible for a *tumtum* to be found to be a female. The Gemara explains: **This is what he said:** A *tumtum* must not perform *ḥalitza*, as **perhaps he will be torn** open **and found** to be **a female,** who certainly cannot perform *ḥalitza*, **and even** if **he is found** to be **a male, perhaps he will be found** to be **a eunuch by natural causes.**

A *baraita* introduces Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda — Rabbi Yehuda's own son, who follows broadly in his father's direction but with a distinct formulation. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda says: *tumtum lo yachlotz shema yikara venimtza saris chama* — a *tumtum* should not perform *chalitza*, lest the *nikra* occur and he be found to be a *saris chama*, a natural eunuch whose sexual organs never fully developed. Since a *saris chama* is exempt from *yibum*, his *chalitza* would be retroactively invalid, and any woman who had proceeded on the basis of that *chalitza* would find herself still bound by *zika* to the remaining brothers.

The Gemara, however, presses on this formulation. Is the primary concern really that the *tumtum* will turn out to be a male *saris chama*? Surely the more immediate concern is that the *tumtum* will turn out to be female altogether. A *tumtum* who is *nikra* and found to be a woman creates no *zika* and no *yibum* obligation; but a *tumtum* who is *nikra* and found to be male but also a *saris chama* leaves the *yevama* in a complicated situation.

The Gemara therefore reconstructs what Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda actually meant: *shema yikarei venimtzah nekeivah ve'afilu nimtza zachar shema yimatzei saris chama* — perhaps, upon rupturing, the *tumtum* will be found to be female; and even if found to be male, perhaps that male will turn out to be a *saris chama*. Both possibilities counsel against permitting *chalitza*.

The Gemara then asks: *mai beinaihu* — where exactly do Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda disagree? Rabbi Yehuda holds that *every* male *tumtum* who is *nikra* is certainly a *saris*. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda says it is a matter of *safek*: perhaps he is a *saris chama*, perhaps not. This distinction has practical consequences.

One must be clear about the underlying scenario. The case involves a *tumtum* together with at least one other living brother. One brother has died; his widow is now a *yevama*. The *tumtum* must not perform *chalitza* because: if the *nikra* reveals him to be female, then *yibum* never applied in the first place; and if the *nikra* reveals him to be male, he might still be a *saris chama*, rendering his *chalitza* null and the widow still bound by *zika* to the other brothers.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: לִפְסוֹל בִּמְקוֹם אַחִין וְלַחְלוֹץ שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹם אַחִין אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical halakhic difference **between** the opinions of Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei? **Rava said: There is** a practical difference **between them** as to whether the *ḥalitza* performed by a *tumtum* **disqualifies** the brothers in a case **where there are** other **brothers** in addition to the *tumtum*. According to Rabbi Yehuda, a *tumtum* is regarded as definitely sexually impotent, and therefore his *ḥalitza* is of no consequence. Therefore, if the *tumtum* went ahead and performed *ḥalitza*, he has not disqualified the other brothers from performing levirate marriage. According to Rabbi Yosei, however, he is only doubtfully sexually impotent, and therefore he has disqualified the other brothers from performing levirate marriage. **And** there is also a difference between them as to whether the *tumtum* must **perform *ḥalitza* where there are no** other **brothers** besides him. According to Rabbi Yehuda he need not do so, whereas according to Rabbi Yosei he must perform *ḥalitza* owing to his uncertain status.

Rava articulates the precise *machloket*: *lifsol bemakom achin velachalotz shelo bemakom achin ika beinaihu* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda concerns two distinct scenarios: whether the *tumtum's* *chalitza* disqualifies the *yevama* to the surviving brothers when those brothers exist, and whether the *tumtum* may perform *chalitza* when there are no other brothers.

According to Rashi's exposition: if the *tumtum* has already performed *chalitza* in the presence of other brothers, Rabbi Yehuda would say his *chalitza* is null — he is certainly a *saris* — and therefore she is not *pasul* to the other brothers and may undergo *yibum*. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, holding that the question remains a *safek*, would say that if there is any chance the *chalitza* was valid, she is now *pasul* to the brothers and they cannot perform *yibum* until the matter is clarified.

In the second scenario, where there are no other brothers, Rabbi Yehuda would say: there is no point in performing *chalitza* at all, since he is a *saris* and no obligation of *yibum* applies. Permitting *chalitza* would needlessly render her *asur* to a *kohen*, for instance. Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, would permit the *chalitza* to proceed. If he turns out to be a fertile male, the *chalitza* is valid and resolves her situation. If not, nothing has been lost. In this reading, Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda's position is significantly more lenient — and more practically accommodating of the *yevama*'s circumstances.

The case of no other brothers also bears on the possibility of *agunah*. If the *tumtum* is not *nikra* and there are surviving brothers, the woman remains in a state of uncertainty, bound by *zika* and unable to remarry. While this is less severe than a full *agunah* — since she is forbidden to the rest of the world only by a *lav* — it remains a pressing practical concern.

## Androgynos Punishment

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אֲחוּהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר זַבְדִּי אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה, מִשְּׁתֵּי מְקוֹמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Shmuel bar Yehuda said** that **Rabbi Abba, brother of Rabbi Yehuda bar Zavdi, said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** With regard to **a hermaphrodite, one is liable to** receive the punishment of **stoning on his account** for intercourse **at two places,** whether one penetrated him anally, in the manner of homosexual intercourse, or through his female organ.

The Gemara records: *androgynos chayavin alav skila mishtei mekomot* — Rav states that one is liable to *skila* (stoning) for sexual intercourse with an *androgynos* in either of two modes. The reference, as established in the prior session, is to both anal and vaginal intercourse.

### R. Eliezer Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמַר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה כַּזָּכָר. בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּזַכְרוּת שֶׁלּוֹ, אֲבָל בְּנַקְבוּת שֶׁלּוֹ — פָּטוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** against this from the following teaching. **Rabbi Eliezer said:** If one had intercourse with **a hermaphrodite,** he **is liable to** be punished with **stoning on his account as if** he had relations **with a male. In what** case **is this statement said?** It is if he had relations with him **through his male** organ, i.e., in the manner of homosexual intercourse, **but** if he engaged in intercourse with him **through his female** organ, he is **exempt.**

A *baraita* is cited that appears to challenge Rav's ruling. Rabbi Eliezer says: *androgynos chayavin alav skila k'vizachar* — one is liable for intercourse with an *androgynos* like one is liable for intercourse with a male — that is, only by way of his male aspect. *Bameh devarim amurim bizchrut shelo* — when are these words said? In the male mode. *Aval b'nikvut shelo patur* — but by way of the female aspect, one is exempt.

### R. Simai Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא דְּאָמַר כִּי הַאי תַּנָּא, דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי סִימַאי אוֹמֵר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה מִשְּׁתֵּי מְקוֹמוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that Rav **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **this *tanna*, as it is taught** in the following *baraita*: **Rabbi Simai says:** With regard to **a hermaphrodite, one is liable to** be punished with **stoning on his account** for intercourse **at two places.**

The Gemara resolves the tension by locating Rav within a different tannaitic tradition. *Hu de'amar ki hai tanna* — Rav speaks in accordance with this *tanna*: Rabbi Simai, who says that one is liable to *skila* for intercourse with an *androgynos* in both modes. Rav is thus not in conflict with Rabbi Eliezer's *baraita*; he simply follows Rabbi Simai's view. And since Rav is categorized as one who has the standing of a *tanna* — *Rav tanna hu u'palig* — a contradicting *baraita* would not have constituted a fatal challenge in any case.

### Midrashic Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי סִימַאי? אָמַר רָבָא: בַּר הַמְדּוּרֵי אַסְבְּרַהּ לִי: ״וְאֶת זָכָר לֹא תִשְׁכַּב מִשְׁכְּבֵי אִשָּׁה״, אֵי זֶהוּ זָכָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שְׁנֵי מִשְׁכָּבוֹת — הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר זֶה אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. וְרַבָּנַן, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית בֵּיהּ שְׁנֵי מִשְׁכָּבוֹת — ״אֶת זָכָר״ כְּתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Simai? Rava said:** The Sage **bar Hamedurei explained** the matter **to me,** based on an allusion to this *halakha* found in the Bible. The verse states: **"And you shall not lie with a male as with a woman [*mishkevei isha*]"** (Leviticus 18:22). The phrase *mishkevei isha*, referring to lying with a woman, appears in the plural. Now, **what male has two** manners of **lying? You must say** that **this** is referring to **a hermaphrodite,** and the plural form *mishkevei*, meaning: Lyings, indicates that there is liability for both manners of intercourse with him. The Gemara asks: **And** how do **the Rabbis** who disagree with Rabbi Simai counter this argument? The Gemara explains: **Although he has two** manners of **lying, it is** nevertheless **written: "With a male,"** indicating that one is liable to be stoned on a hermaphrodite's account only if he had relations with him in the manner of a male.

The Gemara asks: *mai ta'ama d'Rabbi Simai?* — what is the *drashah* that grounds Rabbi Simai's position? Rava bar Hamdurei explains: *v'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei ishah* — the *pasuk* prohibiting homosexual intercourse speaks of "the lyings of a woman" (*mishkevei ishah*). The *derasha* proceeds: *eizehu zachar sheyesh bo shenei mishkavot?* — what kind of male can have two types of *mishkavot*? *Hevei omer zeh androgynos* — one must say this refers to an *androgynos*.

### Sage vs. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, זָכָר גְּרֵידָא מְנָא לְהוּ? מֵ״אִשָּׁה״. בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁלֹּא כְּדַרְכָּהּ מְנָא לְהוּ? מִ״וְּאִשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And the Rabbis,** who explain this entire verse as referring to a hermaphrodite, **from where do they** derive that a man is prohibited from engaging in relations with **an ordinary male?** The Gemara answers: They derive it **from** the words **"a woman."** The Gemara asks further: And **from where do** the Rabbis derive that one is liable to be punished for engaging in intercourse with **a woman** who is forbidden to him even if he engaged in relations **in an unnatural manner,** i.e., anal intercourse? The Gemara responds: They derive it **from** the inclusive "and" in **"and…with a woman."**

This *midrashic* derivation immediately raises a challenge. If the *pasuk* is interpreted to be specifically about an *androgynos*, from where do the Rabbis derive the prohibition of ordinary homosexual intercourse? *Minalhu?* The answer: *me'ishah* — from the word *ishah* at the end of the verse. The extra word *ishah* teaches that even straightforward homosexual intercourse is forbidden. But this generates a further difficulty. In many contexts *mishkevei ishah* is the source for the principle that anal intercourse with any woman one is forbidden to is itself a prohibited act (*be'isha shelo kedarka*). If that derivation is now redirected toward the *androgynos* case, from where is *be'isha shelo kedarka* derived?

The Gemara's answer is that the source lies in the combination of the *vav* at the beginning of *ve'et zachar* and the word *ishah* at the end of the verse: *ve'et zachar lo tishkav mishkevei ishah*. This is admittedly a difficult piece of *midrash*. Some *nuscha'im* read *ve'et ishah* rather than the standard text, which would alter the derivation, and the *Bach* as well as the *Masoret HaShas* note these textual variants. The precise mechanics of the derivation remain elusive. One might indeed suggest, as a general principle, that in certain cases the Rabbis possessed a firm tradition that a given act was prohibited at the *d'oraita* level and were working to find an asmachta in the text — though one would hope for a more transparent *drasha* here.

The upshot of the dispute is that Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages hold the same overall *pasuk* but derive its scope differently. According to Rabbi Eliezer, the *androgynos* is treated as male, and therefore only the male mode of intercourse with him generates liability. The Sages and Rabbi Simai derive from the plural *mishkevei* that both modes of intercourse with an *androgynos* render one liable to *skila*.

## Androgynos Sacrifices

### R. Eliezer Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁזְבִי אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: לֹא לַכֹּל אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס זָכָר מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, שֶׁאִם אַתָּה אוֹמֵר כֵּן, בְּמוּקְדָּשִׁין — יִקְדַּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Shezvi said** that **Rav Ḥisda said: Not in all** regards did **Rabbi Eliezer say** that **a hermaphrodite is a proper male. As, if you say so,** that a hermaphrodite is a proper male in every aspect, then **with regard to consecrated** animals an animal that is a hermaphrodite **should become sacred** if one consecrated it.

Rav Shizbi in the name of Rav Chisda clarifies the scope of Rabbi Eliezer's position: *lo lekol amar Rabbi Eliezer androgynus zachar ma'alya* — Rabbi Eliezer did not say that an *androgynos* is a full male for every purpose. Why not? *Hu she'im attah omer ken bemo kodeshim yikdash* — because if one were to say so, then a hermaphroditic animal offered as a sacrifice would have genuine *kedushat kodashim*, which Rabbi Eliezer plainly does not hold. The Gemara therefore asks: from where do we know that an *androgynos* animal does not acquire *kedusha* in that context?

### Bird Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָלַן דְּלָא קָדֵשׁ — דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַנִּרְבָּע וְהַמּוּקְצֶה וְהַנֶּעֱבָד וְהָאֶתְנַן וּמְחִיר וְטוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה. שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — אַתָּה מוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס מִבֵּינֵיהֶם. וְעוֹף, הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — אִי אַתָּה מוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס מִבֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where do we** derive **that it does not become sacred? As the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to a bird **used for sexual relations** with a human being, **and one set aside** for idolatrous purposes, **and one** that itself was **worshipped** as an idol, **and one** given as **payment** to a prostitute (see Deuteronomy 23:19), **and one** that was **the price of a dog** received in exchange for the sale of a dog (see Deuteronomy 23:19), **and** similarly, a bird that is **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite,** if one killed any of these birds by pinching their necks in the manner of an offering rather than by ritual slaughter, the birds **render** him and the **garments** he is wearing **ritually impure,** when the birds are eaten and come into contact **with** his **throat.** The reason is that pinching is valid only for sacrificial birds; any other bird that is killed by pinching is deemed an unslaughtered carcass. Since none of these birds are fit to be sacrificed, pinching their necks renders them unslaughtered carcasses, and the unslaughtered carcass of a clean bird imparts ritual impurity when it is eaten and reaches the individual's throat. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If one pinched the neck of a bird that is **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite, it does not render** him and the **garments** he is wearing **ritually impure** when it is eaten and comes into contact **with** his **throat,** as the sanctity of an offering does in fact apply to it. **As Rabbi Eliezer would say: Wherever it is** explicitly **stated** in the Torah **"male" and "female," you** are to **remove a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite from among them,** as their gender status is in doubt. This is true of animal offerings, with regard to which the Torah uses the terms male and female. In the case of **a bird-**offering, however, **since male and female are not stated with regard to it,** but instead the Torah simply mentions turtledoves and young pigeons, **you** are **not** to **remove a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite from among them,** as they are fit for the altar. It is evident then that Rabbi Eliezer maintains that a hermaphrodite is not considered a proper male with respect to offerings.

A *baraita* is cited. Among the categories of animals that are *pasul* for the Temple — an animal used for bestiality, one set aside for *avodah zarah*, one that was actually worshipped, one purchased with the fee of a prostitute (*etnan zonah*), and one purchased with the price of a dog (*mchir kelev*, which in the biblical context may refer to male prostitutes, though the Gemara's own understanding may lean toward a literal dog) — the *baraita* includes a *tumtum* or *androgynos*. These animals, the Sages say, cause ritual impurity to garments upon being swallowed, *mitam'im begadim beit habli'ah* — which is the specific impurity that affects one who eats *nivlat of* (the carcass of a bird that died without valid *shechita*). Since birds are not slaughtered in the Temple but rather undergo *melika* (a snapping of the neckbone from behind), an invalid bird — one that cannot be brought as a *korban* — when subjected to *melika* does not achieve the status of valid *shechita*, and therefore what results is a *neveilah*, which triggers this form of impurity.

Rabbi Eliezer, however, says that a *tumtum* or *androgynos* does *not* cause this impurity — *ein metam'im begadim beit habli'ah*. This initially seems to prove the opposite of what is needed: if Rabbi Eliezer holds that *melika* on a *tumtum* or *androgynos* bird does not produce *neveilah* impurity, does that not imply that he considers such animals valid for the Temple?

### Principle Summary

The apparent contradiction is resolved by Rabbi Eliezer's own stated principle: *kol makom shene'emar zachar unekeva ata motzi tumtum ve'androgynus mibeineihem* — wherever the Torah specifies "male" or "female" in connection with sacrifices, a *tumtum* or *androgynos* is excluded. For four-legged animals, the Torah consistently designates the required gender of the offering — *seh zachar tamim*, or *seh nekeva* — and therefore a *tumtum* or *androgynos* animal cannot be brought as a *korban*. *Ve'of ho'il velo ne'emar bo zachar unekeva i ata motzi tumtum ve'androgynus mibeineihem* — but birds, since the Torah does not specify gender when discussing bird offerings, a *tumtum* or *androgynos* bird is not excluded from consideration. Hence Rabbi Eliezer holds that such a bird *can* be brought as a *korban*, the *melika* is valid, and the result is not a *neveilah* — which is precisely why it does not cause garment impurity.

This clarifies Rabbi Eliezer's full position: an *androgynos* is not a *zachar lakol* — not treated as fully male for all purposes. Where the Torah specifies gender (as it does for most four-legged *korbanot*), the *androgynos* is excluded. Where the Torah does not specify gender (as with bird offerings), the *androgynos* may qualify. This is consistent with his view that the *androgynos* is in some respects male (hence *skila* for the male mode of intercourse with him) but not in all respects.

### Temura Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַף אֲנַן נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַכִּלְאַיִם וּטְרֵפָה וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — לֹא קְדוֹשִׁין וְלֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא קְדוֹשִׁין — בִּתְמוּרָה. וְלֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין — בְּעוֹשֶׂה תְּמוּרָה. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: We too learned** this explicitly in a *baraita* that states: **Rabbi Eliezer says: Forbidden crossbred livestock, an animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months** [***tereifa***], an animal **delivered through the abdominal wall,** and an animal that is **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite do not become sacred and do not render** another animal **sacred** in their place. **And Shmuel said: They do not become sacred by way of substitution,** i.e., if one had an animal that had been designated as an offering, and he wished to substitute one of these animals for it, the substituted animal does not become sacred. **And they** themselves **do not render** another animal **sacred when it is made a substitute** for them. If one designated one of these animals as an offering and he wished to substitute another animal for it, it does not become sacred. The Gemara concludes: **Learn from this** that Rabbi Eliezer does not consider a hermaphrodite to be a proper male.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak — who characteristically sees himself as an organizer and cross-referencer rather than an originator of novel rulings — adds: *af anan nami tanina* — I too can cite a *mishnah* to this effect, not merely a *baraita*. The *mishnah* is: *hakilayim utreifa veyotzei dofen tumtum ve'androgynus lo kedoshim velo makdishim* — a hybrid animal, a *treifa*, an animal born by cesarean, a *tumtum*, or an *androgynos* — none of these animals can receive *kedusha*, and none of them can transfer *kedusha* to another.

Shmuel explains that this *mishnah* refers specifically to *temura*, the act of substituting one animal for another. In *temura*, if one attempts to replace a dedicated *korban* by designating a different animal in its stead, the result is that both animals become *kodesh*. But if one tries to perform *temura* using an *androgynos* animal — either as the subject being swapped out or as the replacement — nothing happens. The *androgynos* animal neither acquires *kedusha* through the act of substitution nor transmits it. An *androgynos* animal that is already *kodesh* by virtue of being born from a *kodesh* mother is *kodesh* by birth; but it cannot be an active participant in the mechanics of *temura*.

One might ask: is *androgynos* automatically a *mum* that would disqualify a *korban*? The answer is no — *androgynos* is not classified as a *mum*. An *androgynos* animal born to a *kodesh* mother remains *kodesh* but with no valid sacrificial use, and therefore it grazes (*yir'eh*) until it develops an actual *mum*, at which point its *kedusha* can be transferred and the animal redeemed. The *mishnah*'s ruling confirms that Rabbi Eliezer does not treat the *androgynos* as a *zachar lakol*.

## Final Baraita

### R. Personal Account

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: כְּשֶׁהָלַכְתִּי לִלְמוֹד תּוֹרָה אֵצֶל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ, חָבְרוּ עָלַי תַּלְמִידָיו כְּתַרְנְגוֹלִים שֶׁל בֵּית בּוּקְיָא, וְלֹא הִנִּיחוּנִי לִלְמוֹד אֶלָּא דָּבָר אֶחָד בְּמִשְׁנָתֵינוּ — רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו סְקִילָה כַּזָּכָר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** on this matter in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: When I went to learn Torah from Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua, his students joined** together **against me like the roosters of Beit Bukya,** highly aggressive animals that do not allow other creatures to remain among them, **and they did not let me learn** there. Therefore, I managed to learn **only one thing in our mishna,** which is that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If one had intercourse with **a hermaphrodite,** one **is liable to** be punished with **stoning on his account as if** one had relations with **a male.**

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which Rabbi (Yehudah HaNasi) offers a personal account: *kshehalachti lilmod Torah etzel Rabbi Elazar ben Shamoa* — when I went to learn Torah from Rabbi Elazar ben Shamoa, the famous student of Rabbi Akiva. His disciples crowded around him so aggressively, *ketarnigolim shel beit bukya* — like the roosters of Beit Bukya, a place known for its particularly combative fowl — *velo hinichuni lilmod* — and they did not allow me to learn. The account has a certain pathos. Whether it reflects the disciples' protective jealousy of their time with Rabbi Elazar ben Shamoa, or an almost overwhelming excitement at having the future *Nasi* in their midst, we cannot say. The incident is cited in the *baraita* immediately following a discussion of *of* (birds), and the reference to roosters connects evocatively to the surrounding material — though presumably that juxtaposition is incidental.

### Conclusion

Rabbi reports that despite this interference he was able to learn one thing: *ela davar echad bemishnatenu Rabbi Eliezer omer androgynus chayavin alav skila kezachar* — only one teaching from their *mishnah*: Rabbi Eliezer holds that one is liable to *skila* for intercourse with an *androgynos* as one is liable for intercourse with a male. This single transmitted teaching encapsulates the fundamental ruling of the chapter's concluding passage.

### End He-Arel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הֶעָרֵל
>
> **Translation:** [Concluding formula for the chapter *He-Arel*]

*Hadran alach He-arel* — with this, the chapter *He-Arel* concludes.

## Women Categories

### Mishna Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֵשׁ מוּתָּרוֹת לְבַעְלֵיהֶן וַאֲסוּרוֹת לְיִבְמֵיהֶן, מוּתָּרוֹת לְיִבְמֵיהֶן וַאֲסוּרוֹת לְבַעְלֵיהֶן. מוּתָּרוֹת לָאֵלּוּ וְלָאֵלּוּ, וַאֲסוּרוֹת לָאֵלּוּ וְלָאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There are** women **who are permitted to their husbands and forbidden to their *yevamin*,** while others **are permitted to their *yevamin* and forbidden to their husbands.** Certain women **are permitted** both **to these and to those, and** others **are forbidden to** both **these and to those.**

The opening *mishnah* of the new chapter introduces a typology of women's permissibility and prohibition in the context of marriage and *yibum*. *Yesh mutarot l'va'aleihen va-asurot livyameihen* — there are women who are permitted to their husbands but, should the husband die, are forbidden to the *yavam* (the surviving brother obligated in *yibum*). Conversely, *yesh mutarot livyameihen va-asurot l'va'aleihen* — there are women who are permitted to the *yavam* but were, from the outset, forbidden to the husband himself. And of course the majority case: *mutarot l'elu ul'elu* — most women are permitted to both, in their respective situations. The *mishnah* will proceed to supply concrete examples of each category — a discussion that belongs to the next session.
