# Makkot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Manslayer's 2nd Crime

### Manslayer 2nd Kill

The daf opens on 12b, roughly a third of the way from the bottom, with the first of two *mishnayos* that close out the topic of *arei miklat* and the accidental killer. The *mishnah* poses the following case: *harag b'oso ha'ir* — what is the *halacha* if someone already exiled to a *ir miklat* for an accidental killing now kills again within that same city?

The answer is *goleh mishchuna lishchuna* — he must exile himself from the neighborhood he has been living in and relocate to a different neighborhood within the same *ir miklat*. He cannot move to a different *ir miklat* entirely, since he is not permitted to leave the city he is already confined to. Yet the *mishna* imposes some form of exile upon him, and so his exile takes the form of a neighborhood displacement within the existing city.

This raises an interesting question. If he were to leave the neighborhood to which he has been internally exiled, would he expose himself to being killed by the *goel hadam* of the second killing? Presumably not — he remains within the *ir miklat* itself, which continues to protect him. So there is an intriguing asymmetry here: he could technically violate the terms of his internal exile without thereby exposing himself to the blood avenger. Nevertheless, there is no alternative available to the *halacha*, and this is what it prescribes.

### Ben Levi Goleh

The *mishnah* then addresses a different scenario: *u'ven Levi goleh me'ir le'ir* — if a Levite accidentally kills someone, he is exiled from one of the *arei Levi'im* to another. Since all the *arei Levi'im* serve as *arei miklat*, this is the natural form his exile takes. Unlike the person who committed an earlier accidental killing and was already confined to a *ir miklat*, the Levite who kills has not yet been placed in exile at all. He was simply living in a Levite city, which happened to also be a *ir miklat*. He must therefore be properly exiled, and that means departing for a different *ir miklat*.

### Midbar Precedent

The *gemara* opens with a *braisa* built on the *pasuk* in Shemos: *vesamti lecha makom* — I will give you a place to which the accidental killer may flee. The *drashah* reads *lecha* as *bechayecha*: this will happen in your own lifetime, Moshe, before the people cross into *Ever HaYarden*. This was already operative immediately after *yetzias Mitzrayim*. The *braisa* continues: *mimkomecha* — "from among your places," meaning the *arei miklat* would be drawn from the *arei Levi'im*, and all of them may function as *arei miklat*.

The *pasuk* adds *asher yanus shama* — that he shall flee there — and from this the *braisa* derives: *melamed shehayu sham galim bamidbar*, that this institution of exile was already operative even in the wilderness, before the formal *arei miklat* had been designated. The precise derivation is not fully elaborated, but that is the *braisa*'s conclusion. And so the question arises: where would an accidental killer go in the *midbar*, when there were no designated cities? *Lemachaneh Leviyah* — to the Levite camp. Since the *arei miklat* were to come from the *arei Levi'im*, the *machaneh Leviyah* itself functioned as the *ir miklat* in the wilderness.

From here the *braisa* derives: *mikan amru ben Levi sheharag goleh mipelech lepelech* — a Levite who kills is exiled from his city to another. This is, at one level, simply logical: what else could be done with a Levite who kills? He goes into exile and travels to a different *ir miklat*. But the *mikan amru* is really pointing to the second half of the *braisa*: *ve'im gola lepelcho pelcho kolto* — if he exiles himself within his own city, that same city can absorb and protect him.

What exactly does this mean? *Tosafos* explains that it refers to the case analogous to the *rotze'ach* in the first part of the *mishnah*: the person who commits his second killing while already confined to a *ir miklat* has no choice but to exile himself to another neighborhood. But a Levite — or any person who happens to be living in a *ir miklat* and has only now killed for the first time — has a choice. He can either relocate to a different *ir miklat* entirely, or he can remain in his current city but confine himself to a particular neighborhood, in effect exiling himself internally.

Why would anyone choose the former option over the latter? The answer is straightforward. If one stays within the same city but exiles to a single neighborhood, one is permanently restricted to that narrow area. If one relocates to an entirely different *ir miklat*, one can circulate freely throughout that city. So there is a real practical trade-off.

The *braisa*'s *mikan amru* also provides the conceptual source for this option. In the *midbar* there was only one *machaneh Leviyah*. If a *ben Levi* had killed someone there, the only possibility would have been to exile himself within that same encampment — there was nowhere else to go. This establishes the principle that internal exile within the same city is a valid form of *galus*.

### City Absorbs Him

*Rav Aha b'rei d'Rav Ika* then asks: what is the *pasuk* that supports this? He cites: *ki be'ir miklato yeshev* — in his city of refuge he shall dwell — and reads it to mean: *ir shekaltaso kevar*, even a city that has already absorbed and protected him can continue to serve as his *ir miklat*. This appears to reach beyond the *ben Levi* case and applies as well to someone who was already functioning within a *ir miklat* — that city can absorb him anew. This is a distinct *chiddush* from the *ben Levi* discussion: Rav Aha is addressing the earlier case, where the man who kills a second time remains protected by the same *ir miklat* that had already received him.

## Manslayer Rehab

### Shemitta Parallel

The next *mishnah* opens with the phrase *kayotzei bo* — similarly — though what follows is not similar to the preceding *mishnah* in any obvious way. It is, in fact, a transcription of a *mishnah* from the tenth *perek* of *Shevi'is* (perek 10, *mishnah* 8). The context in *Shevi'is* is that *shemitah* annuls outstanding debts, and the question arises: if a debtor nonetheless wishes to repay, what should the creditor do?

The *mishnah* in *Shevi'is* teaches: *hamachazir chov bashevi'is, yomar lo meshamet ani* — if someone comes to repay a debt during the *shemitah* year, the creditor should declare, "I annul it." If the debtor then responds *af al pi chen* — nevertheless, I still wish to pay — then *yekabel mehen*, the creditor may accept it. The basis is *shene'emar vezeh devar hashemitah* — "and this is the matter of the *shemitah*." The word *davar* teaches that what matters is the verbal declaration: the creditor says the words "I annul," checks that obligation, and thereafter, if the debtor chooses to give the money back voluntarily, it is received as a gift rather than as a loan repayment.

### Honors Despite Crime

The *mishnah* then brings the parallel: *kayotzei bo rotze'ach* — similarly, a *rotze'ach* who has been sent to his *ir miklat*, and the people of the city wish to honor him, *yomar lahem rotze'ach ani* — he must declare, "I am a killer, I cannot accept this honor." If they respond *af al pi chen* — nevertheless — then *yekabel mehen*, he may accept the honor. The source is *shene'emar vezeh devar harotze'ach* — "and these are the words of the killer." Just as *devar* in *shemitah* means you say the words of annulment, here *devar* means the *rotze'ach* must say the words identifying his status. Having done so, and with the community wishing to honor him despite that declaration, he may accept.

### Taxes & Rent

The *mishnah* continues at the top of 13a. The question arises: do the inhabitants of the *ir miklat* provide free lodging and support to the exiled killer, or must he pay for his own expenses? *Ma'alim hayu sachar laleviyim* — they would charge the Levites rent — *divrei Rabbi Yehudah*. According to *Rabbi Yehudah*, the exiled person must pay rent and, as *Tosafos* clarifies, this extends also to property taxes and any other civic financial obligations. Being exiled there does not exempt one from the ordinary financial responsibilities of residence.

*Rabbi Meir* disagrees: *lo hayu ma'alim lahem sachar* — no, they would not charge him rent or other expenses. He is, in a sense, like a prisoner who does not pay for his confinement.

The *gemara* explores the basis for this dispute through *Rav Kahana*, who holds: *machlokes beshesh* — the debate applies only to the six primary *arei miklat*. As for the other 42 Levite cities, both opinions would agree that the exiled person must pay his own way. The reasoning: the *pasuk* says *v'hayu lachem ha'arim* — these cities shall be *lachem*, for you. One opinion (*Rabbi Yehudah*) reads *lachem* as meaning only that they serve the protective function of refuge — *lachem liklita* — but not that all expenses are covered. The other (*Rabbi Meir*) reads *lachem lechol tzarcheichem* — for all your needs, meaning the community bears those costs.

*Rava*, however, pushes back. He argues that the phrase *lachem lechol tzarcheichem* is clearly expansive — it certainly means that at least for the primary six *arei miklat*, there would be no charge, by everyone's agreement. Therefore, *Rava* reframes the entire dispute: *machlokes b'arbayim veshtayim* — the debate is only about the remaining 42. One opinion holds that *ve'aleihem titnu* — the command to add those 42 Levite cities means they too serve purely as refuge (*kehanei liklita*), just like the primary six, and therefore there are no charges for the exiled killer there either. The other opinion holds that only the primary six carry that full benefit, while the remaining 42 do not. But for the original six, says Rava, *divrei hakol lo hayu ma'alim lahem sachar* — by everyone's agreement, no rent was charged.

### Status Recovery

After addressing the financial question, the *mishnah* turns to a broader rehabilitative question: *vachozer lisrarah shehayah bah* — does the exiled person return to the social standing and position he held before? *Divrei Rabbi Meir* — Rabbi Meir says yes. Rabbi Meir is the more lenient voice in both debates: he says the person need not pay rent, and he says the person fully recovers his prior status upon returning home after the *kohen gadol*'s death.

*Rabbi Yehudah*, consistent with his position in both disputes, is more stringent: *lo haya chozer lisrarah shehayah bah* — no, he does not return to the position he previously held.

### Eved Ivri Rehab

The *gemara* introduces a *braisa* that illuminates the debate through the case of the *eved Ivri*. The *braisa* cites the *pasuk*: *veshav el mishpachto ve'el achuzat avotav yashuv* — he shall return to his family and to the inheritance of his fathers. This verse speaks of the Hebrew slave who goes free at *yovel*. The *braisa* derives: *lemishpachto shav* — he returns to his family. But does he return to the inherited status and position his forefathers held? *Rabbi Yehudah* says no: *eino shav lema shehecheziku avotav*. He went and sold himself into servitude; that cannot simply be erased. Whatever ancestral post or inherited position he held before does not automatically revert to him. *Rabbi Meir* disagrees: *af hu shav lema shehecheziku avotav* — he does return to the very standing his ancestors held. The *pasuk* says *el achuzat avotav ke'avotav* — to his fathers' inheritance, just like his fathers — and that includes whatever posts and status they occupied.

### Eved/Manslayer Link

The *braisa* then draws the connection to the *rotze'ach*: *ke'yotzeh bo, vechen begola* — and similarly when it comes to exile. The *pasuk* regarding the exiled killer says *yashuv harotze'ach el eretz achuzato* — he shall return to the land of his inheritance. Rabbi Yehudah reads this literally: *l'eretz achuzato yashuv eino shav lema shehecheziku avotav* — he returns to the land, but not to the ancestral status. *Rabbi Meir* says *af hu shav lema shehecheziku avotav* — he too returns to what his forefathers held.

How does Rabbi Meir derive this from a *pasuk* that only says *eretz achuzato*, making no mention of ancestral status? The answer is *gamar shiva shiva mehatam* — a verbal linkage (*gezeirah shavah*) between the word *yashuv* here and the word *yashuv* in the *eved Ivri* context, where the *pasuk* explicitly says *ve'al achuzat avotav yashuv*. Just as the *eved Ivri* returns *el achuzat avotav* — to the standing of his forefathers — so too does the *rotze'ach* return to that standing, according to Rabbi Meir. There is a striking and genuine belief in full rehabilitation here: both the *eved Ivri* and the *rotze'ach* are returned, in Rabbi Meir's view, not merely to physical home and family, but to the full ancestral position that had been theirs before.

## Galus to Malkos

### Perek Transition

*Hadran alach elu hein hagolin* — so concludes the *perek* on *galus*. The daf now pivots to *ve'elu hein halokin* — these are those who receive *malkus*. The transition deserves a moment's reflection. *Sanhedrin* concluded with the laws of *chayvei misos*, and *Makkos* picks up from there as an extension of *Sanhedrin*. Once *chayvei misos* have been addressed, the tractate turns to the other major punishments that *beis din* imposes: exile (*galus*) for the accidental killer, and now *malkus* for those who transgress a range of prohibitions.

There is also a structural symmetry worth noting. The first *perek* of *Makkos* opened with the case of *edim zomemim* who testified that someone is a *ben gerusha* or *ben chaluza* — the accused does not receive that status, but the false witnesses receive *malkus*. Similarly, a witness who falsely testified that someone is *chayav galus* does not cause the person to be exiled but receives *malkus* himself. So the first *perek* introduced both *malkus* and *galus*, and the second and third *perakim* then expand upon each in turn.

### Sexual Prohibitions

The third *perek* opens with those who incur *malkus*. In principle, any active violation of a *lo ta'aseh* — provided it involves a deed, is not *nitak l'asei* (capable of being corrected by the performance of a positive commandment), and is not subject to capital punishment — incurs *malkus*. There are 365 negative commandments, so the category is vast. The *mishnah* does not enumerate all of them but focuses on cases that carry some *chiddush*.

The first category listed is *chayvei krisos*: those who are liable for *kares* through forbidden sexual unions. The *mishnah* lists: *haba al achoto* — one who has intercourse with his sister; *ve'al achot aviv* — his father's sister; *ve'al achot imo* — his mother's sister; *ve'al achot ishto* — his wife's sister; *ve'al eshet achiv* — his brother's wife; *ve'al eshet achi aviv* — his father's brother's wife; *ve'al haniddah* — a woman who is in the state of *niddah*. All of these are *arayos* that carry the penalty of *kares*, and the *chiddush* of the *mishnah* is that even so, if witnesses warned the person that he would receive *malkus*, he receives *malkus*.

### Priestly Marriages

The *mishnah* continues with the forbidden marriages of *kohanim*: *almana l'kohen gadol* — a widow whom a *kohen gadol* marries — and *gerusha vechaluza l'kohen hedyot* — a divorced woman or a *chaluza* married by an ordinary *kohen*. These are forbidden to the respective *kohanim* and incur *malkus*.

The *chaluza* case carries its own complexity. *Rashi* treats *chaluza* as a *d'oraisa* prohibition that carries full *malkus*. *Tosafos* takes the more standard *gemara* position that *chaluza* is *d'rabbanan*, with the consequence being only *makas mardus* rather than biblical *malkus* — though if the woman is both a *gerusha* and a *chaluza*, the plain reading of the *mishnah* would seem to treat *chaluza* as *d'oraisa*. The *mishnah* also includes: *mamzeres ve'nesina le-mamzer* — a *mamzeret* or a *nesina* (a descendant of the Gibeonites, one of the *shiv'as amim*) to a regular *Yisrael* — and conversely, *bas Yisrael le'nesin u'le'mamzer*. These are straightforward *lavin*, not involving *kares*, and the reason they are grouped here will become clearer in what follows.

### Double Status

The *mishnah* then raises the case of a woman who is simultaneously an *almana* and a *gerusha*. If a *kohen gadol* has intercourse with her, he has violated two separate *lavin* in a single act — the prohibition against a *kohen gadol* marrying an *almana*, and the prohibition against marrying a *gerusha*. He is therefore liable for two separate sets of *malkus*: *chayav aleha mishum shtei shemos*.

However, if the woman is both a *gerusha* and a *chaluza*, the *mishnah* rules *eino chayav ela mishum shem echad bilvad* — he is liable for only one set of *malkus*. A *chaluza* is a subcategory of *gerusha*: both represent a severing of the marital bond, and the *chaluza* is effectively a type of *gerusha*. Whether one holds *chaluza* is *d'oraisa* or *d'rabbanan*, it remains a subset of the same prohibition, and only one punishment applies.

### Temple Impurity

The *mishnah* continues with matters involving the *beis hamikdash* and sacred food. *Ha'tamei she'achal es ha'kodesh* — one who is *tamei* and eats *korbanos*; *ve'ha'ba el ha'mikdash tamei* — and one who enters the *beis hamikdash* while *tamei*. These too carry *kares*, and the *mishnah* teaches they are nonetheless subject to *malkus* under the right conditions.

The *mishnah* adds: *ve'ochel chelev ve'dam ve'nosar u'figul* — eating forbidden fats, blood, *nosar* (a *korban* left beyond its permitted time), and *pigul* (a *korban* whose *shechita* was performed with an intent to consume it outside the permitted time or place). All of these carry *kares*, yet *malkus* can apply. The *mishnah* also includes *ve'tamei* — eating *korbanos* that themselves are *tamei*, which does not carry *kares* but is included here in the same breath.

### Forbidden Foods

The *mishnah* lists: *ve'ha'shocheit u'ma'aleh ba'chutz* — one who *shochets* a *korban* outside the *beis hamikdash*, or who offers up the *eimurim* outside; *ve'ochel chametz ba'Pesach* — eating *chametz* on *Pesach*; *ve'ha'ochel ve'ha'oseh melacha b'Yom Kippur* — eating or performing *melacha* on *Yom Kippur*. These are all *chayvei kares*, and the same principle applies.

The *mishnah* then moves to prohibitions involving the *shemen ha'mishchah* and *ketores*: *ha'mifatem es ha'shemen* — one who compounds the anointing oil in the manner prescribed by the *Torah* for unauthorized use; *ve'ha'mifatem es ha'ketores* — one who similarly compounds the incense; *ve'ha'sach be'shemen ha'mishchah* — one who applies the actual anointing oil to their own flesh. These too incur *kares* and are subject to *malkus*.

The *mishnah* then lists: *ve'ochel neveilos u'treifos shekatzim u'remasim* — eating an animal that died without *shechita*, a *treifa*, and various forbidden insects and rodents. These carry a *lav* and *malkus*, which is not particularly surprising — but as the end of the *mishnah* will demonstrate, the mention of *shekatzim u'remasim* here is deliberate and sets up the closing debate.

It continues: *achal tevel u'ma'aser rishon she'lo nitlah trumaso* — eating *tevel* (untithed produce) or *ma'aser rishon* from which the *terumah* had not yet been separated; *u'ma'aser sheni ve'hekdesh she'lo nifdu* — or *ma'aser sheni* and *hekdesh* that had not been redeemed. All of these are forbidden consumptions that incur *malkus*.

### Minimal Amount

The *mishnah* closes with a debate: *kama yochal min ha'tevel ve'yehei chayav?* — how much must one eat of *tevel* in order to be liable? *Rabbi Shimon omer kol she'hu* — Rabbi Shimon says even the tiniest amount suffices. The *Sages* disagree: *k'zayis* — an olive-sized portion is required, as is the standard measure for eating-related prohibitions.

Rabbi Shimon challenges the Sages: *i ata modeh li b'ochel nemalah shehu chayav kol she'hu?* — do you not agree that one who eats an ant is liable even for the tiniest amount, even less than a *k'zayis*? The Sages respond: *mipnei she'hu k'beriasah* — yes, but that is because the ant is a whole, self-contained creature; it constitutes a *beryah*, an entire entity, and that intrinsic wholeness gives even the smallest specimen halakhic significance. By contrast, *tevel* is grain — fungible, undifferentiated — and therefore requires the standard minimum measure.

Rabbi Shimon retorts: *af chitah achas k'beriasah* — why isn't a single grain of wheat also a *beryah*, a self-contained natural unit? And therefore, he argues, even eating one kernel of *tevel* should render a person liable. The debate continues into the *gemara*.

## Lashes + Karet

### Akiva's View

The *gemara* opens this discussion by noting what the *mishnah* does and does not include. The *mishnah* lists *chayvei krisos* — those liable for *kares* — and teaches that *malkus* applies to them as well. But the *mishnah* conspicuously omits *chayvei misos beis din* — those liable for capital punishment. The inference is that if someone who is liable for the death penalty is warned by witnesses that he will receive *malkus*, he does not in fact receive *malkus*.

On whose view does this *mishnah* operate? *Masnisin mani Rebbe Akiva hi* — the *mishnah* follows the position of Rabbi Akiva.

### Yishmael Both

A *braisa* is then cited presenting the full spectrum of opinions. *Rabbi Yishmael* says: *echad chayvei krisos ve'echad chayvei misos beis din yeshno bichlal malkus arbaim* — whether a transgression carries *kares* or carries the death penalty of *beis din*, the person is included in the category of receiving forty lashes.

This statement can be understood in two ways. One reading is that the transgressor can receive both punishments: both *malkus* and execution, or both *malkus* and *kares*, for the same act. The other reading is that even when the witnesses warned only of *malkus* — without specifying the full punishment — Rabbi Yishmael would allow *malkus* to be imposed even for capital offenses. The *gemara* initially proceeds on the more expansive reading: that both punishments may apply simultaneously.

### Akiva Karet OK

*Rabbi Akiva* draws a distinction. *Chayvei krisos yeshno bichlal malkus arbaim* — yes, those liable for *kares* are included in the category of receiving forty lashes. The reasoning: the ordinary concern against double punishment rests on the principle of *k'dei rishaso* — according to his wickedness, as the *pasuk* says — and the rabbinic principle derived from this, *mishum rishah achas ata mechayvo ve'i ata mechayvo mishum shtei rishayus*: you make a person liable for one act of wrongdoing, not for two punishments from the same act. But *malkus* and *kares* together do not constitute double punishment in a prohibited sense, because *she'im asu teshuvah beis din shel maalah mochalin lahem* — if the person repents, the heavenly court forgives him and *kares* is not actually executed. Since *kares* is conditional on the absence of repentance, it does not collide with the imposition of *malkus* in the way that a certain punishment would.

For capital cases, however, *Rabbi Akiva* says *chayvei misos beis din eino bichlal malkus arbaim* — those liable for the death penalty do not receive forty lashes. Here the prohibition against double punishment applies fully: *she'im asu teshuvah ein beis din shel mata mochalin lahem* — even if a person repents, the earthly *beis din* does not forgo the execution. Repentance is between the person and God and does not affect the court's obligation to carry out the sentence. Therefore, you cannot impose both *malkus* and execution. One punishment precludes the other, and the graver punishment — death — takes precedence.

### Yitzchak No Lashes

*Rabbi Yitzchak* takes the opposite position from Rabbi Akiva's ruling on *kares* cases. He argues: *chayvei krisos bichlal hayu* — those liable for *kares* were originally subsumed within the general category of *malkus*, since any *lav* in principle carries *malkus*. But then the Torah made a specific mention of *kares* in connection with the case of one who sleeps with his sister. The entire concluding section of the *arayos* laws states *kol asher yaaseh mikol ha'to'eivos ha'eleh ve'nichresu* — all who do these abominations shall be cut off — applying *kares* broadly across the forbidden unions. Why then did the Torah single out *kares* specifically in the *achoto* case?

*Laduno b'chareis ve'lo b'malkos* — to teach that the only punishment assigned to that transgression is *kares*, not *malkus*. And this becomes the paradigm: wherever the Torah assigns *kares*, that is the punishment to the exclusion of *malkus*. So Rabbi Yitzchak holds that *chayvei krisos* receive *kares* alone, with no *malkus* at all.

All three positions are thus fully laid out: Rabbi Yishmael holds that both *malkus* and the more severe punishment — whether *kares* or death — can apply together. Rabbi Akiva holds that *malkus* applies alongside *kares*, but not alongside capital punishment. Rabbi Yitzchak holds that *kares* offenses carry only *kares* and exclude *malkus* entirely.

### Yishmael Source

The *gemara* now analyzes Rabbi Yishmael's source. *Mai taama d'Rebbi Yishmael?* The verse states: *im lo sishmor laasos es kol divrei ha'Torah hazos* — if you do not observe and fulfill all the words of this Torah — and then: *v'hifla Hashem es makosecha* — God will make your smitings wondrous. The word *hafla'ah* — wondrous or extraordinary — is unfamiliar as a punishment term. But the *gemara* reads it as a homonym: elsewhere the Torah says *v'hipilo ha'shofet* — the judge shall cast him down — *v'hikahu l'fanav arba'im yakenu* — and he shall be struck forty times. *Hevei omer hafla'ah zu malkos hi* — this *hafla'ah* is *malkus*.

So the verse *im lo sishmor laasos es kol divrei ha'Torah hazos* establishes that anyone who violates anything in the Torah — *kol divrei ha'Torah*, including prohibitions that carry *kares* or capital punishment — receives *malkus*. This is the broad basis for Rabbi Yishmael's position.

### Lav Definition

The *gemara* immediately challenges: *ai hachi, chayvei asei nami?* If the source verse speaks of "all the words of this Torah," why not extend *malkus* to the failure to observe a positive commandment (*asei*) as well? The *gemara* answers: *im lo sishmor kesiv* — the verse says "if you do not *observe* to do," which implies an active violation of something one was supposed to guard against. As *Rabbi Avin* in the name of *Rabbi Ila'a* teaches: wherever the Torah says *hishamer, pen*, or *al* — guard yourself, lest, do not — that formulation always denotes a *lav ta'aseh*, a negative prohibition against active transgression. So *lo sishmor* here means the failure to guard against doing something forbidden, not the mere failure to do something required.

The *gemara* presses further: *lav she'ein bo ma'aseh nami?* If the source verse generates *malkus* for all *lavin*, why not include a *lav* that involves no action — a purely passive violation? The *gemara* answers: *laasos kesiv* — the word *laasos*, "to do," demands that the violation be an active one.

Then: *lav hanitak l'asei nami?* Why not impose *malkus* for a *lav* that is *nitak l'asei* — connected to a positive commandment capable of correcting the violation? Here the *gemara* invokes the paradigm case.

### Chasima Model

The answer is *dumya d'lav d'chasimah* — the laws of *malkus* are modeled on the prohibition of *chasimah*, muzzling an ox while it threshes: *lo sachsom shor b'disho*. The *parasha* about the administration of *malkus* — *v'hipilo ha'shofet v'hikahu l'fanav* — is juxtaposed in the Torah directly to this prohibition of muzzling. This juxtaposition establishes the paradigm: the prohibition against muzzling the ox defines what type of *lav* incurs *malkus*. It must be a *lav ta'aseh* involving an active deed, and it must not be *nitak l'asei*, not correctable by a subsequent positive act. The muzzling prohibition fits both criteria, and all other *lavin* that incur *malkus* must be similar.

The *gemara* then resolves the broader discussion: *hashta d'asis l'hachi, kulhu nami dumya d'lav d'chasimah* — now that we have arrived at the muzzling paradigm, that itself determines the full scope of *malkus*. One need not derive it from the broad verse of *v'hifla Hashem es makosecha* at all; the verse *v'hipilo ha'shofet v'hikahu l'fanav*, juxtaposed to the muzzling prohibition, is the operative source. Anything comparable to the *lav* of *chasimah* — including transgressions that carry *misas beis din* — falls within the category of *malkus* punishments. The broad verse from which Rabbi Yishmael derived his position that even capital offenses carry *malkus* may simply be emphasizing that the full scope of "all the words of the Torah" are potentially included, without independently adding *chayvei misos* if they would otherwise be excluded by the muzzling paradigm.
