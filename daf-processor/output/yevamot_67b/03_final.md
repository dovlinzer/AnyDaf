# Yevamot 67b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Uber & Slave Status

### Braita Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג אוֹכְלִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר. שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest who was married to an Israelite woman and died **left children,** both the slaves of usufruct property **and** the slaves of guaranteed investment **may partake** of *teruma*. The slaves of guaranteed investment are owned by the children, who are priests, and the slaves of usufruct property are owned by the woman, who partakes of *teruma* due to her children. If **he left** his wife **pregnant** and did not leave children, both **these** slaves **and those** slaves **may not partake** of *teruma*. If **he left children and left her pregnant,** the **slaves of usufruct** property who belong to her **partake** of *teruma* **just as she partakes** due to her children. However, the **slaves of guaranteed investment,** who are inherited by the children, **may not partake, due to the fetus's share,** as it too inherits them, **as a fetus can disqualify** one from partaking of *teruma* **but it cannot not enable** one **to partake.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

The daf begins with a *braita* that elaborates upon the position of Rabbi Yose set out in the *mishna*, spelling out the various scenarios in which a pregnant woman married to a *kohen* has died and the question turns on which household members may eat *terumah*.

*Tanu rabbanan*: if the *kohen* left behind born sons and his wife was not pregnant, then both she and all the slaves may eat — the born child entitles the wife to remain connected to the *kohen*'s household, and all the slaves are fully owned by the heirs. If he left the wife pregnant but with no born children, then neither she nor the slaves may eat: she cannot eat on the basis of an unborn fetus, and the slaves cannot eat because they are owned, wholly or in part, by the fetus, who cannot confer upon them the right to eat *terumah*. If he left both born children and a pregnant wife, then her *nichsei melog* slaves — those she brought as her personal dowry property — may eat, because she herself eats by virtue of the born children, and *kederech she-hi ochelet* her slaves follow her status. The *nichsei tzon barzel* slaves, however — those she brought as the husband's property and which remain part of his estate — do not eat, *mipnei chelko shel uber*, because the fetus holds a partial share in what the born sons have inherited, and that share creates the problem. This is summarized as: the fetus *posel ve-eino ma'achil* — he invalidates but cannot feed. This is the ruling of Rabbi Yose, and the *braita* is simply an expansion of the *mishna*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: הַבַּת — מַאֲכֶלֶת, הַבֵּן — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יֹאכְלוּ כּוּלָּן, נְקֵבוֹת לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father:** If the priest left behind **a daughter,** she **enables** the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*; however, **a son does not enable** them **to partake. Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** If among the priest's children there are **males,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma*. But if they are **all females, they do not partake, lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** The male fetus would be the sole inheritor, and it does not enable slaves to partake of *teruma*.

Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose then states in his father's name: *ha-bat ma'achelet, ha-ben eino ma'achil* — the daughter feeds, the son does not feed. The *gemara* notes that this statement is initially opaque and proceeds to unpack it.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai adds: *zecharim yochlu kulam, nekevot lo yochlu* — if there are only male children and the wife is pregnant, all the slaves may eat; if there are only daughters and the wife is pregnant, the slaves may not eat.

### Male Minority Risk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא, דְּעָבְדִינַן לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא כְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** that **we are not concerned about the minority** of cases. Only a minority of fetuses are male inheritors, as roughly half are female, and some are stillborn. Therefore, the majority of fetuses will not become male children. **And if you wish, say** that **actually he holds** that **we are concerned about the minority.** However, **we make an arrangement for** the slaves, **in accordance with** what **Rav Naḥman** said that **Shmuel said.**

The *gemara* presses on Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's ruling: if there are born boys and a pregnant wife, why are the slaves permitted to eat? The fetus might itself be a boy and thereby hold a share in the estate, which should prevent the slaves from eating. The answer given is that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai holds *ein choshishin le-mi'uta* — one need not be concerned about a minority possibility. The chain of logic is this: there is a fifty percent chance the fetus will be female, in which case it holds no share in the estate (daughters do not inherit when sons are present). Even if the fetus is male, not every pregnancy results in a live birth. The combined probability that a live baby boy will be born from this pregnancy falls below fifty percent — a *mi'ut* — and one need not worry about a *mi'ut* in the majority of cases.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶם, בֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶם אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּבוֹרֵר לָהֶם חֵלֶק יָפֶה. הִגְדִּילוּ — יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: הִגְדִּילוּ — אֵין יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת, דְּאִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to minor **orphans who came to** court to **divide up their father's property,** the **court appoints for** each of **them a steward [*apotropos*], and he selects for them a fine share.** When the orphans **have grown up, they may object** to the manner in which the property was divided and redistribute it. **And Rav Naḥman himself said** that when **they have grown up they may not object, as, if** they may object, **what good is the power of the court?** Here too, an appointed steward selects a share of the inheritance on behalf of the fetus, and this share does not include any of the slaves. Therefore, the slaves may partake of *teruma*. However, if all of the children are females, this arrangement is impossible because if the fetus is a son all the property belongs to him.

The *gemara* then asks why, when there are only daughters and the wife is pregnant, the slaves cannot eat. The answer, *chada ve-od*, is twofold: first, if the fetus is female, she too will share in the estate with the other daughters; second, even if the fetus is female and girls generally share in the estate only by rabbinic enactment, there remains the possibility the fetus is male and takes the entire estate — *ve-ein la-banot bi-makom ha-ben klum*, the daughters have no share in the presence of a son.

The *gemara* then raises a second possible reading of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's ruling. Perhaps he does hold *choshishin le-mi'uta* — he is indeed concerned about a forty-eight percent probability — but holds that there is a practical solution available via the ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel. Rav Nachman amar Shmuel teaches that when orphans come to divide their father's estate and some are minors, *beit din* appoints an *apotropus* — a guardian or steward — to choose a good portion on behalf of the minor heir. The *apotropus* can then carve out specific property for the fetus: instead of the fetus holding an undivided fractional interest in every slave, he holds full ownership of two specific slaves (for example), and the remaining eight slaves are fully owned by the born brothers and may eat *terumah*.

A further nuance of Rav Nachman's ruling concerns what happens when the minors come of age: in the name of Shmuel, the ruling states that they may reverse the division if it did not serve their interests, since the *apotropus* only acted on their behalf. Rav Nachman himself, however, rules that once they reach adulthood they *cannot* reverse it — *mah koach beit din yafeh?* For otherwise, what power does *beit din* have?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. וְהָכָא בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the use of Rav Naḥman's ruling to explain Rabbi Shimon's opinion, the Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Rav Naḥman's** opinion **is** corresponding to one side of a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No;** it is possible **that everyone** in the dispute **accepts Rav Naḥman's** ruling, **and here they disagree** only **with regard to** whether **we are concerned about the minority,** as previously suggested, in a case where the arrangement was not made.

The *gemara* then steps back and rejects this entire approach, concluding *lo* — everyone can hold by Rav Nachman's principle, the *apotropus* idea is not the point at all, and the debate among the *tanna'im* is simply whether one is concerned about the *mi'ut* that a live boy will be born.

### Small Estate Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים עָסְקִינַן. וּכְגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא בֵּן בַּהֲדֵי בַּת, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי הַאי דִּמְעַבְּרָא בֵּן הוּא — לָא עֲדִיף מֵהַאי דְּקָאֵי. אִי בַּת הִיא, אַמַּאי אָכְלָה — בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן, כַּמָּה דְּלָא נְפַק לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם לָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Here we are dealing with** a case of an inheritance of **insufficient property** that is enough only to sustain the daughters until they come of age. With regard to this case, the Sages instituted that the daughters receive their sustenance while the sons get nothing. This is **also** a case **where there is** a surviving **son together with** the **daughter. Therefore, no matter what,** the slaves do not partake of *teruma*. **If this** fetus, **with which she is pregnant, is a son, it is no better than this** son **who** already **exists.** Just as the existing son does not inherit the insufficient property, the same applies to the male fetus. **If it is a daughter,** it does not yet receive a share of the inheritance. This can be explained: **Why does** the daughter **partake** of the inheritance? It is **by** virtue of **a rabbinic ordinance.** Therefore, **as long as** the fetus **has not emerged into the atmosphere of the world, the Sages did not establish** that it should receive the inheritance. Consequently, the slaves partake of *teruma* by virtue of the existing daughter, as only she inherits them.

The *gemara* turns to the cryptic ruling of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose: *ha-bat ma'achelet, ha-ben eino ma'achil*. Abaye explains that the entire case concerns *nichsei mu'atim* — a small estate. When the estate is small, a well-known *halacha* applies: the daughters' right to be supported from the estate until marriage takes full precedence over the sons' inheritance. Even if meeting the daughters' maintenance costs consumes the entire estate and leaves the sons nothing, the daughters' rights come first.

The case, therefore, involves the presence of both a born son and a born daughter. *Mima nafshach* — either way: if the pregnant woman's fetus is a boy, he is no better positioned than the born boy who is already present. In a small estate, the daughter already owns the whole of the estate for purposes of support, so the fetus acquires nothing from it even if male. If the fetus is a girl, why do girls eat in this case at all? Because it is by virtue of a *takana d'rabbanan*, a rabbinic enactment — and the rabbis enacted that right only for a born girl, not for an unborn fetus. So when there is a daughter present, the slaves may eat — the daughter holds the estate by rabbinic enactment, and the fetus, whether male or female, does not disturb her hold on it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּאּ — בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא הָעוּבָּר זָכַר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם בֵּן כְּלוּם. אַדְּרַבָּא, נְכָסִים מוּעָטִים דְּבָנוֹת נִינְהוּ! סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לִנְכָסִים מְרוּבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** manner **did you establish** the *baraita*? You established it as referring **to insufficient property.** However, **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **Lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** Yet according to Abaye's explanation, **on the contrary,** the **insufficient property is the daughters',** whether or not there are any sons. The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause we have come to** a different case, in which there is **sufficient property.**

The *gemara* then tests this reading: the end of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's statement spoke of boys getting everything if the fetus is male, which presupposes a large estate where sons inherit everything and daughters are left with nothing. The *gemara* concedes that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's statement applies to a large estate, while Rabbi Yishmael's teaching concerns the special small-estate case.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים דְּבָנוֹת נִינְהוּ? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁקָּדְמוּ וּמָכְרוּ בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים — מַה שֶּׁמָּכְרוּ מָכְרוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another objection to Abaye's explanation: Does an inheritance of **insufficient property** belong **to the daughters? Didn't Rabbi Asi say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If the male **orphans proceeded to sell** the **insufficient property,** although by rabbinic ordinance it is designated for the daughters' sustenance, **what they sold was sold.** Apparently, the Sages did not expropriate the properties from the male inheritors, but merely designated them for the daughters' sustenance. How, then, can the sons' ownership be disregarded with regard to the slaves' partaking of *teruma*?

But a further challenge arises: if the estate is a *nichsei mu'atim*, can one truly say it belongs to the daughters? Rabbi Assi amar Rabbi Yochanan teaches that if orphans in a small estate go ahead and sell property, the sale is legally binding — which demonstrates that ultimate *title* remains with the sons, even if the daughters have a right to be fed. The daughters hold a right akin to a debt against the estate, not outright ownership.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַאי ״בַּת״ דְּקָתָנֵי — אֵם. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי? כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָתָנֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is** the meaning of the word **daughter** in the context of the ruling **that is taught** by Rabbi Yishmael? It means female and is referring to the **mother** of the fetus. She enables her slaves of usufruct property to partake of *teruma*, as her husband's heirs have no share in them, whereas the son does not enable the slaves of guaranteed investment to partake, due to the fetus's share. The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as **Rabbi Yosei's** statement in the first clause. What was added by Rabbi Yishmael? The Gemara answers: Indeed, the **entire** *baraita* **is taught by Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.** There are not conflicting versions of Rabbi Yosei's opinion. Rather, Rabbi Yishmael is clarifying that he is the author of that *baraita*.

The *gemara* therefore abandons the reading that *bat* and *ben* refer to literal daughter and son of the deceased, and proposes instead that the text uses *bat* to mean the mother — the wife — and *ben* to mean the father — the deceased husband. *Ha-bat ma'achelet* means: the mother's own property (*nichsei melog*) feeds her slaves; *ha-ben eino ma'achil* means: the father's property (*nichsei tzon barzel*) does not feed those slaves, because of the fetus's partial share. The *gemara* concludes: *hainu Rabbi Yose* — this is simply what Rabbi Yose already taught, and Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose is merely restating his father's ruling with different terminology, which is admittedly an unusual reading of the *braita*.

## Gray Zone Status

### Gray Zone Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוּבָּר, וְהַיָּבָם, וְהָאֵירוּסִין, וְהַחֵרֵשׁ, וּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — פּוֹסְלִין, וְלֹא מַאֲכִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the fetus** of a divorcée or a widow whose husband left her pregnant; **and a man whose married brother died childless [*yavam*]; and betrothal; and** a married **deaf-mute; and a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who engaged in intercourse with a woman; if any of these men are Israelites and the woman is the daughter of a priest, they **disqualify** her from partaking of *teruma*. **But** if she is an Israelite and they are priests, they **do not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*.

The discussion shifts to a broader framework: a list of cases in which a woman finds herself in a legal gray zone with respect to *terumah* consumption. The governing principle is as follows. When a *bat kohen* marries a *yisrael* and he dies, the presence or absence of children determines whether she remains connected to the *yisrael*'s household or reverts to her father's. If she has children, she remains connected to her dead husband's household and may not return to eating *terumah*. If she has no children, she reverts to her father's house — conceptually, not physically — and resumes eating *terumah*. The mirror image applies to a *bat yisrael* married to a *kohen*: with children she remains in his household and eats *terumah*; without children she reverts to her father's household and does not eat.

The *uber* — fetus — creates precisely the gray zone the *gemara* now catalogs. The fetus is enough of a connection to the dead husband that it prevents the woman from being considered fully reverted to her father's house, thereby *poseling* a *bat kohen* from eating *terumah*. But the fetus is not enough of a connection to the husband's house to entitle a *bat yisrael* married to a *kohen* to eat *terumah* in the first place. The fetus thus occupies both the *posel* function and the non-*ma'achil* function simultaneously.

The *mishna* presents a list of relationships that share this dual gray-zone character: the *uber*, the *yavam*, *erusin*, a *cheresh*, and a *ben tesha shanim ve-yom echad*. In all these cases: *poslin ve-lo ma'achilin* — they invalidate but they do not feed. And even a *safek* of such a relationship, whether it is uncertain if the man is nine years old and a day, or uncertain whether he has reached halachic maturity through two pubic hairs, is sufficient to invalidate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָפֵק שֶׁהוּא בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד סָפֵק שֶׁאֵינוֹ, סָפֵק הֵבִיא שְׁתֵּי שְׂעָרוֹת וְסָפֵק שֶׁלֹּא הֵבִיא,
>
> **Translation:** Likewise, in the case of a boy with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** as to **whether he is nine years and one day old** and **uncertainty** whether **he is not,** who engaged in intercourse with a woman; and in the case of a boy who betrothed a woman, with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** as to **whether he has grown two** pubic **hairs** and is considered an adult **and uncertainty whether he has not grown,** they too can disqualify the woman from partaking of *teruma* and cannot enable her to partake, as in the previous cases.

The *mishna* also mentions, as an aside, a *nafal ha-bayit* case: a house collapsed upon a man and his brother's daughter (his wife), with no known order of death, and he had a co-wife. Since the *tzarah*'s obligation of *yibbum* depends on the order of death — if the niece-wife died first, the co-wife requires *yibbum*; if the husband died first, the co-wife is exempt because a niece cannot create the *yibbum* bond — the *halacha* is that the *tzarah* performs *chalitzah* and not *yibbum*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפַל הַבַּיִת עָלָיו וְעַל בַּת אָחִיו, וְאֵין יָדוּעַ אֵי זֶה מֵת רִאשׁוֹן — צָרָתָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** If **the house fell upon** a man **and upon his brother's daughter,** to whom he was married, **and it is unknown which** of them **died first, her rival wife performs *ḥalitza* and does not enter into levirate marriage.** Entering into levirate marriage is not possible, as, if the wife died after her husband, the surviving wife would be rendered the rival wife of a forbidden relative, since the *yavam* is the father of the wife who died. This status prevents the creation of a levirate bond between him and the surviving wife as well. On the other hand, *ḥalitza* is necessary in case the wife died before her husband, thereby allowing the creation of a levirate bond between her rival wife and her father, the *yavam*.

### Fetus Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָעוּבָּר. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ. ״כִּנְעוּרֶיהָ״ — פְּרָט לִמְעוּבֶּרֶת. אִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לֹא מַאֲכִיל לָהּ, יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is taught in the mishna that **the fetus** disqualifies its mother from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to do so. The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** married **to an Israelite,** and her husband died and left her pregnant, the fetus **disqualifies her** from partaking of *teruma*, as it is stated: "But if a priest's daughter be a widow, or divorced, and have no child, and is returned to her father's house, as in her youth, she may eat of her father's bread" (Leviticus 22:13). The phrase **"as in her youth" excludes a pregnant woman,** whose body has changed from her youth. **If** she **is an Israelite woman** married **to a priest,** the fetus **does not enable her to partake,** as **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake.**

The *gemara* unpacks the *uber* case. If the woman is a *bat kohen* married to a *yisrael*, the fetus *paslah* — invalidates her from returning to *terumah*. The derivation: the verse speaks of her returning to her father's house *ke-ne'ureha* — as in her youth — implying a full reversion; a pregnant woman has not fully reverted. If she is a *bat yisrael* married to a *kohen*, the fetus does not entitle her to eat, because *yalud ma'achil she-ein yalud ma'achil* — only a born child confers that right; an unborn fetus does not.

### Yavam Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיָּבָם. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ. ״וְשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ״ — פְּרָט לְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם. אִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לָא מַאֲכֵיל לָהּ, ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא קִנְיָן דְּאָחִיו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna that the same principle applies to **a *yavam*.** The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** who has a levirate bond **to an Israelite, he disqualifies her,** as it is stated in the verse cited above: **"And is returned to her father's house,"** which **excludes a widow waiting for her *yavam*,** who has not returned to her father's house, as a levirate bond was created with her *yavam*. **If she is an Israelite woman** with a levirate bond **to a priest, he does not enable her to partake** of *teruma*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"The purchase of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** woman **is his brother's acquisition.** The bond with her *yavam* ensued from his late brother's marriage to her, not through any action of his own.

For the *yavam*, the *gemara* reasons as follows. If she is a *bat kohen* and the *yavam* is a *yisrael*, he *paslah*, because the verse says *ve-shavah el beit aviha* — she can return to her father's house — which implicitly excludes a *shomeret yavam*, who remains bound by the *zikah* bond and cannot return until *chalitzah* is performed. If she is a *bat yisrael* and the *yavam* is a *kohen*, she does not eat, because *kinyan kaspo amar rachamana* — the Torah requires that she be his acquisition through money (the paradigm of *kiddushin*), and this woman was acquired by his *brother*, not by him. Once *yibbum* is consummated the relationship is complete, but at the stage of *shomeret yavam* it is not sufficient.

### Erusin Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֵירוּסִין. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the same principle **also** applies to **betrothal.** The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** betrothed **to an Israelite, he disqualifies her,**

For *erusin*, the case involves actual *kiddushin* without *nisuin*. If she is a *bat kohen* and the *arus* is a *yisrael*, he invalidates her: once there is some *kinyan* to another man, the verse *u-vat kohen ki tihyeh l'ish zar* applies and she is no longer considered fully within her father's house — or, more precisely, even if she remains physically in her father's house, her connection to another man is sufficient to sever her *terumah* entitlement based on her father. If she is a *bat yisrael* and the *arus* is a *kohen*, she does not eat — and here the *gemara* invokes the ruling of Ulla. Biblically, it would seem that *kiddushin* should suffice to allow her to eat *terumah*, since the verse speaks of *kinyan kaspo* — his monetary acquisition, encompassing both slaves and wife acquired through *kiddushin*. However, Ulla explains that the rabbis were concerned lest she share the *terumah* with her father and brothers while still residing in her father's house during the period between *erusin* and *nisuin*. Accordingly, the rabbis enacted that she does not eat until *nisuin*. The *gemara* notes that the Yerushalmi actually takes a stronger position: *terumah* entitlement is derived not from *kinyan kaspo* but from *beito hem yochlu belachmo* — the idea of his *bayit*, his household — which is only established at *nisuin*, making this a biblical, not merely rabbinic, exclusion during *erusin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא קַנְיַהּ בַּהֲוָיָה. וְאִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לֹא מַאֲכִיל לָהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּעוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **as he acquired her by means of betrothal. And if she is an Israelite woman** betrothed **to a priest, he does not enable her to partake** of *teruma* **due to** the reason given by **Ulla:** Although by Torah law a priest's betrothed partakes of *teruma*, the Sages rendered it prohibited for her to do so, lest she allow other members of her family to eat it.

### Cheresh Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחֵרֵשׁ. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לַהּ, דְּהָא קַנְיַהּ בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן. וְאִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לָא מַאֲכֵיל, ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לָאו בַּר קִנְיָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **also** taught in the mishna that **a deaf-mute** disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to do so. The Gemara elaborates: **If she is the daughter of a priest** married to a deaf-mute **Israelite, he disqualifies her, as he acquired her through** marriage sanctioned by **an ordinance of the Sages.** Although the marriage of a deaf-mute is invalid by Torah law, the Sages instituted an ordinance validating this type of marriage. **And if she is an Israelite woman** married **to** a deaf-mute **priest, he does not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"The purchase of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** deaf-mute **is not capable of acquisition** by Torah law, as he is not legally competent.

For the *cheresh* — a deaf-mute — the *gemara* notes that while a *cheresh* is not considered legally competent in the biblical sense and thus cannot effect a biblical *kiddushin*, the rabbis nonetheless instituted a form of rabbinic marriage for a *cheresh*, recognizing that he functions at a higher level than, for example, a *shoteh*. If she is a *bat kohen* and the *cheresh* is a *yisrael*, this rabbinic institution is sufficient to remove her from her father's house and *posel* her. If she is a *bat yisrael* and the *cheresh* is a *kohen*, she does not eat, because *kinyan kaspo amar rachamana*, the Torah requires a real biblical acquisition, and a *cheresh lav bar kinyan hu* — a deaf-mute is not capable of effecting a biblical acquisition. So the rabbinic marriage invalidates but does not feed.

### Nine Years Old Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְכוּ׳. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין, בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְבֶן תֵּשַׁע וְיוֹם אֶחָד. לְמַאי? אִי לְמִיפְסַל — קָטָן נָמֵי מִיפְסָל פָּסֵיל, אִי לְאוֹכוֹלֵי — גָּדוֹל נָמֵי לֹא מַאֲכִיל!
>
> **Translation:** § It is **also** taught in the mishna that **a nine-year-old** boy disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to partake. **It enters our mind** that the mishna is referring **to a widow waiting for her *yavam*,** who is **nine** years **and one day old.** The Gemara therefore inquires: With regard **to what** is this taught? **If** it is with regard **to disqualifying** her from partaking of *teruma*, **a younger** *yavam* **also disqualifies** her, as a levirate bond was created and she cannot return to her father's house. And **if** it was with regard **to enabling** her **to partake** of *teruma*, **an older** *yavam* **does not enable** her **to partake either,** as discussed above.

The *ben tesha shanim ve-yom echad* case requires further clarification. One might initially think this refers to a *yavam* who is nine years old and a day performing *bi'ah* for *yibbum* purposes. The *gemara* considers whether the intent is to show that the *yavam*'s minority status alone — even without any act of *bi'ah* — invalidates. But the *gemara* notes that even an infant *yavam* invalidates her as a *shomeret yavam*; and conversely, even an adult *yavam* does not *feed* her until *yibbum* is consummated. Abaye therefore proposes that the case involves a nine-year-old *yavam* who has actually had *bi'ah* with the *yevamah*. Since biblically this act of *bi'ah* is a valid act of *yibbum*, one might think she should be entitled to eat *terumah* if he is a *kohen*. The *chiddush* is that the rabbis treated the *bi'ah* of a nine-year-old not as a complete *yibbum* but as equivalent to *ma'amar* — a stronger but still incomplete form of the *yibbum* bond — and thus she remains in the gray zone and does not eat.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּיָבָם בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד הַבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ עָסְקִינַן. דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קַנְיָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קַנְיָא לֵיהּ וּבִיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה, אֵימָא לוֹכֵיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד כְּמַאֲמָר בְּגָדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Here we are dealing with a nine-year-and-one-day-old *yavam* who** already **engaged in intercourse with his *yevama*, as she was** thereby **acquired by him by Torah** law. It might **enter your mind to say** that **since by Torah** law **she was acquired by him, as** the legal status of **his** act of **intercourse is** that of **intercourse,** perhaps **he enables** her **to partake** of *teruma*. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the Sages **rendered the** legal status of the **intercourse of a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy **like** that of **levirate betrothal** by means of money or a document performed **by an adult man,** which is not sufficient for her to partake of *teruma*. Since levirate betrothal is effective only by rabbinic law, the *yevama* is not considered the acquisition of his money by Torah law and may not partake of *teruma*.

Rava objects to Abaye's reading: the *mishna* states that even a *safek* of nine years old invalidates. If a definite nine-year-old having consummated does not *feed* her, of course a doubtful nine-year-old does not feed her — there is no *chiddush* in the *safek* formulation. Rava therefore reinterprets: the *ben tesha shanim ve-yom echad* in this *mishna* is not about *yibbum* at all. It belongs to a different category — the category of forbidden sexual relations. Starting at age nine years and a day, an act of *bi'ah* with one of the persons listed in the following *braita* is considered a legally significant act of sex that invalidates the woman. That is the *chiddush*, and the *safek* formulation then makes perfect sense: even if it is uncertain whether he was nine years old and a day, the woman is still invalidated by the act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: סָפֵק בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד סָפֵק שֶׁאֵינוֹ — הַשְׁתָּא וַדַּאי בֶּן תֵּשַׁע לֹא מַאֲכִיל, סָפֵק מִיבַּעְיָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so,** consider **the latter clause** of the mishna, **which teaches** that a boy with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** whether **he is nine years and one day old** and **uncertainty** whether **he is not,** disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma*, and he does not enable her to partake. **Now** that, according to your explanation, one who is **definitely nine** years **old does not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*, **is** it **necessary** to teach the same concerning a boy with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** as to whether or not he reached that age?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בְּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד דְּהָנָךְ פְּסוּלִים קָתָנֵי, דְּפָסְלִי בְּבִיאָתָן. וְכִדְתַנְיָא: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, גֵּר, עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי, מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, כּוּתִי, נָתִין, חָלָל וּמַמְזֵר שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל כֹּהֶנֶת לְוִיָּה וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית — פְּסָלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that the mishna **is teaching** this *halakha* **with regard to a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is one **of those unfit** males listed in a *baraita*, **who disqualify** a woman from marrying a priest **by their intercourse,** as they are unfit to enter the assembly of Israel through marriage, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is **an Ammonite or a Moabite convert;** or who is **an Egyptian or an Edomite** convert; or who is either **a Samaritan [*kuti*], a Gibeonite, a *ḥalal*, or a *mamzer*,** when he **engaged in intercourse with a priestess,** i.e., the daughter of a priest, **a Levite, or an Israelite,** he thereby **disqualified her** from marrying a priest, and, in the case of the daughter of a priest, from partaking of *teruma*.

## Forbidden Relations

### Braita Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אִם אֵינָן רְאוּיִן לָבֹא בְּיִשְׂרָאֵל — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ פּוֹסְלִים, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא לָאו בִּפְסוּלִים עָסְקִינַן! רֵישָׁא פְּסוּלֵי קָהָל, סֵיפָא פְּסוּלֵי כְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty **from** the fact **that the latter clause,** the next mishna (69a), **teaches** that **if** men who **are unfit to enter** the assembly of **Israel** by marriage engage in extramarital intercourse with women, **they disqualify** them from marrying into the priesthood: **It may be inferred that** in **the first clause,** the mishna above, **we are dealing not with unfit** individuals but with men fit to marry Jews of unflawed lineage. The Gemara answers: That inference is incorrect. **The first clause** of the mishna is dealing with those **unfit to** enter **the assembly** of Israel by marriage, while **the latter clause** is dealing with those who are merely **unfit for** the **priesthood.** That is why the mishna is referring to them separately. Accordingly, Rava's explanation that the mishna is referring to an unfit nine-year-old boy is viable.

The *gemara* notes a structural distinction in the *mishna*: the first section of the *mishna* addressed the *pesulei kahal* — those forbidden from entering the congregation of Israel altogether, not merely from *kehuna* — including Ammonites, Moabites, *mamzerim*, and the like. The subsequent *mishna* will address *pesulei kehuna* — those specifically forbidden to *kohanim*, such as a divorcée or widow married to a *kohen*. Though different in kind, both categories of forbidden relations invalidate the woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, גֵּר, עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי, מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, כּוּתִי, נָתִין, חָלָל וּמַמְזֵר שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל כֹּהֶנֶת לְוִיָּה וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית — פְּסָלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara addresses the matter **itself** and cites the complete *baraita*. **A nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is **an Ammonite or a Moabite convert;** or who is **an Egyptian or an Edomite** convert; or who is either **a Samaritan, a Gibeonite, a *ḥalal*, or a *mamzer*,** when he **engaged in intercourse with a priestess,** or **a Levite, or an Israelite,** he thereby **disqualified her** from marrying into the priesthood.

### Rabbi Yose View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁזַּרְעוֹ פָּסוּל — פּוֹסֵל, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין זַרְעוֹ פָּסוּל — אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ — אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ — אִי אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** Of the individuals mentioned above, **anyone whose offspring is unfit** to enter the assembly of Israel, **disqualifies** a woman with whom he engaged in intercourse from marrying into the priesthood. However, **anyone whose offspring is not unfit does not disqualify** a woman through intercourse. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Anyone whose daughter you may marry, you may marry his widow,** even if you are a priest. Anyone whose daughter may marry a Jew of unflawed lineage does not disqualify a woman with whom he engaged in intercourse from marrying into the priesthood. **And anyone whose daughter you may not marry, you may not marry his widow** if you are a priest.

Rabbi Yose states: *kol she-zar'o pasul posel* — anyone whose offspring from this woman would be *pasul* (invalid) invalidates the woman through this act of sex. *Ve-kol she-ein zar'o pasul eino posel* — anyone whose offspring would not be *pasul* does not invalidate her. This formulation raises the question of what kind of relationship might be forbidden yet produce valid offspring — for instance, a man who is forbidden to marry into the congregation only for a limited number of generations, where the offspring of the union would fall on the permitted side of that generational threshold.

### Rabban Shimon View

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel offers a parallel formulation: *kol she-ata nosei bito ata nosei almanato* — anyone whose daughter you (the *kohen*) could marry, you could also marry his widow (the woman he had relations with). *Ve-kol she-ei ata nosei bito ei ata nosei almanato* — anyone whose daughter you could not marry, you cannot marry his widow either. This sounds closely parallel to Rabbi Yose's position, and the *gemara* will explore the relationship between the two formulations.

### Parashat Emor Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבַת כֹּהֵן כִּי תִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ זָר״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה לְפָסוּל לָהּ — פְּסָלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that intercourse with an unfit man renders a woman unfit to partake of *teruma* and marry a priest? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The verse states: "And if a priest's daughter be married to a common man [*ish zar*],** she shall not eat of that which is set apart from the sacred" (Leviticus 22:12). It may be derived that **since she engaged in intercourse with one who is unfit for her, he disqualified her** from marrying into the priesthood, as the literal meaning of the expression *ish zar* is a man who is excluded.

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — what is the biblical source for the principle that forbidden sexual relations invalidate a woman from eating *terumah* or from marrying a *kohen*? Rav Yehuda amar Rav derives it from the verse *u-vat kohen ki tihyeh l'ish zar* (Parashat Emor). While the *peshat* of the verse is that a *bat kohen* married to a non-*kohen* (*zar*) may not eat *terumah* of her father's house, Rav reads it as follows: *zar* means someone totally foreign, completely forbidden to her. Once she has been *niv'ala* — had relations — with such a man, *paslah* — she is permanently invalidated from eating *terumah*, not merely suspended during the marriage. This reading converts a temporary status rule into a permanent invalidation based on an act of forbidden sex.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ דְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא בַּת כֹּהֵן דְּמִינַּסְבָא לְזָר — לָא תֵּיכוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **That** verse **is necessary** to teach the *halakha* **that the Merciful One says: The daughter of a priest who marries a non-priest,** even one that she is permitted to marry, **may not partake** of *teruma*. Therefore, it cannot be the source for the *halakha* that intercourse with an unfit man renders a woman unfit to partake of *teruma* and marry a priest.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: we need this verse simply for its *peshat* — to teach that a *bat kohen* married to a non-*kohen* cannot eat *terumah* while married. The *gemara* replies that we could derive that rule from elsewhere: from the verse *ve-shavah el beit aviha* — when she returns to her father's house she may eat — we can infer by implication that while she is married she cannot eat. The *gemara* pushes back: that inference produces only a *lav ha-ba mi-klal aseh* — a negative prohibition inferred from a positive commandment, which carries only the weight of a positive *mitzvah*. We need the verse *ki tihyeh l'ish zar* to establish this as a full, black-letter negative prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא, מִ״וְּשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ כִּנְעוּרֶיהָ מִלֶּחֶם אָבִיהָ תֹּאכֵל״ נָפְקָא. מִדְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ ... תֹּאכֵל״, מִכְּלָל דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא אָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** prohibition **is derived from** the verse "But if a priest's daughter be a widow, or divorced, and have no child, **and is returned to her father's house, as in her youth, she may eat of her father's bread" (**Leviticus 22:13). **From the fact that the Merciful One says: "And is returned to her father's house…she may eat," it may be inferred that initially,** while married to a non-priest, **she** was **not** permitted to **eat.** Therefore, the prohibition against a woman who engaged in intercourse with an unfit man partaking of *teruma* may be derived from the former verse, as it is not necessary for this *halakha*.

The *gemara* counters that the negative prohibition can be derived from the verse *ve-chol zar lo yochal kodesh* — no non-*kohen* may eat *terumah*. By extension, a woman connected to a non-*kohen* may not eat. Then the *gemara* notes that *ve-chol zar lo yochal kodesh* appears twice in the Torah. One instance teaches that a non-*kohen* cannot eat *terumah*; the other, appearing in the section of a *bat kohen*, teaches that a woman married to a non-*kohen* cannot eat. And the word *ve-chol* in that context comes specifically to teach, as Rabbi Yose birabbi Chanina states: *zarut amarti lach ve-lo aninut* — only the disqualification of being a *zar* prevents eating *terumah*; being an *onen* (mourner on the day of burial) does not. An *onen* is forbidden to eat *korbanot* but not *terumah*.

The *gemara* then layers in yet another *halacha* that requires a verse: *Rami bar Avin amar Rav Ashi* teaches from the phrase *u-vat kohen ki tihyeh l'ish zar hi bi-trumat ha-kodashim lo tochel* — the specific phrase *trumat ha-kodashim* teaches that she cannot eat *be-muram min ha-kodashim*, the portions lifted from *korbanot* (i.e., the *chazeh ve-shok*, the breast and thigh of peace-offerings given to *kohanim*). Once a *bat kohen* has been married to a non-*kohen*, even after that husband's death and her return to her father's house, she can resume eating *terumah* but she does not resume eating the portions of *kodashim*. The *gemara* then asks: if the verse only wanted to prohibit *kodashim*, it should have simply said *lo tochel ba-kodashim* — why does it also mention *terumah*? The answer is *shma mina tarti* — the verse is doing double duty, teaching both rules. The full accounting of available verses thus yields: one *ve-chol zar* for the prohibition on a non-*kohen* eating *terumah*, one *ve-chol zar* (with *ve-chol*) for the prohibition on a *bat kohen* married to a non-*kohen*, the *ve-chol* particle specifically excluding *aninut*, and the phrase *ki tihyeh l'ish zar* for the permanent invalidation through forbidden sex.

### Non-Kohen Women

The analysis has established that forbidden sexual relations invalidate a *bat kohen* from eating *terumah*. But how do we know the rule extends to a *Leviyah* and a *Yisraelit* — for them, the relevant consequence is not *terumah* (which they do not eat in their own right) but prohibition from marrying a *kohen*? The *gemara* answers by invoking the ruling of Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav: *bat u-vat* — the extra *vav* in *u-vat kohen* is textually superfluous, since the verse could simply have stated *ki tihyeh l'ish zar* without specifying *bat kohen* at all. The additional *u-vat kohen* comes to include all women — *Leviyot* and *Yisraeliyot* as well. And once the rule applies to a *Leviyah* and *Yisraelit*, who have no connection to *terumah* in the first place, the only operative consequence must be the prohibition from marrying a *kohen*.

The *gemara* asks whether this derivation follows only the view of Rabbi Akiva, who characteristically derives law from extra *vavim*. It answers that even the *rabbanan* concede the point here, because the entire phrase *u-vat kohen* is textually excess — not just a single letter. The verse could have been written simply as *ki tihyeh l'ish zar*; the addition of *u-vat kohen* is therefore available to teach the extension to all women.

### Leviyah Status

Now that the rule has been extended to all women, the *gemara* asks: we have established the rule for *terumah* — but how do we know that forbidden sex prohibits a *Leviyah* or *Yisraelit* from marrying a *kohen*? At first glance, once we extend the verse to a *Leviyah* and *Yisraelit*, who do not eat *terumah*, the only possible implication is marriage — so it is self-evident. But the *gemara* entertains a counterargument: one could construct a *terumah* case for a *Yisraelit*. If she had previously been married to a *kohen* and bore him a son, she would be eating *terumah* by virtue of that son. Now she has forbidden sex with one of the listed men — she is invalidated and may no longer eat. Perhaps, then, even the extension to *Yisraelit* is still only about *terumah*, not about marriage to a *kohen*.

The *gemara* dismisses this with a *kal va-chomer*: if a *bat kohen*, whose personal status entitles her to eat *terumah*, is invalidated by forbidden sex — then a *Yisraelit*, who eats only on account of her son's status and not her own, would surely be invalidated as well. The *gemara* pushes back: *mah la-kohenet*, one can distinguish — a *bat kohen* has inherent *kedusha*, and her personal status is what is impaired by the forbidden act. A *Yisraelit* has no such inherent *kedusha*; her eating *terumah* is entirely derivative of her child, and perhaps her own status is irrelevant to the act. The *kal va-chomer* therefore fails.

The *gemara* leaves us at the threshold: we have now established, through the extension via *u-vat*, that forbidden sexual relations invalidate all women — *bat kohen*, *Leviyah*, and *Yisraelit* alike — and that for *Leviyot* and *Yisraeliyot* the operative consequence must be prohibition from marrying a *kohen*. The further analysis of exactly how that prohibition is derived for purposes of marriage, as distinct from *terumah*, will be taken up in the following session.
