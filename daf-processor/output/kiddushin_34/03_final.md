# Kiddushin 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kibud HaRav

### Standing Req't

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי רַבּוֹ אֶלָּא שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יִהְיֶה כְּבוֹדוֹ מְרוּבֶּה מִכְּבוֹד שָׁמַיִם. מֵיתִיבִי, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְזָקֵן שֶׁלֹּא יַטְרִיחַ – תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זָקֵן וְיָרֵאתָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **A Torah scholar is permitted to stand before his teacher only** once **in the morning and** once in the **evening, so that** the teacher's **honor should not be greater than the honor of Heaven,** as one recites the *Shema*, which is tantamount to greeting God, once in the morning and once in the evening. The Gemara **raises an objection** from an aforementioned opinion. **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says: From where** is it derived that **an elder should not trouble** others to honor him? **The verse states: "An elder, and you shall fear"** (Leviticus 19:32). The collocation of these words comes to teach that the elder, too, must fear God.

The sugya opens on 33b with a ruling attributed to Rav Aivo in the name of Rav Yannai: *ain talmid chacham rashai la'amod mipnei rabbo ella shacharis v'arvis* — a *talmid chacham* is not required, or as Tosafot clarifies, not permitted, to stand before his *rebbe* more than twice a day, morning and evening. The rationale is *k'dai shelo yihei kvodo merubeh mi'kvod shamayim* — so that the honor shown to the *rebbe* should not exceed the honor shown to heaven, which is likewise marked by fixed, discrete moments of recognition rather than a continuous posture of deference.

A *meitivei* challenges this: Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar teaches that a sage should not burden his students unnecessarily, as derived from *zaken v'yareita* — the elder too must be mindful of God and not overreach the honor due him. The question pressed against our ruling is: if students are only obligated morning and evening, then in any given moment the *rebbe* could know whether the obligation has already been discharged. There would be no burden at all! The force of the challenge is that it implies students must actually stand any time they see their *rebbe* throughout the day — *kulei yoma* — and it is in that context that the *rebbe* is told not to force the issue.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית בִּלְבַד, אַמַּאי לָא נִיטְרַח? חִיּוּבָא הוּא! אֶלָּא לָאו כּוּלֵּי יוֹמָא! לָא, לְעוֹלָם שַׁחֲרִית וְעַרְבִית בִּלְבַד, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי כַּמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לֵיהּ – לָא נִיטְרַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **And if you say** one may stand **only** in the **morning and evening, why** does the *baraita* say an elder **should not trouble** others? Standing for an elder only twice a day **is an obligation** for the people, not an imposition. **Rather,** is it **not** correct to say **that** one is obligated to stand before one's teacher at **any** point **during the day?** The Gemara answers: **No; actually** one is obligated to stand **only** in **the morning and evening, and even so, as much as it is possible for** the elder, **he should not trouble** the people to stand.

The *gemara* rejects the challenge: the rule remains morning and evening only, and even for those two moments, the *rebbe* should try to avoid placing even that minimal burden on his students if it is at all possible.

### Tosafot's Limits

Tosafot raises a pointed difficulty. Earlier in the *gemara*, Rava became visibly upset when he passed students on the other side of a river and they failed to stand for him. But if the obligation is only morning and evening, perhaps they had already stood for him in the morning, and perhaps he simply was not aware of it — why was he so aggrieved?

Tosafot resolves this by drawing a critical distinction: the rule limiting standing to morning and evening applies exclusively to students who are *hadarin b'veis harav* — those living in the *rebbe*'s household or, by analogy, students in a *yeshiva* setting who are in continuous proximity to their teacher throughout the day. In such a setting, everyone can be presumed to have stood in the morning, and there is therefore no concern of disrespect. But students who encounter their *rebbe* only intermittently are obligated to stand even a hundred times a day, *afilu meiah p'amim bayom*, because the concern of *chashad* — suspicion in the eyes of onlookers — is very real. A bystander who sees a *rebbe* enter a room and no one rise will draw adverse conclusions about the students' conduct.

Tosafot adds a further nuance: even among resident *yeshiva* students, if visitors arrive, everyone must stand — because the entire exemption rests on communal awareness that the obligation has been fulfilled earlier. When outsiders are present, that shared awareness no longer obtains, and the concern for *chashad* is reactivated.

This notion of *chashad* is deeply bound up with *kavod* itself. Honor, after all, operates in a public register — it is not merely a private sentiment between two people but a social reality, and the *gemara* elsewhere acknowledges that the parameters of *kavod* shift depending on social context.

### R. Elazar Warning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁאֵין עוֹמֵד מִפְּנֵי רַבּוֹ נִקְרָא רָשָׁע, וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים, וְתַלְמוּדוֹ מִשְׁתַּכֵּחַ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְטוֹב לֹא יִהְיֶה לָרָשָׁע וְלֹא יַאֲרִיךְ יָמִים כַּצֵּל אֲשֶׁר אֵינֶנּוּ יָרֵא מִלִּפְנֵי הָאֱלֹהִים״, מוֹרָא זוֹ אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַהוּ – כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״ – הֲרֵי מוֹרָא זוֹ קִימָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said: Any Torah scholar who does not stand before his teacher is called wicked, and he will not live a long life, and his studies will be forgotten, as it is stated: "But it shall not be well for the wicked, neither shall he prolong his days, which are as a shadow, because he does not fear before [*millifnei*] God"** (Ecclesiastes 8:13). **This fear** mentioned in the verse, **I do not know what it is. When** the verse **states:** "And you shall revere the face [*penei*] of an elder, **and you shall fear your God"** (Leviticus 19:32), one can deduce **that this fear** mentioned in the verse **is** referring to **standing.** Consequently, this verse teaches with regard to one who does not stand that he is called wicked, he will not live a long life, and his studies will be forgotten, as indicated by the phrase: "It shall not be well."

Rabbi Elazar sharpens the stakes considerably: *kol talmid chacham she'eino omed mipnei rabbo nikra rasha* — any *talmid chacham* who fails to stand before his *rebbe* is called wicked. Moreover, *eino maarich yamim* — he will not have long days — and *talmudo mishtakeiach* — his Torah will be forgotten. The proof-text cited is *v'tov lo yihiyeh lara'sha* — there will be no good for the wicked, where "good" is understood as Torah, the highest good for a *talmid chacham* — *v'lo yaarich yamim k'tzel asher einenno yarei milifnei Elokim* — and he will not lengthen his days like a shadow, because he has no fear before God.

### Yirah Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִימָא מוֹרָאַת רִבִּית וּמוֹרָאַת מִשְׁקָלוֹת! רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: ״פְּנֵי״ ״פְּנֵי״ גָּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **say** that this is referring to **fear of** God stated with regard to **interest** (Leviticus 25:36), **or** the **fear of** God stated with regard to **weights** (Deuteronomy 25:13–16), as the fear of God is mentioned with regard to these prohibitions as well. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Elazar derives** this *halakha* through a verbal analogy of **"*penei*" and "*penei*,"** as explained previously, not from a verbal analogy of the term "fear."

This raises the question: what precisely is this *mora*, this fear? The *gemara* answers by citing the continuation of the *pasuk* in *vahadata pnei zaken* — *v'yareita mei'Elokecha* — and you shall fear your God. The *gemara* identifies this reverence as referring to the act of standing up. As Tosafot notes, the *pasuk* of *v'hadarta pnei zaken* concludes with *v'yareita mei'Elokecha*, thus linking the act of standing before an elder directly to the fear of God.

The *gemara* immediately asks: but this phrase *v'yareita mei'Elokecha* appears in other contexts as well — by *ribit*, where one is forbidden to take interest, and by the laws of weights and measures. How do we know it refers specifically to standing here? Rabbi Elazar resolves this through a *gezeira shava* of *pnei-pnei* — the word *pnei* appears both in *milifnei ha'Elokim* and in *v'hadarta pnei zaken*, linking the fear clause specifically to the commandment of honoring elders. Tosafot adds that by the weights and measures passage, *Amalek* is juxtaposed in Devarim — and *Amalek* is characterized as *v'lo yarei Elokim* — further anchoring the fear-of-God language to the context of *kibbud* rather than commercial law. Tosafot also notes a textual variant reading *michshalot* rather than *mishkalot*, which would yield a different derivation: *lo titein michshal v'yareita mei'Elokecha*, the prohibition against placing a stumbling block, as an alternative pairing.

Underlying all of this is the broader principle that *moras rabcha k'mora shamayim* — the awe of one's teacher mirrors the awe of heaven — and this is not merely a function of the textual juxtaposition of *v'yareita mei'Elokecha*, but reflects something deep about the nature of honor shown to Torah and to those who represent a kind of *kedushah* in the world.

### Talmid Chacham Q.

A natural question arises: why does the warning specify a *talmid chacham* who fails to stand before his *rebbe*? If the prohibition is serious, it should apply to anyone who neglects this duty. Several answers suggest themselves. One possibility is that a *talmid chacham* might harbor a subtle *yetzer hara* against standing — reasoning that he himself has attained a level of scholarship that makes deference to another seem unnecessary. The prohibition targets precisely this form of pride. Another possibility is that the violation is uniquely severe when committed by a *talmid chacham* because it represents a *pegiya b'kavod ha-Torah* — a blow to the dignity of Torah — when someone who genuinely understands what Torah represents refuses to honor it. 

There is yet a third reading, perhaps the most compelling: the statement may be emphasizing specifically the *rebbe-talmid* relationship, not merely the general obligation to stand before any sage. The failure to stand is qualitatively different when it occurs within a formal relationship of transmission, where the bond between teacher and student carries an obligation that transcends general communal deference. And as a student notes, *v'talmudo mishtakeiach* — the forgetting of one's Torah — is by definition a consequence that can only befall a *talmid chacham*, which may itself be the reason the ruling is framed around that figure.

## Son-Father Conflict

### Precedence Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּנוֹ וְהוּא רַבּוֹ, מַהוּ לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי אָבִיו? תָּא שְׁמַע, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, קוּם מִקַּמֵּי אֲבוּךְ. שָׁאנֵי רַב יְחֶזְקֵאל דְּבַעַל מַעֲשִׂים הֲוָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ מָר שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי קָאֵים מִקַּמֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before them:** With regard to one who is both a man's **son and his teacher, what is** the *halakha* as to whether that son must **stand before his father?** The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: Big-toothed** one, **stand before your father.** Although Rav Yehuda was a great Torah scholar and taught his father, he was still required to stand before him. The Gemara answers: **Rav Yeḥezkel,** Rav Yehuda's father, **is different, as he was a man of** good **deeds,** and **even Mar Shmuel** himself **would stand before him.**

The *gemara* now turns to the tension between *kibbud rav* and *kibbud av va'em*. The background to this discussion is the well-known ruling that if a person must choose between returning a lost object to his *rebbe* or to his father, the *rebbe* takes precedence — because the father brings one into this world, but the *rebbe* brings one into the world-to-come. This priority of *kibbud rav* over *kibbud av* in cases of conflict seems to suggest that wherever the two obligations cannot both be fulfilled simultaneously, the *rebbe's* claim wins.

### Son Stand Q.

But the case now posed is far more complicated. The *ibaya* — the question raised — is: *beno vehu rabbo* — what happens when the son is the father's *rebbe*? This is not a simple matter of choosing between two competing obligations running in the same direction. It creates an inversion. If the father must stand before the son as his *rebbe*, the entire structure of *kibbud av va'em* — in which the child defers to the parent — is turned on its head. The question therefore branches in two directions: does the son still stand before the father even if he is the father's *rebbe*? And does the father stand before the son precisely because the son is his *rebbe*?

### Shmuel Case

To address the first question, the *gemara* cites a well-known exchange: Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda — *Shinana* — *kum mikamei avuch*, "stand before your father." Those who remember the earlier episode will recall that Rav Yehuda had corrected his father Rav Yechezkel who was teaching a *halacha* imprecisely, and Shmuel rebuked him for this breach of respect. Rav Yehuda's superior Torah knowledge was not in question — in that sense, he functioned as the *rebbe* in the exchange — and yet Shmuel insisted that he stand before his father and treat him with deference. This seems to prove that even when the son is the *rebbe*, the son retains his obligation of *kibbud av*.

The *gemara*, however, pushes back: *sha'ani Rav Yechezkel deba'al ma'asim hava*. The case was different because Rav Yechezkel was not an ordinary father — he was a man of extraordinary righteous deeds, a *ba'al ma'asim*, whose personal piety demanded a form of reverence that existed alongside and independently of his role as father. The *gemara* reinforces this by noting that even Shmuel himself would stand before Rav Yechezkel — not because Rav Yechezkel was Rav Yehuda's father, but because Rav Yechezkel himself was worthy of that honor in his own right.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ? הָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: זִימְנִין דְּאָתֵי מֵאֲחוֹרַי, קוּם אַתְּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ וְלָא תֵּיחוּשׁ לִיקָרָא דִידִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Rather, what is** Shmuel **saying** to Rav Yehuda? If he is not teaching him that one who is his father's teacher must stand before his father, why did Shmuel say this to Rav Yehuda? The Gemara answers that **this is what** Shmuel **said to him: Sometimes** your father **comes from behind me** and I do not see him or stand before him. Nevertheless, **you should stand before him and do not be concerned about my honor.**

This reinterpretation entirely changes the nature of Shmuel's instruction. Rather than a ruling about *kibbud av* in the context of a *rebbe-talmid* relationship, the *gemara* rereads it as Shmuel telling Rav Yehuda: sometimes your father will be coming behind me; even if you worry that standing for him in my presence would be disrespectful to my honor as your *rebbe*, *velo teichosh liyikra didi* — do not worry about that, stand for him anyway. The *chiddush* is about showing honor to a *tzaddik* even in the presence of one's *rebbe*, not about the *rebbe-talmid* inversion at all. The attempt to derive a ruling about the son standing for his father therefore fails.

### R. Y.B.L. Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּנוֹ וְהוּא רַבּוֹ, מַהוּ שֶׁיַּעֲמוֹד אָבִיו מִפָּנָיו? תָּא שְׁמַע דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: אֲנִי אֵינִי כְּדַי לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי בְנִי, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד בֵּית נָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** Another **dilemma was raised before them,** with regard to one who is both a man's **son and his teacher, what is** the *halakha* as **to** whether **the father** must **stand before** his son? The Gemara answers: **Come** and **hear, as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: It is not appropriate for me to stand before my son** solely due to his greatness in Torah, as I am greater than him. **But due to the honor of the household of the *Nasi*** I do stand before him, as his son was a son-in-law of the *Nasi*.

The *gemara* then takes up the inverse question: *mahu she'ya'amod aviv mipanav* — does the father stand before the son when the son is his *rebbe*? The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi: *ani eini kedai la'amod mifnei beni ella mishum kavod beit haNasi*. He declares that he himself would not stand before his son on account of the son's Torah standing — the son simply was not great enough in Torah — but he does stand before him because of the son's connection to the house of the *Nasi*, presumably through political or social ties rather than genealogical ones.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דַּאֲנָא רַבֵּיהּ, הָא אִיהוּ רַבַּאי – קָאֵימְנָא מִקַּמֵּיהּ! הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֲנִי אֵינִי כְּדַי לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי בְנִי וַאֲפִילּוּ הוּא רַבַּאי, דְּהָא אֲנָא אֲבוּהּ, אֶלָּא מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד בֵּית נָשִׂיא.
>
> **Translation:** It may be inferred from here that if his son were not in the household of the *Nasi* he would not stand for him, and **the reason** was **that** he could claim: **I am his teacher** and therefore I am not obligated to stand before him. Accordingly, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is indicating that if **he were my teacher I would stand before him.** The Gemara rejects this proof: **This is what** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **is saying: It is not appropriate for me to stand before my son, even** if **he** were **my teacher, as I am his father. But due to the honor of the household of the *Nasi*** I do stand before him.

The *gemara* draws out the implication: *ta'ima de'ana Rebbi* — the reason is that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi is the *rebbe* — *ha ihu rabbai, kamina mikamei* — but if the son were actually his *rebbe*, he would stand before him. This apparently proves that a father must stand before a son who is his *rebbe*.

The *gemara* again deflects: *hachi ka'amar* — what Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi actually means is: *ani eini kedai la'amod mifnei beni v'afilu hu rabbai de'ana avuha* — I should not have to stand before my son even if he were my *rebbe*, because I am his father, and a father should never be placed in a posture of subordination to a son. The only reason he stands is the honor due to the house of the *Nasi*. The attempt to prove that the father would stand for the son if the son were the *rebbe* is thus also successfully deflected — leaving the *ibaya* entirely unresolved.

### Rambam Rule

With both proofs bracketed, the *gemara* offers no definitive resolution. The Rosh and, following him, the Shulchan Aruch rule that both father and son are obligated to stand for one another — each acknowledging the other's status simultaneously. This has an elegant internal logic: if only the father stood for the son, the father's dignity as a parent would be undermined; if only the son stood for the father, the son's status as *rebbe* and bearer of Torah would be diminished. By requiring both, both statuses are preserved and mutually acknowledged.

The Rambam, however, rules differently and asymmetrically: *v'af al pi shehaya talmid beno, ein ha'av omed mifnei haben, aval haben omed mifnei aviv af al pi shehu talmido* — even if the son is the father's student, the father does not stand before the son; but the son stands before the father even though the father is his student. The Rambam thus maintains the integrity of the parent-child relationship as categorically inviolable. Whatever the *halacha* says about prioritizing the *rebbe* over the father in a case of a lost object, it does not extend so far as to place the father in a position of visible subordination to his child.

### Torah Honor Logic

One way to understand the Rambam's position is that it reflects something intrinsic to the nature of Torah itself. The full honor owed to a *talmid chacham* who represents Torah must itself include the *mitzvah* of *kibbud av*. A *rebbe* who allows his father to stand before him — or who does nothing to prevent it — has revealed something hollow about the Torah he carries. The learning, in other words, must translate into action, and one of the central actions that Torah demands is honor to one's father. By contrast, *kibbud av* seems to operate on a more primal, universal register — it does not depend on Torah for its legitimacy, but exists, in some sense, independently of it. When these two obligations come into direct tension in terms of visible posture, it is the father's status that is not to be publicly subordinated.

## Horseback Q (II)

### Riding Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: רָכוּב כִּמְהַלֵּךְ דָּמֵי, אוֹ לָא? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: תָּא שְׁמַע: טָמֵא יוֹשֵׁב תַּחַת הָאִילָן, וְטָהוֹר עוֹמֵד – טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before them:** If one's teacher is **riding** on an animal, is that considered **like walking,** and therefore one must stand before him, **or** is he **not** obligated to stand before him, since he is stationary relative to the animal? **Abaye said: Come** and **hear** a resolution from a different issue (*Nega'im* 13:7): If a leper, who is **ritually impure** and transfers impurity through a tent, i.e., anyone who enters the location of the leper is rendered impure, **is sitting under** the branches of **a tree,** which form a tent over him, **and a pure** person **is standing** under that tree, the pure person is rendered **impure.**

The *gemara* poses a new *ibaya*: *rochev kimahalich dami o lo* — is riding on a donkey halachically equivalent to walking? The question arises in the context of standing before a *rebbe*: if the *rebbe* passes by on his animal, does that trigger the obligation of *kima* the same way as if he were walking? At first glance, this is puzzling. The verse says *mipnei seiva takum v'hadarta pnei zaken* — it does not say anything about whether the *zaken* is ambulatory; the student is the one who must stand, and the *zaken* has simply appeared in one's presence. What should it matter whether the *rebbe* walks or rides?

One line of reasoning might be that standing is only meaningfully reciprocal when the *rebbe* himself is in a standing position, or that the act of "entering one's presence" is tied to active movement — but these remain difficult to articulate cleanly.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא עוֹמֵד תַּחַת הָאִילָן וְטָהוֹר יוֹשֵׁב – טָהוֹר, וְאִם יָשַׁב הַטָּמֵא – הַטָּהוֹר טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** If the **impure** person **is standing under the tree and the pure** person **is sitting** there, he remains **pure.** In this case, as the impure person is not settled there, he does not impart ritual impurity in a tent. **But if the impure** person **sat** and established his place there, **the pure** individual is rendered **impure.**

The *gemara* brings a proof from the laws of *tumah*: *tamei yoshev tachat ha'ilan v'tahor omed — tamei*. The rule is that a *metzora* conveys *tumah* to others sharing the same overhanging space — a tree, a roof — but only when the *metzora* is stationary. If the *metzora* is moving, no *tumah* is transmitted. *Tamei omed tachat ha'ilan v'tahor yoshev — tahor*. And if the *metzora* then sits down, the *tahor* person becomes *tamei*. The same rule applies to a stone afflicted with *tzara'at* that is being carried under a tree: if the one carrying it is standing, the people beneath are *tahor*; if the carrier sits, they become *tamei*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן בְּאֶבֶן הַמְנוּגַּעַת. וְאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר כֹּהֵן: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת רָכוּב כִּמְהַלֵּךְ דָּמֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** That mishna adds: **And the same** *halakha* applies **with regard to a stone afflicted** with a leprous **sore** (see Leviticus, chapter 14), which also imparts impurity of a tent. If one carrying a stone of this kind sits under a tree, a pure person standing under the tree is rendered impure, whereas if the person carrying the stone stands, he does not render the other individual impure. **And Rav Naḥman bar Kohen says: That is to say** that **riding is** considered **like walking,** as although the stone is stationary relative to the person, it is considered to be moving. **Conclude from it** that in all cases riding is like walking.

From this, Rav Nachman bar Kohen derives: *zot omeret, rochev kimahalich dami* — riding is like moving. The reasoning is that the stone itself has no independent mode of transport; its status tracks the one conveying it. If the carrier is sitting, the stone is treated as sitting; if moving, as moving. By analogy, the rider's state is what defines the operative category. The precise application to the laws of standing remains somewhat difficult to fully articulate — the *gemara*'s reasoning here is admittedly not transparent — but the technical conclusion is that riding is treated as equivalent to walking.

## Sefer Torah

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מַהוּ לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה? רַבִּי חִלְקִיָּה וְרַבִּי סִימוֹן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמְרִי: קַל וָחוֹמֶר, מִפְּנֵי לוֹמְדֶיהָ עוֹמְדִים – מִפָּנֶיהָ לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?!
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before them: What is** the *halakha* as **to** whether one should **stand before a Torah scroll? Rabbi Ḥilkiya and Rabbi Simon and Rabbi Elazar say** that this dilemma can be resolved by **an *a fortiori*** inference: If **one stands before those who study** the Torah, is it **not all the more so** true that one should stand **before** the Torah itself?

A new *ibaya*: *mahu la'amod mipnei sefer Torah* — does one stand in the presence of a *sefer Torah*? Rav Hilkiya, Rav Simon, and Rav Elazar argue from a *kal vachomer*: *mipnei lomdeha omdim, mipaneha lo kol shekein* — if one stands before those who learn Torah, certainly one stands before the Torah itself. The underlying logic points toward the primacy of the written text as the source of all that the *talmidei chachamim* transmit.

### R.S.B.A. Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אִלְעַי וְרַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר זַבְדִּי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי. חָלֵיף וְאָזֵיל רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בַּר אַבָּא, וְקָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לְהוּ: חֲדָא – דְּאַתּוּ חַכִּימֵי וַאֲנָא חָבֵר. וְעוֹד, כְּלוּם תּוֹרָה עוֹמֶדֶת מִפְּנֵי לוֹמְדֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Elai and Rabbi Ya'akov bar Zavdi were sitting** and studying Torah. **Rabbi Shimon bar Abba passed before them and they stood before him.** Rabbi Shimon bar Abba **said to them:** You are not obligated to do this, for two reasons. **One** reason is **that you are** ordained **scholars and I** am only **an associate,** i.e., he had not been ordained. **And furthermore, does the Torah stand before those who study it?** Since you are engaged in Torah study at the present moment you are not required to stand before a Torah scholar.

A striking counter-episode is then introduced. Rabbi Ilai and Rabbi Yaakov bar Zvidi were sitting and learning when Rabbi Shimon bar Abba passed by; they rose before him. He rebuked them on two grounds. First: *atun chakimei v'ana chaver* — you are scholars and I am merely a *chaver*, a communally recognized figure of standing but not a *talmid chacham* — why are you standing for me? Second, and more penetrating: *klum Torah omedet mipnei lomdeha* — does Torah stand before those who learn it? His argument, as Rashi explains it, is that these students were engaged in active Torah study, and by interrupting their learning to stand, they in effect caused the Torah — the living *cheftza* of Torah being articulated at that moment — to defer to him, a mere learner of Torah. The Torah does not stand before its learners.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֵין תַּלְמִיד חָכָם רַשַּׁאי לַעֲמוֹד מִפְּנֵי רַבּוֹ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁעוֹסֵק בַּתּוֹרָה. לָיֵיט עֲלַהּ אַבָּיֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: Rabbi Shimon bar Abba **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, as Rabbi Elazar says: A Torah scholar may not stand before his teacher when he is studying Torah,** because he is engaged in honoring the Torah itself. The Gemara adds: Even so, **Abaye cursed** anyone who acted in accordance with this ruling, as he would give the appearance of one who disrespected his teacher.

The *gemara* identifies this position as following Rabbi Elazar's view: *ein talmid chacham rashai la'amod mipnei rabbo b'sha'ah she'osek ba-Torah* — a *talmid chacham* need not rise before his *rebbe* when he is in the midst of Torah study. In Rabbi Shimon bar Abba's reading, the learning itself constitutes the presence of Torah; to interrupt it is to have Torah subordinate itself to a human being.

### Abaye Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהִבִּיטוּ אַחֲרֵי מֹשֶׁה עַד בֹּאוֹ הָאֹהֱלָה״, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא. חַד אָמַר לִגְנַאי, וְחַד אָמַר לְשֶׁבַח.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss the mitzva of standing before a Torah scholar. With regard to the verse: **"And they looked after Moses until he was gone into the tent"** (Exodus 33:8), **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa** disputed its correct interpretation. **One said** that this is stated **unfavorably, and one said** that it is meant **favorably.**

Abaye, however, *laite lah* — cursed this practice. He rejected the position of Rabbi Elazar entirely. Even while engaged in Torah study, one must rise before a *talmid chacham*. The implicit logic is powerful: if one is learning Torah and cannot bring oneself to honor a Torah sage, what does that reveal about the Torah being learned? The very substance of Torah includes *kibbud* toward those who bear it. To exempt oneself from *kima* by invoking one's own learning is to misunderstand what learning is for.

### Moshe Example

The *gemara* closes this section with a discussion of the verse *v'hibitu acharei Moshe b'ohel olo* — they would gaze after Moshe as he entered his tent. Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha dispute whether this behavior is described *l'gnai* — derogatorily — or *l'shvach* — approvingly. The one who reads it derogatorily invokes the *midrashic* tradition that the Israelites made disparaging comments about Moshe as he walked — accusing him of enriching himself at their expense — a behavior scandalous in any context involving a *rebbe*, and all the more so with Moshe.

The one who reads it approvingly proceeds to derive from this verse the distance protocols for standing before sages of different stature — transitioning the discussion toward the practical *halachot* of *kima* and *arba amot*.

## Distance Zones

### Graduated Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר תָּנָא: אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֲשֶׁר תָּשִׂים לִפְנֵיהֶם״ – הִשְׁוָה הַכָּתוּב אִשָּׁה לְאִישׁ לְכׇל דִּינִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה. **[Above from 33b:]** אֲמַר לִי רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, אָמַר רַבִּי אַבְדִּימִי דְּמִן חֵיפָא: חָכָם עוֹבֵר – עוֹמֵד מִלְּפָנָיו אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁעָבַר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת – יוֹשֵׁב. אַב בֵּית דִּין עוֹבֵר – עוֹמֵד מִלְּפָנָיו מְלֹא עֵינָיו, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁעָבַר אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת יוֹשֵׁב. נָשִׂיא עוֹבֵר – עוֹמֵד מִלְּפָנָיו מְלֹא עֵינָיו, וְאֵינוֹ יוֹשֵׁב עַד שֶׁיֵּשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְהִבִּיטוּ אַחֲרֵי מֹשֶׁה עַד בֹּאוֹ הָאֹהֱלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Rabbi Eliezer taught** as follows. **The verse states:** "Now these are the ordinances **which you shall set before them"** (Exodus 21:1), stating "them" in the plural. This **verse equates a woman to a man** with regard **to all judgments in the Torah.** **[Above from 33b:]** As Ḥizkiyya says: **Rabbi Ḥanina, son of Rabbi Abbahu, said to me** that **Rabbi Abbahu says** that **Rabbi Avdimi of Haifa says:** If a Torah **scholar** is **passing, one stands before him** if he passes within **four cubits** of him, **and once he passes four cubits** from him **he sits.** If the **president of the court** is **passing, one stands before him** as soon as he comes **within his range of vision. And once he passes four cubits** from him, **he sits.** If the ***Nasi*** is **passing, one stands before him** as soon as he comes **within his range of vision, and he does not sit until** the *Nasi* **sits in his place, as it is stated: "And they looked after Moses until he was gone into the tent,"** and only afterward did they sit. According to this interpretation, the verse is praising the behavior of the Jews.

The *gemara* now derives, from the verse *v'hibitu acharei Moshe*, the graduated rules for standing before sages of different rank. When an ordinary *chacham* passes by — *chacham over* — one stands when he enters within *arba amot* and may sit once he has passed beyond that radius. When the *Av Beit Din* passes — *melo einav* — one stands as soon as one sees him, and may sit once he has passed more than *arba amot* away. When the *Nasi* passes, the obligation is even more expansive: *omed milfanav melo einav v'eino yoshev ad she'yeshev bimkomo* — one stands as soon as he comes into sight and does not sit until the *Nasi* himself has taken his seat. The verse *v'hibitu acharei Moshe* anchors this final rule, since Moshe functions as the paradigmatic *Nasi* — standing before him until he entered his tent and was seated.

There is an apparent irony in the graduated structure: one might have expected that greater honor demands standing at a greater distance, yet the rule is that for a *chacham* one stands only at *arba amot*, while for a *Nasi* one stands from as far as the eye can see. One way of understanding this is that the *arba amot* requirement is about the individual's personal sphere of honor — marking the space of the *chacham*'s immediate presence — while the more expansive obligation for the *Nasi* reflects a public, institutional dimension that demands recognition across the full visual field.

The question of standing before one's father was briefly revisited in this context. The *gemara* does not explicitly formulate the obligation of *kima* before a parent in the discussion of *kibbud av va'em*, and the Shulchan Aruch's ruling that one is *chayav la'amod mifnei aviv* seems to be derived implicitly from the present *gemara* rather than from an explicit statement elsewhere. The famous story of a sage who said "the *Shechinah* is coming" upon hearing his mother approach suggests some tradition of standing for a parent, but the strict formal requirement, as one student noted, appears to flow primarily from the *rebbe-talmid* context and is applied to parents by derivation.

## Women Exemption

### Core Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, אָנָשִׁים חַיָּיבִים וְנָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **All positive, time-bound mitzvot obligate men and exempt women.**

The *gemara* now pivots sharply to a new topic: the exemption of women from *mitzvot asei she-hazeman grama* — positive commandments whose obligation is triggered by or limited to specific times. The *mishnah*'s rule is unambiguous: *kol mitzvot asei she-hazeman grama, anashim chayavim v'nashim pturot* — all time-bound positive commandments obligate men and exempt women.

### Time-Bound Ex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזוֹהִי מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – סוּכָּה, וְלוּלָב, שׁוֹפָר, וְצִיצִית, וּתְפִילִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What is a positive, time-bound mitzva?** Examples include residing in **a *sukka*, and** taking the ***lulav*,** and blowing the **shofar** on Rosh HaShana, all of which can be performed only at specific times of the year. **And** another example is donning **ritual fringes,** as the mitzva applies only during the daytime due to the verse which states: "Fringes, that you may look upon them" (Numbers 15:39), indicating that the fringes should be seen. **And** the donning of **phylacteries** (Deuteronomy 6:8), which are not worn at night or on Shabbat and Festivals, is also a positive, time-bound mitzva.

The *gemara* illustrates: *sukkah, lulav, shofar, tzitzit*, and *tefillin*. These are the paradigmatic cases. *Tzitzit* does not apply to nighttime garments; *tefillin* does not apply on *Shabbat* and, according to many opinions, not at night.

### Zman Grama Def

Tosafot raises a significant definitional challenge. Strictly speaking, *tzitzit* does apply at night — it simply does not apply to nighttime *garments*, not to daytime garments worn at night. If a person wears a four-cornered daytime garment at night, the obligation of *tzitzit* applies. So why is *tzitzit* categorized as *zeman grama*? Tosafot concludes that *zeman grama* does not mean that the *mitzvah* itself only applies at certain times; rather, it means that time is a *factor* that shapes the scope of the *mitzvah*. Because the type of garment obligated in *tzitzit* is itself time-sensitive, time plays a determinative role, and that is enough to classify it as *zeman grama*.

Similarly with *tefillin*: Tosafot argues that if the *halacha* follows the position that nighttime is actually *zeman tefillin* — one simply refrains from wearing *tefillin* at night out of concern that one may fall asleep in them — then the only time *tefillin* are genuinely not worn is *Shabbat*. Would one otherwise call this *zeman grama*? Probably not — fundamentally, the obligation is constant, with narrow exceptions. Yet because it is not uninterrupted, it falls under the *zeman grama* rubric. The definition of *zeman grama* is thus being stretched and refined by the very examples chosen to illustrate it.

### Non-Bound Ex

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזוֹהִי מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁלֹּא הַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ – מְזוּזָה, מַעֲקֶה, אֲבֵידָה, וְשִׁילּוּחַ הַקֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is a positive mitzva that is not time bound?** Examples include the affixing of **a *mezuza*** (Deuteronomy 11:20), the construction of **a parapet** on a roof (Deuteronomy 22:8), returning **a lost item** (Deuteronomy 22:1–3), **and the release** of the mother bird from **the nest,** i.e., the mitzva of sending away a mother bird when one finds it sitting on chicks or eggs (Deuteronomy 22:6–7).

The *gemara* then gives examples of *mitzvot asei she-lo hazeman grama* — non-time-bound commandments — in which women are obligated: *mezuzah, ma'akeh* (a fence on one's roof), *aveidah* (returning a lost object), and *shiluach ha-ken* (sending away the mother bird before taking the eggs).

### Tosafot Q

Tosafot finds this list troubling, and the difficulty is substantial. With the exception of *mezuzah*, every one of these *mitzvot* is directly bound up with a *lo ta'aseh* — a negative prohibition. By *aveidah*, the Torah says *lo tuchal le-his'alem* — you may not ignore it. By *shiluach ha-ken*, the Torah says *lo tikach ha-em al ha-banim*. By *ma'akeh*, it says *lo tasim damim b'veisecha*. Women are universally obligated in negative commandments. So even if one were to argue that women are exempt from the positive *aseh* in isolation, they would still be bound to act because of the *lo ta'aseh*. Why list these as examples of non-time-bound *mitzvot asei* in which women are obligated, when the obligation may actually be flowing from the negative prohibition rather than from the positive commandment?

Tosafot tries to construct scenarios where the *aseh* and the *lo ta'aseh* can be decoupled. For *aveidah*: if a person picked up a lost object with the intention of returning it and then changed his mind, there would be no violation of *lo tuchal le-his'alem* — since initially he had not ignored it — but the positive *mitzvah* of *hasheiv tishivem* would still apply. For *ma'akeh*: if a person built a fence and it subsequently collapsed, *lo tasim damim b'veisecha* would not be violated — since the person had initially acted — but *va'asita ma'akeh* would still require him to rebuild it. These constructions isolate the pure positive *mitzvah*, but they are evidently somewhat artificial, and the underlying question remains: these *mitzvot* are fundamentally of a different character than a standalone *aseh* with no accompanying prohibition, and the list is curious for including them.

### Rule Unreliable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְלָלָא הוּא? הֲרֵי מַצָּה, שִׂמְחָה, הַקְהֵל, דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא, וְנָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת! וְתוּ: וַהֲרֵי תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה, פְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, וּפִדְיוֹן הַבֵּן דְּלָאו מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ הוּא – וְנָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But is** this an established **principle?** But **there are** the mitzvot of eating ***matza*** on the first night of Passover (Exodus 23:15), of **rejoicing** on a Festival (Deuteronomy 16:9–11), and **assembly** on *Sukkot* following the Sabbatical Year (Deuteronomy 31:10–13). **And** each of these is **a positive, time-bound mitzva, and** yet **women are obligated** in them. **And furthermore,** one can raise a difficulty as follows: **But there are** the mitzvot of **Torah study** (Deuteronomy 6:7), **procreation** (Genesis 1:28), **and redemption of the firstborn** (Exodus 13:12–13), each of which **is not a positive, time-bound mitzva, and** yet **women are exempt** from them.

This brings the *gemara* to its central methodological conclusion on the reliability of the rule itself. The *gemara* asks *v'klala hu* — is this *klal* actually dependable as a rule? The answer is clearly no in both directions: there are time-bound *mitzvot* in which women are obligated (*matzah, simcha, hakhel*), and there are non-time-bound *mitzvot* from which women are exempt (*talmud Torah, pirya v'rivya, pidyon ha-ben*).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין לְמֵדִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״חוּץ״,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One does not learn** practical *halakhot* **from general statements,** i.e., when a general statement appears in a mishna and uses the term: All, it is not to be understood as an all-inclusive statement without exceptions. This is the case **even in a place where it says: Except,** to exclude a specific matter.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore states: *ein l'meidim min ha-klalos v'afilu b'makom she-ne'emar bo chutz* — one cannot derive law from general rules, even when the rule explicitly lists its exceptions. The expectation would be that a rule accompanied by stated exceptions is exhaustive — all other cases follow the rule. Rabbi Yochanan rejects this. The proof comes from elsewhere in *Shas*: the *mishnah* states *b'chol me'arvin u'mishtatfin — chutz min ha-mayim u'melach* — one may make an *eiruv* with anything except salt and water. Yet fungi and mushrooms are also excluded, and the *mishnah* does not list them. The stated exceptions are not exhaustive, and the rule cannot be taken as absolute.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: בַּכֹּל מְעָרְבִין וּמִשְׁתַּתְּפִין חוּץ מִן הַמַּיִם וּמֶלַח, וְתוּ לֵיכָּא? וְהָאִיכָּא כְּמֵהִין וּפִטְרִיּוֹת! אֶלָּא אֵין לְמֵדִין מִן הַכְּלָלוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בּוֹ ״חוּץ״.
>
> **Translation:** A proof for this is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Eiruvin* 26b): **One can establish a joining** of houses in courtyards [*eiruv ḥatzerot*] and a joining of Shabbat boundaries [*eiruv teḥumin*], **and** similarly, **one can merge** courtyards to permit carrying in a joint alleyway on Shabbat. This can be done **with all** types of food **except for water and salt.** This is stated as a *halakha* with specific exceptions, **and** yet one can ask: **Is there nothing else** that cannot be used for an *eiruv*? **But there are truffles and mushrooms,** which also cannot be used for an *eiruv*, because they do not offer nourishment. **Rather, conclude from this** that **one may not learn from general statements, even in a place where it says: Except.**

What remains at stake is the deeper question of how this rule functions. Does it serve as an operative principle — such that when the *halacha* of a particular *mitzvah* is uncertain, one can invoke *zeman grama* to predict the outcome? Or is it merely a descriptive pattern, cataloguing cases already known independently from *masorah*, with no independent predictive power? The Rambam in his *Peirush Hamishnayos* takes the latter view: everything we know, we know from *masorah*, not from this rule. But the *stam gemara* throughout this sugya operates as if the rule does carry predictive weight — if a *mitzvah* is *zeman grama*, the working presumption is that women are exempt unless we have reason to think otherwise.

## Tefillin Source

### K.V. Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת מְנָלַן? – גָּמַר מִתְּפִילִּין, מַה תְּפִילִּין נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת – אַף כׇּל מִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָא נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת. וּתְפִילִּין? – גָּמַר לַהּ מִתַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה, מָה תַּלְמוּד תּוֹרָה נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת – אַף תְּפִילִּין נָשִׁים פְּטוּרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara turns to the sources of this principle. **From where do we** derive that **women are exempt from positive, time-bound mitzvot?** It is **derived** by juxtaposition **from** the mitzva of **phylacteries: Just as women are exempt from** donning **phylacteries, so too, women are exempt from all positive, time-bound mitzvot. And** the exemption of women from donning **phylacteries is derived from** their exemption from **Torah study: Just as women are exempt from Torah study,** as derived from Deuteronomy 11:19, **so too women are exempt from** donning **phylacteries,** as the two issues are juxtaposed in the Torah (Deuteronomy 6:7–8).

The *gemara* now asks for the fundamental source of the entire exemption: *minalan* — from where do we know this? *Gemar mi-tefillin* — the paradigm case is *tefillin*. Women are exempt from *tefillin*, and from *tefillin* we generalize: *af kol mitzvas asei she-hazeman grama nashim pturot* — all time-bound positive commandments exempt women, on the model of *tefillin*.

But this immediately raises the question of how we know that women are exempt from *tefillin* in the first place. The *gemara* answers: *V'tefillin gemar mi-talmud Torah* — we derive it from *talmud Torah*. Just as women are exempt from *talmud Torah* — because the verse says *v'shinantam l'vaneichem*, "you shall teach your sons," which is read as *beneichem v'lo benoteichem*, your sons and not your daughters — so too women are exempt from *tefillin*. The derivation works because *tefillin* is textually juxtaposed to *talmud Torah* in the passage of *krias shema*: *v'dibarta bam... u'kshartam l'os al yadecha*.

The irony is striking and worth noting. *Talmud Torah* is itself not *zeman grama* — it is an obligation that applies constantly, at all times and in all places. Yet *talmud Torah* becomes the *source* from which we learn the exemption for *tefillin*, which then becomes the paradigm from which we generalize to all *zeman grama* cases. The chain of derivation thus runs from a non-time-bound *mitzvah* to a time-bound *mitzvah* to a general rule about time-bound *mitzvot*.

### Tefillin Choice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַקֵּישׁ תְּפִילִּין לִמְזוּזָה! תְּפִילִּין לְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה אִיתַּקּוּשׁ, בֵּין בְּפָרָשָׁה רִאשׁוֹנָה בֵּין בְּפָרָשָׁה שְׁנִיָּה, תְּפִילִּין לִמְזוּזָה בְּפָרָשָׁה שְׁנִיָּה לָא אִיתַּקּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let us** say the opposite **and juxtapose phylacteries to *mezuza*,** which is also mentioned in that passage. *Mezuza* is a mitzva in which women are also obligated. Based on this comparison, women would be obligated in phylacteries as well. The Gemara answers: **Phylacteries are juxtaposed to Torah study** in **both the first paragraph and in the second paragraph** of *Shema*, whereas **phylacteries are not juxtaposed to *mezuza* in the second paragraph.** It is therefore preferable to compare phylacteries to Torah study.

The *gemara* then asks: why do we learn women's exemption from *tefillin* rather than deriving it the other way — that women should be obligated in *tefillin* as they are in *mezuzah*? Both *tefillin* and *mezuzah* appear in the *parshiyot* of *krias shema*; why not link *tefillin* to *mezuzah* rather than to *talmud Torah*?

The answer is that *tefillin* is juxtaposed to *talmud Torah* in both the first and second *parshiyot* of *krias shema*. In the first *parsha*: *v'shinantam... u'kshartam... u'chsavtam* — *talmud Torah*, then *tefillin*, then *mezuzah*. In the second *parsha*: *u'kshartam... v'limadetem... u'chsavtam* — *tefillin*, then *talmud Torah*, then *mezuzah*. In both passages, *tefillin* is directly adjacent to *talmud Torah*. By contrast, in the second *parsha*, *tefillin* is not directly adjacent to *mezuzah* — *talmud Torah* intervenes between them. Since *tefillin* is consistently and doubly linked to *talmud Torah*, and not consistently linked to *mezuzah*, we derive the status of *tefillin* from *talmud Torah* rather than from *mezuzah*.

### Mezuzah Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַקֵּישׁ מְזוּזָה לְתַלְמוּד תּוֹרָה! לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דִּכְתִיב: ״לְמַעַן יִרְבּוּ יְמֵיכֶם״, גַּבְרֵי בָּעוּ חַיֵּי, נְשֵׁי לָא בָּעוּ חַיֵּי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara says: **But** if so, **let us juxtapose *mezuza* to Torah study** and say that women are also exempt from the obligation of a *mezuza*. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: This **could not enter your mind, as it is written** with regard to the mitzva of *mezuza*: **"That your days may be multiplied"** (Deuteronomy 11:21). Can it be said that **men need life** but **women do not need life?** Since the reward for the performance of the mitzva of *mezuza* is extended life, this mitzva applies to women as well.

Having established that *tefillin* is linked to *talmud Torah*, one might ask the further question: could *mezuzah* itself then be linked to *talmud Torah* and thereby exempt women from *mezuzah* as well? The *gemara* says *lo salka daitach* — that cannot be entertained. The verse by *mezuzah* says *lemaan yirbu yemeichem* — so that your days shall be multiplied. To say that women are exempt from *mezuzah* would imply that women do not need long life — *gavrei bei chayei nashei lo bei chayei*? — an obviously untenable conclusion. Therefore women are unambiguously obligated in *mezuzah*, and no *hekkesh* to *talmud Torah* can override that explicit textual anchor.

Tosafot raises a parallel objection: *talmud Torah* itself also says *lemaan yirbu yemeichem* — so should women be obligated there as well? The answer is that the explicit *derasha* of *beneichem v'lo benoteichem* tips the scales specifically in that context. When there is no such explicit exclusion, *lemaan yirbu yemeichem* anchors women's obligation; when there is, the exclusion prevails.

## Rule Testing

### Sukkah Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי סוּכָּה דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, דִּכְתִיב ״בַּסֻּכֹּת תֵּשְׁבוּ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים״, טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא הָאֶזְרָח לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנָּשִׁים, הָא לָאו הָכִי נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But there is** the mitzva of residing in **a *sukka*, which is a positive, time-bound mitzva, as it is written: "In *sukkot* you shall reside seven days"** (Leviticus 23:42), referring to seven specific days of the year. Nevertheless, **the reason** women are exempt from this mitzva is **that the Merciful One writes** in the continuation of the verse: "All **the homeborn** in Israel shall reside in *sukkot*." The definite article "the" is an exclusion, and serves **to exclude the women** from the obligation to reside in a *sukka*. It may be derived from here that **if that was not so, women** would be **obligated.** This indicates that women do not receive a blanket exemption from every positive, time-bound mitzva.

The *gemara* now tests the general rule against specific cases where an explicit *pasuk* was needed to either exempt or obligate women, asking whether the need for such explicit statement reveals something about the baseline presumption. If women are presumptively exempt from *zeman grama mitzvot* by virtue of the general rule, why did the Torah need to exempt them explicitly from *sukkah*? The verse by *sukkah* says *kol ha'ezrach b'Yisrael* — all citizens — and the *derasha* derives from *ha'ezrach* a specific exclusion of women. If the rule already exempted them, what was this *pasuk* doing?

### Abaye Pos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל דִּכְתִיב ״בַּסֻּכֹּת תֵּשְׁבוּ״ – ״תֵּשְׁבוּ״ כְּעֵין תָּדוּרוּ: מָה דִּירָה אִישׁ וְאִשְׁתּוֹ – אַף סוּכָּה אִישׁ וְאִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In the case of residing in a *sukka* a special verse **was necessary** to exempt women, as otherwise **it might enter your mind to say** that **since it is written:** "In *sukkot* you shall reside," this means that you should **reside as you dwell** in your permanent home: **Just as a man and his wife** live together in **a residence, so too, a man and his wife** are obligated to reside together in a ***sukka*.**

Abaye answers: the *pasuk* was necessary despite the general rule — *itzterich*. *Salka daitach amina* — I might have thought that the word *teshvu* in *basukkot teshvu shivat yamim* — you shall dwell in *sukkot* — implies *teshvu ke'ein taduru*, you shall dwell as you dwell in your homes. Since dwelling in a home is done jointly by husband and wife, I might have concluded that *teshvu* demands women's participation in *sukkah* equally. The *pasuk* of *ha'ezrach* was therefore needed to clarify that despite *teshvu*, the general rule of exemption holds. The need for the *pasuk* thus tells us nothing about the baseline presumption — it was needed to override a specific countervailing consideration, not to establish the rule itself.

### Rava Pos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: נֵילַף ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ מֵחַג הַמַּצּוֹת, מָה לְהַלָּן נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת – אַף כָּאן נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **necessary** to state this verse for another reason, as **it might enter your mind to say: Derive** a verbal analogy with regard to *Sukkot*, where the verse states: "On the **fifteenth** day of this seventh month is the festival of *Sukkot*" (Leviticus 23:34), **from** Passover, where the verse states: "And on the **fifteenth** day of the same month is the **festival of Passover"** (Leviticus 23:6). One would then say that **just as there women are obligated** to eat *matza* on the first night of Passover, despite the fact that it is a time-bound mitzva, **so too here,** with regard to the mitzva of residing in the *sukka*, **women are obligated.** Therefore it **was necessary** for the verse to use the term "the homeborn" to exclude women from the obligation to reside in a *sukka*.

Rava offers a different reason why the *pasuk* by *sukkah* was necessary. *Neilef chamisha asar chamisha asar mechag ha-matzot* — one might construct a *gezeira shava* between the fifteenth of Tishrei, the date of *sukkot*, and the fifteenth of Nisan, the date of *matzah*. Since women are obligated in *matzah* — because they too were prohibited from eating *chametz* and therefore share the obligation to eat *matzah* — I might have derived from this *gezeira shava* that women are equally obligated in *sukkah*. The explicit exclusion of *ha'ezrach* was therefore needed to block that derivation. Again, the need for the *pasuk* reveals a specific reason it had to be stated, not a general presumption of women's obligation in *zeman grama*.

In this context, Rava's *gezeira shava* also illuminates an interesting *machlokes Rishonim* about *sukkah* on the first night. Some *Rishonim* hold that on the first night of *sukkot* one must eat in the *sukkah* even if it is raining — because the linkage between *sukkah* and *matzah* implies an absolute dimension to the first night's obligation, parallel to the absolute obligation to eat *matzah* on the *seder* night. This connection between *sukkah* and *matzah* is thus not merely an Amoraic construct; it has real practical implications.

### Olu Reyiyah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי רְאִיָּה, דְּמִצְוַת עֲשֵׂה שֶׁהַזְּמַן גְּרָמָהּ, וְטַעְמָא דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״זְכוּרְךָ״ – לְהוֹצִיא הַנָּשִׁים, הָא לָאו הָכִי נָשִׁים חַיָּיבוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **But there is** the mitzva of **appearance,** i.e., the obligation to bring a burnt-offering on pilgrimage Festivals, **which is a positive, time-bound mitzva. And the reason** women are exempt from this obligation is **that the Merciful One writes,** with regard to this mitzva: "Three times in the year all of **your males** shall appear before the Lord God" (Exodus 23:17), which serves **to exclude women.** It may be derived from here that **if that were not so, women** would be **obligated.** This indicates that women are not necessarily exempt from every positive, time-bound mitzva.

The *gemara* raises the same question about *olat re'iya* — the *korban* brought on the three *regalim*. This is as quintessentially *zeman grama* as any *mitzvah* can be — mandated three times per year on a fixed calendar schedule. The Torah explicitly restricts it to males: *yera'eh kol zechurcha* — all your males shall appear. If the general rule already exempts women, why did the Torah need to say *zechurcha*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: נֵילַף ״רְאִיָּה״ ״רְאִיָּה״ מֵהַקְהֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **was necessary** for the verse to teach the *halakha* in this case as well, as **it might enter your mind to say: Derive** a verbal analogy with regard to **appearance,** where the verse states: Three times in the year all of your males shall appear," **from** the **appearance** stated with regard to the mitzva of **assembly,** about which the verse states: "When all of Israel come to appear before the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 31:11). One would then say that just as women are obligated in the mitzva of assembly, so too they should be obligated to appear on a pilgrimage Festival. It is therefore necessary for the Torah to state explicitly that women are exempt from the mitzva of appearance on a pilgrimage Festival.

The *gemara* again answers: *itzterich*. The reason is that women are explicitly obligated in *hakhel* — the seven-year gathering — where the Torah uses the same language of *appearing*, *yera'eh* or a cognate form. One might have constructed a *gezeira shava* from this shared language: just as women must appear at *hakhel*, perhaps they must appear at the *regalim* as well. The explicit exclusion of *zechurcha* was needed to block this derivation. Again, no inference about the general rule's presumptive direction can be drawn from the need to state the exemption here.

## Paradigm Resolution

### Simcha Not Personal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַדְּיָלְפִינַן מִתְּפִילִּין לִפְטוּרָא, נֵילַף מִשִּׂמְחָה לְחִיּוּבָא! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אִשָּׁה בַּעֲלָהּ מְשַׂמְּחָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the primary proof for the principle that women are exempt from positive, time-bound mitzvot, the Gemara asks: **But before deriving** the *halakha* **from phylacteries, to exempt** women from all positive, time-bound mitzvot, **derive** it **from** the mitzva of **rejoicing** on a Festival, in which women are obligated, **to obligate** women in all these mitzvot. **Abaye said:** The mitzva of rejoicing does not apply directly to women. Rather, **a woman is rendered joyful by her husband,** i.e., the mitzva is for him to gladden her on a Festival.

Having established the general rule and worked through the cases that seem to challenge it from both sides, the *gemara* returns to the original observation that the rule breaks down in both directions — there are time-bound *mitzvot* where women are obligated, and non-time-bound *mitzvot* where they are exempt. The question is which category should serve as the paradigm.

The three cases of women's obligation in time-bound *mitzvot* were *matzah*, *simcha*, and *hakhel*. Abaye disposes of *simcha* first: *isha ba'alah mesamchah* — a woman's *simcha* obligation is not a personal one. The verse *v'samachta b'chagecha ata u'veitecha* places the obligation on the man to make his household rejoice. In Temple times this was accomplished through *shalmei simcha* — the husband brought the peace-offerings and shared the meat with the members of his household, including his wife. The wife participates in *simcha*, but as a recipient of the husband's obligation, not as an independent bearer of it. Accordingly, *simcha* is not a genuine exception to the rule.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְמָנָה מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? בִּשְׁרוּיָה אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What can be said** with regard to **a widow,** who no longer has a husband but is nevertheless obligated to be joyful on a Festival, as it is written: "And you shall rejoice before the Lord your God, you…and the widow" (Deuteronomy 16:11)? The Gemara answers that the mitzva does not apply directly to a widow; rather, it applies **to** the men **with whom she is present,** i.e., they have an obligation to ensure that widows rejoice on the Festivals.

The *gemara* adds that even a widow who lives alone is included in the *mitzvah* — derived from *v'ha-ger v'ha-yatom v'ha-almanah asher b'kirbecha* — but again, as one who must be included in others' *simcha* obligation, not as an independent obligant.

### Two Verses Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנֵילַף מֵהַקְהֵל! מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה מַצָּה וְהַקְהֵל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְכׇל שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִין כְּאֶחָד – אֵין מְלַמְּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **derive** that women are obligated in all positive, time-bound mitzvot **from** the mitzva of **assembly,** in which women are explicitly obligated despite the fact that it is a time-bound mitzva. The Gemara answers: One cannot derive in this manner, **because** the verses concerning ***matza* and assembly are two verses that come as one,** i.e., to teach the same matter, that women are obligated in these mitzvot despite the fact that these are positive, time-bound mitzvot. **And** there is a principle that **any two verses that come as one do not teach** a precedent that applies to other cases. Rather, the two instances are considered exceptions.

With *simcha* removed, two genuine exceptions remain on the obligation side: *matzah* and *hakhel*. The *gemara* invokes the principle of *shnei ketuvim ha-ba'im ke-echad ein melamdin* — when the Torah explicitly states the same rule in two separate contexts, those two instances are treated as exceptions rather than the rule, and neither can be used as the paradigm for a general derivation. *Matzah* and *hakhel* neutralize each other.

### Both Necessary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּפִילִּין וּרְאִיָּה נָמֵי שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד – וְאֵין מְלַמְּדִים! צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא תְּפִילִּין וְלָא כְּתַב רְאִיָּה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: נֵילַף רְאִיָּה רְאִיָּה מֵהַקְהֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the verses concerning **phylacteries and appearance** are **also two verses that come as one,** as they both indicate that women are exempt from positive, time-bound mitzvot, **and** therefore the verses do **not teach** a precedent. The Gemara answers: These are not considered as two verses that come as one, as both **are necessary,** each for its own reason. **As, if the Merciful One had written** that women are exempt from donning **phylacteries and had not written** that they are exempt from the mitzva of **appearance, I would say: Derive** a verbal analogy to obligate women from the verse stated with regard to **appearance from** the **appearance** stated with regard to the mitzva of **assembly.** Therefore, it is necessary for the Torah to teach that women are exempt from the mitzva of appearance.

The *gemara* anticipates an objection: on the exemption side, we now have *tefillin* and *olat re'iya* — two explicit statements of women's exemption. By the same logic of *shnei ketuvim*, should they not also neutralize each other, leaving us without a paradigm in either direction?

The *gemara* answers that *tefillin* and *olat re'iya* are not superfluous. Each was necessary for its own reason. If the Torah had stated the exemption only for *tefillin* and not for *olat re'iya*, one would have derived women's obligation in *olat re'iya* from the *gezeira shava* of *re'iyah-re'iyah* from *hakhel*. Therefore the explicit exemption for *olat re'iya* was needed. Conversely, if the Torah had stated the exemption only for *olat re'iya* and not for *tefillin*, one might have linked *tefillin* to *mezuzah* rather than to *talmud Torah*, and thereby derived women's obligation in *tefillin*. Therefore the explicit statement by *tefillin* was also needed. Since each was independently required, they cannot be read as exceptions — each is doing necessary work. The *shnei ketuvim* principle does not apply when both statements were indispensable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא רְאִיָּה וְלָא כְּתַב תְּפִילִּין, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אַקֵּישׁ תְּפִילִּין לִמְזוּזָה, צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if the Merciful One had written** that women are exempt from **appearance, and had not written** that they are exempt from donning **phylacteries, I would say: I will compare phylacteries to *mezuza*,** which would mean that women are obligated in the mitzva of phylacteries. Therefore, it is **necessary** to state this *halakha* for both phylacteries and appearance, and they are not two verses that come as one.

### Hakhel Superfluous

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַצָּה וְהַקְהֵל נָמֵי צְרִיכִי! לְמַאי צְרִיכִי? בִּשְׁלָמָא אִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הַקְהֵל וְלָא כְּתַב מַצָּה, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: נֵילַף ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ ״חֲמִשָּׁה עָשָׂר״ מֵחַג הַסּוּכּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the verses concerning ***matza* and assembly** are **also necessary,** each for its own reason, and they are not two verses that come as one either. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **For what** purpose are both of them **necessary? Granted, if the Merciful One had written** that women are obligated in the mitzva of **assembly but had not written** that they are obligated in eating ***matza*, I would say: Derive** a verbal analogy with regard to Passover, where the verse states: "And on the **fifteenth** day of the same month is the festival of Passover" (Leviticus 23:6), from *Sukkot*, where the verse states: "On the **fifteenth** day of this seventh month is the festival of *Sukkot*" (Leviticus 23:34), teaching that women are exempt from eating *matza*, just as they are exempt from residing in a *sukka*. Therefore, it is necessary for a verse to teach that women are obligated in eating *matza*.

By contrast, on the obligation side, *hakhel* was superfluous. Had the Torah stated only that women are obligated in *matzah* and said nothing about *hakhel*, one could have derived women's obligation in *hakhel* independently: the Torah states *ha-anashim v'ha-nashim v'ha-taf* — the men, the women, and the children must appear. Since infants are obligated in *hakhel*, and children have a lesser obligation than women in all other contexts, *kol she-kein* women should certainly be obligated. There was therefore no need for the Torah to say explicitly that women are obligated in *hakhel* — it was derivable on its own. The fact that the Torah states it anyway marks it as an exception, not a rule. And since one of the two remaining time-bound cases of women's obligation is superfluous, the *shnei ketuvim* principle does not come into play — only *matzah* is a genuine obligation case, and a single case cannot become the paradigm.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נִיכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא מַצָּה, וְלָא בָּעֵי הַקְהֵל, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: טְפָלִים חַיָּיבִים, נָשִׁים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?! הִילְכָּךְ, הָוֵה לְהוּ שְׁנֵי כְתוּבִים הַבָּאִים כְּאֶחָד, וְאֵין מְלַמְּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **But let the Merciful One write** that women are obligated in eating ***matza*, and it** would **not** be **necessary** to state the same *halakha* with regard to **assembly, and I** would **say** on my own: If **children are obligated** in assembly, as is stated explicitly in the verse "Assemble the people, the men and the women and the children" (Deuteronomy 31:12), are **women not all the more so** obligated? **Therefore,** as it is explicitly stated that women are obligated in assembly, the verses concerning *matza* and assembly are **two verses that come as one, and** consequently do **not teach** a precedent.

### Final Conclusion

The resolution of the entire discussion is therefore as follows. The exemption category — *tefillin* and *olat re'iya* — comprises two cases each of which was necessary to state, and neither of which is superfluous. They therefore cannot be read as exceptions. By contrast, the obligation category — *matzah* and *hakhel* — includes *hakhel*, which was superfluous and therefore marks that whole category as the exceptional one. The general rule thus stands: women are exempt from *mitzvot asei she-hazeman grama*, and the cases where they are obligated are exceptions to that rule, not indicators of its reversal.

The deep irony of the entire sugya is that the chain of derivation begins with *talmud Torah* — itself not *zeman grama* — which is used to exempt women from *tefillin*, which is then elevated as the paradigm for all *zeman grama* exemptions. Had *talmud Torah* been made the paradigm directly, women would have been exempt from all positive commandments entirely, time-bound or not. But by moving through *tefillin*, and by then arguing that the exemption category cannot be characterized as exceptional while the obligation category can, the *gemara* arrives at a precisely calibrated conclusion: women are exempt from time-bound positive commandments, obligated in non-time-bound ones, and the specific cases that deviate from this pattern are identified as exceptions derivable from *masorah* and *drasha*, not as challenges to the rule itself.
