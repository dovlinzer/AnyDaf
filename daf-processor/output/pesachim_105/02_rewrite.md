# Pesachim 105 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blessing Structure

### Bracha Structures

The daf before us is 105a, but it is worth opening with a brief discussion of the issues raised in the previous session, since they illuminate several important structural questions about *brachot* that bear directly on what follows. The general rule governing the structure of a *bracha* is well known: a short *bracha* consists simply of *Baruch Atah*, opening with the divine name and concluding there, while a longer, "paragraph" *bracha* both opens and closes with *Baruch Atah*. There are, however, exceptions to this rule, the most prominent being the *bracha hasmucha lachaverta* — a blessing that is attached to the one preceding it and therefore need not open with *Baruch Atah*. The blessings of *Kriat Shema* operate on a similar principle. These exceptions led Tosafot to a searching inquiry into a number of cases that do not fit the standard framework, and it is worth reviewing one or two of them in detail.

### Bracha Hasmucha

The principle of *bracha hasmucha lachaverta* holds that when a blessing is organically attached to the one preceding it, it is exempt from requiring its own opening *Baruch Atah*. This exemption has both structural and thematic dimensions, and the exceptions Tosafot identifies in the large Tosafot on 104b illuminate the contours of the rule.

### Elokai Neshama Issue

The first case Tosafot raises is *Birkat Elokai Neshama*. Tosafot asks: *ve'teima, Brachat Elokai Neshama lo havei smucha lachaverta* — it is a difficulty, since *Elokai Neshama* is not originally *smucha lachaverta*. The *Gemara* in Masechet Berachot states that upon waking, one would say *Elokai Neshama* — not *Modeh Ani* as we do today — and it was thus not originally attached to any preceding *bracha*. In the siddurim as we have them, *Asher Yatzar* immediately precedes *Elokai Neshama*, so that one ends with the *bracha* of *Asher Yatzar* and flows directly into *Elokai Neshama*, effectively rendering it *smucha lachaverta*. But Tosafot emphasizes that this was not the original structural intent.

How, then, did *Elokai Neshama* originally stand on its own without a *Baruch Atah*? Tosafot's answer, found a few lines down beginning with *v'yesh lomar*, is: *lefi she'einam brachot ela shevach v'tefila b'alma* — these are not really *brachot* in the full sense, but rather words of praise and supplication. The actual *bracha* begins only at the very end. That answer is somewhat difficult, because one could say precisely the same thing about any paragraph-length *bracha* that does not open with *Baruch Atah*: it is simply words of praise leading up to the formal *bracha*. In effect, Tosafot is treating *Elokai Neshama* as something like a *piyyut* that precedes the blessing proper. The textual precision of its formulation makes this characterization a bit surprising, but that is Tosafot's position.

This observation raises a further practical question: if one has already recited *Asher Yatzar* and then moved on to another blessing — such as *tzitzit* — before saying *Elokai Neshama*, is *Elokai Neshama* still considered *smucha lachaverta*? One might try to resolve this by interposing some other *bracha* to recreate the structural adjacency. But Tosafot's deeper point is that even within the standard arrangement, the *smucha lachaverta* character of *Elokai Neshama* was not the original design. This in turn raises an inverse question: if something was originally established as *smucha lachaverta* but was subsequently separated from its neighbor, does that separation alter its halachic status? That question becomes directly relevant in the context of *Sheva Brachot*.

### Machzor Breaks

In the second large Tosafot on this topic — beginning *chutz mi-bracha hasmucha lachaverta* — Tosafot takes up the blessings following the *haftara*. He notes that the blessing *d'tzur kol ha-olamim*, which culminates in *she-kol devarav hem emet v'tzedek*, presents a question not from the *nusach* of the *bracha* itself but from the way it is laid out in the *machzorim* (meaning, in the manuscripts of the period). In the printed Artscroll siddur, the passage flows continuously from *she-kol devarav hem emet v'tzedek* into *Ne'eman Atah Hu Hashem Elokeinu* and concludes with *Baruch Atah Hashem ha-Kel ha-Ne'eman b'chol devarav* — a single *bracha* with proper opening and closing. But Tosafot observed that in the *machzorim* as they were written, a paragraph break appeared between these two sections, giving the impression that the first portion lacked a closing *Baruch Atah* and the second lacked an opening one — *v'ein sham bracha lo b'chatimat harishona v'lo bifteichat hashniya*.

The obvious resolution is that there should be no such break — it is all one *bracha*. Tosafot explains: *v'nireh d'kula bracha achata hi, d'kula mishtayei b'ne'emanut u'va'amittut ha-makom* — the entire passage is one *bracha* because it revolves around a single theme, the faithfulness and truth of the Omnipresent. *Ne'eman Atah*, *ha-Kel ha-Ne'eman* — it is all the same idea. The paragraph break in the *machzorim* may have served as a pause during which the *shliach tzibur* would allow the congregation to respond. What is striking about this Tosafot is the nature of the question itself: the difficulty does not arise from the formal *nusach* of the *bracha* but from the typographical or scribal convention of paragraph breaks in the manuscripts. The white space apparently suggested to some readers that the text had been divided into two structurally separate *brachot*. This phenomenon connects in a fascinating way to the broader *Ashkenazi* practice of *piyutim* — the flexibility with which certain communities inserted liturgical poetry into the fixed *nusach hatfillah*, particularly on *Yamim Tovim*. The pause implied by the paragraph break may also reflect something like the *Sefardi* practice of a responsive moment between the *shliach tzibur* and the *tzibbur*.

### Sheva Brachot

The discussion of *Sheva Brachot* introduces one of the most structurally interesting applications of the *smucha lachaverta* principle. In the *Sheva Brachot* recited under the *chuppah*, the first three blessings — *shehakol bara lichvodo*, *yotzer haadam*, and *asher yatzar et haadam* — each open with *Baruch Atah*. Why should they? Regarding the first two, Rabbeinu Tam explains in the Tosafot that *shehakol bara lichvodo* and *yotzer haadam* open with *Baruch Atah* because they are short, one-line *brachot* that lack a *chatimah*: *lekach poteach b'varuch v'lo chatimah* — they are like blessings over fruit or *mitzvot*, where the opening *Baruch* is simultaneously the entire *bracha* and its conclusion, *ufitichat zazu hi chatimatan*.

The more subtle question is why *asher yatzar et haadam* opens with a new *Baruch Atah*. Since *yotzer haadam* immediately precedes it, one would expect *asher yatzar* to simply continue as *smucha lachaverta* without its own opening *Baruch Atah*. Tosafot's answer is elegant: *u-vaasher yatzar et haadam tzarich liftoach b'varuch she-im lo hayah poteach hayah nireh hakol bracha achat* — *asher yatzar* requires its own opening *Baruch Atah* precisely because without it, the two blessings would appear to be one continuous paragraph *bracha* together with *yotzer haadam*. The thematic proximity of the two passages — both dealing with the creation of the human being — combined with the fact that *yotzer haadam* is a one-liner rather than a paragraph *bracha*, means that without an explicit *Baruch Atah* at the start of *asher yatzar*, the whole sequence would be read as one long *bracha*. This requires *asher yatzar* to assert its independence explicitly through an opening *Baruch Atah*.

One might note that the machzor problem discussed above could theoretically have been solved with a similar device — inserting an explicit *Baruch Atah* to mark the division. But Tosafot's approach in both cases reflects the same underlying logic: structural independence requires formal markers, and in their absence the text will be read as continuous.

This analysis bears directly on a prominent practical question: may the *Sheva Brachot* be distributed among different people, as is universally done at weddings today? Several *poskim* have raised the objection that since the blessings following *asher yatzar* do not open with *Baruch Atah* — being *smucha lachaverta* — if each is given to a different person who has not personally just recited the preceding *Baruch Atah*, they are no longer truly *smucha* to anything. Rav Moshe Feinstein responded that this is not the correct understanding of *smucha lachaverta*: the exemption from opening with *Baruch Atah* is established at the level of the original institution of the *bracha*, not at the level of the individual's execution of it in any given instance. The *bracha* was established as *smucha lachaverta*, and it therefore does not require *poteach b'varuch*, regardless of who recites it or whether the preceding *bracha* was recited by the same person. An interesting corollary: if the *Sheva Brachot* were recited out of order such that no preceding *Baruch Atah* was said by anyone, Rav Moshe's reasoning would apparently still hold, since the institution itself carries the exemption.

The final blessing, *asher bara sasson v'simcha*, opens with its own *Baruch Atah* for a different reason: it is capable of standing entirely on its own. When *panim chadashot* are not present and only a single *bracha* is recited, it is this one, and a *bracha* that can stand independently must open with *Baruch Atah*.

## Kashya L'Ulla

### Ulla's Havdalah

With this context established, we turn to the opening of 105a: *kashya l'Ulla* — this is a difficulty for Ulla. To recall the background: Rav Yehuda sent Rav Yitzchak to observe what Ulla did for *havdalah*, and Rav Yitzchak in turn sent Abaye. The report that came back was that Ulla said only *Hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol*. The Rashbam understands this to mean that Ulla recited a simple one-liner — much like the *havdalah* of Rebbi.

### Tosafot Reading

Tosafot, however, takes a different approach. A one-liner would not generate the difficulty the *Gemara* now poses — if Ulla recited a one-liner, the answer would be obvious: a one-liner does not require a closing *Baruch Atah*. Tosafot therefore reads the episode differently: Ulla recited the full content of *havdalah* — *Hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol, bein or l'choshech, bein Yisrael l'amim* — but omitted the closing *chatimah* entirely. He said the opening *Baruch Atah* but did not end with one. The words *Baruch Hamavdil* that the *Gemara* records are thus read by Tosafot as the opening of a full *bracha* without a conclusion.

### Single Theme Bracha

This is what the *Gemara* finds difficult: how can Ulla recite an opening without a closing *chatimah* for a passage of that length? *Amar lach Ulla* — Ulla would respond: *hani nami k'birchat hamitzvot damyan* — this is analogous to a *birchat hamitzvah*. A *birchat hamitzvah* requires no closing *Baruch Atah*. Why not? *Mishum d'hoda'ah hi* — because it is a single *hoda'ah*, a single act of acknowledgment centered on one idea, with no diversification of themes. And so too here: *hacha nami hoda'ah hi*.

What does this mean in practice? Tosafot explains that the question of whether a *bracha* is "short" or "long" — whether it requires a closing — is not simply a matter of word-count or physical length. It is a question of thematic complexity. A *bracha* that introduces multiple distinct themes requires a closing *Baruch Atah* to signal its conclusion. A *bracha* that, however lengthy, circles around a single unifying theme does not. When Ulla recites *Hamavdil bein kodesh l'chol, bein or l'choshech, bein Yisrael l'amim, bein yom hashvi'i l'sheshet yemei hama'aseh*, every clause is a variation on the same concept — *hamavdil, hamavdil, hamavdil* — the one who distinguishes. The entire passage is therefore a single *hoda'ah* and does not require *chatimah*, even though it is extensive in length.

This connects directly to the point Tosafot made earlier about *HaTov vehaMetiv*, which also lacks a closing *Baruch Atah* not because of its brevity but because it does not introduce genuinely new themes. For those of us accustomed to thinking of length as the trigger for requiring a closing *bracha*, this is a striking reorientation: it is thematic multiplicity, not length, that determines structural requirements. One might note that by this logic, the blessing at the conclusion of the *Megilla* reading could similarly be analyzed — Tosafot raises this as well, though we do in fact require a *chatimah* there, suggesting either a divergence from Ulla's principle or a determination that the themes are sufficiently distinct to warrant one.

## Shabbat Establishes

### Friday Meal Case

The *Gemara* now introduces the case of Rav Chananya bar Shelamya and the students of Rav, who were seated at a meal on Friday afternoon. Rav Hamnuna Saba was attending to them. They said to him: *zil chazi im ikadesh yoma* — go and see if the day has become sanctified, if it has grown dark — *v'nifsik v'nikbeiyeh l'Shabata* — and then we will interrupt and establish Shabbat. The phrase "establish Shabbat" is not entirely transparent here, but the context of the later discussion suggests they intended to stop the meal, recite *Birkat Hamazon*, perform something like the *siluk hashulchan* that appears in Rav Yehuda's approach, and then begin a new meal designated as the *Shabbat* meal.

### Shabata Kava

Rav Hamnuna Saba — the very attendant they were about to dispatch on this errand — instead addressed them with a halachic ruling: *lo tzarichitu* — you need not interrupt your meal and formally reconstitute it as a Shabbat meal. *Shabata kava nafshah* — Shabbat establishes itself.

What does it mean that Shabbat establishes itself? Taken literally, this might suggest one need do nothing at all — the meal automatically becomes a *Seudat Shabbat* without any intervention, akin to the position of Rabbi Yossi. The *Gemara*'s subsequent discussion makes clear that the *Rishonim* do not read it that way. Rather, the meaning is that Shabbat's arrival is sufficient to transform the ongoing meal into a *Seudat Shabbat* — one need not stop, bench, and restart — but the obligation to make *Kiddush* remains. The meal becomes a *Seudat Shabbat* by virtue of Shabbat's arrival, and a *Seudat Shabbat* demands *Kiddush*. The practice of *poreis mapa u'mekadesh* is thus the appropriate response: you do not restart the meal, but you do make *Kiddush*.

### Ma'aser Parallel

*D'amar Rav* — the source for this principle is a ruling of Rav: *keshem she'Shabbat kova'at l'ma'aser kach Shabbat kova'at l'kiddush* — just as Shabbat establishes for *ma'aser*, so Shabbat establishes for *kiddush*.

The *ma'aser* rule requires some explanation. Grain that has been harvested but has not yet undergone *gmar melacha* — the completion of processing, meaning it has been threshed, winnowed, mounded, and brought into the house — may be eaten *achilas arai*, as casual snacking, even before *trumos u'ma'asros* have been separated. Once *gmar melacha* is complete, such casual consumption is forbidden. The *Gemara* teaches that the onset of Shabbat functions as a trigger: because eating on Shabbat carries the character of a sit-down, enjoyment-oriented meal — *seudah* — rather than snacking, any eating done on Shabbat is automatically classified as formal eating rather than *achilas arai*, even if it would otherwise qualify as snacking. Shabbat therefore *establishes* the eating as something more significant than it might otherwise be.

The parallel Rav draws to *kiddush* is direct: just as Shabbat arrives and transforms the nature of eating, redefining even casual snacking as *Seudat Shabbat*, so too Shabbat arrives and takes the ongoing Friday meal and gives it a new identity as *Seudat Shabbat*. You were eating a Friday afternoon meal; Shabbat arrived; that meal is now a *Seudat Shabbat*. Tosafot sharpens the parallel: the *halacha* of *ma'aser* holds that if one was snacking on grain in the field Friday afternoon and Shabbat began, that very grain one was snacking on can no longer be eaten casually — even mid-meal, the arrival of Shabbat redefines what is happening. Similarly, even mid-meal on Friday afternoon, the arrival of Shabbat redefines the nature of the eating being done.

*Kova'at l'kiddush* — Shabbat establishes itself *for* kiddush, not instead of kiddush. As the *Gemara*'s phrasing makes clear, the obligation to make *kiddush* is not dissolved; rather, Shabbat's transformative power means the meal itself now demands *kiddush*, and that *kiddush* can be satisfied through *poreis mapa u'mekadesh* without reconstructing the meal from scratch.

### Kiddush Not Havdalah

One might reason by analogy: just as the beginning of Shabbat transforms the nature of eating and establishes an obligation of *kiddush*, the end of Shabbat should similarly transform the nature of the meal — one who is eating *shalosh seudot* as Shabbat departs should find that meal automatically reclassified as weekday eating, which would then require *havdalah* before continuing. *Savar minah* — this inference seemed compelling. But Rav Amram reports: *hachi amar Rav, le'kiddush kova'as ve'lo le'havdalah kova'as* — Rav says explicitly that Shabbat establishes only for *kiddush* at the entry, not for *havdalah* at the exit. And this is the ruling we follow — if it were otherwise, one could never complete *shalosh seudot* past nightfall.

The asymmetry requires explanation, and the *Gemara* will address it later. At this point the practical consequence is clear: the ongoing *seudat Shabbat* is not disrupted by nightfall and does not require an interruption for *havdalah*. The meal retains its Shabbat character and may continue.

It is worth noting that this ruling simultaneously rejects two positions. It rejects Rabbi Yossi's view, which would have allowed the Friday meal to continue into Shabbat with no intervention whatsoever — since Rav's ruling establishes that *kiddush* is indeed required once Shabbat begins. And it rejects the stricter view that would have required one to stop the *shalosh seudot*, interrupt for *havdalah*, and only then continue eating.

### Kiddush Required

Lest the principle of *shabata kava nafshah* be misread as obviating *kiddush* entirely, the *Gemara* clarifies: *hani mili le'inyan mifsek de'lo mafsekinan* — this principle that Shabbat establishes itself operates only with respect to the requirement to interrupt. One need not stop the meal and reconstitute it. *Aval atchulei lo matchalinan* — but one may not begin eating on Saturday night without first making *havdalah*. The two obligations are parallel: the same way one may not begin eating on Shabbat night without *kiddush*, one may not begin eating on *Motzei Shabbat* without *havdalah*. Rav Soloveitchik has a full discussion of this parallel. The underlying logic appears to be that *kiddush* and *havdalah* serve to mark the boundaries of *Seudat Shabbat*, investing the eating with the identity of the day. *Kiddush* designates what follows as *Seudat Shabbat*; *havdalah* demarcates the eating from that point forward as weekday eating, distinguishing it from *Seudat Shabbat* at the boundary line.

## Eating Rules

### Before Havdalah

*Aval atchulei lo matchalinan* — one may not begin eating after dark without *havdalah*. This is an important practical ruling that is not always observed with sufficient care. *U-mifsak nami lo amrinan ella be'achilah* — the permission to continue eating without interruption applies only to eating; *aval be'shtiyah lo* — if one was merely drinking (not eating a meal), drinking does not carry the same status, and one would need to interrupt. *U-ve'shtiyah nami lo amrinan ella be'chamra ve'shichra* — and the rule about drinking applies specifically to wine and beer. *Aval maya leis lan bah* — but water is not a problem; water may be drunk even after nightfall before *havdalah*.

In summary: one may not begin eating once it is dark, because one must make *havdalah* before eating. One who was already in the middle of a Shabbat meal may continue eating but not drinking. Water alone may be drunk even without *havdalah*. Other beverages — milk and similar liquids — presumably are treated like wine and beer: they do not carry the continuity privilege of an ongoing meal, and one may not begin drinking them before *havdalah*. The legal category is apparently the formal starting of a meal with *Hamotzi*.

### Mincha Ma'ariv Eating

Tosafot raises an important question about the temporal framing of *shalosh seudot*. The *Gemara* here seems to assume that one can begin *shalosh seudot* after *Mincha*, even close to nightfall. Yet our *minhag* is not to begin eating between *Mincha* and *Ma'ariv*. Tosafot notes that Rabbeinu Tam took strong exception to Rabbeinu Meshullam, who apparently instituted the practice of eating between *Mincha* and *Ma'ariv*: *ka'as al Rabbeinu Meshullam she-hinhig le'echol bein Mincha l'Ma'ariv*. The *Midrash* states: *ha-shoteh mayim be'Shabbat bein hashmashos gozel et krovav ha-metim* — one who drinks water on Shabbat during *bein hashmashos* robs from the waters belonging to his deceased relatives. And Tosafot records that in the town of Lutier, those who ate between *Mincha* and *Ma'ariv* encountered *sakana* — danger — a report that Rabbeinu Tam apparently connected to this ruling.

### Dead & Waters

The reference to robbing the dead of their waters during *bein hashmashos* opens onto a dimension of this discussion that goes beyond formal *halacha*. There is a mystical understanding according to which the deceased are granted a reprieve from the fires of *Gehinnom* for the duration of Shabbat. That reprieve extends not merely until three stars appear but until the community has concluded its prayers and made *havdalah*. Under this understanding, there are two related concerns: first, that one should deliberately postpone *havdalah* in order to extend the deceased's day of relief; and second, that consuming water before *havdalah* constitutes a form of taking for oneself the waters in which the deceased are, so to speak, cooling themselves — *gozel et krovav ha-metim*.

This framework also bears on the question of *Kaddish*, which relates to the *nishamos* of the deceased, and on the *minhag* of *Tziduk Hadin* recited on *Motzei Shabbat*, connected to the tradition that Moshe Rabbeinu died at the departure of Shabbat during *bein hashmashos*. A *Gaon* cited by the Rosh notes further customs of that period: not to deliver a public lecture — *midrash* — between the *tefillos* of Shabbat afternoon and evening, *mishum de'chacham she-meis beis midrasho biteilim*, and more broadly the cessation of certain activities during this transitional time.

The practical *halachic* question embedded here is: from which moment does the prohibition against beginning to eat take effect — from *shkia* or from *tzeis hakochavim*? Tosafot does not resolve this cleanly, and the mystical considerations are woven through the discussion in a way that makes it difficult to separate the purely *halachic* from the *agadic*. Rabbeinu Tam's severity regarding the town of Lutier suggests these concerns were very real to the *Rishonim* and not merely rhetorical devices to encourage compliance.

The Rashbam adds a further distinction worth noting. In the passage *lo kapya mayya k'mi havdalah aval k'mi kiddusha kapya* — those who were lenient about water before *havdalah* on *Motzei Shabbat* would nonetheless be strict about it before *kiddush* on Friday night, *mishum kavod Shabbat*, and *ve'hachi hilchasa* — this is the operative ruling. Whatever leniency one might permit regarding water before *havdalah*, there is no comparable debate with respect to the entry of Shabbat and *kiddush*. Water before *kiddush* is not permitted, and the Rashbam states that this is the accepted *halacha*.

## Late Kiddush

### Ravina's Question

*Ba'ei minei Ravina me'Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak* — Ravina asked Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak: *mi she'lo kiddesh be'erev Shabbat mahu she'yekadesh al kol hayom kulo* — if one forgot to make *kiddush* on *erev Shabbat*, may one make *kiddush* throughout the entire following day? The question, as framed, concerns whether the Friday night *kiddush* — the full *kiddush* of *Mekadesh HaShabbat* — may be recited on Shabbat morning if one omitted it the previous night.

The phrasing *mi she'lo kiddesh be'erev Shabbat*, rather than *be'leilei Shabbat*, is worth pausing over. *Erev Shabbat* means the eve of Shabbat itself. The Rambam understands *kiddush* as a demarcation of the transition point, designating the beginning of Shabbat. *Kiddush* need not be made at the precise moment Shabbat begins; it functions to mark the opening of the day, and this demarcation can occur roughly around the beginning. By the Rambam's reasoning, *kiddush* does not inherently constitute *kabbalat Shabbat* — it is not the act by which Shabbat is accepted, but rather the act by which the beginning of Shabbat is formally designated. This means, for example, that one may make *kiddush* on Friday afternoon before Shabbat formally begins, and similarly that *havdalah* may be recited before *shkia* — without the *ner*, obviously — anytime from *Plag HaMincha* onward. This is in fact the operative *halacha*, though one should not do so unless there is pressing need, such as a *devar mitzvah*.

Ravina's question, then, could extend beyond the simple scenario of "can I make *kiddush* on Shabbat morning?" It could also be asking about a case where midnight has already passed — where it is no longer *erev Shabbat* even in the loose sense of "around the beginning" — whether *kiddush* may still be made at that point.

### Parallel to Havdalah

*Amar lei* — Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds by drawing a parallel. *Mi de'amar lei* — since it is established that the children of Rabbi Chiya say: *mi she'lo hivdil be'Motzei Shabbat mavdil v'holech kol hashavua kulo* — if one failed to make *havdalah* on *Motzei Shabbat*, one may make *havdalah* throughout the week, specifically through Tuesday night. The principle is that although ideally *havdalah* belongs to the boundary moment of *Motzei Shabbat*, if it was missed it can be discharged later — it has an extension period. *Hach nami* — so too, one who failed to make *kiddush on erev Shabbat* may make *kiddush* throughout the entire day of Shabbat.

The parallel produces a striking theoretical scenario: if one forgot *kiddush* entirely and then also extended *shalosh seudot* into very late Shabbat afternoon, one could conceivably make *kiddush* at that point and then, moments later, make *havdalah*. A good exam question: when can one make *kiddush* and two minutes later make *havdalah*? The answer: late Shabbat afternoon, having failed to make *kiddush* the previous night, and then *Plag HaMincha* arrives and *havdalah* becomes permissible.

### Tosefta Objection

*Esvei* — an objection is raised from a *Tosefta*: *leilei Shabbat v'leilei Yom Tov yesh bahen kiddush al ha-kos v'yesh bahen hazkara b'Birkat Hamazon* — the nights of Shabbat and *Yom Tov* have *kiddush* on the cup and have the mention of Shabbat or *Yom Tov* in *Birkat Hamazon*. *Shabbat v'Yom Tov* — during the day of Shabbat and *Yom Tov*, *ein bahen kiddush al ha-kos* — there is no *kiddush* on a cup; one makes *Borei Pri HaGafen* but not a formal *bracha* of *Mekadesh HaShabbat*. *V'yesh bahen hazkara b'Birkat Hamazon* — but the mention of Shabbat in *Birkat Hamazon* does still apply. If it were possible to make the Friday night *kiddush* on Shabbat day, then the *Tosefta*'s statement that there is no *kiddush* on the cup during the day would be false — one could always find oneself in such a case, having forgotten the night before.

### Normal Cases

The *Gemara* dismisses this objection straightforwardly: *amar lei, d'ei lo lo tana* — the *Tosefta* is not speaking of unusual cases. As a rule, one makes *kiddush* on the night of Shabbat and *Yom Tov*, not on the day; the *Tosefta* describes the normal situation. The unusual scenario where one has forgotten — and therefore may still make *kiddush* during the day — is not the subject of the *Tosefta*'s formulation. The *Gemara*'s ruling stands: *bedieved*, if one did not make *kiddush* on Friday night, one may still do so on Shabbat day.

## Kiddush Priority

### Kvod Yom & Lyla

A further question: *kvod yom v'kvod layla* — if one has limited resources and must choose between a superior Friday night meal and a superior Shabbat day meal, which takes precedence? The ruling is *kvod yom kodem* — the honor of the day takes precedence. One should invest more in the Shabbat day meal. This reflects the halachic recognition that the day of Shabbat carries its own distinctive significance, amplified by considerations from *Kabbalistic* tradition.

But what if one has only a single cup of wine? The *Gemara* rules: *omer alav kiddush hayom mipnei she'kiddush hayom kodem l'kvod yom* — one uses that cup on Friday night for *kiddush*, because the *kiddush* of Shabbat takes priority even over the honor-of-day principle that would otherwise favor the daytime meal. The word *kiddush hayom* here refers to the Friday night *kiddush* — the sanctification of Shabbat — not to the daytime *kiddush*. The question was whether to use the single cup of wine for *kiddush* on Friday night or to save it for the Shabbat day meal, making the meal more festive. The ruling is to use it for *kiddush*.

### Proper Time

*V'ei issa* — but if it were truly possible to make the Friday night *kiddush* during the day, one could save the single cup, use it for both the day meal and the *kiddush*, and achieve two outcomes with one cup. Why not adopt this strategy? The *Gemara* answers: *amar lei, chaviva mitzvah b'sha'atah* — a *mitzvah* is dear in its proper time. Even though *bedieved* one may make *kiddush* during the day, the ideal performance of the *mitzvah* is at its designated time. We do not deliberately defer a *mitzvah* from its proper moment to achieve an economy of means. *Kiddush* belongs to Friday night; the love and urgency with which Shabbat is greeted — the eagerness of its arrival — is expressed precisely by performing *kiddush* at that moment, not by manipulating it for convenience.

### Havdalah Economy

The *Gemara* then raises an apparent contradiction from the *Havdalah* case: *ve-hatanya* — a *Braisa* teaches that if one arrives home on *Motzei Shabbat* from *shul* — having already said *havdalah* in *Shmoneh Esrei*, which however does not discharge the *havdalah* that permits eating — and has only one cup of wine, one places it aside until after the meal: *menicho ad achar hamazon*, *u-meshalshelan kullan le-acharav* — one eats, benches, and only then says *havdalah* over the wine, chaining it onto *Birkat Hamazon*. The single cup serves both: after *Birkat Hamazon*, one recites *Borei Pri HaGafen* and flows directly into *havdalah*.

But here we *are* manipulating things to achieve two for the price of one, and we *are* permitting eating before *havdalah* in order to make this work. Why is *chaviva mitzvah b'sha'atah* not invoked here? Why don't we say: make *havdalah* immediately on the single cup of wine, don't worry about having wine for *Birkat Hamazon*, and do the *mitzvah* at its proper time? The *Gemara* is pointing out that we explicitly do not apply that principle in the *havdalah* case — we permit a delay, permit eating before *havdalah*, and arrange for the *havdalah* to follow *Birkat Hamazon*, all in order to use the single cup for both purposes. This apparently undermines the argument made for *kiddush*.

## Ayulei vs. Apukei

### Ayulei Yoma

*Shani lan bein ayulei yoma le-afukei yoma* — there is a fundamental distinction between ushering in the day and ushering it out. With respect to *ayulei yoma* — bringing in Shabbat — *kol kamma de-makdeminan leh adif u-mechabevinan leh*: the earlier we perform *kiddush*, the better, and we demonstrate our love for Shabbat by that eagerness. When Shabbat begins, one wants to show that one is embracing it, greeting it, declaring immediately that it has arrived. To postpone *kiddush* — to hold the cup of wine in reserve for the next day — would communicate the opposite: that one is not rushing to affirm Shabbat's arrival. *Chaviva mitzvah b'sha'atah* therefore has full force on the entry side. One does not defer *kiddush* even for a practical benefit.

### Apukei Yoma

With *apukei yoma* — sending Shabbat out — the calculus is reversed. *Machrinan leh ki heichi de'lo lehavei alan tituna* — we deliberately postpone *havdalah* so that Shabbat's departure should not feel like a burden being lifted, like we are rushing to be rid of it. There is an affirmative value in not hurrying Shabbat's conclusion. This connects to the general principle of extending Shabbat at its close — *tosefet Shabbat* at the outgoing end — and to the practice of lighting *Hanukkah* candles before making *havdalah* on *Motzei Shabbat*, which similarly signals that we are not eager to end Shabbat. The value of *chaviva mitzvah b'sha'atah* thus does not apply symmetrically: at entry, we press forward; at exit, we hold back.

### Havdalah Delay

This asymmetry justifies the strategic arrangement of the single-cup scenario on *Motzei Shabbat*: eat first, bench, and then make *havdalah* afterward — *menicho ad achar hamazon*. The delay is not mere convenience; it reflects the positive value of not rushing to conclude Shabbat. The *Borei Pri HaGafen* recited after *Birkat Hamazon* serves double duty, flowing directly into *havdalah*. One practical question: does the introductory paragraph of *havdalah* need to be said? Even without it, the arrangement works. And the meal one ate before *havdalah* is covered by the *bedieved* principle that if one has already eaten, *havdalah* can still be performed and is still valid — a point the *Gemara* will make explicit in the following section.

The same principle — that we are lenient about *havdalah* at the exit but strict about *kiddush* at the entry — is also why one who forgot *kiddush* on Friday night is told to make it on Shabbat day: *bedieved* it is valid, but every effort should have been made to perform it on Friday night, because *ayulei yoma* demands eagerness.

## Shemea Mina

### Havdalah on Cup

*Shema mina mehamasnisa tamni* — from this *Braisa* about coming home on *Motzei Shabbat* and having only one cup, eight things may be inferred. The first: *shema mina hamavdil be'tefila tzarich lehavdil al ha-kos* — one who has said *havdalah* in the *tefila* — in *Shmoneh Esrei* — must still say *havdalah* on a cup of wine. This is self-evidently implied by the *Braisa*: the person coming home from *shul* has already said *havdalah* in the *Shmoneh Esrei*, yet the *Braisa* describes him as needing to say *havdalah* at home. The *havdalah* in *davening* does not discharge the obligation; the *havdalah* that is *matir achila* must be said over the cup.

### Bracha Teuna Kos

The second inference: *shema mina bracha teuna kos* — *Birkat Hamazon* must be recited over a cup of wine. This is built into the very logic of the arrangement. The entire reason for deferring *havdalah* until after the meal — for eating first, benching, and then making *havdalah* — is that the cup of wine will be used for *Birkat Hamazon* as well. If *Birkat Hamazon* did not require a *kos*, there would be no reason to arrange things this way. The fact that the *Braisa* describes this strategic delay makes sense only if *Birkat Hamazon* requires a *kos shel bracha*.

### Minimum Shiur

The third inference: *kos shel bracha tzarich shiur* — the cup of blessing requires a minimum measure. If there were no minimum measure, one could solve the single-cup problem by pouring tiny amounts into two separate vessels and using each independently. The very fact that the *Braisa* treats this as a genuine problem — requiring a creative solution — implies that a *kos* must contain a minimum amount, which is a *revi'is*.

### Tasting Required

The fourth inference: *ha-mevarech tzarich she'yitom* — the one making the *bracha* must taste from the wine. This addresses a further potential solution: why not make *havdalah* over the cup, complete the *bracha*, and then set the wine aside untouched for *Birkat Hamazon*? If the person reciting the *bracha* were not required to drink from the wine, this would be a workable strategy. The fact that the *Braisa* does not contemplate it — and instead requires the elaborate arrangement of deferring *havdalah* — proves that tasting is obligatory. Tosafot notes that it need not be specifically the *mevarech* who drinks; someone present must taste. This reflects the tension between the *kos shel bracha*'s function as a *kos* designated for the *bracha* and the *birchat hanehen* dimension of the *Borei Pri HaGafen* recited over it.

### Tasting Disqualifies

The fifth inference: *ta'amo pagmo* — once one has tasted from the wine, the remaining wine in the cup is no longer usable for another *bracha*. This too follows from the logic of the *Braisa*. One might propose: have a *revi'is* and a half, drink a small amount for *havdalah*, and use the remainder for *Birkat Hamazon*. The *Braisa* does not suggest this solution, which implies it is unavailable. Once a sip has been taken, the remainder in the cup has been *pogem* — rendered unfit for use as a *kos shel bracha* in another context. Whether adding more wine to the cup would rehabilitate it is a further question; what is clear is that the wine remaining after tasting is treated as disqualified.

### Havdalah After Eating

The sixth inference: *ta'am mavdil* — *havdalah* is valid even after one has already eaten. The entire scenario of the *Braisa* involves a person who eats before making *havdalah* on the cup — because the single cup is being held in reserve for *Birkat Hamazon* and *havdalah* together. This *bedieved* scenario is presented without criticism of the eating itself (beyond the acknowledgment that one is not supposed to eat before *havdalah*), and *havdalah* is still performed and still valid at the end. The inference is clear: if one has eaten before *havdalah*, whether intentionally or not, one may still make *havdalah* afterward and discharge the obligation.

### Minyan Requirement

The seventh inference — that *Birkat Hamazon* requires a *kos* — invites Tosafot's important qualification. Tosafot observes: *ktzat mashma de'afilu be'yachid teuna kos* — the *Braisa* implies that even an individual benching alone requires a cup, since *ha-nichnas* — the person arriving home in the *Braisa* — appears to be alone. Tosafot then works through the question and concludes, tentatively, that perhaps the requirement applies specifically when there is a quorum of three. Yet Tosafot acknowledges that the straightforward reading of the *Gemara* — *shema mina bracha teuna kos* — suggests the requirement applies to any individual. In practice, of course, we do not regularly bench on a cup unless there is a *minyan* present. The gap between the *pshat* of the *Gemara* and common practice is notable. The reasoning offered by way of analogy — *ein omrim shira ela al ha-yayin*, that song requires wine — connects *Birkat Hamazon* to the category of formal, elevated religious expression that demands a cup. The full development of how this practice evolved, and why it is limited in practice to a *minyan*, remains a discussion for the *Rishonim*. To be continued.
