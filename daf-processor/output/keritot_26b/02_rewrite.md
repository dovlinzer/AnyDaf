# Keritot 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yediah & Nasi

### Abaye's Challenge

The discussion opens at the final line of 26a, where the question is how we know that if *yom kippur* atones for an *asham talui* and one subsequently becomes aware of the *chet*, a *chatas* must still be brought. The answer proposed until now is that the extra *yediah* mentioned by the *nasi* was unnecessary and therefore serves to teach this rule. Abaye, however, disagrees. He argues from the bottom of 26a: *idei lo ksiv yediah gabi Nasi, meyachid vetzibur lo asi* — had the Torah not written *yediah* by the *nasi*, one could not have derived it from the *yachid* and *tzibur* alone. Why? *Mishum de'ika lemifrach*: there is a refutation available. *Mah leyachid vetzibur sheken einan asuyin lehishtanos* — the *yachid* and *tzibur* cannot change their status; would you say the same of the *nasi*, who can be deposed? Rashi notes that for a *tzad hashaveh* argument, any point of difference is sufficient to invalidate it. Accordingly, Abaye maintains that the *yediah* written by the *nasi* was indeed necessary, and we therefore cannot extract a free derivation from it.

### Gzerah Shavah

How, then, does Abaye know the rule? *Ela amar Abaye mehacha*: since all three categories — *nasi*, *yachid*, and *tzibur* — are linked by a *gzeirah shavah* through the word *mitzvos*, they share the same rules. Once they are all learned from one another, the fact that each writes *yediah* is entirely redundant — *keivan deyalfei mehadadi, lamah li deksav shalosh yedi'os*? The Torah need only have written it once. Therefore, *im eino inyan legufan*, since you do not need the extra mentions for their primary purpose, *tenahu inyan heicha demisyada leh basar Yom HaKippurim* — apply them to the case where one becomes aware after *yom kippur*, teaching that *hoda demaysi Chatas*, one must still bring a *chatas*.

### YK vs Asham Talui

At this point the Gemara raises a further question. Perhaps what we have demonstrated is only that awareness after *yom kippur* obligates a *chatas* — because *yom kippur* did not come specifically for this sin, its *kapparah* is generic, and when one later becomes aware, one must bring. But by an *asham talui*, which *de'al da'ata decheit asi* — which was brought specifically for this doubtful sin — perhaps it atones completely, such that if one becomes aware afterward, *lo maysi Chatas*? This is a striking point, because the rule that an *asham talui* does not wipe away the underlying sin has been assumed throughout the tractate; here the Gemara is pressing on the basis for that assumption.

### Asham Talui Function

*Amar Rava amar kra o hodah elav mikol makom* — Rava derives from the phrase *o hodah*, with its emphatic *o*, that in whatever circumstance one becomes aware, even after an *asham talui*, a *chatas* is still required. One might add, based on Abaye's reading, that two of the three extra *hodas* are available and one could have served this function — yet for some reason the Gemara does not take that approach and instead follows Rava's derivation from *o hodah*.

This then reopens the question already visited in the previous discussion: *Asham Talui lamah ba?* If it neither atones for the sin nor discharges the obligation, what does it accomplish? Rabbi Zeira answers: *she'im meis meis belo avon* — it provides atonement in the event that one dies having never become aware and therefore never brought a *chatas*. Rava objects: *meis? Misah memarekes* — death itself atones, so why is the *asham talui* needed for that purpose? *Ella amar Rava: lehagin alav min hayissurin* — it protects from punishment. It does not address the sin itself; it addresses one's state of guilt that would otherwise draw punishment. *Yom kippur*, when it arrives, similarly serves *magen mishum hayissurin*. The emphasis throughout is that the *asham talui* operates not by wiping away the sin but by something else entirely — addressing guilt or protecting from punishment — which is precisely why subsequent awareness still requires a *chatas*.

## Woman's Chatas

### Rav's Position

The Mishnah now turns — though in fact it doubles back to reunite two scenarios that were split across distant *mishnayos* — to the case of a woman who brought a *chatas ha'of* on doubt, uncertain whether she had given birth. A prior *mishnah* had addressed the case where she became aware before the *melikah* that she had definitely given birth: the offering proceeds as a *chatas vadai*, since *mi she-meivi al lo hoda meivi al hoda* — the same bird brought for doubt serves equally for certainty.

What was not discussed in that earlier *mishnah* is what happens if she becomes aware after the *melikah*. Unlike *asham talui* and *asham vadai*, which are distinct legal categories in the Torah, there is only one category of *chatas* — she simply did not know at the time whether she was obligated. Now that she knows she is obligated, why should she not be *yotzei* with what was already brought? *Amar Rav*: *mazzeh damah u-motzeh damah, v'kipprah u-muteres ba'achilah* — proceed, apply the blood, and it atones; you may eat it.

The text of our *mishnah* then requires clarification. The Gemara interprets Rav as addressing the scenario where *noda* that she was indeed obligated — found out she had given birth — and therefore the offering functions as a valid *chatas*. The reason the *mishnah* says to bury it would then be, on his reading, that no one was guarding it against *tumah* during the interval of uncertainty. The Gemara presses this: *eimas lo mishtameres?* When was it unguarded? Before the *shechitah* it was alive and cannot contract *tumah*; after the *melikah*, *ka minatar lah* — it is being guarded. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ella masnisin benoda lah delo yaldah* — our *mishnah* is in fact the case where she discovers she had not given birth. The offering is *chullin ba'azarah* and is buried. The note that this is *miderabbanan* reflects the position, cited by Rashi, that the *issur achilah* of *chullin ba'azarah* may not be fully *de'oraisa*, though the practical consequence — burial rather than feeding to a dog — follows from its being treated as *chullin ba'azarah*. Rashi raises the strange suggestion that *melikah* might not constitute *chullin ba'azarah*, though the *Shita Mekubetzes* understands the passage as simply reflecting the view that the *issur hana'ah* in this case is at least rabbinic.

### Maris Ha'ayin

Rabbi Yochanan's position differs from Rav's. While he agrees that if she learns after *melikah* that she was indeed obligated she would be *yotzei*, he rules *asurah ba'achilah* — it may not be eaten. The reason: *gezeirah shema yomru chatas ha'of haba'ah al hasafek ne'echeles* — onlookers who see the *kohen* eating will not know that she learned mid-process of her obligation, and will conclude that a *chatas ha'of* brought on doubt may be eaten. One might ask how this concern is realistic when it is only *kohanim* eating at the end of the day, all partaking of the same birds — it is indeed a good question, and the concern about misleading appearances is nevertheless maintained.

### Before/After Melikah

A *baraisa* of Levi is cited in support of Rav: if after *melikah* she learns she had given birth, one applies the blood and it atones, and it may be eaten. A separate *baraisa* aligns with Rabbi Yochanan. That *baraisa* systematically covers both scenarios — before and after *melikah*. Before *melikah*, if she learns she did not give birth: *teitzei l'chullin* — it returns to non-sacred status, or it may be sold to someone who needs to bring a bird *korban*. If before *melikah* she learns she did give birth: *vadai te'aseh vadai* — proceed as a definite *chatas*, since whether she did not know or does know, it is the same *korban*.

### Safek Status Sealed

After *melikah*, if she learns she gave birth: *asurah afilu b'hana'ah*. The language here is striking — *she-al hasafek ba'ah mit'chila kipper sefeika v'halcha lah*. This is the same formula used in the contexts of *egla arufa* and *asham talui*, where those offerings address the state of doubt at the time and their status is sealed accordingly. In truth, those contexts involve atoning for a doubtful sin; here the purpose was to allow her to become *tehora*. The formula is borrowed and does not map perfectly. The basic point, though, is that the offering fulfilled its function, and we will not permit eating it even after the fact, both because of concerns about misleading appearances and — as Rashi reads the language — perhaps because it retains its *safek* status in a more fundamental way, possibly *me-ikar ha-din* rather than merely *miderabbanan*.

## Two Rams for Asham

### Sufficient Ram

The *mishnah* establishes that one who sets aside two *sela'im* for an *asham* and purchases two rams at one *sela* each — so that initially neither appears to meet the minimum — may nonetheless bring the one that is worth two *sela'im* for his *asham*. The Torah does not specify how much one must spend; it specifies how much the animal must be worth, as Tosafos observes: even a gifted ram could in principle qualify, since the requirement speaks to value, not expenditure. The second ram, which carries *kedushat asham* but is not needed, is left to graze until it acquires a *mum*, is then redeemed, and the proceeds go toward a *nedava* — specifically an *olah*, since *mosar asham* is brought as an *olah*. One might ask whether this second ram could be saved until it is worth two *sela'im* and used for a future *asham* or transferred to another person's obligation, but the general principle — analogous to the rule that a *chatas* designated for one sin cannot be applied to another — would seem to preclude that.

### Misuse & Meilah

The *mishnah* then turns to the scenario where the person took the sanctified money and used it to buy two rams for his own secular use — *chullin*. This constitutes *meilah*, misuse of consecrated property. The discussion of *meilah* here serves as a transition toward the next *masechet*: the obligation of *meilah* requires repayment of the principal plus a *chomesh* — a fifth added to the base, which is in practice a quarter of the total repaid — as well as the bringing of an *asham me'ilos*, which likewise requires an animal worth two *shekel*.

The scenario constructed is: one *sela* was stolen from the consecrated money (worth four *zuz*), and the ram purchased with that and related funds turns out to be worth ten *zuz*. The arithmetic is deliberate: two *shekel* (eight *zuz*) plus a *chomesh* (twenty percent of eight, which is the extra two, bringing the total repayment to ten) equals ten *zuz*. The ten-*zuz* ram is brought as repayment toward the original *asham* obligation — the money goes back to the purpose from which it was taken. The eight-*zuz* ram — the one worth exactly two *shekel* — goes toward the *asham me'ilos*, since by committing *meilah* one has incurred a fresh obligation requiring an *asham* of minimum two *shekel*. The logic is that the two obligations happen to be satisfiable with the very animals purchased through the stolen money.

### One Sufficient, One Not

When one of the two rams is worth two *sela'im* and the other is worth ten *zuz*, the *mishnah* directs: *hayafeh shenei sela'im yikarev le'ashamo* — the one of sufficient value is brought for the *asham me'ilos*, and the one worth ten *zuz* — which represents the principal plus twenty percent — goes toward *leme'ilato*, the payback for the original misappropriation. The key principle, derived from *ve'et asher chata min hakodesh yeshalem ve'et chamishito yosef alav*, is that the principal and the *chomesh* are paid back together to the same source from which they were taken. The extra fifth does not get redirected toward one's own *asham* obligation; it belongs to whatever *hekdesh* account was drawn upon.

### Divided Usage

The final scenario in the *mishnah* envisions a case where one ram was legitimately designated as the *asham* and one was taken for secular use — *echad le'asham ve'echad lechullin*. One can imagine a concrete case: the man finds a ram worth two *shekel* selling for one, buys it for his *asham*, and then uses his spare *shekel* to buy something for his flock. If *shel asham yafeh shtei sela'im* — the *asham* ram is worth two *sela'im* — it is brought for the original obligation. The second ram, which represents the *meilah*, serves as the *asham me'ilos* assuming it too is worth two *shekel*. Additionally, *veyavi imah sela vechumshah* — he must separately repay the *shekel* he misappropriated plus its *chomesh*. Since the original *asham* was already brought, the repaid money cannot be used for another *asham* designated for the same sin; instead, as the *halacha* of *mosar asham* dictates, it would be used toward an *olah*.

## Meilah Terminology

### Reisha vs Seifa

The Gemara notices a terminological inconsistency within the *mishnah*. In the first case (*reisha*), the ten-*zuz* ram is described as going *leme'ilato* — for his *meilah* — meaning it represents the repayment of what was stolen. Yet in the final case (*seifa*), *hasheini limeilato* refers to bringing the second ram as an *asham me'ilos* — the obligatory *korban* for having committed *meilah*. The same word *meilah* thus designates two opposite things: in the *reisha*, the payback; in the *seifa*, the *korban* obligation.

### Chomesh Returns

The Gemara resolves this by articulating the underlying principle: *ve'et asher chata min hakodesh yeshalem ve'et chamishito yosef alav* — the *chomesh* is restored together with the principal, both returning to the original source. The extra fifth does not constitute an independent resource for satisfying other *korban* obligations; it is part of the restitution package. In the *reisha*, where the ten-*zuz* ram physically embodies both the principal and the *chomesh* in animal form, it is natural to call that ram *meilato* — the thing by which the *meilah* is paid back. *Keren v'chomesh* — principal and fifth — are brought together, and the animal therefore represents the *gezeilah*, the stolen amount. In the *seifa*, since the repayment is handled separately in coin form rather than through an animal, the word *meilato* shifts to refer to the *korban* obligation that arose from the act of *meilah*.

### Language Shifts

The Gemara formalizes the distinction: *reisha kari lei l'meiloso gezeilo* — in the first case, the word *meilato* means the stolen item being paid back; *seifa kari lei l'meiloso el asham* — at the end, it means the *asham me'ilos* itself. The principle organizing these usages is that *meilato* attaches to whichever obligation is being discharged in animal form. In the *reisha*, the ram *is* the repayment; in the *seifa*, the repayment is in coin and the ram *is* the *asham*. The usage shifts accordingly, and the twenty percent, in every instance, flows back to the function from which it was taken — if the original *korban* had already been brought, the funds become an *olah* as *mosar asham*.

## Shevach Hekdesh

### Rav Menashya's Q

Rav Menashya bar Gada poses a question: *bekinnus chumshin mahu?* — if one has accumulated multiple *chomeshim* from repeated acts of *meilah* against the same sanctified fund, may one use those collected fifths to satisfy the minimum value requirement of a *korban*? The scenario, following the *Shita Mekubetzes*, is where the accumulated fifths reach a full *shekel* — through multiple cycles of misappropriation and repayment — and the question is whether that *shekel*, now being paid back as it were to the fund from which it came, can also serve to satisfy the base cost of a *korban* obligation.

The question is framed against the backdrop of *shevach hekdesh* — the concept of natural appreciation of sanctified property. Normally, *shevach hekdesh* would arise when an animal sanctified as a *korban* gains weight and value after sanctification. Here, the "appreciation" of the *hekdesh* fund comes not from organic growth but from the obligation to pay back more than what was taken, by operation of the *halacha* of *meilah*. The question has two faces. One might say: even if *shevach hekdesh* generally works, here the increase did not come from investment or natural growth — *d'lo tara*, one did not make an effort — and therefore it cannot be used to gain *kapparah*, only the original *kedusha* can. Or one might say: even if natural growth does not work, here the money was directly sanctified for this purpose — *d'afrishei* — and therefore it should work.

### General Question

This frames the general question posed to the *beis medrash*: *mitkaper adam b'shevach hekdesh o lo?* Can a person achieve *kapparah* through the natural appreciation of a sanctified animal — that is, if an animal was sanctified when it was worth less than the required minimum, and it subsequently grew in value to meet the minimum, may it be brought as a valid *korban*?

### Mishnah Support

The Gemara first attempts to answer from our *mishnah* itself. If one of the rams purchased at one *sela* turns out to be worth two — *yikarev l'ashamo*, it is brought for the *asham* — perhaps this is a case of *shevach hekdesh*: the animal was bought for four *zuz* and appreciated to eight? This would demonstrate *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara rejects this reading: *d'ozil ro'eh gabei* — the shepherd gave a discount. At the moment of sanctification the animal was already worth two *shekel*, even though only one was paid. It is therefore not a case of post-sanctification appreciation at all — the requirement has always been about worth, not price.

### Fattening Tosefta

The Gemara turns to an explicit *Tosefta*: *lakach ayil b'sela u-pitmo v'he'emido al shtayim* — he bought a ram for one *shekel*, fattened it, and brought it to a value of two *shekel*. *Kasher* — it is valid. This appears to confirm *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara immediately qualifies: *pitmo shani* — fattening is different, because one invested one's own money into the animal. If the ram cost four *zuz* and the investment of fattening cost another four *zuz*, one has effectively spent eight *zuz* out of pocket; it is not *shevach hekdesh* in the sense of free appreciation, it is a direct investment. The Gemara then considers the *seifa* of that same *Tosefta*: *lakach ayil b'arba'ah v'ashkachei b'tlata v'shavei tamnya* — one bought it for four, invested only three more, yet it is worth eight. Here the single extra *zuz* of appreciation beyond investment is genuine *shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara will eventually come around to acknowledging this constitutes *shevach hekdesh*, but at this stage it maintains that the case is still one of investment.

### Payment Difference

The *Tosefta* continues: *v'yashlim sela* — even though the *korban* works, one must pay back a *sela* (or, as the Gemara clarifies, *tashlumei d'sela* — the remainder of the *sela*, meaning whatever portion was not invested, as little as one *zuz*). This halacha appears strange: if the animal now genuinely meets the minimum value requirement, why must any additional payment be made? The Gemara's answer is: *gezeirah shema yomru haya pachot mishtei sela'im mechaper* — people will see that someone spent less than two *shekel* and conclude that an *asham* of insufficient value can achieve *kapparah*. The requirement to pay the difference is therefore not a function of the *korban* being invalid — it is fully valid; one is indeed *mitkaper* — but a rabbinic *gezeirah* against creating misleading impressions.

One might press: how would anyone know how much was spent? The answer is that people always talk about bargains. In any case, the practical *halacha* is that even if the animal improved entirely by itself without any investment, *me-ikar ha-din* it achieves *kapparah*, and one must nonetheless pay the difference out of pocket to avoid the misleading appearance that a subminimum *asham* was sufficient.

### Elazar & Yochanan

A *tanna'im*-framed *baraisa* then states directly: *de'ein adam miskaper beshevach Hekdesh*. The Gemara immediately resolves the difficulty: this does not render the *korban* invalid, as the prior analysis demonstrated. Rather, as already established, one must pay the shortfall separately. *Le'olam kasavar adam mitkaper beshevach Hekdesh*, and the payment is purely a rabbinic requirement to forestall misleading impressions — the fundamental *din* is that *shevach hekdesh* functions for *kapparah*.

When Rabbi Elazar asks Rabbi Yochanan this very question — *adam mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh o lo?* — Rabbi Yochanan responds with characteristic astonishment: *kamma shanim gadal zeh beineinu velo shama halakha zu mimeni?* — how many years have you been with us and you never heard me teach this *halacha*? The implication, *mikhlal de'amar Rabbi Yochanan in*, is that Rabbi Yochanan rules yes, one is *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*.

### Todah Child

The source Rabbi Yochanan relies upon is: *vlad todah u-tmuratah* — the offspring of a *todah* or a *temurah*, which by themselves do not require bread, but *la-achar kapara* — after the mother has been brought for the obligation — are brought without bread; whereas *lifnei kapara* — before the mother has been brought — the child may serve as the *todah* itself and is brought *taun lechem*, with the full bread complement. This is the ruling sent by Rabbi Chanina in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. What one sees is that the offspring of the sanctified animal — a natural increase after sanctification — can be used to fulfill the *todah* obligation. The birth is a form of *shevach hekdesh*, and it clearly functions to discharge the obligation. *Alma kasavar: miskaper b'shevach hekdesh.*

## Living Animals Q's

### Nidachim Question

Following the pattern established in the prior exchange, Rabbi Elazar now poses a further question to Rabbi Yochanan: *ba'alei chayim nidachim o lo?* — can a living animal be rendered *nidach*, stigmatized and permanently disqualified, or does *dichui* apply only after death? Rabbi Yochanan again responds: *harei kamma shanim gadal zeh beineinu velo shamata halacha zu mimeni?* — you have been with us all these years and never heard me teach this? *Miklal d'Rabbi Yochanan amar in* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that even a living animal can be rendered *nidach*.

### Half-Ownership Case

Rabbi Yochanan's ruling on *ba'alei chayim nidachim* derives from the following case: *behema shel shnei shutfin hikdish chetzyo* — an animal owned by two partners, where one sanctified his half. Since half an animal cannot be brought as a *korban*, the *kedusha* cannot function at the *mizbe'ach* level; it is merely *kedushat damim*, a monetary *kedusha*. The partner then buys back the other half and sanctifies that half as well. The result: *kedusha ve-eina kreiva* — it has sanctity but may not be brought, because the initial sanctification already stigmatized it.

### Three Conclusions

*Shma mina tlat* — three conclusions are drawn. First, *ba'alei chayim nidachim* — even a living animal can be *nidach*: the animal here is alive, yet it is permanently disqualified. Second, *kedushat damim madcha* — even a *kedusha* that was never *kedushat mizbe'ach* from its inception can create *dichui*: the initial sanctification was only *kedushat damim* at the time, yet it stigmatized the animal. Third, *yesh dichui be-damim* — *dichui* can exist even at the level of *kedushat damim* alone. Rashi observes, and notes that Tosafos typically wrestles with distinguishing the second and third conclusions in cases of this recurring *sugyah*, that the two are in essence the same point: the novelty is that *dichui* operates even when the animal was never actually fit to be brought as a *korban mizbe'ach*.

## Minimum Value Q

### Unavailable Value

Rabbi Elazar poses yet another question, this one connecting directly back to the *mishnah*'s framework: *huzlu tlayim ba-olam mahu?* — if rams become so cheap that none are available at a value of two *shekel*, what is one to do? The *asham* requires an animal worth two *shekel*, but we have established that the requirement is about worth, not price. If no animal of the right age meets the minimum value, can one bring the best available and satisfy the requirement on the grounds of *mivchar nidreichem ba'inan* — we require one's best, and this is the best there is? Or does the Torah's formulation of *kesef shekalim ba'inan* — a specific monetary minimum is required — constitute an absolute threshold that cannot be bypassed?

### Absolute Minimum

Rabbi Yochanan responds — though notably, he says *lo limadnu halacha zu*, we have never taught this *halacha* in the *beis medrash* — with a teaching in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *mipnei ma lo nitna kitzva be-mechusrei kapara?* Why did the Torah not specify a minimum value for the *korban* brought by those requiring *kapparah* to permit eating *kodashim* — the *mechasrei kapara* category? Because *shema yuzlu telaim ve-ein lahem takana le-echol be-kodashim* — if rams became cheap and one could not satisfy a minimum, they would be permanently barred from *kodashim*. The Torah therefore intentionally omitted a minimum for that category precisely to avoid that outcome. By implication, in the categories *where* the Torah did specify a minimum — such as *asham* — even if animals become so cheap that the minimum cannot be met, one is simply stuck. The minimum is absolute.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye challenges this reasoning: *ela me-ata chatas chelev tinasen bah kitzva* — if the concern about *mechasrei kapara* is that a minimum would strand people from *kodashim*, the same logic would suggest a minimum for a *chatas chelev* should also be absent. After all, a *chatas* is also purely about *kapparah*, not about restoring eligibility to eat *kodashim*. And yet there is no minimum for a *chatas* either — *ve-lav le-ishtruyei be-achilas kodashim hu*. The argument from *mechasrei kapara* does not cleanly explain why *asham* has a minimum while *chatas* does not.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava presses further: *ela me-ata asham nazir le-havei bei kitzva* — among the various *ashamos*, why does an *asham nazir* not have a minimum? *De-levatala hu de-asi* — an *asham nazir* is unique in that nothing depends on it: it is a *chiyuv*, but it does not unlock *tahara*, does not initiate or conclude the *nezirus*, nothing pivots on it. Rabbi Yochanan himself states in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *ein lach davar she-ba le-vatala ela asham nazir bilvad* — the only *korban* that functions purely as an obligation without anything depending on it is the *asham nazir*. If the logic is that a minimum is tolerable when delay causes no irreversible harm, then by *asham nazir* — where nothing at all depends on it — a minimum should have been imposed. Yet it was not.

### Why Asham Minimum

*Kashya.* The Gemara leaves the question unresolved. The reasoning advanced — that the Torah omitted minimums where they would cause permanent harm, implying that where a minimum exists it is absolute — does not coherently explain the distribution of minimums across the various *korban* categories. Virtually nothing except certain *ashamos* has a minimum value requirement; the exceptional case is not the absence of a minimum but its presence. The more pointed question, as the Gemara's trajectory reveals, is not why *mechasrei kapara* has no minimum, but why the *asham* specifically was singled out to have one at all. That question remains open.
