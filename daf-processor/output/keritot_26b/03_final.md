# Keritot 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yediah & Nasi

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּלָא כְּתִיב יְדִיעָה גַּבֵּי נָשִׂיא, מִיָּחִיד וְצִבּוּר לָא אָתֵי, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמִיפְרַךְ: מָה לְיָחִיד וְצִבּוּר שֶׁכֵּן אֵין עֲשׂוּיִין לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת, תֹּאמַר בְּנָשִׂיא שֶׁעָשׂוּי לְהִשְׁתַּנּוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** **If** the Torah **had not stated** a term of **knowing with regard to a *Nasi*, it** could **not** have been **derived from** the cases of **an individual and** the **community, because** this suggestion **can be refuted: What** is unique **about** the sin offerings of **an individual and** the **community?** They are unique **in that** both are set offerings that **are not apt to change.** Can **you say** the same **with regard to a *Nasi*, whose** offering **is apt to change** if he leaves his position, e.g., when he becomes impure as a leper (see *Horayot* 10a), as in such a case he brings the sin offering of an individual?

The discussion opens at the final line of 26a, where the question is how we know that if *yom kippur* atones for an *asham talui* and one subsequently becomes aware of the *chet*, a *chatas* must still be brought. The answer proposed until now is that the extra *yediah* mentioned by the *nasi* was unnecessary and therefore serves to teach this rule. Abaye, however, disagrees. He argues: *idei lo ksiv yediah gabi Nasi, meyachid vetzibur lo asi* — had the Torah not written *yediah* by the *nasi*, one could not have derived it from the *yachid* and *tzibur* alone. Why? *Mishum de'ika lemifrach*: there is a refutation available. *Mah leyachid vetzibur sheken einan asuyin lehishtanos* — the *yachid* and *tzibur* cannot change their status; would you say the same of the *nasi*, who can be deposed? Rashi notes that for a *tzad hashaveh* argument, any point of difference is sufficient to invalidate it. Accordingly, Abaye maintains that the *yediah* written by the *nasi* was indeed necessary, and we therefore cannot extract a free derivation from it.

### Gzerah Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, מֵהָכָא: מִכְּדֵי ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״ יָלְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּיָלְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי, לְמָה לִי דִּכְתִיב שָׁלֹשׁ יְדִיעוֹת גַּבֵּי יָחִיד וְגַבֵּי נָשִׂיא וְגַבֵּי צִבּוּר? אִם אֵינוֹ עִנְיָן לְגוּפֵיהֶן, דְּהָא גָּמְרִי לְהוֹן ״מִצְוֹת״ ״מִצְוֹת״, תְּנֵהוּ עִנְיָן הֵיכָא דְּמִתְיְידַע לֵיהּ בָּתַר יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said:** The *halakha* that Yom Kippur does not atone for one who is liable to bring a sin offering can be derived **from here: Since** there is a verbal analogy between the word **"mitzvot"** mentioned with regard to the sin offerings of an individual and the word **"mitzvot"** mentioned with regard to a *Nasi* and with regard to the community, the *halakhot* of these three cases **are derived from one another.** And **since** the *halakhot* of these cases **are derived from one another, why do I** need a term of **knowing to be written three** separate times, **with regard to an individual, and with regard to a *Nasi*, and with regard to** the **community? If** this term of knowing **is not** necessary for the **matters of** these sin offerings **themselves,** to teach that one brings a sin offering only for a known sin, **as** this **is** already **derived** from the verbal analogy of **"mitzvot"** and **"mitzvot," apply it** to the **matter** of a case **where** his sin **becomes known to him** only **after Yom Kippur.** The *halakha* in that case would therefore be **that he brings a sin offering.**

How, then, does Abaye know the rule? *Ela amar Abaye mehacha*: since all three categories — *nasi*, *yachid*, and *tzibur* — are linked by a *gzeirah shavah* through the word *mitzvos*, they share the same rules. Once they are all learned from one another, the fact that each writes *yediah* is entirely redundant — *keivan deyalfei mehadadi, lamah li deksav shalosh yedi'os*? The Torah need only have written it once. Therefore, *im eino inyan legufan*, since you do not need the extra mentions for their primary purpose, *tenahu inyan heicha demisyada leh basar Yom HaKippurim* — apply them to the case where one becomes aware after *yom kippur*, teaching that *hoda demaysi Chatas*, one must still bring a *chatas*.

### YK vs Asham Talui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא כִּי מִתְיְידַע לֵיהּ בָּתַר יוֹם כִּפּוּרִים הוּא דְּמַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת, מִשּׁוּם דְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים לָאו עַל הָדֵין חֵטְא קָא אָתֵי, אֲבָל אָשָׁם תָּלוּי, דְּעַל הָדֵין חֵטְא קָאָתֵי, אֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי דְּמִיכַּפַּר, דְּכִי מִתְיְידַע [לֵיהּ] לְבָתַר דְּקָא מַיְיתֵי אָשָׁם תָּלוּי לָא מַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: But **say** that **it is** only **when** his sin **becomes known to him after Yom Kippur that he brings a sin offering, because Yom Kippur did not come** to atone **for that** specific **sin. But** with regard to **a provisional guilt offering, which comes** to atone **for that** specific **sin, say indeed that he has achieved atonement.** Therefore, **if** his sin **becomes known to him after he brought a provisional guilt offering** he should **not bring a sin offering.**

At this point the Gemara raises a further question. Perhaps what we have demonstrated is only that awareness after *yom kippur* obligates a *chatas* — because *yom kippur* did not come specifically for this sin, its *kapparah* is generic, and when one later becomes aware, one must bring. But by an *asham talui*, which *de'al da'ata decheit asi* — which was brought specifically for this doubtful sin — perhaps it atones completely, such that if one becomes aware afterward, *lo maysi Chatas*? This is a striking point, because the rule that an *asham talui* does not wipe away the underlying sin has been assumed throughout the tractate; here the Gemara is pressing on the basis for that assumption.

### Asham Talui Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: אֲמַר קְרָא: ״אוֹ הוֹדַע אֵלָיו״ – מִכׇּל מָקוֹם. הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר: כִּי מִתְיְידַע לֵיהּ מַיְיתֵי חַטָּאת, אָשָׁם תָּלוּי לָמָּה בָּא? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: שֶׁאִם מֵת, מֵת בְּלֹא עָוֹן. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: מֵת, מִיתָה מְמָרֶקֶת! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: לְהָגֵן עָלָיו מִן הַיִּיסּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** in explanation that **the verse states:** "If his sin that he has sinned **be known to him"** (Leviticus 4:28), indicating that one is obligated to bring a sin offering **in any case** where his sin becomes known to him. The Gemara asks: **Now that you say** that **when** one's sin **becomes known to him he brings a sin offering, why does a provisional guilt offering come? Rabbi Zeira said:** The reason is **that if** one **dies** before his sin becomes known to him, he **dies without iniquity. Rava objects to this** claim: If he **dies, death** itself **absolves** him, and therefore there is no need for a provisional guilt offering. **Rather, Rava said:** The reason that a provisional guilt offering is brought is in order **to protect him from suffering** until he brings his sin offering.

*Amar Rava amar kra o hodah elav mikol makom* — Rava derives from the phrase *o hodah*, with its emphatic *o*, that in whatever circumstance one becomes aware, even after an *asham talui*, a *chatas* is still required. One might add, based on Abaye's reading, that two of the three extra *hodas* are available and one could have served this function — yet for some reason the Gemara does not take that approach and instead follows Rava's derivation from *o hodah*.

This then reopens the question already visited in the previous discussion: *Asham Talui lamah ba?* If it neither atones for the sin nor discharges the obligation, what does it accomplish? Rabbi Zeira answers: *she'im meis meis belo avon* — it provides atonement in the event that one dies having never become aware and therefore never brought a *chatas*. Rava objects: *meis? Misah memarekes* — death itself atones, so why is the *asham talui* needed for that purpose? *Ella amar Rava: lehagin alav min hayissurin* — it protects from punishment. It does not address the sin itself; it addresses one's state of guilt that would otherwise draw punishment. *Yom kippur*, when it arrives, similarly serves *magen mishum hayissurin*. The emphasis throughout is that the *asham talui* operates not by wiping away the sin but by something else entirely — addressing guilt or protecting from punishment — which is precisely why subsequent awareness still requires a *chatas*.

## Woman's Chatas

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף הַבָּא עַל הַסָּפֵק. אָמַר רַב: וְכִיפֵּר. אִי הָכִי, אַמַּאי תִּקָּבֵר? לְפִי שֶׁאֵינָהּ מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת. אֵימַת לֹא מִשְׁתַּמֶּרֶת? אִי מֵעִיקָּרָא – חַיָּה הָוְיָא! אִי לְבַסּוֹף – קָמְנַטַּר לַהּ! אֶלָּא מַתְנִיתִין בְּנוֹדַע לָהּ דְּלֹא יָלְדָה, וּבְדִין הוּא דְּמוּתֶּרֶת בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמַאי ״תִּקָּבֵר״ – מִדְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a bird sin offering that is brought due to uncertainty,** e.g., it is uncertain if she gave birth to a non-viable newborn, which results in an obligation to bring an offering, or if it was an amorphous mass, which does not, if it became known to her after the nape of the neck of the bird was pinched, the bird must be buried. It is assumed at this point that the phrase: It became known to her, is referring to a case where it became known to her that she certainly gave birth in a manner for which she is obligated to bring the sin offering of a woman after childbirth. In this context, **Rav says: And** the bird **atoned** for her after its blood was sprinkled and squeezed out on the wall of the altar. The Gemara asks: **If so,** that the bird atones, then it should be eaten by the priests, in accordance with the *halakha* of a bird sacrificed as a sin offering. **Why,** then, does the mishna state that **it must be buried?** The Gemara answers: **Since** a bird brought due to uncertainty is unfit to be eaten, the priest diverted his attention from the bird, and **it** was left **unguarded.** Therefore, there is a concern that the meat became impure. The Gemara asks: **When** was **it** left **unguarded? If** this means it was unguarded **at the outset,** before the pinching of the nape, the bird **was** still **alive,** and a living animal is not susceptible to ritual impurity. **If** this means **later,** after the pinching of the nape, the priest **guards it** at that stage. **Rather, the mishna** is referring to a case **where it became known to her that she did not give birth** to a non-viable newborn and therefore she did not need to bring an offering.**And by right** it should have been **permitted to derive benefit** from the bird, as it is non-sacred. **And** if so, **what** is the reason for the mishna's statement that **it must be buried?** This obligation applies **by rabbinic law,** to prevent people from mistakenly thinking that it is permitted to derive benefit from a bird brought as a sin offering due to uncertainty.

The Mishnah now turns — though in fact it doubles back to reunite two scenarios that were split across distant *mishnayos* — to the case of a woman who brought a *chatas ha'of* on doubt, uncertain whether she had given birth. A prior *mishnah* had addressed the case where she became aware before the *melikah* that she had definitely given birth: the offering proceeds as a *chatas vadai*, since *mi she-meivi al lo hoda meivi al hoda* — the same bird brought for doubt serves equally for certainty.

What was not discussed in that earlier *mishnah* is what happens if she becomes aware after the *melikah*. Unlike *asham talui* and *asham vadai*, which are distinct legal categories in the Torah, there is only one category of *chatas* — she simply did not know at the time whether she was obligated. Now that she knows she is obligated, why should she not be *yotzei* with what was already brought? *Amar Rav*: *mazzeh damah u-motzeh damah, v'kipprah u-muteres ba'achilah* — proceed, apply the blood, and it atones; you may eat it.

The text of our *mishnah* then requires clarification. The Gemara interprets Rav as addressing the scenario where *noda* that she was indeed obligated — found out she had given birth — and therefore the offering functions as a valid *chatas*. The reason the *mishnah* says to bury it would then be, on his reading, that no one was guarding it against *tumah* during the interval of uncertainty. The Gemara presses this: *eimas lo mishtameres?* When was it unguarded? Before the *shechitah* it was alive and cannot contract *tumah*; after the *melikah*, *ka minatar lah* — it is being guarded. The Gemara therefore concludes: *ella masnisin benoda lah delo yaldah* — our *mishnah* is in fact the case where she discovers she had not given birth. The offering is *chullin ba'azarah* and is buried. The note that this is *miderabbanan* reflects the position, cited by Rashi, that the *issur achilah* of *chullin ba'azarah* may not be fully *de'oraisa*, though the practical consequence — burial rather than feeding to a dog — follows from its being treated as *chullin ba'azarah*. Rashi raises the strange suggestion that *melikah* might not constitute *chullin ba'azarah*, though the *Shita Mekubetzes* understands the passage as simply reflecting the view that the *issur hana'ah* in this case is at least rabbinic.

### Maris Ha'ayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי אִיתְּמַר דְּרַב, עַל הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהֵבִיאָה חַטַּאת הָעוֹף בְּסָפֵק, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִמְלְקָה נוֹדַע לָהּ שֶׁיָּלְדָה וַדַּאי – תַּעֲשֶׂה וַדַּאי, שֶׁמִּמִּין שֶׁהֵבִיאָה עַל לֹא הוֹדַע – מְבִיאָה עַל הוֹדַע. אִם מִשֶּׁנִּמְלְקָה נוֹדַע שֶׁיָּלְדָה – אָמַר רַב: מַזֶּה דָּמָהּ וּמוֹצֶה דָּמָהּ, וְכִפְּרָה, [וּ]מוּתֶּרֶת בַּאֲכִילָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲסוּרָה בַּאֲכִילָה, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ: חַטַּאת הָעוֹף הַבָּאָה עַל הַסָּפֵק נֶאֱכֶלֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** statement **of Rav,** that she receives atonement, **was stated with regard to** the ruling of a previous mishna (22b): In the case of **a woman who brought a bird sin offering in** a situation of **uncertainty** as to whether her miscarriage obligated her to bring a sin offering, in which case the sin offering may not be eaten by priests, the *halakha* is as follows: **If before** the nape of the neck of the bird **was pinched it became known to her that she certainly gave birth** to a non-viable newborn, **she should render** the offering **a definite** sin offering, **as from the** same **type** of animal **that she brings** a sin offering **in** a case where it is **unknown** to her that she gave birth, **she** likewise **brings** a sin offering **in** a case where it is **known** to her. **If after** the nape of the bird **was pinched it became known to her that she** certainly **gave birth,** in such a case **Rav says:** The priest **sprinkles its blood and squeezes out its blood** on the wall of the altar, **and she has achieved atonement** and fulfilled her obligation; and the bird is **permitted in consumption** by the priests. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In such a case, even if the blood was sprinkled and squeezed out on the wall of the altar, the bird is **prohibited in consumption,** due to a rabbinic **decree, lest** people **say: A bird** that is **brought** as **a sin offering due to uncertainty is consumed.**

Rabbi Yochanan's position differs from Rav's. While he agrees that if she learns after *melikah* that she was indeed obligated she would be *yotzei*, he rules *asurah ba'achilah* — it may not be eaten. The reason: *gezeirah shema yomru chatas ha'of haba'ah al hasafek ne'echeles* — onlookers who see the *kohen* eating will not know that she learned mid-process of her obligation, and will conclude that a *chatas ha'of* brought on doubt may be eaten. One might ask how this concern is realistic when it is only *kohanim* eating at the end of the day, all partaking of the same birds — it is indeed a good question, and the concern about misleading appearances is nevertheless maintained.

### Before/After Melikah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי לֵוִי כְּוָתֵיהּ דְּרַב: חַטַּאת הָעוֹף הַבָּאָה עַל הַסָּפֵק, אִם מִשֶּׁנִּמְלְקָה נוֹדַע שֶׁיָּלְדָה וַדַּאי – מַזֶּה דָּמָהּ וּמוֹצֶה דָּמָהּ, וְכִפְּרָה, [וּ]מוּתֶּרֶת בַּאֲכִילָה. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: חַטַּאת הָעוֹף הַבָּאָה עַל הַסָּפֵק, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִמְלְקָה נוֹדַע לָהּ שֶׁלֹּא יָלְדָה – תֵּצֵא לְחוּלִּין, אוֹ תִּמָּכֵר לַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ. אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִמְלְקָה נוֹדַע לָהּ שֶׁיָּלְדָה וַדַּאי – תַּעֲשֶׂה וַדַּאי, שֶׁמִּמִּין שֶׁמְּבִיאָה עַל לֹא הוֹדַע מְבִיאָה עַל הוֹדַע. אִם מִשֶּׁנִּמְלְקָה נוֹדַע שֶׁיָּלְדָה – אֲסוּרָה אֲפִילּוּ בַּהֲנָאָה. שֶׁעַל הַסָּפֵק בָּאָה מִתְּחִלָּתָהּ, כִּפְּרָה סְפֵיקָהּ וְהָלְכָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Levi teaches** a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav:** With regard to **a bird sin offering that is brought due to uncertainty, if after** the nape of the neck of the bird **was pinched it became known that she certainly gave birth,** the priest **sprinkles its blood and squeezes out its blood, and she has achieved atonement;** and the bird is **permitted in consumption.** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** With regard to **a bird sin offering that is brought due to uncertainty, if before** the nape of the bird **was pinched it became known to her that she did not give birth,** the bird is **transferred to non-sacred** status **or is sold to another** woman who is obligated to bring a bird sin offering. **If before** the nape of the bird **was pinched it became known to her that she certainly gave birth,she should render** the offering **a definite** sin offering, **as from the type** of animal **that she brings** a sin offering **in** a case where it is **unknown** to her that she gave birth, **she brings** a sin offering **in** a case where it is **known** to her. **If after** the nape of the bird **was pinched it became known that she gave birth,** the priest sprinkles its blood and squeezes out its blood, and she has received atonement; but consumption of the bird is prohibited. And if the blood was already squeezed out, then it **is even prohibited to derive benefit** from the bird, in accordance with the *halakha* of any bird brought as a sin offering due to uncertainty. The reason is **that** this sin offering **was initially brought due to uncertainty;** and **it atoned for its uncertainty, and** that uncertainty **is gone.**

A *baraisa* of Levi is cited in support of Rav: if after *melikah* she learns she had given birth, one applies the blood and it atones, and it may be eaten. A separate *baraisa* aligns with Rabbi Yochanan. That *baraisa* systematically covers both scenarios — before and after *melikah*. Before *melikah*, if she learns she did not give birth: *teitzei l'chullin* — it returns to non-sacred status, or it may be sold to someone who needs to bring a bird *korban*. If before *melikah* she learns she did give birth: *vadai te'aseh vadai* — proceed as a definite *chatas*, since whether she did not know or does know, it is the same *korban*.

### Safek Status Sealed

After *melikah*, if she learns she gave birth: *asurah afilu b'hana'ah*. The language here is striking — *she-al hasafek ba'ah mit'chila kipper sefeika v'halcha lah*. This is the same formula used in the contexts of *egla arufa* and *asham talui*, where those offerings address the state of doubt at the time and their status is sealed accordingly. In truth, those contexts involve atoning for a doubtful sin; here the purpose was to allow her to become *tehora*. The formula is borrowed and does not map perfectly. The basic point, though, is that the offering fulfilled its function, and we will not permit eating it even after the fact, both because of concerns about misleading appearances and — as Rashi reads the language — perhaps because it retains its *safek* status in a more fundamental way, possibly *me-ikar ha-din* rather than merely *miderabbanan*.

## Two Rams for Asham

### Sufficient Ram

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּפְרִישׁ שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים לְאָשָׁם וְלָקַח בָּהֶן שְׁנֵי אֵילִים לְאָשָׁם, אִם הָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים – יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב וְיִמָּכֵר וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who designates two *sela*,** which is the minimal value of a guilt offering,**to** purchase a ram for **a guilt offering,and he purchased two rams for a guilt offering with** the two *sela*, **if one of them is** now **worth two *sela*, he shall sacrifice** it **for his guilt offering. And the second** ram that he purchased with the money he designated does not become non-sacred. Rather, it **shall graze until it becomes blemished; and** then **it shall be sold, and** the **money** received **for it shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings.

The *mishnah* establishes that one who sets aside two *sela'im* for an *asham* and purchases two rams at one *sela* each — so that initially neither appears to meet the minimum — may nonetheless bring the one that is worth two *sela'im* for his *asham*. The Torah does not specify how much one must spend; it specifies how much the animal must be worth, as Tosafos observes: even a gifted ram could in principle qualify, since the requirement speaks to value, not expenditure. The second ram, which carries *kedushat asham* but is not needed, is left to graze until it acquires a *mum*, is then redeemed, and the proceeds go toward a *nedava* — specifically an *olah*, since *mosar asham* is brought as an *olah*. One might ask whether this second ram could be saved until it is worth two *sela'im* and used for a future *asham* or transferred to another person's obligation, but the general principle — analogous to the rule that a *chatas* designated for one sin cannot be applied to another — would seem to preclude that.

### Misuse & Meilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָקַח בָּהֶן שְׁנֵי אֵילִים לְחוּלִּין, אֶחָד יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים וְאֶחָד יָפֶה עֲשָׂרָה זוּז – הַיָּפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי – לִמְעִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** If **he purchased two rams for non-sacred** use **with** those two *sela* designated for a guilt offering, he has misused consecrated property. He is therefore liable to bring a guilt offering and to compensate the Temple treasury for those two *sela* and add one-fifth to the sum for a total of ten dinars, as there are four dinars in a *sela*. If **one** of the rams **is** now **worth two *sela*, and** the other **one is** now **worth ten dinars,** i.e., two and a half *sela*, **the** one **that is worth two *sela* shall be sacrificed as his guilt offering** for misuse of the two *sela*, **and the second one** shall be sacrificed **for his** initial **misuse,** as it is worth two *sela* plus one-fifth.

The *mishnah* then turns to the scenario where the person took the sanctified money and used it to buy two rams for his own secular use — *chullin*. This constitutes *meilah*, misuse of consecrated property. The discussion of *meilah* here serves as a transition toward the next *masechet*: the obligation of *meilah* requires repayment of the principal plus a *chomesh* — a fifth added to the base, which is in practice a quarter of the total repaid — as well as the bringing of an *asham me'ilos*, which likewise requires an animal worth two *shekel*.

The scenario constructed is: one *sela* was stolen from the consecrated money (worth four *zuz*), and the ram purchased with that and related funds turns out to be worth ten *zuz*. The arithmetic is deliberate: two *shekel* (eight *zuz*) plus a *chomesh* (twenty percent of eight, which is the extra two, bringing the total repayment to ten) equals ten *zuz*. The ten-*zuz* ram is brought as repayment toward the original *asham* obligation — the money goes back to the purpose from which it was taken. The eight-*zuz* ram — the one worth exactly two *shekel* — goes toward the *asham me'ilos*, since by committing *meilah* one has incurred a fresh obligation requiring an *asham* of minimum two *shekel*. The logic is that the two obligations happen to be satisfiable with the very animals purchased through the stolen money.

### One Sufficient, One Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד לְאָשָׁם וְאֶחָד לְחוּלִּין, אִם הָיָה שֶׁל אָשָׁם יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים – יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לִמְעִילָתוֹ, וְיָבִיא עִמָּהּ סֶלַע וְחוּמְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where he purchased two rams with those two *sela* designated for a guilt offering, **one for a guilt offering and one for non-sacred** use, **if** the ram **for** the **guilt offering is** now **worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed for his** initial **guilt offering. And** with regard to **the second** ram that he purchased for non-sacred use, if it is now worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed as a guilt offering for **his** present **misuse, and he brings with it** the sum of **one *sela* and one-fifth** to the Temple treasury as payment for his misuse.

When one of the two rams is worth two *sela'im* and the other is worth ten *zuz*, the *mishnah* directs: *hayafeh shenei sela'im yikarev le'ashamo* — the one of sufficient value is brought for the *asham me'ilos*, and the one worth ten *zuz* — which represents the principal plus twenty percent — goes toward *leme'ilato*, the payback for the original misappropriation. The key principle, derived from *ve'et asher chata min hakodesh yeshalem ve'et chamishito yosef alav*, is that the principal and the *chomesh* are paid back together to the same source from which they were taken. The extra fifth does not get redirected toward one's own *asham* obligation; it belongs to whatever *hekdesh* account was drawn upon.

### Divided Usage

The final scenario in the *mishnah* envisions a case where one ram was legitimately designated as the *asham* and one was taken for secular use — *echad le'asham ve'echad lechullin*. One can imagine a concrete case: the man finds a ram worth two *shekel* selling for one, buys it for his *asham*, and then uses his spare *shekel* to buy something for his flock. If *shel asham yafeh shtei sela'im* — the *asham* ram is worth two *sela'im* — it is brought for the original obligation. The second ram, which represents the *meilah*, serves as the *asham me'ilos* assuming it too is worth two *shekel*. Additionally, *veyavi imah sela vechumshah* — he must separately repay the *shekel* he misappropriated plus its *chomesh*. Since the original *asham* was already brought, the repaid money cannot be used for another *asham* designated for the same sin; instead, as the *halacha* of *mosar asham* dictates, it would be used toward an *olah*.

## Meilah Terminology

### Reisha vs Seifa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״מְעִילָתוֹ״ דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לִמְעִילָתוֹ? אִילֵּימָא אֵיל אָשָׁם, לְמֵימְרָא דְּחוֹמֶשׁ בַּהֲדֵי אַיִל מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וְאֵת אֲשֶׁר חָטָא מִן הַקֹּדֶשׁ יְשַׁלֵּם וְאֶת חֲמִישִׁיתוֹ יוֹסֵף עָלָיו״, אַלְמָא בַּהֲדֵי גְּזֵילוֹ מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is the term: **His misuse,** referring to, with regard to the *halakha* **that is taught** in **the first clause** of the mishna: If he purchased two rams for non-sacred use with those two *sela* designated for a guilt offering, if one of the rams is now worth two *sela* and the other one is worth ten dinars, the one that is worth two *sela* shall be sacrificed as his guilt offering **and the second one** shall be sacrificed **for his misuse?** **If we say** that the term: His misuse, is referring to the **ram** that one is obligated to bring as **a guilt offering** for misuse of consecrated property, is this **to say that** the additional **one-fifth** for misuse of consecrated property **is brought together with the ram** that he brings as a guilt offering for that sin? If so, then this would explain why if that ram offering is valued as more than two *sela*, the additional value fulfills the obligation of giving an additional one-fifth. **But isn't it written: "And he shall make restitution for that which he has done amiss with consecrated property, and he shall add to it one-fifth"** (Leviticus 5:16)? The verse **apparently** indicates that **he brings** the additional one-fifth **together with** his repayment for **that which he stole** from consecrated property.

The Gemara notices a terminological inconsistency within the *mishnah*. In the first case (*reisha*), the ten-*zuz* ram is described as going *leme'ilato* — for his *meilah* — meaning it represents the repayment of what was stolen. Yet in the final case (*seifa*), *hasheini limeilato* refers to bringing the second ram as an *asham me'ilos* — the obligatory *korban* for having committed *meilah*. The same word *meilah* thus designates two opposite things: in the *reisha*, the payback; in the *seifa*, the *korban* obligation.

### Chomesh Returns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, קָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: אֶחָד לְאָשָׁם וְאֶחָד לְחוּלִּין, אִם הָיָה שֶׁל אָשָׁם יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים – יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לִמְעִילָתוֹ, וְיָבִיא עִמָּהּ סֶלַע וְחוּמְשָׁהּ, אַלְמָא חוֹמֶשׁ בַּהֲדֵי גְּזֵילוֹ מַיְיתֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, it is taught in the latter clause** of the mishna: In a case where he purchased two rams with those two *sela* designated for a guilt offering, **one for a guilt offering and one for non-sacred** use, **if** the ram **for** the **guilt offering is** now **worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed for his guilt offering. And** with regard to **the second** ram that he purchased for non-sacred use, if it is now worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed **for his misuse, and he brings with it** payment of **one *sela* and one-fifth** to the Temple treasury as payment for his misuse. **Evidently, he brings** the **one-fifth together with** his repayment for **that which he stole** from consecrated property.

The Gemara resolves this by articulating the underlying principle: *ve'et asher chata min hakodesh yeshalem ve'et chamishito yosef alav* — the *chomesh* is restored together with the principal, both returning to the original source. The extra fifth does not constitute an independent resource for satisfying other *korban* obligations; it is part of the restitution package. In the *reisha*, where the ten-*zuz* ram physically embodies both the principal and the *chomesh* in animal form, it is natural to call that ram *meilato* — the thing by which the *meilah* is paid back. *Keren v'chomesh* — principal and fifth — are brought together, and the animal therefore represents the *gezeilah*, the stolen amount. In the *seifa*, since the repayment is handled separately in coin form rather than through an animal, the word *meilato* shifts to refer to the *korban* obligation that arose from the act of *meilah*.

### Language Shifts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, ״מְעִילָתוֹ״ – מַאי דְּאִיתְהֲנִי מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ וְיֶשְׁנוֹ מִשְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים דְּאַפְרֵישִׁנּוּ[ן] לְאָשָׁם וְלָקַח בָּהֶן שְׁנֵי אֵילִים לְחוּלִּין, דְּיָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים מַקְרֵיב לֵיהּ אֵיל אָשָׁם, וְיָפֶה עֲשָׂרָה זוּז יָהֵיב לֵיהּ לְמַאי דְּאִיתְהֲנִי מֵהֶקְדֵּשׁ דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ גְּזֵילוֹ, וְחוּמְשׁוֹ. וּמַאי ״מְעִילָתוֹ״? גְּזֵילוֹ. בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּאּ לִ״מְעִילָתוֹ״ דְּרֵישָׁא – גְּזֵילוֹ. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: אֶחָד לְאָשָׁם וְאֶחָד לְחוּלִּין, אִם הָיָה שֶׁל אָשָׁם יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים – יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי לִמְעִילָה וְיָבִיא עִמָּהּ, סֶלַע וְחוּמְשָׁהּ. אַלְמָא ״מְעִילָתוֹ״ – אֵיל אָשָׁם. רֵישָׁא קָרֵי לֵיהּ לִ״מְעִילָתוֹ״ גְּזֵילוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** when the mishna states: The ram worth ten dinars shall be sacrificed **for his misuse,** it means as repayment for **that which he derived benefit from consecrated** property. **And those are** the **two *sela* that he** initially **designated for a guilt offering and with which he bought two rams as non-sacred** animals. The Gemara explains: The reason for this is **that** the ram **worth two *sela* he sacrifices for his ram of the guilt offering** for his trespass, **and** the ram **worth ten dinars he gives for that which he derived benefit from consecrated** property, as he used two *sela* of consecrated property, **which are** for **that which he stole,** equal to eight dinars **plus** an additional **one-fifth,** the extra two dinars. **And** if so, to **what is** the term: **His misuse,** referring? It is not referring to the ram brought as a guilt offering for his misuse of consecrated property, but rather to the repayment for **that which he stole** from consecrated property. The Gemara asks: With reference **to what** case **did you interpret** the term: **His misuse,** in **the first clause** of the mishna? It was interpreted as referring to the repayment for **that which he stole** from consecrated property. If so, **say the latter clause** of the mishna: In a case where he purchased two rams with those two *sela* designated for a guilt offering, **one for a guilt offering and one for non-secular** use, **if** the ram **for** the **guilt offering is** now **worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed for his** initial **guilt offering. And** with regard to **the second** ram that he purchased for non-sacred use, if it is now worth two *sela*, it shall be sacrificed **for** his **misuse** of consecrated property, **and he brings with it** payment of **one *sela* and one-fifth** to the Temple treasury as payment for his misuse. Since the mishna teaches that he gives one *sela* and one-fifth as repayment for that which he stole, **evidently** the term: **His misuse,** is referring to the **ram** that he brings as **a guilt offering.** The Gemara questions this inconsistency: **The first clause** uses the term: **His misuse, in reference to** the repayment for **that which he stole,**

The Gemara formalizes the distinction: *reisha kari lei l'meiloso gezeilo* — in the first case, the word *meilato* means the stolen item being paid back; *seifa kari lei l'meiloso el asham* — at the end, it means the *asham me'ilos* itself. The principle organizing these usages is that *meilato* attaches to whichever obligation is being discharged in animal form. In the *reisha*, the ram *is* the repayment; in the *seifa*, the repayment is in coin and the ram *is* the *asham*. The usage shifts accordingly, and the twenty percent, in every instance, flows back to the function from which it was taken — if the original *korban* had already been brought, the funds become an *olah* as *mosar asham*.

## Shevach Hekdesh

### Rav Menashya's Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר גַּדָּא: בְּכִינּוּס חוּמְשִׁין מַהוּ שֶׁיִּתְכַּפֵּר? מִי אָמְרִינַן: אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ – מִשּׁוּם דְּקָטָרַח קַמֵּיהּ, אֲבָל הָכָא דְּלָא קָטָרַח – לָא מִתְכַּפֵּר. אוֹ דִלְמָא אִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר אֵין אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אַפְרְשֵׁיהּ, אֲבָל הָדֵין כִּינּוּס חוּמְשִׁין – דְּאַפְרְשֵׁיהּ, אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מִתְכַּפֵּר. דְּאִיבְּעִי לְהוּ: [אָדָם] מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ, אוֹ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Menashya bar Gadda raises a dilemma: What is** the *halakha* **with regard to** one who misused consecrated property several times until the **accumulation of one-fifths** that he owes totals two *sela*: May he bring a guilt offering with that money and thereby **achieve atonement?** The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma. **Do we say: If you say** that **a person can achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property, e.g., if one initially purchased a ram worth one *sela* and then its market value increased to two *sela* while the animal was in his possession, this is **because he exerted** himself **with regard to** the animal by caring for it while it was in his possession. **But here, where he did not exert** himself, but the value accumulated on its own, **he cannot achieve atonement.** **Or perhaps** we say the opposite: **If you say** that **a person cannot achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property, this is **because he did not designate** the ram as an offering when it was worth two *sela*, and its value appreciated on its own. **But here,** with regard to **the accumulation of one-fifths, where he designated** the total value as consecrated property, **it is** possible **to say** that **he can receive atonement** by bringing a guilt offering with that money. The Gemara notes **that** this **dilemma** itself **was raised** before the Sages: Can one **achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property that occurred while the animal was in his possession, **or not?**

Rav Menashya bar Gada poses a question: *bekinnus chumshin mahu?* — if one has accumulated multiple *chomeshim* from repeated acts of *meilah* against the same sanctified fund, may one use those collected fifths to satisfy the minimum value requirement of a *korban*? The scenario, following the *Shita Mekubetzes*, is where the accumulated fifths reach a full *shekel* — through multiple cycles of misappropriation and repayment — and the question is whether that *shekel*, now being paid back as it were to the fund from which it came, can also serve to satisfy the base cost of a *korban* obligation.

The question is framed against the backdrop of *shevach hekdesh* — the concept of natural appreciation of sanctified property. Normally, *shevach hekdesh* would arise when an animal sanctified as a *korban* gains weight and value after sanctification. Here, the "appreciation" of the *hekdesh* fund comes not from organic growth but from the obligation to pay back more than what was taken, by operation of the *halacha* of *meilah*. The question has two faces. One might say: even if *shevach hekdesh* generally works, here the increase did not come from investment or natural growth — *d'lo tara*, one did not make an effort — and therefore it cannot be used to gain *kapparah*, only the original *kedusha* can. Or one might say: even if natural growth does not work, here the money was directly sanctified for this purpose — *d'afrishei* — and therefore it should work.

### General Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמַּפְרִישׁ שְׁנֵי סְלָעִים לְאָשָׁם, וְלָקַח בָּהֶן שְׁנֵי אֵילִים לְאָשָׁם, הָיָה אֶחָד מֵהֶן יָפֶה שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים – יִקְרַב לַאֲשָׁמוֹ, וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִרְעֶה עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֵב וְיִמָּכֵר וְיִפְּלוּ דָּמָיו לִנְדָבָה. מַאי לָאו, דִּזְבַן בְּאַרְבְּעָה אַיִל, וּשְׁבַח, דְּשָׁוֵי תְּמָנְיָא? וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ! לָא, הָכָא בְמַאי עָסְקִינַן? דְּאוֹזֵיל רוֹעֶה גַּבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: With regard to **one who designates two *sela*** to purchase a ram **for a guilt offering and he purchased two rams for a guilt offering with** the two *sela*, if **one of them was** now **worth two *sela*, he shall sacrifice** it **for his guilt offering. And the second** ram that he purchased with the money that he designated does not become non-sacred. Rather, it **shall graze until it becomes blemished; and** then **it shall be sold, and** the **money** received **for it shall be allocated for** communal **gift** offerings. The Gemara explains the proof: **What, is it not** that the mishna is referring to a case **where he bought the ram for four** dinars, which is equal to one *sela*, **and** its value **appreciated** while in his possession **until** it was **worth eight** dinars, or two *sela*, and the mishna rules he may bring this ram as a guilt offering? **And** if so, **conclude from this** mishna that **a person can achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No; what are we dealing with here?** It is with a ram that was worth two *sela* at the time of purchase, but the **shepherd** who sold it **reduced** the price **for him.**

This frames the general question posed to the *beis medrash*: *mitkaper adam b'shevach hekdesh o lo?* Can a person achieve *kapparah* through the natural appreciation of a sanctified animal — that is, if an animal was sanctified when it was worth less than the required minimum, and it subsequently grew in value to meet the minimum, may it be brought as a valid *korban*?

The Gemara first attempts to answer from our *mishnah* itself. If one of the rams purchased at one *sela* turns out to be worth two — *yikarev l'ashamo*, it is brought for the *asham* — perhaps this is a case of *shevach hekdesh*: the animal was bought for four *zuz* and appreciated to eight? This would demonstrate *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara rejects this reading: *d'ozil ro'eh gabei* — the shepherd gave a discount. At the moment of sanctification the animal was already worth two *shekel*, even though only one was paid. It is therefore not a case of post-sanctification appreciation at all — the requirement has always been about worth, not price.

### Fattening Tosefta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לָקַח אַיִל בְּסֶלַע, וּפִטְּמוֹ וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ עַל שְׁתַּיִם – כָּשֵׁר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ! לָא, פִּטְּמוֹ שָׁאנֵי, דְּהָא חָסַר לֵיהּ תְּמָנְיָא. תָּא שְׁמַע: לָקַח אַיִל בְּסֶלַע וַהֲרֵי הוּא בִּשְׁתַּיִם – כָּשֵׁר. הָכָא נָמֵי כְּשֶׁפִּטְּמוֹ. אִי הָכִי הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! רֵישָׁא דִּזְבַן בְּאַרְבְּעָה וְאַשְׁבְּחֵיהּ בְּאַרְבְּעָה אַחֲרִינֵא, דְּחָסַר לֵיהּ תְּמָנְיָא. סֵיפָא דִּזְבַן אַיִל בְּאַרְבְּעָה וְאַשְׁבְּחֵיהּ בִּתְלָתָא, וְשָׁוֵי תְּמָנְיָא. אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: יְשַׁלֵּם סֶלַע – הָא חֲסַר לֵיהּ שִׁבְעָה! מַאי יְשַׁלֵּם? תַּשְׁלוּם דְּסֶלַע. וְאִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּאֵין אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ, כִּי יָהֵיב תַּשְׁלוּם דְּסֶלַע מַאי הָוֵי? אַיִל בֶּן שְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא! לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר: אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:9): With regard to one who **purchased a ram for one *sela* and he fattened** the ram **and** thereby **established** its value **at two** *sela*, it is **valid** for sacrifice as a guilt offering. **Conclude from this** *baraita* that **a person can achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No;** the case of **one who fattened** an animal **is different, as he lost eight** dinars, which is two *sela*, in expenses for the animal, including the price he paid. But he cannot achieve atonement if the animal's value appreciated on its own while in his possession. The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the continuation of the same *baraita*: If one **purchased a ram for one *sela* and** now **it has** appreciated **to** the value of **two** *sela*, **it is valid** for sacrifice as a guilt offering. The Gemara explains: **Here too,** it is referring to a case **where one fattened** the ram. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If so, this is** the same case as in **the first clause** of that *baraita* and is therefore unnecessary. The Gemara answers: **The first clause** is dealing with one **who purchased** a ram **for four** dinars **and he increased** its value **by** spending **another four** dinars to fatten the animal, which means **that he lost eight** dinars, which equal two *sela*, in total expenses on the ram. **The latter clause** is discussing a case **of** one **who purchased a ram for four** dinars **and he increased** its value **by** spending **another three** dinars to fatten it, **and** then the ram appreciated one more dinar on its own, and is now **worth eight** dinars. The Gemara objects: **If so, say the latter clause** of that *baraita*: And in addition **he shall pay** one ***sela*** to the Temple treasury. Why is this the *halakha*? **Didn't he lose seven** dinars in total expenses on the ram, four dinars for its purchase and three for fattening it, and therefore the ram added only one dinar of value on its own? The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of the statement: **He shall pay?** The *baraita* is teaching that he must **complete** the payment **of** the ***sela*,** i.e., by giving one additional dinar. **And if one maintains that a person cannot achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property, **what is** the significance **of** the fact **that he gives** one dinar that is the **completion of** the payment of the ***sela*? We require** him to bring **a ram that** cost him **two *sela*, and there is no** such animal here. The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the *tanna* of this *baraita* in the *Tosefta* **holds** that **a person can achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property.

The Gemara turns to an explicit *Tosefta*: *lakach ayil b'sela u-pitmo v'he'emido al shtayim* — he bought a ram for one *shekel*, fattened it, and brought it to a value of two *shekel*. *Kasher* — it is valid. This appears to confirm *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara immediately qualifies: *pitmo shani* — fattening is different, because one invested one's own money into the animal. If the ram cost four *zuz* and the investment of fattening cost another four *zuz*, one has effectively spent eight *zuz* out of pocket; it is not *shevach hekdesh* in the sense of free appreciation, it is a direct investment. The Gemara then considers the *seifa* of that same *Tosefta*: *lakach ayil b'arba'ah v'ashkachei b'tlata v'shavei tamnya* — one bought it for four, invested only three more, yet it is worth eight. Here the single extra *zuz* of appreciation beyond investment is genuine *shevach hekdesh*. The Gemara will eventually come around to acknowledging this constitutes *shevach hekdesh*, but at this stage it maintains that the case is still one of investment.

### Payment Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תַּשְׁלוּם דְּסֶלַע לֹא יִתֵּן! הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּקָיָהֵיב תַּשְׁלוּמִין דְּסֶלַע – גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ: אַיִל פָּחוּת מִשְׁתֵּי סְלָעִים מְכַפֵּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so, he** should **not give** the one dinar that is the **completion of** the payment of one ***sela*,** as the ram is now worth two *sela*. The Gemara explains: **This is the reason that he must give** the one dinar that is **the completion of** the payment of one ***sela*:** It is a rabbinic **decree, lest** people **say** that **a ram** worth **less than two *sela* atones** as a guilt offering.

The *Tosefta* continues: *v'yashlim sela* — even though the *korban* works, one must pay back a *sela* (or, as the Gemara clarifies, *tashlumei d'sela* — the remainder of the *sela*, meaning whatever portion was not invested, as little as one *zuz*). This halacha appears strange: if the animal now genuinely meets the minimum value requirement, why must any additional payment be made? The Gemara's answer is: *gezeirah shema yomru haya pachot mishtei sela'im mechaper* — people will see that someone spent less than two *shekel* and conclude that an *asham* of insufficient value can achieve *kapparah*. The requirement to pay the difference is therefore not a function of the *korban* being invalid — it is fully valid; one is indeed *mitkaper* — but a rabbinic *gezeirah* against creating misleading impressions.

One might press: how would anyone know how much was spent? The answer is that people always talk about bargains. In any case, the practical *halacha* is that even if the animal improved entirely by itself without any investment, *me-ikar ha-din* it achieves *kapparah*, and one must nonetheless pay the difference out of pocket to avoid the misleading appearance that a subminimum *asham* was sufficient.

### Elazar & Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אָדָם מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ, אוֹ לֹא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כַּמָּה שָׁנִים גָּדַל זֶה בֵּינֵינוּ, וְלֹא שָׁמַע הֲלָכָה זוֹ מִמֶּנִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma:** If consecrated **living animals** were rendered unfit to be sacrificed, are they permanently **disqualified or not? Rabbi Yoḥanan said,** in puzzlement: **How many years** has **this** Rabbi Elazar **grown among us** and learned Torah from me, **and** yet **he did not hear this *halakha* from me.**

When Rabbi Elazar asks Rabbi Yochanan this very question — *adam mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh o lo?* — Rabbi Yochanan responds with characteristic astonishment: *kamma shanim gadal zeh beineinu velo shama halakha zu mimeni?* — how many years have you been with us and you never heard me teach this *halacha*? The implication, *mikhlal de'amar Rabbi Yochanan in*, is that Rabbi Yochanan rules yes, one is *mitkaper b'shevach hekdesh*.

### Todah Child

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נִתְּנָה קִצְבָה בִּמְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה? שֶׁמָּא יוּזְלוּ טְלָאִים, וְאֵין לָהֶם תַּקָּנָה לֶאֱכוֹל בְּקָדָשִׁים. דְּתַנְיָא: וְלַד תּוֹדָה וּתְמוּרָתָהּ, וְכֵן הַמַּפְרִישׁ תּוֹדָתוֹ וְאָבְדָה וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, אֵין טְעוּנָה לֶחֶם. [רַב חֲנַנְיָה] מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְאַחַר כַּפָּרָה, דְּוָלָד אֵין טָעוּן לֶחֶם. אֲבָל לִפְנֵי כַּפָּרָה – טָעוּן לֶחֶם, אַלְמָא קָסָבַר מִתְכַּפֵּר בִּשְׁבַח הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The source Rabbi Yochanan relies upon is: But doesn't Rabbi Yochanan say that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: For what** reason **did the Torah not provide a fixed** value for offerings brought by **those lacking atonement,** who must bring an offering of purification for them to be permitted to eat consecrated meat, e.g., a *zav* and a leper? It is because the price of **lambs might depreciate** below the Torah's fixed value **and they would have no remedy to eat sacrificial** food. It is taught in a *baraita*: With regard to the **offspring** of an animal designated as **a thanks offering or** an animal that is **its substitute, and likewise** if **one designated** an animal as **his thanks offering and it was lost and he designated another in its stead,** and the first animal was then found, the second animal, i.e., the offspring, the substitute, or the replacement, is sacrificed, but it **does not require** the bringing of **loaves** with it. **And** with regard to the offspring of a thanks offering, **Rabbi Ḥanina sent** a letter from Eretz Yisrael to the Sages in Babylonia containing the following statement **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** That mishna **taught that the offspring does not require loaves only** in a case where they were sacrificed **after** the owner achieved **atonement** by sacrificing the mother and sprinkling its blood, since he has thereby fulfilled his obligation, **as** the **offspring does not require** the bringing of **loaves.** **But** if one sacrificed the offspring **before** he achieved **atonement** through sacrifice of the mother, then the offspring **requires** the bringing of **loaves,** as it is the fulfillment of his obligation to bring a thanks offering. **Evidently,** Rabbi Yochanan **holds** that a person **can achieve atonement with an enhancement of consecrated** property, as this offspring of a consecrated animal is considered its enhancement.

The source Rabbi Yochanan relies upon is: *vlad todah u-tmuratah* — the offspring of a *todah* or a *temurah*, which by themselves do not require bread, but *la-achar kapara* — after the mother has been brought for the obligation — are brought without bread; whereas *lifnei kapara* — before the mother has been brought — the child may serve as the *todah* itself and is brought *taun lechem*, with the full bread complement. This is the ruling sent by Rabbi Chanina in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. What one sees is that the offspring of the sanctified animal — a natural increase after sanctification — can be used to fulfill the *todah* obligation. The birth is a form of *shevach hekdesh*, and it clearly functions to discharge the obligation. *Alma kasavar: miskaper b'shevach hekdesh.*

## Living Animals Q's

### Nidachim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִדְחִין אוֹ לֹא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲרֵי כַּמָּה שָׁנִים גָּדַל זֶה בֵּינֵינוּ, וְלֹא שָׁמַע הֲלָכָה זוֹ מִמֶּנִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma:** If consecrated **living animals** were rendered unfit to be sacrificed, are they permanently **disqualified or not? Rabbi Yoḥanan said,** in puzzlement: **How many years** has **this** Rabbi Elazar **grown among us** and learned Torah from me, **and** yet **he did not hear this *halakha* from me.**

Following the pattern established in the prior exchange, Rabbi Elazar now poses a further question to Rabbi Yochanan: *ba'alei chayim nidachim o lo?* — can a living animal be rendered *nidach*, stigmatized and permanently disqualified, or does *dichui* apply only after death? Rabbi Yochanan again responds: *harei kamma shanim gadal zeh beineinu velo shamata halacha zu mimeni?* — you have been with us all these years and never heard me teach this? *Miklal d'Rabbi Yochanan amar in* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that even a living animal can be rendered *nidach*.

### Half-Ownership Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אַמְרַהּ? אִין, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּהֵמָה שֶׁל שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין, הִקְדִּישׁ חֶצְיָהּ, וְחָזַר וְלָקַח חֶצְיָהּ וְהִקְדִּישָׁהּ – קְדוֹשָׁה, וְאֵינָהּ קְרֵיבָה, וְעוֹשָׂה תְּמוּרָה, וּתְמוּרָתָהּ כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Should one conclude **by inference** from this statement **that Rabbi Yoḥanan said** the *halakha* with regard to this matter? The Gemara answers: **Yes, as Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **an animal** that belongs **to two partners,** if one of them **consecrated** the **half of it** that belonged to him, **and then acquired its** other **half** from his partner **and consecrated it,**it is **consecrated but it may not be sacrificed. And it renders** a non-sacred animal for which it is exchanged consecrated as **a substitute, and its substitution is like it,** i.e., it too is consecrated but it may not be sacrificed.

Rabbi Yochanan's ruling on *ba'alei chayim nidachim* derives from the following case: *behema shel shnei shutfin hikdish chetzyo* — an animal owned by two partners, where one sanctified his half. Since half an animal cannot be brought as a *korban*, the *kedusha* cannot function at the *mizbe'ach* level; it is merely *kedushat damim*, a monetary *kedusha*. The partner then buys back the other half and sanctifies that half as well. The result: *kedusha ve-eina kreiva* — it has sanctity but may not be brought, because the initial sanctification already stigmatized it.

### Three Conclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תְּלָת: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ בַּעֲלֵי חַיִּים נִדְחִין. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים מְדַחָה. וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ יֵשׁ דִּיחוּי בְּדָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** One can **conclude from this** ruling of Rabbi Yoḥanan **three** *halakhot*. **Conclude from it** that **consecrated living animals can be** permanently **disqualified.** When he consecrated only half of the animal it was not fit for sacrifice, and this meant that the animal was permanently disqualified even after it became fully consecrated. **And conclude from it** that **sanctity** that inheres in an animal's **value disqualifies** another animal, i.e., the substitute. The sanctity is considered inherent in its value because only half of the animal was initially consecrated. **And conclude from it** that **there is disqualification with regard to monetary** value, i.e., even an animal that is consecrated only for its monetary value can be disqualified from sacrifice.

*Shma mina tlat* — three conclusions are drawn. First, *ba'alei chayim nidachim* — even a living animal can be *nidach*: the animal here is alive, yet it is permanently disqualified. Second, *kedushat damim madcha* — even a *kedusha* that was never *kedushat mizbe'ach* from its inception can create *dichui*: the initial sanctification was only *kedushat damim* at the time, yet it stigmatized the animal. Third, *yesh dichui be-damim* — *dichui* can exist even at the level of *kedushat damim* alone. Rashi observes, and notes that Tosafos typically wrestles with distinguishing the second and third conclusions in cases of this recurring *sugyah*, that the two are in essence the same point: the novelty is that *dichui* operates even when the animal was never actually fit to be brought as a *korban mizbe'ach*.

## Minimum Value Q

### Unavailable Value

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: הוּזְלוּ טְלָאִים בָּעוֹלָם, מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן ״מִבְחַר נִדְרֵיכֶם״ בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא אִיכָּא, אוֹ דִילְמָא ״כֶּסֶף שְׁקָלִים״ בָּעֵינַן, וְלֵיכָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar raises a dilemma:** If the price of **lambs depreciated in the world** and one cannot find a ram valued at two *sela*, **what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara explains the sides of the dilemma: **Do we say** that **we require** an offering that fulfills the condition: **"Your choice vows"** (Deuteronomy 12:11), **and that** requirement **is** fulfilled, as he is bringing the best animal available? **Or perhaps we require** a guilt offering to be purchased in accordance with the verse: **"Silver by shekels"** (Leviticus 5:15), i.e., two *sela*, **and** that requirement **is not** fulfilled.

Rabbi Elazar poses yet another question, this one connecting directly back to the *mishnah*'s framework: *huzlu tlayim ba-olam mahu?* — if rams become so cheap that none are available at a value of two *shekel*, what is one to do? The *asham* requires an animal worth two *shekel*, but we have established that the requirement is about worth, not price. If no animal of the right age meets the minimum value, can one bring the best available and satisfy the requirement on the grounds of *mivchar nidreichem ba'inan* — we require one's best, and this is the best there is? Or does the Torah's formulation of *kesef shekalim ba'inan* — a specific monetary minimum is required — constitute an absolute threshold that cannot be bypassed?

### Absolute Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כַּמָּה שָׁנִים גָּדַלְנוּ בְּבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ וְלֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ הֲלָכָה זוֹ. וְלָא? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: מִפְּנֵי מָה לֹא נָתְנָה תּוֹרָה קִצְבָה בִּמְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה? שֶׁמָּא יוּזְלוּ טְלָאִים, וְאֵין לָהֶן תַּקָּנָה לֶאֱכוֹל בְּקָדָשִׁים. אֵימָא: לֹא לִימַּדְנוּ הֲלָכָה זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: How many years have we grown in the study hall and we have not heard this *halakha*.** The Gemara asks: **And** has this *halakha* **not** been heard? **But doesn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say** that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says: For what** reason **did the Torah not provide a fixed** value for offerings brought by **those lacking atonement,** who must bring an offering of purification for them to be permitted to eat consecrated meat, e.g., a *zav* and a leper? It is because the price of **lambs might depreciate** below the Torah's fixed value **and they would have no remedy to eat sacrificial** food. This statement indicates that one may not bring a ram that is worth less than two *sela* for a guilt offering, as the Torah fixed its value. The Gemara answers: **Say** that Rabbi Yoḥanan said: How many years **have we not taught this *halakha*** in the study hall.

Rabbi Yochanan responds — though notably, he says *lo limadnu halacha zu*, we have never taught this *halacha* in the *beis medrash* — with a teaching in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *mipnei ma lo nitna kitzva be-mechusrei kapara?* Why did the Torah not specify a minimum value for the *korban* brought by those requiring *kapparah* to permit eating *kodashim* — the *mechasrei kapara* category? Because *shema yuzlu telaim ve-ein lahem takana le-echol be-kodashim* — if rams became cheap and one could not satisfy a minimum, they would be permanently barred from *kodashim*. The Torah therefore intentionally omitted a minimum for that category precisely to avoid that outcome. By implication, in the categories *where* the Torah did specify a minimum — such as *asham* — even if animals become so cheap that the minimum cannot be met, one is simply stuck. The minimum is absolute.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, חַטַּאת חֵלֶב, יִנָּתֵן לָהּ קִצְבָה, דִּלְכַפָּרָה אָתְיָא וְלָאו לְאִישְׁתְּרוֹיֵי בַּאֲכִילַת קָדָשִׁים הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to this** statement: **If that is so, let a fixed** value **be provided for a sin offering** brought for eating prohibited **fat,** i.e., a regular sin offering, **as it is brought for atonement and it is not** brought **to permit consumption of sacrificial** food. Similarly, **Rava objects to this** statement: **If that is so, let there be a fixed** value in the Torah **for a guilt offering** brought by an impure **nazirite, as it comes for naught,** i.e., it does not come to permit consumption of sacrificial food, which is achieved by his purification rite of the sprinkling from the ashes of the red heifer upon him on the third and seventh days. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: Nothing comes for naught other than the guilt offering of a nazirite alone.** The Gemara notes that this matter is indeed **difficult.**

Abaye challenges this reasoning: *ela me-ata chatas chelev tinasen bah kitzva* — if the concern about *mechasrei kapara* is that a minimum would strand people from *kodashim*, the same logic would suggest a minimum for a *chatas chelev* should also be absent. After all, a *chatas* is also purely about *kapparah*, not about restoring eligibility to eat *kodashim*. And yet there is no minimum for a *chatas* either — *ve-lav le-ishtruyei be-achilas kodashim hu*. The argument from *mechasrei kapara* does not cleanly explain why *asham* has a minimum while *chatas* does not.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, אֲשַׁם נָזִיר לֶהֱוֵי לֵיהּ קִצְבָה, דִּלְבַטָּלָה הוּא דְּאָתֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: אֵין לְךָ דָּבָר שֶׁהוּא בָּא לְבַטָּלָה, אֶלָּא אֲשַׁם נָזִיר בִּלְבָד! קַשְׁיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava further objects to this** statement: **If that is so, let there be a fixed** value in the Torah **for a guilt offering** brought by an impure **nazirite, as it comes for naught,** i.e., it does not come to permit consumption of sacrificial food, which is achieved by his purification rite of the sprinkling from the ashes of the red heifer upon him on the third and seventh days. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: Nothing comes for naught other than the guilt offering of a nazirite alone.** The Gemara notes that this matter is indeed **difficult.**

Rava presses further: *ela me-ata asham nazir le-havei bei kitzva* — among the various *ashamos*, why does an *asham nazir* not have a minimum? *De-levatala hu de'asi* — an *asham nazir* is unique in that nothing depends on it: it is a *chiyuv*, but it does not unlock *tahara*, does not initiate or conclude the *nezirus*, nothing pivots on it. Rabbi Yochanan himself states in the name of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai: *ein lach davar she-ba le-vatala ela asham nazir bilvad* — the only *korban* that functions purely as an obligation without anything depending on it is the *asham nazir*. If the logic is that a minimum is tolerable when delay causes no irreversible harm, then by *asham nazir* — where nothing at all depends on it — a minimum should have been imposed. Yet it was not.

### Why Asham Minimum

*Kashya.* The Gemara leaves the question unresolved. The reasoning advanced — that the Torah omitted minimums where they would cause permanent harm, implying that where a minimum exists it is absolute — does not coherently explain the distribution of minimums across the various *korban* categories. Virtually nothing except certain *ashamos* has a minimum value requirement; the exceptional case is not the absence of a minimum but its presence. The more pointed question, as the Gemara's trajectory reveals, is not why *mechasrei kapara* has no minimum, but why the *asham* specifically was singled out to have one at all. That question remains open.
