# Zevachim 116 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Noach & Animal Types

### Noach to Mishkan

The daf opens in the midst of a wide-ranging discussion of the pre-*Mishkan* period of sacrificial practice. In the earlier portion of the sugya, the Gemara addressed which functionaries performed the *avodah* before the erection of the *Mishkan* — whether it was the firstborn or the *kohanim* — and when precisely the *kohanim* assumed that role. A parallel question was raised about the details of the *olah*: one view holds that the procedural specifics of the *olah* were already in place at Har Sinai, while another view holds that those details waited until the giving of *Vayikra* at the *Mishkan*. The Gemara now turns to a related but distinct question: what categories of animals were eligible for sacrifice in that entire prior period, from the time of Noach through the *Mishkan*?

The working assumption is that whatever was true from the time of Noach remains in force until the laws change with the erection of the *Mishkan* and the giving of *Vayikra*. This raises an implicit and important question — when exactly did we cease being *Bnei Noach* and become *Bnei Yisrael*, with different rules beginning to apply? The most natural answer is that the transition occurs at *ma'amad Har Sinai*. Yet even granting that transition, the Gemara's position appears to be that many of the sacrificial rules remain unchanged until *Vayikra* introduces the full system, while some rules do shift at Har Sinai itself.

### Kosher Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְכָרִים וּנְקֵבוֹת, תְּמִימִין וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין – דְּאָמַר מָר: תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בִּבְהֵמָה, וְאֵין תַּמּוּת וְזַכְרוּת בְּעוֹפוֹת. וְאִיתַּקַּשׁ בְּהֵמָה לְעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** That which was taught in the *baraita*: All animals were fit to be sacrificed: **Males and females, unblemished and blemished** animals, pertains to **that** which **the Master said** as a principle concerning the *halakhot* of sacrifices: The requirements that an offering must have **unblemished status and** that a burnt offering must have **male status** apply **to animal** offerings, **but** the requirements of **unblemished status and male status** do **not** apply **to birds.** **And** in the verses that recount Noah's sacrifices upon exiting the ark, **a domesticated animal is juxtaposed with a bird,** in the verse: "Of every pure animal, and of every pure fowl" (Genesis 8:20), which teaches that kosher animals had a status identical to that of birds, and could be sacrificed whether male or female, unblemished or blemished.

A *baraisa* states that in this pre-*Mishkan* period, all types of animals were acceptable for sacrifice. The Gemara asks: *Mina'i milei* — what is the source? Rav Huna cites the verse describing Noach's sacrifice after leaving the ark: *Vayiven Noach mizbe'ach l'Hashem, vayikach mikol habeheima hatehora umikol ha'of hatehora* — he built an altar and took from every kosher animal and every kosher bird. The verse imposes no distinctions of male or female, blemished or unblemished. *Beheima kemashma'on* — the word *beheima* in its plain sense includes all domesticated animals; *chaya bichlal beheima* — wild animals such as deer are similarly included. So male and female, blemished and unblemished (*zachar u'nekeiva, temimim u'va'alei mumin*) — all were acceptable.

### Ark Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּמִימִין וּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין – לְאַפּוֹקֵי מְחוּסַּר אֵבֶר, דְּלָא. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לִמְחוּסַּר אֵבֶר שֶׁנֶּאֱסַר לִבְנֵי נֹחַ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וּמִכׇּל הָחַי מִכׇּל בָּשָׂר״ – אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: הָבֵא בְּהֵמָה שֶׁחַיִּין רָאשֵׁי אֵיבָרִין שֶׁלָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: That which was taught in the *baraita*: **Unblemished and blemished** animals, serves **to exclude** animals that are **lacking a limb, which** were **not** fit for sacrifice. As **Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **that** an animal that is **lacking a limb is forbidden to the descendants of Noah,** i.e., gentiles, to be used as a sacrifice? **The verse states** with regard to Noah: **"And of every living being of all flesh,** two of every sort shall you bring into the ark" (Genesis 6:19). With regard to the phrase: "And of every living being," which is superfluous, **the Torah stated: Bring an animal whose limbs are** all **living,** not one lacking a limb, as that animal is disqualified from sacrifice.

One qualification, however, is that the animals must be *tehoros* — kosher species. This is derived directly from the verse's specification of *habeheima hatehora*.

### Exclusions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא לְמַעוֹטֵי טְרֵיפָה? הָהוּא מִ״לְּחַיּוֹת זֶרַע״ נָפְקָא. הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵיפָה אֵינָהּ יוֹלֶדֶת, אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר טְרֵיפָה יוֹלֶדֶת – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? הָאָמַר קְרָא: ״אִתָּךְ״ – בְּדוֹמִין לָךְ. וְדִילְמָא נֹחַ גּוּפֵיהּ טְרֵיפָה הֲוָה? ״תָּמִים״ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ. וְדִילְמָא תָּמִים בִּדְרָכָיו? ״צַדִּיק״ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ. וְדִילְמָא ״תָּמִים״ – בִּדְרָכָיו, ״צַדִּיק״ – בְּמַעֲשָׂיו? אִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ דְּנֹחַ גּוּפֵיהּ טְרֵיפָה הֲוָה, מִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ לְנֹחַ: דִּכְוָותָךְ עַיֵּיל, שְׁלֵמִים לָא תְּעַיֵּיל?! וּמֵאַחַר דְּנָפְקָא לַן מֵ״אִתָּךְ״, ״לְחַיּוֹת זֶרַע״ לְמָה לִי? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: ״אִתָּךְ״ – לְצַוְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא, אֲפִילּוּ זָקֵן אֲפִילּוּ סָרִיס; קָמַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** this phrase: "And of every living being," serves **to exclude an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*]** from being fit as a sacrifice. The Gemara explains: The disqualification of a *tereifa* **is derived from** the phrase: **"To keep seed alive"** (Genesis 7:3), as a *tereifa* cannot propagate. **This works out well according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* cannot give birth.** In this case the disqualification of the *tereifa* is derived from the verse: "To keep seed alive," while the disqualification of the animal lacking a limb is derived from the verse: "And of every living being." **But according to the one who says** that **a *tereifa* can give birth, what can be said?** According to this opinion, a *tereifa* cannot be excluded by the phrase: "To keep seed alive." The Gemara explains: **Doesn't the verse state** with regard to the animals that were brought by Noah into the ark: "You shall bring into the ark, to keep them alive **with you"** (Genesis 6:19)? The term "with you" indicates that the verse is stated **with regard to** animals that are **similar to you,** not a *tereifa*. **But perhaps Noah himself was a *tereifa*.** If so, one cannot exclude a *tereifa* from the comparison of animals to Noah. The Gemara answers: **It is written about** Noah that he was **"complete"** (Genesis 6:9), which indicates that he was physically whole and unblemished. The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps** the verse means that **his ways were complete,** and it is not referring to Noah's physical attributes. The Gemara explains: **It is** already **written about him** that he was **"righteous"** (Genesis 6:9), which means that his actions were perfect. Consequently, when the verse says that he was also complete, it must be referring to his body. **But perhaps** the verse means that Noah was **complete in his manner,** and he was **righteous in his** good **deeds.** Accordingly, the verse would not exclude the possibility that Noah himself was a *tereifa*. The Gemara responds: **If it enters your mind** to say **that Noah himself was a *tereifa*, would the Merciful One have said to him: Bring in** *tereifot* **like you** to the ark, but **do not bring in whole** and perfect animals? It is not reasonable to say that there would be a preference for him to bring *tereifot*. Rather, Noah was certainly not a *tereifa*, and the fact that a *tereifa* is disqualified for sacrifice is derived from "with you." **And once we derive** the disqualification of a *tereifa* **from** the term **"with you," why do I** need the phrase **"to keep seed alive"?** The Gemara answers: If one could derive only from **"with you," you would say** that Noah brought the animals to the ark **only for** the purpose of **companionship,** and therefore **even** an animal that is **old** or **even one who is castrated** can come into the ark, provided that it is not a *tereifa*. Therefore, the Merciful One writes: "To keep seed alive," which **teaches us** that only animals that can bear offspring were allowed to be brought into the ark.

Two further exclusions are introduced: an animal missing a limb (*mechusar eiver*) and a *treifa*. How do we know these were disqualified even in the pre-*Mishkan* period, when the rules applied to *Bnei Noach*?

Rabbi Eliezer derives the disqualification of *mechusar eiver* from the verse describing the animals taken onto the ark: *umikol hachai mikol basar* — "from all that is alive, from all flesh." The word *hachai*, "alive," teaches that all of the animal's limbs must be intact; an animal missing a limb does not fully qualify.

The Gemara immediately challenges this: perhaps *umikol hachai* comes to exclude a *treifa*, since a *treifa* is presumed to be dying and thus not fully "alive"? The Gemara responds: the exclusion of *treifa* is derived instead from *lechayot zera* — the verse stating that animals were brought onto the ark to preserve seed, implying they must be capable of reproduction, and a *treifa* cannot give birth or sire offspring.

But the Gemara immediately raises a further objection: *hani nicha leman de'amar treifa eina yoledet* — this works well according to the opinion that a *treifa* cannot give birth. But according to the opinion that a *treifa* can give birth, the *lechayot zera* verse does not implicitly exclude a *treifa*, and we should need *umikol hachai* for that purpose, leaving us without a source for *mechusar eiver*.

The Gemara resolves this by citing the verse *itcha bedomim lach* — "with you, similar to you." God told Noach to bring animals onto the ark that are "like you." Just as Noach was not a *treifa*, so the animals could not be *treifos*. This frees up *umikol hachai* to address *mechusar eiver*.

The Gemara then asks: how do we know Noach himself was not a *treifa*? The verse says *tamim* — he was perfect — which presumably means physically sound. But perhaps *tamim* refers only to his moral character? The verse also says *tzaddik*, and the Gemara suggests that *tamim bedrachav* and *tzaddik bema'asav* are two distinct moral categories — one referring to character traits (the *mussar* dimension of humble, patient temperament), and the other to righteous actions (the *halachic* dimension of how one actually behaves). On this reading, both words describe his ethical standing, neither necessarily attesting to his physical health.

The Gemara's final resolution is elegant: *iy salka da'atcha d'Noach gufei treifa havay, mi amar lei l'Noach d'chavaseich ayeil sheleimim lo tayeil* — if God knew Noach was himself a *treifa*, it would be incoherent for God to instruct him to bring animals "similar to you," as if God intended him to fill the ark with *treifos*. Clearly, the command *itcha* is only meaningful if Noach was healthy; the verse therefore implicitly establishes that Noach was not a *treifa*, and *umikol hachai* remains available to teach the exclusion of *mechusar eiver*.

The Gemara then asks what *lechayot zera* is adding if *itcha* already excludes *treifa* and *umikol hachai* excludes *mechusar eiver*. The answer is that without *lechayot zera*, one might have thought the animals were brought merely for companionship — *l'tzavta b'alma* — perhaps even old or sterile animals. *Lechayot zera* establishes that the purpose was to repopulate the world, and therefore old (*zaken*) or sterile (*saris*) animals would not have served.

To summarize the emerging picture: kosher animals of all species, male or female, blemished or unblemished, were acceptable in the pre-*Mishkan* period. However, an animal missing a limb or a *treifa* was excluded.

### Anachronism Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טְהוֹרִין אֲבָל לֹא טְמֵאִין. וּמִי הֲווֹ טְמֵאִין וּטְהוֹרִין בְּהַהִיא שַׁעְתָּא?! אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מֵאוֹתָן שֶׁלֹּא נֶעֶבְדָה בָּהֶן עֲבֵירָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* also teaches that before the Tabernacle was constructed, sacrifices were brought from animals and birds that were **kosher, but not** from **non-kosher** species. This is based on the verse that describes what Noah sacrificed when he exited the ark: "And he took of every pure animal and of every pure fowl and offered burnt offerings on the altar" (Genesis 8:20). The Gemara asks: **And were there pure and impure** species **at that time,** during the period of Noah? The distinction between pure, i.e., kosher species, and impure, i.e., non-kosher species, was introduced only after the Torah was given (see Leviticus, chapter 11). **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yonatan says:** The pure animals that Noah took were **from those that had not been used in the performance of sin.**

A striking question now surfaces. The entire derivation depends on the distinction between *beheima tehora* and *beheima teme'a* — kosher and non-kosher species. But the laws of *kashrut* were not yet given at the time of Noach. How can the verse assume Noach knew which animals were *tahor* and which were *tamei*? *Umi havei tamei vetahor beha'hi sha'ata* — since when did this distinction exist at that moment?

It should also be noted that the *girsa* in our text — the version of Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yonatan that reads *me'otan shelo na'avda bahen aveira* ("those not used in sinful acts") — appears to be a textual displacement from a different sugya. Elsewhere, the Gemara asks why the Torah says *ish ve'ishto* ("a man and his wife") regarding the animals boarding the ark, since animals do not observe the institution of marriage. The answer there is that *ish ve'ishto* means animals that had not been used in bestiality or inter-species breeding. That statement of Rabbi Yonatan properly belongs to that discussion, and it appears to have been imported here by association. The relevant *girsa* for our sugya is *me'otan ha'atidim lehitaher*.

### Future Kashrut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הֲווֹ יָדְעִי? כִּדְרַב חִסְדָּא. דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הֶעֱבִירָן לִפְנֵי הַתֵּיבָה; כֹּל שֶׁהַתֵּיבָה קוֹלַטְתָּן – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהוּא טָהוֹר, אֵין הַתֵּיבָה קוֹלַטְתָּן – בְּיָדוּעַ שֶׁהֵן טְמֵאִין. רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהַבָּאִים זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה״ – הַבָּאִין מֵאֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where did** Noah and his sons **know** which animals had been used in the performance of a sin, in order to prevent them from entering the ark? The Gemara answers that it is **in accordance with** the statement **of Rav Ḥisda. As Rav Ḥisda says:** Noah caused all of the animals to **pass before the ark. All** animals **that the ark accepted,** i.e., drew in, were **known to be pure;** if **the ark did not accept them,** it was **known that they** were **impure.** **Rabbi Abbahu says** there is a different explanation as to how Noah knew which animals were pure or impure. **The verse states: "And they that went in,** went in **male and female** of all flesh" (Genesis 7:16), which means: Those **that went in on their own.** Consequently, Noah did not need to distinguish between pure and impure animals, as only the pure ones approached.

The correct resolution is *me'otan ha'atidim lehitaher* — the *tehoros* Noach brought were those animals that the Torah would eventually designate as kosher. It is not that the laws of *kashrut* applied to Noach; rather, those species that would later be classified as *tahor* are what the verse refers to.

But this prompts a secondary question: how did Noach know which species would eventually be so classified? The Gemara answers: *ki d'Rav Chisda* — as Rav Chisda teaches, the animals were passed before the ark, and those the ark admitted were *tahor* while those it did not admit were *tamei*. (This teaching is again borrowed by analogy from another sugya where the ark accepted only animals that had not engaged in sin; here it is adapted to explain the *tahor/tamei* distinction.) Rabbi Abbahu offers a different answer: *vehaba'im zachar u'nekeiva haba'im me'aleihem* — the animals came on their own, and those that came in groups of seven identified themselves as *tahor*, while those that came in pairs of two were *tamei*.

Rashi in his Torah commentary resolves the anachronism differently, stating that Noach had learned Torah — *melameid shelamad Noach Torah*. The Gemara does not give this answer here, a discrepancy that the *Maharsha* discusses at length. One could also note, as the *Ramban* points out in other contexts, that certain concepts antedated the formal commandments — just as the concept underlying *yibbum* appears in the story of Yehuda and Tamar before it was legislated. So too there may have been a way of categorizing animals that predated the formal prohibition on eating non-kosher species. The Gemara, however, does not pursue this line, resting instead on *ha'atidim lehitaher*.

## Olah vs. Shelamim

### Only Olot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְהַכֹּל קָרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת. עוֹלוֹת אִין, שְׁלָמִים לָא. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּזְבְּחוּ זְבָחִים שְׁלָמִים לַה׳ פָּרִים״! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הַכֹּל קָרְבוּ עוֹלוֹת [וּשְׁלָמִים]. וְהָתַנְיָא: אֲבָל שְׁלָמִים לָא, כִּי אִם עוֹלוֹת. עוֹלוֹת אִין, שְׁלָמִים לָא! כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא קֵרְבוּ שְׁלָמִים בְּנֵי נֹחַ – דְּאִיתְּמַר: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא, חַד אָמַר: קֵרְבוּ שְׁלָמִים בְּנֵי נֹחַ, וְחַד אָמַר: לֹא קֵרְבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § In describing the sacrificial service before the Tabernacle was constructed, **the Master said: And all** offerings brought before the construction of the Tabernacle were **sacrificed** as **burnt offerings.** The Gemara infers: **Burnt offerings, yes,** were sacrificed, but **peace offerings** were **not** sacrificed. The Gemara challenges: **But** with regard to the offerings that were sacrificed at Mount Sinai at the time of the giving of the Torah, **it is written:** "And they offered burnt offerings, **and sacrificed peace offerings of oxen to the Lord"** (Exodus 24:5). This event occurred prior to the construction of the Tabernacle. **Rather, say** that the *baraita* means: **All** offerings **sacrificed** were either **burnt offerings or peace offerings.** The Gemara challenges: **And isn't it taught** in another *baraita*: **But peace offerings** were **not** sacrificed before the construction of the Tabernacle; **rather, only burnt offerings** were sacrificed? Clearly, **burnt offerings, yes,** were sacrificed, but **peace offerings** were **not** sacrificed. The Gemara answers that this *baraita* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **the one who says** that **peace offerings were not sacrificed** by the **descendants of Noah. As it was stated** that there is a dispute between **Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina** with regard to this: **One says** that **the descendants of Noah sacrificed peace offerings, and one says** that **they did not sacrifice** peace offerings.

The Gemara now addresses a further assertion from the *baraisa*: that throughout this period, all brought only *olot*. The Gemara asks: *olos in, shelamim lo* — are you truly saying they could not bring *shelamim*? The exclusion of *chatas* and *asham* is understandable, since the entire framework of atoning for specific transgressions had not yet been instituted. But why exclude *shelamim*?

The assertion is that the *olah* was universal — shared by *Bnei Yisrael* and *Bnei Noach* alike — while the *shelamim* was not available to *Bnei Noach*.

### Hevel's Fats

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר קֵרְבוּ שְׁלָמִים בְּנֵי נֹחַ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהֶבֶל הֵבִיא גַם הוּא מִבְּכוֹרוֹת צֹאנוֹ וּמֵחֶלְבֵהֶן״ – אֵיזֶהוּ דָּבָר שֶׁחֶלְבּוֹ קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, וְאֵין כּוּלּוֹ קָרֵב לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: זֶה שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two opinions: **What is the reasoning of the one who says** that **the descendants of Noah sacrificed peace offerings? As it is written: "And Abel, he also brought of the firstborn of his flock and of the fat thereof"** (Genesis 4:4). Abel, like all gentiles, is categorized as a descendant of Noah. The verse emphasizes that the fat was sacrificed. The Gemara analyzes: **What is an item,** i.e., an offering, **the fat of which is sacrificed upon the altar, but** that **is not sacrificed in its entirety upon the altar? You must say: This is the peace offering,** the meat of which is consumed.

The Gemara immediately challenges this from the story of Hevel: *vehaktiv vayizbechu zevachim shelamim la'Hashem parim* — and it is written that they offered *shelamim* to God, cattle, right there at Har Sinai. This seems to contradict the assertion that only *olot* were brought until the *Mishkan*.

The Gemara resolves this by reinterpreting the statement: the *baraisa* should be read as saying that all people — both *Yisrael* and *Bnei Noach* — brought *olot*, meaning the *olah* was universal. The *shelamim*, however, was introduced specifically for *Bnei Yisrael* upon Har Sinai. So the foot of Har Sinai marks not only a transition in personal status from *Bnei Noach* to *Bnei Yisrael*, but also the moment at which *shelamim* became available. The laws governing which animals are eligible remain unchanged until *Vayikra*, but the category of *shelamim* is newly introduced here.

What is the basis for Hevel's *shelamim*? The verse says *umichelveihen* — and from their fats. The paradigm of a *shelamim* is precisely that the fats go on the altar while the meat is eaten: *eizehu davar shechelvoh karev legabei mizbeiach ve'ein kulo karev legabei mizbeiach hevei omer zeh shelamim* — that which has its fat offered on the altar but is not entirely consumed on the altar, that is a *shelamim*. If Hevel was offering only the fats to God while the rest was not burned, this looks exactly like a *shelamim*.

### Isaiah Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לֹא קֵרְבוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״עוּרִי צָפוֹן וּבוֹאִי תֵימָן״ – תִּתְנַעֵר אוּמָּה שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׂיהָ בַּצָּפוֹן, וְתָבוֹא אוּמָּה שֶׁמַּעֲשֶׂיהָ בַּצָּפוֹן וּבַדָּרוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **What is the reasoning of the one who says** that **they did not sacrifice** peace offerings? **As it is written: "Awake [*uri*], O north; and come, south…**Let my beloved come into his garden, and eat his precious fruits" (Song of Songs 4:16). The Gemara interprets this homiletically: The **nation,** i.e., the nations of the world, who are the descendants of Noah, **whose acts,** i.e., sacrifices, are only **in the north,** i.e., they sacrifice only burnt offerings, which are slaughtered and their blood collected in the north of the Temple courtyard, **shall be removed [*titna'er*], and** in its place **shall come** the Jewish **nation, whose acts,** i.e., sacrifices, are **in the north and in the south,** as they sacrifice burnt offerings, whose rites are performed in the north, and peace offerings, whose rites may also be performed in the south, as the entire courtyard is fit for their rites.

The opinion that *Bnei Noach* did not bring *shelamim* supports its position from the verse in Yeshayahu: *uri tzafon uvo'i teiman* — stir yourself from the north and come from the south. The Gemara interprets this as: may the nation that brings only from the north — i.e., only *olot*, which are brought at the northern side of the altar — be replaced by the nation that brings from both the north and the south, i.e., both *olot* and *shelamim*. The nation that brings only *olot* is *Bnei Noach*; the nation that brings both is *Bnei Yisrael*. One must acknowledge that this is a rather weak proof.

The Gemara then tests both sides. To the view that *Bnei Noach* did bring *shelamim*, how do you explain *umichelveihen* by Hevel? The answer: *mishmeneihen* — the verse means from the fat animals, not that only the fat was offered; perhaps the rest was simply discarded. To the view that *Bnei Noach* did not bring *shelamim*, how do you explain the verse in Yeshayahu? The answer: *hu bekibutz galuyot hu dechtiv* — that verse refers to the ingathering of the exiles at the time of *Mashiach*, not to the categories of sacrifice available to *Bnei Noach*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״וּמֵחֶלְבֵיהֶן״! מִשַּׁמִּנֵיהוֹן [דִּידְהוּ]. וּמָר נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״עוּרִי צָפוֹן״! [הָהוּא] בְּקִיבּוּץ גָּלִיּוֹת הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And also** according to **the Master** who holds that the descendants of Noah did not sacrifice peace offerings, **isn't it written: "And of the fat thereof,"** from which it may be derived that Abel sacrificed a peace offering? The Gemara answers: "The fat thereof," does not mean that Abel sacrificed only the fats of his offerings; rather, it means that he sacrificed **the fattest of them,** i.e., the fattest and choicest of his animals. The Gemara asks: **And also** according to **the Master** who holds that the descendants of Noah did sacrifice peace offerings, **isn't it written: "Awake, O north,"** from which it may be derived that the nations of the world do not sacrifice peace offerings? The Gemara answers: In his opinion, **that** verse **is written with regard to the ingathering of the exiles,** i.e., the Jewish exiles will come from the north and the south.

Further verses are tested. The verse *vayomer Moshe gam atah titein beyadeinu zevachim ve'olot* — Moshe saying to Pharaoh that you will give us *zevachim* and *olot* — seems to show *shelamim* were brought before Har Sinai. The Gemara responds: *zevachim la'achilah ve'olot lehakravah* — the *zevachim* referred to meat for eating, not sacrificial *shelamim*, while the *olot* were the actual sacrifices.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר מֹשֶׁה גַּם אַתָּה תִּתֵּן בְּיָדֵינוּ זְבָחִים וְעֹלֹת וְעָשִׂינוּ לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ״! זְבָחִים לַאֲכִילָה, וְעוֹלוֹת לְהַקְרָבָה. וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּקַּח יִתְרוֹ [חֹתֵן מֹשֶׁה] עוֹלָה וּזְבָחִים״! הַהוּא לְאַחַר מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הוּא דִּכְתִיב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinion that the descendants of Noah did not sacrifice peace offerings, the Gemara asks: **But isn't it written: "And Moses said: You must also give into our hand sacrifices [*zevaḥim*] and burnt offerings, that we may sacrifice to the Lord our God"** (Exodus 10:25)? This indicates that sacrifices [*zevaḥim*], i.e., peace offerings, were sacrificed before the Torah was given. The Gemara answers: In this context, **"*zevaḥim*"** is referring to animals to be used **for consumption,** as the word *zevaḥ* can also be translated as an animal for slaughter, **and "burnt offerings"** is referring to animals to be used **for sacrifice.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it written** before the giving of the Torah: **"And Yitro, Moses' father-in-law, took a burnt offering and sacrifices [*zevaḥim*]** for God" (Exodus 18:12)? Since the word *zevaḥim* there is referring to sacrifices, as the verse clearly states that Yitro took them for God, evidently peace offerings were sacrificed before the giving of the Torah. The Gemara answers: **That** verse **was written** with regard to the period **after the giving of the Torah,** when the Jewish people were permitted to sacrifice peace offerings.

### Yitro's Timing

### Three Triggers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: [יִתְרוֹ] אַחַר מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הָיָה; אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: [יִתְרוֹ] קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הָיָה – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? דְּאִיתְּמַר: בְּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי; חַד אָמַר: יִתְרוֹ קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הָיָה, וְחַד אָמַר: יִתְרוֹ אַחַר מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הָיָה. לְמַאן דְּאָמַר יִתְרוֹ קוֹדֶם מַתַּן תּוֹרָה הָיָה – קָסָבַר שְׁלָמִים הִקְרִיבוּ בְּנֵי נֹחַ. כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״וַיִּשְׁמַע יִתְרוֹ כֹהֵן מִדְיָן״ – מָה שְׁמוּעָה שָׁמַע, וּבָא וְנִתְגַּיֵּיר? רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: מִלְחֶמֶת עֲמָלֵק שָׁמַע, שֶׁהֲרֵי כְּתִיב בְּצִדּוֹ: ״וַיַּחֲלֹשׁ יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אֶת עֲמָלֵק וְאֶת עַמּוֹ לְפִי חָרֶב״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר הַמּוֹדָעִי אוֹמֵר: מַתַּן תּוֹרָה שָׁמַע [וּבָא], שֶׁכְּשֶׁנִּיתְּנָה תּוֹרָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הָיָה קוֹלוֹ הוֹלֵךְ מִסּוֹף הָעוֹלָם וְעַד סוֹפוֹ, וְכׇל [מַלְכֵי] אוּמּוֹת הָעוֹלָם אֲחָזָתַן רְעָדָה בְּהֵיכְלֵיהֶן וְאָמְרוּ שִׁירָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּבְהֵיכָלוֹ כּוּלּוֹ אוֹמֵר כָּבוֹד״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **This works out well according to the one who says** that the episode with **Yitro was after the giving of the Torah. But according to the one who says** that the episode with **Yitro was before the giving of the Torah, what can be said? As it was stated: The sons of Rabbi Ḥiyya and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** engage in a dispute concerning this issue. **One says** that the episode with **Yitro was before the giving of the Torah, and one says** that the episode with **Yitro was after the giving of the Torah.** The Gemara answers: **The one who says** that the episode with **Yitro was before the giving of the Torah maintains** that **the descendants of Noah did sacrifice peace offerings.** § The Gemara notes that the disagreement between *amora'im* with regard to when Yitro came to Mount Sinai is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*:** The Torah states with regard to Yitro, before he came to Mount Sinai: **"Now Yitro, the priest of Midian, Moses' father-in-law, heard** of all that God had done for Moses, and for Israel His people, how the Lord had brought Israel out of Egypt" (Exodus 18:1). **What tiding did he hear that he came and converted? Rabbi Yehoshua says: He heard** about **the war with Amalek, as it is written adjacent to** the verses that state that Yitro came: **"And Joshua weakened Amalek and his people with the edge of the sword"** (Exodus 17:13). **Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says: He heard** about **the giving of the Torah and came. As when the Torah was given to the Jewish people, the voice of** the Holy One, Blessed be He, **went from** one **end of the world to the** other **end, and all of the kings of the nations of the world were overcome with trembling in their palaces and recited a song** of praise, **as it is stated:** "The voice of the Lord makes the hinds to calve…**and in his palace all say: Glory"** (Psalms 29:9), i.e., each king in his own palace recited songs of praise to God.

This brings the Gemara to a formal *tannaitic* debate about when Yitro arrived. The sons of Rabbi Chiya and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi dispute: one holds that Yitro came before *matan Torah*, one holds he came after. The Gemara then cites a *baraisa* in which three *tannaim* each identify what Yitro heard that motivated him to come:

Rabbi Yehoshua says Yitro heard about the war with Amalek — which is narrated immediately before the account of Yitro's arrival in the Torah — and came. This places him before *matan Torah*.

Rabbi Elazar HaModa'i says Yitro heard about *matan Torah* and came, placing him after the events of Har Sinai. This reading, though against the plain chronological order of the *pesukim*, is supported by the observation that Yitro's advice to Moshe about delegating judgment makes more sense in the context of a fully constituted legal system after *matan Torah*.

Rabbi Elazar says Yitro heard about *kri'at Yam Suf* and came, which chronologically would place him before Har Sinai but after the Exodus. The verse in *Yehoshua* describing how all the kings of Canaan trembled when they heard about the splitting of the sea is adduced to show how far-reaching that event's impact was.

### Sinai Proclamation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְקַבְּצוּ כּוּלָּם אֵצֶל בִּלְעָם הָרָשָׁע, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: מָה קוֹל הֶהָמוֹן אֲשֶׁר שָׁמַעְנוּ? שֶׁמָּא מַבּוּל בָּא לָעוֹלָם (אָמַר לָהֶם) – ״ה׳ לַמַּבּוּל יָשָׁב״? [אָמַר לָהֶם]: ״וַיֵּשֶׁב ה׳ מֶלֶךְ לְעוֹלָם״ – כְּבָר נִשְׁבַּע הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא מַבּוּל לָעוֹלָם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מַבּוּל שֶׁל מַיִם אֵינוֹ מֵבִיא, אֲבָל מַבּוּל שֶׁל אֵשׁ מֵבִיא – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי (הִנֵּה) בָּאֵשׁ ה׳ נִשְׁפָּט״. אָמַר לָהֶן: כְּבָר נִשְׁבַּע שֶׁאֵינוֹ מַשְׁחִית כׇּל בָּשָׂר. וּמֶה קוֹל הֶהָמוֹן הַזֶּה שֶׁשָּׁמַעְנוּ? אָמַר לָהֶם: חֶמְדָּה טוֹבָה יֵשׁ לוֹ בְּבֵית גְּנָזָיו, שֶׁהָיְתָה גְּנוּזָה אֶצְלוֹ תְּשַׁע מֵאוֹת שִׁבְעִים וְאַרְבָּעָה דּוֹרוֹת קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּבְרָא הָעוֹלָם; וּבִיקֵּשׁ לִיתְּנָהּ לְבָנָיו, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״ה׳ עוֹז לְעַמּוֹ יִתֵּן״. [מִיָּד] פָּתְחוּ כּוּלָּם וְאָמְרוּ: ״ה׳ יְבָרֵךְ אֶת עַמּוֹ בַשָּׁלוֹם״.
>
> **Translation:** At that time, **all of** the kings **gathered around Balaam the wicked,** who was the greatest gentile prophet, **and said to him: What is the tumultuous sound,** i.e., the loud noise, **that we have heard? Perhaps a flood is coming to** destroy **the world, as it is stated: "The Lord sat enthroned at the flood"** (Psalms 29:10)? Balaam **said to them: "The Lord sits as King forever"** (Psalms 29:10), which means that **the Holy One, Blessed be He, already took an oath** after the flood **never to bring a flood to the world,** as it is stated: "And the waters shall no more become a flood" (Genesis 9:15). The kings **said to him: He will not bring a flood of water,** as he vowed, **but** perhaps **He will bring a flood of fire,** as in the future the Lord will punish the nations with fire, **as it is stated: "For by fire will the Lord contend,** and by His sword with all flesh; and the slain of the Lord shall be many" (Isaiah 66:16). Balaam **said to them: He already took an oath that He will not destroy all flesh** in any manner, as it is stated: "To destroy all flesh" (Genesis 9:15). Therefore, there will not be a flood of fire. **And** if so, **what is this tumultuous sound that we have heard?** Balaam **said to them: He has a good** and **precious item in His treasury, that was hidden away with Him** for **974 generations before the world was created, and He seeks to give it to his children, as it is stated: "The Lord will give strength to His people"** (Psalms 29:11). "Strength" is a reference to the Torah, which is the strength of the Jewish people. **Immediately, they all began to say: "The Lord will bless His people with peace"** (Psalms 29:11).

The Gemara elaborates on the position that Yitro came because of *matan Torah*, developing a remarkable *aggadic* passage. When the Torah was given at Sinai, God's voice traveled from one end of the world to the other — *haya kolo holech misof ha'olam ve'ad sofo*. All the idolatrous kings trembled in their palaces. They sang praise, as the verse says *uveheichalo kulo omer kavod* — in His palace all declare His glory. They gathered before Bilam the wicked, their one prophetic access point to divine knowledge, and asked: what is this thunderous sound we have heard? Is it perhaps a flood?

Bilam answered: *Hashem lamabul yashav* — God already sat over the flood; He has already sworn not to bring another flood upon the world.

They pressed him: perhaps not a flood of water but a flood of fire, as the verse says *ki hinei Hashem ba'esh nishpat* — God will judge with fire? Bilam answered: He has already sworn not to destroy all flesh by any means.

Then they asked again: so what is this thunderous voice? Bilam answered: *chemda tova yesh lo beveit genazav* — there is a precious treasure in God's treasure houses, hidden away for 974 generations before the world was created, and God is now giving it to His children, as the verse says *Hashem oz le'amo yiten*. Immediately the kings opened and said: *Hashem yivarech et amo vashalom* — God should bless His people with peace.

The Gemara notes that the verse describing the Torah as *davar tziva le'elef dor* — the word commanded for a thousand generations — points to this same idea: God had been holding the Torah in reserve, not merely since creation but for a span of 974 generations prior, waiting for the moment to give it to *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Rachav Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: קְרִיעַת יַם סוּף שָׁמַע וּבָא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיְהִי כִשְׁמֹעַ כׇּל מַלְכֵי הָאֱמֹרִי״. וְאַף רָחָב הַזּוֹנָה אָמְרָה לִשְׁלוּחֵי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: ״כִּי שָׁמַעְנוּ אֵת אֲשֶׁר הוֹבִישׁ ה׳ אֶת מֵי יַם סוּף״. מַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם, דַּאֲמַר (לֵיהּ) ״וְלֹא הָיָה בָם עוֹד רוּחַ״; וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא, דְּקָאָמַר: ״וְלֹא קָמָה עוֹד רוּחַ בְּאִישׁ״? דַּאֲפִילּוּ אִקַּשּׁוֹיֵי נָמֵי לָא אִקַּשּׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers another explanation of what Yitro heard: **Rabbi Eliezer says: He heard** about **the splitting of the Red Sea and came, as it is stated** in a similar context with regard to the splitting of the Jordan in the days of Joshua: **"And it came to pass, when all the kings of the Amorites,** that were beyond the Jordan westward, and all the kings of the Canaanites, that were by the sea, **heard** how the Lord had dried up the waters of the Jordan from before the children of Israel, until they were passed over, that their heart melted, neither was there spirit in them anymore, because of the children of Israel" (Joshua 5:1). **And even Rahab the prostitute said to Joshua's messengers: "For we have heard how the Lord dried up the water of the Red Sea** before you" (Joshua 2:10). **What is different there,** i.e., with regard to the splitting of the Jordan, **where** the verse **states: "Neither was there spirit in them anymore," and what is different here,** i.e., in the statement of Rahab, **where** the verse **states: "Neither did there remain [*kama*] any more spirit in any man"** (Joshua 2:11)? The Gemara replies that Rahab used this phrase euphemistically, to say that their fear was so great **that** their male organs **were not even** able to **become erect,** as "*kama*" also means rise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְנָא יָדְעָה? דְּאָמַר מָר: אֵין לָךְ כׇּל שַׂר וְנָגִיד שֶׁלֹּא בָּא עַל רָחָב הַזּוֹנָה. אָמְרוּ: בַּת עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים הָיְתָה כְּשֶׁיָּצְאוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרַיִם, וְזִנְּתָה [כׇּל] אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה שֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בַּמִּדְבָּר. אַחַר חֲמִישִּׁים שָׁנָה נִתְגַּיְּירָה, אָמְרָה: ״יְהֵא מָחוּל לִי בִּשְׂכַר חֶבֶל חַלּוֹן וּפִשְׁתִּים״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how did** Rahab **know** this? The Gemara replies: **As the Master said: You do not have any prince or ruler** at that time **who did not engage in intercourse with Rahab the prostitute.** The Gemara adds that the Sages **said** with regard to Rahab: **She was ten years old when the Jewish people left Egypt, and she engaged in prostitution all forty years that the Jewish people were in the wilderness. After** that, when she was **fifty years** old, she **converted** when the two spies visited her. **She said: May** all of my sins of prostitution **be forgiven me as a reward** for having endangered myself with the **rope, window, and flax,** by means of which I saved Joshua's two spies. Rahab first concealed the spies in stalks of flax, and later assisted them in exiting her home by lowering them from the window with a rope (see Joshua 2:6 and 2:15).

Rabbi Elazar's position that *kri'at Yam Suf* was the motivating event leads the Gemara into a digression about Rachav. The verse in *Yehoshua* describing the kings of Canaan — *velo kamah od ruach be'ish* — uses the phrase "spirit did not stand up in a man." Rachav herself says to the spies of Yehoshua that they heard how God dried up the waters of the *Yam Suf*. The Gemara compares an earlier verse about the kings of the *Emorim* — *velo hayah bam od ruach* — which says the spirit was not in them, to Rachav's formulation, which says the spirit did not *stand up*. What is the significance of "stand up" specifically in Rachav's context?

The Gemara answers bluntly: *de'afilu akshuyei nami lo akshu* — they were not even able to achieve an erection. The word *kamah* — to stand, to rise — is used deliberately in Rachav's formulation because she was a prostitute and knew from personal experience. *D'amar mar ein lecha kol sar venagid shelo ba al Rachav ha'Zonah* — there was not a single officer or dignitary who had not come to Rachav, and she thus had direct knowledge of this physiological effect of terror.

The Gemara then records that Rachav was ten years old when *Bnei Yisrael* left Egypt, and she practiced her trade for the entire forty years they were in the wilderness, making her fifty when she converted upon the conquest of Yericho. She said: *yehei machul li bischar chevel chalon u'fishtim* — may I be forgiven in the merit of the rope, the window, and the flax with which she hid the spies. Rashi explains that these very objects had served as tools of her prostitution — the rope to let clients in through the window, the flax to conceal them — and now she was using them in the service of *Bnei Yisrael*, turning the instruments of sin into instruments of redemption.

### Final Position

The Gemara closes this section by establishing the final position. According to the *baraisa*, *Bnei Noach* could bring only an *olah*; the *shelamim* was limited to *Bnei Yisrael*. The position that *Bnei Noach* could also bring a *shelamim* is linked to the view that Yitro came before *matan Torah* — for if he arrived after *matan Torah* and converted, his *shelamim* poses no difficulty. Conversely, if one holds that *Bnei Noach* cannot bring a *shelamim*, one must hold that Yitro came after *matan Torah* and had converted by the time he offered that sacrifice.

One might ask what the conceptual basis is for this distinction between *olah* and *shelamim* with respect to *Bnei Noach*. The *olah* is the universal sacrifice — it is given entirely to God, nothing retained. The *shelamim*, by contrast, involves an act of extraordinary intimacy: the worshipper eats from the same animal, as the Gemara elsewhere puts it, *mishulchan gavoah kazkehu* — as one who partakes from the table on high. It is almost as if one is sharing a meal with God. Perhaps this greater degree of closeness is tied to the covenant of *Har Sinai* and the framework of *shemiras hamitzvos*, a level of relationship not available to those outside that covenant.

There is also something structurally striking in the contrast: *Bnei Noach* are permitted to build private altars anywhere and bring sacrifices without any central location — a dispensation that might seem, on its face, more permissive and perhaps more prone to the diffuse, localized character of paganism. Yet they are restricted to the *olah*, the more austere, entirely surrendered sacrifice. *Bnei Yisrael*, constrained to a single central location, are permitted the *shelamim*, the more intimate form. Perhaps the centralization of location makes the intimacy of the *shelamim* less theologically risky; distributed sacrifice everywhere, combined with the communal meal, might too easily shade into the patterns of ancient pagan feast religion.

## Non-Jewish Bamot

### Private Altars

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וְגוֹיִם בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה רַשָּׁאִין לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״דַּבֵּר אֶל בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל מְצוּוִין עַל שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ, וְאֵין הַגּוֹיִם מְצוּוִין עַל שְׁחוּטֵי חוּץ. לְפִיכָךְ כָּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד בּוֹנֶה לוֹ בָּמָה לְעַצְמוֹ, וּמַקְרִיב עָלֶיהָ כׇּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* that discussed the sacrifice of offerings before the construction of the Tabernacle: **And today gentiles are permitted to do so,** i.e., to sacrifice offerings outside the Temple courtyard, despite the fact that this is forbidden for the Jews. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? **As the Sages taught** with regard to the verses that prohibit the slaughter of offerings outside the Temple: **"Speak** to Aaron, and to his sons, and **to** all **the children of Israel"** (Leviticus 17:2). This indicates that only **Jews are commanded with regard to** offerings **slaughtered outside** the Temple, **but gentiles are not commanded with regard to** offerings **slaughtered outside** the Temple. **Therefore, each and every** gentile may, if he desires, **construct** a private **altar for himself, and sacrifice upon it whatever he desires.**

Having established the *halacha* of *Bnei Noach* and sacrifices, the Gemara turns to a rule that operates in the present: *ve'ovdei kochavim bazman hazeh resha'im la'asot ken* — non-Jews today are permitted to do this. That is, they are permitted to build private altars (*bamot*) and offer sacrifices to God wherever they choose. There is no prohibition of *shechitat chutz* — the prohibition against slaughtering sacrifices outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — that applies to them.

### Source Verse

The source is derived from the verse introducing the prohibition of *shechitat chutz*: *daber el Bnei Yisrael* — speak to *Bnei Yisrael*. *Bnei Yisrael metzuvin al shechutei chutz* — *Bnei Yisrael* are commanded regarding slaughter outside the Temple — *ve'ein ovdei kochavim metzuvin al shechutei chutz* — but non-Jews are not so commanded. Therefore each individual non-Jew may build his own private *bamah* and offer upon it.

It should be noted that the term *ovdei kochavim* in our text is a later scribal substitution imposed by censorship; the original text used the generic terms *goyim* or *nochrim* — simply non-Jews. The irony of the current text is not lost: it uses the word "idolaters" while describing a permission predicated on offering sacrifices to the one God. The underlying legal framework, of course, assumes an idealized context in which the non-Jewish sacrifice is directed to God alone. Historically, the reality was quite different — pagans were offering to multiple gods and certainly not consulting *halachic* authorities about the propriety of their private altars. The rule thus operates within a theoretical framework that rarely matched lived reality.

### No Direct Help

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב בַּר אַחָא אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אָסוּר לְסַיְּיעָן וְלַעֲשׂוֹת שְׁלִיחוּתָן. אָמַר רַבָּה: וּלְאוֹרֹיִנְהוּ שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ya'akov bar Aḥa says** that **Rav Asi says:** Although it is permitted for gentiles to sacrifice offerings outside the Temple courtyard, it is **prohibited** for a Jew **to assist them or to fulfill their agency** in this matter, as sacrificing in this manner is forbidden for a Jew. **Rabba said: But to instruct them** how to sacrifice outside the Temple is **permitted.**

Rabbi Yaakov bar Aha said in the name of Rav Assi: *asur lesayei'a vela'asot shlichutan* — it is forbidden for a Jew to actively assist or serve as agent in bringing a non-Jew's sacrifice. Even though the non-Jew is permitted to offer this sacrifice, and even though the sacrifice is not the Jew's own, a Jew may not be the one to physically place it on the altar or directly carry out the act of sacrifice. Whether this prohibition stems from the Jew's own transgression in performing an act that would be an *aveira* if it were his sacrifice, or whether it is a separate concern about being connected to a phenomenon that is off-limits for him, is a question *Tosafos* addresses. Either way, direct physical assistance is forbidden.

### Indirect Help OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּאִיפְרָא הוֹרְמִיז אִימֵּיהּ דְּשַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא שַׁדַּרָה קוּרְבָּנָא לְרָבָא, שְׁלַחָה לֵיהּ: ״אַסְּקוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם״. אֲמַר לְהוּ לְרַב סָפְרָא וּלְרַב אַחָא בַּר הוּנָא: זִילוּ וּדְבַרוּ תְּרֵי עוּלֵמֵי גּוּלָאֵי, וַחֲזוֹו הֵיכָא דְּמַסְּקָא יַמָּא שִׂירְטוֹן; וּשְׁקֻלוּ צִיבֵי חַדְתֵי, וְאַפִּיקוּ נוּרָא מִמָּרָא חַדְתָּא, וְאַסְּקוּהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** This is **similar to that** incident in **which Ifera Hurmiz, the mother of King Shapur** of Persia, **sent an offering to Rava,** with which **she sent** this message **to him: Sacrifice this for me, for the sake of Heaven.** Rava **said to Rav Safra and to Rav Aḥa bar Huna: Go, take two** gentile **youths of the same age,** i.e., similar to one another, so that the sacrifice will be performed with maximal beauty, **and see where the sea** currently **raises silt [*sirton*],** which is a place that no one has used before. **And take new wood and bring out fire from new vessels, and** the two youths will **sacrifice** the offering **for her, for the sake of Heaven.**

Rava rules: *ve'arunehu lehu sharei* — one may, however, give them instruction and advice. There is a meaningful distinction between actively performing the sacrifice and advising a non-Jew on how to do so properly. This question has broader analogues in *halachic* discourse. For instance, the question of whether a Jew may donate to the rebuilding of a church — a structure that for a Jew would implicate *avoda zara*, but for a non-Jew may be entirely permissible — touches on similar considerations. Directly building such a structure seems clearly problematic for a Jew; contributing money at a remove raises different questions. Here too, direct involvement is forbidden; indirect guidance is permitted.

### Ifra Hormiz Case

Rava instructed his students, Rav Safra and Rav Acha bar Huna: go take two young non-Jewish men. Go find a place where the sea has recently deposited silt — newly exposed land that has never been used for mundane purposes. Take fresh wood that has not previously been used. Bring fire kindled from a new vessel. Have these young men offer the sacrifice on her behalf for the sake of heaven.

### Fresh Materials

Rava's meticulous insistence on fresh, previously unused materials in every dimension of the sacrifice — the land, the wood, the fire, and even the young men themselves — reflects a principle that everything connected to the divine service should be untouched by prior mundane use. The *Maharsha* notes that even the selection of young men who had not yet been associated with any particular trade may reflect this sensibility of freshness and prior non-use, however unusual it seems to apply such a concept to human beings.

## Fresh Materials Rule

### No Prior Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: כְּמַאן – כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ אוֹמֵר: מָה מִזְבֵּחַ – שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בּוֹ הֶדְיוֹט, אַף עֵצִים – שֶׁלֹּא יִשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בָּהֶן הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: **In accordance with whose** opinion was the instruction to sacrifice exclusively with new wood? Was it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua? As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua says: Just as** the **altar** is a place **that is not used by an ordinary** person, **so too,** the **wood** that will be used **must not be used by an ordinary** person.

Abaye asks Rava: according to whose opinion are you acting with such care that everything be fresh and unused? The answer is: *ke'Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua*. As taught in a *baraisa*, Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua derives from the altar itself a principle about the wood used upon it: just as the altar was never used by a commoner (*shelo nishtamesh bo hedyot*), so too the wood used on the altar must never have been put to mundane use. One may not take an old table or discarded furniture and use it as firewood on the altar.

### Bamah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מוֹדֶה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ בְּבָמָה! דְּתַנְיָא, כָּתוּב אֶחָד אוֹמֵר: ״וַיִּתֵּן דָּוִד לְאׇרְנָן בַּמָּקוֹם שִׁקְלֵי זָהָב מִשְׁקָל שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקֶן דָּוִד אֶת הַגֹּרֶן וְאֶת הַבָּקָר בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים חֲמִשִּׁים״; הָא כֵּיצַד? גּוֹבֶה מִכׇּל שֵׁבֶט וָשֵׁבֶט חֲמִשִּׁים, שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת. רַבִּי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אַבָּא יוֹסֵי בֶּן דּוֹסְתַּאי: בָּקָר [וְעֵצִים] וּמְקוֹם מִזְבֵּחַ בַּחֲמִשִּׁים, וְכׇל הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ בְּשֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ אוֹמֵר: בָּקָר וְעֵצִים וּמְקוֹם מִזְבֵּחַ בַּחֲמִשִּׁים, וְכׇל הַבַּיִת כּוּלּוֹ בְּשֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת; דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֲרַוְנָה אֶל דָּוִד: יִקַּח וְיַעַל אֲדוֹנִי הַמֶּלֶךְ [הַטּוֹב בְּעֵינָיו], רְאֵה הַבָּקָר לָעֹלָה וְהַמֹּרִגִּים [וּכְלֵי הַבָּקָר] לָעֵצִים״.
>
> **Translation:** **But doesn't Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua concede** that **in** the case of a private **altar** the wood need not be new? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to David's purchase of the site of the Temple, when he wished to build an altar there at God's instruction, **one verse states: "So David gave to Ornan for the place six hundred shekels of gold by weight.** And David built there an altar to the Lord, and offered burnt offerings and peace offerings" (I Chronicles 21:25–26). **And it is written** elsewhere: **"So David bought the threshing floor and the oxen for fifty shekels of silver.** And David built there an altar to the Lord, and offered burnt offerings and peace offerings" (II Samuel 24:24–25). **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? David would **collect from each tribe** of the twelve tribes **fifty** shekels, **which are** a sum of **six hundred** shekels. **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says in the name of Abba Yosei ben Dostai** that there is another explanation: David purchased the **cattle and** the **wood and the site of** the **altar for fifty** shekels, **and** he purchased the site of **the entire Temple for six hundred** shekels. **Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua says** likewise: David purchased the **cattle and** the **wood and the site of** the **altar for fifty** shekels, **and** the site of **the entire Temple for six hundred** shekels, **as it is written: "And Araunah said to David: Let my lord the king take and offer that which is good in his eyes; see the cattle for the burnt offering, and the threshing tools, and the implements of the cattle for the wood"** (II Samuel 24:22), to which David replied: "No, but I will buy it from you at a price" (II Samuel 24:24).

The Gemara immediately raises an objection: but Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua himself apparently agrees that on a *bamah* — a private altar — used wood is permissible. If so, why did Rava apply the principle of fresh materials to this sacrifice for Ifra Hormiz, which was being offered on a private *bamah*, not on the central altar? Where is the evidence that Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua permits used wood on a *bamah*?

The evidence comes from a *baraisa* that grapples with an apparent contradiction between two verses concerning David's purchase of Araunah's threshing floor — the site of the future Temple. One verse says: *vayiten David l'Arnan bamakom shekalei zahav mishkal shesh meiot* — David gave 600 *shekel* of gold. Another verse says: *vayiken David et hagoren ve'et habakar bekesef shekalim chamishim* — David bought the threshing floor and the cattle for fifty *shekel* of silver. The discrepancy is twofold: 50 versus 600, and silver versus gold.

### Tribes' Gifts

The first resolution: *gava mikol shevet vashevet chamishim, shehen shesh meot* — David collected 50 from each tribe, and with twelve tribes, the total came to 600. This is an elegant harmonization.

### Price Distinctions

An alternative resolution is offered by Rabbi Yosei ben Dostai: the 50 referred to what David paid for the threshing floor itself and for the cattle; the 600 referred to the price of the entire site — *kol habayit kulo beshesh meot*. Each verse describes a different transaction covering a different scope of the purchase.

Elazar ben Shmuel offers a structurally identical resolution with the same breakdown: 50 for the floor and cattle, 600 for the entire site.

### Used Wood OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר לָךְ: הָתָם נָמֵי בְּחַדְתֵי. מַאי מוֹרִיגִּים? אָמַר עוּלָּא: מִטָּה שֶׁל טוּרְבֵּל. מַאי מִטָּה שֶׁל טוּרְבֵּל? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: עִיזָּא דְּקֻרְקְסָא דְּדָיְישָׁן דִּישָׁאֵי. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַאי קְרָא? ״הִנֵּה שַׂמְתִּיךְ לְמוֹרַג חָרוּץ חָדָשׁ בַּעַל פִּיפִיּוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Consequently, according to the opinion of Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua, David purchased the threshing instruments and the furniture of the oxen for use as wood. **And Rava** could have **said to you** in response: **There too,** in the case of David, the verse is dealing **with new** vessels that had not yet been used. The Gemara asks: **What** are **"the threshing instruments [*morigim*]"** mentioned in the verse? **Ulla said:** It is **a *turbal* bed.** The Gemara asks: **What** is **a *turbal* bed? Abaye said:** It is **a heavy, serrated board [*dekurkesa*],** used **for threshing. Abaye said: What is the verse** from which the meaning of *morigim* is derived? **"Behold, I have made you a new threshing board [*morag*] having sharp teeth; you shall thresh the mountains, and beat them small, and shall make the hills as chaff"** (Isaiah 41:15). This verse indicates that a *morag* has grooves and teeth, and is used for threshing.

What makes this *baraisa* relevant to the question about used wood on a *bamah* is the verse in which Araunah offers David: *re'eh habakar la'olah vehamorgim uklei habakar la'etzim* — behold the cattle for the *olah*, and the threshing sledge and the farm implements for wood. Araunah was offering his agricultural implements as firewood for the sacrifice. This appears to be an instance of repurposed, previously used items being offered as wood on an altar — and since David's altar at Araunah's threshing floor was a *bamah*, not the permanent central altar, it would seem that used wood is acceptable on a *bamah*.

Rava responds: *hatam b'chadati* — in that case, the implements were new. Just because Araunah possessed threshing sledges does not mean he had ever actually used them. They were brand new farm implements that happened to be in his possession, and therefore they qualified as unused wood even by Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua's standard.

This resolution is admittedly a bit surprising: farm implements are presumed to be functional tools, and yet the Gemara accepts that they could have been new and unused. Rava apparently holds that the requirement is that the wood not have been used — not that it be unworked or in its natural state — so fashioned but unused implements satisfy the requirement perfectly.

Accordingly, Rava's instructions to Ifra Hormiz's agents are fully consistent with Rabbi Elazar ben Shamua: even on a private *bamah*, Rava required fresh, unused materials throughout.

The Gemara digresses briefly to identify the *morgim*: Ulla says it is a *mita shel Turbal*, and Abaye explains this as a flat plank fitted with spikes on the underside, upon which animals walk while trampling grain to thresh it — a threshing board. The verse in Yeshayahu — *hinei samtich lemorag charutz chadash ba'al pipiyot* — confirms this description: the *morag* is characterized by its sharp, new teeth or spikes.

### Silver/Gold Ratio

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקְרֵי לֵיהּ רָבָא לִבְרֵיהּ, וְרָמֵי לֵיהּ קְרָאֵי אַהֲדָדֵי; כְּתִיב: ״וַיִּתֵּן דָּוִד לְאׇרְנָן וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וַיִּקֶן דָּוִד וְגוֹ׳״; הָא כֵּיצַד? גּוֹבֶה מִכׇּל שֵׁבֶט וָשֵׁבֶט חֲמִשִּׁים, שֶׁהֵן שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת. וְאַכַּתִּי קַשְׁיָין אַהֲדָדֵי – הָתָם כֶּסֶף, הָכָא זָהָב! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: גּוֹבֶה כֶּסֶף בְּמִשְׁקַל שֵׁשׁ מֵאוֹת זָהָב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the contradiction between the verses that relate the sum of shekels paid by David, the Gemara says that **Rava** was **teaching** these verses **to his son, and raised a contradiction between verses: It is written: "So David gave to Ornan…**six hundred shekels of gold by weight" (I Chronicles 21:25), **and it is written: "So David bought…**fifty shekels of silver" (II Samuel 24:24). **How** can **these** texts be reconciled? David would **collect from each tribe** of the twelve tribes **fifty** shekels, **which are** a sum of **six hundred** shekels. The Gemara asks: **But** these verses **are still difficult,** as they contradict **one another,** since **there** in the book of Samuel it is stated that David paid **silver** shekels, while **here** in Chronicles it is stated that he paid **gold** shekels. The Gemara replies: **Rather, this** is what the verses are **saying:** David would **collect** from each tribe **silver** shekels that had the value of fifty gold shekels in weight, so that the value of the final sum was equal to **six hundred gold** shekels.

The Gemara concludes by returning to the contradiction between the two verses about David's payment, which Rava posed as a question to his son. The son resolved the 50-versus-600 discrepancy by noting that each of the twelve tribes contributed 50, yielding a total of 600. But the Gemara points out a remaining problem: the verse that says 50 specifies *silver*, while the verse that says 600 specifies *gold* — and these are fundamentally different in value. You cannot simply say each tribe gave 50 if one figure is silver and the other gold.

The final resolution: *goveh kesef bemishkal shesh meot zahav* — each tribe gave silver, but not merely 50 *shekel* of silver; rather, each gave silver equivalent in value to 50 *shekel* of gold. The contributions were made in silver, but the silver given by each tribe was worth the equivalent of 50 gold *shekel*, so that the total came to 600 gold *shekel* worth. Both verses are accurate: the currency was silver, the value expressed in gold terms was 600.
