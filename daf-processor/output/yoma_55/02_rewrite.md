# Yoma 55 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Keruvim & Jewish Art

### Keruvim Imagery

The daf we take up today picks up in the middle of 54b. The preceding *gemara* dealt with the remarkable imagery of the *keruvim* in the Holy of Holies — their intimate, even erotic dimension — and the significance of the *Kohen Gadol*'s entry into the innermost chamber. The *gemara* quoted from *Shir HaShirim*: *tzror ha-mor dodi li bein shadai yalin* — powerful imagery of God and Israel in the closest of embraces. The *gemara* also drew on the phrase *hevi'ani ha-melech chadarav* and reflected on the very word *bi'ah* — entry — as applied to the placing of the incense between the *badim*. All of this points to the charged, intimate quality of the *avodah* in the *kodesh kodashim*.

What makes this imagery so striking is precisely its hiddenness. The *kohen gadol* alone, once a year on *Yom Kippur*, was privileged to enter — and yet, as the *gemara* noted, on the *regalim* there was a certain revealing of the *keruvim* to *Bnei Yisrael*. The *gemara* also explored how this imagery can be misunderstood when taken out of context, when the sacred is brought into the *shuk* and degraded.

### Machzor Illustrations

Before moving on, there is an important *Tosafot* on 54a — the *Tosafot* beginning *keruvim detzurta* — that uses the *gemara*'s discussion of the *keruvim* engravings in the *Beit Sheni* as a launching point for a broader discussion of Jewish art. The *Tosafot* records a *shaylah* he received: *nishalti al otam she-metzayrim ba-machzorim tziyurat chayot ve-ofot* — I was asked about those who draw images of animals and birds in *machzorim*. Anyone who has visited a Jewish museum or browsed illuminated medieval *machzorim* knows exactly what this refers to: pastoral scenes, fantastical creatures, often deliberately altered in some way to address these very concerns.

The *Ba'al Tosafot* responds: *ilu vadai lo yafeh osim* — they are certainly not acting properly. But his reason is not a technical prohibition; it is *she-mitoch she-mistaklin ba-tziyurim ha-lalu ein mekavnim libam la-Avinu she-ba-shamayim* — because people spend time looking at the pictures and fail to direct their hearts to their Father in Heaven. They are distracted from *davening*.

### Lo Ta'aseh Pesel

He then clarifies: *ein kan issur de-lo ta'aseh lekha fesel* — this does not constitute a violation of the biblical prohibition of making a graven image. He explains that most of the images we see were made by non-Jews, and furthermore, *ve-afilu chashda leika* — there is not even a concern of being suspected of idol worship. Why not? *Lekka ba-tziyurim she-hani mi-nitzva'im be-alma, ve-ein ba-hem mamashut klal* — these are mere colors, mere pigment on a page; they have no physical substance. The concern of *chashda*, of appearing to worship an image, applies only to *chotam bolet* — something that is engraved or protrudes in three dimensions, not to flat illustration.

*Tosafot* proceeds through a full analysis of what types of images might be problematic under *lo ta'aseh lekha fesel*, including the *gemara*'s expansion of that category to figures representing human forms or celestial bodies, and separately, when even the mere possession of an object raises suspicion of worship. This *Tosafot* is essential reading for any *halachic* discussion of Jewish art. The short version: the gravest concerns are confined to three-dimensional figures, not to two-dimensional illustration — and certainly not to photography, which by nearly all opinions falls outside the scope of these prohibitions entirely.

## Foundation Creation

### Shitiyah Stone

The *gemara* now identifies the stone at the base of the *kodesh kodashim* as the *Even ha-Shitiyah* — the Foundation Stone. A *tanna* teaches: *shemimmenah hushtat ha-olam* — from it the world was founded. The *gemara* notes that the *mishnah* aligns with the opinion that says *mi-Tzion nivra ha-olam* — the world was created from Zion — since this stone stands in Zion, on *Har ha-Bayit*. It was the first thing created, and everything was built outward from it.

### Sides & Ocean

A *baraita* then records a three-way dispute. Rabbi Eliezer says: *olam me-emtzaito nivra* — the world was created from its middle. The verse cited — *be-tzeket afar la-mutzak u-regavim yedubaku* — suggests something like a core foundation around which the clods of earth coalesced. The world grew outward from a center point.

Rabbi Yehoshua disagrees: *olam min ha-tzedadim nivra* — the world was created from the sides, with the snow and rain and the forces of nature converging from multiple directions simultaneously to form the earth. Rashi explains the verse *ki la-sheleg yomar hevei aretz* as God commanding the snow to become land; the various elements came together from the periphery inward.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha offers a third position: *even yara ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu ba-yam, mimmenah nishtat ha-olam* — God cast a stone into the ocean and from that stone the world was founded. The verse *al mah adaneha hotbe'u, o mi yara even pinatah* — on what were its sockets sunk, who cast the corner stone — supports the image of creation rising from primordial waters. This resonates with *Bereishit* itself, which begins with an undifferentiated ocean that is then separated to allow dry land to emerge, and with ancient cosmological myths of cities and civilizations rising from the sea.

### Zion Creation

Against these naturalistic accounts, the Sages say simply: *mi-Tzion nivra ha-olam* — the world was created from Zion. The proof-text is from *Mizmor le-Asaf*: *mi-Tzion michlal yofi Elohim hofia* — from Zion, the epitome of beauty, God appeared — which the *gemara* reads as *mimenu michlal yofyo shel olam*, from it the beauty of the world was established.

The difference between these positions runs deeper than cosmology. The Sages' view places a spiritual-religious reality — Zion, the connection between heaven and earth, the site of the *Beit HaMikdash* — at the origin of all existence. Everything emanates from a spiritual essence. The other opinions, by contrast, allow nature a measure of independence: earth from earth, heaven from heaven, ocean giving rise to land. The Zion opinion sees creation as suffused with divine purpose from its first moment.

### Generations Dispute

A further *baraita* records the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer ha-Gadol: *eleh toldot ha-shamayim ve-ha-aretz be-hibaram, be-yom asot Hashem Elohim eretz ve-shamayim* — these are the generations of heaven and earth when they were created. He reads this as teaching that *toldot shamayim mi-shamayim nivre'u, toldot ha-aretz me-aretz nivre'u* — the offspring of the heavens, meaning the sun, moon, and stars, emerged from the heavens themselves, while the offspring of the earth — plants, trees, and presumably animals and humans — sprouted from the earth. Rashi develops this in his commentary on *Bereishit*.

Against this, the Sages maintain *elu va-elu mi-Tzion nivre'u* — both heaven-things and earth-things were created from Zion. The same verse from *Mizmor le-Asaf* supports the claim: *diber va-yikra aretz mi-mizrach shemesh ad mevo'o, ve-omer mi-Tzion michlal yofi Elohim hofia* — God called the earth from the rising of the sun to its setting, and from Zion, the fullness of beauty, God appeared. Everything originates from that single spiritual center.

## Blood Sprinkling

### Sprinkle Procedure

The *gemara* now returns to the *avodah* itself. The *kohen gadol* took the blood from the one who had been stirring it and re-entered the *kodesh kodashim*. The *mishnah* describes the procedure: *ve-hizah mimenu achat le-ma'alah ve-sheva le-matah* — he sprinkled from it once upward and seven times downward. Crucially, *ve-lo hayah mitkavein lehazot lo le-ma'alah ve-lo le-matah* — he did not intend to sprinkle either up or down specifically.

### Kematzlif Motion

What, then, did he do? *Ela kematzlif* — he moved like one lashing. The *gemara* asks *mai kematzlif?* and records that Rav Yehudah demonstrated it physically: *ke-minagda* — like someone administering lashes.

Several dimensions of this comparison deserve unpacking. First, the motion involved: giving lashes is primarily an arm movement rather than a wrist movement. A flick of the wrist alone would not be an effective lash; the force comes from the shoulder and arm. This suggests that the *kohen gadol*'s motion was large and deliberate, not a mere finger-snap. Second, the question of when the release occurs: when administering lashes, the blow lands at the end of the downward arc. If the sprinkling follows the same logic, the blood is released at the lowest point of the motion.

Rashi elaborates: *ke-matah ba-retzu'ah she-machil min ha-ksafayim u-makkeh ve-holekh le-matah* — like striking with a strap, beginning from the shoulders and moving sequentially downward, hitting in a line. This suggests a deliberate, sequential, linear motion. *Tosafot* in *Zevachim*, by contrast, seems to indicate a less targeted motion — more consistent with the *mishnah*'s statement that he was not aiming up or down specifically. The precise mechanics remain somewhat unclear.

### Not Hitting Kapporet

A *tanna* then provides an important clarification: *kshehu mazeh eino mazeh al ha-kapporet ela keneged ovyah shel ha-kapporet* — when he sprinkles, he does not sprinkle onto the *kapporet* itself, but rather opposite its thickness. None of the blood actually strikes the *kapporet*. *Keneged ovyah* — opposite its thickness — means in front of the side face of the lid, not on its upper surface.

To understand this spatially: if the *aron* is oriented west-to-east, with the *kohen gadol* entering from the east and facing west, then *al pnei ha-kapporet kedmah* — on the face of the *kapporet* eastward — refers to the eastern-facing thickness of the lid. *Li-fnei ha-kapporet* — in front of the *kapporet* — means the seven downward sprinklings are done in front of it, not touching it. The *tanna* goes further: even the one upward sprinkling does not strike the surface; it goes past it.

### Rashi vs Chananel

The *tanna* adds a further detail: *kshehu mazeh le-ma'alah, mitzadeid yado le-matah* — when he sprinkles upward, he angles his hand downward; *kshehu mazeh le-matah, mitzadeid yado le-ma'alah* — when he sprinkles downward, he angles his hand upward.

Rashi and Rabbeinu Chananel dispute what this means. Rashi understands *mitzadeid yado le-matah* as the back of the hand facing downward — so for the upward sprinkling, the hand is oriented with the back facing down, in an underhand or somewhat awkward position. For the downward sprinkling, the back of the hand faces up. Rabbeinu Chananel reads it precisely the opposite way: *mitzadeid yado le-matah* means the palm faces downward. For the upward sprinkling, the palm is down, the fingers point upward, and the blood is flicked upward — a natural and easy motion. For the downward sprinkling, the palm faces upward and the fingers point down, which is indeed physically more awkward.

Rabbeinu Chananel also clarifies the spatial orientation: the upward sprinkling travels higher than the *kapporet* and then falls to the ground; the downward sprinklings are directed at the level of the *kapporet*'s thickness — what the *Yerushalmi* calls its *mitzcha*, its forehead — and then fall to the ground as well. One reason suggested by the *mefarshim* for the palm-upward position for downward sprinklings is that with the palm facing down, the blood would run back into the sleeve.

## Torah Redundancy

### Repetition Question

The *gemara* now undertakes a hermeneutic analysis of the relevant *pesukim*. By the *par*, the *Torah* says: *ve-lakach midam ha-par ve-hizah be-etzba'o al pnei ha-kapporet kedmah* — he shall take from the blood of the ox and sprinkle with his finger upon the face of the *kapporet* eastward — and then: *ve-lifnei ha-kapporet yazeh sheva pe'amim min ha-dam be-etzba'o* — and before the *kapporet* he shall sprinkle seven times. So the *par*: one *al*, seven *lifnei*.

By the *se'ir*, the *Torah* says: *ve-asah et damo ka'asher asah le-dam ha-par* — he shall do with its blood as he did with the blood of the ox — and then repeats: *ve-hizah oto al ha-kapporet ve-lifnei ha-kapporet*. Since the *Torah* already established the equation *ka'asher asah le-dam ha-par*, why does it need to say it again explicitly? That repetition demands interpretation.

### Before Not On

Rav Chisda's approach, as cited by Rav Acha bar Yaakov: the repetition is *le-akushei al ve-lifnei* — to link the word *al* (on) with the word *lifnei* (in front of). The logic: *mah lifnei de-lav al, af al de-lav al* — just as *lifnei* does not literally mean "on" (since you sprinkle in front of, not onto, the *kapporet*), so too *al* does not literally mean "on." Everything is done in front of, not on. The phrase *al ha-kapporet* in this context means in the presence of, or opposite, the *kapporet* — not directly upon it.

This is a striking interpretive move. The *Torah* uses the word *al*, which naturally means "on," but the repetition of the verse by the *se'ir* — after the equation has already been established — teaches us that *al* here carries the meaning of *lifnei*.

### Which Redundancy

The *gemara* challenges this: one could argue the opposite. Perhaps the *al* by the *par* is the extra element, since once the *se'ir* is linked to the *par*, you could have learned the upward sprinkling from the *se'ir* retrospectively. If *al* is the extra phrase, then *al* teaches the literal meaning — everything is done *al*, actually on the *kapporet*, including the seven *lifnei* sprinklings. On this reading, even *lifnei* means *al*.

The *gemara* resolves this with *hai mai* — what kind of a question is that? If the seven *lifnei* by the *se'ir* is what is extra — used to teach the principle that nothing is literally *al* — then the *al* by the *par*, which is also extra once the equation is established, has a use: it serves as the basis for the *binyan av* taught in the school of Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, that *al pnei ha-kapporet kedmah* establishes a general principle: wherever *panim* appears, it means eastward. That *pasuk* has independent work to do.

But if instead we say the *al* by the *par* is the extra element, teaching that everything literally means *al*, then the *lifnei* by the *se'ir* would be entirely superfluous — there would be nothing to do with it. Since this reading leaves a *pasuk* without any function, it cannot be correct. Therefore the first reading stands: the *lifnei* by the *se'ir* is the extra element, and it teaches that *al* does not mean literally "on." The *al* by the *par* carries a separate lesson about *panim* meaning east. The upshot: one sprinkling upward and seven downward, none of them actually touching the *kapporet*.

## Quantity Derivation

### Below Seven

The *gemara* now works through a careful logical derivation of the quantities for both *par* and *se'ir*. A *baraita* spells out the reasoning step by step.

By the *se'ir*, the *Torah* says *ve-hizah oto al ha-kapporet ve-lifnei ha-kapporet* — from *oto* (literally "it," in the singular) we derive that the *al* above is one. How many below by the *se'ir*? That is not stated. The *gemara* proposes a derivation: *ne'emru damim le-matah ba-par ve-ne'emru damim le-matah ba-se'ir* — it says blood below by the *par* and blood below by the *se'ir*; *mah le-matah ba-par sheva, af le-matah ba-se'ir sheva* — just as below by the ox is seven, so too below by the goat is seven.

But one might argue the opposite: *ne'emru damim le-ma'alah ba-se'ir ve-ne'emru damim le-matah ba-se'ir* — the *se'ir* itself says above and below; *mah le-ma'alah ba-se'ir achat, af le-matah ba-se'ir achat* — just as the *se'ir* says one above, maybe it is one below as well. Learn the *se'ir* from itself rather than from the *par*.

The *gemara* says: *nireh le-mi domeh* — let us see which is more comparable. *Danin matah mi-matah ve-ein danin matah mi-ma'alah* — learn below from below, not below from above. So learn the seven from the *par* rather than the one from the *se'ir*'s own upper count.

The counter-argument: *danin gufa mi-gufa ve-ein danin gufa me-alma* — learn a thing from itself, not from elsewhere. Since the *se'ir* itself says one above, learn one below from its own internal evidence, not from the *par*.

This impasse is resolved by the equation *ka'asher asah le-dam ha-par*: *she-ein talmud lomar ka'asher asah, u-mah talmud lomar ka'asher asah? she-yihyu kol asiyotav shavot* — the equation is there to make all the acts equal. *Keshem she-le-matah ba-par sheva kakh le-matah ba-se'ir sheva* — just as below by the ox is seven, so below by the goat is seven.

### Category Principle

Now the corresponding question about above: how many times above by the *par*? The *par* itself does not specify. By parallel reasoning the *gemara* asks: learn it from above by the *se'ir*, which is one — *mah le-ma'alah ba-se'ir achat, af le-ma'alah ba-par achat*. Or learn from within the *par* itself: *mah le-matah ba-par sheva, af le-ma'alah ba-par sheva* — just as the *par* is seven below, perhaps it is seven above as well. Once again both directions of reasoning are valid in isolation.

The counter-argument applies again: *danin gufa mi-gufa* — learn the *par* from itself. And once again the equation resolves it: *keshem she-le-ma'alah ba-se'ir achat, kakh le-ma'alah ba-par achat* — just as above by the goat is one, so above by the ox is one.

### Equivalency Role

The equation *ka'asher asah le-dam ha-par* thus does double work. On the one hand, it establishes that below by the *se'ir* is seven, learned across from below by the *par*. On the other, it establishes that above by the *par* is one, learned across from above by the *se'ir*. The key principle operating throughout is *danin ma'alah mi-ma'alah ve-ein danin ma'alah mi-matah* — above is learned from above, not from below — and *danin matah mi-matah ve-ein danin matah mi-ma'alah* — below from below, not below from above.

Once the equation performs this work, it renders certain other phrases redundant: the explicit mention of *al ha-kapporet* by the *se'ir* (since the one above can be derived from the equation) and the explicit mention of *ve-lifnei ha-kapporet* likewise (since the seven below can be derived from the equation). These newly redundant elements then carry the interpretive lesson: *lifnei* does not mean literally *al*, and therefore *al* itself does not mean literally *al*. The full picture — one above, seven below, nothing touching the *kapporet* — is now established through the interplay of the equation and the redundancies it creates.

## Counting Order

### Meir vs Yehudah

With the quantities established, the *gemara* turns to how the *kohen gadol* counted aloud. A *baraita* cites two positions. Rabbi Meir says the count is: *achat, achat ve-achat, achat u-shtayim, achat ve-shalosh, achat ve-arba, achat ve-chamesh, achat ve-shesh, achat ve-sheva* — one; one and one; one and two; one and three... up to one and seven. Rabbi Yehudah says: *achat, achat ve-achat, shtayim ve-achat, shalosh ve-achat, arba ve-achat, chamesh ve-achat, shesh ve-achat, sheva ve-achat* — one; one and one; two and one; three and one... seven and one.

### No Disagreement

The *gemara* resolves: *ve-lo plugi* — they do not actually disagree. *Mar ke-asrei u-mar ke-asrei* — each follows his own regional counting convention. The difference is simply whether one says "twenty-one" or "one and twenty" — whether the larger number comes first or the current count comes first. Both are valid conventions for keeping track.

### Continuing Count

All opinions agree, however, on one crucial point: *ha-haza'ah ha-rishonah tzrikhah minyan im kol achat ve-achat* — the first sprinkling must be counted continuously together with each of the seven. That is, even after completing the one upward *haza'ah*, the *kohen gadol* does not simply begin a separate count of one through seven; he incorporates the first into each subsequent count.

Rabbi Elazar gives one reason: *she-lo yiteh ba-haza'ot* — so that he should not err among the sprinklings. Rashi understands this differently: *she-yehei lo shehut bein-tayim latet libo le-minyan she-lo yiteh* — by reciting the longer count each time, he is slowed down and must give deliberate attention to the number. This prevents careless rushing. One might have thought the purpose was to ensure the first *haza'ah* is not accidentally counted as one of the seven — but Rashi does not seem to be saying that; rather, it is purely a matter of deliberateness and care.

### Verb Repetition

Rabbi Yochanan derives this requirement from the *pasuk* itself: *ve-lifnei ha-kapporet yazeh sheva pe'amim* — the verb *yazeh* (he shall sprinkle) is stated. *She-ein talmud lomar yazeh* — the verb was not necessary; the text could simply have said "seven times." *U-mah talmud lomar yazeh? Le-lamed al ha-haza'ah ha-rishonah she-tzrikhah minyan im kol achat ve-achat* — the repetition of the verb teaches that the original *haza'ah* must continue to be enumerated alongside each of the seven. On Rabbi Yochanan's reading, this is not merely a pragmatic safeguard but a requirement derived directly from the *pasuk*.

### Counting Difference

What is the practical difference between Rabbi Elazar's explanation and Rabbi Yochanan's? *Ika beinaihu de-lo manah ve-lo ta'ah* — the case where he did not count and yet did not make a mistake. According to Rabbi Elazar, the counting is a secondary concern — a precaution against error — so if in fact no error occurred, the *avodah* is valid even without the explicit count. According to Rabbi Yochanan, counting together with the first is a positive requirement derived from the *pasuk* itself, so failure to count is a deficiency regardless of whether a mistake was made.

## Single Pedestal

### Yehudah Confusion

After the sprinkling in the *kodesh kodashim*, the *kohen gadol* exits and places the blood on the pedestal (*kan*) in the *heikhal*. The *gemara* now raises a *mishnah* from elsewhere: Rabbi Yehudah says *lo hayah sham ella kan achat bilvad* — there was only one pedestal, used for both the blood of the *par* and the blood of the *se'ir*. Why not two? Because *mishum de-mekhalfei* — they would get confused and might pick up the wrong bowl, not knowing which is the ox's and which is the goat's.

### Challenge Writing

The *gemara* challenges: why not simply have two pedestals and write on each one — this is the *par*, this is the *se'ir*? The answer: *Rabbi Yehudah leit lei kesivah* — Rabbi Yehudah does not believe that writing alone can be trusted to prevent confusion in practice. People will not reliably pay attention to written labels, especially under the pressure and intensity of the *avodah*.

But this raises an immediate difficulty. If Rabbi Yehudah does not trust writing, how does he account for the *thirteen shofarot* in the *Beit HaMikdash* — each designated for a different purpose by means of writing? Surely they were distinguished by their inscriptions. And yet we find that Rabbi Yehudah himself describes those inscriptions as the means of differentiation.

### Thirteen Shofarot

A *baraita* lists the thirteen *shofarot* and their designations. *Taklin chadatin* — new shekels (the current year's contribution); *taklin atikin* — old shekels, for one who had not paid the previous year; *kinim* — bird sacrifices of the *tor* (pigeon variety); *gozlei olah* — *bnei yonah* (turtledove variety); *etzim* — donations for wood for the altar; *levonah* — donations of incense (notably, one can make a private *nedavah* of incense, which is burned on the outer *mizbeach* — a remarkable *chiddush*, since there is no explicit *Torah* source for private incense donations, and it results in incense being offered in three distinct locations: the daily service on the gold altar in the *heikhal*, the *Yom Kippur* service in the *kodesh kodashim*, and this private donation on the outer altar); *zahav la-kapporet* — gold donations for the Temple vessels (Rashi: *klei shareit*); and six *shofarot* for general *nedavah*, into which surplus funds were eventually channeled. That accounts for all thirteen. Rabbi Yehudah describes the inscriptions on each.

This creates the difficulty: Rabbi Yehudah himself says the thirteen *shofarot* were distinguished by writing. He clearly accepts written designations as effective in that context. Why, then, does he not accept writing as sufficient to distinguish between the *par* blood and the *se'ir* blood, or between the obligatory and voluntary bird funds? The resolution to this tension will be taken up in the next session.

## Bird Sacrifices

### Dead Owner Decree

When Rav Dimi came from *Eretz Yisrael*, he reported: *amri be-ma'arava, gezeirah mishum chatas she-meitu ba'aleha* — they said in *Eretz Yisrael* that the absence of a separate *shofar* for obligatory bird funds is a rabbinic decree, on account of the *chatat* whose owner has died. The concern is as follows: if money from an obligatory bird fund — which is composed of half *chatat* and half *olah* contributions — becomes mixed with the general voluntary bird fund, and in the interim the owner of one of those *chatat* contributions dies, then birds will be purchased and brought with funds belonging to a *chatat* whose owner is no longer alive. This is prohibited. Rather than risk such an outcome, the Rabbis decreed that no separate *shofar* should be maintained for obligatory bird contributions at all. The precise implications of this decree will be explored further in the following session.
