# Berakhot 15 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Order of Shema Paragraphs

### Learning, Teaching, Doing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: לָמָּה קָדְמָה פָּרָשַׁת ״שְׁמַע״ וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa said: Why did the portion of *Shema* precede** that of *VeHaya im Shamoa*? So that one will first accept upon himself the yoke of the kingdom of Heaven and only then accept upon himself the yoke of the mitzvot.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: בַּדִּין הוּא שֶׁיַּקְדִּים ״שְׁמַע״, לִ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״ — שֶׁזֶּה לִלְמוֹד, וְזֶה לְלַמֵּד. ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״ לְ״וַיֹּאמֶר״ — שֶׁזֶּה לִלְמוֹד, וְזֶה לַעֲשׂוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai** offers a different explanation for the order of the portions of *Shema*. He **says: By right, *Shema* should precede *VeHaya im Shamoa* because** the *Shema* includes the directive **to learn, while** *VeHaya im Shamoa* includes the directive **to teach.** Similarly, ***VeHaya im Shamoa*** should precede ***VaYomer*,** the final paragraph of *Shema*, because *VeHaya im Shamoa* includes the directive **to teach, while** the portion of ritual fringes includes the directive **to perform.**

Today's daf begins in the middle of 14b, at the statement of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: *lama kadma parshat Shema* — why does the first paragraph of *shema* precede the others? Before engaging with this question directly, it is worth noting the context. We had just finished discussing the laws of *bein haprakim* — the permissible interruptions between the sections of *shema* — and the interesting question of how *bein haprakim* applies to responding to elements of the *davening*, such as *kaddish*, during the recitation of *shema*. Now we turn to the question of the order of the three *parshiyot*.

It should also be noted, as was mentioned previously, that *parshat tzitzit* — the third paragraph — was largely omitted at night in *Eretz Yisrael*, either because the *mitzvah* of *tzitzit* does not apply at night, or because the *mitzvah* of *zecher yetziat Mitzrayim* may not apply at night. These regional differences are worth keeping in mind as we approach the question of ordering.

A *baraita* is cited: Shimshon ben Yochai teaches that it is fitting for the first paragraph of *shema* to precede *V'haya im Shamoa* because *shema* corresponds to *lilmod* — learning — and *V'haya im Shamoa* corresponds to *lelamed* — teaching. The source for this reading is that *shema* contains the verse *v'dibarta bam*, "and you shall speak of them," while *V'haya im Shamoa* contains *v'shinantam l'vanecha*, "and you shall teach your children" — that is, *V'haya im Shamoa* commands teaching others, not learning oneself. Then *V'haya im Shamoa* precedes the third paragraph, *Vayomer*, because it contains both *lelamed* and *la'asot* — teaching and doing — while *Vayomer* contains only *la'asot*. The structure is thus a progression: learn, then teach, then do.

This is a different organizing principle than *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim* — the acceptance of the yoke of heaven — which was the first reason given. One might compare it to the difference between the *Rambam* and the *Shulchan Aruch* in how they organize their works. The *Rambam* begins with *yesod hayesodot ve'amud hachachamot* — establishing the foundations, the existence of God, and then building outward to *mitzvot* and practical matters. The *Shulchan Aruch* begins immediately with what to do when you wake up in the morning — chronological and user-friendly. The first ordering of *shema* paragraphs (foundation before practical *mitzvot*) parallels the *Rambam*'s approach, while the second (a sequential progression from learning to teaching to doing) has more of the *Shulchan Aruch*'s character.

### Three, Two, One Elements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ ״שְׁמַע״ — לִלְמוֹד אִית בֵּיהּ, לְלַמֵּד וְלַעֲשׂוֹת לֵית בֵּיהּ?! וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וְשִׁנַּנְתָּם״, ״וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״, ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״! וְתוּ: ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״ — לְלַמֵּד הוּא דְּאִית בֵּיהּ, וְלַעֲשׂוֹת לֵית בֵּיהּ? וְהָא כְּתִיב: ״וּקְשַׁרְתֶּם״, ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: בַּדִּין הוּא שֶׁתִּקְדַּם ״שְׁמַע״ לִ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״, שֶׁזֶּה לִלְמוֹד וּלְלַמֵּד וְלַעֲשׂוֹת. ״וְהָיָה אִם שָׁמוֹעַ״ לְ״וַיֹּאמֶר״, שֶׁזֶּה יֵשׁ בָּהּ לְלַמֵּד וְלַעֲשׂוֹת, ״וַיֹּאמֶר״ אֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא לַעֲשׂוֹת בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Is that to say that** the portion of ***Shema* contains** the directive **to learn** but **it does not contain** the directive **to teach and perform? Isn't it written: "And you shall teach them** to your children" (Deuteronomy 6:7), a directive to teach, as well as: **"And you shall bind them** as a sign upon your arm" (Deuteronomy 6:8) and: **"And write them** on your door posts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:9), directives to perform? **Furthermore, does *VeHaya im Shamoa* contain** the directive **to teach** but **it does not contain** the directive **to perform? Isn't it written: "And you shall bind them** as a sign upon your arm" (Deuteronomy 11:18), a directive to perform? **Rather,** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **said as follows: By right,** the portion of ***Shema* should precede *VeHaya im Shamoa* because** the portion of *Shema* includes the directives **to learn, to teach, and to perform,** while *VeHaya im Shamoa* includes the directives to teach and to perform. ***VeHaya im Shamoa* should precede *VaYomer* because** *VeHaya im Shamoa* includes the directives **to teach and to perform, while** *VaYomer* **only** includes the directive **to perform.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges the *baraita*: does *shema* truly have only *lilmod* and not *lelamed* and *la'asot*? But *shema* contains *v'shinantam* — teaching — and *ukshartem ukhtavtem* — references to *tefillin* and *mezuzah*, which constitute *la'asot*. Similarly, does *V'haya im Shamoa* lack *la'asot*? It too contains *tefillin* and *mezuzah*.

The *gemara* therefore reframes the principle: it is not that each paragraph exclusively embodies one element, but rather that each paragraph contains *more* of one than the next. *Shema* has all three — *lilmod*, *lelamed*, *la'asot*. *V'haya im Shamoa* has two — *lelamed* and *la'asot*. *Vayomer* has only one — *la'asot*. The order reflects a descending richness: three, then two, then one.

### Foundation vs. Chronological

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִדְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר, חֲדָא: כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל עָלָיו עוֹל מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם תְּחִלָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ יְקַבֵּל עָלָיו עוֹל מִצְוֹת, וְעוֹד — מִשּׁוּם דְּאִית בַּהּ הָנֵי מִילֵּי אַחְרָנְיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let him derive this,** that the portion of *Shema* is recited first, **from** the statement of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa.** The Gemara responds: **He stated one** reason **and another.** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai does not disagree with Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa; he simply suggested as additional explanation as follows: **One** reason the portion of *Shema* is recited first is **so that one will first accept the yoke of the kingdom of Heaven upon himself and afterward the yoke of the mitzvot; and** the second reason is **because** the portion of *Shema* **contains these other elements** as well.

The *gemara* then asks: given that we already have the teaching of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha about *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim*, why did we need the separate approach of *lilmod u'lelamed ve'la'asot*? The answer is *chada ve'od ka'amar* — one good reason and yet another. One reason is to first accept the yoke of heaven and then accept the yoke of *mitzvot*. A second, additional reason is the progression of *lilmod u'lelamed ve'la'asot* within the paragraphs themselves. Both reasons apply simultaneously and reinforce each other.

It is worth pausing on the phrase *lama kadma* — "why does it precede?" — which implies that the liturgy was already fixed, and the *amoraim* are reasoning retrospectively about the rationale. This is more like a post-hoc speculation than a legislative act, which is why multiple explanations can coexist.

## Rav's Practice & Conflict

### Rav's Daily Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשִׁי יְדֵיהּ, וּקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וְאַנַּח תְּפִילִּין וְצַלִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav washed his hands, recited *Shema*, donned phylacteries, and prayed** in that order.

The *gemara* now moves from the conceptual ordering of the *shema* paragraphs to a discussion of Rav's actual practice, which bears directly on the theme of *lilmod u'lelamed ve'la'asot*. The report is as follows: *Rav mashi yadei ve'kara krias shema ve'anach tefillin ve'tzali* — Rav washed his hands, recited *shema*, donned *tefillin*, and then prayed *Shemoneh Esrei*.

The hand-washing here is not primarily focused on the question of *ruach ra*; the *gemara*'s concern is the washing that prepares a person for *davening* — a matter of cleanliness in preparation for prayer. This practice leads *Tosafot* into a detailed discussion: if one receives one's *tefillin* (or *tallis*) between *shema* and *Shemoneh Esrei*, can one put them on with a *bracha* at that point, or must the *bracha* be recited afterward? *Tosafot* notes that wearing *tefillin* during *Shemoneh Esrei* takes priority over wearing a *tallis*, though the underlying reasoning is not entirely clear, given that the concern about wearing *tefillin* while reciting *shema* — since one speaks of *tefillin* in the text — is itself a significant consideration. *Tosafot* concludes that Rav's practice demonstrates the importance of having *tefillin* on for *Shemoneh Esrei*, but does not resolve the question of when the *bracha* is made. One cannot interrupt *shema* to make the *bracha*, yet since one is still in the midst of fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *tefillin* even after *Shemoneh Esrei*, a *bracha* beforehand would not constitute a *bracha* after the *mitzvah*'s completion.

### Work Interruption Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי עָבֵיד הָכִי? וְהָתַנְיָא הַחוֹפֵר כּוּךְ לְמֵת בַּקֶּבֶר — פָּטוּר מִקְּרִיאַת שְׁמַע וּמִן הַתְּפִלָּה וּמִן הַתְּפִילִּין וּמִכׇּל מִצְוֹת הָאֲמוּרוֹת בַּתּוֹרָה. הִגִּיעַ זְמַן קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע — עוֹלֶה וְנוֹטֵל יָדָיו, וּמַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וּמִתְפַּלֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **How could he do that? Wasn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **One who digs a grave for the dead** in the wall of the family burial cave **is exempt from the recitation of *Shema*, from prayer, from phylacteries, and from all mitzvot mentioned in the Torah.** When **the** appointed **time for the recitation of *Shema* arrives, he emerges** from the cave, **washes his hands, dons phylacteries, recites *Shema*, and prays.**

The *gemara* asks: how could Rav have done this — that is, how could he have said *shema* before donning *tefillin*? A *baraita* is cited: one who is digging a niche for a corpse in a burial cave is exempt from *krias shema*, *tefillah*, *tefillin*, and all *mitzvot* mentioned in the Torah — the principle of *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah*, one who is engaged in a *mitzvah* is exempt from another *mitzvah*. However, when the time for *krias shema* arrives, the *baraita* continues: *oleh venoteil yadav u'meni'ach tefillin vekore krias shema umispaleil* — he ascends from the cave, washes his hands, puts on *tefillin*, recites *shema*, and prays.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא גוּפָא קַשְׁיָא, רֵישָׁא אָמַר פָּטוּר, וְסֵיפָא חַיָּיב?! הָא לָא קַשְׁיָא: סֵיפָא בִּתְרֵי, וְרֵישָׁא בְּחַד.
>
> **Translation:** Before clarifying the problem, the Gemara comments: **This** *baraita* **itself is difficult;** it appears to be contradictory. **The first clause** of the *baraita* **stated** that one digging a grave **is exempt** from the recitation of *Shema*, **and the latter clause** stated that **he is obligated** to emerge and recite *Shema*. The Gemara responds: **That is not difficult. The latter clause** of the *baraita* refers to a case **of two** individuals digging the grave together; one pauses to recite *Shema* while the other continues digging. **The first clause** of the *baraita* refers to a case **of one** individual digging alone, who may not stop.

But this appears internally contradictory: the opening clause said he is exempt from all *mitzvot*, yet the closing clause prescribes a specific order in which he fulfills them. The *gemara* resolves this: the opening clause (*reisha*) refers to a situation where he is the only one working — if he stops, the *mitzvah* ceases entirely. The closing clause (*sayfa*) refers to a situation where two workers are present — he can take a break while the *mitzvah* continues. This resolution itself raises an interesting conceptual point about *osek bemitzvah patur min hamitzvah*: the exemption applies as long as the *mitzvah* is ongoing, even if one personally steps away from it. A slight slowing of efficiency, it seems, does not itself end the *mitzvah*.

In any case, the *baraita*'s closing clause presents the order as: *tefillin* first, then *shema* — the reverse of what Rav is reported to have done. The *gemara* therefore poses the question: *mikol makom kashya le-Rav* — this remains a difficulty for Rav. A resolution is offered: perhaps Rav follows the view of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, who teaches that *ol malchut shamayim* precedes *ol mitzvot* — one first establishes the yoke of heaven, and then performs *mitzvot*. Rav's practice of saying *shema* before putting on *tefillin* would thus reflect this priority: the verbal acceptance of *malchut shamayim* comes before the performance of the *mitzvah* of *tefillin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא לְרַב. רַב כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קָרְחָה סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר עוֹל מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם תְּחִלָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ עוֹל מִצְוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **In any case, this** *baraita* **contradicts Rav** in terms of the order in which the mitzvot are performed. The Gemara responds: **Rav holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa, who said** that the acceptance of **the yoke of the kingdom of Heaven** takes precedence and should **come first, followed by** the acceptance of **the yoke of the mitzvot.** Therefore, Rav first recited *Shema*, and only then donned phylacteries.

### Contradictory Accounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימַר דַּאֲמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה — לְהַקְדִּים קְרִיאָה לִקְרִיאָה, קְרִיאָה לַעֲשִׂיָּה מִי שָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ?! וְתוּ, מִי סָבַר לֵיהּ כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה? וְהָאָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי: זִמְנִין סַגִּיאִין הֲוָה קָאֵימְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְרַב, וּמַקְדֵּים וּמָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ, וּמְבָרֵךְ, וּמַתְנֵי לַן פִּרְקִין, וּמַנַּח תְּפִילִּין, וַהֲדַר קָרֵי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Say** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa said** to **give precedence to recitation** of the portion concerning the acceptance of the yoke of the kingdom of Heaven over **recitation** of other portions. But **did you hear him** say the *halakha* gives precedence to **recitation over performance? And furthermore, does** Rav **really hold in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa? But didn't Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi say: Many times I stood before Rav, and he first washed his hands, recited a blessing, taught us our lesson, donned phylacteries, and then recited *Shema*.

The *gemara* pushes back on this resolution. First: Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha's teaching concerns the relative ordering of recitations — saying *shema* before *V'haya im Shamoa*, not the question of whether one should recite before one performs *mitzvot*. The principle *mah kadma kriya lekriya* — one recitation precedes another — does not obviously extend to the relationship between recitation and action. There may be a fundamental distinction: theoretically, the foundation (*ol malchut shamayim*) is prior to practice (*ol mitzvot*); but in actual performance, doing always takes precedence.

Second, and more significantly, a second account of Rav's practice directly contradicts the first. Rav Chiya bar Ashi testifies: *zimnin sagi'in havei kamina kamei de-Rav* — many times I stood before Rav — and the practice he observed was as follows: Rav would rise, wash his hands, make a *bracha* (understood as *birkas hatorah*), teach his students their chapter of *mishna*, then don *tefillin*, and only then recite *krias shema*. So in this account, Rav performed *tefillin* before *shema* — the conventional order. *Rashi* notes that this account also raises the question of teaching Torah before *shema*, since that constitutes a *mitzvah* performed before *kabbalat ol malchut shamayim*. But the sharper difficulty is the reversal of order: one account has *shema* then *tefillin*; the other has *tefillin* then *shema*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: בִּדְלָא מְטָא זְמַן קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, אִם כֵּן מַאי אַסְהַדְתֵּיהּ דְרַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say:** This was when **the time for the recitation of *Shema* had not** yet **arrived** and that is why he donned his phylacteries first, **then what is** the point of **the testimony of Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi?**

### Late Tefillin Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּאָמַר לַמִּשְׁנָה אֵין צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ, קָמַשְׁמַע לַן דְּאַף לְמִשְׁנָה נָמֵי צָרִיךְ לְבָרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi's story comes **to exclude** the opinion of **the one who said that one need not recite the blessing** on Torah study **for** the study of **mishna. It teaches us that even for mishna, one must recite a blessing.**

One attempted resolution: perhaps the times Rav put on *tefillin* before *shema* were times when it was not yet the *zman* for *krias shema*, so he put on *tefillin* first and waited until the proper time to recite *shema*. This is somewhat striking since *tefillin* is normally worn from *mi-she-yakir* onward, which most opinions regard as identical to the earliest time for *shema*, though there are more marginal views that *shema* cannot be said until *netz*.

The *gemara* immediately challenges this resolution: if this explanation is correct, then what was the point of Rav Chiya bar Ashi's testimony? If the order of *tefillin* and *shema* in that account was incidental to a timing issue, there is nothing significant to learn from it regarding the order. The *gemara* therefore proposes that the story was told for an entirely different purpose: *le-apukei mi-man d'amar le-mishnah ein tzarich levareich* — to exclude the view that one need not recite *birkas hatorah* before teaching *mishna*, as opposed to *psukim*. The story was told to inform us that Rav did in fact make *birkas hatorah* when teaching *mishna*; the order of *shema* and *tefillin* was incidental.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכׇּל מָקוֹם קַשְׁיָא לְרַב! שְׁלוּחָא הוּא דְּעַוֵּית.
>
> **Translation:** **In any case** this *baraita* **is difficult for Rav.** The Gemara responds: **The messenger was at fault** and brought Rav his phylacteries late, so Rav recited *Shema* at its appropriate time and later donned phylacteries.

Yet the *gemara* remains unsatisfied: *mikol makom kashya le-Rav* — the problem persists. The two accounts of Rav's order with *tefillin* are still in tension. The final resolution is brief: *shlucha hu de-avis* — the messenger erred. According to *Rashi*, this means that the assistant who was supposed to bring Rav his *tefillin* was late. Rav's fundamental practice was to don *tefillin* before *shema*, as the *baraita* and the second testimony indicate. The one occasion on which he said *shema* before *tefillin* was simply because his *tefillin* were not available in time. This then returns us to *Tosafot*'s question: what does one do when one's *tefillin* arrive only after *shema* has begun — can one make a *bracha* on them then, or must one wait?

## Tefillin & Shema Requirement

### False Testimony Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא: כָּל הַקּוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע בְּלֹא תְּפִילִּין, כְּאִילּוּ מֵעִיד עֵדוּת שֶׁקֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: Anyone who recites *Shema* without phylacteries, it is as if he has borne false testimony against himself,** as in *Shema*, he mentions his obligation to don phylacteries and in this case fails to don them himself.

Having established that the norm is to don *tefillin* before *shema*, the *gemara* now articulates the conceptual basis for this requirement. *Ulla* teaches: *kol ha-korei krias shema belo tefillin, k'ilu me'id ed sheker b'atzmo* — one who recites *shema* without wearing *tefillin* is as if he testifies falsely against himself. The *shema* contains references to the obligation of *tefillin*, and by reciting these words while not wearing them, one makes a declaration that is belied by one's own conduct.

*Rashi* reads *b'atzmo* as a euphemism — as if one is offering false testimony about God, since one is declaring God's commandment while behaving as if it does not apply. This reading is somewhat puzzling: the wrong is being done by the person, not attributed to God. A simpler reading might be that by reciting a declaration of obligation one is not fulfilling, one is bearing false witness against one's own obligations — a kind of self-incrimination by hypocrisy.

Notably, the *gemara* does not make this point about *tallis*. This is deliberate: the obligation of *tzitzit* applies only when one is actually wearing a four-cornered garment; there is no freestanding obligation to always wear *tzitzit*. The charge of *k'ilu me'id ed sheker* therefore attaches specifically to *tefillin* and not to *tallis*.

### Incomplete Offering Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּאִילּוּ הִקְרִיב עוֹלָה בְּלֹא מִנְחָה, וְזֶבַח בְּלֹא נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** One who recites *Shema* without phylacteries, **it is as if he has offered a burnt-offering without a meal-offering or a peace-offering without libations.** Despite the fact that he fulfilled his obligation, his offering is incomplete.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan offers a milder formulation: *k'ilu hikriv olah b'lo mincha v'zevach b'lo nesachim* — it is as if one offered a *olah* without its accompanying *mincha*, or brought a *zevach* without the *nesachim*. The *olah* and *zevach* are still brought; the *mincha* and *nesachim* were required accompaniments in the Temple service. One has not made a mockery of the enterprise, but one has failed to bring the offering in its fullest form. The act of declaring is not false; it is simply incomplete.

This distinction carries practical significance. The question arises among the *poskim*: when one recites *shema* without *tefillin*, is this an independent transgression — the *k'ilu me'id ed sheker* of *Ulla* — or does it merely constitute a lesser *kiyum* of the *shema* itself? This distinction matters in practice. Consider the case where one awakens very late, close to the *sof zman krias shema*: if one can put on *tefillin* and recite *shema* or say the *birchos krias shema* and recite *shema* but not do both within the remaining time, which does one choose? The accepted *pesak* is that one recites the *birchos krias shema* without the *tefillin*. This establishes clearly that the *birchos krias shema* are more central to the rabbinic fulfillment of *shema* than wearing *tefillin*. Nevertheless, the formulation of Rabbi Yochanan makes clear that *tefillin* does constitute an aspect of the fullest *kiyum* of *shema*, not merely an incidental accompaniment.

One might ask whether this concern about *tefillin* during *shema* extends to other contexts — for example, would a teacher who is not wearing *tefillin* be prohibited from teaching the paragraph about *tefillin*? Likely not, and the reason is instructive: when teaching, one speaks theoretically, whereas when reciting *shema*, one is making an immediate, personal declaration of obligation. The *shema* is not a study exercise but a ritual act of self-declaration, and it is at that moment of personal declaration that the embodiment of the *mitzvah* is demanded. Additionally, once the *shema* has been ritualized as part of the *tefillah*, the expectation is that one fully embodies that ritual moment rather than merely enunciating its words.

### Full Divine Kingship

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיְּקַבֵּל עָלָיו עוֹל מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם שְׁלֵמָה — יִפָּנֶה, וְיִטּוֹל יָדָיו, וְיַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וְיִקְרָא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וְיִתְפַּלֵּל, וְזוֹ הִיא מַלְכוּת שָׁמַיִם שְׁלֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who seeks to accept upon himself the complete yoke of the kingdom of Heaven** should relieve himself, wash his hands, don phylacteries, recite *Shema*, and pray, and that is acceptance of **the complete Kingdom of Heaven.**

Rabbi Yochanan teaches further: *ha-rotzeh she-yekabel alav ol malchut shamayim sheleimah* — one who wishes to accept the yoke of heaven completely — should do the following in sequence: relieve oneself (*yifneh*), wash one's hands, don *tefillin*, recite *krias shema*, and then pray. *Ve-zo hi malchut shamayim sheleimah* — this is a complete acceptance of divine kingship.

Several points in this formulation are worth examining. First, no mention is made of *tallis*, which is more optional in this context; the *gemara* later addresses *tallis* in connection with *Shemoneh Esrei*. Second, the inclusion of *tefillah* in the rubric of *malchut shamayim* is striking, since one might have assumed *malchut shamayim* was centered entirely on *shema*. The answer appears to be that the *tefillah* is the climax and translation of *malchut shamayim*: having acknowledged God's kingship and one's obligations through *shema*, and having been brought into that relationship, one then turns to God in prayer. This is the meaning of *semichat geulah li-tefillah* — joining the blessing of redemption to the *Amidah* without interruption.

More broadly, the formulation answers the question raised implicitly by Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha: should one not begin immediately with *malchut shamayim*? The answer here is no. To arrive at a *full* (*sheleimah*) *malchut shamayim*, one must prepare: attending to physical needs so as not to be distracted or behave inappropriately before God, washing one's hands as an act of physical and conceptual self-purification, and donning *tefillin* to enter a particular mindset. These preparatory steps do not delay the *malchut shamayim*; they make it complete. One further observation: *sheleimah* may be understood not merely as a qualitative description of the person's inner experience, but as a statement about the nature of *malchut shamayim* itself. A *malchut shamayim* that remains abstract and theoretical is incomplete; what makes it *shaleimah* is that it translates into how one actually prepares to encounter God — into prayer, into practice, into relationship.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כָּל הַנִּפְנֶה, וְנוֹטֵל יָדָיו, וּמַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וְקוֹרֵא קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, וּמִתְפַּלֵּל — מַעֲלֶה עָלָיו הַכָּתוּב כְּאִלּוּ בָּנָה מִזְבֵּחַ וְהִקְרִיב עָלָיו קׇרְבָּן, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶרְחַץ בְּנִקָּיוֹן כַּפָּי וַאֲסוֹבְבָה אֶת מִזְבַּחֲךָ ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Anyone who relieves himself, washes his hands, dons phylacteries, recites *Shema*, and prays, the verse ascribes** credit **to him as if he built an altar and offered a sacrifice upon it, as it is written: "I will wash in purity my hands, and I will encircle the altar of the Lord"** (Psalms 26:6).

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds: one who performs all these steps — attending to physical needs, washing hands, donning *tefillin*, reciting *shema*, and praying — without interrupting with other matters, Scripture accounts it as though he built an altar and offered a sacrifice upon it: *ke-ilu banah mizbei'ach ve-hikriv alav korban*. The verse cited is *erchatz be-nikayon kapai va-asovevah et mizbechachah Hashem* — I will wash my hands in cleanliness and go around Your altar, God — connecting hand-washing to the encircling of the altar. The earlier formulation was about the fullness of the *korban* — everything brought together including *nesachim* and *mincha*. Here the emphasis is on preparation: not just bringing the *korban*, but building the altar on which it is placed. The effort of preparation is itself given sacred significance.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: לָא סָבַר לַהּ מָר כְּאִילּוּ טָבַל, דִּכְתִיב: ״אֶרְחַץ [בְּנִקָּיוֹן]״ — וְלָא כָּתַב ״אַרְחִיץ [כַּפָּי]״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: Do you not maintain, Master,** that one who does so, **it is as if he immersed** his entire body, **as it is written: "I will wash in purity," and it is not written: "I will wash my hands"?**

*Rava* adds a comment: since the verse says *erchatz be-nikayon*, with cleanliness, and not *erchatz be-kapai*, with my hands — it should have been said that this is as if one immersed in a *mikveh*, since the word *nikayon* suggests a complete cleansing rather than hand-washing alone. The *gemara* leaves this remark without a direct reply and the discussion moves on.

## Hand-Washing Requirements

### Cleanliness Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרָבָא: חֲזִי מָר הַאי צוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנָן דַּאֲתָא מִמַּעְרְבָא וְאָמַר: מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מַיִם לִרְחוֹץ יָדָיו — מְקַנֵּחַ יָדָיו בְּעָפָר וּבִצְרוֹר וּבְקִסְמִית. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שַׁפִּיר קָאָמַר, מִי כְּתִיב ״אֶרְחַץ בְּמַיִם״? ״בְּנִקָּיוֹן״ כְּתִיב — כֹּל מִידֵּי דִּמְנַקֵּי, דְּהָא רַב חִסְדָּא לָיֵיט אַמַּאן דִּמְהַדַּר אַמַּיָּא בְּעִידָּן צְלוֹתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rava: My Master, look at this Torah scholar [*tzurva merabbanan*] who came from** Eretz Yisrael **and said** something astonishing: **One who has no water with which to wash his hands,** it is sufficient that **he wipes his hands with earth, a rock, or a sliver** of wood. Rava **replied to** Ravina: **He spoke well,** as, **is it written: I will wash with water? In purity, is written** referring to anything that cleans, **as Rav Ḥisda** would **curse one who** went out of his way **to seek water at the time of prayer.**

Ravina poses a question to Rava: did you see that student of the *rabbanan* who came from the west — from *Eretz Yisrael* — who said that one who has no water with which to wash one's hands may clean them with dirt, pebbles, or wood chips (*afar, tzror, kasmit*)? Rava confirms this, saying *shapir ka'amar* — he spoke well. The textual basis is that the verse says *erchatz be-nikayon*, "I will wash in cleanliness," not *erchatz be-mayim*, "I will wash with water." Thus, *kol midei de-manki* — anything that achieves cleanliness — suffices. This is consistent with the underlying purpose, which here is about cleanliness rather than a formal ritual requiring water specifically.

### Non-Water Alternatives

This point is supported by a statement attributed to Rav Chisda, who would curse someone who would go searching for water at the time of *davening*. If it is time to pray, one should not delay the *tefillah* in pursuit of water; any cleansing agent will do.

### Distance for Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לִקְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, אֲבָל לִתְפִלָּה מְהַדַּר. וְעַד כַּמָּה? — עַד פַּרְסָה. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְקַמֵּיהּ, אֲבָל לַאֲחוֹרֵיהּ — אֲפִילּוּ מִיל אֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר. [וּמִינַּהּ] מִיל הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ חוֹזֵר, הָא פָּחוֹת מִמִּיל — חוֹזֵר.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to seeking water, the Gemara comments: **This applies only to the recitation of *Shema*,** as the time for its recitation is limited, and if one goes seeking water he may run out of time. **However, for prayer,** which may be recited all day, **one** must go out of his way **to seek** water. **And how** far must one go out of his way to seek water? **As far as a parasang [*parsa*]. And this,** one *parsa*, **applies only before him but behind him, he need not return even one *mil*. From this** one may infer that **he need not return one *mil*, but one must return less than one *mil*.**

However, the *gemara* then introduces a qualification: *vahani milei le-kri'at shema* — this leniency of non-water alternatives applies to *shema*. But for *tefillah* — for *Shemoneh Esrei* — one should seek water specifically. And how far must one travel? Forward, in the direction one is already heading: even up to a *parsa* (four *mil*, approximately three miles). But if the water source is behind one, one need not retrace one's steps even a single *mil* (approximately three-fifths of a mile). The *gemara* then adds: for less than a *mil* backwards, one would be required to return.

This creates a textual difficulty, since earlier the same *gemara* cited Rav Chisda's condemnation of postponing *davening* for water — yet now it seems to require doing precisely that for *Shemoneh Esrei*. *Tosafot* addresses this by rejecting the *girsa* of this entire passage, arguing that the discussion about how far to travel for water pertains to hand-washing before bread, not before *davening*. The crucial distinction is that hand-washing before bread connects to the world of *tumah va-taharah* — it is a formal ritual requirement for which a *bracha* is recited, rooted in the stringencies surrounding *terumah* and sacred food. Hand-washing before *shema* and *tefillah*, by contrast, is about cleanliness and preparation — *hachon likrat Elokecha Yisrael* — and does not carry the same formal water requirement.

## Hearing Shema Dispute

### Must Hear Shema Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת שְׁמַע וְלֹא הִשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ — יָצָא. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא. קָרָא וְלֹא דִּקְדֵּק בְּאוֹתִיּוֹתֶיהָ, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יָצָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא. הַקּוֹרֵא לְמַפְרֵעַ — לֹא יָצָא. קָרָא וְטָעָה — יַחְזוֹר לִמְקוֹם שֶׁטָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who recites *Shema* and did not** recite in a manner **audible to his own ear,** either because he read inaudibly or because he is deaf, **fulfilled** his obligation. **Rabbi Yosei says: He did not fulfill** his obligation. **One who recited** *Shema* and **was not** sufficiently **precise in** his enunciation of **its letters, Rabbi Yosei says: He fulfilled** his obligation. **Rabbi Yehuda says: He did not fulfill** his obligation. **One who recited** *Shema* **out of order,** meaning he did not read the verses sequentially, **he did not fulfill** his obligation. **One who recited and erred, should return to the place** in *Shema* **that he erred.**

The *gemara* turns to a new *mishna*. The *mishna* states: *ha-korei et shema v'lo hishmia le-ozno yatza* — if one recited *shema* and did not make it audible to oneself, one has fulfilled the obligation. *Divrei Rabbi Yehuda* — this is the view of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosi says: *lo yatza* — one has not fulfilled the obligation; one must hear what one says. A second dispute: if one read but was imprecise in articulating the letters (*lo dikdek be-otiotav*), Rabbi Yosi says one is *yotzei*; Rabbi Yehuda says one is not.

These two disputes might seem to cross unexpectedly: one might have expected the position requiring audibility (Rabbi Yosi) to also require careful articulation, and the more lenient position (Rabbi Yehuda) to be lenient on both counts. Yet the *gemara* does not appear troubled by this alignment and we will see how the positions are eventually reconciled. The *mishna* adds further: if one read the *shema* out of order (*l'mafrea*), one has not fulfilled the obligation; and if one made an error and then realized it, one must return to the place of the mistake and continue in order — since simply repeating the omitted verse at the end would mean reciting out of order.

### Rabbi Yose Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? מִשּׁוּם דִּכְתִיב ״שְׁמַע״ — הַשְׁמַע לְאׇזְנְךָ מָה שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצִיא מִפִּיךָ. וְתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר, ״שְׁמַע״ — בְּכָל לָשׁוֹן שֶׁאַתָּה שׁוֹמֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The discussion in our mishna dealt with the question of whether or not one who recites *Shema* without hearing it fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara clarifies the opinions cited in the mishna: **What is the reason for Rabbi Yosei's** opinion that one must recite *Shema* in a manner audible to his own ear? **Because it is written: *Shema*,** hear, and Rabbi Yosei holds that this is to be understood literally, meaning: **Make your ears hear what your mouth utters. The first *tanna*,** who holds that one fulfills his obligation even if he does not hear his recitation of *Shema*, **holds** that ***Shema*,** hear, comes to teach something else; one may recite *Shema* **in any language that one** can **hear** and understand, and there is no requirement to recite *Shema* specifically in Hebrew.

What is the reason of Rabbi Yosi? The *gemara* cites: *dechtiv shema, hashmea le-oznecha mah she-atah motzi mipicha* — "hear, let your ears hear what proceeds from your mouth." The word *shema* in this reading commands the speaker to make the words audible to himself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, תַּרְתֵּי שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yosei** agrees with the principle derived by the first *tanna* from the word *Shema*; however Rabbi Yosei holds: **Derive two** *halakhot* **from** the word *Shema*; first, one may recite *Shema* in any language, and second, one must recite it in a manner audible to his own ears.

Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, holds that *shema* teaches only *be-chol lashon she-atah shomea* — that *shema* may be recited in any language one understands, without any requirement of audibility. And the *gemara* acknowledges that according to Rabbi Yosi, one can derive both teachings from *shema*: it permits other languages and it demands self-audibility.

### Deaf Person Terumah Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ — לֹא יִתְרוֹם. וְאִם תָּרַם — תְּרוּמָתוֹ תְּרוּמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned there** in a mishna regarding the laws of separating tithes: **A deaf person who can speak but cannot hear may not separate *teruma*** *ab initio*, because he must recite a blessing over the separation of *teruma* and he is unable to hear the blessing. **But** after the fact, **if he did separate it, his *teruma* is** valid ***teruma*.**

The *gemara* now brings a *mishna* from *Terumot* to illuminate the dispute. The *mishna* states: *cheresh ha-medabber ve-eino shomea lo yitrom* — a person who can speak but cannot hear may not take *terumah* at the outset. The reason, as the *gemara* understands it, is that one is obligated to recite a *bracha* before separating *terumah*, and since this person cannot hear the *bracha* he is reciting, he cannot do so *le-chatchilah*. However, *ve-im taram, terumato terumah* — if he did do it, the *terumah* is valid after the fact.

This case focuses attention on a subtle distinction: the issue is not merely that the recitation must be audible (that others could theoretically hear it), but that the person himself must hear it. This is an unusual demand. One could have formulated the requirement as: one's lips must move and produce audible sound — a standard of articulation. But to say that the speaker must specifically be the one who hears it is a stronger claim. In the context of *shema*, this makes sense: *shema* is fundamentally a declaration directed first and foremost at oneself. *Shema Yisrael Hashem Elokeinu Hashem Echad* is not a prayer addressed to God — unlike *Shemoneh Esrei*, which may appropriately be whispered and which is directed to God — but a personal declaration of faith. If the speaker does not hear what he is declaring, the self-directed nature of the declaration is undermined.

### Three Positions Reconciliation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תְּנָא חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דִּתְנַן: הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת ״שְׁמַע״ וְלֹא הִשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ — יָצָא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: לֹא יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who is** this ***tanna*** who holds that if **a deaf person who can speak but cannot hear** separates *teruma*, **it is** considered *teruma* **after the fact,** but ***ab initio*** he may **not** do so? **Rav Ḥisda said: It is Rabbi Yosei, as we learned** in our mishna: **One who recites *Shema* and did not** recite it so it was **audible to his own ear, he fulfilled** his obligation. **This is the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yosei says: He did not fulfill** his obligation.

The *gemara* identifies this position with Rabbi Yosi: it is Rabbi Yosi who holds that failure to hear oneself even *be-di'avad* disqualifies. The implication is that the case of *terumah* — where failure to hear results in disqualification *le-chatchilah* but valid *terumah* *be-di'avad* — should align with some other opinion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לֹא יָצָא, אֶלָּא גַּבֵּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, אֲבָל תְּרוּמָה — מִשּׁוּם בְּרָכָה הוּא, וּבְרָכָה דְּרַבָּנַן וְלָא בִּבְרָכָה תַּלְיָא מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda elaborates: **Rabbi Yosei only stated** that a deaf person **did not fulfill** his obligation even after the fact **with regard to the recitation of *Shema*,** which is a **biblical** obligation. **But** with regard to ***teruma*,** the concern is **due to the blessing recited over** its separation. **And the blessing is** by **rabbinic law,** and the separation of *teruma* itself **is not contingent upon the blessing.** The separation of *teruma* takes effect regardless of whether or not a blessing is recited, so in the case of a deaf person, he fulfilled his obligation after the fact.

The *gemara* now works through the logical possibilities. Rav Chisda initially identifies the *terumah mishna* as following Rabbi Yosi. But the *gemara* challenges this: Rabbi Yosi says that even *be-di'avad* one is not *yotzei* by *shema* without hearing — so how can the *terumah* case allow it *be-di'avad*? The response is that the *terumah* case is different because the problem there is the failure to hear the *bracha*, which is only rabbinic, and *be-di'avad* the *terumah* is valid because the *bracha* requirement does not *taly milta* — does not determine the validity of the *terumah* itself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הִיא, דִּילְמָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא? וְאָמַר גַּבֵּי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע נָמוּ דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא. תֵּדַע, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״הַקּוֹרֵא״, דִּיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the assertion that this mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei: **And from where** do you infer that **this is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei? Perhaps it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and he said that with regard to the recitation of *Shema* as well,** if one did not recite it in a manner audible to his own ears, **he has** fulfilled his obligation **after the fact,** but ***ab initio*** he may **not** do so. This opinion is identical to that of the *tanna* in the case of *teruma*. **Know** that this is true because **it was taught** in the mishna: **One who recites** *Shema* without it being audible to his own ear. The *tanna* formulated the dispute in a case which was **after the fact.** If one already recited *Shema* in this manner, **yes,** he fulfilled his obligation. The *tanna* did not formulate the case in the mishna using *ab initio* language, i.e., one may recite *Shema* in a manner inaudible to his own ears because, ***ab initio*,** he may **not** do so according to Rabbi Yehuda.

But a further challenge arises: if the *terumah* case is Rabbi Yosi's, then what position corresponds to the *bracha* of *birchat hamazon* — which the *gemara* cites as a case where one may not recite it in one's heart *le-chatchilah*, yet if one did recite it that way, *be-di'avad* one has fulfilled the obligation? That middle position — *le-chatchilah* no, *be-di'avad* yes — does not fit Rabbi Yosi (who disqualifies even *be-di'avad* by *shema*) and also does not seem to fit Rabbi Yehuda if we understand him as fully lenient even *le-chatchilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הַאי דְּקָתָנֵי ״הַקּוֹרֵא״, לְהוֹדִיעֲךָ כֹּחוֹ דְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּאָמַר דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא. דְּאִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, אֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof. In explanation, **they say:** The fact **that** the mishna **taught** the *halakha* utilizing the after the fact language: **One who recited,** does not prove that Rabbi Yehuda also holds that one may not *ab initio* recite *Shema* in a manner inaudible to his own ears. Rather, the mishna formulated the *halakha* in that manner **is to convey the far-reaching** nature of the opinion **of Rabbi Yosei, who said that** if one does so, even **after the fact, he did not fulfill** his obligation to recite *Shema*. **As, if it** sought to convey the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** then **even *ab initio* he** may **fulfill** his obligation without hearing the recitation.

The *gemara* considers: perhaps Rabbi Yehuda is precisely the middle position — *le-chatchilah* one should hear, *be-di'avad* one need not. And in that case there would also need to be an entirely permissive position — even *le-chatchilah* one need not hear — to account for the *terumah mishna* cited in the name of Rabbi Yehuda bereih de-Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, who says a person who can speak but cannot hear may take *terumah* even *le-chatchilah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקִימְתָּא — כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? וְאֶלָּא הָא דְתַנְיָא: לֹא יְבָרֵךְ אָדָם בִּרְכַּת הַמָּזוֹן בְּלִבּוֹ, וְאִם בֵּירַךְ — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this conclusion: **How did you establish** the reasoning of the mishna dealing with the laws of *terumot*? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that one who does not hear his recitation does not fulfill his obligation even after the fact. **But** what about **that which was taught** in a *baraita*: **One may not recite the Grace after Meals,** which like *Shema* and unlike the blessing on separating *teruma* is a Torah commandment, **in his heart,** inaudibly, **and if he recited** the blessing in that manner, **he fulfilled** his obligation?

This gives us three positions: (1) Rabbi Yosi — even *be-di'avad* one is not *yotzei* without hearing; (2) a middle position — *le-chatchilah* one must hear, *be-di'avad* one is *yotzei*; (3) a fully permissive position — even *le-chatchilah* one need not hear.

The question is how to distribute these positions between Rabbi Yehuda and the other authorities. One resolution: the middle position (*le-chatchilah* yes, *be-di'avad* it works) is the position of Rabbi Yehuda *in the name of his teacher* — as we find in a *baraita*: *Rabbi Yehuda omer mishum Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya, ha-korei et shema tzarich she-yashmia le-ozno* — one who recites *shema* is required to make it audible to oneself. The word *tzarich* — "is required" — signals a *le-chatchilah* standard without necessarily disqualifying *be-di'avad*. Rabbi Yehuda thus transmits his teacher's position as the middle one.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְלָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּאִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָא אָמַר, לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי יָצָא? אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא מַאי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וְדִיעֲבַד — אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita*? **It is in accordance** neither with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda. As if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't he say** according to the way the Gemara explained his position that even ***ab initio* he may fulfill** his obligation in that manner, and he need not recite it audibly. In that case, why should one refrain from reciting the blessing in his heart? And **if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei;** he holds that even **after the fact, he did not** fulfill his obligation. **Rather, what** must we say? We must revert to the explanation that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda** who holds that **after the fact, yes,** he fulfilled his obligation, but ***ab initio*, no,** one may not recite it in a manner inaudible to his own ears.

### Rabbi Meir: Heart Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא הָא דְתָנֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי: חֵרֵשׁ הַמְדַבֵּר וְאֵינוֹ שׁוֹמֵעַ — תּוֹרֵם לְכַתְּחִלָּה, מַנִּי? לָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי. אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָא אָמַר דִּיעֲבַד אִין, לְכַתְּחִלָּה לָא. אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי — הָא אָמַר דִּיעֲבַד נָמֵי לָא! אֶלָּא, לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְכַתְּחִלָּה נָמֵי. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — דִידֵיהּ, הָא — דְרַבֵּיהּ. דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה: הַקּוֹרֵא אֶת שְׁמַע צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּשְׁמִיעַ לְאׇזְנוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל ה׳ אֱלֹהֵינוּ ה׳ אֶחָד״. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי מֵאִיר: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֶׁר אָנֹכִי מְצַוְּךָ הַיּוֹם עַל לְבָבֶךָ״ — אַחַר כַּוָּנַת הַלֵּב הֵן הֵן הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this: **But** what about **that** *baraita* **which was taught by Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi: A deaf** person **who speaks but does not hear may, *ab initio*, separate *teruma*.** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is that** *baraita*? According to what we have said, **it is in accordance** neither with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda nor** with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. As if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, didn't he say** that **after the fact, yes,** he fulfilled his obligation, although it was inaudible to his own ears, but ***ab initio*, no,** he may not fulfill his obligation in that manner? And **if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, didn't he say** that if he does not hear himself, **even after the fact he** did **not** fulfill his obligation? **Rather,** we must revert to the previous explanation but with a slight revision, and say that **actually it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, and even *ab initio*,** a deaf person may **also** separate *teruma*. **And this is not difficult** and there is no contradiction between the mishna and the *baraita*, **as this is his** own opinion **and that is his teacher's** opinion. **As it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda said in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya: One who recites *Shema* must make it audible to his ears, as it is stated: "Hear, Israel, the Lord is our God, the Lord is One."** This means that he must do so, but after the fact, if he failed to do so, he nevertheless fulfilled his obligation. The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi Meir said to him: It says: "Which I command you this day, upon your heart;"** which Rabbi Meir explains to mean that **the** significance of the **words follows the intention of the heart** and even *ab initio* one need not recite *Shema* audibly.

At this point, *Rabbi Meir* is introduced with a striking position. He cites the verse *asher anochi metzavecha ha-yom al levavecha* — "which I command you today upon your heart." From this Rabbi Meir derives: *achar kavanat ha-lev hen hen ha-devarim* — everything follows the intention of the heart. On this view, not only is audibility unnecessary, but perhaps even the verbal articulation of *shema* is dispensable; pure thought — *hirhur* — may suffice. This opens the question of whether *hirhur ke-dibbur dami* — whether mental contemplation is legally equivalent to speech.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כְּרַבֵּיהּ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ. וְלָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — רַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הָא — רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **Now that you have arrived at this** point and the entire *baraita* has been cited, **even** if **you say** that **Rabbi Yehuda holds in accordance with** the opinion of **his teacher,** that only after the fact, does a deaf person fulfill his obligation, **it is,** nevertheless, **not difficult** and the different *baraitot* are not contradictory. As **this** *baraita* permitting a deaf person to separate *teruma ab initio* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** while **this** *baraita* that holds that he may not recite Grace after Meals *ab initio* but after the fact he fulfilled his obligation is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* concludes by consolidating the framework: now that Rabbi Meir has been introduced as holding that even *le-chatchilah* one need not hear, we can resolve the apparent tension between the middle position and the fully permissive position. The fully permissive position is Rabbi Meir's — who says *le-chatchilah* it is fine not to hear, and perhaps not even to mouth the words. The middle position (*le-chatchilah* one should hear, *be-di'avad* it is valid) is either Rabbi Yehuda in the name of his teacher, or perhaps Rabbi Yehuda himself if we take his own view to be the middle ground.

In sum, the *gemara* has mapped out three positions with respect to the requirement of hearing *shema*: Rabbi Yosi holds that even *be-di'avad* one is not *yotzei* without hearing; the middle position (associated with Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya transmitted through Rabbi Yehuda) holds that *le-chatchilah* one must hear but *be-di'avad* it works; and Rabbi Meir holds that even *le-chatchilah* one need not hear, and perhaps mental intention alone suffices. The open question — which these positions apply to exclusively, and whether *hirhur* alone fulfills the obligation — will continue in tomorrow's discussion.
