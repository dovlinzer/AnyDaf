# Sanhedrin 74 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rape & Ketubah Fine

### Mishnah: Fine Cases

The daf opens by continuing the discussion of cases in which one would be permitted — indeed obligated — to use lethal force to protect a woman from being sexually violated. As established in the preceding pages, this license extends only to acts of intercourse that carry the penalty of *karet* or the death penalty, not to every act of rape. With that framework in place, the Gemara picks up near the bottom of 73b and transitions into a new difficulty raised by a *mishnah* in a different context.

The *mishnah* under discussion enumerates *naarot* — maidens — who are entitled to the statutory *knas* (the fine paid by a rapist or seducer to a girl who is not yet married), even in cases where the man would ordinarily be forbidden to marry her. One such case is *haba al achoto* — a man who violates his sister. Though he has transgressed one of the *arayot* and cannot marry her, he nonetheless pays the fine. The *mishnah* further specifies that if the act was one that carries the death penalty (*chayav mita*), there would be no *knas*, invoking the principle of *kam lei bid'raba minei* — the greater penalty (death) absorbs the lesser one (the monetary fine). But if the act carried *karet*, not the death penalty, the *knas* would still apply.

### Kam Lei Principle

This invocation of *kam lei* generates a difficulty. In the context of the *machteret* (the housebreaker), the Gemara had already established that once a person is doing an act for which his life may be taken, he incurs no monetary obligations in connection with that act — a principle analogous to *kam lei*. If, then, a man is raping a girl and committing an act of *chayav karet*, and we would be permitted to kill him to stop the rape, why does he owe the *knas* at all?

### Gemara's Question

The Gemara poses this question directly: given that bystanders could have killed this man to protect the girl, the act he is performing is one for which his life is forfeit. By the logic of *kam lei*, he should not be obligated in any monetary payment. Why, then, does the *mishnah* say he pays the *knas*?

### Ha'arah & Pagum

The Gemara reports that the rabbis raised this difficulty before Rav Chisda, and he offered the following resolution: *mishaat ha'ara'ah d'fagmah, ishtarei mikatla* — from the very moment that *ha'ara'ah* begins, that is, the initial stage of penetration (or, according to one opinion, the mere external contact of the genitals), she has already been *nifgam* — she has been defiled, she has become, in the Gemara's terminology, spoiled goods. And once she has been *nifgam*, we can no longer kill him in the middle of the act. The license to use lethal force was predicated on protecting her from that very disgrace, and once the disgrace has occurred — from that very first moment — the license evaporates.

The force of this ruling is deeply disturbing to modern sensibilities. The man continues to rape her, and yet the license to intervene has already lapsed, because the harm that justified intervention has already been done. He is thus no longer performing an act for which we could kill him. And therefore — *mamona lo mishalem ad gmar bia* — the monetary obligation, the *knas*, does not crystallize until the act is complete. By that point, since he could no longer be killed during the act, there is no *kam lei* to speak of, and he pays.

Rashi explains that the *knas* does not arise until the end of the act because the fine is for rupturing the hymen, which only occurs upon completion. Tosafot disputes this, arguing that the fine is not specifically for the hymen and would technically be incurred at the very first instant of penetration. According to Tosafot, in a normal case the obligation begins at that first moment of *ha'ara'ah* — but in this case, at that precise first moment, the perpetrator could still be killed, so *kam lei* would apply. The instant after that first moment, he can no longer be killed — she is already *nifgam* — and that is when the obligation of the *knas* takes hold.

### Timing of Pagam

The Gemara then notes that this resolution works well according to the opinion that *ha'ara'ah* means *nishika* — the external touching or "kissing" of the genitals, before penetration. In that case, there are two distinct moments: the moment of *nishika* (at which she becomes *nifgam* and the license to kill him ends), and the moment of actual penetration (at which the *knas* obligation begins and she is no longer considered a *besulah*). These two moments are distinguishable, and so the analysis holds.

But according to the opinion that *ha'ara'ah* means *hachnasat ha'atara* — the insertion of the crown of the penis, the beginning of penetration itself — the analysis breaks down. At that single moment, she both becomes *nifgam* and loses her status as a *besulah*. If the *pagam* occurs at the moment of penetration, and we could still have killed him at that moment, then after that moment she is no longer a *besulah* — and the *knas* only applies to a *besulah*. So there is no remaining window in which the *knas* could apply.

### Case Resolutions

Rav Chisda himself offers a solution to this impasse. The case that generates the *knas* despite *chayav karet* is one where he first engaged in anal intercourse and then vaginal intercourse: *shelo kedarka*, and then *kedarka*. The first act does not cause her to lose her status as a *besulah*, since it is not vaginal penetration. During that first act, we could have killed him, but she has already been *nifgam*. By the time he proceeds to the vaginal act — which does carry the *knas* — she is already *nifgam* and we can no longer kill him, yet she is still a *besulah*, so the *knas* applies.

Rava offers a second resolution: the case is one where the woman herself says not to kill him, out of fear that he will kill her if intervention is attempted (*minachsa shelo yargenu*), and this follows the position of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the license to use lethal force is not merely to protect her from *pagam* but because her life is at risk. Once she declares that her life is not at risk and she does not want him killed, we may not intervene. Since we are not permitted to kill him, *kam lei* does not apply, and the *knas* is owed.

Rav Pappa offers the most straightforward resolution: the case involves *mefutah* — seduction, not rape — and this is *divrei hakol*, accepted by all opinions. The Torah's *knas* applies both to rape and to seduction. In a case of seduction, there is no victim to protect and no license to kill the man, so *kam lei* never arises, and the *knas* applies normally.

### Non-Lethal Intervention

Abbaye's answer introduces a principle that will occupy the Gemara throughout the next section. Abbaye says the case is one where it was possible to stop the perpetrator by injuring one of his limbs rather than killing him — *b'yakhol lehatzilo b'echad mei-evarav* — and this follows the position of Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul. Since it was possible to neutralize him without lethal force, we were not permitted to use lethal force, and therefore the man was never in the category of someone whose life is forfeit. Accordingly, *kam lei* never applies, and the *knas* is owed in full.

## Rodeph Doctrine

### R. Yonatan ben Shaul

The Gemara now opens a dedicated analysis of Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's principle: *rodeph shehaya rodeph achar chaveiro l'horgo, v'yakhol lehatzilo b'echad mei-evarav, v'lo hitzil, neherag alav* — if a pursuer was chasing another person to kill him, and it was possible to stop him by injuring one of his limbs, and instead the intervener killed the pursuer outright, the intervener has committed an act of murder. In practical terms, since the intervener would need to have been warned and to have accepted the warning before being subject to execution, the actual execution of the intervener in a *beis din* setting is difficult to achieve — but the Gemara's point is that the act is categorized as *murder*.

This is a striking ruling, and the Gemara does not take it as self-evident. The obvious concern is that imposing this kind of caution on would-be rescuers — requiring them to first attempt a less-than-lethal intervention — may result in the victim not being saved at all. How much caution can reasonably be demanded of an intervener when a life is in the balance, without that caution itself becoming irresponsible? This is precisely why the Gemara seeks a derivation for Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's position: it is not the intuitively obvious rule, and it is not universally held, though the *halacha* does follow it.

### Miscarriage Case

The proof for Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's principle comes from the law of the miscarrying woman. The Gemara cites the verse: *vechi yinatzu anashim* — when men fight. Rabbi Elazar explains that this describes a fight to the death, as evidenced by the continuation of the passage: *ve'im yihye ason venatata nefesh tachat nafesh* — if there is a fatality, you give a life for a life. The verse's promise of execution tells us that these men were attempting to kill one another; otherwise, accidental killing would not generate the death penalty.

Now the same passage continues: *v'lo yihye ason, anosh ye'anesh* — if there is no fatality but a pregnant bystander is struck and miscarries, the striker must pay a fine. The Gemara asks: how can he ever be obligated to pay this fine? He is engaged in an act of attempted murder, for which he could be killed. Under *kam lei*, that should eliminate any monetary obligation, including the payment for the miscarriage. The answer, says Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul, is that the verse is discussing a case where the pursuer *could* have been neutralized *b'echad mei-evarav* — with one of his limbs. In such a case, we are not permitted to kill him. Since he cannot be killed, he is not in the category of *chayav mita*, and therefore *kam lei* does not apply. He therefore owes the fine for the miscarriage.

To make this vivid: imagine two people fighting to the death. One of them, in the course of pursuing the other, accidentally strikes a pregnant bystander who miscarries. He is liable the payment. Why? Because in this specific scenario it was possible to stop him short of lethal force — perhaps by grabbing him, striking his arm, shooting him in the leg — and since that was possible, we were not permitted to use lethal force against him. He therefore was never in the category of a person who could be killed, *kam lei* never applies, and the monetary obligation for the miscarriage stands.

### Different Victims

The Gemara, however, challenges this proof. Perhaps Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's principle is unnecessary to explain the payment for the miscarriage. Perhaps the explanation is simpler: *misa lizeh ve'tashlumin lizeh* — death is owed to one party and payment is owed to a different party. The principle of *kam lei* might apply only when the victim of the fatal act and the victim of the monetary claim are the same person. Here, the man he is trying to kill and the pregnant woman are two entirely separate individuals, with separate claims before entirely separate tribunals. Perhaps *kam lei* does not operate across different victims.

To draw this out: if someone fires a bullet at Reuven, and the bullet passes through Shimon's stained-glass window before killing Reuven, one might argue that Reuven's relatives' murder claim and Shimon's property claim are entirely separate cases. *Kam lei* eliminates monetary claims that arise from the same act directed at the same victim; it might not reach across to eliminate claims by third parties who were incidentally damaged.

If so, the proof for Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's principle from the miscarrying woman is unnecessary: even without that principle, the man could be killed — and yet he would owe the woman's husband for the miscarriage, simply because she is a different victim.

### Rava on Damage

The Gemara rejects this alternative. Rava teaches explicitly: *rodeph shehaya rodeph achar chaveiro, veshibar et ha-keilim, bein shel nirdaf bein shel kol adam — patur* — a pursuer chasing another person who breaks vessels in the course of the chase, whether those vessels belong to the victim or to a third party entirely, is exempt from payment. The reason: *mischayav b'nafsho hu* — he is doing an act for which he can be killed, and therefore all monetary obligations dissolve, regardless of to whom they would be owed.

This is precisely the case of *misa lizeh ve'tashlumin lizeh*: the pursuer owes death in his relationship with the victim, and the broken vessels belong to a third party. And yet Rava rules he is exempt from paying for those vessels. The principle of *kam lei* operates across different victims. Therefore, the alternative explanation of the miscarrying woman collapses: if the pursuer could be killed, he would be exempt from the miscarriage payment to the husband as well. The only way to explain why he *does* owe the payment is Rabbi Yonatan ben Shaul's principle: in this case, he could not be killed — because he could have been stopped *b'echad mei-evarav*.

### Good Samaritan

Rava's teaching continues with a careful taxonomy of the various parties' obligations. The *nirdaf* (the person being pursued) who, in fleeing, breaks the pursuer's vessels, is exempt — the pursuer has forfeited his life and his property to protect the victim, and so any damage done to the pursuer's property in service of that protection is covered under that forfeiture. But if the *nirdaf* breaks the vessels of a third party in fleeing, he is liable: *she-matzil atzmo b'mamon chaveiro* — he is saving his own life at the expense of another's property, and even though he was permitted to do so, the third party bears no obligation to absorb that loss.

The third case is the intervener — the Good Samaritan — who pursues the pursuer to stop him from killing the victim. If this intervener breaks vessels in the process of intervention, he is exempt, whether those vessels belong to the pursuer, to the victim, or to a third party. The exemption for the pursuer's property is obvious: the pursuer's property, like the pursuer's life, is forfeit. The exemption for the victim's property is also understandable — we assume the victim would consent to having his property used to save his life. But why should the Good Samaritan be exempt from paying a third party whose property he has damaged?

The Gemara answers candidly: *ve'lo min ha-din* — strictly speaking, as a matter of pure law, he should not be exempt. But *she'im i atah omer ken, nimtza ein adam shematzil et chaveiro miyad ha-rodeph* — if you held interveners liable for incidental damage to third parties, no one would intervene. The policy rationale is decisive: we exempt the Good Samaritan from such payment to encourage rescue. The Gemara notes that this is precisely the logic behind secular "good Samaritan" laws, which do not oblige intervention but do protect those who intervene in good faith from civil liability for inadvertent harm.

## Intervention & Aveirot

### Animal Case

A brief but important limiting principle is stated: if someone is "pursuing" an animal — that is, about to commit an act with an animal — one may not use lethal force to intervene. Since there is no human victim whose life or dignity is at stake, the entire logic of the *rodeph* doctrine does not apply.

### Avodah Zarah

The Gemara now cites a *beraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai teaches that one who is about to commit *avodah zarah* — idol worship — may be stopped with lethal force (*nitan lehatzilo b'nafsho*). The derivation is a *kal vachomer*: if to protect the honor of an ordinary person (*pegam hedyot*) one may use lethal force, then certainly to protect God's honor (*pegam gavoah*), which is desecrated by an act of *avodah zarah*, lethal force is permitted.

This is a striking position, because it moves away from the framework of protecting a human victim and into the framework of preventing a severe sin. One way to understand this is that the victim of *avodah zarah* is God — that an act of idol worship is a violation of God's honor, analogous to the violation of a human victim's honor in a case of rape. Whether this violation is inherent in the act itself, committed even in private, or whether it is the community's tolerance of public *avodah zarah* that creates the *chillul Hashem* — that is itself a question worth pressing. But Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's position is that the license to use lethal force is not contingent on the victim being human.

The Gemara asks whether one may derive a *halachic* ruling permitting lethal force from a *kal vachomer*. The answer is that Shmuel holds one may (*onshin min ha-din*), and Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai's ruling follows that view.

### Shabbat Violation

Rabbi Elazar berabbi Shimon extends his father's position: one who violates *Shabbat* may also be stopped with lethal force. He holds like his father that *onshin min ha-din*, and he links *Shabbat* to *avodah zarah* through a *gezeirah shavah* — the word *chillul* appears in the verse regarding *Shabbat* and in the verse regarding *avodah zarah* — thereby extending the license for lethal intervention from *avodah zarah* to *Shabbat* desecration. The underlying logic parallels that of *avodah zarah*: a public desecration of *Shabbat* unchallenged by the community becomes a *chillul Hashem*, a violation of God's honor that demands intervention.

This is not the normative ruling. But it illuminates a coherent worldview in which communal tolerance of public sin — particularly sins that symbolize rejection of God's sovereignty — constitutes its own *chillul Hashem*, which in turn activates the logic of the *rodeph* framework.

## Martyrdom Principle

### Three Categories

The Gemara now transitions to the foundational discussion of *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* — the principle that one must die rather than transgress. This transition is organic: having discussed when bystanders may or must kill a *rodeph* to prevent murder or sexual violation, and having seen the view that lethal intervention is appropriate even for *avodah zarah*, the Gemara now asks the parallel question from the other direction — when must a person surrender his own life rather than commit a sin?

Rabbi Yochanan transmits in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak that the Sages voted and concluded in the attic of the house of Nitza in Lod: with respect to all other *aveirot* in the Torah, if one is told "transgress or be killed," one should transgress and not be killed. The exceptions are *avodah zarah*, *giluy arayot*, and *shfichut damim* — idol worship, forbidden sexual relations, and murder. For these three, one must die rather than transgress. It is worth noting that these three categories correspond precisely to what the Gemara has been discussing: the cases where one may use lethal force to stop a perpetrator are murder, *giluy arayot*, and — according to some — *avodah zarah*. The parallel is not coincidental. The same offenses that justify killing someone to protect others also obligate a person to sacrifice himself rather than commit them.

### Avodah Zarah Debate

The Gemara immediately raises a challenge. A *beraita* records that Rabbi Yishmael permits transgressing *avodah zarah* under threat of death, deriving this from the verse *vechai bahem* — "and he shall live by them, and not die by them." Rabbi Yishmael treats *avodah zarah* no differently from other *aveirot* in this respect. However, he qualifies this: *yachol afilu befarhesya* — one might think this applies even in public. To this he responds: the verse also says *ve-nikdashti betoch bnei Yisrael* — "I shall be sanctified among the Children of Israel" — implying that when the act occurs publicly, it must be resisted even at the cost of one's life. In public, the symbolic weight of the act is entirely different: publicly submitting to *avodah zarah* communicates that one values one's life above one's faith, and the *kiddush Hashem* achieved by public martyrdom is the counter-message that faith is paramount.

The Gemara explains that the ruling requiring martyrdom for *avodah zarah* even in private follows Rabbi Eliezer.

### Ahavat Hashem

A *beraita* records Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning: the verse *ve-ahavta et Hashem Elokecha bechol levavecha uvechol nafshecha uvechol meodecha* — "you shall love the Lord your God with all your heart, with all your soul, and with all your possessions." Rabbi Eliezer asks: if the verse already says *bechol nafshecha* — with all your life — why add *bechol meodecha* — with all your possessions? And if it says *bechol meodecha*, why add *bechol nafshecha*? The answer: for a person whose body is more precious to him than his money, the verse says *bechol nafshecha* — you must give up your life. For a person whose money is more precious than his life, the verse says *bechol meodecha* — you must give up your possessions.

The critical point is that this reasoning operates through *ahavat Hashem* — the love of God — not through the public logic of *kiddush Hashem*. An act of *avodah zarah*, even in private, even when forced, is a momentary betrayal of one's fidelity to God. Just as an act of infidelity in a marriage undermines the relationship even if it is forced and even if it is a single act — the commitment of *ve-ahavta* demands an absoluteness of devotion that cannot be compromised even under compulsion. And therefore, even in private, one must give up one's life rather than perform an act of *avodah zarah*.

It is worth observing that the very area most contested in the martyrdom framework — *avodah zarah* — is the same area contested in the *rodeph* framework. By the *rodeph* doctrine, all authorities agree that lethal intervention is required to protect a murder victim or a rape victim, but it is debated whether lethal intervention is permitted to prevent *avodah zarah*. Similarly in the martyrdom framework, all agree that one must die rather than commit murder or *giluy arayot*, but whether one must die rather than commit *avodah zarah* in private is debated. The *psak halacha* is that one must die rather than commit *avodah zarah* even in private, but one may not use lethal force to prevent another person from committing *avodah zarah*. The two principles do not overlap perfectly, but the structural parallel is striking.

### Giluy Arayot

The Gemara derives the requirement to die rather than commit *giluy arayot* from the verse dealing with the rape of the betrothed maiden: *ki ka'asher yakum ish al re'ehu uretzacho nefesh, ken hadavar hazeh* — "as a man rises against his fellow and murders him, so is this matter." The link to murder teaches in both directions. First, just as by the betrothed maiden one uses lethal force to protect her (*ein moshia lah*), so too for murder one may use lethal force to protect the victim. Second — and this is the point relevant here — just as for murder one must die rather than commit the act, so too by *giluy arayot* one must die rather than transgress.

### Murder Case

The Gemara then asks: where do we know that for murder itself one must die rather than commit it? The answer is *sevara* — logical reasoning, without need of a verse. The case is reported directly: someone came before Rabbah and said that the non-Jewish governor of his city had commanded him to kill a particular person, threatening death if he refused. Rabbah replied: *liktlocha ve-lo tiktol* — let him kill you, and do not you commit murder. Why? *Mi yeimar de-dama didach sumak tefei? Dilma dama de-hahu gavra sumak tefei.* — Who says your blood is any redder than his? Perhaps his blood is redder than yours.

The logic is that the license to violate an *aveira* under compulsion derives from the Torah's intent to protect life. But when a life will be taken regardless — either yours or his — what gives you license to commit murder? Your right to self-preservation does not extend to taking another's life, because there is no principled reason why your life is more valuable than his. Rashi frames this somewhat differently: the Torah permits *aveirot* under duress in order to protect life, but where a life will be taken no matter what, the entire rationale for permitting the *aveira* dissolves.

## Public & Persecution

### Gezerot Malchut

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo shanu ela she-lo bishat gzeirat hamalchut* — the permission to transgress under duress rather than surrender one's life applies only when the government is not engaged in a systematic campaign to uproot the faith. But during a *sha'at hashmad* — a time of religious persecution, when the governing power is actively trying to eradicate Jewish practice — even a *mitzvah kala*, the most trivial commandment, one must die rather than transgress. The reasoning is historical and existential: religions survive because people are willing to martyr themselves for them. Once individuals begin calculating that their lives are more valuable than their faith, the cumulative effect is that the faith itself is extinguished. Only the willingness to die for even small things signals to an oppressive regime that it will not succeed.

### Ravin's Position

When Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael, he transmitted a stricter ruling in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *lo amru ela betzina* — the permission to transgress rather than die applies only when the act occurs in private. But *aval befarhesya* — when the act is public — even when there is no *sha'at hashmad*, even a *mitzvah kala* requires martyrdom.

### Parhesya Definition

This raises the question of how much of a public setting is required. The Gemara records that Rav Yaakov stated in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ein farhesya pachutah mei-asara bnei adam* — *parhesya* requires no fewer than ten people. It is obviously assumed that these must be Jews, as derived from the verse *ve-nikdashti betoch bnei Yisrael* — "I shall be sanctified among the Children of Israel" — which specifies the Jewish community as the relevant audience for *kiddush Hashem*.

The Gemara records a further question from Rav Yirmiya: what if nine of the ten are Jews and one is not a Jew? The answer is drawn from a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *toch* appears both in the *kiddush Hashem* verse and in the verse *hibadlu mitoch ha-eidah hazot* regarding the spies, where there were ten — all of whom were Israelites. Just as there, all ten were Jews, so here all ten must be Jews.

It is worth noting the conceptual distinction between *parhesya* of ten people and the formal *minyan* of ten men required for certain communal prayers. In the context of *parhesya*, there is significant opinion that women count among the ten, since this is not a *minyan* for prayer but a measure of communal impact. The same verse (*betoch bnei Yisrael*) that generates the requirement of ten for *minyan* is here read to generate the requirement of ten for *parhesya*, yet in one context it is read to require men only, and in the other it may be read to include women. The same verse admits of different framings in different contexts.

### Self-Interest Exception

The Gemara introduces a critical qualification to the *parhesya* rule. When a person is coercing a Jew to transgress for the coercer's own benefit — *hana'at atzman* — this does not constitute the kind of public transgression that requires martyrdom, even if it occurs in front of many people. Rava illustrates: if a non-Jew tells a Jew to cut a particular herb on Shabbat and give it to his animal — otherwise he will kill him — the Jew should cut the herb and not be killed. The coercer wants the herb for his animal; this is an act of self-interest, not an attempt to demonstrate that he can make Jews transgress. Everyone present understands this. There is no symbolic undermining of the faith.

But if the same non-Jew says to cut the herb and throw it in the river — that is, without any benefit to himself — then the entire purpose of the coercion is to force a public act of *chillul Shabbat*. In that case, *liktlo ve-lo liktol* — let him kill the Jew, and the Jew should not transgress. Rava states the principle: *la'avorei milta ka ba'ei* — he is trying to cause the transgression itself. And when that is the purpose, it becomes an act requiring martyrdom.

### Esther Exception

The Gemara raises the case of Esther. Her situation was publicly known — she was taken into the king's palace, and everyone was aware. Why did she not give up her life rather than submit?

Abbaye answers: *Esther karka olam hayeta* — Esther was the ground; she was passive. This is a significant halakhic concept. Several *rishonim* understand this to mean that *halacha* categorizes the woman as passive in the act of intercourse — the act is done *to* her, not by her. Since *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* applies to actively committing a transgression, a woman in this position is not subject to the same demand. Tosafot draws a parallel: if someone threatens to kill you unless you allow yourself to be thrown onto an infant and crush the infant's head, you may allow it — you are not doing the act, the other person is.

Rava offers a simpler answer: *hana'at atzman shani* — Achashveirosh acted for his own pleasure and self-interest, not for the purpose of forcing Esther to transgress. This is the *hana'at atzman* exception. Even where the act is public (*befarhesya*), if the coercer is acting for his own benefit, martyrdom is not required. Rava extends this principle to explain another practice: the practice of providing fire-pans to Zoroastrian fire rituals under duress — clearly a public act, but permitted because the Zoroastrians wanted the fire-pans for their own worship, not to cause Jews to transgress.

The deeper question raised by the *karka olam* principle is whether it reflects a general *halachic* characterization of women as passive in all acts of intercourse, or only in the specific context of rape — where, as the Ramban notes, the woman frequently has no choice in the matter even if she wishes to give her life. The Esther case complicates the simpler reading, since Esther was not being raped in the conventional sense. The major *rishonim* debate this at length, including the question of whether *yehareg ve-al ya'avor* in cases of *giluy arayot* applies equally to women. The foundation of that debate is laid here on this *daf*, and it remains one of the most significant unresolved questions of this *sugya*.
