# Bava Metzia 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Neshech & Tarbit

### Beraita: Neshech/Tarbit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶת כַּסְפְּךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לוֹ בְּנֶשֶׁךְ וּבְמַרְבִּית לֹא תִתֵּן אׇכְלֶךָ״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נֶשֶׁךְ בְּכֶסֶף וְרִבִּית בְּאוֹכֶל. נֶשֶׁךְ בְּאוֹכֶל מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ אֹכֶל״. רִבִּית בְּכֶסֶף מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ כֶּסֶף״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and with *marbit* you shall not give him your food"** (Leviticus 25:37). **I have** derived **only** that there is a prohibition of ***neshekh* for** a loan of **money and** a prohibition of ***ribit* for** a loan of **food. From where** is it derived that there is ***neshekh* with regard to** a loan of **food** as well? The *baraita* answers: A different **verse states:** "You shall not lend with interest [*tashikh*] to your brother: *Neshekh* of money, ***neshekh* of food,** *neshekh* of anything that is lent with interest [*asher yishakh*]" (Deuteronomy 23:20). The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that there is ***ribit* with regard to** a loan of **money? The verse states: "*Neshekh* of money."**

The daf opens in the middle of a *beraita* discussing the *pesukim* of *neshech* and *tarbit*. The *beraita* has established that while the *pesukim* in Vayikra use the word *neshech* specifically for money and *tarbit* for food, the *pasuk* in Devarim applies *neshech* to both. The *beraita* therefore concludes that both *neshech* and *tarbit* apply equally to both food and money. The Gemara had already noted that the two terms are conceptually distinct — *neshech* refers to the bite taken out of the borrower, while *tarbit* denotes the profit accruing to the lender — yet because one invariably accompanies the other, there is no way in practice to transgress one without transgressing the other. Whether the loan involves food or money, a lender who charges *ribis* violates both the prohibition of *neshech* and that of *tarbit*.

### Ein Li Ela Beloveh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בְּלֹוֶה, בְּמַלְוֶה מִנַּיִן? נֶאֱמַר נֶשֶׁךְ בַּלֹּוֶה וְנֶאֱמַר נֶשֶׁךְ בַּמַּלְוֶה. מָה נֶשֶׁךְ הָאָמוּר בַּלֹּוֶה – לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בּוֹ בֵּין בְּכֶסֶף בֵּין בְּאוֹכֶל בֵּין בְּנֶשֶׁךְ בֵּין בְּרִבִּית, אַף נֶשֶׁךְ הָאָמוּר בַּמַּלְוֶה – לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין בְּכֶסֶף בֵּין בְּאוֹכֶל בֵּין בְּנֶשֶׁךְ בֵּין בְּרִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **I have** derived a source for this prohibition **only with regard to a borrower,** for whom it is prohibited to pay interest on a loan. **From where** is it derived that there is also a prohibition stated **with regard to a lender? *Neshekh* is stated with regard to a borrower, and *neshekh* is stated with regard to a lender. Just as** concerning ***neshekh* that is stated with regard to a borrower, you did not distinguish with regard to it between** a loan **of money** and a loan **of food,** or **between** whether the interest is forbidden **as *neshekh*** or **as *ribit*, so too,** concerning ***neshekh* stated with regard to a lender, do not distinguish with regard to it between** a loan **of money** and a loan **of food,** or **between** whether the interest is forbidden **as *neshekh*** or **as *ribit*.** 

Continuing from there, the *beraita* makes a statement that may initially seem strange: *ein li ela beloveh* — I have derived the prohibition only with respect to the borrower. This is surprising, since it is the lender who appears to be the primary transgressor. Yet as will become clear, the prohibition applies to the lender, the borrower, and under certain circumstances even the witnesses and the guarantor — all of whom become implicated in the enterprise. The Gemara understands that the word *tashich* in the *pasuk* — the causative form — refers to allowing oneself to be bitten, which the *beraita* reads as pointing to the borrower rather than the lender.

Thus, when the *pasuk* in Devarim says *lo tashich le'achicha neshech kesef neshech ochel*, and we derive from it that *neshech* applies equally to food and to money, and that *marbit* similarly applies to both — all of this is learned from a *pasuk* that refers to the borrower. The question then becomes: *bamalveh minayin* — how do we know the same applies to the lender?

The *beraita* answers by invoking a *gezeirah shavah*: the word *neshech* appears both in the context of the borrower and in the context of the lender. Just as we have derived, regarding the borrower, that *neshech* and *tarbit* apply equally to food and to money, so too by the lender — namely the *pasuk* in Vayikra, *et kaspcha lo titein lo beneshech* — we do not distinguish between money and food, nor between *neshech* and *tarbit*. Both prohibitions apply in full to the lender as well.

### Kol Davar Lerabot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת כׇּל דָּבָר? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ כׇּל דָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived **to include** in the prohibition interest of **any sort? The verse states: "*Neshekh* of anything that is lent with interest."**

The *beraita* then extends the prohibition further. Food and money are explicitly addressed, but what about other objects — things that are neither food nor money? *Talmud lomar: neshech kol davar asher yishach* — the *pasuk* in Devarim makes explicit that *ribis* applies to any object whatsoever. The prohibition against lending with interest is not limited to the typical cases of money or grain; it encompasses any transaction involving any item.

### Ravina's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: לָא נֶשֶׁךְ בְּאוֹכֶל וְלָא רִבִּית בְּכֶסֶף צְרִיכִי קְרָא, דְּאִי כְּתִיב ״אֶת כַּסְפְּךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לוֹ בְּנֶשֶׁךְ וְאׇכְלְךָ בְּמַרְבִּית״ – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ. הַשְׁתָּא דִּכְתִיב ״אֶת כַּסְפְּךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לוֹ בְּנֶשֶׁךְ וּבְמַרְבִּית לֹא תִתֵּן אׇכְלֶךָ״, קְרִי בֵּיהּ הָכִי: ״אֶת כַּסְפְּךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לוֹ בְּנֶשֶׁךְ וּבְמַרְבִּית״, ״וּבְנֶשֶׁךְ וּבְמַרְבִּית לֹא תִתֵּן אׇכְלֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** An explicit **verse is** not **required, neither** to derive ***neshekh* of food nor** to derive ***ribit* of money. As, if it** were **written: You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and your food with *marbit*,** juxtaposing *neshekh* with money alone and *marbit* with food alone, it would be **as you say,** that there is a need for the derivation of the *baraita*. But **now that it is written: "You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and with *marbit* you shall not give him your food,"** interposing both *neshekh* and *marbit* between money and food, **read into** the verse **this** interpretation: **Your money you shall not give him for *neshekh* and for *marbit*; and for *neshekh* and for *marbit* you shall not give your food.**

Ravina raises an alternative approach. He contends that one did not need the *pesukim* in Devarim at all in order to learn that *neshech* and *marbit* apply equally to both food and money. The earlier analysis had assumed that the *pesukim* in Vayikra initially distinguished the two — *neshech* for money and *marbit* for food — and required Devarim to correct that impression. Ravina argues that had the Torah written *et kaspcha lo titein lo beneshech uve'ochlecha bemarbit*, one might indeed have read the two terms as categorically divided. But the *pasuk* as actually written places *neshech* and *marbit* in direct juxtaposition: *et kaspcha lo titein lo beneshech uv'marbit lo titein ochlecha*. This allows for a reading in which the break falls after *b'marbit* — "do not give your money with *neshech* and *marbit*" — and simultaneously for a reading in which *b'neshech uv'marbit lo titein ochlecha* — "with *neshech* and *marbit* do not give your food." Through the juxtaposition of the two terms, the *pasuk* in Vayikra already teaches that both apply equally to food and to money, without needing to invoke Devarim at all.

### Why Gezeirah Shavah Need…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנָּא ״נֶאֱמַר״ ״נֶאֱמַר״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** **But isn't** the ***tanna*** of the *baraita* **saying** that *neshekh* of food and *tarbit* of money are derived by means of a verbal analogy: *Neshekh* **is stated** with regard to a borrower, and *neshekh* **is** also **stated** with regard to a lender?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר קְרָא, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה. עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר קְרָא, גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לָא צְרִיךְ. אֶלָּא גְּזֵירָה שָׁוָה לְמָה לִי? לְ״נֶשֶׁךְ כׇּל דָּבָר אֲשֶׁר יִשָּׁךְ״, דְּלָא כְּתִב בְּמַלְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **This** is what Ravina **is saying: Had** the **verse not stated** the terms *neshekh* and *tarbit* interposed between money and food, **I would have said** that the *halakha* that both terms apply to both money and food would be derived by means of **a verbal analogy. Now, as** the **verse is stated** in that manner, **a verbal analogy is not necessary.** The Gemara asks: **Rather, why do I** need this **verbal analogy?** The Gemara explains: I need the verbal analogy **to** teach that **"*neshekh* of anything that is lent for interest"** (Deuteronomy 23:20), **which is** written with regard to a borrower but **not written with regard to a lender,** applies to a lender as well.

The Gemara raises an objection: does the *tanna* of the *beraita* not explicitly invoke the *gezeirah shavah* of *ne'emar neshech beloveh vene'emar neshech bemalveh*? How can Ravina simply set that aside?

The Gemara resolves this by clarifying what Ravina is actually saying. He does not claim that Devarim is irrelevant for the borrower's side — the *pasuk lo tashich* in Devarim, which addresses the borrower, may still require the derivation. What Ravina is saying is narrower: one need not carry the teaching from Devarim back to Vayikra via the *gezeirah shavah* in order to establish that *neshech* and *marbit* each apply to both food and money as far as the lender is concerned. That much can already be learned directly from the juxtaposition of *neshech* and *marbit* in Vayikra itself.

The Gemara then reads the *tanna*'s formulation accordingly: *ilu lo ne'emar kra* — had the *pasuk* in Vayikra not juxtaposed the two terms in a way that allows the reading described above, one would have needed to derive the equality of food and money for the lender from Devarim through the *gezeirah shavah*. But since the *pasuk* in Vayikra does contain that juxtaposition, the *gezeirah shavah* is not needed for that purpose. The *gezeirah shavah*, however, is still needed — and this is the Gemara's final word — for one specific extension: *leneshech kol davar asher yishach d'lo ktiv b'marbit*. The principle that *ribis* applies to any object at all, beyond food and money, appears in Devarim in relation to the borrower. The *gezeirah shavah* carries that extension over to the lender as well, a point that cannot be derived from Vayikra alone.

## Three Prohibitions

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּרִבִּית, לָאו בְּגָזֵל, לָאו בְּאוֹנָאָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Why do I** need it to be **that the Merciful One writes a prohibition with regard to interest, a prohibition with regard to robbery** (see Leviticus 19:13), and **a prohibition with regard to exploitation** (see Leviticus 25:14), a transaction where one of the parties overcharged or underpaid? There appears to be one principle underlying all three prohibitions: One must not take possession of another's money in illegitimate ways.

Rava opens a wide-ranging discussion about the structure of the Torah's prohibitions against ill-gotten gain. He asks: *lama li dechtav rachmana lav b'ribis, lav b'gezel, lav b'ona'ah* — why did the Torah need to state each of these three prohibitions separately? Why could one not have been inferred from the others? This is both a technical question about the redundancy of *pesukim* and an important conceptual inquiry into how *ribis*, *ona'ah*, and *gezel* relate to one another.

### Ribis ≠ Gezel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּרִבִּית – מִשּׁוּם דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּלֹוֶה אָסְרָה רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** They **are necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** the **prohibition** only **with regard to interest,** one could not have derived the other prohibitions from it **because it is** a prohibition with **a novel element** that does not appear in other *halakhot*. This novel element is **that the Merciful One prohibited** a loan with interest **even for the borrower.**

Had the Torah written only the prohibition against *ribis*, one would have concluded nothing general about stealing, because *ribis* is clearly a *chiddush* — a novel category. Its most distinctive feature is that it prohibits even the borrower. No one would steal something and also be told that the victim of the theft has transgressed. *Ribis* involves a fully consensual, contractual arrangement, and its prohibition signals something other than the ordinary category of taking what belongs to another. It is accordingly significant that *ribis* appears not in *Choshen Mishpat* — the domain of civil and monetary law — but in *Yoreh De'ah*, the section governing religious prohibitions. This placement reflects its character as a prohibition in its own distinct category, from which one cannot infer the general prohibition of *gezel*.

### Ona'ah ≠ Gezel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּגָזֵל – מִשּׁוּם דִּבְעַל כֻּרְחֵיהּ, אֲבָל אוֹנָאָה – אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had the Merciful One written** the **prohibition** only **with regard to robbery,** one could not have derived the other prohibitions from it, as perhaps robbery is prohibited only **due to** the fact **that** it is an action taken **against the will** of the victim. **But** in the cases of **exploitation** and interest, where there is an element of consent, one would **say** they are **not** prohibited.

Had the Torah written only the prohibition against *gezel*, one would have understood why — it is taking something from someone against their will, *baal korchei*. But from that prohibition alone one could not have derived the prohibition of *ona'ah*. In a case of overcharging, the customer willingly hands over the money. He may be uninformed about the going market rate, and there may be a degree of deception involved, but there is no forcible taking. One might therefore have reasoned that since the person consented to the transaction — even if operating without full information — the prohibition of *gezel* does not apply.

### Common Denominator Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּאוֹנָאָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא יָדַע דְּמָחֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **And had the Merciful One written** the **prohibition** only **with regard to exploitation,** one could not have derived the other prohibitions from it, as perhaps exploitation is prohibited only **due to** the fact **that** the victim **does not know that** he was the victim of exploitation and therefore cannot **waive** repayment.

Rava then explores whether any one of the three prohibitions could have been inferred from the other two taken together as a common denominator. He works through each possibility systematically.

Could *ribis* have been derived from the common denominator of *gezel* and *ona'ah*? No. Both *gezel* and *ona'ah* involve a dimension of the person not fully consenting — in *gezel* the taking is explicitly against his will, and in *ona'ah* he is unaware that anything is being improperly taken from him. In *ribis*, by contrast, the borrower knows the terms precisely and agrees to them. *Tomar b'ribis d'mida'ato* — you cannot extend a rule derived from cases involving unwilling or uninformed victims to a case of full knowledge and explicit consent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא מֵחֲדָא לָא אָתְיָא. תֵּיתֵי חֲדָא מִתַּרְתֵּי. הֵי תֵּיתֵי? לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּרִבִּית, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ. מָה לְהָנָךְ שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁלֹּא מִדַּעַת, תֹּאמַר בְּרִבִּית דְּמִדַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **No one** of these prohibitions can **be derived from one** of the other prohibitions. Perhaps **one** of them can be **derived from** the other **two.** **Which** prohibition will you **derive** from the other two? **Let the Merciful One not write** the **prohibition with regard to interest, and** instead **derive** that prohibition **from these** two, robbery and exploitation. **What** is notable **about these** prohibitions? They are notable **in that** they are transgressed **without** the **consent** of the victim. **Will you say** the same **with regard to interest, which** the borrower gives **with his consent?**

Could *ona'ah* have been derived from the common denominator of *gezel* and *ribis*? Again, no. To learn from *gezel*, one might argue that in *gezel* the person is conscious of the taking and therefore could potentially be *mochel* — could forgive the taking — whereas in *ona'ah* he does not know enough to be *mochel*. One could not use *gezel* to reach *ona'ah* on those grounds. And one could not learn it from *ribis* alone, since *ribis* is a *chiddush*. But what of both together? The Gemara says: *mah hanach sheken ein derech mekach u'memkar b'kach* — both *gezel* and *ribis* involve transactions outside the normal framework of commerce. *Gezel* is not a commercial transaction at all, and *ribis* is an abnormal one that the Torah treats as a unique prohibition. The category of *ona'ah*, which arises precisely within ordinary commerce, could not be derived from those two.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּאוֹנָאָה, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ. מָה לְהָנָךְ, שֶׁכֵּן אֵין דֶּרֶךְ מִקָּח וּמִמְכָּר בְּכָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Let the Merciful One not write** the **prohibition with regard to exploitation, and** instead **derive** that prohibition **from these** two, robbery and interest. **What** is notable **about these** prohibitions? They are notable **in that** they are **not** transgressed **in the** typical **manner of buying and selling.**

Could *gezel* have been derived from the common denominator of *ribis* and *ona'ah*? The Gemara considers this. One tries: *mah l'ribis sheken chiddush* — *ribis* is too anomalous to serve as a source. Then *ona'ah tochiach* — let *ona'ah* demonstrate that ill-gotten gain in a more ordinary context is prohibited. But then: *mah l'ona'ah sheken lo yada d'machil* — in *ona'ah* the person does not know enough to forgive, whereas in *gezel* he is fully aware and might have been *mochel*. Then *ribis tochiach* — in *ribis* the person certainly knows the terms and cannot claim ignorance, yet the prohibition holds. The argument cycles: *v'chazar hadin, lo rei zeh k'rei zeh* — no single case has all the features of the others, and the back-and-forth between the three yields no definitive derivation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָא לִכְתּוֹב רַחֲמָנָא לָאו בְּגָזֵל, וְתֵיתֵי מֵהָנָךְ. דְּמַאי פָּרְכַתְּ: מָה לְרִבִּית שֶׁכֵּן חִידּוּשׁ? אוֹנָאָה תּוֹכִיחַ! מָה לְאוֹנָאָה שֶׁכֵּן לָא יָדַע וּמָחֵיל? רִבִּית תּוֹכִיחַ! וְחָזַר הַדִּין: לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה. הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן, שֶׁכֵּן גּוֹזְלוֹ. אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא גָּזֵל!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, let the Merciful One not write** the prohibition **with regard to robbery,** and instead **derive** that prohibition **from these** two, interest and exploitation. **As what refutation** will **you** offer? If you say: **What** is notable **about interest?** It is notable **in that** the prohibition of interest contains **a novel element;** the case of **exploitation will prove** that a novel element is not a factor, as the prohibition against exploitation contains no novel element. If you say, **what** is notable **about exploitation?** It is notable **in that** in this case, the victim **does not know** that he was the victim of exploitation and therefore he cannot **waive** repayment; the case of **interest will prove** that the inability to waive repayment is not a factor, as the borrower is aware of the interest and able to waive repayment. **And the inference has reverted** to its starting point. **The aspect of this** case, interest, **is not like the aspect of that** case, exploitation, **and the aspect of that** case, exploitation, **is not like the aspect of this** case, interest. **Their common denominator is that one robs another** of money, i.e., takes money from another that is not due to him. **I will also bring** the prohibition against **robbery,** which shares that common denominator, and derive it from the other two prohibitions.

### Ill-Gotten Gain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא, לָאו בְּגָזֵל לְמָה לִי? לְכוֹבֵשׁ שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **said: Indeed,** the prohibition against robbery is superfluous. **But** if the prohibition against robbery can be derived from the prohibitions against interest and exploitation, **why do I** need the **prohibition** written in the Torah **with regard to robbery?** That verse is not written to prohibit a standard case of robbery; rather, it serves **to** prohibit the action of one who **withholds the wages of a hired laborer.** In that case, unlike robbery, the employer does not take money from the laborer; he merely fails to pay him his wages.

*Hatzad hashaveh shebahen sheken gozelo* — the common denominator of all three is nonetheless that in each case money is taken that one is not rightfully entitled to. From that shared feature, one could in principle have derived *gezel* as well. This of course raises a difficulty: calling *ribis* a form of *gezel* is genuinely questionable, since the borrower consented, and as will be seen shortly, there are serious authorities who hold there is no obligation to return *ribis* precisely because it is not *gezel*. Yet Rava's point is that the *hatzad hashaveh* argument, at least formally, would have been available — which then raises the question of why an explicit *pasuk* prohibiting *gezel* was necessary.

### Gezel: Why Explicit

If one could have derived *gezel* from the common denominator of *ribis* and *ona'ah*, why did the Torah state the prohibition of *gezel* explicitly? The answer: *l'kovesh schar sachir* — to teach the case of withholding a worker's wages. The case of *kovesh sechar sachir* differs structurally from ordinary theft: the money never moves from the employer's possession. It sits in his pocket, and he simply fails to pay it out. Had the prohibition of *gezel* been left to derivation from *ribis* and *ona'ah*, one might have thought it applied only to active taking; the explicit *pasuk* is needed to include this passive form of withholding.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כּוֹבֵשׁ שְׂכַר שָׂכִיר, בְּהֶדְיָא כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״לֹא תַעֲשֹׁק שָׂכִיר עָנִי וְאֶבְיוֹן״! לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to** one who **withholds the wages of a hired laborer, it is explicitly written: "You shall not oppress a hired laborer who is poor and destitute"** (Deuteronomy 24:14). There is no need to derive this prohibition from the verse concerning robbery. **It is written** so that withholding the wages of a hired laborer always involves **violating two prohibitions.**

The Gemara objects: but the prohibition *lo ta'ashok sachir ani v'evyon* is already stated explicitly — does that not cover *kovesh sechar sachir* on its own? The answer given is that the explicit prohibition of *gezel* in the same context serves to make the violator transgress two *lavin* simultaneously — both *lo ta'ashok* and *lo tigzol* — when he withholds his worker's wages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״לֹא תִּגְנֹבוּ״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? לְכִדְתַנְיָא: לֹא תִּגְנֹב – עַל מְנָת לְמֵיקַט, לֹא תִּגְנֹב – עַל מְנָת לְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **Why do I** need the prohibition: **"You shall not steal"** (Leviticus 19:11), **that the Merciful One wrote?** This is yet another prohibition against taking money by illegitimate means, and it could be derived from the other prohibitions mentioned previously. **For that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **"You shall not steal"** applies in all circumstances, even if you do so only **in order to aggravate** the victim; **"you shall not steal"** applies in all circumstances, even if you do so **in order to pay the double payment** as a gift to the person from whom you stole.

The Gemara then asks about the prohibition *lo tignovu* — the prohibition against burglary, as opposed to *lo tignov* in the *Aseres Hadibros*, which is understood to refer to kidnapping. Could not *lo tignovu* also have been derived from the *tzad hashaveh*? A *beraita* answers: *lo tignov al menas lemeikat, lo tignov al menas leshalem tashlumei kefel* — do not steal even to annoy someone, and do not steal even with the intention that the person will ultimately benefit by receiving *kefel*, double payment. The explicit prohibition of *lo tignovu* is necessary to prohibit burglary even in cases where the thief has no personal gain and the victim may ultimately receive more than he lost — situations that could not have been derived from a *hatzad hashaveh* rooted in the taking of ill-gotten gain.

### False Measures

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יֵימַר לְרַב אָשֵׁי: לָאו דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא בְּמִשְׁקָלוֹת, לְמָה לִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְטוֹמֵן מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו בְּמֶלַח. הַיְינוּ גָּזֵל מְעַלְּיָא הוּא! לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו מִשְּׁעַת עֲשִׂיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yeimar said to Rav Ashi: Why do I** need the **prohibition that the Merciful One wrote with regard to weights:** "You shall do no unrighteousness in judgment, in measure, in weight, or in volume" (Leviticus 19:35)? It is merely another form of robbery. Rav Ashi **said to him:** It is referring **to** a seller who **buries his weights in salt,** in order to lighten them. Rav Yeimar said: **That is** the same as **full-fledged robbery;** therefore, it should not require a separate derivation. Rav Ashi answered: It is written **to** establish that he **violates** the prohibition **from the moment of the act** of burying them. He violates the prohibition even before he actually deceives a buyer with the buried weights.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״לֹא תַעֲשׂוּ עָוֶל בַּמִּשְׁפָּט בַּמִּדָּה בַּמִּשְׁקָל וּבַמְּשׂוּרָה״. ״בַּמִּדָּה״ – זוֹ מְדִידַת קַרְקַע, שֶׁלֹּא יִמְדּוֹד לְאֶחָד בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וּלְאֶחָד בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. ״בַּמִּשְׁקָל״ – שֶׁלֹּא יַטְמִין מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו בְּמֶלַח. ״וּבַמְּשׂוּרָה״ – שֶׁלֹּא יַרְתִּיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: The verse states: "You shall do no unrighteousness in judgment, in measure, in weight, or in volume [*uvamesura*]"** (Leviticus 19:35). **"In measure"; this** is referring to the **measurement of land,** e.g., this means **that** in a case where two people are dividing their jointly owned field, **one may not measure** the land to be given **to one during the summer and** measure the land to be given **to the other during the rainy season,** because the length of the measuring cord is affected by the weather conditions. **"In weight"; this** is referring **to** the fact that **he may not bury his measuring weights in salt. And "in volume";** this teaches **that one may not froth** the liquid one is selling, creating the impression that there is more liquid in the vessel than there actually is.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲלֹא דְּבָרִים קַל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מְשׂוּרָה שֶׁהִיא אֶחָד מִשְּׁלֹשִׁים וּשְׁלֹשָׁה בַּלּוֹג הִקְפִּידָה עָלָיו תּוֹרָה – קַל וְחוֹמֶר לְהִין, וַחֲצִי הִין, וּשְׁלִישִׁית הִין, וּרְבִיעִית הַהִין, וְלוֹג, וַחֲצִי לוֹג, וּרְבִיעִית הַלּוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And are** the following **matters not** inferred ***a fortiori*: And if** with regard to the ***mesura*** volume, which equals **one thirty**-**third of a *log*,** the **Torah was fastidious concerning it** that one may not deceive another, it can be inferred ***a fortiori*** that with regard **to a *hin*,** which equals twelve *log*, **and a half-*hin*, and a third-*hin*, and a quarter-*hin*, and a *log*, and a half-*log*, and a quarter-*log*,** which are all much larger volumes, that one may not deceive another.

Rav Yeimar raises a further question to Rav Ashi: *lav dechtav rachmana bemishkalos lama li* — why did the Torah need to state the prohibition against false weights? Is this not straightforwardly a form of theft, and if anything a *kal vachomer* from *ona'ah*? After all, *ona'ah* involves merely charging above the market rate without explicit deception, whereas using false weights involves an outright misrepresentation of quantity — a clearer case than *ona'ah* itself.

Rav Ashi responds: *letamin mishkelosav bamelach* — to address the case of someone who stores his weights in salt. If the weights have been kept in salt in a way that alters their mass, using them falsely is already prohibited. The Gemara observes that the problem stated this way — *hainu gezel mealya hu* — is simply ordinary theft. The answer is: *laavor alav mishaas asiya* — the explicit *pasuk* establishes that the transgression begins from the moment of preparation, from the act of storing the weights in salt with the intent to use them falsely, before any actual transaction occurs. The *pasuk lo yihyeh v'chisecha even vaeven* prohibits even possessing false weights, and performing an act that renders them false is already a violation.

A *beraita* then elaborates on the various categories of prohibited false measurement. *Lo sa'asu avel bamishpat bamidah bamishkal uvameshurah* — do not do injustice in judgment, in linear measure, in weight, and in liquid measure. *Bamidah* refers to measuring land: one must not measure a plot for one person in the summer and another person's plot in the winter, since a rope used for measurement stretches differently under different climatic conditions and will produce unequal results even when the intent is honest. The *pasuk* could be read as addressed to a *beis din* dividing an estate between heirs, but it also carries a broader message — that commercial dealings in produce or goods are themselves acts of justice, and to falsify them is to pervert justice.

*Bemishkal* restates the prohibition against keeping one's weights in salt. *Uvimsurah* — the liquid measure — means one may not pour wine while it is still foaming and bubbling; one must allow the liquid to settle before considering the measure complete. The *beraita* adds a remarkable observation by way of *kal vachomer*: a *mesurah* is one thirty-sixth of a *log* — roughly a third of an ounce — and yet the Torah was strict even about that smallest unit. How much more so, then, does the prohibition apply to any of the larger standard measures: a *hin*, half a *hin*, a third of a *hin*, a quarter of a *hin*, a *log*, half a *log*, and a quarter of a *log*. Even the smallest discrepancy is a perversion of justice.

## Yetzias Mitzrayim

### Divine Discernment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: לְמָה לִי דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם בְּרִבִּית, יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם גַּבֵּי צִיצִית, יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם בְּמִשְׁקָלוֹת? אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אֲנִי הוּא שֶׁהִבְחַנְתִּי בְּמִצְרַיִם בֵּין טִפָּה שֶׁל בְּכוֹר לְטִפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל בְּכוֹר, אֲנִי הוּא שֶׁעָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁתּוֹלֶה מְעוֹתָיו בְּגוֹי וּמַלְוָה אוֹתָם לְיִשְׂרָאֵל בְּרִבִּית, וּמִמִּי שֶׁטּוֹמֵן מִשְׁקְלוֹתָיו בְּמֶלַח, וּמִמִּי שֶׁתּוֹלֶה קָלָא אִילָן בְּבִגְדוֹ וְאוֹמֵר: ״תְּכֵלֶת הוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Why do I** need **the** mention of the **exodus from Egypt that the Merciful One wrote in** the context of the *halakhot* of the prohibition against **interest** (see Leviticus 25:37–38), and the mention of **the exodus from Egypt with regard to** the mitzva to wear **ritual fringes** (see Numbers 15:39–41), and the mention of **the exodus from Egypt in** the context of the prohibition concerning **weights** (see Leviticus 19:35–36)? **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I am He Who distinguished in Egypt between** the **drop** of seed **that** became **a firstborn and** the **drop** of seed **that** did **not** become **a firstborn,** and I killed only the firstborn. **I am** also **He Who is destined to exact punishment from one who attributes** ownership of **his money to a gentile and** thereby **lends it to a Jew with interest.** Even if he is successful in deceiving the court, God knows the truth. **And** I am also He Who is destined to exact punishment **from one who buries his weights in salt,** as this changes their weight in a manner not visible to the eye. **And** I am also He Who is destined to exact punishment **from one who hangs** ritual fringes dyed with **indigo [*kala ilan*]** dye **on his garment and says it is** dyed with the **sky-blue dye** required in ritual fringes. The allusion to God's ability to distinguish between two apparently like entities is why the exodus is mentioned in all of these contexts.

Rava asks why the Torah invoked *yetzias Mitzrayim* in three seemingly disparate contexts: alongside the prohibition of *ribis*, alongside the commandment of *tzitzis*, and alongside the prohibition of false weights. What is the unifying message?

Rava answers: God says, I am the One who distinguished in Egypt between the drop of semen that produced a firstborn and the drop that did not. That act of discernment — invisible to any human observer, distinguishing between indistinguishable biological realities — is the paradigm for divine surveillance. Just as God perceived what no human eye could see in Egypt, He perceives what no human eye can see in commerce and religious observance.

### Hidden Fraud Cases

The invocation of *yetzias Mitzrayim*, then, carries a specific message in each of its three contexts. In the context of *ribis*: God will call to account one who routes his loan through a non-Jew as a nominal intermediary — *shetoleh ma'otav b'nochri* — in order to lend to a Jew with interest while maintaining the fiction that it is the non-Jew's money. In the context of false weights: God will call to account one who keeps his weights in salt, since no one else can see the alteration. And in the context of *tzitzis*: God will call to account one who uses *kala ilan* — a plant-based dye that closely resembles *tekhelet* in color but is far cheaper — and falsely claims his *tzitzis* are dyed with genuine *tekhelet*. In all three cases, the fraud is hidden from human detection. The reminder of *yetzias Mitzrayim* carries the message: I see what you are doing.

### Rav Chanina Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אִיקְּלַע לְסוּרָא דִפְרָת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא דִּפְרָת לְרָבִינָא: יְצִיאַת מִצְרַיִם דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא גַּבֵּי שְׁרָצִים, לְמָה לִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, אֲנִי הוּא שֶׁהִבְחַנְתִּי בֵּין טִפָּה שֶׁל בְּכוֹר לְטִפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ שֶׁל בְּכוֹר, אֲנִי עָתִיד לִיפָּרַע מִמִּי שֶׁמְּעָרֵב קִרְבֵי דָגִים טְמֵאִין בְּקִרְבֵי דָגִים טְהוֹרִין וּמוֹכְרָן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina happened** to come **to Sura on the Euphrates. Rav Ḥanina of Sura on the Euphrates said to Ravina: Why do I** need **the** mention of the **exodus from Egypt that the Merciful One wrote in** the context of **creeping animals:** "Do not make yourselves detestable with all the creeping animals that swarm…for I am the Lord Who brings you up from the land of Egypt" (Leviticus 11:43–45)? Ravina **said to him: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I am He Who distinguished in Egypt between** the **drop** of seed **that** became **a firstborn and** the **drop** of seed **that** did **not** become **a firstborn,** and I killed only the firstborn. **I am** also **He Who is destined to exact punishment from one who intermingles the innards of non-kosher fish with the innards of kosher fish and sells them to a Jew,** who is unable to distinguish between them.

Ravina visited Sura on the Euphrates, where Rav Chanina of Sura asked him a related but distinct question. The Torah, at the end of *Parshat Shmini*, writes *ki ani Hashem hama'aleh etchem me'eretz Mitzrayim* in the context of *sheratzim*. Why invoke *yetzias Mitzrayim* there? Rav Chanina notes that he could have answered the general question — why mention *yetzias Mitzrayim* — along the same lines as Rava's answer about divine discernment: just as God could distinguish which drop of semen produced a firstborn, He can distinguish one who mixes the innards of non-kosher fish with the innards of kosher fish and sells them to Jews as if they were all kosher. Since the fish itself can be identified by its scales and fins, but the innards cannot be visually verified, this hidden fraud parallels the hidden frauds in the other contexts. But Rav Chanina says his question is more specific: *ana hama'aleh kashya li* — what bothers me is the particular word *hama'aleh*, "the One who raised you up." Elsewhere the verse simply says God took Israel out of Egypt; here it uses the term of elevation. Why?

### Hama'aleh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: אִילְמָלֵא (לֹא) הֶעֱלֵיתִי אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל מִמִּצְרָיִם אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל דָּבָר זֶה, שֶׁאֵין מְטַמְּאִין בִּשְׁרָצִים – דַּיִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Ravina **said to him:** It is **to** teach **as it was taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael. As it was taught in the school of Rabbi Yishmael: The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Had I brought the Jewish people up from Egypt only for this matter,** so **that they** would **not become impure** by consuming **creeping animals, it would be sufficient for Me,** as observance of this mitzva elevates their spiritual stature.

Ravina answers by citing the school of Rabbi Yishmael: *amar Hakadosh Baruch Hu ilmalei lo he'elisi es Yisrael miMitzrayim ela bishvil davar zeh she'ein mitam'in basheratzim dai* — God says, had I redeemed Israel from Egypt for no other reason than this one thing alone, that they should not defile themselves with *sheratzim*, that would have been sufficient. The word *hama'aleh* carries the sense of *aliyah*, elevation. Rashi explains: *she'me'ulin v'na'in* — they are elevated and refined. The observance of these laws does not merely mark the people as obedient; it actually lifts them up.

### Elevation Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וּמִי נְפִישׁ אַגְרַיְיהוּ טְפֵי מֵרִבִּית וּמִצִּיצִית וּמִמִּשְׁקָלוֹת? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא נְפִישׁ אַגְרַיְיהוּ טְפֵי, מְאִיסִי לְמֵכְלִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥanina **said to him: And is the reward for** abstaining from consuming creeping animals **greater than** the reward for observing the *halakhot* with regard to **interest and ritual fringes and weights?** Let the Merciful One write: Who brings you up, in the context of those mitzvot as well. Ravina **said to him: Even though their reward is not greater,** it is more **repulsive** for Jews **to eat** creeping animals. Avoiding those animals brings them up, in the sense that it is praiseworthy and enhances the transcendent nature of the Jews.

Rav Chanina then presses further: but is the reward for refraining from *sheratzim* so much greater than the reward for observing *ribis*, *tzitzis*, and correct weights? In those contexts the Torah did not use the language of elevation.

Ravina responds: *af al gav d'lo nafish agreih t'fei, me'isa l'michlinhu* — even if the reward is not necessarily greater, it is still disgusting to eat them. The point is not about the comparative weight of the commandment or its reward, but about what observing it does to the person. Refraining from *sheratzim* refines one in an aesthetic and physical sense; it does something to the body and to one's sensibility that constitutes a form of elevation even independent of its reward. That is what the word *hama'aleh* is teaching — a particular dimension of the commandment's effect on the person who observes it.

## Ketsutzah vs. Avak

### Ribis Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ תַּרְבִּית, הַמַּרְבֶּה בְּפֵירוֹת. כֵּיצַד? לָקַח הֵימֶנּוּ חִטִּים בְּדִינַר זָהָב וְכוּ׳. אַטּוּ כֹּל הָנֵי דְּאָמְרִינַן עַד הַשְׁתָּא, לָאו רִבִּית הוּא? אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: עַד כָּאן שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ שֶׁל דִּבְרֵיהֶם. וְכֵן אָמַר רָבָא: עַד כָּאן שֶׁל תּוֹרָה, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ שֶׁל דִּבְרֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And which is *tarbit*?** It is the case of **one who** enters into a transaction that yields **an increase in the produce** beyond his investment. **How so?** For example, a case where **one acquired wheat from** another at the price of one *kor* of wheat **for** one **gold dinar,** with the wheat to be supplied at a later date, and such was the market price of wheat at the time he acquired it. The price of one *kor* of wheat then increased and stood at thirty dinars. At that point, the buyer said to the seller: Give me all of my wheat now, as I wish to sell it and purchase wine with it. The seller said to him: Since it is ultimately wine that you want, not wheat, each kor of your wheat is considered by me to be worth thirty dinars, and you have the right to collect its value in wine from me. And in this case, the seller did not have wine in his possession. **Is that to say** that **all these** cases **that we said** in the mishna **until now are not** cases of **interest? Rabbi Abbahu says: Until here,** i.e., in the first two cases it presents, the mishna is referring to cases of interest **by Torah law,** and **from this** point **forward** the mishna is referring to cases of interest **by rabbinic law.** If the lender does not explicitly stipulate that the debtor must pay a sum greater than the value of the loan they do not violate the Torah prohibition of interest, but the Sages prohibited doing so. **And so says Rava: Until here** the mishna is referring to cases of interest **by Torah law,** and **from this** point **forward** the mishna is referring to cases of interest **by rabbinic law.**

The Gemara returns to the *mishnah* and the *beraita* to clarify the distinction between *ribis* that falls under Torah law and *ribis* that is rabbinic. Rabbi Abbahu states explicitly: *ad kan shel Torah mikan v'eilach shel divreihem* — up to this point in the *mishnah* we are dealing with Torah-level *ribis*; from this point forward, with rabbinic. Rava echoes the same formulation. The *mishnah* uses *tarbit* and *marbit* to describe the rabbinic category, though as the Gemara has noted, those terms are also used in the *pesukim* for Torah-level *ribis*. What matters is the structural division: explicit, contracted *ribis* — *ribis ketzutza* — is Torah-level, while the gray-area rabbinic extensions constitute *avak ribis*, the dust of *ribis*.

### Father Collects, Son Inh…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן – ״יָכִין רָשָׁע וְיִלְבַּשׁ צַדִּיק״. עַד כָּאן, וְתוּ לָא? אֶלָּא, אֲפִילּוּ עַד כָּאן – ״יָכִין רָשָׁע וְיִלְבַּשׁ צַדִּיק״!
>
> **Translation:** **Until here** there is a fulfillment of the verse: **The wicked may prepare and the righteous shall don** (see Job 27:17), which is interpreted as referring to the case of a wicked father who collects interest from borrowers. Upon inheriting the father's estate, his righteous son is not obligated to return the interest to the borrower. **Is this verse applicable only in the cases discussed** until here, **and no further?** It is logical that if the heir does not need to return the interest that was prohibited by Torah law, all the more so the heir should not need to return the interest that was prohibited by rabbinic law. **Rather,** Rava said: **Even** in the cases discussed **until here** one can apply the verse: **The wicked may prepare and the righteous shall don.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד כָּאן – רִבִּית קְצוּצָה. מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – אֲבַק רִבִּית.
>
> **Translation:** **Until here** the *tanna* cites cases of **fixed interest,** i.e., where the amount to be paid as interest was fixed at the time of the loan, which is prohibited by Torah law. **From this** point **forward** the *tanna* cites cases with only **a hint of interest,** prohibited by rabbinic law, as there is no fixed sum paid as interest.

Rava then cites the verse *yachin rasha v'yilbash tzadik* — the wicked one prepares and the righteous one wears. In the context of *ribis*, the *rasha* is the lender who collected *ribis*; he will not ultimately benefit from it. Yet his children — who inherit what he gathered — are not obligated to return it. This stands in stark contrast to ordinary *gezel*, where stolen property must be returned regardless of whose hands it passes into. The verse thus implies that even *ribis ketzutza*, Torah-level *ribis*, is not fully equivalent to *gezel*: the father who collected it must give it back, but the son who inherits it does not.

The Gemara then extends this further. *Ad kan v'tulo* — is it only here, meaning only *ribis ketzutza*, that we apply this principle? Certainly by *avak ribis*, rabbinic *ribis*, there should be even less obligation to return. The Gemara clarifies: *afilu ad kan yachin rasha v'yilbash tzadik* — even when we are in the territory of *ribis ketzutza*, the son does not have to return what the father collected. And *ad kan ribis ketzutza, mikan v'eilach avak ribis* — the division in the *mishnah* separates Torah-level *ribis* from rabbinic *ribis*, and in the rabbinic category, even the person who took it need not give it back.

This already reveals how dramatically *ribis* differs from *gezel*. Even in the strongest case — Torah-level, explicit, contracted *ribis* — the father must return it, but not the son. In the rabbinic category, even the original taker has no obligation to return. The practical consequence is that the deterrent effect is considerably reduced when there is no restitution requirement, and it becomes clear throughout the *perek* that the Gemara simply takes it for granted that there are those who do in practice charge *ribis*, violating at minimum the rabbinic prohibition.

### Eliezer: Court Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: רִבִּית קְצוּצָה יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין, אֲבַק רִבִּית אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ רִבִּית קְצוּצָה נָמֵי אֵינָהּ יוֹצְאָה בְּדַיָּינִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Fixed interest** can be **removed** from the lender's possession **by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges.** By contrast, in cases of **a hint of interest,** prohibited by rabbinic law, the money paid **cannot be removed by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even fixed interest cannot be removed by** means of legal proceedings adjudicated by **judges.**

Rabbi Eliezer states: *ribis ketzutza yotza b'dayanim* — Torah-level *ribis* is recoverable through the courts. If the lender refuses to return it, the *beis din* can compel him. *Avak ribis*, however, *eina yotza b'dayanim* — does not leave through the courts; even the person who took it has no court-enforceable obligation to return it.

### Yochanan: Not Gezel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״בַּנֶּשֶׁךְ נָתַן וְתַרְבִּית לָקַח וָחָי לֹא יִחְיֶה אֵת כׇּל הַתּוֹעֵבוֹת הָאֵלֶּה עָשָׂה״ – לְמִיתָה נִיתַּן וְלֹא לְהִישָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan?** It is **as the verse states: "He has given forth with *neshekh* and he took *tarbit*, shall he live? He shall not live. He performed all these abominations,** he shall be executed; his blood shall be upon him" (Ezekiel 18:13). It can be inferred that one who takes interest is **subject to death** at the hand of Heaven **but not to repayment,** as the court cannot compel him to repay the interest.

Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *afilu ribis ketzutza nami eina yotza b'dayanim* — even Torah-level *ribis* is not recoverable through the courts. His reasoning: it is not *gezel*. The borrower agreed to the terms, the transaction was consensual, and however serious the transgression may be, it does not carry the civil consequence of having to make restitution through a court.

### Divine Consequence Only

The verse in Yechezkel presents the matter as a capital offense, yet one that is divinely enforced rather than one that produces restitution. The *pasuk* says *b'neshech natan v'tarbit lakach v'chai lo yichye et kol hato'evos ha'eleh asa* — he gave with *neshech* and he took with *tarbit*, and all of these abominations he has done. The *derashah*: *l'mita nitan v'lo l'heshavon* — he has been assigned to death, not to restitution. The lender who charged *ribis* faces divine consequence — a severe one, expressed in language normally associated with capital crimes — but not a civil remedy. The Torah addresses this through divine judgment, not through the machinery of the earthly court.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר, אָמַר קְרָא: ״אֶל תִּקַּח מֵאִתּוֹ נֶשֶׁךְ וְתַרְבִּית וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ״ – לְמוֹרָא נִיתַּן וְלֹא לְהִשָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that **the verse states: "You shall not take from him *neshekh* or *tarbit*; you shall fear your God** and your brother shall live with you." (Leviticus 25:36). It can be inferred that one who does so is **subject to** shirking the **fear** of Heaven, **but not to repayment.**

Rav Adda bar Ahava supports the same conclusion from a different *pasuk*: *al tikach me'ito neshech v'tarbit v'yareisha me'elohecha* — do not take from him *neshech* or *tarbit*, and fear your God. *Lemora nitan v'lo l'heshavon* — this is addressed to the fear of divine consequence, not to earthly restitution.

### Oppression Dynamic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִגּוּפֵיהּ דִּקְרָא שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, ״מוֹת יוּמָת דָּמָיו בּוֹ יִהְיֶה״. הוּקְשׁוּ מַלְוֵי רִבִּית לְשׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים: מָה שׁוֹפְכֵי דָּמִים לֹא נִיתְּנוּ לְהִשָּׁבוֹן – אַף מַלְוֵי רִבִּית לֹא נָתְנוּ לְהִשָּׁבוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It can be derived from the verse** in Ezekiel **itself,** not by inference, as it is written: **"He shall be executed; his blood shall be upon him"** (Ezekiel 18:13). In the verse, **lenders** who charged **interest were juxtaposed with shedders of blood.** This teaches: **Just as shedders of blood are not subject to repayment, so too, lenders** who charge **interest are not subject to repayment.**

Rava derives the same point from the *pasuk* itself in Yechezkel: *mos yumat damav bo* — he will surely die, his blood is upon him. *Huksha malvei ribis l'shofchei damim* — lenders of *ribis* are compared to murderers. Tosafot notes that the phrase *damav bo* appears elsewhere in contexts not involving murder, but the later portion of the *pasuk* in Yechezkel does make the comparison to bloodshed explicit. The *kal vachomer* drawn: *mah shofchei damim lo nitnu l'heshavon, af malvei ribis lo nitnu l'heshavon* — just as murder cannot be restituted, so too *ribis* cannot be restituted.

This comparison illuminates something deeper about the nature of *ribis*. When a murder is committed, no payment can restore the life taken. Similarly, the harm done by *ribis* is not reducible to the monetary sum charged. The Torah objects to *ribis* as an institution — which is why it implicates not only the lender but the borrower, the witnesses, and the guarantor. The dynamic created by *ribis* is one of subjugation: *eved loveh le'ish malveh*, the borrower is as a slave to the lender. That power relationship, once established, cannot be undone by simply returning the money. The ongoing experience of living under that kind of debt — paying not only principal but accumulating interest — creates a reality that no payment can fully restore. This is fundamentally different from *gezel* or *ona'ah*, each of which constitutes a one-time event. *Ribis* creates a systemic dynamic, and it is that dynamic that cannot be restituted.

### Restore Brotherhood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר? דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״אֶל תִּקַּח מֵאִתּוֹ נֶשֶׁךְ וְתַרְבִּית וְיָרֵאתָ מֵאֱלֹהֶיךָ וְחֵי אָחִיךָ עִמָּךְ״. אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנֵיחֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabbi Elazar** that interest prohibited by Torah law can be reclaimed by means of legal proceedings adjudicated by judges? It is **as the verse** concerning the prohibition against taking interest **states: "You shall not take from him *neshekh* or *tarbit*; you shall fear your God and your brother shall live with you"** (Leviticus 25:36), from which it is derived: **Return** the interest **to him so that he may live.**

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak then turns to explain Rabbi Eliezer's position — that *ribis ketzutza* does leave through the courts. The source is the *pasuk* at the end of the passage on *neshech* in Vayikra: *al tikach me'ito neshech v'tarbit v'yareisha me'elohecha v'chai achicha imach* — and your brother shall live with you. Rabbi Eliezer reads this as an active obligation: *ahadar lei ki heichi d'nicheye* — return it to him so that he may live. The *pasuk* does not merely describe the relationship that *ribis* is meant to protect; it issues a command to restore that relationship when it has been violated.

Crucially, however, the obligation falls specifically on the lender who created the dynamic — the one who was directly party to the oppressive relationship. The command is *v'chai achicha imach*, your brother shall live *with you*, suggesting the restoration of a bilateral relationship between these two specific parties. That is precisely why the obligation does not pass to the son: the son was never part of that relationship, never created that dynamic. It is not about the money being ill-gotten in a proprietary sense — it is about a relationship that was perverted and that the perpetrator is commanded to try to restore.

### Chayecha Kodmim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, הַאי ״וְחֵי אָחִיךָ עִמָּךְ״ מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ? מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהָיוּ מְהַלְּכִין בַּדֶּרֶךְ וּבְיַד אֶחָד מֵהֶן קִיתוֹן שֶׁל מַיִם, אִם שׁוֹתִין שְׁנֵיהֶם – מֵתִים. וְאִם שׁוֹתֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶן – מַגִּיעַ לַיִּשּׁוּב. דָּרַשׁ בֶּן פְּטוֹרָא: מוּטָב שֶׁיִּשְׁתּוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם וְיָמוּתוּ, וְאַל יִרְאֶה אֶחָד מֵהֶם בְּמִיתָתוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ. עַד שֶׁבָּא רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְלִימֵּד: ״וְחֵי אָחִיךָ עִמָּךְ״, חַיֶּיךָ קוֹדְמִים לְחַיֵּי חֲבֵירֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Yoḥanan, what does he do with this** verse: **"And your brother shall live with you"?** The Gemara answers: **He requires** the verse **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: If **two** people **were walking on** a desolate **path and** there was **a jug [*kiton*] of water in the possession of one of them,** and the situation was such that **if both drink** from the jug, both will **die,** as there is not enough water, **but if** only **one of them drinks, he will reach a settled area,** there is a dispute as to the *halakha*. **Ben Petora taught:** It is **preferable that both of them drink and die, and let neither one of them see the death of the other.** This was the accepted opinion **until Rabbi Akiva came and taught** that the verse states: **"And your brother shall live with you,"** indicating that **your life takes precedence over the life of the other.**

Rabbi Yochanan, having taken the position that *ribis ketzutza* also does not leave through the courts, must account for the *pasuk* *v'chai achicha imach* differently. He uses it, the Gemara explains, for an entirely different teaching — the famous *beraita* concerning two people walking in the desert, one of whom has a pitcher of water sufficient only for one person to survive.

Ben Petura taught: better that both should drink and both should die, rather than one witness the death of the other. But Rabbi Akiva came and taught *chayecha kodmim l'chayei achicha* — your life takes precedence over the life of your friend, derived from *v'chai achicha imach*: your brother shall live *with you*, but your own life comes first. This is one of the core principles of Jewish ethics regarding the hierarchy of moral obligations, whereby proximity of relationship generates greater responsibility, beginning with oneself.

This *sugyah* closes the daf on what is, at its core, a profound question about the nature of *ribis*: is it a form of *gezel*, or is it something categorically different? The weight of the discussion strongly suggests the latter — *ribis* is not about proprietary theft but about a relational and systemic harm whose full dimensions cannot be undone by restitution. Whether the *pasuk* of *v'chai achicha imach* grounds an obligation to return, as Rabbi Eliezer holds, or is reserved for a teaching about the priority of one's own life, as Rabbi Yochanan holds, the discussion itself stands as testimony to how seriously the Torah treats the institution of lending with interest, and how carefully the *Gemara* works to locate it precisely in the moral and legal landscape.
