# Menachot 101 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Redemption Rule

### Sanctity Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: וַאֲפִילּוּ הֵן טְהוֹרִין נִפְדִּין. מַאי טַעְמָא? כַּמָּה דְּלָא קָדְשִׁי בִּכְלִי – קְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים נִינְהוּ, וּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים נִפְדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel says: Even if they are ritually pure, they** also may be **redeemed. What is the reason** for this? **As long as they have not been consecrated in** a service **vessel,** they possess **sanctity** that inheres only in their **value,** and items whose **sanctity** inheres only in their **value** may be **redeemed.**

The daf begins approximately fifteen lines down, at the word *lemachar*, picking up with the challenge of Rav Huna bar Manoach. To understand that challenge, it is necessary first to recall the position that Shmuel has advanced: if one sanctifies *menachot* and *nesachim* — the flour, oil, and libations that require sanctification in a *kli sharet* before being offered on the *mizbe'ach* — but they have not yet been placed in a *kli sharet*, one may redeem them even though they are *tahor*.

### Kedushat Damim vs. Haguf

The basis of Shmuel's position is a distinction between levels of sanctity. At the stage before placement in a *kli sharet*, these items possess only *kedushat damim* — their monetary value is sacred — but they have not yet attained *kedushat haguf*, the sanctity of the object itself. Shmuel holds that at the *kedushat damim* stage, redemption is available even without *tumah*. The *mishna*'s reference to *tumah* as a trigger for redemption, in his reading, applies only to items already in a *kli sharet*: once they have been sanctified in a vessel and attained *kedushat haguf*, they can no longer be redeemed even if they become *tamei*.

### Animals vs. Mum

This distinguishes such items from animal sacrifices. An animal possesses *kedushat haguf* and yet may be redeemed if it develops a *mum* — a disqualifying blemish. However, that avenue is specific to animals. Other items that acquire *kedushat haguf* through the *kli sharet* cannot be redeemed when *tamei*, even though *tumah* might seem analogous to a *mum* in rendering the item unusable. That is the framework Shmuel erects, and it is this framework that the *gemara* now proceeds to challenge.

## Rav Huna's Challenge

### Middle List Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוֹתֵיב רַב הוּנָא בַּר מָנוֹחַ: הָעוֹפוֹת וְהָעֵצִים וְהַלְּבוֹנָה וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ, אֵין לָהֶן פִּדְיוֹן, שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱמַר פִּדְיוֹן אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה. בִּשְׁלָמָא עוֹפוֹת – קְדוּשַּׁת הַגּוּף נִינְהוּ, וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר אֶלָּא בִּבְהֵמָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Manoaḥ raises an objection** to Shmuel's opinion that even meal offerings and libations that are pure may be redeemed if they have not yet been consecrated in a service vessel. The mishna states: With regard to consecrated **birds, wood** for the altar, **frankincense, and service vessels, once** they **became ritually impure, they have no** possibility of **redemption, as redemption** of consecrated items **is stated only with regard to an animal** consecrated for the altar that became blemished. **Granted, birds** are not redeemed, since **they are** imbued with **inherent sanctity, and** the Torah **stated** that **only with regard to** blemished **animals,** not birds, is redemption possible for items of inherent sanctity.

Rav Huna bar Manoach raises an objection from the middle portion of the *mishna*, which presents a notably puzzling list. The *mishna* states: *ha-ofot ve-ha-etzim ve-ha-levonah ve-khlei sharet shenitme'u ein lahem pidyon* — birds, wood, frankincense, and *kli sharet* that became *tamei* cannot be redeemed. The reason offered by the *mishna* is *shelo ne'emar pidyon ela be-vehemah* — the concept of redemption applies only to an animal with a *mum*.

### The Specific Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא עֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת – לִיפַּרְקוּ? אֶלָּא לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּטְהוֹרִין בְּעָלְמָא אֵין נִפְדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to **wood, and frankincense** that is not consecrated with inherent sanctity until it is placed in a service vessel, **and service vessels** themselves that became impure, since none of these possess inherent sanctity, **let them be redeemed. Rather, is it not** that these items are not redeemed **because pure** sacrificial items **in general are not redeemed,** even when they do not possess inherent sanctity?

This list is genuinely difficult to understand. Alongside birds, the *mishna* groups wood, frankincense, and *kli sharet* — none of which are animals, and none of which have been placed in a *kli sharet* (indeed, a *kli sharet* cannot be placed inside another *kli sharet*). The challenge this poses to Shmuel is clear: if items with only *kedushat damim* and no *kli sharet* sanctification can normally be redeemed, what is the *mishna* telling us by listing these objects as non-redeemable?

### No Redemption When Tamei

The *gemara* proposes that this list must be conveying something about the general conditions for redemption. The very fact that these items — which should, on Shmuel's account, be redeemable — are listed as non-redeemable suggests that the operative principle is broader than Shmuel allows. The *gemara* will develop this.

## Tahor Status Theory

### Birds Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי נָמֵי, אַף עַל גַּב דְּנִטְמְאוּ – כִּטְהוֹרִים דָּמוּ, דְּעֵצִים וּלְבוֹנָה לָאו בְּנֵי אַשְׁוֹיֵי אוּכְלָא נִינְהוּ, אֶלָּא חִיבַּת הַקּוֹדֶשׁ מַשְׁוֵה לְהוּ אוּכְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And these** items **too,** i.e., the wood, frankincense, and service vessels, **even though they became impure,** they **are** treated **like** they are **pure.** Their impurity is incomplete **because wood and frankincense are not capable of becoming food,** and consequently they should not be susceptible to impurity at all. **Rather, the regard for the sanctity** of sacred property **transforms their** status into that of **food,** which renders them susceptible to ritual impurity.

The *gemara* begins its analysis: *bishlama ofot kedushat haguf ninhu* — it is understandable why birds cannot be redeemed, since birds are *kedushat haguf*. They are offered directly as sacrifices, and their slaughter is performed by the *kohen*'s fingernail rather than with a knife, so there is no *kli sharet* involved. They are thus comparable to animals with *kedushat haguf*, and only an animal with a *mum* may be redeemed. That much is clear.

### Wood & Frankincense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּעֵצִים, כַּמָּה דְּלָא מְשַׁפֵּי לְהוּ לִגְזִירִין – לָא מִיתַּכְשְׁרִי. לְבוֹנָה נָמֵי, כַּמָּה דְּלָא קָידְשָׁה בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת – לָא מִיתַּכְשְׁרָה. כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת נָמֵי, הוֹאִיל וְאִית לְהוּ טׇהֳרָה בְּמִקְוֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **With regard to wood, as long as** one does **not trim it into logs,** it **does not become susceptible** to impurity. With regard to **frankincense as well, as long as** it is **not consecrated in a service vessel,** it **does not become susceptible** to impurity. With regard to **service vessels also, since they have** the capacity to attain **purity in a ritual bath,** their impurity is revocable. Apparently, the reason the mishna teaches that these items are not redeemed is because they are in some sense still regarded as pure, and consecrated items that are considered ritually pure are not redeemed, contrary to the opinion of Shmuel.

But what of wood, frankincense, and *kli sharet*? These items do not possess *kedushat haguf* and have not been placed in a *kli sharet*. Why, then, can they not be redeemed? The *gemara* proposes that the answer lies in the nature of these items: *ela lav mishum de-tehorim be-alma ein nifdim* — the principle must be that items that are fundamentally *tahor* cannot be redeemed. These items are listed because *af al gav de-nitme'u ke-tehorim damu* — even when they become *tamei*, they are effectively still *tahor*.

The *gemara* elaborates. Wood and frankincense are *lav benei ashvuyei okhla* — they are not genuinely edible, and therefore they should not ordinarily be susceptible to *tumah*. It is only the special rule of *hibat ha-kodesh* — the heightened treatment of temple-related objects — that causes them to be treated as if they were food and thus capable of receiving *tumah*. But this is a legal fiction, not a genuine food-status.

Moreover, wood becomes fit to receive *tumah* only once it has been smoothed and prepared as logs for the *mizbe'ach*. Until that stage — *kama de-la mashfei lehu le-gizrin* — the wood is *lo makhshiri*, not yet prepared for *tumah* susceptibility under biblical law. This rule is derived from the verse *ve-ha-basar*, which the *gemara* uses to include wood and frankincense in the laws of *tumah*, but only when they have reached the requisite state of readiness.

### Kli Sharet Purification

Similarly, *levona* does not become fit for *tumah* until it has been sanctified in a *kli sharet*. And a *kli sharet* itself — the third item in the list — presents yet another reason why its *tumah* is not truly definitive: *ho'il ve-is lehu tahara be-mikvah* — since it can be purified by immersion in a *mikvah*, its *tumah* is not a permanent condition and is therefore not the kind of *tumah* that would justify redemption.

### Fundamentally Tahor

The *gemara*'s initial theory, then, is that the middle list in the *mishna* is there to teach a general principle: items that are fundamentally *tahor* — either because they are not genuinely susceptible to *tumah*, or because their *tumah* can be reversed — may not be redeemed. This, on this reading, directly contradicts Shmuel's position that items with only *kedushat damim* may be redeemed even when *tahor*.

## Rarity Explanation

### Shmuel Rejects Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ: טְהוֹרִין בְּעָלְמָא נִפְדִּין, וְהָנֵי – מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, actually, I will say to you** that **in general, pure** items **are redeemed; and these** items are not redeemed, despite the fact that they are not imbued with inherent sanctity, **because they are not** readily **available.** If these items can be redeemed when they are pure, then they may not be available for the Temple service.

The *gemara* rejects this first reading: *lo* — no. *Le-olam eima lach tahor be-alma nifdin* — in truth, Shmuel is correct that *tahor* items may in principle be redeemed. The middle list does not establish a general principle that *tahor* items cannot be redeemed. Rather, *ve-hanei mishum de-lo shechichi hu* — the reason these particular items cannot be redeemed is that they are uncommon.

### General Redemption Rule

Shmuel's position is thus defended: the general rule permits redemption of items with *kedushat damim* even when *tahor*. The middle list represents a specific rabbinic restriction, not a general principle. The Rabbis did not want these uncommon items to be redeemed — they wanted them to reach the *Beit Hamikdash*. If someone has finally sanctified some frankincense, the Rabbis did not want that sanctification undone. But this is a limited exception, not a categorical rule against redeeming *tahor* items.

### Rarity Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא לְבוֹנָה וּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת – לָא שְׁכִיחִי, אֶלָּא עֵצִים – מִישְׁכָּח שְׁכִיחִי! עֵצִים נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר: כׇּל עֵץ שֶׁנִּמְצָא בּוֹ תּוֹלַעַת פָּסוּל לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, הִילְכָּךְ לָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **Granted, frankincense and service vessels are not** readily **available, but wood** is **readily available.** Why, then, may it not be redeemed? The Gemara answers: **Wood** usable for the Temple service is **also** difficult to procure. This is apparent **since the Master said** that **any wood in which a worm is found** is **disqualified for** use on **the altar. Consequently,** wood suitable for the altar is **not** readily **available.**

The *gemara* then examines each item on the list. *Bishlama levona ve-khlei sharet lo shechichi* — frankincense and temple vessels are indeed uncommon, and it is understandable that once sanctified, the Rabbis would want them to remain consecrated. But then the *gemara* raises a difficulty: *ela etzim meshkach shechichi* — wood, however, is common. Why would the Rabbis apply the same restriction to wood?

The *gemara* answers: *etzim nami keivan de-amar mar kol etz she-nimtza bo tola'at pasul le-gabei mizbe'ach* — even wood is not so common in its usable form, because we learn that any wood in which a worm is found is disqualified for the *mizbe'ach*. Wood that is fit for use on the *mizbe'ach* is therefore sufficiently rare that the same rabbinic concern applies. The conclusion is that these two readings of the middle list represent two distinct approaches to understanding why these items cannot be redeemed — one holding that *tahor* items are generally non-redeemable, and the other holding that this is a specific rabbinic restriction grounded in the rarity of these items and the desire to channel them to the *Beit Hamikdash*.

## Temimim Challenge

### Mizbe'ach Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי שְׁמִיעָא לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל הָא דְּתַנְיָא, הַמַּתְפִּיס תְּמִימִים לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת – אֵין פּוֹדִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא לַמִּזְבֵּחַ, שֶׁכׇּל הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ אֵינוֹ יוֹצֵא מִידֵי מִזְבֵּחַ לְעוֹלָם, וְאַף עַל גַּב דִּקְדוּשַּׁת דָּמִים נִינְהוּ – אֵין נִפְדִּין הוֹאִיל וּטְהוֹרִים הֵם, הֲוָה הָדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the opinion of Shmuel that libations and the flour for meal offerings are redeemed even when pure, as long as they have not been consecrated in a service vessel. **Rav Pappa said** that **if Shmuel had heard that which is taught** in the following *baraita*, he would have retracted his opinion. The *baraita* teaches: In the case of one **who consecrates unblemished** animals **for Temple maintenance** rather than for the altar, **they are redeemed only for** use **on the altar.** They may not be redeemed for any other use, in accordance with the principle **that any** consecrated item **that is fit** to be sacrificed **on the altar may never leave the altar. And even though these** animals possess only **sanctity** that inheres in their **value, they are not redeemed, since they are ritually pure** and fit for the altar. Had Shmuel known this *baraita*, **he would have retracted** his opinion.

The *gemara* now presents a challenge from Rav Papa. He cites a *baraita*: *ha-matfis temimim le-vedek ha-bayit ein podin osam ela le-mizbe'ach* — if one dedicates an unblemished animal to the general temple fund (*bedek ha-bayit*) rather than as a sacrifice, when the *Beit Hamikdash* comes to sell it, it may only be sold to someone who will use it as a *korban*. The sanctity will then transfer to the money received, and the animal will be resanctified as an offering.

### Animal Redemption

The reason for this rule is *she-kol ha-rauy le-mizbe'ach eino yotzei mi-yedei mizbe'ach le-olam* — anything fit for the *mizbe'ach* must never be permitted to escape the *mizbe'ach*. The animal was sanctified, it could serve as a *korban*, and therefore we ensure that it ultimately does so, even if the nature of the sanctification was for *bedek ha-bayit* rather than as a direct offering. *Ve-af al gav de-kedushat damim ninhu ve-ein nifdim* — even though it has only *kedushat damim*, it cannot be redeemed in a manner that would take it away from the *mizbe'ach*.

### Parallel Logic

Rav Papa argues that the same logic should apply to *menachot* and *nesachim*. These were sanctified directly for the *mizbe'ach*, they are *tahor*, and they can be placed in a *kli sharet* and offered. The principle that nothing fit for the *mizbe'ach* should escape it ought to mean that one cannot redeem them when they are *tahor*. The *baraita* thus seems to challenge Shmuel directly.

### Shmuel Consistent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, שְׁמִיעָא לֵיהּ וְלָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ. לָאו אָמְרַתְּ הָתָם: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא שְׁכִיחִי – לָא מִיפַּרְקִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **But** that **is not so;** this *baraita* was **heard by him, and** he still **did not retract** his opinion. Rather, he explained it as follows: **Didn't you say there,** i.e., earlier in the discussion of the mishna, that the reason one may not redeem wood, frankincense, and service vessels that were consecrated for Temple maintenance is that **since** they are **not** readily **available,** the Sages decreed that they **are not redeemed?**

The *gemara* defends Shmuel: *velo hi* — it is not so. Shmuel is familiar with this *baraita* and did not reverse his position. The reason is that on Shmuel's account, the explanation for the middle list in the *mishna* — wood, frankincense, and *kli sharet* — is that these items are rare, and the Rabbis wanted to ensure they made it to the *Beit Hamikdash*. *Lav amrat hatam kevan de-lo shechichi lo mifruki* — since those items are uncommon, we do not allow them to be redeemed when *tahor*. By contrast, *hacha nami kevan de-shechichi mumim de-paslei be-vehemah* — since blemishes that disqualify animals are common — *de-afilu be-dukin she-ba-ayin nami paslei*, even a film over the eye disqualifies an animal — *hilkhakh lo shechichi*, it is therefore not common to have a genuinely unblemished animal. And because an unblemished animal fit for the *mizbe'ach* is rare, the Rabbis similarly wanted to ensure that when one such animal was sanctified, even if for *bedek ha-bayit*, it should find its way to the *mizbe'ach*. The *baraita* about unblemished animals thus operates on the same logic as the middle list — it is not a general rule but a specific restriction applied to rare items. Wine and flour, which are common, can be redeemed freely.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דִּשְׁכִיחִי מוּמִין דְּפָסְלִי בִּבְהֵמָה, דַּאֲפִילּוּ בְּדוּקִּים שֶׁבָּעַיִן נָמֵי פָּסְלִי, הִילְכָּךְ לָא שְׁכִיחִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Here, too,** with regard to an unblemished animal that was consecrated for Temple maintenance, **since blemishes that disqualify an animal** as an offering **are common, as even** a blemish as insignificant **as** one **on** the **cornea of the eye also disqualifies** the animal, **therefore,** unblemished animals that are fit to be sacrificed on the altar are **not** readily **available.** That is why the Sages decreed that unblemished animals, even when consecrated for Temple maintenance, may be redeemed only for use as an offering on the altar. By contrast, meal offerings and libations, which were the subject of Shmuel's statement, are readily available, and may be redeemed even when they are still pure.

## Tamei vs. Tahor

### Rav Kahana Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר: טְמֵאִין – נִפְדִּין, טְהוֹרִין – אֵין נִפְדִּין. וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: טְמֵאִין – נִפְדִּין, טְהוֹרִין – אֵין נִפְדִּין. אִיכָּא דְאָמְרִי אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: אֲפִילּוּ טְהוֹרִין – נִפְדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § After analyzing Shmuel's opinion permitting the redemption of meal offerings and libations that are pure and have not yet been consecrated in a service vessel, the Gemara now cites a dissenting opinion: **Rav Kahana said** that only meal offerings and libations that are **impure are redeemed,** but those that are **pure are not redeemed. And Rabbi Oshaya similarly said** that those meal offerings and libations that are **impure are redeemed,** but those that are **pure are not redeemed. There are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Oshaya says: Even pure** ones **are redeemed.**

Rav Kahana takes a categorical position against Shmuel: *tamei'im nifdim, tehorim ein nifdim* — only *tamei* items may be redeemed; *tahor* items, even those with only *kedushat damim*, may not be redeemed. This is the reading of the middle list in the *mishna* that flows from the *gemara*'s first proposed explanation, and it is also consistent with the *baraita* about unblemished animals.

### Rabi Oshaya Agrees

*Ve-khen amar Rabi Oshaya* — Rabi Oshaya expressed the same view: *tamei'im nifdim, tehorim ein nifdim*.

### Alt: Tahor Redeemable

However, there is an alternative version of Rabi Oshaya's position: *ika de-amri amar Rav Oshaya afilu tehorim nifdim* — some report that he said even *tahor* items may be redeemed. On this version, Rabi Oshaya aligns with Shmuel's fundamental position, with specific exceptions for rare items.

### Minchat Chatai Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן טְמֵאִין – נִפְדִּין, טְהוֹרִין – אֵין נִפְדִּין, חוּץ מֵעֲשִׂירִית הָאֵיפָה שֶׁל מִנְחַת חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** With regard **to all** meal offerings, if they are **impure** they **are redeemed,** and if they are **pure,** they **are not redeemed, except for the tenth of an ephah** of fine flour **of a meal offering of a sinner,** which is redeemed even if it is pure.

Rabbi Eliezer offers a position that largely agrees with the restrictive view — *kulam tamei'im nifdim tehorim ein nifdim*, all may only be redeemed when *tamei* — but with one notable exception: *chutz me-asirit ha-eifah shel minchat chotei* — the *mincha* brought as part of the sliding-scale *korban* (*oleh ve-yored*) may be redeemed even when *tahor*.

---

## Minchat Chatai Rules

### Oleh V'Yored System

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהֲרֵי אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״מֵחַטָּאתוֹ״ ״עַל חַטָּאתוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The reason for this is **that the Torah stated** with regard to an extremely destitute individual who brings a tenth of an ephah of fine flour: "And the priest shall effect atonement for him **for his sin [*meḥattato*]** that he has sinned of one of these, and it shall be forgiven for him" (Leviticus 5:13). By contrast, with regard to a wealthy person who brings a lamb as a sliding-scale offering, the verse states: "And the priest shall effect atonement for him **from his sin [*al ḥattato*]** that he has sinned" (Leviticus 5:6). The word "*al*," which can also mean on, indicates that if an extremely destitute individual designates a tenth of an ephah for his meal offering and then becomes wealthy, he redeems his meal offering and adds money on to the original sum in order to purchase an offering that is appropriate for his current financial status. In that case, the meal offering is redeemed even if it is pure.

The *minchat chotei* arises within the *korban oleh ve-yored* system described at the end of Parshat Vayikra. For certain categories of sin, the Torah prescribes a graduated scale of offerings: one who can afford it brings an animal; one who cannot brings birds; and one who cannot even afford birds brings a *mincha*. The scale is designed to ensure that the obligation of *kaparah* is accessible at every economic level.

### Movement Between Levels

The Torah uses the phrase *me-chata'so al chata'so* to convey that the level of obligation tracks the sinner's current financial situation, not the level at which the obligation was originally incurred. If one set aside money for the higher-level offering and subsequently became impoverished, one may redirect a portion of those funds toward the lower-level offering appropriate to one's current means. Conversely, *al chata'so* teaches that if one sanctified a *mincha* — the lowest level — and subsequently became wealthier, one is obligated to step up to the higher-level offering.

### Minchat Chatai Redemption

This is why redemption of a *minchat chotei* is permitted even when *tahor*: the Torah itself demands flexibility in this system. If the person has become wealthier after sanctifying the *mincha*, they must be able to redeem it and bring a bird instead. The Rabbis' general restriction against redeeming *tahor* items was not applied to this case, because the Torah's own framework of *oleh ve-yored* requires the ability to move up and down the levels of obligation. The operative consideration is not the state of the *mincha* but the current financial status of the obligant.

---

## Piggul Food Status

### Piggul in Mincha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: שָׁמַעְתִּי פִּיגֵּל בְּמִנְחָה לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, דִּתְנַן: הָעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another statement that **Rabbi Oshaya says: I heard** that **according to Rabbi Shimon,** when one **rendered a meal offering *piggul*** by sacrificing it with the intent to consume it beyond its designated time, it is not susceptible **to the ritual impurity of food. As we learned** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Okatzin* 3:12): ***Orla*, diverse kinds in a vineyard,**

Rabi Oshaya introduces a further teaching: *shamati pigul be-mincha le-Rabi Shimon eino metamei tumat ochlin* — he heard that according to Rabi Shimon, a *mincha* that has become *piggul* — through a disqualifying intention entertained during the *avodah* — does not contract *tumah* as food (*tumat ochlin*). This is a striking and counterintuitive claim, and the *gemara* will need to explore both its basis and its apparent implications.

### Assur Behenaa Rule

The underlying principle is that for Rabi Shimon, something must be edible — or at least capable of being benefited from by someone — in order to bear the status of "food" for purposes of *tumat ochlin*. A *mincha* that has become *piggul* is forbidden not merely in eating but in any benefit (*assur be-hana'ah*), and therefore, under Rabi Shimon's reasoning, it lacks the status of food entirely.

---

## Assur Behenaa Dispute

### Tanna Kamma View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל וְעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה וְצִפּוֹרֵי מְצוֹרָע וּפֶטֶר חֲמוֹר וּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב כּוּלָּם מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** the flesh of **an ox that is stoned, a heifer whose neck is broken,the birds** sacrificed by **a leper** (see Leviticus 14:4–7), **a firstborn donkey** whose neck was broken, **and meat** cooked together **with milk are all** susceptible to **the ritual impurity of food,** despite the fact that they are forbidden for consumption.

The *gemara* now grounds this discussion in a *mishna*. The *mishna* lists items that are *assur be-hana'ah* — forbidden even for benefit — including: *orlah* and *kilai ha-kerem*; the ox that is sentenced to stoning (*shor ha-niskal*) and the *egla arufa*; the birds of the *metzora* used in the purification ritual; a firstborn donkey whose neck is broken rather than redeemed; and *basar be-chalav* — meat cooked in milk. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that all of these are nonetheless capable of contracting *tumat ochlin* — food-related impurity. Even though they are forbidden in benefit, they retain the legal status of food for purposes of *tumah*.

Rashi notes that by the time the *shor ha-niskal* or *egla arufa* is stoned or has its neck broken, it would in any case be a *neveilah* and *tamei* on that basis. The relevant scenario, therefore, is one where the animal was *shechted* rather than dispatched by the prescribed method — even so, if it is in a state of being *assur be-hana'ah*, the *Tanna Kamma* holds that it is still considered food for *tumah* purposes.

### Rabbi Shimon Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּן אֵין מִטַּמְּאִין טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, וּמוֹדֶה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בְּבָשָׂר בְּחָלָב שֶׁמְּטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, הוֹאִיל וְהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: None of them are susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** since they are all items from which it is prohibited to derive benefit, and they are therefore not considered food. **And Rabbi Shimon concedes with regard to meat** cooked together **with milk that it is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food since it,** i.e., both the meat and the milk, **had a time** that it was **fit** for consumption before it was rendered forbidden.

*Rabi Shimon omer kulam ein metame'im tumat ochlin* — Rabi Shimon disagrees and holds that none of these items can contract *tumat ochlin*, because they are *assur be-hana'ah* and therefore not "food" in the legally operative sense.

### Basar Bechalav Case

However, Rabi Shimon himself concedes one case: *u-modeh Rabi Shimon be-vasar be-chalav she-metamei tumat ochlin* — he agrees that *basar be-chalav* can contract *tumat ochlin*. The reason is *ho'il ve-hayetah lo sha'at ha-kosher* — because it had a prior moment of fitness. The meat and milk each existed separately as permitted foods. Even after they were soaked together, before being cooked, they remained permitted. Only after cooking do they become *assur be-hana'ah*. Because at an earlier stage in their existence they were permissible food, they retain food-status even after becoming *assur be-hana'ah*.

All the other items on the list, by contrast, never existed as foods without the prohibition already in place. Even a live cow, before it became a *shor ha-niskal*, was not food — you cannot eat a living animal. So from the moment these items came into existence as the forbidden object in question, they were already *assur be-hana'ah*. They therefore never had a *sha'at ha-kosher*, and Rabi Shimon holds they cannot be considered food.

## Rabbi Yochanan Explains

### Pesuk Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב אַסִּי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? ״מִכׇּל הָאֹכֶל אֲשֶׁר יֵאָכֵל״ – אוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים קָרוּי אוֹכֶל, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים אֵינוֹ קָרוּי אוֹכֶל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What is the reason for Rabbi Shimon's** opinion that an item from which it is prohibited to derive benefit is not susceptible to impurity of food? It is because it is stated: **"All food which may be eaten [*ha'okhel asher ye'akhel*],** that on which water comes, shall be impure" (Leviticus 11:34). The redundancy in the phrase "food which may be eaten" indicates that specifically **food that you are able to feed to others,** in this case, gentiles, **is termed food** for the purposes of susceptibility to the impurity of food, but **food that you are not able to feed to others is not termed food.** Therefore, items from which it is prohibited to derive benefit and which it is therefore prohibited to feed to others are not considered food in this context.

Rav Assi said in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *mah ta'ama de-Rabi Shimon?* — what is Rabi Shimon's textual basis? The source is the verse *mi-kol ha-ochel asher ye'achel* — "from all food that can be eaten" — which the Torah employs in the context of a *sheretz* rendering food *tamei*.

### Food Definition

From this verse, Rabi Shimon derives: *ochel she-atah yakhol le-achilo la-acherim karui ochel* — food that you can feed to others is called food. The standard need not be that a Jew may personally eat it; it suffices that it can be given to non-Jews, or to a dog, so that some person or creature may derive benefit from it. If that threshold is met, the item bears the status of food for *tumah* purposes.

### Assur Behenaa Not Food

*Ochel she-ei attah yakhol le-achilo la-acherim eino karui ochel* — but if you cannot feed it to anyone, because it is *assur be-hana'ah*, then it is not called food. This is Rabi Shimon's position in its simplest form.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא פִּיגֵּל בְּמִנְחָה נָמֵי, אוֹכֶל שֶׁאִי אַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Oshaya explains how this can be applied to *piggul*: **A meal offering** that one **rendered *piggul* is also food that you are not able to feed to others,** as it is prohibited to derive benefit from it. Consequently, it is not susceptible to the ritual impurity of food according to Rabbi Shimon.

A question arises — one that Tosafot addresses in the name of Rabbi Eliezer mi-Mitz, the *Yere'im* — why, given the verse *asher ye'achel*, does Rabi Shimon only exclude items that are *assur be-hana'ah*, rather than excluding all items that are *assur ba-achilah* (forbidden to eat)? In another context (*Perek Kissui ha-Dam*), Rabi Shimon uses the same phrase *asher ye'achel* to exclude even *tereifah* from the obligation of covering the blood, meaning that there he excludes things forbidden to eat, not merely things forbidden in benefit.

Tosafot's answer: the verse here says *mi-kol ha-ochel* — "from all food" — with the word *kol* indicating an expansive category. Since the verse suggests inclusion broadly, if something is to be excluded, it must be only the most severely restricted items — those forbidden in any benefit at all. Other items that are merely *assur ba-achilah* but *muttar be-hana'ah* would still fall within the scope of "food."

## Basar Bechalav Challenge

### Basar Bechalav OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב נָמֵי תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ, דְּאוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why doesn't he **also** derive that **meat** cooked **in milk** is susceptible to impurity **because it is food that you may feed to others,** as Rabbi Shimon maintains that it is permitted to derive benefit from meat and milk cooked together?

The *gemara* now raises a question regarding Rabi Shimon's treatment of *basar be-chalav*. Before invoking the *sha'at ha-kosher* principle — that it had a prior moment of fitness — there is a more immediate reason why *basar be-chalav* poses no difficulty for Rabi Shimon at all: according to his own ruling, *basar be-chalav* is not *assur be-hana'ah* in the first place.

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״כִּי עַם קָדוֹשׁ אַתָּה לַה׳ אֱלֹהֶיךָ לֹא תְבַשֵּׁל גְּדִי בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״, וּלְהַלָּן הוּא אוֹמֵר ״וְאַנְשֵׁי קֹדֶשׁ תִּהְיוּן לִי וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״, מָה לְהַלָּן אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, אַף כָּאן אָסוּר בַּאֲכִילָה וּמוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon:** With regard to **meat** cooked **in milk, eating** it **is prohibited and** deriving **benefit** from it **is permitted, as it is stated: "For you are a holy people to the Lord your God; you shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk"** (Deuteronomy 14:21). **And elsewhere** the verse **states: "And you shall be holy men to Me; therefore you shall not eat any flesh that is torn by animals [*tereifa*] in the field"** (Exodus 22:30). **Just as there,** with regard to an animal torn by animals, which is forbidden as a *tereifa*, i.e., an animal possessing a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months, **eating** it **is prohibited but** deriving **benefit** from it **is permitted, so too here,** with regard to meat cooked in milk, where being a holy people is also mentioned, **eating** it **is forbidden but** deriving **benefit** from it **is permitted.**

A *baraita* records: *Rabbi Shimon Yehuda omer mishum Rabi Shimon: basar be-chalav assur ba-achilah muttar be-hana'ah* — meat cooked in milk is forbidden to eat but permitted for benefit. The proof is a *gezeirah shavah* from the word *kadosh*: the verse regarding *basar be-chalav* calls Israel *am kadosh* — a holy people — while another verse calls them *anshei kodesh*, in the context of *tereifah*, where *assur ba-achilah* but *muttar be-hana'ah*. The parallel implies that *basar be-chalav* likewise is only *assur ba-achilah* and not *assur be-hana'ah*. (This is not the normative ruling, but it is Rabi Shimon's position.)

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא, דְּאוֹכֶל שֶׁאַתָּה יָכוֹל לְהַאֲכִילוֹ לַאֲחֵרִים הוּא, וְעוֹד, לְדִידֵיהּ נָמֵי הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* that cites Rabbi Shimon's opinion **states one** reason why meat cooked in milk is susceptible to impurity **and** adds **another. One** reason is **that** it **is food that you can feed to others.** Therefore, it is called food for the purpose of being susceptible to impurity. **And another** reason is that **even for him,** i.e., a Jew, although it is currently prohibited to eat the milk and meat, **it had a time** when each was **fit** to be eaten, i.e., before they were cooked together; therefore, they remain susceptible to impurity.

If so, asks the *gemara*, why did Rabi Shimon need to invoke the *sha'at ha-kosher* reasoning for *basar be-chalav*? The *gemara* answers: *chada ve-od ka'amar* — he is offering two independent reasons. First, *de-ochla de-ata yakhol le-achilo la-acherim hu* — his own personal position is that *basar be-chalav* is *muttar be-hana'ah*, so it can be fed to others and is therefore unambiguously food. Second, *le-didi nami haytah lo sha'at ha-kosher* — even for one who holds (unlike Rabi Shimon) that *basar be-chalav* is *assur be-hana'ah*, Rabi Shimon would still agree that it is food, because it had a *sha'at ha-kosher* — a prior moment of fitness before the prohibition came into effect. So he is saying: I have two independent grounds for treating *basar be-chalav* as food.

## Piggul-Notar Problem

### Notar Before/After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר, יֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁהוּא מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, וְיֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Oshaya from a *baraita*: **Rabbi Shimon says** that **there is** a case of the **leftover** of an offering **that is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, but there is** also a case of the **leftover** of an offering **that is not susceptible to the impurity of food.**

The *gemara* now raises a challenge from a *baraita* that quotes Rabi Shimon himself making a distinction regarding *notar* — sacrificial meat left over beyond its permitted time of consumption. Rabi Shimon states: *yesh notar she-metamei tumat ochlin ve-yesh notar she-eino metamei tumat ochlin* — there is *notar* that can contract *tumat ochlin* and there is *notar* that cannot.

### Notar Before Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? לַן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If it was **left overnight before** the **sprinkling** of the blood on the altar, it is not susceptible **to the ritual impurity of food,** as it never became fit for consumption. But if it was left overnight **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, it **is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** since from after the sprinkling of the blood until it was left overnight, it was fit for consumption.

The case of *notar* that cannot contract *tumat ochlin*: meat that stayed overnight *lifnei zerika* — before the blood was ever sprinkled. From the moment this meat existed — from *shechita* — it was never fit to be eaten, because its permissibility to be eaten depends on the blood being sprinkled. Since the blood was never sprinkled, the meat moved directly from being *not yet fit* to being *notar*, without ever passing through a stage of fitness. It therefore never had a *sha'at ha-kosher* and is not food.

### Notar After Zerika

The case of *notar* that can contract *tumat ochlin*: meat that stayed overnight *le-achar zerika* — after the blood was properly sprinkled. Once the blood was sprinkled, the *kohanim* became permitted to eat the meat. It had a genuine *sha'at ha-kosher*. If they then failed to eat it before the deadline and it became *notar*, it retains its food-status and can contract *tumat ochlin*.

### Sha'at Hakoasher

This *baraita* confirms Rabi Shimon's consistency: what determines food-status is whether the item ever had a *sha'at ha-kosher* — a moment during which it was genuinely fit to be eaten. The same principle that governs *basar be-chalav* governs sacrificial meat, and by extension, it will govern the *mincha*.

## Oshaya-Shimon Issue

### Growing Sanctification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וְהָא) וּפִיגּוּל, בֵּין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים בֵּין בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים – אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין. פִּיגֵּל בְּמִנְחָה – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** with regard to ***piggul*, both in** cases of **offerings of the most sacred order as well as in** cases of **offerings of lesser sanctity,** the meat of the offering is not susceptible to **the impurity of food.** This is because it was rendered forbidden for consumption at the beginning of the sacrificial rite, and was never fit for consumption. If the priest **rendered a meal offering *piggul*,** it **is** susceptible to **the impurity of food,** since it did have a period of time when it was acceptable, i.e., when it was still flour before it was consecrated as a meal offering. This ruling contradicts Rabbi Oshaya's understanding that according to Rabbi Shimon, a meal offering that became *piggul* is not susceptible to the impurity of food.

The *gemara* now brings the earlier teaching of Rabi Oshaya into focus in light of this framework. Rabi Oshaya's claim that a *mincha* that became *piggul* is not food according to Rabi Shimon requires explanation — after all, the *mincha* existed as flour and oil before it was sanctified, and flour and oil are edible. The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the scenario: *de-hikdishanu bi-mchubar* — the olives and the wheat were sanctified while they were still attached to the ground, still growing.

### No Sha'at Hakoasher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן – שֶׁהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר, כָּאן – שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: That is **not difficult,** as **here,** in the *baraita* where Rabbi Shimon ruled that the meal offering that became *piggul* is susceptible to the impurity of food, it is referring to a case **where it had a time** in which **it** was **fit** for consumption. **There,** where it is not susceptible to the impurity of food, it is referring to a case **where it did not have a time** in which it was **fit** for consumption.

Because the sanctification took effect from the very beginning — from the moment when these were still raw agricultural produce attached to the earth and not yet edible in any meaningful sense — the resulting *mincha* never had a *sha'at ha-kosher*. By the time the wheat was harvested, milled into flour, and the olives were pressed into oil, and the material was processed into a proper offering, the *kedushah* had already attached. There was never a moment when it existed as ordinary, edible food free of sanctification and available to be eaten.

### Theoretical Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָּמֵי דְּלֹא הָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר? דְּאַקְדְּשִׁינְהוּ בִּמְחוּבָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances where it did not have a time** in which it was **fit** for consumption? Before the flour was consecrated as a meal offering, it was certainly permitted for consumption. The Gemara answers: This would occur **where he consecrated** the wheat **while** it was still **attached** to the ground and was therefore not yet susceptible to impurity. Once harvested, it was already prohibited for consumption.

This is admittedly a somewhat forced reading of Rabi Oshaya's statement, but the *gemara* proceeds on this basis. The question then becomes: in this scenario, does the theoretical possibility of redemption change the analysis?

## Potential Redemption Iss…

### Redemption Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיפְרְקִינְהוּ! הָנִיחָה לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: טְמֵאִין – נִפְדִּין, טְהוֹרִין – אֵין נִפְדִּין, שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: The flour may still have a time in which it was fit. **Let him redeem it** before it is placed in a service vessel. Why is it regarded as not having a time in which it was fit for consumption? The Gemara qualifies the question: **This** distinction, between flour that came from wheat that was consecrated before it was harvested and wheat or flour that was consecrated at a later point, **works out well according to this version of** that which **Rabbi Oshaya said: Impure** meal offerings and libations that have not been consecrated in a service vessel **are redeemed;** if they are **pure,** they **are not redeemed.** Accordingly, it works out **well** that the flour does not have a time in which it was fit for consumption when it came from wheat that was consecrated before being harvested. It could not be redeemed and made fit for consumption.

Even in this unusual scenario — sanctified while still growing — the *gemara* points out that redemption is theoretically available. The *mincha* has *kedushat damim*; depending on the position one adopts about *tahor* items, it could be redeemed. If it can be redeemed, then through redemption it would once again become ordinary food, theoretically edible. Should that theoretical possibility not count as a *sha'at ha-kosher*? The *gemara* engages this question directly.

This entire inquiry is now explicitly connected to the earlier dispute: *hani — le-hakh lishna de-amar Rav Oshaya tamei'im nifdim tehorim ein nifdim* — according to the version in which Rabi Oshaya holds that only *tamei* items may be redeemed, the question does not arise. If the wheat-and-olive *mincha* cannot be redeemed while *tahor*, there is no theoretical redemption-path and therefore no *sha'at ha-kosher*. *Shapir* — that works cleanly. But *ela le-hakh lishna de-amar afilu tehorim nifdim* — according to the version that says even *tahor* items may be redeemed, *ve-lifrikinhu* — why can't we simply redeem it? And if we could redeem it, then shouldn't it be considered food?

### Could/Is Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לְהָךְ לִישָּׁנָא דְּאָמַר, אֲפִילּוּ טְהוֹרִין נִפְדִּין, לִפְרְקִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But according to this version of** what Rabbi Oshaya **said: Even pure** meal offerings and libations **are redeemed,** there remains the possibility of **letting him redeem** the meal offering while it is pure and before is consecrated in a service vessel. Therefore, it should be considered as having a time in which it is fit for consumption.

The *gemara* first offers a narrow answer: *ha-shta mi-hah lo parik* — right now, in practice, it has not been redeemed. We judge things by their actual state, not by theoretical possibilities. But the *gemara* immediately pushes back on this, because it emerges that Rabi Shimon himself holds *kol ha-omed li-fdot ke-fadu dami* — anything that stands to be redeemed is considered as if it has been redeemed. That is, for Rabi Shimon, the theoretical possibility of redemption is not merely hypothetical — it carries normative weight in determining status.

### Para Aduma Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא מִיהָא לָא פָּרֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **In any event, now he has not redeemed it.** Therefore, it is not considered to have had a time in which it is fit for consumption.

The *gemara* cites the source for this principle: a *baraita* in which Rabi Shimon states that a *parah adumah* — the red cow used for purification — can contract *tumat ochlin* as food. This is striking, since the *parah adumah* is sanctified for burning, not for eating. But Rabi Shimon holds *hayetah lo sha'at ha-kosher* — it had a moment of fitness. What moment? Reish Lakish explains: *Rabi Shimon haya omer para nifdit al gabei ma'arachta* — Rabi Shimon would say that the *parah adumah* can be redeemed even when it is already on the woodpile, ready to be ignited. Since it can theoretically be redeemed at any moment, and since redemption would restore it to ordinary food-status, it is considered food even now. And Rabi Shimon explicitly formulates the rule: *kol ha-omed li-fdot ke-fadu dami* — what stands to be redeemed is treated as redeemed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵיוָן דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּרֵיק לֵיהּ, שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר: כׇּל הָעוֹמֵד לִפְדּוֹת – כְּפָדוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can the flour be considered as not having a time in which it is fit for consumption merely because he has not redeemed it, even though he could have redeemed it? **Since** in a case **where if** he **wants,** he **may redeem it,** don't **we attribute to Rabbi Shimon that he said** that for **any** item **that stands to be redeemed,** it **is as if it** already **is redeemed?**

If Rabi Shimon holds this principle explicitly, then why doesn't the same logic apply to the wheat-and-olive *mincha* that can theoretically be redeemed?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: פָּרָה מְטַמְּאָה טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, הוֹאִיל וְהָיְתָה לָהּ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אוֹמֵר הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, פָּרָה נִפְדֵּית עַל גַּב מַעֲרַכְתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a source for this assertion: **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** A red **heifer,** even if it has been slaughtered and it is therefore prohibited to derive benefit from it, **is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, since it had a time in which it was fit. And Reish Lakish said,** explaining how it is possible to derive from this *halakha* that any item that could be redeemed is considered as though it has been redeemed: **Rabbi Shimon would say** that a red **heifer is redeemed** with money even when it has already been slaughtered and placed **upon its pyre** in preparation for being burned. If so, a meal offering that could be redeemed should also be considered fit for consumption, as it is considered as though it has been redeemed.

## Para vs. Menachot

### Para Always Redeemable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּרָה, עוֹמֶדֶת לִפְדּוֹת הִיא, שֶׁאִם מָצָא אַחֶרֶת נָאָה הֵימֶנָּה – מִצְוָה לִפְדּוֹתָהּ. אֶלָּא הָנֵי מְנָחוֹת, מִצְוָה לִפְדּוֹתָן?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **How can** these cases **be compared? Granted,** with regard to the red **heifer,** it **is** considered to be an item that **stands to be redeemed, since if he found another** animal **choicer than it,** there is **a mitzva to redeem** the first one and purchase the choicer one with the money. **But** is there **a mitzva to redeem these meal offerings?**

The *gemara* draws a sharp distinction: *hachi hashta — bishlama para omedet li-fdot hi* — the *parah adumah* is not merely theoretically redeemable; it genuinely and continuously stands in a state of potential redemption. The possibility of being redeemed is always live, always in the foreground, always operative.

### Better Para Search

*She-im matza acheret na'ah mi-menah mitzvah li-fdotah* — because if one finds a more beautiful *parah adumah*, there is a *mitzvah* to redeem the first one and use the superior animal. There is thus an ongoing obligation to seek a better animal and to redeem the first if one is found. This is not merely a legal technicality; it is an active religious imperative. The *parah adumah* is therefore genuinely "standing to be redeemed" in the full sense — we are, in effect, always looking for a reason to redeem it.

### No Menachot Search

*Ela hani menachot — mitzvah li-fdotan?* — but when it comes to *menachot*, is there any such ongoing search or imperative to redeem them and find a better offering? There is not. The scenario of a *mincha* sanctified while still growing is a narrow, unusual case, and there is no analogous institution of constantly seeking a superior *mincha* and redeeming the first. Therefore, the *mincha* does not enjoy the same status as the *parah adumah* under Rabi Shimon's principle. The *mincha*'s theoretical redeemability does not generate a genuine *sha'at ha-kosher*.

## Notar Contradiction

### Meat Unsprinkled

> **Hebrew/Aramac:** וְהָא לָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּמִצְוָה לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, וְאִי בָּעֵי זָרַק, וְקָתָנֵי דְּאֵין מִטַּמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** there is a case where sacrificial meat **remained** overnight **before the sprinkling** of the blood occurred, **where there was a mitzva to sprinkle** the blood the day before, **and if he had wanted** he **could have sprinkled** it, and the offering would not have been disqualified. **And** yet, Rabbi Shimon **teaches** in the *baraita* **that** sacrificial meat that remained overnight **is not susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** even though it should have been considered fit for consumption on the day the offering was slaughtered, as the blood stood to be sprinkled and there was a mitzva to sprinkle it.

The *gemara* now identifies an internal tension within Rabi Shimon's own position. Rabi Shimon said that meat that stayed overnight *before zerika* is not food. But from *shechita* until the moment of *zerika*, there is a *mitzvah* to sprinkle the blood — and if you had sprinkled it, the meat would have become fit to eat. Why doesn't the obligation to do the *zerika* — like the theoretical possibility of redeeming the *parah adumah* — count as a kind of standing readiness that makes the meat food even before the *zerika* is performed?

### Missing Earlier Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here, we are dealing with** a case **where there was not** sufficient **time remaining in the day to sprinkle** the blood, as the offering was slaughtered close to sunset. Therefore, the blood did not stand to be sprinkled and the meat was therefore never fit for consumption.

The *gemara* presses this further in a formal way: if Rabi Shimon's rule is that once there is an opportunity to do the *zerika* the meat is considered food, then the *baraita* should have presented a more subtle internal distinction within the category of meat that stayed overnight *lifnei zerika*. It should have said: *bameh devarim amurim* — when does this meat *not* count as food? *She-lo haytah she'ut ba-yom* — only when the animal was slaughtered with insufficient time remaining in the day to perform the *zerika* (e.g., immediately before sunset). *Aval haytah lo she'ut ba-yom — metamei tumat ochlin* — but if there was ample time during the day to perform the *zerika* and it was not done, then it would be food. The *baraita* does not say this; it simply says that meat before *zerika* is not food, full stop. This seems to imply that the theoretical ability to do the *zerika* does not affect the status.

### Opportunity vs. Obligati…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁהוּת בְּיוֹם, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דִּמְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** in a case where the offering was slaughtered when **there was** sufficient **time remaining in the day** to sprinkle the blood, **what** would then be the *halakha* according to Rabbi Shimon? Would meat left overnight **be susceptible to the ritual impurity of food?**

The *gemara* formally resolves this by saying *hachi nami ka'amar* — yes, that is in fact what is meant. The *baraita* is to be read as implying that if there was sufficient time to perform *zerika* and it was not done, the meat would be considered food. The distinction is between having had no opportunity at all (*shelo haytah she'ut ba-yom*) versus having had the opportunity. This resolution is forced — the *baraita* does not actually say this explicitly — and the *gemara* acknowledges the awkwardness by reading it into the text. But the upshot is: Rabi Shimon is understood to hold that the theoretical possibility of *zerika* does constitute a kind of *sha'at ha-kosher*, much as the possibility of redemption does for *parah adumah*.

## Piggul in Animals

### Piggul at Zerika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּתָנֵי לָן לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין – לִיפְלוֹג בְּדִידַהּ: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים? שֶׁלֹּא הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁהוּת בְּיוֹם, אֲבָל הָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁהוּת בַּיּוֹם – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין!
>
> **Translation:** If so, **rather than** Rabbi Shimon **teaching** the following: Sacrificial meat that was left overnight before the blood was sprinkled is not susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, but if **left overnight after** the **sprinkling** of the blood it **is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food,** instead **let** the *tanna* **distinguish within** the case **itself** in the following way: **In what** case **is this statement,** that if one renders an offering *piggul* the meat is not susceptible to the impurity of food, **said?** It is said in a case **where he rendered** it ***piggul* during** the rite of **slaughtering, but** if he **rendered** it ***piggul* during** the rite of **sprinkling,** it **is** susceptible to **the impurity of food.**

The *gemara* now applies this framework to *piggul* in animal sacrifices. If *piggul* was enacted at the moment of *zerika* — meaning the *kohen* had a disqualifying intention when sprinkling the blood — then between *shechita* and *zerika* there was an obligation to perform the *zerika* correctly, and the meat was theoretically fit to be eaten. Consistent with the principle just developed, this should mean that the meat is considered food, and *piggul* by *zerika* should be *metamei tumat ochlin*.

### Baraita Contrast

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּיגֵּל בְּמִנְחָה אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּפַיגֵּל בִּקְמִיצָה, דְּקוֹמֶץ בְּמִנְחָה כִּשְׁחִיטָה דָּמֵי, אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי – מְטַמֵּא טוּמְאַת אֳכָלִין, הוֹאִיל וְהָיְתָה לוֹ שְׁעַת הַכּוֹשֶׁר מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **It was necessary for him** to contrast it with a case of one who rendered **a meal offering *piggul*** in order to teach **that even though he** already **rendered it *piggul* at** the time of the **removal of the handful,** and the principle is that **the removal of the handful of a meal offering is equivalent to the slaughtering** of an animal offering, and an offering that was rendered *piggul* at the time of slaughtering is not susceptible to the impurity of food, **even so,** the meal offering **is susceptible to the ritual impurity of food, since it initially had a time that was fit for consumption,** when the flour was not yet consecrated as a meal offering.

The *gemara* then asks: if this is the case — if *piggul* by *zerika* would make the meat food — why does Rabi Shimon's *baraita* contrast *piggul* in animal sacrifice (which is not food) with *piggul* in a *mincha* (which is food)? It would have been more instructive to make an internal distinction within animal sacrifice itself: *piggul* at *shechita* (not food) versus *piggul* at *zerika* (food). Why bring in the *mincha* at all?

The *gemara* answers: *piggul bi-mincha itzterikha lei* — the *baraita* specifically wanted to teach the case of *piggul* in a *mincha*, and the reason is to highlight a specific parallel. The *gemara* wanted to contrast *shechita* of an animal (not food when *piggul*) with *kmitza* of a *mincha* (food even when *piggul*). The *kmitza* — the taking of the handful — is structurally analogous to *shechita* in animal sacrifice: both are the initial act that separates the portion destined for the *mizbe'ach* from the remainder.

### Shechita-Kmitza

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לָן מַמָּשׁ, וַאֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דְּפַיגֵּל בִּזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I related** this **discussion in the presence of Rav Naḥman** and explained Rabbi Shimon's opinion differently: **Even** if **you say** that the case in the *baraita* is one in which the meat was **actually left overnight** and there was time to sprinkle the blood during the day, **and even** if **you say** that he **rendered** the offering ***piggul* at** the time of the **sprinkling** of the blood rather than during the slaughtering, Rabbi Shimon does not consider those to be cases in which the offering had a time when it was fit for consumption.

Even though *kmitza* is analogous to *shechita*, the *mincha* is different because the flour and oil existed as ordinary food before the *kmitza* took place — indeed, before the entire process of offering began. The *mincha* therefore had a genuine *sha'at ha-kosher* from its earliest existence, even if at the moment of *piggul* (the *kmitza*) it was no longer fit. The *baraita* therefore made the contrast between *shechita*-of-animal and *kmitza*-of-mincha in order to highlight this structural parallel and the crucial difference in prior food-history. *Aval ein hachi nami* — and nonetheless, *piggul* by *zerika* in an animal sacrifice would also be *metamei tumat ochlin*, for the reasons just discussed.

## Rav Ashi Resolution

### Redeem vs. Sprinkle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִי בָּעֵי פָּרֵיק – אָמְרִינַן, אִי בָּעֵי הֲוָה זָרֵיק לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon **said** only that **if he had wanted,** he **would have redeemed** it, and therefore an item that stands to be redeemed is treated as if it were already redeemed. Redemption is simple and requires only a verbal statement. According to Rabbi Shimon **we do not say that if he had wanted,** he **would have sprinkled** it, i.e., that the sprinkling of the blood and similar actions that stand to take place are treated as having taken place already.

Rav Ashi offers the most satisfying resolution to the tensions that have accumulated throughout this discussion. He reported: *aminah la-shema'ta kamei de-Rav Nachman* — I stated this analysis before Rav Nachman. The core principle: *de-ei ba'ei parik amrinan; ei ba'ei hava zarik lo amrinan* — the fact that you could have redeemed it, we say; the fact that you could have thrown the blood, we do not say. This is the fundamental distinction that organizes Rabi Shimon's entire approach.

### Verbal vs. Act

> **Translation:** The operative consideration is not the state of the *mincha* but the current financial status of the obligant.
>
> Rashi (ten lines down in the narrow column) explains: *de-mechusar pidya lav ke-ma'aseh dami — devarim be-alma ninhu* — the act of redemption lacks the character of a formal act; it consists of words alone. To redeem something from *hekdesh*, one simply declares the transfer of sanctity from the object to money. There is no complex physical procedure, no intervening steps, no new ritual act required. The potential to redeem is therefore present here and now, fully accessible, needing only a verbal declaration.

The difference between these two cases is not merely technical. The act of redemption lacks the character of a formal act; it consists of words alone. To redeem something from *hekdesh*, one simply declares the transfer of sanctity from the object to money. There is no complex physical procedure, no intervening steps, no new ritual act required. The potential to redeem is therefore present here and now, fully accessible, needing only a verbal declaration.

### Here and Now

Because redemption requires only words — *devarim be-alma* — the potential to redeem is genuinely alive in the present moment. Rabi Shimon therefore treats what stands ready to be redeemed as if it has already been redeemed. The *sha'at ha-kosher* is visible, real, and immediate. It is not a hypothetical contingent on a future act; it is as present as a spoken word.

### Without Performance

By contrast, *de-ei ba'ei zarik lo amrinan* — the potential to sprinkle the blood is not treated in the same way. Sprinkling the blood is a full ritual act: it requires catching the blood at *shechita*, carrying it to the *mizbe'ach*, and performing the *zerika* correctly. Until that act is performed, the meat remains in its current state — not yet fit to be eaten, not yet food. The mere possibility that one could perform the *zerika* does not alter the present status of the meat, because the possibility is contingent on a complex act that has not yet occurred. If grandma had wheels she'd be a trolley car — the conditional is real, but the present reality is assessed as it actually stands.

Rav Ashi's resolution thus unifies the entire sugya. When Rabi Shimon says *kol ha-omed li-fdot ke-fadu dami*, he means it specifically for cases where the change in status requires only words. In those cases, the potential is treated as present reality. When the change requires a complex act — *zerika*, or any other ritual procedure — the potential is not treated as present reality until the act is actually performed. With this distinction in place, all the apparent contradictions within Rabi Shimon's position resolve cleanly, and the analysis of the *parah adumah*, the *mincha*, *piggul*, and *notar* all fall into coherent alignment.
