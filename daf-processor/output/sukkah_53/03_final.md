# Sukkah 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Menora & Priests

### Candelabra Height

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: גּוֹבְהָהּ שֶׁל מְנוֹרָה חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה, וְאַרְבָּעָה יְלָדִים שֶׁל פִּרְחֵי כְּהוּנָּה, וּבִידֵיהֶם כַּדֵּי שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים לוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages **taught: The height of the candelabrum is fifty cubits. And there were four children from the priesthood trainees holding and in their hands jugs of oil with a capacity of 120 *log* of oil.**

The daf opens in the middle of the discussion of *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah*, picking up from the bottom of 52b. A *tanna* teaches: *gova shel menora chamishim ama* — the height of the candelabra used for the illumination of the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* was fifty *amot*, comparable in stature to the tree from which Haman was hanged. Four young *kohanim* — *arba yeladim shepirchei kehunah* — would ascend on four separate ladders, each carrying pitchers of oil: *uveyadeihem kadei shemen shel me'ah v'esrim log*, a combined total of a hundred and twenty *log*.

### Oil Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים לוֹג כּוּלְּהוּ, אוֹ דִּלְמָא לְכׇל חַד וְחַד? תָּא שְׁמַע: וּבִידֵיהֶם כַּדֵּי שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל שְׁלֹשִׁים שְׁלֹשִׁים לוֹג, שֶׁהֵם כּוּלָּם מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים לוֹג.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised:** Was it **120 *log* altogether, or perhaps each and every child carried that amount? Come and hear proof from this *baraita*: And in their hands were jugs of oil, each with a capacity of thirty *log*, that were all together 120 *log*.**

The *gemara* raises a question: *me'ah v'esrim log kulhu o dilma l'chol chad v'chad* — was the hundred and twenty *log* the total carried collectively, or did each priest carry a hundred and twenty *log* individually? The latter figure, roughly thirteen or fourteen gallons per person, would have been extraordinary. A *braita* resolves the matter: *uveyadeihem kadei shemen shel shloshim shloshim log* — each carried thirty *log*, and *she'kulam me'ah v'esrim log*, together they amounted to a hundred and twenty *log*. This is a more manageable figure, approximately three to five gallons per person.

### Martha's Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: וְהֵן מְשׁוּבָּחִין הָיוּ יוֹתֵר מִבְּנָהּ שֶׁל מָרְתָּא בַּת בַּיְיתּוֹס. אָמְרוּ עַל בְּנָהּ שֶׁל מָרְתָּא בַּת בַּיְיתּוֹס שֶׁהָיָה נוֹטֵל שְׁתֵּי יְרֵיכוֹת שֶׁל שׁוֹר הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁלָּקוּחַ בְּאֶלֶף זוּז, וּמְהַלֵּךְ עָקֵב בְּצַד גּוּדָל. וְלֹא הֱנִיחוּהוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן, מִשּׁוּם: ״בְּרׇב עָם הַדְרַת מֶלֶךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** One of the Sages **taught: And these young priests who held the pitchers were superior in strength to the son of Marta, daughter of Baitos, who was a priest renowned for his might. They said about the son of Marta, daughter of Baitos, that he would take two thighs of a large bull that was so large that it would be purchased for one thousand *zuz*, and walk up the ramp in small steps, heel to toe, without hurrying, due to his strength. However, his brethren the priests would not allow him do so, due to the principle: "In the multitude of people is the King's glory"** (Proverbs 14:28). The more priests engaged in the Temple service, the greater glory for God. Therefore, it is preferable for the thighs to be carried to the altar by multiple priests.

The *braita* continues with a striking comparison: *v'hem meshubachim hayu yoseir mibnasha d'Mirta bas Paitos* — these priests were considered more praiseworthy, in the specific sense of stronger, than the son of Marta bat Boethus. The tradition relates that this man would take *shtei yerechot shel shor hagadol*, the two thighs of a large ox purchased for a thousand *zuz*, and walk up the ramp to offer them, placing heel immediately beside the big toe of the next foot — tiny, deliberate steps — while bearing this enormous weight. His brother priests, however, would not permit him to do so, invoking the principle of *b'rov am hadrat melech*: the greater the number of participants, the greater the glory to God. The task was to be divided appropriately rather than performed as a feat of individual strength.

### Ramp vs Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מְשׁוּבָּחִים? אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם יוּקְרָא — הָנֵי יַקִּירִי טְפֵי! אֶלָּא: הָתָם כֶּבֶשׁ, וּמְרוּבָּע, וְלָא זְקִיף. הָכָא סוּלָּמוֹת, וּזְקִיף טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In **what sense were these young priests superior? If we say it is due to the weight of the pitchers that they carried, these two thighs are heavier** than the thirty *log* of oil. The Gemara answers: **Rather, the difference is that there, in the case of the son of Marta, he walked on a ramp that was wide, and with a moderate gradient of only one cubit every four cubits of length, and it is not steep; here they climbed ladders, and those are very steep.**

This raises the question: *mai meshubachim* — in what way were the oil-carrying priests actually superior? If the argument rests on weight, it fails, for *hani yakiri tfei* — the two thighs of a large ox are considerably heavier than thirty *log* of oil. The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the physical challenge: *hasham kevesh u'meruba v'lo zakif* — there, in the case of Martha's son, the ascent was via a ramp, angled and not perpendicular; *hacha sulamot v'zakif tuva* — here, the priests climbed ladders that were nearly vertical. Even if the load was lighter, ascending almost straight up with oil is a far greater physical challenge than walking up a gradual incline.

One question raised in passing is worth noting: it is well known that Marta bat Boethus married Yehoshua ben Gamla, who became *kohen gadol*. Why then does the tradition identify the son by his mother's name rather than his father's? The answer remains uncertain — perhaps the son was from a prior marriage — and the *gemara* does not resolve it.

## Simchat Choreography

### Light & Illumination

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָיָה חָצֵר בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם. תָּנָא: אִשָּׁה הָיְתָה בּוֹרֶרֶת חִטִּים לָאוֹר שֶׁל בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And there was not a courtyard in Jerusalem that was not illuminated from the light of the Place of the Drawing of the Water. One of the Sages taught: A woman would be able to sort wheat by the light of the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water.**

The *mishnah* had stated that there was no courtyard in Jerusalem that was not illuminated by the light of the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah*. Situated on *Har HaBayit* at an elevated position, the light from the fifty-*amah* candelabra radiated throughout the city. A *braita* illustrates the intensity of this illumination: *isha hayta boreret chitim le'or Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — a woman could sort and sift wheat, separating even the finest chaff, by the light of the celebration alone.

### Torch Juggling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲסִידִים וְאַנְשֵׁי מַעֲשֶׂה כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, יֵשׁ מֵהֶן אוֹמְרִים: אַשְׁרֵי יַלְדוּתֵנוּ שֶׁלֹּא בִּיְּישָׁה אֶת זִקְנוּתֵנוּ — אֵלּוּ חֲסִידִים וְאַנְשֵׁי מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **The pious and the men of action would dance before the people who attended the celebration. The Sages taught in the *Tosefta* that some of them would say in their song praising God: Happy is our youth, as we did not sin then, that did not embarrass our old age. These are the pious and the men of action,** who spent all their lives engaged in Torah and mitzvot.

The *mishnah* describes how *chasidim ve'anshei ma'aseh* — the righteous and those of good deeds — would take center stage in the rejoicing, dancing and juggling torches while singing words of praise. The *levi'im* would accompany on their musical instruments.

### Songs of Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֵשׁ מֵהֶן אוֹמְרִים: אַשְׁרֵי זִקְנוּתֵנוּ שֶׁכִּפְּרָה אֶת יַלְדוּתֵנוּ — אֵלּוּ בַּעֲלֵי תְשׁוּבָה. אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹמְרִים: אַשְׁרֵי מִי שֶׁלֹּא חָטָא, וּמִי שֶׁחָטָא — יָשׁוּב וְיִמְחוֹל לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And some would say: Happy is our old age, that atoned for our youth when we sinned. These are the penitents. Both these and those say: Happy is he who did not sin; and he who sinned should repent and God will absolve him.**

A *braita* records two distinct categories of song sung during the celebration. Some would say: *ashrei yaldutenu shelo biysha et ziknutenu* — praised is our youth, which brought no shame upon our old age. These are identified as *chasidim ve'anshei ma'aseh*, those who had been righteous throughout their lives. Others would say: *ashrei ziknutenu she'kapra al yaldutenu* — praised is our old age, which atoned for our youth. These are *ba'alei teshuva*, those who had lived more recklessly in their younger years and had since returned.

### Repentance Teaching

Both groups together would say: *ashrei mi shelo chata* — praised is the one who has never sinned — *u'mi shechata yashuv ve'yimchol lo* — and one who has sinned should repent, and it will be forgiven. This formulation coexists in some tension with the well-known teaching that *ba'alei teshuva* occupy a place that even the perfectly righteous cannot attain. Nevertheless, the *gemara* here affirms that it is still preferable never to have sinned in the first place.

## Hillel's Teaching

### Hillel's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל הִלֵּל הַזָּקֵן, כְּשֶׁהָיָה שָׂמֵחַ בְּשִׂמְחַת בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה, אָמַר כֵּן: אִם אֲנִי כָּאן — הַכֹּל כָּאן, וְאִם אֵינִי כָּאן — מִי כָּאן. הוּא הָיָה אוֹמֵר כֵּן: מָקוֹם שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹהֵב — שָׁם רַגְלַי מוֹלִיכוֹת אוֹתִי. אִם תָּבֹא אֶל בֵּיתִי — אֲנִי אָבֹא אֶל בֵּיתֶךָ, אִם אַתָּה לֹא תָּבֹא אֶל בֵּיתִי — אֲנִי לֹא אָבֹא אֶל בֵּיתֶךָ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּכׇל הַמָּקוֹם אֲשֶׁר אַזְכִּיר אֶת שְׁמִי אָבֹא אֵלֶיךָ וּבֵרַכְתִּיךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in the *Tosefta*: They said about Hillel the Elder that when he was rejoicing at the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water he said this: If I am here, everyone is here; and if I am not here, who is here? He would also say this: To the place that I love, there my feet take me, and therefore, I come to the Temple. And the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: If you come to My house, I will come to your house; if you do not come to My house, I will not come to your house, as it is stated: "In every place that I cause My name to be mentioned, I will come to you and bless you"** (Exodus 20:21).

A *braita* records that about Hillel the Elder it was said: *keshehaya same'ach be'Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — when he would rejoice at the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — *amar ken: im ani kan hakol kan, ve'im eini kan mi kan* — he would say: "If I am here, everyone is here; and if I am not here, who is here?" Taken at face value, this sounds strikingly arrogant. After all, Hillel's other celebrated statement — *im ein ani li mi li, u'cheshe'ani le'atzmi mah ani* — "If I am not for myself, who will be for me? But if I am only for myself, what am I?" — is a carefully balanced formulation. The present statement, by contrast, seems to simply repeat the same self-centered point twice. The resolution, as *Rashi* explains, is that Hillel is not speaking in his own voice.

### God's Presence

*Rashi* explains: *doresh haya lerabbim shelo yechete'u bishmo shel HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — Hillel was teaching the masses in the name of God, speaking as it were in the divine voice. The full meaning is: *kol zman she'ani chafetz babayit hazeh u'shchinati sruya bo* — as long as I, God, desire this house and My presence dwells within it — *yihiye kevodo kayam ve'yavo hakol kan* — its glory will endure and all will come here. *Ve'im techete'u ve'asallek shchinati, mi yavo kan* — but if you sin and My *shekhinah* departs, who will come here?

This is a pointed message delivered in the very midst of celebration. The *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* is a time of tremendous joy and communal gathering, a moment when it would be easy to lose sight of the deeper reality: this place matters not because it is a magnificent venue for celebration, but because of the divine presence that inhabits it. If that presence departs, the gathering means nothing. The parallel to the role a *beit knesset* has always played — simultaneously a gathering place and a *mikdash me'at*, a site of worship and divine presence — is instructive.

### Reciprocal Homes

Hillel would also say: *lemakom she'ani ohev, sham raglai molichot oti* — the place that I love, there my legs will bring me. *Im tavo el beiti, ani avo el beitecha; im atah lo tavo el beiti, ani lo avo el beitecha* — if you come to my house, I will come to your house; if you do not come to my house, I will not come to your house. Again Hillel speaks in God's voice: if the Jewish people come to the *Beit HaMikdash*, God in turn will bring His presence into their homes. The verse cited in support is *bechol hamakom asher azkir et shemi avo elecha uverachticha* — wherever I cause My name to be invoked, I will come to you and bless you.

This teaching resonates beautifully with the broader themes of *Sukkot* — the *sukkah* as simultaneously a human dwelling and an evocation of the *ananei kavod*, the divine clouds of glory and the imagery of the *Mikdash*. The *sukkah* represents precisely this intersection: coming to God's house and, in turn, receiving God's presence within one's own.

## Skull & Angel

### Floating Skull

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף הוּא רָאָה גֻּלְגּוֹלֶת אַחַת שֶׁצָּפָה עַל פְּנֵי הַמַּיִם, אֲמַר לַהּ: עַל דְּאַטֵּפְתְּ אַטְפוּךְ וּמְטִיפַיִךְ יְטוּפוּן. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַגְלוֹהִי דְּבַר אִינִישׁ אִינּוּן עָרְבִין בֵּיהּ, לַאֲתַר דְּמִיתְבְּעֵי — תַּמָּן מוֹבִילִין יָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another statement of Hillel the Elder. **Additionally, he saw one skull that was floating on the water and he said to it: Because you drowned others, they drowned you, and those that drowned you will be drowned. That is the way of the world; everyone is punished measure for measure. Apropos following one's feet, Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The feet of a person are responsible for him; to the place where he is in demand, there they lead him.**

At this point the *gemara* introduces the passage familiar from Pirkei Avot: *af hu ra'ah gulgoret achat shetzafah al pnei hamayim* — Hillel also once saw a skull floating on the water, and said to it: *al de'atpacht atpuch, ve'sof mativeych yetufun* — because you drowned others, you were drowned; and those who drowned you will themselves be drowned. The message is one of cosmic justice: suffering does not arrive without cause, and those who inflict it will ultimately face it themselves.

## Hillel's Teaching (II)

### Legs Guarantors

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ תַּרְתֵּי כּוּשָׁאֵי דַּהֲווֹ קָיְימִי קַמֵּיהּ שְׁלֹמֹה, אֱלִיחֹרֶף וַאֲחִיָּה בְּנֵי שִׁישָׁא, סוֹפְרִים דִּשְׁלֹמֹה הֲווֹ. יוֹמָא חַד חַזְיֵיהּ מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת דַּהֲוָה קָא עֲצִיב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי עֲצִיבַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּקָא בָעוּ מִינַּאי הָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי כּוּשָׁאֵי דְּיָתְבִי הָכָא. מַסְרִינְהוּ לִשְׂעִירִים, שַׁדְּרִינְהוּ לְמָחוֹזָא דְלוּז. כִּי מְטוֹ לְמָחוֹזָא דְלוּז — שְׁכִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates with regard to **these two Cushites who would stand before Solomon: "Elihoreph and Ahijah, the sons of Shisha"** (I Kings 4:3), and **they were scribes of Solomon. One day Solomon saw that the Angel of Death was sad. He said to him: Why are you sad? He said to him: They are asking me to take the lives of these two Cushites who are sitting here. Solomon handed them to the demons in his service, and sent them to the district of Luz,** where the Angel of Death has no dominion. **When they arrived at the district of Luz, they died.**

Rabbi Yochanan offers a related reflection on the phrase *sham raglai molichot oti* — there my legs bring me: *ragloi d'var enash inun arvin bei* — a person's legs are his guarantors — *l'atar d'mitbe'i taman movilin yatei* — to the place where he is needed, they bring him. This is not merely a romantic statement about going where one loves; it carries a darker undertone. A person is brought by his legs to the place he must go — ultimately, to the place of his death.

## Skull & Angel (II)

### Shlomo & Luz

The *gemara* now provides the narrative that gives this teaching its full force. There were two Kushim — *Rashi* notes the term is used here, notwithstanding its generally negative connotation in rabbinic literature, to indicate that they were remarkably distinguished individuals — who served as scribes before King Shlomo, named Elihoreph and Achiyah, sons of Shisha, as recorded in Melachim. One day Shlomo perceived that the Angel of Death was deeply sorrowful. When he asked why, the Angel replied: *de-ka ba'u minei hani tartei Kushei* — I have been tasked with taking these two Kushim, *ve-yasvi hakha* — and they are sitting right here. Shlomo, moved to protect them, entrusted them to the *shedim* and sent them to the city of Luz — *shadruhu le-mahoza de-Luz*. As the *gemara* in Sotah records, Luz was a place over which the Angel of Death had no dominion; no one died there.

### Angel Laughs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִמְחַר, חַזְיֵהּ מַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת דַּהֲוָה קָבָדַח! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי בְּדִיחַתְּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בַּאֲתַר דִּבְעוֹ מִינַּאי, תַּמָּן שַׁדַּרְתִּינְהוּ. מִיָּד פָּתַח שְׁלֹמֹה וְאָמַר: רַגְלוֹהִי דְּבַר אִינִישׁ אִינּוּן עָרְבִין בֵּיהּ, לַאֲתַר דְּמִיתְבְּעֵי — תַּמָּן מוֹבִילִין יָתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The following day, Solomon saw that the Angel of Death was happy. He said to him: Why are you happy? He replied: In the place that they asked me to take them, there you sent them. The Angel of Death was instructed to take their lives in the district of Luz. Since they resided in Solomon's palace and never went to Luz, he was unable to complete his mission. That saddened him. Ultimately, Solomon dispatched them to Luz, enabling the angel to accomplish his mission. That pleased him. Immediately, Solomon began to speak and said: The feet of a person are responsible for him; to the place where he is in demand, there they lead him.**

When the two men arrived at Luz, however — *ki matu le-mahoza de-Luz* — they died at its very gates, just before crossing into the city's protection. The next day Shlomo saw the Angel of Death, this time laughing. When he asked why, the Angel replied: *be-asra de-ba'u minei taman shadartinu* — the very place where I needed to take them, that is where you sent them. Shlomo had unwittingly delivered them to the precise location the Angel of Death had been assigned. Immediately, Shlomo declared: *raglohi de-var inish inun arvin bei, le-asra de-misba'ei taman movilin yatei* — the legs of a person are his guarantors; to the place where he is needed, there they bring him. The point is sobering: a person cannot outrun the destination decreed for him. His own legs will carry him there.

## Juggling Acts

### Torches & Kida

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, כְּשֶׁהָיָה שָׂמֵחַ שִׂמְחַת בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה, הָיָה נוֹטֵל שְׁמֹנֶה אֲבוּקוֹת שֶׁל אוֹר, וְזוֹרֵק אַחַת וְנוֹטֵל אַחַת וְאֵין נוֹגְעוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ. וּכְשֶׁהוּא מִשְׁתַּחֲוֶה, נוֹעֵץ שְׁנֵי גּוּדָלָיו בָּאָרֶץ וְשׁוֹחֶה וְנוֹשֵׁק אֶת הָרִצְפָּה וְזוֹקֵף, וְאֵין כׇּל בְּרִיָּה יְכוֹלָה לַעֲשׂוֹת כֵּן, וְזוֹ הִיא קִידָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It is taught in a *baraita*: They said about Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel that when he would rejoice at the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water, he would take eight flaming torches and toss one and catch another,** juggling them, **and, though all were in the air at the same time, they would not touch each other. And when he would prostrate himself, he would insert his two thumbs into the ground, and bow, and kiss the floor of the courtyard and straighten, and there was not any other creature that could do that due to the extreme difficulty involved. And this was the form of bowing called *kidda* performed by the High Priest.**

The *gemara* returns to the theme of the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* and the acts performed during the celebration. It is related that Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel, *keshehaya same'ach simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — when he would rejoice at the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — *haya notel shemonah avukot shel ur* — would take eight torches of flame — *ve-zorek achat ve-notel achat ve-ein nog'ot zu be-zu* — throw one and catch the next, the torches moving in a perfect circle without ever touching one another. He would then perform the *kida* prostration: *ve-no'etz shnei gudlav ba-aretz* — he would plant his two thumbs in the ground (not the flat of his palms), *ve-shocheh ve-noshek et ha-ritzpah* — bow down to kiss the floor in the manner of a prostration done entirely on the thumbs — *ve-zokef* — and then push himself back upright. *Ve-ein kol beryah yecholah la'asot kein* — no other creature could do this.

### Levi's Knives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי אַחְוִי קִידָּה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי וְאִיטְּלַע. וְהָא גְּרַמָא לֵיהּ? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לְעוֹלָם אַל יָטִיחַ אָדָם דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה, שֶׁהֲרֵי אָדָם גָּדוֹל הֵטִיחַ דְּבָרִים כְּלַפֵּי מַעְלָה וְאִיטְּלַע, וּמַנּוּ — לֵוִי. הָא וְהָא גְּרַמָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Levi demonstrated a *kidda* before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and strained his thigh and came up lame. And is that what caused him to be lame? But didn't Rabbi Elazar say: One should never speak impertinently toward God above; as a great person once spoke impertinently toward God above, and even though his prayers were answered, he was still punished and came up lame. And who was this great person? It was Levi. Apparently his condition was not caused by his bow. The Gemara answers: There is no contradiction. Both this and that caused him to come up lame; because he spoke impertinently toward God, he therefore was injured when exerting himself in demonstrating *kidda*.**

The *gemara* notes that Levi once demonstrated *kida* before Rabbi, after the destruction of the *Beit HaMikdash*, to show what this act entailed — and *ve-itla*, he was injured, becoming lame as a result. The *gemara* raises the question whether the lameness was caused by the demonstration itself. It cites Rabbi Elazar's teaching: *le-olam al yati'ach adam devarim klapei ma'alah* — a person should never cast complaints heavenward — *she-harei adam gadol hitich devarim klapei ma'alah ve-itla, u-manu Levi* — for a great person once did so and became lame, and that was Levi. *Rashi* references the passage in Ta'anit where Levi said before God: *alita ve-yashavta lach ba-marom ve-eincha mashgi'ach al banecha* — You have ascended and sat in the heights and are not paying attention to what is happening to Your children. The *gemara* concludes: *ha ve-ha garmu lei* — both factors contributed, the complaint against God and the physical strain of the *kida* demonstration.

### Wine Juggling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵוִי הֲוָה מְטַיֵּיל קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי בְּתַמְנֵי סַכִּינֵי. שְׁמוּאֵל קַמֵּיהּ שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא בִּתְמָנְיָא מְזָגֵי חַמְרָא. אַבָּיֵי קַמֵּיהּ (דְּרָבָא) בִּתְמָנְיָא בֵּיעֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּאַרְבְּעָה בֵּיעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the rejoicing of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel at the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water, the Gemara recounts: **Levi would walk before Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi juggling with eight knives. Shmuel would juggle before King Shapur with eight glasses of wine without spilling. Abaye would juggle before Rabba with eight eggs. Some say he did so with four eggs.** All these were cited.

Continuing with Levi: *Levi hava metayel kamei d'Rebbi b'tamnei skinei* — Levi would perform before Rabbi, juggling eight knives. *Rashi* clarifies that this was not at the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* itself but at some form of celebration in Rabbi's home. *Rashi* adds a poignant detail: Rabbi was the one who bore perpetual anxiety for the welfare of *Klal Yisrael*, and Levi was attempting to cheer him up — *yoma de-michaich bei Rebbi asi puranus le-olam*, a day on which Rabbi would laugh, disaster would befall the world. One assumes this juggling took place before Levi sustained his injury, though the *gemara* does not say so explicitly.

Shmuel, *kamei de-Shvor Malka b'tamnei mazgei chamra* — would juggle eight cups of wine before King Shapur. How one juggles a filled cup of wine without spilling it strains the imagination, yet the *gemara* presents it matter-of-factly.

Abaye, *kamei de-Rava b'tamneya beyei* — before Rava with eight eggs — *ve-amri lah b'arba beyei* — and some say four eggs. There is a clear progression in the tradition: from torches to knives to wine cups to eggs, from eight to four. The *gemara* is essentially presenting a descending ladder of danger and difficulty, and the point is implicit: if one wishes to practice juggling, one is better advised to start with eggs.

## Sleepless Rejoicing

### No Sleep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָה: כְּשֶׁהָיִינוּ שְׂמֵחִים שִׂמְחַת בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה, לֹא רָאִינוּ שֵׁינָה בְּעֵינֵינוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya said: When we would rejoice in the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water, we did not see sleep in our eyes the entire Festival.**

A *braita* records the testimony of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya: *kshehayinu smeichim Simchat Beit HaShoeivah, lo ra'inu sheina b'eineinu* — when we would rejoice at the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah*, our eyes would not see sleep.

### Daily Schedule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? שָׁעָה רִאשׁוֹנָה תָּמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, מִשָּׁם לִתְפִלָּה, מִשָּׁם לְקׇרְבַּן מוּסַף, מִשָּׁם לִתְפִלַּת הַמּוּסָפִין, מִשָּׁם לְבֵית הַמִּדְרָשׁ, מִשָּׁם לַאֲכִילָה וּשְׁתִיָּה, מִשָּׁם לִתְפִלַּת הַמִּנְחָה, מִשָּׁם לְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ לְשִׂמְחַת בֵּית הַשּׁוֹאֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **How so? The first hour of the day, the daily morning offering was sacrificed and everyone came to watch. From there they proceeded to engage in prayer in the synagogue; from there, to watch the sacrifice of the additional offerings; from there, to the synagogue to recite the additional prayer. From there they would proceed to the study hall to study Torah; from there to the eating and drinking in the *sukka*; from there to the afternoon prayer. From there they would proceed to the daily afternoon offering in the Temple. From this point forward, they proceeded to the Celebration of the Place of the Drawing of the Water.**

He explains the schedule that made this possible. The first hour of the day was the morning *tamid shel Shachar*; then *tefillah*; then witnessing the *korban musaf*; then *tefillat hamusafin*; then study in the *beit midrash*; then eating and drinking. Notably, no mention is made of the *sukkah*, despite the fact that it is *Sukkot* and eating presumably took place in one. Then *tefillat mincha*; then the *tamid shel bein ha'arbayim*; and then, once the day's sacrificial service was concluded, *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* — which would last through the night until the cycle began again.

### Dozing Reality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא אִישַׁן שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים״ — מַלְקִין אוֹתוֹ וְיָשֵׁן לְאַלְתַּר! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: לֹא טָעַמְנוּ טַעַם שֵׁינָה, דַּהֲווֹ מְנַמְנְמִי אַכַּתְפָּא דַהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara wonders: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say: One who took an oath that I will not sleep three days, one flogs him immediately for taking an oath in vain, and he may sleep immediately because it is impossible to stay awake for three days uninterrupted. Rather, this is what Rabbi Yehoshua is saying: We did not experience the sense of actual sleep, because they would merely doze on each other's shoulders.** In any case, they were not actually awake for the entire week.

The *gemara* challenges this: *ve-eini*? Is this truly possible? For Rabbi Yochanan taught that if someone takes an oath not to sleep for three days, he is given lashes immediately and may sleep at once, because the oath is impossible to fulfill — three days without sleep is beyond human capacity. How then could these celebrations last five or six days without sleep? The *gemara* resolves: *ela hachi ka'amar: lo ta'amnu ta'am sheina* — what Rabbi Yehoshua meant was not that they slept not at all, but that they never truly tasted deep, satisfying sleep. *Dahavu minamnamai akatfei d'hadadi* — they would doze off on one another's shoulders, but a proper restful sleep was never had during the entire festival.

## Fifteen Steps

### Steps Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְהָהוּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן דַּהֲוָה קָמְסַדַּר אַגָּדָתָא קַמֵּיהּ, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁמִיעַ לְךָ, הָנֵי חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת, כְּנֶגֶד מִי אֲמָרָם דָּוִד?
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: The musicians would stand on the **fifteen stairs** that descend from the Israelites' courtyard to the Women's Courtyard, corresponding to the fifteen Songs of the Ascents in Psalms. **Rav Ḥisda said to one of the Sages who was organizing *aggada* before him: Did you hear with regard to these fifteen Songs of Ascents in Psalms, corresponding to what did David say them?**

The *mishnah* introduces the fifteen steps — *chamesh esrei ma'alot* — that led from the *Ezrat Nashim* up to the *Ezrat Yisrael*. These steps corresponded to the fifteen *Shirei HaMa'alot*, the Songs of Ascent in Tehillim, and it was on these steps that the *levi'im* would stand during the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah*. *Rashi* notes that these steps were on the western side of the *Ezrat Nashim* as it opened into the *Ezrat Yisrael*, and that the *levi'im* standing there were not in the position for the regular *korban* service — that was the eastern end — but specifically for this celebration.

### Songs Origin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכָּרָה דָּוִד שִׁיתִין, קְפָא תְּהוֹמָא וּבָעֵי לְמִשְׁטְפֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא. אָמַר דָּוִד חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת וְהוֹרִידָן. אִי הָכִי, חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה ״מַעֲלוֹת״ — ״יוֹרְדוֹת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** He said to him that **this is what Rabbi Yoḥanan said: At the time that David dug the drainpipes in the foundation of the Temple, the waters of the depths rose and sought to inundate the world.** Immediately, **David recited the fifteen Songs of the Ascents and caused them to subside.** Rav Ḥisda asked: **If so, should they be called fifteen Songs of the Ascents? They should have been called Songs of the Descents.**

Rav Chisda posed a question to a scholar who specialized in *agadatta* and was presenting traditional homiletic material before him: *shemia lach hani chamisar ma'alot keneged mi amaran David* — have you heard what the fifteen *Shirei HaMa'alot* correspond to? The scholar replied in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *beshah shekarah David shitin* — when David dug the *shitin*, those underground caverns beneath the *mizbei'ach*. The *gemara* had previously recorded three opinions about the *shitin* — that they were a natural formation, that they were dug, or that they were simply a hollow in the *mizbei'ach* where the wine would drain — but this tradition proceeds on the assumption that they were dug by David himself.

What happened was this: *kafa tehoma* — David dug too deep and broke through into the underground depths, the *tehom*, and the waters threatened to burst forth and flood the world. David recited fifteen songs of ascent and the waters receded. Rav Chisda immediately objected: if the songs brought the waters back down, they should be called songs of *descent*, not songs of ascent — *achutei mibai leh*! The scholar who had conveyed the tradition replied: *ah, ve'izkartan* — you've helped me remember something. Let me correct the account.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הוֹאִיל וְאַדְכַּרְתַּן (מִלְּתָא), הָכִי אִתְּמַר: בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁכָּרָה דָּוִד שִׁיתִין, קְפָא תְּהוֹמָא וּבְעָא לְמִשְׁטְפֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא. אָמַר דָּוִד: מִי אִיכָּא דְּיָדַע אִי שְׁרֵי לְמִכְתַּב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא, וְנִשְׁדְּיֵהּ בִּתְהוֹמָא וּמַנַּח! לֵיכָּא דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ מִידֵּי. אֲמַר דָּוִד: כׇּל דְּיָדַע לְמֵימַר וְאֵינוֹ אוֹמֵר, יֵחָנֵק בִּגְרוֹנוֹ. נָשָׂא אֲחִיתוֹפֶל קַל וָחוֹמֶר בְּעַצְמוֹ: וּמָה לַעֲשׂוֹת שָׁלוֹם בֵּין אִישׁ לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: שְׁמִי שֶׁנִּכְתַּב בִּקְדוּשָּׁה יִמָּחֶה עַל הַמַּיִם, לַעֲשׂוֹת שָׁלוֹם לְכׇל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ — עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥisda continued and **said to him: Since you reminded me of this matter, this is what was originally stated: At the time that David dug the drainpipes, the waters of the depths rose and sought to inundate the world. David said: Is there anyone who knows whether it is permitted to write the sacred name on an earthenware shard? If it is permitted, we will write it and throw it into the depths, and they will subside. There was no one who said anything to him. David said: Anyone who knows what to say and does not say anything may he be strangled in his throat. Then Ahithophel raised an *a fortiori* argument on his own and said: And just as in order to make peace between a man and his wife in the case of *sota*, when the husband suspects his wife of having committed adultery, the Torah said: My Name that was written in sanctity will be erased on the water to establish peace for the whole world in its entirety, all the more so it is permitted. He said to David: It is permitted.**

Here is the corrected version: when David dug the *shitin* and the *tehom* burst forth threatening to flood the world, David asked: *mi ika de-yada ee sharei lemichtav shem achaspa ve-nishdi bitehoma u-manach* — is there anyone who knows whether it is permissible to write the divine name on a piece of pottery and cast it into the depths to stop them up? No one answered. David then declared: *kol ha-yodeia lomar ve-eino omer yichanek bi-gerono* — anyone who knows the answer and is not saying it, let him be strangled in his throat.

At this, Achitofel constructed a *kal va-chomer*: if the Torah permitted the divine name — written in sanctity — to be dissolved in water in order to make peace between a husband and his wife in the case of a *sotah*, then all the more so should writing the name on pottery and casting it into the depths be permitted in order to restore peace to the entire world. *Amar lei: shari* — David was told: it is permissible.

### Water Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּתֵב שֵׁם אַחַסְפָּא וְשָׁדֵי לִתְהוֹמָא, וּנְחֵית תְּהוֹמָא שִׁיתְּסַר אַלְפֵי גַּרְמִידֵי. כִּי חָזֵי דְּנָחֵית טוּבָא, אֲמַר: כַּמָּה דְּמִידְּלֵי — טְפֵי מִירְטַב עָלְמָא. אֲמַר חֲמֵשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה מַעֲלוֹת, וְאַסְּקֵיהּ חֲמֵיסַר אַלְפֵי גַּרְמִידֵי, וְאוֹקְמֵיהּ בְּאַלְפָּא גַּרְמִידֵי. אָמַר עוּלָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ סוּמְכָא דְאַרְעָא אַלְפָּא גַּרְמִידֵי. וְהָא חָזֵינַן דְּכָרֵינַן פּוּרְתָּא וְנָפְקִי מַיָּא! אָמַר רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: הָהוּא מִסּוּלָּמָא דִפְרָת.
>
> **Translation:** **He wrote the sacred name on an earthenware shard and cast it into the depths, and the waters in the depths subsided sixteen thousand cubits. When he saw that they subsided excessively, he said: The higher the waters in the aquifers, the moister and more fertile the soil of the world. He recited the fifteen Songs of the Ascents and elevated them fifteen thousand cubits, and established them at a depth of one thousand cubits. Ulla said: Learn from here that the thickness of the earth above the waters of the depths is one thousand cubits. The Gemara asks: But don't we see that when we dig a little, significantly less than one thousand cubits, water emerges? Rav Mesharshiyya said: That is from the ascent of the Euphrates River, which flows at a higher altitude than do other rivers. The water flows up through underground passages to reach the river. That is why water emerges when one digs in the hills of Babylonia.**

The name was written on a shard of pottery — *Rashi* explains that pottery sinks rather than floats, making it suitable for this purpose — and cast into the *tehom*. The *gemara* also preserves an additional dimension cited by *Rashi*: the shard David discovered upon digging was not an ordinary piece of pottery but bore an inscription indicating it had been in place since the six days of creation. By disturbing this primordial seal, David allowed the waters to surge, and it was now necessary to return something to that place.

The *tehom* receded — but it receded too far. It went down *shiteisar alfei garmidi* — sixteen thousand *amot*. When it was observed that the waters had descended so dramatically, concern arose in the other direction: *kama de-midlei tefei mirtav alma* — the closer the depths are to the surface, the more moisture reaches the earth above, which is beneficial for the world. David then recited the fifteen *Shirei HaMa'alot*, which raised the water back up *chamesh esreh alfei garmidi* — fifteen thousand *amot* — *ve-ukmeih be-alfei garmidi* — leaving it a thousand *amot* below the earth's surface. Close enough to benefit the world through moisture, but far enough to pose no danger of flooding.

Ulla derived a ruling from this episode: *shema mina ovyah de-ara alfei garmidi* — we learn that the depth of the earth's crust, from the surface down to the *tehom*, is a thousand *amot*, since that is where the *tehom* now rests. The *gemara* raises an obvious objection: we dig far less than a thousand *amot* and reach water — wells are not nearly that deep. Rav Mesharshiya responds: *hahu misuma de-Prat* — those are waters that come through the underground channels of the Prat, the Euphrates. *Rashi* explains that the Prat originates from a great height, and as recorded in Bechorot, *kol ha-neharot le-mata mi-Prat* — all rivers lie lower than the Prat in elevation. Waters from a high source can travel through subterranean veins and emerge at considerable distances, even reaching a similar elevation to their source. What one finds in wells dug in the lowlands of Bavel are these tributary channels of the Prat — not the waters of the *tehom* itself, which lies a full thousand *amot* below the surface.

## Water Procession

### Step Counting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעָמְדוּ כֹּהֲנִים בְּשַׁעַר הָעֶלְיוֹן שֶׁיּוֹרֵד כּוּ׳. בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: לְמַעֲלָה עֲשִׂירִית — דְּנָחֵית חַמְשָׁה וְקָאֵי אַעַשְׂרָה, אוֹ דִלְמָא דְּנָחֵית עַשְׂרָה וְקָאֵי אַחַמְשָׁה? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna continues: **And two priests stood with two trumpets at the Upper Gate that descends from the Israelites' courtyard to the Women's Courtyard, and when those drawing the water reached the tenth stair they sounded the trumpets. Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma: Does the phrase reached the tenth stair mean that he would descend five stairs and stand on the tenth from the bottom? Or perhaps it means that he would descend ten stairs and stand on the fifth from the bottom? The Gemara notes: The dilemma shall stand unresolved.**

The *mishnah* describes how the *kohanim* stood at the upper gate and descended in procession, blowing *tekiah teruah tekiah* at the various stages of the water-drawing ceremony. Rabbi Yirmiya raises a precise question about the choreography: when the *kohanim* performed their blast at what is described as the relevant step — was that the tenth step counting from the bottom, meaning they had descended five from the top? Or was it the tenth step counting from the top, meaning they had gone down ten and were five from the bottom? *Teiku* — this question is left unresolved.

### Eastward Turn

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן, מִמַּשְׁמַע שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּפְנֵיהֶם קֵדְמָה״, אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ שֶׁ״אֲחוֹרֵיהֶם אֶל הֵיכַל ה׳״? אֶלָּא מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֲחוֹרֵיהֶם אֶל הֵיכַל ה׳״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיוּ פּוֹרְעִין עַצְמָן, וּמַתְרִיזִין כְּלַפֵּי מַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages taught: By inference, from the fact that it is stated: "And their faces toward the east," don't I know that "their backs were toward the Sanctuary of the Lord"? The Sanctuary was to the west. Rather, to what purpose does the verse state: "Their backs toward the Sanctuary of the Lord"? It teaches that they would expose themselves and defecate downward, a euphemism for the direction of the Divine Presence.

A *baraita* now addresses the gesture of turning eastward at the conclusion of the procession. The verse in Yechezkel states: *upneihem keimah* — their faces were to the east — describing the sin of those at the time of the First *Beit HaMikdash*. One might think this means simply that their backs were turned to the *Heichal*. The *baraita* points out that this is self-evident — if faces are east, backs face the *Heichal* to the west — so why specify it? *Melamed she'hayu por'in atzman u-matrizin klapei ma'alah* — it teaches that they would expose their rear ends and perform acts of deliberate degradation toward the *Heichal*, combining idolatrous worship with an act of contempt toward God's house.

### God Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָנוּ לְיָהּ וּלְיָהּ עֵינֵינוּ כּוּ׳. אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״שְׁמַע״ ״שְׁמַע״, כְּאִילּוּ אָמַר ״מוֹדִים״ ״מוֹדִים״! אֶלָּא הָכִי אָמְרִי: הֵמָּה מִשְׁתַּחֲוִים קֵדְמָה, וְאָנוּ לְיָהּ (אֲנַחְנוּ מוֹדִים), וְעֵינֵינוּ לְיָהּ מְיַחֲלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: In the Second Temple period they would say: **We are to God, and to God are our eyes.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so?** May one pray in that manner? **Didn't Rabbi Zeira say: One who repeats himself while reciting *Shema* and says: Listen, listen, is like one who says: We give thanks, we give thanks,** and he is silenced, as it appears that he is worshipping two authorities. How then did they recite God's name twice, consecutively? **Rather, this is what they said: They bow toward the east,** while **we give thanks to God, and our eyes turn in hope to God,** so that they would not recite God's name consecutively.

Against this backdrop of the forefathers' disgrace, those at the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* would turn and declare: *anu leYah uleYah eineinu* — we are for God and our eyes are toward God. The *gemara* raises a concern: Rabbi Zeira taught that one who says *Shema Yisrael* twice in succession is as if saying *modim modim* — an expression that suggests dualism. Does not the formula *anu leYah uleYah eineinu* repeat God's name consecutively in a way that raises the same concern? The *gemara* resolves: *anu heyma mishtachavim keimah ve-anu leYah* — they would say that our forefathers bowed toward the east, but we bow to God — *ve-eineinu leYah meyachalot* — and our eyes long for God. By inserting the phrase *eineinu* between the two divine references, the formula avoids the problematic juxtaposition.

There is something deeply striking about the overall choreography here. At the *Simchat Beit HaShoeivah* one finds an extended dialogue taking shape: when the *aravah* is circled, the people declare *yofi lach mizbei'ach, yofi lach mizbei'ach*. Here, before departing, they contrast themselves with their forefathers and declare their fidelity to God. And Hillel, as discussed earlier, embodied God's own voice responding — welcoming those who come to His house and promising to come to theirs. Something about *Sukkot* and the intersection of God's presence and our dwelling evokes this uniquely intimate, reciprocal dialogue between Israel and the divine.

## Tekiot Count

### Tekiot Range

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין פּוֹחֲתִין מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַחַת תְּקִיעוֹת בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאֵין מוֹסִיפִין עַל אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה. בְּכׇל יוֹם הָיוּ שָׁם עֶשְׂרִים וְאַחַת תְּקִיעוֹת בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ: שָׁלֹשׁ לִפְתִיחַת שְׁעָרִים, וְתֵשַׁע לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וְתֵשַׁע לְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם. וּבַמּוּסָפִין הָיוּ מוֹסִיפִין עוֹד תֵּשַׁע.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One sounds no fewer than twenty-one trumpet blasts in the Temple, and one sounds no more than forty-eight.** The mishna elaborates: **Each day there were twenty-one trumpet blasts in the Temple: Three blasts were sounded for the opening of the gates in the morning, nine for the daily morning offering, and nine for the daily afternoon offering,** totaling twenty-one. **And on a day when the additional offerings were sacrificed, e.g., the New Moon, with the additional offerings they would add nine additional blasts.**

Having mentioned the *tekiot* that accompanied the water-drawing procession, the *mishnah* turns to the general institution of trumpet blasts in the *Beit HaMikdash*. The rule is: *ein pochtin m'esrim v'achat tekiot bamikdash* — there are no fewer than twenty-one *tekiot* in the *Mikdash* — *ve-ein mosifin al arbaim u-shmonah* — and no more than forty-eight. Each set of *tekiot* follows the pattern of *tekiah teruah tekiah*, a unit of three blasts. Twenty-one therefore represents seven such units, and forty-eight represents sixteen.

### Daily Blasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת הָיוּ מוֹסִיפִין שֵׁשׁ: שָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְטִיל אֶת הָעָם מִמְּלָאכָה, וְשָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין קֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **And on Shabbat eve they would add six blasts sounded adjacent to the onset of Shabbat: Three to stop the people from their labor, as the blasts inform the people that Shabbat is approaching and they stop working, and three at the onset of Shabbat to demarcate between sacred and profane.**

How does the daily total of twenty-one come about? *Shalosh bif'tichat she'arim* — three *tekiot*, one unit, upon opening the gates in the morning. *Tesha l'tamid shel shachar* — nine more, three units, for the morning *tamid*. *Rashi* clarifies when these nine were blown: at the wine libation accompanying the *tamid*, during which the *levi'im* would sing. *Ha-levi'im omrim shir*, and the song was structured with three pauses — *u-shlosha prakim hayu ba-shir she-hayu mafsikin bo* — at each pause the *kohanim* would sound the *chatzotzrot* and the people would bow down. This choreography transformed the service from a spectacle into a participatory act of worship — the music would pause, the trumpets would sound, and the entire assembly would prostrate themselves. The same nine *tekiot* were then repeated for *tesha l'tamid shel bein ha'arbayim*, the afternoon *tamid*. That yields a total of seven units — twenty-one *tekiot* — as the daily baseline.

### Shabbat Blasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת שֶׁבְּתוֹךְ הַחַג הָיוּ שָׁם אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה: שָׁלֹשׁ לִפְתִיחַת שְׁעָרִים, שָׁלֹשׁ לְשַׁעַר הָעֶלְיוֹן, וְשָׁלֹשׁ לְשַׁעַר הַתַּחְתּוֹן, וְשָׁלֹשׁ לְמִילּוּי הַמַּיִם, וְשָׁלֹשׁ עַל גַּבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ, תֵּשַׁע לְתָמִיד שֶׁל שַׁחַר, וְתֵשַׁע לְתָמִיד שֶׁל בֵּין הָעַרְבַּיִם, וְתֵשַׁע לְמוּסָפִין, שָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְטִיל אֶת הָעָם מִן הַמְּלָאכָה, וְשָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין קוֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** On **Shabbat eve during the festival of Sukkot, there were forty-eight blasts. How so? Three in the morning for the opening of the gates; three for the upper gate; and three for the lower gate; and three for the filling of the vessel with water,** as described in the sequence of the ritual of drawing the water for the water libation (48b); **and three when pouring the water libation upon the altar; nine for the daily morning offering; and nine for the daily afternoon offering; and nine for the additional offerings; three to stop the people from work; and three more to demarcate between sacred and profane,** totaling forty-eight blasts.

If *musafin* were brought, *uv'musafin hayu mosifin od tesha* — an additional nine *tekiot* were added, three more units, for the same reason: the *musaf* offering also involved wine libations with the *levi'im*' song and its three breaks. This brings the total on a *yom tov* to thirty. On *Erev Shabbat*, *uv'erev shabbat hayu mosifin sheish* — six additional *tekiot* were added, two more units. *Shalosh l'havtil et ha'am mi'melacha* — three to announce that labor must cease, communicated presumably by relay through torches visible across the city. *Ve-shalosh l'havdil bein kodesh l'chol* — and three more to mark the onset of *Shabbat* itself. The use of *l'havdil* here in the direction of entering *kedushah* rather than departing from it is an interesting nuance.

### Full Schedule

When *Erev Shabbat* fell in the middle of *Sukkot*, both sets of additional *tekiot* applied simultaneously, yielding the maximum of forty-eight — sixteen units. The full accounting: one unit for the opening of the gates; one unit for the procession from the upper gate; one unit at the lower gate as the procession exits the *Beit HaMikdash*; one unit at the filling of the water; one unit at the pouring of the water on the *mizbei'ach*; three units for the *tamid shel shachar*, *musaf*, and *tamid shel bein ha'arbayim* respectively — totaling nine units thus far, which is fourteen. Then two more units for the *Erev Shabbat* warnings brings the total to sixteen. Sixteen times three is forty-eight.

One might note that the *tekiot* accompanying the *aravah* ceremony — *taku v'heriu v'taku* — do not appear in this count. Whether *Hoshanah Rabbah* fell on *Erev Shabbat* would presumably have added further *tekiot* beyond the forty-eight, though the *gemara* does not address this directly.

## Tekiot Dispute

### Rabbi Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַפּוֹחֵת לֹא יִפְחוֹת מִשֶּׁבַע, וְהַמּוֹסִיף לֹא יוֹסִיף עַל שֵׁשׁ עֶשְׂרֵה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara notes: **The mishna is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, as it is taught in a *baraita* that Rabbi Yehuda says: The one who seeks to minimize the number of blasts shall not minimize their number to fewer than seven blasts. And one who seeks to add to the number of blasts shall not add beyond sixteen.**

*Matenin d'lo k'Rabbi Yehuda* — the *mishnah* does not follow the position of Rabbi Yehuda. A *baraita* records: *Rabbi Yehuda omer ha-pochet lo yifchos mi-sheva ve-ha-mosif lo yosif al sheish esreh* — Rabbi Yehuda holds the minimum is seven and the maximum is sixteen. He counts precisely as the units were enumerated above — seven rather than twenty-one, sixteen rather than forty-eight.

### One or Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָא מִיפַּלְגִי? רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: תְּקִיעָה תְּרוּעָה תְּקִיעָה — אַחַת הִיא, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: תְּקִיעָה לְחוּד וּתְרוּעָה לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **what do they disagree?** The Gemara explains that **Rabbi Yehuda holds: A series of blasts consisting of *tekia*, *terua*, *tekia* is counted as one blast. And the Rabbis hold: A *tekia* is counted separately and a *terua* is counted separately.** They agree with regard to the sequence and the number of the blasts, and disagree only with regard to how the blasts are tallied.

The *gemara* asks: *bimai kamifligi* — what is the substance of the dispute? *Rabbi Yehuda savar tekiah-teruah-tekiah achas hi* — Rabbi Yehuda holds that *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* constitutes one unit. *Ve-Rabbanan savari tekiah lachud u-teruah lachud* — the Sages hold they are distinct sounds. This is not merely a dispute about how to label or count; there is a real halakhic difference regarding whether one takes a breath between the sounds or whether the entire sequence is blown as one continuous unit.

### Verb Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּתְקַעְתֶּם תְּרוּעָה״ (וּכְתִיב: ״תְּרוּעָה יִתְקְעוּ״, הָא כֵּיצַד? תְּקִיעָה וּתְרוּעָה — אַחַת הִיא). וְרַבָּנַן: הַהוּא לִפְשׁוּטָה לְפָנֶיהָ וּלְאַחֲרֶיהָ הוּא דַּאֲתָא (וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְפָנֶיהָ וּלְאַחֲרֶיהָ מְנָלֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״שֵּׁנִית״).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the rationale for the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda?** It is as **the verse states: "And you shall sound [*utkatem*] a *terua*"** (Numbers 10:5), **and it is written: "A *terua* they will sound [*yitke'u*]"** (Numbers 10:6). **How is it that the Torah uses a verb from the root of *tekia* to describe the sounding of a *terua*? Apparently, a *tekia* and a *terua* together compose one blast. And how do the Rabbis interpret these verses? This comes to teach that each *terua* blast is accompanied by a plain unembellished blast, a *tekia*, preceding it and following it.** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yehuda derive that each *terua* must be accompanied by a *tekia* preceding it and following it?** The Gemara explains: **He derives it from the verse when it says: "And you shall sound [*utkatem*] a *terua* a second time"** (Numbers 10:6), indicating an additional *tekia*.

What is Rabbi Yehuda's source? *Amar kra utkatem teruah* — the Torah uses the verb *u-tekia* in relation to *teruah*, which Rabbi Yehuda reads as indicating that the two are halakhically one. The Sages counter: *hahu l'pishuta lifneyha u-le'achareyha hu d'ata* — that verse is merely informing us that a *tekiah* precedes and follows the *teruah*, not that they constitute one sound.

### Torah Allowance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ? דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְהַקְהִיל אֶת הַקָּהָל תִּתְקְעוּ וְלֹא תָרִיעוּ״, וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ: תְּקִיעָה תְּרוּעָה — אַחַת הִיא, אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא פַּלְגָא דְּמִצְוָה עֲבֵיד וּפַלְגָא לָא עֲבֵיד?! וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: הָהוּא לְסִימָנָא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the rationale for the opinion of the Rabbis?** It is **as it is written: "And when congregating the people you shall sound a *tekia* and shall not sound a *terua*"** (Numbers 10:7). **And if it enters your mind that a *tekia* and a *terua* are considered one blast, would the Merciful One say to perform half a mitzva and not to perform the other half of the mitzva? Apparently, each is a separate mitzva.** The Gemara asks: **And how does Rabbi Yehuda interpret the verse?** The Gemara answers: **That single *tekia* mentioned in the context of congregating the people came merely as a signal** to the camps and was not for the purpose of fulfilling the mitzva, which, in Rabbi Yehuda's opinion, always comes in groups of three.

Where do the Sages derive that *tekiah* and *teruah* are distinct? *D'chtiv u-v'hakheil et ha-kahal titke'u ve-lo tari'u* — the Torah permits *tekiah* to be performed without *teruah* in certain contexts, demonstrating that *tekiah* can exist as an independent sound. *Ve-ei salka daitach tekiah-teruah achas hi* — if one held that *tekiah-teruah* were truly one unit — *amar Rachmana palga d'mitzvah avid u-palga lo avid* — the Torah would be instructing one to perform half a *mitzvah* and not the other half. That is impossible. The sounds must therefore be distinct. Rabbi Yehuda responds: *hahu l'simana b'alma hu d'ata* — that verse is simply establishing a *tekiah* as an independent signal for gathering, a function the Torah elevates into a *mitzvah*, but this does not disprove that when performed together they constitute one halakhic unit.

### Yochanan Extreme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן: סִימָנָא הוּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא שַׁוְּיֵהּ מִצְוָה. כְּמַאן אָזְלָא הָא דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אֵין בֵּין תְּקִיעָה לִתְרוּעָה וְלֹא כְּלוּם, כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה. (אִי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה) פְּשִׁיטָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And how do the Rabbis counter that assertion? They say: Indeed, it is a signal to assemble the people; however, the Merciful One rendered it a mitzva.** Therefore, one can derive that a single *tekia* blast is a distinct mitzva. The Gemara asks: **In accordance with whose opinion is this statement of Rav Kahana: There is no pause between a *tekia* and a *terua* at all and they are sounded in one continuous blast? In accordance with whose opinion is it?** It is **in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda.** The Gemara asks: **If Rav Kahana's statement is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda; that is obvious.** Why was it necessary for the Gemara to raise the matter at all?

The *gemara* then asks whose position is served by the statement of Rav Kahana: *ein bein tekiah li-teruah ve-lo klum* — there is absolutely no pause whatsoever between *tekiah* and *teruah*. One might think this statement is so basic that it would be agreed upon by all. But the *gemara* clarifies that it is specifically coming to exclude the extreme position of Rabbi Yochanan: *d'amar shama tesha tekiot b'tesha sha'ot ha-yom yatza* — Rabbi Yochanan holds that on Rosh Hashanah, if one heard the nine *tekiot* spread across nine different hours of the day, one has fulfilled the obligation. This is a dramatic position — not only are the sounds conceptually distinct, they need not even be grouped together; they can be heard individually over the course of an entire day.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: אֲפִילּוּ כְּרַבָּנַן, וּלְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר: שָׁמַע תֵּשַׁע תְּקִיעוֹת בְּתֵשַׁע שָׁעוֹת בַּיּוֹם — יָצָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן. וְאֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי! אִם כֵּן, מַאי ״וְלֹא כְּלוּם״?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It is not obvious that Rav Kahana's statement was stated in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. **Lest you say that Rav Kahana's statement is even in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis, and that he is coming to exclude the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan who said: If one heard nine blasts in nine different hours on the day of Rosh HaShana, despite the considerable gap between them, he fulfilled his obligation. Therefore, the Gemara teaches us that Rav Kahana holds in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda alone and he does not allow even a brief pause between the sounds of the *shofar*. The Gemara asks: **And say it is indeed so that Rav Kahana holds in accordance with the opinion of the Rabbis and he merely comes to exclude the view of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** The Gemara answers: **If so, what is the meaning of the phrase: At all, in Rav Kahana's statement: There is no pause between a *tekia* and a *terua* at all? This indicates that Rav Kahana does not allow even a slight pause between blasts, and that could be only in accordance with Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *gemara* initially suggests that Rav Kahana's statement need not go as far as Rabbi Yehuda; perhaps it is simply rejecting Rabbi Yochanan's extreme position, saying that while the sounds are distinct, one cannot space them hours apart — without claiming they must be blown with literally zero pause. The *gemara* rejects this: *im ken mai ve-lo klum* — if that were the intent, the statement would simply say do not interrupt; the emphatic phrase *ve-lo klum*, not even the slightest pause, indicates that this is indeed the position of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that the entire unit is blown without any break at all.

The result is three logically distinct positions. Rabbi Yehuda holds that *tekiah-teruah-tekiah* is one halakhic unit, blown without any pause. The Sages hold that the sounds are distinct, with a pause between them, though they must presumably be grouped in reasonably close succession. And Rabbi Yochanan takes the most expansive view: the sounds are fully independent and can even be heard across nine separate hours of the day. The dispute ranges from one continuous sound to a unit of three tightly grouped sounds to three entirely independent sonic events spread over the course of a day.
