# Niddah 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## C-section & Korban

### Mishnah Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן דְּלָא, מַאי לַָאו יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן דְּקָדָשִׁים? אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן: לָא, יוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן דִּבְכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains how this *baraita* supports the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan. The *baraita* **teaches, in any event, that** an animal **born by caesarean section** is **not** fit for sacrifice. **What, is it not** referring to one who consecrates an animal born by **caesarean section** and renders it **a sacrificial** animal? Evidently, although Rabbi Shimon maintains that a human birth by caesarean section has the halakhic status of a regular birth, he concedes that animals born in this manner are unfit for sacrifice. **Rav Huna, son of Rav Natan, said: No,** the *baraita* is dealing **with a firstborn** animal born by **caesarean section,** and it is this animal that is disqualified as an offering. A firstborn animal is sacred only if it emerged from the womb.

The daf opens with a review of where the previous session left off. The *mishnah* had established that a baby born through C-section — *yotzei dofen* — does not constitute the type of birth the *Torah* contemplates when it establishes the *tum'ah* and *taharah* of a *yoledes*: the week and thirty-three days, or the two weeks and sixty-six days. This is the position of the *Chachamim*. *Rabbi Shimon*, however, maintains that a C-section does constitute childbirth for those categories. The *Gemara* now clarifies that even according to *Rabbi Shimon*, an animal born by C-section cannot be brought as a *korban*. The *Torah* uses the verb *leida* in the context of a woman giving birth — *ki tazria ve'yalda zachar* — and *Rabbi Shimon* reads that as inclusive enough to confer *yoledes* status. But by animals the *Torah* uses the parallel verb *yivaled* — *shor o chesev o ez ki yivaled* — and through a connection to *bechor*, it is established that for animals, only vaginal birth qualifies.

### Braita: Animal Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכוֹר — מִ״פֶּטֶר רֶחֶם״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to this interpretation of the *baraita*: How can it be referring to **a firstborn** animal? The *halakha* that firstborn status does not apply to an animal born by caesarean section is **derived from** the phrase **"opens the womb,"** which teaches that only animals born in the natural manner are endowed with the sanctity of firstborn animals and may be sacrificed on the altar. Since an animal born by caesarean section is not sacred at all, and it is clear that a non-sacred animal cannot be sacrificed upon the altar, it is obvious that the animal must be taken down if it was placed there in error.

This point is supported by a *braisa* discussed in the previous session: an animal born by C-section and placed on the *mizbeach* must be removed. Most *pasul* offerings may remain on the *mizbeach* once placed there; this animal is so thoroughly outside the relevant category that it must be taken down. This serves as proof that even *Rabbi Shimon* — who is lenient regarding human C-section birth — agrees that for animals the *Torah* requires vaginal birth.

### Bechor vs. Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מַאי, דְּקָדָשִׁים? מֵ״אִמּוֹ״ ״אִמּוֹ״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds to this objection: **Rather, what** would you say? Would you say that the *baraita* is referring to an ordinary animal, not a firstborn, **that** had been consecrated as **a sacrificial** animal? But this animal is not sacred either, as **derived from** the verbal analogy of the term **"its mother"** stated with regard to the firstborn and the term **"its mother"** stated in connection with consecrated animals, as Rabbi Yoḥanan taught earlier. If one seeks to consecrate as an offering an animal that was born by caesarean section, it is not rendered sacred at all. Accordingly, the same reasoning applies as before: It is evident that a non-sacred animal may not be placed on the altar, and it must be removed if placed there in error.

The *Gemara* picks up three lines from the top of 41a. *Katani miyas* — from the *braisa* just discussed — *yotzei dofen delo*, an animal born by C-section cannot remain on the *mizbeach*. The *Gemara* asks: *mai lav yotzei dofen de'kodashim* — does this not mean a standard consecrated animal born by C-section is invalid? *Rav Huna brei de'Rav Natan* responds: no, we are talking about a *bechor*. The *Torah*'s language of *peter rechem* — breaching the womb — means exiting the womb naturally, vaginally. So the *pesul* in the *braisa* is simply that this animal is not a *bechor*; it was never sanctified as such. If it had been a regular *korban*, the problem would not exist.

The *Gemara* immediately presses this. If the only issue is *bechor*, the *pesul* is derivable from *peter rechem* itself — you would not need the extra word *zos* in *zos toras ha'olah*, which excludes certain things from remaining on the *mizbeach*. So why does *zos* appear? *Ela mai de'kodashim* — if you want to say we are talking about a standard *korban*, again, the exclusion should be derivable from the *imo* verse: *shivas yamim yihyeh im imo*, which the *Gemara* had linked to both *bechor* and other *korbanos*, teaching that what applies to one applies to the other. So why do you need *zos*?

### Two Pesukim Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא דְּקָדָשִׁים — הַיְינוּ דְּאִצְטְרִיכִי תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי; חַד לְבֶהֱמַת חוּלִּין דְּאוֹלִיד דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן וְאַקְדְּשַׁהּ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this response: **What is this** comparison between firstborn animals and consecrated animals? **Granted, if you say** that the *baraita* is referring to animals **that are consecrated** to be offerings, **that is why two verses are necessary: One** verse, the verbal analogy between consecrated animals and firstborn animals, teaches **that a non-sacred animal** whose mother **gave birth** to it **by caesarean section and** whose owner subsequently **consecrated it** as an offering is not sacred at all, and must therefore be removed from the altar if it was placed there in error.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד לְבֶהֱמַת קָדָשִׁים דְּאוֹלִיד דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן, וְקָסָבַר וַלְדוֹת קָדָשִׁים בַּהֲוָיָיתָן הֵן קְדוֹשִׁים. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ דִּבְכוֹר — מִ״פֶּטֶר רֶחֶם״ נָפְקָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And one** verse: "This is the law of the burnt offering; that is the burnt offering that goes up on the pyre upon the altar" (Leviticus 6:2), teaches **with regard to a sacrificial animal that gave birth by caesarean section,** that although the offspring is sacred by virtue of its mother's sanctity, it may not be sacrificed and must be removed from the altar if placed there in error. **And** the *tanna* of this *baraita* **maintains** that the **offspring of sacrificial animals are** automatically **sacred upon their emergence** from the womb. **But if you say** that the *baraita* is referring **to a firstborn** animal that was born by caesarean section, the *halakha* that this animal is not sacred **is derived from** the phrase **"opens the womb."**

The *Gemara* resolves this by saying *hai mai* — what kind of question is that? *Ei amart bishlama de'kodashim* — if you say we are discussing regular *korbanos*, *hainu de'itztrichu terei karai* — that is precisely why you need two *pesukim*. One *pasuk* addresses the case where the animal was born by C-section and *then* sanctified — *behemas chullin de'olid derech dofen ve'akdishah*. The other *pasuk* addresses the case where the animal was born of a *korban* — *behemas kodshim d'olidi derech dofen* — meaning the *kedushah* was already present before birth. These are two distinct scenarios requiring independent derivations. But if we were speaking of *bechor*, there is only one scenario — a *bechor* becomes *kadosh* the instant it is born, so there is no case where the *kedushah* precedes the problem. The need for two *pesukim* thus proves we are discussing a standard *korban*.

### Offspring Kedushah Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי: ״הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע וְהַמּוּקְצֶה וְהַנֶּעֱבָד וְהַכִּלְאַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **So too, it is reasonable** to interpret the *baraita* in this manner, that it is referring to an offspring born to a sacrificial animal by caesarean section, **from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches** that **an animal that copulated with a person, and an animal that was the object of bestiality, and** an animal **that** was **set aside** for idol worship, **and** an animal **that was worshipped** as a deity, **and** an animal that is an offspring of **diverse kinds,** must all be removed from the altar if placed there in error.

Here *Rashi* inserts a significant remark: *v'chasavar v'lados kodshim b'havayasan hen kedoshim* — this is according to the view that offspring of *korbanos* acquire *kedushah* not *b'mei imman*, not in the mother's womb, but only at the moment of birth, *b'havayasan*, when they come into being. One might ask why this matters. The *Gemara* needs a scenario where the *kedushah* precedes the defect — i.e., where the animal was already *kadosh* before the C-section birth occurred, making it a greater *chiddush* that it is nonetheless *pasul*. If *kedushah* begins in the womb, that case is easy to construct and is, in fact, the bigger novelty: the *kedushah* came first and yet the C-section still disqualifies. *Rashi* therefore strikes out the phrase *v'chasavar v'lados kodshim b'havayasan*, arguing that the more striking case is precisely when the *kedushah* began in the womb — *b'mei imman hen kedoshim* — and yet is undermined. *Tosafos* offers an answer to this, but broadly agrees with *Rashi*'s principle. The upshot is that even when the *kedushah* is prior — whether it began in the womb or was conferred at birth before the animal was discovered to be a C-section offspring — a separate *pasuk* is needed to teach the *pesul* in each case.

One question arises: if an animal slaughtered while pregnant allows one to eat the fetus without separate *shechita*, does that not suggest the fetus has an independent status even in the womb? The answer is that *ubar yerech imo* — the fetus as a limb of the mother — is a distinct question from when the fetus acquires its own *kedushah* as an independent entity. The question of when *kedushah* takes hold may specifically turn on when the offspring becomes independently *kadosh*, not when it first has any legal standing.

### Other Disqualifications

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא! ״מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ — לְהוֹצִיא הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע, ״מִן הַבָּקָר״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנֶּעֱבָד, ״מִן הַצֹּאן״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַמּוּקְצֶה, ״וּמִן הַצֹּאן״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנּוֹגֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Now **are these** disqualifications **derived from here,** from the verse adduced by the *baraita*? No, they are **derived from elsewhere,** as taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: "You shall bring your offering from the cattle, even from the herd or from the flock" (Leviticus 1:2). The expression **"from the cattle"** serves **to exclude** from eligibility as an offering **an animal that copulated with a person and an animal that was the object of bestiality.** The expression **"from the herd"** serves **to exclude** an animal **that was worshipped** as a deity. **"From the flock"** serves **to exclude** an animal **set aside** for idol worship. The word "or" in the expression **"or from the flock"** serves **to exclude** an animal **that gored** a person, killing him. In all these cases the animal cannot be consecrated at all, and therefore it is not necessary for the Torah to teach that they must be removed from the altar if placed there in error.

The *Gemara* then broadens the discussion. *Hachi nami mistabra* — it also makes sense that our ruling applies to other disqualifications: *ha'rova ve'ha'nirba ve'ha'muktze ve'ha'ne'evad ve'ha'kilayim* — animals used in bestiality (whether in the active or passive role), animals set aside for idolatrous purposes, animals worshipped as a deity, and hybrid animals. All of these, if placed on the *mizbeach*, must also be removed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתוּ, כִּלְאַיִם מֵהָכָא נָפְקָא? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא: ״שׁוֹר אוֹ כֶשֶׂב אוֹ עֵז״ — ״שׁוֹר״ פְּרָט לְכִלְאַיִם, ״אוֹ עֵז״ פְּרָט לְנִדְמֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** is the disqualification of an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds derived from here?** No, it is **derived from elsewhere,** as taught in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When **a bull or a sheep or a goat** is born, it shall be seven days under its mother; but from the eighth day and onward it may be accepted for an offering" (Leviticus 22:27). The term **"a bull"** serves **to exclude** an offspring of **diverse kinds** from being used as an offering, The phrase **"or a goat"** serves **to exclude** an animal that **resembles** another, i.e., a sheep that is the offspring of sheep but that looks like a goat, or vice versa. Once again, as these animals cannot be consecrated at all, it is not necessary for the Torah to teach that they must be removed from the altar if placed there in error.

The *Gemara* asks: *hani mehacha nafka* — do we need the word *zos* to exclude these as well? Surely each has its own *pasuk*. The opening of *Vayikra* teaches: *adam ki yakriv mikem korban min ha'behema min ha'bakar u'min ha'tzon* — the word *min* in each phrase excludes something. *Min ha'behema* excludes *rova ve'nirba*; *min ha'bakar* excludes *ha'ne'evad*; *min ha'tzon* excludes *ha'muktze*; and the extra *vav* of *u'min ha'tzon* excludes an animal that gored and killed. *Kilayim* and hybrid animals are excluded by the verse *shor o chesev o ez* — an ox or a sheep or a goat, not a hybrid; *o ez* further excludes *nidme*, an animal that resembles a different species. So for all these cases the *pesul* is derivable independently — why was *zos* needed at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אִצְטְרִיכוּ תְּרֵי קְרָאֵי: חַד לְבֶהֱמַת חוּלִּין, וְחַד לְבֶהֱמַת קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it must be that **two verses are necessary** for each of these cases: **One to** teach that **a non-sacred animal** that is subject to any of these disqualifications cannot be consecrated, **and** the other **one to** teach that with regard to **a sacrificial animal** that was born with this status by virtue of its mother's sanctity, if it is subject to one of these disqualifications it may not be sacrificed and must be removed from the altar if placed there in error. Accordingly, it stands to reason that **here too,** in the case of an animal born by caesarean section, **two verses** are **necessary** for the same reason: One for a non-sacred animal, to teach that it cannot be consecrated, and another for the offspring of a sacrificial animal.

The obvious answer — that *zos* teaches the animal must be *removed* rather than merely being *pasul* — is noted but, strikingly, the *Gemara* does not deploy it directly. *Tosafos* tries to address this. Instead, the *Gemara* insists that *zos* teaches an additional scenario of invalidity: *chad be'behemas chullin ve'chad be'behemas kodshim* — one *pasuk* for when the animal was born with the problem and then sanctified, another for when it was born to an already-sanctified animal. *Rashi* raises a further difficulty with several of these cases: if the animal started as a *korban*, how could it come to be worshipped as *avoda zara* or given as an *esnan* or *mechir*? He resolves the *avoda zara* case by invoking the opinion of *Rabbi Yosi HaGlili* that *kodshim kalim mamon ba'alim* — light sacrifices are considered the property of their owners sufficiently to allow the owner to render them forbidden. The cases of *esnan* and *mechir* are resolved by constructing a scenario where the fetus was transferred while still in the mother's womb, before it acquired *kedushah* — since on this view, *kedushah* is only conferred at birth.

The *Gemara* concludes: the need for two *pesukim* demonstrates we are discussing standard *korbanos*, not *bechor*, and thus even *Rabbi Shimon* — who counts C-section birth as childbirth for the *tum'ah* of a *yoledes* — agrees that for animal sacrifices, C-section birth does not suffice.

## C-section & Niddah

### Maksha & C-section

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמְקַשָּׁה שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים, וְיָצָא וָלָד דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן — הֲרֵי זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ יוֹלֶדֶת בְּזוֹב, וְדָם הַיּוֹצֵא מִשָּׁם טָמֵא, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מְטַהֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: Although a woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period is rendered a *zava* and must count seven clean days before immersing and purifying herself, if a pregnant woman **experiences** birth **pangs** accompanied by bleeding for **three days** after her menstrual period, at the end of which she gives birth, she is not rendered a *zava*, as the bleeding is attributed to the childbirth. **And** if the **offspring emerged by caesarean section, she is** considered **one who has given birth during** a period of ***ziva*. But Rabbi Shimon says: She is not** considered **one who has given birth during** a period of ***ziva*. And** the **blood that emerges from there is ritually impure, but Rabbi Shimon deems** it **pure.**

With the *kodashim* discussion complete, the *Gemara* turns to a *baraisa* that combines the question of C-section birth with the laws of *niddah* and *zavah*. *Tanu Rabban*: *ha'makshe shlosha yamim ve'yatza vlad derech dofen harei zo yoledes bazov* — a woman who has *koshi*, birth pangs, for three days, and then the child exits by C-section, is considered a *yoledes bazov*. That is, her pre-birth bleeding is not neutralized by the birth; she retains *zavah* status.

To appreciate this, recall the prior discussion: when a woman bleeds during the period of *koshi* — active labor pains — that bleeding is treated differently from ordinary *zavah* bleeding, because it is understood to be caused by the impending birth. When the birth occurs, the bleeding is retroactively associated with childbirth rather than with *zivah*. The question here is whether this applies when the birth is a C-section rather than a vaginal birth.

### Zavah Dispute

The *Chachamim* say that *koshi* bleeding is not neutralized unless it leads to vaginal birth. Since C-section is not the birth the *Torah* intends for *tum'ah* and *taharah* purposes, the *koshi* bleeding stands as *zavah* bleeding and she is *yoledes bazov*. *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds that C-section is halachically a birth for all purposes of *tum'ah* and *taharah*, says correspondingly: *ein zo yoledes bazov* — she is not *yoledes bazov*, because the birth — even C-section — retroactively connects the preceding bleeding to the childbirth and removes the *zavah* status.

The correspondence of positions is internally coherent. One might have argued that the biological reality of a fetus causing the bleeding should be sufficient even according to the *Chachamim* — the fetus is there, the bleeding is caused by the fetus, regardless of how birth eventually occurs. But the *Gemara* teaches otherwise: the halachic status of the birth determines the status of the preceding bleeding. Since the *Chachamim* do not recognize C-section as a birth for *tum'ah* and *taharah*, they do not recognize it as a birth that can neutralize preceding *zavah* bleeding either. One further point: the reason *koshi* bleeding can neutralize *zavah* status is connected to the *taharah* period that follows childbirth — thirty-three days for a boy, sixty-six for a girl. Since that *taharah* period flows from the birth, it can also absorb the prior bleeding. If there is no such birth for purposes of *tum'ah* and *taharah*, there is no *taharah* period, and no mechanism to neutralize the prior bleeding.

### Incision Blood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא רֵישָׁא — רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, אֶלָּא סֵיפָא בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? אָמַר רָבִינָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא וָלָד דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן וְדָם דֶּרֶךְ רֶחֶם, וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְטַעְמֵיהּ וְרַבָּנַן לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **Granted, the first clause** of the *baraita* is clear: **Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning,** cited in the mishna, that birth via caesarean section has the halakhic status of childbirth; **and the Rabbis,** i.e., the first *tanna*, conform **to their** line of **reasoning,** that birth via caesarean section does not have the halakhic status of childbirth. **But** in **the latter clause, with regard to what** matter **do they disagree? Ravina said:** The latter clause is referring to a case **where the offspring emerged by caesarean section, and blood** emerged **through the womb,** i.e., vaginally, during the three days preceding the birth. **And Rabbi Shimon** conforms **to his** line of **reasoning,** that a caesarean birth is a full-fledged birth, and therefore the blood that emerged prior to the birth is ritually pure, **and the Rabbis** conform **to their** line of **reasoning,** that a caesarean birth is not halakhically considered a birth, which means that the blood which emerged beforehand is considered the blood of *ziva*, and is ritually impure.

The *baraisa* continues: *v'dam ha'yotzei misham* — blood that comes from there — *tamei*; *Rabbi Shimon metaher* — *Rabbi Shimon* declares it *tahor*. At first glance this seems to contradict the pattern. One would expect *Rabbi Shimon*, who is more expansive in recognizing C-section birth, to be more inclined to treat blood associated with it as *tamei*. Yet here he says *tahor*. The *Gemara* must explain this.

*Ravina* proposes: *k'gon she'yatza ha'vlad derech dofen v'dam derech rechem* — the case is that the child exited through the incision but the blood being discussed came out vaginally. In that case, both *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Chachamim* are arguing about whether she has the status of a *yoledes*, and specifically whether post-birth vaginal bleeding is *tahor* as the blood of a *yoledes*. *Rabbi Shimon* says yes — she is a *yoledes*, and so the blood that flows vaginally afterward is in the period of *taharah*. The *Chachamim* say she is not a *yoledes*, so that vaginal bleeding makes her *tamei*. Under this reading, the second clause of the *baraisa* is simply a restatement of the same *machlokes* applied to post-birth bleeding.

### Makor Tamei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב יוֹסֵף: חֲדָא — דְּהַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא, וְעוֹד — ״מִשָּׁם״ מְקוֹם וָלָד מַשְׁמַע!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef objects to this** explanation: **One** difficulty is that according to this interpretation, the latter clause of the *baraita* is superfluous, **as** the dispute recorded there **is** identical to that of **the first clause. And furthermore,** the words: **From there,** in the phrase: The blood that emerges from there, **indicate** that this is referring to a place already mentioned in the *baraita*, i.e., the **place** from which the **offspring** emerged, which is the abdominal incision of the caesarean section, not the vagina.

*Rav Yosef* rejects this interpretation on two grounds. First, *chada d'lav dehaynu reisha* — this is merely a repetition of the first *machlokes* about *yoledes* status; no new *halacha* is added. Second, *misham makom vlad mashma* — the phrase "from there" points specifically to the place from which the child emerged, i.e., the incision, not the vaginal canal. If vaginal bleeding were meant, one would not say "blood comes from there."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּגוֹן שֶׁיָּצָא וָלָד וְדָם דֶּרֶךְ דּוֹפֶן, וּבְמָקוֹר מְקוֹמוֹ טָמֵא קָמִיפַּלְגִי; מָר סָבַר: מָקוֹר מְקוֹמוֹ טָמֵא, וּמָר סָבַר: מָקוֹר מְקוֹמוֹ טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Yosef said** that this is the explanation of the latter clause of the *baraita*: It is referring to a situation **where** both the **offspring and blood emerged through** the incision in the **abdomen.** It is in such a case that the first *tanna* deems the blood which emerged impure and Rabbi Shimon deems it pure. **And** the matter **with regard to** which they **disagree** is whether or not **the location** of a woman's **source,** i.e., her uterus, is ritually **impure.** One **Sage,** the first *tanna*, **holds** that **the location** of a woman's **source** is ritually **impure,** and therefore any blood that emerges from it, regardless of how it came out of her body, is impure as well. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon, **holds** that **the location** of a woman's **source** is **pure,** and blood that emerges from there is also pure. Only uterine blood which emerges vaginally is impure.

*Rav Yosef* therefore offers a different reading: *k'gon she'yatza vlad v'dam derech dofen* — both the child and the blood came out through the incision. The question is then not about *yoledes* status at all. The blood is not making her a *niddah* — it is not vaginal flow. So what is the status of this blood? Here the *Gemara* identifies the real dispute: *uv'makor mekomo tamei kamiphlgi* — they are arguing about whether the *makor*, the womb, is intrinsically a source of *tum'ah*. *Mar savar makor mekomo tamei* — one holds that the place itself is *tamei*, meaning anything that was inside the uterus carries *tum'ah* even if it exits in an atypical way. *Mar savar makor mekomo tahor* — the other holds that blood is only *tamei* if it produces *niddah* status through vaginal flow; blood that does not make her a *niddah* carries no independent *tum'ah*.

This question of *makor mekomo tamei* had appeared previously in the *Gemara*. The conceptual difficulty it raises is significant. The *tum'ah* of *dam niddah* is hard enough to understand as a theological matter. The model that *tum'ah* is associated with lost life — potential that did not come to fruition — is frequently offered for seminal emission, and it has been proposed for *niddah* blood as well. But the position that *makor mekomo tamei* — that anything inside the womb is *tamei* by virtue of its location — pushes far beyond that model. It would mean that even discharge entirely unconnected to menstruation, or even uterine fluid that exits via an incision, carries *tum'ah* simply because it was in the womb. The womb, the very site of new life, is here treated as generating *tum'ah* in its own right. This is genuinely difficult to assimilate into any standard explanatory framework. The *yoledes* case itself — where a woman who has just given birth is *teme'ah* — is already the strongest challenge to the "death-represents-*tum'ah*" model. This case intensifies the difficulty.

The *Gemara* at this point is heading toward a ruling that *makor mekomo tamei* is the operative position — not to make the woman a *niddah*, but to treat uterine contents as intrinsically *tamei*. While the practical *halacha* of *tum'ah* and *taharah* is largely moot today, the implication in the era of *terumah* would be that even a non-menstrual uterine discharge could render a woman *tamei* and unable to eat *terumah*. The same would apply to women performing *bedikos*: if the discharge found on a *bedikah* cloth is not red and does not create *niddah* status, it might nevertheless be a uterine discharge and carry its own *tum'ah*.

### Reish Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לְדִבְרֵי הַמְטַמֵּא בַּדָּם — מְטַמֵּא בָּאִשָּׁה, לְדִבְרֵי הַמְטַהֵר בַּדָּם — מְטַהֵר בָּאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: According to the statement of the one who deems the blood impure,** the first *tanna*, **he deems the woman impure** as well, as though it were blood of menstruation. Likewise, **according to the statement of the one who deems the blood pure,** Rabbi Shimon, **he deems the woman pure** as well.

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *l'divrei ha'mitamei b'dam, mitamei b'ishah* — according to the view that the blood is *tamei* because it came from the womb, the blood coming out through the incision also makes the woman herself a *niddah*. This is *Reish Lakish*'s striking extension: it is not merely that the blood you touch is *tamei*; rather, since the blood originated in the womb, even its exit through an incision confers *niddah* status on the woman. The *Chachamim* who hold *makor mekomo tamei* would say the blood is *tamei* — that much is not a *chiddush*. *Reish Lakish* goes further and says she becomes a *niddah*. Interestingly, on this view the woman does not have the status of a *yoledes* — she does not receive the week or two weeks of *tum'ah* associated with childbirth — yet she can still become a *niddah* through the C-section blood. *L'divrei ha'mitaher b'dam, mitaher b'ishah* — if the blood is *tahor*, of course the woman is *tahor* as well; that requires no elaboration.

### Rabbi Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף לְדִבְרֵי הַמְטַמֵּא בַּדָּם — מְטַהֵר בָּאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **But Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Even according to the statement of the one who deems the blood impure,** the first *tanna*, **he deems the woman pure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה טְמֵאָה עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מַדְוֶהָ דֶּרֶךְ עֶרְוָתָהּ? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאִישׁ אֲשֶׁר יִשְׁכַּב אֶת אִשָּׁה דָּוָה וְגִלָּה אֶת עֶרְוָתָהּ אֶת מְקוֹרָהּ הֶעֱרָה״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין אִשָּׁה טְמֵאָה עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא מַדְוֶהָ דֶּרֶךְ עֶרְוָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** standard line of **reasoning** here, **as Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: From where** is it derived **that a woman does not become impure** due to menstruation **unless the flow** of blood **emerges from her nakedness,** i.e., genitalia? **As it is stated: "And a man who lies with a woman having her flow, and shall uncover her nakedness, he has made naked her source"** (Leviticus 20:18) This **teaches that a woman is not impure** due to menstruation **unless** the **flow emerges from her nakedness.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* disagrees: *af l'divrei ha'mitamei b'dam, mitaher b'ishah* — even if you hold that the blood is *tamei*, the woman herself does not become a *niddah*. *Mikor mikomo tamei* may be true as a matter of the blood's status; nevertheless, *niddah* is a category that applies to the woman only through vaginal flow. *Rabbi Yochanan* derives this from a principle transmitted in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai*: *minayin she'ein ishah teme'ah ad she'yetze midveh derech ervatah* — how do we know a woman does not become *teme'ah* until the flow exits through the way of her nakedness? From the verse: *v'ishah asher yishkav et ishah davah v'gilah et ervatah et mikorah he'erah* — "and a man who lies with a woman who is flowing, and uncovers her nakedness, her source he uncovered." The language of "nakedness" and "flow" makes clear that *niddah* status depends on the flow exiting vaginally. Even if the blood carries independent *tum'ah* by virtue of its uterine origin, it does not generate *niddah* status for the woman unless it flows vaginally.

*Tosafos* notes a subtle difficulty: *Rabbi Yochanan* quotes this ruling in the name of *Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai*, who is the same *Rabbi Shimon* mentioned earlier in the *baraisa*. Yet *Rabbi Shimon* in the *baraisa* holds *makor mekomo tahor* — that the blood itself is *tahor*. So when *Rabbi Yochanan* quotes *Rabbi Shimon* to teach that even if the blood is *tamei* the woman is not a *niddah*, what *chiddush* is this for *Rabbi Shimon*, who says the blood is *tahor* to begin with? *Tosafos* provides an answer, which can be examined directly.

### Prolapsed Uterus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה נְשִׂיאָה: מָקוֹר שֶׁנֶּעֱקַר וְנָפַל לָאָרֶץ — טְמֵאָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״יַעַן הִשָּׁפֵךְ נְחֻשְׁתֵּךְ וַתִּגָּלֶה עֶרְוָתֵךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda Nesia:** If a woman's **source,** i.e., her uterus, **became dislodged and fell** out of her body **onto the ground, she is ritually impure, as it is stated: "Because your foundation was poured out, and your nakedness was uncovered"** (Ezekiel 16:36). The word "foundation" alludes to the uterus, and the verse is referring to it after it has been "poured out," i.e., detached, as an uncovering of nakedness, which indicates that it is still a source of impurity even after it has been detached from its place.

*Reish Lakish* then offers a further ruling, in the name of *Rabbi Yehuda Nesi'ah* — the grandson of *Rabbi Yehuda Hanasi*: *mikor she'ne'ekar ve'nafal l'aretz* — if the womb itself becomes displaced and falls to the ground. This describes what appears to be an extreme case of uterine prolapse — indeed the *Acharonim*, notably the *Noda B'Yehuda* and the *Chatam Sofer*, dealt extensively with cases of prolapsed uteri and their impact on a woman's ability to count *shivah neki'im* and immerse in the *mikvah*. Here the *Gemara* describes something even more severe: a piece of the uterus itself, some tissue, exiting the body. *Reish Lakish* says this renders the woman *teme'ah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְטוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה — דָּם אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְלֹא חֲתִיכָה! אֶלָּא לְטוּמְאַת עֶרֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **To what** kind of impurity is this woman subject in this situation? **If we say** that she is subject **to the impurity of seven** days of menstruating women, that is impossible, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah that such impurity is caused by **"blood"** (Leviticus 15:19), **and not a piece** of flesh. **Rather,** she is subject **to impurity** that lasts until the **evening,** as a result of the surface of her body having come into contact with the uterus, which is a source of impurity.

One might have expected *Reish Lakish* — who was willing to say C-section blood makes her a *niddah* — to rule here that she becomes a *niddah* as well. The *Gemara* scales back the ruling: *dam amar Rachmana velo chaticha* — the *Torah* speaks of blood, not flesh. The five colors of *niddah* blood are an exhaustive list, and a piece of flesh does not belong among them. Therefore: *ela letumas erev* — the ruling is only that she is *teme'ah* for one day, i.e., until nightfall, because she has come into contact with something *tamei*. This is consistent with the principle of *makor mekomo tamei*: just as anything inside the uterus is *tamei*, the uterus itself, or a portion of it, when it exits, is *tamei*, and touching it makes her *teme'ah* for the day. But it does not make her a *niddah*.

### Discharge Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מָקוֹר שֶׁהִזִּיעַ כִּשְׁתֵּי טִיפֵּי מַרְגָּלִיּוֹת — טְמֵאָה. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לְטוּמְאַת שִׁבְעָה — חֲמִשָּׁה דָּמִים טְמֵאִין בָּאִשָּׁה וְתוּ לָא! אֶלָּא לְטוּמְאַת עֶרֶב. וְדַוְוקָא תַּרְתֵּי, אֲבָל חֲדָא — אֵימָא מֵעָלְמָא אָתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** In the case of a woman's **source that discharged two** whitish, clear, **pearl-like [*margaliyyot*] drops, she is impure.** The Gemara asks: **To what** kind of impurity is this woman subject in this situation? **If we say** that she is subject **to the impurity of seven** days of menstruating women, that is impossible, as the mishna (*Nidda* 19a) states that there are **five** distinct colors of **ritually impure blood in a woman, but no more,** and pearly white is not one of those colors. **Rather,** she is subject **to impurity** that lasts until the **evening,** as a result of her body having come into contact with a discharge from the uterus, which is a source of impurity. **And** this is the *halakha* **specifically** if there were **two** drops, **but** if there was only **one** such drop she is not impure, as **I can say** that the drop **came from elsewhere,** not from the uterus.

*Rabbi Yochanan* then offers an analogous ruling: *makor she'hiziya kishtei tipei margaliyot* — if the uterus "sweats" two drops that look like pearls, i.e., clear white liquid, the woman is *teme'ah*. Again, this cannot mean she is a *niddah* for seven days — *chamisha damim teme'im veto lo*, we have only five colors of *tamei* blood and nothing more; clear white drops are not among them. So again: *ela letumas erev* — she is *teme'ah* until nightfall by contact, because this fluid was in the womb and is therefore *tamei* as an *av ha'tum'ah*.

This raises a structural point worth noting: *dam niddah* is itself an *av ha'tum'ah*. The principle of *makor mekomo tamei* appears to extend the same *av* status to other uterine contents. If something merely *rishon* had come out and touched her, she would not be *teme'ah* from contact — a person only becomes *tamei* by touching an *av*. The fact that she becomes *teme'ah* by contact with these drops implies that uterine contents carry *av* status, not merely *rishon* status. The *Gemara* further specifies: *vedavka tartei* — specifically two drops. *Aval chada* — a single drop is ambiguous; one cannot be certain it originates from the uterus rather than from some other vaginal source. Two drops establish that the origin is uterine.

The *Gemara*'s ruling here sides with *makor mekomo tamei* as the operative position. While the *tum'ah* of *niddah* and *terumah* is largely theoretical today, the discussion makes clear that any uterine discharge — even one that does not make the woman a *niddah* — carries independent *tum'ah* by virtue of its origin. The practical implications would have been significant in the era of *terumah* and *kodshim*, and they remain relevant conceptually for understanding the full scope of these *halachos*.

## Niddah Threshold

### Outer House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים מְטַמְּאִין בַּבַּיִת הַחִיצוֹן. הֵי נִיהוּ בַּיִת הַחִיצוֹן? אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: כֹּל שֶׁתִּינוֹקֶת יוֹשֶׁבֶת וְנִרְאַת.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **All women become ritually impure** with the flow of blood from the uterus **into the outer chamber,** i.e., the vagina, as it is stated: "And her issue in her flesh shall be blood" (Leviticus 15:19). The Gemara asks: **What** exactly **is the outer chamber? Reish Lakish says: Any** place **which can be seen when a little girl sits** with her legs spread. When the blood reaches that area in the vagina, the woman becomes ritually impure.

The *Gemara* now transitions from the threshold of *niddah* status to the derivations that underlie it, and from there into a parallel discussion of *tum'ah* through seminal emission. *Kol hanashim mitam'os be'veit hachitzon* — all women become *tamei* in the outer house. The spatial metaphor introduced in an earlier *mishnah* — *prozdor*, *cheder*, *aliyah*: an ante-chamber, an inner chamber, an upper chamber — is recalled here. The "outer house" is understood to be the vaginal canal, and the ruling is that a woman becomes a *niddah* even before blood exits her body, when the blood enters this outer house. This stands in contrast to the rule for a man, who becomes a *ba'al keri* only when semen leaves his body. The *Torah* makes this asymmetry explicit: by a woman it says *bivisarah* — in her flesh — indicating that even an interior flow conveys *tum'ah*, whereas by a man it says *tetze mimenu* — it shall exit from him — requiring actual exit.

### Reish Lakish Definition

*Amar Reish Lakish*: *kol she'tinokes yoshevet venireh* — anything that an underage girl, seated with her legs apart, would expose to view. The idea is that in young girls, before external development is complete, when seated in this position one can see slightly into the vaginal opening. That threshold — the space that becomes visible under those conditions — is the *beit hachitzon*. When blood reaches that point, the woman is a *niddah*, even though the blood has not yet exited the body. Under *Reish Lakish*'s definition, this threshold is relatively close to the exterior of the body — only the very outermost portion of the vaginal canal.

The halachic consequence would be significant: if this were the ruling, *bedikos* would need to probe only minimally. A woman could check a very shallow depth and be confident she had examined the relevant space.

### Rabbi Yochanan Rejects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם גָּלוּי הוּא אֵצֶל שֶׁרֶץ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: עַד בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said to** Reish Lakish in objection: **That place is** considered **exposed** even **with regard to** contact with the carcass of a **creeping animal.** If one comes into contact with the carcass of a creeping animal he becomes impure. This is the *halakha* only if the animal touches a part of the body that is exposed, not an internal cavity such as the inside of the mouth. Since the area of the vagina described by Reish Lakish is considered an exposed part of the body for the purposes of the impurity imparted by the carcass of a creeping animal, it should not be necessary for the mishna to derive the *halakha* of her impurity from the expression "in her flesh." **Rather, Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The term outer chamber extends **until** the area **between the teeth-**like projections inside the vagina.

*Rabbi Yochanan* responds: *oto makom galui hu etzel sheretz* — that place *Reish Lakish* described is already considered exposed with respect to a *sheretz*. There is a general principle that contact *tum'ah* — becoming *tamei* by touching a *tamei* object — applies only to external contact. If a *sheretz* touches the interior of one's cheek without touching any outer skin, one does not become *tamei*; only touching an exposed surface conveys *tum'ah* through contact. The operative line between *makom galui* and *beit hastarim* determines when contact takes effect. *Rabbi Yochanan*'s point is that the threshold *Reish Lakish* described — the space visible when a young girl is seated — is, by the standards of the *sheretz* rule, already considered *external*, not internal. A *sheretz* placed there would make one *tamei* as if it had touched exterior skin. Therefore, this cannot be the *beit hachitzon* — the "inner" area from which the *mishnah* says a woman becomes a *niddah* even before blood exits. *Reish Lakish*'s definition is too far outward; it is already the exterior.

## Niddah Threshold Continued

### Between the Teeth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם כְּלִפְנִים אוֹ כְּלַחוּץ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנֵי רַבִּי זַכַּאי: עַד בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם, בֵּין הַשִּׁינַּיִם עַצְמָן כְּלִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Is the area **between the teeth-**like projections itself considered **as internal,** which would mean that blood there would not render the woman impure, **or as external? Come** and **hear** a resolution, **as Rabbi Zakkai teaches** a *baraita*: The term outer chamber extends **to** the area **between the teeth-**like projections, but the area **between the teeth-**like projections **themselves** is considered **as internal.**

*Rabbi Yochanan* therefore offers his own definition: *ad bein hashinayim* — until between the teeth. The *shinayim* — teeth — is the *Gemara*'s term for the cervix. The cervix functions like an orifice that opens and closes, rigid at its inner walls, with the texture of teeth in the *Gemara*'s descriptive framework. *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that the *beit hachitzon* begins at the point where blood passes through the cervix into the vaginal canal. Until that moment, the blood is still in the interior and she is not a *niddah*. Once it passes the cervix — *bein hashinayim* — she is a *niddah*, even though it has not yet left her body.

The practical difference from *Reish Lakish* is enormous. Under *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling, *anywhere* in the vaginal canal is already *beit hachitzon*. A woman becomes a *niddah* the moment blood clears the cervix. This is why *bedikos* must probe deeply — all the way to the cervical os — in order to truly examine the *beit hachitzon*.

### Cervix Status

*Ibaya lehu* — a question is raised: *bein hashinayim kelifnim o kelachutz* — is the space of the cervix itself considered like the interior or like the exterior? This is not merely theoretical. There is anatomical width to the cervix, and indeed medicine describes two distinct openings: the inner os (the opening between the uterus and the cervix) and the outer os (the opening between the cervix and the vaginal canal). Does she become a *niddah* when blood enters the cervix from the uterus, or only when it exits the cervix into the vaginal canal?

### Rabbi Zakkai

*Ta shema detani Rabbi Zakkai: ad bein hashinayim u'vein hashinayim atzman kelifnim* — come and hear, for *Rabbi Zakkai* taught: until between the teeth, and between the teeth themselves are like the inside. The cervix itself is considered interior. A woman does not become a *niddah* while blood is still within the cervix; she becomes a *niddah* only when blood passes the cervical os and enters the vaginal canal. This is the critical *halacha*: the threshold of *niddah* status is the outer opening of the cervix.

### Makom Disha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: מְקוֹם דִּישָׁה. מַאי ״מְקוֹם דִּישָׁה״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מְקוֹם שֶׁהַשַּׁמָּשׁ דָּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught in a *baraita*** that a woman becomes impure when the blood reaches the **place of threshing,** which is a euphemism. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of this euphemism, the **place of threshing? Rav Yehuda says:** It is referring to the **place** in the vagina **where the penis threshes,** i.e., reaches, during intercourse.

A *braisa* adds a further definition of the same boundary: *makom disha* — the place of threshing. *Amar Rav Yehuda: makom she'ha'shamash dash* — the place where the male member threshes, i.e., the depth reached during intercourse. This description matters practically for the *halacha* of *bedikos*. The *Beit Yosef* and subsequent *poskim* grappled with the question of how deep a *bedikah* cloth must be inserted: if a woman becomes a *niddah* as soon as blood passes the cervix, must she insert the cloth all the way to the cervix? The *poskim* — notably citing discussions with women who confirmed the physical constraints — rule that inserting a finger as deeply as possible is sufficient, and the *makom disha* formulation supports this: a finger penetrates roughly the same depth as the *shamash*, and that depth reaches the relevant threshold. The cloth wrapped around the finger and inserted to full depth adequately covers the *beit hachitzon*.

## Blood & Semen Tumah

### Bivesha Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״ — מְלַמֵּד שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ, וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא נִדָּה, זָבָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״זָבָה בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: It is written concerning a menstruating woman: "And if a woman has an issue, and her flow in her flesh shall be blood, she shall be in her menstruation seven days" (Leviticus 15:19). The term **"in her flesh" teaches that she becomes impure** while the blood is still **inside** her flesh just **as** when the blood emerges **outside** her body. **I have** derived **only** that this applies in the case of **a menstruating woman. From where** is it derived that it applies to **a *zava*** as well? **The** same **verse states: "Her flow [*zovah*] in her flesh."**

The *Gemara* now transitions from the threshold of *niddah* status to the derivations that underlie it, and from there into a parallel discussion of *tum'ah* through seminal emission. *Tanu Rabban*: *bi'vesarah* — "in her flesh" — *melamed she'metame'ah mi'bifnim ke'vi'chutz* — teaches that a woman becomes *teme'ah* as a *niddah* even when the flow is interior, just as when it is exterior. The verse is *ishah ki tihyeh zov dam yihyeh zovah bi'vesarah* — "a woman who has a flow of blood, her flow shall be in her flesh." The phrase *bi'vesarah* — in her flesh — is the textual anchor for the rule that interior flow counts.

### Zavah Interior

*Ein li ela niddah, zavah minayin?* — I know this for a *niddah*, but how do I know the same applies to a *zavah*? From the word *zovah* — her flow — *zovah bi'vesarah*, the language of flow "in her flesh" encompasses the *zavah* as well. Both a *niddah* and a *zavah* therefore become *teme'ah* even from an interior flow.

### Seminal Discharge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פּוֹלֶטֶת שִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״יִהְיֶה״. וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: דַּיָּהּ כְּבוֹעֲלָהּ — מָה בּוֹעֲלָהּ אֵינוֹ מְטַמֵּא עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא טוּמְאָה לַחוּץ, אַף הִיא אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה עַד שֶׁתֵּצֵא טוּמְאָתָהּ לַחוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** **From where** is it derived that this also applies to a woman who **discharges semen** after intercourse? **The** same **verse states** the apparently superfluous term **"shall be." And Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of discharging semen, **it is sufficient for her** to be **like the man who engaged in intercourse with her: Just as the man who engaged in intercourse with her does not become impure until** the source of **impurity,** the semen, **emerges outside** his body, **so too, she does not become impure until her** source of **impurity,** the semen, **emerges outside** her body. It does not render her impure while it is still inside her body.

*Poletet shichvat zera minayin?* — how do I know this rule extends to a woman who discharges semen? A woman becomes *teme'ah* when semen flows from her body, but how do I know she becomes *teme'ah* even when the semen has not yet exited — when it is still flowing internally? *Talmud lomar ve'yihyeh* — the word *ve'yihyeh*, "and it shall be," adds a further inclusion, extending the *tum'ah* of interior flow to the case of seminal discharge as well.

*Rabbi Shimon* dissents: *dayah ke'voalah* — she is no worse than the man who had relations with her. *Mah voalah eino metamei ad she'teitzei tum'ah la'chutz* — just as the man becomes *tamei* only when the semen leaves his body, *af hi einah mitamma'ah ad she'teitzei tumatah la'chutz* — she too does not become *teme'ah* until the seminal discharge exits her body. The conceptual question here is how to categorize the *tum'ah* of a woman's seminal discharge: is it analogous to the *tum'ah* of a man whose semen exits his body — in which case the threshold is when the fluid leaves — or is it analogous to a woman's own blood flow, which generates *tum'ah* even while interior? The *Chachamim* treat it as a woman's internal flow; *Rabbi Shimon* treats it as analogous to the man's external emission.

### Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דַּיָּהּ כְּבוֹעֲלָהּ? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״וְרָחֲצוּ בַמַּיִם וְטָמְאוּ עַד הָעָרֶב״, אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי מָה בָּא זֶה לְלַמְּדֵנוּ? אִם לְעִנְיַן נוֹגֵעַ בְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע — הֲרֵי כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר לְמַטָּה: ״אוֹ אִישׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Shimon** in fact **hold** that **it is sufficient for her** to be **like the man who engaged in intercourse with her? But isn't it taught** to the contrary in a *baraita*: The verse states: "The woman also with whom a man shall lie carnally, **they shall both bathe themselves in water, and be impure until the evening"** (Leviticus 15:18). **Rabbi Shimon said: And what does this** verse **come to teach us? If** it teaches **with regard to** one who **comes into contact with semen** that they are impure, **it is already stated below** (Leviticus 22:4): **"Or a man** from whom the flow of seed goes out," from which it is derived that coming into contact with semen renders one impure.

A difficulty arises: does *Rabbi Shimon* truly hold that a woman only becomes *teme'ah* through seminal discharge when it exits her body? A *braita* quotes the verse *ve'rachatzu ba'mayim ve'tame'u ad ha'erev* — that both a man and woman must immerse after intercourse with seminal emission. *Rabbi Shimon* himself asks there: what is the *Torah* teaching us by requiring that the woman immerse? If it were simply because she came into contact with semen — *im le'nogei'a be'shichvas zera* — that is already derivable from a later verse which teaches that touching semen renders one *tamei*. Why would the woman's *tum'ah* from intercourse need its own verse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁטּוּמְאַת בֵּית הַסְּתָרִים הִיא, וְטוּמְאַת בֵּית הַסְּתָרִים אֵינָהּ מְטַמְּאָה, אֶלָּא שֶׁגְּזֵרַת הַכָּתוּב הִיא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this verse is necessary **because** in the case of intercourse the contact with the source of **impurity** occurs in **a concealed part** of the body, **and** contact with **impurity** by **a concealed part** of the body generally **does not render** one **impure. But** here **it is a Torah edict** that the woman does become impure in this manner. This *baraita* proves that according to Rabbi Shimon a woman is rendered impure by semen even when it is inside her body.

*Rabbi Shimon*'s answer: *ela mipnei she'tum'as beit hastarim hi* — because the contact is internal, it is *tum'as beit hastarim*, and contact *tum'ah* generally applies only to external contact. If a *tamei* object touches only the interior of the body without making contact with exposed skin, it does not convey *tum'ah*. Therefore a woman having intercourse, with the semen entirely inside her body, would not become *teme'ah* through the standard rules of contact. The verse is needed to tell us that despite this, she is *teme'ah* — a special scriptural edict, *gezeiras hakasuv*. This means *Rabbi Shimon* holds that a woman becomes *teme'ah* from the act of intercourse with seminal emission even when the semen is wholly internal. This appears to directly contradict his ruling that a woman's seminal discharge only conveys *tum'ah* when it exits the body.

### Two Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא — כָּאן בִּמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת, כָּאן בְּפוֹלֶטֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is **not difficult. Here,** this second *baraita* is dealing **with** a woman who **engages in intercourse,** whereas **there,** the first *baraita* is dealing **with** a woman who **discharges** semen after intercourse. It is only during the act of intercourse that a woman becomes impure due to the semen. If she later discharges semen, she does not become impure, according to Rabbi Shimon, until the semen leaves her body and touches her on the outside.

*Lo kashya* — the *Gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *kan be'mishameshes, kan be'poleses* — the two rulings address two entirely different categories of *tum'ah*. *Mishameshes* is the woman who has just engaged in intercourse with seminal emission. Her *tum'ah* is a special category generated by the act of intercourse itself, and it applies even when the semen is entirely interior. *Rabbi Shimon* agrees with this; it is derived from the verse requiring immersion, and it is a *gezeiras hakasuv* overriding the usual *beit hastarim* limitation.

*Poleses* — or *poletet* — is a distinct scenario: a day has passed, the woman has already immersed and is *tahor* from the *tum'ah* of *mishameshes*, but semen retained in her body is now flowing out. This is a different *tum'ah*, structurally analogous to a man whose semen flows from his body. It is not connected to the act of intercourse; it is a discharge of semen from her body as such. Here *Rabbi Shimon* maintains that this is like the man's case: the *tum'ah* takes effect only when the discharge actually exits the body. The *Chachamim* disagree and apply the woman's *bi'vesarah* rule even here.

The conceptual elegance of this resolution is considerable. The *Torah*'s treatment of a woman's *tum'ah* through semen generates two distinct legal categories that look similar but operate under different rules: the *tum'ah* of intercourse itself (*mishameshes*), which is interior by definition and which all agree conveys *tum'ah*; and the *tum'ah* of a later seminal discharge (*poleses*), which is a hybrid — semen flowing from a woman's body — and about which *Rabbi Shimon* and the *Chachamim* dispute whether to treat it on the model of male *tum'ah* (requiring external exit) or female *tum'ah* (applying even to interior flow).
