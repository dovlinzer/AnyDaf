# Temurah 17 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodshim vs. Temurah

### Mishnah Setup

The daf begins by picking up on the *mishnah* at the bottom of 15b. After a fascinating *gemara* on *halacha lemoshe misinai* and *mesorah*, the discussion returns to a direct analysis of *temurah* — but characteristically, it does so within a comparative framework. Just as earlier material contrasted *korbanos tzibur* with *korbanos yachid*, this *mishnah* compares *temurah* to direct sanctification. Since *temurah* involves attempting to transfer *kedusha* onto another animal, it is natural to ask: in what ways does this resemble, and in what ways does it differ from, an original act of sanctification? The *mishnah* frames this as a two-directional comparison: *chomer bekodshim mibetemurah vechomer betemurah mibekodshim* — there are respects in which sanctification carries greater weight, and respects in which *temurah* carries greater weight.

### Sanctify > Temurah

The first set of cases establishes where direct sanctification has greater weight. *Hakodshim osin temurah ve'ein temurah osah temurah*: sanctified animals can generate a *temurah*, but a *temurah* cannot generate a further *temurah*. The *kedusha* cannot be daisy-chained; only the original animal, not its *temurah*, can serve as a source for the act.

Furthermore, *hatzibur vehashutfim makdishim aval lo memirim*: the community and partners can sanctify but cannot perform *temurah*. This addresses both the animal and the act. The *tzibur* acquires its communal *korban* through sanctification using communal funds; partners sanctify jointly even if only one partner utters the words. But neither the *tzibur* nor *shutfim* can perform *temurah*, and a *korban tzibur* or *korban shutfim* cannot be the subject of *temurah*.

Additionally, *umakdishim ubarim ve'eivarim aval lo memirim*: fetuses and limbs can be sanctified, but *temurah* cannot be performed from them, or to them. As established in an earlier *sugya*, the Torah's language of *behemah bibehemah* requires that the act begin with a whole *behemah* and conclude with a whole *behemah*.

### Temurah > Sanctify

Now the *mishnah* turns to the ways in which *temurah* carries greater weight: *shehakedusha chalah al ba'al mum kavua ve'eino yotzei lechulin legazez vela'avod*. The printed text reads *shehakedusha chalah*, but as the *hagahot habach* notes, the correct reading should be *shehatemurah chalah* — for this clause describes the greater weight of *temurah*, not of sanctification generally.

The underlying principle is this: if one sanctifies an unblemished animal directly and it subsequently develops a blemish, it cannot be offered as a *korban* and must be redeemed. After redemption, however, a residual *kedusha* remains — the animal may not be shorn or worked, because it was once in a state of *kedushat haguf*. By contrast, if one directly sanctifies an animal that already has a blemish, the animal never attains *kedushat haguf* in the first place. Since it was never fit to be a *korban*, the act is analogous to sanctifying one's woodpile or one's car: it produces only *kedushat damim*, monetary sanctity, and upon redemption no residual *kedusha* remains.

*Temurah* operates differently. The Torah states *vehayah hu utemurato yihyeh kodesh* — the exchanged animal becomes *kodesh* just as the original. This applies even if the animal designated as *temurah* already had a blemish. The pasuk *ra betov o tov bera* — one cannot switch good for bad or bad for good — and the ruling of *chazal* that this refers to blemished animals, teaches that even when *temurah* is attempted onto a blemished animal, the *kedusha* takes effect in full, as *kedushat haguf*. Accordingly, when this blemished *temurah* is eventually redeemed, a residual *kedusha* remains, just as with any animal that possessed *kedushat haguf*. Here, then, *temurah* is weightier: the *kedusha* takes hold even where direct sanctification would not have produced *kedushat haguf* at all.

## Shogeg kemezid

### Position Statement

The *mishnah* continues: *Rabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda omer, asah shogeg kemezid betemurah velo asah shogeg kemezid bemukdashin*. Rabbi Yose son of Rabbi Yehuda holds that by *temurah*, unintentional action is treated like intentional action, but this principle does not apply to original sanctification. The *gemara* will spend considerable effort unpacking what precisely this means, as will be seen below. The basis offered is the pasuk *yihyeh kodesh* — the word *yihyeh* is read as an amplification that extends the effect of *temurah* even to unintentional cases.

## Excluded Animals

### R. Eliezer Statement

*Rabbi Eliezer omer: hakilayim vehaterifa vehayotzei dofen vehatumtum veha'androgynos lo kedoshim velo makdishim*. Although the *mishnah* has just established that *temurah* takes effect even on a *ba'al mum*, Rabbi Eliezer teaches that certain categories are treated more severely — or more precisely, differently — than a *ba'al mum*: a crossbred animal (*kilayim*), a mortally wounded animal (*treifa*), one born by Caesarean section (*yotzei dofen*), and animals of uncertain or mixed gender (*tumtum* and *androgynos*). None of these become sanctified through *temurah*, and none of them can serve as the source of *temurah*.

This does not mean, it should be noted at the outset, that these animals cannot be sanctified at all. What is excluded is *kedushat haguf* — intrinsic sanctity, the sanctity of an animal designated for the altar. They can still be subject to *kedushat damim*, monetary sanctity, much like sanctifying a car or a woodpile. But *kedushat haguf* is foreclosed to them. Nor can they serve as the source from which *temurah* is generated, since *temurah* can only flow from something that already possesses *kedushat haguf*. The *gemara* will explore all of this further below.

## Shogeg kemezid…

### Lokeh vs. Kadosh

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ameih deRabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda?* What is the reasoning? The answer is that the Torah says *yihyeh kodesh* — this amplified language extends the law to unintentional cases as well.

But the *gemara* then asks: *heichi dami shogeg kemezid?* What exactly are the cases? Before examining them, a fundamental question must be posed. Anyone glancing at this passage in the *gemara* will notice an abundance of textual variants, parenthetical corrections, and notational marginalia. This reflects a genuine interpretive dispute about what Rabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda is even teaching.

*Temurah* has two dimensions: there is the prohibition against performing the act, and there is the effect — the second animal becomes sanctified regardless. The first dimension involves the actor's culpability; the second concerns the mechanics of *kedusha*. The critical question is: which dimension does Rabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda's statement address? Is he saying that *shogeg kemezid* means one receives *malkot* even for an unintentional act of *temurah* — a remarkable *chiddush* given that lashes ordinarily require *mezid*? Or is he saying that the *effect* of *temurah*, the sanctification of the second animal, takes hold even in cases of error?

The textual evidence for this ambiguity is explicit. *Tosafot* notes: *ve'ika sefarim degarsi gabei tmura lokeh gabei kodshim lo lokeh* — some texts read the ruling in terms of lashes — while other versions read *gabei tmura kadish vegabei kodshim lo kadish* — the ruling is about whether sanctification takes effect. According to *Tosafot*, two major *ba'alei hatosafot* adopted these two readings consistently: Rabbeinu Shimshon (Rav Shimshon miShantz) and Rabbeinu Baruch. Each either worked from a different *girsa* of the *gemara* or interpreted the same text in fundamentally different ways, and their respective readings pervade their treatment of all the cases that follow.

### Olah/Shelamim Case

The *gemara* cites Chizkiya's analysis first: *kasavar shemutar lehamir* — the actor believed the act of *temurah* was permitted. According to the version where lashes are at stake: *gabei tmura laki, gabei kodshim lo laki* — by *temurah* one receives lashes, while by *kodshim* one does not. The comparison to *kodshim* is meaningful because there is in fact a way to incur lashes for an act of sanctification: the prohibition of *hamakdish ba'al mum* — sanctifying a blemished animal — is a *lav*, and the *gemara* has established elsewhere that when a speech-act (*dibbur*) has a legally operative effect, one can receive *malkot* for it. So the comparison is between two scenarios where a verbal act of sanctification produces lashes: one via *temurah*, one via directly sanctifying a *ba'al mum*. The *chiddush* is that *temurah* generates lashes even when the actor believed the act was permissible — a form of *shogeg*.

The alternative *girsa* — *gabei tmura kadish gabei kodshim lo kadish* — raises a difficulty. If the issue were about the *effect*, why would *omer mutar*, believing it to be permitted, prevent the act from taking effect by direct sanctification? If someone tries to sanctify an animal thinking it is permitted, the animal is sanctified. *Omer mutar* has no obvious bearing on whether the *kedusha* takes hold. This reinforces the reading that the debate in this passage concerns lashes, not effect.

Resh Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan offer a different paradigm: *kasavar lomar tmuras olah ve'amar tmuras shelamim* — the person intended to designate an animal as a *temurah* for his *olah* but said *temurah* for his *shelamim*, or vice versa. What emerged from his mouth differed from what was in his mind. In ordinary sanctification, the rule is *kedusha betaos lo kedusha* — a speech-act of *hafla'ah* that does not match the speaker's intent does not take effect. The words must express the actual intent; *devarim shebelev einam devarim* does not apply here, and the dispute of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel is resolved in favor of Beit Hillel: an erroneous designation does not take effect. But by *temurah*, the word *yihyeh kodesh* extends the effect even to this case of verbal error. This answer clearly concerns the *effect* of the act, not lashes — the question is whether the designation takes hold despite the discrepancy between intent and utterance.

The logic, moreover, is coherent: when creating *kedusha* from scratch, the speech-act must be precise and complete, since the act itself generates the sanctity. But *temurah* operates differently — the *kedusha* already exists in the source animal, and the act merely transfers it. It requires less perfection in execution for a transfer to succeed. Just as *temurah* takes effect on a *ba'al mum* in a way that direct sanctification does not, so too it takes effect even in a case of verbal error.

### Black/White Case

The same principle governs the next case: *kasavar lomar lishachor ve'amar lalavan* — one intended to say "I am exchanging for the black animal" but said "the white one." Again, the question is whether the discrepancy between intent and utterance prevents the act from taking effect. The two *girsa'ot* again appear: by *temurah*, it either takes effect (*kadish*) or one receives lashes (*laki*); by *kodshim*, it does not. The case most naturally concerns the question of effect, since the issue is squarely about *kedusha betaos*, whether a mistaken designation has legal force.

### Stipulation Case

Rabbi Yochanan adds a further case: *be'omer tetzei zu vetikanes zu* — the person explicitly stipulates that he wants this animal to exit its sanctity and that one to enter it in its place. In other words, he makes the effectiveness of the *temurah* conditional on the original animal's *kedusha* being transferred away. This is a surprising case. One might think such a stipulation could limit the act — after all, the person is explicitly stating he does not want the *kedusha* to take hold unless the condition is met. And yet *yihyeh kodesh* teaches that the designation takes effect regardless. The *gemara* records this as a distinct case, apparently because the person's explicit stipulation represents a particular form of misunderstanding about the act's mechanics.

### Pre-intent Case

Rav Sheshet presents yet another scenario: *be'omer ekaneis le-bayit zeh ve-akdish ve-amir mida'ati* — someone says in advance, "When I enter that house I intend to sanctify" or "I intend to perform *temurah* intentionally," and then goes in and does so *shelo mida'ato* — without conscious intention at the moment of the act. Rashi understands the person as a *ba'al machshavot*, a distracted or absent-minded individual whose head is elsewhere; he had previously declared his intention, but at the moment of execution his attention had wandered and the act was performed on autopilot.

By *temurah*, the advance declaration of intent frames the subsequent act, and lashes apply (or the effect takes hold, depending on the *girsa*); by *kodshim*, since the person was not consciously intending the act at the moment, there is no consequence.

*Tosafot* offers an alternative reading: the person makes a *tnai*, a formal condition — "when I enter this house, let this animal become a *temurah*" (or "let it be sanctified") — and then when he actually walks into the house, he has completely forgotten the stipulation and is simply going in for a drink of water. The condition was met, but he was wholly unaware of it at the time. On this reading, whether the *kedusha* takes effect is the natural question, since the condition was satisfied independently of his consciousness. But why would the mere forgetting of a *tnai* prevent the effect in a case of direct *kodshim*? This puzzle reinforces the view that Rav Sheshet's case is primarily about lashes — the question is whether one is culpable for *malkot* when one was not consciously willing the transgression at the moment it occurred.

Stepping back, these cases appear to reflect two different lines of analysis: some answers treat the *chiddush* as concerning lashes, others as concerning the effect. Different *amora'im* may have understood Rabbi Yose berabbi Yehuda's statement differently, and the underlying dispute between the *ba'alei hatosafot* is really a dispute among the *amora'im* themselves. If the *chiddush* concerns lashes, then something deeply unusual is being asserted — that one can receive *malkot* even without full willfulness — and the *gemara* works hard to limit such cases as narrowly as possible. If the *chiddush* concerns effect, then the *gemara* is explaining why *temurah* can succeed even where a direct act of sanctification would fail, on the reasoning that transferring existing *kedusha* requires less precision than creating it from nothing.

## Excluded Animals…

### Shmuel Position

Having concluded the discussion of *shogeg kemezid*, the *gemara* turns back to Rabbi Eliezer's list of excluded animals. The *gemara* cites the *baraita* of Rabbi Meir: *ume'achar she'einan kedoshim heich makdishim?* If these animals cannot become sanctified, how could they ever serve as the source of *temurah*? This observation underscores that the exclusion is comprehensive — they cannot receive *kedushat haguf* through *temurah*, and there is equally no direct route to *kedushat haguf* through ordinary sanctification. The only exceptional scenarios in which such an animal might acquire *kedushat haguf* are: (a) an animal was sanctified while unblemished and then became a *treifa*; (b) a fetus was sanctified in utero and then born by Caesarean section; or (c) the mother was sanctified and then gave birth to such an animal, so that the offspring derived its *kedushat haguf* from the mother — and this third case holds only according to Rabbi Yehuda, who maintains that offspring of *kodshim* can perform *temurah*.

Shmuel, however, stakes out a strikingly different position: *hamakdish et hatreifa tzricha mum kavua lifdota alav* — if one sanctifies a *treifa*, the animal requires a formal *mum kavua* (a fixed blemish) in order to be redeemed. The necessity of a blemish for redemption implies that the animal has attained *kedushat haguf*, not merely *kedushat damim*. Shmuel thus appears to hold that directly sanctifying a *treifa* does confer *kedushat haguf*.

### Rav Oshaya

Rav Oshaya rejects this position directly: *eina ela ke-makdish eitzim va'avanim beva'alei mumin* — sanctifying such an animal is like sanctifying wood and stones in the case of *ba'alei mumin*. The act produces only *kedushat damim*, monetary sanctity, not *kedushat haguf*. Given that a *treifa* is worse than a *ba'al mum* in terms of its fitness to be brought as a *korban*, and we already know that directly sanctifying a *ba'al mum* does not produce *kedushat haguf*, it follows all the more that sanctifying a *treifa* cannot do so either. The practical consequence of *kedushat damim* is that the sanctity can be removed through a simple act of redemption without requiring a formal *mum*.

The *gemara* challenges Shmuel's position from the *mishnah* that states: *kol hakodashim shena'asu treifa ein podim osam lefei she'ein podin et hakodashim leha'achilan laklavim* — all sanctified animals that became *treifa* cannot be redeemed, since one may not redeem *kodshim* merely to feed them to dogs. The *gemara* notes: *ta'ama shena'asu* — the scenario in the *mishnah* is that they *became* *treifa* after sanctification. This implies: *ha hayu mei'ikara* — if it was a *treifa* from the start, one *could* redeem it, which would support Shmuel. But the *gemara* deflects this: perhaps that *tanna* holds *kol heicha delo chazi legufah lo nachsa lei kedushat haguf* — wherever an animal cannot be used as a *korban*, *kedushat haguf* does not descend upon it at all. So the *mishnah* does not demonstrate Shmuel's view; it reflects a different authority.

Indeed, the *gemara* presses further: Shmuel himself explained Rabbi Eliezer's statement as meaning *lo kedoshim bitemurah velo makdishim la'asot temurah* — they cannot become *kodesh* through *temurah* and cannot generate *temurah*. But from Rabbi Meir's *baraita* it is evident that they cannot become *kodesh* through direct sanctification either. Shmuel is ultimately driven to say that Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Meir represent one opinion, and that he himself disagrees with them — holding that these animals *can* receive *kedushat haguf* through direct sanctification. Implicitly, Shmuel reads Rabbi Eliezer's ruling as a minority view, and maintains the opposite position.

### Category Distinction

Having laid out the controversy, Rav Pappa asks the pointed question: *mai ta'ama deRabbi Eliezer?* What is Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning for treating these animals so differently from a *ba'al mum*? The answer proposed is: *kebehemah teme'ah* — Rabbi Eliezer compares them to a non-kosher animal. Just as a non-kosher animal is categorically ineligible to serve as a *korban* and cannot attain *kedushat haguf* whether through direct sanctification or *temurah*, these animals too, since they cannot be brought as *korbanos*, are placed in the same categorical exclusion.

Rav Pappa then turns to Rava and asks: *veharei ba'al mum* — why are these treated differently from a *ba'al mum*? After all, even a *ba'al mum* cannot be offered as a *korban*. Rava answers: *ba'al mum karev be-mino* — other animals of the same category and type are indeed brought; the *ba'al mum* is still a sheep or an ox, just one with a defect. These other animals, by contrast, are viewed as belonging to a categorically different grouping altogether. The comparison to a non-kosher animal is apposite precisely because the same categorical othering applies.

### Psul Guf Analysis

Rava sharpens his reasoning: *mah behemah teme'ah psula guf, af go psula guf* — just as a non-kosher animal is disqualified by something intrinsic to its identity (*psul guf*), these animals too are disqualified by an intrinsic quality of their body. *Ba'al mum*, by contrast, is categorized as *psulei chisaron* — a disqualification of lacking, of deficiency, not of essential identity.

It is worth noting what the *gemara* does *not* mean by this. The distinction is not purely physical — that is, it is not simply that a *ba'al mum* has something physically missing while these others are whole. The *gemara* will immediately challenge that reading. Rather, the conceptual distinction is between an animal that is viewed as intrinsically of a different type versus an animal that is essentially the same type but happens to be lacking in some respect.

### Challenge to Rava

The *gemara* challenges this immediately: *amar lei Rav Ada leRava, velo saru'a vekalut katav beparasha* — the Torah lists *saru'a* (an overgrown limb) and *kalut* (fused hooves) among the *mumim*. These are not cases of physical lacking; they are cases where the body has something extra or something differently configured. Yet they are categorized as *ba'alei mumin*, the class Rava is claiming is defined by *psulei chisaron*. So the definition of *ba'al mum* cannot be reduced to physical lacking.

In defense of Rava's conceptual approach: the intent is not that every *mum* involves the physical absence of something, but rather that *ba'alei mumin* are conceptually framed as animals that are essentially of the same type as fit *korbanos* but fall short in some respect — they do not quite meet the standard, yet they belong to the same genus. The excluded categories, by contrast, are framed as categorically other, regardless of whether the "otherness" is physically observable. For *kilayim* and the gender-ambiguous animals (*tumtum* and *androgynos*), this categorization is readily understood; for *yotzei dofen*, Rashi notes that in people's minds a Caesarean birth placed the animal in an entirely different category — so unusual was it that the animal was perceived as wholly other.

### Behemah Tmeia

The *gemara* then acknowledges the remaining difficulty: what about *treifa*? An animal that has a hole in its lung is still a sheep; it has not changed species or category. Rava's first formulation — that these are *psulei guf* in the sense of being categorically other — does not straightforwardly apply to *treifa* in the way it does to *kilayim* or *tumtum*. This is why the *gemara* challenged only on *treifa* and not on *yotzei dofen*.

Rava therefore offers a complementary answer for *treifa*: *behemah teme'ah asura ba'achilah, vetreifa asura ba'achilah, le'apukei ba'al mum demutar ba'achilah* — a non-kosher animal cannot be eaten, a *treifa* cannot be eaten, whereas a *ba'al mum* may be eaten after redemption. The *treifa* is thus excluded not because it is categorically of a different type like a non-kosher animal, but because it shares with the non-kosher animal the decisive characteristic of being forbidden for consumption. This ineligibility to be eaten places it in the same functional category as *behemah teme'ah*, and on that basis it receives the same exclusion from *temurah* and *kedushat haguf*.

The full picture that emerges from Rava is: the excluded animals differ from *ba'al mum* for one of two reasons. Some are categorically viewed as a different type of creature altogether (*kilayim*, *tumtum*, *androgynos*, *yotzei dofen* in the popular imagination). Others — specifically *treifa* — are not categorically different but are excluded by analogy to *behemah teme'ah* on account of their inability to be eaten. Either way, they stand apart from *ba'al mum*, which is ultimately still the same type of animal, merely lacking in some quality that the *beit hamikdash* demands.

## Vlad Shelamim

### Mishnah on Vlad

The daf concludes with the opening of a new *perek*: *elu kodshim shevladosem vetmurosem keyotzei bahen* — the following *kodshim* whose offspring and *temurot* have the same status as themselves and may even be offered as *korbanos*. The *mishnah* begins with *shelamim*: *vlad shelamim vetemurasan, vladan vladan vlad vladan ad sof kol ha'olam, harei hen shelamim* — the offspring of a *shelamim*, its *temurah*, and all subsequent generations of offspring to the end of time are themselves *shelamim*. They require the full treatment of *shelamim*: *semicha*, the wine and flour libations (*nesachim*), and the waving of the breast and thigh (*tenufat chazeh veshok*). They are treated as *shelamim* in every respect. The one limitation that follows from earlier *halachot* is that a *temurah* cannot itself generate a further *temurah* — *ein temurah osah temurah* — but for the chain of offspring, *vlad vevladei vladan ad sof kol ha'olam*, there is no such restriction.

### End of Time

The *gemara* asks: *keivan detanya vladan vevladei vladan, lamma li ad sof kol ha'olam?* If the *mishnah* already teaches that the ruling applies to children and grandchildren, why add "until the end of time"? Surely this is superfluous. The *gemara* answers: *shema leih leRabbi Eliezer de'amar vlad shelamim lo yikrav shelamim* — the phrase is addressing Rabbi Eliezer, who holds that the offspring of a *shelamim* cannot itself be offered as a *shelamim*. Our *tanna* is responding with pointed emphasis: not only do I disagree regarding the children, as I would not concede even that to you, but I extend the ruling to grandchildren and all subsequent generations to the end of time. The exaggerated formulation signals that the *mishnah* is deliberately rejecting Rabbi Eliezer's position in the strongest possible terms. The underlying intuition Rabbi Eliezer might invoke — that later generations are like lower-quality copies of an original, progressively less identical to their source — is explicitly repudiated.

### Zachar Source

The *gemara* proceeds to identify the scriptural basis: where do we know that a *temurah* or offspring of a *shelamim* may be offered as a *korban*? A *baraita* derives this from the verse regarding *shelamim*: *im zachar im nekeivah* — whether male or female. Since the verse could have simply stated the relevant law without specifying gender, the specification must be coming to include additional cases. The word *zachar* comes to include the *vlad* — even an offspring of a *shelamim* may be brought.

One might have thought to derive this from a *kal vachomer*: if a *temurah*, which was not itself gestated within a *korban*, can be brought, then certainly a *vlad*, which was, should be brought. But the *kal vachomer* can be deflected: the *chiddush* of *temurah* is that it applies across all categories of *kodshim*, whereas *vlad* is only possible with female animals, which do not include all categories of *korbanos* (there is no *vlad* from an *olah*-male or an *asham*). Since *vlad* does not apply universally, perhaps it should not be brought. The verse *im zachar* therefore comes explicitly to include the offspring.

### Nekeivah Source

The verse's mention of *nekeivah* then addresses *temurah*: *kodesh yekadash* — the word *nekeivah* (or the grammatical phrase in which it appears) comes to include the *temurah* of a *ba'al mum* and the *vlad* of a *ba'al mum*. Without this derivation, one might have thought that since a *ba'al mum* cannot itself be brought as a *korban*, its offspring or *temurah* should equally be restricted. The verse teaches otherwise — even these attain the status and can, in terms of their inherent *kedusha*, be treated as *shelamim*, though how far this extends (whether they can actually be offered) remains a subject for further analysis.

### Word Gender Debate

A brief but pointed exchange between Rav Safra and Abaye addresses the assignment of the *derashos*. Rav Safra asks: *ipcha mana?* Why derive the *vlad* from *zachar* and the *temurah* from *nekeivah*? Why not reverse the derivations — learn the *temurah* from *zachar* and the *vlad* from *nekeivah*?

Abaye initially understands the question as asking why the *alef-mem* (*im*) prefixes are not learned cross-contextually, but Rav Safra clarifies: *hachi ka'amina lach* — that was not his question. Of course the *im* modifying *zachar* will be learned in conjunction with *zachar*, and the *im* modifying *nekeivah* will be learned in conjunction with *nekeivah*. The real question is about the original assignment: why is *zachar* the source for *vlad* and *nekeivah* the source for *temurah*, rather than the reverse?

Abaye answers: *vlad lashon zachar mashma, temurah lashon nekeivah mashma* — the word *vlad* is grammatically masculine, so it is naturally derived from the masculine term *zachar*; the word *temurah* is grammatically feminine, so it is naturally derived from the feminine *nekeivah*. The derivation follows the grammatical gender of the rabbinic term, not the biological sex of the animal in question. The discussion of the precise import of each derivation — specifically regarding whether the offspring and *temurah* of a *ba'al mum* can actually be offered as a *korban* — will be taken up in the following daf.
