# Bava Batra 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Sets of Terms

### Selling Chatzer & Zivlah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָכַר אַשְׁפָּה – מָכַר זִבְלָהּ. מָכַר בּוֹר – מָכַר מֵימֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** One who **sold a dunghill has sold its manure. One who sold a cistern has sold its water.**

The daf begins with the *mishnah*'s ruling on the sale of a *chatzer* — a dung heap or refuse area. When one sells such a space, the sale includes not only the ground or the pit itself but also the manure and garbage contained within, which can carry significant monetary value. Similarly, the *mishnah* rules that when one sells a cistern, the water inside it is included in the sale.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: כׇּל הָרָאוּי לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלֹא לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת; לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת וְלֹא לַמִּזְבֵּחַ; לֹא לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְלֹא לְבֶדֶק הַבַּיִת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן וּבְמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **elsewhere** (*Me'ila* 12b): With regard to **any** item that is **fit to** be sacrificed on **the altar but** is **not** fit **for Temple maintenance,** or is fit **for Temple maintenance but not for the altar,** or items that are fit **neither for the altar nor for Temple maintenance** in their current state and are slated to be sold with the profit used for the Temple, in all these cases if one consecrates these items and then derives benefit **from them or their contents** for a non-sacred purpose, he thereby is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property and is obligated to bring an offering as atonement.

The *gemara* then invokes a *baraita* from tractate *Me'ilah*: *kol harauy lamizbe'ach velo libedek habayis* — anything fit to be placed upon the *mizbe'ach* but not fit for *bedek habayis*, the construction and maintenance of the *Beis Hamikdash*, is subject to the laws of *me'ilah*, as is anything it contains.

An unblemished animal is the paradigmatic example of something fit for the *mizbe'ach*. Such an animal is not fit for *bedek habayis* — not because one could not theoretically put a sheep into the walls of the *Beis Hamikdash*, but because an unblemished animal that one wishes to sanctify for monetary proceeds of the Temple would constitute a violation of a positive commandment: an unblemished animal, once sanctified, must be dedicated to the *mizbe'ach* itself.

### Four Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? הִקְדִּישׁ בּוֹר מְלֵאָה מַיִם; אַשְׁפּוֹת מְלֵאוֹת זֶבֶל; שׁוֹבָךְ מָלֵא יוֹנִים; שָׂדֶה מְלֵאָה עֲשָׂבִים; אִילָן נָשׂוּי פֵּירוֹת – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן וּבְמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** One who **consecrated a cistern filled with water, dunghills filled with manure, a dovecote filled with doves, a field filled with plants,** or **a tree bearing fruit,** and subsequently derived benefit **from them or their contents** is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property.

Before proceeding further, it is important to identify two distinct sets of terms that are often conflated but must be kept separate.

The first set is *kedushat haguf* versus *kedushat damim*. *Kedushat haguf* means that the thing itself will be used for a *Beis Hamikdash* purpose — it will physically serve in the Temple's service. *Kedushat damim* means the object is valuable only for its monetary proceeds: it will be sold and the money put to use. The object itself is of no direct ritual interest; only its value matters.

The second set is *kedushat mizbe'ach* versus *kedushat bedek habayis*. *Kedushat mizbe'ach* refers to sanctification for use on the altar — sacrificial animals, flour, wine, and the like. *Kedushat bedek habayis* refers to sanctification for the upkeep and maintenance of the Temple. The natural assumption is that *kedushat mizbe'ach* corresponds to *kedushat haguf* (the object itself goes on the altar) and that *kedushat bedek habayis* corresponds to *kedushat damim* (the object will be sold and the proceeds used). But this alignment is not exact.

There are in fact two types of *kedushat bedek habayis*. One type involves the object itself being used directly — construction beams, gold plating, marble for the floor, materials built into the structure of the *Beis Hamikdash*. These would constitute *kedushat haguf* within the framework of *kedushat bedek habayis*. The other type, however, involves objects whose value is the point — a plow donated to work the fields of *hekdesh*, for example. The Temple does not need the plow as a plow for sacred ritual; it needs the income the plow generates. This is *kedushat damim*: the object is interesting only for its worth. As a formulation: *kedushat damim* is like the cynic who knows the price of everything and the value of nothing — *hekdesh* cares only about the monetary return, not the thing in itself.

Thus the four categories are not perfectly mirrored pairs. *Kedushat mizbe'ach* will typically be *kedushat haguf*; but *kedushat bedek habayis* can be either, depending on whether the object itself is incorporated into the Temple structure or merely sold for proceeds.

### Examples & Me'ilah

The *baraita* illustrates the principle by cycling through examples in each category, noting that in every case *me'ilah* applies both to the sanctified item and to whatever it contains. The three operative categories are: things fit for the *mizbe'ach* but not *bedek habayis*; things fit for *bedek habayis* but not the *mizbe'ach*; and things fit for neither — meaning their sanctity is purely *kedushat damim*, their value alone being what is consecrated.

The *baraita* asks: how can there be *me'ilah* on something fit for neither the *mizbe'ach* nor *bedek habayis* directly? The answer is that *kedushat damim* is precisely this third category. If one consecrates one's car to the *Beis Hamikdash*, the car cannot be placed on the altar and cannot be built into the Temple walls; yet the *Beis Hamikdash* will sell it and use the proceeds. That car has *kedushat bedek habayis* in the sense that its ultimate destination is the upkeep of the Temple, even though the car itself is not *rauy libedek habayis* — it will not literally be incorporated into the structure. Accordingly, *velo rauy lo lamizbe'ach velo libedek habayis* is the *baraita*'s way of describing *kedushat damim*.

The specific examples given are: a cistern filled with water (*bor malei mayim*) — water collected in a cistern is not considered fit for the *mizbe'ach* (unlike, say, water from a spring, which might qualify for *nisuch hamayim*); the *Rashbam* notes it is usable for *bedek habayis*, since water can be used to mix cement for construction. Dung heaps (*ashpos mele'os zevel*) are fit for neither the altar nor Temple construction and fall into the *kedushat damim* category. A pigeon coop filled with pigeons (*shovach malei yonim*) is an example of something that can actually be used *lamizbe'ach*, since pigeons are valid sacrifices. A field covered with grass (*sadeh malei asavim*) is fit for neither altar nor structure. A fruit-bearing tree (*ilan oseh peiros*) — the fruit itself cannot go on the altar and is not *bedek habayis*; while *bikurim* are brought in a ceremony at the *Beis Hamikdash*, they go to the *kohen* rather than being offered on the *mizbe'ach*. In all of these cases, regardless of which category they fall into, *me'ilah* applies to the consecrated item and to its contents.

One interesting discussion among the *rishonim* arises from the cases of the cistern and the field. There is a general principle that *me'ilah* does not apply to *karka* — real property, land, or things attached to the ground. The *Rashbam* appears here to hold that there is indeed *me'ilah* by *karka*, a position that other *rishonim* find difficult. One solution offered by *Tosafot* is that *me'ilah* would apply if one uprooted a clump of earth from within the cistern and thereby derived benefit — though at that point the dirt is no longer *mechubar lakarka*, no longer attached to the ground. The *Ramban* offers a more creative answer: if one built a brick wall inside the cistern, that constitutes *talush ulevasof chibro* — something detached that was subsequently attached. If one then derived benefit from that wall — leaning against it, cooling off, scratching one's back — one has derived benefit from something that is arguably no longer *mechubar lakarka*, depending on one's position in the earlier dispute. This remains a genuine open question, but it is worth flagging that the entire discussion proceeds against the backdrop of the general exemption of *karka* from *me'ilah*.

It is also important to clarify what is meant when we say *yesh bo me'ilah* or *ein bo me'ilah*. The question is not only whether one transgresses the prohibition against benefiting from *hekdesh*, but whether one incurs the full consequences of *me'ilah*: the obligation to add a fifth, and the requirement to bring a *korban*. These are the operative stakes in the discussions that follow.

## Gidulei Hekdesh

### Empty vs. Full

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הִקְדִּישׁ בּוֹר וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלֵּא מַיִם; אַשְׁפָּה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלְּאָה זֶבֶל; שׁוֹבָךְ וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלֵּא יוֹנִים; אִילָן וְאַחַר כָּךְ נָשָׂא פֵּירוֹת; שָׂדֶה וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלְּאָה עֲשָׂבִים – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But** with regard to one who **consecrated a cistern and it was later filled with water, a dunghill and it was later filled with manure, a dovecote and it later was filled with doves, a tree and it later bore fruit,** or **a field and it was later filled with plants,** if he derives benefit **from them** he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property but he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property by deriving benefit from **its contents.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.**

The *baraita* that drives the subsequent discussion introduces a distinction: what is the law when one sanctifies an empty vessel or property, and it subsequently becomes filled? The items mentioned are a cistern, a dung pit, a pigeon coop, a tree, and a field — the same objects discussed in the *mishnah*, but now considered in the scenario where they were empty at the time of sanctification and became full only afterward. The case of the tree that subsequently bears fruit is particularly significant, as is the cistern that is later filled by rain.

### R. Yehuda's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: הִקְדִּישׁ אֶת הַשָּׂדֶה וְאֶת הָאִילָן – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן וּבְגִידּוּלֵיהֶם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן גִּידּוּלֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** With regard to one who **consecrated a field or a tree,** he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property if he derives benefit from **them or that which grows from them, because they are growths of consecrated** property.

*Rabbi Yehuda* rules: *me'ilin bahen ve'ein me'ilin bema shebeisochan* — *me'ilah* applies to the sanctified object itself, but not to what fills it afterward. Since one sanctified only the cistern, the tree, the field, or the pigeon coop, but did not sanctify the water, the fruit, the grass, or the pigeons that came later, those subsequent contents carry no *me'ilah*.

### R. Yosi's View

*Rabbi Yosi* disagrees in one specific category: *hamakdish hasadeh veha'ilan me'ilin bahen uvigiduleihen mipnei shehen gidulei hekdesh* — one who consecrates a field or a tree generates *me'ilah* not only on the field and the tree but on whatever grows from them, because those are *gidulei hekdesh*, growth produced by a sanctified object. Notably, *Rabbi Yosi* does not extend this position to the cistern and the water, or to the pigeon coop and the pigeons. His reasoning is specific to produce generated by the consecrated entity itself.

## Two Sets of Terms (II)

### Ownership vs. Sanctity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִקְדִּישָׁן רֵיקָנִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלְּאוּ – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In the case of cisterns, with regard to one who **consecrated them** when they were **empty and they were later filled,** if one derives benefit **from them** he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **but** he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property if he derives benefit **from their contents.** This ruling will be clarified below. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** One is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property **even** by deriving benefit **from their contents.**

The question that all the *rishonim* struggle with is this: if one owns a well and it rains, one acquires the rainwater through *kinyan chatzer* — the principle that objects entering one's domain are automatically acquired. If *hekdesh* owns the well, why does *hekdesh* not similarly acquire the water through *kinyan chatzer*? And if *hekdesh* owns the water, why is there no *me'ilah*?

Two important answers are given. The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* hold that *hekdesh* does not have *kinyan chatzer* at all. *Kinyan chatzer* is a legal fiction specific to human property law; there is no *pasuk* establishing that *hekdesh* acquires objects that enter its domain. Therefore, when rain falls into a consecrated cistern, *hekdesh* does not own the water. Even though in some cosmic sense everything belongs to God — *ki li ha'aretz*, as the Torah says — the legal institution of *hekdesh* as a property-owning entity does not have *kinyan chatzer*.

The *Ramban*, however, offers a more analytically compelling distinction. He insists that we must separate two entirely different concepts that the word *hekdesh* bundles together. On one hand, *hekdesh* is an institution that owns property — like a corporation with a bank account, able to own, sell, and transact. On the other hand, *hekdesh* describes a status of sanctity that attaches to an object when it is verbally consecrated. These are two different things.

According to the *Ramban*, when rain falls into a consecrated cistern, *hekdesh* does indeed own that water — it entered *hekdesh*'s domain and *hekdesh* can sell it, use it, and no one has the right to take it. But the water is not *kadosh*. It has not been sanctified. It was never the subject of *kedushat peh* — a verbal act of consecration. And *me'ilah* requires that the object itself have the status of *kedushah*. Ownership by *hekdesh* and sanctity of the object are independent questions, and it is only the latter that triggers *me'ilah*.

This is a brilliant distinction because it forces a recognition that *hekdesh* plays two distinct roles simultaneously. Sometimes an object has *kedushah* without being owned by the *Beis Hamikdash* — the paradigmatic case being *kodashim kalim*, which according to *Rabbi Yossi HaGlili* are *mammon ba'alim*, remaining the property of their owner even while possessing sanctity. A *korban Pesach* is eaten by its owners; it is theirs, yet it is holy. Conversely, rainwater that falls into a consecrated cistern is owned by *hekdesh* yet lacks the sanctity that triggers *me'ilah*.

This framework illuminates the debate over *gidulei hekdesh* as well. *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yosi* debate whether the fruit of a sanctified tree is subject to *me'ilah*. The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* are notably silent on the *kinyan chatzer* argument in this context, because it is hard to argue that *hekdesh* does not own the fruit of its own tree. For the *Ramban*, however, the framework works elegantly: of course *hekdesh* owns the fruit. The question is whether the fruit was sanctified. *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that no verbal act of sanctification was directed at the fruit — only at the tree — so the fruit lacks *kedushat peh* and there is no *me'ilah*. *Rabbi Yosi* holds that the sanctity of the tree extends to its produce, since the fruit is *gidulei hekdesh*, growth generated by the sanctified entity, and therefore the fruit shares the tree's sanctity.

One participant raised the pointed question of a *bechor behemah* — a firstborn animal that is sacred from birth, *kadosh merachem*, without any verbal act of sanctification. What would the *Ramban* say about that case? The answer offered is that situations in which the Torah itself automatically assigns sanctity — such as a firstborn animal designated directly for the *mizbe'ach* — may operate under a different principle than cases of human consecration. The *Ramban*'s framework focuses on cases where sanctity derives entirely from human ownership and natural processes of acquisition, and where no independent *Torah*-mandated sanctity intervenes. But the question is acknowledged as genuinely sharp.

## Gidulei Hekdesh (II)

### Clarifying Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִקְדִּישָׁן רֵיקָנִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלְּאוּ – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אַף מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה – בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי – בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught:** In the case of cisterns, with regard to one who **consecrated them** when they were **empty and they were later filled,** if one derives benefit **from them** he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property, **but** he is **not** liable for **misuse** of consecrated property if he derives benefit **from their contents.** **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says:** One is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property **even** by deriving benefit **from their contents.** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The statement of Rabbi Yehuda appears** to be correct **in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote,** i.e., if one consecrated an empty cistern or dovecote, the water or doves that later fill it do not become consecrated. **And the statement of Rabbi Yosei** appears to be correct **in** the cases of **a field and a tree.**

A *baraita* then reports that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position appears correct regarding the cistern and the pigeon coop, and *Rabbi Yosi*'s position appears correct regarding the field and the tree. The *gemara* challenges this formulation: the first part makes sense — *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yosi* do debate the case of the tree, so one can say that *Rabbi* agrees with *Rabbi Yehuda* only in the case of the cistern and pigeon coop while rejecting him in the case of the tree. But the second part is strange. *Rabbi Yosi* only explicitly addressed the case of the field and tree — he never claimed *me'ilah* applies to pigeon coop pigeons or cistern water. What does it mean to say *Rabbi* agrees with *Rabbi Yosi* "in the case of the field and tree"? That sounds as though *Rabbi Yosi* had a broader position from which *Rabbi* selectively endorses part.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מַאי? בִּשְׁלָמָא ״נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ״ – מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיג בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן, אֶלָּא ״נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן״ – מִכְּלָל דִּפְלִיג בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ?! וְהָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי ״שָׂדֶה וְאִילָן״ קָאָמַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **this** statement of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi? **Granted,** when he says that **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda appears** to be correct **in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote, by inference** this means **that** Rabbi Yehuda **disagrees** with Rabbi Yosei **in** the cases of **a field and a tree,** and Rabbi Yehuda does explicitly disagree in those cases. **But** when Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says that **the statement of Rabbi Yosei appears** to be correct **in** the cases of **a field and a tree,** this indicates **by inference that** Rabbi Yosei **disagrees** with Rabbi Yehuda **in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote. But Rabbi Yosei stated** his opinion solely **in** the cases of **a field and a tree,** as only plants and fruit grow directly from consecrated property, and this reasoning is not relevant in the case of a cistern or dovecote.

The *gemara* considers the possibility that *Rabbi Yosi* was addressing *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position dialectically — arguing that even if one accepts *Rabbi Yehuda* elsewhere, at minimum the case of the tree should generate *me'ilah*. But this reading cannot be sustained, because a separate *baraita* makes *Rabbi Yosi*'s own scope entirely clear: *ein ani ro'eh divrei Rabbi Yehuda basadeh veilan* — I do not agree with *Rabbi Yehuda* regarding the field and the tree. By implication, regarding everything else — cistern and pigeon coop — *Rabbi Yosi* fully agrees with *Rabbi Yehuda* that there is no *me'ilah* on subsequent contents.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה קָאָמַר; וְהַתַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵין אֲנִי רוֹאֶה דְּבָרָיו שֶׁל רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵן גִּידּוּלֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ. בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ רוֹאֶה, הָא בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ – רוֹאֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that Rabbi Yosei **stated** his opinion **in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yehuda,** whereas he himself holds that even the items found in a dovecote or a cistern are consecrated, this is difficult: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei said: I do not see the statement of Rabbi Yehuda** as correct **in** the cases of **a field and a tree, because** the plants and the fruit **are the growths of consecrated** property? Infer from here that it is **in** the cases of **a field and a tree that** Rabbi Yosei **does not see** and accept the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda. **But in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote, he** does **see** and accept his opinion.

The *gemara* therefore rereads *Rabbi*'s statement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי, בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ – שֶׁאַף רַבִּי יוֹסֵי לֹא נֶחְלַק עָלָיו אֶלָּא בְּשָׂדֶה וְאִילָן, אֲבָל בְּבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ מוֹדֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **this** is what Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **is saying: The statement of Rabbi Yehuda appears to Rabbi Yosei** to be correct **in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote.** In other words, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi is saying **that even Rabbi Yosei disagrees with** Rabbi Yehuda **only in** the cases of **a field and a tree. But in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote, he concedes to him** that the prohibition against misuse of consecrated property does not apply to items that were added afterward and were not present at the time of the consecration.

*Rabbi* is not endorsing *Rabbi Yehuda* against *Rabbi Yosi* in one domain while endorsing *Rabbi Yosi* against *Rabbi Yehuda* in another. Rather, *Rabbi* is saying: *Rabbi Yehuda*'s position is correct — and even *Rabbi Yosi* agrees with *Rabbi Yehuda* — in the case of the cistern and pigeon coop, since the sole dispute between them is the field and the tree. *Rabbi*'s statement is simply a way of saying he *paskens* like *Rabbi Yosi*: both agree there is no *me'ilah* on cistern water or coop pigeons, and *Rabbi Yosi* additionally holds that *me'ilah* does apply to the produce of a consecrated tree or field.

## Well Filling with Water

### Dispute Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְבוֹר וְשׁוֹבָךְ – בְּרֵיקָנִין בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי, וּבִמְלֵאִין בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? בְּרֵיקָנִין – פְּלִיגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבָּנַן; דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן, דְּאָמְרִי: אֵין אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם; וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: אָדָם מַקְנֶה דָּבָר שֶׁלֹּא בָּא לָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote, where** the cistern and the dovecote **are empty, with regard to what** matter **do they disagree? And** similarly, **where** the cistern and the dovecote **are full, with regard to what do they disagree?** The Gemara answers: **In** the cases of a cistern and a dovecote that **are empty, they disagree with regard to** the matter that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis.** The first *tanna* and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, disagree **because the first *tanna* holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say: A person cannot transfer** ownership of **an object that has not** yet **come into the world,** i.e., that one does not currently own. Therefore, one cannot consecrate water or doves that will enter the cistern or dovecote only in the future. **And Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: A person can transfer** ownership of **an object that has not** yet **come into the world.**

On first reading, this *baraita* seems to map cleanly onto the earlier debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Yosi* about a tree that subsequently bears fruit. The *tanna kamma* parallels *Rabbi Yehuda*; *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* parallels *Rabbi Yosi*. And Abaye reports a companion ruling: *hikdishan mele'im me'ilin bahen uvema shebeisochan* — one who sanctified them already filled has *me'ilah* on both the item and its contents — and *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon machalif*, reverses.

This reversal is striking — indeed perplexing. *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* appears to hold that if one sanctifies an empty cistern and it later fills with water, the water is subject to *me'ilah*; but if one sanctifies a cistern already full of water, the water is not subject to *me'ilah*. This seems exactly backwards: surely water present at the moment of sanctification should more readily be included than water that does not yet exist at that moment.

The *gemara* first attempts to locate the dispute in the case of the field and tree — the earlier debate about *gidulei hekdesh*. But the *gemara* immediately rejects this: if one sanctifies an empty tree and then fruit grows, versus sanctifying a tree already bearing fruit — what conceivable logic would exclude the already-present fruit while including future fruit? The reversal makes no sense in the context of a tree.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוֹר דְּשָׁמְעַתְּ לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר – כְּגוֹן פֵּירוֹת דֶּקֶל, דַּעֲבִידִי דְּאָתוּ; הָנֵי – מִי יֵימַר דְּאָתוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ בְּמַיִם הַבָּאִין דֶּרֶךְ חֲצֵרוֹ לַבּוֹר, וְיוֹנִים הַבָּאִין דֶּרֶךְ שׁוֹבָכוֹ לַשּׁוֹבָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: You can **say that you heard Rabbi Meir** express this opinion in a case **such as** the **fruit of palm trees, which are likely to come** into existence, as the trees naturally produce fruit. But with regard to **these** doves and water, **who can say that they will come? Rava said: You** can **find** cases where one consecrates water or doves that are likely to arrive, e.g., **in** the case of **water that comes by way of his courtyard into the cistern** when it rains, so that he does not need to fill the cistern. **And** similarly with regard to **doves that come by way of his** other, full **dovecote into** this empty **dovecote.**

*Rava* therefore reassigns the debate: *machloket bevo'r veshovach* — the entire dispute concerns the cistern and the pigeon coop, not the field and tree. In this reading, the debate about the tree is settled: *divrei hakol me'ilin bahen uvema shebeisochan* — everyone agrees that *gidulei hekdesh* carry *me'ilah*, following *Rabbi Yosi*, and this is not in dispute. The open question is the cistern that fills with water and the coop that fills with pigeons — and it is there that the reversal must be explained.

### Tanna Kamma View

The *gemara* now explains the structure of the debate. The *tanna kamma* holds like the *Rabbanan*: *ein adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* — one cannot sanctify future water or future pigeons because one does not yet own them. When one says "I hereby consecrate this cistern," one consecrates only the cistern. When rain later falls into it, the water becomes owned by *hekdesh* because it entered *hekdesh*'s domain — but it was never verbally sanctified. Following the *Ramban*'s framework: the water is owned by *hekdesh* but is not *kadosh*, having never been the subject of *kedushat peh*. Therefore there is no *me'ilah* on the water.

It is worth noting that the *gemara* here applies the principle of *amira legavoha kimesiraso lehedyot* — that a verbal declaration of consecration has the legal force of a formal act of transfer. This equation means that the rules governing what one *can* transfer to an ordinary party also govern what one *can* consecrate, making the *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* debate directly applicable.

### R. Elazar Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִמְלֵאִים בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי? אָמַר רָבָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ בּוֹר סְתָם; וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן סָבַר לַהּ כַּאֲבוּהּ, דְּאָמַר: דָּנִין דִּין גָּבוֹהַּ מִדִּין הֶדְיוֹט, מָה דִּין הֶדְיוֹט – מָצֵי אֲמַר: בֵּירָא זַבֵּינִי לָךְ, מַיָּא לָא זַבֵּינִי לָךְ; אַף דִּין גָּבוֹהַּ – בֵּירָא אַקְדֵּישׁ, מַיָּא לָא אַקְדֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** in cases **where** the cistern and the dovecote **are full, with regard to what** matter **do they disagree? Rava said:** This dispute concerns a case **where he consecrated a cistern without specification. And Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **his father, who says: One infers the *halakha*** of consecration to the **Most High from the *halakha*** of transactions between one **ordinary** person **[*hedyot*]** and another. **Just as the *halakha*** with regard to transactions between one **ordinary** person and another is that **one can say: I sold you the cistern** but **I did not sell you the water** it contains, **so too, the *halakha*** in the case of consecration to the **Most High** is that one can say: **I consecrated the cistern** but **I did not consecrate the water** within it.

*Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*, by contrast, holds like *Rabbi Meir*: *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* — one can effect a legal transfer of something not yet in existence. When one sanctifies the cistern, there is an implicit — or according to the *Ramban*, an explicit — declaration consecrating the cistern and any future water that will fill it. Similarly for the pigeon coop and future pigeons. Since *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* holds that such a forward-looking declaration is effective, those future contents are subject to *kedushat peh* and there is *me'ilah*.

One might object: *Rabbi Meir*'s principle about *davar she'lo ba l'olam* was stated in contexts where the future item is virtually certain to materialize — like fruit from a palm tree. Who can guarantee that water will ever fall into this particular cistern, or that pigeons will ever enter this coop? Is that really comparable to the near-certainty of fruit growing on a tree?

### Water via Courtyard

*Rava* addresses this: *mishtacha lah bimayim arba'im derech chatzeiro labor, veyonim ba'im derech shovcho leshovach* — the case is one where we know with certainty that water will reach the well because it flows through the owner's courtyard on its way there, and pigeons are coming through the owner's pigeon coop before nesting in the consecrated one.

The reason one's own courtyard is specified is not merely to establish probability. *Tosafot* and the *Ramban* point out that this answers a different and deeper question: even if *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* works, it works only for something that one will own in the future. If I sanctify a palm tree and consecrate its future fruit, the mechanism is that had I not consecrated the fruit, the fruit would have been mine — I have the standing to consecrate what will be mine. But once I have consecrated the cistern, the cistern belongs to *hekdesh*. When rain subsequently falls into *hekdesh*'s cistern, that water belongs to *hekdesh* — not to me. So how can I have standing to consecrate it? I will never own that water; it goes directly into property I have already given away.

The answer is that the water first flows through *my* courtyard — my personal property, not yet *hekdesh*. I take possession of it there. Then it flows into the consecrated cistern. Since at the moment it enters my domain it briefly becomes *mine*, I do have the standing to consecrate it in advance, because it will indeed at some point be mine before it becomes *hekdesh*'s. The same logic applies to pigeons nesting briefly in my personal pigeon coop before moving to the consecrated one.

### Tosafot Critique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֵין דָּנִין דִּין גָּבוֹהַּ מִדִּין הֶדְיוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **And the first *tanna* holds** that **one does not infer the *halakha*** of consecration to the **Most High from the *halakha*** of transactions between one ordinary person and another. Rather, one who consecrates property does so generously, i.e., the most expansive meaning is assumed for his vow of consecration, and therefore even if he did not say so explicitly, he consecrated the water together with the cistern.

*Tosafot* (and the *Ramban* in parallel) press a more fundamental critique. They observe that the *gemara*'s reference to *Rabbi Meir* is puzzling in a different way. *Rabbi Meir*'s principle of *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam* was not originally stated in the context of trees and fruit at all. The *gemara* links the principle to *Rabbi Meir* via cases involving conditional *kiddushin* — a man who says to a woman that he betroths her on condition that he converts, or that she is freed from slavery, or that her husband dies. The extension from those cases to the case of a palm tree and its fruit involves a chain of associations in the *gemara*, and *Tosafot* and the *Ramban* argue that this chain obscures the fact that *Rabbi Meir*'s original cases are quite different. In those cases, there is no assumption that the condition will be fulfilled — the master might never free the slave, the husband might not die. The requirement that the future item be *avida de'asi* — naturally bound to materialize — is a criterion applied later in the discussion, not part of *Rabbi Meir*'s original position.

Both *Tosafot* and the *Ramban* conclude that the *gemara*'s attribution of the palm-tree case to *Rabbi Meir* is somewhat imprecise; it is actually a formulation developed by a later *amora* — perhaps *Rav Huna* — and then retroactively associated with *Rabbi Meir*'s principle. The parameters that govern *Rabbi Meir*'s actual ruling and those that govern the palm-tree case operate with different criteria, and the *gemara* does not resolve this cleanly.

## Already-Filled Well

### Filled When Sanctified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: הִקְדִּישָׁן רֵיקָנִין וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִתְמַלְּאוּ – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן וְאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בְּמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי, וְאֶלָּא הָא דְּתַנְיָא: הִקְדִּישָׁן מְלֵאִין – מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן וּבְמַה שֶּׁבְּתוֹכָן, וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַחְלִיף;
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says:** This **dispute** between the first *tanna* and Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, applies only **in** the cases of **a cistern and a dovecote. But in** the cases of **a field and a tree, everyone agrees** that one is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property if one derives benefit **from them or their contents.** **Abaye said to him: But** consider **that which is taught** in the continuation of the *baraita*: If one **consecrated them** when they were **full** and then derives benefit **from them or from their contents,** he is liable for **misuse** of consecrated property. **And Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, reverses** his previous ruling in this case and holds that if the items were consecrated when full their contents are not subject to the prohibition against misuse of consecrated property.

The companion case is the reverse: *hikdishan mele'im* — one sanctified the cistern when it was already full of water. Here, intuitively, there should be no difficulty establishing *me'ilah* on the water: the water was present at the moment of consecration, there is no issue of *davar she'lo ba l'olam*, and one might expect *me'ilah* to apply straightforwardly. Yet this is precisely where *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* holds there is *no* *me'ilah* on the water.

### Hekdesh = Hedyot

The *gemara* explains that *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* holds like his father — *Rabbi Shimon* — who maintained *danin din gavoah midin hedyot*: the laws governing *hekdesh* are derived from the laws governing ordinary transactions. Whatever is included when a layperson sells something is what is included when one consecrates it; *hekdesh* does not receive more than an ordinary purchaser would receive.

### Well Includes Water?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִין הֶדְיוֹט לָא?! וְהָתְנַן: מָכַר בּוֹר – מָכַר מֵימָיו! אָמַר רָבָא: מַתְנִיתִין יְחִידָאָה הִיא – דְּתַנְיָא: מָכַר בּוֹר – לֹא מָכַר מֵימָיו. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מָכַר בּוֹר – מָכַר מֵימָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** does **the *halakha*** concerning a transaction with **an ordinary** person say that one does **not** sell the water along with the cistern? **But didn't we learn** in the mishna (78b) that one who **sold a cistern has sold its water? Rava said:** The ruling in **the mishna is an individual** opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: One who **sold a cistern has not sold its water. Rabbi Natan says:** One who **sold a cistern has sold its water.**

This generates the central question: in an ordinary sale, does selling a cistern include the water inside it? If yes, then when one consecrates a filled cistern, the water is consecrated along with it, and there is *me'ilah* on the water. If no — if in an ordinary sale the water is excluded unless explicitly specified — then consecrating the cistern similarly excludes the water, since *hekdesh* is no different from an ordinary buyer. *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*, following his father's principle, adopts the latter position: selling a cistern does *not* include the water, and therefore consecrating a cistern does not include the water.

The *gemara* immediately challenges: but our own *mishnah* rules that *machar bor machar meimav* — selling a cistern *does* include the water! If even in an ordinary transaction the water is included, then *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*'s position collapses, because even granting that *hekdesh* equals *hedyot*, the *hedyot* case already includes the water.

*Rava* responds: *matnistin yichidaah hi* — our *mishnah* does not represent the unanimous opinion. A *baraita* preserves the debate explicitly: the first *tanna* holds *machar bor lo machar meimav* — selling a cistern does *not* include the water; *Rabbi Natan* holds it does. Our *mishnah*, which rules that the water is included, reflects the position of *Rabbi Natan* alone. *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* follows the *tanna kamma* of that *baraita*: selling a cistern does not include the water, and accordingly consecrating a cistern does not include the water.

### Reconciliation

With this in place, the apparent paradox of *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*'s reversal resolves into a coherent and consistent position.

Case one: one consecrates an empty cistern, explicitly declaring, "I consecrate this cistern and all water that will fill it." There is no water at the moment of consecration, so the *tanna kamma* — holding *ein adam makdish davar she'lo ba l'olam* — says no *me'ilah* on the water when it arrives. *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*, following *Rabbi Meir*, holds the explicit forward-looking declaration is effective, and since the water will eventually pass through the owner's courtyard, the owner will briefly hold title to it before it enters *hekdesh*'s cistern. The declaration therefore has legal force, and *me'ilah* applies to the future water.

Case two: one consecrates a filled cistern, saying only "I hereby consecrate this cistern" — without mentioning the water. The *tanna kamma* says: either *machar bor machar meimav* applies, so even without explicit mention the water is included; or even if it is not automatically included under ordinary sales law, *hekdesh* receives the benefit of the doubt and is presumed to include more than an ordinary sale. Either way, there is *me'ilah* on the water. *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon*, however, holds both that *hekdesh* is no greater than *hedyot*, and that even in an ordinary *hedyot* sale, a cistern does not include its water unless explicitly stated. Since the consecration mentioned only the cistern and not the water, the water was never consecrated. There is no *kedushat peh* on the water, and therefore no *me'ilah*.

The reversal is thus not truly paradoxical. In case one, *Rabbi Elazar bar Shimon* finds *me'ilah* because there was an explicit forward-looking declaration of *kedushat peh* covering the future water, valid under the principle of *adam makneh davar she'lo ba l'olam*. In case two, he finds no *me'ilah* because the verbal declaration covered only the cistern, not the water; since *hekdesh* follows the same rules as *hedyot*, and *hedyot* sales do not automatically include the water, the water was simply never consecrated.

The key insight to carry away — the *Ramban*'s central contribution — is that *hekdesh* operates simultaneously as a property-owning institution and as a framework of sanctity attaching to specific objects. Ownership by *hekdesh* and sanctity of an object are distinct. An object can be owned by *hekdesh* without being *kadosh*, and it is only sanctity — produced by *kedushat peh*, a verbal act of consecration — that triggers the laws of *me'ilah*. This distinction between the question of ownership and the question of the intrinsic status of *kedushah* is the conceptual spine around which the entire discussion of *Bava Batra* 79 turns.
