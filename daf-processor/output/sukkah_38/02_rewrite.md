# Sukkah 38 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Waving (Tenufah)

### Intro & Menachot

Today's daf is 38, and we pick up at the bottom of 37b, approximately ten lines from the bottom — three lines after the *gemara* begins following the *mishnah*. The *gemara* opens with *tnan hatam*, "we taught over there in Menachot," because the *mishnah* had mentioned the waving of the *lulav* and discussed waving it at different points during *hallel*: at *hodu lashem* at the beginning and end, and at *ana Hashem hoshia na* — and according to Beit Shammai, at *ana Hashem hatzlicha na* as well.

Tosafot had already brought a beautiful explanation linking this to *psukim* in Divrei Hayamim, which speaks of the trees of the forest bursting into song and then quotes the actual *psukim* of *hodu lashem ki tov* and *hoshienu Elohei yishenu*. The shaking of the *lulav*, then, is connected to the image of trees bursting into song, and to the responsiveness of *hodu lashem* and *ana Hashem hoshia na*. There was also the question of our precise practice — not merely the initial *hodu lashem*, but the *shliach tzibur* reciting *yomar na Yisrael*, *yomar na beit Aharon*, with the *tzibbur* responding *hodu lashem* — and Tosafot discussed how that becomes our current practice of waving the *lulav* during *hallel*.

### Shtei Halechem

Now the *gemara* connects this idea of waving to another context entirely. The *mishnah* in Menachot teaches: *shtei halechem ushnei kivsei atzeret* — on *shavuot*, one brings the two loaves of bread from the newly cut wheat together with the two lambs. What is the procedure for waving them? *Maniach shtei halechem al gabei shenei hakvasim* — you place the loaves on top of the lambs — *umaniach yadav tachtehen* — and place your hands beneath them — *umanif umolich umavi ma'aleh umorid* — and you wave, bringing forward and back, up and down.

The verse cited is *asher hunaf va'asher huram* — "that which was waved and that which was lifted up." That specific verse belongs to the *miluim*, the inauguration of the *kohanim* in the *beit hamikdash*, but it is taken as a model for the type of *tnufah* performed throughout the *beit hamikdash*, and therefore assumed to apply here as well.

### Four Winds

What does this waving signify? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan*: *molich umavi lemi she'arba ruchot shelo* — the bringing forward and back is directed toward the One to whom the four winds belong, that is, toward God. The *mishnah* in Menachot had said *umanif umolich umavi* — wave, bring forward and back — suggesting that *manif* might refer to side-to-side motion while *molich umavi* refers to front and back, together encompassing all four directions. And then *ma'aleh umorid* — up and down — *lemi sheshamayim va'aretz shelo*, to the One to whom heaven and earth belong.

### Ward Off Bad

In *eretz Yisrael* they taught this differently. *Amar Rabbi Chama bar Ukva amar Rabbi Yose bar Rabbi Chanina*: *molich umavi kedei la'atzor ruchot ra'ot* — you bring forward and back in order to hold back the bad winds — *ma'aleh umorid kedei la'atzor tlalim ra'im* — and up and down in order to hold back bad rains. It is notable that the purpose is framed negatively: not to draw down good, but to ward off the bad.

### Shirei Mitzvah

*Amar Rabbi Yose bar Avin ve'itayma Rabbi Yose bar Zavda*: *zot omeret* — this teaches us — *shiyarei mitzvah me'akvin et hapuranut*, that even the remnants of a *mitzvah* hold back calamity. Why characterize the *tnufah* as a *shirei mitzvah*, a remnant? Because if one failed to perform the waving, the *korban* remains valid — the waving is important but not indispensable. And yet: *sheharei tnufah shirei mitzvah hi ve'otzeret ruchot utlalim ra'im* — the waving, though not strictly necessary, still holds back bad winds and bad rains.

### Satan's Eyes

*Ve'amar Rava*: *vechein balulav* — and the same applies to the *lulav*. According to Rashi, the direct application is practical: *molich umavi ma'aleh umorid* — this is how the waving is to be performed, to all four directions and then up and down. *Rav Acha bar Yaakov manfi lei umaytei lei* — Rav Acha bar Yaakov would bring it out and bring it in, implementing *molich umavi* in a quite forceful way. Indeed, those who performed it that way would say *dein gira be'einei desitna* — this is like a spear in the eyes of the *satan*. But the *gemara* pushes back: *velav nichuta hi, mishum de'ati le'agruyei beih* — that is not ideal conduct, because you risk provoking the *satan* and drawing his attention rather than warding him off.

### Model Source

This raises an important structural question. Where does the very idea of *nanu'im* — waving — with the *lulav* come from at all? The *Torah* says *ulekachtem lachem* — "you shall take for yourselves" — which might suggest a vigorous taking, but does not obviously mandate waving in a liturgical context. What the *gemara* is doing here is introducing a model: we have an established practice of waving in a religious context — the *tnufah* of the *shtei halechem* — and that serves as the paradigm for what we do with the *lulav*. This is significant because it once again connects the *arba minim* and the *lulav* and *etrog* to the world of *korbanot*, a point that has come up multiple times — the *lulav* is being fashioned into something analogous to a *korban*.

### Agricultural Parallel

But Tosafot goes further and observes that the connection is not merely technical. When you wave the *shtei halechem*, what are you waving? The new grain — the distinctive agricultural product of *shavuot*. And what is the new agricultural product at *sukkot*? The *arba minim*, representing the full harvest gathered in from the fields. So the parallel is not arbitrary: just as we wave the produce of the season on *shavuot*, we wave the produce of the season on *sukkot*. This is a powerful structural correspondence.

### Tosafot Application

Tosafot raises a further question: in *perek kol hamenachot ba'ot matzah* there is a list of all the offerings that require *tnufah*, and perhaps not all of them are performed in precisely these multiple directions. *Ela davka bitnufat shtei halechem de'atzeret, vechein lulav* — perhaps it is only by the *shtei halechem* of *shavuot* that the full directional waving is performed, and we apply that same waving to the *lulav* as well.

### Prayer for Rain

The reason is then drawn from a teaching in the first chapter of Rosh Hashanah: *gezar din nechtam bePesach al hatvu'ah uve'atzeret al peirot ha'ilan uvachag al hamayim* — the judgment is sealed at Pesach regarding grain, at *shavuot* regarding fruit of the trees, and on *sukkot* regarding water, that is, rain. Tosafot therefore says: *ulachen anu menan'im balulav sheyesh bo holachah vahava'ah* — we wave the *lulav* as a type of *tefillah* for rain. The specific application of the *shtei halechem* waving to *sukkot* is thus rooted in the parallel *gezar din*: just as the waving at *shavuot* is oriented toward that season's agricultural judgment, so too the waving at *sukkot* is a prayer for the water upon which the coming year depends.

### Divine vs. Petition

Two distinct symbolic orientations for the *lulav* thus emerge from this discussion. The first, expressed by Rabbi Yochanan, frames the waving as directed toward God — an act of religious expression. The second approach, reflected in the *eretz Yisrael* teaching and Tosafot's application, frames it as a petition: the *lulav* becomes a vehicle for prayer for beneficial agricultural conditions.

There is also a *midrash* — not in our *gemara* and likely later, so its antiquity is uncertain — in which Rabbi Akiva reads each of the *arba minim* as representing *hakadosh baruch hu* directly: *ha-lulav zu ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu, etrog zu ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu*. On that reading, the waving represents our direct connection to God; the *lulav* is not primarily a prayer vehicle but an expression of divine relationship, and the singularity of each species may itself be part of that symbolism.

### Three Symbolisms

Three distinct symbolic orientations therefore present themselves in this sugya. The first situates the waving as pure divine service — directed toward God, as Rabbi Yochanan describes. The second situates it as prophylactic petition — warding off bad winds and rains, and more broadly as a *tefillah* for rain at *sukkot's* *gezar din*. The third connects it to the culmination of the *yamim nora'im* and the *yom hadin* dimension of *sukkot*, which the *gemara* elsewhere associates with *l'arbev et ha-satan* — confusing the *satan* — at the shofar blowing. The heightened mention of the *satan* in *gemara* is concentrated around the *yamim nora'im*, and so the image of poking the *satan*'s eyes may be situating the *lulav* waving as a continuation of that dynamic.

Tosafot also raises a practical question about how exactly to count the wavings, since the Yerushalmi mentions performing the waving three times. Does one count "out" as one and "in" as two, or is "out and in" together counted as one? And does one complete three cycles at a single direction before moving on, or perform the full directional sequence three times over? Our *gemara* does not specify three repetitions, but once the Yerushalmi introduces that number, the counting questions multiply.

## Outside Proper Time

### Journey Scenario

We now turn to the next *mishnah*, which raises a fascinating issue implicit in everything discussed thus far — namely, what happens when a person takes the *lulav* outside the primary context of *hallel* in the *beit knesset*. The *mishnah* states: *mi sheba baderech velo hayah beyado lulav* — someone who has returned from a journey without a *lulav* in hand. *L'kshe'yikanes leveito yitol al shulchano* — when he enters his house, he takes it at the table.

There is already a *chiddush* here before one even arrives at the question of interrupting a meal: he takes it at home, not in the *beit knesset* during *hallel*, which was clearly the assumed primary context. The *mishnah* then continues: *lo natla Shacharis yitol bein ha'arbayim, shekol hayom kasher l'lulav* — if he did not take it in the morning, he may take it in the afternoon, for the entire day is valid for *lulav*. The point of the *mishnah* appears to be that even if the proper time and context were missed — morning, *hallel*, *shul* — the *mitzvah* may still be performed at any point during the day.

### Interrupt Meal

The phrase "at the table" suggests that one interrupts a meal in order to take the *lulav*. This invites comparison to the *mishnah* elsewhere concerning activities one must not begin before *davening Mincha* — but if one already began, *im hitchilu ein mafsikin*, one does not interrupt. Why, then, should one interrupt a meal for *lulav* but not interrupt a meal for *Mincha*?

### Internal Kashya

The internal tension within the *mishnah* itself also demands attention. The first clause — "take it at the table" — implies one interrupts the meal. The second clause — "if he did not take it in the morning, take it in the afternoon" — implies one does not interrupt, for apparently he ate his morning meal without interrupting.

### Rav Safra

*Amar Rav Safra lo kashya*: there is no difficulty. *Ha d'ika sha'os bayom ha d'leika sha'os bayom* — the cases differ depending on whether time remains in the day. When the *mishnah* says to interrupt, it refers to a case where one is eating in the afternoon and night will arrive before he finishes. When it says to take it later in the afternoon, it refers to a case where the morning meal could be completed with time still remaining.

### Rava's View

*Amar Rava*: I do not understand what bothered you at all. *D'omar ha d'Oraisa ha d'Rabbanan* — the *Mincha* case poses no difficulty because *davening Mincha* is *derabbanan*. While there may be a *d'Oraisa* obligation to pray once a day, the specific institution of *Shacharis*, *Mincha*, and *Maariv* is rabbinic. One does not interrupt an ongoing activity to perform a rabbinic *mitzvah*. Taking the *lulav*, by contrast, is a biblical *mitzvah*, so the question never arose. *Ella amar Rava, i kushya ha kushya* — if there is a genuine internal tension in the *mishnah*, Rava reformulates it precisely: the first clause implies one interrupts, and the second clause — the one about taking it in the afternoon because one did not take it in the morning — implies one does not interrupt. That is the actual internal contradiction Rav Safra was resolving.

### Rebbe Zeira

*Amar Rebbe Zeira*: even that formulation is not a problem. *D'omar mitzvah l'afsoukay* — the plain reading of the *mishnah* is simply that you should interrupt your meal. *V'ilo pasak, yitol bein ha'arbayim* — but if you did not interrupt, you can still take it in the afternoon. The *mishnah* states the ideal but does not specify consequences for failure; the fact that someone might not have interrupted their morning meal does not mean the *mishnah* permitted that. And the *Mincha* case remains distinguished by the *d'Oraisa/d'Rabbanan* distinction — no additional resolution is required.

### Chol Hamoed

The *gemara* then notes: *ella amar Rebbe Zeira l'olam k'dikamina me'ikara* — there actually was a problem with the comparison to *Mincha*, and the answer is: *hacha b'Yom Tov Sheni d'Rabbanan askinan* — we are dealing here with *Yom Tov Sheni*, which in this context means *Chol Hamoed*, not the second day of *Yom Tov* observed outside *Eretz Yisrael*. How do we know? The *mishnah* itself says *mi sheba baderech* — he came back from a journey. One does not travel on *Yom Tov*. Therefore the *mishnah* must be discussing *Chol Hamoed*, when travel is permitted. And since taking the *lulav* on *Chol Hamoed* is rabbinic — it applies only *d'Rabbanan* — there is now a genuine problem: why does one interrupt a meal for a rabbinic *mitzvah* here, when one does not interrupt a meal for *Mincha*, also a rabbinic obligation?

### Time Principle

The resolution is now operative at the level of principle. For rabbinic *mitzvot*, whether one must interrupt depends on whether time remains to perform the *mitzvah* afterward. If time remains, one does not interrupt; if time will run out, one does interrupt. For biblical *mitzvot* — the *lulav* on the first day, reciting *Shema* — one interrupts regardless of whether time remains, since having already delayed and then begun another activity while the obligation is pending, one must prioritize the biblical command immediately.

There is a qualification worth noting: if one has a fixed and predictable time to perform the *mitzvah* later — say, attending a *minyan* where *Shema* will be recited — the obligation to interrupt may not be triggered in the same way. But where the obligation was already pending and one began another activity, the biblical *mitzvah* takes precedence unconditionally.

The larger point of the *mishnah* in context remains significant. The *gemara*'s hyper-focus on the interruption question should not obscure the broader implication: the ideal is morning, *hallel*, *shul*. Everything else — taking it at home, taking it in the afternoon — is accommodation to deviation from that ideal.

## Shaking Centrality

### During Hallel

The *mishnah* returns to *hallel*, and this transition itself is revealing. The natural structure of the *daf* has been: an elaborate discussion of waving during *hallel*, then a brief digression about taking the *lulav* outside its proper time and context, and now a return to *hallel*. The implication is that the primary context of the *mitzvah* of *lulav* is precisely the shaking during *hallel* in *shul*. This is not presented as a supplemental enhancement — "first you make the *beracha*, take the *lulav*, and by the way also shake it during *hallel*." Rather, the shaking in the context of *hallel* is the *mitzvah*.

### Tosafot Minor's Obligati…

Tosafot raises a pointed question: when one makes the *beracha* at the moment of taking the *lulav*, must one shake it then, or does merely holding it suffice? *Lo matzinu dechayav lina'anea bitchilat netilah* — we do not find any explicit source requiring shaking at the moment of taking. But then Tosafot invokes the principle underlying the *mishnah*'s formulation of a minor's obligation: *katan hayodeia lina'anea chayav belulav* — a minor who knows how to shake is obligated in *lulav*. Notice: not "a minor who is able to take it," but "a minor who knows how to shake it." This is how the threshold of educational obligation is defined. Since the capacity to shake defines when the *mitzvah* begins to be taught, shaking is evidently central to what the act of the *mitzvah* is.

Tosafot therefore concludes that one does shake the *lulav* when making the *beracha* — even, he adds, *af al pi she'eino yode'a likros es ha'hallel*, even if one does not know how to recite *hallel*. While one would technically be *yotzei* without shaking, shaking is central to the act. This also advances an important independent claim: shaking can be meaningfully performed outside the context of *hallel*.

### Three Possibilities

Three conceptual positions thus emerge. The first is that the *mitzvah* consists purely in *netilah* — the taking — and shaking is entirely secondary. This position is *bedi'eved* true in the sense that one is *yotzei* without shaking, but it cannot represent the *lechatchilah* ideal, given how consistently shaking is foregrounded throughout the *sugya*. The second is that shaking is central to the *mitzvah* and is specifically associated with the *hallel* context. The third — Tosafot's position — is that shaking is central and can exist independently of *hallel*, as evidenced by the minor who shakes without reciting *hallel*.

### Early Departure

Tosafot adduces a further proof from a *baraita* in Berachos: *hikdim latzeis laderech* — if one had to depart early on a journey before *Shacharis* — *mevi'im lo shofar vetoke'a lulav umenane'a megillah vekorei bah* — they bring him a shofar and he blows, a *lulav* and he shakes, a *megillah* and he reads. He makes the *beracha*, shakes the *lulav*, and goes — without *hallel*. *Mashma de'menane'a belo hallel* — this implies one can shake without *hallel*.

One might argue that this case is purely *bedi'eved*, however — he had no choice. It no more proves that shaking is *lechatchilah* independent of *hallel* than it proves that early-morning *lulav* is the ideal.

### Lechatchildlah Ideal

The stronger evidence, one can argue, runs in the opposite direction: the entire opening discussion of the *daf* places shaking squarely in the context of *hallel*. Even the minor's case does not require shaking *without* *hallel* — the minor could perfectly well be in *shul* while others are reciting *hallel*, and the principle of *shomea k'oneh*, which the *gemara* will discuss shortly, means that listening to *hallel* is like reciting it. The fact that he cannot recite it himself does not remove him from the *hallel* context.

This understanding aligns with the actual practice observed: most people wait to make the *beracha* in *shul* and take the *lulav* in the context of *hallel*. Some invoke *zrizin makdimin lemitzvot* — the principle of zealously performing *mitzvot* at the earliest opportunity — to take the *lulav* at home first thing in the morning. But the stronger *lechatchilah* is clearly the shaking in the context of *hallel*, not simply as something done afterward but as the very manner in which the *mitzvah* is performed.

### No Torah Source

This question carries significant weight when one considers that shaking is not mentioned anywhere in the *Torah* at all. *Ulekachtem lachem* — "you shall take for yourselves" — is the relevant verse, and it says nothing about waving. The entire architecture of shaking has been constructed from the connection to *hallel*, from the image of *az yerannenu atzei haya'ar* ("the trees of the forest will burst into song") that Tosafot invokes, and from the model of *tnufah* in the world of *korbanot*. Without the *hallel* context, the very concept of shaking loses its primary grounding.

### Simcha Connection

This connects to the end of the *pasuk* itself: *ulekachtem lachem bayom harishon... usmachtem lifnei Hashem Elokeichem shivas yamim* — you shall take on the first day... and you shall rejoice before God seven days. The *simcha* of the *lulav* — expressed in *simchat beit hasho'evah* and in the festival's character as a gathering of the harvest — finds its natural liturgical expression in *hallel*. Tosafot's citation of *az yerannenu atzei haya'ar* is precisely about trees bursting into song, and that image of joyful expression before God is what *hallel* embodies. The context of *hallel* is not incidental; it is where *simcha* and religious expression converge.

## Hallel Responsive

### Unobligated Reader

The next *mishnah* reads: *mi shehayah eved o isha o katan makrin oto* — if a slave, woman, or minor was reciting *hallel* for you — *oneh achareihem mah shehem omrim* — you repeat after them what they say. This describes a word-for-word responsive recitation.

### Word-for-Word

The scenario assumes the person does not know *hallel* and must be fed the words phrase by phrase, repeating each phrase after the unobligated leader says it.

### Curse & Ignorance

*Vetavo lo me'eira* — a curse should fall upon that person. Why?

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Rashi understands the curse as arising from the display of ignorance: why must you rely on someone else to feed you the words? The very fact of not knowing *hallel* is deeply problematic. Tosafot, however, objects: even if an adult male leads and you repeat after him, you are equally ignorant. Rashi's explanation does not account for why the leader's identity — slave, woman, or minor — matters. Tosafot argues that the problem lies specifically in relying on someone who is themselves not obligated in *hallel*. A woman and minor are exempt from time-bound *mitzvot* and therefore not obligated; a slave similarly. When an unobligated person serves as your conduit, the word-for-word repetition more explicitly displays your ignorance, since there is no official obligated-and-responding structure behind which your lack of knowledge can be concealed. Yet the issue may also be that being led by the unobligated simply looks bad — it reveals reliance on people who are not themselves bound by the obligation.

### Adult Leader

If an adult male leads, the dynamic changes. *Im haya gadol mikra oto oneh acharav Hallelujah* — if an adult was reciting for you, you respond with *Hallelujah* after each phrase. The *chazzan* says *Hallelujah*, the congregation responds *Hallelujah*; he says *hallelu avdei Hashem*, they respond *Hallelujah*; he says *yehi shem Hashem mevorach*, they respond *Hallelujah*. And so on through the chapters. Depending on how the *mishnah* is read, this may have been the standard communal practice even for those who knew *hallel* — the *shliach tzibbur* recites and the *tzibbur* responds *Hallelujah* at each phrase — or it may describe only what those who do not know *hallel* should do when led by an obligated adult.

### Custom Variations

*Makom shenahagu lichpol yichpol* — where the custom was to double certain *psukim* (as we do with *odecha Hashem ki anitani*), one doubles them. *Lifshot* — where the custom was to recite straight, one does so. *Levarech* — where the custom was to add a blessing, one blesses. *Hakol keminhag hamedina* — all follows local practice. This is a notable embrace of diversity in practice across communities.

### Women Leadership

This discussion opens a broader question about women leading *hallel* in a contemporary setting. Our current practice is entirely different from the one assumed by the *mishnah*: everyone recites from the *siddur* independently, there is no responsive structure, and no one is feeding another person the words. The concerns about displaying ignorance and relying on an unobligated leader do not apply in the same way, since everyone fulfills the obligation independently. The limited responsive moment that does exist in our *hallel* raises an interesting question about how to map these *halachot* onto our current realities.

The deeper issue of *tavo me'eira* — the curse — is also important in a different way. In the social reality of the *gemara*, male ignorance contrasted with female knowledge carried particular weight and shame; the *gemara* elsewhere notes that a woman could theoretically receive an *aliyah* but should not out of *kevod hatzibbur*, which may reflect related sensitivities about this kind of inversion. How much of the *tavo me'eira* is rooted in halachic categories — specifically, the asymmetry of obligation levels — and how much is a sociological judgment about what is appropriate or fitting in those societies, is a genuine question that affects how one applies these sources today.

### Women Birchat

Tosafot's final lines are relevant here too. Tosafot mentions: *mishum derabim zila behu milsa* — even if a woman is obligated, having her lead *birkat hamazon* in a setting with many people is considered disrespectful or inappropriate. He supports this with the position of the *Halachot Gedolos* (*Bahag*), which rules that women cannot be *motzi* men in *megillah* reading. Various later Tosafot try to ground that ruling in a technical argument — perhaps men are obligated to *read* the *megillah* while women are obligated only to *hear* it, creating an asymmetry — but this Tosafot appears to frame it as a purely sociological or propriety issue independent of technical obligation levels. This distinction is significant for contemporary discussions of women's liturgical roles.

## Obligation & Blessing

### Son & Slave

A *baraita* teaches: *ben mevarech l'aviv ve'eved mevarech l'rabo* — a son may lead *birkat hamazon* for his father, and a slave for his master. This is despite the fact that a minor son and a slave are at most rabbinically obligated, while the adult father may be biblically obligated.

### Woman Blessing

*Ve'ishah mevareches l'ba'alah* — a woman may lead *birkat hamazon* for her husband as well. Whether women are biblically obligated in *birkat hamazon* is itself a debate in Berachos. *Aval amru chachamim, tavo me'eira l'adam she'ishah u'vanim mevarchin lo* — nevertheless, the Sages said: a curse should come upon a man whose wife and children recite the blessing for him.

### Curse Reason

Why the curse? One explanation focuses on the levels of obligation: a minor is only rabbinically obligated, while an adult man may be biblically obligated, and having the less-obligated party *motzi* the more-obligated party is halachically problematic. A subtle scenario could make the minor rabbinic and the adult rabbinic in the same particular instance — for example, if the man ate only a *kezayit* and his biblical obligation is not triggered, while the minor ate his fill — but even then, the man remains fundamentally in the *d'Oraisa* category and the child does not, and the *baraita* evidently views this as inappropriate.

### Sociological Issue

Tosafot adds the alternative framing that even matching levels of obligation would not fully resolve the issue. There is a deeper propriety concern — the inappropriateness of relying on those who are not generally counted in the same obligatory framework. Whether that is a halachic constraint or a social-contextual one is precisely the question left open by the *sugya*.

## Rava's Approach

### Contemporary Practice

*Amar Rava: hilchata gevirta ika lemishma mei-ha minhaga de-halleila* — Rava says that many important *halachot* can be inferred from the contemporary practice of *hallel* in his time. By Rava's era the original responsive structure had largely fallen away, and most people recited *hallel* in full themselves. But certain vestiges of the older practice had been preserved, and Rava reads those preserved moments as deliberate signals of the underlying *halachot*.

### Opening Hallel

*Hu omer Hallelujah vehem omrim Hallelujah* — the *chazzan* opens with *Hallelujah* and the congregation responds *Hallelujah*. Then he says *hallelu avdei Hashem* and they again say *Hallelujah*. *Mikan she-mitzvah la'anot Hallelujah* — from this we infer that there is a *mitzvah* to respond *Hallelujah* at the opening of *hallel*, even for those who know the full text and are reciting it themselves. This vestige was preserved specifically to signal that responding *Hallelujah* is an obligatory element of *hallel* participation, not merely optional.

### Hodu Lashem

*Hu omer Hodu Lashem vehem omrim Hodu Lashem* — later, when the *chazzan* reaches *hodu lashem*, the congregation again responds *hodu lashem* rather than continuing in unison. *Mikan she-mitzvah la'anot rashei prakim* — from this we infer that there is a *mitzvah* to respond at the opening of each new chapter.

### Chapter Heads

What exactly does *rashei prakim* — responding at chapter beginnings — mean? The answer depends on how one understands the structure of *hallel*. Rashi and Tosafot diverge here.

According to Rashi, each new *perek* of Tehillim within *hallel* would have prompted a shift in the responsive formula. Under the word-for-word practice, the opening phrase of each *perek* would be said by the congregation together (rather than in the usual call-and-response), signaling the transition. So *mitzvas la'anot rashei prakim* means that at each chapter break, the response changes to mark the new section.

Tosafot suggests a different structure. In Rava's time — and perhaps in the original practice as well — the congregation understood all the *psukim* from the initial *Hallelujah* through the *hodu lashem* section to form a single large unit. The opening of that second unit, *hodu lashem ki tov*, was the moment for a new responsive phrase. Accordingly, Tosafot reads the full *hallel* as divided into two great sections: in the first, the response is *Hallelujah*; in the second, the response is *hodu lashem ki tov* — *ki le'olam chasdo* after each phrase. That would have been a genuinely stirring communal experience: half the assembly calling *hodu lashem ki tov* and the other half responding *ki le'olam chasdo* through the entire second half.

### Ana Hashem

*Hu omer Ana Hashem hoshia na vehem omrim Ana Hashem hoshia na. Hu omer Ana Hashem hatzlicha na vehem omrim Ana Hashem hatzlicha na.* Here there are two distinct inferences. The repetition of *ana Hashem hoshia na* — with the congregation echoing exactly — teaches *mikan she'im haya katan makri oto onin acharav mah shehu omer*: if a minor were feeding you the lines, you would need to repeat word for word precisely what he says, not a standard responsive formula. This is word-for-word repetition preserved as a vestige to remind us of that rule.

And what about the second repetition, *ana Hashem hatzlicha na*? *Mikan she'im ba lichfol kofel* — this teaches that doubling certain *psukim* is permitted. We maintain one repeated *pasuk* precisely to signal that the practice of doubling was legitimate and in use.

### Shomeia K'Oneh

*Hu omer Baruch Haba vehem omrim B'Shem Hashem.* Here, uniquely, the congregation does not repeat what the *chazzan* said, nor respond with a fixed formula. Instead the *chazzan* says the first half of a *pasuk* and the congregation completes it with the second half. *Mikan l'shomea k'oneh* — from this we derive the principle that hearing is like saying.

## Shomeia K'Oneh

### Technical Meaning

Why does this particular exchange — the *chazzan* beginning and the congregation completing — teach *shomea k'oneh*? The answer lies in the precise technical meaning of the term. *Shomea k'oneh* is usually translated as "hearing is like saying," but that is imprecise. The word *oneh* does not simply mean "to say" — *vayaanu haleviyim ve'amru kol ha'am* — it means "to answer," to respond. In a responsive context, what is the proper response when the *chazzan* says something? *Hallelujah*, or *hodu lashem*, or *amen*. If one hears and responds with the expected reply, one has clearly fulfilled the obligation. But the *baruch haba / b'shem Hashem* exchange is structurally different: you are not affirming what was said with a responsive *amen* or *Hallelujah* — you are completing the speaker's sentence. Yet even here, the principle holds that having heard is sufficient. This is why the exchange teaches *shomea k'oneh* in a deeper sense than the earlier responses: it establishes that even when there is no verbal response at all, merely hearing the words fulfills the obligation.

### Rambam Hierarchy

The Rambam formulates a hierarchy on this basis. *Ha'oneh amen kemotzi beracha mipiv dami* — one who responds *amen* is treated as if he personally voiced the blessing, because *amen* functions as endorsement and identification: "ditto, I affirm those words as my own." But *vehasho'meia harei zeh ke'oneh* — one who only hears, without saying *amen*, is treated as if he responded with *amen*, though this is one step down from actually saying *amen*. The optimal fulfillment is responding; the acceptable fulfillment is hearing; and the principle that hearing functions as responding is what *shomea k'oneh* denotes.

### Rashi Davening

Rashi applies the principle immediately to the *davening* context: if one is in the middle of *Shmoneh Esrei* and *Kaddish* or *Kedushah* is being recited, one should stand silently and listen — and that listening functions as having made the proper response. One is not required to interrupt *Shmoneh Esrei*.

### Tosafot Interruption

Tosafot objects. If *shomea k'oneh* truly means that hearing is equivalent to responding, then listening to *Kaddish* during *Shmoneh Esrei* constitutes an act of response, which is itself an interruption of *Shmoneh Esrei*. We rule in practice like Rashi — that listening counts as *yotzei* without constituting a formal interruption — but the underlying tension Tosafot identifies is real, and it reflects the precision with which the principle of *shomea k'oneh* needs to be applied.

### Tannaitic View

The *gemara* records *amar Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* that when asked whether hearing without responding counts: *chachamaya vesafraya, rashei ama, vedarshaya* — the wise men, the teachers, the communal leaders, and the expounders have all said *shama velo ana yatza* — hearing without responding, one has fulfilled the obligation.

### Yoshiyahu Source

A formal textual source is then provided: *amar Rebbi Shimon Pazi amar Rebbi Yehoshua ben Levi*: *minayin l'shomea k'oneh?* — from where do we derive the principle? *Dechsiv, es kol divrei hasefer asher kara melech Yehuda* — "all the words of the book that the king of Judah had read." This verse, from the story of the discovery of the *Sefer Torah* in the *Beit Hamikdash* during the reign of Yoshiyahu, describes the king as having "read" the words.

### Hearing = Reading

*Vecha'i Yoshiyahu kara? Velo Shafan keraan?* — but did Yoshiyahu actually read them? Shafan read them, as the verse explicitly says: *vayikraehu Shafan* — Shafan read it before the king. Yet the verse describes the king as the one who read. *Ela mikan l'shomea k'oneh* — this teaches that hearing is like reading. And for the *megillah* context this is particularly important: not merely that hearing is like saying the words, but that hearing is like *reading* the words — which means one who listens to the *megillah* is treated as if one read it.

### Humiliation Proof

*Amar Rav Acha bar Yaakov*: one might object that after Shafan read it, Yoshiyahu himself then read it, and the verse reflects that subsequent personal reading. But *lo salka datchach* — that reading is untenable. The verse says *yaan rach levavcha vatichna lefanai beshamacha* — "because your heart was softened and you humbled yourself before me *when you heard* these things." *Beshamacha velo b'karacha* — when you heard, not when you read. The verse's own language confirms that Yoshiyahu heard rather than read — and yet he is described as having "read." This is the Scriptural grounding for *shomea k'oneh*.

### Our Minhag

Tosafot notes that our current *minhag* of *hallel* does not even follow Rava's practice: we do not respond *Hallelujah* to the opening *Hallelujah* at the beginning of *hallel*. Since the *gemara* itself has already framed the entire discussion in terms of *minhagim* — with the *mishnah* saying *hakol keminhag hamedina* — Tosafot accepts that Rava had a different *minhag* from the *mishnah*, and we have a different *minhag* from Rava, and all of this is consistent with legitimate halachic diversity.

### Practical Use

The practical importance of *shomea k'oneh* is most evident in *megillah* reading. The *mitzvah* is specifically *kria*, reading — yet the universal practice is to listen to one person read rather than for each individual to read independently. This only works because of the principle that hearing is equivalent to reading.

### Self-Performance

One question the *gemara* does not fully address is whether saying something oneself is categorically preferable to hearing it, even when hearing suffices. For communal *mitzvot* — *megillah* reading, where participation in the *tzibbur* is itself part of the *mitzvah* — being part of the communal reading clearly outweighs going off to recite privately. For more private *mitzvot* — *Kiddush*, for example — one occasionally encounters people who prefer to recite *Kiddush* themselves rather than rely on the host, precisely because they feel that active performance is superior to *shomea k'oneh*. The *gemara* leaves open whether *shomea k'oneh* is fully equivalent to *oneh* or merely adequate in the absence of the preferred mode.

Returning in closing to the experience of *hallel* itself: Rava's careful reading of what his contemporaries preserved as vestiges of the older responsive practice reminds us of what that *hallel* was. It was not many individuals reciting in parallel from their prayer books, converging only briefly at *hodu lashem*. It was a communal call-and-response — *Hallelujah* after every phrase, *hodu lashem ki tov* through the second half — a genuinely unified *tzibbur* experience of joy and praise. The *halachot* of *shomea k'oneh* and of responsive recitation are not merely technical accommodations for the ignorant; they are the structure of a liturgical act meant to be experienced together.
