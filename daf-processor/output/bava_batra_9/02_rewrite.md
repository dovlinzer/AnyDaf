# Bava Batra 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pidyon Shevuyim

### Source of Teaching

The Gemara on 8b opens by transitioning into a discussion of *tzedakah* — specifically the obligation of community members to provide for the needs of the poor — building naturally on the earlier discussion of how long one must reside in a town before becoming liable for various communal obligations. Within this context, the Gemara had already introduced a story about the mother of Shvor Malka, who sent a purse full of *dinarei* to Rav Yosef with instructions to use the funds for a *mitzvah rabbah*. Rav Yosef concluded that the supreme *mitzvah* in mind must be *pidyon shevuyim* — the redemption of captives.

The Gemara now opens on 8b with Rava posing a challenge to Rabba bar Mari: *minah ha'milta de'amri rabbanan de'pidyon shevuyim mitzvah rabbah hi?* — where exactly does this teaching come from, that *pidyon shevuyim* is a *mitzvah rabbah*? The answer is drawn from a verse in Yirmiyahu: *ve'haya ki yomru elecha ana netzei ve'amarta aleihem koh amar Hashem asher l'maves l'maves va'asher l'cherev l'cherev va'asher l'ra'av l'ra'av va'asher l'shevi l'shevi* — describing the various fates that will befall the people in the time of the *churban ha'bayis*. Rav Yochanan teaches that each subsequent fate mentioned in this verse is more severe than the one preceding it.

### Hierarchy of Deaths

The Gemara works through the ascending hierarchy with characteristic precision. *Cherev kasheh mi'maves* — death by the sword is worse than natural death. This can be derived either from *sevara* or from a verse. From *sevara*: *hai ka'menuval ve'hai lo ka'menuval* — death by the sword disfigures the body, whereas natural death does not. From a verse: *yakar be'einei Hashem ha'mavesah la'chasidav* — dear to God is the death of His pious ones, meaning that the most precious form of death is natural death, not violent death. Hence the verse lists natural death first and death by the sword second.

Next, *ra'av kasheh mi'cherev* — death by hunger is worse than death by the sword. Again, either from *sevara* — *hai ka'mitztaer ve'hai lo ka'mitztaer* — death by the sword disfigures but is instantaneous, whereas death by hunger involves prolonged suffering — or from an explicit verse: *tovim hayu chalalei cherev me'chalalei ra'av*, better were those who fell by the sword than those who perished by famine.

### Captivity Severity

*Shevi* — captivity — which appears last in the verse, is *kasheh mi'kulam*, more severe than all of them, and no additional verse is needed to establish this: *de'chulhu isnehu bei*, because captivity contains within it all the preceding forms of suffering simultaneously. The captive faces the threat of death by the sword, suffers from hunger, and lives in a state of ongoing fear and degradation. The placement of captivity at the end of the verse thus makes the case for *pidyon shevuyim* as a *mitzvah rabbah* on its own terms.

### Rambam's Teaching

The Rambam's treatment of this topic in *Hilchos Matnos Aniyim*, chapter 8, halacha 10, is characteristically emphatic — unusually so for the Rambam's typically terse style. He writes: *pidyon shevuyim kodem l'parnasas aniyim u'le'chisusan ve'ein lecha mitzvah gedolah ke'pidyon shevuyim* — redeeming captives takes precedence over supporting and clothing the poor, and there is no *mitzvah* as great as *pidyon shevuyim*. The Rambam explains that the captive is simultaneously among the hungry, the thirsty, the naked, and those in mortal danger — precisely the synthesis that the Gemara identified by noting that captivity contains all the preceding forms of suffering. He adds that one who closes his eyes to the obligation of *pidyon shevuyim* violates the prohibitions of *lo te'ametz et levavecha ve'lo tikpotz et yadecha*, along with other transgressions. He concludes: *ve'ein lecha mitzvah rabbah ke'pidyon shevuyim*. The strong rhetorical register here is notable.

This was not merely a theoretical concern. Throughout the medieval period and beyond, *pidyon shevuyim* was a matter of urgent communal practice — entire communities, often including those far removed from the captive's own town, would organize to raise funds. The Gemara in Gittin addresses the inevitable tension between the urgency of immediate redemption and the long-term policy concern that meeting ransom demands incentivizes further captive-taking, a tension illustrated memorably by the case of the Maharam of Rothenburg, whose body was held for ransom.

## Charity Collections

### Kuppah & Tamchui

A *baraita* is introduced: *kuppah shel tzedakah nigbeit bishnayim u'mit'chalekes bishloshah* — the communal charity fund is collected by two people and distributed by three. The reason for requiring two collectors is stated explicitly: *ein osim serarah al ha'tzibbur pachot mishnayim* — one may not exercise a position of authority over the community alone; authority must be shared. This is significant because collecting *tzedakah* is not a purely voluntary matter — there is a compulsory dimension to communal charity obligations, and the collectors effectively exercise coercive power over members of the community. The requirement of two is therefore a structural check on that power.

Distribution requires three, *ke'dinei mamonot* — because assessing the relative needs of recipients and allocating funds accordingly is analogous to adjudicating monetary claims, which requires a panel of three.

Tosafot raises the obvious question: if distribution requires three because of the *dinei mamonot* character of the assessment, shouldn't collection also require three, since assessing how much each household must contribute similarly involves evaluative judgment? Rabbeinu Tam answers that when the contribution per household is already established — either as a flat assessment or by prior determination — the act of collection is merely mechanical, not evaluative, and therefore two suffice.

The *tamchui* — the soup kitchen, which operated by collecting food daily from households and distributing it to the poor on the same day — requires three for both collection and distribution: *nigbeit bishloshah u'mit'chalekes bishloshah*. The reason is practical: *she'gviyata ve'chalukata shavim* — since collection and distribution happen in such rapid succession, it would be impractical to assemble a group of two for collection and then reconvene a different group of three for distribution. The same three handle both.

The *tamchui* was distributed daily, while the *kuppah* was distributed every *erev Shabbat*. Furthermore, the *tamchui* served primarily *aniyei olam* — the itinerant poor passing through town — while the *kuppah* served *aniyei ha'ir*, the established poor of the city. The logic is clear: a weekly monetary allowance is appropriate for a resident who must plan for the week; daily food provision suits the transient who needs a meal today. And practically speaking, a week's allowance given to someone passing through places an undue burden on the community's ongoing fund.

### Fund Reassignment

The *baraita* continues: *rashaim benei ha'ir la'asot kuppah tamchui ve'tamchui kuppah* — the townspeople may reassign funds collected for one purpose to the other. If the *kuppah* has a surplus, those funds may be transferred to the *tamchui*, or vice versa. The Gemara goes further: *u'leshanota le'chol mah she'yirtzu* — and they may redirect the funds to whatever purpose they deem fit.

The first comment in Tosafot extends this considerably: Rabbeinu Tam holds that the funds may even be redirected to *devar ha'reshut*, a non-*mitzvah* purpose. And indeed, Tosafot reports that Rabbeinu Tam himself would regularly draw from *kuppah* funds to pay the wages of those guarding the town. He did not feel it necessary to frame this as a *pikuach nefashos* justification — the *kuppah* funds had been collected by the townspeople for the purposes of the town, and in his view, whoever held the relevant authority could redirect them at discretion. The precise question of who constitutes that governing body — and how collective decisions are made — is notably underdeveloped in the Gemara's framework here, a gap that becomes apparent when one asks why Rabbeinu Tam's apparently unilateral decision sufficed.

### Community Authority

The *baraita* continues: *ve'rashaim benei ir le'hatnos al ha'middos ve'al ha'she'arim* — the townspeople may make stipulations about measures and prices. Setting standardized measures addresses the patchwork of different local measurement systems that appear throughout the Talmud. More significantly, *al ha'she'arim* — setting fixed prices or price ceilings — represents a meaningful curtailment of free market dynamics in order to ensure that basic goods remain accessible. As Rashi notes, the intent is a cap, not a mandated floor price. The *baraita* adds *ve'al sechar po'alim* — and regarding workers' wages — which functions here not as a ceiling but as a minimum, a floor below which wages may not fall.

Finally: *u'le'hasia al kitzatan* — and to impose fines on those who violate these edicts. The townspeople not only have the power to make rules; they have the power to enforce them with financial penalties. The theoretical basis for this collective authority — what grants the townspeople this quasi-legislative and quasi-judicial power — is not spelled out directly, but the Gemara implicitly treats the town as a corporate entity capable of its own governance.

### Two Administrators

Returning to the requirement of two for positions of communal authority: *ein osim serarah al ha'tzibbur pachot mishnayim* — the Gemara asks, *minah hanei milei?* Rav Nachman derives it from the verse *ve'hem yikchu et ha'zahav ve'et ha'techelet ve'et ha'argaman* — where multiple individuals are described as acting together in collecting the donations for the *beit ha'mikdash*, a closely parallel case.

A story is cited to support Rav Chanina's related teaching: Rebbe once appointed two brothers to supervise the *kuppah*. As Rashi notes, the significance is that two brothers, from the standpoint of potential collusion, effectively function as one person — they share interests and loyalties. Yet the requirement of two was deemed satisfied. This demonstrates that the requirement of two is not motivated by suspicion of dishonesty; if it were, two brothers would be inadequate. The requirement is rather structural, about the distribution of power and authority, not about preventing embezzlement.

The Gemara then asks what, concretely, is the coercive power that these positions carry: *mai seraruta?* The answer: *lefi shememashkenin al ha'tzedakah va'afilu be'erev Shabbat* — because the collectors may seize collateral from a person who refuses to give, even on *erev Shabbat*. This power — entering a person's home and taking collateral — is substantial, echoing similar issues in Bava Metzia.

## Power & Trust

### Coercion Ethics

This immediately raises a difficulty: there is a verse, *u'fakadeti al kol lochatzav* — God will punish all those who oppress the poor. And in the name of Rav, Rabbi Yitzchak bar Shmuel bar Marta teaches that this refers *even to the gabbai tzedakah* — even the charity collector who uses excessive force in collecting is guilty of oppression. So how can the *gabbai* seize collateral?

The resolution: *lo kashya — ha de'amid, ha de'lo amid*. The distinction turns on whether the person has means. If the person is genuinely poor — *de'lo amid* — then using coercive power against them is indeed the oppression the verse warns against. If the person is wealthy and simply unwilling to give — *de'amid* — then the collectors are authorized to compel payment and seize collateral.

The practical illustration is striking: *Rava achpei le'Rav Natan bar Ami u'shakal minei arba me'ah zuzei le'tzedakah* — Rava forcibly compelled Rav Natan bar Ami, a fellow *amora*, and extracted four hundred *zuz* from him for *tzedakah*. That a person of Rav Natan bar Ami's standing was resisting is understandable — the sum was substantial, reflecting what was evidently a progressive assessment system. Yet Rava's authority to compel was clear.

A celebrated Tosafot addresses this episode by raising a fundamental tension. A principle taught in the chapter of *kol ha'basar* states that any positive commandment whose reward is explicitly stated in the Torah — *matan sechara kasuva betzidah* — is one that *beit din* below does not have the authority to compel. And *tzedakah* explicitly states its reward: *ki biglal hadavar hazeh yevarchecha Hashem Elokecha*. So how does *beit din* have the authority to force compliance?

Tosafot's resolution develops the dual character of *tzedakah*. It is simultaneously a *mitzvah* — a religious obligation — and something more: a claim the poor have on the resources of the community, with a *dinei mamonot* dimension. When *beit din* compels someone to give *tzedakah*, it is not acting in the capacity of enforcing ritual compliance (as it might with *tefillin* or *sukkah*). It is acting to vindicate the legitimate financial claim of the poor. The reward stated in the Torah speaks to the *mitzvah* dimension; the *dinei mamonot* dimension provides the independent basis for compulsion, irrespective of the stated reward.

The discussion then moves to the famous verse from Daniel: *ve'hamaskilim yazhiru ke'zohar ha'rakia* — those who are wise will shine like the brightness of the sky. The Gemara identifies these as the *dayan she'dan din emet le'amito* — the judge who penetrates to the genuine truth of a case. Tosafot asks why the verse needs *le'amito* — why not simply say *din emet*? The answer: it is possible to follow all the procedural rules and still arrive at a wrong result because one failed to look beneath the surface. When something seems suspicious, when a claim does not quite add up, a judge who simply processes the evidence as presented without exercising deeper scrutiny is not judging *din emet le'amito*. The *maskil* must be able to read between the lines.

*U'matzdikei harabbim ke'kochavim le'olam va'ed* — those who bring merit to the many will shine like the stars forever. The Gemara identifies these as *gabbei tzedakah* — the charity collectors. The term *matzdikei* resonates on multiple levels: it invokes the language of *tzedakah* (providing justice and charity) and of *lehatzdikim* (causing others to act rightly). The *gabbai tzedakah* causes the many to give; he is not merely a giver himself.

A *baraita* offers an alternative reading: in the *baraita*, the *maskilim* include the *gabbei tzedakah* themselves, and *matzdikei harabbim ke'kochavim le'olam va'ed* refers specifically to teachers of young children — *melamdei tinokot* — who secure the future of the community by educating the next generation. Rav suggests the paradigmatic example of such self-sacrificing dedication: Rav Shmuel bar Shilas, who when found by Rav taking a brief walk in his garden, replied that he had not visited his garden in thirteen years, and that even at that moment his thoughts remained with his students. Ravina adds that the *rabbanan* themselves — those who demonstrate love of God through constant Torah study — attain an even greater level, compared not to stars or sky but to the rising sun: *ve'ohavav ke'tzeit ha'shemesh be'gvurato*.

The Gemara then returns to the conduct of the *gabbei tzedakah* and the practical rules they must observe. *Gabbei tzedakah einan rashaim lifrosh ze mi'ze* — the two collectors may not separate from one another when making their rounds. They cannot divide the neighborhood between them, each visiting different houses alone, because at any given moment one must still be operating with the other present. The qualification: if one goes to a gate and the other to a nearby store and they remain within sight of one another, Rashi explains that this is still acceptable.

The Gemara then addresses a series of scenarios designed to keep the *gabbai tzedakah* above any suspicion of financial impropriety — not because the Gemara suspects them, but because *marit ayin* in monetary dealings involving public trust demands a higher standard of visible integrity. If a collector finds coins on the ground — an *aveida* he is legally entitled to keep — he may not place them in his own pocket while on duty. Instead, he places them into the *tzedakah* pouch and retrieves them only upon returning home. Similarly, if someone repays a personal debt to the collector while the collector is on his rounds, the collector places those funds into the *tzedakah* pouch and retrieves his money only at home. The concern is purely one of appearances: any observer seeing money pass to the collector and disappear into his pocket would reasonably suspect that *tzedakah* funds were being pocketed.

When the collectors have no poor to distribute to and possess copper coins that will rust, they may exchange them for silver coins — but only by making the exchange with others, not with themselves. Similarly, *gabbei tamchui* who have perishable food and no poor to receive it must sell it to others, not to themselves. In all these cases, the structural concern is the same: a transaction in which the *gabbai* is on both sides — buyer and seller, debtor and creditor — cannot be kept free of suspicion.

Finally: *ma'ot shel tzedakah ein monin otam shtayim shtayim ela achat achat* — when counting the *tzedakah* coins, one counts them one at a time, not two at a time. Rashi explains this too as a precaution against suspicion: if someone observes the counting and sees coins being handled in pairs, they might suspect that the counter is counting by ones while taking every second coin for himself.

Abaye reports that his teacher Rabba originally refused to sit on the mats in the synagogue, suspecting they had been purchased with *tzedakah* funds that should have gone to the poor. Once Rabba heard the ruling that *tzedakah* funds may be reassigned to any purpose the community sees fit, he was willing to sit on them.

Abaye further reports that Rabba originally maintained two separate pouches — one for the general poor and one for the poor of the city — in order to keep the collections distinct. Once he heard Shmuel's ruling to Rav Tachlifa bar Avdimi — *avid chad kisa ve'atni alah*, maintain one pouch and simply stipulate how it will be distributed — he adopted that practice as well. The *chiddush* here is subtle: even when one explicitly collects for a specific purpose, once the discretion to reassign is established, there is no problem even *lechatchila* mixing the collections. Rav Ashi goes further still: *ana itnuyei nami lo tzerichna* — he holds that no explicit stipulation is even needed, because *de'chol de'ka atei ada'atah didi atei*, anyone who gives money to *tzedakah* and entrusts it to a recognized *tzedakah* collector implicitly accepts that collector's discretion regarding how to distribute it.

## Guild Authority

### Butchers' Pact

The Gemara transitions from the *baraita*'s discussion of townspeople imposing fines for violations of communal rules to an illustrative case. Two butchers — *bei terei tavchei* — made an arrangement between them: they divided the working days of the week, each assigned to work on alternating days. Their agreement stipulated that if either worked on the other's day, the offender's animal hide would be torn to shreds — a significant financial penalty. One of them did in fact work on the other's day. The hide was torn. They came before Rava.

### Rava's Ruling

Rava ruled that the one who tore the hide was obligated to pay. Rav Yeimar bar Shelameya challenged Rava: does the *baraita* not state *u'le'hasia al kitzatan* — that communities may fine those who violate their edicts? Did these butchers not do exactly that? Rava did not respond. Rav Papa said that Rava was right not to respond, and explained the underlying rule: *hanei milei heicha de'leika adam chashuv* — this power to make and enforce such rules applies only where there is no *adam chashuv* in the town. *Aval heicha de'ika adam chashuv lav kol ke'minaihu de'matnu* — where there is an *adam chashuv*, the guild or collective cannot make binding rules with enforceable consequences without his authorization.

### Adam Chashuv Role

The *Ri Migash*, cited by many *rishonim*, explains that *adam chashuv* here requires two qualifications simultaneously: the person must be a *talmid chacham* of significance, and he must hold an actual position of civic leadership in the town. Neither qualification alone suffices. The combination of genuine Torah knowledge and recognized communal authority is what constitutes the *adam chashuv* whose approval is required.

The Ramban offers a compelling explanation of why this oversight is necessary. The butchers' arrangement — alternating days and eliminating competition — is not simply an internal matter between two parties. It has substantial consequences for the entire town: less competition means higher prices and reduced consumer protection. Because the arrangement's effects extend beyond the parties themselves to the general public, those parties cannot impose it unilaterally. They require the approval of someone whose role is to weigh both the legitimate interests of the guild and the interests of the townspeople at large. The *adam chashuv*, combining *halachic* knowledge with civic authority, is precisely that person.

The *rishonim* draw an important boundary around this principle. The power to establish binding guild-like arrangements with enforceable penalties is not available to any group of individuals who happen to share a business interest. It applies specifically to a recognized collective — a town, or something analogous to a guild in the sense of comprising *all* practitioners of a given trade in a locale. If the two butchers in the Gemara's case were merely two out of many, they would not have had this power at all; they would have had to rely on ordinary contract law. The shift the Gemara makes — from the *baraita*'s discussion of townspeople to the case of butchers operating as a guild — is a significant conceptual extension, and the *rishonim* are careful to define its limits. Questions of governance within such collectives — decision-making by consensus, the weight of a minority veto — remain largely unresolved in the Gemara's framework.

## Power & Trust (II)

### Trust Paradox

The Gemara returns to *tzedakah* and a *baraita* that is striking in its implications: *ein mechashvin be'tzedakah im gabbei tzedakah* — one does not audit the accounts of the *gabbei tzedakah*. Similarly, *ve'lo be'hekdesh im gizbarim* — the treasurers of the *beit ha'mikdash* were not subject to post-hoc accounting of their stewardship. The verse brought as a *zecher* (allusion rather than formal proof) is the verse describing those entrusted with temple funds: *ki ve'emunah hem osim* — they acted faithfully, and faithful agents are not subjected to audit.

Rashi explains why this is only a *zecher* and not a full proof: the temple officials supervised so many concurrent activities that maintaining comprehensive records would have been unreasonable. Tosafot's explanation is more pointed: *raya gemura eina de'shani hatam she'hayu tzaddikim gemurim* — the temple case may have been exceptional because those individuals were of extraordinary personal righteousness, which is not necessarily the default condition of all *gabbei tzedakah*.

The tension this creates is real. The institutional logic — that these positions require absolute trust, and auditing undermines that trust — may have made sense in a community context where appointment was careful and reputations were deeply embedded in social fabric. The practical difficulties of applying this principle without careful attention to character and accountability are significant.

### Preliminary Count

Yet the Gemara does not leave the matter entirely without structure. Rabbi Elazar teaches: even if one has a trusted *gizbar* in one's household, *yitzor veyimneh* — one should bind up the funds and count them out explicitly at the time of handover. The verse cited is *va'yatzuru va'yimnu* — they bound up and counted. There is thus a front-end discipline even where back-end audit is not required. The principle seems to be: establish clarity at the outset, so that both parties know precisely what was entrusted, even if subsequent accounting is left to trust.

## Distribution Standards

### Food Investigation

Rav Huna states: *bodkin le'mezonot ve'ein bodkin le'chasut* — one investigates whether a person truly needs food before providing it, but one does not investigate before providing clothing. This can be derived either from *sevara* or from a verse. The *sevara*: *hai ka'meivazeh ve'hai lo ka'meivazeh* — the person who lacks clothing is visibly humiliated in public, while the person who lacks food suffers privately; therefore one acts immediately for the clothing case without delay for investigation. The verse: *halo faros la'ra'ev lachmecha* — where the word *faros* is read as *lifrosh* (to explicate, to investigate), written with a *shin*, meaning: first investigate, then give bread. By contrast, for clothing the verse reads *ki tireh arom ve'chiasito* — as soon as you see the naked person, cover him: *la'altar*, immediately.

### Clothing Check

Rav Yehuda reverses the ruling: *bodkin le'chasut ve'ein bodkin le'mezonot* — one investigates before providing clothing, but not before providing food. The *sevara*: *hai ka'mitztaer ve'hai lo ka'mitztaer* — the person who is hungry is suffering immediately and acutely; delay for investigation is unconscionable. The person lacking clothing is indeed visible and humiliated, but the suffering of hunger is more urgent. The verse is read differently: *halo faros la'ra'ev lachmecha* — *paros*, with the plain reading meaning to break and give bread immediately. And for clothing, the phrasing *ki tireh arom ve'chiasito* is read as: when you see, and then you cover — implying an interval of verification before acting.

The question of whether to investigate at all before giving might seem surprising — the Torah's language of *patoach tiftach et yadecha lo* suggests an immediate response. The *chiddush* is that the Gemara permits, and in some cases requires, some investigation before providing either food or clothing. This reflects the practical reality that resources are limited and genuine need must be distinguished from feigned need, even though investigation introduces delay that can itself be costly.

### Tannaitic Standard

A *tanna* resolves the dispute in accordance with Rav Yehuda: *kesuni — bodkin acharav; parnesuni — ein bodkin, nosnin lo miyad*. If someone says "clothe me," you investigate. If someone says "feed me," you do not investigate — you give immediately.

## Traveling Poor

### Transient Support

The Gemara now cites a *mishnah* from elsewhere: *ein pochtin le'ani ha'over mi'makom le'makom mi'kikar be'pundiyon me'arba se'in be'sela* — one does not give a transient poor person less than a loaf of bread worth a *pundiyon* when four *se'ah* of wheat cost a *sela*. The baseline of a full loaf is non-negotiable even for the passing poor.

If the person spends the night: *notnin lo parnasat linah* — one provides him lodging. Rav Pappa explains: *purya u'visadya* — a bed and a pillow. The communal infrastructure thus extends to shelter, not merely food — effectively a formalized system of overnight refuge.

If he remains through *Shabbat*: *notnin lo mazon shalosh se'udot* — one provides him with the three *Shabbat* meals. The principle is clear: a transient poor person is entitled to observe *Shabbat* with its full complement of meals, not to experience a diminished *Shabbat* by virtue of poverty.

### Self-Sufficiency

A related principle: if the person is actively going door-to-door and raising his own funds — *im haya machzir al ha'petachim ein nizkakim lo* — the community is not obligated to provide communal funds for him. He is self-sufficient through his own efforts.

Yet this principle requires careful application. The Gemara relates that a certain poor person who was going door-to-door came before Rav Pappa, who declined to give him anything from the communal funds. Rav Sama the son of Rav Yeiva pointed out the consequence: if people see that Rav Pappa — the overseer of communal funds — refused, no one else will give him either, and the person may starve. The *baraita* says not to give from communal funds, but surely he should not die.

Rav Pappa's resolution: *ein nizkakim lo le'matanah merubah* — one does not give him the full communal allocation; *aval nizkakim lo le'matanah mu'etet* — but one gives him a small, nominal amount. The concern is not primarily halachic but social and humanitarian: publicly turning someone away sends a destructive message that forecloses all other potential giving. Even where the strict rule limits communal obligation, a minimal act of support is required to avoid causing greater harm.

### Minimum Annual

Rav Assi states: *le'olam al yimna adam et atzmo miletet shlishit ha'shekel be'shanah* — a person should never withhold from giving at least a third of a *shekel* per year. The verse cited is *ve'he'emadnu aleinu mitzvot latet aleinu shlishit ha'shekel be'shanah la'avodat beit Elokeinu*. This is the bare minimum annual *tzedakah* obligation — distinct from *ma'aser kesafim*, which is more fully developed in the *rishonim* and certain *midrashei halacha* rather than in the Gemara itself. Even if all other communal collections bypass a person entirely, this baseline remains.

Rav Assi adds: *shkulah tzedakah ke'neged kol ha'mitzvot* — *tzedakah* is equivalent to all the *mitzvot* combined, derived from the same verse, since it uses the plural *mitzvot* rather than *mitzvah*. Tosafot addresses an apparent difficulty: the verse speaks about giving for the *beit ha'mikdash* — *bedek ha'bayis* — not for the poor. Tosafot's response is powerful in its brevity: *kol shekein le'tzedakah* — if anything, *tzedakah* for the poor is *more* important than building the Temple. People who are suffering are present and immediate. This two-word formulation serves as a pointed corrective to the tendency — then and now — to prioritize visible institutional religious giving over direct provision for the poor.

## Charity Leadership

### Mobilizers

Rabbi Elazar teaches: *gadol ha'me'aseh yoter min ha'oseh* — the one who causes others to give is greater than the one who gives directly. The verse: *ve'haya ma'aseh ha'tzedakah shalom va'avodat ha'tzedakah hashket vavetach ad olam* — the *ma'aseh ha'tzedakah*, the one who facilitates the *tzedakah* of others, achieves *shalom*, while the direct giver achieves *hashket vavetach* — security and tranquility — which is taken as a lesser reward. This returns to the theme of *matzdikei harabbim* — those who cause the many to give carry the merit of all the giving they have enabled.

### Government Role

The Gemara then addresses the consequences of communal failure to give. The verse *halo faros la'ra'ev lachmecha* is read in light of a condition: *zacha* — if you merit — you fulfill the verse by giving to the poor. *Lo zacha* — if you do not — *ve'aniyim merudim tavi bayit*, you bring the downtrodden into your house. Rashi reads *merudim* as referring to those who complain and press their claims — meaning the government. If the Jewish community does not take care of its own poor, the government will step in to extract the funds and administer the collection, a situation far less desirable than communal self-governance.

Rava instructs the people of Mechuza: *bematuta minaichu, usu ba'hadei hadadei ki heichi de'lehavei lachon shalma de'malchuta* — please, take care of one another, so that there will be peace between you and the government. The government's intervention is not abstract; it is a practical consequence of communal failure to provide internally.

### Forced Charity

Rabbi Elazar draws the comparison to the *beit ha'mikdash*: when the Temple stood, *adam shokel shiklo u'mitkapeir lo* — a person gave his *shekel* for the communal sacrifices and was thereby atoned. Now that the Temple is gone, if one gives *tzedakah*, good; if not, *ba'im ovdei kochavim ve'notlim bi'zroa* — the government comes and takes by force.

Yet even forced *tzedakah* carries credit: *ve'af al pi chein nechshav lahem le'tzedakah* — it is still counted for them as *tzedakah*. The verse: *ve'nogshayich tzedakah* — even those who compel you to give are instruments of *tzedakah*, and the giving that results, however involuntary, retains its character as such. The comparison to the *shekel* and *korbanot* reinforces the importance of *tzedakah* as the functional equivalent of Temple service in the current era — both in its potential for atonement and in the consequences of its neglect.
