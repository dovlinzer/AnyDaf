## Mixing Produce

### Mishnah: No Mixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין מְעָרְבִין פֵּירוֹת בְּפֵירוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָשִׁים בַּחֲדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One may not intermingle produce** bought from one supplier **with** other **produce, even** if he intermingles **new** produce **with** other **new** produce and ostensibly the buyer suffers no loss from his doing so.

The Mishnah opens: *ein me'arvin perot be-ferot afilu chadashim be-chadashim* — one may not mix fruit from one field into fruit from another field, even if both are of the same vintage, and therefore presumably the same quality. When a buyer purchases a bushel of grain or apples from a given field, he expects to receive produce from that field, and mixing in produce from elsewhere violates that expectation — even if there is no objective difference in quality. Needless to say, *chadashim be-yeshanim* — mixing new produce with old — is certainly prohibited, since old grain is generally superior: it is more ready to be ground into flour and therefore more desirable and more valuable.

### Wine Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר, חֲדָשִׁים בִּישָׁנִים. בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: בְּיַיִן הִתִּירוּ לְעָרֵב קָשֶׁה בְּרַךְ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַשְׁבִּיחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And needless to say,** one may not intermingle **new** produce **with old** produce, in the event that the old produce is superior, as with grains, since intermingling lowers its value. **Actually, they said: With regard to wine, they permitted one to mix strong** wine **with weak** wine, **because one** thereby **enhances it.**

The Mishnah introduces an exception: *be-emet amru be-yayin hitiru le'arev kasha be-rach mipnei she-mashbicho* — in truth they said that with wine it is permitted to mix harsh wine with soft wine, because the harsh wine actually improves the quality of the soft wine. The Gemara will clarify that *be-emet amru* signals an authoritative *halacha*. Rashi notes that by the same logic, one might add a small quantity of old grain into new grain when the result is genuinely an improvement. The principle is that mixing is forbidden when it deceives, but permitted when it produces a real qualitative enhancement that the buyer benefits from.

### Wine Lees Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְעָרְבִין שִׁמְרֵי יַיִן בְּיַיִן, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת שְׁמָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **One may not** intentionally **mix wine sediment with the wine, but one may give** the buyer wine with **its sediment;** the seller is not required to filter the wine.

The Mishnah further states: *ein me'arvin shimrei yayin be-yayin* — one may not mix the *shmarim*, the lees or sediment at the bottom of a wine vessel, into the wine itself. However, the Mishnah adds, *noten lo et shmarav* — one may give the buyer the lees that belong to that very wine. The precise meaning of this clause will be resolved in the Gemara.

## Diluted Wine Sales

### Diluted Wine Disclosure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב מַיִם בְּיֵינוֹ, לֹא יִמְכְּרֶנּוּ בַּחֲנוּת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הוֹדִיעוֹ. וְלֹא לַתַּגָּר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוֹדִיעוֹ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְרַמּוֹת בּוֹ. מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהָטִיל מַיִם בַּיַּיִן – יָטִילוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One who** had **water mix with his wine may not sell it in the store, unless he informs** the buyer that it contains water. **And he may not** sell it **to a merchant, even if he informs him** of the mixture, **as,** although he is aware that there is water mixed with the wine, **it** will be used **for nothing other than deceit** because the merchant will likely not inform the buyer that it is diluted. In **a place where they are accustomed to place water into the wine** to dilute it and everyone is aware of that fact, **one may place** water in the wine.

In the time of the Gemara, wine was routinely mixed with water before drinking — sometimes at a ratio of three to one, sometimes six to one. This was not because ancient wine was especially potent, but because pure water was not reliably potable and wine served as the vehicle for producing a safe, palatable drink. In the normal course of commerce, however, wine was sold undiluted. If a seller found himself in possession of wine already mixed with water — whether by accident or for his own intended use — he may not sell it in his store, since buyers reasonably assume they are purchasing undiluted wine, unless he explicitly informs the customer: *mi she-nitarav mayim be-yaino lo yimkarenu be-chanut ela im ken hodio*.

### Middlemen Restriction

The Mishnah adds a sharper restriction: *velo le-tagar af al pi she-hodio* — one may not sell diluted wine even to a *tager*, a wholesaler or middleman, even if one informs him. The concern is that the *tager* will in turn sell it to storekeepers without disclosing its diluted state, or sell it directly to consumers without informing them. By handing him a product that is already diluted, one creates an irresistible temptation: the *tager* now bears the burden of disclosure with every subsequent sale, and he may simply not bother. Although this is not precisely *lifnei iver*, since the *tager* hardly needs one's assistance to add water to wine if he wished to deceive, nonetheless one is placing him in a situation of temptation and easing the path toward deception in a way that one must avoid.

### Standard Practice Except…

There is an important qualification: *makom she-nahagu le-hatil mayim be-yayin yatilu* — in a place where it is the accepted practice to dilute wine before sale, one may do so. When dilution is the norm, every buyer already knows what to expect, and there is no element of misrepresentation. The standard ratio — whether half, a third, or a quarter — should be observed according to local custom.

### Middleman Mixing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַתַּגָּר נוֹטֵל מֵחָמֵשׁ גֳּרָנוֹת וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ מְגוּרָה אַחַת, מֵחָמֵשׁ גִּתּוֹת וְנוֹתֵן לְתוֹךְ פִּיטוֹם אֶחָד, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא מִתְכַּוֵּין לְעָרֵב.
>
> **Translation:** The prohibition against mixing different types of produce applies only to an individual selling the produce of his field. By contrast, **a merchant may take** grain **from five threshing floors** belonging to different people, **and place** the produce **in one warehouse.** He may also take wine **from five winepresses and place** the wine **in one large cask [*pitom*], provided that he does not intend to mix** low-quality merchandise with high-quality merchandise.

Having established the prohibition on mixing, the Mishnah introduces a seemingly contradictory permission: *ha-tagar notel me-chamesh geranot ve-noten le-toch magura achat, me-chamesh gitot ve-noten le-toch pitom echad* — a wholesaler may take grain from five different granaries and consolidate it into one storage facility, or take wine from five different wine presses and combine it into a single vat. This is not a contradiction of the earlier prohibition. When a buyer purchases directly from a farmer, he expects and is entitled to receive produce from that specific field. But when dealing with a *tager*, everyone understands that he aggregates from multiple sources. The buyer purchasing from the *tager* — or from a store that in turn purchased from the *tager* — does not assume he is receiving produce from a single field.

### Intentional Deception

The permission is not unlimited, however: *u-vilvad she-lo yehei mitkaven le'arev* — provided that the *tager* is not intentionally mixing in a deceptive manner. This does not mean that he is literally combining products by accident — obviously he is doing so deliberately. Rather, the prohibition is against mixing in a way designed to mislead. For instance, buying the bulk of one's stock from a single source but blending in a small portion from an inferior source in the hope that buyers will not notice — that is forbidden. When the mixing reflects normal wholesale aggregation that is transparent and understood by all parties, it is permitted.

## Grain & Wine Details

### Grain Price Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר חֲדָשׁוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע וִישָׁנוֹת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ דְּאֵין מְעָרְבִין, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ חֲדָשׁוֹת מִשָּׁלֹשׁ וִישָׁנוֹת מֵאַרְבַּע – אֵין מְעָרְבִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאָדָם רוֹצֶה לְיַשְּׁנָן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: Needless to say,** if the price of the **new** produce is **four** *se'a* for a *sela* **and** the price of the **old** produce is **three** *se'a* for a *sela*, **one may not intermingle** them together. That is full-fledged deceit, as one is selling inexpensive produce at the price of expensive produce. **Rather, even** if the price of the **new** produce is **three** *se'a* for a *sela* **and** the price of the **old** produce is **four** *se'a* for a *sela*, **one may not intermingle** them. This is **because** in this case the price of the new produce is higher, as **people want to age** the produce, i.e., new produce is more valuable to those who seek to place it in storage for a lengthy period, although it may be of inferior quality compared to old produce.

The Gemara opens with a *baraita* that extends the prohibition on mixing grain. The obvious case is: *ein tzarich lomar chadashot me-arba ve-yeshanot mi-shalosh de-ein me'arvin* — needless to say that if new grain sells at four units for a *dinar* and old grain at three units for a *dinar* (the old being more expensive because it grinds more readily into flour), one may not mix new grain into old and pass it off as all-old. But the *baraita* goes further: *ela afilu chadashot mi-shalosh ve-yeshanot me-arba* — even if this year the new grain is actually more expensive, fetching three for a *dinar* while old grain fetches four for a *dinar*, one still may not mix the new into the old. Why? *Ein me'arvin mipnei she-adam rotzeh le-yashenan* — because a person who is buying old grain wants old grain specifically, even if new grain currently commands a higher market price. As Rashi explains, the reason new grain might sometimes be more expensive is that some buyers wish to purchase the raw new grain and age it themselves, participating in the process of drying and seasoning. But a buyer who seeks old grain has his own reasons for wanting it, and his expectation cannot be overridden simply because new grain happens to be worth more at the moment. The prohibition on mixing is thus not merely about objective quality but about respecting what the buyer actually wants.

### Wine Pressing Times

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ: בַּיַּיִן הִתִּירוּ לְעָרֵב קָשֶׁה בְּרַךְ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַשְׁבִּיחוֹ וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: עֲדָא אָמְרָה, כֹּל ״בֶּאֱמֶת אָמְרוּ״ – הֲלָכָה הִיא. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וּבֵין הַגִּיתּוֹת שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Actually, they said: With regard to wine, they permitted one to mix strong** wine **with weak** wine **because** mixing the wine **enhances it. Rabbi Elazar said: That is to say, every** time a *halakha* is introduced with the phrase: **Actually they said,** it **is** an established ***halakha*** with regard to which there is no uncertainty. **Rav Naḥman says: And** it is with regard to the period when the wine is **among the winepresses,** i.e., before the wine ferments, that **they taught** this *halakha*. When the wine is still in the process of fermentation, if different wines are mixed and ferment together, this enhances their flavor. By contrast, if they are mixed at a later stage, this will harm their flavor.

Rav Nachman qualifies the wine-mixing permission: *u-vein ha-gitot shanu* — this applies specifically during the pressing season. Rashi explains that during this period, when the wine is still fresh and slightly active, the flavors of the two wines blend naturally when combined. Once the wine has fully settled after pressing, the flavors do not integrate well, and mixing at that point would not genuinely improve the product and is therefore not permitted.

### Modern Wine Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאִידָּנָא דְּקָא מְעָרְבִי שֶׁלֹּא בֵּין הַגִּיתּוֹת? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּיָדְעִי וְקָא מָחֲלִי. רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אַחָא הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אַחָא מַתִּיר בְּדָבָר הַנִּטְעָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And today, when** people **mix** old and new wine even when the wine is **not among the winepresses,** on what basis is mixing permitted? **Rav Pappa said:** It is **because** buyers **are aware** of the potential loss **and waive** it. **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Aḥa, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Aḥa permits** mixing **in** a case where the product **will be tasted** before its purchase. Then there is no deceit, as when the buyer tastes it, he is immediately aware that it is a mixture, and the choice of whether or not to purchase the product is his.

The Gemara then notes: *ve-idna de-ka ma'arvi shelo bein ha-gitot* — yet in practice people mix wine even outside of the pressing season. Rav Pappa explains: *de-yad'ei ve-ka machali* — people are generally aware that this is the practice and they accept it. All prohibitions of this kind are ultimately calibrated to expectation and norm: when buyers know what they are receiving and accept it, there is no deception. An alternative tradition attributes the practical leniency to Rabbi Achi, who holds that when a buyer tastes the wine before purchasing, it is a case of buyer-beware; the tasting constitutes an informed decision, and permission follows not from a general social awareness but from the buyer's specific, concrete knowledge of what he is acquiring.

### Lees Mixing Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵין מְעָרְבִין שִׁמְרֵי יַיִן בְּיַיִן, אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת שְׁמָרָיו וְכוּ׳. וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ רֵישָׁא אֵין מְעָרְבִין כְּלָל! וְכִי תֵּימָא מַאי ״נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת שְׁמָרָיו״? דְּקָא מוֹדַע לֵיהּ, הָא מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: לֹא יִמְכְּרֶנּוּ בַּחֲנוּת אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מוֹדִיעוֹ, וְלֹא לַתַּגָּר אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמּוֹדִיעוֹ, מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא מוֹדַע לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And one may not** intentionally **mix wine sediment with the wine, but one may give** the buyer wine with **its sediment.** The Gemara asks: **But didn't you say in the former clause** of the mishna that **one may not mix sediment at all? And lest you say: What is** the meaning of: **He may give** the buyer wine with **its sediment;** it means **that he informs** the buyer that the wine contains sediment, this suggestion is not tenable. **From** the fact **that the latter clause teaches: One may not sell it in the store unless he informs** the buyer **and** he **may not** sell it **to a merchant even if he informs him, it may be inferred that the former clause** is speaking of a situation where one may sell it in the store **even if he does not inform** the buyer.

The Gemara returns to the clause *ein me'arvin shimrei yayin be-yayin aval noten lo et shmarav* and works through its meaning. The difficulty is plain: the earlier clause prohibits mixing altogether, so what does it mean to say one "may give him the lees"? One might suggest that *noten lo et shmarav* means one may sell wine mixed with its lees provided one informs the buyer. But the Gemara rejects this reading by noting that when the Mishnah intends to convey that disclosure renders something permitted, it uses the word *modio* explicitly — as it does in the case of diluted wine: *lo yimkarenu be-chanut ela im ken modio*. Here no such word appears, implying that the permission to give the lees is without special disclosure. So what is the case?

### Rav Yehuda's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָכִי קָאָמַר, אֵין מְעָרְבִין שְׁמָרִים שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ בְּשֶׁל יוֹם, וְלֹא שֶׁל יוֹם בְּשֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ. אֲבָל נוֹתֵן לוֹ אֶת שְׁמָרָיו. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַשּׁוֹפֶה יַיִן לַחֲבֵירוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יְעָרֵב שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ בְּשֶׁל יוֹם, וְלֹא שֶׁל יוֹם בְּשֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ. אֲבָל מְעָרֵב שֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ בְּשֶׁל אֶמֶשׁ, וְשֶׁל יוֹם בְּשֶׁל יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said: This** is what the *tanna* **is saying: One may neither mix sediment** remaining **from** wine that he poured **the night before with** the wine **of** the next **day, nor** the sediment **of** the next **day with** the wine **of the night before. But he may give** the buyer **sediment of** that wine **itself. That is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **one who pours wine for another,** attempting to pour the liquid while leaving the sediment in the cask, that person **may neither mix** sediment remaining **from** wine that he poured **the night before with** the wine **of** the next **day, nor** the sediment **of** the next **day with** the wine **of the night before. But he may mix** the sediment **of the night before with** the wine poured **the night before, and** the sediment **of** the next **day with** the wine **of** the next **day.**

Rav Yehuda clarifies: *ein me'arvin shmarim shel emesh be-shel yom ve-lo shel yom be-shel emesh aval noten lo et shmarav* — the prohibition is on mixing the lees of one wine with those of a different wine. One may not take the sediment from one vat and add it to another vat's wine, both because it inflates the volume of sediment and because lees from a different wine will negatively affect the taste. The word *me'arvin* — mixing — is the operative term. What is permitted is *noten lo et shmarav*: one may sell wine together with its own lees, the sediment that settled from that same wine. Just as one sells orange juice with its own pulp without a special declaration, wine is understood to come with its own *shmarim* unless explicitly stated otherwise.

### Different Wine Days

A *baraita* confirms this: Rabbi Yehuda teaches that one who is decanting wine — pouring it carefully so the lees remain behind — *lo ye'arev shel emesh be-shel yom*, must not mix yesterday's wine with today's wine, nor today's with yesterday's. Rashi's explanation extends this to any two separate pressings or vats. One may, however, mix the lees of yesterday's wine with those of yesterday's, or today's with today's — meaning, within the same pressing. The prohibition is on combining distinct wine sources, whether the wine itself or their lees.

## Rava Wine Case

### Rava's Situation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי שֶׁנִּתְעָרֵב מַיִם בְּיֵינוֹ, הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִמְכְּרֶנּוּ בַּחֲנוּת, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן מוֹדִיעוֹ וְכוּ׳. רָבָא אַיְיתוֹ לֵיהּ חַמְרָא מֵחָנוּתָא, מַזְגֵיהּ, טַעְמֵיהּ, לָא הֲוָה בְּסִים, שַׁדְּרֵיהּ לְחָנוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **One who** had **water mix with his wine may not sell it in the store, unless he informs** the buyer that it contains water. The Gemara relates: **They brought wine to Rava from a store. He diluted it** with water, **tasted it, and it was not tasty. He sent it** back **to the store,** so they could sell it there.

The Gemara presents a narrative. Wine was brought to Rava from a store. He mixed it with water — the standard practice — tasted it, and found it unpalatable. He wished to return the diluted wine to the storekeeper, with whom he apparently had an arrangement entitling him to return wine he disliked. The difficulty is that he would now be returning wine already mixed with water, and the Mishnah prohibits selling diluted wine to a retailer or middleman.

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן, וְלֹא לַתַּגָּר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהוֹדִיעוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **And he may not** sell it **to a merchant, even if he informs him?**

Abaye raises the objection directly: *ve-hanan tenan ve-lo le-tagar af al pi she-hodio* — does not our Mishnah teach that one may not sell diluted wine to a salesperson, even with disclosure? Returning the wine to the storekeeper is functionally equivalent to selling it to him, and the storekeeper will then presumably sell it on to customers.

### Rava's Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִזְגָּא דִּידִי מִידָּע יְדִיעַ. וְכִי תֵּימָא דְּטָפֵי וּמְחַיְּילֵיהּ וּמְזַבֵּין לֵיהּ – אִם כֵּן, אֵין לַדָּבָר סוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: My dilution** of the wine **is evident** to all, as I add more water than is typically added. **And lest you say** that the storekeeper **will add wine, and sweeten** the mixture, **and sell it** again, when the dilution is no longer evident, **if** this is a concern, **there is no end to the matter.** It should be prohibited to sell any wine to a merchant due to the concern lest he engage in deceit in its resale.

Rava responds: *maziga didi meida yadi'a* — my dilution is well known. Rashi explains that Rava would dilute his wine very heavily, so heavily that anyone who tasted it would immediately recognize it as diluted. There could be no possibility of deception on the storekeeper's part, because the product announces its own nature the moment it is tasted. Abaye's implicit rejoinder — that the storekeeper might simply add more undiluted wine to reduce the dilution ratio and then sell it as normal — is dismissed: *im ken ein la-davar sof* — if we are concerned about that, then one could never sell raw wine at all, since any seller could potentially deceive if he wished to. The prohibition targets cases where one is handing someone a product that creates an obvious and practically irresistible temptation toward deception. When that temptation is structurally absent — as when the dilution is so pronounced that it is immediately detectable — the concern does not apply.

## Wine Practices

### Dilution Standards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָקוֹם שֶׁנָּהֲגוּ לְהַטִּיל מַיִם בַּיַּיִן, יָטִילוּ וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא: לְמֶחֱצָה, לִשְׁלִישׁ, וְלִרְבִיעַ. אָמַר רַב: וּבֵין הַגִּיתּוֹת שָׁנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: In **a place where they are accustomed to place water into the wine** to dilute it and everyone is aware of that fact, **one may place** water in the wine. **It was taught:** One may dilute the wine by adding water that will constitute **one-half, one-third, or one-fourth** of the mixture, in accordance with the local custom. **Rav says: And** it is with regard to the period when the wine is **among the winepresses,** before the wine ferments, that **they taught** this *halakha*. If wine is diluted at a later stage, the dilution will cause the wine to spoil.

The Gemara states: *makom she-nahagu le-hatil mayim be-yayin yatilu* — where it is customary to dilute wine, one dilutes. The standard ratios — half, a third, a quarter — are followed according to local practice. Whatever buyers in that locale expect is what they receive, and expectation neutralizes deception.

### Pressing Time Rule

Rav restates the principle: *u-vein ha-gitot shanu* — the foundational ruling is that wine mixing is calibrated to the pressing season. Outside of that period, mixing was originally not ideal, as noted earlier. Modern practice has departed from this in the manner Rav Pappa described — through general social awareness that mixing occurs regardless of season, or through the tasting practice attributed to Rabbi Achi.

## Fair Competition

### Gifts to Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא יְחַלֵּק הַחֶנְוָנִי קְלָיוֹת וֶאֱגוֹזִין לַתִּינוֹקוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מַרְגִּילָן לָבֹא אֶצְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Yehuda says: A storekeeper may not hand out toasted grain and nuts to children** who patronize his store, **due to** the fact **that he** thereby **accustoms them to come to him** at the expense of competing storekeepers.

The Mishnah now addresses a set of questions about fair and unfair commercial competition. Rabbi Yehuda rules: *lo yechalek chanvani kaliyot ve-egozim le-tinokot mipnei she-hu margilan lavo etzlo* — a storekeeper may not distribute roasted grain and nuts to children as a means of drawing customers to his store. The objection is not about price or quality but about the method of attracting customers. When children receive treats from a particular store, they will pull their parents there regardless of whether that store offers better prices or superior goods. Rabbi Yehuda considers this unfair to competing storekeepers whose goods may be equal or superior but who do not resort to such enticements.

### Sages Permit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis permit** doing so.

The Sages permit it. As the Gemara explains, the competing storekeeper can offer his own treats: *ana mefalgina amgozei ve-at palig shiskei* — I give out nuts, and you give out your own sweets. Since the practice is open and imitable, it does not constitute inherently unfair competition. The playing field remains level enough that each competitor can respond in kind.

### Price Undercutting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִפְחוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁעַר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זָכוּר לַטּוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **And one may not reduce** the price of sale items below **the** market **rate. And the Rabbis say:** If he wishes to do so, he should be **remembered positively.**

Rabbi Yehuda further prohibits selling below the market rate: *ve-lo yifchot et ha-sha'ar* — one may not undercut the prevailing price. His concern is parallel to the concern about treats: that a storekeeper with the means to sell below cost can drive competitors out of business through tactics that have nothing to do with the genuine quality of his goods.

### Competition Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מַרְוַוח לְתַרְעָא:
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It is **due to** the fact **that** by lowering the price **he eases the** market **rate,** i.e., his actions lead to the establishment of a lower market price.

The Sages disagree and go further: not only is price reduction permitted, the storekeeper who reduces prices *zachur latov* — should be remembered for good. The Gemara explains the Sages' reasoning: *de-kamravach le-tara* — because he widens the market rate, meaning he drives down the general price level, which benefits consumers broadly. Healthy price competition is praiseworthy because it serves the public. The Gemara's discussion does not address what the Sages' position would be if the playing field were genuinely unequal — that is, if one competitor had the capacity to sustain losses over time specifically to eliminate rivals rather than to genuinely serve customers. That question is left without explicit treatment.

### Bean Sifting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יָבוֹר אֶת הַגְּרִיסִין, דִּבְרֵי אַבָּא שָׁאוּל. וַחֲכָמִים מַתִּירִין, וּמוֹדִים שֶׁלֹּא יָבוֹר מֵעַל פִּי מְגוּרָה, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כְּגוֹנֵב אֶת הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** One **may not sift ground beans** to remove the waste, lest he charge an inappropriately high price for the sifted meal, beyond its actual value; this is **the statement of Abba Shaul. And the Rabbis permit** doing so. **And** the Rabbis **concede that one may not sift** the meal only **from** the beans that are close to **the opening of the bin** to create the impression that the contents of the entire bin were sifted, **as this is nothing other than deception.**

Abba Shaul introduces a further prohibition: *lo yavor et ha-grisin* — one may not sift ground beans for sale. The concern, as understood through Rashi, is that by sifting the beans and removing the shells, the seller can charge a premium that exceeds what the added labor of sifting is actually worth. The buyer pays not only for the product but for a service whose value may be inflated. An analogy might be a butcher who sells pre-cut thin cutlets at a price above what the actual labor of trimming and pounding would justify.

### Visible Quality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן חֲכָמִים? רַבִּי אַחָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אַחָא מַתִּיר בְּדָבָר הַנִּרְאֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Who** are the **Rabbis** whose opinion is cited in the mishna? It is **Rabbi Aḥa, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Aḥa permits** mixing, and sifting, and the like, in the case of **an item** in **which** the change **is obvious.**

The Sages permit sifting. Their reasoning, attributed by the Gemara to Rabbi Acha, is *matir be-davar ha-nireh* — that when what you are receiving is visible and evident, permission follows. Rashi explains: the buyer can see for himself exactly what he is purchasing; he understands the difference between sifted and unsifted produce; he knows that someone expended effort to sift it; and he chooses to pay the premium for the convenience. The price is not being concealed — it is openly known — and an informed buyer who knowingly pays for convenience is not being wronged.

### Display Deception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין מְפַרְכְּסִין, לֹא אֶת הָאָדָם וְכוּ׳ וְלֹא אֶת הַכֵּלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **One may neither adorn a person,** nor an animal, **nor vessels.**

Even the Sages, who permit sifting for sale, agree on one thing: *shelo yavor me'al pi ha-migura she-eino ela ke-gonev et ha-ayin* — one may not place sifted beans on top of the storage bin while unsifted beans lie beneath, because this deceives the eye. The buyer looking at the display sees a uniform layer of clean, sifted produce and reasonably assumes the entire quantity matches. That is *gonev et ha-ayin* — stealing the eye — and is forbidden regardless of one's general view on sifting.

### Beautification Ban

The Mishnah concludes: *ein mefarchasin lo et ha-adam ve-lo et ha-behema ve-lo et ha-kelim* — one may not beautify a person, an animal, or vessels in order to misrepresent their condition. The Gemara will elaborate on precisely what this means and where the lines are drawn.

## Beautification Details

### Treats Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא מְפַלֵּיגְנָא אַמְגּוֹזֵי, וְאַתְּ פַּלֵּיג שִׁיסְקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for the** opinion of **the Rabbis,** who permit the free distribution of toasted grain and nuts? It is **because** the storekeeper can **say to his** competitors: **I hand out nuts; and you hand out jujubes [*shiskei*].** All merchants are able to hand out goods that will attract children, and consequently this is not unfair competition.

The Gemara resolves the opening debate about treats for children: the Sages permit it because competition is the natural corrective. *Ana mefalgina amgozei ve-at palig shiskei* — I give nuts, you give prunes. Since any storekeeper can offer his own inducements, the practice does not create a structural disadvantage; it invites a response in kind.

### Price Praise

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יִפְחוֹת אֶת הַשַּׁעַר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זָכוּר לַטּוֹב וְכוּ׳. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And one may not reduce** the price of items below **the** market **rate. And the Rabbis say:** If he wishes to do so, he should be **remembered positively.** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason for the** opinion of the **Rabbis,** who not only condone the practice but even praise the person, saying that he should be remembered positively?

On price reduction, the Sages not only permit it but praise it: *de-kamravach le-tara* — because the storekeeper who sells below market price exerts downward pressure on the general going rate. Competition of this kind benefits the broader public, and the one who initiates it deserves positive recognition.

The Gemara then identifies "the Sages" in the dispute with Abba Shaul over sifting as Rabbi Acha: *de-tanya Rabbi Acha matir be-davar ha-nireh* — Rabbi Acha permits wherever the quality is visible and the buyer can assess what he is paying for. Rashi elaborates: the buyer is not simply paying for the absence of shells; he is paying for the convenience of having someone else do the sifting, and that is a legitimate exchange when both parties see clearly what is on offer.

### Beautification Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מְשַׁרְבְּטִין אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה, וְאֵין נוֹפְחִין בַּקְּרָבַיִים, וְאֵין שׁוֹרִין אֶת הַבָּשָׂר בַּמַּיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may neither stiffen** the hair of **an animal** to create the impression that it is more voluminous than it is, **nor inflate innards** sold as meat to create the impression that it is a more substantial piece of meat, **nor soak meat in water** in order to change its color and create the impression that it is a choice cut.

A *baraita* provides concrete illustrations of forbidden beautification: *ein mesharvitin et ha-behema* — one may not do things to the hair of an animal to make it appear ruddier, fuller, or healthier than it is; *ve-ein nofchin be-kirbayim* — one may not inflate an animal's inner organs to make them appear to have greater volume; *ve-ein shorin et ha-basar ba-mayim* — one may not soak meat in water to make it appear larger or to increase its weight. These cases map onto timeless concerns about honest representation. The Gemara observes, pointedly, that selling a small amount of product in an oversized box — though the net weight may appear somewhere on the packaging — raises similar concerns.

### Mesharvitin Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֵין מְשַׁרְבְּטִין? הָכָא תַּרְגִּמוּ: מַיָּא דְחִיזְרָא. זְעֵירִי אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: מַזְקַפְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **One may not stiffen** the hair of **an animal? Here,** in Babylonia, **they explained** that it means to feed the animal **bran water,** which inflates its intestines and causes its hair to stand on end. **Ze'eiri said** in the name of **Rav Kahana:** It means **scrubbing** the hair clean to increase its volume.

The Gemara inquires: *mai ein mesharvitin?* — what exactly does the term *mesharvitin* mean? In Bavel the term is explained as *maya de-chizra* — water of a bran solution, which Rashi identifies as a type of bran-water that when applied to an animal gives its coat a ruddy, healthier appearance. An alternative tradition, cited in the name of Rav Kahana, understands *mazkafita* — a comb — by which one combs the animal's fur to make it stand up and look more impressive. Both amount to cosmetic manipulations intended to give a false impression of the animal's condition.

### Fringes Permitted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל שְׁרָא לְמִרְמֵא תּוּמֵי לְסַרְבָּלָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Shmuel permitted** sellers **to place fringes on a cloak;**

Shmuel permitted *le-mirmay tumei le-sarvela* — placing decorative fringes on a cloak before sale. The rationale is that adding fringes does not misrepresent the age or condition of the garment; it makes the garment more attractive and appealing. The buyer is drawn to a more beautiful object, and while the fringes may make the entire garment appear worth more than the fringes themselves cost, there is no deception about what the garment is. Enhancing attractiveness is categorically different from disguising defects or misrepresenting age.

### Garment Enhancement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה שְׁרָא לְכַסְכּוֹסֵי קִרְמֵי. רַבָּה שְׁרָא לְמִידַּק צַדְרֵי. רָבָא שְׁרָא לְצַלּוֹמֵי גִּירֵי. רַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל שְׁרָא לְצַלּוֹמֵי דִּיקּוּלֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda permitted** them **to clean** and adorn **ornamented garments; Rabba permitted** them **to taper linen garments** to cause them to appear more fine; **Rava permitted** them **to draw arrows** to ornament garments; **Rav Pappa bar Shmuel permitted** them **to draw baskets** for ornamentation.

Rav Yehuda permitted *la-chaschuche karmei* — cleaning and freshening colored garments to make them look crisp and attractive. Rabbah permitted *le-meidak tzaradin* — cutting or tapering linen garments in a way that improves their shape and appearance. In each case, the enhancement makes something genuinely nicer without misrepresenting what it is.

### Decorative Designs

The preceding section already contained this passage. In each case, the enhancement makes something genuinely nicer without misrepresenting what it is.

Rava permitted drawing arrows on garments, and Rav Pappa bar Shmuel permitted drawing ladders, baskets, or similar decorative designs on garments or vessels. The common thread running through all these permissions is that they make objects more visually appealing — drawing the eye and stimulating the desire to purchase — without making anything appear to be something it is not.

### Old vs New

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אֵין מְפַרְכְּסִין לֹא אֶת הָאָדָם, וְלֹא אֶת הַבְּהֵמָה, וְלֹא אֶת הַכֵּלִים! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּחַדְתֵי, הָא בְּעַתִּיקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna: **One may neither adorn a person, nor an animal, nor vessels?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** series of cases, where the Sages permitted adorning merchandise, are cases of **new** merchandise. It may be decorated, as doing so merely enhances its intrinsic beauty. **That** ruling in the mishna, according to which adornment is prohibited, is referring to cases **of old** merchandise, as the adornment is meant to conceal its flaws.

The Gemara draws the governing distinction: *lo kashya ha be-chadtei ha be-atikei* — there is no contradiction between these permissions and the Mishnah's prohibition. The prohibition applies to old items that are being treated to look new — that is a misrepresentation of the thing's essential nature and condition. The permissions apply to new items being enhanced — adding design, fringe, or freshness to something new is not misrepresenting it; it is presenting it attractively. The line is between deception and enhancement.

### Old Slave Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פִּרְכּוּס דְּאָדָם מַאי הִיא? כִּי הָא דְּהָהוּא עַבְדָּא סָבָא, דַּאֲזַל צַבְעֵיהּ לְרֵישֵׁיהּ וּלְדִיקְנֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרָבָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיבְנַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִהְיוּ עֲנִיִּים בְּנֵי בֵיתֶךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Adornment of a person, what is it?** The Gemara relates: It is **as** in **that** incident **involving a certain elderly slave who went** and **dyed his head and beard** black to create a younger impression. **He came before Rava** and **said to him: Purchase me** as your slave. Rava **said to him** that there is a rabbinic adage: **Let the poor be members of your household.** I follow their advice and therefore do not require a slave. If I need assistance, the paupers who frequent my house can assist me.

The Gemara provides a vivid illustration of the prohibition applied to persons: there was an old slave who dyed the hair of his head and beard black to make himself appear young and came before Rava saying, *zavnan* — buy me. Rava declined, saying: *yihiyu aniyim benei beitecha* — let the poor be the members of your household. Rashi explains that Rava preferred, if he was to support dependents, to support poor Jews rather than a non-Jewish slave — for there were no Jewish slaves in the era of the Gemara, when *yovel* was not observed. Rava, in other words, declined for reasons of his own, but the Gemara clearly uses the story to illustrate the prohibition against misrepresenting a person's age or condition.

### Righteous Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל, זַבְנֵיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״אַשְׁקְיַין מַיָּא!״ אֲזַל חַוְּורֵיהּ לְרֵישֵׁיהּ וּלְדִיקְנֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״חֲזִי דַּאֲנָא קַשִּׁישׁ מֵאֲבוּךְ״. קָרֵי אַנַּפְשֵׁיהּ: ״צַדִּיק מִצָּרָה נֶחֱלָץ וַיָּבֹא אַחֵר תַּחְתָּיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The slave **came before Rav Pappa bar Shmuel,** who **purchased him. One day** Rav Pappa **said to** the slave: **Give me water to drink.** The slave **went and** removed the dye and **whitened** the hair on **his head and his beard.** The slave **said to** Rav Pappa: **See that I am older than your father,** and I am unfit to serve you. Rav Pappa **read about himself: The righteous person is delivered from trouble, and another comes in his stead** (see Proverbs 11:8). Rav Pappa applied the verse to the incident of the slave. The righteous person, Rava, was spared the problem of the slave; while another, Rav Pappa bar Shmuel, came in his stead.

The slave then went to Rav Pappa bar Shmuel, who was taken in by the deception and purchased him. One day Rav Pappa instructed the slave to bring him water. The slave went, washed out the dye from his hair and beard, returned with white hair revealed, and told Rav Pappa: *chazi de-ana kashishna me-avuch* — look, I am older than your father; you cannot expect me to fetch water. You have bought yourself a very old man whom you must now support with very little utility in return. Rav Pappa applied to himself the *pasuk*: *tzadik mi-tzara nechlatz ve-yavo rasha tachtav* — the righteous one is saved from affliction, and another comes in his place. Rava was the *tzadik* who wisely avoided this purchase; Rav Pappa was the one who took his place. The Gemara notes, without embarrassment, that the *pasuk* literally says *rasha* — but one does not apply that word to oneself; the point is simply that someone else bore the consequence that Rava escaped.

## Transition to Interest

### End Ona'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַרַן עֲלָךְ הַזָּהָב
>
> **Translation:**

*Hadran alach ha-zahav* — we conclude the chapter *Ha-zahav* and with it the entire discussion of *ona'ah* — overcharging and fairness in business. We now turn to a new and analytically demanding *perek*: *Eizehu Neshech*, which addresses the prohibition of charging interest.

### Neshech & Tarbit

The Torah forbids taking from a borrower *neshech ve-tarbit* — two terms that the Gemara will explore carefully. *Neshech* derives from the root meaning a bite: the lender takes a bite out of the borrower, causing him loss. *Tarbit* derives from *ribui* — increase, profit — because the lender gains more than he lent. These are two sides of the same transaction: one describes what the borrower suffers, the other what the lender gains. As we will see, the Mishnah uses *neshech* to denote *ribis de-Oraisa* — the biblically prohibited form of interest — and *tarbit* for *ribis de-Rabbanan* — the rabbinic extension of that prohibition.

---

## Neshech Definitions

### Neshech Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵיזֶהוּ נֶשֶׁךְ וְאֵיזֶהוּ תַּרְבִּית? אֵיזֶהוּ נֶשֶׁךְ? הַמַּלְוֶה סֶלַע בַּחֲמִשָּׁה דִּינָרִין, סָאתַיִם חִטִּין בְּשָׁלֹשׁ – אָסוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נוֹשֵׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Torah states the prohibition against taking interest: "And if your brother becomes impoverished, and his hand falters with you, then you shall support him; whether a stranger or a native, he shall live with you. You shall not take from him interest [*neshekh*] or increase [*tarbit*]; you shall fear your God and your brother shall live with you. You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and with *marbit* you shall not give him your food" (Leviticus 25:35–37). The mishna asks: **Which is *neshekh*, and which is *tarbit*?** **Which is** the case in which there is ***neshekh*?** With regard to **one who lends** another **a *sela*,** worth four dinars, **for five dinars** to be paid later, or one who lends another **two *se'a* of wheat for three** *se'a* to be returned later, this is **prohibited, as it is taking interest [*noshekh*].**

The Mishnah asks: *Eizehu neshech?* — What is *neshech*? The answer: *ha-malveh sela be-chamisha dinarin* — one who lends a *sela* (worth four *dinarim*) with the agreement that five *dinarim* are repaid. Alternatively, *sa'atayim chitin be-shalosh* — two *se'ah* of grain lent against an obligation to repay three. The Mishnah deliberately cites both money and grain. This is because certain *psukim* — as the Gemara will elaborate — seem to associate *neshech* specifically with money and *marbis* specifically with food, which might imply a categorical distinction. The Mishnah forestalls that reading by designating both as *neshech*, showing that the designation refers to the nature of the transaction — lending X and demanding X plus something in return — not to the commodity involved.

### Tarbit Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵיזֶהוּ תַּרְבִּית? הַמְרַבֶּה בְּפֵירוֹת. כֵּיצַד? לָקַח הֵימֶנּוּ חִטִּין בְּדִינַר זָהָב הַכּוֹר, וְכֵן הַשַּׁעַר. עָמְדוּ חִטִּין בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים דִּינָרִין. אָמַר לוֹ: תֵּן לִי חִטַּי, שֶׁאֲנִי רוֹצֶה לְמוֹכְרָן וְלִיקַּח בָּהֶן יַיִן. אָמַר לוֹ: הֲרֵי חִטֶּיךָ עֲשׂוּיוֹת עָלַי בִּשְׁלֹשִׁים, וַהֲרֵי לְךָ אֶצְלִי בָּהֶן יַיִן, וְיַיִן אֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And which is** the case in which there is ***tarbit*?** It is the case of **one who** enters into a transaction that yields **an increase in the produce** beyond his investment. **How so?** For example, **one acquired wheat from** another at the price **of** one ***kor*** of wheat **for** one **gold dinar,** worth twenty-five silver dinars, with the wheat to be supplied at a later date, **and such** was **the** market **price** of wheat at the time he acquired it. The price of one *kor* of **wheat** then increased and **stood at thirty dinars.** At that point, the buyer **said to** the seller: **Give me** all of **my wheat** now, **as I wish to sell it and purchase wine with it.** The seller **said to him:** Since it is ultimately wine that you want, not wheat, each *kor* of **your wheat is considered by me** to be worth **thirty** dinars, **and you have** the right to collect **its** value in **wine from me. And** in this case, the seller **did not have wine** in his possession. If wine then appreciates in value, the result will be an interest-bearing transaction, as the buyer collects from the seller wine worth more than the wheat for which he paid.

The Mishnah then asks: *ve-eizo tarbit?* — What is *tarbit*? The case is: *lakach heimenu chitin be-dinar zahav ha-kor ve-chein ha-sha'ar* — one purchased grain from a farmer at the market rate of one golden *dinar* per *kor* of grain, at a time when that was the prevailing *sha'ar* — the standard going price at harvest time when sellers are bringing their grain to market. The payment is made up front, but the grain will be delivered gradually over the course of the year.

### Market Rate Sale

This arrangement, taken by itself, is permitted. One might worry that since grain prices typically rise after the harvest period, the buyer who receives grain later in the year receives something worth more than he paid — making the transaction resemble a loan on which the "interest" is the appreciation in value. But the Gemara will explain that this concern does not arise when the grain is sold at the prevailing market rate at a time when the commodity is readily available. Since the grain exists and can be accessed, the arrangement is treated as a genuine sale — albeit with delayed delivery — rather than a loan, and the later appreciation in value does not constitute *ribis*.

### Price Rise Problem

The complication arises in the continuation of the case. After the purchase, *amdu chitin be-lamed dinarim* — the price of grain rises, so that the same quantity now costs thirty silver *dinarim* rather than the original twenty-five. At that point, the buyer says to the farmer: *ten li chitai she-ani rotzeh le-mochran ve-likach bahen yayin* — give me my grain now; I want to sell it at the higher price and use the proceeds to buy wine. The farmer responds: *harei chitecha asui alai be-shloshim ve-harei lach etzli bahen yayin* — your grain as I owe it to you is valued at thirty *dinarim*; let me instead give you thirty *dinarim* worth of wine. The problem is that the farmer does not currently have the wine. He is essentially converting one deferred obligation into a different deferred obligation in a commodity he does not yet possess. This matters because the wine, when eventually delivered, may be worth more than thirty *dinarim* at the time of delivery.

The Mishnah appears to suggest that even had the farmer simply offered to pay thirty *dinarim* in cash — the appreciated value of the grain — that would have been permissible. What makes this case problematic is the commitment to supply wine that he does not have, which introduces new risks of the delivered commodity appreciating further. We will return to the full Gemara analysis of this case in coming days.

## Neshech-Tarbit Unity

### Single Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִדְּשָׁבֵיק לְרִיבִּית דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְקָא מְפָרֵשׁ דְּרַבָּנַן, מִכְּלָל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דְּנֶשֶׁךָ וְתַרְבִּית – חֲדָא מִלְּתָא הִיא. וְהָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: נֶשֶׁךְ כֶּסֶף, וְרִיבִּית אוֹכֶל!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **From** the fact **that** in explaining the term *tarbit*, the *tanna* **sets aside the topic** of **interest by Torah law,** which is interest decided upon at the time of a loan, **and** instead **explicates** a case of lending with interest that is prohibited **by rabbinic law,** one can conclude **by inference** that **by Torah law, *neshekh* and *tarbit* are one matter,** and there is no halakhic distinction between them. The Gemara asks: **But aren't** the **verses written** using the term ***neshekh*** for interest that is on a loan of **money and** *tarbit* or *marbit*, which are cognates of the term ***ribit*,** for interest that is on a loan of **food?** This is as the verse states: "You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and with *marbit* you shall not give him your food" (Leviticus 25:37).

The Gemara makes a striking inference from the structure of the Mishnah itself. Having asked *eizehu neshech ve-eizo tarbit*, the Mishnah might have been expected to explain two distinct biblical categories: *neshech* as one variety of Torah-prohibited interest and *tarbit* as another. The Torah does use both words, and one could have imagined that each term covers a different type or domain of interest. But the Mishnah did not do this. Instead, when it turned to *tarbit*, it described a rabbinic prohibition — a case that the Gemara will categorize as *ribis de-Rabbanan*, not *de-Oraisa*. *Mide-shavik le-ribbis de-Oraisa ve-kamafaresh de-Rabbanan* — from the fact that the Mishnah abandoned any attempt to explain a biblical *tarbit* category and went directly to a rabbinic one, we infer: *michlal de-Oraisa de-neshech ve-tarbis chada milsa hi* — on the Torah level, *neshech* and *tarbit* are one and the same thing.

### Two Sides

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיסְבְּרָא דְּאִיכָּא נֶשֶׁךְ בְּלֹא תַּרְבִּית, וְתַרְבִּית בְּלֹא נֶשֶׁךְ? נֶשֶׁךְ בְּלֹא תַּרְבִּית הֵיכִי דָּמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And can you understand that there is *neshekh* without *tarbit*, and *tarbit* without *neshekh*?** The term *neshekh*, from a root meaning bite, connotes loss to the borrower, while the term *tarbit*, literally increase, connotes profit for the lender. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** where there could be ***neshekh* without *tarbit*?**

The Gemara acknowledges an obvious challenge: the Torah does use *neshekh* in connection with money and *marbis* in connection with food, which might suggest they are not the same but simply different terms applying to different commodities. The Gemara's counter is deeper than merely saying the words are synonyms. It argues that *neshekh* and *tarbit* point to different perspectives on the same transaction — *neshekh* from the borrower's vantage (he is being bitten, he suffers loss), *tarbit* from the lender's vantage (he gains a profit). If the Torah had meant to say that the prohibition by money is specifically about the borrower's loss and the prohibition by food is specifically about the lender's gain, that would require the two to be conceptually separable. But the Gemara demonstrates they are not.

### Currency Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דְּאוֹזְפֵיהּ מְאָה בִּמְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים, מֵעִיקָּרָא קָיְימִי מְאָה בְּדַנְקָא, וּלְבַסּוֹף קָיְימִי מְאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים בְּדַנְקָא. נֶשֶׁךְ אִיכָּא – דְּקָא נָכֵית לֵיהּ, דְּקָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ מִידֵּי דְּלָא יָהֵיב. וְתַרְבִּית לֵיכָּא – דְּלֵית לֵיהּ רַוְוחָא, דְּדַנְקָא אוֹזְפֵיהּ וְדַנְקָא קָא שָׁקֵיל מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **If** it is in a case where **one lends to** another **one hundred** *perutot* **with** the agreement to be repaid **one hundred and twenty,** and **initially one hundred** copper *perutot* **are** worth **one-sixth [*bedanka*]** of a dinar, **and ultimately,** when he pays, **one hundred and twenty** *perutot* **are** worth **one-sixth** of a dinar, this is not an example of one without the other. Although one might say that **there is *neshekh*, as** the lender **reduces** the borrower's assets **since** the lender **takes** in payment **from** the borrower coins **that he did not give** him in the loan, **and there is no *tarbit*, as there is no profit** for the lender in this transaction, **since he lent him one-sixth** of a dinar **and he received from him one-sixth** of a dinar, that is not correct.

*Ve-tisabra de-ika neshech belo tarbis ve-tarbis belo neshech?* — Does it make sense that one could exist without the other? The Gemara constructs a hypothetical. Suppose one lends a hundred pennies with an agreement to receive back a hundred and twenty. At the time of the loan, a hundred pennies were worth one *ma'ah* — call it a dollar. But by the time of repayment, currency has devalued so that it now takes a hundred and twenty pennies to equal a dollar. One might reason: *neshech ika* — there is *neshech*, because the borrower gave back twenty more than he received; *ve-tarbis leka* — but there is no *tarbit*, because the lender received back exactly the same buying power he lent. So the borrower suffered loss but the lender made no gain.

### Value Perspective

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, אִי בָּתַר מֵעִיקָּרָא אָזְלַתְּ – הֲרֵי נֶשֶׁךְ וַהֲרֵי תַּרְבִּית. אִי בָּתַר בְּסוֹף אָזְלַתְּ, לָא נֶשֶׁךְ אִיכָּא וְלָא תַּרְבִּית אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Ultimately, if you go according to the initial** value, when the loan was granted, **there is *neshekh* and there is *tarbit*,** as the borrower agreed to pay more than he received. **If you go according to the ultimate** value, when the loan was repaid, **there is neither *neshekh* nor is there *tarbit*,** as he repaid only the value he received.

The Gemara's response exposes the flaw: the apparent asymmetry disappears once you consistently apply the same temporal perspective. *Sof sof ei basar me'ikara azlat havei neshech ve-havei tarbis* — if you measure by the original values, then yes, the borrower gave back twenty more than he received, so he was bitten; but equally, the lender received back twenty more than he gave, so he made a profit. *Ei basar basof azlat lo neshech ika ve-lo tarbis ika* — if you measure by the values at repayment, then the borrower gave back something of equal buying power to what he received, so he was not bitten; and equally the lender received back something of equal buying power to what he gave, so he made no profit. The same logic applies in reverse for the case of currency appreciation, where one might try to identify *tarbis* without *neshech*: whichever temporal point one chooses as the reference, both perspectives move together. You cannot have one without the other, because they describe two sides of the same transaction observed from the same moment in time.

### Interest Cannot Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אִי אַתָּה מוֹצֵא לֹא נֶשֶׁךְ בְּלֹא תַּרְבִּית, וְלֹא תַּרְבִּית בְּלֹא נֶשֶׁךְ. וְלֹא חִלְּקָן הַכָּתוּב אֶלָּא לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנֵי לָאוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said: You do not find *neshekh* without *tarbit* nor *tarbit* without *neshekh*, and the verse distinguished** between **them only so that** lending with interest always involves **violating two prohibitions.**

Rava states the conclusion: *i ata motzeh lo neshech belo tarbis ve-lo tarbis belo neshech* — you will never find *neshech* without *tarbit* or *tarbit* without *neshech*. *Ve-lama chelkan ha-katuv? Ella la-avor alav bi-shnei lavin* — and why did the Torah use two words? To teach that when one charges interest, one transgresses two prohibitions simultaneously: the prohibition of taking a bite out of the borrower and the prohibition of taking an illicit profit for oneself. They are not separate domains of application; they are two dimensions of one act. And because they cannot be pulled apart conceptually, we will not arbitrarily assign one to money and one to food.

## Torah Sources

### Explicit Mentions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״אֶת כַּסְפְּךָ לֹא תִתֵּן לוֹ בְּנֶשֶׁךְ וּבְמַרְבִּית לֹא תִתֵּן אׇכְלֶךָ״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא נֶשֶׁךְ בְּכֶסֶף וְרִבִּית בְּאוֹכֶל. נֶשֶׁךְ בְּאוֹכֶל מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ אֹכֶל״. רִבִּית בְּכֶסֶף מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״נֶשֶׁךְ כֶּסֶף״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"You shall not give him your money with *neshekh* and with *marbit* you shall not give him your food"** (Leviticus 25:37). **I have** derived **only** that there is a prohibition of ***neshekh* for** a loan of **money and** a prohibition of ***ribit* for** a loan of **food. From where** is it derived that there is ***neshekh* with regard to** a loan of **food** as well? The *baraita* answers: A different **verse states:** "You shall not lend with interest [*tashikh*] to your brother: *Neshekh* of money, ***neshekh* of food,** *neshekh* of anything that is lent with interest [*asher yishakh*]" (Deuteronomy 23:20). The *baraita* continues: **From where** is it derived that there is ***ribit* with regard to** a loan of **money? The verse states: "*Neshekh* of money."**

A *baraita* addresses the biblical sources. The *pasuk* in Vayikra states: *es kaspecha lo siten lo be-neshech u-ve-marbis lo siten achlecha* — do not give your money with *neshech* and do not give your food with *marbis*. This verse explicitly names *neshech* by money and *marbis* by food. *Ein li ella neshech be-chesef ve-ribis be-ochel* — from this alone I would know only the prohibition of *neshech* by money and *ribis* by food. *Neshech be-ochel minayin?* — From where do we derive *neshech* as applied to food?

### Derived Prohibitions

The verse in Devarim answers: *neshech kesef neshech ochel* — *neshech* of money, *neshech* of food. The Torah explicitly applies *neshech* to both domains. *Ribis be-chesef minayin?* — From where do we know that the prohibition of *marbis* applies to money as well? It is never stated in those terms. The derivation is through a hermeneutical move: *im eino inyan le-neshech kesef she-harei kvar ne'emar lo tasshich le-achicha* — since the additional mention of *neshech kesef* in Devarim is redundant (another *pasuk* already establishes *neshech* by money), that surplus mention must be doing additional work. *Atnei inyan le-ribbis kesef* — it comes to teach us that the prohibition of *marbis* applies to money as well.

### Final Conclusion

The upshot is that all four combinations are covered: *neshech* and *marbis* each apply to both money and food. And underlying this symmetry is the principle already established — that *neshech* and *tarbit* are not two separate prohibitions but two perspectives on one: every act of charging interest simultaneously bites the borrower and enriches the lender, and one who does so transgresses both *lavin* at once. Whether the medium is money or grain, and whether one focuses on what the borrower loses or what the lender gains, the prohibition is identical in scope and nature.
