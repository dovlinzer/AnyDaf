# Bekhorot 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Peter Chamor Status

### Intro to Peter Chamor

The sugya opens against the backdrop of a dispute that has occupied the preceding daf: the Torah commands the redemption of the firstborn donkey (*peter chamor*) but leaves unstated the status of both the redemptive sheep and the donkey itself. The Gemara had proceeded on the assumption that the sheep passes entirely into the *kohen*'s ownership without acquiring sanctity, and the central question became: what is the status of the donkey prior to redemption?

This gave rise to a Tannaitic dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yehuda maintained that the donkey is forbidden in benefit (*asur behana'ah*), reasoning that a redemption is only meaningful if it comes to remove an existing status — just as is the case with other sanctified objects. (The apparent counter-proof from the firstborn human was noted but not treated as decisive.) Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, held that the donkey carries no intrinsic sanctity; there is a *mitzvah* to redeem it, but the donkey itself has no forbidden status.

Now the Gemara turns to a further question: what happens if the owner failed to redeem the donkey and instead performed *arifah* — broke its neck? The Gemara has been advancing the claim that even Rabbi Shimon would concede that after the neck-breaking the donkey becomes *asur behana'ah*, drawing the comparison to the *eglah arufah* (the calf whose neck is broken in the ritual described in Deuteronomy 21). The remainder of this daf is devoted to examining whether that concession can be demonstrated, and to recording the divergent opinions of the *Amoraim* on exactly this point.

### Braita: Forbidden Things

The first attempted proof comes from a *braita* that discusses a category of things forbidden in benefit — including the *shor haniskal* (the ox condemned to stoning), the *eglah arufah*, and, notably, the broken-necked donkey — and asks whether these objects are considered "food" for the purposes of *tum'at ochlin* (susceptibility to food-impurity). The Rabbis hold that these items are physically edible and therefore retain the status of food; Rabbi Shimon disagrees, ruling that they are not food because no Jew may eat them and, crucially, no Jew may even give them to a non-Jew to eat — since deriving any benefit from them, even indirectly, is prohibited.

The Rabbis' position turns on physical edibility; Rabbi Shimon's position turns on the *halachic* capacity for consumption and benefit. Importantly, the broken-necked donkey appears in Rabbi Shimon's list of things that are not food precisely because they are *asur behana'ah*. This seems to establish directly that Rabbi Shimon acknowledges the donkey's forbidden status once the neck has been broken.

### Basar Bechalav Exception

There is, however, one item that Rabbi Shimon concedes is considered food even though it belongs to the category of things forbidden in benefit: *basar bechalav* — meat cooked with milk. His rationale is that before the two were cooked together, each component was individually permitted and unambiguously food. They therefore carry that prior status of food into the forbidden state. The one exception Rabbi Shimon admits is grounded in this principle of *sha'at kosher* — the time when the item was permissible.

## Meat & Milk Analysis

### Sha'at Kosher Argument

The Gemara immediately challenges this reasoning. If Rabbi Shimon's justification for treating *basar bechalav* as food is merely that it had a prior permissible status (*sha'at kosher*), one might ask: why rely on that historical consideration at all? Let the reason be simply that *basar bechalav* can be given to a non-Jew to eat, since it is, after all, permissible in benefit — and if something is edible by others, that suffices to give it food-status.

### Shimon's Benefit View

The Gemara's answer is striking: the very same Rabbi Shimon who rules that things *asur behana'ah* are not food is also the Rabbi Shimon who holds that *basar bechalav* is *mutar behana'ah* — permitted in benefit. This is preserved in an explicit *baraita*: Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon (Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai) that meat cooked with milk is forbidden to eat but permitted in benefit. The scriptural basis is a *gezerah shavah*: the Torah uses the phrase "a holy people" (*am kadosh*) both in connection with *basar bechalav* and in connection with *treifah* meat, about which the Torah states explicitly that it may be cast to the dogs. From the fact that *treifah* is *mutar behana'ah* and the same "holiness" language is used for *basar bechalav*, Rabbi Shimon derives that *basar bechalav* too is *mutar behana'ah*. (The practical relevance is well known: the question of whether one may purchase pet food containing cooked meat-and-milk mixtures depends entirely on this dispute; the normative ruling follows the majority view that *basar bechalav* is *asur behana'ah*, but Rabbi Shimon's dissent is real and recorded.)

With this in mind, the Gemara explains that Rabbi Shimon has not one but two independent reasons to consider *basar bechalav* as food: first, he personally holds it is *mutar behana'ah* and may therefore be fed to non-Jews; and second, even granting the general assumption that it is *asur behana'ah*, it still had a *sha'at kosher* — it was food before it was cooked. Either basis would suffice; both are present. By contrast, for every other item on Rabbi Shimon's list of things *asur behana'ah*, neither condition obtains: they cannot be given to non-Jews (since a Jew may derive no benefit), and they had no prior period of permissible status as food. The broken-necked donkey falls squarely in this category — and its inclusion in that list appears to confirm that, according to Rabbi Shimon, once the neck is broken the donkey is *asur behana'ah*.

One might ask about the *eglah arufah*, also on that list: could it not claim a *sha'at kosher*, having once been a living calf that could theoretically have been slaughtered for food? The answer is no — the *eglah arufah* was never actually edible as such; meat and milk, by contrast, were literally food in their separate states before being cooked together. The cases are not comparable.

## Peter Chamor Evidence

### Braita Proof

Returning to the *baraita*, the Gemara draws the following inference: if Rabbi Shimon held that the broken-necked donkey remains *mutar behana'ah* even after the neck is broken, he should have listed it alongside *basar bechalav* as an exception — something that is permissible in benefit and can be fed to non-Jews, thus qualifying as food. The fact that he does *not* make this exception, and instead places the broken-necked donkey among the items that are not food, is a strong indication that in Rabbi Shimon's view the breaking of the neck renders the donkey *asur behana'ah*. This is a direct and seemingly powerful proof.

### Donkey Meat Avoidance

The Gemara, however, attempts an evasion. Perhaps the broken-necked donkey's exclusion from food-status has nothing to do with being *asur behana'ah* at all. Perhaps it is simply that donkey meat is something people do not normally eat. In the *halachot* of *tum'at ochlin*, an item requires either common practice of being eaten or a specific intention (*machshavah*) in order to be classified as food. If the donkey was specifically not prepared and intended as food, it would not be considered food regardless of its benefit-status. On this reading, Rabbi Shimon's statement about the broken-necked donkey tells us nothing about whether it is *asur behana'ah*; it merely reflects the practical reality that no one eats donkey meat.

### Evasion Rejected

This evasion, however, is too easy to sustain. If the entire point of listing the broken-necked donkey is simply that people do not eat donkey meat, then the debate in the *baraita* becomes very strange. The Rabbis, who are disputing Rabbi Shimon's exclusion of these items from food-status, are apparently prepared to say that the broken-necked donkey *is* food. But if this were merely a question of practical eating habits, the Rabbis could not plausibly claim donkey meat is food without some *halachic* basis. The force of their position is that the forbidden status itself is what confers food-significance — the principle that *issuro chishuvo*, its being forbidden implies its being recognized as food. If that principle does not apply (and as we shall see it will be entirely rejected), then the Rabbis have no ground for calling donkey meat food, and there would be nothing to debate. The case must therefore turn on the specific character of the broken-necked donkey as *asur behana'ah*, not on generic donkey-meat practices. The evasion fails.

## Dead Bird Carcass

### Thirteen Things

The discussion now broadens into a significant tangent that will ultimately illuminate the theoretical question at stake. The Gemara introduces a teaching: thirteen rules apply to the *nivlas of tahor* — the carcass of a kosher bird that died without proper *shechitah*. One of these rules is that such a carcass requires *machshavah* (intention to eat it) in order to acquire food-status and thus become susceptible to *tum'at ochlin*; yet it does not require *hechsher* (prior contact with one of the seven liquids that render food susceptible to impurity).

These two rulings reflect distinct principles. The carcass does not require *hechsher* because it is destined to be — or is already — a source of *tumah* in its own right: a carcass of a kosher bird, when swallowed, imparts *tumah* to the one who eats it. Since the item can itself generate *tumah*, it does not need the usual prerequisite of liquid contact to become susceptible to *tumah* from without. But it does require *machshavah* because, under normal circumstances, people do not eat carcasses of dead birds — they are unpleasant, often diseased, and generally avoided. Therefore, the carcass is not automatically treated as food; it becomes food only if the owner consciously intends to eat it.

### Forbidden=Food Debate

At this point the Gemara poses a sharp question: if the Rabbis hold that being forbidden to eat (*issuro*) itself confers the status of food (*chishuvo* — its forbiddenness is its significance), then why should the carcass of a kosher bird require *machshavah* at all? The fact that the Torah declares it forbidden should by itself define it as food — for the Torah would not prohibit eating something it did not acknowledge as food.

The Gemara deflects by suggesting: *ha mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — this teaching follows Rabbi Shimon's position. Rabbi Shimon explicitly holds that forbidden status does not automatically define food-status; only items that are practically eaten count as food. So the rule that a bird carcass requires *machshavah* is entirely consistent with Rabbi Shimon. There is an interesting inversion here worth pausing over: in the primary *baraita* with which the sugya opened, the Rabbis argued that physically edible things are food (the practical criterion), while Rabbi Shimon invoked the *halachic* status of being forbidden in benefit to remove items from food-status. Here, the Gemara suggests the Rabbis employ a *halachic* criterion (forbidden = food) while Rabbi Shimon insists on the practical criterion (is it actually eaten?). The positions are not simply reversed — in the original dispute, Rabbi Shimon uses *halachic* status to *restrict* food-classification, while here the Rabbis use *halachic* status to *expand* it — but the interplay between practical and *halachic* definitions of food is the persistent underlying theme.

### Kosher/Non-Kosher

The Gemara presses further with another *baraita* that provides a more comprehensive taxonomy. The *baraita* distinguishes between several cases. The carcass of a non-kosher animal (*neveilas behemah temei'ah*) — such as a donkey or horse — does not qualify as food in any location without *machshavah*, since it carries two disqualifying features: it is from a non-kosher species, and it is a *neveilah*. Similarly, the carcass of a kosher bird (*nivlas of tahor*) does not qualify as food without *machshavah* in village settings, where demand is low and people would not normally eat it; only in cities, where there is greater demand, is it considered food without specific intention. On the other hand, the carcass of a kosher animal (*neveilas behemah tehorah*) — such as a cow that died without *shechitah* — is considered food everywhere without *machshavah*, since carcasses of dead cows are in practice something people eat. Likewise, a bird carcass in the cities does not require *machshavah*.

The pattern is clear: the operative question is what people in practice eat, not *halachic* categorization. And once again, the Gemara invokes the *issuro chishuvo* principle as a potential challenge: given that the Rabbis hold that forbidden status defines food, why does the carcass of a non-kosher animal require *machshavah* anywhere? The answer, as before, is *ha mani Rabbi Shimon hi* — this must be Rabbi Shimon's position.

### Seifa Proof

But the Gemara cannot escape so easily this time. For the *seifa* — the concluding clause of that same *baraita* — explicitly quotes Rabbi Shimon, which means the *reisha* (the earlier clause) cannot also represent Rabbi Shimon. The end states: *Rabbi Shimon omer* — Rabbi Shimon says — that even the camel, the hare, the *shafan* (rock badger), and the pig do not require *machshavah* and do not require *hechsher* in order to be considered food. Rabbi Shimon thus expands the category of foods that are automatically treated as food even among non-kosher animals. His reason: *ho'il ve-yesh bahen siman taharah* — since these animals possess at least one of the signs of a kosher animal. The pig has split hooves; the camel, hare, and *shafan* chew their cud. Because these animals are not categorically outside the domain of *kashrut* markers, they remain sufficiently within the concept of "food" to qualify without *machshavah*.

The logic is layered. Rabbi Shimon introduces a new variable: not merely whether something is eaten in practice, but how far the *halachah* places it from the domain of food. An animal that is doubly removed from food-status — non-kosher species *and* a *neveilah* — requires *machshavah*. But an animal with at least one kosher *siman* is close enough to food that no *machshavah* is needed. The *reisha* of the *baraita*, which says that the carcass of a non-kosher animal requires *machshavah*, must therefore represent the Rabbis' view, not Rabbi Shimon's. And if the *reisha* is the Rabbis, then the Rabbis do not apply *issuro chishuvo* — the Rabbis too do not hold that being forbidden automatically confers food-status.

## Rejection of Principle

### Rava's Conclusion

Rava states the conclusion plainly: *dechulei alma lo amrinan issuro chashivuto* — no one holds that forbidden status automatically defines food-status. The principle of *issuro chishuvo* has been entirely discharged. The *seifa*'s attribution to Rabbi Shimon proves that the *reisha* reflects the Rabbis, and since the *reisha* requires *machshavah* for non-kosher animal carcasses, the Rabbis clearly do not apply *issuro chishuvo* either. The theoretical justification that had been offered for the Rabbis' position in the original dispute — that being forbidden implies being acknowledged as food — turns out to have no proponent. The original debate between the Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon about whether things *asur behana'ah* count as food must therefore be explained on different grounds entirely.

## Shechita for Practice

### Practice Shechita

With the *issuro chishuvo* principle eliminated, the Gemara returns to the original puzzle. The first evasion — that Rabbi Shimon excluded the broken-necked donkey from food-status simply because people do not eat donkey meat — had been rejected. But now that even the Rabbis agree that being forbidden does not automatically make something food, the Gemara attempts a more refined evasion.

If the donkey case is about the broken-necked donkey as such, then the debate is a debate about a very specific legal situation with a specific *halachic* outcome. But perhaps the *baraita* is not discussing the broken-necked *peter chamor* at all. Perhaps it is discussing an ordinary donkey that was slaughtered — not for food, not because it is a *peter chamor* — but purely for *shechitah* practice. A butcher learning the craft might slaughter a donkey to practice his technique, not intending to eat it (since it is not kosher), but planning to sell the carcass to non-Jews afterward.

The question then is: does performing a valid act of *shechitah* on a donkey, even purely for practice purposes, suffice to render the donkey's carcass food? If it does, then the Rabbis in the *baraita* are saying: yes, a practice-*shechitah* donkey is food. Rabbi Shimon is saying: no, donkey meat is still not food even with a practice *shechitah*, because no one normally eats donkey. The debate in the *baraita*, on this reading, has nothing to do with the *peter chamor* and its broken-neck status.

### Nimos vs Eliezer

The Gemara locates this question in an explicit Tannaitic dispute: the debate of Nimos and Rabbi Eliezer. The *baraita* reports that Nimos — identified as the brother of Rabbi Yehoshua HaGarsi — told Rabbi Yossi: if one slaughters a raven for *shechitah* practice, the blood of that *shechitah* is a *machshir* — one of the liquids that renders food susceptible to impurity. The blood of a valid *shechitah* act, even performed on a non-kosher bird purely for practice, counts halachically as *shechitah*-blood.

Rabbi Eliezer goes further: *dam shechitah le'olam machshir* — the blood of *shechitah* always makes things susceptible. The Gemara notes that at first glance Nimos and Rabbi Eliezer seem to be saying the same thing. The difference, it explains, lies in the principle of *issuro chishuvo* — but here applied in a more limited sense. Nimos holds that the *shechitah* blood is a *machshir* for *other* things — that is, the blood qualifies as liquid — but the raven *itself* does not thereby become susceptible as food. For the raven to be susceptible as food, one would need *machshavah* — deliberate intention to eat it — since no one normally eats ravens. Rabbi Eliezer, by contrast, holds that once *shechitah* was performed on the raven, the raven itself is considered food even without *machshavah*, and therefore susceptible to impurity without any further intention.

The practical upshot: according to Rabbi Eliezer, performing a *shechitah* on a non-kosher animal for practice purposes confers food-status on that animal's carcass without requiring the owner to intend to eat it. According to Nimos, it does not.

### Kosher Signs Factor

The Gemara then questions whether the dispute between Nimos and Rabbi Eliezer is actually about the practice *shechitah* at all. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer's reason for treating the raven as food has nothing to do with the *shechitah* having been performed — perhaps it is simply because a raven has some kosher *simanim*. The Gemara had just established from Rabbi Shimon's ruling (that the pig, camel, hare, and *shafan* are food without *machshavah* because they have partial kosher signs) that partial kosher *simanim* are relevant to food-classification. A raven too has some characteristics that could be linked to this framework. If so, Rabbi Eliezer's position may flow from the *simanim* alone, independent of any consideration of practice *shechitah*.

### Rejection of Reason

This suggestion, however, is immediately undone. If Rabbi Eliezer's sole reason were the partial kosher *simanim*, then the practice *shechitah* would be entirely irrelevant. But we know from Rabbi Shimon's ruling — that carcasses of animals with partial *simanim* are food even without any *shechitah* at all — that partial *simanim* alone already suffice. If Rabbi Eliezer agreed with that principle, he would have no need to invoke a practice *shechitah*. The very fact that the *baraita* frames the case specifically as a *shechitah*-for-practice suggests that the *shechitah* is doing independent work, not merely serving as a vehicle for the *simanim* consideration.

### Debate Uncertainty

The Gemara accepts the point, and concludes: *ein hachi nami* — you are correct. According to Rabbi Eliezer, it may well be that his reason is the *simanim* alone, and the practice *shechitah* is mentioned only because Nimos takes issue with it. Nimos concedes that the *shechitah* blood is a *machshir* for other purposes but denies that it grants the raven food-status. Rabbi Eliezer simply holds — for reasons tied to the *simanim* — that the raven is food regardless.

The Gemara acknowledges that the debate between Nimos and Rabbi Eliezer may therefore not actually hinge on whether practice *shechitah* confers food-status. And if that is so, then the attempt to explain the *baraita*'s donkey-case as a debate about practice *shechitah* has no firm Tannaitic anchoring. The elaborate evasion collapses.

## Explicit Braita Proof

### Lo Ratza Braita

At this point the Gemara introduces a new *baraita* that speaks directly to the question: *lo ratza lishchot* — if the owner did not wish to redeem the firstborn donkey. The *baraita* describes a person who breaks the donkey's neck with a *kupitz* (a cleaver), buries it, and the ruling is that it is *asur behana'ah* — forbidden in benefit. This is the position of Rabbi Yehuda. And then: *Rabbi Shimon matir* — Rabbi Shimon permits it. The *baraita* also specifies that one may not break the neck in aberrant ways — not with a reed, a scythe, an axe, or a saw, and one may not lock the donkey in a room and let it die there — and that while alive the donkey may not be shorn or used for labor (*asur begiza u'va'avodah*). That too is Rabbi Yehuda, and again Rabbi Shimon permits.

### Shimon Permits

The initial reading of this *baraita* strongly suggests that the dispute concerns post-*arifah* status. The first clause describes breaking the neck and declares the carcass *asur behana'ah* (Rabbi Yehuda) or *mutar* (Rabbi Shimon). This appears to be explicit evidence that Rabbi Shimon permits deriving benefit from the broken-necked donkey.

### Evasion Attempt

The Gemara attempts an evasion: perhaps the opening clause means that the donkey is *asur behana'ah* while it is still alive, and Rabbi Shimon permits benefit while it is alive. But the *seifa*'s discussion — which explicitly addresses the living donkey's status (*asur begiza u'va'avodah*, forbidden from shearing and labor) — demonstrates that when the *seifa* speaks about the living donkey, it is clearly doing so as a separate matter. If the *seifa* addresses the living donkey, then the *reisha*'s discussion of breaking the neck and burial must be addressing the post-*arifah* state. The evasion fails.

### Two Benefit Types

The Gemara now offers a different resolution. Perhaps both the *reisha* and the *seifa* address the *living* donkey, and both are needed because they address two distinct types of benefit. The *reisha* addresses *hana'at damim* — monetary benefit, i.e., selling or renting the donkey. The *seifa* addresses *hana'at gufo* — direct physical benefit, i.e., using the donkey for labor or shearing it. These are qualitatively different: indirect monetary benefit is less direct than personal physical use, and one might have expected the *halachah* to distinguish between them.

### Dual Benefit Teaching

On this reading, Rabbi Shimon permits both types of benefit from the living *peter chamor*, and both needed to be stated explicitly. Had only monetary benefit been mentioned, one might have thought Rabbi Shimon permits only that — perhaps because indirect benefit is a more lenient category — but that he agrees with Rabbi Yehuda regarding direct physical use. Had only direct benefit been stated, one might have thought Rabbi Yehuda concedes monetary benefit. *Tzricha* — both are needed. The practical conclusion, on this reading, is that Rabbi Shimon does not take a position on the broken-necked donkey's post-*arifah* status; both clauses are about the living animal.

What emerges from the survey of Tannaitic sources is a genuine tension. The original *baraita* — listing the broken-necked donkey among items that are not food because they are *asur behana'ah* — points toward Rabbi Shimon conceding forbidden status after *arifah*. The *lo ratza* *baraita* seems to point the other direction, with Rabbi Shimon explicitly permitting benefit. Each source has been subjected to reinterpretation; neither reading is without strain. The stage is set for the *Amoraim* to stake out their positions.

## Amoraic Positions

### Nachman: Admits

Rav Nachman, in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha, states the position clearly: *modeh Rabbi Shimon le'achar arifah shehu assur* — Rabbi Shimon concedes that after the neck is broken the donkey is forbidden in benefit. Rava is associated with this same view.

### Gezerah Shavah Proof

Rav Nachman offers a proof from a *baraita*: the Torah uses the word *arifah* in connection with the *peter chamor* and also uses the word *arifah* in connection with the *eglah arufah*. By *gezerah shavah* — verbal analogy between the two usages — just as the *eglah arufah* is forbidden in benefit after its neck is broken, so too the *peter chamor* is forbidden in benefit after its neck is broken. Critically, Rav Nachman argues that this *baraita* must be representing Rabbi Shimon's position — because for Rabbi Yehuda the point is trivially obvious. Rabbi Yehuda holds the donkey was *asur behana'ah* from before the *arifah*, so naturally it remains forbidden afterward; no verse would be needed to teach that. Only Rabbi Shimon, who held the donkey permissible while alive, requires a verse to establish that the *arifah* changes the status.

### Sheshet Counter

Rav Sheshet Safra challenges this proof. He reports that Rav Nachman's colleagues have already explained that the *baraita* can indeed be Rabbi Yehuda, and that the *gezerah shavah* is needed for a non-trivial purpose even within Rabbi Yehuda's framework. One might have thought that *arifah* functions as a substitute for *pidyon* (redemption): just as redeeming the donkey with a sheep removes its forbidden status, so too breaking the neck — the alternative to redemption — might also remove the forbidden status. The verse therefore comes to teach that *mah pidyah materet* — just as redemption removes the prohibition — *af arifah* does not: breaking the neck does not release the donkey from its forbidden status; it remains *asur behana'ah*. This is a necessary teaching even within Rabbi Yehuda's view. Thus the *baraita* need not be Rabbi Shimon.

### Punitive Loss Proof

Rav Nachman turns to a second proof, drawn from another *baraita* — a teaching of Levi: *hu hifsid mamono shel kohen lefikach yufsad mamono* — he caused the *kohen* to lose money (by refusing to redeem and give a sheep), therefore his own money shall be lost. The underlying assumption is that breaking the neck causes the owner a real monetary loss, which Rav Nachman takes to mean the donkey becomes *asur behana'ah* and thus entirely worthless. Furthermore, the statement "his money is lost" makes sense only if, before the *arifah*, the owner could have derived benefit — i.e., the donkey was *mutar behana'ah*. This points to Rabbi Shimon, who held the living donkey permissible; and the statement that the owner now loses proves Rabbi Shimon must concede that post-*arifah* it is forbidden.

### Loss Explanation

The Gemara rejects this proof on two grounds, each of which works independently. First, the loss can be explained according to Rabbi Yehuda: even though the donkey was *asur behana'ah* before the *arifah*, while it was alive the owner could still derive a benefit of a kind — he could redeem it with a sheep of lesser value and retain the difference. A donkey worth one hundred could be redeemed with a fifty-shekel sheep; the owner thus preserved fifty for himself. Once the donkey is killed, that differential benefit is lost. The "loss" described by Levi is therefore the loss of the redemption-differential, not the loss of direct benefit-permissibility.

Second, even if we read the passage as Rabbi Shimon — and even if we hold that after *arifah* the donkey remains *mutar behana'ah* — there is still an obvious loss: *p'chat mita*, the depreciation of death. A living donkey is worth more than a dead one. Even if the carcass is fully permitted in benefit, the owner has lost value simply by reason of the animal's death. No appeal to *asur behana'ah* is necessary to explain the loss.

### Yochanan: Both Permitted

Rabbi Yochanan — and some say it was Rabbi Elazar — takes the opposing position: *adayin hi machloket* — the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon extends to both the living and the dead donkey. Rabbi Shimon holds the *peter chamor* permissible in benefit at all stages: before *arifah* and after *arifah*. The debate does not resolve upon the neck being broken.

### Marriage Mishnah

Some *Amoraim* report that Rav Nachman's statement — that Rabbi Shimon concedes forbidden status after *arifah* — was made not in connection with the *baraita* about the broken-necked donkey, but rather in connection with the following *Mishnah*: *hamekadesh b'peter chamor einah mekudeshet* — if a man betroths a woman using a *peter chamor* as the object of betrothal, the betrothal is invalid. The invalidity presupposes that the donkey is *asur behana'ah*. This seems inconsistent with Rabbi Shimon's position that the living donkey is *mutar behana'ah*. Rav Nachman's resolution is: the *Mishnah* is discussing the donkey after *arifah*, and in that case Rabbi Shimon concedes it is *asur behana'ah*. Hence the betrothal is invalid even according to Rabbi Shimon — not because of a dispute, but *divrei hakol*, universally.

## Marriage Mishnah Detail

### Mishnah Problem

A new difficulty emerges. Even granting Rav Nachman's post-*arifah* reading, the *Mishnah*'s ruling that betrothal with a *peter chamor* is invalid is hard to explain under any position. For Rabbi Shimon, a living *peter chamor* is *mutar behana'ah* and has full monetary value, so a betrothal using it should work. For Rabbi Yehuda, the donkey is *asur behana'ah* even while alive — but even then, it has *some* value. As long as the donkey is alive and unredeemed, the owner can redeem it with a sheep of lesser value and retain the difference. That differential — the gap between the donkey's value and the minimum acceptable sheep — constitutes real monetary benefit. Why then does the *Mishnah* declare the betrothal categorically invalid? A betrothal requires an object of at least minimal monetary value (*shaveh perutah*); that differential, however small, ought to suffice.

### Shekel Minimum

The Gemara resolves this by narrowing the case. The *Mishnah* is discussing a donkey that is worth no more than a *shekel*. Under ordinary circumstances, one could still redeem even this donkey with a sheep worth less than a *shekel*, thereby retaining a small differential. But the resolution depends on a particular ruling about the minimum value of the redemptive sheep.

### Yosi's Minimum

The Gemara cites a *baraita* in which the word *tifdeh* ("you shall redeem") is written twice in the relevant passages, and the two occurrences are expounded. The first teaching is that redemption may be performed immediately, without waiting for the donkey to reach thirty days of age (unlike the firstborn son, where a thirty-day waiting period applies). The second is that redemption may be performed with a sheep of any value, however minimal — *kol shehu*.

Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, however, disagrees: *ein pidyah p'chuta mi'shekel* — the redemptive sheep must be worth at least a *shekel*; there is a minimum. Accordingly, if the donkey itself is worth exactly a *shekel*, and the sheep must be worth at least a *shekel*, there is no longer any differential benefit to the owner. A donkey worth one *shekel* redeemed by a sheep worth a minimum of one *shekel* yields no retained value for the owner. The donkey has, in effect, zero net benefit.

The *Mishnah*'s ruling that betrothal with such a donkey is invalid follows: we are dealing with a donkey whose value is at or below the *shekel* minimum, and the ruling follows Rabbi Yosi b'Rabbi Yehuda, who sets that floor on the redemption value. Rabbi Yehuda's position is thereby vindicated as the basis of the *Mishnah*.

## Unresolved Debate

### Core Question

The daf closes without resolution. The bottom line is a genuine and unresolved dispute among the *Amoraim* about Rabbi Shimon's position once the neck of the *peter chamor* has been broken.

While alive, the positions are clear: Rabbi Yehuda holds the *peter chamor* is *asur behana'ah*; Rabbi Shimon holds it is *mutar behana'ah*. Once the neck is broken, however, the question fractures. Rav Nachman and Rava hold that Rabbi Shimon concedes forbidden status after *arifah* — the *arifah* effects a change analogous to the *eglah arufah*, rendering the animal *asur behana'ah* universally. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar hold that the dispute continues even after the neck is broken: Rabbi Shimon maintains his permissive position at every stage.

Neither the Tannaitic nor the Amoraic material succeeds in delivering a definitive proof. The *baraita* listing the broken-necked donkey among items *asur behana'ah* points toward Rabbi Shimon conceding — but the Gemara offered ways to read around it. The *lo ratza* *baraita* appears to have Rabbi Shimon explicitly permitting benefit after the neck is broken — but that too was reinterpreted as addressing the living animal. The punitive-loss argument and the *gezerah shavah* both fell to counter-explanations. The question — whether for Rabbi Shimon the *arifah* is a transformative act that finalizes the animal's forbidden status, or merely a ritual that carries no such consequence — remains *teku*.
