# Shabbat 126 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Perek Intro & Tircha

### Perek Overview

Today's *daf* is 127, and we begin a new *perek*. The previous *perek* dealt with *muktzeh*, but specifically in the context of *kelim* — which, as we learned, is not classic *muktzeh* in the fullest sense. A *kli* is by definition something usable, not something pushed away and set aside, and so while there is a special *gezeirah* limiting the use of *kelim she-melachtan l'issur*, that restriction is in fact quite narrow. This new *perek* continues the *muktzeh* theme while introducing a distinct additional concern: the opening discussion involves clearing out a granary or moving grain in order to make space for guests or for a *shiur*. In this way, the *perek* introduces the important concept of *tircha* on *Shabbat*.

### Tircha Concept

Many people carry a sense that *tircha*, like *uvda d'chol*, is not a fully formalized *halacha* — not a genuine *issur d'rabbanan*, let alone an *issur d'oraita*. Nevertheless, *tircha* is central to the experiential dimension of *Shabbat*. According to some, this experiential dimension is precisely the content of the *mitzvas aseh* of *shabbaton* — that positive obligation to preserve the character and atmosphere of *Shabbat* beyond the formal prohibition of *melacha*. However one frames it, *Chazal* had much to say about avoiding unnecessary effort or exertion on *Shabbat* even when no *melacha* is involved. The practical question is something like: if you have a storage room filled with boxes and someone needs a place to sleep, can you undertake a major clearing operation on *Shabbat* even without any *melacha*? That is one of the central concerns the *perek* addresses.

### Rav Chisda vs Shmuel

Of course, as we clear the space, the question of *muktzeh* re-enters, because the type of grain one must move determines whether it is *muktzeh* at all. The Gemara's discussion opened with a debate between Rav Chisda and Shmuel. The *Mishnah* states that one may move four or five boxes of grain. Rav Chisda reads this precisely: four boxes under certain circumstances where the space is small and one cannot fully empty it, five in larger situations. Shmuel, by contrast, reads "four or five" as idiomatic — a way of saying a great deal — and holds that there is in fact no fixed limit. Both agree, however, that one may not completely empty the space, because once the floor that was covered is fully exposed, one may come to smooth it out — which would constitute a prohibited *melacha*.

### Granary Muktzeh

To reorient ourselves, the Gemara asks: *u'mai aval lo et ha-otzar* — even according to Shmuel, who permits removing large quantities of grain, what does it mean that one may not remove "the granary"? The answer is *she-lo yigmor kulo* — one may not completely empty it, *dilma atei l'ashvuyei gumos* — lest one come to smooth the holes in the ground. On this Shmuel agrees. The point of disagreement is: *aval at'chulei mat'chil* — one may, according to Shmuel, begin emptying a granary on *Shabbat* even if one had never started before *Shabbat*. One can simply not finish it entirely. The Gemara then identifies whose position this reflects: *Rabbi Shimon hee d'leis lei muktzeh* — this is Rabbi Shimon, who does not hold of *muktzeh*.

A clarification is necessary here, because the statement that Rabbi Shimon does not hold of *muktzeh* is genuinely surprising. It cannot mean he rejects *muktzeh* entirely — the *Mishnah* itself states that *tevel* grain is *muktzeh*, and that grain or legumes that are not *ra'ui l'achila* are *muktzeh*. Even Rabbi Shimon holds of certain categories. What the Gemara means when it says *leis lei muktzeh* is that Rabbi Shimon rejects a larger range of *muktzeh* categories than others. He may not hold of *muktzeh machmas mi'us*, or *muktzeh machmas chisaron kis*, or he may define categories more narrowly than Rabbi Yehuda. The categorical statement *leis lei muktzeh* is an abstraction the Gemara uses — but it should not be read as a blanket rejection of the entire principle.

### Min HaMuchan Gap

What exactly is the *muktzeh* concern with granary grain? The grain is stored there precisely to be used eventually — so why would it be *muktzeh* at all? One way to approach this is through the conceptual gap between *muktzeh* in the strict sense — something actively set aside — and *min ha-muchan*, something that has been prepared and is in one's consciousness as a usable item for *Shabbat*. Most things in our ordinary lives fall somewhere between these poles; we assume that items we have not actively pushed away are implicitly *muchan*, on the theory that we would have expected to use them.

Granary grain, however, sits at a genuine border. On the one hand, it is there to be used and has not been *maktzeh*-ed in any active sense. On the other hand, it is the kind of thing one does not expect to use for perhaps a year — the kind of thing so stored away that it may not even implicitly be within the range of what one would anticipate using. A useful analogy: imagine one bought several cases of wine before *Pesach* and stored them in a closet, unopened. On *Shabbat* one needs wine. On one hand, those bottles were never pushed away — why would one not want to use them? On the other hand, they are so thoroughly stored that they may not even be in the implicit mental range of things one would reach for. That, it seems, is the conceptual terrain the Gemara is navigating with respect to granary grain and the debate about whether beginning to use it is permitted on *Shabbat*. Rabbi Shimon, holding of less *muktzeh*, would permit beginning to empty the granary; Rabbi Yehuda and others would not.

## Granary Rules

### Granary Paths

The Gemara cites a *baraita*: *ein matchilin be-otzar techila* — one may not begin using the granary from the outset on *Shabbat*. However, *aval ose bo shevil kedei she-yikanes ve-yetzei* — one may make a path through it in order to enter and exit. The Gemara immediately notes the apparent tension: if one cannot begin moving the grain, how is making a path permitted? The resolution is *ose bo shevil be-raglav* — the path is made simply by walking through, *bi-chnisato u-vi-ytziato*, as one enters and exits. One is not picking up the grain and actively moving it with one's hands. That kind of direct handling would be *tiltul b'gufo*; walking through and displacing grain with one's feet is closer to *tiltul min ha-tzad*.

### Tzvuah Tzvurah

A second *baraita* addresses a related case: *tzvuah tzvurah* — a mound or pile of grain. *Bi-zman she-hitchil bah me-erev Shabbat* — if one began using the pile before *Shabbat*, *mutar lehistapek mimena be-Shabbat* — one may continue drawing from it on *Shabbat*. *Ve-im lav* — but if one had not begun before *Shabbat*, *asur lehistapek mimena be-Shabbat*. This is attributed to Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Achai vs Shimon

The Gemara notes a problem: *kelapei laya* — this is backward. Rabbi Shimon is supposed to be the lenient voice regarding *muktzeh*, and yet here his position is the restrictive one? It says *Rabbi Achai matir* — Rabbi Achai is the one who permits. The Gemara resolves this by reversing the attribution: *ela eima* — exchange the names. It is Rabbi Achai who says it is *asur*, and *ve-Rabbi Shimon matir* — Rabbi Shimon permits. This restores the expected alignment: Rabbi Shimon is lenient regarding *muktzeh*.

It should be noted that because the Gemara abstracts Rabbi Shimon's position into the categorical formula *leis lei muktzeh*, it is committed to having Rabbi Shimon take the lenient position consistently. A more nuanced treatment might draw finer distinctions within or across categories of *muktzeh*, but once one adopts the categorical abstraction, Rabbi Shimon must always appear on the lenient side.

### Shiur Amounts

The Gemara asks: *tana, kama shiur tzvuah tzvurah* — what is the minimum quantity of piled grain that triggers the restriction, such that one may not begin using it on *Shabbat*? The answer given is a *letech* — half of a *kor*, which equals fifteen *se'ah*. This is a substantial volume. The implication is that below this threshold, the grain is not considered sufficiently "stored away" to raise the *muktzeh* concern, and even according to the stricter opinions one could move it on *Shabbat*. Notably, the volume of a *letech* corresponds to the combined volume of the five boxes mentioned in the *Mishnah* itself. That equivalence helps anchor the two discussions together.

Subsequently, Rav Nechumi bar Zachai asks Abaye the same question, and Abaye confirms: *shiur tevuah tzeburah, beletech* — the measure of piled grain is a *letech*, citing the same *baraita*.

### Trips vs Load

The Gemara returns to our *Mishnah*'s four or five boxes — all subsequent questions, it emphasizes, are according to Rav Chisda, who holds that the numbers are precise. The question is: *be'arba'ah vechamishah kupot in, tefi lo* — does the limit of four or five mean one must use exactly four or five boxes to carry that amount, i.e., that the number of trips is the binding constraint? The reasoning would be *mi'uta be-hilucha adif* — better to minimize the number of trips, even if each trip involves a greater load. Alternatively, *mi'uta be-mas'oi adif* — perhaps it is better to carry less per trip, even if that means making more trips.

The question has an obvious intuitive resonance: when we move things from room to room, the natural temptation is to maximize how much we carry each time rather than make multiple trips. Presumably on *Shabbat* one would want to limit the sense of labor — but does that mean limiting trips or limiting load?

### Bottle Sizes

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from two *baraitot* that appear contradictory. One *baraita* states: *mefanin afilu arba'ah vechamishah kupot shel kadei shemen veshel kadei yayin* — one may clear four or five boxes of oil and wine containers. A second *baraita* says: *be'asarah uvechamishah asar* — ten or fifteen. The natural reading would suggest a dispute about the limit, and one might say they are debating *mi'uta be-hilucha* versus *mi'uta be-mas'oi*. However, the Gemara rejects this: *dechulei alma mi'uta be-hilucha adif* — all agree that fewer trips is preferable. The difference between ten or fifteen and four or five does not refer to the number of boxes but to the number of bottles within the boxes. *Ha demishtaklei chada chada bekupah* — in one scenario each box holds one large flask, so four or five boxes carry four or five bottles. *Ve-ha demishtaklei tartei tartei* — in another, each box holds two medium-sized bottles, yielding ten bottles in five boxes. *Ve-ha demishtaklei tlata tlata* — in a third, each box holds three smaller bottles, yielding fifteen. And there is a reference to *dikurei de-harfanya* — the pitchers of a certain locale known for their smaller size. The bottom line: the number of trips is fixed at four or five; the number of individual containers varies with their size.

### Guests & Limits

The Gemara raises a further question, again according to Rav Chisda: *hani arba v'chamisha d'ka'amar* — when the *Mishnah* says four or five, is this an absolute limit even when *af al gav de'ika orchin tuva* — even when there are many guests? Or is it rather *hakol lefi ha'orchin* — everything proportional to the number of guests, such that more guests permits more? *Tosafot* notes that if more guests yields a higher limit, one must ask what the baseline is. Since the *Mishnah* gives no such baseline, *Tosafot* concludes that four or five boxes applies even for a single guest. Whether more guests multiplies this is precisely the question.

## Space Clearing

### Rabbi Field Story

The Gemara cites the following teaching: *d'amar Rabbah amar Rebbe Chiya* — Rabbah says in the name of Rebbe Chiya: once Rebbe went to a certain place on *Shabbat* and saw that the students were crowded into a small space. He went out to the field and found it filled with *omarim* — bundles of grain. *V'pina Rebbe kol hasadeh kulo* — and Rebbe cleared the entire field of these bundles so the students could sit there.

This is striking on multiple levels. One would expect sheaves of grain standing in a field to carry a stronger *muktzeh* status than grain that has already been brought into a granary — it is in a more primary, unprocessed state. Moreover, the clearing took place outdoors, presumably in a *karmelit*, raising a question of carrying. Rashi addresses this by explaining that Rebbe moved each bundle *pachot pachot me-arba amot* — less than four *amot* at a time, each increment falling within the permitted range in a *karmelit*. One could also ask whether *omarim* in this state are genuinely *ra'ui* for use, given that they have not yet been threshed or winnowed — though if the primary use is animal fodder, they may be considered fit even in this state.

### Individual vs Group

What the Gemara actually introduces this story to resolve is the earlier question: *hakol lefi ha'orchin* — can one move proportionally more for more guests? Since Rebbe (or Rebbe Chiya, per the alternate version attributed to Rav Yosef in the name of Rav Yehoshua) cleared the *entire* field — not a measured four or five boxes — it is evident that *hakol lefi ha'orchin*. Even according to Rav Chisda, a larger group of guests or students creates proportionally greater latitude. *Shma mina* — this can be derived from here.

However, the Gemara notes: *adati iba'i lehu* — there remains a further unresolved question. *Chad gavra mashni l'kulehu o dilma gavra gavra mashni l'nafshei* — can one person clear the entire space on behalf of all the guests, or must each guest clear his own individual portion? The underlying concern is whether the license to exert effort on *Shabbat* is individual and non-transferable.

### Rabbi's Intent

The Gemara attempts to resolve this from the same story: *ta shma v'pina Rebbe* — it says Rebbe cleared it. This implies one person did it for everyone. But the Gemara immediately questions this: *Rebbe b'nafshei pina?* — Do you really think Rebbe, the *Nasi* of all Jewry, personally cleared the field himself, with no one helping?

### Pharaoh Analogy

The Gemara resolves: *ela tziva v'pina* — rather, it means he commanded and it was done. The parallel is to *Paroh*, about whom the verse says *va-ye'esor et richvo* — and he harnessed his chariot — and Rashi explains *sina mekaltelet et ha-shura*, that hatred disrupts the normal order, suggesting that Pharaoh perhaps did this himself in his agitation. But the deeper point is that when a verse says a king "did" something, it means he commanded it and it was carried out. When Bush invaded Iraq, one would not say he personally wielded a rifle. So too here: Rebbe ordered it done, and it was done. The conclusion is therefore *aval kol chad v'chad mafnin etzlo* — but in reality each individual cleared space for himself. The story cannot prove that one person may clear for all.

*Tosafot* adds a brief but pointed comment: *perush kol hasadeh kulah* — the phrasing "the entire field" implies that even if we might have accepted the idea that Rebbe would personally do some of the clearing, no one would imagine that the *Nasi* of Israel personally cleared an entire field. The very scale of the operation implies it must have been done by command.

## Hachnasat Orchim

### Greatness of Guests

Since the preceding discussion involved making space for students and guests, the Gemara takes the opportunity to elaborate on *hachnasat orchim*. *Masechet Shabbat* has a habit of digressing into *aggadata* when a suitable opening presents itself, and this is one such moment.

*Amar Rabbi Yochanan: gedola hachnasat orchin k'hashkamat beit hamidrash* — Rabbi Yochanan says: welcoming guests is as important as rising early to invest oneself in *Torah* study. The basis for the comparison is the text of the ruling itself: one may clear space for guests and one may clear space to prevent people from being unable to study — and the two are placed alongside one another as equally weighty concerns. It is notable that the comparison is to *hashkamat beit hamidrash* — one's personal effort in learning — rather than to enabling the learning of others or to some broader communal benefit.

### Guests vs Shechina

Rav Dimi of Nehardea goes further: *yatir me-hashkamat beit hamidrash* — welcoming guests is more important than extraordinary effort in learning. His reading of the same ruling is that guests are listed first, and concern for disrupted study is listed second — and order implies priority.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: gedola hachnasat orchin me-hakbalat pnei ha-shchina* — greater is welcoming guests than receiving the Divine Presence. The prooftext is from the story of Abraham's visitors: *va-yomer* — and Abraham said. The word that follows, *Alef-Dalet-Nun-Yud*, is ambiguous: it can be read as an address to the visiting men (*adoni* — "my masters") or as God's name (*Adonai*). The Gemara reads it as *kodesh* — a sacred usage — meaning Abraham was addressing God. The verse *im na matzati chen b'eynecha al na ta'avor me'al avdecha* — if I have found favor in Your eyes, do not pass from over Your servant — is read as Abraham asking God to wait while he attends to his guests.

Rabbi Elazar adds a related teaching: *bo u-re'eh she-lo k'middat ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu middat basar v'dam* — come and see that God's way is not the way of human beings. In human affairs, one of lesser status cannot say to one of greater status: wait until I come to you. Yet Abraham was able to say exactly that to God. This formulation is interesting because it shifts the emphasis somewhat: rather than exalting *hachnasat orchim* per se, it emphasizes God's willingness to set aside, as it were, His own honor — to be *mochal* on His *kavod* — in order that Abraham could fulfill his obligation.

### Peah Mishnah

Rav Yehuda bar Shila, citing Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan, teaches: *shisha devarim adam ochel piroseihem ba-olam hazeh ve-ha-keren kayemet lo le-olam ha-ba* — six things: a person eats their fruits in this world, while the principal reward remains for him in the world to come. These six are: *hachnasat orchim*, *bikur cholim*, *iyun tefillah*, *hashkamat beit hamidrash*, *ha-migadel banav le-talmud Torah*, and *ve-ha-dan chavero le-chaf zechus* — judging one's fellow toward the scale of merit.

The Gemara then raises an objection: *v'ha'anan tnan* — we have the famous *Mishnah* from *Peah*, recited in daily *davening*, that presents a comparable but different list: *kibbud av va-em*, *gemilut chasadim*, *ve-hava'at shalom bein adam le-chavero*, and *talmud Torah ke-neged kulam*. Are these the only such things? The two lists do not match.

### List Reconciliation

The Gemara resolves: *hani nami be-hani shaychi* — the first list is subsumed within the second. Rabbi Yochanan's six items are an elaboration of the *Mishnah* in *Peah*. Specifically: *hachnasat orchim* and *bikur cholim* fall under *gemilut chasadim* — acts of kindness. *Hashkamat beit hamidrash* and *ha-migadel banav le-talmud Torah* clearly fall under *talmud Torah ke-neged kulam*. *Ve-ha-dan chavero le-chaf zechus* falls under *hava'at shalom bein adam le-chavero* — Rashi explains that when one consistently assumes the better of other people, one cultivates a far more generous way of engaging with the world, which in turn encourages reciprocity and creates a much greater measure of peace between people than a world in which everyone assumes the worst.

The harder case is *iyun tefillah*. Rashi makes something of a stretch in placing it under *gemilut chasadim*, suggesting that *tefillah* — which involves acknowledging one's dependence on God and one's gratitude for what one has received — is itself a form of recognizing and acknowledging *chesed*. It is an acknowledgment of having received kindness, and as such fits within the broader framework of *gemilut chasadim*. It is admittedly the less intuitive inclusion, but the logic is discernible.

One might also ask how this fits with the closing line of the *Peah Mishnah*: *talmud Torah ke-neged kulam*. If *talmud Torah* outweighs all else, how can *hachnasat orchim* be compared to *hashkamat beit hamidrash*, let alone said to exceed it? The resolution may lie in the distinction between *hashkamat beit hamidrash* and *talmud Torah* itself: *hashkamat beit hamidrash* refers to the effort and dedication involved in showing up, in making the commitment to learning even when it is difficult — what the Gemara elsewhere calls *schar halicha*. This is not identical to the substance of *talmud Torah ke-neged kulam*, which captures the comprehensive weight of Torah study as such. The comparison of *hachnasat orchim* to *hashkamat beit hamidrash* is therefore a comparison to a specific dimension of the *talmud Torah* complex, not a claim that *hachnasat orchim* outweighs *talmud Torah* in its fullest sense.

### Practical Rewards

The formulation *ochel piroseihem ba-olam hazeh ve-ha-keren kayemet le-olam ha-ba* deserves close attention. The *ve-ha-keren kayemet le-olam ha-ba* — the principal remains in the world to come — is straightforwardly metaphysical: one performs these deeds and is rewarded in the next world. But *ochel piroseihem ba-olam hazeh* — eating their fruits in this world — need not be understood in a narrowly metaphysical sense, as if God arranges some special payment in this life. *Tosafot* raises the question of *schar mitzvah be-hai alma ika o leka* — whether one can genuinely receive reward for *mitzvot* in this world at all — a question with significant consequences, since it was partly the observation that righteous people seem not to be rewarded in this world that led Elisha ben Abuya to apostasy. The Gemara's general approach is *schar mitzvah be-hai alma leika* — do not expect the reward in this world.

But *ochel piroseihem* may simply describe the natural, practical benefits that flow from these actions — the kind of world they create when people live by them. Consider *kibbud av va-em*: the Torah promises *lema'an ya'arichun yamecha* — that your days will be lengthened. This could refer to *olam ha-ba*. But it is also true in a very practical sense that a society in which people care for their elderly parents is one in which people live longer and more dignified lives. This echoes the Rambam's insight in *Moreh Nevuchim*, where he explains the rationale for *gemilut chasadim*: the word *gomel* — which means to bestow — also carries the sense of repayment. The world operates in cycles: today one person has means and another is in need; tomorrow the situation may reverse. A society built on mutual aid and *chesed* is one in which everyone benefits, not because God arranges tit-for-tat rewards but because such a society is simply a better one in which to live. *Hava'at shalom bein adam le-chavero* is self-evident on these terms. And *talmud Torah*, like *iyun tefillah*, shapes one's entire orientation toward the world, infusing everyday life with an awareness of what God wants and how His presence manifests — a benefit that is real and practical even before considering any eschatological reward.

## Dan L'Kaf Zechut

### Worker Payment

The Gemara returns to *dan chavero le-chaf zechus* with a series of illustrative stories. *Tanu Rabbanan: ha-dan chaveiro le-chaf zechus, danin oto le-chaf zechus* — one who judges one's fellow favorably will himself be judged favorably. The Gemara is careful not to read this only as a promise of divine reward, though that dimension is present. There is also a realistic, practical dimension: if one habitually assumes the best of others, it becomes very difficult for others to think poorly of him, and it cultivates a generosity of spirit that tends to be reciprocated. The worst human experiences include being suspected of wrongdoing when one is entirely innocent — a suffering the Gemara elsewhere acknowledges by saying one gains great reward for bearing it. That very fact highlights how terrible false suspicion is. A culture in which people assume the best of one another is simply a better world to inhabit.

The first story: a man descended from the upper Galilee and hired himself out to a certain homeowner in the south for three years. On *erev Yom Kippur* (some say *erev ha-regel*), he asked for his wages so he could go home and provide for his wife and children. The employer responded with a succession of *ein li* — I have nothing: no money, no fruit, no land, no animals, no bedding. Each denial seemed plainly false. The worker put his belongings on his back and walked home in despondent silence.

### Employer's Reasoning

After the holiday, the employer came to the worker's home with the full three years' wages — a substantial sum — along with three donkeys laden with food, drink, and delicacies. After they ate and drank together, the employer presented the wages and then asked: *b'mah chashadtani* — what did you suspect me of, each time I said I had nothing?

The worker gave his answers. When you said you had no money: *shema perakmatya b'zol nizdamna lach* — perhaps a great business opportunity presented itself and you invested all available funds. When you said you had no animals: *shema muskarot b'yad acherim* — perhaps they were all rented out. When you said you had no land: *shema arisut b'yad acherim* — perhaps others are sharecroppers on it and you could not convey clean title. When you said you had no fruit: *shema ein m'usarot* — perhaps they had not yet been tithed. When you said you had no bedding: *shema hikdish kol nechasav le-shamayim* — perhaps you had consecrated all your possessions to Heaven.

### Mutual Judgment

The employer responded: *ha'avoda, kach haya* — by the Temple service, that is exactly what happened. *Hadarti kol nechasai bishvil Horkenos b'ni* — I took a vow dedicating all my possessions because of my son Horkenos, who would not study *Torah*, and I did not want him to inherit my property. When I came to my colleagues in the south, *heitiru li kol nedarai* — they annulled my vows, and that is why I am now able to pay you. *U-cheshem she-dantani le-zchus, ha-Makom yadincha le-zchus* — just as you judged me favorably, may God judge you favorably.

The final line raises a question: what does it mean for God to judge one favorably? God knows the truth of one's motivations — He does not need to assume the best. One possibility is that this is simply a blessing by parallel: as you did, so may be done to you. But there is a deeper reading. Very few of our motivations are pure black and white. We act for multiple reasons simultaneously, some more noble and some less. God, in judging us, can choose which aspect of our motivation to emphasize, which framing to adopt. To be judged *le-chaf zechus* by God may mean that God chooses to interpret our actions through the lens of the more positive intentions that informed them, even where less noble impulses were also present. By the same logic, *dan le-chaf zechus* toward another person means choosing to interpret their behavior through its most favorable possible context — recognizing that behind most actions there is a complex web of reasons, and consciously emphasizing the better ones.

### Hasid & Woman

The second story: *ma'aseh be-chasid echad she-padah riva achas bat Yisrael* — a certain pious man redeemed a young Jewish woman from captivity. When they reached an inn during the journey, *u-lemalon hishkivah tachas margelosav* — he had her sleep in the same room, at the foot of his bed. *U-lemachar yarad ve-taval* — the next morning he went to the *mikvah* and then gave a *shiur* to his students. The need to immerse in the morning after being in the same room as the woman carried obvious implications.

### Students' Charity

Before teaching, the *chasid* asked his students: *b'sha'ah she-hishkavti tachas margelosai, b'me chashadtuni* — when I had her sleep at my feet, what did you suspect me of? They answered: *shema yesh bo talmid she-eino baduk le-rebbi* — perhaps there is a student among us whose character you have not yet fully ascertained, and therefore you did not trust her to be outside your room. Even in framing a negative possibility about one of themselves, the students expressed it as a matter of incomplete knowledge rather than established bad character. Then: *b'sha'ah she-yaradti ve-tavalti, b'me chashadtuni* — when I went to the *mikvah*, what did you suspect? *Shema mipnei torach ha-derech ira keri le-rebbi* — perhaps the physical exertion of the journey caused a seminal emission, entirely unrelated to anything involving the woman. The *chasid* confirmed: *ha'avoda, kach haya* — that is exactly what happened. And *keshem she-dantuni le-zchus, ha-Makom yadin eschem le-zchus* — as you judged me favorably, may God judge you favorably.

### R. Yehoshua Story

The third story: *pa'am achat hutzrach davar echad le-talmidei chachamim etzel matronita achas she-kol gedolei Romi metzuyin etzla* — once the Torah scholars needed a certain favor from a Roman matron whose home was frequented by all the great men of Rome, with implications that reflect on her character. They asked: *mi yelech* — who should go? Rabbi Yehoshua volunteered and went with a student. Upon arriving, *chalatz tfillov be-richuk arba amot* — he removed his *tefillin* four *amot* before her threshold, entered, and *na'al ha-delet bifneihem* — locked the door in front of his student. Upon emerging, he went to the *mikvah* and taught.

### Students Interpret

Then, as in the previous story, Rabbi Yehoshua asked his student: *b'sha'ah she-chalatzi tfilli, b'me chashadtuni* — when I took off my *tefillin*, what did you suspect? *Kasavar Rabbi lo yikanesu divrei kedusha be-makom tum'ah* — we assumed you held that matters of holiness should not enter a place of impurity, given that *Chazal* attributed a form of ritual impurity to non-Jews. When he locked the door: *shema davar malchus yesh beino le-veina* — perhaps there was sensitive governmental business to discuss privately. When he immersed: *shema nitza tzinora mipiha al begadav shel Rabbi* — perhaps some of her saliva had landed on his clothing, since *Chazal* attributed to non-Jews an impurity analogous to that of a *zav*. Rabbi Yehoshua confirmed: *ha'avoda, kach haya* — that is exactly how it was. And again: *atem, keshem she-dantuni le-zchus, ha-Makom yadin eschem le-zchus*.

These three stories escalate in the extremity of their circumstances. The point driven home is that one can never know the full context behind another's actions, and one should always strain to find the most favorable explanation possible. Achieving this standard in extreme cases is admittedly difficult, but habituating oneself to it in easier cases is the practice that makes it possible.

One important point deserves mention. The stories prompt a question that was raised in the room: why did these figures — the *chasid*, Rabbi Yehoshua — not act more transparently, so as to forestall suspicion? Michael is correct that *Chazal* elsewhere invoke the principle *ve-heyitem nekiyim me-Hashem u-mi-Yisrael* — one must be above suspicion before both God and Israel. There is a genuine obligation not only to act rightly but to act in ways that minimize the likelihood of false suspicion. One might suggest the figures in these stories were deliberately constructing a teaching situation for their students. But that response is incomplete. The more important lesson may be the asymmetry in how readily we invoke *mara'it ayin* in ritual matters versus in interpersonal ones. The sages were vigilant about appearances in the former domain, and *ve-heyitem nekiyim* functions as the parallel principle in the domain of money and interpersonal ethics. The cases here — involving money, in the first story, and sexual propriety in the others — are precisely the areas of greatest human temptation, and the principle of being above suspicion applies with full force. These questions deserve further investigation.

## Muktzeh Conclusions

### Trumah Tahorah

The Gemara returns to the *muktzeh* discussion. *Mafnin trumah tehorah* — one may move pure *terumah* on *Shabbat*. The Gemara asks: is this not obvious? What is the *chidush*? The answer: *tzricha d'mancha b'yad Yisrael* — the novel case is where *terumah* is in the possession of a *Yisrael* rather than a *kohen*. One might have thought: *keivan d'la chazya lei, asur* — since the *Yisrael* cannot eat the *terumah* himself, it is *muktzeh* for him. The Gemara therefore teaches: *keivan d'chazya le-kohen shapir dami* — since it is usable by a *kohen*, it is not *muktzeh*. This connects to a broader principle: an item that is fit for use by some — even if not by the person holding it — is not *muktzeh*.

### Demai Use

The same logic applies to *demai* — produce purchased from an *am ha-aretz* whose *terumot u-ma'asrot* may not have been properly separated, and which therefore must be re-tithed rabbinically before consumption. One might think *demai* is *muktzeh* because the holder may not eat it as is. But the Gemara responds: *keivan d'ei bei mafkar lei le-nichsei ve-havei ani ve-chazya lei* — since one could theoretically annul ownership of all one's possessions, become poor, and then be permitted to eat *demai* as a poor person, it is considered *chazya lei* — fit even for him, at least in principle, and therefore not *muktzeh*. Moreover — and this suffices independently — *de-tnan: ma'achilim et ha-aniyim dmai v'et ha-achsandya dmai* — as the *Mishnah* teaches, one may feed the poor *demai* and likewise army troops. Rashi explains why the poor were given this leniency: the poor must go door to door seeking *tzedakah* and cannot be selective about from whom they accept food, asking only from *chaverim* and not from *amei ha-aretz*. Since they receive from all, they receive *demai* as a matter of course, and the rabbis did not burden them with the obligation to separate tithes from it.

*Tosafot* notes the obvious point: one could simply have said, as with *terumah*, that since *demai* is fit for the poor, it is not *muktzeh* — no need for the theoretical argument about annulling one's possessions. The Gemara goes the extra mile to show that the item is in principle fit even for the holder himself.

### Beit Shammai Hillel

Finally, Rav Huna cites a *baraita*: *Beit Shammai* holds *ein ma'achilim et ha-aniyim dmai v'et ha-achsandya dmai* — one may not feed the poor or army troops *demai*. *Beit Hillel* holds the opposite: *ma'achilim et ha-aniyim dmai v'et ha-achsandya dmai* — one may. The full elaboration of these positions will continue in the subsequent discussion.
