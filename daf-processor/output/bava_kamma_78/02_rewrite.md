# Bava Kamma 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kilayim & Seh

### Hybrid Theft

Today's daf is 78, and we continue in the middle of a digression regarding whether the word *seh* in the Torah functions to exclude *kilayim* — a hybrid animal. The issue arose because of a practical question: if someone steals a hybrid sheep-goat, does he pay four and five upon slaughtering or selling it? The answer is that he does pay, because the verse uses the phrase *o seh* — the *vav* serving to include *kilayim* — whereas by *korbanot*, that same *o* functions to exclude *kilayim*. In fact, in the context of sacrifices, not only is a hybrid excluded, but even a *nidmeh* — an animal that does not resemble its parent — is excluded as well.

### Rava's Rule

With that established, the *gemara* turns to a general principle attributed to Rava: *kol makom she'ne'emar seh, eino ela l'hotzi et hakilayim* — wherever the Torah uses the word *seh*, it comes to exclude *kilayim*. This is formulated as a *binyan av*, a paradigm applicable across the Torah. The immediate difficulty, however, is that the cases we have seen do not seem to operate on a simple default; each requires an explicit textual signal pointing one way or the other. So the *gemara* presses: when, in practice, is Rava's principle ever independently relevant?

### Is It Relevant?

The *gemara* poses the question directly: *l'mai hilchata?* — for which *halacha* does Rava's general rule actually do any work? The subsequent discussion surveys several candidate areas and shows that in each one the exclusion of *kilayim* is already derivable from a different source entirely, rendering Rava's *drasha* from the word *seh* redundant.

### Kodashim & Maaser

The first candidate is *kodashim* — the category of sacrificial animals. That *kilayim* cannot be brought as a *korban* is already established through the verse *shor o chesev*: the *o* in that context is a *p'rat*, functioning to exclude hybrids. So the *halacha* is true, but it does not derive from the word *seh*. The next candidate is *maaser behemah* — the obligation to tithe one's newborn animals. Here, Rashi notes that the relevant verse does not even use the word *seh* but rather *tzon*. Regardless, *maaser* is excluded by a *gezeirah shavah* of *tachas tachas*, learning from *kodashim*: since *kilayim* is excluded from *korbanot*, it is equally excluded from *maaser*. Notably, the underlying logic is coherent — *maaser* is ultimately brought as a *korban*, and if *kilayim* is unfit for a *korban*, it is likewise unfit for *maaser*. But again, Rava's principle contributes nothing.

### Bechor Case

The third candidate is *bechor* — the firstborn animal, which, if unblemished, is brought as a *korban*. Here too, *kilayim* is excluded independently, through a *gezeirah shavah* of *havara havara*, learning from *maaser*. The verse by *maaser* says *kol asher yaavor tachas hashevet*, and the verse by *bechor* uses *veha'avarta kol peter rechem* — the parallel term establishes the link. Moreover, even *nidmeh* — an animal born of two parents of the same species but that physically resembles a different species — is excluded from *bechor* by a separate *drasha*: *ach bechor shor* implies *ad she'yehei shor u'vechoro shor*, that the firstborn must resemble the father. The *gemara* therefore notes: *kilayim miba'ya* — if even a *nidmeh* is excluded, it is self-evident that *kilayim* is excluded as well. The law is correct in all these cases, but it is never derived from the word *seh*.

### Peter Chamor

The *gemara* concludes that Rava's principle finds its essential application with the *peter chamor* — the firstborn donkey. The relevant obligation is *pidyon*, redemption: *peter chamor tifdeh b'seh*, the firstborn donkey must be redeemed by means of a *seh*. This is precisely where the word *seh* does the excluding work, and no other source independently excludes *kilayim* from this context.

### Pidyon Rules

A *mishnah* delineates the rules of *pidyon peter chamor*: one cannot redeem with a calf, a wild animal, a slaughtered sheep, a *treifah*, a *kilayim* hybrid, or a *koy* — an animal whose precise species classification is uncertain, traditionally understood as a hybrid between a goat and a deer. It is the word *seh* in *peter chamor tifdeh b'seh* that excludes the *kilayim*, and this is the one case where Rava's *binyan av* is independently necessary.

### R. Eliezer

However, a complication immediately arises. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *kilayim* may in fact be used for *pidyon peter chamor* — *mipnei shehu seh*, because a sheep-goat hybrid still falls generically within the category of *seh*. If Rabbi Eliezer permits this, then the word *seh* is not excluding *kilayim* even in the one case where Rava's principle seemed to apply. The *gemara* therefore asks: according to Rabbi Eliezer's position, where does Rava's rule matter at all?

## Parent Kashrut

### Mixed Parents

The *gemara* answers that for Rabbi Eliezer, Rava's principle applies in a different context entirely: *shenolad min ha'tahor ve'iburo min ha'tamei* — an animal born from a kosher mother but whose impregnation came from a non-kosher father. It is the word *seh* that tells us such an offspring is excluded from the relevant *halachot*. This is qualified as holding *udela ke'Rabbi Yehoshua* — and not following Rabbi Yehoshua, who derives the same conclusion from a different verse.

Rashi describes this scenario concretely: a cow that mated with a horse and gave birth to a horse-like animal, or a ewe that mated with a pig and bore a pig-like offspring. These cases raise an immediate conceptual question. When the problem with a sheep-goat hybrid is that it is a *kilayim* — not purely one species or another — the rationale is about pedigree or purity of breed. Both parent species are themselves kosher; the issue is the mixing. But here, the problem is categorically different: the offspring is not merely impure by virtue of mixed lineage, it has a non-kosher parent. One might have thought this is the more fundamental disqualification, entirely separate from the word *seh*.

The *gemara* is nonetheless saying that this is precisely where the word *seh* does its excluding work — it is the textual source for disqualifying an offspring of a kosher and a non-kosher parent. Rabbi Yehoshua, by contrast, does not rely on *seh* for this; he derives it from the phrase *seh ksavim v'seh izim*, which uses the plural form. The word *ksavim* implies both parents must be sheep, and *izim* implies both parents must be goats — meaning both parents must be kosher animals of the same species. The *gemara*, however, is not troubled by the fact that Rabbi Yehoshua derives the rule differently; one way or another, *seh* excludes *kilayim* — either from *peter chamor* or from an offspring with a non-kosher parent — and Rava's principle finds its application in one or the other context.

### Fused Hooves

The *gemara* then raises an objection: *v'tehorah mitamei'ah mi me'abra?* — can a kosher animal actually become pregnant from a non-kosher one? Biologically, a truly non-kosher animal and a truly kosher one could not produce viable offspring. The *gemara* answers: *d'kayma lan d'iyaber miklut k'Rabbi Shimon* — this is possible according to Rabbi Shimon's ruling involving an animal born with fused hooves.

Rabbi Shimon holds that if a cow and an ox — both kosher animals genetically — produce an offspring whose hooves happen to be fused, that animal is not kosher, because the verse describing the camel's non-kosher status (*et hagamal ki ma'aleh gerah hu u'farsah lo hifris*) comes to teach that even an animal of kosher genetic stock, if born with fused hooves resembling a camel, is non-kosher. Such an animal, however, is really just a cow with a physical anomaly, and it can in fact mate with other cows. An offspring from such a union would be the case of a kosher mother with a non-kosher father — non-kosher by reason of the fused hooves, even though genetically kosher. That offspring, too, would be disqualified, and it is from this context that the *drasha* on *seh* or on *ksavim* and *izim* derives its practical relevance.

### Cross-Mating

The *gemara* confirms that what is at work here is not genetic non-kashrut in the full sense, but a scenario where an animal that is halachically non-kosher — on account of its fused hooves — mates with one that is kosher. Their offspring is also not kosher, and it is either the word *seh* (according to Rava) or the plural *ksavim* and *izim* (according to Rabbi Yehoshua) that tells us this. With that, the *gemara* concludes the digression on *kilayim* and *seh*, and returns to the laws of *geneivah*.

## Kodashim Theft

### Ox vs. Lamb

With the *kilayim* discussion concluded, the *gemara* presents a remarkable question from Rava regarding the theft of sanctified animals. The background is the earlier principle — derived from Rabbi Shimon — that when one takes a *neder* of *hekdesh* and is *chayav b'achrayut*, responsible for replacement if the animal is lost or stolen, the animal is treated as belonging to the owner for purposes of *geneivah*. This is the concept of *davar ha'goreim l'mamon k'mamon dami* — something that causes a monetary obligation is treated as money itself — applicable not merely to the *kefel* but even to the *keren*, the principal.

Rava's question: *harei alai olah v'hifrish shor, ba acher v'ganavo, mi patar ganav nafshaih b'keves?* — if someone made a vow saying *harei alai olah*, without specifying the type of animal, and then, choosing to be generous, separated out an ox; and then a thief came and stole that ox — can the thief discharge his obligation by paying back only the value of a lamb? The question is about the *keren*: how much of the owner's property was actually taken? What is the legally mandated replacement value of what was lost?

The thief stole the ox, and the owner will in fact need to bring an *olah* in its place. But the owner is not halachically required to replace it with an ox. He could bring a lamb. So when we consider what the thief owes — what was the true value of the loss to the owner — is it the value of the ox that was dedicated, or only the value of the minimum animal the owner is legally obligated to provide?

The tension is captured in the *gemara*'s formulation: on one hand, *shem olah kabil ileih* — the owner accepted upon himself the obligation of an *olah*, and the thief is returning an animal that fulfills that *olah*; but on the other hand, *ana mitzvah muvcheret beina l'me'avad* — the owner's actions demonstrate that he wanted to perform the *mitzvah* in the most distinguished manner, and he would clearly replace the ox with another ox, so it is that fuller value which was truly lost.

### Vow Default

The debate turns on a *mishnah*: when someone says *harei alai olah* without specifying which animal, what is the default expectation? The *Rabbanan* say he must bring a lamb (*yavi keves*). Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah says he may fulfill his obligation with an *olat ha'of* — a bird offering, a turtledove or a dove. So even by the *Rabbanan*'s standard the minimum is a lamb, and Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's minimum is even lower.

### Intent Question

The question of the thief's obligation maps directly onto this dispute. If one accepts the *Rabbanan*'s position that the legal minimum *olah* is a lamb, then the replacement value of what was lost is the value of a lamb. If one accepts Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah's position, the thief could in principle discharge his obligation with the value of a bird offering. The further question — whether the owner's demonstrable intent to bring an ox shifts the calculus — is what gives the case its depth. His voluntary choice of an ox suggests he means to go beyond the minimum, and that aspiration would be frustrated by the theft.

### Rava's Answer

The *gemara* records that after raising this as a question, Rava subsequently resolved it himself: *ganav patar atzmo b'keves l'Rabbanan, b'olat ha'of l'Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah* — the thief discharges his obligation by returning the value of a lamb according to the *Rabbanan*, or the value of a bird offering according to Rabbi Elazar ben Azaryah. The owner's aspiration to bring a grander offering does not alter the legal calculus. The legally mandated replacement obligation defines the extent of the loss. Whatever the owner would have chosen to do beyond that minimum reflects a personal decision, not a legal consequence of the theft. The thief owes only what the law requires the owner to replace.

It is worth noting that according to Rabbi Shimon, if the vow had been *harei zo olah* — designating a specific animal rather than undertaking a personal obligation — the thief would owe nothing at all upon stealing it, since the owner suffers no replaceable loss. The entire *chiyuv* in our case exists only because the owner has an outstanding personal vow, and only to the extent of the minimum that vow requires.

## Sale Completeness

### Mishnah Cases

The *mishnah* at this point completes a series dealing with variations on stolen animals and what constitutes a valid *mechira* or *shechita* for purposes of four-and-five payment. Three scenarios are given in which one does not pay four and five: selling the animal with a reservation of one part in a hundred (*machar chutz me'echad mimei'ah shebo*); a case of *shutafut*, where the animal was jointly owned so the seller-thief had a partial ownership stake; and a case of defective slaughter — *ha'shohet venitnevlah b'yado*, where the *shechita* went badly and the animal became a *neveilah* in his hands; *ha'noher*, someone who killed the animal without performing *shechita* at all; and *ha'me'aker*, someone who tore out the trachea and esophagus rather than cutting them. In all the slaughter cases, since no valid *shechita* occurred, there is no trigger for four and five, though *kefel* remains owing. The *shutafut* case parallels the earlier case of a thief whose father died before the sale, making the thief a partial heir — since the animal was partly his at the time of the act, it was not entirely someone else's. The *mechira* cases are the more interesting ones and are the focus of the *gemara*'s analysis.

### Rav vs. Levi

The *gemara* asks: when the *mishnah* says one sold it while retaining one part in a hundred, which part matters? Is any retained fraction sufficient to break the *mechira*, or only a particular type of fraction?

Rav says: *chutz midavar ha'nitar imo b'shechita* — the reservation must be of something that becomes permitted through *shechita*. Only meat — something rendered edible by the *shechita* — counts as a meaningful retention. If one retains only the wool, the horn, or a tooth, that is not considered holding something back in the relevant sense, because the *shechita* has no bearing on those items. The framework is: the *mechira* of the stolen animal is measured through the lens of *shechita*, and so retaining something outside the scope of *shechita* does not constitute a partial sale.

Levi disagrees. For Levi, the measure of *mechira* is not filtered through *shechita* at all. If one retains the shearings (*gizoteha*), that is a genuine retention — wool is a substantial part of the animal's value, and selling the animal without its wool is simply not selling the whole thing.

A *braita* is cited in support of Levi: if one sold the animal while retaining the shearings, one does not pay four and five. The *braita* treats this as a self-evident case of incomplete sale.

### Rebbe Opinion

The *braita* also records a three-way dispute. The anonymous *tanna kamma* of the *braita* says that retaining a hand, a foot, a horn, or the shearings all equally constitute a partial retention — the *mechira* must encompass literally everything, and any shortfall disqualifies it. This aligns with Levi's position.

Rebbe (Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi) says: *davar hame'akev b'shechita* — only something that would actually prevent the *shechita* from being valid, if it were missing from the animal, constitutes a meaningful retention. He goes narrower than Rav. Rav requires that the retained portion be something rendered permitted by the *shechita*; Rebbe requires more — the retained portion must be something necessary for the *shechita* itself to be a valid *shechita*. If a specific organ or bone, were it missing, would render the animal a *treifah* and thereby invalidate the *shechita*, then retaining it constitutes a partial sale. But retaining a foot — since a missing foot does not invalidate *shechita* — does not break the *mechira*.

### R.S.b.E.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar takes a different approach entirely, one that does not invoke *shechita* at all. He holds that retaining a horn exempts from four and five, because a horn is something permanently attached to the animal — it is not normally removed, and therefore leaving it behind means one has not sold the whole animal. But retaining the shearings (*chutz migizoteha*) does not exempt, because animals are regularly shorn; their wool is not a fixed, permanent part of the animal. The shearings can come and go, and so they are not part of the definition of the "whole" animal.

### Four Views

The *gemara* maps these four positions onto underlying principles.

The *tanna kamma* of the *braita* holds that both *u'tevaho* and *u'macharo* — slaughter and sale — require the entirety of the animal with no reservation whatsoever. Whole means literally whole. Even retaining a single hair, on this view, would be sufficient to break the *mechira*.

Rebbe holds that *u'tevaho* and *u'macharo* are linked — the measure of the *mechira* mirrors the measure of the *tevichah*, and the *tevichah* is measured by what is necessary for it to be a valid *shechita*. If retaining a foot would not invalidate a *shechita* (since a missing foot does not make the animal a *treifah*), then selling without the foot is still a full sale.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, in the first version of his opinion, defines the whole animal not through *shechita* at all, but through what is permanently affixed: *karna d'lo l'migzaz kayma hava shiyur* — a horn, which is never shorn off, is a genuine retention. Shearings, which are normally removed, are not considered part of the permanent whole.

The *gemara* then resolves the apparent contradiction between the two versions of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar by establishing that these represent *terei tanna'ei aliba d'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — two different *tannaim* transmitting divergent versions of his position.

The second version of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, which corresponds to Rav's position, holds: *yada v'ragla d'tzarchei tavicha hava shiyur* — a foot or a leg is a meaningful retention, not because the *shechita* needs them in the active sense, but because they *need* the *shechita* to become permitted. They fall within the category of things rendered edible by *shechita*. Horns and shearings (*karna v'gizoteha*), which do not require *shechita* to become permitted, are not considered part of the "whole" for *mechira* purposes.

The result is four positions with two distinct conceptual frameworks: two that define wholeness through *shechita* (differing on whether what is excluded is what the *shechita* requires, or what requires the *shechita*), and two that define wholeness independently (differing on whether literally any retained portion counts, or only a permanently attached one).

## Missing Limb

### Defects

The *gemara* now addresses a *baraita*: one who steals an animal that is already missing a limb (*ha'gonev ha'ktua*), or one that is lame (*ha'chigeret*), or one that is blind (*ha'suma*), is still *chayav* — still liable. The threshold of "whole" for purposes of four and five is relative to the state of the animal at the time of the theft. If it was stolen in a defective condition, selling or slaughtering it in that same condition is sufficient to trigger liability.

### Partners

The *baraita* adds two further rulings in proximity: one who steals an animal owned by partners (*gonev behemas ha'shutfim*) is liable, because the animal is entirely not his — joint ownership by others constitutes full ownership by others relative to the thief. But *ha'shutfim she'ganvu paturin* — partners who stole are exempt. The meaning of this latter clause, and its apparent contradiction with a *baraita* that makes partners liable, is the subject of the section that follows.

---

## Shutfim Debate

### Two Cases

Rav Nachman resolves the contradiction by distinguishing two different meanings of "partners who stole": *kan b'shataf she'ganav me'chaveiro* — this refers to one partner stealing from the other, i.e., one partner in the jointly owned animal ran off with the entire animal — and *kan b'shataf she'ganav me'alma* — and this refers to partners who together stole from an outsider. The case of exemption from four and five is the first: when one partner steals the jointly owned animal from his co-owner, he is *chayav* in *kefel* for his partner's half, but since the animal was partly his, he is not selling or slaughtering something that is entirely not his. Four and five require that the *shechita* or *mechira* be of the "whole" stolen animal, and a half-owner cannot commit a theft of a whole animal that belongs entirely to others.

When partners steal together from an outsider, however, they are *chayav*. The wording *shutfim she'ganvu* — partners who stole — in the *braita* establishing liability refers to two people who jointly lifted and removed an animal belonging to a third party.

Subsequently, Rava challenged Rav Nachman's formulation from a different *baraita*, which groups together one partner stealing from the other *and* partners jointly stealing, and concludes both are exempt on the grounds of *u'tvacho kulo ba'inan v'leika* — the *shechita* must be of the entire stolen animal and there is none here. This *baraita* seems to contradict Rav Nachman's ruling that joint theft from an outsider makes them liable.

Rav Nachman therefore refines his answer: *kan b'shataf she'ganav v'tavach l'da'at chaveiro, kan b'shataf she'ganav v'tavach shelo l'da'at chaveiro* — the liability turns not on who stole but on whether the *shechita* was done with the knowledge and consent of the co-thief.

### Knowledge Requirement

If Michael and I together steal David's sheep, and Michael then slaughters it with my full awareness and agreement, we are both *chayav* for four and five. But if Michael slaughters it without my knowledge — I intended to keep the animal alive — Michael is exempt, and so am I. The reason is subtle: Michael slaughtered only his "half" of the stolen property, so to speak, without authorization over my "half." The principle is that a full *shechita* of the whole stolen animal is required. Even though the animal belonged entirely to David and neither Michael nor I had any ownership stake in it, Michael's unauthorized slaughter did not constitute a *shechita* of the complete stolen animal in the sense that triggers four and five — it was a *shechita* done without the combined authority of both thieves together. Only when the act is performed with the mutual understanding of all parties to the theft does it constitute the kind of *shechita* that imposes the fourfold or fivefold payment.

The broader principle at work here connects to the earlier discussion about *shliach l'dvar aveirah* — whether an agent acting in a sinful capacity can create liability for the principal. That issue recurs and is addressed more fully in the section that follows.

## Edge Cases

### Conditions

Rabbi Yirmiah raises a series of questions about cases where the *mechira* is conditional or qualified in some way other than a simple retention of a physical part of the animal.

The first case: *machar chutz mi'shloshim yom* — one sold the animal but retained ownership for the first thirty days, with the transfer of ownership taking effect only thereafter. This is a strange case because one might simply treat it as a deferred sale, and if brought before a court before the thirty days elapsed, the analysis becomes complicated.

The second case is clearer: *chutz mi'mlachtah* — I sell it to you, but so long as you have no interest in slaughtering it, I retain the right to use it for labor. This is plainly an incomplete transfer. The buyer has acquired the animal in some sense, but the seller has retained a meaningful use-right that falls outside the *mechira*. This would seemingly exempt from four and five.

### Fetus

The third case is the most analytically interesting: *chutz me'ubarah* — one sells the animal but retains the fetus, if the animal is pregnant. Is that a full sale or not?

The analysis depends on the underlying dispute about the status of a fetus: *aliba d'man d'amar ubar yerech imo hu* — according to the view that the fetus is a limb of the mother, there is no question: retaining it is retaining a physical part of the animal, and indeed a part that is rendered permissible through *shechita*, so one has unquestionably withheld a *davar ha'nitar b'shechita*. The *mechira* is incomplete and one is exempt from four and five.

The real question is *aliba d'man d'amar ubar lav yerech imo hu* — according to the view that the fetus is not legally part of the mother. The *gemara* poses two approaches in tension. On one hand, *keivan d'mechubar bah havei shiyur* — since the fetus is physically attached to the mother and located within her, perhaps that physical connection constitutes a meaningful retention, even if the fetus is not legally her limb. On the other hand, *keivan d'lemifrash minei kai* — since the fetus is destined to be separated from the mother, perhaps it is analogous to shearings, which are also destined to be removed, and retaining such a thing is not considered retaining part of the animal.

A second formulation of the question inverts the reasoning: perhaps even granting that the fetus is not part of the mother, the fact that it *needs* to remain within the mother in order for the *shechita* of the mother to render it permitted — *d'tzarich l'ishorei baha d'b'shechita*, it requires the *shechita* of the mother to become *muttar* — means that it is *k'man d'shayar b'gufa dami*, effectively like retaining a portion of the body itself.

The *gemara* leaves this question unresolved: *teku*. The earlier cases about retaining labor rights or deferred ownership transfer are also left without formal resolution.

### Cut & Sold

Rav Pappa adds one further question: *ganava, kita'ah, u'machrah, mahu?* — one stole an animal, then cut off a limb, and then sold what remained. Is one liable for four and five?

The question has two competing frames. One might say *mai d'ganav lo zavin* — what he stole, he did not sell, because what was stolen included the limb that is now missing; the sale is therefore not a sale of the whole stolen animal. Alternatively, *mai d'zavin, halo shiyar* — what he sold, he sold completely; he simply amputated a portion first, and then sold the remainder whole. The distinction is whether the threshold of "whole" is measured relative to the original theft or relative to the transaction at hand. The *gemara* does not resolve this question either.

## Transfer Methods

### Ten Cases

A *baraita* teaches ten scenarios in which one who steals an animal and then transfers it in various ways is *chayav* for four and five. These are: stealing and giving to another who slaughters it (*ganav ve'natan le'acher ve'tavach*); stealing and giving to another who sells it (*ganav ve'natan le'acher u'machar*); stealing and sanctifying it (*ganav ve'hikdish*); stealing and selling on credit (*ganav ve'hikif*); stealing and swapping it (*ganav va'hechlif*); stealing and giving it as a gift (*ganav ve'natan matanah*); stealing and using it to repay a debt (*ganav u'para chovo*); stealing and using it to pay off store credit (*ganav u'para b'hikeifeto*); and stealing and sending it as betrothal gifts to his father-in-law's household (*ganav ve'shilach sivlonot b'veit chamiv*). In all these cases the thief is *chayav* for the fourfold or fivefold payment.

### Shliach

The *gemara* identifies two distinct *chiddushim* embedded in this *baraita*. The first comes from the opening case — stealing and giving to another who slaughters — which establishes that *yesh shliach l'dvar aveirah* in this context. Ordinarily, the principle is *ein shliach l'dvar aveirah* — one cannot create agency liability for a sinful act, since the agent bears personal responsibility. Here, however, liability falls on the original thief even when the slaughter is performed by a third party. The *gemara* derives this from the structural parallel between *u'tevaho* and *u'macharo* in the verse: just as *mechira* necessarily involves another party — a buyer — so too *tevichah* can be performed through another person and the thief remains liable. The same principle underlies the ruling about partners who steal together: when one slaughters with the knowledge and consent of the other, it is treated as a *shechita* done on behalf of both, and both are *chayav*.

### Sanctification

The second *chiddush* identified by the *gemara* is the case of *ganav ve'hikdish* — stealing and sanctifying. This is flagged as the novel ruling at the end of the *baraita*. The question is why sanctifying a stolen animal counts as a *mechira* at all. The *gemara* answers: *mah li machar le'hedyot, mah li machar la'shamayim* — what difference does it make whether one sold it to a layperson or sold it, so to speak, to Heaven? A transfer of ownership is a transfer of ownership.

### Ownership

However, an immediate qualification is necessary. We established earlier that stealing and sanctifying an animal as a *korban* — *kodshei mizbe'ach* — does not incur four and five, because such sanctification does not constitute a change in the meaningful ownership structure of the animal: *meikara tura de'Reuven ve'hashta tura de'Reuven* — at the outset it was Reuven's ox, and it remains Reuven's ox, in the sense that the *korban* is brought for the owner's sake, it is identified with the owner, and it is offered in the owner's name.

Tosafot explicitly addresses this and limits the *baraita*'s ruling to *kodshei bedek ha'bayit* — donations to the Temple treasury for maintenance purposes. In that case, the donor transfers genuine title to the *Beit Hamikdash*, which becomes the owner. The transaction is structurally parallel to a sale. The animal exits the donor's legal sphere and enters the Temple's.

*Kodshei mizbe'ach*, by contrast, is not a transaction about ownership at all. It is a process of instilling *kedushah* — of conferring a sacred status — and of dedicating the animal so that its being slaughtered and offered on the *mizbe'ach* will serve as atonement for the owner. The animal acquires its status as a *korban*, it is no longer ordinary property in the conventional sense, yet it remains identified with and named after its owner throughout the process. There is no ownership transfer in the sense that *mechira* requires. Therefore, *kodshei bedek ha'bayit* incurs four and five — it is a genuine transfer of title — while *kodshei mizbe'ach* does not.
