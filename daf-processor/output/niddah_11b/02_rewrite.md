# Niddah 11b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam Tahor: One or Two So…

### Intro: Dam Tahor Excepti…

The daf opens at the very bottom of 11a, with the phrase *vayoshev al dam tahor*. The mishna had established that virtually every woman — even one who does not ordinarily expect her period — is obligated to check twice daily, with two exceptions: a *niddah* who is already menstruating (since checking would be pointless) and a woman who is *yoshevet al dam tahor* — one in the post-childbirth period during which even uterine bleeding does not render her *tamei*. Since such a woman's blood is in any case *tahor*, there is similarly no need for her to check.

### Rav: One Wellspring

The Gemara introduces the phrase *kasalka deitach mevakeshet leishev al dam tahor*, describing the woman who is "about to" begin this period. Her first week (after the birth of a boy) or two weeks (after a girl) have concluded, and she is about to enter the 33 or 66 days during which bleeding is not a halachic problem. The Gemara then states: *hani-cha leRav de'amar ma'ayan echad hu, hatorah tim'ato vehatorah tiharato, shapir* — this works well according to Rav, who holds that there is only one wellspring. The same uterine source that ordinarily produces *dam niddah* is the source during the *dam tahor* period as well; the Torah simply changes its halachic status. On that view, there is no reason to check at the transition — any blood on day eight and beyond is automatically *tahor*, regardless of when it began flowing.

### Levi: Two Wellsprings

Levi, by contrast, holds *shnei ma'ayanot heim* — there are two distinct wellsprings. The blood produced during the post-childbirth period is biologically different from *dam niddah*; it originates from a different physiological source. According to this view, the Gemara argues *tivdok* — she should check. Why? Because if the blood is *tahor* only on account of its distinct biological origin, then a flow that began at the end of day seven and continued uninterrupted into day eight cannot simply be re-categorized as *dam tahor*. It remains the same continuous flow from the same *tamei* source. According to Levi, then, there should at least be a check at the very onset of day eight to confirm that one is not in the middle of such a continuous flow.

### Continuous vs. Separate…

To sharpen the issue: it is not that blood beginning on day eight is ever *tamei* — everyone agrees that blood originating on day eight is *tahor*. The dispute is whether a continuous flow that demonstrably began on day seven, and simply continued into day eight without interruption, may be reclassified. According to Rav, the answer is yes — the Torah's declaration is categorical and temporal, and once day eight arrives, the blood's status changes regardless of continuity. According to Levi, continuity of flow constitutes evidence that the blood is still coming from the *tamei* wellspring; the mere change of date does not override that physical reality.

It is worth noting that our current practice departs from the Gemara's framework here. Post-Gemara, blood during the 33 and 66 days is treated as *dam niddah*. This is part of a broader pattern, which will be discussed further below, of treating all blood uniformly as *tamei* — a generalized *chumra* that emerged in stages.

### Mishna as Beit Shammai

Having established the problem according to Levi's view, the Gemara addresses how the mishna can stand unqualified, without requiring any check at the transition. Levi's response is: *ha mani? Beit Shammai hi* — the mishna is following the position of Beit Shammai. As will emerge later in the tractate, Beit Shammai explicitly holds that even blood from the same biological source is *tahor* during the post-childbirth period, because the Torah's declaration operates independently of physiology. If the mishna is framed according to Beit Shammai, it is entirely coherent — there is never any need to check, since even a continuous flow from the *tamei* wellspring becomes *tahor* the moment day eight arrives.

The Gemara then raises the obvious objection: *vesatam tana keBeit Shammai?* — would an unattributed mishna follow Beit Shammai? The entire chapter, after all, is named for Shammai, but an unattributed ruling in favor of Beit Shammai is nevertheless surprising. The Gemara responds: *stam ve'achar kach machloket* — when an unattributed ruling appears and is followed later by an explicit dispute, the rule is *ein halacha kestam*; we do not necessarily rule according to the anonymous formulation. It is therefore not problematic to have an unattributed mishna here reflect Beit Shammai's position, since the debate is spelled out later.

The Gemara then offers a second resolution: *ve'i ba'it eima, yoshevet katani* — perhaps the mishna does not say "a woman who is about to enter the *dam tahor* period," but rather one who is *already in the middle* of it. If she has already established herself in the 33 or 66 days and is known not to have had any bleeding at the moment of transition, then according to Levi there is no issue of a continuous flow, and the ruling of the mishna holds even according to his position.

### Dam Tahor Irrelevant to…

This leads the Gemara to ask: *ei yoshevet, mai lemeymar?* — if she is already in the middle of the period, why state the obvious? Of course she is *tahor* and need not check. The answer is that one might have thought she should still check even during the 33 and 66 days — not because the blood would render her *tamei*, but because it might be relevant for establishing her *veset*, her menstrual cycle. The Gemara therefore teaches: *dema'ayan tahor lema'ayan tamei lo kava'ah veset* — bleeding from the *tahor* wellspring has no bearing on establishing a cycle associated with the *tamei* wellspring. This holds from within Levi's framework, where the two sources are categorically distinct.

The Gemara then reverses the question and asks it according to Rav: since Rav holds it is the same biological source, perhaps bleeding during the *dam tahor* period *should* be relevant to establishing a cycle. The Gemara answers: *afilu hachi mimei taharah lemimei tumah lo kava'ah* — even so, halachically, since the Torah has declared this blood *tahor*, it is treated as entirely separate from the niddah context, and therefore does not count toward cycle establishment. This is a noteworthy point. As discussed at the end of the previous daf, *oness* bleeding — bleeding caused by an external stimulus — does not establish a *veset* even though it renders a woman *tamei*, because cycle-establishment tracks biological regularity rather than halachic status alone. Here we have the inverse: blood that is halachically *tahor* does not establish a *veset* even if Rav maintains it comes from the same biological source, because the Torah's declaration of *tahor* severs its relevance to the *niddah* framework entirely.

## Hymenal Bleeding Cri (II)

### Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

The Gemara now introduces a later mishna with the words *tnan hatam* — "we learned over there." The case concerns a *tinokes shelo higia zmana lirot* — a young girl, not yet having reached puberty, who has been married. The mishna presents the classic debate between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel regarding the duration during which hymenal bleeding is assumed to be the source of any blood she produces.

Before examining the dispute itself, it is worth situating hymenal bleeding within the broader halachic context. In the framework of the Gemara, *dam betulim* was not only exempt from the status of *dam niddah*, but actually served as a positive attribution that would explain away any bleeding in the relevant period. Nowadays, the practice is to treat hymenal bleeding as *tamei*, just as the 33 and 66 days of *dam tahor* are treated as *tamei*. This shift began, as noted above, through several stages: the *chumra* of treating *dam betulim* as *dam niddah* was introduced already at the time of Rav and Shmuel at the beginning of the Amoraic period; the practice of treating every woman as a *zavah gedolah* requiring seven clean days is associated with *chumra deRebbi Zeira* somewhat later; and both practices solidified further during the Geonic period. The Rambam went so far as to hold that even blood *machmas makah* — blood known to come from a wound or internal medical condition — must be treated as *tamei*, a position universally rejected by other authorities, leaving *dam makah* as the one exception that remains operative in practice.

The mishna's formulation is: *Beit Shammai omrim* — Beit Shammai gives her four nights, during which any bleeding is attributed to the hymen and she is not *tamei*. *Beit Hillel omrim ad shetichye hamaka* — Beit Hillel says the exemption lasts until the wound heals. The practical question then becomes: how does one determine when the wound has healed?

### Sex with Blood: Hymen Ac…

*Amar Rav Gidel amar Shmuel, lo shanu ela shelo paska machmas tashmish, vera'asa shelo machmas tashmish.* Rav Gidel in the name of Shmuel explains the operative criterion for Beit Hillel: the assumption that any bleeding is hymenal applies so long as every act of intercourse continues to produce bleeding. The logic is that intercourse would irritate an intact or healing hymen and provoke continued bleeding. If every time they have relations there is blood, that is ongoing evidence that the hymen remains a factor. Consequently, even bleeding that occurs *not* in direct association with intercourse may still be attributed to the hymen — *vera'asa shelo machmas tashmish* — as long as the pattern of sex-associated bleeding has been maintained without interruption.

### Sex without Blood: Hymen…

The inverse principle is equally important: *aval paska machmas tashmish* — if there was a single instance in which intercourse occurred without any subsequent bleeding, then the assumption that the hymen remains a factor is foreclosed. *Vera'asa* — any bleeding she sees thereafter, whether during intercourse or not, is *tamei*. The hymen can no longer serve as an explanatory source.

Tosafot raises the question of what precisely constitutes "sex without bleeding." The blood associated with intercourse need not appear at the exact moment of the act; it may appear the following morning, for instance, and still be attributed to that night's relations. The limiting principle articulated in the Gemara is: *avar layla achas belo tashmish vera'asa* — if a full night passed during which there was no intercourse, and blood then appeared, that blood cannot be traced back to the earlier act. The example: if they had relations Sunday night and blood appears Tuesday morning — an entire intervening night having passed without intercourse — the blood is not attributable to the earlier sex. But if blood appeared Monday morning, it can still be attributed to Sunday night's relations. If blood appears only after a full night has elapsed with no intervening intercourse, the hymen is no longer considered the source, and she is *tamei*.

This entire discussion pertains to the most lenient of cases — a girl who has not yet reached puberty, for whom there is both the reality of the hymen and the presumption of no menstruation. Beit Hillel's approach here is not simply more lenient or more stringent than Beit Shammai in any linear sense; it is more attentive to the specifics of each case.

### Different Colors as Evid…

A further criterion applies even within the period when the hymen is still considered a factor: *nishtanu marei hadamim shela tamei* — if the color of the blood changes, she is *tamei*. The reasoning is as follows. Blood from a wound, including hymenal blood, tends to be bright red. Menstrual blood, which contains uterine lining material, presents with a different appearance. If the blood she is producing looks different from the hymenal blood she has been producing, it can no longer be attributed to the hymen, and is therefore treated as menstrual blood, rendering her *tamei*.

One might ask: should not this color criterion apply to blood found after intercourse as well, given that the semen and other fluids present might affect the color? This question bridges directly into the next section of discussion.

## Exemptions from Checking

### Mishna Inconsistency Not…

The Gemara introduces a further challenge through the words *amar Rava, eima reisha* — Rava draws attention to an apparent inconsistency within the mishna itself. The mishna, when discussing the twice-daily checking requirement, lists two categories exempt from it: the *niddah* (whose checking is pointless since she is already *tamei*) and the *yoshevet al dam tahor* (whose blood is in any case *tahor*). Notably absent from this list is the virgin bride whose *dam betulim* renders all her blood *tahor*. Yet when the mishna turns to checking around intercourse, it does include the virgin bride as exempt, alongside the woman in the *dam tahor* period — but omits the *niddah* from the intercourse-checking exemption.

The apparent inconsistency: why is the virgin bride not also exempted from the twice-daily check, given that any blood she produces is attributed to the hymen? And why is the *niddah* not included in the intercourse-checking exemption? The answer to the second question is straightforward — a *niddah* is not having intercourse at all. But the first question requires a more substantive resolution.

### Two Different Types of C…

The resolution offered is *kan sheshimsha de'eima shamash akran, kan shelo shimsha* — there is a meaningful distinction between the checking that occurs in the context of intercourse and the checking that occurs independently of it. When blood appears after intercourse, the presence of semen and other fluids may affect its color, meaning that even if the blood looks different from what the hymenal blood has been, one cannot use that color difference as evidence of a non-hymenal source. The act of intercourse itself accounts for the altered appearance. Therefore, checking after intercourse is pointless for the virgin bride — any color will be attributed to the hymen.

But the twice-daily check operates independently of intercourse. In that context, if blood appears and it is a different color from the hymenal blood, there is genuine reason for concern. The twice-daily check therefore remains meaningful for the virgin bride, because it might reveal blood of a color that cannot be explained by the hymen. The mishna is thus internally consistent: the virgin bride is exempted only from checking around intercourse (where color differences are irrelevant), but is not exempted from the twice-daily check (where color differences do matter).

## Hymenal Bleeding Cri (II)

### Color Matters or Not by…

The *beraita* cited by the Gemara restates the rule about continuous sex-associated bleeding and confirms the criterion of color change: *nishtanu mareh damim shelah, tamei'ah* — if the color shifts, she is *tamei*, even within the period when sex-associated bleeding would otherwise be attributed to the hymen. The *beraita* also confirms Tosafot's elaboration: *avar layla achas belo tashmish vera'atah* — a full night without intercourse followed by bleeding removes the blood from the category of *machmas tashmish*.

To state the comprehensive rule: a woman is presumed to have *tahor* hymenal bleeding as long as every instance of intercourse continues to produce blood within approximately a day. Even bleeding not temporally connected to intercourse is attributed to the hymen under those circumstances. The two exceptions that break this presumption are: (1) sex that is not followed by blood within the relevant time frame, and (2) blood of a noticeably different color appearing in a context not associated with intercourse, where the color difference is meaningful. Blood appearing after intercourse, even if differently colored, is attributed to the presence of semen and is not used to draw conclusions about the source of the bleeding.

For a woman without these special conditions — one who does not have an intact or recently ruptured hymen — the color of blood is not similarly analyzed, except in cases where there is positive evidence of an internal wound (*makah*), in which case blood might be attributed to that wound rather than to the uterus. Even then, one must begin from the color and reason backwards to the existence of a *makah*, which makes that inference available primarily where the medical reality is independently established.

## Checking Around Sex

### Purpose of Sex Checking

The Gemara now returns to the broader question of why there is any obligation to check around intercourse at all. The mishna established that certain women must check not only twice daily but also around sex — *meshamashet be'edim*, using cloths. The Gemara clarifies that this arises only in a context of *taharot*. One might ask why concern for *taharot* would extend to checking around intercourse. Two possible explanations emerge. The first, associated with Rashi's reading, is that the Gemara believed intercourse could sometimes provoke uterine bleeding — that is, the act itself might cause the onset of menstruation. From that perspective, checking after intercourse would be a direct safeguard against the possibility that the act brought about *dam niddah*. The second explanation is more diffuse: once a woman is living in a heightened state of concern about *tumah vetaharah* — because of *terumah*, *ma'asrot*, or other purity matters — that general state of alertness simply extends to all areas of potential *tumah*, including intercourse. The checking before intercourse is less well explained by the first theory (since one is concerned about what happens *during* intercourse, not about blood that preceded it), and fits more naturally within the second, generalized framework.

### Post-Gemara Stringency

As noted earlier, the practice after the Gemara's period was to treat all blood as *dam niddah*. This applies to *dam betulim* from the time of Rav and Shmuel onward, to the *dam tahor* period from the Geonic period onward, and more generally to a posture of *lo plug* — making no distinctions and applying maximum stringency across the board. This approach, while driven by a laudable concern for uniformity, has the inevitable consequence of losing all the *kullos* available within the Gemara's more differentiated framework. One does not end up treating everything the same leniently; one ends up treating everything the same strictly. The Rambam's position on blood *machmas makah* is an extreme case of this tendency, and its rejection by all other authorities preserves at least one significant leniency in practice.

### Shmuel: Taharot Context…

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: lo shanu ela letaharos* — Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel states the pivotal halachic ruling: this entire obligation to check around intercourse applies only in the context of *taharot*. Whether this is understood as a historical-societal condition — a time when *tumah vetaharah* was broadly practiced — or as a more individual, case-by-case standard (a woman who personally does not handle *terumah*, does not concern herself with *taharot*, and has no *kohen* in the household), the principle is the same: *aval leba'alah, muteret* — if the concern is only about permissibility to her husband, with no *taharot* considerations operative, she is permitted without any check.

The Gemara initially raises *pshita* — this seems obvious. Of course checking twice a day is not about the husband; one checks in the morning. The Gemara redirects: the ruling applies to the second element of the mishna, *bish'ah shehi overet lishamesh beitah* — when she is about to have intercourse. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: lo shanu ela be'ishah she'asukah betaharah* — this checking before intercourse applies only to a woman engaged in a life of *taharah*. The Gemara explains: *migo de'ba'aya bedikah letaharot ba'aya nami leba'alah* — since she is already required to check for *taharot*, she is required to check for her husband as well. This formulation confirms the second explanatory model: the checking before intercourse is a function of the generalized vigilance that comes with being *asukah betaharah*, not a targeted response to the possibility of sex-induced bleeding.

Conversely, *aval eina asukah betaharah, lo ba'aya bedikah* — if she is not in that world of *taharah*, there is no requirement to check before intercourse with her husband.

The Gemara then challenges the novelty of Shmuel's statement: *tanina, kol hanashim bechezkat taharah leba'aleihen* — we already know from a later mishna that all women are presumed *tahor* to their husbands. If women were required to check before every sexual encounter, that presumption of *taharah* would be meaningless. Shmuel's ruling is therefore not a new principle; it was already implicit. The Gemara responds: *nah eima matnitin havi amina, hani mili be'ishah sheyesh lah veset* — without Shmuel's explicit statement, one might have thought the presumption of *taharah* applies only to a woman with a regular cycle, since her cycle establishes clear periods of expected non-bleeding. *Aval isha she'ein lah veset ba'aya bedikah* — a woman without a regular cycle, whose bleeding could occur at any time, one might have thought must check even absent any *taharot* concern, purely for the sake of permissibility to her husband. This, notably, is a matter with potential application even today. And indeed, according to the Rambam, a woman without a fixed cycle is required to check before every act of intercourse. Mainstream halachic practice does not follow this position.

Shmuel's statement is therefore necessary to teach us *both* extremes: even a woman with a regular cycle, when she is *asukah betaharah*, must check before intercourse (*migo de'ba'aya bedikah letaharot*); and conversely, even a woman without a regular cycle, if she is not *asukah betaharah*, does not need to check for the sake of her husband alone.

The Gemara then reconciles the apparent duplication in Shmuel's rulings — once transmitted through Rav Yehuda and once through Rav Abba bar Yirmiyah — with the principle *chada miklal chavertah itmar*: one ruling was stated explicitly and the other was inferred from it. This is not a redundancy; in the oral culture of the Amoraim, "publishing" a ruling means presenting it formally for transmission, and inferring a second principle from the first is distinct from the first statement itself.

The *beraita* cited corroborates Shmuel's ruling: *bameh devarim amurim letaharot, aval leba'alah, mutteret* — all of this checking is for *taharot*; for the husband alone, she is permitted. An additional clause specifies: *bameh devarim amurim shehinnichah bechezkat taharah* — when does a husband have the right to assume his wife is *tehorah* upon returning home? When he left and she was *tehorah*. If he left when she was a *niddah*, the status quo of *tumah* remains operative: *le'olam hi betumah ta'ad shetomar lo tehorah ani* — she remains presumed *tamei* until she explicitly informs him she is *tehorah*. The Gemara acknowledges this principle will be revisited later, including the question of whether, for a woman with a regular cycle and sufficient elapsed time, we might reasonably presume she has immersed and become *tehorah*.

## Prohibition on Checking

### Voluntary Check Permitte…

The sugya now arrives at what is, for both its halachic and its ethical dimensions, a striking passage. *Ba'ei minei Rav Zeira mei Rav Yehuda: isha mah she-tivdok atzmah leba'alah?* — Rav Zeira asked Rav Yehuda: granted that a woman is not required to check before intercourse in the absence of *taharot* concerns, but what if she *wants* to? What if she wishes to be scrupulous and check voluntarily, to ensure she is not a *niddah*?

### No Voluntary Checking

*Amar leh: lo tivdok* — Rav Yehuda answered: she should not check. The questioner presses: *ve-tivdok, u-mah be-kach?* — why not? What is the harm? She might be being extra careful about a prohibition of *karet*. The answer: *im kein, libo nokfo u-poreish* — if she checks, her husband's heart will trouble him and he will refrain. A man who sees his wife checking will naturally assume she was concerned she might be a *niddah*; even if she reports a clean result, the act of checking has introduced anxiety that was not there before, and this will inhibit intimacy. The concern is not merely that it "breaks the mood" in a casual sense; the concern is that it introduces ongoing halachic anxiety into the husband's orientation toward marital relations.

This is a remarkable passage in its implications. The Gemara here is actively prohibiting an act of religious scrupulousness — not because the act itself is wrong, but because it conflicts with *shalom bayit* and the maintenance of a healthy marital life. One who reflexively reaches for stringency as the "frum" position must reckon with a Gemara that says the opposite: *do not check*, even though the stakes are a potential prohibition of *karet*, because the religious value of marital harmony outweighs the religious value of extra vigilance. It is also worth observing that the Gemara's concern here is exclusively for the husband's state of mind. The possibility that the woman might have her own anxieties — that being forbidden to check might leave *her* unsettled — is not addressed. This is, as one can hardly miss, a perspective shaped entirely by the male vantage point.

### Checking After Sex

*Ba'ei minei Rav Amram mei Rav Huna: isha mah she-tivdok atzmah keshiur veset, kedei lechayev baalah chatas?* — Rav Amram posed a related question to Rav Huna. This time the question is about checking *after* intercourse. One might imagine a scenario in which a woman, perhaps sensing something during intercourse, wishes to check immediately afterward. If she checks within *shiur veset* — effectively instantaneously, as will be defined below — and finds blood, it can be assumed that she was bleeding at the time of intercourse. Both she and her husband would then be *chayav chatas*, having had intercourse while she was a *niddah*, albeit *beshogeg*. (Whether this would be *beshogeg* or *be'ones* is a separate question touched on below.) At a slightly longer interval — not instantaneous but shortly after — the blood cannot be attributed definitively to the time of intercourse, but there is sufficient grounds for concern, raising the possibility of an *asham talui*.

### Shiur Veset Defined

The Gemara clarifies the concept of *shiur veset*. *Eizehu shiur veset? Mashal leshamash ve'eid she'omdim betzad hamashkof* — what is the time frame of *shiur veset*? The Gemara offers a *mashal*: the *shamash* (a euphemism for the organ of intercourse) and the *eid* (the cloth, literally "witness") are standing on either side of the doorframe. As soon as the *shamash* exits, the *eid* enters. The house here represents the woman; the interior of the house is the locus of the check. The *shiur veset* is thus truly instantaneous — one departs and the other enters without any elapsed time.

### Wiping vs. Full Check

*Havei veset she'amru lekinuach velo lebedikah* — this instantaneous interval is sufficient for a *kinuach*, a wiping, but not for a full *bedikah*. A *bedikah* — a thorough internal examination — takes longer than this interval; by the time a full *bedikah* is completed, the moment of certainty has already passed. A quick *kinuach* performed within this instant would, if it reveals blood, allow one to conclude that she was definitely bleeding during intercourse. A full *bedikah* cannot achieve that certainty. The question *ela nu, mahu shetekanneach?* then becomes: fine, if not a full *bedikah*, can she at least perform a *kinuach* immediately after? This reformulation will be addressed below.

### Doubtful Status Check

An alternative version of the question is also recorded: *isha mahu shetivdok atzmah kedei lechayev baalah asham talui* — can she perform a full *bedikah* after intercourse for the purpose of determining whether she might owe an *asham talui*? That is, not to establish certainty of *niddah* at the time of intercourse, but to establish sufficient doubt to warrant bringing a *korban* for uncertain sin. The scenario here is one of genuine anxiety about what may have occurred during intercourse, and the woman wishes to know after the fact.

### No Post-Sex Checking

The answer is identical in both versions: *amar lo, lo tivdok* — she shall not check. And the reasoning is again: *im ken, libo nokfo u-poreish* — if he sees her checking immediately after intercourse, the husband's heart will be troubled and he will refrain from relations in the future. He will carry forward the anxiety that his wife was checking to see whether they may have sinned, and this will inhibit subsequent intimacy. The Gemara is thus consistent: neither checking before intercourse nor checking after it is permitted in the absence of *taharot* considerations, and not even a voluntarily assumed stringency is allowed, because in both cases the resulting anxiety undermines marital life.

This creates a striking inversion for the modern reader. The mishna opened with a robust framework of checking — before sex, after sex, twice daily. The Gemara, through Shmuel's ruling and through the questions of Rav Zeira and Rav Amram, has systematically dismantled the applicability of this framework. Not only is checking *not required* absent *taharot* considerations; it is *forbidden*, even if voluntarily sought, precisely because religious over-vigilance here carries its own halachic cost.

## Tsnuot Standards

### Tsnuot Status

*Amar Rav Ami amar Rabbi Yannai* — this checking with cloths before and after intercourse is the practice *shel tsnuos*, of women who are especially scrupulous. The term *tsnuos* here does not mean simply "modest" in the colloquial sense, but connotes a heightened degree of care and conscientiousness in matters related to bodily purity and intimate conduct.

*Amar lei Rabbi Abba bar Memel leRav Ami: tana tana tsrichos* — but the mishna says they are *required* to check. How can you call it the practice of the especially scrupulous, as though it were supererogatory, when it is in fact an obligation? *Ve-at amret tsnuos?*

*Amar lei: she-ani omer kol hamekayem divrei chachamim nikra tsnua* — Rav Ami replies: I am saying that anyone who fulfills the words of the Sages is called *tsnua*. Not everyone does what they are supposed to do; if you actually observe what is required, that itself rises to the level of being called scrupulous. This may reflect something about the sociological reality of the period — that full compliance with these requirements was not universal, and those who maintained it were distinguished by that fact.

### Fresh Cloth Practice

*Amar Rava: ela tsnuos* — Rava, however, is not satisfied with this reading. He objects that if fulfilling a requirement makes one *tsnua*, then failing to fulfill it ought to make one *rasha* — wicked — not merely less distinguished. The structure of the statement implies that non-observance is merely the absence of a praiseworthy quality, not an active violation, and Rava finds that unacceptable.

Rava therefore offers an alternative definition of what distinguishes *tsnuos*: *ed she-badka bo atzmah lifnei tashmish zeh ein bodkas bo lifnei tashmish acher* — a *tsnuah* is a woman who, having used one cloth before one act of intercourse, will not reuse that same cloth before the next act of intercourse. She uses a fresh cloth each time. This is the mark of the especially scrupulous woman: not merely that she checks, but that she ensures the integrity of each check by using an uncontaminated cloth. A cloth used previously — even if it shows no stain — may carry other residue that could obscure or affect the reading of the next examination.

### Rava's Distinction

*Ve-she'einan tsnuos bodkos velo ichpas lehan* — women who are not *tsnuos* in this sense do check, but do not trouble themselves to use a fresh cloth each time. This is acceptable after the fact (*bediavad*), but the ideal (*lechatchila*) standard is a fresh cloth for every examination. Rava's formulation thus preserves both the obligatory nature of the checking (for those in the *taharot* context) and the additional standard that distinguishes the truly scrupulous: not the fact of checking itself, but the care with which it is performed.
