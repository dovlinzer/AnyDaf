# Niddah 28 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dissolved Fetus Debate

### Mishnah Setup

The discussion begins at the bottom of 27b, which provides the necessary context for the opening of 28a. The *mishnah* presents a dispute concerning a woman who miscarries when the embryonic sac — the placenta — is filled with liquid but contains no visible fetus. The *chachamim* rule that this represents a fetus that dissolved: the woman is a *yoledet* and the house is *tamei*, for there had been a dead baby present, even in dissolved form. Rabbi Shimon concedes that the woman is a *yoledet* — clearly a fetus had existed — but maintains that the house is not *tamei*, since the fetus has lost its halakhic status.

### R. Yochanan View

Rabbi Yochanan explains Rabbi Shimon's position through the principle of *batel berov*: within the placenta, the fetus is overwhelmed by a majority of embryonic fluid and other liquids, and therefore loses its distinct identity. Since it is not discernible as a separate entity, it is nullified.

### Reish Lakish View

Reish Lakish goes further. According to his view, *batel berov* is not required at all. The decisive factor is whether the fetus retains its appearance — particularly whether *tzurat panav*, the form of the face, has been eradicated. Once the form has been destroyed, the fetus loses its status as a corpse, regardless of whether it constitutes a majority or minority of the fluid.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot provides the crucial conceptual underpinning for this entire discussion. Ordinarily, even a small quantity of a corpse — a *kezayit* of flesh or a certain measure of bone — generates *tumah*. But that principle does not apply here, because there is no flesh and no bone. What exists is a liquefied mass. The only basis for *tumah* in such a case is not quantity but identity: it must be considered an entire corpse. Reish Lakish therefore reasons that this identity depends on the integrity of the form. If the form — and particularly the face — has been effaced, it can no longer be regarded as a whole *met*, and the *tumah* lapses. One might ask: is this not like a piece of meat, which has no face and yet causes *tumah*? The answer is precisely the point: that rule applies when you have actual flesh. Here there is no flesh, no bone — only the concept of a complete body. And for that concept to generate *tumah*, the body's integrity must remain. If even a significant portion of that form has been disrupted, one can say it is no longer considered a complete corpse.

## Burnt Corpse Case

### Burnt Corpse Proof

The *gemara* quotes Reish Lakish's position on 27b: *shepir shetirfuhu be-meimav na'aseh kemet shenitbalbel tzurato* — a fetus that has been scrambled with its waters is analogous to a corpse whose form has been destroyed, and therefore loses its status. Rabbi Yochanan immediately challenges: from where do we know that a *met* whose form is destroyed becomes *tahor*? He anticipates that Reish Lakish will seek support from the following teaching: *met shenisraf* — a corpse that has been burnt — if *shildo kayemes*, its bodily form is still present, it is *tamei*. A burnt corpse occupies a conceptual space distinct from the *rekev* of natural decomposition discussed elsewhere. When a body is charred or burned, it is no longer flesh, no longer bone — yet it derives from an entire body. It is, in that sense, precisely analogous to the dissolved fetus: no *basar*, no *atzamos*, but potentially still recognized as a *met* by virtue of having been a whole body. The question is: what is required to maintain that status?

The teaching says that when the form remains, the burnt corpse is *tamei*. The *gemara* then introduces a striking practical illustration at the top of 28a: *ma'aseh hayah ve-time'u lo petachim gedolim ve-teharu lo petachim ketanim* — in an actual case involving a burnt corpse, they ruled the large doors *tamei* and the small doors *tahor*. To understand this, one must recall two *halachos* of *tumah* in an enclosed space. First, if a window of at least a *tefach al tefach* is open, *tumah* passes through it into an adjacent room. Second, even if all openings are closed, there is a principle of *sof tumah latzeis*: the doorway through which the corpse will most likely be carried out is treated as if already open, and *tumah* exits through it. A corpse will not typically be carried through an opening smaller than a *tefach al tefach*, so only the large doors are treated as likely exit points. Since the burnt corpse retained its form, the small closed windows were ruled *tahor*.

Reish Lakish, Rabbi Yochanan assumes, wishes to infer from this case that the entire basis for *tumah* here is *tzuroto kayemes* — the form is still present — and that without form, the burnt corpse would be *tahor* entirely.

### Doors Analysis

Rabbi Yochanan rejects this inference. The fact that the teaching emphasizes the form does not prove that the form is a prerequisite for *tumah* itself. It may instead explain only why the *tumah* exits through the large doors rather than the small ones. If the form is intact, whoever removes the body will want to preserve it and will therefore carry it out through a large door, not pass it piece by piece through small windows. The presence of form determines the *mode of exit*, not the *existence of tumah*.

### Alternative Logic

Rabbi Yochanan elaborates: *duk mineh lehai gisa* — one could actually read the case in the opposite direction. If the form is present, only the large doors are *tamei*, since that is where the whole body will be carried out. But if the form were gone — if it were merely ashes or dismembered remains — then *petachim ketanim nami teme'im*, even the small openings would be *tamei*, because *dechol chad vechad chazi leappokei eivar eivar* — each small window could serve as a passage for an individual limb or portion. The inference from the burnt corpse case therefore proves nothing about whether a formless *met* retains *tumah* status. It only tells us that form affects which doors are implicated, not whether *tumah* exists at all. This then is the crux of the debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish as applied to the dissolved fetus: when there is no flesh and no bone but the concept of a whole *met*, what is required to dissolve that concept? Rabbi Yochanan requires *batel berov*; Reish Lakish requires only the significant loss of form.

## Ashes Tumah

### Ashes Doctrine

The *gemara* now pivots to ask: like whom does Rabbi Yochanan rule? The answer: like Rabbi Eliezer. For we have learned in the *mishnah*: *eifer serufin* — the ashes of burnt corpses — Rabbi Eliezer says their minimum measure to cause *tumah* is a *rova* (a quarter *kav*). Here, then, one has pure ashes: no shape, no form, no flesh, no bone — yet they cause *tumah* provided there is a sufficient quantity. This appears to be a strong support for Rabbi Yochanan's position, and at least potentially for the *chachamim* who disagree with Rabbi Shimon altogether: *tumah* can exist without form, as long as there is a sufficient measure. It also reinforces the underlying conceptual principle: since the basis for *tumah* in these cases is not *basar* or *atzamos* but the identity of an entire *met*, one can maintain that identity even in the form of ashes — at least according to Rabbi Eliezer — as long as a minimum quantity is present.

### Abaye Explanation

The *gemara* now returns to the earlier case of the burnt corpse that retains its form, and asks: how, practically speaking, does a burnt corpse retain its form? Abaye answers: *kagon shesarfo al gabei katafla* — it was burnt on top of a piece of boiled leather. Since fire could not burn through the leather, the charred remains stayed in place upon it. When the body needed to be transported out of the house, one could lift the leather with the form intact. Without such a surface, the act of transport would have scattered and destroyed the form.

### Rava Rashi

Rava offers a different scenario: *kagon shesarfo al gabei apidra* — it was burnt atop a marble surface. The principle is the same: the stable platform preserves the arrangement of the ashes. Rashi adds the detail that such a platform would have low walls around it — *dikulin umechitzos* — preventing the remains from scattering and thereby maintaining the form. Tosafot goes even further, suggesting the possibility that the corpse was placed inside a mold shaped to the human body, *dfus shel adam*, indented to the figure's outline, so that after the burning, the ashes remained fixed within that form. One might wonder whether these scenarios reflect actual historical practice. The *gemara* itself elsewhere imagines a corpse buried naked inside a marble coffin, so it is possible that similar practices occurred, though how common they were is difficult to assess.

### Ravina Position

Ravina offers what is perhaps the most straightforward reading of the case: *kagon de'ichruchi ichruchi* — the corpse was merely charred, not reduced entirely to ashes. It was burnt thoroughly on the outside, completely carbonized, but had not fully disintegrated into ash. In that state, the body retains its shape without requiring any special surface or enclosure. In all of these scenarios, the *tumah* is not generated by flesh or bone but solely by the status of the *met* as a whole — and the question of form remains central to how that status is maintained.

## Unknown Birth Timing

### Severed Limb

The *gemara* now introduces a *braisa*: *hamapeles yad chatucha veregel chatucha imo teme'ah leidah* — if a woman miscarries and what emerges is only a severed limb, an arm or a leg, the mother is nonetheless *teme'ah leidah*. One assumes, of course, that a fully forming fetus had been present — it did not develop as merely a limb — and the woman therefore has the *tumah* of a *yoledet*, even though no complete fetus is visible. *Ve'ein chosheshin shema migufo tumah bah* — and we are not concerned that the fetus might have been so abnormally formed as to lack the capacity to generate *tumas leidah*. The assumption is that a normal fetus existed and simply dissolved or came out incompletely.

### No Tahara Days

Rav Chisda and Rabbah bar Rav Huna both rule: *ein notnim lah yemei tohar* — she is not given *yemei tohar*, the days of *tahara* following childbirth. She is considered a *safek yoledet*, a woman who has possibly given birth: she carries the *tumah* of a week or two weeks (since we are uncertain whether the infant was male or female), but she receives no *tahara* days.

### Delayed Birth Fear

The reason: *eima hirchikah leidatah* — perhaps the birth occurred long ago. Clearly a fetus existed, but when did the majority of the body emerge? It is possible that *rubo*, the greater portion of the fetus which halakhically constitutes birth, came out weeks earlier, with only a single limb remaining in the womb until now. If that is the case, the woman is indeed a definite *yoledet* — that is not in question — but the onset of her *yemei tohar* may have begun and even concluded before the limb appeared. We therefore cannot confidently grant her any *tohar* days from this point forward.

This raises a natural question: should we not also be concerned retroactively about the *tumah* she may have transmitted to *terumah* or other objects in the weeks before the limb appeared? The *gemara's* implicit answer is that retroactive *tumah* of this type was treated very leniently from the beginning of the tractate — it is *derabbanan* and applied loosely — and so the concern does not extend that far back. What we can say with certainty is only that we cannot now declare the blood she will see in the coming weeks to be *tahor*.

### Rav Yosef

Rav Yosef challenges this position. The *mishnah* deals with a woman who miscarried and does not know what the fetus was — *hamapelet ve'ein yadu'a mah hi hipilah*. The ruling there is *teshev le-zachar uli-nekeivah*: she observes the stringencies of both male and female, meaning two weeks of *tumah* and *tahara* days extending through day forty. Rav Yosef argues that if the concern is that birth may have occurred at an earlier unknown time — and we therefore deny *tahara* days entirely — then the *mishnah* should have gone further and included the stringency of *niddah* as well. That is: she should be treated as potentially a *niddah* in addition to being potentially a *yoledet*, so that any blood appearing after the first two weeks of *tumah* would be considered *niddas* blood rather than *tohar* blood. If we are already being strict enough to deny *tahara* days, why does the *mishnah* still grant her the framework of boy-or-girl *yoledet*, which implicitly gives her *tahara* up to day forty?

### Abaye Resolution

Abaye resolves this by distinguishing between two layers of the ruling. The *mishnah* does, in fact, reflect the concern that birth timing is uncertain, and accordingly *tahara* days are not granted with confidence. But the *mishnah* frames the ruling as *teshev le-zachar uli-nekeivah* — not as *niddah* — for a deliberate reason: *ei tana le-niddah hava amina meviah korban ve'eino ne'echal* — had the *mishnah* used the language of *niddah*, one might infer that there is doubt whether she is a *yoledet* at all, and if her status as *yoledet* is in doubt, her *korban* would not be eaten. The use of *niddah* language in contrast to *yoledet* language would have cast doubt on her very status as someone who gave birth. But she is unquestionably a *yoledet*: she definitely miscarried. We simply do not know when the majority emerged. The *mishnah* therefore deliberately says boy-or-girl to affirm her *yoledet* status and her right to bring and eat the *korban*, while the practical effect — that she cannot rely on *tahara* days — follows from the underlying uncertainty about timing. *Kamashmalan denachal*: it teaches us that the *korban* is eaten.

## Fetal Hand

### Rav Huna

The *gemara* introduces a teaching of Rav Huna: *hotzi ubar yado vehachzira imo teme'ah leidah* — if a fetus extended its hand and then retracted it, the mother acquires the *tumah* of a *yoledet*. This is striking, since the general assumption — which will be confirmed by the next *mishnah* — is that birth is defined by the emergence of the head, or at minimum the majority of the body. How can the momentary protrusion of a hand confer *tumas leidah*? Rav Huna grounds this in a *pasuk*: *vayehi belidta vayiten yad* — and it was when she was giving birth, and he put forth a hand — referring to the narrative of Zarach and Peretz. The *pasuk* uses the language of giving birth (*leidah*) in conjunction with the protrusion of a hand, implying that the hand's emergence already carries halakhic significance.

### Rav Yehuda

Rav Yehuda objects from a *braisa*: *hotzi ubar yado ein imo chosheshet lechol davar* — if a fetus extends its hand, the mother need not be concerned about anything. No *tumah*, no status change of any kind. This appears to directly contradict Rav Huna.

### Doubtful Birth

Rav Nachman resolves the tension: the statement of Rav Huna was explained to him directly by Rav Huna himself. The protrusion of the hand creates *chasusha* — concern, a state of doubt — but not definite birth. *Yemei tohar lo yahavinan lah ad denafik rubei*: she is not given *tahara* days until the majority of the body has emerged. In other words, the protrusion of the hand initiates a period of *tumah* out of concern — *lachush chosheshas* — but does not establish the definite status of *yoledet* with its attendant *tahara* days. That status awaits the emergence of the majority of the body, or, in a normal birth, the forehead.

### Rabbinic Concern

The *gemara* presses further: the *braisa* said she need not be concerned about *anything* — *lechol davar* — which would seem to preclude even this rabbinic-level concern when the hand appears. Abaye resolves this: *einah chosheshet lechol davar midioraisa* — biblically, nothing whatsoever begins when only a hand emerges. The *braisa* speaks at the biblical level. *Aval miderabbanan chosheshet* — but rabbinically, we are concerned. The *gemara* then asks: but Rav Huna quoted a *pasuk* — does that not indicate a biblical basis? Abaye answers: *miderabbanan u'kraya asmakhta be'alma* — the ruling is rabbinic, and the *pasuk* is merely an *asmakhta*, a textual support.

An interesting question remains: what precisely is the nature of this rabbinic *chasusha*? Is it a general precautionary measure, analogous to the way rabbis guard their enactments so that people cannot easily circumvent them? Or is it a specific concern that perhaps the head already emerged and retracted unobserved, and the arm's protrusion is evidence that active birth has begun? This question has practical implications — notably for *bris milah* and similar contexts — though the *gemara* here limits the discussion to the domain of *tumah* and *tahara*. For the purposes of *bris milah* and related matters, the next *mishnah* clearly governs: birth is defined by the crown of the head or the emergence of the majority.

## Tumtum Birth

### Mishnah Setup

The *mishnah* introduces a new category of unusual births. *Hamapeles tumtum ve'androgynus* — if a woman gives birth to a *tumtum* or an *androgynus*. A *tumtum* is an infant whose genitalia is covered or concealed — the sex is simply unknown. An *androgynus* (the word carries its Greek derivation transparently) is an infant with both male and female genitalia. In either case, the sex cannot be determined with certainty.

The *mishnah* rules: *teshev le-zachar uli-nekeivah* — the mother observes the stringencies of both male and female. She has two weeks of *tumah* as for a female, but her *tahara* days end at day forty as for a male.

If she gave birth to a *tumtum* and a male twin, or an *androgynus* and a male, the ruling is the same: *teshev le-zachar uli-nekeivah*. The *tumtum* or *androgynus* may be female, and therefore, combined with the definite male, both stringencies apply.

If, however, she gave birth to a *tumtum* and a female, or an *androgynus* and a female, the ruling is *teshev le-nekeivah bilvad* — she observes only the stringencies of a female. The reasoning is that a definite female already brings the maximum *tumah* (fourteen days) and the maximum *tahara* period (sixty-six days). The male's contribution — a shorter *tahara* window — does not diminish the female's grant of sixty-six days of *tahara*. The male gives a thirty-three-day *tohar* period; the female gives sixty-six days. Having the thirty-three does not eliminate the sixty-six. Therefore, the presence of the *tumtum* or *androgynus* is irrelevant: whatever it is, the definite female already gives the longer stringencies in every direction.

## Tumtum Birth (II)

### Twin Gender

The *gemara* raises a question about the *mishnah's* case of *tumtum* and a male twin. If a *tumtum* by itself already requires the stringencies of both male and female, and a male by itself certainly requires male stringencies, what new information does the combined case of *tumtum* and male add? One might initially think the answer is obvious — of course the combined case is at least as strict — and that the *mishnah* was simply leading to the more novel case of *tumtum* and female. But the *gemara* presses: why include the *tumtum-and-male* case if it teaches nothing beyond the *tumtum* alone?

### R. Yitzchak

The answer invokes a theory of fetal sex determination attributed to Rabbi Yitzchak: *isha mazra'at techilah yoledet zachar, ish mazria techilah yoledet nekeivah* — if the woman contributes seed first, the child will be male; if the man contributes seed first, the child will be female. Based on this theory, one might reason that in the case of twins, either the woman went first — producing two males — or the man went first — producing two females. Under this view, boy-girl twins would be impossible, because both twins would be conceived from the same sequential act. If so, seeing a *tumtum* together with a definite male, I might assume both are males, and there would be no reason to apply the stringencies of a female.

### Simultaneous Seed

The *gemara* refutes this: *eima sheneihem hizriu bevat achat* — perhaps both husband and wife contributed seed simultaneously, in which case both a male and a female could emerge from the same conception event. Boy-girl twins are therefore possible, and the *mishnah* needed to teach explicitly that even in the case of a *tumtum* and a male, we cannot assume both are male. We must treat the *tumtum* as potentially female, and apply both stringencies accordingly.

## Gender Impurity

### Emission Status

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rav: a *tumtum* or *androgynus* who observes a white discharge (*laven*, semen) or a red discharge (*odem*, blood) — *ein chayavin al biat mikdash* — is not liable for entering the *beis hamikdash*, and *ein sorfin aleihem et haterumah* — *terumah* they contact is not burned.

The *safek* for a *tumtum* is clear: we simply do not know whether the person is male or female, so we cannot determine whether a white discharge functions as semen (making him a *ba'al keri*) or whether a red discharge functions as menstrual blood (making her a *niddah*). For the *androgynus*, the case is more initially surprising: the individual visibly has both sets of genitalia, so one might think that semen from the male genitalia definitively creates the status of *ba'al keri*, or blood from the female genitalia definitively creates the status of *niddah*. Nevertheless, the *gemara* rules that this is not so. *Ba'al keri* is a status that applies to men; *niddah* is a status that applies to women. If we cannot assign the person a definitive gender identity, we cannot assign the gender-specific *tumah* that flows from that identity — even when the discharge itself is unambiguous.

### Definite Gender

The reasoning is rooted in the nature of these *tumos*. *Ba'al keri* and *niddah* are not merely about the physical substance — they are about personal status, about what the *gemara* elsewhere calls *ro'eh* rather than *nogea*. It is not that the person touched their semen or blood; it is that their very being is in a state of *tumah* related to their maleness or femaleness. If the gender itself is indeterminate, the gender-linked personal *tumah* does not fully attach. One could push further and argue that perhaps these individuals bear no *tumah* at all — but the *gemara* does not go that far, as we will see.

### Mikdash Entry

The *gemara* then asks: what if both discharges occur together? *Ra'u loven ve'odem ke'echad* — they observed both. In that case, regarding *beis hamikdash* entry: *ein chayavin al biat mikdash* — still not liable. Whatever they are, they are definitely *tamei* in some sense. But since we cannot specify the nature of the *tumah* — whether it is the *tumah* of a *ba'al keri* or of a *niddah* — the technical criteria for liability have not been met.

### Terumah Burning

And yet: *aval sorfin aleihem et haterumah* — *terumah* they have contacted must be burned. When both discharges occur, the person is unquestionably *tamei* by one or the other route. The *tumah* is definite; only the category is uncertain. *Terumah* therefore cannot be eaten or preserved — it must be burned. This ruling implicitly clarifies that even the *androgynus* is being treated as either male or female — not as a third gender — since if it were genuinely a third category with no gender *tumah* at all, even the *terumah* burning rule would be questionable.

## Gender Language

### Language Usage

The *gemara* turns to the textual basis for excluding *tumtum* and *androgynus* from liability regarding *beis hamikdash* entry. Rav had stated: *mizachar ve'ad nekeivah tishaleichu* — "you shall send out, from male to female" — means *zachar vadai u'nekeivah vadais*, only a definite male and definite female, excluding *tumtum* and *androgynus*. But Ulla challenges: perhaps the *braisa* that excludes them from *mikdash* liability is not based on this verse at all. Perhaps it follows only Rabbi Eliezer's position in an entirely different context — that to be liable for a *korban* for entering the *beis hamikdash* while *tamei*, one must know specifically *how* one became *tamei*. Just as Rabbi Eliezer rules that if one touched either a *sheretz* or a *neveilah* and cannot recall which, one does not bring a *korban* — because *al he'elem sheretz hu chayav* requires specific knowledge of the defiling contact — so too here, a *tumtum* who has both discharges but cannot identify whether the *tumah* is that of a *ba'al keri* or a *niddah* would be exempt from a *korban* on those grounds alone. The exclusion need not flow from *zachar ve-nekeivah* language at all.

### Arachin

This raises a broader question: does *zachar ve-nekeivah* language always exclude *tumtum* and *androgynus*? A counterexample: in the context of *arachin* — the valuations of Leviticus — the *Torah* writes *ha-zachar*, with the definite article *heh*. A *braisa* derives from this: *ha-zachar* — and not *tumtum* and *androgynus*. One might ask: even if they cannot be valued as men, perhaps they should be valued at the lower female rate? The *braisa* links *ha-zachar* with *ve-im nekeivah* to show that only definite male and definite female have fixed *arachin* values; the ambiguous cases are excluded from both. The question, then, is why in *arachin* the exclusion required the emphatic *heh* of *ha-zachar*, while elsewhere the plain language of *zachar ve-nekeivah* is said to suffice.

### Necessary Language

The *gemara* answers by drawing a fundamental distinction. In *arachin*, the *Torah* had no choice but to use the words *zachar* and *nekeivah* — these terms were the necessary vehicle for expressing different valuation rates for men and women. Since the gender language was obligatory for other reasons, the mere use of *zachar* alone could not be taken as a signal to exclude *tumtum* and *androgynus*. It was only the additional *heh* — *ha-zachar*, the male specifically — that served as the excluding device.

### Category Division

By contrast, in our context, the *Torah* could have used the inclusive term *adam* — person — and simply stated that *tamei* individuals must be sent out. The deliberate choice to say *zachar ve-nekeivah* where *adam* would have sufficed conveys that only those who definitively fall into one of these two categories are subject to this ruling. The gender language, being optional here, becomes the excluding device by its very specificity. Rav's reading therefore stands: *mizachar ve'ad nekeivah* requires definite male, definite female — *zachar vadai u'nekeivah vadais* — and excludes *tumtum* and *androgynus* not merely from the *korban* obligation, but from the full liability structure associated with *biat mikdash* when in a state of gender-specific *tumah*.
