# Nedarim 4 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yad l'Nezirut

### Yad Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן דְּאָמַר דִּבְּרָה תּוֹרָה כִּלְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, וְ״נָזִיר לְהַזִּיר״ דָּרֵישׁ לַעֲשׂוֹת יְדוֹת נְזִירוּת כִּנְזִירוּת, שֶׁהַנְּזִירוּת חָל עַל הַנְּזִירוּת מְנָא לֵיהּ? הָנִיחָא אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵין נְזִירוּת חָל עַל נְזִירוּת. אֶלָּא אִי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר נְזִירוּת חָל עַל נְזִירוּת, מְנָא לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to the one who says that the Torah spoke in the language of men** and therefore nothing can be derived from the phrase "to utter a vow [*lindor neder*]," **and he expounds** the phrase **"the vow of a nazirite to consecrate himself [*nazir lehazir*]" to render intimations of nazirite** vows **like nazirite** vows, **from where does he** derive that a term of **naziriteship takes effect upon a** previously accepted term of **naziriteship? This works out well if he holds in accordance with the one who says** that a term of **naziriteship does not take effect upon a** previously accepted term of **naziriteship; however, if he holds in accordance with the one who says** that a term of **naziriteship takes effect upon a** previously accepted term of **naziriteship, from where does he** derive this *halakha*?

The daf opens approximately six lines down, with Rav Ashi's statement, picking up from the *baraita* that established the parallel between *yad l'nazir* and *yad l'nedarim* — that is, *l'hazir l'hazir*, the principle that an abbreviated statement counts as a full statement. This was the crucial point developed on the previous daf: the concept of *yad*, by which an incomplete verbal formulation is nonetheless legally effective as a *neder*.

### Thought & Speech

Underlying this discussion is the recognition that a valid *neder* requires two distinct components: correct thought and correct speech. One must say the right thing, and one must intend the right thing. The focus now shifts to what constitutes "the right thing to have said" — specifically, whether a complete verbal formulation is required or whether an abbreviated statement suffices. The principle of *yad* answers that an abbreviated statement can, under the right conditions, serve as a complete and binding declaration.

### Abbreviated Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא קְרָא ״לִיזּוֹר״, מַאי ״לְהַזִּיר״ שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Let the verse say: To consecrate himself [*lizor*]. What** is the reason the verse expressed this same idea with the word ***lehazir*? Learn two** *halakhot* **from this:** That intimations of nazirite vows are considered nazirite vows, and that a term of naziriteship takes effect upon a previously accepted term of naziriteship.

The *Gemara* is equally concerned with establishing that just as there is *bal taachar* in the context of *nedarim* — the prohibition of postponing the fulfillment of a vow — so too does *bal taachar* apply to *nezirut*. The parallel drawn between *neder* and *nazir* thus operates on two axes simultaneously: the effectiveness of abbreviated speech, and the obligation to fulfill one's commitment without delay.

## Bal Taachar

### Link to Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַעְרְבָא אָמְרִי: אִית תַּנָּא דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְיָדוֹת מִן ״לִנְדֹּר נֶדֶר״, וְאִית תַּנָּא דְּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ, מִן ״כְּכׇל הַיֹּצֵא מִפִּיו יַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, they **say: There is a *tanna* who derives intimations** of vows **from** the phrase **"to utter a vow [*lindor neder*],"** as he holds that the Torah did not speak in the language of men. **And** conversely, **there is a *tanna* who** holds that the Torah spoke in the language of men, and therefore **derives this** *halakha* of intimations **from** the verse: **"He shall do according to all that proceeds out of his mouth"** (Numbers 30:3). The inclusive formulation of this verse comes to include intimations of vows.

Having established the *yad* framework, the *Gemara* turns to the application of *bal taachar* to *nezirut*. The prohibition of postponement, classically understood as the obligation not to delay bringing a *korban* past three *regalim*, is extended here to the domain of *nezirut*. The same way one transgresses *bal taachar* by delaying a vow of sacrifice, one transgresses it by improperly delaying the commencement or proper continuation of *nezirut*.

### Postponement Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּמָה נְדָרִים עוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל יַחֵל״, וּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״בַּל יַחֵל״ דִּנְדָרִים מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר כִּכָּר זוֹ אוֹכַל וְלֹא אֲכָלָהּ — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל יַחֵל דְּבָרוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited earlier: **Just as** with regard to **vows, one** who breaks his vow **transgresses** the prohibition: **He shall not profane, and** one who does not fulfill his vow in time transgresses the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** so too, the same is true with regard to nazirite vows. The Gemara asks: **Granted, you** can **find** a case **where** one transgresses the prohibition: **He shall not profane, in** the case of **vows. For example, where one said: I will eat this loaf, and he does not eat it, he violates** the prohibition: **He shall not profane his word.**

A question immediately arises: since accepting *nezirut* takes effect automatically upon utterance, how can there be a scenario in which one is obligated to be a *nazir* but has not yet become one — the very precondition for *bal taachar*? Two scenarios were proposed. First, if someone declared "I will become a *nazir* before I die," then every passing day on which he has not yet accepted *nezirut* constitutes a potential postponement, since he might die at any moment; each day of delay is thus a separate violation of *bal taachar*, even as the *nezirut* has not yet formally begun. Second, if one declared *nezirut* while standing inside a *beit hakevarot* (a cemetery), the *nezirut* may be delayed from taking proper effect by virtue of his remaining in that impure location.

### Cemetery Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״בַּל יַחֵל״ דִּנְזִירוּת הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ הָוֵה לֵיהּ נָזִיר. אֲכַל — קָם לֵיהּ בְּ״בַל יֹאכַל״. שְׁתָה — קָם לֵיהּ בְּ״בַל יִשְׁתֶּה״. אָמַר רָבָא: לַעֲבוֹר עָלָיו בִּשְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** with regard to transgressing the prohibition: **He shall not profane, in** the case of **nazirite** vows, **how can you find these** circumstances? **Once he said: I am hereby a nazirite, he is a nazirite** as of that moment. If he then **ate** grapes, **he is liable for** violating the prohibition: **He shall not eat** (see Numbers 6:4), and if he **drank** wine, **he is liable for** violating the prohibition: **He shall not drink** (see Numbers 6:3). When would he ever become liable for violating the prohibition against profanation? **Rava said:** The prohibition against profanation serves **to render him liable for** violating **two** prohibitions. Consequently, if he eats grapes or drinks wine, he transgresses the relevant prohibition in addition to the prohibition against profanation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״ דִּנְזִירוּת הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ הָוֵי לֵיהּ נָזִיר. אֲכַל — קָם לֵיהּ בְּ״בַל יֹאכַל״? בְּאוֹמֵר ״לִכְשֶׁאֶרְצֶה אֱהֵא נָזִיר״. וְאִי אָמַר ״כְּשֶׁאֶרְצֶה״ לֵיכָּא ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: With regard to violating the prohibition: **You shall not delay, in** the case of **nazirite** vows, **how can you find these** circumstances? **Once he said: I am hereby a nazirite, he is a nazirite** as of that moment. If he then **ate** grapes, **he is liable for** violating the prohibition: **He shall not eat.** When would he ever become liable for violating the prohibition against delaying? The Gemara answers: It is **when he** specifically **says: I will become a nazirite when I wish,** in which case he does not become a nazirite immediately. The Gemara asks: **But if he said: When I wish, there is no** prohibition of: **You shall not delay,** as there is no particular time by which he must become a nazirite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: כְּגוֹן דְּאָמַר ״לֹא אִיפָּטֵר מִן הָעוֹלָם עַד שֶׁאֱהֵא נָזִיר״, דְּמִן הָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הָוֵה לֵיהּ נָזִיר. מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה הָאוֹמֵר לְאִשְׁתּוֹ ״הֲרֵי זוֹ גִּיטֵּיךְ שָׁעָה אַחַת קוֹדֶם מִיתָתִי״ — אֲסוּרָה לֶאֱכוֹל בִּתְרוּמָה מִיָּד. אַלְמָא אָמְרִינַן כֹּל שַׁעְתָּא וְשַׁעְתָּא דִּילְמָא מָיֵית. הָכָא נָמֵי, לְאַלְתַּר הָוֵי נָזִיר. דְּאָמְרִינַן: דִּילְמָא הַשְׁתָּא מָיֵית.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is, **for example, when he said: I will not depart the world until I become a nazirite, as he is a nazirite from that time** because he does not know when he will depart this world. This is **just as it is** in the case of a man **who says to his wife: This is your bill of divorce** that will take effect **one hour before my death.** If he is a priest and she is the daughter of a non-priest, **she is prohibited from partaking of *teruma* immediately. Apparently, we say every moment** that **perhaps he is** now **dead** and she is therefore already divorced. **Here, too,** with regard to naziriteship, **he is a nazirite immediately, as we say** that **perhaps he is now** about to **die.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: כְּגוֹן דִּנְדַר וְהוּא בְּבֵית הַקְּבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said:** The prohibition against delaying applies to a nazirite in a case **where one vowed** to become a nazirite **while he was in a cemetery.** Since it is prohibited for a nazirite to be in a cemetery, his term of naziriteship does not come into effect until he leaves the cemetery and ritually purifies himself. If he delays this process, he prevents the vow from taking effect and thereby transgresses the prohibition against delaying.

The *Gemara* went further in the cemetery case: even if the *nezirut* does take effect in the cemetery, by remaining there one delays the transition from *nezirut d'tumah* — the nazirite state under conditions of impurity — to *nezirut d'taharah*, the proper nazirite state of purity. The implication is striking: *bal taachar* is not merely about initiating *nezirut*, but about initiating *nezirut* in its proper form. It is insufficient to have technically begun; one must begin correctly, and delay in achieving that proper beginning is itself a violation.

### Rav Ashi's Chiddush

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר לָא חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ נְזִירוּת מֵאַלְתַּר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מֵאַלְתַּר חָיְילָא עֲלֵיהּ, מִי אִיכָּא ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״? וְעוֹד: הָאָמַר מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי: חָיְילָא נְזִירוּת עֲלֵיהּ מֵאַלְתַּר, וְכִי פְּלִיגִי — לְעִנְיַן מַלְקוֹת פְּלִיגִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who says** that if one takes a vow of naziriteship while in a cemetery **the naziriteship does not apply to him immediately. However, according to the one who said** that the naziriteship **applies to him immediately, is there a** violation of the prohibition: **You shall not delay? And furthermore, didn't Mar bar Rav Ashi say** that all agree that the **naziriteship applies to him immediately** and he need not repeat his vow of naziriteship when he leaves, **and when they disagree** it is only **with regard to the matter of flogging,** and it's only about this that they **disagree,** i.e., whether the nazirite is flogged if he drinks wine or otherwise violates the prohibitions of a nazirite.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי קָם לֵיהּ בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״, מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מְאַחַר נְזִירוּת דְּטָהֳרָה. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הוֹאִיל וְכֵן, נָזִיר שֶׁטִּימֵּא עַצְמוֹ בְּמֵזִיד — עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״ דִּנְזִירוּת טָהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Even so, he is liable for** violating the prohibition: **You shall not delay, because** by remaining in the cemetery **he delays a naziriteship of ritual purity.** Although the *halakhot* of naziriteship take effect upon him immediately, he is still obligated to fulfill a term of naziriteship in a state of ritual purity, and if he delays doing so, he violates the prohibition against delaying. **Rav Ashi said: Since this is so, a nazirite who intentionally renders himself ritually impure violates** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** with regard to **a naziriteship of ritual purity,** as he thereby prevents himself from completing his naziriteship in a state of ritual purity on time.

Rav Ashi extends this principle further with a significant *chiddush*. He rules that a *nazir* who intentionally renders himself *tamei* — impure — transgresses not only the prohibition against a *nazir* becoming *tamei*, but also the prohibition of *bal taachar d'nezirut d'taharah*. The reason is that intentional *tumah* forces the *nazir* to lose all days counted until that point — *hayomim harishonim yiplu ki tamei nizro* — and to restart the entire count from the beginning after completing a process of purification. Since the entire period of *nezirut d'taharah* must effectively begin anew, the gap between the original declaration of *harei ani nazir* and the eventual restart of the proper count constitutes a postponement of the beginning of *nezirut d'taharah*. The *chiddush* is that this applies even though the *nazir* did formally begin his *nezirut* weeks earlier — since the count must be entirely redone, the original start effectively did not count, and the substantial gap between declaration and the re-commencement of the proper *nezirut* is treated as *bal taachar*.

## Giluach & Bal Taachar

### Rav Acha Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִיקָא אָמַר: עוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״ תִּגְלַחְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Ika, stated** another explanation of how the prohibition against delaying applies with regard to naziriteship: The nazirite is commanded to shave his hair at the conclusion of his term, and if he causes this shaving to be delayed, **he violates** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** with regard to **his shaving.**

Rav Acha son of Rav Ika offers a different account of what constitutes *bal taachar* in the context of *nezirut*. In his view, the violation consists of postponing the *tighluach* — the shaving of the head that marks the conclusion of *nezirut*. Here, the concern is not with delaying the *beginning* of *nezirut* but with delaying its *end*. Once the period of *nezirut* is complete, the *nazir* is obligated to shave his head and bring his sacrifices, and postponing this ritual constitutes a violation of *bal taachar*.

### Nature of Giluach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מִיבַּעְיָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תִּגְלַחַת מְעַכֶּבֶת, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תִּגְלַחַת אֵינָהּ מְעַכֶּבֶת, מִצְוַת גִּילּוּחַ מִיהָא לָא מְיקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **And** this explanation **is not necessary** to say, i.e., it is obvious, **according to the one who says** that **shaving is indispensable** for a nazirite and he is subject to all the prohibitions of a nazirite until he shaves, **but even according to the one who says** that **shaving is not indispensable** and the *halakhot* of naziriteship are terminated for him immediately after he brings his offerings, **at least** it can be said that **he does not fulfill the mitzva of shaving** until he actually shaves, and therefore if he delays, he violates the prohibition against delaying.

The *Gemara* notes the conceptual ambiguity surrounding *giluach*. One might view the shaving as an essential ritual that itself terminates the *nezirut*; according to this view, delaying *giluach* directly delays the conclusion of *nezirut*. Alternatively, one might understand *giluach* not as constitutive of the ending but rather as an independent *mitzvah* performed in the *Beis HaMikdash* at the close of *nezirut*. According to this second view, *Abaye* and *Rava* each rule in accordance with their respective positions: the one who holds that *giluach* is indispensable (*me'akev*) for ending *nezirut* would naturally say that postponing it is *bal taachar* of *nezirut* itself; while even the one who holds that *giluach* is *not* indispensable agrees that one transgresses *bal taachar* of the *mitzvah* of shaving by delaying it.

### Giluach Problem

This remains a conceptually strange category of *bal taachar*. Classic *bal taachar* involves the postponement of something given or consecrated to God — a freely accepted obligation in the mold of a *korban*. The *nezirut* itself can be understood as giving oneself to God (*l'hazir l'Hashem*). But postponing the ritual of *giluach* — which is either the act of ending *nezirut* or an accompanying ritual in the *Beis HaMikdash* — sits uneasily within this framework. It is not an act of giving to God; it is, at most, a procedural rite that concludes a previously accepted state. The *Gemara* acknowledges this difficulty and proceeds to consider a more intuitively natural account of *bal taachar* in the context of *nezirut*: the postponement of the *korbanot*.

## Korbanot & Bal Taachar

### Mar Zutra

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב מָרִי אָמַר: עוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״ קָרְבְּנוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Zutra, son of Rav Mari, stated** another explanation of how the prohibition against delaying applies to a nazirite vow: **He transgresses** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** with regard to **his offerings.** When he completes his term as a nazirite he is obligated to bring certain offerings, and if he delays bringing them, he violates this prohibition.

Mar Zutra son of Rav Ashi offers the most straightforward explanation: when we say that a *nazir* transgresses *bal taachar*, what we mean is that he has postponed bringing his *korbanot*. A *nazir* who has completed his *nezirut* is obligated to bring three sacrifices — an *olah*, a *chatat*, and a *shelamim* — and if he delays past three *regalim*, he violates *bal taachar*.

### Special vs. General

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִן הָכָא נָפְקָא לֵיהּ? מֵהָתָם נָפְקָא לֵיהּ: ״כִּי דָרֹשׁ יִדְרְשֶׁנּוּ״ — אֵלּוּ חַטָּאֹת וַאֲשָׁמוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this** *halakha*, that the prohibition against delaying applies to a nazirite vow, **derived from here,** i.e., the juxtaposition of vows and naziriteship? **It is derived from there,** a different exposition based upon the verse: "When you shall take a vow…you shall not delay to pay it; for the Lord your God **will surely require it** of you" (Deuteronomy 23:22). **Those** items included in the phrase "will surely require it" **are sin-offerings and guilt-offerings,** including the sin-offering and guilt-offering of a nazirite.

The *Gemara* immediately challenges this explanation: if *bal taachar* applies to all obligatory *korbanot* — a principle derivable from general *halacha* — why does the *Torah* need to link *nazir* to *neder* at all? The answer should already be obvious from the general rule. This pushes the *Gemara* to examine the textual derivation of *bal taachar* itself.

### Pesukim Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: חִידּוּשׁ הוּא שֶׁחִידְּשָׁה תּוֹרָה בְּנָזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The derivation based on the juxtaposition of vows and naziriteship is still necessary **lest you say that** these offerings are **a novelty that the Torah innovated with regard to a nazirite** and therefore their *halakhot* cannot be derived from *halakhot* of other offerings.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חִידּוּשׁ? אִילֵּימָא דְּלָא מַתְפֵּיס לַיהּ לְחַטַּאת נָזִיר בְּנֶדֶר. הֲרֵי חַטַּאת חֵלֶב, שֶׁאֵין מַתְפִּיסָהּ בְּנֶדֶר, וְעוֹבֵר בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the novelty** that the Torah introduced with regard to the nazirite? **If we say** it is **that he cannot accept** upon **himself the sin-offering of a nazirite through a vow,** i.e., if one who is not a nazirite says: I hereby vow to bring the sin-offering of a nazirite, his vow has no validity, this is not a unique *halakha* with regard to sin-offerings of a nazirite. There is also the case of **the sin-offering of forbidden fat,** i.e., a typical sin-offering that one brings when he inadvertently violates certain prohibitions, one of which is eating forbidden fat, **which one cannot accept through a vow,** as only one who has violated the prohibition may bring it; **and** nevertheless, **one** still **transgresses** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** if he delays bringing the offering.

The *Gemara* cites the relevant *pesukim*. The simple reading of the verse is *ki sidor neder l'Hashem Elokecha lo t'acher l'shalmo* — when you make a free-will vow to God, do not delay fulfilling it. The plain sense thus limits *bal taachar* to freely accepted obligations, *nedarim* and *nedavot*, not to *korbanot* that the *Torah* itself imposes as a consequence of sin or circumstance. However, the doubled language — *darosh yidreshenu* — extends the prohibition: God will *demand* it of you, implying that even *korbanot* that are demanded by the *Torah* (such as *chataot* and *ashamot*) fall under *bal taachar*. Once a *chiyuv korban* exists, whether arising from a freely accepted vow or from a Torah-imposed obligation, one who postpones it transgresses *bal taachar*. Given this general principle, of course there would be *bal taachar* for the *korbanot* of a *nazir* — no special derivation should be needed.

### Chatat Chelev

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַאי חִידּוּשֵׁיהּ — סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִם אָמַר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר״ אֲפִילּוּ מִן חַרְצָן — הָוֵי נָזִיר לַכֹּל, אֵימָא לָא לַיעֲבוֹר עֲלֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is its novelty** that makes the juxtaposition between vows and naziriteship necessary? It might **enter your mind to say** that **since if he said: I am hereby a nazirite, even** if he accepted upon himself only one of the prohibitions of a nazirite, e.g., the requirement to abstain **from grape seeds, he is** nevertheless **a nazirite with regard to all** aspects of naziriteship, therefore **say** that **he does not transgress** the prohibition: **You shall not delay.** Since this *halakha* is a novelty, perhaps the principles of vows do not apply. The verse therefore **teaches us** through the juxtaposition of vows and naziriteship that the prohibition against delaying applies even in this case.

The *Gemara* reinforces this point by invoking the *chatat chelev* — the sin-offering brought by one who inadvertently ate forbidden fat, a paradigmatic sin carrying *chiyuv karet*. One cannot bring a *chatat chelev* through a *neder*; it is only possible if one has actually committed the relevant sin. And yet there is clearly *bal taachar* by a *chatat chelev*. This proves that *bal taachar* applies to obligatory *korbanot* entirely outside the category of *neder*. Thus, the *nazir's korbanot* — being Torah-mandated obligations — should self-evidently fall under *bal taachar*, and we still have not identified what *chiddush* the linking of *nazir* to *neder* was meant to teach.

## Nazir Korban Chiddush

### Hypothetical Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר כִּי נָזִיר מִן חַרְצָן הָוֵי נָזִיר לַכֹּל, אֶלָּא לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן דְּאָמַר אֵין נָזִיר עַד שֶׁיַּזִּיר מִכּוּלָּן — מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר? וְעוֹד: הַאי חִידּוּשׁ לְחוּמְרָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **This works out well according to the one who said** that **when** one vows to be **a nazirite from grape seeds he is a nazirite in all** respects. **However, according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that **he is not a nazirite unless he** explicitly **vows to be a nazirite in all** respects, **what is there to say? And furthermore, this novelty is stringent** in that it renders the individual a nazirite even if he did not explicitly accept all of the *halakhot* of naziriteship. How could this novelty indicate that the prohibition against delaying does not apply in this case, which is a leniency, so that the juxtaposition would be necessary?

The *Gemara* now presses the question: what was the *chiddush* that the *Torah* needed to teach explicitly regarding *bal taachar* and the *korbanot* of a *nazir*? Two answers are proposed, each identifying a reason one might have thought that the *nazir's korbanot* specifically would *not* be subject to *bal taachar*.

### Package Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: מַאי חִידּוּשֵׁיהּ? דְּסָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִם גִּילַּח עַל אַחַת מִשְּׁלׇשְׁתָּן — יָצָא, לָא לַיעֲבוֹר עֲלֵיהּ בְּ״בַל תְּאַחֵר״ — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is its novelty** that makes the juxtaposition between vows and naziriteship necessary? It might **enter your mind to say** that **if he shaves upon** bringing **one of the three** offerings that a nazirite must bring when completing his term as a nazirite, i.e., a burnt-offering, a sin-offering, and a peace-offering, **he has fulfilled** his obligation to shave and the restrictions of a nazirite are lifted, **he** therefore **does not violate** the prohibition: **You shall not delay,** for delaying the other offerings. Consequently, **it teaches us** that the prohibition against delaying applies to these offerings.

The first answer: *nezirut* is an unusual, package-like category. The *Gemara* cites the ruling that if someone declares *hareini nazir min hachartzan* — I am a *nazir* from [eating] the pits of the grapes — he becomes a *nazir* in the full sense, obligated in all the restrictions of *nezirut*, even though he articulated only one component. The Rabbis and Rabbi Shimon debate what happens in such a case: the *Chachamim* hold that he acquires the entire package, while Rabbi Shimon holds that since he did not accept all the obligations, he acquires nothing. Crucially, both agree that *nezirut* operates as an indivisible package — nobody holds that one can be a *nazir* with respect to grapes alone. The debate is only over whether a partial acceptance triggers the full package or triggers nothing. Given this all-or-nothing structure, one might have argued that because *nezirut* is such an anomalous category, *bal taachar* would not apply to its *korbanot* in the ordinary way. The *Gemara*, however, rejects the logical force of this reasoning: the strange, encompassing nature of *nezirut* is not a reason to conclude that one would not transgress *bal taachar* for postponing its *korbanot*.

### Weighting Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מַאי חִדּוּשֵׁיהּ — מִשּׁוּם דְּלֹא מַתְפִּיסוֹ בְּנֶדֶר. וְהָא דְּקָא קַשְׁיָא לָךְ חַטַּאת חֵלֶב — חַטַּאת חֵלֶב קָאָתְיָא לְכַפָּרָה. חַטַּאת נָזִיר לְמַאי אָתְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish say: What is the novelty** of the nazirite? The *halakha* of naziriteship is considered a novelty **because** the offerings of a nazirite **cannot be accepted through a vow. And that which** poses **a difficulty for you** based on the fact that one cannot accept upon himself **a sin-offering of forbidden fat** through a vow either, and nevertheless the prohibition against delaying applies, can be resolved. **A sin-offering of forbidden fat comes for** the purpose of **atonement,** and therefore if one delays bringing it he violates the prohibition against delaying. However, **for what does the sin-offering of a nazirite come?** Since it does not come to atone for a sin, one might have thought that the prohibition against delaying does not apply.

The second and more compelling answer is introduced: *hanicha l'man d'amar i gilach al achat mi-shloshtan yatza* — since a *nazir* who performs the *giluach* in conjunction with any one of the three *korbanot* may be considered to have discharged the essential end of his *nezirut*, the remaining two *korbanot* have a diminished sense of urgency. Having in some sense already "finished," there is less felt pressure to bring the remaining *korbanot* promptly. One might therefore have thought that the obligation to bring these remaining sacrifices lacks sufficient weight to generate a *bal taachar* violation upon delay.

### Lack of Urgency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַהֲרֵי חַטַּאת יוֹלֶדֶת דְּלָא אָתְיָא לְכַפָּרָה, וְעָבַר עֲלַהּ מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תְּאַחֵר״! הָהִיא — קָא שָׁרְיָא לַהּ לְמֵיכַל בְּקָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** there is the precedent of **the sin-offering of a woman who gave birth, which** also **does not come for** the purpose of **atonement, and** yet if she does not bring the offering on time she still **transgresses due to** the prohibition: **You shall not delay.** The Gemara answers: **That** sin-offering of a woman who gave birth **permits** the woman **to eat consecrated food.** Although it does not come to effect atonement, it does come to permit a matter. On the other hand, the sin-offering of a nazirite does not permit anything, and therefore the fact that one cannot accept upon himself an obligation to bring this offering is a novelty. Consequently, it was necessary for there to be a separate source to indicate that one is liable for violating the prohibition against delaying in the case of this offering.

This line of reasoning is further sharpened by comparing the *nazir's korbanot* to other obligatory *korbanot*. When one sins and is obligated to bring a *chatat* for *kaparah* — atonement — there is an urgent underlying pressure: one is in a state of unremedied sin and needs *kaparah*. When one is *tamei* and needs to bring a *korban* to return to a state of *taharah*, there is again an urgent underlying pressure: the desire — at least conceptually — to be able to eat *kodashim* and enter the *Beis HaMikdash*. Both of these cases involve not merely a formal *chiyuv* but an existential pressure that makes the postponement genuinely weighty. The *nazir's* remaining *korbanot*, by contrast, involve no such urgency. There is no sin requiring atonement — even the *chatat nazir*, while it exists, is best understood (as the *Rambam* explains) not as atonement per se but as a change of personal status — and the *nezirut* has in some sense already concluded. One might therefore have thought that the obligation to bring the remaining *korbanot* is insufficiently weighty to generate *bal taachar*, since postponing them involves no deferred *kaparah* and no unresolved *tumah*. The *Torah's* linkage of *nazir* to *neder* therefore teaches — *kamashma lan* — that even these relatively "weightless" obligatory *korbanot* are subject to *bal taachar*.

A second formulation reinforces the same conclusion from a different angle: since the *korbanot* of a *nazir* cannot be created through a *neder* — one cannot say *harei zo alai chatat nazir* and thereby obligate oneself independently, since the *chatat nazir* is only available to one who is actually a *nazir* under *chiyuv* — one might have thought that since *bal taachar* is conceptually rooted in the domain of *neder*, it would not apply to a *korban* so entirely outside that domain. The *Gemara* responds that a *chatat chelev* similarly cannot arise through a *neder* and yet clearly carries *bal taachar*. The rejoinder, however, is that a *chatat chelev* carries *kaparah* as its purpose, and that urgency of *kaparah* is what grants it sufficient weight for *bal taachar*. The *chatat nazir*, which is about change of status rather than *kaparah*, lacks this urgency. The *chiddush* is thus that even such a *korban* — neither arising from *neder* nor serving *kaparah* — is subject to *bal taachar*.

### Chatat Yoledes

The *Gemara* raises a parallel case: the *chatat yoledes*, the sin-offering brought by a woman after childbirth. This *korban* involves no *neder*, no sin, and no *kaparah* in the conventional sense — it is a *korban* of status change, brought to return the *yoledes* from a state of *tumah* to *taharah*. Yet the *Gemara* affirms that there is *bal taachar* for the *chatat yoledes* — and explains this on the basis of Rabbi Yishmael's principle: there is an urgency to becoming *tahor*, because once one is *tahor* one regains the capacity to eat *korbanot* and to enter the *Beis HaMikdash*. Even if these practical privileges are rarely exercised, the conceptual importance of *tahara* — the potential to eat *kodashim*, the ability to access the *Beis HaMikdash* — creates a genuine pressure to resolve the *tumah* promptly. This urgency is sufficient to ground *bal taachar*, even without *neder* or *kaparah*. The *nazir's korbanot*, by contrast, carry no such urgency — not the urgency of *kaparah*, not the urgency of resolving *tumah* — and the *chiddush* that they nonetheless carry *bal taachar* is therefore genuinely novel. The *Gemara* thus arrives at a clear formulation of why a special derivation was necessary: the *korbanot* of a *nazir* are the weakest possible case of an obligatory *korban*, with the least urgency behind them, and the *Torah* teaches that *bal taachar* applies to them nonetheless.

## Annulment Powers

### Baraita Linkage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: וּמָה נְדָרִים, הָאָב מֵיפֵר נִדְרֵי בִתּוֹ וּבַעַל מֵיפֵר נִדְרֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ — אַף נְזִירוּת, הָאָב מֵיפֵר נְזִירוּת בִּתּוֹ וּבַעַל מֵיפֵר נְזִירוּת אִשְׁתּוֹ. לְמָה לִי הֶיקֵּשָׁא? תֵּיתֵי בְּ״מָה מָצִינוּ״ מִנְּדָרִים!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Master said** in the *baraita* cited above: **Just as** with regard to **vows, a father may nullify the vows of his daughter and a husband may nullify the vows of his wife, so too,** with regard to **nazirite** vows, **a father may nullify the nazirite** vows **of his daughter and a husband may nullify the nazirite** vows **of his wife.** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **the juxtaposition** in the verse to teach us this *halakha*? **Let** this *halakha* **come through** the method of analogy known as: **What do we find, from** the *halakha* with regard to **vows.** In other words, the *halakha* in the case of regular vows should serve as a legal precedent that can be applied to nazirite vows even without a biblical juxtaposition.

The *baraita* states the second principle derived from the linking of *nazir* to *neder*: just as a father may annul his daughter's vows and a husband may annul his wife's vows, so too a father may annul his daughter's *nezirut* and a husband may annul his wife's *nezirut*. The *Gemara* asks: why is an explicit textual linkage necessary? One might have derived this by analogy — *bemah matzinu* — directly from the laws of *nedarim*.

### Nezirut as Neder Type

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּילְמָא גַּבֵּי נְדָרִים הוּא דְּמֵיפַר, מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אִית לֵיהּ קִיצּוּתָא. אֲבָל גַּבֵּי נְזִירוּת דְּאִית לֵיהּ קִיצּוּתָא, דִּסְתַם נְזִירוּת שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, אֵימָא לָא — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: If not for the juxtaposition, one might have said that **perhaps** it is only **with regard to vows that he may nullify** her statements **because there is no** time **limit** with regard to vows, **but with regard to naziriteship, which has a** time **limit, as unspecified naziriteship is** for **thirty days,** one might **say no,** he may not nullify her vows. Therefore, **it teaches us** through juxtaposition that he may nevertheless nullify her vows.

The force of the question is this: *nezirut* is not a separate category parallel to *nedarim* — it is a *type* of *neder*. If that is so, there are not two independent categories (apples and oranges) between which a *gezeirah shavah* or textual link is needed. Rather, *nezirut* is a subset of *nedarim*, the way a specific variety is a subset of a broader category. If annulment applies to *nedarim*, it should self-evidently apply to this particular form of *neder* without requiring any additional derivation.

### Time Limit Defense

The *Gemara* responds: one might have thought to distinguish *nezirut* from ordinary *nedarim* on the following grounds. An ordinary *neder* has no intrinsic time limit; by default it is a permanent change of status, and therefore the *Torah* granted the father and husband the power to annul lest someone remain bound indefinitely. *Nezirut*, by contrast, has a built-in time limit — standard *nezirut* is thirty days — and might therefore have been excluded from the annulment power, on the reasoning that it will expire on its own shortly regardless. The textual linkage thus teaches that annulment applies to *nezirut* as well, despite its time-bounded nature.

### Neder Permanence

The *Gemara* briefly addresses the converse possibility: why not learn *in reverse* and say that since *nezirut* is thirty days, perhaps all *nedarim* default to thirty days? The answer lies in the conceptual model underlying *nedarim*. A *neder* operates on the paradigm of *hekdesh* — sanctification. When one consecrates an object as a *korban*, the change of status is permanent; there is no inherent clock on sanctification. Since a *neder* functions by changing the status of an object (rendering it forbidden as though it were sanctified), the default model of a *neder* is a permanent, open-ended change of status. The time-limited character of *nezirut* is thus a feature specific to that institution, not a general model for *nedarim*.

## Hamudar Hanaah

### Mishna Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ מוּדָּר אֲנִי וְכוּ׳. אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּכוּלָּן עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר, ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״, ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** § It is taught in the mishna with regard to **one who says to his fellow: I am avowed** from you, or another intimation of a vow, that the vow takes effect. **Shmuel said: In all** these cases, the vow does not take effect **until he says:** I am avowed from you with regard to **that** which **I eat** of **yours,** or: **That** which **I taste** of **yours.** Without this addition, the statement: I am avowed from you, is not considered even an intimation of a vow.

The *Gemara* now transitions to a new section of the *Mishnah*: the case of *hamudar hana'ah machaveiro* — one who has taken a vow prohibiting himself from deriving benefit from another person. This section of the *Mishnah* is understood by the *Gemara* as a presentation of *yadim* — abbreviated statements that function as effective vows.

### Statement Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״מוּדָּר אֲנִי מִמָּךְ״ ״מוּפְרְשַׁנִי מִמָּךְ״ ״מְרוּחָקֵנִי מִמָּךְ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר. ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״ ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** based on the following *baraita*: If one says: **I am avowed from you,** or: **I am separated from you,** or: **I am distanced from you, he is prohibited** from benefiting from that individual. If he says: **That** which **I eat** of **yours,** or: **That** which **I taste** of **yours, he is prohibited** from eating or tasting that individual's food. This indicates that the phrases mentioned in the first clause of the *baraita* take effect as vows even if he does not add the phrases mentioned in the latter clause.

The *Mishnah* lists several formulations that constitute effective prohibitions: *mudarani mimcha* ("I am vowed away from you"), *mofreshani mimcha* ("I am separated from you"), *she'ani ochel lach* ("the things I would eat of yours"), and *she'ani to'em lach* ("the things I would taste of yours"). Each of these is an abbreviated formulation — none states the full content of a *neder* — yet each is legally effective.

### Statement Components

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָתָנֵי: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּאוֹמֵר: ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״ ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This is what** the *baraita* **is teaching: In what** case **is this statement said** i.e., if one says: I am avowed from you, or the like, the vow takes effect? It is **in** a case of **one who says: That** which **I eat** of **yours,** or: **That** which **I taste** of **yours.**

To understand why these are *yadim*, it is useful to analyze the components of a complete *neder* formulation. A full statement would specify: the object (e.g., "your food"), the relationship of the speaker to the object (e.g., "to me"), and the legal status of the object (e.g., *assur* — forbidden — or *konam* — like a *korban*). A *neder* operates by changing the status of an object through a declaration modeled on *hekdesh*: "Your food is *konam* — like a *korban* — to me." This yields three elements: the object, the relationship, and the status. The formulations in the *Mishnah* each supply only one or two of these elements, leaving the remainder implicit. *Mudarani mimcha* indicates the relationship of separation but does not specify the object. *She'ani ochel lach* specifies the object class (food) but does not supply the status word. Neither supplies the word *assur* or *konam*, which explicitly establishes the forbidden status. Yet because these formulations occur within a recognizable context of *neder*-making, the missing elements are understood as implicit — and that is precisely the function of *yad*.

The *Ran* observes that the phrase *she'ani ochel lach* avoids ambiguity because the particle *she-* marks it as a definite reference — "that which I would eat of yours" — rather than an invitation. If someone meant to say "I would like to eat with you," he would say *ani ochel lach*, not *she'ani ochel lach*. The *she-* construction signals that the speaker is identifying an object class — the food he would eat — and intending to change its status. That is the *yad*: the object is indicated, and the context of *neder*-making supplies the rest.

## Shmuel's Position

### Shmuel's Rule

Shmuel states: *mudarani mimcha* alone accomplishes nothing — it is not even a *yad*. One must say *she'ani ochel lach* or *she'ani to'em lach*. By itself, *mudarani mimcha* is too vague to function as an abbreviated *neder*.

### Braitta Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא אִיפְּכָא: ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״, ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״ — אָסוּר, ״מוּדְּרַנִי מִמָּךְ״ וּ״מוּפְרְשַׁנִי מִמָּךְ״ ״מְרוּחֲקַנִי מִמָּךְ״ — הֲרֵי זֶה אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* in **the opposite** order? If one says: **That** which **I eat** of **yours,** or: **That** which **I taste** of **yours, he is prohibited** from eating or tasting that individual's food, i.e., the vow takes effect. If he says: **I am avowed from you, or: I am separated from you,** or: **I am distanced from you, he is** likewise **prohibited.** In this case, the *baraita* cannot be interpreted as explained above, and it appears that if one said: I am avowed from you, the vow takes effect even if he did not say: I am avowed from you with regard to that which I taste of yours.

The *Gemara* objects from a *baraita*: *mudarani mimcha*, *menudeh ani mimcha*, *mishamtani ani mimcha* — *harei zeh assur*. The *baraita* rules that even the "separation" formulation alone is sufficient to create a prohibition. Similarly, *she'ani ochel lach*, *she'ani to'em lach* — *harei zeh assur* — even just specifying the object class alone is forbidden. Either phrase alone, the *baraita* says, constitutes an effective *yad*. A second *baraita* that reverses the order of the phrases further forecloses the interpretation that the two phrases must be used together: it is clear that each phrase independently creates a prohibition.

### Reading Narrowly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ רֵישָׁא! וְעוֹד: ״אָסוּר״ ״אָסוּר״ לְמָה לֵיהּ לְמִתְנֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as **the first** *baraita* cited above. **And furthermore,** if the vow takes effect only if he combined the statements mentioned in the first and latter clauses of the *baraita*, why does the *baraita* **teach** that he is **prohibited** in the first clause of the *baraita* and then repeat that he is **prohibited** in the second clause of the *baraita*? The repetition of this ruling indicates that these are two separate cases.

After multiple attempts to harmonize Shmuel's statement with the *baraitot* — first by suggesting that the *baraita* describes the combination of both phrases, then by noting that the second *baraita* reverses the order, making such harmonization impossible — the *Gemara* ultimately reinterprets Shmuel. The *Gemara* reads Shmuel's statement not as ruling that *mudarani mimcha* alone is ineffective in all circumstances, but as addressing the differential legal effect of the phrases. Specifically: *she-k'var amar mudani* — when one has already said *mudarani mimcha* and then adds *she'ani ochel lach*. The reinterpretation shifts Shmuel from a ruling about validity to a ruling about scope.

## Shmuel's Distinction

### Both Phrases Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״ ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״, הוּא דְּאָסוּר וַחֲבֵרוֹ מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Shmuel** actually **said** as follows: **The reason is** that **he said:** I am avowed from you with regard to **that** which **I eat** of **yours,** or with regard to **that** which **I taste** of **yours;** it is for this reason **that he is prohibited** from benefiting from his fellow, **and his fellow is permitted** to benefit from him, as this formulation indicates that he is applying his vow only to himself.

Once the *Gemara* has reinterpreted Shmuel, his actual ruling comes into focus. Shmuel holds that when someone says *both* phrases — *mudarani mimcha* together with *she'ani ochel lach* — the result is that only he himself is forbidden from eating the other's food: *hu d'assur v'chavero mutar*. He has specified the object (the food he would eat from the other) and indicated his separation from it; the vow is therefore directed and limited — his eating of the other's food is forbidden, but the other's eating of his food is not affected.

### One Phrase Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אָמַר ״מוּדְּרַנִי הֵימָךְ״ לְחוֹדֵיהּ — שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין. כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: ״מוּדְּרַנִי הֵימָךְ״ — שְׁנֵיהֶם אֲסוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **However,** if **he said only: I am avowed from you, they are both prohibited** from deriving benefit from one another. This is **like that which Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said:** If one says: **I am avowed from you, they are both prohibited.**

When, however, one says only *mudarani mimcha* — "I am separated from you" — without specifying the object, the result is different: *shneihem asurim*, both parties are forbidden to one another. This ruling is supported by Rav Yosef bar Chanina: *mudarani mimcha* — *shneihem asurim*.

### Filling Blanks Logic

The logic of this distinction is the logic of filling in blanks. When both phrases are used together, the second phrase (*she'ani ochel lach*) narrows and specifies the meaning of the first: the separation is with respect to "the food I would eat of yours," i.e., your food is forbidden to me. The *neder* is thus directed and unilateral. When only the first phrase is used — "I am separated from you" — no object is specified, and the court must fill in what "separation" means. Separation, in its unqualified sense, is bidirectional: I am separated from you and you from me. Since there is nothing to indicate that the speaker meant only one direction, the prohibition is interpreted to run both ways — your food is forbidden to me, and my food is forbidden to you. This is not the speaker imposing a *neder* on the other person; rather, the speaker is understood to have changed the status of all items relevant to the relationship of benefit between them, in both directions. Should the other party then take food from the speaker's table and eat it, it is primarily the speaker who has transgressed the *neder* by allowing the prohibited benefit to flow.

## Sheani Ochel Yad

### Girsa Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אֶלָּא הָכִי אִתְּמַר: טַעְמָא דְּאָמַר ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״ וְ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״ הוּא דְּאֵין הוּא אָסוּר אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה. הָא ״מוּדְּרַנִי מִמְּךָ״ — אָסוּר אֲפִילּוּ בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, this is** how Shmuel's opinion **was stated: The reason is** that he said: **That** which **I eat** of **yours, or: That** which **I taste** of **yours.** It is for this reason **that he is prohibited only from eating** anything belonging to the other person. However, if he said: **I am avowed from you,** without further specification, **he is prohibited even from** deriving any form of **benefit** from the other.

A textual question arises regarding the phrase in the *Mishnah*: should the reading be *she'ani ochel lach* ("that which I eat of yours") or *she'eini ochel lach* ("I will not eat of yours")? The distinction carries significant legal weight.

### Reinterpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, לֵימָא שְׁמוּאֵל הָכִי: וְאִם לָא אָמַר אֶלָּא ״שֶׁאֲנִי אוֹכֵל לָךְ״ וְ״שֶׁאֲנִי טוֹעֵם לָךְ״, אֵין אָסוּר אֶלָּא בַּאֲכִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, let Shmuel say as follows: And if he said only: That** which **I eat** of **yours, or: That** which **I taste** of **yours, he is prohibited only from eating** an item belonging to his fellow, but he is permitted to derive benefit from it.

If the correct *girsa* is *she'eini ochel lach* — "I will not eat of yours" — then the *Ran* observes that one appears to have stated a complete sentence. There is ostensibly nothing missing. Yet the *Ran* argues that precisely *here* lies the *yad*: what the speaker has formulated is not a *neder* but a *shvuah*. A *neder* is object-based — it changes the status of an object — while a *shvuah* is action-based and person-based, restricting what the person will do. "I will not eat of yours" is formulated as an oath about the speaker's behavior, not as a declaration about the status of the food. The function of *yad* here is therefore not to supply a missing element of a *neder* formulation, but to *reinterpret* a statement that was phrased as a *shvuah* and treat it as though it were a *neder*. The abbreviated or hybrid nature of the statement — its blending of *shvuah*-language with *neder*-context — is what makes it a *yad*: we read "I will not eat of yours" as "the food of yours that I would eat is forbidden to me."

### Practical Application

This interpretive mechanism creates a noteworthy practical consequence. If *yad* can reinterpret an action-oriented statement as an object-oriented prohibition, then a person who uses *shvuah* language in a *neder* context cannot escape the *neder* simply by pointing out that he formulated it as an oath rather than a vow. The *yad* will do its work and convert the formulation into a *neder* regardless. This principle, taken to its logical conclusion, closes what might otherwise appear to be a convenient loophole: one who impulsively declares "I will never again drive on this road" cannot easily argue that since the formulation sounds like a *shvuah*, no *neder* was created. If the context is one of *neder*-making, the *yad* reinterprets accordingly — and the road itself becomes, in some sense, *assur alav*.
