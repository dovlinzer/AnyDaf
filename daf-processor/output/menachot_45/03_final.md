# Menachot 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'akvin Overview

### Topic Transition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפָּרִים וְהָאֵילִים וְהַכְּבָשִׂים וְהַשְּׂעִירִים – אֵינָן מְעַכְּבִין זֶה אֶת זֶה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיוּ לָהֶם פָּרִים מְרוּבִּים, וְלֹא הָיוּ לָהֶם נְסָכִים – יָבִיא פַּר אֶחָד וּנְסָכִים, וְלֹא יַקְרִיבוּ כּוּלָּם בְּלֹא נְסָכִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Failure to sacrifice one of **the bulls, the rams, the sheep,** which are all brought as burnt offerings, **or the goat** that is brought as a sin offering, **does not prevent** the sacrifice of **the others. Rabbi Shimon says: If** the Temple treasurers **had** sufficient funds for the **numerous bulls** that are required to be sacrificed on that day **but they did not** also **have** sufficient funds for the accompanying **libations,** they should rather **bring one bull and** its **libations, and they should not sacrifice all of them without libations.**

Today's daf, Menachot 45, picks up with the *mishna* at the bottom of 44b. Having concluded the extended discussion of *tefillin*, *sefer torah*, *mezuzah*, and *tzitzis*, the tractate returns to the world of *korbanot* — though *menachos* will continue to figure prominently. We are now in a section organized around the principle of *me'akvin zeh et zeh* or *ein me'akvin zeh et zeh*: when two components of a *mitzvah* or *korban* are brought together, does the absence of one invalidate or prevent the other?

### Me'akvin Principle

This framework was already present in the previous *mishna*, which addressed *minchas nesachim* — the flour, oil, and wine that accompany animal *korbanot*. The present *mishna* extends the analysis to the various animals of the *musafim*, the additional festival sacrifices, though *menachos* will continue to surface throughout the discussion.

---

## Hapirim V'Ha'eilim

### Mishna Text

The *mishna* states: *hapirim veha'eilim vehakevasim ein me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the bulls, the rams, and the sheep do not prevent one another. The context is a *musaf* offering that includes a combination of all three types of animals. If some animals are unavailable, those that are on hand are still brought. As the *gemara* will clarify, this applies not only across species — that missing rams does not prevent bringing bulls — but even within a species: if the full complement of a given animal cannot be obtained, whatever is available is still offered.

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rabbi Shimon* offers a divergent position. If the congregation had enough funds to purchase the full number of bulls — say, fourteen for *Sukkot* — but doing so would leave nothing for the *minchas nesachim*, the flour, oil, and wine that accompany each animal, he rules: *yavvi par echad venesachav velo yakrivu kulam belo nesachim* — it is better to bring one bull together with its *nesachim* than to bring all of them without any *nesachim* at all. In *Rabbi Shimon*'s view, the animal together with its *nesachim* constitutes a complete, unified offering. An animal brought without its *nesachim* is, as it were, a second-rate offering. Therefore, it is preferable to offer one complete unit than to offer many incomplete ones.

## Which Korban?

### Sukkot Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָנֵי פָּרִים וּכְבָשִׂים דְּהֵיכָא? אִילֵּימָא דְּחַג – ״כַּמִּשְׁפָּט״, ״כְּמִשְׁפָּטָם״ כְּתִיב בְּהוּ! אֶלָּא דְּרֹאשׁ חֹדֶשׁ וַעֲצֶרֶת דְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: With regard to **these bulls,** rams, **and sheep** mentioned in the mishna, **on which** festival are they offered? **If we say** that these are the offerings **of the festival** of *Sukkot*, this is difficult: **It is written with regard to those** days that their offerings must be brought: **"According to the ordinance"** (see, e.g., Numbers 29:18), and: **"According to their ordinance"** (Numbers 29:33). This indicates that no deviation from the Torah's prescription is possible. **Rather,** the mishna must be referring to the two bulls, one ram, and seven sheep **of the New Moon and *Shavuot*,** as mentioned in **the book of Numbers** (28:11, 27).

The *gemara* opens by asking: *hanei parim uchevasim deheicha* — which *korbanot* does the *mishna* have in mind? Since the context is a *musaf* with multiple bulls, rams, and sheep, the *gemara* must identify a *musaf* with plurals of all three. *Sukkot* is immediately excluded: the Torah uses the phrase *kemishpat* or *kemishpatam* in connection with the *Sukkot musaf*, which the *gemara* understands to mandate the full complement of every animal. *Sukkot* is thus an exception to the *ein me'akvin* rule, even though the *mishna* does not state this explicitly.

### Rosh Chodesh & Shavuot

The *gemara* therefore identifies the relevant *korbanot* as those of *rosh chodesh* and *shavuot* as recorded in *Bamidbar* — referred to by the *gemara* as *Chumash HaPikudim*, the Book of Numbers. Other holidays are not mentioned because, as *Tosafot* notes, the *gemara* is looking for a *musaf* that features genuine plurals of all three animal types simultaneously. *Pesach*, *Rosh Hashanah*, *Yom Kippur*, and *Shemini Atzeret* each have only a single ram and a single bull, so they do not fit the *mishna*'s language of *parim*, *eilim*, and *kevasim* in the plural.

### Multiple Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵילִים דְּהֵיכָא? אִי דְּהָנְהוּ – דְּאַיִל הוּא, אִי דַּעֲצֶרֶת דְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים – הֲוָיָה כְּתִיב בְּהוּ. לְעוֹלָם דַּעֲצֶרֶת דְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: לָא אֵילִים דְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים מְעַכְּבִי לֵיהּ לְאַיִל דְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים, וָלֹא אַיִל דְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים מְעַכֵּב לְהוּ לְאֵילִים דְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: The mishna mentioned **rams,** in plural; **on which** festival are multiple rams offered? **If** the mishna is referring to the additional offerings sacrificed **on those** days of the new moon and *Shavuot* as prescribed in the book of Numbers, these offerings include only one **ram** and not two. And **if** it is referring to the two rams **of *Shavuot*** that accompany the two loaves, as prescribed **in Leviticus,** a term of **being is written about them** in the verse: "They shall be a burnt offering to the Lord" (Leviticus 23:18). This term indicates that the offerings must be sacrificed exactly as prescribed in order to be valid. Consequently, one may not sacrifice fewer than two rams. **Actually,** it is referring to the two rams **of *Shavuot*** that are prescribed **in Leviticus, and this** is what the mishna **is saying:** Failure to sacrifice the **rams** that accompany the two loaves, as prescribed **in Leviticus, does not prevent** the sacrifice of the **ram** of the additional offering prescribed **in the book of Numbers.** Similarly, failure to sacrifice the **ram** of the additional offering, prescribed **in the book of Numbers, does not prevent** the sacrifice of the **rams** that accompany the two loaves, as prescribed **in Leviticus.**

Even *rosh chodesh* and *shavuot* do not fully satisfy this requirement. The *gemara* examines the relevant verses: for *rosh chodesh* the Torah states *parim b'nei vakar shnayim ve'ayil echad kevasim b'nei shana shiva temimim* — two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. For *shavuot* in *Bamidbar* (*Pinchas*), the formulation is identical: two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. In neither case does one find multiple rams. Where, then, does a *musaf*-like offering with multiple rams appear?

### Emor Passage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, פָּרִים – דַּאֲפִילּוּ אַהֲדָדֵי לָא מְעַכְּבִי, וְאֵילִים – דְּהָכָא וּדְהָכָא הוּא דְּלָא מְעַכְּבִי, אִינְהוּ מְעַכְּבִי. תַּנָּא מִילֵּי מִילֵּי קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** if that is the explanation, then when the mishna mentions **bulls** and sheep it means **that even** the bulls or sheep of the additional offering prescribed in the book of Numbers **do not prevent each other** from being sacrificed, i.e., the inability to sacrifice one of the bulls or sheep does not prevent one from sacrificing the rest. **But** when the mishna mentions **rams, it is the** rams mentioned **here** in Leviticus **that do not prevent** sacrifice of the rams mentioned **there,** in Numbers, and vice versa; but the failure to sacrifice one of **those** rams in Leviticus **does prevent** sacrifice of the other. Consequently, although the mishna mentions bulls, rams, and sheep together, the *halakha* is not the same with regard to these different animals. The Gemara responds: The ***tanna*** of the mishna **teaches each statement** individually, i.e., the *halakha* applies to each of the animals listed in a different manner.

The *gemara* directs attention to *parashat Emor* in *Vayikra*, where the Torah presents a special *olah* brought together with the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot*: *shiv'at kevasim temimim b'nei shana u'far ben bakar echad ve'eilim shnayim yihyu olah la'Hashem* — seven lambs, one bull, and two rams. Here, finally, is a context with multiple rams.

### Korban Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וּבְיוֹם הַחֹדֶשׁ (תִּקַּח) פַּר בֶּן בָּקָר תָּמִים וְשִׁשָּׁה כְּבָשִׂים וָאַיִל תְּמִימִים יִהְיוּ״, ״פַּר״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר?
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites a *baraita* with regard to the offerings sacrificed on the New Moon: The verse states: **"And on the day of the new moon, a young bull without blemish; and six lambs, and a ram; they shall be without blemish"** (Ezekiel 46:6) The *baraita* asks: **Why** does **the verse state "a bull"** when the verse in the Torah requires two bulls, as it is stated: "And on your New Moons you shall present a burnt offering to the Lord: Two young bulls, and one ram, seven lambs of the first year without blemish" (Numbers 28:11)?

This passage, however, reveals a key discrepancy. The *musaf* of *Shavuot* in *Pinchas* reads: two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. The special *olah* of *Emor* reads: one bull, two rams, seven sheep. The counts of bulls and rams are inverted. The *gemara* concludes that these cannot be the same *korban*. The offering in *Emor* is a distinct *korban* — specifically the *olah* connected to the *shtei halechem* — and is separate from the standard *musaf* listed in *Pinchas*.

### Eilim Resolution

The *mishna*'s statement regarding rams. *Lo eilim d'Toras Kohanim me'akvei lei l'ayil d'Chumash HaPikudim v'lo ayil d'Chumash HaPikudim me'akvei lehu l'eilim d'Toras Kohanim* — the two rams of the special *olah* in *Vayikra* do not prevent the single ram of the *musaf* in *Bamidbar*, and conversely. These belong to two distinct *korbanot* that operate independently of one another. It is important to note, however, that internally to the *olah* of *Emor* — which uses the language *yihyu*, "shall be" — all the animals are required and are indeed *me'akev* one another; the *mishna*'s *ein me'akvin* applies only to the relationship between the two separate *korbanot*.

### Different Scenarios

This produces a somewhat jarring reading of the *mishna*, and the *gemara* acknowledges it directly: *tani mili mili katani* — the *mishna* is speaking to different scenarios in different clauses. When it says the bulls and the sheep are *ein me'akvin zeh et zeh*, it refers to the *musaf* itself — even within the *musaf*, if not all the animals of a given species are available, whatever can be brought is brought, and the missing animals do not invalidate the rest. When it speaks of the rams, however, it refers to something entirely different: the relationship between the ram of the *musaf* in *Bamidbar* and the rams of the *korban* associated with the *shtei halechem* in *Vayikra*. The *mishna* thus addresses two distinct halakhic scenarios under a single heading. This is admittedly a complex reading, but it is the *gemara*'s resolution of what would otherwise be an intractable textual problem.

## Yechezkel Difficult Texts

### Yechezkel 46:6

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְפִי שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר בַּתּוֹרָה ״פָּרִים״, וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא שְׁנַיִם מֵבִיא אֶחָד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״פַּר״. ״שִׁשָּׁה כְּבָשִׂים״ – מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְפִי שֶׁכָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה ״שִׁבְעָה״, וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא שִׁבְעָה יָבִיא שִׁשָּׁה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״שִׁשָּׁה״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: **Since it is stated in the Torah** with regard to the offering of the New Moon: "Two young **bulls,"** one might think that it is not acceptable to bring fewer than two bulls under any circumstances. **From where** is it derived **that if** one **did not find two** bulls, **he brings one?** Therefore, **the verse states:** "A young **bull,"** in the singular, to teach that even if one has only one bull it should be sacrificed. The *baraita* discusses the continuation of the verse in Ezekiel, which mentions **"six lambs." Why** does **the verse state** only six lambs when the verse in the Torah requires seven? The *baraita* answers: **Since it is stated in the Torah** with regard to the offering of the New Moon: **"Seven** lambs," one might think that it is not acceptable to bring fewer than seven lambs under any circumstances. **From where** is it derived **that if** one **did not find seven** lambs, **he should bring six? Therefore, the verse** in Ezekiel **states: "Six** lambs," to teach that in the absence of all seven lambs one should sacrifice six.

Having established the basic rules of *me'akvin*, the *gemara* turns to derive and reinforce these *halachos* from the prophecies of *Yechezkel*, while simultaneously resolving passages in *Yechezkel* that appear to contradict the Torah. The first such passage is *Yechezkel* 46:6, which describes the *rosh chodesh* sacrifice of the future *Beis Hamikdash*: *par ben bakar temimim ve'sheses kevasim ve'ayil tamim yihyu* — one bull, six sheep, and one ram. This stands in direct tension with the Torah's prescription of two bulls and seven sheep for *rosh chodesh*.

### Fewer Animals

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by reading *Yechezkel*'s reduced numbers as halakhic instruction. The verse says *par* — a single bull — even though the Torah mandates two. This teaches: *u'minayin she'im lo matza shnayim mevi echad talmud lomar par* — how do we know that if two bulls cannot be obtained, one may be brought? *Yechezkel* tells us so. Similarly, the verse says *sheses kevasim* — six sheep, rather than the seven mandated by the Torah. This teaches that if seven cannot be obtained, six are brought. And the next verse continues: *ve'lakvasim ka'asher tasig yado* — for the sheep, whatever one can afford. This extends the principle down: if six cannot be had, bring five; if five cannot be had, four; and so on, down to even a single sheep. One brings whatever is available.

### Me'akvin Nuance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם לֹא מָצָא שִׁשָּׁה, יָבִיא חֲמִישָּׁה, חֲמִישָּׁה יָבִיא אַרְבָּעָה, אַרְבָּעָה יָבִיא שְׁלֹשָׁה, שְׁלֹשָׁה יָבִיא שְׁנַיִם, וַאֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״וְלַכְּבָשִׂים כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּשִּׂיג יָדוֹ״. וּמֵאַחַר דִּכְתִיב הָכִי, ״שִׁשָּׁה כְּבָשִׂים״ לְמָה לִי? דְּכַמָּה דְּאֶפְשָׁר לְהַדּוֹרֵי, מְהַדְּרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And from where** is it derived **that if he did not find six** lambs, **he should bring five;** and that if he did not find **five** lambs, **he should bring four;** and that if he did not find **four** lambs, **he should bring three;** and that if he did not find **three** lambs, **he should bring two; and** that if he could not find even two lambs, he should bring **even one** lamb? Therefore, **the** next **verse** in Ezekiel **states: "And for the lambs as his means suffice"** (Ezekiel 46:7), indicating that one should bring however many lambs one is able to bring. The Gemara asks: **But once this is written, why do I** need the previous verse to state **"six lambs,"** indicating that if one does not have seven lambs he should bring six? The Gemara answers: It teaches **that** although the minimal obligation is satisfied with even one lamb, nevertheless, **to the degree that it is possible to seek** more lambs, **we seek** them.

This raises an immediate question: if the principle is that one brings whatever one can, why did *Yechezkel* specify six sheep in particular? The *gemara* answers: *kama de'efshar le'haduyei mahadrinan* — to the extent possible, one strives to bring as many as possible. The fact that fewer animals remain valid does not license indifference. One may not reason that since the full seven cannot be had, one animal is just as good as six. Rather, one maximizes the offering within whatever constraints apply.

Yet the very next verse uses the word *yihyu* — "shall be" — by the ram: *ve'ayil tamim yihyu*. Does this not reintroduce the requirement? The *gemara* asks: *u'minayin she'me'akvin zeh et zeh* — how do we know they are *me'akev* one another? And the answer from *yihyu* is somewhat surprising, given everything just said. *Tosafot* offers two readings of what this *me'akev* means in context. First, citing *Rashi*: if the full complement is available and one deliberately brings less, one has not fulfilled the obligation. Second, reflecting the position of the sages who implicitly disagree with *Rabbi Shimon*: having the full number of animals together constitutes a qualitatively distinct *kiyum*, and one must work to achieve that complete unit even if it means forgoing *nesachim*, rather than bringing fewer animals with their *nesachim* as *Rabbi Shimon* would prefer. A third reading offered by *Tosafot*: if one has exactly the right number of sheep, one may not redirect one of them to the afternoon *tamid* sacrifice in order to have a *musaf* sheep with *nesachim* — the full unit of seven for the *musaf* takes priority. In all these readings, *me'akvin zeh et zeh* does not mean that a partial offering is entirely invalidated, but rather that the full complement constitutes an integrated unit that commands halakhic priority.

### Yechezkel 45:18

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כֹּה אָמַר ה׳ אֱלֹהִים בָּרִאשׁוֹן בְּאֶחָד לַחוֹדֶשׁ תִּקַּח פַּר בֶּן בָּקָר תָּמִים וְחִטֵּאתָ אֶת הַמִּקְדָּשׁ״. ״חַטָּאת״ – עוֹלָה הִיא! אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פָּרָשָׁה זוֹ אֵלִיָּהוּ עָתִיד לְדוֹרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the meaning of another difficult verse in Ezekiel: **"So says the Lord God: In the first month, on the first day of the month, you shall take a young bull without blemish; and you shall purify [*veḥitteita*] the Sanctuary"** (Ezekiel 45:18). The Gemara asks: Since this verse speaks of the first of Nisan, which is a New Moon, why does it state **"you shall purify [*ḥitteita*],"** which indicates the sacrifice of a sin offering [*ḥatat*], when in fact each of the two bulls sacrificed on the New Moon **is a burnt offering** (see Numbers 28:11)? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This passage** is indeed difficult, and in the **future Elijah** the prophet **will interpret it.**

The *gemara* now turns to an earlier chapter, *Yechezkel* 45:18, which presents a different set of difficulties. That verse reads: *koh amar Hashem Elokim ba-rishon be-echad la-chodesh tikach par ben bacar tamim ve-chiteita et ha-mikdash* — so says the Lord God, on the first day of the first month you shall take a bull and you shall perform *chittuy* — a purification — of the *Mikdash*. The first of the first month is *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and the sacrifice on that day is the *musaf* of *rosh chodesh*, which is an *olah*. Why, then, does *Yechezkel* describe the bull as being used for a *chitta'ut*, a purification associated with a *chatat*?

### Miluim Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִילּוּאִים הִקְרִיבוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says:** It is possible to explain that this verse is not referring to the additional offerings sacrificed on the New Moon but rather to the offerings of the **inauguration** that **they sacrificed** later **in the days of Ezra, similar to** the offerings **that were sacrificed** during the period of inauguration of the Tabernacle **in the days of Moses.** When the Temple service was restored in the Second Temple, the Jewish people observed eight days of inauguration, initiating the priests in the Temple service, from the twenty-third of Adar through the New Moon of Nisan. During these eight days, they offered a bull for a sin offering in addition to the offerings of the inauguration, just as had been done at the inauguration of the Tabernacle (see Leviticus 9:2).

*Rabbi Yochanan* declares: *parsha zu Eliyahu atid l'darsha* — this passage *Eliyahu* will expound in the future. The verse remains unexplained for now. *Rav Ashi*, however, offers a resolution: *miluim hikrivu bimei Ezra k'derech shehikrivu bimei Moshe* — this passage was prophesying that at the time of the dedication of the Second *Beis Hamikdash*, a *miluim* process would be performed, analogous to the initiation rites performed during the time of *Moshe* for the *Mishkan*. In the original *miluim*, an ox was used and its blood was applied to the altar as a form of purification. The dedication of the Second *Beis Hamikdash* would similarly occur on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and *Yechezkel* was prophesying that corresponding sanctification rites would be performed then. *Tosafot* acknowledges that the parallels are not exact, but maintains that it is nonetheless a form of *miluim*-style initiation.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Rabbi Yose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: פָּרָשָׁה זוֹ אֵלִיָּהוּ עָתִיד לְדוֹרְשָׁהּ. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: מִלּוּאִים הִקְרִיבוּ בִּימֵי עֶזְרָא, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִקְרִיבוּ בִּימֵי מֹשֶׁה. אָמַר לוֹ: תָּנוּחַ דַּעְתְּךָ שֶׁהִנַּחְתָּ דַּעְתִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments that **this** discussion with regard to the interpretation of the verse in Ezekiel **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehuda says: This passage** is indeed difficult, but in the **future Elijah** the prophet **will interpret it. Rabbi Yosei said to** Rabbi Yehuda: This verse is referring to the offerings of the **inauguration** that **they sacrificed** later **in the days of Ezra, similar to** the offerings **that were sacrificed** during the period of inauguration **in the days of Moses.** Rabbi Yehuda **said to** Rabbi Yosei: **May your mind be at ease, as you have put my mind at ease** with this interpretation of the verse.

A *baraita* confirms that this was already a matter of dispute among *Tannaim*. *Rabbi Yehuda* had taken the same position as *Rabbi Yochanan* — *parsha zu Eliyahu atid l'darsha* — I have no satisfactory explanation, and the matter awaits *Eliyahu*. *Rabbi Yose* responded with the *miluim* explanation: this prophesied the initiation rites of the Second *Beis Hamikdash* in the days of *Ezra*, modeled on those performed in the days of *Moshe*. *Rabbi Yehuda* replied: *tanuach datcha shehinachta es daati* — may your mind be at ease, for you have put my mind at ease. His acknowledgment is elegant: by conceding that he had been troubled and that *Rabbi Yose* had resolved his difficulty, he affirms the correctness of the explanation.

### Yechezkel 44

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכׇל נְבֵלָה וּטְרֵפָה מִן הָעוֹף וּמִן הַבְּהֵמָה לֹא יֹאכְלוּ הַכֹּהֲנִים״ – כֹּהֲנִים הוּא דְּלֹא יֹאכְלוּ, הָא יִשְׂרָאֵל אָכְלִי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: פָּרָשָׁה זוֹ אֵלִיָּהוּ עָתִיד לְדוֹרְשָׁהּ. רָבִינָא אָמַר: כֹּהֲנִים אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וְאִשְׁתְּרִי מְלִיקָה לְגַבַּיְיהוּ, תִּשְׁתְּרֵי נָמֵי נְבֵילָה וּטְרֵפָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the meaning of another difficult verse in Ezekiel: **"The priests shall not eat of anything that dies of itself, or is torn, whether it be fowl or beast"** (Ezekiel 44:31). The Gemara asks: **Is it** only the **priests who may not eat** an unslaughtered animal carcass or an animal that was torn and now has a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], **but** an ordinary **Jew may eat** them? In fact, these items are prohibited for consumption by all. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: This passage** is indeed difficult, but in the **future Elijah** the prophet **will interpret it.** **Ravina said** that it **was necessary for** the verse to emphasize that these prohibitions apply to **priests** for the following reason: It might **enter your mind to say** that **since pinching is permitted with regard to** priests, therefore an animal **carcass or a *tereifa* should also be permitted** for them. A bird sin offering is killed by a priest pinching the nape of its neck. This is not a valid method of slaughter and would generally render a bird or animal an unslaughtered carcass, yet the priests are permitted to partake of the bird sin offering. Consequently, one might think that the prohibitions against eating an animal carcass or a *tereifa* in general do not apply to priests. Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that these prohibitions apply to priests as well.

Another difficult passage appears in *Yechezkel* 44: *ve'chol neveila u'treifa min ha'of u'min habeheima lo yochlu hakohanim* — *kohanim* shall not eat any *neveila* or *treifa*, whether from birds or from animals. The obvious difficulty: does this imply that ordinary *Yisraelim* may eat *neveila* and *treifa*? The prohibition against *neveila* and *treifa* applies universally; restricting the verse to *kohanim* makes no sense as a general prohibition. *Rabbi Yochanan* once again resorts to *Eliyahu atid l'darsha*. *Ravina*, however, offers a resolution: the verse is specifically needed to address *kohanim*, because one might have thought they have special license. Since *kohanim* perform *melikah* — severing a bird's neck from the back — in the *Mikdash*, and that act would render a non-sacrificial bird a *neveila*, one might infer that *kohanim* are permitted to eat *neveila* and *treifa* more broadly. The verse teaches otherwise: even *kohanim* may not eat *neveila* or *treifa* outside the permitted sacrificial context. The mention of both *of* and *beheima* is apt precisely because *melikah* applies to birds — yet even for birds, outside the sacrificial framework, the prohibition remains fully in force.

### Par Helem Davar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְכֵן תַּעֲשֶׂה בְּשִׁבְעָה בַחֹדֶשׁ מֵאִישׁ שֹׁגֶה וּמִפֶּתִי וְכִפַּרְתֶּם אֶת הַבָּיִת״, ״שִׁבְעָה״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵלּוּ שִׁבְעָה שְׁבָטִים שֶׁחָטְאוּ, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רוּבָּהּ שֶׁל קָהָל. ״חֹדֶשׁ״ – אִם חִדְּשׁוּ וְאָמְרוּ חֵלֶב מוּתָּר, ״מֵאִישׁ שֹׁגֶה וּמִפֶּתִי״ – מְלַמֵּד שֶׁאֵין חַיָּיבִין אֶלָּא עַל הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר עִם שִׁגְגַת מַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses the meaning of another difficult verse in Ezekiel: **"And so shall you do on the seventh of the month for every one that errs, and for him that is simple; so shall you make atonement for the house"** (Ezekiel 45:20). The Gemara asks: What is the meaning of the expression "on the **seventh** of the month"? There are no special offerings that are sacrificed on the seventh day of any month. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The verse must be reinterpreted as referring to the bull for an unwitting communal sin, which is brought when the majority of the Jewish people have sinned as a result of following a mistaken ruling of the Sanhedrin. **These** seven alluded to in the verse are **seven tribes who sinned.** In such a case, a bull for an unwitting communal sin is brought **even though** the number of individuals who sinned **are not the majority of the congregation.** Because the majority of the individuals in the majority of the tribes have sinned, it is considered a sin of the congregation and not sins of many individuals. Similarly, the word **"month [*ḥodesh*]"** is to be interpreted as meaning that a bull for an unwitting communal sin is brought **if** the court **innovated [*ḥiddeshu*]** a new *halakha* contradicting the Torah, e.g., if **they said** that eating forbidden **fat** is **permitted.** The continuation of the verse: **"For every one that errs, and for him that is simple," teaches that** the Sanhedrin is **liable** to sacrifice the bull for unwitting communal sin **only for a matter** that was **hidden** from the Sanhedrin, i.e., about which the Sanhedrin issued a mistaken ruling, and **accompanied by unwitting action** by the majority of the community, who relied on the mistaken ruling.

The *gemara* addresses yet another passage from *Yechezkel* 45: *ve'chein taaseh beshivah bachodesh me'ish shogeh u'mipeti ve-chipertem es habayis* — and so shall you do on the seventh of the month, from an erring and foolish person, and you shall atone for the House. Following the passage about the *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* purification, this verse mentions an act of atonement to be performed on the seventh of *Nissan*. What does "seventh of the month" mean in this context? *Rabbi Yochanan* explains that this is not a reference to the seventh of the month at all, but to the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* — the communal bull brought when the *Beis Din* rules incorrectly and the majority of the nation follows that ruling in error, the subject of the entire tractate of Horayot.

The reading is ingenious: *shiva* refers not to the seventh day but to seven tribes — if seven of the tribes sin, even if they do not constitute a numerical majority of the entire people, that counts as the nation having followed the ruling. The word *chodesh*, rather than meaning "month," is read as *chidush* — innovation — referring to the *Beis Din*'s innovative but erroneous ruling. And then *me'ish shogeh u'mipeti* — from an erring and foolish person — teaches: *melamed she'ein chayavim ela al he'elam davar im shigegat ma'aseh* — the communal *korban* is not triggered by an incorrect ruling alone; there must also be a corresponding act of error among the people who followed that ruling. Both elements — the ruling and the people's erroneous compliance — are required.

### Par Helem Requirements

This double requirement — an innovative erroneous ruling by the *Beis Din* and subsequent erroneous action by the people following it — is derived elegantly from the combination of terms in *Yechezkel*'s verse. The *he'elam*, the concealment, refers to the ruling; the *shegagah*, the error, refers to the people's action. Both must be present for the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* to be obligated.

### Chanina ben Chizkiya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זָכוּר אוֹתוֹ הָאִישׁ לַטּוֹב, וַחֲנִינָא בֶּן חִזְקִיָּה שְׁמוֹ, שֶׁאִלְמָלֵא הוּא נִגְנַז סֵפֶר יְחֶזְקֵאל, שֶׁהָיוּ דְּבָרָיו סוֹתְרִין דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה. מֶה עָשָׂה? הֶעֱלָה שְׁלֹשׁ מֵאוֹת גַּרְבֵי שֶׁמֶן, וְיָשַׁב בַּעֲלִיָּיה וּדְרָשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes the discussion of specific difficult verses in Ezekiel with the following general statement: **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: That man is remembered for good, and Ḥanina ben Ḥizkiyya is his name. As were it not for him, the book of Ezekiel would have been suppressed** and not included in the biblical canon, **because** various details of **its contents** appear to **contradict statements of the Torah. What did** Ḥanina ben Ḥizkiyya **do? He brought up** to his upper story **three hundred jugs [*garbei*] of oil** for light so that he could study even at night, **and he sat** isolated **in the upper story and** did not move from there until **he homiletically interpreted** all of those verses in the book of Ezekiel that seemed to contradict verses in the Torah.

The *gemara* concludes this sequence with a remarkable statement. *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: *zachor oto ha'ish letov ve-Chanina ben Chizkiya shemo* — that man should be remembered for good, and his name is *Chanina ben Chizkiya*. The reason: *she'ilmala hu nignaz sefer Yechezkel* — were it not for him, the book of *Yechezkel* would have been suppressed and excluded from the canon. Why? *Shehayu devarav sotrim divrei Torah* — because on their face, many of its passages contradict the Torah, as we have seen throughout this *sugya*: the animal counts differ from the Torah, the *chitta'ut* on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* seems anomalous, and the passage about *neveila* and *treifa* appears to grant *kohanim* what it forbids to others. What did *Chanina ben Chizkiya* do? He brought three hundred flasks of oil — fuel to keep the lamps burning through many long nights — sat in his upper chamber, and worked through all the difficult passages until he found ways to interpret them that brought them into harmony with the Torah. This was the scholarly labor that preserved *Yechezkel* as part of *Tanach*.

## Rabbi Shimon & Nesachim

### Eifa Measurements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְאֵיפָה לַפָּר וְאֵיפָה לָאַיִל יַעֲשֶׂה מִנְחָה וְלַכְּבָשִׂים כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּשִּׂיג יָדוֹ וְשֶׁמֶן הִין לָאֵיפָה״. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: וְכִי מִדַּת פָּרִים וְאֵילִים אַחַת הִיא? אֶלָּא, שֶׁאִם הָיוּ לָהֶם פָּרִים מְרוּבִּין וְלֹא הָיוּ נְסָכִים – יָבִיאוּ פַּר אֶחָד וּנְסָכָיו, וְאַל יִקְרְבוּ כּוּלָּן בְּלֹא נְסָכִים. וְאִם הָיוּ לָהֶם אֵילִים מְרוּבִּין וְלֹא הָיָה לָהֶן אֵיפָתָן – יָבִיאוּ אַיִל אֶחָד וְאֵיפָתוֹ, וְלֹא יִקְרְבוּ כּוּלָּם בְּלֹא אֵיפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Concerning this, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"And he shall prepare a meal offering, an ephah for the bull, and an ephah for the ram, and for the lambs as his means suffice, and a *hin* of oil to an ephah"** (Ezekiel 46:7). **Rabbi Shimon says: Is the measure of** the meal offering accompanying **bulls and rams the same,** as stated in this verse that it is an ephah for each? In fact, this is not the *halakha*, as the meal offering accompanying a bull is three-tenths of an ephah of fine flour (see Numbers 15:9), whereas the meal offering accompanying a ram is only two-tenths of an ephah (see Numbers 15:6). **Rather,** the verse teaches **that if** the Temple treasurers **had** sufficient funds for the **numerous bulls** that are required to be sacrificed on that day **but they did not** also **have** sufficient funds for the accompanying **libations, they should bring one bull and its libations, and they should not sacrifice all of them without libations. And** similarly, **if** the Temple treasurers **had** sufficient funds for the **numerous rams** that are required to be sacrificed on that day **and they did not** also **have** sufficient funds for the **ephah,** i.e., the prescribed measure, of flour **for** all of the rams, **they should bring one ram and its ephah** of flour, **and they should not sacrifice all of them without their ephahs** of flour.

The *gemara* now returns to *Rabbi Shimon*'s position stated in the opening *mishna*, and demonstrates that he derives it from a verse in *Yechezkel*. A *baraita* cites *Yechezkel* 45:24: *ve'eifa la'par ve'eifa la'ayil ya'aseh mincha ve'lakvasim ka'asher tasig yado ve'shemen hin la'eifa* — an *eifa* for the bull and an *eifa* for the ram shall he prepare as a *mincha*, and for the sheep as he is able to afford, and oil, a *hin* per *eifa*. *Rabbi Shimon* notes a textual difficulty: is the measure for a bull and a ram truly the same *eifa*? In fact, they do not have the same measure. Moreover, the measure specified here does not match the standard Torah prescription. So what is the verse conveying?

### Animal Priority

*Rabbi Shimon* reads the verse as follows: since the Torah's prescribed measure is not being stated here literally, the verse must be conveying a halakhic principle. *She'im hayu lahem parim merubim velo hayu lahem nesachim yaviu par echad venesachav ve'ayil echad venesachav velo yakrivu kulam belo nesachim* — if they had the funds for many bulls but not enough for their *nesachim*, they should bring one bull with its *nesachim* and not sacrifice all of them without any *nesachim*. The verse teaches that the measure — the *mincha* and *nesachim* — must be paired with its animal, and that a paired offering is qualitatively superior to an unpaired one. The unit of animal-plus-*nesachim* is the ideal, and it is better to realize that ideal for one animal than to sacrifice many animals in a diminished form.

### Ram Priority

The same principle extends to rams: if there are funds for many rams but insufficient funds to provide each with its *nesachim*, one should bring a single ram with its proper *nesachim* rather than many rams without. *Rabbi Shimon*'s position, rooted in his reading of *Yechezkel*, is that completeness of the individual offering takes precedence over multiplicity of incomplete offerings.

## Ha-Par v'Ha-Eilim

### Mishna Text

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפָּר וְהָאֵילִים וְהַכְּבָשִׂים וְהַשָּׂעִיר – אֵין מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, וְלֹא הַלֶּחֶם מְעַכְּבָן. הַלֶּחֶם מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים, וְאֵין הַכְּבָשִׂים מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נַנָּס: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא הַכְּבָשִׂים מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַלֶּחֶם, וְהַלֶּחֶם אֵינוֹ מְעַכֵּב הַכְּבָשִׂים, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ כְּשֶׁהָיוּ יִשְׂרָאֵל בְּמִדְבָּר אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה קָרְבוּ כְּבָשִׂים בְּלֹא לֶחֶם, אַף כָּאן יִקְרְבוּ כְּבָשִׂים בְּלֹא לֶחֶם. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי בֶן נַנָּס, אֲבָל אֵין הַטַּעַם כִּדְבָרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: On *Shavuot* there is an obligation to sacrifice burnt offerings, a sin offering, and peace offerings together with the offering of the two loaves. The burnt offerings consist of a bull, two rams, and seven sheep. A goat is brought for the sin offering. Two sheep are brought as peace offerings and waved together with the two loaves. Failure to sacrifice **the bull, the rams, and the sheep,** which are all brought as burnt offerings, **and the goat** that is brought as a sin offering, **does not prevent** the bringing of **the** two **loaves,** and they are sacrificed nevertheless. Failure to bring **the** two **loaves does not prevent** sacrifice of the accompanying animal offerings. Failure to bring **the** two **loaves prevents** sacrifice of **the** accompanying peace offering of two **sheep, but** failure to sacrifice **the** two **sheep does not prevent** the bringing of **the** two **loaves;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** **Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas says: No, rather** the opposite is true. Failure to sacrifice **the** peace offering of two **sheep prevents** the bringing of **the** two **loaves, but** failure to bring **the** two **loaves does not prevent** sacrifice of **the** accompanying peace offering of two **sheep. As we found that when the Jewish people were in the wilderness** for **forty years** after the exodus from Egypt, **they sacrificed** the two **sheep** as a peace offering on *Shavuot* **without** the two **loaves,** as the two loaves may be brought only from wheat grown in Eretz Yisrael after the Jewish people entered the land. **Here too,** whenever wheat is unavailable, **they** should **sacrifice** the two **sheep** without the two **loaves.** However, the two loaves are not sacrificed without the peace offering of two sheep. **Rabbi Shimon says:** The ***halakha*** is **in accordance with the statement of** Rabbi Shimon **ben Nannas** that failure to sacrifice the two sheep prevents the bringing of the two loaves but failure to bring the two loaves does not prevent sacrifice of the accompanying peace offering of two sheep, **but the reason** for that ruling **is not in accordance with his statement.**

The second *mishna* addresses the relationship between the various animals and the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot*. It states: *ha-par veha'eilim veha'kevasim veha'se'ir ein me'akvin es ha-lechem* — the bull, the rams, the sheep, and the goat do not prevent the *shtei halechem*. The *korban* in question is the special *olah* brought together with the two loaves of *Shavuot*, comprising seven sheep, two rams, and one bull, plus a *se'ir* as a *chatat*. The *mishna* rules that the absence of any of these animals does not prevent bringing the *shtei halechem*, and the reverse is equally true: *velo halechem me'akvan* — if the *shtei halechem* is unavailable, the animals may still be offered. Each of the two *korbanot* can stand independently of the other.

### Rabbi Akiva

This independence between the *olah* and the bread does not, however, resolve the question of the relationship between the *shtei halechem* and the two lambs of *shelamim* that are waved together with it. These are distinct from the *olah*: the Torah states *u'shenei kevasim b'nei shana le'zevach shelamim*, two yearling lambs as a peace offering, and these are waved *al shenei kevasim* — specifically on the two lambs, together with the bread. This is a far tighter halakhic connection than the one between the *olah* and the bread.

Regarding these two lambs, *Rabbi Akiva* rules: *halechem me'akev es ha-kevasim ve'ein ha-kevasim me'akvin es ha-lechem* — the bread prevents the lambs, but the lambs do not prevent the bread. The bread is primary. Without bread, the lambs serve no purpose and should not be brought. But if the lambs are unavailable, the bread may still be brought on its own.

### Ben Nanos

*Ben Nanos* disagrees and reverses the ruling: *ha-kevasim me'akvin es ha-lechem ve'ha-lechem eino me'akev es ha-kevasim* — the lambs prevent the bread, but not the reverse. The lambs can stand without the bread; the bread cannot stand without the lambs. His proof: when *Bnei Yisrael* were in the wilderness for forty years, they brought the *korbanot* prescribed in *Emor* — including these lambs — but did not bring *shtei halechem*, since the Torah conditions the *shtei halechem* on entry into *Eretz Yisrael*. Thus we have historical precedent: the lambs were offered without the bread. The reverse was never done. Therefore, the lambs are the foundation, and the bread depends on them, not the other way around.

### Rabbi Shimon's Ruling

*Rabbi Shimon* rules: *halachah kedivrei Ben Nanos* — the law follows *Ben Nanos*. The lambs may be brought without the bread, but not vice versa. However, he explicitly rejects *Ben Nanos*'s reasoning.

### Wilderness Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁכׇּל הָאָמוּר בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים קָרֵב בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְכׇל הָאָמוּר בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים אֵין קָרֵב בַּמִּדְבָּר, מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ לָאָרֶץ קָרְבוּ אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As all** the offerings that must be sacrificed on *Shavuot* **that** are **stated in the book of Numbers** (see 28:27), i.e., two bulls, one ram, and seven sheep as additional offerings and a goat as a sin offering, were **sacrificed** when the Jewish people were **in the wilderness. But all** the offerings **stated in Leviticus** (see 23:18–20), i.e., the offerings accompanying the two loaves, were **not sacrificed** when the Jewish people were **in the wilderness.** Not only were the two loaves not sacrificed, but the accompanying offerings, including the peace offering of the two sheep, were also not sacrificed, because it was only **when they arrived in Eretz** Yisrael that **these** additional offerings **and those** offerings accompanying the two loaves **were sacrificed.** Neither the additional offerings of *Shavuot* nor the two loaves, and the offerings that accompany them, were sacrificed in the wilderness, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas.

*Rabbi Shimon* does not accept the claim that the lambs were actually offered without the bread during the wilderness period. His own reasoning is based on a different principle: *shekol ha'amur b'Chumash HaPikudim karav ba-midbar ve-chol ha'amur b'Toras Kohanim lo karav ba-midbar* — everything prescribed in *Bamidbar* (*Pinchas*) was offered in the wilderness, but what is prescribed in *Toras Kohanim* — specifically the *Shavuot* passage in *Emor* — was not offered in the wilderness at all. Since the special *olah* and the two lambs of *shelamim* are described in *Emor* in connection with the *shtei halechem*, and the *shtei halechem* only applies upon entry into *Eretz Yisrael*, the entire complex of *korbanot* described in that passage — the *olah*, the lambs, and the bread together — did not come into force until they entered the land. *Ben Nanos* is therefore wrong to say the lambs were offered without the bread in the wilderness; none of it was offered at all.

### Matir Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִפְּנֵי מָה אֲנִי אוֹמֵר יִקְרְבוּ כְּבָשִׂים בְּלֹא לֶחֶם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַכְּבָשִׁים מַתִּירִין אֶת עַצְמָן, וְלֹא הַלֶּחֶם בְּלֹא כְּבָשִׂים – שֶׁאֵין לוֹ מִי יַתִּירֶנּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **for what** reason **do I** nevertheless **say that the sheep should be sacrificed without** the **loaves,** in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Nannas? It is **due to** the fact **that the sheep permit themselves,** as the sprinkling of their blood and the burning of the portions consumed on the altar renders it permitted to partake of their meat. **And** why are **the loaves not** sacrificed **without the sheep?** It is **because there is no** item **to permit** the loaves, as the loaves are permitted only after the sheep are sacrificed.

If the entire *Emor* complex only began in *Eretz Yisrael*, why does *Rabbi Shimon* still rule that the lambs may be brought without the bread? Because the determining factor is not conceptual primacy but the halakhic *matir* — the mechanism that renders a *korban* permitted for consumption after it has been consecrated. A sanctified offering cannot simply be eaten; something must first be placed on the *mizbeach*, and that act serves as the *matir* that releases the rest for consumption. For the two lambs of *shelamim*, the *matir* is their own blood, which is sprinkled on the *mizbeach*. They function as a complete, self-contained *korban* and may be offered independently. For the *shtei halechem*, however, there is no intrinsic *matir*: nothing from the bread itself goes on the *mizbeach*. The only act that serves as a *matir* for the bread is the slaughter of the lambs and the sprinkling of their blood. *Mipnei shehakvasim matirim et atzmam ve'lo halechem matir et atzmam* — the lambs have a *matir* and the bread does not. Consequently, the lambs can be brought without the bread; the bread cannot be brought without the lambs. This is *Rabbi Shimon*'s reasoning — entirely distinct from *Ben Nanos*'s historical argument, though they arrive at the same practical ruling.

## Gemara Analysis

### Obligation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְהִקְרַבְתֶּם עַל הַלֶּחֶם״ – חוֹבָה עַל הַלֶּחֶם, ״שִׁבְעַת כְּבָשִׂים תְּמִימִים״ – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches the *halakhot* of the sacrifices that generally accompany the two loaves on *Shavuot* in a case when the two loaves are not available. The Gemara cites a relevant *baraita*. **The Sages taught:** The verse that mandates these offerings states: "And you shall sacrifice with the bread seven lambs without blemish of the first year, and one young bull, and two rams" (Leviticus 23:18). The phrase **"and you shall sacrifice with the bread"** indicates that it is **obligatory** to sacrifice these burnt offerings **with the loaves** of bread, and if the loaves are not available, then these offerings are not sacrificed. The continuation of the verse: **"Seven lambs without blemish,"** teaches that the lambs are sacrificed **even if there are no loaves** available.

A *baraita* opens: *ve'hikravtem al halechem chova* — "and you shall bring upon the bread" (from the *Emor* passage describing the special *olah*) — this is an obligation incumbent upon you. The text then states *shiv'at kevasim temimim* — seven lambs — and from this formulation the *baraita* derives that this *olah* may be brought even without the bread: *af al pi she'ein lechem*. The *olah* is independently obligatory, not merely incidental to the bread.

### Independence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״עַל הַלֶּחֶם״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁלֹּא נִתְחַיְּיבוּ בַּכְּבָשִׂים קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּתְחַיְּיבוּ בַּלֶּחֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **If so,** that the animal offerings may be sacrificed even without loaves, **what** is the meaning when **the verse states "with the bread"?** It **teaches that they were not obligated to** sacrifice **the sheep before they were obligated to** sacrifice **loaves,** i.e., they became obligated to sacrifice all of these offerings only when they entered Eretz Yisrael. This is **the statement of Rabbi Tarfon.**

This creates an apparent tension: the verse says *al halechem*, "upon the bread," linking the *olah* to the bread, yet the *baraita* derives that the *olah* may be brought without the bread. If so, what work does the phrase *al halechem* do? The *gemara* resolves this below.

### Rabbi Tarfon

*Rabbi Tarfon* explains: *melamed shelo nitchayvu bikvasim kodem shenitchayvu belechem* — the phrase *al halechem* teaches that the obligation to bring these *korbanot* did not take effect until the obligation of the bread took effect. In other words, the entire package — the *olah*, the lambs of *shelamim*, and the bread — was conditioned on entry into *Eretz Yisrael*, since the *shtei halechem* only applied there. The phrase *al halechem* signals that conceptually, all of this is "because of the bread," and therefore none of it began until the bread became obligatory. But in practice, once they entered *Eretz Yisrael*, the *olah* and the bread are not *me'akev* one another — each may be brought independently of the other.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל הֵן הֵן כְּבָשִׂים הָאֲמוּרִים כָּאן הֵן הֵן כְּבָשִׂים הָאֲמוּרִים בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים? אָמַרְתָּ, כְּשֶׁאַתָּה מַגִּיעַ אֵצֶל פָּרִים וְאֵילִים אֵינָן הֵן, אֶלָּא הַלָּלוּ בָּאִים בִּגְלַל עַצְמָן, וְהַלָּלוּ בָּאִים בִּגְלַל לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that the **sheep mentioned here** in Leviticus, which accompany the two loaves, **are the very same ones mentioned in the book of Numbers** (see 28:27), in the passage prescribing the additional offerings. You must **say when you reach** the **bulls and rams** that are enumerated in the two passages that the offerings mentioned in one **are not those** mentioned in the other, as the number of bulls and rams are not equal. Whereas in Leviticus the verse requires the sacrifice of one bull and two rams, in Numbers the verse requires the sacrifice of two bulls and one ram. **Rather,** the two passages are referring to different offerings. **These** mentioned in Numbers **come** upon the altar **for their own sake, and those** mentioned in Leviticus **come** upon the altar **for the sake of** the two **loaves.**

*Rabbi Akiva* builds on this and makes a further argument. One might have thought that the seven lambs described in *Emor* are the same as the seven lambs described in the *musaf* of *Shavuot* in *Pinchas*. If so, the *olah* in *Emor* would simply be the standard *musaf*, and the phrase *al halechem* would link the *musaf* itself to the bread. *Rabbi Akiva* refutes this: when one looks at the bulls and rams, the numbers are reversed. *Pinchas* prescribes two bulls and one ram; *Emor* prescribes one bull and two rams. This proves they are distinct *korbanot*. The *olah* in *Emor* is specific to the *shtei halechem*, while the *musaf* in *Pinchas* is the standard *Shavuot musaf* that has nothing to do with the bread. *Halalu ba'in biglal atzmam ve'halalu ba'in biglal lechem* — the former come on their own account and the latter come on account of the bread. Consequently, the *musaf* of *Bamidbar* was offered in the wilderness; the *olah* of *Emor* was not, consistent with what *Rabbi Shimon* stated in the *mishna*.

### Order Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְצָא, מַה שֶּׁאָמוּר בְּחוֹמֶשׁ הַפְּקוּדִים – קָרֵב בַּמִּדְבָּר, וּמַה שֶּׁאָמוּר בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים – לֹא קָרֵב בַּמִּדְבָּר. וְדִלְמָא פָּרִים וְאֵילִים לָאו אִינְהוּ, הָא כְּבָשִׂים אִינְהוּ נִינְהוּ? מִדְּהָנֵי אִישְׁתַּנּוֹ, הָנֵי נָמֵי דְּאַחֲרִינֵי. וּפָרִים וְאֵילִים, מִמַּאי דְּאִישְׁתַּנּוֹ? דִּלְמָא הָכִי קָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: אִי בָּעֵי פַּר וּשְׁנֵי אֵילִים לִיקְרַב, אִי בָּעֵי שְׁנֵי פָּרִים וְאַיִל אֶחָד לִיקְרַב! מִדְּאִישְׁתַּנִּי סִדְרָן, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אַחֲרִינֵי נִינְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** It is therefore **found** that the offerings **that are mentioned in the book of Numbers were sacrificed** even when the Jewish people were **in the wilderness** and could not bring the offering of the two loaves, **but** the offerings **that are mentioned in Leviticus were not sacrificed in the wilderness,** due to the fact that the two loaves could not be sacrificed in the wilderness. The Gemara challenges: **But perhaps the bulls and rams** mentioned in Numbers **are not those** mentioned in Leviticus, **but the sheep** mentioned in Numbers **are** the same as **those** mentioned in Leviticus. The Gemara explains: **From** the fact **that these** bulls and rams **are different,** it is apparent that **those** sheep **are also different** offerings. The Gemara asks: **But from where** is it proven **that** the **bulls and rams** in Numbers **are different** offerings than the bulls and rams mentioned in Leviticus? **Perhaps** they are actually the same offerings, and **this** is what **the Merciful One is saying: If** you **want, sacrifice a bull and two rams,** as the verse states in Leviticus; and **if** you **want, sacrifice two bulls and one ram,** as the verse states in Numbers. The Gemara answers: **From** the fact **that the order of** the offerings **is different** in the two passages, as the verse in Leviticus mentions the sheep, then the bull, and then the rams, whereas the verse in Numbers mentions the bulls, then the ram, and then the sheep, one may **conclude from it** that **they are different** offerings.

The *gemara* raises a further challenge: perhaps both passages describe the same *musaf* and the Torah is simply offering a choice — two bulls and one ram, or one bull and two rams? The *gemara* rejects this by noting that not only are the numbers different, but the order of presentation is also different. In *Emor*: *shiv'at kevasim temimim u'far ben bakar echad ve'eilim shnayim* — seven sheep first, then one bull, then two rams. In *Pinchas*: *parim b'nei vakar shnayim* — it opens with the bulls. The Torah goes out of its way to present the animals in a different sequence, signaling unambiguously that these are fundamentally different *korbanot*.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַלֶּחֶם מְעַכֵּב אֶת הַכְּבָשִׂים, מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? גָּמַר ״יִהְיוּ״ מִ״תִּהְיֶינָה״. מָה לְהַלָּן לֶחֶם, אַף כָּאן לֶחֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: Failure to bring **the** two **loaves prevents** sacrifice of **the** accompanying peace offering of two **sheep, but** failure to sacrifice the two sheep does not prevent the bringing of the two loaves; this is the statement of Rabbi Akiva. The Gemara asks: **What is the reasoning of Rabbi Akiva?** The Gemara answers: **He derived** the *halakha* based upon a verbal analogy between two verses. One verse states: "And the priest shall wave them with the bread of the first fruits for a wave offering before the Lord, with the two lambs; **they shall be [*yihyu*]** holy to the Lord for the priest" (Leviticus 23:20). The term "they shall be" indicates that it is essential that the offering be brought precisely as commanded, but it is unclear whether this is referring to the loaves or to the sheep brought as peace offerings. This is clarified by means of a verbal analogy **from** the verse: "You shall bring out of your dwellings two loaves of waving of two tenth parts of an ephah; **they shall be [*tihyena*]** of fine flour" (Leviticus 23:17). The verbal analogy teaches that **just as there** the requirement that it be done as prescribed is referring to the **loaves** rather than the sheep, **so too here** it is referring to the **loaves** rather than the sheep. Consequently, failure to bring the loaves prevents sacrifice of the sheep, but failure to sacrifice the sheep does not prevent the bringing of the loaves.

The *gemara* concludes: the *olah* in *Emor* is intrinsically connected to the *shtei halechem* — *al halechem* teaches that its time did not begin until they entered *Eretz Yisrael* — but once it came into force, the two *korbanot* do not prevent one another. The *olah* may be brought without the *shtei halechem* and the *shtei halechem* without the *olah*. Both *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* affirm this principle, differing only in the specific derivations they emphasize. The *mishna*'s ruling — *ein me'akvin* between the animals and the bread — thus holds for this entire complex of offerings. The discussion of the two lambs of *shelamim* and their relationship to the bread, which turns on the *matir* principle analyzed above, will continue in the next *mishna*.
