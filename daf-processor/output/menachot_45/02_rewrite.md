# Menachot 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Me'akvin Overview

### Topic Transition

Today's daf, Menachot 45, picks up with the *mishna* at the bottom of 44b. Having concluded the extended discussion of *tefillin*, *sefer torah*, *mezuzah*, and *tzitzis*, the tractate returns to the world of *korbanot* — though *menachos* will continue to figure prominently. We are now in a section organized around the principle of *me'akvin zeh et zeh* or *ein me'akvin zeh et zeh*: when two components of a *mitzvah* or *korban* are brought together, does the absence of one invalidate or prevent the other?

### Me'akvin Principle

This framework was already present in the previous *mishna*, which addressed *minchas nesachim* — the flour, oil, and wine that accompany animal *korbanot*. The present *mishna* extends the analysis to the various animals of the *musafim*, the additional festival sacrifices, though *menachos* will continue to surface throughout the discussion.

---

## Hapirim V'Ha'eilim

### Mishna Text

The *mishna* states: *hapirim veha'eilim vehakevasim ein me'akvin zeh et zeh* — the bulls, the rams, and the sheep do not prevent one another. The context is a *musaf* offering that includes a combination of all three types of animals. If some animals are unavailable, those that are on hand are still brought. As the *gemara* will clarify, this applies not only across species — that missing rams does not prevent bringing bulls — but even within a species: if the full complement of a given animal cannot be obtained, whatever is available is still offered.

### Rabbi Shimon

*Rabbi Shimon* offers a divergent position. If the congregation had enough funds to purchase the full number of bulls — say, fourteen for *Sukkot* — but doing so would leave nothing for the *minchas nesachim*, the flour, oil, and wine that accompany each animal, he rules: *yavvi par echad venesachav velo yakrivu kulam belo nesachim* — it is better to bring one bull together with its *nesachim* than to bring all of them without any *nesachim* at all. In *Rabbi Shimon*'s view, the animal together with its *nesachim* constitutes a complete, unified offering. An animal brought without its *nesachim* is, as it were, a second-rate offering. Therefore, it is preferable to offer one complete unit than to offer many incomplete ones.

## Which Korban?

### Sukkot Exception

The *gemara* opens by asking: *hanei parim uchevasim deheicha* — which *korbanot* does the *mishna* have in mind? Since the context is a *musaf* with multiple bulls, rams, and sheep, the *gemara* must identify a *musaf* with plurals of all three. *Sukkot* is immediately excluded: the Torah uses the phrase *kemishpat* or *kemishpatam* in connection with the *Sukkot musaf*, which the *gemara* understands to mandate the full complement of every animal. *Sukkot* is thus an exception to the *ein me'akvin* rule, even though the *mishna* does not state this explicitly.

### Rosh Chodesh & Shavuot

The *gemara* therefore identifies the relevant *korbanot* as those of *rosh chodesh* and *shavuot* as recorded in *Bamidbar* — referred to by the *gemara* as *Chumash HaPikudim*, the Book of Numbers. Other holidays are not mentioned because, as *Tosafot* notes, the *gemara* is looking for a *musaf* that features genuine plurals of all three animal types simultaneously. *Pesach*, *Rosh Hashanah*, *Yom Kippur*, and *Shemini Atzeret* each have only a single ram and a single bull, so they do not fit the *mishna*'s language of *parim*, *eilim*, and *kevasim* in the plural.

### Multiple Animals

Even *rosh chodesh* and *shavuot* do not fully satisfy this requirement. The *gemara* examines the relevant verses: for *rosh chodesh* the Torah states *parim b'nei vakar shnayim ve'ayil echad kevasim b'nei shana shiva temimim* — two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. For *shavuot* in *Bamidbar* (*Pinchas*), the formulation is identical: two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. In neither case does one find multiple rams. Where, then, does a *musaf*-like offering with multiple rams appear?

### Emor Passage

The *gemara* directs attention to *parashat Emor* in *Vayikra*, where the Torah presents a special *olah* brought together with the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot*: *shiv'at kevasim temimim b'nei shana u'far ben bakar echad ve'eilim shnayim yihyu olah la'Hashem* — seven lambs, one bull, and two rams. Here, finally, is a context with multiple rams.

### Korban Comparison

This passage, however, reveals a key discrepancy. The *musaf* of *Shavuot* in *Pinchas* reads: two bulls, one ram, seven sheep. The special *olah* of *Emor* reads: one bull, two rams, seven sheep. The counts of bulls and rams are inverted. The *gemara* concludes that these cannot be the same *korban*. The offering in *Emor* is a distinct *korban* — specifically the *olah* connected to the *shtei halechem* — and is separate from the standard *musaf* listed in *Pinchas*.

### Eilim Resolution

With this clarification in hand, the *gemara* explains the *mishna*'s statement regarding rams. *Lo eilim d'Toras Kohanim me'akvei lei l'ayil d'Chumash HaPikudim v'lo ayil d'Chumash HaPikudim me'akvei lehu l'eilim d'Toras Kohanim* — the two rams of the special *olah* in *Vayikra* do not prevent the single ram of the *musaf* in *Bamidbar*, and conversely. These belong to two distinct *korbanot* that operate independently of one another. It is important to note, however, that internally to the *olah* of *Emor* — which uses the language *yihyu*, "shall be" — all the animals are required and are indeed *me'akev* one another; the *mishna*'s *ein me'akvin* applies only to the relationship between the two separate *korbanot*.

### Different Scenarios

This produces a somewhat jarring reading of the *mishna*, and the *gemara* acknowledges it directly: *tani mili mili katani* — the *mishna* is speaking to different scenarios in different clauses. When it says the bulls and the sheep are *ein me'akvin zeh et zeh*, it refers to the *musaf* itself — even within the *musaf*, if not all the animals of a given species are available, whatever can be brought is brought, and the missing animals do not invalidate the rest. When it speaks of the rams, however, it refers to something entirely different: the relationship between the ram of the *musaf* in *Bamidbar* and the rams of the *korban* associated with the *shtei halechem* in *Vayikra*. The *mishna* thus addresses two distinct halakhic scenarios under a single heading. This is admittedly a complex reading, but it is the *gemara*'s resolution of what would otherwise be an intractable textual problem.

## Yechezkel Difficult Texts

### Yechezkel 46:6

Having established the basic rules of *me'akvin*, the *gemara* turns to derive and reinforce these *halachos* from the prophecies of *Yechezkel*, while simultaneously resolving passages in *Yechezkel* that appear to contradict the Torah. The first such passage is *Yechezkel* 46:6, which describes the *rosh chodesh* sacrifice of the future *Beis Hamikdash*: *par ben bakar temimim ve'sheses kevasim ve'ayil tamim yihyu* — one bull, six sheep, and one ram. This stands in direct tension with the Torah's prescription of two bulls and seven sheep for *rosh chodesh*.

### Fewer Animals

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by reading *Yechezkel*'s reduced numbers as halakhic instruction. The verse says *par* — a single bull — even though the Torah mandates two. This teaches: *u'minayin she'im lo matza shnayim mevi echad talmud lomar par* — how do we know that if two bulls cannot be obtained, one may be brought? *Yechezkel* tells us so. Similarly, the verse says *sheses kevasim* — six sheep, rather than the seven mandated by the Torah. This teaches that if seven cannot be obtained, six are brought. And the next verse continues: *ve'lakvasim ka'asher tasig yado* — for the sheep, whatever one can afford. This extends the principle down: if six cannot be had, bring five; if five cannot be had, four; and so on, down to even a single sheep. One brings whatever is available.

### Me'akvin Nuance

This raises an immediate question: if the principle is that one brings whatever one can, why did *Yechezkel* specify six sheep in particular? The *gemara* answers: *kama de'efshar le'haduyei mahadrinan* — to the extent possible, one strives to bring as many as possible. The fact that fewer animals remain valid does not license indifference. One may not reason that since the full seven cannot be had, one animal is just as good as six. Rather, one maximizes the offering within whatever constraints apply.

Yet the very next verse uses the word *yihyu* — "shall be" — by the ram: *ve'ayil tamim yihyu*. Does this not reintroduce the requirement? The *gemara* asks: *u'minayin she'me'akvin zeh et zeh* — how do we know they are *me'akev* one another? And the answer from *yihyu* is somewhat surprising, given everything just said. *Tosafot* offers two readings of what this *me'akev* means in context. First, citing *Rashi*: if the full complement is available and one deliberately brings less, one has not fulfilled the obligation. Second, reflecting the position of the sages who implicitly disagree with *Rabbi Shimon*: having the full number of animals together constitutes a qualitatively distinct *kiyum*, and one must work to achieve that complete unit even if it means forgoing *nesachim*, rather than bringing fewer animals with their *nesachim* as *Rabbi Shimon* would prefer. A third reading offered by *Tosafot*: if one has exactly the right number of sheep, one may not redirect one of them to the afternoon *tamid* sacrifice in order to have a *musaf* sheep with *nesachim* — the full unit of seven for the *musaf* takes priority. In all these readings, *me'akvin zeh et zeh* does not mean that a partial offering is entirely invalidated, but rather that the full complement constitutes an integrated unit that commands halakhic priority.

### Yechezkel 45:18

The *gemara* now turns to an earlier chapter, *Yechezkel* 45:18, which presents a different set of difficulties. That verse reads: *koh amar Hashem Elokim ba-rishon be-echad la-chodesh tikach par ben bakar tamim ve-chiteita et ha-mikdash* — so says the Lord God, on the first day of the first month you shall take a bull and you shall perform *chittuy* — a purification — of the *Mikdash*. The first of the first month is *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and the sacrifice on that day is the *musaf* of *rosh chodesh*, which is an *olah*. Why, then, does *Yechezkel* describe the bull as being used for a *chitta'ut*, a purification associated with a *chatat*?

### Miluim Process

*Rabbi Yochanan* declares: *parsha zu Eliyahu atid l'darsha* — this passage *Eliyahu* will expound in the future. The verse remains unexplained for now. *Rav Ashi*, however, offers a resolution: *miluim hikrivu bimei Ezra k'derech shehikrivu bimei Moshe* — this passage was prophesying that at the time of the dedication of the Second *Beis Hamikdash*, a *miluim* process would be performed, analogous to the initiation rites performed during the time of *Moshe* for the *Mishkan*. In the original *miluim*, an ox was used and its blood was applied to the altar as a form of purification. The dedication of the Second *Beis Hamikdash* would similarly occur on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan*, and *Yechezkel* was prophesying that corresponding sanctification rites would be performed then. *Tosafot* acknowledges that the parallels are not exact, but maintains that it is nonetheless a form of *miluim*-style initiation.

### Rabbi Yehuda & Rabbi Yose

A *baraita* confirms that this was already a matter of dispute among *Tannaim*. *Rabbi Yehuda* had taken the same position as *Rabbi Yochanan* — *parsha zu Eliyahu atid l'darsha* — I have no satisfactory explanation, and the matter awaits *Eliyahu*. *Rabbi Yose* responded with the *miluim* explanation: this prophesied the initiation rites of the Second *Beis Hamikdash* in the days of *Ezra*, modeled on those performed in the days of *Moshe*. *Rabbi Yehuda* replied: *tanuach datcha shehinachta es daati* — may your mind be at ease, for you have put my mind at ease. His acknowledgment is elegant: by conceding that he had been troubled and that *Rabbi Yose* had resolved his difficulty, he affirms the correctness of the explanation.

### Yechezkel 44

Another difficult passage appears in *Yechezkel* 44: *ve-chol neveila u'treifa min ha'of u'min habeheima lo yochlu hakohanim* — *kohanim* shall not eat any *neveila* or *treifa*, whether from birds or from animals. The obvious difficulty: does this imply that ordinary *Yisraelim* may eat *neveila* and *treifa*? The prohibition against *neveila* and *treifa* applies universally; restricting the verse to *kohanim* makes no sense as a general prohibition. *Rabbi Yochanan* once again resorts to *Eliyahu atid l'darsha*. *Ravina*, however, offers a resolution: the verse is specifically needed to address *kohanim*, because one might have thought they have special license. Since *kohanim* perform *melikah* — severing a bird's neck from the back — in the *Mikdash*, and that act would render a non-sacrificial bird a *neveila*, one might infer that *kohanim* are permitted to eat *neveila* and *treifa* more broadly. The verse teaches otherwise: even *kohanim* may not eat *neveila* or *treifa* outside the permitted sacrificial context. The mention of both *of* and *beheima* is apt precisely because *melikah* applies to birds — yet even for birds, outside the sacrificial framework, the prohibition remains fully in force.

### Par Helem Davar

The *gemara* addresses yet another passage from *Yechezkel* 45: *ve-chein taaseh beshivah bachodesh me'ish shogeh u'mipeti ve-chipertem es habayis* — and so shall you do on the seventh of the month, from an erring and foolish person, and you shall atone for the House. Following the passage about the *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* purification, this verse mentions an act of atonement to be performed on the seventh of *Nissan*. What does "seventh of the month" mean in this context? *Rabbi Yochanan* explains that this is not a reference to the seventh of the month at all, but to the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* — the communal bull brought when the *Beis Din* rules incorrectly and the majority of the nation follows that ruling in error, the subject of the entire tractate of Horayot.

The reading is ingenious: *shiva* refers not to the seventh day but to seven tribes — if seven of the tribes sin, even if they do not constitute a numerical majority of the entire people, that counts as the nation having followed the ruling. The word *chodesh*, rather than meaning "month," is read as *chidush* — innovation — referring to the *Beis Din*'s innovative but erroneous ruling. And then *me'ish shogeh u'mipeti* — from an erring and foolish person — teaches: *melamed she'ein chayavim ela al he'elam davar im shigegat ma'aseh* — the communal *korban* is not triggered by an incorrect ruling alone; there must also be a corresponding act of error among the people who followed that ruling. Both elements — the ruling and the people's erroneous compliance — are required.

### Par Helem Requirements

This double requirement — an innovative erroneous ruling by the *Beis Din* and subsequent erroneous action by the people following it — is derived elegantly from the combination of terms in *Yechezkel*'s verse. The *he'elam*, the concealment, refers to the ruling; the *shegagah*, the error, refers to the people's action. Both must be present for the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* to be obligated.

### Chanina ben Chizkiya

The *gemara* concludes this sequence with a remarkable statement. *Rav Yehuda* said in the name of *Rav*: *zachor oto ha'ish letov ve-Chanina ben Chizkiya shemo* — that man should be remembered for good, and his name is *Chanina ben Chizkiya*. The reason: *she'ilmala hu nignaz sefer Yechezkel* — were it not for him, the book of *Yechezkel* would have been suppressed and excluded from the canon. Why? *Shehayu devarav sotrim divrei Torah* — because on their face, many of its passages contradict the Torah, as we have seen throughout this *sugya*: the animal counts differ from the Torah, the *chitta'ut* on *Rosh Chodesh Nissan* seems anomalous, and the passage about *neveila* and *treifa* appears to grant *kohanim* what it forbids to others. What did *Chanina ben Chizkiya* do? He brought three hundred flasks of oil — fuel to keep the lamps burning through many long nights — sat in his upper chamber, and worked through all the difficult passages until he found ways to interpret them that brought them into harmony with the Torah. This was the scholarly labor that preserved *Yechezkel* as part of *Tanach*.

## Rabbi Shimon & Nesachim

### Eifa Measurements

The *gemara* now returns to *Rabbi Shimon*'s position stated in the opening *mishna*, and demonstrates that he derives it from a verse in *Yechezkel*. A *baraita* cites *Yechezkel* 45:24: *ve'eifa la'par ve'eifa la'ayil ya'aseh mincha ve'lakvasim ka'asher tasig yado ve'shemen hin la'eifa* — an *eifa* for the bull and an *eifa* for the ram shall he prepare as a *mincha*, and for the sheep as he is able to afford, and oil, a *hin* per *eifa*. *Rabbi Shimon* notes a textual difficulty: is the measure for a bull and a ram truly the same *eifa*? In fact, they do not have the same measure. Moreover, the measure specified here does not match the standard Torah prescription. So what is the verse conveying?

### Animal Priority

*Rabbi Shimon* reads the verse as follows: since the Torah's prescribed measure is not being stated here literally, the verse must be conveying a halakhic principle. *She'im hayu lahem parim merubim velo hayu lahem nesachim yaviu par echad venesachav ve'ayil echad venesachav velo yakrivu kulam belo nesachim* — if they had the funds for many bulls but not enough for their *nesachim*, they should bring one bull with its *nesachim* and not sacrifice all of them without any *nesachim*. The verse teaches that the measure — the *mincha* and *nesachim* — must be paired with its animal, and that a paired offering is qualitatively superior to an unpaired one. The unit of animal-plus-*nesachim* is the ideal, and it is better to realize that ideal for one animal than to sacrifice many animals in a diminished form.

### Ram Priority

The same principle extends to rams: if there are funds for many rams but insufficient funds to provide each with its *nesachim*, one should bring a single ram with its proper *nesachim* rather than many rams without. *Rabbi Shimon*'s position, rooted in his reading of *Yechezkel*, is that completeness of the individual offering takes precedence over multiplicity of incomplete offerings.

## Ha-Par v'Ha-Eilim

### Mishna Text

The second *mishna* addresses the relationship between the various animals and the *shtei halechem* on *Shavuot*. It states: *ha-par veha'eilim veha'kevasim veha'se'ir ein me'akvin es ha-lechem* — the bull, the rams, the sheep, and the goat do not prevent the *shtei halechem*. The *korban* in question is the special *olah* brought together with the two loaves of *Shavuot*, comprising seven sheep, two rams, and one bull, plus a *se'ir* as a *chatat*. The *mishna* rules that the absence of any of these animals does not prevent bringing the *shtei halechem*, and the reverse is equally true: *velo halechem me'akvan* — if the *shtei halechem* is unavailable, the animals may still be offered. Each of the two *korbanot* can stand independently of the other.

### Rabbi Akiva

This independence between the *olah* and the bread does not, however, resolve the question of the relationship between the *shtei halechem* and the two lambs of *shelamim* that are waved together with it. These are distinct from the *olah*: the Torah states *u'shenei kevasim b'nei shana le'zevach shelamim*, two yearling lambs as a peace offering, and these are waved *al shenei kevasim* — specifically on the two lambs, together with the bread. This is a far tighter halakhic connection than the one between the *olah* and the bread.

Regarding these two lambs, *Rabbi Akiva* rules: *halechem me'akev es ha-kevasim ve'ein ha-kevasim me'akvin es ha-lechem* — the bread prevents the lambs, but the lambs do not prevent the bread. The bread is primary. Without bread, the lambs serve no purpose and should not be brought. But if the lambs are unavailable, the bread may still be brought on its own.

### Ben Nanos

*Ben Nanos* disagrees and reverses the ruling: *ha-kevasim me'akvin es ha-lechem ve'ha-lechem eino me'akev es ha-kevasim* — the lambs prevent the bread, but not the reverse. The lambs can stand without the bread; the bread cannot stand without the lambs. His proof: when *Bnei Yisrael* were in the wilderness for forty years, they brought the *korbanot* prescribed in *Emor* — including these lambs — but did not bring *shtei halechem*, since the Torah conditions the *shtei halechem* on entry into *Eretz Yisrael*. Thus we have historical precedent: the lambs were offered without the bread. The reverse was never done. Therefore, the lambs are the foundation, and the bread depends on them, not the other way around.

### Rabbi Shimon's Ruling

*Rabbi Shimon* rules: *halachah kedivrei Ben Nanos* — the law follows *Ben Nanos*. The lambs may be brought without the bread, but not vice versa. However, he explicitly rejects *Ben Nanos*'s reasoning.

### Wilderness Offering

*Rabbi Shimon* does not accept the claim that the lambs were actually offered without the bread during the wilderness period. His own reasoning is based on a different principle: *shekol ha'amur b'Chumash HaPikudim karav ba-midbar ve-chol ha'amur b'Toras Kohanim lo karav ba-midbar* — everything prescribed in *Bamidbar* (*Pinchas*) was offered in the wilderness, but what is prescribed in *Toras Kohanim* — specifically the *Shavuot* passage in *Emor* — was not offered in the wilderness at all. Since the special *olah* and the two lambs of *shelamim* are described in *Emor* in connection with the *shtei halechem*, and the *shtei halechem* only applies upon entry into *Eretz Yisrael*, the entire complex of *korbanot* described in that passage — the *olah*, the lambs, and the bread together — did not come into force until they entered the land. *Ben Nanos* is therefore wrong to say the lambs were offered without the bread in the wilderness; none of it was offered at all.

### Matir Concept

If the entire *Emor* complex only began in *Eretz Yisrael*, why does *Rabbi Shimon* still rule that the lambs may be brought without the bread? Because the determining factor is not conceptual primacy but the halakhic *matir* — the mechanism that renders a *korban* permitted for consumption after it has been consecrated. A sanctified offering cannot simply be eaten; something must first be placed on the *mizbeach*, and that act serves as the *matir* that releases the rest for consumption. For the two lambs of *shelamim*, the *matir* is their own blood, which is sprinkled on the *mizbeach*. They function as a complete, self-contained *korban* and may be offered independently. For the *shtei halechem*, however, there is no intrinsic *matir*: nothing from the bread itself goes on the *mizbeach*. The only act that serves as a *matir* for the bread is the slaughter of the lambs and the sprinkling of their blood. *Mipnei shehakvasim matirim et atzmam ve'lo halechem matir et atzmam* — the lambs have a *matir* and the bread does not. Consequently, the lambs can be brought without the bread; the bread cannot be brought without the lambs. This is *Rabbi Shimon*'s reasoning — entirely distinct from *Ben Nanos*'s historical argument, though they arrive at the same practical ruling.

## Gemara Analysis

### Obligation

A *baraita* opens: *ve'hikravtem al halechem chova* — "and you shall bring upon the bread" (from the *Emor* passage describing the special *olah*) — this is an obligation incumbent upon you. The text then states *shiv'at kevasim temimim* — seven lambs — and from this formulation the *baraita* derives that this *olah* may be brought even without the bread: *af al pi she'ein lechem*. The *olah* is independently obligatory, not merely incidental to the bread.

### Independence

This creates an apparent tension: the verse says *al halechem*, "upon the bread," linking the *olah* to the bread, yet the *baraita* derives that the *olah* may be brought without the bread. If so, what work does the phrase *al halechem* do? The *gemara* resolves this below.

### Rabbi Tarfon

*Rabbi Tarfon* explains: *melamed shelo nitchayvu bikvasim kodem shenitchayvu belechem* — the phrase *al halechem* teaches that the obligation to bring these *korbanot* did not take effect until the obligation of the bread took effect. In other words, the entire package — the *olah*, the lambs of *shelamim*, and the bread — was conditioned on entry into *Eretz Yisrael*, since the *shtei halechem* only applied there. The phrase *al halechem* signals that conceptually, all of this is "because of the bread," and therefore none of it began until the bread became obligatory. But in practice, once they entered *Eretz Yisrael*, the *olah* and the bread are not *me'akev* one another — each may be brought independently of the other.

### Rabbi Akiva

*Rabbi Akiva* builds on this and makes a further argument. One might have thought that the seven lambs described in *Emor* are the same as the seven lambs described in the *musaf* of *Shavuot* in *Pinchas*. If so, the *olah* in *Emor* would simply be the standard *musaf*, and the phrase *al halechem* would link the *musaf* itself to the bread. *Rabbi Akiva* refutes this: when one looks at the bulls and rams, the numbers are reversed. *Pinchas* prescribes two bulls and one ram; *Emor* prescribes one bull and two rams. This proves they are distinct *korbanot*. The *olah* in *Emor* is specific to the *shtei halechem*, while the *musaf* in *Pinchas* is the standard *Shavuot musaf* that has nothing to do with the bread. *Halalu ba'in biglal atzmam ve'halalu ba'in biglal lechem* — the former come on their own account and the latter come on account of the bread. Consequently, the *musaf* of *Bamidbar* was offered in the wilderness; the *olah* of *Emor* was not, consistent with what *Rabbi Shimon* stated in the *mishna*.

### Order Proof

The *gemara* raises a further challenge: perhaps both passages describe the same *musaf* and the Torah is simply offering a choice — two bulls and one ram, or one bull and two rams? The *gemara* rejects this by noting that not only are the numbers different, but the order of presentation is also different. In *Emor*: *shiv'at kevasim temimim u'far ben bakar echad ve'eilim shnayim* — seven sheep first, then one bull, then two rams. In *Pinchas*: *parim b'nei vakar shnayim* — it opens with the bulls. The Torah goes out of its way to present the animals in a different sequence, signaling unambiguously that these are fundamentally different *korbanot*.

### Conclusion

The *gemara* concludes: the *olah* in *Emor* is intrinsically connected to the *shtei halechem* — *al halechem* teaches that its time did not begin until they entered *Eretz Yisrael* — but once it came into force, the two *korbanot* do not prevent one another. The *olah* may be brought without the *shtei halechem* and the *shtei halechem* without the *olah*. Both *Rabbi Tarfon* and *Rabbi Akiva* affirm this principle, differing only in the specific derivations they emphasize. The *mishna*'s ruling — *ein me'akvin* between the animals and the bread — thus holds for this entire complex of offerings. The discussion of the two lambs of *shelamim* and their relationship to the bread, which turns on the *matir* principle analyzed above, will continue in the next *mishna*.
