# Berakhot 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Prior Daf Review

### Havdalah Ruling

Before turning to the new *perek*, a brief correction is in order regarding *havdalah* in *shomea tefillah*. Although *shomea tefillah* serves as an opportunity to make up missed insertions — such as *tal umatar livracha* — the ruling is that *havdalah* may not be inserted there. The reason is that *shomea tefillah* accommodates only *bakashot*, personal petitions. Just as one does not recite *mashiv haruach umorid hageshem* within *shmoneh esrei* if it was missed, one does not recite *havdalah* in *shomea tefillah*. Accordingly, if *havdalah* was omitted from *chonen hada'at*, it is skipped entirely within *shmoneh esrei* and recited instead *al hakos*.

### Missed Blessings

A related question concerns one who misses something like *tal umatar livracha* and has already passed *shomea tefillah* but has not yet concluded *shmoneh esrei*. There are two opinions: some hold that one returns to *shomea tefillah*, but the ruling is that one returns to the blessing where the insertion properly belongs — that is, to *birkat hashanim*. This parallels the rule that if *ya'aleh veyavo* is omitted, one returns to *retzei*, the blessing to which it belongs.

### Musaf Obligation

The *gemara* on the previous *daf* raised an interesting question surrounding *musaf*. Some opinions hold that the obligation of *musaf* applies only when there is a *tzibur*, or alternatively that an individual fulfills the obligation through the *tzibur* and need not recite *musaf* independently. The reasoning reflects a fundamental distinction: *musaf* is entirely oriented toward the *korban tzibur*, the communal sacrifice. It is therefore not amenable to a *tefillat nedavah*, which presupposes the framework of personal prayer. Though we rule that even a *yachid* with no *minyan* must recite *musaf*, this does not necessarily undermine the core principle that *musaf* belongs to the domain of communal sacrifice rather than personal prayer. The parameters of what constitutes a *tzibur* for this purpose — whether one's city, one's immediate community — are left undefined, presumably because the ruling was never adopted in practice and there was no need to resolve it halachically.

### Beit Midrash Prayer

A final theme from the previous *daf* deserves mention: the *rabbanim* who chose to pray in the *beit midrash* even though there were thirteen synagogues available to them. This reflects a broader and recurring tension — not only between *talmud Torah* and *tefillah* in general, but between two distinct institutional worlds. The *beit midrash* was a rabbinic domain, governed by the *rabbanim*, whereas the *batei kneisiyot* were organized under a different form of leadership. Something of that structural tension is alluded to in that discussion.

## Koved Rosh & Awe

### Standing with Seriousness

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אֵין עוֹמְדִים לְהִתְפַּלֵּל אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ כּוֹבֶד רֹאשׁ. חֲסִידִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים הָיוּ שׁוֹהִין שָׁעָה אַחַת, וּמִתְפַּלְּלִין, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּכַוְּונוּ לִבָּם לַאֲבִיהֶם שֶׁבַּשָּׁמַיִם. אֲפִילּוּ הַמֶּלֶךְ שׁוֹאֵל בִּשְׁלוֹמוֹ — לֹא יְשִׁיבֶנּוּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ נָחָשׁ כָּרוּךְ עַל עֲקֵבוֹ — לֹא יַפְסִיק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One** may **only stand** and begin to pray **from** an approach of **gravity** and submission. There is a tradition that **the early** generations of **pious** men **would wait one hour,** in order to reach the solemn frame of mind appropriate for prayer, **and** then **pray, so that they would focus their hearts toward their Father in Heaven.** Standing in prayer is standing before God and, as such, **even** if **the king greets him, he should not respond to him; and even if a snake is wrapped on his heel, he should not interrupt** his prayer.

The new *perek* moves beyond questions of the text and timing of *davening* — though those discussions contained within them important questions of *kavanah* and the tension expressed by *lo ta'as tefillatkha keva* — toward a direct examination of the mindset one brings to prayer. The *mishna* opens: *ein omdin lehitpallel ela mitoch koved rosh* — one does not stand to *daven* except from a sense of weightiness of head, of seriousness of purpose.

### Ancient Pious Ones

The *mishna* continues: *chassidim harishonim*, the ancient pious ones, would spend a full *sha'ah* — a full hour, or at minimum a significant period of time — before standing to pray, *kedei sheyachavnu libam l'avihem shebashamayim*, so that they might direct their hearts properly to their Father in Heaven when they rose to *daven*.

### No Interruption

From here the *mishna* turns to a related principle: the mindset of standing before God must be maintained throughout prayer itself. Even if a king greets one during prayer, one does not respond. This stands in direct contrast to the rules learned earlier regarding *shema*, where one may interrupt in certain circumstances to respond to a greeting. During *tefillah* there is no such latitude — *afilu nachash karuch al akevo lo yafsik*, even if a snake is coiled around one's ankle, one does not interrupt. The *gemara* will examine just how far this principle extends, including whether it applies literally in cases of genuine danger.

## Sources for Koved Rosh

### Chana's Anguish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: דְּאָמַר קְרָא ״וְהִיא מָרַת נָפֶשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that prayer should be undertaken in an atmosphere of gravity. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? Rabbi Elazar said: They are derived from the verses describing the prayer of Hannah, mother of Samuel, **as the verse states: "And she felt bitterness of soul,** and she prayed to the Lord and she wept and wept" (I Samuel 1:10).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא חַנָּה שָׁאנֵי, דַּהֲוָת מְרִירָא לִבָּא טוּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **From what** does that conclusion ensue? **Perhaps Hannah is different, as** her **heart was extremely embittered,** her prayer was embittered as well. This does not prove that everyone must pray in that frame of mind.

The *gemara* asks: *minayin*, from where is this requirement derived? Rabbi Elazar cites the verse describing Chana as *marat nafesh* — of anguished soul — positioning her as the paradigm for the proper disposition in prayer. This is a striking derivation. The *gemara* itself immediately challenges it: perhaps Chana is simply different, *dehavat mirira liba tufa*, she was exceptionally bitter because she was unable to bear children — that is a particular circumstance, not a universal model. The word *marah* translated literally means bitter, though "anguished" captures the sense more precisely in English.

### Fear & Awe

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, מֵהָכָא: ״וַאֲנִי בְּרֹב חַסְדְּךָ אָבוֹא בֵיתֶךָ אֶשְׁתַּחֲוֶה אֶל הֵיכַל קׇדְשְׁךָ בְּיִרְאָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said,** it can be proved **from here,** as David said: **"But as for me, by Your abundant loving-kindness I will enter Your house, at Your Holy Temple I will bow in reverence for You"** (Psalms 5:8). Entering into prayer like entering the Holy Temple must be performed reverentially.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא דָּוִד שָׁאנֵי, דַּהֲוָה מְצַעַר נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּרַחֲמֵי טוּבָא. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, מֵהָכָא: ״הִשְׁתַּחֲווּ לַה׳ בְּהַדְרַת קֹדֶשׁ״: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״בְּהַדְרַת״ אֶלָּא ״בְּחֶרְדַּת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof as well: **From what** does that conclusion ensue? **Perhaps David is different, as he would excessively afflict himself in prayer** in order to atone for his transgression with Bathsheba. Consequently, his cannot serve as a paradigm for proper conduct in prayer. **Rather, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said,** it can be derived **from here,** from this verse that David said, not about his own worship, but about worship of God in general: "Give, unto the Lord, the honor of His name, **bow to the Lord in the beauty of holiness [*behadrat kodesh*]"** (Psalms 29:2). **Do not read: In the beauty of [*behadrat*]** holiness. **Rather** read: **In trembling of [*beḥerdat*]** holiness; one must enter into prayer from an atmosphere of gravity engendered by sanctity.

Rabbi Yosef bar Rabbi Chanina therefore offers a different verse: *va'ani berov chasdecha avo veitecha, eshtachaveh el heichal kodshecha beyiratecha* — and I, through Your great kindness, will enter Your house; I will bow in Your holy palace, in awe of You. This is closer to the concept of *koved rosh*, since entering prayer with *yirah*, with fear and awe of God, is a more direct parallel than anguish. Again the *gemara* challenges: perhaps David is simply different — he would genuinely anguish himself in his prayers beyond the standard of others, and this verse describes his exceptional practice rather than setting a universal norm.

### Trembling Reinterpretation

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi therefore derives the requirement from yet another verse: *hishtachavu l'Hashem behadrat kodesh* — bow down before God in the glory of holiness. The *gemara* reads: *al tikrei behadrat ela becherdat* — do not read it as *behadrat*, in glory, but as *becherdat*, in trembling. This is a directive addressed to all, not a first-person account from a single figure. The *gemara* presses again: who says the wordplay of *hadrat* becoming *cherdat* is compelling? Why not take the plain meaning of the verse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? דִּילְמָא לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לְךָ, ״הַדְרַת״ מַמָּשׁ. כִּי הָא דְּרַב יְהוּדָה הֲוָה מְצַיֵּין נַפְשֵׁיהּ, וַהֲדַר מְצַלֵּי. אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק מֵהָכָא: ״עִבְדוּ אֶת ה׳ בְּיִרְאָה וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this too: **From what** does that conclusion ensue? **Perhaps, actually I would say to you** that it should be read as it is written: **Specifically, "in the beauty,"** and it means that one should pray in beautiful clothing, **as** in the case **of Rav Yehuda who would adorn himself and then pray.** Rav Yehuda believed that one who comes before the King must wear his most beautiful clothing. The Gemara has yet to find a source for the *halakha* that one must approach prayer from an atmosphere of gravity. **Rather, Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said** it can be derived **from here,** from this verse: **"Serve the Lord in fear and rejoice with trembling"** (Psalms 2:11).

The practical example offered is that of Rav Yehuda, who would dress himself nicely before praying — *metzayein nafshei vehadar matzlei* — suggesting that standing before God calls for a certain decorum and dignity. However, it is noted that proper dress reflects *kavod*, a sense of standing before the King, but this does not necessarily entail the sense of trembling and awe that the *gemara* is seeking to ground in the verse. There is, then, a distinction — and perhaps a productive tension — between *koved rosh* understood as seriousness of purpose, and *koved rosh* understood as trembling awe. The *gemara*'s various derivations explore both dimensions, moving from Chana's anguish through David's fearful intimacy to the trembling demanded of all.

## Service in Awe

### Prayer as Avodah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִכָּאן לָרוֹאֶה בַּחֲבֵרוֹ דָּבָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ הָגוּן, צָרִיךְ לְהוֹכִיחוֹ. ״וַתַּעַן חַנָּה וַתֹּאמֶר לֹא אֲדוֹנִי״. אָמַר עוּלָּא וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: לֹא אָדוֹן אַתָּה בְּדָבָר זֶה, וְלֹא רוּחַ הַקּוֹדֶשׁ שׁוֹרָה עָלֶיךָ, שֶׁאַתָּה חוֹשְׁדֵנִי בְּדָבָר זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **an unseemly matter, he must reprimand him,** is derived. **"And Hannah answered and she said no, my master,** I am a woman of sorrowful spirit, and I have drunk neither wine nor liquor, but I pour out my soul before the Lord" (I Samuel 1:15). Regarding the words: "No, my master," **Ulla, and some say Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said** that **she said to him,** in an allusion: **With regard to this matter, you are not a master, and the Divine Spirit does not rest upon you, as you** falsely **suspect me of this.**

The *gemara* finally settles on the verse from which *koved rosh* is definitively derived: *ivdu et Hashem beyirah vegilu bir'adah* — serve God in awe, and rejoice in trembling. Rashi, though the *gemara* itself does not make the connection explicit, links *ivdu* to the concept of *avodah*: *tefillah she-hee lanu b'makom avodah asu osah b'yirah* — prayer, which stands in place of the sacrificial service, should be performed in awe. Whether or not one draws the explicit link to *korbanot*, the core principle is clear: one approaches God in prayer with a sense of awe, and that is what is meant by *koved rosh*.

### Father & King

A sharp observation arises from the text of the *mishna* itself, where God is referred to as *avinu shebashamayim*, our Father in Heaven — likely the first instance in the *mishna* of this formulation. Yet the *gemara*'s derivations consistently emphasize distance, awe, and trembling before the divine King. This is the precise tension of *avinu malkenu*, which will be given expression so powerfully on the *yamim nora'im*: the closeness and intimacy of a parent alongside the deference and distance appropriate before a sovereign. One who is both prince and the king's child knows this double relationship well — there is still a certain submission because the father is, after all, the king. The discussion here does lean more heavily on the *malkhenu* dimension, on distance and awe, and it is worth noting that tension explicitly.

## Joy with Awe

### Awe Tempers Joy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא אָמַר רַבָּה: בִּמְקוֹם גִּילָה שָׁם תְּהֵא רְעָדָה.
>
> **Translation:** Having cited this verse from Psalms, the Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **rejoice with trembling? Rav Adda bar Mattana said** that **Rabba said:** One may not experience unbridled joy; even **where there is rejoicing, there should be trembling.**

The *gemara* now turns to the second half of the same verse: *vegilu bir'adah* — rejoice in trembling. What is the meaning of this paradoxical pairing? Rav Adda bar Matna said in the name of Rabbah: *b'makom gilah sham tehei ra'adah* — in the very place of rejoicing, there should be trembling. Tosafot notes that the plain sense of the verse may refer to the future: when the day of trembling, the *yom hadin*, arrives, those who have served God faithfully will rejoice. But the *gemara* here reads the verse as integrating both modes simultaneously in the present service of God — a joy in the closeness and intimacy with God, alongside the awe and trembling of standing in God's presence. These are not in conflict but are to be held together.

### Abaye's Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא בָדַח טוּבָא. אֲמַר ״וְגִילוּ בִּרְעָדָה״ כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** On that note, the Gemara relates: **Abaye was sitting before** his teacher **Rabba,** and Rabba **saw that he was excessively joyful. He said** to Abaye: **It is written: Rejoice with trembling,** one's joy should not be unrestrained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא תְּפִילִּין מַנַּחְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him:** It is permissible for me because **I am donning phylacteries** now and as long as they are upon me they ensure that the fear of God is upon me.

Abaye was once sitting before Rabbah and observed that Rabbah was laughing rather heartily — *kahava ka-badach tuva*. This was not during *davening* but apparently in the context of learning. Abaye, though Rabbah's student, offered a pointed remark: *vegilu bir'adah ksiv* — it is written, rejoice in trembling. The implication is that even outside of prayer, in the general conduct of *avodas Hashem*, excessive levity is out of place. Rabbah responded: *ana tfillin manachna* — I am wearing *tefillin*.

### Tefillin Reminder

Rashi explains: the *tefillin* are testimony — *eidus she-memsheles koni u-maruso alai* — that the sovereignty of one's Creator is upon one. Wearing *tefillin* keeps one perpetually in the context of awe. Even in the midst of joy and laughter, the *ra'adah* is maintained because God's presence is constantly signaled by the *tefillin*. Rabbah can laugh at a good moment and maintain the *gilah*, precisely because the *ra'adah* is never absent — it is kept present through the *tefillin* on his arm and head.

### Anguish Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא בָדַח טוּבָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״בְּכׇל עֶצֶב יִהְיֶה מוֹתָר״ כְּתִיב?
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yirmeya was sitting before Rabbi Zeira. He saw that Rabbi Yirmeya was excessively joyful. He said to him: It is written: "In all sorrow there is profit"** (Proverbs 14:23); sorrow is appropriate, not excessive joy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא תְּפִילִּין מַנַּחְנָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya **said to him:** It is permissible for me because **I am donning phylacteries.**

A parallel incident: Rabbi Yirmiyah was sitting before Rabbi Zeira, and Rabbi Zeira observed that Rabbi Yirmiyah was laughing excessively. Rabbi Zeira rebuked him with the verse *b'chol etzev yiheyeh motar* — with all anguish there shall be abundance — suggesting that a posture of anguish is fitting for one serving God. Rabbi Yirmiyah gave the identical response: *ana tfillin manachna*, I am wearing *tefillin*. The *tefillin*, in this reading, supply not merely the sense of divine sovereignty but also a consciousness of seriousness that extends throughout the day. This raises a broader question: is there an expectation of an ongoing consciousness of God at all times? It seems that at a minimum, one should not lose oneself in excessive levity. For those who do not wear *tefillin* throughout the day, one might suggest that the *kippah* serves a corresponding function as a constant reminder of one's standing before God — though the *gemara* itself does not say this.

### Breaking Glass

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא עֲבַד הִילּוּלָא לִבְרֵיהּ, חֲזַנְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דַּהֲווֹ קָבָדְחִי טוּבָא. אַיְּיתִי כָּסָא דְמוֹקְרָא בַּת אַרְבַּע מְאָה זוּזִי, וְתַבַּר קַמַּיְהוּ, וְאִעֲצִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** He brought a valuable cup worth four hundred *zuz* and broke it before them and they became sad.

Mar, the son of Ravina, made a wedding feast for his son. He observed the *rabbanim* having an excessively good time, laughing and jesting. He brought out a fine goblet worth four hundred *zuzei* and shattered it before them, and they became sorrowful. The point was made: even at one's greatest celebration, a consciousness of seriousness must be maintained.

### Rav Ashi's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי עֲבַד הִילּוּלָא לִבְרֵיהּ, חֲזַנְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דַּהֲווֹ קָא בָדְחִי טוּבָא. אַיְּיתִי כָּסָא דְּזוּגִּיתָא חִיוָּרְתָּא, וְתַבַּר קַמַּיְהוּ, וְאִעֲצִיבוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara also relates: **Rav Ashi made a wedding** feast **for his son** and **he saw the Sages, who were excessively joyous. He brought a cup of** extremely valuable **white glass and broke it before them, and they became sad.**

Rav Ashi similarly made a wedding feast for his son, observed the *rabbanim* laughing too much, brought out a white glass goblet of great value, and broke it before them, bringing them back to a state of sobriety. Tosafot draws the explicit line: *mikan nahagu leshaber zechuchit benisuin* — from here derives the custom of breaking a glass at a wedding. Notably, this derivation is distinct from the more commonly cited reason of *zecher lechurban*. Here it is not primarily about mourning the destruction of the Temple; it is about the general principle that even in the place of greatest joy, one must not allow oneself to become untethered from the consciousness of standing before God. The two rationales reflect different dimensions of the same underlying concern.

### Mortality Song

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב הַמְנוּנָא זוּטֵי בְּהִלּוּלָא דְּמָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: לִישְׁרֵי לַן מָר. אֲמַר לְהוּ: וַי לַן, דְּמִיתְנַן. וַי לַן, דְּמִיתְנַן. אָמְרִי לֵיהּ: אֲנַן מָה נַעֲנֵי בָּתְרָךְ? אֲמַר לְהוּ: הֵי תּוֹרָה, וְהֵי מִצְוָה דְּמַגְּנוּ עֲלַן.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, the Gemara relates: **The Sages said to Rav Hamnuna Zuti at the wedding** feast **of Mar, son of Ravina: Let the Master sing for us.** Since he believed that the merriment had become excessive, **he said to them,** singing: **Woe unto us, for we shall die, woe unto us, for we shall die. They said to him: What shall we respond after you?** What is the chorus of the song? **He said to them,** you should respond: **Where is Torah and where is mitzva that protect us?**

At the wedding feast of Mar the son of Ravina, the *rabbanim* asked Rav Hamnuna Zuti to sing for them. He sang: *vai lan demitnan, vai lan demitnan* — woe to us that we will die, woe to us that we will die. When asked for the refrain, he replied: *hei Torah vehei mitzvah demagnu lan* — where is the Torah and where is the *mitzvah* that will protect us? The message conveyed to those assembled: life is short, and its purpose is the service of God through Torah and *mitzvot*. Joy at a wedding is not to be replaced by despair, but it must be tempered by the awareness that *adam le'amal yulad* — a person is born to toil in divine service.

It is worth noting the observations prompted by this passage. The *rabbanim* in question are from the *amora'im* of Bavel, who in many respects were materially comfortable and socially stable. One could argue that precisely when life is going well — when the *rabbanim* of Bavel enjoy relative prosperity and security — the temptation to forget one's seriousness of purpose is greatest. The pointed rejoinder is not that joy itself is wrong, but that one must not lose the thread of purposeful living. The *gemara* here is not demanding constant anguish; it is insisting that even in joy, life must remain framed by the consciousness of one's mission.

### No Excessive Laughter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי: אָסוּר לְאָדָם שֶׁיְּמַלֵּא שְׂחוֹק פִּיו בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז יִמָּלֵא שְׂחוֹק פִּינוּ וּלְשׁוֹנֵנוּ רִנָּה״. אֵימָתַי, בִּזְמַן שֶׁ״יֹּאמְרוּ בַגּוֹיִם הִגְדִּיל ה׳ לַעֲשׂוֹת עִם אֵלֶּה״. אָמְרוּ עָלָיו עַל רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ שֶׁמִּיָּמָיו לֹא מִלֵּא שְׂחוֹק פִּיו בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, מִכִּי שַׁמְעַהּ מֵרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן רַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** In a similar vein, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai: One is forbidden to fill his mouth with mirth in this world,** as long as we are in exile (*ge'onim*), **as it is stated:** "When the Lord returns the captivity of Zion we will be as dreamers" (Psalms 126:1). Only **"then will our mouths fill with laughter and our lips with song"** (Psalms 126:2). **When** will that joyous era arrive? When **"they will say among nations, the Lord has done great things with these"** (Psalms 126:2). **They said about Reish Lakish that throughout his life he did not fill his mouth with laughter in this world once he heard this** statement **from his teacher, Rabbi Yoḥanan.**

Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yochai states — and this ruling is cited in the *Shulchan Aruch* — *assur leadam shimalay sechok piv ba'olam hazeh*, it is forbidden for a person to fill his mouth with laughter in this world. The proof text: *az yimalay sechok pinu uleshonenu rinah* — then our mouths will be filled with laughter and our tongues with song — meaning only at the time of ultimate redemption, when the nations will say that God has done greatly for Israel. Excessive laughter belongs to that future redeemed state; in the present, it is out of place.

### Reish Lakish Stringency

It is further related that Reish Lakish, from the moment he heard this teaching from Rabbi Yochanan, took great care never to fill his mouth with laughter. Beforehand, he had been able to do so freely; after hearing the *halacha*, he maintained this stringency for the rest of his life. It is striking that both Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish are from the *amora'im* of Eretz Yisrael, who had considerably less reason to be reminded of the fragility of existence and the weight of *galut* than their counterparts in Bavel. The *gemara* here is not demanding that one go through life miserable. What it is demanding — and what *Shulchan Aruch* begins with in a different key, *shivisi Hashem lenegdi tamid* — is a constant consciousness of standing before God, which naturally limits the degree to which one can abandon oneself to unrestrained levity.

## Prayer Mindset

### Not During Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין עוֹמְדִים לְהִתְפַּלֵּל לֹא מִתּוֹךְ דִּין, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבַר הֲלָכָה, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ הֲלָכָה פְּסוּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that it is appropriate to stand and begin to pray from an atmosphere of gravity. Regarding this, **the Sages taught: One** may **neither stand** and begin to pray, directly **from** involvement in **judgment nor** directly **from** deliberation over the ruling in a **matter of *halakha*,** as his preoccupation with the judgment or the halakhic ruling will distract him from prayer. **Rather** it is appropriate to pray directly **from** involvement in the study of **a** universally accepted **conclusive *halakha*** that leaves no room for further deliberation and will not distract him during prayer.

The *gemara* returns to the state of mind appropriate for *davening*. A *baraita* teaches: *ein omdin lehitpallel lo mitoch din velo mitoch devar halacha ela mitoch halacha pesuka* — one does not stand to pray from being in the middle of judgment, or from being engaged in an active halachic analysis, but only from *halacha pesuka*, a clear and settled ruling. This is distinct from the *mishna*'s emphasis on *koved rosh*, though it serves a related purpose. If one's mind is occupied with adjudicating a *din Torah* or working through a complex halachic question, one's attention is preoccupied — even with something of genuine importance — and that mental state is not the right launching point for *tefillah*.

### Halacha Pesuka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי הֲלָכָה פְּסוּקָה?
>
> **Translation:** **And** the Gemara asks: **What is an example** of a **conclusive *halakha*?**

One might ask: what is meant by *halacha pesuka*, and how does it differ from any other learning? The *gemara* provides several concrete examples in response to Abaye's question of *heichi dami halacha pesuka* — what does it look like in practice?

### Niddah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: כִּי הָא דְּרַבִּי זֵירָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בְּנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל הֶחֱמִירוּ עַל עַצְמָן, שֶׁאֲפִילּוּ רוֹאוֹת טִיפַּת דָּם כְּחַרְדָּל יוֹשְׁבוֹת עָלֶיהָ שִׁבְעָה נְקִיִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara offers several examples: **Abaye said:** One **like this** *halakha* **of Rabbi Zeira,** as **Rabbi Zeira said: The daughters of Israel were stringent with themselves;** to the extent **that even if they see a drop of blood corresponding to** the size of **a mustard** seed **she sits seven clean** days **for it.** By Torah law, a woman who witnesses the emission of blood during the eleven days following her fixed menstrual period is not considered a menstruating woman; rather she immerses herself and is purified the next day. However, the women of Israel accepted the stringency upon themselves that if they see any blood whatsoever, they act as it if were the blood of a *zava*, which obligates her to count seven more clean days before becoming ritually pure (see Leviticus 15:25).

The first example, offered by Abaye citing Rabbi Zeira, is the famous ruling at the conclusion of *masechet Niddah*: *benot Yisrael hechmiru al atzman* — Jewish women adopted a stringency upon themselves, such that even if they observe a drop of blood as small as a mustard seed — or smaller — they observe *shivah neki'im*, seven clean days. By Torah law, a woman waits seven days from the onset of bleeding and, if bleeding ceases before the end of the seventh day, goes to the *mikvah*. The status of *zavah* requiring seven clean days applies only if bleeding is observed on three consecutive days during the *zavah* period. The accepted practice, however, is to treat even a single drop of blood as conferring *zavah* status requiring *shivah neki'im*. This settled ruling is an example of *halacha pesuka*.

### Grain Tithe Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, כִּי הָא דְּרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: מַעֲרִים אָדָם עַל תְּבוּאָתוֹ וּמַכְנִיסָהּ בַּמּוֹץ שֶׁלָּהּ, כְּדֵי שֶׁתְּהֵא בְּהֶמְתּוֹ אוֹכֶלֶת, וּפְטוּרָה מִן הַמַּעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** Citing an additional example of a conclusive *halakha*, **Rava said:** One **like this** *halakha* **of Rav Hoshaya, as Rav Hoshaya said: A person may employ artifice** to circumvent obligations incumbent **upon** him in dealing with **his grain and bring it into** the courtyard **in its chaff so that his animal will eat** from it, **and** the grain **is exempt** from **tithes.** *Halakha* dictates that one is obligated to tithe grain that has been threshed and piled, regardless of the ultimate purpose for which the grain was intended. By Torah law, one is exempt from tithing grain that was not threshed and is therefore still in its chaff. By rabbinic law, one is prohibited from eating this grain in the framework of a meal. Feeding animals is permitted without first tithing that grain.

Rava offers a second example, citing the teaching of Rav Hoshaia: *ma'arim adam al tevo'aso umakhnisah bemotz shellah kedei shetehei behemto ochelet upeturah min hama'aser* — a person may arrange his grain cleverly by bringing it into the storage area while still in its chaff, so that his animal may eat from it and be exempt from *ma'aser*. The background requires unpacking. Grain ordinarily becomes obligated in *ma'aser* — *hukva lema'aser* — upon the completion of *miruach*, the smoothing of the threshed and winnowed pile, followed by bringing it into storage. Before that point, one may eat from it in a non-fixed manner (*achilas arai*), including feeding it to animals, without taking *terumos u'ma'asros*. If one reverses the order — bringing the grain into storage before *miruach* and performing *miruach* inside — the grain technically never becomes fully obligated, and the entire pile may be used for animal feed (or eaten in a non-fixed manner), without separating *terumos u'ma'asros* at all. That is the ruling.

### Sacrifice Blood Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, כִּי הָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַבִּי זְעֵירָא: הַמַּקִּיז דָּם בְּבֶהֱמַת קׇדָשִׁים, אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּמוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead yet another example of a conclusive *halakha*, which is the recommended prelude to prayer. One **like this** *halakha* **of Rav Huna,** as **Rav Huna said** that **Rabbi Zeira said: One who lets blood from a consecrated animal** that was consecrated as a sacrifice; deriving **benefit** from that blood **is prohibited.** Although blood of an offering that was sprinkled on the altar is not considered Temple property, nevertheless, deriving benefit from the blood of a living, consecrated animal is considered prohibited use of Temple property. In so doing, **one misuses** property consecrated to the Temple, and as in any other case of misusing Temple property, if he did so unwittingly, he is liable to bring a guilt-offering.

A third example, cited by Rav Huna in the name of Rav Zevid: *hamakiz dam bivehemat kodashim assur behanah u'mo'alin bo* — if one lets blood from a sanctified animal, that blood is forbidden for benefit, and using it constitutes *me'ilah*. The general principle is that the blood designated to be thrown on the *mizbeiach* is exempt from *me'ilah*, as the verse states *ani nesativ lachem al hamizbeiach lechaper* — I have given it to you for atonement — implying that the blood is given over for that purpose and not subject to *me'ilah*. However, if blood is drawn from a sanctified animal for a medical reason — to treat the animal — that blood is not being used for the *mizbeiach* and therefore falls under *me'ilah*. Deriving benefit from it is forbidden, and doing so requires a *korban me'ilah*. That too is an example of *halacha pesuka*.

### Two Approaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּנַן עָבְדִי כְּמַתְנִיתִין, רַב אָשֵׁי עָבֵיד כְּבָרַיְיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is related that **the Sages acted in accordance with** the opinion of **our mishna** and rose to pray from an atmosphere of gravity; **Rav Ashi acted in accordance with** the opinion of **the *baraita*** and preceded his prayer with a conclusive *halakha*.

Having established the content of *halacha pesuka*, the *gemara* notes: *rabbanan avdi kemasnishin* — the students of the *yeshiva* acted in accordance with the *mishna*, focusing on *koved rosh*, seriousness and awe. *Rav Ashi avid ke-braisa* — Rav Ashi acted in accordance with the *baraita*, standing to *daven* from *halacha pesuka*. The fact that some followed the *mishna* and others the *baraita* suggests these are not mutually exclusive alternatives or competing rulings, but rather different paths to achieving the same disposition.

### Framing Service

The question arises: why are the examples of *halacha pesuka* so complex? These are not simple rulings like "one must pray three times a day." One could speculate that for the *rabbanim* spending their days in serious study, something too elementary does not engage the mind in the same way. A ruling with a degree of complexity — but one that resolves clearly into a firm bottom line — may be precisely what focuses the *talmid chacham* before prayer.

But there may be a deeper point as well. *Halacha pesuka* is not merely a psychological focusing device. It may represent a positive framing of what *davening* is. To engage with *halacha* immediately before *tefillah* is to make a statement that one's life is directed by God's law, that the details of one's conduct are governed by divine concern. That framing — of a life lived under halachic discipline and purpose — is precisely the right disposition from which to approach God in prayer. It is not merely that one's mind is focused; it is that one presents oneself before God as someone engaged in purposeful, God-directed service. One student's formulation captures this well: when the connection between *halacha* study and *tefillah* is made explicit, one's learning becomes integrated into one's sense of divine service rather than remaining a separate intellectual exercise. The *tefillah* is then not an interruption of learning but its natural culmination.

It is worth noting a contemporary parallel: in the *siddur*, after *birkat haTorah*, there is the custom of reciting a passage of Torah. The prayer most commonly associated with preparation for *shmoneh esrei* at *mincha* — where the structure allows for something to be inserted before *shmoneh esrei*, unlike *shacharis* and *ma'ariv* which are fixed around *geulah* and *tefillah* — is *ashrei*. *Ashrei yoshvei veitekha* is perhaps more an embodiment of the spirit of the *mishna*'s *koved rosh* than of *halacha pesuka* strictly speaking, but the practice points to the same underlying concern: one does not simply walk into *shmoneh esrei* cold.

## Forbidden States

### Negative States List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין עוֹמְדִים לְהִתְפַּלֵּל לֹא מִתּוֹךְ עַצְבוּת, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ עַצְלוּת, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ שְׂחוֹק, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ שִׂיחָה, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבָרִים בְּטֵלִים, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ שִׂמְחָה שֶׁל מִצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** On the topic of proper preparation for prayer, **the Sages taught: One may neither stand to pray from** an atmosphere of **sorrow nor from** an atmosphere of **laziness, nor from** an atmosphere of **laughter, nor from** an atmosphere of **conversation, nor from** an atmosphere of **frivolity, nor from** an atmosphere of **purposeless matters. Rather,** one should approach prayer **from** an atmosphere imbued with **the joy of a mitzva.**

The *baraita* continues with an expanded list: *ein omdin lehitpallel lo mitoch atzvut* — one does not stand to *daven* from sadness, *velo mitoch atzlut* — nor from laziness, *velo mitoch sechok* — nor from having just been engaged in excessive laughter, *velo mitoch sicha* — nor from idle conversation, *velo mitoch kalut rosh* — nor from levity, *velo mitoch dvarim betelim* — nor from frivolous talk. Many of these overlap with what came before; together they paint a picture of the mental states that are incompatible with *tefillah*.

### Simcha Shel Mitzvah

The positive counterpart: *ela mitoch simcha shel mitzvah* — one should stand from a joy of *mitzvah*. This is striking, and seemingly in some tension with the emphasis on awe, trembling, and *koved rosh* that has dominated the discussion until now. The *baraita* here introduces a different register: not awe and weight, but joy.

### Rashi's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לֹא יִפָּטֵר אָדָם מֵחֲבֵרוֹ לֹא מִתּוֹךְ שִׂיחָה, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ שְׂחוֹק, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ קַלּוּת רֹאשׁ, וְלֹא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבָרִים בְּטֵלִים, אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבַר הֲלָכָה. שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בַּנְּבִיאִים הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, שֶׁסִּייְּמוּ דִּבְרֵיהֶם בְּדִבְרֵי שֶׁבַח וְתַנְחוּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Similarly, a person should neither take leave of another from** an atmosphere of **conversation, nor from** an atmosphere of **laughter, nor from** an atmosphere of **frivolity, nor from** an atmosphere of **purposeless matters. Rather,** one should take leave of another **from** involvement in a **matter of *halakha*. As we found in** the books of the Bible dealing with **the early prophets, that they would conclude their talks with words of praise and consolation.**

Rashi elaborates: *simcha shel mitzvah* means *divrei tanchumim shel Torah*, words of consolation and encouragement from Torah. He offers examples: passages adjacent in the *davening* to redemption themes, such as *ratzon yerei'av ya'aseh*, or *lo yitosh Hashem amo*. The point is that one approaches *tefillah* from a state of joy in one's relationship with God, in the awareness of God's care and presence. *Mitzvah* in this context does not refer to performing an act but to this joyful orientation toward divine closeness. This is a markedly different framing from the *halacha pesuka* of the *baraita* — which may speak more specifically to the *rabbinic* class — and a different framing from the *koved rosh* of the *mishna*. These are not so much competing requirements as they are different valid orientations, available to different people or in different circumstances.

### Parting from Friends

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן תְּנָא מָרִי בַּר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא: אַל יִפָּטֵר אָדָם מֵחֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא מִתּוֹךְ דְּבַר הֲלָכָה, שֶׁמִּתּוֹךְ כָּךְ זוֹכְרֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And so Mari, the grandson of Rav Huna, son of Rabbi Yirmeya bar Abba, taught in a *baraita*: One should only take leave of another from** involvement in a **matter of *halakha*, so that, consequently, he will remember him;** whenever he recalls the one from whom he took leave, he will think well of him because of the new *halakha* that he taught him (*Eliyahu Zuta*).

A parallel principle is offered: *vechen lo yiparter adam mechaveiro lo mitoch sicha velo mitoch sechok velo mitoch kalut rosh velo mitoch dvarim betelim, ela mitoch dvar halacha*. When parting from a friend, one should not conclude the encounter with idle talk, laughter, or levity. The parting should involve a *dvar halacha*. The *Hagra* notes that the text is in tension here: we have just spoken of *simcha shel mitzvah* as the positive frame, and now we return to *dvar halacha*. He therefore marks the textual point and suggests making the two consistent — reading *dvar halacha* here as aligned with *simcha shel mitzvah*. Whether or not one accepts that textual emendation, the underlying tension points to the same two orientations that appeared in the context of prayer: the frame of halachic engagement, and the frame of joyful connection to God.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא אַלְוְיֵיהּ לְרַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי מִפּוּם נַהֲרָא עַד בֵּי צִנְיָתָא דְּבָבֶל. כִּי מְטָא לְהָתָם אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָר, וַדַּאי דְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: הָנֵי צִנְיָתָא דְּבָבֶל אִיתַנְהוּ מֵאָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן וְעַד הַשְׁתָּא?
>
> **Translation:** **As** in the incident related by the Gemara **that Rav Kahana accompanied Rav Shimi bar Ashi from** the town of **Pum Nahara to the palm grove** in **Babylonia. When he arrived there,** Rav Kahana **said to** Rav Shimi bar Ashi: **Master, what is meant by that which people say: These palm trees** of **Babylonia have been** in this place from the time of **Adam the first** man **until now?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַדְכַּרְתַּן מִילְּתָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״בְּאֶרֶץ אֲשֶׁר לֹא עָבַר בָּהּ אִישׁ וְלֹא יָשַׁב אָדָם שָׁם״, וְכִי מֵאַחַר דְּלֹא עָבַר הֵיאַךְ יָשַׁב? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ כׇּל אֶרֶץ שֶׁגָּזַר עָלֶיהָ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן לְיִשּׁוּב — נִתְיַשְּׁבָה. וְכׇל אֶרֶץ שֶׁלֹּא גָּזַר עָלֶיהָ אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן לְיִשּׁוּב — לֹא נִתְיַשְּׁבָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Shimi bar Ashi **said to him: You reminded me of something that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina,** said, **as Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Ḥanina, said: What is** the meaning of **that which is written: "In a land through which no man has passed and where no person [*adam*] has settled"** (Jeremiah 2:6)? This verse is difficult; **since it is** a land through which **no** person **has passed, how** could anyone **have settled** there permanently? The statement that "no person has settled there" is redundant. **Rather,** this verse comes **to teach** that **every land** through **which Adam the first** man passed and **decreed that it would be settled was settled, and every land** through **which** Adam passed and **decreed that it would not be settled was not settled.** Based on this, what people say is true, and the palm trees of Babylonia are from the time of Adam, meaning that from the time of Adam this land was decreed to be suitable for growing palm trees (*Me'iri*). The Gemara cited an example of how one who parts from another with Torah learns something new.

The *gemara* grounds this in a broader observation: *shechen matzinu binevi'im harishonim shesiyimu divreihem bedivrei shevach vetanchumim* — the early prophets, with one or two notable exceptions such as Yirmiyahu, concluded their prophecies on a positive note regarding Israel's relationship with God. *Simcha shel mitzvah* in this context means ending on a note that expresses the joy of that divine relationship, a kind of *berakhah* of closeness with God.

### Devar Torah Custom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מָרְדְּכַי אַלְוְיֵיהּ לְרַב שִׁימִי בַּר אָשֵׁי מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא, וְעַד בֵּי כֵיפֵי. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ עַד בֵּי דוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned the mitzva for a student to accompany his Rabbi, the Gemara relates that **Rav Mordekhai accompanied** his mentor, **Rav Shimi bar Ashi,** a great distance, **from** the city of **Hagronya to Bei Keifei; and some say** that he accompanied from Hagronya **to Bei Dura.**

The *gemara* provides a practical illustration: Rav Kahana accompanied Rav Shimi bar Ashi all the way from Pumna to Beit Tzanisa in Bavel. Upon arriving, Rav Kahana remarked that the palm trees of Beit Tzanisa must be so ancient they date back to *Adam haRishon*. Rav Shimi responded: *adkartan milta d'Rabbi Yose bar Chanina* — you have reminded me of a teaching of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina. Rabbi Yose bar Chanina had expounded the verse *b'eretz asher lo avar bah ish v'lo yashav adam sham* — a land that no person has passed through and no *adam* has dwelt in — by asking: if no one ever passed through, how could anyone have dwelt? The verse must be using *adam* in the sense of *Adam haRishon*, teaching that any land *Adam haRishon* designated for settlement was settled, and any he did not designate was not — which is why Beit Tzanisa retained its ancient palm trees to this day.

Rav Mordechai similarly accompanied Rav Shimi bar Ashi from Hagronia to Bei Kifi, or some say to Bei Dura. The point in both cases is the custom of accompanying departing guests — a practice with its own halachic context — and of concluding the encounter with words of Torah. The category of *dvar halacha* proves fairly elastic here: what Rav Shimi offers is a piece of rabbinic *aggadah* and textual interpretation, not a practical ruling. This suggests that *dvar halacha* in this context means broadly any substantive Torah teaching. The frame is what matters: that the parting be shaped by Torah rather than by mere small talk. In the world of the *rabbanim*, the natural way to frame a relationship — both in parting and in approaching *tefillah* — is through the medium of Torah. For those outside that world, or in different contexts, *simcha shel mitzvah*, an expression of the joyful relationship with God, serves the same function.

## Kavana Requirement

### Heart to Heaven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַמִּתְפַּלֵּל צָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּכַוֵּין אֶת לִבּוֹ לַשָּׁמַיִם. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: סִימָן לַדָּבָר, ״תָּכִין לִבָּם תַּקְשִׁיב אׇזְנֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** Returning to the topic of preparation for prayer, **the Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: **One who prays must focus his heart toward Heaven. Abba Shaul says: An indication of** the importance of this **matter** is stated in the verse: "The desire of the humble You have heard, Lord; **direct their hearts, Your ear will listen"** (Psalms 10:17). In other words, if one focuses his heart in prayer as a result of God directing his heart, his prayer will be accepted as God's ear will listen.

The *gemara* now focuses directly on *kavanah*. *Hamispallel tzarich sheyachavein libo lashamayim* — one who prays must direct one's heart to Heaven. *Abba Shaul* frames this as a reciprocal relationship: if one prepares and directs one's heart, God's ears will be attentive. This is the core challenge of *tefillah* as the *gemara* understands it, and it is a formidable one. *Tefillah* is not merely the recitation of words; it is communication, the expression of one's inner state. How does one legislate *kavanah*? The tension between fixed prayer and personal prayer — between the communal structure inherited as *tefillot keneged korbanot* and the deeply interior act of turning one's heart to God — has run through the entire discussion of these chapters, and it is this tension that the present *mishna* now addresses head-on.

### Rabbi Akiva's Practice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כָּךְ הָיָה מִנְהָגוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, כְּשֶׁהָיָה מִתְפַּלֵּל עִם הַצִּיבּוּר — הָיָה מְקַצֵּר וְעוֹלֶה, מִפְּנֵי טוֹרַח צִבּוּר. וּכְשֶׁהָיָה מִתְפַּלֵּל בֵּינוֹ לְבֵין עַצְמוֹ — אָדָם מַנִּיחוֹ בְּזָוִית זוֹ, וּמוֹצְאוֹ בְּזָוִית אַחֶרֶת. וְכׇל כָּךְ לָמָּה? מִפְּנֵי כְּרִיעוֹת וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָיוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to one's intent during prayer, **it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehuda said: This was the custom of Rabbi Akiva, when he would pray with the congregation he would shorten** his prayer **and go up, due to** his desire to avoid being an **encumbrance on the congregation** by making them wait for him to finish his prayer. **But when he prayed by himself** he would extend his prayers to an extent that **a person would leave** Rabbi Akiva alone **in one corner** of the study hall **and** later **find him** still praying **in another corner. And why** would Rabbi Akiva move about **so much? Because of his bows and prostrations.** Rabbi Akiva's enthusiasm in prayer was so great, that as a result of his bows and prostrations, he would unwittingly move from one corner to the other (Rav Hai Gaon).

The *gemara* cites the practice of Rabbi Akiva: *keshehaya mispalel im hatzibur, haya mekatzeir veoleh mipnei torech tzibur* — when he prayed with the congregation, he would shorten his prayer out of consideration for the burden it places on the *tzibur* to wait. But *keshehaya mispalel beino levein atzmo*, when he prayed in private, one might leave him in one corner of the room and return to find him in the other corner — *mipnei kri'os vehishtachavayos*, because of his extensive bowing and swaying, his physical movement in prayer carrying him bodily across the room. Some scholars have suggested that Rabbi Akiva's mystical and kabbalistic orientation may have contributed to a near-rapturous state during private prayer, beyond simple *shokeling*. Whether or not that is the case, the contrast is unmistakable.

### Communal vs. Private

The contrast between Rabbi Akiva's communal and private prayer raises a fundamental question. One explanation is straightforward: there is a *bein adam lachaveiro* dimension to communal prayer — one cannot indulge one's own *kavanah* if doing so burdens the community that is waiting. But the *gemara* may be pointing at something deeper: communal prayer and private prayer exist in different registers. In communal prayer, one participates in a collective act, analogous in its way to the *korban tzibur*, and the communal experience takes precedence over the individual's subjective intensity. In private prayer, by contrast, the full weight of personal *kavanah* can be given free expression.

This surfaces a question that is genuinely unresolved in halachic literature: should one prefer praying with a *minyan* at the cost of some *kavanah*, or praying in private with fuller attention? The *poskim* generally favor communal prayer, emphasizing the external structure of *tefillah betzibur* — in part because *halacha* is better equipped to address and evaluate external forms than internal states, which cannot be quantified or measured. Yet the *gemara*'s presentation of Rabbi Akiva suggests that the private, interior dimension of *tefillah* is equally legitimate and in some respects more demanding. One might also add that the prohibition against audibly vocalizing one's prayer in a way that could distract others in the congregation points to the same tension: when in the *tzibur*, one reins in the more expressive dimensions of prayer for the sake of the communal experience. When praying *beyechidus*, no such restraint is needed.

## Tanakh Models

### Windows in Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: לְעוֹלָם יִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם בְּבַיִת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ חַלּוֹנוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְכַוִּין פְּתִיחָן לֵיהּ וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Many *halakhot* are derived from evoking the prayers of biblical characters. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said: One should always pray in a house with windows, as it is stated** regarding Daniel: "And when Daniel knew that the writing was signed, he went to his house. In his attic **there were open windows** facing Jerusalem, and three times a day he knelt upon his knees and prayed and gave thanks before his God, just as he had done before" (Daniel 6:11).

Rabbi Abba teaches: *le'olam yitpallel adam bevayit sheyesh bo chalonot* — a person should always pray in a house that has windows. The proof is from Daniel, who prayed opposite open windows. There is something to be said for this experientially: the sense of connectedness to the natural world — to sky, light, and the broader creation — can open the person who prays to a sense of divine presence that can be harder to access in an entirely enclosed, artificial setting. The *gemara*'s instruction to pray opposite a window points to this dimension of *kavanah* through the orientation of the body and the eyes toward an opening to the world.

### Three Times Daily

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ — כְּבָר מְפוֹרָשׁ עַל יְדֵי דָּנִיאֵל: ״וְזִמְנִין תְּלָתָא וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** In the *Tosefta*, additional *halakhot* were derived from Daniel's prayer. I **might have** thought **that one could pray** as many times as he wishes **throughout the entire day; it has already been articulated by Daniel,** with regard to whom it is stated: **"And three times** a day he knelt upon his knees and prayed." This teaches that there are fixed prayers.

Perhaps one should pray throughout the entire day? The *gemara* answers: it is already made explicit by Daniel — *vezimnin telata*, three times daily. Notably, the proof is drawn from Daniel and not from the sacrificial calendar. The entire framework here is of prayer as a human act of turning toward God, not as a stand-in for *korbanot*. Three fixed times suffice; they divide and mark the day as oriented toward God.

### Toward Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל מִשֶּׁבָּא לַגּוֹלָה הוּחַלָּה — כְּבָר נֶאֱמַר: ״דִּי הֲוָה עָבֵד מִן קַדְמַת דְּנָא״.
>
> **Translation:** **I might have thought that this** practice of fixed prayer **began** only **when he came to** the Babylonian **exile; it was stated:** "Just **as he had done before."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִתְפַּלֵּל אָדָם לְכׇל רוּחַ שֶׁיִּרְצֶה — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״(לָקֳבֵל) [נֶגֶד] יְרוּשְׁלֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** Further, I **might have** thought **that one may pray** facing **any direction he wishes; the verse states:** The appropriate direction for prayer is **"facing Jerusalem."**

Perhaps one may pray in any direction? The *gemara* answers: one prays *neged Yerushalayim*, toward Jerusalem. Whether this is because Jerusalem is the site of the *Shekhinah* or because it connects to the *korbanot* of the *Beit Hamikdash* is left open. But the thrust of the entire *sugya* — which has consistently cited human models of prayer (Daniel, Chana, David) without invoking sacrificial language — suggests that the orientation toward Jerusalem is about the *Shekhinah* and the locus of divine presence rather than about the sacrificial service.

### Silent Prayer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יְהֵא כּוּלְלָן בְּבַת אַחַת — כְּבָר מְפוֹרָשׁ עַל יְדֵי דָּוִד, דִּכְתִיב: ״עֶרֶב וָבֹקֶר וְצָהֳרַיִם וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** Daniel does not describe how these three prayers are distributed during the day. I **might have** thought **that one may include all** three prayers **at one time; it has already been articulated by David** that one may not do so, **as it is written: "Evening and morning and noon,** I pray and cry aloud and He hears my voice" (Psalms 55:18).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יַשְׁמִיעַ קוֹלוֹ בִּתְפִלָּתוֹ — כְּבָר מְפוֹרָשׁ עַל יְדֵי חַנָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְקוֹלָהּ לֹא יִשָּׁמֵעַ״.
>
> **Translation:** Furthermore, **I might have** thought **that one may make his voice heard in his** *Amida* **prayer; it has already been articulated by Hannah** in her prayer, **as it is stated:** "And Hannah spoke in her heart, only her lips moved **and her voice could not be heard"** (I Samuel 1:13).

Perhaps one's voice should be audible during prayer? The *gemara* answers from Chana: *vekola lo yishama* — her voice was not heard. The model of silent, inward prayer is thus established from Chana, who will emerge throughout these *sugyot* as the paradigmatic figure for the laws of *tefillah*.

### Personal Requests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל יִשְׁאַל אָדָם צְרָכָיו וְאַחַר כָּךְ יִתְפַּלֵּל — כְּבָר מְפוֹרָשׁ עַל יְדֵי שְׁלֹמֹה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לִשְׁמֹעַ אֶל הָרִנָּה וְאֶל הַתְּפִלָּה״. ״רִנָּה״ זוֹ תְּפִלָּה, ״תְּפִלָּה״ זוֹ בַּקָּשָׁה. אֵין אוֹמֵר דְּבַר בַּקָּשָׁה אַחַר ״אֱמֶת וְיַצִּיב״. אֲבָל אַחַר הַתְּפִלָּה, אֲפִילּוּ כְּסֵדֶר וִדּוּי שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** *Halakhot* regarding the order of the prayers were also learned from the prayers of biblical characters. I **might have** thought **that one should request his own needs first, and afterwards recite prayers** of thanksgiving and praise; **it has already been articulated by Solomon** that this is not so, as in Solomon's prayer at the dedication of the Holy Temple **it is stated: "To hear the song and the prayer** that Your servant prays before You today" (I Kings 8:28). In this verse, **song is prayer** in the sense of thanks and praise, and **prayer is** one's **request** of his personal needs. Therefore, one who is praying **does not speak matters of request after** he began to recite ***emet veyatziv*** prior to the *Amida* prayer, which is the essence of prayer. Rather, he begins with praise in the first three blessings of the *Amida* prayer, and only thereafter does he include requests for his needs. **But after the** *Amida* **prayer** there is no limit. If he desires to recite **even the equivalent of the order of the confession of Yom Kippur,** he may **recite** it.

The *gemara* asks one final question: perhaps personal petitions should come *before* prayer? The answer comes from Shlomo: the verse places *rinah* — understood as *tefillah* in the sense of *shevach*, praise — before *tefillah* in the sense of *bakashah*, petition. The praise comes first, the petition follows. Fascinatingly, the word *tefillah* in the verse is mapped onto *bakashah*, while *rinah* is mapped onto the *shevach* dimension — an implicit acknowledgment that *tefillah* in common usage means petition, even as the structural priority of praise is maintained.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר נָמֵי, אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי אָמַר רַב: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ שׁוֹאֵל אָדָם צְרָכָיו בְּ״שׁוֹמֵעַ תְּפִלָּה״, אִם בָּא לוֹמַר אַחַר תְּפִלָּתוֹ — אֲפִילּוּ כְּסֵדֶר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים אוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** This **was also stated** by an *amora*; **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi said** that **Rav said: Although** the Sages **said** that **one requests his** personal **needs in** the blessing: **Who listens to prayer,** that is with regard to one who wishes to do so as part of the *Amida* prayer. **If he comes** to add **and recite** additional requests **after** completing **his** *Amida* **prayer, even** if his personal requests are **the equivalent of the order of the confession of Yom Kippur,** he may **recite** them.

The *gemara* concludes with a practical clarification: one does not interrupt between *emet veyatziv* and *shmoneh esrei* — *geulah* must flow into *tefillah*. But after *shmoneh esrei*, one may extend as long as one wishes, even as long as the *vidui* of *Yom Kippur*. And Rav Yitzchak bar Ashi adds in the name of Rav: *af yishal adam tzerakhav beshome'a tefillah* — one may also insert personal requests within *shmoneh esrei* itself, in the blessing of *shome'a tefillah*. Thus the balance between fixed structure and personal expression is preserved at multiple levels: brief insertions within the body of *shmoneh esrei* in *shome'a tefillah*, and unlimited personal prayer after the conclusion of *shmoneh esrei* before *oker raglav*. The *gemara* continues with the model of Chana, whose prayers will provide the ground for many further laws of *tefillah*.
