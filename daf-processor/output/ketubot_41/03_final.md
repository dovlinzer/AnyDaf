# Ketubot 41 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Goring Ox Damages

### Tam vs. Muad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר: ״פִּתִּיתִי אֶת בִּתּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס. הָאוֹמֵר: ״גָּנַבְתִּי״ — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וְאֵין מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְתַשְׁלוּמֵי אַרְבָּעָה וַחֲמִשָּׁה. ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרִי אֶת פְּלוֹנִי״, אוֹ ״שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — הֲרֵי זֶה מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרִי עַבְדּוֹ שֶׁל פְּלוֹנִי״ — אֵין מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם יָתֵר עַל מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who says: I seduced the daughter of so-and-so, pays** compensation for **humiliation and degradation** based **on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **fine.** Similarly, **one who says: I stole, pays the principal,** the value of the stolen goods, **based on his own** admission, **but does not pay** the **double payment and** the **payment four and five** times the principal for the slaughter or sale of the sheep or ox that he stole. Likewise if he confessed: **My ox killed so-and-so, or:** My ox killed **an ox** belonging to **so-and-so, this** owner **pays** based **on his own** admission. However, if he said: **My ox killed a slave** belonging to **so-and-so, he does not pay** based **on his own** admission as that payment is a fine. **This is the principle: Anyone who pays more than what he damaged,** the payments are fines and therefore **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission. He pays only based on the testimony of others.

The daf begins at the top of 41b, wrapping up a *sugya* that has been unfolding since the previous session. The central question is whether the half-payment (*chatzi nezek*) imposed when an unhabituated ox (*tam*) gores is classified as a *knas* — a monetary fine — or as *mammon*, an ordinary compensatory obligation. The practical difference is significant: a *knas* cannot be collected based on the tortfeasor's own admission (*al pi atzmo*), whereas a *mammon* obligation can. The debate among the *Amoraim* turns on a conceptual question: are oxen presumed to be naturally guarded and non-dangerous, such that when a *tam* gores it falls shy of actionable negligence and the half-payment is therefore a punitive *knas*? Or is a goring ox just barely over the threshold of negligence, so that the half-payment represents partial compensation — *tashlumim* — rather than a fine?

### Muad Direct Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: מָה בֵּין תָּם לְמוּעָד, שֶׁהַתָּם מְשַׁלֵּם חֲצִי נֶזֶק מִגּוּפוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם נֶזֶק שָׁלֵם מִן הָעֲלִיָּיה. וְלָא קָתָנֵי שֶׁהַתָּם אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, וּמוּעָד מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues. **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from a *baraita*: **What is** the difference **between an innocuous and a forewarned** ox? The difference is **that** the owner of **an innocuous** ox **pays half** of the **damage from its body.** Compensation for the damage may be collected only from the body of the ox that gored another ox. If the ox that gored another ox is worth less than half the damage, e.g., if an inexpensive ox killed an expensive one, the injured party receives less than half the damage. **And** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays the entire damage from the** owner's **property,** and the value of the ox that gored another ox has no effect on the payment. The *tanna* **did not teach** an additional difference that the owner of **an innocuous** ox **does not pay** based **on his own** admission **and** the owner of **a forewarned** ox **pays** based **on his own** admission. Apparently, the half damage is a payment of money and not a fine.

The Gemara opens with a *ta shma* — a proof from a *braita*. The *braita* states: if someone says, "My ox gored another person," or "my ox gored another person's ox," he pays based on his own admission. The Gemara asks: does this not refer to a *tam*, thereby demonstrating that the half-payment is *mammon*, since admission suffices? The Gemara rejects this: *lo bemuad* — we are speaking of a *muad*, a habituated ox. And the implication for a *tam* would be the reverse: *eino meshaleim al pi atzmo* — he does not pay based on his own admission — precisely because the half-payment of a *tam* is a *knas*.

### Braita Slave Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַנִּיזָּק וְהַמַּזִּיק בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא מָמוֹנָא, הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁיֵיךְ נִיזָּק בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא, הַשְׁתָּא דְּלָאו דִּידֵיהּ קָא שָׁקֵיל, בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין אִיתֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** We learned** in a mishna that if an innocuous ox gored and killed another's ox, both **the damaged and the damager** share **in the payments. Granted, according to the one who said** that **half the damage** is a payment of **money; that** is how the **damaged party shares in the payments.** By right, the owner of the dead ox should be compensated for his entire loss. However, since the ox that gored his ox was innocuous, the owner of the gored ox bears half the costs. The mishna characterizes him as sharing in the payments. **However, according to he who said** that **half the damage** is payment of **a fine,** by right, the injured party himself is entitled to nothing. **Now,** the owner **takes** half the damage **that** by right **is not his;** can **he** be characterized as sharing **in the payments?**

The Gemara then raises a further question on the *braita*. The *seifa* of the *braita* addresses a different case: if one's ox kills another's slave, he does not pay based on his own admission, since the thirty-shekel payment for a slave is a fixed amount — and as a fixed sum that can exceed actual damages, it is treated as a *knas*. The Gemara asks: why did the *braita* need to introduce the slave case to illustrate the *knas* exemption? It should have made the distinction within the original case itself, contrasting *muad* (where one pays *al pi atzmo*) with *tam* (where one does not). The very fact that the *braita* jumped to the slave case seems to imply that within the goring case itself — whether *muad* or *tam* — one always pays based on admission, indicating the payment is *mammon*. The Gemara answers: *kulah bemuad kamari* — the *braita* is speaking throughout of a *muad*, distinguishing between goring a free person or ox (which is *tashlumim*) and goring a slave (which is *knas*). The case of a *tam* simply was not the *braita*'s focus, and its silence tells us nothing about whether *tam* is *knas* or *mammon*.

### Exceeding Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם יָתֵר עַל מַה שֶּׁהִזִּיק — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. הָא פָּחוֹת מִמַּה שֶּׁהִזִּיק — מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** an additional proof from the mishna: **This is the principle: Anyone who pays more than what he damaged,** the payments are fines, and therefore **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission. The Gemara infers: If he pays **less than what he damaged, he pays** based **on his own** admission. Apparently, payment of half the damage is a payment of money, not a fine.

The Gemara next turns to a *mishna* that formulates a general rule: *kol ha-meshaleim yoter al mah she-hizik eino meshaleim al pi atzmo* — anyone who pays more than the actual damages does not pay based on his own admission. Payment exceeding actual harm — whether because the amount is disproportionate (such as double payment for theft) or because it is a fixed sum like the thirty shekels for a slave — marks the payment as a *knas*. The implication seems to run in the other direction as well: if the payment is *less* than full damages, it would apparently be *tashlumim*, collected even based on one's own admission. This seems to directly support the view that half-payment of a *tam* is *mammon*.

### Less Than Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא תֵּימָא הָא פָּחוֹת מִמַּה שֶּׁהִזִּיק, אֶלָּא אֵימָא: הָא כְּמָה שֶׁהִזִּיק — מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Do not infer and **say:** If he pays **less than what he damaged,** he pays based on his own admission. Infer and **say:** If he pays precisely **what he damaged, he pays** based **on his own** admission.

The Gemara presses this: the *mishna* says if one pays more than damages, he does not pay *al pi atzmo*; by contrast, if one pays less than damages, the implication is that one does pay *al pi atzmo*. This strongly suggests that partial payment is *tashlumim*, not *knas*.

### Contradiction Resolved

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל פָּחוֹת מַאי, אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ? לִיתְנֵי, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם כְּמָה שֶׁהִזִּיק — אֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, דְּמַשְׁמַע פָּחוֹת וּמַשְׁמַע יָתֵר! תְּיוּבְתָּא. וְהִלְכְתָא: פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא. תְּיוּבְתָּא וְהִלְכְתָא?! אִין: טַעְמָא מַאי אִיתּוֹתַב — מִשּׁוּם דְּלָא קָתָנֵי ״כְּמָה שֶׁהִזִּיק״, לָא פְּסִיקָא לֵיהּ. כֵּיוָן דְּאִיכָּא חֲצִי נֶזֶק צְרוֹרוֹת, דְּהִלְכְתָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ דְּמָמוֹנָא הוּא, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** according to that explanation, if he pays **less** than the damage he caused, **what** is the *halakha*? If it is that **he does not pay** based **on his own** admission, **let** the *tanna* **teach** a more general principle: **This is the principle: Anyone who does not pay** the amount **that he damaged does not pay** based **on his own** admission, **as** that formulation both **indicates** one who pays **less and indicates** one who pays **more** than the damage he inflicted. The Gemara concludes: This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, that payment of half the damage is a fine. The Gemara further concludes: **And the *halakha*** is that payment of **half the damage** is **a fine.** The Gemara asks: Is there **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, **and** the ***halakha*** is in accordance with that opinion? The Gemara responds: **Yes,** the *halakha* is in accordance with his opinion, as, **what is the reason** that his opinion **was conclusively refuted?** It is **because** the *tanna* **does not teach:** This is the principle: Anyone who does not pay the amount **that he damaged.** However, the reason the *tanna* did not employ that formulation **is not clear-cut for him, since there is** the payment of **half** the **damage** caused by **pebbles** dispersed by an animal proceeding in its usual manner. **As it is a *halakha* transmitted** to Moses from Sinai **that** the payment for pebbles **is a monetary** payment, not a fine; it is **due to that** fact that the *tanna* **did not teach** the principle: Anyone who does not pay the amount that he damaged does not pay based on his own admission. In the case of pebbles, although he does not pay the amount that he damaged, he pays based on his own admission.

The Gemara, however, refuses to accept this reading of the *mishna* as definitive. It reformulates: do not read the rule as drawing the line between "more" and "less." Rather, the line is between paying *exactly* the amount of damages (which is *tashlumim*) versus anything that diverges — whether more or less — from that precise equivalence (which is *knas*). If so, why didn't the *mishna* simply say: "Whoever does not pay exactly according to the damages does not pay *al pi atzmo*"? The Gemara concedes: *tiyuvta* — this is indeed a challenge to the position that half-damages is a *knas*. The *mishna* strongly implies that only payment exceeding damages is a *knas*, and that less-than-full payment is *tashlumim*.

Yet the Gemara then delivers a striking ruling: *ve-hilkheta palga nizka knasa* — the law is that the half-payment of a goring *tam* is indeed a *knas*. The Gemara itself acknowledges the apparent paradox: you have just presented a *tiyuvta*, and yet you rule in accordance with the contradicted position? The answer: yes — *tiyuvta ve-hilkheta*. The reason the *mishna* did not formulate the rule as "anything other than exact damages is a *knas*" is that such a formulation would have been imprecise. There exists a case of half-payment that is unambiguously *mammon*, not *knas*, and that exception prevented the *mishna* from adopting the broader formulation.

### Pebbles Exception

That exception is the case of *tzurot* — pebbles. When a walking animal kicks up stones that cause damage, the halacha — a *halakha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* — is that one pays half-damages. This is categorized under *regel* (trampling), and the half-payment in this case is unambiguously *mammon*, not *knas*. The *mishna* therefore could not state as an absolute rule that any sub-full payment is a *knas*, because the *tzurot* case is a counterexample: it pays half the damages and is nonetheless *mammon*. Accordingly, the *mishna* confined its stated rule to the case of payment exceeding damages, without claiming that less-than-full payment is always *tashlumim*. The real underlying principle — that anything departing from exact equivalence to actual damages is a *knas*, apart from the *tzurot* exception — remains in force. And within that framework, the half-payment of a goring *tam* is a *knas*.

### Ruling: Knas

The conclusion of this *sugya* is thus somewhat counterintuitive. All the evidence marshaled over the course of the discussion — the *mishna*'s formulation, the logical argument that paying half of something suggests partial compensation rather than a zero-based fine, and the parallel of *tzurot* where half-payment is plainly *mammon* — points in the direction of treating *chatzi nezek* as *tashlumim*. Nevertheless, the Gemara concludes with a firm ruling: *palga nizka knasa*. The tradition is that a *tam*, by its nature, does not rise to the level of actionable negligence — one cannot be truly blamed for failing to prevent an ox that had never gored before from goring — and therefore the payment imposed is punitive rather than compensatory.

This ruling carries two practical consequences developed throughout the *sugya*. First, a *knas* is not collected based on the tortfeasor's own admission: *modeih bi-knas patur*. Second, a *knas* can only be imposed by a court with proper *semikhah* — the ordained authority tracing back to Moshe Rabbeinu — and therefore cannot be collected outside of Eretz Yisrael. Just as such a court is required to impose lashes, so too it is required to impose a monetary fine. An individual's admission cannot substitute for the court's imposition.

## Unusual Animal Damage

### Meshuneh Examples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ פַּלְגָא נִיזְקָא קְנָסָא, הַאי כַּלְבָּא דְּאָכֵל אִימְּרֵי, וְשׁוּנָּרָא דְּאָכֵיל תַּרְנְגוֹלֵי רַבְרְבֵי, מְשׁוּנֶּה הוּא, וְלָא מַגְבֵּינַן בְּבָבֶל. אֲבָל זוּטְרֵי — אוֹרְחֵיהּ הוּא וּמַגְבֵּינַן.
>
> **Translation:** Based on that ruling, the Gemara concludes: **And now that you said** that payment of **half the damage** is **a fine, this dog that ate lambs, and a cat that ate large roosters, is unusual** damage, for which the owner is liable to pay only half the damage if the animal was innocuous, **and** therefore, **we do not collect** it **in Babylonia.** The payment for unusual damage is a fine, and fines cannot be collected in Babylonia, as there are no ordained judges authorized to adjudicate cases involving fines. **However,** if the cat ate **small** roosters, that **is its usual manner, and we collect** the damages in Babylonia, as it is a payment of money.

Having concluded the discussion of *chatzi nezek*, the Gemara now extends the principles of *knas* to cases of *meshuneh* — unusual or unexpected animal behavior. The examples given are a dog that eats large lambs and a cat that eats large chickens. Such behavior is *meshuneh hu* — it is not the normal feeding behavior of these animals. Because it is anomalous and unexpected, it is treated analogously to the *keren* of an unhabituated ox, and only half-damages are assessed.

### Knas Jurisdiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי תְּפַס — לָא מַפְּקִינַן מִינֵּיהּ. וְאִי אָמַר: אַקְבְּעוּ לִי זִימְנָא לְאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל — מַקְבְּעִינַן לֵיהּ. וְאִי לָא אָזֵיל — מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ. בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ מְשַׁמְּתִינַן לֵיהּ. דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: סַלֵּיק הֶזֵּיקָךְ, מִדְּרַבִּי נָתָן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן שֶׁלֹּא יְגַדֵּל אָדָם כֶּלֶב רַע בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, וְלֹא יַעֲמִיד סוּלָּם רָעוּעַ בְּתוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא תָשִׂים דָּמִים בְּבֵיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And** in cases of fines, **if** the injured party **seized** property from the offender in the amount of the fine, even in Babylonia **we do not repossess it from him,** as according to the letter of the law he is entitled to that payment, and the party from whom he seized the property cannot claim that he does not owe that payment. **And if** the injured party **said: Set me a time** to go **to** a court **in Eretz Yisrael,** where cases of fines are adjudicated, **we set** a time **for him, and if** the other disputant **does not go** to Israel as demanded, **we excommunicate him. Either way,** whether or not he agrees to go to Eretz Yisrael, if he keeps the cause of the damage, **we** in Babylonia **excommunicate him, as we say to him: Remove your** cause of **damage,** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Natan. As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Natan says: From where** is it derived **that a person may not raise a vicious dog in his house, and may not place an unsteady ladder in his house?** It is **as it is stated:** "And you shall make a parapet for your roof **that you shall not place blood in your house"** (Deuteronomy 22:8). It is prohibited to leave a potentially dangerous object in one's house, and one who refuses to remove it is excommunicated.

Since this half-payment falls in the category of *knas*, it carries the jurisdictional limitations discussed above. *Ve-lo magbinan b'Vavel* — we do not collect this payment in Babylonia. A *knas* requires a court possessing the authentic chain of *semikhah* going back to Moshe Rabbeinu — courts that were only empowered in Eretz Yisrael. Outside of Eretz Yisrael no such court exists, and therefore a *knas* cannot be imposed there.

There is an interesting conceptual question lurking here: why should *meshuneh* eating be classified under *keren* (intentional damage) rather than *shen* (an animal's ordinary feeding behavior)? Two approaches emerge from the broader discussion in Bava Kamma. One view holds that because the eating is abnormal it gets reclassified as *keren* — willful, unexpected damage. The other approach suggests it may remain categorized as *shen*, but since the animal was behaving in a way the owner could not have guarded against — analogous to the first gorings of a *tam* — perhaps half-payment and *knas* status apply even to what is technically a *shen*-type category. Either way, for our purposes the operative rule is: unusual behavior of this kind yields half-damages assessed as a *knas*, uncollectable in the Diaspora.

### Normal Behavior

When a cat eats small chickens or a dog eats small animals, *orchei hu* — that is simply normal behavior. In such a case, full damages are assessed and collected in the ordinary way.

### Collection Methods

Even in cases where a Diaspora court cannot formally adjudicate a *knas*, the damaged party is not left entirely without recourse. If the damagee has already seized property equivalent to his loss — *tafas* — the court will not compel him to return it. Of course, a court proceeding would still be necessary to determine the amount of damages and whether the seized property was proportionate. Additionally, if the damagee wishes to pursue the matter formally, *ve-ee amar akvu li zimna le-Eretz Yisrael* — he may ask the Diaspora court to set an appointment for him in a court in Eretz Yisrael. If the damager refuses to appear, *meshamtinan lei* — the Diaspora court places him under *cherem* until he complies. And regardless of whether such an appointment is set, the damager may be placed in *cherem* simply for maintaining a dangerous animal, on the grounds that *salek hezekach* — you must remove your hazard.

### Rabbi Natan

This connects to a *braita* citing Rabbi Natan: *minayin she-lo yigadel adam kelev ra be-toch beito* — how do we know that a person may not keep a dangerous dog in his house, nor maintain a shaky ladder? *She-ne'emar: ve-lo tasim damim be-veitecha* — as it is written, "you shall not place blood in your house." The point is that the obligation to remove a hazard applies even in circumstances where the animal's behavior may be a first-time occurrence. Whether this applies universally or only once the dangerous pattern has become established remains an open question.

## Seduction & Rape Payments

### Three vs. Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַעֲרָה שֶׁנִּתְפַּתְּתָה — בּוֹשְׁתָּהּ וּפְגָמָהּ וּקְנָסָהּ שֶׁל אָבִיהָ, וְהַצַּעַר בִּתְפוּסָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a young woman who was seduced,** the compensation for **her humiliation and her degradation and her fine** belong **to her father. And** the same applies to the compensation for **pain in** the case of a woman who was **raped.**

The Gemara now returns to the *mishna* before us, which opens the fourth *perek*. The *mishna* states: a seduced girl (*mefutah*) receives three payments — *boshet*, *pegam*, and *knas* — while a raped girl (*anusah*) receives four, adding *tzaar* (pain and suffering) to the three. This distinction was already known from an earlier *mishna*, as the Gemara will shortly note, but the present *mishna* goes further in specifying to whom the payments go and under what circumstances.

### Payments to Father

All of these payments — the embarrassment, the injury, the fine, and in the case of rape the pain and suffering — go to the father. The *mishna* states this explicitly: *boshet u-pegam ve-knasah shel aviha*, and *ve-ha-tzaar bi-tfusah* — the *tzaar* in a case of forcible seizure likewise belongs to the father.

### Father Alive Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָמְדָה בַּדִּין עַד שֶׁלֹּא מֵת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל אָב. מֵת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל אַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** If the young woman **stood trial** against the seducer or rapist **before the father died, these** payments belong **to her father,** as stated above. If **the father died** before he collected the money from the offender, the payments belong **to** her **brothers.** As the father's heirs, they inherit the money to which he was entitled before he passed away.

The *mishna* then details the distribution of payments according to the timing of various events. The baseline rule is straightforward: if the father is alive when the court case concludes and awards the payment, the money goes to him.

### Father Dies After

If the court has concluded — the father was alive at the time of *gemar din*, the court's final ruling — and only thereafter the father dies, *harei hen shel achin*: the money passes to the brothers. The logic is that once the court has ruled, the debt is owed directly to the father; his death causes it to pass to his heirs like any other asset of the estate.

### Case Before Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא הִסְפִּיקָה לַעֲמוֹד בַּדִּין עַד שֶׁמֵּת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל עַצְמָהּ, עָמְדָה בַּדִּין עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּגְרָה — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל אָב, מֵת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל אַחִין. לֹא הִסְפִּיקָה לַעֲמוֹד בַּדִּין עַד שֶׁבָּגְרָה — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל עַצְמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** However, if **she did not manage to stand in judgment before the father died,** and she was subsequently awarded the money, the compensation belongs **to her,** as she is now under her own jurisdiction due to the fact that she no longer has a father. If **she stood trial before she reached majority,** the payments belong **to her father,** and if **the father died, they** belong **to** her **brothers,** who inherit the money notwithstanding the fact that she has become a grown woman since the trial. If **she did not manage to stand in judgment before she reached majority,** the money belongs **to her.**

Conversely, if the court case had not yet been concluded before the father died — *lo hispika la'amod ba-din ad she-met av* — then *harei hen shel atzmah*: the money goes to the daughter herself. The conceptual framework underlying this, as the Gemara explains, is that the tortfeasor's obligation fundamentally runs to the daughter; the father is entitled to collect it through her by virtue of his legal relationship to her. Once the father has died before the court case, that parental relationship no longer exists, and the daughter receives the award directly.

### Woman Emancipated

A parallel principle applies if the daughter has become a *bogeret* — a fully adult, emancipated woman — before the court case concluded. In that case as well, *hareihen shel atzmah*: the money goes to her. The father's entitlement depends on both his being alive and her being a *naarah* or *ketanah* under his authority at the time of the ruling. If either condition fails — either he dies or she becomes a *bogeret* — the money flows to her directly.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם לֹא הִסְפִּיקָה לִגְבּוֹת עַד שֶׁמֵּת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל עַצְמָהּ,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** Even **if** she stood trial in her father's lifetime but **did not manage to collect** the payments **before the father died,** the brothers do not inherit this money, as it belongs **to her.**

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the *chachamim* regarding the effect of *gemar din*. He holds: *im lo hispika li-gbot ad she-met ha-av hareihen shel atzmah* — if the money was not yet actually collected before the father died, it goes to the daughter, even if the court had already ruled in the father's favor. According to Rabbi Shimon, *gemar din* alone is insufficient to convert the obligation into a direct debt owed to the father. Even after the court's ruling, the payment is still conceptually routed through the daughter — think of it as a kind of automatic transfer that activates only when the money is actually paid. If the father dies before receiving payment, the transfer never activates, and the money remains with the daughter.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, hold that *gemar din* converts the obligation into a direct debt to the father. Once he has been awarded the money by the court, it is his asset; his subsequent death causes it to pass to his sons as part of the estate.

### Earnings vs. Knas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֵׂה יָדֶיהָ וּמְצִיאָתָהּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא גָּבְתָה, מֵת הָאָב — הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל אַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** By contrast, with regard to **her earnings and** the lost **items** that **she** has **found, although she has not collected** them, e.g., she had yet to receive her wages, if **the father died they** belong **to** her **brothers.** These payments are considered the property of their father, as he was entitled to them before he passed away.

This ruling of Rabbi Shimon is, however, limited to the *knas* context. The *mishna* immediately contrasts it with the daughter's earnings and found objects: *ma'aseh yadeha u-metzi'asah af al pi she-lo gavsa met av hareihen shel achin* — her wages and found property, even if not yet collected at the time of the father's death, pass to the brothers. The distinction is that wages and found property become an immediate direct debt to the father the moment they are earned or found. The *knas*, by contrast — according to Rabbi Shimon — continues to flow through the daughter even after *gemar din*, and therefore does not pass to the brothers if the father dies before collection.

## Knas & False Oath

### Gemara's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן? תְּנֵינָא: הַמְפַתֶּה נוֹתֵן שְׁלֹשָׁה דְּבָרִים, וְהָאוֹנֵס אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the mishna **teaching us? We** already **learned** this in a mishna (*Ketubot* 39a): **The seducer pays three types of indemnity and the rapist** pays **four.**

The Gemara opens this section with a challenge: *mai kamashmalan?* — what is the *mishna* actually teaching us? The first part of the *mishna*, stating that the seducer pays three payments and the rapist four, was already known from an earlier *mishna*. And the fact that all payments go to the father — is that not obvious as well?

### Why Seducer Pays

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאָבִיהָ אִיצְטְרִיךְ לֵיהּ. לְאָבִיהָ נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא, מִדְּקָא יָהֵיב מְפַתֶּה, דְּאִי לְעַצְמָהּ — אַמַּאי יָהֵיב מְפַתֶּה? מִדַּעְתָּהּ עֲבַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It **is necessary for** the mishna to teach that the money is given **to her father.** The Gemara asks: It **is also obvious** that the money goes **to her father, from** the fact **that a seducer pays** these types of indemnity, **as, if** one claims that the money goes **to her, why does a seducer pay** her at all? After all, **he acted with her consent;** how can she then claim compensation?

The Gemara then provides the answer: the *chiddush* that all payments go to the father is not entirely self-evident, but it can be inferred from a different principle. The fact that the seducer — whose act was consensual — still owes payment demonstrates that the payment runs to the father, not to the daughter. *De'ilei atzma, amai yahiv mefateh? Midaatah avad* — if the money were owed to the daughter herself, why would the seducer pay? She consented. The very existence of the seducer's liability proves that the injured party, in the relevant legal sense, is the father. The Gemara therefore concludes that the true *chiddush* of the *mishna* is the detailed rules about *gemar din* — the debate between the *rabanan* and Rabbi Shimon about what happens after the court rules and before the money is collected.

### Ones/Pituy Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: ״אָנַסְתָּ וּפִיתִּיתָ אֶת בִּתִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא אָנַסְתִּי וְלֹא פִּיתִּיתִי״. ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״, וְאַחַר כָּךְ הוֹדָה — חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, אֲבָל מְשַׁלֵּם בּוֹשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shevuot* 36b) that if someone said to another: **You raped** my daughter, **or: You seduced my daughter, and** the other **says: I did not rape and I did not seduce,** to which the father replied: **I administer an oath to you, and** the defendant **said: Amen, and afterward he admitted** that he had raped or seduced the man's daughter, he is **obligated** both in the payments of a rapist or a seducer as well as an additional one-fifth, and he must bring an offering for swearing falsely that he did not owe the money. **Rabbi Shimon exempts** him, **as he does not pay** the **fine on his own** admission. The accused individual is not considered to have taken a false oath in denial of a monetary charge because he would not have been obligated to pay the fine on the basis of his own admission of guilt. The Rabbis **said to him: Although he does not pay** the **fine on his own** admission, **indeed he does pay** compensation for **the humiliation and degradation on his own** admission. Consequently, he has denied a monetary claim, and therefore his false oath obligates him to add one-fifth and to bring an offering. This concludes the mishna.

The Gemara now cites a *mishna* from tractate Shevuot to introduce the question of *korban shevuah* — the oath-offering — in the context of *knas*. The general rule of *korban shevuah* is that when a person falsely denies under oath an obligation that, had he admitted to it, he would have been required to pay, he brings a special *korban* and pays the principal plus a fifth. The question is whether this applies when the underlying obligation is a *knas*. The *mishna* in Shevuot records the case: a father says to someone, "You raped or seduced my daughter," and the accused denies it under oath and then admits. Is he liable for a *korban shevuah*?

### No Korban for Knas

Rabbi Shimon holds: *she'eino meshalem knas al pi atzmo* — since you do not pay a *knas* based on your own admission, a false denial of a *knas* does not generate liability for a *korban shevuah*. The *chachamim* respond: even if that is true for the *knas* component, the accused would still pay for *boshet* and *pegam* based on his own admission — those are *mammon*, not *knas* — and therefore a false denial of those components does generate *korban* liability. The debate, as the Gemara notes, is not truly about the underlying legal principles, which both sides share. Both agree: no *korban shevuah* for *knas*, yes *korban shevuah* for *mammon*. The disagreement is about which component the father's claim primarily invokes — is the father demanding the *knas* (in which case denial is exempt), or the *boshet* and *pegam* (in which case denial obligates)?

### Abayes Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרַבָּה, הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ: ״אָנַסְתָּ וּפִיתִּיתָ אֶת בִּתִּי, וְהֶעֱמַדְתִּיךָ בַּדִּין, וְנִתְחַיַּיבְתָּ לִי מָמוֹן״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא אָנַסְתִּי וְלֹא פִּיתִּיתִי, וְלֹא הֶעֱמַדְתַּנִי בַּדִּין, וְלֹא נִתְחַיַּיבְתִּי לְךָ מָמוֹן״, וְנִשְׁבַּע וְהוֹדָה, לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** In light of this mishna, **Abaye raised a dilemma before Rabba:** With regard to **one who says to another: You raped** my daughter, **or: You seduced my daughter, and I made you stand in judgment** for your actions, **and you were found obligated** to pay **me money** but you did not do so, **and** the defendant **says: I did not rape, or: I did not seduce, and you did not make me stand in judgment, and I was not found obligated** to pay **you money, and** the defendant **took an oath** that he was telling the truth **and** subsequently **admitted** his guilt, **according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, what** is the *halakha*?

This sets up the major inquiry of the *sugya*: Abaye poses a question to Rabba. The case: a father says, "You raped or seduced my daughter, and not only that — we already brought the case to court, *amadeticha ba-din*, and the court ruled that you owe me the money." The accused denies everything — the act, the court case, and the resulting obligation — and swears falsely, then admits. *Le-Rabbi Shimon mah?* — what is the law according to Rabbi Shimon?

The question is whether *gemar din* — the court's final ruling — converts a *knas* into *mammon*. The logic supporting conversion is compelling: the reason a *knas* cannot be self-incurred through admission is that it requires a court to impose it; without the court, there is no debt. But once the court has in fact imposed the debt, the obstacle is removed. The person is no longer admitting to something that could not exist through his own admission — he is admitting to a debt already created by the court. Should that admission now obligate him, and should a false denial generate *korban shevuah* liability? Or does the obligation retain its character as *knas* permanently — since it originated as a *knas*, perhaps it always remains one?

The Gemara frames this precisely: *keivan de-amad ba-din mammon havei* — perhaps once there has been a court ruling, the obligation is now simply *mammon*, and we should be liable for the *korban shevuah* since we are disputing an established monetary debt. *O dilma, af al gav de-amad ba-din, knas havei* — or perhaps even after the court has ruled it retains its identity as *knas*, having originated as one, and therefore the *modeh bi-knas* exemption persists and there is no *korban* liability.

### Ravas Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָמוֹנָא הָוֵי, וּמִיחַיַּיב עֲלֵיהּ קׇרְבַּן שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him:** Since he has already stood trial, **it is** considered a regular **monetary** payment, **and he is liable to** bring the **offering of an oath.**

Rava answers Abaye: *mamona hu, mechayve lei korban shevuah* — after *gemar din*, it is *mammon*, and denying it generates *korban shevuah* liability.

### Braita Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״אָנַסְתָּ וּפִיתִּיתָ אֶת בִּתִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא אָנַסְתִּי וְלֹא פִּיתִּיתִי״; ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרְךָ אֶת עַבְדִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא הֵמִית״; אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ עַבְדּוֹ ״הִפַּלְתָּ אֶת שִׁינִּי וְסִימִיתָ אֶת עֵינִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא הִפַּלְתִּי וְלֹא סִימִיתִי״, וְנִשְׁבַּע וְהוֹדָה, יָכוֹל יְהֵא חַיָּיב — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכִחֵשׁ בַּעֲמִיתוֹ בְּפִקָּדוֹן אוֹ בִתְשׂוּמֶת יָד אוֹ בְגָזֵל אוֹ עָשַׁק אֶת עֲמִיתוֹ. אוֹ מָצָא אֲבֵידָה וְכִחֶשׁ בָּהּ וְנִשְׁבַּע עַל שָׁקֶר״, מָה אֵלּוּ מְיוּחָדִין שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹן, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהֵן מָמוֹן. יָצְאוּ אֵלּוּ, שֶׁהֵן קְנָס.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to** Rabba from the following *baraita*. **Rabbi Shimon says:** One **might** have thought that in the case of **one who says to another: You raped** my daughter, **or: You seduced my daughter, and he says: I did not rape** her, **or: I did not seduce** her, or if he claimed: **Your ox killed my slave, and he says:** It **did not kill** him, **or if his slave said to him: You knocked out my tooth, or: You blinded my eye** and you are therefore obligated to emancipate me, **and he says: I did not knock** it **out, or: I did not blind** your eye, **and he takes an oath but** later **admitted** to the truth of the accusation, one **might** have thought that he **should be liable** to bring an offering for a false oath denying a monetary claim. Therefore, with regard to the offering for a false oath in denial of a monetary claim, **the verse states:** "If anyone sin, and commit a trespass against the Lord **and deal falsely with his neighbor in a matter of a deposit or of a pledge or of a robbery or have oppressed his neighbor, or have found that which was lost and deal falsely with it, and swear to a lie"** (Leviticus 5:21–22). **Just as** all these **matters** listed in the verse **are unique** in **that they are monetary** obligations equal in value to the loss one has caused another individual, **so too,** this *halakha* applies to **all** obligations **that are monetary** claims, which **excludes these** payments of a rapist, a seducer, and the like, **as they are fines.**

The Gemara immediately challenges this from a *braita*. Rabbi Shimon states in that *braita*: if someone demands of another "you raped or seduced my daughter," or "your ox killed my slave," or "you knocked out my slave's tooth" or "blinded my slave's eye" — all *knas* cases — and the accused swears falsely and then admits, one might think he is liable for a *korban shevuah*. The *braita* teaches: *yatzu elu she-hein knas* — excluded are cases of *knas*, from which one is exempt. And the *chachamim* hold — from the verse *u-ma'alah ma'al* — that one is *chayav* even in these cases. So we have a *machlokes*: *chachamim* say *chayav*, Rabbi Shimon says *patur*.

## Rabbi Shimon Position

### Reisha/Seifa Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲדָא בְּתָם וַחֲדָא בְּמוּעָד. וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן תָּם: מִשּׁוּם דְּאַכַּתִּי לָא אִיַּעַד, אֲבָל מוּעָד דְּאִיַּעַד — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמוֹעִינַן מוּעָד: מִשּׁוּם דְּקָא מְשַׁלֵּם כּוּלֵּיהּ, אֲבָל תָּם — אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **One** of these *halakhot* is **with regard to an innocuous** ox **and one** is **with regard to a forewarned** ox. The Gemara adds: **And** it is **necessary** to teach both *halakhot*, **as if** the mishna **had taught us** only with regard to **an innocuous** ox, one would understand that its owner is treated with leniency and the owner of the carcass bears the cost of degradation **due to** the fact **that** the ox **has not yet been forewarned; however,** with regard to **a forewarned** ox, **that was forewarned, say no,** the owner of the carcass does not bear the cost of degradation. **And if** the mishna **had taught us** only with regard to **a forewarned** ox, one would understand that its owner is treated with leniency and the owner of the carcass bears the cost of degradation **due to** the fact **that he pays** for the **entire damage,** and therefore, the relatively insignificant cost of degradation is overlooked. **However,** with regard to **an innocuous** ox, **say no,** since the owner pays only half the damage, he must bear the cost of degradation. Therefore, it was **necessary** to state the *halakha* in both cases. Therefore, there is no proof from this mishna whether half the damage is payment of money or payment of a fine.

The Gemara now probes whether the *braita*'s ruling applies before or after *gemar din*. The argument proceeds as follows: the *reisha* of the *braita*, attributed to the *chachamim*, explicitly lists *ones u-mefateh* among the cases where one would be *chayav* for a *korban shevuah*. But there is a logical difficulty: if the obligation were purely *knas* with no *mammon* component whatsoever, and if one would be exempt even when admitting, how could the *chachamim* say one is *chayav* for denying? You cannot be liable for falsely denying something you would owe nothing for even if you admitted. The very fact that the *chachamim* impose *chiyuv* implies we must be speaking of a case where the *knas* has already been converted by *gemar din* — otherwise, how could denial generate liability?

And since the *reisha* (the *chachamim*'s ruling) must refer to *she-amad ba-din*, the *seifa* (Rabbi Shimon's ruling of *patur*) must also refer to *she-amad ba-din*. This would demonstrate that Rabbi Shimon exempts even after *gemar din* — the *knas* retains its identity even then, yielding no *korban shevuah* and (by extension) no obligation upon admission.

Rava responds that he could technically distinguish the *braita*: the *reisha* speaks of *amad ba-din* and the *seifa* of *lo amad ba-din*, and the entire *braita* is Rabbi Shimon's own position rather than a debate with the *chachamim*. But he immediately rejects this answer: *shinuya de-chika lo meshanina lach* — I will not give you a forced answer. For if we read the *braita* that way, Rabbi Shimon's name should have appeared either at the beginning or consistently at the end; the way the *braita* is structured, with Rabbi Shimon appearing only in the *seifa* as a dissent, clearly indicates a debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*. And since both must be addressing the same stage — *amad ba-din* — we see that Rabbi Shimon holds the *knas* remains *knas* even after *gemar din*, at least for the purposes of *korban shevuah*.

Rava therefore concedes: *u-modina lach le-inyan korban shevuah de-Rachmana patrei* — for the specific laws of *korban shevuah*, Rabbi Shimon is correct. There is a special verse — *ve-khichesh ba-amito* — that exempts obligations originating as *knas* from the *korban shevuah* regime, even after *gemar din*. But Rava maintains his core position: in other areas of *halakha*, after *gemar din*, the *knas* does convert to *mammon*. The exemption from *korban shevuah* is a *halakha* specific to the laws of oaths, grounded in a particular verse, and does not reflect the general legal identity of the post-*gemar din* obligation.

### Inheritance Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיתְּנֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא בְּמִשְׁנַתִּנוּ, דִּכְתִיב בַּה: ״רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם לֹא הִסְפִּיקָה לִגְבּוֹת עַד שֶׁמֵּת הָאָב, הֲרֵי הֵן שֶׁל עַצְמָהּ״. הָשְׁתָּא, אִי אָמְרַתְּ — עָמַד בַּדִּין, מָמוֹנָא הָוֵי, לָא בָּעֵי דִּגְבּוֹת, דְּהַשְׁתָּא נִשְׁתַּנְּתָה זְכוּת הָאָב. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: שׁוֹשְׁלַמְתָּ בְּתוֹךְ חַיֵּי אָבִיהָ, לָקְחוּ הָאַחִין. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן, דִּלָּאו הָכִי, דְּעֲדַיִן לַחּוּנוּ הִיא קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that the principle that the money remains with the daughter in a case of non-collection before the father's death is explicit in the present *mishna* in its teaching of Rabbi Shimon's opinion. The Gemara explains the significance of this teaching: **Now, if you said** that once the court rendered its judgment, the payment is a regular **monetary** obligation, there would be no longer **a need to collect** it, as the status of the money would have changed once the court ruled. You might have thought that **now that** the court has rendered judgment and established that the father is entitled to the money, since the obligation was **established within the father's lifetime, the brothers would acquire** their inheritance rights to the money at that moment. The mishna teaches that **this is not so, that it still remains** in the hands of the daughter until it is actually collected.

The Gemara next turns to our own *mishna* to challenge Rava's position. *Itivei* — we raise a contradiction. Rabbi Shimon rules in the *mishna*: if the money was not collected before the father died, it goes to the daughter. Now, if Rava is correct that after *gemar din* the obligation converts to *mammon* in general, then once the court rules it becomes a direct debt to the father, and upon his death it should pass as *yerusha* to his sons — *de-achin ba'ei le-hava*. Why then does Rabbi Shimon say she keeps it? This appears to demonstrate that according to Rabbi Shimon, *gemar din* does not convert *knas* to *mammon* — the obligation remains fundamentally routed through the daughter even after the court rules, and therefore upon the father's death it never becomes part of his estate.

### 22 Year Struggle

The Gemara then delivers a remarkable statement: *hai milta kashya ba le-Rabba ve-Rav Yosef esrin ve-taryan shanin* — this difficulty challenged Rabba and Rav Yosef for twenty-two years without resolution. The *Tosafot* raise an obvious question: if the difficulties were insurmountable, why not simply retract? All the evidence — the *korban shevuah* ruling and the *mishna* about inheritance — points toward Rabbi Shimon treating *knas* as *knas* even after *gemar din*. Why hold on to a tradition for twenty-two years if no reconciliation could be found? *Tosafot* answers: they evidently had a firm tradition that according to Rabbi Shimon, *gemar din* does in general convert *knas* to *mammon*. But they could not reconcile that tradition with the two exceptions — *korban shevuah* and the *mishna* about inheritance — for which the *knas* character clearly persists.

### Rav Yosef's Answer

After Rabba's twenty-two years as *rosh yeshiva*, he passed away and Rav Yosef assumed the position. Rashi notes beautifully that in order to honor Rav Yosef — who was the greater *baki* even if Rabba had been the sharper analyst — he was assisted *min ha-shamayim* in resolving the question that had stymied his predecessor. The answer came to him when he assumed the leadership of the *yeshiva*.

### Korban Exception

Rav Yosef explains that the tradition Rabba had — that *gemar din* converts *knas* to *mammon* according to Rabbi Shimon — is indeed correct as a general rule, but there are two exceptions. The first exception: the laws of *korban shevuah*. Even after *gemar din*, when the original obligation was a *knas*, one is exempt from the *korban shevuah*. This is because the *Torah* requires the *korban* only for obligations that originated as *mammon*. The verse *ve-khichesh ba-amito* establishes that if it started its life as *knas*, the *korban* exemption applies regardless of subsequent conversions through *gemar din*.

### Ones/Pituah Exception

Rav Yosef explains that the tradition Rabba had — that *gemar din* converts *knas* to *mammon* according to Rabbi Shimon — is indeed correct as a general rule, but there are two exceptions. The first exception: the laws of *korban shevuah*. Even after *gemar din*, when the original obligation was a *knas*, one is exempt from the *korban shevuah*. This is because the *Torah* requires the *korban* only for obligations that originated as *mammon*. The verse *ve-khichesh ba-amito* establishes that if it started its life as *knas*, the *korban* exemption applies regardless of subsequent conversions through *gemar din*.

The second exception: the specific case of *ones u-mefateh* — rape and seduction. Even after *gemar din*, these payments are not considered a direct debt to the father; they continue to run through the daughter. The explanation lies in the verse: *ve-natan ha-ish ha-shochev imah le-avi ha-na'arah chamishim kasef* — "the man who lay with her shall give to the father of the girl fifty pieces of silver." The word *ve-natan* — "he shall give" — connotes that the father's entitlement to the money crystallizes only at the moment of actual payment (*netinah*). *Lo zikhtah Torah le-av ella mi-sha'at netinah* — the Torah did not grant the father rights until the moment of giving. Therefore even after *gemar din*, the money remains conceptually the daughter's until it is actually handed over; if the father dies beforehand, it stays with her.

### Yiten vs. Natan

The Gemara then tests whether this principle extends to the case of the slave: the verse regarding a goring ox that kills a slave also uses a payment term — *yitein* — "he shall give thirty shekels of silver to the master." The Gemara distinguishes: *yitein lachud ve-natan lachud* — *yitein* (future) and *natan* (past/completed) carry different legal valences. The verse in the slave case uses *yitein* — "will give" — which does not imply the same condition of actual payment before the right vests. By contrast, the verse in *ones u-mefateh* uses *ve-natan* — connoting a completed act of giving — which establishes that the father's rights in *ones u-mefateh* arise only at the moment of actual payment.

### Final Synthesis

The final synthesis of the *sugya* can now be stated. Rabbi Shimon's position, properly understood, is as follows: as a general rule, *gemar din* converts a *knas* into *mammon* — it becomes an established debt, transferable as part of an estate, and presumably triggering admission-based liability in the ordinary case. However, there are two domains where the *knas* character persists even after *gemar din*. First, the laws of *korban shevuah*: because the *Torah* requires the *korban* only for obligations whose inception was *mammon*, a false oath about an obligation that originated as *knas* — even if since converted through *gemar din* — remains exempt. Second, the payments of *ones u-mefateh*: because the *Torah* conditions the father's rights on actual payment, the obligation remains routed through the daughter after *gemar din*, and therefore does not pass to the brothers upon the father's death. In all other cases of *knas*, after *gemar din* the obligation is *mammon* for all purposes.
