# Nazir 44 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Tiglachat Tumah

### Opening Remarks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁלֹשָׁה מִינִים אֲסוּרִין בַּנָּזִיר: הַטּוּמְאָה, וְהַתִּגְלַחַת, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַגֶּפֶן. חוֹמֶר בַּטּוּמְאָה וּבַתִּגְלַחַת מִבַּיּוֹצֵא מִן הַגֶּפֶן, שֶׁהַטּוּמְאָה וְהַתִּגְלַחַת — סוֹתְרִין, וְהַיּוֹצֵא מִן הַגֶּפֶן — אֵינוֹ סוֹתֵר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Three types** of actions **are prohibited for a nazirite:** Contracting **ritual impurity** imparted by a corpse, **and shaving** his hair, **and** eating or drinking any substances **that emerge from the vine.** There is a greater **stricture with regard to** the prohibitions of **impurity and shaving than** that of substances **that emerge from the vine, as impurity and shaving negate** his naziriteship, i.e., he must add thirty days to his term of naziriteship or start it afresh. **But** if he eats or drinks **that** which **emerges from the vine,** this **does not negate** his naziriteship.

Daf 44b opens as the tractate transitions away from the previous discussions of the prohibitions of *tumah*, *yayin*, and *tiglachat* that defined the earlier sugyot. Each of those three topics, it is worth pausing to note, served as a springboard into broader areas of *halacha*: *tumah* opened the discussion of *tumat kohanim*, *tiglachat* shed light on the general laws of shaving, and *yayin* became a paradigm for the principle of *ta'am k'ikar* in the world of *kashrut*. Having covered those three prohibitions, the Mishnah now turns to the rituals associated with the conclusion of a Nazir's period of impurity — the *tiglachat tumah* — and then to the shaving at the conclusion of the period of purity, the *tiglachat taharah*.

### Mishnah Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תִּגְלַחַת טוּמְאָה כֵּיצַד? הָיָה מַזֶּה בַּשְּׁלִישִׁי וּבַשְּׁבִיעִי וּמְגַלֵּחַ בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, וּמֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בַּשְּׁמִינִי. וְאִם גִּילַּח בַּשְּׁמִינִי — מֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בּוֹ בַּיּוֹם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: מָה בֵּין זֶה לִמְצוֹרָע?
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the **shaving of ritual impurity** performed by a nazirite who became impure during his naziriteship, **how** is it performed? The priest **would sprinkle** the waters of purification on him **on the third and the seventh** days after he contracted his impurity, as performed for all those who contracted impurity imparted by a corpse. **And he shaves** his hair **on the seventh** day **and brings his offerings on the eighth** day. **And if he shaved on the eighth** day **he brings his offerings on that day,** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Tarfon said to him: What is** the difference **between this** ritual **and** that of **a leper?** A leper also shaves on the seventh day and sacrifices his offerings on the eighth. However, if a leper shaves on the eighth day he brings his offerings on the ninth day, not on the day of his shaving.

The Mishnah opens with the question: *tiglachat tumah keitzad* — how is the shaving of impurity performed? The Mishnah addresses a very specific question, as will become clear from what follows.

### Day Seven Ritual

The Mishnah describes the process: *hayah mazeh bashlishi u'vashvi'i* — the sprinkling of the ashes of the *parah adumah* takes place on day three and day seven. Then *u'megalei'ach bashvi'i* — the Nazir shaves on day seven. Finally, *u'meivi korbanotav bashmini* — the *korbanot* are brought on day eight.

### Torah Sources

This structure follows directly from the Torah's language: *v'chi yamut meit alav b'feta pit'om v'tame rosh nizro v'gilach rosho b'yom taharato b'yom hashvi'i yigalchennu* — and on day seven he shaves. *U'v'yom hashmini yavi shtei torim o shnei bnei yonah* — and on day eight he brings the *korban*.

The Mishnah then teaches: *v'im gilach bashmini meivi korbanotav bo vayom divrei Rabbi Akiva* — if for some reason he postponed the shaving until day eight, he may still bring the *korbanot* on that same day eight. The *halacha* is not that the *korbanot* must be brought the day after the shaving; the *halacha* fixes the *korbanot* on day eight. If he shaved on day seven as intended, the *korbanot* come on day eight. If he postponed the shaving to day eight itself, the *korbanot* are still brought that same day eight.

## Rabbi Akiva vs Tarfon

### Tarfon's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: זֶה טׇהֳרָתוֹ תְּלוּיָה בְּיָמָיו, וּמְצוֹרָע טׇהֳרָתוֹ תְּלוּיָה בְּתִגְלַחְתּוֹ. וְאֵינוֹ מֵבִיא קׇרְבָּן אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה מְעוֹרַב שֶׁמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: The purification of this** impure nazirite **depends on his days,** as he immerses on the seventh day like all those who contract impurity imparted by a corpse, which means he is already ritually pure on the eighth day. **But** with regard to **a leper, his purification depends on his shaving.** Any immersion performed earlier is of no account, and must be repeated. **And** a leper **brings** his **offering only if the sun has set** following his immersion. Since offerings are not sacrificed at night, the bringing of his offering is postponed until the following day.

Rabbi Tarfon poses a challenge: *mah bein zeh l'metzora* — how is the Nazir's case different from that of the *metzora*? Rabbi Tarfon takes it as given that if a *metzora* waits to perform his shaving until day eight, the *korban* is deferred to day nine. On that premise, he argues that the same logic should apply to the Nazir: if the shaving was delayed to day eight, the *korban* should only come on day nine.

It should be noted that for the *metzora* the delay is not precisely the same mechanism as for the Nazir — it involves a seven-day wait until the *kohen* revisits him, rather than a seven-day sprinkling with *parah adumah* ashes — but the structural parallel is what Rabbi Tarfon is drawing on.

### Akiva's Answer

Rabbi Akiva responds: *zeh taharato tluyah b'yamav u'metzora taharato tluyah b'tiglachto* — by the Nazir, becoming *tahor* is dependent on the days, whereas by the *metzora*, the shaving itself is part of the *tahara* process.

### Metzora Tahara

What does it mean that the *metzora*'s shaving is part of the *tahara* process, and how do we know it? Tosafot points out that the textual evidence lies in a careful comparison of the respective *pesukim*.

By the Nazir, the Torah simply says *v'gilach rosho* — he shaves his head — and then *u'v'yom hashmini yavi shtei torim* — on day eight he brings the *korbanot*. The shaving is mentioned, but there is no subsequent *mikvah* immersion required as part of the Nazir's *tahara*; the *tahara* from *tumei meit* was accomplished through the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* ashes on days three and seven.

The *metzora*, by contrast, undergoes a different sequence. After the Kohen confirms the healing and the bird ritual is performed, the Torah states: *v'kibbes hamit-taher b'gadav v'gilach et kol se'aro v'rachatz bamayim v'taher v'achar yavo el hamachaneh* — the one being purified launders his garments, shaves all his hair, immerses in the waters, and becomes *tahor*, and thereafter he may return to the camp. There follows a seven-day wait. Then on day seven the Torah specifies again: *yegale'ach et kol se'aro et rosho v'et zkano v'et gabot einav v'et kol se'aro yegale'ach v'kibes et bgadav v'rachatz et bsaro bamayim v'taher* — he shaves all his hair and then immerses in water and becomes *tahor*.

This second shaving of the *metzora* on day seven is thus explicitly followed by a *mikvah* immersion, making that shaving an integral component of the *tahara* process. After immersing in the *mikvah*, the *metzora* is a *tevul yom* — someone who has immersed but for whom sunset has not yet arrived. Since it is clear that the Torah requires waiting until the *tevul yom* status has resolved before bringing the *korban* — the *korban* being on day eight — it follows that if the shaving was delayed to day eight, he would need to immerse again on day eight, become a *tevul yom* again on day eight, and therefore only be able to bring his *korban* on day nine.

### Tosafot Analysis

Tosafot's reading sharpens Rabbi Akiva's distinction through a precise analysis of the *pesukim*. By the *metzora*, the shaving is part of the *tahara* process: it is an act that must precede *mikvah* immersion, and it is only after that immersion and the subsequent nightfall that the *metzora* exits *tevul yom* status and is fully *tahor*. Therefore, if the shaving was delayed by a day, the entire *tahara* sequence is pushed forward, and with it, the *korban*.

By the Nazir who became *tamei meit*, no such chain exists. His *tahara* is accomplished through the days-long sprinkling process. The shaving is a ritual of *nezirut*, not a step within the *tahara* framework. There is no *mikvah* immersion mandated after the Nazir's shaving. Consequently, by day seven, when the sprinkling has been completed and the Nazir goes to the *mikvah* as required for *tumei meit*, he is already a *tevul yom*, and by day eight he is fully *tahor* regardless of whether he has shaved yet. The shaving can therefore take place on day eight without any consequence for the *korban*, which remains fixed on day eight.

### Comparative Analysis

To summarize Rabbi Akiva's principle: *metzora taharato tluyah b'tiglachto* — the shaving is part of the *tahara* process, it is followed by *mikvah* immersion, and the *tevul yom* status that results requires waiting until the next day. *V'ein meivi korban ela im kein he'eriv shimsho* — one may only bring the *korban* after nightfall concludes the *tevul yom* status. If the *metzora* waits to shave until day eight, the *mikvah* immersion on day eight makes him a *tevul yom* on day eight, and the *korban* can only come on day nine. For the Nazir who is *tamei meit*, the *tahara* process is the sprinkling on days three and seven; the shaving is a separate ritual unconnected to the *tahara* mechanism, and postponing it to day eight leaves the *korban* unaffected.

## Braita Interpretation

### Hillel's Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ אוֹ לָא? תָּא שְׁמַע: דְּתָנֵי הִלֵּל: גִּילַּח בַּשְּׁמִינִי — מֵבִיא קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בַּתְּשִׁיעִי. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ קַיבְּלַהּ מִינֵּיהּ, לַיְתֵי קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו בַּשְּׁמִינִי!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: Did Rabbi Tarfon **accept** this claim **from** Rabbi Akiva, **or** did he **not** accept it? **Come** and **hear** an answer to this from **that** which **Hillel** the *amora* **taught:** If a nazirite **shaved on the eighth** day, **he brings his offerings on the ninth. And if it should enter your mind** that Rabbi Tarfon **accepted** the claim **from** Rabbi Akiva, **let** the nazirite **bring his offerings on the eighth** day itself, in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Akiva. Rather, this *baraita* is certainly in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Tarfon, who remains steadfast in his rejection of Rabbi Akiva's opinion.

The Gemara asks: *kiblah mineih o lo* — did Rabbi Tarfon accept Rabbi Akiva's answer? The Mishnah gives us no indication. The Gemara therefore brings a *braita*: *detani Hillel* — Hillel (the Amora, not Hillel HaZaken) taught: *gilach bashmini meivi korbanotav batshi'i* — if he shaved on day eight, he brings his *korbanot* on day nine.

### Braita Attribution

One might ask: who is the subject of this *braita* — the Nazir or the *metzora*? Tosafot explains that there was a tradition (*masorah*) that this *braita* refers to the Nazir, since the ruling regarding the *metzora* is undisputed — if a *metzora* postpones his shaving to day eight, the *korban* is on day nine, and that was never in question. The *braita* is therefore addressing the Nazir's case, and the only authority who would maintain that a Nazir who postponed shaving to day eight must bring his *korban* on day nine is Rabbi Tarfon — implying that Rabbi Tarfon did not concede to Rabbi Akiva's argument.

### Rava's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — דְּטָבַל בַּשְּׁבִיעִי, הָא — דְּלֹא טָבַל בַּשְּׁבִיעִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** This is **not difficult,** i.e., it is possible that Rabbi Tarfon accepted Rabbi Akiva's opinion with regard to a nazirite who shaved on the eighth day, and there is a difference between the two statements: In **this** case of the mishna, it is referring to a nazirite **who immersed on the seventh** day, which means that he is entirely pure on the eighth and can therefore bring his offerings on the same day after shaving. By contrast, in **that** case of Hillel's *baraita*, it is referring to one **who did not immerse on the seventh.** Consequently, as he immerses on the eighth day he may sacrifice his offerings only after sunset, on the ninth day.

Rava offers an alternative reading: *lo kashya* — the *braita* need not be read as a disagreement with Rabbi Akiva at all. The reason the *korban* is deferred to day nine in the *braita*'s case is not that the shaving was postponed, but rather that the *mikvah* immersion was postponed. *Ha de'taval bashvi'i ha delo taval bashvi'i* — one case is where the Nazir immersed in the *mikvah* on day seven; the other case is where he did not immerse until day eight. Even for a Nazir who is *tamei meit*, the *mikvah* immersion produces *tevul yom* status, and if the immersion occurred on day eight, the *korban* can only come on day nine. On this reading, even Rabbi Tarfon agrees with Rabbi Akiva, and the *braita* is simply describing a case of delayed immersion rather than delayed shaving.

### Critical Evaluation

Rava's reading, however, is problematic. If the *braita* intended to say that the immersion was delayed, it should have said *taval bashmini* — he immersed on day eight. Instead, it says *gilach bashmini* — he shaved on day eight. Rava has introduced a reference to delayed immersion that does not appear in the text of the *braita*, and the plain meaning of the *braita* clearly points to shaving as the operative act. The straightforward reading of the *braita*, assuming it refers to a Nazir, supports the position that Rabbi Tarfon did not concede.

## Tevul Yom Status

### Tamei Korban

A question arises: is it really so clear that a *tevul yom* cannot have *korbanot* brought on his behalf? What is the *halacha* regarding a *tamei* person's ability to have his *korbanot* offered?

### Tumah Categories

The *halacha* is that *erev ve-tamei hamishalchin korbanoseihem* — both an *erev* and a *tamei* may send their *korbanot* to be brought by a proxy. However, this applies only to milder forms of *tumah* such as *sheretz* or *neveila*. If the person is *tamei meit*, a *zav*, a *zavah*, or a *metzora*, they may not have a *korban* brought on their behalf. This is a basic point of *halacha*.

### Tosafot on Tahora

Even setting aside that rule, Tosafot makes a more fundamental point: it is implicit from the *pesukim* themselves that, for these specific *korbanot*, the Torah requires the person to be fully *tahor* before bringing them. By the Nazir the Torah specifies day seven for the sprinkling and shaving, and day eight for the *korban* — why would the Torah not simply have him bring the *korban* on day seven itself? By the *metzora*, the Torah similarly separates the *tahara* process from the *korban* by an explicit wait. The plain sense of the *pesukim* is that the Torah demands that the person exit the state of *tumah* entirely — including exiting *tevul yom* status — before offering the relevant *korban*. Whatever may be debated about other *korbanot* such as *shlamim*, for the particular *korbanot* of the Nazir's restart and the *metzora*'s purification, the Torah clearly anticipates full *tahara* as a prerequisite.

## Zav Entry Rules

### Zav's Process

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמֵינָא: אַשְׁכַּחְתִּינְהוּ לְחַבְרֵיהּ דְּרַב נָתָן בַּר הוֹשַׁעְיָא דְּיָתְבִין וְקָאָמְרִין: ״וּבָא לִפְנֵי ה׳ אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד וּנְתָנָם אֶל הַכֹּהֵן״, אֵימָתַי הוּא [בָּא]? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא טָבַל וְעָשָׂה הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ — אִין, לֹא טָבַל וְעָשָׂה הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye said: I encountered** the members of **the assembly of Rav Natan bar Hoshaya sitting and saying** the following: The verse states with regard to a man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*] who immerses on the seventh day of his purification: "And on the eighth day he shall take for himself two turtledoves or two young pigeons **and come before the Lord to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting and give them to the priest"** (Leviticus 15:14). **When** does **he come** to the courtyard to sacrifice his offerings? Only **when he has immersed** on the seventh day **and performed** the requirement to wait until **sunset.** In that case, **yes,** he brings his offerings, but if he **has not immersed and** has not **performed** the requirement to wait until **sunset, no,** he may not enter the courtyard.

Abaye reports that he encountered the colleagues of Rav Nassan bar Oshaya who were discussing the following *braita*. A *zav* undergoes a seven-day *tahara* process: *v'chi yithar hazav mizovo v'safar lo shivat yamim letaharato v'kibes bgadav v'rachatz besaro b'mayim chayim v'taher* — the *zav* counts seven days, launders his garments, immerses in living waters, and becomes *tahor*. On day eight he comes to bring his *korbanot*.

### Entry Conditions

The *braita* asks: *eimatai hu ba* — when may he enter? *Bizman shehu taval v'asah ha'erev shemesh* — only when he has immersed in the *mikvah* and nightfall has passed, meaning he is no longer a *tevul yom*. *Lo taval v'asah ha'erev shemesh* — if he had not done so, *lo* — he may not enter the *Mishkan* grounds.

### Tevul Yom Status

From this *braita*, the colleagues of Rav Nassan bar Oshaya sought to infer: *alma kasavar tevul yom shel zav ke-zav dami* — the *braita* apparently holds that even the *tevul yom* status of a *zav* retains the full status of a *zav* with respect to access to sacred ground. The *zav* is forbidden from entering *Machaneh Leviyah*, and the *braita* suggests this restriction persists even after the *mikvah* immersion, as long as he remains a *tevul yom*.

## Har Habayit Access

### Machaneh Leviyah

This question has concrete practical relevance. *Machaneh Leviyah*, the camp of the Levites in the wilderness, corresponds in the present era to *Har HaBayit*. A *zav* was prohibited from entering *Machaneh Leviyah* and would thereby transgress by entering *Har HaBayit*. The question, then, is whether a *tevul yom* of a *zav* retains this restriction after immersion.

### Modern Issue

The practical issue arises in the context of those who lead groups on tours of *Har HaBayit*. Those tours deliberately avoid the *makom hamikdash* itself, proceeding only through the parts of *Har HaBayit* that correspond to *Machaneh Leviyah*. The assumption underlying such tours is that even *tamei meit* — which we must presume applies to everyone today — does not disqualify a person from *Machaneh Leviyah*. However, one who carries a *tumah yotzei migufo* — a *tumah* that originates from within the body, such as a *zav* or a woman who is a *niddah* — is in a categorically different position.

### Baal Keri Status

The specific problem arises with a man who has had a seminal emission — a *ba'al keri*. Even if he immerses in the *mikvah* before ascending *Har HaBayit*, he is a *tevul yom* during daylight hours. The question is whether a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri*, analogous to a *tevul yom* of a *zav*, carries the same entry restrictions as the original *tumah* — in which case ascending *Har HaBayit* as a *tevul yom* would be prohibited. Whether tour organizers routinely address this scenario is unclear, but the halakhic question is a genuine one that flows directly from this sugya.

One should note that the situation of a woman who is a *niddah* is structurally different: she would not achieve *tevul yom* status during the day in the same way. But for a man who had a seminal emission and immerses in the morning before a daytime tour, the *tevul yom* status is directly operative and the question directly applies.

## Zav-Nazir Compare

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמֵינָא לְהוֹן אֲנָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה גַּבֵּי נָזִיר טָמֵא נָמֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״(וְהֵבִיא אוֹתָם) אֶל הַכֹּהֵן אֶל פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״. אֵימָתַי הוּא [בָּא] — בִּזְמַן שֶׁטָּבַל וְעָשָׂה הֶעֱרֵב שֶׁמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye adds: Upon hearing this, **I said to** those Sages: **If that is so, with regard to an impure nazirite too, as it is written: And he shall bring them,** referring to the verse "And on the eighth day he shall bring two turtledoves or two young pigeons **to the priest, to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Numbers 6:10), this can be explained in a similar manner: **When** does **he come** to the courtyard to sacrifice his offerings? **When he has immersed** himself **and performed** the requirement to wait until **sunset.**

Abaye reports that he responded to the colleagues of Rav Nassan bar Oshaya. He acknowledged the inference that *tevul yom shel zav ke-zav dami*, and then pressed the logic further: if the *pesukim* by the *zav* teach that he cannot enter the sacred precinct until he is no longer a *tevul yom*, then apply the same reasoning to the Nazir. The *pesukim* by the Nazir who became *tamei* also describe his coming *el petach ohel moed* on day eight to bring his *korbanot*. By the same logic, one could infer that on day seven, as a *tevul yom*, the Nazir was prohibited from entering. But this cannot be right.

### Nazir Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי שַׁעֲרֵי נִיקָנוֹר הֵיכָא קָיְימִין — בְּשַׁעֲרֵי לְוִיָּיה. וְהָתַנְיָא: טְמֵא מֵת מוּתָּר לִיכָּנֵס בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה, וְלֹא טְמֵא מֵת בִּלְבַד אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ מֵת עַצְמוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּקַּח מֹשֶׁה אֶת עַצְמוֹת יוֹסֵף עִמּוֹ״, מַאי ״עִמּוֹ״ — עִמּוֹ בִּמְחִיצָתוֹ, עִמּוֹ בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **Now** consider **the Gate of Nicanor,** where all those lacking atonement stand to bring their offerings; **where is it located? In the gates of the Levites.** Three camps are detailed in the verses, each having its own restrictions as to who may or may not enter them (see Numbers, chapter 5). The verses speak of the camps of the Jewish people in the wilderness, and the Sages taught that the statuses of certain areas of Jerusalem corresponded to those camps. The camps in the wilderness were the Israelite camp, the Levite camp, and the camp of the Divine Presence. They correspond to Jerusalem, the Temple Mount, and the Temple itself. The Gate of Nicanor had the status of the Levite camp. **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Kelim* 1:8) that one who is **impure** due to contact with **a corpse is permitted to enter the Levite camp; and not only one impure** from **a corpse, but even a corpse itself** may be brought inside the Levite camp, **as it is stated: "And Moses took the bones of Joseph with him"** (Exodus 13:19). The *baraita* explains: **What is** the meaning of **"with him"?** The phrase **"with him"** indicates that Joseph's bones were taken **within** Moses's **boundary, with him in the Levite camp.** This demonstrates that even a corpse may be brought into the Levite camp. If so, certainly a nazirite who was impure from contact with a corpse and who has been sprinkled and immersed may enter there. Consequently, the *halakha* that a nazirite must wait until the eighth day to sacrifice his offerings cannot be due to a prohibition against entering the Gate of Nicanor. One can similarly infer that the *halakha* that a *zav* who has immersed may not bring his offerings before the eighth day is not because he is prohibited from entering the Gate of Nicanor.

The reason it cannot be right is that the Nazir is *tamei meit*, and *tamei meit* is permitted to enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. Not only that, but even on day six when he was still fully *tamei meit* — before any sprinkling, before any immersion — he could freely enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. On day seven, as a *tevul yom* of *tumei meit*, he is categorically less *tamei* than he was on day six. If on day six he was permitted in *Machaneh Leviyah*, how could day seven's *tevul yom* status be read as creating an entry restriction?

### Dead Body Rule

The point is reinforced by a *braita*: not only is a person who is *tamei meit* permitted to enter *Machaneh Leviyah*, but *afilu meis atzmo* — even a corpse itself may be present in *Machaneh Leviyah*, as we learn from the verse *vayikach Moshe et atzmot Yosef imo* — Moshe took the bones of Yosef with him. *Mai imo — imo b'machaneh leviyah* — the bones of Yosef were present within *Machaneh Leviyah*. Since even a corpse is permitted there, the *pesukim* regarding the Nazir's day eight entry cannot possibly be read as implying that his *tevul yom* status on day seven barred him from *Machaneh Leviyah*. Those *pesukim* must be read differently.

## Abaye Resolution

### Tevul Yom Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא (אָמַר אַבָּיֵי): טְבוּל יוֹם שֶׁל זָב — כְּזָב דָּמֵי. וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, כֵּיוָן דִּמְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה — לָא עָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye says:** The *baraita* should be understood slightly differently than suggested by the assembly of Rav Natan bar Hoshaya: **One who immersed himself that day** to release himself from the status **of a *zav*,** i.e., a full-fledged *zav*, who experienced three emissions, not merely two, **is considered like a *zav*** with regard to the prohibition against entering the Levite camp. **But** the reason for this prohibition is not that a *zav* who immersed himself that day may not enter the Levite camp. Rather, granted that he is permitted to enter the Levite camp in terms of his impurity, **even so, since** he is **lacking atonement,** as he has yet to sacrifice his offerings, he **may not enter** the Levite camp.

Abaye proceeds to distinguish between different categories of *tevul yom*. According to Tosafot and the Rosh, whose *girsa* includes the word *lav*, the correct reading of Abaye's conclusion is: *lav ke-zav dami* — a *tevul yom shel zav* is ordinarily not treated as a *zav* for purposes of entry restrictions. A *ba'al keri* who immerses in the *mikvah* and is now a *tevul yom*, or a *zav* who saw an emission only twice and therefore does not need to bring a *korban*, would fall into this category of an ordinary *tevul yom* — and such a person's *tevul yom* status would not carry the entry restriction of the original *tumah*.

### Special Status

However, the case of the *zav* in the *braita* is different. This *zav* has seen an emission three times and is therefore obligated to bring a *korban* on day eight. After immersing in the *mikvah* on day seven, he is not merely a *tevul yom* waiting for nightfall. He is a *tevul yom* who is also *mechussar kippurim* — someone who still requires a *korban* to complete his *tahara* process. His *tevul yom* status is therefore not the naturally transitory state of someone who simply needs to wait for nightfall; there remains a further affirmative act — the bringing of the *korban* — without which he will not be fully *tahor*. This more complex status is treated more severely.

### Pesach Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי בְּמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה קָאֵי, אַמַּאי קָא קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״? לְמֵימְרָא: מָה הָתָם — מְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה לָא עָיֵיל, אַף לְמַחֲנֵה לְוִיָּיה נָמֵי — מְחוּסַּר כַּפָּרָה לָא עָיֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye elaborates: The reason he may not enter is that the Torah states with regard to a *zav*: "And he shall come before the Lord to the entrance of the Tent of Meeting" (Leviticus 15:14), **and if he is standing in the Levite camp,** at the Gate of Nicanor, when he brings his offerings, **why does** the Torah **call it "the Tent of Meeting"?** Rather, this verse must be coming **to say: Just as there,** with regard to the Tent of Meeting itself, i.e., the camp of the Divine Presence, **one who is lacking atonement may not enter** there, **so too,** with regard to **the Levite camp as well, one who is lacking atonement may not enter** there.

The question then becomes: why does the Torah describe *Machaneh Leviyah* using the terminology of *pesach ohel moed*?

### Extension Logic

By describing *Machaneh Leviyah* with the language *pesach ohel moed*, the Torah signals that the same logic of exclusion applies here. *Mah hatam mechusar kapparah lo ayyil, af lemachaneh leviyah nami mechusar kapparah lo ayyil* — just as a *mechusar kippurim* cannot enter *ohel moed*, so too cannot a *mechusar kippurim* who is also a *tevul yom* enter even *Machaneh Leviyah*. This restriction does not apply to an ordinary *mechusar kippurim* — such a person on day eight traverses *Machaneh Leviyah* to present his *korban* at the entrance to the inner precinct. The restriction is specific to a *tevul yom* who is simultaneously *mechusar kippurim*, a status more burdened than either alone.

## Sacred Zones

### Geography

The three camps of the *Mishkan* map onto the geography of the *Mikdash* as follows. *Machaneh Shechinah* corresponds to the inner precincts — the *azarah*, comprising *Ezrat Kohanim*, *Ezrat Yisrael*, and the *Mishkan* structure itself with the *mizbe'ach*. *Machaneh Leviyah* encompasses *Har HaBayit*, including the *Ezrat Nashim*. The camp of Israel corresponds to the city of Jerusalem itself.

### Tevul Entry

The question that arises from Abaye's analysis is whether an ordinary *tevul yom* — one who has immersed in the *mikvah* and is simply waiting for nightfall, with no further obligation — is permitted to enter *Machaneh Leviyah*. A full *tamei* of most categories may not enter. But a *tevul yom* is significantly less *tamei*.

### Restriction

Abaye's analysis concludes that a *tevul yom* who is also *mechusar kippurim* — specifically a *zav* who has immersed but still must bring a *korban* the next day — is prohibited even from *Machaneh Leviyah*. The textual derivation, as noted, is the use of *pesach ohel moed* to describe a location that is technically *Machaneh Leviyah*, teaching that the restrictions associated with the inner sanctum extend, in this specific case, to the outer camp as well.

### Exception

The ordinary *mechusar kippurim* — who has completed his *tevul yom* status and awaits only the bringing of his *korban* — is of course permitted in *Machaneh Leviyah*. That is precisely how he gets to the entrance to present his *korban*. What is restricted is the combined status of *tevul yom* and *mechusar kippurim* simultaneously. This is the nuanced distinction at the heart of Abaye's analysis.

## Practical Halakha

### Summary

Abaye's resolution is complex. Ordinarily, a *tevul yom shel zav* — according to Tosafot and the Rosh's *girsa* — is *lav ke-zav dami*, meaning his entry restrictions do not carry over from the original *tumah*. But when that same *tevul yom* is also *mechusar kippurim*, the combined status creates a heightened restriction that bars him even from *Machaneh Leviyah*.

### Uncertainty

The Gemara does not arrive at a clean resolution regarding the final *halacha* on the question of *tevul yom shel zav* and *Har HaBayit* access. Whether the *girsa* reads *ke-zav dami* or *lav ke-zav dami* affects the ultimate ruling, and the *maskana* of the sugya is not entirely clear.

### Modern Application

In practice, the contemporary approach to *Har HaBayit* access — for those *poskim* who permit it — appears to proceed on the assumption that a *tevul yom* of a *ba'al keri* is not treated as equivalent to the original *tumah* for purposes of *Machaneh Leviyah* entry. But the question is not trivially resolved, and the present sugya is precisely the *makom* in the Gemara where it is worked through. Anyone seeking to act in this area should be aware that the *gemara*'s discussion here bears directly on the *halacha*, and that clarity on this point depends significantly on how one resolves the textual and conceptual questions raised by Abaye's analysis.

## Tiglachat Taharah

### Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ תִּגְלַחַת הַטׇּהֳרָה כֵּיצַד? הָיָה מֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת: חַטָּאת, עוֹלָה, וּשְׁלָמִים. וְשׁוֹחֵט אֶת הַשְּׁלָמִים וּמְגַלֵּחַ עֲלֵיהֶם, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיָה מְגַלֵּחַ אֶלָּא עַל הַחַטָּאת, שֶׁהַחַטָּאת קוֹדֶמֶת בְּכׇל מָקוֹם. וְאִם גִּלַּח עַל אַחַת מִשְּׁלׇשְׁתָּן — יָצָא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a nazirite's **shaving of purity** after the completion of his term of naziriteship, **how** is it performed? **He would bring three animals:** One for **a sin-offering,** one for **a burnt-offering, and** one for **a peace-offering. And he slaughters the peace-offering and shaves** his hair **after** he slaughters **them.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Elazar says: He would shave only after** he slaughtered **the sin-offering, as the sin-offering precedes** the other offerings **in all places,** and therefore he sacrifices the sin-offering first. He shaves his hair after he slaughters this offering. **And if he shaved after** the sacrifice of any **one of the three of them, he has fulfilled** his obligation after the fact.

The Mishnah now turns to *tiglachat hataharah* — the shaving at the conclusion of a Nazir's full thirty-day *nezirut* period. The Mishnah describes the process: *haya meivi shalosh behemot* — he would bring three animals — *chatat, olah, u'shlamim*.

### Torah Source

The Torah states *v'shachat et hashlamim u'megalei'ach aleihem* — and he shall slaughter the *shlamim* and shave upon them. He takes the hair and places it *al ha'eish asher tachat zevach hashlamim* — on the fire beneath the pot of the *shlamim*.

### Korban Link

The question the Mishnah addresses is: to which *korban* is the act of shaving conceptually connected? The Torah does not explicitly sequence the shaving as following immediately from any particular offering; rather, it presents the sequence of *chatat*, *olah*, and *shlamim*, and then separately describes the shaving and the placing of the hair on the fire. The Mishnah therefore asks which *korban* one is conceptually *megalei'ach al* — shaving in connection with.

## Chatas vs Shlamim

### First Opinion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״וְגִלַּח הַנָּזִיר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד וְגוֹ׳״, בִּשְׁלָמִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara first addresses the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda, whose reasoning was not stated in the mishna. **The Sages taught** that when the Torah states: **"And the nazirite shall shave** his consecrated head **at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Numbers 6:18), **the verse is speaking of the peace-offering,** as the phrase "the entrance to the Tent of Meeting" alludes to a peace-offering, **as it is stated:** "And if his offering is a sacrifice of peace-offering…**and he shall slaughter it at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:1–2).

The first opinion holds that the shaving is connected to the *shlamim*. The textual basis is the phrase *pesach ohel moed* — the same language used in connection with the *shlamim* in the beginning of *Vayikra* (*v'shachato pesach ohel moed*) — linking the shaving of the Nazir to the ritual of the *shlamim*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

Rabbi Eliezer disagrees: *lo haya megalei'ach ela al hachatas, shehachatas kodemes b'chol makom* — the shaving is connected to the *chatat*, because the *chatat* is always brought first. Wherever a list of *korbanot* appears, the *chatat* takes precedence. Therefore, since the shaving is associated with the first *korban* brought, it is associated with the *chatat*.

Tosafot notes an interesting implication of this dispute. According to the opinion that the shaving is connected to the *shlamim*, and given that *shlamim* is always *lashon rabbim* (the word has no singular form, like pants in English), the word *aleihem* in the verse would naturally refer only to the *shlamim*. Tosafot also understands that the first opinion would agree with Rabbi Eliezer's logic that the *korban* with which one shaves is brought first, and therefore the first opinion holds that the *shlamim* is brought first. However, this is not stated explicitly in the Mishnah.

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הֵבִיא שָׁלֹשׁ בְּהֵמוֹת וְלֹא פֵּירֵשׁ, הָרְאוּיָה לְחַטָּאת — תִּקְרַב חַטָּאת, לְעוֹלָה — תִּקְרַב עוֹלָה, לִשְׁלָמִים — תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If a nazirite **brought three animals without specifying** which of them was for which offering, the one **that is fit for a sin-offering,** i.e., a female sheep in its first year, **is sacrificed as a sin-offering;** that which is fit **for a burnt-offering,** a male sheep in its first year, **is sacrificed as a burnt-offering;** and that which is fit **for a peace-offering,** a ram, i.e., a male sheep over the age of one year, **is sacrificed as a peace-offering.**

Rabban Gamliel introduces a third position: *heivi shalosh behemot v'lo peirash* — he brought three animals without specifying which was designated for which offering. In such a case, *hara'uya lachatatat tikrav chatatat, l'olah tikrav olah, u'lishlamim tikrav shlamim* — the animal suited to be a *chatat* is offered as a *chatat*, the one suited for an *olah* as an *olah*, and the one suited for *shlamim* as *shlamim*. Tosafot specifies the practical identification: the female sheep is suited for the *chatat*, the male sheep for the *olah*, and the ram for the *shlamim*. The *kedusha* thus defines itself according to the nature of each animal.

## Shlamim Location (III)

### Historical Location

The Gemara notes — and it is confirmed by other sources — that there was in fact a physical chamber in the *Beit HaMikdash* designated for this purpose: the *Lishkat Nezirim*, located in the *Ezrat Nashim*. This was the room where Nazirites would shave and where the *shlamim* were cooked. The practical setting of the *tiglachat taharah* thus took place in the *Ezrat Nashim*, which falls within *Machaneh Leviyah*.

## Location Analysis

### Braita Location

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַתָּה אוֹמֵר בִּשְׁלָמִים הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד מַמָּשׁ? אָמַרְתָּ: אִם כֵּן — דֶּרֶךְ בִּזָּיוֹן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** Do **you say the verse is speaking of the peace-offering,** i.e., that the nazirite shaves after bringing his peace-offering? **Or** perhaps **it is only** teaching that he shaves at **the actual entrance to the Tent of Meeting,** at the gate to the Sanctuary, as indicated by a literal reading of the verse? **You** can **say** in response: **If so, that is a degrading manner** of service, to shave at the entrance to the Sanctuary.

A *braita* is cited: *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed* — and the Nazir shaves at *pesach ohel moed*. The *braita* then asks: does this phrase indicate the actual location of the shaving, or is it a metaphorical expression meaning that the shaving takes place in conjunction with the *shlamim*?

### Literal vs Metaphor

The *braita* presents both possibilities. Perhaps *pesach ohel moed* literally designates the place where the shaving occurs — namely, within the *makom hamikdash* itself. On this reading, the Nazir would shave in the *Ezrat Yisrael* or similar space within the inner precinct. Alternatively, *pesach ohel moed* is a way of saying that the shaving is conceptually linked to the *shlamim*, which are described elsewhere as being brought *pesach ohel moed*.

### Respect Argument

The *braita* rejects the literal reading on grounds of *derekh bizayon* — disrespect for the *makom hamikdash*. Performing a shaving on the Temple grounds themselves would be degrading to the sacred space. Therefore, *pesach ohel moed* in this context must be read as a reference to the *shlamim* and not as a literal geographic designation. The shaving takes place wherever the *shlamim* are being prepared.

## Pesach Timing

### Door Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּא חָנָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: ״וְגִלַּח הַנָּזִיר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁאֵין פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד פָּתוּחַ — אֵינוֹ מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abba Ḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer** that the verse **"And the nazirite shall shave** his consecrated head **at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** (Numbers 6:18) is referring to the time rather than the place of his shaving, i.e., this verse teaches that **as long as the entrance to the Tent of Meeting is not open, he may not shave.** He may shave only during those hours when the entrance to the Sanctuary is open.

Abba Chanan cites a teaching in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed, kol zman she-ein pesach ohel moed pasuach eino megalei'ach* — as long as the doors of the *Ohel Moed* are not open, the Nazir may not shave. This reading does not treat *pesach ohel moed* as a purely metaphorical reference to the *shlamim*. Rather, it understands the phrase to mean that the shaving must occur while the *Ohel Moed* is open — whenever and wherever the shaving takes place, even in the *Ezrat Nashim*, the doors must be open. This is analogous to the rule governing *shlamim*: if the doors were closed when the *shlamim* was slaughtered, the *korban* is *pasul*. The shaving of the Nazir is similarly rendered invalid if the doors were not open at the time.

## Gender Distinction (IV)

### Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן שֵׁזוּרִי אוֹמֵר: ״וְגִלַּח הַנָּזִיר פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, וְלֹא נְזִירָה,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon Shezuri says:** The phrase **"And the nazirite shall shave** his consecrated head **at the entrance to the Tent of Meeting"** does not refer to the place of his shaving. Rather, the masculine form of the word nazirite serves to emphasize that this applies to a male nazirite **and not a female nazirite.** A woman does not shave her head in the Sanctuary,

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri draws a different inference from the phrase *v'gilach hanazir pesach ohel moed*: the verse specifies *hanazir* — the Nazir — *v'lo nezirah* — but not the *nezirah*, the female Nazir. A male Nazir performs the shaving at or near *pesach ohel moed*; a female *nezirah* does not.

## Pesach Timing (II)

### Offering Parallel

The basis for Abba Chanan's position is the parallel to the *shlamim*: just as the *shlamim* must be offered while the doors of the *Ohel Moed* are open, and if the doors were closed the offering is *pasul*, so too the shaving of the Nazir requires that the doors be open. A shaving performed while the doors were closed would be halakhically invalid.

## Gender Distinction (IV) (II)

### Concern

The reason Rabbi Shimon Shezuri distinguishes between the male Nazir and the female *nezirah* is *shema yisgaru bah pirchei kehunah* — lest the young *Kohanim* be aroused by her, since her hair would be uncovered during the shaving.

## Shlamim Location (III)

### Hair Placement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִצְחָק אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וְלָקַח אֶת שְׂעַר רֹאשׁ נִזְרוֹ וְנָתַן עַל הָאֵשׁ וְגוֹ׳״, מִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְחוּסָּר אֶלָּא לְקִיחָה וּנְתִינָה. יָצָא זֶה, שֶׁהוּא מְחוּסָּר לְקִיחָה, הֲבָאָה, וּנְתִינָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** It is **not necessary** to cite an indirect proof that a nazirite may not shave at the entrance to the Sanctuary, **as** the verse **states** with regard to a nazirite: **"And he shall take the hair of his consecrated head and put it on the fire** which is under the sacrifice of the peace-offering" (Numbers 6:18). The verse is referring to **one who has not yet** performed **only** the stages of **taking and placing** his shaven hair under the pot of the peace-offering, which is outside the Sanctuary. These two stages are the only ones he lacks; he does not have to do any other action. That **excludes this one,** a nazirite who shaved at the entrance to the Sanctuary, **as he has not yet** performed three actions, **taking, bringing** the hair from one sanctified area to another, **and placing** his shaven hair under the pot of the peace-offering.

Rabbi Yitzchak offers a further derivation. The Torah states *v'lakach et se'ar rosh nizro v'nasan al ha'eish* — he takes the hair of his head and places it on the fire. This act of taking and immediately placing the hair on the fire implies that the shaving occurs at the very location where the fire is burning. Since the fire is beneath the pot cooking the *shlamim*, the shaving must take place at the same location where the *shlamim* are being cooked. *Mi she-eino mechusar ela lekichah u'nesinah* — the process requires nothing more than taking and placing; *yatza zeh she-mechusar lekichah, hava'ah u'nesinah* — this rules out a scenario where the shaving occurs in one location and the hair must then be transported elsewhere, which would require an additional act of bringing. The Torah's formulation implies a single, immediate sequence: shave and place.

## Gender Distinction (IV) (III)…

### Tosafot Read

Tosafot's reading of Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's position clarifies the geography. Since the concern is specifically about *pirchei kehunah* — the young *Kohanim* — who would be present in and around the *Ezrat Nashim* area, Rabbi Shimon Shezuri is saying that while a male Nazir shaves in the *Ezrat Nashim* at the *Lishkat Nezirim* (or in its vicinity, which is *pesach ohel moed* in the relevant sense), a *nezirah* shaves in a more private location somewhere in Jerusalem, away from the *Mikdash* precincts altogether.

## Shlamim Location (III)…

### Lishkat Nezirim

From Rabbi Yitzchak's derivation it follows that the shaving does not take place in the *Mikdash* proper but in the *Lishkat Nezirim*, the chamber located in the *Ezrat Nashim*. It is there that the *shlamim* of the Nazir are cooked, and it is therefore there that the shaving takes place, allowing the hair to be placed directly on the fire beneath the *shlamim*. The *Lishkat Nezirim* in the *Ezrat Nashim* is thus the historical location of the *tiglachat taharah* in Temple times.

## Gender Distinction (IV) (III)……

### Sotah Objection

An objection is raised to Rabbi Shimon Shezuri's distinction: *amru lo — lidvarecha sotah tochiah* — according to your reasoning, the case of the *sotah* should disprove your point. By the *sotah*, the Torah writes *v'he'emidah lifnei Hashem* — she is stood before God, in the *Mikdash*, with her hair uncovered — and we do not find any concern raised about *shema yisgaru bah pirchei kehunah*. If uncovered hair in the *Mikdash* were truly a problem, it would have been addressed there.

### Response

Rabbi Shimon Shezuri responds: *zu kochelet u'fokeset* — the *nezirah* has her makeup on, she is groomed and attractive in appearance. *Zu einah kochelet u'fokeset* — the *sotah*, by contrast, is not in that state; she is disheveled and is undergoing a degrading public ordeal. The two cases are not comparable. The *nezirah*, with her hair freshly uncovered and her normal appearance intact, would more likely attract the attention and urges of the young *Kohanim* than the *sotah*, who presents a very different appearance. The Gemara will continue on the following day with the remaining discussion of where exactly the shaving may take place within or outside *Makom HaMikdash*.
