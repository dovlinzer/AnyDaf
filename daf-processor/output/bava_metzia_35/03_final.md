# Bava Metzia 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer's Oath

### Rav Huna Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתוֹ שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ. מַאי טַעְמָא? חָיְישִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא עֵינָיו נָתַן בָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says:** Even if the bailee declares his intention to pay for the deposit, the court **administers an oath to him that** the item was actually stolen or lost and **is not in his possession. What is the reason** for this oath? **We are concerned** that **perhaps he coveted** that item.

The daf begins approximately twenty lines down on 34b, at the words *amar Rav Huna*. The context is the *mishna*'s ruling that a *shomer* who agrees to pay — and in doing so obtains the right to collect *kefal* if the thief is later found — has been incentivized to move things along and ensure the owner receives his principal. The *gemara* has been exploring the various dimensions of that arrangement. Now Rav Huna introduces a further requirement: even when the *shomer* agrees to pay, we still administer an oath. As the *gemara* states, *amar Rav Huna mashvinan lei shvua she'eino birshuso* — we make him swear that the object is not in his possession.

### Shomer's Covetousness

The reason is immediate: *chashinan shema einav nasan bah* — we are concerned that he has set his eye upon the object, that he covets it. It is entirely possible, in other words, that the *shomer* is agreeing to pay precisely because he wants to keep the object for himself. By paying, he silences the owner's claim while quietly retaining the deposit. Rav Huna therefore rules that no *shomer* — not even one who volunteers to pay — is ever fully exempt from swearing that the *pikadon* is genuinely not in his *reshus*. This principle will be tested and challenged at length in the sections that follow.

## Mashkon Cases

### Mashkon Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ עַל הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, וְאָבַד הַמַּשְׁכּוֹן, וְאָמַר לוֹ: סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, שֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה. וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, סֶלַע הָיָה שָׁוֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a mishna (*Shevuot* 43a): In the case of **one who lends** money **to another on** the basis of **collateral, and the collateral was lost, and** the creditor **says to** the debtor: **I loaned you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and that collateral **was worth a shekel,** i.e., a half-*sela*. Therefore you owe me a shekel. **And the other** individual, the debtor, **says** in response to that claim: **That is not** the case. **Rather, you loaned me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral, and the collateral **was worth a *sela*;** I owe you nothing. In this case, the debtor is **exempt** from payment.

The *gemara* now pivots to a *braisa* that will serve as a challenge to Rav Huna's position. The case involves a *malveh* — a lender — who holds a *mashkon*, a piece of collateral, against a loan he has extended to a *loveh*. The *mashkon* is then lost. Since the *malveh* functions as a *shomer* over the collateral, its loss falls upon him, and its value is to be deducted from the outstanding loan. The question that arises is: how much of the loan remains after that deduction? And on that question, lender and borrower disagree.

### Four Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, שֶׁקֶל הָיָה שָׁוֶה, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, שְׁלֹשָׁה דִּינָרִים הָיָה שָׁוֶה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the creditor claimed: **I loaned you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and it was worth a shekel. And the other** individual, the debtor, **says: That is not** the case; **rather, you loaned me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral **and** the collateral **was worth three dinars,** i.e., three-quarters of a *sela*. In this case, the debtor is **obligated** to take an oath, due to the fact that he responded to the claim of the creditor with a partial admission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, שְׁנַיִם הָיָה שָׁוֶה, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, סֶלַע הָיָה שָׁוֶה – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If the debtor said: **You loaned me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and the collateral **was worth two** *sela*, so now you owe me a *sela*. **And the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **said: That is not** the case; **rather, I loaned you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and the collateral **was worth a *sela*.** Here, the creditor is **exempt.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, שְׁנַיִם הָיָה שָׁוֶה, וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, חֲמִשָּׁה דִּינָרִים הָיָה שָׁוֶה – חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** If the debtor said: **You loaned me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and the collateral **was worth two** *sela*. **And the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **said: That is not** the case; **rather, I loaned you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and the collateral **was worth five dinars.** Here, the creditor is **obligated** to take an oath due to the fact that he responded to the claim of the debtor with a partial admission.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי נִשְׁבָּע? מִי שֶׁהַפִּקָּדוֹן אֶצְלוֹ. שֶׁמָּא יִשָּׁבַע זֶה וְיוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת הַפִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna concludes: **Who takes** the **oath? The one** in **whose** possession **the deposit was** located, i.e., the creditor, who took collateral from the debtor. The Sages instituted this provision **lest this** party, i.e., the debtor, **take an oath and the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **produce the deposit** and prove the oath false.

The *braisa* sets out four scenarios, turning on the relative values of the loan and the *mashkon*. In the first sub-case of the opening section: I, the *malveh*, lent a hundred and claim the *mashkon* was worth fifty — so the *loveh* still owes me fifty. The *loveh* says it was worth a hundred, so he owes me nothing. He is *kofer hakol* — denying the entire claim — and is therefore *patur*, exempt from an oath. In the second sub-case: I say it was worth fifty so he owes me fifty; he says it was worth seventy-five so he only owes me twenty-five. That is *modeh b'miktzas* — a partial admission — and he is *chayav*, obligated to take an oath.

The *braisa* then turns to cases where the *mashkon* exceeded the loan. The *loveh* claims the collateral was worth two hundred against a loan of one hundred, so I owe him one hundred. I the *malveh* say it was worth only one hundred — we are even, I owe him nothing. I am *kofer hakol* and exempt. In the parallel case: the *loveh* says it was worth two hundred, I owe him one hundred; I say it was worth one hundred and twenty-five, so I owe him only twenty-five. That is again *modeh b'miktzas* and I am obligated.

The *chiddush* of the *braisa* is its ruling on *who* takes the oath in the *modeh b'miktzas* situations. Ordinarily, it is the party who is making a partial admission — the *loveh* in the first pair, the *malveh* in the second — who swears. The *braisa*, however, rules: *mi shehapikadon etzlo* — the one in whose possession the *pikadon* rested, that is, always the *malveh*, is the one who takes the oath.

### Malveh Swears

Why should the *malveh* always be the one to swear, even in cases where the *loveh* is the *modeh b'miktzas*? The *braisa*'s reasoning: *shema yishba zeh v'yotzi halah et hapikadon* — perhaps the *loveh* will take his oath regarding the value, and then the *malveh* will produce the *mashkon*, rendering that oath either false or moot. If the *loveh* swears that the *mashkon* was worth seventy-five and the *malveh* subsequently produces it and it turns out to be worth only fifty, the *loveh* has now taken a *shvuas sheker*.

Tosfos clarifies that the concern is not primarily for the *loveh*'s personal dishonor — if he swore falsely, one might say he deserves the consequences. The real problem is that it makes the *beis din* look as though it imposed an unnecessary oath: the court compelled the *loveh* to swear about the value of an object the *malveh* had concealed all along. The court process itself is discredited. Therefore, in all such cases, the *malveh* — the party holding the *pikadon* — takes the oath regarding value, because he will not produce the object and prove himself a liar.

### Challenge Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתָא לִדְרַב הוּנָא כֵּיוָן דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע מִלְוֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, הֵיכִי מָצֵי מַפֵּיק לַהּ? אָמַר רָבָא: שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁנִּשְׂרְפָה.
>
> **Translation:** the statement of **Rav Huna is so, once** the **creditor takes an oath that** the collateral **is not in his possession, how can he produce it** thereafter? **Rava said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where there are witnesses that** the collateral **was burned.** Therefore, the creditor need not take an oath that it is not in his possession.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מֵהֵיכָא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: שֶׁיֵּשׁ עֵדִים שֶׁנִּגְנְבָה. סוֹף סוֹף מֵהֵיכָא מַיְיתֵי לַהּ? דְּטָרַח וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** the question remains: Why is the obligation to take the oath transferred from the debtor to the creditor? There is no concern that the creditor will produce the collateral. **From where will he bring it** if it was burned? **Rather, Rav Yosef said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where there are witnesses that** the collateral **was stolen.** The Gemara asks: **Ultimately,** in that case too, **from where will** the creditor **bring** the collateral if it was stolen? The Gemara answers: Although the collateral had been stolen, it is possible the creditor will **exert** himself to locate the thief **and bring** the collateral, thereby proving that the debtor took a false oath.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, כִּי מִשְׁתְּבַע מַלְוֶה נָמֵי, לִטְרַח לֹוֶה וְלַיְתֵי! בִּשְׁלָמָא מַלְוֶה יָדַע מַאן קָא עָיֵיל וְנָפֵק בְּבֵיתֵיהּ, וְאָזֵיל וְטָרַח וּמַיְיתֵי לַהּ. אֶלָּא לֹוֶה, מִי יָדַע מַאן עָיֵיל וְנָפֵיק בְּבֵיתֵיהּ דְּמַלְוֶה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** in a case **where** the **creditor takes an oath as well, let** the **debtor exert** himself **and bring** the collateral, thereby proving that the creditor took a false oath. The Gemara answers: This is unlikely. **Granted,** there is concern that the **creditor** will recover the stolen collateral, as he **knows who enters and exits his house,** so he may have some inkling of the identity of the thief. **And** therefore, **he goes and exerts** himself **and brings** the collateral. **But** with regard to the **debtor, does he know who enters and exits the creditor's house?** He has no inkling who the thief might be.

The *gemara* now turns to challenge Rav Huna directly. The challenge runs as follows: according to Rav Huna, in every case where the *malveh* claims the *mashkon* was lost, he must swear *she'eino birshuso* — that it is not in his possession — before any further dispute about value proceeds. If that is so, then in all four of the *braisa*'s scenarios, the *malveh* has already sworn he does not have the *mashkon*. Why, then, is there any concern that he will later produce it? Having taken that oath, he cannot produce it without convicting himself of perjury. The entire rationale for taking the oath away from the *loveh* and placing it on the *malveh* collapses. The *braisa*'s distinctive ruling — *mi shehapikadon etzlo* — presupposes that the production of the *mashkon* remains a live threat. But Rav Huna has apparently neutralized that threat.

The *gemara* considers and rejects two intermediate answers. Rava first suggests: perhaps the case is one where witnesses testify the *mashkon* was burned — the *malveh* therefore never had to take Rav Huna's oath in the first place, yet we still worry he might produce something. But the *gemara* pushes back: *meheika meisi lei* — from where would he produce burned goods? Rav Yosef then suggests: witnesses testified it was stolen, so again Rav Huna's oath was not required. But the same objection applies: if witnesses saw it stolen, how will the *malveh* produce it? The *gemara* answers: *de-tirach u-maisi lei* — he could expend effort, investigate who stole it, track down the thief, and retrieve the object. The counterargument is then raised: if so, why isn't the same concern applicable once the *malveh* himself takes the oath? The answer is asymmetric knowledge — *bishlama malveh yodeia man ka'ayil v'nafik b'veitei* — the *malveh* knows who has been in and out of his home and can therefore pursue the recovery of the stolen object. The *loveh*, by contrast, has no such knowledge about the *malveh*'s household and cannot similarly investigate.

One might add — and it is a point the *gemara* itself does not make explicitly — that the *malveh* has a strong financial incentive to find the *mashkon*: having already credited its value against the loan, he would be recovering a real economic loss if he retrieves it.

### Rava's Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אוֹמֵר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יִטְעוֹן וְיֹאמַר לוֹ: אַחַר שְׁבוּעָה מְצָאתִיהָ. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: זֶה נִשְׁבָּע וְזֶה נִשְׁבָּע, זֶה נִשְׁבָּע שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, וְזֶה נִשְׁבָּע כַּמָּה הָיָה שָׁוֶה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מִי נִשְׁבָּע תְּחִילָּה? מַלְוֶה נִשְׁבָּע תְּחִילָּה, שֶׁמָּא יִשָּׁבַע זֶה וְיוֹצִיא הַלָּה אֶת הַפִּקָּדוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye says:** Although the creditor takes an oath that the collateral is not in his possession, the obligation to take an oath for partial admission is transferred from the debtor to the creditor, as the Sages issued **a decree lest** the debtor take the oath for his partial admission and the creditor **claim and say to him: I found** the collateral **after** you took the **oath. Rav Ashi says: This** party, the creditor, **takes an oath and that** party, the debtor, **takes an oath. This** party, the creditor, **takes an oath that** the collateral **is not in his possession. And that** party, the debtor, **takes an oath** as to **how much** the collateral **was worth. And this** is what the mishna **is saying: Who takes an oath first? The creditor takes an oath first** that the collateral is not in his possession, **lest this** party, i.e., the debtor, **take an oath and the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **produce the deposit.**

A cleaner resolution is offered by Rava: *zeh nishba v'zeh nishba* — both parties swear. The *braisa* is not saying that the oath moves entirely from the *loveh* to the *malveh*. Rather, it is describing the sequence: the *malveh* first swears *she'eino b'reshuso*, consistent with Rav Huna's requirement, and only then does the *loveh* proceed to take the classic *shvuas modeh b'miktzas* regarding the value. *Mah nishba techila* — who swears first? — *malveh nishba techila*, the *malveh* swears first that it is not in his possession. Once that has been established and the concern of production has been neutralized, the *loveh* makes his own oath. This is admittedly not the plain reading of the *braisa*, but it preserves Rav Huna's position.

### Defense Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: רֵישָׁא דְסֵיפָא תְּיוּבְתָּא לְרַב הוּנָא, סֶלַע הִלְוִיתַנִי עָלָיו, שְׁתַּיִם הָיָה שָׁוֶה. וְהַלָּה אוֹמֵר: לֹא כִּי, אֶלָּא סֶלַע הִלְוִיתִיךָ עָלָיו, סֶלַע הָיָה שָׁוֶה – פָּטוּר. וְאִם אִיתָא לִדְרַב הוּנָא מִגּוֹ דְּמִשְׁתְּבַע מַלְוֶה שֶׁאֵינָהּ בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ, לִישְׁתְּבַע נָמֵי אַגִּילְגּוּל שְׁבוּעָה כַּמָּה הָיָה שָׁוֶה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna bar Taḥlifa said in the name of Rava: The first** part **of the latter clause** of the mishna is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Huna,** who said that the creditor is obligated to take an oath that the collateral is not in his possession. In that clause, the debtor said: **You loaned me a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral, and the collateral **was worth two** *sela*, so now you owe me a *sela*. **And the other** party, i.e., the creditor, **said: That is not** the case, **rather, I loaned you a *sela* on** the basis of that collateral and the collateral **was worth a *sela*.** In this case, the creditor is **exempt. And if** the statement of **Rav Huna is so,** once **the creditor takes an oath that** the collateral **is not in his possession, let him also take an oath by means of extension of an oath** as to **how much** the collateral **was worth,** as one obligated to take an oath can be forced to take other oaths as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא וְאָמַר לִי: תְּהֵא בְּמַאֲמִינוֹ. וּנְהֵמְנֵיהּ לֹוֶה לְמַלְוֶה נָמֵי בְּהָא כַּמָּה הֲוָה שָׁוֶה? לָא קִים לֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּיהּ. וּנְהֵמְנֵיהּ מַלְוֶה לְלֹוֶה, דְּקִים לֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּיהּ? לָא מְהֵימַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: I stated** this ***halakha* before Rav Kahana, and he said to me: Let** the *halakha* in the mishna **be** understood **with regard to** a case where the debtor **trusts** the creditor that the collateral is no longer in his possession. The Gemara challenges: **But** if so, **let the debtor trust the creditor with regard to this** matter of **how much** the collateral **was worth.** The Gemara explains: The creditor is **not certain about** the value of the collateral, as the item did not belong to him, which is why the debtor does not rely upon him to take an oath concerning its value. The Gemara challenges: **But let the creditor trust the debtor, as** the debtor is **certain about** the value of the collateral, as it is his. The Gemara answers: The creditor **does not trust** the debtor.

Rav Huna bar Tachalifa, citing Rava, then identifies a sharper challenge to Rav Huna from the opening sub-case of the second pair in the *braisa* — the *reisha d'seifa*. There, the *loveh* says the *mashkon* was worth two hundred, the *malveh* says it was worth only one hundred, so the *malveh* is *kofer hakol* and *patur*. According to Rav Huna, however, the *malveh* must always swear *eino birshuso*. Once he is making that oath, the *beis din* may apply *gilgul shvua* — the principle that an existing oath can be extended to cover related matters — and require him to swear as well about the value. That would make him *chayav* on the question of value, not *patur*. Yet the *braisa* simply says *patur*. This is a genuine *tiyufta* — a refutation — of Rav Huna.

### Rav Ashi Explains

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this entire discussion before Rav Kahana, who answered: *tehei b'ma'amino* — the case in the *braisa* is one where the *loveh* declared that he trusts the *malveh*. The *loveh* waived his right to compel the *malveh* to swear *eino birshuso*; he simply believed him and released him from that obligation. Since the *malveh* never takes the initial oath, there is no *gilgul shvua* to attach the value-question onto, and he remains *patur*. This is openly acknowledged as a forced reading — *ukimta* — but it is the available defense of Rav Huna's position.

### Trust Asymmetry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא לֹוֶה דִּמְהֵימַן לֵיהּ לְמַלְוֶה, וּמַאי שְׁנָא מַלְוֶה דְּלָא מְהֵימַן לֵיהּ לְלֹוֶה? לֹוֶה מְקַיֵּים בֵּיהּ בְּמַלְוֶה ״תֻּמַּת יְשָׁרִים תַּנְחֵם״, מַלְוֶה מְקַיֵּים בֵּיהּ בְּלֹוֶה ״וְסֶלֶף בּוֹגְדִים יְשָׁדֵּם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is different** so that the **debtor trusts the creditor** that the collateral is not in his possession, **and what is different** that the **creditor does not trust the debtor** to accurately assess the value of the collateral? The Gemara answers: The **debtor** sees **in the creditor fulfillment** of the verse: **"The integrity of the upright shall guide them"** (Proverbs 11:3). He believes that God blesses the creditor with wealth to lend because he is an upright person. The **creditor** sees **in the debtor fulfillment** of the end of that verse: **"But the perverseness of the faithless shall destroy them"** (Proverbs 11:3). The creditor believes that God made the debtor poor because he is a deceitful person.

The *gemara* immediately presses the inconsistency: if the *loveh* trusts the *malveh* enough to waive the oath about possession — thereby sacrificing the advantage of *gilgul shvua* — why doesn't the *malveh* reciprocate and trust the *loveh*'s assessment of the *mashkon*'s value? After all, it was the *loveh*'s object; he knows better than the *malveh* what it was worth. Why does the *malveh* refuse to accept the *loveh*'s claim of two hundred? The *gemara* answers: *kim lei b'gavei* — the *loveh* trusts the *malveh* because the *malveh* is the one who actually held and handled the *mashkon* and knows what he received. But regarding value, the *loveh* believes his own knowledge of his object exceeds the *malveh*'s; he is not willing to accept the *malveh*'s lower valuation. The *malveh*, for his part, does not return the trust.

### Wealth Theology

The *gemara* explains the asymmetry with a striking theological aside. The *loveh* applies to the *malveh* the verse *tumat yesharim tanchem* — the integrity of the upright guides them — reasoning that since the *malveh* is prosperous and able to lend money, he must be a righteous man whose word is reliable. The *malveh*, in turn, applies to the *loveh* the verse *ve-selef bogdim yeshaded* — the perverseness of the treacherous destroys them — reasoning that since the *loveh* is in the position of having to borrow, he is presumably less reliable.

This is a genuinely striking *gemara*. The underlying theology — that wealth is evidence of divine favor and therefore of moral trustworthiness, while poverty signals the reverse — is, as one might note, in significant tension with much of the Torah's own treatment of the poor, which accepts economic variation as a social reality and enjoins compassion toward the impoverished. Even the biblical framework of *sechar v'onesh* speaks in aggregate and communal terms rather than translating individual prosperity directly into individual righteousness. That said, there are certainly biblical precedents — Yitzchak's hundredfold return, Avimelech's recognition that God has blessed him — where prosperity is read as divine approbation. The *gemara* here pushes that sensibility to an uncomfortable extreme in service of explaining a legal asymmetry in who trusts whom.

## Lost Jewelry Case

### Seizing House

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַפְקֵיד כִּיפֵּי גַּבֵּי חַבְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי כִּיפַּי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא יָדַעְנָא הֵיכָא אוֹתְבִי[תִי]נְהוּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל לָא יָדַעְנָא פְּשִׁיעוּתָא הִיא, זִיל שַׁלֵּים! לָא שַׁילֵּם. אֲזַל רַב נַחְמָן אַגַּבְּיֵהּ לְאַפַּדְנֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ, לְסוֹף אִישְׁתְּכֻח כִּיפֵּי וְאִיַּקּוּר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הָדְרִי כִּיפֵּי לְמָרַיְיהוּ. וְהָדְרָא אַפַּדְנָא לְמָרַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **A certain man deposited jewels [*keifei*] with another.** When the period of the deposit was complete, the owner of the jewels **said to** the bailee: **Give me** the **jewels.** The bailee **said to him** in response: **I do not know where I placed them.** The matter **came before Rav Naḥman,** who **said to** the bailee: **Every** circumstance where a bailee claims: **I do not know** where I placed them, **is** in and of itself **negligence. Go pay** him for the jewels. The bailee **did not pay. Rav Naḥman went** and gave instructions to **repossess his palace** and sell it to pay for the jewels. **Ultimately,** not only **were the jewels found, but** they had also **increased** in value. **Rav Naḥman said: The jewels return to their** initial **owner, and the palace returns to its owner,** and the bailee does not profit from the increase in the value of the jewels.

The *gemara* now shifts to a narrative case involving the doctrine of *shuma hadar*. A certain man deposited jewelry with his friend. The friend was asked to return the jewelry and said, *lo yadana heicha otveinhu* — I do not know where I put it. It was not a dramatic loss but a misplacement. The case came before Rav Nachman, who ruled: *kol lo yadana peshiuta hi* — any "I don't know where it is" constitutes negligence. He therefore ordered the *shomer* to pay — *zil shalim*. When the *shomer* failed to do so, Rav Nachman seized his house — *agbei l'apadnei minei* — in payment of the debt.

### Found Item

The jewelry turned up — *ishtakach kifi* — and its value had increased. Rav Nachman then ruled: *hadar kifi l'mareihu v'hadar apadna l'marei* — the jewelry goes back to its owner and the house goes back to its owner.

### Rava's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן, וּפִרְקִין ״הַמַּפְקִיד״ הֲוָה, וַאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: שִׁילֵּם וְלֹא רָצָה לִישָּׁבַע. וְלָא אַהְדַּר לִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: I was sitting** at that time studying **before Rav Naḥman, and our chapter** of study **was** this chapter: **One who deposits,** which is relevant to this case. **And I said to** Rav Naḥman: Isn't this the case of a bailee who **paid** the owner **and did not wish to take an oath?** And it is the bailee **who** receives the double payment, ostensibly because once he paid, the owner transfers ownership of the item to him. **And** Rav Naḥman **did not answer me, and he did well that he did not answer me,** as the question was not worthy of an answer.

Rava reports that he was sitting before Rav Nachman at the time, and they were studying the very *perek* of *Hamafkid*. He raised a challenge from the *mishna*: when a *shomer* pays and thereby acquires the object, he is entitled to any subsequent increase in value — just as he acquires the right to *kefal* if the thief is found. Here, the house was seized as payment; the *shomer* has effectively purchased the jewelry through that payment. The jewelry subsequently increased in value. On what basis do we reverse the transaction and restore both items to their original owners? Rava notes that Rav Nachman did not respond to this challenge — *v'lo hadar li* — and, the *gemara* adds, *shapir avad*, he was right not to respond, because the challenge ultimately fails.

### No Real Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשַׁפִּיר עֲבַד דְּלָא אַהְדַּר לִי. מַאי טַעְמָא? הָתָם לָא אַטְרְחֵיהּ לְבֵי דִינָא, הָכָא אַטְרְחֵיהּ לְבֵי דִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rava continues: **What is the reason** he did not answer me? The reason is that the cases cannot be compared. **There,** in the case of the mishna, the bailee paid at his own initiative. **He did not inconvenience** the owner by compelling him **to go to court.** Therefore, the owner transfers ownership of the deposit to the bailee. **Here,** in the case involving the jewels, the bailee **inconvenienced** the owner and compelled him **to go to court.** Consequently, the owner does not transfer ownership of the deposit to the bailee.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר רַב נַחְמָן דְּשׁוּמָא הָדַר? שָׁאנֵי הָתָם, דְּשׁוּמָא בְּטָעוּת הֲוָה, (דְּקָא) [דְּהָא] הֲוָה כִּיפֵּי מֵעִיקָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rav Naḥman holds that** after property is repossessed in order to pay an unpaid debt based on the court's **appraisal** of the article's value, it is **returned** if the debtor pays the debt? The Gemara rejects that conclusion: In general, the item is not returned. But **it is different there,** in the case of the jewels, **as it was an erroneous appraisal,** in **that the jewels were** in the possession of the bailee **from the outset** and he was merely unable to locate them.

The reason: *mai ta'ama hatam lo atrecha l'vei dina hacha atrecha l'vei dina* — in the case of the *mishna*, the *shomer* voluntarily agreed to pay, thereby expediting the process. Here, the *shomer* refused to pay, forcing *beis din* to intervene and seize the property. One who must be compelled by the court does not enjoy the benefit that flows from voluntary payment.

But the deeper answer goes further. The *gemara* distinguishes this case from a standard debt-payment scenario. Here, the seizure of the house rested on a false premise — *shuma b'ta'us hu d'hava kifi mei'ikara* — because the jewelry was never truly lost. The *shomer* never genuinely incurred the debt. The payment was therefore void from the outset, and once the jewelry surfaces there is no obstacle to restoring both items to their rightful owners. In a normal case of seized property in payment of an actual debt, the situation is entirely different — and that distinction opens the broader discussion that follows.

## Shuma Hadar

### Intro Shuma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי נְהַרְדָּעֵי: שׁוּמָא הָדַר עַד תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא. וְאָמַר אַמֵּימָר: אֲנָא מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא אֲנָא, וּסְבִירָא לִי שׁוּמָא הָדַר לְעוֹלָם. וְהִלְכְתָא: שׁוּמָא הָדַר לְעוֹלָם, מִשּׁוּם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וְעָשִׂיתָ הַיָּשָׁר וְהַטּוֹב״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the reversal of an appraisal, the Sages **of Neharde'a say:** After property is repossessed in order to pay an unpaid debt based on the court's **appraisal** of the article's value, it is **returned** to the debtor, provided he repays the debt from the time of the appraisal **until the twelve months** of **the year** have passed. **And Ameimar said: I am from Neharde'a, and** nevertheless, **I hold that** repossession based on **an appraisal** of an article's value can **always** be **returned.** If the debtor pays his debt, he can reclaim his property at any point. The Gemara rules: **And the *halakha*** is that repossession based on **an appraisal** can **always** be **returned, due to** the fact **that it is stated: "And you shall do that which is right and good"** (Deuteronomy 6:18). The owner of property appreciates his property more than another person would. Therefore, once the debtor repays his debt to the creditor, legal formalism should not prevent return of the debtor's property.

The *gemara* now introduces the doctrine of *shuma hadar* proper. In the standard case: Michael owes a thousand dollars, cannot produce cash, and the court assesses and seizes his property — say, a watch worth a thousand dollars — in payment of the debt. A month later Michael has the cash. Can he buy back the watch? This is the question of *shuma hadar*: when property is assessed and seized in payment of a debt, may the original debtor redeem it by later tendering the monetary amount?

### Nardaei vs Amemar

The scholars of Nardai rule: *shuma hadar treisar yarchei shata* — the debtor may redeem the property for twelve months. Amemar, himself from Nardai, goes further: *ana mi-Narda'ei ana v'sevira li shuma hadar le-olam* — in my opinion, the right of redemption is unlimited. This is, as noted, rather alarming from the creditor's perspective: I seize property in payment of a valid debt and now face the perpetual prospect of having it reclaimed.

### Halakha Shuma

The *halakha* follows Amemar: *ve-hilkhata shuma hadar le-olam* — assessed property can always be redeemed. The justification is explicitly non-legal: *mishum she-ne'emar ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — because of the verse commanding us to do what is right and what is good. Strictly speaking, the creditor is owed only money; property was seized only because cash was unavailable at the time. The right and good thing to do, once the debtor can produce the money, is to return the property and accept the payment that was always the true substance of the obligation. What is remarkable here is that an ethical principle — *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — is being translated into a legally enforceable norm. The spirit of the law becomes the letter of the law. One may note that this creates real complications for creditors who cannot freely develop or rely on seized assets, though it is true that any improvements made by the creditor would presumably have to be compensated by the debtor upon redemption.

### Chain Redemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא: שָׁמוּ לֵיהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב, וַאֲזַל אִיהוּ וְשָׁמַהּ לְבַעַל חוֹב דִּידֵיהּ, אָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: לָא עֲדִיף אַתְּ מִגַּבְרָא דַּאֲתֵית מִינֵּיהּ. זַבְּנַהּ, אוֹרְתַאּ, וְיַהֲבַהּ בְּמַתָּנָה, וַדַּאי הָנֵי מֵעִיקָּרָא אַדַּעְתָּא דְּאַרְעָא נְחוּת וְלָאו אַדַּעְתָּא דְּזוּזֵי נְחוּת.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara clarifies related matters. It is **obvious** that if the court **appraised** property to repay a debt to **a creditor, and** this creditor **went and** had the property **appraised** and repaid his debt **to his creditor, we say to** the second creditor: **Your** rights **are not superior** to those **of the man through whom you came** to possess the property. Just as the first debtor can repay the debt and reclaim his property from his creditor, he can also reclaim the property from the creditor of his creditor. If a creditor who received appraised land **sold it** or **bequeathed it** to his heirs or **gave it as a gift,** the debtor cannot reclaim the land from those who acquired their land. It is **certain** that **from the outset,** when **those** people acquired the land, it was **with the intent** to acquire **the land** itself that **they descended** to it, **and** it was **not with the intent** to receive money that **they descended** to the land.

The *gemara* spells out a significant corollary. If I seized Michael's property and then my own creditor — let us call her Atara — seized it from me in payment of my debt to her, can Michael still redeem it? The answer is yes: *lo adifat mi-gavra de-atit minei* — Atara is no better situated than I was; since I would have had to return it to Michael upon payment, so must she. The reasoning is consistent: Atara, like me, is a creditor who fundamentally sought money. If Michael can produce money, she has no moral claim to retain the land over its original owner's right of redemption.

### Exceptions Property

However, the right of redemption does not travel through all transactions. If I sold the seized property to Atara, gave it to her as a gift, or it passed to someone's heirs through inheritance, the right of redemption is extinguished. In those cases the recipient received the property *as land*, not as a surrogate for money — *ada'ata d'ara nachis v'lav ada'ata d'zuzei nachis* — the transaction was oriented toward the land itself, not toward the monetary claim underlying it. *Ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* requires the creditor to accept money in lieu of land; it does not require someone who received land as land — as a gift, a sale, or an inheritance — to yield it for money.

It should be noted that one obvious strategy would present itself to a sophisticated creditor: seize the property, immediately sell it to a third party, and then repurchase it from that party. At that stage it would presumably be shielded from redemption. Whether that maneuver would be pierced as a sham is not addressed here.

### Husband Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁמוּ לַהּ לְאִשָּׁה וְאִינְּסִיבָא, אוֹ שָׁמוּ מִינַּהּ דְּאִשָּׁה וְאִינְּסִיבָא וּמֵתָה בַּעַל בְּנִכְסֵי אִשְׁתּוֹ לוֹקֵחַ הָוֵי – לָא מַיהְדַּר וְלָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** If the court **appraised** property to repay a debt **to a woman and she** then **married, or** if the court **appraised** property **from a woman** to repay her debt **and she** then **married and died,** since the legal status of **a husband with regard to his wife's property is** that of **a buyer, he does not return** property that was appraised and repossessed to pay his wife's debt. **And we do not return to him** property that was repossessed from his wife if he pays her debt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: בְּאוּשָׁא הִתְקִינוּ – הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁמָּכְרָה בְּנִכְסֵי מְלוֹג בְּחַיֵּי בַּעְלָהּ וָמֵתָה – הַבַּעַל מוֹצִיא מִיַּד הַלָּקוֹחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: In Usha** the Sages **instituted** that in the case of **a woman who sold** part **of her usufruct property during the life of her husband and she died, the husband repossesses** the property **from the purchasers.** The property belongs to the wife, while the profits accrued after marriage belong to the husband. Therefore, the woman does not have the right to sell the property as long as they are married. If she sold the property and died, and her husband is her heir, the Sages instituted that his legal status is that of a buyer and not an heir. His rights to the land precede those of the subsequent buyers. He repossesses the land and reimburses them the sale price of the property.

A nuanced case is addressed next. Either the property was seized from a woman who then married and died, or the woman was owed the money and seized another's property and then married and died. In either configuration, the husband's status vis-à-vis the property is at issue. The ruling is: *ba'al b'nichsei ishto lokeach havei* — the husband is considered a purchaser with respect to his wife's property, not merely an heir. Therefore, *lo mahadrinan v'lo mahadri lei* — we do not compel the return of the property to him, and he cannot compel return of property to himself.

The source for the husband's status as purchaser is the ruling attributed to Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina in the name of the enactment of Usha: if a wife sells her *nichsei milug* — the property whose title she holds but whose usufruct belongs to her husband — during his lifetime, and she subsequently dies, the husband can recover the property from the purchasers. This makes sense only on the theory that the husband is treated as the primary purchaser of his wife's property, so that a subsequent sale by the wife to a third party is subject to the husband's preexisting claim. If he were merely her heir, he would have no stronger claim than any other heir of a vendor, and ordinarily heirs cannot recover property their ancestor sold. The relevance for our context: the husband, being a *lokeach* rather than an heir, stands in the same position as any purchaser, and the right of redemption does not run in his favor or against him.

### Voluntary Seizure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַגְבְּיֵהּ אִיהוּ בְּחוֹבוֹ, פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַב אַחָא וְרָבִינָא. חַד אָמַר: הָדְרָה, וְחַד אָמַר: לָא הָדְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to a case where the debtor, not the court, **authorized** his creditor to **repossess** the land **for his debt,** and now he seeks to pay his debt and reclaim it, **Rav Aḥa and Ravina disagree. One says:** If he pays the debt, the repossession based on the appraisal is **reversed** and he reclaims the land. **And one says:** The repossession based on the appraisal is **not reversed.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הָדְרָה סָבַר: הַאי זְבִינֵי מְעַלְּיָא הִיא, דְּהָא מִדַּעְתָּא דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ אַגְבְּיֵהּ. וּמַאן דְּאָמַר הָדְרָה סָבַר: לָא זְבִינֵי מְעַלְּיָא הוּא, וְהַאי דְּאַגְבְּיֵהּ מִדַּעְתֵּיהּ וְלָא אֲתָא לְדִינָא – מֵחֲמַת כִּיסּוּפָא הוּא דְּאַגְבְּיֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **The one who says** that the repossession based on the appraisal is **not reversed holds** that **this is a full-fledged sale, as he authorized the repossession at his own initiative.** Consequently, he cannot retract it. **And the one who says** that the repossession based on the appraisal is **reversed holds** that it **is not a full-fledged sale. And** the fact **that he authorized the repossession at his initiative and did not** wait to **come to** court for **a ruling** that his land be repossessed does not make it a full-fledged sale. It **was** only **due to** his desire to avoid **embarrassment that he authorized the repossession.**

The *gemara* raises one final variable. What if the debtor voluntarily surrendered the property — without court intervention, without a formal seizure process — saying, in effect, "I owe you a thousand dollars, here, take this land"? *Paligi Rav Acha v'Ravina* — Rav Acha and Ravina dispute the matter. One holds that even voluntary transfer allows redemption — *hadra*. The other holds it does not — *lo hadra* — because *hai zvini ma'alya hi*: since there was no coercion, what occurred was a legitimate sale. The debtor of his own free will exchanged his land for the extinguishment of his debt. There is no moral imbalance of the kind that *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* is designed to correct.

The view permitting redemption even in the voluntary case responds: *lo zvini ma'alya hu* — it was not truly a sale, because *machmat kisufa d'agbei* — the debtor surrendered out of embarrassment, not out of genuine willingness to sell. He preferred to hand over the property quietly rather than suffer the public indignity of a court-ordered seizure. Since the motivation was social pressure rather than a genuine commercial decision, the transaction is not treated as a true sale, and the right of redemption survives. The irony is pointed: by being cooperative and avoiding the courts, the debtor may actually forfeit a legal protection he would have enjoyed had he forced the creditor to go through official channels.

### Ownership Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵאֵימַת אָכֵיל פֵּירֵי? רַבָּה אָמַר: מִכִּי מָטְיָא אַדְרַכְתָּא לִידֵיהּ. אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies the *halakhot* of repossession: **And** when a creditor repossesses the debtor's land, **from when does he consume the produce** of that land? **Rabba said:** He consumes the produce **from** the time **when** the document of **authorization reaches his possession.** This is a document that authorizes him to repossess the property of the debtor in payment of the debt wherever that property is located. **Abaye said:** He need not wait until he receives that document. Rather, **the document's witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** the debtor's land **on his behalf.** From the moment they sign the document, the land is his.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִכִּי שְׁלִימוּ יְמֵי אַכְרַזְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** He consumes the produce **from** the time **when the days of proclamation conclude.** After property belonging to the debtor is located, the court proclaims that the property will be auctioned to raise funds to repay the debt. Therefore, even after the document of authorization reaches him, the creditor is not entitled to the produce, as someone else may purchase it. If the creditor enters the highest bid, he is entitled to the produce.

The *gemara* closes with a further dispute about when, precisely, the creditor acquires ownership of seized property — a question with practical implications for the right to collect the fruits of the property during the interim period. Rabbah holds: *miki mati adrakhta l'yadei* — from the moment the court writ reaches the creditor's hand, he owns the property. Abaye holds: *b'chasimaso* — from the moment the document is signed by the court, even before it is physically delivered. Rava holds: *miki shlimu yemei akhrazta* — only once the full public announcement and auction period has concluded, since during that period a higher bidder might appear, redirect the cash proceeds to the creditor, and take the property. Until that possibility is foreclosed, the creditor's title is not yet perfected. After that period, however — and notwithstanding the doctrine of *shuma hadar* — ownership formally vests.
