# Bava Metzia 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer's Oath

### Rav Huna Rule

The daf begins approximately twenty lines down on 34b, at the words *amar Rav Huna*. The context is the *mishna*'s ruling that a *shomer* who agrees to pay — and in doing so obtains the right to collect *kefal* if the thief is later found — has been incentivized to move things along and ensure the owner receives his principal. The *gemara* has been exploring the various dimensions of that arrangement. Now Rav Huna introduces a further requirement: even when the *shomer* agrees to pay, we still administer an oath. As the *gemara* states, *amar Rav Huna mashvinan lei shvua she'eino birshuso* — we make him swear that the object is not in his possession.

### Shomer's Covetousness

The reason is immediate: *chashinan shema einav nasan bah* — we are concerned that he has set his eye upon the object, that he covets it. It is entirely possible, in other words, that the *shomer* is agreeing to pay precisely because he wants to keep the object for himself. By paying, he silences the owner's claim while quietly retaining the deposit. Rav Huna therefore rules that no *shomer* — not even one who volunteers to pay — is ever fully exempt from swearing that the *pikadon* is genuinely not in his *reshus*. This principle will be tested and challenged at length in the sections that follow.

## Mashkon Cases

### Mashkon Setup

The *gemara* now pivots to a *braisa* that will serve as a challenge to Rav Huna's position. The case involves a *malveh* — a lender — who holds a *mashkon*, a piece of collateral, against a loan he has extended to a *loveh*. The *mashkon* is then lost. Since the *malveh* functions as a *shomer* over the collateral, its loss falls upon him, and its value is to be deducted from the outstanding loan. The question that arises is: how much of the loan remains after that deduction? And on that question, lender and borrower disagree.

### Four Scenarios

The *braisa* sets out four scenarios, turning on the relative values of the loan and the *mashkon*. In the first sub-case of the opening section: I, the *malveh*, lent a hundred and claim the *mashkon* was worth fifty — so the *loveh* still owes me fifty. The *loveh* says it was worth a hundred, so he owes me nothing. He is *kofer hakol* — denying the entire claim — and is therefore *patur*, exempt from an oath. In the second sub-case: I say it was worth fifty so he owes me fifty; he says it was worth seventy-five so he only owes me twenty-five. That is *modeh b'miktzas* — a partial admission — and he is *chayav*, obligated to take an oath.

The *braisa* then turns to cases where the *mashkon* exceeded the loan. The *loveh* claims the collateral was worth two hundred against a loan of one hundred, so I owe him one hundred. I the *malveh* say it was worth only one hundred — we are even, I owe him nothing. I am *kofer hakol* and exempt. In the parallel case: the *loveh* says it was worth two hundred, I owe him one hundred; I say it was worth one hundred and twenty-five, so I owe him only twenty-five. That is again *modeh b'miktzas* and I am obligated.

The *chiddush* of the *braisa* is its ruling on *who* takes the oath in the *modeh b'miktzas* situations. Ordinarily, it is the party who is making a partial admission — the *loveh* in the first pair, the *malveh* in the second — who swears. The *braisa*, however, rules: *mi shehapikadon etzlo* — the one in whose possession the *pikadon* rested, that is, always the *malveh*, is the one who takes the oath.

### Malveh Swears

Why should the *malveh* always be the one to swear, even in cases where the *loveh* is the *modeh b'miktzas*? The *braisa*'s reasoning: *shema yishba zeh v'yotzi halah et hapikadon* — perhaps the *loveh* will take his oath regarding the value, and then the *malveh* will produce the *mashkon*, rendering that oath either false or moot. If the *loveh* swears that the *mashkon* was worth seventy-five and the *malveh* subsequently produces it and it turns out to be worth only fifty, the *loveh* has now taken a *shvuas sheker*.

Tosfos clarifies that the concern is not primarily for the *loveh*'s personal dishonor — if he swore falsely, one might say he deserves the consequences. The real problem is that it makes the *beis din* look as though it imposed an unnecessary oath: the court compelled the *loveh* to swear about the value of an object the *malveh* had concealed all along. The court process itself is discredited. Therefore, in all such cases, the *malveh* — the party holding the *pikadon* — takes the oath regarding value, because he will not produce the object and prove himself a liar.

### Challenge Rav Huna

The *gemara* now turns to challenge Rav Huna directly. The challenge runs as follows: according to Rav Huna, in every case where the *malveh* claims the *mashkon* was lost, he must swear *she'eino birshuso* — that it is not in his possession — before any further dispute about value proceeds. If that is so, then in all four of the *braisa*'s scenarios, the *malveh* has already sworn he does not have the *mashkon*. Why, then, is there any concern that he will later produce it? Having taken that oath, he cannot produce it without convicting himself of perjury. The entire rationale for taking the oath away from the *loveh* and placing it on the *malveh* collapses. The *braisa*'s distinctive ruling — *mi shehapikadon etzlo* — presupposes that the production of the *mashkon* remains a live threat. But Rav Huna has apparently neutralized that threat.

The *gemara* considers and rejects two intermediate answers. Rava first suggests: perhaps the case is one where witnesses testify the *mashkon* was burned — the *malveh* therefore never had to take Rav Huna's oath in the first place, yet we still worry he might produce something. But the *gemara* pushes back: *meheika meisi lei* — from where would he produce burned goods? Rav Yosef then suggests: witnesses testified it was stolen, so again Rav Huna's oath was not required. But the same objection applies: if witnesses saw it stolen, how will the *malveh* produce it? The *gemara* answers: *de-tirach u-maisi lei* — he could expend effort, investigate who stole it, track down the thief, and retrieve the object. The counterargument is then raised: if so, why isn't the same concern applicable once the *malveh* himself takes the oath? The answer is asymmetric knowledge — *bishlama malveh yodeia man ka'ayil v'nafik b'veitei* — the *malveh* knows who has been in and out of his home and can therefore pursue the recovery of the stolen object. The *loveh*, by contrast, has no such knowledge about the *malveh*'s household and cannot similarly investigate.

One might add — and it is a point the *gemara* itself does not make explicitly — that the *malveh* has a strong financial incentive to find the *mashkon*: having already credited its value against the loan, he would be recovering a real economic loss if he retrieves it.

### Rava's Answer

A cleaner resolution is offered by Rava: *zeh nishba v'zeh nishba* — both parties swear. The *braisa* is not saying that the oath moves entirely from the *loveh* to the *malveh*. Rather, it is describing the sequence: the *malveh* first swears *she'eino b'reshuso*, consistent with Rav Huna's requirement, and only then does the *loveh* proceed to take the classic *shvuas modeh b'miktzas* regarding the value. *Mah nishba techila* — who swears first? — *malveh nishba techila*, the *malveh* swears first that it is not in his possession. Once that has been established and the concern of production has been neutralized, the *loveh* makes his own oath. This is admittedly not the plain reading of the *braisa*, but it preserves Rav Huna's position.

### Defense Rav Huna

Rav Huna bar Tachalifa, citing Rava, then identifies a sharper challenge to Rav Huna from the opening sub-case of the second pair in the *braisa* — the *reisha d'seifa*. There, the *loveh* says the *mashkon* was worth two hundred, the *malveh* says it was worth only one hundred, so the *malveh* is *kofer hakol* and *patur*. According to Rav Huna, however, the *malveh* must always swear *eino birshuso*. Once he is making that oath, the *beis din* may apply *gilgul shvua* — the principle that an existing oath can be extended to cover related matters — and require him to swear as well about the value. That would make him *chayav* on the question of value, not *patur*. Yet the *braisa* simply says *patur*. This is a genuine *tiyufta* — a refutation — of Rav Huna.

### Rav Ashi Explains

Rav Ashi reports that he presented this entire discussion before Rav Kahana, who answered: *tehei b'ma'amino* — the case in the *braisa* is one where the *loveh* declared that he trusts the *malveh*. The *loveh* waived his right to compel the *malveh* to swear *eino birshuso*; he simply believed him and released him from that obligation. Since the *malveh* never takes the initial oath, there is no *gilgul shvua* to attach the value-question onto, and he remains *patur*. This is openly acknowledged as a forced reading — *ukimta* — but it is the available defense of Rav Huna's position.

### Trust Asymmetry

The *gemara* immediately presses the inconsistency: if the *loveh* trusts the *malveh* enough to waive the oath about possession — thereby sacrificing the advantage of *gilgul shvua* — why doesn't the *malveh* reciprocate and trust the *loveh*'s assessment of the *mashkon*'s value? After all, it was the *loveh*'s object; he knows better than the *malveh* what it was worth. Why does the *malveh* refuse to accept the *loveh*'s claim of two hundred? The *gemara* answers: *kim lei b'gavei* — the *loveh* trusts the *malveh* because the *malveh* is the one who actually held and handled the *mashkon* and knows what he received. But regarding value, the *loveh* believes his own knowledge of his object exceeds the *malveh*'s; he is not willing to accept the *malveh*'s lower valuation. The *malveh*, for his part, does not return the trust.

### Wealth Theology

The *gemara* explains the asymmetry with a striking theological aside. The *loveh* applies to the *malveh* the verse *tumat yesharim tanchem* — the integrity of the upright guides them — reasoning that since the *malveh* is prosperous and able to lend money, he must be a righteous man whose word is reliable. The *malveh*, in turn, applies to the *loveh* the verse *ve-selef bogdim yeshaded* — the perverseness of the treacherous destroys them — reasoning that since the *loveh* is in the position of having to borrow, he is presumably less reliable.

This is a genuinely striking *gemara*. The underlying theology — that wealth is evidence of divine favor and therefore of moral trustworthiness, while poverty signals the reverse — is, as one might note, in significant tension with much of the Torah's own treatment of the poor, which accepts economic variation as a social reality and enjoins compassion toward the impoverished. Even the biblical framework of *sechar v'onesh* speaks in aggregate and communal terms rather than translating individual prosperity directly into individual righteousness. That said, there are certainly biblical precedents — Yitzchak's hundredfold return, Avimelech's recognition that God has blessed him — where prosperity is read as divine approbation. The *gemara* here pushes that sensibility to an uncomfortable extreme in service of explaining a legal asymmetry in who trusts whom.

## Lost Jewelry Case

### Seizing House

The *gemara* now shifts to a narrative case involving the doctrine of *shuma hadar*. A certain man deposited jewelry with his friend. The friend was asked to return the jewelry and said, *lo yadana heicha otveinhu* — I do not know where I put it. It was not a dramatic loss but a misplacement. The case came before Rav Nachman, who ruled: *kol lo yadana peshiuta hi* — any "I don't know where it is" constitutes negligence. He therefore ordered the *shomer* to pay — *zil shalim*. When the *shomer* failed to do so, Rav Nachman seized his house — *agbei l'apadnei minei* — in payment of the debt.

### Found Item

Subsequently, the jewelry turned up — *ishtakach kifi* — and its value had increased. Rav Nachman then ruled: *hadar kifi l'mareihu v'hadar apadna l'marei* — the jewelry goes back to its owner and the house goes back to its owner.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava reports that he was sitting before Rav Nachman at the time, and they were studying the very *perek* of *Hamafkid*. He raised a challenge from the *mishna*: when a *shomer* pays and thereby acquires the object, he is entitled to any subsequent increase in value — just as he acquires the right to *kefal* if the thief is found. Here, the house was seized as payment; the *shomer* has effectively purchased the jewelry through that payment. The jewelry subsequently increased in value. On what basis do we reverse the transaction and restore both items to their original owners? Rava notes that Rav Nachman did not respond to this challenge — *v'lo hadar li* — and, the *gemara* adds, *shapir avad*, he was right not to respond, because the challenge ultimately fails.

### No Real Debt

The reason: *mai ta'ama hatam lo atrecha l'vei dina hacha atrecha l'vei dina* — in the case of the *mishna*, the *shomer* voluntarily agreed to pay, thereby expediting the process. Here, the *shomer* refused to pay, forcing *beis din* to intervene and seize the property. One who must be compelled by the court does not enjoy the benefit that flows from voluntary payment.

But the deeper answer goes further. The *gemara* distinguishes this case from a standard debt-payment scenario. Here, the seizure of the house rested on a false premise — *shuma b'ta'us hu d'hava kifi mei'ikara* — because the jewelry was never truly lost. The *shomer* never genuinely incurred the debt. The payment was therefore void from the outset, and once the jewelry surfaces there is no obstacle to restoring both items to their rightful owners. In a normal case of seized property in payment of an actual debt, the situation is entirely different — and that distinction opens the broader discussion that follows.

## Shuma Hadar

### Intro Shuma

The *gemara* now introduces the doctrine of *shuma hadar* proper. In the standard case: Michael owes a thousand dollars, cannot produce cash, and the court assesses and seizes his property — say, a watch worth a thousand dollars — in payment of the debt. A month later Michael has the cash. Can he buy back the watch? This is the question of *shuma hadar*: when property is assessed and seized in payment of a debt, may the original debtor redeem it by later tendering the monetary amount?

### Nardaei vs Amemar

The scholars of Nardai rule: *shuma hadar treisar yarchei shata* — the debtor may redeem the property for twelve months. Amemar, himself from Nardai, goes further: *ana mi-Narda'ei ana v'sevira li shuma hadar le-olam* — in my opinion, the right of redemption is unlimited. This is, as noted, rather alarming from the creditor's perspective: I seize property in payment of a valid debt and now face the perpetual prospect of having it reclaimed.

### Halakha Shuma

The *halakha* follows Amemar: *ve-hilkhata shuma hadar le-olam* — assessed property can always be redeemed. The justification is explicitly non-legal: *mishum she-ne'emar ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — because of the verse commanding us to do what is right and what is good. Strictly speaking, the creditor is owed only money; property was seized only because cash was unavailable at the time. The right and good thing to do, once the debtor can produce the money, is to return the property and accept the payment that was always the true substance of the obligation. What is remarkable here is that an ethical principle — *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* — is being translated into a legally enforceable norm. The spirit of the law becomes the letter of the law. One may note that this creates real complications for creditors who cannot freely develop or rely on seized assets, though it is true that any improvements made by the creditor would presumably have to be compensated by the debtor upon redemption.

### Chain Redemption

The *gemara* spells out a significant corollary. If I seized Michael's property and then my own creditor — let us call her Atara — seized it from me in payment of my debt to her, can Michael still redeem it? The answer is yes: *lo adifat mi-gavra de-atit minei* — Atara is no better situated than I was; since I would have had to return it to Michael upon payment, so must she. The reasoning is consistent: Atara, like me, is a creditor who fundamentally sought money. If Michael can produce money, she has no moral claim to retain the land over its original owner's right of redemption.

### Exceptions Property

However, the right of redemption does not travel through all transactions. If I sold the seized property to Atara, gave it to her as a gift, or it passed to someone's heirs through inheritance, the right of redemption is extinguished. In those cases the recipient received the property *as land*, not as a surrogate for money — *ada'ata d'ara nachis v'lav ada'ata d'zuzei nachis* — the transaction was oriented toward the land itself, not toward the monetary claim underlying it. *Ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* requires the creditor to accept money in lieu of land; it does not require someone who received land as land — as a gift, a sale, or an inheritance — to yield it for money.

It should be noted that one obvious strategy would present itself to a sophisticated creditor: seize the property, immediately sell it to a third party, and then repurchase it from that party. At that stage it would presumably be shielded from redemption. Whether that maneuver would be pierced as a sham is not addressed here.

### Husband Status

A nuanced case is addressed next. Either the property was seized from a woman who then married and died, or the woman was owed the money and seized another's property and then married and died. In either configuration, the husband's status vis-à-vis the property is at issue. The ruling is: *ba'al b'nichsei ishto lokeach havei* — the husband is considered a purchaser with respect to his wife's property, not merely an heir. Therefore, *lo mahadrinan v'lo mahadri lei* — we do not compel the return of the property to him, and he cannot compel return of property to himself.

The source for the husband's status as purchaser is the ruling attributed to Rabbi Yosi berabbi Chanina in the name of the enactment of Usha: if a wife sells her *nichsei milug* — the property whose title she holds but whose usufruct belongs to her husband — during his lifetime, and she subsequently dies, the husband can recover the property from the purchasers. This makes sense only on the theory that the husband is treated as the primary purchaser of his wife's property, so that a subsequent sale by the wife to a third party is subject to the husband's preexisting claim. If he were merely her heir, he would have no stronger claim than any other heir of a vendor, and ordinarily heirs cannot recover property their ancestor sold. The relevance for our context: the husband, being a *lokeach* rather than an heir, stands in the same position as any purchaser, and the right of redemption does not run in his favor or against him.

### Voluntary Seizure

The *gemara* raises one final variable. What if the debtor voluntarily surrendered the property — without court intervention, without a formal seizure process — saying, in effect, "I owe you a thousand dollars, here, take this land"? *Paligi Rav Acha v'Ravina* — Rav Acha and Ravina dispute the matter. One holds that even voluntary transfer allows redemption — *hadra*. The other holds it does not — *lo hadra* — because *hai zvini ma'alya hi*: since there was no coercion, what occurred was a legitimate sale. The debtor of his own free will exchanged his land for the extinguishment of his debt. There is no moral imbalance of the kind that *ve-asita ha-yashar ve-ha-tov* is designed to correct.

The view permitting redemption even in the voluntary case responds: *lo zvini ma'alya hu* — it was not truly a sale, because *machmat kisufa d'agbei* — the debtor surrendered out of embarrassment, not out of genuine willingness to sell. He preferred to hand over the property quietly rather than suffer the public indignity of a court-ordered seizure. Since the motivation was social pressure rather than a genuine commercial decision, the transaction is not treated as a true sale, and the right of redemption survives. The irony is pointed: by being cooperative and avoiding the courts, the debtor may actually forfeit a legal protection he would have enjoyed had he forced the creditor to go through official channels.

### Ownership Timing

The *gemara* closes with a further dispute about when, precisely, the creditor acquires ownership of seized property — a question with practical implications for the right to collect the fruits of the property during the interim period. Rabbah holds: *miki mati adrakhta l'yadei* — from the moment the court writ reaches the creditor's hand, he owns the property. Abaye holds: *b'chasimaso* — from the moment the document is signed by the court, even before it is physically delivered. Rava holds: *miki shlimu yemei akhrazta* — only once the full public announcement and auction period has concluded, since during that period a higher bidder might appear, redirect the cash proceeds to the creditor, and take the property. Until that possibility is foreclosed, the creditor's title is not yet perfected. After that period, however — and notwithstanding the doctrine of *shuma hadar* — ownership formally vests.
