# Shabbat 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavod Shabbat & Clothes

### Clothes & Kavod

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִנַּיִין לְשִׁנּוּי בְּגָדִים מִן הַתּוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּפָשַׁט אֶת בְּגָדָיו וְלָבַשׁ בְּגָדִים אֲחֵרִים״, וְתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, לִימְּדָה תּוֹרָה דֶּרֶךְ אֶרֶץ: בְּגָדִים שֶׁבִּישֵּׁל בָּהֶן קְדֵירָה לְרַבּוֹ — אַל יִמְזוֹג בָּהֶן כּוֹס לְרַבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Aḥa bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: From where** is it derived **that changing clothes** is a display of honor? **As it is stated: "And he will remove his garments and will don other garments,** and he will bring the ashes outside of the camp to a pure location" (Leviticus 6:4). **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The Torah taught you etiquette. The clothes in which one prepared food for his master, one does not** wear **to pour his master wine.** Since cooking makes one's clothes dirty, he should wear fresh clothes when serving his master.

The daf picks up at the bottom of 113b, continuing a discussion that arose from a *mishnah* dealing with folding clothes and various *issurim*, which provided an occasion to focus on the concept of *kavod Shabbat* — the honor due to the Sabbath — and the role that clothing plays in expressing that honor. Because the discussion has centered on *kavod* and the idea that clothing itself confers honor, the *Gemara* now turns to a *derasha* on a verse that uses the word *kevodo*.

### Sancheriv Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְתַחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ יֵקַד יְקוֹד כִּיקוֹד אֵשׁ״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״וְתַחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ״, וְלֹא כְּבוֹדוֹ מַמָּשׁ. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן קָרֵי לְמָאנֵיהּ ״מְכַבְּדוֹתַי״. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: ״וְתַחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ״ — תַּחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ מַמָּשׁ. רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר: ״תַּחַת כְּבוֹדוֹ״ — כִּשְׂרֵיפַת בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן. מַה לְּהַלָּן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים, אַף כָּאן שְׂרֵיפַת נְשָׁמָה וְגוּף קַיָּים.
>
> **Translation:** It was mentioned earlier that Rabbi Yoḥanan called his clothing his honor. The Gemara cites the interpretation of the verse that speaks about the downfall of the king of Assyria: "Therefore, the Lord, the Lord of hosts, will send leanness to his fat ones **and under his honor He will burn a burning like a burning fire"** (Isaiah 10:16). **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: "And under his honor," but not his actual honor.** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yoḥanan** follows **his own reasoning,** for he **called his clothing my honor,** which means that the bodies of the king of Assyria's soldiers were burned. However, their garments were miraculously not burned. **Rabbi Elazar said: "And under his honor"** means **in place of his actual honor.** That is to say, their bodies were burned. Since, in Rabbi Elazar's opinion, the word under means in the place of, the verse accordingly means that in the place of his honor, i.e., the body, there remain ashes. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said: Under his honor** means beneath his flesh, **similar to the burning of the sons of Aaron. Just as there,** i.e., the burning of Aaron's sons, **the soul burned while** the **body** remained **intact, so too here,** i.e., the burning of Assyrian soldiers, **the soul burned while** the **body** remained **intact.**

The verse in question is from Isaiah: *v'tachat kevodo yikad yikod kikod esh* — "and beneath his glory shall be kindled a burning like the burning of fire." The subject of the verse is Sancheriv. Three *Tannaim* offer competing interpretations of the phrase *tachat kevodo* — "beneath his glory."

Rabbi Yochanan reads *v'tachat kevodo v'lo kevodo mamash*: beneath the *kavod*, not the *kavod* itself. Rashi explains that this means the bodies of Sancheriv's army burned while the clothes did not. This reading coheres with Rabbi Yochanan's known perspective, cited from the previous discussion: *kari l'manei mechabdosai* — Rabbi Yochanan refers to his clothes as "the things that honor me." It is therefore natural that he would read *kevodo* as a reference to garments, and understand the verse to mean that the bodies burned beneath the clothing while the clothing itself was preserved.

Rabbi Elazar reads *v'tachat kevodo tachat kevodo mamash* — beneath his glory, literally meaning beneath the person himself. This raises an interpretive question: what does it mean to have a burning beneath the person himself, within the person? Rabbi Elazar understands it to mean that the souls were destroyed while the bodies remained.

Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani offers a third reading, drawing an analogy to the death of Aharon's sons: *k'sereifat bnei Aharon, mah lehalan sereifat neshama v'guf kayam af kan sereifat neshama v'guf kayam* — just as there the soul was consumed and the body remained intact, so too here the soul was destroyed and the body remained. On his reading, *kevodo* refers to the body, and *tachat* — within or beneath — indicates that only what is internal, the soul, was destroyed.

All three *drashot* connect to the theme of *kavod* and clothing, but it is Rabbi Yochanan's reading that ties most directly to the *kavod Shabbat* discussion: clothing as the bearer of *kavod*.

### Priestly Garments

The *Gemara* now returns explicitly to the question of changing clothes and seeks a biblical basis for the practice. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: from where do we derive *shinuy begadim*, the changing of clothes, from the Torah? The proof comes from the priestly service of removing the ashes in the *Beit HaMikdash*: *u'pashat begadav v'lavash begadim acherim* — the *kohen* removes his garments and puts on other garments to carry out the ashes. The Torah itself thus mandates changing clothes for different functions, providing the biblical model for dressing differently in honor of *Shabbat*.

### Derech Eretz

The *tana d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* adds a broader lesson: *limidatcha Torah derech eretz* — the Torah is teaching you proper conduct. The illustration given is: *begadim shebishal bahem kederah l'rabo al yimzog bahem kos l'rabo* — the clothes one uses to cook the pot should not be the clothes one uses to serve the cup to one's master. The point is not that some clothes are inherently superior to others, but that clothes should be appropriate to the occasion. One wears an apron while cooking and presents oneself differently when serving or attending dinner. Applied to *Shabbat*: to show *kavod* for the day, one dresses in a manner that is dignified and befitting the occasion.

## Torah Scholars

### Patched Shoes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: גְּנַאי הוּא לְתַלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁיֵּצֵא בְּמִנְעָלִים הַמְטוּלָּאִים לַשּׁוּק. וְהָא רַבִּי אַחָא בַּר חֲנִינָא נָפֵיק! אָמַר רַבִּי אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן: בִּטְלַאי עַל גַּב טְלַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: It is disgraceful for a Torah scholar to go out to the marketplace in patched shoes.** The Gemara asks: **Didn't Rabbi Aḥa bar Ḥanina go out** in patched shoes? **Rabbi Aḥa, son of Rav Naḥman, said:** They only prohibited patched shoes **if there is a patch upon a patch.**

The *Gemara* now shifts from changing clothes for *Shabbat* to a broader concern: how a Torah scholar appears in public regardless of the occasion. Rabbi Chiya bar Abba says in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *gnai hu l'talmid chacham sheyeitzei b'minalim hemetulaim lashuk* — it is a disgrace for a Torah scholar to go out to the marketplace wearing shoes with patches on them. The concern is not about wearing expensive shoes but about appearing in a way that reflects poorly on the Torah he represents.

### Extreme Poverty

The *Gemara* immediately raises an objection: but Rabbi Chiya bar Chanina went out with patched shoes. The response given is: *betlai al gabi tlai* — the problem is only when patches have patches, when someone is so destitute that his patches themselves require patches. This acknowledges the reality of extreme poverty: one cannot simply be told to buy presentable shoes when the means are unavailable. The *Gemara* will return later to the community's obligation to support Torah scholars, but at this stage it simply notes that when poverty is truly extreme, the standard cannot be applied in the same way.

This raises a deeper tension that runs throughout this section. We live in a world where people form judgments based on external appearances, and how one presents oneself affects how others perceive what one represents. There is an acknowledged problem with this — the value of a person lies within, as the verse says: *haadam yireh laeinayim v'Hashem yireh lalevav* — a person sees the eyes, but God sees the heart. Yet the *Gemara* is operating in the real world, where accommodating social norms has consequences for the public perception of Torah.

### Stains & Grease

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כׇּל תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁנִּמְצָא רְבָב עַל בִּגְדּוֹ — חַיָּיב מִיתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל מְשַׂנְאַי אָהֲבוּ מָוֶת״ — אַל תִּקְרֵי ״מְשַׂנְאַי״, אֶלָּא ״מַשְׂנִיאַי״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: A Torah scholar on whose clothes a fat stain is found is liable** to receive the **death** penalty, **as it is stated: "All those who hate me love death"** (Proverbs 8:36), and the Sages said: **Do not read: Those who hate me [*mesanai*]. Rather,** read: **Those who cause me to be hated [*masniai*].** Those who cause people to hate the Torah by creating the impression that those who study Torah are unclean deserve the death penalty.

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba continues in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol talmid chacham shenimtsa revav al bigdo chayav mita* — any Torah scholar found with a grease stain on his garment is deserving of death. The language is harsh. The proof text cited is *kol mesaneay ahavu mavet* — "all who hate me love death" — read with a *derashah*: *al tikrei mesaneay ela masniey*, do not read "those who hate me" but "those who cause others to hate me." If a Torah scholar dresses in a way that repels people and causes them to form a disgusted impression of Torah, he has committed a serious wrong against the Torah's standing in the world.

### Revad vs Revav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבִינָא אָמַר: רְבָד אִיתְּמַר. וְלָא פְּלִיגִי: הָא בִּגְלִימָא, הָא בִּלְבוּשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said:** A fat stain [*revav*] was not stated, but rather **a bloodstain [*revad*] was stated** (Rabbeinu Ḥananel), which is a greater disgrace. The Gemara adds: **They did not disagree** over the *halakha*. Rather, the dispute is whether **that** which we learned concerning stains on a Torah scholar's clothes refers **to an overgarment** that people wear over the rest of their clothes, while **that** which we learned with regard to a bloodstain refers **to an undergarment,** where a bloodstain is disgraceful but other types of spots are not.

Ravina qualifies the statement: *revad itmar*, not *revav* — it was said with *revad*, not *revav*. According to Rashi, *revad* refers to something even more objectionable than a grease stain. And the two cases are not in conflict: *ha biglimi ha bilvushi* — one refers to the outer garment and the other to the inner garment. A grease stain on the inner garment, which is not visible, would be tolerable; on the outer garment, even that would not be acceptable. The distinction thus reconciles what might otherwise appear to be a contradiction between the two formulations.

### Isaiah Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״כַּאֲשֶׁר הָלַךְ עַבְדִּי יְשַׁעְיָהוּ עָרוֹם וְיָחֵף״. ״עָרוֹם״ — בִּבְגָדִים בְּלוּאִים, ״וְיָחֵף״ — בְּמִנְעָלִים הַמְטוּלָּאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** similarly, **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That which is written: "As My servant Isaiah went naked and barefoot** for three years" (Isaiah 20:3), is not to be understood literally. Rather, **naked** means that he wore **tattered clothing, and barefoot** means that he walked **with patched shoes.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba continues in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, citing the verse about Isaiah: *ka'asher halach avdi Yeshayahu arom veyachef* — "as my servant Isaiah walked naked and barefoot." The *Gemara* explains that *arom* does not mean literally naked, but rather *b'vegadim beluim* — wearing garments in tatters. And *yachef*, barefoot, is interpreted as wearing *minalim hametulaim* — patched-up shoes. The verse thus underscores the point made earlier: that wearing tattered clothing or visibly worn shoes is perceived as essentially equivalent to not wearing clothes at all.

### Saddle Stains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: רְבָב עַל הַמַּרְדַּעַת חוֹצֵץ. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: עַד כְּאִיסָּר הָאִיטַלְקִי. וְעַל הַבְּגָדִים, מִצַּד אֶחָד — אֵינוֹ חוֹצֵץ, מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין — חוֹצֵץ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אַף מִצַּד אֶחָד חוֹצֵץ.
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in a mishna **there: A fat stain on** a donkey's **saddle interposes, i.e.,** if the saddle becomes impure, its immersion in a ritual bath is invalid unless the stain is removed. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** A stain interposes only when it is as large **as an Italian *issar*** but not smaller. **And** if there were a fat stain **on clothing on one side, it does not interpose,** but if the stain is **on both sides it interposes. Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: It interposes even on one side.**

The *Gemara* then cites a *mishnah* from elsewhere: *revav al hamarda* — a grease stain on a saddle — constitutes a *chatzitza*, an interposition, for the purposes of *tevila* to render it *tahor*. The relevance here is that the only thing that constitutes a *chatzitza* is something that one is *makpid* about — that one objects to. The fact that a grease stain on a saddle is a *chatzitza* therefore demonstrates that people are genuinely particular about such stains on their equipment and garments. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel qualifies: only if the stain is the size of an *isar italki* or larger does it constitute a problem.

The *mishnah* continues: if a grease stain is on only one side of a garment, *eino chotzetz* — it is not a *chatzitza*; if it has soaked through to both sides, *chotzetz* — it is. Rabbi Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: even one side constitutes a *chatzitza*.

### Saddle Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מֵרַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מַרְדַּעַת, מִצַּד אֶחָד אוֹ מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זוֹ לֹא שָׁמַעְתִּי, כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּהּ שָׁמַעְתִּי. דִּתְנַן רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: שֶׁל בַּנָּאִין — מִצַּד אֶחָד, וְשֶׁל בּוּר — מִשְּׁנֵי צְדָדִין. וְלֹא תְּהֵא מַרְדַּעַת חֲשׁוּבָה מִבִּגְדּוֹ שֶׁל עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish raised a dilemma before Rabbi Ḥanina:** Is a stain on a **saddle** an interposition even if it is **on just one side, or** only if it is **on both sides?** Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: That, I did not hear** explicitly. However, **I heard** something **similar to it. As we learned** in that same mishna that **Rabbi Yosei says:** A garment belonging to **builders [*banna'in*]** is considered to have an interposition if it has a stain **on one side, and that of an ignoramus,** who is not meticulous, is considered to have an interposition when the stain is **on both sides. And** certainly a donkey's **saddle cannot be more important than the garment of an *am ha'aretz*.**

Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish posed a question to Rabbi Chanina: regarding the saddle — was the *chatzitza* of a stain on a saddle assumed to be on one side only, or must it go through to both sides? A stain heavy enough to soak through an entire saddle would have to be substantial indeed, though perhaps the discussion refers to a saddle blanket rather than the saddle itself.

Rabbi Chanina responds: *zu lo shamati* — I have not heard an explicit tradition on this point. But *k'yotzei ba shamati* — I have heard a similar ruling. From the *mishnah*: Rabbi Yossi says that for *benai'im* — literally "builders" — one side is already a problem, while for an *am ha'aretz* it is only a problem from two sides. This comparison provides an analogical answer: if an *am ha'aretz*'s clothing is only a problem when the stain goes through both sides, then a saddle — which surely should not be treated more stringently than the clothes of a common person — would also require the stain to go through both sides. The question of why the *Gemara* needed to invoke Rabbi Yossi's nuanced ruling rather than simply using the *tanna kamma*'s position for ordinary clothes is noted, but the practical conclusion stands.

## Talmid Chacham

### Who is Talmid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בַּנָּאִים״? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵלּוּ תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁעוֹסְקִין בְּבִנְיָנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם כׇּל יְמֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** are the **builders** mentioned here? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: These are Torah scholars, who are engaged in building the world all of their days.**

Having invoked the category of *benai'im*, the *Gemara* now asks: *mai banayim* — who are these "builders"?

### Builders Derasha

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *elu talmidei chachamim she'oskin bevinyano shel olam kol yemeihem* — these are Torah scholars who are engaged in building the world for all their days. The term *benai'im*, builders, is thus read as a reference to those who build the world through Torah, echoing the well-known formulation *al tikrei banayich ela bonayich*. While from an outside perspective it might appear that those who sit and learn are removed from practical contribution, the *Gemara* asserts the opposite: they are the true builders of civilization.

This reading also explains why the standard for Torah scholars regarding clothing stains is stricter than for ordinary people: they represent something of the highest public significance, and therefore the standards of appearance that apply to them are correspondingly heightened.

### Lost Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁמַּחְזִירִין לוֹ אֲבֵידָה בִּטְבִיעוּת הָעַיִן — זֶה הַמַּקְפִּיד עַל חֲלוּקוֹ לְהוֹפְכוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to this, **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who is a Torah scholar** of whom the Sages said **that one** must **return a lost object to him based on visual identification,** even if he does not provide an identifying sign for it? **That is one who makes sure his upper undergarment is turned inward** so that the uneven stitching is not visible. This means that he conducts himself like a Torah scholar in all his ways, even in matters of cleanliness and order (Maharsha).

Rabbi Yochanan now addresses the question of who qualifies as a *talmid chacham* for the purpose of *hashavat aveidah*. The rule is that a *talmid chacham* whose integrity can be trusted may have a lost object returned to him on the basis of *tevi'at ha'ayin* — recognition alone — even without being able to provide a specific identifying *siman*. Ordinarily this would be insufficient, since anyone could claim to recognize something.

### Appearance & Integrity

The *Gemara* defines who merits this level of trust: *ze hamakpid al chaluko lehofcho* — one who is so careful about his personal presentation that if he accidentally puts on his garment inside out, he will take the time to remove it and put it on properly.

This is a striking criterion. The *Gemara* seems to assume a connection between meticulous care about one's personal appearance and the kind of integrity that would make one trustworthy in matters of monetary honesty. The underlying logic seems to be that self-awareness about how one presents oneself to others reflects a broader attentiveness to one's own conduct and its effects on the world.

At the same time, this connection is not without its complications. The care one takes with outward appearance can, in principle, serve as a mask for inner deficiencies of character rather than as a reflection of genuine integrity. A different *Gemara* elsewhere suggests a seemingly more direct criterion for trustworthiness: someone who has never been *meshane b'diburo* — who never misrepresents the truth, except in the three contexts where the rabbis permit a departure from strict accuracy. That standard has a more obvious logical relationship to financial honesty. The criterion of careful dress is noteworthy precisely because it is less obvious, and the *Gemara* treats it as a meaningful indicator of character nonetheless.

### Community Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם שֶׁבְּנֵי עִירוֹ מְצֻוִּוין לַעֲשׂוֹת לוֹ מְלַאכְתּוֹ — זֶה שֶׁמַּנִּיחַ חֶפְצוֹ וְעוֹסֵק בְּחֶפְצֵי שָׁמַיִם. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי לְמִיטְרַח בְּרִיפְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who is the Torah scholar for whom the inhabitants of his city are commanded to perform his labor for him? This is one who sets his own matters aside and engages in matters of Heaven.** It is therefore fitting for the community to support him. **And that applies only to exerting themselves** to provide him with **his bread,** as it is appropriate that they sustain him.

Rabbi Yochanan now turns to a different question: who qualifies as a *talmid chacham* such that *benei iro metzuvin la'asot lo melachto* — the people of his town are obligated to perform his work for him, so that he can devote himself entirely to learning? The answer: *zeh she-maniach cheftzo v'osek b'cheftzei shamayim* — one who completely abandons his own pursuits and devotes himself purely to heavenly matters. The *Gemara* qualifies this obligation: *v'hani milei lemitrach b'rifta* — even this communal obligation extends only to providing basic sustenance, bread, not to providing luxuries.

This passage touches on a much larger debate. The *Rambam* argued vigorously against the institutionalization of community-supported Torah study, noting that the great figures of *Chazal* all had occupations and were not supported by the *tzibur*. He objected particularly to social pressure compelling communities to fund such support. A more sympathetic reading might compare the community support of full-time Torah scholars to the way graduate fellowships fund advanced scholarship in fields that are not financially remunerative but that society judges to have lasting value — with the critical caveat that such support requires a rigorous selection process and should not be extended indiscriminately.

The phrase *meniach cheftzo v'osek b'cheftzei shamayim* itself may already encode some selectivity: it may not refer to anyone who spends hours learning, but specifically to one who never leaves the *beit midrash*, who has genuinely and completely abandoned all personal pursuits.

### Three Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵיזֶהוּ תַּלְמִיד חָכָם — כׇּל שֶׁשּׁוֹאֲלִין אוֹתוֹ הֲלָכָה בְּכׇל מָקוֹם, וְאוֹמְרָהּ. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ — לְמַנּוֹיֵיהּ פַּרְנָס עַל הַצִּיבּוּר. אִי בַּחֲדָא מַסֶּכְתָּא — בְּאַתְרֵיהּ, אִי בְּכוּלֵּיהּ תַּנּוֹיֵהּ — בְּרֵישׁ מְתִיבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Who is** included in the category of **a Torah scholar? Anyone who is asked** about the ***halakha* with regard to any topic and** is able to **state it.** And they say: **What are the practical consequences** of this question? It is **to appoint him a leader of the community. If** he is an expert **in a single tractate,** they appoint him as a leader **in his place; if** he is an expert **in all of his learning,** they appoint him **as the head of the yeshiva.**

The *Gemara* now presents a series of related formulations from Rabbi Yochanan, each asking *eizehu talmid chacham* — who is a *talmid chacham* — but for different purposes. First: *kol she'shoalin oto halacha b'chol makom v'omrah* — one who can be asked a *halacha* question from anywhere and can answer it. This is the criterion for appointing someone *parnas al ha-tzibur*, a rabbinic leader of the community. *Tosafot* reads this as referring to mastery within one's area of specialization, even if that area is small — citing *masechet Kallah* as an example, a single *perek* of relatively accessible material focused on the laws of a wedding. Rashi, by contrast, appears to read it more broadly.

The *Gemara* then distinguishes between levels. *Ee b'chada masechta* — if the scholar is a master of one tractate — *b'asrei* — he may be appointed as a local rabbinic authority in his own community. *Ee b'chula tanuye* — if he is knowledgeable across the breadth of *Shas* — *b'reish mesivta* — he may be appointed as a *Rosh Yeshiva*, a more regional or senior authority. These distinctions apply specifically in the context of communal rabbinic appointment.

The section concludes with a return to the *derasha* that opened this discussion. Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish offers a different interpretation of *benai'im*: not Torah scholars but rather *keilim ha'ulyarin habaim midinat hayam* — bath attendants' garments that come from overseas, garments of a very particular and fine quality for which people are extremely particular. Even a stain on one side would be objectionable because of their quality. The *Gemara* then notes that since stains would presumably be most visible on white garments, this is the implication. But Rabbi Yannai instructed his sons not to bury him in white garments — lest he not merit and appear like a groom among mourners in *Gehenna* — nor in black garments — lest he merit and appear like a mourner among grooms in heaven. Instead, he requested burial in these bath-attendants' garments from overseas. The *Gemara* concludes that these must be a reddish or other color, and resolves the apparent difficulty by distinguishing between inner garments, which would be white, and outer cloaks, which would be of a darker color.

## Yom Kippur & Shabbat

### Olat Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר מְקַפְּלִין כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״עוֹלַת שַׁבָּת בְּשַׁבַּתּוֹ״, לִימֵּד עַל חֶלְבֵי שַׁבָּת שֶׁקְּרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. יָכוֹל אַף שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים בְּשַׁבָּת — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּשַׁבַּתּוֹ״, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל.
>
> **Translation:** We learned in the mishna that **Rabbi Yishmael says: One may fold** clothes and make beds on Yom Kippur for Shabbat if Yom Kippur occurs on Friday, and the fats of the sacrifices that were brought on Shabbat were offered on Yom Kippur that occurs on Sunday. The verse **"The burnt-offering of Shabbat on its Shabbat,** besides the daily offering and its libation" (Numbers 28:10) **taught, with regard to the fats of Shabbat, that** they **are offered on Yom Kippur,** because Yom Kippur is also called Shabbat, and the verse indicates that the Shabbat offering is offered on another Shabbat. I **might** have thought **even** the fats **from Yom Kippur** offerings could be sacrificed **on Shabbat;** therefore, **the verse states "on its Shabbat"** to specify that it is not so; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yishmael.**

Having completed the extended digression on the dignity of Torah scholars, the *Gemara* returns to the *mishnah*'s original topic: the relationship between *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* in matters of preparation and sacrificial service. The *mishnah* had presented the positions of Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva on whether one can prepare on *Yom Kippur* for *Shabbat* and vice versa. A *baraita* is now cited: *olat Shabbat b'shabbato* — the *olah* of *Shabbat* in its *Shabbat* — is read as teaching that the fats of *Shabbat* that were not burned in time may be burned on *Yom Kippur*, since *Yom Kippur* itself is called *Shabbat*. The verse uses the word *Shabbat* to include another *Shabbat*, and the immediate candidate is *Yom Kippur*, which falls on the very next day.

The *baraita* then asks: might this work in the reverse direction as well — could one bring the fats of *Yom Kippur* on *Shabbat*? The verse answers: *b'shabbato* — only in its own *Shabbat* — limiting the direction.

### Hierarchy Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: ״עוֹלַת שַׁבָּת בְּשַׁבַּתּוֹ״, לִימֵּד עַל חֶלְבֵי שַׁבָּת שֶׁקְּרֵבִים בְּיוֹם טוֹב. יָכוֹל אַף בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּשַׁבַּתּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says** that when the verse says: **"The burnt-offering of Shabbat on its Shabbat,"** it teaches **that** the **fats of Shabbat are offered on a Festival** that occurs on the following day. **I might** have thought that they could **even** be offered **on Yom Kippur** that occurred on the day after Shabbat. Therefore, **the verse states "on its Shabbat"** and not on another one.

This is the crux of the debate between Rabbi Ishmael and Rabbi Akiva. The limiting word *b'shabbato* means we can only choose one direction, and the question is which day serves which. In most people's intuition, *Yom Kippur* — called *Shabbat Shabbaton*, a doubled *Shabbat* — would seem holier. Yet the *Gemara* understands that the *kedushah* of the weekly *Shabbat* is in fact greater, as evidenced by the *onesh*: violation of *Shabbat* carries the death penalty, while violation of *Yom Kippur* carries *karet*. Death is the more severe punishment, indicating the higher sanctity of *Shabbat*. Therefore, when a choice must be made, *Yom Kippur* serves *Shabbat* and not the reverse. This is the position of Rabbi Ishmael.

Rabbi Akiva, however, reads *b'shabbato* as an even more restricting statement: each *Shabbat* can only serve itself. Nothing may be transferred from one to the other. But if so, what does the verse come to teach? Rabbi Akiva says it teaches that the fats of *Shabbat* may be brought on *Yom Tov* — a festival day that follows *Shabbat*. *Yom Tov*, not *Yom Kippur*, is the permissible extension.

### Yom Tov Prep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁתִּמְצָא לוֹמַר, לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת קְרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא — לְיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. לְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא נְדָרִים וּנְדָבוֹת אֵין קְרֵבִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא — לְמִישְׁרֵא בְּיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **When you** analyze the matter you **will find** that you can **say that, in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Yishmael, vows and free-will offerings are offered on a Festival.** If sacrifices that fulfill vows can be postponed to a weekday but are nevertheless brought on a Festival, certainly fats from Shabbat can be offered on a Festival that follows it. **And the verse is needed to** permit offering Shabbat fats on **Yom Kippur** that follows it. However, **according to Rabbi Akiva vows and free-will offerings are not brought on a Festival, and** therefore **the verse is needed to permit** sacrificing the fats from Shabbat **on a Festival** that follows it, but on Yom Kippur it is prohibited.

The *Gemara* unpacks this. According to Rabbi Ishmael, *nedarim u-nedavot kravin b'Yom Tov* — free-will offerings may be brought on *Yom Tov*. If that is already established, then the verse need not be teaching us something about *Yom Tov*, which we already knew. The verse must therefore be extending the permission even to *Yom Kippur*. According to Rabbi Akiva, however, *nedarim u-nedavot ein kravin b'Yom Tov* — free-will offerings may not be brought on *Yom Tov*. It would therefore not be self-evident that the fats of *Shabbat* could be burned on *Yom Tov*, and the verse is needed precisely to teach that *Yom Tov* — not *Yom Kippur* — is the permissible case. This also aligns with the *mishnah*'s theme of *eruv tavshilin* and the general principle of *Yom Tov* preparing for *Shabbat*.

### Yom Kippur Friday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: כִּי הֲוֵינָא בְּבָבֶל הֲוָה אָמְרִינַן: הָא דְּתַנְיָא יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — לֹא הָיוּ תּוֹקְעִין, וּבְמוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת — לֹא הָיוּ מַבְדִּילִין, דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל הִיא. כִּי סְלֵיקְנָא לְהָתָם, אַשְׁכְּחִיתֵיהּ לִיהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הִיא, דְּאִי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר חֶלְבֵי שַׁבָּת קְרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים, לִיתְקַע, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֶיהְוֵי יָדְעִי דְּחֶלְבֵי שַׁבָּת קְרֵיבִין בְּיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים. וְאָמֵינָא לֵיהּ אֲנָא: כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִין הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **When I was in Babylonia, I said** with regard to **that which was taught** in a *baraita*: If **Yom Kippur occurred** on **Shabbat eve, they would not sound** the *shofar* as they did every Friday to herald the start of Shabbat; **and** if Yom Kippur occurred **at the conclusion of Shabbat, they would not recite *havdala*** to mark the end of the sanctity of Shabbat and the start of the sanctity of Yom Kippur, is **a statement** accepted by **all. When I went** to Eretz Yisrael, **I found Yehuda, son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi, who sat and said:** This *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva,** as it equates the sanctity of Yom Kippur with that of Shabbat. **As, if** you say that it is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael, then, since** Rabbi Yishmael **said** that **fats from Shabbat are offered on Yom Kippur, let them sound** the *shofar* **so that** the priests **will know** that **the fats from Shabbat are offered on Yom Kippur** and they may begin offering them (Rav Hai Gaon). **And I said to him:** You cannot prove this from here, because **priests are vigilant** and can be trusted to know this on their own, and there is no need to sound the *shofar*.

Rabbi Zeira provides a historical account. When he was still in *Bavel*, he assumed that the *baraita* ruling — that when *Yom Kippur* falls on Friday there is no *tekiah* (no series of shofar blasts to mark the cessation of *melacha*), and when it falls on Sunday there is no *havdala* between *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* — was universally accepted. The reasoning is straightforward: the *tekiah* on an ordinary *erev Shabbat* signals people to stop working, but if *Yom Kippur* already forbids *melacha*, there is no point. Similarly, *havdala* marks a transition from a higher to a lower *kedushah*, but the difference between *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* is marginal — both prohibit *melacha*, with only a difference in the severity of the *onesh* — so no *havdala* is required.

When Rabbi Zeira made *aliyah* to *Eretz Yisrael*, he found Yehuda the son of Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi teaching that this is only the view of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that *Yom Kippur* cannot serve *Shabbat* at all and thus there is nothing to mark. Rabbi Ishmael, who holds that *Yom Kippur* does in fact serve *Shabbat* in the matter of the sacrificial fats, would require a *tekiah* when *Shabbat* transitions into *Yom Kippur*, to announce to people that the day's *kedushah* is slightly different and that the fats may be offered. Rabbi Zeira responded: *kohanim zrizim heim* — the *kohanim* are diligent and knowledgeable; they do not require a shofar blast to inform them of this *halacha*. A *tekiah* is relevant for matters that affect the general public, not for technical sacrificial laws known to the *kohanim*.

### Shofar Blasts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מִי אָמְרִינַן כֹּהֲנִים זְרִיזִין הֵן? וְהָתְנַן: שָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְטִיל אֶת הָעָם מִמְּלָאכָה, שָׁלֹשׁ לְהַבְדִּיל בֵּין קוֹדֶשׁ לְחוֹל. כְּדַאֲמַר אַבָּיֵי: לִשְׁאָר עַמָּא דְּבִירוּשְׁלֶם, הָכָא נָמֵי לִשְׁאָר עַמָּא דְּבִירוּשְׁלֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Mar Kashisha,** the **son** of **Rav Ḥisda, said to Rav Ashi: Do we say** that **priests are vigilant** in that regard? **Didn't we learn in** a mishna that **three** blasts were sounded in the Temple on Friday **to stop the people from work,** and **three** more were sounded **to demarcate between sacred and profane?** Apparently, even priests required the blasts to alert them to the beginning and end of Shabbat. The Gemara answers: **As Abaye said** in a different context, that certain matters were undertaken **for the rest of the people in Jerusalem, so too here,** these blasts were sounded **for the rest of the people in Jerusalem** and not for the priests.

Mar Kashisha son of Rav Chisda challenges Rabbi Zeira's position, citing the *mishnah* that the shofar blasts on *erev Shabbat* were performed in the *Beit HaMikdash* itself — which would seem to imply that the *kohanim* were among those who needed them. The response, citing Abaye's ruling in a different context, is that the blasts in the *Beit HaMikdash* were intended for the people of Jerusalem generally, not for the *kohanim* themselves. Just as the gold *menorah* of Hilni HaMalka that indicated the time of *Shema* was for the general public while the *kohanim* already recited *Shema* before sunrise, so too the *tekiah* from the *Beit HaMikdash* was for the city's population, not for the *kohanim* who were already well-versed in the relevant *halachot*.

## Knivat Yerek

### Tekiah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיתְקַע, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִידְּעוּ דִּשְׁרֵי בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק מִן הַמִּנְחָה וּלְמַעְלָה. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: לְפִי שֶׁאֵין דּוֹחִין שְׁבוּת לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In any event, **Let them sound** the *shofar* **so that they will know that trimming the vegetables,** i.e., removing the leaf heads from vegetables in preparation for cutting them, **is permitted from the** late **afternoon and onward.** On Yom Kippur, it is permitted, starting at a certain point of the day, to prepare vegetables for the meal following the fast. However, this is not permitted on Shabbat, when it is prohibited to perform any action in preparation for the weekdays after Shabbat. **Rav Yosef said:** This is **because a rabbinic prohibition [*shevut*] is not overridden** in order **to permit** another action. Although sounding the *shofar* is not prohibited by Torah law on Shabbat, it is prohibited by rabbinic law on Shabbat and Yom Kippur, and this prohibition is not overridden to permit another action.

The *Gemara* now raises a further question. Given that *Yom Kippur* has a slightly different *kedushah* from *Shabbat* — specifically, the *halacha* of *knivat yerek*, trimming vegetables, which is permitted on *Yom Kippur* from *mincha* time and onward but not on *Shabbat* — why not blow the shofar at the transition between *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* specifically to publicize this? The *tekiah* would serve to inform people: the day is now *Yom Kippur*, and from *mincha* time you will be permitted to trim your vegetables. *Knivat yerek*, it should be noted, is a rabbinic prohibition — some form of *borer* or similar concern, not a biblical *melacha* — and it was permitted specifically on *Yom Kippur* from *mincha* onward. Rashi explains that the permission is granted so that one does not need to rush immediately after the fast to prepare food, which would extend unnecessary discomfort; others understand *agmat nefesh* differently, as whetting the appetite rather than relieving it.

### Tekiah & Permitting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי אָמַר: שְׁבוּת קְרוֹבָה — הִתִּירוּ, שְׁבוּת רְחוֹקָה — לֹא הִתִּירוּ. וּשְׁבוּת קְרוֹבָה הִתִּירוּ? וְהָתְנַן: יוֹם טוֹב שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת עֶרֶב שַׁבָּת — תּוֹקְעִין וְלֹא מַבְדִּילִין. מוֹצָאֵי שַׁבָּת — מַבְדִּילִין וְלֹא תּוֹקְעִין. וְאַמַּאי? לִיתְקַע, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלִידְּעוּ דִּשְׁרֵי בִּשְׁחִיטָה לְאַלְתַּר! אֶלָּא מְחַוַּורְתָּא כִּדְרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Sheisha, son** of **Rav Idi, said: The Sages permitted** doing so for **an immediate rabbinic prohibition,** e.g., sounding the *shofar* to permit prohibited actions immediately. **They did not permit** doing so for **a** more **remote rabbinic prohibition,** such as trimming vegetables, which is allowed only beginning late in the afternoon, several hours after the start of Yom Kippur (Rabbeinu Ḥananel). The Gemara asks: **And** did **they permit** doing so for **an immediate rabbinic prohibition? Didn't we learn** in a mishna that if **a Festival occurs on Shabbat eve, one sounds** the *shofar* to announce that Shabbat has begun, **but one does not recite *havdala*** over wine to mark the conclusion of the Festival, because the sanctity of Shabbat is greater than the sanctity of the Festival? And if a Festival occurs at **the conclusion of Shabbat, one recites *havdala*** to demarcate between the sanctity of Shabbat and the sanctity of the Festival, **but one does not sound** the *shofar*. **And why** does one not sound the *shofar*? **Let them sound** the *shofar* at the beginning of the Festival, **so that** people **will know that slaughtering is permitted immediately** on the Festival, although it was prohibited on Shabbat. **Rather, it is clear** that this must be explained **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rav Yosef,** who says that a rabbinic prohibition is not overridden to permit the performance of actions that were prohibited.

Rav Yosef answers: *lefi she'ein dochin shvut lehatir* — we do not violate a rabbinic prohibition in order to permit another rabbinic act. Blowing the shofar on *Shabbat* or *Yom Kippur* is itself an *issur d'rabbanan*. We would not perform one rabbinic violation in order to announce the permissibility of a different rabbinic act. Rav Sheshet son of Rav Idi offers a second reason: *shvut karov hitiru, shvut rachok lo hitiru* — a rabbinic leniency that takes effect immediately at the transition may be announced, but one that only takes effect at the end of the day, at *mincha* time, is too distant to justify making an announcement at the moment of transition.

The *Gemara* tests these answers against an analogous case. The *mishnah* rules that when *Yom Tov* leads into *Shabbat*, a *tekiah* is sounded — because now many things that were permitted on *Yom Tov* become forbidden on *Shabbat* — but no *havdala* is made, since *Shabbat* is a higher *kedushah* than *Yom Tov*. Conversely, when *Yom Tov* follows *Shabbat*, *havdala* is made between the two — moving from *Shabbat* to a lesser *kedushah* — but no *tekiah* is sounded.

Why is no *tekiah* sounded when *Yom Tov* follows *Shabbat*? One might think a *tekiah* should be blown precisely to tell people: *Shabbat* is over, *Yom Tov* has begun, *shechitah* is now permitted — you can cook, you can slaughter immediately. This is something that takes effect right away, unlike the case of *knivat yerek* at *mincha* time. Yet no *tekiah* is sounded. This demonstrates that we simply do not blow the shofar to announce that something becomes *mutar*. The purpose of a *tekiah* is to signal a stop — to say *assur*. *Havdala* marks a transition, but even when things become permitted, no *tekiah* accompanies that announcement.

This supports Rav Yosef's position: *ela machvarta k'd'Rav Yosef* — the cleaner answer is that of Rav Yosef, who holds that *tekiah* is simply not used as a mechanism for permitting things, regardless of whether the permission is immediate or distant.

### Shabbaton Shevut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — אָסוּר בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק. אָמַר רַב מָנָא, תָּנָא: מִנַּיִן לְיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת שֶׁאָסוּר בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן״, שְׁבוּת. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לִמְלָאכָה — וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה כׇל מְלָאכָה״! אֶלָּא לָאו, אַקְּנִיבַת יָרָק! שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to trimming vegetables on Yom Kippur, the Gemara cites that which **Rabbi Zeira said** that **Rav Huna said, and some say Rabbi Abba said** that **Rav Huna said:** If **Yom Kippur occurs on Shabbat, trimming vegetables is prohibited. Rav Mana said: It was taught** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that **on Yom Kippur that occurs on Shabbat trimming vegetables is prohibited? The verse states: "A solemn rest [*shabbaton*],** a holy Shabbat unto the Lord" (Exodus 16:23), which means that there is an obligation to issue a **rabbinic prohibition** on this day. Issue a rabbinic prohibition **for what** activity? **If you say** this refers to prohibiting actual **labor, isn't it written** explicitly: "But the seventh day is Shabbat for the Lord your God; **you shall not perform any labor"** (Exodus 20:9)? **Rather, does** it not refer to **trimming vegetables,** which is prohibited on Shabbat even though it is not a full-fledged prohibited labor? The Gemara concludes: Indeed, **learn from it** that trimming vegetables is prohibited.

The *Gemara* returns to the question of *knivat yerek* specifically when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*. Rav Zeira says in the name of Rav Huna: *Yom HaKippurim shechal lihyot b'Shabbos assur b'knivat yerek* — when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, trimming vegetables is forbidden. Rav Mana cites a *baraita* that derives this from the verse: *Talmud lomar Shabbaton shevus* — the double language of *Shabbaton shevus* applied to *Yom Kippur* in the Torah. Since the verse cannot merely be repeating the general prohibition of *melacha*, which is already stated explicitly elsewhere, it must be adding the prohibition of *knivat yerek* specifically for when *Yom Kippur* coincides with *Shabbat*. *Tosafot* notes that this is an *asmachta*, since if it were truly a biblical prohibition it would apply to *Yom Kippur* in any case; but the *asmachta* nonetheless supports the rabbinic ruling.

### YK on Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — מוּתָּר בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק. מֵיתִיבִי: מִנַּיִן לְיוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת שֶׁאָסוּר בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שַׁבָּתוֹן״, שְׁבוּת. לְמַאי? אִילֵימָא לִמְלָאכָה — וְהָכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה כׇל מְלָאכָה״ אֶלָּא לָאו, בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק! לָא, לְעוֹלָם לִמְלָאכָה, וְלַעֲבוֹר עָלֶיהָ בַּ״עֲשֵׂה״ וְ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **Yom Kippur occurs on Shabbat, trimming vegetables is permitted.** The Gemara **raised an objection** from what we learned in the *baraita* cited earlier: **From where** is it derived that **on Yom Kippur that occurs on Shabbat trimming vegetables is prohibited? The verse states: "A solemn rest [*shabbaton*],** a holy Shabbat unto the Lord" (Exodus 16:23), which means that there is an obligation to issue a **rabbinic prohibition** on this day. Issue a rabbinic prohibition **for what** activity? **If you say** this refers to prohibiting actual **labor, isn't it written** explicitly: "But the seventh day is Shabbat for the Lord your God; **you shall not perform any labor"** (Exodus 20:9)? **Rather, is it not** referring to **trimming vegetables,** which is prohibited on Shabbat? The Gemara answers: **No,** that is not; rather, solemn rest **actually** refers **to labor.** It is a positive commandment to rest on Shabbat. Although the verse says explicitly: "But the seventh day is Shabbat for the Lord your God; you shall not perform any labor," this adds that one who performs a prohibited labor **violates** both **a positive mitzva and a prohibition.**

Rabbi Chiya bar Avin then cites Rabbi Yochanan to the contrary: *Yom HaKippurim shechal lihyot b'Shabbos mutar b'knivat yerek* — when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, trimming is in fact permitted. The *Gemara* challenges Rabbi Yochanan's view from the very *baraita* just cited, which assumed that *Shabbaton shevus* comes to prohibit *knivat yerek* in precisely this case. Rabbi Yochanan responds by rereading the verse: *Shabbaton shevus* does not come to teach about *knivat yerek* at all. It comes only to tell us that there is both a *lav* and an *aseh* — a negative commandment and a positive commandment — for *melacha* on *Shabbat*. Since the verse *b'lav va'aseh* provides this double reinforcement for the *melacha* prohibition alone, it is not available to extend the prohibition of *knivat yerek* to the case where *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*.

### Mincha Prep

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת — מוּתָּר בִּקְנִיבַת יָרָק. (וְאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יוֹם כִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בַּחוֹל) מְפַצְּעִין בֶּאֱגוֹזִים וּמְפַרְכְּסִין בְּרִימּוֹנִים מִן הַמִּנְחָה וּלְמַעְלָה מִפְּנֵי עׇגְמַת נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If **Yom Kippur occurs on Shabbat,** trimming vegetables is permitted. **And Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If **Yom Kippur occurs on** a **weekday, one may crack nuts and remove pomegranate seeds from** the late **afternoon and onward,** because doing so involves no actual labor and **due to anxiety,** i.e., if a person does not know that there is food prepared for when the fast ends, he suffers more during the final hours of the day (Rabbi Zeraḥia HaLevi).

A *baraita* is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's position: *tanya k'vasi d'Rabbi Yochanan* — we taught consistent with Rabbi Yochanan — that when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, *knivat yerek* is permitted, and furthermore, *maftzi'in b'egozim um'farchasin b'rimonim min haminchah ul'ma'alah* — one may crack nuts and remove pomegranate seeds from *mincha* time onward. These preparations for the meal to be eaten after the fast are permitted beginning at *mincha* time, even when the day is *Yom Kippur*. The reason given is *mipnei agmat nefesh* — on account of the distress associated with the fast — so that after *Yom Kippur* one does not need to rush to prepare food and thereby extend the period of discomfort.

### Rabbah's Letter

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּבֵי רַב יְהוּדָה מְקַנְּבִי כְּרָבָא. דְּבֵי רַבָּה גָּרְדִי קָארֵי. כֵּיוָן דַּחֲזָא דַּהֲווֹ קָא מְחָרְפִי, אֲמַר לְהוּ: אֲתַאי אִיגַּרְתָּא מִמַּעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דַּאֲסִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: The members of **Rav Yehuda's house would trim cabbage.** The members of **Rabba's house would scrub gourds. Once** Rabba **saw** that **they were doing** this **early,** before the late afternoon, **he said to them: A letter came from the West,** i.e., from Eretz Yisrael, **in the name** of **Rabbi Yoḥanan,** saying that doing so is **prohibited.**

However, Rabbah observed that people around him were beginning to abuse the allowance — performing these preparations before *mincha* time rather than waiting. When he saw this overextension, he announced: *asa igrasa mimarava mishme'i d'Rabbi Yochanan d'assur* — a letter has arrived from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of Rabbi Yochanan forbidding it. Whether or not such a letter in fact existed, Rabbah felt it necessary to retract or suppress the leniency because of the way it was being misused, preferring to invoke the authority of Rabbi Yochanan himself to close the door on the abuse.

With this, the *Gemara* concludes: *hadran alach v'eilu k'sharim* — we have completed this *perek*.
