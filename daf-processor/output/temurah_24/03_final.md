# Temurah 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Two Animals Achrayus

### Rav Hoshaiah's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי הוֹשַׁעְיָא: הַמַּפְרִישׁ שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת לְאַחְרָיוּת, מִתְכַּפֵּר בְּאַחַת מֵהֶן, וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ רוֹעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Hoshaya says:** In the case of **one who designates two** animals as **sin offerings as a guarantee,** so that if one animal is lost he will achieve atonement with the other animal, **he achieves atonement with one of them, and the other** is left to **graze** until it develops a blemish. It is then sold, and the money received from its sale is used to purchase communal gift offerings.

The daf opens at 24a with a statement of Rav Hoshaiah that brings a new dimension to the ongoing discussion of *chatas hameisah*. Until now, the Gemara had been exploring the debate between Rebbe and the *Rabbanan* regarding the conditions under which a sin offering must die — both positions requiring, at minimum, that the original animal had first been lost, with the dispute turning on precisely when the second designation or atonement must occur relative to that loss. Rabbi Shimon had introduced the most stringent view, holding that any *chatas* whose owner achieved atonement through another offering dies, categorically and without nuance, subsumed under his rule of *chamesh chataos meisos*.

The Gemara had earlier established that even Rabbi Shimon would concede one case: where the owner designated two separate piles of money from the outset, one as a backup for the other. In that scenario, since neither pile was ever lost, even Rabbi Shimon would agree that the unused pile does not need to be destroyed. Now the Gemara extends the inquiry: what if, instead of two piles of money, a person designated two animals as a *chatas* from the outset — *hamafrish shtei chataos l'achrayus* — one serving as backup for the other? The owner is then atoned through one of them, while the other grazes. What is the law?

The Gemara immediately asks: *aliba d'man* — according to whom is Rav Hoshaiah's ruling a *chiddush*? If it is according to the *Rabbanan*, the novelty is negligible. The *Rabbanan* require actual loss as a precondition for the *chatas hameisah* designation. They hold that even designating a second animal as a replacement for one that was lost (*mafrish l'ibud*) does not suffice to confer quasi-lost status on the second — *lav k'ibud dami*. How much more so, then, if there was never any loss at all, as in a pure *achrayus* arrangement? The Rabbis would obviously say the second animal simply grazes. The real *chiddush* must therefore be directed at Rabbi Shimon, whose position is the most categorical: *nitkaper b'acher*, atoned through another, is sufficient in all circumstances. Under his view, even in the *achrayus* case, one might have thought the remaining animal must die. Rav Hoshaiah is therefore clarifying that this is not so.

But the Gemara quickly leapfrogs over Rabbi Shimon as well: *ela aliba d'Rebbi* — the statement is addressed to Rebbe. Rebbe occupies the middle position, stricter than the *Rabbanan* but not as absolute as Rabbi Shimon. When does Rebbe say the second animal must die? Only *b'ibud* — when the situation builds on the reality of loss, either because one animal was actually lost or because the second was designated in place of a lost one. But *achrayus* — a backup arrangement that never involved any loss at all — does not trigger the *halacha* of *chatas hameisah* even according to Rebbe. Hence the ruling: the animal grazes, and the proceeds go toward a *nedavah*.

### Rabbi Shimon vs. Rebbe

This ruling clarifies an important distinction that is easily obscured. One might have assumed that Rabbi Shimon's position — *nitkaper b'acher, hi meisah* — is simply an extreme version of Rebbe's, differing only in degree. The Gemara now makes clear that there is a qualitative difference. Both Rebbe and the *Rabbanan* ground the entire *halacha* of *chatas hameisah* in the phenomenon of loss. The owner's atonement through a second animal is not sufficient on its own; it must build on a prior reality of *ibud*. The debate between Rebbe and the *Rabbanan* concerns only the details of that framework — whether the second animal was designated before or after the first was found, and whether one counts from the moment of designation or the moment of *hakrava*. But the essential precondition of loss is shared by both.

Rabbi Shimon stands apart. He requires no *ibud* whatsoever. The sole criterion is *nitkaper b'acher*: once the owner achieves atonement through another animal, the original dies regardless of circumstances. This is true even when the first animal was never lost, as long as actual animals — not money — are involved. By contrast, where the owner designated two piles of money, Rabbi Shimon concedes that the unused pile survives, because the conceptual framework of *chatas hameisah* does not apply to money in the same way. But two animals sanctified as *chataos*, one of which atones — that remaining animal dies according to Rabbi Shimon even absent any loss. This is the structural significance of Rav Hoshaiah's ruling: it delineates precisely where Rabbi Shimon's categorical position ends.

## Redeemed Korban Status

### Blemished Sin Offering

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאת, וַהֲרֵי הִיא בַּעֲלַת מוּם — מוֹכְרָהּ, וּמֵבִיא אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם קָרְבָה שְׁנִיָּה קוֹדֶם שֶׁנִּשְׁחֲטָה רִאשׁוֹנָה — תָּמוּת, שֶׁכְּבָר כִּיפְּרוּ בְּעָלֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a challenge to this explanation: **We learned** in the mishna (22b): In the case of **one who designates a sin offering and** the animal **is blemished, he sells** the animal **and brings another** sin offering **in its stead** with the money received in its sale. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: If the second** animal **is sacrificed before** the first **is slaughtered** for non-sacred consumption, the first animal **shall** be left to **die, as** it is considered an animal **whose owner already achieved atonement** with another animal.

The Gemara now pivots to an adjacent *mishnah* discussed earlier in the tractate. The case involves a person who designated a *chatas* that turned out to have a *mum*. The *Mishnah* rules: *hamochrah u'meivi acher tachteha* — you redeem it and bring a different animal in its stead. This is a straightforward application of the laws of redemption. What makes the case interesting is the position of Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon appended to this *Mishnah*: *im karva shniya kodem she'nishchata rishona* — if the second *korban* was brought before the first (the redeemed one) was slaughtered, then the first animal acquires the status of *chatas she'nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher*, a sin offering whose owners were atoned by another, and it must be left to die.

### Redeemed Retains Identity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כְּרַבִּי סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּאַחְרָיוּת נָמֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: It **enters your mind** to say **that Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who says that if one achieved atonement with one sin offering, the remaining animal is left to die. Likewise, even if the remaining animal is no longer sacred, it is left to die if the owner achieved atonement with the other animal. **And** if so, **even** if one initially designated an additional sin offering as **a guarantee,** the remaining animal should **also** be left to die. Consequently, the statement of Rabbi Hoshaya cannot be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

This is a striking position. The first animal had a *mum* and was redeemed — its *kedusha* was transferred. Yet Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon holds that it retains the essential identity of a *chatas* to such a degree that a subsequent atonement through another animal can still doom it. The Gemara asks: according to which earlier authority does this align? The initial assumption was that Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon follows Rebbe — the position that is comparatively stringent. But the Gemara challenges this. Even according to Rebbe, the *halacha* of *chatas hameisah* requires *ibud*, and this animal was never lost. Its situation is analogous to *achrayus*: it could not be used, another was brought in its stead, but there was never a status of being lost. Why should it die?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דִּלְמָא רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כַּאֲבוּהּ סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת מֵתוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No, perhaps Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon,** does not hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. Rather, he **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **his father,** Rabbi Shimon, **who said** that there are **five sin offerings** left to **die,** one of which is a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another animal. Since Rabbi Shimon does not qualify this statement, he evidently maintains that the remaining sin offering is always left to die, even when it is no longer sacred. If so, the statement of Rabbi Hoshaya can be in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi.

The Gemara therefore concludes: *lo, de'Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon k'avuhu svara lei* — he holds like his father, Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Shimon holds that *nitkaper b'acher* suffices regardless of circumstance. If this animal retains its identity as a *chatas* even after redemption, then once the owner is atoned through another, it dies — no *ibud* required. The remaining question, which the Gemara defers for the moment, is the threshold condition: why would we say the redeemed animal still retains its *chatas* identity at all?

### Second Before First

The significance of this exchange is that it sharpens the conceptual landscape further. Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon's position — that a redeemed *korban* retains its identity — is a distinct and independent claim. It interacts with Rabbi Shimon's ruling to produce a uniquely stringent outcome: the redeemed animal dies even though it was never lost, because (a) it retains its *chatas* identity post-redemption, and (b) Rabbi Shimon does not require *ibud*. Neither premise alone would be sufficient; the ruling requires both.

One might ask whether the person who purchased the blemished animal bears any practical responsibility. If the animal he bought was once a *chatas*, and Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon holds it may still be rendered forbidden, he presumably needs to know this. Indeed, minimally one would be obligated to inform the buyer, since it limits his use of the animal: the prohibition on shearing, working, or grazing it applies, and he must slaughter it before the seller's second *korban* is brought.

## Two Animals Achrayus (II)

### Achrayus & Loss

The Gemara now returns to the framework of *achrayus* in order to connect it with the larger discussion of *sheir la'azazel*. The key conceptual move is understanding what the status of the second animal in a pair actually is when the first is used. The Gemara had established that the second *sheir lachatas* — the extra one left over when a new pair is brought — is treated as *achrayus*, a backup. But this raises an immediate difficulty: *achrayus* implies the possibility of choosing either one, whereas here one knows from the outset which will be used. If the second *sheir* is definitively going to be unused, how can it be described as mere *achrayus* rather than as *avud*?

The answer emerges in stages. The Gemara previously concluded that Rav holds *ba'alei chaim einan nidchin* — living animals are not rejected — and therefore *mitzvah b'rishon*, there is an obligation to bring the *sheir lachatas* of the first pair. This means it is not merely optional to use the first pair's *sheir*; it is required. Consequently, the second pair's *sheir lachatas* is not in an ambiguous backup position at all — it is known from the very outset that it will not and cannot be used. That status is equivalent to loss, *k'avud dami*, from the moment of designation. And once it has an *avud*-equivalent status, the framework of *nitkaper b'acher* applies, and a *yachid*-equivalent would die.

This explains a surface tension in the Gemara: earlier in the discussion the second *sheir* was described as *achrayus*, implying choice and optionality, yet here it is treated as effectively foreclosed from use. Tosafos notes that the earlier framing may simply represent the stage before the Gemara's conclusion, and the final position is that since *mitzvah b'rishon* is operative, the second *sheir* has *avud*-equivalent status from the beginning.

A student raised an interesting question during the shiur: what if the *sheir lachatas* of the first pair also dies before the second pair is brought? Would you then bring a third pair, or use the surviving *sheir* from the second pair? The answer is that the label of *chatas hameisah* only crystallizes once *nitkaper b'acher* has actually occurred. Before you have brought one of them and achieved atonement, the status is not yet permanent. So in that scenario, where atonement has not yet been achieved through either, you could go back and use the *sheir* of the second pair — which is perhaps part of why one could initially describe it as having a quasi-*achrayus* status. But the resolution remains: once *nitkaper b'acher* is established, the second one's fate is determined.

## When Is Lost (Avuda)?

### Lost at Designation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנֵי רַב שִׁימִי בַּר זֵירִי קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: אָבְדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה — לְרַבִּי מֵתָה, לְרַבָּנַן תִּרְעֶה. אָבְדָה בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה — לְרַבָּנַן מֵתָה, לְרַבִּי רוֹעֶה.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis, **Rav Shimi bar Ziri taught** the following *baraita* **before Rav Pappa:** In a case where a sin offering was **lost at the time** when one **designated** a sin offering in its stead, and one found the lost sin offering before sacrificing the replacement, there is a dispute between *tanna'im*. **According to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, the offering that is not sacrificed is left to **die,** and **according to the Rabbis it shall** be left to **graze.** If the first sin offering was **lost at the time** when one achieved atonement with another animal, then **according to the Rabbis** the first sin offering is left to **die,** and **according to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi it is left to **graze.**

The Gemara introduces a *braita* taught by Rav Shimi bar Zeira before Rav Papa, which presents a striking formulation of the debate between Rebbe and the *Rabbanan*. The *braita* divides the cases as follows. *Avda b'sha'at hafrasha* — if the animal was lost at the time of the initial designation of the second one, but subsequently found before the second *korban* was actually brought: *l'Rebbe meisah*, Rebbe says it must die; *l'Rabbanan tir'eh*, the *Rabbanan* say it grazes. This is the familiar framing: Rebbe is stringent in holding that the loss at the moment of designation is sufficient, while the *Rabbanan* require the loss to persist through the moment of atonement.

### Lost at Atonement

The *braita* then turns to the converse case: *avda b'sha'at kapara* — lost at the time of atonement, meaning it was found only after the second *korban* was brought. Here, says the *braita*: *l'Rabbanan meisah*, the *Rabbanan* say it dies; but *l'Rebbe ro'eh*, Rebbe says it only grazes. This is deeply surprising. According to this *braita*, Rebbe and the *Rabbanan* are not on a continuum where one is simply stricter than the other in all respects. Rather, they are mirror images: Rebbe is stricter when the animal was lost earlier (at designation) but more lenient when it was lost later (at atonement), while the *Rabbanan* are more lenient about the earlier case but stricter about the later one. One can construct a logic for Rebbe's position: when the animal was found before the second one was brought, the owner actively chose to use the second one rather than reclaim the first — a deliberate rejection. Whereas if the animal was only found after the second was already brought, the owner had no opportunity to choose; the first was simply unavailable, so its non-use was not an active rejection.

### Rav Papa's Correction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה, דְּאָמְרִי רַבָּנַן רוֹעָה, אָמַר רַבִּי מֵתָה — אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה, דִּלְרַבָּנַן מֵתָה, לְרַבִּי לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? אֶלָּא תָּנֵי הָכִי: אֲבוּדָה בִּשְׁעַת הַפְרָשָׁה — לְרַבִּי מֵתָה, לְרַבָּנַן רוֹעָה. בִּשְׁעַת כַּפָּרָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מֵתָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Pappa said to Rav Shimi bar Ziri: This cannot be correct, due to **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And what,** if in the case of a sin offering that was **lost at the time** when its owner **designated** an animal in its stead, **where the Rabbis say** that the offering that is not sacrificed is left to **graze,** and yet **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says** it is left to **die,** then in the case of a sin offering that was **lost at the time** when its owner achieved atonement with another animal, **where according to the Rabbis** the remaining animal is left to **die,** is it **not all the more so** true that **according to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi it is left to die? **Rather, teach** the *baraita* in **this** manner: If a sin offering was **lost at the time** when one **designated** an animal in its stead, and the owner found the lost sin offering before sacrificing the replacement, **according to Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi the offering that is not sacrificed is left to **die,** and **according to the Rabbis** it is left to **graze.** If the first sin offering was lost **at the time** when one achieved atonement with another animal, **all agree** that it is left to **die.**

Rav Papa, however, rejected this *braita* entirely. He argued from a *kal v'chomer*: if even in the case where the animal was found earlier (*b'sha'at hafrasha*) Rebbe says it must die, how much more so should Rebbe agree that it dies when the animal was not found until after atonement was achieved (*b'sha'at kapara*)? The later scenario is unambiguously worse — the atonement was complete, the animal was truly unavailable throughout the entire process. It is logically impossible for Rebbe to be more lenient in the more severe case. Therefore Rav Papa ruled: *ela tani hachi* — emend the *braita* to read as follows. *Avuda b'sha'at hafrasha l'Rebbe meisah l'Rabbanan ro'eh*: when lost at designation and found before bringing, Rebbe says die and the *Rabbanan* say graze — as before. *B'sha'at kapara divrei hakol meisah*: when lost at the time of atonement, everyone agrees it dies. The two positions form a strict continuum; later is always worse, and the earlier disagreement between Rebbe and the *Rabbanan* simply concerns how early a partial status of *ibud* is sufficient for Rebbe's ruling.

## Skin Removal Laws

### Margling on Yom Tov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵין מַרְגִּילִין בְּיוֹם טוֹב, כְּיוֹצֵא בוֹ — אֵין מַרְגִּילִין בִּבְכוֹר, וְלֹא בִּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One may not flay** the skin of an animal **from its feet on a Festival.** Although it is permitted to slaughter and skin an animal on a Festival, one may not do so in such a manner to retain the hide intact to enable it to function as a vessel, e.g., a water skin. **Similarly, one may not flay a firstborn** kosher animal **from its feet** even on a weekday, and even if the animal is blemished, **nor** may one flay from the feet **disqualified consecrated** animals that have been redeemed, as flaying from the feet is considered degrading.

The Gemara now introduces a new *braita*: *ein maraglin b'Yom Tov* — one may not perform the removal of leg skin (*margling*) on *Yom Tov*. The practice of *margling* involves flaying the skin off an animal from the legs upward rather than slitting it lengthwise down the belly. The latter method is simpler but yields a less useful piece of leather, since it is cut open. The leg-based removal is harder but produces a more intact piece suitable for flasks or other whole-leather uses. The reason it is forbidden on *Yom Tov* is straightforward: *d'ka tarach tircha d'la chazi lei* — it involves substantial effort that yields no *Yom Tov* benefit whatsoever. The leather cannot be used on *Yom Tov* itself. *Shechita* is a *tzorech Yom Tov* and therefore permitted, but this ancillary labor-intensive processing is not.

### Beis Shammai Kedusha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּיוֹם טוֹב — דְּקָא טָרַח טִירְחָא דְּלָא חֲזֵי לֵיהּ, אֶלָּא בְּכוֹר — מַאן תְּנָא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא, דְּאָמַר: בְּכוֹר בִּקְדוּשְּׁתֵיהּ קָאֵי, דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: לֹא יִמָּנֶה יִשְׂרָאֵל עִם הַכֹּהֵן עַל הַבְּכוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** everyone agrees that one may not flay an animal from its feet **on a Festival, as one** who does so **exerts effort that is not fit for** the Festival. **But** with regard to **a firstborn** offering, **who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that flaying from its feet is prohibited? **Rav Ḥisda said: It is** the opinion of **Beit Shammai, who say** that **a firstborn** animal **retains its sanctity** even after it becomes blemished. **As we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 32b) that **Beit Shammai say: An Israelite may not be granted a portion** of a blemished **firstborn** animal along **with a priest.** Just as Beit Shammai prohibit an Israelite from partaking of even a blemished firstborn animal, they prohibit flaying the skin of a firstborn animal from its feet.

The *braita* adds: similarly, one may not perform *margling* on a *bechor* or on *psulei hamukdashin* — animals that were consecrated as *korbanos* but developed a *mum* and were redeemed. The prohibition in these cases, unlike the *Yom Tov* case, requires explanation. The Gemara asks: *bechor man tana* — who is the *tanna* responsible for the *bechor* ruling?

Rav Chisda answers: it is Beis Shammai. The *Mishnah* records a dispute between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel regarding a *bechor* with a *mum*. Beis Hillel holds that once a *bechor* has a blemish, while certain prohibitions remain (one may not shear it, work it, or sell it by the piece), the meat may be eaten by anyone, not just *Kohanim*. Beis Shammai, however, holds that the *bechor* with a *mum* retains essentially the same *kedusha* as a *bechor* without one — and therefore a non-*Kohen* may not join with the *Kohen* in the eating (*lo yimaneh Yisrael im hakohen al habechor*). Since, according to Beis Shammai, the *kedusha* of the *bechor*'s meat is preserved even with a blemish, it follows that one may not engage in processing that focuses commercial attention on the leather rather than honoring the meat. The Gemara (later in the *sugya*) cites the phrase *nirah k'avodah b'kodashim* — it appears like performing labor with sanctified property. Whatever the precise mechanism of that concern, the underlying principle is that if the meat retains *kedusha*, redirecting one's effort toward the leather rather than the meat is disrespectful.

### Redeemed Even if Cooked

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין, מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: הָיוּ לְפָנָיו שְׁתֵּי חַטָּאוֹת, אַחַת תְּמִימָה וְאַחַת בַּעֲלַת מוּם — תְּמִימָה תִּקְרַב, בַּעֲלַת מוּם תִּפָּדֶה. נִשְׁחֲטָה בַּעֲלַת מוּם, עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִזְרַק דָּמָהּ שֶׁל תְּמִימָה — מוּתֶּרֶת; מִשֶּׁנִּזְרַק דָּמָהּ שֶׁל תְּמִימָה — אֲסוּרָה. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשַׂר בַּעֲלַת מוּם בַּקְּדֵירָה, וְנִזְרַק דָּמָהּ שֶׁל תְּמִימָה — יוֹצֵא לְבֵית הַשְׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: With regard to **disqualified consecrated** animals, **who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that one may not flay them from their feet? **Rav Ḥisda says: It is** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **there were two sin offerings before him,** where one was designated as a guarantee for the other should it become lost or disqualified, and **one was unblemished and one** became **blemished** after it was designated, then the **unblemished** animal **shall be sacrificed** and the **blemished** animal **shall be redeemed.** The *baraita* continues: If the **blemished** animal **was slaughtered** after it was redeemed, and this occurred **before the blood of the unblemished** animal **was sprinkled** upon the altar, then the meat of the blemished animal is **permitted.** But if it was slaughtered **after the blood of the unblemished** animal **was sprinkled** on the altar, it is **prohibited** to derive benefit from the blemished animal, as it has the status of a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another animal. **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, says: Even** if the **meat of the blemished** animal is already cooking **in the pot, and the blood of the unblemished** sin offering **was** then **sprinkled** on the altar, it is prohibited to derive benefit from the meat of the blemished animal, and it **goes to the place** designated **for burning** disqualified offerings, as it is considered a sin offering whose owner achieved atonement with another animal. Just as Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, prohibits the meat of a disqualified sin offering that was redeemed and slaughtered, he likewise prohibits one from flaying it from its feet.

The *braita* turns to the prohibition by *psulei hamukdashin* — a *korban* that had a *mum* and was redeemed. This case is more extreme than *bechor* because unlike a *bechor* (which is never technically redeemed, its *kedusha* simply being reconfigured), a redeemed *korban* has had its *kedusha* explicitly transferred to money. Why should it retain enough of its *korban* identity to prohibit *margling*?

Rav Chisda answers: it is Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon. The Gemara cites a *braita* that goes further than the *Mishnah* we have already encountered. The *braita* describes a case where someone had two *chataos* — one with a *mum* and one without — sanctified for the same sin. The unblemished one is brought, and the blemished one is redeemed. The *chachamim* themselves hold that if the blemished animal was slaughtered before the blood of the unblemished one was sprinkled, it is permitted — *muteres*. But once the blood of the unblemished one was sprinkled — *mishenizrak dama shel temima assura* — the blemished animal becomes forbidden.

Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon goes further: *afilu basar ba'alas mum b'kdeira* — even if the meat of the blemished animal is already cooking in the pot, once the second *korban*'s blood is sprinkled, the pot's contents must be taken out and burned. The animal retains its identity as a *chatas* so completely that achieving atonement through the second rendering it *chatas she'nitkapru ba'aleha b'acher* — and it goes to *beis hasreifa*.

Tosafos notes that this *braita* is more extreme than our *Mishnah*. In the *Mishnah*, Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon's ruling applied only before the first animal had been slaughtered. Here, even the *chachamim* partially agree, and Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon extends the ruling to the pot itself. Tosafos explains the difference: in the *Mishnah*, the animal first acquired a *mum* and was then redeemed and replaced — a process that more fully transferred its identity to another. By contrast, where both animals were sanctified from the outset for the same sin, the first animal never had anything "taken its place" in the same way; from the very beginning it shared the field with a second animal, and so it retains its *chatas* identity more tenaciously.

A practical question arises from the pot scenario: does the prohibition extend to the other food in the pot? The presumption would be yes — it is a matter of *nosein ta'am*, the absorbed flavor rendering the other contents forbidden as well.

### Reconciling Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב חִסְדָּא, לוֹקְמַהּ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי! דִּלְמָא כִּי אָמְרִי בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — בִּבְכוֹר, דִּקְדוּשְּׁתֵיהּ מֵרֶחֶם, אֲבָל פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין — לָא. וְלוֹקְמַאּ אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן! דִּלְמָא כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — בִּפְסוּלֵי מוּקְדָּשִׁין, דְּאַלִּימִי לְמִיתְפַּס פִּדְיוֹנָן, אֲבָל בִּבְכוֹר — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **And let Rav Ḥisda establish** both **this** *halakha* and **that** *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Beit Shammai.** Why does Rav Ḥisda establish the prohibition against flaying disqualified consecrated animals from the feet specifically in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon? Presumably, just as Beit Shammai prohibit flaying a firstborn from its feet, they also prohibit one from doing so to disqualified consecrated animals. The Gemara explains: **Perhaps when Beit Shammai said** that a firstborn animal retains its sanctity even after it is blemished, they said that only **with regard to a firstborn, whose sanctity** is attained **from the womb,** i.e., from birth. **But with regard to disqualified consecrated** animals, perhaps Beit Shammai do **not** hold that they retain their sanctity after they become blemished and are redeemed. The Gemara objects from the other angle: **But let** Rav Ḥisda **establish** both **this** *halakha* and **that** *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon.** Presumably, just as Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, prohibits one from flaying disqualified consecrated animals from the feet, he also prohibits one from doing so to firstborn animals. The Gemara explains: **Perhaps when Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, said** that the sanctity of a blemished consecrated animal remains even if it was already redeemed and is cooking in the pot, he said this only **with regard to disqualified consecrated** animals, **whose** sanctity is **strong** enough **to apply** to **the redemption** money paid for these animals. **But in** the case of a blemished **firstborn** animal, which cannot be redeemed, Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, does **not** hold that it retains its sanctity even after it is blemished.

The Gemara now asks why Rav Chisda cited separate authorities for the two prohibitions — Beis Shammai for *bechor* and Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon for *psulei hamukdashin* — rather than attributing both to a single source. Why not apply Beis Shammai's principle to *psulei hamukdashin* as well, or apply Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon to *bechor*?

The Gemara resists each consolidation. One cannot simply apply Beis Shammai's position to *psulei hamukdashin*: Beis Shammai may have held the *bechor* retains its *kedusha* specifically because *kedushato merachem* — its sanctity arises automatically at birth, without any human act of designation. More importantly, a *bechor* is never redeemed; its *kedusha* clings to it inherently. *Psulei hamukdashin*, by contrast, have been actively redeemed. The *kedusha* was transferred to money, and there is greater logical basis to say it no longer adheres to the animal. Beis Shammai might not extend their position this far.

Conversely, one cannot simply apply Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon to *bechor*. His rationale for saying a redeemed *korban* retains its identity is, if anything, a function of the *korban*'s *kedusha* being robust enough to transfer to money — *de'alimei le'mitfas pidyon*. A *kedusha* strong enough to "grab" and sanctify money demonstrates that it is a powerful form of sanctity, which is precisely what allows some of it to persist even after redemption. A *bechor*, whose *kedusha* cannot be transferred to money, may actually be weaker in this respect, and Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon might not extend his principle there.

Thus Rav Chisda requires two separate authorities, and neither can straightforwardly be extended to the other's domain.

### Higher Price Leniency

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן, לֵית לֵיהּ הָא דִּתְנַן: כׇּל פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִשְׁחָטִין בָּאִיטְלִיז וְנִמְכָּרִין בָּאִיטְלִיז וְנִשְׁקָלִין בְּלִיטְרָא? אַלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּשָׁרֵית לֵיהּ טָפֵי וְזָבֵין! אָמַר רַב מָרִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב כָּהֲנָא: מַה שֶׁמַּשְׁבִּיחַ בָּעוֹר — פּוֹגֵם בְּבָשָׂר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But does Rabbi Elazar, son of Rabbi Shimon, not hold** in accordance with **that which we learned** in a mishna (*Bekhorot* 31a): **All disqualified consecrated** animals that have been redeemed **may be slaughtered in the butchers' market and sold in the butchers' market,** where there are many buyers, **and** their meat is **weighed by the *litra*,** like non-sacred meat. **Apparently, once you permit** the sale of disqualified consecrated meat in the manner of non-sacred meat, one may **increase** the sale price **and sell** it at a greater profit. Since the Temple treasury stands to gain from such a sale, it is permitted to handle the disqualified consecrated meat in the manner of non-sacred meat. Here, too, as an animal that may be flayed from its feet would be sold for a higher price, the flaying from the feet of disqualified consecrated animals should be permitted, as the Temple treasury stands to gain from that sale. **Rav Mari, son of Rav Kahana, says** in response: **That which improves the hide damages the meat.** If one is careful to remove the entire hide without cutting it, he will inevitably cut away some of the meat, thereby lowering the proceeds from the meat. Consequently, the ability to flay a disqualified consecrated animal from its feet does not increase its overall sale price.

The Gemara raises a challenge from a separate *Mishnah*: *kol psulei hamukdashin nishchatin be'itliz, venimkarin be'itliz, venishkalin belitra* — all disqualified *korbanos* may be slaughtered in the meat market, sold in the meat market, and weighed out by the pound. This treats them essentially like ordinary commercial meat, with seemingly little regard for their former sanctified status. The reason is that since the animal must be redeemed based on its market value, we want to maximize that value by allowing it to be sold in the most commercially favorable way. If greater latitude in selling raises the price, the *hekdesh* treasury benefits more.

Given this rationale, why not also permit *margling* for the same reason — to raise the sale price?

Rav Mari the son of Rav Kahana answers: *ma shemashbiach ba'or pogem babasar* — whatever you gain in the value of the leather, you lose in the value of the meat, because the process of leg-based skinning inevitably cuts into and damages the flesh. There is no net commercial gain, so there is no basis for permitting it on those grounds.

### Avodah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין: מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה כְּעוֹבֵד עֲבוֹדָה בְּקָדָשִׁים. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר אָבִין אָמַר: גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יְגַדֵּל מֵהֶם עֲדָרִים עֲדָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **In the West,** Eretz Yisrael, **they said in the name of Rabbi Avin** another reason it is prohibited to flay disqualified consecrated animals from their feet: It is **because it appears as** though one is **performing labor with sacrificial** animals, which is prohibited. **Rabbi Yosei bar Avin says:** The prohibition against flaying disqualified consecrated animals from the feet is a rabbinic **decree, lest one** delay slaughtering the animals until he finds someone to purchase the hide, and in doing so, he will **raise many flocks** of disqualified consecrated animals, which will likely lead to a transgression of the prohibitions of shearing or working disqualified consecrated animals.

The scholars of the West (*Ma'arava*) offer a different answer in the name of Rav Avin: *mipnei shenireh ke'oved avodah bekodashim* — because it appears like performing labor with sanctified property. This is a qualitatively different prohibition than mere disrespect. Rashi explains that part of the *margling* process involved blowing into the animal's body (*mapuach*) to help separate the skin from the flesh — a procedure that somehow resembles performing *avodah* with sacred property, even after the animal has died.

A final answer is offered by Rav Yose bar Rebbi Avin: *gezeirah shema yigadel adarim adarim* — a rabbinic decree lest one breed the animals in herds. Since the leather market is less reliable and predictable than the meat market, allowing *margling* might tempt people to delay slaughter in order to sell the leather more advantageously. This delay risks the animal breeding, acquiring *rebbiya* complications, and multiplying the problems of managing sanctified animals. Therefore, the latitude granted for marketplace sale is not extended to this case.

The Gemara's analysis thus suggests that while the *Mishnah* initially framed the prohibition on *margling psulei hamukdashin* as connected to Rabbi Elazar b'Rebbi Shimon's view that the animal retains its *korban* identity, the final answers given — financial equivalence, resemblance to *avodah*, risk of breeding — do not necessarily require his position at all, and could apply universally.

## Scheming Around Bech (II)

### Fetus as Olah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כֵּיצַד מַעֲרִימִין עַל הַבְּכוֹר? מְבַכֶּרֶת שֶׁהָיְתָה מְעוּבֶּרֶת, אוֹמֵר: ״מַה שֶּׁבְּמֵעֶיהָ שֶׁל זוֹ אִם זָכָר — עוֹלָה״, יָלְדָה זָכָר — יִקְרַב עוֹלָה, ״וְאִם נְקֵבָה — זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים״, יָלְדָה נְקֵבָה — תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **How** may **one employ artifice to** circumvent the obligation to give **the firstborn** to the priest and utilize the animal for a different offering that he is obligated to bring? The owner approaches **an animal** that is going to **give birth to its firstborn** while **that** animal **was** still **pregnant,** and **says:** That **which is in the womb of this** animal, **if** it is **male,** is designated as a **burnt offering.** In that case, if the animal **gave birth to a male, it will be sacrificed as a burnt offering. And** in a case where he says: **If it is female,** it is designated as a **peace offering,** if the animal **gave birth to a female, it will be sacrificed as a peace offering.**

A new *perek* begins: *keitzad marimin* — how do you scheme? The specific topic is *keitzad marimin al habechor* — how does one maneuver regarding a *bechor*. The framing is important: this is not about avoiding the *kedusha* of *bechor* out of selfishness, but rather about the owner choosing to bring a *korban* himself rather than simply giving the animal to the *Kohen*. The question is whether that aspiration, and the creative halachic move that accomplishes it, is sanctioned.

The *Mishnah* presents the mechanism: *mevakeres shehoyso muveres* — an animal that is carrying its first offspring, while still pregnant. The owner declares: *mah shebibitna shel zo, im zachar, olah* — what is in this animal's womb: if it is male, let it be an *olah*. If it gives birth to a male, it is an *olah* — the owner brings it himself rather than presenting a *bechor* to the *Kohen*. This works because: (a) one can sanctify a fetus in the womb, as established earlier in the tractate; (b) *bechor* status does not attach until birth; therefore (c) the fetus's *kedusha* as an *olah*, conferred before birth, preempts the automatic *bechor* status that would otherwise arise at birth.

## Multiple Fetuses Cases

### Both Sexes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אִם זָכָר — עוֹלָה, וְאִם נְקֵבָה — זִבְחֵי שְׁלָמִים״, יָלְדָה זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה — הַזָּכָר יִקְרַב עוֹלָה, וְהַנְּקֵבָה תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים. יָלְדָה שְׁנֵי זְכָרִים — אֶחָד מֵהֶם יִקְרַב עוֹלָה, וְהַשֵּׁנִי יִמָּכֵר לְחַיָּיבֵי עוֹלָה, וְדָמָיו חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the owner says: **If** it is **male** it is designated as a **burnt offering, and if it is female** it is designated as a **peace offering,** and the animal **gave birth to a male and a female, the male will be sacrificed as a burnt offering and the female will be sacrificed as a peace offering.** If the animal **gave birth to two males, one of them will be sacrificed as a burnt offering and the second will be sold to** those **obligated** to bring **a burnt offering,** who will sacrifice it as a burnt offering; and the **money** received from **its** sale is **non-sacred.**

The *Mishnah* continues. If the owner said *im zachar olah v'im nekeivah shelamim* — if male let it be an *olah*, if female a *shelamim* — and the animal gave birth to *both* a male and a female: both conditions were simultaneously fulfilled. The *Mishnah* rules: *hazachar yikarev olah vehanekeiva tikarev shelamim* — the male becomes an *olah* and the female becomes a *shelamim*. There is no principle of "only one was intended"; both conditions were stated and both were met.

### Two Males

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָלְדָה שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת — אַחַת מֵהֶם תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּימָּכֵר לְחַיָּיבֵי שְׁלָמִים, וְדָמֶיהָ חוּלִּין. יָלְדָה טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **gave birth to two females, one of them will be sacrificed as a peace offering and the second will be sold to** those **obligated** to bring **a peace offering,** who will sacrifice it as a peace offering, and the **money** received from **its** sale is **non-sacred.** If the animal **gave birth to a *tumtum*,** whose gender is unknown, **or a hermaphrodite,** which has both male and female sexual organs, both of which are unfit for sacrifice, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: They are not imbued with sanctity.**

The more complex case: *yolda shnei zecharim* — the animal gave birth to twin males. The owner said "if it is a male" — in the singular. Only one was sanctified. Which one? The *Mishnah* rules: *echad meihem yikarev olah* — one of them is brought as an *olah*, and *hasheni yimmakher le-chayyavei olah v'damav chullin* — the second is sold to someone who is obligated to bring an *olah*, so that it will eventually reach the altar; but since it never actually received *kedusha*, the proceeds of that sale remain *chullin* and the owner may keep them.

This raises a question in the context of *bechor*: if there are two males and one is designated as an *olah* while the other is not, the first one born would normally be the *bechor*. If the owner gets to choose which of the twins is his *olah*, should he not be required to choose the first-born, lest the first-born be the non-sanctified one and thus a *bechor* owed to the *Kohen*? Furthermore, how does the owner have the right to choose at all? When he made his declaration, he said "the male" — but he did not know there would be two. There is no first or second from his perspective. The *Mishnah*'s grant of choice requires examination, and the Gemara will continue to explore this.

### Two Females

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָלְדָה שְׁתֵּי נְקֵבוֹת — אַחַת מֵהֶם תִּקְרַב שְׁלָמִים, וְהַשְּׁנִיָּה תִּימָּכֵר לְחַיָּיבֵי שְׁלָמִים, וְדָמֶיהָ חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **gave birth to two females, one of them will be sacrificed as a peace offering and the second will be sold to** those **obligated** to bring **a peace offering,** who will sacrifice it as a peace offering, and the **money** received from **its** sale is **non-sacred.**

The parallel case of *yalda shetei nekeivot* — twin females, where the owner declared "if female, let it be a *shelamim*" in the singular — is resolved identically: one is a *shelamim*, and the other is sold to someone who needs to bring a *shelamim*, with the proceeds being *chullin*. The same questions about the mechanism of choice apply here, though the *bechor* complication does not arise since females are not *bechoros*.

### Tumtum Androgynos

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָלְדָה טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** If the animal **gave birth to a *tumtum*,** whose gender is unknown, **or a hermaphrodite,** which has both male and female sexual organs, both of which are unfit for sacrifice, **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: They are not imbued with sanctity.**

If the animal gave birth to a *tumtum* (genitalia covered, biological sex indeterminate from appearance) or an *androgynos* (possessing both male and female characteristics), Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules: *ein kedushah chalah aleihem* — no *kedusha* takes effect on them at all, not even on a *safek* basis. The *androgynos* case is perhaps more intuitive: it is neither clearly male nor clearly female. As for *tumtum* — whose sex is indeterminate externally but may well be one or the other in reality — Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position is that the owner's conditional declaration was directed toward a *zachar vadai* or *nekeivah vadais*, a definite male or definite female, and did not contemplate a case of unresolved classification. The Gemara will develop this further.

## Scheming Around Bech (II)

### Blemish Before Birth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: מוּתָּר לְהַטִּיל מוּם בִּבְכוֹר קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֵּצֵא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם. תְּנַן: אוֹמֵר אָדָם ״מַה שֶּׁבְּמֵעֶיהָ שֶׁל זוֹ עוֹלָה״ — עוֹלָה אִין, שְׁלָמִים לָא, וְאַתְּ אָמְרַתְּ דְּמָצֵית מַפְקְעַתְּ לֵיהּ מִקְּדוּשְּׁתַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says:** It is permitted **to inflict a blemish upon a firstborn before it left** the womb and entered **into the air of the world,** as it has not yet become sanctified as a firstborn. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **We learned** in the mishna: **A person says** about an animal that is going to give birth to its firstborn: **That which is in the womb of this** animal, if it is male, is designated as a **burnt offering.** This indicates that as **a burnt offering, yes,** one may designate it in this manner, as the sanctity of a burnt offering is more stringent than that of a firstborn but as **a peace offering, no. And** yet **you say that one may** entirely **abrogate** the animal's **sanctity** by inflicting a blemish upon it before it is born.

Rav Yehudah introduces a different strategy: *muttar le-hattil mum b'vechor kodem she-yatzah le-avir ha-olam* — it is permitted to inflict a *mum* on a *bechor* before it has fully emerged from the womb. The practical mechanism is to make a blemish on the animal (cutting the lip, the ear, or the like) while the head is still emerging, before enough of the body has exited to constitute halachic birth. Since the prohibition against inflicting a *mum* on a *bechor* only applies once it is born, this maneuver is technically prior to the onset of the prohibition. The result: the animal is born with a *mum*, cannot be brought as a *korban*, and is given to the *Kohen* for ordinary consumption.

### Only Raise Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַב יְהוּדָה: הָנֵי מִילֵּי — בִּזְמַן שֶׁבֵּית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ קַיָּים, כִּי קָאָמֵינָא אֲנָא — בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לְהַקְרָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda** could have **said to you: That statement,** that one is permitted to consecrate a firstborn fetus only with a more stringent sanctity, applies **when the Temple is standing** and offerings are sacrificed upon the altar. By contrast, when **I say** that one is permitted to inflict a blemish upon a firstborn and abrogate the firstborn's sanctity, I am referring to **today, when** offerings **are not able to be sacrificed,** and therefore the animal cannot be consumed until it becomes blemished.

The Gemara immediately challenges this in light of our *Mishnah*: *omer adam mah she-bim'ei behemah zu olah — olah in, shelamim lo*. The *Mishnah* permitted the scheme of declaring the fetus an *olah*, but did not permit declaring it a *shelamim*. Why not? A *shelamim* can be female just as much as a *bechor* can — there is no sex-based reason to exclude it. The Gemara explains: the *Mishnah*'s implicit principle is that only a scheme that *raises* the sanctity of the animal is permitted. Declaring the fetus an *olah* means the entire animal is consumed on the altar — a higher use than *bechor* status, where the blood and certain parts go to the altar but the meat is eaten by the *Kohen*. A *shelamim*, by contrast, is comparable in structure to a *bechor*: the blood goes on the altar, the *Kohanim* and the owner eat the meat. There is no elevation, merely a substitution that benefits the owner. Such a scheme is not sanctioned.

This is the principle extracted from the *Mishnah*'s formulation: *keitzad marimin* encompasses only those maneuvers that genuinely elevate the *korban*'s status, not those that merely redirect it for the owner's benefit.

### Blemish vs. Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אִי בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: נִגְזַר, דִּלְמָא נָפֵיק רוּב רֹאשׁוֹ וְקָשָׁדֵי בֵּיהּ מוּמָא. וְאֵימָא: הָכִי נָמֵי! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי — הָא עֲדִיפָא יַתִּירָא, מִדְּאָתֵי בֵּיהּ לִידֵי גִּיזָּה וַעֲבוֹדָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If** Rav Yehuda is referring only to **today, what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? Isn't it obvious? The Gemara answers that Rav Yehuda's statement is necessary, **lest you say: Let us issue a decree** against inflicting a blemish on a firstborn fetus, **lest most of the** fetus's **head emerge** from the womb, which is when the sanctity of the firstborn takes effect, **and** then **one inflicts a blemish upon** the animal. That would constitute the unlawful infliction of a blemish upon a sacred animal. Rav Yehuda therefore teaches us that such a decree was not issued. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** one can **say** that **indeed,** a decree should be issued prohibiting inflicting a blemish on a fetus due to that concern. The Gemara explains: **Even so,** inflicting the blemish on the fetus **is preferable** to leaving it alone, despite that concern, **as** otherwise one might **come to** transgress the prohibitions against **shearing** the firstborn **or working with it.** It is prohibited to work or shear a firstborn, even if it is blemished. If the firstborn is born blemished, it may be slaughtered and eaten immediately. By contrast, if it is unblemished, then one must wait for it to become blemished, and there is a concern that one might work or shear the animal in the meantime.

Rav Yehudah's permission to inflict a *mum* before birth might seem to contradict this principle. After all, a *bechor* with a *mum* is worse than a whole *bechor* — it cannot be brought to the altar at all. How is making a *mum* an "elevation"?

Rav Yehudah clarifies: *hani mili bizman sheBeis HaMikdash kayam* — the *Mishnah*'s rule (permitting only upward schemes) applied when the *Beis HaMikdash* stood and the *bechor* could realistically be brought as a *korban*. In that context, the only permissible maneuver was the one that sent the entire animal to the altar. But Rav Yehudah is speaking *bizman hazeh* — nowadays, when there is no *Beis HaMikdash* and no *korban* can be brought. In that circumstance, there is no possibility of bringing the *bechor* as a *korban* in any case. The better outcome, practically speaking, is to resolve the *bechor* situation efficiently. A *bechor* left intact without a *mum* will sit around for years — presenting repeated opportunities for inadvertent misuse (shearing, working, riding) — until it naturally develops a blemish. Inflicting a *mum* before birth removes this entire gauntlet of potential violations and allows the animal to be given promptly to the *Kohen* for consumption. Nothing is lost and much is gained.

The Gemara raises one concern about this leniency: *dilma nafik rov rosho v'kashadi bei muma* — one might worry that in practice, by the time you try to inflict the blemish, the majority of the head may have already emerged, making the animal halachically born and the infliction a prohibited act. Rav Yehudah responds: even granting that concern, the broader calculus favors permitting this. The risk of accidentally inflicting the *mum* a moment too late is outweighed by the ongoing risk of misuse that comes with raising a *bechor* to adulthood. Big-picture prevention of transgression justifies the practice.

### Selling to Non-Jew

Tosafos raises a sharper challenge: why does the Gemara discuss making a *mum* before birth at all, given the well-established practice of selling part of the mother to a non-Jew? By selling, say, the mother's ear to a non-Jew before the animal's birth, the offspring is not considered fully born to a Jewish-owned animal, and the *bechor* status does not attach. This practice was common in the *Rishonim*'s era. Why is it not mentioned here as the preferred solution?

Tosafos answers (*veomrim Rabbeinu Tam*, in the middle of his comment): nowadays the world's practice is indeed to sell a portion of the mother to a non-Jew (*limkor ktzat min ha'em*) in order to exempt the offspring from *bechorah*, and this is appropriate — provided the transaction is executed with a proper *kinyan*. The reason this is preferred over the *mum* strategy is stated at the end of the Tosafos: *nicha lan tefei lemochro l'ovdei kochavim milhatil bo mum kodem yetzi'at rubo* — it is better to sell to a non-Jew than to risk inflicting the *mum* incorrectly, after the majority of the head has already emerged. The concern about mistiming the blemish is real, and the simpler route of the sale sidesteps it entirely.

One might note that the owner is also economically motivated to avoid giving the animal to the *Kohen*, and economic pressures likely played a role in the development of the sale practice. However, the Gemara itself would not have sanctioned a purely self-interested scheme; the *Gemara*'s analysis shows that these maneuvers are only tolerated when they either elevate the *korban*'s use or, in our day, prevent ongoing halachic risk. The sale practice, as Tosafos presents it, is justified on those grounds — it prevents the misuse hazard — though one does observe that this parallels discussions of other rabbinic mechanisms that have similarly been shaped in part by real-world pressures, as with the sale of *chametz* before *Pesach*.

## Mother Has Kedusha

### Mother is Chatas

The *Mishnah*'s final clause opens a new scenario: *seifa atya le'veheima de'hekdesh* — the end of the *Mishnah* addresses a case where the mother herself is a sanctified animal. In this configuration, the concern is not *bechor* at all. Rather, the concern is the law of *vlad chatas* — the offspring of a *chatas*, which, like five other categories of *chataos*, must be left to die. If the mother is a *chatas*, the fetus born from her would normally become a *vlad chatas* at the moment of birth, inheriting that derivative status and facing the same fate.

Rashi explains (drawing on the previous *perek*) that the best example here is indeed where the mother is a *chatas* rather than an *olah* (which is male and cannot be the mother in this discussion). The owner wants to prevent the fetus from acquiring the *vlad chatas* status and dying. So what can he do?

### Prevent Derivative Kedusha

The mechanism the *Mishnah* contemplates is sanctifying the fetus in the womb as a *shelamim* before it is born. The premise is that the derivative *kedusha* the fetus would receive from its mother — the *vlad chatas* status — takes effect only at the moment of birth. Therefore, sanctifying it as a *shelamim* before birth preempts that derivative *kedusha*. The fetus is born already possessing its own *kedusha* as a *shelamim*, which prevents the *chatas* identity from attaching.

Why is *shelamim* appropriate here, even though the Gemara earlier ruled that a *shelamim* scheme would not be permitted because it fails to raise the status? The answer is that in this context one is not *lowering* the status — one is preventing the animal from being condemned to death. Allowing it to be brought as a *korban shelamim* is plainly better than having it die. There is no issue of subverting an upward trajectory; there is a genuine elevation from certain destruction to a functioning *korban*.

### Two Males Mother Holy

The *Mishnah* then addresses the twin case: *yalda shnei zecharim* from a sanctified mother. If two males are born, one of them has been pre-sanctified as a *shelamim* (or *olah*) by the owner's declaration — and the other? The Gemara explores whether the second should automatically receive the *kedusha* of the mother. *Amri*: if we are in the case of *behemah dehekdesh* — where the mother is sanctified — and the owner has designated one fetus as an *olah*, that one is an *olah*, but the other should inherit the mother's *kedusha* via the rule of *bikdusha d'imeih*.

However, the Gemara clarifies: *seifa asanis lebehemas chullin* — by the time the *Mishnah* discusses the twin-male scenario in detail, it has reverted to the case of a non-sanctified (*chullin*) mother, where the concern is *bechor* status and not derivative *chatas* identity. The Gemara will continue to unpack the distinctions between these two underlying scenarios — the sanctified mother and the ordinary mother — as the discussion continues on the following daf.
