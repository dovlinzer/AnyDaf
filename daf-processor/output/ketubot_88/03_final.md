# Ketubot 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Modeh B'Miktzat

### Oath Cases Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ סָבַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְמֵימַר שְׁבוּעָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא — דְּקָא טָעֵן מָאתַיִם וְקָא מוֹדֵה לֵיהּ בְּמֵאָה, הָוְיָא לֵיהּ הוֹדָאָה בְּמִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה, וְכׇל הַמּוֹדֶה בְּמִקְצָת הַטַּעֲנָה — יִשָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rami bar Ḥama thought to say** that the oath of a woman who vitiates her marriage contract is **an oath** required **by Torah** law, which is the oath of one who makes a partial admission, **as** the husband **claims** that he paid her **two hundred and she concedes to him with regard to one hundred. This is a partial admission of the claim, and** the principle is that **whoever admits to part of a claim must take an oath** according to Torah law to receive the remaining amount.

The daf begins in the middle of 87b, amid a discussion of the various circumstances in which a woman must take an oath in order to collect her *ketubah*. The Gemara enumerates several distinct cases, each generating its own type of *shvua*, and it is worth pausing to understand what is at stake before working through them one by one.

### Partial Payment Concession

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁתֵּי תְשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר: חֲדָא — דְּכׇל הַנִּשְׁבָּעִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִשְׁבָּעִין וְלֹא מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְהִיא נִשְׁבַּעַת וְנוֹטֶלֶת. וְעוֹד: אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There are **two answers in the matter,** in refutation of your argument: **One** response is **that anyone who** is obligated to **take an oath** that is enumerated **in the Torah takes an oath and does not pay.** By Torah law, one takes an oath only to exempt himself from payment, **and** in this case **she takes an oath and takes** her money. **And furthermore,** there is a principle that **one does not take an oath with regard to a denial of a lien on land.** The oaths of the Torah apply only to moveable property, not land. This means that if a claim involves a lien on land of any form, the oath of a partial admission does not apply, and a marriage contract includes a lien on land.

The first case is where the woman herself concedes that part of the *ketubah* has already been paid. This might seem like a straightforward instance of *modeh b'miktzat* — the well-known biblical rule that one who partially admits a claim must take an oath — but it is not quite that. The classical *modeh b'miktzat* applies to the party from whom money is being extracted, whereas here it is the collecting party — the woman — who is making the partial admission. The technical conditions for the biblical oath therefore do not apply.

### D'Rabbanan Oath Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מִדְּרַבָּנַן, דְּפָרַע — דָּיֵיק, דְּמִיפְּרַע — לָא דָּיֵיק, וּרְמוֹ רַבָּנַן שְׁבוּעָה עֲלַהּ כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתִידּוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** This oath is **by rabbinic** law. It was instituted because the one **who pays is precise** and recalls that he paid his debt, whereas the one **who was paid is not precise.** When the husband claims to have paid her, he remembers clearly what happened, **and** therefore **the Sages imposed** the obligation of **an oath upon her, so that she should be precise** and remember exactly what occurred.

The oath imposed in this case is accordingly a *shvua d'rabbanan*. The concern driving it is that she may have forgotten: the fact that part of the debt has been paid creates a real possibility that she has lost track of the full account. The oath functions as a mechanism to force her to review the matter carefully and be doubly certain before collecting. The rabbis instituted this *shvua* precisely because the partial payment introduces reasonable doubt, and the gravity of the oath is meant to prompt genuine recollection.

## Ed Echad & Shvua

### One Witness Testifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵד אֶחָד מְעִידָהּ שֶׁהִיא פְּרוּעָה. סְבַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְמֵימַר שְׁבוּעָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא יָקוּם עֵד אֶחָד בְּאִישׁ לְכׇל עָוֹן וּלְכׇל חַטָּאת״. לְכׇל עָוֹן וּלְכׇל חַטָּאת הוּא דְּאֵינוֹ קָם, אֲבָל קָם הוּא לִשְׁבוּעָה. וְאָמַר מָר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁהַשְּׁנַיִם מְחַיְּיבִין אוֹתוֹ מָמוֹן — אֶחָד מְחַיְּיבוֹ שְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if **one witness testifies that** the marriage contract **was paid** she must take an oath. **Rami bar Ḥama thought to say** that this is **an oath** required **by Torah** law, **as it is written: "One witness shall not rise up against a man for any iniquity, or for any sin"** (Deuteronomy 19:15). From here it is inferred: **It is for any iniquity or for any sin that he may not rise up,** i.e., the testimony of one witness is not enough for these purposes, **but he may rise up for an oath. And the Master said:** In **any place,** i.e., situation, **where two** witnesses are able to **deem one liable** to pay **money,** the testimony of **one** witness **obligates him to take an oath.**

The second case shifts the dynamic considerably. Here, the woman comes to collect, the husband claims he has paid, and she denies it. Ordinarily she would prevail: she holds a *shtar*, and he has no receipt. But suppose the husband produces a single witness — an *ed echad* — who testifies that the *ketubah* has in fact been paid. She must now take a *shvua* to contradict that witness. This too has the structural feel of an oath rooted in biblical law.

### Rami Bar Chama Position

Rami Bar Chama indeed argued that this constitutes a *shvua d'oraita* — a biblical oath. His reasoning proceeds from the verse in Deuteronomy.

### Deut. Source & Ed Echad

The verse states: *lo yakum ed echad b'ish l'chol avon u'l'chol chatat* — one witness shall not rise up against a person for any sin or transgression. The Gemara derives from this that while an *ed echad* cannot prevail outright in court and impose liability, he does have the legal force to compel an oath from the opposing party. This interpretation is the foundation of the entire institution of the *ed echad* demanding a *shvua*.

### Witness Demands Oath

The operative principle is that an *ed echad* does not win the case — he cannot substitute for the two witnesses required to obligate payment — but he destabilizes the status quo enough that the other party cannot simply ignore him. Without any witness, the default rule of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah* governs: the one seeking payment bears the burden of proof. An *ed echad* disrupts that default, and the only way to neutralize him and restore the status quo is to take an oath. The oath does not itself create a new obligation; it dissolves the threat posed by the single witness, returning the parties to where they would have stood without him.

### Shnayim/Echad Rule

This logic is crystallized in a general principle: *kol makom she-shnayim m'chayvin oto mamon, echad m'chaivo shvua* — wherever two witnesses would obligate payment, one witness obligates an oath. It bears noting that this principle applies specifically in monetary matters. In the domain of *issurim*, a single witness is regarded as entirely without legal weight. But in monetary disputes, a lone witness has real, if limited, force: not to compel payment, but to compel an oath.

Applying this to the *ketubah* case: she holds a *shtar* — which functions as two testimonies — and he has one witness who says it is paid. Two witnesses would have cancelled her *shtar* entirely. One witness demands that she take an oath to neutralize his testimony, after which the *shtar* reasserts itself and she collects. The structure is symmetrical with the classical *ed echad* scenario. Rami Bar Chama's position that this is a biblical oath therefore appears quite reasonable.

## Rava's Two Reasons

### Oath Taker Benefits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, שְׁתֵּי תְּשׁוּבוֹת בַּדָּבָר. חֲדָא: דְּכׇל הַנִּשְׁבָּעִין שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה נִשְׁבָּעִין וְלֹא מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְהִיא נִשְׁבַּעַת וְנוֹטֶלֶת. וְעוֹד: אֵין נִשְׁבָּעִין עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** There are **two answers in the matter,** in refutation of your argument: **One** response is **that anyone who** is obligated to **take an oath** that is enumerated **in the Torah takes an oath and does not pay.** By Torah law, one takes an oath only to exempt himself from payment, **and** in this case **she takes an oath and takes** her money. **And furthermore,** there is a principle that **one does not take an oath with regard to a denial of a lien on land.**

Rava, however, rejects this analysis and offers two distinct reasons why the oath imposed here cannot be biblical. His first objection goes to the heart of how biblical oaths function: *kol hanishba'in she-ba-Torah nishba'in v'lo m'shalmin* — every biblical oath in the Torah is one where the oath-taker swears and thereby avoids paying. The paradigm of the biblical *shvua* is defensive: I am accused of owing you money, you have an *ed echad* backing your claim, and I take an oath to neutralize that witness and establish that I owe nothing. The oath is a shield.

### She Swears & Collects

In the *ketubah* case, however, the structure is inverted. The woman is not defending herself against a demand for payment; she is the one seeking to collect. She swears, and then she *takes* money — *nishba'at v'notlet*. That is the opposite of the biblical pattern. The oath here is a sword, not a shield, and the Torah's framework for the *shvua d'oraita* simply does not encompass this configuration. To be sure, the underlying logic is structurally similar: the *ed echad* creates a complication that must be neutralized, and the oath restores the status quo — but the status quo here is that the *shtar* entitles her to collect, not that she is exempt from paying. Biblically, one takes an oath to avoid paying; one does not take an oath in order to collect.

### Karka Lien Issue

Rava's second reason is equally dispositive. A *shtar* with a *ketubah* carries *shibbud karka'ot* — liens on land. When the dispute concerns a document that creates encumbrances on real property, there is a general rule that oaths are not taken with respect to the denial of *shibud karka'ot*. Since the *ketubah* is precisely such a document, the oath cannot be biblical on this ground as well.

### D'Rabbanan to Appease

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: מִדְּרַבָּנַן, כְּדֵי לְהָפִיס דַּעְתּוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** That oath was instituted **by rabbinic** law, **in order to put the husband's mind at ease.** Since a witness contradicts her claim, the Sages imposed an oath upon her so that the husband would be sure that he is not giving away his money for no reason.

With both biblical rationales eliminated, Rava concludes: this oath is purely rabbinic, instituted *kedei le-hafis da'ato shel ba'al* — in order to appease the husband. The husband is agitated; he has a witness who he believes confirms that he paid. The *shvua* is designed to calm him. One might fairly ask how an oath she takes really calms a husband who is convinced he is telling the truth and has a witness to back him up — the oath might strike him as just another layer of her alleged deception. The question is a real one, and the Gemara's choice of this rationale over more obvious alternatives deserves attention.

### Oath & Truthfulness

The more obvious explanation would be that the *ed echad* creates an objective concern that she may not be telling the truth, and the oath addresses that concern by making her formally accountable before God. But the Gemara pointedly does not say this. The reason, it seems, connects to a deep structural tension in the institution of the oath: if someone is willing to steal, will they really hesitate to swear falsely? The Gemara in Bava Metzia raises precisely this question — *chashud al mammon chashud al shvuasah* — and if the answer is that a person who steals will also swear falsely, then the oath does not actually address the concern that she is lying. It only functions as a deterrent if we assume that she is not an outright thief, perhaps that she has rationalized her position or made an honest error.

In the first case — where she conceded partial payment — that assumption was entirely plausible: maybe she forgot, maybe she lost track of the account. The oath prompts recollection. But in this case, where the dispute is total — he says he paid everything, she says he paid nothing — it is not plausible that she simply forgot the entire payment. So the honest-error explanation does not work here. And since the Gemara is implicitly reluctant to say that an oath deters an outright liar, it reaches instead for the relational explanation: it appeases the husband. The function of the oath, on this reading, is not evidentiary but social — it is designed to manage the interpersonal tension rather than to resolve a factual question.

## Biblical Oath Strategy

### Husband's Clever Move

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: אִי פִּיקֵּחַ הוּא, מַיְיתֵי לַהּ לִידֵי שְׁבוּעָה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא. יָהֵיב לַהּ כְּתוּבְּתַהּ בְּאַפֵּי חַד סָהֲדָא, וְסָמֵיךְ סָהֲדָא קַמָּא אַסָּהֲדָא בָּתְרָא, וּמוֹקֵים לְהוּ לְהָנָךְ קַמָּאֵי בְּמִלְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** **If** the husband **is perspicacious,** he can **induce her to** become obligated to take **an oath by Torah** law even in a case where only one witness saw the payment of the marriage contract, as follows: **He** now **gives her** the payment of **her entire marriage contract in the presence of one witness, and joins the first witness to the last witness,** so that there are now two witnesses to the payment of the entire marriage contract. **And** then, **he establishes this first** payment, about which the first witness had testified, **as a loan,** and claims payment of that sum from her, supported by the testimony of the first witness. With regard to this claim, her oath would serve the purpose of exempting her from payment, and it is not connected with a lien on land. Therefore, the witness can obligate her in an oath by Torah law.

The Gemara now introduces a striking practical turn. Rav Pappa observes: *i pike'ach hu, mati lah liydei shvuah d'oraita* — if the husband is clever, he can maneuver the situation so that she ends up taking a biblical oath rather than a rabbinic one.

### Rashi on Weightiness

Before examining the strategy, the Gemara implicitly raises the question of why this matters. Rashi explains: a *shvua d'oraita* is qualitatively different from a *shvua d'rabbanan*. The biblical oath is pronounced using God's name — or at minimum a divine epithet such as *Elokim* or *Shaddai* — and is administered while the swearer physically holds a *Sefer Torah*. The verse *lo yenakeh Hashem es asher yisa shemo la-shav* — God will not absolve one who takes His name in vain — gives the biblical oath a metaphysical weight that a rabbinic oath lacks.

### Curse vs. Oath

A *shvua d'rabbanan*, by contrast, is in essence *klala b'alma* — merely a curse, an invocation of ill fortune upon oneself if one is lying. The psychological and spiritual weight is considerably diminished. The husband, who is convinced he is in the right, has a strong incentive to force the more solemn form of the oath, which may either prompt her to recant or — should she proceed — carry far graver consequences.

### Double Payment Tactic

The husband's strategy, as the Gemara describes it, proceeds as follows: since he will likely have to pay out the *ketubah* anyway once she takes the rabbinic oath and collects, he should go ahead and pay her now — in front of a different witness, call him *Shimon* — even though he already paid her earlier (in front of the original witness, *Reuven*). He is paying her a second time, but calculating that the expenditure is worth the legal position it creates.

### Combining Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: הֵיאַךְ סָמֵיךְ סָהֲדָא קַמָּא אַסָּהֲדָא בָּתְרָא? אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: יָהֵיב לַהּ כְּתוּבְּתַהּ בְּאַפֵּי סָהֲדָא קַמָּא וְסָהֲדָא בָּתְרָא וּמוֹקֵים לְה[וּ] לְהָנָךְ קַמָּאֵי בְּהַלְוָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, objects to this: How can he join the first witness to the last witness** when their testimonies do not refer to the same action? **Rather, Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi, said: He** now **gives her** the payment of **her entire marriage contract in the presence of** both **the first witness and the last witness. And** then, **he establishes this first** payment, about which the first witness had testified, **as a loan,** and claims payment of that sum from her, supported by the testimony of the first witness.

Having now paid her twice, he brings the two witnesses together — *Reuven* and *Shimon* — and confronts her: I have paid you the *ketubah*, I have two witnesses. She cannot deny the May payment because it is proven by both witnesses. Now he turns around and says: but what about the money I gave you in January? That was a loan. You owe me that money back. She denies the loan. He produces *Reuven*, who witnessed the January payment. Now the situation has been restructured: he is the one seeking to recover money, she is the one denying it, and he has an *ed echad* — the classic configuration of the biblical oath. She must swear a *shvua d'oraita* to get out of paying him.

The challenge with this scheme, however, is the problem of combining *Reuven* and *Shimon*: they witnessed different events at different times. Using *Reuven* first to establish that the *ketubah* was paid in January, and then turning around and using *Reuven* to claim that the January payment was actually a loan, creates an internal contradiction. The same hundred dollars cannot simultaneously be *ketubah* payment and a loan, and the same witness cannot serve both characterizations.

### Rav Sheshes Fix

Rav Sheshes, son of Rav Idi, therefore simplifies the scheme: in May, before making the second payment, bring both *Reuven* and *Shimon* together to witness it. The husband says to *Reuven*: come with *Shimon* and watch me pay her now. Now the May payment is unambiguously proven by two witnesses, and it stands on its own without any need to import *Reuven*'s January testimony into the question of whether the *ketubah* was discharged. The January payment, witnessed by *Reuven* alone, can then be characterized purely as a loan, and *Reuven* is not being asked to play contradictory roles.

### Two Ketubos Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אָשֵׁי, אַכַּתִּי יְכוֹלָה לְמֵימַר: שְׁתֵּי כְּתוּבּוֹת הֲוַאי! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הוּא דְּמוֹדַע לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi objects to this:** The woman **can still say: I had two marriage contracts.** She can claim that he wrote two marriage contracts and she collected the payments at two separate points in time, but there was never a loan. **Rather, Rav Ashi said: It** is possible for him to obligate her to take an oath if **he informs** the two witnesseses that on this occasion he is paying her for the one marriage contract that he wrote. She is then unable to claim that it was a different marriage contract, and he can compel her to take an oath by Torah law about the first payment, which is now established as a loan.

Rav Ashi, however, raises a further objection: she can simply claim *bei kesuvos havai* — that there were two separate *ketubot*. She argues: you loved me so much that you wrote me one *ketubah* for a certain sum and another *ketubah* for the same sum. You paid off the first *ketubah* in January and the second in May. There was no loan; both payments discharged *ketubah* obligations. And since *Reuven* acknowledges that the January payment occurred in a *ketubah* context, he effectively corroborates her version: when asked whether there was any discussion of what the January money was for, *Reuven* will confirm it was a *ketubah* payment. She therefore owes the husband nothing and need not swear.

Crucially, she need not produce documentation of two *ketubot*. The evidentiary burden remains on him: he is the one seeking to extract money from her, and absent a *shtar* evidencing the loan, his only leverage is the *ed echad*. If that *ed echad* acknowledges that the January payment was presented in a *ketubah* context, it no longer functions as testimony of a loan, and his entire scheme collapses.

### Witness Confidence

The Gemara's resolution is that before bringing *Reuven* to witness the May payment, the husband takes *Reuven* into his confidence. He explains: look, you and I both know the January payment was the genuine *ketubah* payment. I am being forced to pay her again in May solely because she is denying the first payment and I do not have sufficient witnesses to prove it. This May payment is not a second *ketubah*; it is a forced repayment of the original one. *Reuven* now understands the full picture, and when the question of two *ketubot* arises, *Reuven* is in a position to clarify what he actually witnessed and understood.

### Rarity Assumption

What gives this resolution its legal traction is the sheer rarity of two *ketubot*. It is an extraordinary circumstance for a husband to write two separate *ketubot* for the same wife. Given that rarity, and given that *Reuven* — armed with the husband's explanation — can testify to his understanding of the January payment's purpose, the bet din has ample basis to be skeptical of the two-*ketubah* defense. The implausibility of the claim, combined with *Reuven*'s informed testimony, is sufficient to recharacterize the January payment as a debt and proceed to the *shvua d'oraita*.

The calculus for the husband is ultimately sound: if she would have taken the *shvua d'rabbanan* and collected anyway, he loses the *ketubah* sum regardless. The double-payment scheme costs him the same amount in the worst case, but in the best case — if she refuses to swear the more solemn *shvua d'oraita* — she repays the January sum and he breaks even. He stands to gain and cannot lose more than he already would have.

## Collecting from Heirs

### Dead Husband Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. תְּנַן הָתָם: וְכֵן הַיְּתוֹמִים לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if a woman comes to claim her marriage contract **from liened property** that had been sold to a third party, she must first take an oath. **We learned** in a mishna **there** (*Shevuot* 45a): **And similarly, orphans can collect** payment **only by** means of **an oath.**

The third scenario in which a woman must take an oath to collect her *ketubah* is where the husband has died and she is collecting from his heirs. The underlying logic is consistent with what has already been established: the husband is no longer present to assert a counterclaim, to produce a receipt, or to defend himself against her demand. In his absence — now permanent — she must take an oath that the *ketubah* has not already been paid.

### Shvuot Mishnah

The Gemara adduces a parallel from a *mishnah* in Shvuot: *v'chen hayetomim lo yifre'u ella bishvuah* — heirs of a creditor may likewise only collect on a debt by means of an oath. This provides a structural precedent: when the collecting party is not the original claimant, or when the debtor is no longer present to contest the claim, an oath is required.

### Why Heirs Swear

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאן? אִילֵימָא מִלֹּוֶה — הַשְׁתָּא אֲבִיהֶן שָׁקֵיל בְּלָא שְׁבוּעָה, וְאִינְהוּ בִּשְׁבוּעָה?! אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: וְכֵן הַיְּתוֹמִים מִן הַיְּתוֹמִים — לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From whom** can the orphans collect payment only by means of an oath? **If we say** that they can collect payment only with an oath **from one who borrowed** money from their father, then it is difficult to understand how this can be so. **Now,** can it be that **their father,** the lender, had the right to **take** payment from the borrower **without an oath** by relying on the document, **and they,** the orphans, with regard to whom the Sages were lenient, can claim the loan only **by** means of **an oath? Rather, this is what** the mishna is **saying: And similarly, the orphans** of the lender who come to collect payment from **the orphans** of the borrower **can collect only by** means of **an oath.**

The Gemara immediately raises a difficulty. If the creditor himself could collect the debt without taking any oath — simply by producing the *shtar* and asserting that it has not been paid — why should his heirs, who have inherited that same *shtar*, be required to swear? They are in the same position he was; let them simply assert, as he would have, that to the best of their knowledge the debt has not been discharged, and collect.

### Debtor Dead Principle

The answer is that the *mishnah* in Shvuot is not discussing heirs collecting from the living debtor; it is discussing heirs collecting from *the debtor's heirs*. When the debtor is alive, he can defend himself — produce evidence of payment, dispute the amount, or make any other argument. The creditor's claim stands against the debtor's rebuttal, and no oath is needed. But when the debtor is dead and his heirs are the respondents, the debtor can no longer defend himself. In that circumstance, whoever is collecting — whether the original creditor or his heirs — must swear. The oath fills the void left by the debtor's absence and ensures that the claim is not collected without the check of a solemn affirmation.

### Heirs' Statement Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְרִיקָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִים: אָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא ״לָוִיתִי וּפָרַעְתִּי״, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ: אָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא ״לֹא לָוִיתִי״ — אַף בִּשְׁבוּעָה לֹא יִפָּרְעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zerika said** that **Rav Yehuda said: It was taught** that those who take an oath can collect a debt from orphans **only if** the borrower's **orphans said:** Our **father said to us: I borrowed and repaid. However,** if the orphans **said:** Our **father said to us: I did not borrow,** then **they may not collect** from the orphans **even** with **an oath.**

Rav Zerika, citing Rav Yehuda, adds an important qualification. The oath requirement applies specifically when the heirs of the deceased debtor say: our father told us that he borrowed the money and paid it back. In that case — where they make a positive claim that the debt was discharged — the collecting party must indeed swear. Alternatively, if the heirs say nothing and simply do not know the status of the debt, the oath still applies, because the debtor is unavailable to confirm or deny.

### Complete Denial Rule

But what if the heirs claim: our father told us he never borrowed this money at all? In that case, one might think that the creditor cannot collect even with an oath, since the heirs are denying the existence of the debt entirely — and the creditor has no one to contradict. The Gemara presents this as one possible reading: if they deny the debt wholesale, perhaps even the *shtar* cannot overcome a complete denial.

### Rav Ashi Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא, אַדְּרַבָּה: כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״לֹא לָוִיתִי״ — כְּאוֹמֵר ״לֹא פָּרַעְתִּי״ דָּמֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rava objects to this: On the contrary,** there is a principle in the *halakhot* of claims that **anyone who says: I did not borrow is considered like one who says: I did not repay.** Therefore, when there is evidence that he did borrow, he must pay the entire amount without the lender having to take an oath.

Rav Ashi objects that this is precisely backwards. If the heirs say their father never borrowed the money, that very claim implies he never paid it back either — *kol omer lo lovisi k'omer lo parati dami*, one who says "I never borrowed" is in the same position as one who says "I never repaid." And the creditor has a *shtar* proving the loan took place. The heirs' denial of the loan does not override the documentary evidence.

### Heirs Say Father Paid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִתְּמַר, הָכִי אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב זְרִיקָא אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִים: אָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא ״לָוִיתִי וּפָרַעְתִּי״, אֲבָל אָמְרוּ: אָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא ״לֹא לָוִיתִי״ — נִפְרָעִין שֶׁלֹּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. שֶׁכׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״לֹא לָוִיתִי״ כְּאוֹמֵר ״לֹא פָּרַעְתִּי״ דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the Gemara emends the above statement: **If it was stated, it was stated like this: Rav Zerika said** that **Rav Yehuda said: They taught** this *halakha* **only if the orphans said:** Our **father said to us: I borrowed and repaid. However,** if **they said:** Our **father said to us: I did not borrow,** those who collect debts from them **can collect** even **without an oath, for anyone who says: I did not borrow is considered like one who says: I did not repay.**

The Gemara accordingly inverts the ruling. Rav Zerika's statement must be read as follows: the oath requirement applies when the heirs say their father told them he *paid* — in that case, the creditor or his heirs must swear before collecting. But if the heirs claim their father denied ever having borrowed in the first place, the creditor may collect *without* an oath — because the heirs have effectively conceded non-payment by denying the loan, and the *shtar* is not contradicted by their denial in any meaningful sense. *Shekol omer lo lovisi k'omer lo parati dami.*

## Husband Absent & Women

### Husband in Hawaii

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְרַעַת שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, לֹא תִּפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. אָמַר רַב אַחָא שַׂר הַבִּירָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בָּא לִפְנֵי רַבִּי יִצְחָק בְּאַנְטוֹכְיָא, וְאָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לִכְתוּבַּת אִשָּׁה, מִשּׁוּם חִינָּא. אֲבָל בַּעַל חוֹב — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **one who** comes to **collect** her marriage contract **when not in** her husband's **presence can collect** it **only by** means of **an oath. Rav Aḥa Sar HaBira said: An incident came before Rabbi Yitzḥak in Antioch, and he said: They taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to the wife's marriage contract;** she may collect her marriage contract in her husband's absence, **because** the Sages wanted men to find **favor** in the eyes of women. In order to ensure that women would want to marry, the Sages instituted decrees with regard to a marriage contract that are for the woman's benefit. **However, a creditor** does **not** have the right to collect his debt even with an oath if the borrower is absent, in case he has already been paid.

The fourth scenario brings us to the case of *shelo befanav* — the husband is alive but absent, having gone to a distant land. She wishes to collect her *ketubah* — either because he divorced her before departing, or because she needs to collect *mezonot* from his estate while they remain married — but he is not present in court to contest or confirm the claim.

### Oath for Absent Husband

Here too, the rule is: *v'nifra'at shelo befanav lo tipara ela bishvua* — if she is collecting while he is not present, she may only do so by taking an oath. Again, the oath serves the same structural function: it substitutes for the debtor's absence, providing the bet din with a solemn assurance before allowing collection to proceed.

### Mishum China Rationale

Rav Acha Sar HaBirah reports a ruling of Rabbi Yitzchak in Antioch that introduces an important distinction: *lo shanu ela kesubas isha mishum china* — this dispensation to collect *shelo befanav* was granted specifically for a woman collecting her *ketubah*, and the rationale is *mishum china*.

### Woman's Standing

*Mishum china* is the Gemara's compressed way of referring to concern for the woman's position in society. A divorced or widowed woman who cannot collect her *ketubah* is left in a precarious state — financially vulnerable and disadvantaged in her ability to rebuild her life. The institution of the *ketubah* exists precisely to protect her from this outcome. Allowing her to collect even in the husband's absence, provided she takes an oath, reflects the law's particular solicitude for her circumstances. To withhold collection until the husband could be physically brought before the court would expose her to prolonged hardship that the rabbis were unwilling to countenance.

### Creditor Different

Rav Acha Sar HaBirah's ruling implies that an ordinary creditor whose debtor has gone abroad would *not* enjoy the same dispensation — he would have to wait until the debtor could be brought to court.

### Rava's Broad Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אֲפִילּוּ בַּעַל חוֹב, שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא כׇּל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד נוֹטֵל מְעוֹתָיו שֶׁל חֲבֵרוֹ, וְהוֹלֵךְ וְיוֹשֵׁב בִּמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וְאַתָּה נוֹעֵל דֶּלֶת בִּפְנֵי לוֹוִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rava** said that **Rav Naḥman said: Even a creditor** can collect payment with an oath in the borrower's absence, **so that each and every person will not take his friend's money** by means of a loan **and go and reside in a country overseas** to prevent the lender from collecting the money from his property. **And** if that were to occur, **you would be locking the door in the face of borrowers,** as no one would be willing to lend them money.

Rava, however, citing Rav Nachman, disagrees and extends the rule broadly: *afilu baal chov* — even an ordinary creditor may collect from an absent debtor. The rationale is direct and practical.

### Absconding Risk

We cannot allow a situation where any person can take another's money and simply relocate to a distant country with impunity. If the law provided no recourse against an absent debtor, it would create a standing invitation to defraud creditors by fleeing jurisdiction.

### Creditor Protection

One might think that this concern primarily protects wealthy lenders. In fact the opposite is true. If the legal framework fails to protect creditors, no rational person will lend money, and it is ultimately borrowers — particularly poor ones with no other source of funds — who suffer. The protective instinct here runs in both directions. The *pruzbul* of Hillel illustrates the same logic: rules that seem to favor creditors are often essential for maintaining the lending market that borrowers depend on.

### Tosafot Dispute

This is a striking passage. The Gemara's conclusion, backed by Rava and Rav Nachman and apparently confirmed by the *stam* of the Gemara in *Erchin* as well, is that even a *baal chov* may collect from an absent debtor. Yet Tosafot notes that the actual practice was exactly the opposite: *maasin b'chol yom she'ein nifrin shelo b'fanav l'baal chov* — the daily practice was not to collect for an ordinary creditor in the debtor's absence. Rabbeinu Chananel ruled accordingly.

### Yerushalmi Compromise

How does Tosafot justify a practice that contradicts the plain reading of the Bavli? The answer given is remarkable: *samchu amman d'dachi lei b'Yerushalmi* — they relied on a counter-position found in the Yerushalmi. The Yerushalmi articulates what amounts to a procedural compromise: three letters of summons are sent to the absent debtor. If he appears in response — good; the matter proceeds normally. If after three summons he fails to appear, then the court seizes his property and executes the judgment without him. This graduated approach acknowledges both the creditor's legitimate claim and the absent party's right to be heard, while foreclosing the abuse of indefinite delay. The practice on the ground apparently aligned with this Yerushalmi position despite the Bavli pointing in the other direction — a genuinely unusual case of received practice overriding the *peshat* of our Gemara.

## Rabbi Shimon Limits

### Only Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ וְכוּ׳. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַהֵיָיא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה, אַהָא: וְנִפְרַעַת שֶׁלֹּא בְּפָנָיו, לָא תִּפָּרַע אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבוּעָה. לָא שְׁנָא לִמְזוֹנֵי וְלָא שְׁנָא לִכְתוּבָּה. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְמֵימַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Shimon says: Whenever she claims her marriage contract,** the heirs administer an oath to her. The Gemara asks: **To which** statement in the mishna is **Rabbi Shimon** referring? **Rabbi Yirmeya said:** He is referring **to this** statement: **And one who** comes to **collect** her marriage contract **when not in** her husband's **presence can collect** it **only by** means of **an oath.** This implies that the *halakha* **is no different** if she comes to claim money from the orphans **for sustenance, and** it **is no different** if she demands payment **for** her **marriage contract. And Rabbi Shimon comes to say** that **whenever she claims her marriage contract** the **heirs** can **administer an oath to her** that she has not taken anything of theirs.

The *mishnah* records a cryptic ruling of Rabbi Shimon: *kol zman she-tova'as ketubatah, yorshin mashbi'in otah* — as long as she is demanding her *ketubah*, the heirs can make her swear; when she is not demanding her *ketubah*, they cannot. Taken at face value this seems almost tautological — of course the heirs would only seek an oath when she is demanding her *ketubah*. What is Rabbi Shimon adding?

### Context Identified

Rabbi Yirmiyah identifies the context: Rabbi Shimon is addressing the *shelo befanav* case — the husband alive but absent in a distant land.

### Maintenance & Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵינָהּ תּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — אֵין יוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ. וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּחָנָן וּבְנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים. דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָלַךְ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם וְאִשְׁתּוֹ תּוֹבַעַת מְזוֹנוֹת, חָנָן אוֹמֵר: תִּשָּׁבַע בַּסּוֹף, וְלֹא תִּשָּׁבַע בַּתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** If **she does not claim her marriage contract,** the **heirs do not administer an oath to her. And they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Ḥanan and the sons of the High Priests, as we learned** in a mishna (104b): With regard to **one who went to a country overseas and his wife claims** money for **sustenance, Ḥanan says: She takes an oath at the conclusion,** when she comes to claim her marriage contract, that her husband did not leave her with any money and that she took from his estate only what she needed for her sustenance. **And she does not take an oath at the beginning,** when she takes the allowance for her sustenance from his estate.

That case in fact encompasses two distinct scenarios. In one, they remain married and she needs to put food on the table — she requires *mezonot* from his estate while he is abroad. In the other, he divorced her and she is collecting her *ketubah*. The *mishnah*'s unqualified formulation of *shelo befanav* appeared to require an oath in both scenarios. Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between them: only when she is demanding her *ketubah* — in the divorce scenario — can the oath be required. When she is seeking *mezonot* for ongoing subsistence, we do not impose an oath. The woman must be able to put food on the table without first undergoing a formal swearing procedure.

### Chanan's Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְלְקוּ עָלָיו בְּנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים וְאָמְרוּ: תִּשָּׁבַע בַּתְּחִלָּה וּבַסּוֹף. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כְּחָנָן, רַבָּנַן כִּבְנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **The sons of the High Priests disagreed with him, and said: She takes an oath** that her husband did not leave her any money **at the beginning,** when she comes to take money for sustenance, **and at the conclusion,** when she comes to claim her marriage contract. Rabbi Yirmeya suggests: **Rabbi Shimon** holds **like Ḥanan,** that she takes an oath only when she comes to collect her marriage contract. And **the Rabbis,** who disagree, hold **like the sons of the High Priests,** that she must also take an oath when she collects money for her sustenance.

This distinction maps precisely onto a debate recorded in an earlier *mishnah*, involving Chanan. In the case of a man who went to a distant land while still married, and his wife sought *mezonot* from his estate, Chanan ruled: *tishava basof v'lo tishava bitchila* — she swears at the end, not at the beginning. She may collect *mezonot* now without an oath; later, when she comes to collect her *ketubah* at the conclusion of the marriage, the bet din may then make her swear regarding any funds that may have been prepaid during the husband's absence. The oath is deferred, not waived.

### Bnei Kohanim View

The *Bnei Kohanim Gedolim* — a group apparently identified by this title — disputed Chanan's position and said: *tishava bitchila u'vasof* — she swears at the beginning as well, both when collecting *mezonot* and when collecting the *ketubah*. On their view, any collection from the husband's estate in his absence triggers the oath requirement, without exception.

### Shimon = Chanan

Rabbi Shimon's position aligns with Chanan: he holds that the oath is required only when she is demanding her *ketubah*, not when she is seeking *mezonot*. The urgency of subsistence, on this view, overrides the procedural requirement of the oath.

### Rabbis = Bnei Kohanim

The Rabbis who dispute Rabbi Shimon align with *Bnei Kohanim Gedolim*: they require an oath in both scenarios, treating the *shelo befanav* dispensation uniformly across all collection contexts.

### Heirs Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: הַאי יוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ? בֵּית דִּין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to** Rabbi Yirmeya's statement: If the dispute is with regard to a woman who comes to collect money for her sustenance while her husband is away, why would the mishna employ **this** phrase: The **heirs administer an oath to her? It should** have said that **the court administers an oath to her,** as this oath would be administered by the court.

Rav Sheshes raises a difficulty with this entire reading: Rabbi Shimon's statement uses the word *yorshin* — heirs. If the context is simply that the husband is alive and merely abroad, there are no heirs in the picture at all. Why does Rabbi Shimon say "the heirs can make her swear" when the husband is still living?

## Apotropos Role

### Apotropos Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת, אַהָא: הָלְכָה מִקֶּבֶר בַּעְלָהּ לְבֵית אָבִיהָ, אוֹ שֶׁחָזְרָה לְבֵית חָמִיהָ וְלֹא נַעֲשֵׂית אַפּוֹטְרוֹפְּיָא — אֵין הַיּוֹרְשִׁים מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ. וְאִם נַעֲשֵׂית אַפּוֹטְרוֹפְּיָא — יוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ עַל הֶעָתִיד לָבֹא, וְאֵין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ עַל מַה שֶּׁעָבַר. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְמֵימַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — יוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ, אֵינָהּ תּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — אֵין הַיּוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Sheshet said** that Rabbi Shimon's statement is referring **to this** earlier mishna (86b): If a woman who was exempted from an oath by her husband **went from her husband's grave,** immediately after her husband's death, **to her father's house,** without handling her late husband's property, **or** in a case **where she returned to her father-in-law's house and did not become a steward,** then **the heirs cannot administer an oath to her** with regard to her actions in their father's lifetime. **And if she became a steward, the heirs may administer an oath to her about the future,** i.e., anything she did with the property after the death of her husband, **but they cannot administer an oath to her with regard to what** took place **in the past,** during her husband's lifetime. **And Rabbi Shimon came to say** that **whenever she claims her marriage contract** the **heirs** can **administer an oath to her,** but if **she does not claim her marriage contract,** the **heirs do not administer an oath to her.**

Rav Sheshes therefore proposes a different context entirely. Rabbi Shimon is addressing the scenario of the woman as *apotropos* — the manager of her late husband's estate on behalf of his underage heirs.

### After Husband's Death

The *mishnah* earlier described two paths a woman might take after her husband's death: she might return to her father's house, or she might continue living in her father-in-law's household. In either case, she may or may not have assumed a formal managerial role over the estate left behind for the orphaned heirs.

### No Oath Without Role

If she returned to her father's house and did not become *apotropos*, the heirs have no basis to make her swear at all. The husband had exempted her from oaths relating to past dealings, and if she played no ongoing managerial role, there is nothing about which she could be made to account.

### Future vs. Past

If, however, she did assume the role of *apotropos*, the *mishnah* ruled that the heirs may make her swear regarding *future* dealings — that is, regarding the ongoing management of the estate going forward — but they may not make her swear regarding *past* dealings, since the husband had exempted her from such oaths retrospectively.

### Abba Shaul Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָמִיפַּלְגִי בִּפְלוּגְתָּא דְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל וְרַבָּנַן. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, וְרַבָּנַן כְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Sheshet explains: **And they disagree with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute of Abba Shaul and the Rabbis. Rabbi Shimon** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul,** as the woman is comparable to a steward appointed by the father of the orphans. Therefore, she cannot be compelled to take an oath about the future, unless she comes to claim her marriage contract. **And the Rabbis** here hold **in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis** there, that a steward appointed by the father is obligated to take an oath.

The question of whether and when an *apotropos* can be compelled to swear is itself the subject of a separate Tannaitic dispute between Abba Shaul and the Rabbis.

### Abba Shaul Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס שֶׁמִּינָּהוּ אֲבִי יְתוֹמִים — יִשָּׁבַע. מִינּוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין — לֹא יִשָּׁבַע. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר, חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים: מִינּוּהוּ בֵּית דִּין — יִשָּׁבַע, מִינָּהוּ אֲבִי יְתוֹמִים — לֹא יִשָּׁבַע.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: A steward who was appointed** by **the father of orphans** to take care of their property **must take an oath** when the orphans come of age and he returns their property. He takes an oath that he did not appropriate anything for himself. If **the court appointed him** steward, he need **not take an oath.** The Sages exempted him from an oath so that people would not refrain from serving as stewards. **Abba Shaul says: The matters are reversed.** If the **court appointed him, he must take an oath;** if **the father of orphans appointed him,** he need **not take an oath.** It is an honor to be appointed steward by the court, and to receive this honor he would not mind being obligated to take an oath. If he was appointed by the father, it is clear that the father trusted him and relied on him.

Abba Shaul holds: an *apotropos* appointed by the father of the orphans — appointed, that is, before the father's death — is exempt from swearing, because the father trusted him implicitly. Someone appointed by the court, however, must swear. The reasoning is that a court appointment carries institutional prestige; the appointee accepts the role willingly, knowing an oath may be required, precisely because the honor of a court appointment makes the responsibility worthwhile.

### Rabbis Position

The Rabbis take exactly the opposite view: one appointed by the father must swear, because that appointment was a private arrangement that does not carry the force of a court order. One appointed by the court, however, is exempt — because to require an oath of someone the court has publicly entrusted with this responsibility would deter capable people from accepting court appointments. The exemption is an institutional incentive.

### Shimon = Abba Shaul

Rabbi Shimon aligns with Abba Shaul. On this reading, when Rabbi Shimon says that the heirs cannot make the woman swear in her role as *apotropos*, it is because she was appointed by the father — and, following Abba Shaul, such an appointee is exempt. But when she comes to collect her *ketubah* from the orphans' estate, that is a different matter entirely: there, the general principle that one who collects from an orphans' estate must always swear applies, regardless of any exemption from oath in her capacity as *apotropos*.

## Waiver Exception

### Husband's Waiver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ אַבָּיֵי: הַאי ״כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ״ — אִם תּוֹבַעַת מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye objects to** Rav Sheshet's statement: **This** phrase, that the heirs can administer an oath to her **whenever she claims her marriage contract,** is appropriate only if Rabbi Shimon is more stringent than the Rabbis, who exempt her from an oath in all cases. However, since according to Rav Sheshet his opinion is the more lenient one, **he should** have said: **If she claims,** meaning that she is required to take an oath only when she claims her marriage contract.

The Gemara turns to yet another context: the husband who explicitly wrote in his wife's *ketubah*: *neder ushvuah ein li alaich* — I have no vow and no oath authority over you. He has preemptively waived his right to impose an oath on her.

### Broad Waiver

The waiver could be formulated even more expansively: *neder ushvuah ein li, v'lo l'yorshay, v'lo l'ba'im birshuti alaich, v'lo l'yorshaich, v'lo l'ba'im birshusah* — not I, nor my heirs, nor anyone acting with my authority have any oath authority over you, nor over your heirs, nor over those acting with your authority. In its fullest form, this waiver extends to all successors on both sides. The question becomes: can even this sweeping waiver be circumvented?

### Cannot Waive Heirs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, אַהָא: כָּתַב לָהּ ״נֶדֶר וּשְׁבוּעָה אֵין לִי עָלַיִךְ״ — אֵינוֹ יָכוֹל לְהַשְׁבִּיעָהּ כּוּ׳. ״נֶדֶר וּשְׁבוּעָה אֵין לִי, וְלֹא לְיוֹרְשַׁי, וְלֹא לַבָּאִים בִּרְשׁוּתִי, עָלַיִךְ, וְעַל יוֹרְשַׁיִךְ, וְעַל הַבָּאִין בִּרְשׁוּתִךְ״ — אֵין יָכוֹל לְהַשְׁבִּיעָהּ לֹא הוּא, וְלֹא יוֹרְשָׁיו, וְלֹא הַבָּאִין בִּרְשׁוּתוֹ. לֹא הִיא, וְלֹא יוֹרְשֶׁיהָ, וְלֹא הַבָּאִין בִּרְשׁוּתָהּ. וַאֲתָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לְמֵימַר: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ — יוֹרְשִׁין מַשְׁבִּיעִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that Rabbi Shimon's statement is referring **to this** clause of the mishna (86b): If the husband **wrote for her: I do not have** the right to administer **a vow or an oath upon you,** he **cannot administer an oath to her.** If he wrote: **Neither I, nor my heirs, nor those who come on my authority have** the right to administer **a vow or an oath upon you, or upon your heirs, or upon those who come on your authority,** he **cannot administer an oath** to **her** or them; **not he, nor his heirs, nor those who come on his authority** may administer an oath, **not to her, nor her heirs, nor those who come on her authority. And Rabbi Shimon came to say** that **whenever she claims her marriage contract** the **heirs** can **administer an oath to her.**

Abba Shaul ben Imma Miriam held that the waiver, however broadly written, is ineffective in one specific context. The husband can waive oaths that belong to him to waive — oaths that would have been demanded in his name, relating to claims he himself could have pressed. But there is a principle that stands independent of anything the husband can grant or withhold: *haba lipara minichsei yesomim lo yipara ella bishvuah* — one who comes to collect from an orphans' estate may only do so by means of an oath. This rule protects the orphans, who cannot protect themselves, and it belongs to no party in the dispute to waive it. The husband cannot prospectively extinguish a protection that exists for the benefit of heirs who have not yet lost their father.

### Heirs Oath Rule

The principle, then, is categorical: collecting from an orphans' estate always requires an oath, regardless of any prior waiver.

### Rav Papa's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: הָתִינַח כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁתּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ. אֵינָהּ תּוֹבַעַת כְּתוּבָּתָהּ, מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this: This** works out **well** in explaining Rabbi Shimon's disagreement with the Rabbis, where he said that she must take an oath **whenever she demands** payment for **her marriage contract.** However, **what can be said** about the second part of Rabbi Shimon's statement, where he speaks of one who **does not demand** payment for **her marriage contract?** According to Abaye's explanation, that clause does not add or teach anything.

Rav Papa raises a question about the second half of Rabbi Shimon's formulation. The first half — that she must swear whenever she collects her *ketubah*, notwithstanding any waiver — makes sense in light of the foregoing analysis. But Rabbi Shimon also says that when she is *not* demanding her *ketubah*, the heirs cannot make her swear. Is this not obvious? If she has a broad waiver and is not even seeking to collect the *ketubah*, why would anyone imagine she could be made to swear?

### Excludes R. Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וּמַחְלוּקְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said** that Rabbi Shimon is not referring to that mishna. His opinion is **to the exclusion of Rabbi Eliezer and those who dispute him** (86b), all of whom agree that the woman can be compelled to take an oath that she did not appropriate anything from her husband's property. The Rabbis hold that she can be compelled to take an oath only if she was appointed steward, whereas Rabbi Eliezer holds that she can always be compelled to take an oath. Rabbi Shimon, who disagrees with both opinions, contends that the heirs can administer an oath to her only when she comes to collect her marriage contract, at which point they can administer an oath about other matters, including the work done with her spindle. However, if she does not claim her marriage contract, they cannot administer an oath to her even with regard to her work as steward or storekeeper.

Rav Pappa answers: the second clause of Rabbi Shimon's ruling is not addressing an obvious case — it is explicitly excluding the stricter position of Rabbi Eliezer. Without this second clause, one might have thought that Rabbi Eliezer's view governs: that a husband can compel his wife to swear even during the course of the marriage, regarding her management of household affairs.

### R. Eliezer Strict

Rabbi Eliezer's position is that the husband may impose an oath on his wife about any matter relating to household management — even something as granular as the dough she handled. On this view, a husband's authority to demand an accounting from his wife is quite broad, and it would extend well beyond *ketubah* collection.

### Chachamim Middle

The *Chachamim* occupy a middle position: the husband may compel an oath if his wife runs the shop — that is, a business in which she handles money and accounts on his behalf — but he may not impose an oath regarding purely domestic matters like the preparation of dough.

### Shimon Balanced

Rabbi Shimon takes a position that is simultaneously more stringent and more lenient than both alternatives. He is more stringent than Chanan regarding the *ketubah*: he insists that the oath is always required when she comes to collect it, no matter what was waived. But he is more lenient than the *Chachamim* and far more lenient than Rabbi Eliezer regarding everything else: during the marriage, whatever she does, she may not be compelled to swear. The two halves of his ruling are complementary — absolute accountability at the moment of final collection, and complete freedom from oath-pressure during the ongoing marriage.

## Rabbeinu Tam Ruling

### Ashkenazi Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** This question **shall stand** unresolved.

Tosafot brings this discussion to bear on a concrete contemporary situation — concrete, at least, in the world of medieval Ashkenaz. Women in that community frequently functioned as businesswomen, running shops and managing accounts, often serving as the commercial face of a household enterprise.

### Husband Suspects Wife

In one such case before Rabbeinu Tam, a husband had installed his wife as the manager of their shop — a *chanunis* — and she handled large sums of money in and out of the household. A dispute arose in which the husband suspected her of financial impropriety, or at least sought an accounting of her stewardship, and he wished to compel her to swear.

### No Oath in Marriage

Rabbeinu Tam refused to permit it. Even in this situation — a wife running the family business with full access to its funds — the husband could not force an oath during the marriage.

### Woman Empowerment

The legal basis is the final *halakha* as it emerges from the Gemara: we rule in accordance with Rabbi Shimon, and Rabbi Shimon holds that outside the context of *ketubah* collection, the husband has no authority to impose an oath. This applies even to a woman running a business enterprise on his behalf. The second half of Rabbi Shimon's ruling — that when she is not demanding her *ketubah* the heirs cannot make her swear — translates in practice into a prohibition on the husband making her swear during the course of the marriage, whatever the scope of her commercial activity.

### Practical Conclusion

The ruling of Rabbeinu Tam is not merely a technical application of Rabbi Shimon's position. It reflects a recognition that women who functioned as businesswomen in Ashkenaz needed legal protection from a mechanism — the compelled oath — that could be weaponized to harass or undermine them in their commercial roles. The *halakhic* conclusion, arrived at through a chain running from Abba Shaul ben Imma Miriam through Rabbi Shimon through the final redaction of the *sugya*, thus lands in a place that is both doctrinally grounded and practically protective of women's economic agency.
