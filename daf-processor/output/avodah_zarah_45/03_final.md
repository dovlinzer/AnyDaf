# Avodah Zarah 45 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Bathhouse Answer

### Bathhouse First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שָׁאַל פְּרוֹקְלוּס בֶּן פְּלוֹסְפוּס אֶת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּעַכּוֹ, שֶׁהָיָה רוֹחֵץ בַּמֶּרְחָץ שֶׁל אַפְרוֹדִיטֵי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּתוּב בְּתוֹרַתְכֶם ״לָא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם״, מִפְּנֵי מָה אַתָּה רוֹחֵץ בְּמֶרְחָץ שֶׁל אַפְרוֹדִיטֵי? אָמַר לוֹ: אֵין מְשִׁיבִין בַּמֶּרְחָץ. וּכְשֶׁיָּצָא אָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי לֹא בָּאתִי בִּגְבוּלָהּ, הִיא בָּאָה בִּגְבוּלִי. אֵין אוֹמְרִים: נַעֲשָׂה מֶרְחָץ נוֹי לְאַפְרוֹדִיטֵי, אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר: נַעֲשָׂה אַפְרוֹדִיטֵי נוֹי לַמֶּרְחָץ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A wise gentile, **Proclus ben Plospus,** once **asked** a question **of Rabban Gamliel in** the city of **Akko when he was bathing in the bathhouse of** the Greek god **Aphrodite.** Proclus **said to him: It is written in your Torah:** "And **nothing of the proscribed items shall cleave to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18). **For what reason do you bathe** before an idol **in the bathhouse of Aphrodite?** Rabban Gamliel **said to him: One may not answer** questions related to Torah **in the bathhouse. And when he left** the bathhouse, Rabban Gamliel gave him several answers. **He said to him: I did not come into its domain; it came into my domain.** The bathhouse existed before the statue dedicated to Aphrodite was erected. Furthermore, people **do not say: Let us make a bathhouse** as **an adornment for Aphrodite; rather,** they **say: Let us make** a statue of **Aphrodite** as **an adornment for the bathhouse.** Therefore, the main structure is not the Aphrodite statue, but the bathhouse.

The *gemara* opens by noting that Rabban Gamliel gave a *teshuvah gnuva* — an incomplete answer — in his famous exchange at the bathhouse of Aphrodite. Rashi understands this to mean something like a throwaway response, one that was not sufficiently strong. Tosafot, however, reads it differently: the answer was not invalid, merely incompletely explicated. The *gemara* proceeds to show that stronger points could have been made, and it is in that spirit that Abaye offers his first observation.

Abaye explains that the incompleteness lies in the following argument Rabban Gamliel could have made: *d'amar leh ani lo bati bigvula v'hi ba bigvuli* — "I did not come into its zone; it came into my zone." The bathhouse existed first, and only afterward was the idol of Aphrodite brought in. The idol therefore cannot retroactively change the status of the bathhouse. This principle connects to a broader theme running through the next *mishnah* as well: to what degree can a person's actions redefine an object that is already established, particularly when that object is *mechubar l'karka* — attached to the ground? The more something is rooted in the landscape, the less it is seen as subject to human definition and control. A thing that is simply part of the ground is not so easily brought under the category of *avodah zarah* through a person's declaration or act.

### Aphrodite First

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: גְּנוּבְתַּהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּקָאָמַר לוֹ: אֲנִי לֹא בָּאתִי בִּגְבוּלָהּ וְהִיא בָּאָה בִּגְבוּלִי. וְכִי בָּא בִּגְבוּלָהּ מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָתְנַן: עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ מֶרְחָץ אוֹ גִינָּה — נֶהֱנִין מֵהֶן שֶׁלֹּא בְּטוֹבָה, וְאֵין נֶהֱנִין מֵהֶן בְּטוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: The deception in** Rabban Gamliel's response was **from here, when he said to him: I did not come into its domain, but** rather **it came into my domain.** He explains: **And** even **if it,** the bathhouse, **had come into its domain, what of it?** Even if the idol had preceded the bathhouse, it would still not render use of the bathhouse prohibited; **but didn't we learn** in a mishna (51b): With regard to an object of **idol worship that has a bathhouse or a garden** in front of it, **one may** derive **benefit from** the bathhouse or garden **without** showing **favor** by giving thanks or payment to its priests, **but one may not** derive **benefit from it while** showing it **favor?** Rabban Gamliel's answer was therefore deceptive because the permissibility of using the bathhouse had nothing to do with its antecedence to the statue.

The *gemara* then sharpens the question: even granting that the bathhouse came first, what would the ruling have been had it been the reverse — had the bathhouse of Aphrodite been constructed *ab initio* as an adjunct to the idol? The *gemara* challenges Abaye's reasoning: why would it matter? There is, after all, a *mishnah* that seems to suggest it would not be a problem in any case.

### Mishna Bathhouse

The *gemara* cites a *mishnah*: if an idol has a bathhouse or a garden in front of it — meaning it was constructed with that connection in mind — one may nonetheless derive benefit from those appurtenances, provided this is done *shelo b'tova*, without any expression of gratitude or honor to the idol's priests. The benefit itself is not *asur b'hana'ah*; only the expression of thanks is prohibited. So even if the bathhouse had been built for Aphrodite from the outset, why would Rabban Gamliel's use of it be a problem?

### Showing Honor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ גְּנוּבָה, שֶׁלֹּא בְּטוֹבַת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל כִּבְטוֹבַת אֲחֵרִים דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥama bar Yosef himself disagrees: **And I say** that **it is not a deceptive** answer, **as** even though **Rabban Gamliel** visited the bathhouse **without** showing **favor** by expressing thanks or giving payment, the very fact that such an esteemed visitor paid it a visit **is the equivalent of others** actively showing **favor.**

Abaye responds that in this case, *shelo b'tovas Rabban Gamliel k'tovas acheirim dami* — the very fact of Rabban Gamliel's presence at the bathhouse would itself constitute an honor. Rabban Gamliel was so prominent a figure that his merely bathing there would be tantamount to what for an ordinary person would be an act of gratitude. Had the bathhouse been defined from the outset as the bathhouse of Aphrodite, even the act of bathing there — without any verbal expression of thanks — would have been considered an honor to the idol. That is why the answer was incomplete: the first point, that the bathhouse predated the idol, was doing more work than was immediately apparent. Had the order been reversed, the answer would not have been so simple.

### Rashi Explanation

Rashi explains the concept of *shelo b'tova* as meaning one should not show gratitude to the priests — *shelo yachzik tovah la'chomarim*. The benefit itself is not forbidden: *uve'hana'ah lo mityaser*. He then adds a crucial gloss: *ein hekdesh l'avodat kochavim ad sheyakriv lefanav b'tikroves* — words of designation alone do not create a sanctified connection to an idol; something must actually be offered as a sacrifice before it. The garden, even if designated as Aphrodite's garden, was never offered to the idol. It was not physically placed on the idol to adorn it. The question thus arises: why is it not at least *tashmishei avodah zarah*, an appurtenance of the idol?

Rashi leaves this somewhat open. The categories at play here are several: the *avodah zarah* itself, which is forbidden in benefit; *noy avodah zarah*, something placed on the idol to adorn it; *tikroves avodah zarah*, something actually offered up to it; and *tashmishei avodah zarah*, appurtenances that serve it. A garden or bathhouse constructed in front of an idol seems like it could fall into the category of *tashmishim*, yet Rashi implies it does not — apparently because these things are attached to the ground and sufficiently distant from direct physical connection to the idol object itself.

The question of *shelo b'tova* also generates an important discussion in Tosafot. Rabbeinu Tam notes that in his time, Jews would use monastery ovens and water mills — *ma she'anu ofim ata b'tanur shel komrim vetochunim b'reichayim shel mayim shelahem*. He rules this is not a problem. First, when one pays for the service, there is no expression of gratitude — the relationship is purely transactional: *ein anu machzikim lahem tova*. But he goes further and says even without payment, since these facilities are not located within an actual house of worship and are not directly connected to the objects of worship themselves, the concern does not apply. The key distinction is whether the object in question is *natun ve'omed chaim b'veis avodat kochavim atzmo* — actually situated within the house of worship itself. A separate mill or oven, even if owned by church property, does not carry the same status.

## Human Control

### Urination Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב שִׁימִי בַּר חִיָּיא אָמַר: גְּנוּבְתַּהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּקָאָמַר לוֹ: זוֹ עוֹמֶדֶת עַל הַבִּיב וְכׇל אָדָם מַשְׁתִּינִין בְּפָנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Shimi bar Ḥiyya says: The deception in** Rabban Gamliel's response was **from here, when he said to him: This** statue **stands upon the sewage pipe and all people urinate before it.** He explains: **And if people urinate before it, what of it?**

Rav Shimi bar Chiya offers a different reason why Rabban Gamliel's answer was incomplete. The argument he could have made was: *zo omedes al ha-beev ve'chol adam mashtin befaneha* — this Aphrodite stands by the drainage pipe and everyone urinates in front of it. The consistent public urination in front of the statue demonstrates that nobody treats it as a genuine object of reverence.

### Bitul Discussion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי מַשְׁתִּינִין בְּפָנֶיהָ מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָתְנַן: רָק בְּפָנֶיהָ, הִשְׁתִּין בְּפָנֶיהָ, גֵּירְרָה, וְזָרַק בָּהּ אֶת הַצּוֹאָה — הֲרֵי זוֹ אֵינָהּ בְּטֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** That does not indicate a lack of idolatrous status; **but didn't we learn** in a mishna (53a): If one **spit in front of it, urinated in front of it, dragged it, or threw feces at it,** its status as an object of idol worship **is not revoked?**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: so what if people urinate in front of it? Is that meaningful? The *gemara* cites a *mishnah* that holds that if a non-Jew spat at his idol, urinated on it, dragged it, or threw excrement at it — *harei zo einah betelah* — that does not constitute a *bitul* of the *avodah zarah*. Acts of disgust, without a physical change to the object, do not nullify its status. So why would the urination near the Aphrodite statue make any difference?

### Consistent Disrespect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ גְּנוּבָה, הָתָם — לְפִי שַׁעְתָּא הוּא רָתַח עֲלַהּ, וַהֲדַר מְפַיֵּיס לַהּ, הָכָא — כֹּל שַׁעְתָּא וְשַׁעְתָּא בְּזִלְזוּלַהּ קָיְימָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥama bar Yosef himself disagrees: **And I say** that **it is not a deceptive** answer. **There,** the case of that mishna is of one who **temporarily rages against** the idol, **and afterward he appeases it. Here,** in the case of the Aphrodite statue erected on the sewage pipe, **each and every hour** the statue **remains in** a constant state of **disparagement.** This setup indicates a permanent lack of reverence and an absence of true idolatrous status.

Rav Shimi distinguishes the two cases. The *mishnah* about *bitul* deals with a moment of anger — *lefi sha'ah hu rasach aleha v'hadar me'fayes lah* — the worshipper became furious, acted out, and then made up with the idol. That is not a genuine repudiation; it is temporary rage. Here, by contrast, *kol sha'ata ve'sha'ata b'vizyonah kaymah* — consistently, hour after hour, this is how the statue is treated. The ongoing and structural nature of the disrespect demonstrates that the Aphrodite is simply not regarded as a deity in this context. That is a stronger point than Rabban Gamliel made explicitly, and therefore the answer was incomplete in not articulating it.

### Ornament Hierarchy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר: גְּנוּבְתַּהּ מֵהָכָא, דְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ, אֵין אוֹמְרִים: נַעֲשָׂה מֶרְחָץ נוֹי לְאַפְרוֹדִיטֵי, אֶלָּא: נַעֲשָׂה אַפְרוֹדִיטֵי נוֹי לַמֶּרְחָץ; וְכִי אָמַר ״נַעֲשָׂה מֶרְחָץ לְאַפְרוֹדִיטֵי נוֹי״ מַאי הָוֵי? וְהָתַנְיָא: הָאוֹמֵר ״בַּיִת זֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה״, ״כּוֹס זֶה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה״ — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם, שֶׁאֵין הֶקְדֵּשׁ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ulla said: The deception in** Rabban Gamliel's response was **from here, when he said to him** that people **do not say: Let us make a bathhouse** as **an adornment for Aphrodite; rather,** they say: **Let us make** a statue of **Aphrodite** as **an adornment for the bathhouse.** He explains: **And** even **if** people **say: Let us make a bathhouse** as **an adornment for Aphrodite, what of it? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who says: This house is** hereby dedicated **to idol worship,** or: **This cup is** hereby dedicated **to idol worship, he has said nothing,** i.e., his words take no effect, **as there is no** *halakha* of **consecration with regard to** objects of **idol worship.** While one can consecrate an item to the Temple through verbal designation, there is no such method for according idolatrous status to an object. Therefore, Rabban Gamliel's deception lies in his indication that such a formulation would render the bathhouse forbidden as an object of idol worship.

Rabba bar Ulla offers yet a third explanation for the incompleteness of the answer. He says Rabban Gamliel could have said: *eino omer na'aseh merchatz noy le'Afroditi ela na'aseh Afroditi noy le'merchatz* — one does not say the bathhouse was made to honor Aphrodite; rather, Aphrodite was brought in to beautify the bathhouse. The hierarchy of the relationship is inverted. In a true temple of worship, the structure is built around the idol, to serve it. Here, the bathhouse is the primary entity, and the idol is merely decorative. The Aphrodite is a *tashmish* of the bathhouse, not the bathhouse a *tashmish* of the Aphrodite. This has significant implications for the broader discussion about sanctuaries and the relationship between a place of worship and its contents.

### Noy Concept

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: אֵינָהּ גְּנוּבָה, נְהִי דְּאִיתְּסוֹרֵי לָא מִיתַּסְרָא, נוֹי מִיהָא אִיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ḥama bar Yosef himself disagrees: **And I say** that **it is not a deceptive** answer. **Although** the bathhouse would **not be rendered forbidden** as an object of idol worship due to a verbal designation, **it would at least have** the status of **an adornment** of an object of idol worship, which is also forbidden.

This leads to the question the *gemara* now examines: even if one were to say the bathhouse was made as a *noy* for Aphrodite — what would follow? There is a genuine category of *noy avodah zarah*, and if something functions as an adornment for the idol, it becomes forbidden. So why isn't the bathhouse forbidden as a *noy*? The *gemara* cites the principle: *ha'omer bayis le'avodat kochavim kosel le'avodat kochavim lo amar klum* — if someone merely says "this house is for idol worship, this wall is for idol worship," he has said nothing legally operative. *Ein hekdesh l'avodat kochavim* — words alone do not consecrate something to an idol. No actual offering was made; the bathhouse was merely designated.

The *gemara* then responds that even so, *noy miha ika* — there is still a sense in which it functions as an adornment. This seems circular: we said it is not really forbidden as a *noy*, yet we affirm there is something of *noy* about it. Rashi addresses this directly. He says that while the bathhouse itself would not be *asur b'hana'ah* even if it had been constructed for Aphrodite, the act of bathing there would nonetheless be a problem — not because deriving benefit from the bathhouse is forbidden as an object, but because the act of bathing would constitute showing honor. The *issur* attaches to the act, not to the object.

### Connection Issue

Tosafot picks up the unresolved tension. If the bathhouse is actually being used as an adornment for the *avodah zarah*, why is it not truly *noy* or *tachshit d'avodat kochavim*, forbidden in the way other adornments are? Tosafot ultimately concludes — though without fully spelling out the reasoning — that while the bathhouse does not become *assur* as a formal *noy avodah zarah*, it may be rabbinically prohibited. The *gemara* initially considered treating it as a *bayis shel avodah zarah*, but a mere designation does not make a house forbidden, so that avenue was closed. In the end, Tosafot holds that the technical *noy* category, while not creating full *isur d'oraita* here, does carry at least a rabbinic dimension.

Two underlying reasons may explain why attached structures do not become forbidden in the way that portable adornments do. First, they are not physically connected to the idol object in the way that gold draped over a statue is. A garden or bathhouse is too physically distant and insufficiently attached to the idol itself. Second, and more conceptually, things that are *mechubar l'karka* are part of the landscape; they are not fully subject to human definitional control. A person's declaration or intent cannot impose halakhic identity on something that is simply the ground. This second reason will become the animating principle of the next *mishnah*.

## Mountains Issue

### Permitted Mountains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַגּוֹיִם הָעוֹבְדִים אֶת הֶהָרִים וְאֶת הַגְּבָעוֹת — הֵן מוּתָּרִין, וּמָה שֶׁעֲלֵיהֶן אֲסוּרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא תַחְמֹד כֶּסֶף וְזָהָב עֲלֵיהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to the *halakha* in the case of **the gentiles who worship the mountains and the hills,** the mountains and hills **are permitted, but what is upon them is forbidden.** It is not prohibited to derive benefit from the mountains and hills themselves, and they can be used for planting, harvesting, and the like. But if gentiles coated them with gold or silver, it is prohibited to derive benefit from the coating, **as it is stated:** "The graven images of their gods shall you burn with fire; **you shall not covet the silver or the gold that is on them,** nor take it for yourself, lest you be snared by it; for it is an abomination to the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 7:25).

The next *mishnah* states: *ha'nochrim ha'ovdim es he'harim ve'es ha'geva'ot* — non-Jews who worship mountains and hills. The *mishnah* rules *hein mutarin* — the mountains and hills themselves are permitted. One may derive benefit from them freely: go hiking, farm the land, mine for ore. However, *u'mah she'aleihen asurin* — whatever is on them is forbidden. If the worshippers plated the mountain with silver to honor it as a deity, one may not derive benefit from that silver. The source is the verse: *lo tachmod kesef v'zahav aleihem* — do not covet the silver and gold that is on them. The mountain itself is permitted; the precious metal affixed to it is not.

## Benefit Context

### No Destruction

This is an opportune moment to note a striking feature of this entire tractate. Reading through the *pesukim* in Devarim and Shemot, one is struck by how insistently the Torah commands the destruction of idols and their places of worship — cutting down the trees, tearing down the altars, eradicating every trace. It is one of the most frequently repeated themes in the Torah. Yet this obligation is almost entirely absent from the Talmudic discussion of *avodah zarah*.

### Focus On Benefit

The *gemara*'s sustained focus is entirely on the question of *issur hana'ah* — are we permitted to derive benefit from these objects? Are we allowed to use them, sell them, benefit from them in any way? The obligation to actively destroy them is mentioned, when it is mentioned at all, only in passing, as a brief parenthetical.

### Minority Status

The reason for this shift in emphasis is not hard to find, and it is worth stating directly. The Talmud was composed by a minority community living within a dominant pagan culture. It would be both dangerous and counterproductive to dwell on the theoretical obligation to destroy the idols that surrounded them on every side. Even framing it as an obligation that exists but is suspended due to *ones* or in favor of *shev v'al ta'aseh* would risk inspiring the occasional zealot to act in ways catastrophic for the broader community. The practical *halakhic* discussion therefore focused entirely on a question that was genuinely relevant to daily life: what may we use, and what must we avoid? The destruction passages, where they do appear, surface only incidentally in the course of deriving other principles.

## Mountains Issue…

### Forbidden Plating

Returning to the *mishnah*: the Tanna Kamma derives from *lo tachmod kesef v'zahav aleihem* that while the mountain is permitted, the gold and silver plating upon it is forbidden. This raises the interesting structural question of why the mountain itself is permitted — something the *mishnah* leaves implicit. The implication is that one cannot simply worship the entire earth and thereby forbid the landscape to everyone else. You do not have the right; it is not yours to define. Moreover, something so rooted in the ground, so entirely beyond human control, cannot have its identity altered by anyone's act of worship. Your words and your acts of veneration cannot change what is simply part of the natural world.

### Yose HaGlili

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הֶהָרִים״ — וְלֹא הֶהָרִים אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הַגְּבָעוֹת״ — וְלֹא הַגְּבָעוֹת אֱלֹהֵיהֶם. וּמִפְּנֵי מָה אֲשֵׁירָה אֲסוּרָה? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם, וְכֹל שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ תְּפִיסַת יְדֵי אָדָם — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says** with regard to the verse: "You shall destroy all the places where the nations that you are to dispossess served their gods, upon the high mountains, and upon the hills, and under every leafy tree" (Deuteronomy 12:2): The mitzva to destroy objects of idol worship applies to **"their gods, upon the** high **mountains," but not** to **the mountains** themselves that are **their gods.** Similarly it applies to **"their gods…upon the hills," but not** to **the hills** themselves that are **their gods.** The mishna asks: **And for what reason,** then, is **an *ashera* forbidden?** Doesn't the verse also state: "And under every leafy tree," which indicates that the mitzva to destroy objects of idol worship does not apply to the trees themselves? The mishna answers: It is **because it** is the product of **human involvement** and did not grow by itself, **and** the *halakha* is that **anything that** is the product of **human involvement** is **forbidden.**

Rabbi Yose HaGlili offers an explicit scriptural basis for permitting the mountains themselves. He derives it from the verse: *eloheihem al he'harim ve'lo he'harim eloheihem* — their gods that are on the mountains, but the mountains are not their gods. And similarly: *eloheihem al ha'geva'ot ve'lo ha'geva'ot eloheihem* — their gods on the hills, but the hills are not their gods. The verse, in other words, explicitly excludes the mountains and hills from the category of *avodah zarah*. Whatever god is worshipped on the mountain is on the mountain; the mountain itself cannot become a god.

This raises an immediate question: why then is an *asherah* forbidden? An *asherah* is also rooted in the ground — it is a tree. Does the same logic not apply? Rabbi Yose HaGlili answers: *mipnei she'yesh bah tefissat yad adam* — because an *asherah* involves human control. Trees are planted; they are cultivated, uprooted, and replanted. They fall within the domain of human agency in a way that mountains do not. Therefore, the principle is: *kol she'yesh bah tefissat yad adam assur* — anything subject to human control can be made forbidden. The *gemara* will proceed to work out exactly what degree and type of human control is required.

### Akiva's Map

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲנִי אוֹבִין וְאָדוּן לְפָנֶיךָ, כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא הַר גָּבוֹהַּ וְגִבְעָה נִשָּׂאָה וְעֵץ רַעֲנָן, דַּע שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: I will explain and decide** the matter **before you.** The verse does not indicate limitations to the halakhic definition of idols; rather, it is simply giving indicators of prevalent idolatrous practice: **Everywhere that you find a high mountain, or an elevated hill, or a leafy tree, know that there is idol worship there.**

Rabbi Akiva adds his own observation: *ani avin ve'adun lefanecha kol makom she'attah motzei har gavoha ve'givah nissa'ah ve'etz ra'anan da she'yesh sham avodat kochavim* — I will explain: wherever you find a high mountain, a lofty hill, or a leafy tree, know that there is *avodah zarah* there. Rashi explains the logic: just as the verse says *eloheihem al he'harim* — their gods are on the mountain tops — so too the phrase *tachat kol etz ra'anan* tells you where to look. It is a geographic map for finding idol worship. When entering the land of Israel to destroy the idols, go look at the spectacular natural vistas and the shaded leafy trees — that is where you will find them. The verse is not coming to say that the trees themselves are permitted; another verse — *ve'ashereihem tisrefun ba'esh* — explicitly requires their destruction. Rather, *tachat kol etz ra'anan* is simply giving you directions.

### Mountain Status

There is a deep conceptual debate implicit in all of this, articulated most sharply by Rashi and Tosafot: when we say that mountains are *mutarin*, do we mean that the mountain is still halakhically defined as an *avodah zarah* but carries no *issur hana'ah* — or do we mean that the mountain cannot even be called an *avodah zarah* at all?

The distinction matters enormously. If the mountain is an *avodah zarah* but without *issur hana'ah*, then it is easy to explain why the gold on it is forbidden — because once the mountain is called an *avodah zarah*, things connected to it can acquire the *issur* as *noy* or *tashmish*. It is also easy to explain why acts of worship directed at the mountain make one liable: one is worshipping something that is, by halakhic definition, an *avodah zarah*. The only peculiarity is the absence of *issur hana'ah* — and that can be explained as a *gezerat ha'katuv*, a scriptural exception based on the verse that limits the prohibition to the gold and silver on it rather than the mountain itself.

If, on the other hand, the mountain cannot even be called an *avodah zarah* — because it is so utterly outside the scope of human control that no one's worship can define it — then we face greater difficulties. Why would the gold on it be forbidden? Why would worshipping it make one liable? One would have to say that worshipping a mountain is *avodah zarah* in the sense of the act, even though the mountain itself is not an *avodah zarah* as an object — a *cheftza*. The object-level and the act-level would have to be disentangled.

There is also a fascinating asymmetry here. If one offers a sacrifice to Zeus conceptualized as a god in the sky — with no statue or physical object present — the act may make one liable for *avodah zarah*, yet there would be no *avodah zarah* object to render the offering *asur b'hana'ah*. Whereas if one offers to a statue of Zeus standing before one, not only is the act forbidden, but the offering itself becomes *asur b'hana'ah* through its connection to the *avodah zarah* object. Tosafot leans toward the view that the mountain is still called an *avodah zarah*, because that is the simpler way to explain the other prohibitions that attach to it — the gold, the acts of worship — while saying only that there is no *issur hana'ah* in the mountain itself.

### Plating Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הַיְינוּ תַּנָּא קַמָּא! אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: צִפּוּי הַר כְּהַר אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: צִפּוּי הַר אֵינוֹ כְּהַר וּמִיתְּסַר, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: צִפּוּי הַר הֲרֵי הוּא כְּהַר. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא צִפּוּי הַר אֵינוֹ כְּהַר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili** the same as that of **the first *tanna*?** Both of them indicate that what is on the mountain is forbidden, while the mountain itself is permitted. **Rami bar Ḥama says** that **Reish Lakish says:** The difference **between them** is the issue of whether the status of **the coating of a mountain is like** the status of the **mountain** itself. **The first *tanna* holds** that the status of **the coating of a mountain is not like** the **mountain** itself, **and** is therefore **forbidden, and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds** that the status of **the coating of a mountain is like** the **mountain** itself. Rejecting this explanation, **Rav Sheshet says: Everyone** agrees that the status of **the coating of a mountain is not like** the **mountain** itself and is forbidden.

Rav Sheshet resolves the apparent dispute between the Tanna Kamma and Rabbi Yose HaGlili: *de'kulei alma tzipuyei har eino ke'har* — everyone agrees that the plating of the mountain is not like the mountain. Both agree that the mountain itself is permitted and the gold on it is forbidden. Where they actually disagree is not about mountains at all, but about trees.

## Trees Asherah

### Initial Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכָא בָּאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ קָמִיפַּלְגִי, תַּנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ — מוּתָּר, וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי סָבַר: אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **And here, they disagree with regard to** the status of **a tree that one planted and** only **subsequently worshipped. The first *tanna* holds** that **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped** is **permitted, and Rabbi Yosei HaGelili holds** that **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped** is **forbidden.**

The Tanna Kamma holds: a tree that was planted *ab initio* as an *asherah* — explicitly for the purpose of idol worship — is forbidden. But a tree that was first planted for ordinary purposes and only later worshipped — *ilan she'nata'o u'levasof avado* — is permitted. The explicit verses commanding destruction of the *asherot* apply only to the first case. A tree that was already growing and part of the natural landscape, which a worshipper subsequently decided to venerate, cannot have its identity transformed by that act of worship. It is too much a part of the landscape, too much like the mountain itself, to be subject to that kind of human redefinition.

### Any Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ — אָסוּר, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, מִתּוֹךְ שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הֶהָרִים״ — וְלֹא הֶהָרִים אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, ״אֱלֹהֵיהֶם עַל הַגְּבָעוֹת״ — וְלֹא גְּבָעוֹת אֱלֹהֵיהֶם, שׁוֹמֵעַ אֲנִי ״תַּחַת כׇּל עֵץ רַעֲנָן אֱלֹהֵיהֶם״ — וְלֹא רַעֲנָן אֱלֹהֵיהֶם? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, also holds** that **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped** is **forbidden. As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the verse: "You shall destroy all the places where the nations that you are to dispossess served their gods, upon the high mountains, and upon the hills, and under every leafy tree" (Deuteronomy 12:2): **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: From that** which **is stated** in the verse: "You shall destroy…**their gods, upon the** high **mountains,"** from which the Sages derived: **But not the mountains** themselves that are **their gods,** and: "You shall destroy…**their gods…upon the hills," but not** the **hills** themselves if they are **their gods, I** would **derive** from the next clause in the verse: **"Their gods…under every leafy tree,"** that the mitzva to destroy an object of idol worship does **not** apply to the **leafy** trees themselves that are **their gods.** Therefore, **the** next **verse states:** "And you shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, **and burn their *asherim* with fire"** (Deuteronomy 12:3). Even a tree that was worshipped only after it was planted is forbidden.

Rabbi Yose HaGlili disagrees. He holds that even a tree which was first planted for ordinary reasons and only later worshipped is forbidden — *ilan she'nata'o u'levasof avado assur*. The principle he articulated — *kol she'yesh bah tefissat yad adam assur*, anything subject to human control is forbidden — is broad enough to encompass this case. His formulation did not say that an *asherah* is forbidden only when planted initially for that purpose. He said anything with *tefissat yad adam*. Trees fall within the domain of human agricultural activity: we plant them, cultivate them, uproot them, and replant them. The fact that trees as a category are subject to human control means that any tree that has been worshipped is subject to the prohibition. Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda holds the same position.

The *gemara* infers this from the breadth of Rabbi Yose HaGlili's language. He could have drawn the line explicitly at initial intent. He did not. Instead he articulated a general principle — *tefissat yad adam* — that encompasses the case of a tree already planted and then worshipped. This is the reading that the *gemara* endorses.

### Two Destructions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא ״תַּחַת כׇּל עֵץ רַעֲנָן״ לְמָה לִי? הָהוּא לְכִדְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הוּא דַּאֲתָא, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: אֲנִי אוֹבִין וְאָדוּן לְפָנֶיךָ, כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁאַתָּה מוֹצֵא הַר גָּבוֹהַּ וְגִבְעָה נִשָּׂאָה וְעֵץ רַעֲנָן, דַּע שֶׁיֵּשׁ שָׁם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** if the trees themselves are forbidden, **why do I** need the phrase **"under every leafy tree"? That** phrase **comes to** teach a *halakha* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva; as Rabbi Akiva says: I will explain and decide** the matter **before you. Everywhere that you find a high mountain, or an elevated hill, or a leafy tree, know that there is idol worship there.** From the fact that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, does not derive from the phrase "under every leafy tree" that a tree that was planted and only subsequently worshipped is still permitted, it is apparent that he holds that such a tree is forbidden. This is consistent with the opinion that Rav Sheshet ascribes to Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.

The derivation proceeds as follows. There are two different verbs used in the *pesukim* for destroying *asherot*: *ve'ashreihem tisrefun ba'esh* — burn them with fire — and *ve'ashreihem tigade'un* — chop them down. The presence of two distinct forms of destruction is significant. If there were only one type of forbidden *asherah*, a single verse would suffice. The existence of two different modes of destruction suggests two different categories — and hence two different laws.

### Three Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ מִתְּחִילָּה לְכָךְ. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה נָמֵי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְהָכִי? הָכִי נָמֵי. אֶלָּא אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וַּאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תְּגַדֵּעוּן״, אֵיזֶהוּ עֵץ שֶׁגִּידּוּעוֹ אָסוּר וְעִיקָּרוֹ מוּתָּר? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר: אִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis,** who maintain that a tree that was planted and subsequently worshipped is permitted, **what do they do with this** verse: **"And burn their *asherim* with fire"?** The Gemara answers: This verse **is necessary with regard to** the *halakha* of **a tree that was initially planted for that** idolatrous practice, which must be destroyed and from which deriving benefit is prohibited. The Gemara asks: **But** doesn't **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, also require** this phrase **to** teach **this?** The Gemara answers. **Indeed,** he does. **Rather, from where does he** derive that **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped** is forbidden? **He derives it from** the following verse: "But so shall you deal with them: You shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, **and hew down their *asherim*,** and burn their graven images with fire" (Deuteronomy 7:5). Now, **which is the tree whose trunk is forbidden but its root is permitted,** as the verse instructs one to hew it down? **You must say** that it is referring to **a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped.**

The derivation proceeds as follows. The verse of burning — *tisrefun ba'esh* — addresses trees that were originally planted as *asherot* from the outset; these are destroyed completely. But that leaves the verse of chopping — *tigade'un* — with additional work to do. The *gemara* reasons: what type of tree is it that you chop down but leave a stump? It must be a tree whose upper growth is forbidden but whose root structure is not. And that is precisely the case of a tree planted for ordinary purposes and then worshipped: the original tree that was already in the ground is not forbidden, but what grew afterward — *gidulei avodah zarah* — the growth produced under the identity of an *asherah*, is forbidden. You chop off the new growth and burn it; you leave the original stump.

This is how both Rabbi Yose HaGlili and Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda understand the *pessukim*. Even for them, the prohibition on a tree that was planted first and worshipped later is confined to what grew *after* the worship began. The pre-existing root structure and trunk, already part of the landscape, remain permitted.

### Growth After

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״ קָא נָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא! אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר קָאָמַר: אִילּוּ לֹא נֶאֱמַר ״תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תְּגַדֵּעוּן״ בְּאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ מִתְּחִילָּה לְכָךְ, הַשְׁתָּה דִּכְתִיב ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ״, אִיַּיתַּר לֵיהּ ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תְּגַדֵּעוּן״ לְאִילָן שֶׁנְּטָעוֹ וּלְבַסּוֹף עֲבָדוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks how that verse can be the source of the ruling of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, for deeming forbidden a tree that was planted and only subsequently worshipped. **But doesn't the *baraita*** state that he **adduces** the derivation prohibiting worshipped *ashera* trees from the verse: "And you shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, **and burn their *asherim* with fire"?** The Gemara answers: By deriving this *halakha* from the verse: "And burn their *asherim* with fire," Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **If it were not stated.** The Gemara explains: **If** the verse: **"Burn** their *asherim* **with fire," were not stated, I would have said** that the verse: "And **hew down their *asherim*,"** is referring **to a tree that was initially planted for** idol worship. **Now that it is written: "And burn their *asherim* with fire,"** the verse: **"And hew down their *asherim*," is rendered superfluous** and is consequently interpreted as referring **to a tree that one planted and subsequently worshipped.** Therefore, it is ultimately this latter verse that serves as the source for the implied ruling of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, that it is prohibited to derive benefit from such a tree.

Rabbi Yose bar Yehuda's position is derived from the combination of these two verses. Had only the burning verse existed, one might have assumed that chopping down referred to the same case — trees originally planted as *asherot* — with the two verbs simply describing different stages of destruction. But since we need both verses for distinct laws, the burning verse is assigned to originally planted *asherot*, and the chopping verse is assigned to the *chidush*: trees planted for other purposes that were subsequently worshipped. And what is forbidden in the latter case? Only the new growth — the *gidulei avodah zarah* — produced after the worship began.

### Competing Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן, הַאי ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תְּגַדֵּעוּן״ מַאי עָבְדִי לֵיהּ? לְכִדְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: גִּידּוּעֵי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קוֹדְמִין לְכִיבּוּשׁ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל, כִּיבּוּשׁ אֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל קוֹדֵם לְבִיעוּר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** as for **the Rabbis, what do they do with this** verse: **"And hew down their *asherim*"?** The Gemara answers: This verse is written in order **to** teach a *halakha* **in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi; as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says: The felling of** trees dedicated to **idol worship precedes the conquering of** all of **Eretz Yisrael,** and **conquering Eretz Yisrael precedes the eradication of** all the objects of **idol worship.**

The *gemara* also cites a teaching of Rav Yosef that describes three stages: *nitzatztem* — tear down the altars; then conquer; then *sreifah* — burn thoroughly. And then a third level still: *ve'ibadetem et shemam min ha'makom ha'hu* — completely eradicate even the names. This is understood as requiring that one uproot the very roots of the *avodah zarah*, not merely destroying the visible structure but digging out what lies beneath and ensuring nothing can grow back. It is both a literal instruction — if a tree, dig out its roots — and a metaphorical one: the eradication of *avodah zarah* must be thorough enough that it leaves no possibility of resurgence. For the Rabbis, this third verse — *ve'ibadetem et shemam* — also carries the additional instruction to replace the names of places, so that even the nomenclature of the land does not preserve a memory of idol worship.

### Destruction Levels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנֵי רַב יוֹסֵף: ״וְנִתַּצְתֶּם אֶת מִזְבְּחֹתָם״ וְהַנַּח, ״וְשִׁבַּרְתֶּם אֶת מַצֵּבֹתָם״ וְהַנַּח. וְהַנַּח, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ? שְׂרֵיפָה בָּעֵי! אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: רְדוֹף, וְאַחַר כָּךְ שְׂרוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** **As Rav Yosef teaches** a *baraita*: The verse states: "And you shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, and burn their *asherim* with fire." Rav Yosef explains: **"And you shall break down their altars," and leave** them, as the Torah does not prescribe that they be burned; **"and dash in pieces their pillars" and leave** them. The Gemara asks: **But does it enter your mind** that the Torah is instructing one to **leave** them alone? Doesn't an object of idol worship **require burning,** as it says at the end of the verse: "And burn their *asherim* with fire"? **Rav Huna says: Pursue** the enemy **and then** return to **burn** them. Meaning, first smash their objects of idol worship, then conquer the land, and then return to burn the smashed items.

### Name Eradication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, הַאי סְבָרָא מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מְ״אַבֵּד תְּאַבְּדוּן״ — ״אַבֵּד״, וְאַחַר כָּךְ ״תְּאַבֵּדוּן״. וְרַבָּנַן? הָא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְעוֹקֵר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִ״וְּאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם מִן הַמָּקוֹם הַהוּא״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** derive **this opinion** about the order of priorities in the process of conquering Eretz Yisrael? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: "You shall destroy all the places where the nations that you are to dispossess served their gods, upon the high mountains, and upon the hills, and under every leafy tree." From the double-verb form of the directive **"you shall destroy [*abbed te'abedun*]"** he derives that there are two stages to the destruction of their gods: First **destroy** them **[*abbed*],** i.e., smash them; then go and conquer the land, **and** only **afterward you shall destroy [*te'abedun*]** them completely, i.e., burn or eradicate them. The Gemara asks: **And** what do **the Rabbis** derive from the double verb? The Gemara answers: **This** double verb **is necessary to** teach that when one **deracinates** an object of **idol worship, he needs to root out all traces of it.** The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** derive the obligation **to root out all traces of** idol worship? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from** the verse: "And you shall break down their altars, and dash in pieces their pillars, and burn their *asherim* with fire; and you shall hew down the graven images of their gods; **and you shall destroy their name out of that place"** (Deuteronomy 12:3).

### Nicknames

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? הַהוּא לְכַנּוֹת לָהּ שֵׁם, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מִנַּיִן לְעוֹקֵר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה שֶׁצָּרִיךְ לְשָׁרֵשׁ אַחֲרֶיהָ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְאִבַּדְתֶּם אֶת שְׁמָם״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** what do **the Rabbis** derive from this verse? The Gemara answers: **That** verse teaches that it is a mitzva **to give** a derogatory **nickname to** an idol. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that Rabbi Eliezer says: **From where** is it derived that when one **deracinates** an object of **idol worship, he needs to root out all traces of it? The verse states: "And you shall destroy their name** out of that place."
