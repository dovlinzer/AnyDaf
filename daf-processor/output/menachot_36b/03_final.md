# Menachot 36b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Night & Tefillin

### Night: Time for Tefillin?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא מְצַלּוּ בְּהוּ בְּאוּרְתָּא? הָהוּא פְּלִיגָא. וּמִי אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: סָפֵק חֲשֵׁיכָה סָפֵק (לָא) [אֵין] חֲשֵׁיכָה – לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מַנִּיחַ, הָא וַדַּאי חֲשֵׁיכָה – חוֹלֵץ! הָתָם בְּעֶרֶב שַׁבָּת אִיתְּמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna would pray in the evening with** phylacteries. The Gemara explains: **That** opinion represented in this incident **disagrees** with the ruling of Rav Naḥman. The Gemara asks: **And did Rabba bar Rav Huna** actually **say this,** that the mitzva of phylacteries applies at night? **But doesn't Rabba bar Rav Huna say:** If it is **uncertain** whether it is **nightfall** or whether it is **not nightfall,** one **neither removes** his phylacteries, as it is not yet definitely night, **nor dons** them *ab initio*. **This** indicates that if it is **definitely nightfall,** one **removes** his phylacteries. The Gemara answers: Rabba bar Rav Huna's ruling **there was stated with regard to Shabbat eve,** as one may not don phylacteries on Shabbat, when the mitzva does not apply.

The daf opens in the middle of a discussion about whether night constitutes a proper time for wearing *tefillin*. The *Gemara* had already introduced several opinions about when one must remove *tefillin*: at the moment of halachic nightfall, at *bein hashmashos* (twilight), when people have finished commuting home (*tichleh regel min hashuk*), or when it is time to sleep. The latter two criteria are not, strictly speaking, halachic definitions of night and day. Rather, they reflect a rabbinic concern that if one is still wearing *tefillin* at the hour when people typically go to sleep, one risks falling asleep while wearing them.

### Sleep & Impurity

The concern about falling asleep with *tefillin* is not simply one of discomfort or negligence — it is specifically a concern that one might pass gas while unconscious. This is the driving rabbinic worry. One might reasonably ask: why is this concern limited to nighttime? Throughout the day, a person is awake and can exercise control; if one is about to pass gas, one removes the *tefillin*. The answer, of course, is that nighttime brings the risk of falling asleep unawares, which removes that conscious self-regulation.

### Mitzvah vs. Prohibition

This raises a deeper conceptual question. Even granting that *laila lav zman tefillin* — night is not a time for *tefillin* — why should that fact generate an active prohibition against continuing to wear them? There are many *mitzvos* that do not apply at night: one is not obligated to take *lulav* at night, yet no one suggests it is *assur* to hold a *lulav* after nightfall. *Tzitzis* presents an analogous case — there is debate about whether wearing a four-cornered garment at night creates any obligation, yet we do not routinely tear off our *tzitzis* at nightfall.

Part of the answer may involve *bal tosef* — adding to the commandment in a prohibited manner — if one is intentionally wearing *tefillin* at night as an act of the *mitzvah*. But beyond that, there is a deeper structural point. *Tefillin* involves two distinct dimensions: there is the active dimension of *ukshartam*, the act of binding, and there is the dimension of *vehayu*, a state of being. The *Gemara* will shortly compare *tefillin* to the *tzitz* worn by the *Kohen Gadol*, and the comparison illuminates this point. A state of being that is "always on," without temporal boundaries, loses its character as a meaningful state. For something to function as an *ot* — a sign — it requires limits. If it is perpetually present, it is no longer a sign; it is simply background.

### Rav Huna's Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר לַיְלָה זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, שַׁבָּת נָמֵי זְמַן תְּפִילִּין. אִי קָסָבַר שַׁבָּת לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, לַיְלָה נָמֵי לָאו זְמַן תְּפִילִּין, דְּמֵהֵיכָא דְּמִמַּעֲטָא שַׁבָּת מֵהָתָם מִמַּעֲטִי לֵילוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty with regard to this answer: **What does** Rabba bar Rav Huna **hold? If he holds** that **night** is **a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries,** then **Shabbat** is **also a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries. If he holds** that **Shabbat** is **not a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries,** then **night** is **also not a time** when one performs the mitzva of wearing **phylacteries.** The reason for this statement is **that from** the source **where Shabbat is excluded** from the mitzva of phylacteries, **nights are excluded from there** as well.

The *Gemara* raises a difficulty from an earlier ruling of Rabbah bar Rav Huna. The *Gemara* asks: did Rabbah bar Rav Huna really say one may continue wearing *tefillin* into the night? Yet Rabbah bar Rav Huna himself ruled that during *bein hashmashos* — the ambiguous twilight period — one neither removes the *tefillin* already on nor puts on new ones. The implication of that ruling is that once it is definitely night (*vaddai chashecha*), one must remove them (*choletz*). How, then, could Rabbah bar Rav Huna have kept his *tefillin* on when it came time to *daven Maariv*, as was described at the end of the previous *daf*?

### Shabbat Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְשָׁמַרְתָּ אֶת הַחֻקָּה הַזֹּאת לְמוֹעֲדָהּ מִיָּמִים יָמִימָה״ – ״יָמִים״ וְלֹא לֵילוֹת, ״מִיָּמִים״ וְלֹא כׇּל יָמִים, פְּרָט לְשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא נֶאֱמְרָה חוּקָּה זוֹ אֶלָּא לְפֶסַח בִּלְבָד.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* with regard to the end of the passage of the Torah that discusses both the mitzvot of the Paschal offering and phylacteries: **"And you shall observe this ordinance in its season from year [*miyamim*] to year"** (Exodus 13:10). This indicates that these mitzvot apply during the **days [*yamim*] but not** during the **nights.** Furthermore, the letter *mem*, meaning from, in the term: **"From year [*miyamim*],"** teaches: These mitzvot apply on some days, **but not** on **all days.** This **excludes *Shabbatot* and Festivals,** on which phylacteries are not worn. This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili. Rabbi Akiva says: This** verse, mentioning **an ordinance, is stated only with regard to the Paschal** offering, and it is not referring to phylacteries at all. Evidently, Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who says that at night one is exempt from the obligation of donning phylacteries, says that on Shabbat one is exempt as well.

The *Gemara* resolves this by specifying: *hatam b'erev Shabbos itmar* — the ruling about removing *tefillin* at definite nightfall was stated specifically in reference to Friday night. On a Friday night, one is not merely confronting the question of whether night is a time for *tefillin*; one is confronting *Shabbos*, which everyone agrees is *lav zman tefillin*. Rabbah bar Rav Huna may have taken a more lenient position regarding ordinary weeknights — where the concern is only about sleeping and passing gas, such that he held one need not remove *tefillin* already in place — but on Friday night, the onset of *Shabbos* itself, which is unambiguously *lav zman tefillin*, would require removal.

The *Gemara* then sharpens the question. What does Rabbah bar Rav Huna actually hold? If he holds that *laila lav zman tefillin* — that night is not a time for *tefillin* — then *Shabbos* should follow the same logic, since *Shabbos* is excluded by the same verse. If he holds that night *is* a time for *tefillin*, then *Shabbos* requires a separate exclusion. The *Gemara* answers: it is indeed from a separate source that we exclude *Shabbos*, and this is where the *Gemara* turns to the *derashos*.

## Sources for Exclusions

### V'shamarta Source

The *braisa* cited above contains the key derivation: the verse *v'shamarta es hachuka hazos l'moadah miyamim yamima* — "You shall observe this statute at its appointed time, from year to year" — appears in the context of *Pesach*, but also in proximity to the verse *v'haya l'os al yadecha ul'totafos bein einecha* regarding *tefillin*. The question is whether *v'shamarta* applies only to the *korban Pesach* or also to *tefillin*. The phrase *miyamim yamima* — "from days to days" — is read as: *yamim*, days and not nights; and *miyamim*, some days and not all days, excluding *Shabbosos* and *yamim tovim*. This is the view of Rabbi Yosi ha-Glili.

### Yosi vs. Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: יָכוֹל יַנִּיחַ אָדָם תְּפִילִּין בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וּבְיָמִים טוֹבִים? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְהָיָה לְאוֹת עַל יָדְךָ וּלְטוֹטָפֹת בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״ – מִי שֶׁצְּרִיכִין אוֹת, יָצְאוּ שַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים שֶׁהֵן גּוּפָן אוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba bar Rav Huna **derives** the exemption from the obligation to don phylacteries on Shabbat **from** a different source, the source **where Rabbi Akiva derives it from, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva says:** One **might** have thought that **a person should don phylacteries on *Shabbatot* and Festivals.** To counter this, **the verse states: "And it shall be for a sign for you on your arm, and for a remembrance between your eyes,** so that God's law shall be in your mouth; for with a strong arm God brought you out of Egypt" (Exodus 13:9). This teaches that the obligation to don phylacteries applies when the Jewish people **require a sign** to assert their status as God's nation, i.e., during the week. This serves to **exclude *Shabbatot* and Festivals, as they themselves are signs** of the Jewish people's status as God's nation and a remembrance of the exodus from Egypt. Consequently, no further sign is required on these days.

Rabbi Akiva disputes this reading. He says *lo ne'emar chuka zu ela l'Pesach bilvad* — this statute refers to *Pesach* alone, not to *tefillin*. According to Rabbi Akiva, the verse excluding night and *Shabbos* simply does not apply to *tefillin*. Yet Rabbi Akiva still holds that one does not wear *tefillin* on *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov*. How does he know this?

### Akiva's 'Ot' Logic

The *Gemara* cites another *braisa* where Rabbi Akiva states: one might think a person should put on *tefillin* on *Shabbosos* and *yamim tovim*. Therefore the verse teaches: *v'haya l'os al yadecha ul'totafos bein einecha* — the sign on your hand and the *totafos* between your eyes. The *tefillin* are worn only when they are needed to serve as an *ot*, a sign. Therefore: *yatzu Shabbosos v'yamim tovim she-gufam os* — *Shabbos* and *Yom Tov* are excluded because they themselves are an *ot*. And it is on this basis that Rabbah bar Rav Huna — who follows Rabbi Akiva — held that one may wear *tefillin* on an ordinary night but must remove them on Friday night at the onset of *Shabbos*.

### 'Ot' as Sign

The conceptual significance of the *ot* framework deserves attention. For something to function as a sign, it must have limits. If it is constant, it ceases to be a sign — it is simply the ambient condition. The implication is not merely that one is exempt from wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbos*, but that one is actively prohibited from doing so. Wearing *tefillin* on *Shabbos* would in some sense undermine the *kedushas Shabbos*, treating *Shabbos* as insufficient to serve as its own sign. The result is that both *tefillin* and *Shabbos* are diminished: neither is fully expressing its own unique character as an *ot*.

A striking parallel emerges with the *Kohen Gadol* and the *tzitz*. The *Kohen Gadol* wears the *tzitz* to maintain God-consciousness. But on *Yom Kippur*, when he performs the *Avodas Yom HaKippurim* and enters the *Kodesh Kodashim* in *bigdei lavan*, he does not wear the *tzitz*. Why? Because the *tzitz* exists to remind one of God. If one is directly encountering God in the *Kodesh Kodashim*, the *tzitz* is not only unnecessary — wearing it would in some sense be an incongruity, even a *p'gia*. The same logic applies here: who needs the *ot* of *tefillin* when one is having the encounter of *Shabbos* itself?

### Tosafot: Ot Extension

This leads directly to the question of *Chol HaMoed*, which is not discussed in the *Gemara* at all but is a major *machlokes Rishonim* that remains unresolved until today. The relevant *Tosafos* is found a few wide lines into the *daf*. *Tosafos* says: *yatzu Shabbosos v'yamim tovim she-ein atem keruyin os*. The *ot* of *Yom Tov* is not — *Tosafos* emphasizes — a function of *issur melachah*. He writes explicitly: *de-afilu Chol HaMoed de-shari ba-asiyas melachah ika os* — even on *Chol HaMoed*, where *melachah* is permitted, there is nonetheless an *ot*. What constitutes the *ot* of *Yom Tov*? *Tosafos* answers: it is the *mitzvos ha-yom* — *Pesach* through the prohibition of *chametz*, *Sukkos* through the obligation to dwell in the *sukkah*. These *mitzvos aseh* constitute the experiential memory and distinctive character of the *Yom Tov*. Since *Chol HaMoed* participates in those *mitzvos*, it too is an *ot*, and therefore one does not wear *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed* according to *Tosafos*.

Other *Rishonim* strongly disagree and ground the *ot* exclusively in *issur melachah*. On this view, *Chol HaMoed* does not fully qualify, and *tefillin* should be worn. Different communities have different *minhagim* reflecting this *machlokes*. Some who were careful to perform *melachah* on *Chol HaMoed* would wear *tefillin* there, and when they reached a stage of life where they no longer worked on *Chol HaMoed*, they stopped. A reasonable middle position is to wear *tefillin* on *Chol HaMoed* without a *berachah*, thereby fulfilling the *mitzvah* in case it is obligatory while acknowledging the *safek* — though the practical difficulty is that one tends to recite the *berachah* by force of habit.

One further dimension: the *Gemara* states that the *ot* of the *shel yad* is *lecha le-os* — for you, not for others. *V'lo le-acherim le-os*. The *ot* of the *shel yad* is internal; the *ot* that is visible to others is the *shel rosh*, about which it says *ve-ra'u kol amei ha-aretz*. This distinction is relevant because the question of whether *tefillin* functions as a *lecha le-os* or a public sign shapes how we think about who must be "seeing" the *ot*, and therefore what days or times qualify as already possessing an *ot* sufficient to render *tefillin* superfluous.

*Tosafos* also cites the famous practice of the *benei ma'arava* — the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael* — who would make a *berachah* upon removing their *tefillin*, based on the verse *v'shamarta es hachuka hazos*: *Baruch Atah Hashem... asher kidshanu be-mitzvosav ve-tzivanu lishmor chukkav*. This is a *berachah acharonah* upon removal. *Tosafos* suggests this practice would make sense specifically when one is removing *tefillin* close to nightfall — taking them off because the time of *tefillin*-wearing is ending. This reinforces the conceptual point that *tefillin* is not merely an act (*ukshartam*) but a defined state (*vehayu*), analogous to *Kiddush* and *Havdalah* that bracket *Shabbos*: one enters the state and then formally exits it. The *berachah* at removal reflects this framing of *tefillin* as a period, a bounded state of being, rather than simply a discrete act.

## Active Prohibition

### Elazar's Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: כׇּל הַמַּנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין אַחַר שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה עוֹבֵר בַּעֲשֵׂה, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: עוֹבֵר בְּלָאו. לֵימָא בְּרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ ״פֶּן״ וְ״אַל״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says: Anyone who dons phylacteries after sunset violates a positive mitzva. And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He violates a prohibition.** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that these Sages **disagree with regard to** the principle that **Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a says. As Rabbi Avin says** that **Rabbi Ile'a says: Any place where it is stated** in the Torah any of the terms: **Observe,** or: **Lest, or: Do not,** this means **nothing other than a prohibition,** as these are negative terms.

The *Gemara* now states explicitly: *amar Rabbi Elazar: kol hamaniach tefillin achar shekias hachamah over b'aseh* — one who leaves *tefillin* on after sunset violates a positive commandment. The prohibition is derived from the verse *v'shamarta es hachuka hazos*, which the *Gemara* reads as a command to actively observe the proper time-bound parameters of *tefillin*.

### Elazar vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָא מִיפַּלְגִי: מָר סָבַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ דְּלָאו – לָאו, וְ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ דַּעֲשֵׂה – עֲשֵׂה, וּמָר סָבַר ״הִשָּׁמֶר״ דַּעֲשֵׂה נָמֵי לָאו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara counters: **No, everyone is of** the opinion that the ruling is in accordance with the opinion **that Rabbi Avin** says that **Rabbi Ile'a says, and here they disagree with regard to this:** One **Sage,** Rabbi Elazar, **holds** that the term **"observe"** written **with regard to a prohibition** has the status of **a prohibition,** whereas that same term **"observe"** written **with regard to a positive** mitzva has the status of **a positive** mitzva, as the Torah is issuing a warning to take special care in the observance of a mitzva. Accordingly, the command with regard to the positive mitzva of phylacteries is a positive mitzva. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that the term **"observe"** written **with regard to a positive** mitzva is **also a prohibition.**

Rabbi Yochanan goes further: *over belav* — one violates a negative prohibition. The dispute turns on the rule articulated by Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Ila: whenever the Torah uses the formulation *hishamer*, *pen*, or *al*, it always denotes a *lo sa'aseh*, a negative prohibition. The *Gemara* initially suggests that Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Yochanan may simply disagree about whether to accept this rule. The *Gemara* rejects this: *kulei alma is lehu de-Rabbi Avin* — everyone accepts the rule of Rabbi Avin. The dispute is more nuanced. Rabbi Elazar holds that *hishamer de-aseh* — a *shimur* formulation attached to a positive obligation — produces a positive commandment, not a negative one. Rabbi Yochanan holds that *hishamer de-aseh nami lav* — the *shimur* language always generates a *lo sa'aseh* regardless of whether it is commanding action or restraint.

### Protection Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: וְאִם לְשׇׁמְרָן – מוּתָּר. וְאָמַר רָבִינָא: הֲוָה יָתֵיבְנָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי וְחָשַׁךְ, וְהִנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין, וַאֲמַרִי לֵיהּ: לְשׇׁמְרָן קָא בָעֵי לְהוּ מָר? וַאֲמַר לִי: אִין. וַחֲזֵיתֵיהּ לְדַעְתֵּיהּ דְּלָאו לְשׇׁמְרָן הוּא בָּעֵי, קָסָבַר: הֲלָכָה וְאֵין מוֹרִין כֵּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Elazar says: And** although it is prohibited to don phylacteries at night, **if** one does so in order **to safeguard them** from theft and the like, it is **permitted. And Ravina said: I was sitting before Rav Ashi and** it **grew dark, and he donned phylacteries. And I said to him: Does the Master need to safeguard them? And he said to me: Yes. But I saw that his intention** in donning them was **not** that he **needed to safeguard them;** rather, Rav Ashi **holds:** This is the ***halakha*,** that night is an appropriate time for phylacteries, **but** a public **ruling is not issued to that effect.**

The *Gemara* qualifies the prohibition: *amar Rabbi Elazar: de-im leshomram mutar* — if one's intention in keeping *tefillin* on at night is to protect them, it is permissible. *Rashi* extends this further: it is even permissible to put *tefillin* on at night for the purpose of protecting them — for example, if one is traveling and fears that removing the *tefillin* would result in damage or loss. The classic *Gemara* discussion about *Shabbos* offers a similar context: if one finds *tefillin* in a public domain on *Shabbos* and has no *eiruv*, is one permitted to wear them in order to carry them to safety? This scenario interweaves both the question of *Shabbos lav zman tefillin* and the question of whether wearing them constitutes carrying. The rationale for permitting such wearing for protection is that *tefillin* worn purely for protective purposes are not being worn as an *ot* — and if they are not functioning as an *ot*, the concern that generates the prohibition does not apply.

### Rav Ashi's Case

Ravina reports a striking anecdote: *havina yasivna kamei de-Rav Ashi* — I was sitting before Rav Ashi. It grew dark, and Rav Ashi put on *tefillin*. The others asked him: *lishmor ka bei lehu mar?* — Are you doing this to protect them? He said yes. But Ravina records: *vachaziteih bedateih de-lav lishmor bei* — I could see from his manner that protecting them was not actually his intention. A textual note: the *Tzeidah LaDerech* emends the reading, suggesting Rav Ashi had not put them on but merely left them on, which would better conform to the earlier *Gemara* holding that at minimum everyone agrees one does not *put on* *tefillin* at night. But the core point stands regardless of the version.

### Halacha V'Ein Morin

Why the ruse? *Kasavar: halacha v'ein morin kein.* Rav Ashi held the *ikar halacha* to be that night is indeed a time for *tefillin*, but he held equally that this *halacha* should not be publicly taught — *halacha v'ein morin kein*. The reason is evident: if people knew that wearing *tefillin* at night was permitted, it would inevitably lead to sleeping with them on and passing gas, which the Rabbis were specifically concerned to prevent. Even though *me'ikar hadin* one could leave them on well into the night, this was not something to publicize.

This raises interesting broader questions about the doctrine of *halacha v'ein morin kein*. Does a rabbi in such a situation say the *halacha* is otherwise? Does he simply decline to answer? Does he permit some degree of obfuscation? A famous *Gemara* in *Beitzah* illustrates the tension: someone asked an *Amora* whether it was permitted to extinguish a light on *Yom Tov* for a couple wishing privacy. The *Amora* kept suggesting alternatives — go to another room, put up a partition — and finally expressed exasperation. Several *Rishonim* conclude that the *Amora* held *halacha v'ein morin kein* and therefore refused to state the *halacha* directly, but the questioner was not taking the hint. The broader tension — between accurately representing *halacha* and managing what information flows to the general public — is something the *Gemara* takes seriously as a genuine category of rabbinic authority.

## Constant Awareness

### Touching Tefillin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: חַיָּיב אָדָם לְמַשְׁמֵשׁ בַּתְּפִילִּין בְּכׇל שָׁעָה, קַל וָחוֹמֶר מִצִּיץ, וּמָה צִיץ שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ אֶלָּא אַזְכָּרָה אַחַת, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״וְהָיָה עַל מִצְחוֹ תָּמִיד״, שֶׁלֹּא (תַּסִּיחַ) [יַסִּיחַ] דַּעְתּוֹ מִמֶּנּוּ, תְּפִילִּין שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהֶן אַזְכָּרוֹת הַרְבֵּה, עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Rav Huna says: A person is obligated to touch** his **phylacteries** regularly for **the entire time** that he is wearing them. This is derived from **an *a fortiori*** inference **from** the **frontplate** of the High Priest, as follows: **And if** with regard to the **frontplate, which has only one mention** of God's name, **the Torah states: "And it should be always upon his forehead"** (Exodus 28:38), which means that the High Priest must always be aware that the frontplate is placed on his head and **that he should not be distracted from it,** then with regard to **phylacteries, which have numerous mentions** of God's name, **all the more so** one must always be aware of them.

Rabbah bar Rav Huna teaches: *chayav adam lemashmesh bitefillin bechol sha'ah* — a person is obligated to constantly be touching and aware of his *tefillin* at every moment. The term *lemashmesh* is instructive: while it might suggest literal touching, the underlying intention is to remain continuously conscious of the fact that one is wearing *tefillin*, not to allow one's attention to drift entirely elsewhere.

### Kal Vachomer

This obligation is derived through a *kal vachomer* from the *tzitz*. The *tzitz* bears only a single mention of God's name — *Kodesh l'Hashem*. Yet the Torah says *ve-hayah al mitzcho tamid* — it shall be on his forehead *always*. From the word *tamid*, the *Gemara* derives *she-lo yasiach da'ato heimenu* — he must never allow his awareness of it to lapse. *Tefillin*, by contrast, contain many mentions of God's name. *Al achas kama v'chama* — how much more so must one maintain constant awareness of *tefillin*.

### Purpose: Awareness

The conceptual significance is profound. The purpose of carrying *Shem Hashem* on one's body — whether in the *tzitz* or in *tefillin* — is to sustain God-consciousness. If one's awareness lapses entirely, the purpose of the *mitzvah* is defeated even while one remains technically in compliance with wearing it. The *Gemara*'s comparison to the *tzitz* returns us to the earlier theme: just as the *Kohen Gadol*'s *tzitz* and the *tefillin* of every Jew both function to direct consciousness toward God, both require that consciousness to be maintained to fulfill their purpose. And just as the *tzitz* is not worn in the *Kodesh Kodashim* because that encounter supersedes the need for a symbolic reminder, so too does *Shabbos*, as an encounter with holiness, render the symbolic *ot* of *tefillin* unnecessary — indeed, inappropriate.

## Shel Yad Placement

### 'Yadcha' = Left

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״יָדְךָ״ – זוֹ שְׂמֹאל. אַתָּה אוֹמֵר שְׂמֹאל, אוֹ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא יָמִין? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אַף יָדִי יָסְדָה אֶרֶץ וִימִינִי טִפְּחָה שָׁמָיִם״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״יָדָהּ לַיָּתֵד תִּשְׁלַחְנָה וִימִינָהּ לְהַלְמוּת עֲמֵלִים״, וְאוֹמֵר: ״לָמָּה תָשִׁיב יָדְךָ וִימִינֶךָ מִקֶּרֶב חֵיקְךָ כַלֵּה״.
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** with regard to the verse: "And it shall be for a sign for you on **your arm [*yadkha*]"** (Exodus 13:9), that **this** is referring to the **left** arm. **Do you say** it means the **left** arm, **or is it only** the **right** arm? **The verse states: "Even My hand [*yadi*] has laid the foundation of the earth, and My right hand [*vimini*] has spread out the heavens"** (Isaiah 48:13). **And** another verse **states: "Her hand [*yadah*] she put to the tent pin, and her right hand [*viminah*] to the workmen's hammer"** (Judges 5:26), **and** another verse **states: "Why do You withdraw Your hand [*yadkha*], even Your right hand [*viminekha*]? Draw it out of Your bosom and consume them"** (Psalms 74:11). All these verses employ the term *yad* with regard to the left hand, and use the term *yamin*, literally, right, without the term *yad*, to indicate the right hand.

A *braisa* is cited: *yadcha zu smol* — *yadcha*, "your hand," refers to your left hand. The question is raised: perhaps it refers to the right? Two proofs are offered from *Tanach*. First: *af yadi yasdah eretz vi-ymini tipchah shamayim* — "My hand founded the earth and My right hand stretched out the heavens." Here *yad* is distinguished from *yamin*, which denotes the right, so *yad* by itself would refer to the left. A second proof, from the story of Yael: *yadah la-yased tishlachnah vi-yminah la-halmus amelim* — "Her hand she stretched toward the tent peg, and her right hand toward the workers' hammer." Since Yael needs one hand on the tent peg and the other to wield the hammer, the two hands must be distinct. The *yad* holding the peg is her left hand; the *yamin* wielding the hammer is her right. This proof is stronger because it involves a two-handed action that clearly differentiates the two.

### HaCharum Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַחוֹרָם אוֹמֵר: מָצִינוּ יָמִין שֶׁנִּקְרָא יָד, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא יוֹסֵף כִּי יָשִׁית אָבִיו יַד יְמִינוֹ״. וְאִידַּךְ – ״יַד יְמִינוֹ״ אִיקְּרִי, ״יָד״ סְתָמָא לָא אִיקְּרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaḤorem says:** This is no proof, as **we have found that** the **right** hand is also **called *yad*, as it is stated: "And when Joseph saw that his father was laying his right hand [*yad yemino*]"** (Genesis 48:17). The Gemara asks: **And the other** *tanna*, who maintains that the right hand is not called *yad*, how does he respond to this proof? He maintains that the right hand **is called "his right hand [*yad yemino*],"** but it **is not called a *yad* without** further **specification.**

Rabbi Yosi HaCharum — about whom *Tosafos* notes that *Rashi*'s reading of his name as a reference to a physical feature (*chotmo meshuka*, a pressed-in nose) is difficult, since calling a *Tanna* by a derogatory nickname is problematic; *Tosafos* suggests *Chorem* was the name of a place — objects that *yad* can in fact refer to the right hand. His proof: *vayar Yosef ki yashis aviv yad yemino* — "And Joseph saw that his father placed his right hand." Here *yad* is used with the modifier *yemino*, the right. The *Gemara* responds: *yad yemino ikrei* — with an explicit modifier specifying the right, *yad* may indeed refer to the right. But *yad stama* — *yad* without any modifier — *lo ikrei*, it would not naturally refer to the right. Unmodified *yad* denotes the left.

### Natan: Bind Right

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר: אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּקְשַׁרְתָּם״ ״וּכְתַבְתָּם״ – מָה כְּתִיבָה בְּיָמִין, אַף קְשִׁירָה בְּיָמִין, וְכֵיוָן דִּקְשִׁירָה בְּיָמִין – הַנָּחָה בִּשְׂמֹאל הִיא. וְרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַחוֹרָם, הַנָּחָה דְּבִשְׂמֹאל מְנָא לֵיהּ? נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מֵהֵיכָא דְּנָפְקָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי נָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Natan says:** This proof is **not necessary,** as **it says: "And you shall bind them** for a sign upon your arm" (Deuteronomy 6:8), and then it states: **"And you shall write them** upon the doorposts of your house" (Deuteronomy 6:9). This teaches that **just as writing is with** the **right** hand, as most people write with their right hands, **so too,** the **binding** of phylacteries must be performed **with** the **right** hand. **And since binding is with** the **right** hand, this means that **donning is on** the **left** arm, as one cannot bind the phylacteries with the same hand upon which he is donning them. The Gemara asks: **And from where does Rabbi Yosei HaḤorem,** who holds that the right hand is also called *yad* in the Torah, **derive that donning** phylacteries is **on** the **left** arm? The Gemara answers: **He derives it from where Rabbi Natan derives it.**

Rabbi Natan offers an independent derivation. He says: *eino tzarich* — there is no need for those proofs. The verse says *ukeshartem ukhtavtam* — "you shall bind and you shall write." Just as writing (*ktivah*) is done with the right hand, so too binding (*kshirah*) is done with the right hand. And since the *kshirah* must be performed by the right hand, the *tefillin* must be placed on the left — for one binds upon the arm that bears the *tefillin* using the opposite, stronger hand.

This derivation reframes the *mitzvah* in a significant way. Rather than saying "the *halacha* is that *tefillin* is worn on the weak hand," Rabbi Natan says: "the *halacha* is that *tefillin* must be bound with the right hand; the consequence is that they are placed on the left." This shifts the conceptual center of gravity. The *ikkar* of the *mitzvah*, on this reading, is the act of *ukshartam* — the binding — and the placement on the left arm is a derivative of that primary obligation. This bears on the broader question of whether *tefillin* is primarily a *mitzvah* of *ukshartam*, an active binding, or a *mitzvah* of wearing and the resultant state of *vehayu*.

### Weak vs. Left

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מִ״יָּדְכָה״ כְּתִיב, בְּהֵ״י כֵּהָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: וְאֵימָא ״יָדְךָ״ שֶׁבְּכֹחַ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי כְּתִיב בְּחֵי״ת?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** The requirement that phylacteries be donned on the left arm is derived **from** the verse: "It shall be for a sign upon **your arm [*yadkha*]"** (Exodus 13:16), which **is written with** a letter ***heh*** at the end. This is expounded as though it stated: Your **weak [*keha*]** arm. **Rabbi Abba said to Rav Ashi: But** one can **say** that *yadkha* should be interpreted as *yadko'aḥ*, with a letter *ḥet* at the end instead of a *heh*. If so, this would mean: **Your arm that is of strength [*shebeko'aḥ*],** which is the right arm. Rav Ashi **said to** Rabbi Abba: **Is** this word **written with a *ḥet*?**

The *Gemara* also introduces a third derivation: from the word *yadechah* written with a *kaf-hei* (as opposed to a *kaf sofit*), read as *yad kehah* — your weak arm. Rava challenges: perhaps the *kaf-hei* stands for *koach*, indicating the strong arm. Rav Ashi responds: it is not written with a *ches* but with a *hei*; therefore it means *kehah*, weak, not *koach*, strong.

The *nafka mina* between the objective criterion (left hand) and the subjective criterion (weak hand) is the case of a lefty. According to the objective reading, every person — righty or lefty — wears *tefillin* on their anatomical left hand. According to the *yad kehah* reading, a lefty wears *tefillin* on their right hand, because that is their weak hand. A *braisa* states: *iter maniach tefillin bi-yemino* — a lefty puts his *tefillin* on his right hand, meaning his weak hand, which is anatomically his right. Another *braisa* appears to say even a lefty puts it on his anatomical left. Abaye resolves the contradiction: the second *braisa* refers to *shotef shtei yadav* — an ambidextrous person, who has no dominant hand. For such a person, we follow the majority of people and treat the person as right-handed, placing *tefillin* on the anatomical left.

### Tosafot Refinement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: ״יָדְכָה״ בְּהֵ״י – זוֹ שְׂמֹאל, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״יָדְכָה״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגִּידֵּם. תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵין לוֹ זְרוֹעַ – פָּטוּר מִן הַתְּפִילִּין, אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: ״יָדְכָה״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַגִּידֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that Rav Ashi's opinion, that the *halakha* that phylacteries are donned on the left arm is derived from the term *yadkha*, is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in a *baraita*: ***Yadkha*** is written **with a *heh*,** indicating weakness, and **this** is referring to the **left** arm. **Others say: "Your arm,"** i.e., *yadkha*, serves **to include one without** a complete **arm,** i.e., one whose arm ends at the elbow, in the obligation to don phylacteries, as the remaining part is also categorized as a weak arm. It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: If one **does not have** a left **arm,** i.e., not even above the elbow, he is **exempt from the** mitzva of **phylacteries. Others say: *Yadkha*** serves **to include one without** a left **arm** even above the elbow, teaching that he must don phylacteries on his right arm.

*Tosafos* raises a more nuanced case: what about someone who writes with their right hand but performs most other tasks with their left — or vice versa? *Tosafos* poses the question explicitly: *yeish l'histapeik d'eizeh meihen maniach tefillin* — which hand does such a person use? If we emphasize *ukeshartam ukhtavtam*, the hand of writing is determinative, and such a person is treated as a righty. If we emphasize *yad kehah*, then the question of which is truly the "weak" hand becomes complex, and the answer may depend on which activities we weight more heavily.

*Tosafos* concludes: *v'shema yeish l'dimoto k'shotef shtei yadav* — perhaps such a person is treated like an ambidextrous person, defaulting to the majority rule and placing *tefillin* on the left. The consideration here is that on the *ukeshartam ukhtavtam* reading, writing clearly tips in favor of treating this person as right-handed. And even on the *yad kehah* reading, writing is a highly significant activity, so the *kehah* of the non-writing hand remains arguable. The two considerations compound: *ukeshartam* clearly points to treating the person as a righty, and *yad kehah* is at best indeterminate, so together they favor the anatomical left.

The case becomes more complicated in the mirror image: a person who writes with the left but does everything else with the right. Here *ukeshartam ukhtavtam* points to treating this person as a lefty — placing *tefillin* on the right hand — while *yad kehah* arguably points the other way, or at minimum is unclear. The halachic literature on people who were trained to write right-handed in Europe despite being naturally left-handed engages exactly this question and reflects the genuine conceptual tension between these two derivations.

## Physical Requirements

### Gidam Debate

The *Gemara* addresses the case of a *gidam* — a person with an amputated arm. One *braisa* states: *ein lo zeroa patur min ha-tefillin* — if a person does not have an arm, he is exempt from *tefillin*. Another *braisa* cites *Acheirim*: the word *yadechah* — with the *kaf-hei* of *yad kehah* — comes to include even the *gidam*, indicating that even a person with an amputation is obligated. The presumption is that the amputation is not complete — that enough of the muscle of the upper arm remains to constitute a viable *mekom hanachah*.

### Shulchan Aruch Ruling

The *Rema* in *Siman* 27 rules: a *gidam* who has lost the hand but retains the *zeroa* — the upper arm — puts on *tefillin belo berachah*, without a blessing, splitting the difference between the two *beraisos* given the halachic *safek*. The *Mishnah Berurah* adds: if there is no left arm at all, and there is no place to put the *tefillin shel yad*, then the person does not wear the *shel yad*. One might ask whether such a person wears the *shel rosh* alone. The answer is yes — and with both *berachos*, since the *shel rosh* requires two *berachos* of its own when worn without the *shel yad*.

### Missing Right Arm

If, however, it is the right arm that is missing — meaning the person has no arm with which to perform the binding — the *Mishnah Berurah* rules that someone else performs the *kshirah* on his behalf. He still wears *tefillin* on his left arm; he simply cannot do the binding himself. This ruling is highly revealing for the conceptual question. If the primary *mitzvah* were the act of *ukshartam* — binding — one might say that a person who cannot bind is an *onnes* who is entirely exempt. Instead, the *halacha* assumes that the *mitzvah* of wearing *tefillin* remains fully in force, and the physical binding is merely a means to that end. The person still has a left arm on which to *wear* *tefillin*, and someone assists in placing them there.

### Feinstein: Paralysis

Rav Moshe Feinstein was asked about a person who suffers paralysis in his left arm. Is this person in a situation analogous to not having an arm — and therefore exempt or required to wear without a *berachah* — or is the arm halachically present since it is physically there? Rav Moshe distinguishes carefully. If the arm is shriveled and has no blood flowing through it — even if it remains physically attached — he holds it is not considered a halachically viable arm, and the person would not make a *berachah*. However, if the paralysis stems from a neurological cause — a problem in the brain or spinal cord — and the arm itself is physically healthy with normal blood flow and no atrophy, then the arm is fully present. The inability to move it does not render it halachically absent. Such a person wears *tefillin* on the paralyzed arm with a full *berachah*.

Rav Moshe adds a poignant personal note at the close of the *teshuvah*: *z'chorni she-abba mari hagaon zatzal she-hayah lo alaynu choleh shiva chodashim ha'acharonim machamat machalas haparalysis mitzido hasmoli* — "I recall that my father, the *gaon*, spent the last seven months of his life ill from paralysis of his left side, and he put on *tefillin* on his left paralyzed arm" — in accordance with this ruling.

## Shel Rosh Location

### Anatomy Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא דְּבֵי מְנַשֶּׁה: ״עַל יָדְךָ״ – זוֹ קִיבּוֹרֶת, ״בֵּין עֵינֶיךָ״ – זוֹ קׇדְקֹד. הֵיכָא? אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: מְקוֹם שֶׁמּוֹחוֹ שֶׁל תִּינוֹק רוֹפֵס.
>
> **Translation:** **The school of Menashe taught** with regard to the verse: "And you shall bind them for a sign on your arm, and they shall be as frontlets between your eyes" (Deuteronomy 6:8): **"On your arm"; this** is the **bicep. "Between your eyes"; this** is the **crown** of the head. The Gemara asks: **Where** exactly on the crown of the head are the phylacteries placed? **The school of Rabbi Yannai say:** Phylacteries are placed on the **place where** the bone above **the baby's brain is soft** after birth.

*Tanna d'vei Menashe* provides the anatomical definitions: *al yadecha* — "on your hand" — refers to *kibores*, the muscle of the upper arm. *Bein einecha* — "between your eyes" — refers to *kadkod*, the skull or cranium, not the forehead. These definitions resolve a natural reading: the verse seems to say the *tefillin* of the head should go literally between the eyebrows. *Tanna d'vei Menashe* establishes that *bein einecha* means the top of the skull.

### Infant's Soft Spot

The school of Rabbi Yannai pinpoints the location more precisely: *makom she-mocho shel tinok rofes* — the place where the soft spot on an infant's skull is located. This is the fontanelle, situated not near the forehead but higher up on the crown. The *Gemara* also establishes that *tefillin* must be placed *bemakom se'ar*, where hair grows, and notes that there is a sufficient span of space on the head to accommodate *tefillin* — indeed, the *Gemara* says *makom yesh ba-rosh lehaniach shenei tefillin*, there is room on the head for two sets of *tefillin*, alluding to the dispute between *Rashi* and *Rabbeinu Tam*. In practice, one places the bottom edge of the *tefillin* at the hairline and the *tefillin* sits upward from there.

### Two Heads Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ פְּלֵימוֹ מֵרַבִּי: מִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ שְׁנֵי רָאשִׁים, בְּאֵיזֶה מֵהֶן מַנִּיחַ תְּפִילִּין? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אוֹ קוּם גְּלִי, אוֹ קַבֵּל עֲלָךְ שַׁמְתָּא. אַדְּהָכִי אֲתָא הָהוּא גַּבְרָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיתְיְלִיד לִי יָנוֹקָא דְּאִית לֵיהּ תְּרֵי רֵישֵׁי, כַּמָּה בָּעֵינָא לְמִיתַּב לְכֹהֵן? אֲתָא הָהוּא סָבָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ: חַיָּיב לִיתֵּן לוֹ עֲשָׂרָה סְלָעִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Sage **Peleimu raised a dilemma before Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi: In the case of **one who has two heads, on which of them does he don phylacteries?** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi **said to him: Either get up** and **exile** yourself from here **or accept upon yourself excommunication** for asking such a ridiculous question. **In the meantime, a certain man arrived** and **said to** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi: A firstborn **child has been born to me who has two heads. How much** money **must I give to the priest** for the redemption of the firstborn? **A certain elder came** and **taught him: You are obligated to give him ten *sela*,** the requisite five for each head.

The *Gemara* introduces one of its most striking narratives. Plimo — a name that may itself be significant, since *peli* in Aramaic means to mock or jest — asked Rabbi: if someone had two heads, on which one does he put *tefillin*? Rabbi was outraged and told him: either go into exile (*kum gali*) or accept *shamta* upon yourself — some form of communal sanction — as punishment for asking such a disrespectful, absurd question.

At that very moment, a man walked into the *beis medrash* and said: a baby was just born to me with two heads — *ittalid li yenuka d'is lei srei rashi* — and since it is a *bechor*, how much do I owe the *kohen*? The verse says one gives five *shekalim per gulgoles* — per head. One *bechor*, two heads: does he give five *shekalim* or ten?

A certain elder arrived and taught a *braisa*: *chayav lisein lo yud sla'im* — he must give ten *sela'im*. So the absurd case not only actually occurred, but had been codified in halachic literature. Rabbi had grown furious at what he took to be a frivolous question, only to discover that the question was entirely legitimate, had a precedent in a real case, and had been formally addressed *lehalacha*.

The *Gemara* then notes that a *braisa* of Rami bar Chama raises another complication: the verse *pado tifdeh bechor ha-adam* might imply an obligation of *pidyon* even if the infant is a *treifah* — that is, cannot survive. The *Gemara* cites *ach* to exclude this. The *Gemara* then asks: but the case of two heads may itself suggest non-viability — would such an infant be considered a *treifah*? The *Gemara* responds: *shani hacha de-begulgoleth tala Rachmana* — the Torah's formulation "per head" makes the *pidyon* contingent on heads, and once the questions of viability and *treifah* status are addressed, the question of five or ten *shekalim* is entirely legitimate.

The *Gemara* ends without telling us which head the *tefillin* goes on.

### Height Derivation

The *Gemara* now returns to the derivation. It asks: *minalan* — from where do we know that the *shel rosh* goes on the height of the head rather than literally between the eyes? A *braisa* derives it through *gezerah shavah*: the same phrase *bein einecha* appears in the context of *tefillin* and in the prohibition against making a bald spot: *lo tasimu karcha bein eineichem lamet* — do not make a bald spot between your eyes for the dead. The latter passage refers unambiguously to the area where hair grows, and the *gematria* of bodies confirms this is the height of the head. *Mah lehallan b'gova sheba-rosh makom she-oseh karchah* — just as there it is the height of the head where a bald spot is made, so too here.

### Yehuda Alternative

Rabbi Yehuda offers an independent derivation from the laws of *negaim*, skin lesions. The Torah places *tefillin* on the hand and on the head: *hanach tefillin beyad hanach tefillin barosh*. Just as the *tefillin* of the hand is placed where only a single category of *nega* applies — skin lesions — so too the *tefillin* of the head must be placed where only a single category of *nega* applies. If the *tefillin* were placed literally between the eyes — on the forehead just above the eyebrows — there would be two categories of *negaim* present: the skin of the forehead and the hair of the eyebrows. There would be the *nega* of white hair (*seiar lavan*) and the *nega* of yellow hair (*seiar tzahov*), each applicable to a different substrate. The only place on the head where only a single category of *nega* applies is the scalp covered by hair — *bemakom se'ar* alone — and that is where *tefillin* goes.

With this derivation, the *Gemara*'s treatment of *tefillin* concludes, and the *masechta* turns to the laws of *tzitzis*, which will occupy the next chapter entirely.
