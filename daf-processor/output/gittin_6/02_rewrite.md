# Gittin 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rabbah & Rava Debate

### lishmah vs. Signatures

Our daf begins with a brief but essential summary of the debate developed in the preceding pages. The dispute between Rabbah and Rava concerns the reason for the requirement that a *shaliach* delivering a *get* from *medinat hayam* must declare *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam* — "it was written in my presence and signed in my presence." Rabbah's position focuses on *lishmah*: the declaration ensures that the proper intention required for a *get* — that it be written for the sake of this particular husband and wife — was indeed observed. This concern is reflected primarily in the phrase *bifani nechtav*, in my presence during the writing. Rava, by contrast, understands the declaration as a mechanism for validating the signatures, preventing the husband from later claiming the document was a forgery. His position is reflected more in *bifani nechtam*, in my presence during the signing.

### Both Positions Align

The *gemara* ultimately concludes that the distinction between these two positions is less stark than it initially appears. Rabbah, it emerges, is *also* concerned with the validation rationale that Rava emphasizes — he holds both reasons simultaneously. Rava, for his part, is concerned only with validation. Moreover, the *gemara* noted that since the laws of *lishmah* had come to be well known even in *medinat hayam*, Rabbah's *lishmah* concern was effectively reduced to a *lechatchila* standard. The result is that the two positions converge almost entirely in practice. With this summary established, the *gemara* now proceeds to examine the concrete details of how *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam* operates across a range of circumstances.

## Bar Hadaya Case

### Every Letter Present

The *gemara* introduces the case of Bar Hadaya, who wished to bring a *get* and came before Rabbi Achi, who had been appointed to oversee the proper execution of *gittin* in that city. As Rashi notes, the principle that one who does not know the intricacies of *gittin* and *kiddushin* should have no dealings with them demanded that a figure of genuine authority supervise the process. Rabbi Achi instructed Bar Hadaya: *tzarich at l'amod al kol ot ve'ot* — you must stand present as every single letter of the *get* is written, from the first stroke through the final signature. The requirement of *bifani nechtav* thus raises an immediate question: what does "in my presence" actually demand? How present must the *shaliach* be throughout the writing?

### Leniency & Chumras

Bar Hadaya brought this question to Rabbi Ammi and Rabbi Assi, who ruled that Rabbi Achi's requirement was excessive — the *shaliach* need not be present for every single letter. One might nonetheless argue: even if it is not strictly required, why not adopt Rabbi Achi's stricter standard as a *chumra*? The *gemara* answers this decisively: *nimtza ata motzi la'az al gittin harishonim* — by insisting on a heightened standard, one implicitly casts ill repute upon all earlier *gittin* that were executed without such care. People will hear that one was *makpid* to supervise every letter and will begin to question the validity of documents delivered by *shelichim* who did not observe this practice. This is a critical principle about the real costs of unnecessary stringency: a *chumra* is never without consequences, and those consequences often fall upon others.

### Earlier Gittin Concern

The concern for *gittin harishonim* — not besmirching past practice — serves as an independent brake on the impulse toward stringency in this domain. Where the practical effect of stricter behavior is to impugn what has already been done and accepted, the *gemara* treats that as a sufficient reason to refrain.

## Partial Presence

### One Line Suffices

The *gemara* next records the case of Rabbah bar bar Chana, who brought a *get* in which half had been written in his presence and half had not — the *gemara* does not specify which half. He came before Rabbi Elazar, who ruled: *afilu lo katav bo ela shita achat lishmah shuv eino tzarich* — even if only one line was written *lishmah* in his presence, that is sufficient. The ruling is noteworthy for its invocation of *lishmah*, addressing the Rabbah dimension of the requirement. The reasoning would seem to be that if the *sofer* was observed declaring the proper intent for even one line, one may assume that the *sofer* understood and maintained that intent throughout. Tosafot explains the case somewhat differently: the reference to one line means the *shaliach* saw the first line being written, and from that beginning one may assume *lishmah* was maintained through the end. That reading is perhaps less straightforward than the plain sense of the *gemara*, which speaks of "any one line" and grants sufficiency regardless of position in the document.

### Hearing Quill/Klaf

Rav Ashi goes further still, requiring even less than one line of direct observation. *Afilu kan kulmusa vekan megilta* — even if one merely heard the preparation of the quill and the *klaf* being readied, that is sufficient. Rashi's variant reading is *kal kulmusa*, the sound of the quill, and he explains: if one heard the preparation and, in that context, heard them declaring that this was being done *lishmah* — for the sake of the husband and wife — even that degree of auditory awareness constitutes sufficient presence. The word *bifani* is thus stretched considerably: one did not see the writing, one did not witness the writing itself, one may not even have been present during the writing — and yet the awareness created by hearing the preparation in context suffices.

### Medinat Hayam Case

A *baraita* is cited in support of Rav Ashi's position. A *shaliach* bringing a *get* from *medinat hayam*: even if he was downstairs and the *sofer* was upstairs, or the *sofer* downstairs and the *shaliach* upstairs, and even if he was going in and out throughout the entire day — *kasher*. The document is valid and the declaration *bifani nechtav* may be made.

### Bifani Meaning

The *gemara* pauses to interrogate the *baraita*. If the *sofer* is on the upper floor and the *shaliach* is below, he cannot see anything. How does *bifani* — in my presence — apply? The *gemara* answers: *ela lav d'shama kan kulmusa vekan megilta* — it must mean that he could hear the activity upstairs, that there was some form of awareness even without visual contact. *Bifani*, in my presence, encompasses this kind of ambient awareness. It is also worth observing that this *baraita* does not mention *lishmah* at all. Consistent with the conclusion that Rabbah's concern is ultimately reducible in practical terms, and that the *lishmah* issue is primarily *lechatchila*, the *baraita* appears to address the requirement without any *lishmah* qualification.

## In & Out Motion

### Sofer Marketplace

The *gemara* now examines the phrase from the *baraita* — *afilu nichnas veyotzei kol hayom kulo kasher*, even going in and out all day, it is valid — and asks: who is going in and out? If it refers to the *shaliach*, the question answers itself: since even physical separation of floors is permitted, surely the case where the *shaliach* steps in and out of the room where the *sofer* is present poses no difficulty. That case is obvious and needs no ruling. The *gemara* therefore concludes that the *baraita* must be referring to the *sofer* — the scribe who steps out while the *shaliach* remains. The ruling then tells us that even when the *sofer* leaves the premises and returns, the *shaliach* may still declare *bifani nechtav*.

### Distraction Concern

But even this seems obvious — why would the *sofer* stepping out invalidate anything? The *gemara* answers: *lo tzricha* — the *baraita*'s ruling is needed for a specific case. *D'nafik l'shuka ve'asa* — the *sofer* went out to the marketplace and returned. One might have thought that while in the marketplace, another person approached him and asked him to write a *get* for his own wife. If, when returning, the *sofer* carried that second commission in mind, his *lishmah* intent may have become confused. The *baraita* teaches us that we are not concerned for this possibility. We note that this concern again touches on *lishmah* — suggesting that the question of intent remains relevant in evaluating the *sofer*'s conduct throughout the writing, even as the evidentiary threshold for the *shaliach*'s own presence has been set quite low.

## Bavel's Status

### Rav vs. Shmuel

The *gemara* now turns from the mechanics of *bifani nechtav* to the geographic question of which regions require it at all. The *mishnah* distinguished between Eretz Yisrael, where no declaration is needed, and *medinat hayam*, where it is. The question now is: what is the status of Bavel? At the time of the *Bavli*, Bavel was not a marginal outpost but the center of Jewish life, home to the majority of the Jewish population. The debate is presented thus: *Rav amar k'Eretz Yisrael l'gittin* — Rav holds that Bavel has the same status as Eretz Yisrael, and no declaration is required when transmitting a *get* from one location to another within Bavel. *Shmuel amar k'chutza la'aretz* — Shmuel holds it is like the diaspora, and the declaration is required.

### Two Explanations

The *gemara* initially proposes that this debate maps onto the Rabbah-Rava dispute: perhaps Rav focuses on *lishmah* and holds that Bavel's Torah knowledge is sufficient, while Shmuel focuses on *edim metzuyim lekaymo* — the availability of witnesses to validate signatures — and holds that travel between Babylonian cities is insufficient to ensure such availability. The *gemara* rejects this framing, however, on the grounds that Rabbah himself also holds of Rava's position. Everyone must address the question of witness availability; the *lishmah* issue alone cannot account for the debate. The *gemara* therefore reframes: *ela d'kulei alma edim metzuyim lekaymo* — everyone agrees the concern is about witness availability. The debate is about whether that concern is adequately answered in Bavel. Rav holds that because there are *yeshivot* in Bavel, there is constant travel from city to city; an industry of movement grows up around the great academies, and one can assume witnesses to signatures will be available when needed. Shmuel counters: *misiftei girsaihu tridi* — the *yeshiva* students are absorbed in their learning. The existence of academies does not translate into the kind of commercial and civic movement through which people become familiar with one another's signatures. There may be travel, but not the kind that ensures witnesses for *kiyum shtaros*.

### Rav Huna Statement

The *gemara* adds a tradition: *amar Rabbi Abba amar Rava amar Rav Huna asinu atzmeinu be-Vavel ke-Eretz Yisrael le-gittin* — we have established in Bavel that we treat ourselves as Eretz Yisrael for matters of *gittin*, from the time Rav arrived in Bavel. The same Rav whose ruling opened this section is identified as the pivotal figure: it was his arrival that transformed Bavel's practical status.

### Rashi: Yeshivot

Rashi explains: from the time Rav came to Bavel and established a *yeshiva* in Sura — while Shmuel's *yeshiva* was in Nehardea — there arose a permanent institutional framework driving movement throughout the region. The smaller communities flocked to these centers on days of Torah study; students traveled between Sura and Nehardea; traffic permeated the Babylonian Jewish world. It was this development, in Rashi's reading, that changed Bavel's status — not a pre-existing feature of the landscape, but an institutional transformation that Rav himself created. Although Torah had always been present in Bavel — Hillel himself came from there, and the exile of Yechanya brought *talmidei chachamim* in the aftermath of the first destruction — what Rav and Shmuel established was something qualitatively new: fixed, high-capacity academies generating the kind of constant movement that ensures witness availability.

## Yirmiya Challenge

### Borders & Geography

Rabbi Yirmiya challenges the foregoing from the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* enumerates the outer borders of Eretz Yisrael: Rekem to the east, Ashkelon to the south, Akko to the north — each of these border cities being treated as outside Eretz Yisrael for the laws of *gittin*. And we know from the *pesukim* that Bavel lies to the north of Eretz Yisrael: *mi-tzafon tipasach ha-ra'ah*, the evil shall open from the north — that was the prophecy of Bavel's destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*. Moreover, the *mishnah* states that Rabbi Meir considers Akko equivalent to Eretz Yisrael for *gittin*, and even the sages who disagree with Rabbi Meir do so specifically because Akko is distant from the center — *d'merachka hi*. If even a border city like Akko requires debate, Bavel — which is far more distant — would certainly be treated as *chutz la'aretz*. How, then, can one declare that Bavel has the status of Eretz Yisrael?

### Exception Principle

Rabbi Yirmiya poses his own answer: *le-var mi-Bavel* — the mishnah's geographic framework applies to everything *other than* Bavel. Bavel is a distinct entity unto itself, not simply subsumed under the general category of *chutz la'aretz*. This resolution points to something structurally important. Just as *medinat hayam* had its own defined identity in the *mishnah*, Bavel — as Tosafot notes from the opening of the tractate — has its own category. One might object that since the changed status resulted from Rav's arrival, which postdates the *mishnah*, the *mishnah* cannot be used to challenge it. The answer would be that even before Rav, the *mishnah*'s framework implicitly allowed for such an exception — or alternatively, as Tosafot proposes, the status was always there, waiting to be articulated.

## Bavel's Status (II)

### Tosafot Alternative

Tosafot challenges Rashi's reading of the Rav Huna statement. If Bavel's status genuinely *changed* when Rav arrived — if it was formerly *chutz la'aretz* and became like Eretz Yisrael — then why does the *gemara* treat a challenge from the *mishnah* as meaningful? The *mishnah* predates Rav's arrival; of course it reflects the older reality. Tosafot therefore cites Rabbeinu Tam's alternative: when Rav arrived in Bavel and taught that Bavel has the status of Eretz Yisrael for *gittin*, he was not announcing a change but transmitting a pre-existing *halacha* — grounded in the foundational status of the exile of Yechanya and his *talmidei chachamim* — that Bavel had always carried this distinction. On this reading, there was no change in status, only a clarification of a status that always existed, which is why the *mishnah* remains a relevant source to engage. The *gemara* sounds more naturally like Rashi's reading, but the motivation behind Tosafot's approach is clear: to preserve the coherence of the *gemara*'s dialectic.

## Bavel Boundaries

### Yuchasin Parallel

Having established that Bavel has a distinct status, the *gemara* turns to the question of its precise geographic boundaries. *Ad heichan hi Bavel?* — how far does Bavel extend? Rav Papa rules: *k'machloket l'yuchasin kach machloket l'gittin* — whatever debate exists in tractate *Kiddushin* about the boundaries of Bavel for purposes of *yuchasin* — personal status and the reliability of genealogical records — the same boundaries apply for *gittin*. The considerations differ: *yuchasin* concerns where records were kept and personal status is most assured, while *gittin* concerns the availability of witnesses to validate signatures. One might ask whether these two criteria necessarily produce identical borders. Perhaps the *gemara*'s point is that for practical coherence — *kedei shelo tachalok* — a unified standard is preferable even if the underlying rationales do not map perfectly.

### Bridge Arch Limit

Rav Yosef disagrees. The debate in *Kiddushin* applies to *yuchasin*, but for *gittin* there is a different, universally agreed-upon boundary: *ad arba tinyana d'gashra* — until the second arch of the bridge. The *gemara* understood which bridge and which direction this referred to, and that specific landmark demarcates the extent of Bavel for purposes of the *bifani nechtav* exemption.

## Aktisphon Case

### One-Way Requirement

Rav Chisda introduces an asymmetric case: he required the declaration *bifani nechtav* for a *get* traveling from Aktisphon to Bei Ardashir, but not in the reverse direction. Two cities are thus in a relationship where one requires the declaration going one way but not the other. At first glance, one might explain this the same way Eretz Yisrael and *medinat hayam* differ: perhaps Aktisphon does not know *lishmah* and Bei Ardashir does, so when a *get* arrives from Aktisphon, Bei Ardashir requires the declaration to ensure proper intent, while the reverse is unnecessary. This explanation would work cleanly if *lishmah* were the only concern.

### Marketplace Effect

The *gemara* rejects this framing, again invoking the established principle that Rava holds of Rabbah — everyone must address the witness validation concern. The explanation instead focuses on commercial patterns: *vahani kimeizali beshuka lehasam hanach yadei bechasimeit yadei dehani* — the residents of one city go to the marketplace of the other. When people from city A regularly travel to buy and sell in city B, the residents of city B become familiar with the signatures of city A's residents through the commercial documents — IOUs, bills of sale — that the travelers sign while there. The buyers sign; they go home; the sellers of city B are left holding those documents and develop familiarity with those signatures. City B therefore knows city A's signatures and can perform *kiyum shtaros* if necessary. The reverse is not true: *vahani behanach lo yadei* — city A does not accumulate familiarity with city B's signatures, because city A's residents are busy with their own transactions when in city B and return home without a comparable record. *Mai ta'ama beshuka'i teridei* — they are preoccupied with the marketplace itself. This asymmetry in commercial traffic explains the asymmetry in the *halachic* requirement.

## Neighborhood Cases

### Reshus Division

Rav bar Avuha extends the logic further, requiring the *bifani nechtav* declaration even between opposite sides of a *reshus harabim* — a major thoroughfare. The reasoning, as Tosafot explains clearly, is that one is aware of the movements of those in one's immediate vicinity. If the witnesses to a *get* live on the same side of the street as the wife and her community, she and those around her will know if those witnesses are about to relocate, and can arrange *kiyum shtaros* in advance. But if the witnesses live across the major thoroughfare — the other side of the tracks, so to speak — that awareness is absent. Their departure may go unnoticed until it is too late to validate the signatures. The transience of population, and the limits of what one notices about one's neighbors, thus drives a requirement that would seem geographically trivial.

### Neighborhood Division

Rav Sheshes goes further: the declaration is required even between different neighborhoods within the same side of such a divide. The principle remains the same — one tracks the movements of those most proximate — but the zone of reliable awareness is narrowed further. Even within the same broad residential area, if the witnesses and the wife come from distinct neighborhoods, the risk of unnoticed departure applies.

### Transience Principle

Rava extends the requirement to within a single neighborhood itself. The transience of urban population is so pronounced that even neighbors may not know one another's plans, and a witness could depart without warning even to those living in the same *shechuna*.

### Mechuza Context

The *gemara* qualifies Rava's position: *shani benei Mechuza de'naidi* — Rava spoke from his own experience in Mechuza, a cosmopolitan commercial center with an unusually mobile population. His ruling applies specifically to cities of that character. The earlier, less sweeping positions reflect the realities of less transient communities, where one's neighborhood still provides meaningful notice of departures.

### Modern Application

Tosafot draws the practical conclusion directly from Rava's ruling and its grounding in transience: in all places of the medieval period, where Jewish populations were similarly mobile and cosmopolitan, one should always declare *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam*. Even though we rule like Rava that the concern is *edim metzuyim lekaymo* rather than *lishmah*, and even if the community is learned enough that *lishmah* poses no issue, the transience of population means the witness concern always applies. Everywhere in the world of the *Rishonim* resembles Mechuza, and the declaration should always be made.

## Rav Kahana Case

### No Need Statement

The *gemara* relates that Rav Kahana brought a *get* — Rav Chanin reported the incident — and did not know whether it had traveled from Sura to Nehardea or from Nehardea to Sura; both were major centers of Torah scholarship and, as established, of constant travel. He came before Rav and asked whether he needed to say *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam*. Rav ruled: *lo tzarichat* — you do not need to, because Bavel has the status of Eretz Yisrael for *gittin*, and the dense travel between these two great academies ensures that witness availability is not a concern.

### If Said, Works

The *gemara* adds: *ve'ee avad mahni* — and if one did say it, it works. This is not a trivial addendum. Even in cases where the declaration is technically unnecessary, making it protects the woman: if the husband were to later challenge the *get*, the declared *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam* ensures that his objection will not be entertained. According to Rashi, the risk in the absence of the declaration is that the husband's challenge could actually succeed in invalidating the *get*; according to Tosafot, the concern is rather the *la'az* — the cloud of suspicion — that such a challenge would create. Either way, the declaration, when made even unnecessarily, provides the woman with meaningful protection, and its use should be encouraged wherever possible.

## Kfar Sisai Case

### Witness Prevention

A *baraita* is cited: someone came before Rabbi Yishmael with a *get* he had brought from Kfar Sisai. He asked whether he needed to say *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam*. Rabbi Yishmael asked where he was from; upon learning it was Kfar Sisai, he instructed him: you must say it, *shelo tizakak l'edim* — so that she should not need to resort to witnesses. The framing is notable: the obligation is presented not as an inherent requirement of Kfar Sisai's status, but as a protective measure that spares the woman from having to produce witnesses.

### Ilai Challenge

After the *shaliach* departed, Rabbi Ilai entered and challenged Rabbi Yishmael: Kfar Sisai is swallowed within the borders of Eretz Yisrael — it is closer to Tzipori than Akko is, and Tzipori was a central location. The *mishnah* records that Rabbi Meir considers Akko itself to be like Eretz Yisrael for *gittin*, and even the sages who dispute Rabbi Meir do so only because Akko is distant — *d'merachka hi* — while Kfar Sisai is close. If Akko's proximity was sufficient for Rabbi Meir, Kfar Sisai's greater proximity should be sufficient for everyone. Why then require the declaration?

### Heter Established

Rabbi Yishmael responded: *shtok bni shtok* — hush, my son, hush. *Ho'il v'yatza davar b'heter yatza* — since the matter went forth as a *heter*, as a permission, let it stand. That is: the declaration was instructed not because it was technically required, but because making it activates the protective mechanism. Since the *shaliach* was told to say it and it is effective when said, the woman is now protected. Even if it need not have been required, the fact that it was said and is halachically operative means it serves its purpose.

### Incomplete Hearing

The *gemara* explains Rabbi Ilai's challenge: *lo siyem kamei* — Rabbi Ilai had not heard the complete statement Rabbi Yishmael made. He heard only the instruction to say *bifani nechtav u'bifani nechtam* and reacted before hearing the rationale — *shelo tizakak l'edim*, so that she not need witnesses — which would have clarified that this was protective guidance, not a statement of genuine requirement. Having heard only the ruling and not its explanation, Rabbi Ilai assumed Rabbi Yishmael was declaring Kfar Sisai to require the declaration on its own terms, and challenged accordingly.

## Avyasar Statement

### Travel Leniency

The *gemara* now turns to an entirely different axis of the question: not within Bavel or within Eretz Yisrael, but between them. Rav Avyasar sent a ruling to Rav Chisda: *gittin habaim misham l'kan* — *gittin* coming from there, Bavel, to here, Eretz Yisrael, *ein tzarich lomar bifanei nechtav u'bifanei nechtam*. No declaration is required. One might have thought that since these are two distinct regions — whatever their internal status — the movement between them would require the standard declaration applied to *medinat hayam*.

### Travel Rationale

The *gemara* again notes that a *lishmah*-only explanation is insufficient, since everyone holds of Rava's position requiring witness availability. The answer: *keivan d'ika rabim d'salki v'nechti* — since there are many who go up and come down, making *aliyah* and *yerida* between Bavel and Eretz Yisrael — primarily for purposes of Torah study — *mishkach shchichi*, witnesses are readily available. The volume of travel between the two great centers of Jewish life is so substantial that one can rely on finding someone who can perform *kiyum shtaros* when needed. The same logic that makes Bavel equivalent to Eretz Yisrael internally extends to the corridor between them.

## Yosef Challenge

### Trust Question

Rav Yosef raises a pointed objection to Rav Avyasar's ruling: *man lima lan d'Rav Avyasar bar samcha hu?* — who says that Rav Avyasar is a reliable authority? Since his ruling is a *kula* — a leniency, removing a requirement — the question of his credibility is directly relevant.

### Harsh Missive

Rav Yosef continues: this is the same Rav Avyasar who sent a harsh letter — apparently from Eretz Yisrael to Rav Yehuda in Bavel — condemning *bnei adam haolim misham l'kan*, the Torah scholars who came from Bavel to Eretz Yisrael to learn but neglected their responsibilities at home. They left wives effectively abandoned, failed in their obligations to provide *mezonot* for their families, and were absent from the responsibilities of building a household and having children.

### Verse Critique

The specific verse Rav Avyasar applied to these scholars was: *va'yitnu ha'yeled bazonah v'hayalda machru bayayin va'yishtu* — they gave the child to the prostitute and the girl they sold for wine and drank. The harshness of the charge attracted its own commentary. Tosafot understands *bazonah* as referring to *mezonot* — sustenance — meaning the children had to labor to support themselves because their fathers were gone. A tradition from the *Yerushalmi* goes further: there was an actual case of a man who sold his daughter into servitude in order to fund his Torah study. Rashi reads the critique differently: these scholars lingered in foreign lands, leaving their wives in a state resembling *agunot* — not refusing a *get*, but abandoned and bound — and neglecting the obligations of *pru u'rvu*, such that children who should have existed were never born. Whatever the precise meaning, the letter was a severe indictment, and its character speaks to the kind of authority Rav Avyasar was willing to project.

### Klaf Scoring

Rav Yosef's specific technical critique is this: Rav Avyasar wrote the letter *b'lo sirtut* — without scoring the *klaf*, without the preparatory lines grooved into the parchment to guide straight writing, which is required when writing a substantial text. And Rav Yitzchak taught: one may write two words without scoring, but not three. A *baraita* has a slightly different tradition — three words without scoring are permitted, but not four. Either way, Rav Avyasar wrote an extended text without observing this practice, and Rav Yosef takes this as evidence of a gap in his learning: if he is unaware of, or disregarded, this *halacha*, why should we trust his rulings on more consequential matters?

## Abaye Defense

### Authority Standards

Abaye responds to Rav Yosef's critique: *atu kuli alma lo yad'ei ha d'Rav Yitzchak lav gavra raba hu?* — does everyone know Rav Yitzchak's position, and if one does not, does that disqualify him as a major authority? The underlying principle is that gaps in knowledge of specific traditions do not determine overall reliability.

### Tradition vs. Logic

Abaye draws a principled distinction: *bishlama milta d'talya b'savra lechai* — if the failure were in something that depends on *savra*, on reasoning and logical analysis, that would be a valid critique of someone's reliability as a decisor. If someone cannot reason clearly, that calls into question the quality of his other rulings. But this is different.

### Tradition Knowledge

*Aval ha gmara gmara hi* — this is a matter of *gemara*, of received tradition. *U'gmara lo shmi'a lei* — he simply had not heard this particular tradition. Before the age of printed books and comprehensive libraries, one could not be expected to know every tradition that existed. Rav Yitzchak's teaching about *sirtut* may not have been widely circulated. Failure to have received a specific oral tradition says nothing about someone's analytical capacity or trustworthiness as a *posek*. On this note the *shiur* concludes, with the broader discussion of Rav Avyasar's authority — and the *gemara*'s continuation — to be taken up in the next session.
