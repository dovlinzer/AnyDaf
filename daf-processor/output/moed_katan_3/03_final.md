# Moed Katan 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chol HaMoed Criteria

### Davar ha'avad & Tircha

We continue in Moed Katan, picking up at the bottom of 2b. The Mishnah's underlying assumptions have already been laid out: *melacha* on *Chol HaMoed* is permissible only when two criteria are simultaneously met. First, there must be a *davar ha'avad* — a situation in which inaction will cause an actual financial loss. Mere loss of potential gain, *hefsed revach*, does not suffice; one must face genuine loss. Second, the permitted work must not entail undue *tircha*, undue effort or burden. Watering from a well rather than from a spring, for example, would involve excessive *tircha* and would therefore be impermissible regardless of loss. Both conditions must be present together.

### Rabba vs. Rav Yosef

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: הַמְנַכֵּשׁ וְהַמַּשְׁקֶה מַיִם לִזְרָעִים בְּשַׁבָּת, מִשּׁוּם מַאי מַתְרִינַן בֵּיהּ? רַבָּה אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם חוֹרֵשׁ. רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם זוֹרֵעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that the *amora'im* disputed the following question: With regard to **one who weeds or one who waters seedlings on Shabbat, for what** prohibited labor **do we forewarn him?** Judicial punishment may be administered to a sinner only if he has been forewarned by two witnesses prior to the commission of his offense. This forewarning must include the specific transgression being violated, and on Shabbat it must include the specific category of prohibited labor that the action involves. **Rabba said:** It is **due to** the prohibition against **plowing. Rav Yosef said:** It is **due to** the prohibition against **sowing.**

The Gemara introduces what appears at first to be a separate topic but will shortly prove directly relevant: a dispute between Rabba and Rav Yosef regarding the Shabbat category into which one would place the act of watering or weeding the ground. Rabba holds that such an act falls under the category of *choresh* — plowing — because it works upon the earth itself. Rav Yosef holds it falls under *zoreia* — planting — because it stimulates the seed to sprout. The practical consequences of this dispute are not limited to Shabbat.

### Categories Applied

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף לְרַבָּה: הַמְנַכֵּשׁ וְהַמְחַפֶּה לְכִלְאַיִם לוֹקֶה. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אַף הַמְקַיֵּים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabba** from what is taught in the following *baraita*: **One who weeds** in proximity to diverse kinds of seeds, **or covers** with earth the exposed roots of **diverse kinds, is flogged** for transgressing the prohibition against diverse kinds, i.e., tending different species of crops in one area of the same field, as it is stated: "You shall not sow your field with two kinds of seed" (Leviticus 19:19). **Rabbi Akiva says: Even one who maintains** in his field a mixture of species that he could have uprooted is flogged for violating the prohibition against diverse kinds of seeds.

As the Gemara noted at the close of the previous daf, this categorization carries implications for *kilayim* as well. The Torah states *lo tizra karmecha kilayim* — do not sow your vineyard with mixed species. If instead of planting one waters or weeds mixed species, what is the result? A *Baraita* rules that one is *chayav*, which on its face strongly supports Rav Yosef's position that such acts constitute a form of *zeriah*. Rabba escapes this by arguing that the *Baraita* reflects the view of Rabbi Akiva, who holds that even merely *mekayem* — allowing *kilayim* to continue growing — carries liability, independent of any specific act of *zeriah*. What the Gemara establishes clearly is that the Rabba–Rav Yosef debate is not confined to Shabbat; the categorization of these agricultural acts carries consequences across multiple halachic domains, including *shemita*, to which the Gemara now turns.

## Watering Fields

### Chol HaMoed Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: מַשְׁקִין בֵּית הַשְּׁלָחִין בַּמּוֹעֵד וּבִשְׁבִיעִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to the original topic of discussion: **We learned** in the mishna: **One may irrigate a field that requires irrigation on** the intermediate days of **a Festival as well as during the Sabbatical Year.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מוֹעֵד, מִשּׁוּם טִירְחָא הוּא, וּבִמְקוֹם פְּסֵידָא שָׁרוּ רַבָּנַן. אֶלָּא שְׁבִיעִית, בֵּין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם זוֹרֵעַ וּבֵין לְמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשּׁוּם חוֹרֵשׁ, זְרִיעָה וַחֲרִישָׁה בִּשְׁבִיעִית מִי שְׁרֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to the intermediate days of **a Festival,** where the prohibition against irrigation is **due to** the mandate to avoid excessive **exertion** on the Festival, **in a case of** considerable **loss, the Sages permitted** one to exert himself. **However,** during the **Sabbatical Year, both according to** the **one who said** that one who waters is liable **due to** the prohibition against **sowing, and according to the one who said** that one is liable **due to** the prohibition against **plowing, are sowing and plowing permitted during the Sabbatical Year?** How can these actions be permitted when the Torah explicitly renders prohibited working the land during the Sabbatical Year?

The Gemara cites the opening of our Mishnah: *mashkin beit hashalchin b'moed u'vishvi'is* — one may irrigate an orchard that requires irrigation both on *Chol HaMoed* and during *shemita*. Regarding *Chol HaMoed*, the Gemara finds no difficulty. *Bishlama moed* — the allowance is understandable. Why is the work ordinarily prohibited? *Mishum tircha hu* — because of undue effort. And the Rabbis permitted it *b'makom p'seida* — where inaction would result in loss.

### Tircha vs. Melacha

This terse statement deserves pause, because the Gemara here makes a far-reaching claim. It characterizes the entire framework of *melacha* prohibitions on *Chol HaMoed* — and this is not the relatively peripheral discussion of haircuts or laundry, which may rest on entirely different grounds, but actual agricultural labor — as rooted purely in *tircha*. It is not about a conceptual category of *melacha* at all. Rabbeinu Gershom spells this out explicitly: *lo asir b'shum melacha ela mishum tircha* — the only concern is the concern of excessive effort. The corollary is significant: since the prohibition is grounded in *tircha* and the Rabbis permitted it wherever there is a loss, the entire framework sounds thoroughly rabbinic. The idea of *tircha* as a governing concept has no biblical precedent. This characterization stands in some tension with other passages suggesting that *Chol HaMoed* work prohibitions do have deeper conceptual grounding in categories of *melacha*, but for present purposes the Gemara has staked out this position clearly.

### Shemita Problem

The more pressing difficulty, however, concerns *shemita*. The Gemara asks: *ela shvi'is* — but *shemita*? How can any act of watering be permitted during the sabbatical year? Whether watering falls under *zoreia* or under *choresh*, both planting and plowing are Biblically forbidden on *shemita*. *Zeria v'charisha b'shevi'is mi sharei?* Is any of this really permissible when it appears to be a blanket Biblical prohibition? This is the central question the Gemara now sets out to resolve.

## Abaye: Shemita Rabbinic

### Rabbi's Two Shemitot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁבִיעִית בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה, וְרַבִּי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: ״וְזֶה דְּבַר הַשְּׁמִטָּה שָׁמוֹט״. בִּשְׁתֵּי שְׁמִיטוֹת הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר, אַחַת שְׁמִיטַּת קַרְקַע, וְאַחַת שְׁמִיטַּת כְּסָפִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** The mishna is referring **to the Sabbatical Year in the present time,** when its prohibitions are only by rabbinic decree, **and it is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** When the verse states: **"And this is the manner of the release,** every creditor **will release** that which he has lent to his neighbor" (Deuteronomy 15:2), **the verse speaks of two releases: One** is **the release of land,** that one must refrain from working the land, **and the other** is **the release of money,** that one must refrain from collecting debts.

Abaye offers the first resolution: *b'shevi'is bazman hazeh v'Rabbi* — the Mishnah's leniency assumes we are speaking of *shemita* in the present era, and it follows the position of Rabbi. A *Baraita* records Rabbi's interpretation of the verse *v'zeh devar hashemita shamot kol ba'al masheh yado asher yasheh b'rei'eihu* — anyone who holds a loan against his fellow shall release it. Rabbi notes the doubled language, *shemita shamot*, and derives from it that the verse is speaking of two *shemitot* — *shtei shemitos*: *shemitas karka*, the release of the land, and *shemitas kesafim*, the release of debts.

### Conditional Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁאַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע — אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים, וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאִי אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט קַרְקַע — אִי אַתָּה מְשַׁמֵּט כְּסָפִים.
>
> **Translation:** This verse equates these two releases, indicating that **when you** are mandated by Torah law to **release land, you** must **release money, and when you** are **not** mandated to **release land, you need not release money.** This indicates that according to Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, at the present time, the release of land is not mandated by Torah law. Therefore, observance of the Sabbatical Year is mandated only by rabbinic law, and the Sages were lenient in a case of significant loss.

Rabbi's principle is: *bizman she'atah meshamet karka atah meshamet kesafim, u'bizman she'i atah meshamet karka i atah meshamet kesafim* — when *shemitat karka* applies, debt release applies; when *shemitat karka* does not apply, debt release does not apply either. The primary *chiddush* of Rabbi's statement is to tell us that the release of debts — which one might have thought operates universally, even outside the Land of Israel, given its seemingly universal moral character — is in fact conditioned on the application of *shemitat karka*. But what the Gemara infers from Rabbi's position is a broader implication: Rabbi acknowledges that there are circumstances in which *shemitat karka* itself does not apply. This means that *shemita* is not invariably biblical.

### Post-Churban Status

What would cause *shemitat karka* to lapse? One possibility is that after the *churban*, the Land does not retain the same *kedusha*. Another line of reasoning, with additional support, is that *shemitat karka* is itself dependent on *yovel*: since we do not observe *yovel* — because all the tribes are no longer settled in the Land — *shemitat karka* does not apply, and consequently neither does *shemitat kesafim*. Whatever the precise mechanism, the implication embedded in Rabbi's statement is that *shemita* is not always Biblical in force. The Gemara therefore reads our Mishnah as operating on this assumption: since all the tribes are not settled in the Land and we are in the post-*churban* era, *shemita* nowadays is not Biblical, and the Rabbis can therefore extend leniency in cases of genuine *hefsed*. This is structurally analogous to the reasoning underlying *heter mechira*: if *shemita* is only rabbinic in force today, there is greater halachic latitude to construct workarounds.

## Rava: Avot Only

### Avot vs. Toladot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן — אָבוֹת אָסַר רַחֲמָנָא, תּוֹלָדוֹת לָא אָסַר רַחֲמָנָא. דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבַשָּׁנָה הַשְּׁבִיעִית שַׁבַּת שַׁבָּתוֹן יִהְיֶה לָאָרֶץ שָׂדְךָ לֹא תִזְרָע וְגוֹ׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Even** if **you say** that the mishna was taught in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and maintain that the observance of the Sabbatical Year, even at the present time, is mandated by Torah law, one can understand the leniency in the mishna. The reason for the leniency with regard to irrigation is because only **primary categories of labor were prohibited by the Merciful One,** i.e., by Torah law, whereas **the subcategories** of labor that are derived from them, e.g., watering, **were not prohibited by the Merciful One,** i.e., by Torah law, but only by rabbinic law; and in a case of loss the Sages were lenient. The source for this distinction is **as it is written: "But in the seventh year shall be a sabbath of solemn rest for the land,** a sabbath for the Lord: **Your field you shall not sow,** and your vineyard you shall not prune. That which grows of its own accord of your harvest you shall not reap, nor gather the grapes of your untended vines" (Leviticus 25:4–5).

Rava offers a second, independent resolution. *Afilu teima rabanan* — even if you hold like the Rabbis who dispute Rabbi, meaning even granting that *shemita* is Biblically operative, *avot asar Rachmana, toldot lo asar Rachmana* — the Torah only Biblically forbade the primary *avot* categories explicitly listed in the Torah, not their derivative *toladot*. This is a striking position, particularly for anyone accustomed to the framework of *hilchot Shabbat*, where the *avot-toladot* structure carries full Biblical weight in both directions.

What does the Torah actually prohibit regarding *shemita*? The relevant verse reads: *sadcha lo tizra v'charmecha lo tizmor, et sefiach ktzircha lo tiktzor v'et inbei nezirecha lo tivtzor, shnat shabbaton yihyeh la'aretz* — do not plant your field, do not prune your vineyard, do not harvest the aftergrowth of your grain, do not gather the grapes of your untended vines; it shall be a year of complete rest for the land. The activities that are explicitly named — planting, pruning, harvesting grain, gathering grapes — constitute the *avot*. Acts that one might logically derive from those categories — such as watering, which could be seen as a form of either *zeriah* or *charisha* — would be *toladot*. According to Rava, only the former carry Biblical force; the latter are at most Rabbinic.

It is worth noting that Rashi formulates this somewhat differently. His comment at the top of 3a describes *toladot* as acts like watering seeds, *shelo hayah ba'Mishkan* — acts that were not done in the Mishkan. The framing is striking because no one would suggest that all thirty-nine *melachot* of Shabbat apply to *shemita* — obviously one may cook during the sabbatical year. Yet Rashi appears to be drawing on the conceptual framework of the Mishkan-derived *melachot*, applied specifically to those categories that relate to working the land. This raises an interesting conceptual question: do the prohibited *shemita* acts constitute a subset of the thirty-nine *melachot* as they relate to land-based labor, implying the full *Mishkan*-derived framework? Or do they form their own independent category, where the Torah simply specifies which activities are forbidden without drawing on that conceptual framework? The weight of the simple reading of the *shemita* verses — which explicitly enumerate specific acts — tends toward the latter view, and indeed that is where the Gemara's argument leads.

### Textual Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, זְמִירָה בִּכְלַל זְרִיעָה, וּבְצִירָה בִּכְלַל קְצִירָה. לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Since pruning is included in** the principal category of **sowing,** as its objective is to encourage the plant's growth, **and picking** grapes **is included in** the principal category **of reaping** grain, as both involve removal of produce from a plant, **for** the purpose of teaching **what *halakha* did the Merciful One write them?** Why did the Torah explicitly prohibit pruning and picking grapes, rather than sufficing with the general prohibitions against sowing and reaping?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּאַהָנֵי תּוֹלָדוֹת מִיחַיַּיב, אַאַחְרָנְיָיתָא לָא מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that these were individually enumerated **to say** that only **for these subcategories** of labor **is one liable** to be flogged, while **for other** subcategories of labor, i.e., watering a field, **one is not liable.** Consequently, there are only four types of labor that are prohibited by Torah law during the Sabbatical Year: Sowing and its subcategory of pruning, as well as reaping and its subcategory of grape picking. All other subcategories of labor are prohibited only by rabbinic decree.

The Gemara derives Rava's position from a close reading of the verse's structure. It observes: *mechdi zemira bichlal zeriah u'vtzira bichlal ktzira* — pruning a vineyard should logically be subsumed under planting, and grape-gathering should be subsumed under harvesting grain. If these were meant to be included in the broader derivation, the Torah need not have stated them explicitly; one could have derived them independently. The question is therefore: *l'mai hilchata katvinhu Rachmana?* Why did the Torah go to the trouble of explicitly listing both *zeria* and *zimira*, both *ktzira* and *btzira*?

The answer: *l'meimra d'hanei toladot mechayev, achranyasa lo mechayev* — the Torah spelled out these particular derivative activities specifically to tell us that these, and only these, carry Biblical liability. Other derivatives that one might otherwise have expected to be forbidden Biblically — because they could be placed within the conceptual category of, say, *zeriah* — are not Biblically prohibited. The very specificity of the Torah's enumeration signals limitation rather than expansion: had the Torah intended us to extrapolate broadly, it would not have needed to enumerate examples that could themselves be derived.

### Baraita Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא תִזְרַע וְכַרְמְךָ לֹא תִזְמוֹר״. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זֵירוּעַ וְזִימּוּר, מִנַּיִן לְנִיכּוּשׁ וּלְעִידּוּר וּלְכִיסּוּחַ — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא״, ״כַּרְמְךָ לֹא״. לֹא כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּשָׂדְךָ, וְלֹא כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּכַרְמְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** is there really **no** liability for other subcategories of labor? **Isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **"Your field you shall not sow, and your vineyard you shall not prune"** (Leviticus 25:4). **I have** derived from here an explicit prohibition **only** against **sowing and pruning. From where** do I derive that **weeding,** i.e., uprooting weeds, **hoeing, and cutting weeds,** even without uprooting them, are also prohibited? **The verse states: "Your field** you shall **not** sow" and **"your vineyard** you shall **not** prune." This wording indicates that **no labor** may be performed **in your field, and no labor** may be performed **in your vineyard.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מְקַרְסְמִין וְאֵין מְזָרְדִין וְאֵין מְפַסְּגִין בָּאִילָן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא״, ״כַּרְמְךָ לֹא״. לֹא כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּשָׂדְךָ, וְלֹא כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּכַרְמְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **from where** is it derived that **one may not prune trees, and one may not trim dry branches** from trees, **and one** may **not cut large branches [*mefasegin*] from trees? The verse states: "Your field** you shall **not** sow" and **"your vineyard** you shall **not** prune," thereby teaching that **no labor** may be performed **in your field, and no labor** may be performed **in your vineyard.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנַּיִן שֶׁאֵין מְזַבְּלִין וְאֵין מְפָרְקִין וְאֵין מְאַבְּקִין וְאֵין מְעַשְּׁנִין בָּאִילָן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא״, ״כַּרְמְךָ לֹא״. כׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּשָׂדְךָ לֹא, וְכׇּל מְלָאכָה שֶׁבְּכַרְמְךָ לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, **from where** is it derived that **one** may **not fertilize** fields and vineyards, **and one may not remove stones** that surround the base of a tree and impede its growth, **and one** may **not** cover exposed roots with **dust, and one may not fumigate a tree** in order to exterminate worms? **The verse states: "Your field** you shall **not** sow" and **"your vineyard** you shall **not** prune," thereby indicating that **no labor** may be performed **in your field, and no labor** may be performed **in your vineyard.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יָכוֹל לֹא יְקַשְׁקֵשׁ תַּחַת הַזֵּיתִים, וְלֹא יְעַדֵּר תַּחַת הַגְּפָנִים, וְלֹא יְמַלֵּא נְקָעִים מַיִם, וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה עוּגִיּוֹת לַגְּפָנִים — תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שָׂדְךָ לֹא תִזְרָע״.
>
> **Translation:** The apparent conclusion from this is that all labor in a field or a vineyard is prohibited. One **might** have thought that **one may** also **not hoe lightly under olive** trees **and one may not hoe under grapevines, and one may not fill cracks** in the ground **with water, and one may not construct circular ditches** around the bases of **grapevines** in order to collect rainwater. Therefore, **the verse states: "Your field you shall not sow."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זְרִיעָה בַּכְּלָל הָיְתָה, וְלָמָּה יָצְתָה — לְהַקִּישׁ אֵלֶיהָ, לוֹמַר לְךָ: מָה זְרִיעָה מְיוּחֶדֶת עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁבַּשָּׂדֶה וְשֶׁבַּכֶּרֶם, אַף כֹּל שֶׁהִיא עֲבוֹדָה שֶׁבַּשָּׂדֶה וְשֶׁבַּכֶּרֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the derivation: **Sowing was included** in the general prohibition against performing agricultural labors during the Sabbatical Year, as the verse states "But in the seventh year shall be a sabbath of solemn rest for the land" (Leviticus 25:4). **Why was** sowing **singled out** and mentioned explicitly? In order **to compare** other types of labor **to it** and to **say to you: Just as sowing is unique in that it is labor** performed both **in the field and in the vineyard, so too, any** other **labor** performed both **in the field and in the vineyard** is prohibited. However, labors performed only in the vineyard but not in the field, i.e., whose objective is merely to sustain the vines from year to year, such as the labors mentioned above, are permitted. In any case, this *baraita* indicates that many subcategories of labor are prohibited during the seventh year, and not just sowing and pruning, as Rava had stated.

The Gemara now raises a serious challenge to Rava's position from a *Baraita*. Beginning from the verse *sadcha lo tizra v'charmecha lo tizmor*, the *Baraita* asks: *ein li ela zeria v'zimur* — I would know only planting and pruning. How do I know about *nikush* (weeding), *idur* (loosening the dirt around vines), and *kissuach* (cutting weeds)? The *Baraita* derives these from the formulation *sadcha lo, karmecha lo* — by juxtaposing the noun (field, vineyard) directly with the word *lo* (no), without the intervening verb, the Torah generates a blanket categorical prohibition: simply no, everything is forbidden.

From this same formulation the *Baraita* extends to orchard trees: *ein mekarsmin v'ein mezardim v'ein mefasgin b'ilan* — one may not prune, thin, or support trees. *Karsem* is pruning away damaged branches; *zarded* is general thinning of the tree; *pisuag* Rashi explains as a form of support. All are derived from *sadcha lo, karmecha lo*. The *Baraita* extends further still: one may not fertilize (*mezavlin*), remove stones (*mefarkin*), cover exposed roots with dust (*me'avkin*), or fumigate to remove infestation (*me'ashnin*) — all derived from the same blanket formulation.

This amounts to a categorical biblical prohibition covering virtually every form of agricultural work. The *Baraita* does carve out one limiting principle from the end of the verse, where the specific verb *tizra* reappears: *zeria bichlal haitah v'lamah yatzat l'hachish eleha* — if *zeriah* was already encompassed in the blanket prohibition, why did the Torah single it out at the end? In order to serve as a model: *mah zeriah meyuchedet avodah she'ba'sadeh v'she'ba'kerem, af kol she'hi avodah she'ba'sadeh v'she'ba'kerem* — just as *zeriah* is an act applicable equally in the grain field and in the vineyard, so too only those acts that apply equally in both contexts are Biblically forbidden. Acts that are specific to only one type of field — such as filling crevices with water or making circular irrigation ditches around vines, which are vineyard-specific — are excluded from the biblical prohibition.

This creates an internal tension in the *Baraita* itself, as activities like *idur* are first listed as Biblically forbidden and then apparently permitted, a contradiction that the Gemara will address. But the more pressing challenge for Rava is clear: this *Baraita* treats the entire sweep of prohibitions as Biblical, derived from the Torah's own language. How can Rava maintain that only the explicit *avot* carry biblical force?

### Asmachta Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this opinion: These labors, with the exceptions of sowing, pruning, grape picking, and reaping, are all prohibited only **by rabbinic law, and the verse** that is cited as a source from the Torah **is a mere support,** and not a bona fide source.

The Gemara resolves the challenge directly: *miderabbanan v'kra asmachta be'alma* — all of these prohibitions enumerated in the *Baraita* are in fact Rabbinic in nature, and the Torah verses cited throughout are merely *asmachta* — they provide a textual hook or support for Rabbinic legislation without indicating that the rulings are themselves Biblically mandated. This answer is, admittedly, striking. The *Baraita* does not read like an *asmachta*: it engages in detailed textual analysis, draws inferences from the precise formulation of the verse, and derives its conclusions from the *p'shat* of the biblical language. Yet the Gemara, in order to preserve Rava's position, characterizes the entire construction as Rabbinic. Rather than treating the *Baraita* as representing an opposing opinion, the Gemara holds that the *Baraita* is consistent with Rava — all of it is *derabbanan* — and the verse is being used only as an *asmachta*.

The resolution of the *Baraita*'s internal contradiction about *kiskus* follows from this same logic. One *Baraita* permits loosening the dirt under trees while another prohibits it. Rav Ukva bar Chama explains: *trei kiskusei havei* — there are two types. *Avruyei ilan* — loosening the dirt to strengthen and improve a healthy tree — is forbidden. *Situmei peili* — filling dangerous holes that threaten the tree's survival — is permitted. The Pseudo-Rabbeinu Gershom makes explicit what is implicit in the distinction: *situmei peili* is permitted because it prevents *hefsed*, genuine loss; *avruyei ilan* is forbidden because it is purely for *revach*, profit and growth enhancement. Once we have established that all of these prohibitions are Rabbinic, the familiar rule applies: the Rabbis permit their own prohibitions in cases of genuine loss but not for the sake of increased profit.

So, to summarize Rava's complete position: whether during *Chol HaMoed* or during *shemita*, any act of watering is at most Rabbinic. During *Chol HaMoed* because the entire *melacha* framework there is governed by *tircha* concerns and is Rabbinic; during *shemita* because Rava holds that even when *shemita* is Biblical, only those specific acts explicitly enumerated in the Torah — *zeriah*, *zimira*, *ktzira*, *btzira* — carry Biblical prohibition. Everything else is Rabbinic. And once it is Rabbinic, the Rabbis can permit it in cases of *hefsed*.

## Charisha Lashes Debate

### Lashes Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: הַחוֹרֵשׁ בַּשְּׁבִיעִית, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, חַד אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Elazar** disagreed with regard to **one who plows during the Sabbatical Year. One said: He is flogged** for doing so, **while the** other **one said: He is not flogged.**

The Gemara now turns to an *itmar* — an Amoraic dispute — regarding the case of one who plows (*choresh*) during *shemita*. Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar disagree: one says the violator receives lashes (*lokeh*) and one says he does not (*eino lokeh*). The imposition of lashes, of course, is reserved for Biblical violations; Rabbinic prohibitions do not carry that penalty. So this dispute is, at its core, a dispute about whether plowing on *shemita* is Biblically forbidden.

### Klal u'Prat Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בִּדְרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר כְּלָל בַּעֲשֵׂה וּפְרָט בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה — אֵין דָּנִין אוֹתוֹ בִּכְלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree with regard to** the principle that **Rabbi Avin** said that **Rabbi Ile'a said,** and one accepts this principle while the other does not. **As, Rabbi Avin said** that **Rabbi Ile'a said: Wherever a generalization is stated** in the Torah **as a positive** mitzva, **and a detail** relating to that generalization is stated **as a negative** mitzva, **one does not apply** the hermeneutical principle of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization,** according to which the *halakha* under discussion is expanded to all cases that resemble the detail.

The Gemara initially suggests that this dispute may hinge on the principle of Rabbi Avin in the name of Rabbi Ila: *kol makom she'ne'emar klal b'aseh u'frat b'lo ta'aseh, ein donin oto b'klal u'frat u'klal* — wherever the Torah states a general *aseh* (positive commandment) and a specific *lav* (prohibition), one does not apply the standard hermeneutical rule of *klal u'frat u'klal* to broaden the scope of the prohibition.

What does this mean in the context of *shemita*? The verse structure is as follows. *V'shavtah ha'aretz* — the land shall rest — is a broad, general *aseh*, a *klal*. *Sadcha lo tizra v'charmecha lo tizmor* — do not plant your field, do not prune your vineyard — are specific negative prohibitions, a *prat*. And *shabbaton tihyeh la'aretz* — it shall be a *shabbaton* for the land — constitutes another general statement, a second *klal*. We thus have the classic *klal u'prat u'klal* structure. Normally, under this hermeneutical principle, one would read the specific cases in light of the surrounding *klalim* and broaden the prohibition to include anything similar in kind. But Rabbi Avin argues that this methodology does not apply where the *klal* appears in the form of a positive commandment and the *prat* appears in the form of a prohibition. In such a case, the specific prohibitions stand alone and are not broadened.

If Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar are debating Rabbi Avin's rule, then the one who says *lokeh* rejects Rabbi Avin's rule and does apply *klal u'prat u'klal*, thereby extending the Biblical prohibition to include *charisha*, for which one receives lashes. And the one who says *eino lokeh* accepts Rabbi Avin's rule, leaving the specific prohibitions unenriched by the surrounding *klalim*, and *charisha* would be at most Rabbinic.

### No Rabbi Avin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לֵית לֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי אָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי אִילְעָא, מַאן דְּאָמַר לוֹקֶה — שַׁפִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** one can say that **everyone** holds that the *halakha* **is not** in accordance with the principle **that Rabbi Avin** said that **Rabbi Ile'a said.** According to **the one who said he is flogged,** all is **well,** as he applies the hermeneutical principle of a generalization, a detail, and a generalization to derive a prohibition for plowing.

However, the Gemara rejects this framing. *D'kulei alma leis lei d'Rabbi Avin amar Rabbi Ila* — in fact, everyone rejects Rabbi Avin's position. Both Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Elazar agree that the *klal u'prat u'klal* methodology does apply here, meaning the *aseh* can be used to broaden the scope of the prohibitions beyond the specific activities listed. Given this, the one who says *lokeh* is straightforwardly consistent: we apply *klal u'prat u'klal*, we broaden the prohibition, and we include *charisha*, which therefore carries lashes. The question becomes: what is the basis of the opinion that says *eino lokeh*?

### Rava's Specificity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה, אָמַר לָךְ: מִכְּדִי זְמִירָה בִּכְלַל זְרִיעָה וּבְצִירָה בִּכְלַל קְצִירָה, לְמַאי הִלְכְתָא כַּתְבִינְהוּ רַחֲמָנָא? לְמֵימַר דְּאַהָנֵי תּוֹלָדוֹת הוּא דְּמִיחַיַּיב, אַתּוֹלָדָה אַחֲרִינָא לָא מִיחַיַּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **And the one who said** that **he is not flogged** could have **said to you: Since pruning is** included **in** the primary category of **sowing, and** grape **picking is** included in the primary category **of reaping, for** the purpose of teaching **what *halakha* did the Merciful One write them?** They were individually enumerated **to say** that only **for these** specific **subcategories** of labor **is one liable** to be flogged, but **for** performing **other subcategories** of labor **one is not liable.** According to this opinion, the verse comes to teach that one is not liable to be flogged for performing any labor not explicitly enumerated in the Torah, including plowing.

The Gemara explains that the opinion exempting from lashes returns to Rava's argument: *mechdi zemira bichlal zeriah u'vtzira bichlal ktzira, l'mai hilchata kasvinu Rachmana* — if the Torah truly intended broad derivation via *klal u'prat u'klal*, why did it enumerate so many specific cases that could have been inferred from one another? The very fact that the Torah specifies *zimira* separately from *zeriah*, and *btzira* separately from *ktzira*, is itself a limiting signal: *d'hanei toladot michayev, atoldah achrina lo michayev* — it is only these derivative acts that carry liability; all others do not.

What the Gemara has done here is allow two distinct voices to stand. There is a view — grounded in *klal u'prat u'klal* and rejecting any limiting inference from the Torah's redundancy — that *charisha* is Biblically forbidden and incurs lashes. And there is Rava's view, which reads the Torah's specificity as a deliberate limitation, confining Biblical liability to the named *avot* and their enumerated derivations, leaving everything else as Rabbinic. The resolution of the earlier contradiction in the *Baraita* — where activities like *idur* and *kiskus* appeared first as forbidden and then as permitted — follows from distinguishing between cases of *hefsed* and *revach* within the Rabbinic framework, as explained above.

One additional textual observation deserves mention. There exists a verse in Shemot — *bacharish u'vakatzir tishbot* — rest during the plowing and the harvesting. According to one Rabbinic interpretation this verse addresses *shemita* rather than Shabbat. If so, it provides an independent textual source that explicitly mentions *charisha* in the context of *shemita*. Even within Rava's framework — where one limits Biblical prohibition to acts explicitly mentioned in the verse — this verse could potentially establish *charisha* as Biblically forbidden on *shemita*, through a distinct source. The Gemara does not develop this line here, but it is worth noting as it will become relevant when the discussion of *tosefet* unfolds.

## Rav Dimi & Tosefet

### Dimi's Confusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: יָכוֹל יִלְקֶה עַל הַתּוֹסֶפֶת. וְנָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא לִפְטוּרָא, וְלָא יָדַעְנָא מַאי תַּלְמוּדָא וּמַאי תּוֹסֶפֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he stated** a tradition he had heard from the Sages in Eretz Yisrael: One **might** have thought **that one would be flogged for the addition, but a teaching states an exemption** from lashes. Rav Dimi noted: **But I do not know what teaching or what addition** this tradition is referring to.

When Rav Dimi came from Eretz Yisrael, he reported the following: *yachol yilkeh al ha'tosefet* — he had heard in the *batei midrash* of Eretz Yisrael a discussion to the effect that one might receive lashes for the additional matter, *ha'tosefet*, and that the conclusion of the discussion in the *yeshivot* of Eretz Yisrael was that one is exempt. But Rav Dimi confessed: *v'lo yadana mai talmuda u'mai tosefet* — he did not know what the substantive conclusion of the discussion was, nor did he know what *tosefet* itself referred to. Two interpretations of that discussion are now offered.

### Tosefet as Activities

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר: חֲרִישָׁה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: יָכוֹל יִלְקֶה עַל חֲרִישָׁה דְּאָתְיָא מִכְּלָל וּפְרָט וּכְלָל, וְנָסֵיב לֵיהּ תַּלְמוּדָא לִפְטוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages disputed the meaning of this tradition. **Rabbi Elazar said:** The addition in question is **plowing** during the Sabbatical Year, for which there is no explicit prohibition in the Torah, and so it may be regarded as an addition to the labors explicitly enumerated in the Torah. **And this is what it is saying:** One **might** have thought that **one would be flogged for plowing** during the Sabbatical Year, **as it is derived by way** of the hermeneutical principle of **a generalization, and a detail, and a generalization** that teaches that plowing is prohibited. **But a teaching states an exemption** from lashes for the labor of plowing.

Rabbi Elazar reconstructs the discussion as follows: *charisha* was *tosefet* — the term referred to additional activities not explicitly enumerated in the verse. The question being debated in Eretz Yisrael was whether one receives lashes for *charisha*, which can be included in the Biblical prohibition only through the methodology of *klal u'prat u'klal*. The conclusion was that one is exempt, because the specificity of the Torah's enumeration — as Rava argued — signals that only the explicitly named acts carry Biblical liability, and the *tosefet* of additional activities remains Rabbinic.

### Tosefet as Days

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יָמִים שֶׁהוֹסִיפוּ חֲכָמִים לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה. וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: יָכוֹל יִלְקֶה עַל תּוֹסֶפֶת רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, דְּאָתְיָא ״מִבֶּחָרִישׁ וּבַקָּצִיר תִּשְׁבֹּת״, וְנָסֵיב לַהּ תַּלְמוּדָא לִפְטוּרָא, כִּדְבָעֵינַן לְמֵימַר לְקַמַּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** This addition is referring to **the** extra **days that the Sages added** to the prohibition against performing agricultural labor, **before Rosh HaShana** of the seventh year, when the Sabbatical Year formally begins. **And this is what it is saying:** One **might** have thought that **one would be flogged for** working the land during **the additional** period prior to **Rosh HaShana** of the Sabbatical Year, **as** this prohibition **is derived** from the verse: **"In plowing and in reaping you shall rest"** (Exodus 34:21). This seemingly superfluous verse is understood as teaching that not only is working the land prohibited during the seventh year, but plowing a field during the sixth year to prepare the land for the seventh year, and reaping what grew in the seventh year during the eighth year are also prohibited. **But a teaching states an exemption** from lashes for these actions, **as we are about to state below.**

Rabbi Yochanan offers a different reconstruction entirely: *yamim she'hosifu chachamim lifnei Rosh Hashanah* — *tosefet* refers to additional days that the Sages added prior to Rosh Hashanah during which agricultural work is prohibited in preparation for the *shemita* year. Just as there is a concept of *tosefet Shabbat* — adding a period of time before the onset of Shabbat during which one already refrains from *melacha* — there is an analogous concept of *tosefet shevi'it*, a period before *shemita* begins during which field work is prohibited.

The question debated in Eretz Yisrael, on Rabbi Yochanan's reading, was whether performing agricultural work during this added period incurs lashes. The *tosefet* period may be derived from the verse in Shemot, *bacharish u'vakatzir tishbot*, and one might therefore think that working during this period constitutes a Biblical violation warranting lashes. The conclusion was that one does not receive lashes — *nasiv la talmuda d'istira* — and as the Gemara notes, the reason for this will be clarified later. The reason one does not receive lashes for working during *tosefet* may simply be that the entire *tosefet* framework is Rabbinic; or, even if *tosefet* has a quasi-Biblical grounding, it does not constitute the actual *shemita* itself and therefore does not carry lashes.

Tosafot raises a pointed question here: how could lashes ever have been considered a live possibility for *tosefet shevi'it*, given that the *tosefet* obligation derives only from a positive commandment (*bacharish u'vakatzir tishbot* — *tishbot* being an *aseh*), and lashes are not given for violating a positive commandment alone? Tosafot's answer is that once the verse teaches the concept of *tosefet* — that *shemita* in some sense begins earlier than the calendar date of Rosh Hashanah — one might reason that the *shemita* year truly does extend into the preceding year: *v'kol din shevi'it yehe b'sheshit* — all the laws of *shemita* apply to that earlier period as well. On that reading, working during *tosefet shevi'it* would not merely violate the *mitzvah* of *tosefet* but would constitute an actual violation of *shemita* itself, potentially warranting lashes. The conclusion that no lashes apply reflects the rejection of this broader extension.

The analogy to *tosefet Shabbat* is instructive but imperfect. In the Shabbat context, no one ever suggested that performing *melacha* during *tosefet Shabbat* — after candle-lighting but before sunset — would render one liable to *mita* or lashes. Yet interesting questions do arise: can one fulfill the obligation of one of the three *seudot* during *tosefet Shabbat*, before sunset? Can one fulfill the *mitzvah* of *yeshivat sukkah* during *tosefet Yom Tov*? These questions turn on exactly the same underlying conceptual issue: does *tosefet* extend the sanctity of the day itself backward in time, or does it create an independent lesser obligation to refrain? The sharper stakes of the *shemita* discussion — the possibility of lashes — make the conceptual question unavoidable.

## Plowing Periods

### Mishna Periods

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי יָמִים שֶׁלִּפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, כְּדִתְנַן: עַד מָתַי חוֹרְשִׁין בִּשְׂדֵה אִילָן עֶרֶב שְׁבִיעִית? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיָּפֶה לַפְּרִי — וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד הָעֲצֶרֶת. וּקְרוֹבִין דִּבְרֵי אֵלּוּ לִהְיוֹת כְּדִבְרֵי אֵלּוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What** are the extra **days before Rosh HaShana? As we learned** in a mishna (*Shevi'it* 1:1): **Until when may** one **plow an orchard** on **the eve of the Sabbatical Year? Beit Shammai say:** One may plow **so long as** the plowing **is beneficial for the fruit** already on the trees. Once the plowing serves to benefit only the tree itself and the fruit it will produce the following year, it is prohibited. **And Beit Hillel say:** One may plow **until *Shavuot*.** The mishna notes: **And the statement of these,** Beit Shammai, **is close to being like the statement of these,** Beit Hillel; i.e., in practice, there is little difference between the dates established by the two opinions.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעַד מָתַי חוֹרְשִׁין שְׂדֵה הַלָּבָן עֶרֶב שְׁבִיעִית? מִשֶּׁתִּכְלֶה הַלֵּחָה, וְכׇל זְמַן שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם חוֹרְשִׁים לִיטַּע מִקְשָׁאוֹת וּמִדְלָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna (see *Shevi'it* 2:1) additionally states: **And until when may** one **plow a white field,** i.e., a grain field, on **the eve of the Sabbatical Year?** One may plow **until the** residual **moisture** in the fields from the rain **ceases and so long as people** continue to **plow** their fields in order **to plant cucumbers and gourds,** which are planted at the end of the winter.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם כֵּן נָתְנָה תּוֹרָה שִׁיעוּר לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד בְּיָדוֹ. אֶלָּא: בִּשְׂדֵה הַלָּבָן — עַד הַפֶּסַח, וּבִשְׂדֵה הָאִילָן — עַד הָעֲצֶרֶת. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: עַד הַפֶּסַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: If it is so** that no set time was established, then **the Torah has given an** individual **measure** of time **into the hands of each and every individual.** One may plow until a self-determined time, as he can always claim that he is plowing in order to plant during the sixth year. **Rather,** a fixed time must be established: **In a white field** one may plow **until Passover, in an orchard** one may plow **until *Shavuot*, and Beit Hillel say: Until Passover.**

The Gemara now cites the first Mishnah of *Shevi'it* in full. *Ad masai chorshin b'sdei ilan erev shevi'it?* — Until when may one plow an orchard in the year preceding *shemita*? Beis Shammai answers: *kol zman sheyafe l'pri* — as long as the plowing benefits the existing fruit on the trees, since one is then serving the current year's growth rather than preparing the field for the *shemita* year. Beis Hillel says: *ad ha'Atzeret* — until Shavuot. The Gemara notes these positions are roughly contemporaneous in practice. For a white field — a wheat field — the cutoff is *mishe'ichla halacha* — once the moisture from the rains has been absorbed — or as long as people are still plowing to plant various vegetables for the current year.

Rabbi Shimon objects to the open-ended standard based on current practice. *Im kein nasata toras kol echad v'echad b'yado* — such a standard places the law in the hands of each individual, producing chaos across different communities and regions where planting seasons vary. He therefore standardizes the cutoffs: for a wheat field, until Pesach; for an orchard, until Shavuot, as Beis Hillel said.

### Gamliel Annuls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן פַּזִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִשּׁוּם בַּר קַפָּרָא: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית דִּינוֹ נִמְנוּ עַל שְׁנֵי פְּרָקִים הַלָּלוּ, וּבִטְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi said** that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said in the name of bar Kappara: Rabban Gamliel and his court** discussed and then **voted about the** prohibitions of **these two periods,** i.e., from Passover or *Shavuot* until Rosh HaShana, **and nullified them,** thereby permitting plowing until Rosh HaShana, the actual beginning of the Sabbatical Year.

Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi then reports in the name of Bar Kappara: Rabban Gamliel and his *Beis Din* voted (*nimnu*) to abolish these two cutoff periods — Pesach and Shavuot — *u'vitlum*. They permitted continued field work beyond those dates, effectively removing the extended *tosefet* restrictions that Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel had established.

### Override Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי זֵירָא לְרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית דִּינוֹ הֵיכִי מָצוּ מְבַטְּלִי תַּקַּנְתָּא דְּבֵית שַׁמַּאי וּבֵית הִלֵּל? וְהָא תְּנַן: אֵין בֵּית דִּין יָכוֹל לְבַטֵּל דִּבְרֵי בֵּית דִּין חֲבֵירוֹ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ בְּחָכְמָה וּבְמִנְיָן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira said to Rabbi Abbahu, and some say** that it was **Reish Lakish** who said **to Rabbi Yoḥanan: How could Rabban Gamliel and his court nullify an ordinance** instituted by **Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel,** who were greater authorities than they were? **Didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Eduyyot* 1:5): **A court cannot nullify the ruling of another** court **unless it surpasses it in wisdom and in number?**

This raises an immediate and serious problem. Rav Zeira put the question to Rabbi Abba, and some say Resh Lakish put it to Rabbi Yochanan: *Rabban Gamliel u'veito dino heichi matzi mevatli takanta de-Veit Shammai u'Veit Hillel?* — How could Rabban Gamliel and his court annul a *takana* of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel? The principle stated in a Mishnah is explicit: *ein Beis Din yachol levatel divrei Beis Din chaveiro ella im ken gadol mimenu b'chochma u'veminyan* — a court cannot override the rulings of a previous court unless it surpasses it in both wisdom and number. Rabban Gamliel's court could not plausibly claim superiority over Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel. The question is profound, and the Gemara records that upon hearing it, the respondent was momentarily stunned — *ishtomem k'sha'ah chada* — quoting the language of the book of Daniel.

### Stipulation Clause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֶשְׁתּוֹמַם כְּשָׁעָה חֲדָא״. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵימוֹר, כָּךְ הִתְנוּ בֵּינֵיהֶן: כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לְבַטֵּל — יָבוֹא וִיבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Abbahu **"was astonished for a while"** (Daniel 4:16), and then **said to him: Say** that when Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel established their decree, **they stipulated among themselves: Anyone who** later **wishes to nullify** this decree **may come and nullify** it.

The Gemara's resolution, offered after that pause: *imar kach hayah tena'o* — one must say that this was always the condition embedded in the original *takana*. *Kol ha'rotzeh levatel yavo v'yevatel* — whoever wishes to annul it may come and annul it. The original enactment of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel was promulgated with a built-in escape clause granting any future court the right to repeal it if circumstances warranted. This is why Rabban Gamliel's court had the authority to do what it did.

This resolution might seem convenient, but Rabbeinu Gershom points out the underlying logic that makes it credible: the economic consequences of prohibiting agricultural work not merely for the *shemita* year itself but for an additional stretch extending back to Pesach — potentially a period of a year and a half of constraints — were significant. The Rabbis who established the extended *tosefet* restrictions may well have anticipated that future communities might find such a burden unsustainable and deliberately left the door open for a future court to lift the Rabbinic addition while leaving the core prohibition intact.

A clarifying note on the phrase *b'chochma u'veminyan*: the requirement for a superior court is always conjunctive — both greater wisdom *and* greater number are required. There is a significant debate among the *Rishonim* as to the precise meaning of *minyan* in this context, but the requirement is never disjunctive.

## Tosefet Source

### Eser Netiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּידְהוּ הִיא? הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי הִיא! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא אִישׁ בִּקְעַת בֵּית חוֹרְתָן: עֶשֶׂר נְטִיעוֹת, עֲרָבָה, וְנִיסּוּךְ הַמַּיִם, הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is this** ordinance **theirs?** Did Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel institute the ordinance and as such have the authority to attach stipulations to it? **It is a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai. As Rabbi Asi said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said in the name of Rabbi Neḥunya from the valley of Beit Ḥortan:** The *halakha* of **ten saplings,** the *mitzva* of bringing **willow** branches to the Temple on *Sukkot* and standing them up around the altar, and the *halakha* of **water libation** on *Sukkot* are all ***halakhot*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** Consequently, the prohibition against plowing on the eve of the seventh year is a not a rabbinic ordinance from the Second Temple period, but rather an oral tradition dating back to Moses at Sinai.

Having established that the extended *tosefet* restrictions are Rabbinic and were repealed by Rabban Gamliel, the Gemara now asks a more fundamental question: *didehu hi?* Is the concept of *tosefet shevi'it* itself a Rabbinic innovation? Or is it rooted in something more ancient? The Gemara answers: *hilchata l'Moshe mi'Sinai hi* — it is a tradition going back to Moses at Sinai.

Rabbi Assi reports in the name of Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Nechunya of *Bikat Beit Chortan* that three things are *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*: the rule of *eser netiyot* (ten saplings), the use of the *arava* in the *Beit HaMikdash* on Sukkot, and the water libations on Sukkot. Our focus is on the rule of *eser netiyot*. If ten saplings are distributed within a *beit sa'ah* — an area of approximately 50 by 50 *ammot* — in the proper spacing, then one is permitted to work that entire field right up until Rosh Hashanah of the *shemita* year. The spacing matters: too sparse and the trees are too few to justify the work; too crowded and the trees are not deriving adequate benefit from the labor.

### Implication

This *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* carries a critical implication. The tradition specifies a special allowance permitting work up to Rosh Hashanah only in this particular case of ten saplings with optimal spacing. Why would such a special dispensation be needed? Because without it, working the field in close proximity to Rosh Hashanah of the *shemita* year would be prohibited. The very fact that the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* grants an exceptional allowance for this particular configuration demonstrates that the general rule — prohibiting work close to Rosh Hashanah — is itself the operative norm. This means the concept of *tosefet shevi'it* is not a Rabbinic invention; it is established by a tradition from Sinai itself.

### Month & Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: כִּי גְּמִירִי הִלְכְתָא, שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם לִפְנֵי רֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה, וַאֲתוֹ הָנֵי תַּקּוּן מִפֶּסַח וּמֵעֲצֶרֶת, וְאַתְנוֹ בְּדִידְהוּ: כָּל הָרוֹצֶה לְבַטֵּל יָבוֹא וִיבַטֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: When they learned** this ***halakha*** as a tradition dating back to Moses at Sinai, the prohibition applied from only **thirty days before Rosh HaShana.** Afterward, **these** Sages of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel **came** and **instituted** lengthier periods of restriction, **from Passover and from *Shavuot*,** respectively, **but they stipulated among themselves: Anyone who** later **wishes to nullify** this decree **may come and nullify** it. Rabban Gamliel and his court were therefore able to nullify extended restrictions instituted by Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel.

How do we reconcile this with the earlier framing of *tosefet* as a Rabbinic *takana* established by Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel — and subsequently annulled by Rabban Gamliel? Rabbi Yitzchak provides the resolution: *ki gemiri hilchasa shloshim yom lifnei Rosh Hashanah* — the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* establishes a *tosefet* of exactly thirty days — one month — prior to Rosh Hashanah of the *shemita* year. That is the Sinaitic tradition. *V'asu hani* — then came the Sages and extended it further: *tiknu mi'Pesach me'atzeret* — they enacted that for certain fields one must stop work already from Pesach, and for others from Shavuot. This extension beyond the one month is the Rabbinic *takana* of Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel.

### Rabbinic Portion

It was specifically this Rabbinic extension that Rabban Gamliel's court annulled. *V'asnu b'didhu* — and they stipulated in their portion, the Rabbinic portion, *kol ha'rotzeh levatel yavo v'yevatel* — whoever wishes to annul it may come and annul it. The one-month *tosefet* grounded in the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* remains untouched; Rabban Gamliel did not and could not repeal that. What he repealed was the additional Rabbinic overlay that had pushed the restriction back to Pesach and Shavuot. The original Rabbinic enactment had contained its own escape clause precisely anticipating this possibility — because the economic and agricultural burden of prohibiting work for so extended a period beyond the *shemita* year itself was significant enough that the original enactors allowed for future relief.

The result is a layered structure: a *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* establishing one month of *tosefet*, a Rabbinic extension reaching back to Pesach and Shavuot, and a subsequent annulment of the Rabbinic extension by Rabban Gamliel's court. The question of whether *tosefet* also has a Biblical basis in the verse *bacharish u'vakatzir tishbot* — beyond the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — remains open for the continuation of the discussion, which will be taken up in the next shiur.
