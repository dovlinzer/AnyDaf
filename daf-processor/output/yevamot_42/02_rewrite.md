# Yevamot 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Three-Month Wait

### Three-Month Wait Intro

The daf opens on 42b, picking up approximately five lines from the top, with the Mishnah's statement *achas besulos v'achas beulos* — whether the woman is a virgin or a non-virgin, whether her first marriage was *eirusin* or *nissuin*, she must wait three months before remarrying. The rationale is the familiar principle of *lo plug*: even in cases where logic would seem to confirm that pregnancy is impossible, we do not make distinctions. The Gemara then opens by asking why the Mishnah needed to list both categories of virgin/*arusa* and non-virgin/*nesuah* separately, since they appear to overlap entirely.

### Virgin/Non-Virgin Distin…

The Gemara resolves this: *hachi ka'amar* — what the Mishnah is saying is that the categories of virgin and non-virgin simply map onto the categories of *eirusin* and *nissuin*, respectively. They are not independent variables but rather explanatory: women widowed or divorced from *eirusin* and women widowed or divorced from *nissuin*. The two terms elaborate the same two categories rather than introducing additional ones.

Following this, we are told that *Rabbi Eliezer* did not come to the *beis medrash* on a particular occasion. He later encountered *Rabbi Assi* and inquired what had been taught. Rabbi Assi reported: *hachi amar Rabbi Yochanan, halacha k'Rabbi Yossi* — Rabbi Yochanan ruled that we follow Rabbi Yossi, who holds that we do *not* apply *lo plug*. Both Rabbi Yossi and Rabbi Yehuda agree that the three-month wait is only necessary when the first marriage involved *nissuin* and the new marriage will also be *nissuin* — i.e., when there was an opportunity for marital relations at both ends. If either stage is merely *eirusin*, there is no concern, and Rabbi Yossi adds a separate point regarding *aveilus* that we will address later.

### Lo Plug as Daas Yachid

This ruling prompts the Gemara to observe: *mikhlal d'yechida pligi alei* — does this imply that the *stam* voice in the Mishnah, which demands the three-month wait universally and thus represents the position of *lo plug*, is in fact not the consensus but a *daas yachid*, a single minority opinion? The Gemara answers: *in* — yes, and it can be demonstrated from a *braisa*.

### Pregnancy-Impossible Cas…

The *braisa* enumerates a long series of cases in which we can effectively presume that the woman is not pregnant, yet the requirement to wait three months nonetheless applies. Among these: a woman who was always running off to her father's house during the marriage (*redufa l'beis aviha*), a woman who was in open conflict with her husband (*katata im ba'ala*), a woman whose husband was imprisoned, or was elderly, or was sick, or who was herself ill. Additionally: a woman who suffered a miscarriage after her husband's death, a woman who was infertile (*akara*), a woman who was post-menopausal (*zkeina*), a woman who was too young, a woman born incapable of bearing children (*ailonis*), or any woman for whatever reason incapable of giving birth. In all of these cases, *Rabbi Meir* says she must wait three months.

Then Rabbi Yehuda — and some have the variant reading *Rabbi Yossi*, since both make a similar distinction — says: *matir l'areis v'linasei miyad*, she may perform *eirusin* immediately, and in these specific cases where pregnancy is impossible, she may even proceed to *nissuin* immediately. The *lo plug* position is thus identified as the view of *Rabbi Meir* alone — a *daas yachid* — which is why Rabbi Yochanan was able to rule against it, following Rabbi Yehuda or Rabbi Yossi, who permit immediate *nissuin* in circumstances where pregnancy is demonstrably impossible.

## R. Yochanan Reversal

### Chazar Bo Ruling

Rabbi Chiya bar Abba then reports: *chazar bo Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan reversed himself. He now reverts to *lo plug* and holds that the three-month wait is always required. Rav Yosef explains the reason for this reversal: *mi-misneisa d'karma hadar bei* — he reversed himself on account of the *braisa* from *Kerem B'Yavne*. As the *braisa* records, Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka testified: I heard from the sages gathered in *Kerem B'Yavne* — the seat of rabbinic authority established after the destruction of the Temple, so called because the sages sat in rows like a vineyard — *kulam tzrichin l'hamtin shlosha chodashim*, all women, without exception, must wait three months. No distinction is made based on the possibility or impossibility of pregnancy.

### Stam Mishna Challenge

Rabbi Yirmiya then instructs Rabbi Zeira: when you appear before *Rabbi Abbahu*, present him with the following contradiction. On one hand, Rabbi Yochanan ruled *halacha k'Rabbi Yossi*, following the opinion that we do not apply *lo plug*. On the other hand, Rabbi Yochanan is the one who established the principle that *halacha k'stam mishna* — we rule in accordance with the anonymous, unattributed Mishnah. And our Mishnah states *stam*, without attribution, that all women must wait three months regardless of whether they are virgins or not. How could Rabbi Yochanan have originally ruled against that *stam*?

Rabbi Abbahu's response is sharp: *lo chash l'kimchei* — the one who raised that question has not ground his flour properly; it is an unworthy question. The case before us is not a simple *stam mishna* — it is a case of *stam v'acharav machlokes*: the Mishnah first presents an anonymous ruling and then immediately records a dispute. Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yossi disagree right there in the very same Mishnah. When Rabbi Yochanan says we rule according to the *stam mishna*, that principle applies only when the *stam* stands uncontested in its own context, while dissenting opinions are found elsewhere — in other *mishnayos* or in a *braisa*. But when the Mishnah itself voices the disagreement immediately after the *stam*, that is a case of *stam v'acharav machlokes*, and that configuration carries different weight.

### Stam V'Achar Machloket

The governing rule is stated explicitly: *machlokes v'acharakav stam, halacha k'stam* — if a dispute appears first and is then followed by a *stam*, we rule according to the *stam*. But *stam v'acharakav machlokes, ein halacha k'stam* — if the *stam* comes first and is then followed by a dispute, we do not necessarily rule according to the *stam*. This does not necessarily mean we rule against the *stam*, but it does mean we are not obligated to follow it in the way we would follow an unchallenged *stam*. This is why Rabbi Yochanan was initially free to rule like Rabbi Yossi, notwithstanding the *stam* in the Mishnah. When he subsequently reversed himself, it was due to the independent authority of the *braisa* from *Kerem B'Yavne*, not because of the *stam*.

This principle — that *stam* followed by *machlokes* does not carry binding force — has broad significance well beyond our *sugya*. The same hermeneutic logic applies to the interpretation of the *Shulchan Aruch*: when the *Shulchan Aruch* states a ruling anonymously and then introduces *v'yesh omrim*, there is a developed jurisprudence around which opinion is operative, parallel to the Talmudic questions of *stam v'yesh omrim* and *yesh omrim v'stam*.

It is worth noting that the Gemara had earlier identified the *stam* voice in this Mishnah as representing *Rabbi Meir*. This observation is appropriate, since *stam mishna aliba d'Rabbi Meir* — the anonymous Mishnah generally reflects Rabbi Meir's position. The Gemara's point that the *stam* is really a *daas yachid* carries extra weight here, since every *stam* is in some sense traceable to Rabbi Meir rather than to a genuine consensus.

### Stam/Machloket Configs

Rabbi Abbahu's attendant (*shammash*) uses their time together — as Rabbi Abbahu was being supported on the attendant's shoulders and the attendant was gathering *halachos* from him — to work through several configurations of *stam* and *machlokes* across different sources. The attendant asks: *machlokes v'achar kach stam, mai?* — if the dispute appears first and the *stam* follows, what do we say? Rabbi Abbahu answers: *halacha k'stam*, we rule according to the *stam*. And if the *stam* appears first and the *machlokes* follows? *Ein halacha k'stam*.

Then the question of cross-source configurations: *stam* in the Mishnah, *machlokes* in a *braisa* — what is the rule? *Halacha k'stam*, still follow the *stam*. If anything, this is even clearer than the case where the debate is embedded within the Mishnah itself. What about the reverse — *machlokes* in the Mishnah and *stam* in a *braisa*? Rabbi Abbahu answers: *v'chi Rabbi lo shna'ah, Rabbi Chiya m'na lei* — if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi chose not to codify that position as a *stam* in the Mishnah, on what basis would Rabbi Chiya's transmission of it as a *stam* in the *braisa* override that? The Mishnah carries supreme authority; the compiler's editorial choices are themselves normative signals. The fact that Rabbi chose not to present a given opinion as an uncontested ruling in the Mishnah cannot simply be overridden by Rabbi Chiya's different editorial decision in the *braisa*.

## Combs Digression

### Flax/Wool Combs

The attendant now challenges Rabbi Abbahu's principle by producing a *stam mishna* that everyone knows is not ruled upon in practice. The Mishnah in question concerns combs — specifically, flax combs and wool combs — and whether they remain susceptible to *tumah* when their teeth have been broken off.

The relevant Mishnah teaches: regarding a flax comb (*masreika shel pishtan*) whose teeth have been removed — if two teeth remain together (*nishtayaru vo shtayim*), the comb is *tamei*, meaning it retains its status as a functional vessel and can become *tamei*. If only one tooth remains (*achas*), it is *tehorah*, since a comb with a single tooth is no longer functionally a comb and loses its *kli* status. As for the individual teeth themselves that break off one by one, they are *temeios* — susceptible to *tumah* — because a single tooth can be repurposed as something like a toothpick.

The Mishnah then turns to a wool comb (*shel tzemer*): if teeth are missing such that there is a gap of one between every two remaining teeth (*achas mibein shtayim*), it is *tehorah*. If three teeth remain in one place (*nishtayaru vo gimel bemakom echad*), it is *tamei*. If the outermost tooth is one of those three, it is *tehorah*, since the outer teeth are less functionally significant than the inner ones. If two teeth broke off together and can serve as a tweezer-like implement, they are *temeios*. If a single tooth was fashioned into a lamp cleaner or thread-stretcher (*tikkena l'ner o l'misuach*), it is *tamei*.

The point of invoking this Mishnah is this: *dekayma lan de'ein halacha k'zos hamishna* — we know that the *halacha* is not like this Mishnah. Here is an apparently uncontested *stam mishna* that is nonetheless not followed. How then can one maintain that we always rule according to a *stam mishna*?

The Gemara does not immediately accept the attendant's challenge at face value, however, and instead probes whether the Mishnah is internally consistent at all. The section on the wool comb appears contradictory: one clause implies that two teeth together render the comb *tamei*, while the concluding clause states that only three together do so. The Gemara asks: *umai kashya* — is this truly a contradiction? Perhaps the two clauses refer to different types of teeth — inner teeth versus outer teeth (*gavyaysa* vs. *varaysa*), as Rashi explains in terms of a two-tiered comb structure where the outer teeth scrape and the inner teeth collect. The outer teeth require three in one place to constitute a functional configuration; the inner teeth require only two.

The Gemara then pursues a different internal contradiction between the flax and wool sections. By the flax comb, individual broken teeth are *temeios* without any modification — it is assumed they can naturally serve as toothpicks. By the wool comb, a single tooth is *tamei* only *if* it was modified and fashioned into a usable instrument. That appears contradictory. The Gemara proposes: perhaps one speaks of a tooth that broke off with a piece of the base still attached (*b'kataya*) and one of a tooth that broke off cleanly without the base (*b'lo kataya*) — the tooth with a base-piece has a natural handle and needs no modification, while the bare tooth requires fashioning. Rav Papa offers yet another resolution: perhaps the difference is between thin teeth and thick teeth (*katinta* vs. *alimta*), where the thinner ones naturally lend themselves to reuse while the thicker ones do not.

### Comb Mishna Attribution

After exhausting these resolutions, the Gemara concedes that the internal contradictions can in fact be resolved — the Mishnah may not be genuinely problematic at all. But then the Gemara discloses the true reason we do not rule like this Mishnah: *mishum demasaymi bei dokanay, zu divrei Rabbi Shimon* — those who are precise about the text (*dokanai*) conclude the Mishnah with the words *zu divrei Rabbi Shimon*: "these are the words of Rabbi Shimon." It is therefore not a true *stam mishna* in the first place. Other textual versions attribute the ruling explicitly to Rabbi Shimon, and once the opinion carries an attribution, the rule of following the *stam* does not apply. The attendant's challenge thus collapses: this is not a case where a genuine *stam mishna* is overridden — it is a case where the *stam* is more apparent than real. Certain manuscripts and precise transmitters record it with explicit attribution, and that suffices to classify it as a named opinion.

This entire digression — remarkable in how many distinct sub-questions it generates about combs and tumah — is in the end a vehicle for clarifying the limits and the reliability of the principle of *halacha k'stam mishna*.

## Day 90 vs. Day 91

### Eirusin Within 3 Months

Returning to the main topic of the three-month wait, the Gemara records a ruling transmitted by *Rabbi Yitzchak bar Avin*: *ma'arsin toch gimel* — one may perform *eirusin* within the three-month period. This reflects the approach of Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that *eirusin* does not place the woman in a context where she could become pregnant, and therefore does not trigger the waiting requirement. Furthermore, *v'chein osin ma'aseh* — this is the operative practice. Rabbi Elazar is also cited as having taught similarly in the name of *Rabbi Chanina hagadol*.

Associated with this position is the teaching that *rubo shel rishon, v'emtza'i mishalem, v'ruvo shel shelishi* — the majority of the first month, the entirety of the middle month, and the majority of the third month would suffice as the requisite waiting period. This formulation is striking, and neither Rashi nor Tosafot engage with it directly in the context of the Gemara's discussion. One possible reading is that this opinion does not accept the Gemara's earlier principle that a viable birth can occur after six months and two days of gestation — perhaps this view requires a full seven months. If so, then waiting two months plus a portion of a third would be sufficient to rule out the earliest possible birth being attributed to the deceased husband. This remains somewhat unclear from the Gemara's framing. Tosafot suggests this lenient calculation applies only to the case of an *arusah* — it represents not a complete waiver of the three-month period but a more flexible definition of how those three months are measured.

### Amimar vs. Rav Ashi

*Ameimar* went further and ruled that one may even perform *eirusin* on day ninety itself — the ninetieth day counting from the husband's death. This reflects a measure of leniency in defining the conclusion of the three-month period, stopping short of requiring a full ninety-one days. Rav Ashi challenged this position.

### Rav/Shmuel Exclusion Rule

Rav Ashi invoked the joint ruling of *Rav and Shmuel*, who both stated: *tzricha l'hamtin gimel chodashim, chutz mi'yom she'meis bo v'chutz mi'yom she'nis'arsah bo* — the woman must wait three months, not including the day of the husband's death and not including the day of the *eirusin*. The practical implication is that the *eirusin* must be performed on day ninety-one, not day ninety. Ameimar attempted to deflect this: he argued that the citation from Rav and Shmuel was not about the three-month wait at all, but about the twenty-four-month nursing period — the rule that a nursing mother must wait twenty-four months before remarrying, *chutz mi'yom she'nolad bo v'chutz mi'yom she'nis'arsah bo*, not counting the day of the birth and not counting the day of the *eirusin*, so that the wedding takes place on the twenty-fifth month. Rav Ashi rejected this reading: the formulation applies to both contexts.

### Final: 3 Months Always

The Gemara then records a case involving someone who made a *seudat erusin* — a betrothal feast — on the ninetieth day; *Rava* voided the event and required them to wait until the following day. The Gemara notes that this case was actually a *seudat nissuin*, a full marriage feast, not merely *eirusin* — and naturally, for *nissuin*, day ninety-one is unambiguously required.

The *hilchata* — the authoritative conclusion — is stated: for the nursing mother's twenty-four-month wait, not counting the day of the birth and not counting the day of *kiddushin*, so the wedding falls on the twenty-fifth month. For the three-month remarriage wait, not counting the day of death and not counting the day of *eirusin*, so *eirusin* falls on day ninety-one.

The most significant point of the conclusion, beyond the day-ninety versus day-ninety-one question, is that *lo plug* is fully in force: even *eirusin* requires a complete three-month wait. The discussions of leniency — whether to permit *eirusin* within three months, or to count day ninety as sufficient — are consistently ruled against. The final position is unequivocal: three months are required across the board, including for *eirusin*.

## Pregnancy Testing

### Pregnancy Test Validity

One might conclude from the *lo plug* ruling that modern pregnancy tests cannot satisfy the requirement — that we always demand three months regardless. But this may not follow. The Gemara itself, in earlier portions of the *sugya*, entertained the possibility of physical tests to establish the woman's status. That suggests the underlying concern is not three months for its own sake, but rather a reliable determination of whether she is pregnant.

Understood this way, there are two distinct questions. The first is whether we require *some* means of determination in all cases. The second is whether the only valid means is the three-month wait, or whether an alternative means — such as a modern pregnancy test — would be equally acceptable. There is a coherent argument that *lo plug* operates on the first question, not necessarily on the second. The rationale of *lo plug* is not that we genuinely believe a post-menopausal woman might be pregnant; rather, we do not wish to create distinctions between cases, lest some genuine cases of potential pregnancy slip through undetected. If that is the concern, then *lo plug* demands that we require *a* determination in all cases — but the determination itself need not be limited to the passage of three months. A reliable pregnancy test, even for a woman where pregnancy is biologically impossible, might satisfy the requirement under this logic: we require the test universally, but we allow the test to substitute for the three-month wait. Whether *poskim* in fact permit this is a *machlokes haposkim*, and the question is live.

## Eirusin/Aveilus Issue

### R. Yossi Widow Exception

We now turn to the second dimension of Rabbi Yossi's position: his exception for widows. As the Mishnah records, even if *eirusin* is otherwise permissible within the three-month period — because *eirusin* does not place the woman in a context of marital relations — a widow (*almanah*) may not enter into *eirusin* during the first month, because she is still in *aveilus* for her deceased husband. The paradigmatic case involves a woman who was *nesuah* to her first husband, who died; she is in *aveilus* for him. She is now going to perform *eirusin* with a second man. Since the new relationship is only *eirusin* — not yet *nissuin* — there is no concern of pregnancy attribution. But the first month of *aveilus* for the deceased husband still applies, and during that period, the *eirusin* cannot take place.

The complication that the Gemara will work through is this: the assumption that there is no *aveilus* for a relationship that was merely *eirusin* is universal and undisputed. If the deceased husband and this woman were only *arusim* — never *nesuim* — she would not be in *aveilus* at all. So Rabbi Yossi's exception must be addressing a case where the first marriage was *nissuin*, meaning she genuinely is in *aveilus*, and the new relationship is going to be *eirusin*, which is why there is no pregnancy concern — but the *aveilus* concern remains.

### Kal V'Chomer Argument

*Rav Chisda* challenges this by means of a *kal vachomer*: if during a period when laundering is forbidden — such as the first week of *shiva* — *eirusin* is nonetheless permitted, then certainly during the rest of the month of *aveilus*, when laundering *is* permitted, *eirusin* should be permitted. The *kal vachomer* rests on the premise that *eirusin* does not constitute the kind of *simcha* that is prohibited during *aveilus* — only *nissuin* does. It is well established, after all, that one may perform *kiddushin* even on *Tisha B'Av* itself, as *Shmuel* rules based on the concern *shema yekadmena acher* — lest someone else preempt him in betrothing the woman. If *kiddushin* is permitted on *Tisha B'Av*, how can it be restricted during the less stringent later period of personal *aveilus*?

### Tisha B'Av Kiddushin

The Gemara probes the sources underlying this *kal vachomer*. A Mishnah teaches that during the week in which *Tisha B'Av* falls (*shavua she'chal bo Tisha B'Av*), one may not get a haircut or do laundry. A *braisa* then states that beginning from *Rosh Chodesh Av*, the public limits its commerce, building, and planting — but *ma'arsin*, *kiddushin* may be performed. One may not, however, bring the woman into the home (*lo konsin*) — *nissuin* is prohibited. Nor may one hold a *seudat erusin*, a betrothal feast, though the act of *kiddushin* itself is permitted.

The Gemara then complicates the *kal vachomer*: perhaps the *braisa*'s phrase "before this time" (*kodem hazman hazeh*) does not mean during *Rosh Chodesh Av* in anticipation of *Tisha B'Av*, but rather before the week of *Tisha B'Av* itself — and during the actual week of *Tisha B'Av*, even *kiddushin* is forbidden. Rava responds: *v'kodem d'kodem nami kal v'chomer* — even if *kiddushin* is forbidden during the week of *Tisha B'Av*, I can reconstruct the *kal vachomer* using the earlier period. During the nine days when business is restricted (*mishenikhnas Av mema'atin b'simcha*), *kiddushin* is still permitted. Certainly then, during the month of personal *aveilus* when business is *not* restricted, *kiddushin* should be permitted.

### Case Flip Strategy

At this point the Gemara shifts strategy. The *kal vachomer* appears compelling — *eirusin* seems like it should always be permitted during *aveilus*. The Gemara therefore concludes that it must reread Rabbi Yossi's position entirely. Rather than accepting the plain reading of the Mishnah — that Rabbi Yossi says *all women may do eirusin* except for the *almanah* who is in *aveilus* — the Gemara proposes: *lo teima d'Rabbi Yossi omer kol hanashim ye'arsu; ella eima kol hanashim yinashu* — do not read it as saying all women may do *eirusin*; read it instead as saying all women may do *nissuin*, except for the *almanah* due to her *aveilus*. On this reading, *eirusin* is entirely unmentioned and unproblematic; it is *nissuin* that Rabbi Yossi is discussing.

The Gemara immediately asks: but *nissuin* is a problem for all women during the three-month waiting period — why would the *almanah* be singled out? The answer: *leit lei l'Rabbi Yossi l'havchana* — Rabbi Yossi is not concerned with establishing paternity. He does not require the three-month wait in principle. So for Rabbi Yossi, *nissuin* is generally permitted immediately after widowhood or divorce; the only constraint is *aveilus*.

An alternative formulation is then proposed: perhaps Rabbi Yossi is saying that *all arusot grushot* — women who were *arusot* and are now *grushot* (divorcees), not *almanos* — may proceed to *nissuin*. The case is inverted: the first marriage was *eirusin* (hence no pregnancy concern), and the question is whether the woman may proceed to *nissuin* in the new marriage. The *almanah* from *eirusin* cannot, because — but now the Gemara will question even that, since it is precisely the case of an *almanah from eirusin* that raises the problem: does she have *aveilus* at all?

### Braita Reinterpretation

The Gemara continues to work through this reinterpretation in a *braisa*. The *braisa* states: *Rabbi Yossi omer kol hanashim yit-arsu chutz m'almanah min ha-eibol* — all women may perform *eirusin* except for the *almanah* due to *aveilus*, and the period of her *aveilus* is thirty days. Furthermore, all women may not do *nissuin* until three months have passed.

The plain reading of this *braisa* corresponds to the plain reading of the Mishnah: *neshuot* may do *eirusin*, with the only exception being the *almanah* during her month of *aveilus*. The Gemara had earlier resolved the Mishnah by inverting its terms; it now attempts the same with the *braisa*. *Okmina b'arusot* — we interpret the Mishnah's references not to *neshuot* doing *eirusin*, but to *arusot grushot* doing *nissuin*. And *hachi nami kol arusot grushot yinase'u* — the *braisa* similarly is reread: all *arusot grushot* may proceed to *nissuin*.

The end of the *braisa* — *v'kulan lo yinase'u ad sheyihiye lahem gimel chodashim* — yields a further inference: *ha arusah shapir dami* — if the woman started as an *arusah*, she may presumably perform *eirusin* and even proceed to *nissuin* immediately. This would imply that Rabbi Yossi permits a *nesuah* to perform *eirusin* freely. Rava intervenes: *tartzei v'eima hachi* — reread the end of the *braisa* as well. Rabbi Yossi's ruling is now to be read as: all *arusot grushot* may do *nissuin*, except for the *almanah* who is in *aveilus* for thirty days. And in the reverse case: *nesuos lo yisarsu ad she'yehu lahem shlosha chodashim* — women who were *nesuot* may not do *eirusin* until three months have passed.

This rewriting is acknowledged as extremely complex. What is not fully explained in the Gemara is why Rabbi Yossi, once his case is flipped to be about an *arusah* doing *nissuin*, should thereby be committed to prohibiting a *nesuah* from doing *eirusin* in all circumstances. Why could he not hold, like Rabbi Yehuda, that as long as one of the two stages is *eirusin*, there is no pregnancy concern? The Gemara does not fully resolve this, but the result of the entire textual reworking is to demonstrate that the Gemara is bothered by any reading of Rabbi Yossi under which *eirusin* would be restricted during *aveilus*, and it therefore inverts his case so that only *nissuin* is at stake.

### No Aveilus Eirusin

The fundamental premise underlying all of this is now made explicit: does a woman who was only *arusa* — never *nesua* — to a man have *aveilus* if he dies? A *braisa* transmitted by *Rabbi Chiya bar Ami* is unequivocal: if a man's wife was only his *arusa*, he is not an *onen* for her if she dies, and if he is a *kohen*, he does not become *tamei* for her. Similarly, if the husband dies, she is not an *onenet* and has no burial obligations toward him. If she dies, he does not inherit her. If he dies, she is entitled to collect her *kesubah* — the basic sum of two hundred *zuz* is owed from the time of *eirusin* — but she has no *aveilus* obligations. There is thus no *aveilus* whatsoever in an *eirusin* relationship, neither for the man nor the woman.

This brings the Gemara back to where it began. No one disputes that there is no *aveilus* for an *eirusin* relationship. Therefore, if the case involves a woman who was only *arusa* to her first husband and he died, she is in no *aveilus* at all and could in principle do *kiddushin* immediately. If the case involves a woman who was *nesuah* to her first husband, she is in *aveilus*, but in order to remove the three-month pregnancy concern, the new relationship must be *eirusin* — and that raises the question of why *eirusin* would be problematic during *aveilus*.

### Tannaim Debate

The Gemara resolves the tension: *ella tanai hi* — the question of whether *eirusin* is prohibited during personal *aveilus* is itself a *machlokes tannaim*. A *braisa* records: from *Rosh Chodesh Av* until the fast, one limits business and also refrains from *me'ares u'milisa* — from *eirusin* and *nissuin*. Then, during the week in which *Tisha B'Av* falls, haircuts and laundry are also prohibited. Some say the haircut and laundry restrictions extend through the entire month.

According to one opinion in this *braisa*, then, even *eirusin* is prohibited during the nine days preceding *Tisha B'Av*. It is that position which Rabbi Yossi follows when he prohibits *eirusin* during personal *aveilus*.

Rav Ashi then raises a careful question: when the *braisa* prohibits *eirusin* before *Tisha B'Av*, does it really mean the formal act of *kiddushin*, or does it mean only the *seudat erusin* — the betrothal feast? Perhaps the act of *kiddushin* itself is always permitted, and only the feast is restricted. The Gemara pushes back: by the same token, when the *braisa* prohibits *nissuin*, would we say only the *seudat nissuin* is forbidden but the actual bringing-in of the bride is permitted? Obviously not — *nissuin* without a feast still constitutes the full *simcha* of marriage. But *eirusin* without a feast is a different matter: *erusin belo seuda mi ika simcha?* — is there any *simcha* at all in *eirusin* without a feast? The answer is that there is not. Therefore, the *braisa*'s prohibition of *eirusin* must refer only to the feast, and the act of *kiddushin* itself is always permitted.

We are thus back to the original question: if *kiddushin* itself is always permitted even during the *Tisha B'Av* period, what is Rabbi Yossi's basis for restricting it during personal *aveilus*?

### Aveilus Chadasha

*Rav Ashi* resolves this definitively: *shani aveilus chadasha m'aveilus yeshana* — a fresh, new *aveilus* differs from an old, commemorative one. *Tisha B'Av* is an *aveilus yeshana*, an ancient collective mourning; its demands are real but not of the same intimate, personal character as fresh personal mourning. Additionally: *shani aveilus d'rabbim m'aveilus d'yachid* — communal *aveilus* differs from individual *aveilus*. While one might argue that communal *aveilus* is more weighty in some respects — it affects the entire people — it is also less personally intimate than the acute grief of an individual mourner. Any evidence drawn from the *Tisha B'Av* laws is therefore inapplicable to the case of personal, fresh *aveilus*. Rabbi Yossi is entitled to hold — and Rabbi Yehuda may disagree — that in a personal, newly occurring *aveilus*, there is an *issur* of *kiddushin*. And with that resolution, we return to the simple reading of the Mishnah: Rabbi Yossi says *eirusin* is restricted during the first month of personal *aveilus*.

## Four Brothers Setup

### Mishna: 4 Brothers

The daf concludes with the opening of a new Mishnah. *Arba'ah achim nesuim arba nashim u'metu* — four brothers were married to four women, and all four brothers died. The Mishnah does not specify that the four wives are sisters; it is simply presenting a scenario in which a single surviving fifth brother is faced with multiple *yibum* obligations simultaneously, from the wives of four of his brothers. The question is how he may and should proceed. *Im ratza hagadol shebahem l'yavem et kullan, hareshut b'yado* — if the oldest brother wishes to perform *yibum* with all four of them, the prerogative is his. The responsibility devolves upon the eldest surviving brother, and he may choose to perform *yibum* with any or all of the *yevamos*.

### Chalitzah Disqualifies

A related rule is introduced: *mi she-hayah nasui l'shtei nashim u-meit* — if a man was married to two women and died, and *chalitzah* was performed with one of them, or marital relations were established with one of them, the co-wife (*tzarah*) is automatically released. Either *chalitzah* or *biah* with one of the *yevamos* exempts all the others.

### Preference Strategy

A practical principle follows: *hayetah achas k'sheirah v'achas p'sulah* — if one of the two wives was *k'sheirah* and the other was already *p'sulah l'kehunah* — for instance, she was a *grushah* from a prior marriage, and performing *chalitzah* with her would not add to her *pesulah* status since she already bears it — then if the *yavam* intends to perform *chalitzah*, he should perform *chalitzah* with the *p'sulah*, thereby not rendering the *k'sheirah* wife *p'sulah* to a *kohen* by virtue of *chalitzah*. Conversely, if the *yavam* intends to perform *yibum*, he should perform *yibum* with the *k'sheirah* — the more strategically and halachically preferable match. Whether this means he should exclusively do *yibum* with the *k'sheirah*, or merely that it is preferable, will be clarified by the Gemara in subsequent discussion.
