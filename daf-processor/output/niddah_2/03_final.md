# Niddah 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Taharos & Niddah

### Seder Taharos Overview

With *Masechet Niddah* we begin *Seder Taharos*. Indeed, we will conclude *Seder Taharos* when we finish this *masechet*, for it is the only tractate in *Taharos* upon which there is a *Gemara*. The reason is self-evident: *Niddah* is unique in that it carries not only the classic concerns of *tumah v'taharah* but also profound practical implications for what we today call *taharas hamishpacha* — namely, the prohibition against a man having relations with a woman who is a *niddah* or who bears one of the related *tumot* that the Torah associates with *tumat niddah*, such as the *tumah* of a *zavah* or a *yoledet*. It is that practical dimension that warrants a full tractate's treatment.

### Dual Aspects: Tumah & Sex

*Masechet Niddah* thus occupies a dual territory. On one level, it engages the pure *tumah v'taharah* question: on another, it addresses the *isur* of sexual relations. One notes that *niddah* first appears in *Tazria*, where the Torah speaks exclusively of *tumat niddah* alongside *tumat zav* and *zavah* — there is no mention there of any sexual prohibition. It is only in *Achrei Mos* and *Kedoshim* that the prohibition of relations with a *niddah* emerges. This makes *niddah* virtually singular among the *tumot*: it is the only *tumah* carrying so direct and severe an associated *isur*. One might note the case of a *metzora*, but that is not truly parallel. The *metzora* — based on the verse *michutz la'oholo* — is understood by *Chazal* to be prohibited from marital relations, but this applies only to the male *metzora*, not the female; it is at most an *aseh*, not remotely in the same category as the *isur* associated with *niddah*. The fascinating question of why *niddah* alone carries this dual character is one worth carrying throughout the *masechet*.

### Practical vs. Theoretical

A significant portion of the *masechet* deals not with the prohibition of sex at all, but with the purely theoretical questions of *tumah v'taharah* — whether a woman may touch *terumah*, enter the *Beis Hamikdash*, and so forth — concerns that have no practical application today. This means we will frequently encounter discussions whose operative context is a world of *tumah v'taharah* that no longer obtains. The *rishonim* explicitly raise this in connection with various topics, such as *bedikos* and *kesamim*, asking whether requirements developed in the context of *tumah v'taharah* carry over to our world, where the operative concern is solely the prohibition of sexual relations.

### Tumah-Sex Linkage

The relationship between a woman's *tumah* status and the applicability of the *isur* of sex is itself a recurring and genuinely open question throughout this *masechet*. The Torah addresses the *isur* of relations specifically with a *niddah*, and *Chazal* extend this to a *zavah* and *yoledet* on the basis that those *tumot* are related. But the precise linkage — whether being *tamei* for purposes of *terumah* and *korbanot* automatically entails *tumah* for the purpose of the sexual prohibition — is not always straightforward. As we will see, certain requirements applicable in a full world of *tumah v'taharah* may not carry over into the domain of the *isur*, and that interplay will recur throughout the *masechet*.

## Shammai's Position

### Shammai: Present Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי' שַׁמַּאי אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים דַּיָּין שְׁעָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Shammai **says:** For **all women** who do not have a fixed menstrual cycle, **their time is sufficient.**

The first *mishnah* opens with a ruling of *Shammai* himself — notably not *Beis Shammai*, indicating considerable antiquity — and the principle he articulates is *kol hanashim dayan sha'atan*: all women, their moment suffices. The *mishnah* is addressing the case of a woman who discovers she has bled, with the question being when her *tumah* takes effect retroactively. *Shammai* rules that we are concerned only from the moment of discovery onward; she is not *tamei* for any prior period.

### Tumah from Interior

The force of this ruling becomes clear once one appreciates the underlying principle: *isha mitama mibifnim kimibachutz* — a woman becomes *tamei* even while the blood is still interior to her body, before it has exited externally. This is not a minor technicality; it has sweeping implications. If a woman's *tumah* commences from the moment blood leaves the uterus and passes the cervix, regardless of whether it has yet exited the body, then the question of *when exactly* she became *tamei* is genuinely open. This is also the reason why, in a world of *tumah v'taharah*, the practice of internal *bedikos* developed — if *tumah* began only upon external blood flow, internal checking would be entirely unnecessary.

### Terumah Case Example

The practical stakes are illustrated by a concrete case: suppose a woman touched *terumah* in the morning and only later discovers she has had her period. Is that *terumah* now *tahor* or *tamei*? The question depends entirely on when her *tumah* commenced. *Shammai* says it commenced at the moment of discovery, so the *terumah* she touched earlier remains *tahor*. If she was a *bat kohen* or *eshet kohen* and inadvertently ate *terumah* — well, if it was not the time of month she expected her period, she had no reason to anticipate it and would be considered an *ones*; if it was during her expected *onah* or *veset*, the calculus changes, since she should have been more cautious.

### Chazakah Principle

The foundation of *Shammai*'s ruling, as the *Gemara* will elaborate, is the halachic principle of *chazakah*: she possessed a presumed status of *taharah*, and we maintain that status until we have definitive evidence to the contrary. Since we have no knowledge that she became *tamei* prior to the moment of discovery, the *chazakat taharah* carries her forward to that moment.

### Two Shammai Reasons

*Shammai* in fact operates on two related grounds. First is the *chazakah* just described. Second, *Shammai* is not concerned that blood might have been delayed in the internal passages for any significant time — he operates on the assumption that once blood begins to move, it exits promptly, and we need not posit lengthy internal delays. Both of these points will become subjects of detailed *Gemara* analysis.

## Hillel & Chachamim

### Hillel: Checking Window

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלֵּל אוֹמֵר: מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ לְיָמִים הַרְבֵּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Hillel says: From examination [*mipekida*] to examination,** i.e., she assumes ritual impurity status retroactive to the last time she examined herself and determined that she was ritually pure, **and** this is the *halakha* **even** if her examination took place **several days** earlier.

*Hillel* takes the opposite approach: *mipkuda lipkuda* — from checking to checking, and *afilu yamim harbeh*, even over many days. If she checked on Monday and confirmed she was *tahor*, then checked again on Thursday and found she had bled, she must be concerned that she was in fact *tamei* for the entire intervening period. Anything she touched during those days carries a status of *safek tamei*. There is no temporal cap; the retroactive window extends as far back as the last confirmed *bedikah*.

### Twice-Daily Checking

This ruling would of course have been wildly impractical — and practically devastating — if women were checking only infrequently. The *mishnah* therefore reflects a world in which women checked twice daily, morning and evening, every single day of the month, not merely around the time they expected their period. This practice arose because in a world of *tumah v'taharah*, the concern of having inadvertently touched *terumah* or *chullin b'taharah* in a state of *tumah* was ever-present. There was also the practice, as the *mishnah* mentions with the term *hameshameshet b'edim*, of checking before sexual relations — using the cloth that functions as an *ed*, a witness, testifying to the woman's status. The *Gemara* notes, however, a countervailing concern: if a woman checks immediately before relations, the man (*libo nokfo u'foresh*) becomes anxious and may refrain from relations, which is also problematic. As for whether this checking practice before relations applies today — in a world where the concern is the *isur* of sex rather than the world of *tumah v'taharah* — there is a *machlokes* among the *poskim*, and many *rishonim* indicate that such practices were calibrated to a world of continuous *tumah v'taharah* vigilance and do not apply in the same way today.

### 24-Hour Limit Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: לֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, וְלֹא כְּדִבְרֵי זֶה, אֶלָּא מֵעֵת לְעֵת מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה, וּמִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה מְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** The *halakha* is **neither in accordance with the statement of this** *tanna* **nor in accordance with the statement of that** *tanna*; **rather,** the principle is: A twenty-four-hour **period reduces** the time **from examination to examination.** In other words, if her final self-examination took place more than twenty-four hours earlier, she need only concern herself with ritual impurity for the twenty-four-hour period prior to discerning the blood. **And from examination to examination reduces** the time **from** a twenty-four-hour **period.** In other words, if she examined herself in the course of the previous day and discovered no blood, she was certainly ritually pure prior to the examination.

The *chachamim* chart a middle course: *lo k'divrei zeh v'lo k'divrei zeh*. They reject both *Shammai*'s leniency and *Hillel*'s potential extremity, replacing them with a dual limitation. A woman must be concerned retroactively for whichever is lesser: *me'et l'et* — the preceding twenty-four hours — or *mipekida l'fekida* — since the last checking. These two criteria limit one another. A woman need never be concerned beyond twenty-four hours, and if she checked within that twenty-four-hour window and was confirmed *tahor*, the retroactive concern extends only back to that check, not further.

One might ask — as was raised in the discussion — whether this twenty-four-hour limit is a formalistic legal rule or reflects a factual assumption about biological reality. *Shammai* and *Hillel* can each be explained in formal halachic terms: *Shammai* relies on *chazakat taharah*; *Hillel* applies the woman's current *tamei* status retroactively to the entire gap. But twenty-four hours is neither purely formal nor easily derived from a text. The *Gemara* itself seems to treat it as grounded in *metzius* — the reasonable assessment that a woman would not have bled more than twenty-four hours earlier without becoming aware of it. Once the underlying retroactive concern is rabbinic in origin, it is also possible that the rabbis simply imposed a practical temporal boundary, even if somewhat conventional. The *Gemara*'s own treatment of this question merits attention.

The phrase in the *mishnah*, *kol hanashim*, meaning all women regardless of category, is deliberate: *Shammai*'s leniency applies universally, while *Hillel* will begin to distinguish between women. Categories such as *mesulakot damim* — women for whom periods are ordinarily suspended, such as pregnant or nursing women — and women with a fixed *veset* will all potentially receive different treatment. *Kol hanashim* signals that for *Shammai*, no such distinctions are necessary.

### Veset & Regularity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל אִשָּׁה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָהּ וֶסֶת — דַּיָּה שְׁעָתָהּ, וְהַמְשַׁמֶּשֶׁת בְּעֵדִים — הֲרֵי זוֹ כִּפְקִידָה, וּמְמַעֶטֶת עַל יָד מֵעֵת לְעֵת וְעַל יָד מִפְּקִידָה לִפְקִידָה.
>
> **Translation:** For **any woman who has a fixed** menstrual **cycle [*veset*],** and she examined herself at that time and discovered blood, **her time is sufficient,** and it is only from that time that she transmits ritual impurity. **And** with regard to a woman **who engages in intercourse while** using **examination cloths [*be'edim*]** before and after intercourse, with which she ascertains whether her menstrual flow began, the halakhic status of such an action **is like that of an examination, and** therefore **it reduces** the time **from** a twenty-four-hour **period, and** reduces the time **from examination to examination.**

The *mishnah* proceeds to articulate an important leniency applicable even within *Hillel*'s framework and certainly within the *chachamim*'s: *kol isha she'yesh lah veset — dayah sha'atah*. Any woman who has a regular, predictable *veset* — a fixed menstrual cycle — her moment suffices. If a woman bleeds precisely when she expects to bleed, there is no reason to posit that it began at an earlier point. Even *Hillel* concedes this, since the regularity of the *veset* effectively answers the concern that animates his ruling.

One might probe how precise this leniency actually is. A woman establishes her *veset* through external observation of bleeding; what guarantees that the internal onset of *tumah* — which, recall, precedes external bleeding — did not actually begin earlier than she realized? If she established her *veset* only by observing when blood appeared externally, perhaps the internal process had always commenced somewhat earlier. This is a genuine question that would need to be explored in later *gemaros* dealing with the mechanics of establishing a *veset* through internal *bedikah*.

### Counting from Sighting

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאָמְרוּ, מְטַמְּאָה מֵעֵת לְעֵת, אֵינָהּ מוֹנָה אֶלָּא מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁרָאֲתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Although** the Rabbis **said** that a woman without a fixed menstrual cycle **transmits ritual impurity** retroactively **for** a twenty-four-hour **period,** a woman with a fixed cycle **counts** her menstrual days **only from the moment that she saw** blood.

A critical ruling concludes this section of the *mishnah*: *einah moneh ella mishe'ra'ata* — even though the *chachamim* declare a woman retroactively *tamei* for up to twenty-four hours before she actually saw blood, she counts her seven days of *niddah* only from the day she actually saw. This point is of enormous practical consequence. A woman who bled on Sunday counts Sunday as day one; the night after the seventh day — *Shabbos* night — she may go to the *mikvah*. One might think: since we are already treating her as *tamei* going back to *Shabbos*, she should begin her count from *Shabbos* and go to the *mikvah* a full day earlier. The answer is that would be a leniency the *mishnah* cannot permit. The retroactive *tumah* for the intervening period is a *safek* — a doubt — imposed rabbinically. But the seven-day count is a *d'oraisa* obligation, and permitting her to go to the *mikvah* earlier on the basis of that *safek* could result in a woman who is genuinely a *niddah de'oraisa* going to the *mikvah* before completing the full seven days. Therefore, the retroactive concern is imposed stringently for anything she may have touched or affected in the interim, while the count toward *taharah* begins only from the confirmed sighting. The principle is *machmir* in both directions simultaneously: the retroactive window imposes doubt on prior contacts, and the seven-day count is pushed forward to the day of definite sighting.

## Gemara: Chazakah Logic

### Shammai's Chazakah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּשַׁמַּאי? קָסָבַר: הַעֲמֵד אִשָּׁה עַל חֶזְקָתָהּ, וְאִשָּׁה בְּחֶזְקַת טְהוֹרָה עוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that when a woman discerns that her menstrual blood emerged, Shammai holds that her impurity status starts from that moment, while Hillel maintains that it begins from the time of her most recent self-examination. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Shammai?** The Gemara answers: Shammai **holds** that as there is a principle that when the status of an item is uncertain it retains its presumptive status, here too: **Establish** the **woman in her presumptive status, and a woman remains with the presumptive status of** being **ritually pure.**

The *Gemara* opens its analysis of the *mishnah* with the question: *mai ta'ama d'Shammai?* — what is *Shammai*'s reasoning? The answer is direct: *kasavar ha'amed isha al chazakatah* — establish the woman upon her *chazakah*. She stands with a presumed status of *taharah*, and we maintain that status until the last possible moment, until we have definitive evidence otherwise. *V'isha bechezkas tahara omedes* — a woman stands in a *chezkat taharah*.

### Hillel: Rei'usa Migufa

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלֵּל? כִּי אָמַר: הַעֲמֵד דָּבָר עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ — הֵיכָא דְּלֵית לֵיהּ רֵיעוּתָא מִגּוּפֵיהּ, אֲבָל אִיתְּתָא כֵּיוָן דְּמִגּוּפַהּ קָחָזְיָא — לָא אָמְרִינַן אוֹקְמַהּ אַחֶזְקָתַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** what is the reasoning of **Hillel?** He claims that **when** one **states** the principle: **Establish an item in its presumptive status,** that applies only **where there is no weakening** in the presumptive status that is a result **of** the item **itself. But** in the case of **a woman,** **since** it is her nature **to see** blood flow **from her body** at regular intervals, **we do not say: Establish her in her presumptive status** of ritual purity.

*Hillel*'s rejoinder, as the *Gemara* frames it, does not deny the general validity of *chazakah* but contests its application here. *Ki amar ha'amed davar al chazakaso* — when do we apply the principle of establishing something upon its *chazakah*? *Heicha d'leis lei re'usa migufei* — only when there is no *re'usa* emanating from the thing itself, no inherent reason to question the status from within. But here, *kivan d'migufa kachazya* — since she is seeing blood from her very body — *lo amrinan okmah a'chazkata* — we do not say to establish her upon her prior *chazakah*.

### Rei'usa Interpretation

What exactly does *re'usa migufa* mean? Two interpretations present themselves. The first, and perhaps the more elegant, is the concept of *chazakah ha'asuyah lehishtanot* — a presumptive status that is inherently subject to change. Ordinarily, *chazakah* functions as a reliable presumption precisely because the prior status was stable. But a woman's *taharah* status is not stable in this sense: by the nature of biology, it will regularly and predictably change. When a status quo is inherently transient and always on the verge of changing, it carries less force as a *chazakah*. This seems to be the preferred reading.

The second interpretation is somewhat more technical: there are in fact two competing *chazakot* here. There is a *chazakah de'me'ikara* — the prior presumption that she was *tahor* — pulling in one direction, and a *chazakah de'hashta* — her current status as *tamei* — pulling in the opposite direction. The prior status argues that we should project *taharah* forward and assume the change occurred only at the last moment; the current status argues that we should project *tumah* backward and assume the change occurred as early as possible. When *chazakot* compete in this fashion and press against each other, the *chazakat taharah* is weakened. Either framing — inherent instability of the status, or competing *chazakot* — explains *Hillel*'s position that the prior *chazakat taharah* does not simply carry the day.

## Mikvah Comparison

### Mikvah Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מִמִּקְוֶה? דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁנִּמְדַּד וְנִמְצָא חָסֵר — כׇּל טְהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּיו לְמַפְרֵעַ, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד — טְמֵאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinions of both Shammai and Hillel, the Gemara asks: **And** in **what** way **is** this case **different from** that of **a ritual bath? As we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 2:2): In the case of **a ritual bath** that was known to have contained the requisite forty *se'a*, **which was** then **measured and found lacking** in its quantity of water, **all pure items that had been rendered** pure **in it,** i.e., any impure items that had been purified through immersion in this ritual bath, **retroactive** to when the ritual bath was last measured, **whether** this ritual bath is found **in the private domain or in the public domain, are impure.**

The *Gemara* now introduces a parallel case: *u'mai shna mi-mikveh?* — how is this case different from the *mikvah*? The reference is to a ruling cited by the *Gemara*: if a *mikvah* was measured and found lacking — *mikvah she-nimdad ve-nimtza chaser* — and it had been used for *tevilah* since the last time it was confirmed to have adequate water, then *kol teharos she-na'asu al gabav lemafrea* — all *teharos* accomplished through immersion in it retroactively — *bein bi-reshut ha-rabim bein bi-reshut ha-yachid* — whether in the public or private domain — are *tamei*. Everything and everyone who relied on that *mikvah* during the intervening period is treated as having remained *tamei*.

### Safek vs. Vadai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְשַׁמַּאי קַשְׁיָא לְמַפְרֵעַ. לְהִלֵּל קַשְׁיָא וַדַּאי, דְּאִילּוּ מֵעֵת לְעֵת שֶׁבְּנִדָּה תּוֹלִין — לֹא אוֹכְלִין וְלֹא שׂוֹרְפִין, וְאִילּוּ הָכָא טוּמְאָה וַדַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **According to** the opinion of **Shammai,** the mishna poses **a difficulty from** its statement that the change in the ritual bath's status is assumed **retroactive** to the time when it was last measured, as he maintains that a menstruating woman's status changes only at the present moment, not retroactive to the last time she examined herself. **According to** the opinion of **Hillel,** the mishna also poses **a difficulty** with regard to its ruling that the items purified in the ritual bath are deemed impure with **certainty, whereas** a menstruating women does not assume retroactive impurity with certainty. In other words, although according to the Rabbis there is a twenty-four-hour **period** of retroactive ritual impurity in the case **of a menstruating woman,** and according to Hillel the retroactive impurity extends back to her most recent examination, nevertheless any *teruma* that she touched during that period is not deemed definitely impure to the extent that it must be burned. Rather, the impure status of the items she touched is uncertain and the status of the *teruma* is **suspended,** i.e., one may **neither eat** it **nor burn** it. **Whereas here,** in the case of the ritual bath, any item purified in it is retroactively deemed **definitely impure.**

This case presents a challenge on multiple levels simultaneously. According to *Shammai*, any retroactive *tumah* at all is problematic — yet here retroactive *tumah* is imposed. And according to *Hillel*, the retroactive concern for a *niddah* yields only a *safek tamei* — you suspend the *terumah*, you neither eat it nor burn it; it is not definitively *tamei*. But the *mikvah* case seems to declare everything definitively *tamei*, which is even more stringent than *Hillel*'s position. How is the *mikvah* case to be distinguished from the *niddah* case?

### Reshut Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הַעֲמֵד טָמֵא עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ, וְאֵימָא לֹא טָבַל. אַדְּרַבָּה, הַעֲמֵד מִקְוֶה עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ, וְאֵימָא לֹא חָסַר! הֲרֵי חָסֵר לְפָנֶיךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of a ritual bath, it is different, **since it can be said: Establish** the **impure** item **in its presumptive status and say** that **it was not** properly **immersed.** In other words, the presumptive status of the item as ritually impure is in keeping with the current deficient state of the ritual bath. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **On the contrary,** one should **establish the ritual bath in its presumptive state** of validity **and say that** the ritual bath **was not** previously **lacking** the requisite measure of water. The Gemara responds: There is no presumptive state of validity, as the ritual bath **is lacking before you,** i.e., at present, and this offsets the presumptive status that it was full.

The phrase *bein bi-reshut ha-rabim bein bi-reshut ha-yachid* — whether in the public or private domain — reflects a principle familiar from elsewhere in *halakha*: doubts of *tumah* arising in a *reshut ha-rabim* (public domain) are resolved leniently toward *taharah*, while those arising in a *reshut ha-yachid* (private domain) are resolved stringently toward *tumah*. This rule is derived from the laws of *sotah*: the *sotah* situation involves a woman who secluded herself with a man in a private setting, and from the language of *ve-nitma'ah* the rabbis extrapolate a general rule about how to treat doubts of *tumah* based on the domain in which they arise.

Critically, the *mikvah* case overrides this distinction: whether in the public or private domain, the retroactive *tumah* is imposed. This seems to indicate that the *mikvah* case is more severe than the ordinary *safek* framework would suggest. A large *Tosafos* at the beginning of the tractate raises the question of why the *niddah* case does not similarly discuss the public-private domain distinction. *Tosafos* offers several answers. An additional consideration is that *niddah* occupies a somewhat hybrid halachic status — at the intersection of classic *tumah v'taharah* and *isur arayot* — which may affect how it is categorized for these purposes.

### Status-Change Obstacle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, הֲרֵי דָּם לְפָנֶיךָ! הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דַּחֲזַאי. הָכָא נָמֵי, הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דַּחֲסַר!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **Here too,** in the case of a menstruating woman, she is one who has experienced a discharge of **blood before you,** i.e., at present. Just as the ritual bath's presumptive status of validity is offset by its present lack of the requisite measure of water, so too the woman's previous presumptive status of ritual purity is offset by her present state of impurity due to menstruation. The Gemara answers: In the case of the menstruating woman, it is possible that **it is** only **now that she saw** the first emission of her menstrual blood. The Gemara retorts: One can say the same with regard to the ritual bath: **Here too, it is** only **now that** it **became lacking** in the requisite measure of water.

The *Gemara* then introduces a biological observation to press the comparison further. One might try to argue that the *mikvah* case is worse because water is constantly draining — *chaseir ve-atei chaseir ve-atei* — the *mikvah* is always in the process of losing water, making its *chazakah* of completeness perpetually fragile. A woman, the argument would go, is not constantly bleeding a little at a time until it finally appears. Therefore her *chazakah* of *taharah* is more robust.

### Biological Reality

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: חֲסַר וַאֲתָא, חֲסַר וַאֲתָא. הָכָא מִי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: חֲזַאי וַאֲתָא, חֲזַאי וַאֲתָא? וּמַאי קוּשְׁיָא?! דִּלְמָא הַגַּס הַגַּס חֲזֵיתֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **How can** these cases **be compared? There,** in the case of the wine, **it can be said** that it was **continuously turning to vinegar.** Therefore, it is possible that the wine turned to vinegar much earlier without anyone realizing. By contrast, in the case of the menstruating woman, **can it be said** that she was **continuously seeing** menstrual blood? Once again, the Gemara responds: **And what is the difficulty?** The same possibility does in fact exist in the case of a menstruating woman, as **perhaps she saw** the blood depart **bit by bit.**

The *Gemara* challenges this: *dilma hagas hagas chaziteih* — perhaps the blood slowly accumulates and grows until she sees it. In other words, perhaps the biological process of menstruation, like the gradually draining of the *mikvah*, is in constant incremental progress. This generates genuine biological inquiry. The uterine lining builds up and then sloughs off; at what point in that process does one say that the woman is definitively in a transitional state? The *Gemara* is suggesting that the woman's internal process may be analogous to the continuously draining *mikvah* — both are *chazakot* that are *asuyos lehishtanot*, statuses inherently in the process of change. If so, what distinguishes them?

### Two Problems vs. One

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי לְרֵיעוּתָא, הָכָא אִיכָּא חֲדָא לְרֵיעוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states a different answer: **There,** in the case of the ritual bath, **there are two** factors **that weaken** the suggestion that the items are ritually pure: First, the ritual bath is lacking at present; second, the item has a presumptive status of impurity. **Here,** by contrast, **there is** only **one** factor **that weakens** the possibility that the items touched by the currently menstruating woman were rendered ritually impure, i.e., that she is currently experiencing a menstrual flow. Therefore, according to Shammai she assumes impurity status only from that moment onward, and according to Hillel her retroactive impurity status applies only as an uncertainty.

The *Gemara* arrives at its final and decisive answer: *hasam ika tarti lerei'usa, hacha ika chada lerei'usa* — there, by the *mikvah*, there are two problems; here, by the *niddah*, there is only one. By the *niddah*: one problem — she is now *tamei*, and we must determine the implications of that for the period before we knew. By the *mikvah*: two problems — first, the *mikvah* itself is now *chaser*, raising doubt about its prior validity; second, and independently, the person or vessel that immersed began with a definite *chezkat tumah* that can only be removed by a demonstrably valid *mikvah*. When both of these problems compound, the result is that the retroactive *tumah* of the *mikvah* case is imposed as a *vadai*, whereas in the *niddah* case, the single problem of her current *tumah* leaves the prior period in the realm of *safek* — according to *Hillel* a *safek* sufficient to impose practical stringencies, according to *Shammai* not even that given her *chazakat taharah*. This is a conceptually satisfying resolution that clarifies the underlying logic of both cases.

## Wine Barrel Analogy

### Barrel Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי שְׁנָא מֵחָבִית? דִּתְנַן: הָיָה בּוֹדֵק אֶת הֶחָבִית לִהְיוֹת מַפְרִישׁ עָלֶיהָ תְּרוּמָה וְהוֹלֵךְ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצָא חוֹמֶץ — כׇּל שְׁלֹשָׁה יָמִים (הָרִאשׁוֹנִים) וַדַּאי,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to Shammai, who holds that her time is sufficient and there is no retroactive impurity, **what is different** in the case **of a barrel? As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Terumot* 2:8): If someone **would inspect** the contents of **a barrel** to see if it still contained enough wine **to continually** mentally **separate *teruma* from it,** i.e., to exempt other untithed wine of his until all the wine in this barrel will be *teruma*, **and afterward** the contents of the barrel **were found** to have turned to **vinegar,** which cannot be set aside as *teruma* for untithed wine, then for **all of the first three days** following his most recent inspection, it is **definitely** considered to have been wine.

The *Gemara* introduces a second comparison: *mai shna me-chavis* — how is the *niddah* case different from the barrel case? The case is as follows: a person designated a particular barrel of wine as the source from which he would continually separate *terumah* — *haya bodek et ha-chavis l'hiyot mafrish aleha terumah v'holech*. He checked the wine, confirmed it was good, and on an ongoing basis designated portions within the barrel as *terumah* for his other wine. Then one day — *ve-acharkach nimtza chometz* — he discovered the wine had turned to vinegar. The problem: *terumah* must be wine taken on wine of the same *min*. If the barrel had already become vinegar when he was designating it as *terumah*, that *terumah* was either *terumah b'ta'us* or ineffective on account of the change in *min*, depending on the *rishonim*'s reading. When exactly did the wine turn?

### Three-Day Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ — סָפֵק. קַשְׁיָא לְשַׁמַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From that** point **forward,** i.e., more than three days after the most recent inspection, it is **uncertain** whether it had already turned to vinegar, and consequently any untithed wine for which *teruma* was separated during those days remains uncertainly tithed. This poses **a difficulty for Shammai,** who maintains that there is no such retroactive consideration in the case of a menstruating woman.

The *mishnah* rules: *kol gimmel yamim vadai* — the first three days are definite; *mikan v'eilach safek* — from that point onward, a doubt. There is a *machlokes* in the *Gemara* about which direction this cuts — whether the first three days after the last good check are definitely fine (the vinegar came only later) or the first three days before the discovery are definitely bad (the wine was already vinegar) — but either way, there is a substantial middle period of genuine *safek* during which the wine's status is uncertain.

### Parallel Structure

This presents a challenge for *Shammai*. The barrel case involves a prior *chazakah* of valid wine and a current reality of vinegar, with a questionable middle period — structurally identical to the *niddah* case. Yet the barrel case yields a *safek* that is not resolved in favor of the prior *chazakah* of good wine. If *Shammai* would say in the *niddah* case that the *chazakat taharah* carries the day, should he not similarly say in the barrel case that the *chazakah* of valid wine carries the day?

### Tevel Obstacle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר: הַעֲמֵד טֶבֶל עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ, וְאֵימַר לֹא נִתְקַן. אַדְּרַבָּה, הַעֲמֵד יַיִן עַל חֶזְקָתוֹ, וְאֵימַר לֹא הֶחְמִיץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **There,** in the case of the barrel, it is different, **since it can be said: Establish the untithed produce in its presumptive status,** as when it first grew it was definitely untithed, **and say** it was **not amended,** i.e., exempted, and its status continues to be untithed produce. The Gemara asks: **On the contrary,** one should **establish the wine in its presumptive status, and say** that it had **not turned to vinegar.**

The answer parallels the *mikvah* analysis: *hasam mishum d'ika lemeimar amed tevel al chazkaso v'eimar lo nitken* — there, you can argue: establish the *tevel* upon its *chazakah* and say it was never properly fixed. Just as with the *mikvah* case, the barrel is the mechanism intended to change another object's status. The farmer's other wine begins as definite *tevel* — improperly tithed produce that is prohibited for consumption. He needs the designated wine in the barrel to have been valid *terumah* at the moment of designation in order to rectify the *tevel*. If there is genuine doubt about whether the barrel wine was still valid wine at that moment — because it might already have been vinegar — then that doubt is insufficient to override the definite *chezkat tevel* of the other wine. *Shammai* would agree: as a standalone question about the barrel and its wine, the *chazakah* of good wine might have carried things forward; but when you need it to change the definite status of something else, the doubt is simply not enough.

### Safek/Vadai Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הֶחְמִיץ לְפָנֶיךָ! הָכָא נָמֵי, הֲרֵי דָּם לְפָנֶיךָ! הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דַּחֲזַאי. הָתָם נָמֵי, הַשְׁתָּא הוּא דְּהֶחְמִיץ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this suggestion: This is impossible, as **it is turned to vinegar before you,** i.e., at present. The Gemara replies: **Here too,** in the case of a menstruating woman, one who has experienced a discharge of **blood** is **before you,** at present, so what is the difference? The Gemara explains: With regard to the woman, it is possible that **it is** only **now that she saw** the beginning of her menstrual blood. The Gemara responds: **There too,** it is possible that **it is** only **now that it turned to vinegar,** but not earlier.

The *Gemara* then presses the comparison in the other direction: *u'rami chavis a'mikvah* — the barrel case and the *mikvah* case seem to conflict with each other. By the *mikvah*, the ruling was *vadai tamei* — the person who immersed is definitively *tamei* retroactively. But by the barrel, the ruling is only *safek*. If both cases involve the same structure — a prior *chazakah*, a current bad status, and the need to change another object's definite status — why does the *mikvah* case yield a *vadai* while the barrel case yields only a *safek*?

The *Gemara* works through this tension. The same back-and-forth that appeared in the *mikvah*-*niddah* comparison recurs: *ha'amed yayin al chazkaso v'eimar lo hachmitz* — establish the wine on its *chazakah* and say it did not become vinegar. But *harei hachmitz lefanecha* — it has become vinegar before you. *Hacha nami harei dam lefanecha* — and similarly by the *niddah*, you see blood before you. *Hashta hu dechazai* — perhaps she only just bled now. *Hasam nami hashta hu d'hachmitz* — perhaps the wine only just became vinegar now. *Hashta? Hashta?* — the *Gemara* presses: can you really say now? *Hasam ika lemeimar hachmitz v'asa hachmitz v'asa* — there, the wine is in a constant process of gradually turning to vinegar. *Hacha mi ika lemeimar chazai v'asi chazai v'asi* — here, can we say a woman is gradually in a constant process of bleeding, a little at a time? And the answer circles back: *dilma hagas hagas chazai* — perhaps the blood slowly increases until she sees it, which makes the cases fully parallel once again.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא? הָתָם אִיכָּא תַּרְתֵּי לְרֵיעוּתָא, הָכָא אִיכָּא חֲדָא לְרֵיעוּתָא. וּרְמִי חָבִית אַמִּקְוֶה: מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא וַדַּאי, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא סָפֵק? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מִסּוּרָא: מַאן תְּנָא חָבִית? רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דִּלְגַבֵּי מִקְוֶה נָמֵי סְפֵקָא מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides a different answer: **There,** in the case of the barrel, there are **two** factors **that weaken** the suggestion that the wine is tithed: First, it is vinegar at present; second, the wine has a presumptive status that it is untithed. **Here,** by contrast, **there is** only **one** factor **that weakens** the possibility that the items touched by the menstruating woman were rendered ritually impure, i.e., that she is currently experiencing a menstrual flow. **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction between** the *halakha* of the **barrel** and that **of** the **ritual bath: What is different here,** in the case of the ritual bath, where the items are **definitely** impure, **and what is different there,** with regard to the barrel, where it is only **uncertain** that the produce remains untithed? In both cases the current situation, that the ritual bath is deficient and the contents of the barrel have turned to vinegar, should lead to a definite status. The Gemara answers: **Rabbi Ḥanina of Sura says: Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** the *halakha* of the **barrel? It is Rabbi Shimon, who, with regard to a ritual bath also considers it** as a matter of **uncertainty,** rather than as definitely impure.

At this point the *Gemara* resolves the tension with a striking identification: *amar Rabbi Chanina mi-Sura: man tanna chavis — Rabbi Shimon hi*. Who is the author of that *mishnah* about the barrel that rules *safek*? It is *Rabbi Shimon*. And *Rabbi Shimon*, says the *Gemara*, would rule *safek* even in the *mikvah* case — *de-legabei mikvah nami safek meshavei lah*. The *Gemara* demonstrates this from a *baraisa*: regarding the *mikvah* measured and found lacking, the standard ruling — *bein bi-reshut ha-rabim bein bi-reshut ha-yachid, temei'os* — is that everything is definitively *tamei*. But *Rabbi Shimon omer*: in the *reshut ha-rabim* the *teharos* are *tehoros*, and in the *reshut ha-yachid* they are *tolin* — suspended in doubt, not definitively *tamei*. For *Rabbi Shimon*, even the *mikvah* case yields only a *safek*, not a *vadai*.

### Competing Chazakot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: מִקְוֶה שֶׁנִּמְדַּד וְנִמְצָא חָסֵר — כָּל הַטְּהָרוֹת שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ עַל גַּבָּיו לְמַפְרֵעַ, בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים בֵּין בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, טְמֵאוֹת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים טְהוֹרוֹת, בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד תּוֹלִין.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Mikvaot* 2:2): In the case of **a ritual bath** that was known to have contained the requisite forty *se'a*, **which was** then **measured and found lacking** in its quantity of water, **all pure items that had been rendered** pure **in it,** i.e., any impure items that had been purified through immersion in this ritual bath, **retroactive** to when the ritual bath was last measured, **whether** this ritual bath is located **in the private domain or in the public domain, are impure.** The mishna continues: **Rabbi Shimon says:** If the impure items were immersed in a ritual bath that was **in the public domain,** they are deemed **pure.** But if they were immersed in a ritual bath that was **in a private domain,** the status of any *teruma* that touched them is **suspended,** i.e., it is neither consumed nor burned.

What emerges from this entire discussion is a fundamental principle: when there are competing *chazakot* — a prior *chazakah* of one status and a current reality of a different status — and this uncertainty surrounds a mechanism intended to change the definite status of a third object (whether a person who immersed in the *mikvah* with a *chezkat tumah*, or other wine that is definite *tevel*), the question of whether that *safek* is sufficient to actually change the other object's definite status depends on whether one holds like *Rabbi Shimon* or like the *chachamim*. According to the *chachamim*, the doubt is not sufficient — the third object's definite status (*vadai tamei*, *vadai tevel*) prevails, and the retroactive *tumah* is imposed as a *vadai*. According to *Rabbi Shimon*, even this compound uncertainty yields only a *safek*, which in the *reshut ha-rabim* resolves to *taharah* and in the *reshut ha-yachid* to suspension rather than definitive *tumah*. The discussion will continue from there.
