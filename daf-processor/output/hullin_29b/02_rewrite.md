# Hullin 29b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shechita Timing

### Reish Lakish vs. R. Yoch…

We begin on 29b with one of the central conceptual questions of the entire masechta. The Gemara presents a foundational dispute about how to conceptualize the act of shechita as a process. Reish Lakish said in the name of Levi Sava: *eino li-shechita ela be-sof* — shechita occurs only at the end. The final cut or the final moment that completes the majority of the simanim is what constitutes the act of shechita. Rabbi Yochanan, by contrast, says *yeshno li-shechita mi-techilah ve-ad sof* — it spans from the very beginning to the end.

This is a genuinely interesting conceptual question. A process only finishes at the end, but that does not tell us whether we consider the entire process to be the act itself, or only its culminating moment. The logic strongly favors Rabbi Yochanan's position. If one said that shechita occurs only at the very end, one must grapple with an obvious problem: what happened before the end? By the time you reach the final cut, the animal is already a treifa — you have already severed part of its neck. Clearly something of significance occurred before the final moment. Furthermore, the Gemara elsewhere recognizes a pesul of shehiya — a pause during the cutting — which would be entirely inexplicable if nothing halakhically meaningful were happening until the last instant.

These are excellent questions, and the Gemara is aware of them. To make sense of the position that shechita is only at the end, one must reframe the debate. The question is not whether one is *in the process* of shechting — that everyone would agree is true from the beginning — but whether one has yet performed a *completed act* of shechita. According to the view that shechita is only at the end, one is in the process of shechting throughout, which is why the animal is not a treifa and why shehiya is a pesul, but one cannot yet say that shechita *has been performed* until the process is complete. According to Rabbi Yochanan, even at an intermediate stage one can already be said to have done shechita, even if incompletely. This framing makes the first position more internally coherent, though it admittedly makes the second position somewhat harder to understand.

### Rava's Agreement Cases

Rava begins by identifying scenarios where both opinions would agree on the outcome. *Ha-kol modim heicha di-shachat siman echad oved kochavim ve-siman echad Yisrael she-hi pesulah* — if a non-Jew cut the first siman and a Jew cut the second, everyone agrees it is invalid. This requires no appeal to the debate about when shechita occurs. Even if one says the act of shechita is only at the end, by the time the Jew arrives, the animal is already a treifa. The non-Jew's act rendered it a treifa; it is not the non-Jew's shechita that is the problem but the prior physical state of the animal.

Similarly, regarding an olas ha-of — a bird brought as an olah — if one cut one siman at the bottom of the altar and one siman at the top, and an olah bird must be performed at the top, everyone agrees it is pesulah. One might have thought: according to the view that shechita is only at the end, the act performed at the bottom does not yet count as shechita, so no violation has occurred there. But that reasoning fails. Even if the cut at the bottom is not yet a shechita act, it does constitute *ma'aseh chatas ha-of lemata* — it is an act equivalent to the shechita of a bird chatat performed at the bottom, which is precisely how a chatat bird is treated. By doing that at the bottom of the altar, one has treated the olah as a chatat, which everyone agrees is an invalidation. So both of these cases are not disputed regardless of which position one holds.

### Shechuting Outside

Where then is the practical difference between the two opinions? *Lo nechelku ela kegon she-shachat siman echad ba-chutz ve-siman echad bi-fnim* — the original framing of the debate is a case where one shechted one siman of a korban outside the Beit Hamikdash and completed the second siman inside. One who shechts a korban outside the Beit Hamikdash transgresses the prohibition of shochtei chutz. According to Rabbi Yochanan, who says the process begins at the beginning, one has already performed a shechita act outside — one is chayav. According to Reish Lakish, one has only begun a process; no shechita has yet been done, and one is not chayav.

However, Rava bar Shimi reports in the name of Rav Yosef that this framing is incorrect. *Heicha di-shachat siman echad ba-chutz ve-siman echad bi-fnim, nami pasul* — even according to the view that shechita is only at the end, everyone would agree that shechting one complete siman outside transgresses the prohibition, because cutting one siman is sufficient for a bird chatat, and thus it constitutes a complete act relative to that category of korban. Accordingly, the proper framing of the debate must involve a lesser act.

*Lo nechelku ela kegon she-shachat miyut simanim ba-chutz u-gmaro bi-fnim* — the debate applies where one shechted only a minority of a siman outside and completed the rest inside. According to the view that shechita begins at the beginning, even cutting 30 percent of the trachea outside counts as the beginning of a shechita act, and one is chayav. According to the view that shechita is only at the end, one has not yet performed shechita; one was merely in the middle of a process and is not chayav.

### Rav Yosef Refines

Rav Yosef's refinement thus limits the practical scope of the dispute. The debate only surfaces in a case of *miyut simanim* — a minority of the siman cut outside. Only there does the question of whether the process-beginning constitutes an act become determinative. A full siman cut outside generates liability regardless, because it independently constitutes a complete act for the category of chatat ha-of.

## Parah Adumah

### Parah Tumah Rules

Rabbi Zeira raises a challenge to the preceding debate from a Mishnah concerning the parah adumah. The Torah states that all who engage in the process of the parah adumah become tamei. The Mishnah teaches that this tumah — strong enough to cause one's clothing to become tamei — applies to anyone involved *mi-techilah ve-ad sof*, from the beginning of the process to the end. The first ritual act upon the parah adumah is the shechita; prior to that, everything is preparatory. Additionally, performing any other melacha simultaneously with any stage of the parah adumah process invalidates the parah entirely.

### Pesul During Shechita

The Mishnah then addresses cases of invalidity. If some form of pesul occurred during the shechita — either the shechita itself was performed incorrectly, rendering the animal a neveilah, or, according to Tosafot, a simultaneous melacha was performed — then both those involved before the pesul and those involved after the pesul do *not* become metamei begadim. The reasoning is: if the shechita is ruined, it was never a shechita at all; if it was never a shechita, the process of parah adumah never commenced; and if the process never commenced, no involvement in it can generate tumah.

### Pesul During Haza'ah

The rule differs when the invalidity occurs at the stage of haza'ah — the sprinkling of the blood of the parah. Here, the distinction is temporal: one who was involved before the pesul *does* become metamei begadim, because at the time of that involvement the process was still valid. One who becomes involved after the pesul does not become tamei. This contrast — full preclusion of tumah when shechita fails versus partial preclusion keyed to the timing of the pesul when haza'ah fails — sets up the Gemara's central question.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava poses a sharp question: *ve-lei aflog nami bi-shchitata* — if the shechita process begins at the beginning, why does the Mishnah not draw the same before/after distinction for the shechita stage that it draws for haza'ah? If shechita is a process that begins from the start, then someone involved before the pesul occurred during shechita should become tamei, and someone involved only after the pesul should not. Why does the Mishnah treat the shechita stage as a complete non-event for purposes of tumah?

### Rava's Response

Rava himself resolves the challenge: *niskalkela shechita ka'amrat? — shani hatam, d'igla milta le-mafrea d'lav shechita hi klal* — when the shechita itself is ruined, it has been retroactively revealed that no shechita ever took place. The key qualifier is that the position *yeshno li-shechita mi-techilah ve-ad sof* — that shechita begins at the beginning — only applies when the process is successfully completed. Once it is complete, we look back and say the process began from the first cut. But if the shechita ends in failure, there is retroactive revelation that nothing was ever being done. It was not a process of shechita in the first place; it was merely an attempt. Accordingly, no one involved in the failed shechita becomes tamei, and there is no before/after distinction to draw.

Rashi understands the pesul as referring to a ruined shechita that produced a neveilah. Tosafot takes the pesul as referring to a simultaneous melacha. For Rashi, the point is straightforward: since the shechita failed, we retroactively read back that there was never a shechita occurring. For Tosafot, the argument is slightly more nuanced: since the parah adumah process was not validly executed — because an extraneous melacha was performed — the process of *shechitat parah adumah* never commenced, even if the physical cutting was technically valid.

### Two People Shechting

Rava raises an additional question: given the debate between the two positions, why does the Mishnah on parah adumah not illustrate its point with a simpler case? Even in a fully valid, uninterrupted parah adumah shechita, one could distinguish when tumah attaches by positing two people shechting — one cutting the first siman and one cutting the second. According to the view that shechita is only at the end, only the second person would become tamei; the first would not. Why doesn't the Mishnah discuss this scenario to illustrate the principle?

Rav Yosef responds: *trei gavrei be-chad zivcha ka'amarta* — two people with one sacrifice? The verse *tizbach* — you shall slaughter — is read without the vav, in the singular, indicating that only one person may slecht a given sacrifice. Since the parah adumah has quasi-sacrificial status and is called a chatat, this rule applies to it as well. Accordingly, the scenario of two people shechting the parah adumah is impossible, and the Mishnah cannot use it.

### Abaye's Handkerchief

Abaye objects: this exclusion of two people from shechting a parah adumah is the view of Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Shimon, who is the stam author of that Mishnah, but the Chachamim permit two people to shecht one sacrifice. So the question stands: why not use the two-person scenario according to the Chachamim?

And, continues Abaye, even according to Rabbi Eliezer son of Rabbi Shimon who prohibits two people, one can construct an equivalent scenario with one person and two garments. *K'gon she-shachat chad gavrah bi-shnei sudarin* — if the shochet was wearing one garment at the beginning of shechita and a different garment at the end, the first garment was only present during the beginning of the process and not at its completion. According to the view that shechita is only at the end, only the final garment would become tamei. This scenario requires only one person and could have been stated by the Mishnah to illustrate the principle.

### Gemara Clarifies

The Gemara acknowledges the force of Abaye's question but explains why the Mishnah does not use such scenarios: *ela be-psulah de-para ka'amiri, bi-cheshirah lo ka'amiri* — the Mishnah was discussing cases where the parah adumah process was invalidated; it was not interested in exploring scenarios where the process was completed validly and the question is only which garment one was wearing. The structural focus of the Mishnah is on when invalidity disrupts the process and how that affects tumah. Exploring the nuances of which moment during a valid shechita generates tumah was simply not the Mishnah's concern. In principle, however, Abaye is correct: if shechita is only at the end, only the garment worn at the end would become tamei.

## Pesach Timing

### Pesach on Chol Hamoed

The Gemara introduces a new challenge from a passage concerning the korban pesach. The rule is well known from Zevachim: a korban pesach shechted not on the fourteenth of Nisan, and shechted with a different designation in mind, is reclassified as a shelamim. There is also a prohibition derived from the verse *lo tishchat al chameitz dam zivchi* — one may not slecht a sacrifice while one possesses chametz. This applies not only to the korban pesach itself but to any valid korban during Chag Hamatzos when chametz is present.

The Mishnah states: *u-ve-moed li-shmo patur, she-lo li-shmo chayav* — if on Chol Hamoed Pesach one slechts what was designated as a korban pesach *li-shmo*, with the intent to bring it as a pesach, one is exempt from the violation of slaughtering over chametz, because the sacrifice is invalid. If one slects it *she-lo li-shmo*, consciously redesignating it as a shelamim, it is a valid sacrifice and one is chayav.

### Auto Identity Change

The Gemara asks: the reason one is chayav when acting *she-lo li-shmo* implies that if one acts *stam*, with no particular intent, one would be patur — meaning the korban would still function as a pesach and be invalid. But does a pesach not automatically become a shelamim when brought at the wrong time? If so, even with no intent, it should be a valid shelamim and one should be chayav.

The inference is that a pesach outside its proper time does *not* automatically change identity; it requires conscious *akira* — an active uprooting of its original designation — in order to become a shelamim. Without such akira, it remains a pesach at the wrong time and is invalid.

### R. Chiya Context

Rabbi Chiya bar Gamda clarifies the context. This ruling was taught in a setting where the reason the pesach had not been brought on the fourteenth of Nisan was that the owners were temei meitim — ritually impure from contact with a corpse. They are therefore eligible to bring a pesach sheini, a second pesach on the fourteenth of Iyar. Since their animal is still earmarked for pesach sheini, its identity as a pesach remains active and compelling; it does not automatically shift to shelamim. Only through a deliberate act of redesignation would it become a shelamim. In a case where there is no prospect of pesach sheini — a pesach brought during the rest of the year with no upcoming opportunity — the animal automatically becomes a shelamim without any conscious akira.

## Pesach Mum

### Mum Changes Identity

The Gemara now applies the earlier debate about shechita timing directly to this case. *Ei amrat bishlama yeshno li-shechita mi-techilah ve-ad sof* — if shechita begins at the beginning, then from the very first moment one starts cutting the animal, one has begun a shechita act upon a pesach. It is already being shechted as a pesach at the wrong time, and it is immediately invalid. The framework is coherent.

But *ei amrat eino li-shechita ela be-sof* — if shechita is only at the end — then when one begins cutting the animal, one has not yet performed any shechita act. One has merely made a cut, which creates a mum. As soon as the animal has a mum, it can no longer be brought as a pesach sheini next month. As soon as it can no longer serve as a pesach sheini, why should it not automatically be redesignated as a shelamim — even without conscious akira — since its future as a pesach is foreclosed the instant one starts cutting? If so, by the time one finishes the shechita, the animal is already a shelamim, not a pesach, and one should be chayav even without any intent. The Mishnah's ruling that *stam* intent leads to exemption becomes inexplicable.

This is a penetrating question and highlights the recurring difficulty with the view that shechita is only at the end. As has been noted throughout this sugya, if nothing has been halakhically accomplished until the very last moment, then the earlier cuts must be characterized as mere physical damage — creating mum, making the animal a treifa — which would seem to have implications for the animal's status. Yet the proponents of *eino li-shechita ela be-sof* must somehow bracket those implications for most halakhic purposes while invoking them selectively. This tension runs through the entire discussion and is left without complete resolution.

### Pesach Category Remains

Abaye provides the response: *nehi d'id'chayei lah mi-pesach* — it is true that from the moment one begins cutting, the animal can no longer be brought as a pesach sheini in its current form. *Bi-dmei pesach mi id'chayei* — but it has not been removed from the *category* of pesach entirely. Even after acquiring a mum, the animal could be redeemed: its kedusha could be transferred to money, and that money could be used to purchase an animal for the pesach sheini. Therefore, the animal has not been fully uprooted from its pesach identity. It remains within the ambit of pesach, and without a conscious act of akira it will not become a shelamim.

One might object: once one has begun shechita, can the animal still be redeemed? The verse requires that the animal be stood before the kohen and evaluated while alive — *ha'amada ve-ha'aracha* — which would seem to foreclose redemption once shechita has begun. Abaye responds: the animal is not yet dead when shechita begins, and the Mishnah elsewhere teaches that even after two simanim have been cut, as long as the animal is still *mefarchesets* — convulsing, showing signs of life — it retains a degree of halakhic vitality. Therefore, theoretically, even during an ongoing shechita the possibility of redemption has not been entirely foreclosed, and the animal remains within the pesach category.

The broader discussion is admittedly frustrating at a conceptual level. If one defines the two positions as: (a) one is *in the process of shechita* from the beginning versus (b) the *act of shechita* is only complete at the end, then in some sense both positions ought to be simultaneously true — one is always in the process from the beginning, and the act is always complete at the end. The only meaningful debate would be whether being in-process is itself halakhically significant for specific purposes, such as triggering chiyuv for shochtei chutz or generating tumah in the context of parah adumah. That framing helps make both positions intelligible, but it does not fully eliminate the difficulties around why the animal is not a treifa or ba'al mum for most halakhic purposes even according to the view that nothing counts until the end.

## Multiple Locations

### Shechita in 2-3 Places

The Gemara introduces a new halacha: *ha-shochet bi-shnayim u-vi-shloshah mekomot, shechitato kesherah* — if one shechted in two or three places, the shechita is valid. The scenario envisioned is one where the knife did not trace a single continuous path across the siman but rather cut 40 percent at one location, then 20 percent at a different location, such that combined they constitute the required majority, yet no single contiguous cut covers the full majority. The question is whether this is valid or whether a single exposed, unbroken cut is required.

When this ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav was reported to Shmuel, he disagreed: *beinan shechita meforaat ve-leika* — we require *shechita meforaat*, an exposed cut, and this case lacks it. A valid shechita must produce a cut that is open and visible as a single line, comparable to the straight path of an arrow.

### Arrow Analogy

Reish Lakish provides the textual source for the requirement of shechita meforaat. The verse states *chetz shachut leshono mirma diber* — their tongue is like a sharpened arrow when they speak treachery. The same verb *shachut*, cutting, that appears in the verse describing an arrow's straight path, applies to shechita. Just as an arrow cuts in a straight, unimpeded line, so must shechita produce a straight, unimpeded cut.

### Two Knives Case

Rabbi Elazar challenges this requirement from a Mishnah: *bi-shnayim ochazim be-sakin ve-shochtim* — two people holding knives and shechting, *afilu echad mi-lema'ala ve-echad mi-lemata, shechita kesherah* — even if one is working from above and one from below, the shechita is valid. This appears to describe exactly the scenario of shechita in two separate locations, with each person contributing part of the cut at a different place on the siman. Why would that be valid if shechita meforaat is required?

### One Knife Two Handles

Rabbi Yirmiah resolves the apparent contradiction: *mishnatenu be-sakin echad u-shnei bnei adam* — our Mishnah is not describing two separate knives cutting at separate locations. It describes two people holding the two handles of a single knife, operating it like a two-handled saw. In this configuration, the cut is indeed a single path — it is just being drawn by two sets of hands from opposite ends. The requirement of shechita meforaat is therefore not violated.

### Angled Cutting

Rabbi Avin raises a further question. A Mishnah states that if one shechted the veshet below and the kanah above, or the veshet above and the kanah below, the shechita is valid. This describes cutting at an angle — the blade passes through the siman diagonally such that the cut across the veshet is at a different vertical point than the cut across the kanah. Is this not a case of shechita in two separate locations? If shechita meforaat is required, how can an angled cut qualify?

### Quill Edge

Rabbi Avin himself answers the question: *bi-shechita de-siki de-kumsa* — it is like the edge of a quill. The cutting edge of a quill feather is not perpendicular but diagonal — it forms a flat, angled plane. An angled cut of the veshet and kanah similarly produces a single exposed plane of cutting, visible in its entirety when the beit ha-shechitah is opened. This is not a case of the knife jerking to a different location; it is a single diagonal stroke. The requirement of shechita meforaat is satisfied because the entire cut is visible and continuous, even if not perpendicular. What remains genuinely unresolved is whether a knife that truly does jerk — cutting in one location and then an entirely separate location — would be valid. That is the underlying question the sugya has been circling.

### Cow Story

A practical story is brought to illustrate how this was handled in practice. A cow was shechted in two or three separate locations — presumably exactly the case of genuinely discontinuous cuts on the siman. The matter was brought before Rav Yosef bar Shmuel bar Marta. He proceeded to take the choicest portions of the meat for himself — a pointed practical ruling that the shechita was entirely kosher and required no further deliberation.

### Rav Zeira Infers

Rav Zeira draws an inference from this ruling: *limadatenu rabbeinu, mishnatenu bi-shnei sakinim u-vi-shnei bnei adam* — from Rav Yosef bar Shmuel bar Marta's action, one can infer that the Mishnah describing two people shechting should indeed be understood as referring to two separate knives cutting at two separate locations on the siman, not merely two handles on one knife. His lenient ruling on the cow with multiple cuts supports the reading that genuinely discontinuous shechita in two locations is valid.

The overall ruling of this passage appears to be lenient: shechita meforaat as an absolute requirement for a single unbroken line may not be as strict as Shmuel suggested, and shechita in two or three locations can be valid. The rishonim debate the precise parameters of this case, and the practical ruling is also disputed, though the Gemara's trajectory is clearly toward leniency.

## Chaldah

### Chaldah Definition

The Gemara introduces the pesul of chaldah — burrowing. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *hechlid et ha-sakin bein siman le-siman u-fesako, pesulah* — if one inserted the knife between the two simanim and then cut through them, it is invalid. The proper method of shechita requires cutting from the outside inward; one may not begin in the interior, between the simanim and the spinal cord, and then work outward. This is why, as discussed earlier in the masechta, the front edge of a shechita knife is flat rather than pointed — so that it cannot burrow through and pierce from the middle.

### Tachat Ha-Or Valid

An important distinction is immediately introduced: *tachat ha-or, kesherah* — if one inserted the knife not between the simanim themselves but between the simanim and the outer skin, and then proceeded to cut the simanim in the normal outward motion, this is valid. Inserting the knife beneath the skin is not considered chaldah, because the burrowing that is prohibited is specifically entering the interior space between the simanim and the spinal cord. Beginning beneath the skin while still cutting from the outside toward the simanim and beyond does not constitute the prohibited form of burrowing.

### R. Yeshevav vs. Akiva

The Gemara notes that there is already a Mishnah addressing a related case: *o she-hechlid et ha-sakin tachat ha-sheni u-pesakah* — if one inserted the knife above the second siman and then cut downward. Rabbi Yeshevav says this produces a neveilah; Rabbi Akiva says it is a treifa. The distinction between neveilah and treifa here will be addressed elsewhere in the masechta.

### Cutting Direction

The Gemara clarifies what the new ruling adds beyond the Mishnah. One might have thought that the problem of burrowing above the second siman is specifically because one is cutting in the wrong direction — moving away from the spinal cord rather than toward it, which is the natural direction of shechita. Perhaps burrowing is only a pesul when one cuts in the wrong direction. If so, inserting the knife between the simanim but then cutting toward the spinal cord — the correct direction — might be valid.

The ruling of Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav teaches that even burrowing in the correct direction is invalid. Even if one inserts the knife between the two simanim and cuts in the direction of the spinal cord — the correct shechita motion — this is still chaldah and the shechita is pesulah.

### Cloth Wool Cases

The passage then raises a series of unresolved questions. The school of Rav held that *tachat ha-or eini yodea* — they were uncertain whether inserting the knife beneath the outer skin, as described above, is in fact valid or not. And building on this uncertainty, the Gemara asks two further questions that remain in teiku. *Tachat ha-matlit mahu* — what if there was a cloth wrapped around the neck of the animal and one inserted the knife beneath the cloth? The cloth is not part of the animal at all, yet if beginning beneath even the skin of the animal is already suspect, perhaps beginning beneath an external cloth is equally or more problematic.

*O tachat tzemer mesubach mahu* — or what if it is a sheep whose wool covers the area of shechita, and one begins cutting beneath the wool? The wool at least belongs to the sheep and grows from its body, so it is not as external as a cloth, but it is also not the skin proper. Both questions — burrowing under cloth and burrowing under wool — remain unresolved.

The underlying structure of the pesul of chaldah is now clear. What is prohibited is beginning the cutting from the interior of the neck — between the simanim and the spinal cord — and working outward. This is chaldah regardless of direction. What is permitted is beginning from outside the neck entirely and cutting inward in the normal fashion. The gray area, which the Gemara leaves undecided, is how far "outside" one must truly begin: does the very outer edge of the skin suffice, or must one begin even before the skin? And if one begins beneath a cloth or beneath the wool of the sheep, has one begun "outside" in the required sense? These questions are left for further analysis.
