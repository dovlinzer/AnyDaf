# Shevuot 37 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shevuat HaPikadon Intro

### Definition & Nature

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן נוֹהֶגֶת בַּאֲנָשִׁים וּבְנָשִׁים, בִּרְחוֹקִים וּבִקְרוֹבִים, בִּכְשֵׁרִים וּבִפְסוּלִים;
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who takes a false oath denying that he is in possession of an item that another deposited with him is liable to return the item with an additional one-fifth of its value and to bring a guilt-offering (see Leviticus 5:20–26). The *halakhot* of **an oath on a deposit apply to men and to women, to non-relatives and to relatives,** i.e., even if the owner of the deposit and the purported bailee are related, **to** those **fit** to serve as witnesses **and to** those **disqualified** from doing so.

The daf opens with the beginning of a new *perek* devoted to *shevuat hapikadon*, the false oath concerning entrusted or owed property. This oath is distinct from *shevuat ha'edut* — the oath of testimony — which occupied the preceding chapters. Whereas *shevuat ha'edut* arises when a person is asked to testify and falsely denies knowing relevant testimony, *shevuat hapikadon* is described directly in the Torah as an oath one takes oneself, denying that another person's object is in one's possession or that one owes another a debt. The Torah characterizes this transgression in strikingly strong terms: *ma'al ma'al bashem* — a trespass against God — using the very language of *me'ilah*, misappropriation of sacred property. Accordingly, the consequence mirrors that of *me'ilah*: one brings a *korban asham* and pays a *chomesh*, an additional fifth. Notably, it is not a *korban oleh veyored* — a variable offering scaled to the transgressor's means — but a fixed *korban asham* for all. What is also distinctive is that the Torah frames this false oath more explicitly than other false *shevuot* as a *ma'al bashem*, a trespass against the Divine, which explains why both the terminology and the consequences draw from the laws of *me'ilah*.

### Mishnah Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וְשֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין – מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ; וּמִפִּי אֲחֵרִים – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיִּכְפְּרֶנּוּ בְּבֵית דִּין. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין מִפִּי עַצְמוֹ בֵּין מִפִּי אֲחֵרִים – כֵּיוָן שֶׁכָּפַר בּוֹ חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** These *halakhot* apply when the oath is taken **in the presence of a court and** when taken **not in the presence of a court,** as long as the oath is taken **on his own,** i.e., stated by the defendant himself. **But** if the oath is administered **by others,** he is **not liable unless he denies** the claim **in court;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** **And the Rabbis say: Both** when the defendant takes an oath **on his own and** when the oath is administered **by others, once** he has falsely **denied** the claim against him, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering and to pay restitution and an additional one-fifth, even if the oath was not administered in the presence of a court.

The Mishnah opens by enumerating the broad applicability of *shevuat hapikadon*: it applies to men and women (*noheget be-anashim u-be-nashim*), to relatives and non-relatives (*be-rechokim u-be-krovim*), and to those eligible and ineligible to testify (*be-k'sherim u-be-psulim*). This is a significant contrast to *shevuat ha'edut*, which is intrinsically linked to the capacity to testify and carries its own set of eligibility requirements. Here, since the oath concerns a denial of property rather than a denial of testimonial knowledge, none of those testimonial restrictions apply. Likewise, the oath applies whether taken in the presence of a *beit din* or outside it (*bifnei beit din ve-shelo bifnei beit din*).

### Imposed vs. Self Oath

The Mishnah further distinguishes between an oath initiated by the denier himself (*mipi atzmo*) and one imposed upon him by another (*mipi acherim*). The Torah's primary formulation describes the case where the denier himself invokes God's name in denial. The category of *mipi acherim* — where another party imposes the oath and the denier responds — is derived from the laws of *shevuat ha'edut*. This creates a point of legal tension, since *shevuat ha'edut* required denial specifically in the presence of *beit din*.

Rabbi Meir takes the position of *don mina u-mina* — that when we derive one law from another, we import the contextual requirements along with the law itself. Therefore, when the oath is imposed upon the denier (*mipi acherim*), just as in *shevuat ha'edut* the denial had to occur before *beit din*, so too here the denial must occur before *beit din* to generate liability. When the denier initiates the oath himself, however, liability obtains even outside *beit din*. The Mishnah specifies that the category of *mushba mipi acherim* in which liability requires *beit din* refers to the case where someone declares *mashpia ani alecha* and the other simply asserts "I don't have it" — not saying *amen*, which would convert it into a *mipi atzmo* oath. Saying *amen* to an imposed oath is equivalent to initiating the oath oneself, and therefore generates liability even outside *beit din*.

The *chachamim*, by contrast, apply *don mina ve-okimeh be-asrah* — they derive the rule of *mipi acherim* from *shevuat ha'edut* but transpose it into the native context of *shevuat hapikadon*, which has no inherent requirement of *beit din*. Therefore, whether the oath is self-initiated or imposed by another, once denial has occurred the person is liable regardless of location.

### Intent & Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַיָּיב עַל זְדוֹן הַשְּׁבוּעָה, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ עִם זְדוֹן הַפִּקָּדוֹן; וְאֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ גְּרֵידְתָּא. וּמָה חַיָּיב עַל זְדוֹנָהּ? אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one is **liable** to bring an offering **for intentionally** taking a false **oath** on a deposit **and for unwittingly** taking a false oath about the **intentional** misappropriation of **the deposit,** i.e., if one knowingly took a false oath but was unaware that he is liable to bring an offering for taking the oath. **But he is not liable for unwittingly** taking a false oath **by itself,** where he mistakenly thought that he did not owe anything. **And what is he liable for** when he **intentionally** takes a false oath? He must bring **a guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels.**

The Mishnah then addresses the interplay of willful and unknowing violation. One is liable for *shevuat hapikadon* both when one willfully violates it (*zadon ha-shvua*) and when one is unknowing with respect to the oath's consequences (*shegagatah*), provided in both cases that one is willfully lying about the underlying fact (*zadon ha-pikadon*). The denier must, in all scenarios, be knowingly lying; one who genuinely believes one is telling the truth is considered an *oness* — coerced by circumstance — and is entirely exempt. A pure *shogeg*, meaning one who does not know that taking a false oath is prohibited, or alternatively one who mistakenly believes one is telling the truth, is exempt from bringing a *korban*. Rashi addresses the *shogeg* case: one possibility is that the person believes he is telling the truth, though Rashi himself notes that this is more properly an *oness*; another possibility is that the person knows he is lying but genuinely believes there is no prohibition at all involved, which is the more technically precise *shogeg* scenario.

### Korban Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: "תֵּן לִי פִּקְדוֹנִי שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״; אוֹ שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: ״אֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״, ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – הֲרֵי זֶה חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: **What** is the case of **an oath on a deposit?** It is where the claimant **said to** the defendant: **Give me my deposit, which is in your possession,** and the defendant replied: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession; or** the defendant **said to him: Nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** the claimant responded: **I administer an oath to you, and** the defendant **said: Amen.** In either case **this** defendant is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering if he lied.

When one does bear liability, what does one bring? An *asham b'kesef shkalim* — an *asham* worth at minimum two *shekel*, not an *oleh veyored*, which varies in cost depending on the transgressor's financial means. This fixed minimum is derived from *asham me'ilot*, the *asham* brought for *me'ilah*, from which all other *ashamim* are learned. The requirement of a costly animal offering, regardless of one's wealth, is itself one of the features that distinguishes this *shevuah* from others.

## Multiple Oaths Analysis

### Five Impositions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁבִּיעַ עָלָיו חָמֵשׁ פְּעָמִים, בֵּין בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין וּבֵין שֶׁלֹּא בִּפְנֵי בֵּית דִּין, וְכָפַר – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: מָה טַעַם? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּכוֹל לַחֲזוֹר וּלְהוֹדוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** If the claimant **administered an oath to him five times, whether in the presence of a court or not in the presence of a court, and** the defendant falsely **denied** each claim, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering **for each and every** denial. **Rabbi Shimon said: What is the reason?** It is **due to** the fact **that he is able to retract and confess** after each oath and repay the claimant. Since he did not do so, each oath is considered a separate denial of a monetary claim.

The Mishnah proceeds to analyze scenarios involving multiple oaths or multiple claimants, asking when separate *korbanos* are incurred. The first scenario: one party imposed a *shevuah* on the denier five separate times — *hashpia alav chamesh pe'amim* — whether in the presence of *beit din* or not, and each time the denier denied. The ruling is that he is liable for each instance separately (*chayav al kol achat ve-achat*).

This is contrasted with *shevuat ha'edut*, where a witness who denied five times before *beit din* brings only one *korban*. The reason for the difference is that in *shevuat ha'edut*, once a witness has denied testimony before *beit din*, he cannot retract — *keivan she-he'id shuv eino chozer u-magid*. Therefore, subsequent denials add nothing. But in *shevuat hapikadon*, the denier is a litigant (*nito'an*), not a witness, and can at any moment go back and admit — *she-yachol lachzor u-l'hodot*. Since each denial forecloses a fresh opportunity to come clean, each instance is independently actionable. This applies even when the denial occurs before *beit din*, since even there the denier retains the option to admit. Rabbi Shimon articulates this reasoning explicitly: it is the ongoing capacity to repent and acknowledge that makes each successive denial a distinct transgression.

### Co-owners Scenario

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ חֲמִשָּׁה תּוֹבְעִים אוֹתוֹ – אָמְרוּ לוֹ: ״תֵּן לָנוּ פִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ לָנוּ בְּיָדְךָ״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לָכֶם בְּיָדִי״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת. ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי, וְלֹא לְךָ וְלֹא לָךְ״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר שְׁבוּעָה בָּאַחֲרוֹנָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר שְׁבוּעָה לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** If **five** people **were suing him** and **they said to him: Give us back our deposit that is in your possession,** and the defendant says: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** he is **liable** for **only one** false oath. But if he responds to each claimant: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession, and nothing** of **yours, and nothing** of **yours,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim that he denied. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** He is not liable for his oath concerning each claim **unless he says:** On my **oath, at the end** of the denial, i.e., he says: Nothing of yours is in my possession, and nothing of yours, on my oath, so that it is clear that he is taking an oath to each one. **Rabbi Shimon says:** He is not liable for his oath concerning each claim **unless he says:** On my **oath, to each and every** claimant, i.e., he says: On my oath nothing of yours is in my possession, and on my oath nothing of yours, to each claimant separately.

A second scenario involves five co-owners who together demand the return of an object they jointly entrusted: *hayu chamishah tov'im oto* — five people demanding property they hold in partnership. They approach the denier together and say: give us the object we entrusted to you as partners. If he responds with a single oath — *shevuah she-ein lachem b'yadi* — swearing globally that he holds nothing of theirs, he incurs only a single *korban*, since there was one oath and one consolidated claim.

However, if he responds by separately identifying each co-owner — *lo lecha v'lo lecha v'lo lecha* — pointing to each and denying that individual's claim, he is liable for each denial separately. Rabbi Shimon disagrees and holds that for these to count as separate *shevuot* regarding individual co-owners' shares of a jointly held object, the denier must say the word *shevuah* at the conclusion — *ad she-yomar shevuah b'achronah*. Without that terminal invocation, even though he distinguishes between the parties, Rabbi Shimon does not regard these as formally distinct *shevuot*.

### Multiple Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תֵּן לִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד גָּזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי פִּקָּדוֹן וּתְשׂוּמֶת יָד וְגָזֵל וַאֲבֵידָה״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the claimant said: **Give me** back **my deposit, and pledge, stolen item, and lost item that are in your possession,** and the defendant responds: On my **oath nothing** of **yours** is **in my possession,** he is **liable** for **only one** false oath. But if he responds: On my **oath I do not have in my possession your deposit, or pledge, stolen item, or lost item,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim.

A third scenario: the claimant demands multiple distinct types of property — a *pikadon* (entrusted object), a *teshumas yad* (property misused while in one's care), *gazel* (stolen property), and an *aveida* (lost item that was not returned) — all belonging to him and presently in the possession of the denier. If the denier swears simply *shevuah she-ein lecha b'yadi*, without specifying the individual items, he incurs only a single *korban*. But if he enumerates each category specifically — *shevuah she-ein lecha b'yadi pikadon u-teshumas yad v-gazel va-aveida* — then he is liable separately for each, and here even Rabbi Shimon agrees that one need not repeat the word *shevuah* for each item, since these are genuinely distinct debts arising from distinct transactions.

### Single Transaction Grains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״תֵּן לִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין שֶׁיֵּשׁ לִי בְּיָדְךָ״; ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי״ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב אֶלָּא אַחַת, ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁאֵין לְךָ בְּיָדִי חִטִּין וּשְׂעוֹרִין וְכוּסְּמִין״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אָמַר ״חִטָּה וּשְׂעוֹרָה וְכוּסֶּמֶת״ – חַיָּיב עַל כׇּל אַחַת וְאַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the claimant said: **Give me** back **my wheat, and barley, and spelt that are in your possession,** if the defendant responds: On my **oath nothing of yours** is **in my possession,** he is **liable** for **only one** false oath. But if he responds: On my **oath I do not have in my possession your wheat, barley, or spelt,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim. **Rabbi Meir says: Even** if the defendant **says:** On my oath I do not have in my possession your **grain of wheat, or grain of barley, or grain of spelt,** he is **liable for** his oath concerning **each and every** claim.

The fourth scenario addresses a single transaction involving multiple types of grain. One person gave another a consignment containing wheat (*chitin*), barley (*seorin*), and spelt (*kusmin*), and now demands them back. If the denier swears *shevuah she-ein lecha b'yadi* without specifying, he is liable for only one *korban*. If, however, he enumerates: *shevuah she-ein lecha b'yadi chitin u-seorin v-kusmin*, then he is liable separately for each type.

Rabbi Meir adds that even if he identified each grain type individually, the principle is the same: *chayav al kol achat ve-achat*. The force of his statement is not entirely clear in the Mishnah's formulation, but the general principle is established: identification of distinct objects within a single transaction generates independent liability for each. The pattern across all four scenarios is consistent with the structure we encountered in *shevuat ha'edut* — specificity of denial is what multiplies the exposure to *korbanos*.

## Injury & Penalty Claims

### Rape/Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אָנַסְתָּ וּפִיתִּיתָ אֶת בִּתִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא אָנַסְתִּי וְלֹא פִּיתִּיתִי״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר: ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן פּוֹטֵר, שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם קְנָס עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ, מְשַׁלֵּם בּשֶׁת וּפְגָם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If one accuses another: **You raped or you seduced my daughter, and** the other **says: I did not rape and I did not seduce** your daughter, to which the father replied: **I administer an oath to you, and** the defendant **said: Amen,** the defendant is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering if it is a false oath. **Rabbi Shimon deems** him **exempt, since one does not pay a fine based on his own** admission. Had he confessed he would have been exempt from paying the fine; he is therefore not liable for his denial. The Rabbis **said to him: Even though he does not pay** the **fine based on his own** admission, **he does pay** compensation for **humiliation and** compensation for **degradation** resulting from her being raped or seduced, which are monetary claims and not fines, **based on his own** admission. He is therefore liable for a false oath, as he denied a monetary claim.

The Mishnah now turns to claims involving bodily injury or monetary penalties (*knas*) — categories that intersect with *shevuat hapikadon* in complex ways. The first case: a father says to a man, *anasta u-fitita et biti* — you raped or seduced my daughter — and the accused responds with a denial under oath.

Rabbi Shimon exempts him from the *korban* (*Rabbi Shimon poter*). His reasoning: *she-eino meshallem knas al pi atzmo* — normally one does not pay a *knas* if one admits to it (*modeh b'knas* is exempt). Therefore, even if the denier were to admit the act, he would not be obligated to pay the fifty-*shekel* *knas* prescribed by the Torah. Since the denial does not effectively keep a *mammon* — a compensatory monetary obligation — away from the claimant, Rabbi Shimon holds that it is not the kind of denial that triggers *shevuat hapikadon*.

The *chachamim* respond: even though he cannot be compelled to pay the *knas* upon admission, the claim of rape or seduction is not exclusively a claim of *knas*. It also encompasses *boshet* (embarrassment), *pegam* (the diminution of the daughter's value), and other forms of direct compensation that constitute genuine *mammon* and that he would be obligated to pay were he to admit. Since those *mammon* elements are embedded within the same claim, the denial under oath constitutes a denial of *mammon*, and he is therefore liable.

### Theft of Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״גָּנַבְתָּ אֶת שׁוֹרִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא גָּנַבְתִּי״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. ״גָּנַבְתִּי, אֲבָל לֹא טָבַחְתִּי וְלֹא מָכַרְתִּי״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, in a case where one person accuses another: **You stole my ox, and the** defendant **says: I did not steal** your ox, if the claimant replied: **I administer an oath to you, and** the defendant **said: Amen,** he is **liable** to pay for the ox due to the theft and to bring a guilt-offering if he lied, since by his oath he is denying that he owes the value of the ox that he would have to pay if he admitted to stealing it. But in a case where the claimant accuses the defendant of stealing the ox and slaughtering or selling it, and the defendant says: **I stole** the ox, **but I did not slaughter or sell** it, and this is a lie, if the claimant replied: **I administer an oath to you, and he said: Amen,** then the defendant is **exempt** from the fivefold payment for slaughtering or selling another's ox, since it is a fine.

The next case: *ganavta et shori* — you stole my ox — and the accused denies it under oath. He is liable, since the basic monetary obligation to return the value of the ox (*keren*) is *mammon* and would be payable upon admission. But consider a refinement: the claimant asserts both theft and slaughter or sale — triggering the Torahitic penalty of four- or fivefold repayment. If the accused admits to the theft but denies the slaughter or sale, his admission to theft establishes the *keren* obligation, and the only remaining dispute concerns the *knas* of *kefel* or *arba'ah va-chamishah*. In that case, everyone agrees he is exempt from the *korban*, since what he is denying is exclusively a *knas* — and even were he to admit, he would be exempt.

### Ox Damages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרְךָ אֶת שׁוֹרִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא הֵמִית״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. ״הֵמִית שׁוֹרְךָ אֶת עַבְדִּי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר ״לֹא הֵמִית״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If the claimant says: **Your ox killed my ox, and** the defendant lies and **says: It did not kill** your ox, to which the claimant replied: **I administer an oath to you, and he said: Amen,** then he is **liable** for his false oath. But if the claimant says: **Your ox killed my** Canaanite **slave** and you are therefore liable to pay me a fine of thirty shekels, **and he** lies and **says:** It **did not kill** your slave, to which the claimant replied: **I administer an oath to you, and he said: Amen,** then he is **exempt,** because payment for the slave is a fine.

The principle extends to damage caused by one's ox. If one's ox killed another's ox, and the owner denies it, he is liable — this is pure *mammon*, half-damages (*chatzi nezek*) assessed on the basis of the goring ox's value, which is a standard civil obligation. But if one's ox killed a slave and the owner denies it, the fixed thirty-*shekel* payment is considered a *knas* in character — it is not proportional compensation but a flat Torah-mandated sum. Therefore, even were the owner to admit, he would be exempt from paying it as *knas*, and accordingly his denial under oath does not obligate him to bring a *korban*.

### Wounding Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: ״חָבַלְתָּ בִּי וְעָשִׂיתָ בִּי חַבּוּרָה״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא חָבַלְתִּי וְלֹא עָשִׂיתִי בְּךָ חַבּוּרָה״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – חַיָּיב. אָמַר לוֹ עַבְדּוֹ: ״הִפַּלְתָּ אֶת שִׁינִּי וְסִימִּיתָ אֶת עֵינִי״, וְהוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא הִפַּלְתִּי וְלֹא סִימִּיתִי״; ״מַשְׁבִּיעֲךָ אֲנִי״, וְאָמַר ״אָמֵן״ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** If the claimant **said to him: You injured me and caused me a wound, and** the defendant **says: I did not injure** you **and I did not cause you a wound,** to which the claimant replies: **I administer an oath to you, and he said: Amen,** he is **liable.** But if **one's** Canaanite **slave said to him: You knocked out my tooth, or: You blinded my eye,** and you are therefore required to emancipate me, **and he says: I did not knock out** your tooth, **or: I did not blind** your eye, to which the slave replies: **I administer an oath to you, and he said: Amen,** he is **exempt** from bringing a guilt-offering even though he lied, since the obligation to emancipate one's slave in these cases is a penalty.

Similarly, if one person wounded another (*chavalta bi*) and the claim is denied under oath, the denier is liable, since the payments for bodily harm — pain, medical costs, lost work, humiliation — include genuine *mammon* components that the denier would be obligated to pay upon admission. However, if a slave claims to his master that the master knocked out his tooth or blinded his eye (*hipalta et shini v-simita et eini*), entitling the slave to his freedom, this is treated as a *knas* in character. The consequence — freedom — is uniform regardless of the severity of the injury, making it analogous in structure to a *knas* rather than proportional *mammon* compensation. Were the master to admit, he would not pay in the ordinary monetary sense; the slave would simply go free. Accordingly, denial of this claim does not constitute denial of *mammon* and does not obligate the master in *shevuat hapikadon*.

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל הַמְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – חַיָּיב, וְשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם עַל פִּי עַצְמוֹ – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **This is the principle:** For **any** claim that the defendant would have to **pay based on his own** admission, he is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering for taking a false oath concerning that claim. **And** for any claim **that** he would **not pay based on his own** admission but would pay only by the testimony of witnesses, he is **exempt** from bringing a guilt-offering for taking a false oath concerning that claim.

The Mishnah concludes with a general principle (*ze ha-klal*): whoever would be obligated to pay upon admission is liable when he denies (*kol ha-meshallem al pi atzmo chayav*); whoever would be exempt upon admission is exempt when he denies (*she-eino meshallem al pi atzmo patur*). The borderline debates — such as the rape or seduction case — concern claims that contain both *mammon* and *knas* components, where the question is whether the composite character of the claim brings it within the scope of *shevuat hapikadon* or removes it.

## Rav Kahana's Question

### Lashes vs. Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב אַחָא בַּר הוּנָא וְרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה וְרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, תְּנוֹ שְׁבוּעוֹת בֵּי רַבָּה. פְּגַע בְּהוּ רַב כָּהֲנָא, אָמַר לְהוּ: הֵזִיד בִּשְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן וְהִתְרוּ בּוֹ, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דְּחִידּוּשׁ הוּא – דִּבְכׇל הַתּוֹרָה לָא אַשְׁכְּחַן מֵזִיד דְּמַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן, וְהָכָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן – לָא שְׁנָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ וְלָא שְׁנָא לָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ? אוֹ דִּלְמָא, הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ – מִילְקָא לָקֵי, קׇרְבָּן לָא מַיְיתֵי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, הָא וְהָא עָבְדִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Aḥa bar Huna and Rav Shmuel, son of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, and Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, studied** tractate ***Shevuot*** in **the study hall of Rabba. Rav Kahana encountered them** and inquired with regard to a matter in the mishna. He **said to them:** If one **intentionally** took a false **oath on a deposit and** witnesses **forewarned him, what is** the *halakha*? Is it the case that **since** the *halakha* of an oath on a deposit **is a novelty, as in the entire Torah we do not find** another case where one who transgressed **intentionally brings an offering** for atonement, **but here** he does **bring an offering** for transgressing the prohibition intentionally, there **is no difference** whether witnesses **forewarned him and** there **is no difference** whether **they did not forewarn him,** and he is liable to bring a guilt-offering, though generally, there is no offering brought when there was forewarning? **Or perhaps, that matter** of bringing a guilt-offering when one intentionally takes a false oath applies only **when they did not forewarn him; but when they forewarned him,** he is **flogged,** as this is the standard punishment for an intentional transgression, and he does **not bring an offering. Or perhaps, we impose both** lashes and a guilt-offering.

The Gemara opens with a scene: three rabbis — Rav Acha bar Huna, Rav Shmuel son of Rabbah bar bar Chana, and Rav Yitzchak son of Rav Huna — were studying *Shevuot* in the academy of Rabbah, when Rav Kahana encountered them and posed a question that had apparently been on everyone's mind. The scenario of *shevuat hapikadon* — as we have just studied it — presupposes that the denier is a *mezid*, knowingly lying, since one must be willfully dishonest to trigger liability. Rav Kahana's question: *mazid bi-shvuat hapikadon ve-hitru bo mahu* — what is the law if this *mezid* denier was also formally warned (*hatara*) before swearing? In the ordinary structure of Torah law, formal warning followed by willful violation results in *malkot* — lashes — rather than a *korban*. The entire reason *shevuat hapikadon* generates a *korban* even for the *mezid* is that the Torah treats it as a *chidush*, an innovation. But does that mean the *korban* applies even when warning was given — which would normally trigger lashes — or does warning displace the *korban* in favor of lashes? Or perhaps both apply?

Rav Kahana identifies three possibilities: first, that the *korban* applies regardless of warning and no lashes are administered; second, that warning triggers lashes and eliminates the *korban*; third, that both lashes and a *korban* are administered. The question is left open for the gathered scholars to address.

### Braita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ, תְּנֵינָא: חֲמוּרָה הֵימֶנָּה שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן, שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים. מִדְּקָאָמַר לֵיהּ עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת – מִכְּלָל דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ; וְקָאָמַר מַכּוֹת אִין, קׇרְבָּן לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **They said to** Rav Kahana: **We learn** in a *baraita*: The *halakhot* of an **oath on a deposit are more stringent than** the *halakhot* of an oath of testimony, **as** one is **liable** to receive **lashes for intentionally** taking a false oath on a deposit, **and for unwittingly** taking a false oath on a deposit one is liable to bring **a guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels;** whereas one who takes a false oath of testimony, whether intentionally or unwittingly, brings a sin-offering. **From** the fact **that** the *baraita* **states** that **for intentionally** taking a false oath on a deposit one **is liable** to receive **lashes, by inference** it can be understood that the *baraita* is referring to a case where witnesses **forewarned him,** as one cannot receive lashes without forewarning, **and it is stating: Yes,** he is liable to receive **lashes,** but he is **not** liable to bring **an offering.** The *baraita*, then, resolves the question of Rav Kahana.

The students respond that they have a *braita* that addresses the issue: *chamura himena shvuat hapikadon she-chayavin al zedonah malkot ve-al shigagatah asham b'kesef shkalim* — *shevuat hapikadon* is weightier than *shevuat ha'edut* in two respects: when it is done willfully (*zadon*), one receives lashes; and when it is done unknowingly with respect to consequences (*shogeg*), one brings a fixed *asham* of two *shekel* rather than a variable *oleh veyored*. This *braita*, taken at face value, appears to answer Rav Kahana's question directly: for *zadon* with warning, the consequence is lashes.

One might also ask why lashes apply specifically to *shevuat hapikadon* and not to *shevuat ha'edut*. Rashi suggests that by *shevuat ha'edut*, establishing that someone knows testimony is inherently uncertain, making a formal warning difficult — what Rashi terms *hatra'at safek*. Tosafot objects that this characterization is imprecise, since the obligation concerns testimony actually witnessed, and proposes instead that uncertainty about whether the witness genuinely forgot at the moment of the oath makes warning unreliable. In the case of *shevuat hapikadon*, by contrast, knowing possession of another's property is more readily verifiable, making warning and its consequences more operable. In any event, the *braita* appears to establish that for *shevuat hapikadon* with warning, the consequence is lashes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? דְּנִיחָא לֵיהּ לְאִינִישׁ דְּמַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן וְלָא לִילְקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **And what is the stringency** in the fact that one who intentionally takes a false oath on a deposit receives lashes? It is due to the fact that **a person prefers to bring an offering rather than receive lashes.**

The Gemara then notes a further inference from the *braita*: since it says willful violation (*zadon*) results in *malkot* and not a *korban*, and inadvertent violation (*shogeg*) results in the *asham*, the *braita* seems to present these as the only two consequences — lashes in the willful case, *korban* in the inadvertent case, with no mention of receiving both. The *braita* thus implies that warning displaces the *korban* in favor of lashes — since people generally prefer bringing a *korban* to receiving lashes, the heavier consequence is the lashes alone.

### Rabbi Shimon View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רָבָא בַּר אִיתַּי: מַאן תָּנָא זְדוֹן שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן לֹא נִיתַּן לְכַפָּרָה – רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן; אֲבָל לְרַבָּנַן – קׇרְבָּן נָמֵי מַיְיתֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Itai said to** the students of Rabba: You cannot answer the question from that *baraita*, as **who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that **intentionally** taking **a false oath on a deposit is not subject to atonement?** It is **Rabbi Shimon. But** according **to the Rabbis,** he is liable to **bring** a guilt-**offering, as well** as receiving lashes. Therefore, the question remains unanswered.

Rava bar Isu raises an objection to the students' proposed proof. He points out that the *braita* need not represent the majority opinion. It may reflect the view of Rabbi Shimon, who — as we learned in the preceding sugya — holds that *shevuat hapikadon* when done willfully (*zadon*) is characterized by the phrase *ma'al ma'al bashem* as not admitting of atonement through a *korban*. According to Rabbi Shimon, the *korban* for *shevuat hapikadon* applies only to the *shogeg*, not to the *mezid*. If so, then of course the *braita*'s statement that willful violation results in lashes is unproblematic — Rabbi Shimon simply does not recognize a *korban* for *mezid* at all, so lashes is the natural consequence in that case. But this means the *braita* does not resolve the question for the *rabbanan*, who do hold that a *mezid* brings a *korban*: for them, it remains an open question whether warning would additionally generate lashes or not.

### Alternative Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב כָּהֲנָא: בַּר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא; דַּאֲנָא תָּנֵינָא לַהּ – וְהָכִי תָּנֵינָא לַהּ: אֶחָד זְדוֹנָהּ וְאֶחָד שִׁגְגָתָהּ, אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים. וּמַאי חוּמְרָא? דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם חַטָּאת בַּת דַּנְקָא, וְהָכָא אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said to** the students: **Apart from this,** you cannot answer my question from this *baraita*, **as I** am the one who **teaches** this *baraita* **and this** is how **I teach it:** An oath on a deposit is more stringent than an oath of testimony, since for taking a false oath on a deposit **either intentionally or unwittingly** one is liable to bring **a guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels. And what** is **the stringency** in this *halakha*? It is **that there,** with regard to an oath of testimony, he is liable to bring **a sin-offering** that can be **worth** even **one-sixth** of one dinar, **and here,** the **guilt-offering** incurred for an oath on a deposit must be **worth** at least two **silver shekels.**

Rav Kahana then reveals that the students' proof from that *braita* was additionally problematic for a different reason: he himself knows that *braita* in a different version. *Ana tanina lah v'hachi tanina lah* — I have a version of that same *braita*, he says, and it reads very differently. In his version, the *braita* says: *echad zedonah ve-echad shigagatah asham b'kesef shekalim* — whether the violation was willful or inadvertent, the consequence is identically a *korban asham* worth two *shekel*. There is no mention of lashes at all. This establishes that there are two variant texts of this *braita*, one of which describes lashes for *zadon* and one of which prescribes a *korban* for both cases. Since the texts contradict one another, neither can be used as conclusive proof.

### Weightiness Defined

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיגְמַר מִינַּהּ! דִּלְמָא דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him derive** an answer to his question **from** his version of the *baraita*, which indicates that one who intentionally takes a false oath on a deposit brings an offering and does not receive lashes. The Gemara responds: **Perhaps** it is referring to a case **where they did not forewarn him;** had he been forewarned, he would be flogged.

Given Rav Kahana's version of the *braita*, in which both *zadon* and *shogeg* result in a *korban*, one must ask: in what way does this *braita* claim *shevuat hapikadon* is weightier than *shevuat ha'edut*? The answer is straightforward and has nothing to do with lashes. By *shevuat ha'edut*, the *korban* brought is a *chatat* of the *oleh veyored* variety — potentially as inexpensive as a few small coins if one is very poor. By *shevuat hapikadon*, the *korban* is always a full *asham* worth a minimum of two *shekel* of silver, with no option for a cheaper substitute based on one's financial circumstances. This fixed minimum, along with the requirement to bring a proper animal, is the measure of weightiness — not lashes.

## Inconclusive Evidence

### Abaye's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִישָּׁנָא אַחֲרִינָא, תָּא שְׁמַע: אֵין חַיָּיבִין עַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ. מָה הֵן חַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ? אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים. מַאי, לָאו בִּדְאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ? הָכָא נָמֵי דְּלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara presents **a different version** of the previous discussion: **Come** and **hear** an answer to the question of Rav Kahana from the mishna: **One is not liable for** taking a false oath on a deposit **unwittingly.** And **what is he liable for** when he **intentionally** takes a false oath? **A guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels.** The Gemara explains: **What, is it not** that the mishna is referring to a case **where** witnesses **forewarned him,** and the mishna nevertheless rules only that he must bring an offering? The Gemara rejects the proof: **Here, too,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the witnesses **did not forewarn him.**

Even setting aside the conflict between the two *braita* versions, Abaye raises a further difficulty. Rav Kahana's version of the *braita* — which says that both *zadon* and *shogeg* bring a *korban* — cannot straightforwardly settle the question about warning, because it may simply be describing cases without warning (*dilma d'lo asru bei*). Nothing in the text of that *braita* specifies that warning was given. Accordingly, it cannot be used to conclude that even when warning is given, the consequence is a *korban* rather than lashes.

### Alternate Braita

A second *braita* is then cited: *ein chayavin al shigagatah*, one is not liable for a pure *shogeg*, and the liability is *asham b'kesef shekalim* for *zadon*. One might suggest that *zadon* here encompasses even cases with warning. The Gemara rejects this as well with the same response — *hacha nami d'lo asru bei* — here too, the *braita* is plausibly speaking of cases without warning and therefore cannot resolve the question in either direction.

### Nazir Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: לֹא; אִם אָמַרְתָּ בְּנָזִיר טָמֵא – שֶׁכֵּן לוֹקֶה; תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן – שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה?! מִדְּקָאָמַר לוֹקֶה – מִכְּלָל דְּאַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ; וְקָאָמַר תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – אֲבָל קׇרְבָּן מַיְיתֵי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to seek an answer to Rav Kahana's question: **Come** and **hear** an answer from a *baraita*. After comparing the case of a nazirite to one who takes an oath on a deposit, the *baraita* states: **No, if you said** that the *halakha* that *baraita* discusses is true **with regard to an impure nazirite, who is indeed flogged** for intentionally becoming impure, **shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to** one who took **an oath on a deposit, who is not flogged?** The Gemara notes: Now, **from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **states** that a nazirite **is flogged, by inference** it can be understood that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** witnesses **forewarned him,** as one is not liable to receive lashes without forewarning; **and** yet the *baraita* **states: Shall you say** that this is the case **with regard to** one who took **an oath on a deposit, who is not flogged?** One can infer: **But** he does **bring** a guilt-**offering.**

The Gemara introduces a *braita* that compares the *nazir tamei* — a Nazirite who becomes ritually impure — to *shevuat hapikadon*. The *braita* states: *lo, im amarta b'nazir tamei she-ken lokeh, tomar bi-shvuat hapikadon she-eino lokeh* — you cannot derive a ruling about *shevuat hapikadon* from the case of the *nazir tamei*, because while the *nazir tamei* receives lashes, *shevuat hapikadon* does not. The implication is that the *braita* is discussing parallel scenarios — presumably both with warning — and using the *nazir tamei* as the heavier case and *shevuat hapikadon* as the lighter. If the *nazir tamei* receives lashes, and *shevuat hapikadon* does not, this would seem to directly answer Rav Kahana's question: *shevuat hapikadon* does not generate lashes even with warning.

### Abaye Reinterprets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – דְּאֵינוֹ נִפְטָר בְּמַלְקוֹת. מִכְּלָל דְּנָזִיר טָמֵא נִפְטָר בְּמַלְקוֹת?! הָא ״קׇרְבָּן״ כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ! הָתָם דְּמַיְיתֵי קׇרְבָּן כִּי הֵיכִי דְּתֵיחוּל עֲלֵיהּ נְזִירוּת בְּטׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects the proof: **What** does the *baraita* mean when it states that **he is not flogged?** It means **that** he is **not exempted through lashes** alone and he must also bring an offering. The Gemara asks: **By inference,** does this mean **that an impure nazirite is exempted through lashes** alone? **Isn't it written** in the Torah explicitly **with regard to him** that there is a requirement to bring **an offering?** The Gemara responds: The offering brought by an impure nazirite does not serve as an atonement. Rather, **there,** the nazirite **brings an offering in order** to purify himself so **that his naziriteship can go into effect** while he is **in** a state of **purity.** The offering must be brought even if the nazirite became impure unwittingly.

Abaye, however, refuses to accept this reading. He proposes a counter-reading of the *braita*: *mai eino lokeh? de-eino niftar be-malkot* — when the *braita* says *shevuat hapikadon* does not receive lashes, it does not mean lashes are absent. It means lashes alone are insufficient to satisfy the obligation. The person receives lashes *and* brings a *korban*. This reverses the comparative weight of the two cases: rather than *nazir tamei* being heavier (lashes) and *shevuat hapikadon* lighter (no lashes), *shevuat hapikadon* is now heavier — it requires both — while *nazir tamei* gets off with lashes only. The Gemara immediately objects: *miklal d'nazir tamei niftar be-malkot?* Does this mean the *nazir tamei* is fully discharged by lashes alone, with no *korban*? But the Torah explicitly states that the *nazir tamei* brings a *korban*! The Gemara responds: the *korban* of the *nazir tamei* is not an atonement offering in the ordinary sense — it is the mechanism by which his new period of *nezirut* begins after the defilement has interrupted his previous count. With that reframing, Abaye's reading remains technically possible, but the overall result is that neither *braita* succeeds in settling the question cleanly, and the *sugya* remains without a definitive resolution on whether warning generates lashes for *shevuat hapikadon*.

## Rava's Position

### Rava's Thesis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבָּה. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מִכְּלָל דְּכִי לָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ וְאִיכָּא עֵדִים – מִיחַיַּיב?! כְּפִירַת דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא הוּא!
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said** this matter **before Rabba,** i.e., they related the question of Rav Kahana as to whether one who intentionally takes a false oath and was forewarned by witnesses is liable to bring an offering. Rabba **said to them:** May one conclude **by inference that** in a case **when they did not warn him but there are witnesses** to the fact that the defendant owes money to the claimant, **he is liable** to bring an offering? Isn't this case **merely a verbal denial** that does not have any effect with regard to liability to pay? Since there are witnesses who will testify that the defendant owes the claimant, his denial and false oath did not exempt him from payment and he therefore should not be liable to bring a guilt-offering for taking a false oath. The Gemara infers: **Evidently, Rabba holds** that **one who denies a monetary** claim **to which there are witnesses** is **exempt** from bringing a guilt-offering for taking a false oath, since the witnesses will testify to the validity of the claimant's claim and the denial of the defendant is of no consequence.

The scholars who had been debating these questions returned to the *beit midrash* of Rava and recounted the entire exchange with Rav Kahana. Rava's response is remarkable: rather than engaging directly with the question of lashes versus *korban*, he identifies a more fundamental assumption embedded in the whole discussion that he believes is mistaken. The scenario posited by Rav Kahana assumed that there were witnesses who knew the denier was lying and warned him accordingly. Rava points out that this implicit assumption — that a *korban* would be owed in such a case even absent warning — is itself not obvious to him.

His position: *kfirat devarim b'alma hu* — when the denier denies a debt and there are witnesses who can prove the debt exists and who could testify against him in court, his denial is mere words. It carries no legal consequence because the witnesses will simply come forward and establish the obligation in court. The denier has not actually kept money from the claimant; he has only said words that will be overridden by testimony. Rava therefore holds that if witnesses are present who can independently establish the monetary claim, the denier does not trigger *shevuat hapikadon* at all — not because the oath was insincere, but because the act of denial in such a context lacks the operative force of *kfirat mammon*, actual withholding of money.

### Rabbi Chanina's Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חֲנִינָא לְרַבָּה, תַּנְיָא דִּמְסַיַּיע לָךְ: ״וְכִחֵשׁ בָּהּ״ – פְּרָט לְמוֹדֶה לְאֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּתָּפִין. ״וְנִשְׁבַּע עַל שָׁקֶר״ – פְּרָט לְלֹוֶה בִּשְׁטָר וּלְלֹוֶה בְּעֵדִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanina said to Rabba:** A *baraita* **is taught that supports your** opinion: The verse states with regard to an oath denying a monetary claim: **"And deal falsely therein"** (Leviticus 5:22), which serves to **exclude one who admits to** the truth of the claim of **one of** a group of **brothers or to one of** a group of **partners,** even if he denies the claim to the rest of the brothers or partners. The same verse also states: **"And swear to a lie,"** which serves to **exclude one who borrows** money **with** a promissory **note or one who borrows** money **in** the presence of **witnesses.** Evidently, one is not liable for taking a false oath concerning a monetary claim when there are witnesses who can testify to it.

Rabbi Chanina immediately offers a *braita* that he believes supports Rava's position. The *braita*, expounding the Biblical phrase *ve-kichash bah* in the section on *pikadon*, offers two exclusions: first, *prat l'modeh l'echad min ha'achim o l'echad min ha'shutafim* — excluding the case where one admits to one of the brothers or partners who jointly hold a claim; second, *prat l'loveh b'shtar u-loveh b'edim* — excluding a case where one borrowed with a document or with witnesses. Rabbi Chanina reads this second exclusion as support for Rava: if witnesses or a document can prove the debt, the denier's false oath does not generate liability, precisely because the denial is meaningless.

### Rava's Rebuttal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי מִשּׁוּם הָא – לָא תְּסַיְּיעַן; בְּאוֹמֵר ״לָוִיתִי, וְלֹא לָוִיתִי בְּעֵדִים״, ״לָוִיתִי, וְלֹא לָוִיתִי בִּשְׁטָר״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: If** your proof is **because of that** *baraita*, **you have not supported us,** since that *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the defendant **says: I borrowed, but I did not borrow in** the presence of **witnesses,** or he says: **I borrowed, but I did not borrow with** a promissory **note.** Since his denial is only with regard to the circumstances of the loan and not to the actual debt, he is exempt from liability for taking the false oath.

Rava declines to accept this *braita* as support. *Ei mishum ha lo tesayeyan* — if that is your only evidence, it does not help me. Rava reinterprets what the *braita* is describing: *b'omer loviti ve-lo loviti b'edim loviti ve-lo loviti b'shtar* — the *braita* is not discussing a case where the denier denies the underlying debt itself, saying "I owe you nothing." Rather, the *braita* describes a case where the denier admits the debt but denies the evidentiary framework around it. He says: "Yes, I borrowed money from you, but there were no witnesses to it" or "Yes, I borrowed from you, but there was no *shtar* drawn up." In that case, what he denies is not the *mammon* — the money — but the witnesses or the document, which are indeed *kfirat devarim*, a denial of mere circumstances. Whether witnesses recorded the loan is immaterial to the borrower's obligation to repay it; admitting the loan is what matters. A denial that concedes the debt but contests the evidentiary record is therefore a true case of *kfirat devarim* in a strong and obvious sense.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדְּקָתָנֵי ״וְכִחֵשׁ בָּהּ״ – פְּרָט לְמוֹדֶה לְאֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין אוֹ לְאֶחָד מִן הַשּׁוּתָּפִין. הַאי לְאֶחָד מִן הָאַחִין הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּאוֹדִי לֵיהּ בְּפַלְגָא דִּידֵיהּ – הָא אִיכָּא כְּפִירָה דְּאִידַּךְ! אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאָמְרִי לֵיהּ: מִתַּרְוֵינַן יְזַפְתְּ; וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא, מֵחַד מִינַּיְיכוּ יְזַפִי – דְּהָוְיָא לֵיהּ כְּפִירַת דְּבָרִים בְּעָלְמָא? וּמִדְּרֵישָׁא כְּפִירַת דְּבָרִים – סֵיפָא נָמֵי כְּפִירַת דְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** Rava elaborates: **From where** in the *baraita* is this explanation apparent? **From** the fact **that it teaches** that the verse **"and deal falsely therein"** serves to **exclude one who admits to one of the brothers or to one of the partners. What are the circumstances of that** case where one admits **to one of the brothers? If we say that he admitted** only **the portion** of the debt owed **to that** brother, then why is he exempt? **Isn't there the denial** of the claim **of the other** brother, which is a denial of a monetary claim? **Rather, is it not that** the brothers **said to him: You borrowed from both of us, and he said to them: No, I borrowed** all of the amount in question **from** only **one of you, which is merely a verbal denial,** as he admits that he owes the claimed amount and disputes only the circumstances of the debt. **And since the first clause** of the *baraita* is referring to a case of **a verbal denial, the last clause** is **also** referring to **a verbal denial.** Consequently, one cannot cite a proof from this *baraita*.

Rava argues that this reading is confirmed by the first clause of the *braita*, which describes someone who admits to one of the brothers or partners. In that scenario, the denier admits to the full debt — he just disputes the internal allocation among the partners (*d'amri lei mitravaihu yazafta ve-amar lo m'chad minaichu yazafi*). He borrowed from both of them jointly but says he borrowed only from one of them. He concedes the entire principal; the only dispute is which individual's share he owes — something that can be sorted out between the brothers without any loss to the overall claimant. That too is *kfirat devarim b'alma*, denial of something immaterial to the monetary obligation. Since both halves of the *braita* share this character of denying something incidental while admitting the substance, the *braita* says nothing about what happens when a person actually denies the debt itself in the presence of witnesses who can prove it.

## Multiple Witnesses Proof

### Two Witness Sets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָיוּ שְׁתֵּי כִּתֵּי עֵדִים, כָּפְרָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְאַחַר כָּךְ כָּפְרָה הַשְּׁנִיָּה – שְׁתֵּיהֶן חַיָּיבוֹת, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה עֵדוּת לְהִתְקַיֵּים בִּשְׁתֵּיהֶן. בִּשְׁלָמָא שְׁנִיָּה תִּחַיַּיב – דְּהָא כָּפְרָה לָהּ כַּת רִאשׁוֹנָה; אֶלָּא רִאשׁוֹנָה אַמַּאי מִיחַיְּיבָא?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a mishna (31b) that discusses an oath of testimony: If **there were two sets of witnesses** who took an oath of testimony, and **the first** set falsely **denied** knowledge of the matter **and then the second** set falsely **denied** knowledge of the matter, **both are liable, because the testimony can exist with either of them.** The Gemara clarifies: **Granted, the second** set **should be liable, since the first set** already **denied** knowledge of the incident and the validity of the claimant's monetary claim now depends upon their testimony. **But why is** the **first** set **liable?**

The Gemara continues to test Rava's position. A *braita* is cited from the laws of *shevuat ha'edut*: if there were two separate groups of witnesses, and the first group denied knowing testimony and then the second group also denied, both groups are liable (*shneihem chayavot*). The reasoning given: since either group alone would have sufficed to establish the testimony, each group's denial was independently consequential.

But this *braita* raises a sharp question against Rava. One might accept that the second group is liable — since the first group already denied, the entire weight of the testimony rested on the second group when they denied. But why is the first group liable? At the time the first group denied, the second group still existed as a valid set of witnesses! The testimony could still be established. The fact that the first group is nonetheless found liable suggests that even when another set of witnesses can prove the truth, a denial still generates full *korban* liability — precisely contrary to Rava's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא קָיְימָא שְׁנִיָּה!
>
> **Translation:** **Doesn't the second** set **stand** ready to testify, so that the refusal of the first set of witnesses does not affect a monetary claim? Evidently, a denial of a monetary claim to which there are witnesses is still considered a denial.

### Ravina's Escape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה שְׁנִיָּה בִּשְׁעַת כְּפִירַת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה קְרוֹבִים בִּנְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן, וּנְשׁוֹתֵיהֶן גּוֹסְסוֹת. מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רוֹב גּוֹסְסִין לְמִיתָה; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הַשְׁתָּא מִיהַת חָיֵי נִינְהוּ וְלָא שְׁכִיבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Here we are dealing with** a case **where at the time of the denial by the first** set, **the second** set of witnesses **were related** to one another **through their wives,** so that the second set was unfit to provide testimony; **and their wives were moribund. Lest you say: Most moribund people** actually **die** soon thereafter, and the witnesses are considered fit to provide testimony, the *baraita* **teaches us** that **in any event they are currently alive and have not died.** The second set was therefore unfit to provide testimony.

Ravina provides an escape for Rava. The *braita* need not be describing a case where the second group was fully valid at the time the first group denied. The scenario can be read as: *k'gon she-haytah shniyah b'sha'at kfira rishona kruvin b'nshoteihem*, the members of the second group were, at the very moment the first group took its oath, related to one of the parties through their wives (*kruvin b'nshoteihem*), rendering them invalid witnesses. Their wives were, however, on their deathbed (*nshoteihem gossot*). One might have thought that since *rov gossim lemita* — most of the critically ill die — we treat the wives as effectively already dead and the second group as already valid. The *baraita* teaches that we do not reason this way: *hashta miha chayei ninhu v'lo shchivi*, at present they are alive and have not yet died, so the second group was invalid at the moment of the first group's denial. Therefore, the first group was indeed the only viable set of witnesses at the relevant moment, making its denial fully consequential. This reading protects Rava from the *braita*, since the case is not one where an alternative means of proof was genuinely available.

### Theft Claim Braita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: בַּעַל הַבַּיִת שֶׁטָּעַן טַעֲנַת גַּנָּב בְּפִקָּדוֹן, וְנִשְׁבַּע, וְהוֹדָה, וּבָאוּ עֵדִים; אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא בָּאוּ עֵדִים הוֹדָה – מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ וְאָשָׁם. אִם מִשֶּׁבָּאוּ עֵדִים הוֹדָה – מְשַׁלֵּם תַּשְׁלוּמֵי כֶפֶל וְאָשָׁם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: In the case of **a homeowner** acting as a bailee **who** falsely **claimed** that **a thief**  stole **a deposit** from him, **and** the homeowner **took an oath** to that effect **and** then **admitted** that he was lying, **and witnesses came** and testified that the item was not stolen from the homeowner, the *halakha* depends on the circumstances. **If he admitted** to his lie **before** the **witnesses came** and testified, he **pays** the **principal** value of the item **and** the additional **one-fifth** payment for denying that he possessed the deposit, **and** he brings **a guilt-offering** as atonement for a false oath on a deposit. **If he admitted** his guilt **after** the **witnesses came** and testified, **he pays** the **double payment and** brings **a guilt-offering.** The *baraita* indicates that even in the case of a monetary claim to which there are witnesses, one is liable to bring a guilt-offering.

A further *braita* is introduced. It addresses the case of a *ba'al ha'bayit* who makes a false claim that his entrusted object was stolen — *ta'anat ganav* — in order to collect the *kefel* that a thief would be required to pay. He swears falsely and then either admits or is exposed by witnesses. The *braita* distinguishes: if he admitted before witnesses arrived, he pays the *keren* and *chomesh* and brings an *asham* — but does not pay *kefel*, since *modeh b'knas* exempts one from the fine. If, however, witnesses came first, he pays *kefel* and still brings the *asham*. The very structure of this *braita* — that he brings a *korban asham* — presupposes that witnesses arrived and he is still obligated. This appears to contradict Rava's position directly: witnesses have established the truth, yet the false oath under *shevuat hapikadon* still generates a *korban*.

### Witnesses Become Valid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, כִּדְרָבִינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Here, too,** explain this *baraita* **as Ravina** explained the previous *baraita*, that at the time the homeowner took his oath, the witnesses were related through their moribund wives and were unfit to provide testimony.

Ravina again provides a defense. Here too, we can posit that at the moment the oath was taken, the witnesses were actually invalid — for example, their wives were still alive, rendering them relatives of the parties and unfit to testify. Subsequently, the wives died, the witnesses became valid, and only then did they come and testify. In that circumstance, valid witnesses were not present at the time of the oath, and therefore the oath did generate full *korban* liability. It only appears that witnesses "came" and established the truth; technically, those witnesses became competent only after the oath was already sworn. Ravina's maneuver preserves Rava's position by ensuring that neither *braita* straightforwardly depicts a case where the denier swore falsely in the face of presently valid, available witnesses.

## Proof from Nazir

### Nazir Comparison

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי, תָּא שְׁמַע: חֲמוּרָה מִמֶּנָּה שְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן – שֶׁחַיָּיבִין עַל זְדוֹנָהּ מַכּוֹת, וְעַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים. מִדְּקָאָמַר לוֹקֶה – מִכְּלָל דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים; וְקָאָמַר: עַל שִׁגְגָתָהּ אָשָׁם בְּכֶסֶף שְׁקָלִים!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said to Rav Ashi: Come** and **hear** another proof from that which is taught in a *baraita*: The *halakhot* of **an oath on a deposit are more stringent than** the *halakhot* of an oath of testimony, **as** one is **liable** to receive **lashes for intentionally** taking a false oath on a deposit, **and** one is liable to bring **a guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels for** taking the oath **unwittingly.** Ravina infers: **From** the fact **that** the *baraita* **states** that one **is flogged, by inference** it can be understood that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where there are witnesses** to the fact that the deposit is in the defendant's possession and the defendant was forewarned, **and** yet it **states:** One is liable to bring **a guilt-offering worth** at least two **silver shekels for** taking the oath **unwittingly.**

Ravina then turns to Rav Ashi and proposes a proof that cannot be escaped by the same maneuver. He returns to the *braita* about the *nazir tamei* and *shevuat hapikadon*. The *braita*, as previously cited, establishes that the *nazir tamei* is the lighter case and *shevuat hapikadon* the heavier, since the *nazir tamei* gets lashes while the denier in *shevuat hapikadon* does not — or, alternatively, in the reading Ravina is now advancing, the *nazir tamei* gets lashes and the denier in *shevuat hapikadon* gets both lashes and a *korban*. In either interpretation, the *braita* is drawing a parallel between the two cases that both involve witnesses. Ravina argues that by the *nazir tamei*, the scenario is one where witnesses are present who observed the defilement — otherwise, why would there be warning and why would lashes be applicable? And yet lashes are given. In the parallel case of *shevuat hapikadon*, even though the *nazir tamei* is the lighter case, the point of comparison requires that witnesses be equally present in both scenarios.

### Weightiness Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּלֵיכָּא עֵדִים – אַמַּאי לוֹקֶה? אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא דְּאִיכָּא עֵדִים; וְקָתָנֵי: תֹּאמַר בִּשְׁבוּעַת הַפִּקָּדוֹן שֶׁאֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה; מִלְקָא הוּא דְּלָא לָקֵי – אֲבָל קׇרְבָּן מַיְיתֵי; תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה! תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: **What are the circumstances** of the *baraita*? **If** it is a case **where there were no witnesses, why is** the nazirite **flogged? Rather,** isn't it **obvious that there are witnesses, and** yet, the *baraita* **teaches: Shall you** also **say** that this is the case **with regard to** one who took **an oath on a deposit, who is not flogged?** It may be inferred that **he does not receive lashes but** does **bring an offering,** even though there are witnesses. The Gemara concludes: **The refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba** is indeed **a conclusive refutation.**

The interpretive upshot is this: the *nazir tamei*, who has witnesses and receives lashes, is actually the lighter case. *Shevuat hapikadon*, where the denier has witnesses against him and does not escape with lashes alone but instead brings a *korban* — or perhaps both lashes and a *korban* — is the weightier case. This is a striking reversal of what one might have assumed. The *braita*'s contrast of the two cases therefore requires that *shevuat hapikadon* generates *korban* liability even in a context where witnesses are present.

The Gemara concludes: *tiyuvta de-Rava tiyuvta* — a complete and definitive refutation of Rava. Rava had maintained that if witnesses are present and can prove the debt, the denial is mere *kfirat devarim b'alma* and no *korban* is owed. The *braita* comparing *nazir tamei* to *shevuat hapikadon* demonstrates that this is not so: even when witnesses are present and could otherwise establish the obligation, the act of denial under *shevuat hapikadon* is legally operative and obligates the denier in a *korban*. All of Ravina's previous maneuvers — hypothesizing that witnesses were temporarily invalid through their wives — are unable to rescue Rava from this final proof, which stands as a clean structural argument rather than a factual scenario that can be re-characterized. Rava's position is rejected in full.

## Witnesses vs. Documents

### Rabbi Yochanan Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: הַכּוֹפֵר בְּמָמוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – חַיָּיב. בִּשְׁטָר – פָּטוּר. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַאי טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן? עֵדִים – עֲבִידִי דְּמָיְיתִי, שְׁטָר – הָא מַנַּח.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who denies a monetary** claim **to which there are witnesses** is **liable** to bring a guilt-offering for a false oath on a deposit. But if he denies a debt **concerning** which there is a promissory **note,** he is **exempt. Rav Pappa said: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan?** It **occurs that witnesses die,** and it is therefore possible that he would not be found liable through their testimony; he is therefore considered to have denied a monetary claim. By contrast, a promissory **note remains** in its place, and his denial would never have exempted him from payment.

Following Rava's refutation, the Gemara introduces a more nuanced position — that of Rabbi Yochanan — which navigates between Rava's rejected view and the straightforward rule now established. Rabbi Yochanan holds: *ha-kofer b'mamon she-yesh alav edim chayav* — one who denies a debt for which there are witnesses is liable. This is consistent with the conclusion just reached against Rava. However, Rabbi Yochanan adds: *aval bi-shtar patur* — but one who denies a debt attested by a *shtar* (a formal legal document) is exempt. This distinction requires explanation, since documents are ordinarily considered at least as reliable as witnesses, if not more so.

### Rav Pappa Reason

Rav Pappa offers an initial explanation of Rabbi Yochanan's distinction. Witnesses, he says, are always somewhat precarious: *avidi d'meisi* — they might die; they might travel abroad; circumstances might render them unavailable. Therefore, the denier's claim that he owes nothing is not entirely without consequence — the witnesses might not materialize, and the denial might actually succeed in keeping the money from the claimant. The denial is therefore a real act of *kfirat mammon*, and the *korban* is owed. A *shtar*, however, once written and held in one's possession, sits in a safe deposit box, so to speak — *shtar ha-munach* — and is virtually certain to be produced. In such a case, the denial truly is *kfirat devarim b'alma*; it will be proven false with certainty, and no money will actually be withheld.

### True Reason

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ לְרַב פָּפָּא: שְׁטָרָא נָמֵי עֲבִיד דְּמִרְכַס! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: הַיְינוּ טַעְמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן – מִשּׁוּם דְּהָוֵה שְׁטָר שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת, וְאֵין מְבִיאִין קׇרְבָּן עַל כְּפִירַת שִׁעְבּוּד קַרְקָעוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said to Rav Pappa:** This cannot be the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan, as it **also occurs that a promissory note becomes lost. Rather, Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, said: This is the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** It is **because** a promissory **note** comprises **a lien on land,** since the promissory note places a lien on the debtor's property, **and one does not bring an offering** for an oath on a deposit **for denying a lien on land,** since one does not take an oath concerning land.

Rav Huna son of Rabbi Yehoshua objects to Rav Pappa's reasoning: *shtar nami avidi d'mirkas* — documents also get lost! The security of a *shtar* is not so absolute that it can be considered a certainty. He therefore proposes an entirely different explanation for Rabbi Yochanan's ruling: the real reason the *shtar* case is different has nothing to do with the reliability of the evidence. *Mishum d'havei shtar shibud karkaot* — a formal *shtar* creates a lien on land (*shibud karkaot*). When a debt is documented in a *shtar*, the creditor acquires a legal claim on the debtor's real property. Therefore, when the debtor denies the debt attested by the *shtar*, he is not merely denying a monetary obligation — he is denying an encumbrance on his land. And the general principle of *shevuot* is that land is excluded from the entire domain of oath liability: *ein meivi'in korban al kfirat shibud karkaot* — there is no *korban* for denial of a land-related obligation. Since the debt under the *shtar* is inextricably tied to a claim on land, the denial does not trigger the *korban* of *shevuat hapikadon*.

### Land Exemption

This reasoning — that a *shtar* implies *shibud karkaot* and therefore removes the case from the purview of oath liability — is the operative rationale behind Rabbi Yochanan's exemption. It is not about the certainty of proof or the futility of denial; it is about the substantive legal category of the debt being denied. A debt attested by a *shtar* carries with it a land dimension, and oaths concerning land simply fall outside the framework that generates *korban* consequences.

## Land Testimony Debate

### Land Oath Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מַשְׁבִּיעַ עֵדֵי קַרְקַע – פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר; חַד אָמַר חַיָּיב, וְחַד אָמַר פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated:** In a case where one **administers an oath to witnesses** who deny knowing information with regard to ownership **of land** and they deny knowledge of the matter, **Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Eleazar disagree: One says** that the witnesses are **liable** to bring a sin-offering for a false oath of testimony, **and one says** that they are **exempt.**

The Gemara now extends this principle to the related question of *shevuat ha'edut* regarding land. The issue: if witnesses are summoned to testify about a land matter — for example, that a certain plot of land belongs to one party — and they falsely deny knowing such testimony, is there a *korban*?

### Yochanan/Eleazar

Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Eleazar disagree. One holds the witnesses are liable (*chayav*); the other exempts them (*patur*). The Gemara does not immediately specify which opinion belongs to which *amora*, but moves to identify the positions from context.

### Proof Structure

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּסְתַּיַּים דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּאָמַר פָּטוּר – מִדְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הַכּוֹפֵר בְּמָמוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – חַיָּיב, שְׁטָר – פָּטוּר; וְכִדְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ. תִּסְתַּיַּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **It may be concluded that** it is **Rabbi Yoḥanan who says** they are **exempt.** This can be inferred **from** the fact **that Rabbi Yoḥanan says: One who denies a monetary** claim **to which there are witnesses** is **liable,** but one who denies a claim concerning which there is a promissory **note** is **exempt. And** this conclusion is **in accordance with** the explanation **of Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua,** that the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan is that a promissory note comprises a lien on land, and one does not bring an offering for denying a lien on land. The Gemara affirms: Indeed, **it may be concluded.**

The Gemara concludes that it is likely Rabbi Yochanan who holds that witnesses about land are exempt. The proof: Rabbi Yochanan himself just ruled that denial of a *shtar*-attested debt is exempt because the *shtar* implies *shibud karkaot* — and Rav Huna son of Rabbi Yehoshua explained that this is because denial of a land-linked obligation does not generate *korban* liability. Consistent with that position, Rabbi Yochanan would naturally hold that witnesses summoned to testify about land — whose denial is a denial of testimony about land — are similarly exempt. The same principle that exempts the debtor who denies a debt tied to land would exempt witnesses who deny testimony concerning land. The debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Rabbi Eleazar on *edei karka* — witnesses for land claims — thus mirrors the structure of the earlier positions and will be taken up in the continuation of the *sugya*.
