# Niddah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Elderly Woman & 90 Days

### Mesulakat Damim

The daf begins with a *baraita* — *tanu rabbanan* — that takes up the theme of *mesulakat damim*, the woman who does not ordinarily expect to receive her period. As we have seen in the preceding pages, such a woman, when she discovers she has bled, is not obligated to extend her concern retroactively. The legal category of *mesulakat damim* now comes to bear on a specific case: an elderly woman who is in the process of transitioning to a post-menopausal state.

### First 90 Days

The *baraita* opens: *zekeina she-averu aleha shelosha onot vera'ata* — an elderly woman (a category whose parameters were defined earlier) over whom three *onot* have passed, meaning ninety days have elapsed without any menstrual bleeding. This woman is now classified as *mesulakat damim*. Accordingly, when she does bleed, the rule is *dayah sha'atah*: she is concerned only from the moment of seeing, not retroactively for the preceding twenty-four hours.

### Second 90 Days & Rebleed

The *baraita* continues: *ve'od averu aleha shelosha onot vera'ata* — another ninety days pass and she bleeds again. One might have expected that this second bleeding, coming after the initial ninety-day gap, would constitute counter-evidence against her status as *mesulakat damim* — essentially proving that she is still capable of menstruating. And indeed, had she bled again within thirty days, that subsequent bleeding would have retroactively undermined her *mesulakat damim* status and subjected her to the twenty-four-hour retroactive window. But the crucial point here is that she did not bleed within thirty days. She waited another ninety days before bleeding again. That second ninety-day gap re-establishes her *mesulakat damim* status. Her renewed bleeding is therefore again governed by *dayah sha'atah*. The same dynamic occurs a third time: yet another ninety days passes without bleeding, she bleeds again, and again the rule of *dayah sha'atah* applies.

### Three 90-Day Pattern

Now the *baraita* introduces a twist. After a fourth ninety-day gap — *ve'od averu aleha shelosha onot* — one might expect the familiar conclusion: the gap has again restored her *mesulakat damim* status, so *dayah sha'atah* governs once more. But that is not the ruling. *Harei hi ke-chol ha-nashim* — she is now treated like all other women — *umetamei me-et le-et umipekida li-fekida*: she is concerned retroactively twenty-four hours or since her last bodily examination, whichever is the shorter interval.

Why? Because three cycles of ninety-day gaps followed by bleeding now constitute a recognized pattern. Her bleeding every ninety days — whether exact or approximate — establishes that she is not truly *mesulakat damim*. She is simply a woman with a very long cycle. The ninety-day intervals are no longer evidence of post-menopausal cessation; they have become her *veset*, her regular pattern.

### Exact vs. Inexact

The *baraita* adds: *velo she-kivna ela afilu pichsa ve-hotira* — and not only when the intervals were exactly ninety days, but even when they were inexact, say ninety, ninety-two, ninety-four days. The *gemara* pauses over the phrasing. The *baraita* appears to say that it goes without saying (*lo mibaya*) that when she is exact the concern applies, and that the novelty (*chidush*) lies in the inexact case. But the *gemara* challenges this framing sharply.

### Gemara Analysis

*Adarabba* — the *gemara* says the opposite ought to be true. When the woman's intervals are exact — ninety days, ninety days, ninety days — she has established a *veset kavua*, a fixed cycle. And as our opening *mishnah* taught, a woman with a *veset kavua* is *dayah sha'atah* — she is not concerned retroactively, because she bleeds predictably when her cycle arrives. So an exact pattern would seem to *reduce* her retroactive concern, not establish it. It is the inexact pattern that is the genuine *chidush*, where one might have argued that without a fixed cycle the ninety-day gaps still qualify as *mesulakat damim* status. Accordingly, the *gemara* concludes *tani* — emend the text: read it as *lo shepichsa ve-hotira ela afilu kivna*, not only when she is inexact is she concerned retroactively, but even when she is exact.

This preserves the underlying *halacha* — both exact and inexact patterns generate a retroactive twenty-four-hour concern — while correcting the *baraita*'s formulation to reflect the proper direction of the argument.

The *gemara* then offers an alternative resolution: *ve-iba'it eima hachi ka'amar* — perhaps the *baraita* can be read as stated without emendation. On this reading, the case of exact intervals is excluded from the twenty-four-hour concern precisely because it constitutes a proper *veset kavua*, and the *baraita* is authored according to Rabbi Dosa's position that a woman with a fixed cycle is *dayah sha'atah*. The only case generating a retroactive concern is the inexact one, and this reading attributes the *baraita* to Rabbi Dosa.

The underlying *halacha* bears emphasis: a woman who passes through three cycles of ninety-day gaps punctuated by bleeding has established a pattern. Whether those intervals are exact or approximate, the three repetitions are sufficient to transform what had appeared to be post-menopausal cessation into a recognized, if unusually long, menstrual cycle. There is a genuine *chidush* even in the inexact case, because one might have argued that without a fixed pattern the ninety-day gaps should perpetually restore her *mesulakat damim* status — and the *gemara* rejects that reading.

## Rabbi Eliezer 90-Day

### Rabbi Eliezer Ruling

The *gemara* now turns to Rabbi Eliezer's position: *kol isha she-avra aleha shelosha onot* — any woman who has gone ninety days without bleeding is classified as *mesulakat damim*, regardless of age or life stage. Rabbi Eliezer's view is not limited to post-menopausal women; it extends to any woman for any reason. The biological reality acknowledged here is that women may cease menstruating due to causes other than menopause — including, as will become immediately apparent, malnutrition.

### Heitalu Story

To support his position, Rabbi Eliezer cites a precedent: *ma'ase be-riva achas be-Heitalu* — there was an incident involving a young woman in a place called *Heitalu*, who went ninety days without bleeding. When she subsequently bled, the case came before the sages, and they ruled *dayah sha'atah* — she was not concerned retroactively, because her ninety-day gap had conferred upon her the status of *mesulakat damim*, even though she was young.

### Rabbis' Response

The sages' counter-response is direct: *ein sha'at ha-dechak re'ayah* — an emergency ruling is not a proof for general law. The ruling in the *Heitalu* case was issued under extenuating circumstances and cannot be generalized into standard *halacha*.

### Famine & Loss

What were those extenuating circumstances? The *gemara* offers two explanations. The first: *shenei vatzoret havu* — it was a time of famine. Ruling that this woman was *tamei* retroactively for twenty-four hours would have required invalidating *terumah* handled during that period, and during a famine one is exceedingly reluctant to destroy food. It is also worth noting, though the *gemara* itself does not make this connection explicitly, that famine-induced malnutrition is itself a well-known cause of menstrual cessation — which would explain how a young woman came to go ninety days without bleeding in the first place.

The second explanation is more local and arguably more striking: *tahara apisha la-vidah* — in this particular woman's case, she had handled an unusually large quantity of *terumah* in the preceding twenty-four hours. The retroactive *tumah* would therefore have entailed an exceptional loss specific to this individual. This second explanation makes clear that the *sha'at ha-dechak* need not be a national emergency — even a case of extraordinary individual financial loss can qualify.

If anything, the second explanation sharpens the debate: whatever prompted this young woman's ninety-day gap, whether illness, famine, or something else entirely, the rabbis' leniency was grounded entirely in the magnitude of the potential loss of *taharot*, not in any independent assessment of whether her *mesulakat damim* status was genuine. The general principle thus stands: absent a *sha'at ha-dechak*, a young woman who has gone ninety days without bleeding is not thereby classified as *mesulakat damim* according to the majority of the sages.

## Rabbi's Reversal

### Rabbi Reconsiders

The *gemara* now records a remarkable episode: *ma'aseh ve-asah Rebbi ke-Rabbi Eliezer* — Rabbi once issued a ruling in accordance with Rabbi Eliezer's position, treating a woman as *mesulakat damim* after ninety days regardless of her age. *Le-achar she-nizkar* — afterward, once he recalled his error, he acknowledged that he should not have ruled that way.

### Formal vs. Practical

Yet even upon recognizing the error, Rabbi did not retract the ruling entirely. Instead, he articulated a principle: *kedai Rabbi Eliezer lismoch alav be-sha'at ha-dechak* — Rabbi Eliezer's opinion is reliable enough to be leaned upon in a case of urgent need. The ruling already given would stand on that basis.

What exactly had Rabbi recalled? *Le-achar she-nizkar de-lav yachid palig alei ela rabim palig alei* — he remembered that it was not a single dissenting opinion that stood against Rabbi Eliezer, but a majority of the sages. There had been no explicit formal ruling that *halacha* does not follow Rabbi Eliezer — *de-lo itmar hilcheta lo ke-mar ve-lo ke-mar* — but the ordinary calculus of *pesak* would have led inescapably to that conclusion. Rabbi had acted on Rabbi Eliezer's position without weighing that calculus carefully enough.

### She'at Ha-Dechak

This episode illuminates a broader principle of fundamental importance. A minority opinion — *da'at yachid* — that has not been formally and explicitly rejected retains a latent viability. In a case of *sha'at ha-dechak*, extenuating circumstances, it may be relied upon even against the weight of the majority. The critical variable is precisely the distinction between an opinion that has been formally overruled and one that has simply been passed over in practice. Rabbi Eliezer's position was squarely in the latter category.

This generates an ongoing and consequential tension in *pesak halacha*: at what point does an opinion become so definitively rejected — whether by explicit ruling or by sufficiently overwhelming consensus — that it may no longer serve as a *sha'at ha-dechak* fallback? The same question arises in later *halachic* literature with respect to the *Shulchan Aruch* and the *Rema*. When the *Shulchan Aruch* quotes a position under *yesh omrim* and then rules against it, does that position remain a viable *da'at yachid* for emergencies? The *gemara*'s treatment of Rabbi Eliezer's ruling here is a foundational moment in thinking through that question.

### Tosafot Explanation

*Tosafot* raises an obvious difficulty: if the larger *sha'at ha-dechak* of a famine was itself sufficient for even the majority sages to be lenient (as the *Heitalu* story implies), then what exactly was Rabbi's error? Why should a case of comparable emergency require reliance on Rabbi Eliezer at all?

*Tosafot*'s answer is striking. The *sha'at ha-dechak* in Rabbi's case was not the recipient's distress but Rabbi's own inconvenience. He had issued the ruling and the person had already left, residing several towns away. Retracting and traveling to reverse himself would have been a considerable burden. That — the practical difficulty of self-correction — was the *sha'at ha-dechak* that Rabbi invoked. This explanation accounts for why a more serious *sha'at ha-dechak* might be handled by the majority sages without recourse to Rabbi Eliezer at all, while a comparatively minor personal inconvenience could still invoke the *da'at yachid* principle. The threshold for *sha'at ha-dechak* is contextual, not absolute.

## Young Girl Bleeding

### First Two Sightings

The *gemara* now turns to the opposite end of the life cycle: *tinoket shelo higia zemanah lirot* — a young girl who has not yet reached the age of puberty, who nonetheless bleeds. The rule for such a girl is articulated in stages.

The first time she bleeds: *dayah sha'atah* — no retroactive concern. It is treated as a total fluke. Even after puberty, the first bleeding of a woman who has been *mesulakat damim* is *dayah sha'atah*, and all the more so for a girl who by physiological expectation should not yet be bleeding at all.

The second time: still *dayah sha'atah*. Even having bled once, a girl of ten years old who bleeds a second time has not yet established that she is menstruating. Normally, a woman who has bled once is presumed to have left her *mesulakat damim* status and is subject to greater concern thereafter. But because this girl is below the age of puberty, even the second occurrence is treated as an isolated incident.

### Third Sighting

The third time: *harei hi ke-chol ha-nashim* — she is now treated like all other women — *metamei me-et le-et umipekida li-fekida*. She is concerned retroactively twenty-four hours or since her last examination. Three occurrences, even in a pre-pubescent girl, establish that this is not a fluke. She is bleeding, however anomalously early, and *halacha* responds accordingly.

The structure here reflects a debate elsewhere in the *gemara* between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel over how many repetitions constitute a *chazakah*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires three; Rabbi holds that two suffice to establish a pattern. The *gemara* will later note that the assumption underlying this *baraita* — that the third bleeding generates a twenty-four-hour retroactive concern — implies that the *pattern* was established already by the second bleeding, and it is that established pattern which causes the third bleeding to be treated as regular. This is consistent with Rabbi's view that two repetitions create a *chazakah*.

### Pattern Recovery

Now the *baraita* continues: *avru aleha shalosh onot* — ninety days pass after those three bleedings without any further bleeding. The same principle we saw with the elderly woman applies here. This ten-year-old girl, having gone ninety days without bleeding — and here the *gemara* will later note this operates according to Rabbi Eliezer's position that ninety days re-establishes *mesulakat damim* status for any woman — is again considered *mesulakat damim*. When she then bleeds, *dayah sha'atah* applies. It is as if there has been a reboot.

Another ninety days elapse: again *mesulakat damim*, again *dayah sha'atah* upon bleeding. Another ninety days — and now, as with the elderly woman, three repetitions of ninety-day gaps followed by bleeding establish a pattern. She is no longer *mesulakat damim*; she is a woman with very long cycles, and the retroactive twenty-four-hour concern resumes: *harei hi ke-chol ha-nashim umetamei me-et le-et umipekida li-fekida*.

The parallel to the post-menopausal woman is exact: three ninety-day gaps prove a pattern, not cessation.

## Reboot Status Debate

### Reboot Question

Having established that a ninety-day gap produces a *dayah sha'atah* reboot, the *gemara* now poses a more delicate question. The scenario involves the same young girl who bled three times and is now subject to twenty-four-hour retroactive concern. A ninety-day gap then occurs, resetting her to *mesulakat damim*. Now she bleeds again. We have established that this bleeding is *dayah sha'atah*. But what is her precise status going forward?

Here is the crux of the problem. Before the ninety-day gap, this girl had bled three times and was treated *ke-chol ha-nashim* — like an ordinary menstruating woman. The question is: after the reboot, has her status reverted entirely to that of a pre-pubescent girl (i.e., one who has never yet established that she bleeds), or does she retain some residual status as someone who has already demonstrated that she bleeds?

The practical stakes are significant. A girl who is treated as pre-pubescent requires three bleedings before she is subject to the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern. A girl at the age of puberty — ready to bleed — is subject to that concern already by the second bleeding. After the ninety-day reboot, if the first bleeding is *dayah sha'atah*, what happens when she bleeds again at thirty days? Is that second bleeding already a cause for twenty-four-hour retroactive concern, as it would be for a girl at the age of puberty? Or does she require a third bleeding, as a pre-pubescent girl would?

### Complete Reboot

*Amar Rav Gidel amar Rav*: the reboot is complete. *Pa'am rishonah u-sheniyah dayah sha'atah, shelishit metamei me-et le-et umipekida li-fekida* — after the ninety-day gap, the first and second bleedings are both *dayah sha'atah*; only the third bleeding triggers the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern. In other words, the girl has returned entirely to pre-pubescent status, as if the earlier pattern never existed. Just as she originally required three bleedings to establish regularity, so too after the reboot she requires three.

The logic, as Rav articulates it, is that the ninety-day gap is not merely a temporary interruption. It constitutes genuine counter-evidence that neutralizes the earlier pattern. The earlier three bleedings proved she was bleeding; the ninety-day gap proved she had stopped. The two pieces of evidence balance each other out, and in the resulting equilibrium the formal rule reasserts itself: she is treated as a pre-pubescent girl whose bleeding status is undetermined.

One might object — and the *gemara* acknowledges this implicitly — that this seems biologically artificial. In fact, irregular menstrual patterns in early adolescence are entirely normal, and a ninety-day gap after an initial series of bleedings is not particularly surprising physiologically. Whether the *gemara* was unaware of this biological regularity or was applying a formal legal framework that operates independently of biological expectation is a question worth considering. Both possibilities are live.

### Full vs. Partial

The *gemara* now pushes the question further: *ve-od ra'ata gimmel onot u-firsa* — she has now bled twice more after the reboot (with *dayah sha'atah* for both, as Rav Gidel ruled) and now a second ninety-day gap occurs. She bleeds again. Is *this* reboot also complete — such that she once again requires three bleedings before the twenty-four-hour concern — or has the accumulation of evidence (three initial bleedings, then two more after the first reboot) established something more durable?

### Limited Reboot

*Amar Rav Kahana amar Rav Gidel amar Rav*: the second reboot is limited, not complete. *Pa'am rishonah dayah sha'atah, sheniyah metamei me-et le-et umipekida li-fekida* — after the second ninety-day gap, the first bleeding is *dayah sha'atah*, but the second bleeding already triggers the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern.

After this second reboot, the girl is no longer treated as pre-pubescent. She is treated as a girl who is at the age of puberty and expected to bleed — for whom the second bleeding, not the third, marks the onset of the twenty-four-hour concern. The first ninety-day gap could fully neutralize the initial pattern of three bleedings. But once she has bled again after that first reboot — and then bled again after the second ninety-day gap — the cumulative weight of evidence is too strong to permit a full reversion to pre-pubescent status. The reboot is partial: it restores *dayah sha'atah* for the first bleeding, but not the full three-bleeding threshold of the pre-pubescent category.

## Pattern Attribution

### Rabbi Two Sightings

With the substantive *halacha* of the reboot established, the *gemara* turns to a question of attribution. The structure of the ruling — specifically the fact that it is the *third* bleeding (in the full-reboot scenario) that triggers the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern, not the third bleeding itself as a first instance — implies that a pattern was already established by the *second* bleeding. The third is treated as the realization of an established pattern. This presupposes that two bleedings suffice to establish a *chazakah*. As noted, this is the position of Rabbi (*Rebbi*), who holds *be-trei zimnei havei chazakah*.

### Attribution Mix

This raises a difficulty. The end of the same *baraita* states that even a girl who has reached the age of puberty and begun bleeding returns to *dayah sha'atah* status after a ninety-day gap. That ruling — that a ninety-day gap makes any woman *mesulakat damim* — is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, as we established earlier. How can a single *baraita* simultaneously reflect the view of Rabbi (two bleedings establish a pattern) and the view of Rabbi Eliezer (ninety-day gap applies universally)?

### Dual Attribution

The *gemara* resolves this with a precise bifurcation: *ela Rabbi Eliezer hi, u-vi-zmanim savar lah ke-Rebbi* — the author of the *baraita* is Rabbi Eliezer, whose position governs the ninety-day reboot; but when it comes to the number of bleedings required to establish a pattern, Rabbi Eliezer agrees with Rabbi that two suffice. The *baraita* thus represents Rabbi Eliezer's comprehensive position, incorporating Rabbi's principle on pattern establishment. No internal contradiction remains.

## Stains & Purity

### Stain 1-2

The *gemara* now shifts to the question of *kesamim* — bloodstains — found on a garment or surface, in the context of the pre-pubescent girl. The principles governing *kesamim* differ from those governing actual sightings of blood, because with a stain there is inherent uncertainty: the blood may have come from the woman, or it may have come from any number of external sources. In the environment the *gemara* presupposes — where women lived in proximity to slaughtered animals, unsanitary conditions, and various other sources of blood — a stain on an inner garment layer did not automatically implicate the woman.

For a stain found between the first and second bleeding of a pre-pubescent girl: *tahor* — unconditionally pure. At this stage, her two bleedings have been treated as flukes, and we attribute no regularity to her at all. The stain is simply not assigned to her.

### Stain 2-3 Debate

The question becomes more interesting when the stain is found between the second and third bleeding. Here, a *machlokes* is recorded between Chizkiyah and Rabbi Yochanan.

### Chizkiyah Impure

Chizkiyah: *tamei*. His reasoning is *keivan de-ilu chazya mitamya* — since were she now to bleed a third time, that bleeding would be treated as *tamei* and subject to the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern (because the pattern is already established by two bleedings, following Rabbi's view), so too her stain should now be treated as *tamei*. The potential for regular status is already present, and it casts its shadow onto the stain.

### Rabbi Yochanan Pure

Rabbi Yochanan: *tahor*. His reasoning: *keivan de-lo itchazka be-dam* — since she has not yet actually had her status treated as that of a regular menstruant through actual bleeding, we do not yet apply that status to the stain. The pattern has been established in potential by two bleedings, but it has not yet been actualized. Until actual blood triggers the full *halachic* status of regular menstruation, the stain remains *tahor*.

*Rashi* notes that this is consistent with Rabbi's two-sighting principle: yes, the pattern is established by two bleedings, but it is only *applied* — given practical *halachic* effect — at the third actual sighting. The stain, found between the second and third sighting, has not yet been swept into that applied pattern.

One might ask: is there not a parallel to the case of a *betulah* — a virgin — whose post-wedding hymenal stains are treated as *tahor*? Rabbi Ila raises precisely this comparison. If Chizkiyah treats the pre-pubescent girl's stain as *tamei* between two and three, how is this different from the virgin's stains, which are treated as *tahor* even though she is certainly bleeding?

### Virgin Distinction

Rav Zeira answers: *zu tzirpa metzuyah ve-zu ein tzirpa metzuyah* — for the virgin, there is an available alternative source of blood (the hymenal flow, metaphorically described as *tzirpa*, the sap-flow), to which the stain can be attributed. Her stains can therefore be rendered *tahor* because we assign them to that alternate source. The pre-pubescent girl who has bled twice has no such alternative attribution available. Her stains must therefore be considered as coming from her, rendering them *tamei* — at least in Chizkiyah's view.

### Marketplace Stain

The *gemara* now cites a further ruling: *amar Ulla amar Rabbi Yochanan mishum Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak* — in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak, a pre-pubescent girl who has bled once and twice and whose spit (*roka*) or surface she sat on (*midrasah*) is found in the marketplace (*ba-shuk*) — those items are *tahor*. The principle of *sefeika de-reshut ha-rabim tahor* — doubt in the public domain is resolved as pure — applies, and since we do not know when she sat or spat, or whether at that moment she was *tamei*, we resolve the doubt leniently.

Rabbi Yochanan connects this ruling back to the question of the *kesem* between two and three: just as the *midras* in the marketplace is *tahor* because of the doubt, so too her *kesem* is *tahor* in that interval.

The *gemara* then notes a question about attribution: when Rabbi Yochanan cites this in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak, is this Rabbi Yochanan transmitting his *rebbe*'s view, or is it Rabbi Yochanan's own view that he attributes to his *rebbe*? The practical difference is whether we have two opinions (*Chizkiyah* vs. *Rabbi Yochanan*) or three (*Chizkiyah* vs. *Rabbi Yochanan* himself vs. *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak*), which affects the vote count in the *machlokes*.

### Rabin's Report

*Ki ata Ravin be-chol nachetei yama* — when Ravin came from Eretz Yisrael together with those who traveled from there to Bavel, they reported that the ruling of *tahor* for the *kesem* between two and three was indeed that of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak as an independent opinion distinct from Rabbi Yochanan's own. This establishes that two authorities — Rabbi Yochanan and his teacher Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak — both hold the stain to be *tahor*, against Chizkiyah's single contrary opinion of *tamei*. With two against one, the *halacha* is that the *kesem* is *tahor*.

## Continuous Bleeding

### Continuous Seven Days

*Amar Rav Hilkiya bar Tuvi*: a pre-pubescent girl who bleeds continuously for seven days — *shofas kol shiva* — this counts as only one sighting (*reia achat*), not as multiple sightings. She has not thereby established a pattern of multiple bleedings that would render her regular.

### Continuous Challenge

The *gemara* challenges this on its own terms: *afilu shofas* — you say even if she flows continuously? It would seem obvious that *continuous* bleeding is one event. The non-obvious case would be *interrupted* bleeding. The *gemara* suggests that the ruling should have been stated about the interrupted case rather than the continuous one, since it is the interrupted case where one might have thought multiple sightings were accumulating.

### Interrupted Multiple

*Adarabba* — in fact, the *gemara* says, interrupted bleeding (*poseskes*) — bleeding that stops and resumes — should be considered *multiple* sightings. If a girl bleeds on day one, stops on day two, bleeds again on day three, stops on day four, and bleeds again on day seven, even though this all occurs within a single week, each separate episode of bleeding counts as a distinct *reia*. Three such episodes within a short period would therefore establish her as regular and subject to the twenty-four-hour retroactive concern.

This is admittedly somewhat counterintuitive from a biological standpoint. The stopping and starting is part of a single menstrual event, and it might seem odd that its episodic character makes it count as multiple separate sightings establishing regularity. Yet the *gemara* applies the rule formally: separate visible episodes of bleeding, with gaps between them, are distinct *re'iyot* for these purposes.

### Dripping Definition

*Amar Rav Shimi bar Hiyya* introduces a further distinction: *medalefet eina ke-shofas* — a woman who is merely dripping (*medalefet*), as opposed to flowing, is not treated as one who is flowing continuously (*shofes*). Rather, *eina ela ke-poseskes* — she is treated as one whose bleeding is interrupted.

The implication is that continuous flow that would constitute a single *reia achat* requires actual sustained flow, not a gradual trickle. If the blood is merely dripping — slowly and intermittently, even if without discrete stops — it is functionally equivalent to interrupted bleeding, and each episode of dripping can count toward establishing a pattern of multiple *re'iyot*.

The *gemara* gently notes the implicit absurdity of the alternative: would a girl have to flow *ke-nahar* — like a river — for it to count as a single continuous sighting? That extreme is obviously not the intent. The clarification is that what counts as *shofas* (continuous flow amounting to one sighting) is actual sustained flow, while dripping or gradual seepage falls into the category of interrupted bleeding that counts as multiple sightings.

## General Presumptions

### Pre-Pub Presumed Pure

The daf closes with a broad *baraita* establishing general presumptions: *tanu rabbanan chazakah bnot Yisrael ad shelo higiu le-firkam harei hen be-chezkat tahara* — girls of Israel who have not yet reached the age of puberty are presumed to be in a state of *tahara*. No one needs to check them. They are not expected to bleed, and absent specific evidence to the contrary, *tahara* is assumed.

### Post-Pub Risk Status

Once girls reach puberty — *mi-she-higiu le-firkam harei hen be-chezkat tumah* — they enter a presumptive state of concern. This does not mean they are definitively *tamei*, but it means that menstruation is expected and must be accounted for. Other women assist in checking them — *ve-nashim bodkot osan* — because the onset of menstruation, combined with the obligations of *bedikah* before handling *terumah* or before marital relations, meant that a girl newly arrived at puberty required ongoing monitoring.

### No Manual Check

*Rabbi Yehuda omer ein bodkin osa be-yad* — Rabbi Yehuda prohibits manual internal examination, because *mipnei she-ma'avtos osan* — such examination risks causing physical harm. The concern is twofold: the examination could rupture the hymen, or it could scratch the interior and cause bleeding that would then complicate the assessment of her actual menstrual status.

### Oil Method

Instead, Rabbi Yehuda recommends an alternative method: *sachos osan be-shemen* — they rub her with oil, including internally, *umekanchos osan mi-bachutz* — and wipe externally. Through this approach, *ve-hayn nizakot me-alehen* — if she is ready to bleed, evidence of bleeding will become apparent through the process without the risks associated with a direct manual examination.
