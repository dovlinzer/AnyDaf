# Keritot 14 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Multiple Chataos

### Four + One Overview

The *mishnah* at the bottom of 13b continues the broader discussion of how multiple *chatosos* arise from a single act of eating. Earlier in the tractate we considered cases where eating multiple *kezaysim* over time, or eating different types of forbidden food, generates multiple *chatosos*. Now the *mishnah* presents the reverse scenario: a single *kezayis* that carries within it four distinct sin offerings and one *asham*.

The *mishnah* closely parallels the discussion in Makkos, where the question is how many sets of lashes one can incur from a single forbidden act. Here, in *Kerisot*, the parallel question concerns *chatosos* brought *beshogeg* — unintentionally.

### Fat/Impure/Leftover/Kods…

The *mishnah* states: *yesh ochel achilah achas vechayav aleha arba chatos ve'asham echad* — there is one who eats one eating and is liable for four sin offerings and one guilt offering. The case is constructed as follows. A *tamei* person eats *cheilev* — forbidden fat. The fat is simultaneously *nosar* — leftover sacrifice — which itself carries *kares*, adding a second sin offering. The food is *min hamukdashin*, sanctified sacrificial meat, and since a *tamei* person who eats *kodshim* is liable for an *oleh veyored* offering, that is a third. The eating takes place on *Yom Kippur*, generating a fourth *kares* obligation and thus a fourth *chatas*. The *asham* arises from *me'ilah*: since the fat always goes upon the *mizbe'ach* and no person is permitted to eat it, there is a transgression of deriving benefit from sanctified property, which carries an *asham me'ilah*.

### Rabbi Meir's Sixth

*Rabbi Meir* adds: if it happened to be *Shabbos* and the person carried the food out into the public domain while eating, he is liable for yet another offering. The Sages respond that this additional liability stems not from the act of eating but from carrying — it is a separate act, not the eating itself. The respondents' point is that the *mishnah* is specifically enumerating how many obligations can flow from the act of eating alone; a simultaneous but distinct act, such as carrying or lighting a fire, is not part of that count.

## Issur Chal Issur

### Can Forbiddance Stack

The *gemara* frames the entire *mishnah* around the question of *issur chal al issur* — the principle that once an object is forbidden in one way, a second prohibition generally cannot take effect upon it. The object's forbidden status is already defined, and it cannot be multiply defined. This is fundamentally an object-based principle: it does not apply when one is simultaneously performing two separate forbidden acts, but rather when a single object already carries a prohibition and a new prohibition seeks to attach to it.

The *gemara* notes that *Rabbi Meir* is the named *Tanna* in the *mishnah*, and asks: does this *mishnah* reflect *Rabbi Meir*'s view that *issur chal al issur* does apply — that prohibitions can stack upon a single object? The *gemara* answers: no. In general, *Rabbi Meir* does not hold of *issur chal al issur*. However, he does accept the principles of *issur kolel* and *issur mosif*, which are recognized exceptions to the rule.

### Three Exceptions

There are three concepts that permit a second prohibition to take effect upon an already-forbidden object.

The first is *issur kolel* — the new prohibition encompasses a broader set of objects. When the new *issur* comes into being, it simultaneously forbids many other things; and because it affects a wider range of objects, it also attaches to this one. A classic example is becoming *tamei*: when a person becomes *tamei*, all *kodshim* become forbidden to him. Since more objects have now become forbidden through this new status, that new status also attaches to an object that was already forbidden on other grounds.

The second is *issur mosif* — the new prohibition affects a broader population. The same object that was forbidden to one person, or one group, now becomes forbidden to more people. When the forbidden circle expands, the new prohibition takes effect even upon those who were already forbidden from this object on other grounds.

The third is *issur chamur* — a weightier prohibition. Here the same object becomes forbidden to the same population, but the nature of the prohibition is more severe. This category is less universally accepted, and the *gemara* notes some skepticism about whether it suffices to generate a second liable status; nonetheless, in our *sugya* the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that it does.

### Fat Becomes Kodshim

The *gemara* now works through each element of the *mishnah*. Regarding *cheilev*: originally, when one was *tahor*, the fat was forbidden only in eating. *Tahor mi-kara lo assar ela be-achilah*. Then the person sanctified it — *akdeshei*. Since sanctification adds *issur hana'ah* — the fat is now forbidden not merely to eat but to derive any benefit from — the prohibition has become weightier. *Migo de-itosef bei issur hana'ah*, since an additional prohibition of benefit-derivation has been added, *itosef bei legabei cheilev*, that additional status attaches to the fat as well. This is the case of *issur chamur*.

One might ask: since sanctifying a piece of fat necessarily involves sanctifying the entire animal of which it is part, should we not consider this *issur kolel* — a broader set of objects now becoming forbidden? This is a genuine question, but the *gemara* proceeds on the assumption that the relevant category here is *issur chamur*, treating the sanctification as primarily creating a weightier prohibition rather than one that sweeps in new objects.

### Notar Case

The *gemara* then turns to *nosar*. The fat — now *kodshim* — was originally forbidden to human beings but permitted for placement on the *mizbe'ach*: *legabei hedyot hu de-assur aval le-gavoha shari*. Then it is left overnight and becomes *nosar*. Now it is *assur legabei gavoha* as well — forbidden even to God, forbidden for the altar. *Migo de-itosaf issura legabei gavoha*, since the circle of those to whom it is forbidden has expanded to include, so to speak, the divine domain, *itosaf bei legabei hedyot* — an additional prohibition attaches to the ordinary person as well. This is *issur mosif*: a larger population is now subject to the prohibition.

### Yom Kippur Addition

The final layer is *Yom Kippur*. It must be acknowledged that *Yom Kippur* sits oddly in this framework of *issur chal al issur*, since *Yom Kippur* is not a status of the object but rather an obligation upon the person — the food is merely the vehicle through which the fast is broken. The framework of *issur chal al issur* is fundamentally object-oriented, so introducing *Yom Kippur* here is somewhat anomalous and difficult to fully explain. Nevertheless, the *gemara* proceeds: *chal aleih Yom HaKippurim* — *Yom Kippur* arrives. *Migo de-itosaf bei issura legabei chullin* — on *Yom Kippur* all foods become forbidden, not just this one. Since more objects become forbidden, that is *issur kolel*, and therefore *itosaf bei legabei gavoha* — yet another dimension of prohibition is added. The object accumulates yet another forbidden status.

In summary: *issur kolel* (more objects forbidden — e.g., becoming *tamei*, or *Yom Kippur*), *issur mosif* (a broader population is forbidden — e.g., when the fat becomes *nosar*), and *issur chamur* (a weightier prohibition — e.g., the fat becoming *kodshim*) are the recognized exceptions to *issur chal al issur*. These are what allow the multiple *chatosos* of the *mishnah* to be intelligible.

## Same Object Question

### Five Chataos Challenge

The *gemara* poses a challenge: rather than four *chatosos* and one *asham*, why not enumerate five *chatosos*? If in the same eating one also consumed a *kezayis* of *piggul* — sacrificial meat slaughtered with the wrong intention regarding the time of eating, which also carries *kares* — that would add a fifth. The proposed formulation is: *v'nisni hei chatos, v'nukma kegon d'achal kezayis piggul b'chada kezayis piggul*.

This question is immediately striking, because *piggul* and *nosar* cannot coexist in the same piece of meat. *Nosar* is meat that was fit for the altar but was left over; *piggul* is meat that was rendered unfit through improper intent at slaughter. Since *piggul* renders the sacrifice unfit, it cannot subsequently become *nosar*. Therefore the *gemara* acknowledges that the *piggul* would have to be a separate *kezayis* from a separate animal. But once we allow separate objects into the same act of eating, one could equally ask: why not also include a *kezayis* of blood, or *chametz on Pesach*, and so on indefinitely? The point the *gemara* is pressing toward is that the *mishnah* is discussing a single object, not multiple objects.

### One Limb/One Kezayit

The *gemara* establishes a series of narrowing constraints. First: *b'chada beheima ka'amri* — we are speaking of one animal. Since *piggul* and *nosar* cannot coexist in the same animal, that rules out combining them across two animals. The *gemara* then asks: perhaps one limb of a single animal was placed on the altar — the altar contact causes the limb to lose its *piggul* status, as stated by *Ulla*: *kometz piggul she'he'ela legabei mizbeiach, paka piggulei mimenu v'havei lei nosar* — a *piggul kometz* placed on the altar loses its *piggul* status and becomes *nosar* if not consumed in time. If such a limb then fell off the altar, it would be *nosar*, while the remainder of the animal that was never placed on the altar would remain *piggul*. Eating one *kezayis* from each would, it seems, yield both prohibitions from one animal.

The *gemara* closes this gap: *b'eiver echad ka'amri b'shnei evarim lo ka'amri* — we are speaking of one limb, not two. Could half a limb rest on the altar while the other half hangs outside, yielding *nosar* in one half and *piggul* in the other? The *gemara* rejects this: one follows the majority of the limb. If the majority is on the altar, the entire limb is treated as on the altar; if the majority is outside, the entire limb is treated as outside. The *gemara* adds that this resolution would in fact settle an open question of *Rami bar Chama* — whether a limb is defined by the majority of its placement — though since *Rami bar Chama* left this as an unresolved inquiry, one cannot simply invoke it as settled.

The narrowing continues to its final form: *bekezayis echad kama'iri bishnei zeitim lo kama'iri* — we are speaking of a single *kezayis*, the minimum unit of forbidden food. If one wishes to incorporate additional prohibitions, that requires an additional *kezayis*, which is a separate act.

A complication is then raised: *Yom Kippur* is not actually violated by eating a *kezayis*. The minimum measure for *Yom Kippur* is a *kosevet* — approximately the volume of a pressed date — which is larger than a *kezayis* and contains at least two *kezaysim*. If the *mishnah*'s case inherently involves two *kezaysim* for the *Yom Kippur* prohibition to apply, should we not be able to assign one of those *kezaysim* to an additional prohibition such as *piggul*?

Three answers are offered. *Rav Zevira* says: *kegon de'achal kulya bechelva* — we are speaking of eating a kidney together with its surrounding fat. The *cheilev* is one *kezayis* and carries all the listed prohibitions; the kidney brings the total volume to that of a *kosevet* for *Yom Kippur*, but the kidney is not itself a separate forbidden item that generates a new *chatas*.

*Rav Pappa* offers a different resolution: *kegon d'maliye b'tamrei* — perhaps the person had already eaten a *kezayis* of dates beforehand. The current single *kezayis* of fat is sufficient to complete the *Yom Kippur* prohibition in combination with what was eaten moments earlier, yet the current act consists of only one *kezayis*.

*Rav Adda bar Ahava* actually preserves a different version of the *mishnah* that reads five *chatosos*, incorporating both a *kezayis* of *piggul* and a *kezayis* of *nosar* through the mechanism of a limb partially placed on the altar (as described above), without recourse to the previous explanations.

### Beth Habliah Limit

The *gemara* then asks why the count stops where it does. If we are willing to incorporate a second *kezayis* in the mouth alongside the first, why not also add a *kezayis* of blood, yielding six? The *gemara* answers: *v'shi'uru chachamim d'ein beit habli'ah machazik yoter mishnei zeisim* — the Sages measured that the *beit habli'ah*, the capacity of the throat for a single swallow, cannot accommodate more than two *kezaysim*. Since eating requires swallowing, a single act of eating is bounded by this anatomical limit. No matter how much one chews simultaneously, the maximum that can constitute one *achilah* is two *kezaysim*.

## Shabbat Carrying Debate

### Meir's Shabbat Idea

The *gemara* returns to *Rabbi Meir*'s addition: if the person was carrying the food out on *Shabbos*, he incurs an additional *chatas* for *hotza'ah*. The *gemara* seeks to understand the precise formulation — why *Rabbi Meir* specifies *Shabbos* rather than simply stating that carrying in the course of the event adds another obligation.

### Why Specify Shabbat

The *gemara* asks: *mai ta'ama d'katani hayta Shabbos* — what is the reason the *mishnah* qualifies the case specifically as *Shabbos*? The scenario already involves *Yom Kippur*, and *Yom Kippur* presumably also prohibits carrying. If so, *Rabbi Meir* could have simply said: if he also carried, he is liable. Why add the specification that it was *Shabbos*? If carrying on *Yom Kippur* is already forbidden, the *Shabbos* element is redundant.

### Carrying Weakness

To understand the *gemara*'s analysis, some background is necessary. The *melachah* of *hotza'ah* — carrying from one domain to another — is traditionally described by the *Rishonim* as a *melachah geru'ah*, a deficient or weak *melachah*. Its distinguishing feature is that it is non-productive and non-creative; it merely transfers an object from one place to another. This is in contrast to every other *melachah* on *Shabbos*, each of which involves some form of creative or transformative act.

This weakness of *hotza'ah* may explain why carrying is treated so permissively on *Yom Tov*. We instinctively think of carrying as simply permitted on *Yom Tov*, not merely permitted for the sake of food preparation. One could argue that carrying's permission on *Yom Tov* is not rooted in *ochel nefesh* — the license to perform *melachos* necessary for food — but rather reflects a categorical permissibility arising from its status as the weakest of all *melachos*: where *Shabbos* restrictions do not fully apply, *hotza'ah* is the first to fall away.

### Carrying Permitted

*Rafram* draws an audacious conclusion from *Rabbi Meir*'s choice of language. He says: *zos omeres eiruv v'hotza'ah l'Shabbos v'ein eiruv v'hotza'ah l'Yom HaKippurim* — the reason *Rabbi Meir* had to specify *Shabbos* rather than simply invoking *Yom Kippur* is that there is in fact no biblical prohibition of *hotza'ah* on *Yom Kippur*. Carrying is not forbidden on *Yom Kippur* at the *d'Oraisa* level, only on *Shabbos*. Hence *Rabbi Meir* needed to specify that it was *Shabbos* — because only then does carrying generate a biblical *chatas*.

*Rafram* finds support for this position in a *beraisa* discussing the *se'ir l'Azazel* — the scapegoat sent out on *Yom Kippur*. The verse states *v'shilach b'yad ish iti* — the goat shall be sent by a ready man. The *beraisa* derives from this that any man may lead it out, even a *zar*, even someone who is *tamei*, and — crucially — even on *Shabbos*. *Rafram* asks: why does the *beraisa* need to tell us it may be done even on *Shabbos*? If carrying were already prohibited on *Yom Kippur*, the *beraisa* need only say it overrides *Yom Kippur* restrictions. The fact that the *beraisa* adds *afilu b'Shabbos* implies that *Yom Kippur* itself does not prohibit carrying; only *Shabbos* does. Therefore the novelty of *afilu b'Shabbos* is real: it tells us the *Shabbos* prohibition of *hotza'ah* is overridden.

### Rejection of Rafram

The *gemara* rejects *Rafram*'s argument. *Shabbos shani se'ir mishtale'ach d'achshar b'Yom HaKippurim bekach* — the *se'ir l'Azazel* is different, because it is part of the *avodah* of *Yom Kippur* itself. It is natural that *Yom Kippur* restrictions would be overridden for the sake of the *Yom Kippur* service; it is not at all obvious that *Shabbos* restrictions would similarly yield. The *beraisa*'s inclusion of *afilu b'Shabbos* is therefore significant precisely because it is not self-evident — *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbos* are independent systems of prohibition, and the license for the *Yom Kippur avodah* does not automatically extend to *Shabbos* prohibitions. This does not prove that carrying is permitted on *Yom Kippur* absent the *avodah*; it proves only that the *beraisa* needed to explicitly authorize overriding *Shabbos* prohibitions for the sake of the *mitzvas hayom*.

The *gemara* concludes bluntly: *ella d'Rafram brusi* — *Rafram*'s statement is dismissed as unfounded. Carrying is indeed forbidden on *Yom Kippur*. The more interesting observation, however, is what the *gemara*'s *hava amina* reveals: that the permissibility of carrying on *Yom Tov* may be grounded not in *ochel nefesh* but in the categorical weakness of *hotza'ah* as a *melachah*. On that basis, one could imagine a world in which *Yom Kippur* — despite sharing many *Shabbos*-like characteristics — might not prohibit carrying, since *Yom Kippur* is not *Shabbos* per se. The *gemara* completely rejects this, noting that *Yom Kippur* is called *Shabbos Shabbason*, but the *hava amina* itself is not entirely without conceptual foundation.

## Sex Prohibitions

### Six from One Act

The *mishnah* now transitions from eating to the domain of prohibited sexual relations. Just as a single act of eating can generate multiple *chatosos*, so too a single act of intercourse can generate six sin offerings: *yesh biah achas vechayav aleha shesh chatos*.

### Daughter/Sister Case

The first case: *haba al bito* — a man who had intercourse with his daughter. The *mishnah* enumerates the prohibitions: *mishum bito*, as his daughter; *ve'achot ishto*, his wife's sister; *ve'achoto*, his sister; *eshet achiv*, his brother's wife; *ve'eshet achi aviv*, his uncle's wife; *ve'eshet ish*, a married woman; and *veniddah*, a menstruant.

How can a single woman be simultaneously his daughter and his sister? The scenario requires that this man previously had intercourse with his mother and the result was a daughter. That girl is therefore both his daughter and the daughter of his mother — his sister. The act with the mother is not counted here because it was a prior, separate act.

The *gemara* addresses *Rabbi Meir*'s implicit agreement with this *mishnah* in light of the principle of *issur chal al issur*. The fact that the woman is forbidden both as daughter and as sister does not raise an *issur chal al issur* problem because both prohibitions took effect simultaneously at her birth — and the principle applies only when prohibitions are sequential, not when they arise at the same moment.

Subsequent prohibitions are explained through *issur mosif*. When she married his brother, she became forbidden not only to him but to all of his brothers — *niset le-achiv, migo de-itosaf issur legabei achiv, itosaf issura legabei didei*. The expanded circle of those who became forbidden is *issur mosif*. When she then married his uncle (*niset le-achi aviv*), she became forbidden to all of his uncle's brothers as well — again, a larger population. *Migo de-itosaf issura legabei she'ar achi aviv, itosaf legabei didei*. When she became a married woman, she became forbidden to all men in the world who are not her husband — the maximum possible expansion of the forbidden population. When she became a *niddah*, she became forbidden even to her husband, the one person who had been permitted to her — *migo de-itosaf issur legabei ba'alah, itosaf nami legabei didei*. Each stage represents a genuine *issur mosif*, permitting the additional prohibition to take effect.

### Granddaughter Case

The next *mishnah*: *haba al bat bito* — a man who had intercourse with his granddaughter. The prohibitions enumerated are: *mishum bat bito*, his granddaughter; *ve-chalato*, his daughter-in-law; *ve-eshet achiv*, his brother's wife; *ve-eshet achi aviv*, his uncle's wife; *ve-achot ishto* (noted as a more complex case); and *ve-eshet ish* and *veniddah*.

How can his granddaughter also be his daughter-in-law? If the granddaughter is through his daughter, then she is the daughter's daughter — his son's niece. His son is permitted to marry his own niece. If the son did marry her, she is simultaneously the man's granddaughter and his daughter-in-law.

How can she be his brother's wife? After the son divorced her, she remained forbidden as a daughter-in-law; nevertheless, his brother (her grand-uncle by marriage) can marry her since she is his niece. The brother then divorced her or died, leaving the prohibition of his brother's wife.

How can she also be his uncle's wife? After the brother, his uncle can marry her — she is his uncle's grand-niece — and the uncle's death or divorce adds *eshet achi aviv* to the list.

The *achot ishto* case — that she is also his wife's sister — involves a more involved generational construction that requires a detailed chart to trace clearly, which we set aside here.

### Father Marriage Case

*Rabbi Yossi* adds: *im avar zaken venasa chayav aleha mishum eshet av* — if this man's father went ahead and married the granddaughter, then she is also forbidden as *eshet av* — his father's wife.

The immediate question is how this can be a binding marriage. The granddaughter is the father's great-granddaughter. Marrying one's great-granddaughter would seem to be a prohibited union (*arayos*), which would render the *kiddushin* void. *Rabbi Yochanan* resolves this: *kegon shenafelu lo leyibbum* — the situation is that this woman came before the father for *yibbum*. One of the scenarios in the *mishnah* was that she had married the father's brother — the man's uncle — and the uncle died childless. The father is therefore obligated in *yibbum*. The *gemara* then asks: but the father would still be transgressing in marrying his great-granddaughter, even through *yibbum*. Why does the *mishnah* say he transgressed (*avar*)?

The answer lies in a *beraisa*: *kallato ervah, kallat bno shniyah* — his own daughter-in-law is a full *ervah*, but his son's daughter-in-law is only a secondary *ervah*, a *shniyah* — a rabbinic prohibition. Similarly, the *beraisa* extends: *v'chen atah motzei b'vat b'no u'vat bat b'no ad sof kol hadorot* — a granddaughter and any further descendant is forbidden only rabbinically, not biblically. This great-granddaughter is thus only rabbinically prohibited to the father. Similarly, the marriage was possible because the uncle who died was the father's brother, making this woman related to the father as *kallat bno* (his son's daughter-in-law, in the generational sense) — also only a rabbinic prohibition. Since both the prohibition of *bat bno* at this generational remove and the prohibition of *kallat bno* at this generational remove are rabbinic, the marriage was binding at the biblical level, even though the father transgressed rabbinic prohibitions in contracting it. Thus the marriage is valid, and she becomes forbidden to the man as *eshet av*.

## Rabbi Yossi Position

### Multiple Penalties

A different *mishnah* teaches: *avar aveirah sheyesh bah shtei mitot nidon b'chamurah* — if someone committed an offense carrying two death penalties, he is judged by the more severe one. The four forms of capital punishment — *skilah*, *sreifah*, *hereg*, *chenek* — are graded in severity, and when a single act generates two of them, the more severe is applied.

### Yossi Rejects Stacking

*Rabbi Yossi* disagrees: *nidon b'zikah rishonah sheba'ah lo* — he is judged by the first prohibition that attached to this person. The underlying logic is that *Rabbi Yossi* holds *ein issur chal al issur* — the second prohibition never took effect, and therefore only the first remains operative.

The *beraisa* illustrates: *chamoto v'na'asit eshet ish* — his mother-in-law who subsequently became a married woman. Initially she was forbidden only as his mother-in-law. Then she remarried and became forbidden also as a *married woman*. The Sages say the *issur* of *eshet ish* takes effect — it is *issur mosif*, since she is now forbidden to the broader world — and since *eshet ish* is punished with *chenek* while *chamoto* receives *sreifah*, one gives the more severe, *sreifah*. *Rabbi Yossi* says: the *issur* of *eshet ish* never took effect. She is forbidden only as *chamoto*, and the punishment is *sreifah*.

### Yossi Agrees Mosif

This appears to establish that *Rabbi Yossi* categorically rejects *issur chal al issur* even in cases of *issur mosif*. Yet the *gemara* records that *Rabbi Abbahu* teaches: *modeh Rabbi Yossi be-issur mosif* — *Rabbi Yossi* actually does agree when the new *issur* is an *issur mosif*. Similarly, *Ravin* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *modeh Rabbi Yossi be-issur mosif*.

This is a striking *gemara*. If *Rabbi Yossi* agrees to *issur mosif*, how does it follow that in the mother-in-law case he says the second prohibition does not take effect? The mother-in-law becoming a married woman is precisely *issur mosif* — a broader population becomes forbidden to her.

### First Prohibition Wins

The resolution is elegant. *Rabbi Yossi* agrees that when an *issur mosif* occurs, the second prohibition does take effect. But the practical outcome will always be that the first prohibition's penalty is what is applied. Why? Consider the two possible sequences.

If the first prohibition was *chamoto* (*sreifah*) and then she became *eshet ish* (*chenek*): *eshet ish* is not *issur mosif* — she was already forbidden to everyone as *chamoto*, and becoming *eshet ish* does not expand the circle of those for whom she is forbidden. The second prohibition does not take effect. One is judged by *chamoto*, the first — and it is also the more severe.

If the first prohibition was *eshet ish* (*chenek*) and then she became *chamoto* (*sreifah*): here *sreifah* would be *issur mosif* — becoming forbidden as a mother-in-law forbids her to a new class of people (the son-in-law). *Rabbi Yossi* agrees this takes effect. But the first prohibition was *eshet ish*, and *Rabbi Yossi* says one is judged by the first prohibition. So one applies *chenek*, the lighter penalty.

In all scenarios, it works out that the first prohibition is the operative one. *Rabbi Yossi*'s rule — *nidon b'zikah rishonah* — always yields the first prohibition, and it is internally consistent with his agreement to *issur mosif*, because when *issur mosif* applies, the second prohibition is in fact weaker (having the lighter penalty), and the first still prevails.

### Father Example

The *gemara* asks: what is the concrete example of *issur mosif* in the *mishnah*'s case where *Rabbi Yossi* adds the prohibition of *eshet av* when the grandfather marries the granddaughter?

The answer is: *de-ika bra le-saba* — the man in question also has a son. When the grandfather marries the granddaughter, she becomes forbidden as *eshet av* not only to this man but also to his son, to whom she is now his grandfather's wife (which falls under the broad *issur* of *eshet av* extending to all preceding generations). Since there is now a new person — the son — to whom she has become forbidden, this constitutes *issur mosif*. *Migo de-itosaf issura legabei brei, itosaf issura legabei didei* — since the prohibition expanded to include the son as well, the prohibition also attaches in a new dimension to the man himself.

Specifically: for this man, the two *issurim* now operative are *bat bno* (his granddaughter) and *eshet av* (his father's wife). For his son, the two *issurim* are *bat ach* (his brother's daughter, since she is the son's niece through the other branch) and *eshet av* (his grandfather's wife). The *issur* of *eshet av* extends through all generations in both directions, so the grandfather marrying the granddaughter creates precisely this web of interlocking prohibitions — and it is the expansion of the prohibition to the son that provides the *issur mosif* allowing the new *issur* to attach.
