# Nazir 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Safek Case Overview

### Basic Case Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָזִיר שֶׁהָיָה טָמֵא בְּסָפֵק וּמוּחְלָט בְּסָפֵק — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים אַחַר שִׁשִּׁים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a nazirite who,** on the first day of his naziriteship, **was impure** from a corpse as a matter of **uncertainty and** was also **a confirmed leper** as a matter of **uncertainty,** i.e., it was uncertain whether or not he had leprosy, how can he fulfill the shaving obligations of a pure nazirite and an impure leper? The problem facing this nazirite is that a leper must shave both when he begins his purification process and at the close of it, one week later. However, a nazirite is prohibited from shaving. Additionally, a leper may not partake of sacrificial food, but a nazirite may. Therefore, **he may partake of sacrificial food sixty days after** he may have become impure, when the uncertainty with regard to leprosy will have been clarified. He shaves for the first time for his leprosy after thirty days, and for the second time thirty days later, the shaving of the end of the purification process; at which point he brings the offerings of a purified leper and may partake of sacrificial food.

The *tani ala* with which this daf opens presents a case examined previously: a person who is a *safek nazir tamei* — perhaps he contracted *tumat meit* — and simultaneously a *safek metzora*. The Gemara's ruling is that he must wait 120 days in total: 60 days to exit the status of *metzora* and 60 days to exit *nezirut tumah*.

### Four Shavings Timeline

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן, וּמִטַּמֵּא לַמֵּתִים אַחַר מֵאָה וְעֶשְׂרִים יוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** **But** he **may drink wine and become impure from the dead,** effectively ending his naziriteship, only **after 120 days.** This is because he might have been a full-fledged leper, which means that his shavings count toward his leprosy, not his naziriteship. Consequently, after the first sixty days he must observe another thirty days of naziriteship and shave again. Even then he has yet to fulfill all his obligations, as he might have been impure from a corpse, which means his shaving after ninety days was for his impurity. He must therefore remain a nazirite for another thirty days, before shaving one final time at the end of 120 days to fulfill his naziriteship obligation.

The structure of that 120-day period flows from the fact that such a person will ultimately require four shavings (*giluachim*). As a *nazir metzora*, one shaves upon becoming *tamei* and again at the completion of the *tahara* process. As a *metzora*, one shaves when the *tzara'at* begins to clear and again seven days later at the end of the *tahara* process. As a *nazir tamei*, one shaves upon the onset of *tumah* and once more upon completing the *nezirut* of *tahara*. Taken together, four distinct shavings emerge. Under ordinary circumstances these could occur in rapid succession, but our case demands substantial gaps between each one.

### 30-Day Gap Rationale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁתִּגְלַחַת הַנֶּגַע דּוֹחֶה תִּגְלַחַת הַנָּזִיר בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא וַדַּאי. אֲבָל בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא סָפֵק — אֵינוֹ דּוֹחֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna notes: The reason that he cannot shave for his leprosy after seven days and perform the second shaving of a leper seven days later is **because the shaving of leprosy overrides** the prohibition of **the shaving of a nazirite** only **when his** status as **a leper** is **definite. However, when his** status as **a leper** is **uncertain,** the shaving **does not override** his naziriteship, and therefore he must wait thirty days before each of his shavings for leprosy.

The reason 30-day gaps are required is that at every stage, the individual might be a *nazir tahor* — in the midst of a standard, uninterrupted *nezirut* of *tahara* — and shaving in the middle of one's *nezirut* is prohibited by *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*. Consequently, at each juncture where a shaving might be called for, he must first wait out a full 30 days, since only then will even a *nazir tahor* have completed his term and be permitted to shave.

### Board Walkthrough

To walk through this concretely: a normal *nazir* completes 30 days and then shaves and brings a *korban*. A *nazir tamei* waits seven days, shaves and brings a *korban*, and then does 30 days of *tahara* before a second shaving and *korban* — technically day 37. A *metzora*, when the condition clears, performs the bird ritual (*tziporim*), shaves after a waiting period, and then seven days later shaves again and brings a *korban*.

Now, for our *safek* individual: he cannot begin by immediately performing any shaving, because he might be a *nazir tahor* who cannot shave mid-*nezirut*. He must first wait 30 days. After those 30 days, each possible status is satisfied: even a *nazir tamei* has long since passed his seven-day threshold; a *metzora* was always ready to shave; and a *nazir tahor* has now completed his requisite 30 days. So the first *giluach* takes place at day 30.

Following that first shaving, the *nazir tamei* scenario would ordinarily call for 30 days of *tahara* followed by a second shaving. The *metzora* scenario would ordinarily call for shaving again after just seven more days. But because our individual might be a *nazir tahor* who has just begun a new 30-day term, he cannot shave again after a mere seven days — doing so would violate a possible *nezirut tahara*. He must wait another 30 days. At day 60, the second *giluach* occurs. At this point, the *nazir tamei*-turned-*nazir tahor* scenario is complete, and the *metzora* who had his bird ritual at day 30 and his second shaving now at day 60 is also done with his *metzora* process.

However, the person who was both a *nazir tamei* and a *metzora* is not yet finished, because — and this is an important principle the Gemara will develop — the *giluach* of *metzora* does not count toward the *giluach* of *nezirut tumah*. These shavings do not double-count. So after day 60, this individual's scenario of being a *nazir tamei metzora* requires restarting: he still needs a *giluach* for *tumah* and a *giluach* for *tahara*. But he cannot immediately perform the *giluach* for *tumah* because, at this point, he might be entering a fresh *nezirut tahara* — and shaving would again violate it. So he waits yet another 30 days, arriving at day 90 for the third *giluach*, at which point he can address his *nezirut tumah*. Then, yet another 30 days must pass — since the *nazir tahor* term might be running again — until the fourth and final *giluach* at day 120, completing his *nezirut tahara*.

The principle woven throughout is that each waiting period is designed so that no possible *nezirut tahara* is violated. It is worth noting that being a *metzora* does not count toward one's *nezirut*: even a *vadai nazir metzora* must still complete a full 30 days of *nezirut* after the *metzora* period concludes.

### Year-Long Nezirut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בִּנְזִירוּת מוּעֶטֶת, אֲבָל בִּנְזִירוּת בַּת שָׁנָה — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים,
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Sage **taught: In what** case **is this statement** of the mishna **said? With regard to a short naziriteship** of thirty days. **However, with regard to a naziriteship of a year, he partakes of sacrificial food** only **after two years.** He cannot shave until a year has passed, in case he is not a leper; he may shave the second time only a year later, in case he was impure during the first year and this second year was his naziriteship observed in purity. At this stage he may partake of sacrificial meat, for if he was a full-fledged leper he has shaved twice.

This same structure applies if the *nezirut* in question was a year-long *nezirut*. Since all of the 30-day gaps exist to avoid violating a possible *nezirut tahara*, if the *nezirut* is one of a full year, those gaps must correspondingly expand to year-long intervals. The Gemara states: *binezirut bas shanah*, for a year-long *nezirut*, he may eat *kodshim* only after two years and 36 days, and may become *tamei* for the dead only after an even longer period.

## Korbonot Overview

### Permissible Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: וּמְגַלֵּחַ אַרְבַּע תִּגְלָחִיּוֹת. תִּגְלַחַת רִאשׁוֹנָה — מֵבִיא צִפּוֹרִים,
>
> **Translation:** **And it is taught with regard to** the *halakha* of the mishna (*Tosefta* 6:1): **And he shaves** with **four** separate acts of **shaving,** each time for its own reason. How so? For his **first shaving,** after the thirty days of an unspecified naziriteship or at the end of the period he vowed to observe naziriteship, he **brings** two **birds** of a leper: One is slaughtered over spring water and the other is sent away (Leviticus 14:4–7).

Turning from the timeline to the *korbanot* that accompany each stage, the question is: in a *safek* situation, which offerings can actually be brought? It is helpful to survey the relevant *korbanot* for each possible status.

For a *metzora* at stage one, the process consists of the bird ritual (*tziporim*) — one bird is slaughtered, the other is dipped and released. This is performed outside the *beit hamikdash* entirely, and since it does not constitute a formal *korban*, anyone may technically perform this ritual regardless of his status.

At stage two of *metzora*, the full *korban* normally consists of two lambs, a *chatat*, and the like. However, the Torah makes an exception for a poor person (*dal*): *v'im dal hu v'ein yado maseget*, he brings instead a *chatat ha'of* (bird *chatat*) and an *olat ha'of* (bird *olah*), along with an *asham*. The *chatat ha'of* is significant here because a *chatat ha'of* may be brought *b'safek* — the Torah permits a doubtful *chatat ha'of* in a way that is not permitted for an ordinary animal *chatat*.

For a *nazir tamei*, he too brings a *chatat ha'of* and an *olat ha'of* (again, this applies specifically if he is a *dal*, though the details may require further verification). This *chatat ha'of* can likewise be brought *b'safek*.

For a *nazir tahor* completing his *nezirut*, the standard *korban* consists of an *olah*, a *chatat*, and a *shelamim*. The *olah* can be brought conditionally (*b'tenai*): it is either his obligatory *olah* or, if it is not needed as an obligation, it counts as a *olat nedavah* — a free-will offering. The *tziporim*, as noted, are simply a ritual performed outside and carry no prohibition for anyone.

### Safek and Tenai Logic

The underlying logic is as follows. A standard *chatat* cannot be brought in a state of doubt — the rule of *safek chatat* is well established. But the Torah treats a *chatat ha'of* differently, and it may be brought even when one is uncertain whether the obligation applies. An *olah*, on the other hand, is inherently flexible: it can be either a fulfillment of an obligation or a gift — *olat nedavah*. Therefore, in any *safek* situation, an *olah* can be brought *b'tenai*: if this is my obligatory *olah*, it counts as such; if not, it is a free-will sacrifice. The *tziporim* ritual, occurring entirely outside the *mikdash*, raises no issues of bringing *kodshim* in vain — it is simply a ritual that may be performed without harm regardless of whether the obligation applies.

The reason it is necessary to bring these *korbanot* at each stage at all is that in order to advance from one stage to the next — whether from *tumah* to beginning *tahara*, or from *metzora* status to the next phase — a minimal *korban* must be brought. A *nazir tamei* cannot begin his *nezirut tahara* without bringing his minimal *korban*. A *metzora* cannot advance to the next stage without a minimal *korban*. And a *nazir tahor* cannot complete his *nezirut* without bringing his *korban* either. Since progression at each stage requires some minimal set of offerings, the *halacha* requires bringing whatever subset of *korbanot* can be legitimately offered in a *safek* context.

## Korbonot Overview (II)

### Four Stages Korbonot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף, וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה. שְׁנִיָּה — מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, שְׁלִישִׁית — מֵבִיא חַטַּאת הָעוֹף וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה, רְבִיעִית — מֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he also brings **a bird sin-offering** for his shaving of impure naziriteship, as he may have contracted impurity from a corpse, **and an animal burnt-offering** for his shaving of purity, as he may not have contracted impurity from a corpse. For his **second** shaving he **brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering.** For his **third** shaving he again **brings a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering.** And for the **fourth** shaving he **brings an offering of purity.**

The *braita* itself states: *umegalei'ach arba tiglachyot* — he shaves four shavings. At the first shaving, he brings *tziporim*, a *chatat ha'of*, and an *olat behema*. At the second shaving, a *chatat ha'of* and an *olat behema*. At the third shaving, again a *chatat ha'of* and an *olat behema*. At the fourth shaving, he brings the full *korban tahara*, with a conditional stipulation regarding the *olah*.

The rationale at each stage follows directly from which statuses are still plausible. At the first shaving, the individual might be at *metzora* stage one (requiring *tziporim*), or a *nazir tamei* (requiring a *chatat ha'of*), or a *nazir tahor* completing his *nezirut* (requiring an *olat behema*). Hence all three categories of offerings are brought.

At the second shaving, having already performed the *tziporim* at the first shaving, stage one of *metzora* is concluded — there is no longer any need to repeat that ritual. The remaining possibilities are: completing *metzora* stage two (requiring a *chatat ha'of*), or completing a *nezirut tahara* (requiring an *olat behema*). So two types of *korban* suffice.

At the third shaving, *metzora* as a category is entirely off the table — it was disposed of by the second shaving. Now the possibilities are: the individual was a *nazir tamei* who has arrived at the end of his *nezirut tumah* (requiring a *chatat ha'of*), or he is a *nazir tahor* completing his term (requiring an *olat behema*). Again, two types.

At the fourth shaving, only one status remains: *nezirut tahara*. He brings the full *korban tahara* — *olah*, *chatat*, and *shelamim* — and makes the conditional stipulation described below regarding the *olah*.

### Asham Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא בָּעֵי אָשָׁם! רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן הִיא, דְּאָמַר: מַיְיתֵי וּמַתְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** isn't a leper **required** to bring **a guilt-offering** to complete his atonement and permit him to partake of sacrificial food? The Gemara answers: **This** ruling is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon, who said** that one **may bring** a guilt-offering **and stipulate** as follows: If I am obligated to bring this offering then it is for my obligation; and if not, it shall be a voluntary peace-offering.

One glaring omission stands out from this analysis: the *braita* makes no mention of an *asham*. This is puzzling, because the *asham* is critical for the *metzora* — the Gemara elsewhere establishes that the *asham* is the central vehicle of *kapara* for a *metzora*, and without it his *tahara* is not complete; it is *me'akev*. An *asham* for *nazir tamei* exists as well, though it is not *me'akev* in the same way. The *braita*'s silence on the *asham* is therefore striking.

### Rabbi Shimon Solution

The Gemara resolves this difficulty by invoking the position of *Rabbi Shimon*, who holds that one may bring an *asham b'tenai*: the animal is brought conditionally — if it is indeed needed as an *asham*, it functions as one; if not, it becomes a *shelamim*. This works because the *halacha* is that the remainder of an *asham* (*mosar asham*) goes to *shelamim* — the same animal that would serve as an *asham* can, under certain conditions, be redirected as a *shelamim*. Accordingly, the *braita* follows *Rabbi Shimon's* position, which allows the *asham* to be brought conditionally even in a *safek* context.

The Gemara does not directly address what becomes of this individual if one does not rule in accordance with *Rabbi Shimon*. That question is left without a clear resolution.

One might further ask — as *Tosafot* does — why the *braita* does not also mention an *olat ha'of* at various stages (which could be brought *b'tenai* just like an *olah behema*), or a *shelamim* with an appropriate stipulation. The answer given by *Tosafot* is that the goal throughout is to do the bare minimum that is *halachically* required to be *yotze*. There is no benefit to bringing additional *korbanot* whose obligation is uncertain, and the *braita* accordingly confines itself to the minimal set that is both necessary and permitted.

## Braita Four Shavings

### Shaving One: All Three

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: תִּגְלַחַת רִאשׁוֹן מֵבִיא וְכוּ׳. מִמָּה נַפְשָׁךְ שַׁפִּיר קָמַיְיתֵי, דְּאִי וַדַּאי מְצוֹרָע הוּא וְלָאו טָמֵא הוּא — צִיפּוֹרִין חוֹבָתוֹ, וְחַטַּאת הָעוֹף סָפֵק, אָזְלָא לִקְבוּרָה, וְעוֹלָה הָוְיָא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the details and reasons for this *halakha*: **You said** in this *baraita* that for the **first shaving** he **brings** the two birds of a leper, as well as a bird sin-offering and an animal burnt-offering. The Gemara explains: In this manner **he brings** the **proper** offerings **whichever way you** look at it. **As, if his** status as **a leper** is **definite and he is not impure,** the **birds** he brings are for **his obligation** to purify himself from leprosy. **And** as **the bird sin-offering** in case he was impure was brought due to **uncertainty,** it **goes for burial,** like all bird sin-offerings sacrificed in a case of uncertainty, which cannot be eaten. **And** the bird **burnt-offering** that he brought in case he was an impure nazirite **is a gift** offering.

At the first shaving, the individual might be any one of three things: at *metzora* stage one, a *nazir tamei*, or a *nazir tahor*. The *braita* therefore prescribes three categories of *korban*: *tziporim* for *metzora* stage one, a *chatat ha'of* for *nazir tamei*, and an *olat behema* for *nazir tahor*.

### Shaving Two: Two Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּגְלַחַת שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית צִיפּוֹרִין לָא צְרִיךְ, דְּהָא עֲבַיד, מַאי אִיכָּא: דִּילְמָא וַדַּאי מוּחְלָט הוּא, חֲדָא — לִסְפֵק סְפָרוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** For the **second and third shavings** he does **not need** to bring **birds** again, **as he has** already **performed** this ritual. **What is** the potential concern? That **perhaps he** was previously **a confirmed** leper of **definite** status. For this reason, **one** of the bird sin-offerings he brings at the second shaving is **for** the days of **his counting** when his status was **uncertain.** After the first purification of a confirmed leper he counts seven days, or thirty days in this case due to the uncertainty, and brings a sin-offering.

At the second shaving, the two remaining possibilities are completing *metzora* stage two or completing a *nezirut tahara*. A *nazir tahor* who finished at stage one is done; a *nazir tamei* who shaved at stage one has now transitioned and is completing *nezirut tahara* at stage two. The *braita* thus prescribes only a *chatat ha'of* and an *olat behema*.

### Shaving Three: Two Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲדָא, לִסְפֵק טוּמְאָתוֹ. תִּגְלַחַת רְבִיעִית — מֵבִיא קׇרְבַּן טׇהֳרָה וּמַתְנֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **And** the **one** sin-offering he brings at his third shaving is **for his uncertain impurity.** If he was a leper, the offerings he brought on the previous occasion were for purification of his leprosy, not his naziriteship. This means that, if he was impure, he is still required to bring a sin-offering for his impurity as a nazirite. Furthermore, the animal burnt-offerings he brings for his second and third shavings are for his naziriteship of purity. For his **fourth shaving he brings an offering of purity and stipulates** as follows:

At the third shaving, *metzora* has been fully resolved. What remains are the possibilities of ending a *nezirut tumah* (requiring *chatat ha'of*) or ending a *nezirut tahara* (requiring *olat behema*). Again, the *braita* prescribes a *chatat ha'of* and an *olat behema*, though for different underlying reasons than at the second shaving.

### Shaving Four: Full Korban

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי וַדַּאי נָזִיר הוּא — עוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה חוֹבָה, וְזוֹ נְדָבָה. וְאִם טָמֵא וּמוּחְלָט הוּא — עוֹלָה הָרִאשׁוֹנָה נְדָבָה, וְזוֹ חוֹבָה, וְזֶה שְׁאָר קׇרְבָּנוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he was a definite nazirite** from the start and was neither impure from a corpse nor a leper, **the first burnt-offering** he brought after his initial shaving was for his **obligation** of pure naziriteship, **and this** one he is bringing now is **a gift** offering. **And if he was impure** from a corpse **and a confirmed** leper, **the first burnt-offering** he brought was **a gift** offering **and this** one he is bringing now is for his **obligation. And these,** the guilt-offering and peace-offering he is currently bringing, are for **the rest of his** obligatory **offerings.** This concludes the Gemara's analysis of the case of the *baraita*.

At the fourth and final shaving, only *nezirut tahara* remains. He brings the full *korban tahara* — the complete set of *korbanot* owed by a *nazir tahor* at the end of his term — and stipulates regarding the *olah*: if I was already a *nazir tahor* at one of the earlier stages and brought my obligatory *olah* then, that earlier *olah* was my *chovah* and this one is a *nedava*; if only now am I completing my *nezirut tahara*, then any *olah* I brought earlier was a *nedavah* and this present one is my *chovah*. Either way, the *chatat* and *shelamim* are brought in full as the remainder of his *korban*, since even if he discharged his *olah* obligation earlier, the rest of the *korban* was never brought.

## Gemara Analysis

### First Shaving Justified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְגַלְּחוֹ תּוֹךְ שִׁבְעָה אִי אֶפְשָׁר, דְּדִילְמָא לָאו מוּחְלָט הוּא, וְרַחֲמָנָא אָמַר ״תַּעַר לֹא יַעֲבֹר עַל רֹאשׁוֹ עַד מְלֹאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further explains: **But** it **is not possible to shave him** right **after seven days,** as one usually does when purified from leprosy, **as perhaps he is not a confirmed** leper and has no obligation to shave but is bound by the naziriteship vow, **and the Merciful One says: "No razor shall come upon his head; until** the days are **fulfilled** in which he consecrated himself to the Lord" (Numbers 6:5). This verse teaches that he cannot shave until the end of his naziriteship. Therefore, he shaves only after thirty days.

The Gemara now works through each stage in detail, confirming that the offerings prescribed at each shaving cover every possible scenario without violating any prohibition. At the first shaving, the Gemara says: *mima nafshach* — whichever way you look at it, everything brought at stage one is appropriate. *Shapir ka mayti* — it is well brought.

If he was a *vadai metzora* and not a *nazir tamei*, then *tziporim chovato* — the bird ritual is his obligation. The *chatat ha'of* that he brought because he might be a *nazir tamei* is brought *b'safek* and accordingly goes to burial (*likvurah*) — that is what is done when a doubtful *chatat* is set aside. And the *olat behema* he brought in case he was a *nazir tahor* counts as a free-will *nedavah*. No prohibition has been violated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָאו מְצוֹרָע וַדַּאי הָוֵי, וְהוּא טָמֵא — חַטַּאת הָעוֹף חוֹבָתוֹ, וְצִיפּוֹרִין אַבָּרַאי קָא מִתְעַבְדִין, וְלָא הָוֵי חוּלִּין בַּעֲזָרָה. וְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה הָוְיָא נְדָבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he is not a definite leper but he is impure** from a corpse, **the bird sin-offering** is for **his obligation** as an impure nazirite, **and** there is no problem with the unnecessary rituals of a leper's **birds,** as **they are performed outside** the Temple, **and are not** subject to the prohibition against bringing **non-sacred** animals **into the** Temple **courtyard.** Therefore, the slaughter of one of the birds outside the Temple is not a transgression. **And** the **animal burnt-offering is** considered **a gift** offering.

If he was not a *metzora* but was a *nazir tamei*, then the *chatat ha'of* is his obligatory *korban*. The *tziporim* ritual that was performed — *avra'i ka mita'avdin* — was done entirely outside the *beit hamikdash* and constitutes no *korban* at all, so it is harmless; it is not a case of *chulin ba'azarah*. The *olat behema* is a *nedavah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי לָאו מְצוֹרָע הוּא וְלָאו טָמֵא הוּא — צִיפּוֹרִין אַבָּרַאי קָא מִתְעַבְדִין, חַטַּאת הָעוֹף לִקְבוּרָה, עוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה הָוְיָא חוֹבָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he is neither a leper nor impure** he has also acted correctly, as **the birds are performed outside** the Temple, which means no transgression is committed, while the **bird sin-offering** of uncertain necessity goes **to burial,** and the **animal burnt-offering is** for **his obligation,** like any nazirite who completes his naziriteship.

And if he was neither a *metzora* nor a *nazir tamei* — meaning he was simply a *nazir tahor* — then the *tziporim* were done outside with no consequence, the *chatat ha'of* is buried as a doubtful offering, and the *olat behema* is his *chovah*. All three are fully accounted for. Additionally, why not shave immediately within seven days rather than waiting 30? Because *dilma lav metzora hu* — perhaps he is not a *metzora* at all, and the Torah says *ta'ar lo ya'avor al rosho*; as a possible *nazir tahor*, shaving is forbidden until *ad melot*.

### Shavings Two-Three Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תִּגְלַחַת שְׁנִיָּה וּשְׁלִישִׁית צִיפּוֹרִין לָא צְרִיךְ, דְּהָא עֲבַיד, מַאי אִיכָּא: דִּילְמָא וַדַּאי מוּחְלָט הוּא, חֲדָא — לִסְפֵק סְפָרוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** For the **second and third shavings** he does **not need** to bring **birds** again, **as he has** already **performed** this ritual. **What is** the potential concern? That **perhaps he** was previously **a confirmed** leper of **definite** status. For this reason, **one** of the bird sin-offerings he brings at the second shaving is **for** the days of **his counting** when his status was **uncertain.**

At the second and third shavings, the Gemara notes that the *tziporim* are no longer required — *tziporin lo tzarich*. The bird ritual was only relevant at stage one of *metzora*, and since that was already done at the first shaving, *deha avid* — it has been discharged. What then remains at stage two? The Gemara puts it neatly: *mai ikka dilma vadai muchlat* — you might say he is definitively a *muchlat*, a confirmed *metzora*. At that stage, one *chatat ha'of* covers *safek sipro* — the doubt as to whether he is completing *metzora* stage two — and the *olat behema* covers his possible *nezirut tahara*. At stage three, one *chatat ha'of* covers *safek tumato* — the doubt as to whether he is ending his *nezirut tumah* — and again the *olat behema* covers *nezirut tahara*.

### Double-Function Shaving

The Gemara identifies a particularly elegant convergence at the second shaving. At that point, two scenarios simultaneously find resolution in a single *giluach*: the *metzora* stage two shaving and the completion of a *nazir tahor*'s term. The two are not the same act performed for two different reasons in a single moment — rather, each *korban* brought at that shaving maps precisely onto one of the two possible statuses. The *chatat ha'of* addresses the *safek* of being a *muchlat* completing his *metzora* cycle (*safek sipro*), while the *olat behema* addresses the *safek* of being a *nazir tahor* completing his *nezirut*. They are distinct *korbanot* for distinct obligations — and the shaving, occurring once, technically serves each in its own right. In a *safek* situation, each individual *korban* is justified by the relevant possible status, and the *giluach* itself is the one act that all scenarios require at that juncture.

## Mixed Certainty Cases

### Safek Tamei Vadai Muchlat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא סָפֵק וּמוּחְלָט וַדַּאי — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים, וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים לְאַחַר שִׁשִּׁים וְשִׁבְעָה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** A nazirite about whom it was **uncertain** if he was **impure** from a corpse, **and** he was **a confirmed** leper whose status was **definite, partakes of sacrificial food after eight days.** He counts seven days, after which he is purified from his leprosy. On the following day he brings his offerings and may partake of sacrificial food. **And he may drink wine and become impure from the dead after sixty-seven days.** He is required to count another thirty days for his naziriteship and shave, at which point he must observe another thirty days, in case his first shaving was for his impurity, and his second shaving is for his purity.

Having worked through the fully *safek* case, the Gemara turns to mixed scenarios where one of the two statuses is certain. The first such case is *tamei safek u'muchlat vadai* — he is definitively a *metzora* (*vadai muchlat*), but only possibly a *nazir tamei*. The Gemara rules that he may eat *kodshim* after eight days, and may become *tamei* for the dead after 76 days.

The reasoning runs as follows. Because he is *vadai metzora*, the *halacha* is that the shaving of a definite *metzora* takes precedence even over the prohibition against a *nazir* shaving. Therefore, he shaves immediately at the outset of his *metzora* process, and again after seven days — the standard *metzora* timeline. The *korban* is then brought on day eight, allowing him to eat *kodshim*. At this point, the *metzora* process is complete.

He is now left with the *safek* of being a *nazir tamei*. Since he is only *possibly* tamei, he cannot immediately shave for *tumah* — he must wait lest he be a *nazir tahor* who cannot shave mid-*nezirut*. He therefore waits 30 days and shaves, and then waits another 30 days for his *nezirut tahara* and shaves again. That yields an additional 67 days beyond day eight, totaling 76 days until he is fully out of his *nezirut* status.

### Vadai Tamei Safek Muchlat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מוּחְלָט סָפֵק וְטָמֵא וַדַּאי — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁבְעָה יָמִים, וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים לְאַחַר שִׁבְעִים וְאַרְבָּעָה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses another case: A nazirite whose status as **a confirmed** leper was **uncertain and** who was **definitely impure** from a corpse **partakes of sacrificial food after thirty-seven days.** He shaves after seven days for his definite impurity and starts counting thirty days of naziriteship afresh. At the conclusion of this period he shaves again, which renders him pure from any uncertain leprosy, and may partake of sacrificial food. However, **he may drink wine and become impure from the dead** only **after seventy-four days.** Since he might have been a confirmed leper, his first two shavings, after seven and thirty-seven days, count only toward the two stages of his purification from leprosy, for his first shaving and after the days of his counting. He must therefore observe a further thirty days for his naziriteship of purity.

The second mixed case is *muchlat safek v'tamei vadai* — he is definitively a *nazir tamei* but only possibly a *metzora*. The Gemara rules that he may eat *kodshim* after 37 days and may become *tamei* for the dead after 74 days.

Here the logic is more nuanced. He cannot shave immediately, even as a *nazir tamei*, because if he is also a *metzora*, then the *metzora* process must be addressed first — *tzara'at* takes precedence. However, since he is *vadai nazir tamei*, he may shave after seven days, as the seven-day *tahara* process for *tumat meit* is complete. Crucially, that shaving — mandated by his definite *nazir tamei* status — can simultaneously serve as *giluach* number one for his possible *metzora* status, since he is shaving anyway.

After that first shaving at day seven, the next shaving in the *metzora* sequence would ordinarily follow after another seven days. But because he may now be beginning his *nezirut tahara* as a *nazir tamei*, he cannot shave again within that time without potentially violating a *nezirut tahor*. He must therefore wait 30 days — arriving at day 37 — where he performs the second *metzora* shaving. At this point, his *safek metzora* status is fully resolved, and he may eat *kodshim* at day 37 (strictly speaking, after the day-37 *korban*, which the Gemara counts as eating *kodshim l'achar shloshim v'shivah yamim*).

Only now, with the *metzora* process behind him, can he address his *nazir tamei* status as its own matter. The shaving at day seven cannot double-count for both *metzora* and *nazir tamei* simultaneously — that is the principle the Gemara will elaborate on extensively. He must now wait seven days (for *tumat meit* purification) and then 30 days (for *nezirut tahara*), adding another 37 days. Seven plus 30 plus 37 equals 74 days total until he is fully clear.

### Vadai Both Definite

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טָמֵא וַדַּאי וּמוּחְלָט וַדַּאי — אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לְאַחַר שְׁמוֹנָה יָמִים, וְשׁוֹתֶה יַיִן וּמִיטַּמֵּא לְמֵתִים לְאַחַר אַרְבָּעִים וְאַרְבָּעָה יָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara mentions yet another case: A nazirite who was **definitely impure** from a corpse **and** he was **a confirmed** leper whose status was **definite partakes of sacrificial food after eight days.** He immediately shaves his first shaving for his leprosy, then counts seven days and shaves again for his leprosy and partakes of sacrificial food on the following day. **And he may drink wine and become impure from the dead after forty-four days.** He counts a further seven days of hair growth for his shaving of impurity, shaves, and finally observes thirty more days for his naziriteship of purity.

The simplest case is *tamei vadai u'muchlat vadai* — he is definitively a *nazir tamei* and definitively a *metzora*. The Gemara rules that he may eat *kodshim* after eight days and may become *tamei* for the dead after 44 days.

Since both statuses are certain, the *vadai metzora* shaving proceeds immediately: shaving one on day one, shaving two on day seven, *korban* on day eight — he eats *kodshim* on day eight. Then, because the *metzora* shavings do not count for *nazir tamei*, he must now do the full *nazir tamei* sequence: seven days for *tumah*, followed by 30 days of *nezirut tahara* — 37 additional days. Eight plus 37 gives 44 days until he may become *tamei* for the dead again.

## Double-Counting Question

### Student Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאֲלוּ תַּלְמִידָיו אֶת רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי: נָזִיר טָהוֹר וּמְצוֹרָע, מַהוּ שֶׁיְּגַלֵּחַ תִּגְלַחַת אַחַת וְעוֹלָה לוֹ לְכָאן וּלְכָאן? אָמַר לָהֶן: אֵינוֹ מְגַלֵּחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **The students of** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **asked Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai:** With regard to one who was **a pure nazirite and a leper, what is** the *halakha* concerning the possibility **that he may shave one shaving and it will count for him** both **for this and for that?** In other words, can it serve for his shaving of leprosy as well as for his naziriteship? **He said to them: He may not shave** once for both requirements.

The concluding section of the *daf* moves to a *tosefta* that addresses what has been implicit throughout: why cannot any single shaving serve simultaneously for two purposes? The students of *Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* pose this directly: *nazir tahor u'metzora mahu she'yigale'ach tiglachat achat v'olah lo l'kan u'l'kan* — if a person is a *nazir tahor* and also a *metzora*, why cannot one shaving count for both? To sharpen the case: suppose a man's 30 days of *nezirut* were complete, and immediately before he could bring his *korban* and shave, he contracted *tzara'at*. When his *metzora* period concludes and that final shaving takes place, why should it not simultaneously discharge his obligation as a *nazir tahor*?

### Hair Functions Differ

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: אִילּוּ זֶה לְגַדֵּל וְזֶה לְגַדֵּל, וְזֶה לְהַעֲבִיר וְזֶה לְהַעֲבִיר — יָפֶה אַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים. עַכְשָׁיו, נָזִיר — לְהַעֲבִיר, וּמְצוֹרָע — לְגַדֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to them: If** the aim of both shavings were the same, **this one to grow** hair **and that one to grow** hair, **or this one to remove** hair **and that one to remove** hair, **you** would have **spoken well. Now** in actual fact the two shavings have different functions: **A nazirite** shaves **to remove** his hair, **and a leper** shaves **to grow** hair, so that he can shave again after the days of his counting.

*Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai* answers: *eino megale'ach* — he may not. The reason, he explains, is that the shavings are not functionally identical. *Ilu zeh l'gadel v'zeh l'gadel, v'zeh l'ha'avir v'zeh l'ha'avir, yafeh atem omrim* — if both shavings were performing the same function, either both serving to grow the hair back afterward (*l'gadel*) or both serving to remove the hair as a terminal act (*l'ha'avir*), then the students' argument would be compelling. But *achshav nazir l'ha'avir u'metzora l'gadel* — the *nazir*'s shaving is *l'ha'avir*, a terminal removal, while the *metzora*'s shaving is *l'gadel*, the beginning of a process after which the hair grows back and there will be another shaving.

### Case One: Tahor Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלֹא תַּעֲלֶה לוֹ לִימֵי חִלּוּטוֹ, וְתַעֲלֶה לוֹ לִימֵי סְפָרוֹ! וְאָמַר לָהֶן: אִילּוּ זֶה לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים וְזֶה לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים — יָפֶה אַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים, אֶלָּא מְצוֹרָע לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, וְנָזִיר לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** His students posed another question: **But** even if his shaving of naziriteship does **not count for him as** the shaving of the completion of **his days of confirmed** leprosy, **let it** at least **count for him** as the shaving at the end of the **days of his counting,** which is not followed by another act of shaving, and therefore is performed only for the purpose of removing his hair. **And** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **said to them: If this one** were performed **before the sprinkling of blood and that one before the sprinkling of blood, you** would have **spoken well. However, a leper** shaves **before the sprinkling of** his offering's **blood, and a nazirite** does so **after the sprinkling of** the **blood.** Therefore, the two shavings are not equivalent.

In the case of *nazir tahor* combined with *metzora*: the *nazir tahor*'s shaving at the end of his *nezirut* is *l'ha'avir* — he shaves and he is done, the hair grows back naturally and there is no further obligation tied to it. The *metzora*'s shaving, by contrast, is *l'gadel* — it is the first of a two-stage shaving sequence, and the whole point is that after this shaving the hair grows back so that it can be shaved again on the seventh day. These are structurally different acts, and they therefore cannot collapse into one.

### Case Two: Tamei Metzora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְלֹא תַּעֲלֶה לוֹ לִימֵי צָרַעְתּוֹ וּנְזִירוּתוֹ, וְתַעֲלֶה לוֹ לִימֵי צָרַעְתּוֹ וְטוּמְאָתוֹ! אָמַר לָהֶן: אִילּוּ זֶה לִפְנֵי בִּיאַת מַיִם וְזֶה לִפְנֵי בִּיאַת מַיִם — יָפֶה אַתֶּם אוֹמְרִים. אֶלָּא טָמֵא לְאַחַר בִּיאַת מַיִם, מְצוֹרָע לִפְנֵי בִּיאַת מַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai's students asked him yet another question: **And** granted that his shaving does **not count for his days of leprosy and his naziriteship, let it** at least **count for his days of leprosy and his** shaving of naziriteship of **impurity,** both of which are performed before the sprinkling of the blood. **He said to them: If this one** shaved **before** his **immersion in water, and that one before immersion in water, you** would have **spoken well. However, an impure** nazirite shaves **after immersion in water,** whereas **a leper** shaves **before immersion in water.**

The students then press further: even granting that point, what about the *metzora* stage two shaving — the one on day seven of the *metzora* process — and the shaving of a *nazir tamei* who is also a *metzora*? Here both shavings come *before* a *korban* is brought, so perhaps they can double-count? *Rabbi Shimon* responds: *ilu zeh lifnei zrikat damim v'zeh lifnei zrikat damim, yafeh atem omrim* — if both came before the sprinkling of blood (*zrikat damim*), the argument would hold. But *metzora lifnei zrikat damim* — the *metzora*'s day-seven shaving takes place before the *korban* is brought (and the blood is sprinkled) on day eight — while *nazir l'achar zrikat damim* — the *nazir tamei*'s shaving takes place after the blood of his *korban* has been sprinkled. They occupy different positions in the ritual sequence relative to the *korban*, and they are therefore doing functionally different things.

The students raise one further question: what about the case of *metzora* stage two combined with *nazir tamei* — both of which, it might be argued, precede the *korban* on one reading — and compare it with a case where both shavings come before *bi'at mayim* (immersion in the *mikvah*)? *Rabbi Shimon* answers: *ilu zeh lifnei bi'at mayim v'zeh lifnei bi'at mayim, yafeh atem omrim* — if both came before *bi'at mayim*, fine. But the shaving of the *nazir tamei* comes *after* the *tahara* process involving the *efer parah adumah* and immersion in the *mikvah* — it is the conclusion of the *tahara* process. The *metzora*'s shaving on day seven, however, comes *before* that immersion, which follows the shaving. They are therefore on opposite sides of the *mikvah* in the ritual sequence.

### Rabbi Chiya Summary

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* תָּנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: זֶה לִפְנֵי בִּיאַת מַיִם (חַיִּים) וְזֶה לְאַחַר בִּיאַת מַיִם, זֶה לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים וְזֶה לְאַחַר זְרִיקַת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya teaches** the same *halakha*: The shaving of a leper does not count for the shaving of naziriteship, as **this** one, a leper, shaves **before immersion in water, and that** one, an impure nazirite, shaves **after immersion water. This** one, the leper, shaves **before** the **blood is sprinkled, and that** one, the pure nazirite, shaves **after** the **blood is sprinkled.**

*Rabbi Chiya* distills all of these distinctions into a compact formulation: *zeh lifnei bi'at mayim v'zeh l'achar bi'at mayim* — one comes before immersion and one comes after; *zeh lifnei zrikat damim v'zeh l'achar zrikat damim* — one comes before the sprinkling of blood and one comes after; and there is a third distinction, that of stage number — *chaluto* versus *sipro*, first phase versus second phase. Examining these three variables — position relative to *mikvah*, position relative to *korban*, and stage within the process — one finds that no pair of shavings from different obligations ever matches on all three. It is this triple divergence that establishes, systematically, that no two shavings can ever double-count for one another.
