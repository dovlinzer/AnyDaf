# Niddah 57 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ketamim Overview

### Review & Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַכְּתָמִים הַנִּמְצָאִים בְּכׇל מָקוֹם — טְהוֹרִין, חוּץ מִן הַנִּמְצָאִים בַּחֲדָרִים, וּבִסְבִיבוֹת בֵּית הַטְּמָאוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All** blood **stains** on garments **that are found anywhere** where Jews and gentiles reside **are ritually pure,** since they must not belong to Jews, who put away their stained garments. This is the *halakha* **except for** the stained garments **that are found in the** inner **rooms** of the house, as these might be garments that the Jews put away there; **and** except for the stained garments found **in proximity to the house of impurity,** i.e., the room that women used when they were impure due to menstruation.

The present daf picks up with the *mishnah* at the bottom of 56b, launching us squarely into the world of *ketamim* — stains. The previous daf introduced two central questions that arise when a stain is discovered: first, how far back in time must a woman assume the stain was present, with the primary implication being from when she must consider herself a *niddah*; and second, whether the appearance of the garment — whether it looks as though it has been laundered — can serve as evidence to establish at least the latest possible time of origin.

An important preliminary observation governs all that follows: even when we rule that a *ketem* is *tamei*, that ruling is only Rabbinic in force. The *tumah* of stains is never biblical.

### Non-Jew Stains

Among the first leniencies the *mishnah* introduces regarding stains is the rule that stains found on the garments of non-Jewish women present no problem whatsoever. Although we rule Rabbinically that non-Jewish women can be *niddot* and that their *niddah* blood is *tamei*, their *ketamim* — their stains — do not create *tumah* for a Jew who comes into contact with them, nor if such a stain contacts *terumah*.

One might ask: does the prohibition against relations with a non-Jewish woman not derive partly from her *niddah* status? Indeed, Rabbinically she carries a *niddah* designation, but there are in fact five separate Rabbinic statuses applied to non-Jewish women to render such relations forbidden — the mnemonic *nasgaz* captures them: *niddah*, *shifchah*, *goyah*, *zonah*, and so on. The *niddah* status is thus only one thread in a larger web of prohibition.

### City Stains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִבֵּין יִשְׂרָאֵל וּמִבֵּין הַכּוּתִים — טְמֵאִין, דְּכוּתִים גֵּרֵי אֱמֶת הֵן. הַנִּמְצָאִים בְּעָרֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל — טְהוֹרִין, שֶׁלֹּא נֶחְשְׁדוּ עַל כִּתְמֵיהֶם, וְאַצְנוֹעֵי מַצְנְעִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Blood stains on garments that come **from among the Jews and from among the Samaritans are ritually impure, as** everyone agrees the **Samaritans are true converts.** With regard to blood stains **that are found in** the **towns of Jews, they are pure, as they are not suspected of** failing to put away **their** garments on which there are blood **stains, and they** certainly **put them away.** Therefore, the stains necessarily come from gentiles.

The practical upshot of these rules is that a random bloodstain found on a garment in the public domain is rarely *tamei*. The *mishnah* at the bottom of 56b states: *kol haketamim hanimtzaim bechol makom tehorim* — stains found in the public domain of Jewish cities are *tahor*, because we assume that Jews are careful with garments bearing *niddah* stains and would not leave them out where others might become *tamei*. There is therefore a presumption that publicly found stains originated from non-Jews, and they are consequently not a problem.

The exception is *ketamim* found in *chadarim* — inner, private rooms — where one would not expect such casual discarding. The precise referent of *cheder* is not entirely clear, but it certainly means something more like a private inner chamber than an open living space. In a private room, the assumption shifts, and we do presume the stain may be *niddah* blood.

## Beit HaTme'ot

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית הַטְּמָאוֹת שֶׁל כּוּתִים מְטַמְּאִין בְּאֹהֶל, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהֵם קוֹבְרִין שָׁם אֶת הַנְּפָלִים. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: לֹא הָיוּ קוֹבְרִין, אֶלָּא מַשְׁלִיכִין, וְחַיָּה גּוֹרַרְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The house of impurity of Samaritans imparts** the **impurity** that is imparted by a corpse **by means of a tent, due to** the fact **that they bury the stillborn** children **there. Rabbi Yehuda says:** The house of impurity of Samaritans does not impart that impurity, as **they would not bury** a stillborn child there. **Rather, they would cast** it outside **and an animal would drag it** away.

The *mishnah* adds a second exception: *uvisvivot beit hatme'ot* — stains found in the surroundings of the "house of impurity." *Rashi* defines *beit hatme'ot* as a room that women would make use of during their days of *niddah*: *cheder shenashim mishtamshot bo bimei niddotan*.

### Societal Attitudes

This reference is remarkable and deserves attention. Many societies throughout history have maintained menstrual taboos requiring women to physically separate themselves from society during menstruation — to be removed from the household, seen as carrying something dangerous, even possessing some form of demonic or metaphysical power that necessitated isolation. The *Ramban* on the *Torah* alludes to this theme when he explains *Rachel*'s words to *Lavan*, *lo uchal lakum mipanecha ki derech nashim li* — on his reading, she invoked her *niddah* status precisely to create distance, given the perceived metaphysical dangers associated with it.

### Halakhic Treatment

What is striking is that essentially none of this appears in the *Gemara's* halakhic treatment. Quite the opposite: the *halacha* permits a husband and wife to sleep in the same bedroom even when she is a *niddah*, notwithstanding the general rule that a man and a forbidden woman may not be secluded together (*yichud*). The various activities that other societies restricted to *niddot* — including food preparation, which was thought to carry dangerous power — are entirely non-issues in *halacha*. The formal *halacha*, in other words, not only ignores the societal taboo but in certain respects legislates against it.

### Halacha vs Practice

That said, one cannot always assume that the official *halacha* perfectly mirrors the attitudes and practices of the community. There is frequently a gap between what is ruled and what is observed, whether in the direction of additional stringency, leniency, or simply a different set of attitudes that persists despite — or alongside — the official position. The *beit hatme'ot* reference in the *mishnah* offers the faintest hint of this phenomenon: women were somehow using this room specifically during their *niddah* period. Whether this amounts to full seclusion — as practiced, for example, by the Ethiopian Jewish community, where women actually left the household during menstruation — or something less formal, is not entirely clear.

What is clear is that some of these attitudes never fully disappeared, even in communities that had access to normative *halachic* literature. The most obvious contemporary expression is the set of attitudes — rooted in medieval *Ashkenaz* but likely with far older antecedents — regarding women touching *Sifrei Torah* or entering the synagogue while a *niddah*. These practices are not grounded in *halacha* but reflect the persistence of older menstrual taboos alongside it. Shaye Cohen's work on women and the sanctum addresses precisely this phenomenon.

### Samaritan Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמָנִים לוֹמַר ״קָבַרְנוּ שָׁם אֶת הַנְּפָלִים״, אוֹ ״לֹא קָבַרְנוּ״, נֶאֱמָנִים לוֹמַר עַל הַבְּהֵמָה אִם בִּכְּרָה אִם לֹא בִּכְּרָה, נֶאֱמָנִים עַל צִיּוּן קְבָרוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Samaritans **are deemed credible to state: We buried the stillborn** children **there,** in a certain place, and it transmits ritual impurity; **or** to state: **We did not bury** the stillborn children there, and it does not transmit ritual impurity. **They are** likewise **deemed credible to state about an animal whether it** previously **gave birth** or **whether it did not** previously **give birth;** and their testimony is accepted with regard to determining whether the animal's offspring has the status of a firstborn animal, which is sacred. They **are** also **deemed credible** to testify **about the marking of graves,** i.e., that where they marked is deemed a grave and where they did not mark is deemed a place where there is no grave.

The *mishnah* then raises a question that recurs throughout the *Gemara's* treatment of Samaritans: can one believe their testimony on this matter? The specific question is whether they can be believed — *ne'emanim lomar* — to say that they buried a fetus there, or that they did not. The *mishnah* asserts that they can be believed. It then extends this trustworthiness to several additional areas: whether an animal has previously given birth and thus whether the next offspring would be a *bechor*, and whether grave sites have been properly marked.

The *mishnah* equally specifies areas where Samaritans cannot be trusted: regarding *sechachot* (overhanging branches), *peru'ot* (protruding stones), and *beit hapras* (a field plowed over a grave). The organizing principle stated in the *mishnah* is *zeh haklal davar shechashudim bo ein ne'emanim alav* — as a rule, regarding matters in which they themselves are suspect, one cannot trust their testimony.

## Samaritan Trust

### Drasha Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' מַאי דְּרוּשׁ? ״לֹא תַסִּיג גְּבוּל רֵעֲךָ אֲשֶׁר גָּבְלוּ רִאשֹׁנִים בְּנַחֲלָתְךָ״. כֹּל שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ נַחֲלָה — יֵשׁ לוֹ גְּבוּל, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין לוֹ נַחֲלָה — אֵין לוֹ גְּבוּל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the Samaritans do not observe burial customs for stillborn children. The Gemara asks: **What** verse **did they interpret** as a source for this practice? The Gemara replies that they interpreted the verse: **"You shall not remove your fellow's boundary marker, which was bounded by the first ones, in your inheritance** that you shall inherit, in the land that the Lord your God gives you to possess it" (Deuteronomy 19:14). The Gemara explains: The Sages derived from this verse that it is prohibited to sell one's ancestral burial ground. In accordance with this interpretation of the verse, the Samaritans derived that **any** individual **who has an inheritance,** i.e., who stands to inherit land, **has a boundary,** i.e., a burial place, whereas **any** individual **who does not have an inheritance** in the land, e.g., a stillborn child, **does not have a boundary,** i.e., a burial place. The Samaritans therefore concluded that the mitzva of burial does not apply to stillborn children.

The *Gemara* opens this section by asking *mai darush* — what *derashah* leads Samaritans to the practice of not burying fetuses properly, or at all? The *Gemara* assumes this derives from a textual source rather than being mere custom. The verse cited is *lo tasig gevul re'acha asher gavlu rishonim benachalatcha* — do not move the boundary marker of your neighbor set by those who came before, in the land you are inheriting.

The Samaritan reading: *kol sheyesh lo nachalah* — anyone who has an inheritance, which requires being a viable newborn to receive, *yesh lo gevul* — has a boundary, understood here as a grave site, a defined place of burial. *Kol she'ein lo nachalah* — anyone who does not inherit, *ein lo gevul* — has no grave, and may be disposed of in any fashion. Since a fetus does not inherit, it need not be given a proper burial.

### Trust Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמָנִים לוֹמַר קָבַרְנוּ. וְהָא לֵית לְהוּ ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״! אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: בְּכֹהֵן עוֹמֵד שָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that Samaritans **are deemed credible to state: We buried** the stillborn children in a certain place, or to state that they did not bury the stillborn children there, and that place does not transmit ritual impurity. The Gemara objects: **But** the Samaritans **do not accept** the Sages' interpretation of the verse: **"And you shall not put a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14), that one may not cause another to sin. Since they are not concerned about misleading others, why is their testimony accepted? **Rabbi Abbahu says:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** a Samaritan **priest is standing there,** on that spot, which indicates that he genuinely maintains it is not impure with the impurity of a corpse.

The *Gemara* then turns to the trust question raised by the *mishnah* — *ne'emanim lomar kavnu* — are they believed to say whether they buried a fetus there or not? Here the *Gemara* makes a move that fundamentally reframes the *mishnah's* assumption: *velait lehu lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — but they do not subscribe to the prohibition of *lifnei iver*, placing a stumbling block before the blind. They interpret that prohibition only literally and are not concerned with causing others to sin. If so, even if they themselves are personally careful about a given practice, why should one trust their testimony to others about it?

This is a striking move. The *mishnah* assumes that if Samaritans are themselves careful about a given practice, that personal carefulness is grounds for trust. The *Gemara* fundamentally rejects this: no, one cannot trust them even when they are personally careful, because they have no compunction about misleading others.

### Kohen Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִילְמָא כֹּהֵן טָמֵא הוּא? דְּנָקֵיט תְּרוּמָה בִּידֵיהּ, וְדִילְמָא תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה הִיא? דְּקָאָכֵיל מִינַּהּ. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָא בְּקִיאִי בִּיצִירָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **But perhaps he is an impure priest** and therefore he does not refrain from standing in an impure place. The Gemara explains: The mishna is referring to a situation **where** the priest **is holding *teruma* in his hand,** which indicates he is ritually pure. The Gemara further objects: **But perhaps it is impure *teruma*.** The Gemara explains: The mishna is referring to a case **where** the priest **is partaking of** the *teruma*, which indicates that it is not impure, as it is prohibited to consume impure *teruma*. The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if this is the circumstance, it is obvious that the Samaritan priest's testimony can be accepted. Then **what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The ruling of the mishna is necessary, **lest you say** that the Samaritans **are not knowledgeable with regard to** the stages of the **formation** of an embryo, and they might bury a fetus believing that it is an unformed fetus that does not transmit impurity, when it is actually a forty-day-old fetus, which is impure. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that they are sufficiently knowledgeable, and their testimony is accepted.

If trust cannot be based on their stated care, what can it be based on? *Amar Rabbi Abahu bechohen omed sham* — *Rabbi Abbahu* answers that what we look for is their demonstrated behavior. A Samaritan *kohen* is standing in that space — that is, they are themselves acting in a way that shows they believe it is *tahor*.

But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: perhaps the *kohen* was already *tamei*, in which case his presence there proves nothing. The *Gemara* responds: *denakat terumah beyado* — he is holding *terumah*. A *tamei* person would not hold *terumah*. But perhaps the *terumah* was already *tamei*? *Deka'achil mineih* — he is eating it. One does not eat *tamei terumah*, so the combination of eating *terumah* in that space establishes that they believe it to be entirely *tahor*.

One might ask: what is the *chidush* of all this? The *Gemara* answers: *mahu deteima lo beki'ei beyetzirah* — one might have thought that even if they believe the space to be *tahor*, they might lack sufficient expertise to know whether a fetus was sufficiently developed to transmit *tumah*. Perhaps they assumed the fetus was under forty days' development when it in fact was not. The *Gemara* therefore teaches — *kamashma lan* — that when their behavior clearly demonstrates they believe all is *tahor*, we trust that they also possess sufficient knowledge of the relevant details and are not simply making an inadvertent mistake.

## Gemara Reframe

### Reject Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמָנִים עַל הַבְּהֵמָה וְכוּ׳. וְהָא לֵית לְהוּ ״וְלִפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״! אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּגוֹזֵז וְעוֹבֵד. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: לָא בְּקִיאִי בְּטִינּוּף, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the Samaritans are **deemed credible** to state **with regard to an animal** that it previously gave birth, and its subsequent offspring does not have the sacred status of a firstborn animal. The Gemara objects: **But** the Samaritans **do not accept** the Sages' interpretation of the verse: **"And you shall not put a stumbling block before the blind,"** that one may not cause another to sin. Why, then, is their testimony accepted? **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The mishna is referring to a case **where** the Samaritan **is shearing and working** the offspring of the animal. Since the Samaritans are meticulous with regard to Torah law, it is evident that it is not a firstborn. The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if this is the circumstance, **what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The ruling of the mishna is necessary, **lest you say** that the Samaritans **are not knowledgeable with regard to a murky** discharge emitted from the uterus, which is indicative of a fetus and exempts subsequent births from the mitzva of the firstborn (see *Bekhorot* 21a). It is possible that the Samaritan mistakenly believes the animal previously emitted a murky discharge and therefore its offspring is not a firstborn. Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that they are sufficiently knowledgeable, and their testimony is accepted.

The *Gemara's* reframing here represents a clean departure from the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* posited that trust follows from personal carefulness — if Samaritans are themselves careful about a practice, they can be believed about it. The *Gemara* categorically rejects this premise. Even when they are personally careful, one cannot trust their testimony to others, because they feel no obligation regarding *lifnei iver* and are not concerned about causing Jews to transgress.

The implication of this reframing is important: all cases where the *mishnah* permits relying on Samaritans must now be re-explained. It is not sufficient that they care about something for themselves. They must be observed actually behaving in a way that demonstrates their belief that the situation is permitted — and that demonstration must be unambiguous.

### Lifnei Iver

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶאֱמָנִים עַל צִיּוּן וְכוּ׳. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּמִדְּרַבָּנַן הוּא, כֵּיוָן דִּכְתִיבָא — מִזְהָר זְהִירִי בֵּיהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְרָאָה עֶצֶם אָדָם וּבָנָה אֶצְלוֹ צִיּוּן״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that the Samaritans **are deemed credible** to testify **with regard to** the **marking** of graves, as the Samaritans mark their graves, and we rely on their marking as an indication that a corpse is buried there. Therefore, any place where there is no marking is considered ritually pure. The Gemara explains: **Even though** the marking of graves **is** required only **by rabbinic law,** and Samaritans generally do not observe rabbinic law, **since it is written** in the Bible, the Samaritans **are meticulous with regard to it, as it is written:** "And those that pass through shall pass through the land, **and when one sees a human bone he shall set up a marking by it,** until the buriers have buried it in the valley of Hamon-gog" (Ezekiel 39:15).

The *Gemara* thus sets up a framework in which Samaritan testimony, standing alone, is inherently unreliable. Whatever the historical realities behind this characterization — and the antagonism between the rabbinic class and the Samaritan community goes back at least to *Ezra* and *Nehemiah* — the *Gemara* treats it as given that they will not scruple to mislead Jews.

One might distinguish between their personal practice and their responsibility to others: some people are personally stringent but care nothing about whether their statements lead others astray, particularly where there is animosity or financial interest involved. It is precisely this split that the *Gemara* attributes to Samaritans. The *mishnah* assumed otherwise — that personal care translates into honest testimony — and the *Gemara* overrides that assumption. Crucially, this is not a question of Samaritans being ignorant of the *halacha*; quite the opposite. The *Gemara* assumes they are fully informed but simply indifferent to the consequences for others. Were ignorance the explanation, things would be even worse: even their demonstrated behavior could not be relied upon, since they might simply be acting from a mistaken belief.

## Animal/Grave Trust

### Firstborn Animals

The *mishnah* states that Samaritans are trusted regarding whether an animal has previously given birth — *ne'emanim al habehema im bikra o lo bikra* — which determines whether the next offspring constitutes a *bechor* requiring special treatment. The *Gemara* immediately applies its reframing: *veha leit lehu lifnei iver lo titein michshol* — but they do not care about *lifnei iver*, so why should they be trusted even here, particularly given the potential financial interest?

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* citing *Rabbi Yochanan* answers: *begozez veoved* — they are themselves shearing and working the animal, demonstrating by their own behavior that they consider it not to be a *bechor*.

## Gemara Reframe…

### Knowledge/Intent

The *Gemara* again raises the *mahu deteima*: perhaps they are simply mistaken about whether a prior discharge constituted a miscarriage. Here, as *Dov* correctly observed, the concern is not a *halachic* disagreement but a knowledge gap — specifically, whether they can correctly identify a discharge as a miscarriage and thus know that the next animal is no longer a *bechor*. The *Gemara* answers *kamashma lan* that we assume they possess this knowledge and will not err on these factual matters, parallel to the resolution in the fetus case above.

## Animal/Grave Trust…

### Grave Markers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין לֹא עַל הַסְּכָכוֹת וְכוּ׳. סְכָכוֹת — דִּתְנַן: ״אֵלּוּ הֵן סְכָכוֹת — אִילָן הַמֵּיסֵךְ עַל הָאָרֶץ״. פְּרָעוֹת — דִּתְנַן: ״אֲבָנִים פְּרוּעוֹת הַיּוֹצְאוֹת מִן הַגָּדֵר״. בֵּית הַפְּרָס. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב שְׁמוּאֵל: מְנַפֵּחַ אָדָם בֵּית הַפְּרָס, וְהוֹלֵךְ. רַב יְהוּדָה בַּר אַמֵּי מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: בֵּית הַפְּרָס שֶׁנִּדַּשׁ טָהוֹר. וְתָנָא: הַחוֹרֵשׁ בֵּית הַקְּבָרוֹת — הֲרֵי זֶה עוֹשֶׂה בֵּית הַפְּרָס, וְעַד כַּמָּה הוּא עוֹשֶׂה? מְלֹא מַעֲנָה, מֵאָה אַמָּה, בֵּית אַרְבַּעַת סְאִין. רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: חָמֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **But** with regard to the following cases in which the exact location of a grave is unknown, the Samaritans **are not deemed credible** to testify: They are **not** deemed credible to testify **about overhanging boughs,** nor about the protrusions that jut out of stone fences. The Gemara explains these terms: The term **overhanging boughs** should be understood **as we learned** in a mishna (*Oholot* 8:2): **These are overhanging boughs: A tree that hangs over the ground.** The term **protrusions** should be understood **as we learned** in the *Tosefta* (*Oholot* 9:4): **Protruding stones that jut out of a fence.** § The mishna teaches that the Samaritans are not deemed credible to testify about **a *beit haperas*.** With regard to a *beit haperas*, **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav Shmuel says:** The reason the Sages deemed a *beit haperas* impure is due to the concern that the bones, but not the flesh of the corpse, were dispersed by the plow throughout the field. The *halakha* is that a bone transmits impurity by carrying or by contact, if it is at least the size of a barley grain, but it does not transmit impurity by means of a tent. Therefore, if **a person** is carrying ritually pure items, or if he wishes to remain ritually pure so that he may consume consecrated items, and yet he must pass through a *beit haperas*, he **may blow** on the earth of the ***beit haperas*** before each step, so that if there is a bone beneath the dust he will expose it and avoid it. **And** in this manner he may **walk** across the area while remaining ritually pure, even though he might step over a bone. **Rav Yehuda bar Ami says in the name of Rav Yehuda: A *beit haperas* that has been trodden** underfoot by many people **is pure,** as it may be assumed that any bone fragments at least as large as a barley grain that were on the surface were either broken or removed. **And it was taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **one who plows a graveyard, this** individual thereby **renders** it **a *beit haperas*. And to what** extent **does he render** it a *beit haperas*, i.e., how far does the concern apply that bones might have been dispersed? The field is rendered a *beit haperas* to the extent of **a full furrow [*ma'ana*], one hundred cubits** by one hundred cubits, which is the **area** required for sowing **four *se'a*** of seed. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The area rendered a *beit haperas* is the area required for sowing **five** *se'a* of seed.

The *mishnah* states Samaritans are trusted regarding whether an animal has previously given birth — *ne'emanim al habehema im bikra o lo bikra* — which determines whether the next offspring constitutes a *bechor* requiring special treatment. The *Gemara* notes a potential difficulty: this is itself only a Rabbinic practice. Given that the *Gemara* has been distinguishing between biblical and Rabbinic categories — with Samaritans following biblical but not Rabbinic law — why should they be trusted here?

The answer is *keivan dechetiva* — because the obligation to mark graves is rooted in a verse: the verse in *Yechezkel* says *ra'ah etzem adam uvanah etzlo tziyun* — if one sees a human bone, one should erect a marker beside it. Because this traces to a biblical text, Samaritans treat it with the seriousness due biblical law, and one can therefore assume they mark their graves conscientiously.

The practical implication follows: since they mark for their own purposes, they are not creating a situation where you must distrust an absence of a marker. Where there is no marker, a *kohen* may walk without concern. This also explains why the fetal burial issue remains separate — presumably a *nefel* does not receive the same treatment, and there would not be a marker to rely upon.

### Suchot/Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מְחַזְּקִינַן טוּמְאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם? וְהָתַנְיָא: נִמְצֵאת עַל קִשְׁרֵי אֶצְבְּעוֹתֶיהָ — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁיָּדַיִם עַסְקָנִיּוֹת הֵן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do we not presume** that **ritual impurity** travels **from place to place? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If blood **was found on the joints of her fingers** on the back of her hand **she is impure,** despite the fact that blood from her source is not usually found on that part of her hand **because hands are active?**

The *mishnah* declares that Samaritans cannot be trusted regarding *sechachot* and *peru'ot*. The *Gemara* explains: *sechachot detnan* — as taught in the *mishnah*, these are overhanging objects: *ilan hamaysech al ha'aretz* — a tree spreading its branches over the ground. The concern is Rabbinic.

There are two approaches to what makes this Rabbinic. According to *Tosfos*, the branches and leaves, however widespread, never combine to form a genuine *ohel* — a halachically significant "tent" — since they do not constitute a solid surface of *tefach* by *tefach*. Rabbinically, however, we treat such overhanging as an *ohel* because it could grow into one. According to *Rashi*, the issue is different: a *meis* was buried near a cemetery at nightfall, and those who buried it forgot exactly which tree it lies under. There is therefore an area of doubt, and the Rabbis rule that a *kohen* may not walk under any of those trees. Samaritans do not accept this Rabbinic stringency and let their *kohanim* walk under all such trees without concern. Because they dismiss this Rabbinic restriction, one cannot trust their testimony regarding it.

*Peru'ot* — protruding stones jutting from a wall — involve a similar dynamic: a case where it is unknown beneath which stone a *meis* lies, and Rabbinic concern prohibits certain movement in the vicinity. Samaritans disregard this as well.

The point is not that they are being deceptive; the point is that they themselves do not treat these restrictions as binding and therefore their assurances that a particular space presents no problem carry no weight — they would say the same thing regardless.

### Beit HaPras

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא מְהֵימְנִי? וְהָתַנְיָא: שָׂדֶה שֶׁאָבַד בָּהּ קֶבֶר, נֶאֱמָן כּוּתִי לוֹמַר ״אֵין שָׁם קֶבֶר״. לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵעִיד אֶלָּא עַל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל קֶבֶר. אִילָן שֶׁהוּא מֵיסֵךְ עַל הָאָרֶץ — נֶאֱמָן לוֹמַר ״אֵין תַּחְתָּיו קֶבֶר״, לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ מֵעִיד אֶלָּא עַל גּוּפוֹ שֶׁל קֶבֶר. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּמְהַלֵּךְ וּבָא עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלָּהּ. אִי הָכִי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רְצוּעָה נָפְקָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the ruling of the mishna that the Samaritans are not deemed credible to testify about a *beit haperas*, the Gemara asks: **And aren't they deemed credible? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a field in which a grave was lost,** which has the status of a *beit haperas*, **a Samaritan is deemed credible to say: There is no grave there?** The *baraita* explains: This is **due to** the fact **that he is not** considered to be **testifying** about a case of uncertain impurity; **rather,** he is testifying **about** the location of the **grave itself,** which is a matter of Torah law, and the Samaritans are deemed credible with regard to a matter of Torah law. Likewise, in the case of **a tree that is hanging over the ground,** a Samaritan **is deemed credible to say: There is no grave beneath it, as he is testifying only about** the location of the **grave itself.** This indicates that the Samaritans are deemed credible with regard to overhanging boughs and protrusions. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** in explanation: The *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the Samaritan **is walking** to **and fro over the entire** area, and therefore if there was a grave there he would certainly have become impure. Consequently, one may rely on his statement with regard to the purity of the place. By contrast, the mishna is speaking of a case where the Samaritan did not traverse the entire area, and therefore his testimony is not accepted, as they are not meticulous with regard to cases of uncertainty. The Gemara asks: **If so,** it is obvious that his testimony is credible, and **what** is the purpose **of stating** this *halakha*? The Gemara answers: The ruling of the *baraita* is necessary, **lest you say** that perhaps a narrow **strip** of land, which is called by the same name as this field, **extends** into a nearby field, and the Samaritan presumes the grave is located in that strip of land. If so, even if the Samaritan traversed the entire field his testimony cannot be accepted, as he traversed the field because he considered it merely a case of uncertain impurity. The *baraita* therefore **teaches us** that if the Samaritan traverses the entire field his testimony is accepted, as this concern is not an issue.

The third category in which Samaritans cannot be trusted is *beit hapras* — a field in which a grave has been plowed over, scattering small chips of bone throughout. Because a chip of bone less than an olive's bulk can still transmit *tumah* in certain configurations, the Rabbis declared the entire surrounding area — *melo ma'aneh*, the width of the plow's length, which works out to an area of 100 *amah* by 100 *amah* around the grave — to be a restricted zone.

The *Gemara* cites a ruling of *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel*: *menapeiach adam beit hapras vehaloch* — one can blow the ground ahead of one step at a time when traversing a *beit hapras*. By blowing, one moves any bone chip without bodily contact and thus avoids *heiseit* — moving a corpse — which would be a more serious form of *tumah*. It is something like navigating a minefield one step at a time. Additionally, *Rav Yehuda bar Ami* in the name of *Rav Yehuda* rules that a *beit hapras* that has been thoroughly trampled is *tahor*, as all loose bone chips would have been either ground into the earth or displaced.

The *Gemara* further teaches: *hachoresh beit hakevarot harei zeh oseh beit hapras* — one who plows over a gravesite creates a *beit hapras*. The area so created is *melo ma'aneh* — 100 *amah* by 100 *amah*, or *beit arba se'in* — the area needed to plant four *seah* of grain. *Rabbi Yossi* says five *seah*. In any event, it is a very large area.

Samaritans do not accept these restrictions and are therefore unreliable regarding them. However, a *baraita* introduces a nuance: *sadeh she'avad bah kever ne'eman Kuti lomar ein sham kever lepi she'eino me'id ela al gufa shel kever* — if a grave has been lost in a field, a Samaritan can be believed to say there was never a grave there at all, since he is then testifying directly on the existence of the grave itself, which is something he agrees is a biblical problem. Similarly, *ilan shemeisech al ha'aretz, ne'eman lomar ein tachtav kever* — regarding an overhanging tree, he can be trusted to say no grave was ever buried beneath it — again, testifying on a biblical issue he takes seriously.

The *Gemara* then adds that *Rav Yochanan* says this trust applies only when he demonstrates it through his own behavior — *bemehalich ube'afnei kulo* — walking throughout the entire field or under all of the trees. Merely walking through part of the field would not suffice; one might worry that he is avoiding the one spot where he suspects the grave actually lies. The *Gemara's* *chidush* here is that we do not entertain this concern — *ritzua nafka*, that there might be a strip he is avoiding — and we take his demonstrated behavior at face value.

### General Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** זֶה הַכְּלָל כּוּ׳. זֶה הַכְּלָל לְאֵתוֹיֵי מַאי? לְאֵתוֹיֵי תְּחוּמִין וְיֵין נֶסֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **This is the principle** governing the credibility of Samaritans: In the case of any matter of *halakha* that they are suspected of not fulfilling, they are not deemed credible to testify about it. The Gemara asks: **What is added** by the term: **This is the principle?** The Gemara answers: It serves **to add** that Samaritans are not deemed credible with regard to Shabbat **boundaries,** i.e., to say that a Shabbat boundary extends until a certain point, as the *halakha* of Shabbat boundaries applies by rabbinic law. **And** likewise, the Samaritans are not deemed credible with regard to the status of **wine** used for **a libation** in idol worship, as the Samaritans do not refrain from drinking wine touched by a gentile.

The *mishnah's* closing statement — *zeh haklal, davar shechashudim bo ein ne'emanim alav* — is taken by the *Gemara* to include additional Rabbinic laws such as *techumim* (the two-thousand *amah* Shabbat boundary) and *yayin nesech* (wine touched by a non-Jew). These are Rabbinic prohibitions that Samaritans do not observe, and they therefore cannot be trusted regarding them.

The overall movement of this section is significant. The *mishnah* and certain *baraitot* assumed that personal carefulness generates trustworthiness. The *Gemara* systematically displaced that assumption: Samaritans, even when personally careful, may still be misleading you. Trust must be grounded either in demonstrated behavior or in testimony touching on matters they accept as biblically binding. This, the *Gemara* concludes, is the organizing principle beneath all the individual rulings of the *mishnah*.

## Stains Body/Garment

### Body Locations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָרוֹאָה כֶּתֶם עַל בְּשָׂרָה כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַתּוּרְפָּה — טְמֵאָה, וְשֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַתּוּרְפָּה — טְהוֹרָה. עַל עֲקֵבָהּ וְעַל רֹאשׁ גּוּדָלָהּ — טְמֵאָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A woman **who sees** a blood **stain on her flesh adjacent to** her **vagina [*beit haturpa*],** i.e., a place where blood that originated in her vagina could be found, **becomes ritually impure,** as there is a concern that it originated in the uterus and is menstrual blood. **And** if it was discovered on her flesh in an area **not adjacent to** her **vagina** she remains **ritually pure,** as it certainly did not originate in the uterus. If the stain was discovered **on her heel or on the tip of her large toe,** although it is not adjacent to her vagina she is **ritually impure,** as blood from the uterus could have reached there.

With *hadran alach dam nida* — the conclusion of the previous *perek* — the *Gemara* opens *perek haroa ketem*, which marks a turning point: from here to the end of the *masechet*, the laws of *ketamim* are treated very much in their application to contemporary *halacha*, and the discussions become highly practical.

The new *mishnah* opens: *haroa ketem al besarah* — a woman who sees a stain on her own body. The fundamental question is whether the stain can be attributed to an external source. If so, there is no problem. Even if it cannot, the *tumah* remains only Rabbinic. If the stain is *keneged beit hatorfa* — opposite the genital area — she is *teme'ah*. If it is elsewhere on her body — *shelo keneged beit hatorfa* — she is *tehorah*. The *mishnah* then elaborates on what "opposite" and "not opposite" mean in practice.

### Heel/Toe

The *mishnah* specifies: *al akeivah ve'al rosh godlah* — a stain on her heel or on the top of her big toe renders her *teme'ah*. These are areas where blood can naturally fall as she walks, given the position of the body relative to the genital area.

### Calf/Foot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַל שׁוֹקָהּ וְעַל פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ — מִבִּפְנִים טְמֵאָה, מִבַּחוּץ טְהוֹרָה, וְעַל הַצְּדָדִין מִכָּאן וּמִכָּאן טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the stain was discovered **on her leg or on her feet,** if it was **on the inner** side she is **ritually impure,** as blood from the uterus could have reached there. If it was **on the outer** side she is **ritually pure, and** if it was **on the sides,** either **from here,** i.e., on the front of her leg or foot, **or from there,** i.e., on the back of her leg or foot, she is also **ritually pure,** as blood from the uterus could not have reached there.

*Al shokah ve'al parsoteha mibifnim* — on her calf (*shok* refers to the calf, not the thigh) and on the inner surface of her feet (the surfaces that face one another), she is *teme'ah*, since blood could fall to those inner-facing surfaces. *Mibachutz* — on the outside of her calf, facing away — she is *tehorah*. The distinction, then, is inside versus outside, where "inside" means the surfaces oriented toward the center of the body and where falling blood could plausibly reach, and "outside" means facing away. Front and back — *mikan umikan* — are likewise *tehorah*.

### Garment Belt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאֲתָה עַל חֲלוּקָהּ מִן הֶחָגוּר וּלְמַטָּה — טְמֵאָה, מִן הֶחָגוּר וּלְמַעְלָה — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where the woman **saw** a blood stain **on her robe,** if it was **from the belt and below** she is **ritually impure,** as blood from the uterus could have reached there; if it was **from the belt and above** she is **ritually pure.**

Turning to stains on garments: *ra'atah al chalukah min hachagor ul'matah teme'ah* — if the stain appears on her garment below the belt, she is *teme'ah*. *Min hachagor ul'ma'alah* — above the belt — she is *tehorah*, since she can assume that blood did not originate from there.

### Sleeve Stains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָאֲתָה עַל בֵּית יָד שֶׁל חָלוּק, אִם מַגִּיעַ כְּנֶגֶד בֵּית הַתּוּרְפָּה — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם לָאו — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **she saw** the stain **on the** end of the **sleeve of the robe, if** the sleeve can **reach adjacent to** her **vagina** she is **ritually impure; and if not,** i.e., if the stain is in a place on the sleeve that does not reach adjacent to the vagina, she is **ritually pure.**

Regarding a stain on the sleeve: *im magia keneged beit hatorfa* — if that portion of the sleeve is of a type that could naturally come into direct contact with the genital area, she is *teme'ah*. *Ve'im lav* — if it could not come into direct contact with that area, she is *tehorah*.

### Ein Machzikin

An important principle emerges here: one might think that even if the sleeve could not come into direct contact, it might have touched an intermediate surface — first the blood fell on one part of her body or garment, was transferred to her hand, and from there to the sleeve. The *mishnah* does not entertain this reasoning. The operative principle is *ein machzikin tumah mimakom l'makom* — we do not concern ourselves with indirect transmission of *tumah*. Only if a stain could have gotten to a particular location by direct contact do we treat it as *teme'ah*. Since all of this is in the domain of Rabbinic law, the framework is highly formalized and does not simply follow likelihood of actual contamination.

## Blanket/Shawl

### Blanket Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיְתָה פּוֹשַׁטְתּוֹ וּמִתְכַּסָּה בּוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה, כָּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנִּמְצָא בּוֹ כֶּתֶם — טְמֵאָה, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא חוֹזֵר, וְכֵן בַּפּוּלְיוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** If it was a robe which **she would remove and with which she would cover** herself **at night, wherever** on the robe **that the stain is found,** the stain renders her **ritually impure, due to** the fact **that** the robe **moves** while the woman is asleep and therefore the blood could have originated in the uterus. **And likewise with regard to a kerchief [*bapoleyos*],** no matter where the blood is found on the kerchief, the woman is impure.

A critical qualification: *hayetah pashtato umitkasah bo balailah* — if she removed the garment and used it as a blanket at night, then *kol makom shenimtza bo ketem tme'ah* — wherever on that garment a stain appears, she is *teme'ah*. The reason is *mipnei shehu chozer vechozer* — because it shifts around during sleep and therefore any part of it could have come into direct contact with any part of her body.

### Moving Garments

The same rule applies to *palyot* — a large shawl or head covering that moves about the body — which also could have touched any part of her body directly. In these cases, the location of the stain on the garment does not narrow the analysis in the way it would for a normally worn garment. Of course, the principle of *ein machzikin tumah mimakom l'makom* still applies: we are not concerned that the garment first touched one part of her body and then transferred *tumah* to another surface. The concern is only about the direct contact of the garment itself.

## Shmuel/Sensation

### Checked Ground Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ' אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בָּדְקָה קַרְקַע עוֹלָם, וְיָשְׁבָה עָלֶיהָ, וּמָצְאָה דָּם עָלֶיהָ — טְהוֹרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״ — עַד שֶׁתַּרְגִּישׁ בִּבְשָׂרָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the cases discussed in the mishna concerning a blood stain found on a woman, **Shmuel says:** If a woman **examined the ground** beneath her to see if it was clean from blood and other substances, and she found nothing, **and** subsequently she **sat upon it and** then **found blood on it,** although it might be assumed that this blood came from her, **she is ritually pure.** The reason is **as it is stated:** "And her issue **in her flesh** shall be blood, she shall be in her menstrual state seven days" (Leviticus 15:19). This verse teaches that a woman does not become impure **unless she senses,** i.e., experiences some type of sensation, **in her flesh** that she emitted blood from her uterus. Since this woman did not sense an emission of blood, she is pure.

We now arrive at what is perhaps the most consequential passage in this entire section of the *masechet* for contemporary *halacha*. *Shmuel* states: *badkah karka olam ve'yashvah aleha* — a woman checked the ground before sitting on it and confirmed there was no blood; she sat, and upon rising, found blood there. The question is almost rhetorical: where else could the blood have come from? Presumably it came from her. Yet *Shmuel* rules she is *tehorah*.

### Biblical Requirement

The basis for this ruling is the verse *be'vasarah* — in her flesh. *Shmuel* derives from this word: *ad she'targish be'vasarah* — there must be a sensation *within* her flesh. She must experience the blood as it flows from her body. If blood exits without any such sensation, she does not become a biblical *niddah*, regardless of whether we know with certainty that it came from her body.

### Woman as Subject

This ruling has implications at both the conceptual and practical levels. On the conceptual level, *halacha* throughout this *masechet* has to some degree treated the woman as an object — a physical entity from which blood either does or does not flow, with the *halacha* adjudicating based on observable evidence. *Shmuel's* ruling fundamentally shifts that framework. A critical criterion for biblical *niddah* status is now a subjective, first-person experience — her *hargashah*, her sensation — to which the *rabbis* have no direct access. This makes the woman genuinely a subject in the halakhic determination, not merely an object of it.

The consequences of this conceptual shift are also deeply practical: the *posekim* found themselves in the position of having to ask women what this sensation feels like — whether it corresponds to what we would call menstrual cramps, or something else entirely — in order to bridge the gap between *Shmuel's* formulation and the subjective reality of women's experience. The fact that the *rabbis* were consulting women to understand what the *hargashah* is illustrates how the subjective has entered into the halakhic process in an unusual way.

### Rabbinic Category

The practical consequences for contemporary *halacha* are enormous. Prior to *Shmuel*, the entire framework for *ketamim* rested on *doubt*: we could never be entirely certain a stain came from the woman, and it was precisely that uncertainty that kept *ketamim* in the Rabbinic category, allowing various leniencies to apply. But what about cases where there is *no* doubt — where a stain is found on tight-fitting underwear, on toilet paper used to wipe, where the source is definitively the woman's own body? Without *Shmuel*, one would have to treat such cases as biblical *niddah*, outside the lenient framework of *ketamim* entirely.

*Shmuel* changes everything. He says that what creates the *stain* category — as opposed to biblical *niddah* — is not doubt about origin but the *absence of hargashah*. Even when the blood is one hundred percent demonstrably from her body, if there was no sensation, she is not a biblical *niddah*. The practical effect is that it places nearly all stain discoveries, even those where origin is certain, into the Rabbinic framework of *ketamim* with all the associated leniencies. This, the *Gemara* will elaborate, is precisely the intent: *bebe'vasarah velah bishapir velah bachaticha* — the blood must touch her flesh directly, and must be accompanied by sensation, for biblical *niddah* status to be triggered.

## Challenge Shmuel

### Within Flesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ, שֶׁמְּטַמְּאָה בִּפְנִים כְּבַחוּץ! אִם כֵּן, לֵימָא קְרָא ״בְּבָשָׂר״, מַאי ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: עַד שֶׁתַּרְגִּישׁ בִּבְשָׂרָהּ. וְאַכַּתִּי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ ״בִּבְשָׂרָהּ״, וְלֹא בְּשָׁפִיר וְלֹא בַּחֲתִיכָה! תַּרְתֵּי שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can Shmuel interpret the verse in this manner? After all, **he requires this** term: **"In her flesh,"** to teach a different *halakha*, **that** a woman **becomes impure** by finding blood **inside** her body **just as** by seeing blood **outside** her body, i.e., provided that the blood is uterine blood, even if it is currently situated inside her vaginal canal, she is impure. The Gemara answers: **If so,** if it serves to teach only that blood inside is like blood outside, **let the verse say: In the flesh. What** is the reason that the verse states: **"In her flesh"? Conclude from** this term that a woman does not become impure **unless she senses in her flesh.** The Gemara asks: **And still,** Shmuel **requires** the term **"in her flesh"** to teach that she is impure only if the blood touches her flesh, **and not** through blood found **in a gestational sac, nor** through blood found **in** an amorphous **piece** of tissue that she emitted. The Gemara answers: **Conclude two** conclusions **from** this verse, as the plain meaning of the term teaches all these *halakhot*.

The *Gemara* raises a preliminary question about the word *be'vasarah*: is it not needed to teach that blood must be *within* her flesh — that is, directly contacting the vaginal wall and not contained within an embryonic sac or membrane? This is itself an important rule (*be'vasarah velo bishapir velo bachaticha*). The *Gemara* responds: *tarti shma mina* — we learn both from the single word: the requirement of direct contact with the flesh, and *Shmuel's* requirement of *hargashah*.

### Urinating Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהִיא עוֹשָׂה צְרָכֶיהָ, וְרָאֲתָה דָּם. רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: אִם עוֹמֶדֶת — טְמֵאָה, וְאִם יוֹשֶׁבֶת — טְהוֹרָה. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאַרְגִּשָׁה — יוֹשֶׁבֶת אַמַּאי טְהוֹרָה? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אַרְגִּשָׁה, וְקָתָנֵי: עוֹמֶדֶת טְמֵאָה! לְעוֹלָם דְּאַרְגִּשָׁה, וְאֵימוֹר ״הַרְגָּשַׁת מֵי רַגְלַיִם הֲוַאי״. עוֹמֶדֶת — הֲדוּר מֵי רַגְלַיִם לְמָקוֹר וְאַיְיתִי דָּם, וְיוֹשֶׁבֶת — טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinion of Shmuel, the Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a mishna at the beginning of the next chapter (59b): In the case of **a woman who is urinating and sees blood** intermingled with the urine, **Rabbi Meir says: If** she urinates while **she is standing** she is **ritually impure,** as the blood could have originated in the uterus. **And if she is sitting** she is **ritually pure,** as it is clear that the blood is from a wound. The Gemara analyzes this mishna: **What are the circumstances? If she sensed** while urinating, then in the case where **she is sitting, why is she ritually pure,** according to Shmuel? **Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where she did not sense** while urinating? **And** yet the mishna **teaches** that if she urinates while **she is standing** she is **ritually impure.** This indicates that her status does not depend on her sensing, which contradicts the statement of Shmuel. The Gemara answers: This affords no proof, as the mishna is **actually** referring to a case **where she sensed** while urinating, **and** yet since this feeling accompanied urination, one might **say** it **was the sensation of urine.** Consequently, if she urinated while **standing,** the **urine would return to** her **uterus and bring blood** with it. **But** if she urinated while **sitting** the urine cannot return to the uterus and therefore she is **pure,** as the sensation is attributed to her urine.

The *Gemara* now subjects *Shmuel's* ruling to a series of challenges. *Ta shema* — come and hear: a woman who is urinating (*oseh tzorcheha*) and sees blood in the urine. *Rabbi Meir* rules: *im omedes teme'ah ve'im yoshevet tehorah* — if she is standing when the blood appears, she is *teme'ah*; if she is sitting and urinating, she is *tehorah*, since then she may assume the blood comes from a non-uterine source.

The *Gemara* challenges: *ei de'argishah* — if she is having a *hargashah*, then why should sitting render her *tehorah*? The sensation should confirm that it is uterine blood. *Ela lav delo argishah* — must it be that she has no *hargashah*? But then, how can she be *teme'ah* even when standing? This seems to contradict *Shmuel*.

### Misidentified Sensation

The *Gemara's* answer: *le'olam de'argishah* — in fact she does have a sensation. *Ve'eima hargashat mei raglayim havei* — but perhaps that sensation is the sensation of urinating. The sensation exists, but it may be misidentified: it might be the ordinary physiological experience of urination rather than the distinctive experience of menstrual flow. Consequently, neither the presence nor the absence of sensation is decisive on its own. We must look to other evidence — her posture — to determine the likelihood of the blood's source. *Hadur mei raglayim lemakor ve'aysei dam* — when standing, the urinary flow may be drawing blood from the uterus; when sitting and urinating normally, there is no reason to assume this.

The *Gemara's* answer here establishes a pattern it will follow throughout this section: there is always a sensation, but the sensation may be ambiguous, misattributed to another bodily experience occurring simultaneously.

### Checking Cloth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: עֵד שֶׁהָיָה נָתוּן תַּחַת הַכַּר, וְנִמְצָא עָלָיו דָּם — אִם עָגוֹל טָהוֹר, וְאִם מָשׁוּךְ טָמֵא. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאַרְגִּישָׁה — עָגוֹל אַמַּאי טָהוֹר? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אַרְגִּישָׁה, וְקָתָנֵי: מָשׁוּךְ טָמֵא. לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאַרְגִּישָׁה, וְאֵימוֹר ״הַרְגָּשַׁת עֵד הֲוַאי״, מָשׁוּךְ — וַדַּאי מִגּוּפַהּ אֲתָא, עָגוֹל — טָהוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** the mishna below (58b): With regard to **an examination cloth that was placed beneath the pillow and blood was** later **found on** the cloth, and it is unclear whether it is the blood of an examination or the blood of a louse that was crushed beneath it, **if** the stain is **round** it is **ritually pure.** There is no concern that this blood might have come from her examination, as a woman examines through an act of wiping and a stain produced in this manner would not be round. **And if** the stain is **elongated** it is **ritually impure,** as this shape can be formed by an examination. The Gemara analyzes the mishna: **What are the circumstances? If she sensed,** then in the case where the stain is **round why** is it **pure? Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where she did not sense? And** yet the mishna **teaches** that **an elongated** stain is **impure.** This contradicts the opinion of Shmuel that a woman is rendered impure only if she sensed. The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is **actually** referring to a case **where she sensed, but** since she also performed an examination one might **say** this **was the sensation of the examination cloth.** Consequently, if the stain was **elongated,** as appropriate for a stain produced by an examination, the blood **certainly came from her body,** whereas if the stain was **round** it is **pure,** as this is not the usual appearance of a stain from an examination.

*Ta shema*: a checking cloth placed under the pillow — *hanasun tachas hakar* — and examined in the morning. A spot of blood is found. Given that blood of a louse was a common concern, the *mishnah* teaches: *im agol tahor* — if the spot is round, it is *tahor*, as crushed louse blood forms a round mark; *ve'im mashuch tamei* — if elongated, it is *tame'ah*, as that shape results from blood emerging through an internal check.

The *Gemara* again asks: *ei de'argishah* — if she had a sensation, why should a round spot render her *tehorah*? *Ela lav delo argishah* — if she had no sensation, how is she *teme'ah* for an elongated spot?

### Check Ambiguity

The *Gemara's* answer: *le'olam d'hirgisha, ve'eima hargashat ed havei* — she did have a sensation, but perhaps what she felt was simply the physical act of performing the internal check (*bedikah*). She experienced something, but she cannot know whether it was the sensation of the *bedikah* itself or the sensation of menstrual flow. As with urination, there is a competing explanation for the sensation, and therefore one cannot determine *niddah* status from the sensation alone; one must look to corroborating evidence, in this case the shape of the spot.

### Post-Coital Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלּוֹ — טְמֵאִין, וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן. נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ אֹתְיוֹם — טְמֵאִין, וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן. נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ לְאַחַר זְמַן — טְמֵאִים מִסָּפֵק, וּפְטוּרִין מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּאַרְגִּישָׁה, לְאַחַר זְמַן אַמַּאי פְּטוּרִין מִן הַקׇּרְבָּן? אֶלָּא לָאו דְּלָא אַרְגִּישָׁה, וְקָתָנֵי נִמְצָא עַל שֶׁלָּהּ אֹתְיוֹם טְמֵאִין וְחַיָּיבִין בְּקׇרְבָּן! לָא, לְעוֹלָם דְּאַרְגִּישָׁה, וְאֵימָא הַרְגָּשַׁת שַׁמָּשׁ הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** another mishna (14a): If blood **was found on his,** i.e., the husband's, examination cloth following intercourse, the woman and her husband are both **ritually impure** for seven days, in accordance with the *halakha* of a menstruating woman and one who engages in intercourse with her, **and they are** each **liable to** bring a sin **offering** for unwittingly performing an action punishable with excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]. If blood **was found on her** cloth **immediately** after intercourse, the woman and her husband are likewise **ritually impure** for seven days **and are** each **liable to** bring a sin **offering.** If blood **was found on her** swatch **after time** passed, they are both **ritually impure due to uncertainty,** as it is possible that the blood appeared only after intercourse, **and they are exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering.** The Gemara analyzes this mishna: **What are the circumstances? If** it is referring to a situation **where she sensed,** then in the case where blood was found on her cloth **after time** passed, **why are they exempt from** bringing **the** sin **offering? Rather, is it not** referring to a case **where she did not sense? And** yet the mishna **teaches** that if blood **was found on her** cloth **immediately** after intercourse, they are **ritually impure** for seven days **and are** each **liable to** bring a sin **offering.** Once again, this contradicts the opinion of Shmuel. The Gemara answers: **No,** the mishna is **actually** referring to a case **where she sensed, but** since she was engaging in intercourse at the time, one might **say** this **was the sensation** of the male **organ.**

*Ta shema*: a man and woman who checked themselves after relations. *Nimtza al shelo tme'im vachayavim bekorban* — if blood is found on his examining cloth, it is clear that she was bleeding during intercourse, and they must bring a *korban*. *Nimtza al shelah samuch l'shimesh* — if found on her cloth immediately after intercourse, likewise *tme'im vachayabim bekorban* — we assume the blood was already present during intercourse. *Al shelah l'achar zman* — if found on her cloth some time after — *tme'in misafek, ufatur min hakorban* — they are doubtfully *tamei* but exempt from a *korban*, as she may have begun bleeding only after intercourse concluded.

The *Gemara* again challenges: *im d'hirgisha* — if she had a sensation, *l'achar zman amai patur min hakorban*? If she sensed the onset during intercourse, there is no doubt. *Ela lav d'lo hirgisha* — must she not have had a sensation? But if so, how is she *teme'ah* even in the immediate case?

### Sex Sensation

The *Gemara's* answer follows the now-familiar pattern: *le'olam d'hirgisha, ve'eima hargashat shamash havei* — there was indeed a sensation, but it may have been the physical sensation of intercourse itself — *shamash* here referring literally to "the servant," i.e., her husband, or the act of intercourse. Once again, a concurrent physiological experience provides an alternative explanation for whatever she felt, generating enough ambiguity that one cannot determine *niddah* status from the sensation alone.

## Synthesis & Modern

### Sensation Summary

The pattern across all three cases — urination, internal checking, and intercourse — is consistent. *Shmuel's* doctrine requires *hargashah* for biblical *niddah* status. But the *Gemara* never actually concedes a case where there is definitively no sensation. In each challenge, the *Gemara* resolves: there was a sensation, but it was of uncertain nature. The woman felt *something*, but the something could have been attributed to urinating, checking, or intercourse rather than to menstrual onset. The resolution in each case is that we rely on other evidence — posture, the shape of a blood spot, timing relative to intercourse — to adjudicate the doubt. The framework thus becomes: sensation is necessary but not always sufficient; and absence of clear sensation places the matter in the domain of stains rather than biblical *niddah*.

### Modern Question

This raises the pressing question for contemporary *halacha*, as the *Gemara* itself telegraphs by setting up these cases. The *Gemara* introduced the ambiguity of sensation as a *leniency* — a way to explain why, despite the presence of a sensation, there can still be doubt, so that the woman is not necessarily a biblical *niddah*. But the same framework can be used in the stringent direction. If a woman discovers blood after urinating, or after an internal check, or after intercourse — does the *Gemara's* logic compel us to say that a sensation is assumed to have occurred, even if she does not report one? And if so, does that push the case into the more stringent category?

This question — when we presume sensation to have occurred and what consequences flow from that presumption — will be taken up as this discussion continues. It bears directly on how *posekim* treat some of the most commonly encountered scenarios in contemporary *halachah* of *niddah*.
