# Ketubot 77 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blemishes & Burden

### Stipulation & Timing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמוּמִין שֶׁבַּסֵּתֶר. אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: וְנִכְפֶּה — כְּמוּמִין שֶׁבַּסֵּתֶר דָּמֵי. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי דִּקְבִיעַ לֵיהּ זְמַן, אֲבָל לָא קְבִיעַ לֵיהּ — כְּמוּמִין שֶׁבַּגָּלוּי דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: In what** case **is this statement said? With regard to hidden blemishes.** But he cannot claim to have been unaware of visible blemishes. **Rav Naḥman said: And an epileptic is considered like a hidden blemish,** for it is possible that nobody is aware of her ailment. The Gemara comments: **And this applies** only **if** the sickness comes **at regular intervals,** as the woman and her family can conceal her illness. **But** if the attacks do **not** appear **at regular** intervals and can occur at any time, this **is considered like a visible blemish,** as it is impossible that her condition is unknown to others.

The daf opens two lines from the bottom of 76b, wrapping up the *mishnah* that addresses the situation where a husband stipulated — or perhaps even without stipulating — that his wife should have no blemishes, and blemishes were subsequently discovered. The central question is one of timing: did the blemishes predate the *kiddushin* or arise afterward?

The *mishnah*'s framework turns on location. If the blemishes are discovered while the wife was still in her father's house, the burden of proof falls on her. If they are discovered in his house, the burden falls on him. The underlying logic is that each party must prove what happened within the other's domain of possession.

### Proof Models

The *Gemara* had examined this framework through an analogy involving the exchange of animals and the case of one who sells a cow to a *shochet*, only for the animal to be discovered a *treifa*. In working through that case, the *Gemara* held up two distinct models of burden of proof. The first is the familiar principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya* — the burden falls on whoever is trying to extract money. The second model, which ultimately governs both the *shochet* case and our *mishnah*, is the *reshut* model: the burden of proof is determined by in whose possession, in whose domain, the problem was discovered. If the *shochet* finds the animal to be a *treifa* in his own *reshut* after purchase, it is his burden to prove the condition predated the sale. This result stands in some tension with the default assumption of *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*, and the *Gemara* resolves it in favor of the *reshut* model.

### Hidden Blemishes

The discussion concludes on 76b with the category of *mumin shebeseter* — blemishes that are concealed and not ordinarily exposed to observation. Here the *Chachamim* distinguish between *mumin shebegaluy*, blemishes visible to anyone, and those that are hidden. The ruling is that with respect to visible blemishes, or blemishes that could reasonably have been detected — even those covered by clothing — we presume the husband knew or could have known. This is buttressed by the bathhouse principle: since female relatives of the husband would attend the bathhouse together with the potential bride, the husband had a practical avenue through which to investigate hidden blemishes. If he did not raise an objection, we presume he was aware and consented.

### Epilepsy Case

Within this context, Rav Nachman rules that a person with epilepsy has a condition that is classified like *mumin shebeseter*, since — as Rashi explains — if the epileptic episodes occur at a fixed and predictable time, it would be possible for the woman to conceal them. She could ensure she was not observed during those intervals, so the husband cannot claim he would certainly have known. However, *vehanei milei dekvia lei zman* — this applies only when the timing of the seizures is fixed. If the timing is not fixed, *kemumin shebegaluy dami* — it is treated like a visible blemish, since there would have been no way to reliably conceal episodes that could strike at any moment.

### Case Structure

Before proceeding to the next *mishnah*, one structural point about the passage deserves clarification. The *mishnah* began with the case where the husband stipulated *al menat she'ein bach mumin* at the time of *kiddushin*, and then conducted the *chuppah* without repeating the stipulation (*kinnesa stam*). The *Gemara* raised the question of whether the second clause — *kinnesa stam* — is dependent on the prior stipulation at *kiddushin*, or whether it stands as an entirely independent case where no stipulation was made at all, neither at *kiddushin* nor at *chuppah*.

If the latter reading is adopted — that even without any stipulation, a husband may divorce a wife with certain blemishes without paying the *ketubah* — then the ruling is quite sweeping: the mere presence of certain pre-existing blemishes entitles the husband to divorce without *ketubah* payment, even in the absence of any explicit condition. This reading will bear on the *halacha l'maaseh* to be discussed shortly, particularly the *Rambam*'s ruling that even when no stipulation was made, if the wife had blemishes prior to the marriage that the husband neither knew of nor could reasonably have discovered, he is entitled to divorce her without a *ketubah*.

## Husband Blemishes

### Man Blemishes Post

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאִישׁ שֶׁנּוֹלְדוּ בּוֹ מוּמִין — אֵין כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא. אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמוּמִין הַקְּטַנִּים, אֲבָל בְּמוּמִין הַגְּדוֹלִים — כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a man who developed blemishes** after marriage, the court **does not force him to divorce** his wife. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said?** It is said **with regard to minor blemishes. However, with regard to major blemishes,** which will be defined later in the Gemara, the court **does force him to divorce** her.

The *mishnah* now turns to the reverse scenario: *ha-ish she-noldu bo mumin*, a man who develops blemishes after the marriage. The *tanna kamma* rules *ein kofin oto lehotzi* — we do not force him to divorce his wife. The wife, in other words, must tolerate a husband who has acquired blemishes during the marriage. Notably, the parallel question about forcing a husband to divorce a wife who developed blemishes does not arise in the same way, since a husband who is dissatisfied may divorce of his own accord; the *mishnah* governing blemishes of the wife concerned primarily his entitlement to do so without paying the *ketubah*.

### Gamliel's Rule

Rabban Gamliel introduces a critical distinction. *Bameh devarim amurim bemumin ktanim* — the rule that we do not force a divorce applies only to small blemishes. *Aval bemumin gedolim kofin oto lehotzi* — for large blemishes, we do force him to give his wife a *get*. Tosafot notes, as a matter of course, that forcing a divorce in this context implies that the husband must also pay the *ketubah*; the wife is not required to tolerate these conditions, and she is entitled to both the divorce and full *ketubah* payment.

The asymmetry is already apparent. When the husband is dissatisfied with his wife's blemishes, no discussion of forcing a divorce is necessary — he simply divorces her, subject only to whether he must pay the *ketubah*. When the wife is dissatisfied with the husband's blemishes, she cannot simply leave; the question is whether the *beit din* will intervene and compel a *get*.

### Girsa Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ רַב יְהוּדָה תָּנֵי נוֹלְדוּ. חִיָּיא בַּר רַב תָּנֵי הָיוּ. מַאן דְּאָמַר נוֹלְדוּ, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן הָיוּ — (דְּקָסָבְרָה) [דְּהָא סְבַרָה] וְקַבִּילָה. מַאן דְּאָמַר הָיוּ, אֲבָל נוֹלְדוּ — לֹא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda teaches** the mishna in accordance with the version quoted above: The man **developed** blemishes after marrying his wife. Conversely, **Ḥiyya bar Rav teaches:** The man **had** blemishes prior to the marriage. The Gemara clarifies the difference between the two opinions: **The one who says** that the man who **developed** blemishes after marriage does not have to divorce his wife says that the same *halakha* applies **all the more so** to one who **had** blemishes beforehand, **as she was aware** of them **and accepted** them. However, **the one who says** that the mishna is referring to one who **had** blemishes prior to his marriage would say that only in that case he is not compelled to divorce her, **but** if **they developed** after the marriage this is **not** the *halakha*, as she did not marry him under such conditions.

The *Gemara* records a dispute over the correct text of the *mishnah*. Yehuda's version of the text reads *noldu* — the blemishes arose after the marriage. Chiyya bar Rav's version reads *hayu* — the blemishes existed even before the wedding.

The *Gemara* reasons as follows. *Man d'amar noldu* — according to the reading that the blemishes arose after the marriage, and the *tanna kamma* rules we do not force a divorce, then *kol sheken hayu*: certainly if the blemishes existed from before the marriage, and she presumably saw them and accepted them, there would be no grounds to force a divorce.

The reverse also follows. *Man d'amar hayu* — if the *tanna kamma* is speaking of pre-existing blemishes and still rules we do not force a divorce, then *aval noldu* — when the blemishes arose after the marriage and she had no opportunity to anticipate or consent to them, perhaps in that case we *would* force a divorce, since she never accepted these conditions.

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן, אָמַר רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמוּמִין קְטַנִּים, אֲבָל בְּמוּמִין גְּדוֹלִים — כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״נוֹלְדוּ״, הַיְינוּ דְּשָׁאנֵי בֵּין גְּדוֹלִים לִקְטַנִּים. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר ״הָיוּ״ — מָה לִי גְּדוֹלִים מָה לִי קְטַנִּים? הָא סְבַרָה וְקַבִּילָה!
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel said: In what** case **is this statement said?** This is said **with regard to minor blemishes, but with regard to major blemishes** the court **forces him to divorce** her. The Gemara poses a question: **Granted, according to the one who says** that the correct version is: **Developed** blemishes, **this is** the reason **that there is a difference between major and minor** blemishes, as only major blemishes are grounds for divorce. **But according to the one who says** that the correct version is: **Had** blemishes, **what** difference is it **to me** whether they were **major** blemishes, and **what** difference is it **to me** whether they were **minor** ones? Either way, **she was aware** of them **and accepted** them.

Rabban Gamliel's distinction between *mumin ktanim* and *mumin gedolim* now comes into focus in light of the *girsa* debate. The *Gemara* observes that the distinction makes better sense according to the *noldu* reading. If the case involves blemishes that arose after the marriage and the *tanna kamma* says we do not force a divorce, Rabban Gamliel can logically respond: for small blemishes I agree, but for large blemishes we will force a divorce.

If instead the case is pre-existing blemishes and the *tanna kamma* says she implicitly accepted them by going through with the marriage, it becomes harder to understand why large blemishes would be treated differently. The *Gemara* answers on behalf of the *hayu* reading: *savrah hi shehi yacholah lekabel ve'achshav einah yacholah lekabel* — even if she thought she could tolerate it at the time, perhaps she was swept up in the romance and persuaded herself it would be manageable. It now turns out that she genuinely cannot bear it. The fact that she said she was willing earlier cannot be held against her when the condition is one that is objectively intolerable. This is the logic attributed to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position: for genuinely large blemishes, her original acceptance does not bind her permanently.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּסְבוּרָה הִיא שֶׁיְּכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל, וְעַכְשָׁיו אֵין יְכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל. וְאֵלּוּ הֵן מוּמִין גְּדוֹלִים? פֵּירֵשׁ רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: [כְּגוֹן] נִיסְמֵית עֵינוֹ, נִקְטְעָה יָדוֹ, וְנִשְׁבְּרָה רַגְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: In the case of major blemishes she can claim **that she** initially **thought that she could accept** a husband with such blemishes, **but now** that she is married she realizes that she **cannot accept** such an arrangement. The Gemara inquires: **And what are** these **major blemishes** of a husband that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel considers grounds for divorce? **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel explained: For example,** if **his eye was blinded,** or **his hand cut off, or his leg broken.**

The examples the *Gemara* gives of *mumin gedolim* according to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel include blindness in one eye, amputation of a hand, or loss of a foot — severe disabilities. In those times, before modern prosthetics and medical intervention, these were of course particularly serious conditions.

### Halacha Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל. רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: הֲלָכָה כְּדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated: Rabbi Abba bar Yaakov** said that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. Rava** said that **Rav Naḥman said: The *halakha* is in accordance with the statement of the Rabbis** that there is no difference between minor and major blemishes.

Rav Abba bar Yaakov cites Rabbi Yochanan: *halacha keRabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. This would mean that even for blemishes that arose after the marriage, if they are sufficiently serious, we force a divorce.

Against this, Rava citing Rav Nachman rules *halacha k'divrei Chachamim* — the law follows the sages who do not force a divorce, even presumably for new and large blemishes.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁשָּׁנָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בְּמִשְׁנָתֵנוּ — הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, חוּץ מֵעָרֵב, וְצַיְדוֹן, וּרְאָיָה אַחֲרוֹנָה. אָמוֹרָאֵי נִינְהוּ וְאַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara poses a question: **And did Rabbi Yoḥanan** actually **say so,** that the *halakha* follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? **But Rabba bar bar Ḥanna said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel taught in our Mishna, the *halakha* is in accordance with him, apart from** three cases: The *halakha* of **a guarantor** (*Bava Batra* 173b); the *halakha* he stated with regard to the divorce case in **Sidon** (*Gittin* 74a); and the **latter** of his disputes with the Rabbis with regard to the halakhot of **evidence** (*Sanhedrin* 31a). Since Rabbi Yoḥanan issued a statement that the *halakha* is in accordance with Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in all but three exceptional cases, why would it be necessary for him to issue a special ruling in the present discussion? The Gemara answers: **They are *amora'im*, and** they disagree **with regard to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan.** Rabbi Abba bar Yaakov maintains that Rabbi Yoḥanan did not issue a general directive, but rather provided a separate ruling for each case.

The *Gemara* then raises a difficulty: Rabbi Yochanan had himself endorsed the general principle — cited by Rabbah bar bar Chanah — that wherever Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel appears in our *Mishnah*, the *halacha* follows him, with only three exceptions (*areiv*, *Tzidon*, and *ra'ayah acharonah*). How then could Rabbi Yochanan rule against Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel here? The *Gemara* resolves this: *amora'ei ninhu ve'aliba d'Rabbi Yochanan* — there is a dispute among *Amora'im* as to what Rabbi Yochanan's view actually was, and the authority who ruled *halacha k'divrei Chachamim* represents one valid position attributed to Rabbi Yochanan.

The combined effect of the strict readings of the *Gemara*, if one follows Rav Nachman, is striking: even a new blemish, even a large one, will not suffice to force a divorce against the husband's will. The asymmetry between husband and wife — already noted in connection with pre-existing blemishes and the binding force of *kiddushin* — is here magnified. If the wife has pre-existing blemishes, in certain circumstances the *kiddushin* itself may be void as a *mekach ta'ut*. If the husband has pre-existing blemishes, we have noted that the *Gemara*'s assumption is that she is more tolerant and the *kiddushin* remains binding. And even for new blemishes of the husband, the law may decline to force a divorce at all.

## Intolerable Conditions

### Disgusting Professions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא: מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין, וּבַעַל פּוֹלִיפּוּס, וְהַמְקַמֵּץ, וְהַמְצָרֵף נְחוֹשֶׁת, וְהַבּוּרְסִי. בֵּין שֶׁהָיוּ עַד שֶׁלֹּא נִישְּׂאוּ, וּבֵין מִשֶּׁנִּישְּׂאוּ נוֹלְדוּ. וְעַל כּוּלָּן אָמַר רַבִּי מֵאִיר: אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִתְנָה עִמָּהּ, יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר: ״סְבוּרָה הָיִיתִי שֶׁאֲנִי יְכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל, וְעַכְשָׁיו אֵינִי יְכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these** are the defects **for which** the court **forces him to divorce** her: One **afflicted with boils; or one who has a polyp; or one who** works as **a gatherer, or one who** works as **a melder of copper, or one who** works as **a tanner** of hides, all of whose work involves handling foul-smelling materials. **Whether** he **had** these defects **before they** got **married, or whether they developed after they** got **married,** the court forces them to divorce. **And with regard to all of these, Rabbi Meir said: Even though he stipulated with her** ahead of time that he suffers from this particular ailment or this is his line of work, **she can** nevertheless demand a divorce and **say: I thought I could accept** this issue **but now** I realize **I cannot accept** it.

A new *mishnah* opens: *ve'eilu shekofin oto lehotzi* — these are cases in which, notwithstanding the general principle that we do not force a husband to divorce, we nonetheless compel a *get*. The list includes *mukei shechin* — one afflicted with boils or serious skin disease; *ba'al polipos* — a condition the *Gemara* will define shortly; *hamikametz* — a gatherer, which the *Gemara* identifies as one who collects dog excrement for industrial use; *hametzaref nechoshet* — a copper smelter; and *habursi* — a tanner. The *mishnah* specifies explicitly that these cases apply *bein shehayu ad shelo nis'u u'vein mishenis'u noladu* — whether these conditions or professions existed before the marriage or arose afterward.

The common thread among these cases is not disability or impairment but rather noxious odor and conditions that are profoundly repellent. The contrast with the *mumin gedolim* of the previous *mishnah* is notable: losing a limb or going blind — severe by any measure — does not suffice to force a divorce under the majority view, yet persistent foul odors and revolting work conditions do. The category of the intolerable, apparently, is not defined by objective severity of physical impairment alone, but by what renders cohabitation genuinely unendurable.

### Rabbi Meir Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מְקַבֶּלֶת הִיא עַל כׇּרְחָהּ, חוּץ מִמּוּכֵּה שְׁחִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּמִקָּתוֹ. מַעֲשֶׂה בְּצַיְדוֹן בְּבוּרְסִי אֶחָד שֶׁמֵּת, וְהָיָה לוֹ אָח בּוּרְסִי. אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: יְכוֹלָה הִיא שֶׁתֹּאמַר ״לְאָחִיךָ הָיִיתִי יְכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל, וּלְךָ אֵינִי יְכוֹלָה לְקַבֵּל״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** If she initially agreed **she must accept it against her will, apart from** a situation in which her husband is **afflicted with boils.** In that case the Rabbis concede that he must divorce her, **because** the disease **consumes** his flesh when they engage in marital relations. The mishna relates an additional account: **An incident** occurred **in Sidon involving a certain tanner who died** childless, **and he had a brother** who was also **a tanner.** This brother was required to enter into levirate marriage with the widow. **The Sages said: She can say: I could accept** living with a tanner **for your brother but I cannot accept it for you,** and therefore he must perform *ḥalitza* with her.

Regarding all of these cases, Rabbi Meir adds: *af al pi shehitnah immah* — even if the husband explicitly made a condition with his wife before the marriage and she agreed, nevertheless *yacholah hi shetomar savrah hayiti she'ani yacholah lekabel ve'achshav eini yacholah lekabel* — she may say: I thought I could manage, and now I find I cannot. Even an explicit pre-marital agreement accepting these conditions cannot bind her permanently when the conditions are of this category.

The *Chachamim* qualify this: if she made an explicit agreement, generally she is bound by it and cannot retract — *except* in the case of *mukei shechin*, the one afflicted with boils, which we will return to below.

### Tosafot Safeguard

Tosafot raises an obvious concern: if the rule is that even an explicit pre-nuptial agreement can be set aside, and the consequence of forcing a divorce is that the husband must pay the *ketubah*, then every opportunistic woman could agree to marry a tanner or gatherer, execute the marriage, and then declare she cannot tolerate the conditions — effectively engineering a *ketubah* payment. The *Gemara* acknowledges this worry implicitly. Tosafot suggests that in a case where the *beit din* perceives fraudulent scheming, it may still force the divorce but decline to award the *ketubah*. The assessment is ultimately left to the judges, who must evaluate whether the woman's claim of intolerance is genuine or manufactured.

### Polipus Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״בַּעַל פּוֹלִיפּוּס״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: רֵיחַ הַחוֹטֶם. בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: רֵיחַ הַפֶּה. רַב אַסִּי מַתְנִי אִיפְּכָא. וּמַנַּח בַּהּ סִימָנָא: שְׁמוּאֵל לָא פָּסֵיק פּוּמֵּיהּ מִכּוּלֵּיהּ פִּירְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara inquires about several unclear terms which appear in the mishna: **What is one who has a polyp? Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** This is one who has **a** foul **odor of the nose. It was taught in a *baraita*:** A polyp is **a** foul **odor of the mouth. Rav Asi teaches the reverse,** that Shmuel is the one who said a polyp is odor of the mouth. **And he provided a mnemonic** device **for** his opinion: **Shmuel did not close his mouth from our entire chapter,** meaning that he studied it and commented on it extensively. This statement was formulated in a way that contains a hint that Shmuel's opinion involves the mouth.

The *Gemara* asks: *mai ba'al polippus* — what exactly is this condition? Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel says *rei'ach hachotem* — a malodorous discharge from the nose, some kind of persistent nasal odor. A *baraita* however teaches *rei'ach hapeh* — bad breath. Rav Assi teaches the reverse attribution: he has Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel as the authority for bad breath, and the *baraita* as the source for the nasal condition. He offers a mnemonic to remember: Shmuel's name is associated with the mouth, so Shmuel is the one who says it is bad breath.

This classification prompts a broader observation. The *mishnah* listed among the *mumin gedolim* that do not suffice to force a divorce: blindness in an eye, loss of a hand, loss of a foot. These are grievous physical injuries. Yet now we see that persistent bad breath or chronic nasal discharge is classified in a *more severe* category — one that compels a divorce even over explicit prior agreement. The criterion, evidently, is not the objective gravity of the physical condition but the subjective unendurability of living with it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְקַמֵּץ. מַאי ״מְקַמֵּץ״? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: זֶה הַמְקַבֵּץ צוֹאַת כְּלָבִים. מֵיתִיבִי: ״מְקַמֵּץ״ זֶה בּוּרְסִי! וּלְטַעְמָיךְ תִּיקְשֵׁי לָךְ מַתְנִיתִין: ״הַמְקַמֵּץ וְהַמְצָרֵף נְחוֹשֶׁת וְהַבּוּרְסִי״!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna taught, in the list of defects for which the husband is forced to divorce his wife: **Or one who** works as **a gatherer.** The Gemara asks: **What is** the meaning of **a gatherer? Rav Yehuda said: This** is referring to **one who gathers dog excrement** for use in tanning. The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **A gatherer, this** is **a tanner.** The Gemara responds: **And according to your reasoning, the mishna** itself **should present a difficulty for you,** as it states: **One who** works as **a gatherer, or one who** works as **a melder of copper, or one who** works as **a tanner** of hides, which indicates that the mishna holds that the gatherer and the tanner are not the same.

The *mishnah* also records a case from Tzidon (*ma'aseh beTzidon*) involving a tanner who died, leaving his brother — also a tanner — as the potential *yavam* for his widow. The *Chachamim* ruled that the widow may say: *le'achicha hayiti yacholah lekabel, ulecha eini yacholah lekabel* — I could tolerate your brother because I genuinely loved him, but I cannot tolerate you. The practical effect is that the rabbis will strongly push the *yavam* to perform *chalitzah* rather than *yibbum*, since the widow has grounds to refuse the levirate union with a man in this profession with whom she had no prior bond.

### Profession Details

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מַתְנִיתִין לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן בְּבוּרְסִי גָּדוֹל, כָּאן בְּבוּרְסִי קָטָן. אֶלָּא לְרַב יְהוּדָה קַשְׁיָא! תַּנָּאֵי הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: ״מְקַמֵּץ״ זֶה בּוּרְסִי, וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: זֶה הַמְקַמֵּץ צוֹאַת כְּלָבִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Granted, the mishna** is **not difficult,** as one can say that **here,** where the tanner is listed separately from the one who gathers, it is referring **to a large**-scale **tanner,** and **there,** when the *baraita* states that a gatherer is a tanner, it is speaking **of a** small-scale **tanner. But according to Rav Yehuda it is difficult.** The Gemara answers: **It is** a dispute between ***tanna'im*, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A gatherer, this** is **a tanner, and some say: This** is **one who gathers dog excrement.** Rav Yehuda follows this latter opinion.

The *Gemara* turns to define each profession. *Mah mikameitz* — what does the *mikametz* gather? Rav Yehuda says: *zeh mikabetz tzo'at kelavim* — this is one who collects dog excrement. Rashi explains that dog excrement was needed as a preparation material, applied to hides before washing in connection with the laundering or tanning process. Tosafot adds that the connection to tanning is direct, since dog excrement was used in the tanning process itself.

The *Gemara* raises an objection: if a *mikametz* is essentially a *bursi* — a tanner — then why does the *mishnah* list them separately? The *Gemara* answers: *kan beborsi raba, kan beborsi zuta* — one refers to a large-scale tanner and the other to a small-scale one, with Tosafot noting they may differ in the intensity of the smell or the nature of the work. The *Gemara* then notes that *tannai hi* — this is in fact a dispute among *Tanna'im*: one view identifies the *mikametz* with the tanner, while another says the *mikametz* is specifically one who collects dog excrement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַמְצָרֵף נְחוֹשֶׁת וְהַבּוּרְסִי. מַאי ״מְצָרֵף נְחוֹשֶׁת״? רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: חָשְׁלֵי דּוּדֵי. רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר: זֶה הַמְחַתֵּךְ נְחוֹשֶׁת מֵעִיקָּרוֹ. תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: אֵיזֶהוּ ״מְצָרֵף״? זֶה הַמְחַתֵּךְ נְחוֹשֶׁת מֵעִיקָּרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And a coppersmith and a tanner.** The Gemara poses a question: **What is** the meaning of **a coppersmith? Rav Ashi said: A kettle smith,** that is, one who beats copper in order to make kettles; his handling of copper leaves him with a bad odor. **Rabba bar bar Ḥanna said: This is one who hews copper from its source** in the ground. The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabba bar bar Ḥanna: Who is a coppersmith? This is one who hews copper from its source.**

For *metzaref nechoshet* — the copper smelter — two opinions are offered. Rav Ashi says it refers to one who beats out copper vessels, working with molten copper to shape pots and implements. Rabbah bar bar Chanah says it is one who extracts copper from its original ore — a *braita* supports this latter position. Both involve extremely noxious conditions; as one might note, processing metal sulfide ores produces hydrogen sulfide gas, the characteristic smell of rotten eggs.

### Food Refusal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר ״אֵינִי זָן וְאֵינִי מְפַרְנֵס״ — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָּה. אֲזַל רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אַמְרַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתָּא קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל. אָמַר: אַכְּסוּהּ שְׂעָרֵי לְאֶלְעָזָר. עַד שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא — יִכְפּוּהוּ לָזוּן.
>
> **Translation:** § As the mishna discusses situations in which the court forces the husband to divorce his wife, the Gemara mentions a similar case. **Rav said:** A husband **who says: I will not sustain** my wife **and I will not provide a livelihood** for her, **must divorce** her **and give her** the payment of her **marriage contract. Rabbi Elazar went** and **recited** this ***halakha* before Shmuel.** Shmuel **said: Feed [*akhsuha*] barley,** animal fodder, **to Elazar.** In other words, he has spoken nonsense, as **rather than forcing him to divorce** her, it would be better for them **to force him to sustain** his wife.

Rav introduces a further case: *ha'omer eini zan ve'eini mefarnes* — a husband who declares he will not support his wife financially, refusing to provide food and maintenance. The ruling is *yotzi veyitein ketubah* — he is forced to divorce and pay the *ketubah*.

Rabbi Elazar presented this ruling before Shmuel. Shmuel responded with notable sharpness: *achisuhu se'arei le'Elazar* — feed Elazar barley, meaning this ruling deserves scorn. Shmuel's objection was: *ad shekofin oto lehotzi yichpuhu lazun* — rather than forcing a divorce, simply force him to fulfill his obligation to support her. Why go straight to compelled divorce?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב: אֵין אָדָם דָּר עִם נָחָשׁ בִּכְפִיפָה. כִּי סְלֵיק רַבִּי זֵירָא, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי בִּנְיָמִין בַּר יֶפֶת דְּיָתֵיב וְקָאָמַר לַהּ, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: עַל דָּא אַכְּסוּהּ שְׂעָרִין לְאֶלְעָזָר בְּבָבֶל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Rav** respond to this argument? He bases his ruling on the principle that **a person does not reside in a basket,** i.e., in close quarters, **with a snake.** In other words, a woman cannot share her life with a man who provides for her needs only when compelled to do so by the court. The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Zeira ascended** to Eretz Yisrael, **he found Rabbi Binyamin bar Yefet sitting and reciting** this *halakha* of Rav's **in the name of Rabbi Yoḥanan. He said to him: On account of this** matter **they fed Elazar with barley in Babylonia.**

Rav's response invokes the principle *ein adam dar im nachash bekififa* — a person cannot live together with a snake in the same basket. The force of this is that a husband who must be compelled by the *beit din* to feed his wife has revealed a depth of hostility that makes the marriage genuinely unlivable. The existence of a theoretical remedy — forcing him to pay the food bill — does not cure the underlying marital breakdown. Indeed, requiring a court order to fulfill the most basic of marital obligations is itself a mark of an intolerable situation, and a *get* is the appropriate remedy.

This case is instructive in illuminating the concept of intolerability. Noxious professions are intolerable; so is a marriage in which the husband's obligations can only be extracted through coercion. These are grouped together as conditions that warrant compelled divorce, in contrast to physical disabilities and impairments, however severe, which — under the majority view — do not.

## Forced Divorce Scope

### Rav Assi Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: אֵין מְעַשִּׂין אֶלָּא לִפְסוּלוֹת. כִּי אַמְרִיתַהּ קַמֵּיהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל, אֲמַר: כְּגוֹן אַלְמָנָה לְכֹהֵן גָּדוֹל, וּגְרוּשָׁה וַחֲלוּצָה לְכֹהֵן הֶדְיוֹט, מַמְזֶרֶת וּנְתִינָה לְיִשְׂרָאֵל, בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְנָתִין וּלְמַמְזֵר. אֲבָל נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְשָׁהָה עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים וְלֹא יָלְדָה — אֵין כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues to discuss cases in which a husband is compelled to divorce his wife. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav Asi said:** The court **forces** men to divorce their wives **only** if they were married **to** women **unfit** to marry them. **When I recited this** *halakha* **before Shmuel, he said:** This applies to cases such as, **for example, a widow** married **to a High Priest, a divorcée or a *yevama* who underwent *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*]** married **to a common priest, a daughter born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship [*mamzeret*], or a Gibeonite woman** married **to an Israelite,** or **a daughter of an Israelite** married **to a Gibeonite or to a *mamzer*.** In all of these cases the marriage is prohibited by Torah law. **But** if someone **married a woman and stayed with her** for **ten years and she did not give birth,** although he is guilty of neglecting the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply, the court **does not force him** to divorce her.

Rav Assi introduces a general limiting principle: *ein masin ela psulot* — the mechanism of forced divorce applies only in cases of marriages that are *psulot*, meaning prohibited unions. His paradigm cases are a *kohen* married to a *grusha*, a *grusha* or *chalutzah* married to a *kohen hedyot*, a *mamzeret* or *netinah* married to a *Yisrael*, or a *bat Yisrael* married to a *mamzer* or *netin*. In these situations, an active biblical violation is ongoing, and the *beit din* has grounds to intervene.

### Childlessness Rule

What does this principle come to exclude? The *Gemara* indicates that Rav Assi's limitation is directed at the case of *nasa ishah ve-sha'ah imah eser shanim velo yaldah* — a man who has been married to a woman for ten years without children. Even though there is a *mitzvah* of *pru u'revu*, the *Gemara* rules — according to Rav Assi — *ein kofin oto*, we do not force a divorce. This is because the situation differs fundamentally from the cases of prohibited marriages or intolerable conditions.

In all the cases discussed until now, at least one party is unhappy. The wife wants to leave the smelly tanner, or the husband wishes to divorce the wife with blemishes. The question was whether the *beit din* would intervene to compel the outcome one party sought. Here, however, the *Gemara* imagines a couple who are both entirely content with their marriage. They have not been able to have children — perhaps through no fault of either party — but they have no desire to separate. The *beit din* would be coming in from the outside to dissolve a happy marriage. That is a very different kind of intervention, and one that Rav Assi limits to cases of active biblical prohibition.

### Words vs Force

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר אֲבִימִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֲפִילּוּ נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְשָׁהָה עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים וְלֹא יָלְדָה — כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ. תְּנַן, אֵלּוּ שֶׁכּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ לְהוֹצִיא: מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין וּבַעַל פּוֹלִיפּוּס. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְרַב אַסִּי: דְּרַבָּנַן קָתָנֵי, דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא לָא קָתָנֵי. אֶלָּא לְרַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר אֲבִימִי, לִיתְנֵי נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְשָׁהָה עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים וְלֹא יָלְדָה — כּוֹפִין אוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Taḥalifa bar Avimi** said that **Shmuel said: Even** in the case of someone who **married a woman and stayed with her** for **ten years and she did not give birth,** the court **forces him** to divorce her, as he is in violation of a positive mitzva. The Gemara poses a question: **We learned** in the mishna: **And these are** the defects **for** which the court **forces him to divorce** his wife: One **afflicted with boils or one who has a polyp. Granted, according to Rav Asi,** only cases in which the court compels a divorce **by rabbinic** law **are taught** in the mishna, but those that are **by Torah** law, such as a widow married to a High Priest, **are not taught. However, according to Rav Taḥalifa bar Avimi, let** the mishna also **teach** that if he **married a woman and stayed with her** for **ten years and she did not give birth,** the court **forces him** to divorce her, as this is also a rabbinic enactment.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּמִילֵּי, הָא בְּשׁוֹטֵי. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא: ״בִּדְבָרִים לֹא יִוָּסֶר עָבֶד״! אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: הָא וְהָא בְּשׁוֹטֵי,
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said:** This is **not difficult,** as in **this** case the mishna discusses someone who is forced to divorce his wife **by verbal** means alone, but in **that** case it is referring to compelling him **by** beating him with **rods.** Although the court does compel a man to divorce his wife if she has not had children, the court does so only by speaking with him. **Rabbi Abba strongly objects to this:** Can there be a *halakha* of coercion by verbal means alone? But the verse states: **"A servant will not be corrected by words"** (Proverbs 29:19). **Rather, Rabbi Abba said:** Both **this and that** are referring to coercion **by** beating him with **rods,**

Rav Nachman offers a different analysis that attempts to reconcile the competing positions: *ha bedivari, ha beshoti* — one category of intervention uses words and persuasion; the other uses physical compulsion. Rav Nachman suggests that in the case of childlessness, one might indeed intervene, but only through persuasion and moral pressure, not through physical force. Since the *mishnah* is cataloguing cases of genuine compulsion (*kofin*), the childlessness case simply does not belong in that list — even according to those who hold that some intervention is warranted.

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם, כִּי אָמְרָה ״הָוְיָנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ״ — שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ. הָכָא, אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמְרָה ״הָוְיָנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ״ — לָא שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ. וַהֲרֵי מוּכֵּה שְׁחִין, דְּאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲמָרָהּ ״הָוְיָנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ״ — לָא שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ. דִּתְנַן: חוּץ מִמּוּכֵּה שְׁחִין, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמְּמִקָּתוֹ וְקָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** but **there,** with regard to the blemishes specified in the mishna, **if she says: I** want **to be with him, we leave her** alone with him and do not force a divorce, whereas **here,** in the case of a woman who has not given birth, **even though she says: I** want **to be with him, we do not leave her** alone, as her husband is neglecting the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** there is the case of a husband **afflicted with boils, where even though she says: I** want **to be with him, we do not leave her** alone with him. **As we learned** in the mishna: **Apart from** a situation where her husband is **afflicted with boils, because** it **consumes** his flesh. **And** yet this *halakha* **is taught** in the same mishna as the others, which indicates that our above analysis is incorrect.

Rava challenges this resolution by citing a verse from *Mishlei*: *bedevarim lo yivaser eved* — a stubborn servant will not be disciplined through words. The implication is that if the couple is resistant to persuasion, words are ultimately futile. Therefore, if one believes the childlessness case warrants *kefiya* — compulsion — it would logically have to be the same physical force used in other cases. The distinction between words and force collapses for an obstinate party. So we are back to the question: if one rules *kofin* in the childlessness case, why is it absent from our *mishnah*?

### Happy Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם כִּי אָמְרָה ״דָּיְירָנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ בְּסָהֲדֵי״ — שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ. הָכָא אַף עַל גַּב דְּאָמְרָה ״דָּיְירָנָא בַּהֲדֵיהּ בְּסָהֲדֵי״ — לָא שָׁבְקִינַן לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Even so, there is a difference between these cases, as **there,** with regard to a man with boils, **if she says: I** am willing to **live with him** under the supervision **of witnesses;** that is, I will not seclude myself with him but I will nevertheless remain his wife, **we leave her** alone. However, **here, even though she says: I** will **live with him** under the supervision **of witnesses, we do not leave her** alone, but rather force him to divorce her.

Rava resolves this by identifying the essential conceptual distinction. In all the cases in the *mishnah*, the issue is whether one party can be compelled against resistance — the husband does not want to divorce, but the wife insists, or vice versa. The *mishnah* governs forced divorce when one party is unhappy. *Hatam, ki amrah ha zina, u'v'hadei shafkinan* — if in those cases the wife says she is content to remain married, we allow it. The *mishnah* is addressing the unhappy party's claim.

The childlessness case is structurally different. *Af al gav de'amrah ha zina u'v'hadei shafkinan, lo shafkinan lah* — even if both parties declare they are happy to remain together, we do not allow it, because we are forcing ourselves into a genuinely happy marriage on grounds of a *mitzvah* they are not fulfilling. That is why it is in a different category and not listed in the *mishnah*.

The *Gemara* raises an apparent counter-example: what about *mukei shechin* — the man with boils? There too, even if the wife says she is willing to remain married, we override her consent for his benefit, since sexual relations aggravate his condition. Does this not also involve disrupting a nominally consensual marriage? The *Gemara* distinguishes: in the *mukei shechin* case, if she is willing to maintain the marriage but the couple agrees to abstain from intimacy, and she can arrange to have witnesses ensure the separation of bedrooms, *shafkinan lah* — we allow the marriage to continue, because the concern was specifically about the harm caused by sexual activity. Remove the sex, and there is no obstacle to the marriage continuing.

In the childlessness case, by contrast, even separate bedrooms are no solution. *Lo shafkinan lah* — we do not allow the marriage to continue under any arrangement, because the problem cannot be sidestepped. The entire marriage must be dissolved if one insists on intervention.

The upshot of the discussion is that the *mishnah*'s catalogue of forced divorces all involve a complaining party, and the *beit din*'s role is to adjudicate that claim. Forced dissolution of a happy marriage is a categorically different and more radical intervention — reserved for cases of active biblical prohibition, and contested even there in the context of non-fulfillment of *pru u'revu*. Whether physical force is warranted in such cases, and whether the *mishnah*'s silence on the childlessness case is because no intervention is warranted or merely because its character differs, is the question left unresolved by the *Gemara*.

## Practical Halacha

### Rama Ruling

The Rama, in *Even HaEzer* 154, rules: *d'bazman hazeh eino minhag lichof klal* — in our times, it is not the practice to ever force a divorce. This sweeping statement raises the question of its scope. Does it apply only to a case like childlessness — where the *Gemara* itself was uncertain about the propriety of intervening in what may be a happy marriage — or does it extend even to cases of active prohibited unions, such as a *kohen* married to a *grusha*? The Rama's language is broad: *lichof klal*, not to force at all. In an era when Jewish communities retained a degree of juridical autonomy and the physical means to enforce their rulings, this was a deliberate policy choice — not an admission of inability, but a principled withdrawal from coercive marital intervention, particularly where the marriage itself is happy.

### Rambam Pre-marital

Turning to the *Rambam*, chapter 25 of *Hilchot Ishut*: *hamotzi isha stam*, a man who marries a woman without explicit stipulation, and *nimtzu bah mumin*, blemishes are found on her — *tetze belo ketubah*, she leaves without a *ketubah*. The *Rambam* applies this even in the absence of an explicit condition, provided the husband neither knew of the blemishes nor had reasonable grounds to discover them. The *Rambam* then elaborates on the distinction between *mum shebegaluy* and *mum shebeseser*, and the bathhouse principle. He even addresses an interesting nuance: what if the husband has no relatives in the city to send to the bathhouse, but has friends? Can he send friends to investigate? The *Rambam* seems to treat this as a factor relevant to what counts as reasonable available knowledge.

The upshot: even without any stipulation at *kiddushin*, if the wife had pre-existing blemishes that the husband could not have known about, he is entitled to divorce her without paying a *ketubah*. His claim is essentially that the marriage was entered under false pretenses — a *mekach ta'ut* — even if the *kiddushin* itself remains formally binding. The absence of a stipulation does not obligate him to pay when the foundational premise of the marriage was violated.

### Rambam Post-marital

The *Rambam* draws a clear line at timing. If the blemishes arose *after* the marriage — *achar shenasa* — then these are circumstances neither party could have anticipated, and the husband cannot invoke them to avoid *ketubah* payment. If he wants to divorce her, he may, but he must pay the *ketubah*.

On the husband's side, the *Rambam* rules: *ha-ish she-noldu bo mumin achar she-nasa* — a man who develops blemishes after the marriage, *afilu niktea yado o nismis eino*, even if his hand is amputated or his eye goes blind, *ein kofin oto lehotzi veleiten ketubah* — we do not force him to divorce and pay the *ketubah*. If the wife absolutely refuses to remain in the marriage, she may obtain a *get*, but the *Rambam* rules she leaves *belo ketubah kedin kol hamordot* — without a *ketubah*, as the law of a *moredet*.

One might ask whether this is truly asymmetrical or simply parallel: if she develops blemishes after marriage, he must pay the *ketubah* if he divorces her; if he develops blemishes, she is not forced to stay but she loses the *ketubah* if she leaves. The structural parallel holds in one respect — neither is blamed for post-marital conditions. But the asymmetry surfaces clearly: he retains the effective power of decision. If she develops blemishes after the marriage, it is his choice whether to stay or divorce (paying the *ketubah*). If he develops blemishes, the *Rambam* says she can insist on divorce, and *kofin* may apply if she genuinely insists — but she will not recover the *ketubah*. He cannot be blamed, but the consequences fall on her.

The *Rambam* continues: *nasa ishah ve-sha'ah imah eser shanim velo yaldah kofin oto lehotzi veyiten ketubah* — if a man was married for ten years without children, we do force him to divorce and pay the *ketubah*. The *Rambam* rules that intervention is warranted in this case, even though the couple may be happy — a position that the *Gemara* left contested.

### Gender Asymmetry

The asymmetry emerging from all of this is substantial. Where the wife has pre-existing blemishes, the husband may divorce without *ketubah*, and in certain readings the *kiddushin* itself may be voided as a *mekach ta'ut*. Where the husband has pre-existing blemishes, the *Gemara*'s assumption is that the wife accepted them or could tolerate them; the *kiddushin* is binding and — according to the majority view — we will not force a divorce even if the problems are large. Where blemishes develop after the marriage, the symmetry is closer on the surface — neither party is blamed — but in practice the husband retains greater latitude: he may divorce at will (paying the *ketubah*), whereas she, if she wants out, may be treated as a *moredet* and forfeit the *ketubah*.

The *Gemara* is candid that this asymmetry rests on a gendered presumption about tolerance — the assumption that women are more willing to tolerate an imperfect husband than men are to tolerate an imperfect wife. The *Gemara* itself registers the difficulty this creates for *get* and *agunah* situations where the husband has severe problems, yet the legal structure proceeds on this presumptive base.

## Ba'alei Raatan Stories

### Raatan Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: שָׂח לִי זָקֵן אֶחָד מֵאַנְשֵׁי יְרוּשָׁלַיִם: עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה מוּכֵּי שְׁחִין הֵן, וְכוּלָּן אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים תַּשְׁמִישׁ קָשֶׁה לָהֶן, וּבַעֲלֵי רָאתָן קָשֶׁה מִכּוּלָּן. מִמַּאי הָוֵי? דְּתַנְיָא: הִקִּיז דָּם וְשִׁימֵּשׁ — הָוַיִין לוֹ בָּנִים וִיתִיקִין. הַקִּיזוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם וְשִׁימְּשׁוּ — הָוַיִין לוֹ בָּנִים בַּעֲלֵי רָאתָן. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא דְּלָא טָעֵים מִידֵּי, אֲבָל טָעֵים מִידֵּי — לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei said: A certain Elder from** among **the residents of Jerusalem told me** that there **are twenty-four** types of patients **afflicted with boils, and** with regard to **all of them the Sages said** that **sexual relations are harmful to them, and** those **afflicted with *ra'atan*,** a severe skin disease characterized by extreme weakness and trembling, **are harmed** even more **than all of the** others. The Gemara asks: **From where** and how does this disease **come about?** The Gemara answers: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: One **who let blood and** immediately afterward **engaged in sexual relations will have weak** [***vitaykin***] **children.** If **both of them let blood and** then **engaged in sexual relations, he will have children afflicted with *ra'atan*. Rav Pappa said** in response: **We said** this **only if he did not taste anything** between bloodletting and intercourse, **but** if **he tasted something we have no** problem **with it,** as it is not dangerous.

Rabbi Yochanan records a tradition he received from a man described as a coal-man (*pecham*) from the people of Jerusalem, relating to twenty-four types of skin disease — a list directly relevant to the coming Torah portion of *Metzora*. The *Gemara* states that the *Chachamim* ruled regarding all twenty-four that *tashmish kasheh lahem* — sexual relations aggravate the condition. The worst of all is the condition known as *ra'atan*, which the *Gemara* describes as involving a worm or bug that enters the skull and causes a particularly severe form of skin or neurological disease.

### Raatan Treatment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי סִימָנֵיהּ? דָּלְפָן עֵינֵיהּ, וְדָיְיבִי נְחִירֵיהּ, וְאָיתֵי לֵיהּ רִירָא מִפּוּמֵּיהּ, וּרְמוּ דִּידְבֵי עִילָּוֵיהּ. וּמַאי אָסוּתֵיהּ? אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: פִּילָא, וְלוּדָנָא, גִּירְדָּא דֶאֱגוֹזָא, וְגִירְדָּא דְּאַשְׁפָּא, וּכְלִיל מַלְכָּא, וּמְתַחְלָא דְּדִיקְלָא סוּמָּקָא. וְשָׁלֵיק לְהוּ בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, וּמְעַיֵּיל לֵיהּ לְבֵיתָא דְשֵׁישָׁא. וְאִי לָא אִיכָּא בֵּיתָא דְשֵׁישָׁא — מְעַיֵּיל לֵיהּ לְבֵיתָא דְּשַׁב לִבְנֵי וַאֲרִיחָא, וְנָטֵיל לֵיהּ תְּלָת מְאָה כָּסֵי עַל רֵישֵׁיהּ, עַד דְּרָפְיָא אַרְעִיתָא דְמוֹחֵיהּ, וְקָרַע לְמוֹחֵיהּ וּמַיְיתֵי אַרְבַּע טַרְפֵי דְאָסָא וּמַדְלֵי כֹּל חַד כַּרְעָא וּמוֹתֵיב חַד, וְשָׁקֵיל בִּצְבָתָא וְקָלֵי לֵיהּ. דְּאִי לָא — הָדַר עִילָּוֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What are the symptoms** of *ra'atan*? **His eyes water, his nose runs, drool comes out of his mouth, and flies rest upon him.** The Gemara further inquires: **And what is his cure** to remove the insect found in his head, which is associated with this illness? **Abaye said:** One takes ***pila* and ladanum** [***lodana***], which are types of grasses; **and the ground shell of a nut; and shavings of smoothed hides; and artemisia [*kelil malka*]; and the calyx of a red date palm. And** one **cooks them together and brings** the patient **into a marble house,** i.e., one that is completely sealed. **And if there is no marble house** available, the one performing the treatment **brings** the patient **into a house** whose walls have the thickness **of seven bricks and** one **small brick. And** the one performing the treatment **pours three hundred cups** of this mixture **on** the patient's **head until his skull is soft, and** then he **tears** open the patient's skull **to** expose **his brain, and brings four myrtle leaves and lifts up each** time **one foot** of the insect that is found on the patient's brain, **and places one** leaf under each foot of the insect so as to prevent it from attempting to cling to his brain when it is forcibly removed, **and** subsequently **takes** it **with tweezers. And** he then **burns** the insect, **because if** he does **not** burn it, it **will return to him.**

The *Gemara* then addresses the etiology and treatment of *ra'atan*. A *baraita* teaches that if a person with this condition (*ba'al ra'atan*) had blood let and then engaged in sexual relations, the children would be *vitikin* — weak. If both husband and wife had blood let and then had relations, the children would be *ba'alei ra'atan* — afflicted with this same condition. Rav Papa qualifies: this applies only if neither party ate anything after the bloodletting. *Aval ta'im midi leit lan bah* — if they ate something afterward, restoring their strength, we have no such concern.

The signs of *ra'atan* are described: *dalfan eineih vedayivin nechireih* — the eyes run with tears, the nostrils discharge mucus, and there is saliva dripping from the mouth, with flies hovering around the person.

As for the cure, Abaye describes an elaborate procedure. A preparation is made from various plant materials and substances — species of bark, nutshell shavings, and, notably, the covering of a red unripe date (*mithla dedikla sumka*) — all boiled together. The patient is brought into a house built of marble, or if unavailable, a house with walls seven and a half bricks thick, providing protection from the elements and creating something approaching a sterile surgical environment. Three hundred cups of the boiled liquid are poured over his head, *ad dlayfei al ishta demokhayei* — until the floor of his skull is softened. The skull is then cut open (*ukaru lemokhayei*), and the worm in the brain is found to have four legs. The procedure requires placing a myrtle leaf under each leg before attempting to remove it — if the worm is pulled without the leaf in place, it grips the skull. Finally, the worm is removed with tweezers and burned in fire. *Ve'i la hadar amran* — if the worm is not burned, it will re-enter the man's skull.

### Contagion Care

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכְרִיז רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הִזָּהֲרוּ מִזְּבוּבֵי (שֶׁל) בַּעֲלֵי רָאתָן. רַבִּי זֵירָא לָא הֲוָה יָתֵיב בְּזִיקֵיהּ. רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר לָא עָיֵיל בְּאֻהְלֵיהּ. רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי לָא הֲווֹ אָכְלִי מִבֵּיעֵי דְּהָהִיא מְבוֹאָה. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי מִיכְרָךְ בְּהוּ וְעָסֵיק בַּתּוֹרָה. אָמַר: ״אַיֶּלֶת אֲהָבִים וְיַעֲלַת חֵן״, אִם חֵן מַעֲלָה עַל לוֹמְדֶיהָ, אַגּוֹנֵי לָא מַגְּנָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan** would **announce: Be careful of the flies** found **on those afflicted with *ra'atan*,** as they are carriers of the disease. **Rabbi Zeira would not sit** in a spot where the **wind** blew from the direction of someone afflicted with *ra'atan*. **Rabbi Elazar would not enter** the **tent** of one afflicted with *ra'atan*, and **Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi would not eat eggs from an alley** in which someone afflicted with *ra'atan* lived. Conversely, **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi** would **attach himself** to **them and study Torah, saying** as justification the verse: "The Torah is **a loving hind and a graceful doe"** (Proverbs 5:19). **If** it **bestows grace on those who learn it,** does **it not protect** them from illness?

Rabbi Yochanan announced a warning: *hizaharu mizvuvei shel ba'alei ra'atan* — beware the flies of those with *ra'atan*, since the flies may carry and transmit the disease. The awareness of contagion is reflected in the precautionary behavior of several sages. Rabbi Zeira would not sit in the same area of airflow as a person with *ra'atan*, understanding that the disease could be carried by wind. Rabbi Elazar would not enter the same tent. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi would not eat even eggs from the same courtyard where a *ba'al ra'atan* lived — an indication of concern about indirect contamination extending through the food supply of a shared space.

This is a striking cluster of precautions. The Babylonian Talmud here displays explicit awareness of contagious disease transmission — by air, by proximity, and apparently by indirect contact — that modern readers might not expect to find articulated in so concrete a form.

### R. Yehoshua Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הֲוָה שָׁכֵיב, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְמַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת: זִיל, עֲבֵיד לֵיהּ רְעוּתֵיהּ. אֲזַל אִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחְוִי לִי דּוּכְתַּאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְחַיֵּי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי סַכִּינָךְ, דִּלְמָא מְבַעֲתַתְּ לִי בְּאוֹרְחָא. יַהֲבַהּ נִיהֲלֵיהּ. כִּי מְטָא לְהָתָם דַּלְיַיהּ, קָא מַחְוֵי לֵיהּ. שְׁוַור נְפַל לְהָהוּא גִּיסָא. נַקְטֵיהּ בְּקַרְנָא דִגְלִימֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּשְׁבוּעֲתָא דְּלָא אָתֵינָא. אֲמַר קוּדְשָׁא בְּרִיךְ הוּא: אִי אִיתְּשִׁיל אַשְּׁבוּעֲתָא — נֶיהְדַּר. אִי לָא — לָא נֶיהְדַּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי סַכִּינַאי. לָא הֲוָה קָא יָהֵיב לֵיהּ. נְפַקָא בַּת קָלָא וַאֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: הַב נִיהֲלֵיהּ, דְּמִיתַּבְעָא לְבִרְיָיתָא. מַכְרִיז אֵלִיָּהוּ קַמֵּיהּ: פַּנּוּ מָקוֹם לְבַר לֵיוַאי! פַּנּוּ מָקוֹם לְבַר לֵיוַאי!
>
> **Translation:** **When** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **was** on the verge of **dying, they said to the Angel of Death: Go** and **perform his bidding,** as he is a righteous man and deserves to die in the manner he sees fit. The Angel of Death **went** and **appeared to him.** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said to him: Show me my place** in paradise. **He said to him: Very well.** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said to him: Give me your knife** that you use to kill mortals, **lest you frighten me on the way. He gave it to him. When he arrived there,** in paradise, **he lifted** Rabbi Yehoshua so he could see his place, and **he showed it to him.** Rabbi Yehoshua **jumped** and **fell into that** other **side,** thereby escaping into paradise. The Angel of Death **grabbed him by the corner of his cloak.** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **said to him: I swear that I will not come** with you. **The Holy One, blessed be He, said: If he** ever in his life **requested** dissolution **concerning an oath** he had taken, **he must return** to this world with the Angel of Death, as he can have his oath dissolved this time also. **If** he did **not** ever request dissolution of an oath, he need **not return.** Since Rabbi Yehoshua had in fact never requested dissolution of an oath, he was allowed to stay in paradise. The Angel of Death **said to him:** At least **give me my knife** back. However, he **did not give** it **to him,** as he did not want any more people to die. **A Divine Voice emerged and said to him: Give** it **to him, as** it **is necessary to** kill **the created** beings; death is the way of the world. **Elijah** the Prophet **announced before him: Make way for the son of Levi, make way for the son of Levi.**

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, by contrast, took no such precautions. He would wrap himself among the *ba'alei ra'atan* and study Torah in their company, invoking the verse from *Mishlei* — *ayelet ahavim ve'ya'alat khen*, the doe of love and graceful mountain goat — to argue that Torah brings charm and protection upon those who learn it, shielding him from any harm.

One might note that this behavior invites comparison with certain figures in the New Testament accounts who would go among the sick and diseased while the Pharisees maintained their distance. Here it is Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi who breaches that distance — but explicitly through the protective power of Torah study rather than ritual impurity considerations.

When Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi was dying, heaven instructed the angel of death: *zil avid lei re'uteih* — go fulfill his wishes. The angel appeared to him, and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's first request was: show me my place in *Olam HaBa*. The angel agreed. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi then asked for the angel's knife, saying he was afraid of being frightened by it along the way. The angel handed it over.

When they arrived at the entrance to *Olam HaBa* and the angel pointed to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's place, Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi leaped over the fence and landed on the other side — alive — in *Olam HaBa*, seeking to enter without undergoing the experience of death. The angel grabbed him by the edge of his cloak. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi declared under oath that he would not return. God then ruled: if he ever had an oath annulled during his lifetime, we will annul this oath and send him back; if he never annulled an oath, his oath will stand and he will remain. Since he had never annulled an oath, he was allowed to stay.

But the angel of death demanded his knife back. *De-mit-bei le-biryata* — other creatures need to die, and without the knife, death cannot proceed. A *bat kol* declared: return the knife. He did.

Elijah then announced in *Olam HaBa*: *panu makom le-bar Liva'i* — make way for the son of Levi. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi found Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai seated on thirteen golden stools. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai asked him: did you ever see a rainbow during your lifetime? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi answered: yes. Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai said: if so, you are not truly Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and you do not deserve the honor being accorded to you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל, אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַאי דַּהֲוָה יָתֵיב עַל תְּלָת עֲשַׂר תַּכְטָקֵי פִּיזָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַתְּ הוּא בַּר לֵיוַאי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵן. נִרְאֲתָה קֶשֶׁת בְּיָמֶיךָ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵן. אִם כֵּן, אִי אַתָּה בַּר לֵיוַאי. וְלָא הִיא, דְּלָא הֲוַאי מִידֵּי. אֶלָּא סָבַר: לָא אַחְזֵיק טֵיבוּתָא לְנַפְשַׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi **went** and **found** in paradise **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai sitting on thirteen golden stools [*takhtekei*].** Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **said to him: Are you the son of Levi? He said to him: Yes.** Rabbi Shimon said to him: Was **a rainbow** ever **seen in your days? He said: Yes.** Rabbi Shimon retorted: **If so, you are not the son of Levi,** as he is a completely righteous man. During the lifetimes of completely righteous people no rainbows are visible, as they are a sign that the world deserves to be destroyed by a flood; whereas the merit of the righteous protects the world from such things. The Gemara comments: **And that is not so, for there was no** rainbow seen **at all** during the lifetime of Rabbi Yehoshua, **but** he **thought: I do not want to take credit for myself** by presenting myself as such a righteous person.

The explanation: a rainbow is the sign of the covenant that God will not destroy the world. If a truly righteous person exists in a generation, the merit of that *tzaddik* is itself sufficient to protect the world, rendering the rainbow unnecessary. Furthermore, as the Maharsha notes, a *tzaddik* who never violated an oath provides the guarantee of God's own covenant-oath not to destroy the world, making the rainbow redundant. If Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi saw a rainbow, it implies his generation lacked that perfect protection — which would suggest he was not at that highest level.

The *Gemara* then comments: *ve-la hi* — that was not actually the case. There was never a rainbow in Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's lifetime. He really was that righteous, and his generation really was protected by his merit. He simply did not want to credit himself with that stature and so acknowledged having seen a rainbow rather than proclaiming his own spiritual greatness.

### R. Chanina Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא שׁוֹשְׁבִינֵיהּ הֲוָה. כִּי הֲוָה קָא נָיְחָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ לְמַלְאַךְ הַמָּוֶת: זִיל עָבֵיד לֵיהּ רְעוּתֵיהּ. אֲזַל לְגַבֵּיהּ וְאִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שִׁבְקַי תְּלָתִין יוֹם עַד דְּנַהְדַּר תַּלְמוּדַאי. דְּאָמְרִי: אַשְׁרֵי מִי שֶׁבָּא לְכָאן וְתַלְמוּדוֹ בְּיָדוֹ. שַׁבְקֵיהּ, לְבָתַר תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין אֲזַל אִיתְחֲזִי לֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַחְוִי לִי דּוּכְתַּאי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לְחַיֵּי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַב לִי סַכִּינָךְ דִּלְמָא מְבַעֲתַתְּ לִי בְּאוֹרְחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּחַבְרָךְ בָּעֵית לְמִיעְבַּד לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates a similar incident: **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa was a friend** of the Angel of Death and would see him frequently. **When** Rabbi Ḥanina **was** on the verge of **dying, they said to the Angel of Death: Go** and **perform his bidding. He went before him and appeared to him. He said to** the angel: **Leave me** for **thirty days until I have reviewed my studies, for they say: Happy is he who comes here,** to paradise, **with his learning in his hand. He left him,** and **after thirty days he** again **went** and **appeared to him. He said to** the Angel of Death: **Show me my place** in paradise. **He said to him: Very well.** Rabbi Ḥanina **said to him: Give me your knife, lest you frighten me on the way.** The Angel of Death **said to him:** Do **you wish to do to me as your friend** Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi did, and escape?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה וַחֲזִי מִי אִיכָּא מִידֵּי דִּכְתִיב בֵּיהּ דְּלָא קַיֵּימְתֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי אִיכְרַכְתְּ בְּבַעֲלֵי רָאתָן וְאִיעֲסֵקְתְּ בַּתּוֹרָה? וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, כִּי נָח נַפְשֵׁיהּ אַפְסֵיק לֵיהּ עַמּוּדָא דְנוּרָא בֵּין דִּידֵיהּ לְעָלְמָא. וּגְמִירִי דְּלָא מַפְסֵיק עַמּוּדָא דְנוּרָא אֶלָּא לְחַד בְּדָרָא אוֹ לִתְרֵין בְּדָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: Bring a Torah scroll and see: Is there anything written in it that I have not fulfilled?** I am therefore worthy of entering Paradise alive, as did Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. **He said to him: But did you attach yourself to those afflicted with *ra'atan* and study Torah,** as he did? The Gemara comments: **And even so,** despite the fact that he was not equal to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, **when he passed away a pillar of fire separated him from everyone. And** it **is learned** as a tradition **that a pillar of fire separates** in this manner **only for one in a generation or for two in a generation.**

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa had been a *shushvin* — a close companion — either of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi or of the angel of death (the *Gemara*'s phrasing is ambiguous). When Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa was dying, heaven sent the same message: go fulfill his wishes. The angel appeared to him. His first request was: give me thirty days to review my learning, for it is said: *ashrei mi she-ba le-chan ve-talmudo be-yado* — praised is the one who arrives in *Olam HaBa* with his learning in hand. He was granted the thirty days.

After thirty days, the angel returned and showed him his place. Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa likewise asked for the knife. But the angel declined: *ke-chaverach de-chamsht le-avad Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* — you want to do to me what your companion Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi did? The angel then challenged him: bring the *sefer Torah* and show me if there is any commandment written in it that you did not fulfill — if so, you are as worthy as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, and I should give you the knife.

Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa could not make that claim. The angel asked specifically: *mi adbakt be-ba'alei ra'atan* — did you involve yourself with those afflicted with *ra'atan*, studying Torah in their midst? He had not. That was the distinction. One is moved to think of healthcare workers and others who place themselves at genuine personal risk in caring for the vulnerable; the *Gemara* seems to acknowledge that this kind of selfless exposure represents a particular level of *madrega* that not every *tzaddik* attains.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קְרַב לְגַבֵּיהּ רַבִּי אֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִי, אָמַר: ״עֲשֵׂה בִּשְׁבִיל כְּבוֹד חֲכָמִים״. לָא אַשְׁגַּח. ״עֲשֵׂה בִּשְׁבִיל כְּבוֹד אָבִיךָ״. לָא אַשְׁגַּח. ״עֲשֵׂה בִּשְׁבִיל כְּבוֹד עַצְמְךָ״, אִיסְתַּלַּק. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמַּאן דְּלָא קַיֵּים (אֲפִילּוּ אוֹת אַחַת). אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַדָּא בַּר מַתְנָא: לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִמָּר דְּלָא אִית לֵיהּ מַעֲקֶה לְאִיגָּרֵיהּ. וְלָא הִיא: מִיהְוָה הֲוָה, וְהָהִיא שַׁעְתָּא הוּא דְּשַׁדְיֵיהּ זִיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** Due to the pillar of fire they could not go near Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa to attend to his burial. **Rabbi Alexandri approached him** and **said: Make** the pillar of fire disappear **in honor of the Sages.** He did **not pay attention** to him. He said: **Make** it go away **in honor of your father.** Again he did **not pay attention** to him. Finally he said: **Make** it go away **in your own honor,** at which point the pillar **disappeared. Abaye said:** The purpose of the pillar of fire is **to exclude** him **from** the company of **those who have not fulfilled even one letter** of the Torah. **Rav Adda bar Mattana said to him:** It comes **to exclude** him **from the Master** himself, **who does not have a guardrail for his roof.** Rav Adda bar Mattana took this opportunity to rebuke Abaye. The Gemara comments: **And that is not so** as he in fact **did have** a guardrail, **but the wind had** just **blown it off at that time.**

Nevertheless, when Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa died, *ifsik lei amuda de-nura bein didey l'maya* — a pillar of fire separated him from the rest of the world. No one could come close to his coffin. The tradition is that such a pillar of fire accompanies only a single individual in a generation, or at most two. He was, in his own right, of that singular stature — even if he had not reached the precise level of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi.

To address the practical problem of burial, a voice called out: lower the pillar of fire for the sake of the honor of the sages; for the honor of your father; for your own honor, so that you can be buried. *Mi-yad istalek* — immediately, the fire retreated, and they were able to bury him. But it had made its point about who he was.

Abaye comments on the significance of the pillar of fire: *l'afukei mi-man d'lo kiyem afilu os achas* — it serves to exclude from his space anyone who has not fulfilled even one letter of the Torah, who is not at the same *madrega* as Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi. Rav Adda bar Matna responds pointedly to Abaye: *l'afukei mi-mar* — like yourself, since you lack a *ma'akeh* on your roof! You don't fulfill every obligation, so you wouldn't be admitted into that space. The *Gemara* adds that Abaye actually did have a *ma'akeh*, but at that particular moment the wind had blown it down and he had not yet gotten around to repairing it — a very human detail.

### Babylon Prevention

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין בַּעֲלֵי רָאתָן בְּבָבֶל — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאוֹכְלִין תְּרָדִין, וְשׁוֹתִין שֵׁכָר שֶׁל הִיזְמֵי. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מִפְּנֵי מָה אֵין מְצוֹרָעִין בְּבָבֶל — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאוֹכְלִין תְּרָדִין, וְשׁוֹתִין שֵׁכָר, וְרוֹחֲצִין בְּמֵי פְרָת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina said: For what reason are there no people afflicted with *ra'atan* in Babylonia? Because** the Babylonians **eat beets [*teradin*] and drink beer made from the *hizmei*** plant. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: For what reason are there no lepers in Babylonia? Because they eat beets, drink beer, and bathe in the waters of the Euphrates,** all of which are good for the body.

Rabbi Chanina asks: *lama ein ba'alei ra'atan b'Bavel* — why are there no *ba'alei ra'atan* in Babylon? Because the Babylonians eat *silki* — beets — and drink *shichra d'hizmei*, a mead made from a particular shrub. These dietary habits apparently prevent the condition.

Rabbi Yochanan adds the parallel question: *lama ein metzoraim b'Bavel* — why are there no people with the skin disease *tzara'at* in Babylon? Because they eat beets, drink beer, and bathe in the waters of the Euphrates. Three protective practices — diet, drink, and bathing in a specific river — are credited with keeping this biblical affliction at bay in Babylonia.

>
> **Translation:** May we return to you [chapter] "One who vows that his wife."
