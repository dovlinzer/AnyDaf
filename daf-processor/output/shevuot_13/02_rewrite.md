# Shevuot 13 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yom Kippur Atonement

### Opening & Transition

Today's daf is 13, and we are finally turning from the last part of the *mishnah*, which devoted its opening portion to working out the distinct functions of the various *musafim* — the *chatat* offerings and their relationship to *tumat mikdash* — and then a lengthy derivation establishing that *shevuot shtayim she'hein arba* follows the same structural pattern as other cases of "two that are four." The final portion of the *mishnah*, however, shifts to a topic more directly connected to our own lives: not the question of *tumat mikdash* and the *korbanot* that address it, but the broader question of what the goat of *Yom Kippur* atones for.

### Goat of Yom Kippur

The *mishnah* states that the goat sent over the cliff on *Yom Kippur* atones for sins whether they are weighty or light, *mezid* or *shogeg*, or whether the sinner knew or did not know — with the *gemara* clarifying that "knew or did not know" refers to a case of initial *shogeg* where there is now uncertainty as to whether an *asham* is required. One thing the goat does not accomplish is releasing someone from the obligation of a *chatat*: if you sinned *beshogeg* and are certain of it, the *chatat* obligation remains. But with that established, we pick up at the bottom of 12b.

### Lack of Teshuvah

The *gemara* asks: what is the case where the goat atones for a positive *mitzvah* one failed to perform? If one did not do *teshuvah* — *idelo avid teshuvah* — why should a sacrifice atone at all? *Zevach reshaim to'evah* — the sacrifice of the wicked is an abomination. The *gemara* is framing the question in a striking way: it is not merely asking whether atonement is deserved, but suggesting that if one remains entirely unrepentant toward a particular sin, the very act of bringing a sacrifice in that context borders on the contemptible — a kind of invalid or unwanted offering. On the other hand, if one *has* done *teshuvah* — *id'avid teshuvah* — then *kol yoma nami*: any day on which *teshuvah* is done for a *mitzvat aseh* suffices, and *Yom Kippur* with the goat seems superfluous.

### Teshuvah for Mitzvat Aseh

A *baraita* is cited: *avar al mitzvat aseh ve'asah teshuvah*, if one transgressed a positive *mitzvah* and did *teshuvah*, *lo zaz mi-sham ad she-mochalin lo* — one does not move from there before God forgives. The imagery evokes something like *Choni HaMa'agal* standing his ground: immediate forgiveness is available upon *teshuvah* for a *mitzvat aseh*. So who then needs the goat, and who needs *Yom Kippur*?

### Omed BeMiddo

Rabbi Zeira answers: this case concerns someone who is *omed be-meredo* — standing in a state of rebellion. The point is subtle and important. The failure to do *teshuvah* is not a passive oversight; it is an active ongoing choice to remain unrepentant, an implicit declaration of indifference. Nevertheless, *Yom Kippur* forgives even this.

### Ramban on Teshuvah

This connects to a remarkable observation of the Ramban in his *drasha le-Rosh Hashanah* — a work that is essentially a fifty-page commentary on *Masechet Rosh Hashanah*, with only its final paragraph devoted to *mussar*. There the Ramban writes that the opportunity for *teshuvah* God has granted us is simultaneously a great gift and a great liability. It is a gift because *teshuvah* earns forgiveness. But it is a liability because: had God never given us *teshuvah*, a person who sinned would have sinned only once. Now that *teshuvah* exists, choosing not to do *teshuvah* is itself an ongoing sin — every passing moment becomes a renewed act of *omed be-meredo*, a continued embrace of one's transgression. This is precisely what Rabbi Zeira captures: not doing *teshuvah* is not simply the absence of an action; it is an active posture of rebellion. And yet, *Yom Kippur* still atones.

## Rabbi's Position

### All Sins Atoned

The *mishnah*, the *gemara* tells us, follows the position of *Rabbi* — that is, Rabbi Yehudah HaNasi. A *baraita* states his teaching explicitly: *al kol averos she-ba-Torah bein asah teshuvah bein lo asah teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — for all the transgressions in the Torah, whether one has done *teshuvah* or has not done *teshuvah*, *Yom Kippur* atones. The vision is of a kind of divine amnesty: God desires that each year, every person begin with a clean slate. Even a willful *mechalel Shabbat* with no remorse whatsoever — come *Yom Kippur*, the slate is wiped clean.

It is worth noting a subtle but critical shift that occurs here and continues throughout the day's discussion: the *gemara* has quietly transitioned from speaking about the goat going over the cliff as the instrument of atonement to speaking of *Yom Kippur* itself as *mechaper*. This shift is obviously momentous for us, who no longer have the goat. The *gemara* allows this transition to happen organically without drawing explicit attention to it.

### Three Exceptions

There is, however, an exception — in fact, three. *Chutz* — excepted from the blanket atonement of *Yom Kippur* are: *porek ol*, *megaleh panim ba-Torah*, and *mefeir brit basar*.

### Porek Ol Defined

*Porek ol* means casting off the yoke — an outright rejection of God and His *mitzvot*. This is not mere willful sinning driven by desire or appetite; it is an ideological rejection of the entire covenantal relationship.

### Megaleh Panim

*Megaleh panim ba-Torah* is frequently misread, particularly when a gloss of *she-lo ka-halacha* is inserted, suggesting it refers to someone who interprets a *halacha* incorrectly. That misreading obscures what is actually a far more serious category. The phrase *gilui panim* means acting brazenly, with insolence. Applied to *Torah*, it describes someone whose relationship to the words of the *Torah* is one of dismissive scoffing — not sincere philosophical questioning, but a contemptuous "ugh, why would God have said such a thing?" — a posture of mockery and denigration toward the *Torah* itself.

### Rashi on Megaleh Panim

Rashi makes this vivid. Three lines from the top of the relevant passage he writes: *ba al divrei Torah be-chutzpah u-vegillui azut panim, ke-gon Menashe she-haya yoshev ve-doresh be-agadot shel dofi* — like Menashe who would sit and expound stories of *Torah* in a manner of derision: Why did Moshe need to write the story of Timna who was a concubine? What is the episode of Reuven going out in the days of the wheat harvest doing in the *Torah*? Not as a genuine question, but as contemptuous dismissal. Rashi thus reads *megaleh panim* as describing a specific relationship to the *Torah* — one of brazen scoffing — rather than to God or *mitzvot* directly. The Rambam in his *Peirush HaMishnayot*, by contrast, reads it as referring to someone who sins *le-hachis*, in active defiance and provocation.

### Mefir Brit

*Mefeir brit basar* — annulling the covenant of the flesh — refers to reversing *brit milah*. The word *basar* makes clear this is not merely metaphorical. It is the cancellation of the physical covenant. Whether this extends to someone who grows up in a household that never performed the *brit* and makes a conscious ideological choice to remain uncircumcised is an interesting question; the language suggests active rejection rather than mere passivity.

For these three, *teshuvah* does work — *she-im asah teshuvah Yom HaKippurim mechaper* — if *teshuvah* is done, *Yom Kippur* then atones. But without *teshuvah*, *Yom Kippur* does not atone for these three. By contrast, one might wonder whether the classic three cardinal sins — *gilui arayot*, *shefichut damim*, and *avodah zarah* — fall within or outside this framework. According to Rabbi, as long as the person has not crossed into ideological rejection of God, Torah, or the covenant — as long as they remain within the *berit* even while sinning grievously — *Yom Kippur* atones even for those sins without *teshuvah*.

One should also be clear about what atonement without *teshuvah* means and does not mean. As Rav Soloveitchik once articulated, there is a distinction between *kapparah* — the erasure of culpability, the cleaning of the slate — and the personal transformation that genuine *teshuvah me-ahavah* accomplishes. A person whose slate is wiped clean by *Yom Kippur* without *teshuvah* is still, *be-gavra*, a *rasha* — they have simply escaped punishment for their past sins. *Teshuvah* in its deepest sense transforms who a person is, converting the *rasha* into a *tzaddik*. The mechanisms are not equivalent.

### Hikareit Tikareit

How does Rabbi derive that these three are not covered by the general atonement of *Yom Kippur*? A *baraita* cites: *ki devar Hashem baza* — because he has denigrated the word of God. Rashi explains that *devar Hashem* in its primary sense refers to *Anochi Hashem Elokecha* — the first word of the *aseret ha-dibrot* — so that *ki devar Hashem baza* describes the person who denigrates God's word entirely: *ze ha-porek ol u-megaleh panim ba-Torah*. And *ve-et mitzvato hefar* — he has annulled His commandment: *ze ha-mefeir brit basar*. What *mitzvah* symbolizes the covenant more than any other? The *brit* of the flesh.

Then the verse continues: *hikaret tikaret* — he shall surely be cut off. The doubling is exegetically significant. *Hikaret* — before *Yom Kippur*. *Tikaret* — after *Yom Kippur*. The verse is teaching that even after *Yom Kippur* passes, these three are not atoned for. This is Rabbi's proof that *Yom Kippur* does not atone for these three — not that he needs a proof that *Yom Kippur* atones for everything else, which seems to be his starting assumption.

### Avona Bah

But the verse then adds: *avona vah* — the sin is in her, or more precisely, the sin persists with the person. *Lo amarti ela bi-zman she-avona vah* — this is only said when the sin continues to persist, when the person is still grasping it. Once *teshuvah* is done — once the person is no longer holding fast to the sin — even these three receive atonement. Implicitly, this also establishes Rabbi's view for everything else: since the verse specifies that these three are not atoned for specifically due to *avona vah* — because the sin persists — we can derive that for everything else, *Yom Kippur* atones even without *teshuvah*.

## Rabbanan's Position

### Olam HaBa Reading

The *rabanan* who disagree with Rabbi — who hold that *Yom Kippur* does *not* atone without *teshuvah* — read the verse differently. *Hikaret* — cut off in *olam hazeh*. *Tikaret* — cut off *le-olam haba*. On this reading, the doubling of *hikaret tikaret* says nothing about *Yom Kippur* at all; it is describing the punishment of *karet* in its two dimensions across this world and the next. The reference to *olam haba* then implies that we are discussing what happens after death.

### Death Atones

For the *rabanan*, therefore, the verse is about *misa* — death. Death, *misa memarkeset*, cleanses. The *rabanan* hold that death atones even for those who have not done *teshuvah*, at least for all sins other than the three cardinal ones. Rashi adds that this works in conjunction with *Yom Kippur*. The term *memarkeset* is telling: it suggests something more like scouring than the gentle *mechaperes* — the erasure is complete but the process is more severe, perhaps less total in its redemptive quality.

For the big three, the *rabanan* hold that even death atones only if *teshuvah* was done. And as for *Yom Kippur*: in the view of the *rabanan*, *Yom Kippur* always requires *teshuvah*.

### Atonement Chart

To map the positions clearly: Rabbi holds that *Yom Kippur* atones for everything without *teshuvah*, with the exception that for the three — *porek ol*, *megaleh panim*, and *mefeir brit* — one needs *Yom Kippur* plus *teshuvah*. The *rabanan* hold that *Yom Kippur* always requires *teshuvah*, and that death (*misa*) atones without *teshuvah* for everything other than the three, while the three require *teshuvah* even for death to atone.

### Mitzvat Aseh Agreement

One important point of agreement: for a *mitzvat aseh*, both Rabbi and the *rabanan* agree that *teshuvah* alone suffices — no *Yom Kippur* and no other mechanism is required. Forgiveness is immediate upon genuine *teshuvah*.

## Mishnah Attribution

### Challenge to Mishnah

The *gemara* now asks: if our *mishnah* follows Rabbi — who holds that *Yom Kippur* atones even without *teshuvah* — how does that square with the apparent evidence from the end of the *mishnah*?

### End of Mishnah

The *seifa* of the *mishnah* states: *echad Yisrael ve-echad Kohanim ve-echad Kohen Mashuach* — all three categories, the ordinary Israelite, the *Kohen*, and the *Kohen Gadol*, receive equal atonement. This reflects the position that when it comes to *she'ar aveiros* — the sins other than *tumat mikdash* — the goat that goes over the cliff provides atonement equally for all. Whereas for *tumat mikdash*, Kohanim have a distinct atonement through the ox and Israelites through the goat, for all other sins they share one vehicle. Now, this position — that they share equally in the goat for *she'ar aveiros* — is identified as belonging to Rabbi Yehudah. If the *seifa* is Rabbi Yehudah, the *gemara* argues, so must be the *reisha*, and the *mishnah* should therefore not be attributed to Rabbi.

### Rav Yosef's Solution

Rav Yosef responds: the *mishnah* is indeed Rabbi — *Rabbi hi* — and Rabbi simply adopts Rabbi Yehudah's position regarding the equality of Kohen and Yisrael for the goat. *Ve-savar lah ke-Rabbi Yehudah*. In other words, Rabbi, as author of the *mishnah*, held like Rabbi Yehudah on this particular *halacha* while maintaining his own distinctive position that *Yom Kippur* atones without *teshuvah*.

### Abaye's Challenge

Abaye presses further: was Rav Yosef being precise in saying that *Rabbi* holds like Rabbi Yehudah — as opposed to saying the *mishnah* is Rabbi Yehudah who holds like Rabbi? After all, either formulation could account for the evidence.

### Mutual Agreement

Rav Yosef responds: *davka ka-amina* — yes, I was being precise. Rabbi holds like Rabbi Yehudah, but Rabbi Yehudah does not hold like Rabbi regarding *Yom Kippur* atoning without *teshuvah*. Why phrase it with Rabbi as the one borrowing? *Orcha de-milsa* — it is simply more natural to say that the later authority, Rabbi (who postdates Rabbi Yehudah), embraced an earlier position, rather than the reverse. But the precision is real: Rabbi Yehudah actively disagreed with Rabbi's core position on *teshuvah* and *Yom Kippur*.

## Baraita Evidence

### Sifra Source

The evidence comes from a *baraita* drawn from the *Sifra* — the collection of tannaitic legal exegesis on *Vayikra*, whose anonymous voice is by tradition attributed to Rabbi Yehudah. The *baraita* opens: *yachol yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper al shavim ve-al she-einam shavim* — perhaps *Yom Kippur* should atone both for those who have done *teshuvah* and for those who have not?

### Logical Derivation

A logical analogy is offered: *chatas* and *asham* atone, and *Yom Kippur* atones; just as *chatas* and *asham* only atone for those who have done *teshuvah* — since bringing a sin-offering includes the act of *viduy*, verbal confession — so too *Yom Kippur* should atone only for those who have done *teshuvah*. *Af Yom HaKippurim ein mechaper ella al shavim*.

### Counter-argument

But this analogy is challenged: *mah le-chatas ve-asham she-ein mechaperim al ha-mezid ke-shogeg* — one could distinguish *chatas* and *asham* by saying their atoning power is inherently limited, applying only to unintentional sins. Their restricted scope explains why *teshuvah* is also required: the most forgivable sins (unintentional at the outset, repented at the end) earn the atonement of a *chatat*. *Tomar le-Yom HaKippurim she-mechaper al ha-mezid ke-shogeg* — but *Yom Kippur* is explicitly described in the *pasuk* as atoning even for willful sins, *u-mipisheihem le-chol chatosam*. Since *Yom Kippur* possesses this expanded atoning power, perhaps it functions as a complete and unconditional mechanism, working even without *teshuvah*.

### Ach Limitation

The *baraita* responds with a verse from Vayikra 23: *ach be-yom hazeh yechapper*. The word *ach* introduces a limitation. *Ach* — only, but. *Yom Kippur* does not have total scope. And therefore: it atones only for those who have done *teshuvah*. Even if the sin was *mezid*, *Yom Kippur* still requires *teshuvah* to function. The combination is: yes, it can cover even willful sins, but only when accompanied by *teshuvah*.

### Sifra Attribution

This entire *derasha* comes from the *Sifra*. *Stam Sifra mani? Rabbi Yehudah* — the anonymous voice of the *Sifra* is Rabbi Yehudah. The *baraita*'s conclusion that *Yom Kippur* works only *al shavim* — only for those who have returned — is therefore explicitly Rabbi Yehudah's position. This confirms Rav Yosef's claim: the part of our *mishnah* that implies *Yom Kippur* atones even without *teshuvah* cannot be Rabbi Yehudah; it must be Rabbi.

## Sifra Contradiction

### Violating Yom Kippur

The *gemara* now raises a striking contradiction between two anonymous passages of the *Sifra* — *rami stam Sifra a-stam Sifra*. The first *Sifra* we just cited says *Yom Kippur* works only for those who have done *teshuvah*. But a second *baraita* says the opposite, and in a dramatically expanded direction: *yachol lo yehei Yom HaKippurim mechaper ela im kein hisana bo ve-kara mikra kodesh ve-lo asah bo melachah* — one might think *Yom Kippur* atones only for one who fasted on it, acknowledged its sacred character, and refrained from *melachah*.

### Non-Observant Scenario

The *gemara* then entertains a scenario that borders on the radical: what about someone who did none of those things? Who did not fast, did not acknowledge the day, and actively performed *melachah*?

### Day of Atonement

*Talmud lomar: Yom Kippurim hu* — the verse simply states, it is *Yom Kippur*. *Mikol makom* — regardless. The day carries its atoning power independent of the person's acknowledgment or observance. This is a far more expansive claim than anything in the first *Sifra* passage, and it seems to posit that *Yom Kippur* atones even for someone actively violating it.

### Abaye's Resolution

Abaye resolves the contradiction by attribution: *ha Rabbi ve-ha Rabbi Yehudah*. The first *Sifra* — requiring *teshuvah* — is Rabbi Yehudah's position. The second *Sifra* — atoning even for the violator — is Rabbi's position.

### Rava's Position

Rava disagrees: *ha ve-ha Rabbi* — both passages represent Rabbi's position. The tension between them will be resolved not by attributing them to different authorities but by reading them in a more nuanced way.

---

## Rava's Interpretation

### Teshuvah for Violation

Rava's resolution is elegant. Both *Sifra* passages represent Rabbi. The earlier statement — that *Yom Kippur* requires *teshuvah* — is not a general statement about all sins. It refers specifically to the violation of *Yom Kippur* itself. *Ve-modeh Rabbi be-kareit di-yoma* — Rabbi concedes that *Yom Kippur* does not atone for its own violation without *teshuvah*.

### Other Sins Atoned

And yet — and this is the crucial move — even someone who violates *Yom Kippur* still benefits from *Yom Kippur*'s atonement for all his other sins. Violating *Yom Kippur* does not strip the day of its atoning power vis-à-vis everything else. The person eating on *Yom Kippur* will emerge, if he does not repent for the eating itself, with that particular sin unatoned — but his *mechalel Shabbat* and whatever else he has done throughout the year will be atoned for by the day.

### Karet Problem

This creates an internal pressure: if Rabbi holds that *Yom Kippur* atones for everything including its own violation, then why does the *Torah* prescribe *karet* for eating on *Yom Kippur*? If I am eating and *Yom Kippur* is atoning for me with every bite, what is left to punish? *De-i lo tima hachi kareit di-yoma le-Rabbi leit leh* — if you do not make this concession, Rabbi would have no scenario in which someone who eats on *Yom Kippur* receives *karet*, which the *Torah* explicitly prescribes.

### Karet Scenarios

The *gemara* briefly entertains the possibility of constructing a scenario where *Yom Kippur* atones for its own violation and *karet* still applies in some edge case: perhaps someone ate at night and died before the day of *Yom Kippur* arrived (*ke-gon de-avid be-layla u-mis ve-lo havi yemama le-khapporei leh*), since the *pasuk* says *ki ba-yom hazeh yechapper* — it is the *day* that atones. Or perhaps someone ate and choked as they were swallowing (*de-achal umtza ve-chankei u-mis*), dying before the *aveira* was complete enough for atonement to apply. Or someone ate right up until *shekiat ha-chama* with no remaining time for atonement to take effect.

### Rabbi's Agreement

Ultimately, Rava establishes: Rabbi agrees that *Yom Kippur* does not atone for its own violation without *teshuvah*. The two *Sifra* passages are both Rabbi's, and they are not contradictory: the first addresses the one thing *Yom Kippur* cannot atone for without *teshuvah* (its own violation), and the second addresses everything else (which *Yom Kippur* atones for regardless, even if one is violating the day). To the earlier framework of Rabbi's three exceptions — *porek ol*, *megaleh panim*, *mefeir brit* — we now add a fourth category requiring *teshuvah* according to Rabbi: the violation of *Yom Kippur* itself.

## Rambam's Ruling

### Concern for Incentive

One might reasonably worry about the practical implications of Rabbi's position. If people learn that *Yom Kippur* wipes the slate clean without *teshuvah*, the incentive to actually engage in *teshuvah* would be severely weakened.

### Teshuvah Essential

The Rambam rules definitively in *Hilchos Teshuvah*: *kol mitzvot she-ba-Torah bein asei bein lo ta'asei, im avar adam al achat mehem bein be-zadon bein bi-shigaga, she-ya'aseh teshuvah ve-yashuv me-cheto chayav le-hitvadot* — for every commandment in the *Torah*, positive or negative, intentional or unintentional, the obligation is to do *teshuvah* and *viduy*. And further: all the various mechanisms of atonement — *mitat beit din*, *korban*, and the rest — are insufficient without *teshuvah*.

### Se'ir HaMishtaleach

Concerning the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach*, the Rambam states: *she-hu kapparah kol Yisrael* — it is an atonement for all of Israel, atoning for all transgressions in the *Torah*, light and grave, *mezid* and *shogeg*, our *mishnah* — *ve-hu she-asah teshuvah*. Even the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* requires *teshuvah*. The Rambam is applying the *rabanan*'s framework — or Rabbi Yehudah's — rather than Rabbi's more expansive position, plainly because he does not want *Yom Kippur* to become a reason to defer *teshuvah*.

### Without Temple

Finally: *bi-zman ha-zeh she-ein Beit ha-Mikdash kayam, ein lanu mizbe'ach kapparah, ein sham ela teshuvah. Ha-teshuvah mechaperet al kol ha-aveiros* — in our time, without the *Beit ha-Mikdash*, we have no altar of atonement, and there is nothing but *teshuvah*. *Teshuvah* alone atones for everything.

## Kohen Equality

### Mishnah Statement

The *mishnah* states: *echad Yisrael ve-echad Kohanim ve-echad Kohen Mashuach* — the Israelite, the *Kohen*, and the *Kohen Gadol* are all equal. The question is what precisely they are equal in, because the *mishnah*'s formulation seems to say they are all the same and then immediately asks what distinguishes them.

### Clarification

Rav Yehudah clarifies: *echad Yisrael ve-echad Kohanim ve-echad Kohen Mashuach miskapprin bi-se'ir ha-mishtale'ach bi-she'ar aveiros* — all three categories receive atonement from the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* for all sins other than *tumat mikdash*. In the domain of *she'ar aveiros*, there is no distinction among them.

### Tumat Mikdash Distinction

The distinction exists only for *tumat mikdash*: *ella she-ha-par mechapper al Kohanim al tumat mikdash*. For the sins of *tumat mikdash*, the ox atones for the *Kohanim* and the goat atones for *Yisrael*. The *Kohanim* require separate atonement for *tumat mikdash* for two reasons: they are present in the *Mikdash* constantly and therefore more likely to render it impure, and they bear the special responsibility of maintaining and protecting its purity.

### Rabbi Yehudah Confirmed

This idea — that for *she'ar aveiros*, Kohanim and Yisrael share equally in the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* — is Rabbi Yehudah's position, as the *gemara* confirms will be demonstrated from a *baraita*.

## Atonement Sources

### Mikdash Cleansing

The *baraita* from the *Sifra* begins with the *Kohen Gadol*'s service on *Yom Kippur* and traces through the various spaces it atones for: *ve-kipper et Mikdash ha-Kodesh* — he should atone for the sanctuary.

### Inner Sanctuary

*Zeh lifnai ve-lifnim* — that is the innermost chamber, the *Kodesh Kodashim*. *Et Ohel Mo'ed zeh Heichal* — the *Heichal*, where the *shulchan* and *menorah* stood. *Ve-al ha-mizbe'ach ke-mashma'o* — the altar, which Rashi explains refers to the *mizbe'ach ha-zahav*, the inner golden altar where the incense was offered.

### Outer Areas

*Yekhapper — elu azarot* — the *azarot*, the outer courtyards including the *ezrat Kohanim* and *ezrat Yisrael* where the outer altar stood. All of these spaces are atoned for through the blood of the ox and the blood of the goat.

### Both Groups

Then the *pasuk* concludes: *ve-al ha-Kohanim ve-al kol am ha-kahal yekhapper* — for the Kohanim and for all the congregation of the people he will atone. *Kohanim ke-mashma'o am ha-kahal elu Yisrael* — the Kohanim and the people of Israel. At this point, the blood of the ox is working for the Kohanim and the blood of the goat is working for Yisrael, for their respective *tumat mikdash* obligations.

### Extra Yechapper

But the verse ends with a final word: *yekhapper* — he will atone. What does this extra *yekhapper* come to include? *Elu ha-Levi'im. Hushvu kullam le-kapparah achat* — the Levites. They are all made equal to a single atonement. But wait: one has an ox and one has a goat. In what sense are they all equivalent? The *baraita* answers: *she-miskapprin bi-se'ir ha-mishtaleach bi-she'ar aveiros — divrei Rabbi Yehudah*. The equivalence indicated by that final *yekhapper* is that for all sins other than *tumat mikdash*, they all receive atonement through the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach*. That is Rabbi Yehudah's reading.

## Yehudah vs. Shimon

### Yehudah: Equality

Rabbi Yehudah holds that for *she'ar aveiros* — all sins other than *tumat mikdash* — both Kohanim and Yisrael receive atonement from the same source: the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* that goes over the cliff. The extra *yekhapper* in the verse establishes their equality in this domain.

### Shimon: Separation

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. He too reads the *pasuk* carefully: the blood of the inner goat atones for *tumat mikdash* for Yisrael; the blood of the ox atones for *tumat mikdash* for Kohanim — *keshem she-dam sa'ir ha-na'aseh bi-fnim mechapper Yisrael al tumat mikdash ve-kodashav kakh dam par mechapper al Kohanim al tumat mikdash ve-kodashav*. So far there is agreement. But Rabbi Shimon argues that even for *she'ar aveiros*, there is a parallel split. The *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* does not atone for Kohanim for *she'ar aveiros*; something else does.

### Shimon's Link

What is his proof? *Ve-lakach et shenei ha-se'irim* — take the two goats: the inner goat and the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach*. These two are linked by the verse. Just as the inner goat is specified as *asher la-am* — belonging to the people — and therefore atones only for Yisrael (not for Kohanim) with respect to *tumat mikdash*, so too the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach*, being linked to the inner goat, atones only for Yisrael for *she'ar aveiros* as well. *Af se'ir ha-mishtaleach eino mechapper al Kohanim bi-she'ar aveiros*.

### Bull Viduy

What then atones for the Kohanim for *she'ar aveiros*? The *viduy* recited over the ox. The *Kohen Gadol* places his hands on the ox and confesses: *chatasi, avisi, pashati, ani u-veiti* — and it is this *viduy* on the ox that carries the atonement for the Kohanim's other sins, just as the *viduy* on the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* carries that atonement for Yisrael. The ox, therefore, has two distinct atoning functions: its blood atones for *tumat mikdash* for Kohanim, and its *viduy* atones for *she'ar aveiros* for Kohanim.

### Same Result

On this reading, the extra *yekhapper* in the verse is not establishing that they all share the same vehicle of atonement. Rather, *hushvu kullam le-kapparah achat* means only that they all end up atoned — *de-bnai kapparah ninhu* — the outcome is the same. But the process differs: *kol chad ve-chad mechapper mi-denafshei* — each group receives atonement from its own distinct source.

### Yehudah Counter

Rabbi Yehudah responds to Rabbi Shimon's linkage argument: yes, the two goats are linked by the verse — but they are linked only in the sense that *shehayu shavin be-mareh u-ve-komah u-ve-damim*: they must be identical in appearance, equal in height, and purchased at the same price. *Hu de-asa* — that is all the linkage establishes. It does not follow that because one is exclusively for Yisrael, so too must the other be exclusively for Yisrael.

### Own Source

For Rabbi Yehudah, therefore, the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach* atones for *she'ar aveiros* for everyone — Kohen and Yisrael alike. For Rabbi Shimon, even in the domain of *she'ar aveiros*, each group draws atonement from its own separate source: Yisrael from the *se'ir ha-mishtaleach*, and Kohanim from the *viduy* of the ox.
