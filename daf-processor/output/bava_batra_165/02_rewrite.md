# Bava Batra 165 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mekushar & Lashon Hara

### Mekushar before Rabbi

The Gemara picks up approximately twenty lines from the bottom of the page with the words *hahu mekushar de'ata lekamei deRabbi* — a *get mekushar* that came before Rabbi. We have seen before that Rabbi was at times unfamiliar with certain aspects of how a *mekushar* was executed, which likely reflects his general preference for the *pashut* form and his disinclination toward the *mekushar* altogether. So Rabbi looked at this *get mekushar* and declared, *ein zman bazeh* — this *shtar* lacks a date and is therefore invalid.

### Date Between Folds

Rabbi Shimon, his son, said to him: *shema bein sharav muvla* — perhaps the date is hidden between the folds. This is somewhat amusing, since our general understanding of a *mekushar* is that everything relevant is between the folds. Nevertheless, apparently some information appeared outside the folded area, so the date's placement inside was unexpected. *Palyei* — he undid the knots and opened it, and *vechazyei* — he saw it there, the date written inside the folded-over section.

### Yehuda the Tailor

*Hadar chaza bei Rabbi bevishut* — afterwards Rabbi looked at his son Rabbi Shimon with what can only be described as disapproval, a bad eye, an expression of anger. It was clear to Rabbi Shimon that his father had not been familiar with this aspect of the *mekushar*, and Rabbi's displeasure was palpable. Rabbi Shimon, doing what children have always done when accused by a parent, deflected responsibility: *lav ana kasivtei* — I did not write this *shtar mekushar*. *Yehuda Chaita kasvei* — Yehuda the tailor wrote it. One might note that a tailor is perhaps ideally positioned to write a *mekushar*, given that sewing the folds is part of the process.

### Lashon Hara Rebuke

Rabbi's response was pointed: *klakh milashon hara zeh* — now you are speaking *lashon hara*. The coverup, as it were, was worse than the underlying issue. What is striking here is that Rabbi considered identifying Yehuda as the author of this *shtar mekushar* to constitute *lashon hara* — because in Rabbi's view, producing such a document was something negative, and to say that someone did it was therefore to say something harmful about them in the eyes of others.

### Lashon Hara Definition

This occasions a brief but important discussion of the term *lashon hara* itself. In the technical sense that has become standard — particularly since the *Chofetz Chaim* — *lashon hara* is distinguished from *motzi shem ra*: the latter is speaking falsely about someone, while *lashon hara* is speaking truthfully but in a way that damages the person's reputation or standing. In the Gemara, however, *lashon hara* also carries a broader meaning, referring to any speech that could be harmful in various ways. The case of Rabbi Shimon's statement about Yehuda fits the narrower technical definition as well, since it was true but cast the man in a negative light in Rabbi's eyes.

### Tehillim & Handwriting

The Gemara then recounts another incident. *Zimnin hava yasiv kamei veka pasek sidra besefer Tehillim* — there were occasions when Rabbi Shimon was sitting before Rabbi and was learning from *Sefer Tehillim*, finishing one of its five books. Rabbi looked at the scroll — this being an era of handwritten parchment scrolls — and exclaimed, *kama miyushar ksav zeh*, how beautiful and straight is this writing! Rabbi Shimon immediately replied, *lav ana kasivtei* — I did not write it. *Yehuda Chaita kasvei* — Yehuda the tailor wrote it. The man was evidently multi-talented, skilled both in writing *shtarot* and in scribal work for *sfarim*. Rabbi's response: *puk milashon hara zeh* — go away from this *lashon hara*.

The Gemara is puzzled. *Bishlama hatam ika lashon hara* — I understand the first case, that was *lashon hara*, since Rabbi did not approve of the *mekushar* and Rabbi Shimon was implicating someone else. But here, what *lashon hara* is there in praising the man's handwriting? The Gemara answers: *mishum de-Rav Dimi*, following the teaching of Rav Dimi, brother of Rav Safra. *Le'olam al yesaper adam betovato shel chavero* — one should never speak about another person's good qualities, *she-mitoch tovato ba lidei ra'ato* — because through speaking of the good, one can come to speak of the bad.

The *Chofetz Chaim*, from what one recalls, explains this to mean either that one should not be overly effusive in praise — because such exaggeration invites pushback and someone will then offer an unflattering counter-example — or that one should be careful about praising someone in the presence of a person who is already hostile or resentful toward them, since the praise will only provoke a negative reaction. Both interpretations could apply here: Rabbi was already displeased with Yehuda, and bringing up his virtues in that charged atmosphere was precisely the kind of comment that would invite a dismissal and then a recounting of the negative. There is something genuinely funny about the story as well — Rabbi Shimon seems unable to say anything about Yehuda, positive or negative, without being rebuked.

## Lashon Hara Deep Dive

### Three Daily Sins

The Gemara, having been drawn into the topic of *lashon hara*, introduces a related teaching. *Amar Rav Amram amar Rav: shalosh averot ein adam nitzol mehem bechol yom* — three transgressions from which no person escapes on any given day: *hirhur avera* — sinful thoughts, which often connotes sexual thoughts in particular; *iyun tefilla* — which we will address momentarily; and *lashon hara*.

The Gemara immediately pushes back: *lashon hara salka da'atach?* — can it really be that people speak outright *lashon hara* every single day? The answer is: *ela avak lashon hara* — rather, the *dust* of *lashon hara*, consistent with what we established above. Even if one does not intend to speak ill of someone, even saying something good that will lead to something bad, or making an offhand comment that carries some slight edge — these constitute *avak lashon hara*, the residue or shadow of *lashon hara*. The language of one's speech carries such risk that even when one is not consciously speaking badly, it is nearly impossible to navigate an entire day without some trace of it slipping through.

### Iyun Tefillah & Kavana

What is *iyun tefilla*? The *Rashbam* explains it to mean that after praying, a person examines his prayer and thinks: I prayed so well, surely God will answer. In this reading, *iyun tefilla* is a kind of excessive confidence in one's own prayer, a subtle presumption of divine response that amounts to testing God.

*Tosafot*, however, objects strenuously. The statement is that no one escapes this *every single day* — *bechol yom*. Would it not be wonderful if people davened with such devotion that they genuinely believed their prayer to have been worthy of a divine response? That would be an extraordinary achievement, not a daily failing. It cannot mean that. So *Tosafot* inverts the reading entirely: *iyun tefilla* refers to the *lack* of proper attention in prayer, not to overconfidence. It is the failure of *kavana* — praying by rote, going through the motions — that is the universal daily shortcoming.

*Tosafot* supports this from a famous line in the *Yerushalmi*, which he cites near the bottom of the passage. *Amar Rabbi Masneh: machzikna tivusa l'reisha* — I must be grateful to my head, *d'chi mati l'Modim kara m'garmeh* — because when I reach *Modim*, it bows down on its own. The sage is mocking himself gently: my body is on such autopilot that I don't even need to be consciously present; my head knows to bow when we arrive at *Modim*. This is the failing — the davening continues without one truly being there. Nobody, says *Tosafot*, truly manages to concentrate in every prayer every single day, and that is the inescapable daily shortcoming the Gemara is lamenting.

### Rav Yehuda's Distribution

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav: rov b'gezel u'miut b'arayos v'hacol b'lashon hara* — most people sin with *gezel*, theft; a minority with sexual transgressions; and everyone with *lashon hara*. Again, the Gemara asks: *b'lashon hara salka da'atach?* — surely not outright *lashon hara* every single day. The answer is the same: *avak lashon hara*. Talking about other people, saying something about the rabbi after synagogue, commenting on how someone conducted themselves — these things are nearly universal, and almost inevitably some trace of *avak lashon hara* enters in. It is exceedingly difficult to speak only of people in strictly positive terms at all times.

### Avak Gezel & Commerce

The claim that *rov* — the majority of people — sin with *gezel* is arresting. One should look at the *Rashbam*, who explains: *rov b'nei adam chashudim al hagezel k'ein gezel shemorim heiteira b'masa u'matan* — most people are, in some form, implicated in something resembling theft, rationalizing in the context of commerce and business dealings, preventing someone from earning what is legitimately theirs, taking small liberties with others' property. One might call this *avak gezel* — not brazen theft, but the dust of it. People take a pen from the supply cabinet at work and bring it home; they rationalize small appropriations that they would never consciously categorize as theft. 

The psychological mechanism here is well illustrated by a point about how people distinguish between different types of dishonesty: taking a dollar bill that belongs to someone feels clearly like stealing, whereas taking a soda from a communal table does not register in the same way, even if its monetary value is comparable. People do not operate purely from a rule system; they operate based on whether an action makes them see themselves differently. Sticking one's hand in someone's purse and removing money forces one to confront the identity of being a thief. Taking a pen from the supply room does not. Yet the moral equivalence is clear, even if the psychological experience is entirely different.

## Minhag HaMedinah

### Rabban Shimon's Rule

The Gemara turns to the ruling of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *hacol k'minhag hamedina* — all the procedures for how a *shtar* is written follow the custom of the place. The rules governing how a *shtar mekushar* or *shtar pashut* is to be executed are shaped by the prevailing norms of the community in which the document is produced.

### Tanna Kamma's Position

The Gemara immediately asks: *v'Tanna Kamma leis lei minhag hamedina?* — does the *Tanna Kamma* not accept the principle of *minhag hamedina*? One might have supposed that since the Rabbis established fixed rules for the composition of legal documents, those rules would need to be followed absolutely, independent of local custom. What is fascinating is that the Gemara seems to take for granted that everyone would agree that the parameters of a *shtar mekushar* or *shtar pashut* should in principle be shaped by *minhag hamedina*. After all, when two parties engage in a transaction, the implicit framework governing their agreement is shaped by the norms of the society in which they operate.

Yet this raises a genuine difficulty. There is a distinction between the substantive obligations between the parties — which may indeed be colored by communal convention — and the formal requirements for a document to constitute a valid *shtar* as a matter of law. Even if both parties agree that a document should serve as a legitimate *shtar*, it is not clear that their agreement makes it so; validity flows from an objective legal framework. It is therefore genuinely interesting that the Gemara assumes *minhag hamedina* should shape even the formal parameters of a *shtar*.

### Rav Ashi's Construction

*Amar Rav Ashi* — Rav Ashi now constructs a case not found in the *Mishnah* itself, one that would explain where Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the *Chachamim* genuinely disagree. The disagreement is not about the technical details of how a *shtar* is written; it concerns the very choice between writing a *shtar mekushar* and a *shtar pashut*.

Here is his construction. *Ba'atra de-nehigi pashut* — in a place where the norm is to write a simple *shtar* — *ve-amar lei avid li pashut* — and the person instructs the *sofer*: write me a simple *shtar* — *ve-azal avid lei mekushar* — and the *sofer* went and wrote a *mekushar* instead. The Gemara says: *kfeida* — this is an insistence, a genuine instruction, and the *shtar* is invalid. The underlying principle is *da'as hamitchayiv* — a *shtar* requires the authorization of the party being obligated. A *shtar* written against that party's will or without conforming to what they authorized is not a valid *shtar*. Since both the prevailing custom and the explicit instruction pointed toward a *pashut*, writing a *mekushar* fell outside the scope of what was authorized.

### Kfeida vs. Mar'eh Makom

The opposite case: where the custom is *mekushar* and the instruction was for a *mekushar*, and the *sofer* produced a *pashut* — again, *kfeida*, invalid. The more interesting case, and the one that generates the actual dispute, is when both options are available in that locale: *ki pligi ba'atra de-nehigi bi-pashut u-mekushar* — where both *pashut* and *mekushar* are acceptable. The person tells the *sofer*: write me a *pashut* — and the *sofer* produces a *mekushar*.

The *Tanna Kamma* would say this is *kfeida* — an insistent instruction. Even though both options were open, the person specified the simpler form, and the *sofer* was only authorized to produce that form. Departing from the instruction, even toward what might seem like a superior document, invalidates the *shtar*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, by contrast, holds *mar'eh makom hu lo* — the instruction was merely a pointer, not an insistence. The person was saying, in effect: at minimum, a *pashut* will do; they were not demanding that it be *pashut* exclusively. Producing a *mekushar* — the more elaborate option — does not violate the authorization.

The logic of *mar'eh makom* deserves attention. If the person said: write me a *mekushar*, and the *sofer* produced the simpler *pashut*, one would presumably expect fewer grounds for complaint, since the *sofer* chose the easier option. The interesting case is specifically when the *sofer* upgraded: the person asked for the simpler version and received the more elaborate one. According to Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, the instruction was not a ceiling but a floor. One might observe that this mirrors a common domestic dynamic — whether a request for a specific item at the market is an absolute specification or merely a suggestion of minimal acceptability.

The Gemara then uses this conceptual distinction — *kfeida* versus *mar'eh makom* — as a lens to examine parallel debates elsewhere.

### Three Sages Agree

*Amar Abaye: Rabbi Shimon ben Gamliel v'Rabbi Shimon v'Rabbi Elazar kulhu sevira lehu mar'eh makom hu lo* — Abaye identifies three *Tannaim* who all share the position that in certain contexts, instructions are to be read as guidelines rather than as absolute specifications.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel is the case already discussed. Rabbi Shimon is cited from a *Mishnah* dealing with *kiddushin*: if a man says to a woman's agent, betroth her with this silver *dinar*, and the agent betrothed her with a gold *dinar* — Rabbi Shimon holds she is betrothed, *im hite'ato l'shevach harei zu mekudeshet*, because she was deceived in a way that was advantageous to her. The man was not insisting on silver as the only acceptable metal; he was merely indicating that he was not prepared to offer more than silver. This is somewhat parallel to *mar'eh makom*, though one might note it is not quite identical — there the question is whether the description functions as an exact specification or merely as a minimum representation.

Rabbi Elazar is cited from a *Mishnah* in *Gittin*: a woman says to an agent, go receive my *get* from my husband in such-and-such a place, and the agent received it in a different place. Rabbi Elazar validates the *get*. This is perhaps the clearest parallel of the three. Was she specifying the location because it was essential to her — *kfeida* — or was she merely indicating where she expected her husband to be found — *mar'eh makom*? Rabbi Elazar holds the latter: she was helping the agent locate her husband, not mandating the precise venue of receipt. The other opinion holds *kfeida* — her specification was binding, and a *get* received elsewhere was received without proper authorization.

## Shtar Be-Ed Echad

### Mishna's Necessity

The *Mishnah* has taught that a *mekushar* written with two witnesses — rather than the required three — is invalid, and a *pashut* written with only one witness is invalid. The Gemara asks: why did the *Mishnah* need to teach the latter case at all? The invalidity of a document bearing only one witness seems entirely obvious. One witness has never sufficed to win a case in court; why would the *Mishnah* bother stating what is self-evident?

The answer, from Abaye, is that the *Mishnah*'s ruling is needed to address a case that is not obvious at all.

### Abaye's Position

*Amar Abaye lo nitzrecha* — the novelty of the *Mishnah* is specifically in the following case: *de-afilu ed echad bi-chtav ve-ed echad bal peh lo mitztarfi* — that even one written witness in a *shtar* and one oral witness testifying before the court do not combine to produce valid *edus*. The case would be: a *shtar* says Charlie borrowed a hundred *zuz* from David, signed by one witness. David now brings another person into court who testifies orally that he saw the same transaction. One might have thought these two testimonies — one preserved in writing, one delivered orally — combine to constitute valid two-witness testimony. Abaye says no: they do not combine. The *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* is precisely this.

### Rashbam's Interpretation

One might further ask: does Abaye's position mean that a *shtar* bearing a single witness's signature is entirely without value? Under normal circumstances, a lone witness can at least compel the opposing party to take a *shevua* — an oath. If a single person comes to court and testifies that Charlie owes David money, Charlie cannot simply ignore it; he must swear that the claim is false. Does a *shtar be-ed echad* carry at least this weight?

The *Rashbam* holds that it does. Examining his language: *lemishnei ed echad be-pashut pasul* — the *Mishnah* is not saying a *shtar* with one witness is valueless as a form of *edus*. It is saying that it lacks the specific weight of a proper *shtar* — it cannot create liens on encumbered property, because that power, *shenei edim kesuvin be-shtar hu de-mashvei leh kala*, derives from the publicity established by two witnesses signing the document. Without two signatories, the document has no greater force than an oral loan, collectible only from *bnei chorin* — unencumbered assets. But as the testimony of a single witness, it retains force. The *Rashbam* thus reads Abaye as saying: they do not combine to give the document the status of a full *shtar*, but taken together, two testimonies — one written, one oral — do amount to the combined testimony of two witnesses.

*Tosafot* challenges this reading. If even one *shtar* with one witness retains the force of *ed echad* testimony, one might ask why the Gemara never raises the question of combining two separate *shtarot*, each signed by a different single witness. The silence on that point, *Tosafot* argues, signals that a *shtar be-ed echad* carries no testimonial weight at all. The Torah established *shtarot* as a form of valid written testimony — but only when executed properly, with two witnesses. A document bearing only one signature is not a deficient *shtar*; it is not a *shtar* at all. And since it is nothing, it cannot combine with anything.

### Amemar's Position

*Ve-Amemar achshir be-ed echad bi-chtav ve-ed echad ba'al peh* — Amemar goes further and holds that a written witness and an oral witness do combine and produce valid testimony. Rav Ashi challenged him: what about Abaye's position? Amemar replied, *lo shmiya li* — which the Gemara glosses as *lo svira li*, meaning not that he had never encountered Abaye's view, but that he did not accept it.

If Amemar is correct, a difficulty arises from the *Mishnah*: the *Mishnah* appears to teach that a *shtar pashut be-ed echad* is invalid, which would seem to contradict Amemar's ruling that it can be validated in combination with oral testimony. The Gemara resolves this: the *Mishnah* is not teaching primarily about the *pashut* with one witness. The *chiddush* of the *Mishnah* is about the *mekushar* with two witnesses. *De-shnayim ba-mekushar ke-echad ba-pashut* — two witnesses in a *mekushar* are treated as equivalent to one witness in a *pashut*. Just as one witness in a *pashut* standing alone is completely invalid — *pasul de-oraita* in the sense that we treat it as wholly insufficient — so too two witnesses in a *mekushar* are treated as if they were equally and totally invalid. The *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* note that since the three-witness requirement for a *mekushar* is rabbinic in origin, *de-oraita* the document with two witnesses might be acceptable; but the rabbinic posture is to treat it as if it were *pasul de-oraita* entirely.

## Rabbi Yirmiyah Story

### Yirmiyah Expelled

The Gemara now tells us that Amemar's position — that a written and oral witness combine — can be supported from the famous story of Rabbi Yirmiyah. The colleagues sent questions to Rabbi Yirmiyah, and it is noted that this occurred after Rabbi Yirmiyah had been expelled from the *beit midrash*. The background is well known from the opening chapters of *Bava Batra*: Rabbi Yirmiyah was in the habit of raising extreme boundary cases, asking what the law would be in scenarios that teetered right on the edge of a rule. When discussing the principle that a bird found within fifty *amot* of its nest is presumed to belong to the nest's owner, Rabbi Yirmiyah asked: what if one leg is within fifty *amot* and the other is outside? *Al da afkuhu le-Rabbi Yirmiyah mi-bei midrasha* — for this kind of question they expelled him from the *beit midrash*.

It is worth noting a thematic undercurrent that runs through the questions that follow. Many of them concern whether something that is separated, or partially outside, can be combined with something that is inside — whether a boundary case can be drawn back into the group. Rabbi Yirmiyah was himself expelled for asking about things that are partly in and partly out. The questions sent to him are, structurally, about combining what has been separated. And the resolution of the narrative, as we will see, is his reintegration into the *beit midrash*. The story has the shape of an extended metaphor.

### First Question

*Shalchu mitam chavraya le-Rabbi Yirmiyah* — the colleagues sent to Rabbi Yirmiyah: *ed echad bi-chtav ve-ed echad al peh, mahu she-yitztarfu?* — if one witness is in a document and one testifies orally, can they combine?

### Tanna Kamma Debate

Before presenting Rabbi Yirmiyah's answer, the Gemara frames the question against a known dispute in *Sanhedrin*: the debate between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha regarding the combination of witnesses who have not witnessed the same event. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that two witnesses can only combine if they testify to the *same* event. If one says: I saw Charlie borrow a hundred *zuz* from David on Sunday, and another says: I saw him borrow a hundred *zuz* on Monday — they are testifying to two distinct events and cannot combine, even though their testimony would point to the same conclusion. Similarly, if one witnessed a loan and another heard the borrower's admission, those are different facts and cannot be joined. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha disagrees: witnesses testifying to different events or different manifestations of the same obligation can combine.

The Gemara reasons that under the *Tanna Kamma*'s view, the question does not even arise. *De-afilu shnayim bi-chtav vi-shnayim al peh, lo mitztarfi* — even if both testimonies were written or both were oral, if they are testifying to different things they cannot combine under the *Tanna Kamma*. An implicit assumption in the Gemara is that when a *shtar* bears only one witness's signature alongside a single oral witness, it is presumed they are testifying to different events — otherwise, why does one not have two signatories on the *shtar* from the outset? If two witnesses were both present, the standard procedure would be to have both sign. The existence of a *shtar be-ed echad* alongside a separate oral witness implies that they each saw a different aspect of the situation.

According to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, however — who permits combination even when witnesses testify to different events — the question is live: perhaps even one written and one oral can combine, or perhaps the difference in *medium* of testimony creates a special barrier even where the difference in *content* would not.

### Yirmiyah's Response

*Shalach lehu* — Rabbi Yirmiyah sent back: *ani eini kedai she-shlachtem li* — I am unworthy of the question you have sent me. After being expelled from the *beit midrash*, he addresses the scholars with remarkable humility: *ella kach da'at talmidchem noteh* — here is the way your student's thinking inclines. *She-yitztarfu* — that they should combine. According to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha, one written and one oral witness should be capable of combining.

### Second Question

The Gemara then introduces another version of the questions sent to Rabbi Yirmiyah, attributed to Abaye: *shnayim she-he'idu echad be-veit din zeh ve-echad be-veit din zeh, ma'u she-yavo beit din etzel beit din ve-yitztarfu?* — two witnesses who testified, one before this *beit din* and one before that *beit din*: may the two courts confer and combine the testimonies into a single valid *edus*?

### Testimony Timing

This question maps onto another parallel dispute — that between the *Tanna Kamma* and Rabbi Natan — concerning not whether witnesses saw the same event, but whether they testified at the same time. The *Tanna Kamma* holds that witnesses must testify simultaneously, in a single unified act before the court. Rabbi Natan permits witnesses to testify at different times and holds their testimonies can still combine. According to the *Tanna Kamma*, the question does not arise: *de-afilu be-chad be-dina nami lo mitztarfi* — even within a single *beit din*, if the witnesses testified at separate times they do not combine, so a fortiori testifying before separate courts would certainly not work. According to Rabbi Natan, however, who permits separate-time testimony within a single court, the question is whether testimony before entirely different courts can similarly be joined.

### Courts Combine

*Shlach lehu* — Rabbi Yirmiyah sent back: *ani eini kedai she-atem shlachtem li*. *Ella kach da'at talmidchem noteh she-yitztarfu* — here is where your student's thinking inclines: they should combine.

A third version of the story, attributed to Mar bar Rav Ashi, poses a different construction. Two witnesses gave their full testimony — properly received by two witnesses simultaneously — before *beit din* A. For whatever reason, that court delayed. The witnesses then repeated their testimony before *beit din* B, which also received it properly. Can one now convene a reconstituted court composed of members from each of the two courts to adjudicate the case? The difficulty is that the reconstituted court did not, as a body, receive the testimony together. The Gemara frames this as a *kal vachomer* relative to the *Tanna Kamma*'s position: if Rabbi Natan permits witnesses to combine even when testifying at different times, certainly once the testimony has been received properly and in full, the judges may combine. And even according to the *Tanna Kamma* — who would not permit witnesses who testified at different times to combine — it may be that *dayyanim*, judges, who received testimony fully and properly in their respective courts, can combine their court membership. *Shlach lehu: ani eini kedai she-atem shlachtem li. Ella kach da'at talmidchem noteh she-yitztarfu* — they should combine.

### Third Question

*Shlosha she-yashvu le-kayem et ha-shtar* — three judges sat to perform *kiyum ha-shtar*, to validate a document. A *beit din* of three is required for this process. *U-met echad me-hem* — and one of them died. The question: *tzrichi le-michtav be-moshav tlata havina ve-chad leitei?* — must they write, in the body of the validation, "we were three who sat together and one is no longer present"? Or may they simply write that the three of them sat and were *mekayem* the *shtar*, without disclosing the absence?

### Acknowledging Absence

There is something quietly resonant in this question given the narrative context. The three sat together as a complete *beit din*, and now that completeness has been disrupted; one is no longer among them. How much must this reality be acknowledged? There is an echo here of the brothers of Yaakov before the viceroy of Egypt — *ha-echad einennu*, the one is no longer present. How does a diminished group speak about its own reduction?

*Shlach lehu: ani eini kedai she-atem shlachtem li. Ella kach da'at talmidchem noteh she-tzrichim le-michtav be-moshav tlata havina ve-chad leitei* — they must write it. They must acknowledge that one is no longer present.

### Yirmiyah Restored

*Ve-al da ayiluhi le-Rabbi Yirmiyah le-vei midrasha* — and because of this, they brought Rabbi Yirmiyah back into the *beit midrash*. Whether it was the wisdom of his answers, the humility with which he delivered them, or some unspoken mutual desire for reconciliation, he was restored. The one who had been expelled for asking what happens when something is partly inside and partly outside was now brought fully back inside.

## Shtar Be-Ed Echad (II)

### Tosafot's Limitation

Returning now to the substantive question of the *shtar be-ed echad*, *Tosafot* imposes a significant limitation on the entire discussion. *Tosafot* is deeply uncomfortable with any reading that grants an *shtar* bearing a single witness's signature independent testimonial value. The Torah established *shtarot* as a valid modality of written testimony; but this institution applies, in *Tosafot*'s view, only to documents executed with the full complement of two witnesses. A document bearing only one signature is not a diminished *shtar*; it is simply not a *shtar* at all, and therefore carries no testimonial weight of any kind.

This creates an obvious difficulty: if a *shtar be-ed echad* is worth nothing, how can it combine with anything? The Gemara's discussion of combination presupposes that the written witness contributes something to the combination. *Tosafot* resolves this by radically limiting the case. The oral witness in the combination is not testifying to the same underlying transaction as the signature in the *shtar*. Rather, the oral witness is testifying specifically to *edei mesirah* — to the act of handing over the *shtar*. In matters of *gittin* and similar documents, there is a principle that the witnesses to the *mesirah*, the act of delivery, are closely associated with the document itself, to the point of being treated as co-signatories in some sense. So when the Gemara entertains the combination of one written and one oral witness, what it actually means is: Eli signed the *shtar* as the one who observed Charlie borrowing a hundred *zuz*, and then I — a separate person — testify that I saw Charlie hand that *shtar* over to David. My testimony concerns the *handing over*, not the underlying loan. Because witnesses to the *mesirah* are functionally treated as part of the document's authentication, my testimony can be considered in combination with Eli's signature to produce what functions as a two-witness *shtar*.

This also explains, for *Tosafot*, why the Gemara frames the written and oral testimonies as concerning different events — which would be exactly right if one concerns the loan and the other concerns the delivery. The apparent strangeness of treating them as witnesses to different things is, in *Tosafot*'s reading, a direct consequence of what they are each actually witnessing.

*Tosafot* then cites a parallel from the *Yerushalmi* at the bottom of the *Tosafot* on 165a. *Ba'ei Rabbi Zeira: ed echad bi-chtav ve-ed echad al peh, mahu she-yitztarfu?* — can one written and one oral witness combine? The *Yerushalmi* immediately asks: *ve-ed echad bi-chtav klum hu?* — is a single witness in a document worth anything at all? The *Yerushalmi* thus shares *Tosafot*'s basic premise: there is no such thing as a half-valid *shtar*. It then limits the question accordingly: *k'she-hayu shnayim u-matzu le-kayeim ksav yado shel rishon ve-lo matzu le-kayeim ksav yado shel sheini* — the case is where there *were* two witnesses on the *shtar*, but we were only able to validate one of their signatures. Here the *shtar* is not inherently one-signed; it is a proper two-witness document whose second signature simply cannot be independently confirmed. That is a very different question from a *shtar* that was deliberately produced with only one signatory.

The overall structure of this sugya, then, involves a real dispute with significant halakhic stakes: does a *shtar be-ed echad* carry any weight at all — as a full *shtar*, as the testimony of an *ed echad*, or as nothing? The *Rashbam* reads Abaye as granting it the weight of *ed echad* testimony, with *Amemar* going further and granting it the full weight of a *shtar* when reinforced by oral testimony. *Tosafot* denies it any independent value and limits the entire discussion to the specific scenario of *edei mesirah*. How to rule remains a matter requiring further investigation.

## Contradictions in Shtar

### Minimalist Principle

The Gemara now moves to a series of cases addressing contradictions or ambiguities within the body of a *shtar* itself, and the principles for resolving them. The first case: the *shtar* says Charlie borrowed from David *zuzin mea de-innun sela-in esrin* — a hundred *zuz* which are twenty *sela*. The difficulty is arithmetical: a hundred *zuz* is actually twenty-five *sela*, not twenty. The stated equivalents do not line up. *Ein lo ela esrin* — David may collect only twenty. The governing principle is *yad ba'al ha-shtar al ha-tachtona*: the holder of the *shtar*, who is trying to extract money from another, bears the burden of proof, and where there is ambiguity or contradiction, the *shtar* is read in its most minimal, most restrictive way. David cannot collect more than what the least generous reading of the document permits.

The *Rashbam* notes that in the world of precious metal coinage, it is possible that light *zuzin* and heavy *sela-in* could actually align numerically — so the contradiction may not be as stark as it seems in modern terms. But even if there is genuine contradiction, the minimalist reading controls.

The converse case: the *shtar* says *zuzin mea de-innun tlatin sela-in* — a hundred *zuz* which are thirty *sela*. Again, arithmetic is off in the other direction — thirty *sela* is more than a hundred *zuz*. *Ein lo ela mane* — David may collect only a hundred *zuz*. The rule is consistent: collect the lesser of the two figures.

### Erased Amount

*Kasav zuzin de-innun ve-nimchak* — the *shtar* recorded silver *zuzin* to the amount of, and the amount was erased. If David tried to alter the erased figure before presenting the *shtar* to *beit din*, the evidence of the erasure would be visible and the forgery apparent. So presumably no such tampering occurred. But the *beit din* is now confronted with a *shtar* that says Charlie borrowed silver coins in the amount of blank. *Ein pachos mishnayim* — the most David can collect is two, the minimum that the word "coins" in the plural can imply. The minimalist principle applies here as well.

Similarly, *kesef sela-in de-innun ve-nimchak* — silver *sela-in* in the amount of blank — *ein pachos mishnayim*. And *darkonos de-innun ve-nimchak* — gold *darkonin* in the amount of blank — *ein pachos mishnayim*. In every case where the amount has been lost, the document is construed as minimally as possible.

### Bottom Takes Precedence

The case of clear, unambiguous contradiction: *kasav bo milmala mane u-milmata masayim* — the *shtar* says at the top that Charlie borrowed a hundred, and at the bottom that he borrowed two hundred. Or the reverse — two hundred at the top and a hundred at the bottom. *Hakol holeich achar ha-tachton* — everything follows the bottom. This is a different rule from the minimalist principle applied in the ambiguous cases above. Where there is plain contradiction between two stated figures, we do not automatically go with the smaller; we go with what appears at the end of the body of the document.

It should be noted that this refers to what is written at the *bottom* of the narrative portion of the *shtar* — not the final line of the document, from which no legal conclusions are drawn. The bottom of the substantive text is the controlling part.

### Backup Function

*Im kein lama kosevin et ha-elyon?* — if the bottom controls, why write the upper part at all? The *Mishnah* itself raises this question and answers it: *she-im timachek os achas min ha-tachton tilmad min ha-elyon* — if a letter or word in the bottom portion is erased or damaged, one can fill in the ambiguity from the fuller narrative at the top. The upper portion of the *shtar* serves as a kind of backup or reference copy. It is not dispositive, but it is not useless: it allows the court to resolve ambiguities introduced by later damage or erasure in the binding lower section.

### Coin Types & Minimums

*Tanu Rabbanan: amar kesef* — if the *shtar* simply says Charlie borrowed *kesef*, silver, *ein pachos mi-dinar kesef* — the minimum is one silver *dinar*. *Kesef dinarin* or *dinarei kesef*, using the plural form, *ein pachos mishnei dinarin kesef* — the plural indicates at minimum two silver *dinarin*. *Kesef bi-dinarin* — silver in the amount of *dinarin* — *ain pachos mi-bishnei dinarim zahav* — the minimum is two gold *dinarin*, because the preposition *bi-* before *dinarin* signals that the *dinarin* refers to a different metal than the one named.

## Currency Terminology

### Silver Default

If the *shtar* simply states *kesef* — silver — the minimum obligation is one silver *dinar*.

### Silver Dinars Plural

If the *shtar* states *kesef dinarin* or *dinarei kesef*, employing the plural form, the minimum is two silver *dinarin*. The plural form cannot be resolved below two.

### Silver in Gold Units

If the *shtar* states *kesef bi-dinarin* — silver in the amount of *dinarin* — the minimum is two gold *dinarin*. The use of *bi-dinarin*, silver measured *in* dinars, signals that the *dinarin* being referenced are of a different metal — here, gold.

### Gold Default

If the *shtar* simply states *zahav* — gold — the minimum is one gold *dinar*.

### Minted Coins

A natural objection arises: when the *shtar* says *kesef*, perhaps it means a silver bar or ingot — a *naska* — rather than a coin. Rabbi Elazar answers: *de-kasiv bei matbea* — it is explicit in the document that we are dealing with minted coins, not raw metal. But then a second objection: perhaps it means a silver *pruta*, a penny-sized silver coin? That is a much smaller denomination than a *dinar*.

### Geographic Variation

Rav Pappa answers: *ba'atra de-lo sagya bi-pruta de-chaspa* — in the locale in question, small silver denominations of the size of a *pruta* simply do not circulate. Silver coinage in that region begins at the *dinar* level. One cannot interpret *kesef* as a *pruta* when silver *prutos* do not exist there.

### Gold in Silver Units

Parallel to the silver case: *zahav bi-dinarin* — gold in the amount of *dinarin* — the minimum is two silver *dinarin*. When gold is measured *bi-dinarin*, the *dinarin* refers to the other metal, i.e., silver.

### No Gold Pennies

The final objection is raised in the gold case: perhaps *zahav* means a tiny gold coin — the equivalent of a gold penny. The Gemara dismisses this with a straightforward empirical observation: *de-pruta de-dahava lo avdi inshi* — people do not mint gold pennies. The physical and economic absurdity of a gold coin at the *pruta* scale means that the interpretation simply cannot stand. Gold coinage begins at the *dinar*, and the interpretation of *zahav* in a *shtar* follows accordingly.

The Gemara's discussion of *kesef bi-dinarin* and *zahav bi-dinarin*, and the precise logic of why the preposition *bi-* signals the opposite metal, will continue in the following day's shiur.
