# Eruvin 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Overlapping Cities & Sta…

### Chazon Ish Squares

Before engaging with the new material on daf 60, it is worth consolidating the key principles established in the preceding discussion. The Chazon Ish addresses the practical consequence of squaring off adjacent cities: if the squared-off boundaries of two cities overlap, they are treated as a single unified area. This has dramatic real-world implications, effectively merging what might appear to be geographically separate municipalities into one contiguous halachic zone.

### 600K Criterion

The broader discussion of cities turning from private to public status, and vice versa, revolves around the question of *reshut harabim*. Rashi holds that the operative criterion is whether the city has the potential to contain 600,000 people, which is the threshold for a biblical *reshut harabim*. The Ramban agrees that the issue is fundamentally about *reshut harabim*, but reads the criterion far more narrowly: it must pertain to actual public ownership of the streets, and he is deeply skeptical of the 600,000-person standard as a halachic measure.

### Leaving Off Sections

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָהּ הָיוּ מְשַׁעֲרִים חֲכָמִים, וְהִיא הָיְתָה שִׁיּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** And **the Sages would use it to measure the size of the section that must be excluded from an *eiruv*, and it itself was the excluded section of the *eiruv* of a larger city that was adjacent to it.**

The *mishna* established two rules. First, if a city began as a city of the *rabim* — a public city — that identity remains even if its character subsequently became more private. As a result, to signal that this city retains the character of a *reshut harabim*, one cannot make a single unified *eruv* encompassing the entire city. A section must be left off. One explanation is symbolic: the exclusion of a portion reflects the fact that this is not a unified private domain but rather a space belonging to the masses. A related rationale is that it reminds people of the prohibition of carrying — just as some communities once annually suspended their *eruv* on a given day so that people would not forget the underlying *melacha*.

### Unified or Multiple

The second rule in the *mishna* cuts in the opposite direction. If a city began as privately owned — an *ir shel yachid* — even if it has since grown and become more public, the requirement is to make a unified *eruv*, not multiple separate ones. The underlying logic is that such a city retains its identity as a private space, and any shared areas could be halachically problematic without a unified *eruv*. Additionally, the *mishna*'s prior discussion raised the complication of whether one can even make an *eruv* around a genuine *reshut harabim* using only a *tzurat hapetach* or *lechi* and *kora*, since the Gemara had discussed the need for at least one closed end. These distinctions — whether to unify or leave off, and whether an *eruv* can be constructed at all — together frame the opening context of daf 60.

## Ladder/Wall Status

### Rav Ami Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַמֵּי בַּר אַדָּא הַרְפַּנָּאָה מֵרַבָּה: סוּלָּם מִכָּאן וּפֶתַח מִכָּאן, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב: סוּלָּם תּוֹרַת פֶּתַח עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ami bar Adda from Harpanya raised a dilemma before Rabba: If a public domain has a ladder on one side, to allow people to scale the wall that blocks it, and an entrance on the other side, what is the *halakha*?** Is it considered a public domain that is open on both sides? **Rabba said to him that Rav said as follows: A ladder has the status of an entrance,** and therefore the public domain is considered open on both sides.

The Gemara now presents a question posed by Rav Ami bar Ada to Rabba. The scenario involves a *reshut harabim* that has a wall on one side with a ladder leaning against it — *sulam mikan u-fetach mikan*, a ladder on one side and an opening on the other. The question is whether this configuration is treated as a genuinely open-ended *reshut harabim* — which would make it impossible to encircle with an *eruv* — or whether the ladder-wall constitutes a sufficient closure. Rav, as reported to Rabba, is said to have ruled that *sulam torat petach alav*: a ladder confers upon the wall the status of an opening, rendering the *reshut harabim* open-ended and problematic for *eruv* purposes.

### Dual Function

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב נַחְמָן: לָא תְּצִיתוּ לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר רַב אַדָּא אָמַר רַב: סוּלָּם תּוֹרַת פֶּתַח עָלָיו, וְתוֹרַת מְחִיצָה עָלָיו. תּוֹרַת מְחִיצָה עָלָיו, כְּדַאֲמַרַן. תּוֹרַת פֶּתַח עָלָיו, בְּסוּלָּם שֶׁבֵּין שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵירוֹת, רָצוּ — אֶחָד מְעָרֵב, רָצוּ — שְׁנַיִם מְעָרְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to them: Do not listen to him. Rav Adda said that Rav said as follows: A ladder has the status of an entrance in certain cases, and it has the status of a partition in other cases. It has the status of a partition in the case that we mentioned, where there is a ladder at the end of a public domain.** In this case, the ladder is not considered an entrance and therefore the public domain is considered closed at that end. **It has the status of an entrance in the case of a ladder between two courtyards. If the residents of the courtyards wish, they may join the two courtyards by means of the ladder and establish one *eiruv*; if they wish, the two courtyards may each establish a separate *eiruv*.**

Rav Nachman intervenes with a correction, telling the questioner not to accept that account of Rav's position. The accurate tradition, transmitted by Rav Ada bar Ahava in the name of Rav, is more nuanced: *sulam torat petach alav v'torat mechitza alav* — a ladder on a wall functions sometimes as an opening and sometimes as a barrier, depending on which leniency is needed. As a *mechitza*, it renders the wall-plus-ladder sufficient to close off a *reshut harabim*, enabling the construction of an *eruv*. As a *petach*, it operates differently, and the Gemara proceeds to explain when that function applies.

### Two Courtyards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן הָכִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר וְאַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, אִם יֵשׁ לִפְנֵיהֶם דַּקָּה אַרְבָּעָה — אֵינָהּ אוֹסֶרֶת, וְאִם לָאו — אוֹסֶרֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara asks: Did Rav Naḥman say this? Didn't Rav Naḥman say that Shmuel said: With regard to residents of the ground floor of a courtyard and residents of a balcony, i.e., the floor above the ground floor, who forgot and did not establish a joint *eiruv*, if there is a partition four handbreadths wide in front of it, the balcony does not prohibit the residents of the courtyard from carrying; and if not, it prohibits the residents of the courtyard from carrying?** This indicates that a ladder is considered an entrance, as the courtyard and the balcony are considered connected.

The *petach* function of a ladder is illustrated in the case of *besulam shebein shtei chatzerot* — a ladder positioned between two courtyards separated by a wall. In that context, the ladder functions like a doorway: *ratzu echad me'arvin ratzu shnayim me'arvin* — if the residents wish, they may treat themselves as one courtyard and make a single unified *eruv*; if they prefer, they may treat themselves as two distinct courtyards and each make their own *eruv*. This is precisely the halachic flexibility afforded by an actual doorway. A fully open boundary would force unity; a solid wall would require separation; a doorway — or a ladder functioning as one — permits choice.

This raises a pointed question from the Gemara. Rav Nachman himself had previously cited a ruling from Shmuel regarding *anshei chatzer ve-anshei mirpesset* — the residents of a courtyard below and the residents of a balcony above who share access to that courtyard, and who forgot and did not make a unified *eruv*. The ruling there is that if the people of the balcony placed a small barrier — a *daka* of four *tefachim* — in front of their ladder-access to the courtyard, they are considered legally separate and do not create a prohibition for one another. Without such a barrier, they do forbid one another.

Now, if a ladder has the dual function described — capable of serving as either a *petach* or a *mechitza* — why should such a barrier be necessary at all? If the residents of the balcony wish to treat the ladder as a *mechitza*, they should simply be able to do so and thereby exempt themselves from the *eruv* requirement without constructing any additional barrier. And even if they treat it as a *petach*, a *petach* allows for the choice of separate *eruvin*, so they should still be independent without a *daka*. Why, then, does the *baraita* require the extra barrier?

### Low Balcony

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, בִּדְלֹא גְּבוֹהָ מִרְפֶּסֶת עֲשָׂרָה. וְאִי לֹא גְּבוֹהָ מִרְפֶּסֶת עֲשָׂרָה, כִּי קָא עָבֵיד דַּקָּה, מַאי הָוֵי? בִּמְגוּפֶּפֶת עַד עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, דְּכֵיוָן דְּעָבֵיד דַּקָּה — אִיסְתַּלּוֹקֵי אִיסְתַּלּוּק לֵיהּ מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **With what are we dealing here? With a case where the balcony is not ten handbreadths high from the ground.** Consequently, it does not constitute a domain in its own right, and it is part of the courtyard. **If the balcony is not ten handbreadths high, when one places a partition, what of it?** The balcony should nevertheless be considered part of the courtyard. **We are dealing here with a balcony that is entirely fenced off except for a section up to ten cubits wide, which serves as an entrance. In that case, since the residents of the balcony place a partition at this entrance, they thereby remove themselves entirely from the courtyard.**

The Gemara resolves this by limiting the *baraita* to a highly specific case: *bidlo gavoah mirpeset asarah* — the balcony is less than ten *tefachim* off the ground. If the balcony is below ten *tefachim*, the concept of a wall-with-ladder is inapplicable, because nothing below ten *tefachim* constitutes a valid *mechitza* in the first place. The balcony residents are effectively considered to be on the ground level, meaning there is no wall and no ladder for halachic purposes — they are simply part of the courtyard.

This immediately generates a follow-up difficulty: if they are considered flush with the ground and the balcony is below ten *tefachim*, then what does the small *daka* of four *tefachim* accomplish? The Gemara answers: *bimgufefet ad eser amot* — the case involves a balcony that is surrounded by a wall on all sides except for a gap of up to ten *amot*. An opening of ten *amot* or more is not legally a *petach* — it is treated as a complete break, not a doorway. Therefore, even though the balcony is low and the residents are treated as being on ground level, the ten-*amah* opening prevents them from being considered separate. Once they place even a small barrier at the top of the ladder access, they effectively narrow that opening below ten *amot*, transforming it back into a valid *petach* — and a *petach* permits separation. The barrier achieves precisely this narrowing, and that is why it helps. The *baraita*, then, is not a general case but a very particular configuration, and it poses no challenge to Rav's position that a ladder on a wall can function as either a *petach* or a *mechitza*.

## Continuous Ladders

### Rav Yehuda Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כּוֹתֶל שֶׁרְצָפָהּ בְּסוּלָּמוֹת, אֲפִילּוּ בְּיָתֵר מֵעֶשֶׂר — תּוֹרַת מְחִיצָה עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said that Shmuel said: With regard to a wall that one lined with ladders, even along a length of more than ten cubits, it still retains the status of a partition.** The ladders do not constitute an opening that is more than ten cubits wide, which would cause the wall to be regarded as breached and would invalidate the wall as a partition.

Rav Yehuda rules in the name of Shmuel: *kotel sheritzo b'sulamot* — a wall that has been covered continuously with ladders, *afilu b'yeter me'eser* — even if it spans more than ten *amot* — still retains the status of a *mechitza*. This requires explanation. The Gemara had established earlier that a gap of more than ten *amot* in a wall is not considered a *petach* but a complete breach, rendering the structure not a wall at all, and two areas separated by such a gap are therefore considered a single space, requiring a unified *eruv*. One might think that a series of ladders covering a continuous eleven-*amah* stretch would operate the same way as a break of eleven *amot*, since the people can cross over freely. Rav Yehuda rules otherwise: the physical presence of the wall itself, even adorned with continuous ladders throughout its entire length, preserves the status of a *mechitza*. The ladders do not transform the wall into an open breach, because the wall remains structurally intact.

### Challenge/Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָמֵי לֵיהּ רַב בְּרוֹנָא לְרַב יְהוּדָה בְּמַעְצַרְתָּא דְּבֵי רַב חֲנִינָא: מִי אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל תּוֹרַת מְחִיצָה עָלָיו? וְהָאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אַנְשֵׁי מִרְפֶּסֶת וְאַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר שֶׁשָּׁכְחוּ וְלֹא עֵירְבוּ, אִם יֵשׁ לְפָנֶיהָ דַּקָּה אַרְבָּעָה אֵינָהּ אוֹסֶרֶת, וְאִם לָאו — אוֹסֶרֶת. הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, דְּלֹא גְּבוֹהָ מִרְפֶּסֶת עֲשָׂרָה. וְאִי לֹא גְּבוֹהָ מִרְפֶּסֶת עֲשָׂרָה, כִּי עָבֵיד דַּקָּה מַאי הָוֵי? בִּמְגוּפֶּפֶת עַד עֶשֶׂר אַמּוֹת, דְּכֵיוָן דְּעָבֵיד דַּקָּה — אִיסְתַּלּוֹקֵי אִיסְתַּלַּק מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Beruna raised a contradiction to Rav Yehuda in the winepress at Rav Ḥanina's house: Did Shmuel say that such a wall has the status of a partition? Didn't Rav Naḥman say that Shmuel said: With regard to the residents of a balcony and the residents of a courtyard who forgot and did not establish a joint *eiruv*, if there is a partition four handbreadths wide in front of the entrance to the balcony, the balcony does not prohibit the residents of the courtyard to carry; and if not, it prohibits the residents of the courtyard from carrying? This indicates that a ladder is considered an entrance, as the courtyard and the balcony are considered connected. With what are we dealing here? [where the balcony is not ten handbreadths high.] If the balcony is not ten handbreadths high, when he places a partition, what of it? The balcony should nevertheless be considered part of the courtyard. We are dealing here with a balcony that is entirely fenced off except for a section up to ten cubits wide, which serves as an entrance. In that case, since the residents of the balcony place a partition at this entrance, they thereby remove themselves entirely from the courtyard.**

Rav Bruna challenges Rav Yehuda with a contradiction from the same *baraita* about *anshei chatzer ve-anshei mirpesset* — the people of the courtyard and the balcony who forgot to make a unified *eruv*. The *baraita* states that if there is a *daka* of four *tefachim*, they do not create a prohibition for one another; otherwise they do. The implication seems to be that without the *daka*, the ladder alone — even in a wall — does not suffice to establish separation. But if a wall with ladders is a proper *mechitza*, the ladder on the wall between the balcony and the courtyard should already be sufficient to render them separate without needing any *daka*.

The resolution is the same as before: *hachi asinan delo gavah mirpeset asarah* — the *baraita* is addressing the case where the balcony is less than ten *tefachim* high, so the wall-with-ladder framework is irrelevant. And the further question of why the *daka* helps in that case is answered, again, by the *megufefet ad eser amot* scenario: the *daka* narrows a ten-*amah* gap into a valid *petach*, enabling separation. This is a repetition of the earlier resolution, demonstrating that the *baraita* presents no obstacle to Rav Yehuda's position. Rashi notes in passing that the general principle operative here — that a ladder on a wall can function as either a *petach* or a *mechitza*, whichever is more lenient — reflects the broader principle that in matters of *eiruvin* we apply leniency.

## Kakunai Story

### Abaye's Task

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנְהוּ בְּנֵי קָקוּנָאֵי דְּאָתֵי לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: הַב לַן גַּבְרָא דְּלִיעָרֵב לַן מָאתִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ לְאַבָּיֵי: זִיל עָרֵב לְהוּ, וַחֲזִי דְּלָא מְצַוְוחַתְּ עֲלַהּ בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Certain residents of the city of Kakunya came before Rav Yosef and said to him: Provide us with someone who will establish an *eiruv* for our city. Rav Yosef said to Abaye: Go, establish an *eiruv* for them, and see to it that there is no outcry against it in the study hall,** i.e., make sure the *eiruv* is valid beyond any doubt.

The Gemara now relates an instructive narrative. The people of Kakunai came before Rav Yosef and asked him to send them someone who could arrange an *eruv* for their city. Rav Yosef directed Abaye to go, with a specific instruction: *zil eirev lehu*, go and make an *eruv* for them, *ve-chazi delo metzavchu alach be-bei midrasha* — but be careful that they do not have grounds to complain about you in the *beis medrash*. The charge is thus twofold: do the job correctly, but do not impose unnecessary stringencies that would leave people without a proper *eruv* or impose undue burdens upon them. The term *eirev* here clearly encompasses the entire enterprise of halachically preparing a city to permit carrying — far more than simply placing bread.

### River Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲזַל, חֲזָא לְהָנְהוּ בָּתֵּי דִּפְתִיחִי לְנַהֲרָא. אָמַר: הָנֵי לֶהֱוֵי שִׁיּוּר לְמָתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He went and saw that certain houses opened to the river and not to the city. He said: Let these houses serve as the section excluded from the *eiruv* for the city.**

Abaye arrived and observed that some houses on the edge of the city opened only onto a river — *ptichi le-nahara* — and did not have openings into the public space of the city itself. Since this was an *ir shel rabim*, Abaye needed to identify a section of the city to leave off from the *eruv*. These river-facing houses seemed like an ideal candidate: since their residents accessed the river but not the city's interior, leaving them out of the *eruv* would impose minimal disruption on them. Abaye's initial instinct was to designate them as the *shiyur* — the excluded portion.

### Includability Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר: ״אֵין מְעָרְבִין אֶת כּוּלָּהּ״ תְּנַן, [מִכְּלָל] דְּאִי בָּעֵי לְעָירוֹבֵי, מָצֵי מְעָרְבִי. אֶלָּא אֶיעְבֵּיד לְהוּ כַּוֵּוי, דְּאִי בָּעוּ לְעָירוֹבֵי דֶּרֶךְ חַלּוֹנוֹת מָצוּ מְעָרְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye subsequently retracted and said: This cannot be done, as we learned in the mishna: One may not establish an *eiruv* for all of it; by inference, if they wanted to establish an *eiruv* for the entire city, they would have been able to establish such an *eiruv*, if not for the requirement to exclude a section of the city from the *eiruv*. However, these houses, which do not open to the city, could not have joined in an *eiruv* with the rest of the city in any case, and therefore they cannot serve as the excluded section. Rather, I will create windows for them between the courtyards of their houses and the rest of the city, so that if they want to establish an *eiruv* with the rest of the city by way of the windows, they can establish such an *eiruv*,** and then these houses will be fit to serve as the excluded section.

Abaye then reconsidered. The *mishna* states that one cannot make an *eruv* for the entire city of an *ir shel rabim* — implying that a section must be left off that, in principle, *could* have been included. *Ein me'arvin et kullah* implies *de-va'alei eruvi matzi me'arvei* — that the residents excluded are ones who could theoretically have been part of the *eruv*. The river-facing houses, however, had no openings into the city whatsoever. Since they did not open onto any shared public space, they could not be incorporated into the *eruv* even if one wanted to include them. Leaving off a section that was never includable in the first place does not satisfy the requirement. The *shiyur* must be a section one is choosing to exclude, not a section that was already excluded by circumstance.

### Window Solution

Abaye therefore proposed a solution: *avid lehu kivei* — have them cut windows into their back walls, opening onto the public space of the city. If such windows exist, then in theory these residents *could* access the city and *could* be included in the *eruv* — they would simply have to use the window rather than the river entrance. Their theoretical includability would be established, and Abaye could then legitimately designate them as the excluded portion. The practical reality that they would likely never use the windows, and thus lose little by the exclusion, made them still the most convenient section to leave off.

### Mechuza Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר: לָא בָּעֵי, דְּהָא רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ מְעָרֵב לַהּ לְכוּלַּהּ מָחוֹזָא עַרְסְיָיתָא עַרְסְיָיתָא מִשּׁוּם פֵּירָא דְּבֵי תוֹרֵי, דְּכׇל חַד וְחַד הָוֵי שִׁיּוּר לְחַבְרֵיהּ. וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִי בָּעוּ לְעָרוֹבֵי בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי, לָא מָצוּ מְעָרְבִי.
>
> **Translation:** **He subsequently retracted again and said: This is not necessary, as Rabba bar Avuh established an *eiruv* for the entire city of Meḥoza, which was a public city that had become a private one, neighborhood by neighborhood, due to the fact that the neighborhoods were separated by ditches from which the cattle would feed.** In other words, Rabba bar Avuh established a separate *eiruv* for each neighborhood without excluding any of them, **as he maintained that each one was an excluded section for the other. And although the neighborhoods would not have been able to establish an *eiruv* together even if they wanted to, due to the ditches separating them, the neighborhoods were still able to serve as excluded areas for each other.**

Abaye then reconsidered again, because requiring residents to break windows into their back walls was exactly the kind of unnecessary burden Rav Yosef had warned him to avoid. He recalled a precedent: Rav bar Avuha had made an *eruv* for all of Mechuza — the Babylonian city — by arranging separate *eruvin* for each neighborhood individually, *arisyata arisyata*, because of the large ditches filled with animal fodder (*peira de-vei tori*) that ran between the neighborhoods and effectively separated them. The principle articulated there was *de-kol chada ve-chada havei shiyur le-chavertah* — each neighborhood's *eruv* serves as the excluded portion for every other neighborhood's *eruv*, so that no single *eruv* encompasses the entire *ir shel rabim*.

The crucial point, Abaye reasoned, is that those ditches would have prevented a unified *eruv* in any case — the neighborhoods could not halachically have been merged. Yet each neighborhood's *eruv* still counted as the required *shiyur* for the others. This suggested that a *shiyur* need not be a section that *could* have been included. By analogy, the river-facing houses — which also could not have been incorporated — could still serve as the legitimate *shiyur* for Kakunai's *eruv*.

### Grain Storage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אָמַר: כַּוֵּוי נָמֵי לָא בָּעֵי, דְּהָהוּא בֵּי תִיבְנָא דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לְמָר בַּר פּוֹפִידְתָּא מִפּוּמְבְּדִיתָא, וְשַׁוְּיַהּ שִׁיּוּר לְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **He subsequently retracted yet again and said: Windows are also not necessary. As, that storehouse of straw which belonged to Mar bar Pofidata from Pumbedita was designated as the section excluded from the *eiruv* arranged for the city of Pumbedita,** which proves that it is not necessary for the excluded section to be one that could have been included in an *eiruv* with the rest of the city.

However, Abaye immediately challenged this analogy as well: *hadar amar lo dami* — upon reflection, it is not comparable. The Mechuza neighborhoods were separated by ditches, but there was rooftop overhang connecting them — *ibai lei eruvei derech gagot* — meaning one could theoretically have made a unified *eruv* by routing it across the rooftops. The ditches did not represent a complete and total impossibility of inclusion; the rooftops provided an alternative avenue. Consequently, each neighborhood retained theoretical includability, and therefore qualified as a *shiyur*. The river houses, with no window and no rooftop connection, were simply outside the system entirely.

At this point Abaye concluded that the windows would after all have to be constructed. But then he recalled yet another precedent: *de-hahu beit tivna de-hava lei le-Mar bar Papidutsa bi-Pumbedita ve-shavyei shiyura li-Pumbedita* — there was a grain storage facility belonging to Mar bar Papidutsa in Pumbedita that was designated as the *shiyur* for the entire city's *eruv*. A grain storage facility is not a residence; it is not the kind of dwelling that could ordinarily be part of a residential *eruv*. Yet it was accepted as a valid *shiyur*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר, הַיְינוּ דְּאָמַר לִי מָר: ״חֲזִי דְּלָא מְצַוְוחַתְּ עֲלַהּ בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא״.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to himself: This is what the Master meant when he said to me: See to it that there is no outcry against it in the study hall.** Abaye now understood the many factors that had to be considered and how wary one must be of reaching a hasty conclusion.

Tosafot and other *rishonim* address the obvious difficulties: one would need to posit that there was some small domicile attached to the structure (as is required for bridges and graveyards in analogous cases), and regarding the minimum shiur requirement — which Rabbi Shimon sets at three *chatzerot* of two houses each — one would have to say either that the grain facility was one of the three, or that we follow the opinion (to be discussed momentarily) that one house in one courtyard suffices. In any case, the precedent established that a section not naturally part of the residential *eruv* enterprise could nonetheless constitute a valid *shiyur*. Abaye concluded that the grain storage case resolved his problem: the river houses could serve as the *shiyur* for Kakunai without any windows being required. Rav Yosef's instruction — that they should not be yelling about this in the *beis medrash* — was fulfilled: the *eruv* was valid, the excluded section was legitimate, and no resident was burdened unnecessarily.

## Shiur Amount

### Chadashah Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן עָשָׂה חוּצָה לָהּ כָּעִיר חֲדָשָׁה. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: עִיר אַחַת הָיְתָה בִּיהוּדָה וַחֲדָשָׁה שְׁמָהּ, וְהָיוּ בָּהּ חֲמִשִּׁים דָּיוֹרִים אֲנָשִׁים וְנָשִׁים וָטַף, וּבָהּ הָיוּ מְשַׁעֲרִים חֲכָמִים, וְהִיא הָיְתָה שִׁיּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **There was a certain city in Judea and its name was Ḥadasha, and it had fifty residents including men, women, and children. And the Sages would use it to measure the size of the section that must be excluded from an *eiruv*, and it itself was the excluded section of the *eiruv* of a larger city that was adjacent to it.**

The Gemara now cites a *baraita* establishing the required size of the *shiyur*. Rabbi Yehuda states: *ir achat hayetah bi-Yehuda ve-Chadashah shemah* — there was a city in Judea named Chadashah (this city is explicitly mentioned in Sefer Yehoshua in the inheritance of Judah, as Tosafot notes). *Ve-hayu vo nun diyorin anashim ve-nashim ve-taf* — it had fifty residents, counting men, women, and children together (unlike the count of 600,000, which tallies only adult males). *U-vah hayu misha'arim chachamim* — this city was the model the Rabbis used to define the minimum size of the section that must be left off from the *eruv* of an *ir shel rabim*.

### Chadashah Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: חֲדָשָׁה מַהוּ? חֲדָשָׁה, כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִיהִי הָוְיָא שִׁיּוּר לִגְדוֹלָה — גְּדוֹלָה נָמֵי הָוְיָא שִׁיּוּר לִקְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before the Sages: As for Ḥadasha, what is the *halakha*?** Is it permissible to establish an *eiruv* for Ḥadasha itself without excluding a section of the city from the *eiruv*? **With regard to Ḥadasha, just as it was the excluded section of the larger city, the larger city was also the excluded section of the smaller city.**

This immediately raises a striking question: *Chadashah mahu*? What would the city of Chadashah itself do if it needed to make an *eruv* as an *ir shel rabim*? If fifty people is the minimum *shiyur*, and Chadashah has exactly fifty, there is no one left to designate as the excluded portion. One might note in passing that this *baraita* itself is quite difficult to reconcile with Rashi's approach equating *ir shel rabim* with the 600,000 criterion — Chadashah has fifty residents and the Gemara straightforwardly treats the question of its *eruv* as an *ir shel rabim* concern.

The Gemara first assumes that the question concerns not the city of Chadashah as a standalone entity, but a fifty-person neighborhood within a larger city. In that case, the answer is elegant: *Chadashah ke-heichi d-havei shiur le-gedolah, gedolah nami havei shiur le-ktanah* — the same way the fifty-person section is the *shiyur* for the large city, the large city is the *shiyur* for the fifty-person section. Each serves as the other's excluded portion. No unified *eruv* covers everything; each makes its own *eruv* while excluding the other.

### Reciprocal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא כְּעֵין חֲדָשָׁה, מַהוּ? רַב הוּנָא וְרַב יְהוּדָה, חַד אָמַר: בָּעֲיָא שִׁיּוּר, וְחַד אָמַר: לָא בָּעֲיָא שִׁיּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, the question pertains to a small city like Ḥadasha that stands by itself, not in proximity to a larger city: What is the *halakha*?** Does a small city require an excluded section or not? **Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda disagreed about this issue. One said: It requires an excluded section; and one said: It does not require an excluded section.**

The Gemara then sharpens the question: *ke-ein Chadashah mahu* — what about a city that is itself like Chadashah, meaning a standalone city of only fifty people? If it must leave off fifty people as its *shiyur*, there is nobody remaining. A city of fifty-one could theoretically have one person left off while fifty make their *eruv* — but even that would require the *shiyur* to be fifty, leaving the excluded party alone and unable to have their own meaningful *eruv*. The question sharpens further as the numbers get tighter.

Rav Huna and Rav Yehuda disagree: one holds that even such a small city requires a *shiyur* in the technical sense, meaning the *eruv* simply cannot be made in full if there is no viable portion to leave off; the other holds that in such a case, no formal *shiyur* is required, presumably because a city so small does not truly carry the character of an *ir shel rabim* to the same degree.

### R. Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר שָׁלֹשׁ חֲצֵירוֹת וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר גּוּרְיָא אָמַר רַב: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. רַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ בַּיִת אֶחָד וְחָצֵר אַחַת. חָצֵר אַחַת סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךְ?! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: בַּיִת אֶחָד בְּחָצֵר אַחַת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says:** The excluded area must be large enough to include at least **three courtyards with two houses each. Rav Ḥama bar Gurya said that Rav said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon.** However, **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Even one house and one courtyard suffice.** The Gemara expresses surprise at the wording of this statement: **Can it enter your mind that one courtyard even without a house is sufficient? Rather,** correct it and **say as follows: One house in one courtyard.**

Rabbi Shimon disagrees with the premise that fifty people must be left off. In his view, the required *shiyur* is far more modest: *gimmel chatzerot shel shnei batim* — three courtyards, each containing two houses. Rav Chama bar Gurya rules in the name of Rav that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Shimon.

### R. Yitzchak

Rav Yitzchak goes further still: *afilu bayit echad ve-chatzer achat* — even one house within one courtyard suffices as the *shiyur*. The Gemara initially reads this as a courtyard without any house, which would make no sense, since a courtyard without a residential structure has no halachic significance in this context. The Gemara therefore corrects the reading to *bayit echad be-chatzer achat* — one house situated within one courtyard.

### Gemara or Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הָא דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק, גְּמָרָא אוֹ סְבָרָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי נָפְקָא לַן מִינַּהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: גְּמָרָא גְּמוֹר, זְמוֹרְתָּא תְּהֵא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: Is that ruling of Rabbi Yitzḥak based on oral tradition or his own logic?** Rav Yosef **said to him: What** practical **difference does Rabbi Yitzḥak's source make to us?** Abaye **said to him,** quoting a well-known adage: **When you study Talmud is it merely a song?;** Is the material you study like the lyrics of a song that you do not understand? It is proper to investigate all aspects of the statements of the Sages, regardless of the practical ramifications.

Abaye asks Rav Yosef: *ha de-Rav Yitzchak, gemara o sevara* — is Rav Yitzchak's position a received tradition, or did he derive it through independent reasoning? Rav Yosef replies: *mai nafka minah* — what practical difference does it make? Abaye's famous rejoinder: *gemara gmur zmoreta hi* — should we just learn and chant our rulings like a song? The question of whether a ruling derives from tradition or reasoning matters enormously: a position arrived at by independent *sevara* is more open to challenge and debate, while a transmitted tradition carries greater authority.

Tosafot engages with the question of whether Abaye was actually challenging the *halacha* — which would be difficult, since Rav Yitzchak is an *amora* arguing on a *tanna* (Rabbi Shimon), which requires either a tradition or extraordinary reasoning — or merely raising a theoretical point. Tosafot suggests the exchange was more theoretical, while the *Ramo* maintains that even in theoretical terms the distinction between *gemara* and *sevara* has importance. Some *rishonim* do in fact incline toward accepting Rav Yitzchak's position *le-ma'aseh*.

## Eruv Techumim

### Two Scenarios

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁהָיָה בַּמִּזְרָח וְאָמַר לִבְנוֹ: ״עָרֵב לִי בַּמַּעֲרָב״, בַּמַּעֲרָב וַאֲמַר לִבְנוֹ: ״עָרֵב לִי בַּמִּזְרָח״, אִם יֵשׁ הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְבֵיתוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, וּלְעֵירוּבוֹ יוֹתֵר מִכָּאן — מוּתָּר לְבֵיתוֹ, וְאָסוּר לְעֵירוּבוֹ. לְעֵירוּבוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה, וּלְבֵיתוֹ יָתֵר מִכָּאן — אָסוּר לְבֵיתוֹ, וּמוּתָּר לְעֵירוּבוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who was to the east of his home when Shabbat began, and he had said to his son before Shabbat: Establish an *eiruv* for me to the west; or, if he was to the west of his home and he had said to his son: Establish an *eiruv* for me to the east,** the *halakha* is as follows: **If there is a distance of two thousand cubits from his current location to his house, and the distance to his *eiruv* is greater than this, he is permitted to walk to his house, but it is prohibited for him to walk to the spot where his son had deposited his *eiruv*. If the distance from one's current location to his *eiruv* is two thousand cubits, and the distance to his house is greater than this, he is prohibited from walking to his house, and he is permitted to walk to the spot of his *eiruv*.**

The *mishna* now returns to the topic of *eruv techumim*, after the preceding discussion of *ir shel rabim* and *eruv chatzerot* which had been interpolated within this *perek* — itself a somewhat puzzling arrangement that invites further study. The *mishna* presents two scenarios. In the first: a person is in the east, sends his son to place an *eruv* in the west, and the result is that from him to his house is 2,000 *amot*, but to his *eruv* it is more than that. The ruling is *mutar le-beiso ve-asur le-eiruvo* — he may walk to his house but not to his *eruv*. The *eruv* is ineffective because at the moment *Shabbos* begins, he is more than 2,000 *amot* away from it, making him *chutz la-eruv* relative to the *eruv* itself. Since the *eruv* cannot take effect — one explanation being that a valid *eruv* requires the ability to access it at the onset of *Shabbos* — he reverts to his natural residence as his starting point. Rashi and Tosafot note that this is preferable to certain other failure modes: when an *eruv* fails under some conditions, the person is left with only four *amot*; here, however, since the *eruv* is simply null, his home serves as his default, and he retains the full range of movement centered on his house.

In the second scenario: to his *eruv* is 2,000 *amot*, but to his house it is more. In this case, *asur le-beiso u-mutar le-eiruvo* — he cannot reach his house, but his *eruv* is effective and he may travel to it.

### Relative to Son

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ: ״לַמִּזְרָח״, לְמִזְרַח בֵּיתוֹ. ״לַמַּעֲרָב״, לְמַעֲרַב בֵּיתוֹ. בִּשְׁלָמָא ״הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְבֵיתוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה וּלְעֵירוּבוֹ יָתֵר מִכָּאן״ — מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ, דְּמָטֵי לְבֵיתֵיהּ וְלָא מָטֵי לְעֵירוּבוֹ, אֶלָּא ״הֵימֶנּוּ וּלְעֵירוּבוֹ אַלְפַּיִם אַמָּה וּלְבֵיתוֹ יָתֵר מִכָּאן״ — הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It might enter your mind to say that when the mishna states that one was standing to the east, it means that he was standing to the east of his house and that he had instructed his son to establish an *eiruv* to the west of his house.** Similarly, when it states that he was standing to the west, it means that he was positioned to the west of his house and that he had instructed his son to establish an *eiruv* to the east of his house. In such a case, the person's house is located between him and his *eiruv*. **If so, the question arises: Granted, the mishna's case where there is a distance of two thousand cubits from his current location to his house, and the distance to his *eiruv* is greater than this, you can find, as it is possible that he can reach his house without traveling two thousand cubits and he cannot reach his *eiruv*. But where do you find a case where there is a distance of two thousand cubits between him and his *eiruv*, and the distance to his house is greater than this?** The person's house is located between him and his *eiruv*.

The second scenario raises an immediate geometric puzzle. If the person is in the east, his house is to the west, and he placed his *eruv* even further west, how can he be closer to the *eruv* than to the house? On a straight east-west line, the house must always be closer to him if it lies between him and the *eruv*. The Gemara resolves this by reinterpreting the directional terms: *lemizrach lemizrach beno, lemaarav lemaarav beno* — "east" and "west" in the *mishna* refer to the son's position, not the house's. The person was somewhere, and he told his son to place an *eruv* "to the west" — meaning west of where the son was standing. The *eruv* thus ends up in a location that happens to be closer to the father than the house is, because the geometry is defined by the son's position.

### Diagonal Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא בַּר רַב שֵׁילָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא ״לַמִּזְרָח״ — לְמִזְרַח בֵּיתוֹ, וְ״לַמַּעֲרָב״ — לְמַעֲרַב בֵּיתוֹ, כְּגוֹן דְּקָאֵי בֵּיתֵיהּ בַּאֲלַכְסוֹנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava bar Rav Sheila said: Even if you say that to the east means to the east of his house and to the west means to the west of his house,** the mishna can be understood as referring to **a case where his house stood along a diagonal line in relation to the person and his *eiruv*.** In that case, although he is to the west of his house and the *eiruv* is located to its east, he can still be closer to his *eiruv* than he is to his house.

Rava bar Rav Shila offers a more elegant geometric solution. *Afilu seima lemizrach lemizrach beiso lemaarav lemaarav beiso* — the directional terms may indeed refer to the house. The *eruv* is placed to the west of the house, and the person is to the east. What makes the *eruv* closer than the house? *De-kai beiso be-alkhsona* — the house is positioned diagonally. The person and the *eruv* lie on a straight east-west line, but the house is far off to the side, at an angle. In that geometry, the straight-line distance from the person to the *eruv* is 2,000 *amot*, while the diagonal distance from the person to the house, which lies far to the north or south, exceeds 2,000 *amot*. This is an entirely coherent reading of the *mishna* that requires no reinterpretation of terms.

### Within Extension

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹתֵן אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ בְּתוֹךְ עִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר, לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **One who places his *eiruv* within the outskirts of the city,** i.e., within an area of slightly more than seventy cubits surrounding the city, **it is as though he has not done anything.** The two thousand cubits of one's Shabbat limit are measured from the edge of the outskirts of the city even if there is no *eiruv*, and one therefore gains nothing from placing an *eiruv* within this area.

The *mishna* then rules: *ha-noten et eruvo be-iburah shel ir lo asah velo klum* — if one places his *eruv* within the squared-off extension of the city (*ibbur ha-ir*), it is entirely ineffective. Since the *eruv* is within the extended city boundaries, and a person who begins *Shabbos* in the city already receives the benefit of all those boundaries plus 2,000 *amot* from the edge, placing the *eruv* within those confines gains nothing. It is already included in the city's base measurement.

### Outside Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְתָנוֹ חוּץ לַתְּחוּם, אֲפִילּוּ אַמָּה אַחַת — מַה שֶּׁנִּשְׂכַּר הוּא מַפְסִיד.
>
> **Translation:** If, however, **he placed his *eiruv* outside the city's boundary, even if he placed it only one cubit beyond the city, what he gains in distance through his *eiruv* on one side of the city he loses on the other side.**

The *mishna* continues: *nasno chutz la-tchum afilu amah achas, mah she-niskar hu mafsid* — if he placed the *eruv* even one *amah* outside the city's squared-off boundaries, whatever he gains on one side he loses on the other. If the city counts for zero (as the person's *arba amot* of residence), then one *amah* outside the city gives him 2,000 *amot* in one direction but only 1,999 in the other, for a net gain of one *amah* and a loss of one *amah*. Tosafot notes the question of whether the city counts for exactly zero or for four *amot*, and whether the position outside the city similarly counts for four *amot*, and works through the arithmetic at length — the rough principle holds either way.

## City Counts Query

### Baraita Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹתֵן אֶת עֵירוּבוֹ בְּתוֹךְ עִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר, לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם. נְתָנוֹ חוּץ לְעִיבּוּרָהּ שֶׁל עִיר אֲפִילּוּ אַמָּה אַחַת, מִשְׂתַּכֵּר אוֹתָהּ אַמָּה וּמַפְסִיד אֶת כָּל הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁמִּדַּת הָעִיר עוֹלָה לוֹ בְּמִדַּת הַתְּחוּם!
>
> **Translation:** **Wasn't it taught in a *baraita*: With regard to one who places his *eiruv* within the outskirts of the city, he has not done anything; if, however, he placed it outside the outskirts of the city, even one cubit outside, he gains that cubit and loses the entire city because the measure of the city is included in the measure of his Shabbat limit?** If one's Shabbat residence had been in the city, the two thousand cubits of his Shabbat limit would have been measured from the edge of the city's outskirts; now that he has established his Shabbat residence outside the city, the city itself is included in the two thousand cubits, and he may lose far more on that side than he will gain on the other side.

The *mishna*'s formulation — that placing the *eruv* one *amah* outside the city causes only a one-*amah* net loss — assumes that the city itself counts for zero against the *techum*. But a *baraita* teaches something strikingly different: *hanoten et eruvo betoch ibburah shel ir lo asah velo klum; nasno chutz le-ibbura shel ir, afilu amah achas, mistaker osa ama u-mavsid et kol ha-ir kulah, mipnei she-midas ha-ir olah lo be-midas ha-techum* — if the *eruv* is placed even one *amah* outside the city, one gains that one *amah* but loses the entire city, because the city now counts against one's *techum*. This is a far more severe ruling than the *mishna*, which implies only a one-*amah* exchange.

### Midway vs. Complete

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן שֶׁכָּלְתָה מִדָּתוֹ בַּחֲצִי הָעִיר, כָּאן שֶׁכָּלְתָה מִדָּתוֹ בְּסוֹף הָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here the *baraita* is referring to a case where his measure of two thousand cubits terminated in the middle of the city; whereas there the mishna is referring to a case where his measure terminated at the far end of the city.**

The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, kan she-kaltah midato ba-chatzi ha-ir, kan she-kaltah midato be-sof ha-ir* — the two rulings address different situations. If one's 2,000-*amah* measurement ends in the middle of the city — meaning the city is large enough that 2,000 *amot* from one's position does not carry one to the far side — then the city counts against the *techum*, and the loss is the entire city. If, however, the 2,000 *amot* carries one completely through the city to its far end, then the city is entirely contained within the *techum* and counts as nothing — only four *amot* — and one gains 2,000 *amot* in the other direction. The *mishna* speaks of the latter case (a small city fully traversable within 2,000 *amot*); the *baraita* speaks of the former.

### Rav Idi's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְרַבִּי אִידִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אִידִי אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: הָיָה מוֹדֵד וּבָא וְכָלְתָה מִדָּתוֹ בַּחֲצִי הָעִיר — אֵין לוֹ אֶלָּא חֲצִי הָעִיר. כָּלְתָה מִדָּתוֹ בְּסוֹף הָעִיר — נַעֲשֵׂית לוֹ הָעִיר כּוּלָּהּ כְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וּמַשְׁלִימִין לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁאָר.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is in accordance with the opinion stated by Rabbi Idi, as Rabbi Idi said that Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said:** If one **was measuring the two thousand cubits of his Shabbat limit from the location of his Shabbat residence outside the city, and his measure terminated in the middle of the city, he has only half the city,** i.e., he may walk only to the end of his two thousand cubits. If, however, **his measure terminated at the far end of the city, the entire city is regarded as four cubits, and he completes the rest of the Shabbat limit on the other side of the city.**

This distinction is attributed to Rav Idi, who cites it in the name of Rav Yehoshua ben Levi. Rav Idi formulates it as follows: if one is measuring off one's *techum* and finds oneself approaching a city, and *kaltah midato be-chatzi ha-ir* — the 2,000 *amot* runs out in the middle of the city — *ein lo ela chatzi ha-ir*, one receives only that half of the city within the 2,000 *amot* and no more. But *kaltah midato be-sof ha-ir* — if the 2,000 *amot* reaches the far end — *na'asit lo ha-ir kulah ke-arba amot u-mashlimin lo et ha-shar*, the entire city is treated as merely four *amot*, and one receives the balance of the *techum* beyond the city.

### Prophecy Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִידִי: אֵין אֵלּוּ אֶלָּא דִּבְרֵי נְבִיאוּת. מָה לִי כָּלְתָה בַּחֲצִי הָעִיר, מָה לִי כָּלְתָה בְּסוֹף הָעִיר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Idi said: These are nothing more than words of prophecy,** i.e., I do not see the logic behind this statement. **What difference is it to me if the measure terminated in the middle of the city, or if it terminated at the far end of the city?**

Rav Idi himself responds to his own ruling with a striking comment: *ein elu ela divrei nevi'os* — this is nothing but words of prophecy. *Mah li kaltah ba-chatzyah mah li kaltah be-sofah* — what difference does it make whether the 2,000 *amot* ends in the middle or at the end of the city? Either the city counts or it doesn't; why should a distinction that hinges on whether one's measurement ends three steps earlier or later produce such dramatically different outcomes?

Rashi interprets *divrei nevi'os* as a sharp criticism — the phrase is equivalent to saying the ruling has no rational basis, that it is being pronounced ex cathedra without any logical foundation, like a prophet who speaks without offering reasons. In *Torah she-be'al peh*, such arbitrary-seeming distinctions carry no weight unless backed by tradition. Rashi concludes that Rav Yehoshua ben Levi must have had a received tradition for this ruling, because on purely logical grounds he never would have articulated so counterintuitive a distinction.

Tosafot takes a diametrically opposite reading: *divrei nevi'os* here is a compliment, an expression of awe — as if to say, this is such a precise and illuminating insight that it is almost prophetic in quality. The debate between Rashi and Tosafot here is thus about the valence of the phrase, with Rashi reading it as rebuke and Tosafot as admiration. Both agree that the substantive distinction itself is non-obvious; they differ only on whether Rav Idi was criticizing or praising it.

### Rava Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, תַּרְוַיְיהוּ תְּנַנְהִי: אַנְשֵׁי עִיר גְּדוֹלָה מְהַלְּכִין אֶת כׇּל עִיר קְטַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** They are not words of prophecy, as **both cases were taught in the following mishna: The residents of a large city may walk through an entire small city** that is fully included within its Shabbat limit; the small city is considered as though it were four cubits, and the rest of the Shabbat limit is measured from the other side of the city.

Rava responds to Rav Idi's challenge — the claim that the distinction is arbitrary — by arguing that in fact both positions are inferable from *mishnayos*, not merely from Rav Yehoshua ben Levi's independent ruling. *Atu Braisa tanya?* — as if this is only from a *baraita*? It follows from our own *mishnayos*. Rava thus defends the distinction not as prophetic intuition but as grounded in standard *mishnaic* reasoning.

### Large/Small Cities

The *mishna* in question states: *anshei ir gedolah mahalchin et kol ir ketanah, ve-ein anshei ir ketanah mahalchin et kol ir gedolah* — the people of a large city can traverse the entire small city adjacent to it, but the people of the small city cannot traverse the entire large city. Rava argues that this *mishna* implicitly encodes exactly the distinction Rav Idi articulated. The people of the large city begin in their city — which, being large, encompasses their full *techum* and more — and when they reach the small adjacent city, their 2,000 *amot* carries them through the entire small city. Since the small city is fully within their *techum*, it counts as nothing and they receive 2,000 *amot* beyond it. The people of the small city, by contrast, begin in their city and their 2,000 *amot* runs out somewhere in the middle of the large city — they do not get through it, so it counts against them and they are stuck. This is precisely the midway-versus-complete distinction.

### Alternative Reading

However, this inference from the *mishna* is not accepted by all. An alternative reading of the *mishna* about large and small cities is proposed — that it is not addressing measurement endpoints at all, but rather the scenario of *nesuyin*, where a person wishes to affiliate his *Shabbos* residence with the other city by placing his *eruv techumim* there. Under this reading, the *mishna* is saying that a resident of either city may choose to place his *eruv* in the neighboring city and thereby walk throughout that city on *Shabbos*. The asymmetry between large and small cities would then reflect different practical constraints of that scenario, not anything about where one's 2,000-*amah* measurement ends. Under this alternative reading, the *mishna* is entirely irrelevant to the midway-versus-complete distinction, and Rava's proof collapses.
