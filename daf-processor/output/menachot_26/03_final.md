# Menachot 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Remnants & Kometz (III)

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִטְמְאוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ, נִשְׂרְפוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ, אָבְדוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ – כְּמִדַּת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּשֵׁירָה, וּכְמִדַּת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If after the handful was removed **the remainder of** the meal offering **became ritually impure,** or if **the remainder of** the meal offering **was burned,** or if **the remainder of** the meal offering **was lost, according to the principle of Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that with regard to an animal offering the blood is fit for sprinkling even if there is no meat that can be eaten, the meal offering is **fit,** and the priest burns the handful. **But according to the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua,** who says that with regard to an animal offering the blood is fit for sprinkling only if there is meat that can be eaten, it is **unfit** and the priest does not burn the handful, as the handful serves to render permitted the remainder.

The *mishna* opens with a case involving the *shirayim* — the remnant of the *mincha* left over after the *kometz* has been taken. Three scenarios are presented in which the *shirayim* are lost to the *kohein*: *nitme'u shi'areiha*, the remnant became *tamei*; *nisrefu shi'areiha*, it was burned; *avdu shi'areiha*, it was lost. The question is whether, in any of these circumstances, the *kometz* may still be brought and the *mincha* remain valid. The *mishna* records a dispute: *k'midat Rabbi Eliezer k'sheira* — according to Rabbi Eliezer's view, it is valid — while *u'k'midat Rabbi Yehoshua psulah* — according to Rabbi Yehoshua's view, it is invalid.

### R. Eliezer vs. R. Yehosh…

This dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua is known from elsewhere in *Shas*. Their debate concerns whether one may perform *zerikat hadam* — the sprinkling of the blood — if nothing remains of the *korban* aside from the blood itself. Does there need to be some fat to burn on the *mizbe'ach*, or some meat either to burn (in the case of an *olah*) or to eat (in the case of other *korbanot*)? Rabbi Eliezer holds that even if all one has is the blood, the *zerikat hadam* may be performed and the *korban* is valid. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the blood functions as a *matir* — it enables the next stage, whether eating or burning — and if there is nothing left for it to permit, it cannot serve that function. The same logic applies to the *kometz* of a *mincha*: does the *kometz* require *shirayim* to exist in order to be valid?

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב: וְהוּ שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ כׇּל שְׁיָרֶיהָ, אֲבָל מִקְצָת שְׁיָרֶיהָ – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the mishna's statement that according to Rabbi Yehoshua the meal offering is unfit if its remainder is rendered impure, **Rav says: And this** is the *halakha* only **when all of its remainder became impure. But** if only **a part of its remainder** became impure, the meal offering is **not** unfit.

The *gemara* opens with a statement of Rav: *v'hu shenitme'u kol shi'areiha* — when the *mishna* identifies *tum'ah* as a problem according to Rabbi Yehoshua, it refers specifically to a case where *all* of the *shirayim* became *tamei*. If only a minority of the *shirayim* became *tamei*, Rabbi Yehoshua would concede that the *kometz* may be brought, since something remains.

### Tumah & Tzitz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ: נִטְמָא – אִין, אָבוּד וְשָׂרוּף – לָא. מַאי קָסָבַר? אִי קָסָבַר: שִׁיּוּרָא מִילְּתָא הִיא – אֲפִילּוּ אָבוּד וְשָׂרוּף נָמֵי! אִי קָסָבַר: שִׁיּוּרָא לָאו מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְנִטְמָא מַאי טַעְמָא – דִּמְרַצֵּה צִיץ? אִי הָכִי, כׇּל שְׁיָרֶיהָ נָמֵי! לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר: שִׁיּוּרָא מִילְּתָא הִיא, וְנִטְמָא – וְהוּ הַדִּין לְאָבוּד וְשָׂרוּף, וְהַאי דְּקָאָמַר ״נִטְמָא״ – רֵישַׁיְיהוּ נָקֵט.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **enters your mind** that Rav holds that only if a part of the remainder **became impure,** then **yes,** the meal offering is fit; but if part of the remainder **was lost or burned,** then the meal offering is **not** fit. The Gemara asks: **What does** Rav **hold? If he holds** that **what remains is significant,** so that even if a portion of the remainder cannot be eaten the handful is still sacrificed to render the rest permitted, then why would this not also be the *halakha* **even** if part of the remainder **was lost or burned?** Alternatively, **if he holds** that **what remains is not significant,** and the Gemara interjects: **And** accordingly, **what is the reason** that the handful is sacrificed if a part of the remainder **became impure?** It is **because** the **frontplate effects acceptance** for the impurity; **if that is so,** then **even** if **all of the remainder** became impure, the handful should still be sacrificed. The Gemara explains: **Actually, he holds** that **what remains is significant, and** just as when a part of the remainder **became impure** but the offering is still fit, the rest of the remainder is sacrificed, **the same is true** with regard **to** a case where a part of the remainder **was lost or burned. And** the reason **that he stated** this *halakha* specifically in a case where it **became impure** is that **he employed** the terminology of **the beginning of** the mishna, which discusses a case where the remainder became impure.

The *gemara*, however, focuses on Rav's specific invocation of the case of *tum'ah* and asks: *mai kasavar* — what precisely is his reasoning? *Ka salka daitach* — one might have thought — that Rav singled out *tum'ah* because it enjoys a special status by virtue of the *tzitz*. The *tzitz*, the golden frontplate worn by the *Kohen Gadol*, has the power of *ritzui*, rendering certain otherwise-problematic *korbanot* acceptable. A question debated in the previous *mishna* was whether the *tzitz* is *meratzeh al ha'ochlin* — whether its acceptance extends not only to things burned on the *mizbe'ach* but also to the edible portions of a *korban*. Crucially, this does not mean the *tzitz* permits eating *tamei* meat; rather, the question is whether, *vis-à-vis* establishing the validity of the *korban*, the *tzitz* can bracket the problem posed by the *tum'ah* of the edible portions. If it can, then even when all the *shirayim* are *tamei*, the *tzitz* would treat them as if they were present and valid, and Rabbi Yehoshua would concede.

With this in mind, the *gemara* presses: *mai kasavar* — what does Rav actually hold? If he holds *shiura milta hi* — that even a small remnant is sufficient — then why does he single out *tum'ah*? Even if most of the *shirayim* were lost or burned, as long as a little remains it should suffice. If, on the other hand, he holds *shiura lav milta hi* — that a small remnant is not significant and all of the *shirayim* must remain — then why does *tum'ah* not constitute a problem? Is it because the *tzitz* renders it as if the *shirayim* still exist?

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *le'olam kasavar shiura milta hi* — Rav's actual position is simply that a small remnant suffices. His reference to *tum'ah* carries no special significance; he mentioned *tum'ah* only because the *mishna* itself opens with the case of *tum'ah* (*reisha hu nakat*), not because *tum'ah* enjoys any distinct legal status in this context.

## Tzitz Validation

### Tzitz Scope

The *gemara*'s discussion of the *tzitz* bears clarification. When it asks whether the *tzitz* works *al ha'ochlin*, it does not mean that *tum'ah* is lifted such that the *tamei* meat could be eaten. The *tzitz* operates exclusively in the domain of making the *korban* valid — it does not override prohibitions of eating in a state of *tum'ah*. The question is purely functional: can the *tzitz*'s power of *ritzui* stand in, *vis-à-vis* the validity of the *korban*, for edible portions that have become *tamei*? If yes, Rabbi Yehoshua could agree that the *korban* is valid even when the only remaining *shirayim* are *tamei*, because the *tzitz* treats those portions as though they are halachically present.

A related question, noted but not yet fully resolved in the tractate, is whether the *ritzui* of the *tzitz* requires the *Kohen Gadol* to actually be wearing it at the moment, or whether the mere existence of the *tzitz* — so long as it has not been broken or lost — is sufficient. That debate remains open.

## Remnants & Kometz (III)

### Shiura Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּדְתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁבְּתוֹרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּיֵּיר מֵהֶן כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר אוֹ כְּזַיִת חֵלֶב – זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם. כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בָּשָׂר וְכַחֲצִי זַיִת חֵלֶב – אֵינוֹ זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם, וּבָעוֹלָה אֲפִילּוּ כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בָּשָׂר וְכַחֲצִי זַיִת חֵלֶב – זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ כָּלִיל, וּבַמִּנְחָה אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלָּהּ קַיֶּימֶת – לֹא יִזְרוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** Rava's statement accords with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** With regard to **all the offerings in the Torah from which there remains an olive-bulk of meat** that is fit to be eaten **or an olive-bulk of fat** that is fit to be sacrificed on the altar, the priest **sprinkles the blood.** Similarly, if a part of the remainder can be eaten the handful is still sacrificed, as the status of the remainder relative to the handful corresponds to the status of the meat relative to the blood. The Gemara cites the continuation of the *baraita*: If all that remains is **half an olive-bulk of meat and half an olive-bulk of fat,** the priest **does not sprinkle the blood.** This is because the half olive-bulk of meat and the half olive-bulk of fat do not combine to form one olive-bulk, since the former is eaten and the latter is sacrificed on the altar. **And with regard to a burnt offering, even** if all that was left was **half an olive-bulk of meat and half an olive-bulk of fat,** the priest **sprinkles the blood, because it is consumed** on the altar **in its entirety.** Since both the meat and the fat are sacrificed on the altar, they combine to form one olive-bulk. **And with regard to a meal offering, although all of it remains** pure, the priest **shall not sprinkle** the blood.

The *gemara* brings a *baraita* to support the conclusion that Rabbi Yehoshua does not require all the *shirayim* to be present, but only some meaningful portion: *Rabbi Yehoshua omer kol hazvachim shebatorah shenishtayer mehen kezayit basar o kezayit chelev* — Rabbi Yehoshua says that for all animal *korbanot* in the Torah, as long as a *kezayit* of meat or a *kezayit* of fat remains, *zorek et hadam* — one may perform the *zerikat hadam*. If only half a *kezayit* of meat and half a *kezayit* of fat remain, *eino zorek et hadam* — one may not.

There is, however, an exception for the *olah*. Since the entire *olah* is burned — both the meat and the fat — a half *kezayit* of meat combined with a half *kezayit* of fat yields a full *kezayit* of something that will be burned on the *mizbe'ach*, and that suffices. By contrast, when the *korban* is not an *olah*, the fact that one has half a *kezayit* to eat and half a *kezayit* to burn does not constitute a meaningful *kezayit* of either function; one needs a full *kezayit* of one or the other. The principle applies equally to the *mincha*: Rabbi Yehoshua does not require all the *shirayim* to remain, only that some significant amount — a *kezayit* — be present.

### Minchat Nesachim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִנְחָה מַאי עֲבִידְתַּהּ? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מִנְחַת נְסָכִים, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ – הוֹאִיל וּבַהֲדֵי זֶבַח קָא אָתְיָא, כְּגוּפֵיהּ דְּזִיבְחָא דָּמְיָא, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions the last ruling of the *baraita*: **What is** the mention of **a meal offering doing** here? The discussion is about sprinkling blood, which is not relevant in the case of a meal offering. **Rav Pappa said:** The meal offering mentioned is **the meal offering** that accompanies the **libations** that accompany animal offerings. It could **enter your mind** to say: **Since** this meal offering **accompanies** the animal **offering,** it is **comparable to the offering itself,** and therefore if the offering became impure but the meal offering remained pure, the blood of the offering is sprinkled due to the remaining meal offering. To counter this, the *baraita* **teaches us** that this is not the *halakha*.

The *gemara* raises the following question: if none of the meat of the animal remains but the *minchat nesachim* — the accompanying *mincha* — is entirely intact, may one perform *zerikat hadam*? The reasoning one might advance in favor of permitting it is *ho'il uvahadei zevach ka'atya* — since the *minchat nesachim* comes together with the animal sacrifice, one might think it has the status of the animal's body itself (*kegufei dizevach dami*), and thus constitutes the requisite remnant.

Rav Papa rules that this reasoning is rejected. The *minchat nesachim* is not considered part of the body of the sacrifice itself. When the *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehoshua requires something of the *korban* to remain — specifying *chelev* — it establishes that the remnant must be an intrinsic part of the animal, not something attendant to it. The *minchat nesachim* does not meet that standard, and therefore even if it remains intact, one may not perform *zerikat hadam* if no portion of the animal itself survives.

### Kezayit Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל, וּמָטוּ בַּהּ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן חֲנַנְיָא: אָמַר קְרָא ״וְהִקְטִיר הַחֵלֶב לְרֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ לַה׳״, חֵלֶב – וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בָּשָׂר. וְאַשְׁכְּחַן חֵלֶב, יוֹתֶרֶת וּשְׁתֵּי כְּלָיוֹת מְנָלַן? דְּקָתָנֵי: וּבַמִּנְחָה, אֲפִילּוּ כּוּלָּהּ קַיֶּימֶת לֹא יִזְרוֹק. מִנְחָה הוּא דְּלֹא יִזְרוֹק, הָא יוֹתֶרֶת וּשְׁתֵּי כְּלָיוֹת – יִזְרוֹק. מְנָלַן? רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״ – כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה מַעֲלֶה לְרֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ. וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִכְתַּב ״חֵלֶב״, וְאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְמִיכְתַּב ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״, דְּאִי כְּתַב ״חֵלֶב״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: חֵלֶב – אִין, יוֹתֶרֶת וּשְׁתֵּי כְּלָיוֹת – לָא, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״. וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״לְרֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ״ הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: אֲפִילּוּ מִנְחָה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״חֵלֶב״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to its discussion of the *halakha* that if only an olive-bulk of the fat remains, the priest sprinkles the blood of the offering. **From where is this matter** derived? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael, and** there are those who **determined** that **it** was stated **in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Ḥananya: The verse states:** "And the priest shall sprinkle the blood against the altar of the Lord at the door of the Tent of Meeting, **and he shall make the fat smoke for a pleasing aroma to the Lord"** (Leviticus 17:6). This verse never mentions the meat, but only the **fat,** indicating that the blood is sprinkled **even if there is no** ritually pure **meat,** but only fat. The Gemara asks: **And we found** a source for the *halakha* that the priest sprinkles the blood if only **fat** remains. **From where do we** derive that the priest sprinkles the blood if all that is left is the **lobe** of the liver **or** the **two kidneys,** which are also sacrificed on the altar? The Gemara answers: The *halakha* that the priest sprinkles the blood in that case is derived from **that** which **is taught** at the end of the *baraita*: **And with regard to a meal offering, although all of it remains** pure, the priest **shall not sprinkle** the blood. This teaches that **it is** in the case of **a meal offering that** the priest **shall not sprinkle** the blood, as the meal offering is not part of the animal; **but** if the **lobe** of the liver **or** the **two kidneys** remain, the priest **sprinkles** the blood. The Gemara asks: **From where do we** derive this *halakha*? The Gemara answers that **Rabbi Yoḥanan himself says:** The verse states: **"For a pleasing aroma** to the Lord" (Leviticus 17:6). This teaches that the blood is sprinkled whenever **anything that you offer up** on the altar **for a pleasing aroma** remains. This includes anything burned on the altar. The Gemara notes: **And** it **was necessary to write "fat"** in that verse, **and** it **was necessary to write "for a pleasing aroma." As, if** the Merciful One **had written** only **"fat," I would say** that if **fat** remains, **yes,** the priest sprinkles the blood, but if only the **lobe** of the liver **or** the **two kidneys** remain, since they are not as significant as the fat, the blood is **not** sprinkled. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "for a pleasing aroma." And if the Merciful One had written** only **"for a pleasing aroma," I would say** that it includes **even a meal offering** brought with the libations that accompany animal offerings. Therefore, **the Merciful One wrote "fat,"** to teach that this *halakha* applies only to sacrificial parts of the animal, but not to accompanying libations and meal offerings.

A *baraita* confirms that for Rabbi Yehoshua, what is needed is that a *kezayit* of the animal itself — either of the parts to be burned or the parts to be eaten — be present at the time of *zerikat hadam*. The *baraita* also introduces the question of the *minchat nesachim* — the *mincha* that accompanies the wine libation brought together with an animal sacrifice.

## Tzitz Validation (II)

### Tzitz Wearing

The question of whether the *tzitz* requires active wearing by the *Kohen Gadol* or whether its mere presence suffices is a debate that surfaces in the *gemara*, though it has not yet been explicitly encountered in the course of this tractate's discussion. Under normal circumstances, the *Kohen Gadol* wore the *tzitz* as part of his standard priestly vestments whenever he performed the *avodah*. The practical upshot of the *tzitz*'s *ritzui* is not to permit eating *tamei* portions *lechatchilah*, but rather that *bedi'avad*, if a *korban* was offered under such circumstances, it is considered accepted. This is the framework within which Rabbi Eliezer's position operates as well — he holds that even if the entire *shirayim* are gone, the *korban* is valid — though the *gemara*'s analysis of *tum'ah* and the *tzitz* was directed primarily at clarifying Rabbi Yehoshua's more limited concession.

### Kezayit Standard

A *baraita* confirms that for Rabbi Yehoshua, what is needed is that a *kezayit* of the animal itself — either of the parts to be burned or the parts to be eaten — be present at the time of *zerikat hadam*. The *baraita* also introduces the question of the *minchat nesachim* — the *mincha* that accompanies the wine libation brought together with an animal sacrifice.

## Minchat Nesachim

### Blood with Mincha

The *gemara* raises the following question: if none of the meat of the animal remains but the *minchat nesachim* — the accompanying *mincha* — is entirely intact, may one perform *zericat hadam*? The reasoning one might advance in favor of permitting it is *ho'il uvahadei zevach ka'atya* — since the *minchat nesachim* comes together with the animal sacrifice, one might think it has the status of the animal's body itself (*kegufei dizevach dami*), and thus constitutes the requisite remnant.

### Rav Papa's View

Rav Papa rules that this reasoning is rejected. The *minchat nesachim* is not considered part of the body of the sacrifice itself. When the *baraita* teaches that Rabbi Yehoshua requires something of the *korban* to remain — specifying *chelev* — it establishes that the remnant must be an intrinsic part of the animal, not something attendant to it. The *minchat nesachim* does not meet that standard, and therefore even if it remains intact, one may not perform *zericat hadam* if no portion of the animal itself survives.

## Remnant Amount

### Kezayit Source

The *gemara* then asks: *minayin* — what is the source? Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Yishmael (and some versions attribute it to Rabbi Yehoshua ben Chananya), derives the answer from the *pasuk*: *vehiktir hachelev lerei'ach nichoach l'Hashem* — "and he shall burn the fat as a pleasing scent to God." The surrounding verse reads: *ve-zarak hakohein et hadam al mizbe'ach Hashem* — "the *kohein* shall sprinkle the blood" — followed immediately by the burning of the fat. The *gemara* reads this as establishing a connection: the *zericat hadam* is linked to the subsequent burning of the *chelev*. From this we learn that *chelev af al pi she-ein basar* — even if all that remains is the fat (and no meat), one may perform the *zericat hadam*. And once we accept that not everything must remain — that even a single component suffices — the broader principle follows: one needs a meaningful amount, a *kezayit*, of something that will be burned or eaten, but not the entirety of the *korban*.

This is precisely the point of disagreement between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Eliezer holds that the *zericat hadam* is itself the essential act of the *korban*, and it stands on its own. Rabbi Yehoshua holds that the blood functions as a *matir* — it must be enabling something at the next stage — and if there is nothing left to enable, the *dam* cannot serve its proper function. The *matir* must have something to permit. Crucially, what it permits must be something of the animal itself, not something that merely accompanies it.

### Organs Suffice

The *gemara* then asks: *ve'ashkechan chelev* — we have established the rule from the *pasuk* for the case of fat. But what of the *yoteret u-shtei klayot* — the lobe of the liver and the two kidneys? These too are placed on the *mizbe'ach* together with the *chelev*, but they are organs, not fat in the strict sense. Does their presence suffice to permit *zericat hadam* even if no *chelev* remains?

The answer is derived from the *baraita* itself: since Rabbi Yehoshua rules that even when the entire *mincha* of the accompanying libation is intact, *lo yizrok* — one may not sprinkle the blood — the *baraita* contrasts this with a case where what remains is the *yoteret ushtei klayot*, and rules *yizrok* — one may sprinkle. This establishes that Rabbi Yehoshua accepts any part of the animal's body that is designated for the *mizbe'ach* as the requisite remnant, not only the *chelev* in the narrow sense.

### Torah Redundancy

Rabbi Yochanan in his own name then explains the *pasuk*-level derivation. The *pasuk* says *lerei'ach nichoach* — a pleasing scent — which is a broad category encompassing anything burned as part of the *korban*. *Kol she'ata ma'aleh lerei'ach nichoach* — anything that you burn as a pleasing scent — would suffice to permit *zericat hadam*. This would seem to include even the *minchat nesachim*.

Why, then, does the Torah write *chelev* — fat — at all? *De'i ktav chelev hava amina chelev in yoteret ushtei klayot lo* — had the Torah written only *chelev*, one might have thought that only the fat counts, and that the lobe and kidneys do not. Therefore the Torah writes *lerei'ach nichoach* to broaden the category to include those as well. Conversely, *ve'i ktav rachmana lerei'ach nichoach* alone, one might have thought even the *minchat nesachim* suffices — since it too is burned with the *korban*. Therefore the Torah writes *chelev* to establish that the remnant must be part of the animal's body itself.

The logic is elegant: the two expressions are each necessary. *Chelev* excludes the *minchat nesachim*; *lerei'ach nichoach* includes the *yoteret* and *shtei klayot*. Neither term alone would achieve both results.

## Kli Sharet Debate

### R. Yehuda's Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְלֵי שָׁרֵת – פָּסוּל, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן מַכְשִׁיר. גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן? אָמַר קְרָא ״קֹדֶשׁ קָדָשִׁים הִיא כַּחַטָּאת וְכָאָשָׁם״. בָּא לְעוֹבְדָהּ בַּיָּד כְּחַטָּאת – עוֹבְדָהּ בְּיָמִין, כְּחַטָּאת. בִּכְלִי – עוֹבְדָהּ בִּשְׂמֹאל, כְּאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: A handful of a meal offering **that was not** sanctified **in a service vessel** is **unfit, and Rabbi Shimon deems** it **fit.** GEMARA: **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, says: What is the reasoning of Rabbi Shimon? The verse states** with regard to a meal offering: **"It is most holy, as the sin offering, and as the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 6:10). Rabbi Shimon derives from here that the handful of the meal offering may be placed on the altar in the manner of the blood of either a sin offering or a guilt offering. If a priest **comes to perform the** sacrificial **rites of** a meal offering **with** his **hand, as** one performs the sprinkling of the blood of **a sin offering,** which is performed with the priest's right index finger, **he must perform its rites with** his **right** hand, **like the sin offering.** If he performs the sacrificial rites **with a vessel,** as one performs the sprinkling of the blood of a guilt offering, whose blood is sprinkled from a vessel on the altar and whose sprinkling may be performed with the priest's left hand, **he may perform its rites with** his **left** hand, **like the guilt offering.**

The *mishna* states: *shelo bekli sharet* — if the second stage of the *mincha* service, after taking the *kometz*, was performed without a *kli sharet*, it is *pasul*. The *kometz* must be received into a sanctified vessel, parallel in some ways to the reception of blood in a *kli sharet* during animal sacrifices. Yet Rabbi Shimon declares it valid.

Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiyya explains Rabbi Shimon's reasoning from a *pasuk*: *kodesh kodashim hi ka-chatat ve-ka-asham* — the *mincha* is compared both to a *chatat* and to an *asham*. The comparison carries halachic weight. Rabbi Shimon holds, based on positions expressed elsewhere, that by an *asham*, where the blood is thrown from a vessel rather than applied with a finger, the requirement of right hand does not apply — even a left hand is valid. From this he constructs a two-track approach for the *mincha*: *ba le-ovdah beyad ovdah biyamin ka-chatat* — if you wish to perform the burning by hand, use your right hand, as with a *chatat*. *Bikli ovdah besmol ka-asham* — if you wish to use a vessel, you may use your left hand, as with an *asham*. There is therefore no absolute requirement of a *kli sharet* as such; rather, there are two valid modes: hand to *mizbe'ach* using the right hand, or vessel using the left.

Two different framings of Rabbi Shimon's position are possible. One is that there is simply no requirement of a *kli sharet* after taking the *kometz* — just a requirement of getting the *kometz* onto the *mizbe'ach*, either by right hand or by vessel. The other framing is that the right hand of a *kohein* itself counts as a *kli sharet*, and if one instead uses a physical vessel, the left hand becomes permissible as well. Both framings, however, agree that there is no absolute mandate of a physical *kli sharet* when using the right hand.

### R. Yannai Extreme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יַנַּאי אָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁקְּמָצוֹ מִכְּלֵי שָׁרֵת, מַעֲלֵהוּ וּמַקְטִירוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בְּהֶמְיָינוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בִּמְקִידָּה שֶׁל חֶרֶשׂ. רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק אָמַר: הַכֹּל מוֹדִים בַּקּוֹמֶץ שֶׁטָּעוּן קִידּוּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yannai says:** According to Rabbi Shimon there are no restrictions on the manner in which the handful is sacrificed, as **once** the priest has **removed the handful from a service vessel, he may bring it up and burn it even** if he placed it **in his belt, or even in an earthenware vessel. Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak says: All concede that** the **handful requires sanctification** in a service vessel before it is sacrificed.

Rabbi Yannai goes considerably further. He states: *keivan she-kamtzo mi-kli sharet, ma'alehu u-maktira afilu be-hemyano ve-afilu be-makida shel cheres* — once you have taken the *kometz* from the *kli sharet*, you may place it on the *mizbe'ach* even if you are carrying it in the *kohein*'s belt or in a pottery bowl. There is no requirement whatsoever of a *kli sharet* after the initial taking, nor even of the right hand.

According to Rabbi Yannai's reading of Rabbi Shimon, the essential act of the *mincha* service is the taking of the *kometz*. From that point onward, the process of getting the *kometz* onto the fire is not formally defined in the way that the sprinkling of blood is formally defined. It does not matter how you carry it or in what vessel. What matters is that it reaches the *mizbe'ach* and burns.

This position invites a deeper reflection on the nature of the *kometz* as a sacrificial act. The *kometz* occupies an unusual space: on the one hand, it is burned as an offering to God, paralleling the burning of *chelev* and the limbs of an *olah*; on the other hand, it functions as a *matir*, enabling the eating of the *shirayim*, paralleling the role of the blood. If one emphasizes its *matir* function — its resemblance to the *dam* — then the formal process of how it is carried to the *mizbe'ach* becomes significant, just as the catching and sprinkling of blood is formally defined. If, however, one emphasizes its nature as something burned, then what is significant is its presence on the fire — not the act of placing it. Rabbi Yannai reads Rabbi Shimon as emphasizing the latter: the *kometz* is ultimately about *lerei'ach nichoach*, about being on the *mizbe'ach* and burning, and not about a carefully delineated sequence of acts.

This is reinforced by the observation that even Rabbi Yannai concedes that a *piggul* thought is meaningful when the *kometz* is burned on the *mizbe'ach*, even if not when it is merely being carried. The act of placing it on the fire has at least the status of an *avodah*; what Rabbi Shimon downplays is the procedural formality surrounding how one arrives at that moment.

### R. Nachman Middle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הֶקְטֵר חֲלָבִים וְאֵבָרִים וְעֵצִים שֶׁהֶעֱלָן, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בִּכְלִי, בֵּין בְּיָמִין וּבֵין בִּשְׂמֹאל – כְּשֵׁרִין; הַקּוֹמֶץ וְהַקְּטוֹרֶת וְהַלְּבוֹנָה שֶׁהֶעֱלָן, בֵּין בַּיָּד בֵּין בִּכְלִי, בֵּין בְּיָמִין בֵּין בִּשְׂמֹאל – כְּשֵׁרִין. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya, from a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Zevaḥim* 1:11): With regard to **the burning of** the **fats, and** the **limbs, and** the **wood** that were brought up to the altar, **that** the priest **brought them up** to the altar, **whether by hand or with a vessel, whether with** the **right** hand **or with** the **left** hand, **they are fit.** With regard to **the handful, and the incense, and the frankincense, that** the priest **brought them up** to the altar, **whether by hand or with a vessel, whether with** the **right** hand **or with** the **left** hand, **they are fit.** The Gemara suggests: This is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** who stated that if the handful is sacrificed by hand, it must be sacrificed only with the right hand.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak takes the most restrictive position among those who read Rabbi Shimon leniently. He argues: *hakol modim bekometz she-ta'un kiddush* — everyone agrees, even Rabbi Shimon, that the *kometz* must be placed into a *kli sharet* after it is taken. What Rabbi Shimon permits is only the transfer from the *kli sharet* to the fire — the *kohein* need not upturn the *kli sharet* directly onto the altar but may remove the *kometz* with his hand and then place it on the fire. The debate between Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim* is therefore narrower than it appears: it concerns only the final step, whether one must burn the *kometz* directly from the *kli sharet* or may take it out first.

As the *gemara* works through this position, it consistently finds that the evidence does not support it. The *baraitot* cited indicate clearly that Rabbi Shimon and Rabbi Eliezer do not require a *kli sharet* even at the stage of placing the *kometz* into a vessel after it is taken. The *gemara* ultimately concedes: *tyufta d'Rav Nachman tyufta* — this is a conclusive refutation of Rav Nachman's position.

### Tosefta Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא: לִצְדָדִין קָתָנֵי – בַּיָּד בְּיָמִין, בִּכְלִי – בֵּין בְּיָמִין בֵּין בִּשְׂמֹאל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabbi Yehuda, son of Rabbi Ḥiyya,** could **say to you** that the *tanna* of the *baraita* **teaches** it **disjunctively,** and the statement should be understood as follows: If these items are brought up **by hand, with** the **right** hand, or **with a vessel, whether with** the **right** hand **or with** the **left,** they are fit.

A *tosefta* from Zevachim provides striking support for Rabbi Yannai's reading. It states that the burning of fats (*chalavim*), limbs (*eivarim*), and wood — *bein beyad bein bikli, bein biyamin bein bismol* — whether by hand or vessel, right or left, are all valid. The *tosefta* then adds: so too the *kometz*, the *levona*, the *ketoret*, and the *minchat nesachim*, burned *bein beyad bein bikli bein biyamin bein bismol* — are all valid regardless of how they are placed on the *mizbe'ach*.

This *tosefta* is the conceptual foundation for Rabbi Yannai's position. The first category — fat, limbs, wood — are clearly not the sacrifice in the primary sense; they are what is burned, but the blood is the *ikar*. The second category — the *kometz*, *levona*, and *ketoret* — are the primary elements of their respective services, analogous to blood in animal sacrifices. Yet the *tosefta* treats them identically: in both cases, what matters is that they are on the *mizbe'ach* and burning, not the act by which they arrived there. The *tosefta* therefore implicitly argues that the *kometz* is more like burning than like blood.

## Hand & Vessel

### Hand Options

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: קָמַץ בִּימִינוֹ וְנָתַן בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ – יַחְזִיר לִימִינוֹ, בִּשְׂמֹאלוֹ וְחִישֵּׁב עָלֶיהָ בֵּין חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ בֵּין חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין בּוֹ כָּרֵת. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: כֵּיוָן שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לִשְׂמֹאל – פְּסָלַתּוּ מַתְּנָתוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דְּבָעֵי קְדוּשָּׁה בִּכְלִי, וְכֵיוָן שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ לִשְׂמֹאל – נַעֲשָׂה כְּדָם שֶׁנִּשְׁפַּךְ מִצַּוַּאר בְּהֵמָה עַל הָרִצְפָּה וַאֲסָפוֹ, שֶׁפָּסוּל. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא בָּעוּ מַתַּן כְּלִי, תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַב נַחְמָן, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof. **Come** and **hear:** If the priest **removed the handful with his right** hand **and put** it **in his left** hand, **he shall return it to his right** hand. If the handful was **in his left** hand **and he intended** to partake **of** the meal offering in an improper manner, **whether outside its** designated **area or beyond its** designated **time,** the offering is **not valid, but there is no** liability to receive ***karet*** if one partakes **of it.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon. And the Rabbis say: Once he put** the handful **in** his **left** hand, the **placing** of it in his left hand **renders it unfit** and it cannot be rendered fit by returning it to his right hand. **What is the reason?** It is **because it requires sanctification in** a service **vessel, and once he put it in** his **left** hand, **it is considered like blood that spilled from an animal's neck onto the floor** before being collected in a service vessel **and one** then **gathered it, which** is **unfit** and cannot be rendered fit by then being placed in a service vessel. **By inference,** one can conclude **that Rabbi Elazar and Rabbi Shimon do not require** sanctification of the handful by **placing** it in a service **vessel.** Accordingly, this serves as **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion **of Rav Naḥman** bar Yitzḥak, who stated that even Rabbi Shimon requires sanctification in a service vessel. The Gemara affirms: This is **a conclusive refutation** of his opinion.

The *gemara* returns to the position of Rabbi Yehuda the son of Rabbi Chiyya and asks how it reconciles with the *baraita* that permits the use of the left hand. He explains: *lastudim ktani* — the *baraita* does not permit any combination freely but rather presents two valid tracks that must be read in parallel. "By hand" means by the *right* hand; "by vessel" means by vessel, and in that case either right or left is permitted. The *baraita* does not sanction left hand without a vessel, nor does it sanction any arbitrary mix.

This reading is admittedly strained — the plain sense of the *baraita* seems to allow all combinations — but it is the interpretation Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiyya must adopt to preserve his position.

### Three Stages

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: קוֹמֶץ טָעוּן כְּלִי שָׁרֵת, כֵּיצַד? קוֹמְצוֹ מִכְּלִי שָׁרֵת, וּמְקַדְּשׁוֹ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת, וּמַעֲלוֹ וּמַקְטִירוֹ בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁקְּמָצוֹ מִכְּלִי שָׁרֵת, מַעֲלוֹ וּמַקְטִירוֹ שֶׁלֹּא בִּכְלִי שָׁרֵת וְדַיּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another refutation of Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak's opinion from a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 4:15). **Come** and **hear: And the Rabbis say:** The **handful requires** sanctification in **a service vessel. How** is this sanctification performed? The priest **removes the handful from a service vessel, and sanctifies it in a service vessel, and brings it up and burns it in a service vessel. Rabbi Shimon says: Once the handful is removed from a service vessel,** the priest may **bring it up and burn it** even if it is **not in a service vessel, and** this **is sufficient for it.**

A second *baraita* elaborates the position of the *chachamim* with precision: the *kometz* must be taken from a *kli sharet*, placed into a *kli sharet*, and burned from a *kli sharet*. This is a three-stage requirement, each stage requiring a sanctified vessel. *Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabbi Shimon makhshirim mimatan kli* — Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, however, declare it valid in a case where the intermediate stage — the placing into a *kli sharet* — was omitted.

The *gemara* initially considers whether the *baraita* might be reread — *eima mimatan kli* — to say that Rabbi Shimon only waives the requirement of burning *from* the *kli sharet*, not the requirement of *placing into* it. But as with all such attempts, the plain sense of the text resists this reading, and the *gemara* finds it necessary to revise the text artificially.

### Shimon vs. Sages

The essential dispute, stated plainly, is this: the *chachamim* hold that every stage of the *kometz*'s journey from the initial taking through to its burning requires a *kli sharet*. Rabbi Shimon holds that once the *kometz* has been taken, the subsequent stages are not formally constrained in the same way.

### Supporting Text

A further *baraita* states the positions clearly: the *chachamim* require that the *kometz* be taken from a *kli sharet*, placed in a *kli sharet*, and burned from a *kli sharet*; *Rabbi Shimon omer keivan she-kamat* — Rabbi Shimon says: once it has been taken from the *kli sharet*, it may be placed directly on the fire. The *gemara* again attempts to reframe this as referring only to the final step (*mimatan kli va-eilach*), but the straightforward reading continues to support the more expansive position attributed to Rabbi Yannai.

### Left Hand Invalid

A *baraita* now poses a direct challenge: *kometz beyamino ve-natan bismalo* — one took the *kometz* in his right hand and then transferred it to his left, *yachzir leyamino* — he must return it to his right. If while the *kometz* was in his left hand he had a thought of *piggul* — *bein chutz limkomo bein chutz lizmano* — whether about the wrong place or wrong time — the *korban* is *pasul* but there is no *karet*, because the left hand alone is not a valid position for such a thought to be halachically significant. The *baraita* concludes: *divrei Rabbi Eliezer v'Rabbi Shimon* — this is the view of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon. The *chachamim*, by contrast, say: *keivan shenatno lishmalo psalto matnato* — once it was transferred to the left hand, it is invalidated, and one cannot return it to the right.

### Left Hand Status

The *gemara* analyzes the *chachamim*'s position: *mishum d'ba'ei kedusha bikli* — they hold that the *kometz* must immediately go into a vessel upon being taken. If instead it goes into the left hand, it is treated like *dam she-nishpach mitzvar behemah al haritzpah* — blood that spilled from the animal's neck onto the ground rather than being caught in a vessel. Just as such blood cannot be gathered and then sprinkled, so too the *kometz* in the left hand cannot be retrieved and corrected.

Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Shimon, by contrast, do not require immediate placement into a *kli sharet*. Accordingly, the left hand is not a fatal deviation — it is simply not a valid resting position. It is not good enough for a *piggul* thought to take effect (since it is not a position of valid *avodah*), yet it does not irrevocably invalidate the *kometz* either, because the left hand is not inherently disqualifying — it is one of two potential valid modes if a vessel accompanies it.

This analysis supports Rabbi Yehuda son of Rabbi Chiyya's framing most naturally: the left hand without a vessel is a kind of liminal state, neither valid nor invalid, and therefore must be corrected by returning to the right hand or placing in a vessel. The *gemara* acknowledges that this *baraita* provides support for his position (*mesaye'a lei*), while noting that it poses a difficulty for Rabbi Yannai's more expansive reading. Rabbi Yannai responds that this *baraita* simply does not reflect his view — he reads Rabbi Shimon according to the other *baraita*, the *tosefta*, which explicitly permits left hand, right hand, vessel, and no vessel without distinction, and he maintains that the two *tannaitic* sources represent differing opinions.

## Multiple Stages

### Halves & Quarters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִקְטִיר קוּמְצָהּ פַּעֲמַיִם – כְּשֵׁרָה. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: ״פַּעֲמַיִם״ – וְלֹא פַּעֲמֵי פַעֲמַיִם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: ״פַּעֲמַיִם״ – וַאֲפִילּוּ פַּעֲמֵי פַעֲמַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the priest **burned the handful of** a meal offering **twice,** i.e., in two increments, it is **fit.** The Gemara comments: **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says:** The handful is fit if it is burned **twice,** where half of the handful is burned each time, **but not** if it is burned **several times,** in smaller increments. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** It is fit if it is burned **twice, and** it is fit **even** if it is burned **several times.**

The *mishna* states: *hiktir komtza pa'amayim k'sheira* — if the *kometz* was burned in two stages, it is valid. The *gemara* now turns to this second clause.

### R. Y. ben Levi

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi qualifies the ruling: *pa'amayim v'lo pa'amei pa'amayim* — the *mishna* permits burning in two halves, but not in halves of halves — that is, not in quarters. This is not an arbitrary distinction; it rests on two premises. First, *ein kometz pachot mishnei zeisim* — every person's handful is at minimum the size of two *zeisim*, so each half is at least one *zayit*. Second, *ein haktara pechuta mi-kezayit* — there is no valid act of *haktara* with less than a *kezayit*. If every half is at least a *kezayit*, then burning in halves is valid. But dividing into quarters would result in each part being less than a *kezayit*, which would not constitute a valid *haktara*.

### R. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: יֵשׁ קוֹמֶץ פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנֵי זֵיתִים, וְיֵשׁ הַקְטָרָה פְּחוּתָה מִכְּזַיִת אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the basis for the dispute **between** the two opinions? **Rabbi Zeira said:** The dispute **between** the two **is** with regard to whether **there is** significance to **a handful** that is **less than** the size of **two olives and whether there is** significance to the **burning** of **less than an olive-bulk** on the altar.

Rabbi Yochanan permits even quarters: *pa'amayim ve'afilu pa'amei pa'amayim*. He holds *yesh kometz pachot mishnei zeisim* — there are individuals whose handfuls are smaller than two *zeisim*. If the *mishna* permits burning in halves and the *mishna*'s ruling must be universally applicable, it must be that a *haktara* less than a *kezayit* can be valid when it is part of a larger process. Once that principle is established, there is no distinction between halves and quarters.

### Per-Stage Size

As Rabbi Zeira frames it: *yesh kometz pachot mishnei zeisim v'yesh haktara pechuta mi-kezayit ika beinayhu* — the practical debate between Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi and Rabbi Yochanan comes down to whether a *haktara* of less than a *kezayit* at each individual stage is a valid act, when the cumulative total constitutes a full *kometz*. Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi says no: each act must independently be a *kezayit*, and the *mishna*'s permission of halves implicitly guarantees that. Rabbi Yochanan says yes: the *mishna* permits halves precisely because it accepts the principle that individual acts in a staged *haktara* need not each be a *kezayit*, and once that door is open, quarters are equally permissible.

This debate carries a practical implication worth noting: if a normal *kometz* is at least two *zeisim*, that provides useful perspective on the size of a *kezayit* more generally.

## Kometz Validity

### Shirayim Permission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: קוֹמֶץ מֵאֵימָתַי מַתִּיר שִׁירַיִם בַּאֲכִילָה? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא אוֹמֵר: מִשֶּׁמָּשְׁלָה בּוֹ (אֶת) הָאוּר, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מִשֶּׁתַּצִּית בּוֹ (אֶת) הָאוּר בְּרוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated: From when** precisely **does** the sacrifice of the **handful render permitted** the **remainder** of the meal offering **for consumption** by the priests? **Rabbi Ḥanina says: From when the fire takes hold of it,** i.e., when it ignites. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: From when the fire consumes most of** the handful.

The *gemara* now addresses a question that connects directly to the nature of the *kometz* as a *matir*: *kometz mei'emaisai matir shirayim ba'achilah* — from what point does the burning of the *kometz* render the *shirayim* permissible for eating? This question underscores how the *kometz* is unlike the *dam* of an animal sacrifice. By *zerikat hadam*, the moment the blood touches the wall of the *mizbe'ach*, the meat becomes permitted. But the *kometz* is burned — it does not simply make contact; it must undergo a process. So at what stage of that process has the *kometz* discharged its function?

### R. Chanina

Rabbi Chanina says: *mi-she-timshila bo ha'or* — from the moment the fire takes hold of it. The *shirayim* are permitted as soon as the fire begins to burn the *kometz*.

### R. Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *mi-she-tatzis bo ha'or be-rubo* — only when the fire seizes the *majority* of the *kometz*. The word *berubo* — the majority — is what Rabbi Yochanan's formulation adds, and Rashi identifies this as the critical distinction between the two positions, rather than the difference in the verbs (*timshila* versus *tatzis*).

### Oven Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יְהוּדָה לְרַבָּה בַּר רַב יִצְחָק: אַסְבְּרַהּ לָךְ טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְהִנֵּה עָלָה קִיטֹר הָאָרֶץ כְּקִיטֹר הַכִּבְשָׁן״, אֵין כִּבְשָׁן מַעֲלֶה קִיטוֹר עַד שֶׁתַּצִּית הָאוּר בְּרוּבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said to Rabba bar Rav Yitzḥak: I will explain to you the reasoning of Rabbi Yoḥanan. The verse states: "And behold, the smoke of the land went up as the smoke of a furnace"** (Genesis 19:28), and **a furnace does not release smoke until the fire takes hold of the majority of** the fuel. Rabbi Yochanan derived from this verse that the majority of the handful must be consumed by the fire, since the priests are instructed to make the handful smoke, as it is written: "And the priest shall make the memorial part thereof smoke upon the altar" (Leviticus 2:2).

Rav Yehuda explains Rabbi Yochanan's reasoning to Rabba bar Rav Yitzchak. The *pasuk* describing the destruction of Sodom uses the phrase *ve-hineh ala kitor ha'aretz ke-kitor ha-kivshan* — the smoke rose from the earth like the smoke of an oven. The word *kitor* — pillar of smoke — is related to the root of *lehaktir*. The *gemara* reasons: *ein kivshan ma'aleh kitor ad she-tatzis ha'or be-rubo* — an oven does not produce a pillar of smoke until the fire seizes the majority of its contents. Since *lehaktir* means to cause the *kitor* to rise, the act of *haktara* is not complete until the majority of the object has been seized by fire.

This is a powerful conceptual point. *Lehaktir* is not simply "to place on fire" — it means to cause the thing to rise up as smoke, as incense. That process does not begin the moment the object touches the flame; it is only underway when the fire has taken hold of most of it. Until then, the *kometz* has been placed on the fire but has not yet been *muktir*.

### Day vs. Night

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִין בַּר רַב אַדָּא לְרָבָא: אָמְרִי תַּלְמִידֶיךָ אָמַר רַב עַמְרָם, תַּנְיָא: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא דְּבָרִים שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לִיקְרַב בַּלַּיְלָה, כְּגוֹן אֵבָרִים וּפְדָרִים, שֶׁמַּעֲלָן וּמַקְטִירָן מִבּוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, וּמִתְעַכְּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה כּוּלָּהּ. דְּבָרִים שֶׁדַּרְכָּן לִיקְרַב בַּיּוֹם, כְּגוֹן: הַקּוֹמֶץ, וְהַלְּבוֹנָה, וְהַקְּטֹרֶת, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֲנִים, וּמִנְחַת כֹּהֵן מָשׁוּחַ, וּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים, שֶׁמַּעֲלָן וּמַקְטִירָן מִבּוֹא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ? וְהָא אָמְרַתְּ דַּרְכָּן לִיקְרַב בַּיּוֹם! אֶלָּא, עִם בֹּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ, שֶׁמִּתְעַכְּלִין וְהוֹלְכִין כׇּל הַלַּיְלָה – מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״זֹאת תּוֹרַת הָעֹלָה״ – רִיבָּה. וְהָא עִם בֹּא הַשֶּׁמֶשׁ – לָא מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ שֶׁתַּצִּית הָאוּר בְּרוּבּוֹ. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן לִקְלוֹט, כָּאן לְהַתִּיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravin bar Rav Adda said to Rava: Your students say** that **Rav Amram said** that **it is taught** in a *baraita*: **I have** derived **only** with regard to **items whose** usual **manner** is **to be sacrificed at night, for example,** the **limbs** of the burnt offering **and** the **fats [*pedarim*]** of the burnt offering, **that** the priest **may bring them up and burn them after sunset and they are consumed throughout the entire night.** This is derived from the verse: "This is the law of the burnt offering: It is that which goes up on its firewood upon the altar all night unto the morning" (Leviticus 6:2). The *baraita* continues: With regard to **items whose** usual **manner is to be sacrificed during the day, for example, the handful** of the meal offering, **the frankincense, the incense, the meal offering of priests, the meal offering of** the **anointed priest,** i.e., the High Priest, **and the meal offering** that accompanies the **libations,** from where is it derived **that** the priest **may bring them up and burn them after sunset?** The Gemara interjects: Why would they be allowed to be burned after sunset? **But didn't you say** that these are items **whose** usual **manner is to be sacrificed during the day?** The Gemara clarifies: **Rather,** the question of the *baraita* is as follows: **From where** is it derived that these items may be brought up and burned concurrent **with the setting of the sun,** in **which** case **they are consumed throughout the entire night** and not during the day? **The verse states: "This is the law of the burnt offering"** (Leviticus 6:2), which **included** everything that is sacrificed on the altar. Ravin bar Rav Adda challenges: **But** if the handful is brought up and burned concurrent **with the setting of the sun, you do not find that the majority of it is consumed by the fire** before sunset. How does this *baraita* accord with Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement that the majority of the handful must be consumed by the fire in order to render permitted the consumption of the remainder by the priests? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. Here,** where the *baraita* does not require the consumption by fire of the majority of the handful, it is referring only to that which is required in order for the altar **to receive** the handful, so that it is considered the food of the altar and may continue to burn all night long. **There,** Rabbi Yochanan states that in order **to render permitted** the consumption of the remainder by the priests, the majority of the handful must be consumed by the fire.

Rav Amram cites a *baraita* that introduces a complication. The *baraita* distinguishes between things normally offered at night — such as the *eivarim* and *padarim*, the limbs and fats of an *olah*, which are placed on the fire after sunset and continue burning through the night — and things normally offered during the day: the *kometz*, the *levona*, the *ketoret*, the *minchat kohanim*, the *minchat kohen mashiach*, and the *minchat nesachim*. For the latter category, the *baraita* asks: *minayin* — how do I know that even these may be placed on the fire before sunset and continue burning into the night?

The question appears paradoxical: if these are things that must be offered during the day, how can any part of the *haktara* extend into the night?

### Sunset Resolution

The *gemara* resolves this: *kan liklot kan lehaktir* — there is a distinction between *klitatah* — the moment the fire takes hold of it and it is considered attached to the *mizbe'ach* — and *haktara* — the completion of the burning process. The act that constitutes the *avodah*, the placing on the fire, must occur during the day. But the completion of the burning — the majority of the *kometz* being seized by fire — may extend into the night. The *baraita* teaches that *zot torat ha-olah* — the law of the *olah* — comes to include that these items, placed on the fire before sunset, remain valid even as they continue to burn afterward.

This is not a contradiction of Rabbi Yochanan's position. Rather, it refines it: the *kometz* does not become fully *muktir* — and thus does not fully permit the *shirayim* — until the majority of it has been seized by fire. But what must happen before sunset is the placing on the fire, not the completion of the burning. The completion can occur afterward, and the *kometz* is still valid.

### Act vs. Completion

This resolution reflects a broader principle about acts that are initiated by a human being and then completed by an autonomous process. The *gemara* draws a parallel to the laws of *Shabbos*: if one plants seeds on *Shabbos*, one is liable even though the seeds do not take root until weeks later. The act of planting is defined by the human initiation of the process, not the biological completion. Similarly, the act of *haktara* is initiated by placing the *kometz* on the fire before sunset; the completion — the fire seizing the majority — may occur afterward, and the *korban* is nonetheless valid.

This insight dovetails elegantly with the positions discussed earlier regarding the process of placing the *kometz* on the *mizbe'ach*. Rabbi Shimon, and most fully Rabbi Yannai, argued that the formal procedural requirements of *how* one gets the *kometz* onto the fire are not the primary concern — what matters is that it burns. The present discussion reinforces that perspective from a different angle: the moment of validity is not the act of placing but the completion of burning. The human act initiates the process; the *haktara* is the result. And in that sense, the *kometz* is indeed more like the burning of fat and limbs than like the sprinkling of blood — it is not a discrete instantaneous act but a process, one that begins with the *kohein* and concludes on its own, by fire.
