# Avodah Zarah 11 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Jewish Minority Cont (II)

### Integration vs. Distinct…

The tractate of *Avodah Zarah* opens up a fundamental question about how we think about the larger world. Is this tractate primarily about idolatry in the technical sense, or is it about a different type of life altogether — a life that is not oriented around *Torah* and *mitzvos*? Whatever the answer, the Rabbis are manifestly aware of the world in which they live. They know all of these pagan holidays intimately, and more than that, the entire calendar is structured around events of the larger world — the *minyan shtarot*, not the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* and not the creation of the world. Even the socio-structural organization of time reflects this embeddedness: the discussion of counting from *Tishrei* rather than from *Nissan* evokes a year beginning in January rather than on *Rosh Hashanah*. We are in many ways one fabric with that larger world, even as we work to retain our distinctiveness.

### Antoninus & Rabbi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי שָׁכֵיב אַנְטוֹנִינוּס, אָמַר רַבִּי: נִתְפָּרְדָה חֲבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When Antoninus died, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: The bundle is separated.**

The figure of Antoninus — and the question of exactly which Caesar this refers to is itself a significant one, as Keafter has a very long note attempting to identify precisely which ruler is meant, since the dates do not work for Marc Antony — appears in this context as a counterpart to *Rebbi*, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi. It is possible that this friendship has some kernel of historical fact, since part of the role of the *nesi'im* in *Eretz Yisrael* was to maintain a positive relationship with Rome. But the story as told — with Antoninus sneaking away from Rome nightly and traveling through some secret cave, crossing improbable distances in order to have private meetings with *Rebbi*, and at one point even serving as *Rebbi*'s footstool — is of course quite humorous to read. It is also a very powerful story, and one must think carefully about its intended audience.

The audience is fellow Jews who are feeling the weight of minority existence — oppressed, defenseless, wondering what it means to be part of a tradition that seems so insignificant before the overwhelming success of Rome. These stories are powerful precisely because the message comes through even as one senses how non-factual they are. The whole event that recurs once every seventy years — *Eisav* riding atop a lame *Yaakov* — seems to be the opposite message, Rome triumphant over Israel. But the punchline, as we will see at the end of the *daf*, is that even in the midst of that great display of dominance, the final proclamation is a lament: woe to us when the other one arises. Even they, deep down, recognize the ultimate truth.

## Roman History & Caesar

### Sefer Josippon Account

Before proceeding further into the *daf*, there is a small but remarkable *Tosafos* on 10b, *kol nesi'eha*, that distills in roughly six lines an entire account of Roman history. The source it draws on is *Sefer Josippon* — not the actual Josephus, but a Hebrew pseudo-Josephus written in the Middle Ages that was believed to be a translation of Josephus and became very popular in that period. The *Tosafos* introduces this with the words *da'amar Ri*, and then proceeds to tell the following story.

### Roman Oath & Caesar

*Shenishbu Romiyim shelo lehamlich aleihem od melech* — the Romans took an oath never again to install a king over themselves. This oath arose *bishvil echad shelakach isha achas bechazaka* — because of the rape of a woman, a reference to the rape of the Sabines. From that point forward, Rome decided it could never have a king. Instead, *ela shehayu memanim yashish echad* — they would appoint one elder, essentially a consul, along with *shelosh me'os yo'atzim*, three hundred advisors representing the Senate. Yet *vesof kama shanim malach aleihem echad bechazaka* — after many years, one man imposed his power over them and became a real ruling force.

### Caesar Etymology

This man's origins carry a further detail: *shemesa imo belidta venivka bitna* — his mother died in childbirth and her womb was cut open, meaning he was born through cesarean section. *Umetzauhu chai umalach aleihem* — he was found alive and ultimately ruled over them. *Venikra Keisar belashon Romi belashon karus beIvris* — he was called *Keisar* in the language of Rome, which corresponds to the word for "cut" in Hebrew. *Ve'al shemo nikre'u kol hamelachim she'achirav Keisar* — and after him, all subsequent rulers were called *Keisar*. This is, notably, reasonably close to what actually happened historically. A beautiful distillation of Roman history in roughly six lines.

## Onkelos Proselyte

### Onkelos Recognition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוּנְקְלוֹס בַּר קְלוֹנִימוּס אִיגַּיַּיר, שַׁדַּר קֵיסָר גּוּנְדָּא דְּרוֹמָאֵי אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, מַשְׁכִינְהוּ בִּקְרָאֵי, אִיגַּיּוּר. הֲדַר שַׁדַּר גּוּנְדָּא דְּרוֹמָאֵי [אַחֲרִינָא] אַבָּתְרֵיהּ, אֲמַר לְהוּ: לָא תֵּימְרוּ לֵיהּ וְלָא מִידֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara mentions other Romans who converted to Judaism. It relates: **Onkelos bar Kelonimos converted** to Judaism. The Roman **emperor sent a troop [*gunda*] of Roman** soldiers **after him** to seize Onkelos and bring him to the emperor. Onkelos **drew them** toward him **with verses** that he cited and learned with them, and **they converted.** The emperor **then sent another troop of Roman** soldiers **after him,** and **said to them: Do not say anything to him,** so that he cannot convince you with his arguments. The troops followed this instruction, and took Onkelos with them.

At the top of 11a, the *Gemara* relates the story of *Onkelos HaGer*, which functions as a similar narrative to the Antoninus and *Rebbi* story: even a non-Jew of the highest standing recognizes greatness and converts. Moreover, when Roman officers were sent to apprehend *Onkelos*, all he had to do was give them a small glimpse of the true path, and they too converted. The story functions to reinforce a sense of dignity and confidence — even in a condition of minority and oppression, the power of the faith we represent speaks for itself.

It is worth noting in this context that Jews in the Roman world likely constituted a much higher percentage of the population than Jews represent in the world today. What accounts for the dramatic decline is a combination of persecutions, forced conversions, and assimilation over the subsequent centuries. But within the Roman world, Jewish presence was far from insignificant.

## Rivka Womb Reading

### Goyim as Ge'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ לָהּ, שְׁנֵי גוֹיִם בְּבִטְנֵךְ״. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַל תִּקְרֵי ״גּוֹיִם״ אֶלָּא ״גֵּיִים״, זֶה אַנְטוֹנִינוּס וְרַבִּי, שֶׁלֹּא פָּסְקוּ מֵעַל שׁוּלְחָנָם לֹא חֲזֶרֶת וְלֹא קִישּׁוּת וְלֹא צְנוֹן, לֹא בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְלֹא בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, דְּאָמַר מָר: צְנוֹן מְחַתֵּךְ אוֹכֶל, חֲזֶרֶת מְהַפֵּךְ מַאֲכָל, קִישּׁוּת מַרְחִיב מֵעַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of Antoninus: When the matriarch Rebecca was pregnant with Jacob and Esau, **"the Lord said to her: Two nations [*goyim*] are in your womb"** (Genesis 25:23). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Do not read** it as ***goyim*,** meaning nations; **rather** read it as ***geyim*,** meaning proud ones. **This** verse was fulfilled in two prominent individuals who descended from Rebecca, **Antoninus and Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **whose tables,** due to their wealth, **never lacked for lettuce, nor cucumbers, nor radish, neither in the summer nor in the rainy season,** despite the fact that these foods do not grow year round. The reason they ensured that these items were always present at their tables is **that the Master said: A radish breaks up food, lettuce stirs up food,** and **cucumbers expand the intestines.**

The *Gemara* now takes up the verse *Shnei goyim bevitnech* — "two nations are in your womb" — said by God to *Rivka*. *Amar Rav Yehuda amar Rav al tikrei goyim ela ge'im* — do not read it as "nations" but as "haughty ones," or ones of great stature, *de'Antoninus ve'Rebbi* — referring to Antoninus and *Rebbi*. This is a classic example of the *al tikrei* reading, rerouting the plain meaning of a biblical verse through a subtle orthographic shift to generate a new layer of significance.

## Jewish Minority Cont (II)

### Yaakov-Eisav Paradigm

This brings us to a broader reflection on how Jews read history and interpret world events. Yosef Yerushalmi, in his small but essential book *Zachor*, argues that Jews did not do history in the modern sense — they did memory. The concern was not with establishing facts for their own sake, but with constructing a sense of Jewish memory, interpreting history through a biblical lens, and fitting past events into a set of archetypes that make sense of the present and provide confidence about the future. Every anti-semite becomes a *Haman*; every crisis maps onto a prior paradigm.

Within this framework, the central warring model in the *Torah* — leaving aside the Pharaoh who enslaved the Israelites — is *Yaakov* and *Eisav*: two entities in constant striving against one another. But *Eisav* becomes *Edom*, and *Edom* eventually dies out as an ancient nation. The question then becomes who, in the post-biblical period, inherits the role of *Edom* in the cosmic drama. The answer the tradition settled on is Rome. The translation was taken for granted: *Eisav* is *Edom* is Rome. And when Rome as a political entity ceased to exist, the model shifted to the relationship between Judaism and Christianity — a transition helped considerably by the fact that the Holy Roman Empire adopted Christianity as its religion. All of this history — Jews versus Rome, Judaism versus Christianity — gets read through the *Yaakov*-*Eisav* lens.

The *Gemara*'s reading of *shnei goyim bevitnech* as referring to Antoninus and *Rebbi* represents a rare moment in this paradigm: the one time when these two figures got along.

## Seasonal Produce Luxury

### Off-Season Vegetables

The *Gemara* records that both Antoninus and *Rebbi* enjoyed vegetables year-round, regardless of season: *shelo pasak me'al shulchanam lo chazeret velo kishut velo tzenon, lo biyemei hachama velo biyemei hageshamim* — neither lettuce, nor cucumbers, nor radishes ever ceased from their table, whether in summer or in winter. This is presented as a mark of extraordinary wealth, since in the ancient world access to vegetables out of season was by no means taken for granted.

The *Gemara* offers medical explanations for why these particular vegetables were valued: *tzenon mechatech ochel* — radishes help chop up and digest food; *chazeret mehappech ma'achal* — lettuce helps churn the food around in the stomach; *kishut marchiv mei'ayim* — cucumbers help expand the digestive capacity. *Tosafos* adds that the point was not merely that they had out-of-season vegetables, but that they had specifically those vegetables which were necessary for good digestion, because their tables were so overflowing with delicacies that digestive aids were a necessity.

### Cucumber Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: לָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָן קִישּׁוּאִין? מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקָּשִׁין לְגוּפוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם כַּחֲרָבוֹת! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא בְּרַבְרְבֵי, הָא בְּזוּטְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But isn't it taught** in the **school of Rabbi Yishmael: Why are they called cucumbers [*kishuin*]?** It is **because they are as harmful [*kashim*] to a person's body as swords.** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult. This** statement, that they are harmful to the body, is referring **to large** cucumbers, whereas **that** statement, explaining why they were always present on the tables of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Antoninus, is referring **to small ones.**

The *Gemara* then raises a complication. *Ve'atanyah devei Rabbi Yishmael lama nikre sheman kishu'in mipnei shekashin legufo shel adam kecharavot* — a *Baraisa* in the school of Rabbi Yishmael states that cucumbers are called *kishu'in* precisely because they are as hard and damaging to the body as swords. This seems to contradict the positive assessment just offered. The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya, ha berabbeyei ve'ha bezozei* — there is no difficulty; one statement refers to large cucumbers and the other to small ones, with the large ones presumably being harmful.

## Birth & Death Days

### Birth Death Holiday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹם הַלֵּידָה וְיוֹם הַמִּיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The birthday** of the king **and the day of the death** of the king are considered gentile festivals.

The *Gemara* returns now to the topic of pagan holidays, listing *yom leida ve'yom mita* — the day of birth and the day of death among the occasions that constitute pagan holidays for the purposes of the *Mishnah*'s prohibitions.

### Funeral Pyre Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר: לָא שְׁנָא מִיתָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה, וְלָא שְׁנָא מִיתָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה — פָּלְחִי בַּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, אַלְמָא שְׂרֵיפָה לָאו חוּקָּה הִיא. מִכְּלָל דְּרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: שְׂרֵיפָה חוּקָּה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **By inference,** this means **that Rabbi Meir holds** that **there is no difference** whether it is **a death that includes** public **burning, and there is no difference** whether it is **a death that does not include** public **burning;** in either case, **they engage in idol worship on** that occasion. **Evidently,** Rabbi Meir holds that the **burning** performed at the death of the king **is not** an idolatrous **custom,** as it is not the cause of the prohibition. The Gemara continues: From here, one can conclude **by inference that the Rabbis hold** that the **burning** upon the death of the king **is** an idolatrous **custom.**

This leads to an important *halachic* discussion. Rabbi Meir holds that a death day is a pagan holiday only when it involves a funeral pyre — *mita sheyesh bah sereifa* — while a death day without a funeral pyre is not considered a pagan holiday. The Rabbis do not make this distinction. The *Gemara* reads the positions as follows: *miklal de'Rabbi Meir savar, lo shena mita sheyesh bah sereifa velo shena mita she'ein bah sereifa, palchi lah la'avodat kochavim* — Rabbi Meir's position implies that pagan worship takes place at all funerals regardless of whether there is a funeral pyre. The funeral pyre is therefore not itself the problem; it is not a *chuka* in the relevant sense. *Alma sereifa lav chuka hi* — the funeral pyre is not a *chuka*, meaning it is not per se a pagan ritual. Pagan worship occurs at all funerals whether the pyre is present or not, so the presence or absence of the pyre changes nothing.

Conversely, *ve'rabbanan savri sereifa chuka hi* — the Rabbis appear to hold that the funeral pyre is itself a *chuka*, a pagan rite, and only when it is present do we have a genuine pagan holiday. Without it, the death day does not constitute a prohibited occasion.

### Chashivuta Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא תַּנְיָא: שׂוֹרְפִין עַל הַמְּלָכִים, וְלֹא מִדַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי, וְאִי חוּקָּה הִיא, אֲנַן הֵיכִי שָׂרְפִינַן? וְהָכְתִיב: ״וּבְחֻקֹּתֵיהֶם לֹא תֵּלֵכוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **We burn** items **upon the** death of **kings** as an expression of grief, **and** this is **not of the ways of the Amorites,** but rather a Jewish custom? **And if** this **is** an idolatrous **custom, how could we** perform this public **burning? But isn't it written: "And you shall not walk in their statutes"** (Leviticus 18:3)?

The *Gemara* immediately raises a challenge from a *Baraisa*: *sorfin al hamelachim ve'lo midarchei ha'emori* — one may burn for the death of Jewish kings and this is not considered the ways of the Amorite, which is another formulation for the prohibition of *bechukoteihem lo telechu*. This seems to flatly contradict the Rabbinic view that a funeral pyre is itself a pagan ritual — for if it were, how could it be permitted for Jewish kings? *Ve'ei chuka hi, anan heichi sarfinan, vehaktiv uvchukoteihem lo telechu* — if this really is a *chok*, how can we practice it?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא שְׂרֵיפָה לָאו חוּקָּה הִיא, אֶלָּא חֲשִׁיבוּתָא הִיא, וְהָכָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי: רַבִּי מֵאִיר סָבַר — לָא שְׁנָא מִיתָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה, וְלָא שְׁנָא מִיתָה שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה — פָּלְחִי בַּהּ לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי — מִיתָה שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה חֲשִׁיבָא לְהוּ וּפָלְחִי בַּהּ, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׂרֵיפָה לָא חֲשִׁיבָא וְלָא פָּלְחִי בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, everyone** agrees that the public **burning** itself **is not** an idolatrous **custom. Rather, it is** performed due to the great **importance** of the king who passed away. **And here,** in the mishna, **they disagree about this: Rabbi Meir holds** that **there is no difference** whether it is **a death that includes** public **burning and there is no difference** whether it is **a death that does not include** public **burning;** in either case, in practice **they engage in idol worship on** that occasion. **And the Rabbis hold** that **a death that includes** public **burning** is **important to** the gentiles, **and** therefore **they engage in** idol **worship on** that occasion, **but** a death **that does not include** public **burning** is **not important** to them, **and they do not engage in** idol **worship on** that occasion.

The resolution: *ela dechulei alma sereifa lav chuka hi* — in fact, everyone agrees that the funeral pyre is not intrinsically a pagan ritual. *Ela chashivuta hi* — rather, it is a signal of *chashivuta*, of great significance and importance. It marks the death of an especially important person and marks the occasion as a funeral of great weight. It is not itself a form of pagan worship, but it is a marker that a funeral of this magnitude will be accompanied by pagan worship.

This then is the actual dispute: *Rabbi Meir savar lo shena mita sheyesh bah sereifa velo shena mita she'ein bah sereifa palchei leh le'avodat kochavim* — Rabbi Meir holds that pagan worship takes place at all funerals, important or not, because pagans will always incorporate some form of worship into a death. Therefore, whether there is a funeral pyre matters not at all. *Ve'rabbanan savri mita sheyesh bah sereifa chashiva lehu upalchei bah* — the Rabbis, however, hold that the pyre marks this as an important occasion, and it is at important occasions that pagan worship is conducted. *She'ein bah sereifa lo chashiva ve'lo palchei* — without the pyre, the funeral is not significant enough to occasion pagan worship.

The practical upshot is that we ourselves may perform this burning ritual for our kings and *nesi'im* — not because we are following pagan practice, but because the ritual itself is not pagan in content. It signals importance and honor, which is a Jewish value as much as a Roman one. An analogy: whenever a certain society dresses formally for an event, one knows they will conduct their pagan worship there. That does not make formal dress itself forbidden; the two simply co-occur. We are permitted to signal honor for our great ones in this way.

This *Gemara* opens onto a larger discussion about the scope of *bechukoteihem*. *Tosafos* is bothered because in *Sanhedrin* the prohibition of *bechukoteihem* seems to have a broader scope — for instance, the discussion of whether execution by sword constitutes *bechukoteihem*. The *Gemara* there also indicates that if a practice has a basis in *Torah* or *Nach*, it is not a problem of *bechukoteihem*. And the burning of kings, as we will see shortly, is indeed sourced in *Nach*.

*Tosafos* resolves this by distinguishing two categories within *bechukoteihem*. The first category consists of things genuinely associated with foreign religions or with *avodah zarah* — those remain off-limits regardless of whether there is a parallel in *Torah*, once they are truly associated with those other religions. The second category consists of arbitrary or irrational practices that they happen to have — like executing by sword. In that second category, if we have our own independent basis in *Torah* for the practice, it is permissible. The present *Gemara* thus narrows *bechukoteihem* to things specifically associated with *avodah zarah*, while other *Gemaros* suggest it can reach further into neutral practices of the surrounding society — though with less stringency, and more room to say we are not really deriving it from them if we have our own basis.

This question about the scope of *bechukoteihem* is in fact central to the broader conversation about Jewish integration into the surrounding world. If *bechukoteihem* prohibits only practices that are genuinely *avodah zarah*-related, then one can dress, live, and appear in many ways similar to the surrounding society while retaining one's distinctiveness through observance of *mitzvos*. If *bechukoteihem* extends to neutral everyday practices, then the demand is not merely for distinctiveness but for a more thoroughgoing separateness. *Rav Moshe Feinstein* in a *teshuvah*, asked whether it is *assur* to give secular names, points out that many great rabbis throughout history had secular names and that *lo shinu shemam umalbusham uleshonam* applied specifically before the giving of *mitzvos*, when external markers were needed to maintain distinctiveness. Now that we have *mitzvos*, he argues, one need not be as stringent about these external signs — a position that speaks directly to the difference between Modern Orthodox and *Haredi* approaches. But this is a much larger discussion; the *Gemara* here raises it only in passing.

## Burning for Kings

### Torah Source Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: שׂוֹרְפִין עַל הַמְּלָכִים, וְאֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בְּשָׁלוֹם תָּמוּת וּבְמִשְׂרְפוֹת אֲבוֹתֶיךָ הַמְּלָכִים וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁשּׂוֹרְפִין עַל הַמְּלָכִים, כָּךְ שׂוֹרְפִין עַל הַנְּשִׂיאִים.
>
> **Translation:** Having mentioned this *baraita*, the Gemara returns to discuss **the** matter **itself.** The *baraita* teaches: **One burns** items **due to the** death of **kings** as an expression of grief, **and** this **is not subject to** the prohibition of imitating **the ways of the Amorites,** since it is a Jewish custom. **As it is stated** that Jeremiah prophesied to Zedekiah king of Judah: **"You shall die in peace; and with the burnings of your fathers, the former kings** that were before you, so shall they make a burning for you" (Jeremiah 34:5). **And just as one burns** items **upon** the death of **the kings, so too one burns** items **upon** the death of **the heads of the Sanhedrin.**

The *Gemara* now returns to the *Baraisa*: *sorfin al hamelachim ve'ein bah mishum darchei ha'emori*. The *pasuk* adduced in support comes from Jeremiah's prophecy to *Tzidkiyahu*: *beshalom tamut* — you will die in peace — *uvemisrafos avotecha hamelachim asher sarfu lefanecha* — and you will have a burning like those of the kings who preceded you. This demonstrates that these burnings were practiced throughout the period of the Judean monarchy and were accepted practice.

### What Burned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָה הֵם שׂוֹרְפִין עַל הַמְּלָכִים? מִיטָּתָן וּכְלֵי תַּשְׁמִישָׁן, וּמַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁמֵּת רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הַזָּקֵן, וְשָׂרַף עָלָיו אוּנְקְלוֹס הַגֵּר שִׁבְעִים מָנֶה צוֹרִי. וְהָאָמְרַתְּ: מָה הֵן שׂוֹרְפִין עֲלֵיהֶם — מִיטָּתָן וּכְלֵי תַּשְׁמִישָׁן! אֵימָא: בְּשִׁבְעִים מָנֶה צוֹרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** items **do they burn upon the** death of **kings?** They burn the kings' **beds and their utensils,** so that no one else can make use of them. **And** there was **an incident in which Rabban Gamliel the Elder died, and upon** his death **Onkelos the convert burned seven thousand dinars** in valuable **Tyrian** coinage. The Gemara asks: **But didn't you state** in response to the question: **What do they burn upon** the death of kings, that they burn **their beds and their utensils?** Why, then, did Onkelos burn money? The Gemara answers: **Say** that Onkelos burned items that were valued **at seven thousand dinars** in **Tyrian** coinage.

*U'keshem shesorfin al melachim kach sorfin al nesi'im* — just as this applies to kings, so it applies to *nesi'im*, the leaders of the Jewish community. *U'mahi hem sorfin? Mitatan u'chlei tashmishan* — what is burned? Their beds — which may refer either to their literal beds or to the funeral bier on which the body was carried out — and their personal effects.

### Onkelos Burning

The *Gemara* records: *u'ma'aseh shemet Rabban Gamliel hazaken, ve'saraf alav Onkelos hager shiv'im mana tzuri* — when Rabban Gamliel died, *Onkelos HaGer* burned seventy *mana* of Tyre in his honor, an enormous sum. The *Gemara* immediately questions this: *mai hem sorfin alav mitatan u'chlei tashmishan* — we said only personal effects are burned? *Eima b'shiv'im mana tzuri* — the text means that the value of the personal effects burned came to seventy *mana* of Tyre.

It is notable that it is *Onkelos HaGer* who makes such an extravagant gesture here. In the context of the whole discussion establishing that this practice is not pagan — that it is in fact rooted in *pesukim* and represents Jewish tradition — the person making the most emphatic statement of honor is one who comes from the outside culture and has now converted in. One wonders whether, had it been someone else, they might have felt less urgency to demonstrate that *Rebbi* should be treated no differently from a Roman emperor.

On the historical question of whether *Onkelos* could have been present at the death of Rabban Gamliel: the *Gemara* states that *Onkelos* was a student of *Rebbe Eliezer* and *Rebbe Yehoshua*, placing him roughly in the period of *Rabbi Akiva*, around 130 CE. Rabban Gamliel HaZaken, the elder, died well before the destruction of the Temple, creating a significant chronological difficulty. However, many *Rishonim* — including the *Rif*, the *Rosh*, and the *Ran* — do not have the word *hazaken* in the text, which would place this as a reference to the later Rabban Gamliel, who lived in approximately the right period. The *Tosefta* likewise does not include *hazaken*, which lends weight to the reading without it.

### Hobbling Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: עוֹקְרִין עַל הַמְּלָכִים, וְאֵין בּוֹ מִשּׁוּם דַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: סוּס שֶׁרָכַב עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** are **other items not** destroyed in order to accord honor to the deceased king, apart from his utensils? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **we detach** the hooves of livestock **upon the** death of **kings, and** this **is not subject to** the prohibition of **the ways of the Amorites? Rav Pappa says:** That *baraita* is referring to **the horse upon which** the king **rode.** Since that animal was designated as the king's personal item, it is therefore rendered unusable for anyone else, like his personal utensils.

*U'midai achrinei lo?* — is it truly only personal effects that are subject to this practice? A *Baraisa* states: *okin al hamelachim ve'ein bo mishum darchei emori* — one hobbles animals as part of the funeral of a king and this is not considered the way of the Amorite. *Okin* refers to cutting the tendons of the animal's legs. Presumably this is an expression of grief and devastation — analogous to *keriah* on a garment, expressing that one's world has been torn apart, but stopping short of incisions in the flesh, which the *Torah* prohibits as either idolatrous or as going too far in expressions of mourning. These practices occupy a middle ground: deeply destructive and symbolic, but directed at external objects.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבְהֵמָה טְהוֹרָה לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: עִיקּוּר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ טְרֵיפָה — אָסוּר, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ טְרֵיפָה — מוּתָּר, וְאֵיזֶהוּ עִיקּוּר שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ טְרֵיפָה? הַמְנַשֵּׁר פַּרְסוֹתֶיהָ מִן הָאַרְכּוּבָּה וּלְמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** did they **not** detach the hooves of the king's **kosher animals,** which are not used by the king for riding? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: If **removing** the hooves of an animal would **entail** that it becomes **an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** it is **prohibited** to do so. **And when** doing so would **not entail** rendering it **a *tereifa*,** it is **permitted. And what is** a way of **removing** hooves **that does not entail** rendering the animal **a *tereifa*? One who cuts the hooves of** the animal, severing the legs **from the knee and below,** does not render the animal a *tereifa*.**

The *Gemara* seeks to bring even this within the category of personal effects. *Sus sherachav alav* — if it is the horse on which he rode, that is his vehicle, his personal effect. But this raises the question of whether non-riding animals would also be subject: *u'vehemah tehorah lo?* A *Baraisa* states that if the hobbling was performed in a manner that rendered the animal a *tereifah*, it may not be eaten; if done in a manner that did not render it a *tereifah*, it may be eaten. *Tosafos* notes the *chiddush*: one might have thought that the animal's involvement in this ritual would give it a status of being off-limits for benefit, somewhat like an object dedicated to a prohibited use. The *Baraisa* therefore teaches that if it is not a *tereifah*, it remains fully permitted.

*Eizehu ikur she'ein bo tereifah? Hamnasher parsotav min ha'arkuva u'lemata* — hobbling that does not create a *tereifah* status means cutting from the ankle down. And the objection that kosher animals who were not personal effects were also subject to this is resolved by *Rav Pappa*: *ba'agala hamoshechet bakaron* — it refers to the calf or animal that drew the king's wagon. That too is effectively his personal vehicle, his personal effect.

## Ethical Limits

### Tza'ar & Kavod

*Tosafos* on 11b raises a question that at first glance might seem beside the point but opens into significant *halachic* territory. *De'amai lo parich de'ika tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — why does the *Gemara* not ask how this practice is permitted at all, given that it inflicts pain on animals? And for that matter, *de'ika mishum bal tashchit* — is there not also a concern of wanton destruction, given that valuable property is being burned?

*Tosafos* answers the *bal tashchit* concern readily: *keivan delikhvodo shel melech osin ken ein kan hashchata* — since this is done in honor of the king, it is not considered wanton destruction. *Bal tashchit* requires destruction that is purposeless and wasteful. When destruction serves a purpose — even a symbolic one, an expression of grief or honor — it does not constitute *bal tashchit*. One's instinct to treat *bal tashchit* as a sweeping prohibition against any destruction of property is misplaced; the prohibition is specifically against wanton, purposeless destruction.

The *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* concern is more serious. *Tosafos* says: *deshani kvod hamelech shehu kvod kol Yisrael* — the honoring of the king is the honoring of all Israel, when it concerns a Jewish king or *nasi*. Honor in society is defined by social norms — to fail to do what is customarily done in honoring a great person is itself a statement of dishonor, and to do it is an act of genuine honor. *Ve'ati kvod rabim vedachi tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — and *kavod rabim*, the honor of the community, comes and overrides *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*.

### Animal Suffering Narrow

This *Tosafos* is important not only for this specific case but for what it implies about the general permissibility of causing animal suffering. According to *Tosafos* here, the permission is quite narrow: it applies specifically when a *Torah* value of the weight of *kavod rabim* is at stake. This is one *Torah* value being weighed against another *Torah* value, and a decision must be made between them. General human benefit — the desire for cosmetics, or veal, or any number of commercially motivated practices — does not constitute a *Torah* value and therefore would not, according to this reading of *Tosafos*, override *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*.

### Benefit Permissible

However, other *Rishonim* — the *Piskei Tosafos* at the back, the *Ramban*, and others — read the permission much more broadly, and hold that any human benefit is sufficient to permit *tza'ar ba'alei chayim*. This broader position is what gets adopted *le'halacha*. The practical consequences are significant: on the lenient end, it permits practices like plucking feathers from geese for down, which would be difficult to permit under a stricter reading. On the permissive end, it opens the door to justifying practices that cause significant animal suffering on thin grounds of human benefit.

This creates a genuinely unsatisfying state of affairs from the perspective of those concerned with animal welfare. *Halacha* could in principle have developed a calculus: a certain quantity of benefit must outweigh a certain quantity of animal suffering. Instead, the ruling went against the more carefully calibrated position of *Tosafos* and toward a binary — any benefit permits *tza'ar ba'alei chayim* — that leaves little room for distinguishing between trivial convenience and genuine necessity. The present *Tosafos*, which does not hold this way, at least maintains the tension.

## Beard Shaving

### Lock Interpretations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ. אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ, הֵיכִי קָתָנֵי? יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ וְהַנָּחַת בְּלוֹרִיתוֹ, אוֹ דִלְמָא יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ וְהַעֲבָרַת בְּלוֹרִיתוֹ? תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתַנְיָא תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ וְהַנָּחַת בְּלוֹרִיתוֹ, יוֹם תִּגְלַחַת זְקָנוֹ וְהַעֲבָרַת בְּלוֹרִיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **The day of shaving his,** i.e., the gentile's, **beard** and his locks. **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **What is** the mishna **teaching** here? Is it referring to **the day of shaving his beard and** head, when he cuts the hair of his head and as a result **his** long **locks** at the back of his head are **left** as a form of idol worship? **Or perhaps** the mishna is speaking of **the day of shaving his beard and removing his locks,** which are removed some time after they were left on the head? The Gemara responds: **Come** and **hear** an answer, **as both** opinions **are taught** in *baraitot*: One *baraita* mentions **the day of shaving his beard and** head when **his** long **locks** at the back of his head are **left,** whereas a different *baraita* specifies **the day of shaving his beard and removing his locks.**

The *Gemara* continues listing pagan holidays: *yom tiklachat zikno* — the day of the shaving of the beard. A question is raised: *heichi tani? Yom tiklachat zikno ve'hanachat blurito* — is the holiday the day of the shaving of the beard together with the setting-aside of the *blurit*, a lock or ponytail of hair? Or is it *yom tiklachat zikno ve'ha'avarat blurito* — the day of the shaving of the beard together with the cutting off of the *blurit*? It appears from *Rashi* that the practice involved growing the hair for a full year and then cutting it off in a ceremony. The question is which event — the initiation of the growing period or the culmination in the cutting — constitutes the pagan holiday. The *Gemara* resolves: *tashma de'tanya travaihu* — a *Baraisa* records both, meaning both events constitute pagan holidays.

## Seventy-Year Spectacle

### Eisav Riding Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: עוֹד אַחֶרֶת יֵשׁ [לָהֶם] בְּרוֹמִי, אַחַת לְשִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה מְבִיאִין אָדָם שָׁלֵם, וּמַרְכִּיבִין אוֹתוֹ עַל אָדָם חִיגֵּר, וּמַלְבִּישִׁין אוֹתוֹ בִּגְדֵי אָדָם הָרִאשׁוֹן, וּמַנִּיחִין לוֹ בְּרֹאשׁוֹ קַרְקִיפְלוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss Roman festivals. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: They have another** festival **in Rome: Once every seventy years they bring a man** who is **whole** and free from any defect **and have him ride on a lame man,** to symbolize the healthy Esau ruling over Jacob, who walked with a limp after his fight with the angel. **And they dress him** in the **garments of Adam the first** man **and place on his head the scalp [*karkifelo*] of Rabbi Yishmael,** which the Romans flayed when they executed him.

*Amar Rav Yehuda amar Shmuel: od acher yesh lahem* — there is yet another Roman holiday not mentioned in the *Mishnah*. *Be'Romi, achat l'shiv'im shana* — in Rome, once every seventy years, they conduct the following spectacle. *Mevi'im adam shalem* — they bring a person of full, healthy body. *U'markivin oto al adam chiger* — and they have him ride on top of a lame man. The healthy man represents *Eisav*, the lame man represents *Yaakov* — who walked with a limp after his encounter with the angel.

*U'malbishin oto bigdei Adam Ha-Rishon* — and they dress the man on top in the clothes of *Adam Ha-Rishon*. How the Romans would have come to possess the clothes of *Adam Ha-Rishon* is accounted for by the tradition that *Eisav* — whose *bigdei chamudot*, the desirable garments mentioned in the *Torah*, are identified by the *Midrash* as the clothes of *Adam Ha-Rishon* handed down through generations — would have passed them to his descendants. *U'manichin lo be'rosho karkaflo shel Rabbi Yishmael* — and upon the head of the rider they place the skull, or the face, of Rabbi Yishmael. Rabbi Yishmael was one of the martyrs, and the story of how the skin of his face was flayed off is well known.

### Adam HaRishon Clothes

The imagery is deliberate and multilayered: *Eisav*-Rome rides atop a broken *Yaakov*-Israel, clothed in the primordial garments of humanity that were always rightfully Israel's, and wearing the face of the greatest Jewish martyr. It is an act of cultural appropriation as much as of dominance.

### Rabbi Yishmael Head

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָלוּ לֵיהּ [בְּצַוְּארֵיהּ] מַתְקַל [מָאתַן] זוּזָא דְּפִיזָּא, וּמְחַפִּין אֶת הַשְּׁוָוקִים בְּאִינָךְ, וּמַכְרִיזִין לְפָנָיו: סַךְ קִירִי פְּלַסְתֵּר, אֲחוּהּ דְּמָרַנָא זַיְיפָנָא. דְּחָמֵי חָמֵי, וּדְלָא חָמֵי לָא חָמֵי, מַאי אַהֲנִי לְרַמָּאָה בְּרַמָּאוּתֵיהּ וּלְזַיְיפָנָא בְּזַיְיפָנוּתֵיהּ? וּמְסַיְּימִין בַּהּ הָכִי: וַוי לְדֵין כַּד יְקוּם דֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** **And they hang gold on his neck weighing two hundred dinars, and cover the markets with onyx, and announce before him: The calculation [*sakh*]** of **the master [*kiri*]** Jacob with regard to the time of the redemption is **fraudulent [*plaster*]; the brother of our master,** i.e., Esau, **a forger.** They further announce: One **who witnesses** this festival **witnesses** it, **and whoever does not witness** it will **not** witness it ever, as it was celebrated only once every seventy years. **What purpose does deceit serve for the deceiver, and forgery for the forger? And they conclude in this** fashion: **Woe unto this** one, Esau, **when that** one, Jacob, **will arise,** as this will cause Esau's downfall.

*Ve'talu leh be'tzavarei mitkal reish zuzei de'piza* — and around his neck they hang a certain weight in gold — with different textual traditions giving different amounts — representing the gold tax that Jews were historically required to pay to Rome. *U'mechappin et ha'shvakim be'onach* — and they pave the marketplaces with onyx, evoking the idea that Roman streets are paved with wealth, wealth that came from Jewish subjugation.

*U'machrizin lefanav* — and a crier goes before him and announces. *Rashi* understands the opening word *Sach* as referring to the calculation or reckoning of the great one, while *Tosafos* reads it differently, arguing that the reference is to *Yaakov* — not *Yitzchak*, who as *Eisav*'s father might be acknowledged as great — and that *Yaakov* would not be styled the great one in this context. Either way, the proclamation is: the calculation of this great one is all a forgery. *Yaakov* prophesied an *acharit hayamim*, an end of days in which Israel would be triumphant and ascendant. The proclamation declares: *achva demarna zaifana* — the brother of our master, meaning *Eisav*'s brother *Yaakov*, is a forger. He deceived *Eisav*, stole the *bechora*, made false prophetic claims. *Dechami chami, udelo chami lo chami* — whoever has a chance to see this spectacle will see it; if you miss it, you will not see it again, because it comes only once every seventy years, and you will not live to see the next one.

This also reflects and amplifies the anti-Semitic trope of *Yaakov* as schemer and deceiver: *mai ahani lei lerama birmautei, ul'zaifana b'zaifnutei* — what did that schemer gain from his scheming, and the forger from his forgery? You tried to outmaneuver us, you tried to claim a heritage that was not yours, and look at you now.

### Final Woe Line

*U'mesaymei behachi: vay l'den kad yakum den* — and this is how they conclude: woe to this one when the other arises. Even at the height of this elaborate display of Roman dominance, the final line is an acknowledgment of what the end of days will bring. The Jews will ultimately be ascendant, and Rome knows it. Deep down, even as they mock and degrade, they know.

This is, of course, an extraordinary passage. Whether it has any kernel of historical fact is difficult to say — the anachronism of Rabbi Yishmael's head is itself a significant problem, and *Rav Yehuda* and *Shmuel* were Babylonian *Amora'im* who would not have had direct access to such a Roman event. At most, given the seventy-year cycle, the event could have occurred only a handful of times by the end of *Shmuel*'s life. But the story's power lies not in its historical reliability but in its function: here is the message Jews always receive from the outside world — you count for nothing, you are deceivers, we hold all the power — and here is the answer: even they, at the end, admit what will be.

## Annual Holidays

### Annual Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתָנָא דִּידַן מַאי טַעְמָא לָא קָחָשֵׁיב לַהּ לְהַאי? דְּאִיתַהּ בְּכֹל שַׁתָּא וְשַׁתָּא קָחָשֵׁיב, דְּלֵיתַהּ בְּכֹל שַׁתָּא וְשַׁתָּא לָא קָחָשֵׁיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And what is the reason** that **the *tanna* of our** mishna **does not count this** festival in his list of gentile festivals? The Gemara answers: The *tanna* of the mishna **counts those** festivals **that occur each and every year,** and **he does not count** those festivals **that do not occur each and every year.**

*Detana didan mai ta'ama lo kachashiv lah?* — Why does our *Mishnah* not include this seventy-year Roman spectacle among the pagan holidays? The *Gemara* answers: *deha de'ita bechol sheta vesheta kachashiv, deha d'leita bechol sheta vesheta lo kachashiv* — the *Mishnah* lists only annual holidays; events that do not recur every year are not included.

### Five Temples

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי דְּרוֹמָאֵי, וּדְפָרְסָאֵי מַאי? מוּטְרְדֵי וְטוּרְיַסְקִי, מוּהַרְנְקֵי וּמוּהְרִין. הָנֵי דְּפָרְסָאֵי וּדְרוֹמָאֵי דְּבַבְלָאֵי מַאי? מוּהַרְנְקֵי וְאַקְנִיתָא, בַּחְנוּנֵי וַעֲשַׂר בַּאֲדָר. אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר רַב, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ אָמַר רַב חָנָן בַּר רָבָא אָמַר רַב: חֲמִשָּׁה בָּתֵּי עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה קְבוּעִין הֵן, אֵלּוּ הֵן: בֵּית בֵּל בְּבָבֶל, בֵּית נְבוֹ (בְּכוּרְסֵי) [בְּבוּרְסִיף], תְּרַעְתָּא שֶׁבְּמַפַּג, צְרִיפָא שֶׁבְּאַשְׁקְלוֹן, נִשְׁרָא שֶׁבְּעַרְבִיָּא. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי הוֹסִיפוּ עֲלֵיהֶן יָרִיד שֶׁבְּעֵין בֶּכִי, נִדְבָּכָה שֶׁבְּעַכּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Those** festivals enumerated in the mishna are the festivals **of the Romans.** The Gemara asks: **And what** are the idolatrous festivals **of the Persians?** The Gemara answers: ***Mutredei* and *Turyaskei*, *Moharnekei* and *Moharin*.** The Gemara asks: **Those** are the festivals **of the Persians and the Romans,** and **what** are the festivals **of the Babylonians?** The Gemara answers: ***Moharnekei* and *Akenitei*, *Beḥanunei* and the tenth of Adar. **Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda says** that **Rav says, and some say** that it was **Rav Ḥanan bar Rava** who **says** that **Rav says: There are five established temples of idol worship,** and **they are: The temple of Bel in Babylonia; the temple of Nebo in** the city of **Khursei; Tirata**, which is located **in** the city of **Mapag; *Tzerifa*, which is** located **in Ashkelon;** and ***Nashra*, which is** located **in Arabia. When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said: The Sages **added to these** places **the marketplace that is** located **in Ein Bekhi** and ***Nadbekha*, which is** located **in Akko.**

*Hani deromai* — those are the Roman holidays. *Deparsai mai?* — what about the Persian ones? *Mutadri veturiski muhranki umaharin* — those are the Persian names. *Debevlai mai?* — and the Babylonian ones? *Muhraniki veachnishi bachnuni ve'asara b'adar* — those are the Babylonian holidays, including the tenth of Adar.

*Amar Rav Chanan bar Rav Chisda amar Rav, ve'amrei lah amar Rav Chanan bar Rava amar Rav: chamesh batei avoda kochavim kevuin hen* — there are five fixed places of idolatry, and these are they: *Beit Baal debevel* — the temple of Baal in Bavel; *Beit Nevo b'Kursi* — the temple of Nevo in Kursi; *Tar'ata she-be-Mapag* — the gate in Mapag; *Tsrifa she-be-Ashkelon*; and *Nashra she-be-Arvaya* — in Arabia. *Ki ata Rav Dimi, osifu aleihem* — when Rav Dimi came, he added: *Yerid she-be-Ein Bechi* and *Nazbakha she-be-Akko*, with an alternative tradition reversing the locations of these last two.

### Daily Worship Sites

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חָנָן בַּר רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַב חִסְדָּא: מַאי ״קְבוּעִין הֵן״? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי אָמַר אֲבוּהּ דְּאִימָּךְ: קְבוּעִין הֵן לְעוֹלָם. תְּדִירָא, כּוּלַּהּ שַׁתָּא פָּלְחִי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥanan bar Rav Ḥisda said to Rav Ḥisda: What** does it mean that these temples of idol worship **are established?** Rav Ḥisda **said to him: This is what your mother's father,** Rav Ḥanan bar Rava, **said:** In contrast to festivals, which last for one or several days, **they are always fixed** as the site of idol worship, as **constantly, all year** round, **worship** takes place there.

*Amrei leh Rav Hanan bar Rav Hisda l'Rav Hisda: mai kevuin hen?* — what does it mean that these temples are "fixed"? *Amar leh: hachi amar avuha d'immach: kevuin hen l'olam, tidira kula shata palchu lehu* — this is what your mother's father said: they are fixed forever; they conduct worship there throughout the entire year, on a daily basis. These are not merely sites of occasional festivals but permanent, ongoing centers of idolatrous worship.

## Shmuel's Leniency

### Holiday Day Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּגּוֹלָה אֵינוֹ אָסוּר אֶלָּא יוֹם אֵידָם בִּלְבַד, וְיוֹם אֵידָם נָמֵי מִי אֲסִיר? וְהָא רַב יְהוּדָה שְׁרָא לֵיהּ לְרַב בְּרוֹנָא לְזַבּוֹנֵי חַמְרָא, וּלְרַב גִּידֵּל לְזַבּוֹנֵי חִיטִּין בְּחַגְּתָא דְּטַיָּיעֵי! שָׁאנֵי חַגְּתָא דְּטַיָּיעֵי, דְּלָא קְבִיעָא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** The *halakha* is that **in the Diaspora,** engaging in business with gentiles **is prohibited only** on **the day of their festival** itself, not during the days preceding and following the festival. Since Jews live among the gentiles, they are unable to refrain from engaging in business with them for such an extended period. The Gemara asks: **And is** it **prohibited even** on **their festival day** itself? **But didn't Rav Yehuda permit Rav Beruna to sell wine** to gentiles, **and** permitted **Rav Giddel to sell wheat, on the festival of the Arab** merchants? The Gemara answers: **The festival of the Arab** merchants **is different, as** it does **not** have **a fixed** time, and therefore the Sages did not include it in the prohibition.

*Amar Shmuel: ba'gola eino assur ela yom eidam bilvad* — in exile, only the day of the holiday itself is forbidden; the days before are not. This addresses the *Mishnah*'s apparent implication that three days before a pagan holiday is prohibited, and limits that to *Eretz Yisrael*.

## Merchants Holiday

### Merchant Holiday

The *Gemara* asks: *yom eidam nami mi asar?* — is even the day of the holiday actually forbidden? But *Rav Yehuda* permitted *Rav Bruna* to buy wine, *ul'Rav Giddel l'zivni chittin* — and permitted *Rav Giddel* to buy wheat, *be'chaga d'atayya* — on the holiday of the merchants. The *Gemara* answers: *shani chaga d'atayya d'lo kevia* — the holiday of the merchants is different because it is not a regular, fixed annual holiday.

### Market Days Activity

What is striking about this passage is the underlying reality it reveals. Around these holidays, enormous commercial activity was taking place — everyone preparing, buying wine and wheat, conducting business. The holiday of the merchants was itself structured around market activity. The close association between pagan holidays and marketplace commerce — days of buying and selling, merchants' fairs — is precisely why the prohibitions on commercial interaction were necessary in the first place. It was not merely that a holiday happened to be occurring while people happened to be doing business. The holiday and the commercial activity were intertwined.

*Tosafos* draws on this point in dealing with the practical reality of Sunday as a major market day in medieval Europe. The argument that Sunday was simply a merchants' day — not a serious pagan holiday — is already anticipated by the *Gemara*'s own logic, though *Tosafos* finds other grounds as well, including a significant reconceptualization of the nature of Christian worship relative to *avodah zarah*.

## Shmuel's Leniency (II)

### Practical Impossibility

Why does *Shmuel* rule leniently in exile? *Rashi* explains: *ein anu yecholim le'hamir atzmenu mi'lisa u'litain imahem she'beineihem anu yoshvim* — we cannot impose upon ourselves restrictions against doing business with them, for we live among them. The use of "we" is notable: *Rashi* is no longer simply describing a historical ruling but is projecting his own reality. Outside of *Israel*, where one cannot structure society independently, the luxury of avoiding commercial interaction for three days before every pagan holiday simply does not exist. If one lives deeply embedded within the surrounding culture, one cannot simply cease normal economic activity on a rotating schedule tied to pagan festivals.

### Fear of Anger

*Rashi* adds a second rationale: *ve'od mishum yira* — also because of fear that they will be angered. The leniency is thus grounded not only in practical impossibility but in the real danger of provoking hostility by ostentatiously refusing commerce around their holidays. This is the same logic that the *Rishonim* drew on more broadly to explain why their own practice deviated from the *Gemara*'s apparent standards: the realities of exile impose constraints that the *halacha* must accommodate.

## Geographic Restrictions

### City Suburb Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עִיר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — חוּצָה לָהּ מוּתָּר, הָיָה חוּצָה לָהּ עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה — תּוֹכָהּ לָהּ מוּתָּר. מַהוּ לֵילֵךְ לְשָׁם? בִּזְמַן שֶׁהַדֶּרֶךְ מְיוּחֶדֶת לְאוֹתוֹ מָקוֹם — אָסוּר, וְאִם הָיָה יָכוֹל לְהַלֵּךְ בָּהּ לְמָקוֹם אַחֵר — מוּתָּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a city in which there is** active **idol worship,** it is **permitted** to engage in business transactions with gentiles who live **outside of** the city. If the **idol worship** is **outside** the city, it is **permitted** to engage in business **within** the city. **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **traveling there,** a place where a pagan festival is being celebrated? **When the road is designated** only **for that place,** it is **prohibited** to use the road, as onlookers will assume that the traveler intends to join the festival. **But if one were able to travel on it to** arrive at **another place,** it is **permitted** to use the road to reach the place that is observing the festival.

The next *Mishnah* states: *ir she'yesh bah avodas kochavim, chutzah lah mutar* — if a city contains idolatry, outside of it is permitted; *hayah chutzah lah avodas kochavim, tochah lah mutar* — if the idolatry is outside the city, inside the city is permitted. The plain meaning of this *Mishnah*, in context, is about pagan holidays and the question of conducting business. If a pagan holiday is being celebrated in the town, one may still do business in the suburbs; if it is being celebrated in the surrounding villages, one may still do business in the town itself. The prohibition applies only in the region where the holiday is being celebrated, not in adjacent areas.

### Rambam Stricter

*Rambam*, however, reads this *Mishnah* quite differently, and his reading is striking. He understands it to say that one is forbidden from living in — and indeed from even entering — a city that contains *avodah zarah*, meaning a house of worship of foreign religion. His formulation in the *Perush HaMishnayos* is explicit: *lefi she'assur lehikanes le'ir she'yesh bah avodas kochavim, kol she'ken ladur bah, ve'kol she'ken lishor bah* — it is forbidden to enter a city that has *avodah zarah*, how much more so to live there, and how much more so to conduct commerce there. And: *kol ir shel umah she'hayah lahem beis tiflah shel avodas kochavim, belo sofek osah ir assur la'avor bah be'chavanah* — any city of a nation that has a house of worship of *avodah zarah*, it is without doubt forbidden to deliberately enter that city. *Kol she'ken ladur bah* — how much more so to live there.

This is an extreme position, and *Rambam* himself acknowledges it. If the *Mishnah* is really prohibiting entry into cities with houses of foreign worship, then the entire earlier discussion of doing business before pagan holidays becomes somewhat puzzling — one asks, why discuss the three days before if you cannot even be in such a city to begin with?

### Rambam Exile

*Rambam* confronts this directly: *aval anachnu tachat yedeihem ba'avonoseinu ve'shochnim be'artzam anusim* — but we are under their control, in our sins, and we dwell in their land as those who have no choice. *Ve'nitkayem banu mah she'ne'emar va'avadtem sham elohim ma'aseh yedei adam* — and the verse "you will serve there gods made by human hands" has been fulfilled for us. The further he goes, the sharper it becomes: *ve'im ha'ir hadin ken, kol she'ken din beis avodas kochavim atzmah she'assur lanu kim'at lir'osah* — if this is true regarding the city, how much more so regarding the actual house of worship, which one may barely even look at. *Kol she'ken lehikanes bah* — how much more so to enter it.

This is a position that *Rambam* maintains in principle while acknowledging it is practically impossible to observe under the conditions of exile. The *Gemara* itself does not convey this level of stringency, and the question of whether the *Mishnah* is really addressing the presence of idols or simply the occurrence of pagan holidays is central to understanding the gap between *Rambam*'s reading and the simpler contextual reading.

## Entry & Maris Ayin

### Path Determination

The *Mishnah* concludes with the question of going into the town during a pagan holiday: *mahu lalechet lesham?* — what about going to such a place? *Bizman shehaderech meyuchedet leoto makom assur* — when the road leads exclusively to that town and ends there, it is forbidden to travel it on the holiday. *Ve'im hayah yachol lehalech lemakom acher mutar* — if the road continues through to another destination, it is permitted.

### Onlooker Assumption

The reasoning is about what observers will assume. If the road leads only to the town, and that town is having its pagan holiday today, anyone who sees you traveling down that road will assume you are going to participate in the holiday. There is no other explanation for why you would be going there. The concern is therefore not primarily about what you actually do when you arrive but about what your journey signals to those who see you.

### Maris Ayin Scope

This raises an important question about the scope of *maris ayin*. A classic case of *maris ayin* involves something that looks forbidden but is not — eating a soy-based food that resembles a cheeseburger, for instance. But this case is different: the observer does not think the act you are performing is itself forbidden; they think you are on your way to do something forbidden. Is that *maris ayin*? This is a genuine debate among the *Rishonim*. The present *Mishnah* would appear to be evidence that *maris ayin* applies even in cases where the observer thinks you are about to do something forbidden, not that the thing you are currently doing is itself problematic. One might argue that *avodah zarah* is a special case warranting a broader application — but the principle seems to extend further.

### Pilgrimage Practice

*Rabbeinu Yonah* offers a different analysis that cuts through the *maris ayin* question. He argues that for pilgrimage-style holidays, the act of traveling to the location is itself part of the religious practice. Think of the *Hajj*: traveling to Mecca is not a preliminary to the worship — it is the worship. When observers see you traveling toward a town that is celebrating its holiday, they do not merely infer that you intend to worship when you arrive. They see the traveling itself as participation. The journey is intrinsically an act of the holiday's practice.

Under this analysis, the concern is not simply *maris ayin* in the sense of "looking like" you will do something forbidden. It is that the act of traveling to the holiday destination is itself a form of religious participation, at least as perceived by those who see you. The prohibition on using a dedicated road is therefore not only about the appearance of wrongdoing but about the act of going itself constituting, in this context, a form of participation. How much weight to give *Rabbeinu Yonah*'s framing — whether the act of traveling is intrinsically participatory or whether the concern remains one of appearance — has significant implications for how broadly we apply the lesson of this *Mishnah* to other contexts.
