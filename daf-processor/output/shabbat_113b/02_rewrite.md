# Shabbat 113b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kavod Shabbat & Clothes

### Kavod Shabbat Intro

The daf opens in the context of a *mishnah* that dealt with folding clothes on *Shabbat* and various *issurim*, but which gave occasion to introduce the broader theme of *kavod Shabbat*: refraining from extraneous activities, changing one's clothes, and a range of behaviors either performed or avoided in honor of the day. Because *kavod* is so central to this discussion, and because clothing is identified as one of the primary vehicles through which honor is expressed, the *gemara* now turns to a *drasha* on the concept.

### Vesachas Kvodo Drasha

The verse under discussion is *vesachas kvodo yekad yekod kikod esh* — "and beneath his *kavod* burned like the burning of fire" — referring to *Sancheriv*. *Rabbi Yochanan* comments: *vesachas kvodo velo kvodo mamash* — beneath his *kavod*, but not the *kavod* itself. *Rashi* explains that when the army of *Sancheriv* was destroyed, the bodies burned while the clothes were left intact. The explanation for *Rabbi Yochanan*'s reading is consistent with his general outlook: *Rabbi Yochanan l'taimei*, *Rabbi Yochanan* goes according to his own reasoning, for as we learned previously, *Rabbi Yochanan karei l'manei mechabdisai* — *Rabbi Yochanan* calls his clothes "the things that honor me," *mechabdisai*. It is therefore natural that he reads *kvodo* as a reference to clothing, and that the *drasha* emphasizes the clothes being spared while the bodies were consumed.

*Rabbi Elazar* offers a different reading: *tachas kvodo mamash* — *kvodo* refers to the person himself. If the burning is described as beneath the person himself, this must mean the souls were destroyed while the bodies remained intact.

*Rabbi Yishmael bar Nachmani* then adds: *vesachas kvodo kishreifat b'nei Aharon* — like the burning of the sons of Aharon. Just as in that case, *sreifat neshama veguf kayam* — the soul was consumed while the body remained — so too here, *af kan sreifat neshama veguf kayam*.

In any case, these are various *drashot* that tie into the concept of *kavod*, and at least according to *Rabbi Yochanan*, *kavod* refers specifically to one's clothing.

### Biblical Source

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *minayin l'shinnui bgadim min HaTorah?* — from where do we derive the idea of changing clothes from the *Torah* itself? After all, we cited earlier the verse from Ruth — *v'rachatzat v'sacht* — put on your *simlotayich*, understood as *bgadei Shabbat*. But where else does the *Torah* indicate that changing into appropriate clothes for different occasions reflects proper honor?

The answer is drawn from the *avoda* in the *Beit HaMikdash*: *upashat et bgadav v'lavash bgadim acherim* — the *kohen* removes his garments and wears other garments to take out the ashes. This establishes a precedent that different functions call for different clothing.

### Derech Eretz

*V'tana d'bei Rabbi Yishmael*: *limadcha Torah derech eretz* — the *Torah* is teaching you proper conduct. *Bgadim shebishal bahen kedera l'rabo al yimzug bahen kos l'rabo* — the clothes in which you cooked the pot for your master you should not use to pour his cup. You wear an apron while cooking, but dress differently when attending or serving at dinner. The point is not that some clothes are inherently better than others, but that clothes must match their purpose. If one wishes to show particular honor for *Shabbat*, one dresses in a manner befitting that occasion.

## Torah Scholars' Dress

### Patched Shoes Disgrace

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *gnay hu letalmid chacham sheyeitzei biminalim hametulaim lashuk* — it is a disgrace for a Torah scholar to go out into the marketplace wearing shoes with patches on them. One need not wear expensive shoes, but one should not appear tattered. This is because the Torah scholar represents Torah itself, and his appearance affects how others perceive Torah and those who study it.

The *gemara* immediately raises a challenge: *veha Rabbi Chiyya bar Chanina nafik?* — but did *Rabbi Chiyya bar Chanina* go out wearing patched shoes? *Rabbi Abba brei deRav Nachman* answers: *telai al gav telai* — the problem is only when one's patches have patches. As the expression goes, he is so poor his patches have patches. This raises a real difficulty: what is a person supposed to do when he genuinely cannot afford better shoes? The *gemara* will later discuss the community's obligation to provide for Torah scholars, but for now the point stands that even the appearance of destitution — entirely beyond one's control — creates a problematic impression.

This is admittedly a tension that runs much deeper in the tradition. There is an analogous difficulty with the *bigdei kehuna* and the question of *mumim* — how much one accommodates existing social norms about external appearance versus how much one works to reshape those norms. The *gemara* here seems to be accommodating the reality that people judge by externals, even while acknowledging that *ha'adam yireh la'einayim v'Hashem yireh lalivav* — a person looks at what is visible while God looks at the heart. The practical position taken is that, in the interim before broader sensibilities change, the Torah scholar must be mindful of the impressions created by his appearance.

### Stains & Desecration

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *kol talmid chacham shenimtza revav al bigdo chayav mita* — any Torah sage who is found with a *revav* — a grease stain, something particularly conspicuous and repellent — on his garment is deserving of death. This is strong language, and it is supported by the verse: *kol mesan'ai ahavu mavet* — all those who hate me love death. The *gemara* reads this as *al tikrei mesan'ai ella mashni'ai* — do not read "those who hate me" but rather "those who cause others to hate me." The Torah scholar who goes about with a disgusting stain on his garment causes people to hate Torah and has, in effect, done something terrible to how Torah and Torah study are perceived.

Here, unlike the case of patched shoes, it may be more within one's control to launder one's clothes — though laundry also costs money, and one should not be cavalier about that reality. Nevertheless, the principle is clear: how one dresses reflects the respect one shows toward the people around oneself, toward the occasion, and toward what one represents. It also directly affects the respect others will accord to those things in turn.

### Revav vs Revvad

*Ravina amar revad itmar* — the word stated was not *revav* but *revvad*. And according to *Rashi*, *revvad* refers to something worse — specifically a semen stain. *V'lo pligi* — and they do not actually dispute one another. *Ha b'glima ha b'levusha* — one refers to the outer garment and the other to the inner garment. *Rashi* explains: for the inner garment, which is less visible, a regular grease stain might be tolerable, but a *revvad* is still unacceptable. The outer garment, by contrast, presents a problem even with an ordinary grease stain.

### Isaiah Nakedness

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* said in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: what is meant by the verse *ka'asher halach avdi Yeshayahu arom v'yachef* — like my servant Isaiah walked naked and barefoot? *Arom* does not mean literally naked — it means *b'v'gadim bluyim* — with garments in tatters. *V'yachef* barefoot means *b'minalim hamutlaim* — with patched-up shoes. This underscores the point that tattered garments and patched shoes are perceived as tantamount to not being properly clothed at all.

### Chatzitza Stains

A *mishnah* elsewhere teaches: *revav al hamarda* — a grease stain on a saddle is a *chatzitza*, a barrier to valid immersion. This applies when there is still surface grease that has not been fully absorbed, since something is only considered a *chatzitza* when one is *makpid* — when people find it objectionable. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* qualifies: only if the stain is the size of an Italian *issar* or larger does it constitute a problem; smaller than that is not an issue.

Regarding garments: *v'al hab'gadim mitzad echad eino chotzetz* — if a grease stain on a garment appears on one side only, it is not a *chatzitza*. *Me'shnei tzdadim chotzetz* — if it has been absorbed so thoroughly that it is visible on the other side as well, then it is a *chatzitza*. *Rabbi Yehuda* says in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael*: *apilu mitzad echad chotzetz* — even from one side alone it constitutes a *chatzitza*.

*Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* asked *Rabbi Chanina* about the saddle discussed earlier: *mitzad echad o mi'shnei tzdadim* — does it become a *chatzitza* from one side or only from two sides? *Rabbi Chanina* replied: *zu lo shamati* — I have no explicit tradition on this. But *k'yotze bah shamati* — I have heard a parallel teaching. The *mishnah* records: *Rabbi Yosi omer — hab'nayim mitzad echad* — for *benayim*, one side is already a problem. As for an *am ha'aretz*, only from two sides is it a problem. The *gemara* will explain momentarily that *benayim* here refers to Torah scholars. *V'lo tihye marda chashuva mi'bigdo shel am ha'aretz* — a saddle should not be treated more stringently than the clothes of an ordinary person. Therefore, even the saddle presents a problem only when the stain penetrates both sides — which, it must be said, would require a remarkably heavy stain.

### Scholars & Stains

*Mai Banayim?* — what is the meaning of *benayim* in the *mishnah*? *Amar Rabbi Yochanan: elu Talmidei Chachamim she'oskim bevinyano shel olam kol yimeihem* — these are Torah sages who spend their entire lives engaged in building the world. The *gemara* thus reads *benayim* through the lens of the well-known *drasha: al tikri banayich ella bonayich* — do not read "your children" but "your builders." This designation carries genuine weight: the *gemara* is countering the perception that Torah scholars merely sit and waste time, emphasizing instead that they are the ones constructing the fabric of the world.

With this identification established, the ruling follows naturally: because Torah scholars must be especially careful about their appearance and what it communicates, a stain on even one side of their garment is already a problem. For ordinary people, however, a stain only becomes a *chatzitza* when it penetrates both sides of the garment.

One might ask why the *gemara* needed to invoke *Rabbi Yosi*'s ruling rather than simply citing the *Tanna Kamma*'s position — that for ordinary people a stain is only a problem when it goes through to both sides — and inferring from there about the saddle. The *gemara*'s choice to cite *Rabbi Yosi* specifically remains somewhat puzzling, but the halachic conclusion is the same.

## Talmid Chacham

### Lost Objects Trust

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yochanan: eizehu talmid chacham she-machzirin lo aveidah bitvi'at ha-ayin?* — who is the Torah scholar to whom we return a lost object based merely on visual recognition? It is worth noting that the term *talmid chacham* in this context does not carry its modern connotation of a great *gadol*, but refers more literally to one who is a student of the *chachamim* — still a disciple, but one who devotes his time entirely to Torah study.

Ordinarily, returning a lost object requires the claimant to provide a specific identifying mark — a *siman*. If someone claims to recognize an object by sight without being able to articulate a precise description, that is not normally sufficient, since anyone could make such a claim. Yet the *gemara* entertains the possibility that for a Torah scholar whose integrity can be relied upon absolutely, visual recognition alone may suffice.

### Appearance & Integrity

*Zeh hamakpid al chaluko l'hafcho* — this is someone so attentive to how he presents himself that if he has put on his garment inside out, he will take the trouble to remove it and reverse it so it appears properly. The *gemara* identifies this level of self-awareness and care about one's presentation as a marker of the *talmid chacham* whose word can be trusted regarding lost objects.

This is a striking combination of assumptions. On one hand, the underlying hope — not always realized — is that devotion to Torah leads to greater focus on *middot*, character, and honesty. On the other hand, there is the claim that attention to one's external presentation is somehow connected to that integrity. One might reasonably object that people can be very careful about how they appear without that reflecting anything about their actual character. Indeed, the *gemara* elsewhere offers a more intuitive criterion for this category of trust: someone who has never been *meshaneh b'diburo* — who never misrepresents the truth except in the three cases the *gemara* itself permits for the sake of peace. That seems a more direct measure of the integrity at stake when returning lost objects. The assumption that careful dress correlates with honesty, while interesting, is clearly not airtight.

### Community Support

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yochanan: eizehu talmid chacham she-b'nei iro metzuvim la'asot lo melachto?* — who is the Torah scholar that the people of his town are obligated to perform his work for him, so that he may devote himself entirely to his study? *Ze she-maniach cheftzo v'osek b'cheftzei shamayim* — someone who completely abandons his own pursuits and devotes himself purely to heavenly matters.

The *gemara* qualifies: *v'hani mili le-mipshat b'riftah* — and even this obligation extends only to providing him with bread. It does not entail a fine house or luxury provisions — only basic sustenance. This *gemara* connects to a much larger debate. The *Rambam* famously and vigorously opposed the institutionalization of communal support for Torah scholars who did not work, noting how many of *Chazal* maintained professions. He viewed the social pressure to donate to such support as highly problematic. At the same time, the practice of supporting individuals devoted entirely to Torah has a long history in many communities. The phrase *maniach cheftzo ve'osek becheftzei Shamayim* could be read to mean not merely that one devotes considerable hours to learning, but that one never leaves the *beit midrash* for any other purpose. In any event, the *gemara* is clear that even if such support is warranted, the community's obligation extends to *riftah* — bread — and no more.

### Rabbinic Leadership

*Ve'amar Rabbi Yochanan: eizehu talmid chacham she-memanin oto parnas al ha-tzibbur?* — who is the Torah scholar that the community is prepared to appoint as *parnas* over them? While *parnas* often denotes a lay leader, in this context it almost certainly refers to a rabbinic leader. *Ze she-sholin oto d'var halacha b'chol makom v'omer* — someone who can be asked a halachic question from anywhere and can answer it immediately, *va'afilu b'masechet kallah* — even from *Masechet Kallah*, a relatively small and obscure tractate dealing with *sheva brachot* and matters associated with the wedding.

*Tosafot*, however, attempts to limit this standard on the basis of a later *gemara*, arguing that the requirement is not encyclopedic knowledge of all of *Shas* but expertise in one area — even if that area is as limited as *Masechet Kallah*. That would be a significantly different reading from *Rashi*'s, which understands the standard at face value.

The *gemara* subsequently revisits this question — *kol she'shoalin oto halacha bechol makom ve'omrah, lemai nafka mina? leminuyei parnas al hatzibur* — and qualifies it: *ei bechada masechta, be'asreih* — if he is a master of one tractate, he can be appointed as a local rabbinic authority in his own community. *Ei bechulah tenuyei, bereish metivta* — if he is knowledgeable across the entirety of *Shas*, he can be appointed at the level of *rosh metivta*, a broader regional authority. This graduated standard is what *Tosafot* had in mind when softening the seemingly absolute demand of the original formulation.

At this point, *Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish* returns the discussion to where it began — the question of what type of garment is meant by the ruling that one side of a stain suffices. He says: *elu keilim ha'ulyarin haba'in mimedinat hayam* — these are the bath attendants' robes that come from overseas. *Rashi* explains that these are the special robes one puts on immediately after bathing, which come from overseas and are particularly fine. Since one puts them on having just washed, even a single-sided stain is objectionable.

The *gemara* then asks: *lemeimra dechivari ninhu* — presumably you are saying these are white garments, since stains would show most clearly on white. But *Rabbi Yannai* told his sons: *al tikberuni lo becheilim levanim velo becheilim shechorim* — do not bury me in white clothes or in black clothes. *Levanim* — white — because if he does not merit and goes to *Gehinnom*, he will look like a bridegroom among mourners. *Shechorim* — black — because if he does merit and ascends to a higher place, he will look like a mourner among grooms. *Ela becheilim ha'ulyarin haba'in mimedinat hayam* — rather, bury me in these bath attendants' robes from overseas. The implication is that these robes are not white. *Alma sumkei ninhu* — they must be reddish or some other color. The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha biglimei ha belivushei* — the inner garment is presumably white while the outer cloak is a darker color, and the ruling about one-sided stains applies specifically to the outer garments.

## Yom Kippur & Shabbat

### Can YK Serve Shabbat

Having completed the extensive digression about *Shabbat* clothes and Torah scholars, the *gemara* returns to the *mishnah*, which dealt with the relationship between *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* — specifically whether one may prepare on *Yom Kippur* for *Shabbat* and vice versa, and whether the fats of sacrifices from one sanctified day may be burned on the other. *Rabbi Ishmael* held that *Yom Kippur* can serve *Shabbat* — that is, preparations or offerings may carry over from *Yom Kippur* to *Shabbat* — while *Rabbi Akiva* disagreed and said neither day can serve the other.

## Yom Kippur & Shabbat (II)

### BeShabbato Limit

*Tano Rabbanan: Olah Shabbat beShabbato* — the verse regarding the *Shabbat olah* says "in its *Shabbat*," which is read as permitting the fats of *Shabbat* to be offered on *Yom Kippur*, since *Yom Kippur* is the next day and is itself called *Shabbat*. The question is whether this can work in reverse — can the fats of *Yom Kippur* be burned on *Shabbat*? *Talmud lomar beShabbato* — the limiting word *beShabbato* forecloses that direction.

### Kedusha Comparison

The reasoning behind the asymmetry is that even though *Yom Kippur* is called *Shabbat Shabbaton* — doubly sanctified, as a *Shabbat* from *melacha* and a *Shabbat* from eating and physical pleasures — the *kedusha* of the weekly *Shabbat* is nonetheless considered higher, as demonstrated by the severity of its punishment. Violation of *Shabbat* carries the death penalty, whereas violation of *Yom Kippur* carries *kares*. Therefore, when a choice must be made, *Yom Kippur* serves *Shabbat* and not the reverse.

This is the view of *Rabbi Ishmael*. *Rabbi Akiva* disagrees and reads *beShabbato* as limiting each day to serving only itself: *Olah Shabbat beShabbato* — the *Shabbat olah* remains in its own *Shabbat*. What then does the verse come to permit? *Limed al chelvei Shabbat shekravin beYom Tov* — it teaches that fats of *Shabbat* can be offered on an ensuing *Yom Tov*, not on *Yom Kippur*. *Yakhol af beYom HaKippurim? Talmud lomar beShabbato* — one might think even *Yom Kippur*, but the verse limits: only in its own *Shabbat*.

The underlying debate, then, is over which secondary day the verse is permitting. For *Rabbi Ishmael*, the baseline assumption is that *nedarim u'nedavot krevim beYom Tov* — free-will offerings can be brought on *Yom Tov* — and this is already known without a verse. Therefore, when the verse says something can serve *Shabbat*, it must be adding something new: even *Yom Kippur*. For *Rabbi Akiva*, *nedarim u'nedavot ein krevim beYom Tov* — free-will offerings cannot be brought on *Yom Tov* — so the verse is needed precisely to establish that *Yom Tov* may serve *Shabbat* in this way; it does not extend to *Yom Kippur*.

The connection to *Eruv Tavshilin* is worth noting: cooking on *Yom Tov* for *Shabbat* is itself a biblical act of preparation, and it is *Rabbi Akiva* who anchors the permissibility of *Yom Tov* serving *Shabbat* in this *pasuk*.

### Tekiah Debate

*Rabbi Zeira* said: when I was in *Bavel*, I used to assume that the following *Baraita* would be agreed upon by everyone: if *Yom Kippur* falls on a Friday, one does not sound the *shofar* — since everyone is already refraining from *melacha*, the usual signal to stop work before *Shabbat* is superfluous. And if *Yom Kippur* falls on a Sunday, one does not make *havdalah* between *Shabbat* and *Yom Kippur* — since they share essentially the same level of *kedusha* and the same prohibition of *melacha*, there is no meaningful distinction requiring formal separation. (One does make *havdalah bein kodesh le-kodesh* between two *Yom Tovim*, but the latter is only *d'rabbanan*, whereas *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* share effectively the same standing.)

When *Rabbi Zeira* made *aliyah* to *Eretz Yisrael*, however, he found *Yehuda* the son of *Rabbi Shimon ben Pazi* sitting and teaching otherwise: that ruling applies only according to *Rabbi Akiva*, who holds that *Yom Kippur* cannot serve *Shabbat* in any way. But according to *Rabbi Ishmael*, who permits the fats of *Shabbat* to be offered on *Yom Kippur*, there is a meaningful halachic distinction between the two days — and therefore when *Shabbat* leads into *Yom Kippur*, one should perform a *tekiah* to signal that distinction and alert people that the fats of *Shabbat* may be burned on *Yom Kippur*.

### Shofar Purpose

*Rabbi Zeira* responded: *kohanim zrizim hem* — the *kohanim* are already careful and knowledgeable; they do not require a *shofar* blast to inform them of this nuance. A *tekiah* would only be warranted for something relevant to the broader public.

*Mar Kushisha brei d'Rav Chisda* challenged *Rav Ashi*: do we really say that *kohanim* do not need such signals? The *mishnah* records that three *shofar* blasts were sounded in the *Beit HaMikdash* — three to signal people to stop working and come in from the fields, and three to signal that *Shabbat* itself had arrived and all *melacha* must cease. If these blasts were sounded in the *Beit HaMikdash*, presumably even the *kohanim* needed them.

The *gemara* answers by invoking *Abaye*'s statement in a different context — where the menorah of *Helni HaMalka* in the *Beit HaMikdash* served as a signal for the time of *Shema*, and the *gemara* explained that the *kohanim* themselves said *Shema* before sunrise, so the signal was *l'sha'ar ama d'vi-Yerushalayim* — for all the other residents of *Yerushalayim*. Similarly here: *hacha nami l'sha'ar ama d'vi-Yerushalayim* — the *shofar* blasts in the *Beit HaMikdash* were for the general population, not for the *kohanim* themselves, who were sufficiently expert to know these *halachot* without a public announcement.

## Knivat Yerek

### Trimming Allowance

This discussion of the relationship between *Yom Kippur* and *Shabbat* concludes with a notable practical detail. One thing permitted on *Yom Kippur* that is not permitted on *Shabbat* is *knivat yerek* — trimming vegetables, the removal of leaves from produce. The exact nature of the prohibition on *Shabbat* is not entirely clear; it appears to be *d'rabbanan*, perhaps related to *borer* or a similar category.

On *Yom Kippur*, this trimming is permitted starting from *mincha* time onward — *min hamincha u'lemala* — on account of *agmas nefesh*. *Rashi* interestingly interprets this in the opposite direction from what one might expect: rather than alleviating distress, it is permitted in order to *increase* the sense of *agmas nefesh* — wetting one's appetite as the fast draws near to its end, heightening awareness of the deprivation. The note on the side of the *gemara* offers a different explanation: it is permitted so that one need not spend time after *Yom Kippur* engaged in this preparation, which would needlessly extend the effective experience of the fast.

### YK on Shabbat

When *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, is the trimming of vegetables still permitted? *Rav Zeira* said in the name of *Rav Huna*, who said in the name of *Rav Yehuda*, who said in the name of *Rav Ami*, who said in the name of *Rav Huna*: *assur b'knivas yereq* — it is forbidden. The *Shabbat* character of the day overrides the leniency permitted on *Yom Kippur*.

*Rav Mana* then cites a *Baraita*: from where do we know that when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat* one may not trim vegetables? *Talmud lomar Shabbaton Shevus* — the verse employs the doubled language of rest. The *drasha* proceeds: *ileima l'melacha v'kasiv lo sa'aseh kol melacha* — if you say this doubled language is only about *melacha*, we already have an explicit verse forbidding *melacha*; therefore the doubled language must be coming to address *knivat yerek*.

*Tosafot* is quick to note that this is evidently an *asmachta* — a rabbinic support adduced from a biblical verse, not a genuine biblical prohibition — since if the prohibition were *d'Oraita*, it would apply on *Yom Kippur* itself for any purpose, which it does not.

*Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba* then says in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*: *Yom Kippur shechal lihyos b'Shabbos muttar b'knivas yereq* — when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, the trimming of vegetables is in fact *permitted*. The *gemara* challenges this from the *Baraita* just cited, which assumed it was forbidden. The *gemara* responds: *lo l'olam l'melacha* — the doubled language of *Shabbaton Shevus* is not addressing *knivat yerek* at all; it is simply adding an additional positive and negative prohibition regarding *melacha* itself, establishing that refraining from *melacha* on *Shabbat* involves both a *lav* and an *aseh*. *Tanya k'vosei d'Rabbi Yochanan* — a *Baraita* is then cited in support of *Rabbi Yochanan*: when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*, *muttar b'knivas yereq* — one is permitted to trim vegetables.

### Nuts & Seeds

The *Baraita* continues: *maftzin b'egozim umfarchisin b'rimonim min hamincha u'l'ma'ala mipnei agmas nefesh* — one may also crack nuts and remove pomegranate seeds starting from *mincha* time on, by reason of *agmas nefesh*. This applies whether it is a regular *Yom Kippur* or, according to *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position, when *Yom Kippur* falls on *Shabbat*.

### Rava's Response

The *gemara* reports that in the household of *Rav Yehuda* they would trim cabbage on *Yom Kippur*. In *Rava*'s household they would scrape gourds. However, when *Rava* observed that people — likely his household members or servants — were beginning to do this too early, before *mincha* time, he said to them: *asa igresa mi'ma'arava mishemei d'Rabbi Yochanan d'assur* — a letter has just arrived from *Eretz Yisrael* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* that it is forbidden. He felt they were abusing the permitted leniency, and he responded by invoking an alternative ruling — whether or not he truly held by it — to put a stop to the premature preparation.

*Hadran alach v'eilu k'sharim.* A new *perek* begins tomorrow.
