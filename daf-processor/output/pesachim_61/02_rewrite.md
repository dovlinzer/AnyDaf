# Pesachim 61 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shelo Lishmo Overview

### Pesach Shelo Lishmo Intro

The daf opens at the bottom of 60b, where the Gemara is completing its treatment of the *mishnah* dealing with the *korban pesach* slaughtered *shelo lishmo* — not for its proper designation. The *korban pesach* is one of only two *korbanot* for which *shelo lishmo* renders the sacrifice invalid; the other is the *chatat*. The Gemara has not yet, at this point in the sugya, provided the derivation for why *shelo lishmo* creates this invalidity, but the conceptual logic is apparent: the entire purpose of the *korban pesach* is to be eaten by its designated owners, and — similar to the *chatat*, which is bound up with the personal atonement of its bringer — the identity of the sacrifice is inextricably tied to the intention with which it is brought. Whatever its theoretical underpinnings, it is established that *shelo lishmo* invalidates, and the Gemara has been working through a series of associated questions, including the ruling that even a partial *shelo lishmo* intention suffices to invalidate.

### Pesach Off-Season Q

The Gemara now raises a new question: *Pesach sheshachto bish'ar yemot hashanah bishinuy be'alim mahu?* — If one slaughtered a *korban pesach* during the rest of the year — say, on Purim — with a *shinuy be'alim*, a change of owners, what is the law?

To appreciate the question, some background is necessary. We have already learned that what invalidates the *pesach* on the fourteenth of Nisan can, paradoxically, validate it when brought at the wrong time of year. If one brings a *korban pesach* on Purim for the sake of a *shelamim*, designating it as a different type of offering altogether, it becomes valid as a *shelamim*. If, however, one brings it on Purim still intending it as a *pesach*, it remains a *pesach* and is therefore invalid — a *korban pesach* has no place outside its proper time.

We have also learned that, as will be developed in the next *mishnah*, slaughtering the *pesach* for the wrong owners — *shelo limnuav*, not for the group counted on it — is itself an invalidity. By other *korbanot*, a change of owners (*shinuy be'alim*) does not invalidate the *korban* outright; it merely fails to provide atonement or credit to the rightful owner. By the *korban pesach*, however, it invalidates entirely.

The question, then, is whether this parallel holds in the off-season as well: just as *shinuy kodesh* — slaughtering it as a different type of *korban* — transforms the *pesach* into a valid *shelamim* on Purim, does *shinuy be'alim* — slaughtering it for the wrong owners — similarly transform it into something valid? There is, as the Gemara immediately senses, an obvious conceptual difficulty: it is one thing to designate a *pesach* as a *shelamim* and thereby actively alter its *korban* identity; it is quite another to claim that intending different owners somehow performs the same transformation.

### Rav Papa Analogy

*Amar Rav Papa* — Rav Papa reports that he presented this discussion before Rava. Notably, he says he "told over the *shema'ta*" — the discussion — not merely the question, implying the matter had been under active debate, though no definitive answer had yet emerged. Rav Papa's own proposal was as follows: *hoil veshinuy kodesh posel bizmano veshinuy be'alim posel bizmano* — since *shinuy kodesh* invalidates the *pesach* on the fourteenth and *shinuy be'alim* likewise invalidates it on the fourteenth, perhaps the same parallel holds in the positive direction. Just as *shinuy kodesh* which invalidates on the fourteenth *machshir shelo bizmano* — validates when it is not the fourteenth — so too *shinuy be'alim* which invalidates on the fourteenth should validate *shelo bizmano*, on Purim. By this reasoning, intending the wrong owners on Purim would transform the *pesach* into a valid offering.

## Rava vs Rav Papa

### Identity vs Ownership

Rava rejected Rav Papa's analogy outright. *Im amarta beshinuy kodesh* — if you wish to say this of *shinuy kodesh*, the reason it has the power to transform the *pesach* into a *shelamim* off-season is *sheken poslo begufo* — it strikes at the very identity of the *korban* itself. When the *kohen* declares or intends a different type of sacrifice, he is making a statement about what this offering fundamentally *is*. That intention, because it is so directly constitutive of the *korban*'s identity, carries the power to reshape it when brought at a time when a *pesach* has no place. *Shinuy be'alim* — the owners — is by contrast more tangential to the *korban*'s essential nature. Ownership is secondary; the identity of the sacrifice as a *pesach*, a *shelamim*, or a *chatat* is primary. This is Rava's first distinction.

### Four Avodos Debate

Rava's second distinction: *yeshno be-arba avodos* — *shinuy kodesh* creates invalidity at any of the four *avodos*: *shechita*, receiving the blood, conveying the blood, and *zerika*. The concern about what type of *korban* is being offered is relevant from the very first act of service, because the identity of the *korban* is at stake from the moment it is slaughtered. *Shinuy be'alim*, by contrast, is only relevant at *zerikat hadam*. The reason for this limitation will be developed further below, but the point here is structural: *shinuy kodesh* is pervasive across all stages of the *avodah*, whereas *shinuy be'alim* is restricted to one. This asymmetry reflects that *shinuy kodesh* is a more deeply rooted invalidity.

### After Death Analysis

Rava's third distinction: *ve-yeshno le-achar misa* — *shinuy kodesh* applies even after the owner's death, whereas *shinuy be'alim* does not. This requires some unpacking.

It is a remarkable *halacha* that if an individual obligated himself to bring a *korban* — saying *harei alai*, committing to bring, say, a *shelamim* — and he dies before fulfilling that obligation, the obligation survives him. The *Beis Hamikdash*, as it were, holds a lien against his estate, much like an unpaid creditor. His heirs must bring the *korban* from the estate. This is one of those areas of *halacha* where the relationship between a person and God is translated into concrete legal-financial obligations — the *Beis Hamikdash* operates not merely as a spiritual institution but as an entity capable of holding real claims on property.

Now, if the *kohen* slaughters that posthumous *shelamim* with an intention of the wrong *korban* type — thinking *chatat* rather than *shelamim* — this constitutes *shinuy kodesh* and invalidates even though the owner is dead. The estate will be liable to bring a replacement. The identity of the *korban* type is so fundamental that it remains relevant regardless of whether the person for whom it is brought is alive.

By contrast, according to the mainstream position, *shinuy be'alim* does not apply after the owner's death. If someone dies and leaves behind a *shelamim*, and the *kohen* performs the *zerika* thinking about different owners than the deceased, there is a position that this does not invalidate. Why? Because the concept of *shinuy be'alim* — whose *korban* this is, who receives the personal credit — is less meaningful once the owner has died. The *kapara* dimension of *korbanot* is a living person's concern; *lo ha-meisim yehallelu yah* — the dead do not praise God. The estate's obligation remains, and the *korban* is valid as a discharge of that obligation, but the personal ownership dimension is attenuated. The *shelamim* or *olah* in this context functions more as a debt being paid than as a personal religious act. This stands in contrast to the *chatat*, which is not brought after death at all, precisely because its *kapara* is an entirely personal matter.

### Tzibbur Exemption

Rava's fourth distinction: *ve-yeshno betzibbur ke-viyachid* — *shinuy kodesh* applies equally to a *korban tzibbur* as to a private *korban yachid*. If a *kohen* slaughters the *tamid* or *musaf* intending it as a *shelamim*, he has invalidated it. But *shinuy be'alim* does not apply by a *korban tzibbur*. One might think that by intending the *tamid* for a specific individual — intending it for Michael rather than for the *tzibbur* — one has committed *shinuy be'alim*. But the *halacha* is that this does not invalidate. Since Michael is himself a member of the *tzibbur*, he is inextricably linked to the community, and no individual designation can sever that connection. The *tzibbur*'s *korban* is, by its nature, everyone's *korban*. *Shinuy kodesh*, by contrast, cuts at what the *korban is*, and no degree of communal belonging can override that.

So Rava has offered four ways in which *shinuy kodesh* is a more weighty and pervasive invalidity than *shinuy be'alim*: it operates on the *korban*'s essential identity, it applies at all four *avodos*, it applies even after the owner's death, and it applies to *korbanot tzibbur* as well as private ones. Given these differences, the analogy Rav Papa drew — that whatever *shinuy kodesh* does in the off-season, *shinuy be'alim* should do as well — fails to persuade.

One might also ask, tangentially, why the *kohen*'s error is not the *Beis Hamikdash*'s problem rather than the individual owner's — a question that touches on whether causing non-physical damage (*hezek she'eino nikar*) creates liability, a matter discussed in Gittin and elsewhere. The answer the tradition gives is simply that concrete legal consequences flow from concrete acts, regardless of how we might intuit moral responsibility to fall.

### Tartei Miyadki

The Gemara, however, does not simply accept all four of Rava's distinctions. It notes: *af al gav d'tartei lav davka* — although Rava offered four distinctions, two of them are not so precise. Only two of the four hold up well as distinctions.

The first distinction — that *shinuy kodesh* is *poslo begufo*, it strikes at the *korban*'s intrinsic identity, while *shinuy be'alim* does not — the Gemara challenges as follows: *depsul machshava be'alma hi* — but changing the *korban* type is also "merely" a matter of *machshava*, of mental intention. You haven't done anything physically different to the animal. And the same is true of *shinuy be'alim*. Both are purely matters of thought. One cannot say one is more "intrinsic" than the other on this basis.

This is, frankly, a somewhat surprising move by the Gemara. The intuitive force of the distinction seems clear: a thought that pertains to *what the korban is* seems more constitutive of its identity than a thought about *whose korban it is*. If the very purpose of the off-season question is whether the wrong-owner intention can transform the *korban*'s status — that is, whether it can strip it of its *pesach* identity — then surely the more relevant consideration is whether the thought pertains to identity. A thought that says "this is a *shelamim*" speaks more directly to that question than a thought that says "this belongs to Rivka." The Gemara's dismissal of this distinction seems overly formalistic, but it stands.

The second imprecise distinction — that *shinuy be'alim* does not apply after death — is also challenged. *U'lerav Pinchas brei d'Rav Ami*, for Rav Pinchas son of Rav Ami, who holds that *shinuy be'alim does* apply even after the owner's death, there is no distinction here at all. So on that view, the third of Rava's distinctions dissolves.

### Rav Pinchas Opinion

Rav Pinchas son of Rav Ami maintains that *yesh shinuy be'alim le-achar misa* — *shinuy be'alim* does apply even after the owner's death. On his view, the dimension of personal ownership remains relevant even posthumously, and therefore performing the *zerika* for the wrong owner even after the original owner has died constitutes a valid *shinuy be'alim* that invalidates the *korban*. The Gemara cites this opinion to show that Rava's third distinction is contested and cannot be relied upon as a firm demarcation between the two invalidities.

The Gemara concludes that two of the four distinctions remain solid: the distinction regarding the four *avodos* versus only *zerika*, and the distinction regarding *korban tzibbur*. These two — that *shinuy kodesh* applies across all four *avodos* while *shinuy be'alim* is limited to *zerika*, and that *shinuy kodesh* affects *korbanot tzibbur* while *shinuy be'alim* does not — are genuinely precise distinctions (*miyadki*) that demonstrate the greater weight and pervasiveness of *shinuy kodesh*.

## Rava's Solution

### Pesach Without Owner

Having rejected Rav Papa's analogy — though unable to sustain all four of his distinctions — Rava articulates his own position on the original question. *Pesach sheshachato bish'ar yemot hashanah bishinuy be'alim* — a *pesach* slaughtered during the rest of the year with a *shinuy be'alim* — *na'aseh kemi she-ein lo be'alim bizmano u-fasul*: it becomes like a *pesach* that has no owners at its proper time, and it is invalid.

Rava's point is subtle and important. He is not saying that *shinuy be'alim* transforms the *pesach* into a valid *shelamim*, as *shinuy kodesh* would. Rather, because *shinuy be'alim* is not about the *korban*'s identity but only about ownership, all it accomplishes is severing the *korban* from its rightful owners. The result is not a newly valid offering of a different type — it is a *pesach* that has been stripped of its owners. And a *pesach* without owners is invalid.

### No Transformation

This is the crucial difference between *shinuy kodesh* and *shinuy be'alim* in the off-season context. *Shinuy kodesh* — intending the *pesach* as a *shelamim* — actively reconstitutes the *korban*'s identity. It says: this is now a *shelamim*, and a *shelamim* on Purim is perfectly valid. The transformation is real and effective. *Shinuy be'alim*, by contrast, does not reconstitute the *korban* as anything. It merely removes the owners. The *korban* is still a *pesach* — but now a *pesach* without an owner — and that is not a valid *shelamim*. There has been no transformation of identity, only a negation of ownership. And so, contra Rav Papa, *shinuy be'alim* off-season does not produce a valid *korban*. It produces an invalid, ownerless *pesach*.

## Shechita Invalidities

### Wrong Eaters/Owners

The Gemara moves to the *mishnah* that deals with the proper eaters and designated owners of the *korban pesach*. Before turning to the *mishnah* itself, a clarifying word on the structure of these invalidities is in order. We have already encountered the concept of *shelo limnuav* — slaughtering the *pesach* for the wrong designated group — and now the *mishnah* introduces the parallel concept of *shelo le'ochlav*, slaughtering it for those who are not valid eaters. Crucially, both of these invalidities are weaker than *shelo lishmo*: a partially mixed intention of *shelo lishmo* invalidates, but a partially mixed *shelo le'ochlav* or *shelo limnuav* does not.

The *mishnah* states: *shachato shelo le'ochlav* — if one slaughtered it for those who cannot eat from it, such as the elderly, the ill, the *arelim* (uncircumcised), or the *temeim* (ritually impure) — *pasul*, it is invalid. Similarly, *shelo limnuav* — for those not counted among the designated group — is invalid. But a mixed intention — some valid eaters and some invalid, some designated owners and some not, some circumcised and some not, some *tamei* and some *tahor* — *kasher*, it remains valid. This is a significant leniency relative to *shelo lishmo*, where even a partial wrong intention invalidates.

### Shechita vs Zerika

The *mishnah* is entirely about the moment of *shechita*. This is distinctive and requires emphasis. The requirement that the *pesach* be slaughtered for its proper eaters — *shelo le'ochlav* — is a rule unique to the *korban pesach* and applies specifically at *shechita*. The source is the verse *ish lefi ochlo takhosu* — each person according to his ability to eat, you shall slaughter — and since *takhosu* refers to the act of slaughter, the concern about proper eaters attaches to *shechita*.

*Shinuy be'alim* — the wrong owners — by other *korbanot* is relevant at *zerika*, since *kapara*, the atonement credit, is achieved through the throwing of the blood. For the *korban pesach*, however, wrong owners can also invalidate at *shechita*, as an additional *pesach*-specific requirement. But it is critical to keep these two dimensions separate: *shelo le'ochlav* (wrong eaters) is exclusively a *pesach* concern and applies only at *shechita*. *Shinuy be'alim* (wrong owners) is a general *korban* concern, relevant at *zerika* for all *korbanot*, and additionally at *shechita* for the *pesach* alone.

A practical implication of all this, noted somewhat wryly: the safest posture for a *kohen* during the *avodah* is simply not to think about anything extraneous. No positive intention is required; only wrong intention invalidates. The right advice is to focus on performing the *avodah* correctly and not to let the mind wander to owners, eaters, or alternative sacrifice types.

### Mixed Intention OK

As the *mishnah* makes clear, mixed intentions — *le'ochlav v'shelo le'ochlav*, *limnuav v'shelo limnuav*, *lemulim v'le'arelim*, *le'temeim u'le'tehorim* — are all *kasher*. The invalidity of *shelo le'ochlav* and *shelo limnuav* requires an exclusively wrong intention. Any admixture of valid eaters or valid owners preserves the *korban*'s validity.

The *mishnah* then continues with timing-related rules: *shachato kodem chatzos*, if slaughtered before midday, is invalid, since the verse specifies *bein ha'arbayim*, between the afternoons. But *shachato kodem latamid kasher* — if slaughtered before the afternoon *tamid* but after midday, it is valid *bedi'avad*. In that case, one should ideally stir the blood to prevent coagulation and arrange to perform the *zerika* only after the *tamid*'s blood has been thrown — *u'vilvad she'echad memares b'damo ad sheyizarek hatamid*. And even if the *zerika* was performed before the *tamid*, it remains valid. This is one of the notable distinctions of the *pesach*: it is essentially the only *korban* whose valid offering window is restricted to a specific part of the day.

## Examples & Proofs

### Wrong Eater Types

The Gemara provides examples of *shelo le'ochlav*: a *korban pesach* slaughtered for the sake of a sick person who cannot eat, or an elderly person who is physically unable to consume the meat. These are cases where the intended eaters are part of the group but are incapable of eating a *kezayit*, and therefore the slaughter on their behalf is *shelo le'ochlav*.

### Wrong Chavura

An example of *shelo limnuav*: one group was counted in for a particular *pesach*, and the *kohen* slaughtered it with in mind a different group altogether — *minu alav chavura zo u'shachato l'shem chavura acheret*. Since the group that was designated did not match the group the *kohen* had in mind, this constitutes *shelo limnuav* and invalidates.

### Group Designation

The Gemara asks: from where do we know that the *pesach* must be slaughtered specifically for those counted on it, such that failure to do so invalidates? The verse says *mishkhu vekachu lakhem ish seh leveit avot* — take for yourselves, a man for his father's household — emphasizing the group designation. And it says *bemikhsat nefashot*, according to those numbered on it. One might think that slaughtering it *shelo limnuav* is merely a violation of a procedural requirement, but the *korban* itself remains valid — as is often the assumption with *korbanot*, where the default position is that requirements not explicitly stated as blocking validity are merely *lekhatkhila* preferences. The Torah is generally understood by *Chazal* to need to repeat a requirement in order to establish it as *me'akev*, as actually blocking validity.

The Gemara answers: *talmud lomar bemikhsat takhosu* — the double language of *bemikhsat* combined with *takhosu* teaches that this is *me'akev*. The repetition signals that slaughtering for a non-designated group is not merely procedurally improper but actually invalidating.

*Rebbi* offers an alternative reading of *takhosu*. He holds it is not a Hebrew word meaning "you shall be numbered upon it" but rather a Syriac (*sursi*) expression — *lashon sursi hu* — meaning to slaughter, as in the idiom *kos li taleh zeh*, "slaughter this lamb for me." On his reading, *bemikhsat takhosu* means: according to the number, you shall slaughter — i.e., slaughter according to the designated eaters. Rashi notes that the Sages refer to this language as *arami*, Aramaic, and that the scholars of other nations call it *Lingua Suryaya*, Syriac, a dialect of Aramaic. *Rebbi* does not dispute that the emphasis requires the act to be *me'akev*; he simply parses the word differently, reading *takhosu* as the slaughter itself rather than as a second act of designation.

### Eating Requirement

For *shelo le'ochlav* — wrong eaters — the source is: *ish lefi ochlo takhosu* — each person according to his ability to eat, you shall slaughter. The verse places the eaters at the center of the slaughter act. *Hekish ochlim limnuyim* — the verse equates the eaters with the designated members of the group — drawing a parallel such that just as being designated is a requirement at *shechita*, so too being a valid eater is a requirement at *shechita*. Both failures therefore invalidate at the same moment and in the same manner.

## Shechita/Zerika Thought

### Mixed Timing Q

The Gemara now poses a subtler question. It has been established that *shelo le'ochlav* invalidates only at *shechita*, and only by the *korban pesach*. But what if a *kohen* slaughters the *pesach* for the circumcised members of the group — a valid *shechita* — with the explicit intention that when it comes time for the *zerika*, he will perform it for the benefit of the uncircumcised? He has not done anything wrong at *shechita* itself, in the sense that his designees at *shechita* are valid eaters. But he has, during *shechita*, entertained a thought about *zerika* that contemplates wrong eaters.

The question is whether this *machshava* — a thought about *zerika* at the moment of *shechita* concerning those who cannot eat — suffices to invalidate.

### Rav Chisda: Invalid

*Rav Chisda amar pasul* — Rav Chisda holds the *korban* is invalid. His reasoning, as developed by *Tosafot*: if one had the thought of wrong eaters at *zerika* alone — that is, with no attendant thought during *shechita* — everyone would agree there is no problem. *Shelo le'ochlav* is not a *zerika* concern; it attaches to *shechita*. But since the thought is occurring *during shechita*, a thought about *zerika* contemplating wrong eaters is sufficient to invalidate. The thought happens at the operative moment; that it is directed at a future *avoda* does not remove its disqualifying force.

### Rava: Only Shechita

*Rava amar kasher* — Rava holds the *korban* is valid. His position: the invalidity of *shelo le'ochlav* is tethered entirely to the *shechita*. It is only a thought *at shechita* and *about shechita* that can trigger this invalidity. A thought at *shechita* that is directed at *zerika* is simply not the relevant category of *machshava*. The verses that generate this invalidity speak of *shechita*; the act of *zerika* is a separate legal context. Bridging them, in Rava's view, does not work.

## Arelis Braita

### One Arel Q

The Gemara now introduces a *braita* that bears on the question of whether the presence of a single *arel* in a group — or a thought involving even one *arel* — suffices to invalidate, or whether the entire group must be *arelim*. The *braita* begins: *yachol yifs'lu bnei chavura habaim imo* — perhaps the members of the group who accompany the *arel* are all invalidated by his presence? Perhaps the very fact that one member of the group cannot eat is enough to invalidate the entire *korban*?

### Two Comparisons

The *braita* acknowledges that *arlah* (being uncircumcised) invalidates the *pesach* for the uncircumcised individual, and *tumah* invalidates for the *tamei* individual. It now asks which of two possible analogies should govern the *arel* case.

First comparison: *ma tumah lo asa bah miktzat tumah k'chol tumah* — just as by *tumah*, having some *temeim* in the group does not make a partial *tumah* equivalent to total *tumah* (i.e., a mixed intention of *temeim* and *tehorim* does not invalidate), so too by *arlah*, a partial group of *arelim* should not invalidate the whole. This analogy is compelling because both *tumah* and *arlah* are disqualifications specific to the *korban pesach* — neither renders a person incapable of having a *korban* brought on their behalf for *other* sacrifices.

Second comparison: *hu b'arlah poselet v'zman posel* — but *arlah* invalidates and an improper time (*piggul*) invalidates. And by *piggul*, *ma zman asa bo miktzat zman k'chol zman* — a partial intention of eating at the wrong time — even just a *kezayit* worth — renders the entire *korban* *piggul*. If so, *af arlah*, even thinking about just some *arelim* should invalidate.

### Full Group Only

The *braita* itself resolves the question. It reasons: *danim davar she'eino noheg b'chol hazvachim midavar she'eino noheg b'chol hazvachim* — we derive the law of *arelim* from *temeim* because both of these are disqualifications that do not apply to other sacrifices. Being *tamei* or being an *arel* does not prevent a *korban* from being brought for you by other types of sacrifice; it is specifically the *korban pesach* — whose validity is entirely tied up with the eating — that cannot be brought for one who cannot eat. Therefore, the correct analogy is to *tumah*: just as a mixed group of *temeim* and *tehorim* does not invalidate, so too a mixed group of *arelim* and *mulim* does not invalidate. *V'al yochiach piggul* — the *piggul* analogy does not apply, because *piggul* — the intention to eat at the wrong time — is relevant to all *korbanot*, making it a categorically stronger type of invalidity that cannot serve as a model here.

The *braita* then addresses the possibility of comparing *arelim* to *piggul* from a different angle: *danim davar shelo hutra m'klalo midavar shelo hutra m'klalo* — both *arlah* and improper time are absolute prohibitions with no exceptions. But the *braita* counters: *v'al tochiach tumah, she'hutra m'klalah* — we should not prove from *tumah*, because *tumah* is sometimes permitted in context. *Tumah hutrah b'tzibbur* — communal *tumah* is waived for communal sacrifices. Since *tumah* has exceptions, the logic runs, and yet the *halacha* by *tumah* is that a partial group of *temeim* does not invalidate, that is precisely why *arlah* should follow suit. So the conclusion stands: only a full group of *arelim* — if everyone in the group is an *arel* — invalidates.

## Rava's Zos Reading

### Zos Exclusion

*Amar Rava mna amina lah* — Rava asks, where do I derive my position that a thought about wrong eaters at the moment of *zerika* is irrelevant to the *pesach*? He turns to the *braita* just cited and reads into its conclusion.

After the *braita* establishes that a full group of *arelim* invalidates while a partial one does not, it cites the verse *zos chukat hapesach* — *this* is the statute of the *pesach*. The *braita* uses this *zos* to anchor its ruling. Rava asks: *mai zos?* — what work is *zos* doing here? If it is simply proving that a full group of *arelim* invalidates and a partial one does not, that derivation is already available from the verse *kol arel lo yochal bo* — which can be read as *kulloh arlah*, that the entirety of the group must be *arelim* for the invalidity to apply. Why invoke *zos* at all?

Rava answers: *ella lav hachi katana* — the *braita* must be teaching something additional. *V'chol arel* provides the proof that a partial group of *arelim* does not invalidate at *shechita*. *Zos* then excludes *zerika* from this entire discussion. *Bishchita hu d'chulla arlah mi pasla* — it is specifically at *shechita* that the full-group *arlah* rule applies. *Aval zerika, afilu kulam nami lo pasla* — but at *zerika*, even if the entire group consists of *arelim*, it does not invalidate. And by implication, a *shechita* thought directed at *zerika* is equally irrelevant: *zos* — *this*, meaning the *shechita* — is the only domain in which the *arlah* concern operates.

### Shechita Unique

Rava's reading finds support in the structure of the relevant verses. All the Torah's language about *ochlin*, eaters, and the slaughter of the *pesach* — *ish lefi ochlo takhosu*, *bemikhsat nefashot* — is anchored to the act of *shechita*. The entire edifice of *shelo le'ochlav* is built on the idea that *shechita* is the moment that converts the animal into meat, and it is therefore at *shechita* that one's concern about who will eat must be settled. *Zerika* is a separate legal context with its own concerns — primarily *kapara* and ownership — and the *arlah*/*tumah* rules simply have no foothold there.

### Piggul Parallel

Rav Chisda, however, reads the *braita* to support his own position. *Adarabba*, he says — on the contrary — the *braita*'s *zos* points in the opposite direction. Here is his reading: *v'chol arel* proves that a partial group of *arelim* does not invalidate at *shechita* — *shechita hu d'miktzas lo pasla*. But *zos* then teaches something about *zerika*: *aval zerika, afilu miktzas pasla* — by *zerika*, even thinking about some *arelim* invalidates. Why would *zerika* be stricter? Because *zerika* is the moment at which *piggul* is finalized. The principle is *de-lo mikra piggul ella b'zerika* — something becomes *piggul* only at *zerika*, because it is only when the *zerika* is completed correctly, with the prior *piggul* thought left uncorrected, that the *piggul* status is locked in. *Zerika* is therefore the moment of greatest legal significance for *machshava*, and by analogy, *zerika* should be strictest in terms of wrong-eater thoughts as well.

It should be emphasized that neither Rava nor Rav Chisda is claiming that a thought of *shelo le'ochlav* occurring *at* *zerika* — independent of anything at *shechita* — would invalidate. Both agree that would have no effect. The debate is strictly about a *shechita* thought directed at *zerika*. Rava holds that a *shechita* thought must be *about shechita* to engage the *shelo le'ochlav* invalidity; Rav Chisda holds that a *shechita* thought *about zerika* can also trigger it.

Both readings are ultimately being imported into the *braita*, which does not explicitly address either position. The *braita* was discussing full-group versus partial-group *arelim*; Rava and Rav Chisda are each reading their respective positions on *shechita* versus *zerika* into the *braita*'s use of *zos*.

## Rav Ashi Challenge

### Any vs Every

*Masiv lah Rav Ashi* — Rav Ashi raises a challenge to the entire structure of the argument. Both Rava and Rav Chisda had inserted the verse *v'chol arel* as the *braita*'s proof that the full group of *arelim* is required, leaving *zos* to teach something additional about *zerika*. Rav Ashi questions this move: *mi-mai de-hai v'chol arel kullah mashma* — what is the basis for reading *v'chol arel* as meaning "the entire group must be *arelim*"? *V'eima hai v'chol arel mashma kol de-hu arel* — perhaps *v'chol arel* simply means any *arel* at all, even a single one, is a problem.

### Zos Emphasis

If *v'chol arel* means *any* *arel*, not *every* *arel*, then *zos* is not available to prove anything about *zerika* at all. Rather, *zos* is doing exactly what the *braita* originally said it was doing: *ksav rachmana zos de-ad de-ika kullah orlah lo pasla* — the Torah wrote *zos* to emphasize completeness, meaning that it is specifically when the *entire* group consists of *arelim* that there is an invalidity. Not if some are *arelim* and some are *mulim*. On this reading, *v'chol arel* alone would have implied even a single *arel* invalidates; *zos* comes to restrict that, telling us that only a complete group of *arelim* is disqualifying.

And this applies uniformly — *lo shna b'shechita v'lo shna b'zerika* — whether it is *shechita* or *zerika*. Rav Ashi does not insert a distinction between the two. *Zos* simply emphasizes the full-group requirement, and whatever that rule is, it applies equally at both moments. Rav Ashi thus takes the *braita* at its face value without interpolating the secondary *v'chol arel* verse or reading into *zos* anything about the *shechita*/*zerika* debate. The *braita* was about the full-group requirement; *zos* proves the full-group requirement; and the question of *zerika* versus *shechita* remains unresolved by the *braita* itself.

The daf closes with this challenge standing, and the discussion will continue in the next session.
