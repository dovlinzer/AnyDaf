# Yevamot 26 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Four Brothers Case

### Intro: Four Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַרְבָּעָה אַחִין, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים שְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וּמֵתוּ הַנְּשׂוּאִים אֶת הָאֲחָיוֹת — הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְאִם קָדְמוּ וְכָנְסוּ — יוֹצִיאוּ. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: יְקַיֵּים, וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצִיאוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **four brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters, and the ones married to the sisters died, then those** sisters must **perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage.** Since both sisters require levirate marriage with each of the surviving brothers, a levirate bond exists between each sister and the brothers. Each of them is considered the sister of a woman with whom each brother has a levirate bond and is therefore forbidden to him by rabbinic law. **And if they married** the sisters **before** consulting the court, **they should divorce** them, for the Sages decreed that in this situation they may not remain married. **Rabbi Eliezer says** that there is a dispute in this matter: **Beit Shammai say: He may maintain** her as his wife, **while Beit Hillel say: They** must **divorce** them.

The daf opens at the very bottom of 26a, where the Gemara is discussing the Mishnah concerning four brothers — two of whom die, leaving their widows to fall before the two surviving brothers — in a case where each surviving brother cannot perform *yibum* because each widow is *achot zekukato*, the sister of a woman to whom he stands in a *zikah* bond. The ruling of the Mishnah is that each surviving brother performs *chalitzah* with both women. We had already encountered an alternative explanation of this Mishnah: the real concern is *assur levatel mitzvat yabam* — it is forbidden to nullify the opportunity for *yibum*. According to this reading, if Levi were to perform *yibum* with Rachel, we fear that Yehuda might die, at which point Leah would become Levi's *achot ishto*, rendering her ineligible for either *yibum* or *chalitzah*. Therefore, Levi must perform *chalitzah* with Rachel, so that even if Yehuda dies, Levi retains the ability to perform *chalitzah* with Leah.

### Bittul Mitzvat Yabam

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ אֵין זִיקָה, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר אָסוּר לְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. דִּלְמָא אַדִּמְיַיבֵּם חַד — מָיֵית אִידַּךְ, וְקָמְבַטֵּל מִצְוַת יְבָמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Actually, I** could **say to you** that according to this *tanna* **the levirate bond is not substantial,** and yet they are prohibited from entering levirate marriage for a different reason. It is **because** the *tanna* **holds that it is prohibited to negate the mitzva of levirate marriage.** It is prohibited to act in a way that would lead to a situation where the mitzva to perform levirate marriage is negated. How would this situation arise? **Perhaps before one** brother **consummates the levirate marriage, the other** brother **dies,** and only one brother remains. In that case, the second sister would also happen before him for levirate marriage, **and** by performing levirate marriage with one sister **he would** thereby **negate the mitzva of levirate marriage** with the other sister. When the remaining brother marries one of the sisters, the mitzva to enter into levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza* is automatically negated from the second sister, as she is then forbidden to him as his wife's sister.

This alternative explanation — grounded in the concern of *assur levatel mitzvat yabam* rather than in the prohibition of *achot zekukato* — shifts the focus of the Mishnah's ruling from a structural *issur* to a prophylactic concern. The worry is not that the act of *yibum* is itself forbidden, but that performing it carelessly could eliminate the possibility of fulfilling the *mitzvah* for the other widow.

### Why Four Not Three?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, תְּלָתָא נָמֵי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** if this is the rationale behind the ruling in the mishna, then the same concern would exist if there were **three** brothers, **as well.** Why did the mishna specify four brothers? It could have cited the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters. In these circumstances, the concern for negating the mitzva of levirate marriage also exists.

The Gemara immediately challenges this reading: *i hachi, telata nami* — if the concern is *bittul mitzvat yabam*, why does the Mishnah require four brothers? Three would suffice to illustrate the point. If Levi alone survives and both women fall before him, he obviously cannot perform *yibum* with Rachel, since Leah would then be left with no recourse whatsoever. The problem is manifest even with three brothers.

### Zikah & Four Brothers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמְרִינַן: לָא מִיבַּעְיָא תְּלָתָא, דְּוַדַּאי בָּטְלָה מִצְוַת יְבָמִין. אֲבָל אַרְבְּעָה, לְמִיתָה לָא חָיְישִׁינַן — קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Indeed, the ruling would be the same in that case. However, the mishna **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to specify that the women must perform *ḥalitza* in a case involving **three** brothers, **as certainly the mitzva of levirate marriage is negated** with one of the sisters when the *yavam* marries the other sister. **But** in the case of **four** brothers, where there is concern only over the possibility that one of the brothers might die, we might have said that **we are not concerned over** the possibility of **the death** of a brother and therefore allow the brothers to consummate the levirate marriage. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that even in the case where there is concern only for the negation of the mitzva, they must perform *ḥalitza* and not consummate the levirate marriage.

The Gemara responds that the Mishnah employs the principle of *lo miba'aya*: the case of three brothers is so obvious it goes without saying. The *chiddush* lies precisely in the case of four brothers. One might have thought that with two surviving brothers, the concern of *bittul mitzvat yabam* is attenuated — after all, even if Levi performs *yibum* with Rachel, Yehuda is still present and will likely be available to perform *yibum* or *chalitzah* with Leah. The Mishnah therefore teaches that even in this case, where there is a second brother standing by, we remain concerned that Yehuda might die before fulfilling his obligation.

Regarding the *achot zekukato* reading, the *chiddush* of four brothers is different: it is a more novel ruling that *achot zekukato* applies even when the *zikah* is distributed between two brothers, since one might have thought that a shared *zikah* is too weak to generate the prohibition.

### Fear of One Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי — חַמְשָׁה נָמֵי! לְמִיתָה דִּתְרֵי לָא חָיְישִׁינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** i.e., if we are concerned over the possibility that the remaining brother might die, then this should **also** be true in a case of **five** brothers, two of whom were married to two sisters and died, and their wives happened before the three remaining brothers for levirate marriage. The Gemara answers: **We are not concerned over** the possibility of **the death of two** brothers in such a short period of time.

The Gemara then presses further: if we are worried that one brother might die, why not extend the case to five brothers — and fear that both Yehuda and a third brother might die, leaving Leah without *yibum* or *chalitzah*? The Gemara responds: *lemita de-trei lo chayshinan* — we do not entertain the fear that two people will die simultaneously before any act can be performed. The concern for one death is realistic enough to legislate around; the concern for two simultaneous deaths is too remote. With this, the Gemara closes the discussion of the four-brothers case.

## Three Sisters Case

### Setup: Three Sisters

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: שָׁלֹשׁ אֲחָיוֹת יְבָמוֹת שֶׁנָּפְלוּ לִפְנֵי שְׁנֵי אַחִין יְבָמִין — זֶה חוֹלֵץ לְאַחַת וְזֶה חוֹלֵץ לְאַחַת, וְאֶמְצָעִית צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִשְּׁנֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava bar Rav Huna said** that **Rav said:** In a case where **three sisters** who were married to three brothers became ***yevamot*** and **happened before two** remaining **brothers** who were their ***yevamin*** for levirate marriage, then **this** one brother **performs *ḥalitza* with one** of the women, **and that** brother **performs *ḥalitza* with** another **one** of the women. **And the middle,** i.e., third, sister **requires *ḥalitza* with both of** the brothers.

The Gemara now introduces a new case, cited in the name of Rav: three sisters who are *yevamot* fall before two surviving brothers — Yissachar and Zebulun. Let us call the three sisters Rachel, Leah, and Sarah, all sisters of one another, each married to a different brother who has died, leaving the two survivors with a *zikah* obligation to all three women. Since all three women are sisters to one another, any act of *yibum* by either surviving brother would render the other two women *achot zekukato* — a rabbinic quasi-prohibition analogous to *achot ishto*.

The ruling of Rav is: *zeh choletz l'achas v'zeh choletz l'achas v'emtza'it tzericha chalitzah mishneihem* — one brother performs *chalitzah* with one sister, the other brother performs *chalitzah* with another, and the third woman — called *emtza'it*, "the middle one," though she is really the last to become available — requires *chalitzah* from both brothers. Tosafot notes that she is called "middle" because she stands, as it were, between Yissachar and Zebulun.

The case unfolds sequentially: the first husband dies, Yissachar performs *chalitzah* with Rachel; then the second husband dies, Zebulun performs *chalitzah* with Leah; then the third husband dies, and Sarah requires *chalitzah* from both of them. The question is why Sarah requires *chalitzah* from both brothers, and why there is a preference that Zebulun perform *chalitzah* with Leah rather than Yissachar.

### Chalitzah Psula Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבָּה: מִדְּקָאָמְרַתְּ אֶמְצָעִית צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִשְּׁנֵיהֶם, קָסָבְרַתְּ יֵשׁ זִיקָה, וְהָוְיָא לַהּ חֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה — וַחֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה צָרִיךְ לַחֲזוֹר עַל כׇּל הָאַחִין,
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said to them: From** the fact **that you say** that **the middle one requires *ḥalitza* with both of** the brothers, it can be deduced that **you hold** that **the levirate bond is substantial** and creates a familial relationship, **and** similarly **this** *ḥalitza* performed by each of the brothers with the third sister **is invalid *ḥalitza*.** Since the third woman is the sister of a woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza*, it is not possible to consummate the levirate marriage with her. Therefore, the *ḥalitza* performed with her is not entirely valid and does not release her from the levirate bond between her and the two brothers. **And** there is a principle that **invalid *ḥalitza* must be repeated by all of the brothers;** each of them must perform an act of *ḥalitza* with this woman.

Rava frames the logic as follows: *mideka'amret emtza'it tzericha chalitzah mishneihem, kasavar yesh zikah* — since Rav rules that the last sister requires *chalitzah* from both brothers, this ruling presupposes the position of *yesh zikah*, and moreover invokes a distinct principle: *chalitzah pesulah tzarich lachzor al kol ha'achim* — a defective, second-rate *chalitzah* must be obtained from all surviving brothers, not just one.

This is a governing principle that requires careful understanding. Ordinarily, when a man dies leaving multiple wives, *yibum* or *chalitzah* with one wife suffices to release all the others. But this rule applies only when the *zikah* is full and unimpaired. In cases where, for any reason, the *zikah* to a particular woman is weaker or altered in character, the resulting *chalitzah* is considered *pesulah* — not invalid in the sense of being null, but second-rate, a *chalitzah gru'ah*. Rashi explains it as a *chalitzah* performed in a situation where *yibum* is impossible. Tosafot refines this: the definition is not merely that *yibum* is impossible, but specifically that the reason *yibum* is impossible is because something has weakened or complicated the *zikah* itself. When the *zikah* is weakened, the *chalitzah* is correspondingly weakened, and a weakened *chalitzah* must be obtained from all brothers.

Tosafot enumerates the main categories that produce a *chalitzah pesulah*: (1) the woman is *achot zikukato* — for one who holds *yesh zikah*, this creates a quasi-*ervah* status that weakens the *zikah*; (2) the woman is *achot chalutzato* — her sister has already received *chalitzah* from this brother, making her a rabbinic quasi-*ervah*; (3) she is a *ba'alat haget* — the surviving brother gave her a *get*, which, though not halachically required and without full *d'oraita* effect in this context, nonetheless prevents *yibum* and shifts the character of the *zikah*; and (4) she is a *ba'alat ma'amar* — the surviving brother performed *maamar* with her, a rabbinic act of quasi-betrothal that strengthens the bond in one sense but simultaneously removes her from the simple center of the *zikah* relationship, making *chalitzah* less straightforwardly applicable.

In all these cases, the *zikah* has been displaced from its normal configuration — whether weakened by distance or complicated by prior action — and the *chalitzah* performed in such circumstances carries less force, requiring that it be obtained from each of the surviving brothers.

### Sequential Deaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, קַמַּיְיתָא נָמֵי! אִי דִּנְפוּל בְּבַת אַחַת — הָכִי נָמֵי. לָא צְרִיכָא: דִּנְפוּל בְּזוֹ אַחַר זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If that is so, the first** two women should **also** require *ḥalitza* from each of the brothers because each act of *ḥalitza* was invalid, as each woman has the status of the sister of a woman with whom the man has a levirate bond, and it is therefore prohibited for the men to consummate the levirate marriage with them. The Gemara responds: **If** the case is **that they,** i.e., the sisters, **happened** before the brothers **simultaneously, it is indeed so** that the two brothers would each be obligated to perform *ḥalitza* with each of the three sisters. This *halakha* is **necessary only** in the case **where they happened** before the brothers, **one after the other.**

Returning to the three-sisters case, Rava asks: if every woman is *achot zekukato* and therefore in a weakened *zikah* relationship, why don't Rachel and Leah also require *chalitzah* from both brothers? The Gemara responds: *lo tzricha d'naphal zo achar zo* — the case is specifically one where the husbands died sequentially, not simultaneously.

When the first husband dies, only one woman — Rachel — falls before the two brothers. At that moment she is not *achot zekukato* to anyone, since no other woman has yet fallen. Her *zikah* is full and unimpaired. The *chalitzah* Yissachar performs with Rachel is therefore a complete, first-rate *chalitzah*. Then the second husband dies and Leah falls. What is Leah's status vis-à-vis Yissachar? She is *achot chalutzato* — her sister Rachel has just received *chalitzah* from Yissachar. But vis-à-vis Zebulun, she bears no such disability: the *zikah* was fully severed with respect to Yissachar by Rachel's *chalitzah*, and Leah is neither *achot chalutzato* nor *achot zekukato* relative to Zebulun. Therefore Zebulun performs a complete, first-rate *chalitzah* with Leah. By the time the third husband dies and Sarah becomes available, she is *achot chalutzato* of both Yissachar and Zebulun. Any *chalitzah* performed with her by either brother is therefore *pesulah*, and she requires the *chalitzah* to be performed by both.

If, however, all three men had died simultaneously, every woman would stand from the outset as *achot zekukato* to both brothers, and all three would require *chalitzah* from both — the Gemara concedes this point explicitly.

### Rachel & Sarah's Status

Returning to the three-sisters case, Rava asks: if every woman is *achot zekukato* and therefore in a weakened *zikah* relationship, why don't Rachel and Leah also require *chalitzah* from both brothers? The Gemara responds: *lo tzricha d'naphal zo achar zo* — the case is specifically one where the husbands died sequentially, not simultaneously.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְפַלָה חֲדָא — חָלֵץ לַהּ רְאוּבֵן, נְפַלָה אִידַּךְ — חָלֵץ לַהּ שִׁמְעוֹן, נְפַלָה אִידַּךְ, חָלֵץ לַהּ הַאי — מַפְקַע זִיקָתוֹ, חָלֵץ לַהּ הַאי — מַפְקַע זִיקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** How so? **One** sister **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage and then **Reuven,** one of the brothers, **performed** valid ***ḥalitza* with her. Another** sister **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage, and the brother **Shimon performed *ḥalitza* with her.** In this case, Shimon was allowed to consummate the levirate marriage with her if he so desired, as she had no family relationship to him at all. When Reuven performed *ḥalitza* with the first sister, he removed the levirate bond between her and the other brothers, such that Shimon has no relationship with the first sister at all and is free to marry the second sister. Therefore, when he performed *ḥalitza*, the act was valid. **Another** third sister then **happened** before the brothers for levirate marriage. When **this** first brother **performs *ḥalitza* with her, he terminates his levirate bond, and** when **this** second brother **performs *ḥalitza* with her, he terminates his levirate bond.** However, neither brother can, by performing *ḥalitza*, terminate the other brother's levirate bond because each brother's *ḥalitza* is invalid, as each brother is unable to consummate the levirate marriage in this case. This is due to the woman's status as the sister of a woman with whom he performed *ḥalitza*.

The Gemara then asks why Rachel is specifically called *achot zikukato* in Rav's formulation, since the application of that term is what sets the case in motion. The answer follows from the sequential structure of the case: before any death, Rachel stands in a full *zikah* with both brothers. Her status as *achot zikukato* exists only insofar as the *zikah* bond — which is a collective bond — implies that her sisters share in it. Once the deaths become sequential and *chalitzah* begins to be performed, Rachel's full *chalitzah* from Yissachar is the event that establishes Sarah as *achot chalutzato* of Yissachar, and Leah's *chalitzah* from Zebulun establishes Sarah as *achot chalutzato* of Zebulun as well. Sarah's need for *chalitzah* from both brothers thus flows from the sequential accumulation of *chalutzah* relationships as each act of *chalitzah* transforms the status of the remaining women.

## Rav vs. Shmuel

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאָמַר רַב: אֵין זִיקָה! לְדִבְרֵי הָאוֹמֵר יֵשׁ זִיקָה קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How can this statement be cited in the name of Rav? **But didn't Rav say: The levirate bond is not substantial.** This *halakha*, however, was explained based on the assumption that the levirate bond is substantial. The Gemara answers: Indeed, according to Rav himself it is unnecessary to perform *ḥalitza* with multiple brothers. He, however, **states** this *halakha* **in accordance with the statement of the one who says that the levirate bond is substantial,** despite the fact that he does not hold this himself.

Having established the framework of *chalitzah pesulah* and its requirement of *chalitzah* from all brothers, the Gemara turns to the formal debate between Rav and Shmuel. The Gemara notes a potential tension: the ruling about the middle sister requiring *chalitzah* from both brothers was cited in Rav's name, yet we know from elsewhere that Rav holds *ein zikah* — he does not recognize the rabbinic category of *achot zekukato* as generating a true quasi-*ervah* relationship. The Gemara resolves this by clarifying that Rav articulated this ruling according to the position of *yesh zikah*, not necessarily as his own personal ruling.

### Shmuel's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ לְכוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the above case **Shmuel said** a different halakhic ruling: It is sufficient if **one** brother **performs *ḥalitza* with each** of the sisters.

Shmuel's position is stated simply: *echad choletz lekulan* — one brother performs *chalitzah* with all of the women. Shmuel apparently rejects both the requirement of first-rate *chalitzah* in every case and, more strikingly, the principle that *chalitzah pesulah* requires *chalitzah* from all brothers. According to Shmuel, if Reuven has already performed *chalitzah* with Rachel, making him unable to perform first-rate *chalitzah* with Leah — since she is now *achot chalutzato* — he may nonetheless go ahead and perform second-rate *chalitzah* with Leah, and then with Sarah as well. He need not defer to Shimon; one brother suffices throughout.

### Chalitzah Me'alya

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדִי שָׁמְעִינַן לֵיהּ לִשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמַר: חֲלִיצָה מְעַלְּיָא בְּעֵינַן, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָלַץ לַאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת. הֵיכָא דְּקָיְימָא חֲלִיצָה דְּשִׁמְעוֹן חֲלִיצָה כְּשֵׁרָה, חָלֵיץ לַהּ רְאוּבֵן חֲלִיצָה פְּסוּלָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But since we have heard that Shmuel said: We require a full-fledged *ḥalitza*,** and invalid *ḥalitza* does not result in complete exemption, this ruling is difficult. It is not clear why the invalid *ḥalitza* in this case would be sufficient, **as Shmuel said: In the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to several women, including two sisters, and the two married brothers later died, and their wives happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage, if the *yavam* performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters, the rival wives are not** thereby **exempt.** One can deduce from here that since the *yavam* cannot consummate the levirate marriage with the sisters, as each is the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond, then the act of *ḥalitza* is invalid, and invalid *ḥalitza* is ineffective in exempting their rival wives. It is concluded from here that even Shmuel requires valid *ḥalitza*, i.e., *ḥalitza* that occurs when there is a possibility of consummating the levirate marriage. According to this rationale, however, Shmuel's ruling in the above case is difficult: **With regard to the second sister, when there exists** the possibility for **Shimon's *ḥalitza*,** i.e., the *ḥalitza* of the second brother who did not yet perform *ḥalitza*, to be **a valid *ḥalitza*,** would it be allowed for **Reuven,** the brother who already did *ḥalitza* with one sister, **to perform invalid *ḥalitza* with** her?

The Gemara presses Shmuel: do we not know from elsewhere that Shmuel himself holds *chalitzah ma'alya beinun* — that a superior, first-rate *chalitzah* is required? The source for this is a ruling of Shmuel cited elsewhere (122a): *chalatz l'achayot lo nifteru tzarot* — if a man performs *chalitzah* with the sisters, the co-wives are not thereby released; but *chalatz litzarot nifteru achayot* — if he performs *chalitzah* with the co-wives, the sisters are released.

This ruling clearly presupposes that a first-rate *chalitzah* carries greater power than a second-rate one: a *chalitzah* performed with a woman whose status is *achot zekukato* (a second-rate *chalitzah*, grade B) does not release the co-wife, whereas a *chalitzah* performed with the co-wife — who, though herself subject to a one-degree-removed quasi-*ervah* status, stands in a fuller relationship with the *yavam* — does release the sister. If Shmuel values first-rate *chalitzah* to the point that only it can release a co-wife, why does he say that one brother may perform second-rate *chalitzah* with all the women when a first-rate option is available?

## Co-Wives & Chalitzah

### Weaker Chalitzah Insuffi…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חָלַץ לַאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת, לְצָרוֹת — נִפְטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת. חָלַץ לְבַעֲלַת הַגֵּט — לֹא נִפְטְרָה צָרָה, לְצָרָה — נִפְטְרָה בַּעֲלַת הַגֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos of Shmuel's statement, the Gemara examines **the** matter **itself. Shmuel said:** In the case of three brothers, two of whom were married to several women, including two sisters, and the two married brothers later died, and their wives happened before the *yavam* for levirate marriage, if the *yavam* **performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters, the rival wives are not** thereby **exempt.** But if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the rival wives, the sisters are exempt.** Similarly, if he gave a bill of divorce to one of these women, whereby he would no longer be permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with them due to a rabbinic decree, and he then **performed *ḥalitza* with the woman who received a bill of divorce, the rival wife is not** thereby **exempt.** Since he was unable to consummate the levirate marriage with her, the *ḥalitza* performed with her was invalid, and invalid *ḥalitza* does not exempt the rival wife. If he performed the act of *ḥalitza* **with the rival wife,** then **the woman who received a bill of divorce is exempt.**

To understand the stakes, the case that grounds Shmuel's ruling about co-wives must be spelled out carefully. Reuven dies leaving Rachel and her co-wife Devorah; Shimon dies leaving Leah and her co-wife Naomi. Yissachar alone survives. Of these four women, Rachel and Leah are sisters to one another, making each of them *achot zekukato* — a quasi-*ervah* — to the surviving brother. Devorah and Naomi are their respective co-wives.

Can Yissachar perform *yibum* with Rachel? No — she is *achot zekukato*. Can he perform *yibum* with Devorah? Devorah is a degree further removed: she is not *achot zekukato* herself, but *tzaras achot zekukato* — the co-wife of a quasi-*ervah*. This is a weaker prohibition, but it is still a prohibition. However, because Devorah's relationship to Yissachar is less attenuated by the quasi-*ervah* structure, his *chalitzah* with Devorah is of a higher grade than his *chalitzah* with Rachel. And it is this gradation that determines the outcome: a second-rate *chalitzah* with Rachel does not release Devorah, but a relatively better *chalitzah* with Devorah does release Rachel.

### Stronger Chalitzah Works

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא לַאֲחָיוֹת דְּלֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת — דְּהָוְיָא (לוֹ) [לַהּ] אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיקָה? חָלַץ לְצָרוֹת נָמֵי, לָא לִיפַּטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת — דְּהָוְיָא לְהוּ צָרוֹת אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה בְּזִיקָה! קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין זִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** in the two cases? Why, if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the sisters, are the rival wives not exempt?** This is because the sister **is** related **to him** as **the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond.** Since, under these circumstances he would not be permitted to consummate the levirate marriage with her, her *ḥalitza* is then considered invalid *ḥalitza*. However, if that is so, when **he performed *ḥalitza* with the rival wives, the sisters should not be exempt either, as the** rival wives **are** related **to him as rival wives of the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond.** If the woman is forbidden to him due to a relationship created by the levirate bond, then her rival wife is forbidden to him in the same way, and her *ḥalitza* would be invalid as well. The Gemara answers: **Shmuel holds** that **the levirate bond is not substantial,** and therefore the levirate bond does not create a relationship between the *yavam* and the sisters such that the prohibition would be extended to the rival wives as well.

The question returns: if Shimon is available and could perform a full, first-rate *chalitzah* with Leah — since Leah is not *achot chalutzato* or *achot zekukato* relative to Shimon — why should we allow Reuven to perform a second-rate *chalitzah* with Leah? The *chalitzah* from Shimon would be *kesherah* in the fullest sense; *chalitzah* from Reuven would be *pesulah*. If Shmuel holds *chalitzah ma'alya beinun* — that a superior *chalitzah* is preferred — why does he permit the inferior option when the superior is available?

The Gemara offers a first reread of Shmuel: when Shmuel said *echad choletz lekulan*, perhaps he was not saying that one brother literally does *chalitzah* with all three women. Perhaps he meant only to dispute the requirement of obtaining *chalitzah* from all brothers in the case of the last woman, Sarah. He agrees that Reuven performs *chalitzah* with Rachel and Shimon performs *chalitzah* with Leah; his innovation is only that Sarah needs *chalitzah* from just one brother, not both. And since Shimon handled two of the three women, the Gemara permits calling him the one who did *chalitzah* "with all of them" — *keivan deruba gabeih, kari leh kulan*, since the majority was with him. This is acknowledged as a somewhat forced reading but at least allows Shmuel to maintain his preference for *chalitzah ma'alya* while disputing the *tzarich lachzor al kol ha'achim* principle.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״אֶחָד חוֹלֵץ לְכוּלָּן״ נָמֵי דְּקָאָמַר — אַאֶמְצָעִית. וְהָא ״כּוּלָּן״ קָאָמַר! כֵּיוָן דְּרוּבָּה גַּבֵּיהּ, קָרֵי לֵיהּ ״כּוּלָּן״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל חֲלִיצָה מְעַלְּיָא בְּעֵינַן — הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְמִיפְטַר צָרָתָהּ, אֲבָל מִפְטַרא נַפְשַׁהּ — פָּטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara resolves this difficulty by reinterpreting Shmuel's statement. **What** does it mean **that it says: One performs *ḥalitza* with each of them, that** Shmuel **stated?** Shmuel **says that with regard to the middle one,** i.e., the third sister, one of the two brothers performs *ḥalitza* with her. The Gemara asks: **But didn't he say: Each of them,** indicating that one brother performs *ḥalitza* with all of the sisters? The Gemara answers: **Since** the brother who performed *ḥalitza* with one sister repeats the act with another, it turns out that **most** of the acts of *ḥalitza* **are** performed **with him, and this is called:** With **each of them. And if you wish, say** a different answer: **When Shmuel said we require a full-fledged *ḥalitza*, this applies only to exempt her rival wife** by means of that *ḥalitza*. **But to exempt** the woman **herself,** even invalid *ḥalitza* would render her **exempt.** In the case above, since no rival wives are involved, it would be sufficient for one brother to perform *ḥalitza* with each of the sisters.

### Achot Zikukato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי קָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל חֲלִיצָה מְעַלְּיָא בְּעֵינַן — הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְמִיפְטַר צָרָתָהּ, אֲבָל מִפְטַרא נַפְשַׁהּ — פָּטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** a different answer: **When Shmuel said we require a full-fledged *ḥalitza*, this applies only to exempt her rival wife** by means of that *ḥalitza*. **But to exempt** the woman **herself,** even invalid *ḥalitza* would render her **exempt.**

The Gemara then attempts to defend the original, simpler reading of Shmuel, distinguishing when *chalitzah ma'alya* is required. The resolution offered is: *ki ka'amar Shmuel chalitzah ma'alya beinun, hanei mili limftar tzartah* — Shmuel's principle that a superior *chalitzah* is needed applies specifically when the goal is to release a co-wife. Only a first-rate *chalitzah* carries enough force to release another woman. But *aval limftar nafshah patrah* — to release the woman herself, a second-rate *chalitzah* is entirely sufficient. In the three-sisters case, Sarah has no co-wives. Therefore Shmuel sees no reason to insist on first-rate *chalitzah* for her; second-rate suffices, and the dispute with Rav reduces to whether *chalitzah pesulah* requires *chalitzah* from all brothers (Rav: yes; Shmuel: no) and whether a second-rate *chalitzah* is permissible when no co-wife is at stake (Shmuel: yes).

## Sequential Deaths Matter

### Simultaneous Deaths

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי דִּנְפוּל בְּבַת אַחַת — הָכִי נָמֵי. לָא צְרִיכָא: דִּנְפוּל בְּזוֹ אַחַר זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If** the case is **that they,** i.e., the sisters, **happened** before the brothers **simultaneously, it is indeed so** that the two brothers would each be obligated to perform *ḥalitza* with each of the three sisters. This *halakha* is **necessary only** in the case **where they happened** before the brothers, **one after the other.**

The Gemara clarifies a fundamental structural point: the distinction between first-rate and second-rate *chalitzah*, and which woman receives *chalitzah* from which brother, depends entirely on whether the deaths occurred simultaneously or sequentially. If all three husbands had died at once, every woman would stand from the outset as *achot zekukato* to both surviving brothers. No one would have a privileged *chalitzah* relationship with either brother. All three women would occupy equal — and equally weakened — positions, and the question of ordering would carry no special significance.

### Status Changes Sequentia…

Once deaths are sequential, however, each act of *chalitzah* transforms the status of the remaining women for each of the surviving brothers. This is the key insight underlying Rav's ruling. When only Rachel has fallen — because only the first husband has died — she bears no *achot zekukato* disqualification, since no other sister has yet entered the *zikah*. Her *chalitzah* from Yissachar is complete. The moment that *chalitzah* is performed, however, the remaining sisters become *achot chalutzato* of Yissachar. They are not yet *achot chalutzato* of Zebulun — that status accrues only when he performs *chalitzah* with one of them. Thus the sequential nature of events generates a layered accumulation of statuses that varies between brothers.

### Rachel Becomes Achot Cha…

By the time Sarah requires *chalitzah*, she is *achot chalutzato* of Yissachar — because Yissachar performed *chalitzah* with Rachel — and simultaneously *achot chalutzato* of Zebulun — because Zebulun performed *chalitzah* with Leah. Any *chalitzah* performed with Sarah by either brother is therefore *pesulah*. Since both brothers generate only a *chalitzah pesulah* with her, the principle of *chalitzah pesulah tzarich lachzor al kol ha'achim* applies, and she must receive *chalitzah* from both. This is the complete logic underlying Rav's ruling: it is the sequential accumulation of *achot chalutzato* relationships across both brothers that locks Sarah into needing *chalitzah* from each of them.

## Beginning vs. Finishing

### Tzaras Krovat Chalitzato

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמוּאֵל נָמֵי, הִתְחִיל וְלֹא הִתְחִיל קָאָמַר: הִתְחִיל בַּאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא יִגְמוֹר בְּצָרוֹת, דִּתְנַן: אָסוּר אָדָם בְּצָרַת קְרוֹבַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ. הִתְחִיל בְּצָרוֹת — יִגְמוֹר אַף בַּאֲחָיוֹת. דִּתְנַן: מוּתָּר אָדָם בִּקְרוֹבַת צָרַת חֲלוּצָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Shmuel also** meant to distinguish between a case where **he began and** the case where **he did not begin.** This is how his statement should be understood: If **he began** by performing *ḥalitza* **with** one of **the sisters, he may not finish by** performing a second act of *ḥalitza* **with** any one of **the rival wives, as we learned** in a mishna (40b): **A man is forbidden** to marry **the rival wife of a close relative of his *ḥalutza*.** Due to this prohibition, *ḥalitza* performed with the second rival wife is invalid *ḥalitza* and would not exempt the second sister. If, however, **he began with the rival wives** and performed the first *ḥalitza* with the rival wife of Leah, **he may finish with the sisters as well** and perform the second *ḥalitza* with Leah, **as we learned** in a mishna (40b): **A man is permitted to** marry **the close relative of the rival wife of his *ḥalutza*.** Therefore, if he performed *ḥalitza* with Leah's rival wife, then Rachel, who is the sister of the rival wife of his *ḥalutza*, is permitted to him. He can therefore perform a completely valid *ḥalitza* with her and thereby exempt her rival wife.

At this point the Gemara introduces an entirely different reread of Shmuel's ruling — not based on the graduated strength of *chalitzah*, but on a distinct *issur*. The Mishnah elsewhere teaches: *assur adam bitzaras krovat chalitzato* — a man is forbidden to the co-wife of the relative of his *chalutza*. Rashi explains the case: if Reuven performed *chalitzah* with Leah and then a certain Barak — an entirely unrelated man — was married both to Leah's mother Rivkah and to Devorah, Reuven is forbidden to Devorah after Barak's death, because Devorah is the co-wife (*tzara*) of Rivkah, who is a relative of Reuven's *chalutza* Leah (*krovat chalitzato*). The reasoning, though complex, establishes that a *tzaras krovat chalitzato* is independently prohibited, not merely as a subset of the *chalitzah* relationship itself.

This prohibition has direct implications for the case at hand. After Yissachar performs *chalitzah* with Leah, Rachel becomes *achot chalutzato* of Yissachar — Yissachar's *krovat chalitzato*. Naomi, who was Reuven's co-wife alongside Rachel, now stands as *tzaras krovat chalitzato* of Yissachar. This is an independent *issur*, on equal footing with the *issur* of *achot chalutzato*, not a derivative one degree removed. Accordingly, both Rachel and Naomi are now problematic for Yissachar in their own independent ways, and performing *chalitzah* with one of them cannot release the other.

### Cannot Finish with Tzaros

Shmuel's ruling — on this reread — is not about the gradations of *chalitzah* quality at all. Rather, it is about the order in which one begins. *Hitchil ba'achayot, lo yigmor bitzarot* — if he began with the sisters (performing *chalitzah* with Leah first), he should not finish with the co-wives (attempting to use the *chalitzah* with Naomi to release Rachel). The reason is the *issur* of *tzaras krovat chalitzato*: once he has performed *chalitzah* with Leah, Naomi is forbidden to him as *tzaras krovat chalitzato*. A *chalitzah* with Naomi will not release Rachel, because both women are now independently prohibited. Rashi clarifies that *lo yigmor bitzarot* does not mean he cannot perform *chalitzah* with Naomi at all, but rather that doing so will not exempt Rachel; he must do both.

### Can Finish with Achayos

Conversely, *hitchil bitzarot, yigmor af ba'achayot* — if he is prudent and begins with Devorah (the co-wife), before any act of *chalitzah* with the sisters has been performed, then no *achot chalutzato* or *tzaras krovat chalitzato* relationship has yet been created. Both Rachel and Naomi remain fully available to him. In this case, a *chalitzah* with either woman will release the other, because *mutar adam bikrovas tzaras chalitzato* — a man is permitted to the relative of his *chalitzah*-co-wife. This is the mirror image of the earlier prohibition and explicitly not a concern. By beginning with the co-wives before any *achot chalutzato* relationship is established, he preserves the full effectiveness of each subsequent *chalitzah*. The lesson Shmuel is teaching, on this radical reread, is entirely about sequencing: the order in which one begins determines whether the remaining women can be released by a single subsequent *chalitzah*.

## Rav Ashi's Explanation

### Zikah Not Strong Enough

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, וּמִשּׁוּם דְּלָא אַלִּימָא זִיקָה לְשַׁוּוֹיֵי לְצָרָה כְּעֶרְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: Actually,** Shmuel's statement should be interpreted **as you** originally **said,** that Shmuel's rationale for these *halakhot* accords with his opinion that the levirate bond is substantial. As for the objection that was raised as to why the sisters would be exempted by *ḥalitza* performed with the rival wives if these rival wives were considered the rival wife of the sister of a woman with whom the *yavam* had a levirate bond, this can be resolved as follows: This **is because the levirate bond is not** so **strong as to render the** status of **a rival wife like** an actual **forbidden relative.** The levirate bond is sufficient to prohibit levirate marriage with the sister of a woman with whom he has a levirate bond, but not sufficient to prohibit their rival wives to the *yavam*.

Having traversed Shmuel's radical reread, the Gemara finally offers what it presents as the straightforward explanation, attributed to Rav Ashi: *le'olam ked'kamris* — revert to the plain reading of Shmuel. Shmuel indeed holds that performing *chalitzah* with the co-wife (Devorah) is preferable to performing it with the sister (Leah), and that *chalitzah* with the co-wife releases the sister. The reason is not the elaborate sequencing analysis, but rather a principle about the strength of *zikah*: *umishum d'lo alima zika l'shavyei l'tzara k'erva* — the *zikah* is not strong enough to render the co-wife as significant an *ervah* as the sister herself.

Leah is *achot zekukato* — a quasi-*ervah* by rabbinic decree, one degree removed from *achot ishto*. Devorah is *tzaras achot zekukato* — the co-wife of a quasi-*ervah*. Ordinarily, the rule of *tzarat ervah* extends the *ervah* prohibition to co-wives, but that rule assumes a genuine, *d'oraita* *ervah*. Here, even the underlying *ervah* — *achot zekukato* — is only rabbinic. There is already a *chiddush* in declaring that *achot zekukato* generates any *ervah*-like status at all; extending that derivative status one further degree to the co-wife is too attenuated to carry full force. Devorah may therefore not be a *sug alef* (grade A) in the absolute sense, but she is a grade B relative to Leah's grade C. She stands in a fuller, less impaired relationship with the *yavam*, and *chalitzah* with her is therefore the stronger act — strong enough to release Leah.

### Chalitzah with Tzara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרַב אָשֵׁי: חָלַץ לָאֲחָיוֹת — לֹא נִפְטְרוּ צָרוֹת. הָא לְצָרוֹת — נִפְטְרוּ אֲחָיוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא — לָאו מִשּׁוּם דְּקָסָבַר יֵשׁ זִיקָה, וְלָא אַלִּימָא זִיקָה לְשַׁוּוֹיַיהּ לְצָרָה כְּעֶרְוָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: It **is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rav Ashi:** If **he performed *ḥalitza* with the sisters, the rival wives are not exempt** from levirate marriage. From here one can deduce: **Consequently,** if he performed *ḥalitza* **with the rival wives, the sisters are exempt. What is the reason** for this? **Is it not because** this *tanna* **held** that **the levirate bond is substantial,** and therefore the rival wives were not rendered exempt by the *ḥalitza* of the sisters, **but** nevertheless **the levirate bond is not** so **strong as to render the rival wife equivalent to a forbidden relative?** Therefore, the prohibition with regard to the rival wives in this case is less severe than the prohibition concerning the sisters themselves, and when they perform *ḥalitza*, the *ḥalitza* is valid and the sisters are exempt.

The practical application is thus: performing *chalitzah* with Devorah releases Leah, because the *zikah* between Devorah and the *yavam* is sufficiently unimpaired that the *chalitzah* carries full releasing force vis-à-vis a woman who, though *achot zekukato*, is not a true *ervah*. The graduated hierarchy — sisters (*achot zekukato*, grade B), co-wives of sisters (*tzaras achot zekukato*, grade B-plus relative to the sisters) — means that acting on the relatively stronger relationship releases the weaker one, but not vice versa.

## Beit Shammai View

### Beit Shammai Permits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל: הָא מַנִּי — בֵּית שַׁמַּאי הִיא. דִּתְנַן: בֵּית שַׁמַּאי מַתִּירִין הַצָּרוֹת לָאַחִין. אִי הָכִי, יַבּוֹמֵי נָמֵי תִּתְיַיבֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel** rejected this explanation and **said:** In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** *baraita* taught? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Beit Shammai, as we learned** in a mishna: **Beit Shammai permitted the rival wives to** marry **the brothers;** even if they are the rival wives of his actual relatives, they are permitted to enter into levirate marriage. In the case above, where they are merely rival wives of the sister with whom he has a levirate bond, all the more so they are permitted to enter into levirate marriage. The Gemara objects: **If that is so,** if this ruling is in accordance with the opinion of Beit Shammai, who say that the rival wives are permitted, then the rival wife **should enter into levirate marriage as well.** Why does it speak here only of *ḥalitza* but not of the possibility of entering levirate marriage?

Rabbi Abba bar Mammel proposes an entirely different approach to Shmuel's ruling. Rather than resolving the case through the logic of *zikah* strength and graduated *chalitzah* quality, he suggests: *ha mani Beis Shammai hi* — perhaps Shmuel is operating according to the position of Beit Shammai. The Mishnah records that Beit Shammai permits the co-wives (*tzaros*) to marry into the family of the surviving brothers — that is, Beit Shammai does not recognize the category of *tzarat ervah* at all in the context of *yibum*. On this view, Devorah is not subject to any prohibition whatsoever, and there is no tension between performing *chalitzah* with Devorah and subsequently releasing Leah. The preference for beginning with Devorah is simply that, according to Beit Shammai, there is a full *mitzvah* of *yibum* available with Devorah, making her the primary obligee.

### Covering All Views

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי, דְּאָמַר: בּוֹאוּ וּנְתַקֵּן לָהֶן לַצָּרוֹת שֶׁיְּהוּ חוֹלְצוֹת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבְּמוֹת. וְהָאָמַר מָר: לָא הִסְפִּיקוּ לִגְמוֹר אֶת הַדָּבָר עַד שֶׁנִּטְרְפָה הַשָּׁעָה! אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק: אַחֲרָיו חָזְרוּ וְתִקְּנוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The ruling is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri, who said: Come and let us establish** a ruling **that the rival wives must perform *ḥalitza* and may not enter into levirate marriage,** thereby circumventing the dispute between Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel. Although Beit Shammai permitted the rival wives to perform levirate betrothal, they should perform *ḥalitza* instead in order to conform to Beit Hillel's opinion as well. The Gemara objects: **But didn't the Master say** that **they did not succeed in finalizing the matter** and establishing Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri's amendment **before the times of trouble came** in the form of the anti-Jewish decrees, and so this ruling was never actually established? **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said: After his** time, other Sages **returned** to this issue and **established** this amendment in accordance with his opinion.

The Gemara raises the obvious objection: if Shmuel follows Beit Shammai and Devorah is fully permitted, why not perform *yibum* with her rather than *chalitzah*? The Gemara responds by citing Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, who ruled: *bo'u venisaken lahem l'tzaros sheyehu choltzot v'lo misyabmot* — let us enact a *takanah* that co-wives must receive *chalitzah* and may not undergo *yibum*. This ruling is designed to cover both positions: according to Beit Hillel, the co-wife is prohibited and can receive only *chalitzah*; according to Beit Shammai, she is permitted but we impose *chalitzah* rather than *yibum* as a compromise. By requiring *chalitzah*, we satisfy Beit Shammai's concern that the *mitzvah* be fulfilled in some form, without permitting the *yibum* that Beit Hillel would forbid.

### Final Stringency

The Gemara, however, notes a complication: the tradition records that the sages did not complete (*lo hispiku ligmor*) the enactment of Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri before the moment passed. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak responds that a later session revisited the matter and the *takanah* was ultimately enacted — *achar kach chazru v'tiknu*. The force of stringency (*chumra*) ultimately prevailed.

The Gemara leaves this as a possible reading of Shmuel, but the explanation of Rav Ashi — that *zikah* is simply not strong enough to render the co-wife a full *ervah*, so *chalitzah* with the co-wife remains the superior act and releases the sister — is upheld by a *baraita* and stands as the primary resolution. The *baraita* confirms: *chalatz l'achayot lo nifteru tzarot, chalatz l'tzarot nifteru achayot*, and explains the reason as *d'kasavar yesh zikah v'lo almi zika l'shavyei l'tzara k'erva* — exactly as Rav Ashi formulated it. This closes the discussion on 26a.
