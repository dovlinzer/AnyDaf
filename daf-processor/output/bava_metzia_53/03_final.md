# Bava Metzia 53 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ona'ah & Deficient Coins

### Mishna Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנוֹתְנָהּ לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וְאֵינוֹ חוֹשֵׁשׁ, שֶׁאֵינוֹ אֶלָּא נֶפֶשׁ רָעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **may give** the slightly eroded coin **for** use in the desacralizing **of second-tithe** produce **and he** need **not be concerned, as** one who would refuse to accept a slightly eroded coin **is merely a miserly soul,** while the coin is in fact valid for any use.

The daf opens mid-discussion on 52b, continuing the treatment of *ona'ah* — not in the classic sense of overcharging or undercharging for goods, but in the context of coins that are below their proper weight. In the era under discussion, coins carried value roughly equivalent to their metal content, so a coin that was slightly deficient was genuinely worth somewhat less than its face value.

The *mishna* states that such a deficient coin may nonetheless be given for *ma'aser sheni*, and one need not be concerned about this, because anyone who refuses to accept it is simply a *nefesh ra'ah* — a stingy, miserly soul. The question is: precisely what type of deficiency is the *mishna* describing?

### Three Coin Scenarios

There are three possibilities. The coin may be lacking less than the amount of *ona'ah*, exactly the amount of *ona'ah*, or more than the amount of *ona'ah*. The third case is immediately set aside: it would be extreme to permit using such a coin for *ma'aser sheni* at face value. We are therefore left with the first two.

On the most natural reading, the *mishna* is speaking of a coin deficient by less than the threshold of *ona'ah*. In that case, a storekeeper would technically be entitled to refuse it — just as any seller has the right to reject non-standard tender — but someone who actually did so would be considered petty and miserly. Since ordinary people accept such a coin at face value, it may be used at face value for *ma'aser sheni*. This reading is not a great *chiddush*, however, since the coin's loss is genuinely negligible.

The more pointed reading is that the *mishna* permits treating the coin at face value even when it is deficient by a full twenty-fourth — the classic measure of *ona'ah*. Even though the recipient is technically entitled to demand reimbursement for the shortfall, normal practice is that people use such coins without protest. The *chiddush* would then be that despite this technical entitlement, one may use the coin at face value for *ma'aser sheni*. The concern, however, is that when one arrives in Jerusalem to spend the money, merchants there may not honor the face value either — which means *ma'aser sheni* may actually lose out in the transaction.

## Metal vs. Face Value

### Rav Papa's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הַאי מַאן דְּמוֹקֵים אַזּוּזֵי, מִיקְּרֵי נֶפֶשׁ רָעָה. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי הוּא דְּסָגֵי לְהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Conclude from** this formulation of the mishna that **this one who insists upon** the integrity of **his coins** and accepts only unflawed coins **is characterized as a miserly soul.** The Gemara adds: **And this matter** applies **only if** the flawed coins that he rejected still **circulate.**

Rav Papa derives a practical lesson from the *mishna*: someone who is excessively exacting about what currency he will accept, refusing coins that are slightly off, is called a *nefesh ra'ah*. However, this applies only when the coin genuinely has currency — when it circulates normally and merchants accept it. A storekeeper who refuses a coin that most people would take without hesitation is being unreasonably miserly. But if a coin has genuine difficulty circulating, then refusing it is not stinginess but simple prudence.

## Ona'ah & Deficient Coins — Chizkiyahu's Position

### Chizkiyahu's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְחִזְקִיָּה, דְּאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: בָּא לְפוֹרְטָהּ – פּוֹרְטָהּ בְּשׇׁוְיָהּ, בָּא לְחַלְּלָהּ – מְחַלְּלָהּ בְּיָפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: This **supports** the opinion **of Ḥizkiyya, as Ḥizkiyya says:** If one **comes to change** this flawed silver coin for copper coins, **he changes it for its value,** deducting several *perutot* due to erosion. If he **comes to desacralize** second tithe with it, **he desacralizes** the produce **with** it as though its value were that of **an unflawed [*beyafa*]** coin.

The *gemara* notes that the *mishna* appears to support Chizkiyahu's position. Chizkiyahu taught: if you come to exchange a deficient coin into smaller denominations (*ba le-forta*), you will only receive its actual metal value — it is worth less than its face value, and the market will price it accordingly. But if you come to transfer the *kedushah* of *ma'aser sheni* onto it (*ba le-chalela*), you may treat it as though it were a complete, minted coin — that is, you may use it at face value.

This framing suggests that the *mishna* is indeed speaking of a coin deficient by the full amount of *ona'ah*, because only then would the market refuse to give full face value in exchange. The *chiddush* is that even so, you may treat it as face value for purposes of *ma'aser sheni* — even though *ma'aser sheni* ends up losing slightly in the transaction, since the coin you redeem onto is worth less than its stated denomination.

## Metal vs. Face Value — Chizkiyahu Contradiction

### The Contradiction Emerges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּסָבַר חִזְקִיָּה דִּמְזַלְזְלִינַן בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי? וְהָאָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אוֹמֵר: הוּא וְחוּמְשׁוֹ מְחוּלָּל עַל מָעוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת. לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְאָדָם לְצַמְצֵם מְעוֹתָיו!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Is that **to say that Ḥizkiyya holds that we treat second tithe with contempt,** i.e., we redeem it for less than its actual value? **But doesn't Ḥizkiyya say:** In the case of **second-tithe** produce **that does not have the value of** even **one *peruta*** and therefore cannot be redeemed, one **says:** The second-tithe produce **and its one-fifth** that is added when one redeems his own second-tithe produce are **desacralized upon the first coins** upon which I already redeemed second-tithe produce worth at least one *peruta*, **because it is impossible for a person to be precise** with **his coins?** Presumably, the value of the coins with which he redeemed the produce somewhat exceeded the value of the produce. Therefore, he can desacralize additional produce worth less than one *peruta* with those coins. Apparently, Ḥizkiyya holds that one may not display contempt for second-tithe produce by redeeming it on coins worth less than its value.

The *gemara* now raises a sharp difficulty: does this position really reflect Chizkiyahu's view? For Chizkiyahu himself stated elsewhere that if one has *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *pruta*, one should declare: "It and its *chomesh* are hereby transferred to the earlier money" — meaning, transferred onto *ma'aser sheni* money one already possesses. The premise of this ruling is that any sum of money onto which *ma'aser sheni* has been transferred necessarily has some residual value that was not absorbed by the transfer, because it is impossible for a person to assess the exact value of his produce (*lefi she'ei efshar le'adam letzamtzem ma'osav*). The person redeeming always rounds in favor of *ma'aser sheni*, ensuring the money is worth at least as much as the produce. There is always, therefore, some sliver of monetary value left over in the coin beyond what was consecrated.

### The Deeper Contradiction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״בְּיָפָה״, בְּתוֹרַת יָפָה. דִּתְרֵי זִילֵי לָא מְזַלְזְלִינַן בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **What** is the meaning of ***beyafa*?** It means that although the coin has eroded, it is accorded **unflawed status,** and one may desacralize second-tithe produce with it. Nevertheless, it is assessed according to its actual, not its original, value, **as we do not treat** second tithe with **two** forms of **contempt.** One may use an eroded coin, but only according to its actual value.

But this means Chizkiyahu assumes that *ma'aser sheni* is always treated generously — that the money is never fully exhausted by the transfer, always leaving a surplus. This stands in direct tension with what the *mishna* appears to say: namely, that one may transfer *ma'aser sheni* onto a coin at face value even when the coin's metal value is less. In that case, *ma'aser sheni* loses value in the exchange rather than gaining it — precisely the opposite of Chizkiyahu's underlying assumption.

### Reinterpretation

The *gemara* therefore completely reinterprets both Chizkiyahu's statement and the *mishna*. The phrase *mechalelah be-yafeh* — "he redeems it with a fine coin" — does not mean at face value, as it appeared. Rather, following Rashi's reading, it means: treat this deficient coin the same way you would treat any full-value coin when using it for *ma'aser sheni*. And how do you treat a full-value coin? You do not fill it to capacity — you round in favor of *ma'aser sheni*, ensuring that the money retains a small surplus of unconsecrated value. The same applies here: use the deficient coin for *ma'aser sheni*, but transfer only the metal value onto it — not the face value — and leave the standard surplus.

This reading requires a significant stretch of the plain meaning, as Rashi himself acknowledges. What seemed to be a *mishna* telling us we may treat a deficient coin at its full face value for *ma'aser sheni* is now reread as telling us the opposite: we do not treat it at face value; we use only its actual metal value.

### Double Disgrace Principle

The *gemara* explains the underlying logic with the principle of *d'trei zimni lo mezalzalinan beih* — we do not treat *ma'aser sheni* with disrespect twice over. The *drasha* that *ma'aser sheni* must be transferred onto minted coins (*kesef she'yeish bo tzurah*, derived from *v'tzarta ha-kesef be-yadecha*) means that even using a coin that is slightly below its minted value is already one concession — one *zilzul*. We are not going to compound that by additionally pretending the coin is worth more than it actually is. Therefore, even when using such a coin for *ma'aser sheni*, one must calculate by the coin's true metal value, not its face value. The *mishna*'s point about *nefesh ra'ah* is thus reframed: it describes the attitude of a person who would refuse a coin that is nonetheless valid for circulation, not a permission to inflate that coin's worth for purposes of *ma'aser sheni*.

## Less Than Pruta

### The Problem of Sub-Pruta Amounts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. אָמַר חִזְקִיָּה: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אוֹמֵר: ״הוּא וְחוּמְשׁוֹ מְחוּלָּל עַל מָעוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת״, לְפִי שֶׁאִי אֶפְשָׁר לוֹ לְאָדָם לְצַמְצֵם מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** § Apropos the statement of Ḥizkiyya, the Gemara analyzes **the** matter **itself. Ḥizkiyya said:** In the case of **second-tithe** produce **that does not have the value of one *peruta*** and therefore cannot be redeemed, one **says:** The second-tithe produce **and its one-fifth** that is added when one redeems his own second-tithe produce are **desacralized upon the first coins** upon which I already redeemed second-tithe produce worth at least one *peruta*, **because it is impossible for a person to be precise** with **his coins.**

The *gemara* now turns to examine Chizkiyahu's other ruling in its own right. Chizkiyahu taught: *ma'aser sheni* that is worth less than a *pruta* — say, a single olive or two grains of wheat — presents a redemption problem. One might think to simply redeem it onto a *pruta* coin, but this cannot be done: a *pruta* is the minimum unit of monetary significance, and one cannot transfer *kedushah* from something worth less than a *pruta* onto an empty, unconsecrated coin. There is simply not enough value to initiate the transaction.

Chizkiyahu's solution is to say: "It and its *chomesh* are hereby transferred onto the earlier money." The person declares that this sub-*pruta* amount of *ma'aser sheni*, along with the requisite added fifth, is transferred onto money that already bears *kedushat ma'aser sheni* from a previous act of redemption. The logic is, as noted above, that any such money must have a residual sliver of unconsecrated value, because no person is ever able to calibrate the exact value of his produce. He always rounds up in favor of *ma'aser sheni*, so the money is always worth slightly more than what was transferred onto it. That residual — even if it is only a half-*pruta* or less — is enough to absorb the sub-*pruta* olive.

What is the precise mechanism here? It is admittedly somewhat elusive. If the problem is that one cannot initiate a transfer from something worth less than a *pruta*, why should it help that the money already has some *kedushah* on it? And conversely, if there is indeed residual value in the money, why not just transfer the half-*pruta* directly to a fresh coin? The answer Chizkiyahu implies is that the act of transfer works when appended to existing *ma'aser sheni* money — not as a standalone transaction — because the money already has an established *kedushah* identity, and this sub-*pruta* amount is being added to it, not initiating a new consecration from scratch.

### Davar She'yesh Lo Matirim — The Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בַּמַּעֲשֵׂר״ – לָאו מִכְּלָל דְּמַעֲשֵׂר בָּטֵיל בְּרוּבָּא? וְאִם אִיתָא דְּחִזְקִיָּה, הֲוָה לֵיהּ דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין. וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַתִּירִין, אֲפִילּוּ בְּאֶלֶף לֹא בָּטֵיל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of: **Which is not so with regard to** second **tithe?** Is it **not, by inference, that** second **tithe is negated in a majority** of non-sacred produce? **And if it is so that** the opinion of **Ḥizkiyya** is correct and even second-tithe produce worth less than one *peruta* can be redeemed, second tithe **is an item whose** prohibition **has permitting** factors, **and** the principle is that **any item whose** prohibition **has permitting** factors is **not negated even** if it is in a mixture **with one thousand** permitted parts.

This discussion of Chizkiyahu's solution brings us to a much broader issue: the principle of *davar she'yesh lo matirim*. Before reaching it, the *gemara* first sets up the relevant *mishna*, which lists the ways in which *terumah* and *bikurim* are treated more stringently than *ma'aser sheni*. Non-*kohanim* who eat *terumah* are liable to *mitat beit din* and must pay back the value plus a *chomesh*; *terumah* is nullified only in a ratio of one hundred and one; one must wash hands before touching it; and a *tamei kohen* must wait until nightfall after immersing before eating it. The *mishna* then says these rules *do not* apply to *ma'aser sheni*.

The most natural reading of the contrast regarding *bitul* is that *terumah* requires one hundred and one for nullification, whereas *ma'aser sheni* is nullified by a simple majority — or at minimum, by a standard amount, without needing the elevated ratio. The *gemara* then asks: if Chizkiyahu is correct that even sub-*pruta* amounts of *ma'aser sheni* can be redeemed, why should *ma'aser sheni* ever be subject to *bitul* at all?

The principle of *davar she'yesh lo matirim* — an item that has an alternative path to permissibility — holds that such an item cannot be nullified even in a thousand times its volume. The classic illustration is new grain (*chadash*) before the *omer* is brought: if it becomes mixed with permitted grain a week before Passover, it cannot be nullified, because in a matter of days it will become permitted on its own. There is no reason to employ *bitul* when a straightforward path to permissibility exists. The governing logic, as Rashi explains, is: since you have another option, use it — we will not let you eat the forbidden item through the legal fiction of *bitul* when you could simply wait.

Now, *ma'aser sheni* is forbidden to eat outside Jerusalem. It has a path to permissibility: either bring it to Jerusalem and eat it, or redeem it. True, *Tosafot* notes that going to Jerusalem when one is far away is not considered a "feasible alternative" in the way the principle envisions. But redemption is always available. So why do we ever permit *ma'aser sheni* to undergo *bitul*? It should always be a *davar she'yesh lo matirim* — just redeem it, and then eat it freely.

### The Answer: Irredeemable Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ, דְּתַנְיָא בְּהֶדְיָא: מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי בָּטֵיל בְּרוּבָּא. וּבְאֵיזֶה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי אָמְרוּ – בְּמַעֲשֵׂר שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וְשֶׁנִּכְנַס לִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְיָצָא. וְאִם אִיתָא לִדְחִזְקִיָּה, לֶיעְבֵּד לֵיהּ לִדְחִזְקִיָּה, וְנִיחַלְּלֵיהּ עַל מָעוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנוֹת! דְּלָא פָּרֵיק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara states: The inference that second tithe is not negated at all should **not enter your mind, as it is taught explicitly** in a *baraita*: **Second tithe is negated in** a simple **majority. And with regard to which second tithe did** the Sages **say** this? It is **with regard to** second **tithe that is not worth** even **one *peruta*, and which entered Jerusalem and exited.** The Gemara states its objection to the ruling of Ḥizkiyya: **And if it is so that** the opinion **of Ḥizkiyya** is correct and even second-tithe produce worth less than one *peruta* can be redeemed, **let him take action according to Ḥizkiyya and redeem** the second tithe **upon the first coins.** Therefore, as an item whose prohibition has permitting factors, it should not be negated at all. The Gemara answers: This is a case **where he did not redeem** his second tithe, and therefore he has no first coins upon which to redeem the produce.

The *gemara*'s answer is that the *braisa* identifying *ma'aser sheni* as subject to *bitul* must be speaking specifically of a case where redemption is impossible. That is the only scenario in which *ma'aser sheni* would have *ein lo matirim* — no alternative path — and would therefore be eligible for nullification. And what constitutes an unredeemable *ma'aser sheni*? The *braisa* itself supplies two scenarios: *ma'aser sheni* worth less than a *pruta*, and *ma'aser sheni* that was brought into Jerusalem and taken out. In both cases, no redemption is available, and therefore *bitul* can operate.

But this is precisely what collides with Chizkiyahu: according to his ruling, sub-*pruta* *ma'aser sheni* can indeed be redeemed — transferred onto existing *ma'aser sheni* money — and therefore it should retain the status of *davar she'yesh lo matirim* and never be *batel*. This is the contradiction that drives the next section.

## Braisa & Chizkiyahu — Reconciliation Attempts

### Practical Obstacles: Multiple Approaches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי מַעֲשֵׂר דְּאִית לֵיהּ (וְנִצְטָרְפִינְהוּ) [וּנְצָרֵף]! דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וּדְרַבָּנַן לָא מִצְטָרְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him bring** other second-**tithe** produce worth half a *peruta* **that he has, and join it** to the second tithe worth half a *peruta* intermingled with the non-sacred produce, and desacralize them together. It remains an item whose prohibition has permitting factors. The Gemara answers: Second tithe **by Torah law and** second tithe **by rabbinic law do not join.** By Torah law second tithe is negated in a majority of non-sacred produce and retains no sanctity, and it is by rabbinic law that an item whose prohibition has permitting factors is not negated. Therefore, the half-*peruta* of second tithe that he brought, which is not in a mixture and is second tithe by Torah law, cannot be redeemed.

The *gemara* asks: why not simply apply Chizkiyahu's solution? Transfer the sub-*pruta* amount onto existing *ma'aser sheni* money, and the problem dissolves. The *gemara* answers: suppose he has no such money — no funds onto which *ma'aser sheni* has previously been transferred. Then the *gemara* asks: why not get it? The more basic suggestion is: take other *ma'aser sheni* produce that you have not yet redeemed — say, a full quantity of olives — and combine it with this single olive that is mixed in a vat. Declare the whole lot redeemed onto a coin. Since the total sum is worth more than a *pruta*, there is no *pruta* problem, and Chizkiyahu's issue disappears.

The *gemara* rejects this on principled grounds: *de'oraisa* and *derabbanan* obligations cannot be combined (*d'Oraisa v'd'Rabbanan lo mitztarfi*). The unredeemed olives sitting in the warehouse are obligated in *ma'aser sheni* at the biblical level. The one olive mixed into the vat, however, is a different matter. Biblically, once produce mixes with non-*ma'aser* produce, it is *batel* — nullified. The entire discussion of whether it should not be *batel* because it is a *davar she'yesh lo matirim* is itself a rabbinic concern. Even if we ultimately say it is not *batel* due to *yesh lo matirim*, that identity is only rabbinic. So at most, the mixed olive has a *derabbanan* identity as *ma'aser sheni*. It therefore cannot be combined with the warehouse olives, which are biblical *ma'aser sheni*, to form a joint unit for redemption. The *d'Oraisa* and *d'Rabbanan* remain separate, and the sub-*pruta* problem reasserts itself for the residual.

### Demai Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי דְּמַאי! דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי וַדַּאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him bring** half a *peruta* of second-tithe produce from **doubtfully tithed produce [*demai*],** which is by rabbinic law, and join it to the intermingled half-*peruta*. The Gemara explains: One may not do so *ab initio* **lest he come to bring** a half-*peruta* from produce that is **definitely** untithed, as in practice one treats *demai* in the same manner that he treats untithed produce.

One might try to sidestep this by using *demai* — produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz*, which may or may not have had *ma'aser* separated, and is therefore only rabbinically obligated. If the warehouse olives are *demai*, both they and the mixed olive would share the same *derabbanan* status and could in principle be combined. The *gemara* rejects this as well: we will not permit the combination with *demai* because it will lead people to try it with produce that is fully *d'Oraisa* obligated. Since combining *d'Oraisa* with *d'Rabbanan* is invalid, and people will not maintain the distinction, the Sages preemptively prohibit even the technically valid *demai* combination.

### Two-Stage Transfer: The Elegant Approach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת, וּנְחַלֵּל עֲלַיְיהוּ מַעֲשֵׂר בִּפְרוּטָה וּמֶחֱצָה, וּנְחַלֵּל הַאי עַל הַיְאךְ יַתִּירָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And let him bring two *perutot* and desacralize** second **tithe** worth **one and a half *perutot* upon them, and desacralize this** half-*peruta* of second tithe **upon that remaining** half-*peruta*. 

The *gemara* then considers a more elegant solution: instead of combining simultaneously, perform the redemption in two stages. Take two *pruta* coins. Transfer *ma'aser sheni* worth one and a half *prutas* onto them. The first coin absorbs one *pruta*'s worth, and the second coin absorbs the remaining half *pruta*'s worth. Now the second coin has *kedushat ma'aser sheni* on part of it, and its remaining half *pruta* of unconsecrated value is available. At this point, Chizkiyahu's mechanism can operate: the sub-*pruta* olive can be transferred onto that half *pruta* of residual value in the already-consecrated coin.

### The Coin Absorption Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי סָבְרַתְּ: פְּרוּטָה וּמֶחֱצָה תָּפְסָה שְׁתֵּי פְרוּטוֹת? לָא. פְּרוּטָה תָּפְסָה פְּרוּטָה, וַחֲצִי פְּרוּטָה לָא תָּפְסָה. הֲדַר הָוְיָא לֵיהּ דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וּדְרַבָּנַן. וּדְאוֹרָיְיתָא וּדְרַבָּנַן לָא מִצְטָרְפִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this: **Do you hold** that the sanctity of second-tithe produce worth **one and a half *perutot* takes effect** on **two *perutot*? No,** the sanctity of **one *peruta* takes effect** on **one *peruta*** of the coins, **and** the sanctity of the **half-*peruta*** of produce **does not take effect** on anything. Once **again** it **becomes** a case of one half-*peruta* of produce that is second tithe **by Torah law and** the half-*peruta* in mixture that is second tithe **by rabbinic law, and** second tithe **by Torah law and** second tithe **by rabbinic law do not join.**

The *gemara* refutes this with a subtle but decisive point: *pruta tafsa pruta, v'chatzi pruta lo tafsa* — a *pruta* coin absorbs a *pruta*'s worth, and a half *pruta* does not absorb anything. The problem is that the two *pruta* coins are not one large coin; they are two separate, discrete vessels for *kedushah*. When one and a half *prutas*' worth of *ma'aser sheni* is to be transferred, the first coin takes exactly one *pruta*, which fills it precisely. The remaining half *pruta* of *ma'aser sheni* value is now left facing a second coin that is completely empty — unconsecrated. And one cannot initiate a transfer of less than a *pruta* onto an empty, unconsecrated coin. That was the entire problem Chizkiyahu was trying to solve in the first place.

This would not be a problem with a single larger coin. If one starts with an *issar* or a *sela* — a coin worth multiple *prutas* — and transfers one and a half *prutas*' worth of *ma'aser sheni* onto it, the coin is not filled; the surplus remains in the coin as a unit, and Chizkiyahu's solution can operate on that surplus. The problem arises specifically when one uses two separate *pruta* coins and treats them as a combined unit: the halakhah does not permit that. Each coin stands alone. The thinking here becomes, as one might note, suddenly very concrete — these are discrete *kedushah* containers, and half a *pruta* simply does not "stick" to an empty one.

### Impracticality Defense

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנַיְתֵי אִיסָּר! דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְאֵתוֹיֵי פְּרוּטוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And let him bring an *issar*,** worth eight *perutot*, and redeem second tithe worth almost that much, and redeem the intermingled half-*peruta* of second tithe upon the rest. The Gemara answers: One may not do so *ab initio*, **lest he come to bring *perutot*** to redeem the produce, in which case the sanctity of the tithe will not take effect on a half-*peruta*, and the remedy will be ineffective.

The *gemara* ultimately permits using a single larger coin to accomplish the two-stage transfer — but then withdraws that permission: *dilma ati l'atuyei prutas* — we are concerned that people will come to try it with two separate *pruta* coins, which does not work, and will err. Since the practical details are subtle enough that the average person will not appreciate the distinction between using a single large coin and using two separate *pruta* coins of equivalent total value, the Sages do not permit even the valid method.

The consequence of this refusal is significant: because we are not going to allow Chizkiyahu's solution to be implemented in practice, the sub-*pruta* *ma'aser sheni* effectively has no path to permissibility — *ein lo matirim* — and it therefore may undergo *bitul*. Chizkiyahu's ruling stands in theory; the *braisa*'s ruling stands in practice.

## Jerusalem Entry/Exit

### The Second Scenario: Walls Falling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁנִּכְנַס לִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְיָצָא. וְאַמַּאי: וְלִיהְדַּר וּנְעַיְּילֵיהּ! בְּשֶׁנִּטְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the *baraita*: **And which entered Jerusalem and exited.** The Gemara asks: **And why** is the matter of negation in a majority relevant? **Let him bring it back** into Jerusalem and partake of it there. The Gemara answers: The reference is to second tithe **that became ritually impure** outside Jerusalem.

The *braisa* listed a second scenario in which *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed: it was brought into Jerusalem and taken out. The *gemara* asks the obvious question: why not simply walk back into Jerusalem and eat it there? That requires no redemption at all.

### Rabbi Elazar's Redemption Option

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִפְרְקֵיהּ! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לְמַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי שֶׁנִּטְמָא, שֶׁפּוֹדִין אוֹתוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי לֹא תוּכַל שְׂאֵתוֹ״, וְאֵין ״שְׂאֵת״ אֶלָּא אֲכִילָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשָּׂא מַשְׂאֹת מֵאֵת פָּנָיו״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let him redeem it,** in accordance with the statement of Rabbi Elazar? **As Rabbi Elazar says: From where** is it derived **with regard to second-tithe** produce **that became ritually impure that one may redeem it even in Jerusalem,** although ritually pure second tithe cannot be desacralized in Jerusalem? It is **as it is stated: "For you are unable to carry [*se'et*] it…and you shall turn it into money, and bind up the money in your hand" (Deuteronomy 14:24–25). And *se'et* means nothing other than eating, as it is stated: "And he took portions [*masot*] from before him"** (Genesis 43:34). Since ritually impure second-tithe produce may not be consumed, Rabbi Elazar holds that one may desacralize it even if it had been brought into Jerusalem.

The answer is that the produce became *tamei* — ritually impure — and therefore cannot be eaten in its current state even in Jerusalem. But then, the *gemara* says, redeem it: Rabbi Elazar taught that *ma'aser sheni* that became *tamei* may be redeemed even within Jerusalem, despite the general rule that *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed once it has entered Jerusalem. The source is the verse *ki lo tukhal se'eto* — "for you cannot carry it" — understood through the *drasha* that *se'et* refers to eating (as in *vayisa mas'et me'et panav*, where Yosef sends portions of food to his brothers). If you cannot *eat* it because it is *tamei*, the Torah permits redemption even inside Jerusalem — redeem it for money, use the money to purchase *tahor* produce, and eat that.

So we are back to the original question. If the produce is *tahor*, just go back into Jerusalem and eat it. If it became *tamei*, redeem it per Rabbi Elazar. Why is this ever *ein lo matirim*?

### Second-Generation Ma'aser Sheni

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּלָקוּחַ בְּכֶסֶף מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *halakha* of the *baraita* is taught not with regard to second-tithe produce, but **with regard to** food **acquired with second-tithe money,** which cannot be desacralized.

The *gemara* answers: the *braisa* is not speaking of the original *ma'aser sheni* produce itself, but of produce purchased with *ma'aser sheni* money — second-generation *ma'aser sheni*. One had produce in the field, transferred its *kedushah* to money, traveled to Jerusalem, and used that money to purchase food. That purchased food, carrying *kedushat ma'aser sheni*, was subsequently removed from Jerusalem. The assumption here is that second-generation *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed — only the first-generation produce may have its *kedushah* transferred to money; once it has already been transferred once, the resulting food is not subject to a second redemption.

The *gemara* briefly considers whether Rabbi Yehuda's position — that *tamei* second-generation *ma'aser sheni* must be buried rather than redeemed — might resolve matters, but rejects this approach. If Rabbi Yehuda's stringency is the basis for *ein lo matirim*, the *braisa* need not have mentioned that the produce was taken *out* of Jerusalem, since the prohibition on redemption of *tamei* purchased produce would apply regardless of location.

### Walls as the Key

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא ״יָצָא״? אֲפִילּוּ לֹא יָצָא נָמֵי, אֶלָּא: לְעוֹלָם בְּטָהוֹר, וּמַאי ״יָצָא״ – דִּנְפוּל מְחִיצוֹת. וְהָאָמַר רָבָא: מְחִיצָה לֶאֱכוֹל – דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, מְחִיצוֹת לִקְלוֹט – דְּרַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so, why** did the *tanna* teach **specifically** a case where the food **exited** Jerusalem? **Even** if it **did not exit** Jerusalem, that *halakha* **also** applies, as he holds that once the food becomes ritually impure it must be buried. **Rather, actually,** it is taught in the *baraita* **with regard to** second tithe that is **ritually pure, and what** is the meaning of **exited?** It is not that the produce actually exited Jerusalem. Rather, the *baraita* is discussing a case **where the partitions,** i.e., the walls, surrounding the city **fell.** The legal status of that second-tithe produce is that of produce that exited the city. And Rava states: The requirement that **a partition** enable one **to eat** second-tithe produce is **by Torah law,** while the principle that **partitions** of the city **gather** second-tithe produce into the city—preventing its subsequent redemption—is **by rabbinic law.**

Setting aside Rabbi Yehuda, the *gemara* pursues a different resolution. The case is: *tahor* produce, not second-generation, but *ma'aser sheni* that was brought inside Jerusalem's walls — and then the walls fell. The produce did not move; Jerusalem moved, in a sense. With the walls down, the produce can no longer be eaten within Jerusalem, because the *d'Oraisa* obligation is to eat *ma'aser sheni* within the walls. At the same time, it cannot be redeemed, because — and here Rava's principle becomes decisive — the rule that *ma'aser sheni* cannot be redeemed after entering Jerusalem is only *d'Rabbanan*. One might therefore think that once the walls fall, the Sages would not apply their rabbinic prohibition and would permit redemption.

Rava states explicitly: *mechitzah le'echol d'Oraisa, mechitzah liklot d'Rabbanan* — the requirement that Jerusalem's walls be standing in order to *eat* there is biblical; the principle that the walls "absorb" the *ma'aser sheni* and prevent its subsequent redemption is rabbinic.

### The Categorical Rabbinic Decree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי גְּזַרוּ דְּרַבָּנַן – כִּי אִיתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת. כִּי לֵיתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת – לָא גְּזַרוּ רַבָּנַן! לָא פְּלוּג רַבָּנַן בֵּין אִיתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת, בֵּין לֵיתַנְהוּ לִמְחִיצוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** When the Sages issued a decree that partitions gather second-tithe produce in terms of their being considered within the city, they did so only **where there are** intact **partitions,** but **where there are no** intact **partitions, the Sages did not issue a decree?** The Gemara answers: Once they issued the decree, **the Sages did not distinguish between** cases where **there are** intact **partitions** and cases where **there are not** intact **partitions.** Once the Sages issued the decree with regard to partitions and the produce being gathered they applied it globally.

But the *gemara* rules: *lo plug Rabbanan* — the Sages did not distinguish. They enacted a categorical rule that *ma'aser sheni* brought within Jerusalem's walls cannot be redeemed, whether those walls currently stand or not. The Sages made no exception for the case in which the walls subsequently fell. Therefore, the produce that entered Jerusalem cannot be redeemed (rabbinic enactment, no exception), and cannot be eaten (walls are gone, *d'Oraisa* requirement unmet), and is thus in a state of genuine *ein lo matirim* — there is no path to permissibility. This is the scenario in which *bitul* operates.

### Final Reconciliation: Both Conditions Required

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, מַאי אִירְיָא אֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה? אֲפִילּוּ יֵשׁ בּוֹ נָמֵי! לָא מִיבַּעְיָא קָאָמַר. לָא מִיבַּעְיָא יֵשׁ בּוֹ, דְּקָלְטָן לֵיהּ מְחִיצוֹת. אֲבָל אֵין בּוֹ, אֵימָא: לָא קְלַטוּ לֵיהּ מְחִיצוֹת, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **If so, why** did the *tanna* teach **specifically** a case where the produce **does not have the value of one *peruta*?** The same would hold true **even if it has** the value of one *peruta*. The Gemara answers: The *tanna* **is speaking** utilizing the style of: **It is not necessary. It is not necessary** to state that if the produce **has** the value of one *peruta* the *halakha* is **that the partitions gather it** and it can no longer be redeemed, **but** if **it does not have** the value of one *peruta*, **say** that **the partitions do not gather it.** Therefore, **he teaches us** that the partitions gather even second-tithe produce worth less than one *peruta*, and it cannot be redeemed.

The *gemara*'s final answer reconciles everything: the *braisa* is speaking of a single compound case — *ma'aser sheni* that is worth less than a *pruta* and was brought into Jerusalem and taken out (i.e., the walls fell). Both conditions must be present simultaneously. The walls scenario alone might, in principle, raise questions about whether walls really "absorb" a sub-*pruta* amount — one might argue that something without the minimum monetary value was never truly "held" by the *mechitzot* in the first place, and perhaps it could still be redeemed even after entry. The *braisa* therefore teaches us that even in the sub-*pruta* case, the walls did absorb it and the prohibition on redemption applies.

By requiring both conditions, the *braisa* sidesteps the Chizkiyahu objection as well: it is not merely the sub-*pruta* amount alone that generates *ein lo matirim*, but the sub-*pruta* amount combined with the impossibility of redemption due to the walls — circumstances under which even Chizkiyahu's mechanism cannot operate. The resolution is thus not that Chizkiyahu is wrong, but that the *braisa* is describing a scenario in which his solution is inapplicable, leaving the *ma'aser sheni* genuinely without any path to permissibility and therefore eligible for *bitul*.
