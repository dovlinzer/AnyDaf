# Menachot 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Klei Shares & Mincha

### Rav: Sanctify Alone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָמַר: מִנְחָה קְדוּשָׁה בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּלֶחֶם הַפָּנִים שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה שֶׁמֶן וּקְדוּשַׁת כְּלִי מְקַדְּשַׁתָּהּ. וּבְלֹא לְבוֹנָה, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמִנְחַת נְסָכִים שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לְבוֹנָה וּקְדוּשַׁת כְּלִי מְקַדְּשַׁתָּהּ. וּבְלֹא שֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה, שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא שֶׁאֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה לֹא שֶׁמֶן וְלֹא לְבוֹנָה וּקְדוּשַׁת כְּלִי מְקַדְּשַׁתָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: A meal offering is sanctified** by a service vessel **without oil,** since **we find** that the **shewbread,** which **does not require oil,** is sanctified by **the sanctity of** the service **vessel. And** it is sanctified **without frankincense,** since **we find** that the **meal offering** that accompanies the **libations,** which **does not require frankincense,** is sanctified by **the sanctity of** the service **vessel. And** it is sanctified **without oil and frankincense,** since **we find** that the **meal offering of a sinner,** which **does not require either oil or frankincense,** is sanctified by **the sanctity of** the service **vessel.**

The major theme of today's daf concerns the mechanics of *klei sharet* — the sacred Temple vessels — and specifically the question of whether they can sanctify a *mincha* even when not all of its required ingredients are simultaneously present in the vessel.

Rav states: *mincha kedusha belo shemen* — a *mincha* will be sanctified by the *kli sharet* even before the oil has been added. His proof is *shekein matzinu beLechem hapanim*: the *lechem hapanim* is a *mincha* that contains no oil, yet the *klei sharet* sanctify it. He extends this further: without *levona*, *shekein matzinu beminchat nesachim* — the *mincha* accompanying animal offerings contains no frankincense, yet the *klei sharet* function there. And even without both oil and frankincense, *shekein matzinu beminchat chotei* — the sinner's *mincha* contains neither, yet the *klei sharet* sanctify it nonetheless.

This line of reasoning is, on its face, surprising. One might object that in each of these proof-cases, the absence of the ingredient is entirely normative — that is simply how that particular *mincha* is meant to be brought. We are asking something different: whether the *klei sharet* sanctify the flour of a *mincha* that *does* require oil or *levona*, even before those ingredients have been added. The Gemara, however, frames this as *milta mimilta* — reasoning by analogy across cases even where the comparison is imperfect. Because the fundamental question is about the operative power of *klei sharet* and the nature of *kedushat klei sharet*, the analogies remain useful even if they are not perfectly parallel. If the *klei sharet* can function in those cases, it illuminates how *klei sharet* operate in general.

The practical implication of the flour being rendered *kadosh* at that early stage is primarily one of *chumra*: once the flour has intrinsic *kedusha* — *kedushat haguf* — it can no longer be redeemed. If it subsequently becomes *tamei* or otherwise *pasul*, it must be burned rather than redeemed.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁמֶן וּלְבוֹנָה קָדְשִׁים הֵם בְּלֹא זוֹ וּזוֹ בְּלֹא זוֹ, שֶׁמֶן – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בְּלוֹג שֶׁמֶן שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע שֶׁהִיא קָדְשׁוּת כְּלִי. לְבוֹנָה – שֶׁכֵּן מָצִינוּ בִּלְבוֹנָה בַּבָּזִיכִין שֶׁהִיא קָדְשׁוּת כְּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** Not only the flour but **each of the other components individually: the oil and the frankincense are sanctified by a service vessel, each without the other.** **Oil** — since **we find** that the ***log*** of oil **of a leper** is sanctified by a service **vessel.** **Frankincense** — since **we find** that the frankincense placed in the **bowls** of the shewbread is sanctified by a service **vessel.**

Not only the flour but each of the other components individually: *veshemen ulevona kadshi hai belo hai vehai belo hai* — the oil and the frankincense can each be sanctified on their own in a *kli sharet*. For oil, the proof is *shekein matzinu belog shemen shel metzora* — the *log* of oil brought for a *metzora* is an independent offering placed in a *kli sharet*, demonstrating that *klei sharet* can sanctify oil alone. For frankincense, *shekein matzinu belevona habaa bevezikin* — the frankincense placed in the small spoons alongside the *lechem hapanim* is sanctified independently. The lesson is that since each of these ingredients can be sanctified alone when they *are* the entire offering, the *klei sharet* can similarly sanctify them even when they are merely components of a larger *mincha*.

### R. Chanina: All Together

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא זוֹ קְדוֹשָׁה בְּלֹא זוֹ וְלֹא זוֹ קְדוֹשָׁה בְּלֹא זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Neither** is **this** substance **sanctified without that, nor is that sanctified without this.**

Rabbi Chanina disagrees fundamentally: *lo zu kadesha belo zu velo zu kadesha belo zu* — one ingredient cannot be sanctified without the other; all ingredients of the *mincha* must be present together in the *kli* for sanctification to take effect. This is, it must be said, a very reasonable position on its own terms.

### Issaron Anointed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְרַבִּי חֲנִינָא, עִשָּׂרוֹן לָמָּה נִמְשַׁח? (וַהֲלֹא אֵינוֹ עָשׂוּי אֶלָּא לִמְדִידַת קֶמַח בִּלְבַד, וְהַקֶּמַח אֵינוֹ קָדוֹשׁ בְּלֹא שֶׁמֶן!) לְמִנְחַת חוֹטֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And according to** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥanina, for what** purpose was the vessel that measured **a tenth** of an ephah **anointed,** making it possible for it to sanctify items placed inside it? This vessel **was fashioned only for measuring flour, and** according to Rabbi Ḥanina **the flour is not sanctified without oil.** What then does this vessel sanctify? The Gemara answers: The vessel was anointed **for** the purpose of sanctifying the **meal offering of a sinner,** which contains neither oil nor frankincense.

The Gemara now presses a question on Rabbi Chanina's position from an unexpected direction. Among the sacred vessels of the *Mikdash*, a vessel of the *issaron* measure — one-tenth of an *eifah*, approximately 2.49 liters, which is the minimum and standard flour measure for a *mincha* — was anointed with the *shemen hamishcha* and consecrated as a *kli sharet*. The Gemara asks: *verabbi Chanina issaron lama nimshach* — according to Rabbi Chanina, why was it necessary to anoint the *issaron* and make it a *kli sharet* at all?

The difficulty is as follows: if sanctification requires all ingredients to be present simultaneously, and a standard *mincha* consists of an *issaron* of flour together with a *log* of oil — approximately 0.6 liters — then the oil always exceeds the *issaron* measure. One would never place the flour alone into the *issaron* vessel expecting sanctification, because without the oil already present nothing is sanctified. The measuring vessel would function as an ordinary measuring cup, with no need for it to be a *kli sharet*.

The Gemara answers: *leminchat chotei* — the *minchat chotei* is brought without oil, and its measure is exactly an *issaron*. The sinner's *mincha* consists solely of flour in the amount of an *issaron*, and precisely for that case, the *issaron* vessel must be a *kli sharet* in order to sanctify it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלוֹג, לָמָּה נִמְשַׁח? לְלוֹג שֶׁל מְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further asks: **And for what** purpose was the vessel that measured one ***log*** of oil **anointed?** After all, according to Rabbi Ḥanina oil for a meal offering cannot be sanctified by itself. The Gemara explains: It was anointed **for** the purpose of sanctifying the ***log*** of oil **of a leper,** which is not brought as part of a meal offering. This oil is sanctified without flour or frankincense.

Similarly the Gemara asks: *halog lama nimshach* — why was the *log* measure itself consecrated as a *kli sharet*? Since oil is always mixed with flour, one might think a measuring vessel for oil alone would never be needed for sanctification. The Gemara answers: *lelog shel metzora* — the *log* of oil brought for the *metzora* is placed in a *kli sharet* and sanctified independently, and it is for this case that the *log* vessel required consecration.

### Shmuel: Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף שְׁמוּאֵל סָבַר לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַב, דִּתְנַן: כְּלֵי הַלַּח מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַלַּח, וּמִדּוֹת הַיָּבֵשׁ מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ, וְאֵין כְּלֵי הַלַּח מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ, וְלֹא מִדּוֹת הַיָּבֵשׁ מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַלַּח. וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא מִדּוֹת, אֲבָל מִזְרָקוֹת מְקַדְּשׁוֹת אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״שְׁנֵיהֶם מְלֵאִים סֹלֶת בְּלוּלָה בַשֶּׁמֶן לְמִנְחָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And Shmuel also holds in accordance with this** statement **of Rav,** that a service vessel sanctifies the flour of a meal offering even without its oil, **as we learned** in a mishna: **The vessels** used for **liquids sanctify** only **the liquids, and the** vessels that serve as **dry measures sanctify** only **the dry** goods. **But the vessels** used for **liquids do not sanctify the dry** goods, **and the** vessels that serve as **dry measures do not sanctify the liquids. And Shmuel says:** With regard to this mishna: **They taught** that vessels used for liquids do not sanctify dry goods **only** with regard to vessels used as **measures. But bowls** that are used for collecting and tossing the **blood** of offerings **sanctify the dry goods** as well, **as it is stated** with regard to the offerings of the princes brought during the inauguration of the Tabernacle: **"Both of them full of fine flour mixed with oil for a meal offering"** (Numbers 7:13), which indicates that the bowl sanctifies meal offerings, which are dry.

The Gemara adds that even Shmuel holds of Rav's position that *klei sharet* can sanctify the flour independently: *veaf Shmuel savar la leha deRav*. The proof comes from a *mishna* teaching that *klei halach* — liquid vessels — sanctify liquids, and *middot hayavesh* — dry measuring vessels — sanctify dry goods. Neither type sanctifies that which belongs to the other category. To this, Shmuel adds: *lo shanu ella middot* — this rule of strict correspondence between vessel and content applies only to measuring vessels. But *aval mizrakos* — the large non-measuring bowls used for receiving and tossing blood — *mekadeshos es hayavesh*: they would sanctify dry goods as well. A non-measuring vessel of the type ordinarily used for liquids can nonetheless sanctify dry goods when dry goods are placed in it.

Shmuel derives this from the verse describing what the *nesi'im* — the tribal princes — brought: *sheneihem meleim soles belula bashemen lemincha*, "both of them filled with fine flour mixed with oil for a *mincha*." These were *mizrakot*, and they were used for flour mixed with oil — demonstrating that a vessel ordinarily associated with liquids can sanctify a food that is dry in nature.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא מִדִּפְתִּי לְרָבִינָא: מִנְחָה לַחָה הִיא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לַיָּבֵשׁ שֶׁבָּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues its proof: And **Rav Aḥa of Difti said to Ravina,** with regard to this derivation of Shmuel: But the **meal offering** of the verse **is** also considered **a liquid,** as it is mingled with oil. How then can one derive from it the *halakha* with regard to items that are entirely dry? Ravina **said to him:** The verse cited by Shmuel **is necessary only to** teach that **the dry** part **of** a meal offering is sanctified by the bowls as well.

This raises an immediate difficulty: the *mincha* of the *nesi'im* was mixed with oil and therefore moist, not dry. How does this prove anything about sanctifying something dry? The Gemara's answer, *lo nitzrecha ela l'yavesh shebah*, is that the proof concerns the "dry part within it." Rashi understands this to mean that in any *mincha*, the oil is never perfectly distributed and there will inevitably be clumps of flour without oil; the vessel nonetheless sanctifies even those dry portions. This explanation is, admittedly, not entirely satisfying, and Tosafot also struggles to find a fully coherent proof.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אֵין מִנְחָה קְדוֹשָׁה עַד שֶׁיְּהוּ כּוּלָּן, יָבֵשׁ שֶׁבָּהּ הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? וַהֲלֹא כּוּלָּן לַחִים הֵן מִפְּנֵי הַשֶׁמֶן! אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַאי בְּלֹא הַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara concludes its proof with regard to Shmuel's opinion: **And if it enters your mind** that **Shmuel holds** that **a meal offering is not sanctified** by a service vessel **until all** of its components **are** together in the vessel, then **how can you find** a case where the **dry** parts **of** a meal offering are by themselves? **Is it not** correct that when meal offerings are sanctified, **all of them are liquids, due to the oil** that is mixed with them? **Rather, conclude from** here that **Shmuel holds** that **this** substance may be sanctified **without that** one.

A gloss in the text offers an alternative: the "dry part" refers to the *levona* — the frankincense placed in or alongside the *mincha* vessel. If Shmuel did not accept the principle that *hai belo hai* — one ingredient can be sanctified independently — then how could he cite this verse at all as proof that *mizrakot* sanctify dry goods? The *levona* would have been rendered moist from contact with the oily flour underneath it. The fact that Shmuel considers this a valid proof demonstrates that he must be assuming the *levona* was placed in the vessel separately — as an independent dry ingredient — and was sanctified on its own. The very intelligibility of Shmuel's argument thus implies that he accepts the principle that *klei sharet* can sanctify independent ingredients.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִנְחָה, לְגַבֵּי דָּם – כְּיָבֵשׁ דָּמְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead in answer to Rav Aḥa of Difti's question: **A meal offering,** even when it is mixed with oil, is, **relative to blood, considered as a dry** item. Accordingly, one may derive from the verse that the bowls sanctify dry items, and just as a bowl sanctifies a meal offering that contains oil, as it is considered dry in comparison to blood, so too, it sanctifies a meal offering that is entirely dry, i.e., that contains no oil, as claimed by Rav.

The Gemara closes with another answer: *mincha legabei dam ke'yavesh damya* — relative to blood, even a *mincha* mixed with oil is "dry." The *mizrak* is fundamentally a vessel for blood; when flour mixed with oil is placed in it, even though the flour mixture is moist, it is still categorically drier than blood. Therefore, if such a vessel can sanctify what is placed in it, we have demonstrated that a liquid vessel can sanctify something that does not belong to its primary domain. This is a more satisfying proof, even if it is not a demonstration of sanctifying something perfectly and utterly dry.

The bottom line is that Shmuel's statement, despite the interpretive difficulties, is read by the Gemara as consistent with Rav's position — that *klei sharet* can sanctify ingredients independently.

## Heichal Service

### Shlamim OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלָמִים שֶׁשְּׁחָטָן בַּהֵיכָל – כְּשֵׁרִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וּשְׁחָטוֹ פֶּתַח אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד״, וְלֹא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara discusses **the** matter **itself. Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Peace offerings that** were **slaughtered in the Sanctuary are valid, as it is stated: "And slaughter it at the entrance of the Tent of Meeting"** (Leviticus 3:2), i.e., in the courtyard. **And** it is logical that the *halakha* with regard to the **minor** area, i.e., the courtyard, **should not be more stringent than** the *halakha* with regard to **the major** one, the Tent of Meeting or the Sanctuary.

Rabbi Yochanan states: *shlamim sheshachatan baheichal kesheirim* — *shlamim* slaughtered in the *heichal* are valid. The source is the verse *veshachato pesach ohel moed* — "and he shall slaughter it at the opening of the Tent of Meeting." The *heichal* is, in a sense, the interior of the *ohel moed* itself. Since the verse orients the *shechita* in relation to the *ohel moed*, if anything, performing the *shechita* inside the *ohel moed* should be considered even more closely tied to its stated location. *Velo yehei tafel chamur min ha'ikar* — the derivative cannot be stricter than the fundamental: since slaughtering near the *ohel moed* is valid, slaughtering within it is at least equally so.

### Eating Inside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתִירָא אוֹמֵר, מִנַּיִן שֶׁאִם הִקִּיפוּ גּוֹיִם אֶת הָעֲזָרָה, שֶׁהַכֹּהֲנִים נִכְנָסִין לַהֵיכָל וְאוֹכְלִין בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים וּשְׁיָרֵי מְנָחוֹת? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״בְּקֹדֶשׁ הַקֳּדָשִׁים תֹּאכְלֶנּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan from a *baraita*. **Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says: From where** is it derived **that if gentiles surrounded the** Temple **courtyard** and were firing projectiles inside to the point that it became impossible to remain in the courtyard on account of the threat, **that the priests enter the Sanctuary and partake of the offerings of the most sacred order and the remainders of** the **meal offerings** while inside the Sanctuary? **The verse states: "Every meal offering of theirs, and every sin offering of theirs, and every guilt offering of theirs, which they may render unto Me, shall be most holy for you and for your sons. In the Sanctuary you shall eat them"** (Numbers 18:9–10). This indicates that although the mitzva is to consume offerings of the most sacred order in the courtyard, in certain instances the priests may consume these offerings inside the Sanctuary, the most holy place.

A *braita* is quoted in which Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira asks: how do we know that if non-Jews have surrounded the *azara* and the *kohanim* have retreated into the *heichal*, they are permitted to eat *kodshei kodashim* and the remnants of *menachot* there? The verse teaches — at the opening of the ninth amud — *bekodesh hakodashim tochelenu*, "you shall eat it in the Holy of Holies." The reference is slightly out of context, being applied to the *heichal* rather than the innermost sanctum, but the *drasha* uses it to extend eating permission into the interior of the *Mikdash*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא? לֵימָא: ״בַּחֲצַר אֹהֶל מוֹעֵד יֹאכְלוּהָ״, וְלֹא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its objection: **But** according to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan, **why do I** need this **verse? Let him say** here as well that as the verse states: **"In the court of the Tent of Meeting they shall eat it"** (Leviticus 6:9), i.e., in the Temple courtyard, it is logical that the *halakha* with regard to the **minor** area **should not be more stringent than** the *halakha* with regard to **the major** one, i.e., if one may consume a peace offering in the Temple courtyard then all the more so may he consume it in the Sanctuary.

The Gemara raises a challenge: why does Rabbi Yehuda ben Beseira need a verse at all? The same *kal vachomer* used to permit *shechita* in the *heichal* should apply here. The verse already says *bechatzer ohel moed yocheluha* — "in the courtyard of the Tent of Meeting you shall eat it." The *ohel moed* is referenced for eating just as for *shechita*; therefore, by the same logic, eating inside the *ohel moed* should certainly be valid. Why is a separate verse required?

### Eating Undignified

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲבוֹדָה, דְּאָדָם עוֹבֵד בִּמְקוֹם רַבּוֹ, אָמְרִינַן שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר. אֲכִילָה, שֶׁאֵין אָדָם אוֹכֵל בִּמְקוֹם רַבּוֹ, טַעְמָא דִּכְתַב קְרָא, הָא לָא כְּתַב קְרָא – לֹא יְהֵא טָפֵל חָמוּר מִן הָעִיקָּר לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: Consuming an offering is not the same as slaughtering it. The slaughter of an offering is part of the sacrificial **service,** and it is not considered disrespectful **for a person** to **serve** his master **in the place of his master,** i.e., within the Sanctuary as well as in the courtyard. Therefore, **we say that** the *halakha* with regard to the **minor** area **should not be more stringent than** the *halakha* with regard to **the major** one. By contrast, with regard to the **consumption** of an offering, **since a person may not eat in the place of his master, the** only **reason** that it is permitted to consume an offering inside the Sanctuary is **that** it is **written** in **the verse:** "In a most holy place you shall eat them." **Had** this **not** been **written** in **the verse** explicitly, **we** would **not say** that the *halakha* with regard to the **minor** area **should not be more stringent than** the *halakha* with regard to **the major** one.

The Gemara answers with a fundamental distinction: *avodah* — ritual service — is something a person properly performs before his master, *de'adam oved bemakom rabbo amrinan* — we accept that service takes place in the master's domain. If one may serve one's master in the courtyard outside his inner chamber, one may certainly serve him inside the chamber as well. Therefore the *kal vachomer* works for *shechita* and *kemitza*. But *achila* — eating — is different: *she'ein adam ochel bemakom rabbo*, a person does not normally eat in the presence of his master. Eating is at once more personal and, in some sense, more undignified. It satisfies one's appetite; it is messy; it is not the kind of activity one performs in the immediate presence of one's superior. Therefore we would never have extended eating into the *heichal* by *kal vachomer*, and a specific verse was required to permit it.

This point illuminates why the *braita* framed its case so dramatically — with *kohanim* besieged by enemies who have overrun the *azara*. It is not that eating in the *heichal* is technically forbidden; the verse explicitly permits it. But it is something reserved for circumstances where there is truly no other option, because it is not *mipnei kavod* — not appropriate to be eating before one's Master in that way. The Gemara notes that while the eating of the *kohanim* is itself part of the *avoda* — *kohanim ochlim ube'alim miskaprin*, the owners achieve atonement through the *kohanim*'s eating — there is nonetheless something qualitatively less formal about eating that makes it inappropriate *bemakom rabbo*.

## Mincha Location

### Outside Azara

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: בְּלָלָהּ חוּץ לְחוֹמַת עֲזָרָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: פְּסוּלָה, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: כְּשֵׁרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** If one **mixed** the oil of a meal offering into **it outside the wall of** the Temple **courtyard, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that it is **disqualified,** and **Reish Lakish says** that it is **valid.**

Turning to the preparation of the *mincha* itself, the Gemara now addresses a question of location: what if the *blila* — the mixing of the oil into the flour — was performed *chutz lechomat azara*, outside the walls of the *azara*?

To appreciate the question, it is worth clarifying the process. The owner of a *mincha* brings the flour and oil from home, mixes them together in a *kli sharet*, and brings it to the *kohen*, who then takes the *kemitza*. The verse *mikmitza ve'eilach mitzvas kehuna* — "from the *kemitza* onwards is the mitzvah of the priesthood" — establishes that the *kohen* is not required until the *kemitza* stage. The mixing is the owner's act. The question is: does that mixing in the *kli sharet* need to take place inside the *Mikdash*, or may it be done outside?

### R.L. vs. R.Y.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר כְּשֵׁרָה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְיָצַק עָלֶיהָ שֶׁמֶן וְנָתַן עָלֶיהָ לְבוֹנָה״, וַהֲדַר ״וֶהֱבִיאָהּ אֶל בְּנֵי אַהֲרֹן הַכֹּהֲנִים וְקָמַץ״. מִקְּמִיצָה וְאֵילָךְ מִצְוַת כְּהוּנָּה, לִימֵּד עַל יְצִיקָה וּבְלִילָה שֶׁכְּשֵׁרִין בְּזָר, וּמִדִּכְהוּנָּה לָא בָּעֲיָא, פְּנִים נָמֵי לָא בָּעֲיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish explains: **The Sages derived from here that** from the **removal of the handful onward** the rites performed with the meal offering are solely **a mitzva of** the **priesthood.** Accordingly, the verse **taught about pouring and mixing that they are valid** when performed **by a non-priest. And from** the fact **that** the **priesthood is not required** for the mixing, it may be derived that it **is also not required** that its performance be **inside** the walls of the Temple courtyard.

Reish Lakish holds that it is *kesheira* — valid even if mixed outside. His proof is the verse describing the process: *vayitzak aleha shemen venatan aleha levona*, you pour the oil and place the *levona*, *vehevi'a el bnei Aharon hakohanim*, and then bring it to the *kohanim*, *vekamatz*, and then the *kohen* takes the *kemitza*. The sequence makes clear that the *yitzika* and *blila* precede any involvement of the *kehuna*. From here Reish Lakish derives: *limad al yetzika uvelila shekesheira bezar* — the pouring and the mixing are valid when performed by a non-*kohen*. And then: *umidekehuna lo ba'ya, pnim nami lo ba'ya* — since it does not require a *kohen*, it also does not require the interior of the *Mikdash*. It is something the owner can perform anywhere, including outside the walls.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: פְּסוּלָה, כֵּיוָן דַּעֲשִׂיָּיתָהּ בִּכְלִי הוּא, נְהִי דִּכְהוּנָּה לָא בָּעֲיָא, פְּנִים מִיהַת בָּעֲיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This meal offering is **disqualified, since** a meal offering's **performance is in** a service **vessel.** Therefore, **granted that** the **priesthood is not required,** yet **in any event** its performance **inside** the Temple courtyard **is required.**

Rabbi Yochanan holds that it is *psula* — invalid. His reasoning: *keivan de'asiyata bikli hi* — since the preparation is performed in a *kli sharet*, it is part of a sanctification process that requires the environment of the *Mikdash*. *Nehi dekehuna lo ba'ya, pnim miha ba'ya* — true, it does not require a *kohen*, but it does require the *Mikdash*. The *kli sharet* represents the apparatus of sanctification; the use of a *kli sharet* binds the act to the *Mikdash* even if it does not bind it to a *kohen*.

This is a striking position when considered alongside the question raised by Tosafot. If the *mincha* flour becomes *kadosh* through the *kli sharet* — acquiring *kedushat haguf*, intrinsic sanctity that cannot be redeemed — how can it remain outside the *Mikdash*? The rule is that something with *kedushat haguf* cannot be taken out of the *Beit HaMikdash*. Tosafot observes that neither Rabbi Yochanan nor Reish Lakish raises this as a concern, suggesting that the *kedushat haMincha* at this stage does not yet render it *pasul beyotze* — invalid by virtue of leaving the *Mikdash*. One possible explanation is that the Torah, having explicitly designated this phase of preparation as the owner's act before the involvement of the *kehuna*, treats it as a category apart: the flour is sanctified but is not yet subject to the laws of *yotze* because the Torah frames it as an act that occurs in transit, so to speak, before the *Mikdash* fully receives it. Neither Amora challenges on this basis.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בְּלָלָהּ זָר – כְּשֵׁרָה, חוּץ לְחוֹמַת הָעֲזָרָה – פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes that **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan:** If a meal offering was **mixed** by a **non-priest** it is **valid.** But if it was mixed **outside the wall of the** Temple **courtyard** it is **disqualified.**

A *braita* is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's position: *balalah zar kesheira, chutz lechomat ha'azara psulah* — if a non-*kohen* mixed it, it is valid; if it was mixed outside the walls of the *azara*, it is invalid. The *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan.

### Refill Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: מִנְחָה שֶׁחָסְרָה קוֹדֶם קְמִיצָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנָּה, רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לָא יָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to a **meal offering that became lacking** in its full measure **before the removal of the handful, Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that the owner **shall bring** additional flour **from within his home and shall fill** the missing part of the measure of the meal offering. **Reish Lakish says: He shall not bring** flour **from within his home and fill it.** Instead, he must bring a new meal offering.

The Gemara introduces a second debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish, this one concerning *mincha shechasra kodem kemitza* — a *mincha* from which some amount was lost before the *kemitza* was taken. May one supplement it from one's own ingredients and fill it back to the required amount?

## Kemitza Role

### R.Y.: Refill OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מֵבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנָּה, קְמִיצָה קָבְעָה לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that **he brings** flour **from within his home and he fills it,** as the **removal of the handful establishes it** as a meal offering to the extent that it may become disqualified. Before the removal, one may always add flour to the meal offering.

Rabbi Yochanan holds: *yavi mitoch beito veyimla'enah* — bring ingredients from your home and fill it up. His reasoning is *kemitza kav'ah*: it is the *kemitza* that definitively establishes and fixes the identity of the *mincha*. Prior to the *kemitza*, the *mincha* has not yet been fully defined as this specific, bounded object; supplementing it is therefore permissible.

### R.L.: No Refill

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: לָא יָבִיא מִתּוֹךְ בֵּיתוֹ וִימַלְּאֶנָּה, קְדוּשַּׁת כְּלִי קָבְעָה לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: He shall not bring** flour **from within his home and fill it,** as **the sanctity of** the service **vessel establishes it** as a meal offering. Therefore, once the meal offering was placed in a service vessel and sanctified, it is disqualified if it becomes lacking.

Reish Lakish holds: *lo yavi mitoch beito veyimla'enah* — one may not refill it. His reasoning is *kedushat kli kav'ah* — the sanctity imparted by the *kli sharet* has already fixed and defined the *mincha*. Even though Reish Lakish holds that this sanctification can occur outside the *Mikdash*, he insists that once it has occurred, the identity of the *mincha* is established. Nothing extraneous may be added to what has already been sanctified as this *mincha*.

There is a notable tension here, which is worth flagging. One might have expected these positions to align the other way. If Rabbi Yochanan insists the *mincha* must be prepared inside the *Mikdash* — suggesting the *klei sharet* process is more weighty and more determinative of the *mincha*'s sanctified status — one might think he would also say the *kli* already fixed it and nothing can be added. And conversely, if Reish Lakish permits the mixing outside, suggesting the *kli* process is less demanding in terms of setting, one might expect him to be more lenient about refilling. The positions run in the opposite direction from what one might anticipate, and this is worth noting.

### Metzora Log

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: חָסֵר הַלּוֹג, אִם עַד שֶׁלֹּא יָצַק – יְמַלְּאֶנּוּ, תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish,** Reish Lakish, from a mishna (*Nega'im* 14:10) discussing the *log* of oil brought by a leper: In a case where **the *log* lacked** a full measure, **if** it became lacking **before** the priest **poured** from it into his palm in order to place it on the right thumb and big toe of the leper, **he shall fill it.** Clearly the service vessel containing the *log* does not sanctify the oil to the extent that it can become disqualified. The Gemara notes: Indeed, this is **a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of Reish Lakish.

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from a *braita*: *chasar halog* — if some of the *log* of oil belonging to the *metzora* spilled before the *kohen* poured it onto his hand to perform the *avoda*, *im ad shelo yatzak yimla'enu* — if the pouring had not yet taken place, the *log* should be refilled. This is directly analogous to the case of a *mincha* lacking before the *kemitza*: just as the *log* of the *metzora* can be supplemented before the critical act of pouring, so too the *mincha* should be supplementable before the *kemitza*. The Gemara declares: *tiyuvta* — this is a definitive challenge to Reish Lakish. Rabbi Yochanan's position stands.

## Remnant Lacking

### R.Y. vs. R.L.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שִׁירַיִם שֶׁחָסְרוּ בֵּין קְמִיצָה לְהַקְטָרָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: מַקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ עֲלֵיהֶן, וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: אֵין מַקְטִיר קוֹמֶץ עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § **It was stated:** With regard to the **remainder** of a meal offering **that became lacking between** the **removal of the handful and** its **burning** upon the altar, **Rabbi Yoḥanan says** that one **burns the handful on** account of such a remainder, and that the remainder is then permitted for consumption. **And Reish Lakish says** that one does **not burn the handful on** account of the remainder.

The Gemara now addresses a further debate: *shirayim shechasru bein kemitza lehaktara* — the *shirayim*, the remnant of the *mincha*, became lacking between the taking of the *kemitza* and the burning of the *kometz* on the *mizbeach*. May the *kometz* still be offered?

Rabbi Yochanan says: *maktir kometz aleihen* — the *kometz* may be offered even though the remnant is now deficient. Reish Lakish says: *ein maktir kometz aleihen* — the *kometz* may not be offered.

### R. Eliezer vs. R.Y.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **According to** the opinion **of Rabbi Eliezer, everyone agrees** that the handful is burned on account of the remainder. In other words, both *amora'im*, Rabbi Yoḥanan and Reish Lakish, concur that this is the *halakha* according to the *tanna* Rabbi Eliezer. **When they disagree,** it is concerning the *halakha* **according to** the opinion **of Rabbi Yehoshua.**

The Gemara frames this dispute by reference to the tannaitic debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua regarding animal offerings. By animal *korbanos*, the question arose: if the meat and fat are gone, may one still sprinkle the blood? Rabbi Eliezer said yes — even if nothing of the *korban* remains, one may still perform the valid *avoda* of the blood. Rabbi Yehoshua disagreed — there must be some remaining part of the *korban* in order to proceed with the blood service.

The Gemara establishes: *aliba de'Rabbi Eliezer kulei alma lo pligi* — according to Rabbi Eliezer's position, everyone agrees the *mincha* is valid in the present case. If Rabbi Eliezer permits *zrikat hadam* even when nothing of the animal remains, then certainly the *kometz* may be offered even when the *shirayim* are deficient. *Ki pligi aliba de'Rabbi Yehoshua* — the dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish operates within the framework of Rabbi Yehoshua, who requires something to remain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן: נִטְמְאוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ, נִשְׂרְפוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ, אָבְדוּ שְׁיָרֶיהָ – כְּמִדַּת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר כְּשֵׁרָה, כְּמִדַּת רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ פְּסוּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the dispute in question. **As we learned** in a mishna (26a): If, after the handful was removed, **the remainder of** the meal offering **became ritually impure,** or if **the remainder of** the meal offering **was burned,** or if **the remainder of** the meal offering **was lost, according to the principle of Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that with regard to an animal offering the blood is fit for sprinkling even if there is no meat that can be eaten, the meal offering is **fit,** and the priest burns the handful. **But according to the principle of Rabbi Yehoshua,** who says that with regard to an animal offering the blood is fit for sprinkling only if there is meat that can be eaten, it is **unfit** and the priest does not burn the handful, as the handful serves to render the remainder permitted.

A *mishna* is cited that maps the earlier tannaitic dispute directly onto the *mincha*: if the *shirayim* became *tamei*, were burned, or were lost — according to the view analogous to Rabbi Eliezer, the *mincha* is valid; according to the view analogous to Rabbi Yehoshua, it is invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן דְּפָסֵל – כְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וּמַאן דְּמַכְשַׁר – עַד כָּאן לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם אֶלָּא דְּלָא אִישְׁתְּיַיר, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּאִישְׁתְּיַיר – אֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The one who disqualifies** the remainder of a meal offering that became lacking between the removal of the handful and its burning upon the altar, i.e., Reish Lakish, holds **in accordance with** the plain meaning of the opinion of **Rabbi Yehoshua. And the one who deems** that remainder **fit,** i.e., Rabbi Yoḥanan, maintains that when **Rabbi Yehoshua says** that the remainder is unfit, he says so **only there,** in a case **where nothing remained** from the meal offering other than the remainder. Since the handful is intended to render permitted for consumption the remainder of the meal offering, if there is no remainder at all then the handful is not burned. **But** in a case **where there remained** a portion of the remainder, Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that **even Rabbi Yehoshua concedes to** Rabbi Eliezer that the handful is fit to be burned.

The key question then becomes: granted that Rabbi Yehoshua requires *something* to remain — but does he require all of the remnant, or merely a *kezayis*? For by animal offerings, Rabbi Yehoshua permits *zrikat hadam* so long as even one *kezayis* of either meat or fat remains. Why then should a *mincha* require the entire remnant to be present?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה שֶׁנִּשְׁתַּיֵּיר מֵהֶם כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר אוֹ כְּזַיִת חֵלֶב – זוֹרֵק הַדָּם, כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בָּשָׂר כַּחֲצִי זַיִת חֵלֶב – אֵינוֹ זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a proof for Rabbi Yoḥanan: It is clear that this is Rabbi Yehoshua's opinion, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua says:** With regard to **any of the offerings that** are mentioned **in the Torah from which there remains** either **an olive-bulk of meat or an olive-bulk of fat,** the *halakha* is that the priest **sprinkles the blood** of that offering and thereby permits either the remaining meat for consumption or the remaining fat for sacrifice upon the altar. But if there remains only **half an olive-bulk of meat** and **half an olive-bulk of fat, he does not sprinkle the blood,** as the consumption or burning of anything less than an olive-bulk is not significant enough to warrant the sprinkling of the blood. The volumes of the meat and the fat do not combine to form a whole olive-bulk.

The one who invalidates the *mincha* when even a small portion of the *shirayim* is lacking argues: *amar kra, vayarim hakohen min hamincha es azkarasah vehiktir hamizbeachah* — the *kohen* shall take from the *mincha* its memorial portion and burn it on the *mizbeach*. The word *hamincha* — with the definite article — implies that the entire *mincha* must be present: *ad de'ika kulla mincha lo yaktir*, you do not burn the *kometz* unless the whole *mincha* is there. And furthermore, the verse's phrase *min hamincha* applies not only to the moment of taking the *kometz*, but also to the moment of *haktara* — the burning. The *mincha* must be whole at both stages.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבָעוֹלָה, אֲפִילּוּ כַּחֲצִי זַיִת בָּשָׂר וְכַחֲצִי זַיִת חֵלֶב – זוֹרֵק אֶת הַדָּם, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבָּעוֹלָה כּוּלָּהּ כָּלִיל, וּבַמִּנְחָה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכּוּלָּהּ קַיֶּימֶת – לֹא יִזְרוֹק.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And in** the case of **a burnt offering, even** if there remains only **half an olive-bulk of flesh and half an olive-bulk of fat, he sprinkles the blood, because in** the case of **a burnt offering it is entirely consumed,** i.e., as the flesh and fat are both burned upon the altar, there is in fact an olive-bulk of the offering that is designated for burning upon the altar. **And with regard to a meal offering, even though it** remains **entirely intact, he shall not sprinkle.**

The one who validates the *mincha* despite a partial deficiency responds: *min hamincha* means only *mincha shehayesa kvar* — that it was a complete *mincha* when the *kometz* was taken. The verse requires the *mincha* to have been whole at the time of *kemitza*, but says nothing about the *mincha* being whole at the moment of *haktara*.

### Lechem Break

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עַד שֶׁלֹּא פֵּרְקָהּ נִפְרַס הַלֶּחֶם – פָּסוּל, וְאֵין מַקְטִיר עָלָיו אֶת הַבָּזִיכִין, וְאִם מִשֶּׁפֵּרְקָהּ נִפְרַס לַחְמָהּ – הַלֶּחֶם פָּסוּל, וּמַקְטִיר עָלָיו אֶת הַבָּזִיכִין. וְאָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: לֹא פֵּרְקָהּ מַמָּשׁ, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ זְמַנָּהּ לְפָרֵק, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא פִּירְקָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to Reish Lakish** from a *baraita*: If **before** the priest **detached** the arrangement of shewbread and the bowls of frankincense from upon the Table, the **bread broke** into pieces, the bread is **unfit** for consumption **and** the priest **does not burn** the frankincense contained in **the bowls on** account of **it. And if** the **bread broke after** the priest **detached it, the bread is unfit but** the priest **burns the** frankincense contained in the **bowls on** account of **it. And Rabbi Elazar says:** The *baraita* does **not** mean that the priest **actually detached** the shewbread. **Rather,** it means that **once** the **time to detach it has arrived** the frankincense contained in the bowls may be burned, **even though he has not** yet **detached it.** Just as the frankincense contained in the bowls is burned despite the broken shewbread, likewise, the handful of a meal offering should be burned even when the remainder is lacking.

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish directly with a case involving the *lechem hapanim*. The *braita* teaches: *ad shelo parkah nifras lachmah* — if the bread of the *lechem hapanim* broke before you removed the bread from the table and took away the *bazichin* — the small spoons of frankincense — then *halechem pasul ve'ein maktir aleha es habazichin*: the bread is invalid and the frankincense may not be offered. But *im misheparkah nifras lachmah* — if the bread broke after you removed it and took the *bazichin*, then while *halechem pasul* — the bread is still invalid as *shirayim* — nonetheless *umaktir aleha es habazichin*: the frankincense may be offered.

This is directly analogous: once the *bazichin* — the functional equivalent of the *kometz* — have been taken, even if a piece of the bread subsequently breaks away, the *kometz* equivalent is still offered. This supports Rabbi Yochanan's position that one may offer the *kometz* even when the *shirayim* are deficient, as long as the deficiency occurred after the *kemitza* was taken. The case is explicit and comes from a *mishna shleima* — a complete, authoritative *mishna*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָא מַנִּי? רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא אָמֵינָא לָךְ מִשְׁנָה שְׁלֵימָה, וְאָמְרַתְּ לִי אַתְּ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר?
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yoḥanan in response: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this? It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer,** who permits the burning of a handful even when the remainder has been entirely destroyed. And I hold in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: I am stating a complete *halakha* to you,** i.e., a *baraita* of an unspecified opinion, **and you say to me** that this *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer** alone?

Reish Lakish attempts to deflect Rabbi Yochanan's challenge by saying: *hamani Rabbi Eliezer hi* — this ruling follows Rabbi Eliezer, who requires no remaining portion at all. But Rabbi Yochanan is dismissive: *ana amina lach mishna shleima ve'amart li at Rabbi Eliezer* — I brought you an entire *mishna* and you tell me it represents only Rabbi Eliezer's individual opinion?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, מַאי אִירְיָא נִפְרַס? אֲפִילּוּ שָׂרוּף וְאָבוּד נָמֵי מַכְשַׁר! אִישְׁתִּיק.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan added: And furthermore, **if** the *baraita* is in accordance with the opinion of only **Rabbi Eliezer,** then **why** does the *baraita* mention **specifically** an instance where the bread **broke?** According to Rabbi Eliezer, **even** if the bread was **burned or lost** entirely, **he deems** the frankincense contained in the bowls **fit** for burning **as well.** The Gemara notes: Reish Lakish **was silent** and did not respond to the objections of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

Moreover, the challenge cuts deeper: *ei Rabbi Eliezer, mai irya nifchat, afilu nisraf ve'avad nami machshir* — if it truly reflects Rabbi Eliezer's position, why does the *braita* bother with the case of the bread merely breaking? According to Rabbi Eliezer, even if all the bread had been entirely destroyed, the frankincense could be offered. The *braita* would then be stating a far weaker case than it could — which is implausible as a presentation of Rabbi Eliezer's view. *Ishtik* — Reish Lakish had no response.

### Deficiency=Mum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַמַּאי שְׁתֵק? לֵימָא לֵיהּ: צִבּוּר שָׁאנֵי, הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתְּרַי טוּמְאָה לְגַבַּיְיהוּ – אִישְׁתְּרַי נָמֵי חֲסֵרוֹת! אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, הַחֶסְרוֹן כְּבַעַל מוּם דָּמֵי, וְאֵין בַּעַל מוּם בַּצִּבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why was** Reish Lakish **silent? Let him say to** Rabbi Yoḥanan that one cannot cite a proof from a *baraita* that discusses the shewbread, a communal offering, because offerings of the **community are different, since ritual impurity was permitted for them,** i.e., communal offerings may be sacrificed even in a state of ritual impurity. Therefore, it **was also permitted** to sacrifice them when they **are lacking. Rav Adda bar Ahava said:** The fact **that** Reish Lakish did not respond in **this** manner **is to say** that a meal offering that is **lacking** in measure **is considered like a blemished** animal, not merely like an impure offering, **and there is no** instance of **a blemished** animal being permitted for use as an offering, even **in** the case of **a communal** offering.

The Gemara asks why Reish Lakish did not offer an alternative defense: *leima lei tzibbur shani* — perhaps the *lechem hapanim*, being a *korban tzibbur*, is subject to more lenient rules than an individual's *mincha*? By *korbanos tzibbur*, *tumah* is overridden — the communal offering proceeds even in a state of impurity. Perhaps deficiency in the *shirayim* is similarly overridden for a *korban tzibbur*.

The Gemara answers on behalf of Reish Lakish's silence: *afillu tzibbur shani hu de'ishtarei mitumah legabeihu ishtarei nami mechesron* — the leniency of *tzibbur* extends only to *tumah*, not to other forms of disqualification. And since Reish Lakish did not offer this defense, Rav Adda bar Ahava draws a conclusion: *zos omeres hachisaron ke'ba'al mum dami ve'ein ba'al mum b'tzibbur* — the deficiency is treated as analogous to a *mum*, a blemish, rather than to *tumah*. Just as a *ba'al mum* is not overridden even for a *korban tzibbur*, so too deficiency in the *shirayim* is not overridden. This is why Reish Lakish could not invoke the *tzibbur* argument.

It must be emphasized that the deficiency is not literally a *mum* in the halachic sense. If it were, the *kometz* could not be offered regardless of when the deficiency occurred — whether before or after the *kemitza*. The point is more precise: unlike *tumah*, which the *tzibbur* overrides, deficiency belongs to the category of disqualifications that the *tzibbur* does not override, much as it does not override *ba'al mum*. The leniency unique to *korban tzibbur* is limited to *tumah*, and nothing else.

At this stage, Rabbi Yochanan has effectively demonstrated that even according to Rabbi Yehoshua — who requires something to remain — the relevant threshold is a *kezayis*, not the entirety of the *shirayim*. And the case of the *lechem hapanim*, which the Gemara is not prepared to confine to the singular opinion of Rabbi Eliezer, shows that even where some portion of the remnant is lacking after the *kemitza* is taken, the *kometz* may still be offered. The discussion will continue on the following daf.
