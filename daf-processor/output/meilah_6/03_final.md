# Meilah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zrika vs. Kabbala

### Me'ilah by Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד? לָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another source that might resolve the issue: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that can provide a proof with regard to the meaning of Rabbi Yehoshua's statement about a period of fitness to the priests: **Rabbi Shimon says** that **there is** a case of ***notar*,** when the blood was left overnight and was rendered unfit, **where** one is liable for **misusing** the meat of the offering, **and there is** also a case of ***notar* where** one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If the blood was **left over** and someone consumed the meat **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property. But if it was consumed **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property, as the sprinkling removes the meat from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

The topic under discussion concerns how the *me'ilah* status of a *korban* changes once its blood is offered. *Kodashei kodashim* are entirely subject to *me'ilah* before any service, since the offering is wholly consecrated. Once the blood is sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*—in the case of a *chatat* or *asham*—the meat becomes permissible for the *kohanim*, and consequently the meat is no longer subject to *me'ilah*, though *me'ilah* continues to apply to the remainder of the *korban*. The rule is thus: *zrikat hadam* removes the meat of *kodashei kodashim* from the *me'ilah* category.

The dynamic operates in reverse for *kodashim kalim*. There, the offering is fundamentally considered *mamon ba'alim*—it has not been fully surrendered to God—and is destined to be eaten by the owners. Only the portion that goes on the *mizbe'ach*, the *emurim*, becomes subject to *me'ilah*, and only after *zrikat hadam* renders it fit for the altar. So *zrikat hadam* has a dual function: for *kodashei kodashim* it removes the meat from *me'ilah*, and for *kodashim kalim* it brings the *emurim* into *me'ilah*.

### Critical Moment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לָאו דַּהֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, דְּאִי בָּעֵי – זָרֵיק, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **In any event,** Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that if one consumes the meat before the leftover blood was sprinkled, he is liable for **misusing it.** Is this **not** referring to a case **where there was time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood that had already been collected in the service vessel, and therefore, **if he had desired, he could have sprinkled** the blood? Nevertheless, the offering is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. This indicates that merely collecting the blood in the service vessel alone, without actually sprinkling it, does not remove the offering from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּקַבְּלֵיהּ סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, דְּלֹא הָיָה שְׁהוּת לְמִזְרַק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this conclusion: **No,** the *baraita* is not referring to a case where there was time left in the day to sprinkle the blood that had already been collected. Rather, it is referring to a situation **where** the priest **collected** the blood **shortly before sunset, where there was no time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood while it was still daytime. Since the blood could not have been sprinkled, the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. But if there had been time to sprinkle the blood, then that blood would be considered ready to be sprinkled, and the offering would no longer be subject to the prohibition of misuse, in accordance with the opinion that the criteria is the fitness of sprinkling of the blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל הָיָה שְׁהוּת, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** in that case, **what** is the *halakha* in a situation where there **was time** in the day to sprinkle the blood? According to the above claim, **so too** the *halakha* is **that** he is **not** liable for **misusing** the offering.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה״, לִיתְנֵי ״קוֹדֶם שְׁקִיעָה״ וּ״לְאַחַר שְׁקִיעָה״!
>
> **Translation:** If so, **why does** Rabbi Shimon **specifically teach** this distinction between a case **before sprinkling,** when the offering is still subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, and after sprinkling, when the offering is no longer subject to misuse? **Let** Rabbi Shimon instead **teach** a more precise distinction, between a situation where the blood was collected **before sunset** and there was time to sprinkle it but it was left overnight, in which case the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse, **and** a situation where the blood was collected **after sunset,** in which case it is still subject to the prohibition of misuse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָתָנֵי: קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה, וּלְאַחַר שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that this is **indeed** what Rabbi Shimon meant, as he actually **taught: Before** it was **fit for sprinkling,** the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse, **but after** it was **fit for sprinkling,** it is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse.

The *gemara* has been probing a more precise question: is it the moment of *zrika* itself that is critical, or does an earlier moment—*kabbala*, the receiving of the blood—already effect the status change? Once the blood is received it is fit for *zrika*, and the *gemara* refers to this as *heit lizrika*, the fitness for *zrika*. The actual act of *zrika* is called *hetter achila*, the moment the meat becomes genuinely permitted. The tension throughout this sugya is whether *heit lizrika*, achieved through *kabbala*, is the operative moment, or whether one must wait for the *zrika* itself.

## Notar Cases

### Notar Distinction

A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon teaches that with regard to *notar*—meat left over beyond its permitted time—there are cases where *me'ilah* applies and cases where it does not. This immediately raises the question of how a single category, *notar* of *kodashei kodashim*, can produce two different outcomes with respect to *me'ilah*.

### Before/After Zrika

The *baraita* continues: *keitzad*—how so? If the meat lodged overnight (*lan*) before *zrika*, then *moalin*, there is *me'ilah*. If it lodged overnight after *zrika*, then *ein moalin*, there is no *me'ilah*. The plain reading is straightforward: did the disqualifying event—*lina*—occur before or after the blood was sprinkled? If *zrika* had already been performed, the meat had already passed through the moment of *hetter achila* and is no longer subject to *me'ilah*. If *lina* preceded *zrika*, the meat was never rendered permitted, and *me'ilah* remains.

### Heit Lizrika

The *gemara* complicates this reading. It suggests that the *baraita*'s case of "before *zrika*" refers specifically to a situation where the blood was received (*kabbala*) close to *shkiyat hachama*—sunset—such that by the time *kabbala* was complete there was no remaining opportunity to perform *zrika*. The blood was thus never fit for *zrika* from the moment it was caught. On this reading, the reason *me'ilah* applies is not simply that *zrika* had not yet occurred, but that *zrika* was never even possible—the blood was never *heit lizrika*.

### Why Zrika?

This reinterpretation raises a pointed difficulty. If the *gemara* is correct that the operative distinction is whether the blood was ever fit for *zrika* rather than whether *zrika* actually took place, then why does the *baraita* frame the dividing line as "before *zrika*" versus "after *zrika*"? It ought instead to have said: before *shkiya* versus after *shkiya*, since the real question is whether *kabbala* occurred while there was still time to perform *zrika*.

### Reinterpreting Zrika

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the *baraita*'s language as a compressed formulation. When Rabbi Shimon says "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," he does not mean the literal act of *zrika*; he means *kodem she're'uyah l'zrika* and *le'achar she're'uyah l'zrika*—before the blood was fit for *zrika* and after the blood was fit for *zrika*. Fitness for *zrika* is achieved at the moment of *kabbala*. The word *zrika* in the *baraita* is thus shorthand for the moment when *zrika* became possible. The entire analysis is admittedly *dachuk*—strained—but it is the *gemara*'s own resolution, and it converts both *baraita* cases into illustrations of the principle that *heit lizrika*, not *zrika* per se, is the critical moment.

## Pigul Cases

### Pigul Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד? לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof from a similar *baraita*. **Come** and **hear: Rabbi Shimon says** that **there is** a case of **an offering** of the most sacred order that was sacrificed with ***piggul*** intent **where** one is liable for **misusing it, and there is** also a case of ***piggul*** intent **where** one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If someone consumed the meat **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property. If he consumed it **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property, as the sprinkling removed the prohibition of misuse.

A second *baraita* is cited, this time applying the same framework to *pigul*—the disqualification arising from an improper intention during the sacrificial service. Rabbi Shimon again teaches: there is *pigul* with *me'ilah*, and there is *pigul* without *me'ilah*. *Keitzad*—how so? Before *zrika*, *moalin*. After *zrika*, *ein moalin*. On the surface this appears to be the same structure as the *notar* case.

### Pigul Timing Problem

A serious difficulty immediately presents itself. *Pigul* can only be generated at the four *avodos*: *shechita*, *kabbala*, *holacha*, and *zrika*. By definition, then, *pigul* cannot occur after *zrika*. The plain reading—"did the disqualification arise before or after *zrika*?"—cannot work here, because there is no mechanism by which *pigul* can arise after *zrika*.

### Rashi's Interpretation

Rashi resolves this by shifting the referent of "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*" away from when the *pigul* thought occurred and toward when the person was *neheneh*—when he derived benefit from or ate the meat. On Rashi's reading: if one was *neheneh* from the meat before *zrika*, there is *me'ilah*; if one was *neheneh* after *zrika*—even after a *zrikat pigul*—there is no *me'ilah*. This implies that *zrikat pigul* counts as a *zrika* sufficient to change the *me'ilah* status, and thus directly contradicts the position of Rav Giddel citing Rav, who held that *zrikat pigul* is not effective for this purpose. Rashi reads the *baraita* this way because the alternative—having *pigul* occur after *zrika*—is technically impossible.

### Pigul and Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לָאו דַּהֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, דְּאִי בָּעֵי – זָרֵיק, וְקָתָנֵי ״מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ״, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that if one consumes the meat of an offering that was rendered *piggul* **before** the blood was **sprinkled,** he is liable for **misusing it.** Is this **not** referring to a case **where there was time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood that had already been collected in the service vessel, and therefore, **if he had desired, he could have sprinkled** the blood? **And** yet Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that one is liable for **misusing it.** Once again, this would indicate that merely collecting the blood in the service vessel alone, without sprinkling, does not remove the possibility of the prohibition of misuse. **And** accordingly, one may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned** in Rabbi Yehoshua's statement in the mishna.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּלָא הֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ. אֲבָל הֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דִּנְפַק מִידֵי מְעִילָה?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this conclusion: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to a situation where the priest collected the blood shortly before sunset, **where there was no time to sprinkle** the blood while it was still daytime. Since the blood could not have been sprinkled, the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if so, **what** is the *halakha* in a case where there **was time** in the day **to sprinkle** the blood? According to the above claim, the offering is **indeed removed** from being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה״, לִיתְנֵי ״קוֹדֶם שְׁקִיעָה״ וּ״לְאַחַר שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה״!
>
> **Translation:** If so, **why does** Rabbi Shimon **specifically teach** this distinction between **after sprinkling,** when the offering is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, and before sprinkling, when the offering is still subject to misuse? **Let** Rabbi Shimon instead **teach** a more precise distinction, between a situation where the blood was collected **before sunset** and there was time to sprinkle it but it was left overnight, in which case the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse, **and** a situation where the blood was collected **after sunset,** in which case it is still subject to the prohibition of misuse.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה, לְאַחַר שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **that is indeed what** Rabbi Shimon **is saying: Before** it was **fit for sprinkling,** the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse, **but after** it was **fit for sprinkling,** it is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse.

An alternative reading, closer to the plain sense of the *baraita*, takes "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*" as referring to when the *pigul* thought occurred relative to *zrika*, with the understanding that *le'achar zrika* is *lav davka*—not precise—and in practice means *bish'at zrika*, during *zrika*. On this reading: if the *pigul* thought arose before *zrika*—at *shechita* or *kabbala*—then the *zrika* was never *matir* the meat, since a *zrikat pigul* is still, according to some, considered as having occurred. If the *pigul* arose at *zrika* itself, one can dispute whether *zrikat pigul* counts as *zrika* and thereby serves as *matir*. Either way the *baraita* engages the question of Rav Giddel's ruling.

Tosafot notes an independent complication: if *pigul* at the time of *holacha* applies according to Rabbi Shimon, then there is a window—after *kabbala* and before *zrika*—where *pigul* could be generated. This would supply a genuine case of *pigul* arising after *kabbala* but before *zrika*. Yet Tosafot also observes that Rabbi Shimon himself holds *pigul* at *holacha* is not effective, since one can perform the service without *holacha*. Perhaps the case involves a *pigul* thought at the very conclusion of *kabbala*. The *gemara* on its surface treats this as parallel to the *notar* case, but the complications are substantial.

### Challenge to Kabbala

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַפִּיגּוּל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים – מוֹעֲלִין. מַאי לָאו דְּזָרַק, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ! לָא, דְּלֹא זָרַק.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara suggests another proof. **Come** and **hear: An offering of the most sacred order** which is ***piggul*** is subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What, is** this *baraita* **not** referring to a case **where** the priest already **sprinkled** its blood? This would indicate that a fit offering, unlike a *piggul* offering, is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse only once the blood is sprinkled. **And** if so, one may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned** in Rabbi Yehoshua's statement in the mishna, i.e., this fitness of consuming the meat of the offering removes the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** one cannot cite a proof from this *baraita*, as it is possible that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the priest did **not** yet **sprinkle** the blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל זָרַק מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ? מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לִיתְנֵי: כָּאן – לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, כָּאן – לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** if so, **what,** then, is the *halakha* in a case where the priest did **sprinkle** the blood? Is the *halakha* **indeed that** one is **no** longer liable for **misusing it?** If so, **why does** the latter clause of the *baraita* **specifically teach:** Unlike an offering of the most sacred order, **in** the case of the sacrificial portions of **an offering of lesser sanctity** one is **not** liable for **misusing it?** **Let** the *baraita* instead **teach** a distinction within the category of offerings of the most sacred order themselves: **Here,** the offering is subject to the prohibition of misuse because it is **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, and **there,** the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse because it is **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא אָתְיָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן: כֹּל לְאֵיתוֹיֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה, זְרִיקָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה. כֹּל לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִידֵי מְעִילָה, אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ נָמֵי מַפְקַע מִידֵי מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: The *baraita* could have taught that distinction, but it chose to state a distinction between offerings of the most sacred order and offerings of lesser sanctity because it **comes to teach us this** following principle: In **any** case where the result of sprinkling **brings** the offering **into** the category of the *halakhot* of **misuse,** e.g., the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity, to which the *halakhot* of misuse apply only after the blood had been sprinkled, only **sprinkling** the blood **properly brings** the offering **into** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, in **any** situation where the result of sprinkling is **removing** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse,** e.g., the meat of an offering of the most sacred order, which was subject to the *halakhot* of misuse even before the blood was sprinkled, **even sprinkling** the blood **improperly removes** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** This ruling is not in accordance with the opinion of Rav Giddel.

The *gemara* now attempts to use the *baraita* to resolve the underlying dispute about whether *kabbala* or *zrika* is the critical moment. The *baraita* says: if *pigul* preceded *zrika*, *moalin*—even in a case where *kabbala* was performed properly and at a time when *zrika* was entirely possible. The *gemara* suggests: does this not demonstrate that even with a valid *kabbala*—one where there was ample opportunity to perform *zrika*—the status does not change until *zrika* itself? This would confirm that *hetter achila*, not *heit lizrika*, is the operative standard.

The *gemara* proceeds to test this against an earlier *baraita* that stated: *pigul bekodshei kodashim moalin*. The *gemara* asks: surely this refers to a case where *pigul* arose even after *kabbala*, and yet *me'ilah* still applies, demonstrating that *kabbala* is not the critical moment. The *gemara* responds: no, the *baraita* may be speaking specifically of a case where *zrika* never occurred at all—where there was no *kabbala* at the right time to make the blood *heit lizrika*.

### Reinterpreted Timing

As with the *notar* case, the *gemara* resolves by re-reading. When the *baraita* says "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," it means *kodem she're'uyah l'zrika* and *le'achar she're'uyah l'zrika*—before and after the blood became fit for *zrika*. Fitness for *zrika* is achieved at *kabbala*. The *baraita* thus teaches: if the problem arose before *kabbala*, the status never changed; if after *kabbala*, it had already changed. *Zrika* in the *baraita* is again shorthand for *heit lizrika*.

## Into vs. Out of Me'ilah

### Why Kodashim Kalim?

The *gemara* now presses a fresh question. The *baraita* under discussion—*pigul bekodshei kodashim moalin, bekodashim kalim ein moalin*—seeks to illustrate a case where there is no *me'ilah*. But according to the conclusion just reached, that *kabbala* is the operative moment, there ought to be cases within *kodashei kodashim* itself where *me'ilah* does not apply: namely, whenever the *pigul* arose after *kabbala* was performed with a proper opportunity for *zrika*. Why then does the *baraita* need to travel all the way to *kodashim kalim* to find an example of *ein moalin*? It could simply have drawn a distinction within *kodashei kodashim* between *pigul* before *zrika* and *pigul* after *zrika*—or as we now understand, before and after *heit lizrika*.

### Asymmetry Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהָא קָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דִּזְרִיקָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה. אֲבָל לְאֵיתוֹיֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה – מוֹדֵי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דַּאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתָא לִידֵי מְעִילָה,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Both cases **are necessary, as, if** the disagreement **was stated** only **with regard to offerings of the most sacred order, I would say** that it is specifically in **that** case that **Rabbi Eliezer says** that one is liable for **misusing** the meat of the offering, **due to** the fact **that** only **sprinkling** the blood **properly removes** the offering **from** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, sprinkling the blood **improperly,** including for meat that left the courtyard, **does not remove** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** **But** with regard to the issue of when the rite of sprinkling **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** i.e., in the case of sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity, Rabbi Eliezer **concedes to Rabbi Akiva that even** sprinkling the blood **improperly,** as in this case, **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אִיתְּמַר גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים הוּא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ זְרִיקָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתָא לִידֵי מְעִילָה, אֲבָל קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, דִּלְאַפּוֹקֵי הוּא, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **And** by contrast, **if** their disagreement **was stated** only **with regard to offerings of lesser sanctity, I would say** that **it is** specifically **in** the case of **offerings of lesser sanctity that Rabbi Akiva said** one is liable for **misusing them.** This is due to the fact **that** here the act of sprinkling serves to include them in the category of misuse, and therefore **even sprinkling** the blood **improperly,** as in this case, **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** **But** with regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, where** the sprinkling of the blood serves **to remove** the offering from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, one might say that Rabbi Akiva agrees **that** sprinkling the blood **improperly does not remove** the offering **from** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the *tanna'im* disagree in both cases.

The *gemara* answers that the *baraita* ran to the *kodashim kalim* case in order to teach an additional principle that could not have been derived otherwise. The key formulation is: *kol ha'asuy lidei me'ilah, zrika ketiknah mayti lidei me'ilah; kol ha'apukei midei me'ilah, afilu shelo ketiknah nami mapik midei me'ilah*. Translated: anything that is being brought into the *me'ilah* category requires a *zrika* that is in full order; but anything that is being taken out of the *me'ilah* category—even a *zrika* that is not entirely in order suffices to remove it.

The principle rests on an asymmetry between entering and exiting the *me'ilah* category. *Me'ilah* represents the status of *kodshei Hashem*, a highly restrictive and specific category. For the *emurim* of *kodashim kalim* to enter this category, the *zrika* must be completely valid, since what is being asserted is that these items now belong to God in a new and special sense—a claim that requires full legal standing. But for the meat of *kodashei kodashim* to exit *me'ilah*, something less than a perfectly valid *zrika* may suffice: a *zrikat pigul*, or *heit lizrika* without the act of *zrika*, may be enough to remove the item from this heightened category.

### Bidirectional Standards

This yields the conceptual principle that governs the entire sugya: standards for effecting a change in *me'ilah* status may differ depending on the direction of the change. The same *zrika*—or the same quasi-*zrika*—that is insufficient to bring an item into *me'ilah* may nonetheless be sufficient to take an item out of it. This is not simply a matter of being *machmir* or *meikel* in the abstract; it reflects a structural asymmetry in what it means to be in or out of the *me'ilah* category.

The *gemara* will revisit this principle on the next *amud*, and the analysis there will parallel and, in some respects, invert the logic applied here. What emerges from the first *amud*, then, is the following: *zrika* is the operative moment of status change, but three categories of "questionable *zrika*" have been introduced—*zrikat pigul*, *heit lizrika* (without actual *zrika*), and *zrika* in the presence of a *psul yotzei*—and in each case the question of whether such a *zrika* is effective may depend on whether one is seeking to enter or exit the *me'ilah* category.

## Yotzei Debate

### Yotzei in Kalim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּשַׂר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים שֶׁיָּצָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. אֵימוּרַי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna presents a dispute with regard to the status of offerings of the most sacred order, which normally are not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse once their blood has been sprinkled and they have been permitted to the priests. The case of the mishna is **the meat of offerings of the most sacred order,** whose consumption is permitted from the moment their blood was sprinkled, **that left** the Temple courtyard **before the sprinkling of the blood,** and then reentered the courtyard. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The sprinkling of this blood does not permit its consumption by the priests. Consequently, one is liable for **misusing it. And** he is **not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*,** if he partook of it after it was slaughtered with the intent to partake of it or sprinkle its blood beyond its designated time, **or** of ***notar*,** if he partook of the meat after it remained overnight, **or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.** **Rabbi Akiva says:** The sprinkling is effective despite the fact that the meat left the Temple courtyard and was disqualified, and therefore one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** Likewise, other *halakhot* that apply to offerings whose blood was sprinkled apply to it, **and** consequently **one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibitions of partaking of meat that is ***piggul*, or *notar*, or** remained overnight, **or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.** The mishna adds that just as Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva disagree as to whether the sprinkling of blood exempts meat that left the courtyard from liability for its misuse, so too, they disagree with regard to **the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity** consumed on the altar **that left** the Temple courtyard **before the sprinkling of the blood.** The dispute is whether the subsequent sprinkling of the blood generates liability for misuse of those portions. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The sprinkling of the blood is completely ineffective in rendering those portions consecrated to the Lord. Consequently, one is **not** liable for **misusing them. And** similarly, **one is not liable for their** consumption **due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of meat while **ritually impure. Rabbi Akiva says:** The sprinkling is effective, and therefore one is liable for **misusing them. And** likewise, **one is liable for its** consumption **due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

The *gemara* transitions to a parallel case in *kodashim kalim*: the *emurim* of a *shelamim* that were taken outside the permitted area before *zrikat hadam*. This is the mirror image of the *kodashei kodashim* case. Here the question is whether a *zrika* performed after the *emurim* have already become *pasul* through *yotzei* is capable of bringing them into the *me'ilah* category.

### Both Cases Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהָא קָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דִּזְרִיקָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה. אֲבָל לְאֵיתוֹיֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה – מוֹדֵי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דַּאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתָא לִידֵי מְעִילָה,
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to cite **these two** disagreements, i.e., both the case of offerings of the most sacred order and offerings of lesser sanctity? After all, both disagreements are based on the same principle. The Gemara answers: Both cases **are necessary, as, if** the disagreement **was stated** only **with regard to offerings of the most sacred order, I would say** that it is specifically in **that** case that **Rabbi Eliezer says** that one is liable for **misusing** the meat of the offering, **due to** the fact **that** only **sprinkling** the blood **properly removes** the offering **from** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, sprinkling the blood **improperly,** including for meat that left the courtyard, **does not remove** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** **But** with regard to the issue of when the rite of sprinkling **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** i.e., in the case of sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity, Rabbi Eliezer **concedes to Rabbi Akiva that even** sprinkling the blood **improperly,** as in this case, **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.**

As noted above, the *gemara* explains why both the *kodashei kodashim* case and the *kodashim kalim* case were necessary. Had only the *kodashei kodashim* case been stated, one might have argued that Rabbi Eliezer's stringent position—*zrika* must be fully valid to effect any change—applies only when trying to remove *me'ilah*, where leniency must overcome a presumption of stringency. Conversely, had only the *kodashim kalim* case been stated, one might have concluded that Rabbi Akiva's lenient position applies only when introducing *me'ilah*—since making something more *asur* can be accomplished even with a *zrika* that is not entirely in order. By presenting both cases, the *mishnah* teaches that each authority maintains his position regardless of the direction of the change.

### Yotzei in Kodashim

The *mishnah* states: *basar kodshei kodashim sheyatza lifnei zrikat damim*—meat of *kodashei kodashim* that was taken outside its permitted area before *zrikat hadam*. The *psul* of *yotzei* has attached to this meat. Rabbi Eliezer says: *moalin bo*—*me'ilah* applies. Rabbi Akiva says: *ein moalin bo*—*me'ilah* does not apply.

### Eliezer's View

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *yotzei* is a disqualification like any other. The meat, having been taken outside, is *pasul* and was never rendered *mutar le'achila*. The subsequent *zrika*—performed on behalf of the rest of the *korban*—does not change the status of this meat. Since it never passed through *hetter achila*, *me'ilah* remains in force. Furthermore, Rabbi Eliezer rules that such meat does not incur liability for *pigul*, *notar*, or *tumah*, since those prohibitions apply only to meat that would otherwise have been permitted. The meat here was already *pasul*; one who eats it is liable for *me'ilah* and for eating *psulei hamukdashin*, but not for the other *chayyavim*.

### Two Chatasim Case

Rabbi Akiva supports his contrary position with an argument from an analogous case. One who sets aside a *chatat*, loses it, sets aside a replacement, and then recovers the original is left with two *chatasim* before him, only one of which may be brought. At the moment *zrika* is performed for one of them, according to Rabbi Akiva, the blood effects a *petira* not only for the meat of that *chatat* but also for the meat of the other: *lo keshim shedama poter es besara kach hu poter es besar chavertah*—just as its blood exempts its own meat from *me'ilah*, so too does it exempt the meat of its counterpart. Rashi explains the case as one where both *chatasim* were brought into the *Beis Hamikdash*, both were *shechted*, and both had their blood received, such that at the moment of decision there remained a genuine choice as to which blood would be sprinkled—meaning both were conceptually one composite *korban* at that moment.

### Akiva's View

From this case Rabbi Akiva draws his inference: *im potar damah es besar chavertah min hame'ilah*, if the blood of one *chatat* can remove the meat of another from *me'ilah* because they are considered conceptually unified, *kol sheken* that blood should remove from *me'ilah* the meat of its own *korban*, even if that meat carries the *psul* of *yotzei*. The meat remains *pasul* and may not be eaten, but the *me'ilah* status is lifted. Rabbi Akiva further rules that such meat does incur liability for *pigul*, *notar*, and *tamei*—a consequence of treating it as though it had been rendered *ra'uy le'achila* by the *zrika*, even though its *psul yotzei* prevents actual consumption.

The question of why *yotzei* should be singled out as the *psul* light enough to be overridden by *zrika*—while other *psulin* such as *tumah* are not—is not fully resolved. The implicit reasoning appears to be that *yotzei* is a matter of spatial distance rather than an intrinsic ritual defect, and that within the conceptual framework of the *korban* as a unified entity, what has gone outside retains a certain connection to what remains within. But the parallel to *tumah* is at the very least a challenge to the elegance of this reasoning.

## Yochanan's Limitation

### Partial vs. Total Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא – שֶׁיָּצָא מִקְצָתוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא כּוּלּוֹ – לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that *amora'im* disagree with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva in the mishna. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When Rabbi Akiva says** that **sprinkling is effective to** remove the meat of offerings of the most sacred order **that left** the courtyard from the *halakhot* of misuse, that applies specifically in a case **where** only **part of it left** the courtyard and part remained inside. In such a situation, as the sprinkling is effective for the portion that remained inside the courtyard, it also is effective for the portion that left the courtyard. **But** if **all of it left** the courtyard, **Rabbi Akiva did not say** that the sprinkling is effective to remove the meat from the *halakhot* of misuse.

Rabbi Yochanan introduces a qualification to Rabbi Akiva's position. When Rabbi Akiva said that *zrika* is effective for *yotzei*, he meant only the case of *sheyatza miktzaso*—where some of the meat went outside but some remained within the permitted area. In this circumstance the analogy to the two *chatasim* works cleanly: the blood was effective for the meat that remained inside, and by extension—since the *korban* is a unified entity—it is also effective for the meat that exited.

### Complete Removal

If, however, all of the meat went outside—*yatza kulo*—Rabbi Yochanan rules that Rabbi Akiva would not apply his leniency. There is no remaining meat inside for which the *zrika* can first be said to operate, and without that anchor the extension to the *yotzei* meat loses its basis. In such a case, even Rabbi Akiva would agree with Rabbi Eliezer that the *zrika* does not change the status of the meat, and *me'ilah* continues to apply.

## Avud and Saruph

### Pigul on Nothing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַסִּי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּבָר לִימְּדוּנִי חֲבֵירַי שֶׁבַּגּוֹלָה מְחַשְּׁבִין עַל הָאָבוּד וְעַל הַשָּׂרוּף.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: My colleagues in the exile,** i.e., the Sages of Babylonia, **already taught me** that **one** renders an offering *piggul* by having **an intention** at the time of sprinkling to later sacrifice its sacrificial portions after the proper time, even **with regard to** sacrificial portions that were already **lost and with regard to** sacrificial portions that were already **burned.**

Rav Assi reports a ruling in the name of his colleagues from the *golah*—Babylonia: *mechashvin al ha'avud v'al hasaruph*—a *machshevet pigul* can attach to meat or *emurim* that have been lost (*avud*) or burned (*saruph*). The *pigul* thought is not directed at an object that actually exists at the moment of the thought. And yet, the teaching from Babylon is that such a thought is nonetheless legally significant.

### Efficacy of Thought

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָבוּד וְשָׂרוּף לָא אִיתִינּוּ בָּעוֹלָם, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי קָתָנֵי מְחַשְּׁבִין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **But** sacrificial portions that are **lost or burned are not present in the world,** i.e., they are not available, **and even so** it is **taught** that **one** renders an offering *piggul* by **an intention** to consume it after its appointed time.

The observation that follows drives the point home: *ha'avud vehasaruph lo iseinu ba'olam*—what is lost or burned does not exist in the world at all—*v'afilu hachi tani mechashvin*—and yet the tradition teaches that one can have an effective *pigul* thought about it. The import for the broader sugya will emerge in the continuation: the principle that even a non-existent object can anchor a *machshava* has implications for how we think about the necessity of a present, viable object when evaluating whether a *pigul* thought or an analogous legal act is effective. This discussion continues onto the following *daf*.
