# Meilah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Status Changes Overview

### Zrika & Status Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ נוֹתָר שֶׁאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד? לָן לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites another source that might resolve the issue: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* that can provide a proof with regard to the meaning of Rabbi Yehoshua's statement about a period of fitness to the priests: **Rabbi Shimon says** that **there is** a case of ***notar*,** when the blood was left overnight and was rendered unfit, **where** one is liable for **misusing** the meat of the offering, **and there is** also a case of ***notar* where** one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If the blood was **left over** and someone consumed the meat **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property. But if it was consumed **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property, as the sprinkling removes the meat from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse.

The topic under discussion concerns how the status of a *korban* with respect to *meilah* changes once the sacrificial rite is performed. *Kodshei kodashim* — the most holy offerings — are entirely subject to *meilah* because they are, so to speak, wholly consecrated to God. However, once the blood is thrown upon the *mizbeach*, in the case of a *chatat* or an *asham*, the meat becomes permissible for the *kohanim* to eat. From that point forward, the meat no longer carries *meilah*; *meilah* still applies to the remainder of the *korban*, but not to the meat itself. This, in brief, is the rule for *kodshei kodashim*: *zrikat hadam* removes the meat from the *meilah* category.

The reverse obtains for *kodashim kalim*. These offerings are fundamentally regarded as *mamon ba'alim* — essentially the owner's property — and are certainly not wholly surrendered to the divine domain. They will be eaten by the owners, and therefore only the portions destined for the *mizbeach*, the *emurim*, acquire *meilah* status — and only after *zrikat hadam* renders them fit. Thus, *zrikat hadam* for *kodshei kodashim* removes the meat from the *meilah* category, while for *kodashim kalim* it brings the *emurim* into the *meilah* category. That is the foundational rule.

### Zrika vs. Kabbalah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לָאו דַּהֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, דְּאִי בָּעֵי – זָרֵיק, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ! לָא, דְּקַבְּלֵיהּ סָמוּךְ לִשְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה, דְּלֹא הָיָה שְׁהוּת לְמִזְרַק. אֲבָל הָיָה שְׁהוּת, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין? מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה״, לִיתְנֵי ״קוֹדֶם שְׁקִיעָה״ וּ״לְאַחַר שְׁקִיעָה״! הָכִי נָמֵי קָתָנֵי: קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה, וּלְאַחַר שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **In any event,** Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that if one consumes the meat before the leftover blood was sprinkled, he is liable for **misusing it.** Is this **not** referring to a case **where there was time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood that had already been collected in the service vessel, and therefore, **if he had desired, he could have sprinkled** the blood? Nevertheless, the offering is subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. This indicates that merely collecting the blood in the service vessel alone, without actually sprinkling it, does not remove the offering from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse. **And** accordingly, one may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned** in Rabbi Yehoshua's statement in the mishna. It is the fitness of consuming the meat of the offering that removes the possibility of being liable for the prohibition of misuse, not the fitness of sprinkling. The Gemara refutes this conclusion: **No,** the *baraita* is not referring to a case where there was time left in the day to sprinkle the blood that had already been collected. Rather, it is referring to a situation **where** the priest **collected** the blood **shortly before sunset, where there was no time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood while it was still daytime. Since the blood could not have been sprinkled, the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. But if there had been time to sprinkle the blood, then that blood would be considered ready to be sprinkled, and the offering would no longer be subject to the prohibition of misuse, in accordance with the opinion that the criteria is the fitness of sprinkling of the blood. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** in that case, **what** is the *halakha* in a situation where there **was time** in the day to sprinkle the blood? According to the above claim, **so too** the *halakha* is **that** he is **not** liable for **misusing** the offering. If so, **why does** Rabbi Shimon **specifically teach** this distinction between a case **before sprinkling,** when the offering is still subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, and after sprinkling, when the offering is no longer subject to misuse? **Let** Rabbi Shimon instead **teach** a more precise distinction, between a situation where the blood was collected **before sunset** and there was time to sprinkle it but it was left overnight, in which case the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse, **and** a situation where the blood was collected **after sunset,** in which case it is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara answers that this is **indeed** what Rabbi Shimon meant, as he actually **taught: Before** it was **fit for sprinkling,** the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse, **but after** it was **fit for sprinkling,** it is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse.

What the *Gemara* has been examining over the last several days is a series of questions about borderline or quasi-valid *zrikas*. One question concerned *zrikat piggul* — the throwing of blood in a *korban* that had been invalidated by an improper intention. *Zrikat piggul* is in some sense a recognized act, and the question was whether it is treated as a valid *zrika* capable of changing *meilah* status. That question appeared to be answered negatively, though it will be revisited.

The other central question, which occupies the first *amud* of today's *daf*, concerns whether it is the moment of *zrika* itself that is legally decisive, or rather an earlier moment — the moment of *kabbalat hadam*, when the blood is received in the vessel. At that point the blood is already fit for *zrika*, and the question is whether that fitness alone suffices to effect the change in status even before the *zrika* has actually occurred. The *Gemara* captures this distinction in a slightly confusing formulation: the act of *kabbalah* is termed *hetter zrika* — that which makes it fit for throwing — while the actual performance of the *zrika* constitutes *hetter achilah*, the act that renders the meat itself permitted. The question, then, is whether *hetter zrika* alone — the act of *kabbalah* — is enough to alter *meilah* status, or whether the legally operative moment is only *hetter achilah*, the *zrika* itself.

## Notar & Meilah Status

### Rabbi Shimon Notar

The *Gemara* opens with a *ta shema* — "come and hear" — citing a teaching of *Rabbi Shimon*: there is *notar* upon which *meilah* applies, and there is *notar* upon which *meilah* does not apply. The text naturally assumes the context of *kodshei kodashim*. And how can this be? The *Gemara* states: if the meat slept overnight — became *notar* — before the blood was thrown, *meilah* applies, because the meat was never rendered *mutar*. If the *notar* condition arose after the *zrika*, however, *meilah* does not apply, because there was a moment when the meat became permitted.

On its surface this is straightforward: did the problem arise before or after *zrikat hadam*? The reading that seems most natural is that *Rabbi Shimon* is drawing the dividing line precisely at the moment of *zrika*.

### Gemara Challenge

The *Gemara* presses: the case of *notar* before *zrika* — does this not include a scenario where the blood was already received (*kabbalah*) in time, there was ample opportunity to perform the *zrika*, and yet the *zrika* was not done until the next day? In that scenario the blood was *rauy l'mizrak* — fit to be thrown — when it was caught, even if it was not actually thrown until after the problem arose. The *Gemara* therefore argues: *shema mina hetter achilah shaninu* — we see from here that the decisive moment is actual *zrikat hadam*, the moment the meat becomes permitted in fact, not merely the moment it becomes fit for *zrika*.

The *Gemara* responds: no, perhaps the case under discussion is one where the *kabbalah* was performed immediately before *shkiat hachama* — sunset — so that by the time the blood was received, there was no time remaining to perform the *zrika*. The blood was never *rauy l'mizrak* from the outset.

The *Gemara* then asks the obvious follow-up: if that is the case — if the decisive factor is whether there was ever an opportunity to perform the *zrika* — then why does the *baraita* frame the distinction as before versus after *zrika*? It should have framed it as before versus after *shkiya*, or more precisely, as a *kabbalah* that was or was not followed by an opportunity for *zrika*. Why mention *zrika* at all?

### Zrika = Rauy L'Zrika

The *Gemara* answers: *hachi nami ka'amar* — that is indeed what it means. When *Rabbi Shimon* says *lifnei zrika* and *le'achar zrika*, he does not mean literal *zrika*; he means *kodem she'rauy l'zrika* and *le'achar she'rauy l'zrika* — before or after the blood became fit for throwing. The term *zrika* in the *baraita* is not to be read literally but as shorthand for the moment the blood became eligible for *zrika*, i.e., the moment of *kabbalah*. If the *notar* problem arose before the blood was ever fit for *zrika*, there is *meilah*; if it arose after the blood was already fit — even if the actual *zrika* had not yet been performed — then *meilah* does not apply.

This is admittedly a strained reading of the *baraita*, but the *Gemara* presses it through nonetheless. The simple reading of *Rabbi Shimon*'s words would identify the moment of *zrikat hadam* as the critical one — and that is indeed what the plain sense suggests.

### Rashi's Reading

*Rashi* reads the passage differently, and this is worth noting because his reading is driven by what the *Gemara* will go on to say about *piggul*. Rather than reading the *baraita* as a discussion of *when* the problem of *notar* arose, *Rashi* reads it as a discussion of *when the meat was eaten*: if one derived benefit from the meat *before zrikat hadam*, there is *meilah*; if one derived benefit from the meat *after zrikat hadam* — even blood that was *lan* (overnight) and therefore invalid — there is no *meilah*. On this reading, *Rashi* is suggesting that even a *zrika* of *notar* blood, which is not a valid *zrika*, is sufficient to remove the meat from *meilah* status. This reading is admittedly difficult, and it will become clear why *Rashi* is compelled toward it when the parallel case of *piggul* is introduced.

## Piggul Status Question

### Rabbi Shimon Piggul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁמּוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְיֵשׁ פִּיגּוּל שֶׁאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ. כֵּיצַד? לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין, לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה – אֵין מוֹעֲלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests another proof from a similar *baraita*. **Come** and **hear: Rabbi Shimon says** that **there is** a case of **an offering** of the most sacred order that was sacrificed with ***piggul*** intent **where** one is liable for **misusing it, and there is** also a case of ***piggul*** intent **where** one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** The *baraita* elaborates: **How so?** If someone consumed the meat **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is liable for **misusing** consecrated property. If he consumed it **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood, he is **not** liable for **misusing** consecrated property, as the sprinkling removed the prohibition of misuse.

The *Gemara* now cites a parallel teaching from *Rabbi Shimon*: there is *piggul* upon which *meilah* applies, and there is *piggul* upon which *meilah* does not apply. Again: *lifnei zrika, moalin; le'achar zrika, ein moalin*. If the problem of *piggul* arose before *zrika*, there is *meilah*; if after, there is not.

Read at the straightforward level — as we read the *notar* case — this means: did the *piggul* invalidation arise before or after the *zrika*? If before, the *zrika* was never a *matir* and the meat remains subject to *meilah*. If after — or during — the *zrika* was operative and the meat was removed from *meilah*.

Before proceeding, note that the *Gemara* raises a separate issue: why does *Rabbi Shimon* need to state these two cases — *notar* and *piggul* — separately, when the words are essentially identical and could have been combined? One possibility is that the first formulation originated in a *Tosefta*, where *Rabbi Shimon* appears *stam* (anonymously) with the *notar* teaching, and the *piggul* formulation was derived or added later — perhaps the first was original and the second an extension. There is also a practical mnemonic dimension: in oral tradition, the parallel formulations *yesh notar she'mo'alin bo… yesh piggul she'mo'alin bo* may have been more memorable in their separate, rhythmic form than if combined. And as will become clear, there is a substantive reason to distinguish them: the *piggul* case involves serious complications that do not arise in the *notar* case.

### Piggul Timing Issue

Here the analysis becomes genuinely difficult. The *piggul* disqualification can only arise at one of the four *avodos*: *shechita*, *kabbalah*, *holacha*, or *zrika*. To say, as *Rabbi Shimon* seems to, that *piggul* can arise *after zrika* is essentially impossible on its face — *zrika* is the last *avoda*, and *piggul* cannot arise after it. And to say that *piggul* arose *before zrika* and yet *zrika* somehow corrects the situation would argue directly against *Rav Giddel amar Rav*, who maintained that *zrikat piggul* — a *zrika* performed under *piggul* conditions — does count as a valid *zrika* to change *meilah* status.

*Rashi* is therefore compelled to read the passage differently, as noted above: the question is not when the *piggul* thought arose but when the benefit from the meat was taken. If one ate the meat before *zrikat hadam*, there is *meilah*; if one ate after *zrikat hadam* — even a *zrika* of *piggul* blood — then the *zrika*, even an invalid one, has operated to remove the meat from *meilah*. This directly contradicts *Rav Giddel amar Rav*'s position.

The preferred reading in the *shiur*, while acknowledging *Rashi*'s difficulty, is to read *Rabbi Shimon* at the *pshat* level: when did the *piggul* problem arise? If before *zrika*, the *zrika* was not a *matir*; if during *zrika*, then the question becomes whether *zrikat piggul* counts. The scenario of *piggul* at *holacha* — which *Rabbi Shimon* himself holds is a valid moment for *piggul*, since *Tosafos* notes that *Rabbi Shimon* maintains *piggul b'sha'as holacha* — could account for a case of *piggul* arising after *kabbalah* but before *zrika*, though *Tosafos* raises a difficulty even there, since one can perform *zrika* without *holacha*. The *Gemara*, *Rashi* notes simply, treats this as analogous to the earlier discussion of *notar*, but the *piggul* case is demonstrably more complex.

### Piggul & Rauy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהַת: לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה – מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לָאו דַּהֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, דְּאִי בָּעֵי – זָרֵיק, וְקָתָנֵי ״מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ״, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ! לָא, דְּלָא הֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ. אֲבָל הֲוָה שְׁהוּת לְמִיזְרְקֵיהּ, מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דִּנְפַק מִידֵי מְעִילָה? מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי ״לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה״, לִיתְנֵי ״קוֹדֶם שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה״ וּ״לְאַחַר שְׁקִיעַת הַחַמָּה״! הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: קוֹדֶם שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה, לְאַחַר שֶׁיֵּרָאֶה לִזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that if one consumes the meat of an offering that was rendered *piggul* **before** the blood was **sprinkled,** he is liable for **misusing it.** Is this **not** referring to a case **where there was time** left in the day **to sprinkle** the blood that had already been collected in the service vessel, and therefore, **if he had desired, he could have sprinkled** the blood? **And** yet Rabbi Shimon **teaches** that one is liable for **misusing it.** Once again, this would indicate that merely collecting the blood in the service vessel alone, without sprinkling, does not remove the possibility of the prohibition of misuse. **And** accordingly, one may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned** in Rabbi Yehoshua's statement in the mishna. The Gemara refutes this conclusion: **No,** the *baraita* is referring to a situation where the priest collected the blood shortly before sunset, **where there was no time to sprinkle** the blood while it was still daytime. Since the blood could not have been sprinkled, the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But** if so, **what** is the *halakha* in a case where there **was time** in the day **to sprinkle** the blood? According to the above claim, the offering is **indeed removed** from being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** If so, **why does** Rabbi Shimon **specifically teach** this distinction between **after sprinkling,** when the offering is no longer subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, and before sprinkling, when the offering is still subject to misuse? **Let** Rabbi Shimon instead **teach** a more precise distinction, between a situation where the blood was collected **before sunset** and there was time to sprinkle it but it was left overnight, in which case the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse, **and** a situation where the blood was collected **after sunset,** in which case it is still subject to the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara answers that **that is indeed what** Rabbi Shimon **is saying: Before** it was **fit for sprinkling,** the offering is still subject to the prohibition of misuse, **but after** it was **fit for sprinkling,** it is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse.

The *Gemara* now mirrors the earlier analysis. It notes: *katani miha*, whatever the exact reading, the *baraita* says that *piggul* before *zrika* results in *meilah*. Does this not include a case where the *kabbalah* was performed properly, there was time to do the *zrika*, and only then the *piggul* problem arose? If so — *shema mina hetter achilah shaninu* — the key moment is the *zrika* itself, not the *kabbalah*.

The *Gemara* responds: no, perhaps the case is one where there was never time to perform *zrika* — the *kabbalah* was done so close to *shkiat hachama* that the blood was never fit for *zrika*. The *Gemara* then asks: but if there *was* time to do the *zrika* and the *piggul* arose after *kabbalah*, would we say there is no *meilah*? And if so — *mai irya d'tani le'achar zrika*? — why frame the dividing line as before or after *zrika*? Why not before or after *kabbalah*, or before or after the blood was *rauy l'zrika*?

The *Gemara* answers, as before: *hachi nami ka'amar* — *kodem she'ra'uyah l'zrika, le'achar she'ra'uyah l'zrika*. The term *zrika* in *Rabbi Shimon*'s teaching is again shorthand for *rauy l'zrika*, the moment the blood became eligible for *zrika*. The whole analysis is admittedly strained (*dachuk*), particularly for the *piggul* case, and the *Gemara* is essentially reading its conclusions into the text and drawing them back out. Yet the doctrinal point stands: *Rabbi Shimon* holds that the decisive moment is *zrika*, and the *Gemara* has now resolved this in favor of *kabbalah* — or more precisely, *rauy l'zrika* — being the operative moment.

### Piggul Kodshei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַפִּיגּוּל בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים – מוֹעֲלִין. מַאי לָאו דְּזָרַק, וּשְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה שָׁנִינוּ! לָא, דְּלֹא זָרַק. אֲבָל זָרַק מַאי? הָכִי נָמֵי דְּאֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ? מַאי אִירְיָא דְּתָנֵי בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, לִיתְנֵי: כָּאן – לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, כָּאן – לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה! הָא אָתְיָא לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן: כֹּל לְאֵיתוֹיֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה, זְרִיקָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה. כֹּל לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִידֵי מְעִילָה, אֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ נָמֵי מַפְקַע מִידֵי מְעִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara suggests another proof. **Come** and **hear: An offering of the most sacred order** which is ***piggul*** is subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **What, is** this *baraita* **not** referring to a case **where** the priest already **sprinkled** its blood? This would indicate that a fit offering, unlike a *piggul* offering, is no longer subject to the prohibition of misuse only once the blood is sprinkled. **And** if so, one may **conclude from** the *baraita* that it is **fitness** of **consuming** the meat of the offering that **we learned** in Rabbi Yehoshua's statement in the mishna, i.e., this fitness of consuming the meat of the offering removes the prohibition of misuse. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No,** one cannot cite a proof from this *baraita*, as it is possible that the *baraita* is referring to a case **where** the priest did **not** yet **sprinkle** the blood. **But** if so, **what,** then, is the *halakha* in a case where the priest did **sprinkle** the blood? Is the *halakha* **indeed that** one is **no** longer liable for **misusing it?** If so, **why does** the latter clause of the *baraita* **specifically teach:** Unlike an offering of the most sacred order, **in** the case of the sacrificial portions of **an offering of lesser sanctity** one is **not** liable for **misusing it?** **Let** the *baraita* instead **teach** a distinction within the category of offerings of the most sacred order themselves: **Here,** the offering is subject to the prohibition of misuse because it is **before** the **sprinkling** of the blood, and **there,** the offering is not subject to the prohibition of misuse because it is **after** the **sprinkling** of the blood. The Gemara answers: The *baraita* could have taught that distinction, but it chose to state a distinction between offerings of the most sacred order and offerings of lesser sanctity because it **comes to teach us this** following principle: In **any** case where the result of sprinkling **brings** the offering **into** the category of the *halakhot* of **misuse,** e.g., the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity, to which the *halakhot* of misuse apply only after the blood had been sprinkled, only **sprinkling** the blood **properly brings** the offering **into** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, in **any** situation where the result of sprinkling is **removing** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse,** e.g., the meat of an offering of the most sacred order, which was subject to the *halakhot* of misuse even before the blood was sprinkled, **even sprinkling** the blood **improperly removes** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse.** This ruling is not in accordance with the opinion of Rav Giddel.

The *Gemara* now cites a *baraita* that had also been quoted on the previous *daf*: *piggul b'kodshei kodashim, mo'alin; piggul b'kodashim kalim, ein mo'alin*. The *Gemara* asks: does this not mean that even *piggul* at the time of *zrika* — or after *kabbalah* — results in *meilah* by *kodshei kodashim*? If so, *shema mina hetter achilah shaninu* — the operative moment is the actual *zrika*, not the *kabbalah*.

The *Gemara* replies: no, *lav d'lo zarak* — the case is one where the *zrika* was never performed and the *piggul* problem arose before the *zrika*. The *Gemara* then presses: but if the *zrika* was performed with a *piggul* thought (*d'iy zarak, mai*), would there also be no *meilah*? And if so, why did the *baraita* need to go all the way to *kodashim kalim* to find an example of no *meilah*? It could have simply said: even in *kodshei kodashim*, if the *piggul* arose after *kabbalah* there is no *meilah*.

The *Gemara* answers: *ha atya l'ashmueinan* — the *baraita* comes to teach us something else entirely, something that will become a central organizing principle. And that principle is stated as follows:

> *Kol ha'asuy lidei me'ilah, zrika k'tiknah mayti lidei me'ilah; kol ha'apukei midei me'ilah, afilu shelo k'tiknah nami mapik midei me'ilah.*

Anything destined to enter the *meilah* category requires a fully valid *zrika* to get there. But anything being taken *out* of the *meilah* category — even a *zrika* that is less than perfect, not fully *k'tiknah* — can accomplish that removal.

---

## Up/Down Asymmetry

### Into/Out of Meilah

This principle reflects a fundamental asymmetry in how *meilah* status is acquired versus relinquished. *Meilah* is an extremely narrow and tenuous category — the domain of *Kodshei Hashem*. That narrowness has two possible implications that cut in opposite directions.

### Kol Ha'asuy Principle

One approach would say: precisely because *meilah* is such a tenuous and exalted status, it requires something absolutely definitive to dislodge it. If something starts off as *kodshei kodashim* and one seeks to render it *mutar*, that requires a completely valid *zrika* — nothing questionable will do. Conversely, to bring something *into* the *meilah* category, it is always easier to be *machmir*, to treat marginal cases as *asur*.

The other approach inverts this: precisely because *meilah* is a special and narrow category, it is easy to fall out of it. The slightest question mark, the slightest imperfection in the *zrika* — whether it is a *zrikat piggul*, whether it is merely *rauy l'zrika*, whether it is a *zrika* performed on a *yotzei* — is enough to say that the item is no longer unambiguously in the domain of *Kodshei Hashem*, and therefore *meilah* falls away. But to bring something into that category from outside — the *emurim* of *kodashim kalim*, which are fundamentally *mamon ba'alim* — nothing less than a completely valid *zrika* will suffice, because something genuinely marginal cannot be elevated into that special category.

The *Gemara* here is adopting this second logic: *kol ha'apukei midei me'ilah, afilu shelo k'tiknah nami mapik* — it is easy to exit *meilah*, even with a flawed *zrika*; but to enter *meilah* requires a fully valid act. The *baraita*'s discussion of *kodashim kalim* was thus not merely providing an example — it was underscoring that for *kodashim kalim*, any imperfection in the *zrika* leaves the *emurim* in the "no *meilah*" category, since getting *into* the *meilah* category demands full validity.

What emerges from this extended discussion is that the *Gemara* has been wrestling with three types of questionable *zrika*: (1) *zrikat piggul*, a *zrika* performed under the shadow of an improper intention; (2) *rauy l'zrika*, the *kabbalah* that renders the blood fit for *zrika* without actually being *zrika*; and (3) *zrika* performed on an item bearing the *psul* of *yotzei*, which will be the subject of the next *mishnah*. The *maskana* is not yet fully resolved — whether, for instance, *zrikat piggul* would be effective to remove something from *meilah* (which would argue against *Rav Giddel amar Rav*) or whether *rauy l'zrika* alone suffices — but the organizing framework is now clear: the standard may differ depending on the direction of movement, and possibly also depending on whether we are dealing with *kodshei kodashim* or *kodashim kalim*.

## Yotzei Kodshei

### Mishnah Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בְּשַׂר קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים שֶׁיָּצָא לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, וְחַיָּיבִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל וְנוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna presents a dispute with regard to the status of offerings of the most sacred order, which normally are not subject to the *halakhot* of misuse once their blood has been sprinkled and they have been permitted to the priests. The case of the mishna is **the meat of offerings of the most sacred order,** whose consumption is permitted from the moment their blood was sprinkled, **that left** the Temple courtyard **before the sprinkling of the blood,** and then reentered the courtyard. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The sprinkling of this blood does not permit its consumption by the priests. Consequently, one is liable for **misusing it. And** he is **not liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*,** if he partook of it after it was slaughtered with the intent to partake of it or sprinkle its blood beyond its designated time, **or** of ***notar*,** if he partook of the meat after it remained overnight, **or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure. Rabbi Akiva says:** The sprinkling is effective despite the fact that the meat left the Temple courtyard and was disqualified, and therefore one is **not** liable for **misusing it.** Likewise, other *halakhot* that apply to offerings whose blood was sprinkled apply to it, **and** consequently **one is liable for** eating **it due to** violation of the prohibitions of partaking of meat that is ***piggul*, or *notar*, or** remained overnight, **or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

The *Gemara* now turns to *amud bet* and a new *mishnah*. The case: meat of *kodshei kodashim* that was taken outside the permitted area (*yatza*) before *zrikat damim*. This is the *psul* of *yotzei* — once the meat has left the precincts of the *Beis HaMikdash*, it is disqualified. *Rashi* notes that even if one could theoretically bring it back, the disqualification attaches from the moment it exits.

### R. Eliezer Yotzei

*Rabbi Eliezer* rules: *mo'alin bo* — *meilah* applies to this meat even after the *zrika* is performed on the rest of the *korban*. His reasoning is clear: the meat that has become *yotzei* was never *ra'uy la'achilah* — it never became eligible for consumption — and therefore its status does not change. The *zrika* of the blood changes the status of meat that was *kasher*, but this meat was already *pasul*. It therefore remains in the *meilah* category.

Furthermore, *Rabbi Eliezer* holds: *ein chayavin alav mishum piggul, notar, v'tamei* — one who eats this meat is not liable for the *kareis* violations associated with *piggul*, *notar*, or *tamei*. Those prohibitions apply only to meat that was otherwise *kasher*. Here the meat was already *pasul*, so the additional prohibitions do not layer upon it. One who eats it is liable for *meilah*, but not for *piggul*, *notar*, or *tamei*.

### R. Akiva Chatasim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וַהֲרֵי הַמַּפְרִישׁ חַטָּאתוֹ וְאָבְדָה, וְהִפְרִישׁ אַחֶרֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְאַחַר כָּךְ נִמְצֵאת הָרִאשׁוֹנָה, וַהֲרֵי שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹמְדוֹת. לֹא כְּשֵׁם שֶׁדָּמָהּ פּוֹטֵר אֶת בְּשָׂרָהּ, כָּךְ הוּא פּוֹטֵר אֶת בְּשַׂר חֲבֶרְתָּהּ? אִם פָּטַר דָּמָהּ אֶת בְּשַׂר חֲבֶרְתָּהּ מִן הַמְּעִילָה, דִּין הוּא שֶׁיִּפְטֹר אֶת בְּשַׂר עַצְמָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva said,** in support of his opinion: **But** there is the case of **one who designated** an animal as **his sin offering and it was lost, and he designated another** animal **in its stead, and thereafter the first** sin offering **was found and both of them are standing** fit for sacrifice. If he slaughtered both animals at the same time and sprinkled the blood of one of them, which means that the second was disqualified as a leftover sin offering, the question arises as to the status of the meat of the second animal with regard to the *halakhot* of misuse. **Is it not** the case that **just as** the **blood** of the animal whose blood was sprinkled **exempts its meat from** liability for its misuse, **so** too **it exempts the meat of the other** animal? Since he could have chosen to sprinkle the blood of either animal, they are considered as though they were one offering. If so, one may learn from there by an *a fortiori* inference with regard to the case of sprinkling the blood of meat that left the courtyard and returned: **If** the sprinkling of **its blood exempted the meat of the other** animal **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse,** it is only **right that it** should **exempt its own meat** that left the courtyard.

*Rabbi Akiva* disagrees: *ein mo'alin bo* — after the *zrika*, *meilah* does not apply to this meat. His reasoning is that although the meat has the *psul* of *yotzei*, the *zrika* is nonetheless operative to change its status — it is treated as if it were *ra'uy la'achilah*.

*Rabbi Akiva* supports this position with a striking analogy: *hameprish chataso ve'avda* — one who sets aside his *chatat* offering, loses it, sets aside a second one, and then the first is found, leaving both standing before him. Only one can be brought; the other becomes a *chatat shenitkapper ba'aleha be'acher*. Now, does the blood of the one that is brought also function with respect to the meat of the other? *Lo k'sheim she'damah poter et besarah, kach hu poter et besar chaverta* — just as the blood of one *chatat* renders its own meat released from *meilah*, so too does it function to release the meat of its companion.

The mechanics of the case, as *Rashi* explains, are as follows: both *chataot* were brought to the *Beis HaMikdash* and both were *shachtet*; at the moment of *zrika* the *kohen* could have chosen either one's blood, meaning both were conceptually interchangeable at that point. *Rabbi Akiva* argues that because they were effectively one composite *korban* at the decisive moment, the *zrika* of one's blood extends its operative effect to the meat of the other — not to permit it for eating, since it remains a *chatat shenitkapper*, but to remove it from *meilah*. Hence *Rabbi Akiva* uses the language *poter* — releasing from *meilah* — rather than *matir*, permitting for consumption.

It is fair to note that this is a challenging analogy to press into service — the case of two *chataot* is itself no less complex than the case of *yotzei* that *Rabbi Akiva* is trying to prove. Why, moreover, does *Rabbi Akiva* limit his position to *yotzei* and not extend it to other *psulim*, such as *tamei*? One can say that *yotzei* represents a lighter or more spatial disqualification — a matter of location — whereas *tumah* is an intrinsic disqualification, though this distinction itself is not without difficulty.

### Akiva Zrika Works

From this, *Rabbi Akiva* draws his conclusion: if the blood of one *chatat* can remove the meat of the other from *meilah* — which is a far more attenuated connection — then certainly the blood of the very same *korban* should be able to operate on its own meat, even though that meat has the *psul* of *yotzei*. The *zrika* is effective for *yotzei*; the meat is treated as if it were *ra'uy*; and because it is treated as if it were *ra'uy*, one who eats it is also liable — *chayavin alav mishum piggul, notar, v'tamei* — since those prohibitions apply to meat that is conceptually treated as *kasher*.

## Yotzei Kodashim

### Mishnah Emurim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימוּרַי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים שֶׁיָּצְאוּ לִפְנֵי זְרִיקַת דָּמִים, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֵין מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְאֵין חַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, וְחַיָּיבִין עֲלֵיהֶן מִשּׁוּם פִּיגּוּל נוֹתָר וְטָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna adds that just as Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva disagree as to whether the sprinkling of blood exempts meat that left the courtyard from liability for its misuse, so too, they disagree with regard to **the sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity** consumed on the altar **that left** the Temple courtyard **before the sprinkling of the blood.** The dispute is whether the subsequent sprinkling of the blood generates liability for misuse of those portions. **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The sprinkling of the blood is completely ineffective in rendering those portions consecrated to the Lord. Consequently, one is **not** liable for **misusing them. And** similarly, **one is not liable for their** consumption **due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of meat while **ritually impure. Rabbi Akiva says:** The sprinkling is effective, and therefore one is liable for **misusing them. And** likewise, **one is liable for its** consumption **due to** violation of the prohibitions of ***piggul*, *notar*, or** of partaking of the meat while **ritually impure.**

The *mishnah* then presents the parallel case for *kodashim kalim*: the *emurim* of a *shlamim* offering were taken outside (*yatzu*) before *zrikat hadam*. After *zrika*, the *emurim* would ordinarily be subject to *meilah* because they are fit for the *mizbeach*. But these *emurim* have the *psul* of *yotzei*.

### Machlokes Kodashim

*Rabbi Eliezer* rules: *ein mo'alin bahen* — *meilah* does not apply. Since the *emurim* are *pasul*, the *zrika* did not render them fit for the *mizbeach*; their status remains unchanged, and they do not enter the *meilah* category. And since they are *pasul*, the further prohibitions also do not apply: *ein chayavin aleihem mishum piggul, notar, v'tamei*.

*Rabbi Akiva* again disagrees: *mo'alin bahen* — the *zrika* is effective, it is as if the *emurim* are *ra'uy* for the *mizbeach*, and therefore *meilah* applies. And since they are conceptually treated as *ra'uy*, the *korban* is also subject to *chiyuvim* of *piggul*, *notar*, and *tamei*.

### Why Both Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָנֵי תַּרְתֵּי לְמָה לִי? צְרִיכִי, דְּאִי אִיתְּמַר בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: בְּהָא קָא אָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר מוֹעֲלִין בּוֹ, מִשּׁוּם דִּזְרִיקָה כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה. אֲבָל לְאֵיתוֹיֵי לִידֵי מְעִילָה – מוֹדֵי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא דַּאֲפִילּוּ שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתָא לִידֵי מְעִילָה. וְאִי אִיתְּמַר גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: גַּבֵּי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים הוּא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מוֹעֲלִין בָּהֶן, דַּאֲפִילּוּ זְרִיקָה שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ מַיְיתָא לִידֵי מְעִילָה. אֲבָל קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, דִּלְאַפּוֹקֵי הוּא, שֶׁלֹּא כְּתִיקְנָהּ – לָא מַפְּקָא מִידֵי מְעִילָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need the mishna to cite **these two** disagreements, i.e., both the case of offerings of the most sacred order and offerings of lesser sanctity? After all, both disagreements are based on the same principle. The Gemara answers: Both cases **are necessary, as, if** the disagreement **was stated** only **with regard to offerings of the most sacred order, I would say** that it is specifically in **that** case that **Rabbi Eliezer says** that one is liable for **misusing** the meat of the offering, **due to** the fact **that** only **sprinkling** the blood **properly removes** the offering **from** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** By contrast, sprinkling the blood **improperly,** including for meat that left the courtyard, **does not remove** the offering **from** the *halakhot* of **misuse. But** with regard to the issue of when the rite of sprinkling **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse,** i.e., in the case of sacrificial portions of offerings of lesser sanctity, Rabbi Eliezer **concedes to Rabbi Akiva that even** sprinkling the blood **improperly,** as in this case, **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** **And** by contrast, **if** their disagreement **was stated** only **with regard to offerings of lesser sanctity, I would say** that **it is** specifically **in** the case of **offerings of lesser sanctity that Rabbi Akiva said** one is liable for **misusing them.** This is due to the fact **that** here the act of sprinkling serves to include them in the category of misuse, and therefore **even sprinkling** the blood **improperly,** as in this case, **brings** the offering **into** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** **But** with regard to **offerings of the most sacred order, where** the sprinkling of the blood serves **to remove** the offering from being subject to the *halakhot* of misuse, one might say that Rabbi Akiva agrees **that** sprinkling the blood **improperly does not remove** the offering **from** being subject to the *halakhot* of **misuse.** Therefore, the mishna **teaches us** that the *tanna'im* disagree in both cases.

The *Gemara* asks: *v'hani tartei lama li* — why did the *mishnah* need to state both cases, *kodshei kodashim* and *kodashim kalim*? The *machlokes* appears identical in both: does *yotzei* prevent the *zrika* from being operative or not?

### Direction Standards

The *Gemara* answers *tzricha* — both are necessary — and proceeds to explain the asymmetry.

Had the *machlokes* been stated only regarding *kodshei kodashim*, one might have reasoned: *Rabbi Eliezer* says *mo'alin* — the *psul yotzei* blocks the *zrika* from removing the meat from *meilah* — because perhaps by *kodshei kodashim* the standard is more demanding. To *remove* something from *meilah*, it must be a fully valid *zrika*; a *zrika shelo k'tiknah* — a *zrika* on *yotzei* meat — does not suffice to exit the *meilah* category. But one might have thought *Rabbi Eliezer* would agree with *Rabbi Akiva* in the reverse direction: to *bring* something *into* the *meilah* category — since it is always easier to be *machmir* — even a flawed *zrika* would be effective. We therefore would not know *Rabbi Eliezer*'s position for *kodashim kalim*.

Conversely, had the *machlokes* been stated only regarding *kodashim kalim*, one might have reasoned: *Rabbi Akiva* says *mo'alin* — the *zrika* is effective to bring the *emurim* into *meilah* even with the *psul yotzei* — because when you are trying to be *machmir* and impose *meilah*, even a flawed *zrika* achieves that. But one might have thought *Rabbi Akiva* would agree with *Rabbi Eliezer* in *kodshei kodashim*: when you are trying to be *meikel* and remove something from *meilah*, there a fully valid *zrika* is required, and a *zrika* on *yotzei* meat does not accomplish that. We therefore would not know *Rabbi Akiva*'s position for *kodshei kodashim*.

Each *amora* states his position consistently in both cases, and the *mishnah* was therefore compelled to record both. The underlying tension — the asymmetry between the standard for entering the *meilah* category versus the standard for exiting it — remains present in both directions of the argument, and neither logical inference can substitute for the explicit ruling.

## Yotzei Limitation

### Partial Yotzei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כִּי אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא זְרִיקָה מוֹעֶלֶת לַיּוֹצֵא – שֶׁיָּצָא מִקְצָתוֹ, אֲבָל יוֹצֵא כּוּלּוֹ – לֹא אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It **was stated** that *amora'im* disagree with regard to the opinion of Rabbi Akiva in the mishna. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: When Rabbi Akiva says** that **sprinkling is effective to** remove the meat of offerings of the most sacred order **that left** the courtyard from the *halakhot* of misuse, that applies specifically in a case **where** only **part of it left** the courtyard and part remained inside. In such a situation, as the sprinkling is effective for the portion that remained inside the courtyard, it also is effective for the portion that left the courtyard. **But** if **all of it left** the courtyard, **Rabbi Akiva did not say** that the sprinkling is effective to remove the meat from the *halakhot* of misuse.

The *Gemara* records a further qualification from *Rabbi Yochanan*: *ki amar Rabbi Akiva zrika mo'eles l'yotzei* — when *Rabbi Akiva* said that *zrika* is effective for *yotzei* — this applies only *sheyatza miktzaso*, if only part of the meat or *emurim* went out while part remained inside. In that case, one can draw an analogy to the two *chataot*: just as the blood of one *chatat* worked for its companion because they were conceptually interchangeable at the moment of *zrika*, so here the blood works for the meat that remained, and that same *zrika* extends to the meat that went out, since they were all part of one *korban* at the decisive moment.

*Aval yatza kulo* — but if all the meat exited, *Rabbi Yochanan* holds that *Rabbi Akiva* would not say the *zrika* is effective. There, *Rabbi Akiva* would concede to *Rabbi Eliezer* that the *zrika* accomplishes nothing.

### Avud & Saruf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַסִּי לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּבָר לִימְּדוּנִי חֲבֵירַי שֶׁבַּגּוֹלָה, מְחַשְּׁבִין עַל הָאָבוּד וְעַל הַשָּׂרוּף. וְהָא אָבוּד וְשָׂרוּף לָא אִיתִינּוּ בָּעוֹלָם, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי קָתָנֵי מְחַשְּׁבִין!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said to Rabbi Yoḥanan: My colleagues in the exile,** i.e., the Sages of Babylonia, **already taught me** that **one** renders an offering *piggul* by having **an intention** at the time of sprinkling to later sacrifice its sacrificial portions after the proper time, even **with regard to** sacrificial portions that were already **lost and with regard to** sacrificial portions that were already **burned.** The Gemara analyzes this statement: **But** sacrificial portions that are **lost or burned are not present in the world,** i.e., they are not available, **and even so** it is **taught** that **one** renders an offering *piggul* by **an intention** to consume it after its appointed time. It can be derived from here that if the entire offering left the courtyard, which is comparable to a case where it was lost, the sprinkling is effective with regard to the *halakhot* of misuse according to Rabbi Akiva. This apparently contradicts the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan.

*Rav Assi* then presents a challenge to *Rabbi Yochanan* from what his colleagues in exile (*chaverai she'bagolah*) had taught him: *mechashvin al ha'avud v'al hasaruf* — one can have a *machsheves piggul* thought directed at an object that has already been lost or burned, and it nonetheless constitutes a valid *piggul* thought. The *avud* and the *saruf* do not exist in the world at all — *lo isinhu ba'olam* — and yet the *Gemara* teaches that one can still be *mechashev* upon them to create *piggul*.

The point *Rav Assi* is pressing: the ruling of *mechashvin al ha'avud v'al hasaruf* demonstrates that one does not need any residual portion of an object to remain extant for a *halachic* act to be meaningful with respect to it. This seems to challenge *Rabbi Yochanan*'s limitation on *Rabbi Akiva* — the requirement that at least part of the meat remains inside for the *zrika* to operate on the *yotzei* portion. If even a totally destroyed or lost object can be the subject of a valid *piggul* thought, perhaps *Rabbi Akiva* would agree that *zrika* operates even when all the meat has gone out. The *Gemara*'s analysis of this challenge will continue in the next session.
