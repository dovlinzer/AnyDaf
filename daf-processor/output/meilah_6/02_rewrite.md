# Meilah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodshei Kodashim Status

### Basic Me'ilah Rule

The subject under discussion is the way in which a *korban*'s status with respect to *me'ilah* can change once the offering has been brought. *Kodshei kodashim* are entirely subject to *me'ilah* — they belong, as it were, entirely to God. Once the blood is sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*, however, the meat of a *chatas* or *asham* becomes permissible to the *kohanim*, and therefore *me'ilah* no longer applies to that meat. *Me'ilah* still governs the remaining portions of the *korban*, but the meat itself is released. The reverse dynamic operates for *kodshim kalim*: since those offerings are fundamentally considered the owner's property and will be eaten by the owners, they do not initially carry *me'ilah*. Only the *emurim* — the portions designated for the *mizbe'ach* — become subject to *me'ilah*, and only after *zrikas ha-dam*. Thus, *zrikas ha-dam* operates in two directions: for *kodshei kodashim*, it releases the meat from *me'ilah*; for *kodshim kalim*, it brings the *emurim* into the *me'ilah* category.

### Zrikat Pigul

The *gemara* has been examining what happens when the *zrika* is not a fully valid one, but rather a quasi-valid act. One such case is *zrikas pigul* — a sprinkling performed with improper intent. There is a question whether such a *zrika* is considered meaningful enough to effect the status change. That position appears to be rejected, though the *gemara* will return to it. A second question — which occupies the bulk of this *amud* — is whether the critical moment is the actual *zrika* itself or an earlier moment, namely *kabbalas ha-dam*, the catching of the blood. Once *kabbala* has been performed, the blood is *ra'ui l'zrika* — fit for sprinkling — and one might argue that this earlier moment should be sufficient to effect the status change even before the actual *zrika* takes place. The *gemara* refers to the act of *kabbala* as *heter zrika* — the fitting of the blood for sprinkling — while the actual *zrika* constitutes *heter achila*, the moment when the meat actually becomes permitted for consumption.

## Nosar: Before/After Zrika

### Rabbi Shimon: Nosar

The *gemara* opens this section with a citation: *ta shema* — come and hear. Rabbi Shimon teaches that there are cases of *nosar* in which *me'ilah* applies and cases in which it does not. The question is how to understand this distinction. The term *nosar* refers to meat left over beyond its permitted time for consumption, which thereby becomes disqualified.

### Peshat: Nosar Timeline

Reading this at what appears to be the straightforward *peshat* level: Rabbi Shimon's cases are as follows. If the meat stayed overnight — becoming *nosar* — before the blood was sprinkled, and the blood was then sprinkled, the *zrika* at that point was not valid, since the blood itself had also remained overnight; the meat was therefore never rendered *mutar*, and *me'ilah* continues to apply. Conversely, if the blood was sprinkled at the proper time and the meat stayed overnight only afterward, there was a moment when the meat became *mutar*, and consequently *me'ilah* does not apply. The dividing line is simply whether the problem occurred before or after the *zrika*.

The *gemara* then attempts to draw a conclusion from Rabbi Shimon's statement: if he says that when *linah* occurred before *zrika* there is *me'ilah*, that implies that even if the blood had been caught — making it *ra'ui l'zrok* — but the *zrika* was delayed until the next day, the status would still not have changed. From this one might infer that the critical moment is the actual *zrika*, not the *kabbala*. The *gemara*, however, deflects: perhaps the case involves a situation where the blood was caught just before sunset, leaving no time for *zrika* at all — so that there was never a moment when the blood was fit for sprinkling. If that is the case, one cannot derive anything about whether *kabbala* alone, when there was time to sprinkle, would suffice.

### Gemara Challenge

The *gemara* then raises the obvious objection. Granting that interpretation, what of the meat that did stay overnight under those circumstances — when the blood was caught just before sunset? If the key moment is not the *zrika* but rather whether there was time and opportunity for *zrika*, then why does Rabbi Shimon frame the distinction around *zrika* at all? He should have framed the distinction around *sheki'as ha-chamah*: before *sheki'ah* versus after *sheki'ah* — that is, whether the blood was caught when there was still time to sprinkle or not.

### Ra'ui L'zrika

The *gemara* resolves this by saying: indeed, that is precisely what Rabbi Shimon means. When he says "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," he does not mean the literal act of sprinkling. He means before or after the point at which the blood was *ra'ui l'zrika* — fit to be sprinkled. If *linah* occurred before there was any opportunity to perform *zrika*, there is *me'ilah*; if it occurred after *kabbala* had been performed and the blood was fit for *zrika*, the status has already changed and there is no *me'ilah*. On this reading, *zrika* throughout Rabbi Shimon's statement is a shorthand for *ra'ui l'zrika*, and the critical moment is the *kabbala*, not the *zrika* itself. The straightforward sense of the *braita*, however, does indicate that the literal act of *zrikas ha-dam* is the critical moment.

### Rashi: Nosar Reading

Rashi reads the entire passage differently, and he is driven to do so by what follows in the *gemara*. In Rashi's reading, the discussion is not about when the problem of *linah* arose but about when the *hana'ah* — the benefit derived — from the meat took place. If one benefited from the meat before the *zrika* of the *nosar* blood, there is *me'ilah*. If one benefited after the *zrika* of the *nosar* blood — even though that blood was *nosar* — Rashi understands that such a *zrika* nevertheless removes the meat from *me'ilah*, analogously to the way we will discuss *pigul*. This is admittedly a difficult reading, since the blood would also be *nosar* if the meat is *nosar*, and it is not clear why such a *zrika* should be effective. Rashi's reading will become more intelligible in light of the next section of the *gemara*, which is why he adopts it here.

## Pigul: Before/After (II)

### Rabbi Shimon: Pigul

A parallel teaching follows: Rabbi Shimon states that there are cases of *pigul* in which *me'ilah* applies and cases in which it does not. *Pigul* is the disqualification arising from improper intent at the time of the sacrificial service — intention to eat the meat or burn the *emurim* beyond the permitted time. Again, the dividing line is stated as before or after *zrika*.

### Pigul After Kabbalah

Reading this at the straightforward *peshat* level: if the *pigul* intent arose before *zrika*, the *zrika* was never a valid *matir*, and *me'ilah* remains in effect. If the *pigul* intent arose after *zrika*, the *zrika* had already been a valid *matir*, and therefore there is no *me'ilah*. This is clearly analogous to the *nosar* case.

The problem, however, is immediately apparent, and this is precisely what troubled Rashi. *Pigul* can only arise during specific moments of the *avodah*: *shechita*, *kabbala*, *holacha*, or *zrika*. There is no way for *pigul* to arise after *zrika* — by the time *zrika* is complete, all the relevant moments of the service have passed. One cannot therefore simply say "the *pigul* arose after *zrika*," because that is not a possible scenario. Rashi was thus compelled to read the entire passage — both the *nosar* and *pigul* cases — as being about when the *hana'ah* was derived, not about when the disqualifying event arose. On that reading, even a *zrika* of *nosar* blood or *pigul* blood can change the status and take the meat out of *me'ilah* — which directly addresses, and disputes, the ruling of Rav Gidal in the name of Rav.

An alternative reading at the *peshat* level is to take "*achar zrika*" as not strictly meaning after *zrika* but rather *b'sha'at zrika* — at the moment of *zrika* itself. On this reading, if the *pigul* intent arose earlier, the *zrika* was never a valid *matir*; if it arose during the *zrika*, then it argues with Rav Gidal's position, which holds that *zrikas pigul* does not constitute a valid *zrika*. This makes the *gemara* somewhat less strained, even if the problem is not entirely resolved.

### Gemara: Why Zrika Not Ka…

The *gemara* now presses the same challenge it pressed in the *nosar* case. The statement says that if *pigul* arose before *zrika*, there is *me'ilah* — implying that even in a case where the blood had already been caught and was *ra'ui l'zrik*, but the *pigul* arose afterward, there is still *me'ilah*. This would suggest that the critical moment is the literal *zrika*, not the *kabbala*. From this the *gemara* attempts to conclude that *heter achila* — actual permissibility through *zrika* — is what matters, not mere fitness for *zrika* through *kabbala*.

The *gemara* deflects: the case is one where the blood was caught immediately before *sheki'ah*, leaving no time for *zrika*, so that it was never *ra'ui l'zrika* at all. But this invites the same challenge as before: if the key distinction is whether the blood was *ra'ui l'zrika* or not, why not frame the ruling around *sheki'as ha-chamah* rather than around *zrika*?

## Pigul: Before/After (II)

### Zrika = Ra'ui L'zrika

The *gemara* resolves the challenge in the same manner as before: when Rabbi Shimon speaks of "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," he means before or after the point at which the blood was fit for *zrika* — that is, before or after *kabbala*. *Zrika* is throughout a shorthand for *ra'ui l'zrika*. The *pigul* case is acknowledged to be more strained in its application of this reading, given the inherent difficulty of locating a moment when *pigul* could arise after *kabbala* but before *zrika* — the only candidate would be the end of *kabbala* itself, as Tosafot suggests. The difficulties notwithstanding, the overarching conclusion from both the *nosar* and *pigul* discussions is that the critical moment according to Rabbi Shimon is the *kabbala*, reframed as *ra'ui l'zrika*, rather than the literal act of *zrika*.

### Braita: Pigul Me'ilah

The *gemara* then cites a *braita* that was encountered in the preceding discussion: *pigul* in *kodshei kodashim* is subject to *me'ilah*. On a first reading, this might seem to mean that *pigul* occurring at the time of *shechita* is what is described — the problem precedes the *kabbala*, and the *zrika* is therefore never a valid *matir*, so *me'ilah* persists. The *gemara*, however, now assumes the opposite: it assumes the *braita* is speaking of *pigul* that occurred even after *kabbala*, at the time of *zrika*, and that the *braita* is telling us that even then *me'ilah* applies — which would imply that the critical moment is not the *kabbala* but the actual *zrika*. This would argue against the conclusion that *zrika* means *ra'ui l'zrika*. The *gemara* deflects: the *braita* is not speaking of a case where the *zrika* took place; it speaks of *pigul* that arose before *zrika*, with no *zrika* having occurred. And if one then asks what happens when a *zrika* did occur under these conditions, the *gemara* replies that the *braita* must be providing the prior case, and the later case would need to be inferred separately.

## In vs. Out of Me'ilah

### Why Kodshim Kalim

The *gemara* raises a pointed question. The *braita* states: *pigul* in *kodshei kodashim* — there is *me'ilah*; *pigul* in *kodshim kalim* — there is no *me'ilah*. The *braita* is evidently seeking to illustrate a case in which there is no *me'ilah*. But if, as the *gemara* has been arguing, the key moment is *ra'ui l'zrika*, then there exists a case even within *kodshei kodashim* where there would be no *me'ilah* — namely, where *pigul* arose after *kabbala*. Why, then, did the *braita* run to the case of *kodshim kalim* to furnish an example of no *me'ilah*? It could simply have distinguished within *kodshei kodashim* itself: if the problem arose before *zrika* there is *me'ilah*, and if after *zrika* there is not.

### Two Standards

The *gemara* answers that the *braita* deliberately chose the *kodshim kalim* case because it wishes to convey an additional principle — a distinction in standards depending on the direction of the status change. The principle is stated as follows:

*Kol ha-asui lidei me'ilah* — for something that is meant to be brought into the *me'ilah* category — *zrika ke-tiknah mavia lidei me'ilah*: a fully proper *zrika* is required to effect that transition. *Kol ha-apokei midei me'ilah* — for something that is meant to be removed from the *me'ilah* category — *afilu she-lo ke-tiknah nami mapik midei me'ilah*: even an imperfect *zrika* can effect that removal.

There are two ways to understand the underlying logic of this distinction. One approach is that *me'ilah* designates the status of *kodshei Hashem* — belonging to God — which is a tenuous and rarefied category. Because it is such a narrow category, any ambiguity in status means the item is no longer clearly within it. A questionable *zrika* — whether *zrikas pigul*, or merely *ra'ui l'zrika* — is sufficient to remove an item from that category, since doubt dissolves the certainty required to remain in it. To bring something *into* that category, however, requires certainty; the item must be unambiguously designated as *kodshei Hashem*. The other approach frames the question in terms of *chumra* and *kula*: when we wish to be *machmir* — to prohibit — any doubt suffices; but when we seek to be *meikel* — to permit — a clear, unambiguous act is required.

The *braita* therefore goes to *kodshim kalim* not merely for a case of no *me'ilah*, but to underscore this principle: in *kodshim kalim*, where the *emurim* are seeking to enter the *me'ilah* category, any imperfection in the *zrika* — any questionable element — will prevent that transition entirely. The *braita* thus carries a dual message: *kodshei kodashim* with *pigul* retains *me'ilah* because the imperfect *zrika* suffices to keep it from falling out; *kodshim kalim* with *pigul* never acquires *me'ilah* because the imperfect *zrika* does not meet the threshold needed to bring it in.

### Kol Ha-Apokei

The *gemara*'s analysis here is a significant conceptual statement about the asymmetry between entering and exiting the *me'ilah* category. Across the discussions of this *amud* — *zrikas pigul*, *ra'ui l'zrika*, and now this general principle — the *gemara* is consistently examining whether various categories of imperfect or quasi-valid *zrika* are sufficient to effect a status change. The answer, articulated here, is that the standard differs by direction: a lesser *zrika* may suffice to remove something from *me'ilah*, while a fully valid *zrika* is required to bring something into it.

With these observations, the first *amud* concludes. The essential conclusions are: (1) *zrika* is the critical moment, though there is sustained discussion about whether *ra'ui l'zrika* through *kabbala* may serve the same function; (2) *zrikas pigul* does not constitute a valid *zrika* for purposes of changing status; and (3) the standard for what counts as a sufficient *zrika* may depend on whether the question is one of entering or exiting the *me'ilah* category.

## Psul Yotzei: Kodshei

### Mishna: Yotzei

The *gemara* now turns to the *mishna* on 6b, which introduces a new category of *psul*: *psul yotzei*, the disqualification arising when sacrificial meat or *emurim* are removed from the permitted area of the *Beis Hamikdash*. The case discussed is *basar kodshei kodashim she-yatza lifnei zrikat damim* — meat of *kodshei kodashim* that was taken outside the permitted bounds before the blood was sprinkled. The dispute is between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva.

### R. Eliezer: No Change

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *mo'alin bo* — *me'ilah* applies to this meat. Even if a valid *zrika* was subsequently performed for the rest of the *korban*, the meat that went out was already *pasul*. It was never *ra'ui l'achilah* — fit for consumption — and therefore the *zrika* does not change its status. The meat remains in the *me'ilah* category, and moreover, since the meat is already *pasul*, there is no liability of *kares* for the other *pesulim* that might otherwise apply: *ein chayavin alav mishum pigul, notar, v'tamei*. The liability for *pigul*, *nosar*, and *tum'ah* applies only to meat that would otherwise have been *kasher*; this meat was already disqualified, so those categories do not obtain. One who derives benefit from it is liable for *me'ilah* and for eating *psulei mukdashim*, but not for *pigul*, *nosar*, or *tum'ah*.

### R. Akiva: Status Changes

Rabbi Akiva rules: *ein mo'alin bo* — *me'ilah* does not apply. Although the meat has the *psul* of *yotzei*, the *zrika* was nonetheless effective to change its status. The meat is technically not *mutar* for consumption — the *psul yotzei* remains — but the *zrika* creates a virtual permissibility: it is as if the meat were *ra'ui l'achilah*. Because of that virtual status, *me'ilah* does not apply. Conversely, because the meat is treated as though it were otherwise fit, the liabilities of *pigul*, *nosar*, and *tum'ah* do apply: *chayavin alav mishum pigul, nosar, v'tamei*.

Rabbi Akiva offers a proof from a different case. If one sets aside a *chatas*, loses it, and then sets aside a second animal as a replacement, only to find the first animal thereafter — both animals stand before him. Only one can be brought; the other is in the status of *chatas she-kipru ba'aleha b'acheres*, and Rashi understands the case as one where both were slaughtered and their blood caught before the decision as to which would be offered was finalized. Rabbi Akiva argues: *lo k'shem she-dama poter es besara kach hu poter es besar chavertah* — just as the blood of the one sprinkled renders its own meat released from *me'ilah*, so too does it release the meat of its counterpart. His argument is that since at the moment of *kabbala* either animal could have been chosen, they are conceptually unified, and the *zrikas ha-dam* of one therefore operates on the other. He does not go so far as to say the meat of the second animal is *mutar l'achilah* — he says only *poter*, releases, not *matir*. His claim is the narrower one: conceptually they constitute a single *zevach*, and therefore the *zrika* of one removes the other's meat from the *me'ilah* category.

If that is accepted, he argues *kal va-chomer*: if the blood of one *chatas* can release the meat of a different *chatas* from *me'ilah*, surely the blood of a *korban* sprinkled in proper fashion can release its own meat from *me'ilah*, notwithstanding the *psul yotzei* that attaches to that meat. The logic is that *yotzei* is a lesser *psul* — a matter of physical distance rather than intrinsic invalidity — and should not be treated as a more formidable barrier than the separation between two distinct animals. The scope of Rabbi Akiva's leniency is circumscribed, however, to *yotzei*; he does not extend this reasoning to other *pesulim* such as *tum'ah*, presumably because *yotzei* is a less severe disqualification.

## Psul Yotzei: Kodshim

### Parallel Case

The *mishna* then presents the parallel case with *kodshim kalim*: *eimurai kodshim kalim she-yatzu lifnei zrikat ha-dam* — the *emurim* of a *shelamim* that were removed outside the permitted area before the blood was sprinkled. For *kodshim kalim*, recall, a valid *zrika* would normally bring the *emurim* into the *me'ilah* category, since they are designated for the *mizbe'ach*. The question is whether a *zrika* performed after *yotzei* has occurred accomplishes that transition.

### Same Machloket

Rabbi Eliezer rules: *ein mo'alin bahen* — *me'ilah* does not apply. These *emurim* are *pasul*; the *zrika* was therefore ineffective in bringing them into the *me'ilah* category. Correspondingly, *ein chayavin aleihem mishum pigul, nosar, v'tamei* — since the *emurim* are not *ra'ui* for the *mizbe'ach*, the other *pesulim* do not apply. Rabbi Akiva rules: *mo'alin bahen* — *me'ilah* does apply. The *zrika* is effective even with *psul yotzei*, and the *emurim* are treated as if they were *ra'ui* for the *mizbe'ach*. Accordingly, *chayavin aleihem mishum pigul, nosar, v'tamei* as well.

The fundamental *machloket* is thus the same in both cases: Rabbi Eliezer holds that *psul yotzei* is like any other *psul* and prevents the *zrika* from being effective, while Rabbi Akiva holds that *psul yotzei* is sui generis and the *zrika* effects the status change regardless.

### Why Both Cases

The *gemara* asks: *ve-hani tartei lama li* — why were both cases necessary? The debate between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Akiva is substantively identical in both. The *gemara* answers: *tzricha* — both are needed. Had the dispute been stated only for *kodshei kodashim*, one might have understood Rabbi Eliezer's position as follows: he requires a fully valid *zrika* to remove something from *me'ilah* — *zrika ke-tiknah mapka midei me'ilah, she-lo ke-tiknah lo mapka midei me'ilah* — because removal from *me'ilah* requires an unambiguously effective *zrika*. But one might then have thought that in the opposite direction — bringing something *into* the *me'ilah* category — Rabbi Eliezer would agree with Rabbi Akiva. After all, it is always easier to impose a stringency; if the standard for imposing *me'ilah* is lower, then even with the deficiency of *yotzei*, the *emurim* might enter the *me'ilah* category. Stating the dispute in *kodshim kalim* teaches that Rabbi Eliezer maintains his position even there: even when all that is at stake is bringing the *emurim* into *me'ilah*, he requires a fully valid *zrika*, and a *zrika* performed when *yotzei* has occurred is insufficient.

Conversely, had the dispute been stated only for *kodshim kalim*, one might have understood Rabbi Akiva's leniency differently. There, Rabbi Akiva says *me'ilah* applies — that is, he is being *machmir*. One might have thought he is lenient in the sense that even an imperfect *zrika* can impose *me'ilah*, since imposing *me'ilah* is a stringency. But one might then have supposed that when it comes to *kodshei kodashim* — where applying Rabbi Akiva's ruling means saying *me'ilah does not apply*, a leniency — Rabbi Akiva would concede to Rabbi Eliezer. Stating the dispute in *kodshei kodashim* teaches that Rabbi Akiva holds his position in both directions: he is consistently of the view that *psul yotzei* does not prevent *zrika* from effecting the status change, regardless of which direction the change runs. Both cases are therefore necessary to establish the full scope of the *machloket*.

## Miktzato vs. Kulo

### R. Yochanan Clarifies

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Yochanan's qualification of Rabbi Akiva's position. Rabbi Yochanan states: *ad kan amar Rabbi Akiva zrika mo'elet l'yotzei* — Rabbi Akiva only says that *zrika* is effective for *yotzei* in a particular circumstance: *she-yatza miktzato* — when only part of the meat (or *emurim*) went outside the permitted area, while the rest remained within.

### Miktzato vs. Kulo

The parallel to the *chatas* proof now becomes clearer. When Rabbi Akiva argued from the case of two *chatos* — that the blood of one released the meat of the other — the conceptual basis was that they could be seen as unified, since both were present in the *azarah* simultaneously and either could have been chosen. Similarly, when only *part* of the *korban*'s meat or *emurim* went out, the *zrika* that worked for the portion that remained can be said to work for the portion that went out, on the theory that they are all part of one *korban*. But *aval yatza kulo* — if the entirety of the meat or *emurim* went outside before the *zrika* — Rabbi Akiva would not apply his ruling. There, Rabbi Yochanan clarifies, Rabbi Akiva would agree with Rabbi Eliezer that the *zrika* is not effective. When nothing remained within the permitted area, there is no basis for treating the *zrika* as having any purchase on those portions.

## Pigul on Avud/Saruf

### Avud v'Saruf

Following Rabbi Yochanan's qualification, Rav Assi addresses him and reports a teaching from his colleagues in the Diaspora — *chavray she-ba-golah*: *m'chashvin al ha-avud v'al ha-saruf* — one can form the requisite *machsheves pigul* with respect to objects that have been lost or burned. The case is this: during the performance of the sacrificial service, one forms the intent to consume the meat or burn the *emurim* beyond the permitted time — the classic *machsheves pigul*. The novelty is that at the moment this intent is formed, the meat or *emurim* in question have already been destroyed — they no longer exist. One might have thought that such intent is meaningless, since it is directed at an object that is no longer present. Yet the ruling is that *m'chashvin* — such intent does constitute *pigul*, and the *korban* is disqualified. The reason is that *pigul* does not operate narrowly on the specific object contemplated; it disqualifies the entire *korban*. As long as other portions of the *korban* remain, the invalid intent — even when directed at something no longer extant — is sufficient to render the whole *korban pigul*: *v'ha-avud v'ha-saruf lo hayu ba-olam, v'afilu hachi tani m'chashvin*.

### Implication Teased

Rav Assi is raising this teaching in order to press on Rabbi Yochanan's qualification of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yochanan had limited Rabbi Akiva's ruling to *miktzato* — where only part went out — implying that something must remain within the permitted bounds for the *zrika* to be effective for the portion that left. But the ruling about *avud v'saruf* suggests that even when the object in question has been entirely destroyed — not merely removed but gone altogether — certain *halachic* mechanisms can still operate in relation to it, so long as something else in the *korban* remains. Rav Assi is teasing out whether the same logic might extend Rabbi Akiva's position beyond the *miktzato* limitation, perhaps even to *kulo*. The discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
