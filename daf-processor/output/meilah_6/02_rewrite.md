# Meilah 6 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Zrika vs. Kabbala

### Me'ilah by Kodashim

The topic under discussion concerns how the *me'ilah* status of a *korban* changes once its blood is offered. *Kodashei kodashim* are entirely subject to *me'ilah* before any service, since the offering is wholly consecrated. Once the blood is sprinkled on the *mizbe'ach*—in the case of a *chatat* or *asham*—the meat becomes permissible for the *kohanim*, and consequently the meat is no longer subject to *me'ilah*, though *me'ilah* continues to apply to the remainder of the *korban*. The rule is thus: *zrikat hadam* removes the meat of *kodashei kodashim* from the *me'ilah* category.

The dynamic operates in reverse for *kodashim kalim*. There, the offering is fundamentally considered *mamon ba'alim*—it has not been fully surrendered to God—and is destined to be eaten by the owners. Only the portion that goes on the *mizbe'ach*, the *emurim*, becomes subject to *me'ilah*, and only after *zrikat hadam* renders it fit for the altar. So *zrikat hadam* has a dual function: for *kodashei kodashim* it removes the meat from *me'ilah*, and for *kodashim kalim* it brings the *emurim* into *me'ilah*.

### Critical Moment

The *gemara* has been probing a more precise question: is it the moment of *zrika* itself that is critical, or does an earlier moment—*kabbala*, the receiving of the blood—already effect the status change? Once the blood is received it is fit for *zrika*, and the *gemara* refers to this as *heit lizrika*, the fitness for *zrika*. The actual act of *zrika* is called *hetter achila*, the moment the meat becomes genuinely permitted. The tension throughout this sugya is whether *heit lizrika*, achieved through *kabbala*, is the operative moment, or whether one must wait for the *zrika* itself.

## Notar Cases

### Notar Distinction

A *baraita* is cited: Rabbi Shimon teaches that with regard to *notar*—meat left over beyond its permitted time—there are cases where *me'ilah* applies and cases where it does not. This immediately raises the question of how a single category, *notar* of *kodashei kodashim*, can produce two different outcomes with respect to *me'ilah*.

### Before/After Zrika

The *baraita* continues: *keitzad*—how so? If the meat lodged overnight (*lan*) before *zrika*, then *moalin*, there is *me'ilah*. If it lodged overnight after *zrika*, then *ein moalin*, there is no *me'ilah*. The plain reading is straightforward: did the disqualifying event—*lina*—occur before or after the blood was sprinkled? If *zrika* had already been performed, the meat had already passed through the moment of *hetter achila* and is no longer subject to *me'ilah*. If *lina* preceded *zrika*, the meat was never rendered permitted, and *me'ilah* remains.

### Heit Lizrika

The *gemara* complicates this reading. It suggests that the *baraita*'s case of "before *zrika*" refers specifically to a situation where the blood was received (*kabbala*) close to *shkiyat hachama*—sunset—such that by the time *kabbala* was complete there was no remaining opportunity to perform *zrika*. The blood was thus never fit for *zrika* from the moment it was caught. On this reading, the reason *me'ilah* applies is not simply that *zrika* had not yet occurred, but that *zrika* was never even possible—the blood was never *heit lizrika*.

### Why Zrika?

This reinterpretation raises a pointed difficulty. If the *gemara* is correct that the operative distinction is whether the blood was ever fit for *zrika* rather than whether *zrika* actually took place, then why does the *baraita* frame the dividing line as "before *zrika*" versus "after *zrika*"? It ought instead to have said: before *shkiya* versus after *shkiya*, since the real question is whether *kabbala* occurred while there was still time to perform *zrika*.

### Reinterpreting Zrika

The *gemara* resolves this by reading the *baraita*'s language as a compressed formulation. When Rabbi Shimon says "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," he does not mean the literal act of *zrika*; he means *kodem she're'uyah l'zrika* and *le'achar she're'uyah l'zrika*—before the blood was fit for *zrika* and after the blood was fit for *zrika*. Fitness for *zrika* is achieved at the moment of *kabbala*. The word *zrika* in the *baraita* is thus shorthand for the moment when *zrika* became possible. The entire analysis is admittedly *dachuk*—strained—but it is the *gemara*'s own resolution, and it converts both *baraita* cases into illustrations of the principle that *heit lizrika*, not *zrika* per se, is the critical moment.

## Pigul Cases

### Pigul Distinction

A second *baraita* is cited, this time applying the same framework to *pigul*—the disqualification arising from an improper intention during the sacrificial service. Rabbi Shimon again teaches: there is *pigul* with *me'ilah*, and there is *pigul* without *me'ilah*. *Keitzad*—how so? Before *zrika*, *moalin*. After *zrika*, *ein moalin*. On the surface this appears to be the same structure as the *notar* case.

### Pigul Timing Problem

A serious difficulty immediately presents itself. *Pigul* can only be generated at the four *avodos*: *shechita*, *kabbala*, *holacha*, and *zrika*. By definition, then, *pigul* cannot occur after *zrika*. The plain reading—"did the disqualification arise before or after *zrika*?"—cannot work here, because there is no mechanism by which *pigul* can arise after *zrika*.

### Rashi's Interpretation

Rashi resolves this by shifting the referent of "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*" away from when the *pigul* thought occurred and toward when the person was *neheneh*—when he derived benefit from or ate the meat. On Rashi's reading: if one was *neheneh* from the meat before *zrika*, there is *me'ilah*; if one was *neheneh* after *zrika*—even after a *zrikat pigul*—there is no *me'ilah*. This implies that *zrikat pigul* counts as a *zrika* sufficient to change the *me'ilah* status, and thus directly contradicts the position of Rav Giddel citing Rav, who held that *zrikat pigul* is not effective for this purpose. Rashi reads the *baraita* this way because the alternative—having *pigul* occur after *zrika*—is technically impossible.

### Pigul and Zrika

An alternative reading, closer to the plain sense of the *baraita*, takes "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*" as referring to when the *pigul* thought occurred relative to *zrika*, with the understanding that *le'achar zrika* is *lav davka*—not precise—and in practice means *bish'at zrika*, during *zrika*. On this reading: if the *pigul* thought arose before *zrika*—at *shechita* or *kabbala*—then the *zrika* was never *matir* the meat, since a *zrikat pigul* is still, according to some, considered as having occurred. If the *pigul* arose at *zrika* itself, one can dispute whether *zrikat pigul* counts as *zrika* and thereby serves as *matir*. Either way the *baraita* engages the question of Rav Giddel's ruling.

Tosafot notes an independent complication: if *pigul* at the time of *holacha* applies according to Rabbi Shimon, then there is a window—after *kabbala* and before *zrika*—where *pigul* could be generated. This would supply a genuine case of *pigul* arising after *kabbala* but before *zrika*. Yet Tosafot also observes that Rabbi Shimon himself holds *pigul* at *holacha* is not effective, since one can perform the service without *holacha*. Perhaps the case involves a *pigul* thought at the very conclusion of *kabbala*. The *gemara* on its surface treats this as parallel to the *notar* case, but the complications are substantial.

### Challenge to Kabbala

The *gemara* now attempts to use the *baraita* to resolve the underlying dispute about whether *kabbala* or *zrika* is the critical moment. The *baraita* says: if *pigul* preceded *zrika*, *moalin*—even in a case where *kabbala* was performed properly and at a time when *zrika* was entirely possible. The *gemara* suggests: does this not demonstrate that even with a valid *kabbala*—one where there was ample opportunity to perform *zrika*—the status does not change until *zrika* itself? This would confirm that *hetter achila*, not *heit lizrika*, is the operative standard.

The *gemara* proceeds to test this against an earlier *baraita* that stated: *pigul bekodshei kodashim moalin*. The *gemara* asks: surely this refers to a case where *pigul* arose even after *kabbala*, and yet *me'ilah* still applies, demonstrating that *kabbala* is not the critical moment. The *gemara* responds: no, the *baraita* may be speaking specifically of a case where *zrika* never occurred at all—where there was no *kabbala* at the right time to make the blood *heit lizrika*.

### Reinterpreted Timing

As with the *notar* case, the *gemara* resolves by re-reading. When the *baraita* says "before *zrika*" and "after *zrika*," it means *kodem she're'uyah l'zrika* and *le'achar she're'uyah l'zrika*—before and after the blood became fit for *zrika*. Fitness for *zrika* is achieved at *kabbala*. The *baraita* thus teaches: if the problem arose before *kabbala*, the status never changed; if after *kabbala*, it had already changed. *Zrika* in the *baraita* is again shorthand for *heit lizrika*.

## Into vs. Out of Me'ilah

### Why Kodashim Kalim?

The *gemara* now presses a fresh question. The *baraita* under discussion—*pigul bekodshei kodashim moalin, bekodashim kalim ein moalin*—seeks to illustrate a case where there is no *me'ilah*. But according to the conclusion just reached, that *kabbala* is the operative moment, there ought to be cases within *kodashei kodashim* itself where *me'ilah* does not apply: namely, whenever the *pigul* arose after *kabbala* was performed with a proper opportunity for *zrika*. Why then does the *baraita* need to travel all the way to *kodashim kalim* to find an example of *ein moalin*? It could simply have drawn a distinction within *kodashei kodashim* between *pigul* before *zrika* and *pigul* after *zrika*—or as we now understand, before and after *heit lizrika*.

### Asymmetry Principle

The *gemara* answers that the *baraita* ran to the *kodashim kalim* case in order to teach an additional principle that could not have been derived otherwise. The key formulation is: *kol ha'asuy lidei me'ilah, zrika ketiknah mayti lidei me'ilah; kol ha'apukei midei me'ilah, afilu shelo ketiknah nami mapik midei me'ilah*. Translated: anything that is being brought into the *me'ilah* category requires a *zrika* that is in full order; but anything that is being taken out of the *me'ilah* category—even a *zrika* that is not entirely in order suffices to remove it.

The principle rests on an asymmetry between entering and exiting the *me'ilah* category. *Me'ilah* represents the status of *kodshei Hashem*, a highly restrictive and specific category. For the *emurim* of *kodashim kalim* to enter this category, the *zrika* must be completely valid, since what is being asserted is that these items now belong to God in a new and special sense—a claim that requires full legal standing. But for the meat of *kodashei kodashim* to exit *me'ilah*, something less than a perfectly valid *zrika* may suffice: a *zrikat pigul*, or *heit lizrika* without the act of *zrika*, may be enough to remove the item from this heightened category.

### Bidirectional Standards

This yields the conceptual principle that governs the entire sugya: standards for effecting a change in *me'ilah* status may differ depending on the direction of the change. The same *zrika*—or the same quasi-*zrika*—that is insufficient to bring an item into *me'ilah* may nonetheless be sufficient to take an item out of it. This is not simply a matter of being *machmir* or *meikel* in the abstract; it reflects a structural asymmetry in what it means to be in or out of the *me'ilah* category.

The *gemara* will revisit this principle on the next *amud*, and the analysis there will parallel and, in some respects, invert the logic applied here. What emerges from the first *amud*, then, is the following: *zrika* is the operative moment of status change, but three categories of "questionable *zrika*" have been introduced—*zrikat pigul*, *heit lizrika* (without actual *zrika*), and *zrika* in the presence of a *psul yotzei*—and in each case the question of whether such a *zrika* is effective may depend on whether one is seeking to enter or exit the *me'ilah* category.

## Yotzei Debate

### Yotzei in Kalim

The *gemara* transitions to a parallel case in *kodashim kalim*: the *emurim* of a *shelamim* that were taken outside the permitted area before *zrikat hadam*. This is the mirror image of the *kodashei kodashim* case. Here the question is whether a *zrika* performed after the *emurim* have already become *pasul* through *yotzei* is capable of bringing them into the *me'ilah* category.

### Both Cases Needed

As noted above, the *gemara* explains why both the *kodashei kodashim* case and the *kodashim kalim* case were necessary. Had only the *kodashei kodashim* case been stated, one might have argued that Rabbi Eliezer's stringent position—*zrika* must be fully valid to effect any change—applies only when trying to remove *me'ilah*, where leniency must overcome a presumption of stringency. Conversely, had only the *kodashim kalim* case been stated, one might have concluded that Rabbi Akiva's lenient position applies only when introducing *me'ilah*—since making something more *asur* can be accomplished even with a *zrika* that is not entirely in order. By presenting both cases, the *mishnah* teaches that each authority maintains his position regardless of the direction of the change.

### Yotzei in Kodashim

The *mishnah* states: *basar kodshei kodashim sheyatza lifnei zrikat damim*—meat of *kodashei kodashim* that was taken outside its permitted area before *zrikat hadam*. The *psul* of *yotzei* has attached to this meat. Rabbi Eliezer says: *moalin bo*—*me'ilah* applies. Rabbi Akiva says: *ein moalin bo*—*me'ilah* does not apply.

### Eliezer's View

Rabbi Eliezer holds that *yotzei* is a disqualification like any other. The meat, having been taken outside, is *pasul* and was never rendered *mutar le'achila*. The subsequent *zrika*—performed on behalf of the rest of the *korban*—does not change the status of this meat. Since it never passed through *hetter achila*, *me'ilah* remains in force. Furthermore, Rabbi Eliezer rules that such meat does not incur liability for *pigul*, *notar*, or *tumah*, since those prohibitions apply only to meat that would otherwise have been permitted. The meat here was already *pasul*; one who eats it is liable for *me'ilah* and for eating *psulei hamukdashin*, but not for the other *chayyavim*.

### Two Chatasim Case

Rabbi Akiva supports his contrary position with an argument from an analogous case. One who sets aside a *chatat*, loses it, sets aside a replacement, and then recovers the original is left with two *chatasim* before him, only one of which may be brought. At the moment *zrika* is performed for one of them, according to Rabbi Akiva, the blood effects a *petira* not only for the meat of that *chatat* but also for the meat of the other: *lo keshim shedama poter es besara kach hu poter es besar chavertah*—just as its blood exempts its own meat from *me'ilah*, so too does it exempt the meat of its counterpart. Rashi explains the case as one where both *chatasim* were brought into the *Beis Hamikdash*, both were *shechted*, and both had their blood received, such that at the moment of decision there remained a genuine choice as to which blood would be sprinkled—meaning both were conceptually one composite *korban* at that moment.

### Akiva's View

From this case Rabbi Akiva draws his inference: *im potar damah es besar chavertah min hame'ilah*, if the blood of one *chatat* can remove the meat of another from *me'ilah* because they are considered conceptually unified, *kol sheken* that blood should remove from *me'ilah* the meat of its own *korban*, even if that meat carries the *psul* of *yotzei*. The meat remains *pasul* and may not be eaten, but the *me'ilah* status is lifted. Rabbi Akiva further rules that such meat does incur liability for *pigul*, *notar*, and *tamei*—a consequence of treating it as though it had been rendered *ra'uy le'achila* by the *zrika*, even though its *psul yotzei* prevents actual consumption.

The question of why *yotzei* should be singled out as the *psul* light enough to be overridden by *zrika*—while other *psulin* such as *tumah* are not—is not fully resolved. The implicit reasoning appears to be that *yotzei* is a matter of spatial distance rather than an intrinsic ritual defect, and that within the conceptual framework of the *korban* as a unified entity, what has gone outside retains a certain connection to what remains within. But the parallel to *tumah* is at the very least a challenge to the elegance of this reasoning.

---

## Yochanan's Limitation

### Partial vs. Total Yotzei

Rabbi Yochanan introduces a qualification to Rabbi Akiva's position. When Rabbi Akiva said that *zrika* is effective for *yotzei*, he meant only the case of *sheyatza miktzaso*—where some of the meat went outside but some remained within the permitted area. In this circumstance the analogy to the two *chatasim* works cleanly: the blood was effective for the meat that remained inside, and by extension—since the *korban* is a unified entity—it is also effective for the meat that exited.

### Complete Removal

If, however, all of the meat went outside—*yatza kulo*—Rabbi Yochanan rules that Rabbi Akiva would not apply his leniency. There is no remaining meat inside for which the *zrika* can first be said to operate, and without that anchor the extension to the *yotzei* meat loses its basis. In such a case, even Rabbi Akiva would agree with Rabbi Eliezer that the *zrika* does not change the status of the meat, and *me'ilah* continues to apply.

## Avud and Saruph

### Pigul on Nothing

Rav Assi reports a ruling in the name of his colleagues from the *golah*—Babylonia: *mechashvin al ha'avud v'al hasaruph*—a *machshevet pigul* can attach to meat or *emurim* that have been lost (*avud*) or burned (*saruph*). The *pigul* thought is not directed at an object that actually exists at the moment of the thought. And yet, the teaching from Babylon is that such a thought is nonetheless legally significant.

### Efficacy of Thought

The observation that follows drives the point home: *ha'avud vehasaruph lo iseinu ba'olam*—what is lost or burned does not exist in the world at all—*v'afilu hachi tani mechashvin*—and yet the tradition teaches that one can have an effective *pigul* thought about it. The import for the broader sugya will emerge in the continuation: the principle that even a non-existent object can anchor a *machshava* has implications for how we think about the necessity of a present, viable object when evaluating whether a *pigul* thought or an analogous legal act is effective. This discussion continues onto the following *daf*.
