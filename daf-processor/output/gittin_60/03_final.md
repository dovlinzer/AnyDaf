# Gittin 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aliyot Order & Peace

### Kohen-Levi-Yisrael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה, דְּאָמַר קְרָא: ״וַיִּכְתּוֹב מֹשֶׁה אֶת הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת וַיִּתְּנָהּ אֶל הַכּוֹהֲנִים בְּנֵי לֵוִי״ – אַטּוּ אֲנָא לָא יָדַעְנָא דְּכֹהֲנִים, בְּנֵי לֵוִי נִינְהוּ? אֶלָּא כֹּהֵן בְּרֵישָׁא, וַהֲדַר לֵוִי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that at public readings of the Torah, a priest reads first, and after him a Levite. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? What is the source of this *halakha* in the Torah? **Rav Mattana said: As the verse states: "And Moses wrote this Torah, and delivered it to the priests, the sons of Levi"** (Deuteronomy 31:9). The Gemara explains the inference: **Is that to say I do not know that the priests are the sons of Levi?** Why is it necessary for the verse to say this? **Rather,** the Torah was first delivered to the priests and afterward to the other Levites, and this serves as the source for the enactment that first **a priest** reads from the Torah, **and after** him **a Levite.**

The daf opens near the end of the *perek* dealing with *tikkun olam* and *darchei shalom*, before the tractate returns to the laws of *gittin* proper. The sugya at the bottom of 59b concerns the established order of *aliyot* — *kohen*, *levi*, *yisrael* — and the principle that this sequence exists as a function of *darchei shalom*, so that competition over communal honors not become a source of conflict. The Gemara acknowledges that the obligation to honor a *kohen* first derives from *de-Oraita* authority — the concept of *vekiddashto* — and that technically a *kohen* may be *mochel*, may waive his honor in favor of another. The Rabbis, however, instituted that he should not do so, since permitting such waivers opens the door to disputes.

### Shabbat vs Weekday

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת לֹא, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיָמִים טוֹבִים – דִּשְׁכִיחִי רַבִּים, אֲבָל בְּשֵׁנִי וּבַחֲמִישִׁי – לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mattana said:** With regard to **this** matter **that you stated,** that **in the synagogue** a priest is **not** permitted to honor an Israelite and allow him to read first, **we said** this **only concerning *Shabbatot* and Festivals, when many people are present** for the services, **but not on Mondays and Thursdays,** when only a small number of people are there. On those days it is permitted for one to honor his superior, and there is no concern that this will lead to a quarrel.

Rav Mattana introduces a qualification. The prohibition against a *kohen* ceding his *aliyah* to another applies only on *shabbatot* and *yamim tovim* — *deshehichi rabbim*, when the congregation is large and the potential for conflict correspondingly greater. On Tuesdays and Thursdays, however, where the minyan is typically small and intimate, the concern is less acute, and a *kohen* may be *mochel*. An important note is appended by Tosafot in the name of Rabbeinu Chananel: this distinction applied in Talmudic times when weekday attendance was sparse due to people being occupied with their work. Tosafot observes that in their own era, Tuesday and Thursday attendance could rival that of *Yom Tov*, and the leniency would not apply in such circumstances.

### Rav Huna Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רַב הוּנָא קָרֵי בְּכָהֲנֵי בְּשַׁבָּתוֹת וְיוֹם טוֹב! שָׁאנֵי רַב הוּנָא, דַּאֲפִילּוּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – כָּהֲנֵי חֲשִׁיבִי דְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – מֵיכָף הֲווֹ כַּיְיפִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so?** Is it actually prohibited for a priest to honor his teacher and allow him to read first in his place? **But didn't Rav Huna,** who was not a priest, **read** the Torah section ordinarily reserved **for priests,** even **on *Shabbatot* and Festivals?** The Gemara answers: **Rav Huna is different, as even Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi, the most important priests in Eretz Yisrael, were subject to his** jurisdiction. Therefore, there was no concern about a quarrel, because everyone agreed that he was the leading authority of the generation and it was fitting that he should read from the Torah first.

The Gemara raises an objection: we know that Rav Huna, who was not a *kohen*, received the *kohen* *aliyah* on *shabbatot* and *yamim tovim*. This appears to contradict Rav Mattana's rule. The Gemara resolves the difficulty: Rav Huna was categorically different. Even Rav Ami and Rav Assi — the most distinguished *kohanim* in *Eretz Yisrael* — deferred to him. His preeminence was so universally acknowledged that calling him first was not an act of waiver at all; it was simply the objective recognition of his uncontested authority. The *kohen*'s honor was not so much conceded as it was superseded by an undisputed communal consensus.

## No Kohen Present

### Bundle Unravels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, נָקְטִינַן: אֵין שָׁם כֹּהֵן – נִתְפָּרְדָה חֲבִילָה. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי, נָקְטִינַן: אֵין שָׁם לֵוִי – קוֹרֵא כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that **we have a tradition** that if **there is no priest there** in the synagogue at the time of the Torah reading, **the bundle is separated,** i.e., a Levite is not shown precedence over Israelites. **And Abaye said** that **we have a tradition** that if **there is no Levite there** in the synagogue, **a priest reads** in his place.

Abaye introduces the following tradition: *ein sham kohen, nisperdah hachavilah* — if no *kohen* is present, the bundle unravels. The entire edifice of hierarchical ordering rests on the premise that the *kohen* receives the first honor. Without a *kohen*, the structure loses its foundation, and the order collapses entirely. One might ask why, in such a case, one would not simply proceed to *levi* before *yisrael*. The answer lies in the nature of *vekiddashto*: the *mitzvah* of sanctifying and honoring applies fundamentally to the *kohen*. The *levi*, though assigned greater communal responsibility and role, does not carry the same *kedusha* in this regard. Without the *kohen*, there is no longer a basis for the hierarchy, and in principle any *aliyah* may be given to anyone.

---

## No Kohen Present…

### Rashi vs Rav Yitzchak

Rashi's first explanation of *nisperdah hachavilah* is striking: the *levi* loses his *aliyah* entirely — *eino korei kelal*. This is the position of *Mori ha-Zaken* and Rabbeinu Yitzchak ben Yehudah, and it accords with the *Siddur Rav Amram*. Rashi then cites a second view, from Rav Yitzchak ha-Levi, that there is simply no fixed order anymore — a *levi* may receive an *aliyah* somewhere in the middle, as may anyone else, but there is no reason to deprive the *levi* of an *aliyah* altogether.

### Tosafot Position

Tosafot takes a third position: Rav Yitzchak bar Yehudah maintains that the *levi* receives the second *aliyah*. That is, a *yisrael* is given the first *aliyah* — in the slot normally reserved for *kohen* — and the *levi* then reads second, in his proper position within the remaining order. The concern is that placing a *levi* in the first slot too closely mimics the role of *kohen*, creating a blurring of boundaries that might itself carry the character of *pegam kehunah*. A *yisrael* in the first slot signals more clearly that no status claim is being made. The practical custom — giving the remaining *aliyot* freely to *yisraelim* — reflects the *peshat* of the Gemara, though the precise practice when a *levi* is present varies, and the issue remains genuinely contested.

## Aliyot Order & Peace…

### Levi Without Kohen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן, בְּאוֹתוֹ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** When we said that when there is no Levite present a priest reads in his place, we were speaking **of the same priest** who had already read from the Torah, for in that case there is no concern that people will think that a flaw had been found in his status.

To summarize the three positions: first, the *levi* receives no *aliyah* at all; second, the *levi* may receive any *aliyah* in any position, with no fixed order applying to anyone; and third, the *levi* receives the second *aliyah* specifically. The simple reading of the Gemara supports the second view — once the bundle has unraveled, *aliyot* are given without predetermined order. A related tradition from Abaye: *ein sham levi, kari kohen* — if there is no *levi*, the same *kohen* who received the first *aliyah* reads again in the second position, since a *kohen* is, by lineage, also a *levi*. Whether this constitutes a demotion in any sense is something the Gemara proceeds to examine.

## Kohen/Levi Stigma

### Double Status Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כֹּהֵן אַחַר כֹּהֵן לֹא יִקְרָא, מִשּׁוּם פְּגָמוֹ שֶׁל רִאשׁוֹן. לֵוִי אַחַר לֵוִי לֹא יִקְרָא מִשּׁוּם פְּגַם שְׁנֵיהֶם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rabbi Yoḥanan say:** One **priest should not read after** another **priest, because** people might mistakenly think that the second priest was called to read due to **a flaw** that was found **in** the status of **the first** one, i.e., that he was found not to be a priest. And one **Levite should not read after** another **Levite, because** people might mistakenly think that there is **a flaw in both of them.**

Rav Yochanan rules: *kohen achar kohen lo yikra* — one may not call up a second *kohen* immediately after a first *kohen* has already received an *aliyah*, because this will lead to *pegam* on the first: observers will assume that after the first was called, it was discovered that he was not truly a *kohen*, and a second genuine *kohen* had to be substituted. Similarly, *levi achar levi lo yikra* — a second *levi* may not be called after a first *levi* — but here the concern is *mishum pagmam shel sheneihem*, a stigma that falls on both of them, not merely on the first.

### Same Person Twice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמְרִינַן, בְּאוֹתוֹ כֹּהֵן.
>
> **Translation:** When we said that when there is no Levite present a priest reads in his place, we were speaking **of the same priest** who had already read from the Torah, for in that case there is no concern that people will think that a flaw had been found in his status.

This immediately raises the question posed above: if there is no *levi*, the Gemara instructs that the same *kohen* reads again — but does this not create the precise problem Rav Yochanan described? The Gemara resolves it: *ki ka'amrinan be'oto kohen* — when the same *kohen* reads twice, everyone understands the reason. The gabbai announces *ya'amod bimkom levi*, or makes it clear in some equivalent way, and there is no ambiguity. No one imagines that the first *aliyah* was invalid; it is simply understood that no *levi* is present.

### Stories

Two illustrative anecdotes bear on the practical reality of this rule. Hertzel Hefter, himself a *levi*, once studied this passage and was genuinely surprised to learn that the same *kohen* is truly called up twice — having never been in a *shul* when no *levi* was present, he had not encountered the practice. The other story involves Rabbi Weiss, who on a visit to a *Teimani shul* in Israel was given the *kohen aliyah* and discovered — unprepared — that the congregation expected him to read aloud himself. He managed to get through it. The following visit, he prepared the *aliyah* in advance, read it flawlessly, and immediately heard the *gabbai* announce: *ya'amod bimkom levi*.

### Pegam Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא לֵוִי אַחַר לֵוִי, דְּאִיכָּא פְּגַם שְׁנֵיהֶם – דְּאָמְרִי: חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ לָאו לֵוִי הוּא; כֹּהֵן אַחֵר כֹּהֵן נָמֵי, אָמְרִי: חַד מִינַּיְיהוּ לָאו כֹּהֵן הוּא! כְּגוֹן דְּמוּחְזָק לַן בַּאֲבוּהּ דְּהַאי שֵׁנִי, דְּכֹהֵן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a question with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement: **What is different** that in the case where one **Levite** reads from the Torah **after** another **Levite,** Rabbi Yoḥanan says **that there is** concern that people might mistakenly think that there is **a flaw in both of them?** It must be that he is concerned that people might **say** that **one of them,** either the first or the second, **is** certainly **not a Levite.** If so, in the case where one **priest** reads from the Torah **after** another **priest,** he should **also** be concerned that people might **say** that **one of them,** either the first or the second, **is** certainly **not a priest.** Why, then, was Rabbi Yoḥanan concerned only about suspicions that might be raised about the first priest? The Gemara answers: He speaks about a case **where we have a presumption concerning the father of the second** one, **that he is a priest.**

The Gemara then probes why two *leviim* in succession generate a *pegam* on both, while two *kohanim* generate a *pegam* only on the first. The initial answer — that in both cases observers might question either individual's status — does not adequately distinguish the cases. The Gemara refines the analysis with reference to a specific scenario: suppose it is well established that the second *kohen*'s father was genuinely a *kohen*. Can that not also be stipulated for the second *levi*?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמִי, דְּמוּחְזָק לַן בַּאֲבוּהּ דְּהַאי שֵׁנִי, דְּלֵוִי הוּא! אֶלָּא אָמְרִי: מַמְזֶרֶת אוֹ נְתִינָה נְסֵיב, וּפַסְלֵיהּ לְזַרְעֵיהּ. הָכָא נָמִי, אָמְרִי: גְּרוּשָׁה אוֹ חֲלוּצָה נְסֵיב, וְאַחֲלֵיהּ לְזַרְעֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, **here too,** in the case of the Levites let us say that **we have a presumption concerning the father of the second** one, **that he is a Levite. Rather,** the concern here is that even if it is known that he is the son of a Levite, people might **say** that perhaps the father **married a *mamzeret*,** a daughter born from an incestuous or adulterous relationship, **or a Gibeonite woman, and** thereby **disqualified his children,** so that they are considered Israelites rather than Levites. If so, then **here too,** in the case of the priests, there is concern that people might **say** that perhaps the priest's father **married a divorced woman or a *yevama* who underwent *ḥalitza* [*ḥalutza*] and** thereby **disqualified his children** from the priesthood (see Leviticus 21:7).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סוֹף סוֹף, לֵוִי מִי קָא הָוֵי?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Ultimately, is he a Levite?** If the priest is disqualified from the priesthood owing to his blemished lineage, he has the status of an Israelite, not a Levite. Therefore, if he reads from the Torah after another priest, and it is known that his father is a priest, then it must be that he too is a qualified priest. Therefore, the only reason for concern is that people might say that there is a flaw in the status of the first priest.

The Gemara's final answer is structural. If a *kohen* turns out to be a *chalal* — his father having married a *gerusha* and the son thereby losing his *kehunah* — he would receive a *yisrael aliyah*, not a *kohen* one. Therefore, when a second person receives the second *aliyah* after a *kohen*, there is only one available interpretation: the second person is the genuine *kohen* and the first was not. No observer can think the second person is a *chalal*, because a *chalal* would be receiving the *yisrael* slot, not the second position.

With *leviim*, by contrast, the second *aliyah* is also the slot a *yisrael* would receive if the *levi* had been demoted or disqualified. A *levi* who married a *mamzeret* and thereby blemished his descendants' status would produce offspring who might receive a *yisrael aliyah*. So when two people receive what look like consecutive *levi* slots, observers can read it either way: the first person may have been a non-*kohen* substitute later replaced by the real *levi*, or the second person may be a demoted *levi* whose true status is really *yisrael*. The stigma thus branches in two directions and lands on both.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְמַאן? אִי לְיוֹשְׁבִין – הָא קָא חָזוּ לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא לְיוֹצְאִין.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the concern itself, the Gemara asks: **And about whom** is there a concern? Who might mistakenly think that the first priest's status is blemished? **If** you say that the concern is **for those sitting** in the synagogue until the end of the Torah reading, that is not a valid concern, as **they see** that he is counted as one of the seven who must read from the Torah, and therefore he must certainly be a qualified priest. **Rather,** the concern is **for those who leave** before the conclusion of the reading, and do not know that he was counted among the seven readers.

The Gemara further asks: who exactly are these observers whose misinterpretation we fear? Those who remain through the entire *kriat haTorah* would presumably see the full sequence of *aliyot* and understand that the first one counted. The concern therefore applies specifically to those who leave *shul* in the middle, *leyotzim*, who see the second *kohen aliyah* without having witnessed the complete context.

## Higher Hierarchy

### Rabbi Helbo Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי גָלִיל לְרַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: אַחֲרֵיהֶן, מִי קוֹרְאִין? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **The people of the Galilee sent** a question **to Rabbi Ḥelbo: After them,** the priest and the Levite, **who reads** from the Torah? An answer **was not** readily **available to him.**

The people of the Galilee sent a question to Rabbi Helbo: after *kohen* and *levi* have received their *aliyot*, how is the order determined among the remaining recipients? Rabbi Helbo did not have a ready answer and referred the question to Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha.

### Talmidei Chachamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ: אַחֲרֵיהֶן קוֹרְאִין תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הַמְּמוּנִּים פַּרְנָסִים עַל הַצִּבּוּר, וְאַחֲרֵיהֶן תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים הָרְאוּיִים לְמַנּוֹתָם פַּרְנָסִים עַל הַצִּיבּוּר, וְאַחֲרֵיהֶן בְּנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁאֲבוֹתֵיהֶן מְּמוּנִּים פַּרְנָסִים עַל הַצִּבּוּר, וְאַחֲרֵיהֶן רָאשֵׁי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וְכׇל אָדָם.
>
> **Translation:** **said to him: After them read** the **Torah scholars who are appointed as leaders [*parnasim*] of the community. And after them** read **Torah scholars who are fit to be appointed as leaders of the community,** even if in practice they received no such appointment. The Sages said that a Torah scholar who knows how to answer any question asked of him is fit to be appointed as leader of the community. **And after them** read **the sons of Torah scholars whose fathers were appointed as leaders of the community. And after them** read **the heads of synagogues, and** after them **any person.**

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha replied that the post-*levi* *aliyot* go first to *talmidei chachamim* who are actually appointed as *parnasim al hatzibbur* — communal religious leaders. The *parnas* in this context is a figure of genuine Torah authority: elsewhere the Gemara defines one who is fit to be a *parnas* as someone who can answer a halakhic question from any area of Jewish law immediately and without hesitation. This is the model of the city's rabbinic authority, whether or not he formally holds that position.

### Full Hierarchy List

After the appointed *talmidei chachamim* come those who are *ra'uyim lemanotam parnasim* — scholars who are genuinely qualified for rabbinic leadership, but for whom the position is already filled. After them come the children of *talmidei chachamim* whose fathers are appointed as communal leaders. This introduces a notable intersection of two distinct principles: Torah scholarship, which is in theory a pure meritocracy, and inherited status, which mirrors the structure of *kehunah* and *leviyah*. The children of the religious leadership receive preferential standing — which, at a minyan where the same families appeared regularly, would have been a consistent and perhaps uncontested feature of the order.

### Status vs Merit

The hierarchy continues with the *rashei knesiyot* — the heads of the *shuls* — who in the relevant historical period appear to have occupied a civic or administrative role somewhat distinct from the rabbinic authority structure. Archaeological evidence from ancient synagogues in *Eretz Yisrael* confirms that synagogue leadership was not coextensive with rabbinic leadership; the *rosh knesset* was often a prominent communal figure whose authority derived from a different source. After the *rashei knesiyot*, the order is open: *vechol adam*, and from that point any reasonable allocation may be made. The interplay throughout is striking — a system rooted in inherited priestly status transitions, via an intermediate meritocratic hierarchy, back again to something resembling inherited communal prestige.

## Chumashim & Haftarot

### Chumashim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלַחוּ לֵיהּ בְּנֵי גָלִיל לְרַבִּי חֶלְבּוֹ: מַהוּ לִקְרוֹת בְּחוּמָּשִׁים בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת בְּצִיבּוּר? לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. אֲתָא שַׁיְילֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא, לָא הֲוָה בִּידֵיהּ. אֲתָא שְׁאֵיל בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, וּפַשְׁטוּהָ מֵהָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה שֶׁחָסַר יְרִיעָה אַחַת – אֵין קוֹרִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **The people of the Galilee sent** a question **to Rabbi Ḥelbo: What is** the *halakha* with regard **to reading from *ḥumashim*,** i.e., scrolls containing only one of the five books of the Torah, **in the synagogue in public?** Is this permitted, or is it necessary to read from a complete Torah scroll? An answer **was not** readily **available to him. He came and asked Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa,** but an answer **was not** readily **available to him** either. Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **came and asked** this question **in the study hall, and they resolved** the difficulty **from that which Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** With regard to **a Torah scroll that is missing** even **one sheet** of parchment, **one may not read from it** in public. This indicates that an incomplete Torah scroll may not be used for a public Torah reading.

The *bnei Galila* also sent Rabbi Helbo a second question: *mahu likrot be-chumashin be-veit ha-knesset be-tzibbur* — may individual *chumashim* be used for public Torah reading? Here the term *chumash* does not refer to a printed codex; rather, as Rashi emphasizes, it refers to a scroll — but a scroll containing only one of the five books of the Torah rather than all five together. Each such scroll was written with the same technical requirements as a full *sefer Torah*, but it was only a fifth of the complete *chamisha chumshei Torah*. Rabbi Helbo did not know the answer, nor — when the question was referred onward — did Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha. The question was then brought to the *beit midrash* for resolution.

The attempted proof came from a ruling of Rabbi Shimlai bar Nachmani in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: a *sefer Torah* from which one *yeri'ah* — one parchment section — is missing may not be read from publicly. If something that is ninety-nine percent complete is disqualified, surely a scroll containing only one-fifth of the Torah would be as well. The Gemara, however, rejects this reasoning:

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָא הִיא, הָתָם מְחַסַּר בְּמִילְּתֵיהּ, הָכָא לָא מְחַסַּר בְּמִילְּתֵיהּ. רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֵין קוֹרְאִין בְּחוּמָּשִׁין בְּבֵית הַכְּנֶסֶת, מִשּׁוּם כְּבוֹד צִבּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this argument: **But** that **is not so,** i.e., this cannot serve as a proof to the matter at hand. **There,** it is **lacking** part **of the matter** it is addressing, as a sheet of parchment is missing, whereas **here, it is not lacking** part **of the matter** it is addressing, as it contains a complete book. **Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: One does not read from *ḥumashim* in the synagogue out of respect for the community.**

*hatam mechasar be-mileih* — in the case of the incomplete *sefer Torah*, something is actually missing from its contents. *Hacha lo mechasar be-mileih* — a complete *Sefer Bereishit* lacks nothing within itself; it is whole as what it is. The concern with a *chasar sefer Torah* is the *pegam* — the perception of brokenness — which differs from the case of a formally complete but partial scroll.

### Kavod HaTzibbur

The matter is ultimately resolved by Rabba and Rav Yosef, who rule jointly: *ein korin be-chumashin be-veit ha-knesset, mishum kevod ha-tzibbur* — one may not read from individual *chumashim* in *shul* because of the dignity owed to the congregation. The *tzibbur* deserves the full *sefer Torah*, and reading from a partial scroll, however internally complete, fails to meet that standard.

### Rambam Position

The Rambam's position introduces an important nuance. In *Hilchot Sefer Torah* Perek 10, the Rambam lists twenty things that invalidate a *sefer Torah*. He concludes: if any one of these defects is present, *harei hu ke-chumash min ha-chumashim she-melamdim bo ha-tinokot* — it has the status of a *chumash* used to teach children, it lacks the *kedusha* of a *sefer Torah*, and one may not read from it publicly. The implication is that a *pasul sefer Torah* and a *chumash* occupy the same halakhic category: suitable for private study but not for public reading.

### Beit Yosef

The *Beit Yosef* — Rav Yosef Karo, writing in the *Kessef Mishneh* — draws out a critical inference from the Rambam. A responsum attributed to the Rambam states that if a *sefer Torah pasul* is all that is available, one may read from it and even recite a *beracha* over it, and that *geonei olam* practiced accordingly. The *Rashba* found this startling and suspected a textual error. The *Beit Yosef* defends it by arguing that the Rambam's ruling — *ein korin be-chumashin be-tzibbur* — applies only *lechatchila*, as an ideal standard. Where no alternative is available, the *bidieved* situation invokes the framework of *lo efshar*, and reading from a *pasul sefer Torah* is permitted after the fact.

This distinction carries direct practical consequence. If, in the middle of *kriat haTorah*, an error is discovered in the *sefer Torah* — the reader is midway through an *aliyah* and a letter is found to be missing — a second *sefer Torah* is brought, and reading resumes from the point of the error. The *aliyot* already completed from the *pasul sefer Torah* are not repeated. The basis for this ruling, as the *Beit Yosef* explains in *Shulchan Aruch*, is precisely the Rambam's position: since those *aliyot* were read *bidieved* without a valid *sefer Torah*, and the prohibition is *lechatchila*, what was done stands.

One might further ask: if a community's only available *sefer Torah* is *pasul* and there is no time to repair it before *Shabbat*, may they read from it *lechatchila*? According to the *Beit Yosef*'s reading of the Rambam, the answer would be yes. How this plays out *halacha le-ma'aseh* given the resistance this approach encounters in practice remains a live question. One might add that all of this discussion still operates within the framework of a *chumash* understood as a genuine scroll — not a printed codex, which differs in parchment, form, script, and composition in so many dimensions that whether even the Rambam's *bidieved* leniency would apply to it is an entirely separate question.

## Haftara Books

### Haftara Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה וְרַב יוֹסֵף דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: הַאי סֵפֶר אַפְטָרָתָא – אָסוּר לְמִקְרֵי בֵּיהּ בְּשַׁבָּת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּלֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabba and Rav Yosef both say: It is prohibited to** publicly **read** the *haftara*, the portion from the Prophets that is read after the weekly Torah portion, **on Shabbat, from a scroll** containing only **the *haftarot*. What is the reason** for this? It is **because** this type of scroll **may not be written,** as the words of the Prophets must also be written as complete books.

Rabba and Rav Yosef further rule: *ha-sefer aftarta asur le-mikrei bei be-shabbat* — it is forbidden to read the *haftarah* from a compiled book of *haftarot* on *Shabbat*. This refers to exactly the kind of volume familiar from contemporary *shul* practice: a convenient collection of *haftarot* with the relevant *berachot*, organized by *parasha*, in large and legible print.

### Out of Context Problem

The reason is that such a book *lo nitan le-hikatev* — it should not have been written. The concern is distinct from the *sefer Torah* discussion: this is not a question of ritual fitness or *kavod ha-tzibbur* in the same sense. The issue is that excerpting a *perek* of *Yeshayahu* or *Yirmiyahu* from its full context and presenting it as a standalone unit is itself problematic. One who wishes to learn *Sefer Yeshayahu* should learn it from the *sefer* in its entirety, not from an extracted chapter. Since the object was not appropriate to write, it is not appropriate to read from publicly.

### Muktzeh Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: לְטַלְטוֹלֵי נָמֵי אָסוּר. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא לָא חֲזֵי לְמִיקְרֵי בֵּיהּ. וְלָא הִיא, שְׁרֵי לְטַלְטוֹלֵי וּשְׁרֵי לְמִיקְרֵי בֵּיהּ –
>
> **Translation:** **Mar bar Rav Ashi said: To handle** such a scroll on Shabbat **is also prohibited. What is the reason** for this? It is **because it is not fit to be read.** Consequently, it is treated as set-aside [*muktze*] on Shabbat. The Gemara rejects this argument: **But** that **is not so;** rather, **it is permitted to handle** such a scroll **and it is permitted to read from it.**

Rav Ashi extends the ruling: since such a book may not be used for its intended purpose — reading the *haftarah* — it is also *muktzeh* on *Shabbat* and may not even be moved. The logic is that an item lacking any permitted use on *Shabbat* falls under the category of *muktzeh*. This implies a further dimension of concern: not merely that one should not read from it publicly, but that the very existence of the book, as a decontextualized excerpt of *Nach*, has a kind of problematic character even for private engagement.

### Aggadata Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מְעַיְּינִי בְּסִפְרָא דְאַגַּדְתָּא בְּשַׁבְּתָא; וְהָא לֹא נִיתַּן לִיכָּתֵב, אֶלָּא כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, ״עֵת לַעֲשׂוֹת לַה׳ הֵפֵרוּ תּוֹרָתֶךָ״; הָכָא נָמֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר, ״עֵת לַעֲשׂוֹת לַה׳ הֵפֵרוּ תּוֹרָתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** And a proof for this is **that Rabbi Yoḥanan and Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish used to read from a scroll of *aggada*** containing the words of the Sages **on Shabbat. But** such a scroll **may not be written,** for in principle, the statements of the Oral Law may not be committed to writing. **Rather, since it is not possible** to remember the Oral Law without writing it down, it is permitted to violate the *halakha*, as indicated by the verse: **"It is time to act for the Lord; they have nullified your Torah"** (Psalms 119:126). **Here too,** in the case of a *haftara* scroll, **since it is not** always **possible** to write complete books of the Bible, due to the expense, it is permitted to apply the reasoning of **"It is time to act for the Lord; they have nullified your Torah."**

A counterpoint is introduced immediately. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish would study from a *sifra de-aggadata* — a written compilation of Rabbinic stories and homilies — on *Shabbat*. This presents a different but parallel challenge: such a book is *Torah she'baal peh* in written form, and *Torah she'baal peh* is in principle not to be written. Yet they used it. It is tempting to ask what it meant for the transmission of aggadic traditions in their era, and whether such material existed in written form from early on or emerged only gradually. One might also note that there were established traditions of *Shabbat drashot* of an aggadic character — works like the *She'iltot* blend *halacha* and *aggadata* in a homiletical framework, and portions of *Bereishit Rabbah* may themselves reflect sermon traditions. Whether Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish were encountering material outside their own area of expertise and therefore required a written text, or whether the oral transmission of *aggadata* was simply less rigorous than that of *halakha*, is an interesting open question.

### Lo Efshar Principle

The Gemara's resolution invokes a principle: *keivan de-lo efshar, eit la'asot l'Hashem heferu Toratecha* — since it is impossible otherwise, the time demands action for God's sake even at the cost of setting aside one aspect of Torah. This is the same rationale used to justify the writing down of *Torah she'baal peh* more broadly: the imperative to preserve Torah, when transmission by purely oral means becomes impracticable, overrides the formal prohibition against committing the oral tradition to writing.

The same logic applies, says the Gemara, to the *sefer haftarot*. Rashi explains: *she-ein kol tzibbur ve-tzibbur yachol lischir ve-likhtov nevi'im shalem* — not every community can commission and pay for complete scrolls of all the prophetic books from which *haftarot* are drawn. Given this practical impossibility, the compiled *haftarah* book — though not ideal — is permitted by the principle of *lo efshar*. This is precisely the rationale behind the contemporary practice. The speaker notes that he personally tries, when receiving an *aliyah* for *haftarah*, to read from a complete *Tanakh* rather than from a *haftarah* book, since that at least constitutes a *sefer shalem* in the relevant sense — and notes that publishers have even produced *Tanakhim* in which the *haftarah* sections are printed in larger type for exactly this purpose. Some *shulot*, particularly in Israel, have gone further and commissioned actual scrolls — individual *sefarim* of *Yeshayahu*, *Yirmiyahu*, and the other prophetic books from which *haftarot* are read — so that the reading is performed not merely from a *sefer shalem* but from a proper scroll. Whether the value of *shalem* applies equally to a printed book as to a scroll, and how it intersects with the contemporary reality of printed *chumashim* in schools, are questions the Gemara's discussion raises without fully resolving.

## Megillot for Learning

### Megillah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרַבָּה: מַהוּ לִכְתּוֹב מְגִילָּה לְתִינוֹק לְהִתְלַמֵּד בָּהּ? תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: תּוֹרָה מְגִילָּה מְגִילָּה נִיתְּנָה; תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: תּוֹרָה חֲתוּמָה נִיתְּנָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye raised a dilemma before Rabba: What is** the *halakha* with regard to whether it is permitted **to write a scroll** containing only one portion of the Torah **for** the purpose of enabling **a child to study it?** The Gemara notes: **Let the dilemma be raised according to the one who says** that **the Torah was given** from the outset **scroll by scroll,** meaning that Moses would teach the Jewish people one portion of the Torah, and then write it down, and then teach them the next portion of the Torah, and then write that down, and continue in this way until he committed the entire Torah to writing. And **let the dilemma** also **be raised according to the one who says** that **the Torah was given** as **a complete** book, meaning that the Torah was not written down incrementally, but rather, after teaching the Jewish people the entire Torah, Moses committed it to writing all at once.

Abaye poses a question to Rabba: *mahu le-michtav megillah le-tinok le-hitlamed bah* — is it permitted to write a *megillah*, a subsection of the Torah, for a child to learn from? This is narrower than the *chumash* discussion: not a complete book such as *Sefer Bereishit*, but a segment of a book — say, *Parashat Bereishit* alone, or an even smaller unit. The context is pedagogical rather than liturgical; the question is not about public reading but about private study. Perhaps, one might think, the considerations of *kavod ha-tzibbur* and ritual propriety do not apply here, and a child who will spend the year learning a single *parasha* should be able to do so from a self-contained text.

### Megillah vs Chatumah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תּוֹרָה מְגִילָּה מְגִילָּה נִיתְּנָה – כֵּיוָן דִּמְגִילָּה מְגִילָּה נִיתְּנָה, כּוֹתְבִין; אוֹ דִילְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאִידְּבַק – אִידְּבַק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the two sides of the dilemma according to each opinion: **Let the dilemma be raised according to the one who says** that **the Torah was given scroll by scroll.** On the one hand it is possible to say that **since** the Torah **was** originally **given scroll by scroll,** today as well **one may write** the Torah in separate scrolls. **Or** on the other hand, **perhaps** one should say that **since it was** ultimately **joined** together to form a single scroll, **it was joined** together and can no longer be written in separate scrolls.

The question, Abaye notes, has different valences depending on how one understands the original transmission of the Torah. This introduces a fundamental debate about how the Torah was given: was it written down section by section as each passage was revealed — *megillah megillah nitnah* — or was the entire text preserved orally throughout the forty years and written down as a sealed, indivisible unit at the end — *Torah chatumah nitnah*?

If one holds that Torah was given *megillah megillah*, perhaps individual sections have always had a kind of independent identity, and writing a subsection for a child follows from that premise. Or perhaps even on that view, once the sections were joined together — *keivan de-idbik, idbik* — the composition became indivisible and may no longer be broken apart. Conversely, if Torah was given *chatumah* as a single sealed unit, perhaps the very difficulty of study and the principle of *eit la'asot l'Hashem* would nonetheless permit a pedagogical excerpt. The question, in other words, is not easily resolved by appeal to either premise — it is a genuine question either way.

### Tannaitic Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיבְּעֵי לְמַאן דְּאָמַר תּוֹרָה חֲתוּמָה נִיתְּנָה – כֵּיוָן דַּחֲתוּמָה נִיתְּנָה, אֵין כּוֹתְבִין; אוֹ דִילְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּלָא אֶפְשָׁר – כָּתְבִינַן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין. וּמָה טַעַם? לְפִי שֶׁאֵין כּוֹתְבִין.
>
> **Translation:** And **let the dilemma** also **be raised according to the one who says** that **the Torah was given** as **a complete** book. On the one hand it is possible to say that **since it was given** from the outset as **a complete** book, **one may not write** it today in separate scrolls. **Or** on the other hand, **perhaps** one could say that **since it is not** always **possible** to write a complete Torah, **one may write** it in separate scrolls. Rabba **said to him: One may not write** the Torah in separate scrolls. **And what is the reason? Because one may not write** a scroll that is only part of the Torah.

Rabba responds simply: *ein kotvin* — you may not write such a section. The rationale flows from the view that once the Torah was joined together it is *keivan de-idbik, idbik* — an indivisible unit. Even where *eit la'asot l'Hashem* permits certain accommodations, as in the writing of *Torah she'baal peh*, this leniency does not extend to fragmenting *Torah she'bikhtav*, which is treated as sacrosanct and utterly indivisible. The Gemara then brings a *Beraita* framing this as a *tannaitic* dispute. The first position: *ein kotvin megillah le-tinok le-hitlamed bah* — one may not write a section of Torah for a child to learn from it. The modification: *ve-im da'ato le-hashlim, mutar* — but if one intends to complete the full text, it is permitted. The interpretation of this condition is that one begins with the opening *parasha* and continues adding to the scroll as the child advances, ultimately producing a complete *sefer* — a kind of living scroll that grows along with the student's learning.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: אֵין כּוֹתְבִין מְגִילָּה לְתִינוֹק לְהִתְלַמֵּד בָּהּ, וְאִם דַּעְתּוֹ לְהַשְׁלִים – מוּתָּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בִּבְרֵאשִׁית – עַד דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל. בְּתוֹרַת כֹּהֲנִים – עַד ״וַיְהִי בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁמִינִי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The question of whether or not writing a scroll for a child is permitted is **subject to** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it is taught in the following *baraita*: **One may not write a scroll** containing only one portion of the Torah **for** the purpose of enabling **a child to study, but if** the writer's **intention is to complete** the scroll, **it is permitted. Rabbi Yehuda says: In** the book of **Genesis** he may write a scroll from the beginning **until the generation of the flood. In *Torat Kohanim*,** the book of Leviticus, he may write a scroll from the beginning **until "And it came to pass on the eighth day"** (Leviticus 9:1).

### Heleni Tablet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: אַף הִיא עָשְׂתָה טַבְלָא שֶׁל זָהָב, שֶׁפָּרָשַׁת סוֹטָה כְּתוּבָה עָלֶיהָ! אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַנַּאי: בְּאָלֶף בֵּית.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **raised an objection to his** opinion from a mishna (*Yoma* 37b) where it was taught: Queen Helene **also fashioned a golden tablet** as a gift for the Temple **on which the** Torah **portion** discussing **a *sota* was written.** When the priest would write the scroll of a *sota* in the Temple, he would copy this Torah portion from the tablet, so that a Torah scroll need not be taken out for that purpose. This indicates that it is permitted for one to write a single portion of the Torah. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says in the name of Rabbi Yannai:** There is no proof from this mishna, as the tablet prepared by Queen Helene was not written in an ordinary manner, but rather it consisted of the letters **of** the ***alef-beit*,** i.e., only the first letter of each word was written on the tablet, and by looking at it the priest writing the *sota* scroll would remember what to write.

Rabbi Yehuda's position is then cited: he permits writing sections of specific lengths — from *Bereishit* through *Dor ha-Mabul*, or in *Torat Kohanim* through *Vayehi Bayom ha-Shemini* — implying that at least these natural units may stand on their own. Separately, the Gemara raises the case of the golden tablet of Heleni ha-Malka. Queen Helena had a golden tablet made, inscribed with *Parashat Sota*, so that the *kohen* could consult it when writing the *megillat sota* for the *sotah* ceremony. The Gemara initially understands this as a case of a Torah section written as an independent text — but Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish in the name of Rabbi Yannai explains that the text on the tablet was written *be-seirugin*: not in full, but in a partial or abbreviated form, with the beginning of each *pasuk* written out and the remainder indicated only by *rashei teivot*. Rashi understands this to mean the opening words were complete while the endings were abbreviated. The point is that the tablet signaled the text rather than presenting it as a full, self-standing document. Presenting something as the actual, complete, authoritative text — such that it implicitly declares itself capable of standing alone — is precisely the problem. A text written in abbreviated or partial form avoids that declaration. This is what made the Heleni tablet acceptable.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּסֵירוּגִין.
>
> **Translation:** The tablet fashioned by Queen Helene was written **by alternating** complete words and initials. The first words of each verse were written there, but the rest of the words in the verse were represented by initials. Therefore, this contribution of Queen Helene does not resolve the question of whether writing a scroll for a child is permitted.

### Modern Schools

The contemporary practice of using individual printed *chumashim* in schools is worth examining against these standards. The Gemara's concern was with a scroll — a *megillah* in the technical sense. A printed codex differs so dramatically in form, material, and script that it may constitute a categorically different object. Moreover, since *chumash* in the sense of a complete book of the Torah is in principle acceptable — it was the subject of the earlier discussion about public reading — a printed *Sefer Bereishit* may not qualify as the problematic *megillah* at all. There is also the *da'ato le-hashlim* consideration: if the school's intent is that students will eventually learn all *chamisha chumshei Torah*, the individual *chumash* may be understood as part of an ongoing, completable process. One might further note that a *chumash* is precisely a *chumash* — a fifth of the Torah — and the earlier discussion did acknowledge *chumashim* as a distinct category from a *megillah*, which is a sub-section of a *sefer*.

## Torah Transmission

### R. Yochanan vs Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי בְּנָאָה: תּוֹרָה – מְגִילָּה מְגִילָּה נִיתְּנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אָז אָמַרְתִּי הִנֵּה בָאתִי בִּמְגִילַּת סֵפֶר כָּתוּב עָלָי״. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ אוֹמֵר: תּוֹרָה – חֲתוּמָה נִיתְּנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לָקוֹחַ אֵת סֵפֶר הַתּוֹרָה הַזֹּאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara returns to discuss the previously mentioned dispute. **Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Bana'a: The Torah was given** from the outset **scroll by scroll, as it is stated: "Then I said, behold, I come with the scroll of the book that is written for me"** (Psalms 40:8). King David is saying about himself that there is a section of the Torah, "the scroll of the book," that alludes to him, i.e., "that is written for me." This indicates that each portion of the Torah constitutes a separate scroll. **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish says: The Torah was given** as **a complete** book, **as it is stated: "Take this scroll of the Torah"** (Deuteronomy 31:26), which teaches that from the outset the Torah was given as a complete unit.

The debate about how the Torah was given is now examined directly. Rabbi Yochanan, citing Rabbi Banna'ah, holds: *Torah megillah megillah nitnah* — the Torah was given section by section. His prooftext is from *Tehillim*: *Az amarti hinei vati be-megillat sefer katuv alai* — "Then I said, behold I come with a scroll-book written about me." The text calls it a *megillat sefer*, a scroll or sectioned book, and is understood as referring to the Torah — implying that the Torah is a composition of *megillos*, distinct sections. Reish Lakish disagrees: *Torah chatumah nitnah* — the Torah was given as one sealed, indivisible unit. His prooftext is from the end of *Devarim*: *Lakoch et Sefer ha-Torah ha-zot* — "Take this Sefer Torah," the singular *sefer* pointing to one complete and unified document.

### Prooftexts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״לָקוֹחַ״! הָהוּא לְבָתַר דְּאִידְּבַק.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage, Rabbi Yoḥanan, **as well, isn't it written "take,"** indicating that the Torah scroll was given whole? How does he explain this verse? The Gemara answers: **That** verse is speaking about the Torah **after it was joined** together to form a single unit.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב ״בִּמְגִילַּת סֵפֶר כָּתוּב עָלָי״! הַהוּא, דְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלָּהּ אִיקְּרִי מְגִילָּה, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֹּאמֶר אֵלַי מָה אַתָּה רוֹאֶה, וָאוֹמַר אֲנִי רוֹאֶה מְגִילָּה עָפָה״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage, Reish Lakish, **as well, isn't it written: "With the scroll of the book that is written for me,"** indicating that the Torah was given scroll by scroll? How does he explain this verse? The Gemara answers: **That** verse teaches that **the entire Torah is called a scroll.** This is indicated in another verse as well, **as it is written: "And He said to me: What do you see? And I said: I see a flying scroll"** (Zechariah 5:2).

The Gemara works through the counter-prooftexts in both directions. How does Rabbi Yochanan handle *Lakoch et Sefer ha-Torah*? *Hahu le-vatar de-idbik* — that verse refers to after the sections were assembled and joined together; by the end they formed one *sefer*, but that does not speak to how the text was originally transmitted. And how does Reish Lakish handle *megillat sefer*? *Chol ha-Torah kullah ikri megillah* — the word *megillah* can refer to the Torah as a whole, not to any particular section within it. He adduces a verse from *Zechariah*: *va-yomer elai mah attah ro'eh va-omar ani ro'eh megillah afah* — "And he said to me, what do you see? And I said, I see a floating scroll." There the word *megillah* clearly refers to a single whole scroll rather than a section of a larger work.

### Eight Parshiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי נָמֵי, לְכִדְרַבִּי לֵוִי – דְּאָמַר רַבִּי לֵוִי: שְׁמֹנֶה פָּרָשִׁיּוֹת נֶאֶמְרוּ בַּיּוֹם שֶׁהוּקַם בּוֹ הַמִּשְׁכָּן, אֵלּוּ הֵן: פָּרָשַׁת כֹּהֲנִים, וּפָרָשַׁת לְוִיִּם, וּפָרָשַׁת טְמֵאִים, וּפָרָשַׁת שִׁילּוּחַ טְמֵאִים, וּפָרָשַׁת ״אַחֲרֵי מוֹת״, וּפָרָשַׁת שְׁתוּיֵי יַיִן, וּפָרָשַׁת נֵרוֹת, וּפָרָשַׁת פָּרָה אֲדוּמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Alternatively,** this verse serves to allude **to** the sections of the Torah discussed in **that** statement **of Rabbi Levi, as Rabbi Levi says: Eight sections were said on the day that the Tabernacle was erected,** on the first of Nisan. **They are: The section of the priests** (Leviticus 21:1–22:26); **the section of the Levites** (Numbers 8:5–26); **the section of the impure** (Leviticus 13:1– 14:57); **the section of the sending away of the impure** (Numbers 5:1–4); **the section** beginning with the words **"After the death"** (Leviticus, chapter 16); **the section** dealing with priests who have become **intoxicated with wine** (Leviticus 10:8–11); **the section of the lamps** (Numbers 8:1–7); **and the section of the red heifer** (Numbers, chapter 19), as all of these sections are necessary for service in the Tabernacle.

Yet Reish Lakish concedes something to the structure of Rabbi Levi's tradition: *shemonah parshiyot ne'emru be-yom she-hukam bo ha-Mishkan* — eight *parshiyot* were communicated on the day the *Mishkan* was erected. These are: *Parshat Kohanim* — the laws of priestly status (*Emor el ha-kohanim*); *Parshat Leviyim* — the role of the *Leviyim* in the *Mishkan*; *Parshat Tme'im* — the laws of impurity (Rashi understands this as the narrative of those who were *tamei le-nefesh adam* and could not bring the *korban Pesach*; Tosafot reads it as the broader section of *tumat meit*, *tumas sheretz*, and *tumas neveilah*); *Parshat Shiluach Tme'im* — the *mitzvah* of sending the impure out of the camp; *Parshat Acharei Mot* — concerning *Yom Kippur*, understood by Rashi as connected to the tragedy of *b'nei Aharon* and the commandment limiting entry to the *kodesh*; *Parshat Shituyei Yayin* — the prohibition against entering the sanctuary in a state of intoxication, also linked to the death of *b'nei Aharon*; *Parshat Neirot* — the lighting of the *Menorah* (*Be-ha'alotecha*); and *Parshat Parah* — the laws of the *parah adumah*, necessary because the people needed a means of purification in order to enter the *Mishkan*. These eight sections, though dispersed across different locations in the Torah, were communicated together on a single occasion. Reish Lakish may acknowledge that these particular sections had a kind of distinct identity and communicative moment, while still maintaining that the Torah as a written text was given as one sealed unit.

## Written vs Oral

### R. Elazar View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: תּוֹרָה – רוֹב בִּכְתָב וּמִיעוּט עַל פֶּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֶכְתּוֹב לוֹ רוּבֵּי תּוֹרָתִי כְּמוֹ זָר נֶחְשָׁבוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara continues its discussion concerning the writing of the Torah: **Rabbi Elazar says: The majority of the Torah** was transmitted **in writing, while the minority** was transmitted **orally, as it is stated: "I wrote for him the greater part of My Torah; they were reckoned a strange thing"** (Hosea 8:12), meaning that the majority of the Torah was transmitted in written form.

A further debate concerns the relative proportion of *Torah she'bikhtav* and *Torah she'baal peh*. Rabbi Elazar holds: *Torah rov be-chsav u-miut al peh* — the majority of Torah is written, a minority is oral. His prooftext: *Echtov lo rubei Torati* — "I will write for him the majority of my Torah" — and *kemo zar nechshavu*, taken by Rashi to mean "they have treated it like something foreign," i.e., they have not upheld the written Torah. The verse thus asserts that it is the *written* dimension that constitutes the majority.

### R. Yochanan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: רוֹב עַל פֶּה וּמִיעוּט בִּכְתָב, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי עַל פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The majority** of the Torah was transmitted **orally [*al peh*], while the minority** was transmitted **in writing, as it is stated** with regard to the giving of the Torah to Moses on Mount Sinai: **"For on the basis of [*al pi*] these matters** I have made a covenant with you and with Israel" (Exodus 34:27), which indicates that the greater part of the Sinaitic covenant was taught orally.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *rov al peh, u-miut be-chsav* — the majority of Torah is *Torah she'baal peh*, and only a minority is in written form. His prooftext: *ki al pi ha-devarim ha-eileh* — "by the mouth of these things," meaning the oral tradition is the primary medium of transmission. He handles Rabbi Elazar's verse by reading it as a rhetorical question with an exclamatory tone: *Echtov lo rubei Torati?* — could I possibly write down the majority of my Torah? *Halo kemo zar nechshavu* — that would be an extraordinary, even absurd, claim. He thus denies that the verse asserts what Rabbi Elazar took from it.

### Rashi Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָכְתִיב: ״אֶכְתּוֹב לוֹ רוּבֵּי תּוֹרָתִי״! הָהוּא, אַתְמוֹהֵי קָא מַתְמַהּ: אֶכְתּוֹב לוֹ רוּבֵּי תּוֹרָתִי?! הֲלֹא ״כְּמוֹ זָר נֶחְשָׁבוּ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** Sage, Rabbi Yoḥanan, **as well, isn't it written: "I wrote for him the greater part of My Torah"?** How does he understand this verse? The Gemara answers: **This** verse **is** not a statement, but rather a rhetorical question expressing **bewilderment:** For did **I write for him the greater part of My Torah?** In that case **they,** the Jewish people, **would be reckoned as strangers,** meaning that there would be no difference between them and the nations of the world if everything was written down. Rather, the majority of the Torah must remain an oral tradition.

The question as it stands seems almost unanswerable in its plain sense: there are roughly seventy volumes of Rabbinic literature against five books of the written Torah. On any raw count, the oral tradition is vastly larger. Rashi's interpretation resolves the difficulty ingeniously. The debate is not about sheer volume at all. The question is *from where* the content of *Torah she'baal peh* is derived. How much of *Torah she'baal peh* has its ultimate source in, and is derivable from, *Torah she'bikhtav* — through the mechanisms of *midrash*, *derashah*, textual analysis — versus how much exists independently of *Torah she'bikhtav*, whether as *halacha le-Moshe mi-Sinai* or as tradition not anchored in a *pasuk*?

### Derivation Debate

This maps onto a question that occupies scholars of *Midrashei Halacha*: are the Sages genuinely *deriving* their halakhic conclusions from close reading of the text — that is, they did not know the *halacha* and went to the *psukim* to determine it — or did they begin with a received tradition and subsequently find support for it in the *pasuk*? If the former, the majority of *Torah she'baal peh* is ultimately rooted in *Torah she'bikhtav*; if the latter, the majority exists independently of what is written. Rabbi Elazar holds that the derivational source of most *halacha* is textual; Rabbi Yochanan holds that the tradition is primary and the textual connections secondary. This is a remarkable dispute about the epistemological structure of *Torah she'baal peh* itself.

## Written vs Oral

### Separate Genres

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בַּר נַחְמָנִי, מְתוּרְגְּמָנֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ, כְּתִיב: ״כְּתוֹב לְךָ אֶת הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה״, וּכְתִיב: ״כִּי עַל פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה״ – הָא כֵּיצַד? דְּבָרִים שֶׁבִּכְתָב אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לְאוֹמְרָן עַל פֶּה, דְּבָרִים שֶׁבְּעַל פֶּה אִי אַתָּה רַשַּׁאי לְאוֹמְרָן בִּכְתָב, דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל. תָּנָא: ״אֵלֶּה״ – אֵלֶּה אַתָּה כּוֹתֵב, וְאִי אַתָּה כּוֹתֵב הֲלָכוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda bar Naḥmani, the disseminator for Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish, expounded** as follows: **It is written: "Write you these matters"** (Exodus 34:27), **and it is written** later in that same verse: **"For on the basis of [*al pi*] these matters." How** can **these** texts be reconciled? They mean to teach: **Matters that were written you may not express them orally [*al peh*],** and **matters that were** taught **orally you may not express them in writing. The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** The word **"these"** in the mitzva recorded in the verse "Write you these matters" is used here in an emphatic sense: **These** matters, i.e., those recorded in the Written Law, **you may write, but you may not write *halakhot*,** i.e., the *mishnayot* and the rest of the Oral Law.

Rabbi Yehuda bar Nachmani, the *meturgeman* — the formal announcer — of Reish Lakish, expounds the following *derasha*. One verse says *Ktov lecha et ha-devarim ha-eileh* — "Write these words for yourself." Another says *ki al pi ha-devarim ha-eileh* — "by the mouth of these things." The juxtaposition generates a principle: *devarim she-bi-chsav ee attah rashai le-omran be-al peh* — that which is written may not be transmitted orally, and *devarim she-be'al peh ee attah rashai le-omran bi-chsav* — that which is oral may not be committed to writing. Each genre must remain in its proper domain.

The power of this principle is not merely procedural. There is something theologically significant about the *Torah she'bikhtav* being fixed, concrete, and immutable — written in stone, as it were — while the *Torah she'baal peh* remains alive in the relationship between *rebbe* and *talmid*, transmitted by voice and internalized through engagement. Writing *Torah she'bikhtav* anchors it; the orality of *Torah she'baal peh* preserves its capacity for development, application, and response to circumstance. To collapse the boundary — to turn the written into an oral tradition or to freeze the oral in writing — would distort the nature of each.

There is a related tension worth noting: it is sometimes said that the Rabbis may have been pushing back against sectarian groups — such as those behind the Dead Sea Scrolls — who produced *pesharim*, written interpretive texts on *Torah she'bikhtav*. One might argue that writing down *derashot* directly tied to the text gave those interpretations a textual authority they did not deserve. If the *derashot* are presented in written form alongside *Torah she'bikhtav*, they may be mistaken for the original text itself — or at least accorded a similar standing. The school of Rabbi Yishmael's distinction, discussed next, addresses this very boundary.

### Drasha vs Halacha

The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: *eileh* — referring to the written *devarim* — *attah kotev, ve-ee attah kotev halakhot*. You may write *derashot* — the homiletical and exegetical engagements directly linked to the written text — but you may not write *halakhot*, the bottom-line legal rulings of the oral tradition. The *halakhot* as a genre — the kind of material later crystallized in the *Mishnah* — must remain oral. This represents resistance to codification: not merely to writing, but to the concretization of *halacha* into a fixed, authoritative written document. The *Mishnah* itself, it is worth recalling, was not published in written form but transmitted orally; its eventual writing down was itself an act of *eit la'asot l'Hashem*.

The distinction the school of Rabbi Yishmael draws may also reflect a different concern. *Derashot* are explicitly in conversation with *Torah she'bikhtav* — they are anchored in and point toward the text. Writing them alongside the text preserves their derivative status. *Halakhot*, by contrast, are presented as self-standing rulings without explicit textual reference. Writing them could make them appear equivalent in authority to the written Torah, or could give the false impression that human legal reasoning has the same standing as divine revelation. Keeping them oral preserves their character as *Torah she'baal peh* — transmitted, received, and applied through an ongoing human relationship with the tradition.

### Covenant & Oral

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא כָּרַת הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא בְּרִית עִם יִשְׂרָאֵל, אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל דְּבָרִים שֶׁבְּעַל פֶּה – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי עַל פִּי הַדְּבָרִים הָאֵלֶּה כָּרַתִּי אִתְּךָ בְּרִית וְאֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: The Holy One, Blessed be He, made a covenant with the Jewish people only for the sake of the matters that** were transmitted **orally [*be'al peh*], as it is stated: "For on the basis of [*al pi*] these matters I have made a covenant with you and with Israel"** (Exodus 34:27).

The sugya concludes with a statement of Rabbi Yochanan that is at once a legal observation and a theological declaration: *lo karat ha-Kadosh Baruch Hu berit im Yisrael ella bishvil devarim she-be'al peh* — the Holy One, Blessed be He, made His covenant with Israel only by virtue of *Torah she'baal peh*. The prooftext: *ki al pi ha-devarim ha-eileh karati ittecha berit ve-et Yisrael* — "for by the mouth of these things have I made a covenant with you and with Israel." Even if *Torah she'bikhtav* is immutable, fixed, and inviolable — the bedrock of the divine communication — the *brit*, the living covenantal relationship between God and the Jewish people, is constituted through *Torah she'baal peh*: through the ongoing human engagement with interpretation, derivation, and application. The process of *Torah she'baal peh* — its development, its transmission, its responsiveness to history — is not secondary to the covenant but is itself the very medium through which the covenant is enacted and sustained. On this striking note, the *daf* concludes.
