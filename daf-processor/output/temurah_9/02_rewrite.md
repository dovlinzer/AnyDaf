# Temurah 9 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Types of Temurah

### Sheep & Cattle

The ninth daf of *Temurah* opens with a new *mishna* that returns us to the core concerns of the tractate after an interlude on *bechor*. The *mishna* begins: *memirin min hatzon al habakar, min habakar al hatzon* — one may effect a *temurah* exchange from sheep to cattle or from cattle to sheep. It bears repeating that the capacity to perform *temurah* is not a commendable one; the act constitutes a transgression. Nonetheless, it is effective, and the *mishna* establishes that the exchange need not involve animals of the same species. If one has a sheep designated as an *olah*, one may attempt to exchange it with a cow, even a cow not suited to that particular *kedusha*.

### Male & Female

The *mishna* continues: *min hazcharim al hanekeivot, umin hanekeivot al hazcharim* — from males to females and females to males. This holds even where the type of *kedusha* conferred upon the animal is inappropriate for its gender. If, for example, one has a female animal that has been sanctified with the *kedusha* of an *olah* — a category ordinarily requiring a male — the *temurah* is nonetheless effective. What becomes of such an animal in practice, given the mismatch between its sex and its designated sanctity, is a matter the *Gemara* will address later.

### Blemished Animals

The *mishna* goes on: *min hatmimim al ba'alei mumim* — from unblemished animals to blemished ones. This case requires some clarification. As a general rule, an animal that already possesses a *mum* at the time of its sanctification cannot acquire *kedushat korban* — *kedushat haguf* — since it is, in a sense, an improper object for sanctification, no different in principle from consecrating a piece of furniture. The *kedusha* that attaches to such an animal is only *kedushat bedek habayit*, which pertains to the Temple maintenance fund rather than to sacrifice.

However, when an animal is first sanctified in an unblemished state and subsequently develops a *mum*, it retains full *kedushat korban*. It cannot be brought as a *korban* due to its blemish, but its sanctity as a *korban* remains intact. From such an animal, *temurah* is applicable: the *kedusha* that resided in the original animal now passes to the second one, even if that second animal also carries a *mum*. The verse states, *vehaya hu utemurato yihye kodesh* — it and its *temurah* shall be holy — and this takes effect regardless. The *mishna* also mentions the reverse: *min ba'alei mumim al hatmimim* — from blemished to unblemished — which, while perhaps seeming like a beneficial exchange, is equally an act of *temurah*.

### Prohibition Verse

The *mishna* cites the source verse: *lo yachalifenu velo yamir oto tov bera o ra betov* — one shall not exchange it nor transfer it, good for bad or bad for good. The *mishna* then poses a question of interpretation: what constitutes a case of *tov bera*, good for bad? Rashi understands the first term in the verse as referring to the non-sanctified animal being offered in exchange — one is attempting to replace a bad animal (the sanctified one) with a good one. Tosafot read it in the opposite direction. Following Rashi, the *mishna* answers that *tov bera* refers specifically to *ba'alei mumim shekadam hekdesham et muman* — animals whose sanctification preceded the onset of their blemish. That is, *temurah* from a blemished animal is only operative when the animal's *kedusha* was established prior to its blemish.

## Blemish Case

### Rashi Position

The *mishna*'s formulation raises a sharper question about the case of an animal that was sanctified first and then developed a *mum*. Such an animal possesses full *kedushat korban*, yet one is supposed to redeem its value and use those proceeds to purchase a replacement *korban*. If one instead takes a healthy unblemished animal and declares *zeh tachas zeh*, one has committed the transgression of *temurah*. But given that redemption is both permitted and obligatory in such a case, is there a way to transfer the *kedusha* directly to another animal without incurring the prohibition?

### Blemish Timing

The answer depends, according to several *rishonim*, on the precise framing of the act. The *Minchas Chinuch* states in passing, and the *Shita Mekubetzes* develops more fully, that one may in principle transfer the *kedusha* directly to another animal when the original has a *mum*, provided one frames the act correctly. If one says "I am exchanging A for B," that is *temurah* — one has transgressed and incurs *malkut*. If one says "I am redeeming this blemished animal and placing its *kedusha* upon B," that is *pidyon* and is precisely what one is required to do.

The conceptual distinction is not merely semantic. When one frames the act as exchange, the focus rests on the new animal — one prefers it and wishes it to take the place of the original. When one frames the act as redemption, the focus rests on the original: the animal already has a problem and one is removing its *kedusha* and relocating it. The *Shita Mekubetzes* goes further still and suggests the distinction may be expressed even physically: if one places one's hands on the new, unblemished animal and declares *zeh tachas zeh*, that is *temurah*; if one places one's hands on the animal bearing the *mum* — even using the identical words *zeh tachas zeh* — that constitutes *pidyon*, not *temurah*, since one is initiating the act from the seat of the *kedusha* and removing it outward.

### Redemption Strategy

The irony of the situation is worth noting. *Temurah* with a blemished animal is in a certain sense a strange category, because the entire point of having a *korban* with a *mum* is that one is *obligated* to transfer the *kedusha* elsewhere. The prohibition is not against the outcome — transferring the *kedusha* to another animal — but against the manner in which the transfer is framed and initiated.

If the animal never had *kedushat korban* to begin with — because the *mum* preceded the sanctification — then no act of *temurah* is relevant at all. The question of what effect, if any, such a declaration would have in that scenario is an interesting one left for further analysis. Presumably it would also fail to constitute a valid *pidyon*, since there is no *kedushat korban* to redeem.

### Intent & Words

This leads to a broader principle regarding the relationship between *temurah* and *pidyon*. If an animal has no *mum* and one attempts to "redeem" it onto another animal using the language of redemption, that would presumably still constitute *temurah*, since *pidyon* is not a relevant or legitimate category for an unblemished *korban*. The distinction between framing an act as *temurah* versus *pidyon* is operative only where *pidyon* is itself an applicable and appropriate course of action — that is, where the original animal genuinely has a *mum* and is due for redemption. Outside of that context, the attempt to transfer *kedusha* directly to another animal is *temurah* by definition. This point, while not exhaustively analyzed in the early sources, will resurface in a more extreme form at the end of the *Gemara*.

## Gemara Derivation

### Abaye's Duality

The *Gemara* asks *mena hani mili* — where is all of this derived? The *Gemara* cites a *baraita*: the verse in *Vayikra* states *lo yachalifenu velo yamir oto tov bera o ra betov v'im hamer yamir behemah b'vehemah vehaya hu utmurato yihye kodesh* — if one exchanges *behemah b'behemah*, animal for animal. The word *behemah* is entirely generic, and from this the *baraita* derives that all the combinations enumerated in the *mishna* — sheep for cattle, cattle for sheep, sheep for goats, goats for sheep, male for female, female for male, blemished for unblemished, unblemished for blemished — are all subsumed under *behemah b'behemah*. 

The *baraita* then asks: might one think *temurah* applies even when the blemish preceded the sanctification — *yachol afilu kadam mum lehekdesham*? The *Gemara* answers that the verse therefore specifies *tov bera o ra betov* — the language of *tov* and *ra* is interpreted technically, in the sense of unblemished and blemished in a *korban* context, and is used to exclude the case where the animal was already *ra* — blemished — at the moment of its sanctification.

The question then becomes how to derive this exclusion from the language of the verse. Abaye explains: the verse could have said simply *tov bera o ra bo* — good for bad, or bad for it — since having already established the first animal as "bad," the implication of "good" in the second case would be understood. The explicit repetition of the word *tov* — *ra betov* — is therefore a redundancy. That redundancy is the textual hook: *shema minah*, infer from it that the origin animal must possess an element of *tov*. Concretely: *tov me'ikara oseh tmurah, ra me'ikara ein oseh tmurah* — only an animal that was good at the outset, that was unblemished at the time of sanctification, is subject to *temurah*. If it was bad — *ra* — from the outset, its *kedusha* was never *kedushat haguf* and it falls outside the domain of *temurah* entirely.

### Rava's Position

Rava offers a somewhat different analysis. He observes that in fact both uses of the word *tov* in the verse are in some sense redundant — *travaihu tov yateira mi'asrei*. The verse could have simply said *lo yachlifenu velo yamir tov bera o ra bo* and dispensed with both explicit *tov* references. Why, then, are both needed? Rava answers that one extra *tov* is needed to establish that even *tov betov* — even exchanging a good animal for another good animal — carries lashes. The verse could not have left this to inference, since it requires an explicit source for lashes to be imposed. The second extra *tov* then teaches *tov me'ikara*, that the original animal must have been unblemished at the time of sanctification.

### Kal va-Chomer

Abaye challenges Rava's need for an explicit verse prohibiting *tov betov*. The argument seems obvious: *kal vachomer* — if exchanging a good animal for a bad one, which might appear to be an improvement, still incurs lashes, then certainly exchanging good for good, where no improvement is even being attempted, should incur lashes as well. Why does Rava require a verse for that?

Rava responds with the principle *ein onshin min hadin* — one does not impose lashes on the basis of a *kal vachomer* alone. A *kal vachomer* may establish the prohibition in principle, but for lashes to be imposed, the prohibition must have an explicit textual source.

Abaye, however, counters that this is not a genuine *kal vachomer* at all. The argument *mi gara tov mera* — is *tov betov* any worse than *tov bera*? — suggests that *tov betov* is not a separate inference derived from *tov bera* but rather is already included within the same statement. The principle that one may not exchange even for something "better" already encompasses, by definition, the case of exchanging for something equivalent. Just as a prohibition against harming something includes a prohibition against simply not improving it, the inclusion of *tov betov* in the prohibition follows not as a separate *kal vachomer* but as an implicit component of the original rule. Abaye draws an analogy to the case of a full sister in the laws of *arayot*, where a *kal vachomer* from a half-sister is needed and yet the question arises whether this is genuinely a *kal vachomer* or merely implicit in the original prohibition — the *Gemara* elsewhere resolves such cases by treating them as distinct enough to require explicit sources.

### Redundancy Resolution

Having established Rava's position that both extra *tov* references are necessary, and that according to Abaye the *tov betov* prohibition is implicit rather than derived by *kal vachomer*, the *Gemara* still needs to resolve why, according to Abaye, the verse contains an extra *tov* at all if *tov betov* is already implicit. The answer is precisely what Abaye himself stated at the outset: the extra *tov* is not there to teach *tov betov* as a new case but rather to teach *tov me'ikara* — to signal that the animal originating the *temurah* must have possessed an element of *tov* at the time of its sanctification. Once you say you cannot do bad for good, you know that "bad for good" refers to animals that were at least once good — because if the animal started blemished, it was never in the domain of *tov* at all, and the prohibition never touches it.

## Exchange Cases

### Hachlif Case

The *Gemara* now turns to the *baraita*: *lo yachlifenu beshel acherim velo yamiru bo beshel atzmo* — do not exchange it with another person's animal, and do not transfer it on your own. The case of exchange with another person involves a scenario in which one party holds a sanctified *korban* and another holds an unsanctified animal, and they attempt to swap — the sanctified animal passing to one party and the unsanctified animal, now absorbing the *kedusha*, passing to the other. The one who controls the sanctity — the owner of the *korban* — is the one who would incur *malkut*. The verse *velo yamiru bo* covers the separate case where both animals belong to the same individual and that person declares one to be the substitute of the other.

### Two Prohibitions

The *Gemara* asks why both prohibitions are necessary. Could the verse not have stated only *lo yachlifenu* — do not exchange — and left *lo yamiru bo* to be understood by inference? The answer depends on which case is considered more obviously prohibited. One possibility is that when both animals belong to the same person, the act is more obviously a real and meaningful act of exchange, since the individual has full control over both, and therefore *lo yamiru bo* is the more obvious case. One might have supposed that *lo yachlifenu* — exchange with another — could escape prohibition since the actor does not fully control the other party's animal. To preclude that reasoning, the *Torah* states both.

Conversely, it could be argued the other way: perhaps one would have thought that when both animals are mine, I am not truly exchanging but merely extending the *kedusha* — declaring that the second animal shall also carry the *kedusha* of the first without removing it from the first. If that were permitted, the second prohibition would be needed to clarify that even this constitutes a transgression.

This is indeed what some *rishonim* appear to conclude from the *Gemara*: that even a mere attempt to extend *kedusha* — not to replace one sanctified animal with another, but to add *kedusha* to a second animal without removing it from the first — is also considered *temurah*. This is a striking position, since the entire intuitive conception of *temurah* was that it represents an attempt to replace one *korban* with another. Rabbeinu Gershom, however, reads the *Gemara* differently, and his version of the text is more persuasive. His text reads *detzavuyei matzi makdish lehu* — that one has the capacity to sanctify both of them — not that one is actually in the process of sanctifying both. According to Rabbeinu Gershom, the *Gemara* is saying only this: since both animals belong to me, and I have the inherent ability to sanctify both independently, one might have thought the act should be interpreted charitably — that what I really intended was to sanctify the second animal through my own independent power, and therefore perhaps I should not incur lashes. *Ka mashma lan* — the *Torah* teaches that no such charitable reinterpretation applies; the act is one of exchange and lashes follow.

This reading is far more coherent, since it does not require positing a novel category of *temurah* never encountered elsewhere. The contrast with the case of exchange with another person is also illuminated by Rabbeinu Gershom's reading: there, the question is how I have the power to affect another's *korban* at all; here, I do have the power — but that power does not redefine the act I actually performed.

### Matfis Concept

The *Gemara* now examines the case of exchange with another person's animal more closely. *Hai beshel acherim heichi dami* — what exactly is the scenario? If my *korban* is being exchanged for Michael's unsanctified animal, then while I control the *kedusha* I wish to transfer, I do not control Michael's animal — and the verse teaches *ish ki yakdish et beito kodesh l'Hashem*, from which the rule is derived: *mah beito birshuto af kol birshuto* — just as one's house must be in one's possession, so too any sanctification must be of something in one's control. So how could I sanctify Michael's animal?

Conversely, if Michael's *korban* is being exchanged for my unsanctified animal, then I control the object receiving the *kedusha*, but I do not control the *korban* — and therefore how could I transfer the *kedusha* of Michael's *korban* onto my animal?

This raises the technical distinction between *makdish* and *matpis*. *Makdish* refers to direct sanctification — I impose new *kedusha* on something. *Matpis* — from the root *tofes*, to grasp — refers to transferring or extending an existing *kedusha*: I cause something to become *kadosh* by latching it onto, grabbing onto, the *kedusha* that already inheres in another object. The *Gemara* resolves the case by stipulating that the scenario of *lo yachlifenu* is one where the owner of the *korban* — Michael — has publicly announced: *kol harotze lehamir b'vehemti yavo veyamir* — whoever wishes to use my animal for *temurah* is welcome to do so. By granting this permission, Michael enables another person to perform the act of *matpis* — to grab the *kedusha* of his *korban* and transfer it — and that person may then do so and will incur *malkut*.

### Permission Cases

This leads to a fascinating observation: *temurah* is not categorically limited to the owner of the *korban*. The owner has, by default, the exclusive capacity to exercise the act of *temurah*, because only the owner is in full control of the sanctity. But that capacity can be delegated. If the owner makes it known — whether through an explicit declaration or through circumstances that make his consent clearly evident — that others may use his animal for *temurah*, then others can perform a valid act of *temurah* on his animal. The comparison to *terumah* is instructive: just as an owner may authorize others to separate *terumah* from his grain by declaring *kol harotze litrom yavo veyitrom*, so too an owner may authorize others to perform *temurah* on his *korban*.

The observation that such an announcement is itself a violation of *lifnei iver* is noted, but it does not prevent the act from taking effect — a principle already explored in earlier discussions in this tractate regarding the relationship between prohibited intent and the efficacy of the resulting act.

All of this reinforces Rabbeinu Gershom's reading of the earlier passage. The *Gemara*'s central concern throughout the discussion of exchange with another's animal is the question of power and control: does the actor have the capacity to do what he is attempting to do? This is precisely the contrast Rabbeinu Gershom identified in the case where both animals belong to the same person — there the question was not one of capacity but of how the act should be characterized, and the *Torah* teaches that it is still *temurah* regardless.

## Multiple Animals

### Mishna Positions

A new *mishna* presents the question of exchanges involving unequal numbers of animals. *Hamemir echad bishnayim ushnayim be'echad* — what if one takes a single sanctified animal and declares two unsanctified animals to be in its stead? Or takes two sanctified animals and designates one as their replacement? The *mishna* rules that this is indeed an act of *temurah*: it takes effect, lashes are incurred, and the *kedusha* attaches to the receiving animals. The same applies to one for a hundred or a hundred for one.

### Rabbi Shimon

Rabbi Shimon disagrees. *Ein memir ela echad be'echad* — *temurah* is only valid when performed one for one. His source: *vehaya hu utmurato yihye kodesh* — "it and its *tmurato*" — all terms in the singular. Just as *hu* is singular, so the *temurah* object must be a single animal. This derives from the equivalence between the sanctified animal and its *temura*: *mah hu meyuchad af hi o tmurato meyuchadet*.

### Behemah Debate

The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* that the word *behemah b'vehemah* — generic animal for animal — is the source for the *Rabbanan*'s position allowing one for multiple, since *behemah* can denote a plurality of animals, as evidenced by the verse using *behemah rabah* to refer to many animals. Rabbi Shimon responds: *behemah rabah ikrei* — "many animals" may be called *behemah* when explicitly modified by a term indicating plurality. But *stam* — an unqualified *behemah* — does not refer to many; it refers to one. The word *tzon*, by contrast, can on its own denote a flock of many.

### Shimon's Strategy

The *Gemara* observes that in the *mishna*, Rabbi Shimon grounded his position in the word *hu* — in the phrase *hu utmurato*, both the sanctified animal and its *temura* are singular — rather than in the word *behemah*. But the *baraita* presents his position as based on the word *behemah*. The *Gemara* resolves this: initially, Rabbi Shimon argued from *vehaya hu utmurato* — the singular *hu* and the singular *temura*. When he saw that the *Rabbanan* were latching onto the word *behemah* and arguing that it could connote plurality, he responded by fighting on their terrain: even from *behemah b'vehemah* I can derive my position, since *behemah* unmodified is singular. This was a tactical shift — using the *Rabbanan*'s own prooftext against them.

## Sequential Exchanges

### Reish Lakish

Having established that according to Rabbi Shimon only a one-to-one exchange is valid, the *Gemara* turns to the question of what that limitation encompasses. Reish Lakish rules that Rabbi Shimon concedes that one may perform *temurah* sequentially and repeatedly from the same original animal: I have my one *korban*, I declare this one to be its *temura*, I incur lashes — but the original *korban* remains, its *kedusha* untouched. I may then perform another act of *temurah* from that same original animal, and so on. Each act is a valid one-to-one exchange. The reasoning: *kedusha rishona d'hekdesh l'heichan halchah* — where has the original *kedusha* gone? It remains in the original animal, so why should its capacity for *temurah* be exhausted?

### Rabbi Yochanan

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees. *Keshem she'ein memirin shnayim be'echad kach ein memirin ve'chozrin u'memirin* — just as one may not do two animals at once in exchange for one, so too one may not return and do it a second time sequentially. According to Rabbi Yochanan, the limitation Rabbi Shimon imposes on *temurah* means not merely that each individual act must be one-to-one, but that the original animal may be the source of only one *temurah* act in total.

Both a *baraita* supporting Rabbi Yochanan and a *baraita* supporting Reish Lakish are cited. The *baraita* supporting Reish Lakish derives from the verse *vehaya hu utmurato* — *afilu meah b'meah* — even one hundred sequential acts, each one-to-one, are permitted according to Rabbi Shimon, as long as each individual exchange is singular.

### Kedusha Transfer

The debate between Reish Lakish and Rabbi Yochanan turns on a fundamental question: is Rabbi Shimon's limit purely formal — one animal at a time — or substantive — one act of *temurah* per original animal? For Reish Lakish, since the *kedusha* of the original animal is never diminished by an act of *temurah*, the capacity for further *temurah* from that animal logically persists. For Rabbi Yochanan, Rabbi Shimon's *derashah* from the singular *hu* carries a stronger implication: the original animal is entitled to one and only one *temura*, and any further attempt is outside the scope of the Torah's provision. It is worth noting that the principle *ein temura osah temura* — that a *temura* animal cannot itself generate a further *temura* — is a separate rule agreed upon by all, and is not at issue in this debate, which concerns only sequential acts of *temurah* from the original sanctified animal itself.

## Asham Case

### Compound Question

Rabbi Avin poses a compound question that takes the debate to a new level. He asks: according to Rabbi Yochanan, who holds one may not return and perform *temurah* a second time from the same animal, consider the following: one sets aside an *asham*, performs *temurah* once with it — so the question of a second *temurah* is now germane — then the *asham* develops a *mum* and is redeemed onto a second animal. The second animal's *kedusha* is therefore derivative of the first. That second animal is then lost, another *korban* is brought in its place, and the second animal is subsequently found. Under the applicable *halacha*, an *asham shenitkapru ba'aleha be'acher* — an *asham* whose owner has already achieved atonement through a replacement — does not die as a *chatat* would under analogous circumstances; it is instead redirected and becomes an *olah*. Its *kedusha* transforms from *kedushat asham* to *kedushat olah*.

The question Rabbi Avin is asking is whether one may now perform *temurah* with this animal. The animal is different from the original in two respects: it is a physically different animal, since the *kedusha* was transferred due to a *mum*; and it carries a different *kedusha*, since it has transformed from *asham* to *olah*. Does either or both of these differences constitute sufficient novelty such that it is no longer bound by Rabbi Yochanan's rule that one may not return and perform *temurah* again?

### Abaye's Response

Abaye challenges Rabbi Avin's framing. *Mai ka mibaya lei* — what exactly is the question? Why present a compound case that involves two distinct variables simultaneously? If the question concerns a physically different animal carrying derivative *kedusha*, that case can be asked cleanly: the original *asham* develops a *mum* and is transferred to a second animal — *shnei gufim vekedusha achas*. Does the change in physical body constitute sufficient novelty? And if the question instead concerns whether a transformation of the *kedusha* itself provides sufficient novelty, ask that case cleanly: the original *asham* itself becomes an *olah*, no second animal involved — *kedusha acheres b'oso guf*. Why conflate both issues into a single complicated scenario?

### First Lishna

In the first version of the *Gemara*'s presentation, Rabbi Avin's compound question is addressed together. Abaye's rejoinder is that Rabbi Avin should have asked each component independently. The *Gemara* suggests that if one concludes *shnei gufim vekedusha achas* does not permit a further *temurah* — because the *kedusha* is the same *kedusha* upon which *temurah* was already performed once, *mishum d'vaha kedusha itamer bah* — then the question escalates: what if it is *shnei gufim ushnei kedushos*, both a new physical animal and a transformed *kedusha*? The *Gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

### Second Lishna

A second version of the passage presents the questions separately. Rav Avin asks Rabbi Yochanan two distinct questions. First: the original *asham* develops a *mum* and the *kedusha* is transferred to a second animal — same *kedusha*, different body — *mahu sheyachzor veyamir*, may one now perform *temurah*? Second: the original *asham* is found after another has been brought in its place and the *kedusha* transforms from *asham* to *olah* — same body, different *kedusha* — *mahu sheyachzor veyamir*?

Abaye, in the second version, notes that one of these two sub-questions is more obvious than the other, and that this is what shaped Rav Avin's inquiry. The case of *guf echad b'kedusha acheres* — the same physical animal but a transformed *kedusha* — is the more serious and uncertain question, because there one can genuinely argue that the transformation of the *kedusha* from *asham* to *olah* represents a new identity. The case of a new physical animal bearing derivative *kedusha* is, by contrast, more likely to be governed by the original *kedusha*'s exhausted capacity for *temurah*, since the *kedusha* is fundamentally unchanged. If one concludes that even derivative *kedusha* in a new body is still bound by the original limitation — *kol b'oso kedusha ein chozer u'meimer* — then the more pressing question becomes whether the same body with a genuinely new *kedusha* changes the picture. The *Gemara* concludes: *teiku*. In both versions, the questions remain unresolved.

### Teiku Resolution

The *teiku* is apt given the nature of Rabbi Shimon's *derashah*. Rabbi Shimon's position is derived from the singular *hu* in *vehaya hu utmurato*. The question of how literal a reading to adopt — whether *hu* tracks the physical object, the identity of the *kedusha*, or both — is precisely what these unresolved questions probe. If one takes a maximally literal approach, *hu* refers to this specific physical body with this specific *kedusha*, and any change in either element might in principle allow a fresh *temurah*. The *Gemara* declines to resolve how far that logic extends.

## Fifth Addition

### Yehoshua Rule

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi states: *hekdesh rishon mosif chomesh, v'ein hekdesh sheni mosif chomesh* — one adds a fifth when redeeming the original sanctification, but not when redeeming a derivative one. The background: the *Torah* mandates that one who redeems something one has sanctified must add a fifth of its value. The question is whether that obligation applies when one redeems not the original sanctification but a *kedusha* that was derived from it through transfer.

### Rav Pappa

Rav Pappa explains Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi's reasoning. The verse states: *ve'im hamakdish yigal et beito, veyasaf chamishit kesef erkecha alav vekam lo* — if the one who sanctified it redeems his house, he shall add a fifth. The operative word is *hamakdish* — the one who directly sanctified. *Makdish velo matpis* — the verse speaks of the one who directly installed the *kedusha*, not the one who latched onto or transferred an existing *kedusha*. When I sanctified my property directly, I am *hamakdish* and must add a fifth upon redemption. But when the *kedusha* in question arrived at its current object through *hatfasah* — by being transferred or extended from the original sanctification — then I am merely *matpis* with respect to that object, not *makdish*, and the obligation to add a fifth does not apply.

### Asham Redemption

Rabbi Avin then applies this rule to the *asham* case. If one separated an *asham*, it developed a *mum*, one added a fifth and redeemed it onto a second animal, that second animal was then redeemed and transferred, the *asham* was brought from a third replacement, and the original *asham* — now with transformed *kedusha* from *asham* to *olah* — is to be redeemed: does one add a fifth? Here the animal has become different in both ways — it is a physically different animal from the original, and its *kedusha* has transformed from *asham* to *olah*. Perhaps because of both these changes, this should be treated as an original *kedusha* requiring the addition of a fifth rather than as a derivative one exempt from that requirement. The *Gemara* is just reaching Abaye's response to this question as the *daf* concludes, leaving the resolution for the discussion that continues into the next *amud*.
