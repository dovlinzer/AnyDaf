# Menachot 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rashi vs. Rabbeinu Tam

### Kesidran Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר רַב: הֶחְלִיף פָּרָשִׁיּוֹתֶיהָ פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says:** If **one exchanged its passages,** i.e., placed them in a different order within the compartment, the phylacteries **are unfit.**

One final issue in the *machloket* between Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam concerns the *halacha* of *kesidran* — the requirement that the *parshiyot* be written in sequential order, such that if they are written out of order they cannot be used. The question is: what defines "sequential" according to Rabbeinu Tam? Is it the order in which the *parshiyot* are placed in the *tefillin*, or the order in which they appear in the *Torah*?

### Rabbeinu Tam's Logic

Tosafot cannot imagine that there should be one order for the way you write the *parshiyot* and a different order for the way you place them. Nevertheless — and despite quoting various *gemarot* bearing on the debate — that is precisely how we *pasken* within the position of Rabbeinu Tam.

### Shel Yad Writing

For the *shel rosh*, this presents no great difficulty: you simply write the *parshiyot* in the order in which they appear in the *Torah*, and then place them in their compartments in a different order. The challenge arises with the *shel yad*, where all four *parshiyot* are written on a single *klaf*. Numbered in the order they appear in the *Torah*, they are: *Kadesh* (1), *VeHaya Ki Yeviacha* (2), *Shema* (3), and *VeHaya Im Shamoa* (4). The order in which they are placed in the *tefillin* according to Rabbeinu Tam is 1, 2, 4, 3. Yet *kesidran* requires that they be written 1, 2, 3, 4 — the *Torah* sequence. The practical consequence is striking: when writing *tefillin shel yad* according to Rabbeinu Tam, one must leave a blank space, write the fourth *parsha*, and then go back and fill in the third in the space left open, so that the writing proceeds in *Torah* order even though the placement order differs.

### Tosafot & Pasken

Tosafot finds this deeply unsatisfying and objects to the notion of divergent writing and placement orders. Yet, unusual as it is, this is indeed how we *pasken* within the position of Rabbeinu Tam. The underlying logic of Rabbeinu Tam's entire position — expressed memorably in the *She'eilot u-Teshuvot Min HaShamayim*, which frames the dispute as a debate between God and the angels — seems to be that *Shema* should appear on the outside of the *tefillin*. You do not want to bury *Shema* in the middle; it belongs at the outer position. Nevertheless, the accepted ruling follows Rashi, who holds that the order is simply the order in which the *parshiyot* are written in the *Torah*.

One might ask whether the concept of *kesidran* in the Gemara — described as *hakorei*, "the one who reads" — perhaps refers specifically to the *shel yad*, since the *shel rosh* consists of four separate compartments. For Rashi, reading them out in order would naturally correspond to how they are arranged, whereas for Rabbeinu Tam the tension between writing order and placement order makes the phrase *hakorei* a curious one indeed.

## Invalid Reversals

### Hichlif Parshiyot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא גַּוָּיְיתָא לְבָרַיְיתָא וּבָרַיְיתָא לְגַוָּיְיתָא, אֲבָל גַּוָּיְיתָא לְגַוָּיְיתָא וּבָרַיְיתָא לְבָרַיְיתָא – לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: We did not say** this unless it is a case where one exchanges **an inner** passage **for an outer** one, e.g., he placed the passage of Exodus 13:11–16 to the right of Exodus 13:1–10, **or an outer** passage **for an inner** one, e.g., he placed the passage of Deuteronomy 11:13–21 to the right of Deuteronomy 6:4–9. **But** if one exchanges **an inner** passage **for** the other **inner** one, i.e., he exchanges Exodus 13:11–16 with Deuteronomy 6:4–9, **or an outer** passage **for** the other **outer** one, i.e., he exchanges Exodus 13:1–10 with Deuteronomy 11:13–21, **we have no** problem **with it.**

The Gemara on the last two lines of 34b states: *Amar Yochanan amar Rav: hichlif parshiyoteiha pesulah* — if one reverses the order of the *parshiyot*, the *tefillin* are invalid. The order is not merely a preference; it is *me'akev*. This is also why one cannot fulfill both Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam with a single pair of *tefillin* — wearing the *parshiyot* in one order renders them invalid for the other.

### Gavayeta & Briyata

Abaye then refines this ruling. The invalidity does not apply to every possible reversal: *lo amaran ela gevayata l'vrayta u'vrayta l'gevayata* — the problem arises specifically when an inner compartment (compartment 2 or 3) is placed in an outer position (box 1 or 4), or an outer compartment is placed in an inner position. The concern is the inversion of a compartment's inner or outer designation. But *gevayata l'gevayata u'vrayta l'vrayta* — reversing two inner compartments with each other (2 and 3), or two outer compartments with each other (1 and 4) — *leit lan bah*, that is not a concern. This is, incidentally, what Yigael Yadin found at Qumran: *tefillin* structured according to the Rashi order but with the two inner compartments reversed.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: מַאי שְׁנָא גַּוָּיְיתָא לְבָרַיְיתָא וּבָרַיְיתָא לְגַוָּיְיתָא דְּלָא, דְּהָךְ דְּבָעֵי לְמִיחְזֵי אַוֵּירָא לָא קָא חָזְיָיא, וְהָא דְּלָא קָא בָעֵי לְמִיחְזֵי אַוֵּירָא קָא חָזְיָיא? בָּרַיְיתָא לְבָרַיְיתָא וְגַוָּיְיתָא לְגַוָּיְיתָא נָמֵי – הָךְ דְּבָעֲיָא לְמִיחְזֵי אַוֵּירָא דְּיָמִין קָא חָזְיָא אַוֵּירָא דִּשְׂמֹאל, וְדִשְׂמֹאל קָא חָזְיָא אַוֵּירָא דְּיָמִין! אֶלָּא לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **What is different** about the cases of exchanging **an inner** passage **for an outer** one, **and an outer** passage **for an inner** one, such **that** the phylacteries are **not** fit? The reason is **that this** passage, **which needs to see the air,** i.e., to be placed on the outer side, **does not see** it, **and that** passage, **which does not need to see the air, does see it.** But in a case where one exchanges **an outer** passage **for** the other **outer** one **or an inner** passage **for** the other **inner** one, it should be unfit **as well,** as **this** passage, **which needs to see the air of** the **right** side, **sees the air of** the **left** side, **and** that passage, which requires to see the air **of** the **left** side, **sees the air of** the **right** side. **Rather,** there **is no difference** between any of these cases, and any change in the order renders the phylacteries unfit.

Rava challenges Abaye's position. He argues: *mai shna d'yativ l'vrayta vrayta d'yativ* — why is outer-to-inner problematic? Because *d'hach d'ba'ei l'mechzi avira lo kachzi* — the compartment that needs to be exposed to the air is not being exposed, *v'hai d'lo kaba'ei l'mechzi avira kachzia* — and the one that is not supposed to be exposed is being exposed. Each compartment has a proper orientation. But if that logic holds, Rava continues, then reversing outer-for-outer and inner-for-inner should equally be a problem: *braisa l'vrayta gavizta l'gavizta nami* — these too should be invalid.

### Directional Exposure

The reason, Rava explains, is that *hach d'ba'ei l'mechzi avira d'yamin kachzi avira d'smol* — the compartment that should face the right-hand air is now facing the left-hand air, and the one meant for the left is facing the right. One cannot generalize and say "outer" and "inner" are generic categories. Every position is specific: leftmost outer, rightmost outer, leftmost inner, rightmost inner. Any change from the proper position is a change, and any change is invalid. The conclusion — *ela lo shna*, it makes no difference — is that any reversal, of any kind, renders the *tefillin* invalid.

### Historical Variations

Beyond the theoretical legal framework, this passage likely reflects a reality of actual variation in *tefillin* practice. Abaye is presumably not speaking purely in the abstract; there was evidently a degree of divergence in how *tefillin* were constructed, which also helps explain how Rashi and Rabbeinu Tam could each be pointing to different inherited traditions. Whether some of this variation may also reflect sectarian practices — given how much of the *halachot* of *tefillin* rest on *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* rather than explicit *pesukim* — remains an open and suggestive question.

## Titura & Ma'abarta

### Structure Basics

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב חֲנַנְאֵל, אָמַר רַב: תִּיתּוּרָא דִּתְפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מַעְבַּרְתָּא דִּתְפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שִׁי״ן שֶׁל תְּפִילִּין הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי.
>
> **Translation:** § **And Rav Ḥananel says** that **Rav says:** The requirement to have **the *titora* of phylacteries,** i.e., the base of phylacteries upon which the compartments rest, is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai. Abaye said:** The requirement to have **the *ma'ebarta* of phylacteries,** i.e., the passageway through which the straps are inserted, is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai. And Abaye says:** The requirement to have a letter ***shin*** protruding **on** the **phylacteries** of one's head, which is achieved by pressing the hide into the shape of that letter, is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.**

The Gemara states: *d'amar Rav Chananel amar Rav: titura d'tefillin halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — the *titura* of *tefillin* is a *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. The *titura* is the square base on which the *bayit* — the cube containing the *parshiyot* — sits. The *ma'abarta* is the extension projecting beyond the *titura* through which the strap passes, functioning like a bridge. Together these two elements form the distinctive bottom structure of the *tefillin*.

### Notching Requirement

The Gemara specifies that the *ma'abarta* must be notched in order to preserve the visual squareness of the *titura*. Without such a notch, the entire bottom would appear rectangular, and one could not simply draw an imaginary line in the mind to distinguish the two sections. A physical separation is required so that the *titura* retains its square identity as a distinct structural element, *nikar* — recognizable as such.

### One Piece Debate

Rashi and Tosafot debate whether the *titura* must be one continuous piece of leather with the *bayit*. The *bayit* is ideally not made from four separate compartments glued together but rather from a single piece of leather with four protrusions. The further question is whether that structure must be continuous with, or separately attached to, the base. Rashi holds that the *titura* must be one piece together with the *bayit*. Tosafot acknowledges that the accepted *minhag* is indeed to make everything from *or echad* — a single piece of leather — including the *ma'abarta*, achieved by cutting and shaping rather than by assembling separate pieces. In different eras, when the technology or materials did not always permit this, practices varied.

### Halacha L'Moshe Mi'Sinai

This point in the Gemara marks the first explicit introduction of the category of *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* in the discussion of *tefillin*. It is striking in retrospect that all the earlier discussion — the order of the *parshiyot*, the logic of inner and outer, the reasoning through *sevara* — did not invoke this designation. Yet now a string of physical structural features are declared *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* in rapid succession. Abaye adds: *ma'abarta d'tefillin halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. And further: *shin shel tefillin halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — the *shin* embossed on the outside of the *shel rosh* is likewise *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*.

This raises a broader question about the nature of the designation itself. For people who have studied the laws of *tefillin*, *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* appears constantly — it seems to cover virtually everything. But there is an important distinction at work. Some details are derived from *pesukim*, even if *sevara* is needed to work out the specifics. The four *parshiyot*, for instance, can be anchored in the fact that the *Torah* commands *tefillin* four times and in the *drasha* on the word *totafot* — *tat* (two in one language) plus *pat* (two in another) equals four — yielding four compartments. For items of this kind, we do not say *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* because there is at least a *pasuk* to hang the ruling on, however much *sevara* fills in the gaps. But for elements like the *titura*, the *ma'abarta*, the *shin*, and the squareness — where there is no *pasuk* that can even serve as a starting point — the ruling is simply declared *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*.

There is also something intuitively compelling about why *tefillin* in particular should accumulate so many *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* designations. Unlike an abstract *halacha* such as the definition of *melacha* on *Shabbat*, where *Torah she-ba-al Peh* develops interpretive categories through a creative process, *tefillin* describes a specific physical object. If the *Torah* commands you to bind something as a sign on your arm and between your eyes, it almost certainly had a specific physical referent in mind. The Rambam makes a similar point regarding the *arbaah minim*: the *pesukim* that discuss *peri etz hadar* were always accompanied by a *mesorah* identifying exactly which fruit was meant, and the derivations were at most *ex post facto* confirmation of an already-transmitted tradition. The same intuition applies to *tefillin*: the physical object the *Torah* described was always known, transmitted from father to son through the actual craft of making *tefillin*, and the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* label captures that the specific physical details were never up for interpretive debate.

## Compartment Furrow

### Furrow to Base

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּגִּיעַ חָרִיץ לִמְקוֹם הַתֶּפֶר, רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא אָמַר: כֵּיוָן דְּמִנְּכַר, לָא צְרִיךְ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye further says: **And there is a requirement that** the **furrow** between each of the compartments of phylacteries of the head **reach the place of the stitches,** i.e., the *titora*, to which the compartments are sewn. **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a says: Once it is noticeable** that there is a furrow between each of the compartments, **it is not necessary** for them to reach all the way to the *titora*.

The Gemara continues with the requirement: *v'tzarikh sheyagiya kharitz limkom hatefer* — the *charitz*, the furrow between the compartments of the *shel rosh*, must reach the place of sewing. There are two ways to understand this. One reading is that the tip of the *shin* must reach down to the actual base, to the place where the *bayit* contacts the *titura* and is sewn or threaded. The other reading — and likely the more straightforward one — takes *charitz* literally as the furrow running between each compartment. The *shel rosh* is made from a single piece of leather shaped into four distinct protrusions, separated by these furrows. The requirement is that those furrows extend all the way down to the base of the *titura*, ensuring that the compartments are truly and fully separated — not merely indicated at the top but continuous as genuine individual chambers all the way to the foundation.

This must be understood in context of how *tefillin* are actually constructed. The *bayit* is essentially formed like a glove from within, with the compartments pushed up and the furrows pressed down between them. The resulting structure, viewed from the outside, shows only slight visible lines between compartments; the depth of those furrows is an internal structural matter. Nevertheless, Abaye rules that genuine separation must be achieved all the way to the *titura* itself. The compartments must also have some visible separation at the top — they must be visibly distinct — but full structural separation reaching to the base is required.

### Visual Separation

Rav Dimi of Nardea disagrees: *keivan de-minchar lo tzarich* — once it is evident that they are separate compartments, nothing further is required. Even if the furrow does not penetrate all the way to the base, as long as the compartments appear sufficiently separate at the top, the *tefillin* are valid. On this view, the practical construction of *tefillin* — in which the furrows are pressed down but may not fully reach the very bottom — is adequate, provided the separateness of the compartments is visually recognizable.

## Klaf Inspection

### Pre-writing Check

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי קִילְפָּא דִּתְפִילִּין צָרִיךְ לְמִיבְדְּקֵיהּ, דְּדִילְמָא אִית בַּהּ רֵיעוּתָא, וּבָעֵינָא כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה וְלֵיכָּא. רַב דִּימִי מִנְּהַרְדְּעָא אָמַר: לָא צְרִיךְ, קוּלְמוֹסָא בָּדֵיק לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye says:** With regard to **this parchment** upon which one writes the passages **of phylacteries,** the scribe **must examine it** before writing, **as perhaps it has a flaw,** i.e., a perforation, **and complete writing is required, and** that requirement would **not** be fulfilled if a letter were perforated. **Rav Dimi of Neharde'a says: No** prior examination is **required;** rather, **the quill examines it** as one writes, as any perforation which the ink covers is disregarded.

Abaye states: *hai klapha de-tefillin tzarich le-mivdakei de-dilma is bei re'usa* — the *klaf* for *tefillin* must be checked before writing, lest there be a tiny flaw. If a scribe writes a letter and a small hole happens to fall adjacent to it, the letter will not be *mukaf gvil* — fully surrounded by parchment on all sides — and the writing will be invalid.

The stakes here are particularly high because of the requirement of *kesidran*. If one *parsha* is found to be *pasul*, all four *parshiyot* must effectively be redone. Each *parsha* must be written *kasidran* relative to those before and after it in sequence. If, say, *parsha* number 3 of the *shel rosh* is defective, a replacement can only be used if it was written after *parsha* 2 and before *parsha* 4 of that same *tefillin* — a condition that is practically almost impossible to satisfy after the fact. For this reason the Gemara requires: *u-be'inan ketivah tama* — we need a perfect, complete writing — *mukaf gvil*, and a pre-inspection of the *klaf* is the means to secure that.

The point extends to the broader question of why the order of writing matters as opposed to merely the order of placement. Just as one would not regard *tefillin* written in reverse sequence as valid simply because the physical end product looks correct, so too the integrity of the *klaf* itself must be confirmed in advance, because any flaw discovered later triggers the *kesidran* problem in full.

### Quill Detection

Rav Dimi of Nardea offers an alternative: *lo tzarich; kulmosa badik leh* — no prior inspection is needed, because the quill itself will detect any imperfection. As the scribe writes, if the quill encounters a bump or inconsistency in the surface of the *klaf*, he will feel it and know to stop. Pre-inspection is therefore unnecessary according to this view, as the writing process itself provides a built-in form of detection.

## Black Straps Halacha

### Black = HLM

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: רְצוּעוֹת שְׁחוֹרוֹת הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. מֵיתִיבִי: תְּפִילִּין אֵין קוֹשְׁרִין אוֹתָן אֶלָּא בְּמִינָן, בֵּין יְרוּקּוֹת בֵּין שְׁחוֹרוֹת בֵּין לְבָנוֹת, אֲדוּמּוֹת לֹא יַעֲשֶׂה, מִפְּנֵי גְּנַאי וְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** The requirement that the **straps** of the phylacteries be **black** is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **One may tie phylacteries only** with straps **of their** same **type,** i.e., the straps must be made from hide, and it does not matter **whether** they are **green, or black, or white.** Nevertheless, **one should not make red** straps, **because** this is **deprecatory** to him, as it looks like he has wounds on his head, **and** also due to **something else,** i.e., lest people suspect him of engaging in sexual intercourse with a menstruating woman and getting blood on the straps.

Rav Yitzchak states: *retzu'os shechoros halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — black *retzuot* are a *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. This is immediately challenged.

### Color Permissibility

A *baraisa* is cited: *tefillin ein koshrin oso ela be-mino* — the *keshira* of *tefillin* may only be done with something of their own kind, meaning the straps must be leather. But as to color — *bein yerokos bein shechoros bein levanos* — whether green, black, or white, any color is acceptable, as long as the material is leather. One exception is stated explicitly: *adumos lo ya'aseh* — do not make red straps. Two reasons are given: *mipnei gnai ve-davar acher* — because it is unseemly and because of something else left unspoken. Rashi explains the *gnai*: red straps might lead observers to think the *tefillin* rubbed against scabs and became bloodied, which is repulsive in appearance. As for the *davar acher*, Rashi suggests — admittedly fancifully — that people might assume the red came from contact with a *niddah* during a prohibited intimacy. These are the explanations Rashi offers, however far-fetched they may seem.

### Blue Strings Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַעֲשֶׂה בְּתַלְמִידוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא שֶׁהָיָה קוֹשֵׁר תְּפִילָּיו בִּלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל תְּכֵלֶת, וְלָא אָמַר לוֹ דָּבָר. אִיפְשָׁר אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק רָאָה תַּלְמִידוֹ וְלֹא מִיחָה בּוֹ? אָמַר לוֹ: הֵן, לֹא רָאָה אוֹתוֹ, וְאִם רָאָה אוֹתוֹ לֹא הָיָה מַנִּיחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites the continuation of that *baraita*. **Rabbi Yehuda said:** There was **an incident involving Rabbi Akiva's student, who would tie his phylacteries with strips of sky**-**blue wool** rather than hide, **and** Rabbi Akiva **did not say anything to him.** Is it **possible** that **that righteous man saw his student** doing something improper **and he did not object to his** conduct? Another Sage **said to** Rabbi Yehuda: **Yes,** it is possible that the student acted improperly, as Rabbi Akiva **did not see him, and if he had seen him, he would not have allowed him** to do so.

Rabbi Yehuda continues with a story: *maaseh b'talmido shel Rabbi Akiva she'haya kosher tefillav b'lashon shel techeiles* — there was an incident involving a student of Rabbi Akiva who would tie his *tefillin* not with leather straps but with blue strings. This is remarkable: we take the leather strap as so foundational to *tefillin* that it barely seems worth mentioning, yet here is a student of one of the greatest *Tannaim* using something else entirely.

*V'lo amar lo davar* — and Rabbi Akiva said nothing. An anonymous voice in the *baraisa* challenges this: *efshar oso tzaddik ra'ah talmido v'lo micha bo?* — is it possible that such a *tzaddik* saw his student doing this and did not protest? Rabbi Yehuda — or perhaps Rabbi Yehuda responding to that challenge — replies: *hein, lo ra'ahu* — yes, you are right, he did not actually see him doing it. *V'im ra'ahu, lo haya manicho* — had he seen it, he would not have permitted it.

### Red Strings Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַעֲשֶׂה בְּהוּרְקָנוֹס בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן הוּרְקָנוֹס, שֶׁהָיָה קוֹשֵׁר תְּפִילָּיו בִּלְשׁוֹנוֹת שֶׁל אַרְגָּמָן, וְלֹא אָמַר לוֹ דָּבָר. אִיפְשָׁר אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק רָאָה בְּנוֹ וְלֹא מִיחָה בּוֹ? אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֵן, לֹא רָאָה אוֹתוֹ, וְאִם רָאָה אוֹתוֹ לֹא הָיָה מַנִּיחוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: There was **an incident involving Hyrcanus, the son of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hyrcanus, who would tie his phylacteries with strips of purple wool, and** his father **did not say anything to him.** Is it **possible** that **that righteous man saw his son** doing something improper **and he did not object to his** conduct? The Sages **said to him: Yes,** it is possible that his son acted improperly, as Rabbi Eliezer **did not see him, and if he had seen him, he would not have allowed him** to do so. This concludes the *baraita*.

A parallel case follows: *maaseh b'Hurkenos beno shel Rabbi Eliezer ben Hurkenos she'haya kosher tefillav b'lashon shel argaman* — Hurkenos the son of Rabbi Eliezer ben Hurkenos would tie his *tefillin* with *argaman*, purple-red strings. Again, *v'lo amar lo davar* — his father said nothing. Again the challenge: *efshar oso tzaddik ra'ah beno v'lo micha bo?* — could such a righteous man see his own son doing this and not object? And again the reply: *lo ra'ahu* — he did not see it; *v'im ra'ahu, lo haya manicho* — had he seen it, he would not have permitted it.

What is striking about these two stories is not the legal resolution — which is clear, that leather is required — but what they imply about historical practice. The Gemara is not denying that these things actually happened. The only question is whether the great *Tannaim* in question were aware. Presumably they were not, or they would have intervened. But the stories suggest that divergent practices regarding *tefillin* straps were genuinely occurring. One possible explanation for why the fathers did not notice: if blue and purple could plausibly be mistaken for leather straps in certain light, perhaps they saw the *tefillin* from a distance and assumed the straps were simply dark-colored leather.

One might further ask whether some of this variation reflects sectarian practices — particularly given how much of *tefillin* law rests on *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* rather than explicit *pesukim*. Karaites and others who diverged from rabbinic *tefillin* traditions come to mind. The phrase *efshar oso tzaddik ra'ah v'lo micha bo* carries an urgency that seems to go beyond a simple halachic slip, as though something of greater religious consequence is at stake. This remains speculative, but the *gemara* is suggestive in this regard.

### Inner/Outer Color

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי מִיהָא, בֵּין יְרוּקּוֹת בֵּין שְׁחוֹרוֹת וּבֵין לְבָנוֹת. לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן – מִבִּפְנִים, כָּאן – מִבַּחוּץ. אִי מִבִּפְנִים, מַאי גְּנַאי וְדָבָר אַחֵר אִיכָּא? זִימְנִין דְּמִתְהַפְכִין לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection from the *baraita*: **In any event,** the *baraita* **teaches** that it does not matter **whether** the straps are **green, or black, or white,** whereas Rabbi Yitzḥak maintains that it is a *halakha* transmitted to Moses from Sinai that the straps of the phylacteries must be black. The Gemara answers that it is **not difficult. Here,** the *baraita* is referring to **the inside** of the straps, which touch the body. These may be any color other than red. Conversely, **there,** when Rabbi Yitzḥak says that the straps of the phylacteries must be black, he is speaking of **the outside** of the straps. The Gemara raises a difficulty: **If** the *baraita* is discussing **the inside** of the straps, **what deprecatory** matter **or** problem of **something else is there** with straps that are red on the inside? After all, this side is not seen. The Gemara answers: **Sometimes his** straps **become reversed,** and therefore these concerns are applicable.

Having established that the *baraisa* permits multiple colors, the Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction with Rav Yitzchak's statement: *lo kashya; kan mibifnim kan mibachutz* — there is no difficulty. The requirement of black applies to the outer surface of the strap — the side facing away from the body. The inner surface, which rests against the skin, may be any color. This resolution also provides context for why red is specifically prohibited: although one might think that the *gnai* applies only to the inner side (since only there could blood from scabs accumulate), the Gemara notes that *zimnin d'mithapchan leh* — sometimes the strap turns around. Anyone who has worn a *shel rosh* knows that this happens. Therefore red is prohibited even on the inner side, because it may become exposed.

### Black Box Mitzva

The question naturally arises: if black *retzuot* are *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, surely the *bayit* itself must equally require black? In fact, the *Shulchan Aruch* treats these two requirements differently. Regarding the *retzuot*, it states explicitly that they must be black on the outside as a *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. But regarding the *batim* themselves, the *Shulchan Aruch* formulates it differently: *or habatim mitzvah la'asoto shachor* — it is a *mitzvah* to make the *bayit* black. Not *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, but *mitzvah*. And the *Mishnah Berurah* notes: *mashma d'bedi'eved eino me'akev* — this implies that after the fact, black *batim* are not strictly indispensable. For those who wear *tefillin* daily and assume every detail is equally *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, this is a genuinely surprising ruling.

### White Boxes

Tosafot drives the point home explicitly: *yesh she'osim batim shel tefillin miklaf lavan velo ba'ei shachrut ela retzuot* — there were those who made the *batim* of *tefillin* from white *klaf* and required black only for the *retzuot*. This was not a fringe or uninformed position; it reflects the halachic logic that only the *retzuot* are subject to the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* of black coloring.

The practical consequence is worth noting: because the black of the *retzuot* is *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, if the outer surface of a strap becomes worn and loses its black color, it must be corrected immediately. The *bayit*, being only a *mitzvah* rather than *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*, affords somewhat more latitude. As a practical matter, the blackening of *tefillin* can be accomplished with ordinary black paint available at any *tefillin* store — or even, in a pinch, with a permanent marker, though one should be mindful that any substance used ideally have *hazmana* for the *mitzvah*, and there is the additional question of whether the dye or ink must be made from kosher materials.

## Squareness Requirement

### Square HLM

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנָא: תְּפִילִּין מְרוּבָּעוֹת – הֲלָכָה לְמֹשֶׁה מִסִּינַי. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: בְּתִפְרָן וּבַאֲלַכְסוֹנָן.
>
> **Translation:** It is **taught** in a *baraita*: The requirement that **phylacteries** be **square** is **a *halakha*** transmitted **to Moses from Sinai. Rav Pappa says** about this *halakha*: Square means **along their seams and their diagonals,** i.e., they must be perfectly square where the compartments are sewn to the *titora*.

The Gemara cites a *baraisa*: *tefillin meruba'ot halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* — the squareness of *tefillin* is a *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai*. This applies to the *bayit*, the cube itself.

### Diagonal Measurement

Rav Papa refines this: *bitfuran u'be'alchsonan* — squareness is required both in their sewing and in their diagonal. These are two distinct requirements. The sewing requirement means that when the *tefillin* is stitched shut — and we have seen from the pictures that both the compartments and the base involve substantial threading and stitching — the process of sewing must not stretch or distort the shape away from the square. The stitches must be worked carefully so that the tension they create does not pull the *bayit* out of square.

The second requirement — *alchsonan*, the diagonal — refers to how squareness is measured and verified. Rather than simply measuring two adjacent sides and confirming they are equal, squareness is determined by checking the diagonal.

### Math of Diagonal

The Gemara employs the well-known approximation that the diagonal of a unit square is 1.4 — a rough rounding of the square root of 2 (which is 1.414...). This approximation appears in various places in the Talmud and is considerably more accurate than the Talmudic approximation of *pi*. The significance of measuring by diagonal is that it provides a practical standard for what counts as "square enough." Rather than requiring a geometrically perfect square — which would demand measuring side lengths to an impossibly fine degree of precision — the standard is that if the diagonal of the *tefillin* measures approximately 1.4 times the side, it satisfies the requirement. This becomes an important model for understanding *halachic* tolerance more broadly: *lo nitna torah lemalachei hasharet*, and squareness in this context means "square within a defined margin of error," with the diagonal serving as the operational test for that margin.

### Circular Danger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: הָעוֹשֶׂה תְּפִילָּתוֹ עֲגוּלָּה – סַכָּנָה וְאֵין בָּהּ מִצְוָה. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: מַתְנִיתִין דַּעֲבִידָא כִּי אַמְגּוּזָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that a mishna **supports** this opinion (*Megilla* 24b): **One who fashions his phylacteries** in **a round** shape exposes himself to **danger, and it** does **not** enable him to fulfill the **mitzva** of phylacteries. **Rav Pappa said:** This is no support, as one can say that **the mishna** is referring to phylacteries **that are fashioned like a nut,** i.e., their underside is rounded, and therefore there is a danger that if he strikes his head on a wall the underside will press into his head and injure him. By contrast, if the underside is flat one might have thought that it is fit despite the fact that it is not square. Therefore, the *baraita* teaches that phylacteries must be square.

The Gemara attempts to bring a proof that squareness is required: *ha'oseh tefillato agula sakana ve'ein bah mitzvah* — one who makes his *tefillin* circular (or more precisely, spherical) faces a danger and has performed no *mitzvah*. The danger is geometrical: a sphere rests on a point. Whereas a flat-bottomed *bayit* distributes pressure across its surface, a spherical *tefillin* placed on the head creates a concentrated point of contact. If one were to walk into a wall or a doorframe — and this is not entirely hypothetical for anyone who has worn a *shel rosh* while navigating tight spaces — a spherical *tefillin* would drive that point into the skull rather than distributing the impact. Hence the *sakana*. The absence of *mitzvah* follows from the lack of squareness. The *gemara* therefore appears to demonstrate that squareness is a genuine requirement.

### Papa's Objection

Rav Papa objects: *matnitin de'avida ke'egoz* — the *baraisa* is speaking of a nut-shaped *tefillin*, i.e., something ellipsoidal or ovoid that truly comes to a sharp point. That shape creates an acute danger precisely because of its pointed geometry. But one cannot conclude from this that *any* non-square shape is unacceptable. There might be other shapes — not square, but also not nut-shaped — that pose no danger and might still satisfy some form of the requirement. The proof from circular *tefillin* therefore does not conclusively establish that squareness is strictly required beyond the *halacha l'Moshe mi'Sinai* declaration.

This exchange is characteristic of a broader pattern in this section of the Gemara: things that seem intuitively obvious and beyond dispute — the squareness of *tefillin*, the blackness of the *batim* — turn out upon examination to be more nuanced, and the Gemara itself questions whether proofs establish what they appear to establish. One might also note that the requirement as stated — *meruba'ot* — could refer to being square in cross-section, but whether this entails that the *tefillin* must also be a perfect cube (height equal to width equal to depth) is a separate question not fully resolved here.

## Wall Integrity

### Outer Face Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: תְּפִילִּין, כָּל זְמַן שֶׁפְּנֵי טַבְלָא קַיֶּימֶת – כְּשֵׁירוֹת. רַב חִסְדָּא אָמַר: נִפְסְקוּ שְׁתַּיִם – כְּשֵׁירוֹת, שָׁלֹשׁ – פְּסוּלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Huna says:** With regard to **phylacteries** of the head, **as long as the surface of outer hide is intact,** i.e., it is not torn, **they are fit,** even if the hide between the compartments has torn. **Rav Ḥisda says:** If **two** of the walls between the compartments **tore,** such phylacteries **are fit,** but if **three** of these inner walls tore, **they are unfit.**

The Gemara turns to the internal structural integrity of the *shel rosh*. Rav Huna rules: *tefillin kol zman she'pnei tavla kayemes kesheira* — *tefillin* are valid as long as the outer face remains intact. Even if the inner partition walls between the compartments develop holes or breaks, as long as the exterior surface of the *bayit* is undamaged, the *tefillin* remain *kasher*.

### Three Walls Rule

Rav Chisda qualifies this. The *shel rosh* has four compartments separated by three internal walls. Rav Chisda rules that if up to two of those inner walls are broken, the *tefillin* remain valid. But if all three inner walls are broken — *shalosh pesulah* — it is invalid. The logic is straightforward: if all three walls are gone, there are no longer four compartments. The whole structure collapses into a single *bayit*, and the fundamental requirement of four distinct compartments is lost.

### Opposite Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָא דְּאָמְרַתְּ שְׁתַּיִם כְּשֵׁירוֹת, לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא זֶה שֶׁלֹּא כְּנֶגֶד זֶה, אֲבָל זֶה כְּנֶגֶד זֶה – פְּסוּלוֹת. וְזֶה כְּנֶגֶד זֶה נָמֵי לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּחַדְתָּתָא, אֲבָל בְּעַתִּיקָתָא לֵית לַן בַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Concerning **that which you said,** that if **two** of the walls between the compartments tore **they are fit, we said** this **only** if the inner walls that tore are **not aligned with each other,** i.e., they are not adjacent to one another. **But** if the torn walls are **aligned with each other,** the phylacteries **are unfit. And** furthermore, **even** in a case where the torn walls are **aligned with each other, we said** that they are unfit **only with regard to new** phylacteries, as the hide is certainly defective. **But with regard to old** phylacteries, **we have no** problem **with it,** as they tore due to aging.

Rava further refines Rav Chisda's ruling. Even where only two walls are broken and the *tefillin* would otherwise be *kasher*, this leniency applies only when the breaks are *zeh shelo keneged zeh* — not directly opposite one another. Imagine the four compartments arranged in a row with three walls between them. If wall 1 (between compartments 1 and 2) has a hole, and wall 2 (between compartments 2 and 3) has a hole, but those holes are at different heights — one high, one low — then compartment 2 is not fully compromised, because neither hole alone traverses it completely. But if the hole in wall 1 and the hole in wall 2 are directly opposite one another — *zeh keneged zeh* — then effectively compartment 2 has no wall on either side at that point, and the structural separation is lost. In that case, *pesulah*. Rashi offers an alternative reading of *zeh keneged zeh*: if the holes are at the same location within their respective walls — both near the bottom, or both near the top — as opposed to one at the top and one at the bottom. Either way, the principle is that the position of the holes matters, not just their number.

One might also ask: does the size of the hole matter? A pinhole and a gaping break are presumably not equivalent. This too is an open question bearing on when a hole is sufficient to be considered a structural breach.

### New vs. Old

The Gemara adds a further qualification: *ela be'chadta* — this entire discussion of opposite holes causing *pesul* applies specifically to new *tefillin*. For old *tefillin* — *be'atika leit lan bah* — we do not apply these concerns.

The analogy offered is that of a new car: the smallest scratch on a brand-new vehicle is conspicuous and significant, whereas the same scratch on a car owned for years barely registers. But the Gemara's point here has an additional dimension: with new *tefillin*, holes that appear in the leather signal poor quality material. If *tefillin* are newly made and the leather is already cracking or perforating, this is an indictment of the quality of the leather itself — comparable to discovering a serious defect in a product shortly after purchase — and that in turn calls into question the validity of the entire item. With old *tefillin*, wear and minor damage are expected, and isolated holes do not carry the same implications.

### Elasticity Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב יוֹסֵף: הֵיכִי דָּמְיָין חַדְתָּתָא, וְהֵיכִי דָּמְיָין עַתִּיקָתָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כֹּל הֵיכָא כִּי מִיתְּלֵי בֵּיהּ בְּשִׁלְחָא וַהֲדַר חָלֵים – עַתִּיקָתָא, וְאִידַּךְ – חַדְתָּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Yosef: What are the circumstances** of **new** phylacteries, **and what are the circumstances** of **old** phylacteries? Rav Yosef **said to him:** In **any** case **where if one holds the hide and pulls it, it returns to its place [*ḥaleim*],** these phylacteries are considered **old; and** in **the other** case, where hide that was pulled does not return to its place, they are considered **new.**

Abaye asks Rav Yosef: *heichi dami chadta ve'heichi dami atika?* — what exactly defines "new" and "old" in this context? Rav Yosef replies: *kol heicha de'ki matu lei be'shicha ve'hadar chalim atika* — if you stretch the leather and it recovers, pulling back to its original shape, it is classified as old. *Ve'idach chadta* — and the other kind is new. The logic is that leather which has been worn and used over time has already been stretched and conformed to shape; it has reached a stable equilibrium. If you pull it, it does not take a new shape but returns to what it was. Fresh, new leather, by contrast, has not yet been worked in; stretching it will deform it and it will not simply spring back. This elasticity — or lack thereof — serves as the practical diagnostic for determining whether *tefillin* are to be treated as new or old for purposes of the wall-integrity *halacha*.

A variant formulation is also cited: *kol heicha de'ki matu lei be'masna asya basra chadta* — if when you pull the leather it stretches along with you, following the pull and taking a new shape, that is new leather. *Ve'idach atika* — and the other, which does not follow the stretch but returns, is old. Rashi's several readings of this passage converge on the same underlying principle: new leather is deformable under stress, while old leather has settled and resists deformation.
