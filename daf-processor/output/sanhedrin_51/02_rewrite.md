# Sanhedrin 51 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Burning of Kohen's Daugh…

### Overview & Context

Today's daf, 51a, continues in the middle of the broader debate between the *rabbanan* and Rabbi Shimon concerning the ordering of the four court-mandated executions (*misos beis din*). The nexus of that debate, as we have seen, is the question of under what circumstances the daughter of a *kohen* who commits adultery receives the punishment of *sreifah* (burning). Which situations trigger *sreifah* — and why — illuminates how the various executions are ranked relative to one another, and so this question becomes the central battleground of the dispute. Today's daf focuses almost entirely on this issue of the *bat kohen*'s burning, not merely as a vehicle for understanding the order of executions, but as a subject worthy of thorough analysis in its own right.

## Baraita Text & Drasha

### Baraita Setup

We pick up from the top of 51a in the middle of a *baraita* that was introduced on the previous daf. The *baraita* opens with the phrase *ein li ela shenisuah l'kohen* — I know only that she is married to a *kohen*. The premise here is that we already established from the word *liznot* in the verse that the text is discussing a forbidden sexual act, not some other form of desecration. We likewise established from the word *aviha* — through a linkage to the case of the *na'arah hameurasah* — that the woman must be married. The question now becomes: to whom must she be married for the punishment of *sreifah* to apply?

The *baraita* asks how we know the law extends beyond a woman married to a *kohen* — including a woman married to a *levi*, a plain *yisrael*, an *oved kochavim*, a *chalal*, a *mamzer*, or a *nesin*. These represent a wide spectrum of marital situations. A marriage to a non-Jew (*oved kochavim*) is, as the *gemara* itself acknowledges without fully unpacking here, a case that presents particular difficulty, since halachah does not ordinarily treat such a union as a binding marriage — and if it is not binding, intercourse with another man would not technically constitute adultery. The *gemara* does not resolve this here. The *chalal* — for instance, the son of a *kohen* and a *gerushah* — represents a permissible marriage for the *bat kohen*, though it renders her a *chalalah* in the process. The *mamzer* and *nesin* represent unions that are halachically forbidden and yet binding.

The *baraita* derives that the law applies in all these cases from the phrase *u'vat ish kohen* — the daughter of a man who is a *kohen* — rather than identifying her as a *kohenet* in her own right. From this the *baraita* infers *af al pi she'eina kohenet*: even if something has acted to compromise her own *kehunah* status, she is still the daughter of a *kohen*, still subject to the unique desecration that her act represents, and therefore still subject to *sreifah*.

### Drasha on 'Hi'

The verse reads: *u'vat ish kohen ki teichel liznot et aviha hi mechalelet ba'esh tisaref* — the daughter of a *kohen* who commits an act of *znus* desecrates her father; she shall be burned in fire. The *baraita* derives from the word *hi* — "she" — that the *sreifah* is specific to her. *Hi bisreifah v'ein bo'aleha bisreifah*: the man who commits adultery with her does not receive *sreifah*. His sin is adultery in the ordinary sense, punishable by *chenek* (strangulation), or if she is a *na'arah hameurasah*, by *skilah* (stoning) — but in neither case by *sreifah*. The burning is the consequence not of the act of adultery as such, but of what she has done through that act: she has desecrated her father's house, brought shame upon her *kehunah* lineage. Though the man was the instrument through whom she did this, the desecration belongs uniquely to her. That is the force of *hi*.

### Edim Zomemim Exception

From the same word *hi*, the *baraita* further derives *v'ein zomemeha bisreifah*: conspiring witnesses (*edim zomemim*) who falsely testified that she committed this act do not receive *sreifah* either. This is striking, because the rule of *edim zomemim* ordinarily requires that the false witnesses receive precisely the punishment they sought to have inflicted on the accused — even in cases where that punishment could never technically apply to the witnesses themselves. Yet here, this is the one case where the *edim zomemim* do not receive the punishment of the accused.

The reason appears to be that *sreifah* here is not simply the punishment for adultery as a category — it is the punishment for this woman's specific act of desecrating her father's priestly household. Even in a case where *edim zomemim* could be treated as if they had committed a sin they never actually performed, there is no way to treat these male witnesses as if they brought shame upon a *kohen*'s house through an act of adultery. The desecration of the *kehunah* household is something structurally unique to a *bat kohen*. One might push back slightly: is it gender that creates the exception, or the *kehunah* status? Strictly speaking, it is a combination — gender alone is not sufficient, since a non-*kohen* woman committing adultery would not receive *sreifah* at all, and if *edim zomemim* testified falsely against such a woman their punishment would follow hers. But when the *kehunah* family status is layered atop the gendered role of this particular woman, the punishment for the witnesses cannot follow suit. However unusual this exception is in the overall framework of *edim zomemim*, the *gemara* acknowledges it as genuinely anomalous.

### Rabbi Eliezer's Statement

The *baraita* concludes with the enigmatic statement of Rabbi Eliezer: *et aviha bisreifah v'et chamiha biskilah* — with her father it is *sreifah*, with her father-in-law it is *skilah*. This is a curious formulation that the *gemara* will subject to sustained scrutiny. For now we simply note it and move on to the unpacking of the *baraita* as a whole.

## Excluding Alternatives

### Shabbat Violation

Having quoted the full *baraita*, the *gemara* now proceeds *amar mar* — that is, it goes back to examine the text piece by piece. (It is worth noting the distinction between *amar mar* and *gufa*: *amar mar* is used when the full text of a *baraita* has already been quoted and the *gemara* now unpacks it line by line; *gufa* is used when only an excerpt has been quoted and the *gemara* wishes to return to the whole *baraita*.)

The first phrase examined is *yachol afilu chillul Shabbos* — perhaps the verse applies even to a *bat kohen* who desecrates Shabbat. After all, the root *techal* in *ki teichel liznot* could be read as *challel*, desecrate, and the verse also says *et aviha hi mechalelet*, she desecrates her father — so perhaps the theme of desecration extends to Shabbat violation. Setting aside for a moment the word *liznot*, which obviously rules it out, the *gemara* entertains what the *hava amina* might have been.

The response is that *chillul Shabbos* is punishable by *skilah* — and the question is immediately raised: how could one ever have thought that *chillul Shabbos* would bring *sreifah*? The answer is that this *baraita* follows the view of Rabbi Shimon, who holds *sreifah chamurah* — that *sreifah* is a more severe punishment than *skilah*. Within that framework, a theoretical argument could be constructed: since the Torah is particularly stringent with *kohanim*, burdening them with many additional *mitzvos* and prohibitions (*derabi behu mitzvos yeseros*), perhaps a *bat kohen* who violates Shabbat should be punished at an elevated level.

The *gemara* then refines this *hava amina* with a further wrinkle. One might have thought that specifically the *bat kohen* — rather than the male *kohen* himself — would receive *sreifah* for Shabbat desecration. Why not the male *kohen*? Because male *kohanim* are themselves obligated to perform the *avodah* in the *beis hamikdash*, which involves overriding Shabbat. The Torah already permits Shabbat violation for the *kohen*'s sacred service, so the Torah would presumably not impose the most severe punishment on him for Shabbat desecration. But the *bat kohen* has no such Shabbat override — she is burdened by the weight of her family's elevated standard without the corresponding allowance. So perhaps she receives *sreifah*. This is immediately dismissed — *ka mashma lan* that she does not, because the verse says *liznot*, referring exclusively to forbidden sexual activity.

### Unmarried Status

The *gemara* next asks: *yachol afilu pnuyah* — perhaps the verse applies even to an unmarried woman. If *liznot* means something like general promiscuity or loose sexual behavior, maybe the *bat kohen* need not be married at all for the verse to apply. The *gemara* rejects this: the word *aviha* in this verse links to its usage by the *na'arah hameurasah*, making clear that the woman is in the state of marriage. The *hava amina*, then, is that the word *liznot* might refer to sexual activity in a broader sense that does not require a state of marriage.

### Znus Definition

This leads directly into a rich discussion of what *znus* means. In common usage, *zonah* and *znus* do not immediately connote adultery — they suggest promiscuity or prostitution more generally, not specifically intercourse that violates a marital bond. Yet the *gemara* initially proceeds on the assumption that *liznot* implies some form of *issur* — a forbidden sexual act — not merely sex outside of marriage.

Here it is worth digressing briefly on the two biblical terms for a prostitute: *zonah* and *kedeshah*. The conventional explanation of *kedeshah*, following Rashi and others, is that it means "designated" — one set apart for the role of hired sex. But an alternative reading, developed by the commentator Robert Alter in his analysis of the story of Judah and Tamar, proposes that the two terms are actually distinct in kind: *zonah* denotes an ordinary prostitute, while *kedeshah* refers to a cult prostitute — one who performs sexual acts in the context of religious temple ritual. This reading elegantly explains the textual details: when Judah sees Tamar, he takes her for a *zonah*, and later, when his friend is sent to retrieve the pledge, he asks around for a *kedeshah* — a more respectable category of woman — precisely to avoid the embarrassment of publicly announcing that Judah had consorted with an ordinary prostitute. The locals deny any knowledge of a *kedeshah* in the area, which is why she cannot be found. This is an illuminating piece of biblical philology, though its direct halachic relevance here is tangential.

What is halachically central is the position of Rabbi Eliezer, which the *gemara* now introduces as the basis for the *hava amina* that the verse could apply to a *pnuyah*. Rabbi Eliezer holds that *panui haba al pnuyah* — even a single man who has intercourse with a single woman, where no marital bond exists on either side and there is no technical *issur* — if they do so *she'lo l'shem ishus*, without the context of marriage, *asa'ah zonah*, he has made her into a *zonah* in the sense relevant to the prohibition on a *kohen* marrying a *zonah* (*ishah zonah vechalalah lo yikachu*). According to Rabbi Eliezer, *znus* encompasses all sex outside of marriage, not only sex that violates a specific prohibition.

This contrasts with the normative *halacha* as formulated, for example, by the Rambam: a *zonah* in the technical sense is a woman who has had intercourse with a man she is prohibited to marry. Sex while a *niddah*, for instance, does not create *zonah* status, since the prohibition is not about the identity of the man but about her ritual state. But any act of sex with a man she is forbidden to marry renders her a *zonah*. Rabbi Eliezer's broader definition would encompass even sex between two entirely unmarried and otherwise permitted individuals.

The *gemara* explains that the *hava amina* behind *yachol afilu pnuyah* was constructed following the view of Rabbi Eliezer: since *liznot* means, according to Rabbi Eliezer, any sex outside of marriage, the verse might apply even to an unmarried *bat kohen* engaged in casual promiscuity. The *gemara* then answers that *beis aviha* — through its parallel to the *na'arah hameurasah* context — establishes that the woman must be married, and thus *liznot* here must refer to *zikkas habaal*, the bond of marriage, specifically an adulterous violation of that bond. The linkage to *aviha* elsewhere indicates not that she literally had relations with her father, but rather that she is in a marital state parallel to the *meorasah* — in her father's domain — and has committed adultery against that bond.

A further *hava amina* is then considered: perhaps *aviha* in this context means she committed the sin *with her father* — incest — and that is the unique case that generates *sreifah*. The *gemara* quickly disposes of this: if so, *mai irya bat kohen* — why speak specifically of a *bat kohen*? The rule that a daughter who commits incest with her father receives *sreifah* applies to all Jewish women. The *gemara* cites the tradition of Rava in the name of Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi, who derives through the linkage of *hena-hena* and *zima-zima* that a daughter's incest with her father carries *sreifah*: the Torah's prohibition on sleeping with a woman and her daughter uses the word *zima* and specifies *ba'esh yisrefu*, which links via *hena* to the case of granddaughter, and from there encompasses daughter as well. Since *sreifah* for father-daughter incest applies universally, the verse by the *bat kohen* must be teaching something else. The *gemara* then clarifies: *et aviha hi mechalelet* — *hi* being the operative word — means that *she* is the one desecrating, not that he is desecrating her. The *bat kohen* is the one who has done the active wrong, and the wrong she has done is committing adultery with *another man* — not with her father. This interpretation is confirmed and the *hava amina* of father-daughter incest is ruled out.

## Marriage Scope

### Non-Kohen Husbands

The *baraita* then resumes: *ein li ela sheniset l'kohen* — I know only that she is married to a *kohen*. How do I extend the law to a *bat kohen* married to a *levi*, a *yisrael*, an *oved kochavim*, a *chalal*, a *mamzer*, or a *nesin*? The *baraita* derives the extension from *bat ish kohen* — the daughter of a *kohen*, emphasizing that what matters is her paternal lineage, even if her own *kehunah* status has been compromised.

One might wonder why any extension is needed at all: a *bat kohen* is free to marry whomever she chooses — there is no obligation that she marry a *kohen*. Why would we ever have thought that her marriage to a non-*kohen* changes the law? The *gemara* addresses this directly.

### Forbidden Marriages

The *hava amina* is constructed from the verse *ki teichel liznot* — the root *techal* carrying the double meaning of *lehatchil* (to begin) and *lechalel* (to desecrate). Perhaps the punishment of *sreifah* applies when this act represents the *first* desecration of her *kehunah* status. If she has already compromised that status through her marriage, then the act of adultery — however grave — is not the inaugural desecration.

The *gemara* supports this *hava amina* from established *halacha*: *v'vat kohen ki tihye l'ish zar hi bi'trumat ha'kodashim lo tochel* — a *bat kohen* who marries a non-*kohen* may no longer eat *terumah*. She is no longer embedded in a *kehunah* household: she has left her father's house through marriage, and her husband's house is not a *kohanic* household. As long as she remains in that marriage, her *kehunah* connection is severed for purposes of *terumah*. Only if she is widowed or divorced without children can she return to her father's house and resume eating *terumah*. Thus marriage to a *levi* or *yisrael* temporarily displaces her from *kehunah* status, and marriage to a *mamzer*, *nesin*, or non-Jew does so permanently.

This distinction matters enormously. If she marries a man she is forbidden to be with — a *mamzer*, a *nesin*, a non-Jew — not only is she currently barred from *terumah*, but she can never return to her father's household for that purpose. Having had sexual relations with someone who renders her permanently disqualified (*keivan sheniv'ala le-psula, pasla*), her *kehunah* status is fundamentally altered. She can no longer marry a *kohen*; she becomes a *chalalah* or *zonah* in the relevant sense. The same applies, interestingly, to a *chalal*, even though marriage to a *chalal* is technically permissible for her: the *chalal* status is contagious. A male *kohen* who sins by marrying a *gerushah* retains his own *kohen* identity — we may withhold the right to *duchen* from him as a form of penalty, but halachically his status is unchanged. It is his children who suffer the consequence: they are born *chalalin*. And when the *chalal* has intercourse with a woman — even in a fully permitted marriage — she acquires *chalalah* status. So a *bat kohen* married to a *chalal* becomes a *chalalah* and can no longer eat *terumah* or marry a *kohen*.

Given all this, the *hava amina* runs: once a *bat kohen* has already permanently forfeited her *kehunah* status through her marriage to a forbidden man, perhaps the subsequent act of adultery, terrible as it is, no longer constitutes the unique desecration of *et aviha hi mechalelet*. Her *kehunah* status has already been violated. Maybe *sreifah* should not apply. This is not a weak *hava amina* — especially for the cases of *mamzer*, *nesin*, and the rest where the compromise is permanent and complete.

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *baraita* continues with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, recorded in a separate *baraita*. A *bat kohen* who marries a *yisrael* and eats *terumah* inadvertently pays the principal but not the added fifth (*chomesh*), because despite being forbidden to eat *terumah* in her present circumstances, she came from a household where she was entitled to eat it — and were her husband to die without leaving children, she would return to that entitlement. She is therefore not treated as a full *zar* vis-à-vis *terumah*, and does not pay the *chomesh* penalty a complete outsider would. And her execution, if she commits adultery, is *sreifah* — that much is not in dispute for this case.

But if she married one of the disqualifying men — a *mamzer* or the like — Rabbi Meir holds: *misalemes keren v'chomesh*, she pays both principal and fifth, because she is now a complete *zara* in every sense; *u'misasa b'chenek* — if she commits adultery, her execution is *chenek*, not *sreifah*. Rabbi Meir maintains that once her *kehunah* status has been permanently compromised through her forbidden marriage, she no longer falls within the category of *bat ish kohen* that triggers *sreifah*.

### Rabbanan Consensus

The *rabbanan* disagree. *V'Chachamim omrim*: both women — the one married to a *yisrael* and the one married to a man who permanently disqualifies her — pay principal but not the *chomesh*, and both receive *sreifah* if they commit adultery. The reason is that the *kohanic* connection established by birth is never entirely severed. Even after a *bat kohen* has been permanently excluded from *terumah* and permanently disqualified from marrying a *kohen*, she remains the daughter of a *kohen*. The sanctity that status confers — with its attendant demands and consequences — does not dissolve. Having that *kedushah* status has, as the shiur puts it, its pluses and minuses. The *rabbanan*'s position is explicit: our *baraita* was articulated precisely to reject Rabbi Meir.

## Avihah/Chamihah Debate

### Literal Reading

We turn now to the final and most puzzling line of the *baraita*: the statement of Rabbi Eliezer, *et aviha bisreifah v'et chamiha biskilah* — with her father it is *sreifah*, with her father-in-law it is *skilah*. What does this mean?

The most literal reading would be: if the *bat ish kohen* commits adultery with her father (incest), she receives *sreifah*; if she commits adultery with her father-in-law, she receives *skilah*. This reading is immediately challenged: *mai irya bat kohen* — why speak of a *bat kohen* at all? The rule that father-daughter incest is *sreifah* applies to every Jewish daughter, as we already established. And the rule that adultery with a father-in-law is *skilah* is equally unremarkable — it is simply standard adultery by a *nesuah*, which gets *chenek*, or by an *arusah*, which gets *skilah*, depending on the marital stage. The *baraita* would be saying nothing specifically about the *bat kohen*. This reading must be abandoned.

### Domain Reading

A second approach interprets *aviha* and *chamiha* not as the identity of her partner in the act, but as the domain (*reshut*) she occupies. *Birshut aviha bisreifah* — when she is in her father's domain (i.e., she is an *arusah*, betrothed but living at home), she receives *sreifah*. *Birshut chamiha biskilah* — when she is in her father-in-law's domain (i.e., she has moved into her husband's family household as a *nesuah*), she receives *skilah*. This is an appealing reading. An *arusah* who commits adultery receives *skilah* as a *bat yisrael*; as a *bat kohen*, Rabbi Eliezer elevates this to *sreifah*, the weightier punishment. A *nesuah* who commits adultery receives *chenek* as a *bat yisrael*; as a *bat kohen*, Rabbi Eliezer elevates this one degree to *skilah*.

But there is an immediate problem: which of the Tannaitic opinions does this match? The *gemara* systematically tests it against each position. If it follows the *rabbanan* — who hold that *sreifah* is assigned to the *nesuah* (because *skilah*, being worse in their view, belongs to the *arusah*) — then the domain reading is precisely backwards; the *rabbanan* say *sreifah* is *birshut chamiha*, not *birshut aviha*. If it follows Rabbi Shimon — who holds *sreifah chamurah* and says both *arusah* and *nesuah* receive *sreifah* — then Rabbi Eliezer's differentiation between *arusah* and *nesuah* is already a departure from Rabbi Shimon's own stated position. And if it follows Rabbi Yishmael — who also holds *sreifah chamurah* and limits *sreifah* to the *arusah* while the *nesuah* reverts to *chenek* — it is the closest match, but Rabbi Eliezer says *skilah*, not *chenek*, for the *nesuah*.

### Rabbi Yishmael Analogy

The answer that comes closest to working is attributed to Rava, following a version of Rabbi Shimon's approach. Rava proposes: *vekasavar Rabbi Eliezer nesuah ke'arusah* — Rabbi Eliezer treats the *nesuah* analogously to the *arusah*. The argument runs as follows. By a regular *bat yisrael*, an *arusah* receives *skilah* and a *nesuah* receives *chenek* — the *arusah* case is elevated one degree above the *nesuah*. Now apply that structural relationship to the *bat kohen*: since *sreifah* (in Rabbi Shimon's ordering: *sreifah*, *skilah*, *chenek*, *hereg*) is one degree more severe than *skilah*, and since the *arusah* case is where *sreifah* applies, the *nesuah* case for the *bat kohen* should be elevated by one degree above *chenek* — which yields *skilah*. This is an argument by analogy: *mah arusah chad darga maskinan la miskila lisreifah, af nesuah chad darga maskinan la michenek liskilah* — just as the *arusah* goes up one level from *skilah* to *sreifah*, so the *nesuah* goes up one level from *chenek* to *skilah*.

This is a genuinely elegant answer. But the *gemara* objects: Rabbi Chanina notes that Rabbi Shimon himself said explicitly that both *arusah* and *nesuah* receive *sreifah* — so this position, while structurally similar to Rabbi Shimon's framework, departs from Rabbi Shimon's actual ruling.

### Rabbanan Reading

The final answer is given by Ravina: *le'olam kerabanan* — in truth it follows the *rabbanan*. But the *rabbanan*, as we established, hold *skilah* to be more severe and assign it to the *arusah* case, not to the *nesuah*. The *rabbanan*'s position is thus the exact inverse of what Rabbi Eliezer's statement appears to say. Ravina resolves this by proposing that the text of Rabbi Eliezer's statement has been accidentally reversed. It should read: *et aviha biskilah, v'et chamiha bisreifah* — in her father's domain (*reshut aviha*), meaning as an *arusah*, she receives *skilah*; in her father-in-law's domain (*reshut chamiha*), meaning as a *nesuah*, she receives *sreifah*. This is precisely the *rabbanan*'s position. How did the text get inverted? Because the verse itself says *et aviha hi mechalelet ba'esh tisaref* — the word *aviha* naturally associates with *sreifah* in the reader's mind, and so a copyist or transmitter naturally wrote *et aviha bisreifah* when it should have said *et aviha biskilah*. The slip was understandable, but the text is reversed.

### Practical Relevance

Rav Nachman then rules: the *halacha* follows the reading sent from Eretz Yisrael by Ravin in the name of Rabbi Yose bar Chanina — which was the first proposed interpretation. Rav Yosef objects: *hilchasa limshicha*? What is the point of ruling on a matter of court executions that has no practical application today? Abaye responds sharply: do we refrain from learning and ruling on all matters pertaining to *shechitas kodashim* — the tractates of *Zevachim*, *Menachos*, and so forth — because they apply only when the Temple stands? Clearly not. *Ela derush vekabel sachar* — we study it and receive reward for the study. *Hacha nami derush vekabel sachar*.

But Rashi points out a further dimension to Rav Yosef's objection. His concern was not merely that executions are not carried out nowadays — it was a more pointed critique: the entire discussion of *aviha* and *chamiha* was never a *halachic* debate in the first place. It was a question of *peshat* — how to interpret an obscure phrase in Rabbi Eliezer's statement. All of the competing readings ultimately arrive at the same substantive outcome; they differ only in how they parse the words. To issue a *psak halacha* on what is really a question of textual exegesis — *sugya dishma'ta hilchasa ka'amar*? — is itself the anomaly. If all one wanted to know was what Rabbi Eliezer actually meant, the answer, as Rashi wryly observes, can wait: when the time of *techiyas hameisim* comes, we can ask Rabbi Eliezer himself. With that, we have completed the full unpacking of this *baraita*, and the *gemara* will continue from here.
