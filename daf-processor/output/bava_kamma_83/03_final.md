# Bava Kamma 83 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Greek Wisdom

### Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחׇכְמַת יְוָנִית מִי אֲסִירָא?! וְהָאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל: ״עֵינִי עוֹלְלָה לְנַפְשִׁי מִכֹּל בְּנוֹת עִירִי״ – אֶלֶף יְלָדִים הָיוּ בְּבֵית אַבָּא; חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת מֵהֶם לָמְדוּ תּוֹרָה, חֲמֵשׁ מֵאוֹת לָמְדוּ חׇכְמַת יְוָנִית; וְלֹא נִשְׁתַּיֵּיר מֵהֶם אֶלָּא אֲנִי – כָּאן, וּבֶן אֲחִי אַבָּא – בְּעַסְיָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks a question with regard to this *baraita*: **And is Greek wisdom** itself actually **prohibited? But doesn't Rav Yehuda say** that **Shmuel says in the name of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel:** It is written: **"My eye affected my soul, because of all the daughters of my city"** (Lamentations 3:51). Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel applied this verse to a personal tragedy: **There were a thousand children in the household of** my **father,** Rabban Gamliel; **five hundred of them studied** the **Torah, and five hundred of them studied Greek wisdom.** All of them were killed by the Romans; **and the only** ones that **remain of them** are **I,** who is **here, and the son of** my **father's brother,** who is **in Asia Minor [Asya]**. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's statement indicates that it is permitted to study Greek wisdom.

Daf 83 of Bava Kamma opens on the very last line of 82b. The previous portion of the *gemara* had already noted that a *gezeira* was instituted — either during the Hasmonean period or perhaps later, around the time of the *churban* under Roman rule — prohibiting the teaching of *chochmat yevanit*, Greek wisdom, to one's child. The *gemara* now turns to examine what exactly that prohibition entails and whether it is valid at all. *Tosafot* addresses the question of when this *gezeira* was instituted and observes that if the public was unable to keep it, the *Sanhedrin* would not have had the power to institute it to begin with — the principle being *gezeira she'ein hatzibur yachol la'amod bah*. *Tosafot* also flags a textual variant: one *girsa* reads *arur ha'adam she'ylamed et bno chochmat yevanit* — cursed is the person who teaches his child Greek wisdom — while another reads *asur l'adam she'ylamed bno chochmat yevanit* — it is prohibited. Whether the act is merely cursed or actually forbidden will prove to be a *nafka mina* shortly.

### Pigs & Greek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחׇכְמַת יְוָנִית מִי אֲסִירָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי: בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – לָשׁוֹן סוּרְסִי לָמָּה? אוֹ לְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אוֹ לָשׁוֹן יְוָנִית!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further pursues this line of inquiry: **And is Greek wisdom** itself actually **prohibited? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **said: In Eretz Yisrael,** **why** would one speak the **Syriac [*Sursi*] language?** One should speak **either the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **or the Greek language.**

The *mishna* had discussed the prohibition on raising pigs, and the *gemara* now transitions to *chochmat yevanit* by posing a sharp question: *chochmat yevanit mi asar?* — who said that Greek wisdom is actually forbidden? This question is particularly pointed given what the *gemara* itself records elsewhere: several *amora'im* quote from Aesop's fables, a *sefer Torah* may be written in Greek, and the *gemara* in *Megillah* derives from the *pasuk* *yaft Elokim l'Yefet v'yishkon b'oholei Shem* that the Greek language has a legitimate place in relation to Torah study. The broader rabbinic tradition was clearly familiar with Greek culture and did not uniformly condemn it.

### Language vs. Wisdom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָשׁוֹן סוּרְסִי לָמָּה? אוֹ לְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אוֹ לָשׁוֹן יְוָנִית! וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: [בְּ]בָבֶל – לְשׁוֹן אֲרַמִּי לָמָּה? אוֹ לְשׁוֹן הַקֹּדֶשׁ, אוֹ לָשׁוֹן פָּרְסִי! אָמְרִי: לָשׁוֹן יְוָנִי לְחוּד, חׇכְמַת יְוָנִית לְחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **why** would one speak the **Syriac [*Sursi*] language?** One should speak **either the sacred tongue,** Hebrew, **or the Greek language. And Rabbi Yosei said** similarly: In **Babylonia, why** would one speak the **Aramaic language?** One should speak **either the sacred tongue or the Persian language.** At any rate, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi's statement indicates that there is nothing wrong with learning and speaking Greek. The Sages **say** in response: **The Greek language** is **discrete, and Greek wisdom** is **discrete.** In other words, these are two separate issues; only Greek wisdom is prohibited, not the Greek language.

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *amar Rabbi* — Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi declared — that in *Eretz Yisrael*, there is no reason to speak *lashon sursi*, the Aramaic dialect of the Land of Israel. One should speak either *lashon hakodesh*, biblical Hebrew, or *lashon yevanit*, the Greek language. *Rashi* understands *lashon sursi* as a confused or inelegant mode of speech, while *Tosafot* identifies it as Palestinian Aramaic — the dialect of the *Yerushalmi* and the *Targum* — as distinct from the eastern Aramaic of the *Talmud Bavli*. The key point is that Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi regarded Greek as a perfectly legitimate language.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing: *lashon yevanit lachud, chochmat yevanit lachud* — the Greek language is one thing, Greek wisdom is another. One may speak Greek; it is something else entirely — philosophy, literature, or some specific body of knowledge — that is subject to the *gezeira*. The *gemara* does not define precisely what *chochmat yevanit* encompasses, and this ambiguity appears deliberate.

### Lashon HaKodesh

The mention of *lashon hakodesh* prompts a brief but significant digression. Why is Hebrew called *lashon hakodesh*, the holy tongue? There is a famous *machloket* between the *Rambam* and the *Ramban* on this question. The *Rambam* in *Moreh Nevuchim* argues that Hebrew is called *lashon hakodesh* because it lacks vulgar or explicit terminology — it speaks only in euphemism. Even the sexual act is described merely as *biah*, entering, without explicit description. Other languages have direct words for such things; Hebrew does not. The *Ramban*, writing in *Parshat Ki Tisa*, disagrees entirely: Hebrew is *lashon hakodesh* because it is the language in which God gave the Torah, the language of angels, the language of creation itself. Its holiness is intrinsic, not merely a function of its lexical modesty. The *Rambam*, of course, would resist attributing human language to God in any direct sense — which is precisely why the *Ramban*'s formulation imbues the language with a more direct and anthropomorphous divine holiness.

### Aramaic & Persian

Rabbi Yosi makes a parallel statement regarding *Bavel*: *lashon arami lama?* — why do they speak Aramaic in Babylonia? One should speak either *lashon hakodesh* or *lashon parsi*, Persian. This is somewhat striking given that elsewhere *Chazal* speak highly of Aramaic — it is the only language besides Hebrew that appears in the Torah itself (when Lavan uses *Yegar Sahaduta*), and the *Talmuds* and *targumim* were composed in Aramaic. Rabbi Yosi's point seems to be that Greek and Persian, as the languages of the ruling dynasties in their respective locations, are useful, clear, and elegant — perfectly reasonable to know. Aramaic, by contrast, occupies an awkward middle position. The broader implication is that the prohibition, where it exists, is not on the Greek language but on something else entirely.

### Shimon ben Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרִי: שָׁאנֵי בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, שֶׁהָיוּ קְרוֹבִים לַמַּלְכוּת. וְכִדְתַנְיָא: הַמְסַפֵּר קוֹמֵי – הֲרֵי זֶה מִדַּרְכֵי הָאֱמוֹרִי. אַבְטוּלְמוֹס בַּר רְאוּבֵן הִתִּירוּ לוֹ לְסַפֵּר קוֹמֵי, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא קָרוֹב לַמַּלְכוּת. שֶׁל בֵּית רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל הִתִּירוּ לָהֶם לְסַפֵּר בְּחׇכְמַת יְוָנִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁקְּרוֹבִים לַמַּלְכוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **say** in response: **The household of Rabban Gamliel is different, as they** held **close** ties **with the government.** Since knowledge of Greek wisdom was crucial for the members of this family, the Sages exempted them from the general decree, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one** who **cuts his hair** in the fashion of ***komi*,** a certain haircut favored by the Romans, **this is** considered one **of the ways of the Amorites,** i.e., a gentile practice prohibited by the Torah (Leviticus 18:3). Despite this, the Sages **permitted Avtolmos bar Reuven to cut his hair** in the fashion of ***komi*, because he** had **close** ties **with the government.** Likewise, **they permitted** the members **of the household of Rabban Gamliel to discuss** matters **of Greek wisdom, because they** had **close** ties **with the government.**

The *gemara* then cites *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Shmuel* in the name of *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, who offers a *midrash* on the *pasuk* in the third chapter of *Eicha*: *eini olela linafshi mikol benot iri* — my eye weeps most for myself out of all the daughters of the city. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: there were one thousand children in my father's household — understood to include students and members of his broader community. Five hundred studied Torah, and five hundred studied Greek wisdom. Of all one thousand, only he and his cousin living in *Asya* survived the *churban*; nine hundred and ninety-eight perished. This sounds like a powerful indictment of *chochmat yevanit* — and yet the *gemara* immediately offers a justification: *shani beit Rabban Gamliel she'hayu krovim lamalchut* — Rabban Gamliel's household was different because they were *kerovim lamalchut*, close to the ruling class, and needed to be able to engage in diplomatic conversations with the Roman government. Their study of Greek wisdom was therefore permitted as a communal necessity. This principle — that proximity to governmental authority creates exceptions to certain restrictions — runs throughout *halacha*, from Yosef shaving in Pharaoh's court to Esther's conduct in the Persian palace.

### Karov Lamalchus

A *braita* is then cited: if one gets a *kumi* haircut — a style associated with *midarchei ha'Emori*, the ways of the Amorites, which is the rabbinic code for practices connected to *avoda zara* or pagan identification — that is prohibited. Similarly, a *bularit* haircut is prohibited. However, *Avtulumos ben Reuven* was permitted by the rabbis to get such a haircut *mipnei she'hu karov lamalchus* — because of his proximity to the Roman government. (The *gemara* in *Me'ilah* notes that his son Reuven ben Avtulumos was likewise involved in dealings with Rome.) The parallel case follows: the family of Rabban Gamliel was permitted *lesaper be'Chochmat Yevanis* — to engage with Greek wisdom — for the same reason.

There is a delicate question lurking here. If the *kumi* haircut falls under *be'chukoseihem lo seleichu*, the *de'oraysa* prohibition on following non-Jewish practices, how can *karov lamalchus* function as an exception to a biblical prohibition? The *Kesef Mishneh* addresses this by arguing that the Torah's prohibition is formulated in broad terms — *masro hakasuv lachachamim* — and that the rabbis themselves are authorized to define the contours of what falls under that prohibition and what does not. This is a far-reaching principle with significant implications.

### Maharik Framework

This question connects directly to the *Maharik* — Rabbi Yosef Colon of Cologne, writing in the fifteenth century — whose *teshuvah* on a related question became foundational. The *Maharik* was asked whether a Jewish scholar attending university could wear academic robes. His answer introduced a crucial framework: there are two categories of non-Jewish practices. Practices that are entirely irrational and serve no purpose other than to identify one as a non-Jew or to blend into non-Jewish society — those are prohibited under *be'chukoseihem*. But practices that have a clear rational basis — professional attire worn as a marker of one's occupation or academic achievement — are not prohibited, because one is not adopting the practice as an act of identification with non-Jewish ways but rather for an objectively intelligible reason.

The *Maharik*'s framework, codified in the *Shulchan Aruch*, addresses the academic robes question but does not, by itself, address the *kumi* haircut problem. For that, the *Kesef Mishneh*'s principle — that the rabbis have the authority to define and limit the scope of even the *de'oraysa* prohibition — is still required.

### Bechukoteihem

The *Maharik*'s *teshuvah* became the foundation for a much broader ongoing discussion of *be'chukoseihem lo seleichu* in later generations. One of the most significant modern applications appears in the *Seridei Esh*, which addresses the *bat mitzvah* celebration. The question there is not whether non-Jews celebrate *bat mitzvahs* — obviously they do not — but rather whether certain forms of celebration developed by non-observant Jewish communities constitute an imitation of non-Jewish practice and thus raise concerns of *bechukoseihem*. The *Seridei Esh* uses the occasion to develop a broader analysis of the principle as a whole. This represents a striking extension of the original *sugya*: from a *tanna*'s observation about the household of Rabban Gamliel, through the *Maharik*'s Renaissance-era analysis of university robes, to twentieth-century debates about communal religious celebrations.

## Dogs & Border

### Dogs Unchained

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֹא יְגַדֵּל אָדָם אֶת הַכֶּלֶב אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קָשׁוּר בְּשַׁלְשְׁלָאוֹת כּוּ׳. תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לֹא יְגַדֵּל אָדָם אֶת הַכֶּלֶב – אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן קָשׁוּר בְּשַׁלְשֶׁלֶת. אֲבָל מְגַדֵּל הוּא בָּעִיר הַסְּמוּכָה לַסְּפָר, וְקוֹשְׁרוֹ בַּיּוֹם וּמַתִּירוֹ בַּלַּיְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **A person may not raise a dog unless it is tied with chains. The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A person may not raise a dog unless it is tied with a chain. But he may raise** a dog **in a city that is close to the border** of the country, **and** in that case **he** should **tie it during the day but may release it at night.**

The *gemara* continues with a *braita* related to the next section of the *mishna*: *lo yegadel adam et hakelev ela im kein kashur beshalshelet* — a person may not raise a dog unless it is fastened with a chain. The *braita* then introduces an exception: *aval megadel hu be'ir hasmucha lesefar* — one may raise a dog in a city adjacent to the border, *sefar*. Even if the dog is not always chained, it is permitted in border towns.

### Border Defense

The justification for the border-town exception is the same logic that prevents declaring a city *ir hanidachat* at the border of *Eretz Yisrael* — reducing border territory is itself harmful to the collective. A vicious dog roaming the border at night deters encroachment on Jewish-held territory. Accordingly, the *halacha* is *dekoshro bayom umatiro balaila* — the dog must be chained during the day but may be released at night, precisely to deter intruders.

The *gemara* then adds that *Rav Yosef bar Minyumi* in the name of *Rav Nachman* rules: *Bavel ke'ir hasmucha lesefar dami* — Babylonia is treated like a border city for purposes of this *halacha*. *Rashi* specifies that this refers to the city of Nehardea, which was also called Bavel. *Rashi* draws out two criteria that qualify a city for this status: *yishuv kavua*, a permanent settlement, and *Yisrael harbe*, a substantial Jewish population. These two criteria — permanence and communal density — are what allow a diaspora city to be treated, at least for this purpose, as a border settlement of *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הַגָּדוֹל אוֹמֵר: הַמְגַדֵּל כְּלָבִים – כִּמְגַדֵּל חֲזִירִים. לְמַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? לְמֵיקַם עֲלֵיהּ בְּאָרוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer the Great says: One who raises dogs is like one who raises pigs.** The Gemara asks: **What is the** practical **significance** of this statement? Since both are prohibited, what is the point of this comparison? The Gemara answers: The significance is with regard **to determining when one is liable** to be **cursed for it.** Rabbi Eliezer is saying that the same curse meted out to one who raises pigs also applies to one who raises dogs.

*Tanya* — it is taught in a *baraisa* — *Rabbi Eliezer Hagadol omer: hamegadel kelavim kemegadel chazirim* — one who raises dogs is like one who raises pigs. Raising pigs in *Eretz Yisrael* carries the formulation *arur hamegadel chazir*, cursed is one who raises pigs. Rabbi Eliezer Hagadol now extends that comparison to dogs.

The *gemara* asks: *lemai nafka mina?* What practical difference does Rabbi Eliezer's comparison make? After all, the *mishna* already prohibits raising an unchained dog. The answer: *lemekayeim alav be'arur* — the *nafka mina* is whether one who raises a dog in violation of the *mishna* also incurs the curse. According to the *mishna* alone, the act is *asur* — forbidden — but one does not necessarily incur a curse for it. According to Rabbi Eliezer, one who raises dogs as the *mishna* forbids also receives the *arur*, the curse, just as one who raises pigs does.

This connects interestingly to the earlier *Tosafot* about the *gezeira* of *chochmat yevanit*: there *Tosafot* noted that a *girsa* reading *arur* rather than *asur* might actually soften the prohibition — something cursed is not necessarily something forbidden. Here, the logic runs in the opposite direction: the act was already forbidden under the *mishna*, and Rabbi Eliezer adds the additional dimension of the curse.

Regarding the practical *halacha* of dog ownership today: the relevant considerations remain that a *kelev ra*, a vicious dog, is unambiguously prohibited, and that a dog must be restrained in public spaces — this *halacha* continues to apply. The distinction between a working animal and a pet is a modern one that *Chazal* did not contemplate, and contemporary *poskim* have addressed it accordingly.

### Shechina & Dogs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵישׁ רַבִּי דּוֹסְתַּאי דְּמִן בֵּירֵי: ״וּבְנֻחֹה יֹאמַר שׁוּבָה ה׳ רִבְבוֹת אַלְפֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל״ – לְלַמֶּדְךָ, שֶׁאֵין שְׁכִינָה שׁוֹרָה עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁנֵי אֲלָפִים וּשְׁנֵי רְבָבוֹת. חָסֵר אַחַת, וְהָיְתָה אִשָּׁה מְעוּבֶּרֶת בֵּינֵיהֶם וּרְאוּיָה לְהַשְׁלִים, וְנָבַח בָּהּ כֶּלֶב וְהִפִּילָה, נִמְצָא זֶה גּוֹרֵם לַשְּׁכִינָה שֶׁתִּסְתַּלֵּק מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to raising dogs, **Rabbi Dostai from Biri expounded:** It is written in connection to the Ark of the Covenant: **"And when it rested, he said: Return, O Lord, to the myriads of the thousands of Israel"** (Numbers 10:36). This verse serves **to teach you that the Divine Presence does not rest upon the Jewish people** if they number **fewer than two thousand and two myriads,** where one myriad is equal to ten thousand. The plural form of "myriads" and "thousands" indicates at least two of each. If they are **lacking one** individual from this total, **and there was a pregnant woman among them, who was fit to complete** the number by giving birth, **and a dog barked at her and she miscarried** as a result of the fright, **this** owner of the dog **is found** to have **caused the Divine Presence to depart from the Jewish people.**

*Darash Rabi Dostai demin Biri*: the *pasuk* states *uvnucho yomar shuva Hashem rivvot alfei Yisrael* — when the *aron* rested, the cry went up that God should return to the *rivvot alfei Yisrael*, the tens of thousands and thousands of Israel. *Tosafot* points out that this cannot be a multiplication — ten thousand times one thousand yields ten million, far exceeding the six hundred thousand present in the desert — and therefore it is an addition: *rivvot* as twenty thousand (two tens of thousands) plus *alafim* as two thousand, yielding twenty-two thousand.

The *derasha*: *ein Shechina shora al Yisrael pachot mishnei alafim ushnei rivvot* — the *Shechina* does not dwell among the Jewish people unless twenty-two thousand are present. This is the threshold for the *Shechina*'s full dwelling. Smaller groupings carry their own levels of divine presence: ten for a *minyan*, three for a *zimmun*, two for *chavruta*, and even one person learning alone is joined by God. But twenty-two thousand is the threshold for the *Shechina* to dwell in a communal sense.

Now the *gemara* brings a strikingly vivid application. What if there were twenty-one thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine Jews, and a pregnant woman was present — *har'uya lehashlim*, she would complete the count, because the *ubar* she carried would have been the twenty-two thousandth person? *Venavach bah kelev* — and a dog barked at her — *vehipila* — and she miscarried. *Nimtza zeh gorem lash'china shetistalek miYisrael* — that dog caused the *Shechina* to be removed from Israel. This is the aggadic underpinning of Rabbi Eliezer Hagadol's position: the presence of a dangerous, unrestrained dog is not merely a civil hazard but can have cosmic consequences.

### Woman's Retort

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהִיא אִיתְּתָא דְּעַלַּת לְמֵיפָא בְּהָהוּא בֵּיתָא, נְבַח בַּהּ כַּלְבָּא. אֲמַר לַהּ מָרֵיהּ: לָא תִּיסְתְּפַי מִינֵּיהּ, שְׁקִילִי נִיבֵיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: שְׁקִילָה טֵיבוּתָיךְ וְשַׁדְיָא אַחִיזְרֵי, כְּבָר נָד וָלָד.
>
> **Translation:** There was **a certain woman who entered a certain building to bake. A dog barked at her. Its owner said to her: Do not be afraid of it; its canine teeth have been removed. She said to him:** It is too late for your reassurances. **Take your favors and throw them on the thorns!** I have felt that **the baby has already moved** from its place in the womb and will not be born alive.

A related narrative: a woman came to bake at a neighbor's house — as was customary, since women would share millstones and ovens, going to one another's homes for different stages of food preparation. The dog in the house snapped at her. The owner of the dog reassured her — the Aramaic is *lo tid'chali minei*, do not be afraid of it — and offered to have the dog's canine teeth removed. She replied: *shekuli tivutach veshadei achizrei* — take your good wishes and throw them to the pigs. *Kevar nad velad* — the child had already moved in her womb, meaning she knew she was going to miscarry.

*Rashi* frames her response with remarkable emotional depth: *netulah hi tovat'cha umutelet al hakotzim* — your good wishes are stripped away and thrown onto the thorns. *Ma atah menachameni havel* — why are you consoling me with emptiness? The *havel* echoes the *Kohelet* register of futility. There is, in fact, a *kinah* in the Ashkenazic *Kinnot* that opens with the phrase *ma atah menachameni havel*. The *aggadic* principle underlying the narrative is also connected to the *mishna* in *Pirkei Avot* regarding *meto mutal lefanav* — there are moments when a person is simply not yet ready to receive *tanchumin*, and to offer consolation at the wrong time is worse than silence.

## Dove Traps

### 30 Ris Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין פּוֹרְסִין נִישְׁבִּין לְיוֹנִים וְכוּ׳. וּמִי אָזְלִי כּוּלֵּי הַאי? וְהָתְנַן: מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַשּׁוֹבָךְ מִן הָעִיר חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵישָׁט שָׁיְיטִי טוּבָא, כַּרְסַיְיהוּ בַּחֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה מַלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **One may spread out traps for pigeons only** if this was performed at a distance of at least thirty *ris*, which is eight thousand cubits, from any settled area, to ensure that privately owned pigeons are not caught in the traps. The Gemara asks: **And do** pigeons really **travel that** far from their established places? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Batra* 23a): **One must distance a dovecote fifty cubits from the city,** so that the pigeons that settle in the cotes do not eat the crops that grow in the gardens of the city. This shows that the range of a pigeon is only fifty cubits, whereas four *mil* is eight thousand cubits. **Abaye said:** Pigeons **fly** much **more** than fifty cubits away from their cotes, but **their stomachs become full** from the food they find **within fifty cubits,** and therefore they do not eat any more beyond that range.

The final section of *Perek Meruba* deals with dove traps. The *mishna* states: *ein porsin nishvin leyonei* — one may not spread nets to trap doves within thirty *ris* of the city. Thirty *ris* equals four *mil*, which is approximately the distance a person walks in seventy-two minutes — slightly less than four of our miles. The concern is that people who live in cities keep their dove-cotes within a manageable distance of their homes. Spreading nets near the city therefore risks catching doves that belong to other people.

### Hungry vs. Satiated

The *gemara* asks: *umi azlei kulei hai* — do doves actually travel that far? After all, the *mishna* elsewhere states that a dove-cote must be placed only fifty *amah* from the city — implying that doves do not fly very far. *Abaye* resolves the apparent contradiction: *mishat shat yatvu karsaihu bechamishim amah* — when they are hungry, doves settle within fifty *amah*, looking for food. But *umishloshim ris* — once they are full and their crops are satisfied, they will fly as far as thirty *ris*. The two figures describe two different behaviors: the doves forage within fifty *amah* of their cote, but when not eating they may range up to four *mil*. The prohibition on trapping must therefore extend to the full range of their flight.

### 100 Mil Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵישָׁט שְׁלֹשִׁים רִיס וְתוּ לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: בַּיִּשּׁוּב – אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה מִיל לֹא יִפְרוֹס! רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּיִשּׁוּב כְּרָמִים. רַבָּה אָמַר: בְּיִשּׁוּב שׁוֹבָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **flying,** is their range only **thirty *ris* and no more? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **In a settled area, one may not spread out** a trap for pigeons **even** at a distance of **one hundred *mil*** from the settlement? This indicates that a privately owned pigeon might be trapped even one hundred *mil* away from a settled area. Why, then, does the mishna permit trapping beyond four *mil*? **Rav Yosef said:** The *baraita* is not talking about an ordinary city but **about a settled area** where **vineyards** are grown. Since the pigeons rest in the vineyards as they travel, they are able to traverse great distances. **Rabba stated** a different answer: The *baraita* is dealing **with a settled area** where there are many **dovecotes** in which they can rest from their flight.

Another *braita* is then cited: *be-yishuv, afilu me'ah mil lo yifros* — within a settled area, one may not spread nets even one hundred *mil* away. One hundred *mil*, at approximately eighteen minutes of walking per *mil*, represents a journey of many days. This is a dramatically different number. The *gemara* treats this as a genuine difficulty requiring resolution.

### Vineyards/Cotes

*Rav Yosef* explains: the hundred-*mil* rule applies specifically to *be-yishuv kramim* — a settled area with vineyards. Doves can stop and rest in the vineyards along the way, and then resume flying, effectively covering vastly greater distances in stages. *Rabba* offers an alternative: *be-yishuv shovachin* — an area with many dove-cotes. The doves can rest at intermediate dove-cotes along their route, using them as way-stations, and thereby travel much farther than they otherwise would.

### Ownership Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹבָכִין גּוּפַיְיהוּ! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בִּדְגוֹי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בִּדְהֶפְקֵר, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּדִידֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If there are many dovecotes along the way, **let** the *tanna* **derive** the prohibition against setting pigeon traps **due to the dovecotes themselves.** Since the mishna teaches that it is prohibited to trap pigeons from private dovecotes, it should be irrelevant that there is a city one hundred *mil* away. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is dealing with dovecotes **that** belong **to a gentile,** and the prohibition against trapping pigeons from private dovecotes applies only to birds owned by Jews. **Or if you wish, say** that the *baraita* is dealing **with** abandoned dovecotes **that** are **ownerless. Or if you wish, say** that it is referring to dovecotes **that are his,** i.e., they belong to the same individual who is setting the pigeon trap.

The *gemara* then offers further alternatives. One reading distinguishes between non-Jewish dove-cotes and Jewish ones: perhaps a greater distance is required regarding Jewish dove-cotes — reflecting a higher standard of *lifnim mishurat hadin* within the Jewish community — while regarding non-Jewish dove-cotes only the basic fifty *amah* applies. Another reading (*be-hefker*) suggests the rule differs depending on whether the doves are ownerless, in which case the concern of theft is reduced. A third reading (*be-didai*) addresses one's own doves: if you are spreading nets near your own dove-cote, you will only ever catch either wild doves or your own, and you pose no risk of stealing from anyone else.

The underlying concern throughout is theft — not noise or nuisance. The question of how much *lifnim mishurat hadin* one must observe regarding non-Jews, as opposed to fellow Jews, is the subtext of several of these distinctions.

## Chovel Intro

### Five Obligations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַחוֹבֵל בַּחֲבֵירוֹ, חַיָּיב עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם חֲמִשָּׁה דְּבָרִים: בְּנֶזֶק, בְּצַעַר, בְּרִיפּוּי, בְּשֶׁבֶת וּבוֹשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who injures another is liable** to pay compensation **for that** injury **due to five types** of indemnity: He must pay **for damage, for pain, for medical costs, for loss of livelihood, and** for **humiliation.**

With *Hadran alach Meruba*, we turn the daf to 83b and begin *Perek Ha-chovel*, the chapter on bodily injury. The first six chapters of *Bava Kamma* dealt primarily with *nizkei mamon* — damages caused by one's property — with some discussion of *adam ha-mazik*, a person who directly causes damage. *Perek Meruba* addressed *gneiva*, theft, and a wide range of additional topics. Now the *masechta* returns to *adam ha-mazik* directly.

The *mishna* opens: *ha-chovel be-chaveiro chayav alav mishum chamisha devarim* — one who strikes another person is liable under five categories. These are: *nezek* (actual damage), *tza'ar* (pain and suffering), *ripui* (medical expenses), *shevet* (lost wages), and *boshet* (embarrassment). Each of these five categories requires its own method of evaluation, and the *mishna* will now address each in turn.

### Nezek

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנֶזֶק כֵּיצַד? סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ; קָטַע אֶת יָדוֹ; שִׁיבֵּר אֶת רַגְלוֹ – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא עֶבֶד נִמְכָּר בַּשּׁוּק, וְשָׁמִין כַּמָּה הָיָה יָפֶה וְכַמָּה הוּא יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **How** is payment **for damage** assessed? If **one blinded** another's **eye, severed his hand, broke his leg,** or caused any other injury, the court **views** the injured party **as though he were a slave** being **sold in the** slave **market, and** the court **appraises how much he was worth** before the injury **and how much he is worth** after the injury. The difference between these two sums is the amount that one must pay for causing damage.

*Be-nezek keitzad?* — How is *nezek* assessed? If one blinds another's eye, severs a hand, or breaks a foot, we apply the slave-market standard: *ro'im oto ke'ilu hu eved* — we consider what this person would have fetched at the slave market before the injury and what he would fetch now. The difference in value is the *nezek*. We do not attempt to place an absolute monetary value on a hand or a foot; we measure the relative diminution of the person's overall market value. This valuation is itself a *kula* — an attempt to arrive at a figure that is defensibly objective and avoids inflating the sum extracted from the tortfeasor.

There is a *machloket* between *Rashi* and the *Rosh* about which slave-market standard is meant. *Rashi* holds that the reference is to the *eved Ivri* — the indentured Hebrew servant who sells his labor for six years. The *Rosh* objects: an *eved Ivri* is not even a full *mechira*, a sale of the person, but only of his labor; he does not represent the true market value of a person's body. The *Rosh* therefore holds that the standard is the *eved Kena'ani* market, which valued the person as a commodity.

### Tzaar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צַעַר – כְּוָאוֹ (אוֹ) בְּשַׁפּוּד אוֹ בְּמַסְמֵר, וַאֲפִילּוּ עַל צִיפּוֹרְנוֹ מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹשֶׂה חַבּוּרָה – אוֹמְדִין כַּמָּה אָדָם כַּיּוֹצֵא בָּזֶה רוֹצֶה לִיטּוֹל, לִהְיוֹת מִצְטַעֵר כָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** How is payment for **pain** assessed? If **one burned** another **with a skewer [*beshapud*] or with** a hot **nail, or even** if one burned another **on his fingernail,** which is **a place where he does not cause a bruise** that would affect the victim's value on the slave market, the court **evaluates how much** money **a person** with **a similar** threshold for pain **as** the victim **is willing to take** in order to be made **to suffer in this way.** The one who burned the victim must then pay this amount.

*Tzaar* addresses pain and suffering without permanent injury. The *mishna* gives the example of burning someone with a spit or a nail — or stabbing them even on the fingernail, a place that does not leave permanent injury. The method of assessment: *omdin kama adam kayotzei baze rotze litol l'hiyot mitzta'er kach* — we estimate how much a person of similar circumstances would require to be paid in order to willingly undergo such pain. This is the *halacha*'s mechanism for monetizing pure suffering: a willing-participant model, not unlike modern game-show logic, but applied judicially.

Whether *tzaar* encompasses only immediate physical pain or also encompasses ongoing trauma is a *machloket rishonim* that bears on questions of psychological injury.

### Ripui

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רִיפּוּי – הִכָּהוּ, חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ. עָלָה בּוֹ צְמָחִים, אִם מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה – חַיָּיב, שֶׁלֹּא מֵחֲמַת הַמַּכָּה – פָּטוּר. חָיְיתָה וְנִסְתְּרָה חָיְיתָה וְנִסְתְּרָה – חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ. חָיְיתָה כׇּל צוֹרְכָּהּ – אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב לְרַפּאוֹתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** How is payment for **medical costs** assessed? If one **struck** another, then he is **liable to heal him** by paying for his medical costs. In a case where **growths,** e.g., blisters or rashes, **appeared on** the injured party, **if** the growths are **due to the blow,** the one who struck him is **liable;** if the growths are **not due to the blow,** the one who struck him is **exempt.** In a case where the wound **healed, and** then **reopened,** and again **healed, and** then **reopened,** the one who struck him remains **liable to heal** the injured party by paying for his medical costs, as it is apparent that the current wound resulted from the original injury. If the injury **healed fully,** the one who struck him **is not liable to heal him** by paying for any subsequent medical costs.

*Ripui* covers medical expenses. *Hikahu, chayav l'rapoto* — if you struck someone, you are obligated to heal that person, meaning to bear the cost of competent medical care. The *mishna* then addresses complications: *alu bo tzmachim, im machamat hamaka chayav* — if a disease or secondary condition develops as a result of the initial injury, the tortfeasor remains liable. If the complication is wholly unrelated to the original injury, he is exempt.

*Chayeta v'nistara* — if the injury healed and then worsened again, the tortfeasor remains liable, since the recurrence is presumed to be a continuation of the original damage. Only if the injury healed completely — *chayeta kol tzorka* — does liability for subsequent deterioration end: *eino chayav l'rapoto*, the tortfeasor may then plausibly argue that the new condition is independent of the original injury.

The *Rosh* adds an important practical note: if the injured party failed to follow the doctor's instructions and thereby caused or worsened a complication, that is the injured party's own responsibility. The tortfeasor's medical liability does not extend to self-inflicted complications arising from non-compliance with medical treatment.

### Shevet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁבֶת – רוֹאִין אוֹתוֹ כְּאִילּוּ הוּא שׁוֹמֵר קִישּׁוּאִין, שֶׁכְּבָר נָתַן לוֹ דְּמֵי יָדוֹ וּדְמֵי רַגְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** How is payment for **loss of livelihood** assessed? The court **views** the injured party **as though he were a watchman of cucumbers,** and the one who caused him injury must compensate him based on that pay scale for the income that he lost during his convalescence. This indemnity does not take into account the value of the standard wages of the injured party **because** the one who caused him injury **already gave him compensation for his hand or compensation for his leg,** and that compensation took into account his professional skills.

*Shevet* addresses lost wages during the period of incapacitation. *Ro'im oto k'ilu hu shomer kishu'im* — we evaluate the injured party as if he were doing the minimum possible job, that of a watchman over a cucumber or squash field. This is the *halacha*'s equivalent of a minimum wage floor. The reason the assessment is pegged at this minimal figure rather than at the injured party's actual earning capacity is that the tortfeasor has already compensated for the actual injury to the limb through *nezek*. Since the *nezek* payment accounts for the permanent loss of the limb's productive capacity — evaluated via the slave-market differential — it would be double-counting to also pay the full wage that the limb would have earned. The *shevet* payment covers only the period of recovery at a baseline rate.

### Boshet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בּוֹשֶׁת – הַכֹּל לְפִי הַמְבַיֵּישׁ וְהַמִּתְבַּיֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** How is payment for **humiliation** assessed? **It all depends on** the stature of **the one who humiliates** the other **and the one who is humiliated.**

*Boshet* — embarrassment — is assessed differently from all the others: *boshet hakol lefi ham'vayesh v'hamit'bayesh* — everything depends on the relative status of the person who caused the embarrassment and the person who was embarrassed. If a person of high standing humiliates me, that is a greater indignity than if someone of low standing does so. Social rank thus enters the calculation on both sides of the equation.

## Ayin Tachat Ayin

### Kashya Amai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַמַּאי? ״עַיִן תַּחַת עַיִן״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא – אֵימָא עַיִן מַמָּשׁ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why** does the mishna take for granted the fact that one who caused injury is liable to pay compensation to the injured party? **The Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"An eye for an eye"** (Exodus 21:24). You might **say** that this means that the one who caused injury shall lose **an actual eye** rather than pay money.

With the five categories of liability established, the *gemara* poses a striking *kashya*. The entire *mishna* — all five obligations — seems to be undermined by the *pasuk*: *ayin tachat ayin*, an eye for an eye. If the Torah mandates literal physical retaliation, why does the *mishna* prescribe five categories of monetary payment? The *kashya* is not merely a question about the scope of compensation; it challenges the legitimacy of the entire chapter. *Eima ayin mamash* — perhaps the Torah means that you literally extract the wrongdoer's eye in return. The full force of the question is: *lo salka daitach* — this surely cannot be entertained, and yet the *gemara* insists on working through the scriptural derivation carefully.

### Animal Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ; דְּתַנְיָא: יָכוֹל סִימֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ – מְסַמֵּא אֶת עֵינוֹ, קָטַע אֶת יָדוֹ – מְקַטֵּעַ אֶת יָדוֹ, שִׁיבֵּר אֶת רַגְלוֹ – מְשַׁבֵּר אֶת רַגְלוֹ? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״מַכֵּה אָדָם״ וּ״מַכֵּה בְהֵמָה״; מָה מַכֵּה בְהֵמָה – לְתַשְׁלוּמִין, אַף מַכֵּה אָדָם – לְתַשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: That interpretation should **not enter your mind.** The principle implicit in the mishna is derived from a verbal analogy in the Torah, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: Based on the verse: "An eye for an eye, a tooth for a tooth, a hand for a hand, a foot for a foot" (Exodus 21:24), one **might** have thought that if one **blinded the eye of** another, the court **blinds his eye** as punishment; or if one **severed the hand of** another, the court **severs his hand;** or if one **broke the leg of** another, the court **breaks his leg.** Therefore, **the verse states: "One who strikes a person,"** and the verse also states: **"And one who strikes an animal,"** to teach that **just as one who strikes an animal** is liable **to** pay monetary **compensation, so too, one who strikes a person** is liable **to** pay monetary **compensation.**

The first *tanna'itic* source the *gemara* brings: *makeh behema yeshalmena, umakeh adam yumat* — one who strikes an animal pays monetary compensation, and one who strikes a person faces death. From this pairing, the *gemara* asks whether we should derive the rule for injuring a person by comparison to injuring an animal. Just as striking an animal results in monetary payment — the value of the damage — so too, striking a person should result in monetary payment. *Mah makeh behema letashlumin, af makeh adam letashlumin*.

This comparison is the core of the argument, but it immediately raises an obvious difficulty: the *pasuk* of *makeh adam yumat* is plainly about a fatal blow. The *gemara* acknowledges that *hahu be-katala ktiv* — that verse is written about a killing, not an ordinary injury. The *gemara* therefore moves to a different *pasuk*: *makeh nefesh behema yeshalmena nefesh tachat nefesh*, immediately adjacent to *v'ish ki yitein mum ba'amito ka'asher asa ken ye'aseh lo* — if a person injures his fellow, it shall be done back to him. The physical proximity of these two laws — the animal compensation rule and the retaliatory-sounding human injury rule — is itself part of the hermeneutical argument.

### Capital Crime

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם נַפְשְׁךָ לוֹמַר, הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא תִקְחוּ כֹפֶר לְנֶפֶשׁ רֹצֵחַ, אֲשֶׁר הוּא רָשָׁע לָמוּת״ – לְנֶפֶשׁ רוֹצֵחַ אִי אַתָּה לוֹקֵחַ כּוֹפֶר, אֲבָל אַתָּה לוֹקֵחַ כּוֹפֶר לְרָאשֵׁי אֵבָרִים שֶׁאֵין חוֹזְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **And if it is your wish to say** that there is an objection to this derivation, there is an alternative derivation: The verse **states:** "And **you shall not take ransom for the life of a murderer, who is guilty of death,** for he shall die" (Numbers 35:31). This indicates that it is only **for the life of a murderer** that **you shall not take ransom; but you** shall **take ransom for** one who severed another's **extremities,** which is analogous to the death of a limb, **as** severed limbs **do not regenerate.**

*V'im nafshacha lomar* — if you would still press the point — *harei hu omer: lo tikchu kofer lenefesh rotzei'ach asher hu rasha lamut*. The *pasuk* in *Parshat Masei* explicitly prohibits taking *kofer* — a financial redemption payment — in a capital case. The murderer who is *rasha lamut* may not buy his way out; he must face execution or, in the case of manslaughter, go to the *ir miklat*. The *gemara* reads this prohibition by implication: *lenefesh rotzei'ach i attah lokeach kofer, aval attah lokeach kofer l'rashei evarim she'einam chozrim* — you may not take *kofer* in a murder case, but you may — indeed, you must — take *kofer* for *rashei evarim*, for the injury to limbs that do not regenerate. The category of *rashei evarim she'einam chozrim* covers permanent injuries: a severed thumb, a blinded eye, a broken foot that does not fully heal.

Whether the *tzaar* from such injuries encompasses purely physical pain in the moment or also extends to longer-lasting psychological trauma remains a *machloket rishonim*, as noted earlier.

### Comparison Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵי ״מַכֶּה״? אִילֵימָא ״מַכֵּה בְהֵמָה יְשַׁלְּמֶנָּה, וּמַכֵּה אָדָם יוּמָת״ – הָהוּא בִּקְטָלָא כְּתִיב!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: To **which** verse is the *baraita* referring when it quotes: **"One who strikes** a person" and: "One who strikes an animal"? **If we say** that the *baraita* is referring to the verse: **"One who strikes an animal shall pay its compensation, and one who strikes a person shall be put to death"** (Leviticus 24:21), this cannot be, as **that** verse **is written with regard to killing,** not injury, and there is no monetary compensation for killing.

The *gemara* now surfaces a fundamental objection to the entire line of reasoning. The argument has been: learn the human-injury rule from the animal-injury rule, since both deal with *nezikin* rather than with killing. But one could equally argue the opposite: *aderaba danina adam mei'adam* — better to derive the rule for injuring a person from another human-injury case, not from an animal case. Why should a human being be compared to a cow? *Adam mei'adam* would be a more natural comparison than *adam mibehema*. This is a genuine logical challenge: the whole direction of the derivation — learning from animals to people — is counterintuitive.

### Gezeria Shavah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא מֵהָכָא: ״מַכֵּה נֶפֶשׁ בְּהֵמָה יְשַׁלְּמֶנָּה, נֶפֶשׁ תַּחַת נָפֶשׁ״, וּסְמִיךְ לֵיהּ: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי יִתֵּן מוּם בַּעֲמִיתוֹ, כַּאֲשֶׁר עָשָׂה כֵּן יֵעָשֶׂה לוֹ״. הַאי לָאו ״מַכֶּה״ הוּא! ״הַכָּאָה״–״הַכָּאָה״ קָאָמְרִינַן; מָה הַכָּאָה הָאֲמוּרָה בִּבְהֵמָה – לְתַשְׁלוּמִין, אַף הַכָּאָה הָאֲמוּרָה בְּאָדָם – לְתַשְׁלוּמִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the *baraita* references the verse **from here: "One who strikes an animal mortally shall pay its compensation, a life for a life"** (Leviticus 24:18); **and juxtaposed to that** is the verse: **"And if a man maims his neighbor, as he has done, so shall it be done to him"** (Leviticus 24:19). The Gemara challenges: But **this** latter verse **does not** use the expression: **"One who strikes,"** which is the basis for the comparison in the *baraita*. The Gemara responds: **We are stating** an analogy from **striking** to **striking** that is based not upon the exact phrasing of the verse but upon the details of the *halakha*, as follows: **Just as** the act of **striking that is stated with regard to an animal** renders one liable **to** pay monetary **compensation, so too,** the act of **striking that is stated with regard to a person** renders one liable **to** pay monetary **compensation.**

The resolution of the fundamental comparison problem — why learn from animals rather than from a human-to-human comparison — comes through a *gezeria shavah*. The word *haka'ah*, striking, appears both in the animal-injury context and in the human-injury context. This *gezeria shavah* of *makeh-makeh* connects the two: *mah haka'ah ha'amurah bivehemah letashlumin, af haka'ah ha'amurah ba'adam letashlumin* — just as striking an animal mandates monetary payment, so too does striking a person. This forecloses the option of literal retaliation entirely.

*Tosafot* addresses whether the connection is technically a *gezeria shavah* using the word *makeh*, or rather *smuchim* — derived from the textual adjacency of the verses — and notes that different rules apply to each hermeneutical method. A *gezeria shavah* requires received tradition — *ein adam dan gezeria shavah le'atzmo*, one cannot simply invent one's own verbal analogy. The *Ra'ah*, a student in the *Ramban* school quoted in the *Nimukei Yosef*, offers an important qualification: if the *halacha* is already independently established and one is merely seeking a *mnemonic* to anchor it texturally, one may propose one's own *gezeria shavah*-style connection as an *asmachta*. This principle illuminates a famous comment by the *Ramban* in the context of *sukkah* — where *Ramban* links the rule *mitzta'er patur min hasukkah* to a *pasuk* from *Tehillim* and a *pasuk* from *Vayikra*, a *derashah* not present in the *gemara*. Other *rishonim* and *acharonim* questioned how *Ramban* could propose his own *drashah* for an established law. The *Ra'ah*'s framework answers: *Ramban* was offering a mnemonic for a received *halacha*, not inventing a new derivation.

### Evolution & Limit

A final anthropological note places the entire discussion in historical context. *Ayin tachat ayin* appears not only in the Torah but also in the Code of Hammurabi and other ancient Near Eastern law codes. Some scholars have argued that the original function of *ayin tachat ayin* — read literally, in those non-Jewish contexts — was itself a *limitation* on violence. Before codified law, an injury to one person might trigger unlimited retaliation by the entire clan: not only blinding the injurer's eye, but killing him, burning his house, devastating his family. In that environment, *rak ayin tachat ayin* — only an eye for an eye, nothing more — was a cap on escalation.

The Torah takes this evolutionary trajectory even further. The rabbinic tradition does not merely limit retaliation; it eliminates it entirely. *Ayin tachat ayin* is transformed into *ayin tachat ayin — mamon*, monetary compensation and nothing else. There is no retributive injury. There is only financial restitution. This reading — *devarim ha-mekubalim mipi Moshe Rabbeinu*, received tradition going back to Moshe — is also what becomes the central point of polemic against both Christian and Karaite interpretations. Christianity read *ayin tachat ayin* as the paradigm of a harsh Old Testament justice that Jesus superseded with "turn the other cheek." Karaites read it literally: injure the injurer identically. *Halacha* rejects both readings and stands on the position that monetary compensation — not pacifism, and not physical retaliation — is the Torah's true mandate from the outset.
