# Zevachim 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blood Inside Debate

### Mishnah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: כׇּל דָּמִים שֶׁנִּכְנְסוּ לְכַפֵּר בַּהֵיכָל – פְּסוּלִין, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: חַטָּאת בִּלְבַד. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אַף הָאָשָׁם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כַּחַטָּאת כָּאָשָׁם״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches **that Rabbi Akiva would say:** Any blood that is to be presented outside **that entered to atone in the Sanctuary is disqualified, and the Rabbis say:** That is the *halakha* with regard to the blood of an external **sin offering alone,** as it is written: "And any sin offering, whereof any of the blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:23). **Rabbi Eliezer says:** The status of **a guilt offering** is like that of a sin offering **as well, as it is stated: "As is the sin offering, so is the guilt offering"** (Leviticus 7:7), i.e., there is one *halakha* for them. That is not the case with regard to the blood of other offerings.

The *daf* opens three lines from the top of 82a, at the words *sheyehei Rabbi Akiva omer*. The *mishnah* records a dispute concerning the *pasuk* in *Tzav* that states: any *chatat* whose blood is brought inside — *el hakodesh pnima*, to atone in the sanctuary — shall not be eaten but shall be burned. At one level, the *pasuk* is simply describing the category of *chata'ot pnimiyot*, inner *chata'ot* — offerings such as the *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* and those brought on *Yom Kippur* — for which the *mitzvah* is to sprinkle the blood inside and burn the meat outside the *mikdash*.

However, the *pasuk* is also read to mean that a kosher, outer *chatat* whose blood was brought inside is thereby invalidated and must be burned. The question in the *mishnah* is whether this *din* is limited to a *chatat* alone. Rabbi Akiva maintains that it applies to all sacrifices: if one brings the blood of any *korban* inside, it is invalidated and must be burned. Rabbi Eliezer holds that it applies to a *chatat* — and, consistent with his position from the first *mishnah* of the *masechet*, to an *asham* as well, since an *asham* is treated like a *chatat*.

### Wine Mixing Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: מָשָׁל לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁמָּזַג לְרַבּוֹ בְּחַמִּין, וְאָמַר לוֹ: מְזוֹג לִי. אָמַר לוֹ: בַּמֶּה? אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא בְּחַמִּין אָנוּ עֲסוּקִין עַכְשָׁיו?! בֵּין בְּחַמִּין בֵּין בְּצוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** Hear **a parable: To what is this matter comparable? To a student who mixed** wine **with hot water for his teacher. And** the teacher **said to him: Mix** another drink **for me.** The student **said to him: With what** should I mix the wine, hot or cold water? The teacher **said to him: Aren't we dealing with hot** water? **Now** that I requested that you mix me another cup, I mean that you should mix it **either in hot** water **or in cold.**

The *gemara* now seeks to explain where Rabbi Akiva derives his position. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel: *mashal lema hadavar domeh* — to what is this comparable? To a student who had been mixing his master's wine with hot water. The master then asked him to pour more. The student asked: shall I mix it again with hot water? The master replied: need you ask? We have been dealing with hot water all along — you should have assumed hot water. But since you have gone to the trouble of asking me, fine: mix it however you like, with hot water or cold water.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי – מִכְּדִי בְּחַטָּאת עָסְקִינַן וְאָתֵי, ״חַטָּאת״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? אֶלָּא לָאו חַטָּאת קָאָמֵינָא לַהּ, אֶלָּא כׇּל קָדָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too, since we have been dealing with a sin offering** in that passage (see Leviticus 6:17–22), **why do I** need the term **"sin offering" that the Merciful One writes** in this verse? Even without this term it is clear that the Torah is referring to a sin offering. **Rather,** as in the parable, the Torah mentions a sin offering to teach: **I do not say this** *halakha* with regard to **a sin offering** alone, i.e., that only the blood of a sin offering is disqualified when it is brought inside the Sanctuary, **but** the blood of **all sacrificial** animals is disqualified by being brought into the Sanctuary.

The *nimshal*: the *Torah* was already speaking within the context of a *chatat* — *meichdi bechatat askinan*. Why then did it write *vechol chatat asher yuva midamah el ohel mo'ed el hakodesh pnima lo te'achel*? It should simply have written *vechol asher yuva midamah*, since the context already implied *chatat*. The fact that it went ahead and said *chatat* explicitly — just as the master, once pushed to speak, said whatever you like — signals that *chatat* here is not meant to limit but to expand. Thus *ela kol kodshim*: the *halacha* applies to all *kodshim*. The word *chatat*, rather than being purely restrictive, opens the door to a broader inclusion because of its very redundancy.

### Rav Huna Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: מִכְּדֵי אִיתְרַבּוֹ כׇּל קָדָשִׁים לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ לְעִנְיַן מְרִיקָה וּשְׁטִיפָה, ״חַטָּאת״ דִּכְתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמָה לִי? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: חַטָּאת אִין, מִידֵּי אַחֲרִינָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, objects to this** explanation: Accordingly, one should reach the opposite conclusion: **Since all sacrificial** animals that are sacrificed **on the altar are included** in this passage of the Torah **with regard to scouring and rinsing,** as derived by the Sages, despite the fact that this requirement is stated in connection with a sin offering (see Leviticus 6:21), it is already established that this chapter is speaking of all offerings. Consequently, **why do I** need the term **"sin offering" that the Merciful One writes** with regard to the matter of blood that entered the Sanctuary? **Learn from** the verse that in the case of **a sin offering** whose blood entered the Sanctuary, **yes,** it is disqualified, but if the blood of any **other** offering entered the Sanctuary, **no,** it is not disqualified.

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua challenged this reasoning. He argued that the immediate context of the *pasuk* was not narrowly limited to a *chatat* in the first place, and therefore the word *chatat* in the *pasuk* cannot be read as redundant. The surrounding *pesukim* in *Tzav* open with the broad heading *zot torat hachatat* — this is the law of the *chatat* — and proceed through a series of *halachot*: the *chatat* is slaughtered where the *olah* is slaughtered; blood that spatters on a garment must be laundered in a holy place; a pottery vessel used to cook the *chatat* must be broken, while a copper vessel must be scoured and rinsed with water.

Crucially, this *halacha* of *merika ushfifa* — the scouring and rinsing of vessels — is established elsewhere in the *gemara* as applying not only to a *chatat* but to all *korbanot*. Therefore, the *pesukim* immediately preceding the clause about blood brought inside were already speaking in a context broader than *chatat* alone. Rav Huna therefore argues: *chatat dechativ Rachmana lamah li* — when the *Torah* says *chatat* in connection with blood brought inside, it cannot be dismissed as redundant, because the surrounding material was not narrowly about *chatat*. On the contrary, saying *chatat* here was necessary — and it limits the *din* to a *chatat* specifically.

### Expansive Language

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְתַלְמִיד שֶׁמָּזַג לְרַבּוֹ בֵּין בְּחַמִּין בֵּין בְּצוֹנֵן, אָמַר לוֹ: אַל תִּמְזוֹג לִי אֶלָּא חַמִּין!
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna addresses Shmuel's parable: **And** if you wish to provide a comparison, it **is comparable only to a student who** initially **mixed** wine **for his teacher with both hot and cold** water, and **his teacher** then **said to him: Mix** wine **for me only** with **hot** water.

In light of this objection, the *gemara* revises the *mashal*: it is more akin to a student who was mixing wine sometimes with hot water and sometimes with cold water, and the master explicitly said: please mix it with hot water. Hot water means hot water — it is not an expansion, it is a limitation. Accordingly, the *mashal* of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel no longer holds as originally formulated. The reason for Rabbi Akiva's position must be sought elsewhere.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מֵ״חַטָּאת״ ״וְכׇל חַטָּאת״; דְּתַנְיָא: ״חַטָּאת״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַטָּאת; קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל חַטָּאת״. קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל חַטָּאת״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Huna continues: **Rather, the reasoning of Rabbi Akiva** is **from** the fact that the verse does not state simply: **"Sin offering," but** states: **"Any sin offering";** this serves to include all other offerings. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "And any **sin offering,** whereof any of the blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:23). **I have** derived that this *halakha* applies **only** with regard to **a sin offering; from where** is it derived that it also applies to **offerings of the most sacred order? The verse states: "Any sin offering." And from where** is it derived that it also applies to **offerings of lesser sanctity? The verse states: "And any sin offering."** This is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *gemara* concludes that the source for Rabbi Akiva is not the analogy of contextual redundancy but the expansive language of the *pasuk* itself — specifically the word *vechol*. It could have written simply *chatat asher yuva midamah*, but it wrote *vechol chatat* — and the word *vechol* carries an inclusive force. A *braita* makes this explicit: *chatat ein li ela chatat* — from the word *chatat* alone I know only a *chatat*. *Kodshei kodashim*, such as an *olah*, from where? *Talmud lomar vechol chatat*. *Asham* and *kodashim kalim*, such as *shelamim*, from where? *Talmud lomar vechol* — with the additional *vav* — *chatat*. These are the words of Rabbi Akiva.

### Rabbi Yose Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: אֲפִילּוּ אַתָּה מְרַבֶּה כׇּל הַיּוֹם כּוּלּוֹ, אֵינִי שׁוֹמֵעַ לְךָ אֶלָּא חַטָּאת. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַטַּאת יָחִיד, חַטַּאת צִבּוּר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״כׇּל חַטָּאת״. וְאֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַטָּאת זָכָר, חַטָּאת נְקֵבָה מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili said to** Rabbi Akiva: **Even** if **you include** offerings in this manner from the verse **all day long, I** will **not listen to you. Rather,** this verse is referring to a sin offering alone, and it should be expounded as follows: The verse states: **"Sin offering"; I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **the sin offering of an individual. From where** is it derived that the same applies to **a communal sin offering? The verse states: "Any sin offering." And** one can still say: **I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **a male sin offering; from where** is it derived that the same applies to **a female sin offering? The verse states: "And any** sin offering."

Rabbi Yose HaGelili responds to Rabbi Akiva with a characteristic rejoinder: *afilu ata marbe kol hayom kulo eini shomea lach* — even if you try to include as much as you like all day long, I am not buying it. This is the classic tension between Rabbi Akiva, who derives extensive *halachot* from extra letters and words, and Rabbi Yose HaGelili, who resists reading small textual additions as radically expanding the scope of a *pasuk*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא? אֶלָּא הָכִי קָאָמַר: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא חַטָּאת נְקֵבָה, חַטָּאת זָכָר מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״וְכׇל חַטָּאת״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it assumed that the application of this *halakha* to a male sin offering is more obvious than to a female sin offering? **Isn't it the opposite [*kelapei layya*],** as the standard sin offering of an individual is a female? **Rather, this** is what Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **is saying:** One can still ask: **I have** derived **only** that this *halakha* applies to **a female sin offering; from where** is it derived that the same applies to **a male sin offering? The verse states: "And any sin offering."**

To be clear, Rabbi Yose HaGelili does not reject the principle that *vechol* is inclusive. What he rejects is the forced reading that *vechol chatat* can mean something other than a *chatat*, when the *pasuk* explicitly says *chatat*. He accepts that *vechol* expands — but only within the category of *chatat* itself. Thus: *chatat ein li ela chatat yachid* — I know only an individual's *chatat*. *Chatat tzibur*, a communal *chatat*, from where? *Talmud lomar vechol chatat* — any *chatat*. And there is a further inclusion: *ein li ela chatat nekeva*, I know only a female *chatat* — the standard case. A male *chatat*, such as the *sa'ir* of a *nasi*, from where? *Talmud lomar vechol* — the additional *vav*. So *vechol* is inclusive, but its inclusion remains within the domain of *chatat*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: הַאי קְרָא לְהָכִי הוּא דַּאֲתָא?! וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי אוֹמֵר: (לְפִי שֶׁמָּצִינוּ) כׇּל הָעִנְיָן כּוּלּוֹ אֵינוֹ מְדַבֵּר אֶלָּא בְּפָרִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים וּשְׂעִירִים הַנִּשְׂרָפִים – לִשְׂרוֹף פְּסוּלֵיהֶן אַבֵּית הַבִּירָה, וְלַעֲמוֹד בְּלֹא תַעֲשֶׂה עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Rabbi Yosei HaGelili hold** that **this verse comes for this** purpose, to teach the *halakha* of sin offerings whose blood was brought inside the Sanctuary? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili says: As we have found the entire matter** that is stated in the verse: "And any sin offering, whereof any of the blood is brought into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten; it shall be burned with fire" (Leviticus 6:23), is not stated with regard to a standard sin offering improperly brought inside the Sanctuary; rather, it **speaks only about bulls that are burned and goats that are burned,** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili elaborates: These are unique sin offerings, and the Torah states that their blood should be brought inside the Sanctuary. The verse is stated with regard to these sin offerings both in order to command the Jewish people **to burn their disqualified ones in the Temple and** in order **to establish a prohibition with regard to their consumption.**

The *gemara* then asks: does Rabbi Yose HaGelili actually read the *pasuk* as being about an outer *chatat* at all? In fact, a *braita* records that Rabbi Yose HaGelili holds the entire passage is speaking only about *parim hanisrafim use'irim hanisrafim* — the inner *chata'ot* that are burned — and comes to teach two things: that if a *pesul* occurs in an inner *chatat*, its disqualified meat is burned within the *beit hamikdash* itself, in the *beit habirah*; and that there is a *lav* against eating the meat of such an offering. In his reading, the *pasuk* has nothing to do with invalidating an outer *chatat* whose blood was brought inside.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: חַטָּאת שֶׁנִּכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים – מִנַּיִן? אָמַר לָהֶם: ״הֵן לֹא הוּבָא״.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: If you expound the verse in this fashion, then **from where** is it derived that a standard **sin offering whose blood entered the innermost sanctum,** the Holy of Holies, must be burned? Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **said to them** that it is derived from the verse: **"Behold, its blood was not brought** into the Sanctuary within" (Leviticus 10:18). This indicates that had the sin offering referred to in the verse been brought inside the Sanctuary, it should have been burned.

When asked where he then derives the *halacha* that an outer *chatat* is invalidated by having its blood brought inside, he answers: from the story of Moshe and Aharon, where Moshe says *hen lo huva et damah el hakodesh pnimah* — the blood was not brought inside — implying that had it been brought inside, the burning would have been understandable. It is from that narrative that he learns an outer *chatat* is invalidated.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לִדְבָרָיו דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **spoke according to the statement of Rabbi Akiva,** i.e., although Rabbi Yosei HaGelili interprets the verse in a different manner, according to Rabbi Akiva's explanation it should be understood as referring only to a sin offering, not to other offerings.

As the *gemara* concludes: *ledvarav de'Rabbi Akiva ka'amar* — when Rabbi Yose HaGelili was debating Rabbi Akiva, his point was: even according to your reading of the *pasuk* as concerning an outer *korban*, the word *vechol chatat* cannot be legitimately extended beyond the category of *chatat* itself.

## Outside vs. Inside

### Two Bowls Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חַטָּאת שֶׁקִּבֵּל דָּמָה בִּשְׁנֵי כּוֹסוֹת, יָצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן לַחוּץ – הַפְּנִימִי כָּשֵׁר. נִכְנַס אֶחָד מֵהֶם לִפְנִים – רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי מַכְשִׁיר בַּחִיצוֹן, וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹסְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **a sin offering** whose blood placement is on the external altar **whose blood** the priest **collected in two cups,** if **one of them left** the Temple courtyard and was thereby disqualified, **the** cup that remained **inside** the courtyard is **fit** to be presented. If **one of the** cups **entered inside** the Sanctuary and was thereby disqualified, **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili deems** the blood **in the** cup that remained **outside** the Sanctuary, in the courtyard, **fit** to be presented, **and the Rabbis deem it disqualified** from being presented.

The *gemara* now transitions to explore various scenarios in which the invalidity arising from blood brought inside plays out. The *mishnah* presents the following case: a *chatat* whose blood was received in two bowls — *bishnei kossot* — and one of those bowls was taken outside the *beit hamikdash*. The blood in that bowl becomes invalid. But does the entire *korban* become invalid? The *mishnah* rules no: the outer blood is invalid, but the remaining inner bowl remains *kasher*, and if there is sufficient blood remaining, one proceeds to apply it on the *mizbe'ach*. The *korban* is valid. *Hapnimi kasher*.

The next case presents the mirror scenario: one of the two bowls was brought inside — *nichnas echad meihem lifnimah*, brought into the *heichal*. Here the dispute: Rabbi Yose HaGelili rules the outer blood remains *kasher* — *machshir bachitzon*. The *chachamim* rule that the entire *korban* is invalid — *poslin*.

### Partial Blood Inside

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי: מָה אִם בְּמָקוֹם שֶׁהַמַּחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בַּחוּץ – לֹא עָשָׂה אֶת הַמְשׁוּיָּר כַּיּוֹצֵא, מָקוֹם שֶׁאֵין הַמַּחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בִּפְנִים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא נַעֲשֶׂה אֶת הַמְשׁוּיָּר כַּנִּכְנָס?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili said** in support of his opinion: The *halakha* is that if one slaughters an offering with the intent that its blood be presented outside of the Temple courtyard, the offering is disqualified, but if his intention was that the blood be presented inside the Sanctuary, the offering is not disqualified. **Just as in** a case where part of the blood reached **a place where the intent** to present the blood there **disqualifies** the offering, i.e., **outside** the Temple courtyard, and yet when some of the blood is taken there it **does not render the** status of the **remaining** blood disqualified **like** that of blood that **leaves** the courtyard, so too, in a case where part of the blood reached **a place where the intent** to present the blood there **does not disqualify** the offering, i.e., **inside** the Sanctuary, **is it not logical that we will not deem** the status of **the remaining** blood **like** that of blood that **entered** the Sanctuary?

This asymmetry is striking, and Rashi articulates its textual basis clearly. The *chachamim* derive the *din* of blood brought inside from the *pasuk*: *vechol chatat asher yuva midamah el hakodesh pnimah* — the word *midamah*, "from its blood," signals that even some of the blood suffices to invalidate the *korban*. Rabbi Yose HaGelili, however, learns the *din* from the story of Moshe and Aharon, where the *pasuk* says *hen lo huva et damah* — all of its blood — not *midamah*. His source therefore implies that only bringing all of the blood inside would trigger the invalidity.

To clarify the practical scenario: the invalidity applies only when the blood has not yet been validly applied to the *mizbe'ach*. If one bowl was already properly applied and the second bowl was then brought inside, the *korban* is fine. It is only when no valid application has yet occurred that bringing blood inside — even a partial amount — creates the problem.

### Intent & Atonement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכְנַס לְכַפֵּר, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא כִּפֵּר – פָּסוּל. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּכַפֵּר. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם הִכְנִיס שׁוֹגֵג – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If all of the blood of a sin offering whose blood placement is on the external altar **entered** the Sanctuary **to atone** through sprinkling, **despite** the fact **that** the priest **did not** actually sprinkle the blood to **atone,** the offering is **disqualified;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Eliezer. Rabbi Shimon says:** The offering is disqualified **only when he atones** and sprinkles the blood in the Sanctuary. **Rabbi Yehuda says: If he took** the blood **into** the Sanctuary **unwittingly,** the blood remains **fit** to be presented.

The *mishnah* records further *machlokot* concerning blood brought inside. Rabbi Eliezer holds: *nichnas lechaper af al pi shelo chipar pasul* — if one brought the blood inside with the intent to apply it there for atonement, even if he never actually performed the application, the *korban* is invalid. The *pasuk* says *asher yuva midamah el hakodesh lechaper bakodesh* — and Rabbi Eliezer reads *lechaper* as describing intent alone, not requiring the act. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *ad sheyichpar* — the blood must actually have been applied in some atonement ritual inside; mere entry without application does not invalidate. Rabbi Yehuda adds a further condition: *im hichnis shogeg kasher* — if one brought the blood inside inadvertently, the *korban* remains *kasher*, since the *pasuk* emphasizes purposive intent — *lechaper bakodesh*.

### Tzitz Validation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כָּל הַדָּמִים פְּסוּלִין שֶׁנְּתָנוֹ עַל גַּבֵּי הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – לֹא הִרְצָה הַצִּיץ אֶלָּא עַל הַטָּמֵא. שֶׁהַצִּיץ מְרַצֶּה עַל הַטָּמֵא, וְאֵינוֹ מְרַצֶּה עַל הַיּוֹצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **all the blood disqualified** for presentation **that was placed on the altar, the frontplate effects acceptance only** for offerings sacrificed **that** are **ritually impure.** Although it is written with regard to the frontplate worn on the forehead of the High Priest: "And it shall be upon Aaron's forehead, and Aaron shall bear the iniquity committed in the sacred matters" (Exodus 28:38), this does not apply to all disqualifications of offerings. This is **because the frontplate effects acceptance for** offerings sacrificed **that** are **ritually impure but does not effect acceptance for** offerings **that leave** the courtyard.

The *mishnah* then introduces a distinct and independent *halacha*: *kol hadamim pesulim shenitnu al gabei hamizbe'ach lo hurtzu* — invalid blood that was placed on the *mizbe'ach* does not become validated thereby. The *korban* is not rendered *kasher* simply because the blood made contact with the altar. Furthermore, *lo hirtza hatzitz* — the *tzitz*, the golden frontplate worn by the *kohen gadol*, does not validate such blood. The *tzitz* has the power to validate only in the case of *tumat hakodashim*: if blood that became *tamei* was applied to the *mizbe'ach*, the presence of the *tzitz* makes the offering valid. But blood that went outside — *hayotzei* — is not subject to the *tzitz*'s validating power. *Hatzitz mertze al hatamei ve'eino mertze al hayotzei*.

This is a completely separate *halacha* from the question of blood brought inside. It addresses what happens when already-invalid blood is placed on the outer *mizbe'ach*, and it distinguishes between two forms of invalidity: *tum'ah*, which the *tzitz* can overcome, and *yetziah*, blood that left its proper domain, which the *tzitz* cannot rectify.

## Logic of Inside

### Kal Vachomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת, בַּחוּץ – לֹא פָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבִּפְנִים; מְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת, בִּפְנִים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יִפְסוֹל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת שֶׁבַּחוּץ?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the dispute between Rabbi Yosei HaGelili and the Rabbis as to whether the blood of a sin offering in a second cup is disqualified if the blood in the first cup entered the Sanctuary, it **is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili said:** This is **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as in** a case where part of the blood reached **a place where** the **intent** to present the blood there **disqualifies** the offering, i.e., **outside** the Temple courtyard, and yet that **blood that** was taken **outside does not disqualify** the blood that is still **inside** the courtyard, so too, in a case where part of the blood reached **a place where** the **intent** to present the blood there **does not disqualify** the offering, i.e., **inside** the Sanctuary, **is it not logical that** the **blood that** was taken **inside will not disqualify** the blood **that** is still **outside** the Sanctuary?

A *braita* records Rabbi Yose HaGelili's *kal vachomer* at greater length, and the exchange with the *chachamim* that follows illuminates the underlying conceptual structure. Rabbi Yose HaGelili states: *u'mah bemakom shemachshava poselet bachutz lo pasal dam shebachutz et shebifnim* — on the outside, where thought invalidates, some blood brought outside does not invalidate the remaining blood. *Makom she'ein machshava poselet* — on the inside, where thought does not invalidate — *eino din shelo yifsol dam shebifnim et shebachutz* — certainly blood brought inside should not invalidate the rest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֶׁר יוּבָא מִדָּמָהּ״ – אֲפִילּוּ מִקְצָת דָּמָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili: **But** the verse **states:** "And any sin offering, **whereof any of the blood is brought** into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:23). This indicates that **even** if **part of its blood** is brought inside the Sanctuary, the sin offering is disqualified.

The *chachamim* respond: *harei omer asher yuva midamah* — the *pasuk* says *midamah*, even some of the blood. This is explicit: even partial blood brought inside invalidates.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם, קַל וָחוֹמֶר לַיּוֹצֵא מֵעַתָּה: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁאֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת, בִּפְנִים – פּוֹסֵל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת שֶׁבַּחוּץ; מְקוֹם שֶׁמַּחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת, בַּחוּץ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּפְסוֹל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבִּפְנִים?
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili **said to** the Rabbis: If you expound the verse in that manner, then **from now** one can derive **an *a fortiori*** inference **with regard to the** blood that **leaves** the courtyard: **And just as in** a case where part of the blood reached **a place where** the **intent** to present the blood there **does not disqualify** the offering, i.e., **inside** the Sanctuary, the **blood that** was taken **inside disqualifies** the blood that is still **outside,** i.e., in the courtyard, so too, in a case where part of the blood reached **a place where** the **intent** to present the blood there **disqualifies** the offering, i.e., **outside** the Temple courtyard, **is it not logical that** the **blood that** was taken **outside will disqualify** the blood **that** is still **inside** the courtyard? Why do the Rabbis deem this blood fit?

Rabbi Yose HaGelili does not relent. He says: if you are going to accept that *midamah* means even partial blood invalidates on the inside, then by the same *kal vachomer*, partial blood brought outside should certainly invalidate — since the outside is the more powerfully invalidating place. *Kal vachomer leyotzei me'ata*: the outside, where even thought invalidates, should certainly be such that some blood brought outside invalidates the rest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמְרוּ לוֹ: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״אֲשֶׁר יוּבָא״ – הַנִּכְנָס פּוֹסֵל וְאֵין הַיּוֹצֵא פּוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Rabbis **said to** Rabbi Yosei HaGelili in response: **But** the verse **states:** "And any sin offering, whereof any of the blood **is brought** into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:23). This teaches that only blood **that enters** the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the blood in the courtyard, **but** the blood **that leaves** the courtyard **does not disqualify** the blood in the courtyard.

The *chachamim* respond: *harei omer asher yuva — hanichnas posel ve'ein hayotzei posel*. This is a special *halacha* grounded in the *pasuk*: *asher yuva*, specifically when brought inside. The *Torah* has established an asymmetry — inside invalidates even partially, outside does not — and yes, it is counterintuitive, but it is what the *pasuk* teaches. The logic does not dictate the *din*; the *din* is the *din*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּתְהֵא מַחְשָׁבָה בִּפְנִים פּוֹסֶלֶת מִקַּל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבִּפְנִים – מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בַּחוּץ, מְקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁתְּהֵא מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בִּפְנִים?
>
> **Translation:** In light of this discussion, the Gemara asks: **And let intent** to present the blood **inside** the Sanctuary **disqualify** the offering **based on an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as in a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **outside does not disqualify** the rest of the blood **that is inside** the courtyard, and yet **intent** to present the blood in this place **disqualifies** the offering, i.e., **outside** the courtyard, so too, in **a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **inside** the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the rest of the blood **that is outside** the Sanctuary, in the courtyard, **is it not logical that intent** to present the blood in this place **will disqualify** the offering, i.e., **inside** the courtyard?

Stepping back to understand the conceptual logic: blood brought outside the *mikdash* becomes itself invalid — so the remaining blood is unaffected and can still be validly applied. Blood brought inside, however, does not simply become invalid blood. Rather, bringing the blood inside constitutes performing the ritual of an inner *chatat* — it is an act of *lechaper bakodesh*, of doing an atonement ritual in the inner sanctum. That act invalidates the *korban* as a whole. It is not that the inside is a "bad place" that contaminates whatever enters it; rather, performing the inner-*chatat* ritual upon an outer *korban* transforms its status and renders the entire *korban* invalid. That is why the *halacha* requires the element of *lechaper bakodesh* — purposive intent toward inner atonement — and why even a small quantity of blood suffices: it is not a threshold of volume but the performance of a ritual act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַשְּׁלִישִׁי״ – מָקוֹם שֶׁיְּהֵא מְשׁוּלָּשׁ בְּדָם, בְּבָשָׂר, בְּאֵימוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that one does not derive this *a fortiori* inference, as the verse **states:** "And if it be eaten at all **on the third day,** it is *piggul*; it shall not be accepted" (Leviticus 19:7), and the Sages expounded this verse with regard to the *halakha* of intent while slaughtering an offering. This verse teaches that the **place where** improper intent disqualifies the offering **must be triply** functional: **For** the presenting of **the blood, for** the eating of **the meat,** and **for** the burning of **the sacrificial portions** of the offering that are consumed on the altar. In other words, it must be outside its designated place with regard to all three of these issues. Consequently, intent to present the blood in the Sanctuary does not disqualify the blood.

The *gemara* then asks: now that we have established that the inside is more powerful than the outside when it comes to actual blood — should we extend this and say that *machshava* directed inward also invalidates? *Tehei machshava bifnim poselet mikal vachomer*: where the blood itself invalidates powerfully, should thought not do the same? The *gemara* answers: *harei hu omer — bayom hashlishi* — the verse that teaches the *din* of *chutz limkomo* uses the word *shlishi*, and that word requires that the place be *meshulash* — tripled, meaning that blood, meat, and *imurin* are all subject to invalidity there. The outside satisfies this: all three are invalidated when removed from the *mikdash*. The inside does not satisfy this criterion, because meat and *imurin* brought into the *heichal* are not thereby invalidated. Therefore thought about the inside does not invalidate.

### Meshulash Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא תְּהֵא מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בַּחוּץ מִקַּל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה מְקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת שֶׁבַּחוּץ – אֵין מַחְשָׁבָה פּוֹסֶלֶת בִּפְנִים, מְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבְּפָנִים – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁלֹּא יְהֵא פּוֹסֵל מַחְשָׁבָה בַּחוּץ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests the opposite: **And** if so, **let intent** to present the blood **outside** the Sanctuary **not disqualify** the offering **based on an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as** in **a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **inside** the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the rest of the blood **that is outside** in the courtyard, and yet **intent** to present the blood **inside** the Sanctuary **does not disqualify** the offering, so too, in **a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **outside** the courtyard **does not disqualify** the rest of the blood **that is inside** the courtyard, **is it not logical that intent** to present the blood **outside** the courtyard **does not disqualify** the offering?

The *gemara* then turns the *kal vachomer* in the opposite direction. Fine — thought about the inside does not invalidate because the inside is not *meshulash*. But now one might argue: since thought does not invalidate on the inside even though actual blood does, and since the outside is less powerful than the inside when it comes to blood — perhaps thought should not invalidate on the outside either? *Velo tehei machshava poselet bachutz mikal vachomer*: on the inside, where even a little blood invalidates the whole *korban*, thought still does not invalidate. On the outside, where a little blood does not invalidate the rest, certainly thought should not invalidate.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״שְׁלִישִׁי״ – חוּץ לִזְמַנּוֹ, ״פִּגּוּל״ – חוּץ לִמְקוֹמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that **the verse states:** "And if it be eaten at all on the third day, it is *piggul*; it shall not be accepted," and the Sages interpreted this verse as follows: "On the **third** day" is referring to intent to perform its rites **beyond its** designated **time;** "it is ***piggul*"** is referring to intent to perform its rites **outside its** designated **area.**

The answer: *talmud lomar shlishi* — we know from the word *shlishi* in the *pasuk* of *bayom hashlishi* that thought of the wrong time invalidates, and from the extra word *piggul* we learn thought of the wrong place. And *shlishi* establishes that this applies specifically to a place that is *meshulash* — where blood, meat, and *imurin* are all subject to invalidity. The outside satisfies this. Therefore, thought about the outside does indeed invalidate, and no *kal vachomer* can remove that. The *pasuk* has established it explicitly.

What is striking is the irony the *gemara* now makes explicit: thought about the inside does not invalidate even though the inside is more powerful than the outside when it comes to actual blood. Yet this is precisely what the *pesukim* teach. The criterion for *machshava* invalidation is not about which place is more "dangerous" in terms of actual blood, but about the quality of *meshulash* — whether all three components of a *korban* are at risk there.

### Meat Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּשָׂר [הַיּוֹצֵא לַחוּץ – פָּסוּל], הַנִּכְנָס לִפְנִים – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** A *baraita* teaches: The **meat** of offerings **that left** its designated area and was taken **to** a place that is **outside** its designated area for consumption, which is outside the wall of Jerusalem in the case of offerings of lesser sanctity and outside the wall of the Temple courtyard in the case of offerings of the most sacred order, is **disqualified** from being eaten. By contrast, meat of offerings **that entered inside** the Sanctuary is not disqualified and remains **fit** to be eaten.

The *gemara* presses the *kal vachomer* further into the domain of meat. *Bassar hayotzei lachutz pasul, hanichnas lifnim kasher*. Meat that goes outside the *mikdash* is invalid; meat brought inside the *heichal* is not. This is already counterintuitive given the pattern for blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁיְּהֵא בַּדִּין שֶׁפָּסוּל: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבִּפְנִים – בָּשָׂר הַיּוֹצֵא לַחוּץ פָּסוּל, מְקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת שֶׁבַּחוּץ – בָּשָׂר הַנִּכְנָס בִּפְנִים אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁיִּפְסוֹל?
>
> **Translation:** One might have thought **that it should be** derived **based on a logical inference that** this meat is **disqualified,** as follows: **And just as in a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **outside** the courtyard **does not disqualify** the rest of the blood **that** remained **inside** the courtyard, yet **meat that leaves** and is taken **outside** the courtyard is **disqualified,** so too, in **a place where** the part of the **blood that** went **inside** the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the rest of the blood **that is outside** the Sanctuary, **is it not right that meat that enters** the Sanctuary **should be disqualified?**

And the *gemara* asks: should we not say, by *kal vachomer*, that since the inside is more powerfully invalidating when it comes to blood, it should also invalidate meat? *Shehaya badin sheyifsol*: meat brought inside should be *pasul*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״מִדָּמָהּ״ – דָּמָהּ וְלֹא (בשר) [בְּשָׂרָהּ].
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* explains that one does not derive this inference, as the verse **states:** "And any sin offering, whereof any **of the blood** is brought into the Tent of Meeting to atone in the Sanctuary, shall not be eaten" (Leviticus 6:23), which indicates that the **blood of** an offering that is brought inside is disqualified, **but not meat** that enters the Sanctuary.

The answer: *midamah* — the *pasuk* specifies "from its blood." *Damah velo bassar*. Only blood invalidates when brought inside; meat is explicitly excluded by the *pasuk*'s language. The unique *din* of *pnimah* applies only to blood.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַל וָחוֹמֶר מֵעַתָּה: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁפָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבִּפְנִים אֶת שֶׁבַּחוּץ – בָּשָׂר הַנִּכְנָס לְפָנִים כָּשֵׁר; מָקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא פָּסַל דָּם שֶׁבַּחוּץ אֶת שֶׁבִּפְנִים – בָּשָׂר הַיּוֹצֵא לַחוּץ אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁכָּשֵׁר?!
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **From now,** as it has been established that the meat of offerings that is brought inside the Sanctuary is not disqualified, one can suggest **an *a fortiori*** inference: **And just as in a place where blood that** went **inside** the Sanctuary **disqualifies** the blood **that** remained **outside** in the courtyard, and yet **meat that enters** the Sanctuary is **fit,** therefore, in **a place where** the **blood that** went **outside** the courtyard **does not disqualify** the blood **that is inside** the courtyard, **is it not logical that meat that left** and was taken **outside** the courtyard should be **fit?**

Then the *kal vachomer* is reversed: since meat brought inside the *heichal* is not invalid, and the inside is such a powerful invalidator — certainly meat brought outside should also not be invalid? *Kal vachomer me'ata*: on the inside, where blood powerfully invalidates, meat does not. On the outside, which is less powerful in terms of blood, meat should certainly not be invalid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״וּבָשָׂר בַּשָּׂדֶה טְרֵפָה לֹא תֹאכֵלוּ״ – כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא בָּשָׂר חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתוֹ, נֶאֱסַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that one does not derive this *a fortiori* inference, as the verse **states: "And you shall not eat any flesh that is torn of beasts in the field"** (Exodus 22:30). The apparently superfluous term "in the field" teaches a general *halakha*: **Once meat has left** and been removed **outside of its boundary,** i.e., the area in which it is permitted to consume it, it **has become prohibited.**

The answer: *uvassar bassadeh tereifah lo socheilu* — we learn from the principle that meat in an uncontained place, in the *sadeh*, is rendered unfit. *Keivan sheyatza bassar chutz mimchitzato ne'essar* — once meat leaves its proper domain, it is forbidden. That is its own *din*, derived from its own source.

The conceptual resolution the *gemara* has arrived at is this: the inside is not simply a "bad place" that invalidates whatever enters it. The *heichal* is not off-limits in the way the outside is off-limits. The problem of blood brought inside is that it constitutes the performance of an inner-*chatat* ritual. Meat has no such ritual significance inside; it is never the instrument of atonement in the *heichal*. The outside, by contrast, truly is the wrong place — for blood, meat, and *imurin* — because those things are out of their proper *mechitzah* entirely. That is why the *din* of *bassar hayotzei* holds, and why meat brought inside does not trigger any analogous invalidity.

## Kodesh Scope

### Inner Chamber

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״פְּנִימָה״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא פְּנִימָה, הֵיכָל מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה״.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara returns to its discussion of the blood of a sin offering that entered the Sanctuary. **The Sages taught:** It is stated that when Moses questioned Aaron as to why a sin offering was not consumed, he said: "Behold, its blood was not brought into the sacred place **within"** (Leviticus 10:18). This indicates that had the blood been brought within, Moses would have understood that the offering would have been disqualified and rendered prohibited for consumption. The *baraita* comments: **I have** derived **only** that the offering is disqualified if the blood is brought **within,** i.e., into the Holy of Holies; **from where** is it derived that the same applies if it was merely brought into the **Sanctuary? The verse states: "Into the sacred place within,"** and this sacred place is the Sanctuary.

The *gemara* cites a *braita*: *pnimah ein li ela pnimah* — from the word *pnimah* alone I would know only that blood brought into the innermost chamber, the *kodesh kodashim*, invalidates. *Heichal minayin* — how do I know that bringing blood into the *heichal* also invalidates? *Talmud lomar el hakodesh pnimah* — the extra word *kodesh* teaches that even *el hakodesh* — the *heichal* — suffices. So both words are required: *pnimah* for the *kodesh kodashim*, and *kodesh* for the *heichal*.

### Rava's Words

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְיֹאמַר ״קֹדֶשׁ״, וְאַל וְיֹאמַר ״פְּנִימָה״! אָמַר רָבָא: בָּא זֶה וְלִימֵּד עַל זֶה; מִידֵּי דְּהָוֵה אַ״תּוֹשָׁב וְשָׂכִיר״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this derivation: **And let** the verse **state** only **"sacred place," and** it need **not state "within."** If blood that entered the Sanctuary is already disqualified, this *halakha* certainly applies if it was brought farther inside, to the Holy of Holies. **Rava says: This** verse **comes and teaches about that** verse. In other words, had the verse stated only "sacred place," it would have been interpreted as referring to the Holy of Holies. The addition of "within" indicates that this sacred place is the Sanctuary, while the term "within" is referring to the Holy of Holies. The Gemara cites a similar example: This is **just as it is with regard to a tenant and a hired worker.**

But Rava questions this: *viyomar kodesh ve'al yomar pnimah* — the *Torah* should have simply said *el hakodesh* referring to the *heichal*, and by *kal vachomer* I would have known that the more inner *kodesh kodashim* is certainly also included. Why was it necessary to say *pnimah* at all?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״תּוֹשָׁב״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם, ״שָׂכִיר״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning *teruma*: The verse states: "A tenant of a priest or a hired worker shall not eat of the consecrated" (Leviticus 22:10). **"A tenant"; this** is referring to a Hebrew slave who has been **acquired** as **a permanent acquisition,** i.e., one who said he wishes to stay with his master. This slave has his ear pierced and he remains with his master until the Jubilee Year. **"A hired worker"; this** is referring to a Hebrew slave who has been **acquired** for **an acquisition of** six **years,** the standard period of servitude for a Hebrew slave.

Rava answers: *bazeh velimmed al zeh* — this word teaches about the other. Had the *Torah* written only *kodesh*, I would have read *kodesh* to mean *kodesh kodashim* — the inner sanctum is the natural referent of *kodesh* in its most elevated sense. It is only because the *Torah* also writes *pnimah*, which clearly refers to the *kodesh kodashim*, that I know *kodesh* in this verse must mean the *heichal*. The two words thus work in tandem: *pnimah* establishes the outer reference of *kodesh* as the *heichal*, and *kodesh* in turn confirms that the *heichal* triggers the invalidity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יֹאמַר ״תּוֹשָׁב״ וְאַל יֹאמַר ״שָׂכִיר״, וַאֲנִי אוֹמֵר: קָנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם אֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל, קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **Let** the verse **say "tenant" and let it not say "hired worker," and I** would **say:** If one who is **acquired** as **a permanent acquisition does not partake** of his master's, i.e., the priest's, *teruma*, as despite his status as a slave he is not considered his master's property, is it **not all the more so** logical that one who is **acquired** for **an acquisition of** six **years** should not be permitted to partake of *teruma*?

Rava supports this with an analogy: *midi dehava atoshav vesachir* — as we find with the terms *toshav* and *sachir* in the context of a *kohen*'s household. A *toshav* is a Hebrew slave owned until the *yovel*, and a *sachir* is a Hebrew slave for six years. The *Torah* says both cannot eat *terumah*. One might ask: just say *toshav* — the more permanent slave — and from a *kal vachomer* I would know the six-year slave certainly cannot eat *terumah* either.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִילּוּ כֵּן, הָיִיתִי אוֹמֵר: ״תּוֹשָׁב״ – זֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים, אֲבָל קְנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם יְהֵא אוֹכֵל; בָּא ״שָׂכִיר״ וְלִימֵּד עַל ״תּוֹשָׁב״, שֶׁזֶּה קָנוּי קִנְיַן עוֹלָם וְזֶה קָנוּי קִנְיַן שָׁנִים – וְאֵינוֹ אוֹכֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* answers: **If so,** that the verse were stated in this manner, **I would say: "A tenant"; this is** one who was **acquired** for **an acquisition of** six **years,** as the term itself is ambiguous, **but** one who was **acquired** as **a permanent acquisition may partake** of *teruma*. Therefore, the term **"hired worker,"** which is certainly referring to one who is less permanent than a tenant, **comes and teaches about** the meaning of the term **"tenant," that this** one was **acquired** as **a permanent acquisition and that** one was **acquired** for **an acquisition of** six **years, and** both this one and that one **may not partake** of *teruma*. Similar reasoning applies in the above case concerning the terms "sacred place" and "within."

The answer: the reason is that *toshav* and *sachir* clarify each other. Had it said only *toshav*, I might have read it to mean the six-year slave, and concluded the permanent slave can eat *terumah*. But *sachir* clarifies that *toshav* means the permanent one. So too here: *pnimah* establishes what *kodesh* means.

### Abaye Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: בִּשְׁלָמָא הָתָם – תְּרֵי גוּפֵי נִינְהוּ; וְאַף עַל גַּב דַּהֲוָה לֵיהּ לִקְרָא לְמִכְתַּב ״נִרְצָע לֹא יֹאכַל״, וְאִידַּךְ אָתֵי בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר – מִילְּתָא דְּאָתְיָא בְּקַל וָחוֹמֶר טָרַח וְכָתַב לַהּ קְרָא. אֶלָּא הָכָא, כֵּיוָן (דְּאִיכָּא) דְּאִיפְּסֵל בְּהֵיכָל, (הָכָא) לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים מַאי בָּעֵי?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rava: Are these cases comparable? **Granted, there,** the tenant and the hired worker **are two bodies. And** this is significant, as **even though the verse could have written** explicitly that **a pierced** tenant **may not partake** of *teruma*, from which the *halakha* of a Hebrew slave for six years could have been inferred, **and the other** case, that of a slave for six years, is therefore **a matter that can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, it need not be stated explicitly. Nevertheless, there is a principle: At times, with regard to **a matter that can be derived through an *a fortiori*** inference, the **verse** nevertheless **takes the trouble and writes it** explicitly. **But here,** the same blood enters the Holy of Holies via the Sanctuary, and **once it is disqualified in the Sanctuary, why is it necessary** for the verse to teach that this blood is disqualified when it enters **the innermost sanctum,** the Holy of Holies?

Abaye objects to this comparison. *Bishlama hatam trei gufe ninhu* — there it is justified because *toshav* and *sachir* are two different people. The *halacha* genuinely applies to both individuals, and even if one could have been derived from a *kal vachomer*, there is no harm in stating both explicitly — *milta de'ati bekal vachomer tarach vechatav lah kra*. The *Torah* sometimes states explicitly what could have been inferred.

But here, Abaye argues, the situation is fundamentally different. The *Torah* could have written *ohel mo'ed* explicitly — making clear it meant the *heichal* — rather than using the ambiguous term *kodesh*. And then there would be no need to address the *kodesh kodashim* at all. Why not? Because *keivan de'ifshal beheichal, lifnai velifnim mai ba'ei* — once the blood is invalidated in the *heichal*, it is already *pasul*. The *kodesh kodashim* adds no independent invalidity, since the invalidity occurred at the threshold of the *heichal*. So the *Torah* should have been explicit about *heichal* and said nothing about *kodesh kodashim*, since the latter contributes nothing. There is therefore no parallel to the *toshav-sachir* case, where both subjects are independently meaningful.

### Intent & Path

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא נִצְרְכָא אֶלָּא לְדֶרֶךְ מְשׁוּפָּשׁ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא הֲבָאָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: כֹּל מִידֵּי דְּחַשֵּׁיב עֲלֵיהּ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים – לָא מִיפְּסֵל בְּהֵיכָל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye says:** This mention of "within" **is necessary only for** a case where the blood entered the Holy of Holies not via the Sanctuary but in **a roundabout manner,** e.g., from the roof or the loft of the Holy of Holies, without having entered the Sanctuary. **Rava said to** Abaye: **But** an expression of **bringing is written in** the verse: "Behold, its blood was not brought into the sacred place within," which indicates a standard manner of bringing it into the Holy of Holies. **Rather, Rava says:** In **any** circumstance **where** the priest **intended** to bring the blood inside **the innermost sanctum, it is not disqualified** when it is only **in the Sanctuary,** and therefore it was necessary for the verse to teach both disqualifications.

Abaye offers one possible resolution: *lo nitzrecha ela derech meshubash* — the *pasuk* concerning *kodesh kodashim* is needed for a case where one reaches the *kodesh kodashim* via a roundabout route, not through the *heichal*. In that case, the *heichal* threshold is bypassed and the invalidity of the *kodesh kodashim* is independently operative.

However, Rava is not satisfied with this answer: *vehuva ketiv asher yuva midamah* — the *pasuk* says *yuva*, brought in the natural way. An irregular route through the attic, for instance, does not qualify. Therefore Rava states his own resolution: *kol midi dechashiv alei lifnai velifnim lo mipasal beheichal* — any blood whose intent is directed specifically toward the *kodesh kodashim* is not invalidated when it passes through the *heichal*. The *pasuk* conditions the invalidity on *lechaper bakodesh* — purposive intent. If one's intent is to apply the blood on the *aron* in the *kodesh kodashim*, traversing the *heichal* does not trigger the invalidity; the invalidity occurs only when the blood reaches the place it was intended for. Thus the *kodesh kodashim* can serve as an independent source of invalidity for blood whose intent was directed there from the outset, and the *heichal* does not preempt it.

## Deeper Inner

### Par & Saʿir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: פַּר הֶעְלֵם דָּבָר שֶׁל צִבּוּר וּשְׂעִיר עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁהִכְנִיס דָּמָן לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים – מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to the **bull for an unwitting communal sin and** the **goat for** a sin of **idol worship,** whose blood should be presented in the Sanctuary, if it occurred **that** the priest **brought their blood into the innermost sanctum, what is** the *halakha*?

Rava now raises a new question about a specific class of inner *chata'ot*. The *par he'elam davar shel tzibur* and the *sa'ir avodat kochavim* — the communal goat brought for unwitting idolatry — are inner *chata'ot* in the sense that their blood is applied inside, in the *heichal*. However, unlike the *Yom Kippur* offerings, their blood does not go into the *kodesh kodashim*. Entry into the *kodesh kodashim* is restricted by the verse *lo yavo bechol et el hakodesh*, and only on *Yom Kippur*, *bezot yavo Aharon el hakodesh*, does the *kohen gadol* bring blood into the *kodesh kodashim*.

### Yom Kippur Return

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן: ״אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה״ – כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּקָרֵינַן ״אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ קָרֵינַן לֵיהּ ״פְּנִימָה״, כֹּל הֵיכָא דְּלָא קָרֵינַן ״אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ לָא קָרֵינַן ״פְּנִימָה״; אוֹ דִלְמָא שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָן הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains the sides of the dilemma: **Do we say** that since the verse states: **"Into the sacred place within"** (Leviticus 10:18) as a single phrase, and therefore **anywhere that we read** a prohibition against bringing the blood **"into the sacred place,"** i.e., that the blood is disqualified by being brought into the Sanctuary, **we** also **read** a prohibition against bringing the blood **"within,"** i.e., that it is likewise disqualified when it is brought into the Holy of Holies; but **anywhere that we do not read** a prohibition against bringing the blood **"into the sacred place,"** such as with regard to these offerings, whose blood is supposed to be brought into the Sanctuary, **we do not read** a prohibition against bringing the blood **"within"** the Holy of Holies? **Or perhaps,** since in any event the Holy of Holies **is not the** proper **place** for the blood **of these** offerings, it is disqualified.

The question Rava poses: *shehichnis damam lifnai velifnim mahu* — what if the blood of the *par he'elam davar* or the *sa'ir avodat zarah* was brought all the way into the *kodesh kodashim*? Would that invalidate? Two possibilities are considered. On one hand: *mi amrinan el hakodesh pnimah — kol heicha dekarinan el hakodesh karinan lei pnimah* — perhaps the *din* of *pnimah* only applies where the *heichal* itself is already an invalidating threshold. These offerings are supposed to go into the *heichal* — so the *heichal* is not an off-limits place for their blood. Does taking it even further, into the *kodesh kodashim*, trigger invalidity? Or do we say: *she'lo bimkoman hu* — this is simply not their proper place, and bringing them where they do not belong invalidates them regardless?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָן הוּא – פַּר וְשָׂעִיר שֶׁל יוֹם הַכִּיפּוּרִים שֶׁהִזָּה מִדָּמָן עַל הַבַּדִּים, וְהוֹצִיאָן לַהֵיכָל וְהִכְנִיסָן, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that** the Holy of Holies **is not the** proper **place** for the blood **of these** offerings and they are therefore disqualified, one can raise another dilemma: In the case of the **bull and goat of Yom Kippur, where** the High Priest **sprinkled from their blood on the staves** of the Ark, as required (see Leviticus 16:14), **and he brought** their remaining blood **out to the Sanctuary and** subsequently **brought** it **in** again to the Holy of Holies, **what is** the *halakha*? Is the blood disqualified by this improper second entrance into the Holy of Holies?

The question is sharper still for the *Yom Kippur* offerings themselves. The blood of the *par* and *sa'ir* of *Yom Kippur* goes into the *kodesh kodashim* — that is precisely where it belongs. Once the application is made there, one exits and applies blood on the *parochet* and on the *mizbe'ach hazahav*, and then proceeds outward. If one then re-enters the *kodesh kodashim* with that same blood — *ve'hochnisan* — what is the rule? *Mi amrinan mekoman hu* — is the *kodesh kodashim* still simply its proper place? Or do we say *hovei nefik nefik* — once the blood has left, it has left, and re-entry constitutes bringing it to an inner place that it is no longer entitled to enter?

### Altar Zones

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אָמְרִינַן מְקוֹמָן הוּא, אוֹ דִלְמָא הוֹאִיל וּנְפַק נְפַק?
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains the sides of the question: **Do we say** that **it is its place,** as the High Priest is supposed to bring this blood into the Holy of Holies on Yom Kippur at some point? **Or perhaps** one should say that **since** the High Priest has performed the mitzva and the blood has been **brought out,** it has been **brought out,** and the Holy of Holies is no longer considered its place.

A final layer of the question concerns the *mizbe'ach hazahav* itself. On *Yom Kippur*, after applying blood on the *parochet*, one proceeds to the outer portion of the *mizbe'ach hazahav*. The *Torah* uses the word *ve'hotzi* — bringing it out — to describe this movement. One could interpret this as indicating that the outer portion of the *mizbe'ach* is halachically "outside" relative to the inner portion. If one then brings the blood back, not all the way to the *kodesh kodashim* but simply to the inner side of the *mizbe'ach* — *ve'hichnisan* — is that considered bringing it to an inner, off-limits place?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִם תִּימְצֵי לוֹמַר הוֹאִיל וּנְפַק נְפַק – הִזָּה מִדָּמָן עַל הַפָּרֹכֶת, וְהוֹצִיאוֹ לַמִּזְבֵּחַ וְהִכְנִיסָן, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say that since** the blood has been **brought out,** it has been **brought out,** one can raise another dilemma: If the High Priest **sprinkled from their blood on the** Sanctuary **Curtain** and then **brought out** the blood **to the** golden **altar** in the Sanctuary and sprinkled the blood there, as required (see Leviticus 16:18), **but** subsequently **brought** the remainder of the blood **in** toward the Curtain dividing the Sanctuary from the Holy of Holies, **what is** the *halakha*?

### Teiku

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא וַדַּאי חַד מָקוֹם הוּא, אוֹ [דִילְמָא] יְצִיאָה קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains the sides of the dilemma: Do we say that **here** the area of the Curtain and the golden altar **is certainly one place,** as they are both in the Sanctuary, and therefore the blood should not be disqualified by being brought back toward the Curtain? **Or perhaps,** since **we call** the taking of the blood to the golden altar: **Going out,** in the verse: "And he shall go out unto the altar" (Leviticus 16:18), its return to the Curtain should be considered bringing in, and therefore the blood should be disqualified? No answers were found, and therefore the Gemara states that these dilemmas **shall stand** unresolved.

The *gemara* concludes: *teiku* — these questions remain unresolved. The boundaries of sacred space, the role of intent, the interaction between *heichal* and *kodesh kodashim*, and the question of whether blood that has already exited a zone may return — all of these remain as open questions with which we conclude the *daf*.
