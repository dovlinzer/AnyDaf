# Hullin 119 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shomer & Yad from Pasuk

### Rav Chaviva on Neveilah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חֲבִיבָא אָמַר: שָׁאנֵי שׁוֹמֵר דִּנְבֵלָה, כֵּיוָן דְּמַעֲשֵׂה יָד קָא עָבֵיד, אַיָּד שָׁדֵינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥaviva said** in response: The *halakha* of **protection of a carcass is different** because it imports and exports impurity but does not join together with the carcass to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity. **Since** the protection **serves as a handle** in that it only imports and exports impurity, **we apply its** novelty **to** the *halakhot* of **a handle** and not to the *halakhot* of protection.

Today's daf is 119, though we begin by picking up in the middle of 118b. The Gemara has been engaged in a technical discussion of how we know the concepts of *shomer* and *yad* — deriving them from the *pesukim* that speak about something falling onto seeds, and concluding that the word *lachem* teaches us the idea of *yad*, and that the concept of *shomer* by seeds teaches that the *shomer* adds to the minimum size rather than bringing *tumah* in and out.

We begin with one final *drasha* before proceeding to the specific *halachos*. Rav Chaviva addresses the question raised at the end of the previous daf: if we have an extra *pasuk* about *shomer* by *neveilah*, how do we know to read it as teaching a *din* of *yad*? Perhaps it teaches a *halacha* about *shomer* instead?

Rav Chaviva answers: *sha'ani shomer de-neveilah* — the *shomer* of *neveilah* is different. Since we know that a *shomer* by *neveilah* cannot add to the minimum size — *neveilah* is not an agricultural product — the only *halachic* function this *shomer* can serve is that of a *yad*, bringing *tumah* in and out. Therefore we are entitled to apply the *pasuk* to the laws of *yad*, and from there we derive that *yad* both brings *tumah* in and brings *tumah* out.

### Beyond Zera Zerua

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱלָא תְּלָתָא קְרָאֵי כְּתִיבִי: ״עַל כׇּל זֶרַע״, ״זֵרוּעַ״, ״אֲשֶׁר יִזָּרֵעַ״, חַד לְשׁוֹמֵר דִּזְרָעִים, וְחַד לְשׁוֹמֵר דְּאִילָנוֹת, אִידַּךְ לְשׁוֹמֵר בָּשָׂר וּבֵיצִים וְדָגִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, three terms** with the root *zayin*, *reish*, *ayin* **are written** in the verse with regard to protection: **"On any sowing [*zerua*] seed [*zera*] that is sown [*yizzare'a*]." One** term is written **to** teach the *halakha* of **protection with regard to seeds, and one** is written **to** teach the *halakha* of **protection with regard to trees,** and **the other** term is written **to** teach the *halakha* of **protection with regard to meat, and eggs, and fish.** Accordingly, this *halakha* is not limited to protection that is sown together with the food.

With the *drashot* complete, the Gemara proceeds to discuss the practical *halachos* of *yad* and *shomer*. At this point, Rav Yehuda bar Shmuel raises a challenge rooted in the *pesukim* themselves. The entire concept of *shomer* is derived from the phrase *al kol zera zerua asher yizarei'a* — the shell that protects a seed taken out for planting. The paradigm, in other words, is the seed planted together with its shell. But the laws of *shomer* seem to go well beyond this model.

The *Mishnah* teaches, for example, that the *pitema* of a pomegranate — the twig or protrusion connected to the fruit — functions as a *shomer* and combines toward the minimum size. Now, whatever the *pitema* precisely refers to (whether the point of attachment to the branch or the growth at the opposite end, a question debated by the *Rishonim*, and one that intersects with the famous dispute about the *pitema* of the *esrog*), it is clear that one does not plant a pomegranate together with its *pitema*. Rashi explains further that the leaf, by contrast, does not combine, because it is attached to the *pitema* rather than directly to the fruit — making it a *shomer al gabei shomer*.

So the challenge stands: if *shomer* is learned from the model of *zera zerua*, how does it apply to the *pitema* of a pomegranate, which no one plants along with the fruit? And the *Mishnah* also teaches that the skin combines with meat to reach the minimum size for *tumah*. Where does one derive that *shomer* applies to non-agricultural items at all?

The Gemara answers: *ela tlata kra'ei ketivi* — there are actually three separate verses that establish and generalize the concept of *shomer*. The phrase *al kol zera zerua asher yizarei'a* uses the language of planting and seed three times over. Each usage extends the concept to a different category.

The first teaches *shomer de-zera'im* — the *shomer* of seeds, in the tight, intimate sense of something planted together with the seed. The second teaches *shomer de-ilanot* — the *shomer* of fruit, such as the *pitema* of a pomegranate, which protects the fruit even though it is not planted along with it. The third extends the concept even further to *shomer basar, beitzim, ve-dagim* — foods that come not from the ground at all, but from the animal world. Each step represents an abstraction and generalization of the core concept. So while *shomer* is indeed derived from *zera zerua*, the Torah itself provides the warrant to apply it far beyond that original model.

## Yad & Hechsher Debate

### Rav: No Yad Hechsher

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי, אָמַר רַב: יֵשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה, וְאֵין יָד לְהֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥiyya bar Ashi says** that **Rav says: There is** significance to **a handle with regard to** importing and exporting **impurity,** i.e., a handle imports impurity to and exports impurity from the attached food, **but there is no** significance to **a handle with regard to rendering** the attached food **susceptible** to impurity. Rather, the food itself must come into contact with liquid directly.

Having finished the *drashot*, the Gemara turns to the specific parameters of *yad*. The first question: does the concept of *yad*, which we know applies to *tumah*, also apply to *hechsher*?

By way of background: food only becomes susceptible to *tumah* once it has been detached from the ground and has come into contact with liquid. The question is whether the liquid must touch the food directly, or whether it is sufficient if the liquid touches only the *yad* — the handle or protrusion. For example, does water touching the bone to which meat is attached render the meat *huchshar*?

Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi in the name of Rav rules: *yesh yad le-tumah ve-ein yad le-hechsher*. The concept of *yad* applies to the transmission of *tumah* but not to *hechsher*. If liquid only touched the *yad* and never touched the food itself, the food does not thereby become susceptible.

### R. Yochanan: Yad Both

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יֵשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה וּלְהֶכְשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There is** significance to **a handle with regard to** importing impurity to and exporting **impurity** from the attached food, **and with regard to rendering** the attached food **susceptible** to impurity even if only the handle came into contact with liquid.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *yesh yad le-tumah u-le-hechsher*. The concept of *yad* applies in both directions.

### Debate Basis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara clarifies: **With regard to what do** Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan **disagree? If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to **a logical argument. If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to the interpretation of **a verse.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא קְרָא, מָר סָבַר: מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְפָנָיו, וְלֹא לִפְנֵי פָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to the interpretation of **a verse.** One **Sage,** Rav, **holds** that **a verse is interpreted homiletically** based on juxtaposition to the term immediately **preceding it and not** based on juxtaposition to the term **before the one preceding it.** In this case, the verse states: "For you," indicating inclusion of a handle with regard to the matter of imparting impurity, which is stated in the preceding phrase: "It is impure," but not with regard to the matter of rendering the attached food susceptible to impurity through contact with liquid which is stated before the preceding phrase: "If water is put."

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמָר סָבַר: מִקְרָא נִדְרָשׁ לְפָנָיו, וְלִפְנֵי פָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **a verse is interpreted homiletically** based on juxtaposition to the term immediately **preceding it, as well as** to the term **before the one preceding it.** Therefore, he maintains that a handle can also render the attached food susceptible to impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא סְבָרָא, מָר סָבַר: הֶכְשֵׁר תְּחִלַּת טוּמְאָה הוּא, וּמָר סָבַר: הֶכְשֵׁר לָאו תְּחִלַּת טוּמְאָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **If you wish, say** that they disagree with regard to **a logical argument.** One **Sage,** Rabbi Yoḥanan, **holds** that **rendering** food **susceptible** to impurity **is the initial stage of** the process of imparting **impurity** to it. Therefore, just as a handle imparts impurity, it can also render the food susceptible to impurity. **And** one **Sage,** Rav, **holds** that **rendering** food **susceptible** to impurity **is not the initial stage of** the process of imparting **impurity.**

The Gemara then asks: *be-mai miflagi* — what is the basis for this debate? Two possibilities are offered: *iba'is eima kra*, the dispute may turn on textual interpretation of the *pasuk*; and *iba'is eima sevara*, it may turn on a conceptual point.

Taking the textual possibility first: the *pasuk* reads *ve-chi yutan mayim al zera ve-nafal mi-nivlatam alav tamei hu lachem* — if water falls on seeds and a carcass then falls on it, it is *tamei lachem*. The word *lachem* teaches the concept of *yad*. One *amora* holds that a *pasuk* can be read back one step — *mikra nidrash le-fanav ve-lo lifnei fanav* — and so *lachem* applies only to the immediately adjacent phrase about *tumah*, not to the earlier clause about water rendering food susceptible. The other holds that a *pasuk* can be read back two steps — *mikra nidrash le-fanav u-lifnei fanav* — and so *lachem* extends all the way to the beginning of the *pasuk*, reaching the laws of *hechsher* as well.

Alternatively, the debate may be conceptual. One view holds *hechsher techilas tumah hi* — *hechsher* is the beginning of the process of *tumah*, and therefore since *yad* is part of the world of *tumah*, it applies here as well. The other holds *hechsher lav techilas tumah hi* — although *hechsher* renders food susceptible, it has not yet initiated the process of *tumah* itself. The laws of *yad* may belong specifically to that process and not to what merely precedes it.

One might frame the underlying intuition as follows: *tumah* is normally conceived of as a quasi-metaphysical force — it spreads, it is contagious, it fills a space under an *ohel* — and perhaps even indirect contact can transmit such a force through a *yad*. *Hechsher*, by contrast, is not a force; it is a status-change, a determination that this food has now entered a new category. That kind of categorical shift may require actual contact with the food itself.

### Attached to Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּוָותֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּשֵׁם שֶׁיֵּשׁ יָד לְטוּמְאָה, כָּךְ יֵשׁ יָד לְהֶכְשֵׁר, וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁאֵין מְקַבְּלִין טוּמְאָה אֶלָּא לִכְשֶׁיִּתָּלְשׁוּ, כָּךְ אֵין מְקַבְּלִין הֶכְשֵׁר אֶלָּא עַד שֶׁיִּתָּלְשׁוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Yoḥanan: Just as there is** significance to **a handle with regard to** imparting **impurity** to food, **so too there is** significance to **a handle with regard to rendering** the attached food **susceptible** to impurity. **And just as** produce is susceptible to **contracting impurity only when it is detached** from the soil, **so too** produce **cannot be rendered susceptible** to impurity through contact with liquid **until it is detached** from the soil.

A *beraita* is cited in support of Rabbi Yochanan's position: *keshem she-yesh yad le-tumah, kach yesh yad le-hechsher* — just as *yad* applies to *tumah*, so it applies to *hechsher*. The same *beraita* adds a parallel rule: just as produce does not receive *tumah* while it is still attached to the tree, so too *hechsher* does not take effect while it is still attached.

This parallel is not merely symmetry. Rashi explains the rule about *tumah* on logical grounds: were produce susceptible to *tumah* while still growing, one would have to assume that everything in the world was potentially *tamei* — for who can know what dead creatures may have come into contact with every tree? The Torah cannot have intended such an assumption. Tosafot, however, offers a different analysis: the very reason produce does not receive *tumah* while attached is that *hechsher* has not yet occurred, and *hechsher* itself only takes effect once the produce is detached. On this reading, the two rules are not merely parallel; one actually explains the other.

There is also a deeper point here. *Tumah* as a category relates to things that have entered the sphere of human life and civilization — just as vessels become susceptible only once fashioned by human hands, not while they remain raw sticks and stones. Food, similarly, only becomes part of that human world once it is plucked and brought under human control. And *hechsher* — the requirement of contact with water, specifically water that one desires to fall on the food — reflects that same principle. It is not the mere natural fact of contact with water that matters; it is a purposeful human act that brings the food into the world of *tumah*.

## Minimum Size Debate

### Rav: Kezayis & Kepol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: אֵין יָד לְפָחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת, וְאֵין שׁוֹמֵר לְפָחוֹת מִכְּפוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: There is no** *halakha* of **a handle** with regard to imparting impurity **for** a handle that is attached to **less than an olive-bulk** of food. **And there is no** *halakha* of **protection** with regard to imparting impurity **for** protection that is attached to **less than a bean-bulk** of food. In addition, the protection does not join together with the food to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk required to impart the impurity of food.

With the question of *yad* and *hechsher* resolved, the Gemara proceeds to a different set of parameters: does *yad* or *shomer* require a minimum size of food in order to operate? Rav rules: *ein yad le-pachos mi-kezayis ve-ein shomer le-pachos mi-kepul*. The concept of *yad* does not apply to less than a *kezayis*, and the concept of *shomer* does not apply to less than a *kepol* — a large bean, understood to be approximately the size of a fava bean.

### R. Yochanan: No Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: יֵשׁ יָד לְפָחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת, וְיֵשׁ שׁוֹמֵר לְפָחוֹת מִכְּפוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan says: There is** a *halakha* of **a handle** with regard to imparting impurity **for** a handle that is attached to **less than an olive-bulk** of food. **And there is** a *halakha* of **protection** with regard to imparting impurity **for** protection that is attached to **less than a bean-bulk** of food. In addition, it joins together with the food to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk required to impart the impurity of food.

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *yesh yad le-pachos mi-kezayis ve-yesh shomer le-pachos mi-kepul*. Both concepts apply even below these minimum sizes.

One might ask: what would it even mean to have a *yad* connecting to something smaller than a *kezayis*? Since *tumah* in food normally requires the size of a *ke-beitzah* to transmit or receive, why does it matter whether the *yad* applies to a *kezayis* or less?

Rashi's answer is that the *yad* attaches to a piece of food that is a *kezayis* in size, while that same piece is in contact with other pieces of food that together reach the *ke-beitzah* threshold — so that touching the *yad* ultimately produces *tumah* for the combined mass. Tosafot, however, objects that a pile of separate items touching each other does not combine for the minimum size of *tumah*. On Tosafot's reading, the scenario is one where a *kezayis* of food is barely attached to a larger piece — connected by only a thread — so that pulling the *yad* would simply detach the *kezayis* without moving the whole. In that case the *yad* relates only to the *kezayis*, but the *kezayis* itself remains attached to the larger piece and so there is a minimum size. There is also the possibility that the *ke-beitzah* requirement applies only to transmitting *tumah*, not to receiving it.

### Two Bones Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: שְׁתֵּי עֲצָמוֹת, וַעֲלֵיהֶן שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים, וְהִכְנִיס רָאשֵׁיהֶן (שנים) [הַשְּׁנַיִם] לְבַיִת, וְהַבַּיִת מַאֲהִיל עֲלֵיהֶן – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the opinions of both Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan from that which is taught in a *Tosefta* (*Oholot* 4:8): In a case of **two bones** that have **two halves of an olive-**bulk of flesh of a dead person **upon them,** i.e., half an olive-bulk of flesh is attached to one end of each bone, **and one placed the ends of both** bones that are not directly attached to the flesh **inside a house** in such a manner that **the house overlies those** ends of the bones but not the ends of the bones attached to the flesh, it is considered as though the house is overlying the flesh itself, and **the house is impure.**

The Gemara now challenges Rav's position. A *beraita* teaches: two bones from a human corpse, each bearing half a *kezayis* of flesh, were brought with their non-flesh ends under a roof. The house is *tamei*. The principle at work is that since touching the bone would cause one to manipulate the flesh — making the bone a *yad* — so too being under a roof above the bone is like being under a roof above the flesh, and the *tumah* is transmitted. Yet in this case each bone bears only a half *kezayis*, and together they combine to constitute a *kezayis*. This appears to be a case where *yad* operates on less than a *kezayis* — a direct challenge to Rav.

### Combine Halves?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה בֶּן נָקוֹסָא אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב: הֵיאַךְ שְׁנֵי עֲצָמוֹת מִצְטָרְפִין לִכְזַיִת?
>
> **Translation:** **Yehuda ben Nekosa says in the name of Rabbi Ya'akov: How can two bones join together to** constitute **an olive-bulk** if the flesh attached to each bone is less than the measure of an olive-bulk?

The *Tanna Kamma* of the *beraita* holds that the house is *tamei*, apparently accepting that *yad* can operate on two half-*kezayis* portions. But Yehuda ben Nekusa, citing Rabbi Yaakov, objects: *heich shnei atzamos mitztarfin le-kezayis* — how can two separate bones combine for a *kezayis*?

It is worth noting the unusual framing here. The Gemara presents this as a *meitivei* — an objection — rather than the standard *leima k'tannai* — let us say the dispute between Rav and Rabbi Yochanan parallels a Tannaic dispute. Although in substance the Tannaic dispute seems to map directly onto the Amoraic one, the Gemara nevertheless chooses the form of a challenge, obliging both Rav and Rabbi Yochanan to locate their positions within this *beraita*.

The two bones are not touching each other; they are connected only through the shared airspace beneath the roof.

### Positions Aligned

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הַאי בְּמַאי מוֹקֵים לַהּ? אִי בְּיָד, קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the objection: **And** according to **Rav,** who holds that a handle that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of food or protection that is attached to less than a bean-bulk of food is not considered a handle or protection with regard to imparting impurity, **in what** manner **does he interpret this** *baraita*? **If** the *baraita* is discussing the case of a bone without marrow, and therefore the bone constitutes merely **a handle** for the flesh, which measures less than an olive-bulk, then **the first clause** of the *baraita* **is difficult,** because it indicates that a handle that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of food is considered a handle with regard to imparting impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי בְּשׁוֹמֵר – קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא.
>
> **Translation:** **If** the *baraita* is discussing the case of a bone containing marrow, and therefore the bone constitutes **protection** for food measuring less than an olive-bulk, then **the last clause** of the *baraita* **is difficult** because Yehuda ben Nekosa apparently holds that protection that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of food is not considered protection with regard to imparting impurity, contrary to the opinion of Rav, who holds that it is protection unless it is attached to less than a bean-bulk of food.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּשׁוֹמֵר, אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּיָד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that Rav interprets the *baraita* as discussing the case of a bone that constitutes **protection. If you wish, say** instead that Rav interprets the *baraita* as discussing the case of a bone that constitutes **a handle.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּיָד, וְהוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן נָקוֹסָא, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּשׁוֹמֵר, וְהוּא דְּאָמַר כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains its answers: **If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is discussing the case of a bone that constitutes **a handle, and** Rav **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Nekosa,** who maintains that the house does not become impure because he holds that a handle that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh does not impart impurity. **And If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is discussing the case of a bone that constitutes **protection, and** Rav **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** who maintains that the house is impure because he holds that protection attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh imparts impurity if the flesh measures at least a bean-bulk.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כּוּלַּהּ בְּיָד, וְהוּא דְּאָמַר כְּתַנָּא קַמָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said** that **the entire** *baraita* is discussing the case of a bone that constitutes **a handle, and** Rabbi Yoḥanan **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the first *tanna*,** who maintains that the house becomes impure because he holds that a handle that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of food imparts impurity. But he did not state his opinion in accordance with the opinion of Yehuda ben Nekosa, who maintains that the house does not become impure.

The Gemara resolves the tension as follows. If this *beraita* is read simply — as a case of *yad*, the bone serving as a handle for the meat — then the first opinion presents a difficulty for Rav, who holds *yad* does not apply to less than a *kezayis*. If instead it is read as a case of *shomer* — where the marrow inside the bone is the significant substance, and the bone itself is the *shomer* around it — then Rav can accommodate the first opinion, since he allows *shomer* to operate on less than a *kezayis* (only requiring the *kepol* threshold). But then the second opinion becomes difficult: Rabbi Yaakov says a *kezayis* is required, which conflicts with Rav's position on *shomer*.

The Gemara resolves this cleanly: *iba'it eima beyad* — read it as *yad*, and Rav aligns with Rabbi Yaakov, who also requires a *kezayis*. *Ve-iba'it eima be-shomer* — read it as *shomer*, and Rav aligns with the *Tanna Kamma*, who allows less than a *kezayis*, consistent with Rav's own ruling on *shomer*. For Rabbi Yochanan: *kula beyad ve-hu de-amar ke-Tanna Kamma* — read it entirely as *yad*, and Rabbi Yochanan simply follows the *Tanna Kamma*, since he holds that *yad* works even below a *kezayis*.

### Thigh Bone Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר, קוּלִית שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלֶיהָ כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר – גּוֹרֶרֶת כּוּלָּהּ לְטוּמְאָה. אֲחֵרִים אוֹמְרִים: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין עָלֶיהָ אֶלָּא כְּפוֹל – גּוֹרֶרֶת כּוּלָּהּ לְטוּמְאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another objection to the opinions of Rav and Rabbi Yoḥanan from the *Tosefta* (*Okatzin* 2:5): **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **a femur that has an olive-bulk of flesh upon it,** the flesh **draws the entire** femur **into ritual impurity** if any part of the femur comes into contact with a dead creeping animal. The Sage referred to as **Aḥerim says: Even** if **there is only a bean-bulk** of flesh **upon** the femur, **it draws the entire** femur **into impurity.**

A second *beraita* is then adduced. Rabbi Yehuda says: a *kulit* — a large thigh bone — that has a *kezayis* of flesh on it, *goreret kula le-tumah* — it draws everything along for the purposes of *tumah*. Even if the meat is far from where you are touching the bone, touching any part of the bone transmits *tumah* to the meat; the bone functions as a *yad*. The *Acherim* (generally understood to be Rabbi Meir) say: even if there is only a *kepol* of meat on it — not even a *kezayis* — it still draws everything for *tumah*.

This maps onto the earlier debate: Rabbi Yehuda requires a *kezayis* for *yad*, which fits Rav. Rabbi Meir requires only a *kepol*, which fits Rabbi Yochanan at least partially — Rabbi Yochanan might agree that a *kepol* is required even if he does not require a *kezayis*.

Once again the Gemara asks how Rav accommodates both opinions within a single *beraita*. If it is a *yad* case, the *acherim* opinion is difficult for Rav, who requires a *kezayis* for *yad*. If it is a *shomer* case (marrow inside the bone, with the bone serving as the protective covering), then Rabbi Yehuda is difficult for Rav, who requires only a *kepol* for *shomer*, not a *kezayis*. As before, the Gemara offers two readings: *iba'it eima beyad ve-hu de-amar ke-Rabbi Yehuda* — it is *yad*, and Rav follows Rabbi Yehuda; or *iba'it eima be-shomer ve-hu de-amar ke-Acherim* — it is *shomer*, and Rav follows Rabbi Meir. Rabbi Yochanan, meanwhile, reads it entirely as *shomer* and follows the *Acherim*.

It is worth pausing here over something the Gemara itself finds somewhat curious. At the point where Rabbi Yochanan says *kula beshomer ve-hu de-amar ke-Acherim*, one might have expected the Gemara to say *kula beyad ve-hu de-amar ke-Acherim* just as well, since Rabbi Yochanan holds *yad* operates below a *kezayis*. The Gemara seems to prefer reading the *beraita* as about *shomer* at this stage, and this connects directly to what the Gemara discusses next.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הַאי בְּמַאי מוֹקֵים לַהּ? אִי בְּיָד – קַשְׁיָא סֵיפָא, אִי בְּשׁוֹמֵר – קַשְׁיָא רֵישָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** manner **does Rav interpret this** *baraita*? **If** the *baraita* is discussing the case of a femur that constitutes merely **a handle** for the flesh, then **the latter clause** of the *baraita* **is difficult,** because Aḥerim apparently maintains that a handle that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh imparts impurity, contrary to the opinion of Rav. **If** the *baraita* is discussing the case of a femur that constitutes **protection,** then **the first clause** of the *baraita* **is difficult,** because Rabbi Yehuda apparently holds that protection that is attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh does not impart impurity, contrary to the opinion of Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּיָד – וְהוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא בְּשׁוֹמֵר – כַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that the *baraita* is discussing the case of a femur that constitutes **a handle, and** Rav **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that a handle attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh does not impart impurity. **And if you wish, say** instead that the *baraita* is discussing the case of a femur that constitutes **protection, and** Rav **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion introduced with the words: **Aḥerim** says, according to which even protection attached to less than an olive-bulk of flesh imparts impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: כּוּלַּהּ בְּשׁוֹמֵר, וְהוּא דְּאָמַר כַּאֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan** could have **said** that **the entire** *baraita* is referring to case of a femur that constitutes **protection, and** Rabbi Yoḥanan **stated** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion referred to as: **Aḥerim** says, according to which even protection that is attached to a bean-bulk of flesh imparts impurity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲחֵרִים, הָא ״כְּפוֹל״ קָא אָמְרִי!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: How is Rabbi Yoḥanan's opinion consistent with the opinion referred to as: **Aḥerim** says? **Don't** they **say** that protection that is attached to **a bean-bulk** of flesh imparts impurity, and Rabbi Yoḥanan holds that even protection that is attached to less than a bean-bulk of food imparts impurity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַיְּידֵי דְּקָאָמַר תַּנָּא קַמָּא שִׁיעוּרָא, קָאָמְרִי אִינְהוּ נָמֵי שִׁיעוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Aḥerim agrees that even protection that is attached to less than a bean-bulk imparts impurity. Nevertheless, **since the first *tanna* states** the specific **measure** of an olive-bulk, Aḥerim **also states** the specific **measure** of a bean-bulk even though he maintains his opinion with regard to a measure of less than a bean-bulk as well.

### Whole vs Fragmented

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: ״עַל כׇּל זֶרַע זֵרוּעַ אֲשֶׁר יִזָּרֵעַ״, כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁבְּנֵי אָדָם מוֹצִיאִין – חִטָּה בִּקְלִיפָּתָהּ, וּשְׂעוֹרָה בִּקְלִיפָּתָהּ, וַעֲדָשִׁים בִּקְלִיפָּתָן!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** proof from that **which the school of Rabbi Yishmael taught:** With regard to the impurity of food the verse states: **"On any sowing seed that is sown"** (Leviticus 11:37), indicating that the entire seed is susceptible to impurity when it is in a state where it is **typical for people to take it out** to the field for sowing: This applies to **wheat in its shell, and barley in its shell, and lentils in their shells.** It is derived from here that shells and other appendages that protect the food are considered part of the food. Since wheat, barley, and lentil grains are smaller than a bean-bulk, this statement indicates that even protection that is attached to less than a bean-bulk of food imparts impurity, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Ḥanina and Rav.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּרִיָּה – שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: A grain is a distinct **entity,** and therefore its status **is different.** The *halakha* of protection is applicable to a distinct entity even if it measures less than a bean-bulk.

A final proof to Rabbi Yochanan's view is brought from a *beraita* of the *Tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael*: the verse *al kol zera zerua asher yizarei'a* — as people take out for planting: wheat in its shell, barley in its shell, lentils in their shells. Here the *shomer* applies to a single grain of wheat, which is certainly smaller than a *kezayis* and arguably smaller than a *kepol* as well. This seems to directly support Rabbi Yochanan.

Rav's response is the principle of *biryah sha'ani* — the case of a complete, whole item is different. When you are dealing with an intact, whole agricultural item — a single kernel of wheat in its shell — no minimum size is required for either *yad* or *shomer*, because the wholeness of the item itself confers *halachic* significance. The minimums of *kezayis* for *yad* and *kepol* for *shomer* apply only when the food in question is fragmentary — a piece of meat, part of a carcass, something that does not constitute a *biryah*. In those cases, you need a threshold of substance before the *yad* or *shomer* rules apply. But when the entire item is intact, even a tiny complete item is governed by its own integrity, not by size thresholds. Rashi notes that this principle would also have resolved some of the earlier difficulties discussed, particularly the cases involving peas and pods.

## Multiple Shomerim

### No Shomer on Shomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא:
>
> **Translation:** §**Rav Oshaya raises a dilemma:**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנֵי שׁוֹמְרִין מַהוּ שֶׁיִּצְטָרְפוּ?
>
> **Translation:** As the Gemara stated, protection joins together with the food to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity. **What is** the *halakha* with regard to **two protections joining together** with the food to constitute the measure of an egg-bulk required to impart impurity?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא בְּזֶה עַל גַּב זֶה – וּמִי אִיכָּא שׁוֹמֵר עַל גַּב שׁוֹמֵר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of the dilemma? **If we say** that it is referring to a case **where** a food item has two layers of protection, and **this** outer protection **is on top of that** inner protection, **is** the *halakha* of protection applicable with regard to **protection** that is **on top of** another **protection?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתְנַן: רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ קְלִיפּוֹת בְּבָצָל – פְּנִימִית, בֵּין שְׁלֵמָה בֵּין קְדוּרָה – מִצְטָרֶפֶת; אֶמְצָעִית, שְׁלֵמָה – מִצְטָרֶפֶת, קְדוּרָה – אֵין מִצְטָרֶפֶת; חִיצוֹנָה, בֵּין כָּךְ וּבֵין כָּךְ – טְהוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Okatzin* 2:4) that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There are **three peels** surrounding **an onion.** The **inner** peel is considered like the food itself, and therefore, **whether whole or punctured, it joins together** with the onion to constitute the requisite measure to impart the impurity of food. With regard to the **middle** peel, when it is **whole** it provides protection and therefore **joins together** with the onion, but when it is **punctured** it does not provide protection and therefore **does not join together** with the onion. The **outer** peel does not join together with the onion at all, and **both** in **this** case, when it is whole, **and** in **that** case, when it is punctured, it remains **ritually pure.** Evidently, the *halakha* of protection is not applied to a protection that surrounds another protection.

The Gemara now takes up a fresh question posed by Rabbi Oshaya: *shnei shomrim mahu she-yitztarfu* — can two separate *shomrim* combine?

Before reaching the actual question, the Gemara clarifies what is not being asked. The question cannot be about one *shomer* stacked on top of another, because the *Mishnah* has already established that there is no *shomer al gabei shomer*. As proof, Rabbi Yehuda's ruling about the three layers of an onion is cited. The innermost layer, which is actually the beginning of the onion's edible flesh, combines regardless of whether it is whole or punctured. The middle layer, which is a thinner papery shell directly around the body of the onion, combines if whole (since it functions as a *shomer*) but not if punctured (since it no longer protects). The outermost layer is *tahor* in all cases — *bein kach u-vein kach tehorah* — because it is a *shomer* on a *shomer*, and such second-level protection is not recognized as part of the food at all.

The conceptual point is this: a *shomer* functions as an extension of the food itself, and therefore it must be directly connected to the food. Something that protects the *shomer* rather than protecting the food directly cannot achieve that extension; conceptually it is too distant, like a *gezeirah li-gezeirah*.

It is worth distinguishing this from the parallel question about *yad*. A *yad* is about manipulation, not about being an extension of the food. Therefore a *yad* can function at two degrees of remove: the Gemara earlier described a branch serving as *yad* to pods that each contain beans — the branch does not touch the beans directly, yet it manipulates them. For *shomer*, however, the requirement of direct connection to the food is structural to the concept itself.

The question is also raised about the outer shell: when the *Mishnah* says it is *tehorah* in all cases, does that mean it also fails to function as a *yad*? The simple reading of *bein kach u-vein kach tehorah* would imply so. This raises a deeper question about *yad*: is something a *yad* because it enables manipulation, or only because it makes manipulation easier? If the former, it is hard to see why touching the outermost shell would not transmit *tumah*. If the latter, then perhaps when an inner *shomer* is intact, the outer shell adds nothing practically to one's ability to manipulate the food, and therefore fails even as *yad*.

### Split Shomer Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא, שׁוֹמֵר אוֹכֶל שֶׁחִלְּקוֹ קָמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dilemma: **Rav Oshaya** does not raise the dilemma with regard to a food item that has two layers of protection. Rather, he **raises the dilemma** with regard to **protection of food that one divided** such that the food is whole but the protection is divided into separate sections.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּהַאי לָא מַגֵּין אַהַאי, וְהַאי לָא מַגֵּין אַהַאי, לָא מִצְטָרֵף.
>
> **Translation:** The dilemma is as follows: **Since this** section of the protection is protecting this part of the food but **does not provide protection for that** other part of the food, **and that** other section of the protection is protecting another part of the food but **does not provide protection for this** part of the food, must one conclude that the two protections **do not join together** to constitute the requisite measure for the impurity of food?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ דִלְמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּהַאי מַגֵּין אַדִּידֵיהּ, וְהַאי מַגֵּין אַדִּידֵיהּ, מִצְטָרְפִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Or perhaps,** should one reason that **since this** section of the protection **provides protection for its** part of the food, **and that** section of the protection **provides protection for its** part of the food, and the two parts of the food are joined together, therefore the entire entity is considered unified and the two sections of the protection **join together** to constitute the requisite measure?

With the rule about *shomer al gabei shomer* established, the Gemara clarifies what Rabbi Oshaya's question actually is: *shomer ochel she-chilko ka-mibaya lei* — he is asking about a *shomer* that has been split. The scenario, as Tosafot explains it, is a single piece of meat whose skin (the non-edible skin functioning as *shomer*) has been cut into two separate portions, while the meat underneath remains intact and continuous. Each portion of the skin is still attached to the meat, and each protects the section of meat directly beneath it. But neither portion protects the entire piece of meat.

The question is whether these two portions of *shomer* combine. On one hand, *keivan de-hai lo magin a-hai ve-hai lo magin a-hai* — since neither protects the other, perhaps they should not be counted together as a unified *shomer*. On the other hand, *keivan de-hai magin a-didei ve-hai magin a-didei mitztaref* — perhaps since each one protects its own portion of the meat, together they fulfil the protective function over the whole, and they should combine.

### Multiple Foods OK

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן עֲזַרְיָה מְטַהֵר בְּשֶׁל פּוֹל, וּמְטַמֵּא בְּשֶׁל קִטְנִית, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁרוֹצֶה בְּמִשְׁמֻושָׁן!
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a resolution to the dilemma from that which is taught in a mishna cited previously (*Okatzin* 1:5): **Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya deems** a pod containing **beans** that came in contact with a dead creeping animal **ritually pure. But he deems** a pod containing **legumes** that came in contact with a dead creeping animal **impure because one desires** the use of the pod **when handling** the legumes so that he will not damage them. Since the requisite volume for a food item to be susceptible to impurity is an egg-bulk, and the volume of one pod and its legumes is less than an egg-bulk, this statement must be discussing multiple pods joined together. Evidently, protection of food that is divided into separate sections joins together to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity.

The Gemara brings a *beraita* as a possible proof. Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya declares *tahor* the pod of a *pol*, but *tamei* the pod of *kitnit* — because one desires to use the smaller legumes through their pods. The argument — developed above in the minimum size discussion — is that Rashi reads this as demonstrating that multiple *shomrim* over multiple separate pieces of food do combine: no single pod has enough food inside it to reach the minimum size, and so the only way the ruling makes sense is if all the pods and their beans are counted together.

The Gemara notes that this is a significant step: combining two separate *shomrim* that each protect their own food may differ from combining two half-*shomrim* that protect portions of a single piece of food. Nevertheless, Rashi treats the *beraita* as bearing on Rabbi Oshaya's question. Tosafot reads it differently, but that reading is not developed at length here.

### Multiple Foods OK → Grain Row Union

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: בְּקוּלְחָא, וּמִשּׁוּם יָד, וּמַאי ״בְּמִשְׁמֻושָׁן״ – בְּתַשְׁמִישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said** in rejection of this resolution: That mishna is discussing a case **where** a dead creeping animal touched **the stalk** to which the pods are attached but not the pods themselves. **And** the *halakha* in that mishna is not referring to the matter of protection; rather, it is **with regard to** the matter of **a handle,** as the stalk serves as a handle for the pod and its attached grain. **And what** does Rabbi Elazar ben Azarya mean when he says that in the case of legumes, the stalk is impure because one desires the use of the stalk **when handling [*bemashmishan*]** the legumes? He is referring **to using [*betashmishan*],** i.e., carrying, the legumes with the stalk.

Before resolving the question, the Gemara pursues the *beraita* about *kitnit* further and offers a different solution: *be-kulcha u-mishum yad* — we are not talking about the shells as *shomrim* at all; we are talking about the stalk connecting all the pods, which is functioning as a *yad* (as discussed above in the minimum size section). Rabbi Oshaya's unresolved question about combined *shomrim* therefore cannot be resolved from that source.

A second proof is attempted from the *Tanna d'vei Rabbi Yishmael* passage about wheat and barley in their shells being taken out for planting. Again Rashi argues that the only way there can be a minimum size here is if multiple kernels with their multiple shells are being combined, demonstrating that multiple *shomrim* can combine.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, בְּשִׁדְרָה, וּמִשּׁוּם שׁוֹמֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Here too,** the statement of the school of Rabbi Yishmael is not referring to independent grains of wheat and barley, but rather **to the stem** to which the grains are attached, **and** it is **with regard to** the status of all the shells of the grains on one stem as **protection.** All of the grains surrounding the stem protect each other, since if one shell with its grain falls it causes all of the other shells and their grains to fall as well. Therefore, this case is not similar to Rav Oshaya's dilemma, where each protection protects only part of the food.

The Gemara again deflects: *hacha nami be-shidra u-mishum shomer* — here too we are talking about an ear of grain, not separate seeds. On a stalk of wheat, every kernel's presence contributes to holding the other kernels in place. The top kernels would fall if the bottom ones were removed. Accordingly, all the kernels together form a single unified *shomer*, not multiple separate ones.

### Grain Row Union

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא עִילִּיָּתָא צְרִיכִי לְתַתָּיָתָא, אֶלָּא תְּתַּיָּתָא מַאי צְרִיכִי לְעִילִּיָּתָא? בְּחַד דָּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the grains located **higher** on the stem **need** the grains located **lower** on the stem to remain in place in order to not fall. **But** for **what** purpose do the grains located **lower** on the stem **need** the grains located **higher** on the stem? The Gemara answers: The case is one where the grains are all positioned tightly around the stem **in one row** in such a manner that if even one located higher up on the stem were to fall, the remaining grains would fall too.

The Gemara then scrutinizes this resolution. *Bishlama d'ila'ata tzricha l'tata'ata* — granted, the top row requires the bottom row (remove the bottom and the top falls). But *ela tata'ah mai tzericha le-ila'ah* — how does the bottom row require the top row? You can remove the top without destabilizing the bottom. So the claim that every kernel protects every other kernel cannot be symmetric.

The Gemara's answer is *be-chada dara* — we are talking about a single row of kernels, not multiple rows. In one row, every kernel's presence holds its neighbors in place, and the mutual support is symmetric. Removing any kernel from the row destabilizes the adjacent kernels.

### Shimon Wheat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִי אִיכָּא כְּבֵיצָה אֳכָלִים בְּחַד דָּרָא בְּחִטֵּי דְּשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is there** a volume of **an egg-bulk of food in one row** of grain? The Gemara answers: Yes, this *baraita* is stated **with regard to** large grains of **wheat,** such as those grown in the time **of Shimon ben Shataḥ.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, חֲדָא חִטָּה נָמֵי בְּחִטֵּי דְּשִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן שָׁטַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara points out: **Now that you have arrived at this** conclusion, it is not necessary to interpret the *baraita* as referring to many grains tightly packed around the stem. It is **even** possible to explain that the *baraita* is discussing the case of **one** grain of **wheat,** such as the large grains of **wheat** grown in the time **of Shimon ben Shataḥ.**

But this raises an immediate problem: *umi ika ke-beitza ochel be-chada dara* — is there really a *ke-beitza*'s worth of food in a single row of an ear of wheat? The Gemara's whole assumption was that we need multiple items to reach the minimum size. A single row of wheat would not plausibly yield a *ke-beitza*.

The Gemara's answer is: *be-chitei de-Shimon ben Shatach* — we are speaking of the miraculous wheat of Shimon ben Shatach, grains of the kind the *meraglim* reportedly saw in the Land of Israel, where a single row could contain a *ke-beitza* of food.

The Gemara immediately notes that once one resorts to such miraculous dimensions, the need for even a single row is unnecessary: *chada chita nami be-chitei de-Shimon ben Shatach* — a single grain of wheat from Shimon ben Shatach's crop could itself be the size of a *ke-beitza*. The entire question of combining multiple *shomrim* via a single row becomes superfluous. The upshot is that the question of Rabbi Oshaya — whether two separate *shomrim* can combine — remains formally unresolved by the Gemara.

## Hair as Yad

### Bone as Yad Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא: שְׁנֵי עֲצָמוֹת וַעֲלֵיהֶן שְׁנֵי חֲצָאֵי זֵיתִים, וְהִכְנִיס רָאשֵׁיהֶן שְׁנַיִם לְבַיִת, וְהַבַּיִת מַאֲהִיל עֲלֵיהֶן – הַבַּיִת טָמֵא.
>
> **Translation:** §The Gemara proceeds to discuss the *Tosefta* **itself** that was cited earlier (118b): In a case of **two bones upon which there are two halves of an olive-bulk** of flesh of a corpse, i.e., half an olive-bulk of flesh is attached to one end of each bone, **and one placed the ends of both** bones that are not directly attached to the flesh **inside the house** in such a manner that **the house overlies those** ends of the bones but not the ends of the bones attached to the flesh, it is considered as though the house is overlying the flesh itself and **the house is impure.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְהוּדָה בֶּן נָקוֹסָא אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יַעֲקֹב: הֵיאַךְ שְׁנֵי עֲצָמוֹת מִצְטָרְפִין לִכְזַיִת?
>
> **Translation:** **Yehuda ben Nekosa says in the name of Rabbi Ya'akov: How can two bones join together to** constitute **an olive-bulk** if the flesh attached to each bone is less than the measure of an olive-bulk? It may be inferred from here that according to all, if the end of a bone that is attached to an olive-bulk of flesh is outside the house, and the other end that is not directly attached to any flesh is inside the house, the house becomes impure with the ritual impurity of a corpse.

The Gemara now returns to the *beraita* about two bones, each with a half-*kezayis* of flesh, brought under a roof. This time the focus is not on the minimum size question but on a different dispute: what exactly can function as a *yad*?

### R. Lakish Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא עֶצֶם, דְּהָוֵי יָד, אֲבָל נִימָא לָא הָוְיָא יָד. וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִימָא נָמֵי הָוְיָא יָד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to this matter **Reish Lakish said:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **a bone, as it constitutes a handle** vis-à-vis the flesh, **but** if one **hair** of a corpse is attached to an olive-bulk of flesh at one end, and the other end of the hair is inside a house, it does not render the house impure, because the hair **does not constitute a handle** vis-à-vis the flesh. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even a hair constitutes a handle** vis-à-vis the flesh, and therefore the house is rendered impure.

Reish Lakish rules: *lo shanu ela etzem de-havah yad, aval nima lo havah yad* — this law applies only to a bone, which functions as a *yad*, but not to a hair. A hair does not constitute a *yad*. The rationale is that *yad* requires the ability to actually manipulate the object — to pick it up or move it — and a single hair, which will ordinarily simply pull out under tension, does not enable that kind of manipulation.

### R. Yochanan Position

Rabbi Yochanan disagrees: *afilu nima nami havah yad* — even a hair functions as a *yad*. This is a striking claim. The Gemara immediately investigates how Rabbi Yochanan grounds it.

### Hair Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: עוֹר שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו כְּזַיִת בָּשָׂר, הַנּוֹגֵעַ בְּצִיב הַיּוֹצֵא מִמֶּנּוּ, וּבִשְׂעָרָה שֶׁכְּנֶגְדּוֹ – טָמֵא. מַאי לָאו, מִשּׁוּם יָד?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from the mishna taught later (124a): In the case of the **hide** of an unslaughtered animal carcass **upon which there is an olive-bulk of flesh, one who touches a strand** of flesh **emerging from** the flesh, **or** touches **a hair that is** on the side of the hide **opposite** the flesh, **is ritually impure** with the impurity of an unslaughtered carcass, even though he did not touch an olive-bulk of the flesh. **What** is the reason that one who touches the strand of flesh or the hair becomes impure? **Is it not because** they constitute **a handle** for the flesh, contrary to the opinion of Reish Lakish with regard to a hair?

Rabbi Yochanan challenges Reish Lakish from an explicit *beraita*: skin from an animal bearing a *kezayis* of meat from a *neveilah* — one who touches either the *tziv* (a protruding sinew or string of flesh) or the *se'ar* (a hair protruding from the skin) that comes out of it is *tamei*. This *beraita* explicitly includes hair as a vehicle for *tumah*.

### Shomer Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹמֵר. וּמִי אִיכָּא שׁוֹמֵר עַל גַּבֵּי שׁוֹמֵר? חַלְחוּלֵי מְחַלְחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **No,** the ruling of that mishna is not because the hair serves as a handle for the flesh. Rather, it is **because** the hair serves as **protection** for the flesh. The Gemara asks: **But** the hide protects the flesh, and the hair is on top of the hide; **is** the *halakha* of protection applicable with regard to **protection** that is **on top of** another **protection?** The Gemara answers: The hair **penetrates through** the hide and touches the flesh, thereby providing protection directly for the flesh.

*Mai lav mishum yad* — does this not demonstrate that hair functions as a *yad*? Reish Lakish responds: *lo, mishum shomer* — no, the hair is functioning as a *shomer*, not a *yad*. Meaning, it is not transmitting *tumah* through manipulation but by being an extension of the meat.

But this answer is immediately problematic: *umi ika shomer al gabei shomer* — we have established that there cannot be a *shomer* on a *shomer*. The hair is on the skin, and the skin is on the meat. For the hair to be a *shomer* to the meat, it would have to be the skin's *shomer*, making it a *shomer al gabei shomer* — precisely what the rule forbids.

### Hair Penetrates

The Gemara resolves this with a surprising claim: *shachuyei mechalchal* — the hair penetrates downward through the skin, filling the follicle hole all the way to the meat beneath. On this view, the hair is not resting on the surface of the skin; it passes through the skin and is directly in contact with the meat. Therefore it is not a *shomer* on a *shomer* — it is a *shomer* directly on the meat. The skin and the hair are, in a sense, at the same level.

Admittedly, this is anatomically striking, particularly for thick cow hide. But the argument requires that the hair follicle extends below the skin layer into the flesh layer — that the hair fills that channel and is therefore, structurally, directly attached to the meat.

### Tefillin Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, תְּפִילִּין הֵיכִי כָּתְבִינַן? הָא בָּעֵינַן כְּתִיבָה תַּמָּה, וְלֵיכָּא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov objects to this** answer: **If that is so,** that there are perforations in the hide through which the hairs penetrate, **how can we write phylacteries? Don't we require** phylacteries to be written with **a perfect writing,** with no perforations in the letters? **And** that **is not** possible if there are perforations in the hide.

Rav Acha bar Yaakov seizes on this claim. *Tefillin heichi katvinan* — if we accept that hair follicles create actual holes passing all the way through the skin (which becomes *klaf* for writing *tefillin*), how can *tefillin* be written on such parchment? The requirement of *ketiva tama* — complete, uninterrupted writing — would seem to be violated if the ink is being laid over microscopic holes at every follicle.

### Ink Covers Holes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִשְׁתְּמִיטְתֵּיהּ הָא דְּאָמְרִי בְּמַעְרְבָא: כׇּל נֶקֶב שֶׁהַדְּיוֹ עוֹבֵר עָלָיו – אֵינוֹ נֶקֶב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov **overlooked that** *halakha* **which they say in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **Any perforation over which the ink passes** and which it covers **is not** considered **a perforation** that invalidates the writing.

The Gemara's first answer: *kol nekev she-ha-dio over alav eino nekev* — any hole over which ink passes continuously is not considered a hole. If the ink stroke is continuous and unbroken in appearance, the presence of a microscopic gap beneath it does not constitute an interruption in the writing. This is what is said in the West (*ma'arava*, meaning *Eretz Yisrael*), and Rav Acha had simply forgotten this principle.

### Multiple Hairs Yad

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא, כּוּלֵּיהּ מִשּׁוּם יָד, כִּדְאָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעָא: בִּמְלַאי שֶׁבֵּין הַמְּלָאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead a different answer to Rabbi Yoḥanan's objection to Reish Lakish: **The entire** mishna teaches that one who touches either a strand of flesh or a hair becomes impure **because** these appendages serve the flesh as **a handle** and not as protection. Nevertheless, that statement does not contradict the opinion of Reish Lakish that a hair does not constitute a handle for the flesh, because one can explain this mishna **as Rabbi Ela stated** with regard to a different mishna: It is stated **with regard to** the case of **an awn among** many **awns.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי נָמֵי, בְּנִימָא שֶׁבֵּין הַנִּימִין.
>
> **Translation:** **So too,** the mishna taught later is stated **with regard to** the case of **a hair among** many **hairs** and not the case of a single hair. Therefore, the hair constitutes a handle for the flesh because one can hold the hair and lift the flesh without the hair becoming detached.

The Gemara offers a second resolution: *ve-iba'it eima, kulei mishum yad* — alternatively, the hair in that *beraita* is functioning as *yad* after all, not *shomer*. But then Reish Lakish's objection stands — he denies that hair functions as *yad*. The answer, citing a principle of Rav Illa, is that the rule applies specifically *bi-mloi she-bein ha-mlo'im* — when the hair is one among many, embedded within a full group of hairs. A single strand cannot manipulate anything. But when there is a dense covering of hair — as with the hairs on a wheat stalk or the full coat of hair on an animal — the entire mass of hair functions as a *yad*. In that case, even Reish Lakish would agree.

### Wheat Hairs Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהֵיכָא אִתְּמַר דְּרַבִּי אִלְעָא? אַהָא: מְלַאי שֶׁבַּשִּׁבֳּלִים מִיטַּמְּאִין וּמְטַמְּאִין, וְאֵינָן מִצְטָרְפִין. מְלַאי לְמַאי חֲזֵי? אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעַאי: בִּמְלַאי שֶׁבֵּין הַמְּלָאִים.
>
> **Translation:** **And where was** the opinion **of Rabbi Ela stated?** It was stated **with regard to that** which is taught in a mishna (*Okatzin* 1:3): **An awn [*melai*] that is on top of a stalk** constitutes a handle for the stalk. Therefore, **it can become impure and impart impurity, but it does not join together** with the grains to constitute the requisite measure to impart impurity. It was asked: **For what** function **is an awn fit** such that it is considered to be a handle for the stalk? **Rabbi Ilai,** i.e., Rabbi Ela, **said:** That mishna is stated **with regard to** the case of **an awn among** many **awns** and not the case of a single awn. Therefore, the awns constitute a handle for the stalk because one can hold the awns and lift the stalk without the awns becoming detached from it.

This brings the Gemara to Rav Illa's original context. The *mloi she-bein ha-shibolim* — the hairs of the stalks of wheat — *metamin u-metamin ve-ein mitztarfin*: they function as *yad*, transmitting *tumah* in and out, but they do not combine to add to the minimum size. They are *yad*, not *shomer*.

The question is then asked: *le-mai chazi* — what possible use is one individual strand of wheat hair? Rav Illa answers: *bi-mloi she-bein ha-mlo'im* — we are speaking of a hair within a group of hairs. The multiple strands together allow manipulation, and therefore the group functions as a *yad*. A single isolated strand, by contrast, cannot manipulate anything and would not function as *yad* even according to Rabbi Yochanan.
