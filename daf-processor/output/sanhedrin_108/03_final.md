# Sanhedrin 108 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Yehoshua & Yeishu

### Four Lepers Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: לְעוֹלָם תְּהֵא שְׂמֹאל דּוֹחָה וְיָמִין מְקָרֶבֶת, לֹא כֶּאֱלִישָׁע שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְגֵחֲזִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם, וְלֹא כִּיהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּרַחְיָה שֶׁדְּחָפוֹ לְיֵשׁוּ הַנּוֹצְרִי בִּשְׁתֵּי יָדַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Always have the left** hand **drive** sinners **away and the right draw** them **near,** so that the sinner will not totally despair of atonement. This is **unlike Elisha, who pushed away Gehazi with his two hands** and caused him to lose his share in the World-to-Come, **and unlike Yehoshua ben Peraḥya, who pushed away Jesus the Nazarene with his two hands.**

The daf begins by picking up where the previous discussion left off: the Gemara on 107b, wrapping up the individuals who have no *chelek l'olam haba*, had arrived at the figure of Geichazi. Notably, the Gemara handles Geichazi somewhat differently than the others — rather than engaging in the usual technique of *darshhaning* his name or deriving his exclusion from a *pasuk*, it moves almost immediately into the narrative of how Elisha attempted to bring him to *teshuvah* and he refused. The textual support is comparatively thin, yet the Gemara introduces the principle that animated Geichazi's resistance: *kach mekublani mibeit ava, kol ha-choteh u-machti et ha-rabbim ein maspikin beyado la'asot teshuvah* — one who sins and causes the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent. Even if that formulation does not in itself entail the loss of *olam haba*, the Gemara had to explain in what sense Geichazi was *machti et ha-rabbim* — since his episode with Naaman hardly seems to have caused widespread sin — and concluded it was his involvement with Yerovam's golden calves and his keeping students away from Elisha.

Now the Gemara transitions: *v'arba'ah anashim hayu metzoraim petach ha-sha'ar* — the four lepers at the gate of the city. Rabbi Yochanan identifies them as Geichazi and his three sons. This closes the Geichazi episode. And now the Gemara reaches the passage that has been excised from standard printed editions. The expunged text runs in parallel to the Elisha-Geichazi narrative, because the Gemara had already stated: *tanu rabbanan, leolam tehei smol docheh veyamin mekarevet* — one should always push away with the left and draw near with the right — *lo k'Elisha she-dachfo l'Geichazi bishtei yadav*, not like Elisha who pushed Geichazi away with both hands, *velo k'Yehoshua ben Perachia she-dachfo l'Yeishu HaNotzri bishtei yadav*, and not like Yehoshua ben Perachia who pushed away Yeishu HaNotzri with both hands.

### Alexandria Flight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּחֲזִי, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיֵּלֶךְ אֱלִישָׁע דַּמֶּשֶׂק״. לְהֵיכָא אֲזַל? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁהָלַךְ לְהַחֲזִיר גֵּחֲזִי בִּתְשׁוּבָה, וְלֹא חָזַר. אָמַר לוֹ: חֲזוֹר בָּךְ. אָמַר לוֹ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִמְּךָ: הַחוֹטֵא וּמַחְטִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that **Gehazi,** the attendant of Elisha, has no share in the World-to-Come. The Gemara explains that this is **as it is written: And Elisha went to Damascus** (see II Kings 8:7). **Where did he go,** and for what purpose? **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: He went to cause Gehazi to repent, but he did not repent.** Elisha **said to him: Repent.** Gehazi **said to him: This** is the tradition that **I received from you: Whoever sins and causes the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent.**

The *Braisa* then asks: *v'Yehoshua ben Perachia mai hi* — what is the story with Yehoshua ben Perachia that parallels the Elisha and Geichazi narrative? *Ki dekatlinu Yannai Malka l'rabbanan* — when King Yannai was killing the rabbis, Yehoshua ben Perachia and Yeishu fled together to Alexandria of Egypt, since according to this tradition Yeishu was his student. When peace was restored, Shimon ben Shatach sent him a message in the poetic form: *mi-Yerushalayim ir ha-kodesh lichi Alexandria shel Mitzrayim, achoti ba'ali sharuy betochech va'ani yoshevet shomem* — from me, Jerusalem the holy city, to you, Alexandria of Egypt, my sister, my husband dwells among you and I sit desolate. The allegorical "husband" is Yehoshua ben Perachia himself, and the message signals that it is safe to return.

### Inn & Appearance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָם, אֲתָא, וְאִתְרְמִי לֵיהּ הָהוּא אוּשְׁפִּיזָא. עֲבַדוּ לֵיהּ יְקָרָא טוּבָא. אֲמַר: כַּמָּה יָפָה אַכְסַנְיָא זוֹ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רַבִּי, עֵינֶיהָ טְרוּטוֹת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: רָשָׁע! בְּכָךְ אַתָּה עוֹסֵק? אַפֵּיק אַרְבַּע מְאָה שִׁיפּוּרֵי וְשַׁמְּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya understood the message, **arose, came, and happened** to arrive at **a certain inn** on the way to Jerusalem. **They treated him with great honor.** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **said: How beautiful is this inn.** Jesus, his student, **said to him:** But **my teacher, the eyes of** the innkeeper's wife **are narrow [*terutot*].** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **said to him: Wicked one!** Do **you involve yourself with regard to that** matter, the appearance of a married woman? **He produced four hundred *shofarot* and ostracized him.**

Yehoshua ben Perachia arose and began to travel back. He came to a certain inn, *itrami lei hahu ushpiza*, and they gave him great honor. He remarked, *kamah yafah aksanya zo* — how fine this *aksanya* is — meaning, how wonderful this innkeeper has been, or perhaps how fine the inn itself is. Yeishu, however, took his remark differently: *Rebbi, eineiha terutot* — Rabbi, but her eyes are somewhat slanted. Yehoshua ben Perachia was enraged: *rasha, be-chach ata osek* — wicked one, is this what you are occupied with? I was speaking of the warmth and hospitality we were shown, and you are evaluating her looks? The reaction was severe: he took out 400 *shofarot* and placed Yeishu in *cherem*.

### Excommunication

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ כַּמָּה זִימְנִין, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: קַבְּלָן! לָא הָוֵי קָא מַשְׁגַּח בֵּיהּ. יוֹמָא חַד הֲוָה קָא קָרֵי קְרִיאַת שְׁמַע, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ. סָבַר לְקַבּוֹלֵי, אַחְוִי לֵיהּ בִּידֵיהּ. הוּא סָבַר: מִידְחָא דָּחֵי לִי. אֲזַל זְקַף לְבֵינְתָּא וְהִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לָהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲדַר בָּךְ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כָּךְ מְקוּבְּלַנִי מִמְּךָ: כָּל הַחוֹטֵא וּמַחְטִיא אֶת הָרַבִּים אֵין מַסְפִּיקִין בְּיָדוֹ לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּשׁוּבָה. וְאָמַר מָר: יֵשׁוּ כִּישֵּׁף וְהֵסִית וְהִדִּיחַ אֶת יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Jesus **came before** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **several times** and **said to him: Accept our,** i.e., my, repentance. Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **took no notice of him. One day** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **was reciting *Shema*** and Jesus **came before him** with the same request. Yehoshua ben Peraḥya **intended to accept his** request, and **signaled him with his hand** to wait until he completed his prayer. Jesus did not understand the signal and **thought: He is driving me away. He went** and **stood a brick** upright to serve as an idol **and he bowed to it.** Yehoshua ben Peraḥya then **said to** Jesus: **Repent.** Jesus **said to him: This** is the tradition that **I received from you: Whoever sins and causes the masses to sin is not given the opportunity to repent. And the Master says: Jesus performed sorcery, incited** Jews to engage in idolatry, **and led Israel astray.** Had Yehoshua ben Peraḥya not caused him to despair of atonement, he would not have taken the path of evil.

What follows is the parallel to the Elisha-Geichazi story, and in some respects the reaction here seems even more extreme. Yeishu came before him repeatedly — *ata lekameih kama zimnin* — and each time said, *kablan*, take me back, accept me, I wish to do *teshuvah*. Each time, Yehoshua ben Perachia paid him no attention. Where at least the initial response of Elisha to Geichazi carried some justification, here the *cherem* itself appears to have been a significant overreaction, and now the continued refusal to accept Yeishu back compounds it. This is the very dynamic the Gemara is criticizing: not merely that he pushed away with his left hand, but that he was *docheh bi-shtei yadayim* — pushing away with both hands.

### Final Rejection

One day, Yehoshua ben Perachia was in the middle of reciting *keriat shema* when Yeishu appeared before him again. This time, Yehoshua ben Perachia's heart softened — *savar lekabuleih* — he was prepared finally to accept him back. He signaled with his hand, *achvei lei bideih* — gesturing, apparently, for Yeishu to wait a moment while he finished the *shema*. But Yeishu misread the gesture entirely: *hu savar midadcha dachi lei* — he understood it as yet another rejection, as if to say, get away from me. That was the final straw.

### Brick Worship

Yeishu departed, *azal zakaf levinta ve-hishtachavah lah* — he went and stood up a brick and bowed down to it. When Yehoshua ben Perachia then came after him and urged him to repent, Yeishu replied with the same formulation Geichazi had used: *kach mekublani mimcha, kol ha-choteh u-machti et ha-rabbim ein maspikin beyado la'asot teshuvah*. The Gemara then adds: *keshaf ve-heisit ve-hidiah et Yisrael* — he practiced *kishuf*, he seduced and he led Israel astray. This is the sense in which he was *machti et ha-rabbim*, which the brick incident apparently initiated.

### Kishuf & Context

The term *kishuf* is significant. One approach to all the narratives in the Gospels about miraculous acts — the fish and loaves, the water — is simply to dismiss them as fabrication. But the Gemara here offers a different framework: Yeishu was a practitioner of *kishuf*, of magic, and what appeared to be miraculous was in fact sorcery. This framing does not require calling him an *oved avodah zarah* in the strict sense; it positions him as someone who seduced the people away from the traditional rabbinic path through means that could be categorized as *kishuf*. The *heisit* fits within an *avodah zarah* framework, but need not be limited to it.

There is an obvious historical difficulty: Yannai and Yehoshua ben Perachia predate the figure of Yeishu by roughly a century, so the story as told is historically incoherent. But this need not mean the rabbis were not thinking of Jesus when they told it. The Gemara is not writing history; it is constructing a narrative. The historical framing may serve a literary-theological purpose. Having Yeishu as a student of a great rabbi who traveled to Alexandria grounds the story in recognizable rabbinic experience: the student who was close and went astray, and the Alexandria setting explains where the *kishuf* was learned — Egypt, the ancient home of sorcery. That framework answers the implicit question: where did he acquire the power to mislead people? He was in Alexandria, in the world of Egyptian magic. The detail is not incidental.

It is also worth noting the structural point the Gemara is making. There were in theory four possible modes of relating to a student or disciple who errs: one could draw near with both hands; one could push with the left and draw near with the right — *smol docheh veyamin mekarevet* — which is the ideal; one could push with the right and draw near with the left, a harsher approach but still ultimately welcoming; or one could push away with both hands. Neither Elisha nor Yehoshua ben Perachia managed even the third option. They were *docheh bi-shtei yadayim* in the most complete sense.

## Mussar & Balance

### Both Hands Rejection

The Gemara's criticism of both Elisha in the Geichazi episode and Yehoshua ben Perachia in the Yeishu episode is unified under the same rubric: *docheh bi-shtei yadayim*. The ideal is *smol docheh veyamin mekarevet* — pushing away with the left, drawing near with the right — meaning that even when *mussar* must be administered, even when a student's behavior demands a hard response, the ultimate orientation must be toward *kiruv*, toward bringing the person back. Both of these figures failed this standard categorically.

### Left & Right

The *Braisa* now articulates the principle in its positive form: *leolam tehei smol docheh veyamin mekarevet* — always let the left push away and the right draw close. The imaging of left and right is not arbitrary, as noted below, but the primary point is structural: *mussar* and correction belong to the left hand, the minor hand; embrace and closeness belong to the right, the dominant hand. The proportion matters. Even when the left hand must act, the right hand must simultaneously draw near.

### Yetzer & Balance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא: אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, יֵצֶר, תִּינוֹק, וְאִשָּׁה – תְּהֵא שְׂמֹאל דּוֹחָה וְיָמִין מְקָרֶבֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** With regard to the evil **inclination,** to **a child, and** to **a woman, have the left** hand **drive** them **away and the right draw** them **near.** Total rejection of the evil inclination will lead to inaction, unlike channeling its power in a positive direction. One should not draw them too near, lest they lead him to sin, but one should not drive his wife or his child away completely, lest he cause them to abandon the path of righteousness.

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar applies this principle to three domains: the *yetzer*, children, and wives. With respect to the *yetzer* — which in this context refers primarily to sexual desire — the principle of *smol docheh veyamin mekarevet* implies that total suppression is as dangerous as total indulgence. Rashi explains: *ta'avato, im yarchikennah mimenu legamrei, yimna yishuvo shel olam* — if one distances oneself from it entirely, the continuity of the world is foreclosed; *ve-im yekarvenah legamrei, yavo lidei issur she-eino yachol lichbosh yitzro midvar aveira* — and if one indulges it entirely, one will inevitably cross into transgression, since the desire, once fully given its head, becomes uncontrollable. But beyond Rashi's formulation, there is a broader human dimension: if fundamental parts of who we are are denied entirely, they find other outlets, often destructive ones.

Critically, the Gemara frames the ideal as *smol docheh*, not *yamin docheh*. The corrective impulse — the suppressive impulse — belongs to the minor hand. The welcoming, acknowledging, properly channeling impulse belongs to the dominant right. The orientation is ultimately one of integration, not asceticism.

### Children & Wives

The same structure applies to children and wives. Rashi reads the *ishah* here not in a sexual context but in the context of correction: when a husband must rebuke his wife's conduct, or when a parent must discipline a child, the left hand may and sometimes must push away — but the right hand must simultaneously draw near. Push too hard and the child rebels; fail to push at all and the child has no guidance. The Raavad in *Ba'alei HaNefesh* reads the wife passage in the sexual register as well, understanding it as addressing two distinct dimensions: a man's relationship to his own *yetzer* and his responsiveness to his wife's needs — two sides of the same concern about proper channeling rather than suppression.

### Chesed & Din

The symbolism of the right and left hands maps onto a deeper conceptual structure: *yamin* is *chesed*, kindness; *smol* is *din*, judgment. Correction, boundary-setting, and reproof belong to the register of *din*. But *din* must always be held within the larger embrace of *chesed*. There is also a structural insight about what happens when both hands act simultaneously in opposite directions: if one pushes with the left and draws near with the right at the same time, the effect is a reorientation — the person is turned around, given a new perspective, fundamentally redirected. This is the deepest form of *mussar*.

## Aging & Illness

### Abraham's Aging

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עַד אַבְרָהָם לֹא הָיָה זִקְנָה. כֹּל דְּחָזֵי לְאַבְרָהָם אֲמַר: הַאי יִצְחָק. כֹּל דְּחָזֵי לְיִצְחָק אָמַר: הַאי אַבְרָהָם. בְּעָא אַבְרָהָם רַחֲמֵי דְּלֶיהְוֵי לֵיהּ זִקְנָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַבְרָהָם זָקֵן בָּא בַּיָּמִים״. עַד יַעֲקֹב לָא הֲוָה חוּלְשָׁא, בְּעָא רַחֲמֵי וַהֲוָה חוּלְשָׁא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֹּאמֶר לְיוֹסֵף הִנֵּה אָבִיךָ חֹלֶה״. עַד אֱלִישָׁע לָא הֲוָה אִינִישׁ חָלֵישׁ דְּמִיתְּפַח, וַאֲתָא אֱלִישָׁע וּבְעָא רַחֲמֵי וְאִיתְּפַח, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וֶאֱלִישָׁע חָלָה אֶת חׇלְיוֹ אֲשֶׁר יָמוּת בּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos the death of Elisha, the Gemara says: **Until** the time of **Abraham there was no aging,** and the old and the young looked the same. **Anyone who saw Abraham said: That is Isaac,** and **anyone who saw Isaac said: That is Abraham. Abraham prayed for mercy, that he would undergo aging, as it is stated: "And Abraham was old, well stricken in age"** (Genesis 24:1). There is no mention of aging before that verse. **Until** the time of **Jacob there was no weakness,** i.e., illness. Jacob **prayed for mercy and there was weakness, as it is stated: "And one said to Joseph: Behold, your father is ill"** (Genesis 48:1). **Until** the time of **Elisha, there was no ill person who recovered, and Elisha came and prayed for mercy and recovered, as it is stated: "And Elisha was fallen ill of his illness from which he was to die"** (II Kings 13:14). That is the first mention of a person who was ill and who did not die from that illness.

The Gemara now takes a striking turn, reframing three categories of human experience — old age, illness, and recovery from illness — as gifts rather than afflictions, each one instituted at the prayer of a righteous figure.

*Ad Avraham lo hayah ziknah* — until Abraham, no one experienced old age. People died, but they retained the appearance of youth until their final day. The consequence was that Abraham and Isaac looked identical — *kol de-chazi l'Avraham amar Yitzchak, kol de-chazi l'Yitzchak amar Avraham* — anyone who saw one thought he was the other. This created a kind of social confusion: the wisdom and experience of the elder could not be read from his face; society had no visual marker by which to orient its deference. Abraham prayed that *ziknah* should become visible — *d'leihavei lei ziknah* — and the result is recorded in the verse *va-Avraham zaken ba ba-yamim*, which the Gemara identifies as the first instance in the Torah of anyone being described as having grown old. The gift of *ziknah*, the Gemara suggests, allows a society to honor its elders, to recognize their accumulated wisdom, to relate to them differently than to youth. This presupposes, of course, a culture that respects the elderly — and the contrast with contemporary youth-obsessed culture, where the marks of age are to be erased rather than honored, is not lost.

### Jacob's Illness

*Ad Yaakov lo hayah chulsha* — until Jacob, no one became ill. Death arrived without warning and without preparation; people simply expired when their time came. Jacob prayed that illness should precede death — *ba'ei rachamei ve-havah chulsha* — and the result is recorded in the verse *va-yomar l'Yosef, hinei avicha choleh*. Rashi explains the gift: illness gives time to prepare. The person who is dying can gather his family, offer *berakhot*, complete unfinished emotional and spiritual business. The family has time to transition. There is no sudden void. Jacob, who himself gave *berakhot* to his sons on his deathbed, exemplifies exactly what this gift makes possible: the deliberate, conscious passage out of the world, with the people one loves gathered close.

The Gemara notes that in prior generations there were *berakhot* given — Abraham blessed Isaac, Isaac blessed Jacob — but not from a deathbed, not in the context of final parting. They just happened to give *berakhot* at some point. The gift of *chulsha* creates the context in which the final blessing becomes a true *vidui*, a true leave-taking.

### Elisha's Recovery

*Adei Elisha lo hava inish chalish d'mitpach* — before Elisha, no one had ever fallen ill and recovered. Sickness was invariably terminal; it was simply the process of dying, manifesting physically. Elisha prayed, and the possibility of illness followed by recovery was introduced into the world. The proof-text is the verse describing Elisha's own death: *v'Elisha chala et chalyo asher yamut bo* — implying that this final illness was the one by which he would die, meaning he had previously been ill and had *not* died. The gift here is twofold: it gives individuals the opportunity to survive and do *teshuvah*, and it gives God, so to speak, the instrument of *yissurin* — suffering as a wake-up call, as a minor punishment that redirects before the final reckoning arrives. The stories in Tanakh of people who fall ill, pray, and recover now make sense within this framework: illness as divine intervention that opens a door rather than closes one.

## Dor HaMabul

### No Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, וְאֵין עוֹמְדִין בַּדִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָדוֹן רוּחִי בָאָדָם לְעֹלָם״ – לֹא דִּין וְלֹא רוּחַ. דּוֹר הַפְּלַגָּה אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּפֶץ ה׳ אֹתָם מִשָּׁם עַל פְּנֵי כׇל הָאָרֶץ״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּמִשָּׁם הֱפִיצָם״. ״וַיָּפֶץ ה׳ אֹתָם״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וּמִשָּׁם הֱפִיצָם ה׳״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. אַנְשֵׁי סְדוֹם אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְאַנְשֵׁי סְדֹם רָעִים וְחַטָּאִים לַה׳ מְאֹד״ – רָעִים בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, וְחַטָּאִים לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. אֲבָל עוֹמְדִין בַּדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The members of **the generation of the flood have no share in the World-to-Come and will not stand in judgment** at the end of days, **as it is stated: "My soul shall not abide [*yadon*] in man forever"** (Genesis 6:3); **neither** will they stand in **judgment [*din*] nor** shall their **souls** be restored to them. The members of **the generation of the dispersion have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the Lord scattered them from there upon the face of all the earth"** (Genesis 11:8), **and it is written: "And from there did the Lord scatter them** upon the face of all the earth" (Genesis 11:9). **"And the Lord scattered them"** indicates **in this world; "and from there did the Lord scatter them"** indicates **for the World-to-Come. The people of Sodom have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners before the Lord exceedingly"** (Genesis 13:13). **"Wicked"** indicates **in this world; "and sinners"** indicates **for the World-to-Come. But they will stand in judgment** and they will be sentenced to eternal contempt.

The Gemara arrives at the next *Mishnah*, which continues the enumeration of those who have no *chelek l'olam haba*, now moving from individuals to entire groups. The *Mishnah* also introduces a distinction that runs throughout the subsequent discussion: the difference between having no *chelek l'olam haba* and not even being *omed b'din* — not even being resurrected to stand in judgment.

The general eschatological framework the Gemara operates with is roughly as follows: *Mashiach* will come, *techiyat ha-meitim* will occur, everyone will be resurrected, and on the day of judgment the righteous will receive *olam haba* while the *resha'im* will return to *Gehinnom*. *Ein lo chelek l'olam haba* presumably means one is resurrected but at the judgment is not retained — one does not remain for *olam haba*. But there are categories of sinners who do not even reach resurrection — they are not *omed b'din* at all — and this is treated as a worse punishment still, as though one's case is too contemptible even to receive a hearing.

*Dor HaMabul ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the generation of the Flood has no portion in the world to come. The proof-text is *lo yadon ruchi ba-adam l'olam* — My spirit will not contend with man forever. *Lo din* — no judgment; *velo ruach* — no spirit. They are not even resurrected. Why is not being judged worse than being judged and condemned? One reflection: at least a judgment means that one's life was worth adjudicating, that something was at stake, that the case warranted hearing. To be dismissed without a hearing is to be told that one's existence does not rise even to the level of a meaningful *din*.

### Dor HaPlagah

*Dor HaPlagah ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* — the generation of the dispersion, the builders of the Tower of Babel, also have no *chelek l'olam haba*. The proof-text is *va-yafetz Hashem otam mi-sham al pnei kol ha-aretz* — God scattered them — read as: *va-yafetz* in this world; *u-mi-sham hefitzam* for the world to come. Crucially, however, the *Mishnah* does not say that *Dor HaPlagah* is not *omed b'din*. They are scattered — they lose *olam haba* — but they apparently do stand in judgment. What precisely their sin was, and why God viewed it so severely, is not fully transparent from the narrative.

### Disputes on Groups

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין עוֹמְדִין בַּדִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״עַל כֵּן לֹא יָקוּמוּ רְשָׁעִים בַּמִּשְׁפָּט וְחַטָּאִים בַּעֲדַת צַדִּיקִים״. ״עַל כֵּן לֹא יָקוּמוּ רְשָׁעִים בַּמִּשְׁפָּט״ – זֶה דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל. ״וְחַטָּאִים בַּעֲדַת צַדִּיקִים״ – אֵלּוּ אַנְשֵׁי סְדוֹם. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: אֵינָם עוֹמְדִין בַּעֲדַת צַדִּיקִים, אֲבָל עוֹמְדִין בַּעֲדַת רְשָׁעִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** Both **these,** the people of Sodom, **and those,** the members of the generation of the flood, **will not stand in judgment, as it is stated: "Therefore the wicked shall not stand** in judgment, nor sinners in the congregation of the righteous" (Psalms 1:5). **"Therefore the wicked shall not stand in judgment"; this** is referring to **the generation of the flood,** about whom it is written: "The wickedness of man was great upon the earth" (Genesis 6:5). **"Nor sinners in the congregation of the righteous"; these are the people of Sodom,** about whom it is written: "And the men of Sodom were wicked and sinners" (Genesis 13:13). The Sages **said to** Rabbi Neḥemya: **They will not stand** in judgment for resurrection **in the congregation of the righteous, but they will stand** in judgment **in the congregation of the wicked.**

*Anshei Sedom ein lahem chelek l'olam haba*, and they stand for *din*. The proof-text is *v'anshei Sedom ra'im v'chatta'im la-Hashem me'od* — *ra'im ba-olam hazeh, v'chatta'im l'olam haba*. They do, however, stand in judgment.

Rabbi Nechemiah disagrees: both *Dor HaPlagah* and *Anshei Sedom* are not *omed b'din*. His proof-text is *al ken lo yakumu resha'im ba-mishpat, v'chatta'im ba-adat tzaddikim* — the *resha'im* will not stand in judgment; the *chatta'im* will not stand in the congregation of the righteous. He reads *resha'im* as *Dor HaMabul* and *chatta'im* as *Anshei Sedom*, and he argues that the second clause does not simply restrict where they stand but removes them from *din* entirely.

The *Tannaim* respond to Rabbi Nechemiah: *einam omdim ba-adat tzaddikim* — they do not stand *with the righteous*, but that does not mean they receive no judgment at all. The *chatta'im* stand in judgment; they simply stand *ba-adat resha'im*, among the wicked. They will be judged, and they will be found guilty. The distinction between *resha'im* and *chatta'im* maps neatly onto the language of the Sedom verse itself: *ra'im v'chatta'im*, where the more severe term *ra'im* might correspond to those who get no hearing, and *chatta'im* to those who do.

### Meraglim & Midbar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְרַגְּלִים אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיָּמֻתוּ הָאֲנָשִׁים מוֹצִאֵי דִבַּת הָאָרֶץ רָעָה בַּמַּגֵּפָה לִפְנֵי ה׳״. ״וַיָּמֻתוּ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״בַּמַּגֵּפָה״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. דּוֹר הַמִּדְבָּר אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, וְאֵין עוֹמְדִין בַּדִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּמִּדְבָּר הַזֶּה יִתַּמּוּ וְשָׁם יָמֻתוּ״. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, עֲלֵיהֶם הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״אִסְפוּ לִי חֲסִידָי כֹּרְתֵי בְרִיתִי עֲלֵי זָבַח״.
>
> **Translation:** **The spies** who spread an evil report of their visit to Canaan **have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And those men who spread the evil report about the land died by plague before the Lord"** (Numbers 14:37). **"And…died"** indicates **in this world; "by plague"** indicates **for the World-to-Come.** The members of **the generation of the wilderness have no share in the World-to-Come and will not stand in judgment, as it is stated: "In this wilderness they shall be consumed, and there they shall die"** (Numbers 14:35). "They shall be consumed" indicates in this world; "and there they shall die" indicates for the World-to-Come; this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Eliezer says:** The members of the generation of the wilderness were essentially righteous, and **about them** the verse **says: "Gather My pious together to Me, those that have entered into My covenant by offering"** (Psalms 50:5). It is they who entered into the covenant with God and they will certainly be rewarded in the future.

The *Mishnah* includes a line about the *Meraglim* — the spies: *ein lahem chelek l'olam haba*, derived from *vayamutu ha-anashim motzei dibat ha-aretz ra'ah ba-magefah lifnei Hashem* — *vayamutu ba-olam hazeh, ba-magefah l'olam haba*. This line is, however, textually redundant and rendered moot by the following statement: *Dor HaMidbar ein lahem chelek l'olam haba, v'einam omdim b'din* — the entire generation that lived through the forty years in the wilderness has no *chelek l'olam haba* and does not even stand in judgment. Since the *Meraglim* were part of *Dor HaMidbar*, the broader category absorbs the narrower one, which is why the *Meraglim* line was excised.

This ruling about *Dor HaMidbar* is genuinely difficult. In *Chazal*'s eyes, *Dor HaMidbar* is often described as a generation of exceptional righteousness — they received the Torah, they witnessed the *Shekhinah*. And even setting aside their virtues, the nature of their sins does not obviously warrant the most severe eschatological punishment. Perhaps the reasoning relates to the collective, consequential nature of their sin: the rejection of the Land of Israel, the foreclosure of the national destiny for an entire generation. Whatever the explanation, the proof-text — *ba-midbar hazeh yitamu v'sham yamutu* — does not transparently convey *einam omdim b'din*; it seems to speak to *ein lahem chelek l'olam haba* at most.

### R. Eliezer View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֲדַת קֹרַח אֵינָהּ עֲתִידָה לַעֲלוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּכַס עֲלֵיהֶם הָאָרֶץ״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיֹּאבְדוּ מִתּוֹךְ הַקָּהָל״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר, עֲלֵיהֶם הוּא אוֹמֵר: ״ה׳ מֵמִית וּמְחַיֶּה מוֹרִיד שְׁאוֹל וַיָּעַל״.
>
> **Translation:** **The assembly of Korah is not destined to arise** for resurrection, **as it is stated: "And the earth closed upon them"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in this world,** and also: **"And they perished from among the assembly"** (Numbers 16:33), meaning **in the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Eliezer says: About them** the verse **says: "The Lord kills and makes alive; He lowers to the grave, and raises"** (I Samuel 2:6), indicating that the assembly of Korah has a share in the World-to-Come.

Rabbi Akiva holds the severe position on *Dor HaMidbar*. Rabbi Eliezer fundamentally disagrees: *alehem hu omer, isfu li chasidai, kortei vriti alei zavach* — it is about them that the verse says, gather for Me My pious ones, who made My covenant over sacrifice. Far from having no *chelek l'olam haba*, *Dor HaMidbar* in Rabbi Eliezer's view are the *chasidim* who will be gathered to God. Rabbi Eliezer likely held that it was specifically the *Meraglim* who forfeited their *chelek l'olam haba*, while the broader *Dor HaMidbar* does not.

The *Adat Korach* presents a unique case: they are still alive, buried in the netherworld, and the question is not resurrection but return. *Einah atidah la'alot* — they are not destined to ascend. Rabbi Akiva: *va-tichas aleihem ha-aretz ba-olam hazeh, va-yovdu mitoch ha-kahal l'olam haba*. Rabbi Eliezer, consistent with his broader view, says: *Hashem memit u-mechayeh, morid she'ol vaya'al* — God brings them down and God will bring them up. Rabbi Eliezer likely maintained that the *Meraglim* lose their *chelek l'olam haba*, *Adat Korach* will eventually ascend, and *Dor HaMidbar* overall will be among the *chasidei* gathered to God. Rabbi Akiva denies all of them.

## Flood Disputes

### Akiva & Beteira

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל אֵין לָהֶם חֵלֶק לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּמַח אֶת כׇּל הַיְקוּם אֲשֶׁר עַל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה״. ״וַיִּמַח אֶת כׇּל הַיְּקוּם״ – בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה, ״וַיִּמָּחוּ מִן הָאָרֶץ״ – לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בְּתֵירָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא חַיִּין וְלֹא נִדּוֹנִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״לֹא יָדוֹן רוּחִי בָאָדָם לְעֹלָם״ – לֹא דִּין וְלֹא רוּחַ. דָּבָר אַחֵר: ״לֹא יָדוֹן רוּחִי״ – שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא נִשְׁמָתָן חוֹזֶרֶת לִנְדָנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The members of **the generation of the flood have no share in the World-to-Come, as it is stated: "And He obliterated every living thing that was upon the face of the ground,** from man to cattle to creeping animal to the birds of the heaven; and they were obliterated from the earth" (Genesis 7:23). **"And He obliterated every living thing"** indicates **in this world,** whereas **"and they were obliterated from the earth"** indicates **for the World-to-Come;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira says:** The members of the generation of the flood will **neither live nor be judged, as it is stated: "My soul shall not abide [*yadon*] in man forever"** (Genesis 6:3), meaning **neither** will they stand in **judgment [*din*], nor** shall their **souls** be restored to them. **Alternatively, "My soul shall not abide"** means **their souls will not return to their sheaths [*nadan*],** i.e., to their bodies.

A *Braisa* now records the views of additional *Tannaim* on *Dor HaMabul*. Rabbi Akiva's position is: *vayimach et kol ha-yekum asher al pnei ha-adamah* — God wiped out everything that stood, *ba-olam hazeh*; *vayimachu min ha-aretz*, wiped out, *l'olam haba*. They are erased from both worlds.

Rabbi Yehudah ben Beteira sharpens the formulation: *lo chayim v'lo nidonim* — they neither live (in the resurrection) nor are they judged. His proof-text is the same verse already cited in the *Mishnah*: *lo yadon ruchi ba-adam l'olam, lo din v'lo ruach*. He adds a second reading: *she-lo tehei nishmasam chozeret le-nadana* — their *neshamah* does not return to its sheath. The *ruach* will not return to its vessel; they are not resurrected, and there is no day of judgment for them.

### Menachem's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מְנַחֵם בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵף אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשָׁעָה שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מַחֲזִיר נְשָׁמוֹת לִפְגָרִים מֵתִים, נִשְׁמָתָן קָשָׁה לָהֶם בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תַּהֲרוּ חֲשַׁשׁ תֵּלְדוּ קַשׁ רוּחֲכֶם אֵשׁ תֹּאכַלְכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Menaḥem, son of Rabbi Yosef, says: Even at a time when the Holy One, Blessed be He, restores souls to lifeless corpses,** the souls of the generation of the flood **will** afflict **them harshly** as if they were **in Gehenna, as it is stated: "You shall conceive chaff; you shall bring forth straw; your soul is a fire that shall devour you"** (Isaiah 33:11).

Rabbi Menachem bar Rabbi Yose goes further still, raising a difficulty: at the time of *techiyat ha-meitim*, when God restores souls to dead bodies, what happens to *Dor HaMabul*? He resolves it as follows: *nishmasam kashah lahem be-Gehinnom* — even at the moment of resurrection, their soul continues to suffer in *Gehinnom*. The precise mechanism is difficult to parse. One reading is that they are not resurrected in this world at all; rather, the restoration of their soul does not bring them back to the surface but intensifies their suffering wherever they remain below, in *Gehinnom*. Another reading is that they are somehow simultaneously present in the resurrection and still suffering — a kind of dual state, reminiscent of a Horcrux in its structural strangeness.

The proof-text Rabbi Menachem cites is *taharoo chashash, teildoo kash, ruchachem esh tochalechem* — you shall conceive of chaff and give birth to straw; your spirit, a fire will consume you. The image of chaff and straw, materials that ignite easily and burn to nothing, evokes a soul that is present only to be destroyed by fire — there is nothing substantial to resurrect, or whatever is resurrected is immediately consumed.

## Causes of Sin

### Wealth & Pride

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל לֹא נִתְגָּאוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל טוֹבָה שֶׁהִשְׁפִּיעַ לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וּמָה כְּתִיב בָּהֶם? ״בָּתֵּיהֶם שָׁלוֹם מִפָּחַד וְלֹא שֵׁבֶט אֱלוֹהַּ עֲלֵיהֶם״, וּכְתִיב: ״שׁוֹרוֹ עִבַּר וְלֹא יַגְעִל תְּפַלֵּט פָּרָתוֹ וְלֹא תְשַׁכֵּל״, וּכְתִיב: ״יְשַׁלְּחוּ כַצֹּאן עֲוִילֵיהֶם וְיַלְדֵיהֶם יְרַקֵּדוּן״, וּכְתִיב: ״יִשְׂאוּ בְּתֹף וְכִנּוֹר וְיִשְׂמְחוּ לְקוֹל עוּגָב״, וּכְתִיב: ״יְכַלּוּ יְמֵיהֶם בַּטּוֹב וּשְׁנֵיהֶם בַּנְּעִימִים״, וּכְתִיב: ״וּבְרֶגַע שְׁאוֹל יֵחָתּוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Sota* 3:10): **The generation of the flood became haughty** and sinned **due only to the** excessive **goodness that the Holy One, Blessed be He, bestowed upon them. And what is written concerning them,** indicating that goodness? **"Their houses are safe without fear, nor is the rod of God upon them"** (Job 21:9). **And it is written: "Their bull sires, and will not fall; their cow calves, and does not cast her calf"** (Job 21:10). **And it is written: "They send forth their little ones like a flock, and their children dance"** (Job 21:11). **And it is written: "They sing to the timbrel and harp, and rejoice at the sound of the pipe"** (Job 21:12). **And it is written: "They shall spend their days in prosperity, and their years in pleasures"** (Job 36:11). **And it is written: "And peacefully they go down to the grave"** (Job 21:13).

The Gemara now examines the root causes of *Dor HaMabul*'s sin, offering two competing explanations. The first is attributed to Rabbi Yochanan: *Dor HaMabul lo nitga'u ela bi-shvil tovah she-hishpia lahem HaKadosh Baruch Hu* — their arrogance arose solely from the overflowing goodness God had showered upon them. The Gemara then brings a series of verses from *Iyov* to illustrate the extraordinary comfort of their lives. *Bateihem shalom mi-pachad, v'lo shevet Eloha aleihem* — their homes were free of fear, they never felt the rod of divine suffering. *Shoro avar v'lo yag'il, taplet parato v'lo tishakeil* — their oxen went and did not miscarry its seed; their cow gave birth without miscarriage. *Yishalchu khi-tzon avileihem, v'yaldeihem yerakedun* — they sent their children out like a flock, and their children danced. *Yis'u b'tof v'khinor* — they carried drums and harps. *Yivlu yemeihem b'tov, u-shnoteihem bi-ne'imim* — their days were spent in goodness, their years in pleasantness. And then, suddenly: *u-ve-rega she'ol yeichatu* — in a moment they descended to the depths.

### Mocking God

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִיא גָּרְמָה שֶׁאָמְרוּ לָאֵל: ״סוּר מִמֶּנּוּ וְדַעַת דְּרָכֶיךָ לֹא חָפָצְנוּ. מַה שַׁדַּי כִּי נַעַבְדֶנּוּ וּמַה נּוֹעִיל כִּי נִפְגַּע בּוֹ״. אָמְרוּ: כְּלוּם צְרִיכִין אָנוּ לוֹ אֶלָּא לְטִיפָּה שֶׁל גְּשָׁמִים? יֵשׁ לָנוּ נְהָרוֹת וּמַעֲיָנוֹת שֶׁאָנוּ מִסְתַּפְּקִין מֵהֶן. אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: בַּטּוֹבָה שֶׁהִשְׁפַּעְתִּי לָהֶן, בָּהּ מַכְעִיסִין אוֹתִי, וּבָהּ אֲנִי דָּן אוֹתָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַאֲנִי הִנְנִי מֵבִיא אֶת הַמַּבּוּל מַיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And that** success **caused** them **to say to God: "Depart from us; for we desire not the knowledge of Your ways. What is the Almighty, that we should serve Him, and what profit should we have if we pray unto Him"** (Job 21:14-15). The members of the generation of the flood **said: Do we need Him for anything, even for** the **drop of rain** that He causes to fall? **We have rivers and springs from which we take our supply** of water; we do not fear Him. **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: With the goodness that I bestowed upon them, with** that **they infuriate Me and with it I will sentence them, as it is stated: "And behold I will bring the flood of water"** (Genesis 6:17).

God's response: *ba-tovah she-hishpati lahem ba hikh'isuni* — the very goodness I poured out to them is what they used to anger Me, to throw in My face. And you want to talk about water? *Bah ani dan otam* — by that same water I will judge them. *V'ani hineni meivi et ha-mabul mayim.*

### Eye & Lust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אָמַר: דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל לֹא נִתְגָּאוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל גַּלְגַּל הָעַיִן שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְמַיִם, [שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְיִקְחוּ לָהֶם נָשִׁים מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר בָּחָרוּ״]. לְפִיכָךְ דָּן אוֹתָן בְּמַיִם, שֶׁדּוֹמֶה לְגַלְגַּל הָעַיִן, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִבְקְעוּ כׇּל מַעְיְנוֹת תְּהוֹם רַבָּה וַאֲרֻבּוֹת הַשָּׁמַיִם נִפְתָּחוּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: The generation of the flood became haughty due only to the** covetousness of the **eyeball, which is similar to water, as it is stated:** "And the sons of the prominent men saw that the daughters of men were fair; **and they took for themselves wives from anyone they chose"** (Genesis 6:2). **Consequently,** God **punished them with water, which is similar to an eyeball [*legalgal ha'ayin*], as it is stated: "All the fountains [*mayenot*] of the great deep were breached, and the flues of heaven were opened"** (Genesis 7:11).

Rabbi Yosi offers a different etiology: *Dor HaMabul lo nitga'u ela bi-shvil galgal ha-ayin* — their downfall came not from their wealth but from the lust of the eye. This picks up a theme that traces back to the very first sin: one sees, one desires, one takes. The verse cited is *va-yikchu lahem nashim mi-kol asher bacharu* — they took for themselves women from whoever they chose. The *galgal ha-ayin* — the wheel of the eye, the aperture — is what drove the desire and ultimately the taking.

### Creation & Taking

*U-lefichach danam ba-mayim* — therefore God judged them with water, *she-domeh le-galgal ha-ayin* — which resembles the eye. The connection is linguistic and visual: *ma'ayan* shares its root with *ayin*; both describe an aperture, an opening from which something flows. The punishment mirrors the sin in its physical form. *Nivke'u kol ma'ayanot tehom rabbah* — all the wellsprings of the great deep burst forth. *V'aruvot ha-shamayim niftachu* — and the windows of heaven were opened. Eye — aperture — water: the imagery is woven through the judgment.

The contemporary resonance is unmistakable. Powerful men who believe they may take whatever they desire, who see women as objects available to them, enact the very pattern the Gemara is describing: *ra'u, va-yit'avvu, va-yikahu* — they saw, they desired, they took.

### Rabba Echo

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל בְּרַבָּה קִלְקְלוּ, וּבְרַבָּה נִידּוֹנוּ. בְּרַבָּה קִלְקְלוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּרְא ה׳ כִּי רַבָּה רָעַת הָאָדָם״, וּבְרַבָּה נִידּוֹנוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כׇּל מַעְיְנוֹת תְּהוֹם רַבָּה״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שְׁלֹשָׁה נִשְׁתַּיְּירוּ מֵהֶם – בְּלוּעָה דְּגָדֵר, וְחַמֵּי טְבֶרְיָא, וְעֵינָא רַבָּתִי דְּבֵירָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** The members of **the generation of the flood sinned with "great" and were punished with "great." They sinned with "great," as it is stated: "And the Lord saw that the wickedness of man was great"** (Genesis 6:5). **And they were punished with great, as it is stated: "All the fountains of the great deep** were breached" (Genesis 7:11). **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Three** fountains **of** the great deep that were breached in the flood **remained,** and boiling water continues to flow in them as it did during the flood. They are: **Beloa of Gader, and the hot springs of Tiberias, and the great spring of Beiram.**

Rabbi Yochanan draws attention to a verbal echo running through the narrative: *Dor HaMabul be-rabbah kilkelu u-ve-rabbah nidonu* — they corrupted with *rabbah* and were judged with *rabbah*. The word appears first in the account of their sin — *va-yar Hashem ki rabbah ra'at ha-adam* — and then in the account of their judgment — *kol ma'ayanot tehom rabbah*. The verbal correspondence between sin and punishment is deliberate and structural.

### Hot Springs

Rabbi Yochanan adds a topographical note: *shloshah nish'tayru mehem* — three of those primordial hot springs of the *mabul* still exist in the world. *Belu'a de-Gader, Chamei Teverya, ve-Einah Rabbati de-Biram* — the springs at Gader, the famous hot springs of Tiberias, and the great spring of Biram. These remnants of the flood's waters endure as a physical trace of that judgment.

## Flesh Corruption

### Mixed Species

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״כִּי הִשְׁחִית כׇּל בָּשָׂר אֶת דַּרְכּוֹ עַל הָאָרֶץ״, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהִרְבִּיעוּ בְּהֵמָה עַל חַיָּה, וְחַיָּה עַל בְּהֵמָה, וְהַכֹּל עַל אָדָם, וְאָדָם עַל הַכֹּל. אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר כָּהֲנָא: וְכוּלָּם חָזְרוּ, חוּץ מִתּוּשְׁלְמִי.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"For all flesh had corrupted its way upon the earth"** (Genesis 6:12), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** This **teaches that** the people of the generation of the flood **mated** male **domesticated animals with** female **undomesticated animals, and** male **undomesticated animals with** female **domesticated animals, and all** male animals **with human** females, **and human** males **with all** female animals. **Rabbi Abba bar Kahana says: And** after the flood **all of** the creatures **returned** to mate with their own species, **except** for the bird called ***tushlami*,** which continued to mate with other species.

*Ki hish-hit kol basar et darko al ha-aretz* — all flesh had corrupted its way upon the land. Rabbi Yochanan reads this as: *she-hirbi'u behemah al hayah ve-hayah al behemah* — they caused animals to crossbreed with other animals, crossing not merely different species but categories — domesticated with wild, *behemah* with *hayah* — *ve-ha-kol al adam ve-adam al ha-kol* — and humans with all of these. The word *basar* — flesh — signals the sin of the flesh; *hish-hit*, corruption, describes what the crossing of these natural boundaries represented.

### Animal Exception

Rabbi Abba bar Kahana observes that after the *mabul*, the animals returned to mating only within their own *min* — *ve-khullam hazaru* — all of them went back to their kind, except for one bird called *tushlami*, known for its promiscuity, which continued its prior behavior. All others returned to the natural order that had been violated.

### Power of Theft

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיֹּאמֶר ה׳ לְנֹחַ קֵץ כׇּל בָּשָׂר בָּא לְפָנַי״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בֹּא וּרְאֵה כַּמָּה גָּדוֹל כֹּחַ שֶׁל חָמָס, שֶׁהֲרֵי דּוֹר הַמַּבּוּל עָבְרוּ עַל הַכֹּל, וְלֹא נֶחְתַּם עֲלֵיהֶם גְּזַר דִּינָם עַד שֶׁפָּשְׁטוּ יְדֵיהֶם בְּגָזֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי מָלְאָה הָאָרֶץ חָמָס מִפְּנֵיהֶם וְהִנְנִי מַשְׁחִיתָם אֶת הָאָרֶץ״. וּכְתִיב: ״הֶחָמָס קָם לְמַטֵּה רֶשַׁע, לֹא מֵהֶם וְלֹא מֵהֲמוֹנָם וְלֹא מֶהֱמֵהֶם וְלֹא נֹהַּ בָּהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And God said to Noah: The end of all flesh is come before Me,** for the earth is filled with robbery through them, and behold, I will destroy them with the earth" (Genesis 6:13), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Come and see how great is the power of robbery, as the generation of the flood violated every** precept, **but their sentence** to be destroyed **was not sealed until they extended their hands** and engaged **in robbery, as it is stated: "For the earth is filled with robbery through them, and behold, I will destroy them with the earth"** (Genesis 6:13). **And it is written: "Robbery is risen up into a rod of wickedness; nothing comes from them, nor from their multitude, nor from any of them, nor shall there be wailing [*no'ah*] for them"** (Ezekiel 7:11).

*Va-yomer Elohim le-Noach, ketz kol basar ba le-fanai*. Rabbi Yochanan comments: *bo u-re'eh kammah gadol koha shel hamas* — come and see how great is the power of *hamas*, of theft and violence. They had committed every variety of sin — the sexual perversions we have described, the arrogance, the mocking of God — *ve-lo nichtam aleihem gezar dinam ad she-pash'tu yedeihem be-gezel* — yet the decree of their judgment was not sealed until they extended their hands into outright theft. Theft is, in a sense, the culmination of the pattern: you see, you desire, you simply take what belongs to another. *She-ne'emar ki male'ah ha-aretz hamas mi-peneihem ve-hinneni mash-hitam et ha-aretz* — the earth was filled with *hamas* from before them, and I will destroy them with the earth.

### Hamas Testifies

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁזָּקַף עַצְמוֹ כְּמַקֵּל, וְעָמַד לִפְנֵי הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, וְאָמַר לְפָנָיו, רִבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם: ״לֹא מֵהֶם וְלֹא מֵהֲמוֹנָם וְלֹא מֶהֱמֵהֶם וְלֹא נֹהַּ בָּהֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar says:** This **teaches that** robbery **straightened itself like a rod and stood before the Holy One, Blessed be He, and said before Him: Master of the Universe: "Nothing comes from them, nor from their multitude, nor from any of them, nor shall there be wailing for them,"** i.e., none of the robbers are fit to exist.

*Ve-khtiv ha-hamas kam le-matteh rasha* — and *hamas* rises as a rod for the wicked. Rabbi Elazar reads this striking verse as follows: the *hamas* literally stood up like a rod, *ve-ha-hamas amad lifnei HaKadosh Baruch Hu, ve-amar lefanav, Ribbono shel Olam, lo me-hem* — *hamas* itself stood before God and said: Master of the Universe, I can no longer be among them. Even the *hamas* got fed up. Even theft reached a point where it testified against the generation. And God responded in kind: *lo me-hem, ve-lo me-hamonam, ve-lo me-hemahem, ve-lo noah ba-hem*.

### Noah's Favor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** (וְאַף עַל נֹחַ נֶחְתַּם גְּזַר דִּין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְלֹא נֹחַ בָּהֶם״). תָּנָא דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אַף עַל נֹחַ נֶחְתַּךְ גְּזַר דִּין, אֶלָּא שֶׁמָּצָא חֵן בְּעֵינֵי ה׳, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״נִחַמְתִּי כִּי עֲשִׂיתֶם וְנֹחַ מָצָא חֵן בְּעֵינֵי ה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **And the sentence of Noah was also sealed;** he was going to die with the rest of the generation of the flood, **as it is stated: Nor shall there be *No'aḥ* for them.** The Gemara interprets the term spelled *nun*, *heh*, in the aforementioned verse, as if it were spelled *nun*, *ḥet*. **The school of Rabbi Yishmael taught: The sentence of Noah was also decided; but** he was spared through the kindness of God due to the fact **that he found favor in the eyes of God, as it is stated: "For I regret that I have made them. And Noah found favor in the eyes of the Lord"** (Genesis 6:7-8). The juxtaposition of the term "and Noah" to the phrase "for I regret that I have made them" indicates that Noah should have been killed as well.

That final phrase, *ve-lo noah ba-hem*, introduces the figure of Noah — a pun on his name. *Ve-af al Noach nichtam gezar din* — even Noah had the decree of judgment sealed against him. The proof-text is read by *tana de-vei Rabbi Yishmael*: even Noah was included in that verdict. The reason he was saved was not that he was objectively innocent of the decree — *ella she-matza hen be-einei Hashem* — but that he found unearned favor in God's eyes. The *chen* was not a function of his strict desert; it was something else, something of grace.

### Consolation & Regret

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּנָּחֶם ה׳ כִּי עָשָׂה אֶת הָאָדָם בָּאָרֶץ״. כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי אָמַר: אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא, יָפֶה עָשִׂיתִי שֶׁתִּקַּנְתִּי לָהֶם קְבָרוֹת בָּאָרֶץ. מַאי מַשְׁמַע? כְּתִיב הָכָא ״וַיִּנָּחֶם ה׳״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם ״וַיְנַחֵם אוֹתָם וַיְדַבֵּר עַל לִבָּם״.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"*Vayyinaḥem* the Lord that He made man on the earth [*ba'aretz*]"** (Genesis 6:6). The meaning of the term "*vayyinaḥem*" is subject to a dispute. **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** in explanation: **The Holy One, Blessed be He, said: I did well that I prepared graves for** people **in the earth [*ba'aretz*].** The Gemara asks: **From where** is it **inferred** that the term "*vayyinaḥem*" has a positive connotation? The Gemara answers: **It is written here: "*Vayyinaḥem*," and it is written there: "And he comforted them [*vayenaḥem otam*] and spoke to their heart"** (Genesis 50:21).

The verse *va-yinnahem Hashem ki asah et ha-adam ba-aretz* is then read in two ways by *ika de-amrei*: one, God was consoled by the fact that He had arranged for burial in the earth — *yafeh asiti she-hitkaneti lahem kevarot ba-aretz*, I did well in establishing for them graves in the ground; or two, *lo yafeh asiti* — I did not do well, I regret having made graves for them in the earth. Rashi reads both interpretations not as being literally about burial but as metaphors for whether God was comforted or regretful about the destruction of humanity — either finding consolation in the ending of the wicked generation, or regretting that He had brought that destruction at all, wishing He had given them yet another chance.

## Noah Debate

### Yochanan View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״אֵלֶּה תּוֹלְדוֹת נֹחַ [נֹחַ אִישׁ צַדִּיק תָּמִים הָיָה בְּדֹרֹתָיו]״. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״בְּדוֹרוֹתָיו״, וְלֹא בְּדוֹרוֹת אֲחֵרִים. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: ״בְּדוֹרוֹתָיו״, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בְּדוֹרוֹת אֲחֵרִים.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the verse: **"These are the generations of Noah; Noah was a righteous man, and wholehearted in his generations"** (Genesis 6:9), **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** Relative to the other people **of his generation** he was righteous and wholehearted, **but not** relative to those **of other generations. And Reish Lakish says: In his generation** he was righteous and wholehearted despite being surrounded by bad influences; **all the more so** would he have been considered righteous and wholehearted **in other generations.**

The Torah describes Noah as *ish tzaddik tamim hayah be-dorotav* — in his generations. Rabbi Yochanan focuses on that qualifier: *be-dorotav* — for his generation, but not compared to other generations. The implication is that Noah's righteousness was relative; measured against the extraordinary evil of his surroundings he looked righteous, but against a generation of actual *tzaddikim* he would not have stood out.

### Lakish View

Resh Lakish reads the same word in the opposite direction: *be-dorotav, kol she-ken be-dorot acherim* — if he could be a *tzaddik* in that generation, how much more so in others. His ability to resist the overwhelming evil of his environment is evidence of a powerful inner core; had he lived in a more righteous world, that core would have been nurtured to still greater heights.

### Wine Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: מָשָׁל דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לְחָבִית שֶׁל יַיִן שֶׁהָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת בְּמַרְתֵּף שֶׁל חוֹמֶץ. בִּמְקוֹמָהּ – רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף, שֶׁלֹּא בִּמְקוֹמָהּ – אֵין רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף. אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: מָשָׁל דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, לְמָה הַדָּבָר דּוֹמֶה? לִצְלוֹחִית שֶׁל פִּלְיָיטוֹן שֶׁהָיְתָה מוּנַּחַת בִּמְקוֹם הַטִּנּוּפֶת. בִּמְקוֹמָהּ – רֵיחָהּ נוֹדֵף, וְכׇל שֶׁכֵּן בִּמְקוֹם הַבּוֹסֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** There is **a parable** for the statement **of Rabbi Yoḥanan; to what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a barrel of wine that was placed in a cellar** where **vinegar** is stored; **in its place, its fragrance diffuses,** i.e., is noticeable, relative to the odor of the vinegar. When it is **not in its place** surrounded by vinegar, **its fragrance does not diffuse,** and its pleasant odor is not sensed. **Rabbi Oshaya says:** There is **a parable** for the statement **of Reish Lakish; to what is this matter comparable?** It is comparable **to a flask of perfume [*palyaton*] that was placed in a location of filth. In its place its fragrance diffuses** despite the ambient odor, **and all the more so** is its fragrance noticeable if it is placed **in a location** where there is **perfume.**

Rav Chanina illustrates Rabbi Yochanan's view with a *mashal*: a barrel of wine placed in a cellar full of vinegar — *be-mikomo reicho nodef*, in its place the wine's fragrance is noticeable and striking, because it rises above the pervasive smell of vinegar. *Shelo be-mikomo ein reicho nodef* — not in its place, in a cellar of other fine wines, it would be unremarkable. The imagery is carefully chosen: everyone agrees that Noah resisted the vinegar of his generation. But Rabbi Yochanan's point is that the resistance itself does not establish absolute greatness. The wine is still only wine; surrounded by vinegar it seems remarkable, but placed among other fine wines it is merely ordinary. There is also a darker implication: prolonged exposure to vinegar accelerates the wine's own turning; Noah's sustained immersion in his generation's evil was not without cost.

Rabbi Oshaya offers the counter-*mashal* for Resh Lakish's view: a small flask of *palyaton* perfume placed in a filthy, malodorous place. *Be-mikomo reicho nodef* — in that smelly setting, the perfume's fragrance is noticeable; it cuts through the stench. *Kol she-ken be-mekom ha-besamim* — how much more would its true fragrance emerge in a setting of spices and other fine aromas. Here the emphasis falls differently: because of the overpowering bad smell surrounding the perfume, the full power of the scent cannot be appreciated. You smell it, but you do not smell it at its potential best. In a setting of other beautiful fragrances, the full range of its quality would emerge. A personal note has been offered on Resh Lakish's framing — that someone who himself came from a difficult background, as Resh Lakish did, might be particularly attuned to what it means to be good in a bad environment, and to the question of what he might have become had his upbringing been different.

## Flood Narrative

### Animals Mashal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיִּמַח אֶת כׇּל הַיְקוּם אֲשֶׁר עַל פְּנֵי הָאֲדָמָה״ – אִם אָדָם חָטָא, בְּהֵמָה מָה חָטְאָה? תָּנָא מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: מָשָׁל לְאָדָם שֶׁעָשָׂה חוּפָּה לִבְנוֹ, וְהִתְקִין מִכׇּל מִינֵי סְעוּדָה. לְיָמִים מֵת בְּנוֹ. עָמַד וּבִלְבֵּל אֶת חוּפָּתוֹ. אָמַר: כְּלוּם עָשִׂיתִי אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל בְּנִי? עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁמֵּת, חוּפָּה לָמָּה לִי? אַף הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא אָמַר: כְּלוּם בָּרָאתִי בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה אֶלָּא בִּשְׁבִיל אָדָם? עַכְשָׁיו שֶׁאָדָם חוֹטֵא, בְּהֵמָה וְחַיָּה לָמָּה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And He obliterated every living thing that was upon the face of the ground,** from man to cattle to creeping animal to the birds of the heaven" (Genesis 7:23), the Gemara asks: **If man sinned,** in **what** way **did** the **animal** kingdom **sin** that it, too, warranted destruction? The Gemara answers: It was **taught in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa:** There is **a parable** for this matter, **to a man who fashioned a wedding canopy for his son and prepared all sorts of** food for the wedding **feast. Sometime** later, before the wedding, **his son died.** What did the man do? **He arose and dismantled his** son's **wedding canopy. He said: Did I do** this for any reason **other than for my son? Now that** my son has **died, why do I** need **a wedding canopy? So too, the Holy One, Blessed be He, said: Did I create domesticated animals and non-domesticated animals** for any reason **other than for man? Now that man sins** and is sentenced to destruction, **why do I** need **domesticated animals and non-domesticated animals?**

*Va-yimach et kol ha-yekum asher al pnei ha-adamah* — the question is raised: *im adam chata, behemah mah chat'ah* — if the humans sinned, what did the animals sin? The answer is given in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Karcha through a *mashal*: a man who prepared a wedding feast for his son, arranging every variety of delicacy, and then his son died before the wedding. He arose and scattered all the food, tore down the wedding canopy. *Amar: klum asiti ela bi-shvil beni, akhshav she-meit, chuppah lamah li?* — I made all of this for my son's sake; now that he is dead, what do I need the wedding feast for? So too, *Hakadosh Baruch Hu* said: I created domesticated animals and wild animals only for the sake of human beings. If I am going to destroy human beings, I have no need for any of it. *Akhshav she-adam choteh, behemah ve-hayah lamah li?*

### Fish Spared

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״מִכֹּל אֲשֶׁר בֶּחָרָבָה מֵתוּ״, וְלֹא דָּגִים שֶׁבַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** It is written: **"Of all that was on dry land died"** (Genesis 7:22), from which it is inferred: **But not the fish that are in the sea,** which are not on dry land.

*Mi-kol asher be-charavah meitu* — everything on the dry land died, *velo dagim she-ba-yam* — but not the fish of the sea. The fish were spared because they did not participate in the unnatural reproduction that characterized the generation's sin. Fish reproduce through external spawning rather than direct copulation, and so the charge of *hish-hit kol basar et darko* did not apply to them.

### Noah Warns

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי דְּמִן קֵסָרִי: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״קַל הוּא עַל פְּנֵי מַיִם תְּקֻלַּל חֶלְקָתָם בָּאָרֶץ״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה נֹחַ הַצַּדִּיק מוֹכִיחַ בָּהֶם וְאוֹמֵר לָהֶם: עֲשׂוּ תְּשׁוּבָה, וְאִם לָאו הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵבִיא עֲלֵיכֶם אֶת הַמַּבּוּל וּמַקְפֶּה נִבְלַתְכֶם עַל הַמַּיִם כְּזִיקִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״קַל הוּא עַל פְּנֵי מַיִם״. וְלֹא עוֹד, אֶלָּא שֶׁלּוֹקְחִין מֵהֶם קְלָלָה לְכׇל בָּאֵי עוֹלָם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״תְּקֻלַּל חֶלְקָתָם בָּאָרֶץ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei from Caesarea taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "He is swift upon the face of the waters; their portion is cursed in the earth;** he turns not by way of the vineyards" (Job 24:18)? This **teaches that Noah the righteous would rebuke** the people of his generation **and say to them: Repent. And if** you do **not, the Holy One, Blessed be He, will bring a flood upon you and float your corpses on the water like wineskins** filled with air that float on water, **as it is stated: "He is swift upon the face of the waters." Moreover, a curse is taken from them to all who enter the world,** as people will curse others, saying: They shall be like the generation of the flood. **As it is stated: "Their portion is cursed in the earth."**

*Darash Rabbi Yosi de-min Keisari*: the verse *kal hu al pnei mayim, tukulal chelkatam ba-aretz* is read to teach that Noah the righteous was warning the generation and telling them: *asu teshuvah, asu teshuvah* — repent, repent — *ve-im lav, HaKadosh Baruch Hu mevi aleikhem et ha-mabul, u-makfeh nivlatchem al ha-mayim ki-zikin* — and if not, God will bring the flood and your corpses will float on the water like flasks, as it says *kalu al pnei ha-mayim*. Moreover, *kol ba'ei olam* will use you as a curse for all future generations: *tekulal chelkatam ba-aretz* — your portion will be a curse upon the earth.

### Mocking Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״לַפִּיד בּוּז לְעַשְׁתּוּת שַׁאֲנָן נָכוֹן לְמוֹעֲדֵי רָגֶל״? מְלַמֵּד שֶׁהָיָה נֹחַ הַצַּדִּיק מוֹכִיחַ אוֹתָם, וְאָמַר לָהֶם דְּבָרִים שֶׁהֵם קָשִׁים כְּלַפִּידִים, וְהָיוּ בּוֹזִים אוֹתוֹ. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: זָקֵן, תֵּיבָה זוֹ לָמָּה? אָמַר לָהֶם: הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מֵבִיא עֲלֵיכֶם אֶת הַמַּבּוּל. אָמְרוּ: מַבּוּל שֶׁל מָה? אִם מַבּוּל שֶׁל אֵשׁ, יֵשׁ לָנוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד וַעֲלִיתָה שְׁמָהּ. וְאִם שֶׁל מַיִם הוּא מֵבִיא, אִם מִן הָאָרֶץ הוּא מֵבִיא, יֵשׁ לָנוּ עֲשָׁשִׁיּוֹת שֶׁל בַּרְזֶל שֶׁאָנוּ מְחַפִּין בָּהֶם אֶת הָאָרֶץ. וְאִם מִן הַשָּׁמַיִם הוּא מֵבִיא, יֵשׁ לָנוּ דָּבָר וְעָקֵב שְׁמוֹ – וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: עִקֵּשׁ שְׁמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava taught: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "A contemptible torch [*lapid*] in the thought of him that is at ease, a thing ready for them whose foot slips"** (Job 12:5)? This **teaches that Noah the righteous would rebuke** the people of his generation, **and he said to them statements that are harsh as torches [*kelapidim*], and they would treat him with contempt. They said to him: Old man, why** are you building **this ark?** Noah **said to them: The Holy One, Blessed be He, is bringing a flood upon you. They said to him: A flood of what? If it is a flood of fire, we have another item and it is called *alita*,** and it is fireproof. **And if** it is a flood **of water** that **He brings, if He brings** the water **from the earth, we have iron plates with which we** can **plate the earth** to prevent the water from rising. **And if He brings** the water **from the heavens, we have an item and it is called *ekev*, and some say it is called *ikkesh*,** which will absorb the water.

Rava then *darshens*: *lapid buz le-ash-tut sha'anan, nachon le-mo'adei ragel* — a torch of contempt for those at ease, ready for the appointed time of the heel. The *Gemara* teaches that Noah would rebuke them with words as sharp as torches, *devarim she-hem kashim ke-lapidim*, and they would respond with contempt, *buz*. *Amru lo: pachach!* — they mocked him as a fool. *Zaken, teivah zu lama?* — hey, old man, what are you building this box for? He told them: God will bring the flood. *Mabul shel mah?* — what kind of flood?

The population had an answer ready for every scenario. If it is a flood of fire — *yesh lanu davar, ve-alita shemo* — we have something called *alita* that protects against fire. If it is a flood of water rising from the earth — *yesh lanu ashashiyot shel barzel she-anu mechafim bahem et ha-aretz* — we have iron plates with which we will cover the ground and stop the water from rising. And if it is rain from the heavens — *yesh lanu davar, ve-akash shemo* — we have something called *akash* that will serve as a dome of protection. They had engineered a response to every possible mode of flood.

### Between Heels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶם: הוּא מֵבִיא מִבֵּין עִקְּבֵי רַגְלֵיכֶם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נָכוֹן לְמוֹעֲדֵי רָגֶל״. תַּנְיָא: מֵימֵי הַמַּבּוּל קָשִׁים כְּשִׁכְבַת זֶרַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״נָכוֹן לְמוֹעֲדֵי רָגֶל״. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: בְּרוֹתְחִין קִלְקְלוּ בַּעֲבֵירָה, וּבְרוֹתְחִין נִידּוֹנוּ. כְּתִיב הָכָא: ״וַיָּשֹׁכּוּ הַמָּיִם״, וּכְתִיב הָתָם: ״וַחֲמַת הַמֶּלֶךְ שָׁכָכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Noah **said to them:** If He wishes **He will bring** the water **from between your feet** and you can do nothing to prevent it, **as it is stated: "For them whose foot slips." It is taught** in a *baraita*: **The waters of the flood were** as **hard** and thick **as semen, as it is stated: "For them whose foot slips";** foot is a euphemism. **Rav Ḥisda says: With hot** semen **they sinned, and with hot** water **they were punished.** As **it is written here,** at the conclusion of the flood: **"And the waters assuaged"** (Genesis 8:1), **and it is written there: "Then the king's wrath was assuaged"** (Esther 7:10). Just as the term "assuaged" there is referring to the heat of Ahasuerus's wrath, so too, "assuaged" with regard to the flood is referring to the heat of the waters.

God's response to all their preparations: *hu mevi mi-bein ikvei ragleikhem* — He can bring it from between your very heels. *Nachon le-mo'adei ragel* — ready at the appointed time of the heel. The *akev*, the heel, is also used as a metaphor for the genital area, which leads directly into the next passage about the nature of the flood waters.

## Flood Waters

### Hot Waters

*Mei ha-mabul kashim ke-shichvat zera* — the waters of the flood were as hot and dense as semen. The proof-text is *nachon le-mo'adei ragel*. This is a striking statement that connects the mode of judgment directly to the mode of sin: a generation that sinned sexually is judged by waters that carry the character of sexual fluids.

### Rotchin Measure

Rav Chisda articulates the principle of measure for measure: *be-rotchin kilkelu, be-rotchin nidunu* — they sinned with hot liquid and were judged with hot liquid. Semen as a hot liquid used for procreation — the very gift of creation — was perverted through the sexual sins of the generation, and the waters of judgment came in the same mode. It is important to note that the Gemara here is describing the sin of perverted procreation — crossbreeding with animals, the corruption of *kol basar* — rather than the sin of *shichvas zera levatala*. The later poskim who invoke this passage in the context of masturbation are drawing on it in a secondary application; the Gemara's own context concerns something different.

### Verbal Echo

A final verbal correspondence: *khtiv hacha, va-yashoku ha-mayim* — and the waters abated; *khtiv hatam, ve-chamat ha-melekh shakhakhah* — and the king's anger abated. The word *shakhakh* connects the subsiding of the flood to the subsiding of divine wrath, a verbal echo that runs through the narrative of judgment and its conclusion.

## Seven Days

### Metushelach Mourning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וַיְהִי לְשִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים וּמֵי הַמַּבּוּל הָיוּ עַל הָאָרֶץ״, מָה טִיבָם שֶׁל שִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים? אָמַר רַב: אֵלּוּ יְמֵי אֲבֵילוּת שֶׁל מְתוּשֶׁלַח, לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁהֶסְפֵּדָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מְעַכְּבִין אֶת הַפּוּרְעָנוּת לָבֹא. דָּבָר אַחֵר: לְשִׁבְעַת [הַיָּמִים] שֶׁשִּׁינָּה עֲלֵיהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא סֵדֶר בְּרֵאשִׁית, שֶׁהָיְתָה חַמָּה יוֹצֵאת מִמַּעֲרָב וְשׁוֹקַעַת בַּמִּזְרָח. דָּבָר אַחֵר: שֶׁקָּבַע לָהֶם הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא זְמַן גָּדוֹל, וְאַחַר כָּךְ זְמַן קָטָן. דָּבָר אַחֵר: לְשִׁבְעַת הַיָּמִים שֶׁהִטְעִימָם מֵעֵין הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא, כְּדֵי שֶׁיֵּדְעוּ מָה טוֹבָה מָנְעוּ מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the verse: **"And it came to pass that after seven days the waters of the flood were upon the earth"** (Genesis 7:10), the Gemara asks: **What is the nature of** these **seven** additional **days?** **Rav says: These were the days of mourning** for the death **of Methuselah;** and this is **to teach you that eulogies for the righteous prevent calamities from ensuing. Alternatively, "after seven days"** means **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, altered the order of Creation for** that generation, i.e., in seven days He reversed the process of Creation, so **that the sun would emerge in the west and set in the east. Alternatively,** it means **that the Holy One, Blessed be He, designated a substantial period for them,** one hundred and twenty years, to repent, **and thereafter** designated **a brief period** for them, an additional seven days, as a final opportunity for them to repent. **Alternatively, "after seven days"** means **that** during those seven days, God **gave them a foretaste** of the delights **of the World-to-Come,** which will be actualized during the seventh millennium, **so that they would know what munificence** their sins **prevented them from** receiving.

*Va-yehi li-shvat ha-yamim u-mei ha-mabul hayu al ha-aretz* — and after seven days, the waters of the flood were upon the earth. Why did God wait seven days from the decree to the execution? Rav offers the first explanation: *eilu yemei evlo shel Metushelach* — these were the days of mourning for Metushelach. The chronology of the Torah confirms that Metushelach died precisely in the year of the flood. The Gemara understands that God delayed the *mabul* to allow the mourning period for Metushelach to be observed — *le-lamedcha she-hespadan shel tzaddikim me'akvin et ha-pur'anut lavo* — to teach you that eulogizing the righteous holds back the arrival of punishment. Even when the decree has been sealed, the honor due to a *tzaddik* creates a pause in the execution of divine judgment.

### Creation Reversal

*Davar acher*: the seven days reverse and echo the seven days of creation. The *Noach* narrative is structured as an anti-creation story: where creation separates the waters from the dry land, the flood re-merges them; where creation establishes categories and boundaries, the flood dissolves them. The seven days of waiting are a mirror image of the seven days of creation — *she-shinah lahem HaKadosh Baruch Hu sidrei Bereishit* — God reversed the order of creation. When Noah and his family emerge from the *teivah*, God says *peru u-revu*, echoing the blessing of creation; the flood is the undoing and the renewed creation alike.

### 120 to 7

*Davar acher*: *she-kava lahem HaKadosh Baruch Hu zman gadol ve-achar kach zman katan* — God had given them a long deadline and then a short one. The 120 years of Noah's building the *teivah* constituted the long grace period. When that expired, God gave them seven more days — a shorter, final window. There is a certain psychological truth here: the long deadline recedes into abstraction, while the short, urgent one focuses the mind. A week to do *teshuvah* — that is pressing. That is something one might actually act upon.

### Taste of Olam HaBa

*Davar acher*: *she-hit'imam me'ein olam haba* — God gave them a taste of *olam haba*. The seven days, associated with *Shabbat*, offer a foretaste of the world to come. They were given this experience *kedei she-yedu mah tovah manu mei-hem* — so that they should know what goodness they had withheld from themselves through their sins. The extraordinary abundance they had experienced in their earthly lives, and used to reject God, was now given a final, spiritual dimension — a glimpse of what could have been theirs — before the flood came.
