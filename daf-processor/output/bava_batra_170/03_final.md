# Bava Batra 170 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Otiyot Niknas Bimesira

### Rabban Shimon on Doc Ret…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַנּוֹתֵן מַתָּנָה לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְהֶחְזִיר לוֹ אֶת הַשְּׁטָר – חָזְרָה מַתְּנָתוֹ. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַתְּנָתוֹ קַיֶּימֶת. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? אָמַר רַב אַסִּי, נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר לוֹ: שָׂדֶה זוֹ נְתוּנָה לְךָ כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁהַשְּׁטָר בְּיָדְךָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** In the case of **one who gives a gift** of land **to another, and** the recipient **returned the deed to him, his gift** of land **returns** to him as well. **But the Rabbis say: His gift** of land **remains** in the possession of the recipient. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for the opinion **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? Rav Asi says: It becomes as** if the giver **said to** the recipient: **This field is given to you** only **as long as the deed** remains **in your possession.** Therefore, by returning the deed to the giver, the recipient is canceling the gift.

The sugya opens by revisiting a dispute from the preceding amud (169b) concerning whether the return of a sale document to the original seller reverses the transaction. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that it does, while the Rabbis disagree. The *gemara* now locates the source of that disagreement in a broader, foundational principle.

### Letters Acquired by Tran…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבָּה: בְּאוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי – רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabba said** a different explanation: Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and the Rabbis **disagree about** whether or not the **letters,** i.e., the contents of a promissory note, **are acquired by** merely **transferring** the document from one holder of the document to another. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that the **letters are acquired by transferring** the document. Therefore, if the recipient returns the bill of sale to the giver, the land returns to the giver as well. **And the Rabbis hold** that the **letters are not acquired by transferring** the document.

*Rabbah* identifies the crux of the dispute: *ba'otiyot niknas bimesira kamipluki* — the debate turns on whether documents are acquired, and their legal content transferred, by the mere act of physical handover. The classic illustration of this principle concerns a loan document: if Reuven holds an IOU establishing that Gloria owes him a thousand dollars, and he hands that document to David with the intent of transferring the debt, does David thereby become the new creditor? According to the position that *otiyot niknas bimesira* — letters are acquired by transfer — the answer is yes; the physical act of handing over the document accomplishes the legal conveyance, even though the document names a different obligee.

Here the *gemara* applies this principle not to a loan document but to a sale document. Suppose a deed records that in a given year Gloria sold her plot of land to Reuven, and ten years later Reuven hands that very document to David with the intent of conveying his interest. Even though the document names Gloria and Reuven, the act of transfer, according to those who hold *otiyot niknas bimesira*, accomplishes a new *kinyan*. This is a novel mode of acquisition: we are familiar with land being acquired through a *shtar* written in the buyer's name, but this would be a *kinyan* effected by the transfer of someone else's deed.

### Land Sale Application

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that *otiyot niknas bimesira* is operative — documents can be transferred by handover — and therefore, handing the sale deed back to the original seller functions precisely as a conveyance to a third party would: it transfers the land back. The Rabbis, by contrast, hold *ein otiyot niknas bimesira* — documents are not acquired by mere transfer. Consequently, returning the deed accomplishes nothing; to reverse the sale one would need either a new *shtar* or some other act of *kinyan*. Having established this framework, the *gemara* now turns to a second *baraita* to examine whether the same debate underlies it as well.

## Haba Lidon (Version 1)

### Shtar vs. Chazaka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הַבָּא לִידּוֹן בִּשְׁטָר וּבַחֲזָקָה, נִידּוֹן בִּשְׁטָר; דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: בַּחֲזָקָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **one who comes** to court **to be judged** over a claim that land that is in his possession belongs to another, if he claims ownership **based on a deed,** i.e., a bill of sale, **and** claims further that he is the owner **based on presumptive ownership** of the land, as it was in his uncontested possession for three years, and he therefore does not need the deed as proof, his claim is **judged based on** the **deed.** This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** His claim is judged **based on** his **presumptive ownership.**

The *baraita* states: *haba lidon bishtar uvachazaka* — one who comes to adjudicate by means of both a *shtar* and a *chazaka*. The *baraita* then records a dispute: *nidon bishtar* — Rabbi holds one must litigate by means of the *shtar* — while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *bechazaka* — one litigates by means of the *chazaka*.

The terms *shtar* and *chazaka* are notably ambiguous here, and the *gemara* will offer several distinct readings of the *baraita* before arriving at its preferred interpretation. The straightforward reading might be that the *shtar* serves as proof of ownership while the *chazaka* refers to *chazakat gimmel shanim*, the presumption of ownership arising from three years of undisturbed possession — and indeed that is ultimately the most natural reading of the *baraita*, which the *gemara* will reach at the end. However, most of the intermediate interpretations read *shtar* and *chazaka* not as modes of proof but as modes of *kinyan* — acts by which possession of the land is formally acquired. The case: both parties agree that a sale was contemplated, but the seller now disputes whether the sale was ever finalized. The buyer claims he already acquired the land. The question is: through what act did he acquire it — a *shtar* or a *chazaka* (meaning some act of physical possession, such as making a small improvement to the property)?

On this reading, the *baraita's* question is not which form of proof should be presented in court, but which act of acquisition is valid. And the difficulty is immediately apparent: why would either mode of acquisition not be sufficient? Each one ordinarily establishes ownership perfectly well.

### Rav Dimi Explains

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר: בְּאוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: אֵין אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה, וְרַבִּי סָבַר: אוֹתִיּוֹת נִקְנוֹת בִּמְסִירָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes the *baraita*: **With regard to what do** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel **disagree? When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia he **said: They disagree** about whether or not **letters are acquired by transferring** the document from one holder of the document to another. This is a case where one party sold land to another, who then sold it to a third party by transferring the bill of sale to him. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that the **letters are not acquired by transferring** possession of the document. Handing the bill to the third party is therefore not a valid act of acquisition. In order to establish that the land is his, one must prove presumptive ownership by demonstrating that he has lived in the land uncontested for three years. **And Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that the **letters are acquired by transferring** possession of the document. Therefore, if the third party produces the bill of sale that had been transferred to him, this is sufficient proof that the land is his.

When Rav Dimi arrived from Eretz Yisrael, he explained that Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in this *baraita* are likewise debating *otiyot niknas bimesira*. On this reading, the seller had originally purchased the land from a prior owner — call her Gloria — and received a deed. He now wishes to transfer the land to the buyer by handing over Gloria's original deed, which of course bears Gloria's name and the seller's name, not the buyer's name. The question is: does the act of handing over this old deed — not written in the buyer's name — constitute a valid *kinyan*?

Rabbi says *nidon bishtar*: the old deed works, for *otiyot niknas bimesira* — the transfer of the document itself accomplishes the acquisition. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *bechazaka*: handing over a deed not written in the buyer's name cannot effect a *kinyan*; the buyer must instead perform an act of physical *chazaka* to acquire the property.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אִם כֵּן, פְּלוּגְתָּא לִדְמָר! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְתִפְלוֹג!
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** objected and **said to** Rav Dimi: **If that is so,** that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that letters are not acquired by transferring possession of the bill of sale, this would be in **disagreement with** what **the Master,** Rabba, said earlier, that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel maintains that letters are acquired by transferring possession of the bill of sale. Rav Dimi **said** back **to him: And let it be in disagreement.** I am not obligated to be in agreement with Rabba.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ: מַתְנִיתָא לָא מִיתָּרְצָא אֶלָּא כְּדִמְתָרְצָא מָר, וְאִם כֵּן קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אַדְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye clarified his objection and **said to him: This is what I** meant to **say to you. The *baraita*** (168b), which Rabba was explaining, **can be explained only** the way the **Master,** Rabba, **explained** it. **And if so,** i.e., if you do not agree with Rabba's understanding of the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, there is **a difficulty,** as there is a contradiction **between** one statement **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel and** the other statement **of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.**

Abaye raises a pointed objection: if Rav Dimi's reading of the second *baraita* is correct, then the positions of Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel are reversed between the two *baraitot*. In the first *baraita*, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel held that *otiyot niknas bimesira* — handing back the deed reverses the sale. Here, on Rav Dimi's reading, it is Rabbi who holds *otiyot niknas bimesira* while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires a *chazaka* instead. The positions appear to have flipped.

Rav Dimi — or Rabbah — concedes the difficulty. *V'tisplog* — fine, this presents a problem for that interpretation. The point, Abaye clarifies, is that once Rabbah's reading of the first *baraita* is accepted (namely, that Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's position there rests on *otiyot niknas bimesira*), one cannot simultaneously use Rav Dimi's reading of the second *baraita* without generating a contradiction within Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's own rulings. Something must give — either the reading of the first *baraita* or the reading of the second. Abaye therefore sets out to offer a different interpretation of the second *baraita* entirely.

## Pasul Ed Interpretation

### Flawed Witness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּמְצָא אֶחָד מֵהֶן קָרוֹב אוֹ פָּסוּל;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the explanation of the *baraita* concerning one who appears before a court to be judged is as follows: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a case **where one of** the witnesses signed on the bill **was found to be a relative** to one of the parties, **or** he was found to be **disqualified** from bearing witness for another reason, and therefore the document is rendered invalid.

Abaye's first alternative reading: *kegon shenimtza echad meihem karov o pasul* — the case involves a new *shtar* written in the buyer's name, but one of the two witnesses who signed it turns out to be a relative or otherwise disqualified (*karov o pasul*). The debate between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel then concerns whether the buyer can nonetheless use the *shtar* to effect a *kinyan*, or whether the flaw in the signatures renders it unusable, leaving the *chazaka* as the only viable mode of acquisition.

### Edei Mesira/Chatima

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבִפְלוּגְתָּא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר קָא מִיפַּלְגִי – רַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי, וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye continues: **And** the two opinions in the *baraita* disagree **with regard to** the issue that is the subject of **the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: The witnesses of the transmission** of the document **effect** the transaction, meaning that witnesses are not required to be signed on a document at all, provided that there are witnesses who saw the transfer of the document to the relevant party. It is therefore inconsequential that one of the witnesses that signed the document is disqualified. This is why Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi says that the claim is judged based on the deed, i.e., the deed is valid. **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: The signatory witnesses** on the document **effect** the transaction, meaning that it is necessary for a document to have witnesses sign it. If there are no witnesses or if one or both of them are disqualified, the document is not valid even if its transmission is witnessed by qualified witnesses. This is why Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says that the claim is judged based on the claimant's presumptive ownership; this is his only valid proof since the deed he possesses is not valid.

The argument maps onto the well-known dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Elazar regarding *edei mesira* and *edei chatima*. Rabbi Elazar holds *edei mesirah karti* — it is the witnesses who observe the handover of the document who effect the legal act, not those who signed it. Rabbi Meir holds *edei chatimah karti* — the signatures on the document are what give it legal force.

On this reading, Rabbi in our *baraita* follows Rabbi Elazar: the *shtar* is valid for acquisition even with a disqualified signatory, because the witnesses who observed the delivery (*edei mesirah*) are the ones that matter. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel follows Rabbi Meir: only the signed witnesses (*edei chatimah*) are operative, and since one is disqualified, the *shtar* cannot be used; the buyer must resort to *chazaka*.

### Valid Handing Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: מוֹדֶה הָיָה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בִּמְזוּיָּיף מִתּוֹכוֹ, שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects to Abaye's explanation: **But doesn't Rabbi Abba say** that **Rabbi Elazar concedes** that **in** the case of a document whose **falsification is inherent in it that it is not valid?** Rabbi Elazar said only that a document that has no witnesses signed on it at all is valid if its transmission was witnessed. If the document has disqualified witnesses, he agrees it is not valid.

To be precise about the scenario: the witnesses who signed the document are invalid (one is *karov* or *pasul*), but the witnesses who observed the physical act of handing over the *shtar* are perfectly valid. The debate is thus a clean application of the *edei mesirah/edei chatimah* controversy. However, the *gemara* raises an immediate difficulty from a ruling of Rabbi Abba: *modeh hayah Rabbi Elazar bimzuyaf mitocho shepasul* — Rabbi Elazar himself concedes that if the document is internally corrupted — that is, if the flaw appears within the body of the document itself — even he would declare it invalid. A disqualified signatory, it seems, would constitute precisely such an internal flaw, leaving no one who would validate such a *shtar*. This second reading therefore cannot stand as presented.

## Ein Alav Edim

### No Witnesses Written

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבִינָא, הַכֹּל מוֹדִים שֶׁאִם כָּתוּב בּוֹ: ״הוּזְקַקְנוּ לְעֵדוּתָן שֶׁל עֵדִים, וְנִמְצֵאת עֵדוּתָן מְזוּיֶּיפֶת״ – שֶׁהוּא פָּסוּל, כִּדְרַבִּי אַבָּא; לֹא נֶחְלְקוּ אֶלָּא בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים כְּלָל, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי מְסִירָה כָּרְתִי. וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר, דְּאָמַר: עֵדֵי חֲתִימָה כָּרְתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rabbi Avina said** a modification of Abaye's explanation. **All concede that if it is written in** the document: **We,** the court, **engaged in** an investigation of **the testimony of the witnesses** and have determined that they signed the document, **and their testimony was found** to be **false,** the document **is not valid, in accordance with** the statement **of Rabbi Abba. They disagree only with regard to a document upon which there are no witnesses** signed **at all. As Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Elazar, who says: The witnesses of the transmission** of the document **effect** the transaction. Signatory witnesses are not necessary, and the deed can therefore be used as proof. **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who says: The signatory witnesses** on the document **effect** the transaction, so the deed cannot be used. Therefore, the only valid proof would be based on his presumptive ownership.

A third reading (*okimta*) is therefore offered. Rather than positing a *shtar* with a disqualified witness, we posit a *shtar* that bears no signed witnesses at all — *ein alav edim klal*. This cleanly avoids the problem of *mizuyaf mitocho*, since there is nothing internally wrong with the document; it simply lacks signatures entirely.

### Rabbi Abba Principle

The principle of Rabbi Abba — that Rabbi Elazar concedes in a case of *mizuyaf mitocho* — is thereby resolved. Where the document has a positive flaw (a disqualified signatory written into it), even Rabbi Elazar would invalidate it. But where the document simply has no witnesses at all, there is no internal corruption; the absence of signatures is a gap that *edei mesirah* can fill, according to Rabbi Elazar's position.

### Mesira vs. Chatima

On this final version of the reading, the *baraita* describes a *shtar* without any signed witnesses, and the debate is once again between *edei mesirah* and *edei chatimah*. Rabbi holds like Rabbi Elazar — *edei mesirah karti* — so the *shtar* is effective for *kinyan* by virtue of the witnesses who observed the handover, even with no signatures. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds like Rabbi Meir — *edei chatimah karti* — so without signed witnesses the *shtar* is inoperative, and the buyer must use a *chazaka* instead.

This gives us, in summary, three ways of explaining what the *shtar's* defect is, all within the frame of debating the *maaseh kinyan*: (1) it is an old *shtar* written in someone else's name, raising the question of *otiyot niknas bimesira*; (2) it has a disqualified signatory, which the *gemara* rejected because of Rabbi Abba's principle; and (3) it lacks signed witnesses entirely, which the *gemara* accepts as a clean application of *edei mesirah* versus *edei chatimah*. Still, the *gemara* notes that all of these readings frame the question as one of *maaseh kinyan* — does the *shtar* validly effect an acquisition? There remains a further and ultimately more natural reading in which the question is not about *kinyan* at all but about proof of title, which the *gemara* now introduces.

## Modeh Bashetar

### Validity Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּמוֹדֶה בַּשְּׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ. וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **And if you wish, say** instead: The case in the *baraita* is one where the alleged purchaser has a bill of sale for the land, but the alleged seller, although he admits that the document was written with his consent, claims that the sale did not ultimately occur, and that the alleged purchaser took the document from him. And the *tanna'im* in the *baraita* **disagree, with regard to** the *halakha* in the case of a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** whether the creditor **is required to ratify it** in court in order to collect, and, by extension, in a case where a seller admits that he wrote a bill of sale, whether the purchaser must ratify it in court in order to establish its validity. **As, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that in the case of a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order to collect what is owed to him. In this case as well, the purchaser can use the bill of sale as proof that he is the rightful owner of the property. **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that in the case of a debtor who admits that he wrote a promissory note, the creditor **is required to ratify it** in court in order to use it to collect the debt. In this case as well, since the purchaser is unable to ratify it, his only recourse to prove his ownership is showing that he lived in the land uncontested for three years.

The *gemara* introduces yet another way of reading the *baraita*, one that shifts the entire frame from the act of acquisition to the question of evidentiary proof. The scenario: the sale was completed, Michael is in possession of the land, but the original seller now disputes not the signatures themselves but the delivery — he claims he never handed the *shtar* over to Michael, that it fell from his pocket and Michael picked it up without any *kinyan* having been made. Michael, holding the *shtar*, insists the transaction was properly completed.

### Modeh Principle

Here the *gemara* invokes the principle of *modeh bashetar shekatavo eino tzarich lekaymo* — if the original party admits that the document was legitimately written (i.e., he does not claim forgery), the holder need not have the signatures validated in order to rely on it. The original party is effectively conceding the document's authenticity even while contesting the delivery. The logic is that *Chazal* required validation (*kiyum*) only when the document's authenticity was challenged; in the absence of such a challenge, a *shtar* bearing unchallenged signatures has legal force by virtue of those signatures alone.

One might ask why the holder cannot simply rely on a *migu* — the argument that since the original party could have denied the signatures entirely (and thereby defeated the claim outright), his more limited denial should be believed. The answer is that *migu* is beside the point: according to the principle of *modeh bashetar shekatavo eino tzarich lekaymo*, the *shtar* does not require validation in the first place when its authenticity is conceded. The *migu* the original party theoretically had — denying the signatures — was never exercised, and the document therefore stands as valid testimony that the land belongs to its holder.

On this reading, the *baraita's* dispute between Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel concerns not whether the *shtar* effects a *kinyan* but whether it suffices as proof of ownership: Rabbi holds *modeh bashetar shekatavo eino tzarich lekaymo* — the conceded *shtar* proves Michael's title without requiring further validation; Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds *tzarich lekaymo* — even when the original party admits the document was legitimately written, the holder must have the signatures formally validated before the *shtar* can serve as evidence.

### Document as Proof

A closer look at the mechanism reveals something important. The premise of the entire discussion is that Michael is holding a *shtar* that was signed by valid witnesses, those signatures have not been challenged, and the original party concedes both facts while claiming only that delivery never occurred. The holding of a *shtar* bearing valid, unchallenged signatures is, from the *halachic* standpoint, equivalent to those witnesses testifying in court to the facts stated in the document. The document testifies that Michael owns the field; the holder's possession of the *shtar* is the mechanism by which that testimony is placed before the court.

The complication — acknowledged openly — is that the signatures establish only that the document was signed, not that it was delivered. Delivery might have occurred a moment after signing or not at all. The *halachic* force of the *shtar*, however, is that its production in *beis din* by the person for whom it speaks is treated as the document's testimony to the reality it describes. *Edei mesirah*, when required, would close the factual gap regarding delivery; but even without *edei mesirah*, the *shtar* functions as evidence in the view of those who do not require it.

The principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav hare'ayah* operates here as well: Michael, holding a valid *shtar* whose authenticity is uncontested, stands in the position of the *muchzak*; the seller who wishes to dispossess him bears the burden of proof. The seller's claim that delivery never occurred is a contention against a document that is already testifying on Michael's behalf.

## Shnayim Achuzin

### Lender/Borrower Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ! דְּתַנְיָא: שְׁנַיִם אֲדוּקִין בִּשְׁטָר, מַלְוֶה אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁלִּי הוּא וְנָפַל מִמֶּנִּי וּמְצָאתוֹ״, וְלֹוֶה אוֹמֵר ״שֶׁלְּךָ הוּא וּפְרַעְתִּיו לָךְ״ – יִתְקַיֵּים הַשְּׁטָר בְּחוֹתְמָיו. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we hear the opposite concerning** Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel? **As it is taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta*, *Bava Metzia* 1:8) that if **two** people, the debtor and the creditor, **are grasping** a promissory **note,** and the **creditor says: It is mine,** i.e., the debt has not yet been paid, **and it fell from me, and you,** the debtor, **found it, and** the **debtor says: It is** in fact **yours,** i.e., I did borrow the money from you, **but I paid you** and was given the promissory note, and it fell from me, and you found it, **the** promissory **note must be ratified through its signatories,** even though the debtor admits it is a valid document. **This is the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: They should divide** the amount written in the promissory note, i.e., the debtor must pay half of the recorded sum to the creditor.

The *gemara* now cites a *baraita* that presents an apparent contradiction: *shnayim achuzin bashtar* — two people are holding onto the same *shtar* simultaneously. The lender says the document is his — he claims it is still active because the debt was never paid, that it fell from his possession and the borrower found it. The borrower says the document belongs to him as well — he concedes it was the lender's, but claims he paid the debt and the *shtar* fell from him after payment.

### Rabbi's Position

Rabbi rules *yitekayem hashtar bechosamav* — the signatures on the *shtar* must be validated. The implication is that once validated, the lender would be entitled to collect, or at minimum to some portion of the debt.

### Rabban Shimon Split

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules *yachloku* — they divide it. The scenario is analogous to the classic case of *shnayim achuzin bitallis*: each party swears he owns no less than half, and the disputed object is split. Here, the division means the borrower pays half the face value of the *shtar*.

The *gemara* asks: how can Rabbi's position make sense? Even after the signatures are validated, the borrower is holding one end of the *shtar*. Just as in the *tallis* case, should each party not be entitled to only half? Why does Rabbi prescribe validation as the remedy?

### Unvalidated Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רָבָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: בִּמְקוּיָּים – דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּיַחֲלוֹקוּ. כִּי פְּלִיגִי – בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מְקוּיָּים, רַבִּי סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְאִי מְקַיֵּים לֵיהּ – גָּבֵי פַּלְגָא, וְאִי לָא – חַסְפָּא בְּעָלְמָא הוּא; וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל סָבַר: מוֹדֶה בִּשְׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ – אֵין צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ, וְיַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in response to this question **Rava said** that **Rav Naḥman said: In** the case of **a ratified** promissory note, **everyone agrees that they should divide** it. **Where they disagree** is **in** the case of a note **that is not ratified. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that in the case of a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is required to ratify it** in court in order to collect the debt from him. **And if he** successfully **ratifies it,** the creditor **collects half** of the recorded sum, since the two parties are contesting ownership of the document. **And if** he is **not** successful in ratifying the note, it **is merely a shard,** i.e., a worthless piece of paper. **And Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds** that in the case of a debtor **who admits that he wrote** a promissory **note,** the creditor **is not required to ratify it** in court in order to collect payment. Since the note is valid and fit for collection, **they should divide** it, i.e., the creditor collects half the sum, as the ownership of the document is contested.

Rav Nachman resolves the difficulty by establishing the precise terms of the debate. When the *shtar* is already *mekuyam* — when the signatures have already been validated — there is no dispute: all agree the parties divide it, just as in the *tallis* case. The debate arises only *beshe'eino mekuyam*, when the *shtar* has not yet been validated.

In that situation, the borrower is claiming he paid the debt — that is, he concedes the *shtar* was legitimately written, but insists it is no longer operative. This is precisely the case of *modeh bashetar shekatavo*: the borrower admits the document's authenticity while contesting its current validity. Rabbi holds *tzarich lekaymo* — even with the borrower's admission that it was validly signed, the *shtar* is legally inert until formally validated. Therefore, until the lender obtains *kiyum*, he cannot collect even half — the *shtar* is *chaspa be'alma*, a mere shard of pottery. Once he does obtain *kiyum*, he collects half. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, by contrast, holds *eino tzarich lekaymo* — the borrower's concession that the document was validly written is sufficient to give it legal force, and so the *shtar* is already operative without formal validation, entitling the lender to half immediately.

This means that the positions of Rabbi and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in this *baraita* are the reverse of what was attributed to them in the reading of *haba lidon bishtar uvachazaka*: there, Rabbi was said to hold *eino tzarich lekaymo* (the *shtar* suffices), while Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel was said to require *kiyum*. Here it is Rabbi who requires *kiyum* and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel who says it is unnecessary. The *gemara* resolves this either by reversing (*eifoch*) one of the attributions, or by preferring the final reading of the *baraita* — to which the discussion now turns.

## Better Evidence Case

### Verbal Admission

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם לָא תֵּיפוֹךְ, אֶלָּא הָכָא בִּלְבָרֵר קָמִיפַּלְגִי –
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead: **Actually, do not reverse** their opinions. There is no contradiction between them, because the issue in the first *baraita* is not whether or not a bill of sale must be ratified when the seller admits that he wrote it. **Rather, here** the *tanna'im* **disagree with regard to verifying,** i.e., they disagree about whether a litigant is required to verify all his claims in circumstances where his case is strong enough to have the court rule in his favor even if they were to disregard some of his claims. Generally, if one has evidence of presumptive ownership of a field he does not have to produce a bill of sale for it. Here, Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi holds that since the one occupying the land claims that he has a bill of sale, he must verify this claim by producing it. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that he is not required to produce it, as the claim of presumptive ownership suffices.

The *gemara* arrives at what is in all likelihood the most natural reading of *haba lidon bishtar uvachazaka*. On this final interpretation, the *shtar* and *chazaka* are not alternative modes of *kinyan* but alternative modes of proof, and both are available to the litigant. The *chazaka* here refers to *chazakat gimmel shanim* — the presumption of ownership arising from three years of undisturbed possession. The *shtar* refers to an ownership deed. The question is: if a litigant claims to have both, must he produce the superior evidence (the *shtar*), or may he simply rely on the *chazaka*, which ordinarily suffices on its own?

Rabbi says *nidon bashtar* — once you have told the court that a *shtar* exists, you are required to produce it. *Chazaka* is in principle a substitute for a lost *shtar*, something one relies upon when the deed is unavailable. If you are claiming the deed exists, the court demands to see it. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says *bechazaka* — the *chazaka* is independently sufficient and you may always elect to litigate on that basis, regardless of whether a *shtar* also exists. The rule of *halacha* is like Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

The *gemara* illustrates the practical import of this debate with a case involving Rabbi Yitzchak bar Yosef, who had lent money to Rabbi Abba.

### Payment Claim

There was no *shtar* in this case — the loan had been made without a written document. Rabbi Abba admitted the loan but claimed he had already repaid it, specifying that the repayment occurred in the presence of Ploni and Almoni (two named witnesses).

### Witness Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּרַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף הֲוָה מַסֵּיק בֵּיהּ זוּזֵי בְּרַבִּי אַבָּא. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא, אָמַר: פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יָבוֹאוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְיָעִידוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִי לָא אָתוּ – לָא מְהֵימְנִינָא?! וְהָא קַיְימָא לַן: הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בְּעֵדִים – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לְפׇרְעוֹ בְּעֵדִים!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: It is **like this** case **where Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef was owed money by Rabbi Abba.** The case **came before Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa.** Rabbi Abba **said: I** already **repaid you** the debt **in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so. Rabbi Yitzḥak** Nappaḥa **said to** Rabbi Abba: The witnesses you have named, **so-and-so and so-and-so, must come and testify** that they saw you repay the loan. Rabbi Abba **said to** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa: **If they do not come, am I not deemed credible** to say that I repaid the loan? **But don't we maintain** that with regard to **one who lends** money **to another in** the presence of **witnesses,** the debtor **need not repay him in** the presence of **witnesses,** as he is deemed credible to say he repaid the debt even with no supporting testimony?

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha, acting as the *dayan*, responded: let Ploni and Almoni come and testify that the repayment took place.

### Loan Admission

Rabbi Abba objected: if they cannot come, am I not believed even without them? The established principle is *hamalveh chaveiro be'edim eino tzarich lefar'o be'edim* — one who lends money with witnesses present need not repay in front of witnesses. Rabbi Abba had borrowed without any *shtar*; his mere claim of repayment would ordinarily have been sufficient to close the case (subject perhaps to a rabbinic oath, *shvuat hesset*, but in principle fully credited). By volunteering the additional information that specific witnesses were present, he had provided more than the law required. Must he now be penalized for that candor?

### Witness Demand

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲנָא בְּהָהִיא – כִּשְׁמַעְתָּא דְמָר סְבִירָא לִי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה אָמַר רַב: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״פְּרַעְתִּיךָ בִּפְנֵי פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי״ – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיָּבוֹאוּ פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי וְיָעִידוּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa **said to** Rabbi Abba: **With regard to this** issue, **I hold like the *halakha*** said **by the Master,** i.e., you yourself. **As Rabbi Abba says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava said** that **Rav says:** If **one says to another,** i.e., if a debtor says to his creditor: **I repaid you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so,** it is **required that so-and-so and so-and-so come** to court **and testify** that they witnessed the repayment.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha replied pointedly: *ana beha'i shmeita demar sevira li* — I rule in accordance with the very principle that you yourself have established. For it is Rabbi Abba who transmitted in the name of Rav Adda bar Abba the ruling of Rav: *ha'omer lechaveiro peraticha bifnei ploni uploni, tzarich yavo ploni uploni veye'eidu* — one who tells his creditor "I paid you in the presence of Ploni and Almoni" must have Ploni and Almoni come and testify. Rabbi Abba had himself ruled this very case in theory; he was now its subject in practice.

Rabbi Abba then invoked the ruling of Rav Gidel in the name of Rav: *halacha kedivrei Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* — the law follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who holds (on this final reading of the *baraita*) that one may always rely on the sufficient evidence, even if better evidence exists. His simple claim of repayment suffices; he need not produce the named witnesses.

### Lechatchila Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* אָמַר לֵיהּ, הָכִי קָאָמֵינָא לָךְ: מַתְנִיתָא לָא מִיתָּרְצָא אֶלָּא כְּדִמְתָרְצָא מָר, וְאִם כֵּן קַשְׁיָא דְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אַדְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל! אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמוֹדֶה בַּשְּׁטָר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ צָרִיךְ לְקַיְּימוֹ קָא מִיפַּלְגִי –

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha responded: *halacha keRabban Shimon ben Gamliel ve'af Rebbi lo amar ela levarer* — the law indeed follows Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and furthermore, even Rabbi only ever stated his requirement *lechatchila* — as a matter of proper procedure. Rabbi's position, properly understood through the reading of *Tosafot*, is not that failure to produce the better evidence results in losing the case *bedieved*. Rather, when a litigant informs the court that superior evidence exists, the court will demand to see all available evidence before ruling. If ultimately it cannot be produced, the litigant still prevails on the basis of the sufficient evidence he does have.

Rabbi Yitzchak Nafcha then added: *ana nami levarer ka'amina* — that too was all I was saying. When I demanded that Ploni and Almoni come testify, I was not ruling that Rabbi Abba would lose if they failed to appear. I was demanding, as a matter of *lechatchila* procedure, that all available evidence be placed before the court. The demand to produce witnesses one has named is a preliminary requirement, not a condition for victory.

The *Rashbam* reads the exchange differently, holding that *bedieved* — if the named witnesses cannot be produced — the claim of repayment fails and the borrower loses. *Tosafot* disputes this, noting that the entire tone of the exchange suggests the demand is procedural only, and pointing to a further irony the *Rashbam* himself acknowledges: a different sugya rules that if one names witnesses to a repayment and they come to court but testify that they did not see the repayment, the borrower still prevails — on the reasoning that they may simply not have been paying attention at the time. The result, on the *Rashbam's* reading, would be paradoxical: if the witnesses don't come, the borrower loses; but if they come and deny witnessing the payment, the borrower wins. This difficulty strengthens the *Tosafot* reading that the witness demand is *lechatchila* only.

## Partial Payment Case

### Yachlif vs. Shovar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִי שֶׁפָּרַע מִקְצָת חוֹבוֹ, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: יַחְלִיף, רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: יִכְתּוֹב שׁוֹבָר. אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: נִמְצָא זֶה צָרִיךְ לִהְיוֹת שׁוֹמֵר שׁוֹבָרוֹ מִן הָעַכְבָּרִים! אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: כָּךְ יָפֶה לוֹ, וְלֹא יֵרַע כֹּחוֹ שֶׁל זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of a debtor **who repaid part of his debt, Rabbi Yehuda says:** The creditor **should exchange** the promissory note for a new one stating the current balance and tear up the first promissory note. **Rabbi Yosei says:** The creditor may keep the original promissory note, and he **should write a receipt** for the payment he has received and give it to the debtor as proof of his partial payment of the sum recorded in the old note. **Rabbi Yehuda said** with regard to this arrangement: It is **found** that **this** debtor **must** now **guard his receipt against** being destroyed by **mice,** as if he no longer has the receipt, he will have to pay the entire sum recorded in the promissory note. **Rabbi Yosei said to him: This** situation is **fitting for him;** it is better that this procedure be followed, **and the strength of** the claim of **this** creditor **not be weakened.**

The *gemara* transitions to a new *mishnah* dealing with partial repayment of a debt. When a borrower has repaid part of the principal, the parties must decide how to document that fact. Rabbi Yehuda says *yachlif* — replace the original document with a new one reflecting only the remaining balance. Rabbi Yosi says *yichtov shovar* — the lender should write the borrower a receipt for the amount paid, with the original *shtar* remaining in force for the full amount.

### Receipt Loss Risk

Rabbi Yehuda objects to the receipt solution: *nimtza zeh tzarich lihyot shomer shovaro min ha'achbarim* — the result is that the borrower is now required to guard his receipt against the mice, or face repaying the portion he has already paid. Rather than creating this burden, simply replace the thousand-dollar *shtar* with an eight-hundred-dollar *shtar* and eliminate the risk entirely. The borrower already had to guard his assets against the original *shtar*; creating a second document he must preserve introduces an additional and unnecessary risk of loss.

### Creditor Standing

Rabbi Yosi responds: *kach yafe lo* — let the borrower bear that responsibility; and *lo yeira kocho shel zeh* — we must not diminish the lender's standing. The substance of Rabbi Yosi's concern is that replacing a thousand-dollar *shtar* with an eight-hundred-dollar *shtar* causes the lender real harm — specifically, the loss of liens. A *shtar* creates a lien (*shi'abud*) on real property the borrower owned from the time the loan was made. If the original *shtar* is torn up and replaced with a new one dated today, the lender loses all the liens he had acquired over the intervening period. Property that changed hands since the original loan was made — which was subject to collection under the original *shtar* — would no longer be encumbered.

### Beis Din Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב הוּנָא אָמַר רַב: אֵין הֲלָכָה לֹא כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְלֹא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי; אֶלָּא בֵּית דִּין מְקָרְעִין הַשְּׁטָר, וְכוֹתְבִין לוֹ שְׁטָר אַחֵר מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Huna says** that **Rav says:** The ***halakha*** is **not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda, nor** is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei. Rather,** the *halakha* is that **the court tears up the** original promissory **note and writes a different** promissory **note for** the creditor, listing the new sum owed, dated **from the time** of the **first** document.

*Rav Huna* transmitting in the name of *Rav* rules: *ein halacha lo k'Rabbi Yehuda velo k'Rabbi Yosi* — we rule neither like Rabbi Yehuda nor like Rabbi Yosi. Instead, *beis din mekri'in hashtar*, the *beis din* tears up the original *shtar*, *vekotvim lo shtar acher mizman rishon* — and writes a new *shtar* for the reduced amount, but dated from the original date. This resolves both concerns: the borrower has no vulnerable receipt to guard (Rabbi Yehuda's concern is met), and the lender suffers no loss of lien because the new document bears the original date (Rabbi Yosi's concern is met). As *Tosafot* notes, the critical phrase in Rav's formulation is *mizman rishon* — from the original time.

### Edim Authority

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן לְרַב הוּנָא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא לְרַב הוּנָא: אִי שְׁמִיעָא לֵיהּ לְרַב הָא [בָּרַיְיתָא] דְּתַנְיָא: עֵדִים מְקָרְעִין אֶת הַשְּׁטָר, וְכוֹתְבִין לוֹ שְׁטָר אַחֵר מִזְּמַן רִאשׁוֹן – הֲוָה הָדַר בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁמִיעַ לֵיהּ, וְלָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said to Rav Huna, and some say** it was **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba** who said this **to Rav Huna: If Rav would have heard this** following ***baraita*, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that according to Rabbi Yehuda the proper procedure is that the **witnesses tear the** original promissory **note and write another** promissory **note for him,** dated **from the time** of the **first** note, **he would have retracted** his statement that the court tears the promissory note and writes a new one. Rav Huna **said to him:** Rav **heard** the *baraita*, **and** nevertheless **he did not retract** his statement. While Rabbi Yehuda says in the *baraita* that the witnesses write a new promissory note, Rav maintains that this must be done specifically by a court. Why is this?

Rav Nachman (and alternatively Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba) asks Rav Huna: had Rav heard the following *baraita*, would he have maintained his formulation? The *baraita* states: *eidim mekri'in et hashtar vekotvim lo shtar acher mizman rishon* — the witnesses tear up the *shtar* and write a new one from the original date. This *baraita* appears to express in the name of Rabbi Yehuda exactly what Rav said — that the replacement *shtar* is dated from the original time. Had Rav known of it, he might have simply said he was ruling like Rabbi Yehuda as clarified by this *baraita*, rather than departing from the *mishnah* entirely.

Rav Huna responds on Rav's behalf: Rav knew of the *baraita* and did not retract. The reason is that Rav's formulation contains an additional and decisive innovation — not merely the *mizman rishon* dating, but the insistence that this be done specifically by *beis din*, not by the witnesses themselves.

### Beis Din vs. Edim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא בֵּי דִינָא – אַלִּימֵי לְאַפְקוֹעֵי מָמוֹנָא. אֶלָּא עֵדִים שֶׁעָשׂוּ שְׁלִיחוּתָן – חוֹזְרִין וְעוֹשִׂין שְׁלִיחוּתָן?!
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, a court** can write a new document, because the court **has the power to confiscate money.** A creditor has the right to seize any property that the debtor had owned on the day the promissory note was written, even if that property was subsequently sold to others; the earlier the date on the note, the more properties it applies to. By dating the new document on the date of the original document the creditor is once again given the right to seize properties from those who had purchased land from the debtor in between the date of the original loan and the date the new promissory note is actually written. The court has such confiscatory power. **But** with regard to **witnesses, who** already **performed their agency,** i.e., fulfilled their assigned role, the first time they wrote the promissory note, can **they return and perform their agency** again by writing a second promissory note?

*Bishlama beis dina alimei l'afkuei mamona* — it is understandable that *beis din* has the authority to create new financial realities: the principle of *hefker beis din hefker* gives the court power to restructure property and monetary obligations in ways that would otherwise be impossible. A *beis din* can therefore issue a new *shtar* bearing an earlier date, creating liens retroactively, because it acts within its institutional authority to do so.

But the *baraita* attributes this power to the *eidim* — the witnesses themselves. *Ella eidim she'asu shelichutam chozrim ve'osim shelichutam?* — after the witnesses have already performed their function by signing the original *shtar*, how can they be authorized to come back and perform it again in a different form, creating a new document of eight hundred dollars dated from the original time? Once witnesses have signed a *shtar*, their role is discharged. To issue a new predated *shtar* requires the authority of *beis din*, not merely that of the original signatories.

This, then, is Rav's *chiddush*: not the *mizman rishon* dating — the *baraita* says that as well — but the requirement that this procedure be carried out specifically by *beis din*. The witnesses who signed the original *shtar* lack the institutional authority to create a retroactively dated replacement document; only *beis din*, acting under the principle of *hefker beis din hefker*, possesses that power. The *gemara* leaves this issue open for the subsequent discussion.
