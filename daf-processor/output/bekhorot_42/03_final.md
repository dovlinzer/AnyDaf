# Bekhorot 42 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Androgynos Status

### Intro: Gender Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵינוֹ בְּכוֹר כּוּ׳. אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחְלוֹקֶת בְּאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְטוּם — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל סְפֵיקָא הוּא, וְקָדוֹשׁ מִסְּפֵיקָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say:** A hermaphrodite **does not** have **firstborn** status; rather, its status is that of a non-sacred animal that may be shorn and utilized for labor. **Rav Ḥisda says:** This **dispute** applies **to a hermaphrodite,** which the Rabbis maintain is a distinct entity, as it has both male and female sex organs, and therefore it does not have firstborn status. **But with regard to a *tumtum*,** whose genitalia is covered by skin, **everyone agrees** that **it is an uncertain** case, as it is definitely either a male or a female, but there is uncertainty as to which it is. **And** consequently, it is **consecrated due to uncertainty,** and it may not be slaughtered either in the Temple or in the rest of the country.

The daf opens in the middle of a rich discussion about two categories of indeterminate gender: *androgynos*, an individual possessing both male and female genitalia, and *tumtum*, an individual whose genitalia are covered over by a layer of skin and therefore undetectable. These are among the most fascinating discussions in the tractate, touching on questions of gender identity that resonate well beyond their technical halachic context.

### Three Positions

By way of review, three theoretical positions exist regarding the status of an *androgynos*. The first is that it is *safek zachar safek nekeiva* — not that any individual case is unknowable in fact, but that by its very nature the *androgynos* occupies an intrinsically indeterminate space between male and female. The second possibility is that it is a *zachar* with a *mum*, a male with a blemish — since it possesses female genitalia in addition to male. The third possibility is that it constitutes a third gender altogether, a *beriyah bifnei atzmah*, a category unto itself.

It is worth noting that the *Gemara* does not seriously entertain the possibility that an *androgynos* is a female with a *mum*. This reflects the assumption, shared by much of ancient society, that the presence of male genitalia is definitionally masculine; femaleness is understood as the absence of male genitalia, not as a positive category that can be "blemished" by the presence of male anatomy.

### Rav Chisda Position

The *Gemara* then turns to *tumtum*. The Mishnah records that *Chachamim* say an *androgynos* is definitively not a *bechor*, because it is an entirely third type of gender. *Amar Rav Chisda*: the entire debate concerns *androgynos* alone. But *aval be-tumtum divrei hakol safek hu ve-kadosh misafek* — a *tumtum* is agreed by all to be a mere doubt. Unlike the *androgynos*, whose very anatomy is hybrid, the *tumtum* is simply an animal or person whose genitalia are hidden under skin. The doubt here is a doubt in fact — each individual *tumtum* is either male or female, we simply do not know which. Rav Chisda therefore holds that no one could seriously categorize a *tumtum* as its own gender. It is a *safek zachar safek nekeivah*, and the applicable *halachot* are those of any ordinary doubtful case: it is treated as a doubtful *bechor*, not given to a *Kohen*, and a *Yisrael* eats it when it acquires a *mum*.

## Erech Pasukim

### Rava Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, בַּעֲרָכִין יֵעָרֵךְ?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Ḥisda: **If that is so,** that a *tumtum* is certainly either a male or a female, **with regard to valuations,** when someone vows to give the valuation of a *tumtum*, **it should be valuated,** either in accordance with the valuation of a man or a woman.

*Amar lei Rava*: if that is true — if a *tumtum* is nothing more than a standard *safek* — then in the domain of *erchin*, when someone vows the *erech* of an individual, a *tumtum* should generate an obligation. The Torah's chart of valuations is keyed to gender and age; male and female carry different values. If a *tumtum* is simply one or the other and we do not know which, then presumably one should at least pay the male value out of *safek*. Why, then, does a *braita* at the top of 42a teach: *ha-zachar* — and *ve-lo tumtum ve-androgynos*? Both are excluded from the *parsha* of *erchin* entirely.

### Cross Out Tumtum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַלְּמָה תַּנְיָא: ״הַזָּכָר״ — וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס! סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Why,** then, **is** it **taught** in a *baraita* that the verse: "Then your valuation shall be for **the male"** (Leviticus 27:3), includes one whose status as a male is certain **but not a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite?** The Gemara answers: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*,** as it is referring only to a hermaphrodite, which is a distinct entity.

The *Gemara* responds: *sami mikan tumtum* — cross out the word *tumtum*. The *braita* lists *tumtum* and *androgynos* together, but that pairing is incidental; the *halacha* of exclusion from *erchin* really concerns only *androgynos*. The *Gemara* tries a second formulation as well. Perhaps one would think that while a *tumtum* or *androgynos* cannot be assigned the male *erech*, it should at least receive the female value, which is lower. The *Torah* responds by stressing *ha-zachar* and separately *ve-im nekeiva* — and *aval lo tumtum ve-androgynos*. Again, both are excluded. And again the *Gemara* says: *sami mikan tumtum* — cross it out. The *halacha* pertains to *androgynos* only.

### Male Value Default

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: יָכוֹל לֹא יְהֵא בְּעֵרֶךְ אִישׁ, אֲבָל יְהֵא בְּעֵרֶךְ אִשָּׁה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״הַזָּכָר... אִם נְקֵבָה״, וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס! סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** the continuation of that *baraita*: One **might** have thought that these **shall not be** valuated according to **the valuation of a man, but** they **shall be** valuated according to **the valuation of a woman.** Therefore, **the verse states: "The male,"** and in the following verse it states: "And **if** she is **a female,** then your valuation shall be thirty shekels" (Leviticus 27:4), indicating only one whose status as a male or female is certain, **but not a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite,** which are categorized as neither male nor female. The Gemara again answers: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*,** as it is referring only to a hermaphrodite.

This pattern — quoting *tumtum ve-androgynos* as a fixed pair and then erasing *tumtum* — recurs throughout the sugya, and it is genuinely troubling. *Tosafot* himself flags the problem. One might have said: even granting that a *tumtum* is a factual doubt, the *Torah* could legitimately assign it distinct *halachot* precisely because of the manner in which it is in doubt. This is not the same as saying it is a third gender. An individual who is biologically one or the other, but whose sex is impossible to determine in the present state — because determining it requires surgery that may have been considered dangerous — is in a situation that the *Torah* might well treat differently from a mere epistemic uncertainty. This is the concept of *ishtani*: since something about this individual is different, perhaps the *Torah* prescribes different *halachot* for them.

But Rav Chisda's position, as the *Gemara* understands it, is more radical than that. He is not merely saying the *tumtum* is a factual doubt; he is saying there would be no special *halachot* for this person at all, ever. A *tumtum* is treated identically to any other *safek* — no differently from a case where one simply did not know, from behind a curtain, whether an animal was male or female. Consequently, any *braita* that appears to categorically exclude a *tumtum* must be edited to remove the word. According to the *maskanah* where *tumtum* is crossed out, then: by *erchin*, one pays the male value out of *safek*, being *machmir* for the doubt. By *androgynos*, however, since the more plausible reading of this *braita* treats it as a *beriyah bifnei atzmah* — a third-category entity entirely — the *Torah*'s language of male and female simply does not encompass it, and one pays nothing.

## Shelamim Exclusion

### Shelamim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״אִם זָכָר אִם נְקֵבָה״ — זָכָר וַדַּאי נְקֵבָה וַדָּאִית, וְלֹא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita* discussing the peace offering, with regard to which it states: **"Whether male or female"** (Leviticus 3:1). This indicates: Only **a definite male** or **a definite female, but not a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite.** Yet again the Gemara responds: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*.**

The *Gemara* continues with another attempted proof. The *pasuk* governing *shelamim* sacrifices states *im zachar im nekeivah* — whether male or whether female, one may bring it. The *braita* derives: *zachar vadai nekeivah vadai* — only a definitively male or definitively female animal qualifies. *Ve-lo tumtum ve-androgynos* — neither may be brought as a *shelamim*, even though either gender is individually acceptable.

This appears to be a strong proof that *tumtum* is not a *safek*. If it truly were merely a doubt between male and female — and either male or female may be brought as a *shelamim* — then one should be able to bring a *tumtum* as a *shelamim*, since it is certainly one or the other. Yet it is excluded. That exclusion implies something more than mere doubt.

### Second Crossing Out

The *Gemara* responds predictably: *sami mikan tumtum* — erase it. According to Rav Chisda, a *tumtum* may in fact be brought as a *shelamim*.

### Categorical vs. Safek

The force of excluding *androgynos* from *shelamim* lies in the *derasha*: *zachar vadai, nekeivah vadai* — the *Torah* demands a clear, known identity at the moment of *hekdesh*. One could admit that an *androgynos* is one or the other in some abstract sense, and still maintain that the *Torah* requires the owner to *know* which one it is at the time of the dedication. That is a coherent categorical exclusion independent of the *mum* framework.

This also clarifies why *androgynos* is more categorically excluded than a *ba'al mum*. If an animal had a blemish and was dedicated as a *korban*, the dedication takes effect — the animal becomes *kadosh* — even though it cannot be sacrificed. The category of *androgynos*, however, by contrast, is so removed from the valid categories that *kedushah* does not fall upon it even in a standard case of *temurah*. As Michael noted, this confirms that we are not dealing merely with a *mum*; we are dealing with something categorically excluded. And there is a further distinction: an *androgynos* might in some cases have its covered-over portion exposed, unlike a permanent irreversible *mum kavua*, which adds complexity to the *mum* analysis. Ultimately the *Gemara* accepts that for *shelamim*, the exclusion of *androgynos* is categorical, and the exclusion of *tumtum* — per Rav Chisda — is erased.

## Bird Sacrifices

### Neveilah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״הַזָּכָר״ — וְלֹא נְקֵבָה. כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר לְמַטָּהּ ״זָכָר״, שֶׁאֵין תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר; מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר? לְהוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a *baraita*: It states with regard to a burnt offering from cattle: **"A male"** (Leviticus 1:3), from which it can be inferred: **But not a female. When it says below,** with regard to a burnt offering from sheep: **"A male"** (Leviticus 1:10), a second time, it is difficult to understand, **as** there is **no** need for **the verse to state** this. **Why** must **the verse state** "a male" again? This serves **to exclude a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite,** which are disqualified as burnt offerings. The Gemara answers: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*.**

The *Gemara* adduces another *braita* regarding bird sacrifices. When a bird is brought as a *korban*, the act of *melikah* — severing the neck from the back — is what renders it fit. If the bird were categorically unfit as a *korban*, then the *melikah* would not constitute a valid act of *shechitah*, and the bird would be *neveilah*. Consuming *neveilah* renders one's garments *tamei*. The *braita* lists the categories of birds whose *melikah* produces *neveilah* because they cannot be valid *korbanot*: an animal that was used for bestiality (*haneer'va*), one that was set aside for *avodah zarah* (*hamuktzeh*), one given as payment to a *zonah* (*esnan*), one exchanged for a dog (*mechir kelev*) — and *tumtum ve-androgynos*.

### Rabbi Eliezer Birds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס אֵין מְטַמְּאִין בְּגָדִים אַבֵּית הַבְּלִיעָה, שֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: כׇּל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה״ אַתָּה מוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס מִבֵּינֵיהֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says:** If one pinched the neck of **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite, it does not render** the person and **the garments** he is wearing **ritually impure** when they are **in the throat,** as they are in fact fit to be sacrificed. **As Rabbi Eliezer would say: Wherever it is stated** explicitly in the Torah: **"Male," and: "Female," you** should **exclude a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite from among them,** as their status is uncertain. This applies to animal offerings, with regard to which the Torah states "male" and "female."

What makes this case particularly striking is that the *Torah* does not mention gender at all with respect to bird sacrifices. Regarding *shelamim*, the exclusion was derived from the phrase *im zachar im nekeivah* — and where the *Torah* specifies both genders, one can exclude that which fits neither. But birds are never characterized by gender in the *parsha* of *korbanot*. Why, then, should a *tumtum* or *androgynos* bird be excluded?

*Rabbi Eliezer* addresses this directly: *kol makom shene'emar zachar u-nekeivah, ata motzi tumtum ve-androgynos* — wherever the *Torah* says male or female, fine, then you exclude them. That is the *shelamim* case. *Ho'il ve-lo ne'emar bo zachar u-nekeivah, ein ata motzi tumtum ve-androgynos mibeinehen* — but since the *Torah* never mentions gender for birds, you cannot exclude them. Rabbi Eliezer therefore holds that an *androgynos* or *tumtum* bird may actually be brought as a valid *korban*. This is a very reasonable position. Notably, *Tosafot* adds that Rabbi Eliezer is not merely saying such a bird avoids *neveilah* status; he would hold it is actually a *kasher korban*.

According to the *tanna kamma*, however, even birds without gender specification are affected — *tumtum* and *androgynos* are excluded. For *shelamim*, the question is whether the *tumtum* fits one of the specified categories; for birds, the question is why gender matters at all when it goes unmentioned. In either case, the implicit message is that *tumtum* carries some independent ground for exclusion beyond a simple *safek*.

### Third Crossing Out

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹף, הוֹאִיל וְלֹא נֶאֱמַר בּוֹ ״זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה״, אֵין אַתָּה מוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס מִבֵּינֵיהֶם! סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **But** in the case of **a bird** offering, **since "male" and "female" are not stated with regard to it,** but simply doves and pigeons are stated with regard to it, **you do not exclude a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite from among them,** as they are fit for the altar. Now if a *tumtum* is considered either definitely a male or a female, then why does the first *tanna* disqualify it from being sacrificed? The Gemara again replies: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*.**

The *Gemara* again responds: *sami mikan tumtum*. Erase it. A *tumtum* bird may, according to Rav Chisda, be brought as a *korban* or at least cannot be categorically excluded on the basis of gender indeterminacy. The exclusion applies only to *androgynos*. One might note, as was observed during the shiur, that in the world of *korbanot* there exist independent grounds for exclusion — much as *neveilah*, *muktzeh*, and the others on the list are excluded not because of gender but because of their distinct intrinsic *psul*. One could have said that *androgynos* and *tumtum* are excluded on the same independent ground — their physical reality, whatever one calls it — without needing to say it is a gender category. But the *Gemara* follows Rav Chisda's extreme position and refuses to allow even that reasoning for *tumtum*.

## Temurah Status

### Five Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הַטְּרֵיפָה, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס — לֹא קְדוֹשִׁים וְלֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** that **Rabbi Elazar says: An animal with a condition that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*], and** prohibited **diverse kinds** of livestock, **and** an animal **born by caesarean section,** and an animal that is **a *tumtum* or a hermaphrodite do not become consecrated and do not render** another animal **consecrated** in their place.

The *Gemara* now turns to *temurah*, the attempted exchange of sanctity from a *korban* to a non-sanctified animal. Although doing so violates a *lav*, the *temurah* takes effect, and the second animal acquires *kedushah* — provided it is not categorically excluded. A *ba'al mum* acquires *kedushah* even through *temurah*. But a categorically excluded animal does not. The *braita* records: *Rabbi Eliezer omer, ha-treifah ve-ha-kilayim ve-yotzei dofen tumtum ve-androgynos lo kedoshim ve-lo makdishim* — they do not become *kadosh* through *temurah* and cannot sanctify others through *temurah*.

### Kedusha Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא קְדוֹשִׁים בִּתְמוּרָה, וְלֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין לַעֲשׂוֹת תְּמוּרָה! סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel says** in explanation: **They do not become consecrated by substitution,** i.e., if one sought to substitute one of these animals for a sacrificial animal, it does not become sacred. **And** if one of these was born to a sacrificial animal, **they do not render** a non-sacred animal **consecrated** in a case where one wanted **to render** it **a substitute** for them. And if a *tumtum* is either a male or female, why would it not become consecrated and render another animal consecrated? The Gemara answers: **Omit from** this *baraita* the mention of **a *tumtum*.**

*Ve-amar Shmuel*: *lo kedoshim bi-temurah ve-lo makdishim la'asot temurah* — to clarify: they do not acquire *kedushah* when *temurah* is attempted upon them, and if they somehow did possess *kedushah* (for instance, having been born from a *korban* mother), they cannot transmit that *kedushah* through *temurah* to something else. This double exclusion — *lo kedoshim, lo makdishim* — underscores the categorical nature of the disqualification. It is not a *mum*; it is a complete removal from the domain of *kedushah* as applied through *temurah*.

The *Gemara* here makes the conceptual point explicit. When an animal has a *mum*, it is still potentially within the world of *korbanot* — one could redeem it, the *kedushah* has effect, and *temurah* upon it works. But when an animal is categorically excluded — *treifah*, *kilayim*, *yotzei dofen*, or *androgynos* — the relationship to *kedushah* is entirely severed in this context. The *Gemara* adds that even if such an animal did acquire *kedushah* through birth from a *korban* mother, it still cannot propagate that *kedushah* through *temurah*. The mechanism of *temurah* simply does not operate upon categorically excluded animals.

And once again, *tumtum* appears on the list. And once again, this is plainly inconsistent with Rav Chisda's position. A *safek* animal that has a *mum* does not lose the ability to receive *kedushah* through *temurah*; it would be treated as any other *safek*. So *tumtum* cannot belong on this categorical list. The *Gemara* says: *sami mikan tumtum* — erase it. Cross out *tumtum* here as well.

### Text Emendation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: חֲמִשָּׁה לֹא קְדוֹשִׁים וְלֹא מַקְדִּישִׁין, וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: הַטְּרֵיפָה, וְהַכִּלְאַיִם, וְיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן, וְטוּמְטוּם, וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס. וְכִי תֵּימָא הָכָא נָמֵי סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם — הָווּ לְהוּ אַרְבָּעָה! אַפֵּיק טוּמְטוּם, וְעָיֵיל יָתוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a slightly different version of that *baraita*. **Rabbi Elazar says:** There are **five** types of animals that **do not become consecrated and do not render** another animal **consecrated, and they are: A *tereifa*, and** prohibited **diverse kinds** of livestock, **and** an animal **born by caesarean section, and** an animal that is **a *tumtum*, and a hermaphrodite. And if you would say: Here too, omit from this** *baraita* **a *tumtum*,** then **there are** only **four** types of animals listed in the *baraita*. The Gemara answers: **Remove** the mention of **a *tumtum* and** in its place **insert an orphan,** i.e., an animal born after the death of its mother, which is also disqualified as a sacrifice.

But at this point the *Gemara* faces a difficulty. An earlier source enumerates exactly *five* categories: *treifah, kilayim, yotzei dofen, tumtum ve-androgynos* — five items. If one crosses out *tumtum*, only four remain. Saying "cross it out" to get from five to four seems like too crude a move. The *Gemara* therefore goes further: *afik tumtum ve-ayeil yatom* — remove *tumtum* and replace it with *yatom*, an orphaned animal. What is a *yatom* in this context? It is an animal born after its mother died, or one whose mother was not alive for its first seven days. The *pasuk* states *shivat yamim yihyeh tachat imo u-miyom ha-shemini va-halah* — the animal must have been with its mother for the first seven days; if the mother was not alive, it can never be brought as a *korban* at all. So *yatom* is a legitimate fifth category of categorical disqualification, and by replacing *tumtum* with *yatom*, the count of five is preserved, and the list becomes coherent. The text has required significant emendation — but the underlying principle is consistent: *tumtum* belongs nowhere on any list of categorical exclusions.

## Tannaitic Debate

### Rabbi Yishmael

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא כְּתַנָּאֵי? רַבִּי אִילְעַאי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס בְּכוֹר הוּא וּמוּמוֹ עִמּוֹ, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עָלָיו. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: הֲרֵי הוּא אוֹמֵר ״הַזָּכָר״, וְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״זָכָר״ אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא לְהוֹצִיא טוּמְטוּם וְאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that this question of whether a *tumtum* is a distinct entity or is definitely either a male or female **is the subject of** a dispute between ***tanna'im*.** As it is taught in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Ilai says in the name of Rabbi Yishmael: A hermaphrodite is a firstborn, and** it comes **with its blemish; and the Rabbis say: It is not imbued with sanctity. Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda says in the name of Rabbi Shimon:** The verse **states: "Males"** (Deuteronomy 15:19), **and wherever it is stated: Male,"** this serves **to exclude only a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite.** Since according to Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda it is necessary for a verse to exclude a *tumtum*, like a hermaphrodite, from the category of a male, evidently in his opinion a *tumtum* is not considered to be one whose status as a male is uncertain but a distinct entity.

Having worked through the proofs, the *Gemara* now asks: *leima kitannai* — perhaps the dispute between Rav Chisda and his challengers reflects an underlying debate among *tannaim*. The *Gemara* cites: *Rabbi Ilai* in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael* — *androgynos bechor hu u-mumo imo* — an *androgynos* is a *bechor* and its blemish accompanies it. This aligns with Rabbi Yishmael's position in our Mishnah: the *androgynos* is a definite male, the female anatomy constituting a *mum*, and therefore it may be slaughtered and eaten but may not be worked as a draft animal. It is a valid *bechor* with a *mum*.

### Chachamim View

*Ve-Chachamim omrim: ein kedushah chalah alav* — the Sages say: no *kedushah* falls upon an *androgynos* at all. It is a third gender, definitively not male, and therefore entirely outside the framework of *bechor*. One may work it; one need not bring it to the *Kohen*; the normal *halachot* of firstborn animals do not apply.

### Rabbi Shimon Position

Then comes a third position, not found in our Mishnah: *Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda* in the name of *Rabbi Shimon* cites the *pasuk* of *ha-zachar takdish* — the male you shall sanctify. And he rules: *ve-chol makom she-ne'emar zachar eino ela le-hotzi tumtum ve-androgynos* — wherever the *Torah* says *zachar*, the intention is to exclude both *tumtum* and *androgynos*. At first glance this seems redundant with the *Chachamim*, who already say *androgynos* is categorically excluded. But Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda adds one word: *tumtum*. The *Chachamim* spoke only of *androgynos*; Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda explicitly extends the categorical exclusion to *tumtum* as well. If one were to cross out *tumtum* here, Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda would be saying nothing beyond what the *Chachamim* already say — *Rabbi Shimon Yehuda hainu rabbanan*. The only meaningful addition is the word *tumtum*. It therefore appears that the debate between *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda is precisely whether *tumtum* is a separate, excluded category or merely a *safek*.

### Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא: סְמִי מִיכָּן טוּמְטוּם, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה הַיְינוּ רַבָּנַן! אֶלָּא לָאו טוּמְטוּם אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: אֵין קְדוּשָּׁה חָלָה עָלָיו אַאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, אֲבָל טוּמְטוּם סְפֵיקָא הוּא וְקָדוֹשׁ מִסְּפֵיקָא, וַאֲתָא רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם בְּרִיָּה הוּא וְלֹא קָדוֹשׁ! לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא טוּמְטוּם בִּבְרִיָּה לָא מְסַפְּקָא, זָכָר וּנְקֵבָה הוּא דִּמְסַפְּקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this *baraita*: **And if you would say:** Here too, **omit from** this *baraita* **a *tumtum*,** then the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda is** the same as that of **the Rabbis,** as they too maintain that a hermaphrodite is not sacred. **Rather,** is it **not** that the difference **between them** is with regard to the status of **a *tumtum*, as the first *tanna*,** i.e., the Rabbis, **holds: It is not imbued with sanctity,** and they are referring specifically **to a hermaphrodite,** which was the subject of Rabbi Ilai's statement, **but a *tumtum* is an uncertain** case, **and** therefore **it is sacred due to uncertainty. And Rabbi Shimon** ben Yehuda **comes to say: A *tumtum* is** not of uncertain status, but a distinct **entity, and it is not sacred** at all. The Gemara responds: **No, everyone** agrees **with regard to** the statement that **a *tumtum* is** a distinct **entity** that **there is no doubt** that this is incorrect. **Where** there is **doubt is** with regard to whether it is **a male or a female.**

The *Gemara* resists this reading. *D'chulei alma tumtum b'birya lo mesafkei* — nobody actually argues that *tumtum* is a third gender, a *beriyah* unto itself. Rather, the question is *zachar u-nekeivah hu d'mesafkei* — everyone agrees it is a *safek* between male and female; the question is whether and how that *safek* can be resolved. According to this clarification, the *tanna kamma* holds that *tumtum* is a *safek* and the *kedushah* of *bechor* applies *misafek*, and Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda is not saying *tumtum* is a third gender but rather that in the specific case where a *tumtum* urinates from the place where a female would urinate, that evidence suffices to determine it is female and exclude it — while the *tanna kamma* holds that even that evidence is insufficient, since the male organ may be displaced. The debate, in other words, is not over ontological categories but over evidentiary standards for a factual determination.

## Tumtum Evidence

### Both Are Doubtful

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵטִיל מַיִם בִּמְקוֹם זַכְרוּת — דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּזָכָר הוּא, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בְּמֵטִיל מַיִם בְּמָקוֹם נַקְבוּת: מָר סָבַר חָיְשִׁינַן שֶׁמָּא נֶהֶפְכָה זַכְרוּתוֹ לְנַקְבוּתוֹ, וּמַר סָבַר לָא חָיְשִׁינַן. כִּי הָא דְּהוֹרָה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בִּבְהֵמָה מֵטִיל מַיִם בְּמָקוֹם נַקְבוּת — חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** In addition, in a case where the *tumtum* **urinates from the place of the male** sex organ, then **everyone agrees that it is a male. When they disagree** is in the case **of** a *tumtum* that **urinates from the place of the female** sex organ. One **Sage,** the Rabbis, **holds** that **we are concerned** that **perhaps its male** sex organ **was inverted toward its female** sex organ; therefore, it is of uncertain status. **And** one **Sage,** Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda, **holds** that **we are not concerned** about this possibility; rather, it is definitely a female. And Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda maintains **in accordance with that which Rabbi Elazar ruled: With regard to** a firstborn **animal** that **urinates from the place** where **the female** sex organ is found, it is **non-sacred,** as it is certainly a female.

Having clarified that everyone agrees *tumtum* is a *safek zachar safek nekeivah*, the *Gemara* now addresses the question of determination. Each *tumtum* is one or the other in fact; the question is whether we can determine which.

### Urine Tests

*Matil mayim b'makom zachrut* — if the urine exits from the place where male genitalia would normally be, *d'chulei alma lo pligi d'zachar hu* — all agree the animal is male. There is no conceivable mechanism by which a female animal's urine would exit from the anterior male location. *Ki pligi b'matil mayim b'makom nakeivut* — the dispute arises when the urine exits from the posterior, female location.

### Organ Displacement

*Mar savar chashinan shema nehepcha zachruto le-nechvuto* — one opinion holds: since the genitalia are covered over, we cannot know the orientation of the male organ. It may be folded back such that the opening points toward the rear. The urine exiting from the posterior location does not therefore prove female identity; the animal may be male with a displaced organ. *U-mar savar lo chashinan* — the other opinion holds that we are not concerned with this remote possibility. Normally, posterior urination indicates a female, and we apply normal assumptions.

## Case: Female Urination

### Rabbi Ila Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דְּהוֹרָה רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: בִּבְהֵמָה מֵטִיל מַיִם בְּמָקוֹם נַקְבוּת — חוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** And Rabbi Shimon ben Yehuda maintains **in accordance with that which Rabbi Elazar ruled: With regard to** a firstborn **animal** that **urinates from the place** where **the female** sex organ is found, it is **non-sacred,** as it is certainly a female.

The *Gemara* presents a concrete case. *Beheimah ha-matil mayim bi-mkom nekeivah* — an animal that was urinating from the posterior, female location — was a *tumtum* in which a question about *bechor* arose. *Rabbi Ila* (some versions read *Rabbi Elazar*) ruled: *chullin*, it is completely ordinary non-sacred — *lo safek* — not a *safek* at all. It is a definite *nekeivah* and therefore not a *bechor*.

### Yochanan Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּהֵי בַּהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאן דְּלָא חָשׁ לְתַנָּא קַמָּא וּלְרַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל? וְלֵימָא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן נָמֵי: מַאן דְּלָא חָשׁ לְרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי? דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּאַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, אֲבָל בְּטוּמְטוּם — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל סְפֵיקָא!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan wondered** about this ruling of Rabbi Elazar: **Who** is the Sage **who is not concerned about** the opinion of **the first *tanna*** in the mishna **and about** the opinion of **Rabbi Yishmael,** who maintain that a *tumtum* is a case of uncertainty? The Gemara asks: **And let Rabbi Yoḥanan also say: Who** is this **who is not concerned about** the opinion of **the latter Rabbis** in the mishna, **as Rav Ḥisda says:** This **dispute** refers **to a hermaphrodite; but with regard to a *tumtum*, everyone agrees** that **it is an uncertain** case.

*Tamah Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Yochanan was astonished at this ruling. *Man lo chash le-Tanna Kamma u-le-Rabbi Yishmael?* — How could he be unconcerned with the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael? The Mishnah's opening statement treats *tumtum* as a *safek* — and nobody in the Mishnah disputed that. The *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael both accept *tumtum* as a *safek zachar safek nekeivah*. Rabbi Ila's ruling — that posterior urination definitively resolves the question — seems to override this consensus entirely. How does one have the *chutzpah* to *pasken* against the Mishnah?

The *Gemara* asks: why did Rabbi Yochanan only mention the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael? What about the *Rabbanan basrai* — the last opinion in the Mishnah, who say *androgynos* is a third gender? They also implicitly accept *tumtum* as a *safek*, as Rav Chisda explicitly stated. The *Gemara* answers: Rabbi Yochanan does *not* accept Rav Chisda's ruling — *lo sevira lei de-Rav Chisda*. He holds that the same *Rabbanan basrai* who say *androgynos* is its own category would also say *tumtum* is its own category, at least for the purposes of *korbanot*. Therefore Rabbi Yochanan cannot invoke them as additional support for the *safek* position.

### Chisda vs. Yochanan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, אִי לָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, לֵימָא הוּא דְּאָמַר כְּרַבָּנַן בָּתְרָאֵי? הָכִי נָמֵי קָאָמַר: מַאן שָׁבֵיק תְּרֵי וְעָבֵיד כְּחַד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that **Rabbi Yoḥanan does not hold** in accordance with that statement **of Rav Ḥisda;** rather, he maintains that according to the latter Rabbis a *tumtum* is a distinct entity. The Gemara asks: **If Rabbi Yoḥanan does not hold** in accordance with Rav Ḥisda, why did he wonder about Rabbi Elazar's ruling? **Let him say that** Rabbi Elazar **said** his opinion **in accordance with** the opinion of **the latter Rabbis.** The Gemara answers: **That is indeed what he is saying: Who** is the Sage **who leaves** aside the opinion of **two** Sages, the first *tanna* and Rabbi Yishmael, **and acts in accordance with one** opinion, the latter Rabbis?

The debate between Rav Chisda and Rabbi Yochanan is thus substantive and fundamental. Rav Chisda reads the Mishnah's opening *tumtum safek* statement as a unanimous ruling and cross-cuts every *braita* that treats *tumtum* as categorically excluded. Rabbi Yochanan reads the Mishnah differently: the *Rabbanan basrai*, who say *androgynos* is its own category, would extend that reasoning to *tumtum* as well. This reading preserves the integrity of all those *braitot* without erasure.

Rabbi Yochanan's objection to Rabbi Ila's ruling, then, is as follows: even if you disagree with Rav Chisda and follow the *Rabbanan basrai* regarding *androgynos* and *tumtum* as categorical exclusions, you are still adopting the position of only one of three *tannaim* in the Mishnah. The first two — the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yishmael — clearly treat *tumtum* as a *safek*. Who gave you the authority to *pasken* like the one minority position and permit a *bechor* on the basis of urine location, when the majority of the Mishnah's voices treat it as a *safek*?

The *Gemara* answers: *man shavak terei ve-avid ke-chad?* — that is exactly Rabbi Yochanan's point. Abandoning two Mishnaic positions to rule like the third is problematic. And this is not merely an abstract debate about consistency. We are talking about a *bechor*, which may or may not have the *kedushah* of a firstborn animal. Ruling definitively that it is *chullin* based on urination patterns, in the face of a Mishnaic consensus that treats it as a *safek*, requires serious justification.

It is worth pausing on what is conceptually at stake. Rabbi Yochanan is not saying that *tumtum* is definitively a third gender. He is saying that an animal that is biologically one or the other, but whose biology is currently unresolvable — and whose unresolvability is itself a function of its physical constitution — may appropriately receive distinct *halachic* treatment, particularly in the domain of *korbanot*. Whether this is "truly" a third category or merely a *halachic* proxy category for undetermined status is a question that remains open within *Tosafot*'s analysis. What is clear is that Rabbi Yochanan preserves the simple sense of all those *mishnayot* and *braitot* that categorically exclude *tumtum*.

### Resh Lakish Pesak

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר כְּמַאן סָבְרַהּ? כִּי הָא דְּאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא אָמְרוּ טוּמְטוּם סָפֵק אֶלָּא בְּאָדָם, הוֹאִיל וְזַכְרוּתוֹ וְנַקְבוּתוֹ בְּמָקוֹם אֶחָד, אֲבָל בְּהֵמָה — מֵטִיל מַיִם בְּמָקוֹם זַכְרוּת זָכָר, מֵטִיל מַיִם בְּמָקוֹם נַקְבוּת נְקֵבָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And Rabbi Elazar, in accordance with whose** opinion does **he hold?** The Gemara answers that he holds **in accordance with that which Reish Lakish says:** The Sages **said that** the halakhic status of **a *tumtum* is an uncertain** case **only with regard to a person, since the male** sex organ **and the female** sex organ **are in one place,** i.e., a man and a woman urinate from the same area of the body. Since that area is concealed in the case of a *tumtum*, its status is uncertain. **But** with regard to a *tumtum* **animal,** there is no uncertainty whether it is a male or a female, as the genitals of a male and a female are not found in the same place. Rather, if it **urinates from the place** of **the male** sex organ, it is **a male;** if it **urinates from the place of the female** sex organ, it is **a female.**

*Resh Lakish* offers a resolution that is strikingly practical. *Lo amru tumtum safek ela be-adam* — when the Sages said *tumtum* is a *safek*, they meant only by a human being. Why? *Ho'il ve-zachruto u-nekvuto bi-makom echad* — because in a human, the male and female genitalia are located in essentially the same area of the body, and therefore urination location does not disambiguate between male and female regardless of where it exits. But *aval bi-vehemah* — in an animal — *matil mayim bi-mkom zachrut zachar, matil mayim bi-mkom nekeva nekeva* — the male and female anatomies are in distinctly different locations, and urine exiting from the respective location is definitive. We are not concerned that the male organ is bent.

This is a *pesak halacha*, not merely a theoretical position. Resh Lakish is saying: follow Rav Chisda and treat *tumtum* as a *safek*, but then apply evidentiary reasoning to resolve that *safek* in animals based on urination location. This enables a ruling of *chullin* for a *tumtum* that urinates posteriorly in an animal without crossing into categorical exclusion or third-gender territory.

### Oshaya Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב אוֹשַׁעְיָא: וְלֵיחוּשׁ שֶׁמָּא נֶהֶפְכָה זַכְרוּתוֹ לְנַקְבוּתוֹ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּחָיֵישׁ לְמִיעוּטָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Oshaya objects to this: And let us be concerned** that **perhaps its male** sex organ **was turned round to** the place of **its female** sex organ. Abaye **said to him: In accordance with whose** opinion do you raise this objection? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who is concerned about the minority** of cases? The *halakha* is not in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Meir, and therefore one should follow the majority of animals, to whom this does not occur.

*Matkif lah Rabbi Oshaya*: how can Resh Lakish rule with such confidence? *Ve-leicheish shema nehepcha zachruto le-nechvuto* — perhaps the male organ has bent over, and the urine is exiting from the rear even though the animal is male? Resh Lakish responds: should we follow *Rabbi Meir*, who is concerned with remote possibilities? This is not a likely scenario; normally we do not raise concerns about the improbable.

### Abaye Hanina

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי בַּר אָבִין וְרַב חֲנַנְיָא בַּר אָבִין דְאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן, הוֹאִיל וְאִישְׁתַּנִּי אִישְׁתַּנִּי. אִית דְּאָמַר אִישְׁתַּנִּי, וְאִית דְּאָמַר לָא אִישְׁתַּנִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye bar Avin and Rav Ḥananya bar Avin both say: You** may **even say** that Rav Oshaya's objection is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who are not concerned about a minority. The reason is that **since** in this case, the animal **has changed** in that it is different from typical animals, as it is a *tumtum*, perhaps it is also **changed** with regard to its male sex organ being inverted toward the female sex organ. If so, the Sages disagree with regard to this principle: **There are** those **who say** that since it has changed, it has **changed, and there are** those **who say** that the claim that since it has changed, it has **changed** is **not** accepted.

*Abaye Hanina*: *afilu teima Rabbanan, ishtani ishtani* — even if one follows the *Rabbanan* who generally do not concern themselves with remote possibilities, here we must. This entire animal is biologically anomalous — its genitalia are covered over and the normal anatomical assumptions do not hold. When the body has departed from normal in one significant way, you cannot simply apply normal anatomical reasoning to it. *Ishtani ishtani*: since it is different, it is different — meaning that once something about an organism is abnormal, you must remain concerned about other anomalies in the same organ system. And therefore the question of whether the urine location proves anything remains genuinely unresolvable.

## Tumtum Evidence…

### Ishtani Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָ אִישְׁתַּנִּי וְלָא אִישְׁתַּנִּי תַּנָּאֵי? דְּתַנְיָא: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁקִּידֵּשׁ — קִדּוּשָׁיו קִדּוּשִׁין, נִתְקַדֵּשׁ — קִדּוּשָׁיו קִדּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that the question of whether an animal that has changed in one respect has **changed** in a different respect, **or** whether it has **not changed,** is the subject of a dispute between ***tanna'im*. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to **a *tumtum* who betrothed** a woman, the **betrothal is** considered **a betrothal,** due to uncertainty, as the *tumtum* might be a male; and similarly, if the *tumtum* **was betrothed** by a man, the **betrothal is** deemed **a betrothal** due to uncertainty, as the *tumtum* might be a female.

The *Gemara* then asks whether the *ishtani* principle — the idea that an anomalous body cannot be measured by normal biological assumptions — is itself a *tannaitic* debate. *Leima ishtani ve-lo ishtani tana'ei hi* — perhaps the question of whether anomalousness should extend our concerns from one bodily feature to another is the actual axis of the dispute in the *braitot* we have been examining. This sets up the transition to the practical domain of marriage law.

## Marriage Halachot

### Tumtum Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחוֹלֵץ, וְחוֹלְצִין לְאִשְׁתּוֹ, וּמְיַבְּמִים לְאִשְׁתּוֹ. וְתַנְיָא אִידַּךְ: אֵשֶׁת טוּמְטוּם חוֹלֶצֶת וְלֹא מִתְיַיבֶּמֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **And** if the brother of the *tumtum* died childless, and there is no other brother who can perform levirate marriage with the widow, the *tumtum* **performs *ḥalitza*,** i.e., his brother's widow removes his shoe, as the *tumtum* might be a male, and she would require *ḥalitza* to release her from the levirate bond. **And** if the *tumtum* died, one of the brothers of the *tumtum* **performs *ḥalitza* with the wife of** the *tumtum*, **or enters into levirate marriage with the wife of** the *tumtum*, in case the *tumtum* was a male. **And it is taught** in **another** *baraita*: **The wife of a *tumtum* performs *ḥalitza* and does not enter into levirate marriage.**

*D'tanya*: if a *tumtum* marries a woman, the *kiddushin* are binding — *kidushav kidushin* — because he may be male, and at minimum it is a *safek kiddushin*. If a man marries a *tumtum*, likewise, the *kiddushin* are binding because the *tumtum* may be female. The *braita* continues: *ve-choletz ve-choltzin le-ishto* — a *tumtum* who is the sole surviving sibling performs *chalitzah* to his deceased brother's wife, because he may be male and subject to the obligation. And *choltzin le-ishto* — his brothers must perform *chalitzah* to his wife if he dies without children, because he may have been male and the marriage may have been fully valid.

*U-miyabeim et ishto* — additionally, a brother may perform *yibum* with his wife. The logic is: *miman nafshach* — either way it works out. If the *tumtum* was male, *yibum* is a positive obligation. If the *tumtum* was female, the *kiddushin* were never valid, and marrying the widow is simply a new marriage — there is no prohibition of *eshet ach* where there was never a valid marriage.

### Woman Marries Tumtum

The second *braita* addresses the reverse: *eshet tumtum choletzet ve-lo mistayabemet* — the wife of a *tumtum* who dies must undergo *chalitzah* but cannot be taken in *yibum*. The *chalitzah* is required because the *kiddushin* may have been valid. But unlike the first case, *yibum* is not permitted. Why the asymmetry?

### Saris Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סַבְרוּהָ, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: סְרִיס חַמָּה לֹא חוֹלֵץ וְלֹא מְיַיבֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to these two *baraitot*, the Sages **assumed that everyone** holds **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva, who says: A eunuch** whose condition is caused **naturally,** i.e., one who was entirely lacking in sexual capacity from birth, **does not perform *ḥalitza* and does not enter into levirate marriage** with his sister-in-law, as he is not included in the mitzva of levirate marriage. Likewise, if he died his brothers do not perform *ḥalitza* with his wife, and they may not enter into levirate marriage with her, as she is forbidden to them as a brother's wife who does not require levirate marriage.

The *Gemara* initially assumes the debate is rooted in *Rabbi Akiva*'s ruling that a *saris chamah* — a naturally sterile man — does not perform *chalitzah* or *yibum*. A *saris chamah* was never capable of having children, and since the obligation of *yibum* derives from the commandment to perpetuate the deceased brother's line, one who could never have had children is outside that framework. The *Gemara* proposes: the first *braita* that permits *yibum* holds that we do not suspect a *tumtum* of being a *saris* — we do not say *ishtani, ishtani*, that because the genitalia are covered, we should assume fertility is also affected. The second *braita* that forbids *yibum* holds that we do suspect sterility — *ishtani ishtani* applies, and since we are already dealing with a body that is anomalous in one respect, we are concerned about ancillary anomalies as well.

The *Gemara* ultimately rejects this framing, saying that actually all opinions may agree that we apply *ishtani ishtani*. The debate is rather about what *Rabbi Akiva*'s ruling implies for our case: one opinion holds that sterility — even if probable — does not eliminate the obligation of *yibum* (because *Rabbi Akiva*'s exclusion was limited to definite cases), while another holds that even a *safek saris* is sufficient to preclude *yibum*.

### Rabbi Yehuda View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן תַּנָּא אַלִּיבָּא דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא? אִילֵימָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה — הָא וַדַּאי סָרִיס מְשַׁוֵּי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And who is the *tanna*** who holds **in accordance with** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva** that the wife of a eunuch whose condition is caused naturally does not require levirate marriage, and yet he maintains that the wife of a *tumtum* must perform *ḥalitza*? **If we say** it is **Rabbi Yehuda,** this cannot be, as in his opinion even if the genitals of a *tumtum* were revealed and he was found to be a male, **he deems him** like **a definite eunuch** whose condition is caused naturally, and therefore his wife does not require *ḥalitza* at all.

*Ilima Rabbi Yehuda* — if this *braita* that forbids *yibum* reflects Rabbi Yehuda's position, there is a problem. For Rabbi Yehuda holds *vadai saris* — that a *tumtum* found upon investigation to be male is definitively sterile. *Harei zeh lo yachalotz mipnei she-hu ke-saris* — such a person does not perform *chalitzah* because he is treated as a definite *saris*, not merely a doubtful one. If that is so, then the *braita* that says *choletzet* — *chalitzah* must be done — cannot reflect Rabbi Yehuda's view, because Rabbi Yehuda would say the *chalitzah* is entirely unnecessary and meaningless: the man was definitively sterile and the *mitzvah* of *yibum* never applied.

### Rabbi Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם שֶׁנִּקְרַע וְנִמְצָא זָכָר — הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יַחְלוֹץ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא כְּסָרִיס! אֶלָּא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: טוּמְטוּם לֹא חוֹלֵץ, שֶׁמָּא יִקָּרַע וְיִמָּצֵא סָרִיס חַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **As we learned** in a mishna (*Yevamot* 81a) that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** With regard to **a *tumtum* who was torn** open so that his genitals were exposed, **and he was found** to be **a male,** he **must not perform *ḥalitza*, because he is** treated **like a eunuch** whose condition is caused naturally. Just as this *tumtum* does not perform *ḥalitza*, so too, his widow does not require *ḥalitza*. **Rather, it is** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda,** who maintains that if a *tumtum* was torn open and he was found to be a male, he is considered as a matter of uncertainty to be a eunuch whose condition is caused naturally, and consequently his wife requires *ḥalitza*. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, says: A *tumtum* does not perform *ḥalitza*** with the wife of his late, childless brother if there are other brothers to perform *ḥalitza*, as **perhaps** the skin covering his genitals **will be torn** open **and he will be found** to be **a eunuch** whose condition is caused **naturally,** and the widow would not be released from the levirate bond by his *ḥalitza*. According to this opinion, it is not certain that a *tumtum* would be found to be a eunuch whose condition is caused naturally.

*Ela Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda hi* — this *braita* reflects the position of *Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda*. *D'tanya: Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda omer, tumtum lo choletz shema yikara ve-yimatza saris chamah* — a *tumtum* does not perform *chalitzah* because perhaps when the covering is removed, he will be found to be a *saris chamah*. If one might ask: but there is another reason not to perform *chalitzah* — perhaps the *tumtum* turns out to be female! The *Gemara* responds: *shema ka'amar* — Rabbi Yossi is listing possibilities, not giving a single reason. *Shema yikara ve-yimatza nekeivah* — perhaps it will be found to be female, *ei nami zachar, nami shema yimatza saris chamah* — and even if it is found to be male, perhaps it will prove to be naturally sterile.

So according to Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda, there are two sources of concern that preclude *chalitzah* by the surviving *tumtum*: the possibility of being female, and the possibility of being sterile even if male. Neither is assumed to be definite — both are possibilities — but together they prevent confident performance of the *mitzvah*.

### Practical Differences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַטּוּ כֹּל דְּמִיקְּרַע זָכָר מִשְׁתְּכַח, נְקֵבָה לָא מִשְׁתְּכַח? שֶׁמָּא קָאָמַר, שֶׁמָּא יִקָּרַע וְיִמָּצֵא נְקֵבָה. אִי נָמֵי זָכָר נָמֵי, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא סָרִיס חַמָּה. מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אָמַר רָבָא: לִפְסוֹל בִּמְקוֹם אַחִים,
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the opinion of Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, the Gemara asks: Why does this individual not perform *ḥalitza* due only to the possibility that he will be found to be a eunuch whose condition is caused naturally? **Is that to say** that **every** *tumtum* **whose** skin **was torn is found** to be **a male** and is **not found** to be **a female?** The Gemara explains that Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, **said** his opinion in the formulation of: **Perhaps,** which includes multiple uncertainties: A *tumtum* must not perform *ḥalitza*, as **perhaps he will be torn** open **and found** to be **a female,** who can certainly not perform *ḥalitza*. **Alternatively,** if he is found to be **a male,** he may **also** not perform *ḥalitza*, since **perhaps he will be found** to be **a eunuch** whose condition is caused **naturally.** The Gemara asks: Since both Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Yosei, son of Rabbi Yehuda, agree that a *tumtum* may not perform *ḥalitza* for his brother's wife, **what is** the practical difference **between** their opinions? **Rava said:** There is a practical difference **with regard to disqualifying** the widow, through his *ḥalitza*, from entering into levirate marriage **in a case** where there are other **brothers.**

*Mai beinayhu* — what is the practical difference between Rabbi Yehuda, who holds the *tumtum* is a *definite saris*, and Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda, who holds it is only a *possible saris*? *Amar Rava: lifsol bi-mkom achim* — the difference manifests when there are other brothers present.

If this *tumtum* — the surviving sibling of the deceased — performs *chalitzah* to the deceased's wife, the question is whether that *chalitzah* has any force. According to Rabbi Yehuda, the *tumtum* is a definite *saris*: his *chalitzah* is completely meaningless, and other brothers retain the full option to perform *yibum*. According to Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda, the *tumtum* is only a possible *saris*: the *chalitzah* may have been valid, and therefore other brothers cannot perform *yibum* — the possibly-valid *chalitzah* has already precluded that option. They may, however, still need to perform *chalitzah* themselves to definitively free the woman, since we are not certain this *tumtum*'s *chalitzah* was fully effective.

*Ve-lachlotz she-lo bi-mkom achim ika beinayhu* — a second practical difference arises when there are no other brothers at all. If the *tumtum* is the sole surviving sibling and he is a definite *saris* (Rabbi Yehuda), then the widow requires no *chalitzah* whatsoever — the *mitzvah* of *yibum* never applied to this brother, and she is free. But if he is only a possible *saris* (Rabbi Yossi be-Rabbi Yehuda), and he might actually be obligated in *chalitzah*, then she does require him to perform it in order to be released.

## Final Summary

### Androgynos Options

To summarize the range of positions encountered throughout this daf: regarding *androgynos*, there are three distinct theoretical frameworks. The first holds that *androgynos* is intrinsically indeterminate — *safek zachar safek nekeivah* — not in the sense that any individual case is resolvable but that the category itself is defined by irresolvable indeterminacy, with only two genders acknowledged. The second holds it is a male with a *mum*, the female anatomy constituting a blemish that affects the *halachot* of *bechor* but not its fundamental identity. The third holds it is a third gender — *beriyah bifnei atzmah* — entirely outside the binary framework.

### Tumtum Options

Regarding *tumtum*, the picture is more nuanced. The first position, championed by Rav Chisda, holds that *tumtum* is a pure *safek* — each individual case is either male or female in fact, we simply do not know, and the applicable *halachot* are those of any ordinary doubt. This requires erasing the word *tumtum* from every *braita* that treats it as categorically excluded. The second position, associated with Rabbi Yochanan, holds that at least in the domain of *korbanot*, *tumtum* functions as an excluded category, whether because it is genuinely a third category or because its irresolvable factual indeterminacy (which is not epistemic but constitutive of its current state) warrants categorical treatment. The third position seeks to preserve the *safek* framing while allowing for empirical resolution — Resh Lakish's view that in animals, urination location definitively determines gender — though this is challenged by *Abaye bar Avin* and *Rav Hanina bar Avin* on the basis of *ishtani ishtani*, the principle that anomalous bodies cannot be measured by normal anatomical assumptions.

### Next Perek

All of these issues — the three positions on *androgynos*, the three positions on *tumtum*, and the *ishtani* principle — will be revisited in the next *perek*, where the *Gemara* returns to the topic of *mumin* specifically as it applies to human beings. The animal-based analysis of this *perek* provides the foundation; the human dimension, with its additional complexities, will be the subject of what follows.
