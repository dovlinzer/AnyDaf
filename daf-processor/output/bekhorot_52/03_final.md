# Bekhorot 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pidyon HaBen Review

### Ritual vs. Debt

The preceding *mishnayot* raised a conceptual question of considerable depth: what is the relationship between the ritual of *pidyon haben* and the underlying debt that gives it legal force? Our *masechet* has consistently maintained that without the debt—without the ability to demand payment in court—there is simply no *mitzvah* of *pidyon haben*. The ritual does not exist independently of its financial substructure.

### Payment Parameters

Layered atop this foundation, however, are parameters that belong specifically to the ritual dimension of *pidyon*. The payment cannot be made in *karka* (real estate), slaves, or *shtarot* (promissory notes). These restrictions govern the act of payment itself. Yet the underlying debt continues to subsist: if the father and son both die, the estate remains obligated. This duality was illustrated dramatically by a *gemara* case in which a *kohen* returned the *pidyon* money and then doubted whether the next payment was given with full intent. The conclusion was that since what is fundamentally at stake is a debt, it must be discharged with genuine intent of payment.

### Matana Al Menat

At the same time, the *gemara* in Kiddushin introduces the concept of *matana al menat lehachzir*—a conditional gift given on the understanding that it will be returned. This mechanism would apply only if there exists a dimension of *pidyon* that can stand independent of the debt, severing the ritual aspect from the purely financial one. The tension between these two models—*pidyon* as debt and *pidyon* as ritual—is a live and unresolved conceptual question.

### Father vs. Son

A second major theme from the preceding discussion concerns the priority between the father's *mitzvah* and the son's *mitzvah* to perform the redemption. The earlier *mishnah* appeared to frame this as a debate, and the *gemara* attempted to reframe the question: if all else is equal, the individual's obligation to redeem himself takes precedence.

Yet a number of later *acharonim* and *poskim* resist this reading. The *Minchat Chinuch*, for example, poses the following scenario: a father was never redeemed, his son is now an adult and capable of redeeming himself—who bears the primary obligation? The *Minchat Chinuch* rules that the father's *mitzvah* to redeem his adult son precedes the son's own *mitzvah* to redeem himself, and that if the son acts unilaterally, he has effectively stolen the *mitzvah* from his father. This position reflects a deeper principle found in earlier sources as well: the son's obligation is derivative of the father's. The primary and original obligation rests with the father.

## Bechor Double Portion

### Mishnah Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַבְּכוֹר נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאָב, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּנִכְסֵי הָאֵם, וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל בַּשֶּׁבַח, וְלֹא בְּרָאוּי כִּבְמוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The firstborn** son **takes a double** portion, i.e., twice the portion taken by the other sons, **when** inheriting **the property of the father, but he does not take twice** the portion **when** inheriting **the property of the mother. And neither does he take** twice the portion **in** any **enhancement** of the value of the property after the death of the father, **nor** does he take twice the portion **in** property **due** the father, **as** he does **in** property the father **possessed.**

We turn now to the *mishnah* at the bottom of 51b, which opens a new topic: the double portion of the firstborn in matters of inheritance. The *mishnah* states: *bechor notel pi shenayim benichsei ha'av ve'eino notel pi shenayim benichsei ha'em*—the firstborn receives a double portion in the father's estate, but does not receive a double portion in the mother's estate.

The straightforward reading is as follows. If the mother dies after the father, with the father's estate already having been divided, the sons now inherit their mother. In that distribution, the firstborn—whether he is her firstborn, the father's firstborn, or both—does not receive a double portion. The principle of *pi shenayim* applies exclusively to the father's estate.

### Rashi vs. Rashash

Rashi interprets the *mishnah* as referring specifically to the *nichsei melog*—the dowry property that the wife brought into the marriage, over which she retained title throughout. The *Rashash*, however, finds this difficult. He argues that if the wife predeceases the husband, he inherits her *nichsei melog* outright, after which it simply becomes his property and passes to his sons in the ordinary way. If, on the other hand, the husband dies first, the wife reclaims her *nichsei melog* directly, since she never lost title to it. In neither scenario, he contends, do the sons find themselves inheriting *nichsei melog* from their mother in a manner that would require us to invoke the rule. This difficulty deserves a more thorough treatment, but for now it is simply noted.

### Inheritance Sequence

The plain meaning of the *mishnah* is best understood through a concrete sequence: the father dies first, the sons inherit his estate in the ordinary way, and the firstborn takes his double portion. The mother then survives her husband. She reclaims her dowry property—both *nichsei melog* and *nichsei tzon barzel*, though these are treated differently—and collects her *ketubah* payment. She may also possess entirely independent assets. When she subsequently dies, and her husband is no longer alive to inherit her, her sons divide her estate equally. The *bechor* has no claim to *pi shenayim* here.

## Shevach & Ra'uy

### Math of Division

Before examining the limits on *pi shenayim*, a preliminary clarification about the arithmetic is in order. With three brothers, the double portion cannot simply mean two-thirds of the estate—that would be far more than double any single brother's share. The correct calculation is to add a notional fourth share: the estate is divided into four equal parts, the *bechor* receives two of them, and each remaining brother receives one. The *bechor* thus receives double what each of his brothers receives, not double the total estate.

### Shevach Exclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל בַּשֶּׁבַח. דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא לוֹ״, וְלֹא בָּרָאוּי כְּבַמּוּחְזָק, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches further: **And neither does he take** twice the portion **in** any **enhancement** of the value of the property after the death of his father. The Gemara explains that this is **because it is written:** "A double portion **of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), and this enhancement was not in his father's possession at the time of his death. The mishna also teaches: **Nor** does he take twice the portion in property **due** the father, **as** he does in the property **in possession** of the father. The Gemara explains that this too is **because it is written: "Of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), and property due the father is not in his possession.

The *mishnah* further limits the double portion: *ve'eino notel beshevach velo bera'uy kebemuchzak*. The *bechor* does not receive a double portion of *shevach*—physical improvements to the estate that occurred after the father's death. If the father died leaving a field, and before the brothers divided it the grain ripened or the fruit matured, the *bechor* receives only his proportional share of that improvement, like any other brother. Any increase in value after death is divided equally.

### Ra'uy Exclusion

Similarly, the *bechor* does not receive a double portion of *ra'uy*—property that was destined to come to the estate but had not yet arrived at the time of the father's death. The paradigmatic case is inheritance: if the father's father dies after the father, the grandfather's estate passes conceptually through the deceased father and is then distributed among the sons. Nevertheless, because that property was not present when the father died, the *bechor* takes no double portion of it. The logic is illustrated by a structural point: if the grandfather had two sons, one living and one dead with five children, the estate splits evenly between the living son and the five grandchildren—who divide their half equally among themselves—because it passes through the dead father as an equal brother before reaching them. The *bechor* among those grandchildren takes no *pi shenayim* of his grandfather's estate.

## Shevach & Ra'uy (continued)

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָאִשָּׁה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ, וְלֹא הַבָּנוֹת בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֵיהֶן, וְלֹא אֶת הַיָּבָם, וְכוּלָּם אֵין נוֹטְלִין בַּשֶּׁבַח וְלֹא בָּרָאוּי כְּבַמּוּחְזָק.
>
> **Translation:** **And neither does a woman** take these portions, i.e., any enhancement of the value of the property or the property due the husband, from her husband's property **for** payment of **her marriage contract** upon her divorce or her husband's death; **nor** do **the daughters** take this share of the property **for their sustenance,** to which they are entitled from their late father's possessions. **Nor** does **a man whose married brother died childless [*yavam*]** receive these portions, even though he acquires his brother's portion of their shared father's inheritance after performing levirate marriage with his brother's wife. The mishna summarizes: **And all of them do not take** a portion **in** any **enhancement** of the value of the property after the death of the owner, **nor** do they take a portion **in** property **due** the deceased, **as** they do in property **in** his **possession.**

### Tosafot on Loans

Tosafot takes up a question of obvious practical significance in the medieval context: what is the status of a *milveh*, an outstanding loan owed to the deceased? Unlike an inheritance from a relative who might die at any time, a loan is something directly and presently owed to the father. He may even hold liens on someone else's property, giving the debt a tangible legal reality. Yet the money is not physically in his possession.

The *gemara* in Bava Batra actually concludes unambiguously that loans are classified as *ra'uy*, not *muchzak*: *ein habechor notel pi shenayim bemilveh bein shegavsu karka bein shegavsu ma'os*—the *bechor* does not receive a double portion from a loan, whether it is collected in land or in cash. Tosafot's larger concern, then, is not the basic ruling on loans, which is well-established, but the ramifications of that ruling for related cases—most notably the *ketubah* of a wife, and whether it is analogous to a *milveh* or fundamentally different.

## Woman's Ketubah

### Ketubah Limitations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הָאִשָּׁה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ. אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בַּעַל חוֹב גּוֹבֶה אֶת הַשֶּׁבַח! אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: מִקּוּלֵּי כְתוּבָּה שָׁנוּ כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **And neither does a woman** take any enhancement of the value of the property or the property due the husband, from her husband's property **for** payment of **her marriage contract.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But doesn't Shmuel say a creditor collects** the value of **the enhancement** of the field of a borrower after the latter's death? Since a wife's marriage contract is like a promissory note, she is considered a creditor. **Rabbi Abba says:** The Sages **taught here** one **of the leniencies** for the husband that apply to **a marriage contract:** That a woman does not collect the value of the enhancement despite the fact that she is a creditor.

The *mishnah* at the top of 52a introduces the next case: *velo ha'isha bekesuvatah*. A woman collecting her *ketubah* from her husband's estate—whether from the *yorshim* or from those who purchased estate property subject to her lien—cannot collect from improvements that accrued after the husband's death, nor from property that came into the estate after his death. If the estate was worth five thousand at death, improved to ten thousand, or received an infusion from a subsequent inheritance, she may collect only against the original five thousand.

This appears anomalous. As a general rule, a *ba'al chov*—an ordinary creditor holding a lien—collects even from improvements and from property that came into the debtor's estate or into the hands of purchasers after the lien was established. Shmuel states explicitly: *ba'al chov goveh et hashevach*. Why, then, is the woman's *ketubah* treated differently?

### Dowry Categories

This discussion occasions a clarification of the two categories of dowry. *Nichsei melog* is property over which the wife retains title throughout the marriage: the husband enjoys its produce, but she owns the underlying asset. Upon his death or divorce, she simply retakes her property, whatever condition it may be in—no debt is involved. *Nichsei tzon barzel*, by contrast, is property whose title transfers entirely to the husband at marriage. Its value is assessed at the time of marriage, and upon death or divorce the husband owes the wife that assessed value. This is a genuine debt, incurred from the moment of marriage, though it falls due only upon death or divorce. Whether *nichsei tzon barzel* follows the same restrictions as the *ketubah* proper—whose obligation is incurred only upon death or divorce, not from the moment of marriage—is an interesting question that would require further investigation.

### Why Different

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִקּוּלֵּי כְתוּבָּה שָׁנוּ כָּאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **taught here** one **of the leniencies** for the husband that apply to **a marriage contract.**

The *gemara* provides the answer to the anomaly: *mikulei ketubah shanu kan*—Rabbi Abba explains that this is among the leniencies of the *ketubah*. The institution of *ketubah* reflects a deliberate balancing act by the Sages. On one side stands the interest in protecting the woman; on the other stands the concern that an overly burdensome lien would deter people from purchasing property or, more fundamentally, from marrying. If the woman's *ketubah* functioned as a fully empowered lien with all the force of an ordinary creditor's claim, it could impede commerce and the stability of the estate. Therefore, the Sages chose to limit her collection to the property that existed at the time of the husband's death—a restriction that favors the heirs and purchasers at the expense of her full creditor rights.

## Daughters & Yabam

### Daughters' Support

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא הַבָּנוֹת בִּמְזוֹנוֹתֵיהֶן. מַאי טַעְמָא? תְּנַאי כְּתוּבָּה כִּכְתוּבָּה דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Nor** do **the daughters** take this share of the property **for their sustenance,** to which they are entitled from their late father's possessions. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara explains that **a stipulation in the marriage contract,** such as that the daughters are entitled to sustenance from the father's property after his death, **is comparable to the marriage contract** itself, and therefore they cannot claim that which cannot be claimed from a marriage contract, i.e., an enhancement to the father's property.

*Velo habanot bimezonoteihen*—the daughters, too, when they draw upon the estate for their support, are limited to the property existing at the time of the father's death.

What is the source of this obligation? Rashi suggests the *mishnah* describes a case of a stepfather who voluntarily contracted to support his wife's daughters for a fixed period. Tosafot rejects this on two grounds: first, the *mishnah* uses the term *banot* without any indication of an unusual contractual scenario; second, a voluntarily contracted debt would be a standard creditor obligation, which by definition would not be subject to these limitations, since a normal creditor can collect even from *shevach*. Tosafot argues—and the *gemara* confirms—that the *mishnah* is describing the standard *ketubah* obligation that the Sages imposed: upon the father's death, daughters are to be supported from the estate until they marry. Because this is a *tena'i d'ketubah*—an obligation imposed by the Sages rather than freely assumed—it carries the same limitations as the *ketubah* itself. The daughters may draw their support only from the estate as it stood at the father's death, not from improvements or subsequently acquired assets.

This arrangement reflects the social and economic assumptions of the period: daughters were not expected to be independent wage earners, and the obligation of support passed from father to husband. The sons received portions of the estate outright; the daughters received maintenance until the responsibility could be assumed by their future husbands.

### Yabam's Portion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא (אֶת) הַיָּבָם. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״בְּכוֹר״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁבַח שֶׁשָּׁבְחוּ נְכָסִים בֵּין מִיתָה לְיִבּוּם, אֲבָל דְּבֵין יִבּוּם לַחֲלוּקָּה — שָׁקֵיל. מַאי טַעְמָא? ״יָקוּם עַל שֵׁם אָחִיו הַמֵּת״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וַהֲרֵי קָם.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: **Nor** does **the *yavam*** receive these portions. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for this? The Gemara answers that **the Merciful One calls** the *yavam* **a firstborn,** in the verse: "And it shall be, the firstborn" (Deuteronomy 25:6). **Abaye says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only with regard to an enhancement by** which **the property** was **enhanced between the death** of the brother **and the levirate marriage. But** concerning the enhancement **that** occurs **between the levirate marriage and the distribution** of the inheritance, **he does take** it. **What is the reason? The Merciful One states: "Shall succeed in the name of his brother who is dead"** (Deuteronomy 25:6), **and** by that stage **he has succeeded** him, and the enhancement occurred at that stage.

*Velo hayabam*—the *yavam* (the brother-in-law who performs *yibum*) also cannot collect double from improvements. When a man's brother dies childless and he performs *yibum* with the widow, the Torah's phrase *yakum al shem achiv hamet*—he shall stand in the name of his dead brother—is understood by the Sages to mean that he inherits his dead brother's share of the estate. This provides a financial incentive for *yibum*, though the *gemara* elsewhere notes the concern that men might perform *yibum* for improper reasons.

### Yabam Improvements

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ דְּבֵין יִבּוּם לַחֲלוּקָּה נָמֵי לָא שָׁקֵיל, מַאי טַעְמָא? כִּבְכוֹר, מָה בְּכוֹר אֵין לוֹ קוֹדֶם חֲלוּקָּה, אַף יָבָם נָמֵי אֵין לוֹ קוֹדֶם חֲלוּקָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says: Even with regard to** the enhancement that occurs **between the levirate marriage and the distribution** of the inheritance, the *yavam* **does not take** it. **What is the reason?** He is compared **to a firstborn: Just as a firstborn does not have** any special right to the inheritance **before** its **distribution, so too, a *yavam* does not have** any right to the inheritance **before** its **distribution.**

Abaye and Rava dispute the precise scope of the *yavam*'s limitation. Abaye rules: *lo shanu ela b'shevach sheshivchu nechasim bein mita l'yibum*—the limitation applies only to improvements of the estate between the death (of the father, given that the brother is already dead) and the act of *yibum*. But improvements that occur between the *yibum* and the subsequent division of the estate—*bein yibum lachaluka shakil*—he does collect in double. The reason: *yakum al shem achiv hamet amar rachmana v'harei kam*—the Torah said he shall stand in his brother's place, and he has done so. Once he has performed *yibum*, he genuinely represents himself and his dead brother. Any improvement after that point is divided with him receiving two equal shares—his own and his brother's—just as it would be divided if the brother were alive.

Rava disagrees: *afilu d'vein yibum l'chaluka nami lo shakil*—even improvements between *yibum* and division he does not take in double. The reason: *ma bechor ein lo kodem chaluka af yavam nami ein lo kodem chaluka*. The *yavam*'s double portion is derived from his status as a *bechor*-like figure. Just as the *bechor* does not receive a double portion of improvements accruing between death and division, neither does the *yavam*, regardless of whether those improvements occurred before or after the *yibum*.

## Scriptural Basis

### Father Not Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְלוֹ מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכוֹרָה״ — מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכוֹרָה לָאִישׁ, וְאֵין מִשְׁפַּט הַבְּכוֹרָה לָאִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that the firstborn son does not receive a double portion when inheriting the property of his mother. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason?** The Gemara explains that **the verse states: "The law of primogeniture is his"** (Deuteronomy 21:17); this is understood to mean that there is **a law of primogeniture for a man,** i.e., a duty to bequeath a double portion to his firstborn son, **and there is no law of primogeniture for a woman.**

The *gemara* now traces the scriptural basis for each of these rules. *Mai ta'ama*—why does the *bechor* take a double portion only from the father's estate and not the mother's? The *gemara* cites the *pasuk*: *ki hu reshit ono, lo mishpat habechora*. While the plain sense of *lo* refers to the *bechor* himself—he has the right of the firstborn—the *gemara* reads *lo* as referring to the father: to the father belong the laws of *bechora*. *Mishpat habechora le'ish ve'ein mishpat habechora le'isha*: the double portion applies when dividing the father's estate, not the mother's.

### Shevach Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵינוֹ נוֹטֵל בַּשֶּׁבַח. דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא לוֹ״, וְלֹא בָּרָאוּי כְּבַמּוּחְזָק, דִּכְתִיב: ״בְּכֹל אֲשֶׁר יִמָּצֵא לוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches further: **And neither does he take** twice the portion **in** any **enhancement** of the value of the property after the death of his father. The Gemara explains that this is **because it is written:** "A double portion **of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), and this enhancement was not in his father's possession at the time of his death. The mishna also teaches: **Nor** does he take twice the portion in property **due** the father, **as** he does in the property **in possession** of the father. The Gemara explains that this too is **because it is written: "Of all that he has"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), and property due the father is not in his possession.

The exclusion of *shevach* from the double portion is derived from the phrase *bechol asher yimatze lo*—in all that is found to him. The *bechor* receives double of everything that is present in the estate at the moment of the father's death: what is *nimtza*, found there. What comes into the estate afterwards, or what the estate earns through improvement afterwards, is not *nimtza lo* and is therefore divided equally. The same verse is invoked for *ra'uy* as well: anything that was merely *ra'uy* to come—fit to arrive, but not yet present—is excluded.

### Ketubah Creditor

The limitation on the woman's *ketubah* collection is explained as *mikulei ketubah*—a deliberate leniency built into the institution of *ketubah* by the Sages. Though she functions as a creditor and a standard creditor (*ba'al chov*) collects even from *shevach*, the Sages chose to limit her in this respect as part of the broader calibration of the *ketubah*'s obligations and protections.

### Daughters Obligation

Similarly, the daughters' support is classified as *tena'i d'ketubah dami*—comparable to a *ketubah* obligation. Because it is an obligation that the Sages imposed upon the husband rather than one he freely contracted, and because the purpose is to protect the daughters while not unduly burdening the sons who inherit the estate, it shares the same limitation: the daughters may be maintained only from the property that existed at the time of death.

## Yabam Status

### Yabam as Bechor

The *gemara* traces the scriptural basis for the *yavam*'s double portion. The Torah states: *v'haya habechor asher teled yakum al shem achiv hamet*—the firstborn that she bears shall stand in the name of his dead brother. The plain reading refers to a son born of the union who will carry on the dead brother's name. But the Sages read *habechor asher teled* as signifying that the one who chooses to marry this widow is himself granted quasi-*bechor* status. On this reading, his entitlement to a double portion flows not simply from representing his brother, but from being treated, in some sense, as a *bechor*. This is acknowledged as a considerable stretch of the plain meaning of the verse.

### Abaye Position

Abaye's view, as described above in the *mishnah* section, is that the *yavam*'s limitation is confined to the period before he performs *yibum*. Prior to *yibum*, the double portion to which he will eventually be entitled rests on the *bechor*-like status conferred by the Torah, and therefore follows the *bechor* rule: he takes no double portion of improvements occurring in that period. But once he has performed *yibum*, he genuinely stands in his brother's place, and any subsequent improvement is divided with him receiving the double share.

### Rava Position

Rava holds that the *yavam* never receives a double portion of improvements, even those occurring after *yibum*. The reasoning is the formal analogy to *bechor*: just as the *bechor* does not take a double portion of anything that accrued before the division, so too the *yavam*.

### Representing vs. Bonus

This dispute crystallizes a fundamental conceptual question: how do we understand the *yavam*'s double portion? Is it because he represents his dead brother—in which case, once *yibum* is performed, he holds two shares that are simply his own and his brother's, and improvements accrue to both equally? Or is it a *bechor*-like bonus conferred by the Torah as a kind of reward or incentive for performing *yibum*—in which case it is subject to all the same limitations as *bechor*?

If the former view is correct (Abaye), then after *yibum* the *yavam* is entitled to the improvements on both his own and his brother's share. If the latter view is correct (Rava), then his double portion, being structurally like that of a *bechor*, is capped at what existed in the estate at the time of the father's death—he gains nothing extra from *shevach* on the second portion, though of course he gains on his own. The same conceptual question—gift versus inheritance, *bechor*-bonus versus genuine representation—runs beneath the surface of the *yovel* discussion that follows.

## No Return in Yovel

### Mishnah List

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין חוֹזְרִין בַּיּוֹבֵל — הַבְּכוֹרָה, וְהַיּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ, וְהַמְיַיבֵּם אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו. וְהַמַּתָּנָה — דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר, וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: הַמַּתָּנָה כַּמֶּכֶר, רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם חוֹזְרִין בַּיּוֹבֵל, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אַף הַיּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ יַחְזִיר לִבְנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה, וִינַכֶּה מֵהֶן אֶת הַדָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these** are the people **whose** properties, unlike an ancestral field, **do not return** to their original owners **in the Jubilee** Year: The firstborn who inherited his father's property by the right of **primogeniture** need not return the extra portion for redistribution among the brothers; **and one who inherits his wife's** property need not return it to her family; **and one who consummates the levirate marriage with the wife of his brother** and gains the right to his brother's property need not return it for redistribution among the brothers. **And** likewise, **a gift** of land need not be returned to the original owners in the Jubilee Year; this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say:** The halakhic status of **a gift** is **like** that of **a sale,** and it must be returned. **Rabbi Elazar says: All these** lands **return in the Jubilee** Year. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: Even one who inherits his wife's** property **must return** the land **to the members of** her father's **family and should deduct from them the monetary** value of the land, as the Gemara will explain.

The next *mishnah* enumerates cases that do not revert in *yovel*: the *bechor*'s double portion, a husband who inherits his wife, and a *yavam* who does *yibum*. The assumption underlying the list is significant. We know that property acquired by inheritance does not revert in *yovel*—only sales do. But one might argue that the *bechor*'s extra portion is not true inheritance, and the *yavam*'s extra portion is likewise not a standard inheritance—perhaps these should revert. The *mishnah* tells us they do not.

Additionally, the *mishnah* cites *hamatana*—gifts—as Rabbi Meir's position: only sales revert, not gifts and not inheritances.

The Sages disagree and say *hamatana kemacher*: gifts do revert, just like sales. Only genuine inheritance is protected.

### Ben Beroka

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר? מֶכֶר הוּא דְּאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא לֶיהְדַּר בַּיּוֹבֵל, מַתָּנָה וִירוּשָּׁה לָא. וְהָנֵי מִיְּרוּשָּׁה — יְרוּשָּׁה, דְּמַתָּנָה — מַתָּנָה. בְּכוֹר — ״לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם״, מַתָּנָה קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of Rabbi Meir,** who holds that none of these items return in the Jubilee Year? The Gemara answers: **It is** with regard to land acquired specifically by means of **a sale that the Merciful One states** that it **should be returned during the Jubilee** Year (see Leviticus 25:28), whereas **a gift and an inheritance** are **not** returned. **And these** cases listed in the mishna that are derived **from** the *halakha* of **an inheritance** have the status of **an inheritance, and those** that are derived **from a gift** have the status of **a gift.** The Gemara elaborates: With regard to **a firstborn,** it is stated: **"By giving him a double portion"** (Deuteronomy 21:17); here **the Merciful One calls** the portion of a firstborn **a gift,** an item that is given.

Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka introduces a special case: if a man inherits his wife's property and *yovel* arrives, he must return it to the wife's family—but they must pay him for it. The *gemara* immediately notes the internal tension in this position. If he holds that a husband's inheritance of his wife is *de'oraita* (biblical), why must he return it at *yovel*? Property inherited biblically should not revert. And if he holds it is only rabbinic, why are they paying him—if it reverts by virtue of *yovel*, no payment should be necessary?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ לָקוֹחוֹת הֵן, וּמַחְזִירִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּיּוֹבֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance **are** considered **purchasers** from each other, i.e., one does not say they each received the exact inheritance to which they were entitled. **And** as purchasers of land, **they must return** the portions **to each other in the Jubilee** Year, at which point they redistribute the property.

The resolution is that he indeed holds the husband's inheritance is biblical. The return has nothing to do with *yovel* per se. Rather, the case involves a wife who held a family gravesite as part of her estate. He has now inherited a portion of the Klein family burial plot, introducing a stranger into what is fundamentally a family's sacred space. Because of *pgam mishpacha*—the affront to the family's honor—the Sages instituted that in such a case, the family may compel him to sell it back to them at *yovel*, and he is compensated. This is a special rabbinic enactment for the protection of family honor, not a standard *yovel* reversion.

### Rabbi Meir

Rabbi Meir holds that it is sales alone that the Torah mandates must revert in *yovel*. Gifts and inheritances are exempt. The textual basis: the Torah specifies the return of *mecher* (sale). Gifts and inheritances simply were not included. As for the specific cases in the *mishnah*: the *bechor*'s extra portion can be described either as an inheritance (since *pi shenayim* is structured alongside the regular portion) or as a gift (*latet lo*—to give him—suggests a bestowal). A husband's inheritance of his wife is, in Rabbi Meir's view, biblical. The *yavam*'s extra portion is called *bechor* by the Torah and is therefore at minimum a gift. Under either characterization, neither reverts.

### Rabbis Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: מַתָּנָה כְּמֶכֶר. מַאי טַעְמַיְיהוּ דְּרַבָּנַן? ״תָּשׁוּבוּ״ — לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמַּתָּנָה. וְהָנֵי כּוּלְּהוּ יְרוּשָּׁה נִינְהוּ. בְּכוֹר אָמַר קְרָא: ״לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם״, מַקִּישׁ חֵלֶק בְּכוֹרָה לְחֵלֶק פָּשׁוּט — מָה חֵלֶק פָּשׁוּט יְרוּשָּׁה, אַף חֵלֶק בְּכוֹרָה יְרוּשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches: **And the Rabbis say:** The halakhic status of **a gift** is **like** that of **a sale,** and must be returned. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason of the Rabbis?** The Gemara answers that the term **"you shall return"** in the verse: "In the Jubilee Year you shall return every man to his possession" (Leviticus 25:13), is superfluous, as it is already stated: "It shall be a Jubilee for you, and you shall return every man to his possession" (Leviticus 25:10). Therefore, this term serves **to include a gift,** which is also returned in the Jubilee Year. **And these** other cases listed in the mishna **are all** considered as **an inheritance,** and consequently they are not returned in the Jubilee Year. With regard to the husband's inheritance of his wife's property, the Rabbis, like Rabbi Meir, maintain that it applies by Torah law, and they agree that the *yavam* is considered like a firstborn with respect to his portion. As for **a firstborn, the verse states: "By giving him a double portion"** (Deuteronomy 21:17), and the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Meir, who derives from this verse that the portion of the firstborn has the status of a gift. Rather, they maintain that the verse **juxtaposes the** extra **portion of the firstborn to the portion** he receives as **an ordinary** brother: **Just as the portion** of **an ordinary** brother is not redistributed among the brothers in the Jubilee Year, but rather is **an inheritance,** as it is stated: "On the day that he causes his sons to inherit" (Deuteronomy 21:16), **so too the portion of the firstborn** is **an inheritance,** and is not returned in the Jubilee Year.

The Sages derive from the extra word *tashuvu*—you shall return—that gifts as well as sales revert in *yovel*. They must therefore find an alternative characterization for all the cases on the *mishnah*'s list that do not revert. Each must be classified as a genuine *yerusha*. The *bechor*'s extra portion: the verse says *latet lo pi shenayim*, assimilating his extra share to his regular share—*makish chelek bechor lechelek pashut*. Just as the regular portion is inheritance, so too the extra portion. The husband's inheritance of his wife is *de'oraita*, a genuine *yerusha*. The *yavam*'s double portion is called *bechor* by the Torah, and since *bechor* is *yerusha*, it too is *yerusha*. Every case on the list is reclassified as a form of *yerusha* and therefore exempt.

## No Return in Yovel (continued)

### Rabbi Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: כּוּלָּם חוֹזְרִים בַּיּוֹבֵל. סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי: ״תָּשׁוּבוּ״ לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמַּתָּנָה, וְהָנֵי כּוּלְּהוּ מַתָּנָה נִינְהוּ. בְּכוֹר — ״לָתֶת לוֹ פִּי שְׁנַיִם״, מַתָּנָה קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא. וְהַיּוֹרֵשׁ אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ — יְרוּשַּׁת הַבַּעַל דְּרַבָּנַן, וְהַמְיַיבֵּם אֶת אֵשֶׁת אָחִיו — ״בְּכוֹר״ קַרְיֵיהּ רַחֲמָנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna further teaches that **Rabbi Elazar says: All these** lands **return in the Jubilee** Year. The Gemara explains that Rabbi Elazar **holds in accordance with** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say** that the term **"you shall return"** serves **to include a gift, and** he further maintains that **these** cases listed in the mishna **are all** considered **gifts.** The Gemara elaborates: With regard to **a firstborn** it is stated: **"By giving him a double portion."** Through the use of the term "giving," **the Merciful One calls** his portion **a gift.** **And** with regard to **one who inherits his wife's** property, Rabbi Elazar maintains that **the inheritance of a husband** is **by rabbinic law,** and therefore it does not have the status of an inheritance that does not return in the Jubilee Year. **And** finally, **one who consummates the levirate marriage with the wife of his brother** returns the property for redistribution among the brothers, as **the Merciful One calls** him **a firstborn,** and Rabbi Elazar rules that the portion of the firstborn is returned in the Jubilee Year.

Rabbi Eliezer says *kulam chozrin beyovel*—all of them revert in *yovel*. He agrees with the Sages that *tashuvu* extends the reversion to gifts, and additionally holds that none of the cases on the list constitute genuine *yerusha*. The *bechor*'s extra portion: *latet lo pi shenayim*—the word *latet* means to give, it is a gift. The husband's inheritance of his wife: *yerushat haba'al derabbanan*—it is rabbinic, not a biblical inheritance, and therefore not a *yerusha* that exempts from *yovel*. The *yavam*'s extra portion: it is called *bechor* by the Torah, and since *bechor* is classified as a gift, it too is a gift.

This generates a pair of important conceptual questions that run throughout the discussion. First: is the *bechor*'s double portion a genuine inheritance or a gift? The fact that it is limited—*lav bera'uy kebemuchzak*—might suggest it is not fully an inheritance; or it might simply reflect a limitation on an otherwise genuine inheritance. Second: when the *meyabem* receives a double portion, is that because he represents his dead brother (making it straightforwardly an inheritance), or because the Torah grants him a *bechor*-like bonus? These two questions animate the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and the Sages, and the earlier dispute between Abaye and Rava turns on precisely the same axis.

## Brothers Division

### Yochanan Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָאַחִין שֶׁחָלְקוּ לָקוֹחוֹת הֵן, וּמַחְזִירִין זֶה לָזֶה בַּיּוֹבֵל. מוֹתֵיב רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין חוֹזְרִין בַּיּוֹבֵל — הַבְּכוֹרָה!
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: Brothers who divided** property received as an inheritance **are** considered **purchasers** from each other, i.e., one does not say they each received the exact inheritance to which they were entitled. **And** as purchasers of land, **they must return** the portions **to each other in the Jubilee** Year, at which point they redistribute the property. **Rav Hoshaya raises an objection** from the mishna: **And these** are the people **whose** properties **do not return** to their original owners **in the Jubilee** Year: The firstborn who inherited his father's property by **the** right of **primogeniture.**

The *gemara* cites Rabbi Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *ha'achim shechalaku lakochot hen*—brothers who divided an estate are considered purchasers of one another's shares, and must return their respective portions to the common pool at *yovel*. The reasoning is that by dividing the estate, each brother effectively "bought out" the others. If so, what they hold is not inherited property but purchased property, subject to *yovel* reversion.

This is a striking and extreme position. It means that whenever brothers divide their father's estate, the division is impermanent—at *yovel* the estate reconstitutes and must be divided anew. The practical stakes are obvious: if one brother's portion greatly appreciated while another's did not, the *yovel* reversion would force a redistribution, erasing the gains of the fortunate portion.

Tosafot notes that many authorities would classify brothers who divide as purchasers *in the context of inheritance*—that is, the division is still the operating mechanism of *yerusha*, and classifying them as *lakochot* for purposes of how rights are allocated does not necessarily mean that *yovel* must apply. It is Rabbi Yochanan alone who takes the position to its extreme conclusion: since they are purchasers, the entire division reverts at *yovel*.

### Elazar Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מַאי ״אֵין חוֹזְרִין״ — אֵין חוֹזְרִין לְבַטָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said to** Rav Hoshaya: **What** is the meaning of the clause: **They do not return?** It means that **they do not return for naught,** i.e., the firstborn does not forfeit the double portion; rather, when they redistribute the property he again receives a double portion.

Rabbi Elazar offers an interpretation that attempts to resolve the difficulty: *ein chozrin l'vatala*—they do not revert to be annulled. The phrase "does not revert in *yovel*" in the *mishnah* means that the *bechor*'s double portion is not permanently annulled. It does go back temporarily during *yovel*, but then the *bechor* retakes it. The *bechora* is not voided; it merely suspends for the duration of *yovel* and then reasserts itself.

### Sheshet Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מִכְּלָל דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר ״חוֹזְרִין״ — חוֹזְרִין לְבַטָּלָה קָאָמַר? קָרֵי רַב חָמָא עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַב שֵׁשֶׁת: ״טוֹבָה חׇכְמָה עִם נַחֲלָה״, וְלָא שְׁמִיעַ לְהוּ הָא דְּכִי אָתֵי רָבִין אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן שַׁמּוּעַ: מַאי ״חוֹזְרִין״ — חוֹזְרִין לְבַטָּלָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet objects to this: By inference** from this answer, should one conclude **that the one who says** the portion of the firstborn **returns** in the Jubilee Year **is saying** that **it returns for naught?** Why should the firstborn lose his double portion? **Rav Ḥama read** the verse **about Rav Sheshet: "Wisdom is good with an inheritance"** (Ecclesiastes 7:11), i.e., it is not enough to inherit knowledge of *baraitot*, as does Rav Sheshet; rather, one must also be expert in the wisdom of *amora'im*. Rav Ḥama explained: **Didn't** Rav Sheshet **hear this** matter, **that when Ravin came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, **he said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** says, **and some say** that **Rabbi Elazar said** it **in the name of Rabbi Elazar ben Shammua,** who is the *tanna* Rabbi Elazar in the mishna: **What** is the meaning of the clause: **They return** in the Jubilee Year? It means that **they return for naught,** and they forfeit that which they received.

Rav Sheshet objects: *michlal d'man d'amar chozrin chozrin l'vatala ka'amar?* Is it conceivable that any opinion would hold that the *bechor*'s portion goes back permanently—that after *yovel* the *bechor* simply loses his double portion for good? Surely the question was only whether it reverts temporarily, with the *bechor* retaking it afterwards.

Rabbi Chama responds by quoting the verse *tovah chochmah im nachalah*—wisdom together with inheritance is good—in pointed reference to Rav Sheshet, who was renowned for his mastery of *baraitot* but whose question here overlooked a well-known teaching. For Rabbi Yochanan himself—and in one version, Rabbi Elazar citing Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar—explicitly states *mai chozrin? Chozrin l'vatala!* Yes, according to those who say the *bechor*'s portion reverts, they mean it is permanently voided: the *bechor* does not reclaim it after *yovel*.

This emerges from Rabbi Yochanan's framework in which all brothers who divided are considered purchasers and everything goes back to the common estate at *yovel* to be redistributed. Under that framework, the question about the *bechor*'s extra portion is not whether it briefly suspends and then revives, but whether, when the estate is redistributed after *yovel*, the *bechor* once again receives his double portion. Rabbi Yochanan's extreme view apparently allows for the possibility that he does not—that the *yovel* reversion represents a genuine and permanent equalization.

## Family Graveplot

### Burial Plots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר יְרוּשַּׁת הַבַּעַל דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא, וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? כְּגוֹן שֶׁהוֹרִישַׁתּוּ אִשְׁתּוֹ בֵּית הַקְּבָרוֹת, וּמִשּׁוּם פְּגַם מִשְׁפָּחָה אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן: לִשְׁקוֹל דְּמֵי וְלַיהְדַּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka **holds** that **the inheritance of a husband** is **by Torah law, and what are we dealing with here?** We are dealing with a case **where his wife bequeathed to him** her family's **graveyard. And due to** the need to avoid **a family flaw,** i.e., harm to the family name, if the wife's family would be buried in plots belonging to others, **the Sages said he should take money** from them **and return** the graveyard.

Returning to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's ruling, the *gemara* locates it specifically in the case of a family burial plot. His wife held, as part of her estate, a section in a family catacomb. Upon her death, her husband inherited it. He now holds a portion of the Klein family plot—and the Kleins find themselves sharing their sacred family space with an outsider. It is because of *pgam mishpacha*—the affront to family honor—that the Sages instituted that he must return it at *yovel* and be compensated. This is not a standard *yovel* reversion; it is a targeted rabbinic enactment protecting the integrity of the family burial space.

### Baraita Sale

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִדְתַנְיָא: הַמּוֹכֵר קִבְרוֹ, וְדֶרֶךְ קִבְרוֹ, מְקוֹם מַעֲמָדוֹ, וּבֵית הֶסְפֵּדוֹ — בָּאִים בְּנֵי מִשְׁפָּחָה וְקוֹבְרִים אוֹתוֹ בְּעַל כׇּרְחוֹ, מִשּׁוּם פְּגַם מִשְׁפָּחָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** this is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* discussing *halakhot* connected with burial that serve to uphold family honor: In the case of **one who sells his grave, or the path** to **his grave,** or **the place** where visitors would **stand** to comfort the mourners, **or the place of his eulogies, the members of** his **family may come and bury him** in his ancestral plot **against** the purchaser's **wishes, due to** the need to avoid **a family flaw,** i.e., harm to the family name.

A supporting *baraita* broadens the principle beyond inheritance. If a man sold from his family burial plot—his own plot, the pathway to it, the area where the *mamadot* (public standing ceremonies) took place, and the *beit hesped* (eulogy space)—the family may come and bury him there against the buyer's will: *baei bnei mishpacha vekovrin oto al korcho*. The family's right to its burial space overrides the otherwise valid sale, because the honor of the family is at stake. They presumably compensate the buyer, but they are entitled to retake the space. This *baraita* operates entirely outside the *yovel* framework; the right to retake the plot is not contingent on *yovel* arriving. It rests entirely on *pgam mishpacha*.

### Burial Deduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאי ״יְנַכְּלוּ לָהֶם מִן הַדָּמִים״? דְּמֵי קֶבֶר אִשְׁתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what** is the meaning of the clause: **And deduct for them** part **of the monetary** value of the property? It means that he must deduct **the monetary** value **of his wife's grave** from the value of the field, as a husband is obligated to pay for his wife's burial (see *Ketubot* 47b).

Finally, the *gemara* explains the phrase in Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's ruling: *mah yenakeh lahem min hadamim*—what deduction does he make from the payment? The answer is *d'mei kever ishto*: the value of his wife's burial plot. A husband is obligated to bury his wife. If his wife is going to be buried in the family plot—as the family presumably wishes—then the family is effectively taking on the burial that was his financial responsibility. He therefore deducts the cost of her burial plot from the total they owe him. If the plot was worth ten thousand and contained ten sections, he collects ten thousand minus the value of the one section in which his wife will be interred, since he would have been obligated to pay for her burial in any case.
