# Shabbat 124 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kodem Gezeirat Keilim

### Gezeirat Keilim Overview

The daf opens at the bottom of 123b, in the midst of the discussion of *muktzah* as it applies to *keilim* — vessels that are ordinarily used and therefore not *muktzah*. To provide the necessary background: in the time of Nechemiah, owing to rampant *chilul Shabbat*, the Rabbis instituted a sweeping restriction known as *gezeirat keilim*, under which virtually no vessels could be handled on *Shabbat*. Over time there was a gradual pushback, and more and more vessels were permitted. By the period the *Gemara* addresses, the only remaining issues concern either highly specialized professional vessels that are never ordinarily moved, or vessels that fall into the category of *kli shemelachto l'issur* — a vessel whose primary function involves a *melachah* prohibited on *Shabbat* — for which certain restrictions on movement remain.

Against this backdrop, Rabbi Elazar proposes a sweeping historical framework to account for a series of otherwise puzzling *mishnayot*: *amar Rabbi Elazar*, kanim u-maklot, glustra u-maducha — reeds, poles, a door bolt, and a mortar — *kulan kodem l'gezeirat keilim nishnu*, all of these were taught during the ancient period before vessels were permitted. This is his method for dismissing four *mishnayot* that impose extremely restrictive limitations on the handling of these objects. The point is not merely that these *mishnayot* describe an outmoded stringency — it is that we do not rule in accordance with them at all, and the historical framing provides the explanation.

This approach of placing a historical framework on *Torah she-be-al peh* is not without precedent. The *Gemara* itself sometimes distinguishes between *Mishnah rishonah* and *Mishnah achronah*, acknowledging that an earlier teaching was later superseded. Here Rabbi Elazar applies an analogous logic to explain why certain *mishnayot* no longer represent operative *halacha*.

### Kanim - Lechem Hapanim

The first of the four *mishnayot* concerns the reeds used in the *lechem hapanim*. The *Mishnah* states: *lo sidur hakanim v'lo netilatan docheh et hashabbat* — neither the placing nor the removing of the reeds overrides *Shabbat*. This requires some explanation. In the *Beit Hamikdash*, the twelve loaves of *lechem hapanim* were arranged in two columns of six, with thin reeds interposed between each loaf to allow air circulation and prevent spoilage. These reeds functioned essentially as shelves, holding each loaf slightly above the one beneath it. On *Shabbat*, the procedure was to remove the old bread, place the new bread, and only after *Shabbat* reinsert the reeds — the reeds themselves were not arranged or removed on *Shabbat* itself.

The difficulty is evident: these reeds should be entirely permissible to handle. They are a classic *kli shemelachto l'heter* — a vessel whose function is entirely permitted. Why should they be restricted? Rabbi Elazar's answer is that this *Mishnah* originates from the period before *keilim* were permitted in any but the most basic circumstances, a time when even innocuous vessels could not be handled.

One might object: is there not a general rule that rabbinic prohibitions do not apply in the *Beit Hamikdash*? *Tosafot* addresses this directly, citing the principle *ein shevut b'mikdash* yet acknowledging that this rule is not absolute — some rabbinic restrictions did apply even there, and perhaps at this particular moment of crisis, when *chilul Shabbat* was so rampant, the Rabbis extended the restriction even into the *Beit Hamikdash*.

Rashi explains that the reeds were removed together with the bread on *Shabbat*, and then reinserted only after *Shabbat*. The concern reflected in the *Mishnah* thus makes sense specifically in a period when virtually all vessels were forbidden — a state of affairs that no longer applies.

### Maklot - Pesach Sacrifice

The second *mishnah* concerns the poles used for flaying the *Korban Pesach*. The *Mishnah* in *Pesachim* describes how thin, smooth poles were kept in the *Beit Hamikdash* for this purpose: one person would place a pole across his own shoulder and his companion's shoulder, suspend the *Korban Pesach* from it, and flay it. Rabbi Eliezer addresses the situation when *arba'ah asar she-chal lihyot be-Shabbat* — when *Erev Pesach* fell on *Shabbat*. Since the *Korban Pesach* could still be brought on *Shabbat*, the question was what accommodations had to be made. Rabbi Eliezer rules that one should not use the pole; instead, he says, *maniach yado al kteif chaveiro ve-yad chaveiro al kteifo* — one places his arm on the companion's shoulder and vice versa, and flays in that manner.

What is the problem with these poles? One might suggest they are *kli shemelachto l'issur*, but even then, the act of flaying is *l'tzorech gufo* — an entirely legitimate use. Rabbi Elazar's explanation is again that this ruling dates from the period when almost all *keilim* were forbidden — it is a holdover from the era of *gezeirat keilim* in the days of Nechemiah, when restrictions were near-universal.

### Glustra - Door Bolt

The third *mishnah* discusses a door bolt fitted with a rounded knob at its top — a *nagar she-yesh be-rosho glustra*. Rabbi Yehuda rules that one may *shomto min hapetach zeh v'toleh b'chaveiro b'Shabbat* — slip it from one door and attach it to another. Rabbi Dosa goes further and rules that it is simply like any vessel and may be moved freely in the courtyard.

The *Gemara* understands Rabbi Yehoshua's position (evidently the source behind Rabbi Yehuda's ruling) that one cannot move this bolt directly but must drag it, as though it cannot be lifted and carried in the ordinary manner. Rabbi Tarfon, however, permits it fully, stating *harei hu k'chol hakeilim* — it is like all other vessels, which implies that all other vessels are fully permitted. This makes it difficult to argue straightforwardly that this *mishnah* predates the general permission of *keilim*, since Rabbi Tarfon himself seems to presuppose that context. Perhaps the more compelling reading is that Rabbi Yehoshua's tradition was itself a holdover from an earlier period, while Rabbi Tarfon came to correct it — a fascinating instance of one Tanna reflecting an earlier sensibility that his colleague explicitly sought to supersede.

### Meducha - Mortar

The fourth case is the *maducha* — the mortar. The earlier discussion established that one may move a mortar only when it contains a clove of garlic. The question is: even if it is *kli shemelachto l'issur*, one should still be able to move it for its own legitimate purpose. Rabbi Elazar's historical framework provides the explanation — this too is a ruling from the pre-permissive era, and is therefore no longer operative law.

Thus all four *mishnayot* are accounted for under Rabbi Elazar's umbrella explanation: they are relics of a period before *hatarat keilim*, and we do not rule by them.

## Rava's Position on Keilim

### Rava - Post-Gezeirah Ves…

Rava rejects Rabbi Elazar's historical framework: *mi-mai*, what compelled you to explain all these *mishnayot* as predating the general permission of *keilim*? *Dilma le-olam achar hatarat kelim nishnu* — perhaps all four were taught after vessels were already permitted, and the restrictions each *Mishnah* reflects are explainable on independent grounds.

### Need Threshold for Reeds

Regarding the reeds of the *lechem hapanim*, Rava explains: *ta'ama mai mishum ivushai* — the reason reeds are used at all is to prevent the bread from going moldy. But *ve-hai purta*, for this brief interval — the new loaves resting without separation for only a portion of the day before the reeds are reinserted after *Shabbat* — the bread simply will not grow mold. There is therefore insufficient need to permit the use of the reeds. Similarly, for the poles used in flaying the *Korban Pesach*: *efshar k'Rabbi Elazar* — it is possible to manage without the poles, as the Tanna himself demonstrates by describing how people use their arms instead. The restriction therefore flows not from the era of blanket prohibition but from the absence of genuine need.

This raises a pointed question. Even granting that the need is not absolute, these reeds appear to be *kli shemelachto l'heter* — a vessel whose function is entirely permitted. Why should a mere deficiency in pressing need restrict the use of something in that category? This consideration has led some *poskim* to conclude that even a *kli shemelachto l'heter* is only permitted when there is some genuine purpose motivating its use — that the general permission of *keilim* after *hatarat keilim* was not a blanket license to handle vessels arbitrarily, but requires at least some threshold of *tzorech*. This is not the way the broad majority practice nor how the general assumption runs — we operate on the premise that a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be moved freely — but some *poskim* do invoke this *Gemara* as support for imposing at least a minimal threshold of purpose even in that category.

The case of the poles is worth noting in this connection: the poles may actually qualify as *kli shemelachto l'issur* to begin with, which introduces a separate set of considerations, distinct from the reeds.

### Glustra - Domain Analysis

As for the *glustra*, Rava invokes the explanation of the school of Yanai: *k'debei Yanai*, *bechatzer she-eina meureves askinan* — the case involves a courtyard that has not been combined into an *eruv*. The restriction on the bolt is therefore not a *muktzah* problem at all. It is a problem of domain. The bolt sits in the door frame, and the question is whether moving it constitutes carrying from the house into the courtyard.

Rabbi Yehoshua holds *tof hapetach k'dilifnim dami* — the door frame is considered part of the interior of the house. Consequently, moving the bolt from one door directly to another would constitute transferring it from the domain of the house into the courtyard, which is forbidden in a courtyard without an *eruv*. Rabbi Yehoshua's allowance of *shomto* — dragging — reflects an attempt to avoid a direct transfer, though the precise mechanism that makes dragging permissible is not entirely clear.

Rabbi Tarfon, by contrast, holds *tof hapetach k'dilachutz dami* — the door frame is considered part of the courtyard. Since the bolt is already in the courtyard domain, moving it from one door frame to another is simply moving an object within the courtyard, *mana d'chatzer bechatzer kamitaltel*, and is entirely permissible.

This elegantly resolves the problem without invoking *gezeirat keilim* at all — and indeed Rabbi Tarfon's phrase *harei hu k'chol hakeilim* now makes perfect sense as a statement about domain, not as evidence that vessels are already generally permitted.

As for the *meducha*, Rava's answer is simply: *Rabbi Nechemia hi* — that *Mishnah* reflects the position of Rabbi Nechemia, whom the *Gemara* is about to encounter in the next *Mishnah*, and whose approach to *muktzah* and the movement of vessels is considerably more restrictive than the majority view.

So to summarize: we have two approaches to the four restrictive *mishnayot*. Rabbi Elazar situates them historically before *hatarat keilim*. Rava explains each individually — insufficient need for the reeds, alternative method for the poles, a domain concern for the bolt, and Rabbi Nechemia's stringent position for the mortar. A third explanation, applicable in some contexts, is that a vessel can be so specialized or so bound to its professional function that it is genuinely *muktzah* — not merely *kli shemelachto l'issur* but fully set aside. These three categories — the historical period, Rabbi Nechemia's stringency, and genuine *muktzah* — represent the range of explanations available for restrictive rulings in this area.

## Kol Hakeilim Mishna

### Mishna Text - Vessel Mov…

The *Gemara* now turns to the next *Mishnah*: *kol hakeilim nitlin l'tzorech v'shelo l'tzorech* — all vessels may be moved both for a purpose and without a purpose. This echoes the formulation of the opening of the *perek*, *kol hakeilim*, and raises the same underlying question: does this apply to *kli shemelachto l'heter*, to *kli shemelachto l'issur*, or to both? Against this permissive majority view, *Rabbi Nechemia omer ein nitlin ela l'tzorech* — Rabbi Nechemia rules that vessels may only be moved for a purpose, and as the *Gemara* will clarify, purpose here means the vessel's designated function.

### Rava - Purpose Definitio…

The *Gemara* asks: *mai l'tzorech u'mai shelo l'tzorech*? What does it mean to move something "for a purpose" versus "without a purpose"? Rava explains: *l'tzorech* means *davar shemelachto l'heter l'tzorech gufo* — moving a *kli shemelachto l'heter* to use it for its own function. *Shelo l'tzorech* means *davar shemelachto l'heter l'tzorech mekomo* — moving it because it is in the way and you need its space. Both of these are subsumed under the permissive rule of the *Mishnah*. What remains forbidden, on the stricter position attributed here to Abaye, is moving even a *kli shemelachto l'heter* to protect it from damage — *mechama l'tzel*. And a *kli shemelachto l'issur* is one degree more constrained: it can be moved *l'tzorech gufo* — to use it for its legitimate permitted function, such as using a hammer to crack nuts — but not merely *l'tzorech mekomo*, not simply to clear it from a table.

### Rabbi Nechemia - Restric…

Rabbi Nechemia goes further: *afilu davar shemelachto l'heter, l'tzorech gufo in, l'tzorech mekomo lo* — even a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may only be moved for its own use and not merely to clear its space. This is the view attributed to Abaye in earlier discussions, and it is one degree more restrictive than the way we rule. We rule like Rava, according to whom a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be moved for any purpose whatsoever, and a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved *l'tzorech gufo* or *l'tzorech mekomo* — the only forbidden movement being for protection from damage.

### Melachto L'Heter vs. L'I…

The full schema, then, is as follows. According to Rava's ruling — which is the *halacha* — a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be moved for any reason: to use it, to clear its space, or even to protect it from rain or sun. A *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved to use it for a permitted function or to clear its space, but not to bring it in from the sun. Rabbi Nechemia shifts everything one degree more restrictive: a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be moved only for its own designated use or to clear its space, not for protection; and a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may be moved only for its own designated use. We do not rule like Rabbi Nechemia.

It is worth noting that Rabbi Nechemia's position, as it emerges in other *sugyot* beyond this one, is interpreted even more narrowly by some: that a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may be used only for its classic, primary function — not for some secondary or ad hoc use even if technically permitted. That reading does not emerge explicitly from the language of this particular *sugya*, but it informs how Rabbi Nechemia is understood in other contexts, and the *Gemara* acknowledges that there are different places where his view is discussed and different ways to reconcile them.

## Practical Applications

### Clearing Dishes Problem

The *Gemara* now proceeds to a series of practical challenges. Rav Safra, Rav Acha bar Huna, and Rav Huna bar Chinana were sitting and reasoning about what Rabba's position would be, specifically *aliba d'Rabbi Nechemia*: if even a *kli shemelachto l'heter* cannot be moved *l'tzorech mekomo*, then how do we clear the dishes from the table after a meal? The dishes themselves are *kli shemelachto l'heter* — they are ordinary vessels used for entirely permitted purposes — and yet according to Rabbi Nechemia, if one cannot move them simply because one needs their space, the table could never be cleared.

Rav Safra offered the answer: *midi d'hava agraf shel rei* — it is comparable to removing a chamber pot. The Rabbis recognized a special dispensation to move even *muktzah* objects that are disgusting, lest one be demeaned by their continued presence. Similarly, a table full of dirty dishes carries a degree of that logic. Shortly thereafter, Abaye raised this same question directly to Rabba himself, and Rav Safra reported the answer that the colleagues had discussed — attributing it to the group rather than to himself, a gesture of modesty in the presence of the master.

### Chamber Pot Exception

The chamber pot analogy merits brief attention. The rule of *agraf shel rei* represents a specific accommodation for objects whose presence is degrading — the Rabbis could not in good conscience require one to leave such things in place merely because of *muktzah* considerations. Applying that logic to the clearing of dishes extends it to situations of significant discomfort or unseemliness, though the precise scope of the analogy remains a subject of broader discussion.

### Mortar Pestle Cases

Abaye poses a challenge to Rabba from the case of the *meducha*: the *Mishnah* states that a mortar may only be moved when it contains a clove of garlic. If it is a *kli shemelachto l'issur*, there should nonetheless be grounds for moving it *l'tzorech gufo* or *l'tzorech mekomo*. Rabba responds: *hacha be'mai askinan mechama l'tzel* — the case in that *Mishnah* involves moving the mortar to protect it, which is the one category that remains forbidden. That is the only circumstance in which even *l'tzorech gufo* or *l'tzorech mekomo* does not suffice to permit moving a *kli shemelachto l'issur*.

### Pessal - Cutting Board

A parallel challenge is raised from the case of the *pessal* — a broad flat surface used as an improvised cutting board. Both Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel permit using it on *Yom Tov* as a cutting board for meat, in the spirit of *simchat Yom Tov*. But the *Mishnah* states that once one has cut meat on it, *asur letaltelo* — it may no longer be moved. Why not? It is *kli shemelachto l'issur*, but *l'tzorech gufo* or *l'tzorech mekomo* should still be permissible. The answer is again: *hacha be'mai askinan mechama l'tzel* — the case is one of protecting it from damage, and that remains forbidden.

### Log of Wood - Yom Tov

A further challenge: the *Mishnah* teaches that on *Yom Tov* one may not support a pot with a log of wood, and one may not support a door with it either. Now, a log of wood on *Yom Tov* is not *muktzah* in the full sense, since one may light fires on *Yom Tov* and use the log for that purpose. It ought therefore to be *kli shemelachto l'heter* — and if so, why may it not be used to support a pot? It is being used *l'tzorech gufo*. The answer the *Gemara* gives is: *keivan d'be'Shabbat davar shemelachto l'issur hu, gezeirah Yom Tov atu Shabbat* — since on *Shabbat* a log of wood is *kli shemelachto l'issur* (one cannot light fires), we apply by rabbinic decree the *Shabbat* status to *Yom Tov* as well. But this raises the deeper question of why even a *kli shemelachto l'issur* status should prevent use *l'tzorech gufo*, and the *Gemara* answers with a crucial distinction.

## Vessels vs. Non-Vessels

### Firewood Not a Kli

The *Gemara* introduces a foundational principle: *hani mili heicha d'ika torat kli alav* — all the rules about *kli shemelachto l'heter* and *kli shemelachto l'issur* apply only when the object in question carries the status of a *kli*, a vessel. *Heicha d'leika torat kli alav lo* — where something does not qualify as a *kli*, none of those rules apply and it is simply *muktzah*.

This is the key to understanding the log of wood. Our *perek* is *kol hakeilim* — it concerns vessels. Once an object qualifies as a *kli*, it falls within the domain of usable objects on *Shabbat*; at most its use may be limited to *l'tzorech gufo* and *l'tzorech mekomo* rather than *mechama l'tzel*. But if an object is not a *kli* — if it has no fashioned vessel character — it is fundamentally *muktzah*, not subject to the graduated permissions of *kli shemelachto l'issur* at all.

Firewood is not a *kli*. Its very purpose — being burned — removes it from the category of vessel. It is therefore *muktzah* outright on *Shabbat*, not merely *kli shemelachto l'issur*. If one designated a stick before *Shabbat* to serve as a tool for turning something over a spit, that might give it the character of a *kli shemelachto l'issur*, since it has been assigned a specific instrumental function. But designating wood for use as firewood does not make it a *kli* — firewood as such is not a vessel category. One cannot *meiyached* an object for a function that itself lacks the character of vessel use.

### Equating Shabbat-Yom Tov…

The *Gemara* now interrogates the mechanism by which the *Shabbat* status of firewood is applied to *Yom Tov*. The claim is that since firewood is *muktzah* on *Shabbat*, we apply a *gezeirah Yom Tov atu Shabbat* — a rabbinic extension of *Shabbat*'s strictness to *Yom Tov* — and therefore the firewood cannot be used on *Yom Tov* for purposes other than fire. The *Gemara* challenges this: *u'mi gazrinan* — do we truly equate *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* in this way?

### Fruit Skylight Example

The challenge comes from the *Mishnah*: *mashilin perot derech aruba b'Yom Tov aval lo b'Shabbat* — one may lower fruit through a skylight on *Yom Tov* but not on *Shabbat*. The practical case is fruit drying on a roof that is about to be ruined — on *Yom Tov* one may lower it through the skylight, which minimizes exertion, but this would not be permitted on *Shabbat*. This demonstrates that we are willing to maintain a distinction between *Yom Tov* and *Shabbat* and do not simply import every *Shabbat* stringency into *Yom Tov*. The *Gemara* responds: *u'mi lo gazrinan* — but we also do equate them in other respects, as the principle *ein bein Yom Tov l'Shabbat ela ochel nefesh bilvad* indicates that for everything unrelated to food preparation, the two days are treated equivalently — even at the rabbinic level.

### Well Animal Debate

Rav Yosef resolves the apparent contradiction: *lo kashya, ha Rabbi Eliezer, ha Rabbi Yehoshua* — it is a dispute between Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua, as demonstrated by the teaching about an animal and its offspring that fell into a well on *Yom Tov*. Rabbi Eliezer rules that one may pull up the first animal on condition of slaughtering it and must do so; for the second animal, one may only lower food but may not extricate it, since that effort exceeds what *simchat Yom Tov* demands. Rabbi Yehoshua permits a scheme: one declares intent to slaughter the first animal, pulls it up, then changes one's mind; declares intent to slaughter the second, pulls that one up as well; and then decides which one to slaughter. *Ratza zeh shochet ratza zeh shochet* — whichever one he then prefers, he may slaughter.

This *ha'aramah* — legal artifice — allows both animals to be extricated without directly violating a prohibition, since at each moment of lifting there is an ostensible intention to perform the permitted act of *shechitah*. It is worth noting that *tzaar ba'alei chayim* — preventing animal suffering — may well have been part of what motivated the lenient position, though the *Gemara* frames Rabbi Yehoshua's ruling in terms of permitted *ha'aramah*.

The upshot is that those who are more lenient about *Yom Tov* relative to *Shabbat* follow the approach of Rabbi Yehoshua, while the stricter equation of the two days reflects Rabbi Eliezer's stance. The *Gemara* then notes that this mapping cannot be pressed too far: perhaps Rabbi Eliezer is only strict about the animal case because there is an available alternative — one can lower food to sustain the animal in the well — whereas when no alternative exists he might be more lenient. Conversely, perhaps Rabbi Yehoshua permits the *ha'aramah* only because that particular framing is available; where no permissible framing exists, he might be stricter. So the two positions cannot automatically be exported to every *Yom Tov*/*Shabbat* comparison.

Rav Pappa offers the ultimate resolution: the whole question of whether we equate *Yom Tov* to *Shabbat* may simply reflect the dispute of Beit Shammai and Beit Hillel, which we will return to momentarily.

## Beit Shammai vs. Hillel

### Beit Shammai Restrictions

Rav Pappa's resolution invokes an additional *Mishnah*: Beit Shammai says *ein motzi'in et hakatan v'et halulav v'et sefer Torah l'reshut harabbim* — one may not carry a child, a *lulav*, or a *Sefer Torah* into the public domain on *Yom Tov*. This is striking. We might assume there is no issue of carrying on *Yom Tov*, yet Beit Shammai applies restrictions even to objects that seem to involve a *tzorech mitzvah*. The implicit argument of Beit Shammai is that carrying on *Yom Tov* is constrained in ways quite similar to *Shabbat*, at least outside the specific domain of food preparation. Beit Hillel permits this carrying.

### Carrying-Muktzah Link

The *Gemara* now pushes back on Rav Pappa's resolution. Even granting that Beit Shammai is strict about *hotza'ah* — carrying in the *d'oraita* sense — why would that make him strict about *tiltul*, the rabbinic concern of *muktzah*? The reasoning that restricts carrying need not automatically generate a *muktzah* restriction, since the two operate on different levels. At this juncture the *Gemara* introduces one of the most revealing lines of the entire discussion.

### Tiltul-Hotza'ah Connecti…

*V'tiltul gufeih lav mishum hotza'ah hu?* — Is not *tiltul* itself connected to *hotza'ah*? Rashi explains: *hilkhach im mezalzel b'tiltul, mezalzel b'hotza'ah* — if one becomes careless about moving objects, one will come to carry them into a public domain. This single line illuminates the underlying rationale for *muktzah*: at least according to this *Gemara*, the prohibition against moving certain objects on *Shabbat* serves as a fence protecting against inadvertent *hotza'ah*. If one maintains self-discipline about what objects one touches and moves on *Shabbat*, that same mindset will curb one from casually carrying things outside.

Some *Rishonim* understood *muktzah* entirely in these terms — as a fence for carrying. Others explain *muktzah* through the concept of *hekhin*, derived from *v'hekhinu et asher yavi'u* — preparing beforehand. The objects one normally uses are treated as if designated for *Shabbat* use, while objects outside one's routine consciousness are not. Still others, notably the Rambam, ground *muktzah* in the need to make *Shabbat* feel qualitatively different, adding an element of taboo and distinctiveness to the day. And the *Gemara* itself earlier connected *gezeirat keilim* to the social crisis of widespread *chilul Shabbat*, suggesting that restrictions on vessels served a communal psychological function — creating a sense of the inviolate character of *Shabbat*.

What is particularly striking is how thoroughly *muktzah* has come to dominate the popular imagination of *Shabbat* restrictions. Children often attribute every prohibition on *Shabbat* to *muktzah*, which is in a sense an intuitive appreciation — however imprecise — of the way *muktzah* creates a pervasive sense of *Shabbat* as a different order of existence. Half of *Masekhtet Shabbat* concerns carrying and the other major portion concerns *muktzah*, and the *Gemara* here draws a direct line between the two.

## Rav's Melachto L'Issur

### Rav - Mar (Hoe) Case

The *Gemara* now affirms that Rav himself holds in accordance with Rava's lenient position on *kli shemelachto l'issur*. The evidence: *De'amar Rav*, Rav said that if one has a *mar* — a hoe or shovel — and wishes to move it *shelo yiganev*, so it should not be stolen, that is *tiltul shelo l'tzorech v'assur* — moving without purpose and forbidden. This represents the one forbidden category: moving a *kli shemelachto l'issur* to protect it from damage or theft. But the very framing of the prohibition confirms the positive: *aval l'tzorech gufo ul'tzorech mekomo mutar* — for its own use or to clear its space, a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may certainly be moved. Rav thus aligns with Rava.

### Shutza Net Story

Additional evidence comes from a narrative: Rav Kahana came to the house of Rav, and Rav instructed his servants *aisu lei shutza l'Kahana* — bring a net (a *kli shemelachto l'issur*) for Kahana to sit on. The *Gemara* reads significance into the phrasing: did Rav not deliberately specify the purpose — *l'tzorech gufo*, for Kahana's seating — to underscore that while use is permitted, moving without need would not be? The implication would be that *l'tzorech mekomo* alone would not be sufficient, contrary to what we have established.

### Net Protection Context

The *Gemara* resolves this in two ways. First: perhaps what Rav actually said was *shiklu shutza mikamei Kahana* — remove this net from before Kahana — which would make it a case of *l'tzorech mekomo*, clearing the space, and Rav was demonstrating that this too is permitted. Second: *iba'is eima, hasam mechama l'tzel hava* — the net was out in the yard and would have been damaged by exposure; Rav deliberately emphasized the purpose of sitting in order to frame the movement as *l'tzorech gufo* and thereby avoid any confusion about the sole category that is forbidden — moving a *kli shemelachto l'issur* merely to protect it from damage. The story thus confirms, rather than undermines, the permissibility of both *l'tzorech gufo* and *l'tzorech mekomo* for *kli shemelachto l'issur*.

## Cushion Case

### Rav Mari - Sun Question

Rav Mari bar Rachel had a cushion — a *kli shemelachto l'heter* — lying in the sun. He came before Rava and asked *mahu letiltulei* — may he move it out of the sun? Rava responded immediately: *shari*, it is permitted.

### Rava Permits - Questione…

Rav Mari then began introducing qualifications: he did not really need it, he had another cushion available. Rava brushed this aside: *chazya orchin*, perhaps you will have guests. Rav Mari pressed further — he had more than enough cushions for guests too. At this point the exchange becomes somewhat pointed.

### Gilui Da'at - Strict View

Rava said to him: *galya da'atach k'Rava svira lach* — by your persistent resistance you are demonstrating that you yourself hold the stricter view, that even a *kli shemelachto l'heter* may not be moved *mechama l'tzel*. I hold the permissive position. For anyone else who asks, it is *mutar*; *bedidi asar* — if you want to impose the stringency on yourself, that is your prerogative. This is a classic expression of *gilui da'at* — one's own behavior revealing one's operative conviction — and Rava's response is both pointed and principled: a personal stringency is valid, but it does not become the universal *psak*.

## Brooms - Cloth vs. Frond

### Broom Types Distinction

Rabbi Abba citing Rabbi Chiyya bar Ashi citing Rav: *machbadot shel milta* — cloth rags or cloth brooms used to wipe off surfaces — are *kli shemelachto l'heter* and are *mutar letiltalan b'Shabbat*, permitted to be moved on *Shabbat*. *Aval shel tmara* — brooms made of fronds — since their primary use is sweeping dirt floors, which is forbidden on *Shabbat* because it would level the ground, they are *kli shemelachto l'issur* and may not be moved. *V'Rabbi Eliezer omer af shel tmara* — Rabbi Eliezer says even the frond broom may be moved.

### Frond Broom Prohibition

The *Gemara* asks: in what context does Rav prohibit the frond broom? If the concern is *l'tzorech gufo* or *l'tzorech mekomo*, we have already established that Rav holds like Rava and permits a *kli shemelachto l'issur* in both of those cases. So why would Rav restrict the frond broom?

### Protection - Not Purpose

The answer is that the prohibition must be referring specifically to *mechama l'tzel* — moving the frond broom to protect it from damage. That is the one case in which even a *kli shemelachto l'issur* may not be moved, and that is precisely the scenario Rav addresses. The formulation of the ruling thereby confirms the entire framework: Rav restricts the frond broom only in the context of protection, consistent with his general position that *l'tzorech gufo* and *l'tzorech mekomo* remain permitted.

### Rabbi Eliezer Agreement

As for Rabbi Eliezer's statement that even the frond broom is permitted — the *Gemara* resolves a textual difficulty by proposing a change in reading: rather than having Rabbi Eliezer dispute Rav's restriction, he should be understood as agreeing with it: *v'chen amar Rabbi Eliezer* — and so too Rabbi Eliezer agrees that in the context of *mechama l'tzel* it is forbidden. Both authorities thus align in permitting movement of *kli shemelachto l'issur* for use or space, and in restricting it when the sole motive is protection from damage.

## Broken Vessels

### Mishna - Broken Parts

The *Gemara* turns to the next *Mishnah*: *kol hakeilim hanitlin b'Shabbat, shivreihen nitlin imahen* — all vessels that may be moved on *Shabbat*, their broken pieces may also be moved. The qualification: *uvilvad shehen osin me'ein melacha* — as long as the broken pieces can still perform some function. The examples given are a broken kneading trough whose shards can be used to cover a barrel, and broken glass that can be used to cover a small flask. In each case, the piece has some residual utility and therefore retains sufficient character as a *kli* to avoid *muktzah*.

### Rabbi Yehuda - Original…

Rabbi Yehuda imposes a stricter standard: *bilvad shehu osin me'ein melachtam* — the broken pieces must be capable of performing a function similar to their original purpose. A shard of the kneading trough must be large enough to hold porridge, as the original vessel held dough. A piece of broken glass must retain enough curvature to hold oil, as the original bottle held liquids. Rabbi Yehuda requires a functional continuity with the original vessel's character; mere general utility is insufficient.

### Breaking Time Debate

Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel clarifies that the dispute between the *Tanna kamma* and Rabbi Yehuda applies specifically to vessels that broke on *Erev Shabbat*. One opinion holds that what matters is whether the broken piece can do some portion of the original function; the other holds that any function suffices. However, if the vessel broke on *Shabbat* itself — *divrei hakol mutarin* — all agree the broken pieces are permitted, as long as they retain some use. The reasoning: *ho'il u'muchanim agav avihen* — since the vessel was not *muktzah* when *Shabbat* began, its permitted status adheres to the pieces as well.

### Broken Vessels as Firewo…

Rav Zutra raises a challenge from a case involving *Yom Tov*: the ruling that on *Yom Tov* one may use whole vessels as firewood — *masikin b'keilim* — but not broken vessel pieces — *v'ein masikin b'shivrei keilim*. When did these pieces break? If they broke before *Yom Tov*, *eitzim b'alma havu* — they are simply wood, and why should they not be usable as firewood? It must be that they broke on *Yom Tov* itself. Yet the ruling restricts their use — which contradicts the claim that breaking on *Shabbat* or *Yom Tov* makes things *easier* because the original permitted status adheres.

### Nolad - New Creation

The *Gemara* resolves this by inverting the framework: *ela i itmar, hachi itmar* — the original formulation must be reversed. Rav Yehuda citing Shmuel actually says that the dispute applies when vessels broke on *Shabbat*, and breaking on *Shabbat* makes things *harder*, not easier. The reason: if the vessel changed its character on *Shabbat*, there is a concern of *nolad* — a new entity came into existence on *Shabbat*. One opinion holds that the broken pieces are still considered *muchan*, prepared, since the vessel was permitted at the outset of *Shabbat*, and the pieces retain that status as long as they still perform some portion of the original function. The other opinion holds that if the pieces can only perform a *different* function from the original vessel, they constitute *nolad* — something genuinely new has come into being — and are therefore *muktzah*.

By contrast, if the vessel broke *Erev Shabbat*, *divrei hakol mutarin* — all agree the broken pieces may be used — since the *nolad* concern is absent: the breakage occurred before *Shabbat* began, the pieces had some function when *Shabbat* entered, and there is no newly created entity on *Shabbat* itself. The discussion will continue with further elaboration of these distinctions in the next daf.
