# Bava Metzia 24 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Makom as Siman

### Makom - Siman or Not

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב בִּיבִי מֵרַב נַחְמָן: מָקוֹם הָוֵי סִימָן, אוֹ לָא הָוֵי סִימָן? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּנֵיתוּהָ, מָצָא חָבִיּוֹת שֶׁל יַיִן וְשֶׁל שֶׁמֶן וְשֶׁל תְּבוּאָה וְשֶׁל גְּרוֹגְרוֹת וְשֶׁל זֵיתִים – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּמָקוֹם הָוֵי סִימָן, לִכְרוֹז מָקוֹם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Bibi raised a dilemma before Rav Naḥman: Is** the **location** where the lost item was found **a distinguishing mark, or is it not a distinguishing mark? Rav Naḥman said to him** that **you learned it** in the *baraita*: If one **found barrels of wine, or of oil, or of grain, or of dried figs, or of olives, these** belong **to him. And if it enters your mind that location is a distinguishing mark, let** the finder **proclaim** what he found, and have the **location** serve as a distinguishing mark.

The daf begins at the bottom of 28b, picking up from the debate between Abaye and Rava regarding *yeush shelo mida'at*. The ruling follows Abaye: *yeush shelo mida'at lo havei yeush* — we require that the owner be consciously aware of the loss before *yeush* can take effect. The preceding discussion had examined why sheaves found in *reshus harabbim* differ from those found in *reshus hayachid*, with two competing rationales: either *makom* (location) is a *siman* but loses that status when objects get kicked around in a public thoroughfare, or the *siman* on the sheaves themselves would be destroyed by trampling. The working assumption favored by Rava is that *makom* generally constitutes a valid *siman* — but only for objects that were intentionally left behind rather than accidentally dropped, and only when they would not be displaced.

With this backdrop, Rav Bibi posed a direct question to Rav Nachman: *makom havi siman o lo havi siman?* — is location a *siman* or not? Rav Nachman replied: *tani tu* — the answer is implicit in the *braisa* we just studied.

### Barrels - Braita Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב זְבִיד, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּרַקְּתָא דְנַהֲרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid said: With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with the case of a barrel that was found **on the bank of the river.** Since it is the place where ships dock and merchandise belonging to many people is loaded and unloaded, the bank of a river cannot serve as a distinguishing mark.

The *braisa* states: *matza chaviyot shel yayin veshel shemen veshel tvuah veshel grogrot veshel zeitim* — if one finds barrels of wine, oil, grain, pressed figs, or olives, *harey eilu shelo* — he may keep them. These are standard, undistinguished containers filled to standard capacity, so neither the barrel nor the quantity functions as a *siman*. The *braisa*'s ruling that the finder keeps them appears to challenge Rava's position. These are not objects that slip from a pocket or get kicked around; a large barrel of olives is something left behind deliberately. If *makom* were a valid *siman*, the *braisa* should have required the finder to announce where the barrels were found. The fact that it does not suggests that *makom* is not a *siman* — directly against Rava.

### Port Exception

Rav Zavid rose to defend Rava: *hacha b'mai askinan b'riksa denahara* — the *braisa* is speaking specifically about a port, the loading area at a riverbank where ships are loaded and unloaded. In such a location, saying "I found a barrel of olives at the port" is not a meaningful identifier, since barrels of every kind are routinely left there. In general circumstances, however, *makom* would remain a valid *siman*, and Rava's position stands.

### Port Not Siman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב מָרִי: מַאי טַעְמָא אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן רַקְּתָא דְנַהֲרָא לָא הָוֵי סִימָן? דְּאָמְרִינַן לֵיהּ: כִּי הֵיכִי דְּאִתְרְמִי לְדִידָךְ אִתְרְמִי נָמֵי לְחַבְרָךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Mari said: What is the reason that the Sages said** that in the case of a lost item, the location of **the bank of a river is not a distinguishing mark?** It is **because we say to** one seeking to reclaim his item by providing its location on the bank of a river: **Just as it happened** that **you** lost an item there, **it also happened** that **another** person lost an item there.

Rav Mari then asked: *mai ta'ama amru rabbanan riksa denahara lo havi siman?* — why exactly is the port not a valid *siman*? Because, he explains, *ki heichi de'itrami lidiydach hai makom itrami nami l'chavrach* — just as it so happened that you left your barrel of wine there, your neighbor equally could have left his barrel of wine in the same spot. There is nothing distinctive about any given person having left something at the port.

A second formulation of Rav Mari's position takes the conclusion further: this is not merely a special exception for ports but a general principle that *makom* is never a *siman*, because for any location one might name, the same logic applies — another person could equally have left a similar object there. One might press back and note that the intersection of a specific location and a specific object should together form a *siman* even if neither alone would suffice. This is a genuine tension. Yet where objects are common — a twenty-dollar bill on a street corner, for instance — the combination still may not provide meaningful identification. That question was raised and left open.

The practical upshot is two competing readings: either the *braisa* demonstrates that *makom* is generally not a *siman*, or it represents only the exceptional case of the port. Rava's ruling — that *makom* is a valid *siman* for objects that were left (not dropped) and do not get displaced — remains the baseline.

### Pitch Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַשְׁכַּח כּוּפְרָא בֵּי מַעֲצַרְתָּא, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל לְנַפְשָׁךְ. חַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה קָא מְחַסֵּם, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל פְּלוֹג לֵיהּ לְחִיָּיא בְּרִי מִינֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who found pitch** near **the winepress. He came before Rav** to ascertain what he should do with the pitch. Rav **said to him: Go take** the pitch **for yourself.** Rav **saw that** the man **was hesitating,** uncertain that he was entitled to the pitch. Rav, in an attempt to allay his qualms, **said to him: Go divide it with Ḥiyya my son,** as Rav would certainly not want his son to take a share of a stolen item.

The *Gemara* now introduces a case: *hahu gavra de'ashkach kufra be'ma'atzarta* — a certain person found pitch in the processing area, the place where pitch would have been worked. He came before Rav, who told him: *zil shekol lenafshacha* — go and keep it. Presumably there was no meaningful *siman* on the pitch, and even if *makom* could be a *siman* in other contexts, this was the sort of location — a common processing area — where many people could have left similar material, analogous to the port scenario.

Rav noticed the man hesitating and said: *zil pelog lei le'Chiya beri minei* — give half to my son. The offer served both to reassure the hesitant finder that the ruling was sound and to demonstrate that Rav himself had no qualms about it.

### Yeush - Weeds Indicate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא קָא סָבַר רַב מָקוֹם לָא הָוֵי סִימָן? אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא: מִשּׁוּם יֵאוּשׁ בְּעָלִים נָגְעוּ בָּהּ, דַּחֲזָא דְּקָדְחִי בֵּיהּ חִלְפֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that Rav holds that location is not a distinguishing mark. Rabbi Abba said:** That is not Rav's reasoning. Rather, it is **due to** the **despair of its owner** that the Sages **touched upon** this matter and permitted the finder to keep such a found item. **As,** Rav **saw that grass was growing through** the pitch, indicating that it had been there for an extended period.

The *Gemara* pauses to ask: *lima kasavar Rav makom lo havei siman?* — does this mean Rav held that *makom* is never a *siman?*

Rav Abba clarifies: *mishum ye'ush be'alim nagu ba* — no, the ruling was not about the *siman* at all, but about *yeush*. The owner had clearly abandoned hope of recovering it. How do we know? *De'chaza de'kadchei ba chalfi* — weeds had grown over the pitch, indicating it had been sitting there undisturbed for a long time.

This raises a delicate question: at what point does elapsed time and visible neglect translate into presumed *yeush*? One might note that the more uniquely identifying a *siman* is, the longer an owner might reasonably hold out hope — a wallet with a name inside is a different case from a generic lump of pitch. Conversely, where the only potential *siman* is *makom*, the threshold for presumed *yeush* may be lower. The episode is instructive in another way as well: even where the objective circumstances appear to satisfy the conditions for return — a *makom* that might function as a *siman* — the *Gemara* can look past those indicators when the context demonstrates that *yeush* has already occurred. This is consistent with the Abaye/Rava framework: we care about the owner's actual (or objectively presumed) state of mind, not merely the formal presence of a *siman*.

## New Vessels - Siman

### Anporya Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר וְכוּ׳. מַאי אַנְפּוּרְיָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כֵּלִים חֲדָשִׁים שֶׁלֹּא שְׂבָעָתַן הָעַיִן.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says:** If one finds any *anpurya* vessels he is not obligated to proclaim his find. The Gemara asks: **What are *anpurya*** vessels? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** They are **new vessels, as the eye** of its purchaser has **not** yet **sufficiently seen them** to be able to recognize them.

The *Gemara* returns to the *Mishnah*, where Rabban Shimon ben Elazar rules that *klei anporya* may be kept by the finder. The *Gemara* asks: *mai anporya?* Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel explains: *kelim chadashim she'lo sav'atan ha'ayin* — new vessels or garments whose appearance the eye has not yet become fully accustomed to. The concept is that a brand-new object has not yet been in the owner's possession long enough for the owner to develop *tvi'at ayin* — the deep recognition that comes from prolonged familiarity — and therefore even the owner cannot confidently identify it as his beyond all doubt. Exactly how one measures this threshold is not entirely clear. Has the person at least taken it out of the box? Hung it in the closet still in its packaging? The *Gemara* will attempt to work through what this means in practice.

### Taviyas Ayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי אִית בְּהוּ סִימָן – כִּי לֹא שְׂבָעָתַן הָעַיִן מַאי הָוֵי? אִי דְּלֵית בְּהוּ סִימָן – כִּי שְׂבָעָתַן הָעַיִן מַאי הָוֵי? לְעוֹלָם דְּלֵית בְּהוּ סִימָן – נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לְצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן בִּטְבִיעוּת עֵינָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If there is a distinguishing mark on** the vessels, **when the eye** of its purchaser has **not** yet **sufficiently seen them, what of it?** He can describe the mark after even a short glance and claim his item. **If there is no distinguishing mark on** the vessels, then **when the eye** of the one who purchases them has **sufficiently seen them, what of it?** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** it is a vessel **in which there is no distinguishing mark, and the** practical **difference** is with regard **to returning** the vessel **to a Torah scholar** on the basis **of visual recognition.**

The *Gemara* probes the case structure: *ei de'it beh siman* — if the object has a *siman*, then *tvi'as ayin* adds nothing; the owner simply provides the *siman* and the object is returned. *Ei deleit beh siman* — if it has no *siman*, then what does recognition accomplish? How can we trust the claimant? This is precisely the long-running difficulty of *tvi'as eina* — personal visual recognition in the absence of a formal identifying mark. The answer comes sharply: *le'olam deleit beh siman*, there is indeed no *siman* — and the *nafka mina* is *le'ahadurei letzurba me'rabanan betvi'at eina* — one may return the object to a *talmid chacham* based on his declaration that he recognizes it, even without any *siman*.

### Talmid Chacham Trust

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׂבָעָתַן הָעַיִן – קִים לֵיהּ בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ וּמַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ. כִּי לֹא שְׂבָעָתַן הָעַיִן – לָא קִים לֵיהּ בְּגַוַּיְיהוּ, וְלָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּהָנֵי תְּלָת מִילֵּי עֲבִידִי רַבָּנַן דִּמְשַׁנּוּ בְּמִלַּיְיהוּ – בְּמַסֶּכֶת, וּבְפוּרְיָא
>
> **Translation:** When **the eye** of a Torah scholar has **sufficiently seen them he is certain about them, and we return** a lost item **to him** on the basis of his description of the vessel. **When the eye** of a Torah scholar has **not sufficiently seen them, he is not certain about them, and we do not return** a lost item **to him, as Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says: With regard to these three matters** alone, it **is normal** for **Sages to amend their statements** and deviate from the truth: **With regard to a tractate,** if he is asked whether he studied a particular tractate, he may humbly say that he did not, even if he did. **And with regard to a bed,**

This is a striking ruling. Ordinary individuals must be treated with suspicion — they might convince themselves that something is theirs when it is not, or simply misrepresent. But a *talmid chacham* can be trusted to assert recognition honestly. One might ask whether "any person one personally trusts completely" could be treated similarly — and indeed there are analogues in *halacha*, as Rav Moshe addresses in a well-known *teshuva* regarding *ne'emanut* in the context of *kashrus* — but the *Gemara* here is speaking of *talmidei chachamim* specifically.

The reason for this heightened trust emerges from a statement of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *hanei telat milei avidi rabanan demishanu bemileihu* — there are only three matters in which the rabbis will deviate from strict truth, and therefore in all other areas they may be trusted completely. Those three exceptions are: *be'masechet* — regarding one's learning, where modesty may cause a *talmid chacham* to say he has not studied something he has; *uve'puraya* — regarding the bed (Rashi suggests a question about one's intimate life; Tosafos offers an interpretation related to uncertain conditions of the bed; the *Maharsha* wittily proposes that *puraya* means *Purim*, since the *Gemara* requires one to become intoxicated *ad d'lo yada bein arur haman l'baruch mordechai*, and a *talmid chacham* may claim not to know that distinction even without full intoxication); and *uve'ushpiza* — regarding one's host, where one should downplay a host's generosity to prevent others from taking advantage of him.

One might ask why we need to demonstrate the general honesty of *talmidei chachamim* at all. Even a perfectly honest person might inadvertently convince himself that something is his when it is not — *that looks like mine* is not the same as *that is mine*. The answer seems to be that a person who is rigorously careful about truth in all other domains will not allow that kind of self-deception to creep in either, whereas someone who regularly bends the truth for social smoothness may lower his own internal standards without realizing it.

### Aveidot Expansion

The *nafka mina* of this entire discussion is more sweeping than it first appears. According to Rabban Shimon ben Elazar, if an object has reached the stage where the owner could develop *tvi'as ayin* on it, one must retain it even without a *siman* — because a *talmid chacham* might come and claim it by recognition. This radically expands the universe of *aveidot* that a finder is obligated to hold. Forget the entire framework of *simanim*: any object that is in principle the kind of thing a person would come to recognize, found in a place where *talmidei chachamim* are commonly present, must be kept in case such a person appears to identify it. A ישיבה neighborhood, for example, would fall under this obligation across a wide range of objects.

Tosafos pushes further: the reason the *Mishnah* listed items like *krichot*, *igulei dveila*, and similar food bundles as things the finder may keep is precisely because those are not the kind of things anyone develops *tvi'as ayin* on. The implication is that for objects which are recognizable by nature — *devarim she'darkan lihyot bahen tvi'ut ayin* — one is obligated to announce them even if the owner has not had the object long enough to recognize it, provided the find occurs *b'makom d'shchichi rabanan*, in a place where *talmidei chachamim* frequent.

### Three Exceptions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וּבְאוּשְׁפִּיזָא. מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ? אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: לְאַהְדּוֹרֵי לֵיהּ אֲבֵידְתָּא בִּטְבִיעוּת עֵינָא. אִי יָדְעִינַן בֵּיהּ דְּלָא מְשַׁנֵּי אֶלָּא בְּהָנֵי תְּלָת – מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ, וְאִי מְשַׁנֵּי בְּמִילֵּי אַחֲרִינֵי – לָא מַהְדְּרִינַן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he can lie **with regard to a host [*ushpiza*],** as one may say that he was not well received by a certain host to prevent everyone from taking advantage of the host's hospitality. **What is the** practical **difference** that emerges from this statement with regard to matters in which Torah scholars deviate from the truth? **Mar Zutra says:** The practical difference is with regard **to returning a lost item on** the basis of **visual recognition. If we know about him that he alters** his statements **only with regard to these three** matters, **we return** the lost item **to him, but if he alters** his statements **with regard to other matters, we do not return** the lost item **to him.**

What is peculiar about this entire framework is the inversion it creates relative to the *Mishnah*'s earlier discussion. Rabban Shimon ben Elazar, one might assume, is the *machmir* voice here — the one who requires the finder to hold on to more objects. But in Tosafos's reading, it is actually the *rabbanan* who are more demanding: they may hold that for recognizable objects in places where *talmidei chachamim* are present, one must retain the object even before any *tvi'as ayin* could have developed. The position of the *chachamim* as reconstructed from the *Gemara*'s *modeh* formulation — *modeh Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* implying the *rabbanan* require announcement even where he does not — is genuinely puzzling, and the *Gemara* does not resolve it entirely cleanly.

The paradigmatic cases of *klei anporya* that Rabban Shimon ben Elazar says may be kept are things like *badei machin*, strings or branches from which needles are suspended, along with similar clustered, still-packaged assemblages. The common thread seems to be that they have not yet been unwrapped or separated for use — analogous to a purchase still in its original packaging. Once the constituent items are taken off their mounting and used individually, the owner will have developed enough familiarity with them to recognize them. Until then, the set is interchangeable with any other such set, and no *tvi'as ayin* can form.

## Honest vs. Thief

### Stolen Goblet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא חֲסִידָא אִגְּנִיב לֵיהּ כָּסָא דְכַסְפָּא מֵאוּשְׁפִּיזָא. חַזְיֵאּ לְהָהוּא בַּר בֵּי רַב דְּמָשֵׁי יְדֵיהּ וְנָגֵיב בִּגְלִימָא דְחַבְרֵיהּ. אֲמַר: הַיְינוּ הַאי דְּלָא אִיכְפַּת לֵיהּ אַמָּמוֹנָא דְחַבְרֵיהּ. כַּפְתֵיהּ וְאוֹדִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **A silver goblet was stolen from the host of Mar Zutra Ḥasida.** Mar Zutra **saw a certain student** of Torah **who washed his hands and dried** them **on the cloak of another.** Mar Zutra **said: This is** the one **who does not care about the property of another. He bound** that student, **and** the student then **confessed** that he stole the goblet.

The *Gemara* now presents a vignette that sits in productive tension with everything just said about *talmidei chachamim*. Mar Zutra Chasida had a silver goblet stolen from his inn. He observed a *bar bei rav* — a student of the rabbis — *demashi yadei venagiv beglima dechavreih* — who washed his hands and then dried them on someone else's cloak. Mar Zutra said: *hainu hai d'lo ichpat lei amamona dechavreih* — this is the one, he is the thief, for he clearly does not concern himself with other people's property. They seized the man, and he confessed.

### Trust Paradox

The juxtaposition is pointed. We have just established that *talmidei chachamim* are to be trusted with returning lost objects based on recognition alone, because they are scrupulously honest and fudge the truth only in those three specific areas. And yet here is a *talmid chacham* who steals silver goblets. The *Gemara* does not resolve this tension explicitly, but it is clearly deliberate. The implication may be that the category of "trusted *talmid chacham*" is not a blanket credential — it applies to someone *idi'einan bei d'lo meshaneh ela behani telat*, one whom we specifically know does not deviate from truth except in those three areas. If it becomes apparent that a person fudges in other domains as well, *lo mahadrinan lei* — we do not return the *aveidah* to him on the basis of his recognition.

This also raises the broader question: even granting that *talmidei chachamim* are not inclined to lie, there is a real risk of self-deception — an honest person might genuinely but wrongly believe he recognizes something as his. Asserting recognition of an object is not like asserting a point of *halacha*; it is a judgment about memory and resemblance, and even honest people make such errors. Perhaps the deeper point is that a person who is maximally rigorous about truthfulness will also be maximally rigorous about the reliability of his own perceptions before asserting them. The one who dries his hands on someone else's coat has already demonstrated that his internal calibration of "mine" versus "others'" is compromised — and that same compromised calibration may corrupt his identification of objects as well.

## Public Spaces

### Streets & Plazas

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָיָה רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אוֹמֵר: הַמַּצִּיל מִן הָאֲרִי וּמִן הַדּוֹב וּמִן הַנָּמֵר וּמִן הַבַּרְדְּלָס וּמִן זוֹטוֹ שֶׁל יָם וּמִשְּׁלוּלִיתוֹ שֶׁל נָהָר, הַמּוֹצֵא בִּסְרַטְיָא וּפְלַטְיָא גְּדוֹלָה, וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהָרַבִּים מְצוּיִים שָׁם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיָאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** § The *baraita* continues: **And likewise, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar would say:** In the case of **one who rescues** a lost item **from a lion, or from a bear, or from a leopard, or from a cheetah [*bardelas*], or from the tide of the sea, or from the flooding of a river;** and in the case of **one who finds** a lost item **in a main thoroughfare [*seratya*] or a large plaza [*pelatya*], or in any place where the multitudes are found, these** items belong **to him due to** the fact **that the owner despairs of their** recovery.

Returning to the *Mishnah*, Rabban Shimon ben Elazar adds: *hamotza b'astratya u'faltya gdola u'v'chol makom she'harabim mtzuyim sham* — one who finds something on a major thoroughfare (*astratya*) or in a major public square (*faltya*) — the Greek-derived terms for the great colonnaded streets and central plazas of ancient cities, analogous to Broadway or Times Square — or in any place where the masses congregate, *harey eilu shelo*, he may keep it, *mipnei she'habealim mitya'ashin mehen* — because the owners despair of recovering their property.

The reasoning: even if the object bears a *siman*, even if we know it came from a Jewish owner who would normally have the right to reclaim it, the sheer volume of foot traffic in such locations makes it near-certain that someone will have found it who is not scrupulous enough to return it. The owner, knowing this, gives up hope immediately. There are therefore two distinct grounds for permitting a finder in such a location to keep the object: presumed *yeush* by the original owner, and, in the case of a predominantly non-Jewish location, the additional inference that the object likely originated with a non-Jew in the first place.

### Crowd Yeush - Scope

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר – בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם אֲבָל בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל – לָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי אָמַר?
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **When Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar says** that if one finds a lost item in any place where multitudes are found, the item belongs to him, did he refer only to a place **where there is a majority of gentiles; but** in a place **where there is a majority of Jews,** the owner does **not** despair of recovering the item, because he relies on the Jews to return his item? **Or perhaps, even** in a place **where there is a majority of Jews, he also says** that the item belongs to the one who found it.

This ruling prompts an extended *ibaya* — a series of nested unresolved questions: Does Rabban Shimon ben Elazar's rule apply only when the crowd consists predominantly of non-Jews (*berov kena'anim*), or even when the majority is Jewish (*berov Yisrael*)? If it applies only in a non-Jewish majority, do the *rabbanan* who dispute him agree in the case of non-Jews (where the owner cannot assume a Jew will find and return it) while disagreeing in the case of a Jewish majority? And do we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Elazar at all, and if so, to what extent?

The two separate considerations underlying the analysis deserve to be kept distinct. The first is the *demographic* question: given the local population, is it more likely the object came from a Jew or a non-Jew? If it came from a non-Jew, there is no obligation to return it, and independently, even a Jewish owner may infer from the majority non-Jewish population that whoever finds it will not return it. The second is the *traffic volume* question: even in a Jewish neighborhood, if the location has such heavy foot traffic that many unscrupulous people pass through, does that itself generate *yeush* regardless of who lost it?

### Money in Batei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי אָמַר, פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ, אוֹ לָא פְּלִיגִי? וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר פְּלִיגִי, בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל וַדַּאי פְּלִיגִי, בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם פְּלִיגִי אוֹ לָא פְּלִיגִי? וְאִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר פְּלִיגִי אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ אוֹ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ? אִם תִּמְצָא לוֹמַר הֲלָכָה כְּמוֹתוֹ, דַּוְקָא בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם אוֹ אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל?
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that **even** in a place **where there is a majority of Jews, he also said** that the item belongs to the one who found it, **do the Rabbis disagree with him or do they not disagree? And if you say** that **the Rabbis disagree** with him, in a place **where there is a majority of Jews, they certainly disagree.** In a place **where there is a majority of gentiles, do** the Rabbis **disagree, or do they not disagree? And if you say** that **the Rabbis disagree** with him **even** in a place **where there is a majority of gentiles,** is the ***halakha* in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, **or** is the ***halakha* not in accordance with his** opinion? And **if you say** that the ***halakha*** is **in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, does this *halakha* apply **specifically** in a place **where there is a majority of gentiles, or** is the *halakha* in accordance with his opinion **even** in a place **where there is a majority of Jews?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: הַמּוֹצֵא מָעוֹת בְּבָתֵּי כְנֵסִיּוֹת וּבְבָתֵּי מִדְרָשׁוֹת, וּבְכׇל מָקוֹם שֶׁהָרַבִּים מְצוּיִין שָׁם – הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַבְּעָלִים מִתְיָאֲשִׁין מֵהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: In the case of **one who finds coins in synagogues [*bevatei khenesiyyot*] and study halls or in any place where the multitudes are found, these** coins belong **to him, due to** the fact **that the owner despairs of their** recovery.

The *Gemara* attempts to resolve the scope question. *Ta shema*: *hamotze maot bevatei khnesiyot uvvatei midrashot uvchol makom sheharabbim metzuyim sham harey elu shelo mipnei she'habealim mityaashin mehen* — money found in synagogues, study halls, and all places where the masses gather may be kept, since the owners despair. Who subscribes to the principle that heavy-traffic locations generate *yeush* regardless? It must be Rabban Shimon ben Elazar. And these are precisely Jewish institutions. This appears to prove that his ruling applies even to a majority-Jewish congregation.

### Traffic vs. Population

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַנִּי? רַבָּנַן הִיא, תִּפְשׁוֹט מִינַּהּ דְּמוֹדוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם. אֶלָּא לְעוֹלָם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר הִיא, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי. וְהָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּטָמוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? It **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis.** The Gemara suggests: In any case, **resolve** the dilemma **from** the mishna **that the Rabbis concede to Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar** in a place **where there is a majority of gentiles.** The Gemara rejects this explanation: **Rather, actually** the mishna **is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar, and** he stated his opinion **even in** a place **where there is a majority of Jews. And with what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** the found item is **concealed.**

The *Gemara* deflects: perhaps the *batei khnesiyot* and *batei midrashos* referred to here are non-Jewish houses of assembly — *shel kena'anim*. One should note that *beit knesset* literally means "house of gathering" and was not restricted to strictly religious purposes in antiquity; it served as a general communal center. As for *batei midrashos*, Rashi suggests they are Jewish study halls in which non-Jewish watchmen are employed overnight, such that a sufficiently large number of non-Jews frequent the premises. The *Gemara* concedes this response is strained (*dachuk*), but presses it nonetheless to avoid the conclusion that the ruling applies to Jewish crowds. The prima facie evidence from the *batei khnesiyot* passage thus does not cleanly resolve the question.

A further *braisa* is then invoked: if one finds an *aveidah* in a city whose majority is Jewish, he must announce it; if the majority is non-Jewish, he need not. This is cited as evidence that the principle of following the majority *does* distinguish between Jewish and non-Jewish — and that even Rabban Shimon ben Elazar speaks only of a non-Jewish majority.

Here the *Gemara* draws an important analytical distinction. The two cases are fundamentally different in kind. The *batei khnesiyot* discussion concerns specific locations of dense foot traffic, where the concern is that some unscrupulous passerby will have taken the object. The city-wide majority discussion concerns simply assessing *from whom* the object was lost, not how many people are around to take it. A majority non-Jewish city allows us to presume the object originated with a non-Jew; a high-traffic area creates *yeush* regardless of origin. These are two separate legal mechanisms, and the *Gemara* has at several points been drawing on evidence from one to answer questions about the other — a move that the analysis here flags as potentially imprecise.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? בְּטָמוּן. אִי בְּטָמוּן, מַאי עֲבִידְתֵּיהּ גַּבֵּיהּ?! וְהָתְנַן: מָצָא כְּלִי בָּאַשְׁפָּה, מְכוּסֶּה – לֹא יִגַּע בּוֹ, מְגוּלֶּה – נוֹטֵל וּמַכְרִיז. כִּדְאָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, בְּאַשְׁפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְפַנּוֹת, וְנִמְלַךְ עָלֶיהָ לְפַנּוֹתָהּ. הָכָא נָמֵי בְּאַשְׁפָּה שֶׁאֵינָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה לְפַנּוֹת, וְנִמְלַךְ עָלֶיהָ לְפַנּוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **With what are we dealing here?** This is a case **where** the found item is **concealed.** The Gemara asks: **If** the item is **concealed, what** is the reason the item **is with** the finder? Clearly it was placed there and the owner will return to retrieve it. **And didn't we learn** in a mishna (25b): In a case where **one found a vessel in a garbage dump,** if the vessel is **concealed he may not touch it,** but if it is **exposed,** the finder **takes** the item **and proclaims** his find. The Gemara answers: It can be explained **as Rav Pappa says** elsewhere, that it is referring **to a garbage dump that is not designed to be cleared, and** the owner of the land **reconsidered** and decided **to clear it.** If one finds concealed vessels he should proclaim his find, because otherwise the vessels will be cleared with the rest of the garbage dump. **Here too,** the mishna is referring **to a garbage dump that is not designed to be cleared, and** the owner of the land **reconsidered** and decided **to clear it.** If one finds concealed items, his course of action is determined by the identity of the majority of the residents of the city. If they are Jews, he must proclaim his find, and if not, he need not proclaim his find.

The *Gemara* partially resolves the *braisa*'s implication by noting: *hacha b'mai askinan b'tamun* — the *braisa*'s city case involves something that was *hidden or buried*, not merely dropped. A buried object is not exposed to random passersby, so the crowd-based *yeush* mechanism does not apply. The obligation to return in a majority-Jewish city thus follows from the absence of the traffic-based *yeush*, not from any disagreement with Rabban Shimon ben Elazar's general principle. The *Gemara* then accounts for why such a buried object would be in the finder's hands at all: following Rav Papa's reasoning about a garbage dump (*ashpah*) that was not normally cleared but was then unexpectedly cleaned out — once the owner's concealed object is inadvertently exposed, the finder is no longer permitted to leave it, and must take it and announce it.

## Special Cases (III)

### Hidden Objects

The general principle emerges: an object that was intentionally hidden or buried carries no presumption of *yeush*, because the owner placed it somewhere he expected it not to be found and intended to retrieve it. The crowd-based *yeush* mechanism applies only to objects that were *exposed* — dropped or left in a public area where many people pass. The buried object presents the inverse situation: the owner has no reason to despair, since the thing was not exposed to random finders. The complication arises precisely when a hidden object is *inadvertently exposed*, and it is the analysis of that transition — from safely hidden to newly exposed — that the preceding discussion of the ashpah case addresses.

## Population & Ownersh (II)

### Wine - Non-Jewish City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע, דְּאָמַר רַב אַסִּי: מָצָא חָבִית יַיִן בְּעִיר שֶׁרוּבָּהּ גּוֹיִם – מוּתֶּרֶת מִשּׁוּם מְצִיאָה וַאֲסוּרָה בַּהֲנָאָה. בָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנָתַן בָּהּ סִימָן – מוּתֶּרֶת בִּשְׁתִיָּה, לְמוֹצְאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** a proof from that **which Rav Asi says:** If **one found a barrel of wine in a city whose** population has **a majority of gentiles,** keeping the barrel **is permitted in terms of** the *halakhot* of **finding** lost items because it presumably belonged to a gentile, **and** deriving **benefit** from the wine **is prohibited,** as it is presumed to be wine of a gentile. If **a Jew came and provided a distinguishing mark** to describe **it, drinking** the wine **is permitted for its finder,** as it proved to be the wine of a Jew. Nevertheless, it belongs to the finder, because the owner despaired of recovering a barrel misplaced in a public area.

The *Gemara* presents a ruling of Rav Assi: *matza chavit yayin b'ir sheruba kena'anim* — one who finds a barrel of wine in a city whose majority is non-Jewish. *Muteret mishum metziah* — it is permitted as a *metziah*, since one may assume it came from a non-Jew. *Va'asura b'hana'ah* — but it is forbidden to derive benefit from it, since it is the wine of a non-Jew. Then a Jew appears and provides a *siman* identifying the barrel as his — *muteret b'shtiah*, the wine is now permitted for drinking, since we now know it came from a Jew. But *l'motzah* — the finder may still keep it. The barrel has become kasher wine that the finder need not return. The asymmetry is almost comic: the *siman* makes the wine drinkable but does not require its return.

### Wallet - Non-Jewish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּמַאן? כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ כִּי קָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר – בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם אֲבָל בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל לָא. לְעוֹלָם אֵימָא לָךְ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל נָמֵי קָאָמַר, וְרַב אַסִּי סָבַר לַהּ כְּווֹתֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא, וּפְלִיג עֲלֵיהּ בַּחֲדָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof: **In accordance with whose** opinion is this statement of Rav Asi? It is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar. Conclude from it that when Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar stated** his opinion, it was only with regard to a place **where there is a majority of gentiles; but** in a place **where there is a majority of Jews,** the owner does **not** despair of recovering his lost item. The Gemara rejects the proof: **Actually, I will say to you** that **even** with regard to a place **where there is a majority of Jews, Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar also stated** his opinion, **and Rav Asi holds in accordance with** the opinion of Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar **in one** case, that of a place where there is a majority of gentiles, **and disagrees with him in one** case, that of a place where there is a majority of Jews.

This ruling implies that even once the Jewish origin of the object is established, the prior presumption of non-Jewish ownership meant that the finder was entitled to treat it as a *metziah* all along — and the owner's *yeush* is treated as having been established at the outset, regardless of the owner's subjective state of mind. The *Gemara* notes this goes like Rabban Shimon ben Elazar's approach, which focuses on the population of the locale. The finder may keep the barrel itself even after it is established that a Jew owned it; if there is no benefit in the wine itself (since it is *assur b'hana'ah* while presumed non-Jewish), Rav Ashi notes that one may at least use the barrel — *l'kankaneia*.

## Special Cases (III) (continued)

### River Biran

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא גַּבְרָא דְּאַשְׁכַּח אַרְבָּעָה זוּזֵי דְּצַיְירִי בִּסְדִינָא וּשְׁדוּ בִּנְהַר בֵּירָן, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל אַכְרֵיז. וְהָא זוֹטוֹ שֶׁל יָם הוּא! שָׁאנֵי נְהַר בֵּירָן, כֵּיוָן דְּמִתְּקִיל לָא מִיָּאַשׁ. וְהָא רוּבָּא גּוֹיִם נִינְהוּ, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ אֵין הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב גּוֹיִם! שָׁאנֵי נְהַר בֵּירָן, דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל סָכְרוּ לֵיהּ וְיִשְׂרָאֵל כָּרוּ לֵיהּ. כֵּיוָן דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל סָכְרוּ לֵיהּ – אֵימוֹר מִיִּשְׂרָאֵל נְפַל, וְכֵיוָן דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל כָּרוּ לֵיהּ – לָא מִיָּאַשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain man who found four dinars that were bound in a cloth and cast into the Biran River. He came before Rav Yehuda** and asked how to proceed. Rav Yehuda **said: Go proclaim** your finding. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it** a case of an item lost **in the tide of the sea** that should therefore belong to the finder? The Gemara answers: **The Biran River is different. Since it contains obstacles,** the owner **does not despair** of recovering the lost item. The Gemara asks: **But isn't it** a place where the **majority** of the population **is gentiles? Conclude from it that** the ***halakha* is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar even in** a place where there is **a majority of gentiles.** The Gemara answers: **The Biran River is different, as Jews dammed it and Jews dredge it. Since Jews dammed it, say that** the coins **fell from a Jew, and since Jews dredge it,** the owner of the coins **does not despair** of recovering them.

A man found four *zuz* wrapped in a cloth that had been thrown into the river Biran. He came before Rav Yehuda, who told him to announce it. But is this not like *zuto shel yam*, the middle of the ocean, one of the paradigm cases where *yeush* is presumed even without the owner's subjective despair? The *Gemara* answers: *shani nahar biran* — the river Biran is different, *keivan d'mitkli* — because it has various obstructions, dams, and sluice gates, things routinely wash ashore or are recovered when the water is held back. The owner therefore does not despair.

But the majority population in the area is non-Jewish — should that not provide an independent basis for keeping it? *Shani nahar biran d'Yisrael sachru lei v'Yisrael karu lei* — this particular river is controlled by Jews: it is Jews who dam it up and Jews who open the sluice gates. Given that the people working this river are Jewish, we may presume the coins fell from a Jew, and given that Jews are operating the river and will find what washes ashore, the owner does not despair of recovery.

## Population & Ownersh (II)

### Marketplace of Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יְהוּדָה הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל וְאָזֵיל בָּתְרֵיהּ דְּמָר שְׁמוּאֵל בְּשׁוּקָא דְּבֵי דַיְסָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָצָא כָּאן אַרְנָקִי, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. בָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנָתַן בָּהּ סִימָן, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַיָּיב לְהַחְזִיר. תַּרְתֵּי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yehuda was moving along behind Mar Shmuel in the market** where **pounded grain** was sold. Rav Yehuda **said to** Shmuel: If **one found a purse [*arnakei*] here, what is** the *halakha*? Shmuel **said to him** that the *halakha* is as the mishna states: **These** belong **to him.** Rav Yehuda asked him: If **a Jew came and provided a distinguishing mark** to describe it, **what is** the *halakha*? Shmuel **said to him:** The finder is **obligated to return** it. Rav Yehuda asked: These are **two** contradictory rulings. Shmuel **said to him:** By law, it belongs to him. When I said the finder is obligated to return it if he learns the identity of the owner, that was **beyond the letter of the law.**

Rav Yehuda was walking behind Mar Shmuel in the marketplace of Bei Deisa — a place selling pounded grain, presumably with a predominantly non-Jewish and crowded character. Rav Yehuda asked: if one found a wallet here, what is the ruling? Shmuel replied: *harey elu shelo* — he may keep it. The majority population was presumably non-Jewish, and the area was a busy market.

### Middle Position

Then came the harder question: what if a Jew subsequently appeared and gave a *siman* identifying it as his? Shmuel replied: *chayav lehachazir* — he must return it. This creates an apparent tension: if the finder was entitled from the outset to assume the object came from a non-Jew, one of two things must follow. Either we maintain that the owner should have known the finder would make that assumption and was therefore *meyayesh* from the start — in which case even a subsequent *siman* should not compel return. Or we allow for a middle position: the finder had no obligation to actively announce the object, since the majority-non-Jewish presumption relieved him of that duty, but he does not thereby acquire it outright, such that if the true owner appears with a *siman*, he must return it.

### Jew Appears Later

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי הָא דַּאֲבוּהּ דִּשְׁמוּאֵל אַשְׁכַּח הָנָךְ חֲמָרֵי בְּמַדְבְּרָא וְאַהְדְּרִינְהוּ לְמָרַיְיהוּ לְבָתַר תְּרֵיסַר יַרְחֵי שַׁתָּא, לִפְנִים מִשּׁוּרַת הַדִּין.
>
> **Translation:** This is **like that** incident **where Shmuel's father found these donkeys in the desert and returned them to their owner after** the passage of **twelve months of the year,** as he acted **beyond the letter of the law.**

Shmuel's response opts for the second of these: *lifnim mishurat hadin* — beyond the letter of the law. Technically one might argue the finder's presumption entailed the owner's *yeush*, but if it becomes clear the owner never actually despaired and is now identifiable, the right thing to do is to return the object.

This is illustrated by the father of Shmuel, who found donkeys in the wilderness and returned them to their owner after twelve months — *lifnim mishurat hadin*. Rashi understands the *lifnim mishurat hadin* here as acting beyond the presumption that owners of lost animals despair after twelve months. Tosafos disagrees: after twelve months one is permitted to sell the animal and return its monetary value; Shmuel's father instead returned the animals themselves, having cared for them at his own expense. Either way, the case illustrates that even when the finder is technically entitled to retain property, the presence of a known owner and the owner's continued expectation of return creates a *middat chasidut* to restore it.

## Special Cases (III) (continued…)

### Owner Protest

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא הֲוָה שָׁקֵיל וְאָזֵיל בָּתְרֵיהּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן בְּשׁוּקָא דְגִלְדָּאֵי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּשׁוּקָא דְרַבָּנַן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מָצָא כָּאן אַרְנָקִי, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. בָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל וְנָתַן בָּהּ סִימָן, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ שֶׁלּוֹ. וַהֲלֹא עוֹמֵד וְצוֹוֵחַ! נַעֲשָׂה כְּצוֹוֵחַ עַל בֵּיתוֹ שֶׁנָּפַל וְעַל סְפִינָתוֹ שֶׁטָּבְעָה בַּיָּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava was moving along behind Rav Naḥman in the tanner's market, and some say in the marketplace** frequented by **the Sages.** Rava **said to** Rav Naḥman: If **one found a purse here, what is** the *halakha*? Rav Naḥman **said to him** that the *halakha* is as the mishna states: **These** belong **to him.** Rava asked him: If **a Jew came and provided a distinguishing mark** to describe it, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav Naḥman **said to him** that in this case as well, the *halakha* is as the mishna states: **These** belong **to him.** Rava asked: **But isn't** the owner justifiably **standing and screaming** that the purse belongs to him? Rav Naḥman said to him: **He becomes as** one who **screams** to no avail **about his house that collapsed or about his ship that sank in the sea.**

Rava was walking behind Rav Nachman in the marketplace of the leatherworkers — or, in an alternative version, the marketplace of the *rabbanan*. The question was posed: if one found a wallet here, what is the ruling? Rav Nachman replied: *harey elu shelo*, he may keep it. This is significant: if the version is "marketplace of the *rabbanan*," then we are dealing with a majority-Jewish locale. Nevertheless, the finder may keep it — because it is a place of heavy foot traffic, and the crowd-based *yeush* applies even in a predominantly Jewish area. Rav Nachman's ruling thus appears to go like Rabban Shimon ben Elazar even *berov Yisrael*.

But then: what if the owner appears with a *siman*? Rav Nachman again said: *harey elu shelo* — the finder keeps it. Even with a *siman*? Even with the owner present and crying that he never gave up hope? The *Gemara* says: *na'aseh ketzoveiach al beiso shenafal ve'al sefinato shetava beyam* — it is like someone crying over his house that collapsed or his ship that sank at sea. The subjective protest of the owner is legally irrelevant. Some *Rishonim* frame this as *batlah da'ato eitzel kol adam* — his individual hope is nullified against the view of all reasonable people. Where the circumstances are such that the loss is practically total, *halacha* does not recognize a personal refusal to despair as legally meaningful *yeush*; it treats the object as simply and irretrievably lost.

## Practical Rulings

### Vulture Meat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָהוּא דַּיּוֹ דְּשָׁקֵיל בִּשְׂרָא בְּשׁוּקָא וְשַׁדְיֵהּ בְּצִנְיָיתָא דְּבֵי בַּר מָרִיּוֹן. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּאַבָּיֵי, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל לְנַפְשָׁךְ. וְהָא רוּבָּא דְּיִשְׂרָאֵל נִינְהוּ, שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל! שָׁאנֵי דַּיּוֹ, דִּכְזוֹטוֹ שֶׁל יָם דָּמֵי. וְהָא אָמַר רַב: בָּשָׂר שֶׁנִּתְעַלֵּם מִן הָעַיִן – אָסוּר! בְּעוֹמֵד וְרוֹאֵהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: There was **a certain kite that took meat in the marketplace and cast it among the palm trees of the house of bar Maryon.** The one who found the meat **came before Abaye** to ask how to proceed. Abaye **said to him: Go take** it **for yourself.** The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the marketplace of kosher meat a place where there is **a majority of Jews? Conclude from it that** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar even in** a place where there is **a majority of Jews.** The Gemara answers: **A kite is different, as** an item taken by a kite is **similar** to a lost item swept away **in the tide of the sea.** The Gemara raises another issue: **But doesn't Rav say: Meat that was obscured from sight** and unsupervised for a period of time is **forbidden,** as its source is unknown? The Gemara answers: This is a case where the finder **stands and sees** the meat from the moment that it was taken by the kite until it was cast among the trees.

The *Gemara* now turns to a series of cases that apply the accumulated principles. A vulture picked up meat from the marketplace and deposited it among the palm trees of Bei Bar Maryon. Someone found it. Abaye told him: *zil shekol lenafshach* — keep it. One might ask: the marketplace from which the vulture took the meat was presumably majority Jewish — does ruling that it may be kept reflect that we rule like Rabban Shimon ben Elazar even *berov Yisrael*? The *Gemara* deflects: *shani deiyo de'kezuto shel yam dami* — the vulture is a different case entirely; it is analogous to *zuto shel yam*, the middle of the ocean. Once something is in the beak of a vulture, the owner immediately despairs, regardless of where the vulture originally picked it up. The permissibility here has nothing to do with the population of the marketplace; it follows from the near-impossibility of ever recovering something from a flying scavenger.

A separate consideration: Rava notes that *basar shenisalem min ha'ayin assur* — meat that left one's line of sight becomes forbidden due to the possibility of substitution or tampering. The *Gemara* addresses this: the case here is *be'omed vero'ehu* — the finder kept continuous visual watch on the vulture and saw exactly where the meat landed, so the meat is permitted.

### Slaughtered Goat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנִינָא מָצָא גְּדִי שָׁחוּט בֵּין טְבֶרְיָא לְצִיפּוֹרִי וְהִתִּירוּהוּ לוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: הִתִּירוּהוּ לוֹ מִשּׁוּם מְצִיאָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר, מִשּׁוּם שְׁחִיטָה כְּרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ḥanina found a slaughtered young goat between Tiberias and Tzippori and** the Sages **permitted it to him. Rabbi Ami said:** The Sages **permitted it to him in terms of** the *halakhot* of **finding** lost items **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar,** and they permitted it to him **in terms of** the *halakhot* of the **slaughter** of kosher animals, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Ḥananya, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili.**

Rav Chanina found a slaughtered goat between Teverya and Tzippori and was told he could keep it. Rabbi Ami clarified: the permission rests on two independent grounds. First, *mishum metziah ke'Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar* — as a found object in a high-traffic road, Rabban Shimon ben Elazar's principle allows it to be kept even if the locale was predominantly Jewish. Second, *mishum shechitah* — one may assume the *shechitah* was performed by a competent Jewish *shochet*, following the ruling of Rav Chanina the son of Rabbi Yosi HaGelili.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁאָבְדוּ לוֹ גְּדָיָיו וְתַרְנְגוֹלָיו, הָלַךְ וּמְצָאָן שְׁחוּטִין, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹסֵר, וְרַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי מַתִּיר. אָמַר רַבִּי: נִרְאִין דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה כְּשֶׁמְּצָאָן בְּאַשְׁפָּה, וְדִבְרֵי רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי כְּשֶׁמְּצָאָן בַּבַּיִת. מִדְּהִתִּירוּהוּ לוֹ מִשּׁוּם שְׁחִיטָה, רוּבָּא יִשְׂרָאֵל נִינְהוּ, שָׁמְעַתְּ מִינַּהּ הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן אֶלְעָזָר אֲפִילּוּ בְּרוֹב יִשְׂרָאֵל! אָמַר רָבָא: רוֹב גּוֹיִם וְרוֹב טַבָּחֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In a case **where one's young goats and roosters were lost,** and the owner **went and found them slaughtered, Rabbi Yehuda deems** the meat **forbidden, and Rabbi Ḥananya, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, deems** it **permitted.** **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: The statement of Rabbi Yehuda appears** to be correct in a case **where he found** the slaughtered animals **in a garbage dump,** as the concern is that they were thrown away because the slaughter was unfit. **And the statement of Rabbi Ḥananya, son of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, appears correct** in a case **where he found them in the house.** The Gemara infers: **From** the fact **that** the Sages **permitted** the meat **to him in terms of** the *halakhot* of **slaughter,** apparently, this place is one where there is **a majority of Jews. Conclude from it that** the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar even in** a place where there is **a majority of Jews. Rava said:** It is a place where there is **a majority of gentiles but** the **majority of slaughterers are Jews.**

The underlying dispute about *shechitah* is between Rabbi Yehuda and Rav Chanina the son of Rabbi Yosi HaGelili: if someone lost a live animal and found it slaughtered, Rabbi Yehuda forbids it (the *shochet* may have been disqualified), while Rav Chanina permits it. Rabbi Yehudi's synthesis (*nirein divrei Rabbi Yehuda keshemetzan be'ashpah, vedivrei Rav Chanina beno shel Rabbi Yosi HaGelili keshemetzan be'vayis*) reconciles the two positions: Rabbi Yehuda's concern applies when the animal is found in a dump — suggesting it was discarded as *pasul* — while Rav Chanina's leniency applies when it is found in a house, where the presumption is that someone slaughtered it intending to eat it. Tosafos notes that even a *ganav* is presumed to be careful about *kashrus*, so finding it in a house does not undermine the presumption of valid *shechitah*.

Rava then adds a crucial observation: *rov nochrim verov tivchei Yisrael* — the majority of the local *population* was not Jewish, but the majority of the *shochtim* were Jewish. This bifurcation is analytically important. The *metziah* permissibility follows from the predominantly non-Jewish population (allowing the finder to assume the goat was lost by a non-Jew). The *shechitah* permissibility follows from the predominantly Jewish *shochtim* (ensuring the animal was slaughtered properly). The two majorities are independent and pull in opposite directions — but they work together to produce a result where the finder may eat the meat and keep the carcass.

### Fishermen's Bundles

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אַמֵּי אַשְׁכַּח פַּרְגִּיּוֹת שְׁחוּטוֹת בֵּין טְבֶרְיָא לְצִיפּוֹרִי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אַסִּי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל לְנַפְשָׁךְ. רַבִּי יִצְחָק נַפָּחָא אַשְׁכַּח קִיבּוּרָא דְאִזְלֵי בֵּי אָזְלוֹיֵי, אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ בְּבֵי מִדְרְשָׁא, וַאֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: זִיל שְׁקוֹל לְנַפְשָׁךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ami found slaughtered fledglings between Tiberias and Tzippori. He came before Rabbi Asi** to ask how to proceed, **and some say** he came **before Rabbi Yoḥanan, and some say** he came to **the study hall. And they said to him: Go take** it **for yourself. Rabbi Yitzḥak Nappaḥa found a skein** of thread **from which a net was woven. He came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** to ask how to proceed, **and some say** he came to **the study hall. And they said to him: Go take** it **for yourself,** because he found it in a place frequented by the multitudes.

Rav Ami found slaughtered birds between Teverya and Tzippori, and came before Rav Assi — or, in an alternate version, Rabbi Yochanan, or the *Beit Midrash* — who told him: *zil shekol lenafshach*. Rav Yitzchak Nafcha found bundles of cordage used by fishermen for their nets. He too came before Rabbi Yochanan (or the *Beit Midrash*) and was told he could keep them. In both cases the ruling rests on the general framework developed throughout the daf: either the majority population was non-Jewish, or the location was sufficiently high-traffic, or both.

### Open Questions

The daf closes with the overall picture coming into clearer but still not fully resolved focus. The operative principle, emerging from the accumulated cases, is that when the local population is predominantly non-Jewish, one may generally treat a found object as a *metziah* and keep it. That seems to be the direction of the *halacha* as the *Gemara* concludes. The intriguing middle position — that the finder need not actively return such an object but must return it if the owner appears with a *siman* — gives way in the stricter rulings to a position where even the appearance of a Jewish owner with a *siman* does not require return, because the owner's *yeush* is treated as established by the circumstances regardless of his subjective state.

The separate question — what happens when it is a high-traffic location with a majority-*Jewish* population, a "Jewish Times Square" like Kikar Shabbat — is flagged but not clearly resolved. Does crowd-based *yeush* apply even there? The *Gemara*'s most recent rulings, particularly in the marketplace of the *rabbanan*, seem to push in the direction that even *berov Yisrael*, if the traffic is sufficient, the finder may keep the object. But that conclusion remains an inference from the cases rather than an explicit ruling, and the question is left open.
