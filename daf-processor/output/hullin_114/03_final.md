# Hullin 114 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Shmuel's Parameters

### Gedi & Imo Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַחֵלֶב, ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַמֵּתָה, ״גְּדִי״ – לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הַשְּׁלִיל.
>
> **Translation:** § **Shmuel says:** Each of the Torah's three mentions of the prohibition of not cooking a kid in its mother's milk serves to include a different case. The first mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to include** liability for cooking in milk and eating forbidden **fats,** beyond the liability for eating forbidden fat per se. The second mention of the word **"kid"** likewise serves **to include** additional liability for cooking in milk and eating the meat of **a dead** animal carcass. Finally, the third mention of the word **"kid"** serves **to include** liability for cooking in milk and eating an animal **fetus.**

The daf begins at the bottom of 113b, several lines above where the text widens, at the line beginning with the word *behemah*. The preceding discussion had established that Shmuel derived various parameters of *basar be-chalav* from the words *gedi* and *imo* in the verse *lo tevashel gedi be-chalav imo*. Among the rulings Shmuel produced from this derivation is the rule that one is liable even when the meat was never properly slaughtered — that is, even *neveilah* — and even when the meat in question is *chelev*, forbidden fat. Although *chelev* already carries its own *issur*, Shmuel held that the prohibition of *basar be-chalav* takes effect upon it nonetheless, because the verse teaches that one *issur* can take effect on top of another.

### Issur Chal al Issur Deba…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר? וְהָאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: מִנַּיִן לְכֹהֵן טָמֵא שֶׁאָכַל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה שֶׁאֵינוֹ בְּמִיתָה? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ כִּי יְחַלְּלֻהוּ״ – פְּרַט לָזוֹ שֶׁמְּחוּלֶּלֶת וְעוֹמֶדֶת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And does Shmuel** really **maintain** that **a prohibition takes effect** where another **prohibition** already exists? **But didn't Shmuel say in the name of Rabbi Elazar: From where** is it derived that **an impure priest who partakes of impure *teruma*,** i.e., the portion of produce designated for the priest, **is not** punished **with death** at the hand of Heaven as he would had the *teruma* been ritually pure? It is derived from a verse, **as it is stated,** with regard to the prohibition of an impure priest partaking of *teruma*: **"And die therein if they desecrate it"** (Leviticus 22:9), **to the exclusion of this** case of *teruma* that is impure, **which was already desecrated** before the priest ate it. Here, it seems, since impure *teruma* is already prohibited for consumption, the added prohibition of an impure priest partaking of *teruma* does not take effect.

This ruling immediately prompts the Gemara's challenge: *ve-savar Shmuel issur chal al issur?* Does Shmuel truly maintain that one prohibition takes hold upon another? For Shmuel himself is reported to have said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: how do we know that a *kohen tamei* who ate *terumah tme'ah* — impure *terumah* — is not liable to the more severe punishment of death at the hand of Heaven? The verse states *u-metu vo ki yechaleluhu*, "they shall die thereby if they desecrate it," which the Gemara understands as excluding the case where the *terumah* was already pre-desecrated — *she-mechulelet ve-omedet*. The logic, as the Gemara reads it at this stage, is that the new *issur* of the *kohen tamei* eating *terumah* does not take effect upon an object already rendered forbidden by its prior state of impurity. If Shmuel endorsed this position, how can he simultaneously maintain that *issur chal al issur* applies in the case of *basar be-chalav*?

### Three Resolutions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּעָלְמָא אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, וְשָׁאנֵי הָתָם דְּמִיעֵט רַחֲמָנָא ״וּמֵתוּ בוֹ״. אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: בְּעָלְמָא קָסָבַר שְׁמוּאֵל אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּרַבִּי רַחֲמָנָא ״גְּדִי״. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: הָא דִידֵיהּ, הָא דְרַבֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that **in general** Shmuel maintains that **a prohibition takes effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, and it **is different there,** with regard to *teruma*, **as the Merciful One** expressly **excludes** impure *teruma* by the phrase **"and die therein** if they desecrate it," and in this case the *teruma* is already desecrated. And **if you wish, say** that **in general Shmuel maintains** that **a prohibition does not take effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, **and here,** the case of meat cooked in milk, **is different, as the Merciful One** expressly **includes** the meat of an animal carcass and forbidden fat by the repetition of the word **"kid."** **And if you wish, say** that **this** statement of Shmuel with regard to meat cooked in milk is **his** own opinion, as he maintains that a prohibition takes effect where another prohibition already exists, and **that** statement with regard to *teruma* **is his teacher's,** i.e., Rabbi Elazar's, opinion, as he holds that a prohibition does not take effect where another prohibition already exists.

The Gemara offers three possible resolutions. The first: *be-alma issur chal al issur* — perhaps Shmuel's general position is that one prohibition does take effect upon another, and his ruling about *basar be-chalav* simply reflects that general principle. The exceptional case of *terumah tme'ah* is explained by the specific verse *u-metu vo ki yechaleluhu*, which carves out a special exclusion, limiting liability to cases where an act of genuine desecration occurs. That is a textual anomaly, not a window into Shmuel's broader jurisprudence.

The second resolution inverts this: perhaps Shmuel's general principle is that *ein issur chal al issur*, one prohibition does not ordinarily take hold upon another. His ruling regarding *basar be-chalav* is then explained by the redundancy of the word *gedi* — the Torah's deliberate repetition of that term serves to teach that even in cases of pre-existing *issur*, the prohibition of *basar be-chalav* takes effect. The case of *terumah tme'ah* reflects the general rule, while *basar be-chalav* is the exception made explicit by the extra word.

The third resolution dissolves the tension entirely: the opinion permitting *issur chal al issur* is Shmuel's own, while the ruling about *terumah tme'ah* was transmitted in the name of Rabbi Eliezer — a *tanna* who held otherwise. The two statements need not be reconciled because they represent different authorities. One observes that the Gemara does not invoke the familiar formula *Rav tanna hu u-falig* with respect to Shmuel, though in this context the difference in view is between Shmuel and his teacher's tradition, and the subject matter belongs to an established *issur ve-heter* dispute among the *Tannaim*, so Shmuel's position is not without grounding.

The practical stakes here are considerable. The question of whether *basar be-chalav* applies to *neveilah* — non-*shechted* meat — has direct relevance to the overwhelming majority of meat in commercial circulation, and raises downstream questions about whether one may derive *hana'ah* from, say, a cooked mixture of *neveilah* and milk. Even if *ein issur chal al issur* saves a person from *lashes* for eating such a mixture, the prohibition of *hana'ah* — which does not pre-exist in the mixture — would still take effect. These are questions with genuine practical import.

## Milk Parameters

### Pregnant Animal Milk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי מֵרַב: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בַּחֲלֵב גְּדִי שֶׁלֹּא הֵנִיקָה, מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּאִיצְטְרִיכָא לִשְׁמוּאֵל לְמֵימַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – וְלֹא בַּחֲלֵב זָכָר, זָכָר הוּא דְּלָא אָתֵי לִכְלַל אֵם, אֲבָל הַאי, כֵּיוָן דְּבָא לִכְלַל אֵם – אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami raised a dilemma to Rav:** If **one cooks** meat **in milk of a goat that has not** yet **nursed,** but that is about to give birth and already has milk, **what is** the *halakha*? Rav **said to him: From** the fact that **it was necessary for Shmuel to say** that the phrase **"in its mother's milk"** teaches: **And not in the milk of a male** animal, one can infer that **it is** only the milk of **a male** that is excluded, as the male **cannot attain the status of a mother. But** in **this** case, **since** the goat **will attain the status of a mother,** it is **prohibited** to cook meat in its milk.

The Gemara moves to refine the parameters of what qualifies as *chalav* for purposes of *basar be-chalav*. The question posed — attributed in some versions to Rav and in others to Rava — is: *ha-mevashel be-chalav gedi she-lo hinika mahu?* What is the law if one cooks in the milk of an animal that has never nursed? Earlier discussion had already excluded milk found in the udder of a slaughtered animal, since milk that was never expressed during the animal's life is not considered *chalav aim* — the milk of a living mother. The present question concerns a different scenario: an animal that is heavily pregnant, already lactating, but has not yet given birth and therefore has never actually been a mother. The milk has physically left the body of the animal and is available, yet the animal has not yet crossed the threshold of motherhood.

### Female Milk Status

The answer given draws on Shmuel's own formulation, which had excluded only the milk of a male animal. The reasoning: *zachar hu de-lo atei le-chalav* — a male could never reach the status of a milk-producing mother, which is why its milk is excluded. But the pregnant female in question — *kivan de-ba lei le-chalav* — will in fact become a mother. She is on the threshold of that status. Therefore, her milk is *assur*. The criterion is not whether motherhood has already been actualized, but whether the animal belongs to the category of those that can be mothers. A female who has not yet given birth but is fully capable of doing so falls within the prohibition.

## Cooking vs. Eating

### Initial Lashes Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִתְּמַר: הַמְבַשֵּׁל חֵלֶב בְּחָלָב, רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי – חַד אָמַר: לוֹקֶה, וְחַד אָמַר: אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה. לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּמַאן דְּאָמַר לוֹקֶה קָסָבַר: אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה קָסָבַר: אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** It **was stated:** With regard to **one who cooks** forbidden **fat in milk, Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi** disagree as to the *halakha*. **One says** that he **is flogged** for violating the prohibition of meat cooked in milk, **and one says** that he **is not flogged.** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **they disagree about this: That the one who says** he **is flogged maintains** that **a prohibition takes effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, **and the one who says** he **is not flogged maintains** that **a prohibition does not take effect** where another **prohibition** already exists.

The Gemara now turns to a question with both conceptual depth and practical urgency: *ha-mevashel chelev be-chalav* — one who cooks forbidden fat together with milk. We have already established that *chelev* is treated as a form of meat for purposes of *basar be-chalav*. So the question is not whether such a combination is conceptually covered by the prohibition, but whether one receives *malkot* — lashes — for performing the act of cooking.

### Ami & Assi Debate

Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Assi disagree. One holds *lokei* — liable to lashes; the other holds *eino lokei* — not liable. The Gemara's initial framing of the dispute casts it as turning on *issur chal al issur*: the one who says *lokei* holds that one *issur* takes effect on another, meaning the prohibition of *basar be-chalav* operates alongside the pre-existing *issur* of *chelev*, and since one would receive lashes for eating the mixture under *basar be-chalav*, one likewise receives lashes for cooking it. The one who says *eino lokei* holds that *ein issur chal al issur*, so *basar be-chalav* never takes effect on the *chelev* meat at all — and since there is no *basar be-chalav* liability for eating it, there is similarly none for cooking it.

### Primary Concern Framing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, אַאֲכִילָה דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא לָקֵי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַבִּשּׁוּל. מַאן דְּאָמַר לוֹקֶה – חַד אִיסּוּרָא הוּא, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – לְהָכִי אַפְּקַהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲכִילָה בִּלְשׁוֹן בִּישּׁוּל,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **No; everyone** agrees that **a prohibition does not take effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, and therefore **everyone agrees that** one **is not flogged for eating** the mixture. **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to cooking. The one who says** he **is flogged** holds that one who cooks violates only **one prohibition,** that of cooking meat in milk, since it is permitted to cook forbidden fat without eating it. Consequently, this is not a case of a prohibition taking effect where another prohibition already exists. **And the one who says** he **is not flogged** holds that it was **for this** reason that **the Merciful One expressed** the prohibition of **eating** meat cooked in milk in the Torah using **the language of cooking:** "You shall not cook a kid in its mother's milk."

But the Gemara rejects this framing and offers a more penetrating alternative. It may be, the Gemara says, that *everyone* agrees that *ein issur chal al issur* — that for purposes of *eating* the mixture, one would not receive lashes under *basar be-chalav*, since the prohibition of *chelev* already pre-exists and the new prohibition does not take hold. The disagreement, says the Gemara, is exclusively about *bishul* — the act of cooking itself. This reframing forces a fundamental question about the relationship between the Torah's prohibition of cooking and the prohibition of eating *basar be-chalav*.

### Independence Theory

The dispute may hinge on whether *bishul* and *achilah* are independent concerns or whether one is derivative of the other. The Torah never says *lo tochal gedi be-chalav imo*. It says *lo tevashel* — do not cook. So one way of understanding the structure of the prohibition is that cooking is the primary concern, and eating is the derivative safeguard. From this perspective, one might compare the prohibition to that of *kilayim*: the Torah's fundamental concern is the improper act of creating the mixture, and any prohibition on enjoying or consuming the product would be a downstream consequence. When it comes to *bishul*, then, there is only a single *issur* involved — no doubling up — and therefore the one who says *lokei* is saying that cooking is entirely independent of the eating prohibition. Whether or not one will receive lashes for eating the mixture later is irrelevant to whether one transgresses in the moment of cooking.

### Derivative Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיוָן דְּעַל אֲכִילָה לָא לָקֵי, אַבִּישּׁוּל נָמֵי לָא לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** This link between cooking and eating indicates that **since one is not flogged for eating** forbidden fat cooked in milk, as agreed upon above, one **also is not flogged for cooking** the two together.

The other direction, however, is the one reflected in the way rabbinic tradition overwhelmingly relates to *basar be-chalav*: the focus is on the *eating*, and the prohibition of *bishul* is a safeguard erected to prevent one from coming to eat. This is also how the Rambam frames the prohibition — the Torah was so concerned that one not eat this combination that it legislated distance from it by prohibiting even the cooking. On this understanding, cooking is a function of eating; it is a protective hedge rather than an independent prohibition.

From this framing, the Gemara articulates the opinion that says *eino lokei* for cooking in the following terms: *le-hachi afkei Rachmana le-achilah bi-leshon bishul* — the Torah expressed the prohibition of eating in the language of cooking, and this teaches that the two are connected. *Keivan de-achilah lo laki, a-bishul nami lo laki* — since one does not receive lashes for eating the mixture (because *ein issur chal al issur*), one likewise does not receive lashes for cooking it, because cooking is a derivative of eating.

### Derivative Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אַבִּישּׁוּל – כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָקֵי, כִּי פְּלִיגִי אַאֲכִילָה. מַאן דְּאָמַר אֵינוֹ לוֹקֶה – דְּהָא אֵין אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר לוֹקֶה – לְהָכִי אַפְּקַהּ רַחֲמָנָא לַאֲכִילָה בִּלְשׁוֹן בִּישּׁוּל, כֵּיוָן דְּאַבִּישּׁוּל לָקֵי – אַאֲכִילָה נָמֵי לָקֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **And some say** the opposite: **With regard to cooking, everyone agrees that** one **is flogged** for this act, since the principle of a prohibition not taking effect where another prohibition already exists is not relevant. **When they disagree,** it is **with regard to eating. The one who says he is not flogged** applies the principle **that a prohibition does not take effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, and the fat was already prohibited for consumption in its own right before being cooked with milk. **And the one who says he is flogged** holds that it was **for this** reason that **the Merciful One expressed** the prohibition of **eating** meat cooked in milk using **the language of cooking,** to teach that **since one is flogged for cooking** forbidden fat in milk, one **also is flogged for eating** the product.

The *ika de-amri* version of the Gemara reverses the framing entirely. Here, both opinions agree that *bishul* is independent and that one always receives lashes for cooking the mixture — that is the primary prohibition. The debate is then only about *achilah*. The one who says *eino lokei* for eating applies *ein issur chal al issur* in the normal way, with eating saved from *basar be-chalav* lashes by the preexisting *chelev* prohibition. But the one who says *lokei* for eating reasons in the opposite direction: *le-hachi afkei Rachmana le-achilah bi-leshon bishul* — the Torah's formulation of the eating prohibition in the language of cooking teaches that the connection runs the other way. *Keivan de-a-bishul laki, achilah nami laki* — since cooking is the primary concern and one transgresses there, the product of that cooking carries the prohibition through into eating as well, even in a case where one would normally say that the second *issur* does not take hold.

### Possible Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מָר אֲמַר חֲדָא, וּמָר אֲמַר חֲדָא, וְלָא פְּלִיגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests a third explanation of the dispute between Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi: **And if you wish, say** that **one Sage said one** statement **and one Sage said another** statement, **and they do not disagree,** as they are referring to different cases. The Sage who said that one is flogged is referring to the transgression of cooking forbidden fat with milk, whereas the one who said one is not flogged is referring to the consumption of forbidden fat with milk. Everyone agrees that a prohibition does not take effect where another prohibition already exists, and therefore one is flogged only for cooking forbidden fat in milk, but not for eating the two together.

The Gemara closes this sugya with a possible resolution: *mar chada u-mar chada ve-lo pligi* — one statement refers to *bishul* and the other to *achilah*, and they do not actually disagree. The practical upshot, which the Gemara never frames as straightforwardly permissible, is that lashes for the act of eating may be avoided in cases of doubling up — but the prohibition of cooking together remains in force regardless. The Rambam, notably, rules that one does receive lashes for cooking *neveilah* or *chelev* together with milk, reinforcing that the leniency from *ein issur chal al issur* operates, at most, with respect to eating, not to *bishul* itself. This is why the question of cooking *neveilah be-chalav* is a real and live concern for those learning in professional culinary settings, for instance, where the act of cooking such a combination — not merely eating it — is the act in question.

## Exemptions List

### Mei Chalav Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּמֵי חָלָב – פָּטוּר, דָּם שֶׁבִּשְּׁלוֹ בְּחָלָב – פָּטוּר, הַעֲצָמוֹת וְהַגִּידִים וְהַקַּרְנַיִם וְהַטְּלָפַיִם שֶׁבִּשְּׁלָן בְּחָלָב – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *Tosefta* (8:3): **One who cooks** meat **in whey,** the liquid leftover after milk has been curdled, is **exempt** from lashes, as whey is not defined as milk for purposes of the prohibition. Likewise, for **blood that one cooked in milk,** he is **exempt,** as blood is not considered meat. Similarly, with regard to **the bones, the tendons, the horns, and the hooves that one cooked in milk,** he is **exempt.**

The Gemara cites a *baraita* listing cases that are exempt from the *basar be-chalav* prohibition: *ha-mevashel be-mei chalav patur* — one who cooks with *mei chalav*, milk water, is exempt. The identity of *mei chalav* is the subject of a debate among the *Rishonim*. Some identify it as whey — the watery liquid that separates from curds during cheese-making. Others, notably the Rosh, hold that whey retains full biblical *chalav* status. The Rosh identifies *mei chalav* instead as the pure aqueous component of milk, the H₂O constituent that remains once all the substantive milk content has been removed — analogous to what one might think of as the water extracted in the production of powdered milk. Whatever the precise identification, the category is defined by a component of milk so attenuated that it no longer qualifies as *chalav* in the biblical sense.

### Blood Exemption

The *baraita* continues: *dam she-bishlo be-chalav patur* — blood cooked with milk is exempt. The reason is that blood is not considered meat. Just as *mei chalav* fails the milk requirement, blood fails the meat requirement.

### Inedible Parts

Similarly, *atzamot ve-hagidim ve-hakarnayim ve-ha-tlafahim she-bishlam be-chalav patur* — bones, sinews, horns, and hooves cooked with milk are exempt, because they too are not considered meat. The implicit qualification holds that this applies only when there is no marrow or edible content within the bones.

### Forbidden Meat Liable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַפִּגּוּל, וְהַנּוֹתָר, וְהַטָּמֵא, שֶׁבִּשְּׁלָן בְּחָלָב – חַיָּיב!
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* continues: By contrast, with regard to prohibited ***piggul*** meat, i.e., meat from an offering that was sacrificed with the intent to sprinkle its blood or partake of its meat beyond its designated time, **and** similarly prohibited ***notar*,** meat from an offering whose period for consumption has expired, **and** the prohibited **impure** meat of an offering, **that one cooked in milk,** he is **liable** to be flogged for violating the prohibition of meat cooked in milk. This indicates that the prohibition applies to items already prohibited for consumption, contrary to the above opinion attributed to Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi.

In contrast: *ha-piggul, ha-nosar, ve-ha-tamei she-bishlam be-chalav chayav* — if one cooked *piggul* (a sacrificial offering invalidated by improper intent), *nosar* (sacrificial meat left beyond its permitted time), or ritually impure meat together with milk, one is *liable*. These are already forbidden categories of meat. The *baraita* thus makes clear that pre-existing forbidden status of the meat does not exempt from the *basar be-chalav* cooking prohibition.

### Baraita Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי תַּנָּא סָבַר אִיסּוּר חָל עַל אִיסּוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **This *tanna*** of the *baraita* **maintains** in general that **a prohibition takes effect** where another **prohibition** already exists, whereas Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi are of the opposite opinion. It is conceivable that these Sages, who are *amora'im*, disagree with that *tanna*, as the issue of whether a prohibition takes effect where another prohibition already exists is a well-known dispute among the *tanna'im* themselves.

One might think this *baraita* definitively proves that *bishul* is independent of *achilah*: even if one would not receive lashes for eating *piggul* cooked in milk — since the *issur* of *piggul* preexists — the *baraita* declares one liable for the cooking. But the Gemara deflects: *hai tanna savar issur chal al issur* — this *tanna* may hold that one prohibition does take effect upon another, and therefore also receives lashes for eating the mixture. The *baraita* thus does not prove that *bishul* is independent of *achilah* in a case where everyone agrees one would *not* receive lashes for eating. The question remains unresolved.

## Mei Chalav Distinction

### Mishna Mei Chalav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְבַשֵּׁל בְּמֵי חָלָב פָּטוּר, מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, דִּתְנַן: מֵי חָלָב הֲרֵי הֵן כְּחָלָב, וְהַמּוֹחַל הֲרֵי הוּא כְּשֶׁמֶן. אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא לְהַכְשִׁיר אֶת הַזְּרָעִים, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן בִּישּׁוּל בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב – מֵי חָלָב אֵינוֹ כְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** § It was stated in the above *baraita*: **One who cooks** meat **in whey is exempt** from lashes. The Gemara comments: This ruling **supports** the opinion **of Reish Lakish, as we learned** in a mishna, with regard to liquids that render food susceptible to ritual impurity (*Makhshirin* 6:5): The halakhic status of **whey is like** that of **milk,** which is one of the liquids that render food susceptible to impurity, **and** the halakhic status of olive **secretion is like** that of **oil,** which also renders food susceptible to impurity. With regard to this mishna, **Reish Lakish says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard to **rendering seeds,** i.e., produce, **susceptible** to impurity. **But with regard to cooking meat in milk,** the halakhic status of **whey is not like** that of **milk.**

The Gemara notes that the *baraita*'s ruling about *mei chalav* supports a ruling of Reish Lakish. The foundation is a *mishna* that states *mei chalav harei hen ke-chalav* — milk water is treated as milk — as well as *mochel* (the residual liquid from olive pressing after the primary oil has been extracted) being treated as oil. This is stated in the context of *hechsher*: the seven liquids that render food susceptible to *tumah*. One of those seven is *chalav*, and the *mishna* holds that *mei chalav* qualifies as *chalav* for this purpose. Similarly, *mochel* qualifies as oil.

### Reish Lakish Limit

Reish Lakish, commenting on this *mishna*, draws a critical distinction: *lo shanu ela le-hachshir et ha-zra'im* — the ruling that *mei chalav* counts as *chalav* applies only for purposes of making foods and seeds susceptible to *tumah*, where the requirement is simply that a qualifying liquid come into contact with the food. For that purpose, *mei chalav* suffices. However, *aval le-inyan bishul basar be-chalav, mei chalav eino ke-chalav* — when it comes to the prohibition of cooking meat in milk, *mei chalav* does not count as real milk. Reish Lakish's comment distinguishes between the broad category of liquids sufficient for *hechsher* and the more specific requirement of *chalav* needed to trigger the *basar be-chalav* prohibition.

### Baraita Confirmation

The *baraita* examined above confirms Reish Lakish's ruling explicitly, since it exempts cooking with *mei chalav* from *basar be-chalav* liability. Whether Reish Lakish was unaware of the *baraita* and derived his ruling independently, or was paraphrasing its ruling, the two positions are aligned. One should note the Rambam's position in this area: while he holds that things not biblically prohibited may be cooked together without transgression, this leniency applies to categorical exclusions — such as *chazir* or, on his view, *chayah* — rather than to borderline cases that derive from the same species and context as the core prohibition. For items closely adjacent to the main prohibition, such as certain borderline *chalav* derivatives, rabbinic prohibition of cooking would still apply, and the Rambam's treatment of these cases warrants careful review.

## Expansion Logic

### Cross-Species Derivation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ, בַּחֲלֵב פָּרָה וְרָחֵל מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: From the phrase **"in its mother's milk" I have** derived **only** that the prohibition applies **to** the **mother** goat's **milk. From where** do I derive that it also applies **to the milk of a cow and a ewe?**

The Gemara now turns to a *baraita* that works through the logical extension of *basar be-chalav* beyond the literal verse. The *baraita* begins: *bachalav imo — ein li ela bachalav imo, bachalav para ve-rachel minayin?* From the verse's language of *chalav imo* I know only a *gedi* cooked in its own mother's milk. How do I know that the prohibition extends to a calf cooked in cow's milk, or a lamb cooked in sheep's milk — that is, same species but not the literal mother?

### Kal va-Chomer Mother

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ, קַל וָחוֹמֶר: וּמָה אִמּוֹ, שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בְּהַרְבָּעָה – נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בְּבִשּׁוּלוֹ, פָּרָה וְרָחֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֶסְרוּ עִמּוֹ בְּהַרְבָּעָה – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֶסְרוּ עִמּוֹ בְּבִשּׁוּלוֹ! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **You** can **say an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** a kid's **mother, which is not prohibited for mating with** the kid, as they are of the same species, **is** nevertheless **prohibited for cooking with it,** as stated in the verse, **is it not right that a cow or a ewe, which are prohibited for mating with it,** as they are of different species, **should be prohibited for cooking with it?** Therefore, **the verse states** elsewhere: **"In its mother's milk,"** a second time, to include a cow and a ewe.

The *baraita* proposes a *kal va-chomer*: *u-mah imo she-lo ne'esra imo be-harba'ah ne'esra imo be-vishulo* — the mother is not forbidden in terms of *harba'ah*, crossbreeding with the offspring; animals of the same species may be mated even in a mother-child pairing without violating the prohibition of crossbreeding, which is restricted to mixing species. Yet despite this leniency in *harba'ah*, the mother is forbidden together with the offspring in cooking. A cow and a goat, by contrast, *are* forbidden to be crossbred. If the weaker case — same-species mother and child, where crossbreeding is permitted — is forbidden in cooking, then certainly a cross-species case, where the very mating would be prohibited, should all the more be forbidden in cooking. *Eino din she-ne'esar imo be-vishulo?*

### Species Expansion

Therefore, says the *baraita*, the verse *bachalav imo* must be teaching something additional — and one understanding is that it serves to include cow's milk and sheep's milk even beyond the literal goat-and-mother scenario. The verse anchors the prohibition broadly. The Gemara's reasoning here captures an important structural point: the Torah's prohibition simultaneously draws on two conceptual frameworks — the closeness of the mother-child bond on one hand, and the severity attached to cross-species combination on the other. The question of intermediate cases — same species but not the literal mother — lies between these two poles and must be addressed.

## Scripture Necessity

### Rav Ashi Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא? הָא אָתְיָא לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the conclusion of the *baraita*. **But why do I** need **a verse? It was** just **derived** through the *a fortiori* inference.

Rav Ashi raises a challenge: *lama li kra, ha atya lah* — why is the verse needed at all? The *kal va-chomer* just constructed appears sufficient to derive that cross-species cooking is forbidden without requiring a separate scriptural teaching.

### Counter-objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּדִינָא פִּירְכָא, מֵהֵיכָא קָא מַיְיתֵית לַהּ? מֵאִמּוֹ, מָה לְאִמּוֹ שֶׁכֵּן נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, תֹּאמַר בְּפָרָה שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** It is needed **because one can say that** the **refutation of the** *a fortiori* **inference** is present **from the outset,** i.e., there is a difficulty with the comparison: **From where do you derive** the inference? It is derived **from its mother** goat, which the *baraita* presents as being treated more leniently than a cow or ewe since it may be mated with a kid. But there is a stringent aspect of the mother goat not shared by a cow or ewe: **What** is unique **about its mother?** It is unique **in that it is prohibited for slaughter with it,** since one may not slaughter an animal and its mother on the same day (see Leviticus 22:28). **Will you say** the same **about a cow, which is not prohibited for slaughter with it?** Therefore, **the verse states: "In its mother's milk,"** to include a cow and a ewe.

The response is that one could challenge the *kal va-chomer* from its very premise — *me'ikara de-dina pircha*. The argument had proceeded: mother and child, where crossbreeding is permitted, are nonetheless forbidden in cooking — therefore cross-species, where crossbreeding is forbidden, is certainly prohibited in cooking. But one can ask: *mah le-imo she-ken ne'esra imo bi-shchita* — the mother has a particular severity with respect to slaughter; the Torah prohibits slaughtering a mother and child on the same day (*oto ve-et beno*). There is no such prohibition with respect to slaughtering a cow and a goat on the same day. So the mother-child case may be severe not because crossbreeding between them is permitted but because the mother-child relationship itself carries unique stringency, as seen in *shechita*. One cannot therefore automatically extend the prohibition to cross-species cooking, because the reason for the cooking prohibition in the mother case may be precisely this unique mother-child severity, which does not apply across species.

### Verse Necessity

Accordingly, *Talmud lomar bachalav imo* — the verse is necessary to teach that the prohibition extends even in the direction of cross-species cooking, where the mother-child relationship is absent. The verse provides the textual basis for what the *kal va-chomer* alone could not establish, given the objection about the particular severity of the mother-child bond as evidenced by the laws of *shechita*.

## Sister's Milk

### Older Sister Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ, בַּחֲלֵב אֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה מִנַּיִן?
>
> **Translation:** § The above *baraita* accounts for two of the Torah's three mentions of the phrase "in its mother's milk." The Gemara now addresses the third. It **is taught** in **another** *baraita*: From one instance of the phrase **"in its mother's milk" I have** derived **only** that the prohibition applies **to the milk of its mother,** if the mother is less than a year old and has not yet entered the pen for the purpose of its owner's separating the annual animal tithe. **From where** do I derive that it also applies **to the milk of its older sister,** i.e., one that is more than a year old that has already entered the pen for the animal tithe in the previous year?

The Gemara introduces yet another *baraita*: *bachalav imo — ein li ela bachalav imo, bechalav achoto ha-gedolah minayin?* Having established same-species and cross-species cases, the *baraita* now asks about an intermediate scenario: cooking a kid in the milk of its older sister — another animal of the same species, but neither the mother nor a member of a different species.

### Ma'aser Logic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ קַל וָחוֹמֶר, וּמָה אִמּוֹ שֶׁנִּכְנְסָה עִמּוֹ לְדִיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר – נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בְּבִשּׁוּל, אֲחוֹתוֹ שֶׁלֹּא נִכְנְסָה עִמּוֹ לְדִיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בְּבִשּׁוּל! תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **You** can **say an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** a kid's **mother, which enters the pen to be tithed with it, is** nevertheless **prohibited for cooking with it, is it not right that its sister, which does not enter the pen to be tithed with it, should be prohibited for cooking with it?** Therefore, **the verse states** elsewhere: **"In its mother's milk,"** a third time, to include the older sister in the prohibition.

The *kal va-chomer* here operates differently. It argues from the mother: *u-mah imo she-nichnesa imo la-dir le-hit'aser ne'esra imo be-vishul* — the mother can enter the same pen as the child to be counted together for *ma'aser behema* (the tithe levied on newborn animals), since mother and child born in the same year may both be counted for that year's *ma'aser*. Despite this closeness — being in the same *ma'aser* cycle — the mother is forbidden in cooking together with the child. An older sister, *she-lo nichnesa imo la-dir le-hit'aser* — born in a prior year and therefore not countable for *ma'aser* at the same time as the kid — represents a greater degree of difference or distance. If even the closely-reckoned mother is prohibited, should the more-distantly-reckoned older sister not be prohibited all the more so? That is the *kal va-chomer*.

### Rav Ashi Second Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא, הָא אָתְיָא לַהּ? אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר מֵעִיקָּרָא דְּדִינָא פִּירְכָא, מֵהֵיכָא קָא מַיְיתֵית לַהּ? מֵאִמּוֹ, מָה לְאִמּוֹ, שֶׁכֵּן נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה, תֹּאמַר בַּאֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה, שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But why do I** need **a verse? It was** just **derived** from the *a fortiori* inference. **Rav Ashi said:** It is needed **because one can say** that the **refutation of** that *a fortiori* **inference** is present **from the outset. From where do you derive** the inference? It is derived **from its mother.** But one may respond: **What** is unique **about its mother?** It is unique **in that it is prohibited for slaughter with it** on the same day. **Will you say** the same *halakhot* apply **to its older sister, which is not prohibited for slaughter with it?** Therefore, **the verse states: "In its mother's milk,"** including the older sister.

Rav Ashi again raises the objection: *lama li kra* — why does a verse need to teach this? The *kal va-chomer* would appear sufficient.

### Slaughter Difference

The counter-objection is the same as before: *mah le-imo she-ken ne'esra imo bi-shchita* — the mother carries a unique severity through the prohibition of *oto ve-et beno* that the older sister does not. *Tomar be-achoto ha-gedolah shelo ne'esra imo bi-shchita* — an older sister is not subject to any prohibition of being slaughtered together with the kid. Thus the *kal va-chomer* breaks down, and the verse *bachalav imo* is once again required to include this case.

### Common Denominator

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן אֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה, אֲחוֹתוֹ קְטַנָּה מִנַּיִן? אָתְיָא מִבֵּינַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **We have found** a source for the *halakha* that **its older sister** is included in the prohibition of meat cooked in milk. **From where** is it derived that the same applies to **its younger sister,** one that has not yet entered the pen for the tithe? The Gemara responds: **It is derived from between them,** i.e., from the combination of the mother and the older sister.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵהֵי תֵּיתֵי? תֵּיתֵי מֵאִמּוֹ – מָה לְאִמּוֹ שֶׁכֵּן נֶאֶסְרָה עִמּוֹ בִּשְׁחִיטָה? אֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה תּוֹכִיחַ. מָה לַאֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה שֶׁלֹּא נִכְנְסָה עִמּוֹ לְדִיר לְהִתְעַשֵּׂר? אִמּוֹ תּוֹכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the need for both cases in order to derive the third: **From which** of the two cases **should it be derived?** If **it should be derived from its mother,** one can claim: **What** is unique **about its mother?** It is unique **in that it is prohibited for slaughter with it** on the same day, unlike the younger sister. One may respond: **Its older sister can prove** the point, since it is not prohibited for slaughter with the kid, but it is still included in the prohibition of meat cooked in milk. But this can be refuted as well: **What** is unique **about its older sister?** It is unique **in that it does not enter the pen to be tithed with it,** unlike the younger sister. One may respond that **its mother can** then **prove** the point, as it can enter the pen to be tithed with the kid and is still included in the prohibition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחָזַר הַדִּין, לֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, וְלֹא רְאִי זֶה כִּרְאִי זֶה, הַצַּד הַשָּׁוֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן: שֶׁהוּא בָּשָׂר, וְאָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב, אַף אֲנִי אָבִיא אֲחוֹתוֹ קְטַנָּה, שֶׁהוּא בָּשָׂר, וְאָסוּר לְבַשֵּׁל בְּחָלָב.
>
> **Translation:** **And** consequently, **the inference has reverted** to its starting point: **The aspect of this** case **is not like the aspect of that** case **and the aspect of that** case **is not like the aspect of this** case; **their common element** is **that** both the mother and older sister are **meat and** it is **prohibited to cook** them **in milk.** If so, **I will also include its younger sister** in the prohibition, **since it is** also **meat, and** therefore it is **prohibited to cook** it **in milk.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲחוֹתוֹ גְּדוֹלָה נָמֵי תֵּיתֵי מִבֵּינַיָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **If so,** the inclusion of **its older sister** in the prohibition can **also be derived from between them,** i.e., from the combination of the kid's mother and a cow. Although both a cow and the mother possess unique stringencies not shared by the older sister, i.e., the prohibition of mating and of slaughtering with the kid, respectively, neither possesses both stringencies, and the *halakha* with regard to the older sister can be derived from their common element, i.e., that they are both meat and prohibited to cook in milk, as stated above. If so, what need is there to derive this *halakha* from the verse?

At this point, the Gemara extracts the *tzad ha-shaveh* — the common denominator — from the combined cases: *shehu basar ve-assur le-vashel be-chalav*. The common feature of all the prohibited cases is simply that it is meat, and meat may not be cooked in milk. This is a pivotal moment in the Gemara's analysis. Beginning from the very specific and narrow formulation of *gedi be-chalav imo*, the Gemara has traced the logical and exegetical path by which the Sages abstracted the prohibition into the broad principle of *basar be-chalav* — any meat with any milk. It is not about the species of the meat, not about the species of the milk, not about the mother-child relationship, and not about cross-species mixing. It is about the fundamental pairing of meat and milk.

### Verse Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין הָכִי נָמֵי, אֶלָּא ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ לְמָה לִי? מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְתַנְיָא: ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״ – אֵין לִי אֶלָּא בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ,
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Yes,** it **is indeed so;** the *halakha* with regard to the older sister is derived from the common denominator of the mother and a cow. **Rather, why do I** need the verse's additional mention of the phrase **"in its mother's milk?" It is necessary for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: When the verse states: **"In its mother's milk," I have** derived **only** the prohibition to cook the kid **in its mother's milk;**

One might then ask: if we have already derived from the two established cases — mother and cross-species — that the prohibition is general, why was a separate verse needed for the older sister at all? The Gemara replies: *ein hachi nami* — correct, it was not strictly needed for older sister. The verse *bachalav imo* written a second time was needed for a different and more challenging case entirely, to which the Gemara now turns.

## Animal's Own Milk

### Self-Milk Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִיא עַצְמָהּ בַּחֲלָבָהּ מִנַּיִן? אָמַרְתָּ קַל וְחוֹמֶר: וּמָה בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁלֹּא נֶאֱסַר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי בִּשְׁחִיטָה, נֶאֱסָר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם בִּשְׁחִיטָה, מְקוֹם שֶׁנֶּאֱסַר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי בְּבִשּׁוּל – אֵינוֹ דִּין שֶׁנֶּאֱסַר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם בְּבִשּׁוּל? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר ״בַּחֲלֵב אִמּוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** **from where** is it derived that the meat of a mother animal may not **itself** be cooked **in its** own **milk? Say an *a fortiori*** inference: **Just as** with regard to an issue **where** the **fruit is not prohibited with** the **fruit,** i.e., **slaughter,** as it is permitted to slaughter two offspring of one mother on one day, the **fruit is** nevertheless **prohibited for slaughter with the mother, is it not right that** with regard to an issue **where** the **fruit is prohibited with** the **fruit,** i.e., **cooking,** as it is prohibited to cook a mother's offspring with its milk, the **fruit,** i.e., the milk, **should be prohibited for cooking with the mother?** Therefore, **the verse states: "In its mother's milk,"** to prohibit the cooking of the meat of the mother in its own milk.

The final question in the Gemara's chain of derivations is the most conceptually demanding: *atzma be-chalvah minayin?* — how do we know that cooking an animal in its own milk is forbidden? All prior cases involved at minimum two distinct entities: a piece of meat from one animal, and milk from another. Here, there is only one animal. It is simultaneously the source of the meat and the source of the milk. The question is whether the verse needed to be invoked to cover this case, or whether logic sufficed.

### Siblings vs. Mother

The first *kal va-chomer* attempt: *u-mah be-makom she-lo ne'esar pri im pri bi-shchita ne'esar pri im em bi-shchita* — there is no prohibition against slaughtering two siblings on the same day; yet, there is a prohibition against slaughtering a mother with its child. Translating this into the language of *bishul*: two offspring of the mother — meaning a calf and the milk, both understood as products of the mother — should the cooking prohibition not extend to them? *Ne'esar pri im pri be-vishul* — you cannot cook the child (the calf) with another product of the mother (the milk). *Eino din she-ne'esar pri im em be-vishul* — then certainly one should not be able to cook the milk together with the animal that produced it, its mother. That is, if you cannot cook the milk with the calf (because both are *pri* of the mother), you certainly cannot cook the milk with the mother herself — which would be an animal cooked in its own milk.

### Horse-Mule Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא לְמָה לִי קְרָא, הָא אָתְיָא לַהּ? אָמַר רַב אַחָדְבוּי בַּר אַמֵּי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, סוּס בֶּן סוּסְיָא אֲחִי פִּרְדָּה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאָסוּר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי, וּמוּתָּר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם. הָתָם, זֶרַע הָאָב הוּא דְּקָא גָרֵים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **a verse? It was** just **derived** *a fortiori*. **Rav Aḥadvoi bar Ami said:** The verse is necessary **because one can say** that the case of **a horse born of** both a stallion and **mare** but which is **the brother of a mule,** i.e., its mother also bore a mule after being impregnated by a donkey, **proves** this *a fortiori* inference invalid. **As** here the **fruit is prohibited with** the **fruit, but** the **fruit is permitted with the mother.** One may not mate a horse with a mule, but one may mate a horse with its mother. The Gemara rejects this: **There it is the seed of the father that effects** the prohibition of mating the mule with the daughter horse. The two may not be paired for reasons unrelated to their status as offspring of one mother. This ruling therefore proves nothing about the hypothesis that if two fruit cannot be paired, one can learn by *a fortiori* inference that the fruit cannot be paired with the mother.

This *kal va-chomer* is challenged through an analogy involving crossbreeding. Suppose a female horse mated with a donkey and with a stallion, producing a mule and a horse. *She-assur pri im pri* — the horse offspring cannot mate with the mule offspring, because that would constitute *kilayim*, crossbreeding of species. *U-mutar pri im em* — yet the horse offspring can mate with its mother, since horse with horse is not a species violation. Here is a case where *pri im pri* is forbidden but *pri im em* is permitted — the exact inverse of the *kal va-chomer*. Accordingly, the argument from *shechita* does not carry through; the relationship between the two categories is not fixed.

### Reverse Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהָא פֶּרֶד בֶּן סוּסְיָא אֲחִי פִּרְדָּה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁמּוּתָּר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי, וְאָסוּר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** And this explanation must be true, **as** the case of **a mule born of** a donkey and **a mare** and which is **the brother of a female mule proves. As** here one **fruit is permitted with** the other **fruit,** i.e., one may mate the male and female mules since they are of the same species, **and** yet the **fruit is prohibited** to be mated **with the mother,** i.e., the mare. This case illustrates that the prohibition of crossbreeding is contingent on paternity, and does not rest solely on the relation between the mother and the fruit.

The Gemara then examines the reverse scenario: a female horse mated with a donkey, producing two mules. *She-mutar pri im pri* — the two mule siblings can mate with each other, since mule with mule is not a species violation. *Ve-assur pri im em* — yet neither mule can mate with the horse mother, since mule and horse are distinct species. Here the pattern reverses again: *pri im pri* is permitted while *pri im em* is forbidden. The Gemara concludes that the relationship between *pri im pri* and *pri im em* in crossbreeding follows the identity of the father's species, not the mother's. It is not a stable indicator of relative severity and cannot serve as a reliable basis for the *kal va-chomer*. Accordingly, this line of reasoning does not prove the case.

### Slave-Woman Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר מָר בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבִינָא: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, עֶבֶד בֶּן שִׁפְחָה אֲחִי מְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁאָסוּר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי, וּמוּתָּר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם! הָתָם גֵּט שִׁיחְרוּר הוּא דְּקָא גָרֵים, דְּהָא עֶבֶד בֶּן מְשׁוּחְרֶרֶת אֲחִי שִׁפְחָה יוֹכִיחַ, שֶׁמּוּתָּר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי, וְאָסוּר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Mar, son of Ravina, said:** The *a fortiori* inference is invalid **because one can say that** the case of a male Canaanite **slave born of a female slave** and who is **the brother of a female freed** slave **proves** it invalid. **As** here the **fruit is prohibited with** the **fruit,** i.e., the slave may not engage in sexual intercourse with his freed sister, **and** yet the **fruit is permitted with** his slave **mother.** The Gemara rejects this as well: **There it is the bill of manumission that effects** the prohibition. The slave is prohibited to his sister only because she has been freed, not because they are both fruit of one mother, **as** the case of a male **slave, son of a female freed** slave, and **the brother of a female slave proves. As** here the **fruit is permitted with** the **fruit,** i.e., he is permitted to his sister, since they are both slaves, **and** yet the **fruit is prohibited with** his released **mother,** as she is now considered a full-fledged Jew. Clearly the prohibition is entirely contingent on the emancipation of one of the slaves, irrespective of their relationship.

Another attempt is advanced: a slave woman has two children; one is subsequently freed while the other remains a slave. *She-assur pri im pri* — the freed sibling (now a fully Jewish free person) may not engage in prohibited relations with the slave sibling. *U-mutar pri im em* — the slave child may engage in relations with someone of the same status as its mother, i.e., another slave. Again: *pri im pri* is prohibited while *pri im em* is permitted. Yet the Gemara again dismisses this: *hatam get shichrur hu de-ka garem* — it is the document of emancipation that determines the parameters, not the abstract sibling/mother relationship. The Gemara confirms this by presenting the reverse: a free woman's children, one of whom becomes enslaved, can now mate with a slave (someone of their own kind) but not with a free person (someone of the mother's kind). The pattern again inverts. Neither example establishes a fixed logical principle.

### Kilai Zera'im

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר אָבִין: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, כִּלְאֵי זְרָעִים יוֹכִיחוּ, שֶׁאָסוּר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי, וּמוּתָּר פְּרִי עִם הָאֵם. כְּלוּם נֶאֱסָר פְּרִי עִם פְּרִי אֶלָּא עַל יְדֵי הָאֵם? דְּהָא חִיטֵּי וּשְׂעָרֵי בְּכַדָּא וְלָא מִיתַּסְרוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Idi bar Avin said:** The *a fortiori* inference is invalid **because one can say** that the case of **diverse kinds of seeds proves** it invalid. **As** it **is prohibited** to sow **fruit** of one species **with fruit** of another species, **and** yet it is **permitted** to sow all types of **fruit with the mother,** i.e., the ground, from which all fruit grows. The Gemara rejects this as well: **Isn't fruit** of one species **with fruit** of another species **prohibited only by means of the mother,** i.e., the ground? The prohibition of sowing diverse seeds applies only when they are both sown in the ground, **as wheat and barley** can be placed **in** a single **jug and they are not prohibited.**

One more attempt: *kilai zera'im* — crossbreeding seeds. You may not plant wheat and barley (*pri im pri*) together, but you may plant either grain into the ground (*pri im em*, the earth being the mother of all crops). Here *pri im pri* is forbidden and *pri im em* is permitted. The Gemara, however, deflects: *klum ne'esar pri im pri ela al yedei ha'em?* — is the crossbreeding of wheat and barley in the field not always by means of the mother, the earth? You cannot plant them side by side without involving the earth. Indeed, if you put wheat and barley in a bowl together, there is no prohibition. It is always the earth — the mother — that is the operative medium, so you cannot say the mother is uninvolved.

### One Body Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: מִשּׁוּם דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר, מָה לִפְרִי עִם פְּרִי, שֶׁכֵּן שְׁנֵי גּוּפִים, תֹּאמַר בִּפְרִי עִם הָאֵם, שֶׁכֵּן גּוּף אֶחָד, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי אִיצְטְרִיךְ קְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Ashi said:** The *a fortiori* inference is invalid **because one can say: What** is unique **about fruit with fruit,** e.g., the kid and its mother's milk, which are prohibited for cooking together? They are unique **in that** they are **two** separate **bodies** that were never unified. Must **you say** the same prohibition of pairing applies **to** the **fruit with the mother,** i.e., the mother's meat and its milk, **which** were once **one body? Due to this** reason, **the** extra **verse** stating: "In its mother's milk," **was necessary** to include the meat and milk of the same animal in the prohibition.

At last, Rav Ashi provides the decisive answer: *mah li pri mi-pri she-ken shnei gufim* — the *kal va-chomer* from calf-and-milk fails because in that case there are *two bodies* involved. The mother is one body, the calf is another body; the milk is one body, the calf is another body. Even in the case of milk and calf — both called *pri* of the mother — they are physically distinct entities. One could therefore argue that the prohibition requires the presence of two distinct bodies, two animals as it were, and that an animal cooked in its own milk — which is a single entity, *guf echad* — falls outside the prohibition.

### Verse Needed

Accordingly, *Tomar be-pri em she-ken guf echad* — you might indeed say that an animal with its own milk, being one entity, is excluded. *Mishum hachi itztarich kra* — it is for precisely this reason that the verse was necessary. The extra formulation *bachalav imo* in the verse comes to teach that even where only one body is involved — an animal cooked in its own milk — the prohibition of *basar be-chalav* applies. This is the final and most difficult case in the Gemara's systematic expansion of the prohibition from the literal *gedi be-chalav imo* to its full logical scope, and it required explicit scriptural support precisely because logic might have led us to exclude it.
