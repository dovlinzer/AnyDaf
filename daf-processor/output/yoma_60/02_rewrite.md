# Yoma 60 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Dam: Me'ilah & Restricti…

### Blood Disposal Overview

The daf opens at the very top of the page, picking up from where the *Mishnah* had described the disposal of sacrificial blood on *Yom Kippur*. After the blood of the inner service is poured at the base of the outer *mizbeach* — both the special *Yom Kippur* blood deposited on the western side and the ordinary *korbanot* blood deposited on the southern side — all of it flows together through an underground channel into *Nachal Kidron*, the Kidron wadi, where it is sold as fertilizer. Before the sale, the money paid redeems the *kedusha* onto itself; but even before that point, a question arises concerning *me'ilah*.

One might expect that only a rabbinic *me'ilah* would apply at this stage, since the *mitzvah* has already been performed. Yet the *Gemara* makes a bolder claim altogether.

### No Me'ilah Before Mitzvah

The *Gemara* states that there is no *me'ilah* on the blood of *korbanot* even before the *mitzvah* is performed — even while the blood is still sanctified and being brought to the *mizbeach*. This is a striking ruling, and the *Gemara* establishes it on the basis of three distinct *pesukim*.

### Three Pesukim Analysis

The three textual sources are *natati lachem* ("I have given it to you"), *lechaper* ("for atonement"), and the word *hu* in the phrase *ki hadam hu*. The logic of *lechaper* is particularly significant: blood is not being offered to God in the way that limbs and flesh are. Rather, it is placed on the *mizbeach* in order to effectuate *kapara* — but it is not itself an offering. The word *natati lachem* similarly implies that the blood is given over for human purposes, i.e., for the *kapara*, not for the domain of *me'ilah*.

The word *hu* carries its own distinct implication: just as there is no *me'ilah* on blood after the *mitzvah* is done — since at that stage the *mitzvah* is complete — so too there is no *me'ilah* before the *mitzvah* is done. The underlying logic, derived from *hu*, is that the pre-*mitzvah* and post-*mitzvah* statuses mirror each other. The *Gemara* then asks: why not conclude instead that *me'ilah* applies both before and after? The answer is that we know, as a general principle, that *me'ilah* does not apply after a *mitzvah* has been fulfilled — except for a handful of recognized exceptions — and those exceptions serve to prove the general rule.

### Exceptions Prove Rule

The *Gemara* identifies two categories as exceptions: *trumat hadeshen* and *bigdei kehunah*. The ritual placement of ashes (*trumat hadeshen*), performed each morning on the side of the *mizbeach*, remains forbidden in benefit even after the ashes are deposited, and the *bigdei kehunah* worn by the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur* cannot be used again after the service. These two cases, the *Gemara* argues, constitute what is known as *shenei kesuvim habayim ke'echad* — two scriptural exceptions that, rather than establishing a rule, actually demonstrate that the rule runs in the opposite direction. Two exceptions prove that in all other cases, the restriction lapses once the *mitzvah* is done.

It is worth noting a significant *Tosfos* on this point, which was discussed previously. Regarding *trumat hadeshen*, *Rashi* reasons that since benefit from the ashes remains forbidden after the *mitzvah*, we may infer that the *kedusha* persists and that *me'ilah* technically applies. *Tosfos*, however, disagrees: it is not necessary to say that *me'ilah* applies to *trumat hadeshen*, only that it remains *assur behana'ah* — forbidden in benefit. That prohibition after the *mitzvah* is itself sufficient to qualify as an exception and thereby establish the general rule that everywhere else the restriction falls away.

### Rabbi Dosa Position

The *Gemara* then raises a complication: according to *Rabbi Dosa*, the *bigdei kehunah* of *Yom Kippur* may in fact be used afterward — not for a subsequent *Yom Kippur*, but by a regular *kohen* for ordinary service, since the white garments of the *Kohen Gadol* are identical in form to those worn by a regular *kohen*. As *Rashi* notes, this position may seem somewhat ironic — one might have thought that using the *Kohen Gadol*'s garments for a lesser service represents a descent in *kedusha* — but for *Rabbi Dosa* the operative concern is the uniqueness of each *Yom Kippur*. Each year's service requires fresh garments; reuse for *another* Yom Kippur is prohibited, but use by a *kohen hedyot* is entirely permissible. Under *Rabbi Dosa*'s view, the *bigdei kehunah* are no longer prohibited after the *mitzvah* is completed, and therefore cannot serve as the exception that proves the rule.

### Eglah Arufah Exception

With *bigdei kehunah* disqualified as an exception under *Rabbi Dosa*, the *Gemara* substitutes a different case: *eglah arufah*. The heifer whose neck is broken remains forbidden in benefit even after the *mitzvah* of the neck-breaking is performed. Together with *trumat hadeshen*, this gives us two exceptions — and with two exceptions, the rule goes the other way: in all other cases, the restriction lapses after the *mitzvah*. Therefore blood, like everything else, should be permissible after the *mitzvah* is done, and since that is so after the fact, it is likewise free of *me'ilah* even beforehand.

The *Gemara* then asks what happens according to the opinion that maintains that even two exceptions are not conclusive — that two cases could themselves establish the rule rather than prove its opposite. In that case, additional textual support is needed. The *Gemara* responds that each exception contains an extra limiting word: *vesamo* (regarding *trumat hadeshen*, "and he shall place it there") and *veha'arufah* (regarding *eglah arufah*, "the one with the broken neck") — both implying that the restriction applies uniquely to these cases. Even for the opinion that two exceptions are insufficient, the combination of two exceptions plus two *mi'utim* — four limiting indicators in total — is more than adequate to establish that these are the exceptions and that the general rule is leniency after the *mitzvah*.

### Blood Exclusions

Having established that there is no biblical *me'ilah* on blood even before the *mitzvah* is done, the *Gemara* now asks: why were three separate *pesukim* needed to make this point? If every *amora* simply selected one of the three words — *natati lachem*, *lechaper*, or *hu* — as sufficient to exclude blood from *me'ilah*, what work are the other two words doing?

The *Gemara* answers that the three exclusions are not all about *me'ilah*. Each word excludes blood from a different restriction that normally applies to *korbanot*:

One word excludes blood from *nosar*. If blood was left over and not applied to the *mizbeach*, and someone ate it, he would incur the biblical prohibition of eating blood — but not the separate *karet* penalty for eating *nosar*. *Rashi* notes that this is not merely a case of avoiding double punishment: even if one was *shogeg* with respect to the blood prohibition but *mezid* with respect to *nosar*, no penalty for *nosar* attaches. The blood simply does not fall within the category of *nosar* at all.

A second word excludes blood from the prohibitions associated with *tumah*. Eating blood while in a state of *tumah* does not incur the separate prohibition of eating *kodshim* in a state of ritual impurity — the *tumah* restriction that applies to sacrificial flesh does not apply to blood.

A third word, as already discussed, excludes blood from *me'ilah* itself.

This is a remarkable cluster of exclusions. It is one thing to say that *me'ilah* does not apply to blood, since *me'ilah* is a somewhat narrow category — it requires that something be *meyuchad* for God, dedicated to the divine domain, and one could argue that blood, for all its centrality to the *mizbeach*, is being granted to the people (*natati lachem*) and is therefore outside that domain. But *nosar* and *tuma* restrictions flow from the inherent *kedusha* of the offering itself. The idea that blood escapes all of these restrictions — that it is, as it were, freed from the normal stringencies of *korbanot* — is a profound and somewhat startling *chiddush*.

### Pigul & Matir Hu

The *Gemara* notes that there is a fourth prohibition associated with *korbanot* — *pigul* — and asks whether blood likewise escapes liability for *pigul*. The answer is yes, and moreover, no special *pasuk* is needed to derive this exclusion. The *Gemara* cites a principle: *kol sheyesh lo matirin bein l'adam bein l'mizbeach chayavin alav mishum pigul* — anything that becomes permissible (either for human consumption or for the *mizbeach*) by virtue of another act performed upon something else is subject to *pigul*. Thus, the meat of a sacrifice becomes permissible to eat because of the *zrikat hadam*; the *imurim* become permissible on the *mizbeach* because of the *zrikat hadam*; the remnant of a *mincha* becomes permissible because of the *kometz*. In each case, the thing that becomes permissible is the *mutar*, the B, while the thing that permits it — the A — is the *matir*. *Pigul* applies to the *mutar*, not to the *matir*.

Blood, however, is itself the *matir*. *V'hadam atzmo matir hu* — the blood is not the thing that becomes permissible as a result of some other act; it is the thing that does the permitting. Therefore, *pigul* cannot apply to it by definition, without any special verse being needed.

*Rashi* provides the *pesukim* from which this principle is derived. The broader implication is that blood is not simply one component of the *korban* among others — it occupies the structural position of the act that enables all the other components. Everything else in the *korban* derives its permitted status from what the blood does. All the restrictions of *korbanot* — *pigul*, *nosar*, *tumah*, *me'ilah* — attach to the thing that receives its status through the sacrificial process. The blood, which stands at the other side of that equation as the *matir* itself, remains categorically outside all of them. This connects directly to the theme of *lechaper* and *natati lachem*: the blood represents the very essence of the sacrificial act, and it is perhaps for that reason — counterintuitive as it seems — that none of the derivative restrictions of the *korban* framework can reach it.

## Inner Service Completion

### Service Order Review

With the discussion of *dam* concluded, the *Gemara* now steps back to survey the whole arc of the inner *avodah*. The *Kohen Gadol* wears the white garments twice on *Yom Kippur*; the first and more extensive wearing encompasses the entire inner service. What has he done during this period? He has performed the *ketoret*, the blood of the *par*, and then the blood of the *se'ir*. The one major component of the *Yom Kippur* service not yet addressed at this point is the *se'ir la'azazel*, the scapegoat — but that takes place in the *azarah*, not inside the *heichal*. He performs a *viduy* over it and sends it off; the dramatic account of its dispatch over the cliff lies ahead. For now, the focus is on what the *Kohen Gadol* has accomplished through the inner service.

### Pesukim Emphasis

The *Torah*'s own language underscores the significance of this completion. The *pasuk* reads: *vechila mekapper et hakodesh* — when he finishes atoning for the sanctuary — and then proceeds to describe the stages. The sequence in the *pesukim* is notably structured around three distinct locations, each marked with its own *kapara*:

After the *kodesh kodashim* and the *parochet*, the *pesukim* already signal a major stage of completion: *vechila mekapper et hakodesh*, which corresponds to *kodesh kodashim*. Then: *veyatza el hamizbeach asher lifnei Hashem vechiper alav* — even before reaching the *mizbeach*, the text emphasizes that the *kodesh kodashim* and the *parochet* together have achieved a substantial measure of *kappara*. The *Kohen Gadol* then proceeds to the *mizbeach*, where he performs the sprinkling with the mixed blood of the *par* and the *se'ir* upon the *karnot*, followed by the seven sprinklings. And then comes the full closure: *vechila mekapper et hakodesh ve'et Ohel Moed ve'et hamizbeach* — "and he completed atoning for the sanctuary, and the Tent of Meeting, and the *mizbeach*." These three phrases correspond precisely to the three components: *kodesh kodashim*, *parochet* (*Ohel Moed*), and *mizbeach*. The *pasuk* ends: *ve'hikriv et hase'ir hehai* — and he brought forward the living goat. He is done with the inner service; what remains is the *se'ir la'azazel*.

### Nine Steps Enumerated

Reconstructing the order of the service clarifies the structure of what follows. The sequence runs as follows: first, the *shechitat hapar* — performed in the northern section of the *azarah* (this is step one); second, the *chafinat haketoret*, the scooping of the incense (this will become significant shortly, and may be listed as step 2a); third, the *hakravat ketoret*, the burning of the incense inside; then, retrieving the blood of the *par* (step 3a); after which come the sprinklings of *dam hapar* inside the *kodesh kodashim* — first *achat*, then *achat ve'achat*, *achat ve'shtayim*, and so on; then the *shechitat hase'ir*; then the sprinklings of *dam hase'ir* inside the *kodesh kodashim*; then the sprinklings of both bloods on the *parochet*; then the mixing and application of the combined blood on the *karnot* of the *mizbeach*, followed by the seven sprinklings there. Finally, the *shirayin* — the pouring of the remaining blood at the base of the outer *mizbeach*. Looking at those *pesukim* again against this ordered sequence, the *Torah*'s emphasis on completion and *kapara* at each stage comes into sharp relief.

## Order & Sequence Rules

### Out of Order Principle

The *Mishnah* now states: *kol ma'aseh Yom HaKippurim ha'amur al haseder — im hikdim ma'aseh lachaveiro lo asa klum*. The entire *Yom Kippur* service, which has been given an ordered sequence, must be performed in that order; if one act was performed before another, that act accomplished nothing.

An important conceptual point must be underscored here. One might think that performing an act out of order contaminates everything that follows, rendering subsequent steps also disordered and therefore invalid. But that is not how the *Mishnah* reads the rule. Only the act that jumped the line is invalid; it counts as nothing and is treated as though it never happened. Everything that was properly performed before it remains valid, and everything that would have followed it in order simply continues from where it should have been. The errant act is set aside; it did not actually constitute an *avodah* at all, so it cannot render anything else disordered.

### Interruption Protocol

The *Mishnah* then addresses a different type of problem — not a sequencing error but an interruption. If one was in the middle of sprinkling the blood inside the *kodesh kodashim* and the blood spilled — performing *achat*, *achat ve'achat*, *achat ve'shtayim*, and then losing the blood — the remedy is to bring new blood. "Bringing new blood" does not mean drawing from a stored supply; it means slaughtering another ox entirely. The *Mishnah* specifies: *yavi gam acher — v'yachzor bitchila mifnim*. Bring other blood and restart from the beginning of that *avodah*.

This naturally raises a further question: if a new ox must be slaughtered, does that not also require slaughtering a new *se'ir*, since the original sequence of *shechitat hapar* and *shechitat hase'ir* was tied together? The *Mishnah* does not address this directly; its focus is narrowly on where one picks up after an interruption within a single component of the service.

### Three Components

The *Mishnah* then articulates a fundamental conceptual principle: *shekulam kapara bifnei atzman* — each of the three components is an independent *kapara* in its own right. Therefore, if blood spills during the *heichal* service, one goes back to the beginning of the *heichal* service — but not to the *kodesh kodashim*. What was completed there is done; it has been "set aside" and need not be repeated. Similarly, if blood spills during the *mizbeach* service, one repeats only the *mizbeach* component, without returning to the *kodesh kodashim* or the *parochet*. This gives us not only a practical rule but a conceptual framework: the *kodesh kodashim*, the *parochet*, and the *mizbeach* are three structurally independent units of *kapara*.

### Resume Mid-Service View

*Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Shimon* go further: if blood spills mid-service and new blood is obtained, one does not even need to restart the interrupted component from its beginning — one may resume precisely where one stopped. If one had completed *achat ve'shalosh*, one brings new blood and continues from *achat ve'arba*. Their *chiddush* is that the continuity of the *avodah* is not broken by the change of animal; the new blood steps in and continues the same act.

### Ketoret Question

This framework, however, generates a deep difficulty. The *Mishnah* addresses the scenario of spilling blood during the inner service and starting again with a new ox. But if one is starting again with a new ox and going back to the beginning of the *kodesh kodashim* service, one might ask: what about the *ketoret*, which was performed earlier in the sequence? The *ketoret* is part of the ordered service, listed among the steps that precede the inner blood sprinklings. And what if one forgot the *ketoret* entirely — skipping over it and proceeding directly to the blood service?

In that case, everything performed after the missing *ketoret* is by definition *hikdim* — done before the *ketoret* was done. One would have to go back and perform the *ketoret*, and then redo everything that had been rendered out of order. Similarly, if one must now slaughter a new ox and return to step one inside, the question of why one does not also need to re-perform the *ketoret* — which was done earlier in the sequence and is connected to all of this — is not immediately resolved by the *Mishnah* and will be taken up by the *Gemara*.

## Order Disputes Debate

### Tanu Rabanan Intro

A *baraita* opens: *kol ma'asei Yom HaKippurim amurim al haseder* — the entirety of the *Yom Kippur* service which has been given an order. The word *amurim* — "stated" — is itself worth noting: it could refer to the order as stated by the Rabbis in the *Mishnah*, or more directly to the order as stated in the *pesukim* themselves, which the *Mishnah* closely follows. In either reading, the point is the same: there is a prescribed sequence, and deviating from it has consequences. *Im hikdim ma'aseh lachaveiro lo asa klum.*

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

*Rabbi Yehuda* asks: when is this true — when does performing something out of order render that act invalid? His answer: *bidvarim hana'asim bevigdei lavan mifnim* — only things done in the white garments inside. Here, *mifnim* does not mean the *heichal* in general; it means specifically the *kodesh kodashim*. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, the binding force of *seder* — such that a deviation renders the act null — applies only to the actions performed in the white garments while inside the *kodesh kodashim*: the *ketoret*, the *dam hapar* sprinklings, and the *dam hase'ir* sprinklings. Outside of that — including the service at the *parochet* and the *mizbeach*, performed in the white garments but in the *heichal* rather than the *kodesh kodashim* — if one performed an act out of order, *ma she'asa asuy*: what was done is done and remains valid *bedi'eved*.

### Rabbi Nechemiah Position

*Rabbi Nechemiah* disagrees. He holds: *bameh devarim amurim — bidvarim hana'asim bevigdei lavan*. The binding *seder* applies to everything done in the white garments, *bein mifnim bein mibachutz* — whether inside the *kodesh kodashim* or in the *heichal*. The *parochet* service and the *mizbeach* service are equally subject to *seder*; going out of order in either location renders the act invalid.

Where does *Rabbi Nechemiah* draw the line? At the golden garments. *Aval devarim hana'asim bevigdei zahav mibachutz* — things performed while wearing the golden garments outside — such as the *tamid*, the *musaf*, and specifically the *ayil* brought by *Aharon* and the *ayil* brought for the people — if performed out of order, the act remains valid *bedi'eved*. The order requirement applies to what is special, and what is special is the white-garment service.

An interesting question arises about *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s position regarding the *se'ir la'azazel* and the *viduy* performed over it. That too is conducted in the white garments, and it takes place in the *azarah* — which, since *Rabbi Nechemiah* frames his rule as *bein mifnim bein mibachutz*, and since *mibachutz* in the context of *bigdei zahav* includes the *azarah*, it would appear that the *se'ir la'azazel* and its *viduy* are also subject to *seder* according to *Rabbi Nechemiah*. Everything performed in the white garments — whether in the *kodesh kodashim*, the *heichal*, or the *azarah* — requires the proper sequence, while the golden-garment service is excluded.

### Pesuk Derivation

Where do *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Nechemiah* derive their respective positions? The *pasuk* in question reads: *vezot lachem lechukat olam — achat bashanah*. "This shall be for you an eternal edict — once a year." The word *chok* (edict, statute) implies that things must be done exactly as prescribed, leaving no room for deviation. The phrase *achat bashanah* — once a year — signals something unique, something that happens only once annually. The question is: which aspects of the *Yom Kippur* service are being described as that uniquely annual *chok*?

### Rabbi Yochanan Analysis

*Rabbi Yochanan* observes that both *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Nechemiah* derive their positions from this single *pasuk* — *sheneihem mikra echad darshu*.

One might think that *Rabbi Yehuda*'s limitation to the *kodesh kodashim* follows from the word *achat* — once a year, the one place entered only once a year is the *kodesh kodashim*, and therefore that is what is uniquely *chok*. But the *Gemara* objects: the *pasuk* does not say "place" (*makom*), it says "once" (*achat*). Why would *achat* specifically indicate the *kodesh kodashim* and not everything done in the white garments, since the entire white-garment service is performed only on this one unique day?

The *Gemara* therefore reconstructs *Rabbi Yehuda*'s derivation differently. The *pasuk* contains two limiting words: the word *zot* ("this") and the word *achat*. *Zot* is a *mi'ut* — it restricts. *Achat* is also a *mi'ut*. Had we only the word *achat*, one might have concluded as *Rabbi Nechemiah* does — that the entire white-garment service is subject to *seder*. But the word *zot* constricts the category further, narrowing it down to an even smaller subset: specifically, things done inside, in the *kodesh kodashim* itself.

*Rabbi Nechemiah* agrees that there are two limiting words — *zot* and *achat* — but reads them differently. One word excludes the golden-garment service from the *chok* of *seder*; the other word excludes not the *heichal* service (which *Rabbi Nechemiah* maintains is subject to *seder*) but rather the *shirayin*, the pouring of the remnant blood at the base of the *mizbeach*. According to *Rabbi Nechemiah*, if the *shirayin* was performed out of order, it does not invalidate what came before or after, because *shirayin* is not an essential component of the *kapara*.

*Rabbi Yehuda* responds to this in a formulation that initially sounds tautological: *im me'akvei me'akvei ve'im lo me'akvei lo me'akvei* — if it is *me'akev* then it is *me'akev*, and if it is not *me'akev* then it is not *me'akev*. What this means is that *Rabbi Yehuda* refuses to treat the *shirayin* as a distinct category unto itself. If, as *Rabbi Nechemiah* maintains, the entire white-garment service is subject to *seder*, then *shirayin*, which is part of that service and is performed in the white garments, should be subject to *seder* as well. And if one wants to argue that *shirayin* is not subject to *seder* because it is not essential, then one should concede *Rabbi Yehuda*'s own position that the binding *seder* applies only to the truly interior, innermost service. There is no principled middle ground, in *Rabbi Yehuda*'s view, between his own position and *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s as *Rabbi Yehuda* understands it.

## Shirayin Status Debate

### Vichilah Emphasis

Having established the debate about the scope of *seder*, the *Gemara* turns to the broader question of the *shirayin*'s status in the *Yom Kippur* service. The *pasuk* reads: *vechila mekapper et hakodesh* — "and he completed atoning for the sanctuary." This phrase — the language of completion — becomes the focal point of a dispute about when exactly *kapara* is achieved.

### Akiva vs Yehuda

*Rabbi Akiva* and *Rabbi Yehuda* debate the emphasis of the phrase. *Rabbi Akiva* reads it as: *im kipper — kilah* — if one has performed the atonement, then one is done. The emphasis falls on *kipper*: once the essential acts of atonement are complete, the service is finished. *Rabbi Yehuda* inverts the reading: *im kilah — kipper* — only when one has fully completed everything, only then has the atonement been achieved. The emphasis here falls on *kilah*, completion, as the condition for *kapara*.

The practical formulation of *Rabbi Yehuda*'s view is: *sheim chissar achat min hamattanot lo asa velo klum* — if one omitted even one of the applications of blood, one has accomplished nothing. Nothing in the atonement takes effect until every element is performed.

### Shirayin Integral?

A parallel *sugya* elsewhere in the *Gemara* asks: what is the practical difference between the two readings? *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi* debate this. One opinion holds that the difference is only hermeneutic — a matter of how one reads the syntax of the *pasuk*, with no practical halakhic consequence; both readings amount to the same thing. The other opinion holds that the practical difference is precisely this: *shirayin me'akvei ika beinayhu* — whether the *shirayin*, the pouring of the remnant blood, is *me'akev* the *kapara* on *Yom Kippur*.

For the rest of the year, the pouring of *shirayin* is generally not *me'akev* — if one omitted it, the *korban* remains valid and the *kapara* is achieved. But on *Yom Kippur*, the *Torah*'s language of *vechila* — "he completed" — may impose a higher standard. If the emphasis is on finishing everything, then even the *shirayin*, normally secondary, becomes integral to the *kapara* of *Yom Kippur*.

### Nechemiah Contradiction

This brings us to the tension in *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s position. *Rabbi Nechemiah* holds, as described above, that the *seder* of the entire white-garment service is *me'akev* — with the one exception that the *shirayin* may be performed out of order without consequence. His reasoning would presumably be that *shirayin* is not essential to the *kapara*, and therefore the *seder* requirement does not apply to it.

But *Rabbi Yochanan* elsewhere states explicitly — *tana Rabbi Nechemiah kedivrei haomer shirayin me'akvei* — that *Rabbi Nechemiah* holds that *shirayin* is indeed *me'akev*. If that is so, how can *Rabbi Nechemiah* simultaneously say that disrupting the order of *shirayin* carries no consequences? If *shirayin* is an essential *me'akev* component of the service, it ought to be fully subject to the *seder* requirement, just as every other essential component is. The *Gemara* is left with *kashya* — an unresolved difficulty.

The context of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s statement about *Rabbi Nechemiah* is itself instructive. It arises not from *Yom Kippur* directly but from a discussion in *Zevachim* about *shchutei chutz* — the prohibition against performing sacrificial acts outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. One who slaughters a *kodesh* animal outside is liable; one who burns *imurim* on an external altar is liable. The question is whether pouring *shirayin* on the base of an external structure also incurs liability. *Rabbi Nechemiah* holds that it does. *Rabbi Yochanan* asks: on what basis? Normally *shirayin* is not *me'akev*, which would seem to mark it as a lesser *avodah* with insufficient weight to trigger the *shchutei chutz* prohibition. *Rabbi Yochanan* concludes that *Rabbi Nechemiah* must hold *shirayin* is *me'akev* — otherwise his position on *shchutei chutz* makes no sense.

### Tosafot Resolution

The difficulty is that this *sugya* in *Zevachim* appears to make *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s position a general one — that *shirayin* is *me'akev* throughout the year — which stands in tension with the position attributed to him in our *sugya*, where he seems to treat *shirayin* as a lesser component excluded from the *seder* requirement.

*Tosfos* resolves this by arguing that *Rabbi Yochanan*'s statement should not be read as attributing to *Rabbi Nechemiah* a general position that *shirayin* is always *me'akev*. Rather, *Rabbi Nechemiah* would never attribute enough weight to the *shirayin* to make it a *shchutei chutz* violation were it not for the fact that on *Yom Kippur*, at least, *shirayin* rises to the level of *me'akev*. That elevated status — the exceptional weight of *shirayin* specifically on *Yom Kippur* — is what justifies treating the act of pouring *shirayin* as a sufficiently significant *avodah* to trigger liability when performed outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. This is comparable to the burning of *imurim*, which is not *me'akev* in general but is nevertheless a recognized *avodah* whose external performance is prohibited. The *Yom Kippur* context, with its uniquely rigorous standards encoded in *vechila*, provides the underlying weight that *Rabbi Nechemiah* requires.

The overall picture that emerges is as follows: *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the binding *seder* applies only to the *kodesh kodashim* service; *Rabbi Nechemiah* holds that it extends to the entire white-garment service, with the one modification that *shirayin* — whose status as *me'akev* on *Yom Kippur* is itself a matter of debate — is excluded from the *seder* requirement. The tension in *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s position, as identified by the *Gemara*, reflects the deeper ambiguity about whether the unique weight of *Yom Kippur* elevates the *shirayin* to full *me'akev* status (which would make excluding it from *seder* difficult to justify) or whether it remains in a somewhat intermediate category even on *Yom Kippur*.

## Chafinat Haketoret Case

### Ketoret Before Par

The *Gemara* now cites a ruling of *Rabbi Yochanan* (transmitted by *Rabbi Chanina*): if one performed the *chafinat haketoret* — the scooping of the incense — before the *shechitat hapar*, the act accomplished nothing: *lo asa velo klum*.

To understand the significance of this ruling, it must be located within the sequence established earlier. The full order of the service assigns the *shechitat hapar* as step one, the *chafinat haketoret* as step 2a (preparing the incense scoop), and the actual *hakravat ketoret* (the burning of the incense inside) as step 2b. If one performed the scooping of the incense before slaughtering the ox, one has gone out of order.

### Not Rabbi Yehuda

The *Gemara* immediately notes: *keman — dela k'Rabbi Yehuda* — this ruling is not in accordance with *Rabbi Yehuda*. Recall that *Rabbi Yehuda* holds that the *seder* requirement is binding only for acts performed in the white garments inside the *kodesh kodashim*. The *chafinat haketoret* and the *shechitat hapar* both take place outside the *kodesh kodashim* — in the *azarah* and the *heichal* respectively. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, going out of order in that zone carries no *bedi'eved* consequence. Yet *Rabbi Yochanan*'s ruling says the premature scooping is entirely invalid. This can only be consistent with *Rabbi Nechemiah*'s broader position — or at least with a view that extends the *seder* requirement beyond the *kodesh kodashim* interior.

### Tzorech Pnim

The *Gemara* then proposes a surprising response: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehuda — tzorech pnim k'pnim dami*. One may still attribute this ruling to *Rabbi Yehuda*. Although *Rabbi Yehuda* limits the binding *seder* to what is done inside the *kodesh kodashim*, he would agree that acts which are preparatory to the *kodesh kodashim* service share its *seder* requirements. *Tzorech pnim k'pnim dami* — that which serves the interior is treated as if it were the interior itself.

Under this principle, the scope of the binding *seder* according to *Rabbi Yehuda* is in fact broader than his initial formulation suggests. It covers not only the actions performed inside the *kodesh kodashim* but also all the preparatory steps performed on their behalf: the *shechitat hapar*, the *chafinat haketoret*, the *hakravat haketoret*, the retrieval of the *dam hapar*, and the *shechitat hase'ir* — all of steps one through five and their sub-steps. All of these are done for the sake of what happens inside, and therefore the ordering requirements of the inside apply to them as well. The debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Nechemiah* then narrows: it concerns only those elements of the white-garment service that cannot be characterized as preparatory to the inner service — specifically the blood applications at the *parochet* and the *mizbeach*, and the related question of the *shirayin*. On those, the discussion continues.
