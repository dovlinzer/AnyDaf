# Eruvin 22 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Review: Rabbim & Mechitza

### Rabbim Negate Mechitza

Before turning to the *mishna* at the bottom of 22b, it is worth reviewing several key principles that emerged in the *gemara* studied on Shabbos, as they are central to *halacha l'maaseh*. The first is the position of Rav Yehuda: *asi rabbim u-mevatteli mechitza* — the *rabbim* passing through a *mechitza* negate it. This principle is critical because it leads to the conclusion that one cannot establish an *eruv* around a street that constitutes a genuine *reshut harabbim*. The *rabbim* would pass through the *tzurat hapesach*, the nominal wall of the *eruv*, thereby negating it as a *mechitza*. For Rav Yehuda himself this was not a difficulty, since he held that two *mechitzot* suffice. But when one combines Rav Yehuda's principle with the position of the *chachamim* — who require three *mechitzot* — the practical result is that an *eruv* cannot encompass a true *reshut harabbim*.

### Island Walls Problem

The second issue is the question raised at the end of the previous *daf*: why cannot an island serve as a *reshut hayachid*? One might simply use the drop of the sea wall as a *mechitza* on all sides, creating a *reshut hayachid* out of the island. The *gemara* never fully resolves this question. One might argue from the principle of *asi rabbim u-mevatteli mechitza* that travel across the island's edges negates the wall — but this negation only operates at points of actual crossing. To take Manhattan as an example: yes, the George Washington Bridge would break through the *mechitza* at that point, but everywhere else along the perimeter, where there is no crossing, why not regard the river wall as a valid *mechitza*?

### Ownership & Natural Walls

Two answers emerge from the *rishonim*. Tosafot suggests that when walls are entirely natural and not manmade, everyone would agree that *asi rabbim u-mevatteli mechitza* applies — and seemingly would extend this to mean that the very presence of *rabbim* within the *mechitzot*, not merely the act of crossing them, suffices to negate the walls entirely. The precise contours of this position remain somewhat unclear. The Ramban, whose answer is more widely cited among the *poskim*, argues that one must be able to perceive oneself as enclosed within the *mechitzot*. When they are so far apart — or so naturally formed — one cannot see oneself as contained. Some *poskim* note the ambiguity: what, precisely, is the threshold? Not every island is ten miles across, and perhaps the Ramban's standard would permit smaller ones.

The third issue from the previous *daf* concerns ownership and the definition of *reshut harabbim*. The *gemara* had noted that Yehoshua surrendered roads to the *rabbim*, yet where those roads were difficult to traverse they remained privately owned. How does ownership bear on the category of *reshut harabbim*? Tosafot holds that private ownership must be combined with the road's impassability; it is the conjunction of the two factors — not ownership alone — that prevents the space from qualifying as a *reshut harabbim* and limits the application of *asi rabbim u-mevatteli mechitza*. The Ramban, however, takes a more sweeping position: something cannot be a *reshut harabbim* at all unless it is literally owned by the public, regardless of the presence or absence of *mechitzot*. According to the Ramban, a privately owned street is categorically exempt from the status of *reshut harabbim*.

To summarize the three critical issues from the previous *daf*: the principle of *asi rabbim u-mevatteli mechitza*; the question of whether island walls can create a *reshut hayachid* across a large space; and the role of ownership in defining *reshut harabbim*.

## Pasei Biraot Rules

### Well Types & Pasei

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶחָד בּוֹר הָרַבִּים וּבְאֵר הָרַבִּים וּבְאֵר הַיָּחִיד — עוֹשִׂין לָהֶן פַּסִּין. אֲבָל לְבוֹר הַיָּחִיד — עוֹשִׂין לוֹ מְחִיצָה גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a public cistern** containing collected water, **as well as a public well** containing spring water, **and** even **a private well, one may arrange** upright **boards around them** in order to allow one to carry in the enclosed area, as delineated above. **But** in the case of **a private cistern,** there are two deficiencies: It belongs to an individual, and it does not contain spring water. Consequently, it is impossible to permit drawing from it on Shabbat by means of boards set up in the corners; rather, **one must construct for it a** proper **partition ten handbreadths high;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.**

The *mishna* at the bottom of 22b returns to the special leniency of *pasei biraot*. It states: *echad bor harabbim u-ve'er harabbim u-ve'er hayachid osim lahen pasim* — whether the well is a communal cistern of collected rainwater, a communal well of live spring water, or a private well of live spring water, the *pasim* may be erected. The one exception is *bor hayachid* — a privately owned cistern of collected water — for which a genuine *mechitza* of ten *tefachim* surrounding the entire space is required. This is the position of Rabbi Akiva. The concern is twofold: collected water may dry up, and a privately owned well has no community present to remind people that the *pasim* alone do not constitute a full *reshut hayachid*.

### R. Yehuda b. Bava

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא אוֹמֵר: אֵין עוֹשִׂין פַּסִּין אֶלָּא לִבְאֵר הָרַבִּים בִּלְבַד, וְלַשְּׁאָר — עוֹשִׂין חֲגוֹרָה גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava says: One may arrange** upright **boards only for a public well. But for the others,** that is, a public cistern or a private well, **one must set up a belt,** i.e., a partition consisting of ropes, **ten handbreadths high.** Such an arrangement creates a proper partition based on the principle of *lavud*, namely, that solid surfaces with gaps between them smaller than three handbreadths are considered joined.

Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is considerably more restrictive: *ein osim pasim ela le-ve'er harabbim bilvad* — the *pasim* are permitted only for a communal well of live spring water. Everything else requires a full *mechitza* of ten *tefachim*. This stringent position combines both criteria: public ownership and live water.

### Two Criteria Needed

The *gemara* at the top of 23a records that Rav Yosef said in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. And Rav Yosef further said in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *lo hutru pasei biraot ela be-ve'er mayim chayim bilvad* — the *pasim* were permitted only for a well of live water.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לִבְאֵר מַיִם חַיִּים בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. And Rav Yosef** also **said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** Upright **boards** surrounding a **well were permitted only in** the case of **a well** containing potable, running **spring water.**

One might ask: why state both rulings? Is the second not already contained within the ruling that the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava? The *gemara* explains that each statement is independently necessary. Had one stated only that we rule like Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava, one might have understood the emphasis to be on his requirement of *rabbim* — public ownership — while perhaps allowing even collected water so long as it is communally owned, reading the word *ve'er* in his formulation as directed against Rabbi Akiva's leniency regarding private ownership rather than as a requirement for live water per se. Conversely, had one stated only the second ruling — that *pasim* require live water — one might have supposed this applies equally whether the well is public or private, since live water does not dry up. Therefore both statements are needed: we follow Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava in requiring both criteria simultaneously, public ownership and live water.

## Gardens & Courts

### R. Y.b. Bava Garden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא: הַגִּינָּה וְהַקַּרְפֵּף שֶׁהֵן שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים עַל שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים הַמּוּקָּפוֹת גָּדֵר גָּבוֹהַּ עֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ שׁוֹמֵירָה אוֹ בֵּית דִּירָה, אוֹ שֶׁתְּהֵא סְמוּכָה לָעִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore, Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava said:** With regard to **a garden or a *karpef*,** an enclosed courtyard used for storage, **that is** not more than **seventy cubits and a remainder,** a little more, as will be explained below, **by seventy cubits and a remainder, and is surrounded by a wall ten handbreadths high, one may carry inside it,** as it constitutes a proper private domain. This is **provided that it contains a watchman's booth or a dwelling place, or it is near the town** in which its owner lives, so that he uses it and it is treated like a dwelling.

The next *mishna* introduces a new topic, though one thematically continuous with the discussions above. Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava states: *hagina veha-karpef she-hen shiv'im amah ve-shirayim al shiv'im amah ve-shirayim* — a garden or open lot that measures seventy and a fraction by seventy and a fraction in area. The "fraction" will be clarified by the *gemara* as two-thirds of an *amah*, yielding approximately 70.67, which is close to the square root of 5,000. That figure represents *beit satayim* — two *se'ah* — derived from the courtyard of the *Mishkan*, which measured 50 by 100 *amot*, totaling 5,000 square *amot*.

It will be recalled that in the opening *mishna* of this chapter, Rabbi Yehuda had held that *pasei biraot* are limited to *beit satayim*, on the grounds that a larger space is not *hukaf l'dira* — not enclosed for the purpose of habitation — and therefore does not achieve the full status of a *reshut hayachid*. The *chachamim* responded that this limitation to *beit satayim* applies only to a *gina* or *karpef*, whereas spaces that are genuinely *hukaf l'dira* — like a courtyard — have no such size restriction. The governing principle, then, is that a space that is not primarily a place of human habitation is limited to *beit satayim*.

Now the *mishna* continues. Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava holds that even a garden or lot surrounded by a proper wall of ten *tefachim* — thus fully enclosed — is permissible only if *sheyehei va shomira o beit dira* — there is either a guardhouse or a dwelling within the space — or *shetehei smucha la-ir* — it adjoins the city and is effectively accessible as an extension of it. Even in those cases where the space might arguably qualify as *hukaf l'dira*, he demands something concrete to demonstrate its residential character.

Tosafot addresses what seems to be a natural question: does this restriction apply to a proper courtyard attached to a home? Tosafot answers that it does not; Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava is speaking about spaces that are fundamentally gardens — *lo hukaf l'dira* in their essential character — where a dwelling or guardhouse was added as an afterthought. A space that genuinely began as a residential enclosure is not subject to this limitation. The stringency targets a garden in which a house was later inserted to lend it residential standing, which Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava regards as insufficient. Without some genuine residential feature, even a perfectly enclosed space cannot exceed *beit satayim* — or, more precisely, cannot be used for carrying at all if it is fundamentally *lo hukaf l'dira*.

### Stam R. Yehuda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין בָּהּ אֶלָּא בּוֹר וְשִׁיחַ וּמְעָרָה מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** This is not necessary, for **even if it contains only a** water **cistern, an** elongated water **ditch, or a cave,** i.e., a covered pit containing water, **one may carry inside it,** as the water bestows upon it the status of a dwelling.

Rabbi Yehuda — here *stam* Rabbi Yehuda means Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai — takes a position slightly less stringent: *afilu ein bah ela bor ve-siach u-me'arah, metaltelin betokhah* — even if the space contains only a cistern, an irrigation ditch, or a cave, one may carry within it. One does not need a full house, but something must be present that gives the space a measure of connection to human use and activity, enough to justify up to *beit satayim*. Without any such feature, according to both Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava and Rabbi Yehuda bar Ilai, a fundamentally *lo hukaf l'dira* space is entirely forbidden — not merely limited to *beit satayim* but unusable for carrying altogether.

### R. Akiva Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין בָּהּ אַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים עַל שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Even if it has none of these one may carry inside it, provided that it measures** not more than **seventy cubits and a remainder by seventy cubits and a remainder.**

Rabbi Akiva takes the most lenient position, and it is his ruling that we follow in practice: *afilu ein bah achat mikol elu, metaltelin betokhah* — even without any of those features, one may carry within the space, provided it does not exceed seventy and a fraction by seventy and a fraction. According to Rabbi Akiva, if a *lo hukaf l'dira* space is bounded within *beit satayim*, no additional qualifying element is required.

To clarify how these positions interact: all authorities would agree that a space genuinely *hukaf l'dira* — where habitation preceded and defined the enclosure — carries no size restriction. The debate concerns spaces that are fundamentally *lo hukaf l'dira*. There, Rabbi Akiva permits up to *beit satayim* without conditions; the other two views require some residential feature before granting even that allowance.

### R. Yosi Rectangle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אׇרְכָּהּ פִּי שְׁנַיִם בְּרׇחְבָּהּ — מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says: Even if its length is double its breadth, one may carry inside it,** and there is no need to be particular about a square shape.

Rabbi Yosi adds a further leniency regarding shape: *afilu orkhah pi shnayim berochbah, metaltelin betokhah* — even if the length is twice the width, one may carry within it. He does not dispute the *beit satayim* limit, but holds that the space need not be a perfect square. This echoes the dimensions of the *Mishkan*'s courtyard, which was precisely 50 by 100 — a two-to-one rectangle. The implicit question, to which the *gemara* will return, is whether Rabbi Akiva requires a perfect square, and if so, what this means for spaces that are naturally rectangular.

### R. Eliezer Notes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אִלְעַאי: שָׁמַעְתִּי מֵרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר, וַאֲפִילּוּ הִיא כְּבֵית כּוֹר. וְכֵן שָׁמַעְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ: אַנְשֵׁי חָצֵר שֶׁשָּׁכַח אֶחָד מֵהֶן וְלֹא עֵירַב — בֵּיתוֹ אָסוּר מִלְּהַכְנִיס וּלְהוֹצִיא לוֹ, אֲבָל לָהֶם מוּתָּר. וְכֵן שָׁמַעְתִּי מִמֶּנּוּ: שֶׁיּוֹצְאִין בְּעַרְקַבָּלִין בַּפֶּסַח. וְחָזַרְתִּי עַל כׇּל תַּלְמִידָיו וּבִקַּשְׁתִּי לִי חָבֵר, וְלֹא מָצָאתִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elai said: I heard from Rabbi Eliezer** that one is permitted to carry in a garden or *karpef*, **even** if the garden **is** an area of **a *beit kor*,** i.e., thirty times larger than the area of a *beit se'a*. Incidentally, he adds: **And I also heard from him** another *halakha*: If **one of the residents of a courtyard forgot and did not join in an *eiruv*** with the other residents when they established an *eiruv*, and on Shabbat he ceded ownership of his part in the courtyard to the other residents, then it **is prohibited for him,** the one who forgot to establish an *eiruv*, **to bring in** objects **or take** them **out** from **his house** to the courtyard; **however, it is permitted to them,** the other residents, to bring objects from their houses to that person's house via the courtyard, and vice versa. We do not say that the failure of one resident to join in the *eiruv* nullifies the validity of the *eiruv* for the entire courtyard. **And I also heard from him** another *halakha*, **that one may fulfill his obligation** to eat bitter herbs **on Passover with *arkablin*,** a certain bitter herb. With regard to all three rulings, **I circulated among all** of Rabbi Eliezer's **disciples, seeking a colleague** who had also heard these matters from him, **but I could not find** one.

Rabbi Ilai then reports three traditions he received from Rabbi Eliezer. First, *vaafilu hu ke-beit kor* — even a space the size of a *beit kor*, which is far larger than *beit satayim*, is permissible; Rabbi Eliezer apparently holds there is no size restriction even for *lo hukaf l'dira* spaces. Second, regarding a courtyard where all residents joined the *eruv* except one who forgot: Rabbi Eliezer holds that the person who forgot may not bring objects from his house into the courtyard, but the others may bring objects into his house — a position not followed in practice, where the ruling is that the courtyard remains permissible but his house is off-limits to everyone. Third, *she-yotzin be-arkavlin be-Pesach* — a certain vegetable may be used for *maror* on Pesach.

Rabbi Ilai adds that he searched among Rabbi Eliezer's students for a colleague who had heard the same traditions and found none, which left him without corroboration — though it is noted that he was certain of his report. The fact that Rabbi Ilai felt compelled to seek confirmation fits with the broader character of Rabbi Eliezer as a *tanna* who was known for transmitting only what he received from his teachers and never innovating.

## Ve'od Convention

### Ve'od Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי תַּנָּא דְּקָתָנֵי ״וְעוֹד״. אִילֵּימָא מִשּׁוּם דִּתְנָא לֵיהּ חֲדָא לְחוּמְרָא וְקָתָנֵי אַחֲרִיתִי, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי ״וְעוֹד״ — וְהָא רַבִּי יְהוּדָה דִּתְנָא לֵיהּ חֲדָא לְחוּמְרָא וְקָתָנֵי אַחֲרִיתִי, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״וְעוֹד״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara first analyzes the wording of this mishna: **What was taught** previously, **that** the *tanna* **teaches** in this mishna: **And furthermore** Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava said, which implies a continuation of the previous mishna? **If you say** that **because he** first **taught one stringency** concerning the upright boards surrounding a well, **and** then **he teaches another** stringency about an enclosure, and **for that reason** the *tanna* of the mishna **teaches: And furthermore,** then there is a difficulty. **Didn't Rabbi Yehuda** also **teach one stringency and** then **teach another** stringency, **and** yet the *tanna* of the mishna **does not teach: And furthermore** Rabbi Yehuda said?

The *gemara* turns to a literary question: *mai tanna de-katani ve-od* — why does the *mishna* introduce Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava's stringency regarding gardens with the word *ve-od*, "and furthermore"? One might say: Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava was strict in the previous *mishna* — requiring both live water and public ownership for *pasei biraot* — and here he is strict again, limiting *lo hukaf l'dira* spaces; so we use *ve-od* to link two consecutive stringencies of the same *tanna*.

The *gemara* challenges this: that cannot be the criterion, because we can find two consecutive stringencies attributed to *stam* Rabbi Yehuda in the first *mishna* of the chapter — one regarding *pasei biraot* being limited to *beit satayim*, and another regarding *pasei biraot* in a *reshut harabbim* — yet the *mishna* there does not use *ve-od* between them. So what explains the difference?

### Ve'od Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָתָם אַפְסְקוּהּ רַבָּנַן, הָכָא לָא אַפְסְקוּהּ רַבָּנַן. וְכׇל הֵיכָא דְּאַפְסְקוּהּ רַבָּנַן לָא קָתָנֵי ״וְעוֹד״? וְהָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר דְּסוּכָּה, דְּאַפְסְקוּהּ רַבָּנַן, וְקָתָנֵי ״וְעוֹד״? הָתָם, בְּמִילְּתֵיהּ הוּא דְּאַפְסְקוּהּ. הָכָא, בְּמִילְּתָא אַחֲרִיתִי אַפְסְקוּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers that the cases are different: **There, the Rabbis interrupted** Rabbi Yehuda's statements in order to disagree with him, and hence it is not possible to say: And furthermore Rabbi Yehuda said. **Here,** however, **the Rabbis did not interrupt him,** as the two statements of Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava immediately follow one another. The Gemara raises a difficulty: Does this mean that **wherever the** disputing **Rabbis interrupt** their colleague, the *tanna* **does not teach: And furthermore? But** with regard to **Rabbi Eliezer** in a mishna **in** tractate ***Sukka*** (27a), **where the Rabbis interrupted his** statements, nonetheless the *tanna* **teaches: And furthermore.** The Gemara answers: It is not the same; **there, they interrupted** Rabbi Eliezer **with** a ruling with regard to **his** own **topic; here,** however, **they interrupted** Rabbi Yehuda with a ruling with regard to **an** altogether **different matter.** Consequently, his first statement had already been forgotten, and it is not the Mishna's style to join together statements where the sequential link between them has already been severed.

The *gemara* answers: *hasam afsiku rabbanan, hacha lo afsiku rabbanan* — there, the *chachamim* intervened between the two positions of Rabbi Yehuda with a substantial digression, whereas here there was no such intervening digression. In the first *mishna*, between Rabbi Yehuda's two stringencies, the *chachamim* not only responded directly but went on at length — limiting *beit satayim* specifically to *gina* and *karpef*, enumerating what types of spaces may be unlimited, and so on. That constituted a significant departure from the immediate thread. Here, by contrast, the transition was direct.

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge: but we find in *Sukkah* that Rabbi Eliezer's positions are connected with *ve-od* even though the *chachamim* intervened between them. The *gemara* resolves this by distinguishing the character of the interruption. In *Sukkah*, the *chachamim*'s intervening statement — that only the first night of *Sukkah* is required — was directly responsive to Rabbi Eliezer's point and did not veer off into independent subject matter. That brief on-point response did not constitute the kind of digression that would sever the connection. In our first *mishna*, the *chachamim*'s response ranged far beyond the immediate issue and introduced a catalogue of independent rulings. That is the interruption that precludes *ve-od*. The rule that emerges: *ve-od* is appropriate when two positions of the same *tanna* are juxtaposed either without interruption at all, or with an interruption that is immediately on-point and confined to the matter at hand. Where the intervening material digresses substantially into independent topics, *ve-od* is not used.

## Beit Sasayim Size

### R. Akiva = T.K.?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אֵין בָּהּ אַחַת מִכׇּל אֵלּוּ מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בָּהּ שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים עַל שִׁבְעִים אַמָּה וְשִׁירַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Even if it has none of these one may carry inside it, provided that it measures** not more than **seventy cubits and a remainder by seventy cubits and a remainder.**

The *gemara* now turns to the halachic substance of the *mishna*. Rabbi Akiva says that one may carry in a *lo hukaf l'dira* space without any additional requirements, provided it does not exceed seventy and a fraction by seventy and a fraction. The *gemara* asks: *Rabbi Akiva hainu Tanna Kamma* — is Rabbi Akiva simply repeating the position of the first *tanna*? Rashi understands this as a reference to the *Tanna Kamma* at the very beginning of the chapter, who had held that *pasei biraot* need not be limited to *beit satayim* and implicitly placed no additional conditions on *gina* and *karpef* beyond the size limit. Tosafot reads the question more narrowly, focusing on why Rabbi Akiva restates the figure of seventy and two-thirds when it has already been stated.

### Precision Difference

The *gemara* resolves this: *ika beinaihu davar mu'at* — the difference lies in whether the measurement is precisely 70 and two-thirds or 70 and two-thirds plus a small additional amount. As noted, 70 and two-thirds (*amos*) equals approximately 70.67, while the true square root of 5,000 is approximately 70.71. The *Tanna Kamma* allows the small difference between those two figures, permitting anything up to the full *beit satayim* including that fractional remainder. A *braitta* is then cited: Rabbi Yehuda says there is a slight additional amount beyond 70 and two-thirds, and the *chachamim* did not specify the precise figure — understandably so, since the true square root of 5,000 is irrational and cannot be expressed as a simple fraction. Rabbi Akiva insists on precisely 70 and two-thirds, nothing more.

### Mishkan Source

The *gemara* asks: *mena hanei milei* — from where is the size of *beit satayim* derived? Rav Yehuda says the verse states: *orech hachatzer meah ba-amah ve-rochav chamishim ba-chamishim* — the length of the courtyard is 100 *amot* and the width is 50 *amot*, yielding a total of 5,000 square *amot*. This is the courtyard of the *Mishkan*, and it serves as the halakhic benchmark for *beit satayim*. It should be noted that applying this measure as a limit on *lo hukaf l'dira* spaces is a rabbinic enactment; the verse is likely cited as *asmachta*.

### Math Derivation

The verse's phrasing raises a question: it says *chamishim ba-chamishim* — 50 within 50 — yet the courtyard was 50 by 100, not 50 by 50. The *gemara* explains: *tul chamishim ve-sovev chamishim* — take the 50 and surround it by 50. This encodes a geometric construction for approximating the square root of 5,000 without the benefit of positional notation or decimal arithmetic. Rashi explains the procedure as follows: begin with a 50-by-50 square. Take a second 50-by-100 rectangle — the other half of the courtyard — and cut it into strips of 10 by 50. Place four such strips along the four sides of the original 50-by-50 square, adding 10 to each side, expanding it to a 70-by-70 square. There are small square corners left unfilled; take the remaining strip material, cut it to fill those corners, repeat the process with what remains, and one arrives at approximately 70 and two-thirds. This elegant construction is how the ancients identified the side length of a square equal in area to the 50-by-100 courtyard. Rabbi Akiva's insistence on precisely 70 and two-thirds, the *gemara* notes, is also reinforced by the square nature of this construction.

### Pasuk Meaning

A further question arises: in its plain sense, why does the verse say *chamishim ba-chamishim* if the courtyard was 50 by 100? Abaye answers: *ha'ameid Mishkan al sfat chamishim* — position the *Mishkan* at the far end of the 50-*amah* boundary of the courtyard. If one imagines the full 100-by-50 courtyard and draws a line across the middle, the front half — the open approach area before the *Mishkan* — is a perfect 50-by-50 square. The *Mishkan* structure itself, measuring 30 by 10 *amot*, stands symmetrically behind that line, centered with 20 *amot* on each side and 20 *amot* behind it. The verse's *chamishim ba-chamishim* thus highlights the 50-by-50 open forecourt that constituted the primary functional space of the *Mishkan*'s courtyard.

## Square vs. Rectangle

### Contradiction Noted

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אִם הָיְתָה אׇרְכָּהּ יָתֵר עַל רׇחְבָּהּ אֲפִילּוּ אַמָּה אַחַת — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין בְּתוֹכָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer says: If its length is greater than its breadth, even by one cubit, one may not carry inside it,** even though its total area does not exceed an area of two *beit se'a*, because in an area that was enclosed not so that it could be lived in, but for the sake of the field or yard outside, carrying is only permitted if the area is perfectly square.

The *mishna* records that Rabbi Eliezer holds: *im haytah orkhah yeter al rochbah afilu amah achat, ein metaltelin betokhah* — if the length exceeds the width by even one *amah*, one may not carry within it; the space must be a perfect square. Yet a *braitta* attributes to Rabbi Eliezer an apparently contrary position: if the length exceeds the width by more than double, even one *amah* more than a two-to-one ratio, it is forbidden — implying that up to a two-to-one rectangle is permitted. This sounds precisely like Rabbi Yosi's view. The contradiction between the *mishna* and the *braitta* attributed to Rabbi Eliezer requires resolution.

### Text Emendation

Rabbi Bibi bar Abaye proposes reading the *mishna* as the *braitta* reads it — namely, that even the *mishna*'s text should be understood as referring to excess beyond a two-to-one ratio. If so, however, Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yosi appear identical, which the *gemara* rejects.

## Square vs. Rectangle (II)

### Akiva vs. Yosi

The *gemara* resolves the apparent identity between the positions by pointing to a distinction: *ika beinaihu ribuah de-ribuah de-rabbanan* — they differ regarding the square of the rabbanic square. Rashi explains this as a question of preference: one position prefers a square configuration (allowing rectangles only reluctantly), while the other prefers a rectangle modeled on the *Mishkan*'s courtyard dimensions, permitting squares as well but regarding the two-to-one rectangle as the ideal.

### Tosafot Practical

Tosafot raises a pointed objection: what can "prefer" possibly mean in this context? This is not a *mitzvah* with a *lechatchila* — is one supposed to redesign an actual courtyard to conform to the preferred shape? If the space is permitted, it is permitted; if forbidden, it is forbidden. One cannot tell a person to reconfigure his yard from a rectangle to a square or vice versa. Tosafot therefore reinterprets the distinction more technically: one position reads "twice as long" in terms of the diagonal measurement rather than the simple linear dimension, making it a more stringent standard. This at least yields a genuine legal difference without the absurdity of prescribing courtyard renovations.

## Lenient Ruling

### Both Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא. וְאָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לִבְאֵר מַיִם חַיִּים בִּלְבַד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava. And Rav Yosef** also **said** that **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** Upright **boards** surrounding a **well were permitted only in** the case of **a well** containing potable, running **spring water.**

The *gemara* records two rulings transmitted by Rav Yosef and Rav Bibi respectively, both in the name of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: the *halakha* follows Rabbi Yosi (permitting a two-to-one rectangle), and the *halakha* follows Rabbi Akiva (requiring no additional feature beyond *beit satayim*). The *gemara* notes: *ve-tarvaihu le-kula* — both rulings represent the lenient aspect of each authority's position taken together.

### Why Both Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא: דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: דְּרַבִּים — וַאֲפִילּוּ מְכוּנָּסִין, וְהַאי דְּקָתָנֵי בְּאֵר הָרַבִּים — לְאַפּוֹקֵי מִדְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דְּלֹא הוּתְּרוּ פַּסֵּי בֵירָאוֹת אֶלָּא לִבְאֵר מַיִם חַיִּים. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בְּאֵר מַיִם חַיִּים, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לָא שְׁנָא דְּרַבִּים וְלָא שְׁנָא דְּיָחִיד, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה בֶּן בָּבָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And it was necessary** to cite both of these statements, even though their content appears to be the same. **As had** he **taught us** only that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava** that upright boards may only be set arranged for a well, **I would have said** that with regard to water belonging to **the public,** upright boards are permitted not only in the case of spring water, but **even** in the case of water **collected** in a cistern. **And that which was taught:** One may only arrange boards for **a public well,** that was **to exclude** the opinion **of Rabbi Akiva** that upright boards may be arranged even for a private well, but not to allow us to infer that boards may not be arranged for a public cistern filled with collected water. Therefore, Shmuel **teaches us** that **boards** surrounding a **well were permitted only** in the case of **a well of spring water.** **And** in the opposite direction, **had** he **taught us** that upright boards may only be arranged for **a well** containing potable, running **spring water, I would have said** that **there is no difference** whether it is a **public** well **and there is no difference** whether it is a **private** well. Shmuel therefore **teaches us** that the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yehuda ben Bava,** who says that upright boards may be arranged only for a public well, but not for one that belongs to an individual.

Both rulings must be stated explicitly. Had one ruled only like Rabbi Yosi, one might have supposed that Rabbi Yosi addresses shape but still requires a guardhouse or dwelling, since he does not explicitly address that condition. The ruling like Rabbi Akiva clarifies that no such additional feature is needed. Conversely, had one ruled only like Rabbi Akiva, one might have inferred that since his formulation emphasizes the *beit satayim* square, the space must literally be a square. The ruling like Rabbi Yosi clarifies that a two-to-one rectangle is equally permissible. The combined ruling thus establishes a fully lenient position: no additional qualifying features are required, and the space may be rectangular up to a two-to-one ratio, provided the total area does not exceed *beit satayim*.

## Garden in Lot

### Lot to Garden

A *braitta* introducing a more complex case. A *karpef* — an open lot — that is larger than *beit satayim* but was enclosed *hukaf l'dira*, meaning the original intention was residential use (one might think of it as a campground or the like). Subsequently, *nizra rubo* — the majority of the space was planted as a vegetable garden. The question is how this mixed space is categorized. A *gina* is considered *lo hukaf l'dira* by definition; a *karpef* designated for habitation is *hukaf l'dira*. What happens when one contains a majority portion of the other?

This raises a broader and striking principle: the identity of such a space is not fixed by its original designation. Even if the enclosure was made with residential intent, subsequent use as a garden can transform its character. If it has been fully converted, the transformation is complete. But what if only a majority has been converted?

### Majority Rules

The *braitta* rules: *harei hu ke-gina* — if the majority is planted as a vegetable garden, the entire space takes the identity of a garden. Since the original *karpef* was larger than *beit satayim*, and now the whole space is treated as a *gina*, it is *lo hukaf l'dira* and larger than *beit satayim*, and therefore one may not carry within it.

### Trees Exception

However, if the majority of the space was planted not with vegetables but with trees — *nata rubo* — the ruling is different: *harei hu ke-chatzer u-mutar*. Trees do not undermine the residential character of a space. A park planted with trees remains a place where people walk, rest, and spend time; it is an amenity of habitation, not a departure from it. Therefore the space retains its status as the equivalent of a *chatzer* and carrying remains permitted. This yields two significant insights: first, trees do not diminish *hukaf l'dira* status; second, and more broadly, the identity of an enclosed space can shift with changes in its dominant use, and that majority use is what determines the applicable category.

## Minority Garden

### R. Huna Limit

The *gemara* now examines the converse of the *braitta*'s ruling regarding a majority-planted garden. Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua qualifies the *braitta*: *lo amran ela yoter mi-beit satayim* — the ruling that the planted majority renders the entire space forbidden applies only if the planted portion itself exceeds *beit satayim*. If the garden portion is less than *beit satayim*, even if it constitutes the majority of the space, it does not render the surrounding area forbidden.

The logic is as follows: when the planted area itself exceeds *beit satayim*, it becomes an independently forbidden space — a *gina* that is *lo hukaf l'dira* and too large to be permissible. That forbidden space is then open into the remainder of the *karpef* with no dividing wall, and the principle of *patuach le-makom ha-assur lo* — being open to a forbidden area — causes the entire space to become forbidden as well. But if the garden is under *beit satayim*, it does not itself carry independent forbidden status, and so the surrounding lot is not affected.

### Beit Sasayim Threshold

The *gemara* recasts this principle more directly in a subsequent formulation: if the planted portion is a *minority* of the space, it is permissible — *miuta shari*. Yet Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua applies his qualification in reverse as well: *lo amran ela de-lo havei beit satayim* — that leniency holds only as long as the minority garden has not itself reached *beit satayim*. Once the garden portion reaches *beit satayim*, even as a minority, it becomes a problem, because at that threshold it gains an independent identity as a *gina*. Even though a *gina* of exactly *beit satayim* is itself permissible according to Rabbi Akiva's ruling, the problem is now different in character: one now has a *gina* that is open into a *karpef* with no wall between them. Those are categorically distinct types of *reshut hayachid*, and carrying from one to the other raises its own concerns.

### Opening Rule

This brings the *gemara* to an important general principle: if a space is *patuach le-makom ha-assur lo* — open to a domain where carrying is forbidden — the space itself becomes forbidden even if it would otherwise be permissible. When the garden portion exceeds *beit satayim*, it is itself a forbidden space, and by being open into the *karpef*, it renders the *karpef* forbidden as well. This explains Rav Huna bar Rav Yehoshua's original qualification: the majority-planted garden creates a problem only because and when the planted area itself rises above *beit satayim* and becomes forbidden.

### R. Shimon

The *gemara* then asks: *ke-man* — according to whom is this position? The analysis of the minority case, where the space is permissible as long as the garden is under *beit satayim*, requires that a *gina* opening into a *karpef* is not itself treated as a problem when both are permissible. This implies the ruling of Rabbi Shimon. The *mishna* records Rabbi Shimon's position: *echad gagot ve-echad chatzerot ve-echad karpifot, reshut achat hen le-keilim she-shavtu be-tokhan* — rooftops, courtyards, and lots are all considered a single domain with respect to items that were within them at the onset of Shabbat, though not for items that were in a house. Rabbi Shimon thus holds that these distinct types of *reshut hayachid* — rooftop, courtyard, lot, garden — are not treated as separate domains requiring further demarcation between them; they are collectively one domain.

The *chachamim* disagree and hold that each such space has a distinct enough character that one cannot carry freely between them without an *eruv*, even when each is independently permissible. The *gemara*'s final formulation goes like Rabbi Shimon: when the garden portion is a minority of under *beit satayim*, the whole space is permissible because a *gina* opening into a *karpef* presents no difficulty (per Rabbi Shimon). But once the garden reaches even *beit satayim* — gaining full independent identity as a *gina* — the problem arises according to the *chachamim*, and even according to Rabbi Shimon, because within an otherwise *hukaf l'dira* enclosure one now has a sub-space of *beit satayim* that is *lo hukaf l'dira* and therefore forbidden, which by its opening into the surrounding area renders the whole space forbidden.

## Eruv Application

### Eruv Challenge

This analysis carries immediate and substantial implications for practical *eruvin*. One might construct an *eruv* enclosing an entire neighborhood or city, but if within that enclosed area there exists a space larger than *beit satayim* that is *lo hukaf l'dira*, the principle of *patuach le-makom ha-assur lo* threatens to render the surrounding area forbidden as well. Ensuring that no such unqualified space exists within an *eruv* is therefore one of the central practical challenges in *eruvin* construction.

### Lakes & Parks

What types of spaces might qualify as *lo hukaf l'dira* within an urban *eruv*? A park planted with trees, as the *gemara* established, is permissible — people walk there, rest there, and use it as an extension of residential life, so it retains *hukaf l'dira* status. But consider a lake within that park. People do not inhabit a lake. Does a lake constitute a *lo hukaf l'dira* space larger than *beit satayim*? Some authorities argue that if people actually use the lake — rowing boats, floating toy boats, engaging with the water's edge — then the lake is also part of human activity and may qualify as *hukaf l'dira*. There are in fact lakes in Central Park where boats are taken out, and that use may be relevant to the analysis. But the precise boundaries of what constitutes *hukaf l'dira* in such cases remain a matter of careful halakhic determination. What emerges clearly from the *gemara* studied today is that this question — the definition of *lo hukaf l'dira* and the consequences of such a space being open within a larger *eruv* — is not merely theoretical. It is one of the primary concerns that arises in the practical construction and validation of an *eruv*, and the principles learned here on 22b are directly operative in contemporary halakhic practice.
