# Shabbat 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro to Shabbat

### Welcome & Seder Moed

Welcome to Masechet Shabbat, the beginning not only of a new *masechet* but of a new *seder*: Seder Moed. Mazel tov to all who completed Masechet Berachot.

### Length & Duration

Masechet Shabbat is a long *masechet* — 156 *dafim* — and we will be studying it for five months. Together with Eruvin, the two will occupy us for eight months. If one includes Pesachim, the first three great *masechtos* of Moed — Shabbat, Eruvin, and Pesachim — will carry us for approximately a year, covering the first half of Seder Moed.

### Structure & Perakim

Masechet Shabbat contains 24 *perakim*. The ordering of *masechtos* within a *seder* generally follows the number of *perakim*, which is why Shabbat, the longest, comes first. (Eruvin has 10 *perakim*, Pesachim likewise 10.) It is worth noting that, in the *Torah* itself, Nissan and Pesach are always first among the *moadim*, so there is a certain rightness to Pesachim appearing early in our sequence as well.

### Primary Topics

One might expect that a *masechet* devoted to Shabbat would be organized primarily around the 39 *melachot*, their *toladot*, and the associated rabbinic prohibitions. In fact, a large portion of the *masechet* — possibly the majority — is devoted to two or three major topics: *hotza'ah* (carrying), *muktze*, and cooking along with the rabbinic restrictions surrounding it. We do not even reach the *melachot* proper until the seventh *perek*.

The order of the first several *perakim* follows a broadly chronological rather than strictly thematic logic. We begin with an introduction to *hotza'ah*, which leads into discussions of what one may and may not do *erev Shabbat*, followed by further elaborations on carrying, then candle-lighting, and related matters — only arriving at the 39 *melachot* partway into the seventh *perek*. There is something significant, perhaps even intentional, in this structure: six *perakim* lead up to the seventh, which addresses Shabbat proper and its *melachot* — echoing the six days of creation leading to the seventh.

### Org: Chronological vs Th…

This contrast between chronological and thematic organization appears elsewhere in the tradition. The *Shulchan Aruch* generally follows a chronological order: *Hilchot Shabbat* begins with *kabbalat Shabbat*, candle-lighting, and the preparations of *erev Shabbat*. The Rambam, by contrast, always organizes thematically. Yet even the Rambam does not open *Hilchot Shabbat* with the 39 *melachot* — he begins with general categories applicable across all *melachot*, focusing particularly on intentionality, and only arrives at the 39 *melachot* at the beginning of his seventh chapter. The parallel is striking: six chapters in the Rambam, just as six *perakim* in the *masechet*, leading to the seventh where Shabbat proper is treated. This appears intentional. Anyone expecting the *masechet* to begin by laying out the *melachot* of Shabbat should be forewarned: we have approximately two months to wait before we get there.

## Reshuyot & Hotza'ah

### Mishnah: Two Which Are F…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יְצִיאוֹת הַשַּׁבָּת, שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע בִּפְנִים, וּשְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע בַּחוּץ. כֵּיצַד? הֶעָנִי עוֹמֵד בַּחוּץ, וּבַעַל הַבַּיִת בִּפְנִים:
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The acts of **carrying out** from a public domain into a private domain or vice versa, which are prohibited **on Shabbat,** are primarily **two** basic actions **that comprise four** cases from the perspective of a person **inside** a private domain, **and two** basic actions **that comprise four** cases from the perspective of a person **outside,** in a public domain. The mishna elaborates: **How** do these eight cases take place? In order to answer that question, the mishna cites cases involving a poor person and a homeowner. **The poor person stands outside,** in the public domain, **and the homeowner stands inside,** in the private domain.

The first *mishnah* opens: *yetziot haShabbat shtayim she'hein arba bifnim, shtayim she'hein arba bachutz* — the carryings of Shabbat are two which are four on the inside, and two which are four on the outside. This is somewhat cryptic as stated, and its precise meaning will be clarified as the *Gemara* develops. Based on the *Gemara*'s eventual conclusion, the *mishnah* means that there are two biblical and two rabbinic restrictions, both for the person on the inside and for the person on the outside. The *mishnah* then asks *keitzad* — how so? — and introduces its central scenario: *ha'ani omeid bachutz uba'al habayit bifnim*, the homeowner inside his home and the *ani*, the poor person, just outside the door.

### Reshut Hayachid & Harabim

The homeowner stands in a *reshut hayachid*, a private domain, while the *ani* stands in a *reshut harabim*, a public domain. The halachic definitions of these domains, it must be emphasized, are not about ownership but about physical configuration and use.

A *reshut hayachid* for the purposes of Shabbat is an area enclosed by walls — paradigmatically four walls, though three suffice. The criterion is structural enclosure. Even a walled city, in most cases, retains the status of *reshut hayachid* biblically, unless those walls are deemed entirely insignificant.

A *reshut harabim*, by contrast, is not simply everything that is not a *reshut hayachid*. It is a genuine public space — specifically, a major thoroughfare used by large numbers of people for transit, or a major public square serving as a gathering place. The precise threshold — how major the thoroughfare must be, whether it must cut through an entire city, how many people must use it — will be discussed in due course.

One might observe that these two definitions operate on different conceptual axes: *reshut hayachid* is defined by construction (walls), while *reshut harabim* is defined by use (public traffic). This asymmetry has real consequences: a *reshut harabim* that falls out of use is no longer a *reshut harabim* even if topographically unchanged, whereas a *reshut hayachid* is not defined by its patterns of use at all. This asymmetry also helps generate the intermediate category of *karmelit* and raises questions about cases where both criteria are present simultaneously — for instance, a major thoroughfare within a walled city, where it is unclear which definition dominates. Those questions will arise later.

### Karmelit & Makom Patur

Beyond the two biblical domains, there are two rabbinic domains. A *karmelit* is essentially everything in between — an area not enclosed by walls but also not a major thoroughfare. Rabbinically, one must treat it with the stringencies of both a *reshut hayachid* and a *reshut harabim*: one may not carry from a *karmelit* to either biblical domain, nor from either biblical domain to a *karmelit*. And there is a *makom patur* — certain spaces entirely outside the normal zones of use, treated as free zones where these restrictions do not apply. For now, the focus will remain on the primary domains.

### Biblical Carrying Rules

What does the *Torah* prohibit regarding carrying on Shabbat? Biblically, one may not carry from a *reshut hayachid* to a *reshut harabim* or vice versa. Carrying from private to public is called *hotza'ah* — taking out; carrying from public to private is called *hachnasah* — bringing in. As the *mishnah* states and the *Gemara* will explain, both are generically referred to as *hotza'ah*.

A second form of biblical carrying prohibition is *ha'avara* — conveying an object four *amot* (roughly six feet) within a *reshut harabim* itself. Even without crossing domains, picking up an object in the middle of the street, walking four *amot*, and setting it down constitutes a biblical violation.

Critically, both of these prohibitions require two endpoint actions: *akirah* — picking up or removing the object from its resting place — and *hanachah* — setting it down. Without both endpoints, no biblical violation has occurred. One can imagine scenarios where only one endpoint is present: the object was being carried from before Shabbat and was never lifted on Shabbat itself; or someone else did the *akirah* and handed the object to you; or you transferred it to another person who performed the *hanachah*. In all such cases, the full act of carrying has not been completed by any single person.

These requirements — *akirah* and *hanachah* — apply both to *hotza'ah* and *hachnasah* between domains, and to *ha'avara* of four *amot* within a *reshut harabim*.

### Hotza'ah: Non-Creative

A point worth noting before proceeding to the case analysis: *hotza'ah* is categorically different from all other *melachot* of Shabbat. Every other *melacha* involves some form of creation or transformation — some change of state in the physical world. Carrying from one place to another does not, in any obvious sense, create or transform anything. For this reason, the *Rishonim*, including Tosafot, characterize *hotza'ah* as a *melacha geru'ah* — a minor-grade or diminished *melacha*. This does not mean it is any less prohibited; it is equally *chayav mitah* when performed intentionally. But it lacks the natural quality of creative labor.

This characterization raises the question of why it is prohibited at all, beyond the fact that it is derived from the *Mishkan*. Two approaches are offered. One is that there is a subtle element of creativity even in *hotza'ah* — an object's identity and significance are to some degree constituted by its context and location, so transferring it between domains represents a kind of change of state. This is admittedly a stretch. The other approach connects *hotza'ah* to the prohibition against commercial activity on Shabbat. Business activity is only rabbinically prohibited, yet it is the paradigmatic weekday behavior: going to the market, buying, selling, bringing goods home. A central component of that activity is the carrying of goods to and from the marketplace. *Hotza'ah* may thus be understood as standing in for, or being conceptually connected to, the disruption of the Shabbat atmosphere that commercial activity represents.

The non-creative character of *hotza'ah* may also help explain the conceptual model underlying its definitions. The verse in *Yirmiyahu* — *al totziu massa mibateichem b'yom haShabbat* — frames the prohibition as taking from your houses into the public sphere, and the *Gemara* elsewhere derives the *melacha* from the *Mishkan*, where the call to stop bringing contributions (*al yetzei ish*) is understood as occurring on Shabbat. The underlying image is of taking from one's house out into the public thoroughfare — not a symmetric definition for both domains, but an asymmetric one rooted in the concept of one's private space versus the public world outside. This may be precisely why *reshut hayachid* is defined by walls (the conceptual model of a house) while *reshut harabim* is defined by public use.

## Ani & Ba'al Habayit

### Setup: Ani & Ba'al

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשַׁט הֶעָנִי אֶת יָדוֹ לִפְנִים וְנָתַן לְתוֹךְ יָדוֹ שֶׁל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת, אוֹ שֶׁנָּטַל מִתּוֹכָהּ וְהוֹצִיא — הֶעָנִי חַיָּיב וּבַעַל הַבַּיִת פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The poor person** lifted an object in the public domain, **extended his hand into** the private domain, **and placed** the object **into the hand of the homeowner.** In that case, the poor person performed the prohibited labor of carrying from the public domain into the private domain in its entirety. **Or,** the poor person reached his hand into the private domain, **took** an item from the hand of the homeowner, **and carried** it **out** into the public domain. In that case, the poor person performed the prohibited labor of carrying out from the private domain into the public domain in its entirety. In both of these cases, because **the poor person** performed the prohibited labor in its entirety, he **is liable and the homeowner is exempt.**

The *mishnah* presents its cases through a vivid scenario. The *ba'al habayit* stands inside — in the *reshut hayachid* — and the *ani* stands just outside the door — in the *reshut harabim*. Between them is the boundary wall. The *ani* wishes to receive food; the question is who performs which part of the transfer, and who thereby bears halachic liability.

### Case 1: Ani Gives

*Pashat ha'ani et yado lifnim venatan letoch yado shel ba'al habayit* — the *ani* extends his hand inside and places something into the hand of the *ba'al habayit*. The *ba'al habayit* simply holds out his hand and does nothing further. Nevertheless, this is a biblical prohibition — the *ani* has performed the *akirah* in the *reshut harabim*, transferred the object across the domain boundary, and completed the *hanachah* by placing it into the *ba'al habayit*'s hand. The *ba'al habayit* played no active role; his hand served merely as a platform — what the *Gemara* will call *ma'aseh itztaba*. The *ani* is *chayav* (liable to bring a *korban* if done *beshogeig*); the *ba'al habayit* is *patur* — and as the *Gemara* will clarify, in this case not merely *patur aval assur* but essentially *patur umuttar*, since he performed no part of the act at all.

### Case 2: Ani Takes

The *ani* extends his hand inside, and this time takes an object from the hand of the *ba'al habayit* — *o natal mitochah vehotzi*. The *ba'al habayit* was holding the object passively; the *ani* performed the *akirah* (removing it from the *ba'al habayit*'s hand), moved it across the boundary, and brought it into the *reshut harabim*, completing the *hanachah* by holding it or placing it down. Again, the *ba'al habayit* acted as a mere platform. The *ani* is *chayav*; the *ba'al habayit* is *patur*.

These are the *shnayim bachutz* — the two ways the *ani*, standing outside, can perform a complete biblical violation on his own.

### Case 3: Ba'al Gives

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשַׁט בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶת יָדוֹ לַחוּץ וְנָתַן לְתוֹךְ יָדוֹ שֶׁל עָנִי, אוֹ שֶׁנָּטַל מִתּוֹכָהּ וְהִכְנִיס — בַּעַל הַבַּיִת חַיָּיב וְהֶעָנִי פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna cites two additional cases. In these, the prohibited labor is performed by the homeowner, who is in the private domain: **The homeowner** lifted an item in the private domain, **extended his hand into** the public domain, **and placed** the object **into the hand of the poor person.** In that case, the homeowner performed the labor of carrying out from the private domain into the public domain in its entirety. **Or,** the homeowner reached his hand into the public domain, **took** an object **from** the hand of the poor person, **and carried** it **into** the private domain. In that case, the homeowner performed the labor of carrying from the public domain into the private domain in its entirety. In both of those cases, because **the homeowner** performed the prohibited labor in its entirety, he **is liable and the poor person is exempt.**

*Pashat ba'al habayit et yado lachutz venatan letoch yado shel ani* — the *ba'al habayit* reaches his hand outside and places an object into the hand of the *ani*. He has performed the *akirah* inside the *reshut hayachid*, carried the object across the boundary, and effected the *hanachah* by placing it in the *ani*'s hand. The *ba'al habayit* is *chayav*; the *ani*, who did nothing, is *patur*.

### Case 4: Ba'al Takes

The *ba'al habayit* reaches his hand outside and takes something from the *ani*'s hand — *o shenatal mitocho vehi'chnis* — bringing it inside. He performed the full act of *hachnasah*: *akirah* from the *ani*'s hand in the *reshut harabim*, transfer across the boundary, and *hanachah* within the *reshut hayachid*. *Ba'al habayit chayav, ve'ani patur*.

These are the *shnayim bifnim* — the two ways the *ba'al habayit*, reaching out from inside, can complete a full biblical violation on his own.

### Two Become Four

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פָּשַׁט הֶעָנִי אֶת יָדוֹ לִפְנִים וְנָטַל בַּעַל הַבַּיִת מִתּוֹכָהּ, אוֹ שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹכָהּ וְהוֹצִיא — שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּטוּרִין. פָּשַׁט בַּעַל הַבַּיִת אֶת יָדוֹ לַחוּץ וְנָטַל הֶעָנִי מִתּוֹכָהּ, אוֹ שֶׁנָּתַן לְתוֹכָהּ וְהִכְנִיס — שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּטוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** There are four additional cases where neither the homeowner nor the poor person performed the labor in its entirety, and therefore neither is liable: **The poor person extended his hand into** the private domain **and** either **the homeowner took** an object **from** his hand and placed it in the private domain **or** the homeowner **placed** an object **into** the hand of the poor person, and the poor person **carried** the object **out** into the public domain. In those cases and the two that follow, the act of transferring the object from one domain to another was performed jointly by two people, the poor person and the homeowner. Because each performed only part of the prohibited labor, **both of them are exempt.** So too, in a case where **the homeowner extended his hand into the public domain and,** either **the poor person took** an object **from** the homeowner's hand and placed it in the public domain **or the poor person placed** an object **into** the homeowner's hand and the homeowner **carried** the object **into** the private domain. Because each performed only part of the prohibited labor, **both of them are exempt.**

The *mishnah* now addresses the partnership cases — where the two individuals each perform part of the act — and invokes the principle of *shnayim she'asu*: when two people together perform a *melacha*, neither is individually liable biblically. Each has done only half a *melacha*. These cases are nonetheless rabbinically prohibited. It is here that the two become four.

### Partnership: Ani Initi…

*Pashat ha'ani et yado lifnim venatal ba'al habayit mitochah* — the *ani* extends his hand inside holding an object, and the *ba'al habayit* takes it from him. The *ani* performed the *akirah* (in the *reshut harabim*) and the transfer across the boundary; the *ba'al habayit* performed the *hanachah* by taking it into his hand. Both have participated in the act; neither has performed it fully. This is a rabbinic prohibition for both. Following Rashi's principle that we attribute the act primarily to the one who performed the *akirah*, this case is counted primarily as the *ani*'s rabbinic violation, since he initiated the act.

### Partnership: Ba'al Initi…

Conversely, the *ba'al habayit* extends his hand outside, and the *ani* places an object into it — *o shenatan letochah vehi'chnis*. The *ba'al habayit* then brings his hand back inside, completing the transfer and the *hanachah*. Here the *ani* performed the *akirah* (by picking up and placing the object into the *ba'al habayit*'s hand), while the *ba'al habayit* completed the act. Again, both bear rabbinic liability. This case is attributed primarily to the *ba'al habayit* since, although the *ani* performed the *akirah*, the *ba'al habayit* initiated the transfer by reaching out, and it is he who brings it in.

Questions naturally arise here about what precisely constitutes an *akirah* and what constitutes a *hanachah* in these shared scenarios. If an object is resting in someone's outstretched hand, is it already considered *munach* — at rest — so that taking it from there constitutes a new *akirah*? And when someone brings an object toward themselves and simply holds it, at what point is that considered *hanachah*? These questions — what constitutes *akirah* and *hanachah* in edge cases — are not resolved here and will occupy the *Gemara* extensively in the discussions that follow.

One further question is whether these partnership cases are analogous to *chatzi shiur* — the question of whether performing only half a prohibited act carries any liability. They are not identical: *chatzi shiur* involves one person performing a partial act that never reaches completion. Here, the *melacha* is fully accomplished, but through two people. Whether *chatzi shiur* applies to Shabbat at all — and what would constitute a *chatzi shiur* in the context of carrying — is a separate and important question the *Gemara* does address elsewhere.

### Summary: 2 Biblical + 4

The structure of the *mishnah* can now be summarized. The *ani*, standing outside, has two ways to perform a complete biblical violation — taking out or bringing in entirely on his own. These are the *shnayim bachutz*. Similarly, the *ba'al habayit* has two ways to perform a complete biblical violation — the *shnayim bifnim*. Those two become four in each case when one adds the two partnership scenarios in which each is the primary actor (by virtue of performing the *akirah*). Thus: two which are four on the outside, and two which are four on the inside.

A natural question presents itself: why does the *mishnah* begin Masechet Shabbat with this particular scenario — a *ba'al habayit* and an *ani* — rather than with a more systematic exposition of the *melachot*? Tosafot raises precisely this question. He understands why the subsequent *mishnayot*, which address what one may have on one's person before Shabbat begins, belong at the start of the *masechet* — they concern *erev Shabbat* and are chronologically prior. But why open with this case? One answer is that this was a genuinely common scenario, particularly in a world where the poor would come to the door on Shabbat hoping for food. The image of a *ba'al habayit* with his door open and an *ani* standing outside seeking bread was not abstract. Another answer — and perhaps the more resonant one — is that *hotza'ah* is a *melacha geru'ah*, a non-creative act whose halachic details are far from intuitive. So much of the structure of *hotza'ah* — what counts as *reshut hayachid*, what counts as *akirah* and *hanachah*, why partial acts matter — rests on *Torah she'Ba'al Peh*, oral tradition, rather than on any visible logic derived from the nature of the act itself. The *Gemara* elsewhere notes that it is precisely such areas — where the halachic structure is not self-evident — that receive particular emphasis. Beginning the *masechet* here signals to the student that a great deal of what follows will require careful attention to transmitted tradition rather than intuitive reasoning.

The choice of the *ani* and *ba'al habayit* rather than two anonymous individuals may itself carry a message: even when performing an act of *tzedakah* and *chesed*, one does not override the prohibitions of Shabbat. There are other ways to give food to the poor that do not involve a biblical — or even a rabbinic — violation. That point, made through a scenario where someone might naturally assume the good deed justifies the means, is a powerful opening lesson.

## Gemara: Av & Toledah

### Why Different Structure?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תְּנַן הָתָם: שְׁבוּעוֹת, שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע. מַאי שְׁנָא הָכָא דְּתָנֵי ״שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע בִּפְנִים וּשְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע בַּחוּץ״, וּמַאי שְׁנָא הָתָם דְּתָנֵי ״שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע״ וְתוּ לָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in our mishna: The acts of carrying out on Shabbat are two that comprise four. Similarly, **we learned** in the mishna **there,** in tractate *Shevuot*: **Oaths** on a statement, which, when violated, render one liable to bring a sin-offering **are two that comprise four.** The first two cases, which are mentioned explicitly in the Torah, are: One who swore that he would perform a specific action in the future and one who swore to refrain from performing said action. Based on an amplification in the language of the Torah, two more cases are added: One who swore that he performed a specific action in the past and one who swore that he did not perform said action. The Gemara asks: **What is different here that** our mishna **teaches: Two that comprise four inside and two that comprise four outside, and what is different there,** in tractate *Shevuot*, **that** the mishna **teaches** with regard to transfers on Shabbat: **Two that comprise four, and nothing more?**

The *Gemara* opens with a comparative question. It notes that the *mishnah* in Shevu'ot also opens with a formula of "two which are four," as do several other *mishnayot*. But the Shabbat *mishnah* says "two which are four on the inside, and two which are four on the outside" — doubling the formula. The *mishnayot* in Shevu'ot and elsewhere simply say "two which are four," without the inside/outside doubling. *Mai shna hacha d'tani shtayim she'hein arba bifnim v'shtayim she'hein arba bachutz? U'mai shna hatam d'tani shtayim she'hein arba v'tu lo?* — Why the difference?

### Parallels in Other Mishn…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יְדִיעוֹת הַטּוּמְאָה, שְׁתַּיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבַּע. מַרְאוֹת נְגָעִים, שְׁנַיִם שֶׁהֵן אַרְבָּעָה.
>
> **Translation:** Similarly, with regard to **awareness of ritual impurity,** there **are two** cases **that comprise four.** It is prohibited for one who is ritually impure to enter the Temple or to consume a consecrated item. However, one who unwittingly violates this serious prohibition is obligated to bring a sacrifice for his transgression only if he was clearly aware of his ritually impure status both before committing the transgression and thereafter. The two cases of unwitting transgression in this area are: One who was aware and then forgot that he is ritually impure, and then either ate consecrated meat or entered the Temple, and subsequently recalled that he was ritually impure. Two additional cases are: One who was aware of his ritually impure status but was unaware that the food he was about to eat was consecrated and ate it, or he was unaware that he was about to enter the Temple and entered it. **Signs of affliction by leprosy** are **two that comprise four.** The Torah (Leviticus 13) mentions two types of signs of affliction with regard to leprosy, *baheret* and *se'et*. Two additional, secondary signs of affliction were added. They are not as white as those delineated in the Torah. Consequently, there are derivatives of both *baheret* and *se'et*.

To appreciate the *Gemara*'s question, one must survey the *mishnayot* in Shevu'ot that share this structural formula. The opening *mishnah* of Shevu'ot states that there are two types of prohibited *shevu'ot* which are four. The *Torah* explicitly prohibits oaths oriented toward the future: swearing that one will do harm (*le'hara*) — for instance, that one will not eat tomorrow — or swearing to do good (*le'heitiv*) — that one will eat something tomorrow. If one violates such an oath, one has transgressed a biblical prohibition. Those are the two. They become four through the parallel cases of false oaths about the past — swearing that one did not eat yesterday when one did, or that one did eat when one did not. Thus: two oaths about the future and two about the past — all biblical, all generating liability.

The *mishnah* in Shevu'ot also lists *yedi'ot hatumah* — awareness of *tumah* — as two which are four. One who is *tamei* is biblically prohibited from eating *korbanot* or entering the *Beit HaMikdash*. Those are two cases. If one forgot that one was *tamei* (*ne'elam mimenu*) — as the *Torah* puts it, it was hidden from him — and either ate a *korban* or entered the *Mikdash*, he would bring a *korban* upon realizing his error. Those two cases of forgetting one's *tumah* become four when one considers the parallel cases where one remembered one's *tumah* but forgot the status of the object — that the food was a *korban*, or that the space was the *Beit HaMikdash*. So two primary cases of forgetting one's *tumah* expand to four when one adds the cases of forgetting the sanctity of the object or place.

Then there are *maro'ot nega'im* — the varieties of leprous spots — which are also two which are four. The *Torah* enumerates *se'is*, *sapachat*, and *baheret* as categories of *nega'im*. The *Sages* understand that there are two primary categories, *se'is* and *baheret*, and that *sapachat* designates a derivative — a *toladah* — of each. So *se'is* and its *sapachat*, and *baheret* and its *sapachat*, give us the two which are four. Crucially, *sapachat* is itself mentioned in the *Torah*, though as a secondary term. Finally, the *mishnah* there also lists *yetziot haShabbat* — carryings on Shabbat — as two which are four, referring to our *mishnah*.

### Av vs Av+Toledah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא דְּעִיקַּר שַׁבָּת הוּא, תָּנֵי אָבוֹת וְתָנֵי תּוֹלָדוֹת. הָתָם דְּלָאו עִיקַּר שַׁבָּת הוּא, אָבוֹת תָּנֵי, תּוֹלָדוֹת לָא תָּנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here,** in tractate *Shabbat*, which contains **the primary** discussion of the *halakhot* **of Shabbat,** the mishna **teaches the primary categories** of labor that are prohibited on Shabbat, including carrying out from the private to the public domain, and it **teaches the subcategories** of labor that are prohibited on Shabbat, including carrying from the public into the private domain. But **there,** in tractate *Shevuot*, which does **not** contain **the primary** discussion of the *halakhot* **of Shabbat,** the mishna **teaches the primary categories** of labor prohibited on Shabbat but **does not teach the subcategories** of labor.

The *Gemara* answers the structural question as follows: *hacha ikar Shabbat hu — tani avos v'tani toldahos*. Here, since this is a *masechet* dedicated entirely to Shabbat, the *mishnah* discusses both the *av* — the primary category — and the *toladah* — the derivative. That is why the formula is doubled: two which are four on the inside and two which are four on the outside, yielding twice as many cases as in Shevu'ot. *Hatam d'lav ikar Shabbat hu — avos tani, toldahos lo tani*. There, in Shevu'ot, Shabbat is not the central subject. Accordingly, only the *avot* are discussed, not the *toldahos*, and so the formula appears only once.

### Av: Hotza'ah, Toledah: H…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָבוֹת מַאי נִיהוּ? — יְצִיאוֹת, וִיצִיאוֹת תְּרֵי הָוְיָין!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What are the primary** categories of labor prohibited on Shabbat? They are acts of **carrying out** from the private domain to the public domain. However, the Gemara objects: The acts of **carrying out are** only **two** in number: There is the case of the homeowner who takes an object out of the private domain and places it in the hand of the poor person in the public domain and the case of a poor person who takes an object from the homeowner's hand in the private domain and takes it out into the public domain. What are the two additional cases referred to by the phrase: Two that comprise four, in tractate *Shevuot*?

The *Gemara* then asks: what is the *av* and what is the *toledah* in the context of *hotza'ah*? The answer: the *av* is *hotza'ah* proper — taking from a *reshut hayachid* to a *reshut harabim*. This is grounded in the *pesukim*: the verse in *Yirmiyahu* — *al totziu massa mibateichem b'yom haShabbat* — and the verse in the context of the *Mishkan* — *al yetzei ish* — which the *Gemara* understands as referring to bringing the contributions from one's home (a *reshut hayachid*) into the camp of the Levites (a *reshut harabim*). The *toladah* is *hachnasah* — bringing from the *reshut harabim* back into the *reshut hayachid*. Thus the *av* is *hotza'ah*, the *toladah* is *hachnasah*, and by treating both, our *mishnah* produces twice as many cases as the Shevu'ot *mishnah*.

### Shevu'ot Puzzle: Two to…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא, מֵהֶן לְחִיּוּב וּמֵהֶן לִפְטוּר, וְהָא דּוּמְיָא דְּמַרְאוֹת נְגָעִים קָתָנֵי: מָה הָתָם כּוּלְּהוּ לְחִיּוּבָא, אַף הָכָא נָמֵי כּוּלְּהוּ לְחִיּוּבָא!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you say** that the mishna in tractate *Shevuot* enumerates all four cases of carrying out, **among them** those **for** which there is **liability and among them** those **for** which there is **exemption,** including those mentioned in the second half of our mishna in which each individual performs only half of the prohibited labor, that is not feasible. The mishna in *Shevuot* **teaches** the prohibition of carrying out on Shabbat **parallel to the signs of affliction by leprosy. Just as there,** with regard to leprosy, **all** four **of them** are cases **for** which there is **liability, so too, here,** with regard to Shabbat, **all** four **of them** are cases **for** which there is **liability.**

A difficulty now arises. The *Gemara* proposes that the Shevu'ot *mishnah* deals only with the *av* — only *hotza'ah*, taking out. But *hotza'ah* alone yields only two cases (two ways to take something out — the *ani* doing it entirely, or the *ba'al habayit* doing it entirely). How does the Shevu'ot *mishnah* reach four?

One might suggest that the Shevu'ot *mishnah* expands to four by including the rabbinic partnership cases — the *derabbanans* — while still confining itself to the *av* of *hotza'ah* and setting aside *hachnasah* entirely. But this cannot be right. The *Gemara* points to the presence of *maro'ot nega'im* in the same *mishnah*: *dedumya demaros nega'im katani. Ma hasam kulhu l'chiyuva — af hacha nami kulhu l'chiyuva*. The spots of leprosy, where *sapachat* is itself mentioned in the *Torah*, establish that every item in that *mishnah* — all four of the "two which are four" — are biblical transgressions. Therefore, the four cases in the Shevu'ot *mishnah* must all be biblical, not a mixture of biblical and rabbinic. The presence of *tzara'at* in the *mishnah* is the decisive indicator.

### Rav Papa: Chiyuvim & Ptu…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: הָכָא דְּעִיקַּר שַׁבָּת הוּא, תָּנֵי חִיּוּבֵי וּפְטוּרֵי. הָתָם דְּלָאו עִיקָּר שַׁבָּת הוּא, חִיּוּבֵי תָּנֵי וּפְטוּרֵי לָא תָּנֵי. חִיּוּבֵי מַאי נִיהוּ? — יְצִיאוֹת, יְצִיאוֹת תַּרְתֵּי הָוְיָין! — שְׁתַּיִם דְּהוֹצָאָה וּשְׁתַּיִם דְּהַכְנָסָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rav Pappa said** that the difference between the manner in which the *halakha* is cited in tractates *Shevuot* and *Shabbat* must be understood as follows: **Here,** where it contains **the primary** discussion of the *halakhot* **of Shabbat,** the mishna **teaches** both cases of **liability and** cases of **exemption,** meaning cases of carrying out for which one is liable by Torah law as well as those for which one is exempt by Torah law. However, **there,** where it does **not** contain **the primary** discussion of the *halakhot* **of Shabbat,** the mishna **teaches** cases of **liability** but **does not teach** cases of **exemption.** The Gemara asks: **What are** the cases of **liability?** They are acts of **carrying out** from the private domain to the public domain. The Gemara objects on the grounds that there **are** only **two acts of carrying out:** Carrying out while standing inside and carrying out while standing outside. What is the meaning of the phrase in *Shevuot*: Which comprise four? The Gemara answers: It is possible to arrive at a total of four. Cases of carrying in from the public domain to the private domain are also enumerated in tractate *Shevuot*. Consequently, there are **two** cases **of carrying out and two** cases **of carrying in.**

Rav Papa resolves the difficulty: *hacha d'ikar Shabbat hu — tani chiyuvei u'pturei. Hatam d'lav ikar Shabbat hu — chiyuvei tani, pturei lo tani*. Here, in our *masechet*, where Shabbat is the central subject, the *mishnah* discusses both biblical transgressions (*chiyuvim*) and exemptions (*pturim*) — that is, the partnership cases which are exempt from biblical liability but rabbinically prohibited. There, in Shevu'ot, since Shabbat is not the focus, only biblical transgressions are discussed; the rabbinic cases are omitted.

But this pushes the difficulty back: if in Shevu'ot we are restricted to biblical cases of *hotza'ah* alone — the *av* alone — we can account for only two cases, not four. *Chiyuvei mai ninhu? Yetziot! Yetziot tarti havyan* — taking out is only two. So the *Gemara* resolves this by explaining that the four in the Shevu'ot *mishnah* consist of *shtayim d'hotza'ah v'shtayim d'hachnasah* — two cases of taking out (*hotza'ah*) and two cases of bringing in (*hachnasah*). All four are biblical. The two of *hotza'ah* are the *av*; the two of *hachnasah* are the *toladah* — but all four belong to the biblical level.

## Hotza'ah Term Analysis

### Shevu'ot: Hotza'ah & Hac…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא ״יְצִיאוֹת״ קָתָנֵי! אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: תַּנָּא הַכְנָסָה נָמֵי ״הוֹצָאָה״ קָרֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: In *Shevuot*, the phrase: Acts of **carrying out, is taught** in the mishna, not acts of carrying in. **Rav Ashi said:** The ***tanna*** in *Shevuot* **also refers to carrying in as carrying out.**

To summarize the position now established regarding the Shevu'ot *mishnah*: its "two which are four" consists of two ways to perform *hotza'ah* (taking from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut harabim*) and two ways to perform *hachnasah* (taking from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*). The two of *hotza'ah* constitute the *av*, the primary category; the two of *hachnasah* constitute the *toladah*, the derivative. All four are biblical transgressions. The Shevu'ot *mishnah* includes both *av* and *toladah* — but only at the biblical level, with no rabbinic cases.

### Our Mishnah: Ani & Partn…

Our *mishnah* in Shabbat presents a very different configuration. Its "two which are four" — whether *bifnim* or *bachutz* — does not consist of two ways of *hotza'ah* expanding to four by adding *hachnasah*. Rather, the two are the two ways the *ani* transgresses biblically — once by performing *hotza'ah* entirely on his own, and once by performing *hachnasah* entirely on his own. Those two expand to four by the addition of the two partnership cases in which the *ani* is the primary actor (because he performed the *akirah*). The structure is thus: from two biblical cases to four, where the additional two are rabbinic. An analogous structure applies to the *ba'al habayit* on the inside. This is fundamentally different from Shevu'ot, where all four cases are biblical and the expansion moves from *av* to *toladah* rather than from biblical to rabbinic.

### Hotza'ah Generic Term

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִמַּאי? מִדִּתְנַן: הַמּוֹצִיא מֵרְשׁוּת לִרְשׁוּת — חַיָּיב. מִי לָא עָסְקִינַן דְּקָא מְעַיֵּיל מֵרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד, וְקָא קָרֵי לַהּ ״הוֹצָאָה״?
>
> **Translation:** **From where** do I know this? **From that which we learned** in a mishna: **One who carries out** an object **from one domain to another is liable. Are we not** also **dealing** with a case **where he is carrying** it **in from the public domain to the private domain, and** nevertheless the mishna **characterizes it** as **carrying out?**

This analysis creates a terminological difficulty: the *mishnah* uses the word *yetziot* — "takings out" — to refer to the entire category. But if the *mishnah* in Shevu'ot includes *hachnasah* as part of "two which are four," and if even our *mishnah* includes both *hotza'ah* and *hachnasah* within the *ani*'s two cases, how can all of this be covered by the term *hotza'ah*? Rav Ashi addresses this: *tana hachnasah nami hotza'ah kari lei* — the *Tanna* calls *hachnasah* — bringing in — also by the name *hotza'ah*. The *mishnah* in the seventh *perek* which lists the *avot melacha* formulates the prohibition as *hamotzi mereshut lereshut* — taking from one domain to another — without specifying direction. Transfer from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid* is thus equally covered by the term *hotza'ah*. The word is generic, referring to domain transfer in either direction, even though conceptually the *av* is the outward direction and *hachnasah* is the *toladah*.

### Hotza'ah = Removal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְטַעְמָא מַאי? — כׇּל עֲקִירַת חֵפֶץ מִמְּקוֹמוֹ תַּנָּא ״הוֹצָאָה״ קָרֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the reason** that the term carrying out is used to refer to an act of carrying in? **The *tanna* characterizes any** act that involves **lifting of an object from its place** and transferring it to another domain as **carrying out.** Carrying out does not refer only to carrying an object out from one's house. Rather, it is a general depiction of moving an object from the domain in which it is located into another domain.

The etymological basis for this usage is as follows: *kol akiras chefetz mimkomo — tana hotza'ah kari lei* — any removal of an object from its place the *Tanna* calls *hotza'ah*. Even when one is bringing something indoors, there is an element of *hotza'ah* — removal — because the object is being taken out of the place where it was resting. The *akirah*, the picking up, is itself a form of *hotza'ah* — a taking out of position — regardless of the subsequent direction of movement.

### Ravina: Implicit in Mish…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבִינָא: מַתְנִיתִין נָמֵי דַּיְקָא, דְּקָתָנֵי ״יְצִיאוֹת״ וְקָא מְפָרֵשׁ הַכְנָסָה לְאַלְתַּר. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina said: Our mishna is also precise,** and its language leads us to the same conclusion, **as** the expression: Acts of **carrying out** on Shabbat, **was taught** in our mishna, yet **immediately** a case of **carrying in is articulated.** The first case listed in our mishna involves the poor person placing an object into the hand of the homeowner, which is a case of carrying in from the public to the private domain. The Gemara notes: Indeed, **conclude from this** that the term carrying out also refers to carrying in.

Ravina adds that this understanding is actually implicit in our own *mishnah*. The *mishnah* introduces the subject as *yetziot* — carryings out — and then immediately illustrates with a case of the *ani* sticking his hand *inside* and placing something in the *ba'al habayit*'s hand. The first example of *yetziot* is thus a case of *hachnasah*. This demonstrates directly that *hotza'ah* as used in the *mishnah* encompasses *hachnasah* — bringing in — as well.

### Rava: Reshuyot Framework

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר, ״רְשׁוּיוֹת״ קָתָנֵי: ״רְשׁוּיוֹת שַׁבָּת שְׁתַּיִם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The language of the *mishnayot* poses no difficulty. The *tanna* in both of these *mishnayot* did not teach: Acts of carrying out on Shabbat. Rather, **he taught: Domains** of Shabbat. The correct version of the mishna is: **The domains of Shabbat are two** that comprise four, and, according to this *tanna*, there are four instances of prohibited labor in these two domains, inside and outside.

Rava offers yet another reading of the term. According to Rava, *yetziot HaShabbat* does not refer to the direction of transfer at all — neither bringing out nor bringing in — but rather to the *reshuyot* themselves: *reshuyot katani — reshuyot Shabbat shtayim*, the two domains of Shabbat. *Yetziot HaShabbat shtayim she'hein arba* would then mean: there are two domains of Shabbat which generate four obligations. The exact mapping of this reading onto the breakdown of the *mishnah* remains somewhat difficult, but the fundamental point is clear: the term *hotza'ah* encompasses domain transfer in both directions because it refers primarily to the relationship between the *reshuyot* rather than to the directionality of movement.

### Conclusion: Av-Toledah

To conclude: the *av* of *hotza'ah* is carrying from *reshut hayachid* to *reshut harabim*; the *toladah* is *hachnasah*, carrying from *reshut harabim* to *reshut hayachid*. Both fall under the generic rubric of *hotza'ah*, because the defining feature is the removal of an object from its place — *akiras chefetz mimkomo* — rather than the direction of transfer. The Shevu'ot *mishnah* presents a "two which are four" that moves from *av* to *toladah*, all at the biblical level. Our *mishnah* presents a "two which are four" that moves from biblical to rabbinic, covering both *hotza'ah* and *hachnasah* within the biblical cases themselves. The discussion of *akirah* that underlies all of this — what it means to remove something from its place, and how that interacts with *hanachah* in the various partnership scenarios — will be taken up in the *Gemara*'s continuation.
