# Kiddushin 65 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chazakah & Yibum Belief

### Abaye's Clarification

The daf opens mid-*sugya*, carrying over from the previous discussion on 64b. The *mishna* had established that a husband is believed when he says on his deathbed that he has sons or no brothers — statements that would free his wife from *yibum* — but is not believed when he says the opposite, claiming he has brothers or no sons, which would obligate her in *yibum*. The rule appeared to be that he is believed to free her but not to bind her, without an explicit rationale. The *gemara* then cited a *braita* quoting Rebbe to that effect — *ne'eman lehatir ve'eino ne'eman le'asor* — and contrasted it with the position of Rebbe Natan, who held that the husband is believed both ways, at least in the scenario where he had said the freeing statement at the time of *kiddushin* and retracted it on his deathbed.

Abaye now clarifies the underlying logic. Our *mishna*, he says, is dealing with a case where *de'lo muchzak lan b'achi v'lo muchzak lan bivani* — we have no established presumption that he has brothers or sons. Given this starting point, if he says he has children, he is believed, because his statement reinforces the *chazakah* — our baseline presumption that she is *mutar*, not obligated in *yibum*. If he says he has brothers, he is not believed — *lav kol kimeinei de'asar lah akoli alma* — it is not within his power to make her forbidden to the world on his say-so alone.

### Status Quo Principle

The essential principle that emerges is not that the husband is believed to produce a *heter* and disbelieved to produce an *issur* in the abstract. Rather, the *gemara* frames it in terms of the *chazakah*: a person is believed when his statement is consistent with the prevailing presumption, and is not believed when his statement contradicts it. If what he says reinforces her status of being *mutar*, of course we believe him. If she already has a *chazakah* of being *mutar* and he now claims she is *assur*, he is not believed — he does not have the power, through his bare assertion, to override an established presumption and render his wife obligated in *yibum*.

The *braita* involving Rebbe Natan addresses a different factual configuration: *de'muchzak lan b'achi v'lo muchzak lan bivani* — we had reason to presume he had brothers, though no sons were known. In this setting, the woman was presumed obligated in *yibum* before the marriage. He then said at the time of *kiddushin* that he had no brothers, and the *gemara* considers why we believed him then, even against the prevailing presumption.

### Rebbe Natan Reconciliation

The answer is *mah lo leshaker*: why would he lie? One might think he was lying simply to induce her to marry him, promising she would not be bound by *yibum*. But that reasoning fails, because *matsi patarna lach b'gita* — he could simply have promised to give her a *get* that would take effect before his death, rendering the *yibum* question moot regardless. Since he had an alternative means of achieving the same practical result, there is no compelling motive for the lie, and therefore we believe him at the time of *kiddushin*, even to the point of shifting the *chazakah* in her favor.

### Mah Lo Leshaker Logic

This is the critical juncture. The first *mah lo leshaker* — his statement at the time of *kiddushin* — was powerful enough that Rebbe holds it was *k'eidim dami*, like the testimony of witnesses, and *asu eidim akrei chazakah* — it completely uprooted the prior *chazakah*. She is now fully presumed to be free of any *yibum* obligation. Then, on his deathbed, he says the opposite: he has brothers after all. Now Rebbe and Rebbe Natan divide.

Rebbe holds that the first *mah lo leshaker* was so powerful that it totally shifted the presumption. She is now presumed *mutar*, and he cannot overturn that new *chazakah* on his deathbed with a bare claim — especially since the *mah lo leshaker* logic does not apply in reverse. At the deathbed, why would he lie? One cannot say he had no alternative means to bind her; perhaps he is simply trying, in his final hours, to make her life difficult. There is no analogous argument eliminating his motive to lie, and therefore his retraction carries no weight against the established presumption.

Rebbe Natan disagrees. He holds the first *mah lo leshaker* was *k'chazakah dami* — it was like a *chazakah*, sufficient to neutralize the prior presumption, but not sufficient to establish a new one in the opposite direction — *v'lo atei chazakah v'akrei chazakah l'gamrei*. The original presumption was not completely erased, only suspended. When, on his deathbed, he reverts to the original presumption, he is in effect reinforcing what was there to begin with, and Rebbe Natan believes him.

### Mah Lo Leshaker Context

The *gemara*'s larger move, as one steps back, is to neutralize what appeared to be an absolute position of Rebbe Natan — that the husband is simply believed to make his wife obligated in *yibum*. That is not what Rebbe Natan says at all. Even according to Rebbe Natan, if the woman has a full *chazakah* of being *mutar*, the husband is not believed on his bare word to make her *assur*. The only case where Rebbe Natan believes him is precisely because his deathbed statement is reinforcing — even if only partially — a pre-existing *chazakah* that was never fully extinguished. The *mishna* and Rebbe Natan are thus not fundamentally at odds.

The broader application of *mah lo leshaker* — which functions here similarly to *migo*, though the *rishonim* debate the precise relationship between the two concepts — is that it can shift a *chazakah* but only within limits defined by whether it truly eliminates a plausible motive to deceive. At the time of *kiddushin*, the logic is compelling. On the deathbed, it is not — and without it, the man's statement cannot prevail against whatever *chazakah* is in place.

## Unspecified Daughters

### Adult vs. Underage

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna* concerning cases where a father marries off his daughter without specifying which daughter he means. The *mishna* states that *hamakadesh et bito stam* — one who betroths his daughter in general, without designation — *ein babogres bechlal*: the statement does not encompass daughters who have already reached the age of *bogeres*, i.e., who are more than twelve and a half years old and fully mature, because the father no longer has the halachic authority to betroth an adult daughter. The *gemara* will probe what this is teaching us and then turn to the more complex cases.

The *gemara* asks: if there are multiple underage daughters, are they all potentially betrothed by his unspecified statement? This raises the issue of *kiddushin she'einam mesurin l'viah* — a *kiddushin* that can never culminate in consummation, because the husband would never know which daughter he actually married, and any act with one might be a violation with the sister of his actual wife. The *gemara* resolves this by saying the *mishna* is discussing a case where the father has only one underage daughter and one adult daughter — *ein sham ela gedola u'ketana*. The use of the plural form referring to adult daughters is not an indication that this specific father has multiple adult daughters; it simply describes the general class of daughters in such matters.

### Father's Priority

But if there is only one underage daughter and one adult daughter, and the father is empowered to betroth both — since, as the *gemara* considers, the adult daughter may have appointed him as her *shaliach* — why assume the unspecified betrothal refers to the younger one rather than the older?

The *gemara* offers two reasons. First, *lo shavik inish midi d'isleih hana'ah minei v'avid midi d'lo isleih hana'ah minei* — a person does not abandon what yields him benefit and act instead on what yields him none. When a father betroths his underage daughter, he retains the *kesef kiddushin*; when he betroths his adult daughter, the money belongs to her. Even if the adult daughter waived her share as compensation for his efforts as *shaliach*, a second consideration applies: *lo shavik inish mitzvah d'ramya alei v'avid mitzvah d'lo ramya alei* — he would not set aside an obligation incumbent upon him in favor of one that is not. While his daughter is a minor, she is under his guardianship and it is his responsibility — indeed his *mitzvah* — to ensure she is married and protected. The Torah envisions a girl passing directly from *beis aviha* to *beis ba'alah*; the rabbis carve out a space for the adult woman who has not yet married, but that scenario does not exist in the Torah's own framing. The father's presumptive priority is therefore to marry off his younger daughter, and the unspecified statement is read accordingly.

### Two Groups Ambiguity

The *mishna* then introduces the more elaborate case: a man had daughters from two wives — sequentially, not simultaneously — and thus has two groups of daughters, an older cohort and a younger cohort. He says he betrothed *biti hagedola* — his older or oldest daughter — and then cannot remember which daughter he meant. The *mishna* enumerates the possibilities: did he mean the oldest of the older group? The older of the younger group? Or the youngest of the older group, who is nonetheless older than the oldest of the younger group?

According to Rabbi Meir, all of the daughters are *asuros* — forbidden to all other men until the matter is clarified — except for the youngest of the younger group, *chutz min haketana shebaketanos*, because no interpretation of the word *gedola* could possibly apply to her. According to Rabbi Yosi, all are *mutaros* except for the oldest of the oldest group, *chutz min hagedola shebagedolos*, because when a person uses the word *oldest* they mean it in its most precise and unambiguous sense.

The case is then presented in reverse: he said *biti haketana*, my younger or youngest daughter, and cannot remember which he meant. Rabbi Meir says all are forbidden except the oldest of the oldest group; Rabbi Yosi says all are permitted except the youngest of the youngest group.

### Meir vs. Yosi

The *gemara* asks whether it is necessary to state both cases — the *gedola* case and the *ketana* case. It concludes that both are necessary. If we only knew the *gedola* case, one might think Rabbi Meir holds his position only there — because any daughter who is older than someone else can be called *gedola* — but that when the father says *ketana*, surely he means the unambiguous youngest, and even Rabbi Meir would agree with Rabbi Yosi. Conversely, if we only knew the *ketana* case, one might think Rabbi Yosi holds his position only there, but that for *gedola* — a more positive and freely used descriptor — even Rabbi Yosi might agree that it could refer to a wider range, aligning with Rabbi Meir. Therefore both cases must be stated, showing that they debate both.

The underlying question is whether people speak or remember in ways that create ambiguity. Rabbi Meir holds that they do — *machnis inish nafshei l'safeka* — a person will record a memory as "oldest" even if that word does not uniquely identify the intended person. Rabbi Yosi holds the opposite — *lo machnis inish nafshei l'safeka* — a person would not characterize their memory with a term that leaves them in a state of genuine doubt; if he remembers *oldest*, he means it in its most determinate sense.

### Abaye's Two-Group Theory

A *braita* then presents an apparently reversed debate concerning *nedarim*: regarding the *neder* formulation *ad p'nei hapesach* — until the face of *Pesach* — Rabbi Meir says it means until *Pesach* arrives and Rabbi Yosi says it means until *Pesach* departs, which appears to be the exact inverse of their positions in the *mishna*. Rabbi Chanina bar Avdimi cites Rav: *muchlefet hashita* — the names are inverted in one source, and a *braita* indeed presents the *Pesach* positions consistently with the *mishna*, so that the same underlying logic governs in both cases.

Abaye then provides an important clarification of the *mishna* itself: *machlokes bishtei kitei banot* — the dispute between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yosi applies specifically to the case where there are two distinct groups of daughters. In that situation, any daughter from the older group can be described as *gedola* because she belongs to *hakat ha-gedola*, the older set, and thus the label is not anchored solely to the single oldest individual. This is what opens the possibility, under Rabbi Meir's reading, that multiple daughters could fall under the description.

By contrast, says Abaye, if there were only one group — say, four daughters from a single wife — *amtza'is bishmah kari lah*: the middle daughters would simply be called middle daughters. *Gedola* would refer unambiguously to the oldest, and *ketana* unambiguously to the youngest. The ambiguity Rabbi Meir identifies arises only because the two-group structure licenses a broader use of the term: she is *gedola* meaning she is from the older group, not that she is the single oldest individual.

### Rav Adda Challenge

Rav Adda bar Matna challenges Abaye: if his theory depends on the two-group structure, what happens if there are three daughters in the younger group rather than two? According to Abaye, the word *gedola* in the context of the younger group should attach only to the oldest daughter within that younger group, since she is the one who can be labeled *gedola* as its leading member. But then the *mishna* should permit the middle daughter of the younger group — why does it list her among the forbidden ones?

Abaye's response is that the *mishna* is indeed speaking of a case where there are only two daughters per group — *ein sham ela gedola u-ketana* — and this is further confirmed by the internal logic of the *mishna* itself: if there were three daughters in the younger group, the *mishna* would have needed to mention ambiguity about the middle daughter of that group, yet it does not. The *mishna*'s silence on that possibility is itself evidence that no such middle daughter exists in the case under discussion.

The *gemara* then presses: if that argument from silence works for the younger group, it should equally prove the absence of a middle daughter in the older group. The response is that the two situations are not parallel. In the older group, the *mishna* explicitly states that both the oldest and the youngest of that group are *assur* — so of course any middle daughter there is also *assur* and need not be separately listed. In the younger group, by contrast, the *mishna* says only the oldest daughter of that group is *assur*, implying that anyone younger within that group is *mutar* — so if a middle daughter existed, she would need to be mentioned as *assur*, and her absence from the list demonstrates she does not exist.

### Pesach Neder Parallel

Rav Huna the son of Rav Yehoshua raises a challenge to Abaye from the *Pesach* *neder* parallel: the case of *ad p'nei hapesach* was presented as analogous to the daughters debate, but if Abaye is correct that the daughters debate turns on the two-group structure, the *Pesach* case does not fit that framework. The days of *Pesach* are one continuous sequence — one group — not two distinct groups. So why should any intermediate day be understood as a potential *face* of *Pesach* that creates ambiguity?

The *gemara* responds that the *Pesach* debate is not actually about the same question at all. It is not a question of whether language creates ambiguity by being applicable to multiple members of a group; it is a linguistic dispute about the word *pnei* itself. One opinion holds *ad p'nei hapesach* means *ad kamei Pesach* — until before *Pesach*, i.e., until it arrives. The other holds it derives from *lipanot*, to turn away or depart — the Aramaic *mipanei*, to pass — meaning until *Pesach* has concluded. The debate is purely about the semantic range of the word *pnei*, not about the structure of groups or the breadth of ambiguous descriptors. The *Pesach* case and the daughters case thus involve distinct conceptual issues, and the apparent parallel between the two debates should not be overstated.

## He Said/She Said

### He Says/She Denies

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishna* presenting a series of conflicting claims about whether a betrothal occurred. The core principle governing all the cases is that a person's statement binds himself but cannot, through his words alone, obligate another person.

In the first case: a man says to a woman *kidashtich* — I betrothed you — and she says *lo kidashtani* — you did not betroth me. The rule is *hu assur bikrovoteha v'hi muteres bikrovav*: he is forbidden to marry her close female relatives — her mother, sister, daughter — since his own statement binds him, while she is free to marry his relatives, because his unilateral assertion cannot impose a prohibited status upon her.

### She Says/He Denies

In the reverse case: she says *kidashtani* — you betrothed me — and he says *lo kidashtich* — I did not. He is free to marry her relatives, since he made no claim. She, however, is forbidden to his relatives — and indeed, more basically, she is forbidden to any other man entirely, since she herself is asserting that she is married. Her own statement binds her, but it cannot impose any restriction upon him.

### Daughter vs. Mother

The third case: he says *kidashtich* to the mother, and she says *lo kidashta ela et biti* — you did not marry me, you married my daughter. He is forbidden to the mother's relatives — since he claimed the mother — but the mother is free to marry his relatives, since she does not claim to be married to him. He is permitted to marry the daughter's relatives, since he never claimed to have married her. And the daughter — *haktana muteres bikrovav* — the daughter is free to marry his relatives, because the mother's claim that the daughter is married to him does not bind the daughter.

### Mother vs. Daughter

The fourth case is the mirror image: he says *kidashti et bitchich* — I married your daughter — and the mother says *lo kidashta ela oti* — no, you married me. He is forbidden to the daughter's relatives, since he claimed her. The daughter is free to marry his relatives, since no claim was made about her and she made none about herself. He is free to marry the mother's relatives, since he never claimed her. The mother is forbidden to his relatives, since she claims to be married to him.

### Low Cost Principle

The *gemara* asks why it is necessary to state both the case where he makes a claim and the case where she makes a claim — are they not obvious? The response is that one might have thought differently in each direction. When the man says he married a woman, one could argue: *gavra lo ichpat lei u'mikrey amar* — for him, the cost of such a claim is so low that he might simply say it casually, without it being a sincere or reliable statement. After all, during *kiddushin* — as opposed to *nisu'in* — the betrothal does not immediately impose an obligation of *ketubah*, and the man's restriction is primarily limited to her close relatives. The *gemara* teaches us that even so, his statement is taken seriously and does bind him.

### Mother's Claim Invalid

Regarding the case where the mother claims the daughter is married: one might have thought that just as the Torah grants the father authority to effect and attest to the *kiddushin* of his minor daughter — *heimnu rachmana l'av* — the rabbis might extend analogous, if lesser, authority to the mother rabbinically, so that her claim would bind the daughter at least on a rabbinic level. The *mishna* teaches that this is not so. The mother is simply not believed with respect to the marriage status of her daughter — not biblically and not rabbinically — and her statement creates no prohibition upon the daughter at all.

### Get Debate

The *gemara* then records a dispute between Rav and Shmuel concerning the second case — where she claims he married her and he denies it. Shmuel says *m'vakshin mimenu litein get* — we ask him to give a *get* as a matter of good will, since her own statement has rendered her unable to marry any other man, and a *get* would release her. Rav adds: *im natan get mei'atzmo* — if he gives a *get* on his own initiative, unprompted by the court's request, *kofin oto litein ketubah* — we treat that act as an admission that they were married and compel him to pay her *ketubah*.

The *gemara* initially reads these as conflicting opinions and asks how one can *force* him to give a *get*, since writing a *get* would make her his divorcee and prevent him from marrying her relatives — a concrete cost to him that he might legitimately resist. The resolution is that Rav and Shmuel are not contradicting each other but building upon one another — *sh'maita d'adu adadei* — Shmuel establishes that we ask, and Rav adds that if he gives spontaneously, that is taken as an admission, with the consequence that the *ketubah* becomes enforceable. The practical lesson that emerges is that a man in this situation would be well advised to wait for the court's formal request before giving the *get*, rather than acting voluntarily, so that his act of generosity is not construed as a legal admission.

## Witnesses Requirement

### One vs. Two Witnesses

The *gemara* now arrives at what may be the most consequential legal question in the entire tractate, one that has taken sixty-four *dappim* to reach: whether the presence of kosher witnesses is constitutive of *kiddushin* — whether without such witnesses the act of *kiddushin* simply does not take effect as a matter of *halacha*, even if everyone present acknowledges that the betrothal occurred.

Rav Yehuda states: *hamikadesh b'ed echad ein chosheshin lekiddushav* — if a man betroths a woman before only one witness, the *kiddushin* is not binding and we have no concern that it took effect.

### Constitutive Question

This formulation immediately prompts a question that was posed to Rav Yehuda himself: *shnayim hamodim mai* — what is the rule when both parties agree? The question is probing the theoretical foundation of the witness requirement. Is the requirement evidentiary — do we need witnesses to prove that the betrothal occurred? Or is it constitutive — do we need witnesses in order for the act of *kiddushin* to achieve legal standing at all, irrespective of whether anyone doubts what happened? If both parties agree, and a single witness corroborates them, there is no evidentiary question — the act is not in doubt. If the *kiddushin* is still invalid in that case, it must be because witnesses are constitutive. Rav Yehuda's answer on this point was *rafya bidei* — it was unresolved in his hand, and he could not give a decisive ruling.

The question is then resolved by Rav Nachman citing Shmuel: *hamikadesh b'ed echad ein chosheshin lekiddushav v'afilu shneihem modim* — even if both parties fully agree that the betrothal occurred, without two witnesses it is not binding. The requirement for witnesses is constitutive, not merely evidentiary.

### Rava's Challenge

Rava challenges this ruling from the *mishna* we have just studied — the *he said/she said* cases. That *mishna* discusses scenarios where one party claims a betrothal occurred and the other denies it, with no mention of witnesses at all. If witnesses are constitutive — if without two witnesses there is simply no *kiddushin* — then the *mishna* makes no sense. If witnesses were present, how is anyone *mutar*? The witnesses themselves establish the marriage. If witnesses were not present, how is anyone *assur*? There is no binding *kiddushin* without them.

It must therefore be the case, argues Rava, that the *mishna* is operating in a scenario with one witness — and with one witness combined with one party's admission, that combination is sufficient to generate at least a partial status. So it appears that one witness plus an admission does constitute *kiddushin*, which would contradict Rav Nachman's ruling in the name of Shmuel.

### Witnesses Presumed

The *gemara* resolves this by re-reading the *mishna*: *kegon de'amar lah kidashtich bifnei ploni u'ploni vehalchu lahem lemedinas hayam* — the *mishna* is discussing a case where the man says "I betrothed you in the presence of so-and-so and so-and-so," explicitly claiming that two witnesses were present, even though those witnesses are not here to testify now. The *mishna* does not depict a situation where there were objectively no witnesses; rather, it depicts a situation of contested claims where witnesses are alleged but inaccessible.

This reading has sweeping implications for everything that came before. All the *mishnayos* throughout the tractate that discuss who is believed about a betrothal — the father's claim about his daughter, the man's claim at the time of *kiddushin*, all the *he said/she said* cases — must be understood as cases where witnesses are claimed to have been present, even though they are not being produced at the moment. Without that background assumption, none of those cases are legally coherent. The *gemara* has therefore quietly transformed the interpretive framework for the entire preceding discussion.

### Beis Shammai/Hillel

The *gemara* then tests Rav Nachman's ruling against a *braita* about a man who divorced his wife and later shared lodgings with her at an inn. The question is whether we are concerned that they renewed their *kiddushin* through intercourse — *kiddushei biah*. Beis Shammai say no second *get* is required; Beis Hillel say one is.

The *gemara* initially suggests that this debate might hinge on the testimony of a single witness, but quickly recognizes a much simpler explanation: *hacha b'mai askinan k'gon d'ika eidei yichud v'leika eidei biah* — the case involves witnesses to *yichud*, to the fact that the two entered seclusion together, but no witnesses to intercourse itself. The debate between Beis Shammai and Beis Hillel is not about the numerical sufficiency of witnesses but about the legal weight of *eidei yichud*: do witnesses to seclusion function as witnesses to intercourse — *hen hen eidei yichud hen hen eidei biah* — or not?

Beis Shammai hold that *eidei yichud* do not constitute *eidei biah*, and therefore no new *kiddushin* has been established. Beis Hillel hold that when the couple has a prior history of marital intercourse, witnesses to their entering seclusion are treated as equivalent to witnesses to intercourse, and a new *kiddushin* through *biah* has thereby taken effect, requiring a second *get*. Both schools agree, however, that if the couple had never consummated their original marriage — *niseis min ha'erusin* — *eidei yichud* would not be treated as *eidei biah*, because *ein libo gassah bah*: without the prior intimacy, we cannot presume they acted upon the seclusion.

The *gemara* concludes that this entire *braita* has nothing to do with the question of one versus two witnesses, and therefore poses no challenge to Rav Nachman's ruling. The question of how many witnesses are required for *kiddushin* to be constitutive remains firmly established as requiring two — and the question of what those witnesses must have observed is a separate matter, one that the debate between Beis Hillel and Beis Shammai illuminates. That discussion will continue on the following *daf*.
