# Shevuot 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Mishnah Struct…

### Intro & Mishnah Summary

We begin Shevuot 3 by picking up immediately after the very long *mishnah* that constitutes the entire first *perek*. The *mishnah* draws on the structural principle of "two which is four" — *shtayim she-hein arba* — which applies to *shevuot*: two categories of oaths in the past and two in the future. From there, however, the *mishnah* extends this same structural framework to an extensive series of cases involving *tumah*, covering the different ways in which a person who is *tamei* either enters the *Beit HaMikdash* or eats *korbanot*, with the consequence of bringing a *korban oleh veyored* — the sacrifice of variable value. The *mishnah* goes on to enumerate the various sacrificial rites — on *Yom Kippur*, *Rosh Chodesh*, and the *regalim* — that atone for the sin of *tumat mikdash*.

### Impurity & Atonement

The *mishnah*'s sustained focus on *tumat mikdash* is striking. It catalogs the personal *korban oleh veyored* brought when the violation was known at the outset, then forgotten, then recalled — as well as the range of communal *Yom Kippur* sacrifices that address various configurations of this sin.

### Yom Kippur & Scapegoat

In the *Torah*, *Yom Kippur* is centrally concerned with a kind of metaphysical cleansing of the *mikdash* itself, because all sin is understood to defile the sanctuary in a cosmic sense. The *mishnah*, however, shifts the focus away from this metaphysical dimension. Rather than the grand purification of the *mikdash*, the *mishnah* emphasizes atonement for the individual who accidentally entered the sanctuary while *tamei*. Toward the end of the *mishnah*, there is a return to broader scope: the *mishnah* notes that for all other sins — whether *meizid* or *shogeg*, whether punishable by death or relatively minor — the goat sent off the cliff on *Yom Kippur* atones.

### Why Temple Impurity?

This raises a genuine structural question: why do the first two chapters of a tractate named for oaths devote themselves almost entirely to the laws of *tumat mikdash*? At least part of the answer may lie in the final line of this *mishnah*. Having concluded *Sanhedrin* and *Makkot*, which deal exhaustively with the penalties for transgression — death sentences, lashes, exile — one might feel the overwhelming weight of all that sin and its consequences. Our *mishnah* offers an answering perspective: no matter how grave the transgression, even one for which the court would impose a death sentence, this single ritual on *Yom Kippur* — the dispatching of the goat — achieves atonement.

### Context: Sanhedrin & Mak…

The end of *Makkot* offered one reframing of the weight of all those transgressions: it is all *le-zazkot et Yisrael*, to ease the burden on the Jewish people — simply refrain from transgression and receive an enormous reward, so the entire weight of the forbidden is reimagined as an invitation to merit. Our *mishnah* provides a complementary answer: however heavily one has sinned, the mechanisms of *Yom Kippur* atonement remain available. So there is a conceptual bridge between the close of *Makkot* and the opening of Shevuot.

### Oath-Impurity Analogy

Beyond this thematic continuity, there is an analogy being drawn that may explain the deeper reason why *tumat mikdash* receives such extended treatment within a tractate devoted to oaths. The *korban* for a false oath and the *korban* for *tumat mikdash* are structurally identical — both are the *korban oleh veyored* — and in the *Torah* these two cases appear side by side. This juxtaposition is not merely textual coincidence; the *gemara* will make explicit that they appear together because they are genuinely analogous.

### Oaths & God's Presence

There is a further conceptual connection worth exploring. The entire *masechet* concerns the invocation of an oath — typically in God's name, either explicitly or implicitly — as a mechanism for establishing truth. When a person swears an oath, they are in effect summoning the divine presence to testify to the veracity of their claim; God is, as it were, brought into the world to bear witness. When that oath is false, the person has defiled the very space they created by that invocation. In the absence of the *mikdash*, the sworn oath in God's name is among the primary loci of divine presence in the world — and to violate it through falsehood carries something of the quality of *metamei ha-mikdash*, defiling the sanctuary. This, it may be argued, is why a *masechet* devoted to oaths opens with two full chapters on *tumat mikdash*: the two are understood as manifestations of a single underlying concern.

### Q&A: Yom Kippur & Sins

One might ask how *Yom Kippur* atonement relates to sins between one person and another — particularly in cases where the *Sanhedrin* would impose capital punishment. The answer is that *Yom Kippur* rectifies the relationship between the person and God, but human justice runs on a parallel track. A person could achieve the most complete *teshuvah* imaginable and be fully atoned for before Heaven, while the *beit din* would still be obligated to carry out its sentence. The system of earthly justice cannot simply dissolve its procedures on account of repentance, even repentance that is genuine and total — and this remains true in secular legal systems as well, which permit only very limited acknowledgment of transformation or remorse. Furthermore, when the sin involves another person, the *mishnah* in *Yoma* makes clear that *Yom Kippur* cannot atone until the offender has made things right with the person he has wronged. One cannot make things right with God while the interpersonal injury remains unaddressed.

## Gemara: Why This Order?

### Michti Question

The *gemara* opens with the word *michti* — "having just come from" — and poses its first question: *tanna me-heicha salik?* — from where did our *tanna* just depart? The tractate of Shevuot follows *Makkot*. *Mai shena de-tani Shavuot?* — why does Shevuot come after *Makkot*? This is a question not about the internal structure of our *masechet* but about the ordering of tractates within *Seder Nezikin*.

### Tractate Ordering

The scholarly consensus is that tractate ordering follows a principle of size — number of chapters, number of *mishnayot*. Even though Shevuot has more chapters than *Makkot*, if one treats *Sanhedrin* and *Makkot* as a single extended tractate — which the structure of those two *masachtot* effectively invites — then Shevuot follows naturally as the next tractate by size.

### Full List in Shevuot

But the *gemara* goes on to ask a more pointed question. We have here not one but several instances of the two-which-is-four pattern: oaths, *tumah*, *tzara'at*, and *Shabbat*. Each of the relevant tractates — *Shabbat* in *Seder Moed* and *Negaim* in *Seder Taharot* — contains the corresponding two-which-is-four in its own opening *mishnah*, but only the portion directly relevant to that tractate. Here in Shevuot, the full list appears. *Mai shena hacha de-tani lehu le-kulhu?* — why here do we collect all of them together? And conversely, why do *Yetziat ha-Shabbat* and *Mara'ot Negaim* present only their respective portion?

### Torah's Juxtaposition

The *gemara* answers: *amri shevuot vi-yedi'ot ha-tumah de-gabi hadadi kesivan* — oaths and the laws of *tumah* in the *mikdash* are written side by side in the *Torah*. As we saw in the opening verses reviewed earlier, the *Torah* treats the *korban oleh veyored* for a false oath and the *korban oleh veyored* for *tumat mikdash* within the same passage.

### Same Parsha Teaching

These two cases are not merely physically adjacent; they appear together because they are genuinely similar — they share the same *korban*, the *oleh veyored*, and belong to the same *parsha* addressing the same legal mechanism.

### Reasoning for Collection

Once it is established that oaths and *tumat mikdash* belong together, and given that there is no independent tractate devoted to *tumat mikdash*, these two must be addressed within a single tractate. Since two elements of the list are already being brought together, the editorial logic follows: *ve-aydi de-tani tarti, tani kulhu* — since we are going to present two items from the list, we may as well present the entire list here.

### Rabbi's Compilation

This reflects a broader pattern in the editorial work of *Rebbi* in compiling the *Mishnah*. Earlier collections of *mishnayot* often gathered materials with a shared formal structure — parallel constructions, repeated patterns — even when the underlying topics differed entirely. A particularly prominent example is the series beginning *ein bein* in *Megillah*, where some twenty disparate topics are organized around the single formal framework of "there is no difference between X and Y except Z." Such collections were likely compiled as independent units, and when *Rebbi* edited the tractates, he typically distributed the relevant lines to their proper thematic locations. Here, however, the full list is preserved intact, and the *gemara* explains why: the underlying connection between oaths and *tumat mikdash* made Shevuot the natural home for the complete collection.

## Opening Structure Explai…

### Short Material First

Having explained why oaths and *tumat mikdash* appear together and why the full list is presented here, the *gemara* addresses a remaining structural question. The *mishnah* opens with the phrase *shevuot shtayim she-hein arba* — oaths are two which is four — clearly announcing oaths as its theme. Yet the tractate then immediately devotes two full chapters to *tumat mikdash* before returning to oaths at all. Why not proceed directly into the laws of oaths?

The *gemara* answers: *aydi de-zutri mileyhu* — since the material on *tumat mikdash* is relatively short. Two chapters is not nothing, but measured against the full seven chapters of the tractate, it is the shorter element. Rather than interrupting the sustained treatment of oaths midway through with a digression, the better editorial approach is to dispose of the shorter material first.

### Cut & Untie Metaphor

The *gemara* uses a vivid phrase: *pasik sharei lehu* — cut it off and untie it. The image is of completing a discrete task cleanly and fully, so that it does not remain as an open thread. The two chapters on *tumat mikdash* represent a self-contained body of law; better to address it in its entirety before moving on.

### Then Focus on Oaths

Once the *tumat mikdash* material has been fully resolved — *batar hachi tani shavuot de-nafishi mileyhu* — then the tractate turns to oaths, which is its true and primary subject and on which there is a great deal to say across the remaining chapters. The structure of the tractate, in other words, reflects a deliberate editorial choice: address the shorter digression first, then give undivided attention to the main topic.

## Tosafot on Shaving Laws

### Sidelocks & Razors

Before turning to the substance of the *gemara* on oaths, there is an important body of *halachic* material in *Tosafot* that addresses an issue which came up at the end of *Makkot* — the prohibition of shaving the beard and sidelocks — but which was not treated fully in the *Tosafot* there. The relevant *Tosafot* appears here on daf 2b. *Tosafot* states: *nire de-af al gav de-al ha-zakan eino chayav ela be-ta'ar* — even though for the beard one is only liable if one uses a *ta'ar*, a straight razor, as was discussed at the end of *Makkot* (which is why contemporary shavers are permitted for the beard, on the basis that a shaver does not constitute a *ta'ar*, since the prohibition requires *giluach she-yesh bo hashchatah* — a shaving that involves complete destruction of the hair at the root) — *u-ve-hakafat ha-rosh chayav af be-misparayim ke-ein ta'ar*: for *hakafat ha-rosh*, the sidelocks, one transgresses even by using scissors functioning like a razor.

### Beard vs. Sidelocks

This marks a fundamental distinction. For the beard, only a genuine *ta'ar* — a straight razor applied directly to the skin — triggers the prohibition. For the sidelocks, the standard is broader: any implement that functions like a razor, even scissors operating in a razor-like fashion, is prohibited.

### Rambam's View

The *Rambam* disagrees with this position of *Tosafot*. The *Rambam* holds that *misparayim ke-ein ta'ar* — scissors functioning like a razor — are in fact permitted for the sidelocks. The practical difference between the two authorities is significant.

### Modern Shavers Prohibited

The accepted *halacha* follows *Tosafot*, and this is why one who uses a shaver for the beard — which is permitted because the shaver's mechanism does not constitute a *ta'ar* in the halachic sense — may nevertheless not use that same shaver on the sidelocks. Since a shaver operates by bringing a blade flush against the skin in a manner functionally equivalent to a razor, it falls under the category of *misparayim ke-ein ta'ar* and is prohibited for *hakafat ha-rosh*. This explains the common practice of shaving the beard with an electric shaver while leaving the sidelocks untouched — not merely unshaved, but specifically not shaved with any razor-like instrument. Attachments or trimming devices that do not make direct skin contact may be treated differently, depending on how closely they approximate a *ta'ar*, but any device pressed flush against the skin in the area of the sidelocks is problematic.

### Giver & Receiver

The following *Tosafot*, on daf 3a, addresses a second important question in connection with the passage from *Makkot*: *nire de-mashchit ve-nishchat sheneihem chayavim* — it appears that both the one who performs the shaving and the one who receives it are liable. This is not self-evident. The prohibition is expressed as an act performed by one person upon another, and one might have supposed that only the barber transgresses. *Tosafot* makes clear that both parties bear liability.

### Non-Jewish Barber

This leads to a consequential practical ruling. *Tosafot* takes up the case where the barber is a *goy* — a non-Jew who is not obligated in *mitzvot*. One might reason that since the barber himself has no obligation, the Jew receiving the shave cannot be said to have caused a transgression on his behalf, and therefore bears no personal liability. *Tosafot* rejects this reasoning: it does not matter from the perspective of the one receiving the shave whether the one administering it is obligated in *mitzvot* or not. The prohibition on the one receiving the shave is independent. Accordingly, one may not present oneself to a non-Jewish barber for a straight-razor shave of the beard or sidelocks. The liability attaches to the recipient regardless of the status of the one performing the act.

## The Four Categories

### Carrying on Shabbat

The *mishnah* now presents the full list of two-which-is-four cases. *Yetziot ha-Shabbat shtayim she-hein arba* — the acts of carrying on *Shabbat* are two which is four. The canonical description of this is drawn from the opening *mishnah* of *Masechet Shabbat*, which presents the scenario of a homeowner inside a private domain and a poor person standing in the public domain. The paradigmatic form of the *melachah* of carrying (*hotza'ah*) is bringing an object from the *reshut ha-yachid*, the private domain, to the *reshut ha-rabbim*, the public domain — or vice versa. The scriptural basis for this being the primary form is *Moshe*'s instruction to the people not to bring any more donations for the *mishkan* from their private homes into the public collection area, establishing that the transfer between private and public domains is the prototypical prohibited act.

### Four Carrying Ways

The two primary cases are: the homeowner extends his hand out and places an object in the hand of the poor person (transfer from private to public, initiated from within), or the poor person extends his hand inward and takes the object from the homeowner (transfer from private to public, initiated from without). These are the *shtayim* — the two. The *arba* — the four — are the mirror image: the poor person extends his hand inward and places something into the private domain of the homeowner (*hachnasas he-ani*), or the homeowner extends his hand out and takes something from the poor person (*hachnasas ba'al ha-bayit*). All four constitute violations of *Shabbat*, and in this case the two-which-is-four structure maps cleanly onto a uniform *halacha*: all four are prohibited with equal force.

### Leprosy Types

*Mara'ot nega'im shtayim she-hein arba* — the visible signs of *tzara'at* are two which is four. The *Torah* specifies two primary types of skin discoloration that constitute *tzara'at*: *se'eit* and *baheret*. Each of these primary categories has its own subcategory — *se'eit* and its *toldah*, *baheret* and its *toldah*, the latter being called *sapachat*, a secondary type of marking that is equally *metamei* but is conceptually treated as derivative of the primary category. All four types carry the same *halachic* consequence: the person is a *metzora*. Again, the two-which-is-four is not merely a formal categorization; it reflects a uniform legal reality.

### Authorship Question

With the list laid out, the *gemara* now asks: *mani masnisin?* — who is the author of our *mishnah*? The answer, which will require some working through, is: *lo Rabbi Yishmael ve-lo Rabbi Akiva* — neither *Rabbi Yishmael* nor *Rabbi Akiva*.

## Ishmael vs. Akiva

### Ishmael on Future Oaths

The reasoning unfolds as follows. *Ee Rabbi Yishmael — ha-omer eino chayav ela al ha-atid lavo* — *Rabbi Yishmael* holds that one is only liable to bring a *korban* for a false oath about the future. The verse in the *Torah* reads *ki tishava levate bisefatayim le-hara o le-heitiv* — when one swears to do harm or to do good — and the context of the *korban oleh veyored* is explicitly tied by *Rabbi Yishmael* to this formulation, which is future-oriented: one swears about what one will or will not do. The two-which-is-four in that case would be: I will do something and I do it (oath fulfilled, no transgression), I will do something and I don't (oath violated, *korban*), I won't do something and I don't (oath fulfilled), I won't do something and I do (oath violated, *korban*). For *Rabbi Yishmael*, past-oriented oaths — I swear that I did something I didn't do, or that I didn't do something I did — do not generate a *korban*. They may carry other consequences, but the *korban oleh veyored* applies only to the future-oriented cases. So *Rabbi Yishmael* does not hold of the full two-which-is-four for *shevuot*; he holds of only two.

### Akiva's Position

*Rabbi Akiva*, by contrast, does hold of the full two-which-is-four for oaths: one brings a *korban* for violating an oath whether it was past-oriented or future-oriented. But *Rabbi Akiva* holds a different position regarding *tumat mikdash*. The *Torah* states *ki tiga be-chol davar tamei asher yitma bah ve-ne'elam mimenu* — if one touches any *tamei* thing and it becomes hidden from him. The simple reading is that what is hidden is the *tumah* itself — *he'elem tumah* — and this generates two cases (eating *korban* while *tamei*, or entering the *Beit HaMikdash* while *tamei*), which is the primary two. The additional two that make it four come from *he'elem mikdash* — one forgot not just that one was *tamei* but that the place one was entering was the *Beit HaMikdash*, or forgot the sanctity of the *korban* one was eating. *Rabbi Akiva* holds that one is only liable for the case of *he'elem tumah* — forgetting that one was *tamei* — and not for *he'elem mikdash* — forgetting that one was in the sanctuary. So for *Rabbi Akiva*, the *tumat mikdash* category yields only two cases, not four.

### Neither Holds Both

The problem is thus clearly defined. *Rabbi Yishmael* holds of the full two-which-is-four for *tumat mikdash* but only two for oaths. *Rabbi Akiva* holds of the full two-which-is-four for oaths but only two for *tumat mikdash*. Neither sage holds of the complete two-which-is-four for *both* categories simultaneously. Yet our *mishnah* presents the full list across both categories as if they are parallel and complete. So who authored our *mishnah*?

### Partial Application Answ…

The *gemara* first attempts to resolve this by suggesting that the two-which-is-four in our *mishnah* need not mean uniform liability in all four cases. Perhaps the *mishnah* is merely cataloguing four conceptually distinct types, some of which generate liability and some of which do not — *mehem le-chiyuv u-mehem le-patur*. On this reading, *Rabbi Yishmael* could be the author: he acknowledges four types of oaths even though only two generate a *korban*, and he holds of all four *tumat mikdash* cases with full liability. Similarly, *Rabbi Akiva* could be the author: he holds of all four oath cases with full liability, and he acknowledges four types of *tumat mikdash* even though only two generate a *korban*. The *gemara*, however, rejects this approach. The rest of the *mishnah* makes it evident that two-which-is-four means uniform *halacha* applies to all four. The *tzara'at* cases and the *Shabbat* cases are all equally *metamei* or equally prohibited — the structure of the *mishnah* signals legal equivalence, not merely formal categorization. So the partial-application reading is forced and is set aside.

## Lashes Not Korban

### Lashes Interpretation

The *gemara* pivots to an entirely different interpretive approach. Perhaps our *mishnah*'s two-which-is-four is not about *korban* at all. Perhaps it is about *malkot* — lashes. On this reading, the *mishnah* is asserting that all four types of false oaths (past positive, past negative, future affirmative, future negative) generate lashes when violated willfully. This sidesteps the dispute between *Rabbi Yishmael* and *Rabbi Akiva* about which cases generate a *korban*, since now the *mishnah* is speaking of an entirely different legal consequence.

### Past Oaths & Lashes

For past-oriented oaths, it is relatively clear that lashes apply, and the *gemara* will elaborate this through *Rava*'s comparison.

---

## Lashes Not Korban…

### Rava's Comparison

*Rava* states: *be-ferush ribetah Torah shvuat sheker dumya de-shvuat shav* — the *Torah* explicitly compared a false oath (*shvuat sheker*) to a vain oath (*shvuat shav*). The *shvuat shav* — the oath taken in vain — generates lashes by virtue of the verse *ki lo yenakeh Hashem et asher yisa shemo la-shav*: God will not cleanse the one who takes His name in vain. The *gemara* understands this to mean that while God will not cleanse him, the earthly court will — by administering lashes, which constitute an atonement that releases the person from the Heavenly consequence. This is the same principle articulated at the end of *Makkot*: the punishment administered by the court below serves as an instrument of divine cleansing.

### Utterance & Violation

The reason the comparison works — *mah shav le-she'avar af sheker nami le-she'avar* — is that both a vain oath and a past-oriented false oath share the same essential characteristic: the violation occurs at the moment of utterance. A *shvuat shav* is an oath about something obviously true or obviously impossible; the moment it leaves one's lips, whether trivially true or trivially false, the transgression is complete. A past-oriented *shvuat sheker* works the same way: whether I swear that I ate eggs this morning when I did not, or that I did not eat them when I did, the lie is already done the instant I speak it. There is no future act required to complete the transgression.

## Four Oath Cases

### False Past Positive

The first of the four cases: I swear that I ate something when in fact I did not. This is a past-oriented false oath, a *shvuat sheker le-she'avar*. The violation is complete the moment I utter the oath. By *Rava*'s comparison to a *shvuat shav*, lashes apply.

### False Past Negative

The second case: I swear that I did not eat something when in fact I did. This is equally past-oriented and equally a *shvuat sheker le-she'avar*. The same reasoning applies, and lashes follow.

### Future: Will Eat

The third case: I swear that I will eat a loaf of bread tomorrow, and then I eat it — but I swear falsely in the moment, or I take the oath earnestly and then violate it. The complication here is that unlike the past-oriented cases, the violation does not occur at the moment of utterance. Nevertheless, when the future moment arrives and I actively eat the bread in violation of the oath, I have performed a concrete act. *Nami lav she-yesh bo ma'aseh hu* — this is a negative prohibition accompanied by an act. The act of eating is the transgression, and since lashes apply to violations involving an active deed, lashes are given here.

### Future: Won't Eat

The fourth case: I swear that I will eat a loaf of bread, and then I do nothing — I simply fail to eat it. This is the case that generates the difficulty. The day passes, the bread sits uneaten, and I have violated my oath — but purely through inaction.

### Passive Transgression Pr…

The problem is severe: how does one receive lashes for a purely passive transgression? The moment of utterance was not false — at that moment I fully intended to eat the bread — so one cannot assimilate it to the past-oriented cases via *Rava*'s comparison. And since the violation consists entirely of *not* performing an act, there is no positive deed that could anchor the lashes. This is the classic *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — a negative prohibition without a concrete act — and it raises the question of whether such a transgression can ever generate *malkot*.

## Yochanan & Passive Acts

### Yochanan Follows Stam

*Rabbi Yochanan* follows the principle that *halacha* is determined by the *stam mishnah* — an unattributed *mishnah* in the *Mishnah* of *Rebbi*. Our opening *mishnah*, which is unattributed, thus presents itself as the authoritative ruling. If our *mishnah* is speaking of lashes, and it asserts the full two-which-is-four including the case of the passive non-performance, then *Rabbi Yochanan*'s principle would seem to commit him to the position that one does receive lashes for a passive transgression.

### Opening Mishnah Suggests

This is indeed what our *mishnah*, on the interpretation the *gemara* has been developing, appears to teach: that all four types of false oath — including the case of "I will eat" followed by not eating — generate *malkot*.

### Eggs Case: No Lashes

Yet *Rabbi Yochanan* himself rules otherwise. Together with *Reish Lakish*, he holds that if a person swears he will eat a loaf of bread today and the day passes without his eating it, *ein lokeh* — he does not receive lashes. Both *Rabbi Yochanan* and *Reish Lakish* agree on the *halacha*; they disagree only on the reason.

---

## Yochanan & Passive Act…

### Two Reasons for No

*Rabbi Yochanan* says: *ein lokeh mishum de-havei lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — he does not receive lashes because this is a passive transgression, and any negative prohibition that lacks a concrete act does not generate lashes: *ve-chol lav she-ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav*. *Reish Lakish* offers a different reason: *ein lokeh hatrat safek hi* — he does not receive lashes because the warning (*hatra'ah*) required before administering lashes is doubtful in efficacy. For lashes to apply, a valid warning must immediately precede the transgression. Here, the warning would have to be issued in the morning — "you must eat bread today or you will receive lashes" — but at that moment it is entirely unknown whether the person will transgress; perhaps he will eat. The warning does not immediately precede a clear imminent act, and so it is a *hatrat safek*, a doubtful warning, which is insufficient to establish liability for *malkot*.

---

## Support in Notar Mishnah

### No Lashes for Notar

The *gemara* seeks to support *Rabbi Yochanan*'s position by identifying another *stam mishnah* that would allow him to rule against our opening *mishnah*. The candidate it first considers is a *mishnah* which states: *aval ha-motir be-tahor* — one who leaves over the meat of a valid *korban* past the deadline, *eino lokeh et ha-arba'im* — does not receive lashes. This was reviewed in *Makkot*.

### Passive = No Lashes

Why does one not receive lashes for leaving over *notar*? The straightforward answer would be that this is a passive transgression — one simply failed to eat the meat in time — and any passive transgression is exempt from *malkot*. If this is the reason embedded in the *stam mishnah* about *notar*, then *Rabbi Yochanan* has his supporting *stam mishnah*: another unattributed *mishnah* that implies the same principle he is invoking.

### Yehuda's Reason

But the *gemara* challenges this inference. The *mishnah* about *notar* may exempt from lashes for an entirely different reason — the reason of *Rabbi Yehuda*: *mishum de-ba kasuv litein asei achar lo ta'aseh* — because the *Torah* comes to attach a positive commandment (*asei*) after the negative prohibition. When a negative prohibition is linked to a corrective positive *mitzvah* — a *lav ha-nitak le-asei* — lashes do not apply, because there is a remedial act available. The *Torah* says: do not leave over any meat until morning — and whatever is left over shall be burned in fire. The positive commandment to burn the *notar* follows the prohibition against leaving it over, and that linkage may be the reason lashes do not apply, not the principle of *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh*.

### Yaakov's Principle

*Rabbi Yaakov* explicitly rejects the *Rabbi Yehuda* explanation: *lo min ha-sheim zeh* — the exemption from lashes for *notar* does not rest on the connection to a positive commandment. Rather, *ela mishum de-havei lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* — it is simply because leaving over the meat is a passive transgression, and *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh ein lokin alav* — any passive transgression is exempt from lashes as a general principle. So the *mishnah* about *notar* follows *Rabbi Yaakov*'s principle. However, the *gemara* points out that this is uncertain: the *mishnah* about *notar* can be read as following either *Rabbi Yehuda* or *Rabbi Yaakov*, and without knowing which, we cannot confidently derive the general principle from it.

---

## Which Mishnah Prevails?

### Unclear Reasoning

The problem, then, is that the *mishnah* about *notar* does not provide a reliable basis for *Rabbi Yochanan* to override our opening *mishnah*, because it is unclear whether the *notar* exemption rests on the *lav she-ein bo ma'aseh* principle or on the *lav ha-nitak le-asei* principle.

### Two Oaths Mishnah

The *gemara* therefore turns to a different *stam mishnah* — one that addresses the exact scenario at issue. The *mishnah* states: *shvuah she-lo ochal kikar zu* — one takes an oath that he will not eat this loaf of bread. *Shvuah she-lo ochlena* — and then takes a second oath not to eat it. *Ve-achalah* — and then he eats it. *Eino chayav ela achat* — he is only liable once, for one set of lashes. Why? Because the second oath could not take effect: since he was already obligated by the first oath not to eat the bread, he is *mushba ve-omed* — already bound by a prior oath — and a second oath on the same subject has no legal force. So the first oath did take effect, and when he ate the bread, he actively violated it and received lashes.

### Active Violation Only

The *mishnah* then concludes: *zo hi shvuat bituy she-chayavin al zidonah malkot ve-al shigagata korban oleh veyored* — this is the *shvuat bituy* for which one is liable to lashes for willful violation and a *korban oleh veyored* for unwitting violation. The *gemara* reads the phrase *zo hi* — "this is the one" — as a pointed exclusion. *Zo hi de-chayavin al zidonah malkot* — this particular type of violation — where you said you would not eat and you actively ate — is what generates lashes. *Aval ochal ve-lo achal* — but if you said you would eat and then you failed to eat, *lo laki* — you do not receive lashes. The *stam mishnah* about two oaths thus provides *Rabbi Yochanan* with his supporting source: an unattributed *mishnah* that directly addresses our case and implies that passive non-performance does not generate *malkot*.

---

## Rabbi's Compilation Expl…

### Rabbi's Contradiction

This resolution raises a new difficulty. Both *mishnayot* — our opening *mishnah*, which apparently says lashes apply even in the passive case, and the later *mishnah* about two oaths, which implies they do not — are *stam mishnayot*, unattributed rulings that *Rebbi* incorporated into the *Mishnah*. How can *Rebbi* himself have compiled two *mishnayot* that contradict each other on so fundamental a question?

### Initial Teaching

The *gemara* explains that this reflects a process of intellectual development. *Me-ikara savar lav she-ein bo ma'aseh lokin alav ve-satma* — initially, *Rebbi* held that a passive transgression does incur lashes, and he taught this ruling as an unattributed *mishnah*, which became our opening *mishnah* of Shevuot, presenting the full two-which-is-four including the passive case.

### Later Change

*Ve-hadar savar ein lokin alav* — subsequently, *Rebbi* reconsidered and concluded that passive transgressions do not generate lashes. He changed his ruling. The later *mishnah* about two oaths — *zo hi* — reflects this revised position: lashes attach only to active violation.

### Cannot Revoke

But *Rebbi* faced a practical impossibility: *ve-mishnah lo zaza mi-mekomah* — the original *mishnah* could not be uprooted from its place. By the time *Rebbi* changed his mind, the earlier *mishnah* had been disseminated, learned, and memorized by students across the academies. One cannot simply instruct a community to stop learning an established *mishnah* and declare it void; the tradition had already taken root.

### Later Clarification

So *Rebbi* took a different approach. Unable to revoke the earlier ruling, he composed and disseminated a later *mishnah* — the *mishnah* about two oaths — which, through the interpretive phrase *zo hi*, makes clear that lashes apply only to the active case. This later *mishnah* effectively signals the correct *halacha* to those who know how to read it, even as the earlier *mishnah* remains in place.

### Summary & Resolution

The resolution of the *gemara*'s long inquiry is thus as follows. Our opening *mishnah* — *shevuot shtayim she-hein arba* — is speaking of *malkot*, not *korbanot*, and this is what allows it to present a complete two-which-is-four that neither *Rabbi Yishmael* nor *Rabbi Akiva* would endorse if the subject were *korbanot*. The two past-oriented cases clearly generate lashes via *Rava*'s comparison to *shvuat shav*. The two future-oriented cases are the *chiddush*: the *mishnah* asserts that even the passive case — "I will eat" followed by not eating — generates lashes. However, this assertion reflects *Rebbi*'s initial position, which he subsequently revised. The later *mishnah* about two oaths, which *Rabbi Yochanan* relies upon, establishes the authoritative ruling: a passive transgression of a *shevuah* does not generate *malkot*. Only active violation does.
