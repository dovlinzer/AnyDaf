# Yevamot 36 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pregnant Woman Debate

### Rabbi Yochanan's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר: הַחוֹלֵץ לִמְעוּבֶּרֶת וְהִפִּילָה, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין, חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה. וְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ אָמַר: צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין, חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: An amoraic dispute **was stated** with regard to **one who performs *ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman and she** later **miscarried.** Since she miscarried, she was certainly bound to the *yavam* by a levirate bond and may not marry anyone else; rather, she is obligated to consummate the levirate marriage or perform *ḥalitza*. The question is whether the *ḥalitza* that was performed while she was still pregnant is effective in releasing her from the levirate bond. **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: She does not require** another ***ḥalitza* from the brothers.** Similarly, **intercourse with a pregnant woman** who later miscarries **is considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse,** such that she and the *yavam* are considered to be married. **And Reish Lakish said she requires** another ***ḥalitza* from the brothers** because he holds: ***Ḥalitza*** performed **with a pregnant woman is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*, and intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse.**

The daf opens on 35b, four lines before the wide lines begin, with a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish concerning the validity of *chalitzah* and *yibum* performed while the *yevama* is pregnant. The *mishnah* establishes that the outcome of such acts depends on whether the child born is viable (*ben kayama*) or not. The critical question, however, is whether Reish Lakish goes further than the *mishnah* and holds that even if the child turns out not to be viable — meaning, retroactively, that *chalitzah* was necessary — a *chalitzah* performed while the woman was pregnant is nonetheless not a valid *chalitzah* and must be repeated.

Rabbi Yochanan opens by challenging Reish Lakish from the *mishnah*: *ein ha-vlad shel kayama hu assur bi-krovoteha ve-hi assura bi-krovav u-psula min ha-kehuna* — if the child is not viable, the man who performed *chalitzah* is forbidden to her relatives, she is forbidden to his relatives, and she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen*. In other words, the *mishnah* treats the *chalitzah* as having rendered her a *chalutza*, with all its attendant consequences. Rabbi Yochanan presses: *bishlama ledidi* — this works out well according to me, who holds that *chalitzat me'uberet shema chalitzah*, that *chalitzah* performed on a pregnant woman is a valid *chalitzah* if the child subsequently proves non-viable. *Mishum hachi pasla* — that is why the *mishnah* disqualifies her to a *kohen*, because she has genuinely become a *chalutza*. *Ela ledidach* — but according to you, Reish Lakish, who holds that *chalitzat me'uberet lo shema chalitzah*, why does the *mishnah* impose the *chumra* of *psula min ha-kehunah*? By your account, the *chalitzah* was never meaningful to begin with.

### Reish Lakish Challenges

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּלְחוּמְרָא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish **said to** Rabbi Yochanan: Indeed, by Torah law the *ḥalitza* is to be entirely disregarded. The mishna's ruling that she is disqualified from the priesthood is **by rabbinic** decree **and is merely a stringency** lest people not realize that she was pregnant and think that a *ḥalutza* is permitted to marry a priest.

Reish Lakish responds: *miderabbanan u-lechumra*. The Rabbis can treat a *chalitzah* performed on a pregnant woman as a *chalitzah* for purposes of stringency — rendering her a *chalutza* with all the relevant prohibitions — without conceding that it is a valid *chalitzah* at the biblical level. Hence the *mishnah* imposes all the *chumrot* associated with *chalitzah* without affirming that the act actually freed her. She will still require a proper *chalitzah* from the brothers.

### Alt. Version of Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אֵין הַוָּלָד שֶׁל קַיָּימָא — הוּא אָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, וְהִיא אֲסוּרָה בִּקְרוֹבָיו, וּפְסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי ״פְּסָלָהּ מִן הַכְּהוּנָּה״ — לְחוּמְרָא, וְלָא קָתָנֵי ״אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין״. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, ״אֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! אִין הָכִי נָמֵי. וְאַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא רֵישָׁא ״לֹא פְּסָלָהּ״ — תְּנָא [נָמֵי] סֵיפָא ״פְּסָלָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who say** an alternate version of the dispute: **Reish Lakish raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rabbi Yochanan** from the mishna: If *ḥalitza* was performed with a pregnant woman and **the offspring is not viable,** then **he is forbidden to her relatives and she is forbidden to his relatives, and** the *ḥalitza* **disqualifies her from the priesthood.** Reish Lakish explains the challenge: **Granted, according to my** opinion, **as I say that *ḥalitza*** performed **with a pregnant woman is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*, this is** consistent with **that which is taught** in the mishna: The *ḥalitza* **disqualifies her from the priesthood,** which should be understood **as** a rabbinic **stringency, and** it is understandable that the mishna **does not teach: She does not require** another ***ḥalitza* from the brothers,** because according to my opinion, once she miscarries, she does indeed require another *ḥalitza* from the brothers. **However, according to your** opinion that *ḥalitza* performed with a pregnant woman is an effective *ḥalitza*, the mishna **should have** taught the full extent of her permissible status, i.e., that **she does not** even **require** another ***ḥalitza* from the brothers** after she miscarries because the original *ḥalitza* was effective. Rabbi Yochanan **said to** Reish Lakish: **Yes, it is indeed so** that she does not require another *ḥalitza* from the brothers, **but since the first clause taught:** The *ḥalitza* **does not disqualify her** from the priesthood, therefore **the latter clause taught:** The *ḥalitza* **disqualifies her** from the priesthood, in order to directly contrast with the first clause rather than teach the greater novelty.

The Gemara then presents the alternate version: *ika de-amri*, some say the exchange ran in the opposite direction, with Reish Lakish initiating the challenge to Rabbi Yochanan. Reish Lakish cites the same *mishnah* language and argues: *bishlama ledidi* — according to me, who holds that *chalitzat me'uberet lo shema chalitzah*, it makes sense that the *mishnah* teaches only the *chumrot*: she is forbidden to a *kohen*, she is forbidden to his relatives, and so on. *V'lo katni eina tzricha chalitzah min ha-achin* — but it does not tell you the major *chiddush* that she is now fully free and requires no further *chalitzah* from the brothers. That larger leniency is absent because it would be false according to my view. *Ela ledidach* — but according to you, Rabbi Yochanan, the *mishnah* should have led with the primary novelty: that *chalitzah* performed while she was pregnant, if the child proves non-viable, fully exempts her and she needs no further *chalitzah* from the brothers. Why did the *mishnah* omit that *kula*?

Rabbi Yochanan answers: *ein hachi nami*, you are correct that it could have said so. *Aidi detani reisha lo pasla* — but since the opening clause of the *mishnah* established that when the child turns out to be viable, the *chalitzah* carries no weight even *lechumra* — there are no *chumrot* at all, we treat the act as though it never occurred — the *mishnah* set up the parallel in the concluding clause by teaching the *chumra* of *psula min ha-kehunah* for the non-viable scenario. But you are correct, Rabbi Yochanan concedes: she would in fact need no further *chalitzah*, and that *kula* is implicit even if unstated.

### Yibum Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: אֵין הַוָּלָד שֶׁל קַיָּימָא — יְקַיֵּים. בִּשְׁלָמָא לְדִידִי, דְּאָמֵינָא חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, מִשּׁוּם הָכִי קָתָנֵי ״יְקַיֵּים״. אֶלָּא לְדִידָךְ, דְּאָמְרַתְּ חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, ״יַחְזוֹר וְיִבְעוֹל וִיקַיֵּים״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! מַאי ״יְקַיֵּים״ — יַחְזוֹר וְיִבְעוֹל וִיקַיֵּים, דְּלָא סַגִּי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yochanan raised an objection to** the opinion of **Reish Lakish** from the mishna: If a *yavam* had intercourse with his *yevama*, who was pregnant, and **the offspring is not viable,** and therefore she was bound by a levirate bond, **he may maintain** her as his wife. Rabbi Yochanan explains the challenge: **Granted, according to my** opinion, **as I say *ḥalitza*** performed **with a pregnant woman is considered** effective ***ḥalitza*, and** similarly, **intercourse with a pregnant woman is considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse,** it is **due to that** reason that the mishna **teaches: He may maintain** her as his wife, because the levirate marriage was indeed valid. **However, according to your** opinion, **as you said** that ***ḥalitza*** performed **with a pregnant woman is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*, and intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse,** the mishna **should have** taught that **he should proceed to engage in intercourse** with her again in order to consummate the levirate marriage **and** only then **may he maintain** her as his wife. Reish Lakish responded: **What is** the intention of the mishna when it teaches: **He may maintain** her as his wife? Perforce it means that **he should proceed to engage in intercourse** again with her **and** then **he may maintain** her as his wife, **as** any other way **is insufficient** to release her from the levirate bond.

Rabbi Yochanan now presses Reish Lakish from a different angle. If a man performed *yibum* with his *yevama* while she was pregnant, and she subsequently miscarried, the *mishnah* says he may remain married to her — *yikayem*. *Bishlama ledidi* — according to me, who holds that both *chalitzat me'uberet shema chalitzah* and *biat me'uberet shema biah*, the act of *yibum* is retrospectively valid if the child proves non-viable. Hence *mishum hachi tani yikayem* — that is why it says he may remain married. *Ela ledidach* — but according to you, Reish Lakish, who holds the act was not meaningful, the *mishnah* should not merely say *yikayem*; it should say *yachzor v'yibom* — let him go back and perform a proper act of *yibum*, and only then remain married. Why does it simply say *yikayem*?

### Reish Lakish's Response

Reish Lakish responds: *mai yikayem* — what do you think *yikayem* means? It means precisely *yachzor v'yibom v'yikayem* — let him perform another act of *bi'ah* to make the *yibum* binding, and then he may remain married. And there is no other option here: *d'lo sagi b'lav hachi* — there is no way forward without this step.

Rashi explains the reasoning: *d'lo mistra belo biah l'achar she'hipilah* — after she has miscarried, if he now wishes to either stay married or separate from her, he cannot simply give a *get*, because *biat me'uberet lav shema biah* — the earlier act did not constitute a valid act of *yibum*. Nor can a *chalitzah* follow, since he is in any case considered to have had relations with her and performing *chalitzah* after such contact would be entirely inappropriate at least at the rabbinic level. Therefore, his only available step is to have another act of *bi'ah*, which will then constitute binding *yibum*. After that, he may either remain married or give a *get*. Thus, *yikayem* is not merely permission to remain married — it is instruction that this is the only path forward regardless of what he ultimately chooses to do.

The Gemara then presents the alternative version of this exchange as well — *ika d'amri* — in which Reish Lakish uses the *yibum* case proactively to argue against Rabbi Yochanan, and Rabbi Yochanan responds *ein hachi nami*, that *ratza yotzi ratza yikayem* could have been stated, but the *mishnah* chose the parallel construction because the opening clause in the viable-child case required the language of *yotzi* — since there the man must divorce, as the woman is his brother's wife — and so the concluding clause correspondingly used *yikayem*, meaning he is permitted to remain married.

## Abaye Reframes

### Abaye's Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בְּבִיאָה כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לָא פְּלִיגִי דְּלָא פָּטְרָה, כִּי פְּלִיגִי בַּחֲלִיצָה. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר: חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, בִּיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה. רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ סָבַר: בִּיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, וַחֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** that the opinion ascribed to Rabbi Yochanan above is not an accurate portrayal of his opinion. Rather, **with regard to intercourse** with a *yevama* who is pregnant at the time, **everyone agrees that it does not release** her and her rival wives from the levirate bond; **when they disagree** it is only **with regard to** whether ***ḥalitza*** performed with a *yevama* who is pregnant at the time is effective in releasing her and her rival wives from the levirate bond. Abaye continues to explain his opinion: **Rabbi Yochanan holds** that ***ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman is considered** effective ***ḥalitza*,** but **intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse** and therefore does not release her and her rival wives from the levirate bond. And **Reish Lakish holds** that **intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse, and *ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*.** Therefore, the *baraita* cited above does not pose a challenge, since all agree that intercourse with a pregnant woman will not free her or her rival wives from the levirate bond.

The Gemara then raises a *baraita*: *hakones et yevamto v'nimtzeit me'uberet harei zo lo tinasei tzaratah* — a man who performed *yibum* with his *yevama* and it turns out she was pregnant: her co-wife (*tzarah*) may not go ahead and remarry, lest the child be viable. This poses a question: if the child is viable, then *yibum* was unnecessary; if not viable, the *yibum* was valid. Either way, the *tzarah* seems to be released. Why then is she barred from remarrying?

The Gemara emends: the concern is *shema lo yehei havelad ben kayama* — perhaps the child will not be viable, meaning the *yibum* was necessary but invalid because performed during pregnancy. This seems to be strong support for Rabbi Yochanan's opponents. Abaye steps in with a reframing: *bebiat kulei alma lo pligi delo patra* — regarding *bi'ah* performed as *yibum* with a pregnant woman, everyone agrees it does not take effect. The logic is clear: since pregnancy creates a presumption that the child may be viable, the act of *bi'ah* would risk transgressing the prohibition of *eishet ach*, and therefore no retroactive validity can be granted. *Ki pligi bachalitzah* — the dispute is limited to *chalitzah*. Since *chalitzah* separates the man and woman rather than drawing them together, there is at least an argument that it could be valid even if performed during pregnancy. On this Rabbi Yochanan holds *chalitzat me'uberet shema chalitzah*, conceding that *biat me'uberet lo shema biah*; Reish Lakish holds both are invalid.

### Focus on Chalitzah

According to Abaye's reframing, the *tzarah* cannot remarry in the *yibum* case because everyone agrees the act of *bi'ah* did not work, and therefore if the child proves non-viable she remains bound and requires *chalitzah* or valid *yibum*. The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish thus concerns only whether *chalitzah* performed during pregnancy is retrospectively valid.

## Rava's Objection

### Package Deal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי בִּיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה — חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה. וְאִי בִּיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה — חֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת נָמֵי לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה. דְּהָא קַיְימָא לַן, דְּכׇל הָעוֹלֶה לְיִבּוּם — עוֹלֶה לַחֲלִיצָה, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין עוֹלֶה לְיִבּוּם — אֵינוֹ עוֹלֶה לַחֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: Whichever way you** look at it, one cannot differentiate between the validity of consummating the levirate marriage through intercourse and the validity of *ḥalitza*. **If intercourse with a pregnant woman is considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse** because if she miscarries it is apparent that there was always an obligation to perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, then perforce ***ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman** should also **be considered** effective ***ḥalitza*. And if intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse** because in her currently pregnant state there is no obligation to perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*, then perforce ***ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman** should also **not be considered** effective ***ḥalitza*. As we maintain** **that anyone who is eligible for levirate marriage is eligible for *ḥalitza* and anyone who is not eligible for levirate marriage is not eligible for *ḥalitza*.** Therefore, the original understanding of Rabbi Yochanan's opinion, that both the intercourse and the *ḥalitza* of a pregnant woman are valid, was accurate.

Rava objects to Abaye's reframing: *mamma nafshach* — make up your mind. *I biah me'uberet shema biah, chalitzat me'uberet shema chalitzah* — if the *bi'ah* is valid, then the *chalitzah* is valid; *v'i biah me'uberet lo shema biah, chalitzat me'uberet nami lo shema chalitzah* — if the *bi'ah* is not valid, the *chalitzah* is not valid either. The principle is that *kol ha'oleh l'yibum oleh l'chalitzah, v'chol she'eino oleh l'yibum eino oleh l'chalitzah* — whatever is eligible for *yibum* is eligible for *chalitzah*, and whatever is ineligible for *yibum* is ineligible for *chalitzah*. They are a package deal. Abaye's bifurcation — that *yibum* fails but *chalitzah* succeeds — cannot be sustained.

### Child's Birth Factor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הַכּוֹנֵס יְבִמְתּוֹ וְנִמְצְאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא צָרָתָהּ, שֶׁמָּא יְהֵא וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא. וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה, וַחֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה. וְהַוָּלָד אֵינוֹ פּוֹטֵר עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** Rava therefore provides a different defense of Rabbi Yochanan's opinion: **Rather, Rava said** that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** In the case of **one who consummates** the levirate marriage **with his *yevama*** under the assumption that there is a mitzva to do so, **and** then **she was found to** have been **pregnant** at the time of the intercourse, **a rival wife of this** *yevama* **may not marry lest the offspring be viable, and intercourse with a woman pregnant** with viable offspring **is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse, and *ḥalitza* of a woman pregnant** with viable offspring **is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*. And** furthermore, even if **the offspring** is viable, it **does not release** her and her rival wives from the levirate bond **until it comes into the air of the world,** i.e., until it is actually born.

Having rejected Abaye's reframing, Rava now explains the *baraita* differently. *Hachi ka'amar* — here is what the *baraita* is actually saying: the *tzarah* cannot remarry *shema yihiyeh velad ben kayama* — lest the child be viable. Now, if the child is viable, why does it matter whether the *yibum* or *chalitzah* was valid? Because an entirely new principle now enters the discussion: *ha-velad eino poter ad sheyeitzei l'avir ha'olam* — the child does not release the wives from the *zikah* until he is actually born. Even if we know the child will be viable, the co-wives are not free until the moment of birth. Therefore, the *baraita*'s concern is precisely this: if the child turns out to be viable, neither *yibum* nor *chalitzah* was meaningful — everyone agrees on that — but furthermore, even knowing a viable child is coming, the co-wives remain bound until the birth actually occurs. Hence the *tzarah* cannot go ahead and remarry.

### Supporting Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הַכּוֹנֵס יְבִמְתּוֹ וְנִמְצְאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא צָרָתָהּ, שֶׁמָּא יְהֵא וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא, וְאֵין בִּיאָה וַחֲלִיצָה פּוֹטֶרֶת, אֶלָּא וָלָד פּוֹטֵר. וְהַוָּלָד אֵין פּוֹטְרָהּ עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rava:** In the case of **one who consummates** a levirate marriage with **his *yevama*, and** then **she was found to** have been **pregnant** at the time of the intercourse, **a rival wife of this** *yevama* **may not marry lest the offspring be viable.** This is because **intercourse or *ḥalitza*** with a woman pregnant with viable offspring **does not release** a *yevama* from the levirate bond; **rather,** the **offspring releases** her. **And** furthermore, even if **the offspring** is viable, it **does not release her** and her rival wives from the levirate marriage bond **until it comes into the air of the world.**

A *baraita* is then cited in direct support of Rava's framework: *tanya kevateih d'Rava* — we taught a *baraita* consistent with Rava. *Hakones yevamto v'nimtzeit me'uberet harei zo lo tinasei tzaratah shema yihiyeh velad ben kayama* — and now the *baraita* spells out why that is a problem: *v'ein biah v'chalitzah poteret* — neither *bi'ah* nor *chalitzah* exempts in the case of a viable child. *Ela velad poter* — it is the child that exempts. *She-ha-velad eino poter ad sheyeitzei l'avir ha'olam* — and the child does not exempt until he has actually entered the world.

## Co-Wife's Status

### Proof for Reish Lakish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא דְּשֶׁמָּא יְהֵא וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא הוּא, הָא לָא הָוֵי וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא מִיפַּטְרָא צָרָתָהּ, לֵימָא תֶּיהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains that the *baraita* appears to contradict Reish Lakish's opinion: According to the *baraita*, **the** only **reason** that levirate marriage with the pregnant *yevama* does not permit the rival wife to marry **is as** the *baraita* stated: **Lest the offspring be viable.** By inference, **were the offspring not viable, her rival wife would be released** from the levirate bond. If so, **let us say that** this *baraita* **is a conclusive refutation** of the opinion of **Reish Lakish.**

The Gemara now draws an inference from the *baraita*: *taama d'shama yihiyeh velad ben kayama* — the reason the *tzarah* cannot remarry is specifically because the child might be viable and the birth has not yet occurred. *Ha lo havi velad ben kayama, miftar tzaratah* — but if the child were not viable, the *tzarah* would be permitted to remarry. This seems to be a proof in favor of Rabbi Yochanan: the *yibum* performed during pregnancy would be retroactively valid if the child proves non-viable, and the *tzarah* would then be released.

The Gemara therefore asks whether this constitutes a conclusive refutation of Reish Lakish. Reish Lakish responds: *hachi katani* — here is how the *baraita* is truly to be read. The *tzarah* cannot remarry for two independent reasons. First, *shema lo yehei havelad ben kayama* — maybe the child will not be viable, in which case *chalitzat me'uberet lo shema chalitzah u-viat me'uberet lo shema bi'ah* — neither the *chalitzah* nor the *yibum* was meaningful, and the *tzarah* remains bound. Second, even if you invoke *rov nashim velad malya yalden* — most pregnancies produce viable children, and one might assume the *tzarah* is effectively free — the answer is *velad eino poter ad sheyetzei l'avir ha'olam*: she is still not released until the child is born. Thus the *baraita* focused on the viable-child scenario because that is the one people might think resolves the problem, but in truth the *tzarah* is stuck either way — if non-viable, the *yibum* failed; if viable, she must wait for the birth. Nothing effective happens while the woman is pregnant.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: הַכּוֹנֵס יְבִמְתּוֹ וְנִמְצְאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא צָרָתָהּ, שֶׁמָּא לֹא יְהֵא הַוָּלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא, וַחֲלִיצַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ חֲלִיצָה, וּבִיאַת מְעוּבֶּרֶת לֹא שְׁמָהּ בִּיאָה. וְאִם תֹּאמַר: הַלֵּךְ אַחַר רוֹב נָשִׁים, וְרוֹב נָשִׁים וָלָד מְעַלְּיָא יָלְדָן, וָלָד אֵין פּוֹטֵר עַד שֶׁיֵּצֵא לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara defends Reish Lakish's opinion: **Reish Lakish** could have **said to you: This is what** the *baraita* **is teaching:** In the case of **one who consummates** a levirate marriage with **his *yevama*, and** then **she was found to** have been **pregnant** at the time of the intercourse, **a rival wife of this** *yevama* **may not marry lest the offspring not be viable,** which would mean that all the rival wives are bound by the levirate bond. **And** even if one of those wives has intercourse or performs *ḥalitza* with the *yavam*, it would be ineffective in releasing them from the levirate bond because ***ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman is not considered** effective ***ḥalitza*, and intercourse with a pregnant woman is not considered** a valid consummation of levirate marriage through **intercourse.** Reish Lakish explains the need for the final clause of the *baraita*: **And** even **if you say:** Let the wives marry without the need for any levirate marriage or *ḥalitza* because one should **follow** the **majority of women, and** the **majority of women give birth to a full-fledged,** i.e., viable, **offspring,** and therefore one should presume no levirate bond exists, to counter this claim the *baraita* concludes: Even if the offspring will be viable, an **offspring does not release** a *yevama* and her rival wives from the levirate bond **until it comes into the air of the world.**

### Husband Dies Away

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר: אֶפְשָׁר אִיתָא לְהָא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ וְלָא תְּנַן לָהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין? נְפַק, דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דִּתְנַן: הָאִשָּׁה שֶׁהָלַךְ בַּעְלָהּ וְצָרָתָהּ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וּבָאוּ וְאָמְרוּ לָהּ ״מֵת בַּעְלִיךְ״ — הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא וְלֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם עַד שֶׁתֵּדַע שֶׁמָּא מְעוּבֶּרֶת הִיא צָרָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Elazar said:** Is it **possible** that **there is** halakhic acceptance **of this** opinion **of Reish Lakish and it was not** hinted to by something **taught in the Mishna? He went out** of the study hall, carefully **checked** the *mishnayot*, **and found** one that supported Reish Lakish's opinion, **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **a woman whose husband and rival wife went overseas, and** then witnesses **came and said to her: Your husband died,** and her husband had a brother, **this** woman **may neither marry** someone other than his brother, **nor may she enter into levirate marriage** with that brother, **until she knows** whether **perhaps her rival wife is pregnant.** If she discovers that her rival wife is not pregnant, she would then be able to perform levirate marriage or *ḥalitza*. If she discovers her rival wife is pregnant, she would have to wait to see if the pregnancy is viable. If it is found to be viable, only at that point would she be permitted to marry someone else.

Rabbi Elazar then remarks that Reish Lakish's position must have support in a *mishnah* somewhere, and upon investigation he finds it. The *mishnah* teaches: *ha-ishah she-halach ba'alah v'tzaratah limdinat ha-yam* — a woman whose husband went overseas together with her co-wife, and word came back that the husband died. *Harei zo lo tinasei v'lo tityabem* — she may neither remarry nor perform *yibum* with the brother, *ad shetedah shema me'uberet hi tzaratah* — until she knows whether the co-wife is pregnant.

The Gemara proceeds to analyze this. Why can she not perform *yibum* with the brother? *Shema yehei havelad ben kayama v'yifga b'issur eishet ach d'Oraita* — lest the co-wife carry a viable child, making the act of *yibum* a biblical transgression of *eishet ach*, since if a viable child exists there is no *mitzvah* of *yibum* at all. That is clear. But why can she not perform *chalitzah* and then remarry? That question requires careful examination.

### Nine-Month Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא נְחֱזֵי. אֵינִי מוּכְרַח לוֹמַר שֶׁלֹא תַּחְלוֹץ וְלֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא בְּתוֹךְ תִּשְׁעָה, הֲלוֹא יְכוֹלָה לַחְלוֹץ בְּתוֹךְ תִּשְׁעָה וְתִנָּשֵׂא לְאַחַר תִּשְׁעָה — לֵימָא תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Let us think about this. I understand that she cannot perform *ḥalitza* and remarry during** the first **nine** months following her husband's death **since during that period we are uncertain whether the co-wife is pregnant with viable offspring. But why not perform *ḥalitza* during** the first **nine** months following her husband's death **and** then proceed to also **marry after** those **nine** months? At that point, either the *ḥalitza* was valid because the co-wife was not pregnant, or if she was pregnant the child would by then have been born and the co-wife released in any event. This combined approach should satisfy all possibilities. Since the *mishnah* does not offer this option, it appears to prove like Reish Lakish — that *ḥalitza* performed while the co-wife is pregnant is not valid, and therefore performing *ḥalitza* now would be pointless if the co-wife turns out to be pregnant with a viable child.

The Gemara asks: *ela nechzei* — let us think about this. I understand that she cannot perform *chalitzah* and remarry *betoch tishah* — within nine months — since during that period we are uncertain whether the co-wife is pregnant with a viable child. *Ela tachlotz betoch tishah v'tinasei l'achar tishah* — but why not perform *chalitzah* right away and then remarry only after nine months have passed? At that point, either the *chalitzah* was valid because the co-wife was not pregnant, or if she was pregnant the child would by then have been born and the co-wife released in any event. This combined approach should satisfy all possibilities. Since the *mishnah* does not offer this option, it appears to prove like Reish Lakish — that *chalitzah* performed while the co-wife is pregnant is not valid, and therefore performing *chalitzah* now would be pointless if the co-wife turns out to be pregnant with a viable child.

The Gemara, however, questions whether this is a strong proof. Even if the *chalitzah* is valid, one might still object. The Gemara responds: *dilma ika inish d'havi b'chalitzah v'lo havi b'achraza* — the concern is that someone present at the *chalitzah* might later not hear the retraction. As stated by Abaye bar Avin and Rav Chanana bar Abaye: the reason we avoid performing *chalitzah* prematurely is *shema yehei velad ben kayama v'nimtza ata matricha kruz li-khehunah* — lest the child prove viable, in which case the *chalitzah* was meaningless, yet we would have to make a public announcement retracting it. The danger is that someone who was present at the *chalitzah* but missed the retraction might later see this woman marrying a *kohen* and conclude that a *chalutza* may marry a *kohen* — a grave error. To avoid this confusion, the Rabbis prefer not to perform *chalitzah* at all while there is uncertainty about the co-wife's pregnancy.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, בַּר מִינַּהּ דְּהַהִיא. דְּאַבָּיֵי בַּר אַבָּא וְרַב חִינָּנָא בַּר אַבָּיֵי דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שֶׁמָּא יְהֵא וָלָד בֶּן קַיָּימָא, וְנִמְצָא אַתָּה מַצְרִיכָהּ כָּרוֹז לַכְּהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** the discussion of this topic should be held **apart from that** mishna, as the true reason for that mishna's ruling is **as** it is **Abaye bar Abba and Rav Ḥinnana bar Abaye who both say** that she is prohibited from performing *ḥalitza* while one of the wives of the deceased is pregnant because **perhaps** the **offspring will be viable,** in which case any *ḥalitza* performed would be entirely unnecessary and therefore meaningless, and she would remain permitted to marry into the priesthood, as the opening mishna of the chapter rules. However, in this situation people might not realize that the *ḥalitza* she performed was meaningless, and they would think she is a *ḥalutza*, who is prohibited from marrying a priest. **And it would** therefore **emerge** that if she were allowed to perform *ḥalitza* while pregnant, it is possible that **you will** ultimately **require a public announcement** to be made **for her** to attest to the fact that she is in fact still permitted **to** marry into the **priesthood.**

### Abaye's Reading

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִידֵּי ״לֹא תַּחְלוֹץ וְלֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם״ קָתָנֵי? ״לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא וְלֹא תִּתְיַיבֵּם״ קָתָנֵי, בְּלֹא חֲלִיצָה, אֲבָל אִי חָלֵיץ לַהּ — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּשַׁרְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye suggests another rejection of Rabbi Elazar's proof from that mishna: **Abaye said to** Rabbi Elazar: The very formulation of the mishna refutes Reish Lakish's opinion, as **does** the mishna **teach** that **she may neither perform *ḥalitza* nor enter into levirate marriage?** No, the mishna **teaches** only that **she may neither be married nor enter into levirate marriage,** which implies only that she may not marry **without** first performing ***ḥalitza*, but if** the *yavam* **performs *ḥalitza* with her, she would indeed be permitted** to marry after nine months have passed since her husband's death. This understanding of the mishna undermines the basis of Rabbi Elazar's proof from the mishna.

Abaye intervenes with a simpler resolution. He points out that the *mishnah* never in fact said she cannot perform *chalitzah*. *Lo tinasei v'lo tityabem tani* — the *mishnah* said only that she may not remarry and may not perform *yibum*, but it did not say she may not obtain *chalitzah*. Therefore the *mishnah* carries no implication about the validity of *chalitzah* during the co-wife's pregnancy, and thus provides no support for Reish Lakish. *Aval ee chalitz lah hachi nami desharya* — if she does obtain *chalitzah* and then waits nine months to remarry, that is perfectly permissible. The *mishnah* simply rules out *yibum* (due to the risk of *eishet ach*) and remarrying without any prior *chalitzah* — not *chalitzah* itself.

The result of Abaye's reading is that the *mishnah* offers no proof for Reish Lakish, and we are returned to the unresolved debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish.

## Rava's Ruling

### Baraita Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: הַחוֹלֵץ לִמְעוּבֶּרֶת וְהִפִּילָה — צְרִיכָה חֲלִיצָה מִן הָאַחִין.
>
> **Translation:** Even if that mishna does not support Reish Lakish's opinion, nevertheless **it is taught** in a *baraita* **in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish:** In the case of **one who performs *ḥalitza* with a pregnant woman and she miscarries, she requires** another ***ḥalitza* with the brothers** in order to release her from the levirate bond. The *baraita* assumes that the original *ḥalitza* is ineffective because it was done while she was still pregnant, which is in accordance with the opinion of Reish Lakish.

At last, a *baraita* is cited that supports Reish Lakish explicitly. *Ha-choletz l'me'uberet v'hipila* — one who performed *chalitzah* with a pregnant woman, and she subsequently miscarried: *tzricha chalitzah min ha-achim* — she still requires *chalitzah* from the brothers. The *chalitzah* performed during the pregnancy was not binding, even though it turned out retroactively that it would have been necessary.

### Rava Paskins

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הִלְכְתָא כְּווֹתֵיהּ דְּרֵישׁ לָקִישׁ בְּהָנֵי תְּלָת: חַדָּא — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: The *halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion **of Reish Lakish in these three** disputes: **One, this** dispute **that we** already **stated** with regard to the *ḥalitza* of a pregnant woman.

Rava rules: *hilchesa k'vasei d'Reish Lakish* — the *halacha* follows Reish Lakish. This is a striking ruling: the weight of the textual arguments throughout the *sugya* seemed to lean toward Rabbi Yochanan, yet in the end we have a *baraita* explicitly teaching Reish Lakish's position, and Rava rules accordingly. *Chalitzah* performed while the woman is pregnant is not binding.

### Parallel Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִידַּךְ, דִּתְנַן: הַמְחַלֵּק נְכָסָיו עַל פִּיו, רִיבָּה לְאֶחָד וּמִיעֵט לְאֶחָד, וְהִשְׁוָה לָהֶם אֶת הַבְּכוֹר — דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. וְאִם אָמַר מִשּׁוּם יְרוּשָּׁה — לֹא אָמַר כְּלוּם. כָּתַב, בֵּין בַּתְּחִלָּה בֵּין בַּסּוֹף בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע, מִשּׁוּם מַתָּנָה — דְּבָרָיו קַיָּימִין. וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ — לְעוֹלָם לֹא קָנָה עַד שֶׁיֹּאמַר ״פְּלוֹנִי וּפְלוֹנִי יִרְשׁוּ שָׂדֶה פְּלוֹנִית וּפְלוֹנִית שֶׁנְּתַתִּים לָהֶם בְּמַתָּנָה וְיִרָשׁוּם״.
>
> **Translation:** **The other** dispute concerns that **which we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **one who verbally divides up his possessions** among his descendants, stating how he wishes his estate to be divided after his death, if **he increases** the proportion of his estate that should go **to one** of his children **or decreases** the proportion of his estate that should go **to** another **one** of his children, **or** if **he equally** distributes **between them** the double portion of **the firstborn,** then **his words are binding.** **But if he said** explicitly that the receipt of those portions should be considered **as an inheritance,** then it as though the verbal division of his property that **he said is nothing,** i.e., it is non-binding, since his words directly contradict the *halakhot* of inheritance as they are written in the Torah. However, if he wrote a will and somewhere therein **he wrote, whether at the beginning, whether at the end, or whether in the middle,** that the receipt of the portions should be considered **as a gift,** as opposed to an inheritance, then **his words are binding.** **And Reish Lakish said:** The inheritors **will only ever acquire** the portions as defined by the owner of the possessions **once he says: So-and-so and so-and-so shall inherit such and such a field and such and such** a field **that I have given to them as a gift, and they shall inherit them,** i.e., he must state explicitly for each recipient that their receipt of the portions should be considered a gift.

This ruling creates a clean and elegant symmetry. While the woman is pregnant, nothing binding or effective can occur — regardless of which way the pregnancy resolves. If the child will prove viable, then *chalitzah* and *yibum* are simply inapplicable, and moreover the co-wives are not released until the moment of birth. If the child will prove non-viable, then by Reish Lakish's ruling *chalitzah* or *yibum* during the pregnancy still do not take effect. In both scenarios, while she is pregnant, everything is in abeyance. Nothing binding happens until after the birth.

The Gemara then notes that this is one of three cases — *b'hani telat* — in which we rule like Reish Lakish against Rabbi Yochanan, notwithstanding the general principle that the *halacha* follows Rabbi Yochanan in his disputes with Reish Lakish.

The second case involves a deathbed bequest. The *mishnah* rules that a dying man who distributes his property, enlarging one heir's share and diminishing another's, or equalizing the firstborn's portion with those of other sons, his words stand — provided the language of gift (*matana*) appears somewhere in the document, whether at the beginning, middle, or end. The dispute between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish arises when a father uses the language of inheritance (*yerusha*) for one son and the language of gift (*matana*) for another. Rabbi Yochanan holds that the presence of gift language anywhere in the transaction validates the entire bequest, even the portion stated in the language of inheritance. Reish Lakish holds that it will not work unless the gift and inheritance language are bundled together — *ploni u-ploni yirshu sadeh ploni shenatatim lahem b'matana li-yerusha* — naming both heirs jointly. If he separated them, saying let Reuven inherit and let Shimon receive a gift, then Shimon's share is valid as a gift but Reuven's is not, since his portion was stated exclusively in the language of *yerusha*, which cannot override the Torah's inheritance law. We rule like Reish Lakish.

The third case is the major debate between Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish that runs throughout *Shas*: *ha-kotev kol nechasav livno le-achar moto* — a father writes that he is giving his son a field "from now, effective after my death." The rabbis interpret this as: title (*kinyan ha-guf*) transfers to the son immediately, while the right to usufruct (*kinyan peirot*) — the ongoing benefit and produce of the field — remains with the father until death. If the son sells his interest in the field during the father's lifetime and the son subsequently predeceases the father, the question is whether the purchaser acquires anything.

Rabbi Yochanan holds *kinyan peirot ke-kinyan ha-guf dami* — the father's right to the produce is tantamount to full title, and therefore the son's sale of the bare legal title during the father's lifetime, when the father's *kinyan peirot* gave him effective ownership, was meaningless. Since the son is no longer alive when the father dies, the sale yields nothing to the purchaser. Reish Lakish holds *kinyan peirot lav ke-kinyan ha-guf dami* — the right to usufruct does not constitute title, the son was genuinely the title-holder and could validly sell his title even while the father retained *kinyan peirot*, and therefore the purchaser acquires the field upon the father's death. We rule like Reish Lakish.

## Pregnant/Nursing Wife

### Rabbi Eliezer Strict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין הַוָּלָד שֶׁל קַיָּימָא כּוּ׳. תָּנָא: מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמְרוּ — יוֹצִיא בְּגֵט.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states that if a *yavam* consummates a levirate marriage with his *yevama* while she is pregnant, if it emerges that **the offspring is not viable** then he may maintain her as his wife because his intercourse with her was a valid consummation of levirate marriage. An opposing opinion **is taught** in a *baraita*: **In the name of Rabbi Eliezer they said: He must send her out with a bill of divorce.** Although it emerged that the levirate marriage took effect, since at the time he consummated the levirate marriage it was prohibited to do so because the *yevama* was pregnant, he is therefore penalized and required to separate from her.

The Gemara turns to the second section of the *mishnah*: *ein ha-vlad shelo kayama* — if the child proved non-viable and the *yibum* was therefore retrospectively valid, the man may remain married. A *baraita* then adds: *tana mishum Rabbi Eliezer amru, yotzi b'get* — they said in the name of Rabbi Eliezer that he must nonetheless divorce her with a *get*. Even though retroactively the *yibum* was entirely legitimate, the fact that he had relations with his *yevama* while she was pregnant — when in all likelihood it would have constituted a violation of *eishet ach*, a prohibition subject to *kares* — cannot be tolerated even *bedi'eved*. We demand a divorce regardless of how it resolved.

### Rava & R. Meir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. רַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּתַנְיָא: לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ, וּמֵינֶקֶת חֲבֵירוֹ. וְאִם נָשָׂא — יוֹצִיא וְלֹא יַחְזִיר עוֹלָמִית, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יוֹצִיא, וְלִכְשֶׁיַּגִּיעַ זְמַנּוֹ לִכְנוֹס — יִכְנוֹס.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer said the same thing,** i.e., they both expressed the same opinion that if one marries a woman whom he is prohibited from marrying, he is penalized and required to divorce her, even if the reason for the prohibition no longer applies. **Rabbi Eliezer's** opinion was expressed in **this** ruling **we have** just **stated.** Where was **Rabbi Meir's** opinion expressed? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A man may not marry a woman who is pregnant** with the child **of another** man, **nor a woman who is nursing** the child **of another** man. **And if he** transgressed and **married** her, he is penalized for violating the prohibition, and **he must divorce** her with a bill of divorce, **and he may never take** her **back;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: He must send** her **out, and when the time comes** in which it is permitted **to marry** her, i.e., after the child is weaned, **he may** then **marry** her again.

Rava observes: *Rabbi Meir v'Rabbi Eliezer amru davar echad* — Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Eliezer share a common principle, namely that when someone enters a forbidden or problematic union, even if it resolves in a technically permissible direction, we demand that he divorce and separate, and we do not passively accept the fait accompli.

### Nursing Woman Case

The parallel from Rabbi Meir is sourced from a *baraita*: *lo yisa adam uberet chaveiro u-meineket chaveiro* — a man may not marry a woman who is pregnant or nursing from a previous marriage, whether the previous husband has died or divorced her. The concern — developed elsewhere in the Gemara — is that a new pregnancy while nursing poses a danger to the nursing infant. *V'im nasa yotzi v'lo yachazir olamit divrei Rabbi Meir* — if he went ahead and married her, he must divorce her and may never remarry her. Rabbi Meir is extraordinarily strict: even though the prohibition is violated, he will not permit the marriage to stand even after the problematic period has passed. The *Chachamim* are more lenient: *yotzi*, he must separate, but *u-che-sheyagia zmano lichnos, yichnos* — once the nursing period ends and the concern is resolved, he may remarry her.

### Abaye Distinguishes

Abaye challenges Rava's equation of Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Meir: *dilma lo hi* — perhaps the cases are not parallel and the two *amora'im* do not in fact share the same principle.

One could argue in either direction. Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer is strict here specifically because the act in question — having relations with a pregnant *yevama* — risked a *d'Oraita* prohibition of *eishet ach* with its attendant *karet*. We will not tolerate even a retroactively valid *yibum* that was attended by such risk. But Rabbi Meir's case involves only a *d'rabbanan* prohibition — marrying a nursing woman — and perhaps Rabbi Eliezer would be lenient there and agree with the *Chachamim*.

Conversely, perhaps Rabbi Meir's stringency is confined to the *d'rabbanan* case, where the Rabbis applied *chizuk* — extra reinforcement of their own enactments — beyond even what would apply to a *d'Oraita* prohibition, since people tend to treat rabbinic rules lightly. But in the *d'Oraita* case of Rabbi Eliezer, where the prohibition is *eishet ach*, people naturally keep far away from it, and Rabbi Meir might well be lenient *bedi'eved* were it all resolved. The two cases thus cannot be assumed to express a single shared position.

## Divorce Requirement

### Get vs. Parshan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא, וּלְדִבְרֵי חֲכָמִים: יוֹצִיאָהּ בְּגֵט. אָמַר מָר זוּטְרָא: דַּיְקָא נָמֵי, דְּקָתָנֵי: ״יוֹצִיא״, וְלָא קָתָנֵי: ״יַפְרִישׁ״. שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: According to the statement of the Rabbis** who dispute Rabbi Meir and require one who married a pregnant woman to send her out, the intention is that **he must send her out with a bill of divorce** and not merely separate from her. **Mar Zutra said:** The language the Rabbis used **is also precise, as** they **teach: He must send** her **out, and** they **do not teach: He must separate** himself from her. **Conclude from here** that Rava's claim is correct.

The Gemara addresses the precise meaning of *yotzi* in the *Chachamim*'s ruling regarding one who marries a pregnant or nursing woman. Rava clarifies: *ul'divrei Chachamim yotzi b'get* — the *Chachamim* require an actual *get*, a formal bill of divorce, not merely physical separation (*perisha*). This is not a trivial distinction: a *get* renders the woman a *gerusha*, with all consequent legal ramifications — most significantly, that if her husband is a *kohen*, he cannot divorce her with a *get* and subsequently remarry her.

### Mar Zutra's Analysis

Mar Zutra adds that this reading is supported by a close examination of the *mishnah*'s own language: *dika nami d'katani yotzi* — the *mishnah* uses the word *yotzi* (literally: he shall send her out / divorce her), *v'lo katani yafrish* — and not the word *yafrish* (he shall separate from her). *Yotzi* is the standard technical term for divorce, not mere separation. *Shema minah* — this close reading of the text confirms the ruling.

## Kohen Marriage Issue

### Kohen Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אָשֵׁי לְרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: הָתָם תְּנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁשָּׁהָא בְּאָדָם שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם — אֵינוֹ נֵפֶל. הָא לָא שְׁהָא — סְפֵיקָא הָוֵי. וְאִיתְּמַר: מֵת בְּתוֹךְ שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם, וְעָמְדָה וְנִתְקַדְּשָׁה, רָבִינָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: אִם אֵשֶׁת יִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — חוֹלֶצֶת, וְאִם אֵשֶׁת כֹּהֵן הִיא — אֵינָהּ חוֹלֶצֶת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said to Rav Hoshaya, son of Rav Idi: We learned** in a *baraita* **there** that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Any human** baby **that survives for thirty days** after its birth **is not** to be considered **a stillbirth.** Rather, the baby is considered to be viable, and so the wife of the baby's father is never subject to any obligation of levirate marriage. **But,** by inference, **were it not to survive** for thirty days, **there would be uncertainty** whether the baby was viable or not. **And** an amoraic dispute **was stated** in the case in which the only offspring of a man **died during** the first **thirty days** of its life, **and** the widow, under the misconception that she was exempt from any obligation of levirate marriage, **arose and was betrothed.** **Ravina said in the name of Rava: If she** became the **wife of an Israelite,** i.e., the man who betrothed her was not a priest, then **she performs *ḥalitza*** with the *yavam* due to the uncertainty whether or not the offspring was viable, and then they may remain married. **But if she** became the **wife of a priest, she does not perform *ḥalitza*** with him because if she were to do so, she would become a *ḥalutza* and would therefore be prohibited from remaining married to her husband, who is a priest. Therefore, in this case, in order to allow her to remain married to her husband, the Sages did not require her to be concerned for the possibility that the offspring was not viable.

Rav Ashi poses the question to Rav Hoshaya son of Rav Idi: demanding a *get* is well and good when the husband is an ordinary *Yisrael*, who may always remarry the same woman. But would one actually demand a *get* when the husband is a *kohen*, thereby permanently severing the marriage? A *kohen* may not remarry a *gerusha*, and his wife — once divorced — acquires the disqualification of *gerusha* which follows her even with respect to other *kohanim*. The demand for a *get* thus carries far graver consequences for a *kohen* than for a *Yisrael*.

As a prelude to this question, the Gemara cites a *baraita* from Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel: *kol she-shaha b'adam shloshim yom, einu nefel* — if a newborn survives thirty days, he is no longer considered a *nefel* (non-viable infant). Rashi qualifies: this thirty-day rule applies specifically when it is uncertain whether the pregnancy was full-term. If it is known to be full-term, then *tinok ben yomo poter min ha-yibum* — a one-day-old infant, as soon as he is born, releases his mother from the obligation of *yibum*, as taught in Tractate Niddah.

### Safek Chalitza

The Gemara then develops the following scenario: a child was born, it was uncertain whether the pregnancy was full-term, and thus uncertain whether the child was viable. The child died within thirty days — without resolving the uncertainty — and the mother, assuming she was free because she had a child, went ahead and remarried. But in fact her status is uncertain: if the child was not viable, she was never released from *yibbum* and should have obtained *chalitzah* from the brothers-in-law first. She is thus a *safek* in need of *chalitzah*.

Ravina in the name of Rava rules: *im eshet Yisrael hi, choletzet* — if she is now married to a *Yisrael*, she must obtain *chalitzah* now to resolve the doubt. *V'im eshet kohen hi, eina choletzet* — but if she is married to a *kohen*, we do not demand that she obtain *chalitzah*, because doing so would make her a *chalutza* — or at least a *safek chalutza* — and render her permanently forbidden to her husband.

### Demand Chalitza?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא אָמַר: אַחַת זוֹ וְאַחַת זוֹ — חוֹלֶצֶת. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב מְשַׁרְשְׁיָא: בְּאוּרְתָּא אֲמַר רָבָא הָכִי, וּבְצַפְרָא הֲדַר בֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: שְׁרִיתוּהָ? יְהֵא רַעֲוָא דְּתִשְׁתְּרוֹ אַף תַּרְבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a different version of Rav's opinion: **Rav Mesharshiyya said in the name of Rava: Both this** woman **and that** woman **perform *ḥalitza*,** even though by doing so, if she was betrothed to a priest, she would become forbidden to him. **Ravina said to Rav Mesharshiyya: In the evening Rava stated this** ruling in accordance with the way you cited him, **but in the morning he retracted** his opinion and ruled as I cited him. Rav Mesharshiyya **said to him: Do you** really **permit** her to marry without *ḥalitza*? He added sarcastically: **May it be** God's **will that you will even permit** forbidden **fats** as well. In Rav Mesharshiyya's opinion, the prohibition against the widow remarrying without *ḥalitza* was as obvious as the prohibition of forbidden fats.

Rav Mesharshiya, also in the name of Rava, disagrees: *achat zo v'achat zo choletzet* — in both cases she must obtain *chalitzah*, even if this means she must separate from her *kohen* husband.

Ravina then informs Rav Mesharshiya that he may have heard Rava say this at night, but *u-vetzafra hadar bei* — the next morning Rava reversed himself. Rav Mesharshiya responds with sharp skepticism — *sharyuta? yehei ra'ava d'tishrei afilu tarba* — you've found a leniency? Let it be God's will that you can permit even forbidden fat — expressing his refusal to credit the claimed retraction. He heard Rava's ruling from Rava himself, and will not accept a secondhand report of a reversal.

### Unresolved Kohen Q

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא גַּבֵּי מְעוּבֶּרֶת חֲבֵירוֹ וּמֵינֶקֶת חֲבֵירוֹ הַנְּשׂוּאָה לְכֹהֵן, מַאי? מִי עֲבוּד רַבָּנַן תַּקַּנְתָּא לְכֹהֵן, אוֹ לָא.
>
> **Translation:** After citing this dispute, Rav Ashi said to Rav Hoshaya, son of Rav Idi: According to Rava's citation of Rav, when a woman is married to a priest, the Sages were more lenient in order to allow the couple to remain married. **Here, with regard to a woman who is pregnant** with the child **of another** man **or a woman who is nursing** the child **of another** man, **who is married to a priest, what** is the *halakha*? **Did the Sages enact an ordinance for** the benefit of **a priest** and say that it is sufficient for him to separate from his wife and he does not need to divorce her, **or not?**

This debate sets the stage for the governing question with which the daf concludes: *hacha gabi me'uberet chaveiro u-meineket chaveiro ha-nesu'ah l'kohen, mah — mi avadu rabbanan takanta l'kohen o lo?* In our case, where a man married a pregnant or nursing woman from a previous marriage and the Rabbis require him to issue a *get* — did the Rabbis create an exemption (*takana*) for a *kohen*, allowing him to remain married rather than be required to give a *get* that would forever prohibit remarriage? Or does the requirement of *get* apply equally to a *kohen*, at whatever cost? This question remains unresolved, and the Gemara will return to it.
