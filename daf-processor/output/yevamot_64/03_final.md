# Yevamot 64 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ten-Year Rule & Divorce

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְשָׁהָה עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים וְלֹא יָלְדָה — אֵינוֹ רַשַּׁאי לְבַטֵּל. גֵּירְשָׁהּ — מוּתֶּרֶת לִינָּשֵׂא לְאַחֵר. וְרַשַּׁאי הַשֵּׁנִי לִשְׁהוֹת עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים. וְאִם הִפִּילָה — מוֹנֶה מִשָּׁעָה שֶׁהִפִּילָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a man **married a woman and stayed with her** for **ten years and she did not give birth, he is no** longer **permitted to neglect** the mitzva to be fruitful and multiply. Consequently, he must either divorce her and marry someone else, or take another wife while still married to her. If **he divorced her she is permitted to marry another** man, as it is not necessarily on her account that she and her first husband did not have children, **and the second** husband **is permitted to stay with her** for **ten years. And if she had a miscarriage, he counts** the ten years **from the time of the miscarriage.**

The daf opens with a *mishnah* addressing a man who has been married for ten years without his wife giving birth: *nasa isha ve'shaha imah eser shanim velo yalda* — he married a woman and dwelt with her ten years and she did not give birth. The immediate question the *mishnah* raises, though without resolving it explicitly, is whether the absence of children is attributable to her or to him. What is clear from the outset is that the *mishnah* is building on the language of the previous *mishnah*, which discussed when a man may cease pursuing the obligation of *pru u'rvu* once he has a son and a daughter. Here the *mishnah* rules: *eino rashai levatel* — he cannot simply nullify his obligation. Even after ten childless years, he cannot declare the matter closed and allow the situation to remain as it is. He must do something.

### Cannot Nullify Obligation

What, precisely, must he do? The *mishnah* contemplates two possibilities: he may marry a second wife while remaining married to the first — for in a society where multiple wives were permitted, this was legally available — or he may divorce her. The *mishnah* proceeds to note that if he did divorce her, *muteret le'hinasay le'acher* — she is permitted to marry another man. The implication is significant: the other man need not have yet fulfilled *pru u'rvu* himself, because we do not presume that the inability to have children was rooted in her. She may therefore marry another man who hopes to fulfill the *mitzvah* through this marriage. And that second husband, the *mishnah* adds, is similarly entitled to a waiting period: *ve'rashai ha'sheni lishhos imah eser shanim* — the second husband may remain with her for up to ten years before he too must act. The language *rashai* again implies that after ten years the man has no further discretion; he will be required either to divorce her or to take an additional wife.

One further provision: if she miscarried, the count of ten years restarts from the moment of the miscarriage, *moneh mi'sha'ah she'hipila*. A miscarriage is not treated as confirmation of infertility — quite the contrary, it is taken as evidence of potential fertility, of an ability to conceive even if not to carry to term, and so the clock begins anew from that point.

### Divorce & Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: נָשָׂא אִשָּׁה וְשָׁהָה עִמָּהּ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים וְלֹא יָלְדָה — יוֹצִיא וְיִתֵּן כְּתוּבָה, שֶׁמָּא לֹא זָכָה לְהִבָּנוֹת מִמֶּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** If a man **married a woman and stayed with her** for **ten years and she did not give birth, he should divorce** her **and pay** her **marriage contract,** because **perhaps he did not merit to be built,** i.e., to have children, **from her.** It is not certain that their failure to have children is due to her, as it is possible that they are not a suitable match for bearing children.

A *baraita* cited immediately afterward makes the *mishnah*'s ruling more explicit: *nasa isha ve'shaha imah eser shanim velo yalda yotzi ve'yitein kesubah* — he must divorce her and pay her *kesubah*. The requirement to pay the *kesubah* carries its own legal weight. This is a society in which divorce has a fault structure: whether the woman receives her *kesubah* depends in part on whether the fault for the dissolution lies with her or not. By requiring payment of the *kesubah*, the *baraita* is declaring that we do not presume the infertility to be her fault. As the *gemara* phrases it: *shema lo zachah le'hibanot mimenah* — perhaps it is he who has not merited to have children from her. The fault, if any, is assigned to him, and she collects her *kesubah*.

This leads directly to the question of whether the language *yotzi* — he must divorce her — implies actual compulsion. The *Tosafot* addresses this immediately, noting that at the end of tractate *Hamadir* there is a dispute among *amoraim* about whether a husband in this situation is actually forced to divorce or not, and concludes that even verbal compulsion may not apply. The word *yotzi* should be understood as expressing what he ought to do, not necessarily what *beit din* physically compels. This ties into a ruling of the *Rema* in *Shulchan Aruch* that in contemporary circumstances we do not in general compel divorce in cases of this nature, particularly given that Jewish communities no longer wield the autonomous coercive power they once did.

### Abraham & Ten Years

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לַדָּבָר, זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר: ״מִקֵּץ עֶשֶׂר שָׁנִים לְשֶׁבֶת אַבְרָם בְּאֶרֶץ כְּנָעַן״ — לְלַמֶּדְךָ שֶׁאֵין יְשִׁיבַת חוּץ לָאָרֶץ עוֹלֶה לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן. לְפִיכָךְ, חָלָה הוּא אוֹ שֶׁחָלְתָה הִיא, אוֹ שְׁנֵיהֶם חֲבוּשִׁים בְּבֵית הָאֲסוּרִים — אֵין עוֹלִין לוֹ מִן הַמִּנְיָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Although there is no** explicit **proof for the matter** that one must take another wife if he has not had children after ten years of marriage, there is **an allusion to the matter,** as the verse states: "And Sarai, Abram's wife, took Hagar…**after Abram had dwelled ten years in the land of Canaan,** and gave her to Abram her husband to be his wife" (Genesis 16:3). Incidentally, this verse also comes **to teach you that** the years spent **dwelling outside of Eretz Yisrael do not count** as part of **his tally. Consequently,** if **he was sick** during this period **or she was sick, or** if one of **the two of them** was **imprisoned in jail,** it **does not count** as part of **his tally.**

The *baraita* derives the ten-year principle from the episode of *Avraham* and *Hagar*: *mi'ketz eser shanim le'sheves Avram be'eretz Kena'an* — after ten years of Avram's dwelling in the land of Canaan, Sarai gave him Hagar. The plain reading is that ten years had passed and something had to be done. This is taken as the textual basis — *zecher la'davar* rather than a full *ra'aya* — for the ten-year rule of our *mishnah*.

### Exclusions from Count

A further inference is drawn from this source: since the *pasuk* specifies *Avram's dwelling in the land of Canaan*, time spent outside *Eretz Yisrael* does not count toward the ten years. *Rashi* explains that dwelling in *chutz la'aretz* itself may have been a cause preventing them from having children, and so years spent there cannot be used to measure a couple's fertility. The *gemara* generalizes this principle: *lefikach chalah hu o she'chalsa hi* — therefore, if he was ill during some period, or she was ill, or if both were imprisoned — *chavushim be'veis ha'asurim* — those periods do not count toward the ten years, since the absence of children can be attributed to an independent cause. This principle of excluding periods where external factors may explain the childlessness considerably narrows the practical application of the rule. One might extend the principle to other circumstances, such as periods of significant stress, though the *gemara* specifies only illness and imprisonment explicitly.

## Patriarchs' Sterility

### Isaac vs Abraham

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא לְרַב נַחְמָן: וְלֵילַף מִיִּצְחָק, דִּכְתִיב: ״וַיְהִי יִצְחָק בֶּן אַרְבָּעִים שָׁנָה בְּקַחְתּוֹ אֶת רִבְקָה וְגוֹ׳״, וּכְתִיב: ״וְיִצְחָק בֶּן שִׁשִּׁים שָׁנָה בְּלֶדֶת אוֹתָם״! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יִצְחָק עָקוּר הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to Rav Naḥman: Let us derive from Isaac** that one may wait a longer period of time, **as it is written: "And Isaac was forty years old when he took Rebekah**…to be his wife" (Genesis 25:20), **and it is written** with regard to the birth of Jacob and Esau: **"And Isaac was sixty years old when she bore them"** (Genesis 25:26). This indicates that one may wait twenty years. Rav Naḥman **said to him: Isaac** knew that he **was infertile,** and therefore there was no reason for him to marry another woman, as Rebekah was not the cause of their infertility.

The *gemara* raises a pointed question: why derive the ten-year rule specifically from *Avraham*? Could we not equally derive it from *Yitzchak*? As the *pasuk* records, *Yitzchak* was forty years old when he married *Rivka*, and sixty years old when she gave birth — a gap of twenty years. Since he appears to have prayed for children only shortly before the birth, it seems he waited twenty years before taking any action. Why not learn from *Yitzchak* that twenty years is the relevant threshold?

*Tosafot* notes that according to the *midrash*, *Rivka* was only three years old at the time of the marriage, which would mean that *Yitzchak* naturally had to wait until she was of childbearing age — perhaps around twelve — and from that point the twenty years reduce to something closer to eight or nine, which fits comfortably within the ten-year framework. However, the *gemara* itself does not rely on that *midrash*.

Instead, the *gemara* offers a different answer: *Yitzchak akar hayah* — *Yitzchak* himself was sterile. Since the problem was with him, there was no remedial action he could take — he could not simply marry another woman and hope for children from her. His situation was categorically different from the *mishnah*'s case, in which we are uncertain whose fertility is compromised. This raises an important question that the *gemara* will address later: if a husband is himself the *akar*, does that relieve him of all obligation? The answer will involve the question of whether women are themselves obligated in *pru u'rvu* and whether a wife has an independent right to demand children.

### Abraham's Sterility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אַבְרָהָם נָמֵי עָקוּר הָיָה! הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְכִדְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לָמָּה נִמְנוּ שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל יִשְׁמָעֵאל — כְּדֵי לְיַיחֵס בָּהֶן שְׁנוֹתָיו שֶׁל יַעֲקֹב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **If so, Abraham** also should not have married another woman, as he **was also infertile.** Rather, the *tanna* **requires that** verse that states when Jacob and Esau were born **for** that which **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba** taught. This is **because Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Why were Ishmael's years counted** in the Torah, as they do not appear to be relevant to its narrative? **In order to determine through them the years of Jacob,** i.e., Jacob's age at the time that various events took place, as explained in tractate *Megilla* (17a). The verse concerning Jacob's birth was not meant to allude to a *halakha* about remaining married before having children, but to make it possible to determine Jacob's age by relating it to the age of Ishmael.

But the *gemara* continues: if that objection applies to *Yitzchak*, does it not equally apply to *Avraham*? *Avraham nami akar hayah* — *Avraham* too was sterile, as the *gemara* will demonstrate. Why then can we learn the ten-year principle from *Avraham*? The answer is that the twenty years associated with *Yitzchak* is mentioned in the *Torah* not to teach a halachic principle at all, but to provide chronological data needed for an entirely different purpose. *Chizkiyah bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan* taught: why does the *Torah* enumerate *Yishmael*'s years? In order to establish the chronology of *Yaakov*'s life — to calculate how old *Yaakov* was when he fled from home and when he returned, and to track the broader family history. The *psukim* that list *Yitzchak*'s age at marriage and at the birth of his children exist to enable those calculations, not to teach anything about a waiting period. By *Avraham*, however, there is no parallel chronological need; the only reason to highlight that *Hagar* was introduced specifically after ten years of dwelling in *Eretz Yisrael* is to teach the halachic principle itself.

### Isaac's Sterility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: יִצְחָק אָבִינוּ עָקוּר הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיֶּעְתַּר יִצְחָק לַה׳ לְנֹכַח אִשְׁתּוֹ״. ״עַל אִשְׁתּוֹ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״לְנוֹכַח״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶם עֲקוּרִים הָיוּ. אִי הָכִי: ״וַיֵּעָתֶר לוֹ״ — ״וַיֵּעָתֵר לָהֶם״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ! לְפִי שֶׁאֵינוֹ דּוֹמֶה תְּפִלַּת צַדִּיק בֶּן צַדִּיק לִתְפִלַּת צַדִּיק בֶּן רָשָׁע.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Isaac our father was infertile, as it is stated: "And Isaac entreated the Lord concerning [*lenokhaḥ*] his wife** because she was barren" (Genesis 25:21). **It is not stated** that he entreated the Lord **for [*al*] his wife, but *lenokhaḥ*,** which can mean opposite, against, or corresponding to; this **teaches that they were both infertile.** The Gemara asks: **If so,** why does the verse continue: **"And** the Lord **let Himself be entreated of him"?** The verse **should say: And** the Lord **let Himself be entreated of them.** The Gemara answers that their prayers were answered due to Isaac, **because the prayer of a righteous individual** who is **the son of a righteous individual is not similar to the prayer of a righteous individual** who is **the son of a wicked individual,** and Rebekah's father was the wicked Bethuel.

The *gemara* establishes *Yitzchak*'s sterility from the language of the *pasuk*: *vaye'etar Yitzchak l'Hashem lenochach ishto* — *Yitzchak* importuned God *lenochach* his wife. Why does the *pasuk* say *lenochach* rather than simply *al ishto*, for his wife? The *Maharsha* explains this beautifully. The word *lenochach* implies a parallel or facing position — suggesting that both *Yitzchak* and *Rivka* were sterile, *melamed she'shneihem akurim hayu*.

The *gemara* then asks: if both were sterile, why does it say *vaye'etar lo Hashem* — God responded to him — rather than *vaye'etar lahem*, to them? If *Yitzchak* too was sterile, then *Rivka* was also praying for him, and God should have responded to both of them. The *Maharsha* resolves this with an elegant reading. The *gemara* invokes the principle that *kol hamitpallel al chavero hu ne'eneh techilah* — one who prays for another is answered first. *Yitzchak* was praying for *Rivka*, and so he would be answered. But if *Yitzchak* is also sterile, then *Rivka* must also be praying for him — and then the response should address both. The fact that it says only *lo*, to him, requires explanation.

The *gemara* answers: *lefi she'eino domeh tefilat tzaddik ben tzaddik litefilat tzaddik ben rasha* — the prayer of a *tzaddik* who is the son of a *tzaddik* is not equivalent to the prayer of a *tzaddik* who is the son of a *rasha*. *Yitzchak* was the son of *Avraham*; *Rivka* was the daughter of *Betuel*. Both prayed, but it was *Yitzchak*'s prayer that was primarily answered, because of the greater potency of his lineage. This gives rise to a curious stylistic observation: the standard Aramaic idiom *eino domeh X le-Y* typically presents the second term as the more important or potent — as in the famous teaching that in the place where *ba'alei teshuvah* stand, *tzaddikim gemurim* cannot stand, where the *ba'al teshuvah* is the second term. Here, however, the more potent prayer is clearly that of the *tzaddik ben tzaddik*, who is mentioned first. This is a striking inversion of the usual rhetorical structure, and it remains difficult to resolve satisfactorily.

### Why Sterility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: מִפְּנֵי מָה הָיוּ אֲבוֹתֵינוּ עֲקוּרִים — מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִתְאַוֶּה לִתְפִלָּתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים. אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: לָמָּה נִמְשְׁלָה תְּפִלָּתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים כְּעֶתֶר — מָה עֶתֶר זֶה מְהַפֵּךְ הַתְּבוּאָה מִמָּקוֹם לְמָקוֹם, כָּךְ תְּפִלָּתָן שֶׁל צַדִּיקִים מְהַפֶּכֶת מִדּוֹתָיו שֶׁל הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא מִמִּדַּת רַגְזָנוּת לְמִדַּת רַחֲמָנוּת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: For what reason were our forefathers** initially **infertile? Because the Holy One, Blessed be He, desires the prayers of the righteous,** and He therefore wanted them to pray for children. Similarly, **Rabbi Yitzḥak said: Why are the prayers of the righteous compared to a pitchfork [*eter*],** as in the verse: "And He let Himself be entreated [*vaye'ater*]"? This indicates that **just as this pitchfork turns over produce from one place to another, so the prayer of the righteous turns over the attributes of the Holy One, Blessed be He, from** the **attribute of rage to** the **attribute of mercy.**

*Rabbi Yitzchak* asks: *mipnei mah hayu avoteinu akurim* — why were our forefathers sterile? The answer: *mipnei she'Hakadosh Baruch Hu mit'aveh l'tefilatan shel tzadikim* — because God desires the prayers of the righteous. The pattern is strikingly consistent throughout the narratives of the *avot* and *imahot*: in each generation, those whose fertility flows easily — *Nachor* with his twelve sons, *Yishmael* with his twelve princes, *Esav* with his multitude of descendants — are peripheral figures. Those at the center of the covenant struggle intensely. The *gemara* frames this as God's deliberate desire to draw forth prayer, though the theme clearly invites deeper reflection: the covenantal path is not one of effortless blessing but of hard-won closeness.

### Prayer as Pitchfork

*Rabbi Yitzchak* extends this idea with a *mashal*: *lamah nimshelah tefilatan shel tzadikim ke'atar* — why is the prayer of the righteous compared to a pitchfork? Because *Yitzchak* importuned God using the root *vaye'etar*, which shares its root with *atar*, a pitchfork. Just as a pitchfork turns grain from one place to another, so the prayers of the righteous turn God's attributes from *middat rogez* — the attribute of anger — to *middat rachamim* — the attribute of compassion. Prayer is not a plea into a fixed order; it is a force that reorients the divine stance entirely.

### Abraham & Sarah Tumtumim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַמֵּי: אַבְרָהָם וְשָׂרָה טוּמְטְמִין הָיוּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״הַבִּיטוּ אֶל צוּר חֻצַּבְתֶּם וְאֶל מַקֶּבֶת בּוֹר נֻקַּרְתֶּם״, וּכְתִיב, ״הַבִּיטוּ אֶל אַבְרָהָם אֲבִיכֶם וְאֶל שָׂרָה תְּחוֹלֶלְכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ami said: Abraham and Sarah were** originally ***tumtumin*,** people whose sexual organs are concealed and not functional, **as it is stated: "Look to the rock** **from where you were hewn, and to the hole of the pit from where you were dug"** (Isaiah 51:1), **and it is written** in the next verse: **"Look to Abraham your father and to Sarah who bore you"** (Isaiah 51:2), which indicates that sexual organs were fashioned for them, signified by the words hewn and dug, over the course of time.

The *gemara* now substantiates *Avraham*'s sterility. *Rabbi Ami* teaches: *Avraham ve'Sarah tumtumim hayu* — *Avraham* and *Sarah* were originally *tumtumim*, individuals whose genitalia were initially undifferentiated or concealed beneath a membrane, rendering their sex anatomically indeterminate at birth. Even after the condition is resolved, such individuals are presumed to face ongoing challenges with fertility. This is derived from the *psukim* in *Yeshayahu*: *habitu el tzur chutzavtem ve'el makevet bor nukartem* — look to the rock from which you were hewn and the hollow from which you were chiseled — followed immediately by *habitu el Avraham avichem ve'el Sarah techolelchem* — look to *Avraham* your father and *Sarah* who gave birth to you. The language of chiseling and hollowing is read as describing a process of physical completion, of forms not yet fully expressed requiring further formation.

### Sarah as Ayalonit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: שָׂרָה אִמֵּנוּ אַיְלוֹנִית הָיְתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַתְּהִי שָׂרַי עֲקָרָה אֵין לָהּ וָלָד״, אֲפִילּוּ בֵּית וָלָד אֵין לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said: Our mother Sarah was** initially a **sexually underdeveloped woman [*aylonit*], as it is stated: "And Sarah was barren; she had no child"** (Genesis 11:30). The superfluous words: "She had no child," indicate that **she did not have even a place,** i.e., a womb, **for a child.**

*Rava bar Avuha* adds a further dimension: *Sarah Imeinu ayalonit hayata* — our matriarch *Sarah* was an *ayalonit*, a category of woman distinguished from a simple *akara* (infertile woman) by specific physical characteristics indicating a congenitally different physical constitution. The *pasuk* states: *vatehi Sarai akara ein lah valad* — *Sarai* was barren, she had no child. The apparent redundancy — if she was barren, obviously she had no child — is read as adding something: *afilu beit valad ein lah* — she did not even have a uterus. This goes beyond ordinary infertility and places her in the category of *ayalonit*. And all of this, of course, magnifies the *nes*: that from two individuals with such profound physical challenges, a child was ultimately born.

## Generational Adjustments

### Earlier Generations

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר שִׁילַת מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּדוֹרוֹת הָרִאשׁוֹנִים, שֶׁשְּׁנוֹתֵיהֶן מְרוּבּוֹת. אֲבָל בְּדוֹרוֹת הָאַחֲרוֹנִים, שֶׁשְּׁנוֹתֵיהֶן מוּעָטוֹת — שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה, כְּנֶגֶד שְׁלֹשָׁה עִיבּוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda, son of Rav Shmuel bar Sheilat, said in the name of Rav: They taught** that he waits ten years **only** with regard to the people who lived **in former generations, whose years** were **numerous,** i.e., they lived longer. **However,** with regard to the people who live **in later generations, whose years are few,** he waits only **two and half years** before divorcing her, **corresponding to** the time period of **three pregnancies.**

Having anchored the ten-year rule in *Avraham*'s experience, the *gemara* immediately confronts an obvious tension: *Avraham* lived 175 years; the fertility window of his generation was proportionally vast. Does the ten-year standard derived from him apply without modification to generations with dramatically shorter lifespans? *Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav* rules: *lo shanu ela bedorot harishonim*, the ten-year standard was stated only for earlier generations whose years were many — *sheshnoteihem merubot*.

### Modern Timeline

For later generations, with shorter lifespans and narrower windows of fertility, the obligation comes much sooner: *shtei shanim u'mechtza* — two and a half years. The rationale is *keneged shelosha iburim* — corresponding to three pregnancy cycles. Three pregnancies of nine months each yields twenty-seven months, plus time between pregnancies, approximating thirty months or two and a half years. The underlying logic is that three cycles without a successful pregnancy is enough to trigger concern and action. The number of years is contextually determined by the lifespan and fertility window of the generation; the structure of the obligation — that after a sufficient period without children, something must be done — remains constant.

### Three-Year View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּה אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: שָׁלֹשׁ שָׁנִים, כְּנֶגֶד שָׁלֹשׁ פְּקִידוֹת. דְּאָמַר מָר: בְּרֹאשׁ הַשָּׁנָה נִפְקְדוּ שָׂרָה רָחֵל וְחַנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba said** in the name of **Rav Naḥman:** He waits **three years, corresponding to** the **three remembrances** of barren women by God, **as the Master said: On Rosh HaShana Sarah, Rachel, and Hannah were remembered,** i.e., God gave them children. Since God determines on Rosh HaShana whether barren women will conceive that year, one may remain married until three such opportunities have passed.

*Rava* in the name of *Rav Nachman* dissents: the threshold for later generations is *shalosh shanim* — three full years — *keneged shalosh pekidot*, corresponding to three divine visitations. The *gemara* invokes the teaching that *berosh Hashanah nifkedu Sarah Rachel ve'Channah* — on Rosh Hashanah, *Sarah*, *Rachel*, and *Channah* were remembered by God and their fertility was granted. The three-year figure gives a couple three Rosh Hashanahs — three full cycles of divine remembrance and potential intervention — before concluding that conception is not forthcoming.

### Ten Years Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: לֵיתַנְהוּ לְהָנֵי כְּלָלֵי. מִכְּדֵי מַתְנִיתִין מַאן תַּקֵּין — רַבִּי, וְהָא בִּימֵי דָוִד אִימַּעוּט שְׁנֵי, דִּכְתִיב: ״יְמֵי שְׁנוֹתֵינוּ בָהֶם שִׁבְעִים שָׁנָה״.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rabba** himself **said: These principles are not** accepted as *halakha*. Why not? **Now** consider, **who established** the content of the **mishna? Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. Yet, **in the days of** King **David,** many years before the time of Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, **the years** of an average lifespan **were** already **diminished, as it is written: "The days of our years are seventy years,** and if with strength eighty years" (Psalms 90:10). Consequently, if Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi included in the mishna the statement that one remains married for ten years, that must apply even nowadays.

*Rava* then dismisses both of these modifications with a decisive argument: *leis lehu lehani klalei* — neither of those principles applies. Who authored our *mishnah*? *Rabbi*. And *Rabbi* himself lived at a time when people's years were already few — as the *pasuk* says, *yemei shnoteinu bahem shiv'im shanah*, our years are seventy. Yet *Rabbi* codified ten years in the *mishnah*, not two and a half and not three. Therefore, *Rabbi*'s ruling in our *mishnah* is authoritative and stands as the standard for all subsequent generations: ten years is the threshold. The attempted revision based on generational lifespan is rejected, because *Rabbi* himself bridged the gap by writing ten years into the *mishnah* for people of shorter lifespan.

## Fault & Ketubah

### Uncertainty of Cause

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַאי ״שֶׁמָּא לֹא זָכָה לְהִבָּנוֹת הֵימֶנָּה״, וְדִלְמָא אִיהִי דְּלָא זָכְיָא? אִיהִי כֵּיוָן דְּלָא מְפַקְּדָא אַפְּרִיָּה וּרְבִיָּה, לָא מִיעַנְשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks about the language of the *baraita*. **And** what about **this** expression: **Perhaps he did not merit to be built from her; perhaps** it was **she who did not merit** to build a family. The Gemara answers: **She, since she is not commanded** to be **fruitful and multiply, is not punished.** Their worthiness therefore depends on him, not her.

The *gemara* returns to the question of why the husband must pay the *kesubah* upon divorcing his wife after ten childless years. One might argue that since at least one of them is clearly not fertile — the absence of children proves that — the woman should bear the burden of proof that it was his problem rather than hers, in accordance with the principle *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'ayah*, the one seeking to collect from another must prove the claim. Why do we default to protecting her *kesubah* without requiring her to establish that the fault lies with him?

### Woman & Pru U'Rvu

The *gemara* answers: *kivan d'lo amifkada a pru u'rvu lo m'ancha* — since she is not commanded in *pru u'rvu*, she is not punished through it. If we view the absence of children as a form of divine punishment — an inability to fulfill one's obligation — and she bears no such obligation, then we look to see who is being penalized by this absence. That must be the man, who does bear the obligation. Therefore we assign the inability to have children to him rather than to her, and she collects her *kesubah*.

This reasoning is admittedly difficult to receive simply, since women are the ones who conceive, carry, and give birth — the entire physical experience of fertility and its absence falls on them in the most immediate way. To say they are not suffering through it because it was not their *mitzvah* seems jarring. One possible reading, offered in the course of discussion, is not that we are making an objective metaphysical claim that she suffers no consequence — but that *halachically* we choose to assign the fault to him, in a way that works entirely in her favor: she can remarry, the new husband can hope to fulfill *pru u'rvu*, and she retains her *kesubah*. Whether or not that is precisely the *gemara*'s intent, it reflects a legal structure that consistently protects her interests.

### Rabbi Abba Bar Zavda

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא: נְסֵיב אִיתְּתָא וְאוֹלֵיד בְּנֵי, וַאֲמַר לְהוּ: אִי זְכַאי — הֲווֹ לִי מִקַּמַּיְיתָא. הָתָם דַּחוֹיֵי קָא מְדַחֵי לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן, דְּרַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר זַבְדָּא אִיעֲקַר מִפִּרְקֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the mishna's statement that if one did not have children after ten years he should marry a different woman. **Is that so? Didn't the Sages say to Rabbi Abba bar Zavda: Marry a woman and have children, and he said to them: If I had merited, I would** already **have** children **from** my **first** wife? This indicates that there is no obligation to remarry if one did not have children with his first wife. The Gemara answers: **There,** Rabbi Abba bar Zavda **was** merely **putting the Rabbis off** with an excuse, **as** the real reason why he would not marry was because **Rabbi Abba bar Zavda became impotent from Rav Huna's discourse.** Rav Huna's students would hold back from relieving themselves until his lengthy sermons were finished, which caused them to become sterile.

The *gemara* cites a challenge to this framework. The rabbis had said to *Rabbi Abba bar Zavda*: you have had one child, but now your wife is presumably past the point of bearing children — why not remarry and attempt to have more? He replied: *zakai havah li mikamaita* — had I merited it, I would have had children with my first wife. Since I did not, I will not have children with a second wife either.

What does this prove? If he is saying that the infertility lies with him definitively, that seems to undermine the *gemara*'s framework, which holds that perhaps a different pairing will work. The *gemara* responds: *hasam de-chovah ka medachi lehu de-rabbanan* — there he was merely deflecting the rabbis. His case was in fact unique: *Rav Abba bar Zavdi* had become sterile as a direct result of remaining in *Rav Huna*'s *shiur* without excusing himself to relieve himself, a situation that caused him physical damage. The sterility was therefore clearly traceable to him as the cause, unlike the ordinary case of a couple who have not had children and where attribution remains uncertain. In that unusual case there was no sense in remarrying; the problem was entirely and unambiguously his.

### Middle Ground Position

The *gemara*'s full framework is thus revealed as occupying a carefully constructed middle space. On one side: she is entitled to her *kesubah*, because we cannot say with certainty that it was her problem. On the other side: he is still obligated to try to have children, so he must divorce her (or marry another woman), because we cannot say with certainty that it was his problem. The two inferences run simultaneously and in tension: *lo zachah l'hibanot mimenah* — it did not work out between them — a formulation that assigns no blame to either party individually, but maintains the imperative of *pru u'rvu* on the man while protecting the woman financially.

It is a strikingly sophisticated legal position. It protects her right to remarry without the stigma of presumed infertility; it protects her financial entitlement; and simultaneously it preserves his religious obligation and requires him to continue pursuing it. The situation is not that she is at fault, and it is not that he is definitively at fault — it is that together they have not succeeded, and he must try again.

## Two vs Three Times

### Mishnah Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גֵּירְשָׁהּ מוּתֶּרֶת וְכוּ׳. שֵׁנִי — אִין, שְׁלִישִׁי — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that if a man **divorced** his wife after ten years without children, **she is permitted** to marry a second man, who may remain married to her for ten years. The Gemara comments: **A second** husband, **yes,** but **a third** one, **no.** Once she has been married to two men without children for ten years each, it is presumed that she is unable to have children.

The *mishnah* adds that while she may marry a second husband after the first marriage ends childlessly, a third man who has not yet fulfilled *pru u'rvu* should not marry her: *sheni in, shelishi lo* — the second is permitted, the third is not. After two marriages without children, we begin to presume that the infertility may lie with her. The *gemara* notes that this position belongs to *Rabbi* — *matnitin mani Rabbi hi* — and a dispute between *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* about precisely this question is embedded in the broader *sugya* to follow.

### Brit Milah Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִיתִין מַנִּי? רַבִּי הִיא. דְּתַנְיָא: מָלָה הָרִאשׁוֹן וָמֵת, שֵׁנִי וָמֵת, שְׁלִישִׁי — לֹא תָּמוּל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שְׁלִישִׁי תָּמוּל, רְבִיעִי — לֹא תָּמוּל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Who is** the *tanna* of **the mishna? It is Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, who holds that a legal presumption [*ḥazaka*] is established after two occurrences. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman **circumcised** her **first** son **and he died** as a result of the circumcision, and she circumcised her **second** son **and he** also **died, she should not circumcise** her **third** son, as the deaths of the first two produce a presumption that this woman's sons die as a result of circumcision. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: She should circumcise** her **third** son, as there is not considered to be a legal presumption that her sons die from circumcision, but **she should not circumcise** her **fourth** son if her first three sons died from circumcision.

The *baraita* introduces the dispute in the context of *brit milah*: if a family's first son died following circumcision, and the second son also died following circumcision, *Rabbi* rules that the third son should not be circumcised — *shelishi lo simol*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* rules that the third son should still be circumcised, and it is only if that child too dies that the fourth — *revi'i lo simol* — should not be. A complicating *baraita* appears to reverse the attributions, and the *gemara* cannot readily resolve which position belongs to whom from the text alone.

It is worth noting at the outset why this is not simply a debate about what constitutes a *chazakah*. A *chazakah* means something is definitively established as a pattern. Here the question is more nuanced: at what point must one act out of concern — not certainty — that a pattern exists? After one death from circumcision, no one is suggesting we abandon the *mitzvah* entirely, even though the evidence of danger is already present. The question is when the threshold of concern becomes actionable.

### Four Sisters Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא אִי אָתְיָא שְׁלִישִׁית נָמֵי הֲוָה אָמַר לָהּ? אִם כֵּן, מַאי אַסְהָדוּתֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא? וְדִלְמָא הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן דַּאֲחָיוֹת מְחַזְּקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this proof: **Perhaps if** the **third** sister had **come** before him **he would also have said to her** the same ruling. The Gemara asks: **If so, what is** the purpose **of Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba's testimony?** Why would he have related this incident if it does not teach us anything? The Gemara answers: **Perhaps** he comes to **teach us that sisters establish a presumption** in a case like this even though the children who died were not from the same mother.

To help resolve the dispute over attribution, the *gemara* cites a *ma'aseh* reported by *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba* in the name of *Rabbi Yochanan*. In the city of *Tzipori* there were four sisters. The first gave birth to a son, circumcised him, and the boy died. The second and third did likewise, with the same fatal result. When the fourth sister came before *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*, he told her: *al tamoli* — do not circumcise your son. This establishes that *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* required three occurrences before ruling against the *brit milah* — and accordingly, he holds three is the operative number.

The *gemara* objects: perhaps *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* was only responding to the situation as presented. Maybe if the third sister had come to him after only two deaths, he would have ruled against circumcision even then. The *gemara* responds that if so, there would be nothing at all to learn from *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba*'s story — it would communicate no principle. Therefore, the story must be teaching something — and that teaching is the content of the next segment.

### Genetic Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ אֲחָיוֹת מְחַזְּקוֹת, לֹא יִשָּׂא אָדָם אִשָּׁה לֹא מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת נִכְפִּין וְלֹא מִמִּשְׁפַּחַת מְצוֹרָעִים. וְהוּא דְּאִתַּחְזַק תְּלָתָא זִימְנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Now that you have said** that **sisters establish a presumption, a man should not marry a woman from a family of epileptics or from a family of lepers,** as these diseases might be hereditary. The Gemara adds: **And this** applies only if it **was established three times,** i.e., three members of the family are afflicted with the disease.

What *Rabbi Chiya bar Abba*'s story does teach is this: *achayos machazkos* — sisters establish a *chazakah* for one another. Information about what happened in one sister's family is halachically relevant when evaluating risk for another sister. The reason is immediately evident: hemophilia — which is transmitted through females and expressed in males — runs through the maternal line exactly in this fashion. The sisters are carriers; their sons are at risk. The *gemara*'s teaching that one draws inferences from sister to sister maps precisely onto the genetics of hemophilia, and it is almost universally understood that this is the medical reality underpinning these *gemarot*.

This is no small *chiddush*. It means that evidence need not be derived from the individual alone; family medical patterns are legally cognizable in halachic decision-making.

### Family Conditions

This teaching opens onto a painful contemporary reality. The stigmatization of families in certain communities because of known or rumored health conditions — epilepsy in particular — creates enormous social pressure and suffering. The fact that people in these communities sometimes travel to distant hospitals so that no one from their own community will recognize them in the same ward reflects both the weight the community places on this information and the devastation that exposure can cause. The institution of *Dor Yeshorim* — genetic testing for compatibility — was designed precisely to address this: results are never reported to individuals directly; couples are only told whether they are or are not compatible with each other, so that no individual or family is labeled or stigmatized. The *gemara*'s framework provides the halachic foundation, even as its contemporary application requires enormous sensitivity.

## Abaye & Choma

### Yom Kippur Incident

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי הֲוָה עֲלַהּ? כִּי אֲתָא רַב יִצְחָק בַּר יוֹסֵף, אָמַר: עוֹבָדָא הֲוָה קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בִּכְנִישְׁתָּא דְמָעוֹן בְּיוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים שֶׁחָל לִהְיוֹת בְּשַׁבָּת, וּמָלָה רִאשׁוֹנָה וָמֵת, שְׁנִיָּה וָמֵת, שְׁלִישִׁית בָּאָה לְפָנָיו. אָמַר לָהּ: לְכִי וּמוּלִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Which** halakhic conclusion **was about this** matter? Is a presumption established after two occurrences or only after three? **When Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef came** from Eretz Yisrael, **he said: An incident occurred before Rabbi Yoḥanan in the synagogue of** the town of **Maon on** a **Yom Kippur that occurred on Shabbat.** The **first** sister had **circumcised** her son **and he died;** the **second** sister circumcised her son **and he** also **died.** The **third** sister **came before him,** and he **said to her: Go and circumcise** your son, as a presumption is not established after only two occurrences.

The *gemara* now presents a dramatic episode. A story occurred before *Rabbi Yochanan* in the synagogue of *Ma'on* — and it was Yom Kippur that fell on Shabbat. In that setting, circumcisions were being performed, and one after another, children died following their *brit milah*. *Be-yoma di-kippura she-chal lihyot be-Shabbos* — the stakes could hardly have been higher. The first child died, the second died, and the third case was brought before *Rabbi Yochanan*. He ruled: *lechi u'moli* — go ahead and circumcise.

### Abaye's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: חֲזִי דְּקָשָׁרֵית אִיסּוּרָא וְסַכַּנְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to** Rav Yitzḥak: **See** to it that your report is accurate, **as you are permitting** an action that would otherwise constitute **a prohibition and a danger.** If the third baby should not be circumcised, doing so would be a prohibited labor and would endanger the life of the child.

*Abaye* challenged the reporter of this ruling, *Rav Yitzchak bar Yosef*: *chazi de'kashris issura ve'sakanta* — notice that on your testimony you are permitting both *issur* and *sakana*. The *issur* is performing *brit milah* on Shabbat and Yom Kippur when, if the circumcision is medically contraindicated due to risk to life, it constitutes *chilul Shabbat*. The *sakana* is exposing the infant himself to a potentially life-threatening procedure. This is not a trivial ruling; it carries enormous consequences either way.

### Abaye Marries Choma

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סְמַךְ עֲלַהּ אַבָּיֵי, וַאֲזַל נַסְבַהּ לְחוּמָה בְּרַתֵּיה דְּאִיסִי בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, דְּנַסְבַהּ רַחֲבָא דְפוּמְבְּדִיתָא וּשְׁכֵיב, רַב יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה וּשְׁכֵיב, וְנַסְבַהּ הוּא וּשְׁכֵיב.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **Abaye relied on this** report **and went** and **married Ḥuma, the daughter of Isi, son of Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda.** Ḥuma **had** previously **married Raḥava of Pumbedita, and he died,** and then she married **Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rabba bar bar Ḥana, and he died; and he,** Abaye, **married her** nevertheless, without concern that she had been established to be a woman whose husbands die; **and he died** as well while married to her.

The reliance takes a personal and startling turn. *Abaye* went and married a woman named *Choma*, described as the daughter of *Issi* the son of *Rav Yitzchak* the son of *Rav Yehuda*. What was *Choma*'s history? *Rechava of Pumbedisa* had married her and died. *Rav Yitzchak* the son of *Rabbah bar bar Chana* had married her and also died. Two previous husbands had died after marrying her.

*Abaye* proceeded to marry her anyway, and he too died — *ve'shachiv*. *Rava* responded with the observation: *umi ika de'avid uvda be'nafshei ki hai* — where do you find someone who experiments on himself like this? One does not use oneself as the test case for a ruling of this nature.

### Abaye's Unreliable Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: וּמִי אִיכָּא דְּעָבֵיד עוֹבָדָא בְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ כִּי הַאי, וְהָא אִיהוּ דְּאָמַר: אָבִין — דְּסַמְכָא, יִצְחָק סוּמָּקָא — לָאו בַּר סַמְכָא. אָבִין — יֶשְׁנוֹ בַּחֲזָרָה, יִצְחָק סוּמָּקָא — אֵינוֹ בַּחֲזָרָה. וְעוֹד: אֵימַר דִּפְלִיגִי לְעִנְיַן מִילָה, בְּנִישּׂוּאִין מִי פְּלִיגִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: Is there** anyone **who performs an action like this** and endangers **himself** by marrying such a woman? **Wasn't it he,** Abaye, **who said** that **Avin is reliable** but **Yitzḥak the Red,** i.e., Rav Yitzḥak bar Yosef, **is not reliable?** He proceeds to explain the difference between them: **Avin returns** to Eretz Yisrael and hears whether the Sages there rescind their previous rulings, whereas **Yitzḥak the Red does not return** to Eretz Yisrael and never finds out if the Sages there rescind their rulings. **And furthermore, say that they disagree with regard to** whether a presumption is established by two or by three deaths due to **circumcision,** but **do** they necessarily **argue with regard to marriage?**

The episode thus raises the question of the credibility of the evidence *Abaye* relied upon. The *gemara* points out that *Abaye* had based himself on a report from *Yitzchak Sumcha* about what *Rabbi Yochanan* had ruled — and *Abaye* himself had said that *Avin* is reliable when reporting in *Rabbi Yochanan*'s name because *Avin yeshno be'chazarah* — he reviews his learning or goes back to check whether *Rabbi Yochanan* has revised his view. *Yitzchak Sumcha eino be'chazarah* — *Yitzchak Sumcha* does not do this, and so his reports of *Rabbi Yochanan*'s rulings are unreliable. *Abaye*'s reliance on *Yitzchak Sumcha*'s version of this ruling was therefore questionable from the start.

### Causation Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִין, וְהָתַנְיָא: נִיסֵּת לָרִאשׁוֹן וָמֵת, לַשֵּׁנִי וָמֵת, לַשְּׁלִישִׁי — לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: לַשְּׁלִישִׁי — תִּנָּשֵׂא, לִרְבִיעִי — לֹא תִּנָּשֵׂא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Yes, and it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a woman **was married to** her **first** husband **and he died, to a second** one **and he** also **died, she may not get married to a third** husband. This is **the statement of Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: She may get married to a third** husband, but if he also dies, **she may not get married to a fourth** husband.

Moreover, the *gemara* asks: even granting the authenticity of the ruling, *Rabbi* and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* disagree specifically *be'inyan milah* — in the context of circumcision. Does that debate extend to *nisuin*, to marriage? Presumably the causal mechanism in the two cases differs entirely. The *gemara* resolves this: yes, they do in fact debate the marriage case as well. A *baraita* states: if a woman's first husband died, and her second husband died, *Rabbi* rules she should not be married to a third man — *lo sinasei*. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* rules the third may marry her; it is only the fourth who should not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא גַּבֵּי מִילָה, אִיכָּא מִשְׁפָּחָה דִּרְפֵי דְּמָא וְאִיכָּא מִשְׁפָּחָה דִּקְמִיט דְּמָא, אֶלָּא נִישּׂוּאִין מַאי טַעְמָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב מָרְדֳּכַי לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָכִי אָמַר אֲבִימִי מֵהַגְרוֹנְיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב הוּנָא: מַעְיָן גּוֹרֵם. וְרַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: מַזָּל גּוֹרֵם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted with regard to circumcision** a presumption of death due to circumcision can be established because **there are families whose blood is thin** and does not clot well, **and there are families whose blood clots. However,** in the case of **marriage, what is the reason** for concern that a subsequent husband will die? **Rav Mordekhai said to Rav Ashi: Avimi of Hagron-ya said in the name of Rav Huna as follows:** Her **spring** is the **cause.** In other words, the woman has some sort of condition that causes those who have intercourse with her to die. **And Rav Ashi said** that her **constellation** is the **cause** of her husbands' deaths.

The *gemara* then asks what makes sense as the causal mechanism in each case. By *brit milah*, the explanation is clear: *ika mishpacha de'rafi dama ve'ika mishpacha de'kamit dama* — some families have loose, free-flowing blood, others have more viscous blood. A bleeding disorder running in the family — precisely hemophilia — explains why circumcision proves fatal for the children of this family. The cause-and-effect relationship is medically coherent.

But what explains the case of the woman whose husbands die? *Rav Mordechai* in the name of *Bibi of Agronya* in the name of *Rav Huna* offers one answer: *mayan gorem* — the sexual union itself is somehow the cause, suggesting perhaps some form of transmissible disease through intimacy. *Rav Ashi* offers a different answer: *mazal gorem* — it is simply her *mazal*, her fate, that causes harm to those who are bound to her, with no medical mechanism required.

### Practical Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאֵירְסַהּ וּמִית, אִי נָמֵי דִּנְפַל מִדִּיקְלָא וּמִית.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between them?** The Gemara answers: **There is** a difference **between them** in a case **where** a man **betrothed her and died** before the wedding; **alternatively,** in a case **where he fell off a palm tree and died.** If the concern is due to intercourse, then in these cases the husband's death cannot be attributed to his wife. Conversely, if the concern is due to her bad fortune, the husband's death can be attributed to his wife even in these cases.

The practical difference between these two positions: if a fiancé died during *erusin*, before any physical union occurred, the *mazal* theory would treat that death as equally significant — being betrothed to her is enough to be subject to her *mazal*. But if the cause is the sexual union itself, a death before consummation would be irrelevant. Similarly, if a husband fell from a tree and died — an obvious accident — that would count toward the pattern under the *mazal* theory, since bad fate could manifest in any form of death, but would not count under the *mayan* theory, since the death had no connection to the physical relationship. The example of the man who fell from a tree is a vivid illustration that two occurrences of something do not by themselves establish causation — the cause may be entirely independent of what they appear to have in common.

## Differentiated Rulings

### Rav Yosef's View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יוֹסֵף בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְרָבָא, בְּעַי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב יוֹסֵף: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי? וַאֲמַר לִי: אִין. הֲלָכָה כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל? וַאֲמַר לִי: אִין, אַחוֹכֵי אַחֵיךָ בִּי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef, son of Rava, said to Rava: I inquired of Rav Yosef** whether the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **and he said to me: Yes.** I subsequently asked him if the ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and he said to me: Yes. Was he mocking me** by issuing contradictory rulings?

The *gemara* has been circling the question of what the operative *halacha* is. *Rava* reports having asked *Rav Yosef*: do we rule like *Rabbi*? *Rav Yosef* said yes. Do we rule like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*? *Rav Yosef* again said yes. *Rava* thought he was being mocked — *achyuchei achyuch bi* — but *Rav Yosef* clarified: *stamei hi* — it is a *stam* ruling, and the correct answer is that we rule like each of them in different domains.

### Marriage & Lashing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, סְתָמֵי הִיא וּפְשַׁיט לָךְ: נִישּׂוּאִין וּמַלְקִיּוֹת — כְּרַבִּי. וְסָתוֹת וְשׁוֹר הַמּוּעָד — כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said to him: No, there are unattributed** *mishnayot* in accordance with each opinion, **and he resolved for you** that the *halakha* is in accordance with each opinion in particular cases. With regard to **marriage and lashings** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi that two occurrences are sufficient for a presumption. Concerning **set patterns** of menstrual bleeding **and a forewarned ox,** the *halakha* is **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** that a presumption is established after three occurrences.

For *nisuin* — marriage — and for *malkiyos* — lashes — we rule like *Rabbi*: two occurrences are sufficient to require action. By marriage, this means that if a woman has been married twice and both husbands died, a third man who has not yet fulfilled *pru u'rvu* should not marry her. By lashes, the *mishnah* rules that if someone received *malkos* for a transgression and repeated the same transgression a second time, *beit din* does not simply administer another round of lashes and release him — it places him in the *kippah* and feeds him barley until he dies. This is already triggered after the second offense. The language there — *beit din konasin oso la-kippah u-ma'achilin oso seorin ad she-tibaka kreiso ve-yumas* — is arresting. *Tosafot* suggests that the phrase about the stomach bursting is hyperbolic, meaning merely that they confine him and give him coarse barley rations; but in either reading, drastic action follows the second offense.

### Menstrual & Ox

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִישּׂוּאִין — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. מַלְקִיּוֹת — דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁלָּקָה וְשָׁנָה — בֵּית דִּין כּוֹנְסִין אוֹתוֹ לְכִיפָּה וּמַאֲכִילִין אוֹתוֹ שְׂעוֹרִים עַד שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּרֵיסוֹ נִבְקַעַת. וְסָתוֹת — דִּתְנַן: אֵין הָאִשָּׁה קוֹבַעַת לָהּ וֶסֶת, עַד שֶׁתִּקְבָּעֶנָּה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים. וְאֵין מִטַּהֶרֶת מִן הַוֶּסֶת, עַד שֶׁתֵּעָקֵר מִמֶּנָּה שָׁלֹשׁ פְּעָמִים. וְשׁוֹר הַמּוּעָד — דִּתְנַן: אֵין הַשּׁוֹר נַעֲשֶׂה מוּעָד, עַד שֶׁיָּעִידוּ בּוֹ שְׁלֹשָׁה פְּעָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara identifies the aforementioned *halakhot*. **Marriage** is referring to **that which we said** with regard to a woman whose husbands have died. The case of **lashings** is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 63b): **One who was flogged** for transgressing a Torah law, **and repeated** the same transgression and was flogged again, if he then repeats the sin a third time, the **court places him in a** narrow, **vaulted chamber and they feed him barley until his stomach bursts.** Once he has sinned and been flogged twice he has established a presumption of wickedness, and when he sins again he is caused to die so that he will not continue to sin. The case of **set patterns** of menstrual bleeding is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Nidda* 63b): **A woman does not** **establish a set pattern** of menstrual bleeding **for herself,** so that it can be assumed that she will start bleeding at a particular time, **until she has established it three times.** Similarly, **she is not purified from her set pattern until it is uprooted from her three times,** i.e., until she did not experience menstrual bleeding at the expected time according to her pattern on three occasions. **And** the case of a **forewarned ox** is **as we learned** in a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 23b): **An ox does not become forewarned until** witnesses **testify** that **it** has gored **three times.** Consequently, in the cases of set patterns and a forewarned ox, a legal presumption is created only after three occurrences, in accordance with the opinion of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.

For *vestos* — menstrual patterns — and for *shor ha-muad* — the habitually goring ox — we rule like *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel*: three occurrences are required. Regarding *vestos*, the *mishnah* rules that *ein ishah kova'as lah veset ad she-tikba'enah shalosh pe'amim* — a woman does not establish a fixed menstrual cycle until it has occurred three times. Similarly, *einah mitaheres min ha-veset ad she-ta'akrenah shalosh pe'amim* — once a regular cycle is established, it is not considered changed until three consecutive cycles have diverged from it. The halachic implications are significant: if a woman has a fixed *veset* and the time of her expected period arrives, she is treated as if she has become a *niddah* even without a physical check. That presumption requires a three-time foundation. Similarly, a *shor ha-muad* — an ox whose owner is liable for full damages rather than half — attains that status only after *sheyeidu bo shalosh pe'amim*, three witnessed gorings.

### Two vs Three Synthesis

The best resolution of this apparent contradiction — ruling like *Rabbi* in some areas and *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* in others — is that the two thresholds address two categorically different questions. Two occurrences are enough to generate concern and require a response. They establish that we cannot dismiss what has happened as a pure coincidence, and they obligate action. Three occurrences, however, establish something stronger: that the pattern is fixed, regular, and essentially certain. This is the difference between *choshesh* — being apprehensive — and *chazakah* — a legally established presumption.

The *Pnei Yehoshua* and other *achronim* articulate this distinction clearly. For *vestos*, we are not merely saying a woman should be concerned about bleeding on a certain day — we presume she has bled. That is a full legal presumption, requiring three-time establishment. For the *shor ha-muad*, the ox is not merely one to watch carefully — his legal identity changes; he becomes a definitively dangerous animal whose owner bears full liability. That too requires three. But for marriage and for punishing a repeat offender, two times is enough to act: the evidence of danger or habitual wrongdoing is sufficient to respond, even before the pattern is definitively established. This is analogous to the *Chofetz Chaim*'s distinction in *hilchos lashon hara* between believing a report and being *choshesh* for it — between accepting information as true and treating it as warranting concern.

Returning to the *mishnah*'s case: a woman who has gone through two marriages of ten years each without children may not be married by a third man who has not yet fulfilled *pru u'rvu*. Yet the second husband who divorced her must still pay her *kesubah*, because we have not definitively established that the infertility lies with her. These two rulings, taken together, define precisely the space between two-time concern and three-time certainty: there is enough evidence to prohibit the third marriage, but not enough to deprive her of her *kesubah*. That is exactly the distinction between ruling like *Rabbi* in matters of marriage — where two times suffices to require action — and what it would mean to have established her infertility with the certainty of a *chazakah*.

The question of how these principles apply today — particularly in the case of a woman whose multiple husbands have died, where the causal theories of *mayan gorem* and *mazal gorem* are both medically elusive — remains open and, as the *gemara* itself demonstrates through the case of *Abaye*, requires considerable care.
