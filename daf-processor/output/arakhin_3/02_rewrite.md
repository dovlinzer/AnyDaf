# Arakhin 3 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Hakol Overview

### Opening Remarks

Today's daf is 3. We are still at the very beginning and find ourselves with much rich material to work through. The daf continues a sustained inquiry into the word *hakol* — "everybody" — as it appears throughout *mishnayot* and *gemarot* across *Shas*, examining what each such formulation comes to include that one might otherwise have excluded.

### Previous Day Review

The previous session introduced this inquiry after a brief overview of the tractates of *Arakhin* and *Nedarim*, working through a series of *mishnayot* that employ the word *hakol* and asking in each case: *l'atuyei mai* — what does "everybody" come to include? Today we conclude that survey and then turn to a second dimension of *hakol* — those *mishnayot* that not only say "everybody" but go on to spell out explicitly: *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim*. The *gemara* will ask why each of those cases requires saying what seems self-evident.

## Megilla & Women

### Megilla Obligation

The *gemara* opens at the bottom of 2b and top of 3a with two parallel formulations: a *Tosefta* in *Megilla* states *hakol chayavin bemikra megilla* — everybody is obligated to read the *Megilla* — and a *Mishna* states *hakol kesherin likrot et hamegilla* — everybody is *kasher* to read the *Megilla*. The first concerns obligation; the second concerns eligibility to fulfill the *mitzvah* on behalf of others. In both cases, *l'atuyei mai* — what does "everybody" include? The *gemara* answers: *l'atuyei nashim* — to include women. Even though *mikra megilla* is technically a positive time-bound *mitzvah* from which women would ordinarily be exempt, women are nonetheless obligated and may read, following Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi, who says: *nashim chayavot bemikra megilla she'af hen hayu be'oto hanes* — women are obligated because they too were part of that miracle, equally saved. And since their obligation is equal to that of men, they may also read on behalf of men.

### Three Mitzvot

The *gemara*'s invocation of *she'af hen hayu be'oto hanes* — "they too were in that miracle" — is a principle that obligates women in three *mitzvot* connected to the *chagim*: *mikra megilla*, *ner Chanukah*, and *arba kosot*. *Tosafot* raises the obvious question: why is this principle applied only to these three? Women were also part of the miracle of *Mitzrayim* — why not use the same logic to obligate them in eating *matzah* or other *mitzvot* of *Pesach*? One answer offered is that this principle operates only within the domain of Rabbinic *mitzvot*, not Biblical ones — and indeed, all three of these cases are *d'rabbanan*.

### Pirsumei Nisa

A more powerful answer, attributed to Rav Soloveitchik, focuses not on the Rabbinic versus Biblical distinction but on the nature of these three *mitzvot* themselves. What they share is that each is centrally and essentially an act of *pirsumei nisa* — publicizing the miracle. When we light *ner Chanukah* we recite *she'asa nisim*; when we read the *Megilla* we recite *she'asa nisim*. These are not merely private acts of remembrance but public proclamations directed outward. Even the *arba kosot*, while not accompanied by *she'asa nisim*, function as a demonstration — an expression of freedom visible to all present. *Matzah*, by contrast, is a *zecher*, a memorial, but it is not in the same way an outward announcement to others. The key indicator of *pirsumei nisa* is precisely the recitation of *she'asa nisim la'avoteinu*, which appears specifically by *mikra megilla* and *ner Chanukah*. The *arba kosot* are something of a stretch in this framework — once one includes them, the logic threatens to expand further — but the core insight remains persuasive: because women were equal beneficiaries of the miracle, they bear an equal obligation to publicize it. The *mitzvot* whose very essence is that proclamation are precisely the ones where their obligation is most compelling.

### Af Textual Issue

There is a well-known textual difficulty with the phrase itself. *Af hen hayu be'oto hanes* — the word *af* means "also." But "also" in relation to what? If the phrase means that women were also saved in the miracle, who is being compared to whom? And if the alternative reading is that women were instrumental in bringing about the miracle — as in *bizchut nashim tzidkaniyot nigalu avoteinu miMitzrayim*, in the merit of righteous women our ancestors were redeemed from Egypt, or as in the case of *Yehudit* and *Holofernes* for *Chanukah* — then *af* is equally problematic. The *Rashash* attempts a resolution by transposing the emphasis: read it as *hen hayu af be'oto hanes*, meaning the *af* modifies *hanes* rather than *hen* — they were in that miracle too, as opposed to some other miracle. But as is openly acknowledged, this is a difficult reading, and the word *af* remains textually awkward on any account.

### Tosafot Limitation

Turning to the practical *halacha* of women reading the *Megilla* for men: the simple reading of both the *Mishna* and the *gemara* in *Megilla*, as understood by Rashi, the Rambam, and the great majority of *Rishonim*, is that since women's obligation is equal to men's, they may read and fulfill the obligation on behalf of men. *Tosafot*, however, quotes the *Behag*, who in turn cites a *Tosefta*: *nashim va'avadim u'ktanim pturim mikriat megilla* — women, slaves, and minors are exempt from reading the *Megilla*. Based on this *Tosefta*, *Tosafot* limits the scope of the *gemara* in two directions. First, women's obligation is not identical to men's — if it were, why could they not fulfill the *mitzvah* on behalf of men? The implication is that women's *mitzvah* is more receptive in character: they must hear and appreciate the miracle, but their obligation does not carry the active dimension that would allow them to discharge the obligation of others. Second, when the *gemara* says women can read, it means women can read for other women — not for men.

### Reconciliation

The *Tosafot* that begins with *l'atuyei* contains a key passage, introduced with the phrase *ad kan lashon Halachos Gedolos*, explaining that women are *motzi* only other women but not men, and that *hakol chayavim bemikra megilla l'atuyei nashim shemotzi'os nashim acheros* — "everybody is obligated in *mikra megilla*" comes to include women who fulfill the obligation on behalf of other women. The interpretive move here is striking: rather than reject the *Tosefta* because the *Mishna* says otherwise, *Tosafot* attempts to reconcile the two by narrowing what the *Mishna*'s *hakol* means. This approach — taking an extra-*Bavli* source, whether a *Tosefta* not cited in the *gemara* or a *Yerushalmi*, and using it to redefine the scope of the *Bavli* — is a recurring pattern in this passage and will appear again shortly.

This approach also leads some *Rishonim* to distinguish between the *bracha* a woman would recite: perhaps *lishmoa megilla* rather than *likroa megilla*, reflecting the understanding that her *mitzvah* is to hear rather than to read.

### Modern Debates

The *psak* of *Tosafot*, following the *Behag*, became relatively dominant among *Ashkenazim*, yet it is clearly not the *peshat* of the *gemara*. This has generated significant contemporary discussion, particularly regarding the night reading of the *Megilla*. Halachically, the primary *mitzvah* of *mikra megilla* is the daytime reading — the *gemara* in *Megilla* derives it from the verse *v'hayamim ha'eileh nizkarim v'na'asim*, with *yamim* pointing to the day — while the night reading is a later Amoraic addition and therefore carries somewhat less halachic weight. Given that our communal emphasis has shifted toward the night reading, and given that this night reading is halachically less weighty, some contemporary authorities argue that one may rely on the *Rishonim* who follow the simple sense of the *gemara* — that women may read for men — at least for the night reading.

### Teaching Question

One might also ask: if women are obligated in *mikra megilla* and must read or at least understand what they are reading, how did they learn the text, given the traditional limitations on teaching women *Torah*? In practice, written *Torah* was generally considered teachable even to women, so this question is less acute. The more commonly raised concern in this context is *kol isha* — whether a woman's voice reading *Megilla* constitutes a halachic problem for male listeners — which intersects with all these discussions.

## Zimun Obligation

### Zimun Basic

The *gemara* continues: *hakol chayavim b'zimmun* — everybody is obligated in *zimmun*, the invitation formula preceding *Birkat Hamazon* when eating in a group. *L'atuyei nashim va'avadim* — this includes women and slaves. A *baraita* teaches: *nashim mezamnot l'atzman va'avadim mezamnim l'atzman* — women make a *zimmun* among themselves and slaves among themselves. So they are indeed obligated — but why only among themselves?

### Rashi's Reason

Rashi explains that women are excluded from *zimmun* with men because the obligation is not exactly the same. *Birkat Hamazon* as recited by men includes the phrase *al britcha shechatamta bivsareinu* — on the *brit* You sealed in our flesh — referring to *brit milah*. Women are not in the category of *brit* in this sense, and therefore cannot share the identical *zimmun* text with men, which means they cannot be part of a unified *zimmun* group with men.

### Tosafot Pirtzuta

*Tosafot* cites the *gemara* in *Berachot* which offers a different reason: the separation is due to *pritzuta* — concerns about licentiousness from the mixing of men and women, and men and slaves, in contexts where such mixing was considered socially and morally problematic. According to this view, women's obligation in *Birkat Hamazon* is essentially identical to men's, and the separation into distinct *zimmun* groups has nothing to do with the content of the blessing but is purely a social concern.

### Modern Zimun Debate

If the reason is *pritzuta*, this raises a pressing contemporary question: in a family context, or in other settings where those concerns do not apply in the same way, can women participate in a joint *zimmun* with men? A recent halachic article — published and subsequently discussed widely in the Israeli press and in the journal *Akdamot* — worked through the *Rishonim* carefully and argued that women ought to be able to participate in *zimmun*, at minimum in family contexts. The two *Rashei Yeshiva* who responded largely agreed on the halachic analysis, though one added that one should nonetheless refrain for reasons of *slippery slope*. The question remains actively debated.

### Obligation Question

A separate but related question concerns the meaning of *hakol chayavim b'zimmun* itself. The *gemara* says women are *chayavim* — obligated. Does that mean they are fully obligated in the same sense as men, or merely that they have permission (*reshut*) to form a *zimmun*?

### Tosafot: Reshut

*Tosafot* here again, in order to reconcile other *gemarot*, concludes that the *gemara*'s *chayavim* means only *reshut* — women have permission to make a *zimmun* but are not strictly obligated. This would apply even to three women eating together on their own.

### Vilna Gaon View

The Vilna Gaon and others disagree sharply. The *peshat* of the *gemara* is *chayavim* — obligated. On the plain reading, three women eating together are not merely permitted to make a *zimmun* but required to do so. This is again a case where *Tosafot* reads down the force of the *gemara* in order to harmonize it with other sources.

### Mixed Group Issue

This raises the further question of a mixed group: if men and women eat together, and the reason women do not join the men's *zimmun* is *pritzuta*, one might equally question whether women could even participate in answering the *zimmun* in such a setting. The argument in response would be that *pritzuta* concerns the formal halachic constitution of a unified *zimmun* group — defining men and women together as a single collective — rather than simply the act of sharing a meal. But this remains a nuanced and unresolved point.

## Minors & Zimun

### Minor Inclusion

The *gemara* continues: *hakol mitztarfin l'zimmun l'asarah* — everybody combines for *zimmun* of ten, which involves the addition of *Elokeinu* to the *zimmun* formula. *L'atuyei katan* — this includes a minor, provided he knows *l'mi mevarachin* — to Whom one is blessing. Rav Nachman teaches: *hayodea l'mi mevarachin mezamnin alav* — if he knows Who he is benching to, he may be part of the *zimmun*.

### Tosafot Rejects Bavli

Yet *Tosafot* explicitly rejects this position. Even though both this passage and the *gemara* in *Berachot* rule like Rav Nachman — that a minor who knows may be counted — *Tosafot* declares: *d'afilu hachi lo kayma lan k'vateih*, we do not rule accordingly. Instead, *Tosafot* cites a *Yerushalmi* in which Rav Yosi states: many times I ate with Abba Chalafta and with my uncle, and they did not include me in the *zimmun* until I had developed two pubic hairs — that is, until I reached halachic adulthood. *Tosafot* therefore rules that a minor cannot be counted, following the *Yerushalmi* over the explicit *Bavli*.

### Beit Yosef Difficulty

The *Beit Yosef* in *Orach Chayim* expresses considerable difficulty with this. How can we reject explicit *gemarot* in the *Bavli* in favor of a *Yerushalmi*? The general rule is that we rule by the *Bavli*, not the *Yerushalmi*. The *Beit Yosef* tries to offer some reconciliation but ultimately finds no fully satisfying answer.

### Ashkenazi Customs

*Tosafot* is not troubled by this, however. *Ashkenaz* culturally derives not from *Bavel* but from *Eretz Yisrael* — the Jews of *Eretz Yisrael* migrated to Italy and from Italy to *Ashkenaz*, whereas *Sephardi* tradition flows from Babylonian culture. Accordingly, *Tosafot* occasionally follows *minhag Eretz Yisrael* over the *Bavli*. A parallel example is the *Ashkenazi* practice regarding removing *tzitzit* from a *tallis*, which contradicts what the *gemara* says and reflects an *Eretz Yisrael* tradition.

### Mechaber vs. Rama

The practical ruling reflects this divide: the *Mechaber* in the *Shulchan Aruch* follows the *Bavli* and permits counting a minor who knows *l'mi mevarachin* as part of the *zimmun*. The *Rama* follows *Tosafot* and the *Yerushalmi*, ruling that minors are not counted. This is yet another instance where *Tosafot* limits what the *Bavli* straightforwardly permits — in all three cases so far (*mikra megilla*, *zimmun* for women, and *zimmun* for minors) — while the *Mechaber* does not.

### Ten vs. Three

An attempt at reconciliation appears among several *Rishonim*: perhaps the *Bavli* and *Yerushalmi* address different thresholds. A minor cannot be counted for the standard *zimmun* of three — that is the *Yerushalmi*'s ruling. But he may be counted toward the *zimmun* of ten, which requires the addition of *Elokeinu* — that is what the *Bavli* permits. On this reading, the two sources are not in conflict.

### Elokeinu Status

This reconciliation raises a further question: does *zimmun* of ten fall under the category of *devarim shebikdusha* — matters of sanctity that require the standard quorum of adult Jewish males? If the reason one may count a minor toward ten is precisely that this threshold is not governed by the usual *minyan* rules, that reasoning has implications for the women's question as well, since it would mean that ten for *Elokeinu* operates by a different logic than standard *devarim shebikdusha*.

### Practical Question

This is all directly relevant to a practical question: if a man wishes to count his wife and his nine-year-old daughter in his *zimmun*, there are two separate *psak* questions. On the minor: the *Ashkenazi* ruling, based on *Tosafot* following the *Yerushalmi* over the *Bavli*, would not count the child even for the ten. On the wife: whether women participate in a *zimmun* with men depends on the *pritzuta* analysis and the contemporary debates discussed above.

### Sephardi View

The *Sephardi* position, following the *Mechaber*, permits counting a minor even as part of the three, provided he meets the criterion of *yodea l'mi mevarachin*. The precise age at which a child qualifies is left somewhat open — the *Beit Yosef* discusses this at length but the *Shulchan Aruch* itself leaves it fluid, with the standard being the child's actual cognitive grasp rather than a fixed age.

## Impurity Categories

### Zav Status

The *gemara* turns to several categories of *tumah*: *hakol mitamin b'ziva* — everybody can become *tamei* as a *zav*, a man who has an abnormal penile discharge that is not semen. This is a form of *tumah* that applies broadly.

### One-Day Infant

*L'atuyei tinok ben yomo* — this includes even a one-day-old infant. If such a discharge occurs even to a newborn male, he has the *tumah* of a *zav*.

### Ish vs. Zachar

The *gemara* derives this from a *baraita*. The Torah uses the phrase *ish ish ki yihyeh zav mibesaro* — "a man, a man, when he has a flow." The doubled *ish* might imply an adult male specifically. Rabbi Yehuda therefore derives the inclusion of a one-day-old infant from the doubling: *l'rabot tinok ben yom echad shemitamei b'ziva*. Rabbi Yishmael son of Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka disagrees and says no derivation is needed, because a different verse uses the term *lazachar* — any male — rather than *ish*. *Zachar* means any male regardless of age: *kol shehu zachar, bein katan bein gadol* — whether minor or adult. Similarly, *lazachar* is paired with *ulanekeiva* — any female — covering even infant girls who might develop a *zava*-type flow.

### Torah Vernacular

On this reading, why does the Torah use *ish* at all, and why the doubled formulation? *Dibra torah k'lishon bnei adam* — the Torah simply speaks in ordinary human language. One need not press every word for a specific halachic exclusion or inclusion. This is, of course, Rabbi Yishmael's signature principle, in contrast to Rabbi Akiva's method of deriving laws from every grammatical feature of the Torah.

### Corpse Impurity

The *gemara* continues: *hakol mitamei b'tmei meit* — everybody becomes *tamei* through contact with a corpse. Importantly, this does not mean the corpse conveys the *tumah*; it means that anyone who comes into contact with a corpse acquires the *tumah* of *tmei meit*.

### Minor Impurity

*L'atuyei katan* — even a minor. One might have thought otherwise, since the verse says *ish asher yitma v'lo yitchata* — "a man who becomes *tamei* and does not purify himself" incurs *karet* for entering the *Mishkan*. The word *ish* might suggest only an adult male is capable of this *tumah*. The *gemara* overrides this: another verse speaks of sprinkling on *kol hanefashot asher hayu sham* — all the souls that were there — without any qualification for age. Hence even a minor becomes *tamei*. The word *ish* in the earlier verse, then, is not excluding minors from *tumah* but rather excluding them from the punishment of *karet*: if a minor who is *tamei* enters the *Beit HaMikdash*, he does not incur *karet*, since minors are exempt from adult punishments — as is universally true for any sin a minor commits.

### Leprosy Impurity

The same structure applies to *tzara'at*: *hakol mitamin b'nega'im* — everybody can become *tamei* as a *metzora*. *L'atuyei katan* — including a minor. One might have thought that *ish tzarua*, "a leprous man," excludes minors and women, since *ish* implies an adult male.

### Adam vs. Ish

The *gemara* resolves this: the verse also says *adam ki yihyeh b'or b'saro* — "a person when there is on the skin of his flesh" — and *adam* is understood as a more general category than *ish*, encompassing any person regardless of age or gender.

### Comportment Behaviors

If so, what does *ish* come to teach? The answer is the *nihugei tzara'at* — the behavioral requirements imposed on a *metzora*: *begadav yihyu frumim v'rosho yihyeh parua* — his garments shall be torn and his hair shall grow wild, as well as calling out *tamei tamei*. These obligations apply only to *ish* — an adult male. The verse also adds *v'hatzarua asher bo hanega*, adding a more inclusive term that extends *tumah* itself to all persons, while the word *ish* reserves the behavioral requirements for men.

### Women & Tzniut

*Tosafot* asks: one could have read the verses differently — why are we confident that *ish* excludes women from the comportment behaviors rather than from *tumah* itself? The answer is that a consideration of *tzniut* — propriety — clearly governs the reading. Requiring a woman to let her hair grow wild and her garments to be rent would be a serious violation of modesty norms. This principle of propriety therefore directs the reading of the verse, excluding women from these behavioral requirements while leaving them fully subject to *tumah* itself.

## Determining Leprosy

### Seeing Spots

The *gemara* now addresses two related formulations: *hakol ro'in et hanega'im* — everybody can see the spots and assess them — and *hakol kesherin lirot et hanega'im* — everybody is *kasher* to see the *nega'im*. These are from two separate sources (one a *mishna*, the other a *baraita*), but they say the same thing and are treated together.

### Non-Expert

*L'atuyei she'eino baki bahen uvishmoteihen* — this comes to include even someone who is not expert in the laws of *nega'im* and does not know their names and parameters. Yet the *gemara* also teaches: *eino baki bahen uvishmoteihen eino ro'eh et hanega'im* — one who is not expert cannot assess the *nega'im*. This is a direct contradiction.

### Explanation & Understanding

The tension requires resolution: in what sense can a non-expert participate in the process?

### Ravina's Resolution

Ravina resolves the contradiction by distinguishing two types of non-experts. *Ha d'masvri leh v'savar* — if you explain the relevant halachic parameters to this person and he understands, he may participate. *Ha d'masvri leh v'lo savar* — but if you explain it to him and even as the words are coming out of your mouth he cannot grasp them, that is the person who cannot be part of the process. It is not blanket expertise or ignorance that matters, but whether understanding can be communicated and received.

### Kohen & Scholar

The practical application of this ruling is well-known: the determination of *tzara'at* must be made by a *kohen*, who pronounces the person *tamei* or *tahor*. But if the *kohen* has no expertise in the laws of *nega'im*, he brings with him a *talmid chacham* who knows the relevant halachot. The scholar examines the spot and explains the ruling to the *kohen*, who then makes the formal declaration.

### Understanding vs. Parroting

What exactly must the *kohen* understand? The simple reading of the *gemara* — and arguably the correct one — is that the explanation must genuinely be absorbed. The scholar tells the *kohen*: this spot is of a size and color that renders it a *baheret*, and for these reasons it is *tamei* — and the *kohen* grasps this reasoning. Only then may the *kohen* pronounce *tamei*. It is not sufficient for the *kohen* to merely repeat the word without comprehension.

### Tosafot Alternative

*Tosafot* offers a different reading of *d'masvri leh v'savar*: this phrase describes not the *kohen*'s capacity for understanding but the instructor's qualifications — the person explaining need not be an expert in the entire field of *nega'im*, but only in the specific type of *nega* at hand. On this reading, the bar for the instructor is narrowed rather than the bar for the *kohen*.

### Understanding Reasoning

The straightforward explanation, however, remains that the *kohen* must genuinely understand the ruling being communicated to him — not parrot a word, but grasp, at least in basic terms, why the spot before him carries the status it does.

## Para Aduma

### Mixing Ashes

The *gemara* turns to a series of laws relating to *parah adumah*, the red heifer. *Hakol kesherin l'kadesh* — everybody may perform the *kiddush*, the mixing of the ashes of the *parah adumah* with water to create the purification waters. *L'atuyei mai* — who does this include?

### Normal Exclusions

A *Mishna* provides the baseline: everybody may perform the *kiddush* — *chutz mechereish shoteh v'katan*, with the exception of those who lack halachic competence: the deaf-mute, the imbecile, and the minor. Women are ordinarily included.

### Gender vs. Age

Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis dispute along a different axis. Rabbi Yehuda permits a minor to perform the *kiddush* — age is not the disqualifying factor for him — but disqualifies a woman and an *androgynos*, and presumably a *tumtum* as well: anyone of uncertain or female gender is excluded. The Rabbis, by contrast, include women but exclude minors. Each position, then, uses the *hakol* to include the other's exception: for Rabbi Yehuda, *l'atuyei katan*; for the Rabbis, *l'atuyei ishah*.

### Why Men Required

Why should the *kiddush* require specifically male participants? The *gemara*'s derivation of this requirement is discussed elsewhere — in *Yoma* — and will not be fully explored here.

## Hazot & Shechita

### Sprinkling

The *gemara* continues: *hakol kesherin l'hazot* — everybody may perform the *hazayah*, the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* purification waters.

### Arel Included

*L'atuyei arel* — this comes to include even an uncircumcised person. An *arel* is in certain respects in a diminished halachic status, and for some *halachot* carries an aspect of *tumah*, yet he is not disqualified from performing the sprinkling. This follows Rabbi Elazar, who teaches: *arel she'hizah hazato kesherah* — if an *arel* performed the sprinkling, it is valid.

### Marginal Cases

Rashi and *Tosafot* note that other individuals on the margins of eligibility — such as a *tvul yom*, one who immersed in a *mikvah* that day and is waiting for nightfall to complete purification — are also *kasher* for the sprinkling. The *arel* is included in this broader category of those whose apparent disqualification does not extend to this act.

### Shechita

The *gemara* next addresses *shechita*: *hakol shochtin* — everybody may slaughter. This formulation appears twice in *Chullin*, and the *gemara* accounts for both appearances.

### Kuti Included

One instance includes the *Kuti* — the Samaritan — who, despite questions about his reliability in observing certain *mitzvot*, is nonetheless valid to perform *shechita*.

### Mumar Included

The second instance includes the *Yisrael mumar* — the Jew who is rebellious or who violates certain *mitzvot* categorically. Despite concerns about his intent and reliability, his *shechita* is valid in principle.

### Chullin Reference

Both of these cases were the subject of the opening *sugya* of *Chullin* — one of the great discussions in all of *Shas* — which explores questions of categorical exclusion versus concerns about knowledge and performance, and the degree to which unreliability or rejection of rabbinic authority disqualifies a person from performing *shechita*.

## Aliyah Rights

### Aliyah Demand

The *gemara* cites a *Mishna* in *Ketubot*: *hakol ma'alin l'Eretz Yisrael* — everybody may demand *aliyah*, the move to *Eretz Yisrael*, and compel the other party in a marriage to either join them or accept the halachic consequences.

### Spouse Dynamics

As background: within the normal laws of marital residence, a husband has considerable but not unlimited power to relocate. He may move between cities of the same type — city to city, village to village — but not unilaterally from city to village or village to city. And he may not force a move to a lower-class neighborhood — even to a higher-class neighborhood, since the wife can object that the elevated social standard creates burdens she did not take on. The wife thus has significant grounds to resist relocation in general. *Eretz Yisrael*, however, operates under different rules entirely.

### Wife Aliyah

If the wife wishes to make *aliyah* and the husband refuses, the husband must divorce her and pay her *ketubah*. The payment of the *ketubah* signals that the fault lies with the husband — she is blameless in seeking to live in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Husband Aliyah

If the husband wishes to make *aliyah* and the wife refuses, he may divorce her without paying the *ketubah*, since in this case the fault lies with the wife.

### Slave Aliyah

*L'atuyei avadim* — the *hakol* extends even to slaves. According to the Rambam, a slave may demand to be sold to a master living in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Rashi vs. Rambam

Rashi limits this somewhat: if the master is already selling the slave, the slave may insist that the sale be to someone living in *Eretz Yisrael*. The Rambam's position is more expansive — the slave has an independent right to demand such a sale. Both views reflect the extraordinary halachic weight accorded to *Eretz Yisrael* residence.

### Poor Neighborhoods

There is a *tanna* who mentions slaves explicitly in the text. On this reading, *l'atuyei* adds the case of moving from a better to a worse location — *minaveh yafeh l'naveh hara*. The point is that even if *Eretz Yisrael* at a given historical moment is less developed, less economically prosperous, or represents a lower standard of living than one's current location in *chutz la'aretz*, the right to demand *aliyah* remains intact. The value of living in *Eretz Yisrael* is not contingent on socioeconomic conditions.

### Cannot Force Out

*V'ein hakol motzi'in* — the converse: nobody may force someone out of *Eretz Yisrael*. The *hakol* here establishes an absolute right not to be removed.

### Escaped Slave

*L'atuyei eved she'barach mi'chutz la'aretz la'aretz* — this includes even a runaway slave who has fled to *Eretz Yisrael*. Even though a slave who flees is technically in violation of his status, once he has reached *Eretz Yisrael*, his master may not demand his return. This is likely grounded in the verse *imcha yeshev bikirbecha, batov lo lo sonenu* — he shall dwell with you in your midst, in the place he chooses, you shall not oppress him.

### Jerusalem Priority

The *gemara* adds a parallel set of rules for *Yerushalayim*: *hakol ma'alin l'Yerushalayim* — everybody may demand to move to *Yerushalayim*, even if one is already living in *Eretz Yisrael*.

### Nice to Not Nice

*L'atuyei minaveh yafeh l'naveh hara* — even if *Yerushalayim* is less prosperous or comfortable than one's current location, the right to demand relocation there stands. The halachic weight of *Yerushalayim* overrides the socioeconomic calculus.

### Leave Jerusalem

*V'ein hakol motzi'in mi'Yerushalayim* — nobody may be forced to leave *Yerushalayim*. And here *l'atuyei minaveh hara l'naveh yafeh* — even if one's current home in *Yerushalayim* is of lower quality than an available alternative, one cannot be compelled to leave.

### Summary

The entire section thus works through the husband-wife and master-slave dynamics with respect to the *mitzvah* of living in *Eretz Yisrael* and in *Yerushalayim*, establishing in each case both the affirmative right to demand relocation and the absolute protection against being forced to leave.

## Sukkah Obligation

### New Theme Intro

The *gemara* now shifts to a different category of *hakol* formulations — *mishnayot* that say everybody is obligated and then proceed to specify *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* explicitly. This was noted as a puzzling feature already in the opening *mishna* of *Arakhin*. The question to be asked in each case is: why must the *Mishna* spell out what is completely obvious? *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* are always included — they are the normative obligated population. It is the unusual cases — *cheresh, shoteh, v'katan*, women, slaves — that require explicit inclusion or exclusion. When the *Mishna* specifies *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim*, it must be because there is a genuine reason one might have thought to exclude *Kohanim* — and the *gemara* will identify that reason in each case.

### Sukkah Explicit

*Hakol chayavin b'sukkah* — everybody is obligated in the *sukkah* — followed by the explicit listing of *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim*. *Pshita* — of course! *Ei hanei lo mechayvi man mechayvi* — if these are not obligated, who is?

### Why Explicit

The answer: *Kohanim itztricha lei* — you need to tell me specifically about *Kohanim*.

### Kohanim Needed

*Salka da'atach amina* — I might have thought to exempt *Kohanim*. The verse says *basukkot teshvu* — "you shall dwell in *sukkot*" — and the *gemara* elsewhere teaches *teshvu k'ein taduru* — you shall dwell as you dwell at home.

### Dwell at Home

One *girsa* of the *gemara* develops this point via *mah dirah ish v'ishto* — just as ordinary dwelling is husband and wife together, so too the *sukkah* must constitute a dwelling of husband and wife together.

### Kohanim Busy

*V'hanei Kohanim ho'il uvnei avodah ninhu* — and since *Kohanim* are occupied with *avodah* in the *Beit HaMikdash*, particularly during *Sukkot* when all *Kohanim* serve regardless of their regular rotation, they are not home with their wives. Therefore I might have said that since they cannot fulfill the *teshvu k'ein taduru* requirement properly, *lo litchayvu* — they should be exempt.

### Work Hours Exempt

*Ka mashma lan nehi d'ptirei b'sha'at avodah* — true, they are exempt when actually engaged in *avodah*, but *b'lo sha'at avodah chiyuvi mechayvei* — when they are not on duty, they are obligated. The *Kohanim* are not working every moment.

### Travelers Analogy

The analogy is to *holchei drachim* — travelers on *Sukkot*: *holchei drachim bayom pturim min hasukkah* — they are exempt during the day while traveling, since they cannot erect a *sukkah* on the road, *v'chayavim balailah* — but they are obligated at night when they stop and sleep. So too *Kohanim*: exempt while working, obligated during the hours they are home.

### Better Girsa

The side *girsa* of the *gemara* reads *bein bayom bein balailah* — the concern is about day and night, not about *ish v'ishto*. This is a superior reading: the question is whether the fact that *Kohanim* are never home during the day exempts them from the daytime requirement, just as travelers are exempt during the day. The answer is that just like travelers must dwell in the *sukkah* at night, *Kohanim* must do so in whatever hours they are not occupied with *avodah*. This *girsa* is clearly to be preferred.

### Women Exempt

The other dimension of the *ish v'ishto* reading is different: since women are exempt from *sukkah*, a man might argue that *teshvu k'ein taduru* — living as at home — is impossible without his wife, since she will not join him in the *sukkah*, and therefore he too should be exempt. This argument is never accepted as *halacha*.

### Rama's Justification

Yet interestingly, the *Rama* invokes a version of this reasoning to justify the *Ashkenazi* practice of not sleeping in the *sukkah*. The real practical reason, as anyone who has spent *Sukkot* in northern Europe will understand, is that the cold weather renders sleeping in the *sukkah* a case of *mitzta'er* — suffering — which halachically exempts one. But the *Rama* also offers the rationale that since *teshvu k'ein taduru* requires living with one's wife and the wife is exempt, the husband cannot fully achieve the required quality of dwelling. The obvious problem with this justification is that it would have always exempted married men from sleeping in the *sukkah*, which cannot be right. This demonstrates that the *girsa* suggesting *ish v'ishto* as the conceptual basis for the *sukkah* requirement is problematic, reinforcing the conclusion that the alternative *girsa* — *bein bayom bein balailah* — is the correct one.

### Girsa Problems

As for the inclusion of *Levi'im* and *Yisraelim* along with *Kohanim*: the *chiddush* applies to *Kohanim*, and *Levi'im* may be added because they too have *avodah*, while *Yisraelim* follow because one states all three together. The *gemara* assumes this grouping is not problematic — once one element of the list requires explicit mention, the entire formulation *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* follows naturally.

## Tzitzit & Kohanim

### Tzitzit Obligation

*Hakol chayavin b'tzitzit* — everybody is obligated in *tzitzit* — with *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* listed explicitly. Again, *pshita* — obvious. And again, *Kohanim itztricha lei* — you need to tell me specifically about *Kohanim*.

### Why Spell Out

Why would I have thought to exempt *Kohanim*?

### Shatnez Concern

The verse juxtaposes the prohibition of *shatnez* — the mixing of wool and linen — with the obligation of *tzitzit*: *lo silbash shatnez, gedilim ta'aseh lach* — do not wear *shatnez*, you shall make fringes for yourself.

### Exempt from Shatnez

The inference: *man d'lo ishtari kilayim legabei l'visho hu d'michayav b'tzitzit* — someone who is never permitted to wear *shatnez* is the one who is obligated in *tzitzit*. The law of *tzitzit* is conceptually linked to and conditioned by one's relationship to the *shatnez* prohibition.

### Kohanim Shatnez

*V'hanei Kohanim ho'il v'ishtari kilayim legabeihu* — and since *Kohanim* are permitted *shatnez* in the form of their *bigdei kehuna*, specifically the *avnet* (belt), which is a mixture of wool and linen — one might argue that since they have a general dispensation regarding *shatnez*, they should be outside the category of persons obligated in *tzitzit*.

### Avodah Exemption

The *gemara* resolves this: *nehi d'ishtari b'idan avoda* — true, they are permitted *shatnez* during their *avodah*, in the specific garments of their priestly service — *belo idan avoda lo ishtari* — but outside of *avodah*, *Kohanim* are not exempt from the laws of *shatnez*. They remain fully subject to the *shatnez* prohibition in their ordinary clothing, and therefore remain in the category of persons obligated in *tzitzit*.

### Outside Avodah

The principle is again parallel to the *sukkah* case: exemption during *avodah* does not create a general exemption from the *mitzvah*.

### Tzitzit Connection

There is a deeper dimension to note here. *Tzitzit* themselves have an inherent connection to the question of *shatnez*: the *techeilet* string of *tzitzit* is wool, and if attached to a linen garment, it would otherwise constitute a *shatnez* violation. The Torah's juxtaposition permits this. Furthermore, the blue of *techeilet* is also a primary color of the *bigdei kehuna*. 

### Priestly Concept

This has led some to suggest that *tzitzit* function as a kind of *bigdei kehuna* for *Yisraelim* — a way of maintaining, in everyday dress and in the wider world, something of the consciousness of divine service that the *Kohen* embodies in the *Beit HaMikdash*. If so, there is a certain irony in the fact that *Kohanim* might have been thought exempt from *tzitzit* precisely because they already have that garment — but the *gemara* insists they are obligated nonetheless.

## Tefillin & Kohanim

### Tefillin Obligation

*Hakol chayavin b'tefillin* — everybody is obligated in *tefillin* — with *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* listed. *Pshita*. *Kohanim itztricha lei*.

### Why Explicit

Why might *Kohanim* have been thought exempt?

### Shel Yad vs. Shel Rosh

The verse states: *u'kshartam l'os al yadecha v'hayu l'totafos bein einecha* — you shall bind them as a sign on your arm and they shall be as *totafot* between your eyes. The *gemara* derives from here: *hakol d'isa b'mitzvah d'yad isa b'mitzvah d'rosh* — whoever is in the category of *tefillin shel yad* is also in the category of *tefillin shel rosh*, the two being linked by the verse's formulation.

### Chatzitzah Issue

The problem with *Kohanim*: during *avodah*, a *kohen* cannot wear *tefillin shel yad* because it would constitute a *chatzitzah* — an interposition — between the *bigdei kehuna* and the flesh.

### Direct Contact

The verse *v'yilbash al besaro* — "he shall wear it upon his flesh" — requires the *bigdei kehuna* to be in direct contact with the skin: *shelo yehei davar chotzetz beino l'vein besaro*. The *tefillin shel yad* sits on the arm, which is also where the sleeve of the priestly garment sits — so wearing *tefillin shel yad* during *avodah* is forbidden.

### One Doesn't Prevent

Since *Kohanim* cannot wear *tefillin shel yad* during *avodah*, one might conclude they are also exempt from *tefillin shel rosh*, given the linkage between the two. The *gemara* says: *ka mashma lan d'lo me'akvi adadi* — one does not prevent the other. As the *Mishna* teaches: *tefillin shel yad einah m'akeves shel rosh v'shel rosh einah m'akeves shel yad* — if one has only one, one puts on only one. Each *tefillin* is an independent obligation.

### Hair Area

But a further difficulty presents itself: *tefillin shel rosh* must be placed on the head, and the *kohen* wears a *mitznefet* — a hat — which covers his head. Where can *tefillin shel rosh* be placed? The *gemara* quotes a *Tanna*: *sa'aro hayah nir'eh bein tzitz l'mitznefet* — even on the *Kohen Gadol*, there was visible hair between the *tzitz* (the golden forehead plate) and the *mitznefet*. The *mitznefet* sat on the crown, the *tzitz* sat on the forehead, and in the strip of hair between forehead and crown — precisely where *tefillin* are placed — there was exposed space: *v'sham maniach tefillin* — there one places the *tefillin*.

### Hair Space

This is consistent with what we know from archaeology. The *tefillin* found in the Bar Kochba caves, like those in the Dead Sea Scrolls collection, are extremely small — they require very little space. Even a narrow band of visible hair between the *tzitz* and the *mitznefet* would suffice.

### Mitznefes Chatzitzah

The question of whether the straps of the *tefillin shel rosh* must lie directly on the head — or whether they could pass over the *mitznefet* — relates to the general laws of *chatzitzah* by *tefillin* straps. The placement described by the *gemara* places the *tefillin* itself in the exposed hair area; exactly how the straps are arranged in relation to the *mitznefet* is a further question that requires more detailed examination.

## Shofar & Kohanim

### Shofar Obligation

*Hakol chayavin bitkias shofar* — everybody is obligated in the blowing of the *shofar* — with *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim* listed. *Pshita*. *Kohanim itztarich lei* — you still need to tell me about *Kohanim*.

### Why Explicit

What reason might there be to exempt *Kohanim*?

### Daily Blasts

A first suggestion: the verse calls *Rosh Hashanah* a *yom teruah* — a day of blast. The implication might be: *man d'leis lei bitkiah ela chad yoma chayav* — those for whom there is a special blast on only one day are the ones obligated. But *Kohanim*, who hear ritual blasts regularly throughout the year — *ho'il v'isnhu bitkiah kol sha'ata*, as the verse says *u'tkatem bachatzotzros al olosaychem*, you shall blow trumpets over your offerings — are accustomed to ritual blowing and might be thought to stand outside this category. The *gemara* rejects this reasoning immediately: *mi dami? hasam chatzotzros, hacha shofar* — there is no comparison. There it is *chatzotzrot* — trumpets; here it is *shofar*. These are entirely different instruments with different halachic statuses, and familiarity with one does not affect obligation regarding the other.

### Yovel Connection

A more serious line of reasoning: the *Mishna* teaches that *Yovel* — the Jubilee year — is equivalent to *Rosh Hashanah* in its blowing and its *brachot*: *malchuyot, zichronot, v'shofrot*. This equivalence suggests a deep connection between the obligation to hear *shofar* on *Rosh Hashanah* and one's relationship to the *mitzvot* of *Yovel*. If so, one might reason: *d'isa b'mitzvot Yovel isa b'mitzvot Rosh Hashanah, d'leis b'mitzvot Yovel leis b'mitzvot Rosh Hashanah* — those who are subject to *Yovel* obligations are subject to *Rosh Hashanah* obligations; those who stand outside *Yovel* obligations stand outside *Rosh Hashanah* obligations as well.

### Yovel Equality

The connection between *Yovel* and *Rosh Hashanah* through *malchuyot, zichronot, v'shofrot* is striking. *Zichronot* and *shofrot* seem naturally relevant to *Yovel*'s proclamation of liberty and renewal — but *malchuyot* as well, since the Jubilee represents a kind of cosmic reset, a re-proclamation of divine sovereignty over the land and over human freedom.

### Relevance Principle

The principle is clear: if *Yovel* and *Rosh Hashanah* share the same *tekiot* and *brachot*, there must be a category of persons to whom both apply simultaneously — and they are the ones upon whom the *shofar* obligation is primarily imposed.

### Yovel Exclusions

Now: *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* are excluded from certain *Yovel* laws. *Kohanim Levi'im mocherim le-olam v'go'alim le-olam* — they can sell and redeem their fields at any time. *Go'alim le-olam*: normally, a seller of land may redeem it only after two years, with the redemption value calculated by the years remaining until *Yovel*. *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* may redeem immediately — you buy from a *levi* knowing he may take it back the next day. *Mocherim le-olam*: even a sale made during the Jubilee year itself — which for an ordinary Yisrael is automatically nullified — remains effective if made by a *Kohen* or *Levi*. Rashi explains this to mean that the *Kohen* or *Levi* validly receives the sale price even though the field may still revert; it is not that the sale is treated as if it never occurred.

### Partial Inclusion

Given these exclusions from *Yovel* land laws, one might conclude that *Kohanim* and *Levi'im* are sufficiently outside the *Yovel* system to be exempt from *Rosh Hashanah* *tekiot* as well. Hence the need to specify *Kohanim, Levi'im, v'Yisraelim*.

### Enough Inclusion

The *gemara* responds: *nehi d'leis lehu b'shmitat parka* — true, they may be excluded from some of the *Yovel* land laws — *b'shmitat kesafim u'b'shiluach avadim mi-is lehu* — but they remain subject to the release of debts and the freeing of slaves at *Yovel*, along with the other *Yovel* obligations. Since they are included in enough of the *Yovel* structure, they remain within the category of persons subject to *Rosh Hashanah* *tekiot* as well.

### Textual Problem

One difficulty remains with the *gemara*'s phrasing: it mentions *shmitat kesafim* — the release of debts — in the context of *Yovel*. But the release of debts is a law of *Shemitah*, the seventh year, not of *Yovel*, the fiftieth year. According to all other sources, *Yovel* does not trigger debt release. *Tosafot* addresses this difficulty, and it raises genuine questions about whether our text accurately reflects what *Yovel* entails, or whether there are alternative interpretations of the phrase. We will continue the survey of *hakol* tomorrow.
