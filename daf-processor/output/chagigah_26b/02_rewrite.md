# Chagigah 26b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Testimony & Trust

### Intro to Mishnah

The final *daf* of Masechet Chagigah opens in the middle of 26b and concludes at 27a. The passage under discussion concerns a *tanna* who enumerates various circumstances in which people are believed regarding matters of purity. The relevant line appears approximately fifteen wide lines into the page: *tanna ne'emanim al klei cheres b'Yerushalayim ne'emanim al hakodesh* — a *tanna* taught that people are believed regarding clay vessels in Jerusalem and regarding *kodesh*.

### Kodesh Belief

The Mishnah has already established that people are believed regarding *kodesh* at all times, at least in Yehuda. The passage now introduces a formulation that speaks specifically of Jerusalem rather than Yehuda, which at first appears to be a narrowing of the earlier rule — a distinction the *gemara* will go on to address.

### Terumah Pressing

Before reaching that point, the Mishnah has reviewed a series of cases of expanded trustworthiness: belief regarding *terumah* during the time of the olive and grape pressing, and belief regarding vessels associated with *terumah* for as many as seventy days leading up to that period.

### Vessels Outside

With regard to *klei cheres*, belief is extended to anyone within fifteen *mil* of Modi'in and inward toward Jerusalem. This covers a wide range of people returning or transporting objects, including those one might ordinarily be suspicious of, and reflects a deliberate policy of expanding the scope of credibility.

### Jerusalem Vessels

In Jerusalem itself, the Mishnah states *b'Yerushalayim ne'emanim al hakodesh* — people are believed regarding *kodesh* — which, as noted, appears to be more restrictive than the earlier rule about Yehuda. Within the city proper, people are believed concerning all clay vessels made for *kodesh* purposes, including the large ones (*gasin*). Outside Jerusalem, in the area extending to Modi'in, people are believed only about the smaller or thinner vessels — those in more common everyday use.

### No Kilns Logic

The reason the *gemara* provides for this expanded credibility within Jerusalem specifically is *she'ein osim kivshonot b'Yerushalayim* — kilns were not manufactured within Jerusalem itself. Because pottery had to be procured from industrial areas on the outskirts of the city, there was a greater practical necessity to trust those who brought vessels in, and policy therefore dictated a broader scope of credibility within the city walls.

### Vessel Sizes

The distinction between large and small vessels may be understood as follows: the smaller, thinner vessels are in far more common use, there is a higher demand for them, and they circulate much more widely — and so people must be trusted regarding them even at a distance from Jerusalem. The large vessels, by contrast, are less commonly needed, and it is therefore sufficient to extend credibility only within Jerusalem proper. This explanation, it should be noted, represents *sevara* — reasoned inference — of the kind that *Rashi* frequently employs when reconciling a textual difficulty, rather than an explicit received tradition.

## Regel Unity

### Festival Belief

The Mishnah continues: *u'v'she'as haregel af al haterumah* — at the time of the festival, everyone is believed even regarding *terumah*, even though it is not the season of the olive and grape pressing. This is the culminating ruling of the chapter, and indeed of the entire tractate's central concern.

One might have supposed that the policy was motivated by a desire to ensure that *kohanim* have the opportunity to receive *terumah* — a logic parallel to the rule about the pressing season, where Rashi explains that the major time of oil and wine production is also the major time of *terumah* distribution, and one does not want the *chaverim* to be excluded from receiving it. One might similarly have argued that people sometimes wait until *aliyah l'regel* to distribute *terumah*, and therefore a policy of expanded credibility was needed at that time. But the *gemara* makes clear that the reasoning here is quite different — it is far less about the practical distribution of *terumah* and far more about creating a sense of unity among *Bnei Yisrael*.

### Vaye'asof Source

The *gemara* asks *minan hanei mili* — what is the source for this rule? Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi derives it from the verse *Vaye'asof kol ish Yisrael el ha'ir k'ish echad chaverim* — all of Israel gathered to the city as one man, as *chaverim*. The *gemara* then states: *hakatuv asa'an kulan chaverim* — the Torah called them all *chaverim*, friends, giving them the legal status of a *chaver*. The point is clear: the entire purpose of the *aliyah l'regel* is the gathering together of the entire people, and that gathering generates a legal standing that applies to everyone equally.

### Unity Theme

What makes this ruling so striking is that it is not grounded in any fact pattern that would independently justify trustworthiness — unlike, say, the pressing season, where one could argue that people in fact make an effort to maintain purity during that period. Here, the basis is purely the unity of the occasion. The gathering itself transforms the legal status of every participant.

### Exemptions Review

This thematic emphasis on unity acquires additional depth when viewed against the backdrop of the opening of the tractate. Masechet Chagigah begins by delineating all those exempt from *aliyah l'regel*: non-males, those with various physical conditions, and others. In a certain sense, the beginning of the tractate is an enumeration of divisions — of who must come and who need not, and by extension, who belongs to the assembled group and who does not.

### Yerushalmi View

The Yerushalmi, however, reads the obligation of *aliyah l'regel* far more broadly. While it agrees that the exemptions apply to the obligation of bringing a *korban*, it maintains that the obligation of being *oleh l'regel* — ascending for the festival — applies universally, to men, women, and children alike. The Yerushalmi derives this from the verse concerning *Hakhel*: *ba'alot kol Yisrael leraot et penei* — when all of Israel comes up to appear before God — followed immediately by *Hakhel et ha'am, ha'anashim veha'nashim vehataf* — gather the people, men, women, and children. From this the Yerushalmi concludes that the ascent itself knows no distinctions of gender or age.

### Bavli Dispute

The Bavli, by contrast, never explicitly endorses this position, and the opening *Tosafot* in the tractate adduces several proofs from elsewhere in the Bavli suggesting that the Bavli would not agree with the Yerushalmi's reading. The exemptions recognized by the Bavli thus remain in place.

### Tanners Excluded

There is yet another dimension to the opening exemptions that bears noting. The Bavli at the outset of the tractate also excludes those engaged in extremely malodorous occupations — tanners and the like — from the obligation of *aliyah l'regel*, on the grounds that no one would wish to be in a group with them and they could not be welcomed into the assembled community.

### Contrast Structure

Here, then, is the remarkable literary and legal contrast that the tractate constructs. At the beginning: divisions, distinctions, exclusions — this person must come, that person need not; this person is welcome in the group, that person is not. At the end: the complete erasure of those distinctions. At the time of the *regel*, when hundreds of thousands of people are gathered together in Jerusalem, the stratifications of ordinary society — the division between *am ha'aretz* and *chaver*, the anxious guarding of one's vessels from unknown passersby — are entirely set aside. Rather than creating a situation in which every individual would have to guard against everyone else and verify the status of every person they encountered, *chazal* declare that the occasion itself transforms everyone into a *chaver*. The tractate thus opens with division and closes by dissolving it.

## Barrel Status

### Opening Barrel

The Mishnah now raises a consequential question about what happens after the *regel* to items that were handled during it. The case: *ha-pote'ach et chavito ve-hamatchil be'isato agav haregel* — one who opened a barrel of wine or began distributing dough during the *regel*. These items were presumably handled freely by all manner of people during the festival, with no inquiry made into anyone's status. What is the standing of those items after the *regel* is over?

This might seem an odd question — the same logic that operated during the *regel* to make everything *tahor* presumably no longer applies once it is over. But one might have wished to argue the contrary. After all, we have seen elsewhere that status can be conditional on timing: within fifteen *mil* of Modi'in one is believed, beyond that distance the very same person with the very same vessels is not believed. The same jars get closed up and reopened the following year. Similarly, during the pressing season one is believed about *terumah*; if the delivery comes late, one is not. So perhaps the principle could be extended: if the *regel* conferred *tahor* status on items, can that status not be held in suspension until the next *regel*?

### Rabbi Yehuda

Rabbi Yehuda rules *yigmor* — one may finish using the barrel after the *regel*, treating it as *tahor* throughout. The underlying logic is that one would not wish to create a situation in which the barrel becomes unusable immediately upon the conclusion of the *regel*, having been opened and distributed during it.

### Chachamim View

The *chachamim*, however, rule *lo yigmor* — one may not continue to use it after the *regel* in its current state. Once the *regel* is over, the concerns that were suspended during it reassert themselves, and the barrel must be treated as *tamei*.

### Two Sages

The *gemara* introduces a scene: Rav Ami and Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha were sitting on the porch of Rabbi Yitzchak Napcha, and one of them raised the question: *mahu sheyaniachenu leregel acher* — according to the *chachamim* that *lo yigmor*, could one simply set the barrel aside and wait for the next *regel*, at which point the same suspended credibility would operate once more? This is modeled on the practice with wine and oil pressed during *she'as hagittos*, which can evidently be held over from one pressing season to the next.

### Concern Suspension

The other replied: *yadei hakol memashmeshot bah ve'ata amarta yanichenah leregel acher* — everyone's hands have been touching it, and you suggest simply waiting for the next festival? Meaning: the very fact that it has been handled by all and sundry during the *regel* is itself the problem, and one cannot simply suspend that fact indefinitely until the next festival rolls around. The *regel* gave license to handle it; the *regel* is over; that license has expired.

### Status Change

The first responds: *atu ad idna lav yadei hakol memashmeshot bah* — during the *regel* itself, was it not equally the case that everyone's hands were touching it? And yet we suspended the concern then. So why can we not suspend it again?

The reply is incisive: *bishlama ad idna tumas am ha'aretz baregel rachmana taharah* — during the *regel*, the Torah itself rendered the *tum'ah* of an *am ha'aretz* as *tahor*. Note that the phrase *rachmana* is used here, implying the authority of the Torah, even though these stringencies and leniencies are in reality *de-rabbanan* — perhaps because the principle was felt to carry such weight that it was framed as if with the force of Torah law.

### Cannot Revert

But *ela hashta teme'ah hi* — now that the *regel* is over, the vessel has acquired the status of *tamei*. During the *regel*, it never attained that status; it was *tahor* by force of the festival's legal reality. But the moment the *regel* ends, the status of *tum'ah* takes hold. And *netamei hi* — once it becomes *tamei*, there is no mechanism for reverting it to *tahor*. A status that has been prevented from attaching can be prevented again; a status that has actually attached cannot simply be wished away.

The underlying principle is clear: one can live in a state of suspended judgment — what might be called, in literary terms, a willing suspension of disbelief — as long as the justifying framework is in place. Once that framework dissolves and the problem is formally acknowledged, it cannot be unacknowledged.

### Gittos Parallel

What makes this instructive is the contrast to the pressing season. There, the basis for credibility was not merely the existence of a legal declaration but an actual fact pattern: during *she'as hagittos*, people genuinely make efforts to maintain purity, and so there is something to rely upon. When that season ends and the next one begins, the same factual basis reasserts itself. Here, by contrast, the *tahor* status during the *regel* rested on no independent fact pattern — only the legal reality created by the festival gathering. Once the festival ends, nothing remains to support a renewed suspension of concern.

### Tannaim Debate

The *gemara* then asks whether this dispute maps onto a *tannaitic* disagreement. One *baraita* states *yanichenu leregel acher* — one may leave it for the next *regel* — while another states *lo yanichenu leregel acher* — one may not. Is this then a dispute of *tanna'im*?

The *gemara* declines to read it that way. The *baraita* that says *yanichenu leregel acher* represents Rabbi Yehuda — who holds *yigmor*, that one may finish immediately — and *yanichenu* means one does not even need to wait; one may simply continue. The *baraita* that says *lo yanichenu* represents the *rabbanan* — and means one need not leave it to the next *regel* because it is already invalidated. Alternatively the *gemara* reverses the attribution and reads the terms accordingly. Either way, one opinion does permit leaving it for the next *regel*, paralleling the logic of the pressing season, while the other holds that no such option exists — a genuine dispute about how far the analogy to *she'as hagittos* extends.

## Vessel Immersion

### After Festival

The Mishnah now turns to the vessels of the Temple courtyard. *Mishe'avar haregel ma'avirin al taharat azarah* — once the *regel* is over, all the vessels in the *azarah* are taken out and immersed. The assumption embedded in this ruling is itself striking: the vessels of the *Beit HaMikdash* became *tamei* during the *regel*. How that happened is a question the *gemara* will shortly address. For the moment, the Mishnah states the remedy: post-festival immersion of all the *keilim*.

### Friday Exception

If the *regel* concluded on a Friday, the immersion is not performed — *lo ma'avirin mipnei kavod Shabbat*. There is insufficient time to immerse all the vessels and still allow the *kohanim* to prepare properly for *Shabbat*. The concern for *Shabbat* takes precedence over the immediate restoration of *tahorah* to the *keilim*.

### Thursday Exception

Rabbi Yehuda goes further: *af lo beyom hamishi* — even if the *regel* ended on Thursday, the immersion is not performed, because the *kohanim* are still occupied — *she'ein Kohanim penuyim* — and cannot devote themselves to the task while also preparing for *Shabbat*.

### Ash Removal

Rashi provides an additional reason for Thursday's exception. He explains that *motzei regel* — the conclusion of the festival — requires the *kohanim* to remove the enormous accumulation of ash that had built up on the altar throughout the festival days. The volume of *korbanos* offered over the entire *regel* produces a massive quantity of ash, and clearing it is a major undertaking that consumes much of the day following the festival's conclusion. By the time Thursday's ash removal is complete, Friday has effectively arrived, and *Shabbat* preparations must begin. Only from Sunday would the immersion of the vessels take place.

The practical implication is significant: the concern for the vessels' *tum'ah* is suspended not only during the *regel* itself but potentially for two or three additional days afterward — Thursday, Friday, and *Shabbat* — until there is an opportunity to immerse them. The willingness to extend this suspension demonstrates that the underlying principle is not mere tolerance of an emergency but a deliberate and considered policy.

## Temple Access

### Table Warning

The Mishnah records: *hiza'aru shelo tig'u bashulchan* — people were warned during the *regel* to be careful not to touch the *shulchan*, the Table of Showbread. The reason, as the *gemara* explains, is that the *shulchan* cannot be immersed: the verse states *lechem panim lefanai tamid* — the showbread must always remain before God — and therefore the *shulchan* must never be moved far enough to be immersed in a *mikveh*. All other vessels in the *Mikdash* had backup duplicates that could substitute if the originals became *tamei*, and they could all be immersed. The *shulchan* alone had no such option.

### Access Question

This warning raises an obvious and pressing question: who is being warned, and how could anyone in a position to touch the *shulchan* even come into contact with it? The *shulchan* is located not in the *azarah* — the Temple courtyard — but inside the *Heichal*, the inner sanctuary. Under normal circumstances, the *Bnei Yisrael* were permitted only within approximately eleven *amot* of the *azarah*, up to the *Sha'ar Nikanor*. Beyond that lay the *Ezras Kohanim*, accessible generally only to *kohanim*, and beyond that the *Heichal* itself, entered only by the very few *kohanim* assigned to specific tasks — lighting the *menorah*, burning the *ketores*, arranging the showbread on *Shabbat*. The number of people who would ordinarily enter the *Heichal* in any given day is extremely small.

Rashi explains that the warning was directed specifically at *Kohanim am ha'aretz* — *kohanim* who, despite their priestly lineage, did not observe the purity standards of a *chaver*. This is at least a plausible interpretation: during the *regel*, it seems that even less scrupulous *kohanim* were given access to perform certain functions, and the warning was to ensure that they not touch the *shulchan* specifically.

### Table vs Menorah

The *gemara* notes that a parallel *baraita* warns against touching not only the *shulchan* but also the *menorah*: *hiza'aru shema tiga bashulchan uvimenorah*. Yet the *tanna* of the Mishnah mentions only the *shulchan*. Why?

The *gemara* explains: the *shulchan* carries the requirement of *tamid* — it says *lechem panim lefanai tamid*, always before God — and therefore it can never be removed for immersion. The *menorah*, by contrast, does have the word *tamid* associated with it — *leha'alos ner tamid* — but Rashi clarifies that this *tamid* refers only to the nighttime burning: the *menorah* is to be burning from evening to evening, not continuously through the day. Since the *menorah* does not have a round-the-clock *tamid* requirement, it can in principle be removed and immersed during the daytime.

### Tamei Question

The *gemara* then poses a sharp question. If the concern about touching the *shulchan* is that it cannot be immersed afterward, why is this a problem at all? The *shulchan* is a wooden vessel. And any wooden vessel that is *asuy lenachas* — made for remaining in its place — does not become *tamei*. The *gemara* derives this from the Torah's discussion of the *sak* (sack): just as a *sak* can be carried both empty and full, so too only vessels that can be carried both empty and full are susceptible to *tum'ah*. Something permanently fixed in place, like a large piece of furniture that stays where it is, is regarded as attached to the ground and is not subject to *tum'ah* in the first place.

### Wood Status

The *gemara* draws the parallel explicitly to the *shulchan*: precisely because it must remain *tamid* in its place, it should be considered *asuy lenachas* and immune from *tum'ah*. If so, why the warning against touching it?

One might illustrate the principle of *asuy lenachas* with the example of a large armoire or wardrobe that in theory could be moved but in practice always remains fixed — such an object is considered part of its surroundings rather than a portable vessel, and does not become *tamei*.

So the *gemara* asks: if the *shulchan* is permanently fixed by Torah law, it should be *asuy lenachas* and immune. The answer, as *Reish Lakish* teaches, is that the verse itself says *al hashulchan hatahor* — on the *tahor shulchan* — implying that it is *possible* for the *shulchan* to be *tamei*. For if it were intrinsically and permanently *tahor* by virtue of being immovable, there would be no need to call it *tahor*. The Torah's specification of *tahor* demonstrates that *tum'ah* is conceptually possible here.

The *gemara* explains why: *she'mağbihim oto umar'in bo le'olei regalim* — they lift it up and show it to the pilgrims. The *shulchan* is indeed moved — though perhaps only raised slightly in place — in order to display the *lechem hapanim* to those who have come up for the *regel*.

## Table Display

### Showing Purpose

The practice of lifting the *shulchan* is not merely a technical movement that incidentally renders it susceptible to *tum'ah*. It is a deliberate act of *harabim* — showing the people. The *kohanim* would lift up the table and say to those assembled: *re'u chibbaschem lifnei hamakom* — see how beloved you are before God.

### Bread Miracle

The proof of that love was manifest in the *lechem hapanim* itself. The showbread was replaced every *Shabbat*, yet when removed it was as fresh and warm as when it had been placed there a week earlier. *Siluko keshidduru* — its removal was like its arrangement. The verse is adduced: *lasum lechem cham beyom hilakcho* — to place warm bread on the day of its removal — indicating that the bread was warm both when placed and when taken away.

### Yehoshua Tradition

Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi remarks: *nes gadol na'asah belechem hapanim* — a great miracle was performed with the showbread — *kesidduru kach siluko*, that just as it was when arranged, so it was when removed, warm and fresh after a full week.

### Curtain Access

The display of the *shulchan* is one manifestation of a broader practice on the *regel* of granting the people extraordinary access to the inner spaces of the *Mikdash*. The *gemara* indicates that on the *regel*, the curtains separating the *ulam*, the *Heichal*, and even the *Kodesh Kodashim* were drawn aside, so that a clear line of sight extended from the *Sha'ar Nikanor* through to the innermost sanctum. This is a striking practice: the *Kodesh Kodashim* was accessible, in the normal course of things, only to the *Kohen Gadol* on *Yom Kippur*. Yet on the *regel*, the *gemara* states that the curtains were parted and the people could see in. Exactly how they could see past the *mizbei'ach* — given the dispute regarding its precise position — is a separate question, but the *gemara*'s direction is clear.

Rashi's reading of the warning *hiza'aru* — *leKohanim am ha'aretz*, directed at the *kohanim* — suggests that even *kohanim* of lesser standing were given entry during the *regel*, making the warning against touching the *shulchan* practically necessary. The deeper implication of all this is not merely logistical. The *regel* was about *re'iyah* — appearing before God — but the *peshat* of *yera'eh kol zechurcha et penei* moves in both directions: not only appearing, but seeing. The people ascending for the *regel* wanted not only to bring their *korbanos* but to encounter God in some direct sense. And what the *chachamim* arranged was precisely that: the curtains pulled aside, the *menorah* and *shulchan* brought into view, the inner sanctum made as accessible as it could possibly be. The *Tzeddukim* were reportedly opposed to all of this; it was very much the agenda of the *chachamim* and the *Perushim* to make the *Mikdash* accessible to the people.

### Succos Access

On *Succos*, a parallel phenomenon occurs: the *hakafah* of the *aravos* is performed by *Khal Yisrael*, and the plain reading of the Mishnah suggests that the people actually entered the space between the *ulam* and the *mizbei'ach* — an area normally off-limits even to *kohanim* except when necessary. The picture that emerges across all of these practices is consistent: on the *regel*, *klal Yisrael* is brought into a proximity to the sacred that they would never ordinarily be permitted, and the sacred is brought out toward them. This is precisely why the immersion of vessels after the *regel* was necessary: the people had genuine physical contact with the *keilim* of the *Mikdash*, and the concern about *tum'ah* was real.

## Altar Coating

### Backups Rule

The Mishnah states: *kol hakeilim shehayu bamikdash yesh lahem sheniyim ushlishiyim* — all the vessels in the *Mikdash* had second and third backups. *She'im nitme'u harishonim yavi'u sheniyim tachteihem* — if the first set became *tamei*, the second set would be brought in its place. *Kol hakeilim shehayu bamikdash te'unim tevilah* — all the vessels required immersion if they became *tamei* — *chutz mimizbeach hazahav umizbeach hanechoshet* — except for the gold altar and the copper altar. These altars did not become *tamei* because they are considered as if attached to the ground.

Rabbi Eliezer holds that this is because they are literally *ke-karka* — like the ground — while the *chachamim* say it is because they are coated (*metzupin*). This second reason is puzzling at first glance, since a metal coating would ordinarily be expected to make a vessel *more* susceptible to *tum'ah*, not less. The resolution is deferred to the continuation of the *gemara*.

---

## Table Metaphor

### Altar-Table Link

The *gemara* takes note of a remarkable feature of a verse in *Yechezkel* (chapter 41): the passage opens discussing the *mizbei'ach* — calling it *etz*, wood — and closes by saying *zeh hashulchan asher lifnei Hashem* — this is the table that is before God. Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish both ask: *patach bamizbe'ach vesiyem bashulchan* — why does the verse open with the altar and conclude with the table? And from this juxtaposition, the *gemara* derives that the Torah itself classifies the *shulchan* as a wooden vessel — *rachmana karyei etz* — giving it the legal status of wood regardless of its gold coating. This resolves the earlier difficulty about why the *shulchan* does not simply become *tamei* as a metal vessel.

### Atonement Transfer

But Rabbi Yochanan and Reish Lakish draw a further lesson from this verse, and here the *gemara* transitions into *aggadah*. *Bizman shebeit hamikdash kayam mizbei'ach mechaper al adam* — when the *Beit HaMikdash* stood, the altar atoned for a person. *Achshav shulchano shel adam mechaper alav* — now, a person's own table atones for him.

### Domestic Sanctity

What does this mean? Rashi says *behachnasat orchim* — through welcoming guests. But the idea connects to a broader theme that has run throughout the tractate. The levels of purity associated with *kodesh*, *terumah*, and *chullin* — and all the attendant social divisions between *am ha'aretz* and *chaver* — were tied to the world of the *Mikdash* and *korbanos*. That system, with all its hierarchy, was also what created the divisions within *Bnei Yisrael* that the *regel* worked so hard to temporarily dissolve.

Now that there is no *Beit HaMikdash*, the *kedushah* of eating is expressed through *brachot*, through washing of hands, through the conscious sanctification of the meal — practices available to every Jew regardless of status or learning. The table in one's home becomes the *mizbei'ach*, and the access to atonement that was once mediated through the *keilim* of the *Mikdash* is now built into the domestic life of every Jew. This is the *gemara*'s closing vision: not a world of stratification and hierarchy, but one in which the sacred has migrated into the home and become the common possession of the entire people.

## Altar Coating (II)

### Copper Altar

Returning to the *halachic* discussion: the *gemara* explains that the copper altar — the *mizbeach hanechoshet* — is not susceptible to *tum'ah* because the verse says *mizbeach adama ta'ase li* — make for Me an altar of earth. Despite being constructed of metal, it is called *adama*, earth, and is thus treated as permanently attached to the ground.

### Gold Altar

The gold altar — the *mizbeach hazahav* — is the inner altar, and is considerably more portable than the outer one. Yet the *gemara* derives that it too is treated as connected to the ground, through a *hekkesh*: the verse places the *menorah* and the *mizbechot* in the same phrase, linking the altars to one another. Since the copper altar is *ke-karka*, the gold altar is similarly treated.

### Eliezer vs Chachamim

Rabbi Eliezer's reasoning — *ke-karka*, like the ground — is thus supported by explicit Biblical derivation for both altars. The *chachamim*, however, say the reason is *mipnei she'hem metzupin* — because they are coated. The *gemara* presses the difficulty: surely a metal coating should make the altar *more* susceptible to *tum'ah*, not less? The *gemara* considers reading the *chachamim*'s statement as meaning the altars *are* susceptible to *tum'ah* precisely *because* of the coating — but this would create the awkward implication that one would need to immerse a *mizbei'ach*, which is hardly practical.

### Torah Classification

The *gemara* resolves the difficulty by suggesting that even if one were to argue that coating with metal should confer the status of a metal vessel, the Torah itself intervenes. As established above from the verse in *Yechezkel*, the Torah calls the altar *etz* — wood. And from the juxtaposition of *mizbei'ach* and *shulchan* in that same verse, the same classification extends to the *shulchan*. Both are Biblically designated as wooden vessels, and a wooden vessel that is *asuy lenachas* — made for remaining in place — does not become *tamei*, regardless of any metal coating.

## Closing Aggadah

### Scholars Protected

The tractate closes with *aggadah*. Rabbi Abbahu says in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *talmidei chakhamim ein or shel Gehinnom sholetet bahem* — the fire of *Gehinnom* has no dominion over Torah scholars.

### Salamander Proof

The proof is a *kal va-chomer* from the salamander. The ancients believed that the salamander, a creature born from fire, possessed blood with fireproofing properties: *umah salamandra she-toldot esh hi, ve-hasach midama ein or sholetet bo* — the salamander comes from fire, and one who coats himself with its blood is not harmed by fire. If the product of fire can confer protection from fire, how much more so a Torah scholar, *she-kol gufo esh* — whose entire being is fire — will certainly be protected from *Gehinnom*.

### Altar Miracle

A second *kal va-chomer* follows, and this one connects directly to the *halachic* discussion that preceded it. *Mah mizbeach hazahav she-ein alav ela ke-ovya dinar zahav* — the gold altar had only the thinnest layer of gold upon it, perhaps the thickness of a single *dinar* — yet *kamma shanim ein esh sholetet bo* — for all those years, despite the constant fire burning upon it, the coating was never damaged. The fire had no dominion over it.

### Sinners Mitzvos

*Poshei Yisrael she-melei'im mitzvos ke-rimmon* — the sinners of Israel who are filled with *mitzvos* like a pomegranate — *al achas kamma ve-kamma* — how much more so will the fires of *Gehinnom* have no dominion over them.

### Empty Full

The verse is adduced: *ke-felach ha-rimmon rakkatekh* — like a slice of pomegranate are your temples. The *gemara* offers a *midrashic* reading: *al tikrei rakkatekh ela reikanim she-bach* — do not read *rakkatekh* (your temples) but *reikanim she-bach* (the empty ones among you). Even those who appear empty, even those who seem to lack merit — they are filled with *mitzvos* like the seeds of a pomegranate.

### Conclusion

*Hadran alach chomer bakodesh, ve-salika lach Maseches Chagigah* — and with this, Masechet Chagigah is complete, and with it, *Seder Moed*.

It is worth pausing on the note on which the tractate ends. The closing *aggadah* returns us to the theme of Torah — *talmidei chakhamim*, the fire of Torah that suffuses the scholar — and to *mitzvos*, which fill even the seemingly empty. Earlier in the tractate, the transition from *aliyah l'regel* to *oleh lehakbil penei rabbo baregel* — ascending to greet one's teacher on the festival — was significant: the ascent to greet a Torah teacher confers a form of the festival's spiritual encounter that is available to anyone, regardless of physical ability or social station. Here, too, the closing note is one of universality: *mitzvos* are available to all, the table in every home is a *mizbei'ach*, and even those who appear furthest from sanctity are filled with the seeds of commandment. The tractate that began with categories of exclusion — who must ascend, who is exempted, who cannot join the group — ends by insisting on the fullness of every member of *Klal Yisrael*, and the accessibility of the sacred to each of them.
