# Nedarim 49 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Lashon & Cooking Terms

### Perek Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַמְבוּשָּׁל — מוּתָּר בְּצָלִי וּבְשָׁלוּק. אָמַר: "קֻוֽנָּם תַּבְשִׁיל שֶׁאֵינִי טוֹעֵם" — אָסוּר בְּמַעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה רַךְ, וּמוּתָּר בְּעָבֶה, וּמוּתָּר בְּבֵיצָה טוֹרְמוֹטָא וּבַדַּלַּעַת הָרְמוּצָה. הַנּוֹדֵר מִמַּעֲשֵׂה קְדֵרָה — אֵין אָסוּר אֶלָּא מִמַּעֲשֵׂה רְתַחְתָּה, אָמַר: "קֻוֽנָּם הַיּוֹרֵד לִקְדֵרָה שֶׁאֵינִי טוֹעֵם — אָסוּר בְּכׇל הַמִּתְבַּשְּׁלִין בִּקְדֵרָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **one who vows** that **cooked foods** are forbidden to him, he is **permitted** to eat **roasted and boiled** foods, as they are not defined as cooked. If one **said: Cooked** food **is *konam*** for me, and for **that** reason **I will not taste** it, he is **prohibited from** tasting **a loose cooked** food **but** is **permitted to** taste **a thick** one, which people do not generally refer to as a cooked food. **And** he is likewise **permitted to** eat **a *turemuta* egg and the *remutza* gourd,** as they are not considered cooked foods either. In the case of **one who vows** that food **cooked in a dish** is forbidden to him, he is **prohibited** from deriving benefit **only from** food that is **cooked by boiling** it in a dish. However, if **one said: That which enters into a dish is *konam*** for me, and for **that** reason **I will not taste** it, he is **prohibited** from tasting **anything cooked in a dish.**

The sixth *perek* of *Nedarim*, *Hanoder Min Hamvushal*, is organized around a central preoccupation: the role of *lashon* — word choice — in determining the scope of a *neder*. The *perek* demonstrates repeatedly that the precise term a person selects when taking a vow is not merely stylistic but legally determinative. Sometimes the difference lies between a noun and a verb, sometimes between a more general and a more specific term, and in each case the halakhic outcome differs accordingly.

### Mvushal Prohibition

The *mishna* opens: *Hanoder min hamvushal* — if a person takes a *neder* not to eat cooked food — *mutar bitzali uvshaluk*, he is permitted to eat roasted or stewed food. The prohibition is specific: *mvushal* sounds precisely like boiled, and therefore no other method of preparation is included. Those familiar with the *mishna* in *Pesachim* at the tenth *perek* will recognize these cooking terms. The *mishna* there notes that on all other nights one may eat *basar tzali, shaluk, umvushal* — roasted, stewed, or boiled meat — whereas on the night of the *Korban Pesach* only roasted meat is permitted. That context will return shortly in the *sugya*.

### Tavshil Term

The *mishna* then introduces a second case: *amar konam tavshil she-eini to'em* — if a person says a *konam* that he will not taste a *tavshil*, a dish. The word *tavshil*, while derived from the same root as *mvushal*, refers to a broader category — a prepared dish, a side dish — rather than specifically boiled food. The *mishna* rules that such a person is *asur b'maaseh kederah rach*, forbidden to eat a soft, liquidy pot dish. However, he is *mutar b'aveh*, permitted to eat a very thick preparation of the same kind, since that would not qualify as a *tavshil*. The *gemara* will explain that the word *tavshil* denotes specifically something eaten together with bread; a thick *maaseh kederah* is eaten on its own and therefore falls outside the *neder*'s scope. We also see that a person who says *konam tavshil she-eini to'em* is permitted *b'veitzat turmusa* — a type of prepared egg — and *b'idlat hamrimutza*, a variety of stewed squash or pumpkin.

### Maaseh Kederah

The *mishna* continues: *Hanoder mimaaseh kederah* — if a person takes a *neder* specifically against *maaseh kederah* — *ein oser ela b'maaseh ritachta*, he is forbidden only from something actually boiled in a pot. *Tavshil* is a broader category that includes *maaseh kederah* as a subcategory; *maaseh kederah* is the narrower term. The practical hierarchy becomes clear: the more general term encompasses the more specific, and the person's chosen *lashon* determines which tier of the hierarchy is activated.

### Method vs. Type

A further case: *amar konam hayored lekederah she-eini to'em*, if a person says he will not eat anything that "goes into a pot." Here the focus shifts from the type of dish to the method of preparation — *asur bechol hamitbashlim b'kederah*, he is forbidden from anything cooked in a pot. The distinction is between saying, for instance, that one will not eat stew (a category of food) versus saying one will not eat anything prepared in a pot (a method). These overlap substantially in vocabulary, which is precisely what the *mishna* is working through.

### Stam Nedarim Rule

The broader principle underlying this *perek* is *stam nedarim le'hachmir*: when a person takes a *neder* without specifying what it covers, we interpret it inclusively and stringently. Once a person does specify, however, we take their words seriously on their own terms. Importantly, the specification need not come immediately — even if someone later comes to ask whether the *neder* covers a particular food, the person's explanation at that point is credited. Both the rule of stringency in ambiguous vows and the weight given to the person's own stated intent reflect the fundamental principle that *nedarim* track what a person actually means by the words they say.

## Oshaya Dispute

### Oshaya Forbids

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תַּנְיָא: רַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה אוֹסֵר, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין רְאָיָה לְדָבָר — זֵכֶר לַדָּבָר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיְבַשְּׁלוּ אֶת הַפֶּסַח בָּאֵשׁ כַּמִּשְׁפָּט".
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of one who vowed that cooked foods are forbidden to him, **Rabbi Yoshiya** maintains that he is **prohibited** from eating roasted foods. **And although there is no** biblical **proof of the matter, there is an allusion to the matter, as it is stated: "And they cooked the Paschal offering with fire according to the ordinance"** (II Chronicles 35:13). Since the Paschal offering must be roasted, it is evident that roasting can also be referred to as cooking.

The *gemara* now brings a *baraita*: *Tanya Rabi Oshaya oser* — Rabbi Oshaya forbids what the *tana kamma* of the *mishna* permitted. Recall that the opening line of the *mishna* permitted roasted and stewed food to someone who took a *neder* against *mvushal*. Rabbi Oshaya disagrees and holds that roasted and stewed meat are included in the prohibition as well. He acknowledges, however, that *af al pi she-ein raya l'davar*, he does not have a definitive proof — only *zecher l'davar*, a mnemonic support or allusion, drawn from the thirty-fifth chapter of *Divrei HaYamim* (2 Chronicles).

### Pesach Korban

The verse cited is *vayivashlu et ha-Pesach ba-esh kamishpat* — they "cooked" the *Pesach* offering in fire according to its law. This cannot mean they boiled it, since the *mitzvah* of the *Korban Pesach* requires roasting. The negative commandment is explicit: *lo tochlu mimenu na u-vashel mevushal ba-mayim* — do not eat it raw or boiled in water — and from this we derive *ki im tzali esh*, it must be roasted on fire. Yet the verse in *Divrei HaYamim* uses the word *vayivashlu*. The inference Rabbi Oshaya draws is that the root *b-sh-l* can function as a general term for cooking, encompassing roasting as well as boiling. Therefore, a *neder* against *mvushal* might include roasted food as well, since the word *mevushal* in its broader register means simply "cooked."

### Rabi Akiva Eiger

A sharp question appears in the *Gilyon HaShas* of Rabbi Akiva Eiger. He notes that there is a verse from *Parshat Re'eh* in *Devarim* that uses similar language in connection with the *Korban Pesach* — *u-vishalta ve-achalta* — you shall "cook" and eat it. This verse is in the *Torah* itself and makes the same point as the verse from *Divrei HaYamim*. Rabbi Akiva Eiger asks: why did Rabbi Oshaya quote from *Divrei HaYamim* rather than from the *Torah* directly? This question has also attracted attention from those who approach *Tanach* with a source-critical eye, since the usage of *levashel* in *Devarim* in connection with the *Korban Pesach* — which elsewhere clearly requires roasting — has been flagged as an apparent contradiction. The resolution offered by later commentators is that *Devarim* there is addressing not only the *Korban Pesach* (which must be from *tzon*, sheep) but also the *Korban Chagigah* that accompanies it, which may include *bakar* (cattle) and is a *Korban Shelamim* that would be boiled. Alternatively, the word *levashel* in all these contexts simply functions as the general term for cooking, precisely the point Rabbi Oshaya is making.

### Torah vs. Colloquial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא בְּהָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי, דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה סָבַר: הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן תּוֹרָה, וְתַנָּא דִילַן סָבַר: בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that they disagree with regard to this** following principle, **that Rabbi Yoshiya holds** that one should **follow the language of** the **Torah, and our *tanna* holds** that **with regard to vows** one should **follow the language of people.**

The *gemara* initially frames the dispute in terms of a fundamental question about *nedarim*: *leima b'ha kamiflagi* — perhaps their disagreement turns on whether *nedarim* follow *lashon Torah* or *lashon bnei adam*. On this reading, Rabbi Oshaya holds *halach achar lashon Torah* — the *halachic* meaning of a word is determined by how it is used in the *Torah* — and since the *Torah* uses *levashel* to encompass roasting, a *neder* against *mevushal* would include roasted food. The *tana kamma*, by contrast, holds *b'nedarim halach achar lashon bnei adam* — in *nedarim* we follow colloquial usage, and in ordinary speech *mevushal* means specifically boiled. This connects to an earlier debate in the *masechta* regarding *kinuyei nedarim* — whether words like *konam, konas*, and similar terms are artificial constructions invented by the rabbis so that people would not pronounce the sacred terms *neder* or *korban* lightly, or whether they are simply foreign loan words absorbed into ordinary speech. The deeper question is whether *nedarim* as a *halachic* category is created by the *Torah* and then applied to human behavior, or whether the *Torah* is recognizing and giving structure to something people already do naturally. If the latter, then what matters is what words mean to the person saying them.

### Everyone Agrees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא בִּנְדָרִים הַלֵּךְ אַחַר לְשׁוֹן בְּנֵי אָדָם, מָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ וּמָר כִּי אַתְרֵיהּ. בְּאַתְרָא דְּתַנָּא דִילַן — לְצָלִי קָרוּ לֵיהּ צָלִי, וְלִמְבוּשָּׁל קָרוּ לֵיהּ מְבוּשָּׁל. בְּאַתְרָא דְּרַבִּי יֹאשִׁיָּה — אֲפִילּוּ צָלִי קָרוּ מְבוּשָּׁל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this suggestion: **No,** it is possible **that everyone** agrees that **with regard to vows** one should **follow the language of people.** Rather, this **Sage** stated his opinion **in accordance with** the language of **his locale, and** this **Sage** stated his opinion **in accordance with** the language of **his locale. In the locale of our *tanna*, roasted food is called roasted and cooked food is called cooked,** and **in the locale of Rabbi Yoshiya even roasted food is called cooked.**

The *gemara* then reverses course: *la, dekulei alma b'nedarim halach achar lashon bnei adam* — in fact, everyone agrees that *nedarim* follow colloquial usage. Even Rabbi Oshaya does not hold *lashon Torah*. The true explanation is *mar ki atra umar ki atra* — each authority was following the way the word *mevushal* was actually used in his own locale. In Rabbi Oshaya's region, *mevushal* functioned as a general term for cooking — much as the word is used in modern Hebrew today — and therefore a *neder* against *mevushal* would include roasted food. In the locale of the *tana kamma*, the word *mevushal* meant specifically boiled, and so only boiling was prohibited. Both are right; they simply live in different places with different linguistic conventions.

### Regional Variation

The *gemara* spells this out: *be'atra de-Tana dilan, letzali karu lei tzali umevushal karu lei mevushal* — in the place of the *tana* of our *mishna*, roasted was called roasted and cooked was called cooked, each term having a distinct referent. *Be'atra de-Rabbi Oshaya, afilu tzali karu mevushal* — in Rabbi Oshaya's locale, even roasted food was called *mevushal*, cooked. The citation from *Divrei HaYamim* is therefore *asmachta be'alma* — nothing more than an *asmachta*. And indeed, Rabbi Oshaya himself signaled as much when he said *af al pi she-ein raya l'davar, zecher l'davar* — he explicitly disclaimed having a genuine proof.

### Asmachta Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קְרָא נָסֵיב לַהּ! אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: **But doesn't** Rabbi Yoshiya **cite a verse** as proof for his opinion, which shows that his opinion is not based on the vernacular? The Gemara answers: The verse is cited as **a mere support** for his opinion, as implied by his statement that the verse is only an allusion, not a full proof.

This raises a broader question: what is the function of *asmachta* in rabbinic literature? We know that some *mitzvot* are *d'oraita* and learned from *pesukim*, and others are *d'rabbanan*, introduced by the rabbis themselves. Why, then, do the rabbis sometimes attach their own *halachot* to biblical verses by way of *asmachta*, as though providing a scriptural anchor for what is ultimately a rabbinic idea? A significant answer is offered by the *Ritva* in his commentary to *Masechet Rosh Hashanah*, where the question of *asmachta* comes up in connection with whether *zichronot, malchuyot, ve-shofarot* on *Rosh Hashanah* might be *d'oraita*. The *Ritva* explains that *asmachta* is not wordplay or a rhetorical game. Rather, it reflects the essential relationship between *Torah she-bi-khtav* and *Torah she-be-al peh*: God embedded in the words of the written *Torah* the capacity to give rise to the oral *Torah*. When the rabbis find a hook for a rabbinic concept in a biblical text, that *asmachta* demonstrates the *Torah she-bi-khtav*'s *pluripotency* — its inherent capacity to generate and support the *Torah she-be-al peh* as well.

## Tavshil Definition

### Bread Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קֻוֽנָּם תַּבְשִׁיל כּוּ׳. וְהָא מִתַּבְשִׁיל נְדַר! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַאי תַּנָּא כֹּל מִידֵּי דְּמִתְאֲכֵל בֵּיהּ רִיפְתָּא — תַּבְשִׁיל קָרוּ לֵיהּ. וְהָתַנְיָא: הַנּוֹדֵר מִן הַתַּבְשִׁיל — אָסוּר בְּכׇל מִינֵי תַּבְשִׁיל, וְאָסוּר בְּצָלִי וּבְשָׁלוּק וּבִמְבוּשָּׁל, וְאָסוּר בְּהִיטְרִיּוֹת רַכּוֹת שֶׁהַחוֹלִין אוֹכְלִין בָּהֶן פִּיתָּן.
>
> **Translation:** It is stated in the mishna that one who said: **A cooked** food **is *konam*** for me and for that reason I will not taste it, he is prohibited from tasting a loose cooked food but is permitted to taste a thick one. The Gemara asks: Why is he permitted to eat it? **But didn't he vow** that **a cooked** food, which includes one that is thick, is forbidden **to** him? **Abaye said: This *tanna*** maintains that **anything with which bread is eaten is called a cooked** food, whereas a thick dish is eaten without bread. **And it is taught** likewise in a *baraita*: **One who vows** that **a cooked** food is forbidden **to** him is **prohibited from** eating **all types of cooked** foods, **and** he is therefore **prohibited from** eating **roasted, boiled, and cooked** foods. **And he is** also **prohibited from** eating **soft gourds [*hiteriyyot*], with which the sick eat their bread.** This indicates that a cooked food is one eaten with bread.

The *mishna*'s second case — *konam tavshil she-eini to'em* — now receives direct attention from the *gemara*. The *gemara* asks: *ha mitavshil nadar*, didn't he take a *neder* against *tavshil* — why then does the ruling not simply prohibit all cooked foods? *Amar Abbaye: hai tanna kol midei de-mitachal b'rifta tavshil karu lei* — this *tanna* holds that the word *tavshil* denotes anything eaten together with bread. It does not mean any cooked food whatsoever; it means specifically a side dish consumed alongside bread.

### Hamotzi Implications

This definition has direct *halakhic* implications. The accepted rule is that the *brachah* on *pat* — on bread — is *poteret ha-tavshil*, it exempts the recitation of a separate *brachah* on the side dishes. If *tavshil* means specifically something eaten together with and alongside the bread — not merely anything served at a meal where bread is also present — then the scope of the *hamotzi* exemption may be narrower than commonly assumed. The word *kol midei de-mitachal b'rifta* suggests something you actually eat the bread with or on — something you dip your bread into, spread on your bread, or eat bite by bite along with the bread. Questions follow naturally: does the *hamotzi* exemption extend to fruit served as a salad course, to dessert, to different kinds of drinks? These are questions the *poskim* address at length. It is worth noting that Rav Aharon Lichtenstein *zecher tzadik livrachah* was reportedly careful enough about this issue that on one occasion, when a melon was served as a salad course, he placed a piece of it on a roll specifically to bring it within the category of something eaten together with bread.

### Tosefta View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה חֲלַשׁ, עַל לְגַבֵּיהּ הָהוּא אָסְיָא לְאַסּוֹיֵהּ, חֲזָא קַרָא דְּמַחֵת בְּבֵיתֵיהּ, שַׁבְקֵיהּ וּנְפַק. אֲמַר: מַלְאַךְ מוֹתָא אִית לֵיהּ לְדֵין בְּבֵיתֵיהּ, וַאֲנָא אֵיעוּל לְאַסָּאָה יָתֵיהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that the sick eat gourds? **But Rabbi Yirmeya fell ill,** and **a certain doctor came to him to heal him. He saw a gourd that was placed in his house,** and **he left** Rabbi Yirmeya **and exited** the house and **said: This** person **has an angel of death,** a gourd, **in his house, and I will enter to heal him?** This incident teaches that gourds are detrimental for the sick.

The *gemara* now raises a contradiction from the *Tosefta*: *vehanoder min hatavshil asur bechol minei tavshil* — a person who takes a *neder* against *tavshil* is forbidden from every kind of *tavshil* — *veasur betzali uveshaluk umevushal*, he is forbidden from roasted, stewed, and boiled meat — *veasur behitriyot rakot*, and he is forbidden from soft gourds or squashes, *shehacholim ochlin bahen pitam*, which sick people eat with their bread. This *Tosefta* appears broadly inclusive, treating *tavshil* as a wide category that encompasses roasted meat and soft gourds alike — which sets up the discussion that follows.

## Gourds & Health

### Rabbi Yirmiyah Story

The *Tosefta*'s final line — that sick people eat soft gourds with their bread — raises an immediate question: is this actually medically sound advice? *Eini, veha Rabbi Yirmiyah chalash* — is it not so that when Rabbi Yirmiyah fell ill, *al legabeih hahu asya leasuyei*, a certain doctor came to treat him? *Chaza kara demanach beveiteih, shakil venafak* — the doctor saw a gourd lying in the house, was alarmed, and left. The doctor's reaction seems to contradict the *Tosefta*'s implication that gourds are appropriate food for the sick. One commentator — the *Rashash* — notes that this is a peculiar way to practice medicine: if a patient is eating something harmful, you ought to advise him rather than simply walk out. However that may be, the doctor's statement upon departing was: *amar malach hamavet it lei ledein beveiteih, veana eiul leasuyei yateih* — the angel of death is in this man's house, and I am supposed to heal him? The presence of the gourd in the house was so alarming to the physician that he regarded the healing effort as futile.

### Soft vs. Hard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּרַכִּיכֵי, הָא — בְּאַשּׁוּנֵי. רָבָא בַּר עוּלָּא אָמַר: הָא — בְּקַרָא גּוּפֵיהּ, וְהָא — בְּגַוֵּויהּ דִּקְרָא. דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: לוּלִיבָּא דְקַרָא בְּסִילְקָא, לוּלִיבָּא דְכִיתָּנָא בְּכוּתָּחָא. וְדָבָר זֶה אָסוּר לְאוֹמְרוֹ בִּפְנֵי עַם הָאָרֶץ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: This is **not difficult. This** *baraita* is referring **to soft** gourds, which are beneficial to the sick, and **this** incident is referring **to hard** ones, which are detrimental. **Rava bar Ulla said** a different answer: **This** incident is referring **to the gourd itself,** which is detrimental, **and this** *baraita* is referring **to the innards [*luliva*] of the gourd, as Rav Yehuda said: The innards of the gourd** are best eaten **with chard; the innards of flax** seeds are best eaten **with *kuteḥa*,** a dip made from bread crumbs and sour milk. **And one may not say this matter in the presence of an ignoramus,** so that ignoramuses do not uproot flax for consumption of its seeds.

The *gemara* resolves the apparent contradiction: *la kashya, ha berachichei, ha beashunei* — there is no difficulty. One case refers to the soft, tender flesh of the gourd, and the other to the hard outer shell or rind. Certain gourds have a very hard exterior that would be difficult and probably harmful for a sick person to eat, while the soft interior flesh is easily digestible and appropriate. *Rava bar Ulla* offers a variant formulation: *ha bekara gufa, ha beigabei* — one refers to the gourd's own flesh and the other to its shell. A further nuance emerges as well: younger gourds tend to be softer throughout, while older ones become tougher and thicker. Either way, the conclusion is that squash or gourd can be healthy for sick people to eat in certain forms but not in others.

### Vegetable Pairings

*Deamar Rav Yehuda: lulavei dekara besilka, lulavei dechitna bechucha* — it is good to eat the outer parts of the gourd with beets, and the stalks or seeds of flax with *kutakh*. *Kutakh* is the Babylonian condiment that the sages of *Eretz Yisrael* regarded with some revulsion — made from bread crusts, possibly moldy, and sour milk. It received attention in *Pesachim* in connection with the question of whether the *chametz* used in its production is halakhically significant or edible enough to matter.

### Am Ha'aretz Warning

*Ve-davar ze asur le-omro bifnei am ha-aretz* — and this matter should not be said in front of an *am ha-aretz*. The warning is clear, but its rationale is debated.

### Warning Reasons

Three distinct explanations are offered. The *Ran* suggests that if an *am ha-aretz* hears that flax stalks or seeds are healthy to eat, he will pull up all the flax plants to consume them — damaging the flax crop. *Tosafot* offers a different explanation: an *am ha-aretz* will mock the *gemara* for including seemingly obscure and irrelevant medical advice about which varieties of squash are healthy, and this mockery is precisely the kind of objection heard even today from those who ask why the *gemara* contains such material. The *mefaresh* here advances a third interpretation. On his reading, *am ha-aretz* refers not to an uneducated person in the general sense but to the technically defined halakhic category enumerated in *Berachot* — people who deliberately avoid certain *mitzvot* such as *tefillin*, *tzitzit*, and regular *Torah* study. On this reading, the concern is that one should not share this kind of detailed practical guidance with someone who has chosen to be malicious in their religious conduct. Each of these three explanations reflects a different understanding of both the category of *am ha-aretz* and the nature of the concern.

## Cholim Identity

### Rava's Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַאן חוֹלִין — רַבָּנַן. רָבָא לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: כְּמַאן מְצַלִּינַן עַל קְצִירֵי וְעַל מְרִיעֵי [כְּמַאן — כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי]. מִדְּאָמַר קְצִירֵי וּמְרִיעֵי, שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: קְצִירֵי — קְצִירֵי מַמָּשׁ, מְרִיעֵי — רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different response: **Who are** the **sick** mentioned in the *baraita*, for whom a gourd is beneficial? They are **the Sages,** who are weakened by their toil in the study of Torah and their abstention from worldly pleasures, although they are not actually sick. Therefore, there is no contradiction. Gourds are harmful for people who are actually sick. The Gemara comments: **Rava** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as Rava said: In accordance with whose** opinion **do we pray** every day **for the sick and for the suffering? In accordance with whose** opinion? **In accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** who holds that one is judged every day, not only on Rosh HaShana, and therefore it is appropriate to pray for people every day. **From** the fact **that he said: The sick and the suffering,** one can **learn from** his statement that the term: **The sick,** is referring to **actual sick** people, while the term: **The suffering,** is referring to **the Sages,** who typically are physically frail.

*Rava amar: man kholin? Rabbanan* — Rava says: who are these "sick people" mentioned in the *Tosefta*? They are the rabbis, not physically ill people in the ordinary sense.

### Shemoneh Esrei Source

*Rava le-ta'ameih de-amar Rava: ke-man matzlinan ak-tzirei ve-amriei?* — This follows Rava's own stated position about for whom we pray when we pray for *ktzirei* and *mriei*, both of which denote types of sick people. Looking at the *mefaresh*, *matzlinan ak-tzirei* refers to the prayer for the physically ill — *rofeh kholei ammo Yisrael*, the *brachah* in *Shemoneh Esrei* that God should heal the sick. But *mriei*, also a term for the unwell, is associated in the *brachah* with *pleitat sofreichem* — the flourishing of their scholars. Since we have a *brachah* covering both the physically sick and Torah scholars, and since both groups are described using the language of illness, it follows that the rabbis themselves are called *cholim*. *Ke-man? Ke-Rabbi Yosi* — this follows the position of Rabbi Yosi. *Mi-de-amar ktzirei u-mriei, shema mina: ktzirei ktzirei mamash, mriei rabbanan* — from the fact that the prayer uses both *ktzirei* and *mriei*, we infer that *ktzirei* refers to the genuinely physically ill, while *mriei* refers to the rabbis.

### Torah Study Weakness

Why should rabbis be considered sick to begin with? There is a well-known *midrash*, found also in the *gemara* in *Shabbat*, connected to *Parshat Chukat*: *zot torat adam ki yamut be-ohel* — this is the *Torah* of a person who dies in a tent. The *gemara* reads this as teaching that *Torah* is acquired only by one who, metaphorically speaking, kills himself over it — pushing himself to the point of physical exhaustion, studying intensively for long hours, not eating adequately, not exercising, and spending his days and nights in the *beit midrash*. Such a person would naturally become weakened. One commentator connects this to the famous story of *Resh Lakish* and Rabbi Yochanan: *Resh Lakish* had been a physically powerful man, but once Rabbi Yochanan persuaded him to commit himself to *Torah* study, his physical strength left him — as though dedicating oneself entirely to the life of the *neshamah* necessarily diminishes the body. This dynamic has even been noted sociologically: in the *Yeshivish* world, the image of manliness and greatness is not the physically robust outdoor type but rather the pale, bespectacled scholar hunched over his seforim — someone who, by the world's standards, might appear weak, but who embodies the highest communal ideal.

## Thick Kederah

### Thick Exemption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמוּתָּר בְּעָבָה. מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּבַבְלָאֵי, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא: בַּבְלָאֵי טַפְשָׁאֵי, דְּאָכְלִי לַחְמָא בְּלַחְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § It is stated in the mishna that one who vowed that loose cooked food is forbidden to him is **permitted to** taste **a thick** cooked food. The Gemara comments: **The mishna is not in accordance with the** custom of the **Babylonians, as Rabbi Zeira said: Babylonians are foolish, as they eat bread with bread.** They eat thick porridge with their bread, which is essentially eating one kind of bread with another. According to their custom, one who vows that cooked foods are forbidden to him is prohibited from eating even a thick cooked food.

The *mishna* had ruled that one who takes a *neder* against *tavshil* is *asur b'maaseh kederah rach* — forbidden from a thin, soft pot dish — but *mutar b'aveh*, permitted to eat a thick one. The reasoning, as we have established, is that *tavshil* means something eaten together with bread, and a thick *maaseh kederah* would ordinarily be eaten on its own rather than as an accompaniment to bread.

### Bread with Bread

The *gemara* then notes: *matni de-lo ke-Bavlai* — the *mishna* does not reflect Babylonian practice. *De-amar Rabbi Zeira: Bavlai tipshai, de-achli lachma be-lachma* — Rabbi Zeira said: the foolish Babylonians eat bread with bread. What they would do is take a thick porridge — a hot cereal made from wheat or barley meal — and spread it on bread, creating a preparation of carbs on carbs. The comparison to a potato knish is apt: a starchy filling inside a starchy dough. Rabbi Zeira, who was himself a Babylonian who had made *aliyah*, was in a position to observe and critique this practice with some authority. This is one of several moments in the *gemara* where the food cultures of *Eretz Yisrael* and *Bavel* are contrasted, sometimes with pointed humor — we have already seen the *Eretz Yisrael* rabbis' disdain for *kutakh*, and the Babylonian rabbis reciprocated in kind regarding certain Israeli dishes.

### Kederah on Bread

The consequence for *halakhah* is direct: if Babylonians eat even a thick *maaseh kederah* by spreading it on bread — using it as a side dish or condiment to bread — then in Babylonia, even the thick preparation qualifies as *tavshil* and would fall under the *neder*. The *mishna*'s ruling that the thick version is permitted does not apply in the Babylonian context, because the premise of the ruling — that thick *maaseh kederah* is not eaten with bread — is false in Babylonia.

## Babylonian Foods

### Grain Pairings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: דִּמְשַׁאֵיל לְהוֹן לְהָלֵין נַקְדָּנֵי דְּהוּצַל: הָדֵין דַּיְיסָא, הֵיכֵין מְעַלֵּי לְמֵיכְלַהּ? דְּחִיטֵּי — בְּלַחְמָא דְחִיטֵּי, וְדִשְׂעָרֵי — בְּלַחְמָא דִשְׂעָרֵי, אוֹ דִּלְמָא: דְּחִיטֵּי — בְּדִשְׂעָרֵי, וְדִשְׂעָרֵי — בִּדְחִיטֵּי?
>
> **Translation:** In that context, **Rav Ḥisda said that those fastidious** residents **of Huzal,** Babylonia **were asked: How is it best to eat this porridge?** Should **wheat** porridge be eaten **with wheat bread and barley** porridge **with barley bread, or perhaps wheat** porridge should be eaten **with barley** bread **and barley** porridge **with wheat** bread?

*Amar Rav Chisda* — Rav Chisda raised the question of *da'itsa*: *heichei ma'alei, demichla dechittei belachma dechittei, udesia'arei belachma desia'arei* — given that porridge is eaten on bread in Babylonia, which pairing is preferable? Should you eat wheat porridge on wheat bread and barley porridge on barley bread, *o dilma dechittei bedesia'arei udesia'arei bedechittei* — or should you cross them, eating wheat porridge on barley bread and barley porridge on wheat bread? It is worth noting that *Tosafot* there cites *Rabbeinu Chananel* to the effect that *sea'arei* in this context may refer not to barley but to lentils — which would give the porridge a protein component and make the "carbs on carbs" critique somewhat less damning.

### Rava's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אַכְלֵיהּ בַּחֲסִיסֵי. רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אַשְׁכְּחֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא דְּקָאָכֵיל דַּיְיסָא בְּאֶצְבְּעָתֵיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַמַּאי קָאָכֵיל מָר בִּידֵיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב: דַּיְיסָא בְּאֶצְבְּעָתָא בְּסִים, וְכֹל דְּכֵן בְּתַרְתֵּין, וְכֹל דְּכֵן בִּתְלָת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rava would eat** his bread **with *ḥasisei*,** a porridge made of toasted barley grains. **Rabba, son of Rav Huna, found Rav Huna eating porridge with his fingers. He said to him: Why is the Master eating with his hands?** Rav Huna **said to him: This** is what **Rav said: Porridge** eaten **with a finger is tasty, and all the more so** if it is eaten **with two** fingers, **and all the more so with three.** It is more enjoyable to eat porridge with your hands.

*Rava achlei bechasissei* — Rava ate his porridge with *chasissei*, meaning he ate it with roasted or toasted grain rather than in the way others prepared it, dipping his bread into the porridge. The *gemara* in *Pesachim* explains this practice further.

### Fingers & Taste

*Rabba bar Rav Huna ashkeichei leRav Huna deka'achil da'itsa be'etzba'ateih* — Rabba bar Rav Huna found Rav Huna eating porridge with his fingers. Rabba bar Rav Huna expressed surprise: *amai ka'achil mar beyadeih?* Why is my master eating with his hands? Rav Huna replied: *hachi amar Rav: da'itsa be'etzba'ateih bassim* — Rav taught that porridge eaten with the fingers tastes better, because you feel the texture and engage more fully with the eating experience. *Vechol doken bitartein* — and even more so with two fingers — *vechol doken bitlat* — and even more so with three. These were the Amoraim of Babylonia, who did not have forks and for whom the choice was essentially between eating with the hands and eating with a stick, as we shall see momentarily.

### Travel Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ, וְכֵן אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: מְזַמְּנִים לָךְ לְמֵיכַל דַּיְיסָא עַד פַּרְסָה. לְמֵיכַל בִּישְׂרָא דְתוֹרָא עַד תְּלָתָא פַּרְסִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְחִיָּיא בְּרֵיהּ, וְכֵן אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב הוּנָא לְרַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ: כֹּל מִידַּעַם לָא תִּפְלוֹט קַמֵּיהּ רַבָּךְ, לְבַר מִן קַרָא וְדַיְיסָא, שֶׁהֵן דּוֹמִין לִפְתִילְתָּא שֶׁל אֲבָר, וַאֲפִילּוּ קַמֵּי שַׁבּוּר מַלְכָּא פְּלוֹט.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said to his son Ḥiyya, and Rav Huna similarly said to his son Rabba:** If **you are invited to eat porridge,** for such a meal you should travel **up to** the distance of **a parasang [*parsa*].** If you are invited **to eat ox meat,** you should travel **up to three parasangs. Rav said to his son Ḥiyya, and Rav Huna similarly said to his son Rabba: You should not spit out anything before your teacher,** as this is disrespectful, **apart from gourd and porridge, as they are like** a burning **lead wick** in the intestines when they cannot be digested, **and** therefore **spit** them **out even before King Shapur,** due to the danger involved.

*Amar lei Rav leChiaya breih, vehachen amar lei Rav Huna leRabba breih* — Rav told his son Chiaya, and Rav Huna similarly told his son Rabba: *mezamnin lach lemachal da'itsa, ad parsa* — if people invite you to eat porridge, you should travel as much as a whole *parsa* to join them. *Lemachal bisra detora, ad tlata parsa'ei* — if people invite you for steak, you should go even three *parsa'ot*. Sound advice for any season.

### Spitting Rule

*Kol midam lo tiflot kammai rabbach* — do not spit out your food in front of your teacher — *levar mikara veda'itsa* — except for gourds and porridge. These two foods are compared to *ptila shel avar* — the lead wire used in executions by *sreifah*, where molten lead was poured down the throat. The image is of something extraordinarily heavy and oppressive sitting in one's gut, potentially very painful. Therefore, *va'afilu kamei Shavur Malka pelot* — even in front of a king, one is permitted to spit them out.

### Fingers vs. Stick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי וְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה, חַד אָכֵיל דַּיְיסָא בְּאֶצְבְּעָתֵיהּ וְחַד אָכֵיל בְּהוּצָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ דְּאָכֵיל בְּהוּצָא לִדְאָכֵיל בְּאֶצְבְּעָתֵיהּ: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מַאֲכִילֵנִי צוֹאָתְךָ! אֲמַר לֵיהּ דְּאָכֵיל בְּאֶצְבְּעָתֵיהּ לִדְאָכֵיל בְּהוּצָא: עַד מָתַי אַתָּה מַאֲכִילֵנִי רוּקְּךָ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates more incidents: **Rabbi Yosei and Rabbi Yehuda** dined together. **One** of them **ate porridge with his fingers, and** the other **one ate with a fork [*hutza*].** The one **who was eating with a fork said to** the one **who was eating with his fingers: For how long will you** keep **feeding me your filth?** Must I keep eating off of your dirty fingernails? The one **who was eating with his fingers said to** the one **who was eating with a fork: For how long will you** keep **feeding me your spittle,** as you eat with a fork which you then put back in the common bowl.

*Rabbi Yose v'Rabbi Shimon* — two of the great *tannaim* — are brought anonymously here, presumably because the dispute involves something somewhat uncivilized and the *gemara* preferred not to attribute the positions explicitly. *Chad achal daisa b'etzbaotei* — one ate porridge with his fingers — *v'chad achal b'hutza* — and one ate with a stick. The one eating with a stick challenged the one eating with his fingers: *ad matay ata ma'achileni tzo'atecha* — how long will you keep feeding me your filth? Without washing one's hands, eating with the fingers would contaminate the shared pot. The finger-eater replied: *ad matay ata ma'achileni roka'acha* — and how long will you keep feeding me your saliva? The stick, having been in one's mouth, goes back into the communal dish on each bite. Each man's method of eating was, from the other's perspective, a source of contamination.

## Rabbi Yehuda Tales

### Figs Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אַיְיתוֹ לְקַמַּיְיהוּ בְּלוּסְפִיין. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אֲכַל, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא אֲכַל. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מַאי טַעְמָא לָא אָכֵיל מָר? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: אֵלּוּ אֵין יוֹצְאִין מִבְּנֵי מֵעַיִם כׇּל עִיקָּר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁנִּסְמוֹךְ עֲלֵיהֶן לְמָחָר.
>
> **Translation:** ***Belospayin*,** a type of figs, **were brought before Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon. Rabbi Yehuda ate** them, but **Rabbi Shimon did not eat** them. **Rabbi Yehuda said to him: What is the reason** that the **Master is not eating? Rabbi Shimon said to him: These do not leave the intestines at all.** They remain undigested. **Rabbi Yehuda said to him:** If so, **all the more that one can rely on them** to feel full **tomorrow.**

*Rabbi Yehuda v'Rabbi Shimon ayitu l'kameihu belusfin* — Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Shimon were brought *belusfin*, which the *mefaresh* identifies as a kind of very overcooked or stewed figs. *Rabbi Yehuda achal, Rabbi Shimon lo achal* — Rabbi Yehuda ate them; Rabbi Shimon did not. Rabbi Yehuda asked why. Rabbi Shimon explained: *elu ein yotzim mibnei me'ayim kol ikkar* — these will never fully pass through the digestive system; they are too dense and will leave one feeling blocked up. Rabbi Yehuda responded: *kol shekein shenishmoch aleihen l'machar* — all the more reason to eat them now, so that I can rely on them to keep me full until tomorrow. Rabbi Yehuda's intention was not mere gluttony but economy: eating a satiating food now to carry him through the following morning.

### Rutabagas Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה הֲוָה יָתֵיב קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי טַרְפוֹן. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן: הַיּוֹם פָּנֶיךָ צְהוּבִּין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֶמֶשׁ יָצְאוּ עֲבָדֶיךָ לַשָּׂדֶה, וְהֵבִיאוּ לָנוּ תְּרָדִין, וַאֲכַלְנוּם בְּלֹא מֶלַח. וְאִם אֲכַלְנוּם בְּמֶלַח — כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן שֶׁהָיוּ פָּנֵינוּ צְהוּבִּין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda was sitting before Rabbi Tarfon. Rabbi Tarfon said to him: Your face today is ruddy,** i.e., a rosy, healthy color. Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: Last night your servants,** i.e., we students, **went out to the field, and beets were brought to us, and we ate them without salt.** This is the reason for our healthy complexion. **And had we eaten them with salt, all the more so would our faces have been ruddy.**

*Rabbi Yehuda hava yativ kamei d'Rabbi Tarfon* — Rabbi Yehuda was once sitting before Rabbi Tarfon when Rabbi Tarfon remarked: *hayom panecha tzehubim* — today your face is shining, or perhaps very yellow or ruddy. Rabbi Yehuda explained: *emesh yatz'u avadecha lasadeh v'hevi'u lanu tardin v'achalnum b'lo melach* — last evening we went out to the field and they brought us *tardin* — large beets or rutabagas — and we ate them without salt. *V'im achalnum b'melach, kol shekein shehayu paneinu tzehubim* — had we eaten them with salt, our faces would have been glowing even more. The commentators observe that this was a deliberate act of modesty on Rabbi Yehuda's part: his face shone because of his Torah greatness, as we will see momentarily, but he did not wish to embarrass Rabbi Tarfon by suggesting his student outshone him. He attributed the radiance to the rutabagas instead.

### Wine Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרָה הָהִיא מַטְרוֹנִיתָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: מוֹרֶה וְרָוֵי? אֲמַר לַהּ: הֵימָנוּתָא בִּידָא דְּהָהִיא אִיתְּתָא, אִי טָעֵימְנָא אֶלָּא קִידּוּשָׁא וְאַבְדָּלְתָּא וְאַרְבְּעָה כָּסֵי דְפִסְחָא, וְחוֹגְרַנִי צִידְעַי מִן הַפֶּסַח עַד הָעֲצֶרֶת, אֶלָּא: "חׇכְמַת אָדָם תָּאִיר פָּנָיו".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites related incidents: **A certain** gentile **lady [*matronita*] said to Rabbi Yehuda,** whose face was ruddy: How can one **teach** the Jews **and** be **a drunk** at the same time? **He said to her:** I place my **integrity in the hands of this woman** and should no longer be deemed credible **if I** ever **taste** any wine **except** for that of ***kiddush*, *havdala*, and the four cups of Passover. And** after I drink those four cups **I tie my temples from Passover to *Shavuot*,** as wine gives me a headache. **Rather,** my complexion is explained by the verse **"A man's wisdom makes his face to shine"** (Ecclesiastes 8:1).

*Amra hi matronita le-Rabbi Yehuda* — a Roman matron said to Rabbi Yehuda, addressing him with what amounts to a pun — *mora v'ravi*, which plays on *mori v'rabbi*, "my teacher." She accused him: are you teaching Torah while drunk? His face was so bright and flushed that she suspected him of inebriation. *Amar lah: heimanuta b'yada d'hahi itata* — he invoked a kind of oath and replied: have I ever tasted wine except for *kiddush* and *havdalah* and *arba kasse de-Pischa* — the four cups on *Pesach*? Rabbi Yehuda's self-description as a near-teetotaler was not just personal modesty. He added that from *Pesach* to *Shavuot* his head aches — *min ha-Pesach ve-ad ha-Atzeret* — from having drunk the four cups. This passage is frequently cited in discussions of whether the wine used for *kiddush* and *havdalah* must be genuinely alcoholic. The position attributed to Rav Moshe Feinstein in the *Igrot Moshe* is that wine with some measure of alcohol is required, based in part on this very story: Rabbi Yehuda, who avoided wine at great personal cost, did not substitute grape juice even when he might easily have done so. This implies that grape juice does not suffice for these *mitzvot*.

### Tzeduki Insult

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מִינָא לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פָּנֶיךָ דּוֹמִין אִי כְּמַלְוֵי רִבִּית אִי כִּמְגַדְּלֵי חֲזִירִין. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: בִּיהוּדָאֵי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ אֲסִירָן. אֶלָּא — עֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבָּעָה בֵּית הַכִּסֵּא אִית לִי מִן בֵּיתָא עַד בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא, וְכׇל שָׁעָה וְשָׁעָה אֲנִי נִכְנָס לְכׇל אֶחָד וְאֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** **A certain heretic said to Rabbi Yehuda: Your face is similar either to usurers or to pig breeders.** These people would earn a good living without expending much energy, which gave them plump, healthy complexions. Rabbi Yehuda **said to him: Both** of these occupations **are prohibited to Jews. Rather,** my face is ruddy because **I have twenty-four bathrooms** on the way **from my home to the study hall, and all the time I enter each and every one** of them. He did not suffer from constipation, which had a beneficial effect on his complexion.

*Amar lei hahu Tzeduki le-Rabbi Yehuda* — a *Tzeduki* said to Rabbi Yehuda: *panecha domim o ke-malvei ribit o ke-megadlei chazirim* — your face looks like either those who lend money at interest or those who raise pigs. The *mefaresh* suggests the *malvei ribit* comparison is because such people grow wealthy and therefore appear perpetually happy and flush. The image of Moses descending from Sinai with his face radiant is a relevant parallel, as is the verse from *Kohelet* that will appear momentarily. The *Tzeduki* seemed to be insinuating that Rabbi Yehuda's bright face reflected the contentment of someone engaged in prohibited activity.

### Latrine Visits

Rabbi Yehuda replied: *bi-yehudai tarveihu asiran* — for Jews, both of those activities are forbidden. He then offered the true explanation for his shining face: *ella esrin ve-arba'ah batei ha-kisse it li min beita le-bei midrasha* — there are twenty-four latrines on the way from my house to the *Beit Midrash*, *de-khol sha'ah ve-sha'ah ani nikhnas le-khol echad ve-echad* — and every opportunity I have, as I walk to the *Beit Midrash* each day, I make use of every one of them. That is the source of his radiant wellbeing. The *Tzeduki*'s challenge had also implicitly accused him of imitating the shining face that comes from ill-gotten ease; Rabbi Yehuda's answer — that *chokhmat adam ta'ir panav*, a person's wisdom brightens his face (*Kohelet*) — was the reply to the matron's accusation of drunkenness earlier. To the *Tzeduki* he gave a more earthly and pointed answer: proper bodily function, maintained rigorously on the walk to Torah study each day.

## Kiddush Wine

### Kiddush Status

The discussion of Rabbi Yehuda's wine consumption naturally raises the question of the *halachic* status of wine for *kiddush*. The *gemara* in *Pesachim* cites the *baraita*: *zakhor et yom ha-Shabbat le-kaddesho — zakhrehu al ha-yayin* — remember the Sabbath day to sanctify it; remember it over wine. This sounds like a *d'oraita* obligation derived directly from the verse of *zakhor*. Nevertheless, the consensus among the *Rishonim*, drawing on other *gemarot* as well, is that the requirement for wine at *kiddush* is only *d'rabbanan*. *Tosafot* in *Pesachim* suggests a middle position: holding a cup of wine during *kiddush* may be *d'oraita*, but actually drinking it is only *d'rabbanan*. The *mefaresh* in *Masechet Nazir* — who is not actually *Rashi* but a later author writing in that tradition — takes the more expansive view that having wine for *kiddush* is a *mitzvah d'oraita* in the full sense. The *daf* concludes here, at the bottom of 49b, and we will resume from that point at the next session.
