# Pesachim 82 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro: Pesach Tumah

### Pesech Tumah Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִטְמָא שָׁלֵם אוֹ רוּבּוֹ — שׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה מֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה. נִטְמָא מִיעוּטוֹ וְהַנּוֹתָר — שׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתוֹ בְּחַצְרוֹתֵיהֶן אוֹ עַל גַּגּוֹתֵיהֶן מֵעֲצֵי עַצְמָן. הַצִּיקָנִין שׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה בִּשְׁבִיל לֵיהָנוֹת מֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If the **whole** Paschal lamb **or most of it became ritually impure, one burns it before the Temple [*habira*] with wood from the arrangement** of wood on the altar that was given to the owners of the Paschal lamb for this purpose. If **a minority of it became impure, and** similarly, with regard to **the** parts of the Paschal lamb that are **leftover,** which must be burned, the owners of the Paschal lamb **burn it in their courtyards or on their roofs, with their own wood.** Only **the miserly,** who want to save the expenditure of wood, **burn it before the Temple in order to benefit from the wood of the arrangement.**

The overarching discussion on this *daf* concerns *tumah* of a *korban pesach*. The earlier portions of the chapter dealt with the case of the *tzibur*, then moved to the case of an individual, and examined how the *tzitz* operates: not to permit eating the meat, but to render the offering acceptable even when the meat or blood becomes *tamei*, and in certain cases even when the owners themselves become *tamei* under the category known as *tumat hatehom*. The *mishnah* now turns to the practical question of what is done with the meat once parts of the *korban pesach* have in fact become *tamei*.

### Post-Zrika Status

Before proceeding, it is important to recall a foundational principle: once the *korban pesach* has been brought and the *dam* has been *nizrak*, whatever occurs afterward does not affect whether one has discharged the obligation of bringing the *pesach*. If the meat subsequently became *tamei*, or if one failed to eat it, the bringing itself was valid so long as the offering was fit to be eaten at the time of the *zrikat hadam*. The question of the *mitzvah* of eating is a separate matter entirely. The *mishnah* now addresses how one handles the meat in cases where it did become *tamei* after the *korban* was brought.

## Mishnah: Burning Pesach

### Entire/Majority Tamei

The *mishnah* states: *nitmah kulo o rubo* — if the entirety or the majority of the *pesach* became *tamei* (whether before it was dismembered or through contact with something that affected the whole or most of it), *sorfin oto lifnei habirah me'atzei hama'arachah* — one burns it in the presence of the *Birah*, understood here to mean the *Beit HaMikdash*, using wood designated for the *ma'arachah*, the wood pile on the altar. The *gizbar*, the Temple treasurer, provides sanctified wood from the altar's stores, and one uses it to burn the *pesach* that became *tamei*.

### Kodshim Burning Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: כְּדֵי לְבַיְּישָׁן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that the Paschal lamb must be burned before the Temple and that those who prefer to burn it elsewhere are not permitted to do so? **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: In order to embarrass them.** Presumably, the reason that most of the offering became impure is because the owners were not sufficiently careful with it. Therefore, the Sages decreed that it be burned in a public place.

The general *halakhah* regarding *kodshim* that become *pasul* is, as the *Gemara* quoted in the second *perek* and will cite again briefly: *bakodesh ba'esh tisaref* — *kol shepasula bakodesh*. Any sacrifice that became invalid must be burned, and it must be burned on holy ground, in the environs of the *Beit HaMikdash*. This principle applies primarily to *kodshei kodashim* — sacrifices that, when valid, must be eaten within the immediate precincts of the *Beit HaMikdash* and may not leave the *Azarah*. The *pesach*, by contrast, may be eaten throughout all of *Yerushalayim*. As will emerge, this difference creates scenarios where burning outside the *Beit HaMikdash* area is permitted, since the meat itself — when *kosher* — was permitted to be eaten anywhere within *Yerushalayim*.

### Minority Tamei/Notar

The *mishnah* then addresses a less severe case: *nitma mi'uto* — only a minority of the meat became *tamei* — or the case of *notar*, where the meat was not *tamei* but simply remained uneaten as leftover. These are regarded as less severely negligent occurrences. In such cases, *sorfin oto bechatzeroteihen o al gagoteihen* — one burns it at home, in one's courtyard or on one's roof, presumably within *Yerushalayim* where the meat was being eaten. There is no requirement to bring it to the *Beit HaMikdash*. However, *me'atzei atzman* — one must use one's own firewood.

### Two Issues: Location & Wood

There are two distinct issues operating simultaneously throughout this *mishnah*, and the *Gemara* will return to both. The first is where one burns it: burning it wherever one wishes is obviously easier, sparing one the need to bring it to the *Beit HaMikdash*. The second is what wood one uses. The *Gemara* will explain that when the entirety became *tamei*, the requirement to burn it before the *Beit HaMikdash* functions as a kind of fine — a consequence for allowing the entire *korban* to become *tamei* through negligence. The purpose, as will become clear, involves an element of public embarrassment designed to deter such negligence in the future.

## Tosafot: Balance

### Temple Wood Leniency

As for the leniency of using Temple wood, a conceptual difficulty immediately arises: the wood is *hekdesh*, sanctified property of the *Beit HaMikdash* — so how may it be used for burning *pasul* meat? *Rashi* and *Tosafot* both refer to another *Gemara* that employs the principle of *lev beit din matneh aleihem*. Since the wood is sanctified only in the sense of being property owned by the *Beit HaMikdash* — not sanctified in the manner of a *korban* itself — the *Beit HaMikdash* always retains, through a form of prior stipulation, the latitude to employ it in ways that do not constitute direct *hekdesh* usage. Burning *pasul korbanos*, for instance, falls within this conditional permission. The central authority overseeing the *Beit HaMikdash*, understood by *chazal* as a *beit din*, effectively retains control over how this property is deployed, and may stipulate that its *kedushah* does not apply under certain circumstances. This is a separate *sugya* in its own right, but it is the mechanism that makes the leniency of Temple wood workable.

### Reciprocal Balance

There is thus an interesting reciprocal structure in the *mishnah*: on one hand, when the entire *korban* became *tamei*, the offender is inconvenienced and compelled to bring it to the *Beit HaMikdash* area — a *chumra*; on the other hand, Temple wood is provided at no personal cost — a *kula*. *Tosafot* explicitly describes this balance, framing the provision of Temple wood as a counterweight to the harshness of having to perform the burning in a public location.

### Public Burning Purpose

*Tosafot* addresses the rationale directly. Since one is already being subjected to the considerable inconvenience and indignity of having to come to the *Beit HaMikdash* to perform this public burning — *kedei levaysho*, in order to shame him — the authorities did not further compel him to provide his own firewood. The public nature of the burning is embarrassing enough; requiring personal wood on top of that would be excessive. The two considerations are thus kept in careful balance: the location serves the purpose of deterrence through embarrassment, while the provision of materials offers modest relief.

## Mishnah: Hatzikanim

### Hatzikanim Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בָּאוּ לְשׂוֹרְפוֹ בְּחַצְרוֹתֵיהֶן וּמֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה — אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶן. לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה וּמֵעֲצֵי עַצְמָן — אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in the *Tosefta*: If individuals **came** and wanted **to burn** the Paschal lamb that had become ritually impure **in their courtyards and** wanted to take **wood from the arrangement** to use for this purpose, **one does not listen to them.** Similarly, if they wanted to burn it **before the Temple with their own wood, one does not listen to them.**

The *mishnah* introduces one further category: *hatzeikanim*, the stingy people. These individuals would actually make the journey to the public burning area near the *Beit HaMikdash* — *bishvil lehenos me'atzei hama'arachah* — specifically in order to benefit from the Temple's firewood, even when they were not required to come. When only a minority of the *korban* became *tamei* or there was *notar*, they were entitled to burn it at home with their own wood. Yet to avoid the personal expense of providing firewood, they were willing to make the journey and suffer whatever public notice came with it. *Tosafot* addresses the obvious problem: if they are not among those obligated to come to the *Beit HaMikdash*, why should they be allowed to use the Temple wood there? *Tosafot* answers that if we refused to allow such people to use the Temple wood and insisted they provide their own, they would simply not burn the *korban* at all. Since they are evidently only willing to perform the burning under the condition of using public wood, we must accommodate them — otherwise the *tamei* meat or *notar* would go unburned entirely.

## Q&A: Negligence & Funds

### Negligence Assumption

One might ask whether *tumah* is always a reliable indicator of negligence — after all, a *sheretz* can simply find its way in, and there is nothing the owner could necessarily have done. And there is a second, related difficulty: if indeed the degree of negligence is uncertain, why should public funds be used to cover what may be private carelessness?

### Response: Presumption

The answer to the first question is that while one cannot determine negligence with certainty in every individual case, the general presumption is sound. If one is treating *hekdesh* with the vigilance it demands, one should be hyper-vigilant. The combination of negligence occurring *and* it affecting the entire *korban* — how did the whole thing become *tamei*? — makes significant carelessness a reasonable inference in the majority of cases, even if not in every one.

As for the public funds question, this is, admittedly, a somewhat ironic arrangement. *Tosafot*'s answer is that once the *Beit HaMikdash* has already imposed the substantial inconvenience and public shame of bringing the burning to a public location, it would be too much to additionally compel the person to supply the materials for, in a sense, his own public humiliation. That said, the person cannot simply choose to burn it in his backyard with his own wood — that option is not available.

### Gemara Answer: Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא מֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה בְּחַצְרוֹתֵיהֶן אֵין שׁוֹמְעִין לָהֶן — דִּילְמָא פָּיְישָׁן מִינַּיְיהוּ, וְאָתוּ בְּהוּ לִידֵי תַקָּלָה. אֶלָּא לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה מֵעֲצֵי עַצְמָן, מַאי טַעְמָא לָא? רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** if people want to burn it **with wood from the arrangement in their** own **courtyards one does not listen to them.** This is because the Sages were concerned **lest they leave over some of the** wood they took to burn the Paschal lamb **and come to** experience **a mishap with it** by using the wood for another purpose. **But** in the case in which they wanted to burn the offering **before the Temple with their own wood, what is the reason** one does **not** listen to them? **Rav Yosef said:** It is **in order not to embarrass one who does not have** the means to bring his own wood.

The *Gemara* itself cuts directly to the rationale: *amar Rav Yosef amar Rav Chanina* — *kedei le-vayshan*, in order to embarrass them. The purpose is not to derive pleasure from shaming but rather to create a powerful disincentive, making bystanders sufficiently aware that they become more careful in their own practice.

A useful parallel is found in the *Gemara*'s discussion of why the *ba'al koreh* reads aloud for the congregant called to the Torah rather than the congregant reading himself. Among the reasons given is *shelo levayesh et mi she'ein lo* — so as not to embarrass those who cannot read. If some were to read and others not, those who could not would be exposed. The same reasoning applies here: if some individuals were to bring their own wood to the public burning, thereby demonstrating their personal means or piety, those who come with public wood would be embarrassed by the contrast. Policy therefore requires uniformity.

One might further ask about the nature of the *lev beit din* — whose *beit din* is this? While the *Sanhedrin* formally operated in the *Lishkat HaGazit*, in the perspective of *chazal* all operations of the *Beit HaMikdash* fall under rabbinic oversight. The phrase *matneh aleihem* — the stipulation applies to the *etzim*, the wood — reflects the idea that a central rabbinic authority retained ultimate supervisory jurisdiction over *hekdesh* property and could calibrate its use accordingly.

## Braita: Leaving Jerusalem

### Departure from Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמָא מִיעוּטוֹ וְכוּ׳. וּרְמִינְהוּ: וְכֵן מִי שֶׁיָּצָא מִירוּשָׁלַיִם וְנִזְכַּר שֶׁיֵּשׁ בְּיָדוֹ בְּשַׂר קֹדֶשׁ, אִם עָבַר צוֹפִים — שׂוֹרְפוֹ בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, וְאִם לָאו — חוֹזֵר וְשׂוֹרְפוֹ לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה מֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה!
>
> **Translation:** It was stated in the mishna that if **a minority of it became ritually impure,** and similarly, if part of it was left over, it is burned in the owners' courtyards with their own wood. The Gemara **raises a contradiction** based on what was taught in a different mishna: **And similarly,** with regard to **one who left Jerusalem and remembers that there is consecrated meat in his hand,** which is now disqualified because it has left Jerusalem and must therefore be burned, **if he passed** Mount **Scopus, he burns** the meat **at the site** where **he** is located, and he need not return to burn it in Jerusalem; **and if** he has **not** yet passed Mount Scopus, **he must return and burn it before the Temple with wood from the arrangement.**

The *Gemara* now presents a *braisa*: *v'chen mi sheyatzah mi'Yerushalayim venizkar sheyeish biyado basar kodesh* — and similarly, one who was departing from *Yerushalayim* and realized he had *shelamim* meat on his person. Since *shelamim* is *kodshim kalim*, there is greater latitude about where it may be burned than there would be for *kodshei kodashim*, where one would have no choice but to burn it within the *Beit HaMikdash* precincts.

### Tzofim Boundary

The relevant boundary marker is *Tzofim* — possibly *Har Tzofim*, or more precisely, the point from which one can still see *Yerushalayim*. *Rashi* understands it as the location beyond which *Yerushalayim* is no longer visible. Once one has passed that point, one is no longer considered to be within the environs of *Yerushalayim*.

### Beyond Tzofim

*Im avar Tzofim* — if one has already passed *Tzofim* — *sorefo bimkomo*: one burns it where one stands. We do not require a return journey, since the traveler is already beyond the effective orbit of the city.

### Within Tzofim Zone

*V'im lav* — if one has not yet passed *Tzofim* — *chozer vesorefo lifnei habirah me'eitzim hama'arachah*: one must return and burn it before the *Birah*, and one is permitted to use the firewood of the *Beit HaMikdash*. Two issues are again in play: the obligation to return and the availability of Temple wood.

## Gemara Q: Why Difference?

### Problem Statement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חָמָא בַּר עוּקְבָא: לָא קַשְׁיָא, כָּאן — בְּאַכְסְנַאי. כָּאן — בְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥama bar Ukva said:** It is **not difficult. Here,** where it is stated that one burns it in the Temple with wood from the arrangement, it **is referring to a visitor** who does not live in Jerusalem or have wood there, and he may therefore use wood from the arrangement. While **there,** where it is stated that one burns it at home with one's own wood, it **is referring to a resident** of Jerusalem.

The *Gemara* now poses a sharp question. In the *braisa*, the traveler who returns to *Yerushalayim* is permitted to use the wood of the *Beit HaMikdash*. But in the *mishnah*, the person in *Yerushalayim* who has a small portion of *tamei* meat or *notar* is told to burn it at home with his own wood. Why should the traveler returning to *Yerushalayim* be entitled to the Temple wood when the resident of *Yerushalayim* is not? This is the question on which the *Gemara* focuses — not whether someone should be required to return, but why one may use the Temple wood and the other may not.

### Guest vs. Homeowner

The answer provided by *Rav Chama bar Ukva* establishes a key distinction: the *mishnah* addresses a *ba'al bayit*, a homeowner who lives in *Yerushalayim*, while the *braisa* addresses an *achsanya*, a temporary resident or guest. Why is the traveler leaving *Yerushalayim*? Because he does not live there. In the *braisa*, the person is an *achsanya*, a visitor who came to *Yerushalayim* for *Pesach* but is not a resident. We cannot reasonably expect such a person to have his own wood readily available. And by extension, even had he not yet begun his journey home but was still staying as a guest in *Yerushalayim*, he would similarly be entitled to use the Temple wood. The *mishnah*'s requirement of *me'atzei atzman* applies only to residents — *ba'alei batim* who live in *Yerushalayim* and are not given the option of going to the *Beit HaMikdash* to use public wood. They must remain at home and use their own.

### Two Guest Categories

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב פָּפָּא אָמַר: הָא וְהָא בְּאַכְסְנַאי, כָּאן — שֶׁהֶחְזִיק בַּדֶּרֶךְ, כָּאן — שֶׁלֹּא הֶחְזִיק בַּדֶּרֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: This** case **and that** case both refer **to a visitor. Here,** where it is stated that one may use wood from the arrangement, it **is referring to** one who already **set out on the road,** and it is difficult for him to collect wood. **There,** where it is stated that one must use his own wood, it **is referring to** a case in which the visitor **has not** yet **set out on the road,** and he can gather his own wood.

*Rav Pappa* offers a different formulation. He distinguishes between two categories of visitors based on whether one has already begun one's departure or not. A visitor who has not yet started leaving — who is still settled in *Yerushalayim* at his lodgings — is for the time being a temporary resident. Like any resident who wakes up each morning and must provide for himself, we can require him to obtain his own wood and burn the *korban* at home. But the *braisa* addresses one who has already set out on his journey. His mind and body are already outside of *Yerushalayim*, and to now demand that he locate a supplier of wood and manage the logistics of private burning would be unreasonable. In that case, he may proceed to the public burning with Temple wood.

### Rav Zvid: All Guests

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: לְעוֹלָם כִּדְאָמַר מֵעִיקָּרָא — כָּאן בְּאַכְסְנַאי כָּאן בְּבַעַל הַבַּיִת, וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא הֶחְזִיק בַּדֶּרֶךְ, אַכְסְנַאי כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית לֵיהּ עֲשָׂאוּהוּ כְּצִיקָנִין. דִּתְנַן: הַצִּיקָנִין שׂוֹרְפִין אוֹתוֹ לִפְנֵי הַבִּירָה בִּשְׁבִיל לֵיהָנוֹת מֵעֲצֵי הַמַּעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Zevid** disagreed with Rav Pappa and clarified Rav Ḥama bar Ukva's opinion. He **said: Actually,** it can be explained **as** Rav Ḥama **initially said: Here,** it **is referring to a visitor; there,** it **is referring to a resident. And even though he did not** yet **set out on the road, a visitor** may use wood from the arrangement for the following reason: **Since he does not have** his own wood, the Sages **treated him like the misers, as we learned** in a mishna: **The misers burn it before the Temple in order to benefit from the wood of the arrangement.** This indicates that anyone who is unconcerned about his reputation, like a miser, may use wood from the arrangement, and this is not considered to be a misuse of consecrated property. Similarly, the Sages also permitted a visitor to use wood from the arrangement.

*Rav Zvid* clarifies that the original position was correct: the distinction is always and only between *achsanya* and *ba'al habayis*, regardless of whether one has already started one's journey. *Ve'af al gav de'hecheshik baderech* — even if one is mid-journey, the relevant factor remains one's status as a guest, not the journey itself. The reasoning is: *achsanya keivan delais lei* — a guest simply does not have his own property or resources in the city — *asu kemi she'ein lo* — and so they placed him in the same category as those without means to provide their own wood. Even absent the journey, this is the governing principle. If the sole question were the journey, the rule would be stated differently; the *braisa* mentions the journey not to establish it as the criterion, but — as *Rashi* notes — to teach that even one who has begun leaving must return, and not to limit the wood leniency to travelers only.

## Rashi Clarification

### Rashi: Return Demand

*Rashi* addresses the end of Rav Zvid's position by emphasizing what the *braisa* is primarily coming to teach: that even one who has already set out on his journey must turn around if he has not yet passed *Tzofim*. *Le'ashme'inan chazara nakat lah* — the mention of journey status is there to convey the *chazara*, the obligation to return, and not to define the condition for using the Temple wood. The entitlement to Temple wood, according to Rav Zvid, flows from *achsanya* status alone. *Velo mishum etzim de'hu hadin lelo hecheshik* — even a guest who has not yet started his journey is entitled to the wood.

### Unresolved: Why Return?

This raises a question that *Rashi* leaves somewhat open. One understands why a guest receives the leniency of Temple wood. But why is the guest in the *braisa* also required to return to the *Beit HaMikdash* area specifically? The *mishnah* tells residents they may burn at home. Why is the guest not given the same option — burn wherever you are, using your own or locally obtained wood?

### Two Possible Answers

Two possible answers suggest themselves. The first: the *braisa*'s instruction to return to the *Beit HaMikdash* area is not a standalone obligation but rather a conditional one. The obligation is to return to *Yerushalayim*; if one wishes to make use of the Temple wood, that requires going to the *Beit HaMikdash* precincts. But theoretically, if one wished to go to *Yerushalayim* and use one's own wood, that option might also exist. The *braisa* is not demanding a return to the *Beit HaMikdash* per se — it is offering a path that combines the return obligation with the convenience of Temple wood.

The second possibility: *achsanya* status may cut both ways. For a resident, we say "you are rooted here, stay home, burn it at home." For a guest who has already left his lodgings and set out on his journey, there is no longer a fixed location to which we can direct him. Once he must return in any case, we direct him to the appropriate communal location — the *Beit HaMikdash* area — rather than leaving him to improvise burning somewhere without proper resources. The *Gemara* itself does not resolve this fully.

## Braita: Forbidden Mix

### Private + Temple Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי הַחֲשָׁד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** It is **due to suspicion.** People would take back their own unused wood that they had brought, and others might suspect that they were stealing consecrated wood from the Temple.

The *Gemara* now presents a *braisa* addressing forbidden combinations. If individuals come to burn *pasul* meat privately, in their own courtyards, using wood of the *ma'arachah* — *ein shomin lahem* — this is not permitted. Even in a case like that of the *tzikanim*, where we otherwise accommodate the use of Temple wood, we will not permit them to do so in a private setting.

### Public + Personal Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ דְּאַיְיתִי קְנֵי וְחַרְיָוָתָא, דְּלָא חֲזֵי לַמַּעֲרָכָה.
>
> **Translation:* The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between these** two answers? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** the case of one who **brings reeds** or **palm branches that are not fit for the arrangement.** Since these types of branches are not used for the arrangement of wood on the altar, there is no concern that people would be wrongly suspected of stealing them from the Temple; therefore, Rava would permit it. However, the concern that the poor will be embarrassed is still relevant, and so Rav Yosef would prohibit it.

Conversely, if individuals come to burn in the public space before the *Birah* using their own personal wood — *ein shomin lahem* — this too is not permitted. The two considerations — location and wood — are not interchangeable. One cannot mix the leniency of one with the stringency of the other.

### Rav Yosef: Dignity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: שֶׁלֹּא לְבַיֵּישׁ אֶת מִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said:** It is **in order not to embarrass one who does not have** the means to bring his own wood.

The *Gemara* readily explains the first prohibition: *dilma payish mineihu* — perhaps some Temple wood will remain after the burning, and since it would otherwise be *hekdesh*, any leftover would remain *hekdesh*. If that wood is in one's private yard and one keeps the remainder, *ve'atu bah lidei takala* — one will come to stumble by using the sanctified wood for ordinary purposes.

The second prohibition — why one may not use personal wood at the public burning site — is explained by *Rav Yosef*: *kedei shelo levayesh et mi she'ein lo* — so as not to embarrass those who lack personal resources. Once the practice at the public burning site involves private wood being brought by some, those who use the public wood are by implication marked as those without means. The policy of uniformity protects dignity. This principle recurs in several places in *halakhah*, including the practice among *Ashkenazim* where the congregant called to the Torah does not read aloud himself — so that *mi she'ein lo*, those who cannot read, are not exposed. Similarly, it has been suggested that the institution of the *mesader kiddushin* reciting the *birkat eirusin* arose because not all *chatanim* knew it, and to have some recite it and others not would embarrass those who could not. The uniform policy eliminates the distinction.

## Rav Papa: Suspicion

### Leftover Wood Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן הָתָם: רֹאשׁ הַמַּעֲמָד הָיָה מַעֲמִיד אֶת הַטְּמֵאִין עַל שַׁעַר הַמִּזְרָח. מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: כְּדֵי לְבַיְּישָׁן. רָבָא אָמַר: מִפְּנֵי הַחֲשָׁד.
>
> **Translation:** Another mishna teaches a similar dispute between Rav Yosef and Rava on a different subject: **We learned** in a mishna **there,** in tractate *Tamid*: The **head of the watch would stand the ritually impure** priests **at** the entrance to **the eastern gate** each morning. The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** that they did not simply send them home without making them stand at the entrance to the eastern gate? **Rav Yosef said:** It was **in order to embarrass them** for not having been careful to avoid becoming impure. **Rava said:** They would stand them there **to** avoid **suspicion,** so that people would realize that they were not performing the Temple service because they were impure, rather than in order to work in their regular professions.

*Rav Pappa* offers an alternative reason for prohibiting personal wood at the public burning site: suspicion. If one brings one's own firewood, burns part of it, and then takes the remainder home, observers may suspect that the remaining wood is *hekdesh* wood being taken for private use.

### Non-Temple Materials

The practical difference between Rav Yosef and Rav Pappa becomes apparent in a case where one brings materials that are clearly not Temple wood — reeds or palm branches, for example, which burn quickly and would never be used for the *ma'arachah* on the altar. According to Rav Pappa, there is no *chashad* in such a case: no one would suspect these materials of being *hekdesh*. According to Rav Yosef, however, the prohibition would still apply — because bringing one's own materials, of any kind, to the public burning site introduces the invidious distinction between those who can and those who cannot provide their own.

### Tamid Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ מְפַנְּקִי, אִי נָמֵי דְּקָא גָדֵיל שִׁישׁוּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is** the practical difference **between these** two opinions? The Gemara answers: The practical difference **between them is** the case of **pampered people** who never work, whom others would not suspect of avoiding Temple service in order to work in their regular professions. **Alternatively,** it is the case of **one who twists ropes** for a living. No one would suspect someone of missing the Temple service in order to work in such a lowly and non-profitable occupation. These two categories of people would not have to stand at the gate according to Rava's opinion, but would have to according to Rav Yosef's opinion.

A parallel debate appears in *Tamid*, for those familiar with that tractate. The *rosh hama'amad* — the officer overseeing the *kohanim* on duty that day — would station all *tamei kohanim* at *sha'ar hamizrach*, the eastern gate leading into the *Ezrat Nashim*, at the beginning of the day. *Amar Rav Yosef: kedei l'vayeshan* — in order to embarrass them, as a deterrent against allowing oneself to become *tamei*. *Rava amar: mipnei hachashad* — to prevent suspicion, since observers who do not see a *kohen* fulfilling his duties might assume he is neglecting his obligations or absconding to attend to private affairs. The visible presence at the gate makes clear that he is *tamei*, not absent without cause.

### Yosef vs. Papa

The difference between the two positions emerges again in a further case: *mefanki* — those who are idle or independently wealthy. If one has no employment to which one might plausibly slip away, no one would suspect dereliction of duty. According to Rav Pappa's *chashad* reasoning, such a person would not need to be stationed at the gate. According to Rav Yosef's embarrassment reasoning, the practice would still apply, since the purpose is deterrence through shame, not merely the management of perception.

## Intrinsic vs. Extrinsic

### Immediate Burning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַפֶּסַח שֶׁיָּצָא אוֹ שֶׁנִּטְמָא — יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. נִטְמְאוּ הַבְּעָלִים אוֹ שֶׁמֵּתוּ — תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ, וְיִשָּׂרֵף בְּשִׁשָּׁה עָשָׂר. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: אַף זֶה יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד לְפִי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ אוֹכְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **A Paschal lamb that was taken out** of its permissible area **or that became ritually impure should be burned immediately** on the eve of Passover. If **the owners became ritually impure or died, its form must** be allowed to **decay** by leaving it for a period of time instead of burning it immediately, **and it should be burned on the sixteenth** of Nisan, immediately after the first day of the Festival. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka says: This, too, should be burned immediately, because it has no one to eat it,** which is also so severe a disqualification that decay of form is not required.

The *mishnah* now introduces a distinction of fundamental importance. *Hapesach she'yatzah o shenitma yisaref miyad* — if the *pesach* went out of its permissible boundaries (i.e., left *Yerushalayim*) or became *tamei*, it is burned immediately.

### Delayed Burning

*Nitmu ha-ba'alim o she-meisu* — if the owners became *tamei* or died, such that there is no one entitled to eat the meat, the problem is not intrinsic to the meat itself but external to it. In such a case, *tu'ubar tzurato veyisaref beshishas'ar* — one waits until the meat's appearance degrades overnight, which renders it *notar* by the following morning. Since *pasul kodshim* cannot be burned on *Yom Tov*, and the 15th of *Nissan* is *Yom Tov*, burning is delayed until the 16th.

### R. Yochanan Dissent

*Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka omer* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka disagrees. Even when the owners died or became *tamei*, the meat should be burned immediately — *lefi she'ein lo ochlim*, because when there are no owners to eat it, this is tantamount to an intrinsic invalidity in the *pesach* itself.

### Principle Stated

The underlying principle is thus: *kol shepsulo b'gufo yisaref miyad* — whatever invalidity is intrinsic to the body of the *korban* itself is burned immediately. Invalidity that is external — whether a problem with the blood or a problem with the owners — requires waiting until the following morning. The debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka hinges on precisely which problems are classified as intrinsic and which as external.

## Gemara: Biblical Sources

### Tumah & Yatzah Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בִּשְׁלָמָא טָמֵא, כְּתִיב: ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל בָּאֵשׁ יִשָּׂרֵף״. אֶלָּא יוֹצֵא מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Granted,** the fact that **ritually impure** sacrificial meat must be burned **is written** in the Torah, as it says: **"The flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten, it shall be burned in fire"** (Leviticus 7:19). **But** sacrificial meat that **is taken out** of its permissible area, **from where do we** derive that it must be burned?

The *Gemara* now traces the *derasha* foundations of these laws. *Bishlama tamei ksiv* — the requirement to burn *tamei* meat is clear: *vehabasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo yei'achel ba'esh yisaref* — the Torah explicitly states that if the meat becomes *tamei* it may not be eaten and must be burned. But what is the source for the rule that *yatzah* — meat that left its designated zone — is also burned?

### Moshe-Aharon Dialogue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּכְתִיב: ״הֵן לֹא הוּבָא אֶת דָּמָהּ אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה״. אָמַר לוֹ מֹשֶׁה לְאַהֲרֹן: מַדּוּעַ לֹא אֲכַלְתֶּם אֶת הַחַטָּאת? שֶׁמָּא נִכְנַס דָּמָהּ לִפְנַי וְלִפְנִים. אָמַר לוֹ: לָאו. אָמַר לוֹ: שֶׁמָּא חוּץ לִמְחִיצָתָהּ יָצְתָה? אָמַר לוֹ: לָאו, בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ הָיְתָה. אָמַר לוֹ: אִי בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ הָיְתָה וְ״הֵן לֹא הוּבָא אֶת דָּמָהּ אֶל הַקֹּדֶשׁ פְּנִימָה״, מַדּוּעַ לֹא אֲכַלְתֶּם אוֹתָהּ? מִכְּלָל דְּאִי נָפְקָא, אִי נָמֵי עָיֵיל דָּמָהּ לִפְנִים — בַּת שְׂרֵיפָה הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: It is derived from the verses describing Moses admonishing Aaron and his sons about the fact that they did not eat the sin-offering on the eighth day of inauguration, **as it is written: "Behold, its blood was not brought into the Sanctuary within;** you should have eaten it in the Sanctuary, as I commanded" (Leviticus 10:18). This should be understood as follows: **Moses said to Aaron: Why didn't you eat the sin-offering? Perhaps its blood entered the innermost** chamber, the Sanctuary, in which case it must be burned? Aaron **said to him: No. Moses said to him: Perhaps it was taken outside of its boundary,** and that is the reason you did not eat it? **He said to him: No, it was in the sacred** area, i.e., the courtyard. Moses **said to him: If it was in the sacred** area the entire time, **behold, its blood was not brought into the Sanctuary within, why didn't you eat it?** This proves **by inference that if it leaves** its boundary, **or alternatively, if its blood enters within** the Sanctuary of the Temple, **it must be burned.** This can serve as a model for all offerings.

The *Gemara* derives *yatzah* from the exchange between Moshe and Aharon during the *miluim*. Aharon burned the *chatas* instead of eating it, and Moshe sought to understand why. The *pasuk* states: *hen lo huva et damo el hakodesh penimah* — the blood was not brought inside. For a *korban* whose blood is meant to be sprinkled on the outside, bringing the blood into the *heichal* would invalidate it and require burning. Moshe therefore asked: *madua lo achaltem et hachatas* — why did you not eat this *chatas*? Perhaps, he suggested, you brought the blood *lifnai velifnim*, into the innermost sanctum? Aharon replied: no. Moshe then asked: *shema chutz limchitzasah yatzah* — perhaps the meat left the boundary of the *Azarah* and that is why it was burned? Aharon again replied: no — *bakodesh hayasah*, it remained in the *kodesh*.

### Inference: Yatzah

The *Gemara* reads the exchange as implying: had the meat gone out — had it not remained *bakodesh*, *penimah* — it would have been appropriate to burn it. Alternatively, had the blood been brought too far *pnimah* into the *heichal*, it would similarly have required burning. *Miklal de'im nafkuh* — from the logic of Moshe's question we infer that *yatzah* is a ground for burning *pasul kodshim*. The *Gemara* thus treats these as the two primary scriptural anchors: *nitmah* from the explicit *pesuk* about *tamei* meat, and *yatzah* derived from the Moshe-Aharon dialogue.

## Expansion: Kodshim Kalim

### Question: Shelamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא נִטְמָא, גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. אֶלָּא יוֹצֵא, אַשְׁכְּחַן קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Granted,** with regard to sacrificial meat that **became ritually impure, the Merciful One reveals** that *halakha* in the Torah **with regard to offerings of lesser sanctity,** as the verse: "The flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten," is referring to a peace-offering, and it applies **all the more so with regard to offerings of the most sacred order. However,** with regard to sacrificial meat that **is taken out** of its boundary, **we** have **found** a source that this applies to **offerings of** only **the most sacred order.** With regard to **offerings of lesser sanctity,** such as the Paschal lamb, **from where do we** derive that they too must be burned?

The *Gemara* now asks: the source from the Moshe-Aharon exchange addressed *kodshei kodashim* — specifically the *chatas*, whose zone is the *Azarah*. *Kadashim kalim minalan* — where do we derive that *yatzah* applies equally to *kodshim kalim* such as *shelamim*?

### Different Zones

The difficulty is compounded by the difference in zones. For *kodshei kodashim*, the relevant boundary is the *Azarah*; crossing that line is a severe violation. For *kodshim kalim* like *shelamim*, the zone extends to all of *Yerushalayim*. It is therefore far from obvious that crossing the boundary of *Yerushalayim* carries the same severe consequence — invalidating the meat and requiring burning — as crossing the wall of the *Azarah* does for *kodshei kodashim*.

## Blood Invalidity Issues

### Blood Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא טָמֵא, גַּלִּי רַחֲמָנָא בְּקָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן בְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים. אֶלָּא יוֹצֵא, אַשְׁכְּחַן קׇדְשֵׁי הַקֳּדָשִׁים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מְנָלַן? וְתוּ, הָא דְּתַנְיָא: לָן דָּמָהּ, נִשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, יָצָא דָּמָהּ חוּץ לַקְּלָעִים — דְּקַיְימָא לַן בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, מְנָלַן?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted,** with regard to sacrificial meat that **became ritually impure, the Merciful One reveals** that *halakha* in the Torah **with regard to offerings of lesser sanctity,** and it applies **all the more so with regard to offerings of the most sacred order. However,** with regard to sacrificial meat that **is taken out** of its boundary, **we** have **found** a source that this applies to **offerings of** only **the most sacred order. With regard to offerings of lesser sanctity, from where do we** derive that they too must be burned? **And furthermore,** with regard to **that which is taught** in a *baraita*: If **the blood** of an offering **was left overnight** instead of being sprinkled on the altar on the day it was slaughtered, **or its blood was spilled,** or **its blood was taken outside the hangings** that denoted the courtyard of the Tabernacle, **we maintain** that it must be **burned; from where do we** derive this *halakha*?

The *Gemara* raises a further set of cases established throughout *Shas*: *lan dama* — if the blood was not thrown while it was still day and night fell; *nishpach dama* — if the blood was spilled; *yatzah dama chutz le-klaim* — if the blood was taken outside the *Azarah*. In all these situations, *de-kaima lan bi-sreifah* — it is well-established that the meat must be burned. These are problems not with the meat itself but with the blood. What is their scriptural source?

### Source Question

The question is whether these cases can be derived from existing *pesukim* or whether they rest on tradition. The *Gemara* identifies a *pasuk* through a *braisa* in the name of Rabbi Shimon.

### Rabbi Shimon Pesuk

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָפְקָא לַן מִדְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן. דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: ״בַּקֹּדֶשׁ בָּאֵשׁ תִּשָּׂרֵף״ — לִימֵּד עַל חַטָּאת שֶׁשְּׂרֵיפָתָהּ בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ. אֵין לִי אֶלָּא זוֹ בִּלְבַד, שְׁאָר פְּסוּלֵי קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים וְאֵימוּרֵי קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מִנַּיִן? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: ״(וְכׇל) ... בַּקֹּדֶשׁ ... בָּאֵשׁ תִּשָּׂרֵף״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **We derive it from** the exposition of **Rabbi Shimon, as it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon says:** The verse that states: "Any sin-offering from which some blood has been brought to the Tent of Meeting, to make atonement **in the sacred place,** shall not be eaten; **it shall be burned in fire"** (Leviticus 6:23), **has taught that the burning of a sin-offering is in the sacred** area, the Temple courtyard. **I have** derived **only** that the *halakha* applies to **this alone,** a sin-offering; with regard to **the rest of the disqualified offerings of the most sacred order and the portions of offerings of lesser sanctity** that are consumed on the altar, **from where do we** derive that they must be burned in the Temple courtyard, which is their boundary when they are valid? **The verse states: "Any…in the sacred place…shall be burned in fire,"** which teaches that any consecrated item must be burned in a sacred place.

*Rabbi Shimon omer*: the *pasuk* states — *ve-chol chatas asher yuva mi-damah el ohel moed le-chapeir ba-kodesh lo se-acheil ba-esh tisaref* — any *chatas* whose blood was brought into the *Ohel Moed* to effect atonement inside the *heichal* shall not be eaten but shall be burned with fire in a holy place. The phrase *ba-kodesh ba-esh tisaref* establishes two things: first, that a *chatas* invalidated through mishandling of the blood is burned; second, that it is burned *ba-kodesh* — on sanctified ground, within the *Azarah*. *Limed al chatas she-sreifasah ba-kodesh* — this teaches that a *chatas* rendered *pasul* by a blood problem is burned, and burned specifically in the holy area. From *kol she-pasula ba-kodesh*, the *pasuk* is expanded to apply to other *kodshei kodashim* with other invalidities, including the *imurei kodshim kalim* — the portions of *kodshim kalim* such as *shelamim* fats that must remain within the *Azarah*.

## Expansion: Kodshim Kalim

### No Pesuk: Tradition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַשְׁכְּחַן קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים, קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים מְנָלַן? אֶלָּא: כׇּל פְּסוּלוֹ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, לָא שְׁנָא קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים וְלָא שְׁנָא קׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים — גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, וְחַטָּאת דְּאַהֲרֹן מִשּׁוּם מַעֲשֶׂה שֶׁהָיָה כָּךְ הָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: **We found** a source for **offerings of the most sacred order; from where do we derive** that it also applies to **offerings of lesser sanctity? Rather,** we must say as follows: **Anything disqualified in the sacred** area, the Temple courtyard, must be **burned.** It is **no different** whether it is **offerings of lesser sanctity, and** it is **no different** whether it is **offerings of the most sacred order; they learned it as a tradition. And the sin-offering of Aaron** that was burned on the eighth day of inauguration was mentioned only **because the incident that took place, took place in this way.** It was not mentioned in order to teach the *halakhot* of offerings.

When it comes to *kodshim kalim* as a whole — not merely their *imurei* but the meat itself — and for invalidities beyond *tumah*, the *Gemara* is forced to concede: *ela, kol psula ba-kodesh bi-sreifah* — the general rule that *pasul kodshim* must be burned, *lo shena kadashim kalim ve-lo shena kodshei kodashim*, whether *kodshim kalim* or *kodshei kodashim*, cannot be fully derived from *pesukim*. *G'mora g'miri lah* — it is a tradition. The sole *pesuk* we had for *kodshim kalim* addressed *tumah*; for other invalidities affecting *kodshim kalim*, we cannot find a satisfactory scriptural basis, and we must therefore rely on transmitted tradition, akin to the earlier discussion regarding *tumat hatehom*.

## Purpose of Pesuk

### Pesuk Indicates Place

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרִינַן כׇּל פְּסוּלֵי דְקֹדֶשׁ בִּשְׂרֵיפָה, לָא שְׁנָא דְּקׇדְשֵׁי קָדָשִׁים וְלָא שְׁנָא קָדָשִׁים קַלִּים, גְּמָרָא גְּמִירִי לַהּ, ״בַּקֹּדֶשׁ ... בָּאֵשׁ תִּשָּׂרֵף״ לְמָה לִי? הָהוּא מִבְּעֵי לֵיהּ שֶׁשְּׂרֵיפָתָהּ בַּקֹּדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now that we say** that we have accepted the conclusion that **all disqualified sacred** offerings must be **burned,** and it is **no different** whether they are disqualified **offerings of the most sacred order** or **offerings of lesser sanctity,** because in both cases **they learned it as a tradition,** with regard to the verse that says: **"In the sacred place…shall be burned in fire," why do I** need it? What does this verse teach that is not known from oral tradition? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **is required** to teach that the requirement of burning that is learned from the oral tradition cannot take place anywhere; **its burning** must take place only **in the sacred** place, the Temple courtyard.

Given that the obligation to burn *pasul kodshim* ultimately rests on tradition — *gemara gemiri lah* — what purpose do the *pesukim* serve? The *Gemara* resolves this by assigning each *pasuk* its own independent function. The *pasuk* of *bakodesh ba'esh tisaref* does not establish the fact of burning — that comes from tradition — but rather specifies the location: that things whose zone when valid was the *Azarah* must be burned in the *Azarah*. The *pesuk* is a locational instruction, not a burning instruction.

### 'Bakodesh' Zone

*Hahu miba'ei lei l'sreifa bakodesh* — that *pasuk* is needed to tell us that burning must occur *bakodesh*, in the holy area, specifically where its zone was *kodesh*. *Bakodesh ba'esh tisaref* thus informs us not that one burns *pasul kodshim* — that is known from tradition — but that one burns it within the boundaries of the zone that defined its permissible use.

## Purpose of Pesuk (continued)

### Tumah Pesuk Purpose

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** ״וְהַבָּשָׂר אֲשֶׁר יִגַּע בְּכׇל טָמֵא לֹא יֵאָכֵל בָּאֵשׁ יִשָּׂרֵף״, לְמָה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks further: If the source of the *halakha* is an oral tradition, with regard to the verse that states: **"The flesh that touches any impure item shall not be eaten, it shall be burned in fire"** (Leviticus 7:19), **why do I** need it? This *halakha* is included in the oral tradition and should not require its own verse.

This leaves a question about the *tumah pasuk* — *vehabasar asher yiga bechol tamei lo yei'achel ba'esh yisaref*. If burning is already known from tradition and location is taught by *bakodesh ba'esh tisaref*, why do we need this *pesuk*? The *Gemara* answers: *hahi l'gufeih itztareich* — this *pesuk* is needed for its own specific teaching, which could not have been inferred otherwise.

## Aharon's Chatas

### Why Aharon Story?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַהוּא, לְגוּפֵיהּ אִיצְטְרִיךְ. סָלְקָא דַּעְתְּךָ אָמֵינָא כֹּל פְּסוּלֵי דְקֹדֶשׁ, כְּגוֹן: לָן דָּמָהּ, וְנִשְׁפַּךְ דָּמָהּ, וְיָצָא דָּמָהּ, וְנִשְׁחֲטָה בַּלַּיְלָה, דְּבִשְׂרֵיפָה דְּלֵיתַנְהוּ בְּחוּלִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **That** verse is **necessary for itself. It could enter your mind to say** that **disqualified sacred** offerings include only a case **where its blood was left overnight** instead of being sprinkled on the altar on the day the offering was slaughtered; or **its blood was spilled;** or **its blood was taken outside** the hangings; **and** similarly, the case of an offering that **was slaughtered at night.** In all these cases, the offering must be **burned** because these are disqualifications unique to sanctified offerings **that do not exist with regard to unconsecrated** items.

The *Gemara* asks a pointed question: if the *halakhah* that *pasul kodshim* must be burned is already known through tradition — *gemara gemiri lah* — going back as far as one cares to trace it, why did the *Torah* need to record the story of Aharon and the burning of his *chatas* at all? The entire premise of citing a narrative in the *Torah* is that we derive *halakhah* from it. If the *halakhah* is already established independently, what does the story add?

### Horaas Sha'ah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל נִטְמָא, דִּבְחוּלִּין נָמֵי מִפְּסִיל, אֵימָא הוֹאִיל וְאִיתְעֲבִיד בֵּיהּ עוֹבָדִין דְּחוֹל — אֵימָא לָא תִּיבְּעֵי שְׂרֵיפָה, וְתִיסְגֵּי לֵיהּ בִּקְבוּרָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if the meat **became ritually impure, which disqualifies unconsecrated** meat **also, say** that **since it was treated in the manner of unconsecrated items,** as the disqualification is not unique to consecrated items, **it should not require burning, and burial should be sufficient for it.** Therefore, the verse **teaches us** that an offering that is disqualified through ritual impurity must also be burned.

The response: *u'mishum maaseh shehaya kach haya* — the story of Aharon's *chatas* was not recorded in order to teach a general rule but simply as a historical account of what actually happened. It was a *horaas sha'ah* — a ruling specific to that moment and those circumstances — not a normative precedent for all generations.

### Rabbi Nechemia: Onen

Rabbi Nechemia offers a concrete halakhic grounding for this position: *bidmei aninut nisrefah* — the *chatas* was burned because Aharon was an *onen*, a mourner, on that day. A *kohen onen* may perform the *avodah* — *kohen onen makriv onen* — but an *onen* may not eat consecrated meat. Since every *kohen* available at that time was an *onen*, there was simply no one who could legitimately eat the *chatas*, and that is why it was burned. This was a one-time situation — *horaas sha'ah* — arising from entirely unique circumstances, and no general rule should be extracted from it.

## Tumah Question

### Tumah Common

The *Gemara* now addresses the remaining question about why the *tumah pasuk* is independently necessary. *Salka da'atach amina* — one might have thought to limit the burning obligation to those invalidities that are uniquely *korban*-related: *lan dama* (blood not thrown that day), *nishpach dama* (spilled blood), *yatzah dama* (blood taken outside), *nishchetah balaylah* (slaughtered at night) — all of these are forms of *pesul* that have no parallel in non-sacrificial contexts. *D'leisnhu b'chulin* — such invalidities simply do not occur with ordinary *chulin* food. One might therefore have confined the burning obligation to these distinctly sacrificial forms of *pesul*, reasoning that what makes a *korban pasul* in a *korban*-specific way retains its sacred character even in its invalidity and is therefore burned.

### Resolution: Still Burns

By contrast, *tumah* is commonplace. *D'v'chulin nami mipsal* — even ordinary non-consecrated food is rendered *pasul* by *tumah*; one cannot eat *chulin* that has become *tamei* either. One might therefore have argued that *tumah* is so mundane a form of *pesul* that it strips a *korban* of its elevated status, reducing it in effect to the level of an ordinary spoiled item. And if so — *v'tisgi lei bikvura* — perhaps one need not burn it at all but merely bury it, as one does with *tamei* food that is not *kodesh*. The *tumah pasuk*, *ka mashma lan*, therefore comes to teach that even *tumah*, notwithstanding its commonplace character, carries the full burning obligation when it affects *kodshim*.

## Rav Yosef Analysis

### Before Zrika

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִטְמְאוּ הַבְּעָלִים אוֹ שֶׁמֵּתוּ תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתָן וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים אַחַר זְרִיקָה, דְּאִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה. אֲבָל נִטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּלָא אִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that if **the owners** of the offering **became ritually impure or died** before the offering was completed, **its form** must be left to **decay,** and it is burned after the first day of the Festival. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka disagrees and says that it is burned immediately. **Rav Yosef said:** This **dispute** is about a case in which **the owners became ritually impure after the sprinkling** of the blood, **because the meat was fit for eating** since all its services were completed in a valid way. **But** if **the owners became ritually impure before the sprinkling** of the blood, such that **the meat was not fit for eating** even for a moment, **all agree** that **it should be burned immediately.**

*Amar Rav Yosef*: the debate between the *chachamim* and Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka must be carefully located in time. *Machlokes shenitm'u be'alim lifnei zrika* — the controversy over whether owner-related problems are intrinsic or extrinsic applies specifically to the case where the owners became *tamei before* the *zrikat hadam*. At the moment the *korban* was being brought, the problem was already present — the owners were already unable to eat the meat. Yet the problem is not in the *korban*'s body itself; it is in the owners. Here, Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka considers this intrinsic — *d'lo itchazi basar l'achilah*, the meat was never fit for eating at the defining moment — while the *chachamim* regard even this as external.

### After Zrika

However, *aval nitm'u be'alim l'achar zrika* — if the owners became *tamei* or died after the *zrikat hadam*, when the *korban* had already been properly brought and the meat was fully fit to be eaten at that moment — *divrei hakol psulah machamat davar acher* — everyone agrees this is an extrinsic *pesul*. Even Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka would concede that the subsequent loss of owners is categorically external to the *korban*.

### External Logic

The logic is straightforward: when the *korban* was brought, it was completely in order. The meat was *de-chazi l'achilah* — fit to be eaten. The problem that arose afterward is external, a circumstance that befell the owners, not a deficiency in the offering itself. *Psulo machamat davar acher* — its invalidity arises from something entirely outside the *korban's* body.

## Baraita Rule

### General Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי, זֶה הַכְּלָל: כׇּל שֶׁפְּסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ — יִשָּׂרֵף מִיָּד. בַּדָּם וּבַבְּעָלִים — תְּעוּבַּר צוּרָתוֹ וְיֵצֵא לְבֵית הַשְּׂרֵיפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to this statement based on the *Tosefta*: **This is the principle:** In **all** cases in which **its disqualification is in itself, it should be burned immediately.** If the disqualification is **in the blood** of the offering or **in the owners, its form** should be left to **decay, and** then **it should be taken out to the place** designated for **burning.**

The *Gemara* then cites a *braisa* presenting the general principle: *kol shepsulo b'gufo yisaref miyad* — whatever invalidity is intrinsic to the body of the *korban* is burned immediately.

### Specific Cases

*B'dam u'v'ba'alim te'uvar tzurato* — if the problem involves the blood or the owners, one waits for the night to pass and burns it the following morning, or after *Yom Tov* if applicable.

### Logical Link

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָתָנֵי בְּעָלִים דּוּמְיָא דְּדָם: מָה דָּם לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, אַף בְּעָלִים לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** The following inference can be made based on the formulation of the *Tosefta*: **It is teaching** that the case of disqualified **owners** is **similar to** the case of disqualified **blood: Just as** in the case of **blood** the disqualification necessarily occurred **before the sprinkling** of the blood, as the offering is not disqualified if anything happens to the blood after sprinkling, **so too,** the case of **owners** is referring to one in which they became disqualified even **before the sprinkling** of the blood, which contradicts the statement of Rav Yosef.

The *braisa*'s pairing of blood problems and owner problems in the same category of delayed burning illuminates the interpretation. Problems of the blood — *lan dama*, *nishpach dama* and the like — necessarily occur before *zrikat hadam*, since once the blood has been properly thrown there are no further blood-related issues that could arise. Reading the *braisa*'s grouping accordingly: *ma dam lifnei zerikah af ba'alim lifnei zerikah* — just as blood problems occur before *zrikat hadam*, the owner problems referred to in the *braisa* are likewise those that occurred before *zerikah*. This supports Rav Yosef's reading that the debate between *chachamim* and Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka concerns the pre-*zrikat hadam* scenario.

## R.Y. Ben Broka

### His Reasoning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: מַחְלוֹקֶת שֶׁנִּטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים לִפְנֵי זְרִיקָה, דְּלָא אִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה, דְּהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּפְסוּלוֹ בְּגוּפוֹ. אֲבָל נִטְמְאוּ בְּעָלִים לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה, דְּאִיתְחֲזִי בָּשָׂר לַאֲכִילָה — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל פְּסוּלוֹ מֵחֲמַת דָּבָר אַחֵר, וּבָעֲיָא עִיבּוּר צוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, if** this **was stated** in the name of Rav Yosef, **it was stated as follows:** This **dispute** is specifically about a case in which **the owners became ritually impure before the sprinkling** of the blood, such that **the meat was never fit for eating, which** makes it **as though its disqualification is in itself. But** if **the owners became ritually impure after sprinkling** of the blood, such that **the meat was fit for eating, all agree** that **its disqualification is due to a different matter,** i.e., an external factor, **and it requires decay of form.**

According to the revised reading attributed to Rabbi Yochanan (the *amora*), the debate extends even to the post-*zrikat hadam* scenario. *Af l'achar zerikah nami machlokes* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka and the *chachamim* debate not only the pre-*zerikah* case but also what happens after *zerikah*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka's reasoning, even in the post-*zerikah* scenario, is that if there is no one to eat the meat, the meat itself is intrinsically compromised — *lefi she'ein lo ochlim*, the absence of owners renders it like a body without a purpose, which is itself an intrinsic defect.

### Rabbi Nechemia

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן אָמַר: אַף לְאַחַר זְרִיקָה נָמֵי מַחְלוֹקֶת. וְאַזְדָּא רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אָמְרוּ דָּבָר אֶחָד, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה הָא דַּאֲמַרַן. רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה מַאי הִיא? דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: מִפְּנֵי אֲנִינוּת נִשְׂרְפָה זוֹ, לְכָךְ נֶאֱמַר ״כָּאֵלֶּה״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Even** a case in which the owners became ritually impure **after sprinkling** the blood **is** included in **the dispute** about whether decay of form is necessary. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan follows his** line of **reasoning, as Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka and Rabbi Neḥemya said the same thing.** The statement of **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka is that which we said.** With regard to **Rabbi Neḥemya, what is** the ruling in which he expressed his opinion? It is **as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** Aaron's sin-offering that was burned on the eighth day of inauguration **was burned due to acute mourning,** as that was the day his sons Nadav and Avihu died. **For this** reason, **it is stated** that Aaron explained to Moses: "Now that events such **as these** befell me, were I to eat this day's sin-offering would the Lord approve?" (Leviticus 10:19). In other words, it was due to the deaths of his sons, which Aaron referred as "events such as these," that Aaron burned the sin-offering instead of eating it.

Rabbi Yochanan the *amora* aligns Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka with Rabbi Nechemia: *Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka v'Rabbi Nechemia amru davar echad*. The parallel to Rabbi Nechemia is instructive. In the case of Aharon's *chatas*, the *kohanim* were *onenim* — mourners — and could not eat the meat despite the *korban* having been properly offered. This is a post-*zerikah* problem relating to the owners' capacity to eat, not a deficiency in the offering itself. Yet the meat was burned immediately. *Ve-ha aninut l'achar zerikah hi* — this *aninut* problem arose, or is to be treated as arising, in the context of the *zerikah* already having been performed. And despite the fact that the *kohen onen* did perform the *zrikat hadam* — since *kohen onen makriv onen*, an *onen* may perform the service — the inability to eat thereafter was deemed sufficient to require immediate burning.

### Application

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנִינוּת כִּלְאַחַר זְרִיקָה הָוְיָא, וְכִי אִשְׂתְּרוּף — לְאַלְתַּר (נִשְׂתְּרוּף).
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yoḥanan analyzes Rabbi Neḥemya's statement: **Now, acute mourning is similar to** a case of disqualification **after sprinkling** of the blood, because acute mourning merely disqualifies the owners from eating the offering. It does not disqualify the actual performance of the sacrificial service by a High Priest, such as Aaron. Therefore, it is like other disqualifications that take effect after the sprinkling, which simply prohibit the eating of the meat. **And when it was burned, it was burned immediately,** without waiting until the following day. This shows that Rabbi Neḥemya agrees that an offering that becomes disqualified after its blood has been sprinkled should be burned immediately.

The broader application is this: according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Beroka, when owners are unable to eat the meat — whether because they became *tamei* before the *zerikah* or, as Rabbi Nechemia's case illustrates, because they are *onenim* after the *zerikah* — the meat is treated as intrinsically invalid and is burned immediately. The *chachamim*, by contrast, maintain that even pre-*zerikah* owner problems are extrinsic, since the source of the *pesul* lies outside the *korban's* body, and delayed burning is therefore required. The dispute thus turns on whether *psulo b'gufo* — intrinsic invalidity — is defined by the moment of *zerikah* or by the source of the problem, a question that the *Gemara* on this *daf* brings into sharp focus.
