# Yevamot 67 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Pregnant Woman & Slaves

### Baraita Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — אֵלּוּ וָאֵלּוּ אֵין אוֹכְלִים. הִנִּיחַ בָּנִים, וְהִנִּיחָהּ מְעוּבֶּרֶת — עַבְדֵי מְלוֹג אוֹכְלִים כְּדֶרֶךְ שֶׁהִיא אוֹכֶלֶת, עַבְדֵי צֹאן בַּרְזֶל — לֹא יֹאכְלוּ, מִפְּנֵי חֶלְקוֹ שֶׁל עוּבָּר. שֶׁהָעוּבָּר פּוֹסֵל וְאֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If the priest who was married to an Israelite woman and died **left children,** both the slaves of usufruct property **and** the slaves of guaranteed investment **may partake** of *teruma*. The slaves of guaranteed investment are owned by the children, who are priests, and the slaves of usufruct property are owned by the woman, who partakes of *teruma* due to her children. If **he left** his wife **pregnant** and did not leave children, both **these** slaves **and those** slaves **may not partake** of *teruma*. If **he left children and left her pregnant,** the **slaves of usufruct** property who belong to her **partake** of *teruma* **just as she partakes** due to her children. However, the **slaves of guaranteed investment,** who are inherited by the children, **may not partake, due to the fetus's share,** as it too inherits them, **as a fetus can disqualify** one from partaking of *teruma* **but it cannot not enable** one **to partake.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yosei.**

The daf opens at the bottom of 67a, where the Gemara presents a *baraita* that elaborates on the positions already introduced in the *Mishnah*. The *baraita* examines a series of scenarios involving a woman married to a *kohen* whose husband has died, asking in each case whether her slaves may eat *terumah*. The three basic cases are: born children only, a fetus only, and a combination of born children alongside a current pregnancy.

### Born Children

In the first case — *hiniach banim*, where the husband left behind born sons — the ruling is *elu v'elu okhlim*: both the woman and all the slaves may eat *terumah*. The born child entitles the woman to eat, and the slaves, now fully owned by the *kohen*'s heirs, eat by virtue of that ownership. There is no complication.

### Fetus Alone

In the second case — *hiniach ubar*, where the only surviving connection is a fetus — the ruling reverses: *elu v'elu ein okhlim*, neither the woman nor the slaves may eat. The woman cannot eat on behalf of the fetus, since only a born child confers that right. And the slaves cannot eat because they are owned, at least in part, by the fetus — and the fetus cannot cause them to eat.

### Born + Pregnant

The third case — *hiniach banim v'hiniach uberet*, where there are born children and the woman is also pregnant — produces a split ruling. Her own slaves, the *avdei milug*, may eat, because she herself eats by virtue of the born children, and her slaves follow her status. However, his slaves — the *avdei tzon barzel*, which remain part of the estate — may not eat, *mipnei chelko shel ubar*, because the fetus holds a portion of what the born sons have inherited, thereby creating a problem. The principle articulated is *she'ubar posel v'eino ma'achil*: the fetus invalidates — enough to prevent the estate slaves from eating — but does not itself feed, meaning the woman cannot eat on its account. This is identified as the position of Rabbi Yose, consistent with what the *Mishnah* already established.

### R. Shimon Yochai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יוֹחַי אוֹמֵר: זְכָרִים יֹאכְלוּ כּוּלָּן, נְקֵבוֹת לֹא יֹאכֵלוּ, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai says:** If among the priest's children there are **males,** the slaves **partake** of *teruma*. But if they are **all females, they do not partake, lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** The male fetus would be the sole inheritor, and it does not enable slaves to partake of *teruma*.

Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai offers a different position. He rules that if the living children are male — *zekharim yokhlu kulam* — then all the slaves may eat despite the pregnancy. If the living children are female — *nekevot lo yokhlu* — the slaves may not eat, *shema yimatze ubar zakhar*, lest the fetus turn out to be male and thereby inherit the entire estate, leaving the daughters with nothing.

### Minority Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: לְעוֹלָם קָסָבַר חוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא, דְּעָבְדִינַן לֵיהּ תַּקַּנְתָּא כְּרַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** that **we are not concerned about the minority** of cases. Only a minority of fetuses are male inheritors, as roughly half are female, and some are stillborn. Therefore, the majority of fetuses will not become male children. **And if you wish, say** that **actually he holds** that **we are concerned about the minority.** However, **we make an arrangement for** the slaves, **in accordance with** what **Rav Naḥman** said that **Shmuel said.**

The obvious question on Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's position regarding male children is: why aren't we concerned that the fetus might be a boy and thereby hold a portion in the estate? The answer the Gemara provides is that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai holds *ein choshashin l'miuta* — one need not be concerned with a minority. Since there is only a 50% chance the fetus will be a boy, and since not every pregnancy results in a live birth, the probability that a live baby boy will emerge is less than 50% — call it something like 48%. That minority possibility does not generate enough concern to prevent the slaves from eating.

This is, it must be said, a striking position. In the realm of *issurim*, we generally are concerned even with much smaller minorities when there is a *miut hamatzuy* — a commonly occurring minority. *Tosafot* wrestles with precisely this tension. One possible distinction is the unique status of the *ubar*, something that is not yet *lefanenu*, not yet present before us — but the oddness of disregarding a near-50% possibility is not lost on the commentators.

As for the case of daughters only: the Gemara notes that there the problem is actually two-fold. If the fetus is a girl, she splits the estate with the daughters already alive. If the fetus is a boy, he displaces them entirely. So the *baraita*'s formulation — *shema yimatze ubar zakhar* — is not the exclusive reason. The Gemara explains: *chada v'od ka'amar* — the *baraita* states one reason and then an additional, stronger one. First, even a female fetus would create a problem for the existing daughters' portion. And furthermore, there is the additional concern that the fetus may be male and take everything.

### Two Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי אִירְיָא שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר? תִּיפּוֹק לֵיהּ דִּנְקֵבָה נָמֵי פָּסְלָה! חֲדָא וְעוֹד קָאָמַר: חֲדָא, דִּנְקֵבָה נָמֵי פָּסְלָה. וְעוֹד, שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא עוּבָּר זָכָר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם הַבֵּן כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Why** does Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **specifically** explain that if the children are females, the slaves may not partake of *teruma*, **lest the fetus be found** to be **a male? Derive** the ruling that the slaves do not partake of *teruma* from the *halakha* **that a female** fetus **also disqualifies** its slaves from partaking of *teruma*. Since the priest has only daughters, they inherit from him, and the female fetus receives a share in the inheritance too. The Gemara answers: Rabbi Shimon ben Yoḥai **stated one** reason **and another. One** reason is **that a female also disqualifies** its slaves from partaking of *teruma*, **and another** reason is **lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters have no** share in the inheritance **at all in a place** where there is **a son.**

Returning to the case of male children, the Gemara now asks: granted that Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai holds we need not concern ourselves with the minority — but perhaps there is an alternative explanation that does not depend on the *miuta* principle at all. Perhaps Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai actually does hold *choshashin l'miuta*, that one is generally concerned with minorities, but holds that there is an independent mechanism that resolves the problem even if the fetus would be a boy.

### Apotropos Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: יְתוֹמִים שֶׁבָּאוּ לַחֲלוֹק בְּנִכְסֵי אֲבִיהֶם, בֵּית דִּין מַעֲמִידִין לָהֶם אַפּוֹטְרוֹפּוֹס, וּבוֹרֵר לָהֶם חֵלֶק יָפֶה. הִגְדִּילוּ — יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת. וְרַב נַחְמָן דִּידֵיהּ אָמַר: הִגְדִּילוּ — אֵין יְכוֹלִין לְמַחוֹת, דְּאִם כֵּן מָה כֹּחַ בֵּית דִּין יָפֶה.
>
> **Translation:** This is **as Rav Naḥman said** that **Shmuel said:** With regard to minor **orphans who came to** court to **divide up their father's property,** the **court appoints for** each of **them a steward [*apotropos*], and he selects for them a fine share.** When the orphans **have grown up, they may object** to the manner in which the property was divided and redistribute it. **And Rav Naḥman himself said** that when **they have grown up they may not object, as, if** they may object, **what good is the power of the court?** Here too, an appointed steward selects a share of the inheritance on behalf of the fetus, and this share does not include any of the slaves. Therefore, the slaves may partake of *teruma*. However, if all of the children are females, this arrangement is impossible because if the fetus is a son all the property belongs to him.

That mechanism is the ruling of Rav Nachman in the name of Shmuel: *yetomim sheba'u lachalok benichsei avihem* — when orphaned heirs, some adults and some minors, come to divide their father's estate, *beit din* appoints an *apotropos*, a guardian or steward, who selects a favorable portion on behalf of the minors. The question of whether the minors, upon reaching adulthood, can subsequently challenge and reverse that assignment is itself disputed: the ruling in the name of Shmuel suggests they can, while Rav Nachman himself holds *higdilu ein yekholim limchot* — once they come of age, the apportionment stands, because to allow reversal would undermine the authority and effectiveness of *beit din*.

The application to the *ubar* is as follows: rather than allowing the fetus to hold an undivided fractional share in every slave in the estate — which would prevent all of them from eating — *beit din* could appoint an *apotropos* for the fetus and carve out a specific portion of the estate on his behalf. If there are ten slaves, the *apotropos* might assign two of them to the fetus's account. Those two slaves cannot eat, since the fetus owns them and a *ubar* cannot cause slaves to eat. But the remaining eight, now wholly owned by the born brothers, can eat freely. The *apotropos* effectively transforms a fractional co-ownership of every slave into an exclusive ownership of specific slaves — freeing the majority for *terumah* consumption.

One might ask: what happens if the fetus is ultimately a girl? Then the entire premise of the assignment would need to be revisited. This solution was constructed on the assumption the fetus would be a boy. If a girl is born, the allocation would need to be redistributed. And in the case where the existing children are daughters, the *apotropos* solution is more complicated, since a male fetus would be entitled to everything, not just a fractional share.

### Apotropos Rejected

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא דְּרַב נַחְמָן תַּנָּאֵי הִיא? לָא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אִית לְהוּ דְּרַב נַחְמָן. וְהָכָא בְּחוֹשְׁשִׁין לְמִיעוּטָא קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** Based on the use of Rav Naḥman's ruling to explain Rabbi Shimon's opinion, the Gemara suggests: **Let us say** that **Rav Naḥman's** opinion **is** corresponding to one side of a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi Shimon. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **No;** it is possible **that everyone** in the dispute **accepts Rav Naḥman's** ruling, **and here they disagree** only **with regard to** whether **we are concerned about the minority,** as previously suggested, in a case where the arrangement was not made.

The Gemara then considers whether the underlying dispute between the *tanna kamma* and Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai might itself reflect a dispute about the validity of the *apotropos* mechanism — i.e., whether *beit din* can in fact make such an assignment for an *ubar*. But the Gemara dismisses this: *lo*. Everyone can accept the ruling of Rav Nachman. The *apotropos* idea is set aside entirely. The dispute returns to its simpler formulation: the question is simply whether we are concerned with the minority possibility that the *ubar* will be born as a live male. The *tanna kamma* holds *choshashin l'miuta* and is therefore concerned; Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai holds *ein choshashin l'miuta* and is therefore not.

## R. Yishmael's Teaching

### Puzzle Stated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם אָבִיו: הַבַּת — מַאֲכֶלֶת, הַבֵּן — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei, says in the name of his father:** If the priest left behind **a daughter,** she **enables** the slaves **to partake** of *teruma*; however, **a son does not enable** them **to partake.**

The *baraita* also records a teaching of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose, transmitted in his father's name: *ha-bat ma'akhila, ha-ben eino ma'akhil* — the daughter feeds, the son does not feed. This statement, on its face, is deeply puzzling. Everything we have seen until now suggested the opposite: a son in the estate is the mechanism that allows eating, while a daughter does not carry the same inheritance rights and therefore creates different dynamics. How can it be that daughters allow the slaves to eat but sons do not?

### Limited Estate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים עָסְקִינַן. וּכְגוֹן דְּאִיכָּא בֵּן בַּהֲדֵי בַּת, מָה נַפְשָׁךְ: אִי הַאי דִּמְעַבְּרָא בֵּן הוּא — לָא עֲדִיף מֵהַאי דְּקָאֵי. אִי בַּת הִיא, אַמַּאי אָכְלָה — בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן, כַּמָּה דְּלָא נְפַק לַאֲוִיר הָעוֹלָם לָא תַּקִּינוּ רַבָּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Here we are dealing with** a case of an inheritance of **insufficient property** that is enough only to sustain the daughters until they come of age. With regard to this case, the Sages instituted that the daughters receive their sustenance while the sons get nothing. This is **also** a case **where there is** a surviving **son together with** the **daughter.** Therefore, **no matter what,** the slaves do not partake of *teruma*. **If this** fetus, **with which she is pregnant, is a son, it is no better than this** son **who** already **exists.** Just as the existing son does not inherit the insufficient property, the same applies to the male fetus. **If it is a daughter,** it does not yet receive a share of the inheritance. This can be explained: **Why does** the daughter **partake** of the inheritance? It is **by** virtue of **a rabbinic ordinance.** Therefore, **as long as** the fetus **has not emerged into the atmosphere of the world, the Sages did not establish** that it should receive the inheritance. Consequently, the slaves partake of *teruma* by virtue of the existing daughter, as only she inherits them.

Abaye resolves this by positing that the case concerns a *nechasim mu'atim* — a small or limited estate. The relevant *halachah* is that when an estate is small, the daughters' claim to be supported takes priority over the sons' right to inherit. The daughters are entitled by rabbinic enactment to be maintained from the estate until they marry, and if the estate is small enough that fully supporting them leaves nothing for the sons, so be it — the sons may have to go without, while the daughters' support rights are fully honored. In such a case, the daughters' claim effectively controls the estate, even though technical title may still rest with the sons.

### Daughters' Priority

The case Rabbi Yishmael is describing, then, involves a living son, a living daughter, and a pregnant woman. The *baraita*'s teaching can be parsed as follows. *Ha-ben eino ma'akhil* — if there is only a son, the slaves cannot eat, because the fetus might also be a boy and would then hold an equal portion in the estate alongside the living son. *Ha-bat ma'akhila* — but if there is also a daughter, the slaves can eat. Why? Because in a *nechasim mu'atim* situation, the daughter's support rights consume the entire estate. The slaves are effectively at her disposal, and her entitlement is what governs whether they eat.

### Fetus & Daughters

This then raises a further question: even granting the daughter's priority, why doesn't the fetus disrupt her control of the estate? Consider the two possibilities. If the fetus is a boy, does he get a portion of this small estate alongside the daughter? In a *nechasim mu'atim* case, no — the daughter's support rights trump even a male heir's claim. If the fetus is a girl, should she not be entitled to a share of the daughters' support allocation? Here the *halachah* distinguishes sharply: the daughters' support rights established by rabbinic enactment apply only to born daughters. *Kama de-lo nafka la'avir ha'olam, lo takinu rabbanan* — until she emerges into the world as a born person, the Rabbis did not extend their enactment to cover her. The fetus, therefore, cannot diminish the living daughter's claim, whether the fetus would be male or female.

### Estate Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי אוֹקֵימְתַּאּ — בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: שֶׁמָּא יִמָּצֵא הָעוּבָּר זָכַר, וְאֵין לַבָּנוֹת בִּמְקוֹם בֵּן כְּלוּם. אַדְּרַבָּה, נְכָסִים מוּעָטִים דְּבָנוֹת נִינְהוּ! סֵיפָא אֲתָאן לִנְכָסִים מְרוּבִּין. וּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים דְּבָנוֹת נִינְהוּ? וְהָאָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: יְתוֹמִין שֶׁקָּדְמוּ וּמָכְרוּ בִּנְכָסִים מוּעָטִים — מַה שֶּׁמָּכְרוּ מָכְרוּ?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **In what** manner **did you establish** the *baraita*? You established it as referring **to insufficient property.** However, **say the latter clause** of the *baraita*: **Lest the fetus be found** to be **a male, and daughters do not** receive **any** of the inheritance **where** there is **a son.** Yet according to Abaye's explanation, **on the contrary,** the **insufficient property is the daughters',** whether or not there are any sons. The Gemara answers: In **the latter clause we have come to** a different case, in which there is **sufficient property.** ... The Gemara raises another objection to Abaye's explanation: Does an inheritance of **insufficient property** belong **to the daughters? Didn't Rabbi Asi say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** If the male **orphans proceeded to sell** the **insufficient property,** although by rabbinic ordinance it is designated for the daughters' sustenance, **what they sold was sold.** Apparently, the Sages did not expropriate the properties from the male inheritors, but merely designated them for the daughters' sustenance. How, then, can the sons' ownership be disregarded with regard to the slaves' partaking of *teruma*?

The Gemara, however, challenges Abaye's reading. The end of the *baraita* — in Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai's statement — says that if the fetus turns out to be male, the daughters get nothing, *v'ein la-banot bimkom ha-ben klum*. This implies that in the scenario Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai is discussing, the son takes everything and the daughters have no claim. But that is precisely the opposite of a *nechasim mu'atim* case, where the daughters' rights would prevail. So the *baraita* appears to be addressing a *nechasim merubim* — a large estate — not a small one.

Abaye's response is that the two halves of the *baraita* need not address the same scenario: the statement of Rabbi Shimon bar Yochai concerns a large estate, while Rabbi Yishmael's statement concerns a small one.

The Gemara then pushes further. Even granting that the daughters control a small estate in terms of support, the *halachah* established by Rabbi Assi in the name of Rabbi Yochanan is that if the sons of such an estate go ahead and sell it — *yetomim shekadmu u'makhru be-nechasim mu'atim, mah she-makhru makhru* — the sale is binding. This implies that the legal title to the property remains with the sons, not the daughters. The daughters have a *right* against the estate, something like a debt or lien, but title is the sons'. If so, the slaves are the sons' slaves, and the question of whether the daughters "control" the estate in a way that determines eating rights becomes considerably more complicated.

### Mother & Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֶלָּא, מַאי ״בַּת״ דְּקָתָנֵי — אֵם. אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ רַבִּי יוֹסֵי? כּוּלָּהּ רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי קָתָנֵי לַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, what is** the meaning of the word **daughter** in the context of the ruling **that is taught** by Rabbi Yishmael? It means female and is referring to the **mother** of the fetus. She enables her slaves of usufruct property to partake of *teruma*, as her husband's heirs have no share in them, whereas the son does not enable the slaves of guaranteed investment to partake, due to the fetus's share. The Gemara asks: **If so, this is** the same as **Rabbi Yosei's** statement in the first clause. What was added by Rabbi Yishmael? The Gemara answers: Indeed, the **entire** *baraita* **is taught by Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yosei.** There are not conflicting versions of Rabbi Yosei's opinion. Rather, Rabbi Yishmael is clarifying that he is the author of that *baraita*.

Faced with this difficulty, the Gemara proposes an entirely different reading of the *baraita*. The words *bat* and *ben* in Rabbi Yishmael's statement do not mean daughter and son at all — they mean *em* and *av*, mother and father. *Ha-bat ma'akhila* means the mother's property enables eating; *ha-ben eino ma'akhil* means the father's property does not. In other words, the statement is simply recapitulating what Rabbi Yose already established: when there are born children and a pregnant woman, her *avdei milug* eat because she eats, while his *avdei tzon barzel* do not eat because of the fetus's portion in the estate.

This reading is admittedly forced — *bat* and *ben* as stand-ins for mother and father is an unusual usage — but the Gemara accepts it: *kula Rabbi Yishmael beno shel Rabbi Yosi katani*. The entire teaching of Rabbi Yishmael the son of Rabbi Yose was simply an elaboration and explanation of the ruling already stated in his father's name. It adds nothing substantively new.

## Gray Zone Cases

### Gray Zone Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָעוּבָּר, וְהַיָּבָם, וְהָאֵירוּסִין, וְהַחֵרֵשׁ, וּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד — פּוֹסְלִין, וְלֹא מַאֲכִילִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the fetus** of a divorcée or a widow whose husband left her pregnant; **and a man whose married brother died childless [*yavam*]; and betrothal; and** a married **deaf-mute; and a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who engaged in intercourse with a woman; if any of these men are Israelites and the woman is the daughter of a priest, they **disqualify** her from partaking of *teruma*. **But** if she is an Israelite and they are priests, they **do not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*.

The *sugya* now turns to a broader framework that extends well beyond the specific question of slaves and *terumah*. The underlying issue is a woman who stands in an intermediate relationship — partially connected to her husband's household, partially not. The practical consequence of this ambiguity plays out differently depending on the woman's lineage and her husband's status.

To appreciate the framework, consider two mirror-image scenarios. First: a *bat kohen* who married a *Yisrael*. If her husband dies and she has no children, she reverts to her father's household and may once again eat *terumah*. But if she has children, she remains connected to her deceased husband's household and does not revert; she cannot eat *terumah*. Second: a *bat Yisrael* who married a *kohen*. If her husband dies and she has children, she remains connected to his household and may continue eating *terumah*. But if there are no children, she reverts to her father's household and loses the right to eat *terumah*.

The gray zone arises when the connection to the husband is real but incomplete. Such a partial connection is strong enough to pull the woman out of her father's household — and thereby prevent a *bat kohen* who married a *Yisrael* from reverting and eating *terumah* — yet it is not strong enough to fully integrate her into the husband's household, and therefore does not entitle a *bat Yisrael* who married a *kohen* to eat *terumah* on the basis of that connection. In the language of the *Mishnah*: these cases *poslim v'lo ma'akhilim* — they invalidate but do not feed.

The *Mishnah* lists several such cases, each presenting its own variety of partial or ambiguous connection: the *ubar* (fetus), the *yavam* (brother-in-law awaiting *yibum*), *erusin* (betrothal without consummation), a *cheresh* (deaf-mute husband), and a nine-year-old in a specific context. Before unpacking each case, the *Mishnah* also mentions a *safek* variant: situations where it is uncertain whether the man is nine years old, or uncertain whether he has reached halachic adulthood through the appearance of two pubic hairs. Even these doubts are sufficient to invalidate.

The *Mishnah* includes a digression about a collapsed building that killed both a man and his niece — his wife — leaving unresolved which died first. This bears on whether the surviving co-wife undergoes *yibum* or *chalitzah*. The connection to the present context is the shared theme of *yavam* and *safek* cases; the Gemara notes it is a digression, explained by the fact that *yavam* was just mentioned and *safek* cases were at hand.

### Fetus Gray Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָעוּבָּר. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ. ״כִּנְעוּרֶיהָ״ — פְּרָט לִמְעוּבֶּרֶת. אִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לֹא מַאֲכִיל לָהּ, יָלוּד — מַאֲכִיל, שֶׁאֵינוֹ יָלוּד — אֵינוֹ מַאֲכִיל.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: It is taught in the mishna that **the fetus** disqualifies its mother from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to do so. The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** married **to an Israelite,** and her husband died and left her pregnant, the fetus **disqualifies her** from partaking of *teruma*, as it is stated: "But if a priest's daughter be a widow, or divorced, and have no child, and is returned to her father's house, as in her youth, she may eat of her father's bread" (Leviticus 22:13). The phrase **"as in her youth" excludes a pregnant woman,** whose body has changed from her youth. **If** she **is an Israelite woman** married **to a priest,** the fetus **does not enable her to partake,** as **one who was born enables** others **to partake** of *teruma*, whereas **one who is not** yet **born does not enable** others **to partake.**

The Gemara now provides the sourcing for each case. Regarding the *ubar*: if the woman is a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael*, the fetus invalidates her from reverting to eat *terumah*, because the verse says *ki-neureiha* — she returns to her father's house as in her youth — and *perat l'muberet*, the case of pregnancy is excluded, as she is still too connected to be considered fully reverted. If she is a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen*, the fetus does not allow her to eat because of the principle *yalud ma'akhil she'ein yalud ma'akhil* — only a born child, not an unborn one, generates the right to eat. No verse is quoted for this latter point; it is treated as an established axiom.

### Yavam Gray Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַיָּבָם. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ. ״וְשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ״ — פְּרָט לְשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם. אִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לָא מַאֲכֵיל לָהּ, ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהָא קִנְיָן דְּאָחִיו הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is taught in the mishna that the same principle applies to **a *yavam*.** The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** who has a levirate bond **to an Israelite, he disqualifies her,** as it is stated in the verse cited above: **"And is returned to her father's house,"** which **excludes a widow waiting for her *yavam*,** who has not returned to her father's house, as a levirate bond was created with her *yavam*. **If she is an Israelite woman** with a levirate bond **to a priest, he does not enable her to partake** of *teruma*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"The purchase of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** woman **is his brother's acquisition.** The bond with her *yavam* ensued from his late brother's marriage to her, not through any action of his own.

The *yavam* — the brother-in-law awaiting *yibum* — presents the same dual dynamic. If the woman is a *bat kohen* and the brother-in-law is a *Yisrael*, he invalidates her from returning to eat *terumah*, because *v'shava el beit aviha* — she can only return to her father's house — and *perat l'shomeres yavam*, she cannot return while she is still bound to the *yavam* and requires *chalitzah* to free herself. If she is a *bat Yisrael* and the *yavam* is a *kohen*, the *zikah* relationship is not strong enough to entitle her to eat, because *kinyan kaspa amar Rachmana* — the Torah requires that she be his acquisition of money, i.e., properly betrothed — and at this stage she remains a woman his brother married, not yet his own acquisition.

### Erusin Gray Zone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֵירוּסִין. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לָהּ, דְּהָא קַנְיַהּ בַּהֲוָיָה. וְאִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לֹא מַאֲכִיל לָהּ מִשּׁוּם דְּעוּלָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches that the same principle **also** applies to **betrothal.** The Gemara explains: **If she is the daughter of a priest** betrothed **to an Israelite, he disqualifies her, as he acquired her by means of betrothal. And if she is an Israelite woman** betrothed **to a priest, he does not enable her to partake** of *teruma* **due to** the reason given by **Ulla:** Although by Torah law a priest's betrothed partakes of *teruma*, the Sages rendered it prohibited for her to do so, lest she allow other members of her family to eat it.

The case of *erusin* — betrothal without consummation — is almost a *kal va-chomer* from the *yavam*: here there has been genuine *kiddushin*, yet the relationship remains in a gray zone. If the woman is a *bat kohen* and her betrothed is a *Yisrael*, the betrothal invalidates her, because *d'hakanya bahavaya* — the verse ובת כהן כי תהיה לאיש זר, "and a daughter of a *kohen* when she belongs to a foreign man," establishes that once there is any connection to another man, she is no longer entirely in her father's household. Even though conceptually the period of *erusin* is still the time when she lives in her father's house before moving to her husband's house at *nissu'in*, the connection to the other man is real and sufficient to disqualify her from eating on the basis of her father's household.

If she is a *bat Yisrael* and her betrothed is a *kohen*, the betrothal does not entitle her to eat. Here the Gemara invokes the ruling of Ulla. There is actually a dispute between the Bavli and the Yerushalmi on this point: the Bavli's logic would suggest that *kiddushin*, which is effected through money and constitutes a form of *kinyan*, should in principle entitle her to eat already at the stage of *erusin*. The Yerushalmi, by contrast, holds that the mechanism for a wife's eating is *beiso hem yokhlu b'lachmo* — being part of his household — which applies only after *nissu'in*. According to the Yerushalmi, it is actually a biblical prohibition for her to eat *terumah* before *nissu'in*. According to the Bavli, Ulla's rationale is practical and rabbinic: during *erusin* the woman still lives in her father's house, and if the *kohen* sends her *terumah*, she will end up sharing it with her father and brothers, who are not permitted to eat it. This concern provides the rabbinic basis for not allowing her to eat during this period.

### Deaf-Mute Marriage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַחֵרֵשׁ. אִי בַּת כֹּהֵן לְיִשְׂרָאֵל הִיא — פָּסֵיל לַהּ, דְּהָא קַנְיַהּ בְּתַקַּנְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן. וְאִי בַּת יִשְׂרָאֵל לְכֹהֵן הִיא — לָא מַאֲכֵיל, ״קִנְיַן כַּסְפּוֹ״ אָמַר רַחֲמָנָא, וְהַאי לָאו בַּר קִנְיָן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** It is **also** taught in the mishna that **a deaf-mute** disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to do so. The Gemara elaborates: **If she is the daughter of a priest** married to a deaf-mute **Israelite, he disqualifies her, as he acquired her through** marriage sanctioned by **an ordinance of the Sages.** Although the marriage of a deaf-mute is invalid by Torah law, the Sages instituted an ordinance validating this type of marriage. **And if she is an Israelite woman** married **to** a deaf-mute **priest, he does not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*, as **the Merciful One states** in the Torah: **"The purchase of his money,** he may eat of it" (Leviticus 22:11), **and this** deaf-mute **is not capable of acquisition** by Torah law, as he is not legally competent.

A marriage to a *cheresh* — a deaf-mute — presents yet another gradient. The status of the *cheresh* in halachic discourse is significant: treated in the Talmudic period as lacking the legal competence of a fully cognizant adult, though recognized as occupying a considerably higher level than a *shoteh*, the Rabbis nonetheless instituted a form of rabbinic marriage for a *cheresh*. That rabbinic institution is sufficient to remove the woman from her father's household in the relevant halachic sense. Therefore, if she is a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael cheresh*, the rabbinic marriage invalidates her from eating *terumah*, *d'hakanya b'takanata d'rabbanan* — through rabbinic enactment she has been acquired. If she is a *bat Yisrael* married to a *kohen cheresh*, she cannot eat, because *kinyan kaspo amar Rachmana v'hai lav bar kinyanei* — the Torah requires a genuine biblical *kiddushin*, and the *cheresh* is not capable of effecting one biblically. The rabbinic institution of marriage, powerful enough to invalidate, is not powerful enough to entitle.

### Nine-Year-Old

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְכוּ׳. קָא סָלְקָא דַּעְתִּין, בְּשׁוֹמֶרֶת יָבָם לְבֶן תֵּשַׁע וְיוֹם אֶחָד. לְמַאי? אִי לְמִיפְסַל — קָטָן נָמֵי מִיפְסַל פָּסֵיל, אִי לְאוֹכוֹלֵי — גָּדוֹל נָמֵי לֹא מַאֲכִיל! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּיָבָם בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד הַבָּא עַל יְבִמְתּוֹ עָסְקִינַן. דְּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קַנְיָא לֵיהּ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: הוֹאִיל וּמִדְּאוֹרָיְיתָא קַנְיָא לֵיהּ וּבִיאָתוֹ בִּיאָה, אֵימָא לוֹכֵיל, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן עָשׂוּ בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד כְּמַאֲמָר בְּגָדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** § It is **also** taught in the mishna that **a nine-year-old** boy disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma* and does not enable her to partake. **It enters our mind** that the mishna is referring **to a widow waiting for her *yavam*,** who is **nine** years **and one day old.** The Gemara therefore inquires: With regard **to what** is this taught? **If** it is with regard **to disqualifying** her from partaking of *teruma*, **a younger** *yavam* **also disqualifies** her, as a levirate bond was created and she cannot return to her father's house. And **if** it was with regard **to enabling** her **to partake** of *teruma*, **an older** *yavam* **does not enable** her **to partake either,** as discussed above. **Abaye said: Here we are dealing with a nine-year-and-one-day-old *yavam* who** already **engaged in intercourse with his *yevama*, as she was** thereby **acquired by him by Torah** law. It might **enter your mind to say** that **since by Torah** law **she was acquired by him, as** the legal status of **his** act of **intercourse is** that of **intercourse,** perhaps **he enables** her **to partake** of *teruma*. The mishna therefore **teaches us** that the Sages **rendered the** legal status of the **intercourse of a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy **like** that of **levirate betrothal** by means of money or a document performed **by an adult man,** which is not sufficient for her to partake of *teruma*. Since levirate betrothal is effective only by rabbinic law, the *yevama* is not considered the acquisition of his money by Torah law and may not partake of *teruma*.

The case of the nine-year-old is the most complex in this section. Initially, it might seem to refer to a *yibum* scenario: a woman waiting for *yibum* from a nine-year-old brother-in-law who consummates the relationship. The Gemara, however, works through the logic and finds this reading inadequate. If the concern is that the *yavam* prevents her return to her father's house — the *poslim* side — even a one-day-old infant would create that bond, since she is a *shomeres yavam* regardless of the *yavam*'s age. And if the concern is whether he entitles her to eat — the *ma'akhilim* side — then even an adult *yavam* does not entitle her to eat until consummation. So a nine-year-old *yavam* who has actually had relations would be an interesting case: Abaye initially suggests that the novelty is that although the act of *bi'ah* is biblically valid as an act of *yibum* — *de-mid'oraita kani lei* — the Rabbis treated the sexual act of a nine-year-old as equivalent only to *ma'amar*, the rabbinic act of betrothal, rather than full consummation. She might think she is entitled to eat since biblically the *yibum* is complete; the teaching comes to say she is not.

Rava rejects this reading, however, and points out that the subsequent clause of the *Mishnah* — *safek ben tesha shanim, safek eino* — becomes unintelligible under Abaye's interpretation. If a definite nine-year-old who has had relations does not entitle her to eat, it would be obvious that an uncertain nine-year-old also does not. But the *Mishnah* appears to teach it as a separate point, implying that in the case of certainty the answer would be different.

Rava therefore reframes the entire case. The nine-year-old here does not belong to the gray-zone section at all. Rather, it belongs to the following category: people whose sexual act *invalidates* the woman completely. The *Mishnah* is transitioning to a new list — people whose forbidden sexual contact renders the woman a *zonah* or *chalalah* and disqualifies her from marrying a *kohen* or eating *terumah*. Even a nine-year-old from such a category has this effect. And if it is uncertain whether he is nine years old, the doubt itself is sufficient to invalidate — which now makes perfect sense, because even certainty that he is younger would not help her if it is a *safek* whether the disqualifying act occurred.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אִי הָכִי, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי: סָפֵק בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד סָפֵק שֶׁאֵינוֹ — הַשְׁתָּה וַדַּאי בֶּן תֵּשַׁע לֹא מַאֲכִיל, סָפֵק מִיבַּעְיָא?! אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: בְּבֶן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד דְּהָנָךְ פְּסוּלִים קָתָנֵי, דְּפָסְלִי בְּבִיאָתָן. וְכִדְתַנְיָא: בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, גֵּר, עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי, מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, כּוּתִי, נָתִין, חָלָל וּמַמְזֵר שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל כֹּהֶנֶת לְוִיָּה וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית — פְּסָלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to him: If so,** consider **the latter clause** of the mishna, **which teaches** that a boy with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** whether **he is nine years and one day old** and **uncertainty** whether **he is not,** disqualifies a woman from partaking of *teruma*, and he does not enable her to partake. **Now** that, according to your explanation, one who is **definitely nine** years **old does not enable** her **to partake** of *teruma*, **is** it **necessary** to teach the same concerning a boy with regard to whom there is **uncertainty** as to whether or not he reached that age? **Rather, Rava said** that the mishna **is teaching** this *halakha* **with regard to a nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is one **of those unfit** males listed in a *baraita*, **who disqualify** a woman from marrying a priest **by their intercourse,** as they are unfit to enter the assembly of Israel through marriage, **as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **A nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is **an Ammonite or a Moabite convert;** or who is **an Egyptian or an Edomite** convert; or who is either **a Samaritan [*kuti*], a Gibeonite, a *ḥalal*, or a *mamzer*,** when he **engaged in intercourse with a priestess,** i.e., the daughter of a priest, **a Levite, or an Israelite,** he thereby **disqualified her** from marrying a priest, and, in the case of the daughter of a priest, from partaking of *teruma*.

## Forbidden Acts

### Invalidation Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן תֵּשַׁע שָׁנִים וְיוֹם אֶחָד, גֵּר, עַמּוֹנִי וּמוֹאָבִי, מִצְרִי וַאֲדוֹמִי, כּוּתִי, נָתִין, חָלָל וּמַמְזֵר שֶׁבָּאוּ עַל כֹּהֶנֶת לְוִיָּה וְיִשְׂרְאֵלִית — פְּסָלוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara addresses the matter **itself** and cites the complete *baraita*. **A nine-year-and-one-day-old** boy who is **an Ammonite or a Moabite convert;** or who is **an Egyptian or an Edomite** convert; or who is either **a Samaritan, a Gibeonite, a *ḥalal*, or a *mamzer*,** when he **engaged in intercourse with a priestess,** or **a Levite, or an Israelite,** he thereby **disqualified her** from marrying into the priesthood.

With Rava's reframing established, the Gemara turns directly to the new topic: the category of sexual acts that completely invalidate a woman, removing her from the rights of *kehunah* — either the right to eat *terumah* if she is a *bat kohen*, or the right to marry a *kohen* if she is a *bat Yisrael* or *Leviyah*.

### Forbidden Partners

The *baraita* states: a nine-year-old and a day who is a *ger*, a convert of Ammonite or Moabite origin — forbidden by the verse *lo yavo Ammoni bikhal Hashem* — or an Egyptian, an Edomite (before the third generation of conversion), a *Kuti* (Samaritan, treated as not fully Jewish in this context), a *nasin* (of Gibeonite descent), a *chalal* (a *kohen* who has lost his sanctity through a disqualifying union), or a *mamzer* — any of these who engages in sexual relations with a *kohenet*, a *Leviyah*, or a *Yisraelit*, *pasluha*: they invalidate her. If she is a *bat kohen*, she is disqualified from eating *terumah*. If she is a *Yisraelit* or *Leviyah*, she is disqualified from marrying a *kohen*.

It is worth noting the structural diversity of this list. Some of these figures are forbidden to marry *any* Jew — a *mamzer* cannot marry into the community of Israel, nor can an Ammonite or Moabite convert. Others are specifically forbidden to *kohanim* — a *chalal*, for example, does not create a problem for marriage to a *Yisrael* but does affect the woman's *kehunah* status. The Gemara will distinguish between these two sub-categories, identifying the first group as *psulei kahal* — those barred from the general community — and the second group as *psulei kehunah* — those specifically barred in the context of priestly marriage. The *chalal* in particular occupies an unusual position: his act of sex is not itself a forbidden one in the ordinary sense, but it renders the woman a *chalalah*.

### R. Yossi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹסֵי אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁזַּרְעוֹ פָּסוּל — פּוֹסֵל, כֹּל שֶׁאֵין זַרְעוֹ פָּסוּל — אֵינוֹ פּוֹסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei says:** Of the individuals mentioned above, **anyone whose offspring is unfit** to enter the assembly of Israel, **disqualifies** a woman with whom he engaged in intercourse from marrying into the priesthood. However, **anyone whose offspring is not unfit does not disqualify** a woman through intercourse.

Rabbi Yose qualifies the general rule: *kol she-zaro pasul, posel* — only one whose offspring would be disqualified invalidates the woman. *Kol she-ein zaro pasul, eino posel* — one whose offspring would not be disqualified does not invalidate her. The implication is that there are cases of forbidden sexual contact where the resulting child would not be halachically problematic. One such scenario might involve a man who is at the boundary of permissibility — for example, the third generation of Egyptian or Edomite converts, who are just crossing the threshold into full eligibility. In such edge cases, Rabbi Yose would hold that the act does not invalidate the woman.

### R. Shimon Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כֹּל שֶׁאַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ — אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ, וְכֹל שֶׁאֵין אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא בִּתּוֹ — אִי אַתָּה נוֹשֵׂא אַלְמְנָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Anyone whose daughter you may marry, you may marry his widow,** even if you are a priest. Anyone whose daughter may marry a Jew of unflawed lineage does not disqualify a woman with whom he engaged in intercourse from marrying into the priesthood. **And anyone whose daughter you may not marry, you may not marry his widow** if you are a priest.

Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel articulates his position as a memorable rule: *kol she-ata nosei bito, ata nosei almanato* — whoever's daughter you may marry, you may marry his widow (in the sense of the woman with whom he had relations). *V'kol she-ei ata nosei bito, ei ata nosei almanato* — whoever's daughter you may not marry, you may not marry the woman he has rendered a widow. The import is that the eligibility of the man's daughter to marry a *kohen* determines whether his sexual act invalidates the woman. At first glance this seems very close to Rabbi Yose's position — in both cases the determining factor is the status of offspring — and the Gemara will address the relationship between these views.

## Source & Scope

### Verse Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב, אָמַר קְרָא: ״וּבַת כֹּהֵן כִּי תִהְיֶה לְאִישׁ זָר״, כֵּיוָן שֶׁנִּבְעֲלָה לְפָסוּל לָהּ — פְּסָלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived, that intercourse with an unfit man renders a woman unfit to partake of *teruma* and marry a priest? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: The verse states: "And if a priest's daughter be married to a common man [*ish zar*],** she shall not eat of that which is set apart from the sacred" (Leviticus 22:12). It may be derived that **since she engaged in intercourse with one who is unfit for her, he disqualified her** from marrying into the priesthood, as the literal meaning of the expression *ish zar* is a man who is excluded.

Having laid out the rules, the Gemara asks: *mena hani mili*? Where do we derive the foundational principle that forbidden sexual intercourse invalidates a woman — specifically, that a *bat kohen* who has such relations loses her right to eat *terumah*? Rav Yehudah in the name of Rav cites the verse: *u-vat kohen ki tihyeh l'ish zar* — "and a daughter of a *kohen* when she belongs to a foreign man." In its simple reading, this refers to a *bat kohen* who marries a *Yisrael* and can no longer eat *terumah* during the marriage. But the Gemara derives from it a more far-reaching principle: *keivan she-niv'alah l'pasul lah* — once she has had relations with a *zar* in the sense of someone wholly forbidden to her, she is permanently invalidated from eating *terumah*, not merely during the marriage. The word *zar* is read not simply as "non-*kohen*" but as "someone completely foreign to her" — that is, halachically prohibited to her.

The Gemara then carefully accounts for every component of the verse's derivation. One might object that this verse is needed to teach the simple *pshat* — that a *bat kohen* married to a *Yisrael* cannot eat *terumah*. But that, the Gemara replies, can be derived from the continuation of the passage: *v'shava el beit aviha mi-lechem aviha tochel* — "she shall return to her father's house and eat of her father's bread" — implying that while married she does not eat. So the *ki tihyeh* clause is available for the new *derasha*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ דְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא בַּת כֹּהֵן דְּמִינַּסְבָא לְזָר — לָא תֵּיכוּל! הָהִיא, מִ״וְּשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ כִּנְעוּרֶיהָ מִלֶּחֶם אָבִיהָ תֹּאכֵל״ נָפְקָא. מִדְּקָאָמַר רַחֲמָנָא: ״וְשָׁבָה אֶל בֵּית אָבִיהָ ... תֹּאכֵל״, מִכְּלָל דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לָא אָכְלָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **That** verse **is necessary** to teach the *halakha* **that the Merciful One says: The daughter of a priest who marries a non-priest,** even one that she is permitted to marry, **may not partake** of *teruma*. Therefore, it cannot be the source for the *halakha* that intercourse with an unfit man renders a woman unfit to partake of *teruma* and marry a priest. The Gemara answers: **That** prohibition **is derived from** the verse "But if a priest's daughter be a widow, or divorced, and have no child, **and is returned to her father's house, as in her youth, she may eat of her father's bread" (**Leviticus 22:13). **From the fact that the Merciful One says: "And is returned to her father's house…she may eat," it may be inferred that initially,** while married to a non-priest, **she** was **not** permitted to **eat.** Therefore, the prohibition against a woman who engaged in intercourse with an unfit man partaking of *teruma* may be derived from the former verse, as it is not necessary for this *halakha*.

One might then object that if we derive the prohibition of eating during marriage inferentially, it carries only the weight of a *mitzvat asei* — a positive commandment's implicit negative. We need the Torah to state a direct *lav*. But that too is addressed: the prohibition of a *Yisrael* eating *terumah* — and by extension, a woman connected to a *Yisrael* — is derived from *v'chol zar lo yochal kodesh*, "no stranger shall eat of sacred things," which appears twice in the relevant passages. One instance teaches that a non-*kohen* cannot eat *terumah*; the other teaches that a woman married to a non-*kohen* cannot eat. The word *v'chol* in the second instance further teaches, following Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Chanina, that it is specifically the status of *zarus* — foreignness — that disqualifies, and not *aninus* (the state of mourning before burial). An *onen* is forbidden to eat *korbanos* but not forbidden to eat *terumah*. All of this is accounted for, leaving *ki tihyeh l'ish zar* free to teach the invalidating effect of forbidden sexual relations.

The Gemara also notes another teaching derived from this passage. A *baraita* teaches that when a woman reverts to her father's household after her husband's death, *chozeret l'terumah v'einah chozeret la-chazeh v'shok* — she returns to eating *terumah* but does not return to eating the *chazeh v'shok*, the breast and thigh portions of certain offerings. The phrase *bi-trumat ha-kodashim lo tochal* is read as *bi-murum min ha-kodashim lo tochal* — she no longer partakes of what is lifted up from the *korbanos*. Once she has been married to a non-*kohen*, that permanent exclusion from *kodashim kalim* remains, even after she reverts and resumes *terumah*. The phrasing *bi-trumat ha-kodashim* rather than simply *ba-kodashim* signals that both *terumah* and *kodashim* are being addressed in a single formulation.

### Extra Vav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי מֵהַהִיא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: לָאו הַבָּא מִכְּלַל עֲשֵׂה — עֲשֵׂה, כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא הַאי לְלָאו. [לָאו] מִ״וְכָל זָר לֹא יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ״ נָפְקָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this answer: **If** the prohibition against the daughter of a priest who married a non-priest partaking of *teruma* had been derived only **from that** latter verse, **I would have said** that it is a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva, as it is stated in positive form, and according to the principle that **a prohibition that stems from a positive mitzva is a positive mitzva,** she would not be liable to receive a court-imposed punishment. **The Merciful One** therefore **writes that** former verse, **to** establish an explicit **prohibition.** The Gemara counters: The **prohibition** against the wife of a non-priest partaking of *teruma* **is derived from** a different verse: **"No common man may eat of the sacred"** (Leviticus 22:10).

Having established the invalidation of a *bat kohen* from *terumah*, the Gemara asks how this extends to all women — *Leviyah* and *Yisraelit* as well. The answer, following Rabbi Abba in the name of Rav, is derived from the prefix ו in ובת כהן: *bat u-vat* — the extra ו extends the ruling beyond the *bat kohen* to include all women. Since the law of invalidation now applies to a *Leviyah* or *Yisraelit*, the relevant consequence cannot be the loss of *terumah* eating rights — those women never ate *terumah* to begin with. The implication must therefore be the prohibition of marriage to a *kohen*. The Gemara notes that this derivation follows even the *Rabbanan* who generally do not learn from extra letter forms, since here the entire phrase *u-vat kohen* is superfluous — the verse could have begun simply with *ki tihyeh l'ish zar*. The full prefix phrase is thus available as an extension to all women.

### Terumah Loss

At this point in the derivation, the Gemara has established from the verse that forbidden sexual relations invalidate a woman — specifically that a *bat kohen* is disqualified from eating *terumah*. This much follows from the direct reading of the verse about a *bat kohen* and *terumah*.

### Marriage Ban Q

What remains to be established is that the same act disqualifies all women from marriage to a *kohen*. As noted above, this is the implication of extending the ruling to a *Leviyah* or *Yisraelit* through the extra ו. But the derivation merits closer attention: once we say *bat u-vat* includes all women, and for those women the only relevant consequence is marriage prohibition, the *terumah* dimension and the marriage dimension are brought together under a single biblical source.

### Kal VeChomer

Could one not derive the marriage prohibition through a *kal va-chomer*? If forbidden sex invalidates a *bat kohen* — who has her own intrinsic sanctity, *kedushat ha-guf* — surely it should all the more so disqualify a *bat Yisrael* from marriage to a *kohen*. The Gemara rejects this argument with a sharp reversal: *hee ha-notenet* — it is precisely the opposite. A *bat kohen* eats *terumah* by virtue of her own personal sanctity, *kedushat gufah*, and forbidden sex impinges directly on that sanctity. But a *bat Yisrael* who eats *terumah* does so not by virtue of her own status but by virtue of her child — *bishvil benah*. If her eating is not based on her own sacred status but on an external connection, perhaps forbidden sex, which affects personal status, is simply irrelevant to her ability to eat. *Lo kadish gufah* — her body carries no inherent *kedushah* that the forbidden act can desecrate. The *kal va-chomer* thus fails: far from being more stringent for a *bat Yisrael*, the logic of personal sanctity would suggest she should be *less* affected. A verse is therefore necessary, and the verse provides it through the extension to all women.

The *sugya* thus concludes this daf with the question of invalidation from *terumah* fully resolved, and sets the stage for the following discussion: the derivation of the prohibition on marrying a *kohen* that applies to all women who have had forbidden sexual relations — which will be taken up on the following daf.
