# Bava Batra 69 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Town Sale & Enclosures

### Braita: Town Contents

The daf opens with a *braisa* addressing the sale of a town. The *braisa* states that when someone buys a town, he does not acquire the enclosures containing wild animals, birds, and fish — *v'lo bivrin shel chaya v'shel of v'shel dagim*. These are not mere cages but plots of land functioning as nature preserves.

A contradiction, however, is immediately raised. A second *braisa* states that if a town had outlying daughter-towns, or if the town extended partly over water, or if it possessed enclosures of wild animals and fish, *harei elu nimkarim imah* — they are sold along with it. The two *braisos* appear to flatly contradict one another regarding these enclosures.

### Enclosure Orientation

The *Gemara* resolves the contradiction: *lo kashya*. The distinction turns on the orientation of the enclosure's opening. If the opening faces inward toward the town, the enclosure is regarded as an extension of the town and is sold with it. If the opening faces outward, it is not considered part of the town and is excluded from the sale.

### Forest Designations

A further question is raised from the *braisa*'s mention of forests. The *braisa* appears to exclude forests that are *muktzin lah* — designated for the town. The *Gemara* corrects the reading: the word should be understood as *muktzin mimena*, meaning forests that are separated or set apart from the town. It is those separated forests that are not sold; forests that are designated and integrated with the town are included in the sale. With this emendation, the *braisa* is rendered consistent.

## Field Sale Basics

### Included Items

The *mishnah* now turns to the sale of a field — *hamocher et hasadeh*. One noteworthy feature of this *mishnah* is that certain items are sold with the field even when they are not *mechubar* — physically attached to the ground. This contrasts with the earlier discussion of a bathhouse or similar structures, where even necessary equipment that was not nailed down was generally not sold. Here, items that are functionally necessary to the field are included in the sale regardless of physical attachment.

The *mishnah* lists what is sold with a field: stones that are needed for the field, *shehein ltzorkah*; the sticks in a vineyard that are necessary for it, *shehein ltzorko*; grain that is still attached to the ground, *shehi mechuberes l'karka*; a patch of growing reeds that is smaller than a *beis rova*; and a guard's hut that is not plastered over. Regarding the hut, two textual traditions (*girsaos*) exist. According to one version, a hut that is not plastered over is sold with the field because its impermanence marks it as a mere appendage of the field rather than an independent structure. The other *geirsa* inverts this: a hut that is plastered over is more firmly bonded to the ground and therefore sold with it, while an unplastered hut is more akin to a movable object and is not.

### Young Trees Included

The *mishnah* continues: a carob tree that has not yet been grafted, *she'eino murkav*, and the young growth of a sycamore tree, *pasulat hashikmah*, are also sold with the field. The logic is that when trees are still young — before their branches have been cut and grafted — they have not yet achieved an independent identity. They are simply part of the field. Once a carob or sycamore reaches maturity and begins to be grafted and pruned, it takes on a distinct character and is no longer merely an element of the field in which it stands.

One might ask why the grafting of a carob would be relevant to its status. The grafting referred to here is not necessarily a matter of *kilayim* — the prohibition against mixed species. Rather, it refers to the horticultural practice of grafting a more productive branch onto a younger tree, such as grafting a vigorous carob variety onto an existing one, in order to improve yield. It is this process of cultivation that elevates the tree to the status of a significant and independent entity.

### Excluded Items

The second half of the *mishnah* follows almost mechanically from the first. Not sold with the field are: stones that are not necessary for it; the sticks in the vineyard that are not needed; and grain that has already been harvested and cut from the ground. The *mishnah* notes that if the seller explicitly said "the field and all its contents," everything in the previous list becomes included. However, even with that broader formulation, several items remain excluded regardless: a patch of growing reeds that is the size of a *beis rova* or larger, the guard's hut that is plastered over (according to our *geirsa*), a grafted carob, and the mature sycamore.

### Rashbam's Analysis

The *Rashbam* offers a conceptual framework to explain why these last items are excluded even when the seller says "all its contents." His position is that these items are not merely objects *within* the field — they are themselves independent fields. A patch of reeds of sufficient size, a large plastered hut, a grafted carob of considerable maturity, and above all the great sycamore — these are not seen as things located within the field but as distinct parcels situated alongside or within it.

The *Rashbam* illustrates with an analogy: if a person owns three adjacent plots of land, labeled A, B, and C, and sells "A and C and everything within them," the buyer does not receive B, even though B lies between them. So too, when a field contains a section with its own identity — its own name and independent character — it is not "within" the field being sold; it is its own field, and the language "all its contents" cannot reach it.

This same principle, the *Gemara* makes explicit through Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, extends beyond a patch of reeds. Any sub-plot within a field that possesses its own distinct identity — *yesh lah shem bifnei atzmah* — is not sold with the field even under expansive sale language. Rav Papa illustrates: if a small plot of spices or roses within a field is known as "so-and-so's rose garden" — *varda d'planya* — it carries its own name and therefore its own legal identity. Selling "the field and everything in it" does not convey "the rose garden."

This principle of independent identity also applies to the hut, despite the fact that it is physically attached to the ground. A well-constructed, plastered hut has become its own entity. And the grafted carob or mature sycamore, even if it is not large in the absolute sense, once it has been grafted and cultivated, it is regarded as its own thing — *af al gav d'katini* — even if they are not large, they have acquired an independent identity.

The *Gemara* then raises an unresolved question — *ba'ei Rav Eliezer* — regarding doorframes. If one sells a house, do the doorframes go with it? The *Rashbam* explains that doorframes in that era could be attached after construction was complete, affixed with nails rather than built into the wall itself. The *Gemara* asks: where they are affixed with plaster, they are clearly part of the structure and are sold with it; but where they are held in place with nails alone, and can in principle be removed without structural damage, the question is unresolved — *teiku*. Similarly unresolved is the question of window frames (*malbenot shel chalonot*), which may be purely aesthetic, and the padded frames placed under bed legs to prevent rot — where the question depends on whether they move with the bed or remain fixed in place.

## Rabbi Meir Debate

### Weight Stones

The *Gemara* now turns to the *mishnah*'s first item — stones necessary for the field — and asks what type of stones the *mishnah* has in mind. Two explanations are offered. The *Gemara* as interpreted locally in Bavel identifies them as *avnei de'achfa* — weight stones, used to hold down bundled grain so it does not scatter in the wind. The *Rashbam* compares this to placing stones over gathered leaves to prevent them from blowing away.

*Ulla* offers a different explanation: the *mishnah* refers to stones arranged in preparation for building a wall — *avanim hasdurot legader*. These are stones that have been laid out in neat rows, poised to be incorporated into a boundary wall, though the building has not yet commenced. Rabbi Hiyya's *braisa* goes one step further and speaks of *avanim tzevurot legader* — stones piled in an unordered heap, not yet laid in rows, merely gathered in anticipation of wall construction.

The *Gemara* then maps these positions onto the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan*, a debate that properly pervades the entire chapter. Rabbi Meir holds that when a vineyard is sold, the seller conveys *kol tashmisheiha hakarem* — all the accessories of the vineyard; he applies a broader understanding of what "goes with" the sale. The *Rabbanan* take a narrower view.

Applied to weight stones: according to Rabbi Meir, stones that have been designated (*metaknan*) for use in the field are sold with it, even if they have never actually been placed. Designation alone suffices. According to the *Rabbanan*, the stones must have actually been used — placed in position at least once — before they acquire the status of field accessories. Applied to *Ulla*'s explanation: according to Rabbi Meir, stones designated for the wall but not yet laid in rows (*d'lo sidran*) are included; according to the *Rabbanan*, they must actually have been arranged in order.

### Vineyard Sticks

The same analytical structure governs the *mishnah*'s mention of vineyard sticks. The sticks referred to are trellises — supports placed beneath the grapevines to help them grow. The school of Rabbi Yannai explains that these are *kanim hamichulakim*, sticks that have been specially prepared and placed beneath the vines. The *Rashbam* notes that a French gloss in the text sounds cognate to the word "trellis." *Tosafot* reads *michulakim* differently, interpreting it to mean that the sticks were peeled to prevent rot, rather than split or crossed into a lattice.

According to Rabbi Meir, once the sticks have been smoothed and prepared (*mishpayan*), they are considered sold with the vineyard even if they have not yet been erected. According to the *Rabbanan*, the sticks must actually have been put in place — *mokman* — before they are included in the sale.

A student question surfaces the precise nature of the *metaknan* stage: is it a purely verbal designation, or does it require some physical act? The *Rashbam*'s language — *metukanot umezumanot letzorcha* — suggests something more than merely saying "these are for the field." It may require physically moving or setting aside the objects for that purpose, though not necessarily altering them. The *Gemara*'s classic framework of *hazmana milsa hi* versus *hazmana lav milsa hi* — whether designation constitutes a legal act — underlies the entire debate between Rabbi Meir and the *Rabbanan*.

### Unnecessary Items

The parallel structure continues for the items *not* sold with the field. The *mishnah* lists stones not necessary for the field and sticks in the vineyard not needed for it, and one might assume these refer simply to objects with different functions. The *Gemara*, however, reframes the distinction entirely in terms of the stage of preparation. There are not two different categories of stones or sticks — there is simply one category at an earlier stage of designation or use.

For unnecessary stones: according to Rabbi Meir, these are stones that have not yet been designated at all (*d'lo mesaknan*); for the *Rabbanan*, they are stones that were designated but never placed (*d'lo machsan*). For *Ulla*'s version: according to Rabbi Meir, stones not yet designated; according to the *Rabbanan*, stones designated but not yet laid in rows. The logic is perfectly parallel for the sticks: according to Rabbi Meir, sticks not yet smoothed; according to the *Rabbanan*, sticks smoothed but not yet erected.

The *Gemara* also addresses grain that has been harvested. Even grain that has been cut and spread on the ground to dry — which needs and uses the ground to complete the drying process — is not sold with the field once it has been severed. The *mishnah* is clear: the sale of the field conveys only what is physically attached. And regarding the patch of reeds, the formal criterion of size — the *beis rova* — governs absolutely: if smaller than that size it is sold with the field even if the reeds are thick; if it reaches that size it is not sold even if the reeds are thin.

The *Gemara* extends the *Rashbam*'s principle through Rabbi Hiyya bar Abba: the exclusion of patches with independent identity is not limited to a stand of reeds but applies to any sub-plot with its own name. This principle holds even when the seller employs the expansive formula *kol ma shebetocha*. Rav Papa's illustration — "Shimon's rose garden" — conveys that what gives a plot its independent identity is not its size or composition alone, but whether it is understood by the community as a distinct entity with its own name.

## Scripture & Boundaries

### Vayikam Pasuk

The *Gemara* now asks for the scriptural source of the rule that trees are generally sold with a field. Rav Yehuda in the name of Rav cites the verse from the purchase of the Machpelah cave — *vayakam sdei Efron asher bamachpelah asher lifnei mamre, hasadeh vehamearah asher bo, vechol ha'etz asher basadeh asher bechol gvulov saviv* (Genesis 23:17). The verse states that "all the trees in the field, within all its boundaries all around" were conveyed in the transaction.

From this verse, the *Gemara* derives two things. First, the baseline rule: when you buy a field, you receive *kol etz* — all the trees in it. Second, and more subtly, the verse limits this to trees *asher bechol gvulov saviv* — within all the surrounding boundaries. This limiting phrase implies that some trees are excluded: those that do not require the surrounding boundaries to define them. The *Rashbam* explains this as follows. Ordinary trees in a field require boundaries to establish whose side they fall on — neighbors might dispute whether a small tree stands within one plot or the other. But a tree of exceptional size and prominence — a mature sycamore or grafted carob — is so recognizable and significant that there has never been any question whose land it occupies. Everyone always knew. Such a tree therefore does not "need" the surrounding boundary, and the verse's phrase *asher bechol gvulov saviv* pointedly excludes it from the automatic conveyance.

This is admittedly a *drash* rather than strict *pshat*, and this is acknowledged plainly. Yet as a *halacha l'maaseh* it establishes that trees of independent identity are not simply swept up in the sale of a field.

### Boundary Trees

Rav Mesharsheya draws a further inference from the same verse: *mikan l'meitzarin min hatorah* — from here we see the concept of boundary lines as part of the conveyance. The *Rashbam* explains that when one sells a field, the boundary line of the field is included in the sale, as are any trees planted along that boundary. The verse's formulation — *asher bechol gvulov saviv*, surrounding on all sides — encompasses the trees that form or mark the perimeter of the field.

One might wonder what *chiddush* this introduces, since if the boundary trees belong to the seller they obviously pass to the buyer along with the rest of the field. The possible novelty is that one cannot claim, "I sold you the field but retained the boundary trees for myself as a separate entity." The verse establishes that the boundary trees are part of the field conveyance and cannot be independently reserved. This is analogous to a tenant who has charge of everything within an apartment but has no claim on the walls themselves — one might have thought the seller could argue that the boundary trees, like a wall, belong to a separate category. The verse forecloses that argument.

## Deed Language

### Standard Formula

Rav Yehuda now turns to actual documentary practice. When a seller wishes to convey all trees along with a parcel of land, the standard deed formula reads: *kni lach diklin ve'talin ve'hutzin ve'tzitzin* — acquire from me the palm trees, the other trees, the young palms, and the young other trees. The precise meaning of each term is disputed among the *rishonim*. According to the Soncino's interpretation, *diklei* are mature palm trees, *talin* are mature non-palm trees, *hutzin* are immature palm trees, and *tzitzin* are immature non-palm trees. The *Rashbam* understands all four terms as referring to varieties of palm trees.

### Shofra d'Shtara

The *Gemara* immediately notes that this elaborate formula is technically unnecessary. We have just established from the *mishnah* that trees are automatically sold with a field. Yet even so, the formula is used — *afilu hachi shufra d'shtara hu* — it is an embellishment of the deed. *Shofra d'shtara* means making the *shtar* more explicit and elegant, spelling out in formal language what the law would provide by default. The *Rashbam* explains the practical benefit: it ensures that no question arises, and accommodates the possibility that a *beis din* of limited learning might not be aware of the default rule.

There is, however, a risk to verbosity in legal documents. The principle *kol hamosif goreia* — whoever adds, subtracts — means that explicit language can sometimes narrow rather than expand the default scope of a transaction. As the *Gemara* is about to demonstrate, the precise preposition or conjunction used in a deed can significantly alter what is conveyed.

## Deed Interpretation

### Ara v'Diklei

The *Gemara* now examines a case in which the deed reads *ara ve'diklei* — "land and palm trees." The conjunction *vav* links two separate items: the land, and palm trees. Since palm trees are already included in the sale of land by default, this phrasing implies that the palm trees referred to are not the ones within the land — those are already conveyed automatically — but rather palm trees located elsewhere. The seller is conveying this plot of land *plus* additional palm trees he owns in some other location.

The *Gemara* then rules: *chazinan* — we look at the circumstances. If the seller owns palm trees elsewhere, he must give the buyer a minimum of two (*t'rei diklei*), and he gets to choose which two. If he owns no palm trees elsewhere, he must go and purchase two palm trees to deliver. If he does own palm trees elsewhere but they are encumbered by a lien, he must pay off the lien so that the buyer can receive unencumbered trees. The entire analysis flows from reading *ara ve'diklei* as promising something beyond the land itself.

### Tosafot Reading

*Tosafot* challenges this reading with a strikingly different *geirsa* and interpretation. According to *Tosafot*, the palm trees in question are in fact growing within the plot of land. Had the seller said nothing, the buyer would have received all the palm trees automatically. But by saying *ara ve'diklei* — "land and palm trees" — the seller may have inadvertently reduced what the buyer receives. The use of the conjunctive *vav*, which treats the palm trees as a separate item alongside the land rather than as an integral part of it, may signal that only some palm trees are being conveyed, not all.

This reading inverts the intuition. Saying more — specifying the palm trees explicitly — actually grants the buyer less than silence would have. *Tosafot* contrasts this with the earlier formula *diklin ve'talin ve'hutzin ve'tzitzin* and suggests the determinative factor may be the use of *vav* rather than *beis*, treating the elements as separate rather than integrated.

### Ara B'Diklei

The *Gemara* then addresses the formula *ara b'diklei* — "land with its palm trees." Here the preposition *beis* indicates that the palm trees are integrated within the land being sold. The *Gemara* rules: *chazinan* — if there are indeed palm trees in the land, the buyer receives them; nothing is added beyond what the default rule would have provided, but the explicit statement confirms it. If, however, there turn out to be no palm trees in the land, this constitutes a *mekach ta'ut* — a defective transaction. The seller represented the land as one that has palm trees, and the buyer received something materially different from what was described.

The *Rashbam* notes that the issue is not one of overcharging, since the principle of *ein ona'ah b'karka'ot* — that the laws of overreaching (*ona'ah*) do not apply to real estate transactions — would normally protect a seller from claims of overpayment. Rather, the claim here is qualitative misrepresentation: the buyer was promised a field *with palm trees* and received a field without them. That is a *mekach ta'ut* regardless of the financial magnitude.

## Palm Tree Scenarios

### Scenario One

The first scenario revisits *ara b'diklei* — "land with its palm trees." This is the clearest case of seller representation. The seller has described the land as possessing palm trees, and if palm trees are present, the buyer receives a full conveyance. If they are absent, the transaction is a *mekach ta'ut* and can be voided.

### Scenario Two

The second scenario involves the phrase *atra d'diklei* — "land that is a place fit for palm trees," a location suitable for growing palm trees. Here the seller has made no representation that palm trees actually exist on the property. He has said only that the land is capable of supporting them. Accordingly, *leit diklei* — the buyer has no claim to palm trees. And if there happen to be no palm trees on the land, it is not a *mekach ta'ut*, because the seller never promised palm trees — only suitability. As the *Rashbam* explains, *dechazi lediklei ka'amar lei* — he said it was fit for palm trees, not that there were palm trees. The buyer cannot complain.

### Scenario Three

The third scenario is the most striking. The deed reads: *l'bar midikla planya* — "except for that particular palm tree." The seller explicitly reserves one specific palm tree for himself. The *Gemara* asks: what does this exception tell us?

*Chazinan* — we look at what kind of tree it is. If the reserved tree is a productive, high-quality palm tree (*dikla tava hu*), then *shiurei sheyarei* — we read the language literally, and the seller simply kept that one tree for himself, perhaps out of personal attachment to a productive specimen. The rest of the field, including any other palm trees, passes to the buyer.

But if the reserved tree is a non-productive or inferior palm tree (*dikla bisha hu*), then the *Gemara* draws a remarkable inference: *kol sheken hanei* — if the seller is explicitly reserving even this poor specimen for himself, then certainly he is retaining all the good ones as well. The logic is that no one would go to the trouble of carving out an exception for a worthless tree unless it was intended as the least member of a broader category he is retaining. The explicit exception of the worst tree implies retention of all trees, including the superior ones. This is a substantial *chiddush* in the reading of contractual language.

## Exclusion Hierarchy

### Trees Exclusion

The *Gemara* now examines the hierarchy of terms when a seller uses the word *ilani* — trees — as an exception. If the deed states *l'bar me'ilani* — "except for the trees" — the analysis proceeds as follows. If the seller owns trees (*ilani*) in the field, the exception refers straightforwardly to those trees. The buyer receives the field minus the trees, which the seller retains.

### Category Application

But what if the seller has no ordinary trees, only palm trees (*diklei*) or grapevines (*gufnei*)? The *Gemara* establishes a hierarchy of substitution. If there are no ordinary trees but there are palm trees, we apply the word *ilani* to the palm trees — *l'bar midiklei*. If there are neither ordinary trees nor palm trees but there are grapevines, we apply the word to the grapevines — *l'bar migufnei*. The principle is that the word must have some referent, and we apply it progressively down the hierarchy until we find one.

This hierarchy is practically significant. A buyer who knows that a field contains no ordinary trees might assume the word "trees" in the exclusion clause is inoperative — and might be surprised to find the seller retaining the palm trees or even the vines.

### Palm/Vine Ambiguity

The final and most complex case: the seller has no ordinary trees but possesses both palm trees and grapevines, and the deed says *l'bar me'ilani*. To which category does the exception apply? The *Gemara* rules *l'bar migufnei* — the exception applies to the grapevines, not the palm trees. The seller retains the grapevines.

Two rationales are offered. The first is *hamotzi mechavero alav hara'aya*: the burden of proof rests on the one who seeks to extract from another's possession. The grapevines are presumed to be of greater value, and since the term is equally ambiguous between the two, the principle of possession (*muchzak*) favors treating the more valuable item as what the seller retained. The second rationale the *Rashbam* offers is that the word *ilan* applies more naturally to a *gefen* than to a *dekel* — that is, a grapevine is linguistically closer to the general category of "tree" than a palm tree is, since palm trees have a distinct enough identity that they are not instinctively what one thinks of when one hears *ilan*.

The *Gemara* leaves the matter at this practical ruling without elaborating further on which principle ultimately governs. What is clear is that the resolution is framed in terms of the meaning of the word itself — which referent it most naturally reaches — rather than in terms of the financial consequences or some explicit *din* of *safek*. The formal linguistic question of which term *ilan* more closely approximates thus carries operative legal weight, however formalistic the inquiry may appear.
