# Bava Batra 175 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Arev Ketubah & Abaye

### Arev Ketubah Intro

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן הָיָה רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הֶעָרֵב לְאִשָּׁה בִּכְתוּבָּתָהּ [וְכוּ׳]. מֹשֶׁה בַּר עַצְרִי עָרְבָא דִּכְתוּבְּתַהּ דְּכַלָּתֵיהּ הֲוָה. רַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ – צוּרְבָּא מִדְּרַבָּנַן הֲוָה, וּדְחִיקָא לֵיהּ מִילְּתָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֵיכָּא דְּנֵיזִיל דְּנַסְּבֵיהּ עֵצָה לְרַב הוּנָא, דִּנְגָרְשַׁהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ וְתֵיזִיל וְתִגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה מֵאֲבוּהּ, וַהֲדַר נַהְדְּרַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** And so **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would say:** If there is **a guarantor for a woman for her marriage contract,** and her husband is divorcing her, the husband must take a vow prohibiting himself from deriving any benefit from her so that he can never remarry her. The Gemara relates an incident pertaining to this ruling: Someone named **Moshe bar Atzari was a guarantor for the marriage contract of his daughter-in-law,** guaranteeing the money promised by his son in the event of death or divorce. **His son,** named **Rav Huna, was a young Torah scholar, and was in financial straits. Abaye said: Is there no one who will go advise Rav Huna that he should divorce his wife, and she will go and collect her marriage contract from** Rav Huna's **father, and then** Rav Huna should **remarry her?**

The daf opens by picking up from 174b, approximately a third of the way down the page, in the context of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's ruling in the *mishnah* concerning an *arev* for a *ketubah*. The *mishnah* addresses a scenario in which a guarantor underwrites the husband's obligation under the *ketubah* — that is, if the marriage ends in divorce or death and the wife cannot collect from the husband's estate, she may collect from the *arev*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that if such a divorce actually takes place, the court must insist that the husband take a vow — *yadirana hana'ah* — prohibiting himself from deriving benefit from her, so as to forestall any collusive scheme whereby husband and wife manufacture a divorce, collect from the *arev*, and subsequently remarry. This ruling in the *mishnah* goes essentially uncontested.

### Moshe bar Atzri

The *gemara* then introduces a narrative involving a figure named Moshe bar Atzri, who had acted as *arev de'ketuvata de'khalatei* — guarantor of his daughter-in-law's *ketubah*, which is to say he guaranteed his own son's obligation under the *ketubah* to the son's wife. His son, Rav Huna, was a *tzurba me'rabanan* — a Torah scholar — who was suffering severe financial hardship and was unable to provide for his household.

### Divorce Scheme

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לֵיכָּא דְּנֵיזִיל דְּנַסְּבֵיהּ עֵצָה לְרַב הוּנָא, דִּנְגָרְשַׁהּ לִדְבֵיתְהוּ וְתֵיזִיל וְתִגְבֵּי כְּתוּבָּה מֵאֲבוּהּ, וַהֲדַר נַהְדְּרַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Is there no one who will go advise Rav Huna that he should divorce his wife, and she will go and collect her marriage contract from** Rav Huna's **father, and then** Rav Huna should **remarry her?**

Abaye, observing this situation, dropped a pointed suggestion — though notably without taking direct responsibility for the advice — that someone ought to go and inform Rav Huna that he should divorce his wife, allow her to collect her *ketubah* from her father-in-law, the *arev*, and then remarry her afterward. The scheme was technically within the legal framework, but its moral dimension is immediately troubling, and the *gemara* goes on to probe it from several directions.

### Rava's Objection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: וְהָא "יַדִּירֶנָּה הֲנָאָה" תְּנַן!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Abaye: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that when a husband divorces his wife in such circumstances he **must take a vow** prohibiting himself from deriving any **benefit** from **her,** thereby precluding the possibility of remarriage?

Rava raised an objection: but the *mishnah* of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel requires precisely that in such a case the man take a vow not to derive benefit from his divorced wife, which would prevent him from remarrying her. The scheme would not work. What is striking about Rava's objection is that it is purely practical — he raises no moral concern, only the question of feasibility.

### Unmarked Divorce

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: אַטּוּ כֹּל דִּמְגָרֵשׁ – בְּבֵי דִינָא מְגָרֵשׁ?
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Is that to say** that **everyone who divorces** his wife **divorces** her **in a court?** Rav Huna could be advised to divorce his wife outside the court, in which case he could do so without being forced to take the prescribed vow.

Abaye responded: *attu kol de-megarish dina de-megarish* — does every person who divorces his wife do so through a *beis din*? If the divorce were conducted without formal rabbinic oversight, who would enforce the requirement of the vow? This answer is deeply troubling on multiple levels. First, if the *halacha* demands *yadirana hana'ah*, then one is bound by that requirement regardless of whether a *beis din* is present to enforce it. Second, and more fundamentally, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel's insistence on the vow makes plain that the underlying scheme is morally suspect — the *arev* did a kindness by guaranteeing the *ketubah*, and to exploit that guarantee through a sham divorce is precisely the sort of conduct the *mishnah* seeks to prevent. The question of how Abaye could advise such a course hangs heavily over the entire passage.

### Kohen Impediment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְסוֹף אִיגַּלַּאי מִילְּתָא דְּכֹהֵן הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Ultimately, it was revealed that** this Rav Huna **was a priest,** who may not marry a divorcée, even his own ex-wife.

The *gemara* then relates: *u-sof igla'ei milta de-kohen hu* — in the end it emerged that Rav Huna, son of Moshe bar Atzri, was a *kohen*. Had he divorced his wife, he would have been permanently prohibited from remarrying her. Whether this was discovered before or after any action was taken, the scheme would have been undone in any case.

### Poverty Follows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הַיְינוּ דְאָמְרִי אִינָשֵׁי: בָּתַר עַנְיָא אָזְלָא עַנְיוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: This** is in accordance with the adage **that people say: Poverty follows the poor.**

Abaye responded with a folk saying: *basar aniya azla aniyusa* — poverty follows the poor. Even this attempt to help Rav Huna was foreclosed. The remark has an air of resignation — despite all efforts, misfortune kept closing off every avenue of relief.

### Scheming Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי אָמַר אַבָּיֵי הָכִי? וְהָא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֵיזֶהוּ "רָשָׁע עָרוּם"? זֶה הַמַּשִּׂיא עֵצָה לִמְכּוֹר בִּנְכָסִים כְּרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But did Abaye** really **say this?** Would he really encourage giving such advice? **But doesn't Abaye** himself **say: Who is a cunning, wicked person? This** is **one who provides advice to sell property in accordance with** the ruling of **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel.** As taught elsewhere (*Ketubot* 95b), if one says: My property is hereby bequeathed to you, and after you die it will pass to so-and-so, Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel rules that the first recipient can sell the property, which would thereby deprive the second recipient from receiving it. Abaye said that giving advice to someone to pursue such action, though it is legally sound, is considered wicked. Here as well, why would he give advice to Rav Huna to divorce his wife only in order to force his father to pay the marriage contract?

Now the *gemara* poses the serious question that was lurking all along: *mi amar Abaye hachi?* How could Abaye have made such a suggestion? Does not Abaye himself say elsewhere that the *eizehu rasha arum* — the wicked clever person — is one who gives advice enabling someone to sell property in the manner ruled upon by Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel in a different context?

That ruling concerns a case where one person gives a field to Reuven with the condition that after Reuven's death it passes to Shimon — *v'acharecha l'Shimon*. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel holds that if Reuven sells the field outright to a third party, Levi, that sale severs the chain and Shimon never receives the field. Since the field is worth more to Levi than to Reuven — Levi gets it permanently, while Reuven had only a life interest — Reuven could in fact profit by selling it. Reuven himself would be entirely within his legal rights to do this. But if a third party were to come and advise Reuven to take advantage of this legal right, that advisor is considered a *rasha* — because he is injecting himself into a *riv lo lo*, a dispute that is none of his business, benefiting Reuven at Shimon's expense, and subverting the clear intention of the original grantor.

Analogously, Abaye was advising Rav Huna to pursue a course that, while technically legal, was deceitful in its very structure and injurious to the *arev* who had done Rav Huna's family a genuine kindness. Even if legally the *arev* was liable, the whole enterprise was built on deception. How can this be reconciled with Abaye's own stated position?

### Son vs. Rasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּנוֹ שָׁאנֵי, וְצוּרְבָּא מֵרַבָּנַן שָׁאנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: When dealing with one's **son** it **is different.** It is not considered wickedness to force a father to give money to his own son. Moreover, when dealing with **a Torah scholar** it **is different.** It is not considered wickedness to procure money for a Torah scholar, because the money enables him to pursue his studies.

The *gemara* offers two answers: *bno shani v'tzurba me'rabanan shani*. First, the case is different because the money would ultimately be passing from a father to his own son. The *arev* for the *ketubah* was Moshe bar Atzri himself — Rav Huna's father. Unlike the field case, where one is diverting assets from a stranger to benefit another stranger against the grantor's intent, here the money moves within the family. The father may not have agreed to this precise mechanism, but there is no transfer from Reuven's pocket to an unrelated Shimon's pocket. That said, this answer is admittedly not fully satisfying. The father agreed to be an *arev* for a specific purpose and on specific terms; invoking those terms through a sham divorce is not what he contemplated.

Second, Rav Huna was a *tzurba me'rabanan*, a Torah scholar, and this distinguishes the case further. The intended point seems to be not that Torah scholars enjoy license to engage in deception, but rather that ensuring a *talmid chacham* can provide for his household is itself a matter of communal concern — not merely a private interest — and therefore Abaye's involvement was not the gratuitous meddling in another's affairs that characterizes the *rasha arum*. Even so, neither answer fully addresses the fundamental problem of the deception involved in the scheme itself, and the *gemara*'s resolution remains, on any honest reading, quite difficult.

## Arev Ketubah No Obligation

### Father Liability

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא עָרֵב הוּא, [וְקַיְימָא לַן] עָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה לֹא מִשְׁתַּעְבֵּד!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks another question with regard to this incident: **But wasn't** Moshe bar Atzari **a guarantor? And we maintain,** as the Gemara will soon note, that **a guarantor for a marriage contract does not become obligated** to pay.

The *gemara* now shifts to a technical question that the preceding narrative itself raises: how could Moshe bar Atzri have been liable as *arev* for the *ketubah* in the first place?

### Arev Not Obligated

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: עָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the issue under discussion. This is **as it was stated: Everyone agrees** that a standard **guarantor for a marriage contract does not become obligated** to pay the marriage contract.

The *gemara* states the operative principle: *v'kaimalan arev d'ketubah lo mishtabed* — we have an established ruling that an *arev* of a *ketubah* does not in fact obligate himself. The reasoning is that in a normal loan transaction, the lender parts with money in reliance on the guarantor's commitment, and the borrower actually receives funds. The *arev*'s obligation is therefore grounded in real economic reliance. In the case of a *ketubah*, however, no money changes hands at the moment of the guarantee. The *arev* is stepping forward to help the couple get married, and his commitment is presumed to be more ceremonial than genuinely intended — he lacks *g'mar da'at*, the full, settled intention required to create a binding obligation. This ruling cuts directly against our *mishnah*, which presupposes that an *arev* of a *ketubah* is liable — otherwise Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel would have no reason to require the vow against remarriage. The *gemara* does not attempt to reconcile this tension; it simply states the ruling.

This principle is troubling from a different direction as well. The *ketubah* was instituted by *Chazal* specifically to protect the wife's interests. She is not lending money, but she is committing herself — her life — to this man. The need to protect her interests is real and weighty. To say that the *arev* was not being serious undermines precisely the protection she believed she was receiving.

### Arev vs. Kablan

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלָן הֲוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **He was an unconditional guarantor.**

The *gemara* then resolves the question of how Moshe bar Atzri could have been liable at all: *kablan hava* — he was not merely an *arev* but a *kablan*, using the stronger language reviewed in the previous daf, whereby the guarantor speaks as though the debt comes directly to him. Rather than saying "I will guarantee your obligation," a *kablan* says, in effect, "I am taking this obligation upon myself as though I am receiving the money and will pay." This stronger formulation might be sufficient to create a binding obligation even in the case of a *ketubah*.

### Kablan Property Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנִיחָא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: קַבְּלָן דִּכְתוּבָּה – אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית לֵיהּ נִכְסֵי לְבַעַל, מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד – שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא לְמַאן דְּאָמַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, אִי לֵית לֵיהּ לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד – מַאי אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **This works out well according to the one who says** that **an unconditional guarantor for a marriage contract becomes obligated** to pay it **even if the husband has no property** of his own at the time of the marriage. According to this opinion all is **well. But according to the one who says: If** the husband **has** his own property the unconditional guarantor **becomes obligated** to pay, but **if** the husband **does not have** his own property **he does not become obligated, what is there to say?**

But the *gemara* immediately qualifies even this: not everyone agrees that a *kablan* of a *ketubah* is fully obligated in all circumstances. One opinion holds that a *kablan* of a *ketubah* is obligated — *mishtabed* — even when the husband has no assets. Another opinion, however, holds that even a *kablan* of a *ketubah* is obligated only when the husband does have assets (*ais lei mishtabed*), but when the husband has no assets (*leis lei lo mishtabed*), the *kablan* is not bound.

This second position is paradoxical in a way that is difficult to accept. The very moment when a guarantor is most needed is precisely when the primary debtor cannot pay. Yet the *gemara* here suggests that it is in exactly that circumstance — when the husband is impecunious — that the *kablan*'s commitment may not be binding. The logic again returns to *g'mar da'at*: if the husband has no assets and the wedding would not otherwise take place at all, the *kablan* is presumed to be pledging his commitment merely to facilitate the marriage rather than from genuine intention to accept the obligation. If the husband already has assets, the *kablan*'s stepping forward was unnecessary — and therefore reflects genuine, deliberate assumption of liability. The woman, in the very circumstance where she most needs the protection, may find the guarantee legally void.

### Property Destroyed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: מִיהְוֵי הֲוָה לֵיהּ, וְאִישְׁתְּדוּף. וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: אַבָּא לְגַבֵּי בְּרֵיהּ – שַׁעְבּוֹדֵי מְשַׁעְבַּד נַפְשֵׁיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that Rav Huna **had** property when he got married, so that his father's guarantee took effect, **but it became blighted,** i.e., it was ruined or lost in the interim. **And if you wish, say** instead: **A father, vis-à-vis his son,** sincerely **obligates himself** to guarantee his marriage contract, even when the son has no property of his own.

The *gemara* offers two resolutions for how Moshe bar Atzri's obligation as *kablan* could have been operative. The first: *mehavei hava lei v'ishtadaf* — perhaps Rav Huna did have property at the time of the marriage and only afterward his assets were destroyed or depreciated. In that case, the *kablan*'s commitment was made when the husband had assets, satisfying the condition of the stricter opinion. The second: *amalgabei brei shibudei m'shabid nafshie* — because the *kablan* was the father of the husband, we may presume that a father genuinely intends to obligate himself when guaranteeing his son's *ketubah*, beyond the pro forma commitment that we might ascribe to an unrelated *kablan*.

## Arev/Kablan Dispute

### Universal Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאִיתְּמַר: עָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד. קַבְּלָן דְּבַעַל חוֹב – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates on the issue under discussion. This is **as it was stated: Everyone agrees** that a standard **guarantor for a marriage contract does not become obligated** to pay the marriage contract, and **everyone agrees** that **an unconditional guarantor for a creditor,** i.e., for a loan, **becomes obligated** to repay the loan.

The *gemara* now presents the formal statement of the underlying debate that was referenced earlier. It begins with the points of consensus. Regarding *areiv diksuva* — one who serves as a standard *arev* for a *ketubah* — *divrei hakol lo mishtabeid*: all opinions agree, contrary to the apparent implication of our *mishnah*, that such a person does not obligate himself. The rationale is the one already discussed: no money changes hands, the *arev* is simply facilitating the marriage, and without *g'mar da'at* there is no binding obligation.

### Kablan Always Obligates

Conversely, regarding a *kablan* of a regular monetary debt — *kablan deba'al chov* — *divrei hakol mishtabeid*: all agree that such a person fully obligates himself. Here both factors reinforce one another: the language is the strongest possible form of guarantee, and real money is in fact changing hands in reliance on the guarantor's commitment.

### Mixed Cases Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַבְּלָן דִּכְתוּבָּה, וְעָרֵב דְּבַעַל חוֹב – פְּלִיגִי; מָר סָבַר: אִי אִית לֵיהּ נִכְסֵי לְלֹוֶה – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד, אִי לֵית לֵיהּ – לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד; וּמָר סָבַר: בֵּין אִית לֵיהּ וּבֵין לֵית לֵיהּ – מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **an unconditional guarantor for a marriage contract and** a standard **guarantor for a creditor,** the Sages **disagree.** One **Sage holds** that **if the debtor** or husband **has** his own **property,** these guarantors **become obligated** for the guarantees they have undertaken, but **if he does not have** his own property the guarantor **does not become obligated. And** one **Sage holds** that **whether** the debtor or husband **has** property **or does not have** property, the guarantor **becomes obligated** to pay it.

The dispute concerns the two intermediate cases: a *kablan* who uses the stronger language but in the context of a *ketubah*, where no money changes hands; and a standard *arev* using the weaker language but in the context of a genuine monetary loan, *baal chov*. In these mixed cases, *pligi* — the opinions diverge.

### Property Condition

One view holds that in these intermediate cases, the obligation depends on whether the primary debtor (*loveh*) has assets. If the *loveh* has assets, the guarantor is bound; if not, he is not. The logic is that when the primary debtor has resources, the guarantor's intervention was superfluous — no one required him to step forward, and his doing so therefore reflects genuine, settled intention. But when the *loveh* has no assets and the transaction could not proceed without the guarantor, the guarantor's pledge is seen as instrumental — designed to make the transaction happen — rather than as a sincere assumption of liability.

This position is genuinely difficult to accept. From the creditor's standpoint, the guarantor's pledge is precisely what induced him to extend the loan. To say that the guarantee is legally void in the very circumstance where it was most needed — and where the creditor's reliance on it was greatest — seems to make the entire institution of guaranty unreliable.

### No Property Condition

The opposing view holds that in all intermediate cases, regardless of whether the *loveh* has assets, the guarantor is bound — *bein ais lei bein leis lei mishtabeid*. An additional curiosity in the presentation of this debate is that neither position is attributed to a named *amora*. The formulation is simply *mar savar* and *u'mar savar* — "one holds" and "the other holds" — without any identification of who stands behind each view. This is quite unusual for a named *tannaic* or *amoraic* dispute.

### Final Arev Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהִלְכְתָא: עָרֵב – בֵּין אִית לֵיהּ וּבֵין לֵית לֵיהּ, מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד; בַּר מֵעָרֵב דִּכְתוּבָּה – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִית לֵיהּ לְבַעַל, לָא מִשְׁתַּעְבַּד. מַאי טַעְמָא? מִצְוָה הוּא דַּעֲבַד, וְלָאו מִידֵּי חַסְּרֵהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the *halakha*** is that **a guarantor becomes obligated** to pay, **whether** the debtor **has** his own property **or does not have** property. This is **to the exclusion of a guarantor for a marriage contract,** in **which** case **even if the husband has** his own property **he does not become obligated** to pay. **What is the reason** for this? The intention of the guarantor **is to perform** the **mitzva** of facilitating a marriage by encouraging the woman to consent to the marriage as a result of his involvement, but he does not truly intend to obligate himself. **And** furthermore, in contrast to a guarantor for a loan, where the creditor stands to suffer a loss if he is not repaid, the woman **does not** suffer any concrete **loss,** as the husband does not borrow money from the woman. Therefore, the level of commitment of the guarantor in this case is lower. Accordingly, the Sages instituted that the guarantor does not become responsible for payment of the marriage contract from his own property.

The *gemara* concludes: *vehilchata* — and the law is as follows. In all cases of *arev*, regardless of whether the language used is that of *arev* or *kablan*, and regardless of whether the *loveh* has assets or not, the guarantor is obligated. The single exception carved out is *bar me'arev dekesubah* — the case of one who uses the weaker *arev* language in the context of a *ketubah*. There, *af al gav de'is lei l'ba'al lo mishtabeid* — even if the husband has assets, the guarantor is not bound. The reason: *mitzvah hu de'aveid velav midi chasrei* — he is merely performing a good deed, facilitating the marriage; the woman parts with nothing, and his commitment is therefore not taken as a genuine legal obligation. It is possible that in practice such a signing had become an honorific gesture — something akin to a ritual of participation in the wedding — rather than a genuine assumption of legal liability. Whether or not the *gemara* goes that far, the ruling stands, however unsatisfying it may seem from the perspective of the woman whose interests the *ketubah* was meant to protect.

## Deathbed & Hekdesh

### Separate from Hekdesh

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁהִקְדִּישׁ כׇּל נְכָסָיו, וְאָמַר "מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיָדִי" – נֶאֱמָן; חֲזָקָה אֵין אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה קְנוּנְיָא עַל הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Huna says** that **a person on his deathbed who consecrated all his property** to the Temple treasury, **and** then **says: So-and-so has one hundred dinars in my possession,** i.e., I owe one hundred dinars to so-and-so, is **deemed credible,** and the money is to be repaid to that person. The reason for this is that there is **a presumption** that **a person does not collude against** the **Temple** treasury. He would not lie about owing money to someone, thereby causing a loss to the Temple treasury.

The *gemara* transitions to a new topic with a statement of Rav Huna: if a *shchiv mera* — a person on his deathbed — distributes all of his assets to *hekdesh* (consecrated charity), but then states that a particular debt of one hundred *zuz* remains outstanding to Reuven, we believe him. *Chazaka ein adam oseh kenunyah al hekdesh* — there is a presumption that a person will not fabricate a claim in order to cheat *hekdesh* out of assets that he has already consecrated. The heirs must therefore honor this admitted debt and pay Reuven.

### Not Cheating Heirs

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב נַחְמָן: וְכִי אָדָם עוֹשֶׂה קְנוּנְיָא עַל בָּנָיו?! דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר "מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיָדִי"; אָמַר "תְּנוּ" – נוֹתְנִין. לֹא אָמַר "תְּנוּ" – אֵין נוֹתְנִין. אַלְמָא אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת בָּנָיו;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman objects to this** ruling: **And does a person collude against his children?** Of course not. Yet, **as Rav and Shmuel both say** that in the case of **a person on his deathbed who says: So-and-so has one hundred dinars in my possession,** only if **he says** explicitly: **Give** him the money, the children **give** it, but if **he did not say** explicitly: **Give** him the money, the children do **not give** it. The person on his deathbed is not assumed to be telling the truth about owing the money unless he explicitly instructs that the money be given. **Apparently,** then, it is assumed that **a person is prone** to make false statements so as **not to make his sons** appear **sated,** i.e., wealthy.

Rav Nachman challenged this reasoning. The very same logic, he argued, should apply to a person's own children: we similarly presume that a person does not wish to cheat his own heirs. Yet Rav and Shmuel — *amri tuvai* — both say that a *shchiv mera* who admits to owing Reuven money is only believed if he additionally says *tenu* — "give it to him." If he merely says "I owe Reuven a hundred *zuz*" without the directive to give it, the debt is not paid out.

### Tenu Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת בָּנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** A person **acts in ways designed to prevent his children from appearing wealthy.**

The Rav and Shmuel ruling thus requires the affirmative directive *tenu* for the admission to be actionable. Why? Because without *tenu*, we apply the principle that *adam asui shelo lehasbi'a es banav* — a person acts in ways designed to prevent his children from appearing wealthy. A dying father may announce outstanding debts he does not actually owe so that creditors and others will not perceive his children as coming into a large inheritance and seek to exploit them. Unless he says *tenu* — a directive that implies a genuine, active intention to have the money transferred — we treat his statement as possibly motivated by this protective impulse rather than by truthfulness.

### Appearing Poor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא נָמֵי, אָדָם עָשׂוּי שֶׁלֹּא לְהַשְׂבִּיעַ אֶת עַצְמוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Here, too,** with regard to the Temple treasury, apply the same principle and say: **A person is prone** to make false statements so as **not to make himself** appear **sated.**

This logic is then applied back to the case of *hekdesh*: *hacha nami adam asui shelo lehasbia es atzmo* — perhaps a person similarly acts to avoid appearing rich in his own right. If he is giving everything to *hekdesh*, he might claim a large outstanding debt to Reuven so that people do not think of him as a man of great wealth. This rationale is admittedly difficult to fully understand. A person on his deathbed who has already consecrated all his property has little practical reason to manage his public image in the days or weeks that remain. Yet this is the logic the *gemara* invokes, and it accordingly raises the question of whether *tenu* is required in the *hekdesh* case as well.

### Shetar Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי קָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא הָתָם – דְּנָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **When** does **Rav Huna say** that payment should be made **there,** in the case of a person on his deathbed who consecrated all his property? In a case **where** the creditor, to whom the deceased admitted owing money, **holds** a promissory **note** corroborating the deceased's admission.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *ki amar Rav Huna hatam denakat shtar* — when Rav Huna said the *shchiv mera* is believed, he was speaking about a case in which Reuven already holds a *shtar*. Why would the *shchiv mera*'s admission then matter if Reuven already has a *shtar*? Tosafot explains: when collecting from orphans, even a holder of a *shtar* — even one that is *mekuyam* (whose signatures have been verified) — is normally required to take an oath (*shvuah*) that the debt has not been repaid, since the orphans cannot have personal knowledge of their father's business affairs. If the father on his deathbed acknowledged the debt, this relieves the creditor of the need to take that *shvuah*. The admission is thus not creating the debt anew but confirming its continued validity for purposes of relieving the creditor of the *shvuah* requirement.

### Mekuyam & Tenu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּלָל דְּרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל – דְּלָא נָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא; אָמַר "תְּנוּ" – נוֹתְנִין?! מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה הֲוָה, וְרַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: מִלְוֶה עַל פֶּה – אֵינוֹ גּוֹבֶה לֹא מִן הַיּוֹרְשִׁין וְלֹא מִן הַלָּקוֹחוֹת!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: If so, how can it be that if the deceased **says: Give** him the money, the children **give** it to him, i.e., it is collected from the estate left to the heirs, as **it is a loan by oral** contract, **and as Rav and Shmuel both say: One does not collect a loan by oral** contract **from heirs nor from purchasers** who bought land from the debtor after the loan was granted?

This leads the *gemara* to ask whether Rav and Shmuel, who required *tenu*, were also discussing a case where Reuven holds a *shtar*. The *gemara* confirms: yes, both discussions involve a *shtar*. The distinction lies in whether the *shtar* is *mekuyam* — i.e., whether the witnesses' signatures have been formally verified. When Rav Huna said the admission is sufficient (without *tenu*), he was speaking of a *shtar mekuyam*, where the only remaining obstacle to collection from orphans is the requirement of a *shvuah*. In that case, the *shchiv mera*'s statement — even without *tenu* — suffices to waive the *shvuah* requirement. When Rav and Shmuel said *tenu* is required, they were speaking of a *shtar* that is not *mekuyam*, where the creditor would ordinarily need to go through the process of establishing (*mekayem*) the validity of the document's signatures. In that case, even the deathbed admission without *tenu* is insufficient — the creditor must still have the signatures verified. Only with *tenu* does the *shchiv mera*'s statement substitute for the formal process of *kiyum ha-shtar*.

This is a significant narrowing of the original statements, which sounded in their plain presentation as broad claims about belief and presumption. What emerges is a much more precisely calibrated set of rules governing the interaction between deathbed admissions, the existence of a *shtar*, and whether that *shtar* has been formally verified.

It is worth noting that as a matter of practical *halacha*, the rule that an oral loan (*malveh al peh*) cannot be collected from orphans was subsequently modified at the time of the *Geonim*, who established that even such loans may be collected from *yorshim*. This development protects creditors from a total loss in cases where no written instrument was ever executed.

### Tenu as Kiyum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי דְּנָקֵיט שְׁטָרָא, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא דִּמְקוּיָּים, הָא דְּלָא מְקוּיָּים; אָמַר "תְּנוּ" – קַיְּימֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרֵיהּ, לֹא אָמַר "תְּנוּ" – לָא קַיְּימֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara gives a different answer to this question. **Rather, Rav Naḥman said:** Both **this** case **and that** case are discussing **where** the creditor **holds** a promissory **note** corroborating the deceased's admission. **And** yet it is **not difficult.** In **this** ruling of Rav Huna, the case is **where** the creditor's promissory note has been **ratified** by the court, and in **that** ruling of Rav and Shmuel, the case is **where** the creditor's promissory note has **not** been **ratified** by the court. Therefore, in the latter case, if the person on his deathbed **says: Give** him the money, he **has** in effect **ratified** the creditor's promissory **note,** and if he did **not say: Give** him the money, he has **not ratified** the creditor's promissory **note.**

Rav Pappa draws the principle together: when the *shchiv mera* says *tenu* — even where the *shtar* is not *mekuyam* — the *tenu* directive itself functions as a *kiyum* of the document. It is, in effect, a rabbinic substitute for the formal verification process. Without *tenu*, the unverified document remains without the necessary *kiyum* and cannot be used to compel the orphans to pay.

## Orphans & Payment

### Orphans Believed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר "מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיָדִי", וְאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִין "פָּרַעְנוּ" – נֶאֱמָנִין. "תְּנוּ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי", וְאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִין "פָּרַעְנוּ" – אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says** that in the case of **a person on his deathbed who says: So-and-so has one hundred dinars in my possession, and the orphans** who inherited his property subsequently **said: We repaid** the debt, **they are deemed credible.** But if the father said: **Give one hundred dinars to so-and-so,** as I owe it to him, **and** subsequently **the orphans said: We repaid** him the money, **they are not deemed credible.**

Rava now introduces a related scenario. A *shchiv mera* states that he owes Reuven money, and afterward the orphans claim that the debt was paid. The *rishonim* understand this as involving a case where Reuven actually demanded payment and the *shchiv mera* acknowledged the debt, as distinct from a purely volunteered admission. If the father merely said he owed the money — without issuing a *tenu* directive — the orphans are believed when they say the debt was subsequently paid (*ne'emanin*).

### Tenu Bars Orphans

But if the father said *tenu* — give the money to Reuven — and afterward the orphans say they paid, they are not believed.

### Tenu More Credible

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּלַפֵּי לְיָיא? אִיפְּכָא מִסְתַּבְּרָא!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Isn't it the opposite [*kelappei layya*]? The opposite is** more **reasonable.**

The *gemara* immediately challenges this: *klapi laya* — the opposite seems to make more logical sense. If the father said *tenu*, that is a stronger and more explicit directive, and one can more readily assume the orphans acted on it and paid. *Ipcha mistabra* — the reverse is what logic suggests: when he said *tenu*, *keivan d'paska avuhon l'milsa*, since the father definitively commanded the matter, it is more plausible that they carried it out. When he merely said he owed the money without a directive, since he did not press the matter, there is less reason to assume they paid. The *gemara* thus reverses the initial presentation.

### Father Remembered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר, הָכִי אִיתְּמַר: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁאָמַר "מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי בְּיָדִי", וְאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִין: "חָזַר וְאָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא: פָּרַעְתִּי" – נֶאֱמָנִין. מַאי טַעְמָא? אִדְּכוֹרֵי מִידְּכַר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara reformulates Rabba's statement: **Rather, if** Rabba's statement **was stated, it was stated as follows:** In a case of **a person on his deathbed who says: So-and-so has one hundred dinars in my possession, and the orphans** subsequently **said:** After his admission, **Father retracted** his words **and said to us: I repaid** the debt, **they are deemed credible. What is the reason** for this? At first the father had forgotten, but subsequently **he remembered** repaying the debt.

The *gemara* then presents the corrected version of the scenario: *shchiv mera she'amar maneh l'ploni biyadi v'amru yetomim chazar v'amar aba parati* — the *shchiv mera* said he owes Reuven money, and the orphans say not that they themselves paid, but that their father subsequently remembered or stated that he had already paid the debt before his declaration. In that case — where the father himself walked back his admission — *ne'emanin*, the orphans are believed.

### Father Said Remembered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "תְּנוּ מָנֶה לִפְלוֹנִי", וְאָמְרוּ יְתוֹמִין: "חָזַר וְאָמַר אַבָּא: פָּרַעְתִּי" – אֵין נֶאֱמָנִין. דְּאִם אִיתָא דְּפַרְעֵיהּ, לָא הֲוָה אָמַר "תְּנוּ".
>
> **Translation:** But if the father says assertively: **Give one hundred dinars to so-and-so, and the orphans** subsequently **said:** Afterward **Father retracted** his words **and said: I repaid** the debt, **they are not deemed credible.** The reason is **that if it is so that** the father **repaid** the debt or was uncertain about the matter, **he would not say** with such assertiveness: **Give** the money to him.

The reason is *itkurei midkar* — he remembered. When a person merely says he owes money without issuing *tenu*, the statement is tentative enough that a subsequent recollection or correction by the father himself is credible. But if the father had said *tenu* — an unambiguous directive — and then the orphans report that the father said he remembered he had already paid, they are not believed: *d'im ita d'para lo hava amar tnu* — had he truly already paid, he would never have issued the affirmative command to give the money. The absolute certainty implicit in the *tenu* directive forecloses the possibility that the father was mistaken and later corrected himself.

The structure of the two scenarios thus runs in opposite directions depending on what the orphans are claiming. When orphans claim they themselves paid the debt: *tenu* makes them *more* believed, because the father's clear directive makes it plausible they acted on it. When orphans claim the father remembered he had already paid: *tenu* makes them *less* believed, because the father's unequivocal directive is inconsistent with any prior payment.

## Deathbed Admission

### Witnesses Needed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: שְׁכִיב מְרַע שֶׁהוֹדָה, מַהוּ? צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר "אַתֶּם עֵדַי", אוֹ אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר "אַתֶּם עֵדַי"? צָרִיךְ שֶׁיֹּאמַר "כְּתוֹבוּ" אוֹ אֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר "כְּתוֹבוּ"? אָדָם מְשַׁטֶּה בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה, אוֹ אֵין אָדָם מְשַׁטֶּה בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava raises a dilemma:** With regard to **a person on his deathbed who,** upon hearing someone's monetary claim against him, **admitted** owing the money to him, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it **required** for him **to say** to two people: **You are my witnesses,** as is usually necessary in order to prove that an admission is stated in earnest and not in jest? **Or** is it perhaps **not required** for him **to say: You are my witnesses,** since he is a person on his deathbed, whose instructions are generally binding? Is it **required that he say** to two people: **Write** my admission in a document, as is usually required, **or** is it **not required** for him **to say: Write** my admission in a document? The issue in question here is: Does **a person** sometimes **jest at the time of death,** i.e., while on his deathbed, **or** can it be assumed that **a person** does **not jest at the time of death?**

Rava raises several questions about the *shchiv mera*'s admission more broadly. The first: does a *shchiv mera* who admits a debt need to formally designate witnesses — saying *atem edai*, "you are my witnesses" — for his admission to carry legal force? Normally, an extrajudicial admission requires such a designation, since without it, we might treat the statement as casual or dismissive rather than as a formal acknowledgment.

### Automatic Shetar

A related question: if witnesses observe a *shchiv mera*'s admission without being explicitly commanded to record it, may they write it up as a *shtar* on their own initiative? Normally a person must explicitly instruct witnesses to reduce his words to writing; may that requirement be dispensed with in the case of a *shchiv mera*?

### No Joking at Death

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּתַר דְּבַעְיָא, הֲדַר פַּשְׁטַהּ – אֵין אָדָם מְשַׁטֶּה בִּשְׁעַת מִיתָה, וְדִבְרֵי שְׁכִיב מְרַע כִּכְתוּבִין וְכִמְסוּרִין דָּמוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **After** Rava **raised the dilemma, he then resolved it,** and ruled: **A person** does **not jest at the time of death, and** therefore **the statements of a person on his deathbed are considered as** if they were **written** in a document **and transmitted** to the relevant party.

The *gemara* answers both questions together: *batar d'ba'ei hadar pashatei* — after posing these questions, Rava himself resolved them. *Ein adam mashatei b'sha'at mita* — a person does not jest or speak carelessly at the moment of death. There is no concern that a *shchiv mera*'s admission is casual or dismissive; the gravity of the deathbed context endows every statement with seriousness.

### Force of Document

Correspondingly: *divrei shchiv mera k-ktuvin u-k-mesurin dami* — the words of a *shchiv mera* have the legal force of a written document and of a performed *kinyan*. Just as we say that a *shchiv mera*'s dispositions of property take effect by virtue of his words alone without requiring formal *kinyan* procedures, so too his admissions carry the weight of a formally executed *shtar*. He need not say *atem edai*, and witnesses may record his words as a *shtar* without any explicit command to do so.

## Loan Types & Collection

### Shetar Encumbered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמַּלְוֶה אֶת חֲבֵירוֹ בִּשְׁטָר – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים מְשׁוּעְבָּדִים. עַל יְדֵי עֵדִים – גּוֹבִין מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who lends** money **to another by means of** a promissory **note can collect** the debt **from liened property** that had been sold to others by the debtor after the loan was granted. One who lends money **by means of witnesses,** without recording the loan in a promissory note, **can collect** the debt only **from unsold property.**

The *mishnah* at the opening of 175b sets out a taxonomy of loan types and the corresponding scope of collection rights. A loan made with a *shtar* — *ha-malveh chavero b-shtar* — creates a lien on the borrower's properties and entitles the creditor to collect from *nechasim meshubadim*, encumbered property, including property the borrower has sold to third parties after the loan was made. The existence of the *shtar* provides the requisite public notice (*shibudim*) and, notably, even though the *shtar* may not explicitly state that there is a lien on the borrower's other properties, the principle of *achrayut ta'ut sofer* — that the omission of a guarantee clause is treated as a scribal error — means that such liens attach automatically.

### Witnesses Unencumbered

A loan made before witnesses but without a *shtar* does not create liens. The creditor in such a case may only collect from *nechasim bnei chorin* — unencumbered property currently in the possession of the borrower — and cannot reach assets the borrower has sold or transferred.

### IOU Unencumbered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוֹצִיא עָלָיו כְּתַב יָדוֹ שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב לוֹ – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** If one **presents to** a debtor a document in the **handwriting of** the debtor stating **that he owes** money **to him,** but without witnesses signed on the document, the creditor **can collect** only **from unsold property.**

If the debtor himself writes out an acknowledgment of debt — *ktav yado*, a handwritten IOU — that document may be used to compel payment, but because it was not executed before witnesses with the public formality of a proper *shtar*, it too creates no lien. Collection is limited to unencumbered property.

### Late Arev Unencumbered

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָרֵב הַיּוֹצֵא לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – גּוֹבֶה מִנְּכָסִים בְּנֵי חוֹרִין.
>
> **Translation:** In the case of **a guarantor whose** commitment **emerged after the signing of** the promissory **note,** the creditor **can collect** the sum only **from unsold property** of the guarantor.

Finally, if the *arev* affixed his guarantee to the *shtar* after the witnesses had already signed — *arev hayotzei le'achar chitum shtaros* — the creditor may collect from the *arev*, but only from the *arev*'s unencumbered property. Because the guarantee was added after the formal execution of the document, it does not benefit from the lien-creating effect of the witnesses' signatures, and is treated as equivalent to an oral or informal commitment.

## Arev Contingency

### Choking Parable

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הַחוֹנֵק אֶת אֶחָד בַּשּׁוּק, וּמְצָאוֹ חֲבֵירוֹ וְאָמַר לוֹ "הַנַּח לוֹ [וַאֲנִי אֶתֵּן לָךְ]" – פָּטוּר, שֶׁלֹּא עַל אֱמוּנָתוֹ הִלְוָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** If **one was strangling someone in the marketplace,** demanding repayment of a loan, **and another** person **found him** doing so and **said to** the attacker: **Leave him** alone **and I will give you** the money he owes, the person who intervened is **exempt** from paying, **as** the creditor **did not loan** the money in the first place **based on his trust** of the one who intervened.

Ben Nanas illustrates the fundamental principle governing the *arev*'s obligation with a vivid parable: imagine someone who is choking another person in the marketplace, and a bystander says, "Release him and I will give you a hundred *zuz*." Such a promise, says Ben Nanas, is unenforceable. The bystander did not genuinely intend to transfer money; he was acting under duress, trying to save the victim, and no *g'mar da'at* — no settled intention — underlies that promise.

### Guarantee Causation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵיזֶה הוּא עָרֵב שֶׁהוּא חַיָּיב? "הַלְוֵהוּ, וַאֲנִי נוֹתֵן לָךְ" – חַיָּיב, שֶׁכֵּן עַל אֱמוּנָתוֹ הִלְוָהוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, who is a guarantor who is obligated** to repay the loan he has guaranteed? One who tells the creditor before the loan takes place: **Lend** money to **him, and I will give you** the repayment, **as** in that case the creditor **did loan** the money **based on his trust** of the guarantor.

The parable maps onto the *arev* situation as follows: if the *arev* steps in and commits to the guarantee only after the loan has already been made and the money has already changed hands, then the lender did not extend credit in reliance on the *arev*'s commitment. Perhaps the *arev* is intervening to relieve pressure on the borrower — trying to stop the lender from pursuing him aggressively — but the loan itself was not made because of the guarantee. *Shelo al m'nas ken hilvahu* — he did not lend in reliance on this — and therefore *emunaso hilvahu*, the lender's willingness to extend credit was not based on the *arev*'s commitment. In consequence, the *arev* who signed after the fact cannot be compelled to pay.

### When vs. Before

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: הָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּחְכִּים – יַעֲסוֹק בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת; שֶׁאֵין לָךְ מִקְצוֹעַ בַּתּוֹרָה יוֹתֵר מֵהֶן, וְהֵן כְּמַעְיָין הַנּוֹבֵעַ. וְהָרוֹצֶה שֶׁיַּעֲסוֹק בְּדִינֵי מָמוֹנוֹת – יְשַׁמֵּשׁ אֶת שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן נַנָּס.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yishmael** thereupon **said: One who wants to become wise should engage in** the study of **monetary law, as there is no greater discipline in the Torah, and it is like a flowing spring. And,** he added, **one who wants to engage in** the study of **monetary law should attend to,** i.e., become a disciple of, **Shimon ben Nannas.**

The *mishnah* thus delineates the two cases sharply. *Halvahu va'ani noten lach* — "lend him the money and I will pay you": when the guarantee precedes the loan and the money is extended specifically because the *arev* has committed himself, the *arev* is *chayav* — fully obligated — because *al emunaso hilvahu*, the creditor acted in reliance on the *arev*'s word. But when the guarantee comes only after the money has already been lent, no such causal nexus exists, and the *arev* bears no obligation at all, collecting neither from encumbered nor from unencumbered property.

## Praise for Dinei

### Wisdom & Civil Law

The *mishnah* closes the tractate with a celebrated statement of Rabi Yishmael: *harotzeh sheyachkim ya'asok b'dinei mamanos* — one who wishes to acquire wisdom should devote himself to the study of civil law, *dinei mamanos*.

### Greatest Intellectual

The reason: *she'ein lach miktzoa baTorah yoser mehen* — there is no field in the entire Torah that demands greater intellectual capacity or that sharpens the mind more than *dinei mamanos*. The intricate interplay of principles, the layered distinctions between *arev* and *kablan*, the refined analysis of *g'mar da'at*, the fine calibrations of when a *shchiv mera*'s words do or do not carry legal force — all of this has been on full display in the pages of the tractate.

### Flowing Well

*V'hen k'mayan hanove'a* — they are like a flowing, springing well. There is no end to the new ideas, new applications, and new distinctions that emerge from sustained engagement with civil law. The well does not run dry.

### Shimon ben Nanas

And if one wishes to seriously take up the study of *dinei mamanos*, Rabi Yishmael recommends: *yeshamesh es Shimon Ben Nanas* — attend upon Shimon ben Nanas, who is exemplary not merely for his book knowledge but for his practical wisdom in applying the law — precisely the same Shimon ben Nanas whose parable about the man choking his fellow illuminated the foundational principle of the *arev*'s obligation. He is, in Rabi Yishmael's assessment, the master from whom one should seek to learn.
