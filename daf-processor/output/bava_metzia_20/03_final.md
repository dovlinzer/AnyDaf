# Bava Metzia 20 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Receipt/Ketubah Scenario

### Shovar for Ketubah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מָצָא שׁוֹבָר, בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה מוֹדֶה – יַחְזִיר לַבַּעַל. אֵין הָאִשָּׁה מוֹדֶה – לֹא יַחְזִיר לֹא לָזֶה וְלֹא לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: If one **found a receipt** for payment of a marriage contract, in a case **when the wife admits** that it was paid, **he should return** it **to the husband.** If **the wife does not admit** that it was paid, **he should neither return** it **to this** person, the husband, **nor to that** person, the wife.

The daf begins at the bottom of 19b, concluding the discussion of the *mishnah* that enumerated categories of documents one does not return upon finding them — *gittei nashim*, *shichrurei avadim*, and related documents — on the grounds that they may never have been delivered. The final item on that list is a *shovar*, a receipt, and the *baraita* here addresses specifically a receipt for a *kesuva*.

The *baraita* states: *matza shovar* — if one finds a receipt acknowledging that a husband paid his wife's *kesuva* — *bizman shehi moda*, if the wife herself confirms that yes, her husband paid and she issued the receipt, then *yachazir l'ba'al*, one returns the receipt to the husband. *Ein hi moda*, if she does not agree, then *lo yachazir lo lizeh v'lo lizeh* — one returns it to neither party, since she claims she wrote it anticipating payment that never came, while he insists he paid. The operative and interesting clause, then, is the first: when she does acknowledge payment, we return the receipt. The question that immediately arises is why this case differs from an ordinary debt document, where even mutual acknowledgment does not suffice to return the document — given our standard concern that some side arrangement may be at work.

### Nisan-Tishrei Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּזְמַן שֶׁהָאִשָּׁה מוֹדֶה מִיהַת יַחְזִיר לַבַּעַל, וְלֵיחוּשׁ דִּלְמָא כָּתְבָה לִיתֵּן בְּנִיסָן, וְלֹא נָתְנָה עַד תִּשְׁרֵי, וַאֲזַלָה זְבֵנְתַּהּ לִכְתוּבְּ[תַ]הּ בְּטוֹבַת הֲנָאָה מִנִּיסָן עַד תִּשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **In any event,** the *baraita* states that **when the wife admits** that it was paid, one **should return** the receipt **to the husband.** The Gemara asks: **But let us suspect** that **perhaps** the wife **wrote** the receipt intending **to give** it to the husband **in Nisan, but** ultimately **she did not give** it to him **until Tishrei, and she went** and **sold her marriage contract for financial advantage** in the interim, **between Nisan and Tishrei.**

The concern here is classic and familiar: *d'leichush dilma kesavo l'eisein b'Nisan v'lo nasna ad Tishrei* — perhaps she wrote the receipt in Nisan but was not actually paid until Tishrei. One must also assume that she had not even been divorced yet in Nisan; the divorce was anticipated but had not yet occurred. She wrote the receipt in Nisan in preparation, the divorce and payment only materialized months later in Tishrei, and now the receipt bears a date in Nisan. At first glance this seems harmless — the receipt still records that the husband paid. But the question is: who gets hurt by this?

### Cheating Third-Party Buye…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַפֵּיק לֵיהּ לְשׁוֹבָר דִּכְתִיב בְּנִיסָן, וְאָתֵא לְמִטְרַף לָקוֹחוֹת שֶׁלֹּא כַּדִּין!
>
> **Translation:** **And** then after the couple is divorced, and the purchaser collects payment of the marriage contract from the husband, the husband will **produce the receipt that was written in Nisan and** will **come to repossess** property **from** the **purchasers unlawfully.**

The injured party is a third-party purchaser — call him Michael. *V'azla v'zavanta l'kesuvasa b'tovas hana'ah miNisan ad Tishrei* — the wife, anticipating divorce, sold her *kesuva* to Michael in Nisan at a discount. The *kesuva* is worth ten thousand dollars at collection; she sells it to him for five thousand now. Michael assumes the risk — maybe she will never be divorced, or she will predecease her husband — and in exchange receives the prospect of collecting the full face value when the time comes. The wife then does get divorced, the husband pays her in Tishrei, and she hands him a receipt dated Nisan — before she ever sold the *kesuva* to Michael. When Michael comes to collect, the husband produces the receipt and says: I divorced her and paid her before you ever bought this *kesuva*. Your purchase was of a debt that had already been discharged. Michael is left with nothing.

As *Rashi* explains, if a specific plot of land had been designated as the *kesuva* property, the husband now uses the Nisan-dated receipt to keep that land, defeating Michael's attempt to collect. The concern is thus a predated *shovar* that illegally cheats the third-party purchaser out of his collection rights.

### Rava/Shmuel Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: אִיתַאּ לְדִשְׁמוּאֵל, דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הַמּוֹכֵר שְׁטַר חוֹב לַחֲבֵירוֹ, וְחָזַר וּמְחָלוֹ – מָחוּל, וַאֲפִילּוּ יוֹרֵשׁ מוֹחֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** **Conclude from** the fact that this suspicion is not taken into account that the *halakha* **of Shmuel is accepted. As Shmuel says:** In the case of **one who sells a promissory note to another, and** the seller **then forgives** the debt of the debtor, **it is forgiven,** since the debtor essentially had a non-transferable obligation to the creditor alone, **and even** the creditor's **heir can forgive** the debt.

*Amar Rava* — Rava says: *shma mina isa l'diShmuel* — from the fact that the *mishnah* does not voice this concern and simply says to return the receipt when the wife agrees, one can infer that we rule in accordance with Shmuel.

### Shmuel's Principle

What is Shmuel's principle? *D'amar Shmuel hamocher shtar chov l'chaveiro v'chazar u'machalo machul v'afilu yoresh mocheil* — if Reuven sells a debt document to a third party and then forgives the underlying debt owed by Shimon, the forgiveness is effective. And not only that: even Reuven's heir, standing in Reuven's place, can forgive the debt.

### Debt Purchase Logic

The mechanism here requires unpacking. When Reuven sells me a *shtar chov* for five hundred dollars — a debt Shimon owes Reuven of one thousand — the money is still technically owed to Reuven. The purchaser functions, in a sense, as Reuven's designated collection agent or the recipient of Reuven's direct deposit. Shimon's obligation runs to Reuven, not to the purchaser directly. It follows that Reuven retains the legal capacity to be *mocheil* — to forgive — the debt to Shimon. Of course, if Reuven does forgive, he is liable under the laws of *dinei d'garmi* for the loss he caused the purchaser, who can recover the five hundred dollars paid. But the forgiveness itself is legally effective.

This creates a remarkable and admittedly uncomfortable reality for debt purchasers. Once Reuven has sold the *shtar* for five hundred, if Shimon subsequently comes into money, Reuven can approach Shimon and say: instead of paying a thousand to the purchaser, pay me eight hundred, I'll forgive the debt, and I'll reimburse the purchaser his five hundred. The purchaser gets reimbursed but loses the profit he was counting on. This is legally legitimate under Shmuel's principle, though deeply disadvantageous to the purchaser. There are ways to circumvent this — *ma'amad shloshtan*, bringing all three parties together simultaneously, can sever the original creditor's connection to the debt in a manner the halacha recognizes as a direct transfer — but absent such a mechanism, the original creditor retains the power of forgiveness.

How does this address our case? The concern was that the husband could fraudulently cheat Michael by producing a predated receipt. Rava's point is: this is no more harmful to Michael than what the wife could do *legally*, without any fraud at all. She could right now be *mocheil* the *kesuva* debt to her husband — perhaps in exchange for a quiet payment — and under Shmuel's principle that would be effective. Michael would be cheated out of his collection rights entirely through legal means. Since Michael can be legally deprived of the debt, we are not additionally concerned about the illegal mechanism of a predated *shovar*. The category of loss he might suffer is already fully available to the husband and wife through legitimate channels. Hence the *mishnah* does not voice this concern, and from this Rava derives that we hold like Shmuel.

One might ask: does this mean that nobody would ever purchase a *kesuva* or any debt? The answer is that it is a risk the purchaser takes knowingly. There are loss scenarios built into the transaction from the start. The question of whether a purchaser could contractually obligate the original creditor not to forgive the debt is an interesting one — likely a penalty clause could be inserted, making the creditor financially liable for a severe sum if he ever forgives — but the ability to forgive the debt as a legal matter may not itself be contractually waivable. Only through *ma'amad shloshtan* can the creditor be fully cut out.

### Abaye's Three Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא לֵיתַיהּ לְדִשְׁמוּאֵל, הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשֶׁשְּׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יָדָהּ. וְרָבָא אָמַר: אִי מִשּׁוּם שְׁטַר כְּתוּבָּה, חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** This is not conclusive proof. **Even** if **you say** that the *halakha* **of Shmuel is not** accepted, **here we are dealing with** a case **where** the **marriage contract emerges from her possession,** which indicates that she did not sell it. **And Rava** disagreed with Abaye and **said: If** the reason there is no suspicion that she sold her marriage contract is **due to** the fact that the **marriage contract** emerged from her possession, this reason is insufficient, as **we** should **suspect** that there might be **two marriage contracts.**

*Abaye amar* — Abaye disagrees with the framing and says: *afilu teima leiso l'diShmuel* — even if you do not accept Shmuel's principle, there is no concern about cheating Michael here. Why?

Abaye offers a tripartite answer. First: *hacha b'mai askinan b'shtar kesuva yotza mitachas yadaw* — the *mishnah* is speaking of a case where the wife is currently holding the *kesuva* document itself. If the *kesuva* had been sold to Michael, the physical *kesuva* document would have been transferred to Michael at the time of sale. If the wife is the one currently holding the *kesuva*, it is clear she never sold it. There is therefore no Michael to worry about.

Rava challenges this: *im mishum shtar kesuva chashinan l'shtei kesuvos* — the fact that she holds a *kesuva* does not eliminate the concern, since perhaps she has two *kesuvos*. How would that happen? A first *kesuva* was lost and a replacement was written; she sold the replacement and still holds the original. Unlikely, yes, but Rava says we entertain the possibility.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּיֵי אָמַר, חֲדָא: לִשְׁתֵּי כְתוּבּוֹת לָא חָיְישִׁינַן. וְעוֹד, שׁוֹבָר בִּזְמַנּוֹ טָרֵיף. אַבָּיֵי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר: עֵדָיו בַּחֲתוּמָיו זָכִין לוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And Abaye said** in response: **First, we are not concerned about** the remote possibility of **two marriage contracts. And furthermore,** even if there is only one marriage contract, which was sold before the receipt was given to the husband, the receipt is valid, as **a receipt** can be used to **repossess** property **from** the **time** it was written, even if it was given on a later date. In saying this, **Abaye** conforms **to his** standard line of **reasoning, as he says** that once a monetary document is written for someone, **the document's witnesses, with their signatures, acquire** it **on his behalf.**

Abaye responds with his second reason: *l'shtei kesuvos lo chashinan* — we are not concerned about such a remote scenario of two *kesuvos* existing simultaneously.

And third: *v'od shover bizmano taref* — even setting aside both of the above, there is a fundamental reason not to fear the predated receipt cheating Michael. This is the third and most conceptually powerful answer, and it rests on Abaye's own well-known principle.

### Edov b'Chosmov Zachin Lo

*Abaye l'taimei d'amar edov b'chosmov zachin lo* — the witnesses by their signatures acquire on behalf of the beneficiary. Abaye's principle, which we have been applying implicitly throughout this *perek*, holds that when a document is signed by witnesses and that document is to the benefit of its recipient, the signature activates the document retroactively from the date of signing — on the condition that the document is ultimately delivered. This is distinct from *shtarei kinyan*, which take effect immediately upon signing without requiring delivery. Here, delivery is still required, but once delivered, the effect is retroactive to the date of the signatures.

Applied to our case: the wife wrote a receipt in Nisan; the husband actually paid only in Tishrei. Nevertheless, because of *edov b'chosmov zachin lo*, when the husband pays in Tishrei and receives the receipt, the debt is retroactively considered discharged from Nisan — the date the receipt was signed. This means that when Michael purchased the *kesuva* in, say, Iyar, the debt had already — retroactively — been extinguished as of Nisan. Michael purchased something that no longer existed. He is cheated, yes, but legally. He will be entitled to recover his purchase price, but no illegal fraud is being perpetrated. Hence the *mishnah* has no concern about returning the receipt. Abaye thus provides three independent grounds for not worrying: Shmuel's principle, the absence of a second *kesuva*, and retroactive validity through *edov b'chosmov zachin lo*.

## Document Retroactivity

### Witnesses & Delivery

The rule of *edov b'chosmov zachin lo* has been invoked repeatedly throughout this *perek*, but only now does the *gemara* pause to explain its underlying rationale. The practical problem that this principle solves is the following: how can witnesses ever sign a document unless all parties are assembled in the room at the very moment of intended delivery? In the vast majority of cases, a document is signed at one time and delivered at another. If we were to hold that the document takes effect only from the date of delivery, the date written on the document — the date of signing — would systematically misrepresent reality. Worse, all the concerns associated with the gap between signing and delivery — the *Nisan-Tishrei* problem, the possibility of sales to third parties in the interim — would recur perpetually. Abaye resolves all of this: when the document is finally delivered, it is deemed to have been active from the date of signing. The signed document is treated as if the witnesses already acquired on behalf of the recipient from that moment.

### Tosafot on Takanas Chach…

*Tosafot*, commenting at 111a, 117a, and 20b, finally addresses the foundation of Abaye's principle explicitly. *V'nireh shetakanas chachamim hi* — it is a rabbinic enactment. *Shezochin mishas chasima af al pi shelo nimsa lo ad yamim rabim* — the recipients acquire from the moment of signing even if the document was not delivered for many days. The reason for this *takana* is precisely to enable document signing to function practically. *L'olam lo yuchlu edim lachsom ela im yiru hamasira* — witnesses would never be able to sign any document unless they witnessed actual delivery, because the date of signing would otherwise always diverge from the date of effect. *Mishum chashasha d'kozva Nisan v'lo nasan ad Tishrei v'mazbin beinei beinei* — the pervasive fear of the Nisan-Tishrei problem, of sales intervening between signing and delivery, would make the entire institution of legal documents unworkable. Abaye's rule removes all of these difficulties by making the retroactivity operative from the date of signing, once delivery occurs.

### Status Issues & Retroact…

If the principle is rabbinic in origin — a *takanas chachamim* designed to protect monetary interests — a serious question arises regarding its application to personal status matters. We do apply *edov b'chosmov zachin lo* to *shichrurei avadim*, documents of manumission. If a *shtar shichrur* was signed in Nisan and delivered in Tishrei, the slave is retroactively freed from Nisan. But how can a rabbinic ordinance, enacted to protect *lakuchos* — purchasers of property — retroactively alter the personal status of a human being?

*Tosafot* raises the concrete case: *im eved ba al bas Yisroel kodam sheba l'yado get shichrur shepasla* — if a slave had relations with a Jewish woman between the signing and the delivery of his *get shichrur*, did he do so as a slave or as a freed man? If the retroactivity applies to status, she would be *pasul l'kehuna* retroactively from Nisan; if it applies only to monetary matters, his status in the interim remains that of a slave.

*Tosafot* presents two positions. One view: since the entire principle is rabbinic, *ul'inyan issur* — for matters of personal status — the retroactivity does not apply. For money, the document takes effect from signing; for *issur*, only from delivery. The other view: *shema gam gabai issur zochin lo mishas chasima* — perhaps even for status matters the rule applies retroactively from the date of signing, *af al pi d'la shayach ta'ama d'prishas* — even though the original rationale of protecting purchasers does not apply to status issues — because *mikol makom lo plug rabbanan*, the rabbis did not differentiate. Once they enacted the rule, they enacted it uniformly across both domains. The authority for this even in the domain of *issur* rests on the principle of *v'yesh koach b'yad chachamim la'akor davar min haTorah* — the rabbis have the capacity to uproot a Torah law under certain circumstances. By this reasoning, even though the slave is not technically a free man until delivery, the rabbinic retroactivity makes him one from the date of signing. *Tosafot* also raises the question of whether this principle could ever apply to a *get ishah* — a bill of divorce — under circumstances where a *get* might be considered a *z'chus*, a benefit, for the wife. Until now *edov b'chosmov zachin lo* has not been applied to a *get* because a *get* is understood as a *chov* — a burden — on the wife. *V'ein chavin l'adam shelo befanav* — one cannot burden another in their absence. But should a case arise where a *get* functions as a benefit, the question of retroactive validity would re-emerge.

## Court Documents/Maaseh B…

### Maaseh Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָצָא אִיגְּרוֹת שׁוּם וְאִיגְּרוֹת מָזוֹן, שְׁטָרֵי חֲלִיצָה וּמֵיאוּנִין, וּשְׁטָרֵי בֵירוּרִין, וְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין – הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If one **found documents of appraisal** of a debtor's property for the purpose of debt collection; **or documents concerning food,** which were drawn up when one accepted upon himself to provide sustenance for another; **documents of *ḥalitza*; or** documents of **refusal** of a girl upon reaching majority to remain married to the man to whom her mother or brothers married her as a minor after the death of her father; **or documents of *beirurin*,** a concept that will be explained in the Gemara; **or any court enactment,** e.g., a promissory note that has been authenticated by the court, in all of these cases, the finder **must return** the document to its presumed owner.

The final *mishnah* of the *perek* turns to a new category: documents that one may always return without concern. These are *maasei beis din* — documents issued by a court. The *mishnah* opens: *matza igros shum* — one found assessment letters.

### Court Document Types

Each type listed in the *mishnah* shares two structural features that distinguish it from ordinary debt documents: first, it takes effect immediately upon issuance, so there is no concern about a Nisan-Tishrei gap between writing and activation; second, it does not represent a dischargeable debt in the conventional sense, so there is no concern that the document has been paid off and should no longer exist. The document simply records or authorizes something, and remains as evidence in the hands of the recipient.

*Igros shum* — assessment letters — are documents in which the court has evaluated the debtor's assets and determined that specified property, such as a particular plot of land, is to be levied to satisfy a debt. The document is active the moment it is written, and since it designates real property, it is self-evident from external reality whether the property has already been seized or not.

*O igeret mazon* — a support document. *Rashi* explains this as a husband's written commitment to support his stepdaughter, an ongoing obligation rather than a one-time payment. Since the obligation is continuous and not subject to being "paid off" in a single transaction, there is no concern that the document represents a discharged claim.

*Shtar chalitzah* — a document attesting that *chalitzah* was performed, freeing the *yevamah* from the obligation of *yibbum*. *U'miunim* — a document recording that an underage girl, married by her mother or brothers, exercised her right of *miun*, verbally refusing her husband in a valid manner, thereby dissolving the marriage. Both of these are purely evidentiary: they testify to acts that have already occurred. There is no question of the document having been written in anticipation of an act not yet performed; the act precedes the document.

*V'shtarei beirurin* — we are told to await the *gemara*'s explanation. *V'chol maasei Beis Din harei zeh yachzir* — any document issued by a court is to be returned to the appropriate party.

### Sheteri Beirurin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״שְׁטָרֵי בֵירוּרִין״? הָכָא תַּרְגִּמוּ שְׁטָרֵי טַעֲנָתָא. רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה אָמַר: זֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד וְזֶה בּוֹרֵר לוֹ אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **What** is meant by **documents of *beirurin*? Here,** in Babylonia, the Sages **interpret** it to mean **documents** recording each litigant's clarification [*beirur*] **of** his **claims** in a court case. **Rabbi Yirmeya,** who lived in Eretz Yisrael, **said:** It is referring to cases where **this** litigant **chooses [*borer*] one** judge, **and that** litigant **chooses one** judge, and they choose the third judge for the case. The two litigants sign a document in which they declare which judges they choose.

The *gemara* then asks: *mai shtarei birurin* — what are *shtarei beirurin*? In Babylonia the explanation given is *shtarei ta'anata* — documents recording the claims each party made in court. The court reporter, so to speak, drew up a record: Reuven claimed X, Y, and Z; Shimon counterclaimed A, B, and C. This record was then handed to the parties before judgment.

Rabbi Yirmeyah offers a different explanation: *zeh borer lo echad v'zeh borer lo echad* — this refers to the process by which parties constitute an ad hoc arbitration panel. I choose one *dayan*, you choose another, and those two select a third. A *shtar beirurin* is the court-issued document recording that the parties have agreed to submit their dispute to these three specified judges. The document is active immediately — there is nothing to "pay off" — and it binds the parties to the selected tribunal. It is a clear candidate for the category of *maaseh Beis Din* that one returns without concern.

### Get in Beis Din

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכׇל מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר. הָהוּא גִּיטָּא דְּאִשְׁתְּכַח בֵּי דִינָא דְּרַב הוּנָא, דַּהֲוָה כְּתִיב בֵּיהּ ״בִּשְׁוִירֵי מָתָא דְּעַל רָכִיס נַהֲרָא״. אֲמַר רַב הוּנָא: חָיְישִׁינַן לִשְׁנֵי שְׁוִירֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara addresses that which the mishna states: **And** with regard to **any court enactment,** the one who found it **must return** it to its presumed owner. The Gemara relates: There was **a certain bill of divorce that was found** in **the court of Rav Huna, in which it was written** that the bill of divorce was written **in Sheviri City, which is** located **on the Rakhis River. Rav Huna said** about this: **We are concerned for** the possibility that there are **two** cities named **Sheviri,**

The *gemara* then presents a case that sounds familiar: *hahu gita deishtakach be dinei deRav Huna* — a *get* was found in the *beis din* of Rav Huna, bearing the specification that it came from the city of Sheviri, situated on the Rachis river. *Rashi* explains the background: the city of Sheviri on the Rachis is a specific and identifiable location, and presumably there is no one else in that city matching the names written in the *get*. It would seem obvious to return the *get* to the intended party.

### Rav Huna's Concern

Yet *amar Rav Huna bar Rav Yehuda* — even granting all of this, we should be concerned: *chashinan lishnei Sheviri* — perhaps there is another city also named Sheviri, also on the Rachis river. This might seem far-fetched, but rivers pass through many regions, and cities in different areas can independently bear the same name and sit on the same river. As an analogy, along the Mississippi there are many towns sharing names across different states, and there are documented cases of cities in different countries — Stratford-upon-Avon in England and Stratford on the Avon in Ontario, Canada — presenting exactly this kind of coincidence.

### Rabbah's Investigation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב חִסְדָּא לְרַבָּה: פּוֹק עַיֵּן, דִּלְאוּרְתָּא בָּעֵי לַהּ רַב הוּנָא מִינָּךְ. נְפַק דַּק וְאַשְׁכַּח, דִּתְנַן: כׇּל מַעֲשֵׂה בֵּית דִּין הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda said to Rabba: Go out** and **examine** this *halakha*, **as in the evening Rav Huna will ask you** about it. **He went out, examined** it, **and discovered** a relevant source, **as we learned** in the mishna: With regard to **any court enactment,** the one who found it **must return** it to its presumed owner. Since this bill of divorce was found in court, it belongs to this category and should be returned.

*Amar lei Rav Chisda leRabba* — Rav Chisda instructed Rabbah, apparently Rav Huna's legal secretary, to go and investigate: *puk ayen d'le'ursa ba'ei lei Rav Huna minach* — by evening Rav Huna would be asking him whether there is any evidence bearing on whether we need to worry about a second Sheviri. *Nafak dak v'ashkach* — Rabbah went out, investigated, and found: *tnan kol maaseh Beis Din harei zeh yachzir* — the *mishnah* states that any *maaseh Beis Din* is returned. The *mishnah* voices no concern about the possibility of another party with the same name in another city with the same name. The implicit ruling of the *mishnah*, especially given that a *beis din* is a high-traffic location where documents from many parties pass through, is that we do not entertain such concerns. This appears to resolve the question in favor of returning the *get*.

### Rav Ami's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב עַמְרָם לְרַבָּה: הֵיכִי פָּשֵׁיט מָר אִיסּוּרָא מִמָּמוֹנָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: תְּרָדָא, ״שְׁטָרֵי חֲלִיצָה וּמֵיאוּנִין״ תְּנַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Amram said to Rabba: How can the Master resolve** the *halakha* in the case of a bill of divorce, which is **a ritual matter, from** the mishna, which discusses **monetary matters?** Rabba **said to him: Fool, we learned** in the mishna that this *halakha* applies in the case of **documents of *ḥalitza* and** documents of **refusal** as well, which are ritual matters.

*Amar lei Rav Ami leRabbah* — Rav Ami challenged Rabbah: *heichi pashit mar issura mi'mamona* — how can you derive a ruling about personal status from a monetary case? In monetary matters, a standard *siman* suffices to return a lost object; for a *get*, one might require a *siman muvhak*, a far more distinctive identifying mark. Here the *mishnah* is dealing with monetary court documents. How do you know the same confidence level extends to personal status, to permitting a woman to be considered divorced?

### Chalitzah/Miun Evidence

*Amar lei tirda* — Rabbah replied: you dullard. *Shtar chalitzah u'miun tnan* — the *mishnah* itself explicitly includes *shtar chalitzah* and *miun* among the court documents that one returns. These are personal status issues. *Chalitzah*, while less severe than a full *get ishah* — the children of subsequent unions are not *mamzerim* and the prohibition involved is lower-level — is nonetheless a status matter. The *mishnah* makes no distinction between monetary court documents and personal status court documents when it says *kol maaseh Beis Din harei zeh yachzir*. So the *mishnah* itself resolves Rav Ami's question. The exchange concludes with the famous detail that the beam of the study hall burst — *paka arza d'bei Rav* — and each of the two disputants attributed the supernatural event to his own vindication.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּקַע אַרְזָא דְּבֵי רַב. מָר אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם לַתַּאי דִּידִי פְּקַע. וּמָר אָמַר: מִשּׁוּם לַתַּאי דִּידִי פְּקַע.
>
> **Translation:** At that point, the supporting **cedar** beam **of the study hall dislodged.** One **Sage said:** It was **due to my fortune** that it **dislodged,** as you spoke to me offensively, **and** the other **Sage said:** It was **due to my fortune** that it **dislodged,** as it was you who spoke to me offensively.

## Multiple Documents/Ident…

### Containers/Siman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָצָא בַּחֲפִיסָה אוֹ בִדְלוֹסְקָמָא, תַּכְרִיךְ שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת אוֹ אֲגוּדָּה שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה יַחְזִיר. וְכַמָּה אֲגוּדָּה שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת – שְׁלֹשָׁה קְשׁוּרִין זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** If one **found** documents **in a *ḥafisa* or in a *deluskema*,** both of them types of containers, or if he found **a roll of documents or a bundle of documents, he must return** them. **And how many** documents are considered to be **a bundle of documents?** It is **three** that are **tied together.**

The *mishnah* states: *matza b'chafisah o b'diluskama* — if one finds documents in a *chafisah* or a *diluskama*, and the owner can provide a *siman* identifying the container, one returns the documents. Returning to standard *shtarei chov* rather than *maasei Beis Din*, the ordinary concerns about fraud or erroneous return apply, but are overcome when the finder can identify the container. The *gemara* asks: *mai chafisah* — what is a *chafisah*? *Amar Rabbah bar bar Chana*: *chemet ketanah* — a small leather flask, the kind used to carry water or liquids, here apparently repurposed for document storage. *Mai diluskama* — what is a *diluskama*? *Amar Rabbah bar bar Shmuel*: *telika d'savei* — an old person's sack or bag, a container used for storage.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי חֲפִיסָה? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה: חֵמֶת קְטַנָּה. מַאי דְּלוֹסְקָמָא? אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: טְלִיקָא דְסָבֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is a *ḥafisa*? Rabba bar bar Ḥana says:** It is **a small flask. What is a *deluskema*? Rabba bar Shmuel says:** It is **a container [*telika*]** used **by the elderly.**

What exactly constitutes the *siman* in these cases is not fully spelled out. *Tosafot* initially considers whether the unusual nature of the container — who keeps documents in a leather flask? — is itself the *siman*, but then notes the difficulty that the second case involves a more ordinary satchel, where the *siman* would presumably be the specific identifying features of that container.

### Wrapped/Bundle Documents

The *mishnah* continues: *o tachrich shel shtaros o agudah shel shtaros* — or a rolling of documents together, or a bundle of documents tied together. Here again one returns the documents to the person who provides the *siman*. The *siman* in these cases relates to how the documents are packaged — something unusual about the manner of their combination serves to identify them.

### Tachrich & Agudah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כַּמָּה הוּא תַּכְרִיךְ שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת? שְׁלֹשָׁה כְּרוּכִין זֶה בָּזֶה. וְכַמָּה הִיא אֲגוּדָּה שֶׁל שְׁטָרוֹת? שְׁלֹשָׁה קְשׁוּרִין זֶה בָּזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **How many** documents **constitute a roll of documents?** A roll is **three** documents **rolled together. And how many constitute a bundle of documents?** A bundle is **three** documents **tied together.**

The *baraita* specifies: *kama hu tachrich shtarot? Shlosha kruchin ze b'ze* — what constitutes a *tachrich*? Three documents wrapped one within the other. *V'kama hi agudah shel shtaros? Shlosha kshurin zeh bezeh* — what constitutes an *agudah*? Three tied together. The *gemara* then makes a careful distinction between the two methods. A *tachrich* is made by rolling one document and then rolling the second around the first, and the third around that — each one layered on top of the previous, *chada chada b'reisha d'chavarta*. An *agudah* is made by laying all the documents flat together in a pile and then rolling or tying them as a unit — *d'ramu ahadadi u'kruchinhu*. The result looks similar but structurally differs: in a *tachrich* the documents are intertwined and inseparable, while in an *agudah* one could, in principle, push out the middle document separately. Either method, when applied to three or more documents, constitutes a meaningful *siman*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ תַּכְרִיךְ? תַּכְרִיךְ כֹּל חַד וְחַד בְּרֵאשֵׁהּ דְּחַבְרֵיהּ, אֲגוּדָּה דִּרְמוּ אַהֲדָדֵי (וּכְרוּכוֹת).
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** this case **is** identical to the case of **a roll** of documents, which is also mentioned in the mishna. What is the difference between a roll and a bundle? The Gemara answers: **A roll** is referring to a case where **each and every one** of the documents is located **at the top of another one,** i.e., they are rolled together such that the top of each page is near the bottom of the previous page. **A bundle,** by contrast, is referring to a case **where they are located one on top of the other and rolled** together.

### Bundle Minimum

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי מַכְרִיז, מִנְיָן? מַאי אִרְיָא תְּלָתָא אֲפִילּוּ תְּרֵין נָמֵי! אֶלָּא כִּדְאָמַר רָבִינָא: טִבְעָא מַכְרֵיז, הָכָא נָמֵי ״שְׁטָרֵי״ מַכְרֵיז.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** does the person who found the documents **proclaim** so that the owner can claim it? The Gemara answers: He proclaims the **number** of documents that he found, and the owner can describe them by saying that they were rolled together. The Gemara asks: If so, **why** does the *tanna* teach **specifically** a case of **three** documents? **Even** if **two** documents are found, they can **also** be returned in this manner. **Rather,** the finder proclaims his find in a manner **similar to that which Ravina said:** If one finds coins, he simply **proclaims** that he found **coins,** without specifying the number. **Here too,** the finder **proclaims** that he found **documents,** and the owner describes them by both their exact number and the fact that they were rolled together. Therefore, if there are only two documents the description is deficient, as the number two is already implicit in the finder's proclamation that he found documents, which is plural.

The *mishnah* specifies *shlosha kshurin zeh bezeh* — three tied together — as the threshold for a bundle. The *gemara* notes that this might imply the number itself is part of the *siman*, and if so, why do we need three rather than two? The answer given invokes a principle that will be developed more fully in the next *perek*: the *gemara* raises the possibility that *kesher siman* — the type of knot serves as a *siman* — but then notes the complications that entails. The resolution draws on what Ravina will state elsewhere: one does not announce the number but merely announces *shtarei* — I found documents — and the claimant must then provide the identifying features, including both the number and the method of bundling.

### Announcing Found Items

*Mai machriz? Minyan* — what does the finder announce? He announces that he found a number of documents, meaning he gives out the number. Then the identifying *siman* comes from the claimant describing the bundling. The *gemara* asks: if the number is given in the announcement, why must there be at least three? Even two documents found together might be claimed by someone describing the manner of their bundling. The answer, following Ravina's approach, is that one does not actually announce the number at all. One simply announces *shtarei* — I found documents — and both the number and the bundling method together constitute the *siman* that the claimant must supply. The minimum of three is required because two documents bundled together might too easily be guessed by someone with no genuine claim. Three, combined with the specific method of bundling, provides sufficient specificity that the *siman* is meaningful. One notes, wryly, that a sufficiently learned claimant who knows the relevant halacha might reason through the possibilities: the finder said *shtarei*, so it can't be two — too obvious to count as a *siman* — so it must be at least three, and since it's being announced at all, the bundling must be unusual rather than a standard individual-roll-with-string. The system is not entirely immune to gaming by the overly learned.

### One Borrower/Three Lende…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד הַלֹּוֶה מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה יַחְזִיר לַלֹּוֶה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּמַלְוִין נִינְהוּ, מַאי בָּעוּ גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי? דִּלְמָא לְקַיּוֹמִינְהוּ אָזְלִי? דִּמְקַיְּימִי. דִּלְמָא מִידָא דְסָפְרָא נְפֻל? לָא מְשַׁהֵי אִינִישׁ קִיּוּמֵיהּ בִּידָא דְסָפְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says:** If one finds three documents each showing that the same borrower took loans from three different lenders, return the documents to the borrower. The reasoning: why would three different lenders' documents, each naming a different creditor but the same debtor, end up together? The most natural explanation is that they all fell from the borrower, who was carrying his own obligations. That implies the debts have been paid and the documents should go to him. The Gemara questions this: perhaps they fell from the various lenders, each of whom happened to have a document naming this borrower. But how did they end up together? **Perhaps** the borrower had brought them all to *beis din* to be validated simultaneously, sent through a single agent, and they fell from the agent or from the *beis din*. The *gemara* responds: **But** we can assume they are already validated, bearing the court's seal. Once validated, the money has already been lent and the documents should have been delivered to the lenders. No one leaves a validated, active loan document in the hands of a *sofer* or agent once the money has changed hands. Therefore the only plausible explanation remains that all three fell from the borrower.

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel omer: echad ha'loveh mishlosha yachzir laloveh* — if one finds three documents each showing that the same borrower took loans from three different lenders, return the documents to the borrower. The reasoning: why would three different lenders' documents, each naming a different creditor but the same debtor, end up together? The most natural explanation is that they all fell from the borrower, who was carrying his own obligations. That implies the debts have been paid and the documents should go to him.

### Validation Scenarios

The *gemara* questions this: perhaps they fell from the various lenders, each of whom happened to have a document naming this borrower. But how did they end up together? *Dilma l'kiyumeinhu azli* — perhaps the borrower had brought them all to *beis din* to be validated simultaneously, sent through a single agent, and they fell from the agent or from the *beis din*. The *gemara* responds: *d'mitkaymi* — but we can assume they are already validated, bearing the court's seal. Once validated, the money has already been lent and the documents should have been delivered to the lenders. No one leaves a validated, active loan document in the hands of a *sofer* or agent once the money has changed hands. Therefore the only plausible explanation remains that all three fell from the borrower.

### Three Borrowers/One Lend…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁלֹשָׁה שֶׁלָּווּ מֵאֶחָד יַחְזִיר לַמַּלְוֶה. דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ דְּלֹוִין נִינְהוּ, מַאי בָּעוּ גַּבֵּי הֲדָדֵי? דִּלְמָא לְמִכְתְּבִנְהוּ אֲזֻל? דְּכְתִיבִי בִּתְלָת יְדֵי סָפְרֵי. וְדִלְמָא לְקַיּוֹמִינְהוּ אֲזֻל? מַלְוֶה מְקַיֵּים שְׁטָרֵיהּ, לֹוֶה לָא מְקַיֵּים שְׁטָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the promissory notes make reference to the loans of **three** people **who borrowed from one** person, the one who found them **must return** them **to the creditor.** The Gemara explains: The reason for this is **that if it enters your mind that these** promissory notes **belong to** the **debtors, what are they doing together?** The Gemara asks: **Perhaps** the three **went** to one scribe **to write** the promissory notes, and the notes were then lost together. The Gemara answers that the mishna is referring to a case **where** the promissory notes **are written in the handwriting of three** different **scribes.** The Gemara suggests: **And perhaps** the three debtors **went** to the court in order **to ratify** the promissory notes, and they lost them there. The Gemara answers: It is the **creditor** who **ratifies his** promissory **note;** the **debtor does not ratify his** promissory **note.**

The reverse case: *shlosha halovim min echad yachzir lamalveh* — if three documents each show a different borrower taking a loan from the same lender, return the documents to the lender. The three borrowers presumably each took their own loan and would each be holding their own obligation; the only party with reason to hold all three together is the common lender. The *gemara* challenges: *dilma l'michtavinhu azli* — perhaps the three borrowers all came together to a single *sofer* to have their documents drawn up, and the documents fell from the *sofer* before the money was lent and before they were delivered. The *gemara* then responds in a way that connects to the next point.

### Scribe Handwriting Evide…

*D'ktivi bitlata yadei safrei* — the three documents are written in three different scribal handwritings. Since each document bears a distinct hand, they cannot have been written by a single *sofer* for multiple clients in one session. Three different sources produced these three documents. The only way they came to be found together is that they were all held by the single party common to all three — the lender — and fell from his possession. Furthermore, even if one considered the possibility that the borrowers had already received their documents and were now bringing them to *beis din* for validation, the *gemara* notes: *malveh mekiyem* — it is the lender, not the borrower, who initiates the validation process. The validation is the lender's proof that the money was lent; the borrower would not take the initiative to get his own obligation validated. Therefore, documents in three different hands, all naming the same lender, with validation already present, must have fallen from that lender.

## Receipts vs. Debt Docume…

### Receipt Negates Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהֶן סִמְפּוֹנוֹת, יַעֲשֶׂה מָה שֶׁבַּסִּמְפּוֹנוֹת. אָמַר רַב יִרְמְיָה בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַב: סִמְפּוֹן הַיּוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי מַלְוֶה, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁכָּתוּב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ – אֵינוֹ אֶלָּא כִּמְשַׂחֵק, וּפָסוּל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **If there are cancellations of contracts [*simponot*] among** one's documents, **he should do what is** stated **in the *simponot*.** The Gemara cites that which **Rav Yirmeya bar Abba says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **a *simpon* that emerges from the possession of a creditor, even if it is written in his** own **handwriting** and is clearly not forged, **it is** considered **as though he were merely jesting and** the *simpon* **is invalid.**

The *mishnah* states: *im yesh imahem simponos yaaseh mah she'basimponos* — if among one's documents there is found a *simpon*, a receipt or negating document, one acts in accordance with what the *simpon* says and treats the underlying debt as discharged. This is the *mishnah*'s ruling: a receipt found among one's documents is treated as valid.

### Rav Yirmiyah's Rule

The *gemara* presents what appears to be a direct contradiction from an authoritative ruling: *amar Rav Yirmiyah bar Abba amar Rav* — a *simpon hayotzei mitachas yedei malveh*, a receipt found in the possession of the *malveh*, *af al pi she'kasav biksav yado*, even if it is in the *malveh*'s own handwriting, *eino ela k'mesachek u'pasul* — it is nothing but a joke and is invalid. A *malveh* holding his own receipt proves nothing about payment.

### Handwritten Receipt Issu…

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא מִבַּעְיָא כָּתוּב בִּכְתַב יַד סוֹפֵר, דְּאִיכָּא לְמֵימַר סָפְרָא אִתְרְמִי לֵיהּ וּכְתַב, אֶלָּא אֲפִילּוּ כָּתוּב בִּכְתַב יָדוֹ פָּסוּל. סָבַר דִּלְמָא מִתְרְמֵי וְאָתֵי בֵּין הַשְּׁמָשׁוֹת וְקָא פָרַע לִי, דְּאִי לָא יָהֵיבְנָא לֵיהּ לָא יָהֵיב לִי זוּזֵי, אֶכְתּוֹב אֲנָא דְּכִי (אַיְיתַי) [מַיְיתֵי] לִי זוּזַי אֶתֵּן לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **It is not necessary** to state this *halakha* in a case where **it is written in the handwriting of a scribe, as it can be said** that **he happened to** have an opportunity to have **the scribe** write the *simpon*, **and** therefore he had him **write** it before the debt was repaid. **But even** in a case where **it is written in the handwriting of** the creditor **it is invalid.** The creditor may have written the *simpon* himself before the debt was repaid, **thinking: Perhaps** the debtor **will happen to come at twilight** on the eve of Shabbat **and** wish to **repay me.** I should prepare a document of cancellation, **as, if I do not give him** one, **he will not give me** the **money. I will** therefore **write** the document now, so **that when he brings me** the **money, I will give** it **to him.**

The *chiddush* of *af al pi she'kasav biksav yado* — even though it's in his own handwriting — requires explanation, since one might have thought a self-authored receipt is stronger evidence of payment than one written by a *sofer*. The *gemara* explains the logic in reverse. When the *malveh* is not literate and must rely on a *sofer* to write his receipt, there is indeed a concern that the *sofer* was engaged ahead of time because the *malveh* anticipated payment but wasn't sure a *sofer* would be conveniently available at the moment of payment. He pre-writes the receipt so it's ready to hand over the instant the money arrives. This is understandable. But if the *malveh* can write the receipt himself, why would he write it before receiving the money? Surely he would simply write it at the moment of payment. And yet — *savar dilma itrami leh bein hashmashos* — he may have reasoned that the borrower will come to pay right before *shabbos*, or at some inconvenient moment, and if he cannot immediately produce a receipt *d'kaffara li*, the borrower will refuse to pay — *lo yahiv li zuzei*. So he pre-writes the receipt, ready to hand it over the instant payment arrives: *echtov ana d'chi asi li zuzei eiten leh*. Since this is a plausible scenario, even the *malveh*'s own handwritten receipt found in his own possession proves nothing about whether payment was actually received.

### Receipt + Paid Documents

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: אִם יֵשׁ עִמָּהֶן סִמְפּוֹנוֹת, יַעֲשֶׂה מָה שֶׁבְּסִמְפּוֹנוֹת. כִּדְאָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: שֶׁנִּמְצָא בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹת קְרוּעִין, הָכָא נָמֵי שֶׁמְּצָאוֹ בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹת קְרוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the statement of Rav based on that which **we learned** in the mishna: **If there are *simponot* among** one's documents, **he should do what is** stated **in the *simponot*.** This is apparently referring to *simponot* that are in the possession of the creditor. The Gemara answers that the mishna is to be understood **in accordance with** that **which Rav Safra said** in response to another difficulty: It is referring to a case **where** the *simpon* **was found among torn documents.** This indicates that the *simpon* is valid, as had the debt not been repaid, the creditor would not have put the *simpon* note among torn documents. **Here too,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the *simpon* **was found among torn documents.**

This creates an obvious tension with the *mishnah*, which states that one acts in accordance with a *simpon* found among one's documents. *Amar Rav Safra* — Rav Safra reconciles the two: the *mishnah* is speaking of a case where the *simpon* was found among *shtaros kru'in*, torn-up or paid-up documents. The combination of a receipt and its presence in the drawer where the *malveh* keeps his discharged documents — rather than among his active *shtaros* — creates a meaningful presumption that the debt was indeed paid. On its own, a receipt in the *malveh*'s possession proves nothing. But a receipt filed with documents that are definitively no longer active is a different matter. *Rashi* offers two versions of this: either the specific loan document in question was found among the torn-up documents (though itself not torn), or the receipt itself was found there. Either way, the combination provides the additional evidential weight the *simpon* alone lacks. This is acknowledged not to be the *pshat* of the *mishnah* but the operative halacha.

### Same Name Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִמְצָא לְאֶחָד בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹתָיו שְׁטָרוֹ שֶׁל יוֹסֵף בֶּן שִׁמְעוֹן פָּרוּעַ – שְׁטָרוֹת שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּרוּעִין! כִּדְאָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: שֶׁנִּמְצָא בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹת קְרוּעִין, הָכָא נָמֵי שֶׁנִּמְצָא בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹת קְרוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rav's statement from a mishna (*Bava Batra* 172a): If **one found among his documents** a *simpon* that says: The debt mentioned in **the** promissory **note of Yosef ben Shimon is repaid,** and there are two people by that name who owe him money, the debts mentioned in **the promissory notes of both of them are** considered **repaid,** as each can claim that the cancellation is referring to his debt, and the burden of proof rests upon the creditor. Apparently, a *simpon* that is found in the possession of the creditor is valid. The Gemara answers that this mishna, too, is to be understood **in accordance with** that **which Rav Safra said** in response to another difficulty: It is referring to a case **where** the *simpon* **was found among torn documents. Here too,** the mishna is referring to a case **where** the *simpon* **was found among torn documents.**

The *gemara* brings another case to bear: *nimtza l'echad bein shtarosav shtar she'yotzei bein Shimon parua* — among a *malveh*'s documents is found a receipt saying that Yosef ben Shimon has paid his debt, but this *malveh* has two different borrowers both named Yosef ben Shimon, each owing money on separate *shtaros*. The *malveh* knows which *shtar* belongs to which Yosef ben Shimon, but cannot determine which of the two the receipt refers to. The ruling is *shnayim pruim* — both debts are rendered uncollectible. And once again, the fact that the *mishnah* apparently trusts this receipt in the *malveh*'s hands is addressed with the same answer of *Rav Safra*: only when found among paid-up documents does the receipt carry evidentiary weight.

### Orphans' Oath

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: ״שְׁבוּעָה שֶׁלֹּא פְּקָדַנוּ אַבָּא, וְשֶׁלֹּא אָמַר לָנוּ אַבָּא, וְשֶׁלֹּא מָצָאנוּ בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹתָיו שֶׁל אַבָּא שֶׁשְּׁטָר זֶה פָּרוּעַ״. אָמַר רַב סָפְרָא: שֶׁנִּמְצָא בֵּין שְׁטָרוֹת קְרוּעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge to Rav's statement from a mishna (*Shevuot* 45a): If orphans who inherited their father's property demand repayment of a debt owed to their father from the orphans of the debtor, they are required to take an oath stating: We take **an oath that** our **father did not instruct us** on his deathbed that the debt mentioned in this promissory note was repaid and it should be returned to the debtor, **nor did** our **father say to us** on an earlier date that it was repaid, **nor did we find among the documents of** our **father** a *simpon* stating **that this** promissory **note** was **repaid.** This too, seems to indicate that a *simpon* is valid even if it is found in the possession of the creditor. The Gemara answers by citing the statement of **Rav Safra,** who **said** in this context that this reference is to a case **where** the *simpon* **was found among torn documents.**

Another proof text: when orphans seek to collect a debt from other orphans, the halacha requires them to take an oath before collecting. The precise formulation of the oath includes: *shvua she'lo pikadnu abba v'shelo amar lanu abba v'shelo matzanu bein shtarosav shel abba she'shtar zeh parua* — we swear that our father never instructed us about this *shtar*, never told us not to collect it, and that we did not find any documentation among our father's papers indicating this debt was paid. Crucially, had they found such documentation, they would be bound by it. This implies that documentation of payment found among one's papers is treated as valid. Once again, Rav Safra applies: *she'nimtza bein shtaros k'ruim* — only when the documentation is found among the torn-up papers is it treated as conclusive evidence.

### Receipt + Witnesses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: סִמְפּוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו. אֵימָא ״יִתְקַיֵּים מֵחוֹתְמָיו״! דְּשָׁיְילִינַן לְהוּ לְסָהֲדִי אִי פָּרוּעַ אִי לָא פָּרוּעַ.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge from a *baraita*: **A *simpon* upon which witnesses** are signed **is ratified by** means of **its signatories.** The court verifies the validity of the witnesses' signatures and thereby ratifies the document. This too seems to include a *simpon* that is in the possession of the creditor. The Gemara answers: **Say** that the *baraita* reads: **Is ratified** by obtaining confirmation **from its signatories,** **as we ask the witnesses whether** the loan was **repaid** or **whether** it was **not repaid.**

*Tashma: simpon she'yesh alav edim yitkayem b'chosmav* — if a *simpon* bears the signatures of witnesses, validate the signatures and it is good. Since stating this of a receipt in the *loveh*'s possession would be obvious, the *gemara* assumes it refers to a *malveh* holding the receipt. This again appears to validate a *malveh*'s receipt. The *gemara* responds: *mai edim edei kiyum* — the *edim* here does not mean simply that the receipt has witnesses on it; it means the receipt was formally validated in *beis din*. A receipt that was brought before the court and had its signatures authenticated will never have been validated unless it was genuinely active and operative. The court does not validate a document that has not yet taken effect. So this case is not evidence that an ordinary receipt in the *malveh*'s hands is trusted; only one that passed through the formal validation process.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: סִמְפּוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – כָּשֵׁר. מַאי עֵדִים? עֵדֵי קִיּוּם. הָכִי נָמֵי מִסְתַּבְּרָא, מִדְּקָתָנֵי סֵיפָא: וְשֶׁאֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים – פָּסוּל. מַאי ״אֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים״? אִילֵּימָא דְּלֵיכָּא עִלָּוֵיהּ עֵדִים כְּלָל, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר דְּפָסוּל?! אֶלָּא לָאו עֵדֵי קִיּוּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Come** and **hear** another challenge from a *baraita*: **A *simpon* upon which witnesses** are signed is **valid.** Apparently, it is valid even if it is found in the possession of the creditor, as no distinction is made. The Gemara answers: To **what witnesses** is the *baraita* referring? It is referring to **witnesses of ratification.** The fact that the *simpon* was ratified by the court proves its validity. The Gemara notes that **this too stands to reason, from** the fact **that** the *baraita* **teaches** in **the latter clause: And** a *simpon* **upon which witnesses** are **not** signed is **invalid. What** is meant by the expression: **Upon which witnesses** are **not** signed? **If we say** that it means **that there are no witnesses** signed **on it at all,** does it **need to be said that** it is **invalid? Rather, is it not** referring to a *simpon* on which witnesses are signed, just not **witnesses of ratification?**

This reading is confirmed by the *seifa*: *she'ein alav edim pasul* — without witnesses, invalid. *Mai ein alav edim* — what does "without witnesses" mean? If it means no witnesses at all and it was not even in the *malveh*'s own handwriting, of course it's invalid — obviously a receipt with no attestation of any kind is meaningless. So *ein alav edim* must mean it lacks court validation — *lav edei kiyum*. The two halves of the ruling thus align: a receipt with *edei kiyum* is valid; one without court validation is not.

### Receipt on Document

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא. סִמְפּוֹן שֶׁיֵּשׁ עָלָיו עֵדִים – יִתְקַיֵּים בְּחוֹתְמָיו. אֵין עָלָיו עֵדִים וְיוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שָׁלִישׁ, אוֹ שֶׁיּוֹצֵא לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת – כָּשֵׁר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara discusses **the** *baraita* **itself** cited above: **A *simpon* upon which witnesses** are signed **is ratified by** means of **its signatories.** If **there are no witnesses** signed **on it, but** the *simpon* **emerges from the possession of a third party** serving as a trustee, **or** if **it emerges after the signing of** the **documents,** i.e., the *simpon* was written on the promissory note beneath the content of the note and the witnesses' signatures, it is **valid.**

*Simpon she'yesh alav edim kasher* — the *gemara* returns to consider this ruling in its own right. *O she'yatza le'achar chitum shtaros* — or if the receipt appears written after the signatures on the original loan document itself. Rather than a separate document, below the witnesses' signatures on the *shtar chov* itself, the *malveh* has written that Michael paid his ten thousand dollars, and obtained signatures on that as well. This is obviously valid as proof of payment: *d'i lav d'para lo hava marei leh leshtareih* — if it had not been paid, he would never write on the document itself that it had been, thereby destroying his own *shtar*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹצֵא מִתַּחַת יְדֵי שָׁלִישׁ, דְּהָא הֵימְנֵיהּ מַלְוֶה לְשָׁלִישׁ. יוֹצֵא לְאַחַר חִיתּוּם שְׁטָרוֹת נָמֵי, דְּאִי לָאו דִּפְרִיעַ לָא הֲוָה מַרַע לֵיהּ לִשְׁטָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: The reason that it is valid if it **emerges from the possession of a third party** is **that** the **creditor granted credibility to** the **third party** by placing the *simpon* in his possession. **So too,** the *simpon* is valid in a case where **it emerges after the signing of** the **documents, as, if not** for the fact **that** the debt **was repaid,** the creditor **would not have undermined his note** by allowing the *simpon* to be written on it.

### Third Party Holder

*Ein alav edim v'yotzei mitachas yad shalish* — if there are no witnesses on the receipt but it is found in the possession of a third party who affirms its validity. Why does this make it valid? *Deheymnei malveh leshalish* — the *malveh* trusted the *shalish* by the very act of placing the document with him. Whether the *loveh* deposited the receipt with this trusted third party, or whether the *malveh* himself handed it to the third party with instructions to hold it until the debt was paid, in either case the *malveh*'s act of entrusting the document to the third party constitutes an implicit acceptance of whatever testimony the *shalish* now offers about its status. The third party holds and says: the loan was paid. The *malveh*'s original act of trust makes that claim binding.

With this the *perek* concludes, and we recite *hadran alach shenayim ochazin*.
