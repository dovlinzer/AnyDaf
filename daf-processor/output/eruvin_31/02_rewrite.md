# Eruvin 31 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Beis HaPras Access

### Blowing Path Through

The daf opens by returning to the question of where an *eruv* may be placed and, specifically, what makes a location halachically accessible. The *mishna* had already addressed the case of a *kohen* placing his *eruv* in a *beis hapras* — a field in which a grave was plowed over, scattering small fragments of bone throughout the soil — and ruled that this is permitted. The *gemara* now explains the reasoning: as Rav Yehuda said in the name of Shmuel, *menapeach adam beis hapras v'holech* — a person, meaning a *kohen*, may blow the dirt directly in front of him as he walks, clearing it aside to confirm that no bone chips lie in his path. This technique renders the *beis hapras* at least theoretically traversable.

The *gemara* adds a related teaching of Rav Yehuda bar Ami in the name of Rav Yehuda: a *beis hapras* that has been regularly trampled through is *tahor*, on the presumption that any bone fragments along that path have already been crushed to a size below the *tumah*-transmitting threshold. While one might question whether tiny chips could remain lodged in the soil undetected, the operative principle is that theoretical accessibility suffices — and so, since the *kohen* can in principle reach his *eruv*, it is halachically valid.

### Cemetery Placement

Rabbi Yehuda extends the ruling even further, permitting a *kohen* to place his *eruv* in a *beis hakevaros* — a full cemetery — stating *af beis hakevaros*, and not merely a *beis hapras*. A *beraita* explains that this is possible *mipnei sheyachol lachutz v'leilech b'shida teiva u'migdal* — because the *kohen* can make himself a *chatzitza* and traverse the cemetery by enclosing himself within a box, a chest, or a tower-like carriage. Being carried through in such a sealed unit means that any *tumah* rising from graves beneath him is blocked by the floor of the structure.

Rashi notes that this box must be larger than forty *se'ah* in volume, which removes it from the category of a *kli* and thereby ensures it functions purely as a spatial barrier rather than as a vessel that might itself contract *tumah*. This arrangement presumably addresses both the problem of walking over graves and, if the structure has a roof, the problem of overhanging *tumah* — since the enclosed space would constitute a separate domain. The key point is that the *kohen* is not considered to have direct contact with the *tumah* of the cemetery so long as he remains within this self-contained unit.

### Ohel Zaruk Debate

The *gemara* then asks why the Rabbis who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda would not concede to this solution. Their position is grounded in the principle of *ohel zaruk* — whether a structure that is not stationary, but is being carried or thrown through space, qualifies as an *ohel* at all.

The question of *ohel zaruk*, a "thrown tent," is whether a moving overhead structure — one not fixed in place — can function halachically as an *ohel* either to transmit or to block *tumah*. The *gemara* offers a vivid illustration: imagine a plank of wood flying through the air over a corpse. As it passes overhead, does it serve as an *ohel* to transfer the *tumah* to whatever is beneath it? Conversely, does it block *tumah* from rising to something above it? A structurally analogous modern case would be an airplane flying over a cemetery — and indeed, airports are frequently situated near cemeteries, raising questions about whether the fuselage constitutes an *ohel zaruk* that could shield *kohanim* aboard. That discussion, with its additional complications (airplane fuselages are metal, and metal does not function as a *chatzitza* against *tumah*), is not pursued here, but the conceptual category is the same.

### Non-Jewish Land Case

The *gemara* connects the *ohel zaruk* debate to a parallel dispute among *tannaim* regarding someone entering the land of the nations — *eretz ha'amim* — in a box or carriage. The Sages treated *eretz ha'amim* as *tamei* in a manner analogous to *tumas ohel*, as though the very ground carried corpse-*tumah*. The question is whether traveling through it enclosed in a *shida*, *teiva*, or *migdal* provides protection.

*Rabbi metamei* — Rabbi [Yehuda HaNasi] holds that this does not protect and you remain *tamei* — while *Rabbi Yose b'Rabbi Yehuda mitaher* — Rabbi Yose the son of Rabbi Yehuda holds you are *tahor*. The *gemara* identifies the crux: *mar savar ohel zaruk lav shemei ohel* — one holds that a moving structure is not a valid *ohel*, while *u'mar savar ohel zaruk shemei ohel* — the other holds that it is. The debate in our passage about Rabbi Yehuda's position on placing an *eruv* in a cemetery maps directly onto this *tannaic* dispute.

## Accessing the Eruv

### Rashi on Access

Having established the theoretical mechanism for a *kohen* to enter a cemetery, the *gemara* turns to a more fundamental requirement: not only must the *eruv* food be of a kind the person can eat, but the location of the *eruv* must be one the person can actually reach. If a *kohen* cannot get to his *eruv*, it is invalid regardless of any other consideration.

Rashi explains that this requirement — that the *kohen* must be able to access his *eruv* in a *beis hakevaros* — is connected to the position of Sumchus. Rashi's language is explicit: *v'rabanan d'matnitin k'Rabbi sevira lehu, u'vein Rabbi Yehuda u'vein l'rabanan k'Sumchos sevira lei* — both Rabbi Yehuda and the Rabbis of the *mishna* are operating in accordance with Sumchus, *d'amar midi d'chazi lei l'meichlei b'hahu sha'ata ba'inan* — who requires that the *eruv* be something fit for the person himself to eat at that moment. Because Sumchus demands personal eligibility to eat the food, Rashi reads into that principle a corollary: the food must be physically accessible to that person.

### Tosafot Distinction

Tosafot disagree with Rashi's framing, drawing a sharp distinction between the requirement to *eat* the *eruv* and the requirement to *access* it. Tosafot argue that accessibility is a universal condition agreed upon by all — by Sumchus and by the *chachamim* alike. The debate between Sumchus and the *chachamim* concerns only whether the *eruv* food must be fit for *this person* to eat it, or whether it suffices that *someone* could eat it. But on the prior question of whether the *eruv* must be reachable at all, there is no dispute: everyone agrees that if the person cannot get to the *eruv*, it is invalid.

Accordingly, Tosafot hold that the accessibility problem posed by a cemetery applies according to *all* opinions — it is not a consequence specific to Sumchus's view, but a baseline requirement of *eruv* law in general.

## Wooden Rod Solution

### Food Tahor Status

The *gemara* now addresses the more specific case articulated on 31a: *mearvin l'kohen tahor b'trumah tehora b'kever* — one may make an *eruv* for a *tahor kohen* using *tahor trumah* placed directly on top of a grave. This scenario is striking in its specificity, and the *gemara* immediately asks how it is even possible: the moment you place food on a grave, does it not become *tamei*?

The answer is *b'shelo huchshara* — it works only with food that has never come into contact with liquid and has therefore never become susceptible to *tumah*. The principle derives from the verse *ki yutan mayim al zera* — that produce only becomes susceptible to *tumah* once liquid has been applied to it after harvesting. An apple that has never been wetted since being picked, or grain that has never been moistened, cannot contract *tumah* even when resting on a grave.

The *gemara* adds a second case: *o shelo lasha be'mei peiros* — or if the food in question is bread baked without water, using instead fruit juice, eggs, or similar liquids that are not among the seven halachically significant liquids. Such bread, having never come into contact with water (or the other specified liquids), is likewise not susceptible to *tumah* and can rest on the grave while remaining *tahor*.

### Rod Mechanism

With the food confirmed as *tahor*, the *gemara* asks how the *kohen* is to retrieve it without himself contracting *tumah*. He is enclosed in his *shida*, *teiva*, or *migdal* — the carrying box — which protects him as he travels through the cemetery. But once he arrives at the grave, he must somehow take the food without reaching over it with an exposed limb.

The *gemara* proposes using *bepashutei k'lei eitz delo m'kabli tumah* — a simple wooden rod without any receptacle. A flat wooden rod or pole, as opposed to a metal one, and one with no hollow or concave surface, cannot contract *tumah*. The *kohen* inserts this rod from within the box, passes it over the grave, and uses it to push or draw the food toward himself. In this way, the food remains *tahor*, he remains *tahor*, and the *eruv* is valid.

### Rod as Ohel

The *gemara* immediately raises a complication: *veha ka ma'ahil* — but the rod, even if it does not itself become *tamei*, nonetheless forms an *ohel* over the grave. When the rod is extended horizontally over the grave, any *tumah* rising from the corpse beneath would be caught by the rod as if by a tent covering, and that *tumah* would then extend to whatever lies beneath — or be transmitted onward to the *kohen* himself. Rabbinically, even a circumference of a *tefach* is sufficient to render an object a functioning *ohel*, creating this problem even for a relatively thin rod.

### Pointed Rod

The resolution is to use a rod that tapers to a point at the end — *d'mayti lei achudei*, bringing it to a fine tip. Tosafot here emends the text from *achorei* ("by the back," which yields no sensible reading) to *achudei* ("by the point"). The reasoning is straightforward: if the portion of the rod that extends over the grave is pointed rather than flat, it lacks sufficient surface area at that extremity to constitute an *ohel*. It cannot catch and transmit the *tumah* rising from below, and so the *kohen* can draw the food to himself without any halachic concern.

With this, a complete mechanism is in place: the food is rendered unsusceptible to *tumah* through the absence of liquid contact; the *kohen* travels to the grave enclosed in his protective box; and he retrieves the food using a pointed wooden rod that neither contracts *tumah* nor forms an *ohel*.

## Grave as Benefit

### Issurei Hana'ah

Having resolved the technical questions of access and food-preservation, the *gemara* presses further: if the solutions above are available to everyone, why do the Rabbis forbid placing an *eruv* on a grave at all? The answer now shifts to an entirely different plane of analysis.

*Kasvari assur liknot bayit b'issurei hana'ah* — the Rabbis hold that you cannot establish your halachic "house" — the place you are deemed to have begun *Shabbos* — on something from which benefit is prohibited. A corpse, and by extension a grave and everything directly associated with it, falls into the category of *issurei hana'ah*. To place your *eruv* food on a grave, and thereby designate that grave as the locus of your *Shabbos* residence, amounts to deriving benefit from a forbidden source.

The category of *issurei hana'ah* here is instructive in a broader sense. The prohibition against deriving benefit from the dead is fundamentally physical in character. Intellectual or educational uses — such as the halachic discussions surrounding items made from human remains, or questions that arose in connection with the Holocaust regarding objects such as lampshades — touch on these same principles, but the consensus of halachic authorities is that such abstract, non-physical engagement does not constitute the kind of *hana'ah* the Torah forbids. Physical benefit is the target of the prohibition.

### Mitzvot Not Benefit

Rabbi Yehuda's permissive ruling rests on the principle of *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* — the fulfillment of *mitzvot* is not considered *hana'ah* in the halachic sense. The reasoning depends on the assumption, noted already in earlier discussion, that one makes an *eruv techumin* specifically for the sake of a *mitzvah* — to reach another city in order to attend a *bris*, for example. Since the only benefit the *kohen* derives from using the grave as his *eruv* location is the ability to perform a *mitzvah*, and since that benefit is not classified as *hana'ah*, there is no forbidden use of the grave at all.

The principle *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* tends to operate on physical benefit — the Torah's prohibitions on deriving benefit target tangible, bodily enjoyment. The benefit of performing a *mitzvah* is of a different, non-physical order, and Rabbi Yehuda holds that it therefore falls outside the scope of the prohibition.

## Rava's Resolution

### Mitzvah-Only Purpose

The *gemara* now introduces Rava's analysis: *leima k'tannai* — does Rava's principle that *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* reflect a *tannaic* dispute? If the Rabbis forbid placing the *eruv* on a grave, one might infer they hold that *mitzvah* benefit *is* considered *hana'ah* — which would mean Rava's position is contested by these *tannaim*.

Rava rejects this inference. He reframes the entire *tannaic* dispute not as a disagreement about whether *mitzvah*-benefit is *hana'ah*, but as a disagreement about whether an *eruv techumin* may be made exclusively for *mitzvah* purposes or also for entirely discretionary purposes (*lidvar reshus*). According to Rava, *everyone* agrees that *mitzvah*-benefit is not *hana'ah*. If all parties agreed that an *eruv* may only be made *lidvar mitzvah*, then all parties would permit placing it on a grave, since the only benefit derived would be the opportunity to perform a *mitzvah*, and that is not forbidden *hana'ah*. The debate is therefore: *mar savar ein me'arvin ella lidvar mitzvah* — one holds an *eruv* may only be made for a *mitzvah*, while *u'mar savar me'arvin afilu lidvar reshus* — the other permits it even for non-obligatory purposes.

If an *eruv* can be made for discretionary purposes, then when a person designates a grave as his *eruv* location, the benefit he extracts is not merely *mitzvah*-benefit — it is the general freedom to travel wherever he wishes. That constitutes genuine *hana'ah*, and the grave therefore cannot be used. This analysis preserves Rava's principle intact: the debate is about purpose, not about the nature of *mitzvah*-benefit.

It is worth noting that this question of purpose does not collapse the *mitzvah* into its incidental accompaniments. Even when one travels to a *bris* — where one will also eat a festive meal, see friends, and enjoy the occasion — those attendant pleasures do not override the primary *mitzvah* character of the journey. Halachically, the *mitzvah* purpose is what drove the action, and the collateral enjoyments are treated as insignificant alongside it. This relationship — whereby *mitzvot lav lehanot nitnu* extends to minimize the significance of surrounding non-*mitzvah* benefits — is developed more fully in other *sugyot*.

### Rav Yosef Position

The *gemara* then confronts Rava's resolution with a challenge from Rav Yosef's independently stated position: *ein me'arvin ella lidvar mitzvah* — an *eruv* may only be made for the sake of a *mitzvah*. This is not merely a *tannaic* claim; it is Rav Yosef's own ruling. If Rava's resolution of the *tannaic* dispute frames it precisely as a debate over this question, is Rav Yosef now forced to concede that his own ruling is the subject of *tannaic* controversy?

Rav Yosef responds that he can accept Rava's framework without contradiction. He would say: *dechulei alma ein me'arvin ella lidvar mitzvah u'dechulei alma mitzvos lav lehanos nitnu* — everyone agrees that an *eruv* may only be made for *mitzvah* purposes, and everyone agrees that *mitzvah*-benefit is not *hana'ah*. If so, what exactly is the *tannaic* debate about placing the *eruv* on a grave?

### Food Protection

The debate, according to Rav Yosef's reading, concerns what happens after the *eruv* has already taken halachic effect — after the food has already functioned to establish the person's *Shabbos* domicile. *Mar savar kivan de'kaneh lei eruv lo nicha lei d'mitnatra* — one holds that once the *eruv* has been "acquired" (that is, once it has satisfied the halachic requirement and established your *Shabbos* location), you no longer have any interest in the food's physical preservation. You used the grave purely to enable the *mitzvah*-act of *eruv*, and once that act is complete, the grave's role is over. The benefit you extracted was purely and entirely *mitzvah*-benefit, which is not *hana'ah*.

*U'mar savar nicha lei d'mitnatra* — the other holds that even after the *eruv* takes effect, you do want the food to remain protected and available. *De'itzterich l'achila* — because if you become hungry on your journey to the other city, you may want to eat your *eruv* food. If that is the case, then you are using the grave not only as a vehicle for a *mitzvah*-act, but as a storage location — a place to keep food you intend to eat. That is real physical *hana'ah*, even if the initial purpose was a *mitzvah*, and therefore the grave cannot be used.

On this reading, the entire debate is not about whether *mitzvah*-benefit is *hana'ah* (everyone agrees it is not), nor about whether an *eruv* requires a *mitzvah* purpose (everyone agrees it does). The debate is about whether the person has an ongoing interest in the food *beyond* the *mitzvah*-act itself.

Tosafot observes that on this analysis, the prohibition against using a grave for an *eruv* has nothing specifically to do with the *kohen*'s status. The problem applies equally to a non-*kohen* — the question is simply whether anyone, *kohen* or not, is using the grave for benefit beyond the *mitzvah*. The additional *chiddush* of the case in the *mishna* is that even for a *kohen* using *tahor trumah*, the mechanism described above (the sealed box, the pointed wooden rod, the liquid-free food) makes it halachically viable — for those who permit it.

## Food Types

### Demai Permitted

The *gemara* turns to the next *mishna*, which enumerates which foods may and may not be used for an *eruv*. The *mishna* permits using *demai* — produce purchased from an *am ha'aretz*, someone not scrupulous about *terumot u'ma'asrot*, where there is rabbinic doubt whether *ma'aser* was properly separated. One might think *demai* would be invalid because the person cannot currently eat it (since he is rabbinically required to separate *ma'aser* from it first), which would raise the problem highlighted by Sumchus — that the *eruv* must be fit for the person to eat.

The *gemara* resolves this with a *migo* argument: *migo dei baei mapkeir lei lenichsei vahavei ani vachazi lei* — since he *could*, if he chose to, renounce all his property and become a pauper, and the Sages permitted paupers to eat *demai* without separating *ma'aser*, therefore even now the food is theoretically fit for him. *Hashta nami chazi lei* — at this moment too, it is fit for him, since the theoretical pathway to eating it exists.

The source for this leniency toward the poor is a *mishna* that states *machilin es ha'aniyim demai* — the poor may be fed *demai*. Beis Shammai disagree and prohibit this, while Beis Hillel permit it, and the halacha follows Beis Hillel.

Tosafot raises a pointed question: why invoke the *migo* that the person could become poor? Why not simply note that he could separate *ma'aser* from *demai* even during *bein hashmashos* — the twilight period on *Erev Shabbos* — and thereby render it immediately fit for him? The answer appears to lie partly in the formulaic nature of this *gemara*. The precise formulation — permitting *demai* because in theory you could become an *ani* — appears nearly verbatim in numerous places throughout *shas* wherever a *mishna* discusses permissible and impermissible foods. The answer given here is the standard answer, even if alternative resolutions might apply in specific contexts.

### Ma'aser Rishon Case

The *mishna* also permits using *ma'aser rishon* from which *terumah* has been taken (*shenitlah terumato*). This seems obvious — once the priestly portion is removed, *ma'aser rishon* is entirely non-sacred and may be eaten by anyone. The *gemara* therefore asks *mai l'memra* — why does the *mishna* need to say this?

The answer is that the *mishna* is addressing a specific and unusual case: *dehakedimo b'shibolim* — the *levi* came to the farmer while the grain was still in the stalks, before threshing and before the formal obligation of *terumot u'ma'asrot* had taken effect, and collected his *ma'aser* then. In this scenario, he took *terumat ma'aser* (the tenth of his tenth that he must give to the *kohen*) — so *nitlah terumato*, the relevant *terumah* was removed — but *terumah gedolah*, the main priestly portion, was never separated from the grain.

Normally, if *terumah gedolah* has not been taken, the produce remains *tevel* and may not be eaten. The *chiddush* is that when the *levi* collects his *ma'aser* while the grain is still in the stalks, the requirement of *terumah gedolah* does not apply to that *ma'aser* at all. This ruling is attributed to Reish Lakish as cited by Rabbi Abbahu: *maaser rishon shehikdimo b'shibolim patur mitterumah gedolah* — if the *levi* jumped ahead and received his *ma'aser* while the grain was still in stalks, that *ma'aser* is exempt from *terumah gedolah*. The derivation is from the verse *vaharimosem mimenu terumas Hashem* — which, in context, refers to *terumat ma'aser* (the *levi*'s tenth of his tenth), and teaches that the *levi* must give a tenth of a tenth — but not that *terumah gedolah* must first be taken from the *ma'aser* he received early.

Rav Pappa challenged this ruling before Abaye: if this is so, would the same not apply when the *levi* gets there first but the grain has already been threshed and piled — would he also be exempt from *terumah gedolah* in that case? Abaye responds from a different verse: *mikol maasroseichem tarimu es kol terumas Hashem* — from all your *ma'asros* you must take all the *terumos*, including *terumah gedolah*. Once the grain has been transformed — once it has been threshed and constitutes grain in the halachic sense — the obligation of *terumah gedolah* has already attached to it. The *levi* cannot bypass that obligation by collecting early. Only if he collected while it was still in the stalks, before the grain had halachically materialized as grain, can the obligation of *terumah gedolah* be finessed. That distinction is the *chiddush* the *mishna* conveys.

### Tevel Prohibited

The *mishna* prohibits using *tevel* — produce from which *terumot u'ma'asrot* have not been taken. The *gemara* finds this obvious (*pshita*) and explains that the case the *mishna* intends is not biblical *tevel* but rabbinic *tevel* — produce that is rabbinically obligated in separation even though it lacks biblical force. An example would be produce grown in a pot without a hole in the bottom, where the earth is not connected to the ground; such produce is rabbinically (but not biblically) obligated in *terumot u'ma'asrot*.

One might have thought that rabbinic *tevel* should be treated leniently — like *demai*, perhaps — but the *mishna* teaches otherwise. Rabbinic *tevel* is more stringent than *demai*: *demai* has the leniency of *bein hashmashos* separation and the *am ha'aretz* leniency for the poor, whereas rabbinic *tevel* carries none of these escape valves. It is treated with the same stringency as biblical *tevel* and may not be used for an *eruv*.

The *mishna* also excludes *ma'aser rishon* from which *terumah* has not been taken, and *ma'aser sheni* and *hekdesh* that have not been redeemed. For *ma'aser rishon*, the relevant case is the inverse of the permitted case above: here, the *levi* collected his *ma'aser* after the grain had been threshed and piled (*hikdimo b'kri*), took *terumat ma'aser* but not *terumah gedolah* — and since collection at this stage does not exempt from *terumah gedolah*, the produce remains *tevel* and may not be used.

For *ma'aser sheni* and *hekdesh*, the *gemara* explains that the prohibition applies even in cases of flawed redemption: *ma'aser sheni* redeemed on an unminted coin (violating the verse *vetzarta hakesef*, which requires a coin bearing an image) remains unredeemed, as does *hekdesh* redeemed against land (since the verse requires monetary redemption). In contrast, the *mishna*'s permissive ruling on *ma'aser sheni* and *hekdesh* extends even to redemption without the added fifth (*chomesh*) — since the *gemara* rules that the *chomesh* is not essential to complete the redemption.

## Agency Rules

### Incapable Persons

The final *mishna* of the daf shifts from the content of the *eruv* to the question of who may place it. *Hasholeach eruvo biyod cheresh shoteh vekatan* — one who sends his *eruv* via a *cheresh* (deaf-mute), a *shoteh* (a person of insufficient mental capacity), or a *katan* (a minor) — the three categories that halachah treats as lacking *bar da'at* status. The common denominator is not a particular cognitive failing; it is the halachic determination that these individuals are not fully obligated in *mitzvot* and therefore cannot serve as halachic agents. Included in the prohibition is sending the *eruv* through *mi she'eino modeh ba'eruv* — someone who rejects the concept of *eruv* altogether, such as a *kuti* (Samaritan), as Rav Chisda explains. The *eruv* placed through any of these individuals is invalid.

### Techumin vs. Chatzeirot

The *gemara* immediately complicates this by noting Rav Huna's teaching that *a katan* can collect the *eruv* — seemingly contradicting the *mishna*. The resolution: *kan be'eruvei techumin kan be'eruvei chatzeiros* — the *mishna*'s prohibition applies to *eruv techumin*, while Rav Huna's permission applies to *eruv chatzeiros*.

Why the distinction? One might have assumed that *eruv chatzeiros* — which joins together all the residents of a shared courtyard — requires a more significant act of communal consolidation, and therefore demands an adult, while *eruv techumin* is merely placing food in a particular spot. Rashi inverts this intuition. *Eruv techumin* requires the agent to halachically "acquire" (*koneh*) the location as the person's *Shabbos* domicile — an act analogous to establishing residence, which demands someone with full halachic capacity. *Eruv chatzeiros*, by contrast, is more of a formality: the residents are already de facto partners in the shared space, and no new domain is being created. Therefore a minor can suffice for the latter.

Tosafot offers a different rationale: *eruv techumin* traces its roots to a biblical prohibition (*lo yetzei ish mimkomo*), and because it is more closely tied to a biblical concept, greater stringency applies in requiring an adult agent.

### Non-Believer Agent

The exclusion of *mi she'eino modeh ba'eruv* — identified by Rav Chisda as a *kuti* — raises a broader question about agency and categorical belonging. For someone to act as an agent in a halachic matter, they must themselves be within the relevant halachic category. This principle surfaces elsewhere: there is debate about whether a woman may tie *tzitzit* (since she is not personally obligated), or whether a *mumar* for *bris milah* may serve as a *mohel*. The *kuti* case is a sharp example: he is halachically Jewish in many respects, yet his principled rejection of rabbinic *eruv* law removes him from the class of those who can effectuate it for others.

### Indirect Transport

The *mishna* then carves out a permitted role for these incapable individuals: *ve'im amar le'acher lekablo heimenu, harei zeh eruv* — if a qualified adult is waiting at the destination to receive the *eruv* from the *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*, and that adult then places it, the *eruv* is valid. The legally incapable person is functioning only as a courier — performing no halachic act — and anyone can serve as a courier. The *gemara* extends this to its logical extreme: if the *eruv* is placed on an elephant or a monkey and carried to the destination, and an adult receives it and places it, it is valid. The mode of transport is entirely irrelevant; only the person who performs the significant act of placement matters.

### Observation Required

But if the courier is a *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan*, can the sender trust that delivery actually occurred? The *gemara* answers, following Rav Chisda, that trust is not assumed — the sender must personally observe the delivery: *b'omed v'ro'eh*. Unlike a competent adult agent, an incapable courier does not carry the presumption of reliable execution. Visual confirmation is therefore required to establish that the *eruv* actually reached the receiving adult.

### Chazakah Principle

A second question follows: even if the sender observed the *eruv* reach the adult receiver, how can one be certain that the receiver actually accepted it and went through with the placement? Here the *gemara* invokes a different principle: *chazakah shaliach oseh shlichuto* — there is a presumption that an agent carries out his instructions. Since the receiving adult had agreed in advance to serve as the sender's agent in placing the *eruv*, his subsequent performance may be presumed. The *chazakah* applies to a competent adult agent but not to the *cheresh*, *shoteh*, or *katan* courier — which is precisely why visual confirmation is required at the delivery stage but not thereafter.

The distinction is therefore clean: one must observe the courier (incapable) delivering the *eruv* to the adult receiver, but one may rely on the *chazakah* that the adult receiver (a proper *shaliach*) then performs as agreed. The *sugya* of *chazakah shaliach oseh shlichuto* and its broader halachic implications will be taken up at the start of the next daf.
