# Bava Batra 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kotel Gina vs. Chatzer

### Wall Distance Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַבָּא לִסְמוֹךְ, לֹא יִסְמוֹךְ אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבָא: וְהָא ״מִי שֶׁהָיָה כּוֹתְלוֹ סָמוּךְ לְכוֹתֶל חֲבֵירוֹ״ קָתָנֵי!
>
> **Translation:** **One who comes to place** a wall **close** to his neighbor's wall **may place** that wall **close** to the neighbor's wall **only if he distances** his wall **four cubits from the existing wall.** Accordingly, the mishna is discussing one constructing a wall close to his neighbor's wall the first time. **Rava objects to this** explanation: **But** the mishna **teaches: One whose wall was near the wall of another,** which indicates that there had already been a wall there.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא, הָכִי קָתָנֵי: מִי שֶׁהָיָה כּוֹתְלוֹ סָמוּךְ לְכוֹתֶל חֲבֵירוֹ בְּרָחוֹק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, וְנָפַל, לֹא יִסְמוֹךְ לוֹ כּוֹתֶל אַחֵר – אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הִרְחִיק מִמֶּנּוּ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּדַוְושָׁא דְּהָכָא מְעַלֵּי לְהָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** that **this** is what the mishna **is teaching:** In a case of **one whose wall was near the wall of another at a distance of four cubits and** it **fell, he may not place another wall close** to his neighbor's wall **unless he distances** the wall **four cubits from it. What is the reason** that this distance must be observed? The reason is **that walking here benefits there,** i.e., the ground is strengthened by people walking on the land in the area between the walls.

The sugya before us concerns the permissible proximity of one wall to another on neighboring properties. The older *mefarshim* on the *mishnah* — found back on 22a — discuss building a perpendicular wall *l'chatchila*, where walls already exist and one is adding another. But the *gemara*, as we saw with Rabbi Eliezer, is operating under the assumption that this is actually a case where one of the walls has fallen and is now being rebuilt. In that context, when you rebuild, you must leave a certain distance — four *amot* — between your wall and your neighbor's.

### Rav: Gina vs. Chatzer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כּוֹתֶל גִּינָה, אֲבָל כּוֹתֶל חָצֵר – אִם בָּא לִסְמוֹךְ, סוֹמֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says: They taught** that one must leave a space of four cubits between his wall and that of his neighbor **only** if he builds it alongside **the wall of** his neighbor's **garden,** where people do not usually walk due to the seeds. **But** with regard to **the wall of a courtyard,** where people walk, **if he comes to place** his wall **close** by, **he may place** it **close** by.

The *gemara* opens on 22b, eight lines from the top, with *Amar Rav: lo shanu ela kotel gina, aval kotel chatzer, im ba lismoch, somech* — Rav says that the entire rule requiring four *amot* of distance applies only to the wall of a garden, a *gina*. But when it comes to the wall of a courtyard, a *chatzer*, if one wishes to build it somewhat closer, one may do so even within four *amot*.

### Ground Compaction Logic

What is the distinction between a *gina* and a *chatzer*? In a garden where things are grown, no one is walking on one side of the wall; the land there is cultivated rather than traversed. The four *amot* distance is therefore required so that people can walk in the space between the two walls. That walking tramples and compacts the ground, which solidifies the foundation of the wall. In a *chatzer*, however, people are constantly moving about within the courtyard itself, compacting the earth around the wall's base on the interior side. With that side already well-packed, we are less concerned about the absence of a passageway on the other side.

One might fairly ask why packing down only one side is sufficient — surely both sides of the wall need support. The question is a reasonable one, and it points directly to the objection that follows.

### Rabbi Oshia Opposes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא אוֹמֵר: אֶחָד כּוֹתֶל גִּינָּה וְאֶחָד כּוֹתֶל חָצֵר – אִם בָּא לִסְמוֹךְ, אֵינוֹ סוֹמֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Oshaya says:** With regard to **both the wall of a garden and the wall of a courtyard, if one comes to place** his wall **close** by, **he may not place** his wall **close** by.

*Rabbi Oshia omer: echad kotel gina ve-echad kotel chatzer, im ba lismoch, ein somech.* Rabbi Oshia disagrees with Rav's distinction entirely. In his view, there is no difference between the wall of a garden and the wall of a courtyard: in either case, one may not build the new wall closer than the required distance. This is precisely because, as the question above suggested, one side of the wall being well-packed is not sufficient. The other side — the side between the two walls, which belongs to neither party in the usual sense — also needs to be traversed so that the ground there is compacted as well. Therefore, the space must be maintained regardless of what type of enclosure the wall surrounds.

### Old vs. New City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בַּר חֲנִינָא: וְלָא פְּלִיגִי – הָא בְּעִיר יְשָׁנָה, הָא בְּעִיר חֲדָשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yosei bar Ḥanina said: And** the two *amora'im* **do not disagree,** as they are referring to different cases. **This** statement of Rav is referring **to an old city,** whose ground is well trodden and stable, and **that** statement of Rabbi Oshaya is referring **to a new city,** where even the wall of a courtyard requires walking on its adjacent ground to strengthen it.

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina resolves the apparent dispute: *velo pligi* — Rav and Rabbi Oshia do not actually disagree. They are simply addressing two different scenarios. *Ha be-ir yeshana, ha be-ir chadasha* — one is speaking of an old city, and the other of a new city. In an old, established city, the ground has already been compacted over many years of use, and so Rav permits building closer in a *chatzer*. In a newly built city, however, the ground has not yet been packed down, and Rabbi Oshia's stricter requirement applies. There is also a dimension here that resembles the difference between *de facto* urban realities and *l'chatchila* city planning: in old cities the layout has developed organically, with narrow alleyways and dense construction, whereas in a new city one has the opportunity to establish proper infrastructure from the outset.

## Window Privacy Rules

### Mishna: Windows

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הַחַלּוֹנוֹת, בֵּין מִלְּמַעְלָן בֵּין מִלְּמַטָּן בֵּין מִכְּנֶגְדָּן – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת. וְתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: מִלְּמַעְלָן – כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא יָצִיץ וְיִרְאֶה, מִלְּמַטָּן – שֶׁלֹּא יַעֲמוֹד וְיִרְאֶה, וּמִכְּנֶגְדָּן – שֶׁלֹּא יַאֲפִיל.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the claim that the *halakha* of the mishna is due to the need for space for people to walk on the ground between the walls, the Gemara asks: **We learned** in the mishna: And one who desires to build a wall opposite **the windows** of a neighbor's house must distance the wall **four cubits** from the windows, **whether above, below, or opposite. And it is taught** in a *baraita* **with regard to this** ruling: Concerning the requirement of a distance **above,** the wall must be high **enough so that one cannot peer** into the window **and see** into the window; concerning the requirement of a distance **below,** the wall must be low **so that he will not** be able to **stand** on top of it **and see** into the window; **and** concerning the requirement of a distance **opposite,** one must distance the wall from the windows **so that it will not darken** his neighbor's house by blocking the light that enters the house through the window.

The *mishnah* states: *hachalonot bein milmala bein milmata bein miknegdan arba amot* — with respect to windows, whether above, below, or directly opposite, the required distance is four *amot*. The scenario is that Reuven has a window in his wall, and Shimon now wishes to build a wall that will face that window. The *mishnah* establishes that Shimon's wall must be situated such that its top is at least four *amot* above or four *amot* below Reuven's window, and the wall must stand at least four *amot* away if it is directly across.

### Peeping Prevention

A *braita* elaborates on the reasons: *milmala k'dei shelo yatzitz v'yireh* — the wall must be four *amot* above the window so that someone standing atop the wall cannot lean over and peer in. *Milmata shelo yaamod v'yireh* — it must be four *amot* below so that someone standing on the lower wall cannot look up and see in through the window. In both of these cases, the four *amot* measurement reflects the approximate height of a human being — it is the distance beyond which a person cannot effectively peer in or peer up. This is a fascinating point: we usually think of four *amot* as the horizontal personal space around a person, corresponding roughly to one's arm span. Yet here four *amot* appears in a vertical context, measuring against human height — and it is worth noting that arm span and height are in fact approximately equal for most people, which explains how the same unit serves both contexts.

### Opposite Wall Distance

For the wall built directly *miknegdan* — opposite the window — the reason given by the *braita* is *shelo yaafil*, so as not to cause darkness. This is a different concern entirely: not that someone will peer in, but that a wall built too close will block the natural light that would otherwise enter through the window. If the wall is at least four *amot* away, sunlight can still reach the opening.

### Conflicting Reasons

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** טַעְמָא שֶׁלֹּא יַאֲפִיל, אֲבָל מִשּׁוּם דַּוְושָׁא לָא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּבָא מִן הַצַּד.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara analyzes this statement: The **reason** that distance is required opposite the window is so **that he will not darken** his neighbor's house by blocking the light that enters the house through the window, **but** it is **not due to** the fact that **walking** will strengthen the ground. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a wall **that is positioned to the side,** i.e., perpendicular to the wall with the window in it, and therefore it blocks the light from entering the house through the window but does not prevent walking along the length of the neighbor's wall.

The *gemara* now raises a difficulty. The *braita* has given a single reason for the four *amot* requirement in the case of the opposite wall — prevention of darkness. But earlier the *gemara* discussed another reason for the distance requirement: the concern about trampling and packing down the earth (*davsha*). These two rationales cannot both be primary without creating a conflict. If the only reason were *davsha*, there would be no need to invoke darkness; if the only reason were darkness, *davsha* would be superfluous.

### Tarpeling Context

The *gemara* resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan b'va min hatzad* — we are dealing here with a wall being built perpendicular to the wall containing the window, making an L-shape rather than a parallel configuration. In that case, the trampling concern is not directly at issue in the same way — people are not walking through a passage between two parallel walls. The darkness concern, however, is very much applicable: even a perpendicular wall, if built too close to the window, can obstruct the angle of incoming light.

The case involving *davsha*, by contrast, involves walls without windows facing one another, where the passage between them is what matters. The two scenarios — parallel walls facing each other, and a perpendicular wall approaching a windowed wall — each implicate different reasons, and thus different rationales can apply without contradiction.

### Window Width Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה? אָמַר יֵיבָא, חֲמוּהּ דְּאַשְׁיָין בַּר נִדְבָּךְ, מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: כִּמְלֹא רֹחַב חַלּוֹן. וַהֲלֹא מֵצִיץ! אָמַר רַב זְבִיד: בְּמַדִּיר אֶת כּוֹתְלוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And how far** must one distance his wall if it is perpendicular to the other wall? **Yeiva, the father-in-law of Ashyan bar Nidbakh, says in the name of Rav:** As much **as the full width of the window.** The Gemara asks: **But** why is this sufficient? **Can't he** still **peer into** the window if he is that close? **Rav Zevid says:** This is referring **to one who slopes his wall,** i.e., he fashions an incline on the upper surface of the wall so that he will not be able to stand on top of it and look through the window.

Once we are in the case of a perpendicular wall, the question becomes: how far must it stand from the window itself? The *gemara* cites an opinion in the name of Rav: *kemlo rochav chalon* — the perpendicular wall must be set back a distance equal to the full width of the window. So if the window is four feet wide, the perpendicular wall must be placed four feet from the edge of the window opening.

### Curved Wall Top

A difficulty immediately presents itself: if the wall is only one window-width away, someone who climbs atop it will still be at an angle from which they can see directly into the window. This seems to undermine the entire privacy rationale. *Amar Rav Zevid bemadrit kotlo* — Rav Zevid answers that the case is one where the top of the wall has been curved or sloped, creating an incline rather than a flat surface. A curved top makes it practically impossible to stand on the wall and look in. This is similar to walls fitted with barbed wire or shards of glass — security features designed to prevent climbing — and here it serves the additional halakhic purpose of eliminating the concern of *hezek re'iyah*, the damage of unwanted visual intrusion.

### One vs. Two Walls

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֲנַן תְּנַן: אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! לָא קַשְׁיָא: כָּאן מֵרוּחַ אַחַת, כָּאן מִשְׁתֵּי רוּחוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that one must keep the wall **four cubits** away from the window? The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult; here,** he builds the wall **on one side** of the window, whereas **there,** in the mishna, he builds walls **on two sides** of the window. In the latter case, if he builds the walls any closer he will block the light from entering the house through the window even if they are perpendicular.

The *gemara* then reconciles the two measurements — four *amot* and window-width — by distinguishing between scenarios. *Lo kashya: kan beruach achat, kan bishtei ruchot* — there is no contradiction. When there is only one wall being built perpendicular to the windowed wall, the window-width distance suffices. When two walls are being constructed — creating something like a passage that funnels directly toward the window — the full four *amot* distance is required, because the combined structural narrowing intensifies the risk of visual intrusion.

## Gutter & Ladder Rules

### Trampling vs. Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: וְאֶת הַכּוֹתֶל מִן הַמַּזְחֵילָה אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא זוֹקֵף אֶת הַסּוּלָּם. טַעְמָא מִשּׁוּם סוּלָּם, אֲבָל מִשּׁוּם דַּוְושָׁא לָא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּמַזְחֵילָה מְשׁוּפַּעַת, דְּאִי מִשּׁוּם דַּוְושָׁא הוּא – הָא קָא אָזֵיל וְאָתֵי תּוּתֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a further difficulty from the mishna below with regard to the claim that the requisite gap between the walls is for the purpose of walking, as the mishna teaches: **And** one must distance his **wall four cubits from a roof gutter so that** his neighbor **can lean a ladder** in the empty space to clean and repair the gutter. The Gemara analyzes this statement: The **reason** this distance is required is **due to** the fact that he will be able to lean **a ladder, but** it is **not due to** the fact that **walking** will strengthen the ground. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with a sloping roof gutter,** which protrudes beyond the boundary of the wall. **As** in this case, **if** the reason for distancing the wall **is due to walking, one can walk back and forth beneath it.** Nevertheless, a distance of four cubits from the roof gutter is required so that repairs can be performed.

The *gemara* raises the same analytical question about the next *mishnah* as it did about the window rules: when we require a certain distance between a wall and a neighboring gutter, is the reason the *davsha* concern — that people need space to walk and compact the ground — or is it because of the need to be able to set up a ladder to perform maintenance on the gutter?

### Overhang Solution

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the case: *b'mazchila meshupaat* — we are dealing with a gutter that extends on an overhang. Because the gutter protrudes overhead, a person can walk beneath it without any obstruction. Therefore, the spacing of the new wall does not affect the trampling of the ground — people can still pass underneath. The reason the four *amot* distance is still required is entirely for the sake of ladder access: the owner of the gutter needs to be able to erect a ladder to repair and maintain it, and the wall being built nearby must leave enough room for that.

### Dovecote & Ladder

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַסּוּלָּם מִן הַשּׁוֹבָךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תִּקְפּוֹץ הַנְּמִיָּיה. וְאֶת הַכּוֹתֶל מִן הַמַּזְחֵילָה אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, כְּדֵי שֶׁיְּהֵא זוֹקֵף אֶת הַסּוּלָּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One must distance** his **ladder four cubits from** a neighbor's **dovecote so that a mongoose will not** be able to **jump** from the ladder to the dovecote and devour the birds. **And** one must distance his **wall four cubits from a roof gutter, so that** the neighbor **can lean a ladder** in the empty space to clean and repair the gutter.

The *mishnah* states: *marchikin et hasulam min hashovach arba amot kedei shelo tikpotz hanemiya* — one must keep a ladder at least four *amot* away from a dovecote, so that the *nemiya* — a weasel or mongoose — should not be able to leap from the ladder into the dovecote and attack the doves. If the ladder is placed directly across from the dovecote, the weasel can climb the ladder and jump across. At a distance of four *amot*, that leap is beyond its range.

### Gutter Distance

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-et hakotel min hamazchila arba amot kedei sheyehei zokef et hasulam* — and one must keep a wall at least four *amot* from a neighboring gutter, so that the gutter's owner has sufficient space to erect a ladder for maintenance. This is, as the *gemara* has now clarified, the operative rationale in the case of an overhanging gutter — not ground compaction, but ladder access.

## Grama & Bloodletters

### Rabbi Yosi Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי? דְּאִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא אָמַר: זֶה חוֹפֵר בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ, וְזֶה נוֹטֵעַ בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **Let us say that the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, as, if** it were in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei, doesn't** he **say** with regard to planting a tree next to a neighbor's cistern: **This one digs within his** land, **and that one plants within his** land, and neither individual need consider what is happening in the property of the other?

The *gemara* raises the question of whether our *mishnah* — with its various requirements to keep ladders and walls at sensitive distances — is compatible with the position of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi held the principle *zeh chofer betoch shelo ve-zeh noteiah betoch shelo*: each person does as he wishes on his own property, and the impact on his neighbor is not his legal concern. Rabbi Yosi's view is essentially a strong property-rights position: what is mine is mine, what is yours is yours, and I am not obligated to limit my activities on my land simply because they may affect yours.

### Direct vs. Indirect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבִּי יוֹסֵי, הָא אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: כִּי הֲוֵינַן בֵּי רַב כָּהֲנָא, הֲוָה אָמַר: מוֹדֵי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי בְּגִירֵי דִידֵיהּ; הָכָא נָמֵי, זִמְנִין דְּבַהֲדֵי דְּמַנַּח לֵיהּ – יָתְבָא בְּחוֹר וְקָפְצָה. וְהָא גְּרָמָא הוּא! אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר מַתְנָה, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: גְּרָמָא בְּנִיזָּקִין אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **You** may **even say** that the mishna follows the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei,** as **didn't Rav Ashi say: When we were** studying **in the study hall of Rav Kahana, he would say** to us that **Rabbi Yosei concedes with regard to his arrows,** i.e., he concedes that one must distance himself if his actions will cause damage to his neighbor. **Here too, sometimes when he places** the ladder, the mongoose might be **sitting in a hole and** will immediately **jump** up and climb the ladder to the dovecote. The Gemara challenges: **But that is indirect** damage, as he is not the immediate cause. **Rav Tovi bar Mattana said: That is to say** that it **is prohibited** to cause even **indirect damage.**

If the *mishnah* is like Rabbi Yosi, why does it impose restrictions on ladder placement and wall distance? The *gemara* answers by citing a statement of Rav Ashi: *ki havina bei Rav Kahana hava amar: modeh Rabbi Yosi begirei dideh* — Rabbi Yosi concedes in the case of one who shoots arrows directly at another's property. Even Rabbi Yosi agrees that direct aggression against a neighbor's domain is prohibited. And in the case of the ladder and the dovecote, the danger is not truly indirect: weasels live in holes and wait for precisely such a ladder to be placed nearby before climbing up and attacking. Placing the ladder directly across from the dovecote is therefore sufficiently direct — close enough to the equivalent of "shooting arrows" — that even Rabbi Yosi would prohibit it.

### Grama is Asur

But then the *gemara* asks: is this not still a case of *grama* — indirect causation? The person erecting the ladder is not himself attacking the doves; it is the weasel that does so. *Amar Rav Tuvi bar Matna: zot omeret grama b'nezikin asur* — this *mishnah* comes to teach us that *grama b'nezikin* is prohibited. Causing damage indirectly is still halakhically forbidden.

### Rav Yosef's Trees

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָנְהוּ תָּאלֵי, דַּהֲווֹ אָתוּ אוּמָּנֵי וְיָתְבִי תּוּתַיְיהוּ, וְאָתוּ עוֹרְבֵי אָכְלִי דְּמָא, וְסָלְקִי אַבֵּי תָאלֵי וּמַפְסְדִי תַּמְרֵי. אֲמַר לְהוּ רַב יוֹסֵף: אַפִּיקוּ לִי קוּרְקוּר מֵהָכָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav Yosef had certain small palm trees [*talei*],** and **bloodletters** would **come and sit beneath them** and perform their work there, **and crows** would **come, eat the blood, and fly up to the palm trees and damage the dates. Rav Yosef said to** the bloodletters: **Remove these crowing** birds **from here,** i.e., leave in order to avoid further damage.

This principle is illustrated by a vivid incident. Rav Yosef had date trees growing on his property. Bloodletters — practitioners of medical bloodletting — had taken to sitting beneath these trees to conduct their work. The blood they left on the ground attracted ravens, which then flew up into the trees and damaged the dates. Rav Yosef told the bloodletters to take their practice elsewhere.

### Abaye Questions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: וְהָא גְּרָמָא הוּא! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָכִי אָמַר רַב טוֹבִי בַּר מַתְנָה, זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת: גְּרָמָא בְּנִיזָּקִין אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: But it is an indirect** action, as the bloodletters themselves are not damaging the dates. Rav Yosef **said to him** that **Rav Tovi bar Mattana said** as follows: **That is to say** that it **is prohibited** to cause even **indirect damage.**

Abaye challenged Rav Yosef: *v'ha grama hu* — is this not merely *grama*? The causal chain here is long: the bloodletters sit and practice their trade, the blood attracts ravens, the ravens fly into the trees, and only then are the dates damaged. The bloodletters are not directly harming the trees at all.

### No Chazaka for Nezikin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אַחְזֵיק [לְהוּ]! הָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֵין חֲזָקָה לִנְזָקִין. וְלָאו אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ – רַב מָרִי אָמַר: בְּקוּטְרָא, וְרַב זְבִיד אָמַר: בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא?!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye said to Rav Yosef: **But they have established an acquired privilege** to use that particular spot for their work. Rav Yosef replied: **Doesn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: There is no acquired privilege** of use **in** cases of **damage,** i.e., an established situation may not be allowed to continue in the event that damage results. Abaye inquired further: **But wasn't it stated with regard to that** statement of Rav Naḥman that **Rav Mari said** it is referring specifically **to smoke, and Rav Zevid said** it is referring **to a bathroom?** In other words, this principle was stated specifically in the context of damage caused by these substances.

The response invokes Rav Tuvi bar Matna's ruling: *grama b'nezikin asur* — indirect damage is still prohibited. A further question then arises: the bloodletters have been coming to this spot for years. Do they not have a *chazaka* — an established usage — that grants them the right to continue? *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha: ein chazaka l'nezikin* — no *chazaka* applies when one is causing *nezikin*. The mere passage of time does not legitimize an activity that damages a neighbor.

### Rav Mari Qualification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָנֵי, לְדִידִי – דַּאֲנִינָא דַּעְתַּאי, כִּי קוּטְרָא וּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא דָּמוּ לִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: For me, as I am sensitive, these are like smoke and a bathroom to me,** which is why I have the right to demand that the bloodletters leave.

However, *Rav Mari amar b'kutra, v'Rav Zevid amar b'veit hachisei* — Rav Mari and Rav Zevid each qualify the principle of *ein chazaka l'nezikin*, limiting it to especially severe nuisances: cases involving heavy smoke (*kutra*) or a toilet (*beit hakisei*) placed near one's property. These are situations so offensive that no amount of prior usage can normalize them for the affected neighbor.

Rav Yosef accordingly invokes this framework on his own behalf: *l'didi d'anina da'ati, k'kutra u'veit hachisei damyan li* — for him, with his particular sensitivity, the ravens and the blood are subjectively equivalent to smoke or a toilet placed next door. What is striking here is that the *gemara* appears to honor this subjective claim, treating Rav Yosef's personal threshold of sensibility as halakhically operative. The underlying question — whether the *davsha* causing the dates to spoil is the primary damage, or whether it is Rav Yosef's subjective revulsion at the bloodletting scene — animates the discussion without being fully resolved. Either way, the *gemara* endorses Rav Yosef's demand that the bloodletters leave, and *ein chazaka l'nezikin* remains limited in its application to situations of genuinely severe and ongoing harm.

## Dovecote Distances (II)

### 50 Amot Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מַרְחִיקִין אֶת הַשּׁוֹבָךְ מִן הָעִיר חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה. וְלֹא יַעֲשֶׂה אָדָם שׁוֹבָךְ בְּתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן יֵשׁ לוֹ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה לְכׇל רוּחַ. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: בֵּית אַרְבַּעַת כּוֹרִין – מְלֹא שֶׁגֶר הַיּוֹנֶה. וְאִם לְקָחוֹ – אֲפִילּוּ בֵּית רוֹבַע, הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One must distance a dovecote fifty cubits from the city** to prevent doves from eating seeds in the town. **And a person should not establish a dovecote within his own** property **unless he has fifty cubits in each direction** between the dovecote and the edge of his property. **Rabbi Yehuda says** that one must have surrounding the dovecote the **area required for sowing four *kor*** of seed on each side, which generally extends **as far as a dove flies** in a single flight. **And if one bought** the dovecote with the land, **he has the acquired privilege** of its use **even if** it has surrounding it only the **area required for sowing a quarter-*kav* of seed [*beit rova*]** around it, and he need not remove it from there.

The *mishnah* states: *merachikin et hashovach min ha-ir chamishim ama* — one must keep a dovecote at least fifty *amot* from the city. The doves will inevitably forage in the nearby area, and placing the dovecote too close to inhabited land means they will damage the crops or garden plots of city residents. The fifty *amot* buffer is designed to prevent this.

### Circumference Rules

Furthermore, *velo yaaseh adam shovach betoch shelo ela im ken yesh lo chamishim ama lachol ruach* — a person may not build a dovecote in his own property unless he has fifty *amot* available in every direction around it. The doves' natural ranging area is this fifty *amot* radius, and so the owner must control enough land that the doves' foraging does not encroach upon others' fields.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a different measurement: *beit arba korin melo shegar hayona* — a space of four *kor*, which works out to approximately 273 *amot*, described as the full natural flight distance a dove can cover in one sustained effort. This is a considerably larger area than the fifty *amot* of the first opinion.

### Chazaka Permission

Both opinions agree on one dispensation: *ve-im lekacha, afilu beit rova, harei hu b'chezkaso* — if one purchased a property that already had a dovecote in place, even if that dovecote is smaller than required or situated closer to the borders than the rules would allow, it is considered *b'chezkato*, grandfathered in. The assumption is that the previous owner had already arranged some form of understanding with the surrounding neighbors, and the purchaser inherits that established status.

## Dove Trapping Rules

### 30 Ris Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה – וְתוּ לָא? וּרְמִינְהִי: אֵין פּוֹרְסִין נִשְׁבִּין לְיוֹנִים, אֶלָּא אִם כֵּן הָיָה רָחוֹק מִן הַיִּשּׁוּב שְׁלֹשִׁים רִיס!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: Must one distance a dovecote only **fifty cubits** from the city **and no more?** Is that as far as one can expect a dove to fly? **And** the Gemara **raises a contradiction** from a mishna (*Bava Kamma* 79b): One **may spread out traps [*neshavin*] for doves only if** this was performed **at a distance of** at least **thirty *ris*,** or four *mil*, which is eight thousand cubits, **from** any **settled area,** to avoid catching birds that belong to another. Apparently, doves fly a distance of thirty *ris*, whereas the mishna here states fifty cubits.

The *gemara* challenges the fifty *amot* rule by citing a *braita*: *ein porsin nishbin leyona ela im ken haya rachok min hayishuv sheloshim ris* — one may not set out traps for doves unless one is at least thirty *ris* away from an inhabited settlement. If fifty *amot* were the outer limit of a dove's range — beyond which the bird belongs to the finder — why would trapping require staying thirty *ris* away? This seems to imply that doves can travel far greater distances than fifty *amot*.

### Dove Flight Distance

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: מֵישָׁט שָׁיְיטִי טוּבָא, וּכְרֵסַיְיהוּ בַּחֲמִשִּׁים אַמְּתָא מַלְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** Doves do **fly great distances,** which is why one must avoid catching others' birds by keeping traps thirty *ris* away from settled areas. **But** as they eat along their way, **their stomachs are filled** after a distance of **fifty cubits,** at which point they will do no more damage to seeds.

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction: *mishiti tuva ve-chasheu be-chamishim amata milia* — a dove does eat considerably while traveling, and by the time it has traveled fifty *amot* from the dovecote, it has slowed down substantially from all the foraging along the way. The fifty *amot* limit thus represents the extent to which we consider the bird to be reasonably connected to a nearby dovecote, not the absolute outer limit of how far a dove can physically travel.

### 100 Mil Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵישָׁט – שְׁלֹשִׁים רִיס וְתוּ לָא? וְהָתַנְיָא: וּבַיִּשּׁוּב – אֲפִילּוּ מֵאָה מִיל לֹא יִפְרוֹס! רַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: בְּיִשּׁוּב כְּרָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And do they fly** only **thirty *ris* and no more? But isn't it taught** in a *baraita*: **And in a settled area,** one **may not spread out** a trap **even** if the area under his control extends as far as **one hundred *mil*** in each direction? **Rav Yosef says:** That *baraita* is referring **to a settled area of vineyards,** i.e., a contiguous region of vineyards and gardens. In that case the doves pass from place to place even over a great distance.

Yet another *braita* states that within a settled area, one may not set traps even at a distance of a hundred *mil*. This seems far more restrictive than either the fifty *amot* or the thirty *ris* figure.

### Vineyard Context

Rav Yosef resolves this: *b'shuv kramim* — that ruling pertains to a settlement of vineyards. Doves can fly considerably greater distances when vineyards are present, perhaps because the terrain offers different resting and feeding patterns, causing them to continue flying farther than they otherwise would.

### Dovecote Area

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בְּיִשּׁוּב שׁוֹבָכִין. וְתִיפּוֹק לֵיהּ מִשּׁוּם שׁוֹבָכִין גּוּפַיְיהוּ! אִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דִּידֵיהּ, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּגוֹי, וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: דְּהֶפְקֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** The *baraita* is referring **to a settled area of dovecotes,** i.e., where many dovecotes are distributed. The Gemara asks: **And** according to Rava, **let** the *tanna* **derive** that one may not establish a new dovecote there **due to** the other **dovecotes themselves,** as he will trap doves belonging to others. The Gemara answers: **If you wish, say** that this is referring to **his own** dovecotes. **And if you wish, say** it is referring to the dovecotes of **a gentile,** whose property one is not obligated to protect from harm. **And if you wish, say** it is referring **to ownerless** dovecotes.

Rava adds a further case: *b'shuv shovchin* — a settlement densely populated with dovecotes, where the birds are constantly moving between one dovecote and another. In such an area, a dove may hop from cote to cote across great distances, so one must go much farther afield before setting traps.

### Ownership Determination

The *gemara* then asks why the ruling mentions the concern about *shovchin* at all — could one not simply say that the traps are fine because the doves encountered would be from the trapper's own dovecotes? The *gemara* entertains three possibilities: the additional dovecotes belong to the trapper himself, in which case trapping is unproblematic; or they belong to non-Jews, in which case there is no concern for their ownership; or they are ownerless (*hefker*). The different resolutions determine how far from a settlement one must be before setting traps, and which configuration triggers the full hundred-*mil* restriction.

## Dovecote Distances (II)

### Purchaser & Heir Claims

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב זְבִיד: זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת, טוֹעֲנִין לְלוֹקֵחַ וְטוֹעֲנִין לְיוֹרֵשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said, and some say** it was **Rav Zevid: That is to say** that a court **issues a claim** on behalf **of a buyer, and issues a claim** on behalf **of an heir.** This is referring to the *halakha* of taking possession. If one has been physically in possession of an item for a period of time, generally three years, this serves as proof that he is in fact the legal owner. This possession must be accompanied by a claim of how one acquired the item; he cannot simply state that no one protested his possessing the item for three years. Rav Pappa is saying that the court will lodge a claim on behalf of a buyer or heir that they acquired the item from someone who was the owner, just as here the court assumes that the previous owner of the dovecote came to an agreement with his neighbors that he may use it.

*Amar Rav Papa v'iseyma Rav Zevid: zot omeret, to'anin lalo'ke'ach ve-to'anin layoresh* — Rav Papa, and some say Rav Zevid, infer from the *mishnah*'s statement that a purchaser who acquires a smaller or more closely situated dovecote is nonetheless *b'chezkato*, that the *beit din* is prepared to advance legal claims on behalf of both a purchaser and an heir. That is, if a third party challenges the legitimacy of the dovecote's placement, the *beit din* does not require the purchaser or heir to produce positive proof of prior authorization; the *chazaka* of the previous owner is treated as already having established the right, and the *beit din* builds that presumption into the current possessor's position.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹרֵשׁ – תְּנֵינָא: הַבָּא מִשּׁוּם יְרוּשָּׁה – אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ טַעֲנָה! לוֹקֵחַ אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ. לוֹקֵחַ נָמֵי תְּנֵינָא: לָקַח חָצֵר וּבָהּ זִיזִין וּגְזוּזְטְרָאוֹת – הֲרֵי זֶה בְּחֶזְקָתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Why is it necessary for Rav Pappa to state this *halakha*? **We** already **learn** this with regard to **an heir** (41a): In the case of land **that comes as an inheritance, one is not required to make a claim** as to how the land came into his benefactor's possession when one's ownership of the land is challenged. The Gemara answers: **It was necessary for him** to state this *halakha* with regard to **a buyer.** The Gemara asks: With regard to **a buyer as well, we learn** this in a mishna (60a): If one **bought a courtyard in which there are projections and balconies [*ugzuztraot*]** extending into the public domain, this courtyard **retains its presumptive status,** i.e., the owner has the acquired privilege of their use, and the court does not demand their removal.

The *gemara* immediately questions whether we need the *mishnah* here to tell us about an heir — do we not already have an explicit *mishnah* elsewhere establishing that one who comes on the basis of inheritance does not need to bring a formal claim? *Haba mishum yerusha eino tzarich ta'ana*. The novelty must therefore lie in the case of the purchaser, *loke'ach*.

But the *gemara* pushes further: do we not also have an explicit *mishnah* about a purchaser? *Lakach chatzer u-va zizin u-guztra'os harei zeh b'chezkato* — one who purchases a courtyard that has protrusions and balconies already extending into the public domain acquires them with their established status. If we already have that precedent, why do we need to derive the rule again from the dovecote case?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** צְרִיכָא; דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָתָם גַּבֵּי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – דְּאֵימוֹר כּוֹנֵס לְתוֹךְ שֶׁלּוֹ הוּא, אִי נָמֵי אַחוֹלֵי אַחוּל בְּנֵי רְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים גַּבֵּיהּ; אֲבָל הָכָא – לָא;
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: It was **necessary** for the *tanna* of the mishna to state this *halakha* in both cases, **as, if he had taught us this** only **there,** in that mishna, one might have said that it applies specifically **with regard to** a protrusion or a balcony that extends into **the public domain, as** one can **say** that perhaps **it is** a case where the seller had **drawn back into his own** land before adding the projections and balconies, and they in fact do not extend into the public thoroughfare. **Alternatively,** perhaps **the public waived** their right **to him** and allowed him to place them over the common area, as otherwise they would have protested. **But here,** where he causes damage to private individuals, one might have thought that the buyer does **not** have a privilege of use, and therefore the mishna teaches us otherwise.

The *gemara* answers that each case is necessary — *tzricha*. The case of protrusions over a *reshut harabbim* might have been treated leniently on the theory that the public effectively granted permission, or that the owner appeased the public, or that the public simply forgave the encroachment. One could not necessarily extend that to a private property dispute. Conversely, a case involving only private parties might be explained away by the assumption that the dovecote owner personally negotiated with each affected neighbor. But in the *reshut harabbim* case, there is no identifiable party to negotiate with — *man payis?* Who does one even appease? Each scenario thus teaches something the other cannot, and both rulings are required.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָכָא, דְּכֵיוָן דְּיָחִיד הוּא – אֵימָא: פַּיּוֹסֵי פַּיְּיסֵיהּ, אִי נָמֵי אַחוֹלֵי אַחֵיל גַּבֵּיהּ; אֲבָל רַבִּים, מַאן פַּיֵּיס וּמַאן שָׁבֵיק – אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And if he had taught this** only in the mishna **here,** one might **say** that **since** the party potentially suffering damage **is an individual,** the owner of the dovecote **appeased** his neighbor by paying him to permit him to construct it. **Alternatively,** the neighbor might have **waived** his right **to him. But** in a case where damage is caused to **the public,** one might argue: **Whom did he appease, and who yielded** to him? Consequently, one might **say** that the purchaser does **not** retain the privilege of use. Therefore, it is **necessary** for the *tanna* to state the *halakha* in this case as well.

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge: the *mishnah*'s ruling that a purchased dovecote is *b'chezkato* seems to stand in tension with *Rav Nachman's* principle in the name of Rabba bar Avuha — *ein chazaka l'nezikin*. If a dovecote causes damage to neighbors' crops, how can its *chazaka* be honored?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲרֵי הוּא בְּחֶזְקָתוֹ. וְהָא אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: אֵין חֲזָקָה לִנְזָקִין! רַב מָרִי אָמַר: בְּקוּטְרָא, רַב זְבִיד אָמַר: בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one bought the dovecote with the land, **he has the acquired privilege** of use. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rav Naḥman say** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: There is no acquired privilege** of use **for** cases of **damage?** Why should he retain his acquired privilege of use when his doves cause damage? **Rav Mari said:** Rav Naḥman's statement is referring specifically **to smoke,** which causes serious damage, and that is why it overrides an acquired privilege. **Rav Zevid said:** It is referring **to a bathroom,** whose odor is particularly strong.

The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the qualification of Rav Mari and Rav Zevid: *ein chazaka l'nezikin* applies specifically to severe nuisances such as smoke or a toilet. The harm caused by a dovecote — doves eating from nearby fields — is not of that extreme category, and so the *chazaka* for the dovecote is maintained. Not all sources of *nezek* are treated equally: some are severe enough to override any *chazaka*, while others are tolerated once an established usage has been recognized.

## Found Dove Rules

### Nippul Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ נִיפּוּל הַנִּמְצָא בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה – הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁל בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹבָךְ. חוּץ מֵחֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה – הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁל מוֹצְאוֹ. נִמְצָא בֵּין שְׁנֵי שׁוֹבָכוֹת; קָרוֹב לָזֶה – שֶׁלּוֹ. קָרוֹב לָזֶה – שֶׁלּוֹ. מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה – שְׁנֵיהֶם יַחְלוֹקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to a dove **chick [*nippul*] that was found within fifty cubits** of a dovecote, **it belongs to the owner of the dovecote.** If it was found **beyond fifty cubits** from a dovecote, **it belongs to its finder.** In a case where it **was found between two dovecotes,** if it was **close to this one,** it belongs **to** the owner of this dovecote; if it was **close to that one,** it belongs **to** the owner of that dovecote. If it was **half and half,** i.e., equidistant from the two dovecotes, **the two** owners **divide** the value of the chick.

The *mishnah* turns to the case of a *nippul* — a baby dove that cannot yet fly, a *gozal*, found on the ground. *Nippul hanimtza betoch chamishim ama, harei hu shel ba'al hashovach* — a chick found within fifty *amot* of a dovecote belongs to the owner of that dovecote. Since the chick cannot fly, it could only have gotten there by waddling from the nearby cote.

### Beyond 50 Amot

*Chutz michamishim ama, harei hu shel motza'o* — if found beyond fifty *amot*, the chick belongs to the finder. The assumption is that it could not have walked that far from the dovecote, and so its origin is unknown; the finder acquires it.

This *gemara* raises a broader question that connects to the rules of lost objects in *Bava Metzia*: ordinarily one would expect a found animal to require *simanim* before being claimed. Here, however, proximity to a dovecote serves as an automatic assignment of ownership, without requiring formal identification. One might argue that within fifty *amot* of a known dovecote, the chick is not truly a lost object at all — it simply has not yet flown far enough to sever its connection to the cote.

### Two Dovecotes

*Nimtza bein shtei shovcha'ot, karov lazeh shelo, karov lazeh shelo* — if the chick is found between two dovecotes, each within fifty *amot*, ownership is determined by proximity: whichever dovecote is closer, the chick belongs to its owner. If the chick is found exactly equidistant — *mechtzah al mechtzah* — the two owners split it: *shneihem yachloku*.

## Rov vs. Karov

### Rov Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: רוֹב וְקָרוֹב – הוֹלְכִין אַחַר הָרוֹב; וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּרוּבָּא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא וְקוּרְבָא דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא – אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, רוּבָּא עֲדִיף.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** When resolving an uncertainty with regard to the halakhic status of an item, e.g., a found item, if the status of the **majority** of like items indicates that it has one status **but** the item in question is **proximate** to a source that indicates otherwise, one **follows the majority. And even though** the *halakha* of **majority** applies **by Torah law and** the *halakha* **of proximity** also applies **by Torah law, even so the majority is preferable.**

*Amar Rabbi Chanina: rov vekarov holchin achar harov* — Rabbi Chanina raises a pointed challenge. The entire operating principle of the *mishnah* appears to be *karov*, proximity: we assign the chick to the nearest dovecote. But do we not have the competing principle of *rov* — majority? If one dovecote has five thousand doves and another has five, it is statistically far more likely that any given chick came from the larger one. Why does proximity override numerical probability?

### Rov vs. Karov Sources

The *gemara* identifies two distinct *d'Oraita* principles in tension here. On one side is *acharei rabim lehatot* — the Torah's instruction to follow the majority. On the other side is the model of *egla arufa* — the decapitated heifer ritual, where the Torah requires measuring from the discovered corpse to determine which city is closest and must bring the *egla arufa*, without regard for the size of the nearby cities. That procedure clearly operates on *karov*, proximity. *Af al gav d'rov d'Oraita ve-karov d'Oraita afilu hachi rov adif* — Rabbi Chanina maintains that even though both are *d'Oraita* principles, *rov* is the stronger of the two and should prevail.

### Egla Arufa Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵתִיב רַבִּי זֵירָא: ״וְהָיָה הָעִיר הַקְּרֹבָה אֶל הֶחָלָל״ – וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרִיתִי דִּנְפִישָׁא מִינַּהּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Zeira raises an objection** from the Torah's statement with regard to a murder victim where the identity of the murderer is unknown. In a case of this kind, the court measures the distances between the corpse and the nearby towns, in order to determine which town is closest and must consequently perform the rite of the heifer whose neck is broken. The verse states: **"And it shall be, that the city that is nearest to the slain man,** the Elders of that city shall take a heifer of the herd…and shall break the heifer's neck" (Deuteronomy 21:3–4). **And** this town is chosen **even though there** might **be another** town **that is larger** in population **than it.** According to Rabbi Ḥanina, in a case of this kind one should follow the majority.

Rabbi Zeira challenges this by citing the *egla arufa* itself: *vehaya ha-ir hakerova el hechalal* — the verse specifies the city closest to the slain person. *Af al gav de'ika achrita denefisha minah* — and this applies even if there are other, larger cities in the vicinity. This seems to be a direct proof that *karov* can and does override *rov* — at least in that context.

### Mountain Area Limit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּדְלֵיכָּא. וְלֵיזִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דְעָלְמָא! בְּיוֹשֶׁבֶת בֵּין הֶהָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: This verse is referring to a situation **where there is no** other town that is larger than that one. The Gemara asks: **And** still, if one follows the majority, why should the court follow the closest city? **Let us follow the majority of the world,** as most people are found elsewhere. The Gemara answers: This is referring **to** a case where the city **sits** in isolation **between mountains,** and therefore it is unlikely that the murderer arrived from elsewhere.

The *gemara* deflects the proof: the *egla arufa* case is specifically *beyoshevet bein heharim*, situated in isolated mountain terrain. In such a setting, there may not be any significantly larger city available; the nearby towns may all be of comparable size, making *rov* inapplicable. In that narrow context, *karov* governs by default. But in a normal setting where larger cities exist, one might argue that *rov* should prevail, and the *egla arufa* verse would not override it.

This is a striking limitation. Taken at face value, it would seem to confine the *egla arufa*'s *karov* principle to geographically isolated communities alone, which seems difficult to square with the *Torah*'s broader presentation of the *egla arufa* ritual. The *gemara* here appears to be making a theoretical point rather than a comprehensive ruling on the *egla arufa* procedure, but the qualification is notable.

## Chick Movement Rules

### Rav Ukva on Waddling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: נִיפּוּל הַנִּמְצָא בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה – הֲרֵי הוּא שֶׁל בַּעַל הַשּׁוֹבָךְ; וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּאִיכָּא אַחֲרִינָא דִּנְפִישׁ מִינֵּיהּ! בִּדְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to discuss the issue of majority as opposed to proximity. **We learned** in the mishna: With regard to a dove **chick that was found within fifty cubits** of a dovecote, **it belongs to the owner of the dovecote. And** as the mishna does not make a distinction between different cases, it indicates that this is the *halakha* **even though there is another** dovecote **that is larger than** the proximate one in terms of number of birds. This shows that closeness, not majority, is the determining factor. The Gemara answers: This is referring to a case **where there is no** other dovecote in the area.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בִּמְדַדֶּה, דְּאָמַר רַב עוּקְבָא בַּר חָמָא: כׇּל הַמְדַדֶּה – אֵין מְדַדֶּה יוֹתֵר מֵחֲמִשִּׁים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing **with** a chick that **hops** from place to place but does not yet fly. **As Rav Ukva bar Ḥama says:** With regard to **any** creature **that hops,** it **does not hop more than fifty** cubits. Consequently, any bird found within fifty cubits of a dovecote is assumed to have come from there. If it is farther away than that, it likely came from elsewhere or was dropped by travelers.

The *gemara* explains the empirical basis for the fifty *amot* limit by citing *Rav Ukva bar Chama: kol hamedadeh ein medadeh yoter mechamishim amah* — any chick that is waddling (*medade*) cannot waddle more than fifty *amot*. This is the physical reality that underlies the *mishnah*'s rule: waddling chicks are simply incapable of traveling farther than fifty *amot* from the nest.

### Rabbi Yirmiya Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: רַגְלוֹ אַחַת בְּתוֹךְ חֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה, וְרַגְלוֹ אַחַת חוּץ מֵחֲמִשִּׁים אַמָּה – מַהוּ? וְעַל דָּא אַפְּקוּהוּ לְרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה מִבֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yirmeya raises a dilemma:** If **one leg** of the chick was **within fifty cubits** of the dovecote, **and one leg** was **beyond fifty cubits, what is** the *halakha*? The Gemara comments: **And it was for** his question about **this** far-fetched scenario **that they removed Rabbi Yirmeya from the study hall,** as he was apparently wasting the Sages' time.

Into this settled framework steps Rabbi Yirmiya with his celebrated question: *raglo achat betoch chamishim amah ve-raglo achat chutz mechamishim amah mahu* — what if the chick has one foot inside the fifty *amot* boundary and one foot outside it? To whom does such a chick belong?

### Rejection

*Afkuh l'Rabbi Yirmiya mibei midrashah* — Rabbi Yirmiya was expelled from the *beit midrash*. The question was deemed so far outside the bounds of serious inquiry as to warrant removal. Yet the condemnation requires explanation, since the *mishnah* itself just addressed the case of a chick found exactly equidistant between two dovecotes — which implies precisely the kind of borderline scenario Rabbi Yirmiya is raising.

*Tosafot* offers the key insight: it was not the form of the question that offended, but its underlying implication. Rav Ukva bar Chama had stated as a matter of established fact that chicks cannot waddle more than fifty *amot*. Rabbi Yirmiya's question presupposes that a chick might take a half-step past the fifty *amot* line — thereby implicitly challenging the factual ruling of the *Chachamim*. It is this subtle questioning of the received scientific determination, rather than any logical absurdity in the question itself, that led to his expulsion. *Rashi* adds simply that Rabbi Yirmiya was being unnecessarily burdensome — *shehayah matriach aleihem* — and the *beit midrash* had already moved on to larger conceptual questions about human mortality, city proximity, and the principles of *rov* and *karov*. Pedantic hypotheticals about the precise placement of a chick's foot were not welcome.

## Final Rov Resolution

### Equal Dovecotes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: נִמְצָא בֵּין שְׁנֵי שׁוֹבָכוֹת; קָרוֹב לָזֶה – שֶׁלּוֹ, וְקָרוֹב לָזֶה – שֶׁלּוֹ; וְאַף עַל גַּב דְּחַד מִינַּיְיהוּ נְפִישׁ מֵחַבְרֵיהּ! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – שֶׁשְּׁנֵיהֶן שָׁוִין. וְלֵיזִיל בָּתַר רוּבָּא דְעָלְמָא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן –
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear** the mishna: In a case where it **was found between two dovecotes,** if it was **close to this one,** it belongs **to** the owner of this dovecote; if it was **close to that one,** it belongs **to** the owner of that dovecote. The Gemara comments: **And** this is the *halakha* **even though one of them is greater** in number of birds **than the other one.** Apparently, one rules based on proximity, not majority. The Gemara explains: **With what are we dealing here?** We are dealing with a situation **where the two** dovecotes **are equal** in size. The Gemara asks: **But** even so, why should one follow the closer dovecote? **Let us follow the majority of the world,** as there are many other dovecotes besides these, and the number of doves they contain is greater. The Gemara responds: **With what are we dealing here?**

*Nimtza bein shenei shovachot, karov lazeh shelo, karov lazeh shelo, ve-af al gav de-chad mineihu nafish mechavreih* — the chick found between two dovecotes goes to the closer one, even if the other is larger. But if they are equal in size — *sheshneihem shavin* — proximity governs, and the chick belongs to the owner of the nearer dovecote. This resolves Rabbi Chanina's challenge: the *mishnah* is simply not addressing a case where the dovecotes differ in size at all, and so no conflict with *rov* arises.

### Vineyard Exception

But then the *gemara* asks: if the dovecotes are of equal size, why not invoke *rov d'alma* — the majority of all doves in the world? Any given dove is statistically more likely to have originated from some large dovecote elsewhere than from one of two small ones in front of us.

### Vineyard Chick Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁבִיל שֶׁל כְּרָמִים; דְּאִם אִיתָא דְּמֵעָלְמָא אָתֵי, כֵּיוָן דְּמִידַּדֵּי – לָא מָצֵי אָתֵי; דְּכׇל דְּמִידַּדֵּי וַהֲדַר חָזֵי לֵיהּ לְקִינֵּיהּ – מִידַּדֵּי, וְאִי לָא – לָא מִידַּדֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** We are dealing **with a path that** passes between **vineyards,** and these two dovecotes are situated there. **As, if it is so that** you claim the chick **came from** anywhere else in **the world, since it** only **hops, it could not have come** there. The reason is **that any** bird **that hops and turns** and **sees its nest** will continue **to hop; but if it does not** see its nest because it has gone too far, **it will not hop** farther. Consequently, this found chick that hops must have come from one of these two dovecotes.

*Hacha bemai askinan b'shvil shel kramim* — we are dealing with a chick waddling along a path through a vineyard. The key point is that any dove coming from "the world at large" would have had to fly, not waddle. And the *gemara* has already established that a waddling chick cannot travel more than fifty *amot*. If we find a *medadeh* chick waddling along a vineyard path, it cannot have come from afar — it must be from one of the local dovecotes. *D'i ita d'me'alma ati, bimdadei lo matzi ati* — if it had come from the broader world, it could not have gotten there by waddling. Therefore, *rov d'alma* is entirely inapplicable, and *karov* — the proximity to one of the two local dovecotes — is the only operative principle. Whichever dovecote the waddling chick is nearest to, that is where it came from.
