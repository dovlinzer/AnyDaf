# Bava Batra 23 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kotel Gina vs. Chatzer

### Wall Distance Context

The sugya before us concerns the permissible proximity of one wall to another on neighboring properties. The older *mefarshim* on the *mishnah* — found back on 22a — discuss building a perpendicular wall *l'chatchila*, where walls already exist and one is adding another. But the *gemara*, as we saw with Rabbi Eliezer, is operating under the assumption that this is actually a case where one of the walls has fallen and is now being rebuilt. In that context, when you rebuild, you must leave a certain distance — four *amot* — between your wall and your neighbor's.

### Rav: Gina vs. Chatzer

The *gemara* opens on 22b, eight lines from the top, with *Amar Rav: lo shanu ela kotel gina, aval kotel chatzer, im ba lismoch, somech* — Rav says that the entire rule requiring four *amot* of distance applies only to the wall of a garden, a *gina*. But when it comes to the wall of a courtyard, a *chatzer*, if one wishes to build it somewhat closer, one may do so even within four *amot*.

### Ground Compaction Logic

What is the distinction between a *gina* and a *chatzer*? In a garden where things are grown, no one is walking on one side of the wall; the land there is cultivated rather than traversed. The four *amot* distance is therefore required so that people can walk in the space between the two walls. That walking tramples and compacts the ground, which solidifies the foundation of the wall. In a *chatzer*, however, people are constantly moving about within the courtyard itself, compacting the earth around the wall's base on the interior side. With that side already well-packed, we are less concerned about the absence of a passageway on the other side.

One might fairly ask why packing down only one side is sufficient — surely both sides of the wall need support. The question is a reasonable one, and it points directly to the objection that follows.

### Rabbi Oshia Opposes

*Rabbi Oshia omer: echad kotel gina ve-echad kotel chatzer, im ba lismoch, ein somech.* Rabbi Oshia disagrees with Rav's distinction entirely. In his view, there is no difference between the wall of a garden and the wall of a courtyard: in either case, one may not build the new wall closer than the required distance. This is precisely because, as the question above suggested, one side of the wall being well-packed is not sufficient. The other side — the side between the two walls, which belongs to neither party in the usual sense — also needs to be traversed so that the ground there is compacted as well. Therefore, the space must be maintained regardless of what type of enclosure the wall surrounds.

### Old vs. New City

Rabbi Yosi bar Chanina resolves the apparent dispute: *velo pligi* — Rav and Rabbi Oshia do not actually disagree. They are simply addressing two different scenarios. *Ha be-ir yeshana, ha be-ir chadasha* — one is speaking of an old city, and the other of a new city. In an old, established city, the ground has already been compacted over many years of use, and so Rav permits building closer in a *chatzer*. In a newly built city, however, the ground has not yet been packed down, and Rabbi Oshia's stricter requirement applies. There is also a dimension here that resembles the difference between *de facto* urban realities and *l'chatchila* city planning: in old cities the layout has developed organically, with narrow alleyways and dense construction, whereas in a new city one has the opportunity to establish proper infrastructure from the outset.

## Window Privacy Rules

### Mishna: Windows

The *mishnah* states: *hachalonot bein milmala bein milmata bein miknegdan arba amot* — with respect to windows, whether above, below, or directly opposite, the required distance is four *amot*. The scenario is that Reuven has a window in his wall, and Shimon now wishes to build a wall that will face that window. The *mishnah* establishes that Shimon's wall must be situated such that its top is at least four *amot* above or four *amot* below Reuven's window, and the wall must stand at least four *amot* away if it is directly across.

### Peeping Prevention

A *braita* elaborates on the reasons: *milmala k'dei shelo yatzitz v'yireh* — the wall must be four *amot* above the window so that someone standing atop the wall cannot lean over and peer in. *Milmata shelo yaamod v'yireh* — it must be four *amot* below so that someone standing on the lower wall cannot look up and see in through the window. In both of these cases, the four *amot* measurement reflects the approximate height of a human being — it is the distance beyond which a person cannot effectively peer in or peer up. This is a fascinating point: we usually think of four *amot* as the horizontal personal space around a person, corresponding roughly to one's arm span. Yet here four *amot* appears in a vertical context, measuring against human height — and it is worth noting that arm span and height are in fact approximately equal for most people, which explains how the same unit serves both contexts.

### Opposite Wall Distance

For the wall built directly *miknegdan* — opposite the window — the reason given by the *braita* is *shelo yaafil*, so as not to cause darkness. This is a different concern entirely: not that someone will peer in, but that a wall built too close will block the natural light that would otherwise enter through the window. If the wall is at least four *amot* away, sunlight can still reach the opening.

### Conflicting Reasons

The *gemara* now raises a difficulty. The *braita* has given a single reason for the four *amot* requirement in the case of the opposite wall — prevention of darkness. But earlier the *gemara* discussed another reason for the distance requirement: the concern about trampling and packing down the earth (*davsha*). These two rationales cannot both be primary without creating a conflict. If the only reason were *davsha*, there would be no need to invoke darkness; if the only reason were darkness, *davsha* would be superfluous.

### Tarpeling Context

The *gemara* resolves this: *hacha b'mai askinan b'va min hatzad* — we are dealing here with a wall being built perpendicular to the wall containing the window, making an L-shape rather than a parallel configuration. In that case, the trampling concern is not directly at issue in the same way — people are not walking through a passage between two parallel walls. The darkness concern, however, is very much applicable: even a perpendicular wall, if built too close to the window, can obstruct the angle of incoming light.

The case involving *davsha*, by contrast, involves walls without windows facing one another, where the passage between them is what matters. The two scenarios — parallel walls facing each other, and a perpendicular wall approaching a windowed wall — each implicate different reasons, and thus different rationales can apply without contradiction.

### Window Width Distance

Once we are in the case of a perpendicular wall, the question becomes: how far must it stand from the window itself? The *gemara* cites an opinion in the name of Rav: *kemlo rochav chalon* — the perpendicular wall must be set back a distance equal to the full width of the window. So if the window is four feet wide, the perpendicular wall must be placed four feet from the edge of the window opening.

### Curved Wall Top

A difficulty immediately presents itself: if the wall is only one window-width away, someone who climbs atop it will still be at an angle from which they can see directly into the window. This seems to undermine the entire privacy rationale. *Amar Rav Zevid bemadrit kotlo* — Rav Zevid answers that the case is one where the top of the wall has been curved or sloped, creating an incline rather than a flat surface. A curved top makes it practically impossible to stand on the wall and look in. This is similar to walls fitted with barbed wire or shards of glass — security features designed to prevent climbing — and here it serves the additional halakhic purpose of eliminating the concern of *hezek re'iyah*, the damage of unwanted visual intrusion.

### One vs. Two Walls

The *gemara* then reconciles the two measurements — four *amot* and window-width — by distinguishing between scenarios. *Lo kashya: kan beruach achat, kan bishtei ruchot* — there is no contradiction. When there is only one wall being built perpendicular to the windowed wall, the window-width distance suffices. When two walls are being constructed — creating something like a passage that funnels directly toward the window — the full four *amot* distance is required, because the combined structural narrowing intensifies the risk of visual intrusion.

## Gutter & Ladder Rules

### Trampling vs. Ladder

The *gemara* raises the same analytical question about the next *mishnah* as it did about the window rules: when we require a certain distance between a wall and a neighboring gutter, is the reason the *davsha* concern — that people need space to walk and compact the ground — or is it because of the need to be able to set up a ladder to perform maintenance on the gutter?

### Overhang Solution

The *gemara* resolves this by specifying the case: *b'mazchila meshupaat* — we are dealing with a gutter that extends on an overhang. Because the gutter protrudes overhead, a person can walk beneath it without any obstruction. Therefore, the spacing of the new wall does not affect the trampling of the ground — people can still pass underneath. The reason the four *amot* distance is still required is entirely for the sake of ladder access: the owner of the gutter needs to be able to erect a ladder to repair and maintain it, and the wall being built nearby must leave enough room for that.

### Dovecote & Ladder

The *mishnah* states: *marchikin et hasulam min hashovach arba amot kedei shelo tikpotz hanemiya* — one must keep a ladder at least four *amot* away from a dovecote, so that the *nemiya* — a weasel or mongoose — should not be able to leap from the ladder into the dovecote and attack the doves. If the ladder is placed directly across from the dovecote, the weasel can climb the ladder and jump across. At a distance of four *amot*, that leap is beyond its range.

### Gutter Distance

The *mishnah* continues: *ve-et hakotel min hamazchila arba amot kedei sheyehei zokef et hasulam* — and one must keep a wall at least four *amot* from a neighboring gutter, so that the gutter's owner has sufficient space to erect a ladder for maintenance. This is, as the *gemara* has now clarified, the operative rationale in the case of an overhanging gutter — not ground compaction, but ladder access.

## Grama & Bloodletters

### Rabbi Yosi Position

The *gemara* raises the question of whether our *mishnah* — with its various requirements to keep ladders and walls at sensitive distances — is compatible with the position of Rabbi Yosi. Rabbi Yosi held the principle *zeh chofer betoch shelo ve-zeh noteiah betoch shelo*: each person does as he wishes on his own property, and the impact on his neighbor is not his legal concern. Rabbi Yosi's view is essentially a strong property-rights position: what is mine is mine, what is yours is yours, and I am not obligated to limit my activities on my land simply because they may affect yours.

### Direct vs. Indirect

If the *mishnah* is like Rabbi Yosi, why does it impose restrictions on ladder placement and wall distance? The *gemara* answers by citing a statement of Rav Ashi: *ki havina bei Rav Kahana hava amar: modeh Rabbi Yosi begirei dideh* — Rabbi Yosi concedes in the case of one who shoots arrows directly at another's property. Even Rabbi Yosi agrees that direct aggression against a neighbor's domain is prohibited. And in the case of the ladder and the dovecote, the danger is not truly indirect: weasels live in holes and wait for precisely such a ladder to be placed nearby before climbing up and attacking. Placing the ladder directly across from the dovecote is therefore sufficiently direct — close enough to the equivalent of "shooting arrows" — that even Rabbi Yosi would prohibit it.

### Grama is Asur

But then the *gemara* asks: is this not still a case of *grama* — indirect causation? The person erecting the ladder is not himself attacking the doves; it is the weasel that does so. *Amar Rav Tuvi bar Matna: zot omeret grama b'nezikin asur* — this *mishnah* comes to teach us that *grama b'nezikin* is prohibited. Causing damage indirectly is still halakhically forbidden.

### Rav Yosef's Trees

This principle is illustrated by a vivid incident. Rav Yosef had date trees growing on his property. Bloodletters — practitioners of medical bloodletting — had taken to sitting beneath these trees to conduct their work. The blood they left on the ground attracted ravens, which then flew up into the trees and damaged the dates. Rav Yosef told the bloodletters to take their practice elsewhere.

### Abaye Questions

Abaye challenged Rav Yosef: *v'ha grama hu* — is this not merely *grama*? The causal chain here is long: the bloodletters sit and practice their trade, the blood attracts ravens, the ravens fly into the trees, and only then are the dates damaged. The bloodletters are not directly harming the trees at all.

### No Chazaka for Nezikin

The response invokes Rav Tuvi bar Matna's ruling: *grama b'nezikin asur* — indirect damage is still prohibited. A further question then arises: the bloodletters have been coming to this spot for years. Do they not have a *chazaka* — an established usage — that grants them the right to continue? *Amar Rav Nachman amar Rabba bar Avuha: ein chazaka l'nezikin* — no *chazaka* applies when one is causing *nezikin*. The mere passage of time does not legitimize an activity that damages a neighbor.

### Rav Mari Qualification

However, *Rav Mari amar b'kutra, v'Rav Zevid amar b'veit hachisei* — Rav Mari and Rav Zevid each qualify the principle of *ein chazaka l'nezikin*, limiting it to especially severe nuisances: cases involving heavy smoke (*kutra*) or a toilet (*beit hakisei*) placed near one's property. These are situations so offensive that no amount of prior usage can normalize them for the affected neighbor.

Rav Yosef accordingly invokes this framework on his own behalf: *l'didi d'anina da'ati, k'kutra u'veit hachisei damyan li* — for him, with his particular sensitivity, the ravens and the blood are subjectively equivalent to smoke or a toilet placed next door. What is striking here is that the *gemara* appears to honor this subjective claim, treating Rav Yosef's personal threshold of sensibility as halakhically operative. The underlying question — whether the *davsha* causing the dates to spoil is the primary damage, or whether it is Rav Yosef's subjective revulsion at the bloodletting scene — animates the discussion without being fully resolved. Either way, the *gemara* endorses Rav Yosef's demand that the bloodletters leave, and *ein chazaka l'nezikin* remains limited in its application to situations of genuinely severe and ongoing harm.

## Dovecote Distances

### 50 Amot Rule

The *mishnah* states: *merachikin et hashovach min ha-ir chamishim ama* — one must keep a dovecote at least fifty *amot* from the city. The doves will inevitably forage in the nearby area, and placing the dovecote too close to inhabited land means they will damage the crops or garden plots of city residents. The fifty *amot* buffer is designed to prevent this.

### Circumference Rules

Furthermore, *velo yaaseh adam shovach betoch shelo ela im ken yesh lo chamishim ama lachol ruach* — a person may not build a dovecote in his own property unless he has fifty *amot* available in every direction around it. The doves' natural ranging area is this fifty *amot* radius, and so the owner must control enough land that the doves' foraging does not encroach upon others' fields.

Rabbi Yehuda offers a different measurement: *beit arba korin melo shegar hayona* — a space of four *kor*, which works out to approximately 273 *amot*, described as the full natural flight distance a dove can cover in one sustained effort. This is a considerably larger area than the fifty *amot* of the first opinion.

### Chazaka Permission

Both opinions agree on one dispensation: *ve-im lekacha, afilu beit rova, harei hu b'chezkato* — if one purchased a property that already had a dovecote in place, even if that dovecote is smaller than required or situated closer to the borders than the rules would allow, it is considered *b'chezkato*, grandfathered in. The assumption is that the previous owner had already arranged some form of understanding with the surrounding neighbors, and the purchaser inherits that established status.

## Dove Trapping Rules

### 30 Ris Limit

The *gemara* challenges the fifty *amot* rule by citing a *braita*: *ein porsin nishbin leyona ela im ken haya rachok min hayishuv sheloshim ris* — one may not set out traps for doves unless one is at least thirty *ris* away from an inhabited settlement. If fifty *amot* were the outer limit of a dove's range — beyond which the bird belongs to the finder — why would trapping require staying thirty *ris* away? This seems to imply that doves can travel far greater distances than fifty *amot*.

### Dove Flight Distance

Abaye resolves the apparent contradiction: *mishiti tuva ve-chasheu be-chamishim amata milia* — a dove does eat considerably while traveling, and by the time it has traveled fifty *amot* from the dovecote, it has slowed down substantially from all the foraging along the way. The fifty *amot* limit thus represents the extent to which we consider the bird to be reasonably connected to a nearby dovecote, not the absolute outer limit of how far a dove can physically travel.

### 100 Mil Rule

Yet another *braita* states that within a settled area, one may not set traps even at a distance of a hundred *mil*. This seems far more restrictive than either the fifty *amot* or the thirty *ris* figure.

### Vineyard Context

Rav Yosef resolves this: *b'shuv kramim* — that ruling pertains to a settlement of vineyards. Doves can fly considerably greater distances when vineyards are present, perhaps because the terrain offers different resting and feeding patterns, causing them to continue flying farther than they otherwise would.

### Dovecote Area

Rava adds a further case: *b'shuv shovchin* — a settlement densely populated with dovecotes, where the birds are constantly moving between one dovecote and another. In such an area, a dove may hop from cote to cote across great distances, so one must go much farther afield before setting traps.

### Ownership Determination

The *gemara* then asks why the ruling mentions the concern about *shovchin* at all — could one not simply say that the traps are fine because the doves encountered would be from the trapper's own dovecotes? The *gemara* entertains three possibilities: the additional dovecotes belong to the trapper himself, in which case trapping is unproblematic; or they belong to non-Jews, in which case there is no concern for their ownership; or they are ownerless (*hefker*). The different resolutions determine how far from a settlement one must be before setting traps, and which configuration triggers the full hundred-*mil* restriction.

## Dovecote Distances (II)

### Purchaser & Heir Claims

*Amar Rav Papa v'iseyma Rav Zevid: zot omeret, to'anin lalo'ke'ach ve-to'anin layoresh* — Rav Papa, and some say Rav Zevid, infer from the *mishnah*'s statement that a purchaser who acquires a smaller or more closely situated dovecote is nonetheless *b'chezkato*, that the *beit din* is prepared to advance legal claims on behalf of both a purchaser and an heir. That is, if a third party challenges the legitimacy of the dovecote's placement, the *beit din* does not require the purchaser or heir to produce positive proof of prior authorization; the *chazaka* of the previous owner is treated as already having established the right, and the *beit din* builds that presumption into the current possessor's position.

The *gemara* immediately questions whether we need the *mishnah* here to tell us about an heir — do we not already have an explicit *mishnah* elsewhere establishing that one who comes on the basis of inheritance does not need to bring a formal claim? *Haba mishum yerusha eino tzarich ta'ana*. The novelty must therefore lie in the case of the purchaser, *loke'ach*.

But the *gemara* pushes further: do we not also have an explicit *mishnah* about a purchaser? *Lakach chatzer u-va zizin u-guztra'os harei zeh b'chezkato* — one who purchases a courtyard that has protrusions and balconies already extending into the public domain acquires them with their established status. If we already have that precedent, why do we need to derive the rule again from the dovecote case?

The *gemara* answers that each case is necessary — *tzricha*. The case of protrusions over a *reshut harabbim* might have been treated leniently on the theory that the public effectively granted permission, or that the owner appeased the public, or that the public simply forgave the encroachment. One could not necessarily extend that to a private property dispute. Conversely, a case involving only private parties might be explained away by the assumption that the dovecote owner personally negotiated with each affected neighbor. But in the *reshut harabbim* case, there is no identifiable party to negotiate with — *man payis?* Who does one even appease? Each scenario thus teaches something the other cannot, and both rulings are required.

The *gemara* then raises a further challenge: the *mishnah*'s ruling that a purchased dovecote is *b'chezkato* seems to stand in tension with *Rav Nachman's* principle in the name of Rabba bar Avuha — *ein chazaka l'nezikin*. If a dovecote causes damage to neighbors' crops, how can its *chazaka* be honored? The *gemara* resolves this by invoking the qualification of Rav Mari and Rav Zevid: *ein chazaka l'nezikin* applies specifically to severe nuisances such as smoke or a toilet. The harm caused by a dovecote — doves eating from nearby fields — is not of that extreme category, and so the *chazaka* for the dovecote is maintained. Not all sources of *nezek* are treated equally: some are severe enough to override any *chazaka*, while others are tolerated once an established usage has been recognized.

## Found Dove Rules

### Nippul Ownership

The *mishnah* turns to the case of a *nippul* — a baby dove that cannot yet fly, a *gozal*, found on the ground. *Nippul hanimtza betoch chamishim ama, harei hu shel ba'al hashovach* — a chick found within fifty *amot* of a dovecote belongs to the owner of that dovecote. Since the chick cannot fly, it could only have gotten there by waddling from the nearby cote.

### Beyond 50 Amot

*Chutz michamishim ama, harei hu shel motza'o* — if found beyond fifty *amot*, the chick belongs to the finder. The assumption is that it could not have walked that far from the dovecote, and so its origin is unknown; the finder acquires it.

This *gemara* raises a broader question that connects to the rules of lost objects in *Bava Metzia*: ordinarily one would expect a found animal to require *simanim* before being claimed. Here, however, proximity to a dovecote serves as an automatic assignment of ownership, without requiring formal identification. One might argue that within fifty *amot* of a known dovecote, the chick is not truly a lost object at all — it simply has not yet flown far enough to sever its connection to the cote.

### Two Dovecotes

*Nimtza bein shtei shovcha'ot, karov lazeh shelo, karov lazeh shelo* — if the chick is found between two dovecotes, each within fifty *amot*, ownership is determined by proximity: whichever dovecote is closer, the chick belongs to its owner. If the chick is found exactly equidistant — *mechtzah al mechtzah* — the two owners split it: *shneihem yachloku*.

## Rov vs. Karov

### Rov Challenge

*Amar Rabbi Chanina: rov vekarov holchin achar harov* — Rabbi Chanina raises a pointed challenge. The entire operating principle of the *mishnah* appears to be *karov*, proximity: we assign the chick to the nearest dovecote. But do we not have the competing principle of *rov* — majority? If one dovecote has five thousand doves and another has five, it is statistically far more likely that any given chick came from the larger one. Why does proximity override numerical probability?

### Rov vs. Karov Sources

The *gemara* identifies two distinct *d'Oraita* principles in tension here. On one side is *acharei rabim lehatot* — the Torah's instruction to follow the majority. On the other side is the model of *egla arufa* — the decapitated heifer ritual, where the Torah requires measuring from the discovered corpse to determine which city is closest and must bring the *egla arufa*, without regard for the size of the nearby cities. That procedure clearly operates on *karov*, proximity. *Af al gav d'rov d'Oraita ve-karov d'Oraita afilu hachi rov adif* — Rabbi Chanina maintains that even though both are *d'Oraita* principles, *rov* is the stronger of the two and should prevail.

### Egla Arufa Model

Rabbi Zeira challenges this by citing the *egla arufa* itself: *vehaya ha-ir hakerova el hechalal* — the verse specifies the city closest to the slain person. *Af al gav de'ika achrita denefisha minah* — and this applies even if there are other, larger cities in the vicinity. This seems to be a direct proof that *karov* can and does override *rov* — at least in that context.

### Mountain Area Limit

The *gemara* deflects the proof: the *egla arufa* case is specifically *beyoshevet bein heharim*, situated in isolated mountain terrain. In such a setting, there may not be any significantly larger city available; the nearby towns may all be of comparable size, making *rov* inapplicable. In that narrow context, *karov* governs by default. But in a normal setting where larger cities exist, one might argue that *rov* should prevail, and the *egla arufa* verse would not override it.

This is a striking limitation. Taken at face value, it would seem to confine the *egla arufa*'s *karov* principle to geographically isolated communities alone, which seems difficult to square with the *Torah*'s broader presentation of the *egla arufa* ritual. The *gemara* here appears to be making a theoretical point rather than a comprehensive ruling on the *egla arufa* procedure, but the qualification is notable.

## Chick Movement Rules

### Rav Ukva on Waddling

The *gemara* explains the empirical basis for the fifty *amot* limit by citing *Rav Ukva bar Chama: kol hamedadeh ein medadeh yoter mechamishim amah* — any chick that is waddling (*medade*) cannot waddle more than fifty *amot*. This is the physical reality that underlies the *mishnah*'s rule: waddling chicks are simply incapable of traveling farther than fifty *amot* from the nest.

### Rabbi Yirmiya Query

Into this settled framework steps Rabbi Yirmiya with his celebrated question: *raglo achat betoch chamishim amah ve-raglo achat chutz mechamishim amah mahu* — what if the chick has one foot inside the fifty *amot* boundary and one foot outside it? To whom does such a chick belong?

### Rejection

*Afkuh l'Rabbi Yirmiya mibei midrashah* — Rabbi Yirmiya was expelled from the *beit midrash*. The question was deemed so far outside the bounds of serious inquiry as to warrant removal. Yet the condemnation requires explanation, since the *mishnah* itself just addressed the case of a chick found exactly equidistant between two dovecotes — which implies precisely the kind of borderline scenario Rabbi Yirmiya is raising.

*Tosafot* offers the key insight: it was not the form of the question that offended, but its underlying implication. Rav Ukva bar Chama had stated as a matter of established fact that chicks cannot waddle more than fifty *amot*. Rabbi Yirmiya's question presupposes that a chick might take a half-step past the fifty *amot* line — thereby implicitly challenging the factual ruling of the *Chachamim*. It is this subtle questioning of the received scientific determination, rather than any logical absurdity in the question itself, that led to his expulsion. *Rashi* adds simply that Rabbi Yirmiya was being unnecessarily burdensome — *shehayah matriach aleihem* — and the *beit midrash* had already moved on to larger conceptual questions about human mortality, city proximity, and the principles of *rov* and *karov*. Pedantic hypotheticals about the precise placement of a chick's foot were not welcome.

## Final Rov Resolution

### Equal Dovecotes

*Nimtza bein shenei shovachot, karov lazeh shelo, karov lazeh shelo, ve-af al gav de-chad mineihu nafish mechavreih* — the chick found between two dovecotes goes to the closer one, even if the other is larger. But if they are equal in size — *sheshneihem shavin* — proximity governs, and the chick belongs to the owner of the nearer dovecote. This resolves Rabbi Chanina's challenge: the *mishnah* is simply not addressing a case where the dovecotes differ in size at all, and so no conflict with *rov* arises.

### Vineyard Exception

But then the *gemara* asks: if the dovecotes are of equal size, why not invoke *rov d'alma* — the majority of all doves in the world? Any given dove is statistically more likely to have originated from some large dovecote elsewhere than from one of two small ones in front of us.

### Vineyard Chick Case

*Hacha bemai askinan b'shvil shel kramim* — we are dealing with a chick waddling along a path through a vineyard. The key point is that any dove coming from "the world at large" would have had to fly, not waddle. And the *gemara* has already established that a waddling chick cannot travel more than fifty *amot*. If we find a *medadeh* chick waddling along a vineyard path, it cannot have come from afar — it must be from one of the local dovecotes. *D'i ita d'me'alma ati, bimdadei lo matzi ati* — if it had come from the broader world, it could not have gotten there by waddling. Therefore, *rov d'alma* is entirely inapplicable, and *karov* — the proximity to one of the two local dovecotes — is the only operative principle. Whichever dovecote the waddling chick is nearest to, that is where it came from.
