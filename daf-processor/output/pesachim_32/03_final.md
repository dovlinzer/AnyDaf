# Pesachim 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Chametz Under Rubble

### Buried Chametz Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ חָמֵץ שֶׁנָּפְלָה עָלָיו מַפּוֹלֶת הֲרֵי הוּא כִּמְבוֹעָר. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: כׇּל שֶׁאֵין הַכֶּלֶב יָכוֹל לְחַפֵּשׂ אַחֲרָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Leavened bread upon which a rockslide has fallen is considered as though it has been eliminated,** and it is not necessary to dig it up in order to burn it. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Any** leavened bread that has been covered to such an extent **that a dog cannot search after it** is considered to have been eliminated.

The daf opens by picking up the *mishnah* on 31b. The case is *chametz shenaphlah alav mapolet* — leavened bread that has been buried under rubble. The *mishnah* rules *harei hu kimevuar*: the chametz is considered as though it has already been destroyed, since it is inaccessible. Rabban Gamliel, however, applies a more demanding criterion: mere burial is insufficient. What matters is whether *kol she'ein hakelev yakhol lachapes acharav* — a dog would be unable to dig it up. Only if the chametz is buried deep enough that a dog cannot unearth it is it considered *mevuar*.

### Bitul Despite Burial

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: וְצָרִיךְ שֶׁיְּבַטֵּל בְּלִבּוֹ. תָּנָא: כַּמָּה חֲפִישַׂת הַכֶּלֶב — שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Ḥisda said:** Although it is not necessary to dig up the leavened bread, one is nevertheless **required to nullify** the leavened bread **in his heart** lest it become exposed during Passover. Although it may not be visible at the moment, this leavened bread may be uncovered during Passover, and he will transgress a prohibition by its being seen. It was **taught** in the *Tosefta*: **How much,** how deep, **will a dog search?** It will search **three handbreadths** deep.

Rav Chisda adds a critical qualification: even in the case where the chametz is buried and considered destroyed, *tzarich sheyevatel belibo* — one must still perform *bitul* in one's heart. Rashi explains the reasoning: *shema yipaka'ach hagal be-Moed* — perhaps the rubble will be cleared away during *Chol ha-Moed*, and the chametz will then be found intact. At that point, one would be in violation of *lo yeira'eh lekha velo yimatzei lekha*, since the chametz would again be *matzui* — accessible and present. This is why *bitul* remains necessary: since *min ha-Torah* *bitul* alone suffices, performing it in advance provides the essential legal protection.

### Three Tefach Standard

The passage continues with a *tanna* teaching: *kama chapisat hakelev* — how deep can a dog dig? The answer is *sheloshah tephachim*, three *tephachim*, according to the view of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel. The *tephach* is roughly three to four inches, placing the total depth at somewhere between nine and twelve inches. This is the operative standard: if the chametz is buried beneath three *tephachim* of rubble, a dog will not be able to reach it.

### Chametz & Money Storage

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הָא דְּאָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל כְּסָפִים אֵין לָהֶם שְׁמִירָה אֶלָּא בַּקַּרְקַע, מִי בָּעֵינַן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים אוֹ לָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָכָא מִשּׁוּם רֵיחָא בָּעֵינַן שְׁלֹשָׁה טְפָחִים. הָתָם מִשּׁוּם אִיכַּסּוֹיֵי מֵעֵינָא הוּא, וְלָא בָּעֵי שְׁלֹשָׁה. וְכַמָּה? אָמַר רַפְרָם בַּר פָּפָּא מִסִּיכְרָא: טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rav Yosef, said to Rav Ashi: With regard to that which Shmuel said,** that deposited **money is considered to be guarded** securely by an unpaid bailee, who would nonetheless not be responsible if it were stolen, **only** when it is buried **in the ground, is it necessary** to bury this deposited money **three handbreadths deep,** comparable to leavened bread, **or not? He said to him: Here,** with regard to Passover, the concern is that the dog will find the food **due to** its **smell,** and therefore **three handbreadths are required. There,** in the case of money, **it is** necessary to bury the money **in order to conceal it from view.** Therefore, **it is not required** to bury it **three** handbreadths deep, as animals will not search for it and people will not see it. The Gemara asks: If this is the case, then **how** deep is one required to bury it? **Rafram bar Pappa** from the city of **Sikhra said: One handbreadth** is sufficient for the money to be considered concealed.

Rav Acha the son of Rav Yosef poses a question to Rav Ashi by drawing in a different ruling. Shmuel teaches that *kesaphim ein lahen shemirah ela be-karka* — the proper way to safeguard money that has been entrusted to you is to bury it in the ground. Given that we have now introduced the standard of three *tephachim* in the context of chametz, the question arises: *mee ba'inan sheloshah tephachim alav* — must one also bury money three *tephachim* deep in order to fulfill this requirement?

### Smell vs. Visibility

Rav Ashi distinguishes sharply between the two cases. *Hacha mishum reicha ba'inan sheloshah tephachim* — in the case of chametz, the reason three *tephachim* are required is specifically because of smell. A dog will dig only where it detects a scent; below three *tephachim*, the chametz cannot be smelled and therefore will not be sought. But *hatam mishum ikhsoyyei me-ayna hu* — for money, the concern is entirely different: it is simply a matter of concealing it from view. Once it is buried even slightly and the surface is smoothed over, it is considered hidden. *Ve-lo ba'inan sheloshah* — there is no need for three *tephachim*. Indeed, Rav Afram bar Pappa, from Sikhra, rules that for money, a single *tephach* suffices.

## Terumah Chametz Laws

### Terumah Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, בְּשׁוֹגֵג — מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, בְּמֵזִיד — פָּטוּר מִתַּשְׁלוּמִין וּמִדְּמֵי עֵצִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **If one unwittingly eats *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover,** not realizing that the food was *teruma*, then **he must pay the principal and an** additional **fifth.** This is because one who unwittingly eats *teruma* must compensate the priest for the value of the *teruma* and add a fifth of the value, even though the *teruma* is considered to be valueless on Passover. If he **intentionally** ate the *teruma* then **he is exempt from payment;** as he is liable to receive the severe punishment of *karet*, he is therefore exempt from the lesser punishment of payment. If he ate impure *teruma* in this manner then he is not even required to pay **its monetary** value **in wood,** for one who derives benefit from impure *teruma* calculates its value by treating it as though it were fuel for burning. While impure *teruma* can be used in this manner during the rest of the year, one may not derive any benefit from leavened bread on Passover, and therefore such *teruma* is worthless.

The *gemara* transitions to a new topic, introducing the *mishnah* concerning one who eats *terumah* that is *chametz* on Pesach. By way of background: *terumah* is not *hekdesh* — it is not Temple-consecrated property — but it occupies a related sanctified domain. As the *gemara* has already established, one is permitted to maintain in one's possession *chametz* that belongs to *nokrim* and *chametz* that belongs to *hekdesh*, since *shel'cha i atta ro'eh aval atta ro'eh shel acherim veshel gavoah* — the prohibition is against seeing *your* chametz, but you may see the chametz of others or of *gavoah*. The question now is what happens when a non-*kohen* eats, on Pesach, chametz that had the status of *terumah* belonging to a *kohen*.

### Payment & Intent

The intuitive expectation might be that no obligation of restitution would arise, for two distinct reasons. First, one might invoke *kim lei b'drabbah minei*: eating *chametz* on Pesach is an *issur karet*, and when one has incurred a grave spiritual penalty, the accompanying monetary obligation is set aside — the more severe punishment supersedes the lesser one. Second, one might argue that the chametz is simply worthless — *assur be-hana'ah* on Pesach — so what is there to repay?

Nonetheless, the *mishnah* rules otherwise. *Ha'ochel terumat chametz be-Pesach be-shogeg meshalem keren ve-chomesh* — one who eats *terumah chametz* on Pesach unintentionally must pay the principal and an additional fifth, just as with any other inadvertent consumption of *terumah*. However, *be-mezid patur mi-tashlumin u-mi-d'mei etzim* — if done intentionally, he is exempt from payment — and also exempt from payment of the value of the wood (i.e., whatever fuel value the *terumah chametz* might have had). The ruling is thus precisely parallel to the general law of *terumah*: *shogeg* obligates, *mezid* exempts.

### Rashi's Rationale

Rashi's explanation helps clarify what is at stake. The case of *shogeg* that triggers the *keren ve-chomesh* obligation is defined as *shagag bi-terumah* — one who was unaware of the *terumah* status of the food, even if he knew it was *chametz* and knew it was Pesach. *Ve-af al gav d'la havei shaveh midei* — and even though the chametz may not be worth anything in monetary terms, since *chametz shel Pesach assur be-hana'ah*, nonetheless the *terumah* restitution laws still apply. The obligation is grounded in the *terumah* dimension of the transaction, not in the market value of the item consumed.

The verse underlying this law reads: *uve-chamishito yosef alav ve-natan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — with its fifth he shall add to it and give it to the *kohen* as *kodesh*. Rashi notes that restitution is made not in money but in kind — in produce that itself has *terumah* status. And the *gemara* elsewhere further specifies that repayment must be in something *hara'uy lihyot kodesh*, fit to be sanctified, which already begins to hint at why chametz itself, given its legal status on Pesach, raises complications.

### Atonement Principle

If we ask why the *mezid* is exempt, the answer emerges from two complementary angles. On one level, it is *kim lei b'drabbah minei*: intentional consumption of *terumah* carries *misah bi-yedei shamayim*, death at the hands of Heaven, and the principle holds that the graver penalty displaces the monetary one. On a more conceptual level, the payment of *keren ve-chomesh* functions as an act of atonement — *kaparah* — for the violation of *terumah*'s sanctity. The *mezid*, one who violates deliberately, is not considered worthy of that path of atonement. Paradoxically, then, the *shogeg* incurs monetary liability while the *mezid* does not — the *mezid* avoids financial obligation but forfeits the spiritual rectification that payment would represent.

A nuance worth noting: *karet* and *misah bi-yedei shamayim* are distinct categories of Heavenly punishment, with *karet* carrying additional dimensions, including potential effects on one's descendants. Intentional eating of *chametz* on Pesach is *karet*; intentional eating of *terumah* by a non-*kohen* is *misah bi-yedei shamayim*. The *gemara* does not elaborate on these distinctions here, but they bear on the general question of how broadly *kim lei b'drabbah minei* applies — a matter that will be taken up shortly through the ruling of Harkonus ben Hakanas.

## Measure vs. Value

### Core Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: כְּשֶׁהוּא מְשַׁלֵּם, לְפִי מִדָּה מְשַׁלֵּם אוֹ לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם? כׇּל הֵיכָא דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא שָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה זוּזֵי וּלְבַסּוֹף שָׁוְיָא זוּזָא — לָא תִּיבְּעֵי לָךְ, דְּוַדַּאי כִּדְמֵעִיקָּרָא מְשַׁלֵּם לְפִי דָּמִים, דְּלָא גָּרַע מִגַּזְלָן. דִּתְנַן: כׇּל הַגַּזְלָנִין מְשַׁלְּמִין כִּשְׁעַת הַגְּזֵלָה,
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages with regard to the laws of *teruma*: **When he pays** for this *teruma*, **does he pay according to the measure** of the *teruma* **or according to its monetary** value? The Gemara explains the question in greater detail: **Anywhere that** the *teruma* **is worth four *zuz* at the outset,** i.e., at the time he consumed the *teruma*, **and is worth** only **one *zuz* at the end,** at the time of payment, **do not raise a dilemma,** for in that case **he is certainly** required **to pay according to the monetary** value **at the outset.** The rationale behind this ruling is **that he is no worse than a thief,** and therefore the law in this case is the same as if he had stolen property from another person. **As we learned** in a mishna: **All thieves must repay** what they have stolen according to the value of the stolen object **at the time it was stolen,** even if its value subsequently goes down.

Having established the basic *mishnah*, the *gemara* now raises a fundamental question about the mechanics of *terumah* restitution generally: *keshe-hu meshalem, lefi midah meshalem o lefi damim meshalem* — when one pays back, does he pay according to quantity or according to monetary value? To make this concrete: suppose someone consumed a liter of *terumah* olive oil. Does he owe back a liter of olive oil, regardless of price fluctuation? Or does he owe back the monetary equivalent of what he took — say, six dinars' worth — even if that now buys only half a liter?

### Hekdesh Principle

Before analyzing the upside scenario, the *gemara* dispenses with one direction of the question. Where the value has decreased — *d'heicha d'me'ikara shavya arba'a, u'levasof shavya zuza* — where it was originally worth four and is now worth only one — the answer is unambiguous: *vadai k'me'ikara meshalem*, he certainly pays at the original value. This follows from the general principle that applies also to *hekdesh*: the sanctified party never loses out due to depreciation. Furthermore, the minimum standard is that of an ordinary *gazlan*, of whom the *mishnah* rules *kol ha-gazlanim meshalmim k'sha'at ha-gezeilah* — all robbers pay the value as of the time of the theft. Terumah restitution cannot be more lenient than ordinary robbery.

The question, therefore, pertains only to the reverse: *d'me'ikara shaveh zuza u'levasof shaveh arba'a* — where it was worth one at the time of consumption and is worth four now. Does one pay the quantity (one liter, now worth four) or only the original value (one zuz's worth of oil, perhaps now only a quarter liter)?

### Figs & Dates Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף, תָּא שְׁמַע: אָכַל גְּרוֹגְרוֹת וְשִׁילֵּם לוֹ תְּמָרִים תָּבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְפִי מִדָּה מְשַׁלֵּם — אַמְּטוּ לְהָכִי תָּבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה, דְּאָכֵיל גְּרִיוָא דִגְרוֹגְרוֹת דְּשָׁוְיָא זוּזָא, וְקָא יָהֵיב גְּרִיוָא דִתְמָרִים דְּשָׁוְיָא אַרְבְּעָה. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם — אַמַּאי תָּבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה? בְּזוּזָא אֲכַל — בְּזוּזָא קָא מְשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: Come** and **hear** an answer to this question from what was taught in a *baraita*: **One who ate dried figs** that were *teruma* **and paid** the priest **with dates,** may **a blessing rest upon him,** as dates are worth more than dried figs. **Granted, if you say that one** must **repay according to the measure** of *teruma* he ate, it is **due to this** that **a blessing** should **rest upon him, as he ate a *se'a* of dried figs that are worth one *zuz* and gave** in return **a *se'a* of dates worth four *zuz*. However, if you say that** he must **repay according to the monetary** value of the *teruma*, then why **should a blessing rest upon him? He ate a *zuz* worth** of *teruma* **and he paid a *zuz* worth** as compensation; what is laudatory about his payment?

Rav Yosef attempts to resolve this from a teaching: *achal grogerot ve-shileim lo tmarim, tavo alav bracha* — one who ate dried figs and repaid the *kohen* with fresh dates, a blessing should come upon him. Dried figs are worth less than fresh dates; the restorer has thus given back something more valuable. *Tavo alav bracha* indicates he has exceeded his obligation.

Now, says Rav Yosef, this works out well if payment is *lefi midah* — by quantity. I consumed a *kor* of figs worth one zuz, and I returned a *kor* of dates worth four. Since I only needed to return the same quantity, returning something more valuable is praiseworthy. *Ela ei amrat lefi damim meshalem* — but if payment is by value, then I took one zuz's worth of figs, and I return one zuz's worth of dates, which is merely a quarter *kor*. I have given back the same monetary value, just in a different form. Why would there be *tavo alav bracha*? The *kohen* receives precisely what was taken, in terms of value.

This appears to constitute proof that repayment is *lefi midah*.

### Abaye's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: לְעוֹלָם לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם, וְאַמַּאי תָּבֹא עָלָיו בְּרָכָה? דַּאֲכַל מִידֵּי דְּלָא קָפֵיץ עֲלֵיהּ זָבֹינֵיהּ, וְקָא מְשַׁלֵּם מִידֵּי דְּקָפֵיץ עֲלֵיהּ זָבֹינֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Actually,** one can explain that **he** must **repay according to the monetary** value of the *teruma*, **and why** is it stated that a **blessing** should **rest upon him?** This is **because he ate an item that buyers don't jump at,** i.e., it is undesirable to buyers, **but paid with an item that buyers jump at.** Consequently, although the produce he gives is worth no more than the produce he ate, the priest still prefers this type of payment, as he can more easily resell this produce.

Abaye, however, resolves the question without conceding to that conclusion. *Le'olam lefi damim meshalem* — payment is indeed by monetary value. Yet *tavo alav bracha* still applies, not because the restorer gave more value in absolute terms, but because *d'ka meshalem leh midi d'lo kafeitz aleh zabina v'ka meshalem leh midi d'kafeitz aleh zabina* — he repaid something that is difficult to sell (dried figs) with something that is much easier to sell (fresh dates). The *kohen* benefits practically: even if the absolute value is identical, he can liquidate the dates far more readily than the figs. That practical advantage to the *kohen* is what earns the restorer the blessing. The question of *midah* versus *damim* thus remains formally unresolved by this *sugya*, to be further engaged through the lens of *terumah chametz* specifically.

## Chametz Value Debate

### Worthlessness Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, בְּשׁוֹגֵג מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ. אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא לְפִי מִדָּה מְשַׁלֵּם — שַׁפִּיר. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם, חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח בַּר דָּמִים הוּא? אִין, הָא מַנִּי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי הִיא, דְּאָמַר חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara seeks proof with regard to this dispute: **We learned** in the mishna: **One who unwittingly eats *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover** must **pay the principal and an** additional **fifth. Granted, if you say that he** must **pay according to the measure** of *teruma* that he ate, **it is well.** As he ate a *se'a* of *teruma* he must also repay a *se'a*. **However, if you say** that **he** must **pay according to the monetary** value of the *teruma*, this is difficult, for **is leavened bread on Passover of** any **monetary** value? Certainly it is not worth anything, given that it is forbidden to benefit from this food. The Gemara answers: **Yes,** this leavened bread does indeed have monetary value. In accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** mishna? **It is** in accordance with the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who said** that **it is permissible to** derive **benefit from leavened bread on Passover.**

The *gemara* circles back to *terumah chametz* with fresh force. The *mishnah* obligates payment of *keren ve-chomesh* for the *shogeg* who eats *terumah chametz* on Pesach. But if payment is *lefi damim* — by value — and if *chametz shel Pesach* has no monetary value because it is *assur be-hana'ah*, then what is there to repay? The value is zero; a fifth of zero is zero. The obligation seems to collapse under its own logic.

### Rabbi Yosi Position

The *gemara* offers an initial resolution: *hah mani, Rabbi Yose HaGelili hi* — this *mishnah* accords with the opinion of Rabbi Yosi HaGelili, who holds *chametz be-Pesach mutar be-hana'ah*, that chametz on Pesach is not prohibited from benefit and therefore retains monetary value. According to his view, there is no problem: the chametz does have value, one can pay *lefi damim*, and the *mishnah* works smoothly. The mainstream ruling that chametz is *assur be-hana'ah* is a separate matter; this *mishnah* may simply reflect the minority position.

### Akiva-Yochanan Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בְּמֵזִיד פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין וּמִדְּמֵי עֵצִים, אִי רַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, אַמַּאי פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין וּמִדְּמֵי עֵצִים?!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this suggestion: **If that is so,** then **say the latter clause** of the mishna, where it is stated: **If** he consumes the *teruma* **intentionally, then he is exempt from payment** to the priest for the value of the *teruma* **and for its monetary** value **in wood.** But **if** this follows the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili,** then **why** is he **exempt from payment** to the priest for the value of the *teruma* **and for its monetary** value **in wood?** Although he is exempt from paying the additional fifth as he acted intentionally, he nonetheless should be required to compensate the priest for the financial loss he caused him, as in any other case of theft.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה. דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי נְחוּנְיָא בֶּן הַקָּנָה הָיָה עוֹשֶׂה אֶת יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים כַּשַּׁבָּת לְתַשְׁלוּמִין וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **He holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana, as it was taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi Neḥunya ben HaKana rendered** the status of **Yom Kippur the same as** that of **Shabbat with regard to payment.** In his opinion, not only a person who committed a transgression punishable by a court-administered capital punishment, like one who desecrated Shabbat, is exempt from monetary payment incurred at the time of the transgression. Even one who is deserving of a divinely administered capital punishment, such as one who desecrates Yom Kippur and is punished with *karet*, is exempt from monetary payment for property he damaged in the course of such an act. Therefore, since one who consumes another person's leavened bread during Passover is deserving of *karet*, he is exempt from monetary payment incurred by this act.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתַנָּאֵי: הָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח — פָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִין וּמִדְּמֵי עֵצִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי מְחַיֵּיב. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי: וְכִי מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: וּמָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה בִּשְׁאָר כׇּל יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: The question of whether one must repay according to the measurement or the monetary value of the *teruma* is **like** a dispute between ***tanna'im*,** as it was taught in the *Tosefta*: **If one eats *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover,** whether intentionally or unwittingly, then **he is exempt from payment and** for **its monetary** value **in wood;** this is **the statement of Rabbi Akiva.** Whereas **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri deems** him **liable** to pay. **Rabbi Akiva said to Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri: What benefit** can **he derive from this?** What benefit could the priest have derived from this *teruma* as it is prohibited to benefit from this *teruma* and the *teruma* is therefore worthless? **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri said to Rabbi Akiva: What benefit** can **one derive from eating ritually impure *teruma* on the rest of the days of the year,** and yet nevertheless the non-priest is still obligated to **pay** for what he has taken. Despite the fact that a priest may not eat impure *teruma*, a non-priest must reimburse the priest for the principal of the *teruma* and add an additional fifth if he eats it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּתְרוּמָה טְמֵאָה בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה, יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה, תֹּאמַר בָּזֶה — שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ לֹא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה וְלֹא הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה! הָא לְמָה זֶה דּוֹמֶה — לִתְרוּמַת תּוּתִים וַעֲנָבִים שֶׁנִּטְמְאָה, שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ לֹא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה וְלֹא הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Akiva **said to him: No,** a distinction can be made between these two cases: **If you say** that he is obligated to pay **in** a case of **ritually impure *teruma* on the rest of the days of the year, that although it is not permissible to eat it,** the priest **is** nevertheless **permitted to burn it** and derive benefit from the heat generated as a result of this burning, **shall you** also **say** the same **with regard to this,** *teruma* of leavened bread during Passover, **that is not permitted to be eaten or burned? Rather, to what may this be compared?** It is similar **to *teruma* of berries and grapes that became ritually impure, which is not permitted to be eaten or burned,** as berries and grapes are unfit for firewood.

The *gemara* then presents a *tannaitic* dispute that sharpens the analysis. *Ochel terumat chametz be-Pesach — patur min ha-tashlumim u-mi-d'mei etzim, divrei Rabbi Akiva* — one who eats *terumah chametz* on Pesach is exempt from repayment and also from paying for the fuel value of the wood; this is the ruling of Rabbi Akiva. *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri mechayev* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri obligates payment.

Rabbi Akiva challenges: *ve-chi ma hana'ah yesh lo bah* — what benefit does one derive from chametz on Pesach that would justify an obligation to repay? Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri counters: *ma hana'ah yesh le'ochel terumah temeiah bi-sh'ar kol ha-shanah shemeshallem lo* — and what benefit does one derive from eating *terumah temei'ah* during the rest of the year, yet one must still pay? The rest of the year, *terumah temei'ah* is also forbidden to eat, and yet restitution is obligated.

Rabbi Akiva deflects this challenge: the cases are not parallel. With *terumah temei'ah* during the year, *she'af al pi she'ein lo bah heter achilah yesh lo bah heter hasakah* — even though it may not be eaten, it may still be used as fuel. There is some permissible use. But for *terumah chametz* on Pesach, *she'ein lo bah lo heter achilah ve-lo heter hasakah* — according to the position that chametz is *assur be-hana'ah*, there is no permitted use whatsoever. He compares it to *terumah* of strawberries and grapes that became *tamei*: neither edible nor useful as fuel, and thus entirely without value.

### Harkonus Equation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בַּמֶּה דְּבָרִים אֲמוּרִים — בְּמַפְרִישׁ תְּרוּמָה וְהֶחֱמִיצָה, אֲבָל מַפְרִישׁ תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ — דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל אֵינָהּ קְדוֹשָׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The *Tosefta* adds: **In what** case **is this statement said,** that these *tanna'im* disagree about the reimbursement for *teruma*? It was said with regard to a case where **he separated *teruma*** in a permitted manner **and it became leavened** during Passover. **However, if he separated the *teruma* from leavened bread during Passover,** then **everyone agrees that it is not consecrated,** as it is worthless.

The *gemara* then asks: even granting all this, why would the *mezid* be exempt from paying even *d'mei etzim* — the fuel value — according to Rabbi Yose HaGelili, who holds that chametz retains value on Pesach? The answer lies in the principle articulated by Harkonus ben Hakanas. *Harkonus ben Hakanas haya oseh et Yom ha-Kippurim ke-Shabbat le-tashlumim* — he equated Yom Kippur with Shabbat for purposes of *kim lei b'drabbah minei*. The point is this: one might have thought that *kim lei b'drabbah minei* applies clearly in cases of *karet* (as for Shabbat violation) but not necessarily in cases of *misah bi-yedei shamayim* (as for Yom Kippur), since they carry different levels of severity. Harkonus ben Hakanas rules that *kim lei b'drabbah minei* applies in all such cases equally — whether the Heavenly penalty is *karet* or *misah bi-yedei shamayim*, it displaces the accompanying monetary obligation. Therefore, the *mezid* who ate *terumah chametz* on Pesach, having incurred the graver penalty, is exempt from financial restitution even according to Rabbi Yose HaGelili.

### Kedushah of Chametz

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: ״וְנָתַן לַכֹּהֵן אֶת הַקֹּדֶשׁ״ — דָּבָר הָרָאוּי לִהְיוֹת קֹדֶשׁ. פְּרָט לָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח, שֶׁפָּטוּר מִן הַתַּשְׁלוּמִים וּמִדְּמֵי עֵצִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב. וְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַסָּמָא מְחַיֵּיב. אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב לְרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַסָּמָא: וְכִי מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ? אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר חַסָּמָא לְרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב: וְכִי מָה הֲנָאָה יֵשׁ לוֹ לָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמָה טְמֵאָה בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, שֶׁמְּשַׁלֵּם?
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to the verse "And if a man eats a sacred thing in error, then he shall add a fifth part in addition to it, **and shall give to the priest the sacred item"** (Leviticus 22:14), the Sages expound as follows: He must give the priest **an item that is fit to be consecrated, to the exclusion of one who eats *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover, who is exempt from payment** of the *teruma* and even from paying **its monetary** value **as wood;** this is the statement of **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov. Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma deems** him **liable** to reimburse the priest for these items. **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov said to Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma: What benefit can** the priest **derive from this** *teruma* of leavened bread, as it is prohibited to benefit from it? **Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma said to Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov: What benefit can one derive from eating ritually impure *teruma* on the rest of the days of the year,** and yet a non-priest who eats it must **pay** the priest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ: לֹא! אִם אָמַרְתָּ בִּתְרוּמָה טְמֵאָה בִּשְׁאָר יְמוֹת הַשָּׁנָה, שֶׁאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה, תֹּאמַר בָּזוֹ — שֶׁאֵין לוֹ בָּהּ לֹא הֶיתֵּר אֲכִילָה וְלֹא הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה! אָמַר לוֹ: אַף בְּזוֹ יֵשׁ לוֹ בָּהּ הֶיתֵּר הַסָּקָה, שֶׁאִם רָצָה הַכֹּהֵן — מְרִיצָהּ לִפְנֵי כַּלְבּוֹ, אוֹ מַסִּיקָהּ תַּחַת תַּבְשִׁילוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov **said to him: No,** a distinction can be made between these two cases: **If you say** he is obligated to pay **in** a case of **ritually impure *teruma* on the rest of the days of year, that although it is not permissible to eat it,** the priest **is** nonetheless **permitted to burn it** and derive benefit from the heat generated as a result of this burning, **shall you say** the same **with regard to this,** *teruma* of leavened bread during Passover, **that is not permitted to be eaten or burned?** Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma **said to him: Even** *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover **is permitted to be burned, for if the priest wishes, he may throw it before his dog or burn it under his food,** for Rabbi Elazar Ḥisma agrees with the opinion of Rabbi Yosei HaGelili that one may derive benefit from leavened bread during Passover.

A further distinction is introduced: *bamah devarim amurim be-mafresh terumah ve-himtzi'ah* — the foregoing discussion applies specifically where one separated *terumah* from wheat and it subsequently became *chametz*, or where the *terumah* was separated before Pesach from already-leavened dough, at a time when the transaction could be valid. *Aval mafresh terumat chametz* — if, however, one separates *terumah* from chametz at a time when it is forbidden, *divrei ha-kol einah kedushah* — by all opinions the *terumah* does not attain sanctified status at all.

This is derived from the verse *ve-natan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — *davar ha-ra'uy lihyot kodesh*, the item given must be of a type fit to attain *kedushah*. Chametz that is *assur be-hana'ah* cannot become sanctified, and therefore cannot become *terumah* if separated as such *ab initio*. This is the position of Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov, who on this basis rules that one who eats *terumah chametz* on Pesach is *patur min ha-tashlumim u-mi-d'mei etzim* — the underlying *kedushah* was never established. Rabbi Elazar Chisma disagrees and holds one liable.

Rabbi Elazar Chisma then reproduces, nearly verbatim, the exchange between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Rabbi Akiva: what about *terumah temei'ah* the rest of the year, where one still pays? Rabbi Elazar ben Yaakov responds in kind: the rest of the year, *terumah temei'ah* retains fuel value — *yesh lo bah heter hasakah* — whereas *terumah chametz* on Pesach does not. Rabbi Elazar Chisma, however, contends: *af zo yesh lo bah heter hasakah* — even this has fuel value, citing *she'im matza'o kohen mereita fney k'vo'o masikah tachat tavshilo* — if a *kohen* found it, he may feed it to his dog or use it to heat his cooking pot. He holds that some *hana'ah* remains permissible, and therefore value exists and restitution is owed.

### Joint Chamutz View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח אָסוּר בַּהֲנָאָה, וּבְהָא פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם, וְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי סָבַר: לְפִי מִדָּה מְשַׁלֵּם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said: Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov, Rabbi Akiva, and Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri all hold** that **it is forbidden to** derive **benefit from leavened bread during Passover. And they disagree with regard to the following** issue: **Rabbi Akiva holds** that **one pays according to the monetary** value, and therefore he need not pay anything for consuming *teruma* of leavened bread during Passover. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri holds** that **one pays according to the measure** of *teruma* that he consumed, such that even if he ate *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover he must repay this amount.

On *amud bet*, Abaye provides a sweeping clarification of where all these opinions actually stand. *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov, Rabbi Akiva, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri — kulam sevira lehu chametz shel Pesach assur be-hana'ah* — all of them hold that chametz on Pesach is forbidden from benefit. Their dispute is not about whether chametz has value. They all agree it does not. The dispute between Rabbi Akiva and Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri concerns a different question entirely.

### Abaye's Clarification

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּשִׁיטָא! מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא: רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי נָמֵי כְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סְבִירָא לֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר לְפִי דָמִים מְשַׁלֵּם. וְהָתָם הַיְינוּ טַעְמָא דְּקָא מְחַיֵּיב — מִשּׁוּם דְּסָבַר לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יוֹסֵי הַגְּלִילִי, דְּאָמַר: חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח מוּתָּר בַּהֲנָאָה, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: Isn't this **obvious?** There does not seem to be another way to explain these opinions. The Gemara rejects this question: This statement is necessary **lest you say** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri also holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Akiva** that states that **one** must **pay according to the monetary** value of the *teruma*. **And there,** in the case of leavened bread, **this is the reason that** Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri **deems him liable** to pay for the *teruma* **because he holds in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Yosei HaGelili, who said: It is permissible to** derive **benefit from leavened bread during Passover.** Therefore, **he teaches us** that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri agrees that one may not derive benefit from leavened bread during Passover.

*U've-ha plagei: d'Rabbi Akiva savar lefi damim meshalem, ve-Yochanan ben Nuri savar lefi midah meshalem* — their dispute is precisely about whether *terumah* restitution is calculated by value or by quantity. According to Rabbi Akiva, one pays *lefi damim*. Since chametz on Pesach has no value, and since payment is by value, there is nothing to pay — hence *patur*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri holds one pays *lefi midah*: I consumed a particular quantity, and I must return that quantity regardless of its market value. Since I took a *kor* of chametz *terumah*, I must return a *kor* of equivalent *terumah* produce — and value is irrelevant.

One might have supposed otherwise — that Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri obligates payment because he holds, like Rabbi Yose HaGelili, that chametz on Pesach is *mutar be-hana'ah* and thus retains value even *lefi damim*. Abaye rules this out: *ka mashma lan* that this is not his reasoning. All these authorities reject Rabbi Yose HaGelili's position. The dispute is purely structural — *midah* versus *damim* — and Abaye establishes that as the correct framing of the entire *tannaitic* debate.

## Terumah Minimums

### Kezayit vs. Perutah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל כְּזַיִת תְּרוּמָה מְשַׁלֵּם קֶרֶן וָחוֹמֶשׁ, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. מַאי טַעְמָא דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא — אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְאִישׁ כִּי יֹאכַל קֹדֶשׁ בִּשְׁגָגָה״, וַאֲכִילָה בִּכְזַיִת.
>
> **Translation:** After mentioning cases where a person damages *teruma*, the Gemara continues with a discussion of this topic. **The Rabbis taught:** A non-priest **who eats an olive-bulk of *teruma*** must **pay the principal** value of the *teruma* itself **and an** additional **fifth. Abba Shaul says:** He is not required to pay **unless** the *teruma* he ate is **worth a *peruta*.** The Gemara explains: **What is the reason for** the opinion of **the first *tanna*?** It is because **the verse states: "And if a man eats a sacred item in error,** then he shall add a fifth part in addition to it, and he shall give to the priest the sacred item" (Leviticus 22:14). The minimal amount that is halakhically considered **eating is an olive-bulk.**

A *baraita* is now introduced to address the general law of *terumah* restitution: *ochel kezayit terumah meshalem keren ve-chomesh* — one who eats a *kezayit* of *terumah* must pay principal and fifth. This is the view of the *Tanna Kamma*. Abba Saul, however, qualifies: *ad she-yehei bo shaveh perutah* — not so fast; the obligation does not apply unless the item consumed is worth at least one *perutah*, the smallest recognized unit of monetary value. The question now becomes whether these are two independent conditions — one requiring a minimum *kezayit* in size, the other requiring a minimum *perutah* in value — or whether one supersedes the other.

### Tanna Kamma Source

The *Tanna Kamma*'s position is derived from the verse: *ve'ish ki yochal kodesh bi-shgagah* — if a man eats *kodesh* (here referring to *terumah*) inadvertently. The operative word is *yochal*, eating. The minimum measure of *achilah* recognized by *halacha* is a *kezayit* — an olive's volume. The *Tanna Kamma* therefore anchors the restitution obligation in the act of eating, setting the quantity threshold accordingly. Value, on this view, is not the operative factor.

### Abba Saul Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר קְרָא: ״וְנָתַן״, וְאֵין נְתִינָה פָּחוֹת מִשָּׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה. וְאִידַּךְ נָמֵי, הָא כְּתִיב ״יֹאכַל״! הָהוּא, פְּרָט לְמַזִּיק הוּא דַּאֲתָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And what is the reason for** the opinion of **Abba Shaul? The verse states: "And he shall give," and** giving **less than the value of a *peruta* is not** legally considered to be **giving.** The Gemara asks: **And** according to **the other** one, Abba Shaul, **too, isn't it written: "Eats,"** implying that there must be at least an olive-bulk portion? The Gemara answers: **That** verse **comes to exclude one who damages** *teruma* without deriving benefit from it, such that he is exempt from the requirement to add an additional fifth. This is derived from the fact that the verse specifies that only one who eats is required to add a fifth.

Abba Saul derives his position from a different clause of the same verse: *ve-natan la-kohen et ha-kodesh* — and he shall give to the *kohen* the *kodesh*. The word *ve-natan*, giving, appears in multiple contexts where the *halacha* has established that an act of giving is legally meaningful only when it involves something worth at least a *perutah* — *ve-ein netinah pachos mi-shaveh perutah*. Giving something worthless is not a *netinah* in any legally cognizable sense. Abba Saul therefore holds that if the *terumah* consumed was not worth a *perutah*, no restitution obligation arises, regardless of how large a quantity was consumed.

### Tanna Kamma Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתַנָּא קַמָּא, הָכְתִיב ״וְנָתַן״! הַהוּא מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ לְדָבָר הָרָאוּי לִהְיוֹת קֹדֶשׁ (פְּרָט לָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמַת חָמֵץ בַּפֶּסַח).
>
> **Translation:** **And** according to **the first *tanna*,** one can ask: **Isn't it written "And he shall give"?** The Gemara answers: **That** phrase **is necessary** to teach the requirement that *teruma* must be **an item that is fit to be consecrated,** as an item cannot become *teruma* unless it is has some value. This is meant **to exclude one who eats *teruma* of leavened bread on Passover,** since it is worthless and therefore cannot be designated as *teruma*.

Abba Saul's *perutah* requirement would seem to raise a difficulty for the *Tanna Kamma*: the verse also says *ve-natan*, and yet the *Tanna Kamma* does not require a minimum value of a *perutah*. The *Tanna Kamma* resolves this: *ve-natan* teaches something else entirely — *davar ha-ra'uy lihyot kodesh* — the item given in repayment must be of a type that can attain *kedushah*. This is what excludes, for example, paying back *terumah* with chametz on Pesach, or with anything else that cannot become sanctified. The verse's giving-language establishes a qualitative requirement on the repayment, not a minimum monetary floor.

### Abba Saul Response

Abba Saul, for his part, must contend with the *Tanna Kamma*'s reading of *yochal* as setting a *kezayit* threshold. Abba Saul reads *yochal* as *prat le-mazik* — eating is specified to exclude the case of one who damages or destroys the *terumah* without consuming it. A destroyer pays the principal but not the additional fifth, since the verse specifies *achilah*. The *yochal* clause does not, for Abba Saul, establish a minimum quantity for the eating-based obligation; it simply carves out the case of destruction from the *chomesh* requirement. The minimum quantity threshold, therefore, does not follow from this verse at all on Abba Saul's reading, and his sole operative criterion remains the *perutah* of value.

## Sub-Kezayit Cases

### Sub-Kezayit Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: הָאוֹכֵל תְּרוּמָה פָּחוֹת מִכְּזַיִת — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ. הֵיכִי דָּמֵי? אִי דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — קֶרֶן נָמֵי לָא לִישַׁלֵּם, וְאִי דְּאִית בֵּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — חוֹמֶשׁ נָמֵי לִישַׁלֵּם! לְעוֹלָם דְּאִית בֵּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, וַאֲפִילּוּ הָכִי, כֵּיוָן דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ כְּזַיִת — מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַקֶּרֶן וְאֵינוֹ מְשַׁלֵּם אֶת הַחוֹמֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **One who eats less than an olive-bulk of *teruma* must pay the principal, but is not** required **to pay the** additional **fifth.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of this case? **If there was not the value of a *peruta*** of *teruma*, then **he should also not** be required to **pay for the principal** either, because that is less than the amount for which one is obligated to pay. **But if there was the value of a *peruta*** of *teruma*, then **he should** be required to **pay the** additional **fifth as well.** The Gemara explains the case: **Actually,** it should be understood **that there was the value of a *peruta*** of *teruma*, **and nonetheless, since** the food **was not** at least **an olive-bulk, he is** required **to pay** only **the principal, but he does not pay the** additional **fifth.**

A further *baraita* addresses an intermediate scenario: *ha'ochel terumah pachos mi-kezayit meshalem et ha-keren ve-eino meshalem et ha-chomesh* — one who eats less than a *kezayit* of *terumah* pays the principal but is not required to pay the additional fifth.

### Value & Size

The *gemara* immediately asks: how is this case to be understood? If the item consumed is not worth a *perutah*, the principal should not be owed either. And if it is worth a *perutah*, why is the fifth not owed as well? The *gemara* resolves: *le'olam d'it beh shaveh perutah* — the case involves an item that is indeed worth a *perutah* in value. Nevertheless, since it lacks the minimum *kezayit* in size, one pays only the principal and is exempt from the *chomesh*. The logic is that the *chomesh* requirement is tied to the eating-act threshold, which demands a *kezayit*, while the basic principal obligation follows simply from having taken something of value.

### Dual Requirement?

This ruling raises a pointed question regarding Abba Saul's position. Abba Saul holds that the operative factor is value — a *perutah*. Does he also require, in addition, that the consumed item meet the *kezayit* size threshold? Or does he hold that value alone is sufficient, in which case even a sub-*kezayit* item worth a *perutah* would trigger the full *keren ve-chomesh* obligation?

### Rav Papa Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא הָא דְּלָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, דְּאִי כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, הָאָמַר: כֵּיוָן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בָּהּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלֵית בֵּיהּ כְּזַיִת. אָמַר לְהוּ רַב פָּפָּא: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אַבָּא שָׁאוּל, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל תַּרְתֵּי בָּעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said before Rav Pappa** that **this** *halakha* **is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul. As, if it were in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, didn't he say:** One is obligated to pay **because there is the value of a *peruta*, even if it is not** at least **an olive-bulk? Rav Pappa said to them:** This is no proof, as **even** if **you say** that this *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Shaul, Abba Shaul requires two** conditions: That the *teruma* be at least an olive-bulk in volume, and that it be worth at least a *peruta*.

Rav Papa initially rules that even Abba Saul requires both conditions: *Abba Saul tarti ba'ei* — Abba Saul requires both value and size. Thus, even according to Abba Saul, consuming less than a *kezayit* — even if it is worth a *perutah* — would result in the same ruling as the *baraita*: principal yes, *chomesh* no. Under this reading, both the *Tanna Kamma* and Abba Saul converge on requiring both a *kezayit* and a *perutah* in order for the full obligation to apply; they differ only in which of these two conditions generates the affirmative obligation, and which functions as an additional limiting criterion.

### Refutation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי בָּעֵי אַבָּא שָׁאוּל תַּרְתֵּי? וְהָא תְּנַן, אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אֶת שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — חַיָּיב בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין, אֶת שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב בְּתַשְׁלוּמִין. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא אָמְרוּ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן מְעִילָה בִּלְבַד, אֲבָל לִתְרוּמָה — אֵינוֹ חַיָּיב עַד שֶׁיְּהֵא בּוֹ כְּזַיִת. וְאִם אִיתָא, ״כֵּיוָן שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ כְּזַיִת״ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ. תְּיוּבְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Does Abba Shaul** actually **require two** conditions? **Didn't we learn** in the mishna that **Abba Shaul says:** For **that** food **which is** at least **the value of a *peruta*** of *teruma*, **one is liable to** pay **compensation** to the priest, but for **that** food **which does not contain the value of a *peruta*** of *teruma*, **he is not liable to** pay **compensation** to the priest? The Rabbis **said to** Abba Shaul: **They said** that the item must be **worth a *peruta* only with regard to misuse** of consecrated items; **however, with regard to *teruma*, one is liable** to reimburse the priest **only when he eats an olive-bulk** or more. **And if it is so,** that Abba Shaul requires both conditions, and this is a case where there is an olive-bulk, then the Rabbis should have worded their objection differently. **They should** have said: **Since it is** at least **an olive-bulk,** he is liable to pay, even though it is not worth a *peruta*. The Gemara concludes: Indeed, this is **a conclusive refutation,** and Rav Pappa's position is rejected.

This position is immediately challenged. The *mishnah* explicitly quotes Abba Saul: *et she-yesh bo shaveh perutah chayav be-tashlumim, et she-ein bo shaveh perutah eino chayav be-tashlumim* — if it is worth a *perutah*, one is liable; if not, one is not liable. The criterion presented is solely *shaveh perutah*, with no mention of *kezayit*. If Abba Saul truly required both, the *mishnah* should have said *keivan she-yesh bo kezayit* — since it has a *kezayit* — rather than framing the obligation purely in terms of *perutah*.

The *gemara* concludes: *tiyuvta* — this is a decisive refutation of Rav Papa's position. And indeed, *af Rav Papa hadar bei* — Rav Papa himself retracted his view. The upshot is that the *Tanna Kamma* and Abba Saul represent genuinely competing, mutually exclusive positions. The *Tanna Kamma* requires a *kezayit* — size alone is the operative criterion. Abba Saul requires a *perutah* — value alone is the operative criterion. Each condition is sufficient on its own view and neither opinion demands both simultaneously.
