# Pesachim 48 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Rava vs Rav Chisda

### Cooking Yom Tov Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּיוֹם טוֹב, כֹּהֵן וְנָזִיר אַבֵּית הַטּוּמְאָה. וְאִי אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״ — אַהַבְעָרָה לָא לִיחַיַּיב, הוֹאִיל וְחָזֵי לְכִיסּוּי דַּם צִיפּוֹר.
>
> **Translation:** **on a Festival.** Additionally, the person plowing is **a priest and a nazirite,** and he is plowing **a place of ritual impurity,** i.e., a burial site. It is prohibited for a priest and a nazirite to become impure by walking over the burial spot of a corpse. Therefore, the one plowing commits two transgressions simply by traversing the field. **And if we say** the principle: **Since, etc., he should not be liable for kindling** a fire unnecessarily, **since** the fire **is fit for covering the blood of a bird.** One who slaughters a bird or a non-domesticated animal is required by Torah law to cover the blood. Since it is possible that one will need to slaughter many such animals or birds and will not have enough dirt to cover their blood, his plowing may end up facilitating covering the blood. Therefore, it should not be considered a prohibited labor on the Festival.

The daf opens in the midst of an ongoing discussion that has occupied nearly two full pages of Gemara. By way of orientation: the *mishna* earlier addressed the question of how one separates *challah* from *tamei* dough on *Pesach*. The predicament is well-defined — one cannot bake the dough because it is *tamei* and therefore inedible, yet one cannot leave it unbaked because it will become *chametz*. Two opinions emerged. *Rabbi Eliezer* held that one bakes the dough and only afterward designates one loaf as *challah*, so that at the moment of baking no loaf has yet been formally rendered *challah* and the act of baking is therefore not yet a problem. *Rabbi Yehoshua* countered that there is in any case no concern about violating *bal yera'eh* and *bal yimatzei*, since the *challah* is *hekdesh* — a form of consecrated property — and does not belong to the owner in a way that triggers those prohibitions.

Before returning to that debate directly, the Gemara has been engaged with a foundational disagreement between *Rava* and *Rav Chisda* about cooking on *Yom Tov* for a weekday meal. The connection will shortly become apparent, but the discussion of *Rava* and *Rav Chisda* must first be understood on its own terms.

### Rava's Ho'il Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמְבַשֵּׁל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה בְּחָלָב בְּיוֹם טוֹב וְאוֹכְלוֹ — לוֹקֶה חָמֵשׁ. לוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל גִּיד בְּיוֹם טוֹב, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם אוֹכֵל גִּיד, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם מְבַשֵּׁל בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם אוֹכֵל בָּשָׂר בְּחָלָב, וְלוֹקֶה מִשּׁוּם הַבְעָרָה. וְאִי אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״ — אַהַבְעָרָה לָא לִיחַיַּיב, הוֹאִיל דַּחֲזֵי לֵיהּ לְצׇרְכּוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **One who cooks the sciatic nerve in milk on a Festival and eats it is flogged** for **five** distinct prohibitions. How so? **He is flogged due to** the prohibition of **cooking the** sciatic **nerve on a Festival,** which is prohibited because the sciatic nerve is unfit for consumption; **and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **eating** the sciatic **nerve,** which is explicitly prohibited by the Torah; **and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **cooking meat in milk; and he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **eating meat** cooked **in milk; and** lastly, **he is flogged due to** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire unnecessarily on a Festival. **And if we say** the principle: **Since, etc., he should not be liable for kindling** a fire unnecessarily, **since** the fire **is fit for** use in attending to his legitimate Festival needs, e.g., cooking permitted foods.

The practical question is whether someone who cooks on *Yom Tov* intending to eat the food on a weekday receives *malkot*. *Rava* holds that he does not, on the basis of the principle of *ho'il* — literally "since," functioning here as a kind of hypothetical sufficiency. The argument is: since, if a large number of guests were to arrive unexpectedly, the food could in fact be consumed on *Yom Tov* itself, the act of cooking is classified as *tzorech Yom Tov* — cooking for the holiday's needs — even though that was not the cook's actual intention. The theoretical permissibility suffices to render the act legitimate.

### Rav Chisda's Intent Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַפֵּיק הַבְעָרָה, וְעַיֵּיל גִּיד הַנָּשֶׁה שֶׁל נְבֵילָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rabba **said to him: Remove** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire from this list **and add** the prohibition of eating **a sciatic nerve from an animal carcass** that was not properly slaughtered.

*Rav Chisda* disagrees sharply. For him, *ho'il* is not a valid basis for permitting the act. What is required is actual intention to eat the food on *Yom Tov* itself; cooking with the plan to eat on a weekday constitutes a genuine violation and subjects the person to *malkot*. The theoretical possibility of consuming the food now does not transform the nature of the act.

### Furrow Challenge

A challenge to *Rava's* position is raised — though the Gemara presents it without recording *Rava's* own response. If *ho'il* is sufficient to exempt cooking because the food could hypothetically be eaten now, then by the same logic, one who digs a furrow on *Yom Tov* should also be exempt, since the dirt could theoretically be used to cover the blood of a freshly slaughtered bird — a legitimate *Yom Tov* purpose. Yet digging a furrow is a quintessential prohibited labor with no culinary character, and it stretches the principle of *ho'il* beyond the domain of food preparation into acts that are fundamentally not *ochel nefesh*. The Gemara engages with this tension, and we proceed from there.

## Five Lashes Mishna

### Gid Hanasheh Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתָנֵי רַבִּי חִיָּיא: לוֹקִין שְׁתַּיִם עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ, וְשָׁלֹשׁ עַל בִּישּׁוּלוֹ. וְאִי אִיתָא, שָׁלֹשׁ עַל אֲכִילָתוֹ מִיבְּעֵי לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** Abaye responded: **Didn't Rabbi Ḥiyya teach** with regard to this mishna: He **is flogged twice for his eating,** as he violated the prohibitions of eating the sciatic nerve and eating meat cooked in milk, **and three** times **for his cooking,** as he violated the prohibitions of kindling a fire, cooking on a Festival, and cooking meat in milk? **And if it is** so, that the case is one in which the sciatic nerve was taken from an animal carcass, Rabbi Ḥiyya **should have** stated that he is flogged **three** times **for his eating,** as he violated the prohibitions of eating a sciatic nerve, eating meat cooked in milk, and eating an animal carcass.

The more immediately pressing issue is a *beraita* that presents a scenario in which one can incur five separate sets of *malkot* through a single act of cooking on *Yom Tov* — for example, cooking the *gid hanasheh* of an animal. The list of five includes the prohibition of *gid hanasheh* itself, *basar bechalav*, and other forbidden categories, along with — according to the *beraita* as initially read — making a fire on *Yom Tov* without a legitimate purpose. The question pressed against *Rava* is immediate: according to his principle of *ho'il*, one should never incur *malkot* for making a fire on *Yom Tov*, since hypothetically the fire could always serve some legitimate function. Why then does the *beraita* list it as a separate category meriting *malkot*?

This is a particularly resonant challenge because we do in practice grant considerable latitude for making fire on *Yom Tov* even without a specific food-preparation purpose in mind. The Gemara's answer to this challenge is direct: *Rava* is correct, and the fire entry must simply be removed from the list. The *beraita* cannot be counting *hav'ara* as a distinct lashing category — and something else must be substituted in its place.

### Ein Chiluk Melachot

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דַּאֲמַרְתְּ: בְּעַאי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב חִסְדָּא, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: בְּעַאי מִינֵּיהּ מֵרַב הוּנָא: הֵבִיא שֶׂה מֵאֲפָר, וּשְׁחָטוֹ תָּמִיד בְּיוֹם טוֹב, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: Wasn't it you who said: I raised a dilemma before Rav Ḥisda, and some say** the correct version is: **I raised a dilemma before Rav Huna:** If **one brought a lamb from the meadow,** and the lamb is therefore set-aside because it was not designated for use before the Festival, **and he slaughtered it** as the **daily** offering **on a Festival, what is** the *halakha*? Is it permissible to offer this sacrifice?

On *Shabbos*, the thirty-nine categories of *melacha* are each treated as distinct for the purpose of *korbanot*: one who violates multiple categories *beshogeg* may be liable for up to thirty-nine separate sin-offerings. The categories are meaningful because of their relevance to atonement.

On *Yom Tov*, however, there is no *karet* and no *mita* for forbidden labor — *Yom Tov* carries only a *lo ta'aseh* and an *aseh*. Since there is no *karet*, there is no *korban chatat*, and therefore the entire framework of distinct labor categories has no purchase. The only practical consequence of intentional *melacha* on *Yom Tov* in this world is *malkot*, and for *malkot* there is no distinction among the thirty-nine categories. It is a single prohibition — performing *melacha* on *Yom Tov* — that one either violates or does not, regardless of which specific labor is involved.

### Fire Not Separate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְעוֹד, הָא אַתְּ הוּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ: חִילּוּק מְלָאכוֹת לְשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין חִילּוּק מְלָאכוֹת לְיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** Abaye said to Rabba: It is possible to challenge your interpretation of the *baraita* from a different angle. **Wasn't it you who said,** as cited in tractate *Makkot*, that there is **division of labors** with regard **to Shabbat,** and therefore one is required to bring two sin-offerings if he performed two prohibited labors of different primary categories in one lapse of awareness, or if he performed a prohibited labor twice, during separate lapses of awareness; however, there is **no division of labors** with regard **to a Festival,** and therefore one is not punished with multiple floggings for performing multiple prohibited labors? Consequently, how could one be liable for multiple floggings for the prohibition of utilizing set-aside materials and for cooking the sciatic nerve on a Festival?

The implication is decisive. If one dug thirty-nine furrows on *Yom Tov* — or performed one of each of the thirty-nine categories — one receives only a single set of *malkot*, not thirty-nine. The *beraita*'s project of identifying five qualitatively distinct lashing categories is therefore not served by listing *hav'ara* alongside cooking; they are both violations of the same prohibition of *melacha* on *Yom Tov* and do not generate separate *malkot*. Even before the *ho'il* argument is invoked, *hav'ara* simply does not belong in the list.

### Nazir Wine Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אַפֵּיק הַבְעָרָה, וְעַיֵּיל עֲצֵי מוּקְצֶה. וּמוּקְצֶה דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא? אֲמַר לוֹ: אִין, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיָה בַּיּוֹם הַשִּׁשִּׁי וְהֵכִינוּ אֵת אֲשֶׁר יָבִיאוּ״. וְאַזְהָרָתַהּ מֵהָכָא, מִ״לֹּא תַעֲשֶׂה כׇל מְלָאכָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rabba said: **Remove** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire **and add** in its place the prohibition of using **wood** that has been **set aside** from use on the Festival. The Gemara asks: Is the prohibition against utilizing **set-aside** material prohibited **by Torah** law, such that a person is flogged for violating this prohibition? **He said to him: Yes, as it is written: "And it shall come to pass on the sixth day that they shall prepare that which they bring in"** (Exodus 16:5). This verse teaches that anything that has not been prepared before the Festival is considered to be set-aside, and it is prohibited to utilize it. This verse indicates that utilizing set-aside objects is prohibited; however, it does not formulate this prohibition as a negative commandment. Therefore, the Gemara adds that **the warning** indicating that it is a negative commandment **is from here: "You shall not perform any labor"** (Exodus 20:10). This general statement pertaining to Shabbat includes utilizing objects that were not set aside for use before Shabbat.

One might ask whether the problem could be solved by separate *hatra'ot* — independent warnings — rather than by categorically distinct prohibitions. The Gemara alludes to the well-known *mishna* regarding a *nazir* who drinks wine throughout the day: if separate warnings are administered between each act of drinking, separate sets of *malkot* are incurred. The same principle could in theory apply here. But the Gemara dismisses this: the *beraita*'s purpose is to enumerate qualitatively different *isurim* that produce separate *malkot* in a principled way, not to describe the same prohibition triggered multiple times through repeated *hatra'ot*. A *beraita* could construct any number of such scenarios by simply counting repeated warnings; what makes the five-lashes *beraita* interesting and instructive is that it identifies five genuinely distinct prohibitions. *Hav'ara* does not satisfy that criterion, and the attempted substitution of *muktza* wood likewise fails, since *muktza* is not *de-oraita* and therefore cannot generate its own independent lashing category.

## Replacing Fire Category

### Muktza Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ, וְהָא אַתְּ הוּא דַּאֲמַרְתְּ: חִילּוּק מְלָאכוֹת לְשַׁבָּת, וְאֵין חִילּוּק מְלָאכוֹת לְיוֹם טוֹב.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** Abaye said to Rabba: It is possible to challenge your interpretation of the *baraita* from a different angle. **Wasn't it you who said,** as cited in tractate *Makkot*, that there is **division of labors** with regard **to Shabbat,** and therefore one is required to bring two sin-offerings if he performed two prohibited labors of different primary categories in one lapse of awareness, or if he performed a prohibited labor twice, during separate lapses of awareness; however, there is **no division of labors** with regard **to a Festival,** and therefore one is not punished with multiple floggings for performing multiple prohibited labors? Consequently, how could one be liable for multiple floggings for the prohibition of utilizing set-aside materials and for cooking the sciatic nerve on a Festival?

The first proposed replacement for *hav'ara* in the *beraita*'s list of five is *muktza* wood. If one uses wood that was *muktza* — set aside and designated for a use other than burning — to make the fire, that might constitute a separate prohibited act distinct from the *melacha* of cooking. This would circumvent the *ho'il* problem, since *muktza* wood has no theoretical alternative use; one cannot invoke *ho'il* to say it could hypothetically have served another purpose, because as *muktza* it is precluded from any other use. The proposal fails, however, because the Gemara concludes that *muktza* is not *de-oraita*. Without biblical standing, it cannot anchor an independent category of *malkos de-oraita*, and the attempt is rejected.

### Avoda Zara Wood

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אַפֵּיק הַבְעָרָה, וְעַיֵּיל עֲצֵי אֲשֵׁירָה. וְאַזְהָרָה מֵהָכָא, ״וְלֹא יִדְבַּק בְּיָדְךָ מְאוּמָה מִן הַחֵרֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** in order to make this *baraita* consistent with Rabba's opinion, **remove** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire **and add** in its place the prohibition of using **the wood of a tree designated for idolatry [*asheira*]. And the warning,** i.e., the source of the negative commandment associated with using this wood, **is** derived **from here,** a verse that relates to an idolatrous city that is burned: **"Nothing from the spoil shall cling to your hand"** (Deuteronomy 13:18).

The Gemara then proposes *atzei ashera* — wood from a tree that had been worshipped as an object of *avoda zara*. If one uses such wood to fuel the fire with which one cooks the *gid hanasheh*, one incurs a separate prohibition of deriving benefit from *avoda zara*, derived from the verse *velo yidbak beyadcha me'uma min hacherem* — nothing from the banned material shall cleave to your hand. This seems a promising candidate: it is a qualitatively distinct *issur* that is unrelated to the *melacha* of cooking, and the *ho'il* argument does not apply because the prohibition is categorical, not contingent on purpose.

However, the Gemara raises a further objection: there are in fact *two* separate prohibitions associated with *avoda zara* wood — the prohibition of benefiting from it, and the distinct prohibition of *velo tavi to'evah el beisecha*, do not bring an abomination into your house. If both apply, then using *atzei ashera* contributes not one but two sets of *malkot* to the count, pushing the total beyond five. The proposal overachieves, and a different replacement is needed.

### Hekdesh Wood Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרָבָא לְאַבָּיֵי: וְנִלְקֵי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם ״וְלֹא תָבִיא תוֹעֵבָה אֶל בֵּיתֶךָ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Aḥa, son of Rava, said to Abaye:** If this case is referring to using wood from an *asheira*, **one should also be flogged due to** having transgressed the prohibition of: **"You shall not bring an abominable thing into your home"** (Deuteronomy 7:26). However, this would add an extra negative commandment to the list enumerated in the *baraita*.

The final solution offered is *atzei hekdesh* — wood belonging to the Temple treasury, i.e., property of *hekdesh*. Using such wood to fuel the fire constitutes a violation derived from the verse *lo ta'asun kein l'Hashem Elokeichem*, understood homiletically to prohibit the destruction or improper use of that which is consecrated to God. This reasoning — which is also foundational to the prohibition against erasing *Shem Hashem* — provides a single, distinct prohibition tied to the sanctity of *hekdesh* property.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא: אַפֵּיק הַבְעָרָה, וְעַיֵּיל עֲצֵי הֶקְדֵּשׁ. וְאַזְהָרָה מֵהָכָא, ״וַאֲשֵׁרֵיהֶם תִּשְׂרְפוּן בָּאֵשׁ ... לֹא תַעֲשׂוּן כֵּן לַה׳ אֱלֹהֵיכֶם״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, remove** the prohibition of **kindling** a fire **and add** in its place the prohibition of burning **consecrated wood. And the warning,** i.e., the source of this negative command, **is from here: "And you shall burn their *asheira*** trees **with fire...you shall not do this to the Lord your God"** (Deuteronomy 12:3–4). Therefore, one who burns a consecrated item in a destructive manner is punished with lashes. In conclusion, no adequate proof can be adduced from the *baraita* to reject Rabba's opinion.

One might wonder why the prohibition of *me'ila* — misappropriating *hekdesh* — is not invoked as an additional category, which would again push the count beyond five. The Gemara does not fully resolve this, though one possible direction is that the benefit derived is attenuated given that the cooking itself is forbidden and the result cannot lawfully be consumed. Regardless, the Gemara appears satisfied with *hekdesh* wood as the fifth category, and the discussion of the fire-and-lashes *beraita* concludes with *hekdesh* wood replacing *hav'ara*.

## Rami bar Chama's Link

### Same Debate Theory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הָא דְּרַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה — מַחֲלוֹקֶת דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הִיא. דְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר סָבַר: אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״. וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ סָבַר: לָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: This** dispute between **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba** with regard to the principle: Since, etc., **is** a matter of **dispute** between **Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua** in the mishna. **As, Rabbi Eliezer,** who says that *ḥalla* should be separated only after the bread has been baked, **holds** that **we say** the principle: **Since, etc.** Since any portion of the dough could potentially be eaten if another part of the dough is designated as *ḥalla*, therefore, one is permitted to bake bread without separating *ḥalla* from it *ab initio*. **And Rabbi Yehoshua holds: We do not say** the principle: **Since, etc.**

With the extended discussion of *Rava* and *Rav Chisda* now concluded, the Gemara circles back to tie that debate to our *mishna*. *Rami bar Chama* proposes that the dispute of *Rav Chisda* and *Rabba* is in fact the same debate as that of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* in the *mishna* about *challah teme'ah*. The equation is intellectually elegant: both debates turn on the principle of *ho'il*, and *Rami bar Chama* offers a framework for understanding what separates the two Tannaitic opinions.

According to *Rami bar Chama*, *Rabbi Eliezer*, who permits baking the dough and subsequently separating *challah*, operates on a principle of *ho'il*. At the moment the loaves are placed in the oven, every single loaf is theoretically edible. The baker does not yet know which loaf will be designated as *challah*; any particular loaf could be the one he eats. Since no specific loaf is definitively forbidden at the time of baking, and since in theory one could even separate *challah* from each loaf individually and thereby render every loaf edible, there is sufficient theoretical permissibility to classify the baking as *tzorech Yom Tov*. This is the structure of *ho'il*: the act is deemed legitimate because it could, under the right conditions, be entirely permissible.

### Yehoshua Rejects Ho'il

*Rabbi Yehoshua*, who would prohibit the baking, rejects *ho'il* as a basis for permission. For him, it is dispositive that one of the loaves will certainly be inedible — that much is guaranteed by the obligation of *hafrashat challah*. The identity of that loaf may be unknown, but its existence is certain. The argument that any *particular* loaf is theoretically edible does not resolve the fact that one loaf *definitely* cannot be eaten. This parallels *Rav Chisda's* position: theoretical possibility does not suffice; what matters is whether the food will actually be eaten on *Yom Tov*.

### Rav Pappa's Distinction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: וְדִילְמָא עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הָתָם דְּאָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, אֶלָּא דִּבְעִידָּנָא דְּקָא עָיְילִי לְתַנּוּרָא כׇּל חֲדָא וַחֲדָא חַזְיָא לֵיהּ לְדִידֵיהּ. אֲבָל הָכָא, דִּלְאוֹרְחִין הוּא דַּחֲזֵי לְדִידֵיהּ לָא חֲזֵי — אֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said** that this claim can be rejected in the following manner: **Perhaps Rabbi Eliezer only stated that we say** the principle: **Since, etc., there,** in the case of the separation of *ḥalla*, **because at the time that it was placed into the oven, each and every** loaf **was fit for him,** and there was no indication as to which loaf he would designate as *ḥalla*. **However, here,** in a case **where** the bread one is baking on the Festival **is fit for guests** but **is not fit for him, say that so too,** even according to the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer **we do not say** the principle: **Since, etc.**

*Rav Pappa* immediately qualifies *Rami bar Chama's* equation, pointing out that the two debates are not symmetrical. The cases differ in at least two significant respects, and conflating them may produce false conclusions about what each disputant would hold in the other's scenario.

In the *challah* case, *Rabbi Eliezer* says *ho'il* because at the moment of placing the loaves in the oven — *be-idana de-ka-ayeil le-tanura* — every single loaf is genuinely fit for the baker's own consumption. The baker wants to eat all of this *matzah*; the only reason one loaf will be forbidden is the subsequent act of designation, which has not yet occurred. The *ho'il* here is grounded in a situation where the person is fundamentally baking *for himself* and *for today*, with the limitation being a single unknown loaf.

This is structurally different from the case of *Rava* and *Rav Chisda*, where the cook has no intention whatsoever of eating the food on *Yom Tov*. The only basis for invoking *ho'il* there is the hypothetical scenario of unexpected guests — *de-orchim hu de-chazei lei, le-didei lo chazi* — for the cook himself it is not fit at all. It is possible that even *Rabbi Eliezer*, who accepts *ho'il* when one is fundamentally baking for personal consumption today, would not accept *ho'il* when one has no intention of eating today and relies solely on the theoretical possibility of guests.

Conversely, the *challah* case has a feature that makes *ho'il* harder to invoke: one loaf is objectively, certainly going to be forbidden, even if its identity is unknown. In the cooking-for-weekday case, everything is theoretically edible — *d'chazi mihat l'orchin* — the only deficit is the absence of actual guests. *Rav Pappa* suggests that this might lead *Rabbi Yehoshua* to actually be *more* lenient in the Rava-Rav Chisda scenario than in his own, since there no loaf is structurally foreclosed.

The upshot is that the two debates have overlapping features but are not identical, and one cannot straightforwardly read the positions of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* as direct precedents for *Rava* and *Rav Chisda*.

### Sheshet's Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שִׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב אִידִי: וְדִילְמָא לָא הִיא, עַד כָּאן לָא קָאָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ הָתָם דְּלָא אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״, אֶלָּא דְּאִיכָּא חֲדָא דְּלָא חַזְיָא לָא לְדִידֵיהּ וְלָא לְאוֹרְחִין. אֲבָל הָכָא, דַּחֲזֵי מִיהַת לְאוֹרְחִין — אֵימָא הָכִי נָמֵי אָמְרִינַן ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheisha, son of Rav Idi,** rejected Rami bar Ḥama's statement for a different reason and **said: Perhaps that is not so. Rabbi Yehoshua only stated that we do not say** the principle: **Since, etc., there, where there is one** portion of the bread **that is not fit for him or for guests,** because the piece that is designated as *ḥalla* cannot be eaten by anyone due to the fact that it is ritually impure. **However, here,** in the case of one who is baking bread during the Festival so that it can be eaten on a weekday, **when it is at least fit for guests, say that so too,** even according to the opinion of Rabbi Yehoshua, **we say** the principle: **Since, etc.**

*Rav Sheshet brei de-Rav Idi* presses the point further from the opposite direction. Just as *Rav Pappa* argued that *Rabbi Eliezer's ho'il* in the *challah* case does not necessarily extend to *Rava's* position, *Rav Sheshet* argues that *Rabbi Yehoshua's* rejection of *ho'il* in the *challah* case does not necessarily correspond to *Rav Chisda's* position either. *Rabbi Yehoshua* refuses to permit the baking because, despite the baker's overall intention to eat most of the loaves, one loaf will definitively be off-limits to anyone — not merely to the baker himself, but to *le-didei* and *le-orchin* alike. That categorical objective prohibition is what drives *Rabbi Yehoshua's* stringency.

In the case of cooking for potential guests, by contrast, the food is theoretically edible by everyone — guests included. The only problem is their hypothetical nature. *Rav Sheshet* suggests it is possible that *Rabbi Yehoshua* would be more willing to accept *ho'il* in that scenario, since nothing is categorically foreclosed. The debates are thus distinguishable in both directions: the *challah* case has a feature that makes *ho'il* easier to invoke (the baker is personally intending to eat most of the food today) and a feature that makes it harder (one loaf is certainly forbidden). Neither *Rabbi Eliezer* nor *Rabbi Yehoshua* can be read as simple stand-ins for *Rava* and *Rav Chisda*.

## Yirmiyah-Zeira Dispute

### Yirmiyah Accepts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַמְרוּהָ [רַבָּנַן] קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יִרְמְיָה וְרַבִּי זֵירָא. רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה קַיבְּלַהּ, רַבִּי זֵירָא לָא קַיבְּלַהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה לְרַבִּי זֵירָא: מִילְּתָא דְּקַשְׁיָא לַן וְאָתְיָא כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר וְרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, הַשְׁתָּא אַמְרוּהָ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּגַבְרָא רַבָּה, וְלָא נִיקַבְּלַהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara recounts: **The Sages said** Rami bar Ḥama's statement **before Rabbi Yirmeya and Rabbi Zeira. Rabbi Yirmeya accepted it** and **Rabbi Zeira did not accept it. Rabbi Yirmeya said to Rabbi Zeira:** The following **matter has been difficult for us** to explain **for several years: With regard to what** principle **did Rabbi Eliezer and Rabbi Yehoshua disagree? Now** an explanation **has been stated in the name of a great man. Shall we not accept it?**

The question of whether the debates are identical was also discussed before *Rabbi Yirmiyah* and *Rabbi Zeira*. *Rabbi Yirmiyah* enthusiastically accepts *Rami bar Chama's* equation. He describes how for many years the scholars had struggled to identify the core logic underlying the debate of *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* — why exactly *Rabbi Eliezer* permits the baking and on what grounds *Rabbi Yehoshua* would object. Now *Rami bar Chama*, a great authority, has provided a compelling framework: the debate is about *ho'il*. *Rabbi Yirmiyah* sees no reason to reject this powerful explanatory proposal.

### Zeira's Silence Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הֵיכִי אֲקַבְּלַהּ, דִּתְנֵינָא, אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, הֲרֵי הוּא עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״לֹא תַעֲשֶׂה כׇל מְלָאכָה״, וּשְׁתֵיק לֵיהּ. וְאִי אִיתָא, לֵימָא לֵיהּ: טַעְמָא דִּידִי מִשּׁוּם ״הוֹאִיל״.
>
> **Translation:** **He said to him: How can I accept it? We** already **learned** in a *baraita* with regard to their dispute: **Rabbi Yehoshua said to** Rabbi Eliezer: **According to your statement, one violates** the prohibition: **"You shall not perform any labor"** (Exodus 20:9). Rabbi Eliezer could not respond to this claim **and was silent.** But **if it is** as Rami bar Ḥama explained, Rabbi Eliezer **should have said to him: The reason** for **my** opinion is **due to** the principle: **Since, etc.,** on the basis of which no prohibited labor has been performed.

*Rabbi Zeira* disagrees and presents a textual argument. A *baraita* records an exchange in which *Rabbi Yehoshua* confronts *Rabbi Eliezer* with the challenge: *harei over mishum lo ta'aseh kol melacha* — by baking this dough you are transgressing the prohibition of *melacha* on *Yom Tov*, since part of the food cannot be eaten. In the *baraita*, *Rabbi Eliezer* says nothing in response — *v'shatik lei*. *Rabbi Zeira* argues that this silence is telling. If *Rabbi Eliezer's* position were indeed grounded in *ho'il*, he would have responded: "I do not consider this a prohibited *melacha* because *ho'il* — since theoretically every loaf could be eaten." The absence of this defense in the *baraita* suggests that *ho'il* is not actually his reasoning, and therefore *Rami bar Chama's* equation cannot be sustained.

### Yirmiyah's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְלִיטַעְמָיךְ, הָא דִּתְנֵינָא בְּבָרַיְיתָא, אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: לִדְבָרֶיךָ, הֲרֵי הוּא עוֹבֵר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל יֵרָאֶה וּבַל יִמָּצֵא״, וּשְׁתֵיק לֵיהּ. הָכִי נָמֵי דְּלָא אַהְדַּר לֵיהּ? הָא קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין? דִּתְנַן: לֹא זֶהוּ חָמֵץ שֶׁמּוּזְהָרִין עָלָיו מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל יֵרָאֶה וּבַל יִמָּצֵא״, אֶלָּא: שָׁתֵיק לֵיהּ בְּבָרַיְיתָא, וּמַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין. הָכִי נָמֵי, אֵימוֹר שְׁתֵיק לֵיהּ בְּמַתְנִיתִין, וְאַהְדַּר לֵיהּ בִּמְכִילְתָּא אַחֲרִיתִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yirmeya **said to him: According to your reasoning,** with regard to **that which we** already **learned in a *baraita*,** that **Rabbi Eliezer said to him: According to your statement, he transgresses** the prohibitions: **It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found, and** in response to this challenge Rabbi Yehoshua **was silent, did he too not respond to** Rabbi Eliezer? **He responded to him in the mishna, as we learned** in the mishna that Rabbi Yehoshua said: **This is not the leavened bread about which we are warned** with the prohibitions: **It shall not be seen, and: It shall not be found. Rather,** it must be explained in the following manner: **He** appeared to be **silent in the *baraita*** simply because his response was not recorded, **but he responded in the mishna. So too,** here it is possible to **say that he** appeared **silent in the mishna, but he responded in a different tractate.**

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* counters with a parallel argument that undermines *Rabbi Zeira's* methodology. The same *baraita* continues with *Rabbi Eliezer* turning the charge back on *Rabbi Yehoshua*: if you tell me I am transgressing *melacha* by baking it, then according to you, who says to leave the dough and let it become *chametz*, you are causing a violation of *bal yera'eh* and *bal yimatzei*. And *Rabbi Yehoshua* is equally silent — *v'shatik lei*. Does *Rabbi Zeira* conclude from that silence that *Rabbi Yehoshua* has no answer?

Of course not — *Rabbi Yehoshua's* answer is recorded elsewhere: the *mishna* itself states explicitly that *lo zehu chametz she-muzharan alav mishum bal yera'eh* — consecrated *challah* of this kind does not implicate those prohibitions, since it is a form of *hekdesh* and does not belong to the owner in the relevant sense. *Rabbi Yehoshua* is silent in the *baraita* not because he has no answer, but because the *beraita*'s literary form presents the competing arguments dramatically without recording every rejoinder. The answer exists; it simply appears in the *mishna* rather than in the *baraita*.

By parity of reasoning, *Rabbi Eliezer's* silence when challenged about *melacha* does not mean he has no defense. His defense — namely, *ho'il* — may be recorded in another collection of Tannaitic material, *be-mechilta achriti*, even if it does not appear in this particular *baraita*. The absence of a recorded response in any one source does not constitute evidence that the response does not exist. *Rabbi Yirmiyah* therefore maintains that *Rami bar Chama's* equation stands, and *Rabbi Zeira's* proof from silence fails.

## Halacha Bakah Minimum

### Halacha Eliezer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְרַבִּי יִצְחָק אָמַר: הֲלָכָה כְּבֶן בְּתֵירָא.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The ***halakha*** with regard to the separation of *ḥalla* from impure dough during Passover **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. And Rabbi Yitzḥak said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **ben Beteira.**

The Gemara concludes the debate between *Rabbi Eliezer* and *Rabbi Yehoshua* with a *baraita*: "*Rabbi* says the *halacha* is like *Rabbi Eliezer*." A second authority, *Rav Yitzchak*, is cited as ruling like *Ben Betaira*, whose position involves placing the dough in cold water. The practical *halacha* is a matter that the *Rishonim* engage with seriously, particularly *Tosafos*, which notes that we follow *Rabbi* given the principle that *halacha* follows him against his colleagues. *Rashi* similarly addresses the question. This is practically significant because when *matzah* is baked on *Yom Tov* itself — which involves dough that is by definition *tamei* given that we all presume ourselves to be in a state of *tumah* today — the question of how to handle *hafrashat challah* remains operative.

### Dough Size Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכַמָּה שִׁיעוּר עִיסָּה? רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: בְּחִיטִּין — קַבִּין. וּבִשְׂעוֹרִין — שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּין. רַבִּי נָתָן אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר: חִילּוּף הַדְּבָרִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How much dough** may be kneaded at once on Passover without concern that the dough will become leavened in the process? **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: With regard to wheat,** one may use the amount of flour that comes from **two *kav*** of grain; **and with regard to barley,** one may use the amount of flour that comes from **three *kav*. Rabbi Natan says in the name of Rabbi Eliezer: The matters are reversed.** One may knead the flour produced from three *kav* of wheat or two *kav* of barley without concern that it will become leavened.

The Gemara now pivots to a related but distinct question: *kama shiur issah* — what is the maximum amount of dough that a person may work with at one time when baking *matzah* on *Yom Tov* or on *Chol HaMoed*, or in principle even on *Erev Yom Tov*? The concern is that if one kneads an excessively large mass of dough, portions of it that are not being actively worked may begin to ferment and become *chametz* before the baker can attend to them. The question therefore has a ceiling defined by practical safety against *chimutz*.

The question also intersects with a minimum: there is a minimum amount of dough required to obligate *hafrashat challah*. The question of whether these two measurements — the maximum safe for *matzah* baking and the minimum for *challah* obligation — are compatible is genuinely significant. If the maximum safe dough size falls below the *challah* minimum, one could never both bake *matzah* safely and fulfill the obligation of *challah*.

*Rabbi Yishmael ben Rabbi Yochanan ben Broka* provides the following measurements for the maximum safe dough size: for wheat flour, two *kavim*; for barley flour, three *kavim*. These are substantial quantities — two *kavim* of wheat corresponds to approximately eighty egg-volumes, which is significantly more than the minimum *shiur challah* of roughly forty-three eggs. The differentiation between wheat and barley presumably reflects different rates of fermentation.

### Natan Reversal

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָתַנְיָא, רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל בְּנוֹ שֶׁל רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן בְּרוֹקָה אוֹמֵר: בְּחִיטִּין — שְׁלֹשֶׁת קַבִּין, וּבִשְׂעוֹרִין — אַרְבָּעָה קַבִּין! לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּחַסִּיכָתָא, הָא — בִּמְעַלְּיָיתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Wasn't** it **taught** in a different *baraita* that **Rabbi Yishmael, son of Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Beroka, says: With regard to wheat,** one may use the amount of flour that comes from **three *kav*** of grain, **and with regard to barley,** one may use the amount of flour that comes from **four *kav*?** The Gemara answers: This is **not difficult,** as **this** latter *baraita* is referring **to low-quality** grain, and **that** *baraita* is referring **to high-quality** grain. One can obtain a higher proportion of flour from high-quality grain than from low-quality grain, which contains a greater amount of chaff.

*Rabbi Natan* citing *Rabbi Elazar* reverses the figures: barley ferments more quickly than wheat, and therefore the barley maximum is lower — two *kavim* for barley and three for wheat. A *beraita* appears to contradict both positions by citing *Rabbi Yishmael* as giving three *kavim* for wheat and four for barley. The Gemara resolves the apparent contradiction by distinguishing between inferior-grade and superior-grade flour. A larger quantity of inferior grain is required to yield the same effective amount, so the higher figures apply to *chasichta* — lower-quality grain — while the lower figures apply to *maalyusa* — refined grain.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ גְּרִיעִין חִיטֵּי חַסִּיכָתָא מֵחִיטֵּי מְעַלְּיָיתָא טְפֵי מִדִּגְרִיעָן שְׂעָרֵי חַסִּיכָתָא מִשְּׂעָרֵי מְעַלְּיָיתָא. דְּאִילּוּ הָתָם תִּילְתָּא, וְהָכָא רִיבְעָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said: Learn from this** that the extent to which **low-quality wheat is worse than high-quality wheat is greater than** the extent to which **low-quality barley is worse than high-quality barley,** i.e., the discrepancy between the different levels of quality is more significant with regard to wheat, **as there,** in the case of wheat, they differ by **one-third; and here,** in the case of barley, they differ by only **one-fourth.**

*Rav Pappa* draws a further inference from these numbers: the degradation ratio of inferior to superior wheat is greater than the degradation ratio of inferior to superior barley. For wheat, three units of inferior yield two of superior — a loss of one third. For barley, four units of inferior yield three of superior — a loss of only one quarter. The difference in processing loss between the two grains is thus measurable from these very figures.

### Rav's One Kab

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: קַבָּא מְלוֹגְנָאָה לְפִיסְחָא, וְכֵן לְחַלָּה. (וְהָתַנְיָא:)
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said: A *kav*** from the place **Melogna** is the amount that can be used to prepare dough **for Passover. And similarly, with regard to *ḥalla*,** that is the minimum amount of dough from which *ḥalla* must be separated. The Gemara asks: **Wasn't** it **taught** in a *baraita*:

*Rav* states: the *kav de-melugna* — the *kav* of a particular locale called *Melugna* — is the relevant measure for *Pesach* dough, and likewise for the minimum *shiur challah*. This seems considerably smaller than the figures just cited, and the Gemara notes the apparent contradiction with the *mishna*'s requirement of five *reva'im* of flour.

### Five Revaim Min

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמֵשֶׁת רְבָעִים קֶמַח וְעוֹד — חַיָּיבִין בַּחַלָּה. הָכִי קָאָמַר, קַבָּא מְלוֹגְנָאֵי נָמֵי אַהַאי שִׁיעוּרָא קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** Dough made from **five-quarters** of a ***log*** of **flour and** a bit **more obligates** one to separate ***ḥalla*?** The Gemara answers that **this is** what Rav **is saying: A *kav* from Melogna is the same measure as this,** as it is not a regular *kav* but a larger measure, identical to the amount from which one is required to separate *ḥalla*.

The *mishna* establishes that *chamisha reva'im kemach va'od* — five *reva'im* of flour plus a small additional amount — obligates *challah*. A *reva* is one quarter of a *kav*, equivalent to a *log*, which is approximately fourteen ounces by volume. Five *reva'im* thus equals one and a quarter *kavim* — a quantity greater than the single *kav* that *Rav* cited. The Gemara resolves this by explaining that *Rav* was referring to the *kav de-melugna*, a locally larger unit of measurement that in fact corresponds to the one-and-a-quarter *kav* standard for *challah*. The practical equivalence is maintained: the maximum safe dough size for *matzah* baking is the same as the minimum dough size that obligates *challah* separation.

### Combination Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: הָנֵי נְשֵׁי דִידַן נְהוּג לְמֵיפָא קְפִיזָא קְפִיזָא לְפִיסְחָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מַאי דַּעְתָּיךְ, לְחוּמְרָא? חוּמְרָא דְּאָתֵי לִידֵי קוּלָּא הוּא, דְּקָא מַפְקַע לַהּ מֵחַלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: These women of our** family **ordinarily bake *kefiza*** by ***kefiza*,** i.e., three-quarters of a *log* at a time, **on Passover,** since it is easier to prevent small quantities of dough from becoming leavened. **Abaye said to him: What is your opinion?** Do you tell them to do this in order to be **stringent? That** is **a stringency that leads to a leniency, as** by working with small quantities **one removes** the dough **from** the obligation to separate ***ḥalla*.**

*Rav Yosef* raises a practical difficulty. In the domestic setting, women baking *matzah* for *Pesach* would customarily work with very small quantities — he describes the practice as *kaphiza kaphiza*, tiny batch by tiny batch, with *Rashi* explaining that each batch was approximately three *lugim*, or three quarters of a *kav*. This was deliberately conservative, designed to eliminate any risk of *chimutz* through careful portion control.

*Abaye* objects sharply: this *chumra* becomes a *kula*. By keeping each batch below the *challah* minimum, the women exempt themselves from the obligation of *hafrashat challah* entirely — a significant practical leniency achieved through what was intended as a stringency.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: דְּעָבְדָן כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, (דְּתַנְיָא,) רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הָרוֹדֶה וְנוֹתֵן לַסַּל — הַסַּל מְצָרְפָן לַחַלָּה. וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Yosef **said to him: They do** separate *ḥalla* from the dough, **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer. As it was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One who removes** loaves of bread from an oven **and places them in a basket, the basket combines them to** reach the quantity from which one is required to separate ***ḥalla*,** even if each of the loaves would not attain the necessary measure for separating *ḥalla* on their own. **And Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Eliezer.** Therefore, the women of Rav Yosef's household would put all the finished *matzot* into a basket and separate *ḥalla* from them.

*Rav Yosef* responds that the problem is resolved by following *Rabbi Eliezer's* ruling — not the *Rabbi Eliezer* of our *mishna*, but the *Rabbi Eliezer* cited in the tractate of *Challah* — who holds that if separate loaves, each below the minimum, are baked and then placed together into the same basket, *hasal metzarfan le-challah* — the basket combines them into a single quantity sufficient to obligate *challah*. The women bake their small individual batches and keep each one safe from *chimutz*, but by collecting all the finished loaves in the same basket upon removing them from the oven, they aggregate to the required minimum and the *chiyuv challah* is created.

## Basket Combining

### No-Rim Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בָּבֶל שֶׁנּוֹשְׁכוֹת זוֹ מִזּוֹ, אֲבָל כְּעָכִין — לָא. הָא אִיתְּמַר עֲלַהּ, אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: אֲפִילּוּ כְּעָכִין.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said to him: But wasn't it stated with regard to** that *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi said: They taught** that a basket combines the loaves **only** with regard to **Babylonian loaves that bite from one another.** In other words, the loaves are slightly attached, and when one separates them, a bit from one loaf comes off with the other loaf. **However,** it does **not** apply **to** long, **rod-like loaves** [***ke'akhin***] that were baked separately. Therefore, that principle cannot be applied to the case discussed here, in which each batch of *matza* was baked on its own. Rav Yosef answered: **Wasn't it stated with regard to** that *baraita* that **Rabbi Ḥanina said:** The *halakha* is in accordance with the opinion of Rabbi Eliezer **even with regard to** long, **rod-like loaves?** This indicates that Rav Yosef accepted the view of Rabbi Ḥanina.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַבִּי יִרְמְיָה: טַבְלָא שֶׁאֵין לָהּ לְבִזְבְּזִין מַהוּ? תּוֹךְ כְּלִי בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא לֵיכָּא. אוֹ דִילְמָא — אֲוִיר כְּלִי בָּעֵינַן, וְהָא אִיכָּא. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** In light of this discussion, **Rabbi Yirmeya raised a dilemma:** With regard to **a board without a rim [*levizbezin*], what is** the *halakha*? Is it considered to be a vessel that combines loaves baked separately into one unit with regard to *ḥalla*? **Do we require the inside of the vessel** in order to combine the loaves, **and that is lacking,** since the board is flat rather than concave? **Or perhaps we require the airspace of the vessel, and that is** present in this case? The Gemara concludes: **Let it stand** unresolved.

*Rabbi Yirmiyah* raises a question about this combining mechanism: what if the basket has no rim — *tavlah she'ein lah levazbezin*? The operative principle, according to *Rabbi Eliezer* (of *Challah*), is that the basket (*sal*) combines the loaves. But does this require that they be held within the walls of a vessel — *tokh kli* — or is it sufficient that they occupy the *airspace* of a vessel — *avir kli* — even without enclosing walls? A flat board with no rim allows the loaves to rest together without being surrounded on any side. Whether such a surface qualifies as a combining vessel remains unresolved — the Gemara leaves this as *teiku*.

### Basket vs Oven

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַסַּל מְצָרְפָן. רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ אוֹמֵר: תַּנּוּר מְצָרְפָן. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר כִּכָּרוֹת שֶׁל בָּבֶל שֶׁנּוֹשְׁכוֹת זוֹ מִזּוֹ מִצְטָרְפוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer says: A basket combines** different loaves placed in it with regard to the obligation to separate *ḥalla*. **Rabbi Yehoshua says: An oven combines them. Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: Babylonian loaves that bite from one another are combined;** however, if the loaves are connected to any lesser degree, e.g., if they are together in an oven or basket, they are not considered combined for the purpose of separating *ḥalla*.

A *baraita* records the foundational dispute among the *Tannaim*: *Rabbi Eliezer* holds that the basket (*sal*) is what combines the loaves for *challah* purposes; *Rabbi Yehoshua* holds that it is the oven (*tanur*). The precise relationship between these two positions — whether the one who says basket requires the oven as well, or whether the one who says oven excludes the basket — is not fully unpacked by the Gemara. The *beraita* does raise the additional question of whether baking in the same oven is a prerequisite in addition to placement in the same basket, or whether the basket alone suffices regardless of whether the loaves shared an oven.

### Babylonian Breads

*Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* introduces a third position: the loaves must actually touch one another as they expand in the oven — *shenoshkhot zo mizo*, literally biting one another — as is characteristic of *kikarot shel Bavel*, Babylonian-style round loaves that are placed close together so that they press into each other as they rise. Only under those conditions, where the loaves physically merge during baking, does the combination become effective. The Gemara notes the irony that the *matzah*-baking context makes this condition particularly difficult to satisfy, since *matzah* by definition does not rise and expand and therefore will not naturally press into adjacent loaves. *Rabbi Chanina* subsequently rules that even the elongated, non-expanding type of bread — *kakhin*, the *beraita's* term for loaves that do not touch — can combine through the basket even without physical contact in the oven.

## Multi-Woman Baking

### Gamliel Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים לָשׁוֹת כְּאַחַת וְאוֹפוֹת בְּתַנּוּר אֶחָד, זוֹ אַחַר זוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabban Gamliel says: Three women may knead** their dough **as one,** meaning at one time, **and bake** the batches of dough **in one oven, one after the other,** and they need not be concerned that their dough will become leavened while they are waiting to use the oven.

The *mishna* now introduces the question of how multiple women sharing a single oven may bake *matzah* without risk of *chimutz*. *Rabban Gamliel* takes the most lenient position: three women may all knead their dough simultaneously — *lashot k'echat* — and then bake sequentially in the same oven, one after another — *v'ofot b'tanur echad zu achar zu*. Under this arrangement, two women may be waiting while the third bakes, and *Rabban Gamliel* is not concerned that the waiting dough will ferment in the interim.

### Assembly Line Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: שָׁלֹשׁ נָשִׁים עוֹסְקוֹת בְּבָצֵק כְּאַחַת, אַחַת לָשָׁה, וְאַחַת עוֹרֶכֶת, וְאַחַת אוֹפָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Three women** may be **engaged** in preparing **dough as one,** in the following manner: **One kneads** her dough as another **one arranges** her own dough so it takes the form of *matza*, while another **one bakes** her dough.

The *Chachamim* reject this arrangement as insufficiently protective. For them, three women working simultaneously is permissible only if they operate as a continuous assembly line — *shalosh nashim oskot bebatzek k'echat* — so that no woman is ever in a position of simply waiting with idle dough. The structure must be sequential and continuous: at any given moment, each woman is actively engaged in a different stage of production.

### Station Rotation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: לֹא כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים וְלֹא כׇּל הָעֵצִים וְלֹא כׇּל הַתַּנּוּרִים שָׁוִים. זֶה הַכְּלָל: תָּפַח — תִּלְטוֹשׁ בְּצוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva says: Not all women, not all wood, and not all ovens are the same,** and therefore no set rules should be established. Rather, **this is the principle:** If the dough begins to **rise, she** should **spread cold** water in which she immersed her hands, onto the dough, in order to stop the leavening process.

The *Tanna kama* articulates the assembly line in detail. There are three stations. The first woman kneads at station A; she then moves to station B to shape and pat the dough, and the second woman enters station A to begin kneading. The first woman moves from station B to station C — the oven — to bake; the second woman moves from A to B; the third woman enters station A to knead. Then the first woman, having finished baking, returns to station A to begin a new batch; the second moves from B to C; the third moves from A to B. The cycle continues in this rotation — *v'chozeret chalilah* — so that every station is occupied at all times and no dough waits unattended. The governing principle underlying this arrangement is stated explicitly: *kol zman she'oseket babatzek, eino ba lidei chimutz* — as long as dough is being actively worked, it cannot ferment. The danger of *chimutz* arises only during intervals of inactivity.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן. לָשָׁה, הִיא מְקַטֶּפֶת, וַחֲבֶרְתָּהּ לָשָׁה תַּחְתֶּיהָ. מְקַטֶּפֶת, הִיא אוֹפָה, וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ מְקַטֶּפֶת תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית לָשָׁה. אוֹפָה הִיא, לָשָׁה, וַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ אוֹפָה תַּחְתֶּיהָ, וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁית מְקַטֶּפֶת, וְחוֹזְרוֹת חֲלִילָה. כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁעוֹסְקוֹת בַּבָּצֵק — אֵינוֹ בָּא לִידֵי חִימּוּץ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: When the woman who **kneads** first completes her kneading, **she arranges** her dough **and another** woman **kneads in her place.** When the first woman finishes **arranging** her dough, **she bakes and another** woman **arranges** her dough **in her place, and the third** woman **kneads** her dough. When the first woman finishes **baking, she kneads** the dough for her next batch, **and another** woman **bakes in her place, and the third** woman **arranges** her dough, **and they continue in turn. As long as they are engaged** in handling **the dough, it will not become leavened.**

### Akiva No Rules

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר לֹא כׇּל הַנָּשִׁים וְכוּ׳. תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא: דַּנְתִּי לִפְנֵי רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, יְלַמְּדֵינוּ רַבֵּינוּ בְּנָשִׁים זְרִיזוֹת אוֹ בְּנָשִׁים שֶׁאֵין זְרִיזוֹת? בְּעֵצִים לַחִים אוֹ בְּעֵצִים יְבֵשִׁים? בְּתַנּוּר חַם אוֹ בְּתַנּוּר צוֹנֵן? אָמַר לִי: אֵין לְךָ אֶלָּא מַה שֶּׁשָּׁנוּ חֲכָמִים, זֶה הַכְּלָל: תָּפַח — תִּלְטוֹשׁ בְּצוֹנֵן.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that **Rabbi Akiva says** that **not all women,** not all wood, and not all ovens are the same. **It was taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Akiva said: I deliberated** this matter **before Rabban Gamliel,** asking: **May our master teach us** if your statement, cited in the mishna, was said **with regard to diligent women or women who are not diligent?** Was it said **with regard to** an oven fueled with **moist wood or dry wood?** Was it said **with regard to a hot oven or a cold oven?** Rabban Gamliel himself **said to me: You have only what the Sages taught,** which is that **this is the principle:** If the dough begins to **rise** such that there is a concern that it may become leavened, **she should spread cold** water **onto** the dough to prevent it from becoming leavened.

*Rabbi Akiva* rejects the possibility of any fixed rule. *Lo kol hanashim velo kol ha'etzim velo kol hatannurim shavim* — not all women work with the same efficiency, not all wood burns with the same heat or speed, and not all ovens behave identically. A rule that works under one combination of conditions may be dangerously inadequate under another. The variables are too numerous and context-dependent to admit of quantification. This is a striking methodological position: *Rabbi Akiva* refuses to validate either *Rabban Gamliel's* permissive arrangement or the *Chachamim's* assembly-line model as reliable in the abstract. The only valid standard is observation of the dough itself.

### Inspection Principle

A *baraita* records that *Rabbi Akiva* pressed this point directly before *Rabban Gamliel*: are we speaking of efficient women or less efficient ones? Of wet wood or dry wood? Of a hot oven or a cold one? With each question, the inadequacy of any fixed rule becomes more apparent, since the answer would determine how quickly *chimutz* might set in. The *baraita* indicates that *Rabban Gamliel* ultimately conceded *Rabbi Akiva's* point. The one principle both agree upon is expressed in the formula: *tapach* — if the dough begins to expand — *v'tiltosh betzonen* — apply cold water to arrest the process. The inspection of the dough itself, rather than any fixed time-interval or structural arrangement, is the authoritative standard.

## Chametz Stages

### Tapach Expanding

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שִׂיאוּר — יִשָּׂרֵף, וְהָאוֹכְלוֹ פָּטוּר. סִידּוּק — יִשָּׂרֵף, וְהָאוֹכְלוֹ חַיָּיב כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: Dough at the beginning of the leavening process [***siur***], must **be burned, but one who eats it is exempt** from the punishment of *karet* because the dough had not become fully leavened. Dough that has reached the stage of **cracking** must **be burned, and one who eats it** intentionally **is liable to** receive ***karet*,** as he has intentionally eaten leavened bread during Passover.

The beginning of fermentation is visible as expansion — *tapach*. At this stage the process has begun but has not yet crossed any threshold of prohibition. The dough can still be saved: applying cold water — *tiltosh betzonen* — arrests the fermentation and returns the dough to a workable state. This is the window of intervention, and both *Rabban Gamliel* and *Rabbi Akiva* agree that it is the operational criterion.

### Se'or Blanching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיזֶהוּ שִׂיאוּר? כְּקַרְנֵי חֲגָבִים. סִידּוּק — שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ סְדָקִין זֶה בָּזֶה, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: זֶה וְזֶה — הָאוֹכְלוֹ חַיָּיב כָּרֵת. וְאֵיזֶהוּ שִׂיאוּר? כׇּל שֶׁהִכְסִיפוּ פָּנָיו כְּאָדָם שֶׁעָמְדוּ שַׂעֲרוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **What is** considered ***siur*?** Dough that has been leavened to the point that it has cracks that look **like the antennae of locusts.** The stage of **cracking** occurs later in the leavening process, **when the cracks intermingle.** This is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. And the Rabbis say: One who** intentionally **eats** either **this or that,** dough with cracks like locust antennae or with cracks that have become intermingled, **is liable** to receive ***karet*,** as once dough begins to crack it has certainly become leavened. **And what is *siur*?** It is any dough **whose surface has becomes pale** like the face of **a person whose hair stands** on end due to fear.

According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, *se'or* at the stage described in the *mishna* is characterized by blanching of the surface — *k'sheyichsifu panav* — a change in color likened to the face of a person whose hair stands on end in fright. Small, initial cracks — *k'karnei chagavim*, like the antennae of grasshoppers — begin to appear. This stage is rabbinically forbidden: the *se'or* must be burned — *yisaref* — but one who eats it is *patur*, exempt from *karet*, indicating that it has not yet reached biblical *chametz* status.

### Siduq Cracking

*Siduq* is the stage of full biblical *chametz*. According to *Rabbi Yehuda*, this is identified by crisscrossing cracks — *shenitarvu sdekim zeh bazeh* — visible on the surface of the dough. At this stage, *yisaref v'ochlo chayav karet*: the dough must be burned, and one who eats it incurs *karet*. The progression is thus: expanding (arrested by cold water), blanching with minor cracks (rabbinic prohibition only), and crisscrossed cracks (biblical *chametz* with *karet*).

### Yehuda-Meir Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזֶהוּ שִׂיאוּר? כׇּל שֶׁהִכְסִיפוּ פָּנָיו כְּאָדָם שֶׁעָמְדוּ שַׂעֲרוֹתָיו. סִידּוּק — כְּקַרְנֵי חֲגָבִים, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר. וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵיזֶהוּ שִׂיאוּר? כְּקַרְנֵי חֲגָבִים. סִידּוּק — שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ סְדָקִין זֶה בָּזֶה. וְזֶה וָזֶה — הָאוֹכְלוֹ חַיָּיב כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught: What is *siur*?** It is **any** dough **whose surface has become pale** like the face of **a person whose hair stands** on end due to fear. **Cracking** is considered to have occurred when cracks **like the antennae of locusts** appear. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir. And the Rabbis say: What is *siur*?** It is when the dough forms cracks **like the antennae of locusts,** and **cracking** is **when the cracks intermingle. And one who** intentionally **eats** either **this or that is liable** to receive ***karet*.**

The *mishna* and the *baraita* present the debate between *Rabbi Yehuda* and *Rabbi Meir* in inverted form. In the *mishna*, the anonymous *Chachamim* state *zeh v'zeh ha'ochlo chayav karet* — both the intermediate and final stages incur *karet* — which the *baraita* identifies as *Rabbi Meir's* position. For *Rabbi Meir*, the grasshopper-antennae crack stage is already biblical *chametz*; *se'or* at this degree of development is not merely rabbinically forbidden but carries *karet*. *Rabbi Meir's* definition of the rabbinic-only *se'or* stage accordingly refers to the earlier blanching stage, without visible cracks.

*Rabbi Yehuda*, whose position is explicitly named in the *mishna*, draws the biblical threshold higher: only the crisscrossing cracks constitute biblical *chametz*, while the grasshopper stage remains at the level of rabbinic prohibition only — *ochlo patur*. The earlier blanching constitutes the *se'or* that is rabbinically forbidden without *karet*.

### Rava's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָאֲנַן תְּנַן: שִׂיאוּר יִשָּׂרֵף, וְהָאוֹכְלוֹ פָּטוּר, דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. אֵימָא: לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, זֶה וָזֶה הָאוֹכְלוֹ חַיָּיב כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that ***siur* must be burned but** that **one who eats it is exempt** from *karet*; this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda.** The opinion attributed to the Rabbis in the *baraita* appears to be the same as that which is attributed to Rabbi Yehuda in the mishna, but according to Rabbi Yehuda, one who eats *siur* is exempt from *karet*. The Gemara answers: **Say** that the *baraita* should be understood in the following manner: **According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir,** whose opinion was mentioned previously, **one who** intentionally **eats** either **this or that is liable** to receive ***karet*,** whereas according to the Rabbis he is exempt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר — אֵין לָךְ כׇּל סֶדֶק וָסֶדֶק מִלְּמַעְלָה שֶׁאֵין לוֹ כַּמָּה סְדָקִים מִלְּמַטָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: What is the reason for** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir?** It is that **there is no crack above that does not have several cracks below.** Therefore, even if only one small crack appears on the surface, it is a sign that the inside of the dough is filled with cracks and has become leavened.

The Gemara asks: what is *Rabbi Meir's* reasoning for treating even the small grasshopper-stage cracks as biblical *chametz*? *Rava* explains: *ein l'cha kol sedek v'sedek milma'ala she'ein lo kama sedakim milmata* — any crack visible on the surface of the dough necessarily reflects a far more extensive network of cracks developing within the dough below the surface. What appears to the eye as a modest surface crack is in reality an indicator of advanced internal fermentation. *Rabbi Meir* therefore holds that the visible grasshopper crack is not a superficial sign of early-stage leavening; it is evidence that the dough has already fermented deeply, and biblical *chametz* status applies.

This closes the daf with the three-stage model of *chimutz* formation clearly in view. The stages — expansion, surface blanching or minor cracking, and visible crisscrossed cracks — map onto the practical-halachic categories of no prohibition, rabbinic prohibition, and biblical *chametz* with *karet*, though *Rabbi Meir* and *Rabbi Yehuda* disagree about where precisely along that continuum the biblical threshold falls. It is also worth noting that an earlier discussion in the Gemara touched on the possibility of a middle category — *chametz nuksha* — that might carry biblical prohibition without *karet*, though our *mishna* does not surface that intermediate tier explicitly.
