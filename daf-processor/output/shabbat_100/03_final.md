# Shabbat 100 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Mayim al Mayim

### Water on Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: פְּשִׁיטָא לִי מַיִם עַל גַּבֵּי מַיִם — הַיְינוּ הַנָּחָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said: It is obvious to me** that if one poured **water onto water that is its placement,**

The daf opens with a statement of Rava that sets the conceptual framework for much of what follows. *Peshita li*, says Rava — it is obvious to me that *mayim agabi mayim*, water floating on water, is *hainu hanachasan*: that is indeed considered its set location. If one scoops water from the top layer of a pond or well and carries it from a private domain to a public domain, one has performed a proper act of carrying. One has removed the water from the place in which it is *munach* — set, in a fixed location.

This establishes the conceptual axis of the discussion: for carrying on Shabbat to be Biblically prohibited, both the *akira* (removal) and the *hanacha* (placement) must occur at a genuinely fixed, stable location. Something must be taken from a set place and put down in a set way. What follows is a sustained examination of which circumstances qualify as genuinely fixed, and the results are striking.

### Egoz al Mayim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֱגוֹז עַל גַּבֵּי מַיִם — לָאו הַיְינוּ הַנָּחָתָן.
>
> **Translation:** If one placed **a nut onto water,** **it is not** considered **its placement.**

The contrast to water on water is immediate and sharpens the point considerably. *Egoz agabi mayim* — a nut floating on water — is *lav hainu hanachasan*: it is not considered *munach*. If one carries a nut into a *reshut ha-rabim* and sets it down on a pond, or removes a nut from a pond and carries it across a domain boundary or four *amos*, one has performed neither a valid *akira* nor a valid *hanacha*. The nut is not resting on solid ground; it is moving around on the surface of the water and is therefore not in a fixed location.

This is a striking result when placed alongside positions stated elsewhere in the tractate. We have encountered opinions that something passing through the airspace of a *reshut ha-rabim* — *kluta keman shehunacha damya*, "absorbed as if placed" — is considered *munach*; that passing through the interior of a house — *beisa keman demalya dami* — is considered *munach*; that a clump of dates stuck to a wall is considered *munach*. These are all cases of objects in motion or impermanent contact, yet they are treated as having a fixed location. And yet something floating on water is not. Although a date stuck to a wall will eventually fall, at the moment in question it is stable. Something on water, by contrast, is inherently unstable — the water moves, and anything on it partakes of that instability. That basic assumption — that *being on water* disqualifies an object from being considered *munach* — is itself a significant *chidush*, even before the more nuanced sub-cases are examined.

### Egoz Bikli Tzaf

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָבָא: אֱגוֹז בִּכְלִי וּכְלִי צָף עַל גַּבֵּי מַיִם — מַהוּ? מִי אָמְרִינַן בָּתַר אֱגוֹז אָזְלִינַן, וְהָא נָיַיח, אוֹ דִילְמָא בָּתַר כְּלִי אָזְלִינַן, וְהָא לָא נָיַיח. תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** However, **Rava raised a dilemma:** In a case where there is **a nut in a vessel and the vessel is floating on water, what** is the ruling? Is it permitted to lift the nut on Shabbat if one is in another domain? The two sides of the dilemma are: **Do we say** that **we go according to the** status of the **nut, and** it **is at rest** in the vessel? **Or perhaps we go according to** the status of the **vessel, and it is not at rest.** No resolution was found to this dilemma. Therefore, **let it stand** unresolved as well.

Rava then poses a question about relative location. *Egoz bikli v'kli tzaf agabi mayim mahu*? — what is the law if one places a nut inside a vessel, and that vessel is floating on the water? This is essentially a question about the frame of reference for *munach*.

*Mi amrinan basar egoz azlinan, v'hanyach*? Do we look at the nut relative to itself, and note that its location relative to the dish or the boat is perfectly stable? The nut has not moved relative to the vessel; it is fixed vis-à-vis its immediate container. Or do we say *odilma basar kli azlinan, v'halo nayach*? Do we look at the vessel itself, and since the vessel is not at rest — it too is floating on moving water — the nut is not considered to be in a fixed location either?

This question maps neatly onto broader intuitions about relative location. Anything on earth is fixed relative to some more distant frame of reference. The question is which frame of reference is operative for purposes of *hanacha*. At a certain scale — a vessel the size of a city — one might reasonably argue that the vessel itself defines the environment and the water underneath is irrelevant. But in the case of a nut in a small dish, both can still be manipulated directly, and the question is genuinely open. The *Gemara* leaves it unresolved: *teiku*.

### Shemen al Yayin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁמֶן עַל גַּבֵּי יַיִן, מַחֲלוֹקֶת רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי וְרַבָּנַן. דִּתְנַן: שֶׁמֶן שֶׁצָּף עַל גַּבֵּי יַיִן, וְנָגַע טְבוּל יוֹם בַּשֶּׁמֶן — לֹא פָּסַל אֶלָּא שֶׁמֶן. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר: שְׁנֵיהֶם חִיבּוּר זֶה לָזֶה.
>
> **Translation:** However, with regard to **oil** floating **on wine,** there is **a dispute between Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri and the Rabbis. As we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **oil floating on wine, and one who immersed himself during the day,** i.e., one who was impure, immersed himself in a ritual bath, but will not become completely pure until sunset, **touched the oil, he invalidated only** the **oil** and not the wine. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says:** With regard to **the two,** i.e., the oil and the wine, they are considered to have **a connection to each other.** Since he made the oil impure, the wine is also impure. Their dispute is whether or not the oil is considered to be placed atop the wine.

The next question picks up on the issue of two distinct liquids — oil floating on wine — and this has a parallel in a debate already recorded in the *Mishnah*. *Machlokes Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri v'Rabbanan*: there is a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and the Sages, applied here by the *Gemara*.

The source debate concerns *terumah* and *tum'ah*. If oil is floating on wine and both are *terumah*, and a *t'vul yom* — someone who immersed in a *mikvah* that day and is waiting for nightfall — touches the oil, the question is whether the wine beneath is also rendered *tamei*. A *t'vul yom* has the status of a *sheni*, and touching *terumah* renders it *tamei* as a *shelishi*, a *pasul* that cannot transmit further *tum'ah*. If the oil and wine are a single unit, touching the oil is touching the whole — and the wine is invalidated as well. If they are separate, only the oil is rendered *tamei*, and the wine may still be consumed.

The Rabbis hold *lo pasul ela shemen* — only the oil is invalidated. Oil and wine are distinct; they do not constitute a single liquid unit merely because one floats atop the other. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri disagrees: *shneihem chibbur zeh l'zeh* — they are connected to one another. Since both are liquid, the whole constitutes one unit, and touching any part of it is touching all of it.

Applied to our context: according to Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri, oil on wine is like water on water — it is all one liquid unit, and removing oil from wine counts as removing it from a fixed location, a proper *akira*. According to the Rabbis, the oil on the wine is like the nut on the water — they are separate, the oil is not *munach* in a fixed sense, and one who places or removes it is exempt.

## Bor Reshut HaRabim

### Pit with Mat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עֲמוּקָּה עֲשָׂרָה וּרְחָבָה שְׁמֹנָה, וְזָרַק לְתוֹכָהּ מַחְצֶלֶת — חַיָּיב. חִילְּקָהּ בְּמַחְצֶלֶת — פָּטוּר. לְאַבָּיֵי דִּפְשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ דְּמַחְצֶלֶת מְבַטְּלָא מְחִיצְתָּא — כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן חוּלְיָא דִּמְבַטְּלָא מְחִיצְתָּא. לְרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן דְּמִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ חוּלְיָא, מַחְצֶלֶת פְּשִׁיטָא דְּלָא מְבַטְּלָא מְחִיצְתָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** In the case of **a pit in the public domain** that is **ten** handbreadths **deep and** precisely **eight** handbreadths **wide, and one threw a mat into it, he is liable.** However, if **he divided** the pit **with a mat** that split it in two, each one slightly less than four handbreadths wide, **he is exempt** because neither part is considered a private domain. The Gemara comments: **According to** the opinion of **Abaye, for whom it is obvious** that **the mat eliminates the partition** of the pit, **all the more so that a segment** of dirt thrown into a pit that is ten handbreadths deep, rendering it less than ten handbreadths, **eliminates the partition,** and he has no dilemma with regard to Rabbi Yoḥanan's case. According to **Rabbi Yoḥanan, who raised a dilemma with regard to a segment** of dirt, **it is obvious that a mat does not eliminate the partition.**

Abaye now introduces a series of cases involving a pit in a *reshut ha-rabim*, each of which interrogates how deeply a change within the pit can alter its identity as a *reshut ha-yachid*. The first case: *bor bi-reshut ha-rabim amukah asarah ve-rechevah shmonah* — a pit in the *reshut ha-rabim* that is ten *tefachim* deep and eight *tefachim* wide. This is, of course, more than sufficient to constitute a *reshut ha-yachid*, being double the minimum required width of four *tefachim*. If one throws a mat into it and the mat lands flat, one is *chayav* — that is a straightforward case of carrying into a *reshut ha-yachid*.

But what if one divides the pit with the mat — *chelkah be-machtzeles, patur*? If the mat lies along the middle, splitting the eight-*tefach* floor into two sections, then neither half is wide enough to qualify as a *reshut ha-yachid*: both are less than four *tefachim* wide. The act of throwing the mat in has, effectively, dismantled the *reshut ha-yachid* from within. The question is whether we look at the domain's status before the act was completed or after, and whether the mat constitutes a meaningful enough change to the physical configuration.

The *Gemara* draws out the implications of Abaye's position by comparing it to Rabbi Yochanan's position from the previous daf. *Le'Abaye depishita lei demachatzeles mevatla mechitza* — for Abaye it is obvious that even a mat redefines the architecture. If so, *kol shekein chuya demavatla mechitza* — certainly he would agree that throwing in dirt, which is a more permanent alteration, would also redefine the configuration and render one *patur*. Dirt is a heavier, more lasting change, so if a mat suffices, dirt certainly does.

*Le'Rabbi Yochanan demibaia lei chuya* — Rabbi Yochanan, however, was uncertain whether even filling the pit with dirt suffices to exempt one, since he was unsure whether one looks at the domain before or after the act is complete, and was discussing the more serious change of dirt. According to Rabbi Yochanan, it would be obvious that a mat — a much less significant change — does not redefine the architecture. Therefore, in Abaye's case of the mat, Rabbi Yochanan would say one is *chayav*, because the mat fails to alter the fundamental status of the pit as a *reshut ha-yachid*.

Two distinct questions are thus embedded in the dispute. The first is temporal: does one assess the domain's identity before or after the act is completed? Before the mat lands, the pit is a *reshut ha-yachid*; after it lands and divides the space, it is not. Abaye says one looks at the configuration after the act is done. The second question is material: which objects possess sufficient permanence or substance to redefine a domain's architecture? Abaye holds that even a mat meets that threshold; Rabbi Yochanan holds that even dirt is insufficient to settle the question, let alone a mat.

### Pit Filled Water

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: בּוֹר בִּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים עֲמוּקָּה עֲשָׂרָה וּרְחָבָה אַרְבָּעָה מְלֵאָה מַיִם, וְזָרַק לְתוֹכָהּ — חַיָּיב. מְלֵאָה פֵּירוֹת, וְזָרַק לְתוֹכָהּ — פָּטוּר, מַאי טַעְמָא? — מַיִם לָא מְבַטְּלִי מְחִיצְתָּא, פֵּירוֹת מְבַטְּלִי מְחִיצְתָּא. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הַזּוֹרֵק מִן הַיָּם לְאִיסְרַטְיָא וּמִן הָאִיסְרַטְיָא לַיָּם — פָּטוּר. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אִם יֵשׁ בִּמְקוֹם שֶׁזָּרַק עָמוֹק עֲשָׂרָה וְרָחָב אַרְבָּעָה — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **a pit in the public domain** that is **ten** handbreadths **deep and four** handbreadths **wide** and **filled with water,** and **one threw** an object **into it** on Shabbat, **one is liable** because the pit is considered a private domain. And if the pit was **filled with fruit and one threw** an object **into it, he is exempt. What is the reason** for the different rulings? **Water is not** significant enough to **eliminate the partition; fruit eliminates the partition. This was also taught** in a *baraita*: **One who throws** an object **from the sea to the street or from the street to the sea is exempt** because the sea is considered a *karmelit*, and one is not liable according to Torah law in that case. **Rabbi Shimon says: If** the **area** in the sea **where he threw** it **is ten** handbreadths **deep and four** handbreadths **wide, he is liable,** as he is considered as one who threw an object into a private domain. Apparently, the water in the sea does not eliminate the status of a private domain.

Abaye introduces his next case: a pit in *reshut ha-rabim* that is ten *tefachim* deep and four *tefachim* wide — the minimum dimensions for a *reshut ha-yachid* — and it is filled, or partly filled, with water. If one throws something into it, one is *chayav*. The question, given what was just established about the mat, is why the water does not reduce the pit's effective depth. If the pit is half-filled with water, the distance from the water's surface to the top of the pit may be less than ten *tefachim*, which might seem to disqualify it as a *reshut ha-yachid*.

The answer is that *mayim lo mevatlei mechitza* — water does not negate the walls. The pit retains its identity as a *reshut ha-yachid* regardless of how much water fills it. Rashi explains that if one throws a rock in, it sinks to the bottom, and one is still *chayav*. Even if the object thrown is water — and water on water is *munach*, as Rava established — if it lands within the walls of a pit that is ten *tefachim* high, one is *chayav*, for the *hanacha* occurs within a *reshut ha-yachid* regardless of whether the floor is accessible.

A *baraita* provides confirmation and extends the point: *hazorek min ha-yam le'istrya* — if one throws from the ocean to a major thoroughfare, or from the thoroughfare to the ocean, one is *patur*, because the ocean is a *karmelit*. Rabbi Shimon then adds: if at the spot in the ocean floor where one threw, there is a depression that is ten *tefachim* deep and four *tefachim* wide, one is *chayav* — that depression in the ocean floor is a *reshut ha-yachid*. Even though it is submerged and perpetually filled with ocean water, its identity as a distinct *reshut ha-yachid* is not compromised by the water within it.

This is, on reflection, even more striking than the earlier case. The pit at the bottom of the ocean is not merely filled with water temporarily — it will never be emptied. And yet its identity as a *reshut ha-yachid* persists. Perhaps the reasoning is that we always conceive of water as something a pit contains and that stands independent of the pit's definition; or perhaps there is an inherent conceptual distinction between water, which is transparent and fluid, and solid matter that actually displaces space. In any case, the principle holds: water does not alter the architectural identity of a domain.

### Pit Filled Fruit

The above passages make clear that *peirot mevatlei mechitza* — fruit negates the walls. If the pit is filled with fruit and one throws something into it, one is *patur*. The fruit is treated as redefining the physical configuration of the space, in a way that water is not.

This requires explanation, and *Tosafot* addresses it directly. The earlier discussion established that a clump of dates stuck to a wall — *dveilah shemeinah* — does not negate the wall or redefine the architecture. Abaye holds that position. How then does fruit filling a pit negate the walls? Is a granary no longer a *reshut ha-yachid*?

*Tosafot* offers two limiting principles. First, the key distinction between the date pressed against a wall and fruit stored in a pit is one of duration: *dveilah eino omed kol kach lehitbatel sham ba-kotel* — you would not leave such a thing pressed against the wall for any extended period, whereas *k'mo sheragilin levatel peirot ba-bor* — it is conventional to store grain and produce in a pit for a long time, potentially for half a year or more, as in a granary. When something is stored for a long period, it is seen as genuinely transforming the character of the space. The space is no longer experienced as a container with empty volume but as something closer to a solid unit.

Second, *Tosafot* offers an alternative or additional criterion: *bor malei peirot o chetzyo* — perhaps it is only when the pit is filled entirely or at least halfway with fruit that this transformation occurs. A single grain of wheat on the floor of a ten-*tefach* pit obviously does not change the architectural identity of the domain. The threshold is volume: at some point, enough material within the space triggers the reclassification.

The question of exactly where that threshold falls remains somewhat open, and *Tosafot* acknowledges that both criteria — duration and volume — may work in conjunction. The anomaly of the ocean pit, filled with water permanently and completely, persists and is not fully resolved; the distinction seems to rest on an independent principle that water is simply categorically different in the way it relates to the domains that contain it.

## Kochel HaKir

### Lemalah Asarah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזּוֹרֵק אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת בַּכּוֹתֶל, לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאֲוִיר. לְמַטָּה מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים — כְּזוֹרֵק בָּאָרֶץ. הַזּוֹרֵק בָּאָרֶץ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who throws** an object **four cubits** in the public domain, if the object hits **the wall above ten handbreadths** from the ground, which is an exempt domain, it is **as if one threw it in the air,** and he is exempt. If it hits the wall **below ten handbreadths** from the ground, it is **as if he threw it** and it landed **on the ground, and one who throws** an object **four cubits** and it lands **on the ground is liable.**

The *Mishnah* now takes up a different scenario: *hazorek arba amot ba-kotel* — one who throws something four *amos* in a *reshut ha-rabim* and it strikes a wall. If it strikes above ten *tefachim*, it is *k'zorek ba-avir* — like throwing into the air, i.e., not within the operative space of the *reshut ha-rabim*, and one is exempt. If it strikes within ten *tefachim* of the ground, it is *k'zorek ba-aretz* — like throwing on the ground — and since throwing four *amos* on the ground of a *reshut ha-rabim* makes one *chayav*, so too here.

### Chokekin LeHashlim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָא לָא נָח? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: בִּדְבֵילָה שְׁמֵינָה שָׁנִינוּ. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, אָמַר רַב, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא: זָרַק לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה, וְהָלְכָה וְנָחָה בְּחוֹר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא, בָּאנוּ לְמַחְלוֹקֶת רַבִּי מֵאִיר וְרַבָּנַן. לְרַבִּי מֵאִיר דְּאָמַר חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים — מִיחַיַּיב. לְרַבָּנַן דְּאָמְרִי אֵין חוֹקְקִין לְהַשְׁלִים — לָא מִיחַיַּיב. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זָרַק לְמַעְלָה מֵעֲשָׂרָה, וְהָלְכָה וְנָחָה בְּחוֹר כׇּל שֶׁהוּא — רַבִּי מֵאִיר מְחַיֵּיב וַחֲכָמִים פּוֹטְרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that if one throws an object in the public domain a distance of four cubits and it hits a wall above ten handbreadths from the ground, he is liable if he threw it. The Gemara asks: **And we discussed it:** How could he be liable for carrying in that case? Since the object **did not come to rest** on the wall, there was no placement. **And Rabbi Yoḥanan said:** It is **with regard to** the case of **a juicy cake of figs** that sticks to the wall when thrown against it that **we learned** in the mishna. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya said:** If **one threw** a stone at a wall **above ten** handbreadths from the ground, **and it went and came to rest in a hole** in the wall **of any size** less than four handbreadths, **we have come to the dispute** between **Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis. According to** the opinion of **Rabbi Meir, who said: One carves out** the space **to complete** it, **he is liable.** We complete the hole by conceptually carving it to four handbreadths because doing so is theoretically possible. Since the hole is considered ten handbreadths high and four handbreadths wide, one is liable for transferring an object from a public domain to a private one. **According to** the opinion of **the Rabbis, who say: One does not carve out** the space **to complete** it, the thrower **is not liable** because the hole is actually less than four handbreadths wide at present. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: If **one threw** an object **above ten** handbreadths, **and it went and came to rest in a small hole, Rabbi Meir deems** him **liable, while the Rabbis deem** him **exempt.**

The *Gemara* raises the obvious difficulty: *v'ha lo nach* — but it did not come to rest! If it hits a wall and rebounds, it never achieved a proper *hanacha*. Rabbi Yochanan resolves this: *bidveilah shemeinah shaninu* — the *Mishnah* is speaking about a thick, sticky mass, such as a dense clump of dates, which adheres to the wall. That adhesion constitutes coming to rest, and the *hanacha* is valid.

Rav Yehuda then cites a teaching in the name of Rav, citing Rabbi Chiyya: *zarak lemalah me'asarah ve'halachah ve'nachah be-chor kol-shehu* — one throws above ten *tefachim* into a small hole in the wall. Since it is above ten *tefachim* and in a wall, the question becomes whether that small hole qualifies as a *reshut ha-yachid*.

This brings in the dispute between Rabbi Meir and the Rabbis regarding *chokekin le-hashlim* — the principle of "imagining something carved out to complete" a space. The original context of the dispute is whether an arched doorway, whose width narrows at the top due to the arch, qualifies for a *mezuzah*: Rabbi Meir holds that one imagines the archway as if it were squared off — carved out to produce a full rectangular doorframe — while the Rabbis do not apply that principle.

Here the *Gemara* applies the same dispute to *reshuyot*. If the hole in the wall is above ten *tefachim* and too small to constitute a *reshut ha-yachid* in its own right, Rabbi Meir would hold that one imagines carving out enough space to produce four *tefachim* of width, thereby creating a *reshut ha-yachid*. Throwing something into that hole would then constitute carrying into a *reshut ha-yachid*, and one would be *chayav*. The Rabbis, who reject *chokekin le-hashlim*, would say the hole remains insufficient, no *reshut ha-yachid* is created, and one is *patur*.

The *Gemara* acknowledges that there is an obvious difference between applying this principle to a *mezuzah* question and applying it to the law of *reshuyot*, but proceeds to treat them as governed by the same underlying principle. A confirming *baraita* is then cited: one who throws above ten *tefachim* into a small hole — Rabbi Meir holds *chayav*, the *Chachamim* hold *patur*.

## Tel HaMitlaket

### Tel Geometry

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: תֵּל הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו — חַיָּיב. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מָבוֹי שֶׁשָּׁוָה לְתוֹכוֹ, וְנַעֲשָׂה מִדְרוֹן לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים, אוֹ שָׁוָה לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים וְנַעֲשָׂה מִדְרוֹן לְתוֹכוֹ — אוֹתוֹ מָבוֹי אֵינוֹ צָרִיךְ לֹא לֶחִי וְלֹא קוֹרָה. רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: תֵּל הַמִּתְלַקֵּט עֲשָׂרָה מִתּוֹךְ אַרְבַּע, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said:** In the case of **a mound** that is an inclined plane that gradually **attains** a height of **ten** handbreadths **over** a horizontal space of **four** cubits, **and one threw** an object from the public domain and it **came to rest atop** that mound, **he is liable** because it is considered a partition. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **An alleyway that is level inside and becomes an inclined** or declined plane as it enters **the public domain,** which is higher or lower than the alleyway, **or** if the entrance to the alleyway is **level** when entering **the public domain and inside it** is **inclined, that alleyway requires neither a post** alongside its entrance **or a beam** across its entrance in order to distinguish it from the public domain because the incline itself is considered a partition. **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Gamliel says:** In the case of a **mound** that gradually **attains** a height of **ten** handbreadths **over** a horizontal space of **four** cubits, **and one threw** an object from the public domain and it **came to rest atop** that mound, **he is liable.**

Rav Yehuda then cites Rav: *tel ha-mitlaket asarah mitoch arba* — a mound that rises ten *tefachim* within a base of four *amos*. The term *mitlaket* denotes a sloping ascent; the question is what angle of slope is steep enough for the sloped face to be treated as a wall for purposes of *reshut ha-yachid*.

The ruling is that if a mound rises ten *tefachim* over a horizontal distance of four *amos*, its slope is treated as a wall. The top of such a mound constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid*, and if one throws something from a *reshut ha-rabim* and it lands on top, one is *chayav*. If the slope is more gradual than this — even if it eventually rises to ten *tefachim* — it cannot be called a wall, and no *reshut ha-yachid* is created at the summit.

The geometry is precise. Four *amos* equals twenty-four *tefachim* (six *tefachim* to the *amah*), so the slope in question rises ten *tefachim* vertically over twenty-four *tefachim* horizontally. The angle — the arctangent of ten over twenty-four — works out to approximately 22 or 24 degrees. Any slope at that angle or steeper is treated as a *mechitza* in halachic terms.

### Islands Eruvin

This calculation has direct practical application to questions of *eruvin* and island geography. The *Gemara* cites a *baraita* that applies the rule to *eruvin*: *mavoi she'shaveh le-tocho ve-na'aseh madron le-reshut ha-rabim* — if a courtyard is flat on the inside but slopes down to a *reshut ha-rabim* at one end, and that slope meets the angle criterion, then the slope itself serves as the fourth wall of the *mavoi*. No *lechi* or *korah* is needed, because the incline is a genuine *mechitza*. The same applies in the reverse: if the *reshut ha-rabim* is level and the courtyard slopes upward from the entrance.

This has substantial relevance for discussions about whether natural islands can be treated as *reshut ha-yachid* for *eruv* purposes. If the shoreline of an island slopes from sea level to land at the requisite angle, the slope itself functions as a wall. A coral reef or a steep seawall clearly meets this standard. A more gently sloping shoreline must be evaluated against the criterion of ten *tefachim* within four *amos*. In practice, a slope of approximately 22 to 24 degrees is not particularly steep — many hilly areas in Israel and elsewhere would easily exceed it — which suggests considerable scope for treating natural topography as *mechitzot*. The *Ramban* and others have addressed the related question of whether walls that are not experientially perceptible to those within them still function as valid *mechitzot*, and whether such natural inclines serve that function better than the more attenuated mechanism of a *tzurat ha-petach*.

## Giglug v'Nach

### Zarak Giglug

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זָרַק לְתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פָּטוּר. חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וְנִתְגַּלְגֵּל לְתוֹךְ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If **one threw** an object in the public domain, intending for it to land **within four cubits,** meaning that he had no intention of violating the Torah prohibition of carrying, **and** the object **rolled** and went **beyond four cubits, he is exempt.** However, if one threw an object with the intention of it landing **beyond four cubits, and** the object **rolled** back **within four cubits, he is liable** from when he originally threw the object.

The *Mishnah* now presents: *zarak le-toch arba amos ve-nisgalgel chutz le-arba amos, patur* — if one threw something that landed within four *amos* and then rolled out beyond four *amos*, one is *patur*. Conversely, *chutz le-arba amos ve-nisgalgel le-toch arba amos, chayav* — if it landed beyond four *amos* and then rolled back, one is *chayav*. The implicit principle seems to be that when an object strikes the ground, that impact constitutes its coming to rest; subsequent rolling is a separate movement not attributable to the original act.

### V'Ha Lo Nach

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָא לָא נָח? אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: זָרַק חוּץ לְאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת וּדְחָפַתּוּ הָרוּחַ וְהִכְנִיסַתּוּ, וְאַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָזְרָה וְהוֹצִיאַתּוּ — פָּטוּר. אֲחָזַתּוּ הָרוּחַ מַשֶּׁהוּ, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁחָזְרָה וְהִכְנִיסַתּוּ — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: We learned in the mishna that if one threw an object beyond four cubits and it rolled back within four cubits, he is liable. The Gemara asks: The object **did not come to rest** beyond four cubits, so how can the one who threw it be liable? **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: And that** liability was established **when** the object **came to rest atop something. That was also taught** in a *baraita*: If **one threw** an object **beyond four cubits and the wind blew it** while still in the air **and brought it** within four cubits, **he is exempt even though it,** i.e., the wind, **then brought it back out** because the object did not come to rest in the place where it was thrown. However, if **the wind seized it briefly** and it stayed on the ground for a brief period of time (*Tosafot*), **even though** the wind **then brought it in,** the individual **is liable.**

But the *Gemara* immediately challenges this: *v'ha lo nach* — it did not come to rest! If it hit the ground and kept rolling, in what sense has it achieved *hanacha*? The question is sharper than it might appear. Earlier in the tractate, there was a view that objects within three *tefachim* of the ground are treated as though they are on the ground — *lavud* — and that this would be sufficient for *hanacha* without requiring actual cessation of movement. Tosafot notes explicitly that this *Gemara* does not assume that principle. If even proximity to the ground within three *tefachim* were sufficient, the *Mishnah*'s ruling would be obvious: the object, being on the ground, is certainly treated as *munach*. But this *Gemara* assumes a much more demanding standard — the object must genuinely stop moving.

Rabbi Yochanan therefore explains: *ve-hu she-nach al gabei mashehu* — the *Mishnah* is speaking of a case where the object did, for a moment, come to rest on something: it hit a small protrusion or pebble, was arrested in its motion for an instant, and only then continued rolling. That momentary stoppage is what the *Mishnah* counts as *nach*.

### Ruach Nach

A *baraita* extends the discussion into an even more striking direction. *Zarak chutz le-arba amot ve-dachafta ha-ruach ve-hichnisato* — if one threw something beyond four *amos* and the wind blew it back before it came to rest, and even if the wind then carried it back out again, one is *patur* throughout. The continuous fluid motion — out, back in, back out — does not yield a valid *hanacha* at any point, and so one is exempt.

But: *achazata ha-ruach mashehu* — if the wind caused the object to pause momentarily in mid-air, *af al pi shechazra ve-hichnisato chayav*. Even if it was subsequently carried back, one is *chayav*, because that momentary suspension constitutes a *hanacha*. Hovering in mid-air, stabilized for a moment by the wind, is treated as coming to rest.

This requires some reflection. It is not immediately obvious why wind-induced suspension in mid-air should constitute a more valid *hanacha* than a nut floating on a pond. In both cases the object is, for a moment, stable. And yet the pond case does not qualify while the wind case apparently does. *Tosafot* attempts to reconcile these discussions, though the internal consistency is not entirely transparent.

### Rava Toch Gimel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: תּוֹךְ שְׁלֹשָׁה לְרַבָּנַן צָרִיךְ הַנָּחָה עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ. יָתֵיב מָרִימָר וְקָאָמַר לַהּ לְהָא שְׁמַעְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְמָרִימָר: לָאו הַיְינוּ מַתְנִיתִין, וְאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: וְהוּא שֶׁנָּח עַל גַּבֵּי מַשֶּׁהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִתְגַּלְגֵּל קָאָמְרַתְּ — מִתְגַּלְגֵּל אֵין סוֹפוֹ לָנוּחַ, אֲבָל הַאי כֵּיוָן דְּסוֹפוֹ לָנוּחַ, אַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא נָח — כְּמַאן דְּנָח דָּמֵי, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Despite the principle of *lavud*, which states that **within three** handbreadths of the ground an object is considered to be attached to it, **according to the Rabbis,** who maintain that an object in airspace is not considered at rest, the object **must come to rest atop something** to establish liability. The Gemara relates that **Mareimar sat and stated this *halakha*. Ravina said to Mareimar:** **Isn't that** what we learned **in the mishna,** with regard to which **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: That** liability is **when it came to rest atop something,** which means that the object must actually land in order for the one who threw it to be liable. Mareimar **said to** Ravina: **Are you saying** it is a case of **rolling?** One cannot cite proof from a rolling object because **a rolling object will not ultimately come to rest. However,** with regard to **this** object, which passed within three handbreadths of the ground, I would say: **Since it will ultimately come to rest, even though** it has **not** yet **come to rest,** it **is** considered **as an object** that **came to rest.** Therefore, Rava **teaches us** that even in that case one is not liable until it actually comes to rest upon something.

Rava draws the explicit conclusion: *toch gimel le-Rabbanan tzarich hanacha al gabei mashehu* — even within three *tefachim* of the ground, even according to the Rabbis (who disputed Rabbi Akiva's more expansive view of *hanacha* in mid-air), an object must physically come to rest on something. Being within three *tefachim*, or even being on the ground itself while rolling, does not suffice. The object has to actually stop.

This *Gemara* is thus presenting a position more stringent in one sense than anything stated before it in the tractate: not proximity to the ground, not contact with the ground while in motion, but actual cessation of movement is required for *hanacha*.

### Mareimar Challenge

Mareimar was transmitting this teaching of Rava when Ravina raised a challenge: *lav hainu matnitin*? Is this not already implicit in the *Mishnah* itself? The *Mishnah* said the object rolled, and Rabbi Yochanan said it was only *chayav* because it had stopped on something — which already implies that merely being on the ground or within three *tefachim* is insufficient. So what is Rava adding?

The *Gemara* responds: *mitgalgel ka'amrat*? You are speaking of a case involving rolling. *Misgalgel ein sofo lanuach* — in a case where something is rolling and bouncing, it is self-evident that it needs to actually stop; the *Mishnah*'s case required no novel insight, since obviously something actively rolling has not come to rest. What Rava was addressing was different: a case where the object is heading toward rest — it is within three *tefachim*, it is descending toward the ground, *keivan de-sofo lanuach* — since it will inevitably come to rest, *af al gav de-lo nach, keman de-machat dami*, one might have thought it is already treated as though it has landed. *Kama-shma lan*: Rava teaches us that even in that case, until it actually hits the ground and stops, it has not achieved *hanacha*.

Rashi explains the practical stakes: if one remembers in mid-descent, before the object has landed, one would not bring a *korban* — the *melacha* is not yet complete. Alternatively, one can construct scenarios where an object within three *tefachim* is still technically outside four *amos*, and only upon actually landing crosses the threshold into the relevant zone. In those cases it matters precisely when the *hanacha* occurs.

The consistent thrust of this entire *sugya*, from the floating nut to the wind-suspended object to the rolling stone, is a single demanding principle: *hanacha* requires genuine, physical rest. Whatever accommodations were made in other parts of the tractate for airspace and proximity, this *Gemara* presses back firmly and insists on actual cessation of motion.

## Rekak Mayim

### Puddle Depth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזּוֹרֵק בַּיָּם אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — פָּטוּר. אִם הָיָה רְקַק מַיִם, וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְהַלֶּכֶת בּוֹ, הַזּוֹרֵק לְתוֹכוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב. וְכַמָּה הוּא רְקַק מַיִם — פָּחוֹת מֵעֲשָׂרָה טְפָחִים. רְקַק מַיִם וּרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים מְהַלֶּכֶת בּוֹ, הַזּוֹרֵק בְּתוֹכוֹ אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — חַיָּיב.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who throws** an object **four cubits into the sea is exempt. If there was a swamp and the public domain passes through it, one who throws** an object **four cubits into it is liable** like one who carried four cubits in the public domain. **And how** deep **is** this **swamp?** It is **less than ten handbreadths** deep. In the case of **a swamp that the public domain passes through, one who throws** an object **four cubits into** the swamp **is liable.**

The next *Mishnah* addresses *rekak mayim* — a puddle of water. *Hazorek be-yam arba amot patur* — throwing four *amos* into an ocean is exempt, since an ocean is a *karmelit*, not a *reshut ha-rabim*. But *im hayah rekak mayim ve-reshut ha-rabim meholechet bo, hazorek le-tocho arba amot chayav* — if there is a puddle of water in the middle of a thoroughfare that people walk through, throwing four *amos* within the puddle makes one *chayav*, because the puddle is part of the *reshut ha-rabim*. The puddle's depth must be less than ten *tefachim*; at ten *tefachim* it would constitute a *reshut ha-yachid* or at minimum cease to be part of the *reshut ha-rabim*.

### Hiluch Not Tashmish

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אֲמַר לֵיהּ הָהוּא מֵרַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא ״הִילּוּךְ״ ״הִילּוּךְ״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי, הָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן: הִילּוּךְ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק — שְׁמֵיהּ הִילּוּךְ, תַּשְׁמִישׁ עַל יְדֵי הַדְּחָק — לָא שְׁמֵיהּ תַּשְׁמִישׁ. אֶלָּא ״רְקָק״ ״רְקָק״ תְּרֵי זִימְנֵי לְמָה לִי? חַד בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה וְחַד בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים. וּצְרִיכִי. דְּאִי תְּנָא חֲדָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה, דַּעֲבִידִי אִינָשֵׁי דִּמְסַגִּי לְאֹקוֹרֵי נַפְשַׁיְיהוּ, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים — לָא. וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן בִּימוֹת הַגְּשָׁמִים, דְּכֵיוָן דְּמִיטַּנְּפִי לָא אִיכְפַּת לְהוּ, אֲבָל בִּימוֹת הַחַמָּה — לָא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **One of the Sages said to Rava: Granted, passing passing** is mentioned **twice** in the mishna; **this teaches us** that **passage under duress is considered passage,** but **usage under duress is not considered usage. But why do I need it** to mention **swamp swamp twice?** Rava answered him: **One** case **is** referring **to the summer, and one** case **is** referring **to the winter. And** both cases **are necessary, as had** the mishna **taught** only **one** mention of swamp, I would have said that **these matters,** i.e., cases indicating that passage under duress is considered passage, **apply** only **in the summer because people commonly** pass through the swamp **to cool themselves; however, in the winter** I would have thought that it would **not** be so. **And had** the mishna **taught us** only the case of **winter,** I would have said that **since they are filthy** from mud anyway, **they do not mind** walking through the swamp, **but in the summer** it would **not** be so.

The *Gemara* notes that the case is stated twice — the *Mishnah* repeats the scenario of a puddle in a thoroughfare — and asks why the repetition is necessary. Rav Hamnuna explains to Rava that the double statement teaches two distinct principles.

First: *hiluch al yedei ha-dechak shemei hiluch* — walking through something even under duress, when there is no convenient alternative, still counts as walking. Even though wading through a puddle is not a normal or comfortable mode of travel, if people actually do it because they must, that is sufficient use to make the space part of the *reshut ha-rabim*. One need not insist on easy, comfortable traversal.

Second, and in the other direction: *tashmish al yedei ha-dechak lav shemei tashmish* — using a space only with difficulty does not constitute sufficient use to incorporate it into the *reshut ha-rabim*. We had encountered this principle earlier regarding platforms in the *reshut ha-rabim*: a platform nine *tefachim* high and convenient to use is part of the *reshut ha-rabim*; one that is low and awkward to access is not. The repetition of *meholechet bo* in the *Mishnah* emphasizes that it is specifically *hiluch* — walking through — that confers the status of *reshut ha-rabim*, not other forms of use achieved only with difficulty.

### Imot Chama Geshamim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי אֲמַר: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּלָא הָוֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּהָוֵי אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת — אַקּוֹפֵי מַקְּפִי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said:** It is possible to explain this other way. It was **necessary** for the mishna to state swamp twice because **it would have entered your mind to say** that **these matters apply** specifically **where** the swamp **is not four cubits** wide because then people walk through the swamp and do not circumvent it, **but where** the swamp **is four cubits** wide, people **circumvent it.** Therefore, it was necessary to teach that people walk through swamps that are both narrow and wide.

The *Gemara* then asks why the puddle scenario itself needed to be stated twice rather than the single repetition of *meholechet bo* being sufficient. The answer: *chad bimot ha-chama ve-chad bimot ha-geshamim* — one case teaches the summer, the other the winter. Both must be stated, because one might have thought that only one season applies.

Had only the summer case been taught, one might have said that *de'avidi inshi de-maskeli le-akruri nafshaihu* — people enjoy walking through a puddle in the summer to cool themselves off, and that is what gives it the character of a thoroughfare. In winter, when it is already wet and cold, perhaps people avoid puddles enough that they are not sufficiently used to constitute *reshut ha-rabim*.

Conversely, had only the winter case been taught, one might have said that *keivan de-mitanfai* — since clothes are already muddy and wet in winter, people do not mind walking through a puddle and so it is used enough. But in summer, when people want to stay dry and clean, perhaps they would walk around it, making it insufficiently traveled to qualify. The *Gemara* therefore requires both cases to establish that in either season, people use the puddle enough to incorporate it into the *reshut ha-rabim*.

### Abaye Rav Ashi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אִיצְטְרִיךְ, סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דַּהֲוַאי אַרְבָּעָה, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלָא הֲוַאי אַרְבָּעָה — מִיפְסָע פָּסְעִי לֵיהּ. וְאַזְדָּא רַב אָשֵׁי לְטַעְמֵיהּ, דְּאָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: הַאי מַאן דְּזָרֵיק וְנָח אַגּוּדָּא דְגַמְלָא — מִיחַיַּיב, שֶׁהֲרֵי רַבִּים בּוֹקְעִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said** another explanation: It was **necessary** for the mishna to state swamp twice because **it would have entered your mind to say** that **these matters apply** specifically **where** the swamp is at least **four** handbreadths wide, **but where** the swamp **is not four** handbreadths wide, people **step over it** and do not walk through it. The Gemara comments: **And Rav Ashi** follows his own **reasoning, as Rav Ashi said: One who threw** an object **and it came to rest on one of the beams of a bridge is liable.** Even though the width of each beam is less than four handbreadths, it joins together with the other beams to form a single surface of the public domain **because** even though many people step over the beams, still **many people step on it.**

Abaye offers a further reason why the repetition was necessary. *Salka da'atach amina hani mili heicha de-lo havi arba amos* — I might have thought that a puddle is part of *reshut ha-rabim* only when it is narrow, not wide, because *heicha de-havi arba amos, akufi makpai leh* — when it is four *amos* across, people walk around it rather than through it. The repetition teaches that even a wide puddle, if people do walk through it, is still part of *reshut ha-rabim*.

Rav Ashi argues the opposite: *salka da'atach amina hani mili heicha de-havi arba* — I might have thought it only applies when the puddle is wide enough that one must walk through it. If it is narrow — less than four *tefachim* — *mifsi pasi leh*, people simply step over it, and stepping over is not walking through. The *Mishnah* therefore teaches that even a narrow puddle over which people step is incorporated into the *reshut ha-rabim*.

Rav Ashi goes on to apply this consistently: *hai man de-zarak ve-nach aguda de-gamla* — one who throws something and it lands on a beam protruding from a bridge, even if that beam is not flush with the walkway, is *chayav*, because *sheharei rabim bokim bo* — the public walks over it. Whatever form the passage takes, if the public actually traverses a space, that traversal suffices to make it part of *reshut ha-rabim*.

## Zarak Bayam

### Ocean Land Transfer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַזּוֹרֵק מִן הַיָּם לַיַּבָּשָׁה, וּמִן הַיַּבָּשָׁה לַיָּם, וּמִן הַיָּם לַסְּפִינָה, וּמִן הַסְּפִינָה לַיָּם, וּמִן הַסְּפִינָה לַחֲבֶירְתָּהּ — פָּטוּר. סְפִינוֹת קְשׁוּרוֹת זוֹ בָּזוֹ — מְטַלְטְלִין מִזּוֹ לָזוֹ. אִם אֵינָן קְשׁוּרוֹת, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁמּוּקָּפוֹת — אֵין מְטַלְטְלִין מִזּוֹ לָזוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who throws** an object **from the sea to dry land, or from dry land to the sea, or from the sea onto a boat, or from a boat into the sea, or from one boat to another is exempt** because the sea has the legal status of a *karmelit*. If **boats** are **tied together, one may carry** an object **from one to the other** on Shabbat. However, **if they are not tied, even though they are adjacent, one may not carry from one to the other.**

The *Mishnah* opens: *hazorek min ha-yam le-yabashah* — one who throws from the ocean to dry land, or from dry land to the ocean, or from the ocean to a boat, or from a boat to the ocean, or from one boat to another, in all these cases one is *patur*. The ocean is a *karmelit*, and the transfers described are between a *karmelit* and either a *reshut ha-rabim* or a *reshut ha-yachid*, or between two *reshuyot ha-yachid* passing over a *karmelit*.

### Boat Reshut Yachid

The *Mishnah* then addresses the case of a boat in greater detail. A boat, with its walls rising ten *tefachim* on all sides, constitutes a *reshut ha-yachid*. Throwing from one boat to another is thus a transfer between two *reshuyot ha-yachid* passing over a *karmelit* — and one is *patur*.

*Sfinot kshurot zu be-zu, metaltelin mi-zu le-zu* — if the boats are tied to one another, one may carry from one to the other *lechatchila*. *Im einam kshurot, af al pi she-mukafot, ein metaltelin mi-zu le-zu* — if they are not tied, even if they are close to one another and adjacent, one may not carry between them. This is not a question of domain classification but of *eruv*: when two *reshuyot ha-yachid* belong to different owners, carrying between them requires an *eruv*, and adjacency without formal connection does not suffice.

### Shaking Mayim Sfinah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אִיתְּמַר: סְפִינָה, רַב הוּנָא אָמַר: מוֹצִיאִין הֵימֶנָּה זִיז כׇּל שֶׁהוּא וּמְמַלֵּא. רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי: עוֹשֶׂה מְקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה וּמְמַלֵּא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **It was stated** that the Sages disagreed with regard to the manner in which one may draw seawater onto **a boat** on Shabbat. **Rav Huna said: One extends a projection of any size from** the side of the boat as a distinctive sign, **and fills** a receptacle with water from the sea. **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna say: One creates an area,** a frame **of four** by four handbreadths, **and fills** the water from inside it.

The *Gemara* then raises the question of drawing water from the ocean while on a boat on *Shabbat*. The boat is a *reshut ha-yachid*; the ocean is a *karmelit*. Drawing water would involve transferring from a *karmelit* to a *reshut ha-yachid*, which is rabbinically forbidden. Two *amoraim* offer different solutions, and the disagreement turns on a fundamental question about how to measure the *karmelit* of an ocean.

### Rav Huna Makom

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב הוּנָא אָמַר מוֹצִיא הֵימֶנָּה זִיז כׇּל שֶׁהוּא וּמְמַלֵּא, קָסָבַר כַּרְמְלִית מֵאַרְעָא מָשְׁחִינַן וְאַוֵּירָא מְקוֹם פְּטוּר הוּא, וּבְדִין הוּא דְּזִיז נָמֵי לָא לִיבְעֵי, אֶלָּא — כִּי הֵיכִי דְּלֶיהְוֵי לֵיהּ הֶיכֵּרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Rav Huna,** who **said** that **one extends a projection of any size and fills** a receptacle with water, **maintains** that **we measure** the ***karmelit* from the** sea **floor.** Since the sea itself is deeper than ten handbreadths, the boat is considered to be floating in the air, **and the air is an exempt domain,** as it is above ten handbreadths from the ground of the *karmelit*. **And by law** one **should not require a projection** because he is drawing water from an exempt domain into a private domain, which is permitted *ab initio*. **Rather,** the reason a projection is required is **so that he will have a distinctive sign** and not come to draw water from a *karmelit* into a private domain.

Rav Huna's first opinion is that one creates a platform — a *ziz* — of some minimal size protruding from the side of the boat, and that is sufficient to permit drawing water. His reasoning: *kasavar karmelit me-ara mashchina* — the *karmelit* of the ocean is measured from the ocean floor. The first ten *tefachim* above the ocean floor constitute the *karmelit*; everything above that — the vast body of water in between — is *avir makom patur*, the airspace of a *makom patur*, a neutral space from which transfer to a *reshut ha-yachid* is permitted.

On this view, the water one draws is coming from a *makom patur*, not from a *karmelit* proper, and the prohibition should not apply at all. Rav Huna therefore says that in principle no *ziz* is needed; the only reason to require even a minimal protrusion is *ki haychi de-lahavei lei hikera* — so that there be a visible reminder, something to prevent one from confusing this permitted case with a normal *karmelit* where the prohibition does apply. The *ziz* serves as a kind of mnemonic device, a string around the finger, signaling that something unusual is occurring here.

### Rav Chisda Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב חִסְדָּא וְרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא אָמְרִי עוֹשֶׂה מְקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה וּמְמַלֵּא, קָסָבְרִי כַּרְמְלִית מִשְּׂפַת מַיָּא מָשְׁחִינַן — מַיָּא אַרְעָא סְמִיכְתָּא, אִי לָא עָבֵיד מְקוֹם אַרְבָּעָה — קָא מְטַלְטֵל מִכַּרְמְלִית לִרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna say: One creates an area,** a frame **of four** by four handbreadths, **and fills** a receptacle with water. **They maintain** that **we measure** the ***karmelit* from the surface of the water,** and **the water** in the sea has a legal status **like** that of **solid land.** Therefore, **if one does not create an area of four** by four, **he will carry from a *karmelit* to the private domain.**

Rav Chisda and Rabba bar Rav Huna disagree with this analysis. *Kasavar karmelit misfas mayim mashchina* — the ten *tefachim* of the *karmelit* are measured not from the ocean floor but from the surface of the water. *Maya ara smichta* — the water and the ocean floor are treated as a single compact mass, like thick earth; the ground level for purposes of this calculation is the water's surface.

On this view, the water one draws from the ocean is being taken directly from the *karmelit*, since the water at the surface is within the first ten *tefachim* counted from that same surface. Therefore, without some additional mechanism, drawing water would constitute a transfer from *karmelit* to *reshut ha-yachid*, which is rabbinically prohibited. The solution is *oseh makom arba'ah* — one constructs a small enclosure of four *tefachim* width and ten *tefachim* depth attached to the side of the boat, open at the bottom. Since walls are treated as extending downward, this enclosure creates a column of *reshut ha-yachid* reaching down into the water. One then draws from within that *reshut ha-yachid*, and the transfer is entirely within a single domain — permitted.

The dispute thus turns on a fundamental disagreement about the geometry of a *karmelit* over water: whether we measure from the solid floor beneath, treating the ocean depth as part of the ground, or from the visible surface of the water, treating the entire water column as the operative medium of the *karmelit*.
