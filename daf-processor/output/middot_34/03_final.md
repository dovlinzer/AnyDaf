# Middot 34 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Levite Posts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלשָׁה מְקוֹמוֹת הַכֹּהֲנִים שׁוֹמְרִים בְּבֵית הַמִּקְדָּשׁ, בְּבֵית אַבְטִינָס, וּבְבֵית הַנִּיצוֹץ, וּבְבֵית הַמּוֹקֵד. וְהַלְוִיִּם בְּעֶשְׂרִים וְאֶחָד מָקוֹם. חֲמִשָּׁה, עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי הַר הַבַּיִת. אַרְבָּעָה, עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתָיו מִתּוֹכוֹ. חֲמִשָּׁה, עַל חֲמִשָּׁה שַׁעֲרֵי הָעֲזָרָה. אַרְבָּעָה, עַל אַרְבַּע פִּנּוֹתֶיהָ מִבַּחוּץ. וְאֶחָד בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַקָּרְבָּן, וְאֶחָד בְּלִשְׁכַּת הַפָּרֹכֶת, וְאֶחָד לַאֲחוֹרֵי בֵית הַכַּפֹּרֶת:
>
> **Translation:** In three places the priests keep watch in the Temple: in the chamber of Avtinas, in the chamber of the spark, and in the fire chamber. And the Levites in twenty-one places: Five at the five gates of the Temple Mount; Four at its four corners on the inside; Five at five of the gates of the courtyard; Four at its four corners on the outside; One at the offering chamber; One at the chamber of the curtain, And one behind the place of the kapporet.

The *mishnah* opens with a formulation that should sound familiar — it parallels the opening of *Masechet Tamid*: in three places the *kohanim* keep watch over the *Beis HaMikdash* — in *Beis Avtinas*, *Beis HaNitzots*, and *Beis HaMoked*, those three chambers embedded within the walls of the *azarah*. The *mishnah* then turns to the *leviyim*, who guard in twenty-one places.

### Gates of Har HaBayit

The *mishnah* states: *chamisha chamisha shaarei Har HaBayit* — five *leviyim* at the five gates of the *Har HaBayit*. Looking at the full plan of the *Har HaBayit* complex, one can see the five openings in the outer wall: two on the south side and one each in the remaining three directions. Each of these access points to the *Har HaBayit* had a *levi* stationed at it as a guard.

### Corners of Har HaBayit

Beyond the five gates, the *mishnah* assigns *leviyim* to *arba al arba pinosav* — four guards at the four corners of the *Har HaBayit*, stationed on the inside, *mitcho*. The *gemara* in *Tamid* had raised the curious implication that "inside" extended even to the interior of the gate itself; from this *mishnah*, however, one might have read "inside" simply as referring to the inner corners of the compound. In any case, four *leviyim* occupied those corner positions — likely patrolling back and forth along the adjacent walls.

### Gates of Azarah

*Chamisha chamisha shaarei azarah* — five *leviyim* at the gates of the *azarah*. This is worth pausing over. The *kohanim* guarded three of the *azarah*'s gates, and they did so from above, from an elevated position resembling a balcony. The *leviyim*, by contrast, watched from below, and their posts were more numerous — five compared to the *kohanim*'s three. The *mishnah* speaks of five gates of the *azarah*, though most authorities assume there were at least seven. Either this *tanna* holds that there were only five, or — as was suggested in the discussion of *Tamid* — two of the gates required no additional *levi* because they were already covered by the *kohanim* or were otherwise supervised.

### Corners of Azarah

As with the *Har HaBayit*, four *leviyim* were posted at the four corners of the *azarah* — but here the *mishnah* specifies *mibachutz*, from the outside. This distinction is significant: because the *leviyim* were not permitted to sit and rest within the *azarah* itself, all their posts around the *azarah* — at the gates and at the corners alike — were on the exterior.

### Seven Chamber Posts

Beyond the gates and corners, the *mishnah* enumerates seven additional posts. One *levi* was stationed at *lishkat hakorban*. The *Rosh* understands this as the chamber of the sheep within *Beis HaMoked* — the sub-room where the *tamid* offering was kept for inspection — though this is problematic, since that location is inside the *azarah* and, as noted, the *leviyim*' posts were always on the outside. Alternatively, the reference may be to *Shaar HaKorban*, a gate located on the exterior, and *lishkat hakorban* means the chamber associated with that gate. The question remains open.

A second *levi* was at *lishkat haparochet* — the chamber associated with the *parochet*, the curtain — though its precise location is not entirely clear. And a third was stationed *lachorei beis hakapporet*, at the very back of the *Beis HaMikdash*, behind the *Kodesh Kodashim*. This was necessarily on the exterior, in the narrow space between the rear wall of the *Beis HaMikdash* building and the outer wall of the *Har HaBayit*.

## Oversight & Discipline

### Overseer's Rounds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת הָיָה מְחַזֵּר עַל כָּל מִשְׁמָר וּמִשְׁמָר, וַאֲבוּקוֹת דּוֹלְקִין לְפָנָיו, וְכָל מִשְׁמָר שֶׁאֵינוֹ עוֹמֵד, אוֹמֵר לוֹ אִישׁ הַר הַבַּיִת, שָׁלוֹם עָלֶיךָ.
>
> **Translation:** The officer of the Temple Mount used to go round to every watch, with lighted torches before him, and if any watcher did not rise [at his approach] and say to him, "Shalom to you, officer of the Temple Mount,

The *mishnah* introduces the *Ish Har HaBayit*, the appointed overseer of the *Har HaBayit*: *haya mechazer al kol mishmar umishmar* — he would circulate and check every guard post. It bears emphasizing that *mishmar* here does not refer to the twenty-four-week *mishmar* rotation of *kohanim*, but rather to each individual on guard duty. The overseer would make his rounds to ensure that everyone assigned to a post was alert and attentive. While the watch could apply by day as well, the emphasis throughout this context is on the nighttime watch.

### Punishment Protocol

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִכָּר שֶׁהוּא יָשֵׁן, חוֹבְטוֹ בְמַקְלוֹ. וּרְשׁוּת הָיָה לוֹ לִשְׂרֹף אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ. וְהֵם אוֹמְרִים, מַה קּוֹל בָּעֲזָרָה. קוֹל בֶּן לֵוִי לוֹקֶה וּבְגָדָיו נִשְׂרָפִין, שֶׁיָּשֵׁן לוֹ עַל מִשְׁמָרוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** it was obvious that he was asleep. Then he used to beat him with his rod. And he had permission to burn his clothes. And the others would say: What is the noise in the courtyard? It is the cry of a Levite who is being beaten and whose clothes are being burned, because he was asleep at his watch.

The *mishnah* describes what happened when the *Ish Har HaBayit* arrived at a post and the guard failed to rise and declare *shalom alecha*: it was self-evident that the man had been sleeping. The protocol was twofold. First, *chovto bemaklo* — he would strike him with his staff. Second, and more dramatically, *urshus haya lo lishrof es kesuso* — he had the authority to burn the guard's garment.

This second punishment raises an obvious question about the physical safety of the offender, and the likely answer is that the guard was first made to remove the garment before it was set alight. What makes this detail especially pointed is the observation that the burning takes place *ba'azarah* — in the *azarah*, the very courtyard where animal offerings are consumed by fire. There is an implicit message: someone who abandoned his sacred duty deserves, in a symbolic sense, to share the fate of the sacrifices burned in that place. Moreover, the fact that the guard had garments to burn indicates he had arranged himself for sleep — he had not merely dozed off inadvertently but had deliberately settled in. That intentionality makes the humiliation and punishment more fitting.

The witnesses to this spectacle would call out: *mah kol ba'azarah* — what is that sound in the *azarah*? — and the answer: *kol ben levi loke uvgadav nisrafim sheyeishen lo al mishmaro* — it is a *ben levi* being beaten and his garments being burned because he fell asleep on his watch. The exclamation is attributed to *ba'azarah* even though the guard was technically posted outside, presumably because the commotion could be heard that far.

### R. Eliezer ben Yaakov

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר, פַּעַם אַחַת מָצְאוּ אֶת אֲחִי אִמָּא יָשֵׁן, וְשָׂרְפוּ אֶת כְּסוּתוֹ:
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Eliezer ben Jacob said: once they found my mother's brother asleep, and they burnt his clothes.

*Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* gives not merely a legal opinion here but a firsthand account: *paam achas matzu es achi umi yashen vesarfu es kesuso* — once they found my mother's brother sleeping, and they burned his garment. This is a remarkable passage. According to both the *Bavli* and the *Yerushalmi*, *Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov* is identified as the author of *Masechet Middos* — and possibly *Tamid* as well. He lived at the end of the period of the *Beis HaMikdash*, and his name will appear again in the *masechta*. The personal testimony here lends unusual vividness to the law: this was not theoretical but something he witnessed happening to a family member.

One further question left unresolved: the *mishnah* and the punishment described apply clearly to *leviyim*. What of a *kohen* who fell asleep on his watch? Was the *Ish Har HaBayit* authorized to beat *kohanim* and burn their garments? And if so, one would hesitate to burn *bigdei kehunah* — priestly vestments — so perhaps the protocol was different. The *gemara* does not spell out who supervised the *kohanim* on watch, and the matter is not fully resolved.

## Five Gates Overview

### Shaarei Chulda South

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חֲמִשָּׁה שְׁעָרִים הָיוּ לְהַר הַבַּיִת. שְׁנֵי שַׁעֲרֵי חֻלְדָּה מִן הַדָּרוֹם, מְשַׁמְּשִׁין כְּנִיסָה וִיצִיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** There were five gates to the Temple Mount: The two Huldah gates on the south were used both for entrance and exit;

*Mishnah 3* opens: *chamisha shearim hayu lo leHar HaBayis* — there were five gates to the *Har HaBayit*. In the south: *shnei shaarei Chulda min hadarom* — the two gates of *Chulda*, named for *Chulda HaNeviah*, the prophetess. These were the primary entrances and exits: *meshamshim knisa veyitzia* — they served as the main thoroughfare in and out of the *Har HaBayit*.

### West & North Gates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קִיפוֹנוֹס מִן הַמַּעֲרָב, מְשַׁמֵּשׁ כְּנִיסָה וִיצִיאָה. טָדִי מִן הַצָּפוֹן, לֹא הָיָה מְשַׁמֵּשׁ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Kiponus gate on the west was used both for entrance and exit. The Taddi gate on the north was not used at all.

In the west was *Shaar Kiponos* — the gate of *Kiponos*. One explanation holds that *Kiponos* derives from a Greek word connected to garden work, and that a garden of some kind stood nearby. This gate also functioned as an entrance and exit when needed.

In the north was *Shaar Tedi* — the gate of *Tedi*. The name may be a personal name, or, as some *meforshim* suggest, an Aramaic form of *tari*, meaning private or discreet. This is fitting, because the gate's primary known use was for the *kohen gadol* who experienced a nocturnal seminal emission and had to leave the *Beis HaMoked* quietly before the morning service. The *mishnah* says *lo haya meshamesh klum* — it was not used for anything — but that clearly cannot mean it served no purpose at all, since it was used precisely for that sensitive circumstance.

### Eastern Gate & Parah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שַׁעַר הַמִּזְרָחִי, עָלָיו שׁוּשַׁן הַבִּירָה צוּרָה, שֶׁבּוֹ כֹהֵן גָּדוֹל הַשּׂוֹרֵף אֶת הַפָּרָה וּפָרָה וְכָל מְסַעֲדֶיהָ יוֹצְאִים לְהַר הַמִּשְׁחָה:
>
> **Translation:** The Eastern gate over which was a representation of the palace of Shushan and through which the high priest who burned the red heifer and all who assisted with it would go out to the Mount of Olives.

The eastern gate bore the image or inscription of *Shushan Habira* — Shushan the capital — apparently carved into the arch or stonework. This commemorates the Persian period, when Cyrus granted permission to rebuild the *Beis HaMikdash*, and *Shushan* was the imperial capital at that time.

The eastern gate's primary function was the ceremony of the *Parah Adumah*: *shebo Kohen Gadol soref es haparah* — through it the *Kohen Gadol* (though it need not necessarily be the *Kohen Gadol*) would lead the entire retinue of the red heifer — the cow, the attendants, and all the associated personnel — *yotzim lehar hamishchah*, out toward *Har HaMishchah*, the Mount of Anointing, which is *Har HaZeisim*, the Mount of Olives.

## Topography & Gates

### 3D Temple Structure

Looking at a three-dimensional reconstruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* complex helps clarify what the two-dimensional diagrams can obscure. The *Ezras Nashim* appears as a large courtyard in a cross-like shape when viewed from above, and the *Heichal* building itself was enormous in terms of height relative to everything around it. From the front, one sees the great facade of the *ulam* — the entrance hall — and then beyond it, the *heichal* proper. The *ulam*'s roofline extended slightly beyond the *heichal* when viewed from above, but approaching it directly, one encountered simply the massive frontal section of the structure.

The sight lines of the complex also deserve attention. The general Israelite population, entering through the *Ezras Nashim*, could not see the *kohanim* performing the *avodah*. Only upon passing through *Shaar Nicanor* — the Nicanor gates — and entering *Ezras Yisrael* could one actually observe what was happening in the inner *azarah*. The *leviyim* who sang stood on the *duchan*, the steps inside *Ezras Yisrael* — the straight interior steps, not the rounded steps visible in some reconstructions. The rounded steps and the outer broad stairways served a different function entirely.

A remarkable archaeological find bears mention here: a stone was discovered bearing the inscription *l'veis hatkiya* — "for the house of the *tkiya*," the chamber of the *shofar* blasts. This suggests that a specific location existed where the *tekiyot* were sounded, and archaeological reconstructions have attempted to situate it within the overall complex.

### Geographic Context

The deeper question — why were the main gates in the south rather than the east, which faces the primary axis of the sanctuary? — is answered by geography. The old city, *Ir David*, was built along the southern slope of the mountain. All of ancient Jerusalem was concentrated to the south, climbing up the hillside. Worshippers approaching the *Har HaBayit* were naturally coming from the south, which is precisely why the main gates — *Shaarei Chulda* — were placed there. The south was where the population was.

### Chulda Steps Design

The east presented an entirely different situation. Directly to the east of the *Har HaBayit* lies the Kidron Valley — a dramatic drop-off that makes entry from that direction essentially impossible. That is why there was no functional main gate in the east, even though the *Shaar Mizrachi* opened eastward for the *Parah Adumah* ceremony. When the *Kohen Gadol* and the retinue went out toward *Har HaZeisim*, some kind of passage was navigated, because the *kohen* performing the burning had to stand on *Har HaZeisim* in a position from which he could look back and see *Petach Ohel Moed* — the entrance to the sanctuary. But the main flow of traffic to and from the *Har HaBayit* ran through the south.

What remains from the actual *Shaarei Chulda* steps is illuminating in its own right. The surviving steps — with the lowest two courses being modern reconstructions — are strikingly narrow. Archaeological observation confirms that the narrowness of the steps was not incidental: narrow steps force pedestrians to slow down considerably, functioning as a natural mechanism of traffic control at the primary entrance to the most sacred site in the world.

### Chulda Etymology

This observation connects to one proposed etymology of the name *Chulda*. While the standard identification is with *Chulda HaNeviah*, the prophetess, a secondary suggestion holds that the name reflects the Hebrew word for weasel — *chulda* — and that the weasel-like slowness of movement enforced by the narrow steps gave the gates their name. The etymology is speculative, and the weasel is not obviously a slow-moving creature, but the physical evidence of the steps themselves — designed, it seems, to deliberately moderate the pace of those entering — lends the suggestion a certain plausibility.
