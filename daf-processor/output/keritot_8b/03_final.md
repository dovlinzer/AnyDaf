# Keritot 8b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Safek Births/Flows

### Chatas Tahara

The Mishnah at the end of 8a marks a transition in the tractate's overall thematic arc. Up to this point, the discussion has centered on the *chatas* offered as *kaparah* for *kares*-level transgressions. The Mishnah now shifts to a different category of *chatas* — one that forms part of a *tahara* process rather than an atonement for sin. This theme will become fully explicit in the next *perek*, but the current Mishnah already engages its central cases: a woman who must bring a *chatas* at the conclusion of a *leida* process in order to regain her eligibility to eat *kodshim*. This category is known as *mechusrei kaparah* — those who are lacking the completion of their purification — and it will be the explicit subject of the *perek* to come.

### Multiple Safek Births

Within this framework, the preceding Mishnah addressed a woman who had multiple births or miscarriages within certain time intervals, allowing her to combine obligations and bring one *chatas* for several events. The present Mishnah addresses a related but distinct problem: a woman who is uncertain whether her birth or flow generated an obligation at all — for instance, whether a miscarriage occurred before or after forty days, which is the threshold for *leida* status. Even in such a *safek* situation, she must bring at least one *chatas* to enable her to eat *kodshim*. The ruling is that she brings one, but since her obligation is doubtful, she does not eat it and is not liable for the remaining ones. The one she brings serves the *tahara* function, nothing more.

### Price Controls

The Mishnah concludes with the well-known account of Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, who intervened when the price of birds had risen sharply due to heavy demand. His ruling — that women need not bring their full complement of obligatory *korbanos* — drove down demand and accordingly brought prices back to reasonable levels. This was a form of rabbinic legal activism: it did not render *tamei* women *tahor* without any offering, as they still brought one, but it relieved them of the obligation to bring the full number. Parallel examples of similar interventions exist in other areas of halachic life — pressure on the prices of *lulav* and *esrog*, or communal decisions not to purchase fish for *Shabbos* until prices normalized — though the Gemara itself does not address these parallels.

## Vadai & Safek

### Separate Offerings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָמֵשׁ לֵידוֹת וַדַּאי חָמֵשׁ זִיבוֹת סָפֵק אוֹ חָמֵשׁ לֵידוֹת וַדַּאי וְחָמֵשׁ לֵידוֹת סָפֵק – מְבִיאָה שְׁתֵּי קִינִּין, אַחַת עַל הַוַּדַּאי וְאַחַת עַל הַסָּפֵק, שֶׁל וַדַּאי נֶאֱכֶלֶת וְהַשְּׁאָר עָלֶיהָ חוֹבָה, שֶׁל סָפֵק אֵין נֶאֱכֶלֶת וְאֵין הַשְּׁאָר עָלֶיהָ חוֹבָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita* (*Tosefta* 1:10): If a woman has the obligations of **five definite births and five uncertain discharges of a *zava*, or five definite births and five uncertain births, she brings two nests, one for the definite** obligation **and one for the uncertain** obligation. That which she brought **for** the **definite** obligation is **eaten** by the priests, **and the remaining** offerings **are an obligation for her.** That which she brought **for** the **uncertain** obligation is **not eaten, and the remaining** offerings **are not an obligation for her.**

The Gemara now examines the first portion of the Mishnah, citing a *Tanu Rabbanan*. The best textual reading (*girsa*) of this *braisa* presents the cases in matched pairs: five *vadai* *zivohs* and five *safek* *zivohs*, or five *vadai* births and five *safek* births. In such a scenario, what is the woman's obligation? *Mevia shtei kinim* — she brings two *kinim*, two pairs of birds. *Achas al ha-vadai ve-achas al ha-safek*: one for the definite obligations, one for the doubtful ones. As for the one brought against the *vadai* obligations — *shel vadai ne'echeles ve-ha-shar aleiha chova*: it is eaten by the *kohen* (since it is brought for a definite obligation), but the remaining four *vadai* birds still remain an outstanding obligation, since only one is needed to complete the *tahara* process while the others must still eventually be brought. As for the one brought against the *safek* obligations — *shel safek ein ne'echeles ve-ein ha-shar aleiha chova*: it is not eaten (since a *chatas* brought for a doubtful obligation is not eaten), and the remaining four *safek* birds generate no further liability.

### Separate Scenarios

One might ask: if both the *vadai* and *safek* events occurred simultaneously, why is a second bird needed for the *safek* at all? Surely the one brought for the *vadai* would cover the *safek* as well, since worst case there are ten obligations, and bringing even one suffices for the *tahara* process. The answer is that this *braisa* is in fact not describing a single woman who experienced both categories simultaneously. Rather, it describes four separate scenarios: a woman with only *vadai* events, and a woman with only *safek* events — each subcategory presenting the *ziva* and *leida* variants. If *vadai* and *safek* occurred together, one bird for the *vadai* would indeed suffice. The *braisa* is thus enumerating four distinct halachic situations, not one compound case.

## Last vs. Any

### Last Event Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי אוֹמֵר: עַל וַדַּאי תֹּאמַר: "עַל הָאַחֲרוֹנָה" וְתִפָּטֵר. שֶׁל סָפֵק, אִם יֵשׁ וַדַּאי בֵּינֵיהֶן, תֹּאמַר: "עַל הַוַּדַּאי" וְתִפָּטֵר, וְאִם לָאו, תֹּאמַר: "עַל אֶחָד מֵהֶן" וְתִפָּטֵר. רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא אוֹמֵר: בֵּין עַל הַוַּדַּאי בֵּין עַל הַסָּפֵק, תֹּאמַר: "עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן" וְתִפָּטֵר.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri says:** With regard to offerings brought **for** the **definite** obligation, **she should say:** I am bringing the offering **for the last** definite obligation, **and she is exempt** from bringing the rest of the offerings. The reason is so that she should not think that no atonement is required for the others. With regard to offerings brought **for** the **uncertain** obligation, **if there is a definite** obligation **among** the uncertain offerings **she should say:** I am bringing it **for the definite** obligation, **and she is exempt** from the rest. **But if** there is **no** definite obligation **she should say:** I am bringing the offering **for** any **one of them,** not necessarily for the last one, **and she is exempt. Rabbi Akiva says: Whether** she brings her offering **for the definite** obligation or **whether** she brings it **for the uncertain** obligation, **she should say:** I am bringing it **for one of them, and she is exempt.**

The *braisa* continues with a dispute between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Rabbi Akiva regarding the designation of the *korban*. Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri rules: *ha-vadai tomar al ha'acharona ve-tipater* — in a case of five *vadai* obligations, when she brings the single *korban* that effectuates her *tahara*, she must designate it as being for the *last* event. The logic is sequential: if a woman had one birth in January, February, March, April, and May, and she brings her *korban* in June, designating it for the May birth means it is *metaher* the May birth and thereby everything that preceded it as well. But if she designates it for the January birth, it purifies that first event — while the subsequent births in February through May represent new *tumah* states that arose afterwards and remain unaddressed. The word *ve-tipater* is something of a misnomer here, since the remaining four still remain an obligation; the better term would be *ve-titaher*, since what is being accomplished is the *tahara* process itself rather than a full discharge of all liabilities.

As for the *safek* case: *im yesh vadai beineihem tomar al ha-vadai ve-tipater* — if among the five doubtful events there is one that is *vadai*, she designates the *korban* for that one and is covered. *Ve-im lav* — if all five are *safek*, then *tomar al echad meihem ve-tipater*: she may designate the *korban* for any one of the five and the *tahara* process is satisfied. This leniency for the *safek* case will require explanation, and the Gemara addresses it below.

### Any Designation

Rabbi Akiva disagrees: *bein al ha-vadai bein al ha-safek tomar al echad meihem ve-tipater*. Whether the obligations are *vadai* or *safek*, she may designate the *korban* for any event — earlier, middle, or last — and the *tahara* is accomplished. When she brings the *korban*, it purifies her at that moment regardless of which event she declares it to be for. The *korban* acts as a cleansing act, effective at the time of its offering, whatever its stated designation.

## Chatas/Tevilah

### Chatas Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן בַּר יִצְחָק לְרַב פָּפָּא, אֵימָא לָךְ מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרָבָא: הָנֵי תַּנָּאֵי בְּמַאי פְּלִיגִי, דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי מְדַמֵּי לְהוֹן לְחַטָּאת, דְּמַאן דְּחַיָּיב חָמֵשׁ חַטָּאוֹת עַד דְּמַיְיתֵי כּוּלְּהוֹן וְסוֹף מִכַּפַּר לֵיהּ, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak said to Rav Pappa: I will say** a statement **to you in the name of Rava:** With regard to **these *tanna'im*, with regard to what** matter do they **disagree?** Their disagreement is **that Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri,** who requires her to bring the offering for her definite obligation for the last definite obligation, **compares** the offerings of a woman after childbirth **to a sin offering** brought for a transgression. **As, one who is liable** to bring **five sin offerings** does not achieve atonement **until he brings all of them, and** only the offering that he brings at the **end atones for him. Here too,** it **is no different,** and therefore she achieves atonement and may consume sacrificial food only if she brings an offering for her last obligation.

Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak transmits an explanation in the name of Rava: what is the precise point of contention between these two *tanna'im*? *De-Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri medamei lehu le-chatat* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri treats this *korban* as analogous to a *chatas* in the sense of an atonement for sin. By that model, *de-man de-chayav chamesh chataos, ad de-mayisei kuleihu besof mechaper lei* — a person who committed five sins requiring five separate *chataos* is not fully atoned until he brings all five. Under this model, the single *korban* must at minimum address the final event so that it "covers" everything that came before it. That is why Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri insists the designation be for the last birth or flow.

### Tevilah Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מְדַמֵּי לְהוֹן לִטְבִילוֹת, דְּמַאן דִּמְחַיַּיב חָמֵשׁ טְבִילוֹת, כֵּיוָן דְּטָבֵיל חֲדָא זִימְנָא אִיטְּהַר, הָכָא נָמֵי לָא שְׁנָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Akiva compares** the offerings of a woman after childbirth **to immersions** in a ritual bath. **As** with regard to **one who is obligated** to perform **five immersions, once he** has **immersed one time he is purified. Here too,** it **is no different,** and consequently as soon as she brings an offering for any of the obligations she is ritually pure and is permitted to partake of sacrificial food.

Rabbi Akiva, by contrast, *midamei lah le-tevilah* — compares the *korban* to an act of *tevilah*. If a person became *tamei* five different times — touching five *shratzim* or other sources of *tumah* — he need not immerse five times. One immersion in the *mikvah* cleanses all prior *tumah* states regardless of which one he declares he is immersing for. *Keivan de-taval chada zimna itur* — once he has immersed, the act of *tevilah* is fully effective. So too here, once the woman brings her *korban*, it cleanses her *tahara* state entirely, whatever the designated reason. *Hacha nami lo shnai* — here too it makes no difference.

This raises a broader conceptual question about the nature of the *chatas* here, one that connects to discussions earlier in the tractate. The word *chitui* in the Torah — as in *hu yitchata bo ba-yom ha-shlishi u-va-yom ha-shvi'i*, the sprinkling of the *parah adumah* — means cleansing, not moral atonement. The Hebrew root *cheit* and the related noun *chitoi* throughout the Torah carry the primary sense of purification. This is why a *chatas* can be brought in cases where there is no personal guilt whatsoever — as with the *tinok she-nishba*, a child raised without knowledge of Jewish identity who violated *Shabbos*. There is no moral fault, yet there is a metaphysical rupture that requires cleansing. Similarly, the concept expressed in *ha'aretz lo yechupar* — that the land itself is affected by sin — reflects the Torah's understanding that sin creates a metaphysical reality that must be addressed independently of the individual's guilt. The *chatas* is about cleansing that metaphysical reality, not about the personal culpability of the one who brings it. This is not apologetics; it is the *pshat* of the *pesukim*, reinforced by the *pshtanim* and numerous contexts throughout Tanach. The *chatas* of a *yoledet*, a *zav*, and a *zavah* are all cleansing acts with no sin dimension, and that is precisely why the Gemara frames the debate between Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri and Rabbi Akiva in terms of *tevilah* rather than moral atonement.

## Safek Leniency

### Sin Model Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב פָּפָּא: אִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי לְחַטָּאת מְדַמֵּי לְהוֹן, אַמַּאי שֶׁל סָפֵק תֹּאמַר: "עַל אַחַת מֵהֶן", וְתִפָּטֵר? אִילּוּ מַאן דִּמְחַיַּיב חָמֵשׁ אֲשָׁמוֹת תְּלוּיִין, כִּי מַיְיתֵי חַד מִי מִיפְּטַר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said to** Rav Naḥman bar Yitzḥak: **If** it **enters your mind** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri compares** the offerings of a woman after childbirth **to a sin offering, why** then, with regard to offerings brought **for** the **uncertain** obligation, **should she say:** I am bringing it **for** any **one of them, and she is exempt? If one who is obligated** to bring **five provisional guilt offerings, when he brings one** offering **is he exempt** from bringing the rest? **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that **this is the principle:** In **any** case **where** one's transgressions **are divided with regard to sin offerings,** i.e., where one must bring a separate sin offering for each act when he becomes aware that he has sinned, they are likewise **divided with regard to** provisional **guilt offerings,** when there is uncertainty as to whether or not he sinned?

Rav Pappa raises a sharp objection against the framework as initially stated. If Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri truly holds that this *korban* is analogous to a *chatas* for sin, why is he lenient in the *safek* case, permitting the woman to designate the *korban* for any one of the five doubtful events rather than insisting it be for the last? The parallel case in the world of sin would be *chamesh ashamot teluyim* — five *asham talui* offerings, each for a separate doubtful transgression. There too, *ki mayisi chad mipater* — one is not discharged when only one is brought. Moreover, the principle *kol she-chalukin be-chatas chalukin be-asham* establishes that the *asham* functions analogously to the *chatas*: if five *chataos* are required for five sins, five *ashamot* would be required for five doubtful sins. If Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri is truly working from the sin model, he should demand all five and should certainly require that the single permissible one be for the last event — even in the *safek* case.

### Cleansing Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, דְּכוּלֵּי עָלְמָא לִטְבִילוֹת מְדַמֵּי לְהוֹן, וּבְחוֹשְׁשִׁין לִפְשִׁיעָה קָמִיפַּלְגִי, רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן נוּרִי סָבַר: חָיְשִׁינַן לִפְשִׁיעָה, וְרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא סָבַר: לָא חָיְשִׁינַן לִפְשִׁיעָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, everyone** agrees that **we compare** the sin offerings of a woman after childbirth and a *zava* **to immersions,** and therefore by Torah law it is sufficient for her to say: I am bringing it for one of them. **And they disagree with regard to** whether **we are concerned for** her **negligence. Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Nuri maintains we are concerned for** her **negligence.** In other words, if she says: I am bringing it for one of my obligations, if she gives birth in the following years she might think that she does not need to bring an offering for each birth, and she will neglect to bring an offering. By requiring her to declare: I am bringing the offering for the last definite obligation, the Sages thereby remind her that all her births require offerings. **And Rabbi Akiva maintains we are not concerned for** her **negligence,** and therefore it is sufficient for her to say: I am bringing the offering for one of them.

The Gemara's resolution is decisive: *de-chulei alma le-tevilos medamei lehan* — everyone agrees that this *korban* is analogous to *tevilah*. It is a cleansing act, not an act of atonement for sin. The entire framing of the debate in terms of the *chatas* model was only an initial suggestion; the Gemara now establishes that this is not the actual point of disagreement between the *tanna'im*.

### Negligence Concern

What then is the debate? *U-ve-choshshin li-fsheiah ka-mifligi* — the dispute concerns whether we are concerned about negligence (*peshia*). *Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri savar chaishinan; Rabbi Akiva savar lo chaishinan.* Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri is concerned about a practical danger: if a woman with five births brings her *korban* in June and designates it for the January birth, she may draw the wrong lesson for the future. She may reason that once she brought a *chatas* for the January birth, the subsequent births were already "covered" — and next time, she might bring a *chatas* right after her first birth and assume she need not worry about future births. By requiring that the designation be for the *last* event, the message is reinforced that the *korban* only covers what preceded it at that moment, not what will follow. Rabbi Akiva is unconcerned by this possibility. In the *safek* case, Rabbi Yochanan ben Nuri is lenient because it is rarer and the concern about misapplication is correspondingly lower. But on the underlying principle — that the *korban* functions as a cleansing act analogous to *tevilah* — both agree entirely.

## Four Categories

### Mechusrei Kaparah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אַרְבָּעָה מְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה, וְאַרְבָּעָה מְבִיאִין עַל הַזָּדוֹן כַּשּׁוֹגֵג.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: There are **four** individuals whose halakhic status is defined as: **Lacking atonement [*khappara*],** which means they had been in a state of ritual impurity and underwent rituals to purify themselves, but since they have not yet brought the requisite atonement offering to complete the purification process, they may not partake of sacrificial meat. **And** there are also **four** individuals who **bring** an offering **for an intentional** transgression in the same manner **as** they do for **an unwitting** transgression.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאֵלּוּ הֵן מְחוּסְּרֵי כַּפָּרָה: הַזָּב, וְהַזָּבָה, וְהַיּוֹלֶדֶת, וְהַמְצוֹרָע.
>
> **Translation:** **And these are** the four individuals **who lack atonement: The man who experiences a gonorrhea-like discharge [*zav*], the woman who experiences a discharge of uterine blood after her menstrual period [*zava*], the woman after childbirth, and the leper.** In all four of these cases, although the individual has completed all of the other steps of the purification process, the process is not complete until the atonement offering has been brought.

The Gemara now formally opens the fourth *perek*, whose Mishnah states: *arba mechusrei kaparah ve-arba mevi'in al ha-zadon ki-shgaga* — there are four categories of people whose purification process is incomplete until they bring a *korban* (*mechusrei kaparah*), and there are four categories of people who bring a *chatas* even for willful sin. Normally a *chatas* is brought only for inadvertent transgression, so the latter category is a significant exception. The Mishnah first lists the *mechusrei kaparah*: *ha-zav ve-ha-zavah* — a male or female with an unusual flow; *ha-yoledet* — a woman after childbirth; and *ha-metzora* — a person afflicted with *tzara'as*.

These four individuals share a common status: they complete most of the *tahara* process through immersion, counting of days, and other requirements, but the final step — without which they may not enter the *Beis HaMikdash* or eat *kodshim* — is the bringing of a *korban*. The word *mechusrei kaparah*, while it literally invokes atonement, is used here in the sense of completing a purification process; the connection to sin is absent in all four cases. As already established, the *chatas* here functions as a cleansing of a *tumah* state, not as a response to personal wrongdoing.

It is worth pausing on why these four categories involve *tumah* at all — a broader discussion with significant scholarly literature. Some link *tumah* to the domain of death: illness approaches death, childbirth involves risk of death, and the unusual emissions of a *zav* or *zavah* suggest bodily dysfunction. *Niddah* and *leida* have been explained in terms of unfulfilled potential for life. The general view among those who seek a comprehensive theory of *tumah* centers on proximity to death and its attendant phenomena, tying together the *zav*, *zavah*, *metzora*, and *yoledet* into a coherent framework, though the illness model alone does not account cleanly for *leida* or *niddah*, nor for *tumas meis* from animal carcasses. These are important questions but lie beyond the scope of the present sugya.

### Ger & Nazir

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר בֶּן יַעֲקֹב אוֹמֵר: גֵּר מְחוּסָּר כַּפָּרָה עַד שֶׁיִּזָּרֵק עָלָיו הַדָּם. נָזִיר – לְיֵינוֹ וְתִגְלַחְתּוֹ וְטוּמְאָתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer ben Ya'akov says: A convert** also **lacks atonement,** even after he has been circumcised and has immersed in a ritual bath, **until** the priest **sprinkles the blood** of his offering on the altar **on his** behalf. **A nazirite** also lacks atonement **with regard to his** permission for drinking **wine, and cutting his hair, and his** exposure to **ritual impurity** imparted by a corpse, until his offerings are sacrificed.

Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov adds two further cases to the list of *mechusrei kaparah*: a *ger* who has converted must bring a *korban* — *ger mechusar kaparah she-yizrok alav ha-dam*, a convert requires the sprinkling of blood — before he may eat *kodshim*. And a *nazir* who completes his term of *nezirus* brings a *korban chatas* as part of the process that ends his *nazirus* and permits him to drink wine, cut his hair, and become susceptible to *tumah* again.

What Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov is effectively doing is broadening the category of *mechusrei kaparah* beyond *tumah*-related purification. For the *tana kama*, the category is defined by: a *chatas* that enables the person to eat *kodshim* at the conclusion of a *tahara* process. Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov redefines it more broadly: any *korban* that effects a change of status — whether from non-Jew to Jew, or from *nazir* to non-*nazir* — qualifies. Notably, regarding the *ger* specifically, Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov also appears to see the *korban* as tied to *tahara* in some sense, connecting conversion to a transition from *tumah* to *tahara* — an interesting framing that perhaps parallels the way immersing vessels in a *mikvah* is understood, though this framing of the non-Jew as *tamei* is obviously theologically fraught and deserves careful attention.

### Zav/Zava Split

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁנָא זָב וְזָבָה דְּמָנֵי לְהוֹן בִּתְרֵין – מִשּׁוּם דַּחֲלוּקָהּ טוּמְאָתוֹ, דְּזָב לָא מְטַמֵּא בְּאוֹנֶס וְזָבָה לָא מְטַמְּאָה בִּרְאִיּוֹת בְיָמִים,
>
> **Translation:** **What is different** about **a *zav* and a *zava*, that** the *tanna* decided to **count them as two** separate cases? The Gemara answers: They are different **due to** the fact **that the impurity of** a *zav* **is distinct** from that of a *zava*, **as a *zav* does not become impure** if he has a discharge **by accident,** i.e., due to a cause such as an illness or having consumed certain foods, whereas there is no such exemption in the case of a *zava*; **and a *zava*,** unlike a *zav*, **does not become impure by** three **sightings** of menstrual-type blood on one day **like** she becomes impure **by** sightings on three consecutive **days.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דתניא: "מבשרו" – ולא מחמת אונסו. (ומי טמא) [ומיטמא] בראיות כבימים, דתניא: תלה הכתוב את הזכר בראיות ואת הנקבה בימים. וזבה מטמאה באונס, ולא מטמאה בראיות כבימים.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "When any man has a discharge from his flesh, his discharge is impure" (Leviticus 15:2). The term **"from his flesh"** teaches that the discharge is impure only if it is due to an internal cause, **and not due to his accident. And** furthermore, a *zav* **becomes impure by** three **sightings** of gonorrhea-like discharge on one day **like** he becomes impure **by** three such sightings on three consecutive **days, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: **The verse links** the impurity of **the male,** i.e., a *zav*, **to** the number of **sightings** on the same day or on consecutive days, **and** of **the female** *zava* **to** the number of consecutive **days** on which she experiences sightings of blood. **And** by contrast, **a *zava* becomes impure** if she has a discharge **by accident, and does not become impure by** three **sightings** on one day **like** she becomes impure **by** sightings on three consecutive **days.** Therefore, the mishna counts them as two separate cases.

The Gemara asks why the Mishnah counts *zav* and *zavah* as two separate categories rather than one. The answer: *de-chaluka tumasam* — their *tumah* is fundamentally different in its mechanics. *De-zav lo metame be-ones* — a *zav* does not become *tamei* if the emission arises through *ones*: that is, if the flow comes as a result of illness or other involuntary external cause and emerges without the characteristic features of *ziva*, it does not generate *tumah*. The Gemara's description indicates that *ziva* for a male involves a discharge that emerges in a manner associated with the sexual organs' activity — the Gemara later in tractates on this subject specifies the nature of the substance and the manner of its emission. A *zavah*'s *tumah*, by contrast, *metamei be-ones* — even if the blood flow is caused by illness or external factors, it is still considered *zivah*. Additionally, *zav metame be-re'iyos ki-yamim* — a male *zav* becomes *tamei* on the basis of three separate sightings, even if they occur on the same day, whereas *de-tana* — as the Baraita teaches — by a female, the Torah uses the language of *yamim rabim*, multiple days, meaning the three sightings must occur on three separate days. These are substantial differences in how *tumah* is acquired and defined, justifying the counting of *zav* and *zavah* as two distinct categories.

### Metzora Gender

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִלְכָּךְ נַמְנִינְהוּ בִּתְרֵין! זָב וְזָבָה – עִיקַּר טוּמְאָתָן חֲלוּקָה, מְצוֹרָע וּמְצוֹרַעַת – אֵין עִיקַּר טוּמְאָתָן חֲלוּקָה, הַאי וְהַאי כִּגְרִיס הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara now resumes stating its objection: **Therefore, let us count** the male and female leper **as two** separate cases, as the mishna does with regard to the *zav* and *zava*. Why does the mishna not do so? The Gemara explains: With regard to **a *zav* and *zava*,** they are treated as separate cases because the **essence of their impurity is distinct,** as the impurity of a *zav* is based on the number of sightings of discharge, whereas the impurity of a *zava* is based on the number of consecutive days on which she experiences sightings of menstrual-type blood. By contrast, with regard to **a male leper and female leper,** the **essence of their impurity is not distinct,** as in both **this** case of a male leper **and that** case of a female leper, the impurity **is** based on a leprous mark which is at least the area **of a split bean.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמְצוֹרַעַת אֵינָהּ טְעוּנָה פְּרִיעָה וּפְרִימָה, דְּתַנְיָא: אֵין לִי אֶלָּא אִישׁ, אִשָּׁה מִנַּיִן? כְּשֶׁהוּא אוֹמֵר: "וְהַצָּרוּעַ" – הֲרֵי כָּאן שְׁנַיִם.
>
> **Translation:** **And** by contrast, **a female leper does not require letting** her **hair grow wild and rending** her **garments, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: "He is a leprous man" (Leviticus 13:44): **I have** derived **only** that **a man** can be a leper; **from where** do I derive that **a woman** can also be a leper? **When** the verse **states: "And the leper** in whom the mark is" (Leviticus 13:45), without specifying a man, the verse teaches that **there are two here,** i.e., a man and a woman.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן מָה תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אִישׁ"? נִתְּקוֹ הַכָּתוּב מֵעִנְיָן שֶׁל מַעְלָה לְעִנְיָן שֶׁל מַטָּה, לוֹמַר: אִישׁ פּוֹרֵעַ וּפוֹרֵם, וְאֵין הָאִשָּׁה פּוֹרַעַת וּפוֹרֶמֶת. וּמוּתֶּרֶת בְּתַשְׁמִישׁ הַמִּטָּה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְיָשַׁב מִחוּץ לְאׇהֳלוֹ שִׁבְעַת יָמִים" – וְלֹא מִחוּץ לְאׇהֳלָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* asks: **If so, why** does **the verse state:** "A leprous **man"?** The *baraita* answers: **The verse removed it from the matter** stated **earlier,** and applied it **to the matter** stated **later:** "His garments shall be rent, and the hair of his head shall go wild," **to say** that **a man** who is a leper **lets his hair grow wild and rends his garments, but the woman** who is a leper **does not** need to **let her hair grow wild and** does not **rend her garments. And** a female leper **is permitted to** engage in **sexual intercourse, as it is stated: "And he shall dwell outside his tent for seven days"** (Leviticus 14:8), **and not: Outside her tent.**

The Gemara then asks: should male and female *metzora* also be counted as two categories, since they too differ? The answer is: *ein ikar tumasam chaluka* — their fundamental *tumah* is the same. Both become *tamei* through *nega'im* on the body; the size (*ke-gris*) and appearance of the blemishes are identical for both. The differences that exist are only in the *behaviors* required of the *metzora* after becoming *tamei*: a male *metzora* is required to rend his garments, let his hair grow wild, and is forbidden from *tashmish ha-mita* — the latter derived from *ve-yashav michutz le-oholo*, meaning outside of his tent, understood as separation from his wife. A female *metzora* is exempt from all three: *eina te'una peria u-ferimah*, she does not rend her clothes or let her hair grow wild, *u-muteres be-tashmish ha-mita*, and she is not forbidden from marital relations. The Baraita derives this from the verse *ish ki yihye bo nega* — "a man who has a *nega*" — which specifies the male-specific behaviors without excluding women from *tumah* itself. The general words *ve-ha-tzarua ve-ha-metzora* include women in the *tumah*, while the word *ish* applies specifically to the behavioral restrictions. The *Gemara* notes this asymmetry is worth reflection — why would these particular practices not apply to a woman? One possible consideration involves the framework of *tashmish ha-mita* and the husband's rights; another involves the unseemly appearance that *peria* and *perimah* would create. Neither explanation is fully satisfying on its own, and the connection to the *sotah* procedure (where a woman's hair is uncovered as part of the process) may suggest that such outward displays of physical dishevelment carry a particular gendered valence in halachic discourse.

## Ger Conversion

### Ger Kodashim

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גֵּר מְעוּכָּב לֶאֱכוֹל בְּקָדָשִׁים עַד שֶׁיָּבִיא קִינּוֹ, הֵבִיא פְּרִידָה אַחַת שַׁחֲרִית – אוֹכֵל בְּקָדָשִׁים לָעֶרֶב. כׇּל הַקִּינִּים שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה – אֶחָד חַטָּאת וְאֶחָד עוֹלָה, כָּאן – שְׁתֵּיהֶן עוֹלוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A convert is precluded from partaking of sacrificial** meat **until he brings his bird nest,** i.e., his offering of a pair of birds, either pigeons or doves. If he **brought one young bird [*perida*]** in the **morning,** he **may partake of sacrificial** meat **in the evening,** and may bring the second bird at a later time. Furthermore, with regard to **all** other cases **in the Torah** where **bird nests** are mandated as offerings, **one** of the birds is brought as **a sin offering and** the other **one** as **a burnt offering;** but **here,** in the case of the convert, **both of them** are **burnt offerings.**

The Gemara now presents a *Tanu Rabbanan* that forms the core of the discussion of the *ger*'s *korban*. A *ger* is prevented from eating *kodshim* until he brings his *kan* — his pair of birds. This is the *korban* associated with conversion, and it functions as the completion of his transition to full Jewish status with respect to the Temple service and its sanctities.

### Ger Korban Type

The specific nature of the *ger*'s *korban* is established as follows. *Heivi pereidah achas shacharis* — if he brought even one of the two birds in the morning, *ochel be-kodshim le-erev* — he may eat *kodshim* that evening. The parallel to the *yoledet* is explicit: just as a woman who brings one *korban* out of her full obligation completes the *tahara* process even if the rest remain outstanding, so too a *ger* who brings one bird has satisfied the immediate requirement for *tahara* and eligibility for *kodshim*, even though the second bird remains an obligation. Importantly, the *korban* of a *ger* consists not of one *chatas* and one *olah* as would be the case with most *kinim* in the Torah, but of *shtei olos* — two *olos*. *Kol ha-kinim she-ba-Torah achas chatas ve-achas olah; kan shtei olos* — normally when the Torah mandates two birds as an obligatory pair, one is a *chatas* and one is an *olah*; here, both are *olah*.

### Har Sinai Model

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵבִיא חוֹבָתוֹ מִן הַבְּהֵמָה – יָצָא, עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים – יָצָא, מִנְחָה וּשְׁלָמִים – לֹא יָצָא. לֹא אָמְרוּ קֵן אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: If, instead of birds, the convert **brought** a burnt offering **from an animal** as **his obligatory** offering, he has **fulfilled** his obligation, and if he brought **a burnt offering and a peace offering,** he has also **fulfilled** his obligation; but if he brought **a meal offering and a peace offering,** he **has not fulfilled** his obligation. This is because **a bird nest was stated** in the Torah **only to be lenient** for him. Therefore, if he voluntarily brought an animal, which is more expensive, as a burnt offering, he has fulfilled his obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי שְׁנָא מִנְחָה וּשְׁלָמִים דְּלֹא יָצָא, דִּכְתִיב: "כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשׂוּ כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה", מָה אַתֶּם עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים – אַף גֵּר עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is different** about **a meal offering and peace offering, that** if the convert brings these he **has not fulfilled** his obligation? The Gemara answers: It is **as it is written:** "And if a stranger dwell with you, or whosoever may be among you, throughout your generations, and will offer an offering made by fire of a pleasing aroma unto the Lord; **as you do, so he shall do"** (Numbers 15:14). From this verse it is derived: **Just as you** brought **a burnt offering and a peace offering** when you entered the covenant on Mount Sinai, as it is stated: "And they sacrificed burnt offerings, and they sacrificed peace offerings" (Exodus 24:5), **so too, a convert** must bring **a burnt offering and a peace offering.**

The source for why a *ger* brings at all, and why the *korban* is specifically an *olah*, derives from the verse in Bamidbar 15:14: *ve-chi yagur itchem ger o asher be-tochechem le-doroseichem ve-asa ishe rei'ach nichoach la-Hashem ka'asher ta'asu ken ya'aseh* — when a *ger* dwells among you and brings a fire-offering of pleasing aroma to God, he shall do as you do. *Ka'asher ta'asu ken ya'aseh* — as you did, so shall he do. The Gemara understands this not as a directive about what a *ger* must do when he brings voluntary *korbanos*, but as establishing what he must bring upon becoming a *ger*: the same *korban* you brought when you became Jewish. What did the Jewish people bring when they entered the covenant? At the foot of Har Sinai, at the conclusion of Parshas Mishpatim, *va-yishlach es na'arei Bnei Yisrael va-ya'alu olos va-yizbechu zvachim shelamim la-Hashem parim* — Moshe sent the young men of Israel who offered *olos* and *shelamim* to God. Therefore, *ma atem olah u-shelamim af ger olah u-shelamim* — just as Israel brought an *olah* and *shelamim*, so too a *ger* must bring an *olah* and *shelamim*.

### Two Birds Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִם כֵּן, חוֹבָתוֹ אַחַת מִן הַבְּהֵמָה לָא תִּיסְגֵּי עֲלֵיהּ בְּגַוֵּויהּ, דְּהָכְתִיב: "כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשׂוּ כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה"! אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא, יֵשׁ לוֹמַר: לְעוֹף אִיתְרַבִּי, לְעוֹלַת בְּהֵמָה לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** in a case where he brought **one** offering **from an animal** for **his obligation, that should not be enough for him, as isn't it written: "As you do, so he shall do,"** which teaches that he must bring both a burnt offering and a peace offering? Why, then, does the *baraita* state that a single burnt offering from an animal is sufficient? **Rav Pappa says:** One **can say** that this is derived from an *a fortiori* inference: **If a bird** offering **was included** as an inexpensive way for a convert to fulfill his obligation, would the Torah **not all the more so** include a more expensive **animal burnt offering?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ מִנְחָה נָמֵי! מַיעֵט קְרָא: "כֵּן".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **If so,** then **even a meal offering** should fulfill the convert's obligation. The Gemara answers: **The verse restricts** the acceptable offerings, excluding a meal offering, as it states: "Just as you do, **so** he shall do," meaning that he must not do less than that.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וָעוֹף הֵיכָא אִיתְרַבִּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: "כַּאֲשֶׁר תַּעֲשׂוּ כֵּן יַעֲשֶׂה" – מָה אַתֶּם עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים, אַף הוּא עוֹלָה וּשְׁלָמִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כָּכֶם כַּגֵּר". מִנַּיִן לְרַבּוֹת אֶת הָעוֹף? תַּלְמוּד לוֹמַר: "אִשֵּׁה רֵיחַ נִיחֹחַ לַה׳" – אֵיזֶה דָּבָר שֶׁכּוּלּוֹ לַה׳? הֱוֵי אוֹמֵר [זוֹ] עוֹלַת הָעוֹף.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And where was a bird** offering **included** as an option? The Gemara answers that this is **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states: **"As you do, so he shall do"** (Numbers 15:14): **Just as you** entered the covenant with **a burnt offering and a peace offering, so too,** a convert fulfills his obligation with **a burnt offering and a peace offering, as it is stated: "As you are, so** shall **the stranger** be" (Numbers 15:15). **From where** is it derived **to include** the option of **the bird** offering instead of an animal? **The verse states: "An offering made by fire, of a pleasing aroma unto the Lord"** (Numbers 15:14). **Which item is** an offering that is **entirely for the Lord? You must say:** This is referring to **a bird burnt offering,** which is entirely consumed upon the altar.

One might then ask: if the obligation is modeled on *olah* and *shelamim* — presumably large animals — why do we permit the *ger* to fulfill the obligation with two birds? The answer involves the principle that one cannot bring a single bird as an independent *korban*. The Baraita states: *she-lo matzinu be-chol ha-Torah kulla* one bird as a standalone *korban* — in the entire Torah, birds are never brought singly as an obligatory offering; they always come in pairs, either two birds or a bird paired with a larger animal. This pairing principle means that if birds are used at all, two are required. But this is a leniency — *ela le-hakel* — a concession allowing the *ger* to fulfill his obligation without bringing large animals. This raises the further question: why then is not even a *minchah* permitted, if we are being lenient? The Gemara's answer is that the verse *ka'asher ta'asu ken ya'aseh* preserves a minimum standard of equivalence — it must be like what the people brought, which involved *damim*, blood. A *minchah*, regardless of how much it costs, lacks blood and therefore does not qualify. Birds, as animal offerings involving blood, satisfy this minimum criterion.

## Milah/Tevilah

### Milah Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: "כָּכֶם" – כַּאֲבוֹתֵיכֶם, מָה אֲבוֹתֵיכֶם לֹא נִכְנְסוּ לַבְּרִית אֶלָּא בְּמִילָה וּטְבִילָה וְהַרְצָאַת דָּם – אַף הֵם לֹא יִכָּנְסוּ לַבְּרִית אֶלָּא בְּמִילָה וּטְבִילָה וְהַרְצָאַת דָּמִים.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* continues: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The offering of a convert is derived from the verse: **"As you are,** so shall the stranger be" (Numbers 15:15), which means **as your ancestors** were: **Just as your ancestors entered the covenant only through circumcision and immersion** in a ritual bath **and the sprinkling of blood** on the altar, **so too they may enter the covenant only through circumcision and immersion and the sprinkling of** some **blood,** which requires at least a bird offering.

The Gemara now turns to Rebbi's teaching that the Har Sinai model establishes not only the requirement of a *korban* for a *ger*, but all three components of *geirus*: *mah avoseichem lo nichnesu la-bris ela be-milah u-tevilah ve-hartz'as dam* — your ancestors entered the covenant only through *milah*, *tevilah*, and the acceptance of a blood-offering. Rebbi adds the paradigm of *geirus* as a whole, with all three elements sourced from the events at Har Sinai.

Where is *milah* sourced? *Ki mulim hayu kol ha-am ha-yotzim* — the verse in Yehoshua records that all the men who left Egypt had been circumcised, and since bringing the *korban Pesach* requires one to be circumcised, it is evident that *milah* was part of the status of those who entered the covenant. The Gemara also cites the verse from Yechezkel: *va-avor alayich va-erech misboseses be-damayich va-omar lach be-damayich chayi*, referring to the blood of *milah* and the blood of the *korban Pesach* as the dual *damim* through which Israel's covenant was sealed.

A significant halachic discussion emerges here. It is well-attested in *midrashic* tradition that the tribe of Levi did not require a special *milah* before the *korban Pesach* in Egypt — they had maintained the tradition of *bris milah* continuously since Avraham. This means their *milah* was not performed as a formal act of *geirus* or covenant-entry, but simply as the continuation of ancestral practice. From this, many *Rishonim* derive that a *milah* performed as a Jewish *bris milah* — even without specific intent for *geirus* — satisfies the *milah* requirement for a *ger*. The practical application is significant: a baby born to a Jewish father and non-Jewish mother who receives a *bris milah* as a standard Jewish circumcision, and later undergoes conversion, may rely on that original *milah* without requiring a new procedure or even, in some opinions, a ritual drawing of blood (*hatafas dam bris*). The consensus follows that such a *milah* is sufficient. This is not a peripheral leniency; it is a substantive and well-grounded position among the *Rishonim* and has real implications for contemporary conversion practice.

### Tevilah Sources

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַרְצָאַת דָּמִים, דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּשְׁלַח אֶת נַעֲרֵי בְּנֵי יִשְׂרָאֵל". אֶלָּא טְבִילָה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּקַּח מֹשֶׁה חֲצִי הַדָּם וְיִזְרוֹק עַל הָעָם" – וְאֵין הַזָּאָה בְּלֹא טְבִילָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: And granted as well, they entered the covenant through **the sprinkling of blood, as it is written: "And he sent the young men of the children of Israel,** and they sacrificed burnt offerings, and they sacrificed peace offerings" (Exodus 24:5). The Gemara asks: **But from where do we** derive that **immersion** in a ritual bath was also part of the process of entering the covenant? The Gemara answers: **As it is written: "And Moses took half the blood**…**and sprinkled it on the people"** (Exodus 24:6–8), **and there is no sprinkling** of sacrificial blood **without immersion.**

The source for *tevilah* as a requirement for conversion is derived from the verse describing the events at Har Sinai: *va-yikach Moshe chatzi ha-dam va-yizrok al ha-am* — Moshe took half the blood and sprinkled it on the people. The Gemara reasons: *ein haza'ah belo tevilah* — sprinkling in a *tahara* context, as with the ashes of the *parah adumah*, requires prior immersion. Therefore the sprinkling of blood upon Israel at Har Sinai presupposes that they had first immersed. This derivation does, as one might note, depend on viewing the *dam ha-bris* as operating within a *tahara* framework; if it is primarily a covenantal act rather than a *tahara* ritual, the source for *tevilah* is less direct. The tension between viewing the Har Sinai *korban* as a *tahara* process versus a covenantal ritual is precisely the deeper question underlying the dispute between Rabbi Eliezer ben Yaakov and the *Chachamim*.

### Korban Sources

The source for the *korban* requirement is explicit: *va-yishlach es na'arei Bnei Yisrael* — Moshe sent the young men of Israel and they offered *olos* and *shelamim*. This is the act by which Israel entered the covenant at Har Sinai, and it is the act that a *ger* replicates when he converts.

### Olah/Shlamim

The derivation from *ishe rei'ach nichoach* — "a fire-offering of pleasing aroma" — focuses the essential obligation on the *olah*, understood as *eizehu davar she-kulo la-Hashem*, the component that is entirely consumed before God. This verse licenses any form of *olah*, including a bird *olah*, thereby permitting the use of birds. The *shelamim* component, while modeled on Har Sinai, is subsumed under the broader principle of *kachem ka-ger yihyeh* — he is like you — and *la-chem hukashnu ve-lo le-korbanoseichem*, he is equated to you personally, not to your specific offerings. Therefore the precise composition of an *olah* and *shelamim* is not absolutely mandated; the minimum is an *olah* in any form.

## Ola Focus

### Ola Emphasis

The derivation from *ishe rei'ach nichoach* in Bamidbar 15:14 serves to establish the *olah* as the primary and essential component of the *ger*'s *korban*. *Eizehu davar she-kulo la-Hashem* — what is it that is entirely for God? *Hevei omer zeh olas ha-of* or simply *zeh olah* — this must refer to the *olah*, the offering wholly consumed upon the altar. The inclusion of bird *olos* under this rubric is what enables the leniency permitting birds.

### Blood Required

Why can a *minchah* not serve even where leniency might otherwise be applied? *Mah atem minei damim* — as your ancestors entered the covenant through blood-offerings, so too the *ger* must bring something involving blood. The emphasis on blood at Har Sinai is not incidental: it is the *dam ha-bris* that created the binding relationship between Israel and God. A *minchah*, even if fully burned as a kind of substitute *olah*, lacks this blood dimension and therefore cannot fulfill the requirement. *Ka'asher ta'asu ken ya'aseh* sets a minimum standard that cannot be met by a bloodless offering.

### Pairing Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְהָבִיא פְּרִידָה אַחַת – אִי אֶפְשָׁר, שֶׁלֹּא מָצִינוּ בְּכׇל הַתּוֹרָה כּוּלַּהּ. לֹא אָמְרוּ קֵן אֶלָּא לְהָקֵל עָלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The *baraita* adds: And it **is impossible to bring** only **one young bird** as an offering, **as we have not found** an offering of a single bird anywhere **in the entire Torah.** Consequently, if the requirement for the sprinkling of blood is fulfilled with a bird offering, the convert must bring at least two birds. By contrast, if he chooses he may bring a single animal as a burnt offering, because **a bird nest was stated** in the Torah **only** in order **to be lenient for him.**

If the *ger* fulfills his obligation with a large animal *olah*, one animal suffices. But if he uses birds, two are required. As established earlier, there is no instance in the entire Torah of a single bird brought as a standalone obligatory *korban*. Whether the pairing is with a second bird or with a large animal (as in the case of the *yoledet*, who brings one bird *chatas* paired with a ram *olah*), birds always come paired. This principle thus generates the specific ruling of two bird *olos* for the *ger* when he cannot or does not bring an animal.

## Ger Today

### Set Aside Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: גֵּר בִּזְמַן הַזֶּה – צָרִיךְ שֶׁיַּפְרִישׁ רוֹבַע לְקִינּוֹ. אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן: כְּבָר נִמְנָה (עָלָיו) [עָלֶיהָ] רַבָּן יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי וּבִטְּלָהּ, מִפְּנֵי הַתַּקָּלָה. אָמַר רַב אִידִי בַּר גֵּרְשׁוֹם אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: הֲלָכָה כְּרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **A convert in the present time is required to set aside a quarter** of a dinar **for his bird nest,** so that when the Temple is rebuilt he can purchase the offering with this money. **Rabbi Shimon says: Rabban Yoḥanan ben Zakkai already** assembled a panel of Sages who **voted and nullified** this ordinance, **due to** a potential **mishap.** If one would inadvertently use such money, he would be liable for the misuse of consecrated property. **Rav Idi bar Gershom says** that **Rav Adda bar Ahava says:** The ***halakha* is in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon,** who says that a convert should not set aside money for the purchase of offerings.

The *Tanu Rabbanan* teaches that a *ger* in the present era — after the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash* — is required to set aside a *rova* (a quarter of a silver *dinar*, the minimum price of two birds at the time) designated for his *korban*, so that when the *Beis HaMikdash* is rebuilt he will be able to fulfill his obligation. Even though the *korban* is not a prerequisite for *geirus* in the absence of the Temple — as derived from *kachem ka-ger yihyeh*, which establishes that there is always the possibility of a *ger* among Israel and therefore conversion cannot be contingent on what is currently impossible — the underlying obligation of bringing the *korban* remains, to be discharged in the future.

### Practice Nullified

Rabbi Shimon records that Rabban Yochanan ben Zakkai voted to nullify this practice *mipnei ha-takalah* — because of the danger of inadvertent *me'ilah*. If money was set aside as *kodshim* funds, there was a risk that the owner would use it for ordinary purposes, thereby violating the prohibition of *me'ilah*. The Gemara concludes: *Amar Rav Idi bar Gershom amar Rav Adda bar Ahava halacha ke-Rabbi Shimon* — the halacha follows Rabbi Shimon, and no such money is set aside today.

### Korban Replacement

This discussion prompts a fundamental conceptual question: what replaces the *korban* now that the *Beis HaMikdash* is gone? The Gemara's presentation of the Har Sinai model is instructive here. The *korban* at the foot of Har Sinai was not merely a ritual act; it was the tangible expression of the covenant itself. Moshe sprinkled the blood and declared *zeh dam ha-bris asher karat Hashem imachem al kol ha-devarim* — this is the blood of the covenant that God has made with you concerning all of these matters. The *korban* symbolized the *ger*'s acceptance of obligation and entry into a covenantal relationship with God. When a *ger* brought a *korban* to the Temple, this act — in the context of Jewish sacred service — conveyed something analogous: a statement of relationship, obligation, and belonging.

It is notable that several key Talmudic sources on *geirus* enumerate only *milah* and *tevilah*, without explicitly mentioning *kabbalas mitzvos*. Other sources do mention *kabbalas mitzvos*, but do not discuss the *korban*. This may not be coincidental. When the *korban* was available, it served as the covenantal act — the concrete expression of the *ger*'s acceptance of the obligations of Jewish life. Without the *korban*, an explicit verbal declaration of *kabbalas mitzvos* becomes the necessary substitute, filling the same function: expressing the *ger*'s entry into the covenant and acceptance of its demands. The *korban* and *kabbalas mitzvos* may thus be understood as functional equivalents — two forms of the same covenantal act, one ritual and sacrificial, the other verbal and declarative — which is why the sources that have the *korban* do not stress *kabbalas mitzvos*, and why the sources operative today emphasize *kabbalas mitzvos* as the essential third element alongside *milah* and *tevilah*.
