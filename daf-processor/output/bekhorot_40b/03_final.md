# Bekhorot 40b — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Doubled Ear & Pig Tail

### Daf Opening

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ אוֹזֶן הַגְּדִי הָיְתָה כְּפוּלָה, אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: בִּזְמַן שֶׁהוּא עֶצֶם אֶחָד — מוּם, בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ עֶצֶם — אֵינוֹ מוּם. רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָא בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: זְנַב הַגְּדִי שֶׁהוּא דּוֹמֶה לְשֶׁל חֲזִיר, וְשֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שְׁלֹשׁ חוּלְיוֹת — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **the ear of the kid** that **was doubled** and appeared like two ears, **the Sages said: When** the additional ear **is one bone,** i.e., it has its own cartilage, it is **a blemish; when it does not have** its own **bone it is not a blemish. Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel says:** In the case of **the tail of a kid that is similar to** that **of a pig or** one that is so short **that it does not have three joints, that is a blemish.**

We begin today on 40b, picking up in the *mishnah* as it continues its survey of which physical characteristics in an animal constitute a *mum* and which do not.

The *mishnah* presents the following case: *ozen hagedi hayeta kfulah* — the ear of a goat was doubled over. The Sages rule: *bizman shehu etzem echad mum* — if it is a single cartilage (Rashi understands the word *etzem* here to mean the cartilage of the ear), meaning the cartilage has folded over upon itself, causing the ear to fold, that is a *mum*. *Bizman she'ein bo etzem* — or, according to the alternative textual version, *ve'im eino etzem echad*, if it is not one cartilage but rather two separate cartilages, as if a distinct flap is protruding from the ear, then *eino mum* — it is not a *mum*.

This is, on its face, counterintuitive. One might have expected a fully separate additional cartilage to be more abnormal and more disfiguring than a single folded-over one. Indeed, Rashi himself acknowledges as much, noting *ta'ama lo isparish* — he cannot fully explain the distinction. He does suggest that the doubled single-cartilage case is more visible than an extra flap, but the reasoning remains elusive. This difficulty is emblematic of a broader challenge running throughout this *perek*: we are given many specific cases but precious few overarching principles by which to understand them.

### Pig Tail Standard

*Rabbi Chanina ben Gamliel* says that if the tail of a goat resembles that of a pig — *zanav hagedi shehu domeh leshel chazir* — or if it lacks three vertebrae — *she'ein bo shalosh chulyot* — then *harei zeh mum*, that is a *mum*. An unusually shaped or unusually short tail on a goat is sufficiently abnormal and visible to qualify.

### Korban Relevance

The practical stakes of all these determinations become apparent when one considers the restoration of *korbanot*. If an animal has any unusual feature, one must ask: how unusual does it need to be before it disqualifies the animal from being offered? For animals that are entirely normal, there would be no question at all.

### Yerushalmi Questions

It is worth noting that there is very little *Bavli* discussion of these topics — the *gemara* tends to focus its analysis on areas where it has stronger traditions. And there is no extant *Yerushalmi* on *Kodashim*, though the matter is more complicated than it might appear. The *Rambam* in his introduction to his *Peirush Hamishnayot* states that a *Yerushalmi* on *Kodashim* did exist. Yet no manuscript was ever known, and when, roughly a century ago, someone claimed to have discovered one and used it to resolve hundreds of difficult *Rambam*s, it was ultimately exposed as a forgery. One might speculate that in *Eretz Yisrael* it was simply too sensitive — too close to the living reality of the *Beit HaMikdash* — to engage with *Kodashim* extensively, whereas in *Bavel* the discussion could proceed at a greater distance.

## Swelling & Ear Cases

### Mouth & Legs Swollen

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: פִּיו בָּלוּם וְרַגְלָיו מְבוּלָּמוֹת, מֵחֲמַת הָרוּחַ — אֵינוֹ מוּם, מֵחֲמַת הָעֶצֶם — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם. אׇזְנָיו כְּפוּלוֹת, בְּחַסְחֲסוּת אַחַת — הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם, בִּשְׁתֵּי חַסְחֲסֻיוֹת — אֵינוֹ מוּם.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **The Sages taught:** In a case where **the mouth** of a firstborn animal is **swollen, and** likewise, if **its legs are swollen,** the following distinction applies: If it is swollen **due to the wind,** i.e., it simply swelled up, **it is not a blemish.** If it is swollen **due to** a particularly large **bone, this is a blemish.** If **its ears are doubled,** in a case **where** it has **one cartilage, this is a blemish;** in a case **where** it has **two cartilages, this is not a blemish.**

The *gemara* opens with a *baraita*: *piv balum veraglav mevulamot* — if the mouth or legs are *balum*, Rashi offers two explanations: either narrowed and constricted, or swollen and puffed up. If this condition came about *machmat haruach* — as a result of some natural cause such as an infection or the like — then *eino mum*, it is not a *mum*. If, however, it came about *machmat ha'etzem* — because the bone itself is abnormally thick or deformed — then *harei zeh mum*, it is a *mum*. Some have the textual variant *machmat atzmo*, meaning it is naturally deformed in that way as opposed to being caused by some external condition.

### Ears Doubled Rule

The *gemara* then gives a corresponding formulation regarding the ear: *oznav kfulot be-chas-chus achat* — if the ears are doubled with a single cartilage, *harei zeh mum*; *bi-shtei chas-chusiyot* — if there are two separate cartilages, *eino mum*. As noted above, Rashi frankly admits he does not know the reason for this distinction.

### Rounded Tail Shape

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חֲנַנְיָה בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: זְנַב הַגְּדִי שֶׁהוּא דּוֹמֶה לְשֶׁל חֲזִיר. אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: לָא תֵּימָא דְּקַטִּינָא, אֶלָּא דִּכְרִיכָא, אַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲלִימָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabban Gamliel says:** With regard to **the tail of a kid that is similar to** that **of a pig,** that is a blemish. **Rav Pappa says: Do not say** that it is a blemish only **where** its tail is **thin** like that of a pig; **rather,** if it is **round,** i.e., curly like a pig's tail, **even though it is thick,** like the tail of a goat, this is considered a blemish.

*Rav Pappa* then offers a clarification about the pig-like tail: *lo teima dekatina* — do not think the concern is only that the tail is small, since a pig's tail is indeed small and short. Rather, *de'kericha af al gav de'alima* — even if the tail is of normal thickness or size, if it is rounded like a pig's tail, that suffices. As for the meaning of "rounded," Rashi explains that the cross-section of a pig's tail is more circular, whereas that of other animals is more oblong. The possibility that "rounded" means curled is also a natural reading, and Rashi does address that as well.

### Vertebrae Count

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אוֹ שֶׁאֵין בָּהּ שָׁלֹשׁ חוּלְיוֹת וְכוּ׳. אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: בַּגְּדִי — שְׁתַּיִם הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם, שָׁלֹשׁ אֵינוֹ מוּם. בַּטָּלֶה — שָׁלֹשׁ הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם, אַרְבַּע אֵינוֹ מוּם. מֵיתִיבִי: בַּגְּדִי — אַחַת הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם, שְׁתַּיִם אֵינוֹ מוּם. בַּטָּלֶה — שְׁתַּיִם הֲרֵי זֶה מוּם, שָׁלֹשׁ אֵינוֹ מוּם. תְּיוּבְתָּא דְרַב הוּנָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated: **Or** if the tail of a kid is so short **that it does not have three joints,** that is a blemish. **Rav Huna says: In** the case of the tail of **a kid,** if it has **two** joints, **that is a blemish;** if it has **three** joints, **it is not a blemish. With regard to a lamb,** if it has **three** joints, **that is a blemish;** if it has **four** joints, **it is not a blemish.** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*: **In** the case of **a kid,** if its tail has **one** joint, **that is a blemish;** if it has **two** joints, **it is not a blemish. With regard to a lamb,** if it has **two** joints, **that is a blemish;** if it has **three** joints, **it is not a blemish.** The Gemara concludes: This is **a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rav Huna.**

*Rav Huna* then establishes a rule about vertebrae: *be-gedi shtayim harei zeh mum* — by a goat, if it has only two vertebrae, that is a *mum*; three is within the normal range and not a problem. *U've-taleh* — by a sheep, the count is one higher: three is a *mum*, four is acceptable.

### Rav Huna Error

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הוּנָא, מַתְנִיתִין אַטְעִיתֵיהּ; אִיהוּ סָבַר: מִדְּרֵישָׁא בִּגְדִי — סֵיפָא נָמֵי בִּגְדִי, וְלָא הִיא — רֵישָׁא בִּגְדִי, וְסֵיפָא בְּטָלֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **And Rav Huna** issued his statement because **the mishna misled him. He thought** that **from** the fact **that the first clause** is referring **to a kid,** whose tail is similar to that of a pig, it can be inferred that **the latter clause,** with its mention of three joints, is **also** referring **to a kid. But it is not so.** Rather, **the first clause** is referring **to a kid, but the latter clause** is referring **to a lamb,** as explained in the *baraita*.

A *baraita* is then cited that directly contradicts Rav Huna. According to the *baraita*: *be-gedi achat harei zeh mum* — by a goat, even one vertebra is a *mum*, and two is already normal enough; by a sheep, two is a *mum* and three is fine. This *baraita* is thus stricter than Rav Huna by one vertebra in each case. The *gemara* concludes: *tiyuvta de-Rav Huna tiyuvta* — this constitutes a full refutation of Rav Huna.

Why did Rav Huna err? The *gemara* explains: *Rav Huna matnitin it'aya* — Rav Huna was misled by the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* had been discussing the doubled ear of a *gedi*, a goat, and then went on to discuss the tail that lacks three vertebrae. Rav Huna assumed that since the *mishnah* began by speaking of a goat, the tail law also referred to a goat. But the *gemara* says *reisha be-gedi ve-seifa be-taleh* — the beginning spoke about a goat and the end spoke about a sheep. When the *mishnah* says "goat" explicitly, it means goat; but the default, absent specification, is sheep. This was a very honest and understandable mistake — but a mistake nonetheless.

## Eye & Mouth Blemishes

### Yavelet & Mouth

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: אֶת שֶׁיַּבֶּלֶת בְּעֵינָיו, וְשֶׁנִּפְגַּם עֶצֶם יָדוֹ וְרַגְלוֹ, וְשֶׁנִּפְרַק עַצְמוֹ שֶׁל פִּיו, עֵינוֹ אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה וְאַחַת קְטַנָּה, אׇזְנוֹ אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה וְאַחַת קְטַנָּה, בַּמַּרְאֶה אֲבָל לֹא בַּמִּדָּה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: שְׁתֵּי בֵּיצָיו, אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה כִּשְׁתַּיִם שֶׁבַּחֲבֶירְתָּהּ, וְלֹא הוֹדוּ לוֹ חֲכָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says** that these are blemished animals: One **that has a wart in its eyes; and** one **where the bone of its foreleg or hind leg was damaged; and** one **where the bone of its mouth,** i.e., its jaw, **was dislocated;** and an animal with **one of its eyes large and one small,** or **one of its ears large and one small** where the difference in size is detectable **by sight, but not** if it is detectable only **by being measured. Rabbi Yehuda says:** An animal is blemished if with regard to **its two testicles, one is as large as two of the other, but the Rabbis did not agree with his** opinion.

*Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* — one of the more obscure *Tannaim* we have been encountering — says: *yavelet be'einav* — if an animal has a *yavelet*, some type of protrusion or growth, in its eye, that is a *mum*. *Ve'she'nifgam etzem yado ve'raglo* — or if the bone of the foreleg or hind leg was nicked (a lesser injury than the broken bone discussed previously). *Ve'she'nifrak atzmo shel piv* — or if the bone of the mouth — which Rashi interprets as the gums — was loosened or detached. One might have expected a gum injury to be more serious than a tooth injury discussed earlier, but since the gums are almost entirely concealed by the lips, a lesser degree of injury does not suffice; only something that is essentially detached would constitute a *mum*. It is not entirely clear whether anyone disputes this ruling.

### Eyes & Ears Size

The *mishnah* continues: *eino achat gedola ve'achat ketana* — if one eye is large and the other small, or *ozno achat gedola ve'achat ketana* — one ear larger and one smaller, these are a *mum*, but only *be-mareh* — if they are visibly different. *Ve'lo be-mida* — one does not take out a ruler and measure precise dimensions; a barely measurable difference is not sufficient. Only a visible disproportion constitutes a *mum*.

### Testicle Ratio

*Rabbi Yehuda* adds: *shtei beitzim* — regarding testicles — *achat gedola ki-shtayim she-be-chavertah* — if one is twice the size of the other, that too is a *mum*. The Sages, however, did not agree, because this particular asymmetry is far less visible to the observer. The *gemara* makes clear that we are speaking of the testicles themselves, not the appearance of the scrotum — which Rabbi Yehuda might well have agreed would be a *mum* were it visibly deformed.

### Yavelet Conflict

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ לְמֵימְרָא דְּיַבֶּלֶת הָוֵי מוּמָא? וּרְמִינְהוּ: וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין עֲלֵיהֶן לֹא בְּמִקְדָּשׁ וְלֹא בַּמְּדִינָה — בַּעַל גָּרָב וּבַעַל יַבֶּלֶת!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if an animal has a wart in its eyes, it is blemished. The Gemara asks: Is this **to say that a wart is** considered **a blemish? And** one can **raise a contradiction** from the mishna (41a): **And these** are the blemishes **that one does not slaughter** the firstborn **due to them, neither in the Temple nor in** the rest of **the country,** as these are not considered full-fledged blemishes…an animal **with boils** that are moist inside and out, **and** one **with warts.**

The *gemara* now raises a sharp difficulty: the *mishnah* just stated that a *yavelet* on the eye is a *mum* — one that would permit *shechita* outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. Yet a later *mishnah* (which we will reach shortly) places *ba'al yavelet* in an intermediate category: not a *mum* sufficient to permit slaughter, but problematic enough that the animal cannot be offered as a *korban*. Moreover, the *Torah* itself explicitly states *o garav o yavelet* — listing *garav* and *yavelet* among the *mumim* of a *bechor*. So we have a three-way tension: the *Torah* says it is a full *mum*, our *mishnah* says it is a full *mum* on the eye, and the later *mishnah* says it is only a middle-category disqualification.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִסְבְּרָא? הָא כְּתִיבָא ״יַבֶּלֶת״ בְּאוֹרָיְיתָא! אֶלָּא, לָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בְּגוּפוֹ, הָא — בְּעֵינוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **And** how can **you understand** that a wart is not a blemish? After all, **a wart is written in the Torah** in its list of blemishes (see Leviticus 22:22). **Rather,** it is **not difficult,** as **that** mishna, which states that a wart is not a full-fledged blemish, is referring to a blemish **on** the animal's **body,** whereas **this** mention of a wart in the Torah and the mishna here is referring to a wart **in its eye.**

The *gemara* offers an initial resolution: *ha begufa ha be'ayna* — the distinction is between the body and the eye. A *yavelet* on the eye is a full *mum* because it is far more disfiguring; a *yavelet* on the rest of the body falls into the middle category. The *Torah*'s ruling likewise refers to the eye. The *gemara* then challenges this: the *pasuk* says nothing of the kind — *kra stama ketiv*, it speaks without any qualification, making no distinction between the eye and the body. The first resolution is therefore rejected.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי קְרָא סְתָמָא, מָה לִי בְּגוּפוֹ וּמָה לִי בְּעֵינוֹ? אֶלָּא לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא דְּאִית בַּהּ עֶצֶם, הָא דְּלֵית בַּהּ עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Now** consider that **the verse** is written in **an unspecified** manner. **What** difference is it **to me** if the wart is **on its body, and what** difference is it **to me** if the wart is **in its eye?** How is this distinction derived from the verse? The Gemara suggests an alternative explanation: **Rather,** it is **not difficult,** as **this** is referring to a wart **that has a bone,**and **that** is referring to a wart **that does not have a bone.**

The *gemara* then proposes a second answer: *ha de'is bei etzem ha deleis bei etzem* — the distinction is not between eye and body but between whether the growth contains a bone or not. The *Torah*'s case of *yavelet* as a full *mum* involves a growth with a bone, which represents a genuine and serious deformity. Our *mishnah* about the eye and the later *mishnah* about the middle category both involve a *yavelet* without a bone — just a skin flap. In the eye, even a boneless skin flap is sufficiently disfiguring to be a full *mum*; on the body, a mere skin flap without a bone falls into the middle category.

## Yavelet Analysis

### Yavelet w/ w/o Bone

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דִּקְרָא — דְּאִית בַּהּ עֶצֶם, מַתְנִיתִין — דְּלֵית בַּהּ עֶצֶם; בְּעֵינוֹ — הָוֵי מוּמָא, בְּגוּפָא — לָא הָוֵי מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara elaborates: The wart mentioned **in the verse** and the mishna here is one **that has a bone,** whereas the wart addressed by **the mishna** there is one **that does not have a bone.** Consequently, if the wart is **in its eye, it is a blemish,** as stated in the verse and the mishna here, but if it is **on** its **body, it is not a blemish,** although the animal is disqualified from being sacrificed, in accordance with the ruling of the mishna there.

To summarize the framework emerging from the *gemara*: the *Torah*'s ruling that *yavelet* is a *mum* refers to a growth containing a bone — a genuine structural deformity. Both of our *mishnayot* are discussing a boneless growth, merely a skin flap (*tiltul*). Within that category, the *gemara* draws a further distinction between the eye and the rest of the body.

### Eye vs Body

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֵית לַהּ עֶצֶם בְּגוּפָא פְּסוּלָה? הֲרֵי תִּלְתּוּל בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, דִּתְנַן: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: בַּעֲלֵי הַתִּילּוּלִין פְּסוּלִין בָּאָדָם וּכְשֵׁירִין בַּבְּהֵמָה! אֶלָּא, אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בְּעֵינוֹ, וְלָא קַשְׁיָא: הָא — בַּשָּׁחוֹר, הָא — בַּלָּבָן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to a wart that **does not have a bone** and is **on the body,** is the animal **disqualified** from being sacrificed? **It is merely hanging** flesh, **as we learned** in a mishna (45b) that **Rabbi Eliezer says:** With regard to those **with** flesh or skin that **hangs** from their body, such a blemish **disqualifies a person, but with regard to** such **an animal,** it is **fit. Rather,** both **this** mishna here **and that** mishna there are referring to a wart **in the** animal's **eye, and** it is **not difficult,** as **this** mishna is referring to a wart **in the black** of the eye, the pupil, whereas **that** mishna is referring to a wart **in the white** of the eye.

The *gemara* then presses a further question: if the *yavelet* in question has no bone, is it really meaningful to say it is even a middle-category disqualification on the body? *Harei tiltul be'alma hu* — it is just a trivial skin flap; why should it matter at all? This touches on a deeper conceptual challenge that runs throughout the *perek*: how do we define *tamim*? Is the absence of a *mum* sufficient to render an animal *tamim*, or is *tamim* a separate, more demanding standard? And what kinds of conditions are even significant enough to enter the discussion — what makes something worth paying attention to in terms of disfigurement? Color variation, for instance, is presumably not enough to constitute a *mum*. So where does the skin flap fall?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אֵין מוּמִין בַּלָּבָן! אֶלָּא: אִידֵּי וְאִידֵּי בַּלָּבָן, וְאָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: לָא קַשְׁיָא, הָא — דְּאִית בַּהּ שֵׂעָר, הָא — דְּלֵית בַּהּ שֵׂעָר.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** it has been established that **there are no blemishes in the white** part of the eye, which indicates that the animal is not even disqualified from being sacrificed on the altar. **Rather,** both **this** statement that there are no blemishes in the white of the eye **and that** ruling of the mishna that a wart disqualifies the animal from being sacrificed are referring to a wart **in the white** part of the eye. **And Reish Lakish says:** It is **not difficult; this** is referring to a wart **that has a hair,** and therefore the animal is unfit for the altar, whereas **that** statement is referring to a case **where** the wart **does not have a hair.** As for the mishna here, which rules that a wart is a full-fledged blemish, it is referring to a wart in the pupil of the eye, as explained.

The *gemara* brings a ruling of *Rabbi Eliezer*: *ba'alei hatiltulin pesulin be'adam u'ksherim bivehema* — people (*kohanim*) with skin flaps are disqualified for Temple service, but animals with them are entirely acceptable. This raises an apparent difficulty: if skin flaps are entirely acceptable for animals, how can our *mishnah* say they fall into the middle category? The *gemara* responds: *ela idi ve'idi be'einav* — both cases concern the eye. The question then becomes what distinguishes the two *mishnayot* when both deal with a skin flap on the eye.

One proposed distinction, reflecting the *girsa* in the text and the reading of Rashi: *ha beshachar ha belavan* — one case concerns the black of the eye and one concerns the white of the eye. A skin flap on the black of the eye is sufficiently disfiguring to be a full *mum*; one on the white of the eye is less so, placing it in the middle category. The *gemara* then raises a further objection: *ein mumim belavan* — there are, apparently, no *mumim* recognized in the white of the eye at all, which makes it puzzling to say the white-of-eye case even reaches the middle category. The reason for this categorical exclusion of the white of the eye is a matter of its own complexity; the *gemara* elsewhere seems to want to define the relevant *mum* of the eye — *tavlul be'eino* — quite narrowly.

*Tosafot*, troubled by the principle *ein mumim belavan*, prefers a different *girsa*: instead of *ha beshachar ha belavan*, Tosafot reads both cases as being in the black of the eye — *idi ve'idi beshachar*. What then distinguishes the two cases? *Ve'amar Reish Lakish lo kashya ha de'it bei se'ar ha delait bei se'ar* — the distinction is whether hair is growing out of the skin flap. If hair grows from the flap, that is significantly more disfiguring — a full *mum*; if there is no hair, it is a lesser disqualification. The mechanics of how a skin flap with hair could grow from the eyeball are admittedly difficult to visualize, though the possibility was raised that some types of ocular tumors or growths, including papillomas, do occur on and around the eye in animals, and hair-bearing growths are apparently not unknown. In any case, Tosafot's reading preserves the consistent principle that the white of the eye is never the basis of a *mum*, while Rashi's *girsa* allows for at least some role for the white of the eye in the middle category.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עֵינוֹ אַחַת גְּדוֹלָה וְכוּ׳. תָּנָא: גְּדוֹלָה כְּשֶׁל עֵגֶל, קְטַנָּה כְּשֶׁל אַוָּוז.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches with regard to an animal where **one of its eyes** is **large** and one is small that this is a blemish. In this regard, it is **taught** in a *baraita*: When the mishna states the term **large,** it means the eye is as large **as** the eye **of a calf,** and when it states the term **small,** it means it is as small **as** that of **a goose.**

The *gemara* also cites a *baraita* about the disproportionate size ruling: *gedola keshel egel* — if an eye is as large as that of a calf, or *keshel avaz* — as small as that of a duck, those extremes would be too large or too small. It should be noted that this *baraita* appears to be reading the *mishnah*'s formulation of *achat gedola ve'achat ketana* differently: the *mishnah* seems to refer to disproportionality between the two eyes of the same animal, whereas the *baraita* in the *gemara* seems to address absolute size — whether one eye is abnormally large or small compared to the norm — and then specifies how large or small would be too much. This may represent a genuine interpretive divergence between the *mishnah* and the *baraita*, and how we rule on that question remains unresolved.

Regarding the Sages' disagreement with Rabbi Yehuda on testicles, the *gemara* cites a *baraita* suggesting that even a testicle as small as a bean would not constitute a *mum* according to the Sages — implying they may not recognize any size disproportion of testicles as a *mum* regardless of degree.

## Tail Length

### Calf Tail Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ זְנַב הָעֵגֶל — שֶׁאֵינָהּ מַגַּעַת לָעַרְקוֹב, אָמְרוּ חֲכָמִים: כֹּל מַרְבִּית עֲגָלִים כֵּן, כׇּל זְמַן שֶׁיְּהוּ מְגַדְּלִין — הֵן נִמְתָּחוֹת. אֵיזֶהוּ עַרְקוֹב שֶׁאָמְרוּ? רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בֶּן אַנְטִיגְנוֹס אוֹמֵר: בְּעַרְקוֹב שֶׁבְּאֶמְצַע הַיָּרֵךְ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: In the case of **the tail of a calf that does not reach the leg joint [*la'arkov*], the Sages said:** It is a blemish, because **all growth of calves** is in **this** manner:**As long as they grow,** their tails **are extended** beneath the leg joint. **Which is the leg joint** about **which** the Sages **spoke? Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus says:** They are referring **to** the **leg joint that is in the middle of the thigh.**

The *mishnah* states: *zanav ha-egel she-eina maga'at l'arkov* — the tail of a calf that does not reach the *arkov*, the knee joint, is a *mum*. The Sages add: *kol marbit agalim kach hen* — this is the normal situation of all calves. *Kol zman sheheim magdilot hen nimtachot* — as they grow, the tail continues to stretch and lengthen. Rashi reads this not as a disagreement but as an agreement: the Sages are affirming that the normal developmental pattern is for the tail to reach and then extend beyond the knee joint as the animal grows, so an animal whose tail falls short of that range is outside normal and is therefore a *mum*.

### Joint Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנָא: קֶפֶץ הָעֶלְיוֹן וְלֹא קֶפֶץ הַתַּחְתּוֹן, וּכְנֶגְדּוֹ בְּגָמָל נִיכָּר.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: With regard to the case in the mishna of the tail of a calf that does not reach the leg joint, it is **taught** in a *baraita*: This leg joint is **the upper joint,** between the thighbone and the tibia, **and not the lower joint,** between the middle and lower bones. **And** with regard to this upper joint, **the corresponding** bone **in** the leg of **a camel is conspicuous,** but in a calf it is not noticeable from the outside.

The *mishnah* then asks: *eizu hi arkov?* — which knee are we speaking of? *Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* says: *ba-arkov she-be'emtza ha-yarech* — the joint in the middle of the thigh, which corresponds to what we would call the knee, as opposed to the lower joint closer to the foot (which in bovines is higher up than a human ankle but is analogous to it). A *baraita* adds: *kaf ha-elyon ve'lo kaf ha-tachton* — the upper joint, not the lower one. *U'kenegdo be-gamal nikar* — and this joint is particularly visible and recognizable in camels.

## Wrap-Up & Middle

### Consensus Mishna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ עַל אֵלּוּ מוּמִין שׁוֹחֲטִין אֶת הַבְּכוֹר, וּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִפְדִּין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **For these blemishes** enumerated in the previous *mishnayot*, **one slaughters the firstborn** outside the Temple **and disqualified consecrated** animals **may be redeemed on their account.**

The *mishnah* now offers a wrap-up statement: *al elu mumim shochtin et ha-bechor u'psulei ha-mukdashin nifdin aleihem* — on all the *mumim* enumerated above, one may slaughter the *bechor*, and if such a condition occurred in other *korbanot*, they may be redeemed on account of it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הָא תּוּ לְמָה לִי? הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ רֵישָׁא: עַל אֵלּוּ מוּמִין שׁוֹחֲטִין אֶת הַבְּכוֹר! סֵיפָא אִיצְטְרִיכָא: פְּסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִפְדִּין עֲלֵיהֶן. הָא נָמֵי פְּשִׁיטָא — מִישְׁחָט שָׁחֲטִינַן, מִיפְרָק מִיבַּעְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Why do I** need **this additional** statement that for these blemishes one slaughters the firstborn outside the Temple? It was already **taught** in **the first clause,** at the beginning of the chapter: **For these blemishes, one may slaughter the firstborn** animal outside the Temple. The Gemara answers that **the latter clause** of the mishna was **necessary: Disqualified consecrated** animals **may be redeemed due to** these blemishes. The Gemara asks: But **this too** is **obvious;** if **one may slaughter** a firstborn due to these blemishes, **is** it **necessary** to state that **one may redeem them?**

The *gemara* asks: why is this wrap-up statement needed at all? *Ha'tana lei reisha* — did we not already establish at the very opening of the *perek* that these *mumim* permit *shechita* of the *bechor*? The *gemara* answers: *seifa itzterich* — the necessary addition is the second clause: *u'psulei ha-mukdashin nifdin aleihem* — to inform us that the same standard applies to other *korbanot* as well, equating the *mum* of a *bechor* with that of other *kodashim*. The *gemara* then suggests even this is obvious: if we can slaughter on account of a *mum*, surely we can redeem on account of it. But that obviousness only holds if we assume the categories are the same across *bechor* and other *korbanot* — an assumption the *Torah*'s more detailed treatment of other *korbanot* might have led one to question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא, אַיְּידֵי דִּתְנָא שְׁלֹשָׁה הוֹסִיף, וְאָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא שָׁמַעְנוּ אֶת אֵלּוּ, וְקָתָנֵי וְאָתֵי דִּיחִידָאָה, וְקָא סָתֵים לַהּ בְּכוּלְּהוּ: עַל אֵלּוּ מוּמִין שׁוֹחֲטִין אֶת הַבְּכוֹר וּפְסוּלֵי הַמּוּקְדָּשִׁין נִפְדִּין עֲלֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, since** a previous mishna **taught** that Ila **added three** additional blemishes, **and** the Sages **said to him: We did not hear** about **those, and** then the *tanna* of the mishna **proceeded to teach** *halakhot* in the name **of individual** opinions, those of Rabbi Ḥananya ben Gamliel and Rabbi Ḥanina ben Antigonus, one might have thought that the *halakha* is not in accordance with their rulings. Therefore, he states **an unattributed** ruling in this mishna **with regard to all their** statements: **For** all **these blemishes** enumerated in the previous *mishnayot*, **one slaughters the firstborn, and disqualified consecrated** animals **may be redeemed due to them.** This indicates that the *halakha* is in accordance with all of these individual opinions.

The *gemara* concludes: *ela aidei de'tana shalosh osif* — the wrap-up *mishnah* is needed because in yesterday's discussion, the opinion of *Ila* added three additional *mumim* that the Rabbis said *lo shamanu* — we have no tradition about. And the subsequent *mishnayot* present rulings in the names of individual *Tannaim* — notably *Rabbi Chanina ben Antignos* — without explicit recorded disagreement. The wrap-up *mishnah* therefore comes to tell us that *de'kasasnehu be'chulehu* — despite all the individual attributions and earlier uncertainties, the ruling is consensus: all of these are agreed-upon *mumim* for purposes of both *shechita* of the *bechor* and redemption of other *korbanot*. The one explicit debate earlier in the *perek* was resolved when the later *Beit Din* accepted that position as well, so by now everything is in agreement.

### Middle Category

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְאֵלּוּ שֶׁאֵין שׁוֹחֲטִין עֲלֵיהֶן, לֹא בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ וְלֹא בַּמְּדִינָה: חֲוַורְוָר, וְהַמַּיִם שֶׁאֵין קְבוּעִין, וְחוּטִין הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת שֶׁנִּפְגְּמוּ — אֲבָל לֹא שֶׁנֶּעֶקְרוּ, וּבַעַל גָּרָב, וּבַעַל יַבֶּלֶת, וּבַעַל חֲזָזִית, זָקֵן, וְחוֹלֶה, וּמְזוֹהָם, וְשֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בּוֹ עֲבֵירָה, וְשֶׁהֵמִית אָדָם — עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And these** are the blemishes **that one does not slaughter** the firstborn **due to them, neither in the Temple nor in** the rest of **the country: Pale spots** on the eye **and tears** streaming from the eye **that are not constant; and internal gums that were damaged but that were not extracted; and** an animal **with boils** that are moist inside and out **[*garav*]; and** an animal **with warts; and** an animal **with boils [*ḥazazit*];** and **an old or sick** animal, **or** one with **a foul odor; and** one **with which a transgression was performed,** e.g., it copulated with a person or was the object of bestiality; **and** one **that killed a person.** In these latter two cases, the act of bestiality or killing became known **on the basis** of the testimony **of one witness or on the basis of the owner.**

The *mishnah* now introduces a distinct and important intermediate category: *ve'elu she'ein shochtin aleihem lo ba'mikdash ve'lo ba'medinah* — these are animals upon which one may not slaughter, neither in the *Beit HaMikdash* nor outside it. This is the worst of all positions: the animal is not *tamim* enough to be offered as a *korban*, yet it lacks a sufficiently definitive *mum* to permit slaughter outside the *Beit HaMikdash*. One can do nothing with it.

## Transitory Blemishes

### White Spots

The first items in the middle category: *chavarur* — white spots in the eye — and *hamayim she'einan kvu'im* — water in the eye that is not fixed, meaning the condition is not permanent. Rashi explains *chavarur* as white spots on the eye. The underlying principle governing both cases can be generalized: any *mum over*, any transitory *mum*, falls into this middle category. While the condition persists, one cannot bring the animal as a *korban*; but because the *mum* may resolve, one may not slaughter it either.

### Inner Molars

The *mishnah* continues: *ve'hachittin hapnimiyot she'nifgemu aval lo she'ne'ekru* — the roots of the inner molars that were damaged or nicked but not completely uprooted. This is notable because the inner molars are not visible externally. This raises a question about whether the standard of *tamim* for bringing a *korban* is simply the mirror image of the absence of a *mum*, or whether it is an independent and more demanding requirement — one that may be triggered even by non-visible conditions. Alternatively, perhaps under certain circumstances these molars can be observed, or perhaps the standard of visibility for *korbanot* is different from that for *mumim* generally.

## Skin Conditions (II)

### Scabs & Warts

The *mishnah* lists: *u'va'al garav u'va'al yavelet* — those with *garav* (scabs or hardened growths) or *yavelet* (warts or skin protrusions) — and *u'va'al chazazit* — another variety of wart or skin condition. All of these fall into the middle category: not a full *mum* permitting slaughter, but not acceptable for offering as a *korban*. As established in the earlier *gemara* discussion, these conditions on the body — as opposed to the eye — are insufficiently disfiguring to constitute a full *mum*, yet they prevent the animal from being *tamim*.

## Non-Blemish Issues (II)

### Age Sick Smelly

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַזָּקֵן וְהַחוֹלֶה וְהַמְזוֹהָם. מְנָהָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִן הַצֹּאן וּמִן הַכְּשָׂבִים וּמִן הָעִזִּים״ — פְּרָט לַזָּקֵן וְלַחוֹלֶה וְלַמְזוֹהָם.
>
> **Translation:** § Among the conditions that are not considered full-fledged blemishes, but one may not sacrifice an animal with one such condition as an offering either, the mishna lists: **And an old** animal, **or a sick** animal, **or** one with **a foul odor.** The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught:** The verse states: "And if his offering be **from [*min*] the flock, whether from the sheep or from the goats"** (Leviticus 1:10). These three instances of the word "from [*min*]" serve to **exclude the old, and the sick, and the** animal with **a foul odor,** which may not be sacrificed.

The *mishnah* now introduces a categorically different set of disqualifications — not physical deformities at all, but factors relating to the animal's condition and history. *Zaken* — if the animal is very old; *ve'choleh* — or sick; *u'mezoham* — or smelly. These are not *mumim* in the conventional sense, but they render the animal inappropriate as a *korban*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכִי, דְּאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא לְמַעוֹטֵי זָקֵן, דְּלָא הָדַר בָּרֵיא, אֲבָל חוֹלֶה, דַּהֲדַר בָּרֵיא — אֵימָא לָא. וְאִי כְּתַב לְמַעוֹטֵי חוֹלֶה, דְּלָאו אוֹרְחֵיהּ, אֲבָל זָקֵן, דְּהַיְינוּ אוֹרְחֵיהּ — אֵימָא לָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara notes: **And** all three exclusions **are necessary. As, had the Merciful One written** the word "from" once, one might have said that this serves **to exclude an old** animal, **which will not become healthy again,** as it will never get younger. **But** with regard to a **sick** animal, **which** might **become healthy again, say** that it is **not** disqualified. **And,** alternatively, **had** the Merciful One **written** only one term of exclusion, one might have said that it serves **to exclude a sick** animal, **as** it is **not the** natural **manner** of an animal to be sick. **But** with regard to **an old** animal, **as this is the** natural **manner** of all animals to grow old, **say** that it should **not** be disqualified from the altar.

The *gemara* makes clear that all three are necessary and none could be inferred from the others. Had the *Torah* excluded only the old (*zaken*), one might have thought sickness is different because the animal may recover — *delo hadar bari*; had it excluded only the sick, one might have thought old age is different because it is the normal condition of any living being — *dehainu orchia*. Had it excluded only two, one might have thought the smelly is different because it is not physically weakened — *delo kachish*; or conversely, had it excluded only the smelly because it is repulsive — *mishum dema'is* — one might have thought the merely old or sick are not so repulsive and therefore not excluded. Each of the three possesses a distinct character, and therefore all three exclusions are necessary.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא תְּרֵי, לְמַעוֹטֵי הָנֵי דִּכְחִישִׁי, אֲבָל מְזוֹהָם דְּלָא כְּחִישׁ — אֵימָא לָא, וְאִי נָמֵי לְמַעוֹטֵי מְזוֹהָם מִשּׁוּם דִּמְאִיס, אֲבָל הָנֵי דְּלָא מְאִיסִי — אֵימָא לָא, צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And had the Merciful One written** only **two** terms of exclusion, one might have said that they serve **to exclude these weak** animals, the sick and the old, as one should sacrifice only a fat, healthy animal. **But** with regard to an animal with **a foul odor, which is not weak, say** that it should **not** be disqualified. **And alternatively,** had there been only one term of exclusion, one might have said that it serves **to exclude** an animal with **a foul odor, as** it is **repulsive. But** with regard to **these** other two cases of an old and a sick animal, **which are not repulsive, say** that they are **not** excluded. Therefore, all three exclusions **are necessary.**

### Sin-Related Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְשֶׁנֶּעֶבְדָה בּוֹ עֲבֵירָה וְכוּ׳. מְנָא הָנֵי מִילֵּי? דְּתָנוּ רַבָּנַן: ״מִן הַבְּהֵמָה״ — לְהוֹצִיא רוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע, ״מִן הַבָּקָר״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנֶּעֱבָד, ״מִן הַצֹּאן״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַמּוּקְצֶה, ״וּמִן הַצֹּאן״ — לְהוֹצִיא אֶת הַנּוֹגֵחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **And** an animal **with which a transgression was performed,** and one that killed a person that was known on the basis of the testimony of one witness or the owner, are disqualified from being sacrificed. The Gemara asks: **From where are these matters** derived? They are derived from a verse, **as the Sages taught** in a *baraita* that when the verse states: "You shall bring your offering from the cattle, even from the herd or from the flock" (Leviticus 1:2), each mention of the word "from" is interpreted as a term of exclusion. The expression **"from the cattle"** serves **to exclude an animal that copulated with a person and an animal that was the object of bestiality.** The expression **"from the herd"** serves **to exclude** an animal **worshipped** as a deity, and **"from the flock"** serves **to exclude** an animal **set aside** for idol worship. Finally, the word "or" in the expression **"or from the flock"** serves **to exclude** an animal that **gored** and killed a person.

The *gemara* then derives the exclusion of animals involved in *aveira*: from the verse *adam ki yakriv mikem korban min habehema min habakar umin hatzon*, each limiting phrase is understood to exclude a different category. *Min habehema* — to exclude *rove'a ve'nirva'*, animals used in bestiality, whether as the active or passive party. *Min habakar* — to exclude *hane'evad*, an animal that was worshipped as an idol. *Umin hatzon* — to exclude *hamuktza*, an animal set aside for *avodah zarah* even without being actively worshipped. The additional *vav* of *umin hatzon* serves to exclude *hanogei'ach*, an animal that gored a person.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי בְּנֵי קְטָלָא נִינְהוּ! עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי הַבְּעָלִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: In **these** cases of an animal that copulated with a person, an animal that was the subject of bestiality, and an animal that gored and killed a person, **they are liable to death** by stoning. Why, then, is a verse necessary to exclude them? The Gemara answers that this is referring to a case where the incident was known **on the basis** of the testimony **of one witness or on the basis of the owner.** Therefore, the animal is not killed, as there are not two witnesses to the event, but the testimony is sufficient to disqualify the animal as a sacrifice.

A question arises: animals used in bestiality or that gored someone are subject to the death penalty — they are to be stoned. Why is it necessary to say they may not be brought as *korbanot* if they should not even exist? The *gemara* answers: this is why the *mishnah* specifies *al pi ed echad o al pi ha-be'alim* — through one witness or through the owners' own testimony. Without two witnesses in a formal court proceeding, the animal does not acquire the legal status of *shor haniskal*. Even if the facts are known to one witness or to the owner, it does not become *assur behana'ah* without a formal adjudication with two witnesses. The animal is permitted for general use — it can be slaughtered and eaten — but it cannot be brought as a *korban*.

## Androgynos Dispute

### Rava's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁלָמָא טוּמְטוּם, בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ לָא — דִּילְמָא נְקֵבָה הִיא, בַּמְּדִינָה לָא — דִּילְמָא זָכָר הוּא וְלֵית בֵּהּ מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that one does not slaughter a *tumtum* and a hermaphrodite, neither in the Temple nor in the rest of the country. The Gemara asks: **Granted,** one does not slaughter **a *tumtum*, neither in the Temple,** as **perhaps it is a female** and therefore does not have firstborn status and may not be sacrificed, **nor in** the rest of **the country,** as **perhaps it is a male and it does not have a blemish,** in which case he would be slaughtering a sacrificial animal.

The *gemara* now takes up the debate about *androgynos* in greater depth. *Bei Rava* — Rava raises a question: what exactly is Rabbi Yishmael's reasoning when he says *ein lecha mum gadol mizeh* — there is no greater *mum* than this? The question is not about the conclusion but about the conceptual basis.

### Male Status

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אַנְדְּרוֹגִינוֹס, בִּשְׁלָמָא בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ לָא, דִּלְמָא נְקֵבָה הִיא. אֶלָּא בַּמְּדִינָה נָמֵי, נְהִי דְּזָכָר הוּא — תֵּעָשֶׂה נַקְבוּת חָרִיץ, וְיִשְׁחוֹט עָלֶהָ!
>
> **Translation:** **But** in the case of **a hermaphrodite, granted** that one does **not** slaughter it **in the Temple,** as **perhaps it is a female. But with regard to** the rest of **the country, even let it be** considered **a male;** nevertheless, its **female** sex organ **should be considered** as **a crack [*ḥaritz*],** which is included in the blemish of "cracked" [*ḥarutz*] listed in the Torah (Leviticus 22:22), **and** let **one slaughter** it **due to** that blemish.

*Michdi pshita lei* — is it obvious to Rabbi Yishmael that an *androgynos* is definitely male and that the female genitalia is his *mum*? Or does Rabbi Yishmael himself hold that there is doubt whether the *androgynos* is male or female? Under that second reading, Rabbi Yishmael's position would be a legal hedge: *im timtzo lomar bechor hu, harei mumo imo* — even if it turns out to be male, it has a *mum* — and either way you can slaughter and benefit from it. This is a coherent position regardless of the underlying gender question.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אָמַר קְרָא ״אוֹ חָרוּץ אוֹ שָׁבוּר״, חָרוּץ דּוּמְיָא דְּשָׁבוּר, מָה שָׁבוּר בִּמְקוֹם עֶצֶם, אַף חָרוּץ בִּמְקוֹם עֶצֶם.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said** in response: **The verse states: "Or cracked or broken"** (Leviticus 22:22), which teaches that the blemish of being **cracked is similar to** that of being **broken: Just as** the blemish of being **broken** is relevant **in the location of a bone,** as there is no break where there is only flesh, **so too,** the term **cracked** is referring to a blemish found **in the location of a bone,** not in the location of the sex organs.

Abaye had proposed that the female genitalia should constitute a *charutz* — a crevice — and therefore a *mum*, based on the *Torah*'s enumeration of *o charutz* among the *mumim*. The *gemara* discussed at length whether *charutz* must be at a location of bone, as *shavur* (broken) is at a location of bone, or whether *charutz* can apply anywhere on the body. The *gemara* ultimately concluded that *charutz* requires a place of bone, and therefore the female genitalia of an *androgynos* — located where there is no bone — would not technically satisfy the definition of *charutz*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: בְּלָא שָׁבוּר נָמֵי לָא מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ חָרוּץ בִּמְקוֹם בָּשָׂר הָוֵי מוּמָא, דְּאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ הָוֵי מוּמָא, כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר ״גָּרָב״ זֶהוּ חֶרֶס, מִיחְרָץ חֲרִיץ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּמַרְאֵהוּ עָמוֹק מִן הָעוֹר״, כְּמַרְאֵה חַמָּה הָעֲמוּקָּה מִן הַצֵּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said:** Even **without** the comparison to **"broken," you cannot say that a crack in the location of flesh,** where there is no bone, **is a blemish. As, if** it would **enter your mind** that **it is a blemish, since** the *tanna* of the *baraita* cited above **said** with regard to **"*garav*"** that **this is** referring to boils that are hard as **earthenware [*ḥeres*],** evidently this blemish is **a crack** in the skin, **as it is written** with regard to the skin disease of leprosy: **"And its appearance is deeper than the skin"** (Leviticus 13:30), and the Sages explained: **It is similar to the appearance of** an area lit by the **sun that** seems **deeper than the shade,** which appears to cover it.

Rava goes further: even without the comparison to *shavur*, one could not classify a crevice in a place of soft tissue as a *mum*, because the *Torah* separately needed to mention *garav* — which digs into the skin — to establish that it is a *mum*. If a skin-penetrating *garav* required a separate verse, how much more so would a crevice in soft tissue need one. By negative implication, *charutz* alone would not cover a soft-tissue location.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלִיכְתֹּב רַחֲמָנָא ״חָרוּץ״ וְלָא בָּעֵי ״גָּרָב״, וְאָמֵינָא: ״חָרוּץ״ דְּלָא מְאִיס — הָוֵי מוּמָא, ״גָּרָב״ דִּמְאִיס — לֹא כׇּל שֶׁכֵּן? כְּתַב רַחֲמָנָא ״גָּרָב״ לְמֵימַר דְּחָרוּץ בִּמְקוֹם בָּשָׂר — לָא הָוֵי מוּמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** if so, **let the Merciful One write: Crack, and** it would **not need** to state ***garav*, and I** would **say:** If being **cracked, which is not repulsive, is a blemish,** then with regard to ***garav*, which is repulsive,** should it **not all the more so** be considered a blemish? Therefore, **the Merciful One writes *garav*, to say that a crack** found **in the location of flesh is not a blemish.** Consequently, one cannot say that the female sex organ of a hermaphrodite should be considered a blemish for which one may slaughter a firstborn outside the Temple.

### Practical Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל אוֹמֵר: אֵין לְךָ מוּם גָּדוֹל מִזֶּה. כְּאַבָּיֵי לָא אָמַר, חָרוּץ דּוּמְיָא דְּשָׁבוּר לָא אָמְרִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** In this connection, the Gemara cites the statement of the mishna with regard to a hermaphrodite: **Rabbi Yishmael says: You have no blemish greater than that,** and it may be slaughtered. Evidently, Rabbi Yishmael maintains that the female sex organ of a hermaphrodite is considered a blemish. The Gemara explains: Rabbi Yishmael does **not state** a ruling **like** that of **Abaye,** as he maintains that **we do not say** that the blemish of being **cracked is similar to** that of being **broken.** Rather, they are entirely different blemishes, and a crack found on flesh is considered a blemish.

*Lemai nafka mina* — what practical difference does it make whether Rabbi Yishmael considers the *androgynos* definitely male or merely possibly male? The *gemara* offers two answers. First: *lemilkei alei mishum giza va'avoda* — if the *androgynos* is definitely male and merely has a *mum*, then the animal retains *kedushat bechor* until it is slaughtered. If the owner shears it or works it before slaughter, he transgresses. If it is only doubtfully male, one cannot administer lashes because perhaps it was never a *bechor* to begin with. Second, and more practically significant: *lemasvei le-kohen* — does the owner need to give this animal to a *kohen*? If it is definitively a male *bechor* with a *mum*, it must be handed over to the *kohen* who will then slaughter it. But if there is genuine doubt whether it is even male, the owner can say: you cannot prove it is a *bechor*, and I may keep and slaughter it myself.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּרָבָא נָמֵי לָא אָמַר, דִּילְמָא הֵיכָא דְּלָא מִינַּכְרָא, אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּמִינְּכַר — ״מוּם רָע״ קָרֵינָא בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Yishmael **also** does **not state** a ruling **like** that of **Rava,** as **perhaps** his conclusion that a crack found where there is no bone is not a blemish applies only in a case **where it is not conspicuous. But** in a case **where it is conspicuous,** such as the female sex organ, **we call it "an ill blemish"** (Deuteronomy 15:21), and the animal is disqualified for use as an offering.

### Torah Proof

The *gemara* attempts to determine Rabbi Yishmael's actual position from a source. *Rabbi Ilai* says in the name of *Rabbi Yishmael*: *androgynos bechor hu u'mumo imo* — an *androgynos* is a *bechor* and its blemish accompanies it. This sounds like an unequivocal statement that it is definitively male with a *mum*. The *gemara* resists this reading: *dilma im timtzo lomar kamar* — perhaps Rabbi Yishmael means even if you were to say it is a *bechor*, its *mum* comes with it, leaving open the possibility that he is not certain it is male.

The *gemara* then brings a stronger proof: the *Torah* uses the word *zachar* (male) twice in the relevant passages concerning *olah*. Since the *Torah* already established that an *olah* must be male, the second mention of *zachar* would be superfluous. From this we derive *lehotzi tumtum ve'androgynos* — to exclude the *tumtum* and the *androgynos*, which are not fully or definitively male. The question becomes: whose position does this *pasuk* fit?

It cannot fit the first *tana* of the *mishnah*, who considers *androgynos* a matter of doubt. As *Tosafot* explains, there is a critical distinction: the *Torah* may address factual doubts — situations where we humans do not know what the facts are — and tell us how to proceed. But if the question is not a factual uncertainty but a *halachic* definitional question — how do we legally categorize the entity known as *androgynos*? — then that is not a factual doubt for the *Torah* to resolve by telling us to treat it as a *safek*. The *Torah* defines categories; it does not tell us that an inherently ambiguous legal category should be treated as a doubt. So if the first *tana* says *androgynos* is a doubt, the *Torah* verse would not have been needed to tell us to exclude it — the doubt itself would resolve the question. Therefore, the verse cannot be directed at the first *tana*'s position.

Could it be directed at the last Sages, who say *androgynos* is a completely separate gender and not a *bechor* at all? The *gemara* considers this: if that were the case, one *pasuk* saying *zachar* should have sufficed to exclude a being that is categorically not male. Why would two mentions of *zachar* be needed? The *gemara* answers: *ve-gabei bechor tarti kra'ei ktivi* — there are in fact two mentions of *zachar* by *bechor* as well, *hazachar takdish* and *asher yihiye lecha hazacharim l'Hashem*. The reason two are needed, even for the Sages who say it is a completely separate gender, is that one mention of *zachar* might only exclude the opposite gender — the definitive female — while this middle category would not automatically be excluded by a single *zachar*. A second mention is needed to make clear that even this middle category is excluded.

### Final Analysis

The *gemara* thus does not conclusively establish that the two-*pasuk* proof points exclusively to Rabbi Yishmael. The argument that was thought to prove Rabbi Yishmael's position — that two *psukim* are needed only if we are excluding something that would otherwise have been considered definitively male — is answered by saying even a completely separate third gender might need two exclusionary *psukim* rather than one.

The upshot is as follows: the first position holds that the gender of an *androgynos* is in doubt — it may be male or female. The second position, Rabbi Yishmael's, holds either that it is definitively male with a *mum*, or at minimum that even if there is doubt, once it is male it has a *mum* — and either way it may be slaughtered. The third position holds it is a completely separate gender, neither male nor female, and it is not a *bechor* at all. What remains unresolved in the present discussion is whether Rabbi Yishmael's position entails a definitive claim that the *androgynos* is male, or whether he too acknowledges a doubt and merely adds that even under the more favorable assumption it has a *mum*.

One further point is worth noting regarding the framing of the *androgynos* analysis. The *gemara*'s discussion of whether the female genitalia constitutes a *charutz* reflects an implicit conceptual approach: female genitalia are understood as the absence of male anatomy — a *charitz*, a crevice, a lack — rather than as a positive presence of a different kind. This framing explains why the *gemara* never raises the mirror-image possibility of calling the *androgynos* a female with a *mum* (due to the presence of male genitalia). Once male genitalia are present, the animal cannot be classified as female; the only remaining questions are whether it is definitively male with a *mum*, possibly male, or a third category entirely.
