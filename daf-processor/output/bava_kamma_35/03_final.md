# Bava Kamma 35 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Live/Dead Split

### Mishnah Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ יֵשׁ חַיָּיב עַל מַעֲשֵׂה שׁוֹרוֹ וּפָטוּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה עַצְמוֹ, פָּטוּר עַל מַעֲשֵׂה שׁוֹרוֹ וְחַיָּיב עַל מַעֲשֵׂה עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **There** are cases where one is **liable for an act of** damage caused by **his ox, but exempt** from liability **for** the same **action** if he performed it **himself.** Conversely, there are also cases where one is **exempt** from liability **for the action of his ox, but liable for his own action.**

Today's daf is 35. We pick up in the middle of 34b, dealing with the debate in the *mishnah* between Rabbi Yehuda and Rabbi Meir about how to read the *pesukim* in the Torah concerning the division of the live and dead animal. Rabbi Meir reads the verse as indicating that the dead animal is worth nothing — it does not literally mean that you split the dead animal, but rather that you split the depreciation due to death. Rabbi Yehuda, by contrast, reads the verse more literally: you actually split both the live and the dead animal.

### Rabbi Meir's View

According to Rabbi Meir, then, the division of the *neveila* is not a literal transaction but a formulaic way of expressing that the *mazik* pays half the loss attributable to the animal's death. The carcass itself belongs entirely to the *nizak*, and the *mazik* bears no ownership interest in it whatsoever.

### Rabbi Yehuda's View

Rabbi Yehuda insists on a more literal reading: each party takes half of the live animal and half of the dead one. This reading, however, can only work out equitably when the animals are of equal value, as the *gemara* has begun to illustrate. When the animals are worth the same amount, the arithmetic of splitting both produces exactly the right result — each party absorbs half the loss.

### Equal Value Requirement

The condition that makes Rabbi Yehuda's reading coherent is that the two animals must be of equal value at the time of the goring. When they are, a literal split of the live animal and the carcass yields precisely half-damages, no more and no less.

### Mazik Ownership Scope

One might think that if the *mazik* is a part-owner of the carcass, he would also suffer if the carcass depreciates further after the goring. But that would make *tam* more severe than *muad* and other categories of *nezek*, since in all other cases it is the *nizak* who fully owns the carcass and bears all subsequent loss or gain. There is no reason why *tam* should uniquely burden the *mazik* with depreciation risk. The resolution is that according to Rabbi Yehuda, the *mazik*'s partial ownership of the carcass functions only in one direction: if the value of the carcass appreciates, the *mazik* benefits from that appreciation. The ownership does not cut against him if values fall.

## Appreciation Theory

### Yochanan's Principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: שֶׁבַח נְבֵילָה אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ, דְּמָר סָבַר: דְּנִיזָּק הָוֵי, וּמָר סָבַר: פַּלְגָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** Rava's explanation, that Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda disagree with regard to a case where the carcass depreciated in value, should be rejected. Instead, their dispute should be explained according to what **Rabbi Yoḥanan said,** i.e., that the practical difference **between them** is with regard to **the appreciation of the carcass** in value after the ox's death; **as** one **Sage,** Rabbi Meir, **holds** that the entire increased value of the carcass **belongs to the injured** party, since the owner of the belligerent ox has no share in the carcass, **and** one **Sage,** Rabbi Yehuda, **holds** that **half** of the increased value belongs to the injured party and half goes to the one liable for the damage.

The *gemara* now introduces Rabbi Yochanan's resolution of the dispute. *Shvach neveila ika beinaihu* — the practical difference between Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda concerns a case where the carcass increases in value after the goring. That is the crux of their disagreement.

### Depreciation Agreement

In cases where the carcass depreciates further, both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that the *nizak* absorbs that loss. This follows naturally from the rule applicable to *muad* and other forms of *nezek*, where the *nizak* is the sole owner of the carcass. There is no reason for *tam* to differ on this point.

### Appreciation Dispute

Where the carcass appreciates after the goring, the two *tanna'im* diverge. Rabbi Meir holds that even this appreciation belongs entirely to the *nizak* — the *mazik* has no stake in the carcass at all. Rabbi Yehuda holds that the *mazik*, as a partial owner of the carcass, gains half of any appreciation, and that this gain offsets the payment he owes. *De-mar savar denizak havei* — one holds the appreciation belongs to the *nizak*; *u-mar savar palga* — and the other holds the *mazik* takes half. This is the fulfillment, according to Rabbi Yehuda, of the verse that they split the carcass.

## Unequal Value Problem

### Compassion Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהַיְינוּ דְּקָא קַשְׁיָא לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה – הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמְרַתְּ חָס רַחֲמָנָא עִילָּוֵיהּ דְּמַזִּיק, דְּשָׁקֵיל בִּשְׁבָחָא; יָכוֹל שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה מָנֶה, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה חֲמִשִּׁים זוּז – זֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת?
>
> **Translation:** **And this** explains **what was difficult for Rabbi Yehuda** in another *baraita*. **Now that you say** that **the Merciful One has mercy on the one liable for damage, as he takes** a share **of the increased value** of the carcass, **can** it be that in the case of **an ox worth five *sela*,** i.e., twenty dinars, **that gored an ox worth one hundred dinars, and the carcass is worth fifty dinars, this** party **takes half the live** ox **and half the dead** ox, **and that** party **takes half the live** ox **and half the dead** ox? Accordingly, the owner of the belligerent ox receives thirty-five dinars in value, which is more than his ox was worth.

Having established Rabbi Yehuda's position that the Torah shows compassion on the *mazik* by granting him a share in the carcass's appreciation, the *gemara* now follows a logical challenge Rabbi Yehuda himself raises. *Hashta de-amarta chas Rachmana ilvei de-mazik* — now that you say the Torah goes easy on the *mazik* and lets him gain from the appreciation of the carcass, a question arises: why can't this principle extend to cases of unequal-valued animals?

### Unequal Case Setup

Consider the following scenario: a *shor shaveh chamesh sela'im* — an ox worth twenty — gores a *shor shaveh maneh* — an ox worth a hundred. The carcass then depreciates to fifty. The actual damage was fifty. Under a straightforward application of the rule, the *mazik* should pay half the damage, which is twenty-five — meaning the entire goring ox would effectively be transferred to the *nizak*. But what happens if we apply the verses literally?

### Profit Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַרְתָּ: הֵיכָן מָצִינוּ מַזִּיק נִשְׂכָּר, שֶׁזֶּה נִשְׂכָּר? וְאוֹמֵר: ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״ – בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְאֵין בְּעָלִים נוֹטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, **you** should **say: Where do we find** a case where **the one liable for damage gains** from the damage, comparable to this case, **where this** owner of the belligerent ox **gains,** receiving more than the value of his ox? Clearly there is no such case, as one who is liable for damage does not gain from it. **And** in addition, **it says: "He shall pay"** (Exodus 21:36), indicating that **the owner** of the belligerent ox **pays, and the owner does not take** more than the amount his animal was worth at the time of the damage.

If you read the verse as written — *zeh notel chatzi chai ve-chatzi meis ve-zeh notel chatzi chai ve-chatzi meis* — then each party splits the live animal and each party splits the dead one. The *mazik* takes ten from his half of the live ox and twenty-five from his half of the carcass. He walks away with thirty-five. But his ox was worth only twenty to begin with. He has gained fifteen — a clear profit from an act of damage.

### Absurdity Stated

The absurdity is stark: the *mazik* would profit from the goring rather than pay for it. *Heichan matzinu mazik niskar?* — where have we ever found that a *mazik* walks away with a profit? The principle underlying the entire law of *nezek* is that the damager compensates the damaged party, not that he enriches himself through the act of damage.

### Exclusionary Verse

The verse itself closes this door: *shalem yeshalem ba'alim meshalmim ve-ein ba'alim notlin* — the owner of the goring ox pays, he does not take. The *mazik* is the one who pays out of pocket. He cannot walk away having received more than he started with.

## Equal Value Only

### Loss Prohibition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי ״וְאוֹמֵר״? וְכִי תֵּימָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי הֵיכָא דְּאִיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לְנִיזָּק; אֲבָל הֵיכָא דְּלֵיכָּא פְּסֵידָא לְנִיזָּק – כְּגוֹן שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים שֶׁנָּגַח שׁוֹר שָׁוֶה חָמֵשׁ סְלָעִים, וְהַנְּבֵילָה יָפָה שְׁלֹשִׁים זוּז – שָׁקֵיל נָמֵי מַזִּיק בִּשְׁבָחָא; וְאוֹמֵר: ״שַׁלֵּם יְשַׁלֵּם״ – בְּעָלִים מְשַׁלְּמִין, וְאֵין בְּעָלִים נוֹטְלִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: For **what** reason was it necessary to add the claim: **And it says:** "He shall pay"? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that **this matter,** that the one liable for damage does not gain, **applies** specifically **where there is a loss sustained by the injured** party, as in this case, where the decrease in the ox's value is fifty dinars, and if the carcass is shared by the liable party, the injured party receives only thirty-five dinars, thereby sustaining a loss. **But** in a case **where there is no loss sustained by the injured** party, **for example,** the case of **an ox worth five *sela*,** i.e., twenty dinars, **that gored** another **ox worth five *sela*, and the carcass** appreciated in value and is now **worth thirty dinars,** more than the ox was worth when it was alive, in this case **the one liable for damage also takes** a share **of the increased value,** since the injured party ends up not having sustained any financial loss, as he receives a share of ten dinars in the belligerent ox and another fifteen in the carcass, gaining five dinars over the value of his ox. It is to counter this claim that Rabbi Yehuda adds: **And it says: "He shall pay,"** indicating that **the owner** of the belligerent ox **pays, and the owner does not take** more than the amount his animal was worth at the time of the damage.

The *gemara* clarifies: the case where a *mazik* profits at the *nizak*'s obvious expense — the hundred-shekel ox gored by the twenty-shekel ox — is so clearly unjust that no verse is even needed to rule it out. That case was not the reason a verse was necessary. *Hani mili heicha de-ika pseida le-nizak* — this is obvious when there is a clear loss to the *nizak*.

### Mutual Gain Case

The more interesting case requiring a verse is where there is no loss to the *nizak*. Consider two oxen each worth twenty, and the carcass appreciates to thirty after the goring — perhaps because the market for dead oxen has risen. If you split literally: the *mazik* walks away with twenty-five and the *nizak* also walks away with twenty-five. Everybody gains something. The *nizak* is not actually out money compared to what he had before the goring — at least not in absolute terms, even if his ox would have been worth more had it not been gored. One might argue: in such a case, since the Torah anyway grants the *mazik* partial ownership of the *neveila*, perhaps he should own it fully enough to even walk away with a profit.

### Offset Principle

The verse *shalem yeshalem ba'alim meshalmim ve-ein ba'alim notlin* resolves this. The *mazik*'s ownership of the *neveila* is real, but it is instrumental: it exists to *offset* the payment he owes, not to generate a profit. Even in the mutual-gain scenario where the *nizak* does not suffer an absolute loss, the Torah does not permit the *mazik* to hold the carcass as an asset beyond what is needed to cover his payment obligation. His ownership cancels debt; it does not create wealth. He gains from appreciation to offset his liability, but he cannot gain so much that he actually walks away ahead.

A more concrete illustration: suppose that at the time of damage, the carcass was worth ten, each party owned five, but the market later rose and that five dollars grew to fifteen. Rabbi Yehuda's principle says yes, the *mazik* gains from appreciation of the *neveila* — but only enough to offset payment. He cannot gain past the point of full offset into actual profit.

### Fundamental Agreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַחָא בַּר תַּחְלִיפָא לְרָבָא: אִם כֵּן, מָצִינוּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה תָּם מְשַׁלֵּם יוֹתֵר מֵחֲצִי נֶזֶק, וְהַתּוֹרָה אָמְרָה: ״וּמָכְרוּ אֶת הַשּׁוֹר הַחַי וְחָצוּ אֶת כַּסְפּוֹ״! אִית לֵיהּ לְרַבִּי יְהוּדָה: פְּחָת שֶׁפָּחֲתָה מִיתָה – מַחֲצִין בַּחַי. מְנָא לֵיהּ? מִ״וְּגַם אֶת הַמֵּת יֶחֱצוּן״?! וְהָא אַפְּקֵיהּ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת, וְזֶה נוֹטֵל חֲצִי הַחַי וַחֲצִי הַמֵּת! אִם כֵּן, נִכְתּוֹב קְרָא: ״וְאֶת הַמֵּת״! מַאי ״וְגַם״? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ תַּרְתֵּי.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to Rabbi Yehuda's opinion that the two owners split the value of both the belligerent ox and the dead ox between them, **Rav Aḥa bar Taḥalifa said to Rava: If so, we find** that **according to Rabbi Yehuda,** when **an innocuous** ox gores an ox that is worth less than it, its owner **pays more than half the damage. But the Torah stated: "Then they shall sell the live ox, and divide its monetary value"** (Exodus 21:35). Rava answered him: **Rabbi Yehuda** also **holds** that only the **diminished** value of the dead ox **that was diminished by** its **death** is compensated for by **dividing the live** ox between the two parties. The liable party never pays more than half the damage. The Gemara asks: **From where** does Rabbi Yehuda derive this *halakha*? Is it **from** the verse: **"And the carcass they shall also divide"** (Exodus 21:35), from which Rabbi Meir derived it? **But didn't Rabbi Yehuda** already **derive from** this verse **that this** party **takes** the value of **half the live** ox **and half the dead** ox, **and that** party **takes** the value of **half the live** ox and **half the dead** ox? The Gemara answers: **If so,** if that is the only *halakha* indicated by this clause, **let the verse** simply **write: And the carcass** they shall divide. **What is** the reason that the word **"also"** is added? It is added so that one may **conclude two** conclusions **from** the clause: That the carcass is also divided between the two parties, and that the liable party is never required to pay more than half the damage.

At this point the *gemara* addresses a further challenge. *Amar lei Rav Acha bar Tachlifa le-Rava*: if Rabbi Yehuda is going to read the verses literally, what happens in a middle case — say a fifty-shekel ox that gores a forty-shekel ox, and the carcass goes down to twenty? In this case, applying the literal split: the *mazik* walks away with thirty-five, and so does the *nizak*. Neither profits from the other's loss. But the problem is that the *mazik* is paying more than half damages — he loses thirty-five and he only owed twenty shekels' worth of half the depreciation. How can that be, given that Rabbi Yehuda's entire approach was meant to be easier on the *mazik*, and the verse *ve-machru et ha-shor ha-chai ve-chatzu et kaspo* implies a straight split?

The *gemara*'s answer is decisive: *eis lei le-Rabbi Yehuda pachat she-pachtah mitah machatzin be-chai* — Rabbi Yehuda also agrees that in the end, the *mazik* pays only half of the depreciation from live to dead. This is learned from *ve-gam et ha-meit ye-chetzun* — the word *ve-gam*, "also," teaches two things simultaneously. On the one hand, *ha-meit ye-chetzun* — they split the carcass, from which Rabbi Yehuda derives that the *mazik* has a partial ownership in the *neveila* and gains from its appreciation. On the other hand, the *ve-gam* tells you that the fundamental principle remains intact — the payment is half the depreciation, no more and no less.

This is why the literal verses only apply when the animals are of equal value. When they are equal, the arithmetic of splitting both the live and dead animal works out to exactly half the loss. When they are unequal, applying the verse literally produces wildly different results — the *mazik* might profit, the *nizak* might receive more than half, or the *mazik* might pay more than half. None of those outcomes is acceptable. Therefore the verses can only be read in a context where both animals are worth the same.

At the bottom of this entire analysis, everyone agrees: no one reads the *pesukim* literally across all scenarios. Both Rabbi Meir and Rabbi Yehuda agree that the fundamental obligation is to pay half the depreciation due to death, and that the *nizak* holds the carcass or a lien on the goring ox based on that half-damages figure. The one and only practical difference between them is Rabbi Yehuda's principle that the *mazik* holds a partial ownership in the *neveila* such that if it appreciates, that appreciation offsets his payment. The *nizak* retains the carcass; but if it rises in value, that rise reduces what the *mazik* must pay out of pocket.

## Payment/Exemption Cases

### Mishnah Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁבִּיֵּישׁ – פָּטוּר, וְהוּא שֶׁבִּיֵּישׁ – חַיָּיב. שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁסִּימֵּא אֶת עֵין עַבְדּוֹ וְהִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ – פָּטוּר, וְהוּא שֶׁסִּימֵּא אֶת עֵין עַבְדּוֹ וְהִפִּיל אֶת שִׁינּוֹ – חַיָּיב. שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁחָבַל בְּאָבִיו וּבְאִמּוֹ – חַיָּיב, וְהוּא שֶׁחָבַל בְּאָבִיו וְאִמּוֹ – פָּטוּר. שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ בְּשַׁבָּת – חַיָּיב, וְהוּא שֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ בְּשַׁבָּת – פָּטוּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא מִתְחַיֵּיב בְּנַפְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If **his ox caused** a person **humilation,** he is **exempt** from paying compensation, **but** if **he** himself **humiliated** another, he is **liable.** Similarly, if **his ox blinded the eye of his slave or knocked out his** slave's **tooth,** he is **exempt** from having to emancipate the slave for this mutilation. **But if he** himself **blinded his slave's eye or knocked out his tooth,** he is **liable** to emancipate him, as stated in the Torah (Exodus 21:26–27). By contrast, if **his ox injured** the owner's **father or his mother,** he is **liable** to pay damages, **but** if **he** himself **injured his father or his mother,** he is **exempt** from paying compensation. Similarly, if **his ox set fire to a haystack on Shabbat,** he is **liable** to pay damages. **But if he** himself **set fire to a haystack on Shabbat,** he is **exempt** from paying damages. He is exempt from payment in these cases **due to** the fact **that he is liable to** receive the **death** penalty for injuring his father or mother or for desecrating Shabbat.

The *gemara* now turns to a new *mishnah* that presents cases where the liability of an ox's owner and the liability of a human being for the same type of act diverge — in some cases, the ox creates liability while the human is exempt; in others, the human is liable while the ox creates no liability for its owner.

### Embarrassment Case

The first case: if an ox causes embarrassment — *shoro she-bi'eish, patur* — the owner is exempt. Payment for *boshes* is a form of personal-injury compensation, equivalent to *tza'ar*, *ripuy*, and *sheves*. These additional payments for personal injury apply only when a human being directly inflicts harm, not when his animal does so. If my animal gores someone, I pay damages only — no hospital bills, no pain and suffering, no embarrassment. *Ve-hu she-bi'eish chayav* — but if the person himself caused embarrassment, he is obligated to pay.

### Eye/Tooth Case

The second case: if an ox knocks out the eye or tooth of the owner's slave — *shoro she-sima es ein avdo ve-hipil es shino, patur* — the owner is exempt. The Torah's rule that knocking out a slave's eye or tooth liberates the slave applies only to direct action by the master, not to the master's animal acting on his behalf. *Ve-hu she-sima es ein avdo ve-hipil es shino chayav* — if the master personally does so, the slave goes free.

### Parent Injury Case

Now the first of the reversed cases: *shoro she-chaval be-aviv ve-imo chayav* — if an ox wounds the owner's father or mother, the owner is liable and pays the father or mother damages. *Ve-hu she-chaval be-aviv ve-imo, patur* — but if the owner personally inflicts a wound on his parent, he is exempt from monetary payment because *chavala*, a wound that draws blood, when inflicted on a parent, makes the offender *chayav misa*. The gravity of the capital crime overrides any monetary liability.

### Shabbat Fire Case

The parallel case: *shor she-hidlik et ha-gadish be-Shabbat chayav* — if the owner's ox lights a pile of grain on fire on *Shabbat*, the owner is liable for the damage. *Ve-hu she-hidlik et ha-gadish be-Shabbat, patur* — but if the owner himself did so, he is exempt, because he has committed *chilul Shabbat*, a capital offense, and therefore we say *kimlei* — the capital liability overrides the monetary one. *Mipnei she-mitchayev be-nafsho* — because he has incurred liability to his own life.

## Kimlei Principle

### Abbahu's Teaching

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַף הוּא נָמֵי דְּלָא קָבָעֵי לֵיהּ; וְקָתָנֵי: פָּטוּר – מִפְּנֵי שֶׁהוּא נִדּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ! לֹא; שׁוֹרוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִידֵיהּ – מָה הוּא דְּקָבָעֵי לֵיהּ, אַף שׁוֹרוֹ דְּקָבָעֵי לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **so too,** in the case where **he** set the fire, it is presumably a case **where he does not need** the ashes. **And** nevertheless, the mishna **teaches** that he is **exempt because he is sentenced to death.** Apparently, one who lights a fire on Shabbat is liable even if he does not need the ashes, contrary to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** the comparison is the other way around; the case where **his ox** set the fire **is similar to** the case where **he** set the fire. **Just as** the case where **he** is liable for lighting a fire on Shabbat is one **where he needs** the ashes, **so too,** the case of **his ox** setting fire to the haystack is one **where it needs** the ashes.

The *gemara* now examines the principle of *kimlei* in depth. *Tani Rabbi Abbahu kammai de-Rabbi Yochanan* — Rabbi Abbahu taught the following *braita* in the presence of Rabbi Yochanan: *kol ha-mikalkelim peturim chutz me-chovel u-mav'ir* — all who perform a *melacha* on *Shabbat* in a destructive manner are exempt from the prohibition's consequences, with the exception of wounding and making a fire. The general principle is that a *melacha she-einah tzricha le-gufah* — a destructive labor — does not incur liability. But Rabbi Abbahu's *braita* carves out exceptions: wounding and fire remain violations even when done purely destructively. One can appreciate the intuition: *shechita* is a form of wounding that ultimately produces meat, and fire is paradigmatically productive, but both also have a fundamentally destructive dimension — enough to retain their character as *melacha* even in purely destructive form.

### Yochanan's Rejection

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנַן: שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ בְּשַׁבָּת – חַיָּיב, וְהוּא שֶׁהִדְלִיק אֶת הַגָּדִישׁ בְּשַׁבָּת – פָּטוּר. וְקָתָנֵי הוּא דּוּמְיָא דְשׁוֹרוֹ – מָה שׁוֹרוֹ דְּלָא קָבָעֵי לֵיהּ,
>
> **Translation:** **We learned** in the mishna: If **his ox set fire to a haystack on Shabbat,** he is **liable. But** if **he** himself **set fire to a haystack on Shabbat,** he is **exempt. And** the mishna **is** presumably **teaching** a case where **he** set the fire in a scenario that is **similar to** the case **where his ox** did so. **Just as** in the case **where his ox** set the fire **it** clearly **does not need** the ashes, as an ox does not act with such intentions,

Rabbi Yochanan rejected this *braita* entirely: *puk tani le-vara* — go teach that outside. *Chovel u-mav'ir eino mishnah* — this is not a valid teaching. Even wounding and fire incur no *Shabbat* liability when performed purely destructively. *Ve-im timtza lomar mishnah* — and if you insist it is a legitimate source, it must be referring only to a case where there is an element of constructive purpose. *Chovel ve-tzarich le-kabbo* — you wound an animal because you want to extract blood to feed your dog. *Mav'ir ve-tzarich le-afro* — or you burn down a fine table because you want its ashes. In each case, the act is destructive in the macro sense — you have ruined something of value — but you have a constructive purpose. That constructive element, however partial, is what defines the act as *melacha* on *Shabbat* and makes it a capital crime. A purely destructive act — *mikalkel* in the full sense — would not constitute *chilul Shabbat* and therefore would not trigger *kimlei*.

### Intent Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שׁוֹרוֹ הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב אַוְיָא: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּשׁוֹר פִּקֵּחַ שֶׁעָלְתָה לוֹ נְשִׁיכָה בְּגַבּוֹ, וְקָא בָּעֵי לְמִקְלְיֵיהּ וְאִיגַּנְדַּר בְּקִוטְמָא. וּמְנָא יָדְעִינַן? דִּלְבָתַר דְּקַלְיֵיהּ קָמִגַּנְדַּר בְּקִוטְמָא. וּמִי אִיכָּא כִּי הַאי גַוְונָא? אִין; דְּהָהוּא תּוֹרָא דַּהֲוָה בֵּי רַב פָּפָּא דַּהֲוָה כָּיְבִין לֵיהּ חִינְכֵיהּ, עָיֵיל וּפַתְקֵיהּ לְנָזְיָיתָא, וְשָׁתֵי שִׁיכְרָא וְאִיתַּסִּי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: In the case where **his ox** set the fire, **how can you find these** circumstances, where it did so because it needed the ashes? **Rav Avya said to him: Here we are dealing with an intelligent ox that was bitten on its back and wants to burn down** the haystack **and** then **roll around [*iggandar*] in the ashes** in order to heal the bite. The Gemara asks: **And from where do we know** that this is the reason that it set the fire? The Gemara answers: **Because after** the ox **burned** the haystack, **it was rolling around in the ashes.** The Gemara asks: **And is there** really **a case like this?** The Gemara answers: **Yes, as a certain ox that was at the house of Rav Pappa had a toothache. It went inside, and broke the lid** of a utensil, **and drank the liquor** inside **and was cured.** Evidently there are oxen with this level of intelligence.

The *gemara* now presses this point by looking at the *mishnah* case of the ox that burns the grain pile on *Shabbat*. *Ve-katani hu dumya de-shoro* — the *mishnah* presents the human case as parallel in scenario to the ox case. *Mah shor de-lo kaba'i lei* — just as when an ox burns a grain pile it is not interested in the ashes, it is acting purely destructively, *af hu nami de-lo kaba'i lei* — so too when a person does it in our *mishnah*, presumably they are not interested in the ashes. And yet the *mishnah* says *patur*, exempt — because it is *chilul Shabbat*. This would prove that even a purely destructive burning constitutes *chilul Shabbat*, against Rabbi Yochanan's position.

The *gemara* responds: no — read it the other way. *Shor dumya dideh* — the ox's act in the *mishnah* is analogous to the human act, not the human act to the ox. The *mishnah* is in fact describing a case where the human *did* want the ashes, and similarly the ox case in the *mishnah* should be understood as a case where the ox also had some constructive intent. But *shor heichi mishkachas lah* — how would you ever find a case where an ox burns a grain pile in order to get the ashes?

### Clever Ox Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ רַבָּנַן קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב פָּפָּא: מִי מָצֵית אָמְרַתְּ שׁוֹרוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִידֵיהּ?! וְהָא קָתָנֵי: שׁוֹרוֹ שֶׁבִּיֵּישׁ – פָּטוּר, וְהוּא שֶׁבִּיֵּישׁ – חַיָּיב. שׁוֹרוֹ דּוּמְיָא דִידֵיהּ נִתְכַּוֵּון לְבַיֵּישׁ – הֵיכִי מַשְׁכַּחַתְּ לַהּ? כְּגוֹן שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּון לְהַזִּיק. דְּאָמַר מָר: נִתְכַּוֵּון לְהַזִּיק – אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁלֹּא נִתְכַּוֵּון לְבַיֵּישׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said before Rav Pappa: How can you say** that the case of **his ox is** understood to be **similar to** the case involving **him? But doesn't** the mishna **teach:** If **his ox caused** a person **humiliation**, he is **exempt, but** if **he humiliated** someone, he is **liable?** If the case of **his ox is** understood to be **similar to** the case involving **him, how can you find** a case where the ox **intended to humiliate** the person? One is liable for humiliating someone only when he intends to do so, and an ox never has intention to humiliate. The Gemara answers: It is a case **where** the ox **intended to cause injury.** If it was the action of a person, he would be liable for the humiliation he caused as well, **as the Master said:** In a case where **a person intended to cause injury,** he is liable for humiliation **even if he did not intend to humiliate** his victim.

*U-mina yad'inan* — and how would we know that was the ox's intention? *De-le-vasar de-kalyei kamitnafia be-kitmei* — because after burning the pile down, we saw it rolling in the ashes. Such behavior reveals the ox's intent.

*U-mi shecheicha hai gavna* — is this really a realistic scenario? Does an animal ever act with that kind of purposive intelligence? *In* — yes. There was an ox in the house of Rav Papa whose gums were bothering it. *Ayeil pitka le-niza'isa* — it found and uncovered a barrel of mead, *ve-shati shichra* — and drank it, and that soothed its gums. Animals can indeed exhibit remarkably purposive behavior.

The *gemara*'s conclusion regarding the *mishnah* is therefore that even its straightforward scenario must be understood as one in which the human had some constructive intent — namely, wanting the ashes — for it to constitute *chilul Shabbat* and thus trigger *kimlei*. A purely destructive burning does not rise to that level.

### Rava's Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא אָמַר: מַתְנִיתִין בְּשׁוֹגֵג, וְכִדְתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה – דְּתָנָא דְּבֵי חִזְקִיָּה: מַכֵּה אָדָם וּמַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה; מָה מַכֵּה בְּהֵמָה – לֹא חִלַּקְתָּ בָּהּ בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג בֵּין מֵזִיד, בֵּין מִתְכַּוֵּון לְשֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּון, בֵּין דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה לְדֶרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה, לְפוֹטְרוֹ מָמוֹן אֶלָּא לְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן; אַף מַכֵּה אָדָם – לֹא תַּחְלוֹק בּוֹ בֵּין שׁוֹגֵג לְמֵזִיד, בֵּין מִתְכַּוֵּון לְשֶׁאֵין מִתְכַּוֵּון, בֵּין דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה לְדֶרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה, לְחַיְּיבוֹ מָמוֹן אֶלָּא לְפוֹטְרוֹ מָמוֹן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** a different solution with regard to the difficulty that the mishna poses to the opinion of Rabbi Yoḥanan: **The mishna** is referring **to** a case where one lit a fire on Shabbat **unintentionally.** Although he is not liable to receive the death penalty, nevertheless, since he performed a prohibited action that would carry the death penalty were it to be performed deliberately, he is still exempt from monetary restitution. The Gemara explains: This is **in accordance with what the school of Ḥizkiyya taught, as the school of Ḥizkiyya taught:** The verse states: "And he who kills an animal shall pay for it, and he who kills a man shall be put to death" (Leviticus 24:21), indicating that **one who kills a man and one who kills an animal** are comparable. This teaches that **just as** with regard to **one who kills an animal,** the Torah **did not differentiate** between **whether** he does so **unintentionally or whether** he does so **intentionally, whether advertently or inadvertently, whether by way of descent or by way of ascent,** and this was not **to exempt him from** paying **monetary restitution** in all these cases but **rather to render him liable** to pay **monetary restitution,** as one who kills an animal is liable in any event, **similarly, with regard to one who kills a man, do not differentiate** between **whether** he does so **intentionally or unintentionally, whether advertently or inadvertently, whether by way of descent or by way of ascent,** and this was not **to render him liable** to pay **monetary restitution** for the damage he causes in the process of killing him, but **rather to exempt him from** paying **monetary restitution** in any event. It is derived from here that one who commits a transgression carrying the death penalty is exempt from paying damages for his action, even if he is not in practice given the death penalty.

*Rava amar matnisin be-shogeg* — Rava offers a different resolution: our *mishnah* is not talking about an intentional violation of *Shabbat* at all. It is talking about an unintentional one — *shogeg* — meaning the person was not a willful violator. Perhaps he forgot it was *Shabbat*, or did not know that this act was forbidden on *Shabbat*. And this opens up the crucial question: do we say *kimlei* even when somebody committed a capital-worthy act without being willful about it, such that *Beis Din* could never actually impose the death penalty?

### Chizkiya Teaching

The answer comes from the famous teaching of *Dei Chizkiyahu*: *makeh adam u-makeh behemah*. The Torah juxtaposes two verses: *makeh behemah yeshalmena* — one who strikes an animal pays for it — and *makeh adam yumos* — one who kills a person is put to death. The teaching of *Bei Chizkiyahu* draws an analogy: just as the law of striking an animal makes no distinctions — *lo chalakta bo bein shogeg u-vein meizid, bein miskaven le-she'eino miskaven, bein derech yerida le-derech aliya* — whether willful or unintentional, whether directed or misdirected, whether on the downstroke or the upstroke — and the payment applies in all cases, so too for striking a human. No distinctions are made — but in the opposite direction: *ela le-potro mamon* — in all those cases, you are exempted from monetary payment. The weight of the act of taking a human life, or committing any capital crime, permanently displaces any monetary liability — not because a punishment is being imposed, but because the nature of the act itself is so weighty.

### Kimlei with Capital Crime

This teaches a fundamental distinction between two categories of *kimlei*. One category, derived from the principle of *ke-dei rishato* — that *Beis Din* imposes one punishment, not two — applies when *Beis Din* is actually inflicting a punishment. In that case, the simultaneous imposition of *malkus* and monetary payment is precluded because you do not punish someone twice for one act. But that form of *kimlei* requires that the punishment actually be imposed.

The *kimlei* of *Bei Chizkiyahu* is categorically different. When it comes to a capital crime, the exemption from monetary payment has nothing to do with whether *Beis Din* can or will punish the person. A person who committed *chilul Shabbat* entirely *be-shogeg* — who could never be executed — is nonetheless exempt from monetary payment. The gravity of having committed an act that is in the category of capital crime erases all monetary liability, regardless of circumstances.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרָבָא: מִי מָצֵית מוֹקְמַתְּ לַהּ בְּשׁוֹגֵג? וְהָא ״מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּדּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ״ קָתָנֵי! הָכִי קָאָמַר: כֵּיוָן דִּבְמֵזִיד נִדּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ, וְהֵיכִי דָּמֵי – דְּקָא בָּעֵי לְאֶפְרוֹ; הַשְׁתָּא בְּשׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר. כֵּיוָן דִּבְמֵזִיד נִדּוֹן בְּנַפְשׁוֹ, וַאֲפִילּוּ בְּשׁוֹגֵג – פָּטוּר.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages said to Rava: Can you** really **interpret** the mishna as referring **to** a case where the transgression was **unintentional? But doesn't it teach** that the transgressor is exempt **because he is sentenced to death,** which is only the case if the transgression was intentional? Rava answered that **this is** what the mishna **is saying:** He is exempt, **since** when this action is performed **intentionally,** the transgressor is liable to be **sentenced to death. And what are the circumstances** in which one is liable to receive the death penalty for deliberately lighting a fire on Shabbat? It is a case **where he needs the ashes.** Therefore, even **now, when** the transgression was **unintentional, he is exempt** from paying damages. The Gemara concludes: **Since when the transgression is performed intentionally the person is sentenced to death, even when it is performed unintentionally he is exempt** from paying damages.

The *gemara* therefore explains: *keivan de-be-meizid nitan be-nafsho* — since were the person to have done this intentionally they would be deserving of the death penalty, and *hashta be-shogeg patur* — even though in this instance it was unintentional and no death penalty could be imposed, nevertheless the act is one that by its nature belongs to the category of capital crime, and that is sufficient to exempt from payment.

### Rashi/Tosafot Debate

This raises a subtle but important question. Rava's answer establishes that *kimlei* applies even *be-shogeg*, without willful violation. But one might ask: is constructive intent still required to *define* the act as *chilul Shabbat* in the first place? After all, Rava's point was that intent to willfully violate *Shabbat* is unnecessary for *kimlei* to apply. But is the intent to achieve something constructive — wanting the ashes — separately required not as an element of culpability, but as an element of what makes the physical act an act of *chilul Shabbat* at all?

Rashi's reading of Rava's answer seems to be that once we establish *kimlei* applies even *be-shogeg*, intent does not matter. Whether or not the person wanted the ashes, since the physical act of burning a grain pile is the kind of act that can constitute *chilul Shabbat*, and since with the right intent under the right circumstances it would be a capital crime, *kimlei* applies. The act's potential to be capital-level is sufficient.

Tosafot reads it differently. Tosafot insists that constructive intent — the intent to want the ashes, or some other productive purpose — is still required to define the act as *chilul Shabbat* in the first place. What Rava is saying, according to Tosafot, is that you do not need to be a willful violator — you do not need to know and accept that you are transgressing *Shabbat*. But the intent that gives the act its character as a Shabbat-violating *melacha* — the intent to achieve something constructive through the act — is still necessary. Without that, it is simply not *chilul Shabbat*, and *kimlei* cannot apply. To burn a grain pile for purely destructive purposes without wanting the ashes is, according to Tosafot, not *chilul Shabbat* at all, regardless of how the act looks from the outside.

This dispute touches on a deeper conceptual question: at what point does the absence of intent mean not merely that you are not culpable, but that you have not even performed the relevant act? And the extreme case makes the issue vivid: if someone was gesticulating and his hand inadvertently knocked over a torch that started a fire — is that *mechallel Shabbat be-shogeg*, exempting from monetary payment via *kimlei*? Or is the absence of intent so complete that we cannot say he performed an act of *chilul Shabbat* at all, only that something happened through his physical movement?

It is also worth noting, as one might observe, that the precise term *kimlei* does not appear in this *sugya*. The concept operative here — that the weight of a capital crime wipes out monetary liability entirely, even without the punishment being imposed — may in fact be a broader principle than the classic *kimlei* formulation, which is more narrowly about *Beis Din* not imposing two punishments simultaneously.

## Burden of Proof

### Ambiguity Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ שׁוֹר שֶׁהָיָה רוֹדֵף אַחַר שׁוֹר אַחֵר, וְהוּזַּק; זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״שׁוֹרְךָ הִזִּיק״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא בְּסֶלַע לָקָה״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **an ox that was pursuing another ox, and** the ox being pursued **became injured,** but there are no witnesses as to how it was injured, and **this** one, the owner of the injured ox, **says** to the owner of the pursuing ox: **Your ox injured** my ox, and you are liable to pay me damages, **and that** one, the owner of the pursuing ox, **says** in response: **No; rather, it was hurt by a rock,** and I am not liable, then in this case, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** As long as the owner of the injured ox cannot prove that the injury was inflicted by the pursuing ox, the owner of the pursuing ox is not liable.

The *gemara* now turns to the final section of the *mishnah*, which presents a series of cases governed by the principle of *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the one who seeks to extract from his fellow bears the burden of proof. These cases all share the feature that the facts are ambiguous: something clearly happened, but who or what caused it cannot be established with certainty.

### Single Pursuing Ox

*Shor she-hayah rodeif achar shor acher* — one ox was running after another ox. The owners found one ox on the ground, injured. One party says: *shorkha heizik* — your ox gored mine. The other says: *lo ki al sela lakah* — no, it tripped over a rock; my ox had nothing to do with it. *Hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — the one seeking payment bears the burden of proof. The point is that circumstantial evidence — even if it looks very much like goring occurred — may not suffice. The claimant must prove his case.

### Two Pursuing Oxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ שְׁנַיִם רוֹדְפִים אַחַר אֶחָד, זֶה אוֹמֵר: ״שׁוֹרְךָ הִזִּיק״, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר: ״שׁוֹרְךָ הִזִּיק״ – שְׁנֵיהֶם פְּטוּרִים. אִם הָיוּ שְׁנֵיהֶם שֶׁל אִישׁ אֶחָד – שְׁנֵיהֶם חַיָּיבִים.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **two** oxen, belonging to two different owners, **were pursuing one** ox belonging to a third person, and that ox was injured by one of the pursuing oxen, and **this** one, the owner of one of the pursuing oxen, **says** to the owner of the other: It was **your ox** that **caused the injury, and that** one, the owner of the other pursuing ox, **says:** No, it was **your ox** that **caused the injury,** **the two of them are exempt,** since each of them rejects the claim of the injured party that his ox caused the injury. **If both** oxen **belonged to one person, both are liable,** as will be explained in the Gemara.

*Hayu shnayim rodfin achar echad* — two oxen, owned by Reuven and Shimon respectively, were both running after Levi's ox. A witness at a distance saw one of the oxen gore Levi's ox but could not tell which one. Reuven says it was Shimon's ox; Shimon says it was Reuven's. *Shneihem peturin* — both are exempt, because Levi cannot prove from either which one is liable. Each owner points to the other, and neither can be established as the responsible party.

### Single Owner Both Oxes

*Im hayu shneihem shel ish echad* — if both goring oxen belong to one owner, Reuven, then *shneihem chayavim* — both oxen are liable, meaning Reuven is liable. Even though Levi cannot prove which of Reuven's oxen specifically gored his animal, he can prove that one of Reuven's oxen did it. Reuven cannot escape liability by pointing to ambiguity between his own animals. Note that if we rule like Rabbi Akiva that the goring creates a lien on the specific ox that gored, there is a further complication: Levi knows he owns half of one of Reuven's oxen but cannot identify which one. Nevertheless, he knows Reuven holds his property, and some satisfaction can presumably be obtained on that basis.

### Large and Small Oxes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיָה אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן הִזִּיק״; אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״מוּעָד הִזִּיק״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא תָּם הִזִּיק״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the pursuing oxen both belonged to one person, and were both innocuous, so that restitution is paid exclusively from proceeds of the sale of the belligerent ox, and **one was large and** the other **one small,** in this case, if **the injured** party **says** that **the large** ox **caused the damage,** and he is therefore entitled to receive restitution for half the damage from the value of the large ox, **but the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small** ox **caused the damage,** and half of its value is not sufficient to cover half the damage; or, similarly, in a case where **one** ox **is innocuous and one is forewarned,** and **the injured** party **says:** The **forewarned** ox **caused the damage,** and the injured party is therefore eligible to receive full damages, **but the one liable for damage says: No; rather,** the **innocuous** ox **caused the damage,** in which case he is liable to pay only half the damage; in both of the above cases, the principle is that **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.**

*Im haya echad gadol ve-echad katan* — one of Reuven's oxen is large and one is small, both are *tam*. Since for a *shor tam* the payment is limited to the value of the goring ox, the identity of the goring ox is financially significant. *Ha-nizak omer gadol heizik* — Levi claims the large ox gored, so he can recover his full half-damages from the large ox. *Ve-ha-mazik omer lo ki ela katan heizik* — Reuven says the small one gored, so Levi's recovery is limited to the value of the small ox. *Hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — Levi must prove his version; he cannot simply demand recovery based on the large ox.

### Tam and Muad

Similarly, *echad tam ve-echad muad* — one of the oxen is *tam* and one is *muad*. *Ha-nizak omer muad heizik* — Levi claims the *muad* gored him, entitling him to full damages. *Ve-ha-mazik omer lo ki ela tam heizik* — Reuven says only the *tam* gored, so only half-damages apply. Again, *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — Levi bears the burden.

### Multiple Damages/Damagers

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָיוּ הַנִּיזָּקִין שְׁנַיִם – אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, וְהַמַּזִּיקִין שְׁנַיִם – אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן; הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְקָטָן אֶת הַקָּטָן״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְגָדוֹל אֶת הַקָּטָן״; אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד, הַנִּיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״מוּעָד הִזִּיק אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וְתָם אֶת הַקָּטָן״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא תָּם אֶת הַגָּדוֹל, וּמוּעָד אֶת הַקָּטָן״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** If the **injured** animals **were two** oxen, **one large and** the other **one small, and the ones that caused the damage** were also **two** oxen, **one large and one small,** and **the injured** party **says: The large one injured the large one and the small one injured the small one, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small one** injured **the large one,** in which case, if half the value of the belligerent ox does not cover half the damage, he is not required to pay more, **and the large one** injured **the small one;** or, similarly, if **one** of the belligerent oxen was **innocuous and one forewarned,** and **the injured** party **says:** The **forewarned** ox **injured the large** one, **and** the **innocuous** ox injured **the small one, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather,** the **innocuous** ox injured **the large one and** the **forewarned** ox injured **the small one;** here too, **the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.**

The *mishnah* extends this to more complex configurations: two damaged oxen (one large, one small) and two goring oxen (one large, one small) belonging to Reuven and Levi respectively; or one *tam* and one *muad* among the goring oxen. Levi claims the arrangement most favorable to his recovery — the large gored the large, the small gored the small; or the *muad* gored the large and the *tam* gored the small. Reuven claims the opposite arrangement in each case. All of these scenarios are governed by the same principle: *hamotzi mechavero alav haraya*. The claimant must prove his preferred version of events and cannot collect on the more favorable scenario without doing so.

## Admitted Claim Debate

### Anti-Sumchos Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: [זֹאת אוֹמֶרֶת] חֲלוּקִים עָלָיו חֲבֵירָיו עַל סוֹמְכוֹס, דְּאָמַר: מָמוֹן הַמּוּטָּל בְּסָפֵק – חוֹלְקִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna rules that in a case where there is uncertainty with regard to whether it was the pursuing ox that caused the injury to the other ox, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba says: That is to say** that **the colleagues of Sumakhos, who says** that **property of uncertain ownership is divided, disagree with his** opinion.

Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba observes that this *mishnah* as a whole is formulated as a refutation of Sumchos. *Cholkim alav chaverav al Sumchos* — Sumchos' colleagues disagree with him. *De-amar Sumchos: mamon ha-mutal be-safek cholkim* — Sumchos holds that whenever there is a genuine monetary doubt, you split the disputed amount between the parties. Sumchos does not require the objective doubt to arise from nothing — there must be some basis for it, some observable ambiguity that a neutral observer would recognize. In our *mishnah*, clearly something happened, and it is objectively uncertain which ox is responsible. Sumchos would say: ignore the claims and counterclaims, ignore *hamotzi mechavero*, and simply split it. The *mishnah* rejects this and insists that the claimant must prove his case.

### Sumchos Splits Even Bari

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא בַּר מֶמֶל לְרַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא: אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִין, אָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בָּרִי וּבָרִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba bar Memel said to Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba: Does Sumakhos state** this principle **even** in the case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, i.e., where both parties maintain the certainty of their claims, as appears to be the case in the mishna? Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba **said to him: Yes, Sumakhos says** this **even** in a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim.

*Amar lei Rav Abba bar Mammal le-Rabbi Chiyya bar Abba: amar Sumchos afilu bari ve-bari?* — Would Sumchos apply his splitting principle even when both parties are making definite claims — *bari ve-bari* — not merely saying "I don't know what happened" but actively asserting that they know exactly what occurred? Perhaps Sumchos's principle applies only when the parties themselves acknowledge uncertainty. When the *mazik* is making a firm, definite claim that he does not owe money, perhaps even Sumchos would respect the *mochezek* position and apply *hamotzi mechavero*. *Amar in: amar Sumchos afilu bari ve-bari* — the answer is yes: Sumchos applies his splitting principle even when both parties are making definite claims in direct contradiction of each other.

### Challenge to Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִמַּאי דְּמַתְנִיתִין בְּבָרִי וּבָרִי הוּא? דְּקָתָנֵי: זֶה אוֹמֵר שׁוֹרְךָ הִזִּיק, וְזֶה אוֹמֵר לֹא כִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And from where** is it inferred **that the mishna** is referring to a case of **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim? **As it teaches** that **this** one, the owner of the injured ox, **says: Your ox injured** my ox, **and that** one, the owner of the pursuing ox, **says: No, rather** etc., indicating that both make their claims with certainty.

This raises the question: since the *mishnah* is established as a case of *bari ve-bari* — as evidenced by the language *zeh omer shorkha heizik* and *zeh omer lo ki* — is the *mishnah*'s position simply that the *nizak* recovers nothing, or does the *nizak* at least recover something?

### Lesser Claim Recovery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַב פָּפָּא: מִדְּרֵישָׁא בָּרִי וּבָרִי – סֵיפָא נָמֵי בָּרִי וּבָרִי; אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הָיָה אֶחָד גָּדוֹל וְאֶחָד קָטָן, נִיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״גָּדוֹל הִזִּיק״, וּמַזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא קָטָן הִזִּיק״; אֶחָד תָּם וְאֶחָד מוּעָד, נִיזָּק אוֹמֵר: ״מוּעָד הִזִּיק״, וְהַמַּזִּיק אוֹמֵר: ״לֹא כִי, אֶלָּא תָּם הִזִּיק״ – הַמּוֹצִיא מֵחֲבֵירוֹ עָלָיו הָרְאָיָה; הָא לָא מַיְיתֵי רְאָיָה – שָׁקֵיל כִּדְאָמַר מַזִּיק; נֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר נָתָן – דְּאָמַר: טְעָנוֹ חִטִּים וְהוֹדָה לוֹ בִּשְׂעוֹרִים – פָּטוּר?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa objects to this** interpretation of the case: **From** the suggestion **that** the case in **the first clause** of the mishna is one where there is **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim, it may be inferred that **the latter clause,** the subsequent clause in the mishna, **also** is referring to a case where there is **a certain** claim **and a certain** claim. If so, **say the latter clause:** If **one was large and** the other **one small,** and **the injured** party **says** that **the large** ox **caused the damage, and the one liable for damage says: No; rather, the small** ox **caused the damage;** or if **one** ox **is innocuous and one is forewarned,** and **the injured** party **says:** The **forewarned** ox **caused the damage, but the one liable for damage says: No; rather,** the **innocuous** ox **caused the damage, the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.** This indicates that **if he does not bring proof, he takes** compensation **according to what the one liable for damage says.** If it is a case where both parties maintain certainty of their claims, **let us say** that this mishna **is a conclusive refutation of** the statement of **Rabba bar Natan, who says** that if one **claims** that another owes him **wheat,** which he claims he deposited with him, **and** the other **concedes** that he owes **him barley,** which is less expensive than wheat, he is **exempt** from any payment, since he did not admit to what was claimed, and what he did admit was not claimed. Similarly, here, the injured party claims that it was the large ox that injured his ox, and the defendant admits that his small ox caused the injury, but not the large ox. His admission does not correspond to the claim. Yet, he is apparently liable to pay according to his admission, contrary to the ruling of Rabba bar Natan.

*Matkif lah Rav Pappa* — Rav Pappa raises a challenge. If the *mishnah* is dealing with *bari ve-bari*, then consider its end section: the *nizak* claims the large ox gored him and seeks full half-damages from the large ox; the *mazik* claims the small one gored and argues the *nizak* is limited to the small ox. *Hamotzi mechavero alav haraya* — fine, the *nizak* cannot prove his larger claim. But can the *nizak* at least recover based on what the *mazik* himself conceded? After all, the *mazik* has admitted that one of his oxen gored — he is only disputing *which* one. Shouldn't the *nizak* be entitled to at least collect based on the *mazik*'s own concession?

### Wheat/Barley Analogy

Here the *gemara* introduces the case of *ta'ano chittin ve-hoda lo be-se'orin patur* — if I claim you owe me a pound of wheat and you admit only to a pound of barley, you owe me nothing. The logic is that my definite claim for wheat was an implicit rejection of barley as an acceptable substitute. I did not say "you owe me wheat, or barley, or anything of equivalent value." I said "you owe me wheat," which means "not barley." When you concede barley, you are admitting to something I never claimed, something I implicitly denied owing me. My *bari* claim for wheat actually forecloses my recovery of barley.

The same logic applies here. If the *nizak* says definitively that the large ox gored — and in the *tam* scenario, this is a claim about which specific property of the *mazik* he now has a lien on — he is implicitly saying the small ox did not gore. When the *mazik* concedes the small ox gored, he is conceding exactly what the *nizak* implicitly denied. Just as in the wheat-and-barley case, the *nizak*'s definite claim may preclude collection even on the *mazik*'s concession.

### Bari v'Bari Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא בְּבָרִי וְשֶׁמָּא. דְּקָאָמַר בָּרִי מַאן, דְּקָאָמַר שֶׁמָּא מַאן? אִי נֵימָא דְּקָאָמַר נִיזָּק בָּרִי וְקָאָמַר מַזִּיק שֶׁמָּא, אַכַּתִּי לֵימָא תִּהְוֵי תְּיוּבְתָּא דְּרַבָּה בַּר נָתָן! אֶלָּא דְּקָאָמַר נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא, וְקָאָמַר מַזִּיק בָּרִי. וּמִדְּסֵיפָא נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא וּמַזִּיק בָּרִי, רֵישָׁא נָמֵי נִיזָּק שֶׁמָּא וּמַזִּיק בָּרִי; וְאָמַר סוֹמְכוֹס אֲפִילּוּ בְּהָא – דְּאִיצְטְרִיךְ לְאַשְׁמוֹעִינַן דְּלָא?!
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** this clause must be referring to a case where there is **a certain** claim **and an uncertain** claim, i.e., one party maintains certainty of his claim, while the other merely states his claim as a possibility. The Gemara asks: **Who is** the one **who states a certain** claim, and **who is** the one **who states an uncertain** claim? **If we say that** it is **the injured** party who **states** with **a certain** claim that the large ox caused the injury, **and** it is **the one liable for damage** who **states an uncertain claim** that it was the small one, **let us still say that** the mishna **is a conclusive refutation of** the opinion of **Rabba bar Natan,** since the defendant does not admit to the claim of the injured party, and nevertheless he is liable to pay compensation from the small one. **Rather,** it must be a case **where the injured** party **states an uncertain** claim that it was the large ox that caused the injury, **and the one liable for damage states a certain** claim that it was the small one. Since the claimant does not contradict the defendant's admission, the latter is liable to pay the amount that he admitted he owes. The Gemara notes: **And from** the fact **that the latter clause** of the mishna is referring to a case where **the injured** party states **an uncertain** claim **and the one liable for damage** states **a certain** claim, it may be inferred that **the first clause** is **also** referring to a case where **the injured** party states **an uncertain** claim that it was the defendant's ox that injured his ox, **and the one liable for damage** states **a certain** claim that it was injured by a stone. It is not referring to a case of two certain claims as inferred above. The Gemara asks: **And does Sumakhos state** his ruling that the disputed property is divided between the two parties **even with regard to this** case, where the claimant states an uncertain claim, so **that it was necessary** for the mishna **to teach us that** according to the other Rabbis it is **not** divided? Certainly Sumakhos would concede that in this case the burden of proof rests upon the claimant.

This leads to the question with which the *sugya* closes: is our *mishnah* in fact dealing with *bari ve-bari* — both parties making definite, mutually exclusive claims — or might it be dealing with *bari ve-shema* — the *mazik* making a definite claim while the *nizak* only claims it is possible? If the *nizak*'s claim is framed as *shema* — "perhaps" the large one gored — rather than a definite assertion, then the dynamic is entirely different, and the *nizak* may well be entitled to recover based on the *mazik*'s concession without running into the wheat-and-barley problem. We leave off here and will take up tomorrow the question of whether the *mishnah* is indeed *bari ve-bari* or *bari ve-shema*.
