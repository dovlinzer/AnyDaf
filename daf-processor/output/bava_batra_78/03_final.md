# Bava Batra 78 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Wagon & Mules

### Wagon/Mules Trade

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מָכַר אֶת הַקָּרוֹן – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַפְּרָדוֹת, מָכַר אֶת הַפְּרָדוֹת – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַקָּרוֹן. מָכַר אֶת הַצֶּמֶד – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַבָּקָר, מָכַר אֶת הַבָּקָר – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַצֶּמֶד.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who **sold a wagon [*hakkaron*] has not sold the mules** that pull the wagon. Similarly, if one **sold the mules, he has not sold the wagon.** One who **sold a yoke [*hatzemed*] has not sold the oxen,** and one who **sold the oxen has not sold the yoke.**

The daf begins with a new *Mishnah* on 77b. The *Mishnah* establishes a basic principle regarding the sale of composite items: *machar ha-karon lo machar ha-pardot* — if one sells a wagon, the mules that pull it are not included in the sale. Conversely, *machar ha-pardot lo machar ha-karon* — if one sells the mules, the wagon is not sold with them. These items, despite their obvious practical interdependence, are treated as independent objects for the purposes of commercial transactions. The *Mishnah* continues: *machar ha-tzemed lo machar ha-bakar* — selling the yoke does not include the cattle, and *machar ha-bakar lo machar ha-tzemed* — selling the cattle does not include the yoke.

### Money Indicates Intent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַדָּמִים מוֹדִיעִין.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: The** sum of **money indicates** what one has sold.

Rabbi Yehuda dissents with what feels, at first glance, like a refreshingly practical position: *ha-damim modiyim* — the money paid reveals the intent of the parties. One need not rely solely on the words used; the price itself is evidence of what the transaction encompassed.

### Price Example

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? אָמַר לוֹ: "מְכוֹר לִי צִימְדָּךְ בְּמָאתַיִם זוּז" – הַדָּבָר יָדוּעַ שֶׁאֵין הַצֶּמֶד בְּמָאתַיִם זוּז.
>
> **Translation:** **How so?** If the buyer **said to** the seller: **Sell me your yoke for two hundred dinars,** since **it is a known matter that a yoke is not** sold **for two hundred dinars** he clearly intended to purchase the oxen as well.

The *Mishnah* illustrates this with a concrete case: *amar lo mechor li tzimdecha be-matayim zuz* — someone says, "Sell me your yoke for 200 *zuz*." Since everyone knows a yoke alone is not worth 200 *zuz*, and the only thing that commands such a price is the cattle yoked together with it, Rabbi Yehuda holds that the money makes clear what was intended: the word "yoke" was being used to refer to the cattle together with the yoke.

### Words Have Fixed Meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: אֵין הַדָּמִים רְאָיָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: The** sum of **money is not proof.**

The *Chachamim* reject this approach: *ein ha-damim re'ayah* — the money cannot serve as evidence to reinterpret the meaning of words. Words mean what they say. If someone overpaid, that is a separate problem to address, but it does not license a court to redefine the terms of a transaction after the fact.

## Hadamim Modiyim Debate

### Braitah: Harnessed Items

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ תָּנֵי רַב תַּחְלִיפָא בַּר מַעְרְבָא קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: מָכַר אֶת הַקָּרוֹן – מָכַר אֶת הַפְּרָדוֹת. וְהָא אֲנַן "לֹא מָכַר" תְּנַן! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיסְמְיַיהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לָא, תִּיתַּרְגֵּם מַתְנִיתָיךְ בַּאֲדוּקִין בּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Taḥlifa, from the West,** i.e., Eretz Yisrael, **taught** a *baraita* **before Rabbi Abbahu.** If one **sold a wagon, he has sold the mules** together with it. Rabbi Abbahu asked: **But didn't we learn** in the mishna that **he has not sold** the mules? Rav Taḥlifa **said to him:** Should **I erase** this *baraita*, as it is incorrect? Rabbi Abbahu **said to him: No,** do not erase it; you should **explain** that **your *baraita*** is referring to a case **where** the mules **are fastened to** the wagon. In that situation, one who purchases the wagon receives the mules as well.

The *Gemara* opens with a challenge. *Rav Tachlifa bar Arva* taught a *Braita* before *Rav Yafas*: *machar ha-karon machar ha-pardot* — selling a wagon does sell the mules. This directly contradicts our *Mishnah*, which says the opposite. *Rav Yafas* responds: should I simply erase this *Braita* from the corpus? He answers his own question: no. The *Braita* should be understood as applying to a case where the mules are actually strapped and harnessed to the wagon — *adukin bo*. When seller and buyer are standing before a wagon with mules harnessed to it, a sale of "the wagon" naturally encompasses the entire unit as it stands.

### Context & Connection

This resolution presupposes not merely that the mules and wagon were physically connected at some remote location, but that the parties were aware of and referring to them in their combined state. Context matters: are we, as parties to this transaction, looking at a harnessed unit together? That awareness shapes what the words of sale encompass.

### Subset Usage Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָכַר אֶת הַצֶּמֶד – לֹא מָכַר אֶת הַבָּקָר וְכוּ׳. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא דְּקָרוּ לְצִימְדָּא "צִימְדָּא" וּלְבָקָר "בָּקָר" – פְּשִׁיטָא; צִימְדָּא זַבֵּין לֵיהּ, בָּקָר לָא זַבֵּין לֵיהּ! וְאֶלָּא דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ נָמֵי לְבָקָר "צִימְדָּא"? כּוּלֵּיהּ זַבֵּין לֵיהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The mishna teaches: One who **sold a yoke has not sold the oxen;** and the Rabbis and Rabbi Yehuda disagree over whether the sum of money proves exactly what was sold. The Gemara analyzes their disagreement: **What are the circumstances? If we say** that the mishna is referring to a place **where they call a yoke: *Tzimda*, and** they call **oxen: *Bakar*,** it is **obvious** that **he sold him a yoke** and **did not sell him** the **oxen. But** if the mishna is referring to a place **where they also call oxen: *Tzimda*,** then the seller **sold him everything.**

The *Gemara* then turns to sharpen the debate between Rabbi Yehuda and the *Chachamim* by pressing on the underlying scenario. The question is: *heikhi dami* — what exactly is the case?

If we are in a locale where everyone uses *tzemed* exclusively to mean the yoke and *bakar* exclusively to mean the cattle — *peshita*. No one is confused. You said yoke, you get the yoke, regardless of how much you paid. Even Rabbi Yehuda would not suggest that an absurd overpayment can transform the meaning of a word that has no alternative usage.

Conversely, if we are in a locale where *tzemed* is universally understood to mean the cattle together with the yoke — again, *peshita*. There is no question that the cattle are included, and even the *Chachamim* would agree that the money confirms this reading.

### Live Dispute

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא צְרִיכָא, בְּאַתְרָא דְּקָרוּ לֵיהּ לְצִימְדָּא "צִימְדָּא" וּלְבָקָר "בָּקָר", וְאִיכָּא נָמֵי דְּקָרוּ לְבָקָר "צִימְדָּא"; רַבִּי יְהוּדָה סָבַר: הַדָּמִים מוֹדִיעִין, וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: אֵין הַדָּמִים רְאָיָה. וְאִי אֵין הַדָּמִים רְאָיָה, לֶיהֱוֵי בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **No,** their dispute is **necessary in a place where they call a yoke: *Tzimda*, and** call **oxen: *Bakar*, but there are also** those **who call oxen: *Tzimda*.** Since it is unclear what is meant by the term: *Tzimda*, **Rabbi Yehuda holds** that **the** sum of **money indicates** whether he purchased a yoke or oxen, **and the Rabbis hold** that **the** amount of **money does not** serve as **proof.** The Gemara asks: **But if the** amount of **money does not** serve as **proof,** then in a case where the buyer paid two hundred dinars and received only a yoke, **let** the **transaction be nullified.**

The *Gemara* therefore identifies the live dispute: *ba'atar di-kari lei tzimdeh tzimdeh le-bakar bakar, ve-ika nami de-kari le-bakar tzimdeh* — we are in a locale where the standard usage of *tzemed* refers to the yoke alone, and *bakar* refers to the cattle, yet there exists a subset of the population that also uses *tzemed* to refer to the cattle. It is not merely an occasional or idiosyncratic usage, but a recognizable, if minority, practice.

In this situation, Rabbi Yehuda holds *damim modiyim*: if someone uses the word *tzemed* and pays ten thousand *zuz*, it is evident that he is using the word in the manner of that subset of the population — he meant the cattle. The *Chachamim* disagree.

### Burden of Proof

The *Rashbam* explains the *Chachamim*'s position as follows: *keivan de-yesh korin le-bakar bakar ve-lo korin lei tzimdeh, lo amrinan damim modiyim* — since the majority of the population uses *bakar* to mean cattle and would never call it *tzemed*, we do not say the money indicates intent. Why not? Because *dilma hai minaihu hu* — perhaps the buyer is simply of that minority, but we cannot assume so. The operative principle is *yad lokeach al ha-tachtona* — the burden of proof rests on the buyer, and money in the seller's possession is presumed to belong to him. The buyer must prove his claim.

One might frame this slightly differently: a sale requires a meeting of minds. Even if a linguist examining the case would conclude that the buyer "must have meant" the cattle, that is irrelevant if the seller understood the word in its standard sense. The seller used *tzemed* meaning the yoke, as the majority of the population uses it. Are we to reinterpret the seller's words based on the buyer's private or minority usage? The *Chachamim* say no. The question is not merely one of *hamotzi me-chaveiro alav ha-re'aya* — who has the burden of proof — but also the more fundamental point that a transaction depends on the shared meaning of language, not on what one party silently intends. This is closely related to the principle of *devarim she-ba-lev einam devarim* — unexpressed intentions have no legal force. One might counter that paying a wildly disproportionate sum is so self-evident that it rises to the level of *devarim she-ba-lev kol adam*, things so obvious that articulation is unnecessary. But the *Chachamim* reject this: we will not hold that money overrides the standard meaning of words.

### Ona'ah Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי תֵּימָא בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח לְרַבָּנַן לֵית לְהוּ, וְלָא?! וְהָתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹכֵר סֵפֶר תּוֹרָה, בְּהֵמָה וּמַרְגָּלִית – אֵין לָהֶן אוֹנָאָה. אָמְרוּ לוֹ: לֹא אָמְרוּ אֶלָּא אֶת אֵלּוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **And if you would say** that **the Rabbis do not** hold that in a case of exploitation of less than one-sixth one must return the money and that if it was more than one-sixth there is **nullification of** the **transaction,** can it be maintained that they do **not** accept these *halakhot*? **But didn't we learn** in a mishna (*Bava Metzia* 56b) that **Rabbi Yehuda says: Even** in the case of **one who sells a Torah scroll, an animal, or a pearl,** these items **are not** subject to the *halakhot* of **exploitation,** as they have no fixed price. The Rabbis **said to him:** The early Sages **stated** that **only these** items listed earlier in the mishna, i.e., land, slaves, and documents, are not subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation. Therefore, the Rabbis should agree that the sale of the yoke is nullified.

The *Gemara* now presses a sharp question. Suppose the *Chachamim* are correct — the sale is of the yoke alone. The buyer paid ten thousand *zuz* for an object worth a fraction of that. Why is the sale not void on grounds of *ona'ah* — the prohibition against overcharging, which can, when sufficiently extreme, invalidate the transaction as a *bitul mekach*? The *Mishnah* implies that the *Chachamim* hold the sale stands. How?

One might respond by arguing that the *Chachamim* simply do not hold that *ona'ah* voids a sale — they may hold that overcharging is a transgression but does not annul the transaction. But the *Gemara* rejects this: there is a *Mishnah* in which the *Chachamim* explicitly dispute Rabbi Yehuda in a context involving the sale of a *sefer Torah*, an animal, or a pearl — *ein lahem ona'ah*, Rabbi Yehuda says, because such items fluctuate in value. The *Chachamim* respond: *lo amru ela elu* — only the formally excluded categories lack *ona'ah*; everything else is subject to it. This proves the *Chachamim* hold that *ona'ah* can void a sale. The problem therefore remains.

### Void Sale Answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "אֵין דָּמִים רְאָיָה" נָמֵי דְּקָתָנֵי – דְּהָוֵי בִּיטּוּל מִקָּח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **What** is the meaning of the mishna **that teaches** that according to the opinion of the Rabbis **the** sum of **money is not proof?** This means **that the transaction is nullified.**

The *Gemara* offers a first resolution: *mai ein damim re'ayah nami de-katani de-havei bitul mekach* — when the *Chachamim* say the money is not evidence, what they mean is that we do not use the money to conclude the cattle were sold. The words meant yoke, so the buyer got a yoke. But the grotesque overpayment for that yoke renders it a *bitul mekach* — the sale is void. This is not the same as saying the cattle were included; it simply means the entire transaction is unwound.

### Gift Interpretation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיבָּעֵית אֵימָא: כִּי אֲמוּר רַבָּנַן אוֹנָאָה וּבִיטּוּל מִקָּח – בִּכְדֵי שֶׁהַדַּעַת טוֹעָה, אֲבָל בִּכְדֵי שֶׁאֵין הַדַּעַת טוֹעָה – לָא; אֵימוֹר מַתָּנָה יְהַב לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And if you wish, say** instead that the sale of the yoke is not nullified, because **when the Sages spoke** of **exploitation and the nullification of a transaction,** they meant that these *halakhot* apply only in a case **where** the difference in price is an amount about **which one could be mistaken** and believe that this is the correct price. **But when** the difference in price is so great a sum **that one could not be mistaken,** this sale is **not** subject to the *halakhot* of exploitation. In that case, one must **say** that the buyer **gave** the extra money **to** the seller as **a gift;** he could not have thought that this was the actual price of the object.

The *Gemara* then offers a second answer: *ki amar rabanan ona'ah bitul mekach, be-chdei she-ha-da'at to'ah* — the principle that *ona'ah* voids a sale applies when the error is within the range where a reasonable person could be mistaken, that is, where one could genuinely have believed the price was fair. But *aval be-chdei she-ein ha-da'at to'ah* — when no one in the world would believe that a yoke is worth ten thousand *zuz*, there is no *ona'ah* in the relevant sense, because no one was deceived into thinking this was the correct price. In such a case, *eimor matana yahiv lei* — we interpret the excess as a gift. The buyer simply chose, for whatever personal reason, to give the seller a windfall while purchasing his yoke.

### Rashbam Analysis

The *Rashbam* addresses both answers directly. He notes that the first answer is the more logically compelling one: where someone pays slightly more than the market price, we say their mind was mistaken — *savar be-libo she-kach shaveh* — they genuinely believed that was the correct price, and that constitutes *mekach ta'ut*, a mistaken transaction. But in our *Mishnah*'s case, where a yoke worth a *perutah* is sold for a *maneh* or two hundred *zuz*, no such mistake is possible. The *Rashbam* indicates, however, that the second answer is a better fit for the *Mishnah* as written, since the *Mishnah* appears to describe a valid, standing sale — not one that is voided. Yet he acknowledges that the second answer, interpreting an obvious commercial transaction as a gift, is jurisprudentially strained — *ve-chama shinuyei d'chika hu* — it is a difficult reconciliation of the *Mishnah*'s language with the demands of legal logic.

## Donkey & Equipment

### Donkey Mishnah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר – לֹא מָכַר כֵּלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sells a donkey has not sold its vessels,** i.e., its equipment, with it.

The *Mishnah* turns from mules and wagons to a new case: *ha-mocher et ha-chamor lo machar kelav* — one who sells a donkey has not sold its equipment, *kelav*, the various implements normally associated with it. What exactly *kelav* includes will be clarified by the *Gemara*.

### Nachum HaMadi View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נַחוּם הַמָּדִי אוֹמֵר: מָכַר כֵּלָיו.
>
> **Translation:** **Naḥum the Mede says:** He has **sold its vessels.**

*Nachum HaMadi* disagrees: he holds that selling a donkey does include the equipment. *Nachum HaMadi* appears rarely in the *Mishnah* — he is perhaps best known from *Bameh Madlikin* in the laws of *Shabbos* — yet here he takes a clear and expansive position.

### Yehuda Context View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים מְכוּרִים, פְּעָמִים אֵינָן מְכוּרִים – כֵּיצַד? הָיָה חֲמוֹר לְפָנָיו, וְכֵלָיו עָלָיו, וְאָמַר לוֹ: "מְכוֹר לִי חֲמוֹרְךָ זֶה" – הֲרֵי כֵּלָיו מְכוּרִים. "חֲמוֹרְךָ הוּא" – אֵין כֵּלָיו מְכוּרִים.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There are **times** when the vessels **are sold,** and there are **times** when **they are not sold. How so? If the donkey was before him and its vessels** were **on it, and** the buyer **said to him: Sell me this donkey of yours, its vessels are sold.** If the buyer said to him: **Is the donkey yours;** I wish to purchase it, **its vessels are not sold.**

Rabbi Yehuda, consistent with his broader approach of reading intent from context, says: *pe'amim mechurin u-pe'amim einam mechurin* — sometimes the equipment is sold with the donkey and sometimes not, depending on how the transaction was phrased. If the donkey was standing before the parties with its equipment on it and the buyer said *mechor li chamor zeh* — sell me this donkey — the word *zeh* ("this one") emphasizes the donkey in its present state, equipment and all, and the equipment is included. But if the buyer said simply *chamor hu*, identifying the donkey as the seller's animal without that emphatic *zeh*, then only the donkey is sold, not its equipment.

### Equipment Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר עוּלָּא: מַחֲלוֹקֶת בְּשַׂק וְדִיסַקַּיָּא וְכוּמְנִי – דְּתַנָּא קַמָּא סָבַר: סְתָם חֲמוֹר לִרְכּוֹב קָאֵי, וְנַחוּם הַמָּדִי סָבַר: סְתָם חֲמוֹר לְמַשּׂאוֹי קָאֵי; אֲבָל אוּכָּף וּמַרְדַּעַת, קִילְקְלִי וַחֲבָק – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְכוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Ulla says:** The **dispute** in the mishna is referring **to** the donkey's **sack and** the **saddlebag [*disakkaya*] and** the ***kumni*,** a term explained later in the Gemara. **As the first *tanna* holds: An ordinary donkey is** used primarily **for riding,** and therefore these articles, which are not used for riding but for carrying burdens, are not included in the sale. **And Naḥum the Mede holds: An ordinary donkey is** used **for carrying burdens,** and therefore the items that serve this purpose are sold along with the donkey. **But** with regard to **the saddle and the saddlecloth, the harness and the saddle band, everyone agrees** that **they are sold,** as they are used both for riding and for carrying burdens.

*Ulla* clarifies the scope of the dispute: *machlokes be-sak ve-diska ve-chumni* — the disagreement concerns the sacks, carrying bags, and the *chumni*, which the *Rashbam* explains is a side saddle used by women. These are the items in dispute; the basic riding equipment is a separate question.

### Riding vs. Cargo

The *Tanna Kamma* holds: *stam chamor le-rchov kai* — an ordinary donkey is primarily intended for riding. Therefore, the saddle and its associated riding equipment are naturally part of what one means when selling a donkey, but the sacks and bags used for carrying cargo are not.

### Dual Use Argument

*Nachum HaMadi* counters: *stam chamor le-massa kai* — a donkey is also standardly used as a beast of burden, for carrying loads. Therefore, the carrying equipment is equally part of the donkey's normal complement and should be included in the sale. Importantly, both opinions agree that whatever falls within the animal's primary purpose is included; their disagreement is about how broadly to define that purpose. This stands in some contrast to the sale of land, where equipment used on the land is typically not sold with it, while here by movable property — *metaltelin* — items understood as integral to the object's use are more readily included.

## Attached Equipment

### Explicit Kelav

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: "חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ" – הֲרֵי זֶה מָכַר אֶת הָאוּכָּף, וְאֶת הַמַּרְדַּעַת, וְאֶת הַקִּילְקְלִי, וְאֶת הַחֲבָק. אֲבָל לֹא מָכַר שַׂק, וְדִיסַקַּיָּא, וְכוּמְנִי. וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: "הִיא וְכֹל מַה שֶּׁעָלֶיהָ" – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** from a *baraita*. If a seller says: **I am selling you a donkey and its vessels, this** one has **sold the saddle, and the saddlecloth, and the harness, and the saddle band. But he has not sold the sack, and the saddlebag, and the *kumni*. And when** the seller **said to** the buyer: I am selling **it and everything that is on it,** to you; the donkey and **all of these** items **are sold.**

A *Braita* is introduced: *chamor u-chelav ani mocher lach* — "I am selling you the donkey with its equipment." In such a case, one sells the saddle, the saddle blanket, the belly strap, and the breast strap. But one does not sell the sacks, the bags, or the *chumni*. Only if one says *hi ve-chol mah she-aleha* — "it and everything on it" — does everything get transferred.

### Saddle Always Sold

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא הַדִּין דְּאַף עַל גַּב דְּלָא אֲמַר לֵיהּ "חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו" – נָמֵי אוּכָּף וּמַרְדַּעַת מְכוּרִין; וְהָא קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן – דְּאַף עַל גַּב דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: "חֲמוֹר וְכֵלָיו" – שַׂק וְדִיסַקַּיָּא וְכוּמְנִי לָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **The same is true even if** the seller **did not say to him:** I am selling you **a donkey and its vessels.** In that case **as well, the saddle and the saddlecloth are sold. And this** is what the *baraita* **teaches us: That even though** the seller **said to him:** I am selling you **a donkey and its vessels,** the buyer still **does not acquire** the **sack and** the **saddlebag and** the ***kumni*.**

At first glance, the *Braita* implies that the saddle is included only when one explicitly says *kelav*. The *Gemara* rejects this reading. The correct interpretation is: even without saying *kelav*, the saddle is always sold when one sells the donkey — it is sufficiently integral to the riding use of the animal. The *chiddush* of the *Braita* is not that *kelav* is required to include the saddle; rather, it teaches that even when one does explicitly say *kelav*, the sacks and bags are still not included. To obtain those, one must say *ve-chol mah she-aleha*. Thus: *chamor* alone or *chamor u-chelav* both yield the saddle; *kol mah she-aleha* yields everything, including the sacks; but *kelav* alone, even when explicitly invoked, does not reach the cargo equipment.

### On vs. Off Donkey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי "וְכוּמְנִי"? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא בַּר שְׁמוּאֵל: מַרְכַּבְתָּא דְנָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara inquires: **What** is the meaning of: **And** the ***kumni*? Rav Pappa bar Shmuel said:** This is the **saddle** used **by women.**

A question is posed: is the entire dispute between the *Tanna Kamma*, *Nachum HaMadi*, and Rabbi Yehuda taking place when the equipment is actually on the donkey, or when it is not? This parallels the earlier question about the mules and the wagon: does physical attachment at the moment of sale determine what is included?

### Resolution Debate

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו מַחֲלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּשֶׁאֵינָן עָלָיו – מוֹדֵה לְהוּ נַחוּם הַמָּדִי; אוֹ דִלְמָא, בְּשֶׁאֵינָן עָלָיו מַחֲלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו – מוֹדוּ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְנָחוּם? תָּא שְׁמַע: וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: "הוּא וְכֹל מַה שֶּׁעָלָיו" – הֲרֵי כּוּלָּן מְכוּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: Does this **dispute** apply only to a case **where** the vessels **are on** the donkey, **but when** the vessels **are not on** the donkey, **Naḥum the Mede concedes to** the Rabbis that they are not sold? **Or perhaps** the **dispute** applies **to** a case **where** the vessels **are not on** the donkey, **but when** the vessels **are on** the donkey **the Rabbis concede to Naḥum** that the vessels are sold. The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the aforementioned *baraita*: **And when** the seller **said to** the buyer: I am selling **it and everything that is on it,** the donkey and **all of these** items **are sold.** In this case, the vessels are on the donkey, and everything is sold.

One answer: the dispute arises only when the equipment is *not* on the donkey. When it is on the donkey, everyone — including the *Rabbanan* — would agree that everything is sold. Evidence is brought from the *Braita*: it says one sells the saddle etc. when one uses the phrase *kelav*, and sells everything when one says *ve-chol mah she-aleha*. Since saying "everything on it" implies the stuff is on it and yet still needs to be explicitly claimed, this would suggest the *Rabbanan* hold that even when the equipment is on the donkey, it is not automatically sold. This would mean the dispute concerns precisely the case when the equipment is on the donkey.

### Ravina Evidence

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי אָמְרַתְּ בִּשְׁלָמָא בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו מַחְלוֹקֶת, הָא מַנִּי – רַבָּנַן הִיא. אֶלָּא אִי אָמְרַתְּ בְּשֶׁאֵין עוֹדָן עָלָיו מַחְלוֹקֶת, אֲבָל בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו – דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל מְכוּרִין; הָא מַנִּי?
>
> **Translation:** **Granted, if you say** that the **dispute** applies **when** the vessels **are on** the donkey, in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling? **It is** the opinion of **the Rabbis** that although in general one does not acquire the vessels, if the seller explicitly says that he is selling the donkey and everything on it, the buyer acquires it all. **But if you say that** the **dispute** applies **when** the vessels **are not on** the donkey, **but when** the vessels **are on** the donkey **everyone agrees** that **they are sold,** in accordance with **whose** opinion **is this** ruling? Even according to the opinion of the Rabbis there is no need to say explicitly that he is selling everything.

*Ravina* raises the matter before *Rav Ashi*. He cites evidence from Rabbi Yehuda's ruling within the *Mishnah* itself: Rabbi Yehuda says that when the equipment is visibly on the donkey and the buyer says *chamor zeh* — "this donkey" — the equipment is included, whereas saying only *chamor hu* does not include it. The fact that Rabbi Yehuda addresses the case where the equipment is on the donkey suggests the entire debate is situated in that scenario, not in the case when the equipment is elsewhere.

### Wagon Analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְעוֹלָם בְּשֶׁאֵין עוֹדָן עָלָיו מַחְלוֹקֶת, וְרַבָּנַן הִיא; וְאֵימָא: וּבִזְמַן שֶׁאָמַר לוֹ: "הוּא וְכֹל מַה שֶּׁרָאוּי לִהְיוֹת עָלָיו".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Actually,** the **dispute** applies **when** the vessels **are not on** the donkey, **and** the *baraita* **is** in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis, and** the language of the *baraita* should be emended to **say: And when he said to him:** I am selling **it and everything that is fit to be on it,** i.e., those items usually found on a donkey, everything is sold.

A conceptual proof is drawn from the earlier case of the mules and the wagon: when the *Braita* ruled that selling the wagon includes the mules, that was explained as applying when the mules are harnessed — *adukin bo*. The logical corollary is that the *Mishnah*'s default rule — that they are not sold together — applies when they are not connected. By analogy, the same framework governs the donkey and its equipment.

### Case-by-Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַדְּרַבָּה – אֵימָא רֵישָׁא: אֲבָל לֹא מָכַר לֹא אֶת הָעֲבָדִים וְלֹא אֶת הָאַנְתִיקֵי. וְאָמְרִינַן, מַאי "אַנְתִיקֵי"? אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: עִיסְקָא דִּבְגַוַּהּ. וּמִדְּרֵישָׁא בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו. סֵיפָא נָמֵי בְּעוֹדָן עָלָיו! אֶלָּא תְּנָא – מִילֵּי מִילֵּי קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara rejects this proof: **On the contrary, say the first clause,** i.e., the preceding mishna: One who sells a ship sells the mast along with it, **but he has not sold either the slaves or the *antikei*. And we said: What** is the meaning of ***antikei*? Rav Pappa said:** It means **the merchandise that is in** the ship. **But** according to your logic, **since the first clause,** i.e., the mishna concerning the ship, is referring to a case **where** the merchandise **is on** the ship, **the latter clause,** the mishna here, must **also** be referring to a case **where** the vessels **are on** the donkey. **Rather, the *tanna* teaches each statement** individually, and the circumstances of one ruling do not prove that another ruling is referring to a parallel case.

The *Gemara* resists this analogy, however, by introducing a counterexample: when selling a ship, the *Mishnah* earlier established that the cargo and merchandise on board are *not* sold with the ship — even though they are physically on it. This shows that being "on" the object at the time of sale does not uniformly determine whether it is included. The *Gemara* concludes: *tana milei milei katani* — the *Mishnah*'s author treats each case individually, and one cannot mechanically transpose the rule of one case to another. The degree of integral connection varies: mules harnessed to a wagon are more organically part of the wagon's identity than cargo sitting in a ship's hold. Each situation must be assessed on its own terms, and the question of whether the equipment is on the donkey, and what legal weight that carries, remains unresolved.

## Tannaim & Context

### All Tannaim View

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר, וְרַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל, וְרַבִּי מֵאִיר, וְרַבִּי נָתָן, וְסוֹמְכוֹס, וְנַחוּם הַמָּדִי – כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ: כִּי מְזַבֵּין אִינִישׁ מִידֵּי, אִיהוּ וְכֹל תַּשְׁמִישְׁתֵּיהּ מְזַבֵּין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara provides **a mnemonic** based on the letters of the names of the *tanna'im* who appear here: ***Zayin*, *gimmel*, *mem*; *nun*, *samekh*, *nun*. Abaye said: Rabbi Eliezer, and Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel, and Rabbi Meir, and Rabbi Natan, and Sumakhos, and Naḥum the Mede all hold** that **when a person sells an item, he sells it and all of its accoutrements.**

*Abaye* makes a sweeping observation: *kulhu svira lehu ki mazbin inish midi, ihu ve-chol tashmishav mazbin* — all of the following *Tannaim* share the view that when one sells an object, one sells it together with all of its functional equipment, at least in the relevant context. This does not mean they all say identical things; as both the *Rashbam* and *Tosafot* note, each makes this point in a specific context, and one should not assume any of them would extend the rule automatically to other cases.

### Example Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר – דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת בֵּית הַבַּד – מָכַר אֶת הַקּוֹרָה. רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל – דִּתְנַן, רַבָּן שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הָעִיר – מָכַר אֶת הַסַּנְטֵר. רַבִּי מֵאִיר – דְּתַנְיָא, רַבִּי מֵאִיר אוֹמֵר: מָכַר אֶת הַכֶּרֶם – מָכַר תַּשְׁמִישֵׁי הַכֶּרֶם. רַבִּי נָתָן וְסוֹמְכוֹס – בִּיצִּית וְדוּגִית. נַחוּם הַמָּדִי – הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Eliezer** holds this, **as we learned** in a mishna (67b) that **Rabbi Eliezer says: One who sells an olive press has sold the beam** used for pressing the olives, despite the fact that the beam can be removed from the press. **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel** holds this, **as we learned** in a mishna (68b) that **Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel says: One who sells a city has sold the** city's **guardsman. Rabbi Meir** holds this, **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Meir says:** If one **sold a vineyard, he has sold the accoutrements of the vineyard. Rabbi Natan and Sumakhos** hold this, as they state with regard to **the *bitzit* and the *dugit*,** i.e., the light-going boats of the ship, which they claim are sold when the ship is sold (73a). **Naḥum the Mede** holds this, as is evident from **that which we said** in the mishna here.

*Rabbi Eliezer*, as taught in a *Mishnah*, holds that selling an olive press includes the beam — *machar beit ha-bad machar ha-korah* — because the entire enterprise of the olive press is defined by and called after the beam. *Rabban Shimon ben Gamliel* holds that selling a city includes the *santar* — the sentry or guardsman — as an integral functionary of the city's operation.

*Rabbi Meir* holds, in a *Braita*, that selling a vineyard includes the vineyard's equipment — *tashmishei kerem*. The *Rashbam* here makes a notably academic observation: the *Gemara* attributes this ruling to Rabbi Meir with the formula *de-tanya*, implying a *Braita*, but the *Rashbam* notes that no such *Braita* attributing this to Rabbi Meir is known to him. In the *Tosefta*, this position appears in the name of *Abba Shaul*, not Rabbi Meir. The *Rashbam* thus flags a possible textual problem in the attribution.

Rabbi Natan and *Sumchus* both hold, as discussed earlier in the tractate, that the small boats — *betzit* and *dugit* — are sold together with the larger ship.

### Yehuda Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: פְּעָמִים מְכוּרִים וְכוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** There are **times** when the vessels **are sold,** and there are times when they are not sold.

Rabbi Yehuda, as we have seen, takes the contextual approach: whether equipment is included depends on the precise language used. *Pe'amim mechurin pe'amim einam mechurin* — sometimes yes, sometimes no.

### Rava Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: "חֲמוֹרְךָ זוֹ" – יָדַע דַּחֲמָרָא דִידֵיהּ הוּא, וְהַאי דְּקָא אָמַר לֵיהּ: "זוֹ" – מִשּׁוּם כֵּלָיו קָאָמַר לֵיהּ. "חֲמוֹרְךָ הוּא" – דְּלָא יֵדַע דַּחֲמָרָא דִידֵיהּ הוּא, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ: חֲמוֹרְךָ הוּא – שֶׁתִּמְכְּרֶנָּה לִי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said** that when the buyer says: Sell me **this donkey of yours, he knows that the donkey belongs to** the seller, **and** as for **that which he said to him: This, he said** that **to him due to its vessels.** By contrast, when the buyer says: **Is the donkey yours,** this indicates **that** the buyer **does not know that the donkey belongs to** the seller, **and this** is what **he is saying to him: Is the donkey yours that you can sell it to me?** In this case, he is interested only in the donkey and not its vessels.

*Rava* explains the operative distinction in Rabbi Yehuda's approach. When the buyer says *chamor zeh* — "this donkey" — the use of *zeh* is redundant if one already knows it is the seller's donkey: *yadu'a de-chamara dideih hu*. The only reason to say *zeh* must therefore be to indicate the donkey as it presently stands — *mishum kelim ka-amar lei* — with all its tackle, in its current equipped state. By contrast, saying *chamor hu she-timkarenu li* — "is this your donkey that you can sell it to me?" — the word *hu* identifies ownership but does not indicate the current state of the animal. Rabbi Yehuda's approach, then, is the most internally consistent of all the *Tannaim* discussed: throughout, he reads context and language to determine intent.

## Animals & Refuse

### Young Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוֹכֵר אֶת הַחֲמוֹר – מָכַר אֶת הַסְּיָח. מָכַר אֶת הַפָּרָה – לֹא מָכַר אֶת בְּנָהּ. מָכַר אַשְׁפָּה – מָכַר זִבְלָהּ. מָכַר בּוֹר – מָכַר מֵימֶיהָ. מָכַר כַּוֶּורֶת – מָכַר דְּבוֹרִים. מָכַר שׁוֹבָךְ – מָכַר יוֹנִים.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **One who sells** a female **donkey has sold** its **foal** along with it. But one who **sold a cow has not sold its young.** One who **sold a dunghill has sold its manure.** One who **sold a cistern has sold its water.** One who **sold a beehive has sold the bees** in it, and likewise one who **sold a dovecote has sold** the **doves.**

The *Mishnah* continues with a set of rules about what is sold together with what. *Ha-mocher et ha-chamor machar et ha-sayach* — one who sells a donkey has sold its young, the baby donkey. But *mocher et ha-para lo machar et benah* — one who sells a cow has not sold the calf.

*Mocher ashpa machar zivlah* — one who sells a plot of ground used for dumping refuse has sold the manure in it.

*Mocher bor machar meimeiha* — one who sells a cistern has sold the water stored in it. *Mocher kavveret machar devorim* — one who sells a beehive has sold the bees within it. *Mocher shovach machar yonim* — one who sells a pigeon coop has sold the pigeons.

### Rashbam Note

The *Rashbam* points out that these last cases are almost self-evident: of course if I sell you the water in my well, I am not thereby selling you the well itself. The *Mishnah* is therefore not suggesting the reverse — that selling the container sells the contents — is equally automatic. Each direction of the transaction must be examined on its own terms.

## Donkey vs. Cow

### Nursing Descriptor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִי דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ: "הִיא וּבְנָהּ" – אֲפִילּוּ פָּרָה וּבְנָהּ נָמֵי! אִי דְּלָא אָמַר לֵיהּ "הִיא וּבְנָהּ" – אֲפִילּוּ חֲמוֹר נָמֵי לָא!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that if one sells a donkey he has sold its foal, but if one sells a cow he has not sold its calf. The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances? If** this is a case **where** the seller **says to the** buyer that he is selling **it and its young, even the cow and its young** should be sold **as well. If** this is a case **where he does not say to him** that he is selling **it and its young, even the donkey** should **not** be sold with its foal.

The *Gemara* asks a pointed question: *heikhi dami* — what exactly is the case? If the seller explicitly says "she and her young," then even a cow should be sold with her calf. And if the seller says nothing about the young, then even a donkey should not be sold with its foal. So what is the specific scenario that generates the *Mishnah*'s asymmetric ruling?

### Primary Use

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: דְּאָמַר לֵיהּ חֲמוֹר מְנִיקָה וּפָרָה מְנִיקָה אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ; בִּשְׁלָמָא פָּרָה – אִיכָּא לְמֵימַר לַחֲלָבָהּ בָּעֵי לַהּ; אֶלָּא חֲמוֹר – מַאי קָאָמַר לֵיהּ? שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ – הִיא וּבְנָהּ קָאָמַר לֵיהּ; וְאַמַּאי קָרֵי לֵיהּ "סְיָח" – שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ אַחַר סִיחָה נָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** This is referring to a case **where** the seller **said to the** buyer: **I am selling you a nursing donkey, or:** I am selling you **a nursing cow. Granted,** with regard to the **cow, one could say that he needs it for its milk,** and the suckling calf would not necessarily be included in the sale. **But** with regard to the **donkey,** for **what** reason **is he saying to him** that the donkey is nursing? Since he does not need the milk of a donkey, **learn from here** that **he is saying to him** that he is selling **it and its young.** The Gemara adds tangentially: **And why** does the mishna **call** a donkey foal **a *seyaḥ*?** It is **because it follows after** and obeys **pleasant talk [*siḥa*],** whereas an old donkey must be led forcibly.

*Rav Pappa* resolves it: *de-amar lei chamor menika u-para menika ani mocher lach* — the seller says "I am selling you a nursing donkey" or "I am selling you a nursing cow." The word "nursing" is a descriptor. *Bishlama para* — when I tell you I am selling a nursing cow, it makes sense: *ikah le-meimar le-chalvah ba'ei* — one might reasonably interpret the descriptor as indicating the cow is currently producing milk, which is the primary reason one acquires a cow. The buyer needs to know the cow provides milk; without that assurance the sale might be a *mekach ta'ut*. There is therefore a plausible reading of "nursing cow" that does not imply the calf comes with her.

But when one says "nursing donkey," *mai ka-amar lei?* — what could this possibly mean? Donkey milk has no recognized practical value in this context. The descriptor "nursing" can only mean one thing: the donkey comes together with its young. *Shema mina hi ve-yalda ka-amar lei* — we must infer that "nursing" signals that both mother and offspring are being sold together.

### Etymology: Sayach

The *Gemara* pauses to explain the etymology of *sayach* (the young donkey): *she-mehalech achar sicha na'ah* — it walks after pleasant speech, following when called with a kind word. The term is a play on *sicha*, meaning speech or pleasant utterance — the foal follows the voice that calls it gently, as if seduced by agreeable sound. This etymological note serves as the bridge to the remarkable *aggadic* passage that follows.

## Cheshbono Shel Olam

### Meshalim Derasha

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, מַאי דִּכְתִיב: "עַל כֵּן יֹאמְרוּ הַמֹּשְׁלִים וְגוֹ׳"?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara cites a related discussion. **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥman says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "Therefore they that speak in parables [*hamoshlim*] say:** Come to Heshbon! Let the city [*ir*] of Sihon be built and established! For a fire is gone out of Heshbon, a flame from the city of Sihon; it has devoured Ar of Moab, the lords of the high places of Arnon" (Numbers 21:27–28)?

Rabbi Yochanan expounds the verse: *al khen yomru ha-moshlim bou Cheshbon tibaneh ve-tikonen Ir Sichon* — "Therefore the poets say: Come to Cheshbon, it shall be built and established, the city of Sichon" (Bamidbar 21:27). The *Rashbam* notes the plain context: when *Sichon* went to war against the king of *Moav* and conquered his land, the prophets of the nations — attributed here to *Bilaam* and his companions — foretold this victory. Since *Sichon* had conquered the land from *Moav*, *Bnei Yisrael* were permitted to take it from *Sichon* even though they were otherwise prohibited from warring against *Moav* directly. *Cheshbon* was the capital city of *Moav*. The *Rashbam* presses the question: why does the Torah include this foreign poetry? The fact of *Sichon*'s conquest is stated plainly elsewhere — *ki hu nilcham be-melech Moav ha-rishon*. The verse is evidently here for its *aggadic* interpretation: *le-drasha katbah Moshe Rabbeinu*.

### Calculation & Restraint

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "הַמֹּשְׁלִים" – אֵלּוּ הַמּוֹשְׁלִים בְּיִצְרָם. "בּוֹאוּ חֶשְׁבּוֹן" – בּוֹאוּ וּנְחַשֵּׁב חֶשְׁבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם; הֶפְסֵד מִצְוָה כְּנֶגֶד שְׂכָרָהּ, וּשְׂכַר עֲבֵירָה כְּנֶגֶד הֶפְסֵדָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets these verses homiletically. **"*Hamoshlim*"; these are** the people **who rule over [*hamoshlim*] their** evil **inclination.** They will say: **"Come to Heshbon,"** meaning: **Come and let us calculate the account of [*ḥeshbono*] the world,** i.e., the financial **loss** incurred by the fulfillment of **a mitzva in contrast to its reward, and the reward** for committing **a transgression,** i.e., the pleasure and gain received, **in contrast to the loss it** entails.

Who are the *moshlim*? Rabbi Yochanan reads the word not as "rulers" or "poets" but as *elu ha-moshlim be-yitzram* — those who exercise dominion over their own desires and inclinations. These are the *tzaddikim*, those who have mastered the *yetzer*.

*Bou Cheshbon* — come to Cheshbon — is read as *bou ve-nechashev cheshbono shel olam*: come and let us calculate the accounting of the world. Before allowing the *yetzer hara* to draw one into transgression, one should sit and perform the larger reckoning. *Hefsed mitzvah ke-neged scharah* — weigh the loss incurred by fulfilling a *mitzvah* against the reward. *Ve-schar aveirah ke-neged hefsedah* — and weigh the apparent gain from a transgression against the ultimate loss it entails. This is the *cheshbon ha-nefesh*, the soul's accounting: do not be seduced by the immediate gratification or apparent benefit; calculate the full picture.

### Two Worlds

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "תִּבָּנֶה וְתִכּוֹנֵן" – אִם אַתָּה עוֹשֶׂה כֵּן, תִּבָּנֶה בָּעוֹלָם הַזֶּה וְתִכּוֹנֵן לָעוֹלָם הַבָּא.
>
> **Translation:** **"Let it be built and established"** means that **if you** make this calculation, **you will be built in this world and you will be established in the World-to-Come.**

*Tibaneh ve-tikonen* — you will be built and established: *tibaneh be-olam ha-zeh*, built in this world, *ve-tikonen le-olam ha-ba*, and established in the world to come — if you make this calculation and act accordingly.

### Ayir Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "עִיר סִיחוֹן" – אִם מֵשִׂים אָדָם עַצְמוֹ כְּעַיִר זֶה, שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ אַחַר סִיחָה נָאָה – מָה כְּתִיב אַחֲרָיו? "כִּי אֵשׁ יָצְאָה מֵחֶשְׁבּוֹן וְגוֹ׳" – תֵּצֵא אֵשׁ מִמְּחַשְּׁבִין, וְתֹאכַל אֶת שֶׁאֵינָן מְחַשְּׁבִין.
>
> **Translation:** The phrase **"city [*ir*] of Sihon"** means that **if a person fashions himself like this young donkey [*ayir*] that follows after pleasant talk [*siḥa*],** i.e., if one is easily tempted to listen to his inclination, **what is written after it? "For a fire is gone out of Heshbon…**it has devoured," i.e., **a fire will go out from** those **who calculate** the effect of their deeds in the world, **and will consume those who do not calculate** and examine their ways but instead do as they please.

*Ir Sichon* — the city of *Sichon* — is now read through the etymological lens established by the *Mishnah*. *Im mesim adam atzmo ke-ayir zeh she-mehalech achar siyach na'ah* — if a person makes himself like this *ayir*, the young donkey, which follows after pleasant, seductive speech, then one is like the person who is drawn after the *yetzer hara*, seduced by its agreeable-sounding temptations. The wordplay ties together *ayir* (baby donkey), *ir* (city), and *siyach* (pleasant speech / *sicha*).

### Fire Imagery

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "כִּי אֵשׁ יָצְאָה מֵחֶשְׁבּוֹן וְלֶהָבָה מִקִּרְיַת סִיחֹן" – מִקִּרְיַת צַדִּיקִים שֶׁנִּקְרְאוּ "שִׂיחִין". "אָכְלָה עָר מוֹאָב" – זֶה הַמְהַלֵּךְ אַחַר יִצְרוֹ, כְּעַיִר זֶה שֶׁמְּהַלֵּךְ אַחַר סִיחָה נָאָה. "בַּעֲלֵי בָּמוֹת אַרְנֹן" – אֵלּוּ גַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ, דְּאָמַר מָר: כׇּל אָדָם שֶׁיֵּשׁ בּוֹ גַּסּוּת הָרוּחַ, נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם.
>
> **Translation:** A similar interpretation applies to the continuation of the verse: **"A flame from the city of Sihon";** this means that a flame will come **from the city of righteous people, who are called trees [*siḥin*]. "It has devoured Ar of Moab"; this** is referring to **one who follows after his inclination like this young donkey [*ayir*] that follows after pleasant talk. "The lords of the high places of Arnon"; this** is referring to **the arrogant. As the Master says: Every person who has arrogance in him will fall into Gehenna.**

*Ki esh yatzah me-Cheshbon* — fire goes out from *Cheshbon*: *tetzei esh mi-machshavin ve-tochal esh she-einam mechashvin* — fire emanates from those who make the proper accounting and consumes those who are led entirely by their appetites and do not reckon the full consequences. The *Rashbam* notes a connection here to the earlier *aggadic* teaching in the tractate that a person is burned by a friend's *chuppah* — *adam nichveh me-chupato shel chaveiro* — that in the world to come one will be seared by the achievements of those who were more righteous. Here the fire of the *tzaddikim*'s reckoning is what destroys those who abandoned calculation in favor of appetite.

*Ve-lehava mi-kiryat Sichon* — the flame from the city of *Sichon*: here *Sichon* is read not as the seduced donkey-follower but as a *siyach*, a plant or tree — the *tzaddikim* who are compared to a cedar of *Levanon* growing tall. The fire goes out from the righteous and destroys the city of *Moav*, meaning those who go after their *yetzer* like the *ayir* following pleasant speech.

### Arrogance & Judgment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַנִּירָם" – אָמַר רָשָׁע: אֵין רָם, "אָבַד חֶשְׁבּוֹן" – אָבַד חֶשְׁבּוֹנוֹ שֶׁל עוֹלָם; "עַד דִּיבֹן" – אָמַר הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא: הַמְתֵּן עַד שֶׁיָּבֹא דִּין, "וַנַּשִּׁים וְעַד נֹפַח" – עַד שֶׁתָּבֹא אֵשׁ שֶׁאֵינָהּ צְרִיכָה נִיפּוּחַ, "עַד מֵידְבָא" – עַד שֶׁתַּדְאִיב נִשְׁמָתָן. וְאָמְרִי לַהּ: עַד דְּעָבֵיד מַאי דְּבָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara interprets a subsequent verse: "We have shot at them [*vanniram*], Heshbon is perished, even until Dibon, and we have laid waste even until Nophah, which reaches until Medeba" (Numbers 21:30). **"*Vanniram*";** this indicates that the **wicked** person **says: There is no higher [*ein ram*]** power governing the world. **"Heshbon is perished"** means: **The account [*ḥeshbon*] of the world has perished,** i.e., they claim there is no accountability for one's actions. **"Even until Dibon [*divon*]"; the Holy One, Blessed be He, says: Wait until judgment comes [*yavo din*]. "And we have laid waste even until Nophah";** meaning **until the fire comes that does not require fanning [*nippuaḥ*],** i.e., the fire of Gehenna, which will consume them. **"Until Medeba [*Medeva*]";** this means **until their souls are pained [*tadiv*]. And some say** an alternative explanation: It means **until** God **does what He wishes [*mai deva'ei*]** with them and punishes them as they deserve.

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*: the verse hints that divine justice, though deferred, will inevitably arrive. *Vanniram* — the word is read as *ein ram*, "there is no God," the declaration of one who in his arrogance denies divine sovereignty. *Avad Cheshbon* — one who loses the *cheshbon*, the accounting of the world: *avad cheshbono shel olam* — has forfeited the great moral ledger. *Ad Divon* — until *Dibon* — is read as *ad din*, "until judgment arrives." *Va-nashim ad Nofach* — "until Nofach" — is read as *ad she-tavo esh she-einah tzericha nipuach*, until the fire comes that needs no fanning, the self-sustaining fire of *Gehinnom*. *Ad Medva* — until *Medva* — is read as *ad she-tadviv nishmatan*, until their souls are in anguish. An alternative reading: *ad mah de-ba'i* — until God does what God needs to do; the verse hints that divine justice, though deferred, will inevitably arrive.

## Leaving Torah

### Fire Consumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה – אֵשׁ אוֹכַלְתּוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְנָתַתִּי [אֶת] פָּנַי בָּהֶם, מֵהָאֵשׁ יָצָאוּ וְהָאֵשׁ תֹּאכְלֵם".
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** With regard to **anyone who separates** himself **from matters of Torah, a fire consumes him, as it is stated: "And I will set My face against them; out of the fire they come forth, and the fire shall devour them"** (Ezekiel 15:7). The Torah is likened to fire in the verse: "Is not My word like fire?" (Jeremiah 23:29). The verse in Ezekiel teaches: "Out of the fire they come forth," referring to those who separate themselves from the fire of Torah; "and the fire shall devour them," i.e., they are consumed by the fire of Gehenna.

*Rav Yehuda* in the name of *Rav*: *kol ha-poreish mi-divrei Torah, esh ochlato* — whoever abandons the words of Torah will be consumed by fire. The proof text: *ve-natati et panai bahem, me-ha-esh yatza'u, ve-ha-esh tochlem* — "I will set My face against them; from the fire they went out and the fire will consume them." Since Torah itself is fire — *halo kho devarai ka-esh, ne'um Hashem* — one who departs from the fire of Torah is consumed by a different fire.

### Gehinnom Fall

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא רַב דִּימִי, אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: כׇּל הַפּוֹרֵשׁ עַצְמוֹ מִדִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה – נוֹפֵל בְּגֵיהִנָּם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אָדָם תּוֹעֶה מִדֶּרֶךְ הַשְׂכֵּל, בִּקְהַל רְפָאִים יָנוּחַ". וְאֵין רְפָאִים אֶלָּא גֵּיהִנָּם – שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְלֹא יָדַע כִּי רְפָאִים שָׁם, בְּעִמְקֵי שְׁאוֹל קְרֻאֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Dimi came** from Eretz Yisrael to Babylonia, he said that **Rabbi Yonatan says: Anyone who separates himself from the matters of Torah falls into Gehenna. As it is stated: "The man who strays out of the way of understanding shall rest in the congregation of the spirits"** (Proverbs 21:16). "The way of understanding" is the way of the Torah. **And** one who departs from the Torah arrives in the place of the **spirits,** which **is nothing other than Gehenna, as it is stated: "But he does not know that the spirits are there; that those whom she has called are in the depths of the netherworld"** (Proverbs 9:18).

*Rav Dimi* in the name of Rabbi Yochanan adds a parallel teaching: *kol ha-poreish atzmo mi-divrei Torah, nofel le-Gehinnom* — whoever separates himself from Torah falls into *Gehinnom*.

The proof: *Adam to'eh mi-derech ha-sekhel, bi-kehal Refa'im yanuach* — "A man who strays from the way of knowledge will rest in the congregation of the *Refa'im*" (Mishlei 21:16). And *ein Refa'im ela Gehinnom* — the *Refa'im* are none other than *Gehinnom* itself, as another verse states: *ve-lo yada ki Refa'im sham, be-imkei She'ol keru'eha* — "He does not know that the *Refa'im* are there, in the depths of *She'ol* are those called to it." *She'ol* is *Gehinnom*. Whoever abandons the way of knowledge finds his resting place in the depths of the netherworld.
