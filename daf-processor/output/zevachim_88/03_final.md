# Zevachim 88 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Airspace = Mizbeach

### Kli Sharet Review

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חַטַּאת הָעוֹף פְּסוּלָה הֵיכִי מַזֶּה מִדָּמָהּ? הָוֵה לֵיהּ יָרוּד! שְׁאָר פְּסוּלִים הֵיכִי זָרֵיק לְהוּ מִדָּמָהּ?
>
> **Translation:** then with regard to the case of **a disqualified bird sin offering** that was pinched at the top of the altar, **how** does one **sprinkle from its blood** on the wall of the altar? When the priest raises the bird in his hand in order to sprinkle its blood, the bird **is** considered to have **descended** from upon the altar and he cannot sprinkle its blood, as the *halakha* with regard to all disqualified items is that once they have descended from upon the altar they shall not ascend. Likewise, concerning the blood of **other** offerings that were **disqualified** that ascended upon the altar, **how** does he **sprinkle from their blood,** since it is sprinkled from the airspace above the altar? Rather, it must be that the airspace above the altar is considered as the altar.

Today's *daf* is 88, and we pick up at the very bottom of 87b, in the middle of the discussion about whether the airspace over the *mizbeach* counts as the *mizbeach* itself — such that if a *korban* is in that airspace, it is treated as already on the *mizbeach*, and therefore, if it is *pasul*, it need not be taken down.

Before continuing, a brief clarification is in order regarding the previous session. The *mishnah* states that placing something in a *kli sharet* is also *mekadesh*. *Reish Lakish* asked *Rabbi Yochanan* whether this means one could even offer it up *lechatchila* even if it is *pasul* — for what else would the *mishnah* mean? *Rashi* explains that the alternative is one of the normal effects of a *kli sharet*: it elevates the *korban* to a higher level of sanctity, such that it can no longer be redeemed; it is no longer merely verbally designated but is fully consecrated.

### Meila & Kli Sharet Effect

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּמַגַּע לְהוּ. הָא הַזָּאָה הִיא?! מִיצּוּי הִיא! הָא זְרִיקָה?! שְׁפִיכָה הִיא! וְעוֹד, דֶּרֶךְ הַזָּאָה בְּכָךְ?! דֶּרֶךְ זְרִיקָה בְּכָךְ?!
>
> **Translation:** It is possible that in such cases one does not sprinkle the blood in its normal fashion, but in such a manner **that he presses it** against the wall of the altar immediately without the blood passing through the air. The Gemara rejects this suggestion: **Is this** considered **sprinkling?** It **is squeezing,** an act that is performed for a bird burnt offering, not for a bird sin offering. Likewise, if one sprinkles the blood of other disqualified offerings in this manner, is **this sprinkling? It is pouring.** **And furthermore,** with regard to a disqualified bird burnt offering, is **the manner of sprinkling in such** a fashion? And in the case of other disqualified offerings, is **the manner of sprinkling in such** a fashion? It is not. Rather, the airspace above the altar must be considered as the altar.

One point from yesterday requires explicit correction: it was mistakenly stated that entry into a *kli sharet* has an implication for *meila*. That is incorrect. *Meila* applies as soon as verbal sanctity takes place — this is a *meforash mishnah* in *Meila*. A *kli sharet* has two primary *nafkaminot*: first, the item placed within it can no longer be redeemed; second, it becomes *pasul* through *linah*, *yotzei*, lower levels of *tumah*, and removal from the *Beit Hamikdash*. If one sanctifies flour and oil at home verbally, the item does not become *pasul* by leaving the *Beit Hamikdash*; only after it enters a *kli sharet* does it acquire that susceptibility. *Meila*, however, begins at the moment of verbal consecration.

### Tosafot Alternative

*Tosafot* raises a lengthy discussion, troubled by the fact that across many *sugyot* in *Shas*, the evidence is consistent: placing something *pasul* in a *kli sharet* does not permit it to be offered *lechatchila* on the *mizbeach*. *Tosafot* never fully resolves the *hava amina* that would have allowed this, and ultimately concedes that all the evidence cuts against such a position, requiring one to reinterpret those many *gemarot*.

What *Tosafot* does offer, however, is an alternative explanation to *Rashi* of what the *mishnah* could mean when it says a *kli sharet* is *mekadesh*. Rather than reading it as relating to the laws of redemption or heightened susceptibility, *Tosafot* suggests the *mishnah*'s *chiddush* is as follows: if the item is in a *kli sharet* and is on the *mizbeach*, one does not have to remove it. One might think this is self-evident — it is on the *mizbeach* — but it is not, because the item is not physically touching the *mizbeach* itself. Even granting that the airspace of the *mizbeach* counts as the *mizbeach*, if the item is inside a *kli sharet*, there is a classical Talmudic question: if something is in my vessel but in your domain, in whose domain is it? So if an item is in a *kli sharet* sitting in the airspace of the *mizbeach*, one might have thought it is considered outside the *mizbeach* — in the separate domain of the vessel — and therefore should be removed. The *mishnah*'s *chiddush*, according to *Tosafot*, is that even in such a case the item is treated as fully on the *mizbeach* and need not be taken down.

*Reish Lakish*'s question to *Rabbi Yochanan*, then, was the further question: if the item was not yet placed on the *mizbeach*, can one place it there and thereby create a valid *korban*? And again, all the evidence across *Shas* is that one cannot — placing something *pasul* in a *kli sharet* does not permit it to be offered up.

### Bird Sacrifice Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אִי דְּנָקֵט לְהוּ בְּרֹאשׁ הַמִּזְבֵּחַ – הָכִי נָמֵי; כִּי קָאָמַר – דִּתְלָנְהוּ בְּקַנְיָא. מַאי? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said: If** the question concerns a case **where** the priest **held** the blood or limbs while standing **at the top of the altar,** they would **indeed** be considered as having ascended the altar, and shall not descend from it. But **when** the dilemma was **stated** with regard to the airspace above the altar, it was with regard to an instance **where he suspended them with a pole** above the altar, while he himself stood on the floor of the Temple courtyard. **What** is the *halakha* in such a case? The Gemara responds that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

Having clarified the previous discussion, the *gemara* now resumes at the bottom of 87b. The *gemara* asks: having spent so much time discussing the case of bird sacrifices and the question of *piggul* thought, let us use that discussion to actually prove the original question — *de'avir mizbeach kemizbeach dami*, that the airspace of the *mizbeach* counts as the *mizbeach* itself.

The proof proceeds as follows. If one performed *melikah* on a *chatat ha'of* that was *shelo lishma*, the bird is invalid. Yet the *halacha* — according to the position that birds are included in the rule that the *mizbeach* sanctifies — is that one may leave it on the *mizbeach* and may even sprinkle its blood. But consider the mechanics: when one holds the bird at the *mizbeach*, the blood is not on the *mizbeach* itself — it is only in the airspace. If the airspace does not count as the *mizbeach*, the blood should be considered *yerud*, having descended from the altar, and thus unusable. The fact that we permit sprinkling this blood demonstrates that the airspace counts as the *mizbeach*.

### Blood Airspace Problem

The *gemara* extends the same challenge to the case of other *pesulim*. Consider a case where an animal was *shechted* with a *pigul* thought or *shelo lishma*, the blood was collected while invalid, and it was then placed on the *mizbeach*. According to the opinion that the *mizbeach* sanctifies liquids — including blood — one may leave it there and presumably sprinkle it on the sides of the *mizbeach*. But how? The blood is sitting on the *mizbeach*, and the moment one lifts it and swings it at a distance to throw it, it leaves contact with the *mizbeach*. It is at best in the airspace. If the airspace is not equivalent to the *mizbeach*, this should be forbidden.

The *gemara* attempts to resolve this by suggesting *demagley lehu* — perhaps one holds the bird right up against the wall of the *mizbeach* and presses the blood directly against the surface without lifting it. But the *gemara* rejects this: *ha haza'ah hi? mitzuy hi* — is that sprinkling? That is squeezing. By a bird *olah* one squeezes; by a bird *chatat* one stands at a distance. And even setting aside the categorization, springing the act in such an unusual way — *derech haza'ah bekach, derech zerikah bekach* — is itself problematic; performing an act of blood service in a manner that departs from the normal form renders it invalid even if it does not technically fall into a different category. The inescapable conclusion is that once an item is on the *mizbeach*, the airspace plainly counts as the *mizbeach* itself.

### Rav Ashi Distinction

*Rav Ashi* steps in to clarify what the unresolved question about airspace actually was. *Amar Rav Ashi: ee dinakloo berosh mizbeach hachi nami* — if one is standing on the *mizbeach* and holding the bird or the blood in the airspace above, that is unquestionably considered on the *mizbeach*, for one's feet are anchored on the *mizbeach* and whatever one holds is treated as an extension of that position. There is no question about this case.

The residual question — left with *teyku* — concerns a different scenario: *kee kammar detaleenoo bekanya*, where one is not standing on the *mizbeach* at all, but is suspending the item in the airspace over the *mizbeach* or its ramp from outside, without one's feet touching the *mizbeach*. For example, one is standing off the ramp holding entrails in a basket, and those entrails hover over the airspace of the ramp without making contact. Does this count as having reached the *mizbeach*, such that they may now be brought up and burned? None of the cases discussed in the *gemara* addressed this precise configuration, and so it remains an open question.

## Kli Sharet Sanctification

### Liquid vs. Dry Vessels

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כְּלֵי הַלַּח מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶת הַלַּח, וּמִדּוֹת יָבֵשׁ מְקַדְּשׁוֹת אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ. אֵין כְּלִי הַלַּח מְקַדֶּשֶׁת אֶת הַיָּבֵשׁ, וְלֹא יָבֵשׁ מְקַדֵּשׁ אֶת הַלַּח. כְּלֵי הַקּוֹדֶשׁ שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ, אִם עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶן מֵעֵין מְלַאכְתָּן שֶׁהָיוּ עוֹשִׂין, וְהֵן שְׁלֵימִים – מְקַדְּשִׁין, וְאִם לָאו – אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין. וְכוּלָּן אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא בַּקּוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The mishna elaborates on the *halakha* taught in the previous mishna (86a) that service vessels sanctify items placed in them. **The** service **vessels** used for **liquids sanctify** only **liquids** used in the service, **and** the service vessels that serve as **dry measures sanctify** only **dry** items used in the service. **The** service **vessels** used for **liquids do not sanctify dry** items, **and** the service vessels used for **dry** items **do not sanctify liquids.** With regard to **sacred vessels that were perforated, if one** continues to **utilize them** for a use **similar to the use for which they would utilize them** previously when **they** were **whole, they** continue to **sanctify** their contents. **And if not, they do not sanctify** their contents. **And all of** these vessels **sanctify** items **only** when they are **in the sacred** area, i.e., the Temple courtyard.

The *gemara* now turns to the next *mishnah*, which addresses the parameters of when *klei sharet* sanctify the items placed within them. The *mishnah* states: *klei lach mekadshin et halach* — the vessels used for liquids sanctify liquids placed in them. *Umidos yavesh mekadshin et hayavesh* — and dry measures sanctify dry things placed in them. The vessels for wine, blood, and related liquids sanctify liquids; the vessels used to measure out flour sanctify dry contents. A hybrid case — such as a *minchah* containing both flour and oil — will be addressed in the *gemara*.

The *mishnah* further states: *ein klei halach mekadshin et hayavesh velo hayavesh mekadshin et halach* — they do not sanctify cross-category: wine into a dry measure or flour into a liquid vessel does not produce sanctification.

### Rashi vs. Tosafot

*Rashi* notices the terminological asymmetry in the *mishnah*: it calls the liquid category *kelim* — vessels — but calls the dry category *midot* — measures. Why not use the same word for both? *Rashi* explains that in the *Beit Hamikdash*, liquids were handled in both measuring vessels and non-measuring vessels (bowls for catching blood, dishes for holding a *minchah*), whereas for dry goods, the only dedicated vessels were measuring vessels — the *issaron* and the *chatzi issaron*. Therefore, the *mishnah* naturally calls the dry category *midot*, because that is all they were used for.

*Tosafot* disagrees. He argues that *Rashi*'s premise is false, and provides a list of counterexamples: the *biza* discussed in Perek Hakometz Rabbah, the *machta* used for the *ketoret* (which was used to scoop, not measure), the *kaf*, the *machteshet* mentioned in Shevuot, the *bazichin*, the *shulchan* — all of these were dry-purpose vessels in the *Mikdash* that were not measures. *Tosafot* therefore cannot accept *Rashi*'s reasoning.

### Liquid Measure Difficulty

*Rabbeinu Tam*, as cited in *Tosafot*, offers a different explanation of the terminological asymmetry: dry goods can be measured either level (*machuk*) or heaped (*gadush*), in any manner one chooses, which makes the concept of a "measure" apt and meaningful for dry goods. Liquid, by contrast, cannot be heaped — it cannot form a mound — and furthermore, *Tosafot* argues, liquid measurement is inherently less uniform: wine is thicker than water, and oil thicker than wine, meaning that volume measures of liquids do not translate consistently even across liquids, let alone as weight measures. Therefore, one cannot call a liquid vessel a *midah* in the same sense that one calls a dry vessel a *midah*.

This explanation is admittedly difficult. If these are purely volume measures, the issue of density or thickness should be irrelevant to volume. And if the concern is that dry goods can be measured both by volume and approximated by weight (because the same flour will always occupy roughly the same volume and weight), while liquids can only be volume measures — the point about inability to mound remains unclear as a distinguishing principle. The precise reasoning of *Tosafot* here remains elusive.

Returning to the *mishnah* itself: the *mishnah* concludes that if you use the wrong type of vessel for the contents — liquid in a dry measure, dry in a liquid measure — it does not sanctify. *Rashi* notes that for this reason, virtually all vessels except the two explicit dry measures (*issaron* and *chatzi issaron*) are categorized as *klei halach*. When a *minchah* of flour and oil is prepared, once measured it would be transferred to one of the generic bowls; according to *Rashi*, such bowls, even if they are "liquid vessels," sanctify the *minchah*.

### Broken Vessels

The *mishnah* continues: *klei hakodesh shenikvu* — if sacred vessels develop holes. *Im osim bahen me'ein melachtan* — if they can still perform a function similar to the function they performed when whole, *mekadshin* — they still sanctify. The standard is whether the vessel can still be used in substantially the same way it was used before the damage. If the hole is such that a vessel formerly used for blood can no longer hold blood but could still hold a *minchah*, that is not sufficient; it must be able to perform the same function as before. If, however, the hole is positioned so that it does not affect usability — say, a hole near the top that does not compromise capacity — then it remains valid. *V'im lav ein mekadshin* — if not, it loses its status as a *kli sharet*.

*Vichullan ein mekadshin ella bakodesh* — all of these vessels only produce sanctification when used within the sacred precinct, i.e., the *azarah*. This is internally coherent: one of the effects of a *kli sharet* is that the contents become *nifsal b'yotzei* — invalidated by removal from the *azarah*. Consequently, this effect can only be triggered in the *azarah* itself.

One might ask: if a vessel is taken outside the *azarah* and then brought back in, does it resume its sanctifying capacity when something is placed in it upon return? The answer would appear to be yes — it is the act of placing something within the vessel in the *azarah* that produces sanctification.

### Shmuel's 4 Conditions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּלֵי שָׁרֵת – אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא שְׁלֵימִין, אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא מְלֵאִין, אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא מִתּוֹכָן. וְאָמְרִי לָהּ: אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא שְׁלֵימִין וּמְלֵאִים וּמִבִּפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** Additionally, **Shmuel says: Service vessels sanctify** items **only** when the vessels are **whole,** i.e., they do not have a hole; **they sanctify only full** measures, i.e., when they contain a measurement fit for offering; and **they sanctify** items **only from within them** and not items that merely touched their exterior. **And some say** there is another version of the statement of Shmuel: Service vessels **sanctify** items **only** when the vessels are **whole, and** when they contain **full** measures, **and from inside.**

*Amar Shmuel: klei sharet ein mekadshin ella sheleimim* — first, the vessels must be whole, unbroken. *Ein mekadshin ella mele'im* — second, they must be full. This does not mean literally filled to the brim, but that the full required measure has been placed within. If one was supposed to place an *issaron* worth of flour and placed only half, it has not been sanctified. *Ve'ein mekadshin ela mitokan* — third, only what is inside the vessel is sanctified, not anything mounded above the lip. *Ve'amrei lei*: some versions add that sanctification only operates *bipnim* — inside the *Beit Hamikdash*, i.e., the *azarah*, which is either a fourth independent condition (as *Rashi* reads *bipnim*) or simply a variant phrasing of *mitokan* (as *Tosafot* reads it).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי בֵּינַיְיהוּ? אִיכָּא בֵּינַיְיהוּ בֵּירוּצֵי מִדּוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What** is the difference **between** these two versions? The Gemara responds: The difference **between them is** with regard to **heaping measures.**

The *gemara* asks *mai beinaihu* — what is the practical difference between saying *mitokan* versus *mibipnim*? The answer: *beirurei middot* — the mound that rises above the lip of the vessel. According to *mitokan*, only what is physically within the walls of the vessel is sanctified; anything mounded above the lip is excluded. According to *mibipnim*, the requirement is merely that the contents be on the inside rather than the outside — even mounded contents above the lip would count as *mibipnim*, while something placed in a hollow on the exterior of the vessel would not. So *mitokan* is a stricter standard than *mibipnim*, excluding the overflowing mound, whereas *mibipnim* would include it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא: אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין אֶלָּא שְׁלֵימִין, וּמְלֵאִים, וּמִתּוֹכָן, וּבִפְנִים.
>
> **Translation:** **According to the first version, that service vessels sanctify only items that are within them, nothing that overflows is included. The Gemara notes that it was taught in a *baraita*:** Service vessels **sanctify** items **only** when they are **whole, and** only **full** measurements, **and from within them, and inside.**

A *braita* teaches all four conditions together: *sheleimim, mele'im, mitokan, ubipnim* — where *bipnim*, in that formulation, clearly refers to inside the *Beit Hamikdash*, as *Rashi* maintains, making it a separate condition from *mitokan*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁאֵין דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף, אֲבָל דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן קוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to the *halakha* that service vessels sanctify only full measurements, **Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They taught** this *halakha* **only when** the priest's initial **intention** was **not to add** to that which was already placed inside the vessel. **But** if **his** initial **intention** was **to add,** then **each initial** amount placed in the vessel becomes **sacred,** no matter how small.

*Amar Rav Ashi amar Rav Yochanan: lo shanu ella she'ein da'ato lehosif* — the rule that partial filling does not sanctify applies only when one is done — one deliberately placed only a half-measure and is finished. But if one is in the process of filling, *aval da'ato lehosif rishon rishon kodesh* — if one intends to continue adding until the full measure is reached, each increment becomes sanctified as it is placed in. This is somewhat counterintuitive — one might have argued that a mid-process state cannot produce sanctification — but the reasoning is that what matters is whether the contents are conceptually part of a complete measure. If the final measure will be complete, each portion is already part of that whole and is sanctified as it enters.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: מְלֵאִין – אֵין מְלֵאִין אֶלָּא שְׁלֵימִין. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹסֵי: אֵימָתַי – בִּזְמַן שֶׁאֵין דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף; אֲבָל דַּעְתּוֹ לְהוֹסִיף – רִאשׁוֹן רִאשׁוֹן קוֹדֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** **This** distinction **is also taught** in a *baraita*: With regard to the *halakha* that service vessels sanctify **full** measurements, **full** measurements are **nothing other than whole** measurements. **Rabbi Yosei said: When** are full measurements whole ones? It is **at a time that** the priest's **intention** was **not to add. But** if **his intention** was **to add, each initial** amount is **sacred.**

*Rabbi Yossi* makes the identical ruling in the *braita*: *eimatai bizman she'ein da'ato lehosif* — the rule of non-sanctification applies when one does not intend to add more; *aval da'ato lehosif rishon rishon kodesh* — if one intends to continue, it is sanctified incrementally.

### Sanctify for Pasul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין כְּלִי הַלַּח מְקַדֵּשׁ [וְכוּ׳]. אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אַסִּי: אֵין מְקַדְּשִׁין לִיקְרַב, אֲבָל מְקַדְּשִׁין לִיפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that **the** service **vessels** used for **liquids do not sanctify** dry items. With regard to this *halakha*, **Rav says, and some say** that **Rav Asi** says: The service vessels used for liquids **do not sanctify** dry items to permit them **for sacrifice** upon the altar, **but they sanctify** dry items in order for the items **to be disqualified** by them, i.e., dry items placed in such vessels may be disqualified by that which disqualifies only sanctified items, e.g., if they are touched by one who immersed that day, or if they emerged from the Temple courtyard.

The *mishnah*'s rule that a liquid vessel does not sanctify dry contents and a dry measure does not sanctify liquid contents prompts *Amar Rav ve'iteima Rav Assi: ein mekadshin likarev aval mekadshin lipasel* — using the wrong category of vessel does not sanctify the contents so as to render them fit to be offered; but it does sanctify them in the negative sense, making them more susceptible to the invaliding consequences of *kli sharet* — they cannot be redeemed, they become *pasul* through *linah*, *yotzei*, and the like. The wrong vessel thus triggers the negative consequences of sanctification without conferring the positive effect of enabling the offering. This principle, as *Rav* notes, connects back to the opening discussion of the *daf*: placing something *pasul* in a *kli sharet* does not elevate it to be offered *lechatchila*.

### Forbidden Grain

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיכָּא דְּמַתְנֵי לַהּ אַהָא: אֵין מְבִיאִין מְנָחוֹת וּנְסָכִים וּמִנְחַת בְּהֵמָה וּבִיכּוּרִים מִן הַמְדוּמָּע, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר מֵעׇרְלָה וְכִלְאֵי הַכֶּרֶם. וְאִם הֵבִיא – לֹא קִדֵּשׁ. אָמַר רַב, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב אַסִּי: לֹא קִדֵּשׁ לִיקְרַב, אֲבָל קָדַשׁ לִיפָּסֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **There are** those **who teach this** statement **with regard to this** *halakha*: **One may not bring meal offerings, or libations, or meal offerings** accompanying **an animal, or first fruits, from a mixture** containing *teruma*, since that which may not be consumed by all Jews may not be used for an offering. **And needless to say,** one may not bring these items **from** the fruit of a tree that is ***orla*,** i.e., a tree during the first three years after its planting, from which it is prohibited to eat, **or** from **diverse kinds** sown **in a vineyard,** both of which are prohibited for consumption to priests as well. **And if he brought** an offering from them, it is **not sanctified.** With regard to this issue, **Rav says, and some say** that **Rav Asi** says: It is **not sanctified for sacrifice** upon the altar, **but** it is **sanctified** in order **to be disqualified.**

A *braita* records: *ein mevi'in min hachosem umenachas behema uvikurim min hameduma* — one may not bring these *menachot* and associated offerings from *meduma* (a mixture of forbidden produce with permitted produce). *Rashi* understands *meduma* here in the context of the principle *mimashkeh Yisrael* — only what is permissible to all Israel may be offered on the altar — and extends this remarkably to say that even *terumah*, which is accessible only to *kohanim*, cannot be offered on the altar. *Tosafot* disagrees, understanding *meduma* to refer specifically to a mixture involving *orlah* or *kilayim* — genuinely forbidden produce. The *braita* confirms: certainly pure *orlah* and *kilay hakerem* cannot be offered.

*Ve'im heivi lo kidesh* — if one went ahead and placed such forbidden flour in a vessel, it was not sanctified. *Amar Rav ve'iteima Rav Assi: lo kidesh lekarev* — it has not been made fit to be offered. *Aval kidesh lefasul* — but the negative consequences of sanctification do apply: it cannot be redeemed. The question of what "becoming *pasul*" means for something already invalid resolves precisely here: the primary relevant consequence is that the vessel's sanctity has now made the item irredeemable. Just as using the wrong type of vessel sanctifies only for invalidity, so too forbidden grain is sanctified only in the negative sense — it acquires the irredeemability of consecrated property without becoming eligible for offering.

## Repair & Maintenance

### No Vessel Repair

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כְּלֵי קֹדֶשׁ שֶׁנִּיקְּבוּ – אֵין מַתִּיכִין אוֹתָן, וְאֵין מַתִּיכִין לְתוֹכָן אֲבָר. נִפְגְּמוּ – אֵין מְתַקְּנִין אוֹתָן. סַכִּין שֶׁנִּפְגְּמָה – אֵין מַשְׁחִיזִין אֶת פְּגִימָתָהּ. נִשְׁמְטָה – אֵין מַחְזִירִין אוֹתָהּ. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: סַכִּין מַטְרֶפֶת הָיְתָה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְנִמְנוּ עָלֶיהָ כֹּהֲנִים וּגְנָזוּהָ.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to perforated vessels, **the Sages taught:** In the case of **sacred vessels that were perforated, one may not melt them** in order to seal the perforation, **and one may not melt lead into them** for such a purpose. If the vessels **were damaged, one may not repair them.** Concerning **a knife that was damaged, one may not sharpen** the spot of **its damage.** If the blade **separated** from the handle, **one may not restore it. Abba Shaul says: There was** a certain **knife in the Temple** whose metal was soft and easily damaged, such that when used it would often **render** animals **prohibited,** thereby disqualifying them. Accordingly, the **priests voted concerning it, and** elected to **hide it.**

The *gemara* continues with a *baraita* on the physical maintenance of sacred vessels and implements. *Klei kodesh shenikvu ein matichin osan* — if sacred vessels develop holes, one may not melt them down to fill the hole using their own material. *Ve'ein matichin l'sochan avar* — nor may one use lead (the ancient equivalent of soldering) to fill the gap. *Nifgemu* — if a vessel develops even a chip rather than a hole — *ein metaknin osan* — one does not repair it. The principle is that in the *Beit Hamikdash*, patching and repair have no place; once a vessel is compromised, it is retired. The *Mikdash* is a place of wealth, and nothing substandard remains in service.

### Knife Care

The *baraita* continues with examples of implements that cannot be repaired. *Sakin shenifgam ein mashchizin es pgimasov* — a knife that develops a nick may not be sharpened. *Rashi* is somewhat troubled by the literalism of this: surely removing a nick through normal sharpening is not so severe an act? *Rashi* therefore clarifies that what is prohibited is sharpening in a manner that is visibly a repair — where the correction is apparent. If the sharpening were so thorough that no evidence of the prior damage remained, it would presumably not be prohibited. The simple reading of the *baraita*, however, forbids the repair.

*Nishmetah ein machzirin otah* — if the blade detaches from the handle, one does not reattach it. Again, the presumption is that once detached, a blade can never be perfectly restored — it will always be slightly loose or imprecise in its fit. And the broader principle is clear: the *Mikdash* maintains only pristine implements.

*Abba Shaul* relates an incident: *sakin metarefet haytah b'Mikdash* — there was once a knife in the *Mikdash* so delicate that in the very act of *shechitah*, the blade itself would nick, and the nick would render the animal a *treifah*. The *kohanim* took a vote and had it removed. If a knife is causing every animal to become a *treifah*, the conclusion is obvious.

### Priestly Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה, אֵין עוֹשִׂין אוֹתָם מַעֲשֵׂה מַחַט אֶלָּא מַעֲשֵׂה אוֹרֵג, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״מַעֲשֵׂה אֹרֵג״. נִתְגַּעֲלוּ, אֵין מְכַבְּסִין [אוֹתָן] לֹא בְּנֶתֶר וְלֹא בְּאָהָל.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: Priestly vestments are not fashioned** by **needlework,** i.e., by stitching various parts together, but **rather** through **woven work,** whereby the entire garment is initially woven into one entity, **as it is stated: "Woven work"** (Exodus 28:32). If the garments **were soiled one may not launder** them, **neither with natron nor with soap,** two common detergents.

A similar standard applies to the priestly garments. *Bigdei kehunah ein osim ma'aseh machat* — the priestly garments may not be sewn; one does not take separate pieces of cut fabric and join them by needle and thread. *Ela ma'aseh oreg* — the entire garment must be woven as a single whole piece, just as the *menorah* was fashioned from a single block of gold. *She'ne'emar ma'aseh oreg* — as the Torah says, "the work of a weaver." The craftsmanship required to produce an entire garment — sleeves and all — as one continuous woven piece is extraordinary.

### Garment Soiling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָא בַּמַּיִם – מְכַבְּסִין?! אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: הוּגְּעוּ בְּמַיִם – מְכַבְּסִין אוֹתָן בְּנֶתֶר וְאָהָל; הוּגְּעוּ לְנֶתֶר וְאָהָל – אַף בְּמַיִם אֵין מְכַבְּסִים. וְיֵשׁ אוֹמְרִים: אֵין מְכַבְּסִין אוֹתָן כׇּל עִיקָּר, שֶׁאֵין עֲנִיּוּת בִּמְקוֹם עֲשִׁירוּת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** may it be inferred from this that **with water one may launder** the priestly vestments? **Abaye said: This** is what the *baraita* **is saying:** If the dirtied garments have only **reached** the point where laundering them **with water** alone would suffice, **one may launder them with natron and soap,** as they are not considered soiled. But if the garments became so dirty that they **reached** a point that laundering them would require the use of **natron or soap,** then **one may not launder** them, **even with water. And some say: One may not launder** the priestly vestments **at all,** even if laundering them with water would suffice, **because there is no poverty in a place of wealth,** i.e., only priestly vestments that were clean as new should be worn, as is befitting the Temple service, and those that were laundered should not be worn.

*Nitga'alu* — if a garment becomes soiled. *Ein mechabsin* — one does not launder it. *Lo b'neter v'lo b'ohel* — neither with *neter* (a form of lye) nor with *ohel* (another type of cleanser). The *gemara* asks: does this mean one may clean it with plain water? Even plain water cleans less effectively than these agents, so why would water be permitted if the goal is to preserve the garment's integrity?

*Amar Abaye hachi ka'amar*: Abaye reframes the *baraita*. If the soiling is minor — the word *hugu* (*Rashi*: related to *gi'ul*, something repellent but mild, the sort of thing a little water could remove) — if plain water alone would suffice, then one is permitted to use *neter* and *ohel* to clean it and return it to service. That is, for a minor stain, proper cleaning and reuse is permitted. *Hugu l'neter v'ohel* — but if the soiling is severe enough that real cleansers are required — *af b'mayim ein mechabsin* — then one may not clean it at all, not even with water. It must be retired.

*V'yesh omrim ein mechabsin otam kol ikar* — and some say that one never launders the priestly garments at all, in any case. *She'ein aniyut b'makom ashirut* — there is no poverty in a place of wealth; one simply produces new garments. This raises the obvious question, noted by students: the *kohanim* were handling blood continuously. Blood spatters were unavoidable. Does one produce entirely new garments constantly? The sources indicate that worn-out garments were eventually repurposed — for instance, as wicks for the *Simchat Beit Hashoevah* — but the precise practice with blood-stained garments remains a genuinely puzzling question given the practical realities of the *avodah*.

## Meail Construction

### Meail Design

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְעִיל – כּוּלּוֹ שֶׁל תְּכֵלֶת הָיָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיַּעַשׂ אֶת מְעִיל הָאֵפוֹד כְּלִיל תְּכֵלֶת״. שׁוּלָיו כֵּיצַד? מֵבִיא תְּכֵלֶת וְאַרְגָּמָן וְתוֹלַעַת שָׁנִי שְׁזוּרִין, וְעוֹשֶׂה אוֹתָן כְּמִין רִימּוֹנִים שֶׁלֹּא פִּיתְּחוּ פִּיהֶן, וּכְמִין קוֹנָאוֹת שֶׁל קְנָסוֹת שֶׁבְּרָאשֵׁי תִינוֹקוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § With regard to the priestly vestments, **the Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **The robe** of the High Priest **was** sewn **entirely of sky-blue wool, as it is stated: "And he made the robe of the ephod** of woven work, **all of sky-blue wool"** (Exodus 39:22). With regard to **its skirts,** concerning which it states: "And they made upon the skirts of the robe pomegranates of sky blue, and purple, and scarlet, twined" (Exodus 39:24), **how** were they fashioned? The tailor **brings sky-blue wool, and purple wool, and scarlet wool,** which are **twined** together, **and fashions them** to appear **as pomegranates that have not opened their mouths,** i.e., they are sewn in the appearance of pomegranates that are not yet ripe enough for the crown on top to open, **and as** the **cones [*konaot*] of** the **helmets [*kenasot*] that** are found **on the heads of children.**

The *gemara* describes the construction of the *me'il* worn by the *Kohen Gadol*. *Me'il kulo shel techeiles haya* — the *me'il* was entirely of sky-blue (*techeiles*), as the verse states: *vaya'as es me'il ha'efod kelil techeiles* — he made the *me'il* of the *efod* entirely of *techeiles*.

The hem (*shulov*) consisted of alternating pomegranates and bells. The pomegranates were fashioned from three threads spun together: *techeiles*, *argaman* (purple), and *tola'as shani* (crimson). They were made in the form of pomegranates whose tops had not yet opened — complete, closed forms — and *Talmud* compares them further to the conical protrusions on helmets worn by children. The precise visual implication of this comparison is not entirely clear, but the general shape is that of a closed, rounded form.

### Bells Pomegranates

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמֵבִיא שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם זַגִּין שֶׁבָּהֶן שִׁבְעִים וּשְׁנַיִם עִינְבָּלִין, וְתוֹלֶה בָּהֶן שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה בְּצַד זֶה וּשְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה מִצַּד זֶה. רַבִּי דּוֹסָא אוֹמֵר מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: שְׁלֹשִׁים וְשִׁשָּׁה הָיוּ – שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה מִצַּד זֶה וּשְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה מִצַּד זֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **And** in order to fulfill that which is stated: "And they made bells of pure gold, and put the bells between the pomegranates" (Exodus 39:25), he **brings seventy-two bells,** i.e., the outer part of bells, made from gold, **that** contain **inside them seventy-two bell clappers, and he suspends** them **on** the skirts: **Thirty-six** of each, i.e., pomegranates and bells, **on this side** of the robe, **and thirty-six** of each **on that side,** as the verse states: "A bell and a pomegranate, a bell and a pomegranate, upon the skirts of the robe around it" (Exodus 39:26). **Rabbi Dosa says in the name of Rabbi Yehuda: There were thirty-six** bells suspended around the skirt, **eighteen from this side and eighteen from that side.**

*U'mevi shivim u'shnayim zugim* — one brings 72 bells, with 72 clappers inside them. *Ve'toleh bahen shloshim ve'shishah mitzad zeh ve'shloshim ve'shishah mitzad zeh* — 36 bells are hung on one side and 36 on the other, alternating with pomegranates: 36 pomegranates and 36 bells on each of the two sides of the garment.

*Rabbi Dosa omer mishum Rabbi Yehudah*: the total was 36 bells and 36 pomegranates — 18 on each side. *Amar Rav Inini bar Sasson: kemachlokes kan kach machlokes be'tzara'as* — the same dispute of 72 versus 36 recurs in the context of the visible appearances of *nega'im*: *Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas* says 36, and *Akavya ben Mahalalel* says 72. The reason this parallel is drawn will become clearer momentarily.

## Garments Atone

### Sacrifices & Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי עִינְיֹנִי בַּר שָׂשׂוֹן: לָמָּה נִסְמְכָה פָּרָשַׁת קׇרְבָּנוֹת לְפָרָשַׁת בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה? לוֹמַר לָךְ: מָה קׇרְבָּנוֹת מְכַפְּרִין, אַף בִּגְדֵי כְהוּנָּה מְכַפְּרִין.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara cites another statement of this sage: **And Rabbi Inini bar Sason says: Why** was **the passage** in the Torah that discusses **offerings** (Leviticus, chapters 1–7) **juxtaposed to the passage** that discusses the **priestly vestments** (Leviticus, chapter 8)? It was juxtaposed **to tell you** that **just as offerings effect atonement, so too, priestly vestments effect atonement.**

Having quoted *Rav Inini bar Sasson*, the *gemara* continues with an *aggadic* teaching from the same *amora* that provides a fitting conclusion to the *masechet*. *Lama nismacha parashas korbanos le'parashas bigdei kehunah?* — why were the *parashah* of sacrifices and the *parashah* of priestly garments juxtaposed in *Tzav*? In *Shemot*, the construction of all the *Mikdash* furnishings and the garments are naturally grouped together. But in *Tzav*, the first half discusses the bringing of *korbanos*, and the second half discusses the dressing of *Aharon* and the *kohanim* in their *begadim*. The juxtaposition is deliberate. *Lomar lecha: mah korbanos mechaperim af bigdei kehunah mechaperim* — to tell you: just as sacrifices atone, so the priestly garments atone.

### Kesones Bloodshed

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּתוֹנֶת – מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל שְׁפִיכוּת (דם) [דָּמִים], שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּשְׁחֲטוּ שְׂעִיר עִזִּים וַיִּטְבְּלוּ אֶת הַכֻּתֹּנֶת בַּדָּם״. מִכְנָסַיִם – מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל גִּילּוּי עֲרָיוֹת, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַעֲשֵׂה לָהֶם מִכְנְסֵי בָד [לְכַסּוֹת (אֶת) בְּשַׂר עֶרְוָה]״. מִצְנֶפֶת – מְכַפֶּרֶת עַל גַּסֵּי הָרוּחַ. מִנַּיִן? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: יָבֹא דָּבָר שֶׁבַּגּוֹבַהּ, וִיכַפֵּר עַל גּוֹבַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The **tunic atones for bloodshed, as it is stated** with regard to the brothers of Joseph after they plotted to kill him: **"And they killed a goat, and dipped the tunic in the blood"** (Genesis 37:31). The **trousers atone for forbidden sexual relations, as it is stated** with regard to fashioning the priestly vestments: **"And you shall make them linen trousers to cover the flesh of their nakedness"** (Exodus 28:42). The **mitre atones for the arrogant. From where** is this derived? **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** It is logical that **an item** that is placed **at an elevation,** i.e., on the head of a priest, **shall come and atone for** the sin of **an elevated** heart.

*Kesones mechaperes al shefichus damim* — the basic tunic worn by every *kohen* atones for the spilling of blood. The verse cited: *vayishchatu se'ir izim vayitbelu es hakesones badam* — they slaughtered a goat and dipped the *kesones* in blood, the episode of *Yosef*. Because the *kesones* was used to conceal something adjacent to bloodshed — the near-murder of *Yosef* and the slaughter of the animal — it correspondingly atones for bloodshed. The *Maharsha* explains the association structurally: the *kesones* is the garment closest to the body. The body is where blood resides; the *kesones* most intimately represents the human body. Therefore, the *kesones* atones for acts directed against the body — namely, murder.

An important qualification must be stated at the outset, and it will be elaborated further below: this does not mean that a murderer is forgiven because the *Kohen Gadol* wears a *kesones*. As *Tosafot* explains across this passage, these atonements address communal responsibility — the shared culpability of the community for having allowed such sins to occur, for failing to prevent them, to prosecute them, or to create a society in which they would not arise. Atonement for the individual sinner is a separate matter entirely.

### Breeches Miter Belt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבְנֵט – מְכַפֵּר עַל הִרְהוּר הַלֵּב, הֵיכָא דְּאִיתֵיהּ. חוֹשֶׁן – מְכַפֵּר עַל הַדִּינִין, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וְעָשִׂיתָ חֹשֶׁן מִשְׁפָּט״. אֵפוֹד – מְכַפֵּר עַל עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אֵין אֵפוֹד וּתְרָפִים״.
>
> **Translation:** Rabbi Inini bar Sason continues: The **belt atones for thought of the heart.** The Gemara elaborates: The belt atones for the sins occurring **where it is** situated, i.e., over the heart. The **breastplate** of the High Priest **atones for** improper **judgments, as it is stated: "And you shall make a breastplate of judgment"** (Exodus 28:15). The **ephod** of the High Priest **atones for idol worship, as it is stated:** "And **without ephod or teraphim"** (Hosea 3:4), meaning that when there is no ephod, the sin of teraphim, i.e., idol worship, is found. Therefore, it may be inferred that if there is an ephod, there is no sin of idol worship.

*Michnasayim mechaper al giluy arayot* — the breeches (*michnasayim*) atone for sexual immorality. The connection is anatomical: the *michnasayim* covers *besar ervah*, the flesh of nakedness, as the verse states explicitly. Again, the *gemara*'s understanding, as clarified by *Tosafot*, is that this refers to communal responsibility for the prevalence of sexual sin.

*Mitznefet mechaper al gasei haruach* — the miter atones for arrogance. *Amar Rabi Chanina: yavo davar shebegovah veychaper al govah* — something placed at a height comes and atones for the one who exalts himself with haughtiness. The miter sits at the highest point of the body.

*Avnet mechaper al hirhur halev* — the belt atones for evil intentions of the heart. This is striking, since the tradition sometimes holds that improper thoughts alone are not reckoned by God as action. Yet the *gemara* here insists that thoughts of the heart do require atonement — perhaps the point being that while God does not treat evil thoughts as equivalent to acts, they are nonetheless not nothing. Notably, an *olah* is elsewhere said to atone for *hirhur halev* as well. The *avnet* is placed near the heart — not at the waist as one might assume, but higher, close to the chest and elbows, anatomically proximate to the seat of intention.

### High Priest Garments

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מְעִיל – מְכַפֵּר עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע. מִנַּיִן? אָמַר רַבִּי חֲנִינָא: יָבֹא דָּבָר שֶׁבַּקּוֹל, וִיכַפֵּר עַל קוֹל הָרָע. וְצִיץ – מְכַפֵּר עַל עַזּוּת פָּנִים; בְּצִיץ כְּתִיב: ״וְהָיָה עַל מֵצַח אַהֲרֹן״, וּבְעַזּוּת פָּנִים כְּתִיב: ״וּמֵצַח אִשָּׁה זוֹנָה הָיָה לָךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** The **robe** of the High Priest **atones for malicious speech. From where** is this known? **Rabbi Ḥanina says:** It is logical that **an item that** produces **sound,** i.e., the robe, which has bells, **shall come and atone for an evil sound. And** the **frontplate** of the High Priest **atones for brazenness.** This is derived from the fact that **with regard to the frontplate it is written: "And it shall be upon Aaron's forehead"** (Exodus 28:38), **and with regard to brazenness it is written: "And you had a harlot's forehead"** (Jeremiah 3:3).

The *Kohen Gadol* wears four additional garments, each with its own atoning function.

*Choshen mechaper al hadinim* — the *choshen* atones for a perversion of justice. The verse: *ve'asita choshen mishpat* — the *choshen* of justice.

*Efod mechaper al avodat kochavim* — the *efod* atones for idolatry. The verse: *ein efod utrafim* — there is no *efod* and no *trafim*. As the *Maharsha* notes, the *efod* was historically associated also with idolatrous practice — it was a garment worn by priests of other cults — and so the verse's pairing of *efod* and *trafim* links it to idolatry. Read as a consequence clause, the verse implies: because there is no *efod*, you will be punished for your idolatry.

*Me'il mechaper al lashon hara* — the *me'il* atones for evil speech. *Amar Rabi Chanina: yavo davar shebekol veyechaper al kol hara* — something that makes sound atones for the evil sound, the evil voice. The verse: *venishma kolo bevo'o el hakodesh* — his sound shall be heard when he enters the sanctuary; the bells produce sound with every movement. This is also why, *Maharsha* notes, the *gemara* earlier drew the parallel between the dispute about the number of bells and the dispute about the appearances of *tzara'at* — *tzara'at* too is traditionally the punishment for *lashon hara*, making the connection between the bells of the *me'il* and *tzara'at* thematically coherent.

*Tzitz mechaper al azut panim* — the *tzitz*, the golden plate worn on the forehead, atones for brazenness. Brazenness (*azut panim*) is distinct from arrogance (*gaavah*): one may be inwardly proud without acting with *chutzpah*, and one may be brazenly shameless without the internal haughtiness that the *mitznefet* addresses. The verse for the *tzitz*: *vehayah al metzach Aharon* — it shall be on *Aharon*'s forehead. The verse for brazenness: *umetzach ishah zonah hayah lach* — you had the forehead of a brazen woman. The forehead is the anatomical symbol of unrestrained boldness.

### Atone Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן לֵוִי: שְׁנֵי דְּבָרִים לֹא מָצִינוּ לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה בְּקׇרְבָּנוֹת, וּמָצִינוּ לָהֶן כַּפָּרָה מִמָּקוֹם אַחֵר; וְאֵלּוּ הֵן: שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים וְלָשׁוֹן הָרָע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that the priestly vestments atone for these sins? **But doesn't Rabbi Yehoshua ben Levi say:** There are **two matters** that **we do not find for them an atonement with offerings, but we find for them an atonement from another place, and they are: Bloodshed and malicious speech.**

The *gemara* raises an objection: is it really true that these garments atone for *shefichus damim* and *lashon hara*? *Ve'amar Rabi Yehoshua ben Levi: shenei devarim lo matzinu lahem kaparah bekorbanot umatzinu lo kaparah mimakom acher* — for two things we find no atonement in sacrifices, and we found atonement elsewhere: *shefichus damim* and *lashon hara*. *Shefichus damim* finds atonement through *egla arufa*; *lashon hara* finds atonement through the *ketoret*. This creates an apparent contradiction with our *baraita*, which assigns atonement for these sins to the *kesones* and the *me'il* respectively.

### Murderer Known/Unknown

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים – מֵעֶגְלָה עֲרוּפָה, וְלָשׁוֹן הָרָע – מִקְּטֹרֶת. דְּתָנֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָה: מִנַּיִן לִקְטֹרֶת שֶׁמְּכַפֶּרֶת? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַיִּתֵּן אֶת הַקְּטֹרֶת וַיְכַפֵּר עַל הָעָם״.
>
> **Translation:** With regard to **bloodshed,** its atonement comes **from** the **heifer whose neck is broken.** This is referring to a case where a murdered body is found but the identity of the murderer is not known. In such an instance, the Torah mandates that the neck of a heifer must be broken as an atonement for the murder. **And** with regard to **malicious speech,** its atonement comes **from incense, as Rav Ḥananya teaches** in a *baraita*: **From where** is it derived that **the incense effects atonement? As it is stated** after the Israelites spoke slanderously against Moses and Aaron and a plague was sent against them: **"And he put on the incense, and made atonement for the people"** (Numbers 17:12).

*Lo kashia* — there is no contradiction. *Ha deyedia man katlei, ha delo yedia man katlei* — the *egla arufa* applies when the murderer is unknown; that is the classic case for which *egla arufa* was prescribed. When the murderer is known, the *kesones* atones — for the communal responsibility that accrued despite knowledge of the perpetrator's identity.

But here a further question arises: if the murderer is known, the community should simply prosecute him. Why does any atonement remain necessary? *Bemezid velo azhero bei* — he acted deliberately (*mezid*) but was not formally warned (*hatra'ah*) before committing the act. Without proper warning, he cannot be executed by the court even though he is known to be the murderer. The community knows who did it, cannot execute him, yet cannot claim complete innocence — they bear a residual communal responsibility for having created the conditions under which murder occurred and went unpunished. For this shared responsibility, the *kesones* atones.

### Speech Privacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתָנֵי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יִשְׁמָעֵאל: עַל מָה קְטֹרֶת מְכַפֶּרֶת? עַל לָשׁוֹן הָרָע. יָבֹא דָּבָר שֶׁבַּחֲשַׁאי, וִיכַפֵּר עַל מַעֲשֶׂה חֲשַׁאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: **And** similarly, **the school of Rabbi Yishmael teaches: For what does incense effect atonement?** It effects atonement **for malicious speech,** in order that **an item that** is offered **in private,** i.e., the incense, which is offered by a priest acting alone, **shall come and atone for an action** generally occurring in **private,** i.e., malicious speech.

*Lashon hara lashon hara* — there is also an apparent contradiction regarding evil speech: does the *ketoret* atone for it, or the *me'il*? *Nami lo kashia* — this too is resolved. *Ha betzina, ha befarhesia* — the *ketoret* atones for *lashon hara* spoken in private; the *me'il* atones for *lashon hara* spoken publicly.

The derivation: *yavo davar shebachashai veyechaper al maaseh chashai* — something brought in secret atones for acts done in secret. The *ketoret* is brought in the *heichal*, and while it is being offered, no other *kohanim* are permitted to be present — not only on *Yom Kippur* but as a general rule. The *ketoret* is inherently a private act. *Lashon hara*, characteristically, is also spoken privately; people do not typically spread slander publicly. So the *ketoret*, brought in private, atones for speech that is spread in private. For *lashon hara* broadcast publicly, the *me'il* — which makes sound publicly, heard by all — provides atonement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קַשְׁיָא לָשׁוֹן הָרָע אַלָּשׁוֹן הָרָע, קַשְׁיָא שְׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים אַשְּׁפִיכוּת דָּמִים! לָא קַשְׁיָא; הָא דִּידִיעַ מַאן קַטְלֵיהּ, הָא דְּלָא יְדִיעַ מַאן קַטְלֵיהּ. אִי דִּידִיעַ מַאן קַטְלֵיהּ – בַּר קְטָלָא הוּא! בְּמֵזִיד וְלָא אַתְרוֹ בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Accordingly, there is **a difficulty** between that which is stated with regard to **malicious speech and** that which is stated with regard to **malicious speech,** as according to Rabbi Inini bar Sason the robe atones for malicious speech, whereas according to the *baraita* it is only the incense that effects atonement for that transgression. Likewise, there is **a difficulty** between that which is stated with regard to **bloodshed and** that which is stated with regard to **bloodshed,** as according to Rabbi Inini bar Sason the tunic effects atonement for bloodshed, whereas according to the *baraita* only the heifer whose neck is broken effects atonement for it. The Gemara answers: With regard to bloodshed, it is **not difficult,** as **this,** the tunic, effects atonement for bloodshed in an instance **where it is known who killed** the victim, and **this,** the heifer, effects atonement in an instance **where it is not known who killed** the victim. The Gemara challenges: **If it is known who killed** the victim, that man **is deserving of death,** and there is no atonement for the community otherwise, as it is stated: "And no atonement can be made for the land for the blood that is shed within it, but by the blood of him that shed it" (Numbers 35:33). The Gemara responds: It is referring to a case where he murdered **intentionally but** witnesses **did not forewarn him** of the consequences of committing murder. Therefore, the court may not execute him, as no earthly punishment may be administered without forewarning.

*Tosafot* again emphasizes that in all of these cases the atonement is for communal responsibility, not for the individual perpetrator.

### Communal Responsibility

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלָשׁוֹן הָרָע אַלָּשׁוֹן הָרָע נָמֵי לָא קַשְׁיָא – הָא בְּצִינְעָא, הָא בְּפַרְהֶסְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And** with regard to the contradiction between that which is stated with regard to **malicious speech and** that which is stated with regard to **malicious speech,** it is **also not difficult. This,** the incense, effects atonement for malicious speech spoken **in private,** whereas **this,** the robe, on which the bells that produce noise are placed, effects atonement for malicious speech spoken **in public.**

It is striking to close *masechet Zevachim* here. The vast majority of the tractate is relentlessly technical: what makes a *korban* valid or invalid, what constitutes *pasul* and why, the precise mechanics of *shechitah*, *zerikah*, *melikah*, the parameters of *kli sharet*, and so on. The focus throughout is almost entirely on procedural correctness, not on the experiential or moral significance of what the *avodah* accomplishes.

Yet here, at the end, the *gemara* pivots entirely — to a discussion of what the *avodah* actually atones for, and to categories of sin that are not defined by technical halachic categories like *karet* and *chatas*, but by the texture of moral life: arrogance, evil thoughts, sexual misconduct, slander, brazenness, perversion of justice. These are the sins that ordinary people contend with, regardless of their familiarity with the laws of sacrifices.

And the mode of atonement described here is similarly accessible. *Korbanos* are remote from contemporary experience. But clothing — how one dresses, what one's garments represent, how one presents oneself to one's community — is not remote. The *gemara* elsewhere draws implicit comparisons between *tzitzit* and the *begadim* of the *Kohen Gadol*, and between *tefillin* and the *mitznefet*. The wearing of garments is itself an act of representing oneself, of signaling one's relationship to community and to moral aspiration. The placement of each atoning garment on a specific part of the body — the *mitznefet* on the head for arrogance, the *avnet* near the heart for hidden thoughts, the *michnasayim* over the loins for sexual sin — is not merely mnemonic. It suggests that the body itself is the site of moral responsibility, and that the priest who serves on behalf of the community is, in a sense, wearing the community's moral accounting on his person.

The *gemara* does not connect all the dots explicitly, and these observations should not be overstated. But the juxtaposition — ending a *masechet* of extraordinary technical detail with an *aggadic* passage about real, recognizable, human moral failures and the accessible language of clothing and comportment — feels deliberate and significant. *Hadran lach mizbeach mekadesh.*
