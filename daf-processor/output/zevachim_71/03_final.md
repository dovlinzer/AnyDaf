# Zevachim 71 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Ta'arovet

### Perek Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בְּחַטָּאוֹת הַמֵּתוֹת אוֹ בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת בְּרִיבּוֹא – יָמוּתוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **All the offerings that were intermingled with** animals from which deriving benefit is forbidden, e.g., **sin offerings** left to **die, or with an ox that** was sentenced to be **stoned, even** if the ratio is **one in ten thousand,** deriving benefit from them all is prohibited and **they all must die.**

Daf 71 of Zevachim opens a new *perek*, *Kol HaZevachim*, found at the very bottom of 70b. This chapter marks a conceptual shift in the tractate: rather than analyzing the precise mechanics of how *korbanot* are offered, the *gemara* now turns to broader halakhic questions — most centrally, the laws of *ta'arovet*, the mixing of one thing with another. This is a topic with a wide footprint across rabbinic literature: it appears in Chullin in the context of *basar b'chalav* and *gid hanasheh*, in Zeraim in discussions of *kilayim* and *orlah*, and here in Kodshim as well. The nature of the items under discussion differs across each domain, but the underlying conceptual framework is shared.

It is worth noting at the outset that 71a is a very short *amud*, containing some notably large *Tosafot* — some directly relevant to our *sugya*, others addressing much broader questions. The *Tosafot* most pertinent to the present discussion will be treated here; a particularly substantial passage will be addressed when we reach it tomorrow.

### Ta'arovet Concept

The *perek* unfolds through a series of cases, each involving a different type of mixture, and works toward establishing both what the halakhic problem is and, where possible, how it can be resolved. The cases escalate in complexity: from a single forbidden animal mixed into a large flock of *korbanot*, to mixtures of *chullin* and *kodshim*, to mixtures of different categories of *kodshim* with one another. Throughout, the central question is whether *bitul* — the ordinary halakhic mechanism of nullification — can operate, and if not, what alternative remedies exist.

## Chataot HaMetot

### Vitamna Mnemonic

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בְּחַטָּאוֹת הַמֵּתוֹת אוֹ בְּשׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת בְּרִיבּוֹא – יָמוּתוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** **All the offerings that were intermingled with** animals from which deriving benefit is forbidden, e.g., **sin offerings** left to **die, or with an ox that** was sentenced to be **stoned, even** if the ratio is **one in ten thousand,** deriving benefit from them all is prohibited and **they all must die.**

The *mishna* opens: *kol hazevachim shenitarvu bechatat hameitot* — all animal *korbanot* that became mixed with a *chatat* that must die. There are five categories of *chatat* subject to this fate, remembered through the mnemonic *vitamna* (ותמנ"ע). Each letter stands for one of the five types.

### Five Categories

The *vav* stands for *vlad chatat*: if a female *chatat* gives birth, the offspring inherits a kind of *chatat* status, yet since there is no underlying *chet* that it was designated to atone for, it cannot be offered. The *tav* stands for *tmurat chatat*: if someone attempts to exchange a *chatat* for another animal — an act that does not succeed in removing the *kedushah* but does sanctify the substitute — the *temurah* animal cannot itself be brought as a *korban*. The *mem* stands for *metu ba'aleha*: the owners of the *chatat* have died, so while the animal was set aside for a genuine *chet*, bringing it no longer serves any meaningful purpose. The *nun* stands for *niskapru ba'acher*: the original *chatat* was lost, a replacement was found and offered, and atonement was achieved — and then the first animal turned up; since the *chet* has already been atoned for, the original animal is now superfluous. Finally, the *ayin* stands for *avra shnatah*: a *chatat* that is no longer within its first year of life and is therefore invalid.

What unites all five categories is a conceptual irony: each animal bears the status of a *chatat* without there being any *chet* that it can actually address. The *halacha lemoshe misinai* for all five is that such animals must be left to die — unlike other invalid *korbanot*, which can be allowed to develop a *mum* and then redeemed, these cannot.

### One in 10,000 Rule

The *mishna* continues: *o beshor haniskal* — or an ox that gored and killed a person and was therefore condemned to be stoned. If either a *chatat hameitah* or a *shor haniskal* becomes mixed into a flock of otherwise valid *korbanot*, *afilu achat beribo* — even if there is only one such forbidden animal among ten thousand kosher ones — *yamutu kulam*, they must all die.

The logic here becomes clear once one appreciates why neither *bitul* nor redemption offers a way out. As the *gemara* will elaborate, *bitul* does not apply to live animals because each animal retains its individual significance — but even setting that aside, there is a more fundamental problem. The *chatat hameitah* and *shor haniskal* are not merely *pasul* for the *mizbe'ach*; they are *issurei hana'ah* — one may derive no benefit from them whatsoever. Even if the entire flock were redeemed, converting the *kedushah* to money, the problem would persist: since any individual animal in the flock might be the forbidden one, no individual animal can be redeemed or benefited from. The only outcome is that all must die.

## No Bitul for Animals

### Ba'alei Chaim

One might immediately ask: why does *bitul* not operate here? In most contexts of *ta'arovet*, when a forbidden substance falls into a larger quantity of permitted substance, the forbidden item is *battel* — nullified — and the mixture may be used. The *gemara*'s ultimate answer, as it will develop, is that live animals — *ba'alei chaim* — are categorically different from inanimate substances. Each animal is a discrete, identifiable entity with its own standing, and therefore no single animal can be absorbed into a larger group and deemed insignificant. This is why the rule of *bitul berov* (nullification by majority) does not apply here.

### Issurei Hana'ah

The severity of the case involving *chatat hameitah* and *shor haniskal* flows directly from their status as *issurei hana'ah*. These animals cannot be offered on the *mizbe'ach* — that much is obvious from their disqualification — but crucially, they also cannot serve any purpose for a layperson. One may not benefit from a *shor haniskal*; one may not benefit from a *chatat hameitah*. Therefore, even the ordinary remedy of redeeming the flock once the animals develop a *mum* is unavailable: since any animal in the flock might be the *shor haniskal*, and since the *shor haniskal* cannot be redeemed or benefited from, there is no permissible use to which even the redeemed money could be put with confidence. The only result is that all must die.

### Permissible for Benefit

The *mishna* then pivots to a different category: animals that cannot be brought as *korbanot* but are nonetheless permitted for a layperson to benefit from. This distinction is critical, because it opens the door to practical remedies that the *issurei hana'ah* case forecloses. The *mishna* proceeds to enumerate which animals fall into this intermediate category, and it is for these cases that the *mishna* will offer solutions.

## Forbidden Animals

### Sin Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהֵמִית אֶת הָאָדָם עַל פִּי עֵד אֶחָד אוֹ עַל פִּי הַבְּעָלִים, בְּרוֹבֵעַ וְנִרְבָּע, בְּמוּקְצֶה וְנֶעֱבָד, בְּאֶתְנַן וּמְחִיר,
>
> **Translation:** an ox **that** is known to have **killed a person based on** the testimony of **one witness or based on** the admission of **the owner.** Had two witnesses testified, deriving benefit from the ox would have been prohibited. Additional examples include when an offering is intermingled **with an animal that copulated with a person; or an animal that was the object of bestiality;** or **with** an animal that was **set aside** for idol worship; **or** one that **was worshipped** as a deity; or **with** an animal that was given as **payment** to a prostitute **or** as the **price** of a dog, as it is written: "You shall not bring the payment of a harlot, or the price of a dog, into the house of the Lord your God" (Deuteronomy 23:19).

The *mishna* offers several examples of animals in the category of *shor she'ne'evda bo aveira* — an ox in connection with which a sin was committed. The paradigm case is an ox that killed a person: *she'heimis es ha'adam al pi ed echad* — where it caused a death but there was only one witness rather than the two required. With two witnesses, the ox would be a full *shor haniskal*, condemned to stoning and forbidden in all benefit. With only one witness, or where the admission comes from the owners themselves (*al pi haba'alim*), the ox cannot be brought as a *korban* — it is disqualified for the *mizbe'ach* — but there is no prohibition on a layperson deriving benefit from it. It is the witness distinction that determines whether the animal crosses into the domain of *issurei hana'ah* or merely *pesul korban*.

### Bestiality Animals

Similarly, *be'rove'a ve'nirva* — an animal involved in an act of bestiality, whether as the active or passive party — follows the same structure. With two witnesses, such an animal becomes forbidden for benefit entirely; this emerges from other *gemarot* and *rishonim* even if not made fully explicit in this *mishna*. With one witness or the owner's admission, it is disqualified from the *mizbe'ach* but remains permissible for layperson use. Rashi and *Tosafot* note that the precise scriptural source for the *issur hana'ah* in the two-witness case requires some reconstruction, but the operative distinction — one witness versus two — is consistent with the pattern established above.

### Idolatry Animals

The *mishna* then mentions *be'muktzeh* — an animal set aside (*muktzeh*) for *avoda zara* — and *ve'ne'evad* — an animal actually worshipped as *avoda zara*. Strikingly, these do not render the animal *assur behana'ah* to a layperson, and the reason is a fundamental principle: the rules of *avoda zara* that create *issurei hana'ah* apply to inanimate objects. Live animals cannot become *assur behana'ah* through *avoda zara*, even if they were actually worshipped. They are, however, disqualified from serving as *korbanot*. This is a genuinely counterintuitive ruling — one might have expected that actual worship would produce the most severe prohibition — yet the halakha draws a firm line between animate and inanimate objects in this context.

### Other Forbidden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּכִלְאַיִם וּבִטְרֵיפָה, בְּיוֹצֵא דּוֹפֶן – יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵי הַיָּפֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן מֵאוֹתוֹ הַמִּין.
>
> **Translation:** Additional examples include an offering that was intermingled **with** an animal born of a mixture of **diverse kinds,** e.g., the offspring of a ram and a goat, **or with an animal with a wound that will cause it to die within twelve months [*tereifa*],** or **with** an animal **born by caesarean section.** In all these cases the animals that are intermingled **shall graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice **and** then **they shall be sold. And** from the money received in the sale, the owner **shall bring** another offering **of the monetary value of** the **highest-quality** animal **among them, of the same type** of offering that the intermingled offering was.

The *mishna* continues: *be'esnan u'mechir* — an animal given as payment to a prostitute (*esnan*) or exchanged for a dog (*mechir kelev*). Both of these are explicitly disqualified from the *mizbe'ach* by a *pasuk* in Devarim, yet both remain entirely permissible for layperson consumption and benefit. Next, *be'kilayim* — a hybrid or crossbreed animal. For such an animal to find its way into this case, it must be one that cannot be visually distinguished from a kosher animal, since it is supposedly getting mixed into a flock of valid *korbanot* without anyone noticing. Finally, *u've'treifa* — a *treifa*, an animal with a fatal organic defect. The *gemara* will need to work out how a *treifa* could be mixed into a flock undetected; and the *treifa* presents a further complication, since unlike the other animals in this category, it cannot be eaten by humans — only used for purposes such as dog food. This will require a modified solution, which the *mishna* will address.

### Cesarean Section

The final example in this portion is *be'yotze dofen* — an animal born by Cesarean section. We derive from the *pasuk* *shor kesev o ez ki yivaled* — a bull, sheep, or goat when it is born — that a *korban* must be born through a normal birth. A Cesarean birth disqualifies the animal from the *mizbe'ach*. There is, however, no prohibition on layperson benefit, so this animal belongs fully in the remedial category that the *mishna* is now about to address.

## Mum & Redemption

### Yir'u Ad Yista'avu

The *mishna* here prescribes the following remedy: *yir'u ad she'yista'avu* — let them graze until they develop a *mum*, a physical blemish. Once all the animals in the mixed flock have developed a blemish, they can be redeemed: their *kedushah* transfers to money in the act of sale, they become ordinary *chullin*, and the proceeds can be used to purchase valid replacement *korbanot*.

### Money Transfer

The practical mechanics are as follows. Suppose nine valid *olos* are mixed with one *yotze dofen* that cannot be identified. All ten are allowed to graze until each develops a *mum*. They are then sold together. At the moment of sale, the *kedushah* of the nine valid *olos* transfers to the money received for them. The buyer, who receives ten animals that are now *chullin*, is permitted to eat or otherwise use them — and since the problematic animal (say, a *yotze dofen* or *kilayim*) is permissible for layperson consumption, there is no obstacle to his using any of the ten. The money received, carrying the transferred *kedushah*, is then used to purchase nine valid *olos* of the same *min* — the same species and type of *korban* — which are brought in place of the originals.

### Dmei Hayafeh

The *mishna*'s phrase *bidmei hayafeh she'bahen me'oso hamin* — using the money of the finest among them, of the same type — captures an important refinement. Some portion of the money received in the sale need not go toward replacement *korbanot*, because one of the original animals was not *kodesh* to begin with; the seller may pocket that amount. But how much?

If all the animals were of equal value — say, one hundred dollars each — then for ten animals sold at one thousand dollars, nine hundred goes to purchase nine replacement *olos* and one hundred is kept, representing the value of the one non-*kodesh* animal. But if the animals were of different values, the principle of *bidmei hayafeh* applies: one must assume that the *hekdesh* animals were the more valuable ones. So if the animals ranged in value from one hundred dollars down to ten, only ten dollars — the value of the least expensive animal — may be kept. The possibility that the non-*kodesh* animal was actually worth ninety dollars must be disregarded, because we must give the benefit of the doubt to *hekdesh*. The higher-valued animals are assumed to have been the *korbanot*, and their full value goes toward the replacement offerings.

### Treifa Solution

The *treifa* presents a complication within this framework. Since a *treifa* cannot be eaten by humans, the buyer of the mixed flock cannot simply eat them as he would in the ordinary case. However, the *treifa* is permissible for dog food — it can be slaughtered and sold for that purpose. Therefore, even in the *treifa* case, some form of salvage remains possible: all ten animals are sold, and though the seller will receive a reduced price (since the animals cannot be sold for human consumption), at least partial value is recovered and the mechanism of *kedushah* transfer can still function. The solution is imperfect but operative.

## Chullin-Kodshim Mix

### Chullin Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְעָרְבוּ בְּחוּלִּין תְּמִימִים – יִמָּכְרוּ הַחוּלִּין לְצוֹרְכֵי אוֹתוֹ הַמִּין.
>
> **Translation:** The mishna continues: If sacrificial animals **were intermingled with unblemished, non-sacred** animals, which, if consecrated, are fit for sacrifice, **the non-sacred** animals **shall be sold for the purpose** of purchasing offerings **of the same type** as the offering with which they were intermingled.

The *mishna* now addresses a simpler case: *nis'arvu chullin bi'tmimim* — non-sanctified animals mixed with valid, unblemished *korbanot*, where neither animal has any intrinsic disqualification. Here the solution is straightforward. One need only find a buyer who himself needs to bring a *korban* of the same type. That buyer pays the value of one animal, and it is declared that whichever animal in the mixed pair is currently *chullin* is now being purchased and sanctified as that buyer's *korban*. Both animals are then given to the *kohen*, who offers both. One works for the original owner; the other works for the new buyer. Since a *kohen* need not have any specific owner in mind when offering a *korban*, the offering is valid regardless of which animal is actually which. Both animals end up as valid *korbanot* brought for identified owners, and the problem dissolves.

### Same Type Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים בְּקָדָשִׁים מִין בְּמִינוֹ – זֶה יִקְרַב לְשֵׁם מִי שֶׁהוּא, וְזֶה יִקְרַב לְשֵׁם מִי שֶׁהוּא.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **sacrificial** animals were intermingled **with** other **sacrificial** animals, if it was an animal of one **type** of offering **with** animals of the same **type** of offering, **one shall sacrifice this** animal **for the sake of whoever is** its owner **and one shall sacrifice that** animal **for the sake of whoever is** its owner, and both fulfill their obligation.

*Kodshim bekodshim min bemino* — what if two *kodshim* of the same type, say Reuven's *olah* and Shimon's *olah*, become mixed and cannot be distinguished? This is even simpler: *ze yikarev leshem mi shehu veze yikarev leshem mi shehu* — each is offered for whoever it belongs to. Since a *kohen* does not need to specify the owner at the time of offering, both can be brought without difficulty. In fact, the *chullin-kodshim* case resolves into this scenario once a buyer is found: the formerly *chullin* animal is sanctified, making both animals *kodshim* of the same type, and both can then be offered.

### Different Type Mix

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** קָדָשִׁים בְּקָדָשִׁים מִין בְּשֶׁאֵינוֹ מִינוֹ – יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ וְיִמָּכְרוּ, וְיָבִיא בִּדְמֵי יָפֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן מִמִּין זֶה, וּבִדְמֵי הַיָּפֶה שֶׁבָּהֶן מִמִּין זֶה, וְיַפְסִיד הַמּוֹתָר מִבֵּיתוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where **sacrificial** animals were intermingled **with** other **sacrificial** animals, where an animal of one **type** of offering was intermingled **with** animals **not** of the same **type** of offering, e.g., two rams, where one is designated as a burnt offering and one as a peace offering, **they shall graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice **and** then **they shall be sold. And** from the money received in the sale, the owner **shall bring** another offering **of the monetary value of** the **highest-quality** animal **among them as this type** of offering, **and** another offering **of the monetary value of** the **highest-quality** animal **among them as that type** of offering, **and he will lose the additional** expense of purchasing two highest-quality animals, when he had sold only one highest-quality animal, **from his** own **assets.**

*Kodshim bekodshim min beshe'eino mino* — a more complex problem arises when two *kodshim* of different types are mixed together, such as an *olah* and a *shelamim*. Here the problem is not about ownership but about procedure: an *olah* is fully burned on the altar, while a *shelamim* is eaten; the blood applications differ as well. If one cannot tell which animal is which, one cannot bring either correctly. The problem is compounded when the mixture involves, say, an *olah* and a *chatat*, where the ritual requirements diverge even more sharply.

### Two Piles Method

The solution begins, as before, with *yir'u ad she'yista'avu* — let them graze until each develops a *mum*, after which the *kedushah* can be moved to money. But here there are two distinct categories of *korban* to be replaced, requiring two piles of money. The procedure is as follows: one declares that whichever animal is the *olah* should have its *kedushah* transferred to this pile of money — and since the *olah* might be the more expensive animal, one must put an amount equal to the highest value of any animal in the group. Similarly, one declares that whichever animal is the *shelamim* should have its *kedushah* transferred to a second pile of money — again at the highest value. The first pile is used to purchase a replacement *olah*; the second to purchase a replacement *shelamim*. Both can then be offered properly.

The inevitable consequence is that the original owner must absorb a financial loss: *veyafsid hamutar mibeito* — the surplus must come out of his own pocket. Since one does not know which was more expensive, and since one must assign the maximum value to each animal for the purpose of the transfer, one ends up paying out more in total than the animals were actually worth. If one animal was worth one hundred dollars and the other ninety, one must place one hundred in each pile — a total of two hundred dollars — to buy replacements that together cost only one hundred and ninety. The extra ten dollars is lost; that is the price of the uncertainty.

## Olah-Bechor Mix

### Bechor Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִתְעָרְבוּ בִּבְכוֹר וּבְמַעֲשֵׂר – יִרְעוּ עַד שֶׁיִּסְתָּאֲבוּ, וְיֵאָכְלוּ כִּבְכוֹר וּכְמַעֲשֵׂר.
>
> **Translation:** In a case where sacrificial animals **were intermingled with a firstborn** offering **or with** an animal **tithe** offering, **they shall graze until they become unfit** for sacrifice **and they shall** both **be eaten as a firstborn** offering **or as** an animal **tithe** offering.

The *mishna* now raises a more difficult scenario: *nitarvu bevochor uvima'aser* — an *olah* becomes mixed with a *bechor* (firstborn animal) or *ma'aser beheimah* (animal tithe). The problem is compounded here, because *bechor* and *ma'aser*, unlike ordinary *kodshim*, cannot be redeemed. They have an inherent *kedushah* that cannot be transferred to money. One cannot therefore apply the two-piles-of-money solution used above for *olah* and *shelamim*: one can redeem the *olah* side of the mixture, but there is no way to redeem the *bechor* or *ma'aser* side.

Nor can one simply bring them all: an *olah* is entirely burned, while a *bechor* and *ma'aser* are eaten; the blood of a *bechor* or *ma'aser* may be applied with one *matanah* at the base of the *mizbe'ach*, whereas an *olah* requires *matanah* on all sides. With no way to distinguish which animal is which, neither can be brought correctly.

### Eating as Bechor

The solution, once again, begins with *yir'u ad she'yista'avu* — let all the animals graze until they develop a *mum*. Once blemished, the *olah* can be redeemed: its *kedushah* transfers to money, which is used to purchase and bring a replacement *olah*. Now one is left with two blemished animals, one of which is *chullin* (the redeemed former *olah*) and one of which is *bechor* or *ma'aser* with a *mum*. A blemished *bechor* or *ma'aser* is permitted to be eaten without being brought to the *mizbe'ach*; and *chullin* may of course be eaten as well. So both animals can simply be eaten.

### Bechor Restrictions

The *mishna* specifies: *yei'achel kevochor ukhima'aser*. What does this mean in practice? The *halacha* governing *bechor* with a blemish is that it may not be sold in a marketplace like ordinary meat, nor slaughtered and displayed in a butcher stall. In normal cases of *kodshim* that have been redeemed, the *kedushah* is entirely removed and the animal may be sold at full market price in any setting. But *bechor* and *ma'aser* are never fully redeemed — their *kedushah*, though diminished once a *mum* appears, is never entirely stripped away — and therefore the usual considerations of obtaining the best market price do not apply in the same way. They are to be treated with a degree of reverence even in their permissible state.

Since one of the two animals being eaten is in fact a *bechor* or *ma'aser*, and since one cannot know which is which, both must be treated as *bechor* and *ma'aser* and eaten under those restrictions.

## Exceptions & Summary

### Chatat-Asham Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַכֹּל יְכוֹלִין לְהִתְעָרֵב, חוּץ מִן הַחַטָּאת וְהָאָשָׁם.
>
> **Translation:** **All** offerings **can become** indistinguishably **intermingled** with each other, **except for a sin offering and a guilt offering,** as the Gemara will explain.

The *mishna* concludes with a general principle: *hakol yecholim lehitaarev chutz min hachatat ve'asham* — all types of *korbanot* can in principle become mixed with one another and generate the types of problems described above, except for a *chatat* and an *asham*. The reason is simple: a *chatat* is always female, and an *asham* is always male. A mixture that requires one to ask "is this the *chatat* or the *asham*?" can never arise, since the two are externally distinguishable by sex. Accordingly, the *mishna* excludes this pairing from the entire framework of *ta'arovet* discussed in the *perek*.

## Gemara Clarification

### Mai Afilu

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאי ״אֲפִילּוּ״?
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches the *halakha* of all the offerings that were intermingled with animals from which deriving benefit is forbidden. This indicates that one offering became intermingled with a majority of prohibited animals, as it is usual to describe the smaller unit as being intermingled with the larger unit. The Gemara asks: If so, **what** is the meaning of the term: **Even,** in the clause: Even if the ratio is one in ten thousand, deriving benefit from them all is prohibited and they all must die. If the permitted animal is rendered prohibited by a simple majority, of course it is prohibited if the ratio is one in ten thousand.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכִי קָאָמַר: כׇּל הַזְּבָחִים שֶׁנִּתְעָרְבוּ בָּהֶן חַטָּאוֹת הַמֵּתוֹת אוֹ שׁוֹר הַנִּסְקָל, אֲפִילּוּ אֶחָד בְּרִיבּוֹא – יָמוּתוּ כּוּלָּן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **This** is what the mishna **is saying: All the offerings in which were intermingled sin offerings** left to **die, or** in which **an ox that** was sentenced to be **stoned** was intermingled, **even** if the ratio is **one** forbidden animal intermingled **with ten thousand** offerings, **they all must die.**

### Temurah Reference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תְּנֵינָא חֲדָא זִימְנָא: כָּל הָאֲסוּרִין לְגַבֵּי מִזְבֵּחַ אוֹסְרִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהֵן, הָרוֹבֵעַ וְהַנִּרְבָּע!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises a difficulty: Why is this mishna necessary? **We** already **learn** this *halakha* on **another occasion,** in a mishna (*Temura* 28a): With regard to **all** animals **whose** sacrifice **on the altar** is **prohibited,** if they are intermingled with animals whose sacrifice is permitted **they render** the entire mixture **prohibited in any amount,** regardless of the ratio of permitted to prohibited animals. The mishna adds that these are the animals whose sacrifice is prohibited: **An animal that** actively **copulated with a person, and an animal that was the object of bestiality.** That mishna proceeds to add other categories of animals to this list, including ones mentioned in the mishna here. In any event, the basic *halakha* of the mishna is also taught in tractate *Temura*.

### Rav Ashi Query

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: אַמְרִיתַהּ לִשְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ קַמֵּיהּ דְּרַב שִׁימִי, וְאַצְרִיכַן – דְּאִי מֵהָתָם, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְגָבוֹהַּ, אֲבָל לְהֶדְיוֹט אֵימָא לָא;
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi says: I said this *halakha* in the presence of Rav Shimi, and he** explained to **me** that both *mishnayot* **are necessary,** as each teaches a novelty not included in the other. Rav Ashi clarifies: **As, if** this *halakha* was learned only **from there,** the mishna in *Temura*, **I would say** that **this statement,** that prohibited animals render a mixture prohibited in any ratio, applies only to prohibiting the animals from being sacrificed **to the Most High; but** with regard to prohibiting the animals even **to an ordinary** person **[*hedyot*],** e.g., that if they became intermingled with an ox that is to be stoned they all must die without the possibility of redemption, one might **say** that they are **not** all rendered prohibited in benefit, as the prohibited animal is nullified in a majority. Therefore, the mishna here teaches that even with regard to deriving benefit, all the animals in the mixture are prohibited.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי מֵהָכָא, הֲוָה אָמֵינָא: הָנֵי הוּא אִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה נִינְהוּ, אֲבָל הָנֵי אֵימָא לָא; צְרִיכִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi continues: **And if** this *halakha* were learned only **from here I would say** that **it is** only **these** categories that are mentioned in this mishna, i.e., sin offerings that were condemned to die or an ox that was sentenced to be stoned, that render a mixture prohibited in any ratio. The *halakha* is stringent with regard to them, as **they are items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited. But** with regard to **these** categories mentioned in the mishna in *Temura*, e.g., an animal that copulated with a person, which are not items from which deriving benefit is prohibited, one might **say** that they are **not** disqualified from being sacrificed, and they are nullified in a majority. Therefore, both *mishnayot* **are necessary.**

## Both Cases Needed

### Kodshim Strictness

The lesson is clear: not only can the animals not be brought as *korbanot*, but even after the fact, they cannot be redeemed and benefited from by a layperson. The *shor haniskal*'s *issur hana'ah* infects the entire mixture such that there is no permissible avenue left — and this is something the Temurah case, which deals with animals that are merely *pasul* for the *mizbe'ach* but permissible in benefit, would not have taught us.

### C-Section Cases

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּלָאו אִיסּוּרֵי הֲנָאָה נִינְהוּ – הָא תְּנָא לֵיהּ! מִי קָתָנֵי בְּכַמָּה?! ״כׇּל שֶׁהֵן״ – הָתָם קָתָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara questions this explanation: This justifies the mishna here, but concerning the *halakha* stated in *Temura*, why did that mishna teach the *halakha* with regard to those animals **that are not items from which** deriving **benefit is prohibited,** such as an animal that copulated with a person; the *tanna* already **taught this** *halakha* in the mishna here. The Gemara answers: **Does** the mishna here **teach by what** ratio they render the mixture prohibited? The important measure: **In any amount, is taught there,** in *Temura*, not in the mishna here, and that is the novelty of the mishna in *Temura*.

One might then ask: is our *mishna* itself sufficient, and the Temurah case superfluous? The *gemara* argues no. Our *mishna* concerns *issurei hana'ah* — in the case of the *shor haniskal* and *chatat hameitah*, the forbidden animals are of the gravest halakhic weight. One could argue that specifically because of this severity — because the *issur* is so absolute — *bitul* fails to operate, and the result is that all must die. But perhaps if the disqualified animal mixed in were something lighter in *issur*, such as a *yotze dofen* — an animal that is merely *pasul* for the *mizbe'ach* but entirely permissible for layperson benefit — perhaps then *bitul* would apply and the mixture could be used for the *mizbe'ach* after all. *Aval hanei eima lo* — for those lesser cases, one might say, the mixture remains valid.

The Temurah case addresses precisely this: it involves animals like a *yotze dofen* — not *issurei hana'ah*, simply *pasul* for *korban* use — mixed into *chullin*, and even there, the entire mixture becomes forbidden for *mizbe'ach* use. The lesson is that even the lighter category of *pesul korban* without *issur hana'ah* is sufficient to contaminate a mixture and prevent its use on the *mizbe'ach*.

### Takanata Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְנִיתְנֵי הָא, וְלָא בָּעֵי הָא! תַּקַּנְתָּא אִיצְטְרִיכָא לֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges: **But** if so, **let** the *tanna* **teach that** mishna in *Temura*, **and** then he would **not require this** mishna here. Why teach the second clause of the mishna here? The Gemara explains: It **was necessary for** the *tanna* to mention **the remedy,** i.e., that the animals that are intermingled shall graze until they become unfit for sacrifice and then they shall be sold; and from the money received in the sale, the owner shall bring another offering of the monetary value of the highest-quality animal among them as the same type of offering that the sacrificial animal was. This *halakha* is not stated in the mishna in *Temura*.

At this point the *gemara* raises a further challenge. The second half of our *mishna* — the part dealing with *yotze dofen* and similar cases mixed with *kodshim* — covers essentially the same ground as the Temurah *mishna* (disqualified-but-permissible animals mixed with other things, forbidding *mizbe'ach* use). So why state it twice? Why not rely entirely on the Temurah case for that lesson, and limit our *mishna* to the *issurei hana'ah* cases?

The *gemara* answers: *takanata itztricha lei* — our *mishna* is needed for its remedial content. The Temurah *mishna* simply declares that the mixture is forbidden; it does not tell you what to do about it. Our *mishna*, by contrast, provides a complete framework for how to address the problem: let the animals graze until a *mum* develops, redeem the *kedushah* onto money, use that money to purchase replacement *korbanot*, and so forth. This practical guidance — the *takana* — is what our *mishna* uniquely contributes in the case of *yotze dofen* mixed with *kodshim*. The Temurah *mishna*, dealing with a mixture of disqualified animals with *chullin*, naturally does not prescribe these remedies, since the situation there (disqualified animals mixed into non-sacred stock) does not require the same restorative procedure. Both *mishnayot* are therefore indispensable: Temurah for its principle (*afilu kol shehu*), our *mishna* for its practical remedy.

## Issurei Hana'ah

### Ve'eilo Asurin

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּהֶדְיוֹט נָמֵי תְּנָא לֵיהּ – וְאֵלּוּ אֲסוּרִין וְאוֹסְרִין בְּכׇל שֶׁהֵן: יֵין נֶסֶךְ וַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara raises another difficulty. The *halakha* of the mishna that items from which deriving benefit is prohibited render a mixture prohibited in any amount even **to an ordinary** person, as stated with regard to the sin offerings that were condemned to die and an ox that was sentenced to be stoned, is **also taught** in a mishna (*Avoda Zara* 74a): **And these** following items are themselves **forbidden, and any amount** of them **renders** other items with which they become mixed **forbidden: Wine** used for **a libation** that became mixed with kosher wine, **and** objects of **idol worship** that were intermingled with permitted items.

The *gemara* now turns to a further question arising from the earlier discussion. It was said that our *mishna*'s case involving a *shor haniskal* — an *issur hana'ah* mixed into a flock of *korbanot* — implicitly teaches that *issurei hana'ah* are not *battel* even for a layperson, since there is no remedy available and all must die. But does our *mishna* need to be the source of this principle? Is it not already established elsewhere?

The *gemara* points to a *mishna* — *ve'eilu asurin ve'osrin bechol shehu* — "the following things are forbidden and render any mixture forbidden even in the smallest amount": *yein nesech* (wine of *avoda zara*) and an idol that was actually worshipped. These are paradigmatic *issurei hana'ah*, and the *mishna* there already establishes that *issurei hana'ah* are not subject to *bitul* even in infinitesimal proportions. The principle that *issurei hana'ah* cannot be nullified was thus already on the books.

### Preventing Bitul

The *gemara* therefore asks: if the principle that *issurei hana'ah* cannot be *battel* is already taught in the context of *avoda zara* and similar cases, why does our *mishna* need to teach it again through the *shor haniskal* case? Conversely, if our *mishna* teaches it, why does the case in the *avoda zara* context need to exist? The *gemara* answers: *tzrichi* — both are necessary, for the same structural reason that applied in the prior discussion. Each case operates in a distinct domain, and a ruling in one would not unambiguously extend to the other without being explicitly stated.

### Final Principle

The *gemara* works out the logic as follows. Had only the *avoda zara* case been stated — teaching that *issurei hana'ah* cannot be *battel* for a layperson — one might have reasoned that this applies specifically in the domain of ordinary use by laypeople, but not for *kodshim*. When it comes to *korbanot*, one might argue, the stakes are different: on one hand, we want to protect the sanctity of *kodshim* and surely should not allow a *shor haniskal* to potentially be offered — but on the other hand, perhaps we do not want to condemn an entire flock of valid *korbanot* to death when *bitul* might otherwise apply. So perhaps, in the domain of *kodshim*, *bitul* would operate even for *issurei hana'ah*. Our *mishna* therefore comes to teach: no, even there, *issurei hana'ah* are not *battel*, and all must die.

And had only our *mishna* been stated — teaching that *issurei hana'ah* are not *battel* in the context of *kodshim* — one might have attributed the ruling to the special stringency of *kodshim*: *kodshim dema'is*, sacred offerings that would be rendered repugnant by any possible admixture of something so deeply forbidden. But perhaps for a layperson, where no such concern about the integrity of sacred service applies, *bitul* might operate even for *issurei hana'ah*. For ordinary use, where there is no comparable revulsion, perhaps the majority would suffice to nullify even an *issur hana'ah*. The *mishna* in the context of *avoda zara* is therefore also needed, to establish that *issurei hana'ah* are not *battel* in any context, whether for laypersons or for *kodshim*.

The combined teaching of both sources yields a comprehensive principle: *issurei hana'ah* cannot be nullified through *bitul* — not in the domain of *kodshim* (where the concern is the integrity of sacred offerings), and not in the domain of *chullin* (where the concern is the absolute prohibition on layperson benefit). Each case is logically necessary; neither alone would have been sufficient. With this, the *gemara* sets the stage for the next discussion, which will examine more directly the question of why *bitul* fails to operate for live animals — a question to which we will turn tomorrow.
