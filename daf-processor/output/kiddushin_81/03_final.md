# Kiddushin 81 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Aninus & Yichud

### Tanna Kamma v. Abba Shaul

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מַתְנִיתִין דְּלָא כְּאַבָּא שָׁאוּל, דְּתַנְיָא: כׇּל שְׁלֹשִׁים יוֹם – יוֹצֵא בְּחֵיק, וְנִקְבָּר בְּאִשָּׁה אַחַת וּשְׁנֵי אֲנָשִׁים, אֲבָל לֹא בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד וּשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים. אַבָּא שָׁאוּל אוֹמֵר: אַף בְּאִישׁ אֶחָד וּשְׁתֵּי נָשִׁים.
>
> **Translation:** Shall **we say the mishna is not in accordance with** the opinion of **Abba Shaul, as it is taught** in a *baraita*: If a child dies **any** time during the first **thirty days** after his birth, he is not given a proper funeral but **is carried out** held **in** their **bosom,** not on a bier, **and buried by one woman and two men. But** he **may not** be buried **by one man and two women,** due to the prohibition against seclusion. **Abba Shaul says:** He may **even** be buried **by one man and two women.** This indicates that Abba Shaul deems it permitted for a man to be secluded with two women.

The daf opens with a question about whether the *mishnah*'s ruling on *yichud* is consistent with the position of Abba Shaul. The *mishnah* distinguishes between two men and one woman — which does not constitute *yichud* — and two women and one man — which does. The *gemara* asks: does this reflect a disagreement with Abba Shaul?

The source for the question is a *braisa*: *kol shloshim yom yotzei b'cheik v'nikbar b'isha achat u-shnei anashim* — an infant who dies within the first thirty days is taken out not on a bier but carried in one's arms, and the burial takes place with only one woman and two men. This *halacha* reflects a more restrained practice in the face of high infant mortality, keeping the mourning low-key. Abba Shaul adds: *af ish echad u-shtei nashim* — one man and two women would likewise be acceptable. On its face, this seems to conflict with the *tanna kamma* of the *mishnah*, who holds that one man and two women constitutes *yichud*.

### Tevir Yitzrei in Aninus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא אַבָּא שָׁאוּל, בִּשְׁעַת אֲנִינוּת תְּבִיר יִצְרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **Abba Shaul,** as he permits it only in the case of the *baraita*, because **at the time of acute mourning,** i.e., immediately after a close relative has died, one's **inclination** to sin **is broken,** and there is no concern that he might come to sin.

The *gemara* resolves the tension: *afilu teima Abba Shaul* — even Abba Shaul can agree with the general rule that one man and two women is problematic. In this specific case, however, *b'shaas aninus tevir yitzrei* — at the moment of *aninus*, the acute shock of bereavement before burial, a person's sexual desire is subdued. Since all three individuals accompanying the infant are presumably immediate family, the concern for *yetzer hara* is diminished, and the parameters of *yichud* may be understood differently.

### R. Yitzchak's Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי לַהּ כְּרַבִּי יִצְחָק. דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: ״מַה יִּתְאוֹנֵן אָדָם חָי גֶּבֶר עַל חֲטָאָיו״ – אֲפִילּוּ בִּשְׁעַת אֲנִינוּתוֹ שֶׁל אָדָם יִצְרוֹ מִתְגַּבֵּר עָלָיו. וְאַבָּא שָׁאוּל? כִּי כְּתִיב הָהוּא – בְּמִתְרַעֵם עַל מִדּוֹתָיו כְּתִיב, וְהָכִי קָאָמַר: מָה יִתְרַעֵם עַל מִדּוֹתָיו, וְכִי גָּבַר עַל חֲטָאָיו? דַּיּוֹ חַיִּים שֶׁנָּתַתִּי לוֹ!
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis,** who render seclusion forbidden even then, **hold in accordance with** the statement of **Rabbi Yitzḥak, as Rabbi Yitzḥak says** as follows with regard to the verse: **"Why does a living man complain, a powerful man due to his sins?"** (Lamentations 3:39): **Even at the time of a person's acute mourning, his inclination** to sin **overpowers him.** The Gemara asks: **And** how does **Abba Shaul** explain this verse? The Gemara answers: **When that was written, it was written** with regard **to one who complains about** God's **ways. And this is** what the verse **is saying: Why does** one **complain about** God's **ways** and claim that he has been treated unjustly? **Has he overpowered his sins?** God responds: **The life I have given him is sufficient for him,** and he deserves no more.

If that is so, why do the *rabbanan* maintain the ordinary laws of *yichud* even in a situation of *aninus*? The *gemara* answers: *rabbanan savri la k'Rebbi Yitzchak* — the *rabbanan* follow Rabbi Yitzchak's position.

Rabbi Yitzchak cites the verse *mah yitonen adam chai gever al chatav* — why should a living man bewail, why should a man complain over his sins? The *gemara* reads this as teaching that *afilu b'shaas aninuto, yitzro mitgaber alav* — even in one's darkest hour of tragedy, the *yetzer* is still active and must be fought. As long as a person is alive, *gever al chatav* — he must be a *gibbor* and master his desire for sin. The *rabbanan* therefore do not relax the laws of *yichud* even during *aninus*.

Abba Shaul, however, disputes this reading of the verse entirely. He contends: *mi ktiv hachi? me-chata'av midotav ktiv* — the verse does not say *me-chata'av* (his sins) but *midotav* (his attributes or lot). The meaning, on Abba Shaul's reading, is: *mah yitonen adam al midosav? vechi gavar al chata'av?* — How can a man complain about what befalls him in life? Has he ever truly conquered his sins? *Dayo chayim she-natati lo* — he should simply be grateful to be alive given how much he has sinned. The verse thus has nothing to do with mastery over lust in a moment of mourning.

### Woman at Funeral Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבָּנַן? כִּי הָהוּא מַעֲשֶׂה דְּהַהִיא אִיתְּתָא דַּהֲוָה עוֹבָדָא וְאַפֵּיקְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis** are concerned about the possibility of sin even in times of acute mourning, **like that incident involving a certain woman, as there was an incident where she removed** her husband from his grave. When visiting her husband's grave, she engaged in intercourse with a man who was tasked with guarding the body of one executed by the king. Meanwhile, that body was taken, and she suggested that they disinter her husband so that the guard could claim that he fulfilled his task properly. This demonstrates that even at a time of mourning one may succumb to temptation.

The *rabbanan* respond with an additional argument: *kahahu maaseh d'hahi iteta d'hava uvda v'afikteh* — there was an actual incident involving a woman in which they took her out. Rashi explains that this was a ruse: individuals wished to leave the city for a sexual encounter and staged a pretend infant's funeral as cover. Therefore, even if one were to concede that a genuine *aninus* context reduces sexual desire, the formal laws of *yichud* cannot be relaxed, because one can never be certain what people's true intentions are.

## Formal vs. Contextual

### Formalism Discussion

This discussion opens a broader and recurring question about *yichud*: to what degree does *halacha* respond to context, and to what degree does it apply formal rules uniformly? In many areas of *halacha*, the principle is *lo plug* — no distinctions are drawn based on individual circumstances. The classic example is the *gezeirah* against reading by candlelight on *Shabbat* lest one tilt the flame: we do not say "but I have a large-print book" or "I am particularly careful around fire." The rule applies across the board.

Yet the laws of *yichud* seem to operate differently. The original distinctions in the *mishnah* — two men versus two women, husband present versus absent — were themselves calibrated to the *gemara*'s understanding of when the *yetzer hara* is more or less operative. So the question becomes: when we encounter a particular scenario, do we look at that context and ask whether the underlying concern actually applies, or do we say the formal rule governs regardless?

### Elderly Woman Scenario

Consider a concrete case: a fifteen-year-old boy is alone in the house with a ninety-year-old woman. Is this *assur* because of *yichud*? One approach says yes — a man and a woman, end of story. Another approach looks at the contextual reality and concludes that no one is genuinely concerned about sexual activity taking place. Which approach is correct?

This becomes an ongoing question threading through the rest of the *daf*. It arises directly from the present discussion about whether *aninus* modifies the concern.

### Vulnerability & Temptation

One additional distinction is worth noting: some of the cases we are discussing involve the protection of a vulnerable party — a power differential, or the risk of exploitation — while others are about temptation between consenting adults. Both categories are addressed within the same formal framework of *yichud*, without explicit differentiation between them. That is itself a striking feature of these laws.

The argument for formalism is strengthened, paradoxically, by the very contextual reality of the *gemara*'s own story — *v'hahi uvda*, the incident where what appeared to be a funeral was actually a sexual liaison. If context itself can be fabricated, then perhaps that is precisely the reason to maintain formal rules regardless of apparent circumstances. The power of the *yetzer hara* is strong enough, and the capacity for self-deception and deception of others great enough, that clear, uniform rules are actually the contextually appropriate response. This is also why institutions — including Jewish ones — require unambiguous guidelines: not because everyone is assumed to be planning to sin, but because *halacha* recognizes that situations can develop in ways no one initially intended or desired.

## Conditions for Yichud

### Kasherim Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲבָל אִשָּׁה אַחַת. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בִּכְשֵׁרִים, אֲבָל בִּפְרוּצִים – אֲפִילּוּ בֵּי עַשְׂרָה נָמֵי לָא. הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה, וְהוֹצִיאוּהָ עֲשָׂרָה בַּמִּטָּה. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: תִּדַּע, דְּמִיחַבְּרִי בֵּי עַשְׂרָה וְגָנְבִי כְּשׁוּרָא וְלָא מִיכַּסְפִי מֵהֲדָדֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: **But one woman** may be secluded with two men. **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: They taught** this *halakha* **only** with regard **to** men of **fit** morals, **but with regard to** those **steeped** in sexual immorality, she may **not** be secluded **even with ten** men. **There was an incident where ten** men **carried out** a woman **on a bier,** as though she were dead, and engaged in intercourse with her. **Rav Yosef says: Know** that this is so, **since ten** people **will join together and steal a** heavy **beam without being ashamed before one another.** Similarly, several men can join together for a licentious act without shame.

The *gemara* turns to another limiting principle: *aval isha achat* — a single woman with two men. Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav: *lo shanu ela b'ksherim* — this rule that two men suffice applies only when those men are *kasherim*, men of upstanding moral character.

### Ten Men Story

*Aval b'pritzim* — if the men are of licentious character, *afilu b'asara nami lo* — even ten men would not be sufficient. The *gemara* cites an incident — *hahu ma'aseh v'hotziu asara b'mita* — in which ten men took a woman out on a bed. Rashi explains: *bechezkas shemeisa, d'kilkilu ima v'ishes ish haita* — they proceeded under the pretense that she had died, and she was in fact a married woman. Like the earlier story of the staged infant funeral, this was a scheme to leave the city under cover of a pretended burial. Whether the woman was a willing participant is a serious question, but in any case, when individuals are *prutzim*, the presence of additional men provides no deterrent.

### Collective Complicity

Rav Yosef explains why: *de-mechabrei bei asara ve-gandei kishura ve-lo michsafei mihadei* — when ten people are together in something, they have no shame before one another. He compares it to ten people stealing a beam together: each person needs all the others to carry it, and collective involvement eliminates the inhibition that comes from being observed. When everyone is equally implicated, no one serves as a check on anyone else.

### Sotah Escorts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: מוֹסְרִין לוֹ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים, שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ. תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים – אֵין, אִינָשֵׁי דְּעָלְמָא – לָא? שָׁאנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים דְּיָדְעִי לְאַתְרוֹיֵי בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: Shall **we say** the following mishna (*Sota* 7a) **supports him:** It was taught with regard to one who is bringing his wife, whom he suspects of having committed adultery [*sota*], to the Temple to perform the ritual of the bitter water, that **they provide him with two Torah scholars** to accompany them **lest he engage in sexual intercourse with her along the way,** as until the ritual has been performed she remains forbidden to her husband? It can be inferred from here: Two **Torah scholars, yes;** their presence will assure that no one will engage in forbidden intercourse. **Regular men, no;** there is still a concern that they may engage in intercourse. This indicates that ordinary people are not relied upon with regard to seclusion with a woman. The Gemara rejects this proof: The reason there is a need for them to be Torah scholars is that **Torah scholars are different, in that they know how** **to warn him** not to engage in intercourse with her, since that would neutralize the effectiveness of the examination of the waters.

The *gemara* attempts to bring a proof for Rav Yehuda's distinction from the case of the *sotah*. When a husband suspected his wife of adultery and she was to be brought before the *beit din* in Jerusalem, two Torah scholars were assigned to accompany them on the journey: *shema yavo aleha baderech* — lest the husband have relations with her on the way, which would nullify the effect of the *sotah* waters.

The *gemara* asks: why specifically Torah scholars? Ordinary people could equally prevent *yichud*. The answer is: *shani talmidei chachamim deyadei le'as-hudei beih* — Torah scholars are needed not simply to prevent *yichud* but to be able to warn the husband, and to testify if he violated the prohibition. The two are required to serve as witnesses; they are not required to be Torah scholars merely to prevent seclusion.

### City vs. Journey

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בָּעִיר, אֲבָל בַּדֶּרֶךְ – עַד שֶׁיִּהְיוּ שְׁלֹשָׁה, שֶׁמָּא יִצְטָרֵךְ אֶחָד מֵהֶם לְהַשְׁתִּין, וְנִמְצָא אֶחָד מִתְיַיחֵד עִם הָעֶרְוָה. נֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ: מוֹסְרִים לוֹ שְׁנֵי תַּלְמִידֵי חֲכָמִים שֶׁמָּא יָבֹא עָלֶיהָ בַּדֶּרֶךְ. תְּרֵי וְאִיהוּ, הָא תְּלָתָא! הָתָם כִּי הֵיכִי דְּנִיהְווֹ עֲלֵיהּ סָהֲדִי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** The Sages **taught** that two men may seclude themselves with one woman **only in a city,** where others are present, **but on the road** it is not permitted **unless there are three.** Why are two men insufficient on the road? **Perhaps one of them will need to urinate** and will walk away, **and it will turn out that one** person **will be secluded with a woman forbidden to him.** The Gemara suggests: Shall **we say** the mishna quoted above **supports him: They provide him with two Torah scholars** to accompany them **lest he engage in sexual intercourse with her along the way? He and two** Torah scholars **are three,** which indicates that there is a requirement for three men when they are traveling. The Gemara answers: That is no proof, as **there,** in the case of the *sota*, there is a requirement for an additional two men **in order that they should** serve as **witnesses about him,** to testify whether or not he engaged in intercourse with her along the way.

A further distinction: Rav Yehuda said in the name of Rav, *lo shanu ela b'ir* — the rule that two men suffice applies only in a city. *Aval baderech, ad d'ika tlata* — on a journey, three men are required. The reason: *shema yitzaterech echad mehem lehastin venimtza echad mityached im ha'erva* — one of the two men may need to step off the road, and the remaining man and woman would be left alone together.

The *gemara* again examines the *sotah* case as a potential proof. Two scholars accompany the couple on the road to Jerusalem — *trei v'ihu ha tlata*, two plus the husband makes three. Why three? The answer is that the two scholars are needed as witnesses, not to prevent *yichud*; one additional man would have sufficed for that purpose alone. The two and their status as Torah scholars serve independent functions unrelated to the general rule about journeys.

## Yetzer Hara Stories

### Rav Flees Woman

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וְרַב יְהוּדָה הֲווֹ קָאָזְלִי בְּאוֹרְחָא, הֲוָה קָאָזְלָא הַהִיא אִתְּתָא קַמַּיְיהוּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב לְרַב יְהוּדָה: דַּל כַּרְעָיךְ מִקַּמֵּי גֵיהִנָּם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְהָא מָר הוּא דְּאָמַר בִּכְשֵׁרִים שַׁפִּיר דָּמֵי! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִי יֵימַר דְּבִכְשֵׁרִים כְּגוֹן אֲנָא וְאַתְּ? אֶלָּא כְּגוֹן מַאי? כְּגוֹן רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פַּפֵּי וַחֲבֵירָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rav and Rav Yehuda were walking along the way,** and **a certain woman was walking ahead of them. Rav said to Rav Yehuda: Raise your feet** and walk quickly **away from Gehenna** so that we do not remain secluded with her. Rav Yehuda **said to him: But wasn't it** you, **Master, who said that** it is **permitted** in the case **of** men of **fit** morals? Rav **said to him: Who says** that I referred **to** men of **fit** morals **such as you and me?** Rav Yehuda responded: **Rather, such as whom?** Rav answered: **Such as Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappi and his colleagues,** who have proven that they can withstand temptation (see 39b). All other people are not trusted in this matter.

The *gemara* now turns to a series of narratives that illustrate the power of the *yetzer hara*. Rava and Rav Yehuda were walking together on the road when a woman walked ahead of them. Rava said to Rav Yehuda: *dal karecha mikamei Gehenom* — lift your legs, hurry, get in front of her, so as not to be walking in her proximity, lest they be tempted and fall into *gehennom*. This passage clearly frames the concern from the male perspective: the woman as the object of temptation, the *yetzer hara* as a male problem.

Rav Yehuda responded with a challenge: *uvmai d'amart biksherim shapir dami* — according to your own ruling that two *kasherim* men may be with one woman without concern, we are two *kasherim* men and there is one woman; why are you worried? Rava replied: *mi yeimar d'biksherim kegon ana ve-at* — who says that men like you and me qualify as *kasherim* in the relevant sense?

The *gemara* asks: well, what standard of *kasherim* would suffice? *K'gon Rabbi Chanina bar Pappi v'chaveraiv* — men like Rabbi Chanina bar Pappi and his peers, who were great *chasidim*. Ordinary people, even distinguished *amoraim* like Rava and Rav Yehuda, cannot assume they meet that standard.

This is a striking passage. On one reading it sounds like an extra *chumra* beyond the letter of the law. Yet the Rambam takes it seriously as *halacha*, ruling that since no one today can be considered a *kasher* in the relevant sense, the normal rule permitting two men with one woman no longer applies without qualification — and in a generation where no one is fully *kasher*, even larger numbers may not suffice for *prutzim*. In practice, however, we do rule that we are considered *kasherim* and the ordinary rules apply.

This raises a further contemporary question: how does one define *parutz* today? Someone who has casual one-night stands is clearly *parutz*. But what of someone who is not observant but has intimate relations only within committed long-term relationships? Does that person count for purposes of these rules? The *gemara* does not give a precise definition, and the question remains genuinely open.

## Enforcement of Yichud

### Lashes without Assumption

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מַלְקִין עַל יִיחוּד, וְאֵין אוֹסְרִין עַל הַיִּיחוּד. אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: לָא אֲמַרַן אֶלָּא בְּיִיחוּד פְּנוּיָה, אֲבָל בְּיִיחוּד דְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ – לָא, שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא מוֹצִיא לַעַז עַל בָּנֶיהָ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** The court **flogs** a man **due to** his being **secluded** with a woman. But a wife **is not thereby rendered forbidden** to her husband, and an unmarried woman is not prohibited from marrying a priest **due to** being **secluded,** as it cannot be stated definitively that the secluded pair engaged in sexual intercourse. **Rav Ashi says: We stated** the *halakha* that one is flogged **due to** being **secluded only** with **an unmarried** woman, **but for** being **secluded** with **a married woman,** one is **not** flogged. Why not? It is **so that there should not be rumors spread concerning her children.** If the secluded pair is flogged, everyone will assume that they engaged in intercourse, and people will consider her children to be *mamzerim*, whereas in fact they were flogged only for being secluded.

Rav rules: *malkin al ha-yichud v'ein osrin al ha-yichud* — one who violates *yichud* receives lashes, but the woman is not thereby rendered forbidden. Even if the woman is married, we do not presume that intercourse occurred. The transgression of *yichud* is enforced — publicly and seriously — but the enormous legal consequences of presuming adultery (which would require the woman to leave her husband) are not triggered. Tosafot adds that even in the case of an unmarried woman, we do not presume intercourse occurred in a way that would render her forbidden to a *kohen*. The lashes here are rabbinic rather than biblical in character, consistent with Rashi's reading that the prohibition of *yichud* is derived by way of *asmachta*.

Rav Ashi qualifies: *lo amrinan ela be-yichud penuyah* — lashes are given only when the seclusion involved an unmarried woman. *Aval be-yichud eshet ish, lo* — if the woman is married, we do not give lashes at all. The reason: *she-lo tehei motzi la'az al baneha* — even if we intend only to punish the transgression of *yichud* and not to impute adultery, the public will draw its own conclusions. Once people hear that a man and a married woman received lashes for *yichud*, questions will inevitably arise about whether her children are *mamzerim*. To prevent that inference from taking root, we do not give lashes in such cases at all.

### Mar Zutra's Method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מָר זוּטְרָא מַלְקֵי וּמַכְרֵיז. אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב נַחְמָן מִפַּרְהַטְיָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: מָר נָמֵי לַילְקֵי וְלַכְרֵיז! אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אִיכָּא דְּשָׁמַע בְּהָא וְלָא שָׁמַע בְּהָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Mar Zutra would** even **flog** one who was secluded with a married woman, **and he would proclaim** the reason for the punishment. **Rav Naḥman from Parhatya said to Rav Ashi: Let the Master also flog and proclaim** the reason. Rav Ashi **said to him:** I am hesitant to do so, in case **there are those who hear about this,** i.e., the flogging, **and do not hear about that,** i.e., the reason for the flogging.

Mar Zutra proposed a solution: give lashes *and* make a public announcement — *malkei u-makhriz* — clarifying that no inference of adultery should be drawn and that her children's status is unaffected. Rav Nachman of Parhatya suggested this approach to Rav Ashi. Rav Ashi rejected it: *ika de-shama be-ha ve-lo shama be-ha* — some people will hear about the lashes and miss the clarifying announcement. Moreover, an announcement that "we know she did not commit adultery" is not something one can actually assert with authority; one can only say "draw no conclusions" — and that is unlikely to prevent people from doing exactly that.

### Bad Reputation Lashes

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: מַלְקִין עַל לֹא טוֹבָה הַשְּׁמוּעָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַל בָּנָי כִּי לוֹא טוֹבָה הַשְּׁמֻעָה״. מָר זוּטְרָא מוֹתֵיב לֵהּ אַפְסֵירָה עַל כַּתְפֵּיהּ וּמַקְרֵי לֵיהּ: ״אַל בָּנָי״
>
> **Translation:** **Rav says:** The court **flogs** one **due to** his being the subject of **a bad rumor,** meaning that not only is a person flogged when the facts have been ascertained, but even when he has only reportedly committed transgressions he may be flogged. **As it is stated: "No, my sons, for it is no good report"** (I Samuel 2:24). When **Mar Zutra** would flog a person for being the subject of a bad rumor, **he would place** the **bridle** of a donkey **on** the person's **shoulders and recite before him** when administering lashes: **"No, my sons,** for it is no good report," so that people would know why he was being flogged.

A further ruling from Rav: *malkin al lo tovah ha-shmu'ah* — lashes are given not only for a proven *yichud* violation but also when someone has a bad reputation regarding sexual matters, even without direct evidence of seclusion. The proof-text is *al banai ki lo tovah ha-shmu'ah* — "do not, my son, for the report is not good." The language *al* carries the sense of prohibition: one is forbidden to conduct oneself in a manner that generates a bad reputation. If one does, even absent substantive evidence, one has transgressed something. Mar Zutra would place a yoke on the neck of such a person — a form of public shaming — and announce this verse over him, apparently as an alternative to lashes proper.

## Major Exceptions

### Husband in City

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבָּה: בַּעְלָהּ בָּעִיר – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד. אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: פֶּתַח פָּתוּחַ לִרְשׁוּת הָרַבִּים – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד. רַב בִּיבִי אִיקְּלַע לְבֵי רַב יוֹסֵף. בָּתַר דִּכְרַךְ רִיפְתָּא אֲמַר לְהוּ: שְׁקוּלוּ דַּרְגָּא מִתּוּתֵי בִּיבִי. וְהָא אָמַר רַבָּה: בַּעְלָהּ בָּעִיר – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד! שָׁאנֵי רַב בִּיבִי דְּשׁוֹשְׁבִינְתֵּיהּ הֲוַאי וְגָיְיסָא בֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba says:** If a woman's **husband is in town, there is no concern due to** her being **secluded** with a man. People are afraid to sin with her, since they cannot be sure when her husband will return. **Rav Yosef says:** If there is **an open entrance to the public domain there is no concern due to** being **secluded.** The Gemara relates: **Rav Beivai arrived at Rav Yosef's house. After he wrapped** his **bread,** i.e., ate his meal, he **said to** the members of the household: **Remove the ladder from beneath Beivai,** who was going to sleep in the upper story, so that he not be able to climb down, due to the prohibition of being secluded with Rav Yosef's wife. The Gemara asks: **But doesn't Rabba say:** If **her husband is in town, there is no concern due to** her being **secluded** with a man? The Gemara answers: **Rav Beivai is different, since** Rav Yosef's wife **was his friend and she was familiar with him,** and therefore there was more cause for concern.

Rava rules: *ba'alah ba-ir ein chosheshin mishum yichud* — if the woman's husband is in the city, there is no concern of *yichud*, because she is always apprehensive that he might appear unexpectedly. This is notably asymmetric: in the *mishnah*, the equivalent protection for a man is only operative if his wife is physically present with him — *be-zman she-ishto imo*. The asymmetry reflects the different power dynamics of the rabbinic household: a wife is more fearful of her husband's surprise appearance than a husband is of his wife's.

Several nuances arise within this exception. If the husband is in the city but does not know where his wife is, presumably the fear of his arrival does not operate — she cannot be said to be deterred by a husband who has no idea of her whereabouts. Conversely, if the husband knows where she is — say, working late at a professional setting — but it is the kind of location he would never plausibly drop by at that hour, is that still *ba'ala ba-ir*? These are live *halachic* questions.

### Petach Patuach

Rav Yosef rules: *petach patuach l'reshut ha-rabim ein chosheshin mishum yichud* — an open doorway to the public thoroughfare eliminates the concern of *yichud*, because passersby may look in or enter at any time. Questions arise about the precise parameters: must the door actually be open, or does unlocked suffice? Would a window through which the interior is visible serve the same function even if the door is closed? What constitutes sufficient openness to the public? These questions have significant practical implications.

### Ladder Removal Story

Rav Bibi came to the house of Rav Yosef. After they ate together upstairs, Rav Yosef descended and instructed his household to remove the step-ladder from beneath Rav Bibi — *shkulu darga mitutei d'Bibi* — so that Rav Bibi could not come down to where Rav Yosef's wife was. The *gemara* asks: but Rav Yosef was in the city; does not the rule of *ba'ala ba-ir* apply? The *gemara* answers: *shani Rav Bibi d'shushvinisei havai* — Rav Bibi was different because he was Rav Yosef's *shushvin*, his very close intimate companion — and indeed Rashi specifies that she was closely bonded with him as well.

### Shushvinin Closeness

The principle emerging here is that a *shushvin* — an intimate friend, associated particularly with the groom's circle — may feel sufficiently comfortable to allow himself liberties he would not otherwise take. Not sinister or calculated liberties, but the relaxation of the ordinary social distance that normally deters indiscretion. One sits closer, remains longer, interacts more freely — and one thing, the *gemara* implies, can lead to another. Additionally, the very closeness of the friendship may blunt the deterrent effect of *ba'ala ba-ir*: if she is so comfortable with the guest, she may be less vigilant about her husband's potential arrival. This explains Rav Yosef's precaution, even though his presence in the city would ordinarily be sufficient.

### Inner/Outer Rooms

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא: אֲנָשִׁים מִבַּחוּץ וְנָשִׁים מִבִּפְנִים – אֵין חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד. אֲנָשִׁים מִבִּפְנִים וְנָשִׁים מִבַּחוּץ – חוֹשְׁשִׁין מִשּׁוּם יִיחוּד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana says:** If the **men** are located **on the outside,** i.e., in the outer room, **and the women in the inside,** i.e., in the inner room, **there is no concern due to** being **secluded.** Even if one of the men were to enter the inner room, he would be seen by the other men. By contrast, if there were **men in the inside and women on the outside, there is a concern due to** being **secluded,** since one of the men can claim that he is leaving and in fact join the women.

Rav Kahana rules on a case of two rooms — an inner room and an outer room — with men and women situated in separate spaces. The ruling has two parts: *anashim mibachutz v'nashim mibifnim ein chosheshin mishum yichud* — men outside and women inside, no concern; *anashim mibifnim v'nashim mibachutz chosheshin mishum yichud* — men inside and women outside, there is concern.

Rashi explains the reasoning. When men are outside and women are inside, the worry would be that a man might enter the inner room where the women are. But his companions outside would notice and question why he would go into a room where he has no business. The social pressure prevents it. Furthermore, even if a woman were to come out to the outer room where the men are, it would not create *yichud* because one woman with multiple men is not *yichud*. The reverse arrangement is more dangerous: a man might slip into the inner room on the pretext of needing to go out, and no one in the outer room would notice or have reason to stop him, because he would not be going into a space clearly designated for the other sex.

### Baraita Disagreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַתְנִיתָא תָּנָא אִיפְּכָא. אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא הָכִי וְתָנָא מַתְנִיתָא אִיפְּכָא, אֲנָא נַעֲבֵיד לְחוּמְרָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **The opposite was taught in a *baraita*,** that if the men are on the outside and the women are inside there is concern due to being secluded, as one of the men might venture inside without being noticed, but if the men are inside and the women are outside, he knows that one of the other men might go out through the women's quarters at any time, and therefore there is no concern due to being secluded. **Abaye said: Now that Rav Kahana has said** the *halakha* in **this** manner **and a *baraita* teaches the opposite, we shall act stringently** in both cases.

The *braisa*, however, has the concern reversed. The *braisa* reasons that when men are inside, a man who goes into the outer room with women will still be deterred, because he knows someone else may exit the inner room at any moment. But when men are outside, a man who slips into the inner room is not afraid of being observed — no one in the outer room has business entering the inner room, so no one will come and discover him.

Abaye concludes: *anan na'avid le-chumra* — since Rav Kahana and the *braisa* disagree about which direction poses the greater risk, we treat both as problematic.

### Rashi vs. Rambam

This ruling marks a significant fork in interpretation. Rashi's reading, as explained above, understands the concern as a *gezeirah* — we prohibit a present situation because it might develop into *yichud*. Right now, with men in one room and women in the other, there is no *yichud*; the question is whether we forbid the arrangement because it could lead to *yichud*. According to this approach, certain configurations must be avoided even when *yichud* does not yet exist.

The Rambam and the Shulchan Aruch read the case differently. For them, the question is not whether a currently permissible situation might become *yichud*, but whether the situation as it actually stands — once someone has entered the other room — constitutes *yichud* or not. The presence of others who might cross between rooms may function as something akin to *ba'ala ba-ir*, creating a deterrent that means even his actual presence with the women does not rise to *yichud*. The practical difference between the two readings is substantial: according to Rashi, we may forbid arrangements that are not currently *yichud* on the grounds they might become so; according to the Rambam and Shulchan Aruch, only an actually existing *yichud* situation triggers the prohibition.

### Sister Sleepover Example

This disagreement has direct practical application. Consider the common case: a sister needs a place to stay for the night and will sleep in her brother's room while his roommates occupy the other rooms. Is that permitted? According to the Rambam and Shulchan Aruch, as long as everyone retires to their own rooms before going to sleep — no *yichud* exists at that moment — there is no prohibition, even though one could imagine a scenario in which someone wanders the apartment in the middle of the night and an encounter occurs. The Tzitz Eliezer supports this position. Many other *poskim*, however, invoking something like Rashi's approach, hold that one may not permit a situation so easily capable of becoming *yichud*. The question turns precisely on whether Rashi's *gezeirah* framework or the Rambam's current-state analysis governs.

## Gender Separation (III)

### Abaye's Pots

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אַבָּיֵי דָּיַיר גּוּלְפֵי. רָבָא דָּיַיר קְנֵה. אָמַר אָבִין: סְקָבָא דְשַׁתָּא רִיגְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye** would **arrange rows** of **pitchers** between the men and women, so that they would not be able to cross from one area to the other without making noise. **Rava** would **arrange rows** of **reeds** to prevent passage. **Avin said: The wound [*sakva*],** i.e., the vulnerable point, **of the year** is **the Festival,** since men and women would come together on these joyous occasions, and this would lead to sin.

Abaye, when men and women gathered together — for a *derasha* or a *chupah*, as Rashi explains the context — would arrange pots between the two groups to maintain separation, so that anyone attempting to cross from one side to the other would trip over them and make noise. The concern was that individual men or women might slip over to the other side, potentially creating situations of *yichud*.

## Rav Amram Story (III)

### Captive Women Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנָךְ שְׁבוּיָיתָא דַּאֲתַאי לִנְהַרְדְּעָא. אַסְּקִינְהוּ לְבֵי רַב עַמְרָם חֲסִידָא, אַשְׁקוּלוּ דַּרְגָּא מִקַּמַּיְיהוּ. בַּהֲדֵי דְּקָא חָלְפָה חֲדָא מִנַּיְיהוּ נְפַל נְהוֹרָא בְּאִיפּוּמָּא. שַׁקְלֵיהּ רַב עַמְרָם לְדַרְגָּא דְּלָא הֲווֹ יָכְלִין בֵּי עַשְׂרָה לְמִדְלְיֵיהּ, דַּלְיֵיהּ לְחוֹדֵיהּ, סָלֵיק וְאָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara relates: **Those captive** women **who were brought to Neharde'a,** where they were redeemed, **were brought up to the house of Rav Amram the Pious. They removed the ladder from before them** to prevent men from climbing up after them to the attic where they were to sleep. **When one of them passed** by the entrance to the upper chamber, it was as though **a light shone in the aperture** due to her great beauty. Out of his desire for her, **Rav Amram grabbed a ladder that ten** men **together could not lift, lifted it on his own** and **began climbing.**

A group of captive women who had been redeemed were brought to Nehardea and placed in the house of Rav Amram Chasida for safekeeping until they could return to their homes. They were housed in the attic. The step-ladder was removed — *ashkulu darga mikamaya* — so they would be protected in their own space and no one could ascend to be with them.

## Rav Amram Story (III) (Continued)

### Amram's Temptation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי מְטָא לְפַלְגָא [דְּ]דַרְגָּא אִיפְּשַׁח, רְמָא קָלָא: נוּרָא בֵּי עַמְרָם! אֲתוֹ רַבָּנַן, אֲמַרוּ לֵיהּ: כַּסֵּיפְתִּינַן. אֲמַר לְהוּ: מוּטָב תִּיכַּסְפוּ בֵּי עַמְרָם בְּעָלְמָא הָדֵין וְלָא תִּיכַּסְפוּ מִינֵּיהּ לְעָלְמָא דְּאָתֵי. אַשְׁבְּעֵיהּ דְּיִנְפַּק מִינֵּיהּ, נְפַק מִינֵּיהּ כִּי עַמּוּדָא דְנוּרָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חֲזִי, דְּאַתְּ נוּרָא וַאֲנָא בִּישְׂרָא, וַאֲנָא עֲדִיפְנָא מִינָּךְ.
>
> **Translation:** **When he was halfway** up **the ladder, he strengthened** his legs against the sides of the ladder to stop himself from climbing further, **raised his voice,** and cried out: There is **a fire in the house of Amram.** Upon hearing this, **the Sages came** and found him in that position. **They said to him: You have embarrassed us,** since everyone sees what you had intended to do. Rav Amram **said to them: It is better that you be shamed in Amram's house in this world, and not be ashamed of him in the World-to-Come. He took an oath that** his evil inclination **should emerge from him, and** an apparition **similar to a pillar of fire emerged from him. He said to** his evil inclination: **See, as you are fire and I am** mere **flesh, and** yet, **I am** still **superior to you,** as I was able to overcome you.

As one of the captive women moved across the upper story, sunlight shone through the skylight and illuminated her, and Rav Amram was overcome with desire. He then seized the step-ladder — *d'la hayu yachlin be'asara l'midalya*, so heavy that ten men could not have moved it — and moved it by himself alone, the way Yaakov Avinu moved the stone. He climbed the steps toward the woman's quarters. When he reached the halfway point — *ki mata l'palga d'darga* — he stopped himself, held himself firm, and cried out: *nura be'Amram* — fire in Amram's house! The *talmidei chachamim* came running, and when they saw there was no fire, they understood what had transpired and told him he had shamed himself. He replied: *mutav techsefnu be'Amram be'alma hadein, v'lo techsefnu minei b'alma d'atei* — better that Amram be shamed in this world than that you be ashamed of him in the world to come.

He then imposed an oath on his *yetzer hara* that it should depart from him. It left him *ke'amuda denura* — like a pillar of fire. He addressed it: *chazi de'at nura ve'ana bisra, ve'ana adifa minach* — you are fire and I am flesh, yet I am greater than you.

This story echoes the notion of *gera b'chata'av* — mastery over one's sins. It simultaneously demonstrates the terrifying power of the *yetzer hara* — capable of enabling a man to move what ten men cannot — and the possibility of human mastery over it. The narrative is not primarily a *halachic* ruling about separate rooms; it is an *aggadic* illustration of why these laws exist and what they are guarding against.

## Gender Separation (III) (Regalim Concern)

### Regalim Concern

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אָבִין: סְקָבָא דְשַׁתָּא רִיגְלָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Avin said: The wound [*sakva*],** i.e., the vulnerable point, **of the year** is **the Festival,** since men and women would come together on these joyous occasions, and this would lead to sin.

Avin teaches: *saqva d'shata rigla* — the weakness of the year is the *regalim*, the pilgrimage festivals. That is the time when large gatherings of men and women come together to hear *derashot* and celebrate, and when the risk of individuals slipping away together into *yichud* is greatest.

## Rav Amram Story (III) (Fire Alarm & Mastery)

### Fire Alarm Strategy

The device of crying fire — *nura be'Amram* — served to transform private temptation into a public crisis, trading the possibility of future shame in the world to come for immediate earthly embarrassment. The choice was deliberate: public shame now, not hidden sin later.

### Amram's Mastery

Rav Amram's declaration — *chazi de'at nura ve'ana bisra, ve'ana adifa minach* — you are flame, I am flesh, yet I am greater — is the culminating statement of the story. It recalls the earlier framing of *gera b'chata'av*, the idea that a human being can exercise genuine mastery over the *yetzer hara*, even when that *yetzer* burns with the intensity of fire.

## Sages vs. Yetzer

### Rav Meir Mocks

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי מֵאִיר הֲוָה מִתְלוֹצֵץ בְּעוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה. יוֹמָא חַד אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ שָׂטָן כְּאִיתְּתָא בְּהָךְ גִּיסָא דְנַהֲרָא, לָא הֲוָה מַבָּרָא – נְקַט מִצְרָא וְקָא עָבַר. כִּי מְטָא פַּלְגָא מִצְרָא שַׁבְקֵיהּ, אָמַר: אִי לָאו דְּקָא מַכְרְזִי בִּרְקִיעָא ״הִזָּהֲרוּ בְּרַבִּי מֵאִיר וְתוֹרָתוֹ״ שַׁוֵּיתֵיהּ לִדְמָךְ תַּרְתֵּי מָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Meir would ridicule transgressors** by saying it is easy to avoid temptation. **One day, Satan appeared to him as a woman** standing **on the other side of the river.** Since **there was no ferry** to cross the river, **he took hold of a rope bridge and crossed** the river. **When he reached halfway** across **the rope bridge,** the evil inclination **left him and said** to him: **Were it not for** the fact that **they proclaim about you in heaven: Be careful with regard to Rabbi Meir and his Torah, I would have made your blood like two *ma'a*,** i.e., completely worthless, since you would have fallen completely from your spiritual level.

Rav Meir used to mock those who gave in to sexual sin — *hava mitlotzetz be'ovrei aveira*. One day the *satan* — whom Rashi identifies with the *yetzer hara* — appeared to him as a woman on the far bank of a river. There was no boat crossing available. Rav Meir grabbed a rope spanning the river and shimmied across. When he reached the midpoint, the *yetzer hara* released him and said: had they not announced in heaven "Take care of Rav Meir and his Torah," I would have valued your life at two *ma'ei* — you were nearly mine. For all your mocking of sinners, here you were, halfway across a river.

### Plimo's Story

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** פְּלֵימוֹ הֲוָה רְגִיל לְמֵימַר כׇּל יוֹמָא ״גִּירָא בְּעֵינֵיהּ דְּשָׂטָן״. יוֹמָא חַד מַעֲלֵי יוֹמָא דְכִיפּוּרֵי הֲוָה. אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ כְּעַנְיָא. אֲתָא קְרָא אַבָּבָא, אַפִּיקוּ לֵיהּ רִיפְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יוֹמָא כִּי הָאִידָּנָא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא גַּוַּאי וַאֲנָא אַבָּרַאי? עַיְּילֵיהּ וְקָרִיבוּ לֵיהּ רִיפְתָּא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: יוֹמָא כִּי הָאִידָּנָא כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא אַתַּכָּא וַאֲנָא לְחוֹדַאי? אַתְיוּהּ אוֹתְבוּהּ אַתַּכָּא. הֲוָה יָתֵיב מְלָא נַפְשֵׁיהּ שִׁיחְנָא וְכִיבֵי (עֲלֵיהּ) וַהֲוָה קָעָבֵיד בֵּיהּ מִילֵּי דִּמְאִיס. אֲמַר לֵיהּ:
>
> **Translation:** The Sage **Peleimu had the habit to say every day: An arrow in the eye of Satan,** mocking the temptations of the evil inclination. **One day, it was the eve of Yom Kippur,** and Satan **appeared to him as a pauper** who **came and called** him **to the door,** requesting alms. Peleimu **brought out bread to him.** Satan **said to him: On a day like today, everyone is inside** eating, **and** shall **I** stand **outside** and eat? Peleimu **brought him inside and gave him bread. He said to him: On a day like today, everyone** is sitting **at the table, and** shall **I** sit **by myself? They brought him and sat him at the table. He was sitting** and had **covered himself with boils and pus, and he was doing repulsive things** at the table. Peleimu **said to** the pauper:

Plimo would declare each day: *gira b'einei d'Satan* — arrows into the eyes of the *satan*. One *Erev Yom Kippur*, the *yetzer hara* appeared to him as a poor man calling at the door. Plimo brought him bread. The poor man complained that everyone else was inside at the *seudah mafseket* while he was left outside. Plimo brought him inside. The man complained he was eating apart from the family. Plimo brought him to the table. There the guest sat — covered in boils and sores, behaving repulsively, popping his blisters, spitting phlegm into his cup. When Plimo rebuked him, the guest acted as though he had died. People cried out: *Plimo katal gavra* — Plimo has killed a man! Plimo fled and hid in the outhouse outside the city.

### Meir's River Rope

The story of Rav Meir and the river rope belongs to the same pattern as Rav Amram's story: someone who mocked sinners was himself nearly brought down by the very temptation he scorned. The *yetzer hara*'s message is consistent — those who think themselves immune are the most vulnerable.

### Akiva's Palm Tree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא הֲוָה מִתְלוֹצֵץ בְּעוֹבְרֵי עֲבֵירָה. יוֹמָא חַד אִידְּמִי לֵיהּ שָׂטָן כְּאִיתְּתָא בְּרֵישׁ דִּיקְלָא. נַקְטֵיהּ לְדִיקְלָא וְקָסָלֵיק וְאָזֵיל. כִּי מְטָא לְפַלְגֵיהּ דְּדִיקְלָא שַׁבְקֵיהּ, אָמַר: אִי לָאו דְּמַכְרְזִי בִּרְקִיעָא ״הִזָּהֲרוּ בְּרַבִּי עֲקִיבָא וְתוֹרָתוֹ״ שַׁוֵּיתֵיהּ לִדְמָךְ תַּרְתֵּי מָעֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Akiva would** likewise **ridicule transgressors. One day, Satan appeared to him as a woman at the top of a palm tree.** Rabbi Akiva **grabbed hold of the palm tree and began climbing. When he was halfway up the palm tree,** the evil inclination **left him and said** to him: **Were it not for** the fact that **they proclaim about you in heaven: Be careful with regard to Rabbi Akiva and his Torah, I would have made your blood like two *ma'a*.**

Rabbi Akiva similarly used to mock those who yielded to sexual desire. One day the *satan* appeared to him as a woman at the top of a palm tree. He began climbing toward her. When he reached the midpoint, the *yetzer hara* released him and delivered the same warning: had they not announced in heaven to protect Rabbi Akiva and his Torah, his life would have been worth two *ma'ei*.

### Escalating Seduction

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תִּיב שַׁפִּיר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַבוּ לִי כָּסָא, יְהַבוּ לֵיהּ כָּסָא. אַכְמַר שְׁדָא בֵּיהּ כִּיחוֹ. נְחַרוּ בֵּיהּ. שְׁקָא וּמִית. שְׁמַעוּ דַּהֲווֹ קָאָמְרִי: פְּלֵימוֹ קְטַל גַּבְרָא! פְּלֵימוֹ קְטַל גַּבְרָא! עֲרַק וּטְשָׁא נַפְשֵׁיהּ בְּבֵית הַכִּסֵּא. אָזֵיל בָּתְרֵיהּ, נְפַל קַמֵּיהּ. כִּי דְּחַזְיֵיהּ דַּהֲוָה מִצְטַעַר גַּלִּי לֵיהּ נַפְשֵׁיהּ. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי טַעְמָא אָמְרַתְּ הָכִי? וְאֶלָּא הֵיכִי אֵימָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: לֵימָא מָר ״רַחֲמָנָא נִגְעַר בֵּיהּ בְּשָׂטָן״.
>
> **Translation:** **Sit properly** and do not act in a revolting manner. Satan then **said to him: Give me a cup. They gave him a cup. He coughed up his phlegm and spat it into** the cup. **They berated him** for acting this way, at which point Satan pretended to **sink** down **and die. They heard** people around them **saying: Peleimu killed a man! Peleimu killed a man!** Peleimu **fled and hid himself in the bathroom.** Satan **followed him** and **fell before him. Upon seeing that** Peleimu **was suffering, he revealed himself to him.** Satan **said to him: What is the reason that you spoke this way,** provoking me by saying: An arrow in the eye of Satan? He replied: **But what then should I say?** Satan **said to him: Let the Master,** i.e., Peleimu, **say:** Let **the Merciful One rebuke the Satan.**

The *yetzer hara*, in Plimo's story, arrives as a poor man, escalates his demands step by step — from bread at the door, to food inside, to a seat at the table — all under the guise of legitimate needs. The Maharsha notes the parallel to an *aggadata* in which the *yetzer hara* begins as a casual visitor, then a guest, then the master of the house. Each small accommodation leads to the next, and the total result is the complete surrender of one's home and self. The bread, the wine, the seat at the table — all of it carries metaphorical weight about how desire enters and establishes residence through incremental compromise.

### Yetzer Reveals Self

The *yetzer hara* followed Plimo to the outhouse, fell before him, and revealed itself: this is who I am. It then asked: *mai ta'ama amart hachi?* — why did you go about declaring arrows into my eyes? What did you think you were accomplishing?

### God's Rebuke

*Leima mar Rachmana niga'ar bei b'Satan* — rather than say "arrows into the eyes of the Satan," one should say: may God rebuke the Satan. The distinction is significant. Trying to destroy the *yetzer* is counterproductive — that is the lesson the *yetzer* itself delivers. The *yetzer* is part of human nature, created by God; it cannot simply be annihilated. The appropriate prayer is not for its destruction but for divine assistance in restraining and redirecting it. This also connects to the psychiatric observation that denying natural desire entirely, rather than finding licit channels for it, tends to make one more rather than less vulnerable to its power — as the following story will demonstrate still more starkly.

## R. Chiya Bar Ashi

### Years Without Intimacy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אָשֵׁי הֲוָה רְגִיל כׇּל עִידָּן דַּהֲוָה נָפֵל לְאַפֵּיהּ הֲוָה אָמַר: ״הָרַחֲמָן יַצִּילֵנוּ מִיֵּצֶר הָרָע״. יוֹמָא חַד (שְׁמַעְתִּינְהוּ) [שְׁמַעְתֵּיהּ] דְּבֵיתְהוּ, אֲמַרָה: מִכְּדֵי הָא כַּמָּה שְׁנֵי דְּפָרֵישׁ לֵיהּ מִינַּאי, מַאי טַעְמָא קָאָמַר הָכִי?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Ashi was accustomed to say, whenever he would fall on his face** in prayer: **May the Merciful One save us from the evil inclination. One day his wife heard him** saying this prayer. **She said: After all, it has been several years since he has withdrawn from** engaging in intercourse with **me** due to his advanced years. **What is the reason that he says this** prayer, as there is no concern that he will engage in sinful sexual behavior?

Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi had the practice, every time he performed *tachanun* — falling on his face in prayer — of saying: *HaRachaman yatzileinu miyetzer hara* — may God save us from the evil inclination. One day his wife overheard him and reflected: it has been many years since we have had relations — and yet he still prays to be saved from the *yetzer hara*? She resolved to investigate.

### Disguise as Charuta

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חֲדָא הֲוָה קָא גָרֵס בְּגִינְּתֵיהּ. קַשִּׁטָה נַפְשַׁהּ, חָלְפָה וְתָנְיָיה קַמֵּיהּ. אֲמַר לַהּ: מַאן אַתְּ? אֲמַרָה: אֲנָא חָרוּתָא דַּהֲדַרִי מִיּוֹמָא. תַּבְעַהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: אַיְיתִי נִיהֲלַי לְהָךְ רוּמָּנָא דְּרֵישׁ צוּצִיתָא. שְׁוַור, אֲזַל אַתְיֵיהּ נִיהֲלַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **One day,** while **he was studying in his garden, she adorned herself and repeatedly walked past him. He said: Who are you? She said: I am Ḥaruta,** a well-known prostitute, **returning from my day** at work. **He propositioned her. She said to him: Give me that pomegranate from the top of the tree** as payment. **He leapt up, went,** and **brought it to her,** and they engaged in intercourse.

One day, as Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi was studying in the garden, his wife adorned herself and altered her appearance so that he would not recognize her. She walked back and forth before him. He took notice and asked: *man at?* — who are you? She answered: *ana Charuta* — I am Charuta, a famously known prostitute, just returned to town. He propositioned her for sex.

She said to him: *aiti nihalai l'hach rumna d'reish tzutzita* — bring me that pomegranate from the top of that tree. He leaped up, climbed the tree, and brought it to her. The sexual imagery of the pomegranate at the tree's summit, and the act of scaling it, is unmistakable.

### Oven Suicide

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כִּי אֲתָא לְבֵיתֵיהּ הֲוָה קָא שָׁגְרָא דְּבֵיתְהוּ תַּנּוּרָא, סָלֵיק וְקָא יָתֵיב בְּגַוֵּיהּ. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: מַאי הַאי? אֲמַר לַהּ: הָכִי וְהָכִי הֲוָה מַעֲשֶׂה. אָמְרָה לֵיהּ: אֲנָא הֲוַאי. לָא אַשְׁגַּח בַּהּ, עַד דִּיהַבָה לֵיהּ סִימָנֵי. אָמַר לַהּ: אֲנָא מִיהָא לְאִיסּוּרָא אִיכַּוַּונִי. כׇּל יָמָיו שֶׁל אוֹתוֹ צַדִּיק הָיָה מִתְעַנֶּה עַד שֶׁמֵּת בְּאוֹתָהּ מִיתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **When he came home, his wife was lighting** a fire in the **oven. He went and sat inside it. She said to him: What is this? He said to her: Such and such an incident** occurred; he told her that he engaged in intercourse with a prostitute. **She said to him: It was I. He paid no attention to her,** thinking she was merely trying to comfort him, **until she gave him signs** that it was indeed she. **He said to her: I, in any event, intended to transgress.** The Gemara relates: **All the days of that righteous man he would fast** for the transgression he intended to commit, **until he died by that death** in his misery.

When Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi returned home, his wife was firing up the oven. He climbed into it — *salik v'ka yativ b'gaveih* — overwhelmed by guilt, intent on self-destruction. The inversion of the Yehuda and Tamar story is difficult to miss: Tamar was to be thrown into the fire, condemned for the very act that Yehuda's uncontrolled desire had caused; here it is Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi who throws himself in, condemned by his own.

### Intent to Sin

His wife asked what had happened. He confessed: he had been with a prostitute — *hachi v'hachi hava ma'aseh*. She revealed herself: *ana havai* — that was me. He did not believe her until she produced confirming signs — again echoing Yehuda and Tamar, where the signs established the truth of an unexpected identity. Even after she revealed herself and he realized no actual sin had been committed, he remained inconsolable: *ana miha l'isura ikvanna* — even so, I had intended to sin. The intention was sufficient to condemn him in his own eyes.

### Lifelong Fasting

He fasted for the rest of his days — *kol yomav d'oto tzadik hava mit'aneh* — until he died through self-denial. The story is a direct continuation of the lesson from Plimo's encounter with the *yetzer hara*. Rabbi Chiya bar Ashi, by denying himself intimacy with his wife — presumably in an effort to achieve a higher spiritual state — made himself far more vulnerable to temptation, not less. The licit channel for sexual desire was closed off; the desire itself remained. The *yetzer hara*'s point to Plimo applies here with full force: do not try to destroy the desire; find the proper, permitted expression for it. Refusing one's wife does not eliminate the *yetzer hara* — it merely displaces it.

## Intent & Objective Deed

### Nazirite Vow Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: ״אִישָׁהּ הֲפֵרָם וַה׳ יִסְלַח לָהּ״, בַּמָּה הַכָּתוּב מְדַבֵּר? בְּאִשָּׁה שֶׁנָּדְרָה בְּנָזִיר, וְשָׁמַע בַּעְלָהּ וְהֵפֵר לָהּ, וְהִיא לֹא יָדְעָה שֶׁהֵפֵר לָהּ בַּעְלָהּ, וְהָיְתָה שׁוֹתָה יַיִן וּמִטַּמְּאָה לְמֵתִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the source that one who intended to transgress is punished even though he did not actually sin. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* concerning a husband who nullified the vow of his wife: **"Her husband has made them null; and the Lord will forgive her"** (Numbers 30:13). **With regard to what** case **is the verse speaking?** Why would the woman require forgiveness if her husband has nullified her vow? It is referring **to a woman who vowed** to **be a nazirite, and her husband heard and nullified her** vow. **And she did not know that her husband had nullified her** vow, **and she drank wine and contracted impurity from a corpse,** violating her presumed vow.

The *gemara* closes with a cluster of teachings about the relationship between conscious intent and objective deed. The *braisa* cites the verse *ishah heferam va-Hashem yislach lah* — her husband annulled her vows and God will forgive her. The question is: if the husband annulled the vow, why does the woman need divine forgiveness? The case is that of a woman who took a *nazir* vow and whose husband annulled it without her knowledge. She then drank wine and became *tamei* — consciously believing herself to be sinning, because she did not know the vow had been annulled.

### Forgiveness for Intent

Objectively, she committed no sin: the vow was already void. But because she acted with the conscious intention of sinning, divine forgiveness is still required. The subjective intention to sin creates culpability even when the objective act is permissible.

### Akiva's Tears

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא כִּי הֲוָה מָטֵי לְהַאי פְּסוּקָא הֲוָה בָּכֵי, אָמַר: וּמָה מִי שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל בְּשַׂר חֲזִיר וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ בְּשַׂר טָלֶה, אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: צְרִיכָה כַּפָּרָה וּסְלִיחָה, מִי שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל בְּשַׂר חֲזִיר וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ בְּשַׂר חֲזִיר – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **When Rabbi Akiva came to this verse he would cry. He said: And if** with regard to **one who intended to eat pork, and** kosher **lamb came up in his hand,** like this woman who intended to violate her vow but in fact did not, **the Torah** nevertheless **says: She requires atonement and forgiveness, all the more so** does **one who intended to eat pork and pork came up in his hand** require atonement and forgiveness.

Rabbi Akiva would weep when he reached the following *pasuk*: *u-mah mi she-nitkavein le-echol basar chazir ve-alah be-yado basar taleh, amerah Torah tzrichah kaparah u-selichah* — someone who intended to eat pork and accidentally ate kosher meat — the Torah says even this person requires atonement and forgiveness. If one who intended to sin but did not actually do so still needs forgiveness, all the more so one who intended to sin and succeeded.

### Kal V'Chomer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּשֶׁהָיָה רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא מַגִּיעַ לְפָסוּק זֶה הָיָה בּוֹכֶה: וּמָה מִי שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל שׁוּמָּן וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ חֵלֶב – אָמְרָה תּוֹרָה: ״וְלֹא יָדַע וְאָשֵׁם וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״, מִי שֶׁנִּתְכַּוֵּין לֶאֱכוֹל חֵלֶב וְעָלָה בְּיָדוֹ חֵלֶב – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: ״וְלֹא יָדַע וְאָשֵׁם וְנָשָׂא עֲוֹנוֹ״ – עַל דָּבָר זֶה יִדְווּ כׇּל הַדּוֹוִים.
>
> **Translation:** **In a similar manner, you** can **say** that the same lesson can be derived from the verse: **"Though he know it not, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity"** (Leviticus 5:17). **When Rabbi Akiva came to this verse he would cry.** He said: **And if** with regard to **one who intended to eat** permitted **fat, and** forbidden **fat** mistakenly **came up in his hand, the Torah states: "Though he know it not, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity," all the more so** is this true for **one who intended to eat** forbidden **fat and** forbidden **fat came up in his hand. Isi ben Yehuda says** with regard to the verse **"Though he know it not, yet is he guilty, and shall bear his iniquity": With regard to this matter all sufferers shall grieve,** since the verse teaches that one is punished even for sinning unawares.

The *kal v'chomer* follows: *mi she-nitkavein le-echol basar chazir ve-alah be-yado basar chazir — al achat kamah ve-chamah* — someone who intended to sin and succeeded in sinning requires all the more forgiveness. The conscious intent to transgress, combined with the actual transgression, demands the greatest degree of *kaparah*.

### Closing Reflection

Rabbi Akiva further weeps over the reverse case: *mah mi she-nitkavein le-echol shuman ve-alah be-yado chelev* — someone who intended to eat kosher fat and accidentally ate *chelev*, forbidden fat. Even though the person had no intention of sinning, the Torah says *ve-lo yada ve-ashem ve-nasa avono* — he does not know, yet is guilty, and bears the burden of his sin. If someone who sought to do right and accidentally sinned still bears guilt, then *al achat kamah ve-chamah* — someone who sought to sin and succeeded bears guilt all the more.

Issi ben Yehuda adds a final, devastating observation: *ve-lo yada ve-ashem ve-nasa avono — al davar zeh yidagu kol ha-devuyim* — all those who grieve and wail should wail over this: that even one who sinned without any intention whatsoever still bears the burden of the sin. The Torah assigns culpability not only to deliberate sin but even to inadvertent violation.

Taken together, these teachings close the *daf* with a powerful warning about the situations one allows oneself to enter. Whether the objective act is sinful, whether the subjective intent was sinful, or whether one only tried to sin without succeeding — all carry moral and spiritual weight. The laws of *yichud*, with all their nuance and complexity, are ultimately an effort to keep people from entering situations in which any one of these three forms of culpability might arise.
