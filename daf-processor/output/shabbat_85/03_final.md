# Shabbat 85 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Five Seeds Overview

### Opening & Context

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ מִנַּיִן לַעֲרוּגָה שֶׁהִיא שִׁשָּׁה עַל שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים שֶׁזּוֹרְעִין בְּתוֹכָהּ חֲמִשָּׁה זֵרְעוֹנִין, אַרְבָּעָה עַל אַרְבַּע רוּחוֹת הָעֲרוּגָה וְאַחַת בָּאֶמְצַע, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״כִּי כָאָרֶץ תּוֹצִיא צִמְחָהּ וּכְגַנָּה זֵרוּעֶיהָ תַצְמִיחַ״ — ״זַרְעָהּ״ לֹא נֶאֱמַר, אֶלָּא ״זֵרוּעֶיהָ״.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The Gemara continues to discuss an additional *halakha* based on a biblical allusion. **From where** is it derived **that** in **a garden bed that is six by six handbreadths, that one may plant five** different types of **seeds in it?** He may do so without violating the prohibition of sowing a mixture of diverse kinds of seeds in the following manner. One sows **four** types of plants **on** each of **the four sides of the garden bed and one in the middle.** There is an allusion to this in the text, **as it is stated: "For as the earth brings forth its growth, and as a garden causes its seeds to grow,** so will the Lord God cause justice and praise to spring forth before all the nations" (Isaiah 61:11). **Its seed,** in the singular, **is not stated; rather, its seeds,** written in the plural. Apparently, it is possible that several seeds may be planted in a small garden.

The *daf* today places us squarely in the world of *Zraim*, the agricultural laws. The *mishnah* treated yesterday established that in a vegetable garden, a space of six by six *tefachim* can accommodate five different types of seeds without creating a *kilayim* problem, and it supported this ruling with a *pasuk*. What is striking is that when this *mishnah* appears in its proper home in Masechet Kilayim, it does not cite the *pasuk*. Reading our *perek* in that light, one way to understand it is that our *mishnah* is asking: where do we know what was taught in Kilayim? And here it supplies the scriptural basis.

This raises a broader question that scholars have engaged with: did this cluster of *mishnayot* in our *perek* — a collection of diverse rulings with a structural similarity — originate first as an early anthology, later divided and distributed throughout *Shas*? Or did the *mishnayot* appear elsewhere first, and our *perek* is a later compilation providing their *psukim*? Either way, the ruling itself stands: five different types of vegetables planted in a six-by-six space do not constitute *kilayim*.

### Mishnah Rule

The core teaching is that one may plant five distinct species in a six-by-six *tefachim* plot without transgressing the prohibition of mixed seeds. The question, of course, is how exactly they are to be arranged.

### Rashi's Layout

Rashi's diagram, found on 85b, places four rows along the outer margins of the plot — one on each side — and situates a fifth seed in the center. The logic governing this arrangement flows from the two central concerns of *kilayim*: to what degree does the planting *look* mixed together, and to what degree are the plants actually sharing nutrients drawn from the same soil?

### Kilayim Logic

According to Rashi and others, the primary issue in *kilayim* is one of appearance. So long as it is visually clear that different species are separate, the fact that they may share nutrients underground is not determinative. A compelling proof for this position is the case of adjacent fields: one neighbor may plant up to his side of a shared fence while the other plants up to his side, with different species on each side. The plants certainly share nutrients beneath the ground, yet because the fence makes the division visually evident, there is no *kilayim* problem. Rashi concludes that visual distinction is the operative criterion.

On this view, the four rows along the edges do not create a problem because they are oriented in perpendicular directions relative to one another — horizontal rows face vertical ones, and they do not touch. The fifth seed in the center, however, is not going in any "opposite direction" from its neighbors. It has no orientation that by itself signals separation. Therefore, the center planting requires physical space around it to establish its independence.

### Spacing Rules

That required space is three *tefachim*. The principle is that a plant draws nutrients from the soil within a radius of one and a half *tefachim*. To ensure that two plants' zones of nutrient absorption do not overlap, one must therefore maintain three *tefachim* between them. In a six-by-six plot, with three *tefachim* on each side of the central seed, this spacing requirement is met — though Rashi acknowledges that the three *tefachim* is not entirely empty, as it includes the space occupied by the seeds themselves, and holds that this is nonetheless acceptable.

### Alt Configurations

Others — though this is not discussed in our *Gemara* directly — propose more creative approaches that allow for far greater coverage of the plot. One such reading is that each species occupies a distinct angular segment, with species arranged so that each touches its neighbor only at a single point while approaching from a perpendicular direction. The difference in shape and orientation is itself sufficient to establish visual distinction, allowing the planter to fill almost the entire space. Another version positions the central species as the largest, with four corner species arranged at angles radiating outward. Again, the perpendicular approach of each group is what grants the necessary visual separation — yielding considerably more planting coverage than Rashi's approach.

### Rashi vs Tosafot

Tosafot is actually more stringent than Rashi in one important respect. Tosafot is not satisfied with mere visual distinction through directionality. Rather, Tosafot requires that the angular distance between each species' planting zone actually measures three *tefachim* from everything else. The geometry works as follows: the patches are arranged so that even at the closest diagonal point — the angle between them — the effective distance is three *tefachim*. If the individual segments are small enough, the trigonometry supports this. For Rashi, by contrast, once plants are oriented in different directions, the three-*tefach* requirement is waived; directional distinction alone suffices. The result is that Rashi permits far more compact arrangements than Tosafot.

## Rabbinical Knowledge

### Gemara Challenge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּחַמְשָׁא בְּשִׁיתָּא לָא יָנְקִי מֵהֲדָדֵי. וּמְנָלַן דְּהָא דְּקִים לְהוּ לְרַבָּנַן דְּחַמְשָׁא בְּשִׁיתָּא מִילְּתָא הִיא?
>
> **Translation:** **And the Sages** have **an accepted** tradition **that five** seeds **in** a **six-**handbreadth space **do not draw sustenance from one another.** The Gemara asks: **And from where do we** derive **that** when **the Sages** have **an accepted** tradition it **is a** substantial **matter,** meaning that the tradition is reliable?

The *Gemara* now asks a pointed question: *minallan dekimlu lehu lerabbanan milsa hi?* — from where do we know that the Rabbis have correctly determined that five different seeds in a six-by-six space do not draw nutrients from one another? The question is essentially: since when are the Rabbis authorities on agriculture? What entitles them to declare with confidence that within three *tefachim* plants do not encroach on one another's nutrient zones?

### Boundary Verse

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּאָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר אַבָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב: ״לֹא תַסִּיג גְּבוּל רֵעֲךָ [אֲשֶׁר גָּבְלוּ רִאשׁוֹנִים]״ — גְּבוּל שֶׁגָּבְלוּ רִאשׁוֹנִים לֹא תַסִּיג. מַאי ״גָּבְלוּ רִאשׁוֹנִים״?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Abba said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "You shall not cross your neighbor's border, which they of the old times have set** in your inheritance that you shall inherit" (Deuteronomy 19:14)? It means that **you shall not cross the border that the early** generations **set,** establishing the parameters necessary for each plant. The Gemara asks: **What** is the meaning of the phrase: **The early** generations **set?**

The *Gemara* turns to a teaching of Rabbi Chiya bar Abba in the name of Rabbi Yochanan. The verse cited is *lo tassig gevul re'acha asher gavlu rishonim* — "do not move the boundary of your neighbor that the early ones have set" (Devarim 19:14). The plain meaning of the verse (*peshat*) is a prohibition against encroaching on a neighbor's land by moving boundary markers — a form of theft with its own specific prohibition. But in the *midrashic* reading of the *Gemara*, the phrase *asher gavlu rishonim* — "that the earlier ones set" — is taken as a reference to a body of knowledge about the land that predates even the Rabbis.

### Seirites' Knowledge

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי שְׁמוּאֵל בַּר נַחְמָנִי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹנָתָן: מַאי דִּכְתִיב ״אֵלֶּה בְנֵי שֵׂעִיר הַחֹרִי יוֹשְׁבֵי הָאָרֶץ״, אַטּוּ כּוּלֵּי עָלְמָא יוֹשְׁבֵי רָקִיעַ נִינְהוּ?! אֶלָּא שֶׁהָיוּ בְּקִיאִין בְּיִשּׁוּבָהּ שֶׁל אֶרֶץ, שֶׁהָיוּ אוֹמְרִים: מְלֹא קָנֶה זֶה לְזַיִת, מְלֹא קָנֶה זֶה לִגְפָנִים, מְלֹא קָנֶה זֶה לִתְאֵנִים. וְ״חֹרִי״ — שֶׁמְּרִיחִים אֶת הָאָרֶץ. וְ״חִוִּי״, אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שֶׁהָיוּ טוֹעֲמִין אֶת הָאָרֶץ כְּחִוְיָא. רַב אַחָא בַּר יַעֲקֹב אָמַר: ״חֹרִי״ — שֶׁנַּעֲשׂוּ בְּנֵי חוֹרִין מִנִּכְסֵיהֶן.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shmuel bar Naḥmani said** that **Rabbi Yonatan said: What is** the meaning of that **which was written: "These are the sons of Seir the Horite who inhabit the land,** Lotan and Shoval and Zibeon and Ana" (Genesis 36:20)? **And is everyone** else **inhabitants of the heavens,** that it was necessary for the verse to emphasize that these inhabit the land? **Rather,** it means **that they were experts in the settlement of the land, as they would say: This** tract of land that is the **full** length **of a rod** is fit **for olive** trees; **this full** length **of a rod** is fit **for grapes, this full** length **of a rod** is fit **for figs. And** the members of this tribe were called **Horites [*ḥori*]** since **they smelled [*heriḥu*] the earth** to determine what is fit to be grown there. The allusion is based on a transposition of the letters *ḥet* and *reish*. **And** in explanation of why the early inhabitants of Seir were called **Hivites [*ḥivi*]** (see Genesis 36:2), **Rav Pappa said:** Because **they would taste the earth like a snake [*ḥivya*]** and determine what should be grown there according to the taste. **Rav Aḥa bar Ya'akov said** that they were called **Horites [*ḥori*]** because **they became free [*benei ḥorin*] of their possessions** when the children of Esau drove them from their lands. Their primary name was actually Hivites.

This reading is then grounded in a verse from Bereishit: *eleh bnei Seir ha-Chori yoshvei ha'aretz* — "these are the descendants of Seir the Horite, who dwelled in the land." The *Gemara* asks: is it noteworthy that they "dwelled in the land"? Does everyone else dwell in the sky? Rather, the phrase must convey something beyond physical habitation: *she-hayu beki'in be-yishuva shel aretz* — they were experts in the character of the land, they truly understood its qualities and what it required. They would examine a tract of soil and declare: this expanse is ideal for olive trees; this stretch is suited to vines; here, one should plant figs. Their intimate familiarity with the land was such that by examining it they could prescribe what should be planted and where.

### Wordplay Analysis

The *Gemara* then derives this expertise from the very names by which these people are called. The word *chori* — Horite — is read as a play on *meriach*, to smell: *she-merichim et ha-aretz*, they would smell the dirt and discern from it what should be planted. A second name, *Chivi*, applied to one of their descendants — as in *bat Tzivon ha-Chivi* — is interpreted by Rabbi Pappa as *she-hayu to'amim et ha-aretz ke-chivya*, they would taste the earth like a snake. The snake is always close to the ground, seemingly consuming dirt, and the verse's curse of *ve-afar tochal* — "you shall eat dirt" — resonates here. These ancient inhabitants of Seir, by tasting and smelling the soil, could determine with precision which crops were suited to each parcel of land.

There is one additional *derasha* worth noting. The word *chori* can also be read as *she-na'asu b'nei chorin mi-nichaseihen* — that they became free, or rather freed, from their possessions. This picks up on an important irony: they were the very people most intimately connected to the land, who understood it better than anyone — and yet they were driven from it when the descendants of Esav came and dispossessed them. *U-vnei Esav yirashum va-yishvu* — Esav's children inherited and settled the land. It is the history of nations.

What all of this seemingly demonstrates is that human beings can possess genuine expert knowledge of agriculture — knowledge that can be passed down. The *Gemara* is connecting the Rabbis' determinations about plant nutrition zones to this tradition of inherited understanding about how the land and its plants operate. The verse *asher gavlu rishonim* implies that this boundary-knowledge of the earth was established by those early masters and transmitted forward.

Tosafot, it should be noted, reads the verse as doing even more work: not merely demonstrating that such knowledge can exist, but as containing an additional prohibition against planting seeds of one type within the nutrient-drawing space of another. *Lo tassig gevul re'acha* thus becomes, in Tosafot's reading, a separate prohibition of encroachment applicable to the planting of *kilayim* — not one that carries lashes, but an additional dimension of the *pasuk*'s meaning.

### Moshe MiSinai

One might ask: could this knowledge not simply have been transmitted directly to Moshe at Sinai? This is in fact part of a broader question about the nature of *Torah she-be'al peh*. Two broad positions exist. One holds that everything any future Torah scholar would discover was given to Moshe at Sinai in its entirety — all knowledge was transmitted at that moment, even if some was subsequently forgotten or lost, or perhaps Moshe received multiple possibilities. The opposing view holds that Moshe received very little beyond the Written Torah that was not derivable from it; everything else was determined by later Rabbis through their reading of *psukim* and their interpretive methods, with factual matters ascertained through the knowledge available in each generation.

The *Gemara* here begins with the phrase *kim lei l'rabbanan* — the Rabbis have determined — which sounds more like the second approach, suggesting active rabbinic determination rather than direct transmission. But one could equally read this *Gemara* as expressing the first approach: that the knowledge goes back to Sinai precisely because it predates Sinai — Moshe had access to the Seirites' understanding, which was then passed down. The *Gemara* accommodates both readings, and as the Rambam's approach reflects, there is considerable range in how one understands the relationship between received tradition and rabbinic derivation. In any case, some combination of the *pasuk* (*ki aretz totzi tzimchah*) and the Rabbis' agricultural knowledge yields the specific ruling of five seeds in a six-by-six space — since the *pasuk* alone does not specify that number or that configuration.

## Border Specs

### Border Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אַסִּי: עֲרוּגָה תּוֹכָהּ שִׁשָּׁה חוּץ מִגְּבוּלֶיהָ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: עֲרוּגָה תּוֹכָהּ שִׁשָּׁה. גְּבוּלֶיהָ בְּכַמָּה?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said:** The **garden bed** in the mishna whose area is six by six handbreadths is one whose **internal** area **is six** by six handbreadths **excluding** the area of **its boundaries,** which must be added to the total area. **That was also taught** in a *baraita*: **The internal** area **of a garden bed is six** by six handbreadths. The Gemara asks: **How much** is the size of **its boundaries?**

The *Gemara* now shifts to technical specifications. Rav Assi teaches: *aruga toch vav chutz mi-gvulah* — when we say a six-by-six *arugah*, the six by six refers to the planted area itself, not including the border around it. There would be a path or walking space surrounding the vegetable patch that one would not plant. The six-by-six measurement denotes the actual planted zone, *chutz mi-gvulah* — not counting the surrounding border.

The *Gemara* confirms this from a *baraita*: the *arugah* is six by six, and separately one asks how large the surrounding border is.

### Footstep Width

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כְּדִתְנַן, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: רוֹחַב — כִּמְלֹא רוֹחַב פַּרְסָה. אָמַר רַבִּי זֵירָא, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַבִּי חֲנִינָא בַּר פָּפָּא: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה — דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהִשְׁקִיתָ בְרַגְלְךָ כְּגַן הַיָּרָק״ — מָה רֶגֶל טֶפַח, אַף גְּבוּל נָמֵי טֶפַח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers, **as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Yehuda says:** The **width** of the border **is like the width of a foot.** And **Rabbi Zeira said, and some say** it was **Rabbi Ḥanina bar Pappa** who **said: What is the reason** for the statement of **Rabbi Yehuda? As it is written: "And you water it with your foot like a garden of herbs"** (Deuteronomy 11:10), meaning that **just as** one's **foot is a handbreadth** wide, **so too, the boundary** between garden beds where one walks to water plants **is also a handbreadth** wide.

The *Gemara* cites the *mishnah* where Rabbi Yehuda rules that the width of a path separating different planting zones is *ke-melo rochav parsa* — the width of a footstep. Specifically, this means the breadth of a single foot — one *tefach* — which is how one would walk in a narrow pace between garden rows, one foot placed directly in front of the other. Rabbi Yehuda's ruling, though stated in a different context within *kilayim*, is understood to apply here as well: the standard border around a vegetable patch is one *tefach* wide.

Rabbi Zeira, and some attribute this to Rabbi Chanina bar Pappa, explains Rabbi Yehuda's reasoning from a verse in Devarim: *ve-hishkita be-raglecha ke-gan ha-yarak* — "you shall water it with your feet, like a vegetable garden." Why does the verse specify "with your feet"? The Torah could simply have said it is watered like a vegetable garden. The specification of feet indicates that when one walks through a garden to water it, the path one traverses is defined by the width of a single foot. *Ma regel tefach — af po nami tefach*: just as your foot is a *tefach* wide, so the paths and borders separating vegetable plots are one *tefach* wide. The singular *be-raglecha* — "with your foot," not "with your feet" — reinforces this: even the space of a single foot defines the walking path.

The reason this ruling matters here is that it sets up the problem about to be addressed. If neighboring vegetable patches are each surrounded by a one-*tefach* border, then two adjacent patches will be separated by a total of two *tefachim* between their planted areas. But we have established that the required separation is three *tefachim*. The standard border spacing is therefore insufficient to prevent a *kilayim* problem between neighboring patches — and this is precisely the difficulty the next *sugya* confronts.

## Rav vs Shmuel

### Rav's Position

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב: עֲרוּגָה — בְּחוּרְבָּה שָׁנִינוּ. וְהָאִיכָּא מָקוֹם קְרָנוֹת! אָמְרִי בֵּי רַב מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַב: בִּמְמַלֵּא אֶת הַקְּרָנוֹת. וְלִיזְרַע מֵאַבָּרַאי וְלָא לִימַלֵּא מִגַּוַּאי!
>
> **Translation:** **Rav said:** When we learned in the mishna that one may plant five kinds of seeds within **a garden bed** without violating the prohibition of diverse kinds, **we learned this** with regard to a garden bed **in a desolate area** not surrounded by other plants. However, if the garden bed is among other garden beds, it is prohibited to plant that many species there because seeds from the different beds will intermingle. The Gemara asks: If the mishna is dealing with a solitary garden bed, **isn't there space** in **the corners,** where more species of seeds could be planted without encountering the prohibition of diverse seeds? **The school** of **Rav taught in the name of Rav:** Rav's statement is referring to a case **where one fills** the **corners** of the flowerbed with the five species of seeds, leaving no room in the corners for other varieties. The Gemara asks: **And let him plant on the outside and not fill up the inside,** to increase the different seed types instead of filling up the corners.

Rav declares: *aruga be-chorva shaninu* — when we taught the ruling about the five-species vegetable patch, we were speaking of a case where the *arugah* sits in a desolate, empty area with nothing else around it. The only planting to consider is this one six-by-six plot; there are no neighboring vegetable patches anywhere nearby. The ruling operates in a vacuum.

The *Gemara* asks: but what about the space at the corners? Why must the patch be entirely isolated? Could one not arrange the neighboring patches in a way that avoids a *kilayim* problem?

The question requires understanding what "corners" refers to in the debate between Rashi and Tosafot, and both approaches yield different readings of the *Gemara*'s thrust.

On Rashi's reading, the question is not "there are corners left over" but rather "why not solve the problem by leaving the corners unplanted?" Consider the following: if you plant a row but stop short of the corners — leaving the corner areas of the plot unfilled — then every species along a given edge goes only partway, and the neighboring species coming from the adjacent side will always be moving in a perpendicular direction relative to any nearby planting. Nothing going in the same direction will be adjacent to anything else going in the same direction. So the *Gemara* is asking: why not use this strategy — plant partial rows while leaving the corners empty — to allow neighboring patches without creating *kilayim*?

To this, the *Gemara* answers on Rav's behalf: *amarei bei Rav mishmeih de-Rav bememalei et ha-kranot* — we are speaking of a case where one fills up the corners, going the full length of each row. Given that, if a neighboring patch is planted in parallel rows, those rows will be going in the same direction with only two *tefachim* of separation between them — which is insufficient.

### Gezeirah Shema

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּזֵירָה שֶׁמָּא יְמַלֵּא אֶת הַקְּרָנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav's reasoning must be: It is **a decree lest one will fill the corners** after having first planted the garden bed, and thereby violate the prohibition of diverse kinds.

The *Gemara* then asks: but why plant the full row? Why not adopt the strategy of not filling the corners? To this, Rashi explains the answer as a *gezeirah*: *gezeirah shema yemalei et ha-kranot* — we are concerned that once we permit planting a partial row, the person will eventually go ahead and fill in the corners completely. Therefore, the Rabbis treat every case as if the corners will be filled in, because we anticipate that they will be. And since filling in the corners creates a problem with adjacent patches, the ruling is that the *arugah* must stand alone.

### Tosafot Angles

Tosafot's approach to the same passage is quite different. Recall that Tosafot's entire framework requires that every species in the patch be three *tefachim* away from every other, measured along the angle between them. On that reading, the corner areas of the plot are actually left unused — the planted portions of each side stop before reaching the full corner. The question in the *Gemara*, then, is from Tosafot's perspective: even if you fill in the corners, why is there a problem with neighboring patches? After all, those corner zones and the angles between patches already provide sufficient space. The *Gemara*'s answer is that we are speaking of a case where the corners are filled in completely — *memalei et ha-kranot* — so the angular buffer no longer exists, and adjacent patches are too close.

### Rosh Tor Concept

This brings us to the concept of *rosh tor* — the "head of a row." Rashi describes this as what happens at the edge of a planted row: rather than maintaining its full rectangular width, the end of a row narrows, coming to a point. The idea is that this pointed or angled terminus visually defines the row as ending there and signals that what lies beyond it is distinct. This is the operative principle behind the ruling that if one species' row ends in a *rosh tor* pointing into an adjacent planting, the two are considered visually separate and there is no *kilayim*.

### Neighboring Patches

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְלֹא יְהֵא אֶלָּא רֹאשׁ תּוֹר יָרָק? מִי לָא תְּנַן: הָיָה רֹאשׁ תּוֹר יָרָק נִכְנָס לְתוֹךְ שָׂדֶה אַחֵר — מוּתָּר, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁנִּרְאֶה סוֹף שָׂדֶה!
>
> **Translation:** Rather, Rav's reasoning must be: It is **a decree lest one will fill the corners** after having first planted the garden bed, and thereby violate the prohibition of diverse kinds. The Gemara asks: **And** why should that matter, the legal status of the garden bed **should merely be** like **a triangular [*rosh tor*]** plot of **vegetables. Didn't we learn** in a mishna: If **a triangular** plot of **vegetables was protruding into another field, it is permitted;** there, it is not considered a prohibited mixture of diverse kinds, **because the end of the field is distinguishable.** Based on the shapes of the two fields where they intersect, the demarcation between them is clear.

The *Gemara* asks: given that *rosh tor* works to distinguish one planting from another, why can Rav not allow neighboring vegetable patches? Even if rows are planted in the same direction, the edge of each row would come to a point — a *rosh tor* — and that should establish the visual distinction needed between the patches. The *Gemara* supports the premise by citing a *mishnah* teaching: if the *rosh tor yerek* — the head of a vegetable row — extends into another field, it is permitted, *mipnei she-nireh sof sadeh* — because it looks like the edge of a field. One can see that this is a distinct, separate zone. So why can the same logic not be applied to justify two neighboring vegetable patches?

### Rosh Tor Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין רֹאשׁ תּוֹר בַּעֲרוּגָה.
>
> **Translation:** And the Gemara answers: **There is no** leniency with regard to a **triangular plot in a garden bed.** This allowance with regard to a triangular section of one field jutting into another field applies only to a large field where the triangular shape can clearly be attributed to a different field, but in a small garden bed where the seeds are adjacent to one another, it is impossible to distinguish between the seeds.

The *Gemara* answers: *ein rosh tor ba-arugah* — the principle of *rosh tor* does not apply as between two *arugot*. Rashi explains this explicitly: *ein toras heter rosh tor me-arugah le-arugah* — the leniency of *rosh tor* operates only between a large field and a vegetable patch (*misadeh gedolah le-toch arugah* or vice versa), or between two large fields. What makes *rosh tor* work is the combination of the angled shape *and* the contrast between the large surrounding context and the smaller distinct element. When you have a vast wheat field on one side and a small vegetable garden on the other, the pointed incursion is obviously something different; the surrounding context makes it legible as separate. But when you have two small vegetable patches side by side — *shtei arugot* — they are too similar, too small, and tend to merge visually into a single undifferentiated area. The *rosh tor* shape is not sufficient by itself to create the requisite distinction; it needs the larger contextual contrast to function. And therefore two adjacent *arugot* cannot rely on *rosh tor* to avoid *kilayim*.

This, then, is the full force of Rav's position: the five-species *arugah* must be isolated. Even the strategy of leaving partial rows — which might seem to solve the directional problem — is foreclosed by the *gezeirah* that one will go on to fill the corners. And even if corners were left empty, neighboring patches cannot rely on *rosh tor* because that principle does not apply between two *arugot*.

It is worth noting, as Rashi himself acknowledges with remarkable candor, that the reading of this entire passage is deeply contested. Rashi writes: *velo pirshu rabosai ken* — my masters did not explain this *Gemara* the way I have. They read *kranot* as referring to *gvulin*, boundaries, not corners. Rashi says he has many difficulties with their reading — *yesh li gimgum ba harbeh* — and that in all of *Shas* he has never found *kranot* to mean anything other than *zaviyot*, corners. He cites the support of his uncle Rabbi Shimon, transmitting in the name of Rabbeinu Gershom Me'or ha-Golah, and notes that Rabbeinu Yitzchak ben Yehuda offered three different approaches to this *Gemara* — none of which Rashi found satisfactory. The point is well taken: this is a genuinely difficult passage with a long history of contested interpretation.

### Shmuel's Rosh Tor

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּשְׁמוּאֵל אָמַר: עֲרוּגָה בֵּין הָעֲרוּגוֹת שָׁנִינוּ. וְהָא קָא מִיתְעָרְבִי בַּהֲדָדֵי! בְּנוֹטֶה שׁוּרָה לְכָאן וְשׁוּרָה לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** **And Shmuel said:** When we learned in the mishna that one may plant five kinds of seeds within a garden bed without violating the prohibition of diverse kinds, **we learned this** even with regard to **a garden bed among garden beds,** not only in a solitary bed. The Gemara asks: **Don't the seeds become intermingled with one another?** The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where one inclines** one **row to here,** in one direction, **and** one **row to here,** in a different direction (*Me'iri*). In that way they do not appear intermingled.

Shmuel disagrees with Rav. *Aruga bein arugot shaninu* — the ruling about the five-species *arugah* is stated in the context where it is surrounded by other vegetable patches, not in isolation. Shmuel holds that neighboring *arugot* are permitted. On Rashi's reading, Shmuel maintains that *rosh tor* does apply even between two vegetable patches — the edge of a row coming to a point is sufficient to distinguish them. Combined with the standard two-*tefach* separation between neighboring borders, this provides enough visual distinctness to permit adjacent patches.

### Leaf Entanglement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָא מִיתְעָרְבִי בַּהֲדָדֵי! בְּנוֹטֶה שׁוּרָה לְכָאן וְשׁוּרָה לְכָאן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Don't the seeds become intermingled with one another?** The Gemara answers: It is referring to a case **where one inclines** one **row to here,** in one direction, **and** one **row to here,** in a different direction (*Me'iri*). In that way they do not appear intermingled.

The *Gemara* now presses Shmuel: *ve-ha ka-mit'arvi bahadadi* — but the leaves of the vegetables in neighboring patches will become intertwined! If the plantings are adjacent, even with two *tefachim* of separation, the leaves growing above ground will spread into each other and create the visual appearance of a single mixed planting.

This question is notable. It does not arise regarding the five species within a single *arugah* — somehow, the internal arrangement of opposite orientations manages that concern, or it is simply less acute when the species are arranged correctly within one plot. According to Tosafot, this question does not arise within a single *arugah* at all, because Tosafot requires three *tefachim* between every species within the plot; with that much space, there is no worry about leaf intertwining.

The problem arises specifically between two neighboring patches. Even on Shmuel's view — where the patches may be adjacent, separated by two *tefachim* total and distinguished by *rosh tor* — the leaves above ground can spread and intermingle, making everything look like one confused planting.

The *Gemara*'s answer is *de-notteh shura le-kan ve-shura le-kan*: you manipulate the leaves, directing the growth of one row's leaves in one direction and the adjacent row's leaves in the opposite direction — using trellising or twine or some other means. With the leaves artificially arranged to point away from each other, the intertwining problem is resolved.

This, then, is the conclusion of the Rav-Shmuel debate: Rav holds the *arugah* must stand alone; Shmuel holds it may be surrounded by other patches, provided the rows are shaped so as to come to a *rosh tor* at their edges and the leaves are managed so as not to intertwine.

## Alt Solutions

### Furrow Divider

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר עוּלָּא, בְּעוֹ בְּמַעְרְבָא: הִפְקִיעַ תֶּלֶם אֶחָד עַל פְּנֵי כּוּלָּהּ, מַהוּ?
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla said: They raise a dilemma in the West,** Eretz Yisrael: **What** is the *halakha* if **one opened a single furrow across its entirety?** Is it considered demarcation between the garden beds if one dug a furrow between two garden beds (*ge'onim*, *Tosafot*)?

Ulla poses a question that was raised in the academies of the West (*ma'arava*): if one digs a single furrow — *telem echad* — all the way around a vegetable patch, does that serve as a sufficient divider between neighboring patches? The image is something like a moat: rather than relying on visual orientation or spatial distance alone, the planter physically excavates a trench, one *tefach* deep, surrounding the *arugah*. Each patch would be separated from its neighbor not merely by a gap of soil but by an actual ditch. Perhaps this visible demarcation should be sufficient to establish the patches as separate, distinct plantings — even where leaves might otherwise intertwine above ground and even where the two-*tefach* gap alone would be insufficient.

### Furrow Overridden

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: בָּא עִרְבּוּב וּבִיטֵּל אֶת הַשּׁוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said:** The **intermingling** of these garden beds **comes and nullifies the row.** The furrow is not considered to be a demarcation between the beds.

Rav Sheshet responds: *ba eiruv u-vitla tzurata* — the intertwining of the leaves (*eiruv*) comes and overrides (*mevatel*) the visual form (*tzurata*). That is, even if the furrow creates a genuine physical and visual distinction at ground level, the fact that the leaves spread and intermingle above ground undoes that distinction. From the perspective of the viewer, the overall visual impression is one of a mixed, undivided planting — and it is that impression which determines the *kilayim* question. The furrow below cannot overcome the intertwining above.

### Rav Ashi Disagree

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי אָמַר: אֵין עֵירוּבוֹ מְבַטֵּל אֶת הַשּׁוּרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi said: Its intermingling does not disqualify the row.**

Rav Ashi disagrees: *ein eiruv mevatel tzurata* — the intertwining of leaves does not override the visual form established by the furrow. The question of visual distinctness must be assessed carefully: if one looks closely, the furrow is there and the patches are clearly demarcated as separate. The issue is whether *kilayim* is evaluated by the overall impression from a distance — in which case intertwining leaves create one merged mass — or by the underlying structural reality, which the furrow makes plain. Rav Ashi maintains that the furrow's visual form persists and cannot be nullified by what the leaves do above ground.

### Vegetable Divider

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: הַנּוֹטֵעַ שְׁתֵּי שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל קִישּׁוּאִין, שְׁתֵּי שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל דִּילּוּעִין, שְׁתֵּי שׁוּרוֹת שֶׁל פּוֹל הַמִּצְרִי — מוּתָּר. שׁוּרָה אַחַת שֶׁל קִישּׁוּאִין וְשׁוּרָה אַחַת שֶׁל דִּילּוּעִין וְשׁוּרָה אַחַת שֶׁל פּוֹל הַמִּצְרִי — אָסוּר!
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina raised an objection to** the opinion of **Rav Ashi:** We learned that **One who plants two rows of cucumbers** and **two rows of gourds** and **two rows of Egyptian beans, it is permitted** because each of the species is distinct. However, if he plants **one row of cucumbers and one row of gourds and one row of Egyptian beans, it is prohibited** because in single rows there is no clear demarcation between the species. This indicates that a furrow between different species of seeds does not prevent intermingling between them.

Ravina challenges Rav Ashi with a *baraita*: one who plants two rows (*shetei shurot*) of *kishu'in* (cucumbers), two rows of *dilu'in* (gourds), and two rows of *pul ha-mitzri* (Egyptian beans) — this is permitted. Tosafot notes that these particular species are chosen because gourds are visually distinct enough from cucumbers on one side and from beans on the other to serve as a dividing buffer; gourds between the other two creates sufficient visual differentiation. However: one row of cucumbers, one row of gourds, and one row of beans — *assur*, forbidden. The implication is that a single row of a different vegetable is not sufficient to serve as a dividing element between two outer species, but two rows are.

Ravina's challenge to Rav Ashi follows from this: if a single furrow can serve as a divider — as Rav Ashi maintains — why do we need two full rows of gourds and not just one? One row of a visually distinct vegetable seems at least as significant a divider as one furrow in the ground, yet the *baraita* requires two rows. The analogy would seem to undermine the sufficiency of a single furrow.

### Two Rows Analysis

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שָׁאנֵי הָכָא דְּאִיכָּא שְׁרָאכָא.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Ashi replied: The case in that mishna is an exception. **Here, it is different because there are branches** that grow out from these species which become entangled with each other, nullifying the furrow between them. Other vegetables, whose branches do not become entangled, may be planted with a single furrow between them.

Rav Ashi responds: *shani hacha de-ika sarcha* — this case is different because here there is *sarcha*, extensive intertwining. Rashi explains: *zemuroteihen arukot u-mit'arvot harbeh mi-lema'alah* — the vines and branches of these plants are long and intertwine heavily above ground, more so than in other cases. When the plants spread and intermingle to such a degree, one row of a different species cannot preserve the visual distinction; the intertwining overwhelms it, and only two rows — providing a wider buffer — can do so. The furrow, by contrast, is a clear physical demarcation at ground level, and the plants around a furrow may not spread as severely, so a single furrow may suffice even where a single row of vegetables would not.

One might have thought the simpler answer is that a furrow is simply a much more significant divider than a neighboring row of vegetables — after all, the entire problem of *kilayim* involves different vegetables next to each other, so using a different vegetable as the divider itself seems conceptually strange. But the *Gemara*, as Rashi reads it, does not take that approach. It focuses specifically on the comparative degree of leaf intertwining, distinguishing the cases on factual rather than conceptual grounds.

## Filling Garden

### Circle Solution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב כָּהֲנָא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: הָרוֹצֶה לְמַלְּאוֹת כׇּל גִּינָּתוֹ יָרָק — עוֹשֶׂה עֲרוּגָה שִׁשָּׁה עַל שִׁשָּׁה וְעוֹגֵל בָּהּ חֲמִשָּׁה, וּמְמַלֵּא קַרְנוֹתֶיהָ כׇּל מַה שֶּׁיִּרְצֶה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Kahana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: One who wishes to fill his entire garden with vegetables** and does not want to distance the rows of seeds from one another may **make a garden bed** that is **six by six** handbreadths **and make five circles inside it** (*ge'onim*, Rambam). He plants different species in the different circles **and fills its corners** with **whatever** additional species that **he wants.**

Rav Kahana, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, addresses a different question: what if one wants to fill an entire garden with vegetables, not merely plant one isolated six-by-six *arugah*? The approach taught is: make a six-by-six *arugah*, inscribe within it a five-by-five square, and within that five-by-five square inscribe a circle. The circular inner zone can hold one species, and the four corner areas — the spaces between the circle and the square's corners — can each hold a different species. At the points where the corners approach each other and where they approach the circle, they come to a narrowing, pointed shape that functions as a *rosh tor*, visually defining each as a separate zone.

### Corner Filling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא אִיכָּא דְּבֵינֵי וּבֵינֵי! אָמְרִי דְּבֵי רַבִּי יַנַּאי: בְּמַחֲרִיב בֵּין הַבֵּינַיִים. רַב אָשֵׁי אָמַר: אִם הָיוּ זְרוּעִין שְׁתִי — זוֹרְעָן עֵרֶב, עֵרֶב — זוֹרְעָן שְׁתִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Aren't there** seeds **between** the circles, which intermingle with the species that are in the circles? **The school of Rabbi Yannai say** in response to this: This is referring to a case **where one leaves** the space **in between** them **barren** and does not plant there. **Rav Ashi said:** He may fill the entire bed with seeds, and demarcate between the different types of seeds in the following manner. **If** the circles **were planted lengthwise, he plants** the seeds in between **widthwise;** and if the circles were planted **widthwise, he plants** the seeds in between **lengthwise.**

Furthermore, the five-by-five inner square, when positioned within the six-by-six outer space, leaves a half-*tefach* border on each side between the inner square and the outer edge. This means that between the planted corners of neighboring *arugot* — each pulling back a half-*tefach* — there is a combined gap of a full *tefach* on each side, adding up to the necessary three *tefachim* between the planted zones. The result is that by pulling in slightly and using this configuration, one can fill almost the entire garden while maintaining the required separations between species.

The *Gemara* asks: but is there not still the problem of the gaps between the corner plantings — the spaces between them (*beini u-veinayim*)? It appears to have understood that *kol ma she-yirtze* — filling as much as one wishes — included filling even those interstitial spaces. The *school of Rav Yannai* clarifies: *be-machrei beinei u-veinayim* — no, one leaves those spaces between the corners empty. That gap is precisely what provides the three *tefachim* of separation needed between neighboring species. The claim is not that every square inch is planted, but that almost everything is filled while the necessary separating gaps are preserved.

Rav Ashi offers an alternative method. *Im hayu zor'in shesi, zor'in erev; erev zor'in shesi* — if the warp is planted one way, plant the woof in the other direction; if the woof goes one way, plant the warp perpendicularly. In other words, make certain that neighboring planted zones are always oriented perpendicular to each other. The striking aspect of Tosafot's explanation of this ruling is that one can actually fill the space between adjacent patches — even the gap — as long as that fill is planted perpendicularly to the patches on either side. The visual distinction created by the perpendicular orientation is itself sufficient, even though the perpendicular planting is drawing from the same ground. This is once again the primacy of visual distinction: what matters is that the eye perceives differentiated, directionally distinct zones, regardless of what happens underground.

### Rectangle Requirement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רָבִינָא לְרַב אָשֵׁי: עֲבוֹדַת יָרָק בְּיָרָק אַחֵר, שִׁשָּׁה טְפָחִים. וְרוֹאִים אוֹתָם כְּטַבְלָא מְרוּבַּעַת. כְּטַבְלָא הוּא דִּשְׁרֵי, הָא לָאו הָכִי — אָסוּר! הָתָם לְאַקּוֹלֵי בָּהּ קוּלָּא אַחֲרִינָא, לְהַתִּיר רֹאשׁ תּוֹר הַיּוֹצֵא הֵימֶנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** We learned that **the work** space **of a vegetable** of one species, when planted with **a vegetable** of a different species, is **six handbreadths. And one views them as a** square **board.** All of the *halakhot* of planting various species were stated with regard to a square-shaped garden bed. By inference: When it is **like a** square **board, it is permitted;** and **when that is not** the case, it is **prohibited.** Planting different species in horizontal and vertical rows without a space between the species is ineffective, even with circles. Rav Ashi replied: When the *baraita* says a square board, it does not mean that the only way to demarcate between different species is when the rows are in that configuration. Rather, it is **to introduce a different leniency.** The *baraita* came **to permit a triangular plot that protrudes from it** into another field. This means that a triangular protrusion into another field is considered a conspicuous demarcation only if the bed was square; in that case, no additional measures are necessary.

Ravina challenges Rav Ashi with a *baraita* stating that when one plants different vegetables within six *tefachim* of one another, one must view them *ke-tavla meruba'as* — like a square tablet. Only when configured in a square shape is it permitted; otherwise it is forbidden. If so, the circular arrangement proposed by Rav Kahana should be problematic — a circle is not a square.

Rav Ashi responds: the *baraita* is not saying that circular shapes are forbidden. The phrase *ke-tavla meruba'as* means that when one is using square, rectangular shapes, *lakuli be-kula achrina la-hatir rosh tor ha-yotzei mimenah* — the rectangular shape's terminus produces a *rosh tor* that enables the leniency with respect to neighboring plantings. The point of the *baraita* is that the square shape is what gives rise to *rosh tor*, which is the mechanism for permitting adjacent zones. A circular arrangement does not generate a *rosh tor* in that sense — but that does not mean a circle is forbidden. It simply relies on a different mechanism: the combination of the inscribed circle, the five-by-five square, and the maintained gaps between corners. The circle is permitted by its own logic, even without recourse to *rosh tor*.

With that clarification, the *sugya* of the five-species vegetable garden, together with all its attendant debates about borders, neighboring patches, furrows, and filling strategies, reaches its conclusion.
