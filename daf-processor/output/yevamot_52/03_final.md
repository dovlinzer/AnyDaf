# Yevamot 52 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Ma'amar/Get debate

### Opening context

Today's daf is 52. We pick up on 51b, in the middle of the extended discussion concerning *ma'amar* after *ma'amar* and *get* after *get*. The most technically demanding portions of that analysis are largely behind us. What the *gemara* is doing now is clarifying how these principles interact with related questions raised earlier in the sugya.

### Rabban Gamliel's rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר מָר: עָשָׂה מַאֲמָר בָּזוֹ וּמַאֲמָר בָּזוֹ, רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אוֹמֵר: נוֹתֵן גֵּט לָרִאשׁוֹנָה וְחוֹלֵץ לָהּ, וְאָסוּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹתֶיהָ, וּמוּתָּר בִּקְרוֹבוֹת שְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Master said:** If **he performed levirate betrothal with this one and levirate betrothal with that one, Rabban Gamliel says: He gives a bill of divorce to the first one and performs *ḥalitza* with her and is forbidden to her relatives, but he is permitted to the relatives of the second one.**

When Rabban Gamliel says *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar v'ein get achar get*, the *gemara* considers a specific scenario: the *yavam* gave a *get* to two *yevamot*, and according to Rabban Gamliel he may ignore the second co-wife and proceed to perform *chalitzah* with the first. The *gemara*'s question is how this squares with Shmuel, who holds that a *chalitzah* following a *get* is a weakened *chalitzah pesulah* — because the *get* has already attenuated the *zika* — and in such a case the *chalitzah* must be extended to the other co-wife as well. Yet Rabban Gamliel rules that even in the case of *chalitzah* after *get*, the other woman is completely released. The *gemara* resolves this by attributing to Shmuel the position of *yesh zika*: the *get* weakens the *zika*, thereby weakening the *chalitzah* that follows. Rabban Gamliel, by contrast, holds *ein zika*, so the *get* has no such attenuating effect on the *chalitzah*.

### Zika disagreement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי קָסָבַר רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל אֵין מַאֲמָר אַחַר מַאֲמָר, רִאשׁוֹנָה נָמֵי תִּתְיַיבֵּם? גְּזֵירָה דִּלְמָא אָתֵי לְיַיבּוֹמֵי לִשְׁנִיָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Since Rabban Gamliel holds** that **levirate betrothal is not** effective **after levirate betrothal,** and the second levirate betrothal is of no consequence, the **first** woman **should also be permitted to enter into levirate marriage.** Why must he perform *ḥalitza* with her? It is prohibited due to a rabbinic **decree lest he perform levirate marriage with the second** woman.

The *gemara* then suggests that the *rabbanan* who disagree with Rabban Gamliel hold *yesh zika*, and examines the end of the relevant *mishnah* — the case of two *yevamin* and one *yevamah*, where both brothers gave her a *get* and the *chalitzah* is performed by only one. If the *rabbanan* hold *yesh zika*, the *chalitzah* following the *get* would be a *chalitzah pesulah*, and by the principle stated in the name of Rav Abba bar Avina in the name of Rav — that a *chalitzah pesulah* requires going back to all the brothers — each brother should have to perform *chalitzah* separately. Yet the *mishnah* indicates that one *chalitzah* suffices, which would seem to contradict Rav Abba bar Avina's ruling if the *rabbanan* hold *yesh zika*.

### Ein Zika consensus

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָךְ רַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא: בֵּין לְרַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל בֵּין לְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי אֵין זִיקָּה, וְהָכָא בְּגֵט אַחַר גֵּט וּמַאֲמָר אַחַר מַאֲמָר קָמִיפַּלְגִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara responds: **Rabba bar Rav Huna** could have **said to you: Both Rabban Gamliel and the Rabbis hold** that **the levirate bond is not substantial,** whereas my statement is in accordance with the opinion that the levirate bond is substantial. **And here** the dispute does not concern the topic of the levirate bond at all, but rather it only involves the explicitly mentioned issue: They **disagree with regard to** the efficacy of **a bill of divorce after a bill of divorce and levirate betrothal after levirate betrothal.**

Rav Abba bar Avina resolves this by stating that both Rabban Gamliel and the *rabbanan* in fact hold *ein zika*. The entire framework of a weakened *chalitzah* requiring repetition across all brothers and co-wives depends on the position of *yesh zika*, whereby the *get* drains strength from the *zika* and consequently from the *chalitzah* that follows. Since both Rabban Gamliel and the *rabbanan* reject this — they hold *ein zika* — the *get* has no impact on the *chalitzah*, the *chalitzah* is not weakened, and it is therefore not a challenge to Rav Abba bar Avina's principle. Their dispute is confined to the question of *ma'amar achar ma'amar* and *get achar get*, not to the consequences of *zika*.

## Gezeirah concern

### Amar Mar setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** כֵּיצַד? עָשָׂה מַאֲמָר כּוּ׳.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna states: **How so?** If he **performed levirate betrothal** with his *yevama* and gave her a bill of divorce, **etc.**

The *gemara* now returns to examine a previously cited statement more closely. The case: a *yavam* performed *ma'amar* with each of two co-wives. Rabban Gamliel rules: ignore the second *ma'amar* as meaningless, and treat only the first as operative. To extricate himself from the first, he gives her a *get* to dissolve the *ma'amar* and then performs *chalitzah*. He is then permitted to marry the relatives of the second woman, since the second *ma'amar* was legally void and created no relationship. He does not perform *chalitzah* with the second woman, since doing so would damage her status unnecessarily; the relevant legal relationship exists only with the first.

### Why break first?

This generates an obvious difficulty. If the second *ma'amar* was entirely meaningless — as Rabban Gamliel explicitly holds — then the first *ma'amar* remains uncompromised. Why must he break off from the first wife with a *get* and *chalitzah* at all? He should simply be permitted to proceed directly to *yibum* with her, since the second *ma'amar* never affected their relationship.

### Gezeirah rationale

The *gemara* answers: *gezeira*. Even though the *yibum* with the first wife would be entirely permitted in this case, we are concerned that observers will draw an incorrect inference. They will see that a *ma'amar* was followed by *yibum* and reason that the same logic applies to the second *ma'amar* — thus permitting *yibum* after the second *ma'amar* in some future case, which would be forbidden because one cannot establish two households simultaneously (*bayis echad hu boneh v'eino boneh shenei batim*). Once two co-wives are implicated, we require complete dissolution even according to Rabban Gamliel — not because the first relationship is legally compromised, but to preserve clarity and prevent confusion in analogous cases. The *gezeira* is admittedly somewhat far-fetched, since the present case itself presents no problem once the *yibum* is performed with one. Nevertheless, the rabbis preferred to keep the rules clean rather than risk someone drawing the wrong conclusion in a structurally similar situation.

## Ma'amar opinions

### Rabban Gamliel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל וּבֵית שַׁמַּאי וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן וּבֶן עַזַּאי וְרַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה — כּוּלְּהוּ סְבִירָא לְהוּ מַאֲמָר קוֹנֶה קִנְיָן גָּמוּר. רַבָּן גַּמְלִיאֵל — הָא דַּאֲמַרַן.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Rabban Gamliel, and Beit Shammai, and Rabbi Shimon, and ben Azzai, and Rabbi Neḥemya, they all hold** that **levirate betrothal acquires** the *yevama* as **a full-fledged acquisition,** like a regular betrothal. The source for **Rabban Gamliel's** opinion is **that which we said** above, that levirate betrothal is not effective after levirate betrothal. Rabbi Yoḥanan maintains that the second levirate betrothal is not effective because she was already fully acquired by the first one.

Rabbi Yochanan states that Rabban Gamliel, Beit Shammai, Rabbi Shimon ben Azzai, and Rabbi Nechemiah all hold, in varying formulations, that *ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur* — *ma'amar* effects a complete *kinyan*. It is important to note, as Tosafot emphasizes repeatedly, that saying all these *tannaim* hold the same position means they share a family resemblance of approach, not an identical ruling. Beginning with Rabban Gamliel: his position, as already established, is *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar*. Once a *ma'amar* has been performed, no second *ma'amar* can take hold. Within the *ma'amar* slot, the first act fills it completely and leaves no room for a subsequent one — demonstrating that *ma'amar* is a full *kinyan*.

### Beit Shammai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֵּית שַׁמַּאי — דִּתְנַן: שְׁלֹשָׁה אַחִים, שְׁנַיִם מֵהֶם נְשׂוּאִים לִשְׁתֵּי אֲחָיוֹת, וְאֶחָד מוּפְנֶה, מֵת אֶחָד מִבַּעֲלֵי אֲחָיוֹת, וְעָשָׂה בָּהּ מוּפְנֶה מַאֲמָר, וְאַחַר כָּךְ מֵת אָחִיו הַשֵּׁנִי, בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: אִשְׁתּוֹ עִמּוֹ, וְהַלֵּזוּ — תֵּצֵא מִשּׁוּם אֲחוֹת אִשָּׁה.
>
> **Translation:** The source for the opinion of **Beit Shammai** is **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of **three brothers, two** of whom **were married to two sisters, and one who was single,** the following occurred: **The husband of one of the sisters died** childless, leaving behind his wife, **and the single** brother **performed levirate betrothal** with this wife. **Afterward, the second brother died,** whereby the second brother's wife, the sister of the betrothed, happened before him for levirate marriage as well. In this case, **Beit Shammai say: His wife remains with him,** i.e., the woman he betrothed is considered like his wife, and he is not required to divorce her. **And this other leaves** the *yavam* and is exempt from levirate marriage **due to** the fact that she is **the sister of a wife.** This indicates that Beit Shammai hold that the levirate betrothal performed with the first woman makes her fully betrothed, thereby nullifying the levirate bond with her sister.

The case for Beit Shammai involves three brothers: two married to two sisters and one *mufneh* (unmarried). One of the married brothers dies. The single brother performs *ma'amar* with that brother's widow, Rachel. Then the second married brother dies, leaving his widow Leah. At this point the *yavam* has a *zika* to both women — who are sisters. Normally, since he is bound to both, he would need to perform *chalitzah* with each, since consummating with one would render the other *achos zikukaso*, a prohibited relation under *arayot*.

Beit Shammai rules: since he has already performed *ma'amar* with Rachel, it is as if he has already consummated the *yibum*. The *ma'amar* operates at a *d'oraita* level for Beit Shammai, reframing the relationship as though Rachel is already his wife. Leah therefore walks away entirely as an *ervah* — she requires no *chalitzah*. This is the paradigmatic expression of *ma'amar koneh kinyan gamur*: the *ma'amar* is treated as equivalent to consummation itself, with full *d'oraita* consequence.

### Rabbi Shimon

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן — דְּתַנְיָא: אֲמַר לְהוּ רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לַחֲכָמִים: אִם בִּיאַת רִאשׁוֹן בִּיאָה — בִּיאַת שֵׁנִי אֵינָהּ בִּיאָה. אִם בִּיאַת רִאשׁוֹן אֵינָהּ בִּיאָה — בִּיאַת שֵׁנִי נָמֵי אֵינָהּ בִּיאָה. וְהָא בִּיאַת בֶּן תֵּשַׁע, דִּכְמַאֲמָר שַׁוְּיוּהָ רַבָּנַן, וְקָאָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אֵינָהּ בִּיאָה.
>
> **Translation:** The source for the opinion of **Rabbi Shimon** is based on the following case cited in a mishna (96b): In the case of a boy aged nine years and one day old who had relations with his *yevama*, and afterward his brother, who is also nine years and one day old, had relations with her, the second brother disqualifies her from performing levirate marriage with the first one. Rabbi Shimon says he does not disqualify her. **As it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Shimon said to the Rabbis: If the intercourse of** the **first** brother is considered effective **intercourse, the intercourse of** the **second** brother **is not** considered effective **intercourse** such that it would disqualify her from performing levirate marriage with the first brother, as the first brother has already acquired her through his intercourse. **If the intercourse of** the **first** brother **is not** considered effective **intercourse, the intercourse of** the **second** brother **is also not** effective **intercourse. And the Sages considered the intercourse of a nine-year-old** boy to be **like levirate betrothal, and Rabbi Shimon says** that the intercourse of the second boy **is not** considered **intercourse.** This proves that in his opinion the intercourse of a nine-year-old fully acquires the *yevama*, and similarly, so does levirate betrothal.

The case for Rabbi Shimon involves two brothers who are both under the age of Bar Mitzvah — nine years old and a day — who each had relations with a *yevamah*. The *halacha* establishes that *bias katan* (intercourse by a minor) is treated by the rabbis as equivalent to *ma'amar*: it is not a full act of *yibum*, since the boy has not reached adulthood, but it carries sufficient legal significance to be treated as a rabbinic *ma'amar*.

If both underage brothers had relations with the same *yevamah*, this constitutes the analog of *ma'amar achar ma'amar*. The *chachamim* hold that both must give a *get*. Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *im bias rishon bia, bias sheni einah bia* — if the first act was legally meaningful, the second is not. And *v'im bias rishon einah bia, bias sheni nami einah bia* — if the first is not meaningful at all, neither is the second. Either way, there is no need to address the second brother's act. This is the direct parallel to Rabban Gamliel's *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar*, applied to the domain of *bias katan*.

### Ben Azzai

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בֶּן עַזַּאי — דְּתַנְיָא, בֶּן עַזַּאי אוֹמֵר: יֵשׁ מַאֲמָר אַחַר מַאֲמָר בִּשְׁנֵי יְבָמִין וִיבָמָה אַחַת, וְאֵין מַאֲמָר אַחַר מַאֲמָר בִּשְׁתֵּי יְבָמוֹת וְיָבָם אֶחָד.
>
> **Translation:** This source for **ben Azzai's** opinion is **as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **ben Azzai says: Levirate betrothal is** effective **after levirate betrothal** in the case of **two *yevamin* and one *yevama*, but levirate betrothal is not** effective **after levirate betrothal** in the case of **two *yevamot* and one *yavam*.** Because the latter case involves only one *yavam*, his levirate betrothal fully acquires the *yevama*, and therefore the levirate betrothal he performs with the second woman is of no account, as he is already betrothed to the first *yevama*.

Ben Azzai's position is more nuanced. He holds *yesh ma'amar achar ma'amar bishnei yevamin v'yevamah achas* — if there are two brothers-in-law and one *yevamah*, the second *ma'amar* (from the second brother) is meaningful. However, *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar bishtei yevamot v'yavam echad* — if there is one *yavam* and two *yevamot*, once he has used his *ma'amar* on one woman, he cannot perform a valid *ma'amar* on the other. There is a dispute between Rashi and Tosafot over whether the *girsa* should be read in the opposite direction. The underlying question is which configuration better fits the *kiddushin* model: intuitively, it makes more sense that a woman who is already *mekudeshes* cannot be the object of a second *ma'amar* from another man, but the *girsa* and its reversal are debated. Whichever reading one accepts, Ben Azzai agrees with Rabban Gamliel in at least one configuration of *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar*.

### Rabbi Nechemiah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה — דִּתְנַן, רַבִּי נְחֶמְיָה אוֹמֵר: אַחַת בְּעִילָה, וְאַחַת חֲלִיצָה, בֵּין בַּתְּחִלָּה בֵּין בָּאֶמְצַע בֵּין בַּסּוֹף — אֵין אַחֲרֶיהָ כְּלוּם. וְהָא בִּיאָה פְּסוּלָה, דִּכְמַאֲמָר שַׁוְּיוּהָ רַבָּנַן, וְקָתָנֵי: אֵין אַחֲרֶיהָ כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The source for **Rabbi Neḥemya's** opinion is **as we learned** in a mishna that **Rabbi Neḥemya says:** With regard to **both intercourse and *ḥalitza*, whether at the beginning, in the middle, or at the end, nothing is** effective **after it. And the Sages considered invalid intercourse** to be **like levirate betrothal, and** Rabbi Neḥemya **teaches** that **nothing is** effective **after it.** This indicates that he maintains that no form of acquisition is effective after levirate betrothal, as levirate betrothal completely acquires the *yevama*.

Rabbi Nechemiah states: *achas bia v'achas chalitza bein batchila bein ba-emtza bein basof, ein achareiha klum* — whether it is *bia* or *chalitza*, whether performed at the beginning, middle, or end of the process, nothing counts afterward. This is more sweeping than what we have established elsewhere. We have generally said that *bia pesulah* (defective intercourse) is treated rabinically as equivalent to *ma'amar*, and that something may still be done afterward. Rabbi Nechemiah disagrees: even a *bia pesulah* in the middle of the process terminates it entirely. The *gemara* notes that this position presupposes *ein ma'amar achar ma'amar*, since *bia pesulah* has been assimilated to *ma'amar*, and Rabbi Nechemiah holds that once that has occurred, nothing further can follow. It should be noted that Rabbi Nechemiah's view is not identical to Rabban Gamliel's — these *tannaim* share the general orientation that *ma'amar* or its equivalent forecloses what follows, but the specific applications differ.

## Ma'amar mechanics

### Keitzad meaning

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַאי גֵּט אַחַר גֵּט הוּא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, הָכִי קָאָמַר: גֵּט אַחַר הַגֵּט, וּמַאֲמָר אַחַר מַאֲמָר — כְּדַאֲמַרַן, יָבָם אֶחָד וִיבָמָה אַחַת כֵּיצַד הַתָּרָתָן? עָשָׂה מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ וְנָתַן לָהּ גֵּט — צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ חֲלִיצָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara is puzzled: Is **this case** upon which the mishna elaborates the case of **a bill of divorce after a bill of divorce** that is mentioned first in the mishna? The mishna first is referring to a bill of divorce after a bill of divorce, but when it comes to the specifics, it mentions a bill of divorce after levirate betrothal. **Rav Yehuda said: This is what** the *tanna* **is saying:** With regard to **a bill of divorce after a bill of divorce, and levirate betrothal after levirate betrothal,** it is **as we said** and these cases were explained in the *baraita*, but with regard to **one *yavam* and one *yevama*, how is their** complex bond **released?** He then proceeds to delineate: If **he performed levirate betrothal with his *yevama* and gave her a bill of divorce, she requires *ḥalitza* from him.**

The *mishnah* now turns to the question *keitzad asa ma'amar* — how is *ma'amar* performed? But before the *gemara* can address the mechanics, it must clarify what the *mishnah* is doing. The section proceeds to discuss hybrid cases — *ma'amar* followed by *get*, *get* followed by *chalitzah*, and so forth — rather than the pairings of *ma'amar* after *ma'amar* or *get* after *get* that dominated the preceding discussion. The *gemara* notes the apparent incongruity: the *mishnah* says *keitzad* as though elaborating on *get achar get*, yet it goes on to discuss different acts combined with each other. Rav Yehuda clarifies: the debate between Rabban Gamliel and the *chachamim* regarding *get achar get* and *ma'amar achar ma'amar* is what was already stated. *Keitzad* introduces the hybrid cases — one *yavam* and one *yevamah* — where two qualitatively different acts are combined, and asks what the implications of each sequence are.

### Basic sequences

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עָשָׂה מַאֲמָר וּבָעַל — הֲרֵי זוֹ כְּמִצְוָתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches: If the *yavam* **performed levirate betrothal and engaged in intercourse, this is** done **in accordance with its mitzva.**

In all hybrid cases, both Rabban Gamliel and the *chachamim* agree that the second act remains meaningful, since it is of a different type than the first. The *mishnah* then proceeds through the various combinations. *Asa ma'amar v'natan lah get, tzricha heimenu chalitzah* — if he began with *ma'amar* and then gave a *get*, he has not concluded the process and she still requires *chalitzah* from him. This is the standard incomplete-release scenario. The *mishnah* also addresses *ma'amar* followed by *yibum*: *asa ma'amar u'va'al harei zu kemitzvatah* — if he performed *ma'amar* and then consummated the *yibum*, this is the proper procedure. Just as *kiddushin* precedes *nisuin* in ordinary marriage, here the *ma'amar* precedes and frames the act of *yibum*.

### Rav Huna

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לֵימָא מְסַיַּיע לֵיהּ לְרַב הוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִצְוַת יְבָמָה — מְקַדֵּשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ בּוֹעֵל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Let us say that** this mishna **supports** the statement of **Rav Huna. As Rav Huna said: The mitzva of a *yevama* is** properly performed when the *yavam* **betroths** the *yevama* **and** only **afterward engages in intercourse.**

The *gemara* asks whether the phrase *harei zu kemitzvatah* supports Rav Huna's ruling. Rav Huna states: *mitzvat yavam mekadesh v'achar kach bo'el* — the ideal manner of performing *yibum* is first to perform *ma'amar* (the rabbinic analog of *kiddushin*) and only then to have intercourse, mirroring the structure of normal marriage in which *kiddushin* precedes *nisuin*. The logic is compelling not only formally but substantively: one should not begin the relationship with the physical act. The relationship ought to be framed legally and relationally before the intimacy follows — even where the *d'oraita* act is the intercourse itself and the *ma'amar* is purely rabbinic.

### Reframing question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** סָלְקָא דַּעְתָּךְ אָמֵינָא: כֵּיוָן דְּאָמַר מָר הָעוֹשֶׂה מַאֲמָר בִּיבִמְתּוֹ — פָּרְחָה הֵימֶנּוּ זִיקַּת יְבָמִין, וְחָלָה עָלֶיהָ זִיקַּת אֵרוּסִין וְנִשּׂוּאִין, אִימָא לָאו מִצְוָה קָעָבֵיד, קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** It was indeed necessary for the mishna to teach us this, for it might **enter your mind to say** that **since the Master said** above (29b): **One who performs levirate betrothal with his *yevama* causes the levirate bond to dissipate from himself, and a** standard **bond of betrothal and marriage takes effect on him,** you might **say** that he **no** longer **performs a mitzva** when he carries out levirate marriage, as the levirate bond is no longer in place. The *tanna* therefore **teaches us** that we consider the levirate betrothal and the subsequent intercourse as part of the same process, which constitutes a mitzva.

The *gemara* raises a concern: when *ma'amar* precedes *yibum*, does this not reframe the entire relationship such that it no longer looks like *yibum* at all? Once the *ma'amar* is performed, the *gemara* reasons, *parchah himenu zikat yavam v'chalah aleha zikat eirusin* — the *yibum* bond falls away and is replaced by an *erusin* bond. The woman is no longer treated as a *yevamah* but as an *arusah* in the ordinary sense. If that is so, perhaps the ideal procedure should avoid *ma'amar* before *yibum*, lest it transform the relationship into something that loses its character as *yibum*.

### Acceptable answer

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתַּנֵּי נָמֵי אֵין אַחַר בִּיאָה כְּלוּם. אַבָּיֵי וְרָבָא דְּאָמְרִי תַּרְוַיְיהוּ: תָּנֵי אֵין אַחַר בִּיאָה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara dismisses this concern by noting that the mishna can also be read as indicating that **even this is an acceptable, indeed praiseworthy, manner of performing the *mitzvah*.** The reframing is not a problem; it is in fact the preferred result. [The Gemara notes that later authorities would comment on the blessing of *sheva brachot* and ketubah recited with this arrangement.]

The *gemara* dismisses this concern: *af zo kemitzvatah* — even this is an acceptable, indeed praiseworthy, manner of performing the *mitzvah*. The reframing is not a problem; it is in fact the preferred result. Remarkably, the Rambam rules (*Mishneh Torah, Hilchot Yibum*) that when a *yavam* does *ma'amar* with his *yevamah*, he recites *birkat nisuin* — *sheva brachot* — with ten men present, and writes a *ketubah*. The act is treated in every external respect exactly like a normal marriage. The underlying *d'oraita* category remains *yibum*, but the rabbinic overlay of *ma'amar* causes the relationship to present itself in precisely the form of ordinary *kiddushin* and *nisuin*, and according to the Rambam this is entirely the point.

## Rav's lashes

### Lashes question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גּוּפָא, אָמַר רַב הוּנָא: מִצְוַת יְבָמִין — מְקַדֵּשׁ וְאַחַר כָּךְ בּוֹעֵל, וְאִם בָּעַל וְעָשָׂה מַאֲמָר — קָנָה. אִם בָּעַל וְעָשָׂה מַאֲמָר — פְּשִׁיטָא, דְּהָא קַנְיַהּ בְּבִיאָה! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: אִם בָּעַל בְּלֹא מַאֲמָר — קָנָה. וְהָתַנְיָא לוֹקֶה!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues: With regard to **the** matter **itself, Rav Huna said: The mitzva of levirate marriage** is properly performed when the *yavam* **betroths** the *yevama* **and afterward engages in intercourse, and if he engaged in intercourse and** later **performed levirate betrothal, he has acquired** the *yevama*. The Gemara is puzzled: **If he engaged in intercourse and** then **performed levirate betrothal it is obvious** that he has acquired her, **as he has** already **acquired her by intercourse.** The levirate betrothal does not affect the issue one way or another. **Rather, say** as follows: **If he engaged in intercourse without** prior **levirate betrothal,** even in that case **he has acquired** her. The Gemara challenges this: **But isn't it taught** in a *baraita* that a *yavam* who has intercourse without levirate betrothal **is flogged?**

The *gemara* cites Rav Huna's full statement: *mekadesh v'achar kach bo'el* is the ideal; however, *sheim ba v'lo asah ma'amar kanah* — if he had intercourse without having performed *ma'amar* at all, the *yibum* is nonetheless valid. The *gemara* immediately questions this: *peshita*, of course the *yibum* takes effect — *kaniah be'via*. The question is therefore refined: the point is that even if he reversed the order entirely, having intercourse without ever performing *ma'amar*, it still works. But the *gemara* counters with a *baraita*: such a person receives *makat mardut* — rabbinic lashes for defying the rabbinic norm.

### Makat Mardut

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַכַּת מַרְדּוּת מִדְּרַבָּנַן. דְּרַב מְנַגֵּיד מַאן דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּבִיאָה, וּמַאן דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּשׁוּקָא, וּמַאן דִּמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּלָא שִׁדּוּכֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The lashes are not because the *yibum* is invalid. The *yibum* is entirely valid *d'oraita*. Rather, the lashes are **lashes for rebelliousness given for transgressing a rabbinic law,** namely for acting in an immodest manner. The Gemara cites other instances where the Sages administered lashes for immodest behavior. **As Rav** would **flog one who betroths** a woman **by intercourse,** despite the fact that betrothal is effective by this method, because he acted in a promiscuous manner. **And** he would likewise **flog one who betroths** a woman **in the marketplace,** rather than at home, as this too is loose behavior, **and** he would also administer lashes **to one who betroths** a woman **without** a prior **marriage agreement.**

The lashes are not because the *yibum* is invalid. The *yibum* is entirely valid *d'oraita*. Rather, the lashes are *makat mardut midrabbanan* — punishment for contravening the rabbinic preference for *ma'amar* to precede intercourse. The institution of *ma'amar* was created precisely so that *yibum* not be inaugurated purely by the physical act, mirroring the rabbinic insistence in ordinary marriage that *kiddushin* precede *nisuin*. Bypassing this framework violates the rabbinic expectation, even though it does not invalidate the underlying transaction.

### Broader principle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמַאן דִּמְבַטֵּל גִּיטָּא, וּמַאן דְּמָסַר מוֹדָעָא אַגִּיטָּא. [דְּמִתְפַּקַּר בִּשְׁלוּחָא] דְרַבָּנַן, וּמַאן דִּ[מְ]שַׁהֵי שַׁמְתָּא דְרַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ תְּלָתִין יוֹמִין, וְלָא אָתֵי לְבֵי דִינָא וְתָבַע לְשַׁמְתֵּיהּ. וְעַל חַתְנָא דְּדָאֵיר בְּבֵי חֲמוּהִי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** he would further lash **one who nullifies a bill of divorce** he had earlier sent by declaring in the presence of witnesses that the bill of divorce is nullified. This action is effective, but by doing so he transgresses the rabbinic ordinance of the Sages that bans such an action as it might lead his wife to unlawfully wed another. **And** he would also flog **one who delivers a declaration** preemptively invalidating **a bill of divorce,** by informing three people before giving a bill of divorce that he is not doing so of his own free will and he wants to cancel it ahead of time. Here too he will mislead his wife, who will assume it is a valid bill of divorce. [The text continues with additional cases where Rav administered lashes.]

This connects to a broader principle of Rav's, who would administer rabbinic lashes in several analogous situations. Rav would give *malkut* to one who performed *kiddushin* by means of *bia* — using intercourse as the act of *kiddushin* rather than *kesef* or *shtar*. This is striking, since *kiddushei bia* is itself a biblical institution — the first *mishnah* of *Kiddushin* lists *kesef, shtar, v'via* as the three modes of *kiddushin*. Yet Rav held this to be morally and socially unacceptable, regardless of its legal validity *d'oraita*. His principle: sex should not inaugurate the relationship; the legal commitment should precede the physical one. Similarly, Rav would give lashes for one who performed *kiddushin be-shuka* — giving *kesef kiddushin* in a public marketplace. Whatever the precise objection (whether it is the lack of intimacy in the setting, the impulsiveness it suggests, or the objectifying nature of a public transaction), the act is deemed improper. And likewise for one who performed *kiddushin belo shidukhei* — without any prior courtship or engagement process. Just as the physical relationship should be preceded by the legal commitment, the legal commitment (*kiddushin*) should itself be preceded by a considered process of *shidukhim*. One should not leap directly into *kiddushin* without having built a relationship first.

Rav extends these lashes further: to one who effects *bittul* of a *get* when the *shaliach* is not present (causing the woman to believe she is divorced when she is not); to one who submits a *moda'a* before signing a *get* (a prior declaration that the document he is about to sign is given under duress and therefore invalid — Tosafot notes that even where these acts may technically fail on legal grounds, the mere doing of them casts doubt on the woman's status and constitutes *motzi la'az* on the *get*); to one who behaves contemptuously toward a rabbinic messenger; and to one who has had a *niduy* placed on him for thirty days without appearing before the court to resolve it. Finally, Rav addresses one who lives in his father-in-law's house, where the concern is proximity to his mother-in-law in her husband's absence. The *gemara* qualifies: *dedayer in, dechalif lau* — only one who actually resides there, not one who merely passes by. The case of Rav Sheshet, who administered lashes to someone who was merely passing the door of his father-in-law's house, is explained differently: that individual was already under suspicion of an improper relationship, and the frequent appearances made the concern concrete.

### Nahardai limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נְהַרְדָּעֵי אָמְרִי: בְּכוּלְּהוּ לָא מְנַגֵּיד רַב, אֶלָּא לִמְקַדֵּשׁ בְּבִיאָה וּבְלָא שִׁדּוּכֵי. וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי: אֲפִילּוּ בְּשִׁדּוּכֵי נָמֵי מִשּׁוּם פְּרִיצוּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Sages **of Neharde'a** would **say: In all** these cases **Rav would not flog, apart from** the case of one who **betrothed by intercourse and without** a prior **marriage agreement. And there are** those **who say** he would flog a man who betrothed by intercourse **even** if he did so **with** a prior **marriage agreement, due to the immorality** involved, as he must invite witnesses to observe the act.

The *nahardeai* qualify Rav's practice significantly: in none of these cases would Rav actually administer lashes except when two factors converged — *kiddushin* performed via *bia* and the complete absence of *shidukhim*. Only when the intercourse was the very first act, with no prior courtship whatsoever, did Rav act. A dissenting view among the *nahardeai* holds that even when *shidukhim* did precede, if the *kiddushin* were effected via *bia* it is still *mishum pritzuta* — licentious — and lashes are appropriate. This latter view coheres with the general orientation noted earlier: the insistence that *ma'amar* precede *yibum*, the parallel insistence that *kiddushin* precede marital intercourse, and the further insistence that *shidukhim* precede *kiddushin* — all reflect a consistent rabbinic concern that relationships be built in ordered stages, each stage providing the framework within which the next properly occurs.

## Ma'amar wording

### Cash/Shtar method

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: כֵּיצַד מַאֲמָר? נָתַן לָהּ כֶּסֶף אוֹ שָׁוֶה כֶּסֶף. וּבִשְׁטָר כֵּיצַד? בִּשְׁטָר כֵּיצַד?! כְּדַאֲמַרַן: כָּתַב לָהּ עַל הַנְּיָיר אוֹ עַל הַחֶרֶס, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין בּוֹ שָׁוֶה פְּרוּטָה: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְקוּדֶּשֶׁת לִי״!
>
> **Translation:** § **The Sages taught: How** is **levirate betrothal** performed? **He gives her money or the equivalent** value of **money** and declares: You are hereby betrothed to me. The Gemara asks: **And with a document, how** does he betroth her? The Gemara is puzzled by this question: **With a document, how** does he betroth her? It is **as we have said** by the *halakhot* of a regular document of betrothal: If **he wrote to her on paper or on earthenware, even though it is not worth a *peruta*,** the words: **You are hereby betrothed to me,** it is effective.

A *baraita* now addresses the mechanics of *ma'amar*: *natan lah kesef o shaveh kesef* — he gives her money or something worth money, precisely as in regular *kiddushin*. Rashi understands that accompanying words are required — something like *hitkadshi li* or *hitkadshi li b'ma'amar yevamin* — though the *gemara* itself focuses on the act of giving rather than specifying the formula. The *gemara* proceeds to ask about *ma'amar* by *shtar*: if a *get* of a *yevamah* is rabbinic, is the *shtar* of *ma'amar* analogous to a normal *shtar kiddushin*? The answer given is *ki-de-amran* — yes, like a standard *shtar kiddushin*, written on paper or pottery, worth less than a *perutah*, bearing the formula *harei at mekudeshet li*. The *gemara* does not posit different language for *ma'amar* in this context.

### Shtar wording

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי, הָכִי קָאָמַר: שְׁטַר כְּתוּבַּת יְבָמִין כֵּיצַד? כָּתַב לַהּ: ״אֲנָא פְּלוֹנִי בַּר פְּלוֹנִי קַבֵּילִית יָת פְּלוֹנִית יְבִמְתִּי עֲלַי, לָזוּן וּלְפַרְנְסָהּ כָּרָאוּי, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁתְּהֵא כְּתוּבָּתָהּ עַל נִכְסֵי בַּעְלָהּ הָרִאשׁוֹן״. וְאִי לֵית לַהּ מֵרִאשׁוֹן — תַּקִּינוּ לַהּ רַבָּנַן מִשֵּׁנִי, כְּדֵי שֶׁלֹּא תְּהֵא קַלָּה בְּעֵינָיו לְהוֹצִיאָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye **said** that **this is what** the *baraita* **is saying:** With regard to **the document of a marriage contract for levirate marriage, how** is it written? The Gemara explains that **he writes to her: I, so-and-so, son of so-and-so, have accepted so-and-so, my *yevama* upon me, to feed and maintain her in a fitting manner, provided that her marriage contract will** still **be** payable **from the property of her first husband** and not from the property of the *yavam*. The Gemara adds: **But if the first** husband **does not have** property, **the Sages instituted for her** that she should receive her marriage contract **from the second** husband, i.e., the *yavam*, for the same reason that they instituted the marriage contract in the first place: **So that she will not be demeaned in his eyes such that** he will easily **divorce her.**

Abaye reframes the question: the *mishnah*'s inquiry about *shtar* is actually asking how one writes a *ketubah* for *yibum* — *shtar ketuvas yevamin keitzad*? The *ketubah* obligation has now transferred to the *yavam*, and a new document must be written. The formula: *ana ploni bar ploni kabbaleit yat plonit yevamti alai la-zun u-le-farnesah ke-ra'uy* — I, so-and-so son of so-and-so, accept so-and-so, my *yevamah*, upon me, to sustain and support her as is fitting. This constitutes the ongoing *tenai ketubah*. The *gemara* adds: the lien for collecting the *ketubah* falls on the estate of the first husband — the *ketubah* obligation follows the original matrimonial bond. If the first estate lacks sufficient assets, the *rabbanan* established that she may collect from the second husband, *kedei she-lo tehei kallah b'einav le-hotzi'ah* — so that it should not be easy for him to dismiss her without financial consequence.

### Get wording test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָא מִינֵּיהּ אַבָּיֵי מֵרַבָּה: נָתַן לָהּ גֵּט, וְאָמַר: ״הֲרֵי אַתְּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת הֵימֶנִּי, וְאִי אַתְּ מוּתֶּרֶת לְכׇל אָדָם״. מַהוּ? גֵּט יְבָמָה דְּרַבָּנַן הוּא, גֵּט דְּמַהֲנֵי בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ — מַהֲנֵי בִּיבָמָה, גֵּט דְּלָא מַהֲנֵי בְּאֵשֶׁת אִישׁ — לָא מַהֲנֵי בִּיבָמָה, אוֹ דִלְמָא אָתֵי לְאִחַלּוֹפֵי בְּגִיטָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye inquired of Rabba:** If a *yavam* **gave** his *yevama* **a bill of divorce, and said** the following formula: **You are hereby divorced from me but you are not permitted to any** other **man, what is** the status of such a bill of divorce? **Is the bill of divorce of a *yevama*** a bill of divorce **based on rabbinic** law and therefore subject to the *halakhot* of a regular bill of divorce; and consequently, **a bill of divorce that is effective for a married woman is** also **effective for a *yevama*,** and **a bill of divorce that is not effective for a married woman is not effective for a *yevama*?** Since this type of a divorce is invalid in the case of a married woman, it is similarly ineffective in the case of a *yevama*. **Or perhaps** the Sages were concerned that **perhaps** people **will come to confuse** this bill of divorce **with** an unqualified **bill of divorce** given by a *yavam* and they therefore decreed that it should affect the levirate bond, preventing the *yavam* from marrying the *yevama*.

Abaye poses a question to Rabba: if the *yavam* said to the *yevamah*, *harei at megureshet mimmeni v'i at muteret le-chol adam* — "you are divorced from me, but not permitted to anyone else" — does this constitute a valid *get* for the purposes of *yibum*? This formulation would be meaningless in an ordinary marital *get*, since a *get* that fails to render the woman *muteret le-chol adam* does not fully dissolve the marriage for all purposes. But the *get* of a *yevamah* is rabbinic in nature, and its function is specific: it operates to prevent *yibum*, to *pasul* her from the other brothers, and to implicate the co-wives. The question is whether this half-formulated expression carries enough *get*-like character to trigger those consequences.

### Reiach HaGet

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רַבָּה בַּר חָנָן: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, יָהֵיב לַהּ נְיָירָא בְּעָלְמָא — הָכִי נָמֵי דְּפַסְלַהּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הָתָם לָא פָּסֵיל בִּכְהוּנָּה, הָכָא קָפָסֵיל בִּכְהוּנָּה, דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְאִשָּׁה גְּרוּשָׁה מֵאִישָׁהּ לֹא יִקָּחוּ״, אֲפִילּוּ לֹא נִתְגָּרְשָׁה אֶלָּא מֵאִישָׁהּ — ״לֹא יִקָּחוּ״, וְהַיְינוּ רֵיחַ הַגֵּט שֶׁפּוֹסֵל בִּכְהוּנָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabba bar Ḥanan strongly objects to this: However, if that is so,** that we are stringent with the bill of divorce of a *yevama* out of concern that people might confuse the two types of bills of divorce, if **he gives her a mere** piece of **paper,** which does not mention divorce, **so too will it disqualify her? He said to him: There** it is different, as a mere piece of paper has no effect on any other woman, for **it does not disqualify** her **for** marrying into **the priesthood.** If a husband gives his wife a piece of paper that contains nothing about divorce, even if he says: This is a bill of divorce, his action is of no consequence, not even to forbid her to a priest. **Here,** however, a bill of divorce of this kind at least **disqualifies** a woman from marrying **into the priesthood. As it is taught** in a *baraita*: "They shall not take a woman that is a harlot, or profaned; **and a woman divorced from her husband they shall not take,** as he is holy to his God" (Leviticus 21:7). This verse lists the women whom a priest is prohibited from marrying. From this verse it can be inferred: **Even** if **she was only divorced from her husband** who said when giving her a bill of divorce: You are divorced from me, but did not permit her to other men, even such a woman **they may not take** in marriage. Although a bill of divorce of this kind does not permit the woman to others, it does suffice to prohibit her from marrying a priest. **And this is** what is referred to as **the trace of a bill of divorce, which disqualifies** a woman from marrying **into the priesthood.**

Rabba bar Chanan objects: if we say that anything resembling a *get* carries its consequences, one could argue that handing over a blank piece of paper while saying *harei zeh gittech* would equally *pasul* her. Surely there must be some minimum standard.

The *gemara* distinguishes: *hatam lo pasil bekhuna, hacha ka pasil bekhuna*. The relevant criterion is whether the act is sufficient to render the woman a *gerusha* with respect to *kehuna*. The *gemara* cites the *baraita*: *v'isha gerusha meisha, lo yikachu* — the prohibition on a *kohen* marrying a divorced woman applies to one who is *gerusha me'isha*, divorced from her husband, even if not *muteret le-chol adam*. The Talmudic derivation — *afilu lo nitgareshah ela meisha* — indicates that the one-sided formula *harei at megureshet mimmeni* is sufficient to confer the status of *gerusha* at a rabbinic level for purposes of *kehuna*, even though *d'oraita* the marriage remains intact.

This is the concept of *reiach ha-get* — the "smell of a *get*." A document or act that shares enough of the characteristics of a *get*, even if technically invalid, may carry sufficient *get*-like quality to produce certain rabbinic consequences: rendering a woman a *gerusha* for *kehuna* purposes, and — as the present discussion shows — having real legal effect in the domain of *hilchos yibum*. Not all *gittin psulim* possess *reiach ha-get*; determining which defective *getin* have sufficient formal integrity to be treated as carrying this "smell" is a question that will recur throughout *masechet Gittin*. What this passage establishes is that *reiach ha-get* operates in two distinct areas simultaneously: *issur kehuna* and the laws of *yibum*.

## Future Get questions

### Get for Arusah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: הֲרֵי אָמְרוּ, אָמַר אֶחָד לְלַבְלָר: כְּתוֹב גֵּט לַאֲרוּסָתִי, לִכְשֶׁאֶכְנְסֶנָּה אֲגָרְשֶׁנָּה — הֲרֵי זֶה גֵּט, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁבְּיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said: They said** that **if one said to a scribe** [***lavlar***]: **Write a bill of divorce for my betrothed** now, **such that when I marry her I will divorce her** with the bill of divorce, if he indeed gave her this bill of divorce after their marriage **it is** a valid **bill of divorce.** Why? **Because it is** already **in his power to divorce her** while she is betrothed to him, and therefore the bill of divorce written during their betrothal is valid.

Rami bar Chama introduces a series of questions by first laying out established rules of *hilchos gittin*. If a man told a scribe to write a *get* for his *arusah* — a woman to whom he had already performed *kiddushin* — with the intention of divorcing her after *nisuin*, the *get* is valid even though written before *nisuin*. The reasoning: *mipnei she-beyado le-garshah* — he could have given it to her immediately, since one may divorce an *arusah*. The fact that he intended to wait does not affect the validity of the writing. By contrast, if he instructed a scribe to write a *get* for a woman with whom he had no current marital relationship — merely intending to betroth her in the future — the *get* is invalid: *mipnei she-ein beyado le-garshah* — he has no current power to divorce her, and a *get* requires a present relationship to which it is addressed.

### Get for stranger

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְאִשָּׁה בְּעָלְמָא — אֵין גֵּט, מִפְּנֵי שֶׁאֵין בְּיָדוֹ לְגָרְשָׁהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **But** if he wrote a bill of divorce **for** a **regular woman** who has no connection to him, even if he later married her, it is **not a** valid **bill of divorce.** This is **because** at the time of the writing of the bill of divorce, **it is not in his power to divorce her,** and therefore the bill of divorce is of no consequence.

These two cases establish the principle: the validity of a *get* written before the marital bond it is intended to dissolve depends on whether the relationship exists at the time of writing. A *get* written for an *arusah* is valid because the bond of *kiddushin* is already in place; a *get* written for a woman with whom no relationship yet exists is not.

### Yevama Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא: לִיבִמְתּוֹ, מַהוּ? כֵּיוָן דַּאֲגִידָא בֵּיהּ — כַּאֲרוּסָתוֹ דָּמְיָא, אוֹ דִלְמָא: כֵּיוָן דְּלָא עֲבַד בַּהּ מַאֲמָר — לָא? תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** All this is evident, but in light of the above *halakhot* **Rami bar Ḥama inquires:** If he wrote a bill of divorce **for his *yevama*** and did not give it to her immediately, but only after they were married, **what is** the *halakha*? Is it a valid bill of divorce or not? The Gemara clarifies the options: On the one hand, **since she is tied to him** she is **considered like his betrothed; or perhaps, since he has not** yet **performed levirate betrothal with her,** she is **not** even considered the equivalent of his betrothed, and the bill of divorce is not effective? The Gemara does not have a resolution for this and states that the question **shall stand** unresolved.

Rami bar Chama now poses his question: *le-yevamto mahu*? A man's brother has died; he has a *yevamah* bound to him by *zika*. May he instruct a scribe to write a *get* now, planning to give it after performing *ma'amar*? Is the *zika* sufficient to treat her as analogous to an *arusah*, such that a *get* written at the stage of *zika* is valid for use after *ma'amar*? Or is the *zika* alone insufficient — no *get* can be written because she is not yet truly his in any legally actionable sense?

Rashi suggests the question may alternatively concern whether a *get* can be written at the stage of *zika* for use after *yibum* itself has been performed, but the more straightforward reading keeps the discussion within the rabbinic plane of *zika*, *ma'amar*, and *get*.

### Yevama status

Two positions emerge. On one view: *keivan de-agidah bei ka-arusato damya* — since she is bound and knotted to him via *zika*, she is comparable to an *arusah* and the *get* written now would be valid. On the other view: *keivan de-lo avad bah ma'amar*, since he has not yet performed *ma'amar*, there is no real relationship yet, and the *get* is meaningless. The *gemara* leaves this unresolved: *teiku*.

## Partial Get

### Divided Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בָּעֵי רַב חֲנַנְיָה: כָּתַב גֵּט לְזִיקָּתוֹ וְלֹא לְמַאֲמָרוֹ, לְמַאֲמָרוֹ וְלֹא לְזִיקָּתוֹ, מַהוּ? מַאֲמָר עִילָּוֵי זִיקָה קָא רְמֵי, וְהָוֵה לֵיהּ כִּמְגָרֵשׁ חֲצִי אִשָּׁה, וְהַמְגָרֵשׁ חֲצִי אִשָּׁה לֹא עָשָׂה וְלֹא כְלוּם, אוֹ דִּלְמָא: הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ḥananya inquires:** If **he wrote a bill of divorce for his levirate bond,** by specifying in the bill of divorce that he is thereby severing the levirate bond between them, **but not for his levirate betrothal,** or if he wrote a bill of divorce **for his levirate betrothal and** stated that it is **not for his levirate bond, what is** the *halakha*? Is the **levirate betrothal superimposed upon the levirate bond,** so that they are considered a single, continuous link, **and he is like one who divorces half a woman, and one who divorces half a woman has done nothing; or perhaps this stands on its own and that stands on its own,** and he can therefore cancel either the levirate bond or the levirate betrothal separately.

Rav Chanina raises a further question: *katav get le-zikato v'lo le-ma'amaro, le-ma'amaro v'lo le-zikato* — after a *ma'amar* has been performed, the *yavam* writes a *get* and declares that it is intended to sever only the *ma'amar* component of his relationship, not the *zika*. Or alternatively, only the *zika* but not the *ma'amar*. Is such a targeted *get* legally effective?

To understand the stakes: if the *get* attacks only the *zika*, it would in principle prevent *yibum* entirely (since *yibum* depends on *zika*) and perhaps affect the co-wives, even though the *ma'amar* remains nominally in place. If the *get* attacks only the *ma'amar*, it restores the situation to pre-*ma'amar* *zika* — the woman remains a *yevamah* but the rabbinic *ma'amar* layer is peeled away.

### Half-divorce

The initial analysis frames this as analogous to divorcing half a woman: *v'havay lei ke-migaresh chatzi isha, ha-migaresh chatzi isha lo asa v'lo klum* — the woman carries a double-layered status (*zika* and *ma'amar*), and trying to dissolve only one stratum while leaving the other intact is like divorcing half a wife — a legal nullity. The *get* would have no effect whatsoever, leaving intact both the *zika* and the *ma'amar*, and theoretically leaving open the possibility of *yibum* afterward.

### Independence theory

The counterposition: *o dilma hai lachud v'hai lachud* — perhaps *zika* and *ma'amar* are independent legal statuses. They were created by distinct acts and occupy distinct legal registers. If so, a *get* directed at one could validly sever that one, even without addressing the other.

### Rava precedent

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִפְשׁוֹט לֵיהּ מִדְּרָבָא, דְּאָמַר רָבָא: נָתַן גֵּט לְמַאֲמָרוֹ — הוּתְּרָה צָרָתָהּ!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** why do you not **resolve** the dilemma **from** a teaching of **Rava? For Rava said:** If a *yavam* **gave a bill of divorce for his levirate betrothal, her rival wife is permitted.** The levirate betrothal he had previously performed is thereby canceled, which leaves the bond intact, and the rival wife of this *yevama* remains available for levirate marriage. This indicates that levirate betrothal and the levirate bond are not connected.

The *gemara* suggests that Rava's ruling offers support. Rava states: *natan get le-ma'amaro hutra tzarata* — if after performing *ma'amar* with one co-wife, the *yavam* gives a *get* addressed to the *ma'amar*, it frees the co-wife and returns the situation to its prior state. This shows that a *get* can operate exclusively against the *ma'amar* layer, without simultaneously addressing the *zika*. That the *ma'amar*-targeted *get* is effective is thus established.

### Conclusion

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְרָבָא פְּשִׁיטָא לֵיהּ, לְרַב חֲנַנְיָה מִיבַּעְיָא לֵיהּ, מַאי — תֵּיקוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **To Rava it is obvious,** but **to Rav Ḥananya it is a dilemma. What,** then, is the *halakha*? Since no other proof is forthcoming, and Rav **Ḥananya** does not accept the teaching of Rava, the question remains undecided. The Gemara states that the dilemma **shall stand** unresolved.

For Rava, the question of whether a *get* can target only the *ma'amar* (*peshita lei*) — obviously yes. What remains genuinely uncertain is whether a *get* can be directed exclusively at the *zika* layer without addressing the *ma'amar* built upon it — attacking the foundation while leaving the superstructure. Rav Chanina's question precisely concerns this more difficult case. The *gemara* concludes: *teiku*.

## Chalitzah Ma'amar

### Setup

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** חָלַץ וְעָשָׂה מַאֲמָר.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: If the *yavam* **performed *ḥalitza* and** then **performed levirate betrothal,** nothing is effective after *ḥalitza* and this levirate betrothal is not valid.

The *gemara* now turns to the case of *chalatz v'achar kach ma'amar*. The *yavam*'s brother has died, leaving two *yevamot*, call them Sarah and Rivkah. He performs *chalitzah* with Sarah. He then attempts to perform *ma'amar* — either with Sarah or with Rivkah. What is the status of this *ma'amar*?

When the *yavam* performs *ma'amar* after *chalitzah*, he is attempting to effect *kiddushin* with a woman who is now his *chalutza*. This raises an immediate question about the nature of his prohibition toward her.

### Issur Lav

The prohibition on a *yavam* remarrying his *chalutza* is not *arayot* — it is not *kareit*-level. Rather, it derives from the *lav* of *keivan she-lo bana, shuv lo yivneh* — since he failed to "build" with her via *yibum* and instead performed *chalitzah*, he may not build with her again. It is a simple *issur lav*. The question is therefore: does *kiddushin* — or its rabbinic analog *ma'amar* — take hold on a woman who is subject to an *issur lav*? This is the general principle of whether *kiddushin tossin b'chayei lavin* — whether *kiddushin* is binding when the woman is prohibited to the man via a *lav* rather than an *ervah*.

### Rabbi Akiva

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי עֲקִיבָא, דְּאָמַר: אֵין קִדּוּשִׁין תּוֹפְסִין בְּחַיָּיבֵי לָאוִין. אֲבָל חֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: יֵשׁ אַחַר חֲלִיצָה כְּלוּם.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: If the *yavam* **performed *ḥalitza* and** then **performed levirate betrothal,** nothing is effective after *ḥalitza* and this levirate betrothal is not valid. **Rav Yehuda said** that **Rav said: This is the statement of Rabbi Akiva, who says** that **betrothal does not take effect on** those who are forbidden as they are **liable for** violating **a prohibition.** Once he has performed *ḥalitza*, the woman is forbidden to him due to the prohibition derived from the verse "So shall it be done to the man who does not build his brother's house" (Deuteronomy 25:9), and betrothal is ineffective. **But the Rabbis say something is** effective **after *ḥalitza*.** Although the woman who performed *ḥalitza* is forbidden to him, if he does betroth her, his betrothal is effective, as with any regular prohibition.

According to Rav Yehuda citing Rav, the ruling that *ma'amar* after *chalitzah* is entirely void (*ein achar chalitzah klum*) follows Rabbi Akiva. Rabbi Akiva holds *ein kiddushin tossin b'chayei lavin* — *kiddushin* does not bind when the woman is prohibited to the man by a *lav*. Since the *chalutza* is forbidden to him by *lav* (*shuv lo yivneh*), neither *ma'amar* nor regular *kiddushin* can take hold.

### Chachamim

The *chachamim* disagree: *yesh achar chalitzah* — even after *chalitzah*, a subsequent *ma'amar* creates binding *kiddushin*. The *chachamim* do not subscribe to Rabbi Akiva's blanket rule that *kiddushin* cannot attach when the woman is subject to a *lav*. In their view, *kiddushin* is a sufficiently robust legal act that it binds even in the presence of a *lav* prohibition, leaving the man in a position where he has violated a *lav* but the *kiddushin* is nonetheless valid.

The *gemara* now raises a difficulty: the *mishnah* states that if the *yavam* gave a *get* to the *yevamah* and then performed *ma'amar*, the *ma'amar* is effective and she requires a *get* to dissolve it. But if Rabbi Akiva holds *ein kiddushin tossin b'chayei lavin*, and if a *get* of a *yevamah* creates the same prohibition as *chalitzah* — namely that she becomes forbidden to him — then the *ma'amar* after *get* should equally fail. Why does the *mishnah* treat *ma'amar* after *get* as effective? Rabbi Akiva even derives from the verse *lo yuchal ba'alah ha-rishon asher shilchah* — *achar shiluach* alone is sufficient to create a permanent prohibition — suggesting the *get* of a *yevamah* is *d'oraita*. If so, the prohibition created by the *get* should be at least as strong as that created by *chalitzah*, and *ma'amar* should equally fail afterward.

### Abiyi resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי: גֵּט יְבָמִין מִדְּרַבָּנַן, וּקְרָא אַסְמַכְתָּא בְּעָלְמָא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ashi said:** This is not difficult because **the bill of divorce for levirate marriage** was established by the Sages and only has the force of **rabbinic** law, **and the** aforementioned **verse** is not a Torah prohibition, but rather **a mere support.** Therefore there is no Torah prohibition against marrying a *yevama* who received a bill of divorce, and the levirate betrothal performed afterward is effective according to Rabbi Akiva as well.

Abaye resolves this: *get yavni mi-de'rabbanan* — the *get* of a *yevamah* is rabbinic, not *d'oraita*. The verse (*lo yuchal ba'alah ha-rishon asher shilchah*) is merely an *asmachta* — a rabbinic support text, not the source of a biblical prohibition. After a *get*, therefore, she is forbidden to him only at the rabbinic level, not the *d'oraita* level. Since Rabbi Akiva's principle (*ein kiddushin tossin b'chayei lavin*) operates only with respect to *d'oraita* prohibitions, a rabbinic-level prohibition does not block *kiddushin* from taking hold. *Ma'amar* after *get* therefore works. By contrast, after *chalitzah* the prohibition is *d'oraita*, and under Rabbi Akiva's rule, *kiddushin* — including *ma'amar* — cannot bind.

## Framing matters

### Yibum framing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא אִידַּךְ: הַחוֹלֵץ לִיבִמְתוֹ וְחָזַר וְקִדְּשָׁהּ, רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: אִם קִדְּשָׁהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת — צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט, לְשׁוּם יַבְמוּת — אֵין צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in **another** *baraita*: With regard to **one who performs *ḥalitza* with his *yevama* and then betroths her, Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says: If he betrothed her for the purpose of** regular **marriage she requires a bill of divorce from him;** if he betrothed her **for the purpose of levirate marriage she does not require a bill of divorce from him.**

A *baraita* introduces a further distinction, attributed to Rabbi (Rebbi). Rabbi holds that the effectiveness of *kiddushin* performed after *chalitzah* depends on how the *kiddushin* was framed. If the *yavam* said *hitkadshi li b'ma'amar yevamin* — framing the act explicitly as a *ma'amar* of *yibum* — then it does not take effect. The reasoning is that *ma'amar* is structurally built upon *zika*: it is the rabbinic *kiddushin* that operates within and atop the *yibum* framework. Once *chalitzah* has severed the *zika*, the foundation upon which *ma'amar* rests no longer exists. An act framed as *ma'amar yevamin* thus reaches for a foundation that has been dissolved.

### Regular framing

However, if after *chalitzah* the *yavam* said simply *harei at mekudeshet li* — the ordinary formula of *kiddushin* — without invoking the *yibum* context, Rabbi holds this should in principle bind as regular *kiddushin*. Regular *kiddushin* does not depend on *zika* as its foundation; it stands on its own. The prohibition she carries toward him is a *lav*, and the question of whether *kiddushin tossin b'chayei lavin* is debated — but Rabbi does not necessarily accept Rabbi Akiva's position on that question.

### Baraita

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וַחֲכָמִים אוֹמְרִים: בֵּין שֶׁקִּדְּשָׁהּ לְשׁוּם אִישׁוּת, בִּין שֶׁקִּדְּשָׁהּ לְשׁוּם יַבְמוּת — צְרִיכָה הֵימֶנּוּ גֵּט.
>
> **Translation:** **And the Rabbis say: Whether he betrothed her for the purpose of** regular **marriage, or whether he betrothed her for the purpose of levirate marriage, she requires a bill of divorce from him,** as this betrothal is effective.

The *baraita* formalizes Rabbi's distinction: *im kidshah le-shum ishus tzricha heimenu get* — if he betrothed her framed as ordinary marriage (*ishus*), she requires a *get* to be released; *le-shum yevamus ein tzricha heimenu get* — if framed as *yevamus* (as an act of *yibum*), she does not require a *get*, because the *kiddushin* did not take hold.

### Chachamim

The *chachamim* reject this distinction entirely: *bein she-kidshah le-shum ishus bein she-kidshah le-shum yevamus tzricha heimenu get* — regardless of how the *kiddushin* was framed, if he said *harei at mekudeshet li*, she requires a *get*. The *chachamim* hold that the words *harei at mekudeshet li* are operative regardless of the speaker's framing intent.

### Ger analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יוֹסֵף: מַאי טַעְמָא דְּרַבִּי — עֲשָׂאוּהָ כְּעוֹדֵר בְּנִכְסֵי הַגֵּר וּכְסָבוּר שֶׁלּוֹ הֵן, דְּלָא קָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yosef said: What is the reason for Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi's distinction between one who betroths his *ḥalutza* for the purpose of levirate marriage and one who does so for the purpose of regular marriage? It is because the Sages **considered him like** one who **hoes the property of a convert** who died without leaving heirs. The property of such a convert is ownerless, and whoever takes possession of it acquires its title. As one of the methods of taking possession of land is hoeing, if one hoed the property of this convert with the intention of acquiring it, it belongs to him. However, if he hoed the convert's property **while** mistakenly **thinking** that the land **was his own,** even though he performed an act of acquisition, since he lacked the requisite intention **he has not acquired** the land. Here too, since the *yavam* intended to betroth her for the purpose of levirate marriage, and the levirate bond no longer exists, his action is not effective.

Rav Yosef defends Rabbi's position with an analogy: one who plows in the field of a *ger* (a convert whose estate is ownerless upon death, claimable by the first person who works it) without knowing it belongs to a *ger* — thinking it is his own field — does not acquire it. *Lo kavan le-mikni* — he had no intent to perform a *kinyan*. The acquisition fails not because the act was wrong but because the specific *kinyan*-intent was absent. Similarly, one who says *harei at mekudeshet li b'ma'amar yevamin* did not intend to perform regular *kiddushin*; his intent was directed at a *yibum*-framed act, and since that frame is no longer available (the *zika* having been severed by *chalitzah*), the *kiddushin* fails.

### Counter analogy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי: מִי דָּמֵי? הָתָם לָא קָא מְכַוֵּין לְמִיקְנֵי, הָכָא קָא מְכַוֵּין לְמִיקְנֵי! הָא לָא דָּמְיָא אֶלָּא לְעוֹדֵר בְּנִכְסֵי גֵּר זֶה וּכְסָבוּר שֶׁל גֵּר אַחֵר הוּא, דְּקָנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to him: Are** the two situations **comparable? There,** in the case of hoeing, **he does not intend to acquire** the land as he works, as he thinks that it is already his, whereas **here he does intend to acquire** the woman. **This** case **is similar only to the one who hoes the property of this** deceased **convert while thinking it is that of a different convert.** The *halakha* in that case is **that he acquires** the land, despite his imprecise knowledge of what he is acquiring. Here too the betrothal should take effect regardless of his error.

Abaye counters: the analogy does not hold. *Hatam lo kavan le-mikni* — in the *ger* case, the person truly had no *kinyan*-intent at all; he thought he was working his own property. *Hacha kavan le-mikni* — in the *kiddushin* case, the *yavam* fully intends to acquire this woman; he is simply operating under a mistaken classification of the type of acquisition he is effecting. A better analogy: one who plows in the field of one *ger*, thinking it belongs to a different *ger* who died — both fields being ownerless, both eligible for acquisition by *chazakah*. In that case, the *kinyan* succeeds: the person has full *kinyan*-intent directed at this field, even if his identification of the deceased owner is mistaken. Similarly, here the *yavam* has full intent to acquire this woman via *kiddushin*; his characterization of the *kiddushin* as *yibum*-framed is immaterial to the operative intent.

### Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן, כְּגוֹן דַּאֲמַר לַהּ: הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמַאֲמָר יְבָמִין. רַבִּי סָבַר: מַאֲמָר עִילָּוֵי זִיקָה קָא רָמֵי, וַאֲתַאי חֲלִיצָה אַפְקַעְתַּהּ לְזִיקָּהּ. וְרַבָּנַן סָבְרִי: הַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי וְהַאי לְחוֹדֵיהּ קָאֵי. מֵעִיקָּרָא אִילּוּ אֲמַר לַהּ ״הִתְקַדְּשִׁי לִי בְּמַאֲמַר יְבָמִין״, מִי לָא מַהֲנֵי? הַשְׁתָּא נָמֵי מַהֲנֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Abaye said** that the dispute between Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi and the Rabbis cannot be explained in the above manner. Instead, he suggests: **With what are we dealing here?** It is a case **where he said to** his *ḥalutza*: **Be betrothed to me with levirate betrothal. Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **holds** that **levirate betrothal is superimposed upon** the **levirate bond,** and is therefore only possible when the bond exists, **and** since ***ḥalitza* comes** and **releases the levirate bond,** levirate betrothal is not effective for her. **And the Rabbis hold: This,** the levirate bond, **stands on its own, and that,** levirate betrothal, **stands on its own.** Although the bond has been canceled, the formula of: Be betrothed to me with levirate betrothal, is effective. **At the outset,** before *ḥalitza*, **if he had said to her: Be betrothed to me with levirate betrothal, would** this **not be** an **effective** betrothal despite the lack of connection between the levirate betrothal and the levirate bond? **Now too** it should be **effective,** even though she performed *ḥalitza*.

Abaye resolves the difficulty by clarifying the precise scope of Rabbi's ruling. Rabbi does not hold that background intent — thinking one is doing *kiddushin yevamin* rather than regular *kiddushin* — suffices to defeat the *kiddushin*. Rather, Rabbi's ruling applies only where the actual words spoken framed the act as *ma'amar yevamin*. When the formula itself invokes *yevamin*, the act is constituted as a *ma'amar* — an act that by definition builds on the *zika*. Since the *chalitzah* severed the *zika*, there is no foundation left for the *ma'amar* to rest on, and the act is void. The *rabbanan*, by contrast, hold *hai lachud v'hai lachud* — *zika* and *ma'amar* are independent, and even without *zika*, the words *harei at mekudeshet li* — even with the appended *b'ma'amar yevamin* — carry their own *kiddushin* force and bind the woman. The dispute thus turns on whether *ma'amar* is so intrinsically constituted by its dependence on *zika* that an explicit invocation of *ma'amar yevamin* renders it a structural nullity once *chalitzah* has dissolved the *zika*, or whether the *kiddushin* language stands independently regardless of the framing.
