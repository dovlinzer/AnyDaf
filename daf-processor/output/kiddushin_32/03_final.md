# Kiddushin 32 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kibbud Stories

### Avimi's Deference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ: כְּגוֹן אֲבִימִי בְּרִי קִיֵּים מִצְוַת כִּיבּוּד. חַמְשָׁה בְּנֵי סְמִיכִי הֲוָה לֵיהּ לַאֲבִימִי בְּחַיֵּי אָבִיו, וְכִי הֲוָה אֲתָא רַבִּי אֲבָהוּ, קָרֵי אַבָּבָא, רָהֵיט וְאָזֵיל וּפָתַח לֵיהּ וְאָמַר: אִין, אִין, עַד דְּמָטֵאי הָתָם.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abbahu said:** One **such as Avimi, my son,** properly **fulfilled the mitzva of honoring** his parents. The Gemara relates: **Avimi had five sons during his father's lifetime** who were **ordained** to issue halakhic rulings, and he too was ordained. **And** yet **when Rabbi Abbahu,** his father, **came and called at the gate** to enter, Avimi **would** himself **run and go to open** the door **for him. And before he arrived there, he** would already **say: Yes, yes,** so that his father would not think that he was being ignored.

The daf opens at the last line of 31a with a *tanna* attributed to Avimi the son of Rabbi Abbahu: *yesh ma'achil le'aviv pisyoni v'tardo min ha'olam, v'yesh mat'chino b'reichayim u'mevi'o l'chayei ha'olam ha'ba* — there are those who feed their parents pheasants and thereby drive them from the world, and there are those who make their parents grind behind a millstone and thereby bring them to *olam ha-ba*. As Rabbi Linzer discussed the previous day, this opening statement captures the deep subjectivity — or rather, the essential need for context — in understanding the obligations of *kibbud av va'em* and *mora em va'av* in a way that other *mitzvot* perhaps do not require. The same act can honor or humiliate depending on the relationship and the circumstances.

Immediately following, Rabbi Abbahu himself offers a living example: *ke-gon Avimi brei* — my son Avimi exemplifies the fulfillment of the *mitzvah* of *kibbud*. The *gemara* then details precisely how. Avimi had five *benei samchei* — the term is somewhat ambiguous; it may mean sons who had received *semicha*, or more plausibly sons who were already grown and dependent on his table — in his father's lifetime. Yet whenever Rabbi Abbahu would come to visit, Avimi himself would run to the door and open it, calling out "Yes, yes, I'm coming" until he reached the threshold. He did not delegate this act of *kibbud* to his own grown children but insisted on performing it himself.

This narrative touches on something worth noting: the overwhelming majority of *gemara* stories about *kibbud av va'em* concern adult children navigating the care of aging, and in some cases declining, parents — not teenagers relating to middle-aged parents. Avimi had a household of his own to manage. And according to *Tosafot*, Rabbi Abbahu lived in *Eretz Yisrael* while Avimi lived in *Bavel*, meaning these visits were infrequent, which makes Avimi's scrupulous deference all the more striking.

### Vigil & Merit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יוֹמָא חַד אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַשְׁקְיַין מַיָּא. אַדְּאַיְיתִי לֵיהּ נַמְנֵם. גָּחֵין קָאֵי עֲלֵיהּ עַד דְּאִיתְּעַר. אִיסְתַּיַּיעָא מִילְּתֵיהּ וּדְרַשׁ אֲבִימִי ״מִזְמוֹר לְאָסָף״.
>
> **Translation:** **One day** Rabbi Abbahu **said to** Avimi his son: **Give me water to drink. Before he brought him** the water, Rabbi Abbahu **dozed off.** Avimi **bent over** and **stood over him until** his father **awoke.** The performance of this mitzva **aided him,** i.e., as a reward God helped him in his studies, **and** Avimi succeeded in homiletically **interpreting** the psalm: **"A song to Asaph"** (Psalms 79).

The *gemara* continues with a second episode. One day, Rabbi Abbahu asked Avimi for a glass of water. By the time Avimi returned, his father had already dozed off. Rather than waking him or setting the water down and leaving, Avimi stood vigil beside him until he woke on his own. The *gemara* records that as a result of this act, *ista'yei miltei* — he merited to expound the *mizmor* of *Mizmor l'Asaph, Elohim nitzav b'adat el* — which is, as it happens, the *shir shel yom* of that very day. The connection drawn between *kibbud av va'em* and the relationship between human beings and God runs as a quiet current beneath this entire sugya.

*Tosafot* raises a separate question about which *Mizmor l'Asaph* is intended here — *Mizmor l'Asaph, Elohim bau goyim b'nachalatecha* presents an alternative — and the *rishonim* engage with the textual question at length, though that discussion is set aside here.

### Mother vs Father

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַב יַעֲקֹב בַּר אֲבוּהּ לְאַבָּיֵי: כְּגוֹן אֲנָא, דְּעַד דְּאָתֵינָא מִבֵּי רַב אַבָּא מְדַלֵּי לִי כָּסָא וְאִמָּא מָזְגָה לִי, הֵיכִי אֶיעֱבֵיד? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מֵאִמָּךְ קַבֵּיל, וּמֵאֲבוּךְ לָא תְּקַבֵּל דְּכֵיוָן דְּבַר תּוֹרָה הוּא חָלְשָׁה דַּעְתֵּיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Ya'akov bar Avuh said to Abaye:** With regard to one **such as I,** so beloved by my parents that **before I return from the study hall my father brings me a cup and my mother pours for me, how should I act?** Is it disrespectful to accept this honor from them? Abaye **said to him: Accept** it **from your mother, but do not accept it from your father, as, since he is a Torah scholar he will be disheartened** if his son does not show him the proper level of respect.

The next narrative introduces Rabbi Yaakov bar Abuha — a name which itself prompts a brief observation, since *bar Abuha* means "son of his father," and one always wonders in the *gemara* whether *Abba* was a proper name or a term of address substituted for a parent whose name one could not directly invoke. Rabbi Yaakov bar Abuha posed his question to Abaye: *ke-gon ana* — let me offer my own example. When he would return from the *beis midrash*, his father would pour him a glass of wine, and his mother would mix it with water. What should he do? His parents were being deferential to him, and both had prepared something for him.

Abaye's answer is instructive: accept the drink from your mother, but do not accept it from your father, *de-keivan de-bar Torah hu chalsha datei* — because your father is himself a Torah scholar, and he will be more pained if his son accepts the drink from him. The role reversal here is striking on multiple levels. These are parents serving their son the way the son is supposed to be serving them. And the complicating factor is precisely his status as a *talmid chacham* — his father's honor for Torah learning is entangled with the familial dynamic in a way that cannot be easily separated. Each of these episodes could sustain extended psychological and *halachic* analysis; the *gemara* presses forward.

### Tarfon's Footstool

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי טַרְפוֹן הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא אִמָּא דְּכֹל אֵימַת דַּהֲוָת בָּעֲיָא לְמֵיסֵק לְפוּרְיָא גָּחִין וּסְלִיק לַהּ, וְכֹל אֵימַת דֵּהוָת נָחִית, נֶחְתַּת עֲלוּיָה. אֲתָא וְקָא מִשְׁתַּבַּח בֵּי מִדְרְשָׁא. אָמְרֵי לֵיהּ: עֲדָיִין לֹא הִגַּעְתָּ לַחֲצִי כִּיבּוּד. כְּלוּם זָרְקָה אַרְנָקִי בְּפָנֶיךָ לַיָּם וְלֹא הִכְלַמְתָּהּ?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara relates: **Rabbi Tarfon had a certain** manner of treating his **mother, that whenever she wished to ascend into** her **bed he would bend over and** help **her to ascend, and whenever she wished to descend** from the bed, **she would descend onto him. He came and praised** himself in the **study hall** for performing the mitzva of honoring one's father and mother so thoroughly. **They said to him: You still have not reached even half of** the **honor** due to her. **Has** it ever happened that **she threw a purse into the sea in front of you, and you did not embarrass her?**

The *gemara* then turns to the well-known story of Rabbi Tarfon, who would lie down on the floor each time his mother needed to get into or out of bed, serving as a living footstool. *De-chol eimat de-hava baei lemisik le-purya gachin ve-salik lei* — whenever she needed to ascend to her bed, he would crouch down and she would step up on him; and whenever she needed to descend, she would step down upon him. Whether his mother was infirm or simply unable to manage the height of the bed on her own, the image is vivid and deliberate.

When Rabbi Tarfon came to the *beis midrash* and spoke proudly of this practice, the response was immediate and sobering: *adayin lo higata le-chatzi kibbud* — you have not yet reached half of what *kibbud* requires. The standard set by the *gemara* is this: *klum zarka arneki be-fanecha le-yam ve-lo hichlamta* — have you reached the point where if she threw your wallet into the ocean in front of you, you would not be shamed or angered?

### Wallet Test

This wallet question becomes a *sugya* in its own right for the *poskim*: how much financial loss and how much personal humiliation is a child obligated to absorb without reacting? These are actually two separate questions. *Rashi* reads the test primarily as a question of public embarrassment — the shame of allowing a parent to treat one this way before others. But the financial dimension is also real: a parent destroying your property, in public or in private, tests the inner composure that *kibbud* demands. Both threads will return later in the *daf*.

### Rav Yosef Rising

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב יוֹסֵף כִּי הֲוָה שָׁמַע קָל כַּרְעָא דְאִמֵּיהּ, אָמַר: אֵיקוּם מִקַּמֵּי שְׁכִינָה דְּאָתְיָא.
>
> **Translation:** **When Rav Yosef heard his mother's footsteps, he** would **say: I will stand before the arriving Divine Presence.**

A brief but vivid vignette follows. Rav Yosef, upon hearing the sound of his mother's footsteps approaching, would say: *ekum mikamei Shechina de-asya* — I am rising before the *Shechina* that is coming. The analogy between honoring one's mother and receiving the divine presence appears here not as a homily but as the stated reason for Rav Yosef's own practice.

### Orphanhood Blessing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: אַשְׁרֵי מִי שֶׁלֹּא חֲמָאָן. רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן כִּי עִבְּרַתּוּ אִמּוֹ – מֵת אָבִיו, יְלָדַתּוּ – מֵתָה אִמּוֹ. וְכֵן אַבָּיֵי. אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר אַבָּיֵי: אֲמַרָה לִי אֵם! הָהִיא מְרַבְּיָנְתֵּיהּ הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yoḥanan said: Fortunate is one who never saw** his father and mother, as it is so difficult to honor them appropriately. The Gemara relates that **Rabbi Yoḥanan** himself never saw his parents. **When his mother was pregnant with him, his father died;** and when **she gave birth to him, his mother died. And the same** is true of **Abaye.** The Gemara asks: **Is that so,** that Abaye never saw his mother? **But didn't Abaye say** on many occasions: **My mother told me?** The Gemara answers: **That** mother **was** actually **his foster mother,** not his birth mother.

This leads into one of the more striking statements in the *gemara*. Rabbi Yochanan said: *ashrei mi she-lo chamman* — fortunate is one who never saw them. *Rashi* explains the difficult word but the meaning is arresting: better perhaps never to have had parents than to have had them and failed so consistently at the *mitzvah* of *kibbud*. The *gemara* then explains this in terms of Rabbi Yochanan's own biography: *ki ivarta imeh meis aviv, ki yeladtih misa imeih* — when his mother was pregnant with him, his father died; when she gave birth, his mother died. He was orphaned from the outset. In a deeply ironic sense, he never had the opportunity to fail at *kibbud av va'em* — and this is offered, somewhat ruefully, as a kind of grace. One can read this either as a psychological justification that Rabbi Yochanan constructed for himself, or as a genuine *gemara* observation about the extraordinary difficulty of the *mitzvah* and the near-impossibility of fulfilling it perfectly.

The same was true of Abaye, the *gemara* continues — *ve-khen Abaye*. A trope emerges: the orphaning of great *amora'im* is not merely biographical detail but seems to suggest that greatness and deprivation were intertwined, and that these sages were spared the burden of potential failure at *kibbud av va'em* precisely through their loss. But then the *gemara* immediately challenges this: *eyni ve-ha-amar Abaye amra li em* — doesn't Abaye constantly quote his *em*? The resolution: *hahi amar bnitei havei* — that was his nurse, his adoptive mother who raised him. The question of how *halacha* regards adoptive parents is genuinely significant, and the *poskim* take it seriously. The principle of *melamdo Torah k'megadlo* — that one who teaches another Torah is considered as though he raised them — points in the same direction: those who form us and nurture us carry a kind of *kibbud* weight that approximates, and in some cases equals, that owed to biological parents.

### Rav Asi's Flight

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב אַסִּי הֲוָה לֵיהּ הָהִיא אִמָּא זְקֵינָה. אֲמַרָה לֵיהּ: בָּעֲיָנָא תַּכְשִׁיטִין. עֲבַד לַהּ. בָּעֲיָנָא גַּבְרָא: נְיעַיֵּין לָךְ. בָּעֲיָנָא גַּבְרָא דְּשַׁפִּיר כְּוָתָךְ, שַׁבְקַהּ וַאֲזַל לְאַרְעָא דְיִשְׂרָאֵל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Asi had an elderly mother. She said to him: I want jewelry,** and **he made** jewelry **for her.** She said to him: **I want a man** whom I can marry, and he said to her: **I will seek** one **for you.** She said to him: **I want a husband who is as handsome as you.** At this point, he realized that she was senile, and that he would be unable to fulfill all her requests. Therefore, **he left her and went to Eretz Yisrael.**

The concluding narrative in this section is among the most humanly complex in the *daf*. Rav Asi had an elderly mother. She asked him for jewelry; he provided it. She asked for a husband; he said he would look. She asked for a husband as handsome as him — and at that point he fled to *Eretz Yisrael*. He subsequently heard that she had followed him. He came before Rabbi Yochanan and asked: *mahu l'tzeit me-Eretz la-chutz la-Aretz* — may I leave *Eretz Yisrael* and go back to *chutz la-Aretz*? Rabbi Yochanan answered: *asur* — forbidden. He then asked: what if I am going to greet my mother? Rabbi Yochanan said: *eyni yodeia* — I do not know. The *gemara*'s implication is that Rabbi Yochanan sensed something complicated in the story; the question about going to visit his mother was not quite candid.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שְׁמַע דְּקָא אָזְלָה אַבָּתְרֵיהּ. אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי יוֹחָנָן, אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַהוּ לָצֵאת מֵאֶרֶץ לְחוּצָה לָאָרֶץ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אָסוּר. לִקְרַאת אִמָּא מַהוּ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ. אִתָּרַח פּוּרְתָּא, הֲדַר אֲתָא. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אַסִּי, נִתְרַצֵּיתָ לָצֵאת – הַמָּקוֹם יַחֲזִירְךָ לְשָׁלוֹם.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi **heard that she was following him** to Eretz Yisrael. **He came before Rabbi Yoḥanan** and **said to him: What is** the *halakha* with regard to **leaving Eretz** Yisrael to go **outside of Eretz** Yisrael? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him:** It is **prohibited.** Rav Asi further asked: If one is going **to greet** his **mother, what is** the *halakha*? Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: I do not know.** Rav Asi **waited a little** while, and **then came** back to him. Rabbi Yoḥanan **said to him: Asi, you are** evidently **determined to leave.** May **the Omnipresent return you in peace,** and he said no more.

Somewhat later, Rav Asi returned and reported that Rabbi Yochanan had blessed him: *Ha-Makom yachzirekha l'shalom* — God should return you safely. He then went to Rabbi Elazar and related what had happened. Rabbi Elazar responded: *chas v'shalom mirtach rattach* — *Rashi* explains this as Rabbi Elazar's worry that Rabbi Yochanan would be angry at him for effectively ruling against him. But the ambiguity is genuine: who exactly would be angry at whom? Rabbi Elazar nonetheless concluded that if Rabbi Yochanan had offered a *berakha*, he clearly was not planning to get angry: *v'im ita de-rattach lo hamvarkh lakh* — if he were truly angry, he would not have blessed you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲתָא לְקַמֵּיהּ דְּרַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: חַס וְשָׁלוֹם, דִּלְמָא מִירְתָּח רָתַח? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מַאי אֲמַר לָךְ? אֲמַר לֵיהּ: הַמָּקוֹם יַחֲזִירְךָ לְשָׁלוֹם. אֲמַר לֵיהּ: וְאִם אִיתָא דְּרָתַח – לָא הֲוָה מְבָרֵךְ לֵךְ. אַדְּהָכִי וְהָכִי שְׁמַע לַאֲרוֹנַאּ דְּקָאָתֵי. אָמַר: אִי יְדַעִי לָא נְפַקִי.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Asi **came before Rabbi Elazar,** because he did not know how to interpret Rabbi Yoḥanan's statement. **He said to** Rabbi Elazar: **God forbid, perhaps** he **is angry** with me that I wished to leave? Rabbi Elazar **said to him: What** exactly **did he say to you?** Rav Asi **said to him:** May **the Omnipresent return you in peace.** Rabbi Elazar **said to him: If it is so that he was angry, he would not have blessed you.** Rabbi Yoḥanan certainly gave you permission to leave. **In the meantime,** while he was traveling to meet her, Rav Asi **heard that her coffin was coming,** i.e., his mother had died and her coffin was being brought to Eretz Yisrael. **He said: Had I known I would not have left,** as after his mother's death he was not obligated to leave Eretz Yisrael to honor her.

Then, in mid-deliberation, Rav Asi heard that his mother's coffin was being brought. *Amar: i yadai lo nafki* — if I had known, I would not have left. She had died in *Bavel* and was being transported for burial, and someone else — presumably other family members — had already attended to everything. The weight of what he had failed to do, failed to witness, failed to participate in, settles over the story without commentary. She died alone, in some sense, while he was negotiating the *halacha* of whether to go back to her.

## Halakhic Framework

### Kibbud & Mora

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: מְכַבְּדוֹ בְּחַיָּיו, וּמְכַבְּדוֹ בְּמוֹתוֹ. בְּחַיָּיו כֵּיצַד? הַנִּשְׁמָע בִּדְבַר אָבִיו לְמָקוֹם, לֹא יֹאמַר: ״שַׁלְּחוּנִי בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי״, ״מַהֲרוּנִי בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי״, ״פִּטְרוּנִי בִּשְׁבִיל עַצְמִי״. אֶלָּא כּוּלְּהוּ: ״בִּשְׁבִיל אַבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: One honors** his father **in his life and honors him in his death. How** does he honor him **in his life? One who goes to a place on the command of his father should not say** to the people to whom he has been sent, to hurry them along: **Send me** on my journey **on my own behalf,** or: **Hurry up on my own behalf,** or: **Allow me** to **take leave** of this business **on my own behalf. Rather,** he should say **all** of the above in the following manner: Act in this manner **on Father's behalf,** as a mark of respect for his father.

The *gemara* now transitions from narrative to systematic *halachic* formulation. A *beraita* is cited: *tanu rabbanan mechabdo be-chayav u-mechabdo be-moto* — there is an obligation of *kavod* toward one's parents both during their lifetime and after their death. The *gemara* asks: how does one fulfill *kavod* during the parent's lifetime? The answer: if you are about to go do something in your parent's service, you may not say "send me along for my own sake" or "speed me up for myself" — *ela kulhu bi-shvil abba* — everything is framed as being done for the parent.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמוֹתוֹ כֵּיצַד? הָיָה אוֹמֵר דָּבָר שְׁמוּעָה מִפִּיו, לֹא יֹאמַר: ״כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא״, אֶלָּא: ״כָּךְ אָמַר אַבָּא מָרִי, הֲרֵינִי כַּפָּרַת מִשְׁכָּבוֹ״. וְהָנֵי מִילֵּי תּוֹךְ שְׁנֵים עָשָׂר חֹדֶשׁ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ אוֹמֵר: ״זִכְרוֹנוֹ לִבְרָכָה לְחַיֵּי הָעוֹלָם הַבָּא״.
>
> **Translation:** **How** does he honor him **in his death?** If **he says a matter he heard from his** father's **mouth, he should not say: So said Father. Rather,** he should say: **So said Father, my teacher, may I be an atonement for his resting** soul. **And this** *halakha* **applies within twelve months** of his death. **From this** time **onward he says: May his memory be for a blessing, for the life of the World-to-Come.**

After death, *kavod* takes the form of how you transmit teachings received from them. You may not say simply "this is what my father said" — you must say *kach amar abba mori ha-reini kaparat mishkavo* — "thus said my father, my teacher, for whose resting place I am an atonement." The *gemara* qualifies: *ve-hanei milei toch shneim asar chodesh* — this formulation applies only within the first twelve months. After that, one says *zichrono l'vracha l'chayei ha'olam ha'ba*.

The phrase *ha-reini kaparat mishkavo* carries genuine ambiguity. It has become associated with the practice of saying *kaddish* and attending *minyan* regularly after a parent's passing — a way of acting on their behalf in the post-mortem realm, whether understood as easing their passage through *Gehinnom* or as adding merit toward *olam ha-ba*. *Rashi* reads the phrase in its more severe sense — may all evil due to them befall me instead — while a more positive reading frames it as *le-ilui nishmat*, that the child's good deeds are beneficial to the deceased parent's soul. Many of the mourning practices that are normative in *Ashkenazi halacha* flow from *Chasidei Ashkenaz*, and both readings are consistent with that tradition.

### Name Change

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: חָכָם – מְשַׁנֶּה שֵׁם אָבִיו וְשֵׁם רַבּוֹ. תּוּרְגְּמָן – אֵינוֹ מְשַׁנֶּה לֹא שֵׁם אָבִיו וְלֹא שֵׁם רַבּוֹ. אֲבוּהּ דְּמַאן? אִילֵימָא אֲבוּהּ דִּמְתוּרְגְּמָן – אַטּוּ תּוּרְגְּמָן לָאו בַּר חִיּוּבָא הוּא?
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: A Sage** who lectures in public must **change the name of his father,** i.e., when he quotes his father he should not mention him by name. **And** similarly, he changes the **name of his teacher. The disseminator,** who explains the statements of a Sage to the audience, **changes neither the name of his father nor the name of his teacher.** The Gemara asks: To **whose father** is this referring? **If we say** it is referring to **the father of the disseminator,** whom the Sage mentioned in his lecture, **is that to say** that **the disseminator is not obligated** to observe the mitzva of honoring one's father? How can a disseminator mention his own father by name?

The next *beraita* addresses the *chacham* teaching publicly: *chacham meshaneh shem aviv ve-shem rabo* — when quoting one's parent or teacher, a scholar must alter how he refers to them. He cannot simply say "Rabbi so-and-so said this" but must say "my *rebbe* said such and such." The *turgaman* — the one who sits beside the lecturer and repeats the teaching aloud, more slowly and with elaboration — is exempt from this requirement.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: שֵׁם אָבִיו שֶׁל חָכָם, וְשֵׁם רַבּוֹ שֶׁל חָכָם. כִּי הָא דְּמָר בַּר רַב אָשֵׁי כִּי הֲוָה דָּרֵישׁ בְּפִירְקָא, אִיהוּ אָמַר: אַבָּא מָרִי, וְאָמוֹרֵיהּ אָמַר: הָכִי אָמַר רַב אָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said:** This is referring to the **name of the Sage's father and the name of the Sage's teacher.** This is **like that which Mar bar Rav Ashi** would do, as **when he would teach** Torah **at his regular lecture** and would mention a *halakha* in the name of his father, Rav Ashi, **he would say:** So said **my father, my teacher; and his disseminator** would **say: So said Rav Ashi.** Although a son may not mention his father's name, the disseminator of his lecture may do so.

But whose father and whose teacher are meant? If it is the *turgaman*'s own father, the *gemara* objects: *atu turgaman lav bar chiyuva hu* — is a *turgaman* somehow exempt from showing *kavod* to his parents? Rather, Rava clarifies: it is the *chacham*'s father and the *chacham*'s teacher whose names must be modified — not the *turgaman*'s. The *chacham* has that specific relationship of honor to express; for the *turgaman*, whose function is clarity of transmission, the obligation to modify names does not apply in the same way. The practical illustration involves Mar bar Rav Ashi: when he taught, he would refer to his father simply as *Abba Mari*, while the *turgaman* would repeat the teaching by name — *kach amar Rav Ashi*.

### Mora vs Kibbud

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אֵיזוֹ מוֹרָא וְאֵיזוֹ כִּיבּוּד? מוֹרָא – לֹא עוֹמֵד בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, וְלֹא יוֹשֵׁב בִּמְקוֹמוֹ, וְלֹא סוֹתֵר אֶת דְּבָרָיו, וְלֹא מַכְרִיעוֹ. כִּיבּוּד – מַאֲכִיל וּמַשְׁקֶה, מַלְבִּישׁ וּמְכַסֶּה, מַכְנִיס וּמוֹצִיא.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught: What is fear and what is honor? Fear** of one's father includes the following: **One may not stand in his** father's fixed **place, and may not sit in his place, and may not contradict his statements** by expressing an opinion contrary to that of his father, **and he may not choose sides** when his father argues with someone else. What is considered **honor? He gives** his father **food and drink, dresses and covers** him, and **brings** him **in and takes** him **out** for all his household needs.

The *gemara* now formally distinguishes between the two separate *mitzvot*: *mora* and *kibbud*. What constitutes *mora*? *Lo omed bimkomo, velo yoshev bimkomo, velo soter devarav, velo machrio* — do not stand in the parent's place, do not sit in the parent's place, do not contradict or overturn their words. The formulation *soter devarav* — literally "demolish his words" — is noteworthy. What constitutes *kibbud*? *Ma'achil u-mashkeh malbish u-mechaseh machnis u-motzi* — providing food, providing drink, clothing (*malbish* referring to daytime clothing, *mechaseh* to nighttime coverings), and transportation, bringing the parent to where they need to go and returning them.

This formal framework raises a broader *halachic* question: how literally must these categories be applied? If a parent is not particular about which chair he sits in, but is deeply sensitive about something else entirely, does *mora* require technical adherence to these specific categories, or does it mean cultivating the actual relational state of reverence — adapting its expression to what truly affects this particular parent in this particular context? Major *halachic* works on *kibbud av va'em* have been written taking both more formalist and more contextual approaches, and this tension is genuine.

### Money Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיבַּעְיָא לְהוּ: מִשֶּׁל מִי? רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר: מִשֶּׁל בֵּן. רַב נָתָן בַּר אוֹשַׁעְיָא אָמַר: מִשֶּׁל אָב. אוֹרוֹ לֵיהּ רַבָּנַן לְרַב יִרְמְיָה, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לִבְרֵיהּ דְּרַב יִרְמְיָה, כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר מִשֶּׁל אָב.
>
> **Translation:** **A dilemma was raised before** the Sages: **From whose** funds must one give his father food and drink? **Rav Yehuda says: From** the money **of the son. Rav Natan bar Oshaya said: From** the money **of the father. The Sages gave** this following **ruling to Rav Yirmeya, and some say** they gave this following ruling **to the son of Rav Yirmeya:** The *halakha* is **like the one who says** it must be paid **from** the money **of the father.**

The *gemara* then raises the question of *mishel mi* — whose money must pay for all of the obligations of *kibbud*? The children bear the responsibility to ensure their parents have food, clothing, shelter, and transportation; but must the children pay for this from their own resources?

Rav Yehuda says: *mishel ben* — from the child's own property. Rav Natan bar Oshaya says: *mishel av* — from the parent's property. On the latter view, parents are expected to have prepared financially for old age; once they are no longer able to manage their own affairs, the children administer the parental resources appropriately. The *rabanan* told Rabbi Yirmiyah — or some say, his son — that the *halacha* follows *mishel av*: it is the parent's money that funds the obligations of *kibbud*.

### Lost Money

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: נֶאֱמַר: ״כַּבֵּד אֶת אָבִיךָ וְאֶת אִמֶּךָ״, וְנֶאֱמַר: ״כַּבֵּד אֶת ה׳ מֵהוֹנֶךָ״. מָה לְהַלָּן בְּחֶסְרוֹן כִּיס, אַף כָּאן בְּחֶסְרוֹן כִּיס. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁל אָב, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ? לְבִיטּוּל מְלָאכָה.
>
> **Translation:** **The Gemara raises an objection** from the following *baraita*: **It is stated: "Honor your father and your mother"** (Exodus 20:11), **and it is stated: "Honor the Lord with your wealth"** (Proverbs 3:9), which teaches the following verbal analogy: **Just as there** one honors God "with your wealth," i.e., **through monetary loss, so too here** one must honor his father **through monetary loss. And if you say** that one honors him **from** the money of **the father, what difference** does it make **to** the son, i.e., what monetary loss does he suffer? The Gemara answers: It makes a difference to him with regard **to** the **neglect of** his **work.** Although he is not required to spend his own money, the son must leave aside his work to honor his father, which will cause him some financial loss.

But a *meitivei* is raised: the *pasuk* says *kabbed et avicha ve'et imecha*, and in *Mishlei* it says *kabbed et Adonai mehoncha* — honor God from your wealth. Just as honoring God requires *chisron kis*, actual financial expenditure and loss, so too *kibbud* of parents must involve *chisron kis*. If the obligation is *mishel av*, what does the child actually lose? The *gemara* answers: *le-bittul melacha* — the financial loss is in lost wages, the time taken away from work in order to care for the parent. This is a genuinely different conception of *chisron kis*: not the direct expenditure of funds, but the opportunity cost of time. One might compare it to other contexts in *halacha* where refraining from productive work — *bittul melacha* — is treated as a form of financial sacrifice.

### Ma'aser Ani

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שְׁנֵי אַחִים, שְׁנֵי שׁוּתָּפִין הָאָב וּבְנוֹ, הָרַב וְתַלְמִידוֹ – פּוֹדִין זֶה לָזֶה מַעֲשֵׂר שֵׁנִי, וּמַאֲכִילִין זֶה לָזֶה מַעְשַׂר עָנִי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara suggests: **Come** and **hear** a proof from a *baraita*: **Two brothers,** or **two partners** in the ownership of produce, or **a father and son,** or **a rabbi and his student, may redeem** the **second tithe for each other** without adding one-fifth, as one who redeems the tithe of another, including these individuals, is not required to add one-fifth. **And they may feed each other** the **poor man's tithe.** If one of them is poor, the other may give him the poor man's tithe that he separated from his produce, and it is not considered as though the pauper ate the poor man's tithe from his own produce.

A *beraita* is then brought: two brothers, two business partners, a father and son, a teacher and student — *podin ze la'ze ma'aser sheni u-machilin ze la'ze ma'aser ani* — they may redeem each other's *ma'aser sheni* (avoiding the additional *chomesh* that applies when one redeems one's own) and they may feed each other *ma'aser ani*. The case of *ma'aser ani* is where the *gemara*'s argument turns sharp.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁל בֵּן, נִמְצָא זֶה פּוֹרֵעַ חוֹבוֹ מִשֶּׁל עֲנִיִּים! – לָא צְרִיכָא, לְהַעְדָּפָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the proof from this *baraita*. **And if you say** that the obligation to honor one's father is **from** the money **of the son,** one **finds** that **this** son **repays his obligation from** the produce **of the poor,** as he is taking care of his father with produce that should go to the poor. The Gemara rejects this proof: **No,** it is **necessary to** state this *halakha* in a case where he has covered all of his father's basic needs with his own money. At this stage, if his father needs **surplus** money, he may give it to him from the poor man's tithe.

If we were to say the obligation of *kibbud* is *mishel ben* — from the child's own resources — then feeding one's parents with *ma'aser ani* would constitute *poreia chovo mishel aniyim*: paying off your own financial obligation to your parents using money that belongs to the poor. *Kibbud av va'em* is not merely a pious aspiration; it is a *chov*, a genuine legal obligation, and satisfying that obligation with charity funds designated for others is improper. The *gemara* responds: perhaps the *beraita* addresses surplus beyond the actual *ma'aser* — extra produce that was set aside but not technically *ma'aser ani*. And even so, Rabbi Yehuda in the *beraita* is pointed: *tavo me'eira l'mi she-ma'achil et aviv ma'aser ani* — a curse on one who feeds his father *ma'aser ani*. The final note: even if technically permitted, *zeila bei milta* — it is simply not appropriate. The spirit of *kibbud* is not satisfied by finding technical workarounds.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִי הָכִי, הַיְינוּ דְּקָתָנֵי עֲלַהּ: אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: תָּבֹא מְאֵירָה לְמִי שֶׁמַּאֲכִיל אֶת אָבִיו מַעְשַׂר עָנִי. וְאִי לְהַעְדָּפָה, מַאי נָפְקָא מִינַּהּ?! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי זִילָא בֵּיהּ מִילְּתָא.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **If so,** consider **that which is taught with regard to** this *baraita*. **Rabbi Yehuda says: May a curse come upon one who feeds his father** the **poor man's tithe. And if** this *halakha*, that one may feed his father the poor man's tithe, was said **with regard to a surplus, what difference is there?** Since the son has fulfilled his obligation and simply adds something so that his father will have more, why is this person cursed? The Gemara answers: **Even so, it is a disrespectful matter** for one to feed his father with money that has been designated as charity for the poor.

## Extremes & Tests

### Eliezer's Standard

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: שָׁאֲלוּ אֶת רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר עַד הֵיכָן כִּיבּוּד אָב וָאֵם? אָמַר לָהֶם: כְּדֵי שֶׁיִּטּוֹל אַרְנָקִי וְיִזְרְקֶנּוּ לַיָּם בְּפָנָיו וְאֵינוֹ מַכְלִימוֹ. וְאִי אָמְרַתְּ מִשֶּׁל אָב, מַאי נָפְקָא לֵיהּ מִינֵּיהּ? בְּרָאוּי לְיוֹרְשׁוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara further suggests: **Come** and **hear: They asked Rabbi Eliezer how far** one must go in **honoring** his **father and mother.** Rabbi Eliezer **said to them: Such that** the father **takes a purse and throw it into the sea in front of** his son, **and** the son **does not embarrass him. And if you say** that the son honors him **from** the money **of the father, what difference** does it make **to** the son? Why would the son care if his father throws away his own purse? The Gemara answers: This is referring **to** a son who is **fit to inherit** from **him.** Since the son thinks that the money will eventually belong to him, he has cause for anger.

The *gemara* returns directly to the wallet question introduced in the Tarfon narrative. A *beraita* asks: how far must *kibbud av va'em* extend? Rabbi Eliezer answers: *k'dei she-yitol arnaki v'yizrakenu l'yam b'fanav v'eino machlimo* — to the extent that if your parent took your wallet and threw it into the sea in front of you, you would not become upset. This is the measure of *kibbud*.

One might ask: does this preclude later legal recourse? Can the child not sue for damages? The *gemara* does not address that directly here, but the emotional and behavioral standard is clear: in the moment, there can be no anger and no shaming of the parent. If the obligation is *mishel ben*, the money belongs to the child, and the parent is simply destroying the child's property. The test is whether the child's composure holds even then.

This connects back to the *gemara* from twenty *dafim* earlier, in the *sugya* of *harei at mekudeshet li* — where a woman takes the *kiddushin* money and throws it into the sea. Does that act represent acceptance and exercise of ownership, or rejection? Who would be entitled to hire divers to retrieve it? The emotional and legal parsing of who owns a thrown object, and what that act means, surfaces across multiple *sugyot* in this tractate.

The *gemara* then raises: *ve-eima be-mishel av* — perhaps we can explain the wallet test from the perspective of *mishel av*. If the money is the parent's money to begin with, why would the child get angry? The *nafka mina* would be in the case of *ra'uy le-yorsho* — property that the child stands to inherit. Even if the parent is spending their own money, a child who knows they will one day inherit that estate might resent seeing it wasted. The wallet test thus applies even in the *mishel av* framework: the child must not become angry even when witnessing the squandering of what they might eventually inherit.

### Huna's Test

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי הָא דְּרַבָּה בַּר רַב הוּנָא. דְּרַב הוּנָא קְרַע שִׁירָאֵי בְּאַנְפֵּי רַבָּה בְּרֵיהּ. אָמַר: אֵיזִיל אִיחְזֵי אִי רָתַח אִי לָא רָתַח. וְדִלְמָא רָתַח וְקָעָבַר אַ״לִּפְנֵי עִוֵּר לֹא תִתֵּן מִכְשֹׁל״! דְּמָחֵיל לֵיהּ לִיקָרֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And this is as** reflected in an incident involving **Rabba bar Rav Huna, when Rav Huna tore silk garments in front of his son Rabba.** Rav Huna had **said** to himself: **I will go** and **see if he becomes angry or does not become angry,** i.e., he wanted to test him and see whether his son Rabba would honor him. The Gemara asks: **But perhaps** his son **would become angry** and Rav Huna would thereby **violate** the prohibition of: **"Nor put a stumbling block before the blind"** (Leviticus 19:14), as by testing his son Rav Huna would have caused him to sin. The Gemara answers: It was a case **where** the father had **forgone his honor** from the outset. Consequently, even if the son grew angry with him, he would not have violated the mitzva.

This leads to one of the most unsettling stories on the *daf*: *ve-chi ha de-Rabbah bar Rav Huna de-Rav Huna kara shirai de-anpai Rabbah berei* — Rav Huna tore an expensive silk garment in front of his son Rabbah. *Ve-amar eizil achezi i ratach i lo ratach* — and Rav Huna said: I am going to go and see whether he gets angry or not. He was testing his son's *kibbud av va'em*.

This is a striking story, and the *gemara* immediately recognizes its *halachic* problems. First: perhaps Rabbah will get angry — and then *ka avar al lifnei iver* — Rav Huna has set up a situation in which his own son is caused to sin. You do not set traps designed to make people transgress. Second: even if we say Rav Huna was confident Rabbah would not get angry and had pre-emptively been *mochel* on being bothered, there remains *bal tashchit* — the prohibition against wantonly destroying objects of value. Third: if Rabbah knew his father was deliberately testing him, perhaps he would not get angry precisely because he understood it was a test — in which case the test proves nothing.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא קָעָבַר מִשּׁוּם ״בַּל תַּשְׁחִית״! דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ בְּפוּמְבְּיָינֵי. וְדִילְמָא מִשּׁוּם הָכִי לָא רָתַח? דְּעָבֵד לֵיהּ בִּשְׁעַת רִיתְחֵיהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** by tearing his clothes, **he violates the prohibition: Do not destroy** (see Deuteronomy 20:19). The Gemara answers **that** Rav Huna **made** a tear **at the seam,** so that the garment could be repaired. The Gemara asks: **Perhaps** it was **due to that** reason that **the son did not become angry,** because he saw that his father caused no actual damage? The Gemara answers: **He did this when** the son was already **angry** for some other reason, so that he would not notice this detail.

The *gemara* resolves each objection in turn, though the resolutions have a somewhat forced quality. On the *lifnei iver* problem: *de-machil lei* — Rav Huna must have already signaled some kind of forgiveness, perhaps indicating to Rabbah in advance that this was a test and that anger would not constitute a genuine transgression in context. On *bal tashchit*: *de-avad lei be-pumbeioni* — he tore it in the hem, in a place where a tailor could repair it and the value would not be diminished. It looked dramatic but caused no actual material loss. On the problem of Rabbah knowing it was a test: *de-avad lei be-sha'at retichei* — Rav Huna did it at a moment when Rabbah was already emotionally agitated, already primed to react, making the self-restraint genuinely tested.

One reasonable reading of the story is that Rav Huna was simply not thinking clearly — an elderly man behaving erratically — and the *gemara* is working to retroactively justify actions that were in the moment instinctive rather than calculated. This would itself be a kind of commentary on the earlier observation about elderly parents whose behavior may reflect cognitive or emotional decline.

There is an extended *sugya* within this section about a case from *Sanhedrin*: Rabbi Yechezkel asked his son Rami about what happens when those condemned to *sreifah* become mixed among those condemned to *sekilah*. Rabbi Shimon holds they should all receive *sekilah*, on the grounds that *sreifah* is more severe — the lesser punishment is applied. Rav Yehudah, the son, responded: *Abba, lo titnaya hachi* — Father, don't teach it this way. He proposed an alternative rationale: since the majority of those present are *niskalin*, the rule of *rov* governs, and there is no need to appeal to relative severity of punishment at all. His father pushed back from the *seifa* of the *mishna*, where the *chachamim* seem to require the severity-based rationale. Rav Yehudah held that the *seifa* represents the *chachamim* responding to Rabbi Shimon, pressing him on his own terms — and so *rov* remains the primary principle while the severity-comparison belongs to the internal *machloket* between Rabbi Shimon and the *chachamim*.

The entire discussion is brought here specifically because of the mode of speech: Rav Yehudah said to his father, *lo titnaya hachi* — don't teach it like this. That directness is what occasions Shmuel's rebuke in the following subsection.

### Shmuel Rebuke

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לֵיהּ שְׁמוּאֵל לְרַב יְהוּדָה: שִׁינָּנָא, לָא תֵּימָא לֵיהּ לַאֲבוּךְ הָכִי. דְּתַנְיָא: הֲרֵי שֶׁהָיָה אָבִיו עוֹבֵר עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה, אַל יֹאמַר לוֹ ״אַבָּא עָבַרְתָּ עַל דִּבְרֵי תוֹרָה״, אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״אַבָּא כָּךְ כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה״. ״כָּךְ כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה״ – צַעוֹרֵי קָא מְצַעַר לֵיהּ! אֶלָּא אוֹמֵר לוֹ: ״אַבָּא, מִקְרָא כָּתוּב בַּתּוֹרָה כָּךְ״.
>
> **Translation:** Later, **Shmuel said to Rav Yehuda: Big-toothed** one, **do not speak to your father like that,** as it is disrespectful. **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: If **one's father was transgressing a Torah matter, he should not say to him** explicitly: **Father, you transgressed a Torah matter. Rather, he should say to him: Father, so it is written in the Torah.** The Gemara asks: If he says to him directly: **This is** what is **written in the Torah, he will cause him suffering. Rather,** he should **say to him: Father, this verse is written in the Torah,** and he should proceed to quote the verse, from which his father will understand on his own that he has acted improperly.

Shmuel addressed Rav Yehudah — calling him by the nickname *Shinena*, which may refer to his sharpness in learning or to his broad smile — and said: *lo teima lei le-avuch hachi* — do not speak to your father this way. A *beraita* is cited: if you see your father violating a matter of Torah, do not say *Abba avarta al divrei Torah* — Father, you have transgressed the Torah. Rather say: *Abba, kach katuv ba-Torah* — Father, it is written thus in the Torah. The deferential formulation presents the teaching without accusation.

But even that formulation, the *gemara* pushes further, might cause the parent *tza'ar* — distress — to be told that the Torah says something that contradicts what they just did. Therefore the most refined approach: *ela omer lo Abba mikra katuv ba-Torah kach* — say, Father, there is a *pasuk* that reads such-and-such. Do not even frame it as "this is written in the Torah" with its implicit weight of accusation; simply cite the verse. Whether a given practice is *d'oraita* in the explicit sense or derived through *drashot* is itself a layered question — the *Rambam* in *Sefer Ha-Mitzvot* and *Peirush Ha-Mishna* is careful to distinguish between what is literally written in the Torah and what is derived through rabbinic interpretation, even when the latter carries *de'oraita* weight. But the *halachic* principle here is clear: deferential speech toward a parent who is erring requires the softest possible framing.

## Prioritization

### Father vs Mitzvah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלְעָזָר בֶּן מַתְיָא אוֹמֵר: אַבָּא אוֹמֵר: ״הַשְׁקֵינִי מַיִם״, וּמִצְוָה לַעֲשׂוֹת – מַנִּיחַ אֲנִי כְּבוֹד אַבָּא, וְעוֹשֶׂה אֶת הַמִּצְוָה. שֶׁאֲנִי וְאַבָּא חַיָּיבִים בַּמִּצְוָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Elazar ben Matya says:** If my **father says: Give me water, and** there is **a mitzva** for me **to perform** at the same time, **I set aside the honor of my father and perform the mitzva, as my father and I are** both **obligated in the mitzva.**

Elazar ben Masya raises a case: his father asks him for a glass of water, and at that same moment he has a *mitzvah* to perform. Should he set aside *kibbud av* and go do the *mitzvah*? His reasoning: *she-ani ve-abba chayavim be-mitzvah* — both my father and I are equally obligated in this *mitzvah*. Since the father is no less obligated than the son, the son need not subordinate himself to serve his father when an equal obligation beckons. The force of the question depends on the nature of the *mitzvah* as well — a *mitzvah overes*, one that will expire if not done immediately, poses the case more acutely than one that can wait.

### Delegation Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: אִם אֶפְשָׁר לַמִּצְוָה לֵיעָשׂוֹת עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים – תֵּיעָשֶׂה עַל יְדֵי אֲחֵרִים, וְיֵלֵךְ הוּא בִּכְבוֹד אָבִיו. אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה: הֲלָכָה כְּאִיסִי בֶּן יְהוּדָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Isi ben Yehuda says: If it is possible for** this **mitzva to be performed by others, let it be performed by others, and he should go** and attend **to the honor** due to **his father,** as the honor of his father is his obligation alone. **Rav Mattana says:** The ***halakha*** with regard to this matter **is in accordance with** the opinion of **Isi ben Yehuda.**

Issi ben Yehuda offers a more nuanced principle: *im efshar le-mitzvah le-asos al yedei acherim, te'aseh al yedei acherim, vayeilechu be-kibbud av* — if the *mitzvah* can be performed by others, let others do it, and you go fulfill *kibbud av*. The category of *mitzvah efshar la'asot al yedei acherim* — a *mitzvah* that others can discharge — versus a *mitzvah* that only you personally can fulfill appears across many *halachic* contexts. A *mitzvah shebegufo* or a *mitzvah* for which you bear a specific personal obligation cannot be handed off; nobody else can hear the *megillah* on your behalf. But communal obligations, or tasks that any qualified person can do, can in principle be delegated — and in such cases, *kibbud av va'em* takes precedence.

This principle has been applied in serious contemporary *halachic* discussions, including in *chardal* communities regarding whether a *yeshiva* student may serve as an EMT or volunteer medic during his *yeshiva* years. If there are other qualified individuals who can serve, it may be argued that ambulance work is a *mitzvah efshar la'asot al yedei acherim*, and the student's primary obligation is to remain in learning. Other communities reason differently, and the *gemara* explicitly records *beraitot* about *yeshiva* students joining military campaigns when necessary. The tension between different categories of obligation, and the finite hours in a day, is precisely what the *gemara* is navigating throughout this material.

## Teacher's Honor

### Forgiveness Limits

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר שֵׁילָא אָמַר רַב מַתְנָה אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: הָאָב שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל. הָרַב שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – אֵין כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Sheila says** that **Rav Mattana says** that **Rav Ḥisda says:** With regard to **a father who forgoes his honor, his honor is forgone,** and his son does not transgress if he does not treat him in the proper manner. By contrast, with regard to **a rabbi who forgoes his honor, his honor is not forgone.**

Rav Chisda (as reported through a chain of transmission: Rav Yitzchak bar Shila citing Rav Matna citing Rav Chisda) establishes a fundamental asymmetry: *ha-av she-machal al kevodo, kevodo machul* — if a parent forgives their own honor, waives the *kibbud* owed to them, that honor is indeed forgiven. The *mitzvah* is the parent's to waive. But *ha-rav she-machal al kevodo, ein kevodo machul* — if a teacher forgives their honor, that forgiveness is not effective. The honor owed to a teacher cannot be extinguished simply because the teacher consents.

The reason is embedded in the nature of the obligation. The *beraita* already established that *ein kavod ella Torah* — there is no honor except for Torah. The honor you show your teacher is not ultimately about the person; it is about the Torah they represent and transmit. And because it is *kavod ha-Torah*, not merely *kavod ha-rav*, the teacher cannot personally waive something that is not wholly theirs to waive.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב יוֹסֵף אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ הָרַב שֶׁמָּחַל עַל כְּבוֹדוֹ – כְּבוֹדוֹ מָחוּל. שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״וַה׳ הֹלֵךְ לִפְנֵיהֶם יוֹמָם״.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yosef says: Even** with regard to **a rabbi who forgoes his honor, his honor is forgone, as it is stated: "And the Lord went before them by day"** (Exodus 13:21). God Himself, the Teacher of the Jewish people, had forgone the honor due Him and took the trouble to guide the people.

Rav Yosef dissents: *afilu ha-rav she-machal al kevodo, kevodo machul* — even a teacher who forgives their honor has forgiven it. His proof: *va-Adonai holech lifneihem yomam* — God led the entire camp of Israel through the desert, and by doing so, God was in a sense *mochel* on God's own *kavod*, descending to lead and to serve. If God could be *mochel* on divine honor, a teacher can certainly waive their own.

### Torah Ownership

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: הָכִי הַשְׁתָּא?! הָתָם, הַקָּדוֹשׁ בָּרוּךְ הוּא עָלְמָא דִּילֵיהּ הוּא, וְתוֹרָה דִּילֵיהּ הִיא – מָחֵיל לֵיהּ לִיקָרֵיהּ, הָכָא תּוֹרָה דִּילֵיהּ הִיא?! הֲדַר אָמַר רָבָא: אִין, תּוֹרָה דִּילֵיהּ הִיא, דִּכְתִיב: ״וּבְתוֹרָתוֹ יֶהְגֶּה יוֹמָם וָלָיְלָה״.
>
> **Translation:** Rava **said: How can** these cases **be compared? There,** with regard to **the Holy One, Blessed be He, the world is His and the Torah is His,** and therefore He can **forgo His honor.** By contrast, **here, is it his Torah,** that the teacher can forgo its honor? **Rava then said: Yes,** if he studies, **it is his Torah, as it is written:** "For his delight is the Torah of the Lord, **and in his Torah he meditates day and night"** (Psalms 1:2). This indicates that at first it is "the Torah of the Lord," but after he studies, it becomes "his Torah."

Rava responds sharply: *heichi hashta? Hatam Hakadosh Baruch Hu alma dilei, ve-Torah dilei, machil likra. Hacha Torah dilei hi?* — How can you compare? There, God's world and God's Torah — so God can waive the honor. But here, is the Torah really the teacher's Torah?

This challenge is then answered by Rava himself, who resolves it with a counterintuitive move. He cites the verse *u've-Torato yehgeh yomam va-lailah* — and in *his* Torah he meditates day and night. The possessive *Torato* is interpreted as meaning that a person who truly engages with Torah acquires it as their own — it becomes *his* Torah. And therefore the honor a student shows a teacher *is* directed at the teacher's own Torah, not merely at something borrowed from God. On this reading, Rava affirms — rather than overturns — the position that the teacher's honor is genuine and personal, and therefore the teacher *does* have the right to waive it. The *sugya* thereby comes to rest on a view close to Rav Yosef's, though the reasoning is refined: the teacher's Torah is genuinely theirs because they have acquired it through total immersion, and they may therefore be *mochel* on the *kavod* that flows from it.

## Standing & Status

### Wine & Standing

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי?! וְהָא רָבָא מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי הִלּוּלָא דִּבְרֵיהּ וְדַל לֵיהּ כָּסָא לְרַב פָּפָּא וּלְרַב הוּנָא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוֹשֻׁעַ, וְקָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. לְרַב מָרִי וּלְרַב פִּנְחָס בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא, וְלָא קָמוּ מִקַּמֵּיהּ. אִיקְּפַד וַאֲמַר: הָנוּ רַבָּנַן רַבָּנַן, וְהָנוּ רַבָּנַן לָאו רַבָּנַן?
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But Rava served drinks** to the guests **at his son's wedding celebration, and he poured a cup for Rav Pappa and Rav Huna, son of Rav Yehoshua, and they stood before him** when he approached them. When he poured a cup **for Rav Mari and** for **Rav Pineḥas, son of Rav Ḥisda, they did not stand before him.** Rava **became angry and said:** Are **these Sages,** i.e., Rav Mari and Rav Pineḥas, **Sages, and** are **those Sages,** who stood to honor me, **not Sages?** Do you think you are so great that you are not required to honor a Sage?

The *gemara* closes with a pair of narratives about standing as a form of honor. Rava was serving wine at his son's wedding. Rav Papa and Rav Huna the son of Rabbi Yehoshua were present, and they stood up before Rav Mari and Rav Pinchas the son of Rav Chisda — but they did not stand before Rava. Those present said: *hani rabbanan rabbanan, ve-hani rabbanan lav rabbanan?* — are those Sages Sages, and are these Sages not Sages? Rava was offended. The story illustrates both the social stakes of the standing-up obligation and the judgment being made in real time about who merits such recognition.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:* וְתוּ, רַב פָּפָּא הֲוָה מַשְׁקֵי בֵּי הִלּוּלָא דְּאַבָּא מָר בְּרֵיהּ וְדַלִּי לֵיהּ כָּסָא לְרַבִּי יִצְחָק בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב יְהוּדָה, וְלָא קָם מִקַּמֵּיהּ וְאִיקְּפַד! אֲפִילּוּ הָכִי הִידּוּר מֶיעְבַּד לֵיהּ בָּעוּ.
>
> **Translation:** **And furthermore,** it happened that **Rav Pappa was serving drinks** to the guests **at the wedding celebration [*hillula*] of Abba Mar, his son, and he poured a cup for Rav Yitzḥak, son of Rav Yehuda, and he did not stand before him, and** Rav Pappa **became angry.** These anecdotes indicate that even when a rabbi forgoes the honor due to him by serving drinks to his guests, his honor is not forgone. The Gemara answers: A rabbi can forgo the full measure of honor due to him, but **even so,** others are **required to perform** some act of **reverence,** such as preparing to stand before him.

Similarly, Rav Papa was serving wine at the wedding of his son Abba Mar — whose name was likely Papa bar Papa, giving rise to the title *Abba Mar brei* — and he poured wine for Yitzchak his son and for Rav Yehudah, but did not stand up for them. Those present were offended. Yet the *gemara* notes: *afilu hachi hidar mavdei* — even so, they performed a *hidur*. A *hidur* is a partial rising, a gesture of deference that falls short of full standing — one begins to rise without fully completing the motion. This qualified rising is recognized as a meaningful intermediate form of honor, distinct from both full standing and complete indifference.

Here the *daf* concludes.
