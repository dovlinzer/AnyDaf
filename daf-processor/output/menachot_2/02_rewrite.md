# Menachot 2 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Opening & Overview

### Greeting & Masechet Intro

We begin a new *masechet*: Menachot, starting with *daf* 2. The first *mishna* is directly parallel to the first *mishna* in Zevachim, and the themes of the two tractates are deeply intertwined.

### Menachot Contents

Menachot deals with all of the grain sacrifices, and because it is quite a large *masechet* — comparable in scope to Zevachim — a range of additional topics manages to come up along the way. The *Omer*, for instance, is itself a type of grain sacrifice, and its attendant laws arise naturally. But through digression, there is also a fascinating section in the middle of the tractate devoted to *Tefillin*, *Mezuzah*, and *Tzitzit*. Other subjects will emerge as well.

---

## Mishna: Intent & Validity

### Shelo Lishman Principle

The *mishna* opens: *Kol ha-menachot she-nikmetzu shelo lishman kesherot ela shelo alu la-be'alim leshem chovah* — all *menachot* that were taken with the wrong intent are still valid, yet do not count toward the owner's obligation. If a *kohen* performed the *kemitzah* — the taking of the handful — with the wrong intent (and Rashi emphasizes here, as he did in Zevachim, that intent means articulated intent rather than a mere passing thought), the *mincha* as a *korban* remains fully valid: the *kohanim* may burn the *kometz* on the altar and eat the *shirayim*. However, if the owner had taken a vow to bring this *mincha*, that vow is not discharged.

### Exceptions: Chatat & Ken…

There are two exceptions: the *minchat chotei* and the *minchat kenaot*. Just as in Zevachim the *chatat* is exceptional — because a sin offering that becomes detached from its particular owner loses its entire purpose — so too here. The *minchat chotei* is a grain offering brought as an atonement. There are sacrifices that operate on a sliding scale (*oleh ve-yored*): certain oaths and certain cases of *tumah* allow a person of limited means to descend from the animal offering to a bird offering, and if they cannot afford even that, they bring a *mincha*. This *mincha* is a substitute for a *chatat*, and therefore carries the same *halacha*: if it is performed *shelo lishmo*, it is fully invalidated and must be destroyed rather than consumed. Similarly, the *minchat kenaot* — the *mincha* of the *sotah*, brought to determine whether she has committed adultery — is intrinsically linked to a specific individual and a specific determination. If it is not performed *lishmo*, it too is fully invalidated.

### Stamah & Explicit Intent

A further point carried over from Zevachim: *stamah* — unexplicated intent — is treated as valid. The problem arises only when there is explicitly wrong intent. As for what constitutes *shelo lishmo*, the most straightforward form is having in mind a different owner (*shinui be'alim*). But the more conceptually central form of *shelo lishmo* is having in mind a different *korban* — a different type of sacrifice. With animal sacrifices this is relatively easy to visualize: one might slaughter an *olah* while thinking it is a *shelamim* or a *chatat*. With a *mincha*, the question of what "wrong type" means will become a central issue in the *Gemara*.

---

## Four Services & Intent

### Parallel Services

The *mishna* is not limited to the *kemitzah* alone. The four *avodot* of a *mincha* are explicitly paralleled to the four *avodot* of an animal sacrifice. *Shechita* corresponds to *kemitzah*; catching the blood in a vessel corresponds to placing the *kometz* into a vessel; conveying the blood to the *mizbeach* corresponds to conveying the *kometz*; and sprinkling the blood corresponds to placing the *kometz* on the altar. The verse does not make this parallel explicit, but the Sages derived it. Therefore, just as we started with *shechita* in Zevachim, here we start with *kemitzah*, and just as all four acts in the animal sacrifice can be affected by wrong intent, so too *natan bi-kli* — placing in the vessel — *ve-halach* — the conveyance — and *ve-hiktir* — the burning — performed *shelo lishman* carry the same *halacha*: the *korban* is valid but the owners do not fulfill their obligation.

### Double Intent Cases

The *mishna* also addresses scenarios of double intent: *lishman ve-shelo lishman* or *shelo lishman ve-lishman* — where one's intent is partly correct and partly not. The ruling is *pesulah*: as long as the wrongful intent is present, whether it comes first or second, it invalidates the relevant aspect. The case of *lishman ve-shelo lishman* described in the *mishna* is: if one says this is a *minchat chotei* — which, as we have seen, requires *lishmo* — and simultaneously thinks it is also a *minchat nedava*, or vice versa, the wrong intent is operative and the result is invalidation.

### Oven/Fried Example

As a preview of where the *Gemara* will take us: the scenario of *shelo lishmo* for an ordinary *minchat nedava* is not about confusing a *mincha* with a *shelamim* or an *olah* — that would be almost nonsensical. Rather, it involves confusing one physical type of *mincha* with another. If, for example, a *ma'afeh tanur* — a baked oven *mincha* — is being offered and the *kohen* intends it as though it were a *marcheshes* — a deep-fried, bubbly *mincha* — that constitutes *shelo lishmo* for this purpose. The *Gemara* will explore whether such an intent can even be meaningful given the obvious physical reality in front of the *kohen*, but that is the scenario the *mishna* has in mind when it discusses double intent for an ordinary *minchat nedava*: *lishmo* as a *minchat chotei* and *shelo lishmo* as a *minchat nedava*, or the reverse. In such a case, what makes the intent "wrong" is not that anyone confuses a *mincha* with an animal sacrifice, but that the purpose — sin-expiation versus free-will — differs.

## Ela & Implications

### Use of Ela

The *Gemara* poses a stylistic question about the *mishna*: *l'mai li l'misnei ela* — why does the *mishna* use the word *ela* ("except") rather than simply saying *ve-lo* ("and not")? The *mishna* could have said the *menachot* are valid and did not count for the owners; instead it emphasizes that they are fully valid, *except* that the owners' obligation is unfulfilled. The force of *ela* is to underscore that the *mincha* as an object — as a *korban* — is entirely *kosher*. The unfulfilled obligation of the owner is a real and important point, but it is presented as a qualification of an otherwise complete and valid sacrifice, not as a general diminishment of status.

### No Multiple Invalidations

This emphasis has a practical implication: *ve-asur li-shunuyei* — one is forbidden to add further wrongful intent. One might reason: now that this *korban* has already been affected by one instance of *shelo lishmo*, what is the harm in yet another? The answer is that no: two acts of *shelo lishmo* do not combine to produce a *lishmo*. More fundamentally, once the *mishna* declares the *mincha* entirely and intrinsically *kosher*, the same standards apply to it as to any other *kosher* *mincha*. Its elevated status is not compromised; one is not released from the requirement to have proper intent for subsequent acts simply because one *shelo lishmo* thought has already occurred. This is analogous to a principle found elsewhere: if someone breaks their fast by eating inadvertently, the *Gemara* does not conclude they may now eat freely. Even when a status has been technically compromised, that does not license further violations — and certainly not here, where the *mishna* insists the *korban* itself remains fully intact.

### Rava's Proof

This principle is supported by Rava: *olah she-shachatah shelo lishmo asur lizrok damah shelo lishmo* — if an *olah* was slaughtered with wrong intent, it is still forbidden to sprinkle the blood with wrong intent. The fact that one act was performed *shelo lishmo* does not grant a license to continue doing so. The *Gemara* offers two grounds for this principle: *sevara* — pure logic (*mishum d'shani bah kol hani, lishnei bah pneisa?* — just because you made one mistake you would make another?) — and a verse: *motza sefasecha tishmor ve-asisa ka'asher nadarta la-Shem elokecha nedava*. This verse, whose analysis is developed further in the next section, teaches that even when a *neder* ends up functioning as a *nedava*, the full standards of proper service still apply.

---

## Neder vs. Nedava

### Neder Definition

The verse reads: *motza sefasecha tishmor ve-asita ka'asher nadarta la-Shem elokecha nedava* — what comes out of your lips you shall observe and do, as you vowed to God, a *nedava*. The *Gemara* is puzzled: if one took a *neder*, why does the verse call it a *nedava*? The two categories are halakhically distinct. A *neder* is a personal obligation — *harei alai*, "I accept upon myself an obligation to bring a *mincha*" — such that even if the designated *korban* is lost or destroyed, the person must bring another. A *nedava* is a designation of the object itself — *harei zo*, "this is hereby consecrated" — such that if it is lost, the obligation lapses along with it.

### Vow Fulfillment

The *Gemara* resolves the ambiguity: *ella im ke-moshe nadarta asita* — if you performed it as you originally vowed. If the *korban* was brought with the correct *lishma*, in accordance with the original *neder*, then *yehei neder* — it counts as a *neder* and the person receives full credit for the discharge of their obligation. This verse thus provides one of the textual anchors for the *mishna*'s foundational principle that a *korban* brought *shelo lishmo* remains valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation.

### Nedava Status

*Ve-im lav* — but if it was performed with different intent — *tehei nedava*, it functions as a *nedava*. The *korban* is entirely *kosher* and the *shirayim* may be eaten and the *kometz* burned, but the owner receives no credit toward any personal obligation. This is because what was a *neder* has now been transformed, in its function, into a *nedava*. Importantly, this principle also makes clear that one may not say: since this *korban* no longer satisfies the owner's obligation, it now has the status of a mere *nedava*, and since a *nedava* never satisfies an obligation, there is no harm in another act of *shelo lishma*. That reasoning is flawed. Even an ordinary *nedava* — one that began as a free-will offering with no attendant personal obligation — must be performed with proper intent. The requirement of proper *kavanah* is not merely a consequence of the obligation; it is a function of the integrity of the *korban* itself. A *nedava* is a fully valid *korban* and must be treated as such. Thus, the *kohen* performing the service is never given license for wrong intent on the grounds that the owner's obligation is not at stake.

## Mishna Authorship

### Rabbi Shimon's View

The *Gemara* now turns to a striking question: *leima masnisatin de-lo ke-Rabbi Shimon* — perhaps our *mishna* does not reflect the position of Rabbi Shimon? A *baraisa* is cited in which Rabbi Shimon states: *kol ha-menachot she-nikmetzu shelo lishman kesherot ve-alu la-be'alim leshem chovah* — all *menachot* taken with wrong intent are valid and do indeed fulfill the owners' obligation. Rabbi Shimon does not dispute the general principle that *shelo lishma* is problematic; he holds that by *menachot* specifically, such a thought simply cannot be operative in any meaningful way.

### Physical Evidence Argument

The reason Rabbi Shimon gives is: *she-ein ha-menachot domot le-zevachim* — because *menachot* are not like animal sacrifices. By a *mincha*, the physical reality renders wrong intent self-evidently absurd. The act itself proclaims what it is.

### Examples of Self-Evidence

Rabbi Shimon provides illustrations. If you perform the *kemitzah* of a *machavat* — a flat, lightly-oiled fried *mincha* — while thinking it is a *marcheshes* — a deeper, bubbly, more heavily-oiled type — *ma'aseha mochiachin aleha she-hi machavat*: the act speaks for itself. The thing in your hands is plainly what it is; your contrary thought is simply contradicted by the observable reality. Similarly, if it is a *charevah* — a dry *mincha* such as a *minchat chotei* — and you are thinking it is a *belulah* — mixed with oil — the flour in your hand contains no oil and your thought is manifestly false. By contrast, *aval bi-zevachim eino kein* — with animal sacrifices this is not the case. *Shechitah achat le-kullan ve-zrikah achat le-kullan* — the act of slaughter looks the same regardless of what type of sacrifice is being offered, as does the act of sprinkling; there is nothing in the physical reality that reveals the intended category of the *korban*.

---

## Intent & Act Definition

### Intent Interpretation

This position of Rabbi Shimon admits of two possible interpretations. The first is that when *kavanah* is directly contradicted by physical reality, we simply discard it as silly — it is *batel da'ato*, nullified, because no reasonable person would credit it. The second, and arguably more precise, interpretation is that wrong *kavanah* does not invalidate merely because there is a thought in one's head; rather, it invalidates because *kavanah* defines the quality of an act. When one slaughters an *olah* with the intent of a *chatat*, one has performed a *shechitat chatat* on an *olah* — the *kavanah* has redefined what the act is. Similarly, a *zrikat shelamim* on an *olah* redefines the act. Intent is operative precisely because it gives the act its halakhic character.

### Physical Reality Priority

On this second interpretation, when physical reality is neutral — when nothing visible distinguishes one type of *shechita* from another — the *kavanah* can step in and define the act. But when physical reality speaks loudly and unambiguously, the *kavanah* that runs against it cannot succeed in redefining the act. It is not possible to say one performed a *kemitzat marcheshes* when the material in hand is plainly a *machavat* or a *ma'afeh tanur*. The act is defined far more powerfully by what is physically in front of the *kohen* than by a thought that contradicts it. This is the more persuasive formulation of Rabbi Shimon's position: wrong *kavanah* fails not because it is discarded as irrational, but because it cannot achieve the only thing that makes it halakhically significant — namely, redefining the act — when the act is already defined by incontrovertible physical evidence.

### Stamah Principle

This framework also elegantly explains why *stamah* — the absence of any expressed intent — is valid. One might ask: if there is a positive requirement for *kavanah*, why should the absence of *kavanah* suffice? The answer is that the requirement is not that one must affirmatively think the right thought; the requirement is that the act be, halakhically, a *shechitat olah* or a *zrikat olah*, as the case may be. In the absence of any problematic intent, the act is defined by the identity of the *korban* itself. If the animal is an *olah* and one slaughters it without expressing any contrary intent, one has performed a *shechitat olah* by default. The physical metaphysical reality — that the animal is an *olah* — is what defines the act when *kavanah* is neutral. *Kavanah* becomes operative only when it tries to redefine what would otherwise be correctly categorized. So *stamah* is perfectly valid, but an articulated wrong *kavanah* derails, because it attempts to — and under conditions of physical ambiguity, succeeds in — redefining the act away from its proper category. Rabbi Shimon's contribution is to say that when physical reality removes all ambiguity, the *kavanah* cannot succeed in this redefinition.

## Rav Ashi's Resolution

### Vessel vs. Category

The *Gemara* now considers the apparent contradiction between two statements of Rabbi Shimon: in one place he says wrong intent is meaningless by *menachot*, and yet shortly below (as the *Gemara* will show in full) he seems to say it does have effect. The *Gemara* introduces the resolution of Rav Ashi: *kan be-kometz machavat le-shem marcheshes, kan be-kometz minchat machavat le-shum minchat marcheshes* — the two cases differ in one critical word. In the first, one says "a *machavat* for the sake of a *marcheshes*" — referring to the physical vessel or form. In the second, one says "a *minchat machavat* for the sake of a *minchat marcheshes*" — referring to the halakhic category of *mincha*.

### Form vs. Halakha

When one's intent is articulated purely in terms of the physical vessel — *machavat* versus *marcheshes* — it is a thought about the concrete, observable thing, and since the physical reality directly contradicts it, it is meaningless and does not invalidate. But when one's intent is phrased in terms of halakhic category — *minchat machavat* versus *minchat marcheshes* — the situation is different. Halakhic categories do not always map cleanly onto observable physical reality. Just as halakhic adulthood (*bar da'as*) does not perfectly track visible maturity, so too the halakhic identity of a *mincha* as belonging to one category or another is not purely a matter of what one can observe in the hand. Therefore, a *kavanah* expressed in terms of halakhic category — "I am performing this as a *minchat marcheshes*" — is not obviously and absurdly contradicted by the physical reality, and retains the capacity to redefine the act halakhically. That *kavanah* therefore does invalidate.

### Reconciliation Attempt

According to Rav Ashi, there is thus no contradiction in Rabbi Shimon's positions. The *baraisa* where Rabbi Shimon says wrong intent is meaningless refers to intent expressed about the physical form of the vessel. The sources where Rabbi Shimon acknowledges that wrong intent has effect refer to intent expressed about the halakhic category of the *mincha*. And our *mishna* — which discusses *menachot* brought with wrong intent — is dealing with the case of *minchat machavat* versus *minchat marcheshes* in the halakhic-category sense, and therefore could in principle be consistent with Rabbi Shimon's view.

---

## Rabbah's Distinction

### Owner vs. Offering Type

The *Gemara* now turns to the resolution proposed by Rabbah: *lo kashya, kan be-shinuie kodesh kan be-shinuie ba'alim* — the distinction is not about the form of expression but about the content of the wrong intent. Rabbi Shimon concedes that an intent directed at a different *owner* — *shinui ba'alim* — does invalidate. The reason is that nothing in the physical act reveals who the owner is; the identity of the owner is not apparent from observing the *kemitzah*. Therefore, a *kavanah* about the wrong owner is not contradicted by observable reality and retains its halakhic force. By contrast, an intent directed at a different *type* of offering — *shinui kodesh* — is precisely what is revealed by the physical reality of the *mincha*, and therefore does not invalidate according to Rabbi Shimon.

### Mishna Fit

The *Gemara* then asks: but our *mishna*'s illustrative example of *shelo lishman* — the case of *lishm minchat chotei* and *lishm minchat nedava* — is a case of *shinui kodesh*, a change in the type of offering, not *shinui ba'alim*. And that is exactly the case where Rabbi Shimon says wrong intent does not invalidate. Therefore, according to Rabbah, our *mishna* cannot be reconciled with Rabbi Shimon — the scenario the *mishna* uses to illustrate invalidation is precisely the scenario Rabbi Shimon would permit.

---

## Rabbi Shimon Contradicti…

### Position 1: No Invalidat…

To this point, we have seen Rabbi Shimon in the *baraisa* saying that wrong intent by *menachot* is meaningless because the physical reality speaks for itself.

### Position 2: Intent Inval…

Yet in another *baraisa*, Rabbi Shimon says explicitly: *kodesh kodashim hi ke-chatat u-ke-asham* — this verse comparing the *mincha* to the *chatat* and the *asham* yields practical consequences. *Minchat chotei harei hi ke-chatat*: since the *minchat chotei* is like a *chatat*, performing it *shelo lishmah* renders it *pesulah* — fully invalid, just as a *chatat* done *shelo lishmo* is invalid. And *minchat nedava harei hi ke-asham*: an ordinary *minchat nedava* done *shelo lishmah* is *kesheirah* — valid — just as the *asham* is valid when done with wrong intent. But just as the *asham* in such a case is *kasher ve-eino meratzeh* — valid as a sacrifice but not credited to the owner — so too the *minchat nedava* done *shelo lishmah* is valid but does not fulfill the owner's obligation. So Rabbi Shimon does acknowledge that *shelo lishmo* can affect a *mincha* — either fully invalidating it (*minchat chotei*) or at minimum leaving the owner without credit (*minchat nedava*).

### Reconciliation Needed

These two positions appear to be in direct tension: Rabbi Shimon says wrong intent is meaningless by *menachot*, and yet Rabbi Shimon also says wrong intent by *menachot* can fully invalidate or at least deprive the owner of credit. The *Gemara* must determine which cases fall into which category.

## Abiyi's Challenge

### Broad Comparison Issue

Rabbah's resolution was that Rabbi Shimon distinguishes between *shinui ba'alim* (wrong owner — intent has effect) and *shinui kodesh* (wrong type — intent has no effect). Abaye challenges this: *michdi machshava de-fasla Rachmana heikesha hi* — the principle that *kavanah* can invalidate a *mincha* derives from the verse's comparison to *chatat* and *asham*. That verse draws a full and undifferentiated comparison. If the verse makes this comparison across the board, what justifies applying it only to *shinui ba'alim* and not to *shinui kodesh*? *Mai li shinui kodesh, mai li shinui ba'alim* — once the verse links the *mincha* to the *chatat* and *asham* in terms of *kavanah*, all forms of wrong intent should be equally operative.

### Sevara Limits Scope

Rabbah responds: *mah semuchin de-ka amar Rabbi Shimon, sevara hi* — the verse may appear categorical, but Rabbi Shimon applies his *sevara*, his logical reasoning, to determine the scope of the verse's application. This is the classic principle that Rabbi Shimon *doresh ta'ama de-kra* — he interprets the scope of a verse in light of its underlying reason. The most famous instance is the prohibition against taking as collateral the garment of a widow (*lo tachavol beged almana*): Rabbi Shimon holds that if a wealthy widow is involved, the reason for the prohibition does not apply, and therefore the prohibition does not apply. (His reasoning for the restriction itself is characteristically unexpected: he holds the concern is that constantly returning a poor person's collateral each evening requires repeated visits to the widow's home, which raises questions about propriety; a wealthy widow, whose collateral need not be returned daily, generates no such concern.) The broader principle is: Rabbi Shimon allows his understanding of the *ta'am* — the reason — to limit the scope of an otherwise categorical verse.

Here too, the verse compares *menachot* to *chatat* and *asham* in terms of *kavanah*, but Rabbi Shimon uses his *sevara* to limit that comparison. The principle is: *machshava de-lo minkara pasla Rachmana* — if a thought is not obviously contradicted by observable reality, the Torah does invalidate based on it. *Machshava de-minkara, lo pasla Rachmana* — if the thought is obviously contradicted by what is manifestly happening, it does not invalidate. The verse is thus not categorical; its application is modulated by Rabbi Shimon's logical principle that intent is only meaningful when it is not self-evidently absurd.

## Bird Sacrifices Analysis

### Location-Based Intent

The *Gemara* now probes Rabbi Shimon's principle by examining bird sacrifices. A bird *olah* (*olat ha-of*) is performed at the top of the *mizbeach*, while a bird *chatat* (*chatat ha-of*) is performed at the bottom. The *Gemara* asks: if one performed the *melika* — the pinching of the neck — of a bird *olah* at the top of the *mizbeach* with the intent that it is a bird *chatat*, should not the physical location make that intent self-evidently wrong and therefore meaningless according to Rabbi Shimon?

### Flexibility of Acts

The *Gemara* responds: *melika be-chol makom ba-mizbeach kesheirah* — the act of *melika* may be performed at any location on the altar. What determines above or below is where one squirts or squeezes out the blood, not where the *melika* itself occurs. Since a *melika* at the top is entirely consistent with a *chatat ha-of* — the *kohen* could perform *melika* at the top and then bring the bird's neck to the bottom for the *haza* — the act of *melika* at the top does not obviously betray that this is an *olah* rather than a *chatat*. The *kavanah* is therefore not obviously contradicted, and retains its force. The *Gemara* then moves to the blood act itself: if one squeezed out the blood (*mitzui dam*) of a bird *olah* at the top with intent for a *chatat ha-of*, that should be self-evidently wrong, since the blood of a *chatat ha-of* is squirted (*haza*) at the bottom, not squeezed at the top. Yet the *Gemara* responds here as well: *dilma mitzui de-vatar haza hu* — perhaps this squeezing is the squeezing of residual blood performed after the *haza* at the bottom, a procedure which may be done anywhere on the altar. *Mitzui dama be-chol makom ba-mizbeach kesheirah.* So even this act, performed at the top, is not obviously inconsistent with a *chatat ha-of*; such an act could exist as part of the procedure for a *chatat ha-of* after the primary *haza* has already been done at the bottom. The *kavanah* therefore retains its capacity to redefine the act.

### Bird vs. Grain

This is the key contrast with *menachot*: by bird sacrifices, even acts that appear to be inconsistent with the intended category turn out to be potentially consistent with it, because of the flexibility of where various steps may be performed. There is always some possibility that the act could belong to the intended category. That is sufficient to keep the *kavanah* operative.

### Reverse Case

The *Gemara* then takes the reverse case: a bird *chatat* whose blood was squirted (*haza*) at the bottom with the intent that it is a bird *olah*. Here the *Gemara* agrees: *hachi nami* — you are correct. In this case, the *kavanah* is self-evidently wrong. *Maaseh mochiah alav de-chatas hi* — the act speaks for itself as a *chatat*: the blood is being squirted at the bottom, and an *olah* is always done at the top with squeezing, not squirting. The wrong intent here is indeed obviously contradicted by the physical reality of the act, and therefore, consistent with Rabbi Shimon's principle, it would not invalidate.

### Tosafot's Cases

Tosafot raises a related challenge, noting that the *Gemara* itself could have asked about certain animal sacrifices where the act is also self-evident. The *Gemara* notes the general formulation that by Zevachim *shechitah achat le-kullan* — all slaughters look alike — but Tosafot points out that by *zerika* this is not always true. A *chatat* whose blood is applied at the upper corners with a finger (*lemala be-karnot u-ve-etzba*) while one has the intent for an *olah* — whose blood is applied below at the base (*lemata be-yesod u-vi-zerika min ha-keli*) — presents obvious physical differences that should make the wrong intent self-evidently absurd. Tosafot suggests that *ein hachi nami*: one could indeed apply Rabbi Shimon's principle there as well, and perhaps Rabbi Shimon would agree that in such cases the wrong intent does not invalidate for the animal sacrifice either. The *Gemara* may simply not have raised these cases because they are not the primary focus, but the logic of Rabbi Shimon's principle potentially extends there.

## Rabbi Shimon Synthesis

### Wrong Owner Matters

To summarize Rabbi Shimon's position as it emerges from the *sugya*: Rabbi Shimon agrees that wrong intent regarding a different *owner* — *shinui ba'alim* — has full halakhic effect. The identity of the owner is not revealed by any observable physical act; there is nothing in the *kemitzah* or the burning of the *kometz* that tells an observer whose *korban* this is. Therefore, a *kavanah* directed at the wrong owner is not contradicted by the physical reality and retains its capacity to redefine the act. It is a *machshava de-lo minkara* — a thought that is not self-evidently falsified — and therefore invalidates.

### Offering Type Variable

As for wrong intent regarding the *type* of offering — *shinui kodesh* — Rabbi Shimon's position depends on whether the wrong intent is self-evident. When the type of *mincha* is physically obvious from what is in the *kohen*'s hand, the wrong *kavanah* is meaningless and does not affect the validity or the owner's fulfillment of obligation. When the type is not self-evident — whether because the *kavanah* is expressed in terms of halakhic category rather than physical form (Rav Ashi's distinction), or because the act itself is physically ambiguous (as with certain acts of bird sacrifice) — the wrong *kavanah* does retain force and can invalidate or deprive the owner of credit.
