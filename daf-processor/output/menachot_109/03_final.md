# Menachot 109 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Kedusha Transfer

### Sanctification Types

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָצָה יָבִיא בִּדְמֵיהֶן שְׁנַיִם. אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר זְבִיד אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״שׁוֹר זֶה עוֹלָה״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה״ – הוּקְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** if **he wishes he may bring two** bulls **with its** redemption **money. Rabbi Menashya bar Zevid says** that **Rav says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where one said: This bull is** hereby **a burnt offering. But** if he **said: This bull** is incumbent **upon me** to bring as **a burnt offering,** his responsibility for it in a case where it became blemished **is fixed.**

We begin slightly back on 108b, at the passage before the mishna: *Ratza yavi bidmeihem shenayim* — one may bring two animals with the redemption money. The context is the transfer of kedusha: when a person sanctifies an animal and it subsequently develops a mum, the kedusha is transferred through redemption, and the proceeds may then be applied to other animals for an olah.

Rav Menashya bar Zavid said in the name of Rav: this applies only when one says *שור זה *Olah** — "this ox is an olah." In that formulation, the kedusha adheres directly to the animal. If the animal develops a mum, one redeems it and may apply the proceeds to any other suitable animal for an olah — even a different species or a different number of animals. However, if one says *שור זה עלי *Olah** — "this ox is upon me as an olah" — then *hukva*, it is fixed. The phrasing creates a personal obligation, not merely a sanctification of the object, and one would therefore be required to bring a single ox of the same kind when transferring the kedusha.

### Alai Lehavio Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְדִלְמָא ״עָלַי לַהֲבִיאוֹ״ קָאָמַר? אֶלָּא אִי אִיתְּמַר הָכִי אִיתְּמַר, אָמַר רַב מְנַשְּׁיָא בַּר זְבִיד אָמַר רַב: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״שׁוֹר זֶה עוֹלָה״, אִי נָמֵי אָמַר ״שׁוֹר זֶה עָלַי עוֹלָה״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״שׁוֹר זֶה וְדָמָיו עָלַי עוֹלָה״ – הוּקְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **But perhaps** when **he said:** This bull is incumbent upon me, he meant: It is incumbent **upon me to bring it** as an offering, but he did not intend to accept responsibility in case it becomes blemished. **Rather, if it was stated** that there is a case where one is responsible if the animal becomes blemished, **it was stated as follows: Rabbi Menashya bar Zevid says** that **Rav says:** The Sages **taught** this *halakha* **only** in a case **where one said: This bull is** hereby **a burnt offering, or he said: This bull** is incumbent **upon me** to bring as **a burnt offering. But** if **he said: This bull and its value** are incumbent **upon me** to bring as **a burnt offering,** his responsibility for it in a case where it becomes blemished **is fixed.**

The Gemara is not satisfied with this distinction. It asks: *Ve-dilma alai lehavio ka-amar* — perhaps even when one says "upon me," one means "upon me to bring this particular one." That is, the two formulations are being conflated: saying "this ox upon me as an olah" occupies a middle ground between the purely personal obligation ("I obligate myself to bring an ox") and the direct sanctification ("this ox is an olah"). But in either case, once the animal develops a mum and can no longer be brought, the obligation cannot be satisfied by that animal anyway, and thus the flexibility of transfer should apply regardless.

The Gemara therefore reformulates the teaching. Rav Menashya bar Zavid said in the name of Rav: the rule permitting flexible transfer applies whether one says *שור זה *Olah** or *שור זה עלי *Olah** — in both cases, since the specific animal is named and cannot be brought once blemished, the kedusha may be transferred to whatever one wishes. The process is not a direct exchange but a two-step redemption: one first redeems the blemished animal with money and then designates that money for a new animal.

### Animal vs. Value

*[Continued from above]*

The case where transfer is restricted is a different formulation altogether: *שור זה ודמיו עלי *Olah** — "this ox and its monetary value I accept upon myself to bring as an olah." Here, by explicitly including the value of the animal in the obligation, one has undertaken to bring a korban equivalent in value to a single ox. If the animal develops a mum, the kedusha must be transferred to something of comparable value — not to two sheep or two oxen of lesser value than the original — because the obligation has been fixed both to the animal and to its value. This concludes the final mishna of the previous discussion, and we move on to a new topic.

## Ambiguous Designation

### Two vs. Three Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָאוֹמֵר ״אֶחָד מִכְּבָשַׂי הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ וְ״אֶחָד מִשְּׁוָרַי הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, וְהָיוּ לוֹ שְׁנַיִם – הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶקְדֵּשׁ. שְׁלֹשָׁה – בֵּינוֹנִי שֶׁבָּהֶם הֶקְדֵּשׁ. ״פֵּירַשְׁתִּי, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַה שֶּׁפֵּירַשְׁתִּי״, אוֹ שֶׁ״אָמַר לִי אַבָּא, וְאֵינִי יוֹדֵעַ מַה״ – הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one who says: One of my lambs is consecrated, or: One of my bulls is consecrated, and he has two** lambs or bulls, **the larger of them is consecrated.** If he has **three** lambs or bulls, **the middle-sized** animal **among them is consecrated.** If one says: **I specified** the lamb or bull that is to be consecrated **but I do not know what** animal **I specified** in my vow, **or** he says **that: My father said to me** before his death that he consecrated one of the lambs or the bulls, **but I do not know what** animal he consecrated, **the largest of them is consecrated.**

The next mishna introduces a related but distinct question. If someone says *אחד מכבשי *hekdesh they're probably giving** — "one of my sheep is hekdesh" — or similarly for oxen, but does not specify which animal, how do we interpret the declaration?

If the person has two animals, the mishna rules: *hagadol shebehen hekdesh* — the larger one is hekdesh. The assumption is that a person who sanctifies does so generously, giving the more valuable gift. If the person has three animals, the mishna rules: *Beinoni she-ba-hen hekdesh* — the middle one is hekdesh. This is initially puzzling. If we assume generous intent, why not always assume the most expensive? The Gemara will need to address this tension.

The mishna then introduces another case: if one explicitly specified which animal was meant but has forgotten — or if one's father, on his deathbed, said he had sanctified one of his animals but died before clarifying which — and the person has a number of animals in the herd, the ruling is *hagadol shebehen hekdesh* — the largest is hekdesh. This appears to contradict the three-animal case and requires explanation.

### Shmuel's Explanation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא מַקְדִּישׁ – בְּעַיִן יָפָה מַקְדִּישׁ, אֵימָא סֵיפָא: בֵּינוֹנִי שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶקְדֵּשׁ, אַלְמָא מַקְדִּישׁ – בְּעַיִן רָעָה מַקְדִּישׁ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The first clause of the mishna teaches that if one says: One of my lambs is consecrated, and he has two lambs, the larger one is consecrated. The Gemara infers: **Apparently, one who consecrates, consecrates generously.** But **say the latter clause** of the mishna: If he has three lambs, **the middle-sized** animal **among them is consecrated. Apparently, one who consecrates, consecrates sparingly.** How can this contradiction be resolved?

The Gemara observes: **Alma* *Makdish somebody* בעין יפה *Makdish somebody** — from the two-animal case we see that one who sanctifies does so with a generous eye, giving the most valuable. But from the three-animal case: *Alma makdish b'ayin ra makdish* — one who sanctifies does so with a stingy eye, giving only the middle one. How are these reconciled?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: חוֹשְׁשִׁין אַף לְבֵינוֹנִי, דִּלְגַבֵּי קָטָן – עַיִן יָפָה הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Shmuel said:** The presumption is that one who consecrates, consecrates generously. When the latter clause of the mishna states that the middle-sized animal is consecrated, it does not mean that only the middle-sized animal is consecrated. Rather, the larger animal is consecrated, and additionally **we are concerned,** i.e., we must take in consideration the possibility, **that the** middle-sized animal is consecrated, as **compared to** consecrating **the small** animal, consecrating the middle-sized animal **is generous.** Therefore, the vow could have been referring to either the large animal or the middle-sized animal.

Shmuel resolves the tension: *חיישינן אף לבינונית דלגבי קטן עין יפה **Shechitah lav sheirus hu*** — we are also concerned for the possibility that the middle one was intended, because the middle one is generous relative to the worst. The ruling does not mean we are certain the person meant only the middle animal. Rather, when there are three animals, the statement is genuinely ambiguous — we cannot be certain whether the person meant the second or the first. What we can say is that the person meant something better than the worst, and therefore the smallest is excluded. The middle animal and the best animal are both live possibilities.

### Blemish Technique

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֵיכִי עָבֵיד? אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא בַּר רַב: מַמְתִּין לוֹ עַד שֶׁיּוּמַם, וּמַחֵיל לֵיהּ לִקְדוּשְּׁתֵיהּ בַּגָּדוֹל.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **How should he act?** He consecrated only one of them, and it is uncertain which animal should be sacrificed. **Rabbi Ḥiyya bar Rav said: He should wait until** the middle-sized animal **becomes blemished and** then **desacralize it** by transferring its sanctity **onto the large** animal, which is then sacrificed on the altar.

This raises the practical question: *heichi avid* — what does one actually do? Rabbi Chiya bar Abba said: *mamtin lo ad sheyumam* — one waits until the middle animal develops a blemish. Once it is blemished, one transfers its kedusha to the larger animal: *ומחליא לקדושתיה אגדול*. One then brings the large animal as the korban.

The logic is elegant. If the original declaration referred to the middle animal, then now that it has a mum, one may transfer its kedusha to the large animal and bring the large one. If the original declaration referred to the large animal, then the transfer is superfluous but harmless — one brings the large animal in any case. Either way, the large animal is brought.

One cannot simply exchange one animal directly for another, because the Torah prohibits *תמורה* — direct substitution of a sanctified animal: *lo yamir oto tov b'ra ve-ra b'tov*, and the Gemara understands *ra b'tov* to include even a blemished animal being replaced by an unblemished one. The workaround is indirect: one redeems the blemished animal with money and then designates the money for the new animal. This is entirely permissible.

### Mixed-Up Animals

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן, אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא דְּאָמַר ״אֶחָד מִשְּׁוָורַי הֶקְדֵּשׁ״, אֲבָל אָמַר ״שׁוֹר בִּשְׁוָורַי הֶקְדֵּשׁ״ – הַגָּדוֹל שֶׁבָּהֶן הֶקְדֵּשׁ, ״תּוֹרָא בְּתוֹרַאי״ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna teaches that if one had three lambs or bulls, the middle-sized animal is consecrated. **Rav Naḥman said** that **Rabba bar Avuh said:** The Sages **taught** that the middle-sized animal is consecrated **only when he said: One of my bulls is** hereby **consecrated. But** if **he said: A bull** from **among my bulls is** hereby **consecrated,** only **the largest of them is consecrated.** It is as if **he said: The** most valuable **bull** from **among my bulls [*tora betorai*]** is consecrated.

Rav Nachman said in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha: this entire discussion about the middle animal applies only when one says *אחד משורי *hekdesh they're probably giving** — "one of my oxen." But if one says *שור בשורי *hekdesh they're probably giving** — "an ox from my oxen" — the ruling is simply that the largest is hekdesh, because the phrase *Torah b'torai ka'amar* — you are saying "the choice ox of my oxen," and the most prominent member of the herd is implied.

### House Among Houses

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָאָמַר רַב הוּנָא, אָמַר רַבִּי חִיָּיא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּעוּלָּא: הָאוֹמֵר לַחֲבֵירוֹ ״בַּיִת בְּבָתַּי אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״ – מַרְאֵהוּ עֲלִיָּיה, לָאו מִשּׁוּם דִּגְרִיעַ? לָא, מְעוּלֶּה שֶׁבַּבָּתִּים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Is that so? But didn't Rav Huna say** that **Rabbi Ḥiyya said in the name of Ulla: One who says to another: I am selling you a house** from **among my houses, can show him an loft [*aliyya*],** since he did not specify which house he is selling? Is this **not because** the loft is the **worst** of his houses? If so, when one says: A bull from among my bulls is hereby consecrated, he is presumably referring to the least valuable of his bulls. The Gemara answers: **No,** Ulla did not say that the seller gives the purchaser a loft, but rather **the best [*me'ula*] of** his **houses.**

The Gemara challenges this: surely the same reasoning should apply to the case of a house? If someone says *bayit beveiti ani mocher lach I am selling* — "I am selling you a house from among my houses" — one might think this means the finest house. Yet we rule that one may point to the attic (*עלייה*) and say that is what was sold. An attic is a valid *bayit* but is less desirable than the ground floor. So the phrase "a house from my houses" does not mean the best house. Why then does "an ox from my oxen" mean the best ox?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לוֹקֵחַ קָא אָמְרַתְּ? שָׁאנֵי לוֹקֵחַ, דְּיַד בַּעַל הַשְּׁטָר עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Are you saying** that the statement of Rabba bar Avuh applies in the case of **a purchaser? A purchaser is different, as** there is a principle in the *halakhot* of commerce that in a case involving a dispute between the seller and the purchaser, **the owner of the document** of sale, i.e., the purchaser, **is at a disadvantage,** as a document is always interpreted as narrowly as possible. Therefore, the seller can claim that he has sold the buyer the fallen house or the dead slave.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מֵיתִיבִי: ״בַּיִת בְּבָיתַּי אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״, וְנָפַל – מַרְאֵהוּ נָפוּל, ״עֶבֶד בַּעֲבָדַיי אֲנִי מוֹכֵר לָךְ״, וָמֵת – מַרְאֵהוּ מֵת.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara **raises an objection** to the statement of Rabba bar Avuh from a *baraita*: If one says to another: **I am selling you a house** from **among my houses, and** one of the houses subsequently **fell, he can show him** the **fallen** house, and say to him: This is the one I sold you. Similarly, if one says to another: **I sell you a slave** from **among my slaves, and** one of the slaves **dies, he can show him** the **dead** slave and say: This is the slave I sold you.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַשְׁתָּא דְּאָתֵית לְהָכִי, אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא עֲלִיָּיה דִּגְרִיעָה – יַד בַּעַל הַשְּׁטָר עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: **Now that you have arrived at this** explanation, the objection posed earlier to the statement of Rabba bar Avuh from the statement of Ulla can be rejected easily. Ulla said that if one says to another: I am selling you a house from among my houses, since he did not specify which house he is selling, he can show him an attic [*aliyya*]. Although this was explained above as referring not to a loft but to the best [*me'ula*] of his houses, now **you** may **even say** that it is referring to **a loft, which is the worst** of his houses, due to the principle that **the owner of the document is at a disadvantage.**

This is fundamentally different from hekdesh. In hekdesh, there is no party trying to extract property from another. The question is how we as a legal system choose to interpret the declarant's words. There the Gemara chooses to interpret in the direction of generosity — not necessarily the maximum possible generosity, as the three-animal case shows, but at least something better than the minimum.

This points to a broader theme running through the recent mishnayot: the distinction between trying to recover a speaker's subjective intent and making a legal determination about how to interpret ambiguous language. When we know what was said but not which option was meant, we read the language with a certain generosity — not necessarily maximally, but not minimally either. When no text survives — as in the final case of the mishna, where a father declared something before dying and no one knows what he said — we ask what he probably meant, and since people giving to hekdesh probably give their best, we assume the largest animal.

## Beit Chonyo Vows

### Seller's Prerogative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה״ – יַקְרִיבֶנָּה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאִם הִקְרִיבָהּ בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – לֹא יָצָא. ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה שֶׁאַקְרִיבֶנָּה בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ״ – יַקְרִיבֶנָּה בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאִם הִקְרִיבָהּ בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – יָצָא. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֵין זוֹ עוֹלָה.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: One who says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering, must sacrifice it in the Temple** in Jerusalem. **And if he sacrificed it in the temple of Onias** in Egypt, **he has not fulfilled** his obligation. One who says: It is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering that I will sacrifice in the temple of Onias, must sacrifice it in the Temple** in Jerusalem, **but if he sacrificed it in the temple of Onias, he has fulfilled** his obligation. **Rabbi Shimon says** that if one says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a burnt offering that I will sacrifice in the temple of Onias, **it is not** consecrated as **a burnt offering;** such a statement does not consecrate the animal at all.

The next mishna introduces an entirely different subject. *Harei alai olah yakrivenah b'Mikdash* — someone says: I accept upon myself an olah, to bring it in the *Beit HaMikdash*. If such a person brings it in Beit Chonyo instead, *לא **yatza*** — the vow is not fulfilled. Beit Chonyo was an alternate Jewish place of sacrifice established in Alexandria, Egypt. We have independent historical corroboration of its existence from sources such as Philo and Josephus. Beyond not fulfilling the vow, the person also transgresses the biblical prohibition of *shchutei chutz* — bringing korbanot outside the *Beit HaMikdash*.

### Olah in Beit Chonyo

*[Continued from above]*

The more striking part of the mishna follows: *Harei alai olah she'yakrivenah b'Veit Honyo* — if someone says "I accept upon myself an olah that I will bring in Beit Chonyo," what then? The mishna says: *yakrivenah b'Mikdash* — one should bring it in the *Beit HaMikdash*, and: *v'im hikrivah b'Veit Honyo yatza* — if one brought it in Beit Chonyo, one is yotzei.

This is striking. On one reading, the person has brought a korban outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, which is an act for which one is **chayav kares** — yet the mishna frames the outcome with the word **yatza**, suggesting fulfillment. At minimum, the mishna conspicuously omits mentioning the karet liability. The Gemara will become harsher, but the mishna itself speaks very softly.

Rabbi Shimon disagrees: *אין זה *Olah** — this is not an olah at all. One cannot make a binding vow to bring a korban in Beit Chonyo; such a vow is simply invalid, and there is nothing to fulfill and nothing to transgress.

The mishna then discusses the parallel nazirite case. *הריני **nazir** יגלח במקדש* — if one vows to be a nazir and stipulates that the concluding rituals will take place in the *Beit HaMikdash*, but then brings the hair-cutting and korbanot in Beit Chonyo, *ולא **yatza*** — one has not fulfilled the nezirut. And if one vows nazirut specifying that the concluding rituals will take place in Beit Chonyo, but then performs them in the *Beit HaMikdash*, **yatza** — the nezirut is ended. But if one actually performs the rituals in Beit Chonyo: **yatza**. This is deeply puzzling, because nezirut can only be concluded by bringing the prescribed korbanot in the legitimate *Beit HaMikdash*. Rabbi Shimon again says: *אין זה **nazir*** — no such vow of nezirut exists at all.

### Vow Fulfillment

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ יָצָא? הָא מִקְטָל קַטְלַהּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna teaches that one who says: It is incumbent upon me to bring a burnt offering that I will sacrifice in the temple of Onias, and sacrifices it in the temple of Onias, has fulfilled his obligation. The Gemara asks: How has he **fulfilled** his obligation? By sacrificing it in the temple of Onias, **hasn't he** merely **killed it** without sacrificing it properly?

### Rav Hamnuna's Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב הַמְנוּנָא: נַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה, עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא אֶתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָהּ״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Hamnuna says:** The mishna does not mean that he has fulfilled his vow to bring an offering. Rather, **he is rendered like one who says: It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering on the condition that I will not be responsible for it** if I kill it beforehand. When the mishna says that he has fulfilled his obligation it simply means that if the animal he consecrated is no longer alive, he does not have to bring another animal in its place.

Rav Hamnuna answers: *Na'aseh k'omer harey alay olah al mnas shelo* — we reinterpret the vow as though the person said "I accept an olah upon myself on condition that I will not be liable for replacing it." Normally, if one sanctifies an animal for a korban and the animal dies or is lost, the obligation remains and one must bring a substitute. Here, by specifying Beit Chonyo, the person is in effect saying: my obligation extends only as far as the act of bringing this animal in Beit Chonyo. Once I have done that, even though halachically it does not constitute bringing a korban, my personal obligation is discharged. We thus reinterpret the word "Beit Chonyo" to delimit the scope of the obligation rather than to identify a legitimate place of sacrifice.

### Nazirite Difference

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָבָא: אֶלָּא מֵעַתָּה, סֵיפָא דְּקָתָנֵי הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר שֶׁאֲגַלֵּחַ בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – יְגַלַּח בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ, וְאִם גִּלַּח בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – יָצָא, הָכִי נָמֵי דְּנַעֲשָׂה כְּאוֹמֵר ״הֲרֵינִי נָזִיר עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא אֶתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּת קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו״? נָזִיר כַּמָּה דְּלָא מַיְיתֵי קׇרְבְּנוֹתָיו – לָא מִתַּכְשַׁר!
>
> **Translation:** **Rava said to** Rav Hamnuna: **If that is so,** what about **the latter clause** of the mishna, **which teaches** that if one says: **I am hereby a nazirite** provided **that I will shave in the temple of Onias, he must shave in the Temple** in Jerusalem, **but if he shaved in the temple of Onias, he has fulfilled** his obligation? In this case do you **also** maintain **that he is rendered like one who says: I am hereby a nazirite on the condition that I will not be responsible for** bringing **its offerings** if I kill them beforehand? Such a condition cannot exempt **a nazirite** from bringing his offerings, because **as long as he does not bring his offerings, he is not fit** to conclude his term of naziriteship and is still bound by all of the restrictions of a nazirite.

Rava challenges Rav Hamnuna's interpretation from the nazirite case. For a nazir, it is not just a matter of personal liability — *kama d'lo maysei korbanosav lo miskasher* — until one brings the korbanot properly, the nazir status simply does not end. It is a matter of objective halachic standing, not personal obligation. Even if one could argue that a person's liability for the sacrifices ends upon performing the rituals in Beit Chonyo, the nazirite status itself cannot be terminated by an act that has no halachic validity. The mishna's statement that one is *yotzei* in the nazirite case cannot be explained by Rav Hamnuna's approach.

### Rava's Conditional Read

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אָמַר רָבָא: אָדָם זֶה לְדוֹרוֹן נִתְכַּוֵּין, אָמַר: אִי סַגִּיא בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – טָרַחְנָא, טְפֵי – לָא מָצֵינָא לְאִיצְטַעוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Rather, Rava said** there is a different explanation: The animal was never consecrated at all, as **this person intended** merely **to** bring the animal as **a gift [*doron*],** but not to consecrate it as an offering. He presumably lives closer to the temple of Onias than to the Temple in Jerusalem, and must have **said** to himself: **If it is sufficient** to sacrifice this animal **in the temple of Onias, I** am prepared to **exert** myself and bring it. But if it is necessary to do **more** than that, i.e., to bring it to Jerusalem, **I am not able to afflict myself.** The mishna teaches that although the person never intended to bring the offering to Jerusalem, ideally, he should sacrifice the animal properly, in the Temple in Jerusalem. If he did not bring it there, but sacrificed it in the temple of Onias, he has fulfilled his obligation, and is not required to bring any other offering in its place.

Rava offers an alternative interpretation of the entire mishna: *Adam zeh lidoro niskaven* — this person genuinely intended to give a gift to God. He is not acting in bad faith; he simply believes, perhaps as a diaspora Jew living in Egypt surrounded by fellow Jews who bring sacrifices to Beit Chonyo, that this is a legitimate way to fulfill the obligation. His vow should be interpreted as follows: *אי סגי בבית *first* *tarachna I'll go** — if it works in Beit Chonyo, I will go to the trouble of bringing it there. **Tfei more* לא מצינא לאיצטעורי* — but I am not able to go to the effort of traveling to Jerusalem.

On this reading, the vow is implicitly conditional: I intend this to be binding only if Beit Chonyo constitutes a valid place of sacrifice. Since it does not, the vow was never binding in the first place. When the mishna says **yatza**, it does not mean "fulfilled a binding vow" — it means there was never a vow to fulfill. And when it says that if one brings it in the *Beit HaMikdash* one is *yotzei*, the meaning is that if one chose to go to Jerusalem, that choice reveals that one was in fact prepared to fulfill the obligation properly, and the vow is retrospectively binding and fulfilled.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נָזִיר נָמֵי, הַאי גַּבְרָא לְצַעוֹרֵי נַפְשֵׁיהּ קָא מִיכַּוֵּין, אָמַר: אִי סַגִּיא בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – טָרַחְנָא, טְפֵי לָא מָצֵינָא לְאִיצְטַעוֹרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** This is the explanation of the latter clause of the mishna **as well:** If one said that he would be **a nazirite** provided that he will shave in the temple of Onias, **this man** did not intend to accept upon himself the halakhic status of naziriteship. Rather, he merely **intends to practice abstinence** by not drinking wine, along with observing the other restrictions of a nazirite. Therefore, **he said** to himself: **If it is sufficient** to shave **in the temple of Onias, I** am prepared to **exert** myself and do so. But if it is necessary to do **more** than that, i.e., to go to Jerusalem to shave and bring the required offerings, **I am not able to afflict myself.** The mishna teaches that ideally, he should go to the Temple in Jerusalem to shave and bring all his offerings. If he shaved and brought his offerings in the temple of Onias, he has fulfilled his vow and has no further obligation.

The same logic applies to the nazirite case: the person wanted to afflict himself as a nazir, but only conditionally — *אי סגי בבית *first* *tarachna I'll go** — if it works in Beit Chonyo I will take on the nezirut. If told it does not work there, he did not want to be a nazir at all. If he goes to the *Beit HaMikdash*, that action reveals genuine intent, and the nezirut is binding.

### Rav Yochanan's Ruling

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב הַמְנוּנָא אָמַר לָךְ: נָזִיר – כִּדְקָאָמְרַתְּ, עוֹלָה – עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא אֶתְחַיֵּיב בְּאַחְרָיוּתָהּ קָאָמַר.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Hamnuna** could have **said to you** in response to Rava's challenge: With regard to the case of one who vowed to become **a nazirite** on the condition that he would shave and bring his offerings in the temple of Onias, the interpretation of the mishna is **as you said.** But with regard to one who vows to bring **a burnt offering** in the temple of Onias, his intent is as I explained, and it is as if **he says:** It is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering on the condition that I will not be responsible for it** if I kill it beforehand.

The difference between Rava's reading and Rabbi Shimon's is therefore subtle. Both agree that the specification of Beit Chonyo is halachically void. Rabbi Shimon holds that this voids the entire declaration absolutely: there is no vow at all, and one can never retroactively validate it. The Chachamim, as read by Rava, hold that the choice to bring it in the *Beit HaMikdash* reveals that the person did fundamentally intend to be obligated, and that intent can be honored.

Rav Hamnuna responds to Rava: in the nazirite case, he concedes that Rava's reading is necessary, since there is no way to dissolve a nazirite status short of saying the vow was never binding. But in the olah case, there is more room to say the animal was genuinely sanctified, and the mention of Beit Chonyo only limits personal liability rather than nullifying the entire vow — which is a stronger reading of the declarant's words.

### Disqualification Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַף רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן סָבַר לַהּ לְהָא דְּרַב הַמְנוּנָא, דְּאָמַר רַבָּה בַּר בַּר חָנָה אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: ״הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה שֶׁאַקְרִיבֶנָּה בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ״, וְהִקְרִיבָהּ בְּאֶרֶץ יִשְׂרָאֵל – יָצָא, וְעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Yoḥanan also** holds in accordance with **that which Rav Hamnuna** said, **as Rabba bar bar Ḥana said** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan said** that if one says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering** on the condition **that I will sacrifice it in the temple of Onias, and he sacrificed it in Eretz Yisrael** but not in the Temple, **he has fulfilled** his obligation, **but** his actions **are** also **punishable by excision from the World-to-Come [*karet*]** because he sacrificed an offering outside of the Temple. This is in accordance with the opinion of Rav Hamnuna that the animal is consecrated.

The Gemara notes that Rav Yochanan holds like Rav Hamnuna. Rabbah bar bar Chana said in the name of Rav Yochanan: if one said *Harey alai olah she'akrivena be'veis chonyo somebody says* and one brought it not in Beit Chonyo but in the Land of Israel outside the *Beit HaMikdash* — *yatza ve'eyno be'kares* — one is yotzei and is liable for karet. This formulation makes clear: *yotzei* because on Rav Hamnuna's reading, the animal was genuinely sanctified regardless of the Beit Chonyo specification, and the obligation is discharged whenever the animal is slaughtered — wherever that occurs. Liable for karet because the animal was genuinely sanctified and was slaughtered outside the *Beit HaMikdash*, constituting shichutei chutz.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: הֲרֵי עָלַי עוֹלָה שֶׁאַקְרִיבֶנָּה בַּמִּדְבָּר, וְהִקְרִיבָהּ בְּעֵבֶר הַיַּרְדֵּן – יָצָא, וְעָנוּשׁ כָּרֵת.
>
> **Translation:** **This** explanation of Rav Hamnuna and Rabbi Yoḥanan **is also taught** in a *baraita*: If one says: **It** is incumbent **upon me** to bring **a burnt offering** on the condition **that I will sacrifice it in the wilderness** of Sinai, thinking that the wilderness of Sinai still has sanctity since the Tabernacle had been located there, **and he sacrificed it on** the east **bank of the Jordan, he has fulfilled** his obligation, **but** his actions **are** also **punishable by *karet*** because he sacrificed an offering outside of the Temple.

A *baraita* confirms this ruling: one who vows to bring an olah in the wilderness brings it in Ever HaYarden — *yatza ve'eyno be'kares*. Rashi notes that the person perhaps thought the wilderness retained sanctity from the days of the Mishkan. The Gemara interprets: we do not take the specification of the wilderness or of Beit Chonyo literally. The meaningful halachic content of such a declaration is: I am sanctifying this animal but limiting my personal liability to replacing it if it is lost or dies. Any subsequent slaughter of the animal — wherever it occurs — discharges that obligation. But because the animal was genuinely sanctified, slaughtering it outside the *Beit HaMikdash* carries karet.

This produces a clear three-way map of positions. Rabbi Shimon holds the entire vow void. The Chachamim, on Rava's reading, hold the vow conditionally binding — binding if performed in the *Beit HaMikdash*, non-binding if performed in Beit Chonyo. The Chachamim, on Rav Hamnuna's and Rav Yochanan's reading, hold the vow unconditionally binding — the animal is genuinely sanctified from the moment of declaration, and Beit Chonyo merely limits personal replacement liability.

## Kohanim Disqualification

### Shechita Exception

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַכֹּהֲנִים שֶׁשִּׁמְּשׁוּ בְּבֵית חוֹנְיוֹ – לֹא יְשַׁמְּשׁוּ בַּמִּקְדָּשׁ שֶׁבִּירוּשָׁלַיִם, וְאֵין צָרִיךְ לוֹמַר לְדָבָר אַחֵר, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״אַךְ לֹא יַעֲלוּ כֹּהֲנֵי הַבָּמוֹת אֶל מִזְבַּח ה׳ בִּירוּשָׁלִָם כִּי אִם אָכְלוּ מַצּוֹת בְּקֶרֶב אֲחֵיהֶם״. הֲרֵי אֵלּוּ כְּבַעֲלֵי מוּמִין – חוֹלְקִין וְאוֹכְלִין וְלֹא מַקְרִיבִין.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The priests who served in the temple of Onias may not serve in the Temple in Jerusalem; and needless to say,** if they served **for something else,** a euphemism for idolatry, they are disqualified from service in the Temple. **As it is stated: "Nevertheless the priests of the private altars did not come up to the altar of the Lord in Jerusalem, but they did eat *matza* among their brethren"** (II Kings 23:9). The halakhic status of **these** priests is **like** that of **blemished** priests in that **they receive a share** in the distribution of the meat of the offerings **and partake** of that meat, **but they do not sacrifice** offerings or perform any of the sacrificial rites.

The next mishna rules: **wanted* ששימשו בבית *first* לא ישמשו במקדש שבירושלים* — kohanim who served in Beit Chonyo may not serve in the *Beit HaMikdash* in Jerusalem. **Ve'ein tzarich lomar ledavar acher** — it goes without saying that kohanim who served in *avodah zarah* are excluded entirely. A pasuk from Yechezkel supports this: **ach lo ya'alu kohanei habamos el mizbach Hashem* — the kohanim of the *bamot* may not ascend to the altar of Hashem in Jerusalem, but **ki im achlu matzos betoch acheihem** — they may eat *matzot* among their brothers. The Gemara explains: *הרי אלו כבעלי מומים חולקין ואוכלין ולא מקריבין* — they are like kohanim with physical blemishes: they receive a portion of the sacrificial foods but may not perform the service. Their disqualification is a spiritual *mum*, a religious blemish — not complete exclusion, but permanent removal from active service.

The implicit point of the mishna's formulation — *it goes without saying* regarding *avodah zarah* — is that Beit Chonyo is not itself a house of *avodah zarah*. The *baraita* later confirms: *Tanya keman de’amar *Beis Chonyo* lav avodah zarah* — we taught in accordance with the opinion that Beit Chonyo was not *avodah zarah*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: כֹּהֵן שֶׁשָּׁחַט לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Rav Yehuda says:** With regard to **a priest who slaughtered** an offering **for idol worship** and who subsequently repented and came to the Temple in Jerusalem to serve, **his offering is** acceptable and considered to be **an aroma pleasing** to the Lord.

### Zrika Debate

Rav Yehuda rules: a kohen who slaughtered an animal for *avodah zarah* and subsequently did teshuvah — *korbano reiach nichoach* — his subsequent korban in the *Beit HaMikdash* is accepted. The act of shechita for *avodah zarah* does not disqualify him. Rav Yitzchak bar Avdimi derives the disqualification of kohanim who actually served *avodah zarah* from a pasuk in Yechezkel: **ya'an asher yeshartu osam lifnei giluleihem* because* — because they performed service before their abominations — **velo yigshu elai lechahen li** — they may not draw near to Me to serve as priests.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יִצְחָק בַּר אַבְדִּימִי: מַאי קְרָאָה? ״יַעַן אֲשֶׁר יְשָׁרְתוּ אוֹתָם לִפְנֵי גִלּוּלֵיהֶם וְהָיוּ לְבֵית יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמִכְשׁוֹל עָוֹן עַל כֵּן נָשָׂאתִי יָדִי עֲלֵיהֶם נְאֻם ה׳ אֱלֹהִים וְנָשְׂאוּ עֲוֹנָם״, וּכְתִיב בָּתְרֵיהּ: ״וְלֹא יִגְּשׁוּ אֵלַי לְכַהֵן לִי״. אִי עֲבַד שֵׁירוּת – אִין, שְׁחִיטָה לָאו שֵׁירוּת הוּא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yitzḥak bar Avdimi says: What is the verse** from which it is derived? The verse states: **"Because they served them before their idols and became a stumbling block of iniquity unto the house of Israel, therefore I have lifted up My hand against them, says the Lord God, and they shall bear their iniquity"** (Ezekiel 44:12). **And it is written afterward: "And they shall not come near to Me, to serve Me in the priestly role"** (Ezekiel 44:13). This indicates that **if** a priest **performed a service** for an idol that is considered a sacrificial rite in the Temple, he **is** disqualified from serving in the Temple, but the **slaughter** of an offering **is not** considered **service,** as it is not considered a sacrificial rite in the Temple and can be performed in the Temple even by a non-priest.

The key word is *ישרתו* — they performed *sheirus*, priestly service. Shechita, however, is *sheirus* — it is not categorized as a priestly service, since a non-kohen may also serve as a *shochet*. Therefore, although slaughtering for *avodah zarah* is a grave sin, it does not besmirch one's kehuna in the specific way that performing priestly service for *avodah zarah* does. After teshuvah, such a kohen may resume service.

### Rav Nechman Lenient

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִיתְּמַר: שָׁגַג בִּזְרִיקָה, רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **It was stated:** If a priest **unwittingly** performed the **sprinkling** of the blood of an idolatrous offering and then repented and came to serve in the Temple, **Rav Naḥman says** that **his offering** is accepted and **is an aroma pleasing** to the Lord. **Rav Sheshet says: His offering is not a pleasing aroma** to the Lord, as he is not fit to serve in the Temple.

The Gemara now turns to a more complex case: a kohen who performed *zrika* — the sprinkling of blood — for *avodah zarah*, but did so *bishogeg*, unintentionally. Rav Nachman rules: *korbano reiach nichoach* — his korban is subsequently accepted; he is not disqualified. Rav Sheshet rules: *ein korbano reiach nichoach* — he is disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ, דִּכְתִיב: ״וְהָיוּ לְבֵית יִשְׂרָאֵל לְמִכְשׁוֹל עָוֹן״, מַאי לָאו – אוֹ מִכְשׁוֹל אוֹ עָוֹן, וּ״מִכְשׁוֹל״ – שׁוֹגֵג, וְ״עָוֹן״ – מֵזִיד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Sheshet said: From where do I say** that if a priest sprinkled blood unwittingly for idol worship he cannot serve in the Temple? **As it is written: "And they became a stumbling block of iniquity unto the house of Israel." What, is it not** referring to one who served in idol worship **either** as **a stumbling block or** as **an iniquity?** Accordingly, neither may perform the service in the Temple. **And** the term **"stumbling block"** is a reference to one who sins **unwittingly, and** the term **"iniquity"** is a reference to **an intentional** sinner. Therefore, even one who unwittingly served in idol worship may not subsequently serve in the Temple.

Rav Sheshet's source: the pasuk reads **vehayu l'veis Yisrael l'michshol avon** — they shall be a stumbling block of sin. *מכשול* refers to an unintentional act (*shogeg*) and *עון* to a willful one (*meizid*). Since both are listed, even a shogeg in zrika disqualifies. Rav Nachman reads *michshol deavon* as a single phrase — the stumbling that is of a willful kind — meaning only meizid disqualifies.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן: מְנָא אָמֵינָא לַהּ? דְּתַנְיָא: ״וְכִפֶּר הַכֹּהֵן עַל הַנֶּפֶשׁ הַשֹּׁגֶגֶת בְּחֶטְאָה בִשְׁגָגָה״, מְלַמֵּד שֶׁכֹּהֵן מִתְכַּפֵּר עַל יְדֵי עַצְמוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman said: From where do I say** that if a priest sprinkled the blood of an idolatrous offering unwittingly his subsequent offering in the Temple is accepted? **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: The verse states with regard to one who unwittingly committed idolatry: "And if one person sin through error, then he shall offer a she-goat in its first year for a sin offering. **And the priest shall effect atonement for the soul that errs unwittingly, when he sins unwittingly,** before the Lord, to effect atonement for him; and he shall be forgiven" (Numbers 15:27–28). The phrase: "For the soul that errs unwittingly" **teaches that a priest** who sins unwittingly **may receive atonement** by sacrificing his sin offering **on his own.**

Rav Nachman adduces support from a *baraita*: **should* שעבד עבודת כוכבים *b'shogeg* — קרבנו ריח ניחוח* — a kohen who worshipped *avodah zarah* bishogeg, his korban is subsequently accepted. The baraita adds that he must have done teshuvah. Now: if the case were shogeg, why would teshuvah be needed to restore his status? A shogeg who was never invalidated obviously does not require teshuvah to be re-accepted. Therefore the baraita must be discussing meizid — and yet it says that after teshuvah he is accepted. And if the case were zrika bemeizid — the most severe form — teshuvah would be irrelevant, since he has performed a priestly act in service of *avodah zarah* and is irrevocably excluded. Therefore the case must be shechita bemeizid: willful slaughter for *avodah zarah*, after which teshuvah restores full status.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי? אִילֵּימָא בִּשְׁחִיטָה – מַאי אִירְיָא שׁוֹגֵג? אֲפִילּוּ מֵזִיד נָמֵי! אֶלָּא לָאו בִּזְרִיקָה.
>
> **Translation:** Rav Naḥman clarifies: **In what** manner did this priest commit idolatry? **If we say** he sinned **through slaughtering** an idolatrous offering, **why** does the verse indicate **specifically** that a priest who slaughtered an idolatrous offering **unwittingly** can bring his own sin offering? This is obvious, as **even** one who did so **intentionally** may serve in the Temple after repentance. **Rather, is it not** referring to a priest who committed idolatry **by sprinkling** the blood of an idolatrous offering? Accordingly, if he did so unwittingly his subsequent service in the Temple is valid, but if he did so intentionally, he is disqualified from serving in the Temple.

### Rav Sheshet Strict

*[Continued from above]*

Rav Nachman's position can now be stated precisely: shechita for *avodah zarah* — even bemeizid — never invalidates, and teshuvah is required only as a matter of general repentance, not to restore kehuna status. Zrika for *avodah zarah* bishogeg also does not invalidate. Only zrika bemeizid irrevocably disqualifies.

### Other Avodah Zarah Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר לָךְ: לְעוֹלָם בִּשְׁחִיטָה, וּבְמֵזִיד – לֹא נַעֲשָׂה כּוֹמֶר לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה?!
>
> **Translation:** **And** as for **Rav Sheshet, he** could have **said to you: Actually,** the verse is referring to a case **where** the priest sinned **through slaughtering** an idolatrous offering. **And** although Rav Yehuda said that a priest who slaughtered an idolatrous offering may serve in the Temple after repentance, that statement applies only to one who slaughtered an idolatrous offering unwittingly. But if he did so **intentionally,** the priest is disqualified from serving in the Temple. Rav Yehuda's reasoning is that slaughter is not a sacrificial rite in the Temple; but **does** one who slaughters an idolatrous offering intentionally **not become a servant of idol worship?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָזְדוּ לְטַעְמַיְיהוּ, דְּאִתְּמַר: הֵזִיד בִּשְׁחִיטָה – רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And** Rav Naḥman and Rav Sheshet **follow their** respective lines of **reasoning, as it was stated** that if a priest acted **intentionally in** the **slaughter** of an idolatrous offering and subsequently repented, **Rav Naḥman says** that **his offering** in the Temple **is an aroma pleasing** to the Lord, i.e., it is not disqualified, **and Rav Sheshet says** that **his offering** in the Temple **is not an aroma pleasing** to the Lord, i.e., it is disqualified.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, דְּלָא עֲבַד שֵׁירוּת. רַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, נַעֲשָׂה כּוֹמֶר לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Naḥman says** that **his offering is an aroma pleasing** to the Lord, **because he did not perform service** for an idol that is considered a sacrificial rite in the Temple. **And Rav Sheshet says** that **his offering is not an aroma pleasing** to the Lord, as by slaughtering the idolatrous offering intentionally **he became a servant of idol worship.**

Rav Sheshet interprets the baraita differently. He reads the case as shechita bishogeg. Why does teshuvah matter? The baraita is saying: *im shav me'ikaro* — if he remained in a state of teshuvah from the outset, meaning if his act was genuinely inadvertent throughout, then *korbano reiach nichoach* — he is accepted. If not, *ein korbano reiach nichoach* — he is not. On this reading, even shechita bishogeg can be invalidating unless one maintained genuinely inadvertent intent. Rav Sheshet's consistent position: shechita bemeizid invalidates, not because it is a priestly act, but because one who willfully serves *avodah zarah* — *na'aseh mesharais la'avodas kochavim* — has become identified as a servant of idolatry, and that identity is incompatible with kehuna service regardless of the particular act.

### Shimon's Prophecy

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ. הוֹדָה לַעֲבוֹדָה זָרָה – רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר: קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ, וְרַב שֵׁשֶׁת אָמַר: אֵין קׇרְבָּנוֹ רֵיחַ נִיחוֹחַ.
>
> **Translation:** § The Gemara lists other similar disagreements between Rav Naḥman and Rav Sheshet. In a case where a priest **bowed to** an object of **idol worship, Rav Naḥman says:** If he subsequently repents and serves in the Temple, **his offering is an aroma pleasing** to the Lord. **And Rav Sheshet says: His offering is not an aroma pleasing** to the Lord. In a case where a priest **acknowledges** an object of **idol worship** as a divinity, **Rav Naḥman says:** If he subsequently repents and serves in the Temple, **his offering is an aroma pleasing** to the Lord. **And Rav Sheshet says: His offering is not an aroma pleasing** to the Lord.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּצְרִיכָא, דְּאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הָךְ קַמַּיְיתָא – בְּהַהִיא קָאָמַר רַב שֵׁשֶׁת מִשּׁוּם דַּעֲבַד לֵיהּ שֵׁירוּת, אֲבָל שְׁחִיטָה דְּלָא עֲבַד לֵיהּ שֵׁירוּת – אֵימָא מוֹדֶה לֵיהּ לְרַב נַחְמָן.
>
> **Translation:** Having listed four similar disputes between Rav Naḥman and Rav Sheshet, namely, with regard to a priest who unwittingly sprinkled the blood of an idolatrous offering, a priest who intentionally slaughtered an idolatrous offering, a priest who bowed to an idol, and a priest who acknowledged an idol as a divinity, the Gemara explains: **And** it was **necessary** to teach the dispute with regard to all four cases. **As, had** the Sages **taught us** only **this first** case, where a priest sprinkles the blood of an idolatrous offering unwittingly, one might have thought that only **in that** case **Rav Sheshet says** that the priest's subsequent service in the Temple is disqualified, **because he performed a service for** idolatry that is considered a sacrificial rite in the Temple. **But** in a case where the priest merely performed **slaughter, since he did not perform a service for** idolatry that is a sacrificial rite in the Temple, there is room to **say** that Rav Sheshet **concedes to** the opinion of **Rav Naḥman.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן שְׁחִיטָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּעָבֵד לֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה, אֲבָל הִשְׁתַּחֲוָה דְּלָא עֲבַד לֵיהּ עֲבוֹדָה – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the Sages **taught us** only the dispute with regard to a priest intentionally performing **slaughter** for an idolatrous offering, one might have thought that Rav Sheshet says that the priest's subsequent service in the Temple is disqualified **because he performed** a sacrificial **rite for** idolatry. **But** if he merely **bowed** to the idol, **since he did not perform** a sacrificial **rite for** idolatry, there is room to **say** that Rav Sheshet does **not** disqualify the priest's subsequent service in the Temple. Therefore, it was **necessary** to teach this case as well.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִי אַשְׁמְעִינַן הִשְׁתַּחֲוָאָה, מִשּׁוּם דְּעָבֵיד לֵיהּ מַעֲשֶׂה, אֲבָל הוֹדָה דְּדִיבּוּרָא בְּעָלְמָא – אֵימָא לָא. צְרִיכָא.
>
> **Translation:** **And had** the Sages **taught us** only the case of a priest **bowing** to an idol, one might have thought that in this case Rav Sheshet says that the priest's subsequent service in the Temple is disqualified **because he performed an action for** idolatry. **But** if he only **acknowledged** the idol as a divinity, **which is mere speech,** there is room to **say** that Rav Sheshet does **not** disqualify the priest's subsequent service in the Temple. The Gemara concludes: Therefore, it was **necessary** to teach this case as well.

The Gemara extends the dispute to further cases. If a kohen bowed down — *hishtachavah l'avodas kochavim let's say* — Rav Nachman says *korbano reiach nichoach*: bowing is not a priestly act in the *Beit HaMikdash* context, and therefore does not besmirch the kehuna. Rav Sheshet says *ein korbano reiach nichoach*: any willful service of *avodah zarah* disqualifies. If a kohen merely accepted an idol as his deity — *hodah l'avodas kochavim let's say* — without performing any act of worship, Rav Nachman still says *korbano reiach nichoach* and Rav Sheshet still says *ein korbano reiach nichoach*.

The Gemara explains why all three cases must be stated: had only the zrika case been taught, one might have thought Rav Sheshet is strict only there because it is a genuine priestly act, but would be lenient by shechita. The shechita case teaches that he is strict even there. Had only the shechita case been taught, one might have thought that at least shechita involves an act performed in the Temple context — but hishtachava, which is not a Temple-related act at all, Rav Sheshet might accept. The hishtachava case teaches otherwise. Had only hishtachava been taught, one might have thought that verbal acceptance — *hoda'ah*, which is only words — would not suffice to disqualify. Hence all cases must be stated.

On the other side, Rav Nachman's consistent leniency is clear: only the narrowly defined misuse of priestly service — zrika bemeizid — disqualifies. Every other act, however serious a personal sin, does not carry over into the domain of kehuna invalidation.

## Chonyo-Shimei Story

### The Garb Trick

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא כְּמַאן דְּאָמַר בֵּית חוֹנְיוֹ לָאו עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה הוּא, דְּתַנְיָא: אוֹתָהּ שָׁנָה שֶׁמֵּת שִׁמְעוֹן הַצַּדִּיק, אָמַר לָהֶן: שָׁנָה זוֹ הוּא מֵת, אָמְרוּ לוֹ: מִנַּיִן אַתָּה יוֹדֵעַ?
>
> **Translation:** **It is taught** in a *baraita* **like the one who says** that **the temple of Onias is not** a temple of **idol worship. As it is taught:** During **the year in which Shimon HaTzaddik died, he said to** his associates: **This year, he will die,** euphemistically referring to himself. **They said to him: From where do you know?**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לָהֶן: כׇּל יוֹם הַכִּפּוּרִים נִזְדַּמֵּן לִי זָקֵן אֶחָד לָבוּשׁ לְבָנִים, וְנִתְעַטֵּף לְבָנִים, וְנִכְנַס עִמִּי וְיָצָא עִמִּי. שָׁנָה זוֹ נִזְדַּמֵּן לִי זָקֵן אֶחָד לָבוּשׁ שְׁחוֹרִים, וְנִתְעַטֵּף שְׁחוֹרִים, וְנִכְנַס עִמִּי וְלֹא יָצָא עִמִּי.
>
> **Translation:** Shimon HaTzaddik **said to them:** In previous years, **every Yom Kippur,** upon entering the Holy of Holies, I had a prophetic vision in which **I would be met by an old man** who was **dressed in white, and** his head was **wrapped in white, and he would enter** the Holy of Holies **with me, and he would leave with me.** But **this year, I was met by an old man** who was **dressed in black, and** his head was **wrapped in black, and he entered** the Holy of Holies **with me, but he did not leave with me.** Shimon HaTzaddik understood this to be a sign that his death was impending.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְאַחַר הָרֶגֶל, חָלָה שִׁבְעַת יָמִים וָמֵת, וְנִמְנְעוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים מִלְּבָרֵךְ בַּשֵּׁם.
>
> **Translation:** Indeed, **after the pilgrimage festival** of *Sukkot*, **he was ill for seven days and died. And his fellow priests refrained from reciting the** Priestly **Benediction with the** ineffable **name** of God.

The Gemara establishes from the mishna's formulation that Beit Chonyo was not regarded as a house of *avodah zarah*. A *baraita* then begins the narrative of how it came to be founded. In the year that *Shimon HaTzaddik died*, he emerged from the Kodesh HaKodashim on Yom Kippur and said to those around him: this year, he himself would die. They asked how he knew. He told them: every Yom Kippur, when I entered the Kodesh HaKodashim, I would encounter an elderly man dressed entirely in white, wrapped in white, who would enter and depart with me. This year I saw an elderly man dressed entirely in black, wrapped in black, who entered with me but did not come out with me — a sign of mourning and departure.

After Sukkot he became ill, lingered seven days, and died. Following his death, his fellow kohanim ceased to pronounce the *Shem HaMeforash* — the explicit divine name — in birkat kohanim, since the level of piety required to utter that name safely was no longer present among them.

### Two Versions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּשְׁעַת פְּטִירָתוֹ, אָמַר לָהֶם: חוֹנְיוֹ בְּנִי יְשַׁמֵּשׁ תַּחְתַּי. נִתְקַנֵּא בּוֹ שִׁמְעִי אָחִיו, שֶׁהָיָה גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה. אָמַר לוֹ: בֹּא וַאֲלַמֶּדְךָ סֵדֶר עֲבוֹדָה. הִלְבִּישׁוֹ בְּאוּנְקְלִי, וַחֲגָרוֹ בְּצִילְצוֹל, (העמידו) [וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ] אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. אָמַר לָהֶם לְאֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים: רְאוּ מָה נָדַר זֶה וְקִיֵּים לַאֲהוּבָתוֹ: ״אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁאֶשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְהוּנָּה גְּדוֹלָה אֶלְבּוֹשׁ בְּאוּנְקְלִי שֶׁלִּיכִי וְאֶחְגּוֹר בְּצִילְצוֹל שֶׁלִּיכִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **At the time of his death, he said to** the Sages: **Onias, my son, will serve** as High Priest **in my stead. Shimi,** Onias' **brother, became jealous** of him, **as** Shimi **was two and a half years older than** Onias. Shimi **said to** Onias treacherously: **Come and I will teach you the order of the service** of the High Priest. Shimi **dressed** Onias **in a tunic [*be'unkeli*] and girded him with a ribbon [*betziltzul*]** as a belt, i.e., not in the vestments of the High Priest, and **stood him next to the altar.** Shimi **said to his fellow priests: Look what this** man **vowed and fulfilled for his beloved,** that he had said to her: **On the day that I serve in the High Priesthood I will wear your tunic and gird your ribbon.**

At the moment of his death, Shimon said: *Chonyo beni yeshamesh tachtai* — my son Chonyo shall serve in my place as Kohen Gadol. Shimei, Chonyo's brother, was jealous — *shehayah gadol mimenu shtei shanim vachetzi* — because Shimei was two and a half years older and had been passed over. He approached his younger brother and said: come, let me teach you the order of the Temple service. He dressed Chonyo in a cloak (*unkeili*) and girded him with a belt (*tziltzul*) — both garments associated with women — and stationed him at the altar. He then turned to the other kohanim and said: look at what this man vowed and fulfilled for his beloved wife — he said that on the day he would serve as Kohen Gadol he would wear her robe and her belt.

Tosafot notes a difficulty: if Chonyo had been designated as Kohen Gadol, how could he have been duped into thinking this was a normal inauguration ceremony? Tosafot offers two answers. First, he may have been chosen for his exceptional piety rather than his knowledge of Temple procedure, so he was genuinely unfamiliar with the inauguration rites. Second, perhaps he had never witnessed the inauguration of a Kohen Gadol and believed this was the unusual ceremony involved.

### Alexandria Altar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְּשׁוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים לְהׇרְגוֹ, רָץ מִפְּנֵיהֶם, וְרָצוּ אַחֲרָיו. הָלַךְ לַאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם, וּבָנָה שָׁם מִזְבֵּחַ, וְהֶעֱלָה עָלָיו לְשׁוּם עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה. וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: מָה זֶה שֶׁלֹּא יָרַד לָהּ – כָּךְ, הַיּוֹרֵד לָהּ – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי מֵאִיר.
>
> **Translation:** **The fellow priests of** Onias **wanted to kill him** because he had disgraced the Temple service with his garments. Onias **ran** away **from them and they ran after him. He went to Alexandria in Egypt and built an altar there, and sacrificed** offerings **upon it for the sake of idol worship. When the Sages heard of the matter they said: If this** person, Shimi, **who did not enter** the position of High Priest, acted with **such** jealousy, **all the more so** will **one who enters** a prestigious position rebel if that position is taken away from him. This is **the statement of Rabbi Meir.** According to Rabbi Meir, the temple of Onias was built for idol worship.

This is Rabbi Meir's version. The fellow kohanim, outraged at the perceived mockery of the kehuna, sought to kill Chonyo. He fled; they pursued him. He went to Alexandria of Egypt. *u-vanah sham mizbeach ve-he-elah alav le-shem avodat kochavim* — and he built an altar there and offered upon it for the sake of *avodah zarah*. The rabbis, hearing of this, commented: one who was never given the office, and was so consumed by jealousy that it led to this, how much more so one who actually holds a position of power — *המבקש לירד לה על אחת כמה וכמה* — will go to terrible lengths to seize or retain it.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר לוֹ רַבִּי יְהוּדָה: לֹא כָּךְ הָיָה מַעֲשֶׂה, אֶלָּא לֹא קִיבֵּל עָלָיו חוֹנְיוֹ, שֶׁהָיָה שִׁמְעִי אָחִיו גָּדוֹל מִמֶּנּוּ שְׁתֵּי שָׁנִים וּמֶחֱצָה, וְאַף עַל פִּי כֵן נִתְקַנֵּא בּוֹ חוֹנְיוֹ בְּשִׁמְעִי אָחִיו. אָמַר לוֹ: בֹּא וַאֲלַמֶּדְךָ סֵדֶר עֲבוֹדָה, וְהִלְבִּישׁוֹ בְּאוּנְקְלִי, וַחֲגָרוֹ בְּצִילְצוֹל, וְהֶעֱמִידוֹ אֵצֶל הַמִּזְבֵּחַ. אָמַר לָהֶם לְאֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים: רְאוּ מָה נָדַר זֶה וְקִיֵּים לַאֲהוּבָתוֹ ״אוֹתוֹ הַיּוֹם שֶׁיִּשְׁתַּמֵּשׁ בִּכְהוּנָּה גְּדוֹלָה אֶלְבּוֹשׁ בְּאוּנְקְלִי שֶׁלִּיכִי וְאֶחְגּוֹר בְּצִילְצוֹל שֶׁלִּיכִי״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda said to him:** The **incident was not like this. Rather, Onias did not accept** the position of High Priest **because his brother Shimi was two and a half years older than him,** so Shimi was appointed as High Priest. **And even so,** even though Onias himself offered the position to Shimi, **Onias was jealous of his brother Shimi.** Onias **said to** Shimi: **Come and I will teach you the order of the service** of the High Priest. **And** Onias **dressed** Shimi **in a tunic and girded him in a ribbon and stood him next to the altar.** Onias **said to his fellow priests: Look what this** man, Shimi, **vowed and fulfilled for his beloved,** that he had said to her: **On the day that I serve in the High Priesthood I will wear your tunic and gird your ribbon.**

Rabbi Yehuda offers a completely different version. In his account, it was Chonyo who declined the appointment — *לא קיבל עליו *first** — out of deference to his older brother Shimei, saying Shimei was the elder and should serve. Shimei became the Kohen Gadol. Nevertheless, Chonyo was jealous of the brother whose advancement he had himself enabled. He approached Shimei and said: come, let me teach you the inauguration procedure. He dressed Shimei in the same female garb, stationed him at the altar, and announced to the assembled kohanim: look at what this man vowed and fulfilled for his beloved wife — on the day he would serve as Kohen Gadol he would wear her robe and her belt.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בִּקְּשׁוּ אֶחָיו הַכֹּהֲנִים לְהׇרְגוֹ, סָח לָהֶם כׇּל הַמְאוֹרָע, בִּקְּשׁוּ לַהֲרוֹג אֶת חוֹנְיוֹ, רָץ מִפְּנֵיהֶם, וְרָצוּ אַחֲרָיו, רָץ לְבֵית הַמֶּלֶךְ, וְרָצוּ אַחֲרָיו, כׇּל הָרוֹאֶה אוֹתוֹ אוֹמֵר: זֶה הוּא, זֶה הוּא, הָלַךְ לַאֲלֶכְּסַנְדְּרִיָּא שֶׁל מִצְרַיִם, וּבָנָה שָׁם מִזְבֵּחַ, וְהֶעֱלָה עָלָיו לְשֵׁם שָׁמַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״(וְהָיָה) בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיֶה מִזְבֵּחַ לַה׳ בְּתוֹךְ אֶרֶץ מִצְרָיִם וּמַצֵּבָה אֵצֶל גְּבוּלָהּ לַה׳״.
>
> **Translation:** **His fellow priests wanted to kill** Shimi. Shimi then **told them the entire incident,** that he had been tricked by his brother Onias, so the priests **wanted to kill Onias.** Onias **ran** away **from them, and they ran after him.** Onias **ran to the palace of the king, and they ran after him. Anyone who saw him would say: This is him, this is him,** and he was not able to escape unnoticed. Onias **went to Alexandria in Egypt and built an altar there, and sacrificed** offerings **upon it for the sake of Heaven. As it is stated: "In that day shall there be an altar to the Lord in the midst of the land of Egypt, and a pillar at its border, to the Lord"** (Isaiah 19:19). According to Rabbi Yehuda, the temple of Onias was dedicated to the worship of God.

The other kohanim now wanted to kill Shimei. Shimei explained what had actually happened and turned the anger back toward Chonyo. They then sought to kill Chonyo. *kol haro'eh oto omer zehu zehu* — everyone who saw him called out that he was the one. He fled to the king's palace, then to Alexandria.

### Power Danger Lesson

According to Rabbi Yehuda's version: *u-vanah sham mizbeach ve-he-elah alav le-shem shamayim* — and he built an altar and offered upon it for the sake of Heaven. He was pursuing power through devious means, but his original motivation had been piety — he initially declined the appointment — and he was now fleeing his own deviousness rather than fleeing a rejection of himself. The story reads differently when the founder of Beit Chonyo was someone who set it up as a genuine house of worship, even if driven to Egypt by unworthy jealousy.

Rabbi Yehuda provides a scriptural basis for the founding of an altar in Egypt: *ba-yom ha-hu yihyeh mizbeach l'Adonai be-toch eretz Mitzrayim* — on that day there shall be an altar to Hashem in the midst of the land of Egypt; *ומצבה אצל גבולה לה'* — and a pillar at its border to Hashem (Yeshaya 19:19).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּכְשֶׁשָּׁמְעוּ חֲכָמִים בַּדָּבָר, אָמְרוּ: וּמָה זֶה שֶׁבָּרַח מִמֶּנָּה כָּךְ, הַמְבַקֵּשׁ לֵירֵד לָהּ – עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And when the Sages heard of the matter they said: If this one,** Onias, **who fled from** the position of High Priest and offered it to his brother, still was overcome with **such** jealousy to the point where he tried to have Shimi killed, **all the more so** will **one who wants to enter** a prestigious position be jealous of the one who already has that position.

When the rabbis heard of it, they commented: *u-mah zeh she-*barach mimena* kach somebody* — this person who ran away from the position of greatness, who did not seek it — and yet jealousy still led him to such lengths. *המבקש לירד לה על אחת כמה וכמה* — how much more so will one who actively pursues authority go to improper lengths to acquire or defend it.

### Shaul Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא, אָמַר רַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן פְּרַחְיָה: בַּתְּחִלָּה, כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר ״עֲלֵה לָהּ״ – אֲנִי כּוֹפְתוֹ וְנוֹתְנוֹ לִפְנֵי הָאֲרִי; עַתָּה, כׇּל הָאוֹמֵר לִי לֵירֵד מִמֶּנָּה – אֲנִי מֵטִיל עָלָיו קוּמְקוּם שֶׁל חַמִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § As a corollary to the statement of the Sages with regard to one who is jealous and wants the position of another, **it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Yehoshua ben Peraḥya said: Initially,** in response to **anyone who would say** to me: **Ascend to** the position of *Nasi*, **I would tie him up and place him in front of a lion** out of anger for his suggestion. **Now** that I have become the *Nasi*, in response to **anyone who tells me to leave** the position, **I** would **throw a kettle [*kumkum*] of boiling** water **at him** out of anger at his suggestion.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** שֶׁהֲרֵי שָׁאוּל בָּרַח מִמֶּנָּה, וּכְשֶׁעָלָה בִּקֵּשׁ לַהֲרוֹג אֶת דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** It is human nature that after one ascends to a prestigious position he does not wish to lose it. **As** evidence of this principle, **Saul** initially **fled from** the kingship, as he did not wish to be king, as stated in the verse: "When they sought him he could not be found…Behold he has hidden himself among the baggage" (I Samuel 10:21–22). **But when he ascended** to the kingship **he tried to kill David,** who he thought was trying to usurp his authority (see I Samuel, chapters 18–27).

A *baraita* records Rabbi Yehoshua ben Perachiah's personal statement: *בתחילה כל אומר עלי לה אני כופתו ונותנו לפני אריה* — originally, if anyone had told me to take a position of authority, I would have bound him and thrown him before a lion. He regarded the pursuit of office as a dangerous thing to be avoided at all costs.

*עתה כל האומר לי לירד ממנה אני מטיל עליו קומקום של חמים* — now, having taken such a position, anyone who tells me to relinquish it, I would throw a flask of hot water upon him. The lesson: *שהרי *Shaul first didn't want* ברח ממנה* — just as Shaul fled from kingship — as we read in this week's haftarah — *ועלה ביקש להרוג את *David** — and once he was in the position, he sought to kill David to protect it. The desire for status and the fear of losing it can transform even a person of admirable initial humility.

### Five Cities Alternative

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ מָר קַשִּׁישָׁא בְּרֵיהּ דְּרַב חִסְדָּא לְאַבָּיֵי: רַבִּי מֵאִיר, הַאי קְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה מַאי עָבֵיד לֵיהּ?
>
> **Translation:** § **Mar Kashisha, son of Rav Ḥisda, said to Abaye: What does Rabbi Meir do with this verse of Rabbi Yehuda?** Since Rabbi Meir holds that the temple of Onias was dedicated to idol worship, how does he explain the verse in Isaiah?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְכִדְתַנְיָא: לְאַחַר מַפַּלְתּוֹ שֶׁל סַנְחֵרִיב, יָצָא חִזְקִיָּה וּמָצָא בְּנֵי מְלָכִים שֶׁהָיוּ יוֹשְׁבִין בִּקְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב, הִדִּירָן שֶׁלֹּא לַעֲבוֹד עֲבוֹדָה זָרָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: ״בַּיּוֹם הַהוּא יִהְיוּ חָמֵשׁ עָרִים בְּאֶרֶץ מִצְרַיִם מְדַבְּרוֹת שְׂפַת כְּנַעַן וְנִשְׁבָּעוֹת לַה׳ צְבָאוֹת״.
>
> **Translation:** Abaye answered Mar Kashisha and said that Rabbi Meir uses this verse **for that which is taught** in a *baraita*: **After the downfall of Sennacherib,** the king of Assyria who besieged Jerusalem (see II Kings, chapters 18–19), King **Hezekiah emerged** from Jerusalem **and found the** gentile **princes** Sennacherib had brought with him from his other conquests, **sitting in carriages [*bikronot*] of gold. He made them vow that they would not worship idols,** and they fulfilled their vow, **as it is stated** in Isaiah's prophecy about Egypt: **"In that day there shall be five cities in the land of Egypt that speak the language of Canaan** **and swear allegiance to Hashem of Hosts;**

Thus, according to Rabbi Meir, the verse from Yeshaya refers not to Beit Chonyo but to the altar built in Alexandria by those whom Chizkiya had brought to acknowledge God, centuries before Chonyo's time. For Rabbi Yehuda, who holds that Beit Chonyo was built *leshem shamayim*, the same verse applies to both.
