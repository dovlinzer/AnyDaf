# Nedarim 91 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Belief & Disbelief

### Mishnah Overview

The daf before us is 91, the last daf of Nedarim, and we pick up at 91b at the *gemara* following the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* presents a powerful set of cases: a woman who claims she has been raped, or who claims that she committed adultery, or who claims that she is otherwise unable to remain with her husband — and the question is whether she is believed. The *mishnah* wrestles with the consequences of that belief, particularly regarding the *ketubah*.

### First Position

The opening position of the *mishnah* — the *mishnah rishona* — holds that the woman is believed, and that her belief extends even to the *ketubah*. There is no concern that she might be fabricating her claim in order to force a divorce; she is credited fully, even though believing her provides her with no financial disincentive to lie.

### Second Position

The later position — the *mishnah acharona* — reverses course. There, the *rabbanan* express concern that the woman is inventing the entire account in order to extract a *get*, and therefore she is not believed in any of these cases.

### Rambam Middle Path

Between these two poles lies the Rambam's middle position, highly significant in its practical implications: if a woman declares that she finds her husband so intolerable that she cannot bear to live with him and cannot maintain intimate relations, the Rambam holds that she is believed — but she does not collect her *ketubah*. Critically, the Rambam rules that the husband is compelled to grant her a *get*. This position, which has largely been abandoned in the *Ashkenazi* world, bears directly on contemporary *agunah* discussions as a path that has been foregone.

## Terumah & Doubt

### Terumah Question

The *gemara* opens with a *she'elah*: in the case of a wife of a *kohen* who claims she was raped — which is the paradigm case for the *mishnah*, since rape renders such a woman forbidden to her *kohen* husband while it would not render the wife of a *Yisrael* forbidden — and who is not believed according to the *mishnah acharona*, what is her status with respect to *terumah*? Can she continue eating *terumah*? How thoroughly do we disbelieve her?

### Rav Sheshet

*Rav Sheshet* rules that she continues to eat *terumah*. To act otherwise — to tell her to refrain from *terumah* — would be to communicate to the world that she has a doubtful status, that there is a *safek* regarding her. We cannot adopt a position that formally says we do not believe her and simultaneously signal to the outside world that we half-believe her.

### Shelo Totzi La'az

The operative concern is *shelo totzi la'az al baneha* — that casting doubt on her status will raise questions about her children's legitimacy as *kohanim*. If she is actually forbidden to her husband and subsequently bears children, those children would be *chalalim*. Any public signal of ambiguity regarding her status would therefore taint the status of children born during the marriage. So *Rav Sheshet* insists: we must project a complete, unambiguous position that she is fully permitted to her husband.

### Rava Position

*Rava* disagrees. He rules that she should not eat *terumah*. His reasoning is that abstaining from *terumah* does not necessarily communicate a problematic status to the outside world — *efshar d'achla chullin*, she might simply be eating the *chulin* food in the house rather than the *terumah*. The practical oddity of this logic is readily apparent: it is hardly inconspicuous when someone consistently avoids a particular category of food, and those who visit would certainly notice. Nevertheless, that is *Rava*'s position.

### Rava's Qualification

*Rava* adds, however, that even *Rav Sheshet* would agree that if she becomes widowed or divorced, she should stop eating *terumah*. Why? Because while her husband was alive and she was eating *terumah*, any children born during that period will be assumed by observers to have been born under a period of her uncontested status. If, after her husband's death, people notice she has stopped eating *terumah*, they will naturally assume something occurred *after* the husband died — a subsequent rape or other incident — and that will not retroactively cast doubt on the children born during the marriage. Thus, *Rav Sheshet*'s policy of protecting her status for the sake of the children can, in *Rava*'s view, be suspended once the marriage has ended, since the temporal gap protects the children's status regardless.

Yet this is a deeply strange position. *Rava* is saying that we should instruct this woman to begin adopting a practice that will broadcast to the community that she has a problematic status. The underlying tension is significant: *Rava* is unable to fully set aside his discomfort with entirely disbelieving her. He cannot fully let go of the possibility that her claim might be true, and that unease resurfaces once she leaves the marriage.

What is most striking is that both *Rav Sheshet* and *Rava*, despite their disagreement on practical policy, are each reflecting a residual uncertainty. Neither has fully made peace with the position that she simply is not believed. *Rav Sheshet* sets aside his discomfort for reasons of public policy; *Rava* cannot. But both are signaling that the doubts have not been fully extinguished.

## Standards of Belief

### Cheskas Tahara

Setting aside the question — which arose in the previous discussion — of *shavya nafshah chaticha de'issura*, of what she herself knows in her own mind, it is worth stepping back to recall that it is not so straightforward that a woman's self-report should be automatically credited to change her status. Consider the analogy: if a pile of grain had an established *chezkas tahara* and a single witness reported that a *tamei* person touched it, there would be a genuine question about *eid echad ne'eman be'issurin* versus the force of an existing *chazaka*. The question of whether testimony is effective when the subject is *beyado* — within the person's own control or knowledge — versus when it is not, adds further complexity. Personal status raises these questions with even greater intensity. It is simply not self-evident that a woman's own testimony about herself is sufficient, for all purposes, to change her status vis-à-vis the rest of the world.

### Proof Threshold

This raises the deeper threshold question: what standard of proof is actually required to establish that a woman is forbidden to her husband, or to compel a husband to divorce his wife? If one were asked *ab initio*, the answer would not be "whatever she says is sufficient." So when the *mishnah rishona* declares that she is believed and the *mishnah acharona* declares she is not, the shift may be less dramatic than it appears. The bigger structural problem, as noted previously, is that according to her own words she is claiming to be engaged in an *aveira* by continuing to live with her husband — which makes it difficult to understand how we can compel her to remain in the marriage regardless. That tension does not disappear simply because we formally decline to believe her.

### Takana Logic

One way to understand the shift from the *mishnah rishona* to the *mishnah acharona* is as a *takana* — a rabbinic enactment that simply overrides the default rule and refuses to credit her testimony regardless of what the *halacha* would otherwise require. The alternative understanding is that once the possibility of ulterior motive has been raised — once the concern of *shema einah natna be'acher* has been introduced — this is enough to reassess her believability altogether, not as an override, but as a genuine recalculation that brings us back to the status quo of her being permitted to her husband. Under this reading, there is no *takana* at all; it is simply a recognition that once motive and veracity are called into question, she no longer meets the threshold of credibility needed to change her status.

Both *Rav Sheshet* and *Rava*, as noted, suggest that whatever the mechanism, the doubts have not fully dissipated — and that lingering uncertainty is what animates their respective positions on *terumah*.

## Ones & Ketubah

### Rav Pappa Quiz

*Rav Pappa* reports that *Rava* posed a question to them as a kind of test: if the wife of a *kohen* was raped and is therefore forbidden to continue living with her *kohen* husband and must be divorced — does she collect her *ketubah* or not? The intuitive answer would seem to be yes, since it is not her fault. But the *hava amina* runs as follows: since *ones* — rape — in the case of the wife of a *kohen* is equivalent in its legal consequences to willful adultery in the case of the wife of a *Yisrael*, in the sense that both require a divorce, perhaps she should receive no *ketubah*, just as the willfully adulterous wife of a *Yisrael* receives none.

### Woman's Counter

The *gemara* frames the counter-argument in a striking way. Rather than resting simply on the absence of fault, it has the woman say, in effect: *ana hazina* — I am actually fit. My personal status has not been compromised. Even after being raped, I remain fully eligible to marry a *Yisrael*. *Degavra hu denistachfa sadeh* — it is you, the husband, whose field has been flooded. The disability that requires the divorce is a function of your status as a *kohen*, not of any fault of mine. The *gemara* is not suggesting the woman bears any moral blame — the *Torah* itself declares *velanaarah lo ta'aseh davar*. Rather, the question being probed is: when divorce is required and neither party is at fault, does the *ketubah* attach? And the answer the *gemara* works toward is that since the cause of the divorce is rooted in the husband's status as a *kohen*, it is, to some degree, attributable to him.

### Resolution

The resolution comes from the *mishnah* itself. The *mishnah* discusses a woman who says *tme'ah ani lach* — "I am forbidden to you" — and rules that she has a *ketubah*. The *gemara* works through the possible cases: if she is the wife of a *Yisrael* claiming willful adultery, she would not receive a *ketubah*; if she is the wife of a *Yisrael* claiming rape, she is not forbidden to her husband at all, so there is no question. The only case that makes sense of the *mishnah* is the wife of a *kohen* claiming rape. And the *mishnah* rules she receives her *ketubah*. *Rav Pappa* and the other students informed *Rava* that they could prove the answer from the *mishnah* itself — and they passed the test.

## Gerashtani Question

### Case Intro

The *gemara* now turns to a new question: *amra leh lebaal gerashtani* — a woman says directly to her husband's face, "You divorced me," without producing a *get*. She claims the *get* was lost, but insists the divorce occurred. What is her status?

### Rav Hamnuna

*Rav Hamnuna* reasons from the *mishnah*. Even according to the *mishnah acharona*, which rules that a woman is not believed when she claims rape or adultery, the reason for the disbelief is that *hatam hu demeshakra* — there we suspect her of lying. What enables her to lie in those cases? *Deyad'a debaa'la lo yada bah* — she knows that her husband has no way of independently verifying her claim. She knows he cannot contradict her. This, combined with the possibility that she harbors ulterior motives — *shema einah natna be'acher* — is what makes her statement insufficiently credible. But in the case of *gerashtani*, even if she has motivations to be free of the marriage, *hayad'a bah* — the husband would know whether or not he gave her a *get*. A man does not forget whether he divorced his wife. And therefore, *chazaka ein isha me'iza paneha bifnei ba'alah* — there is a presumption that a woman will not brazenly lie to her husband's face about something he knows to be false. In such a case, *Rav Hamnuna* rules, she is believed.

### Husband Knows

The deeper implication of *Rav Hamnuna*'s position is significant: the *mishnah acharona*'s refusal to believe the woman is not an absolute, overriding *takana* that silences her in every conceivable circumstance. Rather, the *mishnah acharona* has raised doubts and questions about her credibility in specific types of cases. Those doubts can be overcome where there are stronger countervailing reasons to believe her. The *chazaka* of *ein isha me'iza paneha bifnei ba'alah* supplies such a reason.

---

## Rava Counter

### Shame Logic

*Rava* challenges this analysis. He argues that even the *mishnah rishona*'s decision to believe the woman did not reflect a general disposition to credit whatever she says. Rather, it rested on specific circumstances that made her claim particularly credible — namely, that nobody would make a claim of rape or adultery if it were false, because even for the victim, even in a case where she bears no moral blame, such a disclosure carries an element of shame and vulnerability. The *mishnah rishona* believed her *davka* because of that logic — and even then, the *mishnah acharona* ultimately chose not to believe her.

### Motivation Override

For the case of *gerashtani*, *Rava* disagrees with *Rav Hamnuna*. He acknowledges the force of *chazaka ein isha me'iza paneha*, but argues that when a woman is sufficiently motivated to exit the marriage — *dezimnin detakif lah min gavra* — she can summon the resolve to confront her husband directly and lie to his face. The desire to leave the marriage, when powerful enough, overrides the *chazaka* that she would be too ashamed to lie in his presence.

### Absolute Position

This raises the further question of whether *Rava*'s position amounts to saying that the woman can never be believed once we have reached the *mishnah acharona*. It is possible that *Rava*'s view has a categorical dimension: given that even in the *mishnah rishona*, where the case for believing her was at its strongest — where there was genuine *davka* reason to credit her — the *mishnah acharona* nevertheless refused to do so, *Rava* may be unwilling to permit any exceptions. Having established a regime of non-belief, he may hold that it admits no case-by-case reappraisal.

## Testing Positions

### Hashamayim Case

*Rav Mesharshya* raises a challenge to *Rava* from the *mishnah*. The *mishnah* includes the case of a woman who says *hashamayim beini u'veinecha* — "heaven is between you and me." As the *Bavli* reads this phrase, it is a claim about the husband's inability to father children — specifically, a claim that he cannot ejaculate with sufficient force. The *mishnah rishona* believes her in this case. But if *Rava* is correct that the *mishnah rishona* only believed the woman in cases where her claim was intrinsically credible due to the shame involved in making it, how does the *hashamayim* case fit? Here she is saying something embarrassing about *him*, not about herself. There is no shame to her in making this claim, and yet the *mishnah rishona* credits her. This seems to undercut *Rava*'s reading of the *mishnah rishona*'s rationale.

### Fertility Claim

*Rava* responds that the case is more specific than it appears. For the woman to invoke *hashamayim beini u'veinecha* in the relevant sense, she cannot simply speak in vague terms — she must articulate explicitly the nature of the deficiency, specifically that he *ein yorek ke-ḥetz*, that he cannot ejaculate with the force of an arrow. And *Rava* argues that this explicit formulation — describing intimate physiological detail about her husband in front of a *beit din* — is itself something that she would not say if it were not true. Even though the embarrassment attaches to him rather than to her, the sheer intimacy and specificity of the statement, made in a public judicial forum, is such that she would not articulate it unless it were accurate.

### Rava Reasserts

Thus *Rava* reasserts his position: the *mishnah rishona* believed her across all its cases *davka* because each case presented a specific and identifiable reason to credit the claim. In no case did the *mishnah rishona* operate from a general presumption of female credibility. And in each case, notwithstanding those reasons, the *mishnah acharona* declined to believe her.

### Acharona Test

The *gemara* then runs the test in the other direction: if *Rav Hamnuna* is correct that the *mishnah acharona*'s regime of disbelief admits exceptions when the husband would know the truth of the matter, should the *hashamayim* case also constitute an exception under the *mishnah acharona*? After all, the husband knows whether or not *ein yorek ke-ḥetz* applies to him — he is the one to whom it is happening. If she is willing to say that to his face, and he would know whether she was lying, should she not be believed even under the *mishnah acharona*?

### Self-Conviction

*Rav Hamnuna*'s position here is that she would not be believed even in this case under the *mishnah acharona*, because *michezi debe'iah yada* — while the husband may be aware of what occurs during the act of intercourse itself, *beyoreh ke-ḥetz lo yada* — he does not have independent clarity about the precise nature of how he ejaculates in the technical sense relevant to the claim. More to the point: it is not what the husband actually knows that determines whether the woman will lie, but what she can convince *herself* that he does not know. If she can persuade herself that he might have doubts, or that he won't be able to definitively refute her, that is enough for her to be willing to make the claim. *Umishum hachi meshakra* — and for that reason she will lie. The decisive variable is her own psychology of rationalization, not the objective state of the husband's knowledge.

## Halakhic Ruling

### Follow Hamnuna

The *halakhic* ruling is that we follow *Rav Hamnuna* against *Rava*. The principle of *chazaka ein isha me'iza paneha bifnei ba'alah* is accepted broadly throughout the *gemara*, and the ruling here is in line with that general acceptance. The implication is significant: it is not only that we believe a woman when she says *gerashtani* — it is that the *mishnah acharona*'s regime is not absolute, and there are circumstances in which a woman will be believed even within it.

### Ran on Relations

The *Ran* draws out a further consequence. Once we accept *Rav Hamnuna*'s principle — that where the husband would know the truth of the matter, she will be believed even under the *mishnah acharona* — it follows that a woman who says her husband refuses to have intimate relations with her altogether would also be believed. *She'omeret eino nizkak imi klal* — she claims he will not be with her at all. Since the husband knows whether this is true, and since she would not lie to his face about something he knows, she is believed — *keivan deba'ala yada bah afilu lemishna acharona meheimna* — and he is compelled to give her a *get* and pay her *ketubah*.

### Ketubah Categories

The *Ran* notes the broader *halakhic* implication: a man who refuses to have relations with his wife is failing to fulfill the *mitzvah* of *onah*, and the woman's testimony about that refusal is sufficient to compel divorce and payment of *ketubah*. Similarly, the *Ran* cites the principle that if a man refuses to have relations unless they remain clothed — if he will not fulfill *onah* in the manner *halakhically* required — he must also *yotzi ve-yitein ketubah*, divorce her and pay her *ketubah*. In all these cases, the woman's claim is credited precisely because the husband would know its truth, and the *chazaka* of *ein isha me'iza paneha* therefore applies.

### Ran's Caveat

The *Ran* adds an important qualification, attributed to the *Ri*: the woman is believed and receives her *get* and *ketubah* only when her expressed demand is for the *get* itself — for the divorce. If she draws attention to the *ketubah*, if she emphasizes the financial dimension of her claim, this introduces a new motive and weakens her credibility. The focus on money raises fresh concerns of the same kind as *shema einah natna be'acher*. But if she says simply that she wants to be free of the marriage and demands a *get*, without foregrounding the monetary claim, she will receive both the *get* and the *ketubah*. In practice this points to the need for careful framing — which, as *Tosafot* noted somewhat wryly, is precisely why one needs competent legal counsel.

## Story One

### Daily Ritual

The *gemara* now presents a series of narrative cases that conclude the *perek*. The first involves a woman who, as a regular intimate gesture following relations with her husband, would wash his hands. The *Ran* suggests she may have done this for reasons connected to *tumah* — perhaps her husband was a *kohen* — though what precisely this would address is not entirely clear, since more significant *tumah* concerns require immersion in a *mikveh*. In any case, it functioned as a gesture of intimacy.

### Husband Surprised

One day, the husband noticed she had brought water to wash his hands, even though, as he told her, they had not had relations that day. Why was she performing this ritual?

### Wife's Explanation

Her response was striking: if it wasn't you, then it must have been one of those non-Jewish fragrance merchants — *ahaloyei*, aloe merchants — who had been present in the area. It must have been dark, she must have heard someone she took to be her husband, and she went to him. It was essentially the same logic as the classic case of mistaken identity in the dark, familiar from other contexts in the *gemara*.

The reason the *gemara* specifies that the other man was a non-Jew is relevant to the question of her status: for the wife of a *kohen*, even a mistaken or coerced act renders her forbidden, so the identification of the man as a non-Jewish stranger is the legally operative detail that would create her prohibition. For the wife of a *Yisrael*, even if her account were believed, she would not be forbidden to her husband since she acted under the belief that it was her husband — a case of *shogeg* that would not render her forbidden.

### Rav Nachman

*Rav Nachman* ruled: *ein anu mashgichin bah* — pay no attention to her. *V'leit bei mashasha b'mila* — there is no substance to her words. She is seeking a divorce and has constructed an ingenious account — one designed to appear as if the disclosure was involuntary, drawn out by the husband's question rather than volunteered — precisely in order to make it look credible. Despite how convincing the circumstantial presentation might seem, *Rav Nachman* rules categorically that we do not believe her.

This is noteworthy: the ruling is categorical, not case-by-case. *Rav Nachman* does not undertake a granular assessment of the plausibility of her story. This connects to the earlier discussion about whether the *mishnah acharona*'s disbelief is absolute or contextual. Here, the ruling appears to favor the categorical approach — and, as we noted, this actually serves the interests of a woman who does not want to be divorced, since by not believing her we allow the marriage to continue.

## Story Two

### Painful Relations

The second case involves a woman who was visibly not in a good mood with her husband. He asked her why. She replied that she had never experienced such pain during relations as she had that day.

### No Relations

Her husband told her: we did not have relations today.

### Merchants Again

She responded: if that is so, then it must have been those non-Jewish fragrance merchants who had been present in the house that day — *dilma chad minhon*, perhaps one of them.

### Another Man

*Rav Nachman* again ruled: do not pay attention to her — *natna einayha be'acher hi hi*, she has set her eyes on another man. Once more, despite the apparent circumstantial weight of the evidence, the ruling is categorical. The couple's ability to remain married is preserved precisely through that categorical disbelief.

## Story Three

### Caught & Run

The third case is different in kind. A man was hiding behind closed doors with a married woman — not his wife — when the husband came home. The man broke through the wicker gate and fled.

### Hiding Logic

*Rava* ruled: *itteta sharya* — the woman is permitted. His reasoning: *im itta de-avar issura*, had he actually committed adultery with her, *mikhse hava mirchas* — he would have hidden more carefully. He would have taken greater care to ensure that he was not seen fleeing. The very brazenness of his visible escape suggests that there was nothing he needed to conceal.

### Ran Clarifies

The *Ran* addresses a question that lurks beneath the surface of this ruling: does this mean that without *Rava*'s reasoning, the circumstantial evidence of a man found hiding with a married woman would be sufficient to render her forbidden to her husband? The *Ran* is explicit that it does not. *She-ein itteta niaseret al ba'alah* except through *kinnuy ve-stira* — the formal *sotah* procedure involving a witnessed warning followed by witnessed seclusion — *o be-eidei davar barur*, or through witnesses who observe the act itself with substantial clarity. Circumstantial evidence alone, however suggestive, is insufficient to render a woman forbidden to her husband.

### Reassurance

If that is so, what is *Rava* accomplishing with his reasoning? The *Ran* explains: *hayu romi b'chei hai gavna v'chol bar nafesh d'chush al atzmo k'dei la'asot yedei shamayim* — the husband, even without a *halachic* obligation to divorce his wife, might nevertheless feel that morally or personally he cannot remain in the marriage given what he witnessed. He suspects that something occurred even if he cannot prove it. *Rava*'s reasoning is directed at *him* — at calming his fears and providing him with a rational basis for concluding that his suspicions are unfounded. The rabbinic authority here operates not only on the level of formal *halacha* but as a form of pastoral reassurance, requiring considerable confidence and leadership on the rabbi's part.

## Story Four

### Cress & Snake

The final case is the most vivid. An adulterer — described as such, though the irony of that label will become apparent — had entered a woman's home and was hiding behind the door or in the closet when the husband arrived. On the table was cress that a snake had bitten from. The concern about uncovered food bitten by a snake — *gilu'i*, the category of leaving food exposed — is that snake venom may be left behind.

### Life-Saving Warning

The husband, unaware of the danger, was about to eat from the poisoned cress. *B'lo da'ata de-itteta* — and the wife, who may have known about the snake but had not noticed her husband was about to eat, was also unaware of what was happening in that moment. The man hiding in the closet, seeing the husband about to eat the poisoned food, called out: *lo teichul mihanech d'tam'inhu chivya* — do not eat from that; the snake has bitten from it. He exposed himself in order to save the husband's life.

### Rava's Logic

*Rava* ruled: *itteta sharya* — the woman is permitted. *Ei ita de-avad issura* — had he actually committed adultery with her, *nicha lei de-leichul ve-leimut* — he would have wanted the husband to eat the poisoned cress and die. The *gemara* supports this from a verse: *ki nafu ve-dam biyadeihem* — they committed adultery and blood was on their hands. An adulterer, the logic runs, would not recoil from the husband's death; indeed he would welcome it.

### Secret Affair

The *gemara* anticipates the counter-argument: perhaps he *did* commit adultery, and yet *davka* wanted the husband to live — *ke-dei de-tehavi itteta leh*, so that the woman would remain available to him as a secret lover. For as the verse says, *mayim genuvim yimtaku ve-lechem setarim yinam* — stolen waters are sweet and hidden bread is tasty. He prefers the clandestine intrigue of adultery over marriage; he has no interest in the husband's death and every interest in his continued existence. The *gemara* rejects this counter-argument and maintains *Rava*'s ruling: the presumption is that an adulterer would want the husband out of the way, and the man's life-saving warning is therefore proof that he did not commit adultery. The woman is permitted to her husband.

## Shalom Bayis Synthesis

### Perek Context

It is worth pausing to observe where these stories appear. They close an entire *perek* whose central subject is a husband's power to annul his wife's vows. Whatever dimension of power that dynamic reflects, it also represents a *halakhic* mechanism aimed at reducing sources of marital tension — an instrument for preventing or dissolving vows that generate discord between husband and wife.

### Dual Method

The concluding stories operate with a dual method. On the formal *halakhic* level, the *gemara* consistently refuses to believe the woman's claim that she committed adultery or was violated — a categorical disbelief that, paradoxically, protects the integrity of marriages in which neither party actually wants a divorce. On the informal level, the *gedolei Yisrael* appearing in these stories — *Rav Nachman*, *Rava* — employ circumstantial reasoning and pastoral reassurance to address the husband's fears and suspicions, providing him with a rational and emotional basis for remaining in the marriage.

### Goal

The dual method — formal disbelief combined with circumstantial reassurance — achieves the goal of reducing marital discord while maintaining *halakhic* integrity. The *chazal* are not simply dismissing the woman's words; they are navigating an intricate balance between the demands of *din*, the welfare of the marriage, and the dignity of both parties. The final image of the *gemara* — the man hiding in the closet calling out to warn the husband not to eat the poisoned food — is almost cinematically resonant: the would-be adulterer becomes, in the end, the husband's unlikely rescuer, and *itteta sharya*.
