# Gittin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get in Reshus HaRabim

### Gittin Only

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן קִדּוּשִׁין. אָמַר רַבִּי אַסִּי אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: לְגִיטִּין אָמְרוּ, וְלֹא לְדָבָר אַחֵר.
>
> **Translation:** § The mishna stated the *halakha* about a bill of divorce that was closer to him or her, and the mishna added: **And** the **same** *halakhot* apply **with regard to betrothal. Rabbi Asi says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says: They said** these *halakhot* only **with regard to bills of divorce, but not with regard to another matter.**

The daf opens in the middle of the previous discussion on 78b, concerning the scenario in which a husband throws a *get* in *reshus ha-rabim* and it falls near the woman. The earlier sugya established a debate about what "near her" means: one position holds it must land within her *arba amot*, the classic mode of rabbinic *kinyan*; the other, attributed to Rabbi Yochanan, holds that as long as she is in a position to guard and protect it — even if it is far from her — it is considered under her control. Rabbi Yochanan's reading underscores a fundamental principle: the act of giving a *get* need not conform to the same formal requirements as *kinyan* of an ordinary object. It can be a *netina* without a conventional *kinyan*. At the same time, the *Gemara* closed with a significant practical stringency — endorsed by Tosafot — that in practice the *get* must reach her hand directly, or at minimum land in her yard. None of the more lenient constructions of the *Mishnah* are relied upon *le-halacha*.

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* on our daf turns to the final clause of the *Mishnah*, which extends the same rule — that throwing works — not only to *gittin* but also to *kiddushin* and repayment of a debt. This raises a sharp question: if Rabbi Yochanan's position is that the *get* works even when it lands far away, provided she can protect it, why would such a non-standard act of transfer function in *kiddushin* or debt repayment, which are governed by ordinary *kinyan* principles?

The *Gemara* opens with Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan: *L'Gittin amru v'lo l'davar acher* — this principle was stated for *gittin* alone and does not apply to anything else. This amounts to reading the *Mishnah* as if its additional cases simply do not mean what they appear to say.

### Kiddushin & Debt

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְרַבִּי אַסִּי: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן קִדּוּשִׁין! שָׁאנֵי הָתָם דִּכְתִיב ״וְיָצְאָה״–״וְהָיְתָה״.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba raised an objection to** the statement of **Rabbi Asi** from what was explicitly taught in the mishna: **And** the **same** *halakhot* apply **with regard to betrothal.** He responded: **It is different there** with regard to betrothal, **as it is written: "And she departs** out of his house, and goes **and becomes** another man's wife" (Deuteronomy 24:2). The Torah thereby compares betrothal, through the use of the word "becomes," to divorce, through the use of the words "and she departs," so one can learn the *halakhot* of betrothal from the *halakhot* of divorce. One cannot learn *halakhot* concerning other matters from divorce.

Rabbi Abba immediately challenges Rav Assi: the *Mishnah* explicitly states *v'chein l'inyan kiddushin* — and likewise for *kiddushin*! How can Rav Assi, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, simply bracket the plain text of the *Mishnah*?

### Yatza V'Hayata

The *Gemara* responds that *kiddushin* is not a true counter-example. The verse in Devarim reads *v'yatza v'hayata* — she leaves one husband and becomes the wife of another — which links *geirushin* and *kiddushin* as conceptually mirrored. If the mechanism works for *gittin*, it works for *kiddushin* as well, because the Torah itself draws this parallel.

This linkage is less surprising once one recalls that *kiddushin* already operates differently from ordinary *kinyan*. *Kiddushei kesef*, for instance, can be effected through *bahahi hana'ah* — the abstract benefit a woman derives from some act done on her behalf — without any concrete object passing into her possession at all. There are even opinions that one can perform *kiddushin* with a stolen object, since the woman acquires certain rights in it even absent formal ownership. Given these anomalies, it is not so strange to say that just as *gittin* can be effected through a protected-but-not-formally-transferred object, *kiddushin* can parallel it.

### Debt Condition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵיתִיבֵיהּ: וְכֵן לְעִנְיַן הַחוֹב, ״זְרוֹק לִי חוֹבִי״ וּזְרָקוֹ לוֹ, קָרוֹב לַמַּלְוֶה – זָכָה הַלֹּוֶה, קָרוֹב לַלֹּוֶה – הַלֹּוֶה חַיָּיב, מֶחֱצָה עַל מֶחֱצָה – שְׁנֵיהֶם יַחְלוֹקוּ!
>
> **Translation:** **Another objection to** his statement was **raised** based on what is taught in a *baraita*: **And** the **same** *halakhot* apply **with regard to a debt.** If a creditor said to a debtor: **Throw** the payment for **my debt to me, and he threw it to him** and the money fell **closer to the creditor,** the **debtor merits,** i.e., his debt is repaid. If it fell **closer to the debtor** and the money was lost, **the debtor** is still **obligated** to pay. If it was **equally balanced** and was lost, **the two of them divide** it, i.e., the debtor owes half of the amount. Accordingly, the *halakhot* of debts are compared to the *halakhot* of divorce.

The debt case, however, is different and cannot be resolved so easily. The *Mishnah* says: *Zrok li chovi v'zarko lo, karov l'malveh zachah lo* — if the debtor throws the money and it lands near the creditor, the creditor has acquired it; if it lands nearer to the debtor, the debtor remains liable; and if it falls midway, they split. This appears to be a genuine application of the "proximity-as-acquisition" principle in an ordinary property context, which directly contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's insistence that this mechanism is unique to *gittin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״זְרוֹק לִי חוֹבִי וְתִיפָּטֵר״. אִי הָכִי מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? לָא צְרִיכָא, דַּאֲמַר לֵיהּ: ״זְרוֹק לִי חוֹבִי בְּתוֹרַת גִּיטִּין״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara answers: **Here we are dealing with** a case **where** the creditor **said to** the debtor: **Throw** the payment for **my debt to me, and you will be absolved** of your obligation to pay through this. The Gemara challenges this: **If so, what** is the purpose **of stating** this? Since he made an explicit stipulation, it is obvious that the outcome will be in accordance with his stipulation. The Gemara answers: **No,** it is **necessary** in a case **where** the creditor **said to** the debtor: **Throw** the payment for **my debt to me** according **to the procedure** in effect with **bills of divorce,** meaning that the *halakha* that applies to divorce should apply here too.

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha b'mai askinan d'amar lei zrok li chovi v'tipater* — the *Mishnah* is discussing a case where the creditor specifically stipulated, "Throw it to me and you will be exempt." In other words, it is not that throwing near the creditor is inherently an act of debt repayment; rather, the creditor himself created a special condition under which the throw would discharge the obligation.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאַכַּתִּי, מַאי לְמֵימְרָא? מַהוּ דְּתֵימָא, מָצֵי אָמַר לֵיהּ מְשַׁטֶּה אֲנִי בָּךְ; קָא מַשְׁמַע לַן.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And still, what** is the purpose **of stating** this, since the creditor stipulated explicitly: According to the procedure in effect with bills of divorce? The Gemara answers: **Lest you say** that the creditor **can say to** the debtor: **I am teasing you,** and what I stipulated has no validity. Therefore the mishna **teaches us** that if he stipulated that it should be treated as a bill of divorce, then his stipulation takes effect.

The Gemara immediately objects: if that is all that is happening, *mai le-memra* — what is the *chiddush*? Of course if the creditor declared that the throw would suffice, it works. The *Gemara* then refines the case further: *d'amar lei zrok li chovi b'toras gittin* — the creditor said, "Throw me my debt in the manner of a *get*," i.e., as if you were giving me a *get*. The *Gemara* again presses: still, what is the novelty? And the final answer is: *mahu d'teima mechi amar lei meshachek ani bach* — one might have thought the debtor could later claim he was joking, that he never seriously meant to frame the payment in those unusual terms. The *Mishnah* therefore teaches that once he made that declaration, he cannot retract it.

This is, as the lecturer notes, a strikingly forced reading of the *Mishnah*. The plain implication of the *Mishnah* is that throwing a debt payment near the creditor functions as a valid act of repayment — yet Rabbi Yochanan, through Rav Assi, refuses to accept this, driven by the conviction that his radical reading of *gittin* (that a *get* can work even without a conventional *kinyan*) is so exceptional that it cannot bleed into normal monetary law. He is therefore willing to impose a nearly impossible reading on the *Mishnah* rather than concede that this mechanism is a general mode of acquisition.

It is worth pausing to note that a debt repayment is arguably not identical to an ordinary *kinyan*. When one sells a basketball, the object was never the buyer's, and acquisition requires a formal *ma'aseh kinyan*. But when repaying a debt, the money in some sense already belongs to the creditor — it is merely being returned. Whether that distinction would affect the formal requirements of repayment is an independent question the *Gemara* does not resolve here, but it sharpens why Rabbi Yochanan's position, however forced as a reading of the *Mishnah*, has internal logic.

### Direct Handover

Rabbi Yochanan's stringency bears revisiting in light of all this. The same Rabbi Yochanan who expands the *get's* validity to cover situations where the woman can merely protect the document — even a hundred *amot* away — also insists, in practice, that the *get* must reach her hand directly. Whatever theoretical latitude the *Mishnah* provides, *le-ma'aseh* Rabbi Yochanan does not rely on it, and a woman who received a *get* by any of these indirect means would be in a state of doubt. This is a significant practical *chumra*, one that transforms what might seem like a liberalization of the *Mishnah* into a stringency that renders her status as *megoreshet* uncertain unless the *get* came directly to her.

## Physical Control

### Get & Strings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: גֵּט בְּיָדָהּ וּמְשִׁיחָה בְּיָדוֹ; אִם יָכוֹל לְנַתְּקוֹ וְלַהֲבִיאוֹ אֶצְלוֹ – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת, וְאִם לָאו – מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מַאי טַעְמָא? בָּעֵינַן כְּרִיתוּת, וְלֵיכָּא.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says:** If **the bill of divorce was in her hand and a string** tied to the bill of divorce was **in his hand,** as he gave her the bill of divorce in this way, **if** the husband **can** still **pull** the bill of divorce out of her hand **and bring it to him, she is not divorced; and if** he is **not** able to do so, **she is divorced. What is the reason** that she is not divorced, despite the fact that he gave her a bill of divorce? The reason is that **we require severance, and it is lacking,** since the husband still has a hold on the bill of divorce.

Rav Chisda addresses a case of a *get* given with strings still attached: *get beyada u-meshicha beyado* — the *get* is in her hand, but the husband is holding a string connected to it. The rule is: *im yachol lenatko u-lehavyo etzlo einah megureshet* — if he can yank the string and retrieve the *get*, she is not divorced. If the string is effectively slack — if he cannot use it to reclaim the document — she is divorced. The reasoning is *ba'inan kerisus* — the Torah requires a complete severance between husband and wife, and if he retains the ability to reclaim the document, that severance has not been achieved. This is, of course, entirely literal, but it lends itself naturally to the metaphorical expression of a divorce "with strings attached."

### Sloped Hands

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה: הָיְתָה יָדָהּ עֲשׂוּיָה כְּקָטַפְרֵס, וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ; אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לְיָדָהּ – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. אַמַּאי? הָא כִּי נָפֵיל – בְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת דִּידַהּ קָא נָפֵיל! בִּדְלָא נָח.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says:** If **her hand was positioned like a slope and he threw it to her, despite** the fact **that the bill of divorce reached her hand, she is not divorced,** since it cannot remain in her hand and will fall out. The Gemara asks: **Why? But when** the bill of divorce **falls** from her hand, **it falls within her four cubits.** The Gemara answers: Rav Yehuda is discussing a case **where** the bill of divorce **did not rest** within her four cubits. Rather, it fell further away.

Rav Yehuda addresses another scenario: *haysah yada asuya ke-tafres* — her hand was positioned like a slope, angled downward. The husband threw the *get* into her sloped hand, and despite physically reaching her hand, it immediately slid off. The ruling is *af al pi she-higi'a le-yada einah megureshet* — even though it reached her hand, she is not divorced.

### Did Not Settle

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְתִיגָּרְשׁ מֵאַוֵּירָא דְּאַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת! תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּבָעֵי רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר – אַרְבַּע אַמּוֹת שֶׁאָמְרוּ, יֵשׁ לָהֶן אֲוִיר אוֹ אֵין לָהֶן אֲוִיר? תִּפְשׁוֹט דְּאֵין לָהֶן אֲוִיר! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּעוֹמֶדֶת עַל גַּב הַנָּהָר, דְּמֵעִיקָּרָא לְאִיבּוּד קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But** why not **let her be divorced** because the bill of divorce was in **the airspace of** her **four cubits? And if you do not say this, then** **resolve** the dilemma **that Rabbi Elazar raised** with regard to **the four cubits that** the Sages **stated** in the *halakhot* of acquisition: **Do they have airspace** with the same legal status **or do they not have airspace? Resolve** that dilemma from here, i.e., **that they do not have airspace.** The Gemara rejects this: One cannot resolve the dilemma from here since it is possible to say that **here we are dealing with** a woman who is **standing at the riverbank, as** the bill of divorce **stands to be destroyed from the outset,** since if it does not remain in her hand it will fall into the river. In such a case, everyone agrees that the airspace of the four cubits does not acquire the item.

The *Gemara* asks: even if we accept that the *get* slipping from her hand means she did not acquire it *via* her hand, why does it not take effect by virtue of landing within her *arba amot* once it hits the ground? The *Gemara* answers: *delo nach* — it did not come to rest. According to Rashi, before the *get* reached the ground it was blown away, or perhaps burnt, and never actually settled within her *arba amot*.

A further question arises: even if it did not come to rest, should she not acquire it *me-avira de-arba amot* — from the airspace of her *arba amot*? This raises an open question: when the Rabbis instituted *kinyan arba amot* in *reshus ha-rabim*, did that *kinyan* include the airspace above those four *amot*, or only the ground level?

The *Gemara* notes that this question — *yesh lahen avir ein lahen avir* — was itself posed by Rabbi Elazar and left unresolved. One might think the sloped-hands case resolves it: since the *get* in the airspace did not effect a divorce, perhaps *arba amot* has no airspace rights. But the *Gemara* deflects this inference: the case involves a woman standing at the edge of a river, so that when the *get* slid off her hands, it immediately passed over the airspace above the river. Since the riverbed is not a surface on which something can come to rest, the airspace above it does not belong to her *arba amot* — *mi-ikara le-ibud ka'ei*, it was headed for destruction from the outset.

### Airspace Rights

The broader principle — that airspace above a space where objects cannot come to rest does not constitute protected domain — carries implications for the subsequent discussion of rooftops and enclosed areas. The question of whether *arba amot* includes airspace thus remains formally unresolved, but the practical direction of the sugya leans toward excluding it, with the rooftop cases relying instead on property-based airspace rather than the rabbinic *kinyan* of *arba amot*.

## Rooftop Scenarios

### Woman Above

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת עַל רֹאשׁ הַגָּג וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לַאֲוִיר הַגָּג – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. הוּא מִלְּמַעְלָה וְהִיא מִלְּמַטָּה, וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא מֵרְשׁוּת הַגָּג, נִמְחַק אוֹ נִשְׂרַף – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: If a woman **was standing on top of the roof** and her husband was standing below, **and he threw** a bill of divorce **to her, once** the bill of divorce **reaches the airspace of the roof, she is divorced.** If **he** was **above** on the roof **and she** was **below, and he threw it to her, once it leaves the area of the roof,** even if the wording **was erased or** the document **was burned** before it fell to the ground, **she is divorced.**

The next *Mishnah* (79a) opens with two scenarios involving different vertical positions. In the first: *haisah omedes al rosh ha-gag* — the woman is standing on the rooftop and the man is at ground level; he throws the *get* up to her. The rule is: *keivan she-higi'a le-avir ha-gag harei zu megureshet* — once the *get* reaches the airspace of the rooftop, she is divorced, even before it lands. This introduces airspace of one's own property as a mechanism distinct from the *arba amot* discussed earlier. One's property certainly carries airspace rights, and once the *get* enters that airspace, if under normal circumstances it would have come to rest, she is already divorced — even if something intervenes (a wind, a fire) and it never actually lands.

### Unprotected Airspace

The second scenario reverses the positions: *hu mi-lema'ala ve-hi mi-lematah* — he is on the rooftop and she is below in the yard; he throws it down to her. The *Mishnah* states that once the *get* leaves the domain of the rooftop — *keivan she-yatza me-reshus ha-gag* — and enters the airspace of the yard below, even if it is subsequently erased or burnt in mid-air, she is divorced.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ וְהָא לָא מִינְּטַר! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: בְּגַג שֶׁיֵּשׁ לוֹ מַעֲקֶה עָסְקִינַן.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the airspace of the roof **unsecured?** Since the bill of divorce can be blown away from the roof by the wind, it should be considered an unsecured courtyard, which does not acquire items. If so, why is she divorced once the bill of divorce reaches the airspace of the roof? **Rav Yehuda said** that **Shmuel said: We are dealing with a roof that has a parapet.** Therefore, it is like a secured courtyard, since the bill of divorce cannot be blown away.

The *Gemara* immediately raises the obvious difficulty: *lo mintar* — the rooftop has no fence. It is a flat roof, open to wind and other elements. What makes the airspace above it or the airspace below it "protected"? The whole theory of airspace-as-acquisition rests on the concept of *chatzer ha-mishtameres* — a guarded domain that keeps things within it. An open rooftop, or the open air above a yard with no enclosing walls sufficiently high, is not inherently protective in this sense.

### Fenced Airspace

Rav Papa resolves this: *begag she-yesh lo ma'akeh askinan* — we are dealing with a rooftop that has a fence around it. Once there is a fence, the *get* must enter the space *within* the fence — not merely above the level of the fence — and then it is genuinely contained and protected. The *Mishnah*'s statement that she is divorced once it enters the airspace of the rooftop is predicated on the existence of such an enclosure.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מִלְּמַעְלָה: וְהָא לָא מִינְּטַר! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ מְחִיצוֹת הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת עוֹדְפוֹת עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: If **he** was **above** and she was below, and he threw the bill of divorce to her, once it leaves the area of the roof, she is divorced. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the airspace of the courtyard **not secured,** since the bill of divorce can be blown out of the courtyard by the wind? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** We are discussing a case **where the lower partitions** in the courtyard **extend beyond the upper** partitions on the roof, so that the bill of divorce will certainly fall in the courtyard.

The same principle applies to the second case, where the woman is below. The airspace through which the *get* travels after leaving the rooftop must be enclosed and protected. The *Gemara* cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *kegon she-hayu mechitzot ha-tachtonot odofot al ha-elyonot* — the walls of the lower yard are higher than the walls of the rooftop building. If one imagines a building standing in the middle of a large courtyard whose surrounding walls are taller than the rooftop, then the moment the *get* leaves the rooftop it is already within the enclosed, protected airspace of the courtyard below. This ruling is stated in parallel by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya, and by Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכֵן אָמַר רַבִּי אֶלְעָזָר אָמַר רַבִּי אוֹשַׁעְיָא: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ מְחִיצוֹת הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת עוֹדְפוֹת עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת. וְכֵן אָמַר עוּלָּא אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ מְחִיצוֹת הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת עוֹדְפוֹת עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** **And so Rabbi Elazar says** that **Rabbi Oshaya says:** We are discussing a case **where the lower partitions extend beyond the upper** partitions. **And so Ulla says** that **Rabbi Yoḥanan says:** We are discussing a case **where the lower partitions extend beyond the upper** partitions.

Rav Papa then asks Abaye: *ke-man ke-Rabbi?* — is this position consistent only with the view of *Rebbi*, who holds *kluta ke-mi she-hunchah damya* — that an object absorbed into a domain's airspace is legally equivalent to having landed there? And this would mean that only according to *Rebbi* does the *get* take effect the moment it enters the protected airspace, even before it comes to rest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רַבִּי אַבָּא לְעוּלָּא: כְּמַאן, כְּרַבִּי – דְּאָמַר: קְלוּטָה, כְּמִי שֶׁהוּנְּחָה דָּמְיָא?
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba said to Ulla: In accordance with whose** opinion is this *halakha* that once the bill of divorce enters the woman's domain, it is considered as though it was given to her, even though it was destroyed before it landed on the ground? Is it **in accordance with** the opinion of **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi, **who said** that with regard to the prohibition of transferring an item from one domain to another on Shabbat, an object **in airspace is considered at rest?** This means that any item in the airspace of a domain is considered to be placed within that domain.

Abaye responds: *afilu tima rabbanan* — even the *Rabanan* who dispute *Rebbi* on this point do so only for *Shabbat* purposes. The *Rabanan* hold that for *Shabbat*, being in the airspace of *reshut ha-yachid* is not equivalent to resting there — so one who throws an object into the airspace of a private domain but does not land it has not violated *Shabbat* according to the *Rabanan*. But here the concern is not about *Shabbat* at all. The question for *gittin* is whether the object is *mintar* — protected — and once it is in the enclosed protected airspace, *ve-ha kamintar*, it is protected. The two frameworks operate by entirely different criteria.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא רַבָּנַן – עַד כָּאן לָא פְּלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עֲלֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי, אֶלָּא לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת; אֲבָל הָכָא – מִשּׁוּם אִינְּטוֹרֵי הוּא, וְהָא קָא מִינְּטַר.
>
> **Translation:** Ulla **said to him: You** can **even say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of **the Rabbis,** who disagree with Rabbi Yehuda HaNasi, since **the Rabbis disagree with Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **only with regard to Shabbat.** They maintain that with regard to the *halakhot* of Shabbat, the item must actually come to rest in the domain to which it was transferred; while it is in the air, it is not considered as though it has landed on the ground. **But here,** in the case of divorce, **it is because** the bill of divorce **is secured** that she is divorced, **and** in this case **it is secured** while within the airspace of the partitions.

### Three Tefachim Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** עוּלָּא בַּר מְנַשְּׁיָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דַּאֲבִימִי אָמַר: הָכָא בְּפָחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַגַּג עָסְקִינַן, דְּכֹל פָּחוֹת מִשְּׁלֹשָׁה סָמוּךְ לַגַּג – כְּגַג דָּמֵי.
>
> **Translation:** **Ulla bar Menashya said in the name of Avimi** that there is another answer: **Here we are dealing with** a case in which the bill of divorce is **within three** handbreadths **of the roof, as anything within three** handbreadths **of the roof is** considered to be **like the roof** itself, based on the principle that views two solid surfaces as connected if the gap between them is less than three handbreadths wide.

Ulla bar Manashya, in the name of Avimi, offers a further approach: even without a fence, if the *get* is within three *tefachim* of the rooftop surface, it works — *d'chol pachot mi-shlosha samuch le-gag ke-gag dami* — because within three *tefachim* of the ground (or of a surface), halachic law treats the object as if it were already resting on that surface. This principle, familiar from other areas, means that for the first case — the woman above — even without an enclosure, if the *get* clears the lip of the roof and is within three *tefachim* of the roof surface, it is as though it has landed and she is divorced.

### Man Above

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הוּא מִלְּמַעְלָה: וְהָא לָא מִינְּטַר! אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: כְּגוֹן שֶׁהָיוּ מְחִיצוֹת הַתַּחְתּוֹנוֹת עוֹדְפוֹת עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנוֹת.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna: If **he** was **above** and she was below, and he threw the bill of divorce to her, once it leaves the area of the roof, she is divorced. The Gemara asks: **But isn't** the airspace of the courtyard **not secured,** since the bill of divorce can be blown out of the courtyard by the wind? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** We are discussing a case **where the lower partitions** in the courtyard **extend beyond the upper** partitions on the roof, so that the bill of divorce will certainly fall in the courtyard.

In the second case — the man above, woman below — the *Gemara* applies the same framework of higher surrounding walls. This is described by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, and repeated by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya and by Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kegon she-hayu mechitzot ha-tachtonot odofot al ha-elyonot*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁלֹשׁ מִדּוֹת בְּגִיטִּין; הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: קְלוּטָה כְּמִי שֶׁהוּנְּחָה, וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עָלֶיהָ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִינְּטוֹרֵי הוּא, וְהָא מִינְּטַר;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** There are **three** unique **qualities with regard to bills of divorce,** as compared to the *halakhot* of Shabbat. The first is **that which Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** An object **in airspace is** considered **at rest; and the Rabbis disagree with him. This matter** about which they disagree **applies** only **with regard to Shabbat. But here,** in the case of divorce, **it is because** the bill of divorce **is secured** that she is divorced; **and** once the bill of divorce is within the airspace of the partitions, **it is secured** and the Rabbis agree that she is divorced.

Rava articulates three distinct principles that emerge from comparing *gittin* to *Shabbat* law in this area. First, *Rebbi's* ruling that *kluta ke-mi she-hunchah damya* — an object is treated as having landed once it enters a domain's airspace — is disputed by the *Rabanan* for *Shabbat* purposes, but applies for *gittin* because in *gittin* the criterion is protection, not rest. Second, Rav Chisda had earlier ruled that placing an object atop a hundred-*amah* pole in *reshut ha-yachid* renders one liable for *Shabbat* — *reshut ha-yachid olah ad la-rakia*, private domain extends upward to the heavens — so even at that height, if the object has landed, one is liable. But that ruling is *hani milei l'inyan Shabbat*; by *gittin*, *mishum neturei hu*, the *get* must be in a protected space, and an object balanced on the top of a hundred-*amah* pole is not protected from forces that could dislodge it, regardless of its technical presence within *reshut ha-yachid*. These two cases are mirror images of one another: the first involves an object that is protected but has not rested; the second involves an object that has rested but is not protected. For *Shabbat*, the object in the first case does not create liability (lacking rest), while the object in the second does (having rested within *reshut ha-yachid* regardless of protection). For *gittin*, precisely the opposite: the first case works (protected space suffices), and the second does not (lack of protection disqualifies it).

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נָעַץ קָנֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּבְרֹאשׁוֹ טְרַסְקָל, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו – אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ מֵאָה אַמָּה, חַיָּיב, לְפִי שֶׁרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִינְּטוֹרֵי הוּא, וְהָא לָא מִינְּטַר;
>
> **Translation:** **And** the second matter is **that which Rav Ḥisda says** concerning the *halakhot* of Shabbat: In the case of one who **embedded a reed in** the ground of **a private domain, and on its top was a basket [*teraskal*], and he threw** an object from the public domain and **it rested on top of it, even** if the reed was **a hundred cubits high, he is liable because the private domain extends upward until the sky.** Therefore, the object landed in a private domain. **This matter applies** only **with regard to Shabbat,** as with regard to Shabbat domains are defined based on their partitions and the way in which they are used, which differs from the way domains are defined in other halakhic areas. **But here,** in the case of divorce, **it is because** the bill of divorce **is secured** that she is divorced, **and** when it is on the reed **it is not secured.**

### Trajectory

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִמְחַק: אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁנִּמְחַק דֶּרֶךְ יְרִידָה, אֲבָל נִמְחַק דֶּרֶךְ עֲלִיָּיה – לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מֵעִיקָּרָא לָא לְמֵינַח קָאֵי.
>
> **Translation:** § It was taught in the mishna that once the bill of divorce leaves the domain of the roof, even if the writing **was erased** before it reached her hand, she is divorced with it. **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: They taught** this *halakha* **only when** the writing **was erased** when it had already left the confines of the roof and was on its **descent,** entering her domain. **But** if the writing **was erased** while it was on its **ascent,** i.e., as it arched upward before falling down, then **no,** she is not divorced. **What is the reason** for this? Because **initially,** before it began its descent, the bill of divorce **is not destined to land** in her domain, since it could be blown elsewhere. Consequently, it cannot be considered as though it is already resting in her courtyard at that time.

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha rules that the case of *nimchak* — the *get* being erased in mid-air — depends critically on the direction of travel at the moment of erasure. *Lo shanu ela she-nimchak derech yerida* — the ruling that she is divorced applies only when the *get* was erased on its downward trajectory. *Aval nimchak derech aliyah lo* — if it was erased while still going upward, she is not divorced, *me-ikara lo le-minach ka'ei* — because at that moment it was not yet headed toward coming to rest in the yard. An object on an upward trajectory, even if gravity will eventually bring it down, is directionally oriented away from the receiving domain and cannot be considered as entering that domain's space at the moment of erasure. Only when the object is descending is it definitively in the process of landing within the protected space.

### Burning Get

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נִשְׂרַף: אָמַר רַב נַחְמָן אָמַר רַבָּה בַּר אֲבוּהּ: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא שֶׁקָּדַם גֵּט לִדְלֵיקָה, אֲבָל קָדְמָה דְּלֵיקָה לְגֵט – לָא. מַאי טַעְמָא? מֵעִיקָּרָא לִשְׂרֵיפָה קָאָזֵיל.
>
> **Translation:** It was taught in the mishna that once the bill of divorce leaves the domain of the roof, even if the document **was burned** before it reached her hand, she is divorced with it. Here, as well, **Rav Naḥman says** that **Rabba bar Avuh says: They taught** this *halakha* **only when** the throwing of the **bill of divorce preceded the fire. But** if the **fire preceded the** throwing of the **bill of divorce,** then the woman is **not** divorced. **What is the reason** for this? Because **initially,** when the bill of divorce was thrown, **it was heading toward being burned,** and therefore it is considered that he never gave her a bill of divorce.

Similarly, the case of *nisraf* — the *get* catching fire — depends on timing relative to when the fire began. If the *get* was thrown before the fire broke out, and the fire then destroys it in mid-air, she is divorced. But if there was already a fire burning in the yard when the husband threw the *get*, she is not divorced: *me-ikara le-sreifah ka-azil* — from the outset it was thrown into a fire, not into a protected domain. If a wind subsequently blew the *get* into a fire that was not in its path when thrown, that would presumably be treated as *kadam get le-dleikah* — the *get* preceded the fire — and she would be divorced, since the throw itself had nothing to do with the conflagration.

## Separate Domains

### Different Buildings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: רְשׁוּיוֹת חֲלוּקוֹת בְּגִיטִּין.
>
> **Translation:** § **Rav Ḥisda says: Domains are divided with regard to bills of divorce.** If there are several areas within the same domain, e.g., a house and a courtyard, or a courtyard and a roof, they are not all considered a single domain with regard to bills of divorce. Rather, each one is considered to be a separate domain.

Rav Chisda introduces the principle of *reshuyot chalukot be-gittin* — separate domains in the context of *gittin*. The case involves a structure or courtyard above which the rooftop appears as a single continuous surface, but below which different sections are owned by different people. Rav Chisda's ruling is that the separate ownership below creates separate domains above as well, such that even if on the roof it all looks like one flat space, the airspace over one building's section is distinct from the airspace over another's.

### Source Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ רָמֵי בַּר חָמָא לְרָבָא: מְנָא לֵיהּ לְסָבָא הָא? אֲמַר לֵיהּ, מַתְנִיתִין הִיא: הָיְתָה עוֹמֶדֶת עַל רֹאשׁ הַגָּג וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ, כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ גֵּט לַאֲוִיר הַגָּג – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת.
>
> **Translation:** **Rami bar Ḥama said to Rava: From where does the Elder,** i.e., Rav Ḥisda, derive **this** *halakha*? Rava **said to him: It is** derived from **the mishna** that taught: If the woman **was standing on the top of the roof and** her husband **threw** a bill of divorce **to her** from below, **once the bill of divorce reaches the airspace of the roof, she is divorced.**

Rami bar Chama asks Rava: *mina lei le-saba ha?* — from where does Rav Chisda derive this principle? Rava responds: *masnitin hi* — it comes from the *Mishnah* itself.

### One Space

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן? אִילֵימָא בְּגַג דִּידַהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידַהּ, לְמָה לִי אֲוִיר הַגָּג? אֶלָּא בְּגַג דִּידֵיהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידֵיהּ, כִּי הִגִּיעַ לַאֲוִיר הַגָּג מַאי הָוֵי?
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains: **With what are we dealing? If we say** that we are dealing **with** a case of **her roof and her courtyard, why do I** require the bill of divorce to reach **the airspace of the roof** in order for her to be divorced? Even if the bill of divorce remained in the courtyard, she would be divorced, since it is her courtyard. **Rather,** we must be dealing **with** a case of **his roof and his courtyard.** But **when** the bill of divorce **reaches the airspace of the roof, what of it?** Why is she then divorced, if the bill of divorce has not left his domain?

The *Gemara* works through the logic as follows. The *Mishnah* presents two scenarios: (1) she is on the rooftop and he throws from below — once it reaches the airspace of the roof she is divorced; (2) he is on the rooftop and she is below — once the *get* leaves the domain of the roof she is divorced. In both cases, the *Mishnah* simply describes where each party is standing without specifying who owns which space.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא פְּשִׁיטָא – בְּגַג דִּידַהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידֵיהּ. אֵימָא סֵיפָא: הוּא מִלְּמַעְלָה וְהִיא מִלְּמַטָּה וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ; כֵּיוָן שֶׁיָּצָא מֵרְשׁוּת הַגָּג, נִמְחַק אוֹ נִשְׂרַף – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. וְאִי בְּגַג דִּידַהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידֵיהּ, אַמַּאי מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת? אֶלָּא בְּגַג דִּידֵיהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידַהּ;
>
> **Translation:** **Rather,** it **is obvious** that we are dealing **with** a case of **her roof and his courtyard.** Rava questions this conclusion: **Say the latter clause** of the mishna, which taught: If **he** was **above** on the roof **and she** was **below, and he threw it to her, once it leaves the area of the roof,** even if the wording **was erased or** the document **was burned** before it fell to the ground, **she is divorced. And if** we are dealing **with** a case of **her roof and his courtyard, why is she divorced? Rather,** it is necessary to say that we are dealing **with** a case of **his roof and her courtyard.**

Now, who owns what? If she owns both the rooftop and the yard below, there is no *chiddush* in the airspace of the rooftop — the entire space is hers and of course she acquires whatever enters it. Conversely, if he owns both rooftop and yard, neither scenario makes sense, because neither the airspace of the roof nor the airspace of the yard is hers. The *Gemara* therefore says: in the first scenario (*reisha*), she must own the roof and he owns the yard; in the second (*seifa*), he owns the roof and she owns the yard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רֵישָׁא בְּגַג דִּידַהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידֵיהּ, סֵיפָא בְּגַג דִּידֵיהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידַהּ?!
>
> **Translation:** Rava asks: Is it possible that **the first clause** in the mishna is dealing **with** a case of **her roof and his courtyard, and the latter clause** in the mishna is dealing **with** a case of **his roof and her courtyard?** How could the same mishna discuss two *halakhot* where each one is dealing with a different reality, without mentioning this distinction?

But this creates a difficulty: how can the *Mishnah* switch the ownership arrangements between the first and second clauses without saying so? Rava's resolution is: in truth, *all* the space is his — both the roof and the yard. What the *Mishnah* is describing is a case of *d'oshla makom* — he has granted her the use of whichever space she is currently standing in for the purpose of receiving the *get*. When she is above, he is effectively lending her the rooftop as her domain for the *get*. When she is below, he is lending her the yard.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא לָאו דְּאוֹשְׁלַהּ מָקוֹם – דְּחַד מָקוֹם מוֹשְׁלִי אִינָשֵׁי, תְּרֵי מְקוֹמוֹת לָא מוֹשְׁלִי אִינָשֵׁי?
>
> **Translation:** Rava explains Rav Ḥisda's inference: **Rather, is it not** so **that** the mishna is dealing with a case **where** both the roof and the courtyard belong to him, but **he lent her** a **place** in his domain wherein she could acquire her bill of divorce, and she is standing in that place? In the first clause he lent her the roof, and in the second clause he lent her the courtyard, Consequently, only once it reaches that domain is she divorced. The reason why she does not acquire the bill of divorce until it reaches the specific domain that he lent her is **that people lend** only **one place; people do not lend two places.** Rav Ḥisda understood that one can infer from the mishna that the courtyard and roof remain as separate domains, and concludes that, with regard to bills of divorce, different areas in one domain are considered to be multiple domains.

This reading has a further implication: *chad makom moshli inshi, trei mekomot lo moshli inshi* — people ordinarily lend one space, not two. Therefore, if there are two distinct buildings below, even if the rooftop looks like one unbroken surface above, the husband's permission to use "a space" grants her rights in only one of those spaces. If the *get* lands in the airspace over the other building's section, she has not acquired it, even though from above it appears to be the same continuous roof. This is the derivation of Rav Chisda's principle of *reshuyot chalukot be-gittin*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ: מִידֵי אִירְיָא?! דִּלְמָא הָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ וְהָא כִּדְאִיתַהּ – רֵישָׁא בְּגַג דִּידַהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידֵיהּ, סֵיפָא בְּגַג דִּידֵיהּ וְחָצֵר דִּידַהּ.
>
> **Translation:** Rami bar Ḥama **said to** Rava: **Are the cases comparable? Perhaps this** case is **as it is and that** case is **as it is,** i.e., **the first clause** in the mishna is dealing **with** a case of **her roof and his courtyard,** and **the latter clause** is dealing **with** a case of **his roof and her courtyard.** In some instances, one mishna deals with two different cases. Therefore, the mishna is not an adequate proof for Rav Ḥisda's statement.

Rava's challenger responds: *dilma hachi ve-dilma hachi* — perhaps the two clauses of the *Mishnah* simply describe two different factual scenarios, each with its own ownership configuration, without requiring the harmonized reading. In the *reisha* she owns the roof; in the *seifa* he owns the roof. The *Mishnah* switched cases without announcing it — a reading that, while less elegant, requires no theory of implicit permission-granting. The *Gemara* acknowledges this alternative without definitively rejecting it, leaving Rava's reading as a compelling but not exclusive interpretation.

### Gittin vs. Shabbat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רָבָא: שָׁלֹשׁ מִדּוֹת בְּגִיטִּין; הָא דְּאָמַר רַבִּי: קְלוּטָה כְּמִי שֶׁהוּנְּחָה, וּפְלִיגִי רַבָּנַן עָלֶיהָ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִינְּטוֹרֵי הוּא, וְהָא מִינְּטַר; וְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: נָעַץ קָנֶה בִּרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד וּבְרֹאשׁוֹ טְרַסְקָל, וְזָרַק וְנָח עַל גַּבָּיו – אֲפִילּוּ גָּבוֹהַּ מֵאָה אַמָּה, חַיָּיב, לְפִי שֶׁרְשׁוּת הַיָּחִיד עוֹלָה עַד לָרָקִיעַ – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל הָכָא מִשּׁוּם אִינְּטוֹרֵי הוּא, וְהָא לָא מִינְּטַר;
>
> **Translation:** **Rava says:** There are **three** unique **qualities with regard to bills of divorce,** as compared to the *halakhot* of Shabbat. The first is **that which Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** An object **in airspace is** considered **at rest; and the Rabbis disagree with him. This matter** about which they disagree **applies** only **with regard to Shabbat. But here,** in the case of divorce, **it is because** the bill of divorce **is secured** that she is divorced; **and** once the bill of divorce is within the airspace of the partitions, **it is secured** and the Rabbis agree that she is divorced. **And** the second matter is **that which Rav Ḥisda says** concerning the *halakhot* of Shabbat: In the case of one who **embedded a reed in** the ground of **a private domain, and on its top was a basket [*teraskal*], and he threw** an object from the public domain and **it rested on top of it, even** if the reed was **a hundred cubits high, he is liable because the private domain extends upward until the sky.** Therefore, the object landed in a private domain. **This matter applies** only **with regard to Shabbat,** as with regard to Shabbat domains are defined based on their partitions and the way in which they are used, which differs from the way domains are defined in other halakhic areas. **But here,** in the case of divorce, **it is because** the bill of divorce **is secured** that she is divorced, **and** when it is on the reed **it is not secured.**

The third of Rava's principles addresses the analogue in *Shabbat* law. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: one may not stand on one rooftop and collect rainwater from a neighbor's rooftop — *she-keshem she-diyurin chalukin mi-lematah, kach diyurin chalukin mi-lema'alah* — because just as the inhabitants are separate below, the domains are separate above, even though the two rooftops form one continuous surface. This is a *Shabbat* ruling: carrying from one section of the roof to the other constitutes carrying between separate *reshuyot* and requires an *eiruv*.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא דְּאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: לֹא יַעֲמוֹד אָדָם בְּגַג זֶה וְיִקְלוֹט מֵי גְשָׁמִים מִגַּגּוֹ שֶׁל חֲבֵירוֹ, שֶׁכְּשֵׁם שֶׁדִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין מִלְּמַטָּה, כָּךְ דִּיּוּרִין חֲלוּקִין מִלְּמַעְלָה – הָנֵי מִילֵּי לְעִנְיַן שַׁבָּת, אֲבָל לְעִנְיַן גֵּט – מִשּׁוּם קְפִידָא הוּא, וְכוּלֵּי הַאי לָא קָפְדִי אִינָשֵׁי.
>
> **Translation:** **And** the third matter relates to **that which Rav Yehuda says** that **Shmuel says:** On Shabbat **a person should not stand on this roof and collect rainwater from the roof of another** if there is no joining of courtyards. **As just as the residences are separated below, so too, the residences are separated above.** Each residence has a separate domain, and it is forbidden by rabbinic law to transfer an item from a private domain to another private domain if they are not joined. Similarly, despite the fact that the roofs are not totally separated from one another and nobody lives there, each roof is considered to be its own domain. Rava explains: **This matter applies** only **with regard to Shabbat. But with regard to a bill of divorce,** if it fell onto another roof that is adjacent to the roof that he lent her for the purpose of acquiring the bill of divorce, she is divorced. The reason that a woman is usually not divorced when a bill of divorce falls into a different place, even though that place also belongs to her husband, is **due to** the fact that a husband **is particular** and does not want to lend her more than one place; **but people are not particular to that extent,** i.e., in this case the husband would not be particular about allowing her to temporarily use an adjacent rooftop that also belongs to him.

However, Rava notes: *hani milei l'inyan Shabbat, aval l'inyan get, mishum kepeida hu ve-chulei hai lo kapdi inshi* — this rule applies for *Shabbat*, but for *gittin* the analysis differs. The rationale in *gittin* is about what the husband actually intends when he tells her she may use "a space." When it comes to a rooftop, *ein tashmisham kavu'a* — people do not regularly use rooftops — and the two halves of a flat rooftop are not meaningfully distinct in the way a yard and a room are. A husband who says "you may use this space" for her *get* would not typically distinguish between the two halves of a continuous rooftop. *Lo kapdi inshi* — people are not particular about that. Therefore, the *reshuyot chalukot* principle that prohibits carrying across rooftop sections for *Shabbat* does not disqualify a *get* received on the "wrong" half of a flat rooftop. This is a meaningful divergence between the rules for *gittin* and the rules for *Shabbat*, reflecting the different underlying concerns of each framework.

### Connected Roofs

Rashi spells this out explicitly: even if the husband specifically named one rooftop when granting her permission — *hish'il lah makom be-zeh le-kabel et gittah* — and she received the *get* on the other rooftop, the *Gemara* says *lo amrinan hacha reshuyot chalukot*. This is because *mishum kepeida hu* — it is about what he cares about, and regarding adjacent rooftops that share one flat surface, *ein tashmisham kavu'a ve-gas*, people do not invest particular significance in which half of the roof is being used. The contrast is with the earlier principle that *yesh mash'il chatzeiro u-makpid al beito* — one who lends a yard does not thereby grant use of the inner rooms. There, the distinction is functionally obvious and significant. On a flat rooftop, it is not. Therefore, *lo kapdi inshi* — he would not be *makpid* about which half of his own connected rooftops she used — and a *get* received on either half is valid.

## Yards & Baskets

### Nested Yards

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר אַבָּיֵי: שְׁתֵּי חֲצֵרוֹת זוֹ לִפְנִים מִזּוֹ, פְּנִימִית שֶׁלָּהּ וְחִיצוֹנָה שֶׁלּוֹ, וּמְחִיצוֹת הַחִיצוֹנוֹת עוֹדְפוֹת עַל הַפְּנִימִיּוֹת; וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ; כֵּיוָן שֶׁהִגִּיעַ לַאֲוִיר מְחִיצוֹת הַחִיצוֹנָה – הֲרֵי זוֹ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מַאי טַעְמָא? פְּנִימִית גּוּפַהּ – בִּמְחִיצוֹת הַחִיצוֹנָה קָא מִינַּטְרָה.
>
> **Translation:** § **Abaye says:** If there are **two courtyards** that are configured such that **this** courtyard is **within that** courtyard, and the **inner** courtyard **is hers and** the **outer** courtyard **is his, and** the **partitions** of **the outer** courtyard **extend higher than** the partitions of **the inner** courtyard, **and he threw** a bill of divorce **to her** into her courtyard, **once it reaches the airspace of the partitions of the outer** courtyard, i.e., it reaches the area above the inner courtyard at a height lower than the height of the partitions of the outer courtyard, **she is divorced. What is the reason** for this? The **inner** courtyard **itself is secured by** the **partitions** of **the outer** courtyard. Therefore, the outer partitions service the inner courtyard as well. If the bill of divorce is secured by being encompassed by the outer partitions, it can be viewed as belonging to the inner courtyard once it reaches its airspace.

Abaye presents a case of two concentric yards: *pnimit shelah ve-chitzonah shelo* — the inner yard belongs to her and the outer yard belongs to him. The walls of the outer yard are higher than those of the inner yard, such that the inner yard is fully enclosed within and protected by the outer walls. The husband throws the *get* to her. The ruling: *keivan she-higi'a le-avir mechitzot ha-chitzonah harei zu megureshet* — once the *get* enters the airspace of the outer walls, she is divorced. The rationale is *pnimit gufah be-mechitzot ha-chitzonah kamintara* — the inner yard itself is what is protected by the outer walls. Her property (the inner yard) is implicitly shielded by his enclosure. This is a case where she acquires the *get* while it is still technically over his property (the outer yard), but because her property is the beneficiary of the protection his walls provide, it counts as sufficiently within her domain.

### Nested Baskets

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַה שֶּׁאֵין כֵּן בְּקוּפּוֹת – שְׁתֵּי קוּפּוֹת זוֹ בְּתוֹךְ זוֹ, פְּנִימִית שֶׁלָּהּ וְחִיצוֹנָה שֶׁלּוֹ; וּזְרָקוֹ לָהּ; אֲפִילּוּ הִגִּיעַ לַאֲוִיר פְּנִימִית – אֵינָהּ מְגוֹרֶשֶׁת. מַאי טַעְמָא? דְּהָא לָא נָח.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara comments: **This is not so with regard to baskets.** In a case where there were **two baskets, this** one **within that** one, resting in a domain that does not belong to either of them, and **the inner** basket **is hers and the outer** basket **is his, and he threw** a bill if divorce **to her** into her basket, **even** if the bill of divorce **reached the airspace** of **the inner** basket but was burned or taken before it landed therein, **she is not divorced. What is the reason** for this? It is **because** the bill of divorce did **not** yet **rest** within the basket, and in this case, the walls of the outer basket do not service the inner basket.

The *Gemara* then draws a pointed contrast: *ma she-ein ken be-kupot* — this is not true for baskets. If she has the inner basket and he has the outer (larger) basket, and he throws a *get* into the nested baskets: *afilu higi'a le-avir pnimit einah megureshet* — even if the *get* reaches the airspace of the inner basket, she is not divorced, because it has not yet landed. She must wait until it actually comes to rest.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְכִי נָח מַאי הָוֵי? כִּלְיוֹ שֶׁל לוֹקֵחַ בִּרְשׁוּת מוֹכֵר הוּא! הָכָא בְּמַאי עָסְקִינַן – בְּקוּפָּה שֶׁאֵין לָהּ שׁוּלַיִים.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And when it rests** within the basket, **what of it?** They **are** like the **vessels of a buyer in the domain of the seller,** since her basket is within his basket, which is his domain. She cannot acquire the bill of divorce, despite the fact that it is in her basket, since it is within his domain. The Gemara answers: **With what are we dealing here? With a basket that has no bottom,** and consequently the inner basket is resting on the ground and not inside the outer basket. Therefore, once the bill of divorce lands inside the inner basket, she is indeed divorced.

### Property vs. Vessels

The reason is fundamental: *de-ha lo nach* — it has not come to rest. The concept of acquiring something through airspace is a feature of real property and enclosed domains, not of movable vessels. A basket has no airspace rights. *Kinyan kli* — acquisition through a vessel — requires the object to actually land in the vessel; the airspace above a basket conveys nothing. Yards and property have airspace. Baskets do not.

A further complication arises: *kli she-lokeach be-reshut mocher hu* — if her basket is sitting inside his basket, then her vessel is sitting within his domain, which raises a question about whether it counts as her domain at all. The *Gemara* addresses this by positing that the outer basket has no bottom — *kuppa she-ein lah shulayim* — so that it does not constitute a closed domain in which her basket is "sitting." But as the *Gemara* makes clear, the operative *chiddush* throughout is the first one: even the airspace of her own basket, standing alone, conveys no acquisition. The contrast between *chatzer* and *kli* in this regard is the defining line: property carries airspace rights; movable vessels do not.

## Old Get

### Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ בֵּית שַׁמַּאי אוֹמְרִים: פּוֹטֵר אָדָם אִשְׁתּוֹ בְּגֵט יָשָׁן. וּבֵית הִלֵּל אוֹסְרִין. וְאֵיזֶהוּ גֵּט יָשָׁן – כֹּל שֶׁנִּתְיַיחֵד עִמָּהּ מֵאַחַר שֶׁכְּתָבוֹ לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **Beit Shammai say: A man may send,** i.e., divorce, **his wife with an outdated bill of divorce, and Beit Hillel prohibit** him from doing so. **And what is an outdated bill of divorce? Any** case **where he was secluded with her after he wrote it for her** and before he gave it to her.

The daf concludes with a new *Mishnah* addressing the *get yashan* — an old *get*: *kol she-nityached imah mei-achar she-ketavo lah* — a *get* that was written, not yet given, and during the intervening period the husband was secluded (*yichud*) with the wife.

### Shammai & Hillel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ בְּמַאי קָמִיפַּלְגִי? בֵּית שַׁמַּאי סָבְרִי: לָא אָמְרִינַן גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ גִּיטָּהּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנָהּ; וּבֵית הִלֵּל סָבְרִי: אָמְרִינַן גְּזֵרָה שֶׁמָּא יֹאמְרוּ גִּיטָּהּ קוֹדֵם לִבְנָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara explains: **With regard to what do they disagree? Beit Shammai hold** that **we do not say** that the bill of divorce is not valid due to a rabbinic **decree, lest they say** that receipt of **her bill of divorce precedes** conception of **her son.** If he gives her the bill of divorce long after it was written, she may give birth to children from him in the interim. There is a concern that people will say that she was actually divorced on the date written on the bill of divorce before the children were born, and the children were conceived through licentious sexual intercourse. **And Beit Hillel hold** that **we do say** that this bill of divorce is not valid due to a rabbinic **decree, lest they say** that receipt of **her bill of divorce precedes** conception of **her son.** Consequently, if a woman was secluded with her husband following the writing of the bill of divorce, the bill of divorce is not valid.

*Beit Shammai* permit the husband to divorce his wife with such a *get*: they do not invoke the concern *shema yomru gitah kodem livnah* — that people will say the divorce preceded the child she may have conceived during the *yichud*. *Beit Hillel* forbid using it — *osrim* — out of precisely that concern. If the *get* is eventually used and she later has a child, people looking at the date on the *get* might conclude that she was pregnant after the formal date of the divorce, creating a false impression about the circumstances of the birth.

### Forbidden Not Invalid

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אִם נִישֵּׂאת, לֹא תֵּצֵא.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Abba says** that **Shmuel says:** Even according to Beit Hillel, **if** the woman **was married** on the basis of an outdated bill of divorce given to her by her previous husband, who did not ask advice from the rabbis, **she** need **not leave** her second husband. In such a case, this decree is not severe enough to invalidate the bill of divorce.

Critically, *Beit Hillel* said *osrim* — they forbid it — not *pasul* — they do not invalidate it. This distinction is legally operative: a prohibition against using the *get* is a rabbinic *gezeirah*, but it does not go so far as to invalidate the *get* retroactively if it was used.

### Retroactive Validity

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאִיכָּא דְּאָמְרִי, אָמַר רַבִּי אַבָּא אָמַר שְׁמוּאֵל: אִם נִתְגָּרְשָׁה, תִּינָּשֵׂא לְכַתְּחִלָּה.
>
> **Translation:** **And there are** those **who say** that **Rabbi Abba says** that **Shmuel says: If she was divorced** with an outdated bill of divorce, this woman can **marry** even ***ab initio*** on the basis of this bill of divorce. There is no requirement for her to wait for her first husband to write her a new bill of divorce.

Rabbi Abba in the name of Shmuel rules accordingly: *im nise'it lo tetze* — if she used this *get* and remarried, she need not leave the second marriage. *Be-di'eved* the *get* is entirely valid. A second version of the ruling goes further: *im nitgarshah tinasei le-chatchilah* — once the *get* has been given (even though it should not have been), she may remarry *le-chatchilah*. The rabbinic concern of *Beit Hillel* was never about the validity of the divorce itself, only about the social misperception that might follow. Once the *get* has been given and the divorce has taken effect, she is fully and retroactively divorced, with no restrictions on her remarriage.
