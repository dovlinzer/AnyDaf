# Gittin 79 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Get in Reshus HaRabim

### Gittin Only

The daf opens in the middle of the previous discussion on 78b, concerning the scenario in which a husband throws a *get* in *reshus ha-rabim* and it falls near the woman. The earlier sugya established a debate about what "near her" means: one position holds it must land within her *arba amot*, the classic mode of rabbinic *kinyan*; the other, attributed to Rabbi Yochanan, holds that as long as she is in a position to guard and protect it — even if it is far from her — it is considered under her control. Rabbi Yochanan's reading underscores a fundamental principle: the act of giving a *get* need not conform to the same formal requirements as *kinyan* of an ordinary object. It can be a *netina* without a conventional *kinyan*. At the same time, the *Gemara* closed with a significant practical stringency — endorsed by Tosafot — that in practice the *get* must reach her hand directly, or at minimum land in her yard. None of the more lenient constructions of the *Mishnah* are relied upon *le-halacha*.

Against this backdrop, the *Gemara* on our daf turns to the final clause of the *Mishnah*, which extends the same rule — that throwing works — not only to *gittin* but also to *kiddushin* and repayment of a debt. This raises a sharp question: if Rabbi Yochanan's position is that the *get* works even when it lands far away, provided she can protect it, why would such a non-standard act of transfer function in *kiddushin* or debt repayment, which are governed by ordinary *kinyan* principles?

The *Gemara* opens with Rav Assi citing Rabbi Yochanan: *L'Gittin amru v'lo l'davar acher* — this principle was stated for *gittin* alone and does not apply to anything else. This amounts to reading the *Mishnah* as if its additional cases simply do not mean what they appear to say.

### Kiddushin & Debt

Rabbi Abba immediately challenges Rav Assi: the *Mishnah* explicitly states *v'chein l'inyan kiddushin* — and likewise for *kiddushin*! How can Rav Assi, in the name of Rabbi Yochanan, simply bracket the plain text of the *Mishnah*?

### Yatza V'Hayata

The *Gemara* responds that *kiddushin* is not a true counter-example. The verse in Devarim reads *v'yatza v'hayata* — she leaves one husband and becomes the wife of another — which links *geirushin* and *kiddushin* as conceptually mirrored. If the mechanism works for *gittin*, it works for *kiddushin* as well, because the Torah itself draws this parallel.

This linkage is less surprising once one recalls that *kiddushin* already operates differently from ordinary *kinyan*. *Kiddushei kesef*, for instance, can be effected through *bahahi hana'ah* — the abstract benefit a woman derives from some act done on her behalf — without any concrete object passing into her possession at all. There are even opinions that one can perform *kiddushin* with a stolen object, since the woman acquires certain rights in it even absent formal ownership. Given these anomalies, it is not so strange to say that just as *gittin* can be effected through a protected-but-not-formally-transferred object, *kiddushin* can parallel it.

### Debt Condition

The debt case, however, is different and cannot be resolved so easily. The *Mishnah* says: *Zrok li chovi v'zarko lo, karov l'malveh zachah lo* — if the debtor throws the money and it lands near the creditor, the creditor has acquired it; if it lands nearer to the debtor, the debtor remains liable; and if it falls midway, they split. This appears to be a genuine application of the "proximity-as-acquisition" principle in an ordinary property context, which directly contradicts Rabbi Yochanan's insistence that this mechanism is unique to *gittin*.

The *Gemara* answers: *hacha b'mai askinan d'amar lei zrok li chovi v'tipater* — the *Mishnah* is discussing a case where the creditor specifically stipulated, "Throw it to me and you will be exempt." In other words, it is not that throwing near the creditor is inherently an act of debt repayment; rather, the creditor himself created a special condition under which the throw would discharge the obligation.

The *Gemara* immediately objects: if that is all that is happening, *mai le-memra* — what is the *chiddush*? Of course if the creditor declared that the throw would suffice, it works. The *Gemara* then refines the case further: *d'amar lei zrok li chovi b'toras gittin* — the creditor said, "Throw me my debt in the manner of a *get*," i.e., as if you were giving me a *get*. The *Gemara* again presses: still, what is the novelty? And the final answer is: *mahu d'teima mechi amar lei meshachek ani bach* — one might have thought the debtor could later claim he was joking, that he never seriously meant to frame the payment in those unusual terms. The *Mishnah* therefore teaches that once he made that declaration, he cannot retract it.

This is, as the lecturer notes, a strikingly forced reading of the *Mishnah*. The plain implication of the *Mishnah* is that throwing a debt payment near the creditor functions as a valid act of repayment — yet Rabbi Yochanan, through Rav Assi, refuses to accept this, driven by the conviction that his radical reading of *gittin* (that a *get* can work even without a conventional *kinyan*) is so exceptional that it cannot bleed into normal monetary law. He is therefore willing to impose a nearly impossible reading on the *Mishnah* rather than concede that this mechanism is a general mode of acquisition.

It is worth pausing to note that a debt repayment is arguably not identical to an ordinary *kinyan*. When one sells a basketball, the object was never the buyer's, and acquisition requires a formal *ma'aseh kinyan*. But when repaying a debt, the money in some sense already belongs to the creditor — it is merely being returned. Whether that distinction would affect the formal requirements of repayment is an independent question the *Gemara* does not resolve here, but it sharpens why Rabbi Yochanan's position, however forced as a reading of the *Mishnah*, has internal logic.

### Direct Handover

Rabbi Yochanan's stringency bears revisiting in light of all this. The same Rabbi Yochanan who expands the *get's* validity to cover situations where the woman can merely protect the document — even a hundred *amot* away — also insists, in practice, that the *get* must reach her hand directly. Whatever theoretical latitude the *Mishnah* provides, *le-ma'aseh* Rabbi Yochanan does not rely on it, and a woman who received a *get* by any of these indirect means would be in a state of doubt. This is a significant practical *chumra*, one that transforms what might seem like a liberalization of the *Mishnah* into a stringency that renders her status as *megoreshet* uncertain unless the *get* came directly to her.

## Physical Control

### Get & Strings

Rav Chisda addresses a case of a *get* given with strings still attached: *get beyada u-meshicha beyado* — the *get* is in her hand, but the husband is holding a string connected to it. The rule is: *im yachol lenatko u-lehavyo etzlo einah megureshet* — if he can yank the string and retrieve the *get*, she is not divorced. If the string is effectively slack — if he cannot use it to reclaim the document — she is divorced. The reasoning is *ba'inan kerisus* — the Torah requires a complete severance between husband and wife, and if he retains the ability to reclaim the document, that severance has not been achieved. This is, of course, entirely literal, but it lends itself naturally to the metaphorical expression of a divorce "with strings attached."

### Sloped Hands

Rav Yehuda addresses another scenario: *haysah yada asuya ke-tafres* — her hand was positioned like a slope, angled downward. The husband threw the *get* into her sloped hand, and despite physically reaching her hand, it immediately slid off. The ruling is *af al pi she-higi'a le-yada einah megureshet* — even though it reached her hand, she is not divorced.

### Did Not Settle

The *Gemara* asks: even if we accept that the *get* slipping from her hand means she did not acquire it *via* her hand, why does it not take effect by virtue of landing within her *arba amot* once it hits the ground? The *Gemara* answers: *delo nach* — it did not come to rest. According to Rashi, before the *get* reached the ground it was blown away, or perhaps burnt, and never actually settled within her *arba amot*.

A further question arises: even if it did not come to rest, should she not acquire it *me-avira de-arba amot* — from the airspace of her *arba amot*? This raises an open question: when the Rabbis instituted *kinyan arba amot* in *reshus ha-rabim*, did that *kinyan* include the airspace above those four *amot*, or only the ground level?

The *Gemara* notes that this question — *yesh lahen avir ein lahen avir* — was itself posed by Rabbi Elazar and left unresolved. One might think the sloped-hands case resolves it: since the *get* in the airspace did not effect a divorce, perhaps *arba amot* has no airspace rights. But the *Gemara* deflects this inference: the case involves a woman standing at the edge of a river, so that when the *get* slid off her hands, it immediately passed over the airspace above the river. Since the riverbed is not a surface on which something can come to rest, the airspace above it does not belong to her *arba amot* — *mi-ikara le-ibud ka'ei*, it was headed for destruction from the outset.

### Airspace Rights

The broader principle — that airspace above a space where objects cannot come to rest does not constitute protected domain — carries implications for the subsequent discussion of rooftops and enclosed areas. The question of whether *arba amot* includes airspace thus remains formally unresolved, but the practical direction of the sugya leans toward excluding it, with the rooftop cases relying instead on property-based airspace rather than the rabbinic *kinyan* of *arba amot*.

## Rooftop Scenarios

### Woman Above

The next *Mishnah* (79a) opens with two scenarios involving different vertical positions. In the first: *haisah omedes al rosh ha-gag* — the woman is standing on the rooftop and the man is at ground level; he throws the *get* up to her. The rule is: *keivan she-higi'a le-avir ha-gag harei zu megureshet* — once the *get* reaches the airspace of the rooftop, she is divorced, even before it lands. This introduces airspace of one's own property as a mechanism distinct from the *arba amot* discussed earlier. One's property certainly carries airspace rights, and once the *get* enters that airspace, if under normal circumstances it would have come to rest, she is already divorced — even if something intervenes (a wind, a fire) and it never actually lands.

### Unprotected Airspace

The second scenario reverses the positions: *hu mi-lema'ala ve-hi mi-lematah* — he is on the rooftop and she is below in the yard; he throws it down to her. The *Mishnah* states that once the *get* leaves the domain of the rooftop — *keivan she-yatza me-reshus ha-gag* — and enters the airspace of the yard below, even if it is subsequently erased or burnt in mid-air, she is divorced.

The *Gemara* immediately raises the obvious difficulty: *lo mintar* — the rooftop has no fence. It is a flat roof, open to wind and other elements. What makes the airspace above it or the airspace below it "protected"? The whole theory of airspace-as-acquisition rests on the concept of *chatzer ha-mishtameres* — a guarded domain that keeps things within it. An open rooftop, or the open air above a yard with no enclosing walls sufficiently high, is not inherently protective in this sense.

### Fenced Airspace

Rav Papa resolves this: *begag she-yesh lo ma'akeh askinan* — we are dealing with a rooftop that has a fence around it. Once there is a fence, the *get* must enter the space *within* the fence — not merely above the level of the fence — and then it is genuinely contained and protected. The *Mishnah*'s statement that she is divorced once it enters the airspace of the rooftop is predicated on the existence of such an enclosure.

The same principle applies to the second case, where the woman is below. The airspace through which the *get* travels after leaving the rooftop must be enclosed and protected. The *Gemara* cites Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel: *kegon she-hayu mechitzot ha-tachtonot odofot al ha-elyonot* — the walls of the lower yard are higher than the walls of the rooftop building. If one imagines a building standing in the middle of a large courtyard whose surrounding walls are taller than the rooftop, then the moment the *get* leaves the rooftop it is already within the enclosed, protected airspace of the courtyard below. This ruling is stated in parallel by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya, and by Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan.

Rav Papa then asks Abaye: *ke-man ke-Rabbi?* — is this position consistent only with the view of *Rebbi*, who holds *kluta ke-mi she-hunchah damya* — that an object absorbed into a domain's airspace is legally equivalent to having landed there? And this would mean that only according to *Rebbi* does the *get* take effect the moment it enters the protected airspace, even before it comes to rest.

Abaye responds: *afilu tima rabbanan* — even the *Rabanan* who dispute *Rebbi* on this point do so only for *Shabbat* purposes. The *Rabanan* hold that for *Shabbat*, being in the airspace of *reshut ha-yachid* is not equivalent to resting there — so one who throws an object into the airspace of a private domain but does not land it has not violated *Shabbat* according to the *Rabanan*. But here the concern is not about *Shabbat* at all. The question for *gittin* is whether the object is *mintar* — protected — and once it is in the enclosed protected airspace, *ve-ha kamintar*, it is protected. The two frameworks operate by entirely different criteria.

### Three Tefachim Rule

Ulla bar Manashya, in the name of Avimi, offers a further approach: even without a fence, if the *get* is within three *tefachim* of the rooftop surface, it works — *d'chol pachot mi-shlosha samuch le-gag ke-gag dami* — because within three *tefachim* of the ground (or of a surface), halachic law treats the object as if it were already resting on that surface. This principle, familiar from other areas, means that for the first case — the woman above — even without an enclosure, if the *get* clears the lip of the roof and is within three *tefachim* of the roof surface, it is as though it has landed and she is divorced.

### Man Above

In the second case — the man above, woman below — the *Gemara* applies the same framework of higher surrounding walls. This is described by Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel, and repeated by Rabbi Elazar in the name of Rabbi Oshaya and by Rabbi Ami in the name of Rabbi Yochanan: *kegon she-hayu mechitzot ha-tachtonot odofot al ha-elyonot*.

Rava articulates three distinct principles that emerge from comparing *gittin* to *Shabbat* law in this area. First, *Rebbi's* ruling that *kluta ke-mi she-hunchah damya* — an object is treated as having landed once it enters a domain's airspace — is disputed by the *Rabanan* for *Shabbat* purposes, but applies for *gittin* because in *gittin* the criterion is protection, not rest. Second, Rav Chisda had earlier ruled that placing an object atop a hundred-*amah* pole in *reshut ha-yachid* renders one liable for *Shabbat* — *reshut ha-yachid olah ad la-rakia*, private domain extends upward to the heavens — so even at that height, if the object has landed, one is liable. But that ruling is *hani milei l'inyan Shabbat*; by *gittin*, *mishum neturei hu*, the *get* must be in a protected space, and an object balanced on the top of a hundred-*amah* pole is not protected from forces that could dislodge it, regardless of its technical presence within *reshut ha-yachid*. These two cases are mirror images of one another: the first involves an object that is protected but has not rested; the second involves an object that has rested but is not protected. For *Shabbat*, the object in the first case does not create liability (lacking rest), while the object in the second does (having rested within *reshut ha-yachid* regardless of protection). For *gittin*, precisely the opposite: the first case works (protected space suffices), and the second does not (lack of protection disqualifies it).

### Trajectory

Rav Nachman in the name of Rabbah bar Avuha rules that the case of *nimchak* — the *get* being erased in mid-air — depends critically on the direction of travel at the moment of erasure. *Lo shanu ela she-nimchak derech yerida* — the ruling that she is divorced applies only when the *get* was erased on its downward trajectory. *Aval nimchak derech aliyah lo* — if it was erased while still going upward, she is not divorced, *me-ikara lo le-minach ka'ei* — because at that moment it was not yet headed toward coming to rest in the yard. An object on an upward trajectory, even if gravity will eventually bring it down, is directionally oriented away from the receiving domain and cannot be considered as entering that domain's space at the moment of erasure. Only when the object is descending is it definitively in the process of landing within the protected space.

### Burning Get

Similarly, the case of *nisraf* — the *get* catching fire — depends on timing relative to when the fire began. If the *get* was thrown before the fire broke out, and the fire then destroys it in mid-air, she is divorced. But if there was already a fire burning in the yard when the husband threw the *get*, she is not divorced: *me-ikara le-sreifah ka-azil* — from the outset it was thrown into a fire, not into a protected domain. If a wind subsequently blew the *get* into a fire that was not in its path when thrown, that would presumably be treated as *kadam get le-dleikah* — the *get* preceded the fire — and she would be divorced, since the throw itself had nothing to do with the conflagration.

## Separate Domains

### Different Buildings

Rav Chisda introduces the principle of *reshuyot chalukot be-gittin* — separate domains in the context of *gittin*. The case involves a structure or courtyard above which the rooftop appears as a single continuous surface, but below which different sections are owned by different people. Rav Chisda's ruling is that the separate ownership below creates separate domains above as well, such that even if on the roof it all looks like one flat space, the airspace over one building's section is distinct from the airspace over another's.

### Source Question

Rami bar Chama asks Rava: *mina lei le-saba ha?* — from where does Rav Chisda derive this principle? Rava responds: *masnitin hi* — it comes from the *Mishnah* itself.

### One Space

The *Gemara* works through the logic as follows. The *Mishnah* presents two scenarios: (1) she is on the rooftop and he throws from below — once it reaches the airspace of the roof she is divorced; (2) he is on the rooftop and she is below — once the *get* leaves the domain of the roof she is divorced. In both cases, the *Mishnah* simply describes where each party is standing without specifying who owns which space.

Now, who owns what? If she owns both the rooftop and the yard below, there is no *chiddush* in the airspace of the rooftop — the entire space is hers and of course she acquires whatever enters it. Conversely, if he owns both rooftop and yard, neither scenario makes sense, because neither the airspace of the roof nor the airspace of the yard is hers. The *Gemara* therefore says: in the first scenario (*reisha*), she must own the roof and he owns the yard; in the second (*seifa*), he owns the roof and she owns the yard.

But this creates a difficulty: how can the *Mishnah* switch the ownership arrangements between the first and second clauses without saying so? Rava's resolution is: in truth, *all* the space is his — both the roof and the yard. What the *Mishnah* is describing is a case of *d'oshla makom* — he has granted her the use of whichever space she is currently standing in for the purpose of receiving the *get*. When she is above, he is effectively lending her the rooftop as her domain for the *get*. When she is below, he is lending her the yard.

This reading has a further implication: *chad makom moshli inshi, trei mekomot lo moshli inshi* — people ordinarily lend one space, not two. Therefore, if there are two distinct buildings below, even if the rooftop looks like one unbroken surface above, the husband's permission to use "a space" grants her rights in only one of those spaces. If the *get* lands in the airspace over the other building's section, she has not acquired it, even though from above it appears to be the same continuous roof. This is the derivation of Rav Chisda's principle of *reshuyot chalukot be-gittin*.

Rava's challenger responds: *dilma hachi ve-dilma hachi* — perhaps the two clauses of the *Mishnah* simply describe two different factual scenarios, each with its own ownership configuration, without requiring the harmonized reading. In the *reisha* she owns the roof; in the *seifa* he owns the roof. The *Mishnah* switched cases without announcing it — a reading that, while less elegant, requires no theory of implicit permission-granting. The *Gemara* acknowledges this alternative without definitively rejecting it, leaving Rava's reading as a compelling but not exclusive interpretation.

### Gittin vs. Shabbat

The third of Rava's principles addresses the analogue in *Shabbat* law. Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel rules: one may not stand on one rooftop and collect rainwater from a neighbor's rooftop — *she-keshem she-diyurin chalukin mi-lematah, kach diyurin chalukin mi-lema'alah* — because just as the inhabitants are separate below, the domains are separate above, even though the two rooftops form one continuous surface. This is a *Shabbat* ruling: carrying from one section of the roof to the other constitutes carrying between separate *reshuyot* and requires an *eiruv*.

However, Rava notes: *hani milei l'inyan Shabbat, aval l'inyan get, mishum kepeida hu ve-chulei hai lo kapdi inshi* — this rule applies for *Shabbat*, but for *gittin* the analysis differs. The rationale in *gittin* is about what the husband actually intends when he tells her she may use "a space." When it comes to a rooftop, *ein tashmisham kavu'a* — people do not regularly use rooftops — and the two halves of a flat rooftop are not meaningfully distinct in the way a yard and a room are. A husband who says "you may use this space" for her *get* would not typically distinguish between the two halves of a continuous rooftop. *Lo kapdi inshi* — people are not particular about that. Therefore, the *reshuyot chalukot* principle that prohibits carrying across rooftop sections for *Shabbat* does not disqualify a *get* received on the "wrong" half of a flat rooftop. This is a meaningful divergence between the rules for *gittin* and the rules for *Shabbat*, reflecting the different underlying concerns of each framework.

### Connected Roofs

Rashi spells this out explicitly: even if the husband specifically named one rooftop when granting her permission — *hish'il lah makom be-zeh le-kabel et gittah* — and she received the *get* on the other rooftop, the *Gemara* says *lo amrinan hacha reshuyot chalukot*. This is because *mishum kepeida hu* — it is about what he cares about, and regarding adjacent rooftops that share one flat surface, *ein tashmisham kavu'a ve-gas*, people do not invest particular significance in which half of the roof is being used. The contrast is with the earlier principle that *yesh mash'il chatzeiro u-makpid al beito* — one who lends a yard does not thereby grant use of the inner rooms. There, the distinction is functionally obvious and significant. On a flat rooftop, it is not. Therefore, *lo kapdi inshi* — he would not be *makpid* about which half of his own connected rooftops she used — and a *get* received on either half is valid.

## Yards & Baskets

### Nested Yards

Abaye presents a case of two concentric yards: *pnimit shelah ve-chitzonah shelo* — the inner yard belongs to her and the outer yard belongs to him. The walls of the outer yard are higher than those of the inner yard, such that the inner yard is fully enclosed within and protected by the outer walls. The husband throws the *get* to her. The ruling: *keivan she-higi'a le-avir mechitzot ha-chitzonah harei zu megureshet* — once the *get* enters the airspace of the outer walls, she is divorced. The rationale is *pnimit gufah be-mechitzot ha-chitzonah kamintara* — the inner yard itself is what is protected by the outer walls. Her property (the inner yard) is implicitly shielded by his enclosure. This is a case where she acquires the *get* while it is still technically over his property (the outer yard), but because her property is the beneficiary of the protection his walls provide, it counts as sufficiently within her domain.

### Nested Baskets

The *Gemara* then draws a pointed contrast: *ma she-ein ken be-kupot* — this is not true for baskets. If she has the inner basket and he has the outer (larger) basket, and he throws a *get* into the nested baskets: *afilu higi'a le-avir pnimit einah megureshet* — even if the *get* reaches the airspace of the inner basket, she is not divorced, because it has not yet landed. She must wait until it actually comes to rest.

### Property vs. Vessels

The reason is fundamental: *de-ha lo nach* — it has not come to rest. The concept of acquiring something through airspace is a feature of real property and enclosed domains, not of movable vessels. A basket has no airspace rights. *Kinyan kli* — acquisition through a vessel — requires the object to actually land in the vessel; the airspace above a basket conveys nothing. Yards and property have airspace. Baskets do not.

A further complication arises: *kli she-lokeach be-reshut mocher hu* — if her basket is sitting inside his basket, then her vessel is sitting within his domain, which raises a question about whether it counts as her domain at all. The *Gemara* addresses this by positing that the outer basket has no bottom — *kuppa she-ein lah shulayim* — so that it does not constitute a closed domain in which her basket is "sitting." But as the *Gemara* makes clear, the operative *chiddush* throughout is the first one: even the airspace of her own basket, standing alone, conveys no acquisition. The contrast between *chatzer* and *kli* in this regard is the defining line: property carries airspace rights; movable vessels do not.

## Old Get

### Definition

The daf concludes with a new *Mishnah* addressing the *get yashan* — an old *get*: *kol she-nityached imah mei-achar she-ketavo lah* — a *get* that was written, not yet given, and during the intervening period the husband was secluded (*yichud*) with the wife.

### Shammai & Hillel

*Beit Shammai* permit the husband to divorce his wife with such a *get*: they do not invoke the concern *shema yomru gitah kodem livnah* — that people will say the divorce preceded the child she may have conceived during the *yichud*. *Beit Hillel* forbid using it — *osrim* — out of precisely that concern. If the *get* is eventually used and she later has a child, people looking at the date on the *get* might conclude that she was pregnant after the formal date of the divorce, creating a false impression about the circumstances of the birth.

### Forbidden Not Invalid

Critically, *Beit Hillel* said *osrim* — they forbid it — not *pasul* — they do not invalidate it. This distinction is legally operative: a prohibition against using the *get* is a rabbinic *gezeirah*, but it does not go so far as to invalidate the *get* retroactively if it was used.

### Retroactive Validity

Rabbi Abba in the name of Shmuel rules accordingly: *im nise'it lo tetze* — if she used this *get* and remarried, she need not leave the second marriage. *Be-di'eved* the *get* is entirely valid. A second version of the ruling goes further: *im nitgarshah tinasei le-chatchilah* — once the *get* has been given (even though it should not have been), she may remarry *le-chatchilah*. The rabbinic concern of *Beit Hillel* was never about the validity of the divorce itself, only about the social misperception that might follow. Once the *get* has been given and the divorce has taken effect, she is fully and retroactively divorced, with no restrictions on her remarriage.
