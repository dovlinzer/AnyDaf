# Chagigah 10 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Heter Nedarim

### Flying in Air

The tenth *daf* of Masechet Chagigah opens with a *mishnah* that is famous in its own right, yet only tangentially connected to the tractate's core subject matter. The *mishnah* will carry us into a broad and far-reaching discussion that occupies both this *perek* and the next. It begins with a striking declaration: *heter nedarim porchin ba'avir* — the annulment of vows flies in the air. The Torah does provide a mechanism for a husband or father to annul a woman's vow, but the institution the *mishnah* is describing is quite different: the practice whereby any person who has made a vow may present himself before a sage, explain that had he fully appreciated the circumstances he never would have made the vow, and thereby be released from his obligation. This institution, the *mishnah* declares, has nothing to rest upon — *ve'ein lahem mah sheyismachu*. There is no verse, no real anchor in the Torah text. To keep the *mishnah*'s own metaphor, *semicha* means physical support, something solid beneath you. *Heter nedarim* is simply airborne, with nothing to ground it.

### Three Categories

The *mishnah* then enumerates three further areas of law: *hilchos Shabbos*, *chagigot* — the holiday sacrifices — and *me'ilot*, the laws governing misuse of consecrated property. These three, the *mishnah* says, are *kehararin hatluyim bese'ara* — like mountains suspended from a hair. Unlike *heter nedarim*, which has no verse at all, these three areas do have some scriptural basis, but the ratio is wildly inverted: the textual support is vanishingly thin — a single hair — while the halachic superstructure built upon it is vast, a mountain. The *mishnah*'s inclusion of *chagigah* alongside *Shabbos* and *me'ilah* is, incidentally, what occasions its placement in this tractate.

It is worth pausing on the humor hidden in the comparison. That *hilchos Shabbos* involves *halachot merubot* — an enormous body of law — is self-evident. But how many laws does *chagigah* really have? The tractate itself has not generated that many. And *me'ilah*, by contrast, is genuinely enormous and its laws genuinely intricate.

The *mishnah* rounds out its taxonomy with a third category: civil law, Temple service, laws of ritual purity and impurity, and forbidden sexual relationships — these have solid scriptural support, *yesh lahem mah sheyismachu*. They are well-grounded.

### Hein Vehen Gufei Torah

The *mishnah* closes with the declaration *vehen hen gufei Torah* — and they are the body of Torah. As the text stands, this seems to refer only to the last, well-supported category, implying that the earlier categories are somehow lesser. The *Gemara* therefore emends the reading to *hen vehen gufei Torah* — these and those, meaning all three categories alike, whether the area floating without any scriptural support, the mountain on a hair, or the domain resting on firm biblical ground, are all equally Torah. The degree of textual support does not diminish or elevate the binding force or centrality of the law. That is the *mishnah*'s fundamental claim.

## Deriving Heter Nedarim

### R. Eliezer: Yafla

The *Gemara* now records a *baraita* in which five tannaitic figures resist the *mishnah*'s stark claim that *heter nedarim* has no scriptural basis, each offering a verse that provides at least some thin support. Rabbi Eliezer says: there is indeed something to rely on. The word *ki yafli* — to enunciate clearly, to make an unambiguous verbal declaration — appears twice in the Torah, once in the context of *nezirut* (*lidor neder nazir*) and once in the context of *nedarim* generally (*neder be'erkecha*). Why twice? *Achat hafla'ah al isur ve'achat hafla'ah al heter* — one enunciation creates the prohibition, the other hints at the possibility of an enunciation that dissolves it. That is the source for *heter nedarim*. Candidly, this is extremely weak. One might fairly ask: is this a hair's breadth, or is it truly nothing? Rabbi Eliezer evidently considered it a hair. The *Gemara* will return to assess the strength of each derivation.

### R. Yehoshua: Oath

Rabbi Yehoshua offers a different source. The verse in *Tehillim* reads: *asher nishbati be'api* — "I swore in my anger" — followed by *im yevo'un el menuchati* — "they shall not enter my resting place." The implication Rabbi Yehoshua draws is that an oath taken in anger, not in a settled state of mind, carries within it the seeds of its own retraction, *v'hazarti bi*. From this he derives the principle that vows made under conditions the vow-taker might not have fully endorsed can be revisited and annulled.

The *Tosafos* grapples seriously with this derivation. The verse says "they shall not enter my resting place," and in fact the generation of the desert did not enter *Eretz Yisrael*. Perhaps *menuchah* refers not to the Land but to *olam haba*, opening the broader question of whether *dor hamidbar* have a portion in the world to come. The *Tosafos* addresses this, but the underlying tension remains: if anything, the verse might suggest the opposite of what Rabbi Yehoshua reads into it. Even if a vow is taken in anger, one is bound by it — the vow was made, its consequence was real. The debate about whether Rabbi Yehoshua's verse actually supports *heter nedarim* or quietly undermines it is precisely why the *Gemara* will ultimately set it aside.

### R. Yitzchak: Generous

Rabbi Yitzchak derives *heter nedarim* from the verse *kol nediv libo* — whoever is generous of heart shall bring his vow to the Temple. The implication is that the generosity of heart — the inner alignment of the person with his commitment — is a condition for the vow's continuing force. If that alignment is broken, there may be grounds for release. As with the earlier derivations, this is quite thin. The verse says nothing explicit about annulment; it is at most a hint that the vow depends on the ongoing will of the one who made it.

### Chananya: Two Parts

Chananya ben achi Rabbi Yehoshua — Rabbi Yehoshua's nephew — cites the verse *nishbati va'akayeimah lishmor mishpatei tzidkecha*: "I have sworn and I will perform, to observe your righteous statutes." He notes the two-part structure: *nishbati* alone, and then separately *va'akayeimah*, "and I will fulfill it." The fact that after swearing there is still a further affirmation of fulfillment hints, he argues, at the logical space *nishbati velo akayeimah* — I may have sworn and yet perhaps not fulfill it. That gap is the opening for *heter nedarim*.

The *Gemara* notes, however, that the same verse is cited by Rav Gidal in the name of Rav to teach something entirely different: *minayin shenishba'im lekayem et hamitzvot* — how do we know that one may take an oath to fulfill a *mitzvah*? One might think such an oath is meaningless because one is already obligated, or that it is a kind of forbidden oath. The verse *nishbati va'akayeimah lishmor mishpatei tzidkecha* shows it is not only permitted but meritorious — it is a way of motivating and urging oneself to act. According to *Rashi*, *lishmor mishpatei tzidkecha* means the person who vows to fulfill a *mitzvah* should thereby spur himself to do it promptly. If that is what the verse is teaching, it says nothing about *heter nedarim*.

Similarly, the *Gemara* notes that Shmuel derives from the phrase *nediv lev* that on occasion even a vow formed silently in the heart, *gamar belibo*, without verbal enunciation, *bisfatav*, may carry legal weight in certain limited circumstances — though this is the minority position. If that is what Rabbi Yitzchak's verse is about, it too says nothing about annulment.

### Shmuel: Others Can

The most celebrated derivation is that of Rav Yehuda in the name of Shmuel. Had he been present among the *Tannaim*, he says — *i havah hasam, amina lehu didi adifa midid'chu* — he would have offered something better than all of them. The verse is the well-known *lo yachel devaro* — "he shall not profane his word." Shmuel reads this with grammatical precision: *hu eino mochel* — he cannot waive his own obligation, he cannot release himself from the debt his words created. *Aval acherim mochalin lo* — but others can release him from that obligation. The sage who annuls the vow is not the vow-maker; he is one of those "others" who has the capacity to *meichel*. Therefore, the Torah itself, by the word *lo yachel devaro*, hints at a mechanism by which a third party may dissolve the vow.

## Shmuel Best Answer

### Pilpelta Hacharipta

Rava, and according to some the attribution is Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak, endorses Shmuel's derivation with a proverb that has become famous in its own right: *tava chada pilpelta charifta mimalei tzanna de-kari* — one sharp peppercorn is better than a basket of bland gourds. One tight, incisive answer outweighs a collection of weak ones. The *Gemara* explicitly rules: *vechulhu it behu pircha levar midishmuel* — all the other derivations are subject to challenge, *deleit lei pircha* — Shmuel's alone is not. Interestingly, this same phrase — Shmuel declaring that had he been there he would have given a better answer, followed by Rava's endorsement with the peppercorn proverb — appears in three separate places in the *Gemara*. One is here, regarding *heter nedarim*. Another is on the question of permitting life-saving *chilul Shabbos*, where Shmuel's formulation *vechai bahem velo sheyamut bahem* is the answer we always cite. The third is in Masechet Megillah, regarding the scriptural basis for the Megillah's divine inspiration, where Shmuel's derivation from *kiymu vekiblu hayehudim* — *kiymu lemata mah shekiblu lemala* — is perhaps less self-evidently superior to the alternatives, but Rava endorses it nonetheless.

Why is Shmuel's answer the sharpest? The challenges leveled against the others are straightforward. Rabbi Eliezer's use of *yafla* appearing twice may well be teaching, as Rabbi Yehuda in the name of Rabbi Tarfon rules, that *nezirut ela lehafla'ah* — *nezirut* requires complete unambiguity at the moment of utterance. In the classic case where Reuven says "if that approaching man is a *nazir*, then I am a *nazir*," and Shimon says "if that man is not a *nazir*, then I am a *nazir*" — according to Rabbi Yehuda citing Rabbi Tarfon, neither of them is ever a *nazir*, because at the moment of enunciation it was not objectively clear which declaration was operative. The word *ki yafli* thus teaches clarity of effect at the moment of utterance, not the possibility of annulment. Rabbi Yehoshua's verse may mean the opposite: *be'api nishbati velo hadarna bi* — I swore in my anger and I did not retract. Chananya's verse, *nishbati va'akayeimah*, may simply be teaching Rav Gidal's principle that one may vow to fulfill a *mitzvah*. Shmuel's derivation from *lo yachel devaro*, by contrast, yields a clean and direct reading with no natural alternative interpretation.

### Rabbinic Power

A deeper question lurks beneath the surface of this entire discussion. Normally, laws that are fully binding yet have no scriptural basis fall into the category of *halacha lemoshe misinai*. But *halacha lemoshe misinai* typically covers technical, standalone details — matters that do not overtly conflict with explicit biblical law. *Heter nedarim* is categorically different. The Torah says repeatedly, in the clearest possible terms, that a person who makes a vow is bound by it. The entire thrust of the relevant passages is that there is no escape. And yet the Rabbis assert an institution that releases a person from that obligation entirely, without any vow-specific scriptural basis. That is not a technical detail filling in a gap; it is a direct override of what the text appears to say.

Moreover, the practical ramifications are genuinely unsettling. The release of vows is not managed by a centralized authority with established case law and accountability. Three laypeople suffice. One can shop around for a favorable *chacham*. The institutional checks that might lend the system integrity are largely absent. If someone has pledged a substantial sum — to the Temple, to charity, as part of a business undertaking — the existence of this institution means that pledge may be dissolved by a quorum of three friends. The corrosive effect on the enforceability of oaths and vows in social life is real.

### Torah SheBa'al Peh

What the *mishnah*'s closing line *hen vehen gufei Torah* is ultimately asserting is the equal authority of *Torah she-be'al peh* regardless of how thin its connection to any specific verse. And in a way, *heter nedarim* serves as a paradigmatic model of exactly this dynamic. A person has an existing obligation — a vow, binding and explicit. A *chacham*, exercising his authority, comes along and dissolves it. The Torah's text created the bond; the rabbinic institution, grounded in no explicit text, undoes it. Is this not a compressed image of *Torah she-be'al peh* itself in its relationship to *Torah she-bichtav*? The written text creates obligations; the oral tradition comes and, through its authoritative interpretation, reconfigures them — sometimes amplifying, sometimes limiting, sometimes, as here, releasing entirely.

There is a further parallel within the mechanics of *heter nedarim* itself. When a *chacham* seeks to annul a vow, the method is to probe for some small opening — had you realized this circumstance, would you still have made this vow? One keeps searching until a tiny basis is found, a thread, and through that thread the obligation is unwound. Is this not exactly what the *Tannaim* are doing when they search the *Torah* for a verse to legitimate the institution? They are looking for the hair, the tiny opening that allows the whole mountain to hang. The method of the legal institution and the method of its own legitimation mirror each other precisely.

## Hilchos Shabbos

### Multiple Verses

The *Gemara* now turns to *hilchos Shabbos*, which the *mishnah* describes as a mountain on a hair. The *Gemara* objects: *michdei kesiva* — but the Torah writes it explicitly, many times over. The prohibition of *melacha* on *Shabbos* is stated and restated throughout the Torah. How can this be called a hair?

The honest answer is that the objection, while formally apt, somewhat misses the point. Yes, the Torah says *lo taasaseh melacha* many times, but it says the same thing each time. What it does not say is what those thirty-nine *melachos* are, what the *toldos* of each are, the enormous edifice of detail that constitutes Masechet Shabbos. At most, one has *lo seva'aru eish bechol moshvoseichem*, a specific prohibition of fire; the case of the *mekoshesh eitzim*; and perhaps *becharish uvakatzir tishbos*, which, if referring to *Shabbos*, provides a couple of concrete examples. That is a few hairs, perhaps, but not a foundation for the mountain.

### Chofer Guma

The *Gemara* answers by focusing on a specific law: the case of Rabbi Abba, *hachofer guma* — one who digs a hole on *Shabbos* but *ein tzarich ela le'afrah*, needs only the dirt, not the hole itself. In such a case the digger is *patur*, exempt. The question the *Gemara* then raises is: according to whom? The *melacha* of digging, understood as a form of *boneh* — constructing a hole as a usable structure — would normally be a violation. If the person is digging for the dirt and not for the purpose of creating a hole, this resembles *melacha she'eina tzericha legufa*, a *melacha* done for a purpose other than its own intrinsic function, which Rabbi Shimon exempts but Rabbi Yehudah holds liable. If this is a uniquely Shimonite ruling, it does not represent the consensus, and it cannot ground the *mishnah*'s general claim.

The *Gemara* resolves this: *afilu teima Rabbi Yehudah* — even according to Rabbi Yehudah the digger is exempt in this case. *Hasam metaken hacha mekalkel* — where Rabbi Yehudah holds one liable despite wrong intent, the result is constructive; here the result is *mekalkel*, destructive or at least unwanted. The person not only does not need the hole; having an unintended pit in one's yard makes things worse. Even Rabbi Yehudah would not classify an action whose result is an unwanted degradation as a full-fledged *melacha*.

### Melechet Machsheves

But why is this exemption a "mountain on a hair"? The *Gemara* answers: *meleches machsheves asrah Torah* — the Torah prohibited *melechet machsheves*, a purposive, intentional act directed toward its own intended outcome. And the problem is that *meleches machsheves lo kesiva* by *Shabbos* — this concept does not appear in the *Shabbos* passages themselves. It is derived from the *Mishkan*: the construction of the Tabernacle, with which *Shabbos* is repeatedly juxtaposed in the Torah, was built through *melechet machsheves*, deliberate and purposive craft. By the association of *Shabbos* to the *Mishkan*, we apply the same conceptual framework to *Shabbos* violations. And from this derived category flows an enormous range of leniencies. A large proportion of the practical *kulos* one encounters in *hilchos Shabbos* — *eino miskavein*, *pesik reisha*, *melacha she'eina tzericha legufa* according to some — trace back ultimately to this concept of intentionality and the requirement that the act and its desired outcome be aligned.

### Unsatisfactory Answer

This answer is, to be frank, unsatisfying. The truly enormous mountain that *hilchos Shabbos* rests upon is not the category of *melechet machsheves* but the thirty-nine *melachos* themselves, with all their *toldos* and ramifications, none of which are spelled out in any biblical verse. The Torah says *lo taasaseh melacha*; the identification of what constitutes *melacha* — fire, plowing, reaping, writing, the entire list — is derived from the activities of the *Mishkan*, but that too is not explicit in any verse. That is the real mountain. By comparison, the category of *melechet machsheves* and the exemption for the person who digs for dirt rather than for a hole — while not *pashut* — is not so startling a *chiddush* that it requires a special accounting. The intuition that an act is only a *melacha* when performed in sync with its intended purpose is not counterintuitive; it follows naturally from almost any reasonable understanding of culpability. The *Gemara*'s question and its answer, in this section, are genuinely puzzling. Why focus on *melechet machsheves* as the hair on which the mountain hangs, rather than on the identification of the *melachos* themselves?

## Chagigah Definition

### Vechagstem Oso

The *Gemara* turns to *chagigah*. Again: *michtav kesiva* — the Torah writes it explicitly. The verse *vechagosem oso chag laAdoshem shivat yamim* — you shall celebrate it as a festival to God for seven days — appears to mandate a *chagigah* sacrifice. So why is this a mountain on a hair?

The challenge is introduced through a question Rav Papa poses to Abaye: *mimai dehai vechagosem oso chag l'Adoshem zvichah* — how do you know that "celebrate a festival to God" means slaughtering sacrifices? Perhaps *choga chaga kamar Rachmana* — perhaps the Torah simply means: have a holiday, celebrate, rejoice. There was much rejoicing.

The *Gemara* initially counters from Moshe's words to Pharaoh: *veyachogu li bamidbar* — let them hold a celebration for Me in the wilderness. Is Moshe merely asking for a party? No — he is requesting sacrifices. *Veyomer Moshe gam atah titein beyadeinu zvachim ve'olos* — and Moshe said, you too will provide us with sacrifices and burnt-offerings. The *chag* is sacrificial.

### Chelev Chagi

But the *Gemara* presses back: perhaps even Moshe was only requesting a celebration with meat and drink — *ichlu v'shasu v'chogu chaga kamai* — without specifying Temple-style sacrifices. The word *zevach* in Moshe's subsequent statement need not mean a formal *korban*; it can simply mean slaughter. And the *olah* Moshe mentions is not a *chagigah* in any case.

The *Gemara* therefore turns to another verse: *velo yalin chelev chagi ad boker* — the fat of my *chag* shall not remain overnight until morning. If *chag* meant only a celebration, in what sense would a celebration have *chelev*, fat? The fat belongs to a sacrifice. *V'isalka daitach dechoga hu, tarba l'chaga is lei* — if you think *chag* means merely festivity, does a celebration possess fat? The conclusion would seem forced: *chagi* refers to the fat of a specific sacrifice called a *chagigah*.

The *Gemara* resists this too. Perhaps *chelev haba bizman chag* — fats of any sacrifices brought during the festival period — *lo yalin*, may not be left overnight. The word *chagi* would then mean sacrifices brought at the time of the *chag*, not a specific *korban chagigah*. But this is also challenged: the verse *kol halayla ad haboker* already requires burning the fat of *olot* overnight, so the general law is established regardless. The verse about *chelev chagi* must therefore be adding a specific *lav* applying to the *chagigah* sacrifice as such — not merely a general overlap with already existing law.

### Gezeirah Shava

The *Gemara* ultimately turns to a *gezeirah shavah*. The verse here says *veyachogu li bamidbar* — "they shall celebrate for me in the *midbar*." The verse in *Amos* says *hazvachim umincha higashtem li bamidbar* — "did you bring me sacrifices and grain offerings in the wilderness?" The shared word *bamidbar* connects the two passages: just as the verse in *Amos* uses *zvachim* — sacrifices — so too the *chag* Moshe speaks of means sacrifices. *Ma lehalan zvachim af kan zvachim*.

### Verses Not Clear

But the *Gemara* concedes the weakness of this final step: *divrei Torah midivrei kabbalah lo yalfinan* — we do not derive biblical law from *Nevi'im* or *Ketuvim*. The verse in *Amos* is *divrei kabbalah*, transmitted prophetic text, and while the *Gemara* acknowledges this principle is often honored in the breach, it stands in principle. Therefore, the derivation that *chagigah* is a *korban* is not fully clean. The verse *vechagosem oso chag* could mean just celebration; the *gezeirah shavah* that would clinch the sacrificial interpretation draws on a non-Torah source. That is why the obligation of *chagigah* is described as a mountain on a hair: not because there is no verse, but because the verses we have are not sufficiently unambiguous to establish beyond doubt that they mandate a *korban chagigah* specifically.

Returning to the *mishnah*'s formulation — *halachot merubot umikra muat* — one notes that the *Gemara* seems far more exercised about the *mikra muat* than the *halachot merubot*. Both for *Shabbos* and for *chagigah*, the *Gemara*'s concern is whether there is an explicit *pasuk* to begin with, not whether the quantity of *halachot* is disproportionate. Once you have an explicit *pasuk*, however thin, the *Gemara* does not treat the enormity of the derived law as troubling. The lack of a clear starting-point verse is what makes something a *searah*.

## Me'ilot

### Verses Explicit

The *mishnah* lists *me'ilot* — the laws of misappropriating consecrated property — as the third mountain on a hair. The *Gemara* raises the same challenge: *mikra kesiva* — there are explicit verses about the prohibition of misusing *hekdesh*. How is this a *searah*? Rami bar Chama answers: *lo nitzrecha ela lechidetnan* — the point is not about the basic prohibition but about a specific law taught in a *mishnah*.

### Shaliach Melacha

The law is this: *hashaliach she-asah shlichuso, ba'al habayis maal; lo asah shlichuso, shaliach maal* — if the agent did what he was sent to do, the homeowner has transgressed *me'ilah*; if the agent deviated from his instructions, the agent has transgressed.

The scenario: a person reaches into his wallet, unaware that he is handling money he had sanctified as *hekdesh*. He hands Michael twenty dollars and sends him to buy apples and bananas. Michael, unaware of any problem, goes to the store. If Michael buys exactly the apples and bananas he was asked to buy, *he* has done nothing independent — he is simply the extension of the one who sent him, and the one who sent him has committed *me'ilah* by effectively spending the *hekdesh* money. If Michael exercises his own judgment and instead buys oranges and pineapples, he has stepped outside his mandate, is no longer an extension of his sender, and is the one who has committed *me'ilah*.

### Michael's Transgression

The *Gemara* asks sharply: *vechi zeh choteh vezeh mitchayev* — can one person sin and the other be held responsible? Michael bought the apples and bananas — he is the one who physically transferred the *hekdesh* money. Why should the person at home be the transgressor?

*Hainu deka'amri matluyim besa'arah* — that is why it is a mountain on a hair. The logic does not cohere on its own terms.

Rava responds: *dilma shani me'ilah degamrinan cheit cheit mitrumah* — perhaps *me'ilah* is different because we derive it through a verbal analogy from *terumah*. Just as one may appoint a *shaliach* to separate *terumah* on one's behalf — *shlucho shel adam kemoto*, an agent is like the principal — the same principle applies here: when Michael buys the apples and bananas as instructed, he is functioning as the arm of the one who sent him, and the *me'ilah* is attributed to that person.

A deeper account of why this works can be offered. In the case of *terumah*, the legal act is separating the produce; it does not particularly matter who performs it, since what matters is the result — that *terumah* be designated. By analogy, in the spending of *hekdesh* money, the focus is less on the physical act of handing money to a storekeeper than on the context and outcome: the *hekdesh* funds have been misappropriated, and the one who set that process in motion, who caused the money to be misspent, bears the responsibility. The causal locus is the person who dispatched the agent, not the agent who executed the task.

### Beam Transfer

But the *Gemara* refines the question further. Rava says: *lo nitzrecha ela lechid'tanya* — the truly difficult case is a different one. Here the situation is: the homeowner remembers, mid-transaction, that the money he sent with Michael is *hekdesh* — *nizkar ba'al habayis velo nizkar shaliach*. He remembers; Michael does not. At the moment the homeowner realizes the error, he no longer wants Michael to be his *shaliach*. He revokes the agency, at least in his own mind, because he cannot reach Michael in time. Michael, still acting in complete innocence, proceeds to do exactly what was asked of him. *Shaliach maal* — the agent has committed *me'ilah*. As the *Gemara* exclaims: *shaliach anya* — poor Michael! He did exactly what he was told, he has no knowledge of any problem, and yet he is the one who has transgressed. *Hainu harim hatluyim basa'arah* — that is a mountain on a hair.

Rav Ashi challenges this: *dilma midi d'hava amotzi ma'os hekdesh* — is this really more difficult than the ordinary case of someone who accidentally reaches into the wrong pocket and spends *hekdesh* money he did not know was *hekdesh*? He too had no intent, and he too is liable for *me'ilah* *beshogeg*. The distinction the *Gemara* implies is that in that case the person had at least some responsibility to keep track of which money was sanctified. Michael had no such responsibility whatsoever. His liability is entirely devoid of any causal connection to the wrong.

Rav Ashi then redirects: *lo nitzrecha ella k'ditnan* — the case where the mountain truly hangs on a hair is this *mishnah*. If a person takes a beam or stone belonging to *hekdesh*, he has not yet committed *me'ilah*. *Nesana l'chavero* — if he then gives it to another person, *hu maal v'chavero lo maal* — he has committed *me'ilah*, and the recipient has not. The *Gemara* then asks: *mishkal shaklei* — but he took it either way. Whether he brought the beam home to his own house or handed it to someone else, in both cases he removed it from the Temple storehouse. Why does he commit *me'ilah* only upon transfer to another, and not upon taking it home himself?

*Hainu harim hatluyim basa'arah.* That is why it is a mountain on a hair.

### Gizbar Special Case

Rav Ashi's challenge to his own formulation is immediately answered by reference to Shmuel's resolution. Shmuel was sufficiently troubled by the logic of that case that he circumscribed it: *hacha b'gizbar hamsuros lo avnei binyan askinan* — the case concerns the Temple treasurer, the *gizbar*, to whom the building materials of *hekdesh* are entrusted. As the official custodian of the Temple's physical resources, wherever the *gizbar* moves the beams and stones — even if he takes them to his own home — they remain within his zone of authorized responsibility. *D'chol heicha d'mancha birshusa didei mancha* — wherever they rest, they are still considered within *hekdesh*'s *reshus*. Since they are never outside *hekdesh*'s domain while in his hands, no act of *me'ilah* can occur until he actually transfers them to someone else, removing them definitively from *hekdesh*'s *reshut*. At that point, and only at that point, has he committed *me'ilah*. The discussion of which case in *hilchos me'ilah* constitutes the true "mountain on a hair" — defying logical resolution — will continue.
