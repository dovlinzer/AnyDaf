# Moed Katan 27 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Blasphemy & Keriah

### Direct vs. Repeated Hear…

The *daf* under discussion is 26a, and we pick up in the middle of a *baraita* addressing the obligation of *keriah* upon hearing God's name blasphemed — *birchas hashem*. The *Tanu Rabanan* teaches: *echad hashome'a* — whether a person hears the blasphemy directly — *v'echad hashome'a mi'pi hashome'a* — or hears it being repeated, presumably not merely a report that it occurred, but an actual literal repetition of the words. *Tosfos* clarifies the context: this refers to witnesses who, in the course of testifying against the blasphemer, are required to actually repeat what was said. The result is that everyone present hears the blasphemy a second time from the witnesses' lips. In either case, *chayav likro'a* — one is obligated to perform *keriah*.

### Witnesses & Precedent

The *baraita* continues: *v'ha'edim einam chayavim likro'a* — the witnesses themselves, when they repeat the blasphemy during testimony, are not obligated to tear again, *she'kevar kar'u b'sha'as sheshamu* — because they already tore when they first heard it. The *Gemara* challenges this: what does it matter that they heard it once before? They are hearing it again now, from their own lips — *haksha'ami hashta*. The *Gemara* responds, *lo salkas daiteich* — do not think that way — *dichsiv*, for the verse tells us: *vayehi kishmo'a hamelech Chizkiyahu* — when King Chizkiyahu heard the report of *Ravshakeh*'s blasphemy, *vayikra es begadav* — he rent his garments. The verse records that the king tore, *hamelech kara v'heim lo kar'u*, but the people who came and reported it — who repeated the very words — did not tear again. It says they arrived with rent garments, not that they rent them anew.

### Gezerah Shavah Rule

The *Gemara* then asks: *v'lo mis'achi minalan* — how do we know that the tearing in these circumstances, whether for *birchas hashem*, the death of a *nasi* or *av beis din*, or national tragedy, cannot be sewn up? The answer is *asya kriya kriya* — we make a *gzeira shava*. Just as we previously learned regarding *keriah* for one's parents and teacher, deriving from the story of *Eliyahu* and *Elisha* that the tear is not repaired, so too here, since the same language of *tikra* appears, the *gzeira shava* teaches that the tear is permanent.

### Tosafot's Challenge

*Tosfos* raises an immediate difficulty: *teima im kein neileif kol kriyos shelo yis'achu* — if this *gzeira shava* applies, should we not derive that every act of *keriah* is irreparable, since the word *kriya* appears throughout? The answer given is *v'shema lo mistavra lei* — it simply does not make sense to extend the principle universally. *Tosfos* acknowledges that *Chazal* had a prior understanding of which acts of *keriah* were appropriate to repair and which were not, and applied the *gzeira shava* selectively to those that fit. This is a striking *Tosfos*, because in *yeshivos* these *drashos* are often taught as though they operate mechanically. Here we see explicitly that the application of a *gzeira shava* is not automatic — there is an element of judgment in determining to which cases the textual link applies, and what its implications are.

## Sefer Torah Burnt

### Yehoyakim Story

The *Gemara* now turns to the case of a *Sefer Torah* that has been burnt: *Sefer Torah she-nisraf minalan* — where do we learn the obligation of *keriah* in such a circumstance? The verse in Yirmiyahu says: *vayehi kikhro Yehudi shalosh delatot v'arba* — when the servant read three or four sections. This refers to the reading of the scroll that Yirmiyahu had written, understood by the *Gemara* to be *Megilas Eicha*.

The *Gemara* tells us that they informed Yehoyakim, *katav Yirmiyah Sefer Kinot* — Yirmiyahu wrote a book of lamentations. Yehoyakim asked what was written in it. They told him: *Eicha yashvah vadad* — how does the city dwell alone? He said: *ana malka* — I am a king, nothing will befall me. They continued: *bacho tivkeh ba-lailah* — it cries at night. He replied again: I am a king. *Galtah Yehudah me-oni* — Judah will be exiled from oppression. Still: I am a king, it will not affect me. *Darchei Tziyon aveilot* — the ways of Zion mourn. I am a king. But then: *hayu tzareha lerosh* — its enemies will become its rulers. At this he became alarmed and asked, *man amrah* — who said this? For it was no longer about Zion in the abstract. Then: *Ki Hashem hogah al rov p'shaeha* — God planned this because of its many sins. Suddenly this was about God acting against him. *Mi-yad* — immediately, *kadar kol ha-azkarot she-bah* — he cut out all the divine names from the scroll, *v'sorfan ba-esh* — and burnt them in the fire, as an act of defiance against God.

### Non-Response Paradox

The *Gemara* then notes: *v'hainu dichsiv velo pachadu velo kar'u es bigdeihem* — and that is why the verse records that those present did not fear and did not rend their garments. The implication — *miklal di-vau le-mikra* — is that the proper response would have been *keriah*, and their failure to do so is what the verse calls attention to.

### Scroll vs. Names

One might note that the narrative as the *Gemara* presents it places particular emphasis on the burning of *shem Hashem* — the divine names — rather than the destruction of the entire scroll. *Tosfos* raises this directly: the simple sense of the verse, *vayikraeha b'ta'ar hasofer* — he cut it with the scribe's razor — implies the entire scroll was destroyed. *U'vi-Yerushalmi lo garsinan azkarot* — and in the Yerushalmi the text does not specify that it was specifically the divine names that were burnt. This matters because emphasizing the names would shift the focus of the *keriah* obligation, distinguishing between the destruction of the physical scroll and the destruction of God's name.

### Rav Papa's Question

Rav Papa raises a further question to Abaye: *eima mishum shmuot raot* — perhaps the obligation of *keriah* in this narrative derives from the terrible news of the impending national catastrophe, since the scroll spoke of exile and destruction? Abaye replies: *shmuot raot b'hahi sha'ata mi havu* — those were predictions, not events that had yet occurred. The obligation of *keriah* for bad tidings applies only to tragedies that have actually taken place, not to prophecies of future calamities.

### Two Tearings Rule

Rabbi Chelbo said in the name of Rav Huna: *ha-roeh Sefer Torah she-nikrah chayav likro shtei kriyos* — one who sees a *Sefer Torah* that has been torn is obligated to perform two acts of *keriah*, *achas al ha-gevil v'achas al ha-ketav* — one for the parchment and one for the script. The source is: *acharei s'rof ha-melech es ha-Megilah v'es ha-dvarim* — after the king burnt the scroll and the words. The physical embodiment and the content of the words are each an independent reason for *keriah* — the attempt to destroy the words and meaning of the text is at least as weighty a cause as the destruction of the physical medium.

## Tefillin Story

### Ostrich Incident

The *Gemara* now presents a story that partially illuminates the parameters of this obligation. Rabbi Abba and Rav Huna bar Chiya were sitting before Rabbi Abba. Rabbi Abba needed to relieve himself and removed his *tefillin*, setting them on his garment. An ostrich came — ostriches being known to swallow anything — and nearly swallowed them. He said: *hashta chayvinan l'shtay kriyos* — had it swallowed my *tefillin*, I would have been obligated to perform two acts of *keriah*, since it would be analogous to the destruction of a sacred text, which contains *shem Hashem*.

### Counter-Experience

Rav Huna bar Chiya responded: *minalach ha* — where do you get that idea? He related that the very same thing had happened to him — something similar involving his *tefillin* being swallowed or nearly so. He went to Rav Matna, who did not know the answer. He then went to Rav Yehuda.

### Willful vs. Accidental

Rav Yehuda told him: *hachi amar Shmuel* — this is what Shmuel said: *lo amru ela b'zroa uke'ma'aseh shehayah* — the obligation was stated only for an act done with force and violence, like the incident that occurred with Yehoyakim, where the text was willfully and forcibly destroyed. An act of nature — an ostrich swallowing the *tefillin* — would not trigger the obligation.

What exactly constitutes *zroa*? Rashi and Rabbeinu Gershom explain that it means a situation where *she'ein yachol l'hatzilo* — there would have been no way to save the text even had someone been present, as opposed to the ostrich scenario where one could chase the ostrich away, whereas no one could stop a king. The Yerushalmi, as cited by *Tosfos*, appears to add a different dimension: *lo amru ela b'zroa shesarafam melech Yisrael b'zroa* — it was not merely that the text could not be saved, but that it was a willful act of destruction carried out by one in power. The emphasis is on the deliberate, denigrating character of the act — an exercise of coercive authority to destroy the sacred. An accidental loss, however tragic, does not carry that meaning.

## Judea's Ruins

### The Verse Source

The *Gemara* turns to *arei Yehuda minalan* — how do we know the obligation of *keriah* upon seeing the cities of Judea in their destroyed state? The verse relates: *vayavo'u anashim miShchem miShilo umiShomron* — and men came from Shchem, from Shilo, and from Shomron, *shmonim ish* — eighty men, *meguchei zakan* — with shaved beards, *ukru'ei begadim* — and rent garments, *umitgodedim* — and who had lacerated their flesh, carrying meal-offerings and frankincense to bring to the house of God, in the immediate aftermath of the destruction.

### Tosafot's Question

*Tosfos* raises a potential objection: one might have argued, *eima ad d'ika tlat* — perhaps all three cities, Shchem, Shilo, and Shomron, would need to be destroyed to generate the obligation. *Tosfos* dismisses this, noting that each of the people in the verse presumably came from a different city, so no such requirement follows. However, *Tosfos* raises a different difficulty: the verse also mentions shaving one's beard and lacerating one's flesh, both of which are prohibited. Since those elements of the verse describe excessive or forbidden acts, perhaps the entire verse is too problematic to derive *halacha* from — *d'shamen d'lo shayach l'meimar d'lo neitanya klal*. *Tosfos* resolves this by saying that rather than rejecting the verse entirely, *Chazal* chose to learn from it selectively — deriving the principle of *keriah* while disregarding the excessive elements. This parallels the earlier observation about the selective application of the *gzeira shava*.

### Seeing Ruins

Rabbi Chelbo said in the name of Ulla Biri, who said in the name of Rabbi Elazar: *ha-ro'eh arei Yehudah bechorbanam omer* — one who sees the cities of Judea in their destroyed state recites: *areich kodshecha hayu midbar* — your holy cities have become a wilderness — *v'kore'ah* — and performs *keriah*. It is striking that the obligation applies not only to witnessing the destruction as it happens, but to seeing the ruins even many years or centuries later. That a person viewing ancient ruins would still be obligated is itself a considerable *chiddush* that goes beyond the plain sense of the original verse.

### Jerusalem & Temple

*Yerushalayim bechurbana* — one who sees Jerusalem in its destroyed state recites: *Tziyon midbar hayta Yerushalayim shemama* — Zion has become a wilderness, Jerusalem a desolation — *v'kore'ah*. *Beis HaMikdash bechurbano* — one who sees the Temple in its destroyed state recites: *Beis kodsheinu v'sifartenu asher hillalucha avoseinu haya lisrefas esh v'chol machmadeinu hayu l'charba* — our holy and glorious House where our fathers praised You has been consumed by fire, and all our treasured things have become ruins — *v'kore'ah*. All of these verses are from Yeshayahu. This *halacha* is recorded in the *Shulchan Aruch*, notably not in *Hilchos Aveilut* but at the end of *Hilchos Taanis*, since the fast days are primarily connected to the destruction of the *Beis HaMikdash*.

The *Shulchan Aruch* rules: *kore'ah al HaMikdash umosif al Yerushalayim* — one performs *keriah* for the *Beis HaMikdash* and then adds to it for *Yerushalayim*. This raises an obvious question about sequencing.

### Order & Priority

The *Gemara* notes a contradiction. One *baraita* implies that one does a single *keriah* for the *Mikdash* and adds to it for *Yerushalayim*. Another *baraita* rules: *v'kore'ah al HaMikdash bifnei atzmo v'al Yerushalayim bifnei atzma* — one does a separate *keriah* for the Temple and a separate *keriah* for Jerusalem. That is the real tension: the first source implies one *keriah* with an addition, the second implies two distinct acts.

The *Gemara* resolves: *lo kashya* — there is no difficulty. *Ha d'paga b'Mikdash breisha* — if a person first encounters the Temple site, as in the case of someone who entered Jerusalem in a box (*b'shida teivah umigdal*) without having seen the city first, then he tears for the more severe *Mikdash* first and can add to it for *Yerushalayim*. *Ha d'paga b'Yerushalayim breisha* — the normal case, where a person travels through Jerusalem toward the Temple Mount, he tears for *Yerushalayim* first. Since the *Mikdash* is the more severe of the two, it may not serve merely as a secondary addition to a *keriah* already done for *Yerushalayim* — it requires its own independent act of *keriah*. A correction is worth noting here: it is the more severe obligation that cannot be reduced to a mere *hosafa* on a less severe *keriah*. If one begins with the more severe, one may add for the less severe; but one may not make the more severe an addendum to the less severe.

## Keriah Mechanics

### Stitching Types

The *Gemara* turns to the mechanics of *keriah*. A *baraita* teaches: *sholal, malal, v'oket, v'la'asos k'min sulamos* — all of these are various modes of loose stitching — *aval lo l'achosom* — but one may not fully seal the tear. The specific prohibition is *ichuy* — a complete reweaving that restores the garment to wholeness. The terms *sholal*, *malal*, *oket*, and *sulamot* represent different ways of threading the two halves together loosely — *sulamot*, meaning like a ladder, suggests rungs that hold the halves but with wide spacing between stitches. These partial repairs are permissible; a tight weave that truly makes the garment whole again is not.

### Alexandrian Stitching

Rav Chisda adds a qualification: *u'v'ichuy Alexandri* — even among forms of *ichuy*, only Alexandrian stitching constitutes the prohibited complete repair. This is apparently a technique so thorough as to approximate a true reweaving of the garment. This qualification functions in two directions, as we shall see.

### Tearing Through Stitches

A subsequent *baraita* addresses the case of one who performs *keriah* through previous stitches. If the garment had been loosely restitched using *sholal*, *malal*, *oket*, or *sulamot* — the very modes just identified as permissible partial repairs — and a person then tears through those stitches when performing a new act of *keriah*, *lo yatza* — he does not fulfill his obligation. The reason is that since those stitches do not constitute a full repair, the garment is already considered torn. One cannot perform a meaningful act of *keriah* on something that is already, in halakhic terms, in a state of being rent.

However, *mitoch ha-ichuy* — if the garment had been thoroughly restitched, such that it was now considered whole, then performing *keriah* through those stitches *yatza* — fulfills the obligation. This applies, for instance, when the tear was not from a prior act of *keriah* (and thus was not subject to the prohibition of sewing up), or when the garment tore accidentally and was tightly repaired. Once the garment is restored to wholeness through such stitching, tearing it constitutes a genuine act of *keriah*. Rav Chisda again qualifies: this applies only to Alexandrian *ichuy*, the truly complete form of repair. This yields both a *chumra* and a *kula*: when assessing whether the prohibition of sewing up has been violated (as with *keriah* for father or mother), only Alexandrian *ichuy* is prohibited. And when assessing whether one has fulfilled the obligation by tearing a previously mended garment, only Alexandrian *ichuy* restores the garment to a state where a new *keriah* is meaningful.

### Inverting Garment

The *Gemara* presents a striking ruling: *rashai l'hofcho l'mata v'l'achoto* — a person may turn the garment upside down and sew up what is now the hem. The logic is that once the garment is inverted, what had been the tear is now located at the hemline. A tear at the hem does not carry the symbolic weight of *keriah* — one does not fulfill *keriah* by tearing at the hem, and therefore what now sits at the hem no longer represents the tear in the halakhically meaningful sense. Since it no longer bears that status, it may be sewn. There is something noteworthy about this: one might ask what permits this transformation — inverting the garment to circumvent the prohibition. The answer appears to lie in the nature of the prohibition itself. *Keriah* that cannot be sewn is prohibited because one may never directly repair that symbolic rent. Inverting the garment does not directly repair the tear; it relocates it halakhically by changing what the garment represents. As was discussed, the symbolism of leaving a tear unsewn points to something that can never be made whole. Inverting is not making it whole — it is, in a roundabout way, changing what the tear signifies.

This is the position of the *Tanna Kamma*. However, the small letter alongside the ruling indicates the ruling we follow.

### Rabbi Shimon's View

Rabbi Shimon ben Elazar forbids this: *oser l'achoto* — wherever the tear is located on the garment, it may not be sewn up. And as the textual notation indicates, this is the ruling we follow. No amount of repositioning changes the prohibition; the tear must remain unsewn regardless of where on the garment it comes to rest.

### Object Status

The *Gemara* states: *u'cheshem she'hamocher asur l'achoto, kach ha'lokeach asur l'achoto* — just as the seller may not sew up the torn garment, so too the purchaser is prohibited from doing so. In Brisker terminology, this is a *halacha* not only in the *gavra* but in the *cheftza* — the status inheres in the object itself, not merely in the person who performed the *keriah*. The garment retains its status regardless of who holds it. Consequently, *mocher tzarich l'hodia lalokeach* — the seller must inform the buyer that this garment had undergone a *keriah* whose tear is permanent, so that the buyer not inadvertently violate the prohibition.

### Non-Jew Question

*Tosfos* raises a fascinating question: *lo shamati im mutar lemochro lenochri* — may one sell the garment to a non-Jew? The question is whether the prohibition is entirely about the object's status — in which case no one, including a non-Jew, may sew it — or whether the seller's obligation to inform the buyer is specifically about ensuring that a Jewish buyer not come to violate the *halacha*, in which case a non-Jewish buyer, who is not subject to this *halacha*, would be permitted to sew it. *Tosfos* writes: *nehi delokeach assur l'achoto im hu nochri mutar* — perhaps if the purchaser is a non-Jew it is permitted; *af al pi de'mocher tzarich l'hodia lalokeach hainu kedei shelo yavo lokeach lidei issur* — the seller's disclosure obligation exists only to prevent a Jewish buyer from violating the prohibition. *Tosfos* himself leans toward permitting it: *ulichora haya mutar* — it would seem to be allowed. However, the asterisk notation in the text directs attention to *Masechet Semachos*, end of chapter 9, where it is ruled explicitly that it is *assur* — forbidden. The question of how fully the status resides in the *cheftza* remains a genuinely interesting one.

## Keriah Measurements

### Rabbi Meir Position

The *Gemara* turns to the required measurements of *keriah*. A *baraita* states: *tchilas kriah tefach* — the initial *keriah* must be a *tefach* in length. *V'tosefet shalosh* — if a person who has already torn for one relative must tear again for another who has died (of equal or lesser halakhic weight, such that the second tear may be added to the first rather than requiring an independent act), the addition need only be three finger-widths. The reasoning is that the act of tearing for the second relative does not need to produce a full *tefach* of new tearing — the garment is already torn a *tefach* from the first act, and the small additional amount results in a garment of sufficient size. This is the position of Rabbi Meir.

### Rabbi Yehuda Position

Rabbi Yehuda says: *tchilas kriah shalosh etzbaot v'tosefet kol shehu* — the initial *keriah* need only be three finger-widths, not a full *tefach*, while any additional tearing may be even a minimal amount, since the principle governing the addition — that the resulting tear is meaningful — applies a fortiori when even the initial requirement is smaller.

### Ruling Applied

Ulla rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Meir bikriah* — we rule like Rabbi Meir that the initial *keriah* must be a *tefach*. *V'halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda betosefet* — we rule like Rabbi Yehuda that the addition may be minimal. A *baraita* supports this combined ruling, as Rabbi Yossi is recorded saying: the initial *keriah* must be a *tefach* and the addition may be any amount.

### Sequential Deaths

The *Gemara* now presents the case of multiple deaths occurring in sequence. The *baraita* teaches: *amru lo meis aviv v'kara* — he was told his father died and he performed *keriah*. *Meis beno v'hosif* — then his son died and he added to the tear. Now, halakhically, the death of a parent carries a weightier status by virtue of *kibbud av va'eim*, even though the death of a child may be more emotionally devastating. Therefore, the death of a child, being of lesser or equal halakhic weight, may be expressed as an *hosafa* — an addition to the original *keriah* done for the father. In this case: *tachton mit'acheh, elyon eino mit'acheh* — the lower portion of the tear, being the addition for the son, carries a status that may be sewn up, while the upper portion, done for the father, carries a permanent status and may not be sewn.

### Father Priority

The reverse case is then addressed: *meis beno v'kara* — his son died and he performed *keriah*. *Meis aviv v'hosif* — then his father died and he added to the tear. The result is the opposite: *elyon mit'acheh, tachton eino mit'acheh* — the upper portion, which was done for the son, may be sewn; the lower addition, done for the father, may not be. The single tear thus carries two distinct halakhic statuses depending on which portion one examines.

### Simultaneous Deaths

The *baraita* continues with the scenario of multiple deaths occurring together: *meis aviv, meis imo, meis achiv, meis achoto* — father, mother, brother, and sister all die. In such a case, *korea kera echad l'chulan* — one performs a single *keriah* for all of them. Sequential deaths require sequential acts or additions; when news arrives simultaneously, a single act suffices for all.

### Beteira's View

Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira disagrees: *al kulan kera echad, al aviv v'imo kera echad* — while one *keriah* suffices for all the others together, the *keriah* for father and mother must be kept separate — *lefi she'ein mosifin al kera aviv v'imo* — because one may not add to the tear done for a father or mother. Rav Nachman bar Yitzchak explains: *lefi she'einan b'tosefet* — because *keriah* for parents is not in the category of additions. *Tosfos* wrestles with what this adds beyond a circular statement, but the operative rule is clear: the tear for father or mother requires its own independent act.

It is worth clarifying the relationship to what was said earlier about *Yerushalayim* and the *Mikdash*. There it was ruled that one who encounters the *Mikdash* first may add for *Yerushalayim*, but not the reverse — the more severe may not be reduced to an addendum. Here, Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira is making a different and stricter point: the tear for father or mother cannot even serve as the base to which another is added. It is entirely separate from the regime of *hosafos*.

## Adding Keriah Limits

### Keriah vs. Aveilus

Shmuel rules: *halacha k'Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira* — the law follows Rabbi Yehuda ben Beteira, requiring a separate *keriah* for parents and a separate one for other relatives. The *Gemara* raises an apparent contradiction: does not Shmuel himself hold that in matters of *aveilus* we rule leniently? The *Gemara* resolves with the foundational principle: *aveilus lachud, kriya lachud* — *aveilus* and *keriah* are distinct domains. As we have been learning throughout this discussion, *keriah* exists independently of *aveilus* — it is performed at the moment of death, prior to the onset of the mourning period itself, and applies in many contexts entirely outside *aveilus*. The lenient principle in *aveilus* does not automatically carry over to *keriah*.

### After Shiva Addition

The *Gemara* then presents a debate transmitted by Rav Matna, Mar Ukva, and Levi in the name of Shmuel's father regarding the timing of permissible *hosafa*. One position: *kol shiva korea, l'achar shiva mosif* — throughout the entire first week, a person must perform a new *keriah*; only after *shiva* may one add to the existing tear. The logic is that since the tear may be repaired after *shiva*, it is then in a state of being eligible for repair, and therefore a new act of tearing upon it is halakhically meaningful. During *shiva*, when the tear cannot be sewn, it retains full status as a *kera* and adding to it is insignificant.

### After Shloshim Addition

The second position: *kol l'achar shiva korei, l'achar shloshim mosif* — throughout all thirty days, a new *keriah* must be performed; only after *shloshim* may one add. The difference between these positions concerns which degree of potential repair renders the original *kera* eligible to receive an *hosafa*: is it the partial sewing permitted after *shiva*, or only the complete repair permitted after *shloshim*?

### Zeira's Challenge

Rav Zeira challenges the first position: according to the one who says *kol shiva korea* — why must a separate *keriah* be done throughout *shiva*? Because *dlo nitan l'sholev* — the tear cannot be sewn during *shiva*, and therefore the tear retains its full status, making any addition meaningless. But if that is the reasoning, what about the ruling that a woman is permitted to sew her tear immediately even during *shiva* — *ha'isha sheshalala l'altar* — for reasons of modesty and dignity? According to the logic just given, should a woman then be permitted to perform *hosafa* even during *shiva*, since her tear is considered fit to be fixed?

### Woman's Exception

The *Gemara* answers: *hatam mishum kavod isha* — the woman's permission to sew during *shiva* is granted out of concern for her dignity, not because the tear itself is considered, on its own terms, fit to be repaired. The status of the tear remains that of a full, unrepairable *kera*; the sewing is an accommodation for *kavod ha-beriyos*. Therefore, performing an *hosafa* during *shiva* would still not be meaningful, because the tear's own halakhic status remains that of an unsewable *kera*.

### Parent Exception

The corresponding challenge to the stricter position — that after *shloshim* the original *kera* becomes eligible for *hosafa* — is: what about *keriah* for father or mother, which *dlo nitnu l'achot l'olam* — may never be sewn up? By the same logic, should *hosafa* for *aveilus* of father or mother be forever impossible? The *Gemara* answers: *hatam mishum kavod aviv v'imo hu* — there it is the reverse situation. For father and mother, the tear is in principle deserving of being sewn — its own intrinsic status would permit it — but we do not sew it out of respect for father and mother. Since on its own terms the tear is fit to be repaired, performing an act of *keriah* upon it after *shloshim* is meaningful. The prohibition against sewing is external to the object's status; the object's own status is one of repairability, and thus *hosafa* remains significant. The *Brisker* resonance here is clear: the distinction is between the *cheftza*'s status as fit to be sewn and the *gavra*'s prohibition against doing so — and these are not the same thing.

## Special Keriah Cases

### Rented Garment Issue

The *Gemara* now turns to a series of special cases. A *baraita* teaches: one who appears before a deceased person wearing a garment that was already torn — and therefore gives the appearance of having performed *keriah* when he has not — *harei zeh gozel ha-meitim v'ha-chayim* — has stolen from both the dead and the living. He has failed to give the deceased the proper respect of genuine *keriah*, and he has deceived the living through *gneivas da'as*, creating a false impression that he has mourned.

### Rabban Gamliel Case

Rabban Gamliel presents a case designed not as a trick but as a genuine halakhic question. A person says to his friend: *hashileni chaluqcha v'elech v'avaker es abba shehu choleh* — lend me your garment so I may go visit my father who is ill. He borrows the garment to appear presentable when visiting his sick father. The halakhic baseline is that one does not perform *keriah* on a borrowed garment, because doing so damages another's property without permission. The question here is how much the lender's awareness of the father's precarious state implies an implicit permission: if the borrower disclosed that he was going to visit a father who might be near death, does the lender implicitly consent to *keriah* being performed on his garment if death occurs?

### Limited Permission

The ruling is: *v'halach u'metzao shemeis, korea* — he went and found the father had died — he performs *keriah*, because the circumstances under which he borrowed it created an implicit understanding. *U'me'ache* — but he then sews up the tear. Even though he is mourning a father, for whom the tear would normally be permanent, the fact that the garment is not his own means his obligation to restore the object to as whole a state as possible overrides the prohibition against sewing — within the limits of what was implicitly permitted. *U'cheshe-bo l'veito machzir lo chaluqo v'nosein lo dmei krio* — when he returns home he returns the garment and compensates the owner for the depreciation caused by the tear and the repair. The permission extended was minimal: enough to perform the act, but with full financial responsibility for the resulting loss.

*V'im lo hodio* — but if the borrower did not disclose why he was borrowing the garment, *harei zeh lo yiga bo* — he may not touch it, since there was no implied consent to potential *keriah*.

### Protecting the Ill

The *Gemara* then addresses the case of a dangerously ill person — *choleh she-meis lo meis* — whose relative has died. The *halacha* is: *ein modiin oso shemet shema titoraf da'ato alav* — one does not inform him of the death, lest the emotional distress worsen his physical condition. Nor does one perform *keriah* in his presence — *v'ein mokrin befanav* — so as not to reveal to him that a death has occurred. And one silences the professional mourning women in his presence — *u'meshashtkin es ha-nashim mipanav* — so that their wailing not reach him and convey the news.

### Minor's Keriah

Moving to other special cases: *umokrin l'katan mipnei agmas nefesh* — one tears the garment of a minor in mourning, even though minors are not halakhically obligated in the observances of *shiva*. The tearing is done not for the minor's sake but for everyone else — it adds to the collective grief and sense of tragedy that the entire community shares, and thereby honors the deceased.

### In-Law Keriah

Similarly, *v'korin al hamiv v'al hamato mipnei kavod ishto* — a man performs *keriah* for his father-in-law and mother-in-law out of respect for his wife. Again, the obligation is not strictly his own but is performed out of deference to another's grief. Whether this is practiced *l'halacha* in current custom appears to be doubtful.

## Feeding Mourner

### Bed Positioning

The concluding passage of the *daf* addresses the practice of *havra'ah* — the first meal provided to the mourner upon returning from the cemetery. A *baraita* teaches: *v'ein mavrin ela al mitot zkufot* — one feeds the mourner only with upright beds. The context here may be *Chol HaMoed*, where the beds are not overturned, in contrast to the regular practice of the mourning period. A subsequent *baraita* refines the picture: *ha-holech l'beis ha-avel im haya libo gas bo* — one who goes to the house of a mourner, if he is on intimate terms with him, *yavruhu al mitot kfuyos* — then both sit on overturned beds together while the guest feeds the mourner. *V'im lav* — if he is not so intimate, *yavruhu al mitot zkufos* — the feeding takes place with upright beds.

### Rava Story

The *Gemara* illustrates this with an incident. Rava had a death in his family. Abba bar Marta — who is the same person as Abba bar Minyumi — came to comfort him. Rava was reclining on an upright bed; Abba bar Marta, the *menachem*, was on an overturned bed. Rava remarked: *kama leis beih da'ata l'hai tzurba m'Rabbanan* — how little understanding does this rabbinic student show. The *menachem* had positioned himself on the overturned bed while the *avel* reclined on the upright one — precisely the reverse of what is appropriate. The exact reason *Rava* himself was on an upright bed (and not an overturned one) is a major question among the *Rishonim*, and likely relates to the *Chol HaMoed* context. But whatever the circumstances, the reversal of positions — the visitor on the bed of mourning while the mourner sat upright — was clearly an error that demonstrated a fundamental lack of understanding of these laws.
