# Nedarim 33 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Intro & Transition

### Welcome & Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵין בֵּין הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לַמּוּדָּר הֵימֶנּוּ מַאֲכָל אֶלָּא דְּרִיסַת הָרֶגֶל וְכֵלִים שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין בָּהֶם אוֹכֶל נֶפֶשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **The difference between one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow [*hamuddar hana'a meḥaveiro*] and one for whom** benefit **from his food is forbidden by vow** concerns **only setting foot** on the other person's property **and** borrowing from that person **utensils that one does not use** in **preparation of food** but for other purposes. Those two benefits are forbidden to the former but permitted to the latter.

Today's daf is 33, and we begin once again from the bottom of 32b — from the opening of the new chapter, the new *perek* — and its fresh *mishnah*. As noted in the previous session, we are now shifting from a discussion of *when* a vow is binding to a discussion of *what* is included in a vow once it has been established as binding. Specifically, the subject is what constitutes *hana'ah* — benefit — since the classic *neder* forbids the subject from deriving benefit from a particular person or object.

### Previous Perek Review

The previous *perek* addressed the conditions under which a *neder* is binding: examining the reasons behind it, the context in which it was made, and the motivations of the one who made it, in order to determine whether the person truly intended the vow and under what circumstances it takes effect. That entire discussion assumed the question of whether the *neder* is valid at all. The current *perek* makes a decisive shift: we assume the vow is valid and binding. The question now is what falls within its scope.

### Scope of Vows

The concept of *hana'ah* — benefit — is the organizing category. A generic *neder* forbids deriving benefit from a person or object in all its forms. But what counts as benefit? This is the central question that will occupy the daf. Closely related is the narrower formulation — forbidding only food-related benefit — and understanding the precise difference between these two types of vows is what the *mishnah* and subsequent *gemara* set out to clarify.

## Generic vs Food Hana'ah

### Ein Bein Formula

*Ein bein hamudar hana'ah mechavero lemudar heimenu ma'achal* — there is no difference between one who is forbidden all benefit from his friend and one who is forbidden only food from him. This formulation comes from the list of *ein bein* distinctions, familiar from Tractate Megilla. At first glance, one would expect an enormous gap between a comprehensive prohibition on benefit and a prohibition limited to food. The literal translation of *mudar heimenu ma'achal* is simply "forbidden from his food," which would seem to leave the vast majority of human interactions untouched. The *gemara* will have to explain why, in practice, the gap is so narrow.

The *mishnah* states that the only difference between the two is *drisas ha-regel* — the ability to cut across his yard — and *keilim she-ein osim bahem ochel nefesh* — the borrowing of vessels not used in food preparation. Under a generic prohibition on all benefit, one may not cut across his neighbor's yard and may not borrow anything at all: a rake, a car, anything whatsoever. But if the vow is limited to food benefit, one may cut through the yard and may borrow those categories of vessels.

### Mishnah Permitted Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הַמּוּדָּר מַאֲכָל מֵחֲבֵירוֹ לֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ נָפָה וּכְבָרָה וְרֵיחַיִם וְתַנּוּר, אֲבָל מַשְׁאִיל לוֹ חָלוּק וְטַבַּעַת וְטַלִּית וּנְזָמִים.
>
> **Translation:** Therefore, with regard to **one for whom** benefit **from another's food is forbidden by vow,** that person **may not lend him** utensils used in the preparation of food, e.g., **a sieve, or a strainer, or a millstone, or an oven. However, he may lend him a garment, or a finger ring, or a cloak, or nose rings,** as these are not used in the preparation of food. However, he may not lend them to one for whom benefit from him is forbidden by vow.

The *mishnah* continues: *hamudar ma'achal mechaveiro* — if one is forbidden from deriving food benefit from his friend — *lo yash'ilennu* — his friend should not lend him *nafah ve-kivar ve-reichayim* — a sieve, another type of sieve, and a millstone or mill — *ve-tanur* — and an oven. All of these are prohibited because, even if one has vowed only against food benefit, it clearly encompasses things that lead to food or assist in obtaining it: objects that help one make food. A plate would fall into the same category.

On the other hand, *aval mash'ilo chaluk ve-tallit ve-tabba'at ve-nezamim* — one's friend may lend him a shirt, a cloak, a ring, and various other adornments. Between the forbidden category of food-preparation vessels and the permitted category of clothing, however, there remains a vast middle ground — most things in the world — whose status is not yet addressed by the *mishnah*.

### Gap in Understanding

A question naturally arises regarding the structure of the *mishnah*: the *seifa* — the second clause enumerating specific permitted items — appears redundant to the general phrase *ve-keilim she-ein osim bahem ochel nefesh*. It likely functions as a clarification of that terse *ein bein* formulation, which in its original context in Megilla was highly compressed. The *seifa* here may represent a later gloss or expansion, unpacking that concise statement with concrete examples.

## Vittur & Rabbi Eliezer

### Rabbi Eliezer Attribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מַאן תְּנָא? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא, דְּתַנְיָא רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ וִיתּוּר אָסוּר בְּמוּדַּר הֲנָאָה.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: **Who is the *tanna*** who taught this mishna? **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: It is Rabbi Eliezer, as it is taught** in a *baraita* that **Rabbi Eliezer said: Even overlooking [*vittur*]** a matter for which one is typically indifferent to the actions of others, e.g., people setting foot on one's property, **is prohibited in** the case of **one prohibited by vow from deriving benefit** from another.

The *gemara* asks: *man tanna* — who is the author of this *mishnah*? *Amar Rav Adda bar Ahava: Rabbi Eliezer hi* — it is Rabbi Eliezer. This is demonstrated from a *braita*.

### Vittur Concept

*De-tanya: Rabbi Eliezer omer, afilu vittur assur be-mudar hana'ah* — even something that is a *vittur* — something not normally charged for, a giveaway, a freebie, something that people simply waive — is forbidden in a case of *mudar hana'ah*. Even something that no one would ever assign a monetary price to, that no one normally minds about, constitutes benefit if it passes from one person to another.

### Vittur Examples

The example given is the baker's dozen: if I sell twelve loaves and throw in a thirteenth at no charge, that thirteenth item is a *vittur*. More directly relevant is the case of *drisas ha-regel* — cutting through someone's yard. A person generally does not mind if his neighbor occasionally takes a shortcut across his lawn; he would never charge for it and would never think twice about it. Nevertheless, under a general prohibition on benefit, even this is forbidden. And so, if Michael has taken a vow that he cannot receive benefit from me, he may not cut across my yard — even though this is precisely the sort of thing people routinely overlook and do not care about.

The concept extends to other everyday informalities: borrowing someone's books, picking up a volume and leafing through it for a few minutes. People generally do not object. Perhaps the best translation of *vittur* is simply "things people don't mind about." Nonetheless, there is something received from the other person, and that is sufficient to constitute *hana'ah*.

### Abstract Benefits

It is worth noting that examples involving transactions — like the baker's extra item — are a somewhat lesser *chiddush*, because in those cases something concrete is being transferred as part of an existing commercial exchange. The more striking point is that Rabbi Eliezer's ruling extends to entirely abstract benefits: things that normally carry no monetary value and that people would never think to withhold. Even those, under *mudar hana'ah*, are forbidden. The ruling thus establishes that *hana'ah* is not defined by economic value or normal social practice, but by the bare fact of receiving something from another person.

## Hana'ah Leads to Food

### Sieve Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא מִן מַאֲכָל נָדַר! אָמַר רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן לָקִישׁ: בְּאוֹמֵר ״הֲנָאַת מַאֲכָלְךָ עָלַי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **But didn't he vow** that he is prohibited **from** partaking of **food,** and those items are not food items? **Rabbi Shimon ben Lakish said:** The mishna is referring to a case **where he says: Benefit from your food is forbidden to me,** which includes any benefit associated with food.

We turn now to the *gemara*'s central analytical challenge: *veha mi ma'achal nadar* — you only forbade food. So why is it forbidden to borrow the sieve? A sieve is not food. If the vow was limited to food benefit, on what basis do we prohibit borrowing vessels used in food preparation?

### Rav Shimon Interpretation

*Amar Rav Shimon ben Levi: be'omer hana'as ma'achalcha alai* — the formulation is not simply "your food is forbidden to me" but rather "the benefit of your food is forbidden to me." This subtly shifts the scope: not just eating the food, but deriving benefit from it. What would benefit from food look like? One could not use a cucumber to scratch one's back; one could not take wheat, chew it without swallowing, and apply it as a poultice to a wound. These are forms of benefit from the food itself that go beyond eating.

Tosafos notes that *hana'as ma'achalcha* might even encompass causing food to serve as the vehicle for benefit — not just using food directly but participating in a process by which food is made useful in some way. However, even with this expansion, the formulation does not obviously reach the sieve: borrowing a sieve is not benefit *from* food in any sense; it is benefit that helps one *process* food. The problem therefore remains.

### Rava Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֵימָא שֶׁלֹּא יִלְעוֹס חִיטִּין וְיִתֵּן עַל מַכָּתוֹ! אָמַר רָבָא: בְּאוֹמֵר ״הֲנָאָה הַמְּבִיאָה לִידֵי מַאֲכָלְךָ עָלַי״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Say that** the result of a vow formulated in that manner is that **he may not chew wheat** kernels belonging to the one from whose food benefit is forbidden **and place** them **on his wound,** as that is a benefit that results from food. However, that vow does not render items used in the preparation of food forbidden. **Rava said** that the mishna is referring to a case **where he says: Benefit that leads to** preparation of **your food is forbidden to me.**

The *gemara* therefore advances a broader interpretation. *Amar Rava: be'omer hana'ah hamevia lidei ma'achalcha alai* — I have forbidden myself any benefit that brings about — that leads to — food-related benefit. Accordingly, borrowing vessels that assist in the preparation of food is prohibited, because in doing so one is receiving something from the other person that ultimately translates into food benefit. The sieve helps one sift flour; the millstone grinds grain; the oven bakes bread. Each is a link in the chain from *hana'ah* to *ma'achal*. On this reading, any benefit received that constitutes a practical step toward food benefit falls within the scope of the vow.

### Monetary Savings

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ וְכׇל דָּבָר שֶׁאֵין עוֹשִׂין בּוֹ אוֹכֶל נֶפֶשׁ, מָקוֹם שֶׁמַּשְׂכִּירִין כְּיוֹצֵא בָּהֶן — אָסוּר.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: **And** with regard to **any item that one does not use in the preparation of food,** in **a place where one rents** items **of that kind,** that item is **forbidden.** Meaning, one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow is prohibited from borrowing this type of item from the one who vowed and imposed the prohibition. This is because one can use the money saved by borrowing the item rather than renting it to purchase food.

This principle — *hana'ah hamevia lidei ma'achal* — also explains why the gap between generic *hana'ah* and food *hana'ah* is so narrow in practice. A subsequent *mishnah* sheds light on this: *vechol davar she'ein osim bahem ochel nefesh, makom shemashkirin kiyotze bahem, assur* — any item not used for food preparation, if such items are normally rented out, is also forbidden. The logic is as follows: if Michael's neighbor normally rents out his donkey and Michael says to him, "Sure, go ahead, use it, no charge," then by not charging Michael, he has saved Michael money. And money saved is money that can be used to purchase food. Thus, monetary benefit — any concrete financial saving — is subsumed under food benefit, because money is the most direct path to food.

The picture that emerges is this: food benefit encompasses two categories. First, things that practically or directly contribute to the preparation or obtaining of food. Second, things that are normally rented — that is, things whose free provision saves money, which in turn can be used to acquire food. Anything that is neither used in food preparation nor normally rented — a category like cutting through one's yard or borrowing a book no one would ever charge for — remains outside the prohibition when the vow is limited to food.

This broader discussion also reflects a principle noted elsewhere in the *gemara*: the reason that the word *lo yochel* — do not eat — is understood, as Rashi explains, to encompass all forms of *issur hana'ah* is that virtually any benefit one receives can ultimately be converted into food. Food is the most fundamental category of human need, and therefore a prohibition on food benefit is inherently expansive.

## Rav Pappa Expansion

### Transportation Objects

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב פָּפָּא: שַׂק לְהָבִיא פֵּירוֹת, וַחֲמוֹר לְהָבִיא עָלָיו פֵּירוֹת, וַאֲפִילּוּ צַנָּא בְּעָלְמָא, הֲנָאָה הַמְּבִיאָה לִידֵי מַאֲכָל הוּא. בָּעֵי רַב פָּפָּא: סוּס לִרְכּוֹב עָלָיו וְטַבַּעַת לֵירָאוֹת בָּהּ, מַהוּ? מִיפְסַק וּמֵיזַל בְּאַרְעֵיהּ, מַאי?
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Pappa said:** Borrowing from him **a sack** in which **to bring produce, or a donkey upon which to bring produce, or even merely a basket,** each renders **benefit that leads to food,** and this benefit is forbidden. **Rav Pappa raised a dilemma:** If he seeks to borrow **a horse upon which to** ride **or a ring with which to be seen** when attending a feast, to create the impression that he is wealthy, **what is** the ruling? Is it prohibited to borrow these items, since having them in one's possession may indirectly result in his being served before others or being served higher-quality food; and therefore, borrowing those items provides benefit that leads to food? With regard to **traversing and walking on his land** that facilitates one's quick return home, enabling him to eat sooner, **what** is the *halakha*?

*Amar Rav Pappa*: the category of food-related benefit includes not only objects used in the preparation of food but also objects used for the transportation of food. A sack to carry fruit — *sak lavei peirot* — or a donkey to transport produce — *chamor lehavi alav peirot* — are both included. A shopping cart would be the modern equivalent. Even *tzana be'alma* — a simple basket — falls under this category if used for the transport of food, because transporting food is part of the chain of benefit that leads to *ma'achal*.

### Rav Pappa Queries

However, Rav Pappa raises a series of further questions. *Sus lirkov alav* — a horse to ride on — *ve-tabba'at lera'ot bah* — a signet ring to display — *ve-mifsaya umizal be'armaya* — and cutting through someone's yard: what is the ruling in each of these cases?

### Getting to Food

The key distinction Rav Pappa is probing is the difference between objects that bring food to me or help me prepare food, and objects or situations that bring *me* to food. These cases are not about the food itself or its preparation; they are about facilitating my journey toward food. The yard example is perhaps the clearest: if I cut through your yard in order to get to a neighbor's barbecue, that does seem to be a *hana'ah hamevia lidei ma'achal* — but it is less directly connected to the food itself and more about my movement toward it. The horse is similar: borrowing a car to get to a dinner.

### Clothing & Appearance

The signet ring is more unusual. The *rishonim* explain that if one borrows impressive clothing or a signet ring and arrives at a meal looking important and distinguished, the hosts may seat him in a better section and serve him better food. So the ring or garment, by affecting one's appearance, can indirectly determine the quality of the food one receives. Is that *hana'ah hamevia lidei ma'achal*?

### Inconclusive Proof

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּא שְׁמַע: אֲבָל מַשְׁאִיל לוֹ חָלוּק וְטַלִּית נְזָמִים וְטַבָּעוֹת. הֵיכִי דָמֵי? אִילֵּימָא שֶׁלֹּא לֵירָאוֹת בָּהֶן, צְרִיכָא לְמֵימַר? אֶלָּא לָאו, אֲפִילּוּ לֵירָאוֹת בָּהֶן, וְקָתָנֵי ״מַשְׁאִילוֹ״!
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara proposes: **Come** and **hear** a proof from the mishna: **However, he may lend him a garment, and a cloak, and nose rings, and finger rings.** The Gemara asks: **What are the circumstances** of that situation? **If we say** it is a case **where** it is **not** the borrower's intent **to be seen with them,** and therefore there is no benefit that leads to food, does this **need to be said?** The vow rendered only food forbidden. **Rather, isn't** this *halakha* stated **even** in a case where he borrowed those items **to be seen with them, and it is taught** in the mishna that **he may lend** it to **him?**

The *gemara* attempts to resolve the question from the *mishnah*'s *seifa*, which permits lending clothing, rings, and the like. But the *gemara* responds: *eilima shelo lera'ot bahen* — perhaps the *mishnah* is discussing the ordinary case where the clothing is not being used to appear important, in which case *tzricha lemeimar* — of course it is permitted, since there is no food-related dimension at all. The *mishnah* need not be addressing the specific case of borrowing clothes to look distinguished at a meal. The *gemara* concludes *lo — leolam shelo lera'ot* — the *mishnah* is indeed speaking of the ordinary case, and the *seifa* only follows naturally from the *reisha*, without intending to resolve the harder question. The question of benefits that help one *get to* food or present oneself advantageously at a meal remains unresolved.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לָא, לְעוֹלָם שֶׁלֹּא לֵירָאוֹת, וְאַיְּידֵי דְּקָתָנֵי רֵישָׁא ״לֹא יַשְׁאִילֶנּוּ״, תְּנָא סֵיפָא ״מַשְׁאִילוֹ״.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara refutes this. **No, actually** the mishna is referring to a case where he borrowed those items with the intention **not to be seen** with them. In response to the question: Is it necessary to say so, the Gemara answers that it is not necessary to teach this *halakha*. However, **since it is taught** in **the first clause: He may not lend him,** when listing the matters that may not be loaned, the *tanna* **taught the latter clause** of the mishna with a parallel formulation: **He may lend him.** Rav Pappa's dilemma remains unresolved.

## Items Normally Rented

### Monetary Substitution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ מִכְּלָל דְּרֵישָׁא, אַף עַל פִּי שֶׁאֵין מַשְׂכִּירִין. מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב אַדָּא בַּר אַהֲבָה: רַבִּי אֱלִיעֶזֶר הִיא.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara states: **By inference,** one may conclude **that the first clause** of the mishna, which states that the one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow is prohibited from deriving benefit from utensils used in the preparation of food, e.g., a sieve or a strainer, applies **even if** they are in a place **where one does not rent** items of that kind but typically lends them at no cost. The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** this mishna? **Rav Adda bar Ahava said: It is Rabbi Eliezer,** who maintains that overlooking is prohibited in the case of one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow.

The second *mishnah* explains why the practical gap between generic *hana'ah* and food *hana'ah* is so narrow: any item that is normally rented out effectively constitutes monetary benefit when lent for free, and monetary benefit translates into food benefit. This is the mechanism by which the scope of food *hana'ah* expands far beyond what one might initially expect.

### Mashkirin Concept

*Mekom shemashkirin kiyotze bahen* — in a place where such items are rented out. The *gemara* raises a question about exactly what this criterion means. Does *mashkirin kiyotze bahen* require that people regularly rent this specific item on a personal, neighborly basis? Or does it mean that when one cannot borrow the item from a friend, one would have to rent it elsewhere — so the category is defined by the availability of a commercial alternative?

### Car Rental Example

The modern equivalent clarifies the principle well. Suppose Michael cannot get food benefit from his neighbor, and the neighbor says, "Of course, borrow my car, no problem." Even if neighbors routinely lend each other cars without any thought of charging, the fact remains: had the neighbor been unable to lend it, Michael would have had to go to a rental agency and pay. By lending the car, the neighbor has saved Michael money — money that could be used to buy food. That savings constitutes *hana'ah*, even if no one would ever have dreamed of charging in this particular social context. The point is not whether this neighbor *would* have charged, but whether the object is the sort of thing that can be and is rented commercially. If so, the free loan constitutes a monetary benefit, which in turn falls under food benefit.

### Attribution to Rabbi Eliezer

The *gemara* asks *michlal de-reisha af al pi she'ein maskirin* — from the earlier clause, which deals with things like sieves and millstones, it appears that even items not normally rented are forbidden when they contribute to food preparation. This is true even though no one charges for the use of such items. *Man tanna hu?* — who holds this position? *Amar Rabba bar bar Chana: Rabbi Eliezer* — as established earlier, it is Rabbi Eliezer, consistent with his ruling that even *vittur* — things normally overlooked and not charged for — constitutes *hana'ah*. Even if no one in the world would charge to lend a sieve to a neighbor, if it contributes to food preparation it is forbidden. Perhaps the deeper principle here is: the question of whether people normally care is irrelevant. Whatever one receives from another person, even something routinely given for free, constitutes *hana'ah* in a legal sense if it falls within the prohibited domain.

## Mudar Hana'ah Paradoxes

### Three Permitted Acts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ הַמּוּדָּר הֲנָאָה מֵחֲבֵירוֹ, שׁוֹקֵל לוֹ אֶת שִׁקְלוֹ וּפוֹרֵעַ אֶת חוֹבוֹ, וּמַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת אֲבֵידָתוֹ. מָקוֹם שֶׁנּוֹטְלִין עָלֶיהָ שָׂכָר — תִּפּוֹל הֲנָאָה לַהֶקְדֵּשׁ.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: With regard to **one prohibited by vow from deriving benefit from another,** if that other person chooses, **he may contribute the half-shekel** to the Temple **on his** behalf, **and repay his debt, and return his lost item to him,** and the one prohibited from benefiting is not considered to have benefited from him. In **a place where one takes payment** for returning a lost item, that **benefit should fall into** the category of **consecrated** Temple property.

The *mishnah* now shifts to *hamudar hana'ah mechaveiro* — one who is entirely forbidden from deriving benefit from his friend — and enumerates three things that are nonetheless permitted. Each of these is, on its surface, a substantial benefit, and understanding why each is permitted requires careful analysis.

### Temple Tax

*Shokel et shiklo* — one may pay the other's *shekel* obligation to the *Beit HaMikdash*. On the surface, this appears to be a very real benefit: someone else is fulfilling my financial obligation to the Temple on my behalf. Why is this not *hana'ah*?

### Paying Off Debts

*Poreia et chovo* — one may pay off the other's debts. This is even more striking. If Michael owes a million dollars to a creditor and his neighbor pays it off, Michael's net worth has just increased by a million dollars. His liabilities column has been entirely erased. One would think this is among the most direct and significant forms of benefit imaginable. Yet the *mishnah* permits it.

### Lost Objects

*Umachzir lo et aveidato* — one may return his lost object to him. This third case is the most intuitively understandable: the finder is simply giving the owner back something that was already his. The finder performs a *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah*, and the owner receives his own property. Whatever service was rendered, in terms of what the owner actually receives, he receives only his own thing. One might wonder whether the gratitude or relief experienced constitutes *hana'ah*, but in terms of the actual transfer of property, no new benefit has been conferred.

### Payment for Return

A further nuance arises in cases where one would normally pay the finder for his time and effort — *makom she-notnu aleha sechar* — because the finder has taken time away from his regular work. In such cases, *tipol hana'ah le-hekdesh* — that *hana'ah* goes to *hekdesh*. Why must it go to *hekdesh*? The scenario is as follows: Reuven cannot get benefit from Shimon. Shimon finds Reuven's wallet and returns it — so far, so good: Reuven is only getting back his own property. Reuven wants to give Shimon twenty dollars for his trouble. Shimon says, "Forget it, keep your money." Now Reuven is in a bind: if Shimon waives the payment, and Reuven keeps the twenty dollars that he was prepared to give, he is now receiving something from Shimon — namely, Shimon's waiver of the fee. That becomes *hana'ah*. So the money must go to *tzedakah*, and Reuven cannot simply pocket it.

## Scaring Away Lion

### Creditors as Lions

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** גְּמָ׳ אַלְמָא אַבְרוֹחֵי אֲרִי בְּעָלְמָא הוּא, וּשְׁרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The mishna allowed one who vowed and imposed the prohibition to pay the financial obligations of the one who is prohibited by vow to derive benefit from him. Based on this, the Gemara concludes: **Apparently,** repaying his debts is tantamount to **merely driving away a lion** from him, **and** it is **permitted.** He is not actually giving him anything. Rather, he is preventing potential future harm. That is not considered a benefit.

The *gemara* provides a striking conceptualization of why paying off someone's debt is not considered conferring *hana'ah* upon him. *Avruchei ari be'alma hu ve-shari* — it is merely the scaring away of a lion, and therefore it is permissible. The image is of creditors as a lion bearing down on the debtor; paying them off does not give the debtor anything — it merely prevents an external force from taking something away from him. One is not providing a positive benefit; one is removing a threat. The lion metaphor is, admittedly, somewhat strained: a lion represents an undeserved, unjust attack, whereas a creditor has a legitimate claim. But the structural logic holds: one is not giving the debtor anything directly; one is pushing away an external claim. A model of *grama* — indirect causation — might have served equally well: you are not directly giving the debtor something good, but removing something bad.

### Chanan Case

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאן תַּנָּא? אָמַר רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא: זוֹ דִּבְרֵי חָנָן הִיא. רָבָא אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ תֵּימָא דִּבְרֵי הַכֹּל, גַּבֵּי מוּדָּר הֲנָאָה דְּיָהֵיב עַל מְנָת שֶׁלֹּא לִפְרוֹעַ. מַאי חָנָן? דִּתְנַן: מִי שֶׁהָלַךְ לִמְדִינַת הַיָּם, וְעָמַד אֶחָד וּפִירְנֵס אֶת אִשְׁתּוֹ. חָנָן אָמַר: אִיבֵּד אֶת מְעוֹתָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Who** is the *tanna* who **taught** that in performing the actions listed in the mishna one is merely preventing harm? **Rav Hoshaya said: This is the statement of Ḥanan** in a dispute pertaining to one who pays the debt of another. Ḥanan holds that he cannot demand to be reimbursed for that payment, since he merely prevented potential damage. **Rava said: Even if you say** that **everyone agrees** that this is the *halakha*, it was stated **with regard to one prohibited by vow from deriving benefit from another** who borrowed money, and the creditor stipulated **that** it was **on the condition that** if he so chooses **he** does **not** need **to repay** the loan. **What** is the opinion of **Ḥanan?** The Gemara answers that it is **as we learned** in a mishna: In the case of a husband **who went to a country overseas, and one** other man **arose and supported his wife** on his own initiative and then demanded to be reimbursed for that support when the husband returned, **Ḥanan said:** The one who took the initiative to support the wife **lost his money,** since the husband neither asked him to do so nor committed to compensate him.

*Amar Rabbi Oshaya: zu divrei Chanan* — this ruling reflects the opinion of Chanan. The *gemara* identifies the position with Chanan, a figure whose ruling appears in a *mishnah* in Ketubot. The case: a man went on a business trip without making any financial provision for his wife. A neighbor — Chanan — stepped in and provided money so the wife could purchase food, then sought reimbursement from the husband upon his return. The husband refused. Chanan ruled: *ibbad et me'otav* — the neighbor lost his money. Because no explicit stipulation was made with the husband, the neighbor was simply paying off the husband's creditor — his wife, who was owed maintenance. Since there was no agreement for repayment, and since paying off a creditor does not constitute concretely giving anything to the debtor, the neighbor cannot collect. The *benei kohanim gedolim* disagreed: *yishava kamma hotzi ve-yitol* — the neighbor may take an oath as to what he spent and collect accordingly. This is the debate about *mavriach ari* — whether removing a creditor's claim is or is not considered a genuine benefit to the debtor.

### Rava's Caveat

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** נֶחְלְקוּ עָלָיו בְּנֵי כֹּהֲנִים גְּדוֹלִים, וְאָמְרוּ: יִשָּׁבַע כַּמָּה הוֹצִיא, וְיִטּוֹל. אָמַר רַבִּי דּוֹסָא בֶּן הַרְכִּינָס כְּדִבְרֵיהֶם. אָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן בֶּן זַכַּאי: יָפֶה אָמַר חָנָן, הִנִּיחַ מְעוֹתָיו עַל קֶרֶן הַצְּבִי.
>
> **Translation:** **The sons of High Priests disagreed with him and said:** The one who took the initiative to support his wife **will take an oath** as to **how much he spent and take** repayment from the husband. **Rabbi Dosa ben Harkinas said in accordance with** the **statement** of the sons of High Priests. **Rabbi Yoḥanan ben Zakkai said: Ḥanan spoke well,** as in any case of this type **he placed his money on the antler of a deer,** i.e., a risky venture with no guaranteed return.

*Rava amar: afilu teima divrei hakol* — Rava argues that even the Rabbis who disagree with Chanan and hold that the neighbor can collect in that case would nonetheless agree that the *mishnah* here is permissible. He explains: *de-yahiv al menat shelo lifro'a* — the case in the *mishnah* involves a debt structured such that repayment could never be legally enforced. It is a loan in the sense that there is a personal moral obligation, but the creditor explicitly stipulated that he would never demand repayment in *beit din*. In such a case, even if paying off a real, enforceable debt would constitute *hana'ah*, paying off a non-enforceable debt would not — because it never appeared in the debtor's liabilities column in any legally meaningful sense. Shimon's payment freed Reuven from a moral weight, perhaps from social embarrassment, but never from any real financial liability. Accordingly, even the Rabbis would agree it is not *hana'ah*. This reading, however, is an *okimta* — a forced narrowing of the *mishnah* to a special case — and does not represent the straightforward *pshat* of the text.

### Deer's Horn Metaphor

*Amar Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas ke-divrehem* — Rabbi Dosa ben Horkinas sided with the *benei kohanim gedolim* that the neighbor can collect. *Amar Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai: yafeh amar Chanan* — Rabbi Yochanan ben Zakkai sided with Chanan. *Henniach me'otav al keren ha-tzvi* — the neighbor placed his money on the horn of a deer; he flushed it away. Since no stipulation was made with the husband, and since the act of paying a creditor is not seen as directly giving anything to the debtor, the neighbor cannot claim reimbursement. And by the same logic, it would not be prohibited by *issur hana'ah*.

### Rav Hoshaya Response

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רָבָא לָא אָמַר כְּרַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא, דְּקָא מוֹקֵים לָהּ לְמַתְנִיתִין כְּדִבְרֵי הַכֹּל. רַב הוֹשַׁעְיָא לָא אָמַר כְּרָבָא, גְּזֵירָה שֶׁלֹּא לִיפָּרַע מִשּׁוּם לִיפָּרַע.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains the dispute between Rava and Rav Hoshaya with regard to attribution of the mishna: **Rava did not say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of Ḥanan, **as did Rav Hoshaya,** and he preferred a different explanation, **as he establishes the mishna in accordance with the statements** upon which **everyone** agrees, rather than attributing it to an individual *tanna*. **Rav Hoshaya did not say** that the mishna is in accordance with the opinion of all the *tanna'im* **as did Rava,** as there is basis to issue a rabbinic **decree** prohibiting repayment of a loan for one for whom benefit from another is forbidden by vow **on the condition that he** does **not** need **to repay** the loan, **due to** a standard loan that he is required **to repay.** Therefore, he prefers to establish the mishna in accordance with the opinion of Ḥanan.

*Rava lo amar ke-Rav Hoshaya* — Rava did not follow Rav Hoshaya's approach. Rava preferred to read the *mishnah* as acceptable to all opinions — both Chanan and the Rabbis — by limiting it to the special case of a non-enforceable debt. Rav Hoshaya, on the other hand, would not accept this *okimta*, for two reasons. First, it requires an uncomfortable narrowing of the *mishnah*. Second, and more importantly: *gezerah shelo lifro'a mishum lifro'a* — even if we were to say that paying off a non-enforceable debt is not *hana'ah*, if we permit Shimon to pay off Reuven's non-enforceable debts, people will come to permit him to pay off Reuven's real, enforceable debts as well. As a protective *gezerah*, one would prohibit even the marginal case to prevent violation of the clearer case. Rav Hoshaya therefore maintains that the *mishnah* follows Chanan: paying off any creditor, real or nominal, is simply *mavriach ari*, and that is why it is permitted.

The *pshat* of the *mishnah* clearly aligns with Rav Hoshaya: Chanan's position is that *mavriach ari* is not *hana'ah*, and the *mishnah* can be read straightforwardly on that basis without requiring any special limiting case.

## Returning Lost Objects

### Rav Ami & Rav Assi

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַחֲזִיר לוֹ אֶת אֲבֵידָתוֹ. פְּלִיגִי בַּהּ רַבִּי אַמֵּי וְרַבִּי אַסִּי, חַד אָמַר: לֹא שָׁנוּ אֶלָּא בְּשֶׁנִּכְסֵי מַחְזִיר אֲסוּרִין עַל בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה, דְּכִי מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ — מִידַּעַם דְּנַפְשֵׁיהּ קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ. אֲבָל נִכְסֵי בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה אֲסוּרִין עַל מַחְזִיר — לָא קָא מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, דְּקָא מְהַנֵּי לֵיהּ פְּרוּטָה דְּרַב יוֹסֵף.
>
> **Translation:** § We learned in the mishna: **He returns his lost item to him. Rabbi Ami and Rabbi Asi disagree about this. One said: They taught** this **only** in a case **where the property of the one returning** the lost item **is forbidden to the owner of the lost item, as when he returns** it **to him he is returning** to him **something of his own** and is not giving him anything new. Consequently, returning a lost item in no way violates the vow. **However,** in a case **where the property of the owner of the lost item is forbidden to the one returning** the lost item, **he may not return** it **to him,** as in that case the owner indirectly **benefits** the one returning the lost item by enabling **him** to acquire the ***peruta* of Rav Yosef.** Rav Yosef said that the legal status of one tending to the return of a lost item is like that of a paid bailee. Since one who is engaged in a mitzva is exempt from performing another mitzva, while he is tending to the lost item he is exempt from giving charity to a pauper. Since the one returning the lost item profits from engaging in the return of the lost item, it is prohibited for him to do so, as he is prohibited by vow from deriving benefit from the owner of the lost item.

The *gemara* now addresses the third case in the *mishnah* — *machzir et aveidato*, returning a lost object — and reports a debate between Rav Ami and Rav Assi.

### Own Property Doctrine

*Chad amar: lo shanu ela be-she-nichsei machazir asurin al ba'al aveidah* — one opinion holds that the *mishnah*'s permission for the finder to return the lost object applies only when the finder's property is what is forbidden to the owner. That is, the owner cannot get benefit from the finder. In that case, *d'chi mahadar lei midam d'nafshei ka-mahadar lei* — when the finder returns the object, he is simply returning to the owner something that was already his own property. No new benefit passes from finder to owner; the owner merely receives back what was already his.

### Irony of Prohibition

*Aval nichsei ba'al aveidah asurin al machazir, lo ka-mahadar lei* — but here is the irony. What if the prohibition runs in the other direction: Shimon cannot get benefit from Reuven, and it is Shimon who has found Reuven's object? One would intuitively think this is entirely unproblematic — Shimon is giving *to* Reuven; Shimon is not getting anything. Why would that be a problem? The answer is surprising.

### Pruta of Rav Yosef

*De-ka-mahanei lei pruta de-Rav Yosef* — because Shimon is in fact getting benefit from Reuven, in the form of the *prutah* of Rav Yosef. The background: in Bava Metzia, there is a question about what level of *shomer* — guardian — the finder of a lost object is considered. Ordinarily one might say *shomer chinam* — an unpaid watchman — since no one is paying him to hold it. Rav Yosef, however, ruled that the finder is a *shomer sachar* — a paid watchman with a higher level of liability. The reason is that *osek be-mitzvah patur min ha-mitzvah* — one who is engaged in performing a *mitzvah* is exempt from other *mitzvos* that present themselves in the meantime. Therefore, if a *schnorrer* — a collector for charity — were to approach Shimon while Shimon is in the process of returning the lost object, Shimon would be exempt from giving *tzedakah* in that moment. That exemption — that saved dollar — is the *prutah* of Rav Yosef. It is a concrete benefit Shimon receives in the course of fulfilling the *mitzvah* of *hashavat aveidah*. And since it is Reuven's object that creates the occasion for this benefit, Shimon is, in effect, deriving benefit from Reuven.

The striking result: paying off a million-dollar debt is considered *mavriach ari* and not *hana'ah*, because the benefit is indirect. But the theoretical possibility of saving a single dollar in charity is considered *hana'ah* — because it is a concrete, direct benefit that accrues to the finder in the course of handling the owner's object. The contrast is remarkable, and it speaks to the precision with which the *gemara* defines *hana'ah*: not by magnitude, but by directness.

### Rarity of Benefit

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְחַד אָמַר: אֲפִילּוּ נִכְסֵי בַּעַל אֲבֵידָה אֲסוּרִין עַל מַחְזִיר מַהְדַּר לֵיהּ, וּמִשּׁוּם פְּרוּטָה דְּרַב יוֹסֵף לָא שְׁכִיחַ.
>
> **Translation:** **And one said: Even** in a case **where the property of the owner of the lost item is forbidden to the one returning** the lost item, **he returns** it to him. **And** with regard to the concern **due to** the ***peruta* of Rav Yosef,** it is not a concern because it is **uncommon** for a pauper to happen upon a person just when he is tending to the lost item. Therefore, it cannot be said that there is profit in the return of a lost item.

The second opinion in the debate — *ve-chad amar afilu nichsei ba'al aveidah asurin al ha-machzir, mahadar lei* — holds that even when Shimon cannot get benefit from Reuven, Shimon may still return the lost object. *U-mishum pruta de-Rav Yosef lo shechiach* — the scenario of the *prutah* of Rav Yosef is simply too rare to take into account. The likelihood that a *tzedakah* collector will actually appear while Shimon is in the process of returning the object is low enough that we do not treat the act of returning the *aveidah* as inherently one that generates benefit. If it happens that a collector does appear and Shimon saves a dollar, that is a contingent outcome of a rare event — not a defining characteristic of the act itself. And therefore, in principle, returning the *aveidah* is not considered an act by which Shimon derives *hana'ah* from Reuven, and it remains permissible.
