# Sanhedrin 21 — Daf Yomi Shiur

## Solomon's Declining Power

### Reish Lakish Overview

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רֵישׁ לָקִישׁ: בַּתְּחִילָּה מָלַךְ שְׁלֹמֹה עַל הָעֶלְיוֹנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וַיֵּשֶׁב שְׁלֹמֹה עַל כִּסֵּא ה׳", וּלְבַסּוֹף מָלַךְ עַל הַתַּחְתּוֹנִים, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי הוּא רֹדֶה בְּכׇל עֵבֶר הַנָּהָר מִתִּפְסַח וְעַד עַזָּה".
>
> **Translation:** **Reish Lakish says: Initially, Solomon ruled** even **over the supernal** worlds, **as it is stated: "Then Solomon sat on the throne of the Lord"** (I Chronicles 29:23), indicating that his kingdom was like the Lord's kingdom, reigning over all worlds. **But ultimately,** after he married foreign wives, **he ruled over** only **the lower** worlds, **as it is stated: "For he had dominion over all the region on this side of the river, from Tiphsah even to Gaza"** (I Kings 5:4).

The daf opens with a teaching from Reish Lakish tracing the gradual diminishment of King Solomon's authority. The tradition he cites follows an arc of progressive contraction, beginning at the heights of cosmic dominion and ending in near-total loss.

### Heavenly to Earthly Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר, "תִּפְסַח בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם וְעַזָּה בְּסוֹף הָעוֹלָם", וְחַד אָמַר, "תִּפְסַח וְעַזָּה בַּהֲדֵי הֲדָדֵי הֲווֹ יָתְבִי", וּכְשֵׁם שֶׁמָּלַךְ עַל תִּפְסַח וְעַל עַזָּה כָּךְ מָלַךְ עַל כׇּל הָעוֹלָם כּוּלּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed with regard to the meaning of this verse: **One says** that **Tiphsah** is a name of a place **at the end of the world,** at one end of Solomon's kingdom, **and Gaza is at the** other **end of the world. And one says** that **Tiphsah and Gaza sat next to each other, and** the verse serves to teach that **just as he ruled over Tiphsah and over Gaza, so he ruled over the whole world.**

Rav and Shmuel each expound upon this same point while arriving at it differently. One holds that Tifsach and Azah were at opposite ends of the world — *sofa olam* — so that ruling from one to the other means ruling over everything between. The other holds that Tifsach and Azah were situated adjacent to one another, *bahadei hadadi havu yatevei*, and that ruling over both therefore implies ruling over all that lies in every direction around them — analogous to the formulation *me-Hodu ve-ad Kush*. Either way, the conclusion is that Solomon had once ruled over the entire world.

### Israel to Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבַסּוֹף לֹא מָלַךְ אֶלָּא עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "אֲנִי קֹהֶלֶת הָיִיתִי מֶלֶךְ עַל יִשְׂרָאֵל וְגוֹ'". וּלְבַסּוֹף לֹא מָלַךְ אֶלָּא עַל יְרוּשָׁלַיִם, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "דִּבְרֵי קֹהֶלֶת בֶּן דָּוִד מֶלֶךְ בִּירוּשָׁלִָים". וּלְבַסּוֹף, לֹא מָלַךְ אֶלָּא עַל מִטָּתוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "הִנֵּה מִטָּתוֹ שֶׁלִּשְׁלֹמֹה וְגוֹ'".
>
> **Translation:** Reish Lakish continues: **And ultimately,** Solomon declined further still in that **he ruled only over Israel, as it is stated: "I, Koheleth, have been king over Israel** in Jerusalem" (Ecclesiastes 1:12). **And ultimately, he ruled over only Jerusalem, as it is stated: "The words of Koheleth, the son of David, king in Jerusalem"** (Ecclesiastes 1:1). **And ultimately, he ruled over only his bed, as it is stated: "Behold, it is the bed of Solomon;** threescore mighty men are about it, of the mighty men of Israel" (Song of Songs 3:7).

From there the decline continues. *U-levasof malach ela al Yisrael* — ultimately he reigned only over Israel, as the verse says, *ani Kohelet hayiti melech al Yisrael*. The identification of Kohelet with Solomon is assumed throughout. And then further still: *u-levasof malach ela al Yerushalayim* — he reigned only over Jerusalem, as it is written, *Divrei Kohelet ben David melech bi-Yerushalayim*.

### Throne to Staff

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּלְבַסּוֹף לֹא מָלַךְ אֶלָּא עַל מַקְלוֹ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "זֶה הָיָה חֶלְקִי מִכׇּל עֲמָלִי". רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר מַקְלוֹ, וְחַד אָמַר גּוּנְדּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **And ultimately,** he declined so much that **he ruled over only his staff, as it is stated: "And this was my portion from all of my labor"** (Ecclesiastes 2:10). **Rav and Shmuel** disagreed with regard to the meaning of this latter verse as well. **One says** that the term "this" is a reference to **his staff, and one says** that it is a reference to **his cloak.**

The contraction does not stop at geography. *U-levasof malach ela al mitato* — in the end, all Solomon had dominion over was his own bed, as it is written, *hinei mitato sheli-Shlomo*. And at the very last stage: *u-levasof malach ela al maklo* — he held authority over nothing but his staff, derived from the verse *ze hayah chelki mikol amali* — this is all I had from all my toil. The connection between *ze* and a staff is that *ze* implies something one can physically point to or hold. Rav and Shmuel dispute what exactly this refers to: one says *maklo*, his staff; the other says *gundo*, his cloak. In either interpretation, the king had been reduced to virtually nothing. This episode is connected in a note to the story of *Ashmedai*, the demon king, who assumed Solomon's form and expelled him from his throne — a narrative recalled from tractate *Gittin*.

### Restoration Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הֲדַר אוֹ לָא הֲדַר? רַב וּשְׁמוּאֵל: חַד אָמַר הֲדַר, וְחַד אָמַר לָא הֲדַר. מַאן דְּאָמַר לָא הֲדַר – מֶלֶךְ וְהֶדְיוֹט, וּמַאן דְּאָמַר הֲדַר – מֶלֶךְ וְהֶדְיוֹט וָמֶלֶךְ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **Did he return** to reign over the whole world, **or did he** ultimately **not return? Rav and Shmuel** disagreed about this: **One says** that **he returned, and one says** that **he did not return. The one who says** that **he did not return** reasons that Solomon was first **a king and** then **an ordinary** person **[*hedyot*]** and did not return to his reign; **and the one who says** that **he returned** reasons that Solomon was first **a king and** then **an ordinary** person **and** ultimately returned to be **a king.**

The Gemara then raises the question *hadar lo hadar* — did Solomon ultimately recover his former stature? Rav and Shmuel again divide: one says *hadar*, he was restored; the other says *lo hadar*, he was not. According to the view that he was not restored: *malach ve-hediyot* — he was a king who became a commoner. According to the view that he was restored: *malach ve-hediyot u-malach* — king, commoner, and king again.

## Royal Spoils & Treasure

### Treasure Distribution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תָּנוּ רַבָּנַן: אוֹצְרוֹת מְלָכִים – לַמֶּלֶךְ, וּשְׁאָר בִּיזָּה שֶׁבּוֹזְזִין – מֶחֱצָה לַמֶּלֶךְ וּמֶחֱצָה לָעָם.
>
> **Translation:** **The Sages taught** in a *baraita*: **Treasures of kings** taken as spoils in war belong **to the king, and** as for **the rest of the spoils that are taken** in a war, **half** is **for the king and half** is **for the people.**

The Gemara proceeds to discuss the king's portion of war booty. A *baraita* teaches: *otzrot melachim la-melech* — the treasure houses of conquered kings go to the victorious king. *Ve-shear biza shebozezin, mechetza la-melech u-mechetza la-am* — and the remainder of all spoils taken are split equally between the king and the people. Being the winning king, in short, is extremely lucrative.

### Abaye's Question

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֲמַר לֵיהּ אַבָּיֵי לְרַב דִּימִי, וְאָמְרִי לַהּ לְרַב אַחָא: בִּשְׁלָמָא אוֹצְרוֹת מְלָכִים לַמֶּלֶךְ – אוֹרְחָא דְמִלְּתָא, אֶלָּא שְׁאָר בִּיזָּה שֶׁבּוֹזְזִין מֶחֱצָה לַמֶּלֶךְ וּמֶחֱצָה לָעָם – מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּמְשְׁחוּ לַה׳ לְנָגִיד וּלְצָדוֹק"
>
> **Translation:** **Abaye said to Rav Dimi, and some say** that he said this **to Rav Aḥa: Granted, treasures of kings** belong **to the king.** This is **the typical manner** of kings, as it is fitting that the king should use the treasures of the kings he conquers; **but** with regard to **the rest of the spoils that are taken** in a war, where **half** is **for the king and half** is **for the people, from where do we** derive this *halakha*? He answered Abaye: The source is **as it is written: "And they anointed Solomon unto the Lord to be leader, and Tzadok** to be priest" (I Chronicles 29:22).

Abaye raises a challenge to Rav Dimi (or, in another version of the text, to Rav Acha): *bishlama otzrot melachim la-melech, orcha de-milta hi* — it is understandable that the conquered king's treasury goes to the victor, for that is simply the way of things. But from where do we derive that even the remaining general spoils are split with the king getting half? The source, the Gemara answers, comes from a verse: *vayimshchu l-Hashem le-nagid u-le-Tzadok la-kohen* — they anointed Solomon as ruler and Tzadok as *kohen*.

### Solomon-Tzadok Parallel

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַקִּישׁ נָגִיד לְצָדוֹק: מָה צָדוֹק – מֶחֱצָה לוֹ וּמֶחֱצָה לְאֶחָיו, אַף נָגִיד – מֶחֱצָה לוֹ וּמֶחֱצָה לְאֶחָיו.
>
> **Translation:** This verse **compares a leader,** referring to King Solomon, **to Tzadok,** the High Priest: **Just as** for **Tzadok, half** of the shewbread given to the priests is **for him,** the High Priest, **and half** is **for** all **his brothers,** the other priests, **so too** for **the leader, half** is **for him and half** is **for his brothers,** the rest of the army.

From this juxtaposition, a *hekkesh* is drawn: the king (*nagid*) is compared to Tzadok the *kohen*. *Makish nagid le-Tzadok* — just as Tzadok the *kohen* receives half of something, so too the king receives half.

### Priestly Share Source

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְצָדוֹק גּוּפֵיהּ מְנָלַן? דְּתַנְיָא: רַבִּי אוֹמֵר: "וְהָיְתָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּלְבָנָיו" – מֶחֱצָה לְאַהֲרֹן וּמֶחֱצָה לְבָנָיו.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **And** with regard to **Tzadok himself, from where do we** derive that the High Priest takes half? The Gemara responds: **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: **Rabbi** Yehuda HaNasi **says:** The Torah states with regard to the apportionment of the shewbread: **"And it shall be for Aaron and his sons,** and they shall eat it in a holy place" (Leviticus 24:9), from which it is inferred: **Half for Aaron and half for his sons.**

The Gemara then asks: *ve-Tzadok gufei mena lan* — but from where do we know that the *kohen* gets half? The answer comes from a *baraita* in the name of Rabbi: when the Torah speaks of the *lechem ha-panim* and says it shall be *le-Aharon u-le-vanav* — for Aaron and his sons — this is not simply referring collectively to the *kohanim*. Rather, Aaron himself receives half and his sons collectively receive the other half. This principle is then extended more broadly.

A parallel is noted from *Matot*, where the spoils of the war against Midyan were divided between the fighters and the rest of the nation, with the fighters contributing one out of five hundred to Elazar and the people contributing one out of fifty — far from a half-and-half split. No king existed at the time; the role was filled by Yehoshua. The proportions there are quite different, and the Gemara does not draw on that precedent for the present *halacha*.

## Royal Wives Limit

### Eighteen Wives Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְנִי׳ ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים״ אֶלָּא שְׁמֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה. רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מַרְבֶּה הוּא לוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מְסִירוֹת אֶת לִבּוֹ. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת וּמְסִירָה אֶת לִבּוֹ – הֲרֵי זֶה לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. אִם כֵּן, לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר ״לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים״? דַּאֲפִילּוּ כַּאֲבִיגַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** MISHNA: The king **"shall not add many wives for himself"** (Deuteronomy 17:17), **but** only **eighteen. Rabbi Yehuda says: He** may **add many** wives **for himself, provided that they are not** like those who **turn his heart** away from reverence for God. **Rabbi Shimon says: Even** if he wants to marry only **one** wife, if **she turns his heart away, he should not marry her. If so, why is it stated: "He shall not add many wives for himself"?** This teaches **that even** if his wives are **like Abigail,** who was righteous and prevented David from sin (see I Samuel, chapter 25), it is prohibited for him to have many wives.

The *mishnah* states: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ela shemoneh esrei* — the king may not have more than eighteen wives.

Before addressing the *machloket* between Rabbi Yehudah and Rabbi Shimon, the broader *parashah* of *Shoftim* is examined, as it provides the scriptural grounding for the entire discussion. The relevant passage in Devarim 17:14 begins with the people anticipating their entry into *Eretz Yisrael* and saying *asim alai melech ke-chol ha-goyim asher svivotai* — let me appoint a king like all the nations around me. This phrasing is notable because it echoes the complaint lodged against the people's request for a king elsewhere — that they asked *ke-chol ha-goyim*, which was seen as the wrong framing. Yet the Torah itself uses this formulation here, which leaves the question of whether kingship is ideal or merely permitted as a genuine *machloket*.

The passage continues: *som tasim alecha melech* — you shall appoint a king over yourself — which the Gemara reads as requiring that the king's authority be felt, that one hold him in genuine awe. *Mikerev achecha tasim alecha melech* — he must be from among your brethren; *lo tuchal latet alecha ish nochri asher lo achecha hu* — a foreigner cannot serve as king, which is the root of the problem with Hordus, who was a slave. The sages of that era were criticized for flattering him — being *machnif* — when they should not have been.

The passage then lists restrictions: *rak lo yarbeh lo susim* — he may not accumulate too many horses — and this prohibition extends to bringing the people back to Egypt to acquire them. The verse adds: *ve-lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-lo yasur levavo* — he should not have too many wives so that his heart not be led astray — *ve-chesef ve-zahav lo yarbeh lo me'od* — and he should not accumulate excessive silver and gold. The verse provides a reason for the horses restriction (not returning to Egypt), and a reason for the wives restriction (*ve-lo yasur levavo*), but states the money restriction without any stated rationale — presumably because the reason is self-evident.

The verse that anchors the number eighteen is the passage from Shmuel 2, chapter 12, where the prophet Nathan, rebuking David over Batsheva, conveys God's words: *va-etna lecha et beit adonecha ve-et neshei adonecha be-cheikecha* — I gave you the house of your master and the wives of your master — and adds: *ve-im m'at ve-osifa lach kahena ve-kahena* — and if that was too little, I would have given you more, *kahena ve-kahena*, here and here. Since David at that point had six wives — born to him in Chevron as enumerated in Shmuel 2, chapter 3 — each *kahena* is taken to equal six. Six plus six plus six equals eighteen.

### Yehudah: Conditional

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי יְהוּדָה אוֹמֵר: מַרְבֶּה הוּא לוֹ, וּבִלְבַד שֶׁלֹּא יְהוּ מְסִירוֹת לִבּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Yehuda says: He** may **add many** wives **for himself, provided that they are not** like those who **turn his heart astray.**

Rabbi Yehudah holds that the king *may* have more than eighteen wives, provided that they do not turn his heart astray: *marbe hu lo u-vilvad she-lo yehu mesirot libo*. In his view, the prohibition is triggered not by the number but by the consequence.

### Shimon: No-Astray Rule

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת וּמְסִירָה אֶת לִבּוֹ הֲרֵי זוּ לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. אִם כֵּן לָמָּה נֶאֱמַר וְלֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים? דַּאֲפִילּוּ כַּאֲבִיגַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** **Rabbi Shimon says: Even** if he wants to marry only **one** wife, if **she turns his heart away, he should not marry her. If so, what does the Torah add by saying he should not *multiply* wives? **That even** if they are all as righteous as Avigayil, having too many is still prohibited.**

Rabbi Shimon takes the opposing position: *afilu achat u-mesira et libo harei zu lo yisana* — even one wife who will turn his heart astray, he may not marry. *Im ken lama ne'emar ve-lo yarbeh lo nashim* — if so, what does the Torah add by saying he should not *multiply* wives? *D'afilu ke-Avigayil* — even if they are all as righteous as Avigayil, having too many is still prohibited.

### Dispute Resolution

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** לְמֵימְרָא דְּרַבִּי יְהוּדָה דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִּקְרָא, וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן לָא דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִּקְרָא? וְהָא אִיפְּכָא שָׁמְעִינַן לְהוּ!
>
> **Translation:** GEMARA: The Gemara asks: **Is this to say that Rabbi Yehuda interprets the rationale** behind the mitzva **in the verse** and draws halakhic conclusions based on that interpretation, **and Rabbi Shimon does not interpret the rationale in the verse? But didn't we hear them** hold the **opposite** opinions with regard to interpreting the rationale behind a mitzva in a verse?

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** דְּתַנְיָא: אַלְמָנָה, בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲנִיָּה בֵּין שֶׁהִיא עֲשִׁירָה, אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "לֹא תַחֲבֹל בֶּגֶד אַלְמָנָה" – דִּבְרֵי רַבִּי יְהוּדָה. רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אוֹמֵר: עֲשִׁירָה – מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ, עֲנִיָּיה – אֵין מְמַשְׁכְּנִין אוֹתָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** **As it is taught** in a *baraita*: In the case of **a widow, whether she is poor or whether she is wealthy, one may not take collateral from her** for a loan, **as it is stated: "You may not take the garment of a widow for a pledge"** (Deuteronomy 24:17); this is **the statement of Rabbi Yehuda. Rabbi Shimon says:** In the case of **a wealthy** widow, one may **take collateral from her.** But in the case of **a poor** widow, **one may not take collateral from her.**

The Gemara asks: does this mean Rabbi Yehudah is the one who *darish ta'ama de-kra* — who interprets the law through its underlying reason — while Rabbi Shimon does not? This appears to invert the usual alignment, since Rabbi Shimon is famously associated with *derisha ta'ama de-kra* elsewhere. The classic example is the law of *lo tachbol beged almana* — one may not take a cloak as collateral from a widow. Rabbi Yehudah holds that this applies whether the widow is rich or poor — he does not look to the reason and applies the law categorically. Rabbi Shimon holds that a rich widow's cloak *may* be taken as collateral, since the reason for the prohibition — that she is poor and will be embarrassed going to retrieve it from her creditor — does not apply to a wealthy woman. Rabbi Shimon elaborates: if one does take a *mashkon* from a poor widow, she will inevitably return to retrieve it, and her neighbors will draw unseemly conclusions about why she is constantly visiting a man's home, damaging her reputation. For a rich widow, this dynamic does not arise. Thus Rabbi Shimon *is* the one who reasons from *ta'ama de-kra*, and Rabbi Yehudah is not.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** בְּעָלְמָא, רַבִּי יְהוּדָה לָא דָּרֵישׁ טַעְמָא דִּקְרָא, וְשָׁאנֵי הָכָא דִּמְפָרֵשׁ טַעְמָא דִקְרָא: מַה טַּעַם "לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים"? מִשּׁוּם דְּ"לֹא יָסוּר לְבָבוֹ".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara explains: **Generally, Rabbi Yehuda does not interpret the rationale in the verse, but it is different here, as** the verse itself **explains the rationale in the verse: What is the reason** that **"he shall not add many wives for himself"?** He should not add many wives **because of** what is articulated in the continuation of the verse: **"His heart should not turn."**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְרַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן אָמַר לָךְ: מִכְּדִי בְּעָלְמָא דָּרְשִׁינַן טַעְמָא דִּקְרָא, אִם כֵּן לִכְתּוֹב קְרָא "לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים" וְלִישְׁתּוֹק, וַאֲנָא אָמֵינָא: מָה טַעַם "לֹא יַרְבֶּה"? מִשּׁוּם דְּ"לֹא יָסוּר". "לֹא יָסוּר" לְמָה לִי? אֲפִילּוּ אַחַת וּמְסִירָה אֶת לִבּוֹ – הֲרֵי זוֹ לֹא יִשָּׂאֶנָּה. אֶלָּא מָה אֲנִי מְקַיֵּים "לֹא יַרְבֶּה"? דַּאֲפִילּוּ כַּאֲבִיגַיִל.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rabbi Shimon** could have **said to you: Since generally we interpret the rationale in the verse,** then **if** it is **so** that the prohibition against marrying many wives applies only to wives that are likely to lead his heart astray, **the verse should write** only: **"He shall not add many wives for himself" and** then **be silent.** In that case, **I would say,** on my own, **what is the reason** that **he shall not add many** wives? It is **so that** his heart **should not turn away.** Accordingly, **why do I** need the additional phrase "his heart **should not turn away"?** It serves to teach another *halakha*, that **even** if he wants to marry only **one** wife, if **she turns his heart away, he should not marry her. But** then **how do I realize** the meaning of the verse: **"He shall not add many** wives for himself"? It means **that** he should not have many wives **even** if they are **like Abigail.**

How, then, do we reconcile the reversal apparent in the king's wives case? The answer given is: *shani hacha de-mefaresh ta'ama de-kra* — this case is different because the Torah itself explicitly states the reason. In the widow case, no reason is given, so Rabbi Shimon infers one. Here, the Torah openly states *ve-lo yasur levavo*. Given that explicit reason, Rabbi Yehudah naturally incorporates it into his ruling: so long as the wives will not turn the king's heart astray, the prohibition does not apply. Rabbi Shimon, for his part, says: precisely because I generally derive reasons myself, I know that if the Torah had intended to convey only this principle it could simply have written *lo yarbeh lo nashim* and remained silent — *ve-lishtok*. I would have inferred the reason myself. Since the Torah went out of its way to add *ve-lo yasur levavo*, it must be teaching something beyond what I would have derived on my own: namely, that even one wife who will lead him astray is forbidden. And since that is the lesson of the added phrase, the main prohibition of *lo yarbeh* must teach something distinct: *afilu ke-Avigayil* — even if all the wives are righteous, exceeding the number is still prohibited.

### Kahena Calculation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** הָנֵי שְׁמוֹנֶה עֶשְׂרֵה מְנָלַן? דִּכְתִיב: "וַיִּוָּלְדוּ לְדָוִד בָּנִים בְּחֶבְרוֹן וַיְהִי בְכוֹרוֹ אַמְנוֹן לַאֲחִינֹעַם הַיִּזְרְעֵאלִית. וּמִשְׁנֵהוּ כִלְאָב לַאֲבִיגַיִל אֵשֶׁת נָבָל הַכַּרְמְלִי וְהַשְּׁלִישִׁי אַבְשָׁלוֹם בֶּן מַעֲכָה ... וְהָרְבִיעִי אֲדֹנִיָּה בֶן חַגִּית וְהַחֲמִישִׁי שְׁפַטְיָה בֶן אֲבִיטָל. וְהַשִּׁשִּׁי יִתְרְעָם לְעֶגְלָה אֵשֶׁת דָּוִד אֵלֶּה יֻלְּדוּ לְדָוִד בְּחֶבְרוֹן".
>
> **Translation:** § As for **those eighteen** women that the king may marry, **from where do we** derive that number? The Gemara responds: **As it is written: "And to David sons were born in Hebron; and his firstborn was Amnon, from Ahinoam the Jezreelitess; and his second, Chileab, from Abigail the wife of Nabal the Carmelite; and the third, Absalom, son of Maacah,** the daughter of Talmai, king of Geshur; **and the fourth, Adonijah, son of Haggith; and the fifth, Shephatiah, son of Abital; and the sixth, Ithream, of Eglah, David's wife. These were born to David in Hebron"** (II Samuel 3:2–5). In these verses, a total of six wives are mentioned.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְקָאָמַר לֵיהּ נָבִיא: "אִם מְעַט, וְאֹסִיפָה לְּךָ כָּהֵנָּה וְכָהֵנָּה". "כָּהֵנָּה" – שֵׁית, "וְכָהֵנָּה" – שֵׁית, דְּהָווּ לְהוּ תַּמְנֵי סְרֵי.
>
> **Translation:** And the **prophet** Nathan **said to** King David in his rebuke: "And I gave you your master's house and your master's wives into your bosom and I gave you the house of Israel and Judah; and **if that were too little, then I would add to you like these and like those"** (II Samuel 12:8). **"Like these";** this is referring to the wives enumerated above, meaning another **six. "And like those";** this is referring to another **six,** so **that** all together there **are eighteen** he would be permitted to marry.

The derivation of eighteen from the *kahena ve-kahena* formula is that since David had six wives at the time of Nathan's rebuke, each *kahena* equals six. The *tanna de-didan* holds that the two instances of *kahena* each add six, bringing the total to 6 + 6 + 6 = 18. This is supported by a principle that the second *kahena* is linked to the first: *makish kaheina basra le-kaheina kamma, mah kaheina kamma sheis af kaheina basra sheis*.

### Higher Wife Counts

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַתְקֵיף לַהּ רָבִינָא: אֵימַר, "כָּהֵנָּה" – תַּרְתֵּי סְרֵי, "וְכָהֵנָּה" – עֶשְׂרִין וְאַרְבַּע.
>
> **Translation:** **Ravina objects to this** explanation: **Say** instead: **"Like these";** this is referring to an additional six, like the wives enumerated above, totaling **twelve. "And like those";** this is referring to all of those enumerated previously, totaling **twenty-four.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: "לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים" – יוֹתֵר מֵעֶשְׂרִים וְאַרְבַּע. לְמַאן דְּדָרֵישׁ וָיו, אַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה הָווּ. תַּנְיָא נָמֵי הָכִי: "לֹא יַרְבֶּה לוֹ נָשִׁים" – יוֹתֵר מֵאַרְבָּעִים וּשְׁמֹנֶה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara adds: Support for Ravina's interpretation **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"He shall not add many wives to himself";** this means he may not marry **more than twenty-four** women. The Gemara comments: **According to the one who interprets** the letter ***vav*,** translated as the conjunction "and" in the term "and like those," to add and expand upon what came before, the *vav* is written in order to add more, and therefore, **there are forty-eight** women. The Gemara comments: Support for this interpretation **is also taught** in a *baraita*: **"He shall not add many wives to himself";** this means he may not marry **more than forty-eight** women.

Alternative readings produce higher totals. If the second *kahena* is taken as doubling all that came before rather than adding a flat six, then 6 + 6 = 12, and *kahena* again doubles to yield 24. A *baraita* is cited in support: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-yoter me-esrim ve-arba* — he may not have more than twenty-four. Alternatively, taking the doubling principle further: 6 + 6 = 12, then a *kahena* equivalent to all of that is 12, so 12 + 12 = 24, and a final *kahena* equal to 24 produces 48. Another *baraita* is cited in support: *lo yarbeh lo nashim ve-yoter me-arba'im u-shemoneh* — he may not have more than forty-eight.

## Michal & David's Wives

### Michal as Eglah

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָא הֲוַאי מִיכַל! אָמַר רַב: עֶגְלָה זוֹ מִיכַל, וְלָמָּה נִקְרָא שְׁמָהּ עֶגְלָה? שֶׁחֲבִיבָה עָלָיו כְּעֶגְלָה. וְכֵן הוּא אוֹמֵר: "לוּלֵי חֲרַשְׁתֶּם בְּעֶגְלָתִי וְגוֹ'".
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the notion that David had only six wives. **But there was** also **Michal,** and so he had at least seven wives. **Rav said:** One of those six wives, **Eglah, is Michal, and why was she called Eglah** in the verse? It was **because she was dear to him like a calf [*egla*], and so** the verse **states** that Samson referred to his wife with the same term: **"If you had not plowed with my calf** you would not have found my secret" (Judges 14:18).

Among the six wives listed in Shmuel 2, chapter 3 is a woman named *Eglah*, which is an unusual name. The Gemara, citing Rav, identifies this as Michal: *amar Rav, Egla zu Michal*. The epithet *egla* is a term of endearment — *she-havivah alav ke-egla*, she was beloved to him like a calf. A parallel is drawn to Shimshon's riddle: *lulei charashtem be-eglati lo metzasem chidosi* — had you not plowed with my *egla*, meaning extracted the answer through my wife, you would not have solved the riddle.

### Michal's Children Problem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וּמִי הֲווֹ לְמִיכַל בְּנֵי? וְהָכְתִיב: "וּלְמִיכַל בַּת שָׁאוּל לֹא הָיָה לָהּ וָלָד עַד יוֹם מוֹתָהּ"! אָמַר רַב חִסְדָּא: עַד יוֹם מוֹתָהּ לֹא הָיָה לָהּ, בְּיוֹם מוֹתָהּ הָיָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the identification of Eglah with Michal: **And did Michal have children? But isn't it written: "And Michal the daughter of Saul had no child until the day of her death"** (II Samuel 6:23)? **Rav Ḥisda said: Until the day of her death she had no** child, but **on the day of her death she had** a child.

This identification immediately raises a difficulty: *u-mi havu le-Michal banim?* Did Michal actually have children? The verse in Shmuel explicitly states: *u-le-Michal bat Shaul lo hayah lah yeled ad yom motah* — Michal the daughter of Shaul had no child until the day of her death. This verse follows the episode in which Michal rebuked David for dancing before the *aron*, saying *mah nichbad hayom melech Yisrael*, accusing him of demeaning himself by dancing before everyone *ke-higlot niglot achad ha-reikim* — like one of the empty commoners. David responded that he would humble himself even further and would be held in esteem by the *shfachot*. After this exchange, the verse records that Michal had no offspring until her dying day.

### Hebron vs Jerusalem

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מִכְּדֵי, בָּנִים הֵיכָא קָא חָשֵׁיב לְהוּ? בְּחֶבְרוֹן. וְאִילּוּ מַעֲשֶׂה דְּמִיכַל בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם הֲוָה, דִּכְתִיב: "וּמִיכַל בַּת שָׁאוּל נִשְׁקְפָה בְּעַד הַחַלּוֹן וַתֵּרֶא אֶת הַמֶּלֶךְ דָּוִד מְפַזֵּז וּמְכַרְכֵּר לִפְנֵי ה׳ וַתִּבֶז". וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה, וְאִיתֵּימָא רַב יוֹסֵף: שְׁקַלְתֵּהּ מִיכַל לְמִיטְרַפְסַהּ! אֶלָּא אֵימָא: עַד אוֹתוֹ מַעֲשֶׂה – הָיָה לָהּ, מִכָּאן וְאֵילָךְ – לֹא הָיָה לָהּ.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **Now,** these **children** of David's, **where does it count them? In Hebron,** as Eglon was listed above with David's wives in Hebron. **But the incident with Michal,** in the context of which the verse says she had no children, **was in Jerusalem, as it is written:** "And it was so, as the Ark of the Lord came into the city of David, **that Michal the daughter of Saul looked out the window and saw King David leaping and dancing before the Lord, and she despised** him in her heart" (II Samuel 6:16). **And Rav Yehuda says, and some say** it is **Rav Yosef** who says: **Michal received her punishment [*lemitarpesah*]** immediately, and therefore could not have had children afterward. **Rather, say** a different explanation: **Until that incident, she had** a child; **from that** point **forward, she did not have** a child.

Two resolutions are offered. Rav Chisda proposes: *ad yom motah lo hayah lah, be-yom motah hayah lah* — she had no child *until* the day she died, meaning she gave birth on the very day of her death, presumably dying in childbirth from the son Yitream listed among David's Chevron children. The second resolution notes that the children enumerated in Shmuel 2:3 were born to David in *Chevron*, whereas the entire incident with Michal and the dancing before the *aron* took place in *Yerushalayim* — as the verse explicitly places the scene there. Therefore *mipnei baneha she-chashavu be-Chevron* — the children attributed to her were conceived in Chevron — and *ve-ilu ma'aseh de-Michal be-Yerushalayim havah* — the episode that resulted in her barrenness occurred only afterward in Yerushalayim. Rav Yehudah (or Rav Yosef) adds that she was punished for her words to the king: *shekaltah Michal de-mitrefah*. The fuller formulation reconciles the texts: *ad oto ma'aseh hayah lah, mi-kan ve-eilach lo hayah lah* — until that incident she had children; from that point on she did not.

This resolution is coherent: David reigned first in Chevron with a more limited kingdom, then consolidated power and moved to Yerushalayim. The children, including the one born of Michal (*Eglah*), predate the Yerushalayim episode.

### Merav-Michal Solution

The Gemara also addresses the verse in Shmuel 2, chapter 21 that refers to *chameshah bnei Michal bat Shaul* — five sons of Michal the daughter of Shaul — which seems to attribute even more children to her. One possible resolution, already noted on 19b, is: *Merav yaldah, Michal giddelah, be-chach nikreu al shemah* — Merav bore them, Michal raised them, and therefore they are called by her name. The word *yaldah* would normally imply she gave birth, but the Gemara is willing to entertain the principle that raising children can render them attributable to the one who raised them. This provides an alternative avenue of resolution independent of the Chevron-versus-Yerushalayim answer, though the Gemara employs both.

## Concubines & 400 Children

### Concubine Definition

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְהָכְתִיב: "וַיִּקַּח דָּוִד עוֹד פִּלַגְשִׁים וְנָשִׁים בִּירוּשָׁלַיִם"? לְמַלּוֹיֵי שְׁמוֹנָה עֶשְׂרֵה. מַאי "נָשִׁים" וּמַאי "פִּלַגְשִׁים"? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: נָשִׁים – בִּכְתוּבָּה וּבְקִידּוּשִׁין, פִּלַגְשִׁים – בְּלֹא כְּתוּבָּה וּבְלֹא קִידּוּשִׁין.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges the notion that David had only this limited number of wives. **But isn't it written: "And David took more concubines and wives in Jerusalem** after he came from Hebron" (II Samuel 5:13). The Gemara responds: All of these were **to complete** the tally of **eighteen** and no more. The Gemara asks about this verse: **What** is the meaning of **"wives" and what** is the meaning of **"concubines"** in that verse? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: Wives** receive **a marriage contract and betrothal; concubines are** taken **without a marriage contract and without betrothal.**

The verse in Shmuel 2, chapter 5 states that David took additional *pilagshim* and *nashim* in Yerushalayim — *vayikach David od pilagshim ve-nashim mi-Yerushalayim*. The Gemara asks: *mai nashim mai pilagshim* — what distinguishes a wife from a *pilegesh*? Rav Yehudah citing Rav explains: *nashim be-ketubah u-ve-kiddushin* — wives have a *ketubah* and *kiddushin*; *pilagshim belo ketubah u-velo kiddushin* — concubines have neither. The relationship is essentially a sexual one, without the formal legal structure of marriage. Whether such an arrangement is normatively permissible, and under what conditions — whether it was specific to kings, to the biblical period, or to the very wealthy — is not addressed here; the Gemara only records what the distinction is.

### 400 Yefat Toar Children

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אַרְבַּע מֵאוֹת יְלָדִים הָיוּ לוֹ לְדָוִד, וְכוּלָּן בְּנֵי יְפַת תּוֹאַר הָיוּ, וּמְגַדְּלֵי בְלוֹרִיּוֹת הָיוּ, וְכוּלָּן יוֹשְׁבִין בִּקְרוֹנוֹת שֶׁל זָהָב, וּמְהַלְּכִין בְּרָאשֵׁי גְיָיסוֹת הָיוּ, וְהֵם הָיוּ בַּעֲלֵי אֶגְרוֹפִין שֶׁל בֵּית דָּוִד.
>
> **Translation:** **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: David had four hundred children** in his army, **and all of them were sons of beautiful women** taken captive from their gentile homes during war (see Deuteronomy 21:10–14). **And they grew** their hair in **a gentile hairstyle, and they all sat in carriages [*bikronot*] of gold. And they walked at the head of the troops, and they were the strong-arm** enforcers **of the house of David,** on whose loyalty David's monarchy relied.

Rav Yehudah citing Rav teaches: *arba me'ot yeladim noldu le-David ve-khullam bnei yefat to'ar hayu* — four hundred children were born to David, and all of them were sons of *yefat to'ar*. The *yefat to'ar* is the beautiful captive discussed in *Ki Tetzei*: a non-Jewish woman captured in war whom a soldier desires. David was a highly martial king who fought many wars, and he apparently desired women captured in those campaigns, with the result that four hundred children were born from such relationships. These children all had *beloriot* — the braided hairstyle associated with non-Jewish culture — and they would sit in golden chariots, *bekronot shel zahav*, and lead the troops. They were the powerful fighters of the house of David: *ba'alei egrofim shel beit David*.

### Blorit Hair Problem

This creates a serious halakhic problem. The *belorit* is not merely a foreign aesthetic; it falls squarely within the prohibition of *chukkot ha-goyim* and possibly even *avodah zarah*. The Rambam in *Hilchot Avodah Zarah*, chapter 11, *halacha* 1, lists among the prohibited acts: *lo yilbash malbush ha-miyuchad lahem* — one may not wear clothing distinctive to them; *ve-lo yigadel tzitzit kemo tzitzit rosham* — one may not grow hair in the style of their hair; *ve-lo yegalach min ha-tzdadim ve-yaniach ha-se'ar be-emtza, kemo she-hem osim* — one may not shave the sides and leave hair in the center as they do. And: *ve-zehu ha-nikra blorit* — this is what is called *blorit*. According to the Rambam, violating these prohibitions incurs *malkut*.

### Rambam's Close-to-Royalty

The Rambam himself provides a limited dispensation: *Yisrael she-hayah karov la-malchut ve-tzarich liyeshev lifnei malcheihem ve-yehei gnai im lo yidameh lahen* — a Jew who is close to the royal court and must appear before non-Jewish kings, for whom it would be demeaning not to resemble them, *harei zeh mutar lilbosh ke-malbushan u-le-galach ha-se'ar keneged panav ke-derech she-hem osim* — he is permitted to dress as they dress and to style his hair as they do.

### Kesef Mishneh Defense

The *Kesef Mishneh* cites the case of Avtulos bar Reuven, who was permitted to have a *komi* haircut — a non-Jewish style — because he was *karov la-malchut*, in proximity to royalty. From this the Rambam derived the broader dispensation for dress as well.

But the *Kesef Mishneh* presses further: if the Rambam rules that one incurs *malkut* for these acts, how could the *chachamim* permit them even for those close to royalty, given that such proximity does not ordinarily override a *lav* of the Torah? The answer offered by the *Kesef Mishneh* is: *mishum hatzalat Yisrael hu de-sharu* — it was permitted on grounds of saving Jewish lives. Those who served as emissaries or advisors to kings were in a position to protect the Jewish people, and thus the allowance falls under the principle of *pikuach nefesh*. The *Kesef Mishneh* then applies this reasoning directly to the four hundred children: *nami hatzalat Yisrael hu de-havu meva'atim et ha-oyvim* — this too was permitted for the sake of *hatzalat Yisrael*, because these men, placed at the forefront of battle looking entirely non-Jewish, would strike fear into the enemy. Seeing what appeared to be an enormous, non-Jewish-looking army advancing against them, the enemy would lose heart — even their own population appeared to oppose them. The *belorit* served a strategic military function of psychological intimidation, and on that basis the *Kesef Mishneh* justifies it.

An alternative formulation is also raised: *she-ha-Torah lo partah davar ela amrah uvechukoteihem lo telechu* — the Torah did not specify the precise content of this prohibition, only stating the general rule not to follow their customs. The Torah entrusted the details to the *chachamim*, and the *chachamim* determined that it does not apply to those close to royalty. Under this reading there is no true *Torah*-level prohibition being overridden; the *chachamim* have simply defined its scope.

## Amnon & Tamar Incident

### Tamar as Yefat Toar

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** וְאָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: תָּמָר בַּת יְפַת תּוֹאַר הָיְתָה, שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "וְעַתָּה דַּבֶּר נָא אֶל הַמֶּלֶךְ כִּי לֹא יִמְנָעֵנִי מִמֶּךָּ". וְאִי סָלְקָא דַעְתָּךְ בַּת נִישּׂוּאִין הֲוַאי, אֲחָתֵיהּ מִי הֲוָה שַׁרְיָא לֵיהּ? אֶלָּא שְׁמַע מִינַּהּ: בַּת יְפַת תּוֹאַר הָיְתָה.
>
> **Translation:** **And Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** David's daughter **Tamar was the daughter of a beautiful woman** taken captive in war and was born before her mother converted. Therefore, Tamar was not considered the daughter of David according to *halakha*. The proof of this is in what she said to Amnon, son of David, **as it is stated: "Now, therefore, speak, please, to the king, for he will not withhold me from you"** (II Samuel 13:13). **And if it enters your mind** to say that **she was the daughter of** a woman David **married, would** David **have permitted** Amnon's **sister to him** as a wife? **Rather, learn from** this verse that **she was the daughter of a beautiful woman** who converted after Tamar was born, so halakhically Tamar was not a daughter of David.

Rav Yehudah citing Rav teaches: *Tamar bat yefat to'ar hayeta* — Tamar was the daughter of a *yefat to'ar*. This is derived from her own words during the assault, when she pleaded with Amnon: *ve-atah daber na el ha-melech ki lo yimna'eni mimecha* — speak to the king, for he will not withhold me from you. How could Tamar have suggested that the king would permit her marriage to Amnon, given that they were half-siblings sharing the same father? The answer is that she must have been a *bat yefat to'ar* — a daughter born of a captive woman — and thus there was a question whether her status as David's daughter was halakhically equivalent to a full sister. *Michdei bat achva me-nisuin hava, achva hi ve-i sharei lei?* — she was indeed his sister through their father. The very fact that she could plausibly argue the king might permit it indicates she knew there was a technical basis for such a position, and that basis is her being a *bat yefat to'ar*.

This raises a further question addressed in *Tosafot*. The *Yerushalmi* records a *machloket* between Rav and Shmuel: *Rav amar eina muteret ba-milchamah ela bi'ah rishonah* — Rav holds that relations with a *yefat to'ar* are permitted once, in the heat of battle, before the full conversion process. *Ve-Shmuel amar af lo bi'ah rishonah* — Shmuel holds that even the first act of relations is not permitted until the conversion ritual is complete. According to Shmuel's position, by the time a man could have legitimate relations with a *yefat to'ar*, she would already be Jewish — so how could Tamar have been born before her mother converted?

*Tosafot* offers two answers. First: *she-imah kvar hayeta uberet ke-she-ba'ah la-milchamah* — Tamar's mother may have already been pregnant with Tamar from her previous non-Jewish partner at the time she was captured. David then performed the *yefat to'ar* ritual and formally married her, but Tamar herself was conceived before that. Thus, even according to Shmuel, David never had relations with Tamar's mother before she was Jewish — he only did so after the conversion — but Tamar herself was conceived by a different father beforehand. Second: *mitoch she-gadlah be-cheiko shel David karu lah bat ha-melech* — since Tamar grew up in David's household, she was called the king's daughter, even if David was not her biological father. This reading can absorb the textual reference to her as David's daughter without requiring that David fathered her at all. Neither answer forecloses the other, and together they demonstrate that the *Tamar* narrative cannot serve as definitive proof in either direction regarding the *bi'ah rishonah* question.

### Yonadav's Evil Advice

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וּלְאַמְנוֹן רֵעַ וּשְׁמוֹ יוֹנָדָב בֶּן שִׁמְעָה אֲחִי דָוִד וְיוֹנָדָב אִישׁ חָכָם וְגוֹ'". אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: אִישׁ חָכָם לְרִשְׁעָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara continues to interpret the story of Amnon and Tamar. The verse states: **"And Amnon had a friend whose name was Jonadab, son of Shimeah, David's brother,** and Jonadab was a very **wise man"** (II Samuel 13:3). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: A wise man for wickedness.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיֹּאמֶר ... מַדּוּעַ אַתָּה כָּכָה דַּל בֶּן הַמֶּלֶךְ ... וַיֹּאמֶר לוֹ יוֹנָדָב שְׁכַב עַל מִשְׁכָּבְךָ וְהִתְחָל וְגוֹ'", עַד "וְעָשְׂתָה לְעֵינַי אֶת הַבִּרְיָה". "וַתִּקַּח הַמַּשְׂרֵת וַתִּצֹק לְפָנָיו". אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: שֶׁעָשְׂתָה לוֹ מִינֵי טִיגּוּן.
>
> **Translation:** The verse recounts Jonadab's words to Amnon: **"And he said to him: Why, son of the king, are you so, becoming leaner** from day to day… **and Jonadab said to him: Lie on your bed and feign illness,** and when your father comes to see you, say to him: Let my sister Tamar come, please, and give me bread, **and she should dress the food in my sight…And she took the pan and poured them out before him"** (II Samuel 13:4–5, 9). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** This means **that** Tamar **prepared** various **kinds of fried [*tiggun*]** food **for** Amnon.

The narrative returns to the incident itself. The text introduces Amnon's associate: *ve-le-Amnon rei ve-shmo Yonadav ben Shima achi David* — Amnon had a friend named Yonadav, the son of David's brother Shima, making him David's nephew. He is described as *ish chacham* — a clever man. Rav Yehudah citing Rav glosses: *ish chacham hayah le-rish'ah* — he was clever in wickedness, for he devised the scheme. Yonadav advised Amnon: *shechav al mitatecha ve-hitchala* — lie on your bed and feign illness. When people come to you, say that only Tamar's cooking can restore you. Rav Yehudah citing Rav adds that what Tamar made was *minei tigun* — fried foods. The reason the ruse required Tamar to cook in front of him specifically was that fried food must be served immediately; it loses its quality if it sits. He could therefore legitimately demand that it be prepared fresh in his presence, providing the pretext to clear everyone else from the room.

### Amnon's Hatred

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיִּשְׂנָאֶהָ אַמְנוֹן שִׂנְאָה גְּדוֹלָה מְאֹד".
>
> **Translation:** At the end of the story, the verse states: **"Then Amnon hated her with exceeding, great hatred,** for greater was the hatred with which he hated her than the love with which he had loved her" (II Samuel 13:15).

After the assault, the verse states: *vayisna Amnon sin'ah gedolah me'od* — Amnon hated her with a very great hatred. The reversal of passion into revulsion is noted without extended commentary. The hatred he felt was likely intensified by the sight of Tamar, which confronted him constantly with his own transgression.

### Tamar's Pubic Hair Issue

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** מַאי טַעְמָא? אָמַר רַבִּי יִצְחָק: נִימָא נִקְשְׁרָה לוֹ וַעֲשָׂאַתּוּ כְּרוּת שׇׁפְכָה. וְכִי נִקְשְׁרָה לוֹ, אִיהִי מַאי עֲבַדָה? אֶלָּא אֵימָא: קָשְׁרָה לוֹ נִימָא וַעֲשָׂאַתּוּ כְּרוּת שׇׁפְכָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara asks: **What is the reason** for Amnon's intense hatred? **Rabbi Yitzḥak says:** While he raped her, **a hair [*nima*]** of hers **became tied** around **his** penis **and caused him to be one whose penis has been severed. The Gemara asks: But if** the hair **became tied** around **his** penis, **what did she do?** Why would Amnon hold this against her? **Rather, say** that **she** intentionally **tied a hair around his** penis during intercourse, **and she made him one whose penis has been severed** in order to take revenge on him, and for this he hated her.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אִינִי? וְהָא דָּרֵשׁ רָבָא: מַאי דִּכְתִיב "וַיֵּצֵא לָךְ שֵׁם בַּגּוֹיִם בְּיׇפְיֵךְ"? שֶׁאֵין לָהֶן לִבְנוֹת יִשְׂרָאֵל לֹא שְׂעַר בֵּית הַשֶּׁחִי וְלָא בֵּית הָעֶרְוָה! שָׁאנֵי תָּמָר, דְּבַת יְפַת תּוֹאַר הֲוַאי.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara challenges this: **Is that so? But didn't Rava interpret** a verse **homiletically: What** is the meaning of that **which is written: "And your renown went forth among the nations about your beauty"** (Ezekiel 16:14)? This teaches **that Jewish women do not have armpit hair or pubic hair.** Therefore, Tamar would have had no hair to injure Amnon in that way. The Gemara responds: **Tamar is different, as she was the daughter of a beautiful woman,** who was a gentile.

The Gemara then addresses a strange textual issue. The phrase *mai Tamar* is read to suggest that Tamar did something involving a strand of hair tied in a knot, and this caused Amnon to become a *k'rut shofcha* — one whose generative capacity is severed or destroyed. The verse is read: *kashra lo nima ve-asta lo k'rut shofcha* — she tied a hair-strand around him and thereby rendered him sterile. It is understood here as her act of resistance — she managed to strike back at her attacker in some way, leaving him permanently incapacitated.

This raises an apparent contradiction with a statement of Rava, who teaches from the verse *vayeitzei shemech ba-goyim be-yofyeich* — your fame went out among the nations for your beauty — that Jewish women do not have hair in the underarm or the pubic area: *she-ein lahem li-vnot Yisrael lo sarchat ha-shachi ve-lo beit ha-erva*. If Jewish women lack pubic hair, how could Tamar have used one? The resolution proposed is that Jewish women of that era were scrupulous about shaving — they did grow hair naturally, but maintained careful grooming such that they were smooth. This is not a claim that Jewish women biologically lack body hair (*nishtana ha-teva* is not invoked), but rather a description of their customary practice. How then could Tamar have done this? *Ve-shani Tamar de-bat yefat to'ar havi* — Tamar, as the daughter of a *yefat to'ar*, was not in the same category. Alternatively, since she was an unmarried woman who was not having sexual relations, she would have had no occasion or reason to shave, and so she was in a different practical situation than married women.

### Yichud Decree on Women

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַתִּקַּח תָּמָר אֵפֶר עַל רֹאשָׁהּ וְאֶת כְּתֹנֶת הַפַּסִּים אֲשֶׁר עָלֶיהָ קָרָעָה". תָּנָא מִשְּׁמֵיהּ דְּרַבִּי יְהוֹשֻׁעַ בֶּן קׇרְחָה: גָּדֵר גָּדוֹל גָּדְרָה תָּמָר בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה. אָמְרוּ: לִבְנוֹת מְלָכִים כָּךְ, לִבְנוֹת הֶדְיוֹטוֹת עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אִם לַצְּנוּעוֹת כָּךְ, לַפְּרוּצוֹת עַל אַחַת כַּמָּה וְכַמָּה. אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: בְּאוֹתָהּ שָׁעָה גָּזְרוּ עַל הַיִּיחוּד, וְעַל הַפְּנוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** The verse relates that after Amnon raped her: **"And Tamar put ashes on her head and rent her garment of many colors that was on her"** (II Samuel 13:19). The Sages **taught in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korḥa: Tamar established a great fence at that time** by way of her public outcry, as people **said:** If **such** an occurrence could happen **to the daughters of kings, all the more so** could it happen **to the daughters of ordinary** people. If **such** an occurrence could happen **to modest women** like Tamar, who resisted, **all the more so** could it happen **to licentious women. Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says: At that time they decreed** **about seclusion,** that a man should not be secluded with women who are forbidden to him, **and about a single woman.**

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** יִחוּד? דְּאוֹרָיְיתָא הוּא! דְּאָמַר רַבִּי יוֹחָנָן מִשּׁוּם רַבִּי שִׁמְעוֹן בֶּן יְהוֹצָדָק: רֶמֶז לְיִיחוּד מִן הַתּוֹרָה מִנַּיִין? שֶׁנֶּאֱמַר: "כִּי יְסִיתְךָ אָחִיךָ בֶן אִמֶּךָ". וְכִי בֶּן אֵם מֵסִית, בֶּן אָב אֵינוֹ מֵסִית? אֶלָּא לוֹמַר לָךְ: בֵּן מִתְיַיחֵד עִם אִמּוֹ, וְאֵין אַחֵר מִתְיַיחֵד עִם כׇּל עֲרָיוֹת שֶׁבַּתּוֹרָה.
>
> **Translation:** The Gemara objects: **Seclusion** with a woman forbidden by familial ties **is** prohibited **by Torah** law, and was not a rabbinic decree issued in the time of David. **As Rabbi Yoḥanan says in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak: From where** is there **an allusion to** the *halakha* that **seclusion** is forbidden **by Torah** law? **As it is stated: "If your brother, the son of your mother, entices you"** (Deuteronomy 13:7). One can ask: **But does the son of a mother entice, and does the son of a father not entice?** Why mention only the son of a mother? **Rather,** this verse serves **to tell you** that only **a son** may **be secluded with his mother.** Sons are frequently with their mother, and two half-brothers of one mother consequently have the opportunity to grow close to one another. **But another** individual **may not be secluded with those with whom relations are forbidden by the Torah,** including a stepmother. Therefore, half-brothers of one father spend less time together.

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** אֶלָּא אֵימָא: גָּזְרוּ עַל יִיחוּד דִּפְנוּיָה.
>
> **Translation:** Since seclusion, then, is prohibited by Torah law, how did Rav say that it was prohibited by a decree issued in King David's time? **Rather, say** that **they decreed against seclusion** of a man **with a single woman,** to prevent occurrences like that of Amnon and Tamar.

The narrative concludes with a communal consequence. The verse describes Tamar placing ash on her head and rending her garment of many colors in mourning. A *tanna* in the name of Rabbi Yehoshua ben Korcha teaches: *gader gadol gadrah Tamar be-otah sha'ah* — Tamar erected a great fence through this incident. The reasoning: *amru bnot melachim kach, bnot hedyotot al achat kama ve-chama; im li-tzenu'ot kach, li-frutzot al achat kama ve-chama* — if this could happen to the modest daughter of a king, *kal ve-chomer* to anyone; if even a modest woman was vulnerable, certainly an immodest one would be.

Rav Yehudah teaching: *ba-otah sha'ah gazru al ha-yichud ve-al ha-penuyah* — at that time they decreed regarding *yichud* and regarding the unmarried woman. The Gemara clarifies: is *yichud* not already prohibited from the Torah? Yes — *yichud de-Oraita hu* — seclusion with a married woman is a Torah-level prohibition, derived from the verse *ki yesitcha achicha ben imcha*: the Torah specifies "the brother, the son of your mother" rather than simply "your brother" to convey that only a son may be secluded with his own mother; any other pairing involving the forbidden relations of the Torah falls under *yichud*. That derivation is attributed to Rabbi Yochanan in the name of Rabbi Shimon ben Yehotzadak.

If *yichud* was already prohibited, what was new in the decree? The Gemara clarifies: *ela eima gazru al yichud de-penuyah* — the decree addressed *yichud* with an unmarried woman. Until that point, an unmarried man and an unmarried woman could be alone together, since neither was technically *arayot* to the other. After the incident with Tamar — which demonstrated the danger even in a royal, modest setting — the rabbis extended the prohibition to cover seclusion between unmarried men and women as well.

## Adoniyah's Rebellion

### Failed Coronation

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַאֲדֹנִיָּה בֶן חַגִּית מִתְנַשֵּׂא לֵאמֹר אֲנִי אֶמְלֹךְ". אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: מְלַמֵּד שֶׁבִּיקֵּשׁ לְהוֹלְמוֹ, וְלֹא הוֹלַמְתּוֹ.
>
> **Translation:** Apropos Amnon, the Gemara cites traditions about another son of David: **"Now Adonijah, son of Haggith, exalted himself, saying: I will be king"** (I Kings 1:5). **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** The term "exalted himself" **teaches that he sought** for the monarchy **to fit him, but it did not fit him.**

The *mishnah* concludes with Adoniyah the son of Chagit, who declared *ani emloch* — I shall be king — and made an attempt to claim the throne. Rav Yehudah citing Rav interprets this: *melamed she-bikkesh le-holmo ve-lo holmato* — the text teaches that he sought to have the crown placed on his head, and it did not fit.

### Speedy Runners Enhancement

> **Hebrew/Aramaic:** "וַיַּעַשׂ לוֹ רֶכֶב וּפָרָשִׁים וַחֲמִשִּׁים אִישׁ רָצִים לְפָנָיו". מַאי רְבוּתָא? אָמַר רַב יְהוּדָה אָמַר רַב: כּוּלָּן נְטוּלֵי טְחוֹל וַחֲקוּקֵי כַּפּוֹת רַגְלַיִם הָיוּ.
>
> **Translation:** The verse continues: **"And he prepared for himself chariots and riders and fifty people to run before him"** (I Kings 1:5). The Gemara asks: **What is the novelty** of these actions, since other wealthy people do the same, even if they are not the sons of kings, with designs on the throne? **Rav Yehuda says** that **Rav says:** What was unique was that the runners **all had** their **spleens removed and had the soles of their feet hollowed,** removing the flesh of their feet, and these two procedures enhanced their speed.

The verse records that Adonijah prepared a chariot and cavalry and fifty runners to go before him. The Gemara asks: is that remarkable? Many princes have entourages. The answer is what distinguished these fifty men: *kulam ntulei tchol ve-chakukei kipot raglayim hayu* — they had all undergone spleen removal, and their foot-soles had been treated — literally, carved. The spleen removal was apparently understood to enhance running endurance. The treatment to the feet refers to the deadening of nerve endings: since runners of that era ran barefoot, they would be slowed or stopped by sharp thorns and rocks underfoot. By killing the nerve endings in the soles of their feet, these runners could traverse any terrain without pain, enabling them to travel greater distances at greater speed. These were deliberately engineered elite runners, prepared through what amounts to surgical intervention.
